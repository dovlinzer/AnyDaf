# Temurah 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eshkolot & Decline

### Glory & Eshkolot

The daf picks up from the bottom of 15b, where the Gemara had quoted *pesukim* relating to the return of the Jewish people and the building of the second *Beit HaMikdash* — how they wept, remembering the glory of the first *Beit HaMikdash* — alongside the concluding *pasuk*, *gadol yihiyeh kevod habayit ha'acharon min harishon*, that the glory of the latter house would ultimately surpass that of the former. From there, the Gemara transitions into quoting a *Mishnah* in Sotah that likewise reflects on the greatness of earlier generations, citing Yosef ben Yoezer Ish Tzeredah and Yosef ben Yochanan Ish Yerushalayim as the last of the *Eshkolot* — those who possessed everything within them.

### Eshkolot Definition

Rashi explains that *Eshkolot* — literally "clusters," like a cluster of grapes — is expounded by the Gemara as *ish shekol bo*, a person who is complete: possessing *Torah*, *Yirat Chet*, and *Gemilut Chasadim*. The Gemara now focuses on the *Torah* dimension, asserting that after these two figures, nobody learned *Torah* through them the way one learned through Moshe Rabbeinu — meaning, apparently, that they transmitted it with perfect fidelity. The Gemara proceeds to challenge this claim.

### Righteous Man & Goat

The Gemara cites the *Baraita*: *lo hayah bahem shum dofi* — from the time of Moshe until Yosef ben Yoezer died, the *Eshkolot* had not even the slightest *dofi*, no defect of any kind. *Mikan va'eilach hayah bahem shum dofi* — from that point onward, there were problems.

But what exactly is the *dofi* in question? The Gemara clarifies that the issue here is not primarily the *Torah* transmission dimension; it concerns the *Eshkolot*'s behavior, character, and *shmirat mitzvot*. To illustrate, the Gemara cites a *Baraita*: *ma'aseh bechasid echad shehayah gone'ach milibo* — there was a righteous individual who suffered from chest pains. Doctors were consulted, and they prescribed that each morning he drink freshly milked warm milk — that is, milk drawn directly from the animal while still at body temperature. To facilitate this, he tied a goat to his bedpost and would draw milk from it each morning.

The following day his friends came to visit, and when they saw the goat they exclaimed: *listim mezuyan betoch beito ve'anu nichnasim levakro*? — "There is an armed robber inside his house, and we are coming to visit him?" The prohibition against raising small grazing animals — sheep and goats — in the settled areas of the Land of Israel was well known, since such animals devastate the ground, cropping the grass to its roots and causing serious harm to the land. The friends were not moved by the man's medical need, and according to the Tosefta's version of the story, they left.

After he died, they examined his entire record and found that the only transgression in his life was this one goat — *velo matzu bo avon ela shel osah ez bilvad*. And he himself acknowledged as much on his deathbed: *she-avarti al divrei chaverai* — I violated the ruling of my colleagues, *she-ein megadlin behemah dakah be-eretz yisrael*. What is striking is the way he frames the transgression: not as an intrinsic sin, but as a violation of a rabbinic policy and the authority of his colleagues. One might question whether a *pikuach nefesh*-adjacent need might have warranted an exception, or whether the issue was more a matter of the precedent and policy implications of keeping an animal in one's home rather than grazing it on open land.

### Chasid Stories

The Gemara then notes: *kol heicha de'amar ma'aseh bechasid echad* — whenever a story mentions a *chasid*, it was either Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava or Rabbi Yehuda berabi Ilai. This creates a difficulty, because those Rabbis lived many generations after Yosef ben Yoezer. If after these two *Eshkolot* there could be no one without *dofi*, how can such later figures be held up as exemplars of near-perfect conduct? The Gemara is suggesting that even after Yosef ben Yoezer and Yosef ben Yochanan, individuals existed whose only failing was something as minor as this one goat — *shum dofi* in the meaningful sense, then, hardly applied to them.

### Dofi Smichah

Rav Yosef resolves the tension: *dofi shel smicha ktani* — the *dofi* referred to in the *Baraita* is not about personal observance or *Yirat Chet* at all. In those respects, great figures continued to exist even after that generation. The *dofi* pertains specifically to the realm of *Torah* transmission and *halacha* — and in particular it refers to *smicha*. Here *smicha* does not mean rabbinic ordination, but rather the earliest recorded disputes among the *zugot* concerning whether one may perform *smicha* — the laying of hands — on an animal sacrifice brought on *Yom Tov*. This controversy, which persisted across many generations of the *zugot*, only began after Yosef ben Yoezer and Yosef ben Yochanan. Before them, no debates in *halacha* arose; the transmission was perfect. The *dofi* was, therefore, the emergence of *halachic* debate — not a failure of moral character.

The Gemara anticipates an objection: Yosef ben Yoezer himself was a party to the original *smicha* controversy. The response: *ki plig bah besof shnei* — that controversy arose only at the very end of his life, *debatzer liba*, when the sharpness of transmission had already begun to erode. Until that point, the transmission had been flawless.

## Transmission Debate

### Forgetting View

This brings the Gemara to a broader question that was lurking throughout: why are there so many debates recorded in the Talmud? This critique was famously leveled by the Karaites, who argued that the sheer volume of disagreement in the *Talmud* demonstrates that *Torah shebe'al peh* rests on a faulty transmission — and if the *masorah* is unreliable, it cannot be trusted at all. One response available to the Rabbis is to note that the vast majority of basic *halachic* parameters are universally agreed upon, and that the debates concern only a subset of questions.

The approach implied by a number of *Gemarot*, including the present one, is more direct: debates arose because things were forgotten. This is reinforced by the well-known statement about Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel — *mishe-rabu talmidei Beit Shammai uBeit Hillel shelo shimshu kol tzarchan* — that when students did not attend sufficiently to their teachers and did not absorb the tradition fully, disputes proliferated. The entire narrative arc of this Gemara — which is building toward the *chamesh chataot hameitot* of our *Mishnah* — presses this theme: had things been transmitted perfectly, there would have been no debates.

### Rambam's View

The Rambam emphatically rejects this position. Both philosophically and as a response to the Karaite challenge, he insists that the transmission was always perfect: nothing that was *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* was ever forgotten or disputed. In his view, all *halachic* debates arise not from forgotten traditions but from the application of received principles to new cases — the clarification of parameters that were never explicitly transmitted in the first place. This is a powerful and intellectually coherent position. Even granting perfect transmission of every received *halacha*, enormous tracts of *halachic* reality remain unaddressed, and it is in navigating that territory that debates inevitably emerge.

The difficulty, of course, is that the present Gemara appears to cut directly against the Rambam, framing debates as products of forgetting. The Rambam's approach is also notably in tension with *Rashi*'s reading of the earlier passage, which interprets the debates that arose after Moshe's death as attempts to recall forgotten *halachot*.

### Gadol Yihiyeh

One may ask whether the surface nostalgia of this entire *sugya* — its lament for the great transmission that eroded — contains a hidden subtext. The *pasuk* that opened the *sugya*, *gadol yihiyeh kevod habayit ha'acharon min harishon*, suggests that the "latter house" ultimately surpasses the first. Elsewhere the Gemara weighs in clearly on the relative merits of received transmission versus creative intellectual engagement: Rabbi Akiva, who derived and developed, is preferred to Rabbi Eliezer, who essentially received and transmitted. One might wonder whether beneath the apparent mourning over forgotten *halachot* lies an implicit acknowledgment that *Torah shebe'al peh* — in its fullest form — is not merely retrieval but creation, and that perhaps the intellectual vitality of later generations represents its own kind of *gadol yihiyeh*. This reading may press beyond what the text explicitly supports, but the tension is present.

## Forgetting & Retrieval

### 3000 Halachot Lost

The Gemara now turns to the theme of forgetting and retrieval in full. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *shloshet alafim halachot nishtakechu bimei evlo shel Moshe* — 3,000 *halachot* were forgotten during the mourning period after Moshe's death.

The people turned to Yehoshua and said: *she'al* — go and ask God, retrieve what we have lost. Yehoshua replied: *lo bashamayim hi* — it is not in heaven. After Moshe, the process of divine supplementation of *Torah* is closed.

They then turned to Shmuel and said: *she'al*. He replied: *eileh hamitzvot* — these are the commandments God gave to Moshe; they are complete, and no prophet may innovate thereafter. The principle *she'ein navi rashai l'chadesh davar mei'ata* — that no prophet after Moshe may add to *halacha* — applies here as well. Even if one wished to frame the request not as innovation but as recovery, the answer *lo bashamayim hi* forecloses it.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha then notes: *af chatat shemetu ba'aleha nishtakecha bimei evlo shel Moshe* — the *halacha* regarding a *chatat* whose owners died was among the things forgotten during that mourning period. The alternate textual reading, cited by the *Shita Mekubetzet*, replaces *shemetu* with *shenitkapru ba'aleha*, tying the forgotten *halacha* directly to the dispute in our *Mishnah* — whether the *chatat* of a *tzibur* that brought another *korban* must die or may graze.

One might wonder how *halachot* could be forgotten during a mourning period of only forty days, particularly *halachot* about relatively rare scenarios such as a *chatat shemetu ba'aleha*. The answer may lie in how rarely such cases arose in practice — a *halacha* that was never applied during the forty years in the wilderness might simply never have been internalized in a way that survived the upheaval of Moshe's death. Beyond that, the people were profoundly dependent on Moshe, much as they had been during the episode of the golden calf when he was absent only briefly. The trauma of his death, and the grief of mourning, could have been enough to disrupt even memories that were held.

They then turned to Pinchas and said: *she'al*. He responded: *lo bashamayim hi*. They turned to Elazar and said: *she'al*. He responded: *eileh hamitzvot she'ein navi rashai l'chadesh davar mei'atah*. It is notable that the Gemara groups these four figures into two pairs, each pair giving a different formulation of the refusal. Yehoshua and Shmuel — political leaders who also had prophetic capacity — gave the *lo bashamayim hi* answer. Elazar and Pinchas — whose primary role was as *Kohen Gadol* and communicators of divine will through the *Urim VeTumim* — gave the second answer. The people may have hoped that those who wore the *Urim VeTumim*, whose very function was to ask God's guidance in critical moments, might succeed where the political leadership had not. Yet the answer is the same: from this point forward, *halacha* cannot be retrieved from Heaven.

### Yehoshua's Loss

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: at the moment Moshe was about to depart to *Gan Eden*, he said to Yehoshua — *she'al mimeni kol sefeikot sheyesh lach* — ask me whatever questions or doubts you have. Yehoshua responded: *Rebbi, kelum hinachticha sha'ah achat vehalachti lemakom acher* — "My teacher, have I ever left your side even for a moment? Did you not yourself write of me: *umesharto Yehoshua bin Nun na'ar lo yamish mitoch ha'ohel*? I never left the tent of Torah. I have nothing to ask."

*Miyad tashash kocho shel Yehoshua* — immediately Yehoshua's intellectual strength was sapped. *Venishtakechu mimenu shalosh me'ot halachot* — he forgot 300 *halachot*, *venoldu lo sheva me'ot sefeikot* — and 700 doubts arose for him.

Rashi explains the punishment in interpersonal terms: *she'hitzta'er Moshe Yehoshua be'atzmo shehayah gadol kamohu* — Yehoshua pained Moshe by essentially declaring himself Moshe's equal, "I have everything I need from you." To someone on his deathbed, the appropriate response is not confidence but need, not self-sufficiency but expression of dependence. Yet one may also read a deeper moral in Yehoshua's failure: the Gemara elsewhere warns that *ba'al ga'avah*, arrogance, causes one's *Torah* to be forgotten. Even if Yehoshua's statement was factually accurate at that moment, a disposition of humility demands that one never consider oneself complete — that one seize every opportunity to learn. The very last moment with one's teacher is not the moment for declarations of self-sufficiency.

The loss was especially irreversible because, as God later told Yehoshua: *lomar lach ee efshar* — I cannot tell you what was forgotten. *Lo bashamayim hi* now applied in full. The moment of Moshe's final teaching had passed, and with it the last window for this kind of recovery. Yehoshua was told instead: *lech u'tordan b'milchama* — direct their energies toward war, which was in any event the next stage of the national mission. The people, furious that their *halachot* had been lost and demanding from Yehoshua what he could not supply, needed to be redirected — and the conquest of the land was at hand.

It should be noted that the 3,000 *halachot* forgotten during the mourning for Moshe and the 300 forgotten because of Yehoshua's response are not contradictory traditions; they describe different moments and different losses.

### 1700 Hermeneutics Lost

The *Baraita* then adds a further dimension: *elef u'shva me'ot kalin v'chamurin, u'gezeirot shavot, v'dikdukei sofrim nishtakechu bimei evlo shel Moshe* — 1,700 instances of *kal v'chomer*, *gezeirot shavot*, and *dikdukei sofrim* were forgotten during the mourning for Moshe.

This is a striking formulation. *Kal v'chomer* and *gezeira shava* are the foundational hermeneutical tools of *Torah shebe'al peh* — the very instruments of rabbinic legal reasoning. The Gemara here frames even these as part of the *masorah*, as something transmitted and then forgotten, rather than as exercises of independent creative intellect. Indeed, the overriding thrust of this *sugya* is to present the entire enterprise of *Torah shebe'al peh* as essentially an act of retrieval.

What are *dikdukei sofrim*? Rashi (cited in the context of *Rabbeinu Gershom*) explains that *dikdukei sofrim* refers to the practice of organizing *halachot* into counted groups — *sefirot sefirot* — as, for example, the five *chataot hameitot*, or the thirteen things said about such-and-such topic. The role of the *sofrim* was apparently to take the existing data and organize it, to assign it a number and pull it together. What was forgotten was the content that would have permitted this organizing to be done — so the categories and counts could not be reconstructed. Rashi adds the phrase *she'adayin lo tzirfum* — things that had not yet been pulled together — suggesting that the *dikdukei sofrim* represented the one dimension of the rabbinic enterprise that was not pure transmission but involved an act of organization. Even that was lost.

The full weight of the Gemara's framing here is that the rabbinic tools most associated with intellectual creativity — *kal v'chomer* most of all, since it famously requires no *mesorah* — are being cast as part of the received tradition. The implication is that even rabbinic derivation, on this reading, is ultimately a form of recovery rather than innovation.

Rabbi Abahu then announces the resolution: *hechiziran Osniel ben Kenaz mitokh pilpulo* — Osniel ben Kenaz recovered all of them through his sharpness. The reference point is the verse in which Kalev offered his daughter in marriage to whoever could conquer *Kiryat Sefer*, and Osniel ben Kenaz, his brother, won her. The name *Kiryat Sefer* — a city of the *sofrim*, of counting — is read as a reference to the *dikdukei sofrim* that had been lost, and Osniel's conquest of it through *milchama* is read as his recovery of the lost tradition through the *milchamta shel Torah*, the battle of intellectual engagement.

### Achsah & Provision

The Gemara now digresses on the figure of Achsah, Kalev's daughter given to Osniel in marriage. Why was she called *Achsah*? *Shekol haro'ah otah ko'eis al ishto* — because anyone who saw her became angry at his own wife, so striking was her beauty.

The narrative then turns to the *pasuk*: *vayehi b'vo'ah vatasteihu lish'ol mei'et aviha sadeh* — when she came to Osniel, she enticed him to ask her father Kalev for a field. *Vatitznach mei'al hachamor* — she lowered herself, or in the Gemara's reading, she brayed, from the *chamor*. The Gemara reads *vatitznach* as a kind of complaint. Rava said in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak: she said to Osniel — *mah chamor zeh keivan she'ein lo ma'achal b'avuso miyad tzo'ek* — just as a donkey, when it has no food in its trough, immediately cries out, *kach ishah she'ein lah tevu'ah b'tokh beitah miyad tzo'eket* — so too a wife who has no grain in her house immediately cries out. In short: we are about to start a life together, and someone must attend to practical provision.

She then goes to her father and says: *tnah li bracha ki eretz hanegev netatani* — give me a blessing, for you have given me a dry land. The Gemara reads this allegorically: *bayit shemenugav mikol tova* — a house emptied of all good. *Adam she'ein bo ela Torah bilvad* — a man who has nothing but *Torah* alone. What, she says to her father, have you given me? *Gulos mayim* — springs of water? She is saying: a *talmid chacham* is all well and good, but who puts food on the table?

Kalev's response — *vayiten lah Kalev et gulos eliyot v'et gulos tachtiyot*, he gave her the upper and lower springs — is also read allegorically. The Gemara reads his reply as: *mi shekol razei elyonim vetachtiyot*, the one who knows all the secrets of the upper and lower worlds — that is, this great *talmid chacham*, your husband who was able to restore what was lost from the time of Moshe — *yevakesh mimenu mezonos*, from him you should seek your sustenance.

Two readings of this response emerge in the *Rishonim*. Rashi, citing *Eishes chayil hayesah k'oniyot socheir*, reads it as a reassurance: if your husband is a *talmid chacham*, you need not worry — God will provide. *Rabbenu Gershom* takes a sharper tone: you have been given a man who knows the secrets of heaven and earth; is that not enough? Must you also ask for material comfort? You should know where your priorities lie.

The contrast between wife and husband here is pointed. Achsah is presented as a strong, practical figure — she is the *eishes chayil* who looks after her household, presses her father for what the family needs, and refuses to be satisfied with abstractions. Osniel is the scholar who restored 1,700 lost traditions through *pilpulo* and who, as we will shortly see, turned to God for what he truly needed. Both perspectives are given their due.

### Osniel Genealogy

The Gemara pauses to address a genealogical question. The *pasuk* calls Osniel *achi Kalev* — brother of Kalev — and refers to him as *ben Kenaz*. This would imply that Kalev too was *ben Kenaz*. Yet we know that Kalev is consistently called *ben Yefunneh* in the *Torah*. The Gemara resolves this: *mai Yefunneh? She'pannah me'atzat meraglim* — Yefunneh is not a proper name but a description, meaning "the one who turned away from the counsel of the spies." His real patronymic is *ben Chetzron*, as attested by the verse *v'Kalev ben Chetzron holid et Azuvah* in Divrei HaYamim. Rava explains: *chorgei d'Kenaz hava* — Kalev was Kenaz's stepson. Kalev's mother remarried Kenaz, and Osniel was born of that union. Thus Osniel and Kalev were half-brothers — Osniel was *ben Kenaz* in the literal sense, and Kalev was *ben Kenaz* only through his mother's remarriage.

### Osniel's Prayer

The Gemara identifies Osniel ben Kenaz with Yavetz, mentioned elsewhere in *Divrei HaYamim*. *Hu Osniel hu Yavetz* — they are the same person. His real name was Yehudah achi Shimon. He was called *Osniel* because *she'annao El* — God answered him. He was called *Yavetz* because *ya'atz v'ribbetz Torah* — he advised and spread *Torah* throughout Israel.

The proof that God answered him is drawn from the *pasuk*: *vayikra Yavetz l'Elohei Yisrael lemor* — Yavetz called out to the God of Israel. The prayer that follows is read by the Gemara not in its *peshat* sense as a request for land and territory, but as a *talmid chacham*'s prayer for the conditions necessary to sustain his learning.

*Im barech tevarcheni* — bless me, *baTorah*. *V'hirbita et gevuli* — expand my borders, *b'talmidim*, with students. *V'hayetah yadcha immi* — and Your hand should be with me, *she'lo yishtakach talmudi mi'libbi*, that my Torah should not be forgotten from my heart — echoing the central theme of this entire *sugya*. *V'asita mera'ati* — the word *mera'ati* is read as *merei'im*, friends: *she'yizdamnu li rei'im kamoti*, that I should have colleagues like myself. *L'vilti otzvi* — that the *yetzer hara* should not seduce me, *she'lo yasgiveni yetzer hara mi'lishnot*, into straying from my path, into abandoning *Torah* for other pursuits or priorities.

Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi offers an alternative reading. Rather than reading the prayer as entirely about *Torah*, he reads it as a prayer for both spiritual and material sustenance: *piryah verivyah* — that God bless him with many children; *b'vanim uvevanot* — sons and daughters; *bemassa umattan* — success in commerce, to provide for them; and health sufficient to pursue his responsibilities. This reading acknowledges the practical dimension that Achsah had pressed: a *talmid chacham* who understands his obligations to his household prays not only for Torah but for the means to fulfill his domestic responsibilities.

In both readings, the prayer ends the same way: *im atah oseh ken mutav* — if You do this, it is good, and if not, *hareini holech benisisi lishol* — I will go down in anguish to the depths. The passion here is remarkable. This is not a polite petition; it is a declaration that the life he has committed to is everything — and without divine support to sustain it, there is nothing.

*Vayavei Elohim et asher sha'al* — God gave him what he asked.

The Gemara closes this passage with a parallel *pasuk*: *rash ve'ish tichachim nifgashu, me'ir einei sheneihem Hashem* — a poor man and a man of substance encounter one another; God illumines the eyes of both. In the first reading, this refers to a student who comes to his *rebbi* and says: *lamdeni Torah* — teach me Torah. If the *rebbi* teaches him, *me'ir einei sheneihem Hashem* — God illumines both. If he refuses, *ashir varash nifgashu ose chulam Hashem* — God can reverse their fates: *mi she'asa chacham laze oseh oto tipeish, tipeish laze oseh oto chacham* — whoever was made wise will become foolish, and whoever was not will take his place. In the second reading, the *pasuk* applies to a poor person who insists on fulfilling his duty to provide for his household and seeks help from the wealthy — if he is helped, good; if refused, God can reverse their fortunes. In both cases the moral is the same: insist on your proper priorities, press them from God and from people, and do not waver.

## Five Deaths Debate

### Five Deaths Listed

The Gemara now returns to the *halacha* that lies at the center of the *Mishnah*. Rabbi Shimon states: *chamesh chata'ot metot* — there are five types of *chatat* that must be left to die when they arise in the case of an individual: *velad chatat* — the offspring of a *chatat*; *tmurat chatat* — a *temura*, an exchange made on a *chatat*; *chatat shemetu ba'aleha* — whose owners died before it was offered; *chatat shenitkapru ba'aleha* — whose owners achieved atonement through another offering; and *chatat she'avra shnata* — that has passed its first year.

### Three Don't Apply

Three of these five cannot arise in the case of a *tzibur*. A communal *chatat* is never female, so there can be no *velad chatat* of a *tzibur*'s offering. The *tzibur* cannot perform *temura*, as we have established. And the *tzibur* does not die — so *chatat shemetu ba'aleha* has no communal application. These three are therefore exclusively *yachid* scenarios.

### Group Unity Logic

Given that three of the five apply only to the *yachid*, Rabbi Shimon argues that the other two — *chatat shenitkapru ba'aleha* and *chatat she'avra shnata* — must likewise apply only to the *yachid*. The logic is *yilamed satum min hameforash*: the unexplicated cases are learned from the explicated ones. Since the five form a unified group, and three of them can only operate in the context of an individual, the entire group is understood to apply only to the *yachid* and not to the *tzibur*.

### Rav Natan: One Lost

Reish Lakish complicates this further by introducing the dimension of transmission. He suggests that the *mesorah* was actually for four *chataot* to die, not five. The fifth was uncertain — they knew there were four, they knew one could graze, but they did not know which was which. Rather than leave one unassigned, they extended the ruling to all five. The point about five forming a unified group still holds: since those four that had to die could only have been stated in the context of a *yachid* — the communal scenarios simply do not exist for enough of them — the entire *halacha* was said *beyachid*.

Rav Natan goes further still, to the opposite extreme: the *mesorah* was in fact for only one *chatat* to die. The rest were to graze. Four were lost, and since they did not know which of the five was the one, they extended the ruling to all of them.

### Double Forgetting

The Gemara then asks: even granting Rav Natan's position, might they not have at least remembered whether the one that dies was in the category that applies only to a *yachid*, or in the category that applies to both *yachid* and *tzibur*? The answer is: *trei shchiach shchu* — they forgot two things. Not only did they forget which specific *chatat* had to die; they also forgot whether it belonged to the group that applies exclusively to a *yachid* or the group that applies to both. Two layers of forgetting compounded one another.

Nevertheless, a resolution is still available. The *mesorah* that four had to graze — whatever its relationship to the five — was at minimum stated in a context where not all four could exist for a *tzibur*. At most two of the four could apply *betzibur*. Since those four form a unified group, and some of them apply only *beyachid*, the entire group of four must have been stated *beyachid*. And once we establish that the four that graze are a *yachid* matter, it follows that the one that dies is also a *yachid* matter. Therefore the entire *halacha* of *chamesh chataot meitot* applies only to the *yachid* and not to the *tzibur*.

### Transmission Problem

The full force of this *sugya* now comes into view. The *halacha* of *chamesh chataot meitot* is generally cited as a paradigmatic case of *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — a law transmitted directly from Sinai. Yet the Gemara has now shown that even this *halacha* was subject to the problems of transmission and forgetting. Whether the number was five, four, or one; whether it applied to *yachid* alone or also to *tzibur* — these were not questions about application to new cases, but questions about the transmission itself. The Gemara that began with the glory of perfect transmission and the *Eshkolot* who sustained it ends by demonstrating, case after case, that transmission was in fact vulnerable — that forgetting occurred, that debates arose from that forgetting, and that even foundational *halachot l'Moshe miSinai* were among the casualties.

This is, to put it directly, powerful evidence against the Rambam's position that disputes in *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* never occurred. The entire trajectory of this daf — from the mourning for Moshe through Yehoshua's loss, through the 1,700 forgotten hermeneutical traditions, through Osniel's recovery, and finally through the uncertainty at the heart of *chamesh chataot meitot* — is a sustained meditation on the fragility of transmission and the centrality of that fragility to the nature of *Torah shebe'al peh*.
