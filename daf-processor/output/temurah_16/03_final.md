# Temurah 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eshkolot & Decline

### Glory & Eshkolot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: מִשֶּׁמֵּת יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה וְיוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹחָנָן אִישׁ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, בָּטְלוּ הָאֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת. אִישׁ שֶׁהַכֹּל בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in a mishna there (Sota 47a): From the time when Yosef ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida and Yosef ben Yoḥanan of Jerusalem died, the **clusters [*eshkolot*] ceased,** i.e., they were the last of the clusters. The Gemara asks: What is the meaning of *eshkolot*? It means **a man who contains all [*ish shehakol bo*],** i.e., both Torah and mitzvot.

The daf picks up from the bottom of 15b, where the Gemara had quoted *pesukim* relating to the return of the Jewish people and the building of the second *Beit HaMikdash* — how they wept, remembering the glory of the first *Beit HaMikdash* — alongside the concluding *pasuk*, *gadol yihiyeh kevod habayit ha'acharon min harishon*, that the glory of the latter house would ultimately surpass that of the former. From there, the Gemara transitions into quoting a *Mishnah* in Sotah that likewise reflects on the greatness of earlier generations, citing Yosef ben Yoezer Ish Tzeredah and Yosef ben Yochanan Ish Yerushalayim as the last of the *Eshkolot* — those who possessed everything within them.

### Eshkolot Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל אֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת שֶׁעָמְדוּ לָהֶן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִימוֹת מֹשֶׁה עַד שֶׁמֵּת יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר — הָיוּ לְמֵדִין תּוֹרָה כְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — לֹא הָיוּ לְמֵדִין תּוֹרָה כְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: All** the **clusters who stood** at the head of the Jewish people, **from the days of Moses until Yosef ben Yo'ezer died, would study Torah** in the manner **of Moses, our teacher. From that** point **forward they would not study Torah** in the manner **of Moses, our teacher.**

Rashi explains that *Eshkolot* — literally "clusters," like a cluster of grapes — is expounded by the Gemara as *ish shekol bo*, a person who is complete: possessing *Torah*, *Yirat Chet*, and *Gemilut Chasadim*. The Gemara now focuses on the *Torah* dimension, asserting that after these two figures, nobody learned *Torah* through them the way one learned through Moshe Rabbeinu — meaning, apparently, that they transmitted it with perfect fidelity. The Gemara proceeds to challenge this claim.

### Righteous Man & Goat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: כׇּל אֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת שֶׁעָמְדוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִימוֹת מֹשֶׁה עַד שֶׁמֵּת יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה — לֹא הָיָה בָּהֶם שׁוּם דּוֹפִי, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — הָיָה בָּהֶן שׁוּם דּוֹפִי.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in a *baraita*: All the **clusters who stood** at the head of the Jewish people **from the days of Moses until Yosef ben Yo'ezer died had no flaw in them. From this** point **forward** the clusters, i.e., the leadership of the Jewish people, **had flaws in them.**

The Gemara cites the *Baraita*: *lo hayah bahem shum dofi* — from the time of Moshe until Yosef ben Yoezer died, the *Eshkolot* had not even the slightest *dofi*, no defect of any kind. *Mikan va'eilach hayah bahem shum dofi* — from that point onward, there were problems.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָסִיד אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה גּוֹנֵחַ מִלִּבּוֹ, וְשָׁאֲלוּ לָרוֹפְאִים, וְאָמְרוּ: אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁיִּינַק חָלָב רוֹתֵחַ שַׁחֲרִית, וְהֵבִיאוּ עֵז וְקָשְׁרוּ לוֹ בְּכַרְעֵי מִיטָּתוֹ, וְהָיָה יוֹנֵק מִמֶּנָּה חֵלֶב.
>
> **Translation:** But isn't it taught in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving a certain pious man who was groaning,** i.e., suffering, **due to** a pain in **his heart. And they asked the physicians** what to do for him, **and they said: There is no other remedy for him but that he should suckle warm milk** every **morning. And they brought** him **a goat and tied it to the leg of the bed for him, and he would suckle milk from it.**

But what exactly is the *dofi* in question? The Gemara clarifies that the issue here is not primarily the *Torah* transmission dimension; it concerns the *Eshkolot*'s behavior, character, and *shmirat mitzvot*. To illustrate, the Gemara cites a *Baraita*: *ma'aseh bechasid echad shehayah gone'ach milibo* — there was a righteous individual who suffered from chest pains. Doctors were consulted, and they prescribed that each morning he drink freshly milked warm milk — that is, milk drawn directly from the animal while still at body temperature. To facilitate this, he tied a goat to his bedpost and would draw milk from it each morning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָחָר נִכְנְסוּ חֲבֵירָיו לְבַקְּרוֹ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאוּ הָעֵז אָמְרוּ: לִיסְטִים מְזוּיָּין בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וְאָנוּ נִכְנָסִים לְבַקְּרוֹ?! יָשְׁבוּ וּבָדְקוּ, וְלֹא מָצְאוּ בּוֹ עָוֹן אֶלָּא שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ הָעֵז בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** On the following day, his friends entered to visit him. When they saw the goat tied to the leg of the bed they said: There is an **armed bandit in this** man's **house, and we are entering to visit him?** They referred to the goat in this manner because small animals habitually graze on the vegetation of neighbors, stealing their crops. The Sages **sat and examined** this pious man's behavior, **and they could not find** any **transgression** attributable **to him other than** the sin **of** keeping **that goat** in his house **alone.**

The following day his friends came to visit, and when they saw the goat they exclaimed: *listim mezuyan betoch beito ve'anu nichnasim levakro*? — "There is an armed robber inside his house, and we are coming to visit him?" The prohibition against raising small grazing animals — sheep and goats — in the settled areas of the Land of Israel was well known, since such animals devastate the ground, cropping the grass to its roots and causing serious harm to the land. The friends were not moved by the man's medical need, and according to the Tosefta's version of the story, they left.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף הוּא בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָתוֹ אָמַר: יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי בְּעַצְמִי שֶׁאֵין בִּי עָוֹן, אֶלָּא שֶׁל אוֹתָהּ הָעֵז בִּלְבַד, שֶׁעָבַרְתִּי עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירַי, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין מְגַדְּלִין בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** And that man himself also said at the time of his death: I know for a fact with regard to myself that I have no transgression attributable to me but the sin of that goat in my house alone, as I transgressed the statement of my colleagues, the Sages. As the Sages said in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 79b): One may not raise small domesticated animals, i.e., sheep and goats, in inhabited areas of Eretz Yisrael, because they graze on people's crops.

After he died, they examined his entire record and found that the only transgression in his life was this one goat — *velo matzu bo avon ela shel osah ez bilvad*. And he himself acknowledged as much on his deathbed: *she-avarti al divrei chaverai* — I violated the ruling of my colleagues, *she-ein megadlin behemah dakah be-eretz yisrael*. What is striking is the way he frames the transgression: not as an intrinsic sin, but as a violation of a rabbinic policy and the authority of his colleagues. One might question whether a *pikuach nefesh*-adjacent need might have warranted an exception, or whether the issue was more a matter of the precedent and policy implications of keeping an animal in one's home rather than grazing it on open land.

### Chasid Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַיְימָא לַן, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר "מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָסִיד אֶחָד" — אוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא אוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִילְעַאי, וְרַבָּנַן בָּתַר יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה דָּרֵי דָּרֵי הֲווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** And we maintain that **anywhere that it says:** There was **an incident involving a certain pious** man, the man in question is **either Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava or Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai. And** these **Sages** lived **many generations after Yosef ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida.**

The Gemara then notes: *kol heicha de'amar ma'aseh bechasid echad* — whenever a story mentions a *chasid*, it was either Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava or Rabbi Yehuda berabi Ilai. This creates a difficulty, because those Rabbis lived many generations after Yosef ben Yoezer. If after these two *Eshkolot* there could be no one without *dofi*, how can such later figures be held up as exemplars of near-perfect conduct? The Gemara is suggesting that even after Yosef ben Yoezer and Yosef ben Yochanan, individuals existed whose only failing was something as minor as this one goat — *shum dofi* in the meaningful sense, then, hardly applied to them.

### Dofi Smichah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: דּוֹפִי שֶׁל סְמִיכָה קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef said in response: The *baraita* is not referring to a flaw due to some sin; rather, it is teaching about the **flaw of** the early dispute over the *halakha* of **placing hands** on the head of an animal brought as a Festival peace offering, as taught in tractate *Ḥagiga* (16a).

Rav Yosef resolves the tension: *dofi shel smicha ktani* — the *dofi* referred to in the *Baraita* is not about personal observance or *Yirat Chet* at all. In those respects, great figures continued to exist even after that generation. The *dofi* pertains specifically to the realm of *Torah* transmission and *halacha* — and in particular it refers to *smicha*. Here *smicha* does not mean rabbinic ordination, but rather the earliest recorded disputes among the *zugot* concerning whether one may perform *smicha* — the laying of hands — on an animal sacrifice brought on *Yom Tov*. This controversy, which persisted across many generations of the *zugot*, only began after Yosef ben Yoezer and Yosef ben Yochanan. Before them, no debates in *halacha* arose; the transmission was perfect. The *dofi* was, therefore, the emergence of *halachic* debate — not a failure of moral character.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר גּוּפֵיהּ מִיפְלָיג פְּלִיג בִּסְמִיכָה! כִּי אִיפְּלִיג בַּהּ בְּסוֹף שְׁנֵיהּ, דִּבְצַר לִיבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But **Yosef ben Yo'ezer himself disputed** the *halakha* of **placing hands** on the head of an offering. The first Sages to dispute this issue were Yosef ben Yo'ezer and Yosef ben Yoḥanan. The Gemara answers: When they disputed it, that was **at the end of the years** of Yosef ben Yo'ezer's life, **when** the understanding of **his heart was limited,** due to old age. Therefore, the dispute is considered as though it occurred after his lifetime.

The Gemara anticipates an objection: Yosef ben Yoezer himself was a party to the original *smicha* controversy. The response: *ki plig bah besof shnei* — that controversy arose only at the very end of his life, *debatzer liba*, when the sharpness of transmission had already begun to erode. Until that point, the transmission had been flawless.

## Transmission Debate

### Forgetting View

This brings the Gemara to a broader question that was lurking throughout: why are there so many debates recorded in the Talmud? This critique was famously leveled by the Karaites, who argued that the sheer volume of disagreement in the *Talmud* demonstrates that *Torah shebe'al peh* rests on a faulty transmission — and if the *masorah* is unreliable, it cannot be trusted at all. One response available to the Rabbis is to note that the vast majority of basic *halachic* parameters are universally agreed upon, and that the debates concern only a subset of questions.

The approach implied by a number of *Gemarot*, including the present one, is more direct: debates arose because things were forgotten. This is reinforced by the well-known statement about Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel — *mishe-rabu talmidei Beit Shammai uBeit Hillel shelo shimshu kol tzarchan* — that when students did not attend sufficiently to their teachers and did not absorb the tradition fully, disputes proliferated. The entire narrative arc of this Gemara — which is building toward the *chamesh chataot hameitot* of our *Mishnah* — presses this theme: had things been transmitted perfectly, there would have been no debates.

### Rambam's View

The Rambam emphatically rejects this position. Both philosophically and as a response to the Karaite challenge, he insists that the transmission was always perfect: nothing that was *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* was ever forgotten or disputed. In his view, all *halachic* debates arise not from forgotten traditions but from the application of received principles to new cases — the clarification of parameters that were never explicitly transmitted in the first place. This is a powerful and intellectually coherent position. Even granting perfect transmission of every received *halacha*, enormous tracts of *halachic* reality remain unaddressed, and it is in navigating that territory that debates inevitably emerge.

The difficulty, of course, is that the present Gemara appears to cut directly against the Rambam, framing debates as products of forgetting. The Rambam's approach is also notably in tension with *Rashi*'s reading of the earlier passage, which interprets the debates that arose after Moshe's death as attempts to recall forgotten *halachot*.

### Gadol Yihiyeh

One may ask whether the surface nostalgia of this entire *sugya* — its lament for the great transmission that eroded — contains a hidden subtext. The *pasuk* that opened the *sugya*, *gadol yihiyeh kevod habayit ha'acharon min harishon*, suggests that the "latter house" ultimately surpasses the first. Elsewhere the Gemara weighs in clearly on the relative merits of received transmission versus creative intellectual engagement: Rabbi Akiva, who derived and developed, is preferred to Rabbi Eliezer, who essentially received and transmitted. One might wonder whether beneath the apparent mourning over forgotten *halachot* lies an implicit acknowledgment that *Torah shebe'al peh* — in its fullest form — is not merely retrieval but creation, and that perhaps the intellectual vitality of later generations represents its own kind of *gadol yihiyeh*. This reading may press beyond what the text explicitly supports, but the tension is present.

## Forgetting & Retrieval

### 3000 Halachot Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁלֹשֶׁת אֲלָפִים הֲלָכוֹת נִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ בִּימֵי אֶבְלוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ: שְׁאַל! אָמַר לָהֶם: "לֹא בַשָּׁמַיִם הִיא".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself. Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Three thousand *halakhot* were forgotten during the days of mourning for Moses.** The Jewish people **said to Joshua: Ask** for guidance from Heaven so that you can reacquire the forgotten *halakhot*. Joshua **said to them: "It is not in heaven"** (Deuteronomy 30:12). Once the Torah was given on Sinai, the Sages of each generation must determine the *halakha*. No new *halakhot* may be added or subtracted by heavenly instruction or through prophecy.

The Gemara now turns to the theme of forgetting and retrieval in full. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *shloshet alafim halachot nishtakechu bimei evlo shel Moshe* — 3,000 *halachot* were forgotten during the mourning period after Moshe's death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ לִשְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁאַל! אָמַר לָהֶם: "אֵלֶּה הַמִּצְוֹת" — שֶׁאֵין הַנָּבִיא רַשַּׁאי לְחַדֵּשׁ דָּבָר מֵעַתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Many years later the Jewish people again **said to Samuel: Ask** for halakhic guidance from Heaven. **He said to them:** This is not possible, as the Torah states: **"These are the commandments** that the Lord commanded Moses to tell the children of Israel at Mount Sinai" (Leviticus 27:34). The word "these" indicates **that from now** on **a prophet is not permitted to introduce any** new **element** related to the Torah and its mitzvot through prophecy.

The people turned to Yehoshua and said: *she'al* — go and ask God, retrieve what we have lost. Yehoshua replied: *lo bashamayim hi* — it is not in heaven. After Moshe, the process of divine supplementation of *Torah* is closed.

They then turned to Shmuel and said: *she'al*. He replied: *eileh hamitzvot* — these are the commandments God gave to Moshe; they are complete, and no prophet may innovate thereafter. The principle *she'ein navi rashai l'chadesh davar mei'ata* — that no prophet after Moshe may add to *halacha* — applies here as well. Even if one wished to frame the request not as innovation but as recovery, the answer *lo bashamayim hi* forecloses it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא: אַף חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ נִשְׁתַּכְּחָה בִּימֵי אֶבְלוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the topic of the chapter, **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa says: Also,** the *halakha* of **a sin offering whose owner has died was** one of those **forgotten during the days of mourning for Moses.**

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha then notes: *af chatat shemetu ba'aleha nishtakecha bimei evlo shel Moshe* — the *halacha* regarding a *chatat* whose owners died was among the things forgotten during that mourning period. The alternate textual reading, cited by the *Shita Mekubetzet*, replaces *shemetu* with *shenitkapru ba'aleha*, tying the forgotten *halacha* directly to the dispute in our *Mishnah* — whether the *chatat* of a *tzibur* that brought another *korban* must die or may graze.

One might wonder how *halachot* could be forgotten during a mourning period of only forty days, particularly *halachot* about relatively rare scenarios such as a *chatat shemetu ba'aleha*. The answer may lie in how rarely such cases arose in practice — a *halacha* that was never applied during the forty years in the wilderness might simply never have been internalized in a way that survived the upheaval of Moshe's death. Beyond that, the people were profoundly dependent on Moshe, much as they had been during the episode of the golden calf when he was absent only briefly. The trauma of his death, and the grief of mourning, could have been enough to disrupt even memories that were held.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לְפִנְחָס: שְׁאַל! אָמַר לָהֶם: "לֹא בַשָּׁמַיִם הִיא". אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְאֶלְעָזָר: שְׁאַל! אָמַר לָהֶם: "אֵלֶּה הַמִּצְוֹת", שֶׁאֵין נָבִיא רַשַּׁאי לְחַדֵּשׁ דָּבָר מֵעַתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** At the time of Moses' death, the people **said to Pinehas: Ask** for halakhic guidance from Heaven so that you can relearn the forgotten *halakhot*. Pinehas **said to them: "It is not in heaven"** (Deuteronomy 30:12). The people **said to Elazar: Ask** for halakhic guidance from God. **He said to them** that the verse states: **"These are the commandments,"** to teach **that a prophet is not permitted to introduce any** new **element from now** on.

They then turned to Pinchas and said: *she'al*. He responded: *lo bashamayim hi*. They turned to Elazar and said: *she'al*. He responded: *eileh hamitzvot she'ein navi rashai l'chadesh davar mei'atah*. It is notable that the Gemara groups these four figures into two pairs, each pair giving a different formulation of the refusal. Yehoshua and Shmuel — political leaders who also had prophetic capacity — gave the *lo bashamayim hi* answer. Elazar and Pinchas — whose primary role was as *Kohen Gadol* and communicators of divine will through the *Urim VeTumim* — gave the second answer. The people may have hoped that those who wore the *Urim VeTumim*, whose very function was to ask God's guidance in critical moments, might succeed where the political leadership had not. Yet the answer is the same: from this point forward, *halacha* cannot be retrieved from Heaven.

### Yehoshua's Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּפְטַר מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לְגַן עֵדֶן, אָמַר לוֹ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ: שְׁאַל מִמֶּנִּי כָּל סְפֵיקוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, כְּלוּם הִנַּחְתִּיךָ שָׁעָה אַחַת וְהָלַכְתִּי לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר? לֹא כָּךְ כָּתַבְתָּ בִּי "וּמְשָׁרְתוֹ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן נַעַר לֹא יָמִישׁ מִתּוֹךְ הָאֹהֶל"? מִיָּד תָּשַׁשׁ כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְנִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת הֲלָכוֹת, וְנוֹלְדוּ לוֹ שְׁבַע מֵאוֹת סְפֵיקוֹת, וְעָמְדוּ כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהׇרְגוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda says that Rav says: Just before the time when **Moses, our teacher, left** this world and went **to the Garden of Eden, he said to Joshua: Ask from me all** the cases of **uncertainty** in matters of *halakha* **that you have,** so that I can clarify them for you. Joshua **said to him: My teacher, did I** ever **leave you** for even **one moment and go to another place? Didn't you write this about me** in the Torah: **"But his minister, Joshua, son of Nun, a young man, did not depart out of the tent"** (Exodus 33:11)? If I would have had any case of uncertainty I would have asked you earlier. **Immediately** after he said this, **Joshua's strength weakened, and three hundred *halakhot* were forgotten by him, and seven hundred** cases of **uncertainty emerged before him, and the entire Jewish people arose to kill him,** as he was unable to teach them the forgotten *halakhot*.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: at the moment Moshe was about to depart to *Gan Eden*, he said to Yehoshua — *she'al mimeni kol sefeikot sheyesh lach* — ask me whatever questions or doubts you have. Yehoshua responded: *Rebbi, kelum hinachticha sha'ah achat vehalachti lemakom acher* — "My teacher, have I ever left your side even for a moment? Did you not yourself write of me: *umesharto Yehoshua bin Nun na'ar lo yamish mitoch ha'ohel*? I never left the tent of Torah. I have nothing to ask."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: לוֹמַר לָךְ אִי אֶפְשָׁר, לֵךְ וְטוֹרְדֵן בְּמִלְחָמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיְהִי אַחֲרֵי מוֹת מֹשֶׁה עֶבֶד ה׳ וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Joshua: It is **impossible to tell you** these *halakhot*, as the Torah is not in Heaven. But to save yourself from the Jewish people who want to kill you, **go and exhaust them in war,** so that they will leave you alone. **As it is stated: "Now it came to pass after the death of Moses, the servant of the Lord, that the Lord spoke** to Joshua, son of Nun, Moses' minister, saying: Moses My servant is dead, now therefore arise, go over this Jordan" (Joshua 1:1–2). This shows that immediately after the death of Moses, God commanded Joshua to lead the nation into battle.

*Miyad tashash kocho shel Yehoshua* — immediately Yehoshua's intellectual strength was sapped. *Venishtakechu mimenu shalosh me'ot halachot* — he forgot 300 *halachot*, *venoldu lo sheva me'ot sefeikot* — and 700 doubts arose for him.

Rashi explains the punishment in interpersonal terms: *she'hitzta'er Moshe Yehoshua be'atzmo shehayah gadol kamohu* — Yehoshua pained Moshe by essentially declaring himself Moshe's equal, "I have everything I need from you." To someone on his deathbed, the appropriate response is not confidence but need, not self-sufficiency but expression of dependence. Yet one may also read a deeper moral in Yehoshua's failure: the Gemara elsewhere warns that *ba'al ga'avah*, arrogance, causes one's *Torah* to be forgotten. Even if Yehoshua's statement was factually accurate at that moment, a disposition of humility demands that one never consider oneself complete — that one seize every opportunity to learn. The very last moment with one's teacher is not the moment for declarations of self-sufficiency.

The loss was especially irreversible because, as God later told Yehoshua: *lomar lach ee efshar* — I cannot tell you what was forgotten. *Lo bashamayim hi* now applied in full. The moment of Moshe's final teaching had passed, and with it the last window for this kind of recovery. Yehoshua was told instead: *lech u'tordan b'milchama* — direct their energies toward war, which was in any event the next stage of the national mission. The people, furious that their *halachot* had been lost and demanding from Yehoshua what he could not supply, needed to be redirected — and the conquest of the land was at hand.

It should be noted that the 3,000 *halachot* forgotten during the mourning for Moshe and the 300 forgotten because of Yehoshua's response are not contradictory traditions; they describe different moments and different losses.

### 1700 Hermeneutics Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתִין תָּנָא: אֶלֶף וּשְׁבַע מֵאוֹת קַלִּין וַחֲמוּרִין, וּגְזֵירוֹת שָׁווֹת, וְדִקְדּוּקֵי סוֹפְרִים נִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ בִּימֵי אֶבְלוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in a *baraita*: **One thousand and seven hundred *a fortiori* inferences, and verbal analogies, and minutiae of the scribes were forgotten during the days of mourning for Moses.**

The *Baraita* then adds a further dimension: *elef u'shva me'ot kalin v'chamurin, u'gezeirot shavot, v'dikdukei sofrim nishtakechu bimei evlo shel Moshe* — 1,700 instances of *kal v'chomer*, *gezeirot shavot*, and *dikdukei sofrim* were forgotten during the mourning for Moshe.

This is a striking formulation. *Kal v'chomer* and *gezeira shava* are the foundational hermeneutical tools of *Torah shebe'al peh* — the very instruments of rabbinic legal reasoning. The Gemara here frames even these as part of the *masorah*, as something transmitted and then forgotten, rather than as exercises of independent creative intellect. Indeed, the overriding thrust of this *sugya* is to present the entire enterprise of *Torah shebe'al peh* as essentially an act of retrieval.

What are *dikdukei sofrim*? Rashi (cited in the context of *Rabbeinu Gershom*) explains that *dikdukei sofrim* refers to the practice of organizing *halachot* into counted groups — *sefirot sefirot* — as, for example, the five *chataot hameitot*, or the thirteen things said about such-and-such topic. The role of the *sofrim* was apparently to take the existing data and organize it, to assign it a number and pull it together. What was forgotten was the content that would have permitted this organizing to be done — so the categories and counts could not be reconstructed. Rashi adds the phrase *she'adayin lo tzirfum* — things that had not yet been pulled together — suggesting that the *dikdukei sofrim* represented the one dimension of the rabbinic enterprise that was not pure transmission but involved an act of organization. Even that was lost.

The full weight of the Gemara's framing here is that the rabbinic tools most associated with intellectual creativity — *kal v'chomer* most of all, since it famously requires no *mesorah* — are being cast as part of the received tradition. The implication is that even rabbinic derivation, on this reading, is ultimately a form of recovery rather than innovation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַף עַל פִּי כֵן הֶחְזִירָן עׇתְנִיאֵל בֶּן קְנַז מִתּוֹךְ פִּלְפּוּלוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּלְכְּדָהּ עׇתְנִיאֵל בֶּן קְנַז אֲחִי כָלֵב וַיִּתֶּן לוֹ אֶת עַכְסָה בִתּוֹ לְאִשָּׁה". וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ "עַכְסָה"? שֶׁכׇּל הָרוֹאֶה אוֹתָהּ כּוֹעֵס עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu says: **Even so, Othniel, son of Kenaz, restored them through his sharp** mind **[*pilpulo*], as it is stated:** "And Caleb said: To he who smites Kiriath Sefer, and takes it, to him will I give Achsah my daughter as a wife. And Othniel, son of Kenaz, the brother of Caleb, took it; and he gave him Achsah his daughter as a wife" (Joshua 15:16–17). The name "Kiriath Sefer," which literally means the village of the book, is homiletically interpreted as a reference to those parts of the Torah that were forgotten, while the phrase "took it" is referring to Othniel's acumen and learning. The *baraita* adds: And why is she called Achsah? The reason is that **anyone who sees her became angry [*ko'es*] about his** own **wife,** who was not as beautiful as Achsah.

Rabbi Abahu then announces the resolution: *hechiziran Osniel ben Kenaz mitokh pilpulo* — Osniel ben Kenaz recovered all of them through his sharpness. The reference point is the verse in which Kalev offered his daughter in marriage to whoever could conquer *Kiryat Sefer*, and Osniel ben Kenaz, his brother, won her. The name *Kiryat Sefer* — a city of the *sofrim*, of counting — is read as a reference to the *dikdukei sofrim* that had been lost, and Osniel's conquest of it through *milchama* is read as his recovery of the lost tradition through the *milchamta shel Torah*, the battle of intellectual engagement.

### Achsah & Provision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיְהִי בְּבוֹאָה וַתְּסִיתֵהוּ לִשְׁאֹל מֵאֵת אָבִיהָ שָׂדֶה וַתִּצְנַח מֵעַל הַחֲמוֹר, מַאי "וַתִּצְנַח"? אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אָמְרָה לוֹ, מָה חֲמוֹר זֶה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מַאֲכָל בַּאֲבוּסוֹ — מִיָּד צוֹעֵק, כָּךְ אִשָּׁה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁאֵין לָהּ תְּבוּאָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתָהּ — מִיָּד צוֹעֶקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident involving Achsah. The verse states: "And it came to pass, when she came to him, that she persuaded him to ask of her father a field; and she alighted from off her donkey; and Caleb said to her: What do you want?" (Joshua 15:18). The Gemara asks: What is the meaning of: "And she alighted [*vatitznaḥ*]," which can also be understood as crying out? **Rava says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** Achsah **said to** Caleb: **Just as** in the case of **this donkey, when it has no food in its trough it immediately cries out, so too** in the case of **a woman, when she has no produce in her house she immediately cries out.**

The Gemara now digresses on the figure of Achsah, Kalev's daughter given to Osniel in marriage. Why was she called *Achsah*? *Shekol haro'ah otah ko'eis al ishto* — because anyone who saw her became angry at his own wife, so striking was her beauty.

The narrative then turns to the *pasuk*: *vayehi b'vo'ah vatasteihu lish'ol mei'et aviha sadeh* — when she came to Osniel, she enticed him to ask her father Kalev for a field. *Vatitznach mei'al hachamor* — she lowered herself, or in the Gemara's reading, she brayed, from the *chamor*. The Gemara reads *vatitznach* as a kind of complaint. Rava said in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak: she said to Osniel — *mah chamor zeh keivan she'ein lo ma'achal b'avuso miyad tzo'ek* — just as a donkey, when it has no food in its trough, immediately cries out, *kach ishah she'ein lah tevu'ah b'tokh beitah miyad tzo'eket* — so too a wife who has no grain in her house immediately cries out. In short: we are about to start a life together, and someone must attend to practical provision.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַתֹּאמֶר תְּנָה לִּי בְרָכָה כִּי אֶרֶץ הַנֶּגֶב נְתַתָּנִי, בַּיִת שֶׁמְּנוּגָּב מִכׇּל טוֹבָה, "וְנָתַתָּה לִּי גּוּלּוֹת מָיִם" — אָדָם שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה בִּלְבַד. "וַיִּתֶּן לָהּ כָּלֵב אֵת גּוּלּוֹת עִלִּיּוֹת וְאֵת גּוּלּוֹת תַּחְתִּיּוֹת" — אָמַר לָהּ: מִי שֶׁדָּר עֶלְיוֹנִים וְתַחְתּוֹנִים יְבַקֵּשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ מְזוֹנוֹת?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites yet another verse involving Achsah: "And she said: Give me a blessing; for that you have set me in the land of the South [*negev*], and you have given me springs of water. And he gave her the upper springs and the lower springs" (Joshua 15:19). She said to her father: You have given me **a home dried [*menugav*] of all goodness. "And you have given me springs of water";** this is referring to **a man who has nothing other than Torah,** which is metaphorically called water. But as he is unable to provide me with food, how can I live? **"And gave her the upper springs and the lower springs."** Caleb **said to her:** Does someone learned in Torah, **who dwells in the upper** worlds **and the lower** worlds, **require** that **sustenance be requested for him?**

She then goes to her father and says: *tnah li bracha ki eretz hanegev netatani* — give me a blessing, for you have given me a dry land. The Gemara reads this allegorically: *bayit shemenugav mikol tova* — a house emptied of all good. *Adam she'ein bo ela Torah bilvad* — a man who has nothing but *Torah* alone. What, she says to her father, have you given me? *Gulos mayim* — springs of water? She is saying: a *talmid chacham* is all well and good, but who puts food on the table?

Kalev's response — *vayiten lah Kalev et gulos eliyot v'et gulos tachtiyot*, he gave her the upper and lower springs — is also read allegorically. The Gemara reads his reply as: *mi shekol razei elyonim vetachtiyot*, the one who knows all the secrets of the upper and lower worlds — that is, this great *talmid chacham*, your husband who was able to restore what was lost from the time of Moshe — *yevakesh mimenu mezonos*, from him you should seek your sustenance.

Two readings of this response emerge in the *Rishonim*. Rashi, citing *Eishes chayil hayesah k'oniyot socheir*, reads it as a reassurance: if your husband is a *talmid chacham*, you need not worry — God will provide. *Rabbenu Gershom* takes a sharper tone: you have been given a man who knows the secrets of heaven and earth; is that not enough? Must you also ask for material comfort? You should know where your priorities lie.

The contrast between wife and husband here is pointed. Achsah is presented as a strong, practical figure — she is the *eishes chayil* who looks after her household, presses her father for what the family needs, and refuses to be satisfied with abstractions. Osniel is the scholar who restored 1,700 lost traditions through *pilpulo* and who, as we will shortly see, turned to God for what he truly needed. Both perspectives are given their due.

### Osniel Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָלֵב בֶּן קְנַז הוּא? וַהֲלֹא "כָּלֵב בֶּן יְפוּנֶּה" הוּא! מַאי "יְפוּנֶּה"? שֶׁפָּנָה מֵעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And **Caleb,** was **he the son of Kenaz? Wasn't he Caleb, son of Jephunneh** (Joshua 15:13)? The Gemara explains that Jephunneh was not the name of his father, but a description of Caleb. **What** does the word **Jephunneh** mean? It means **that he turned [*sheppana*] from the advice of the spies** and did not join with them in their negative report about Eretz Yisrael.

The Gemara pauses to address a genealogical question. The *pasuk* calls Osniel *achi Kalev* — brother of Kalev — and refers to him as *ben Kenaz*. This would imply that Kalev too was *ben Kenaz*. Yet we know that Kalev is consistently called *ben Yefunneh* in the *Torah*. The Gemara resolves this: *mai Yefunneh? She'pannah me'atzat meraglim* — Yefunneh is not a proper name but a description, meaning "the one who turned away from the counsel of the spies." His real patronymic is *ben Chetzron*, as attested by the verse *v'Kalev ben Chetzron holid et Azuvah* in Divrei HaYamim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי בֶּן קְנַז הוּא? בֶּן חֶצְרוֹן הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: "וְכָלֵב בֶּן חֶצְרוֹן הוֹלִיד אֶת עֲזוּבָה". אָמַר רָבָא: חוֹרְגֵיהּ דִּקְנַז הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: But still, was he the son of Kenaz? **He was the son of Hezron, as it is written: "And Caleb, son of Hezron, begot children of Azubah** his wife, and of Jerioth, and these were her sons: Jesher, and Shobab, and Ardon" (I Chronicles 2:18). **Rava said:** Caleb was actually the son of Hezron, but after his father passed away his mother remarried Kenaz, and consequently **he was the stepson of Kenaz.** Othniel, son of Kenaz, was therefore his maternal half brother.

Rava explains: *chorgei d'Kenaz hava* — Kalev was Kenaz's stepson. Kalev's mother remarried Kenaz, and Osniel was born of that union. Thus Osniel and Kalev were half-brothers — Osniel was *ben Kenaz* in the literal sense, and Kalev was *ben Kenaz* only through his mother's remarriage.

### Osniel's Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא: הוּא עׇתְנִיאֵל, הוּא יַעְבֵּץ, וּמָה שְׁמוֹ? יְהוּדָה אָחִי שִׁמְעוֹן שְׁמוֹ. "עׇתְנִיאֵל" — שֶׁעֲנָאוֹ אֵל, "יַעְבֵּץ" — שֶׁיָּעַץ וְרִיבֵּץ תּוֹרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** A **tanna*** taught in a *baraita*: The same person **is** known as **Othniel** and **he is** also known as **Jabez. And what is his** actual **name? Judah, brother of Simeon, is his name.** He was known as **Othniel, as God answered [*ana'o El*]** his prayer. He was also known as **Jabez [*yabetz*] because he advised and spread [*ya'atz veribetz*] Torah among the Jewish people.**

The Gemara identifies Osniel ben Kenaz with Yavetz, mentioned elsewhere in *Divrei HaYamim*. *Hu Osniel hu Yavetz* — they are the same person. His real name was Yehudah achi Shimon. He was called *Osniel* because *she'annao El* — God answered him. He was called *Yavetz* because *ya'atz v'ribbetz Torah* — he advised and spread *Torah* throughout Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן שֶׁעֲנָאוֹ אֵל? דִּכְתִיב: "וַיִּקְרָא יַעְבֵּץ לֵאלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵאמֹר אִם בָּרֵךְ תְּבָרְכֵנִי וְהִרְבִּיתָ אֶת גְּבוּלִי וְהָיְתָה יָדְךָ עִמָּדִי וַעֲשֵׂה מֵרָעָתִי לְבִלְתִּי עׇצְבִּי וַיָּבֵא אֱלֹהִים אֵת אֲשֶׁר שָׁאָל".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And **from where do we** derive **that God answered him? As it is written: "And Jabez called on the God of Israel, saying: If You will bless me indeed, and enlarge my border, and that Your hand may be with me, and that You will work deliverance from evil, that it may not pain me! And God granted him that which he requested"** (I Chronicles 4:10).

The proof that God answered him is drawn from the *pasuk*: *vayikra Yavetz l'Elohei Yisrael lemor* — Yavetz called out to the God of Israel. The prayer that follows is read by the Gemara not in its *peshat* sense as a request for land and territory, but as a *talmid chacham*'s prayer for the conditions necessary to sustain his learning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם בֵּרַךְ תְּבָרְכֵנִי — בַּתּוֹרָה, "וְהִרְבִּיתָ אֶת גְּבוּלִי" — בְּתַלְמִידִים, "וְהָיְתָה יָדְךָ עִמָּדִי" — שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁתַּכֵּחַ תַּלְמוּדִי מִלִּבִּי, "וַעֲשֵׂה מֵרָעָתִי" — שֶׁיִּזְדַּמְּנוּ לִי רֵיעִים כְּמוֹתִי, "לְבִלְתִּי עׇצְבִּי" — שֶׁלֹּא יְשַׂגְּבֵנִי יֵצֶר הָרָע מִלִּשְׁנוֹת. אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — הֲרֵינִי הוֹלֵךְ בִּנְסִיסִי לַשְּׁאוֹל. מִיָּד, "וַיָּבֵא אֱלֹהִים אֵת אֲשֶׁר שָׁאָל".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets this verse. The phrase: "If You will bless me indeed," means that he prayed for a blessing **with regard to Torah. "And enlarge my border,"** means that he prayed for a blessing **with regard to students. "And that Your hand be with me," that my studies not be forgotten from my heart. "And that You will work deliverance from evil [*mera'ati*]," that I will find friends [*re'im*] like me. "That it may not pain me," that the evil inclination should not grow stronger** and prevent **me from studying** Torah. Othniel further prayed: **If You do so, good; and if not, I will go depressed [*linsisi*] to** my grave and **the netherworld. Immediately,** God answered him, as the verse states: **"And God granted him that which he requested."**

*Im barech tevarcheni* — bless me, *baTorah*. *V'hirbita et gevuli* — expand my borders, *b'talmidim*, with students. *V'hayetah yadcha immi* — and Your hand should be with me, *she'lo yishtakach talmudi mi'libbi*, that my Torah should not be forgotten from my heart — echoing the central theme of this entire *sugya*. *V'asita mera'ati* — the word *mera'ati* is read as *merei'im*, friends: *she'yizdamnu li rei'im kamoti*, that I should have colleagues like myself. *L'vilti otzvi* — that the *yetzer hara* should not seduce me, *she'lo yasgiveni yetzer hara mi'lishnot*, into straying from my path, into abandoning *Torah* for other pursuits or priorities.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּיוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: "רָשׁ וְאִישׁ תְּכָכִים נִפְגָּשׁוּ מֵאִיר עֵינֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם ה׳". בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַתַּלְמִיד הוֹלֵךְ אֵצֶל רַבּוֹ וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ: "לַמְּדֵנִי תּוֹרָה", אִם מְלַמְּדוֹ — "מֵאִיר עֵינֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם ה׳", וְאִם לָאו — "עָשִׁיר וָרָשׁ נִפְגָּשׁוּ עוֹשֵׂה כֻלָּם ה׳". מִי שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ חָכָם לָזֶה — עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ טִיפֵּשׁ, טִיפֵּשׁ לָזֶה — עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ חָכָם. זוֹ מִשְׁנַת רַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, you say likewise with regard to the following verse: "The poor man and the oppressor [*tekhakhim*] meet together; the Lord gives light to the eyes of both of them" (Proverbs 29:13). When the student, who is poor in his knowledge, goes to his teacher, i.e., one who knows enough to teach but requires further enlightenment himself, as he is a man between [*tokh*] the levels of a Sage and a commoner, and says to him: Teach me Torah, if the teacher agrees to teach him, then **the Lord gives light to the eyes of both of them,** as they both become greater as a result. But if the teacher will not teach the student, then **"the rich and the poor meet together; the Lord is the maker of them all"** (Proverbs 22:2). This verse teaches that He Who made this one wise now makes him foolish, and He Who made that one foolish now makes him wise. This is the exposition of Rabbi Natan.

Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi offers an alternative reading. Rather than reading the prayer as entirely about *Torah*, he reads it as a prayer for both spiritual and material sustenance:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא אוֹמֵר: "אִם בָּרֵךְ תְּבָרְכֵנִי" — בִּפְרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, "וְהִרְבִּיתָ אֶת גְּבוּלִי" — בְּבָנִים וּבְבָנוֹת, "וְהָיְתָה יָדְךָ עִמָּדִי" — בְּמַשָּׂא וּבְמַתָּן, "וְעָשִׂיתָ מֵרָעָתִי" — שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא בִּי מֵיחוֹשׁ רֹאשׁ וּמֵיחוֹשׁ אׇזְנַיִם וּמֵיחוֹשׁ עֵינַיִם, "לְבִלְתִּי עׇצְבִּי" — שֶׁלֹּא יְשַׂגְּבֵנִי יֵצֶר הָרָע מִלִּשְׁנוֹת. אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — הֲרֵינִי הוֹלֵךְ בִּנְסִיסִי לַשְּׁאוֹל, "וַיָּבֵא לוֹ אֱלֹהִים אֵת אֲשֶׁר שָׁאָל".
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says an alternative interpretation of the prayer of Jabez: "If You will bless me indeed" means **with procreation. "And enlarge my border"** refers to blessing **with sons and with daughters. "And that Your hand may be with me,"** indicates **in business. "And that You will work deliverance from evil,"** so **that I will not have a headache or an earache or an eye ache. "That it may not pain me," that the evil inclination will not grow strong against me** and prevent **me from studying** Torah. Jabez then said to God: **If you do so, good; and if not, I will go depressed to** my grave and **the netherworld. Immediately,** God answered him: **And God granted him that which he requested.**

*piryah verivyah* — that God bless him with many children; *b'vanim uvevanot* — sons and daughters; *bemassa umattan* — success in commerce, to provide for them; and health sufficient to pursue his responsibilities. This reading acknowledges the practical dimension that Achsah had pressed: a *talmid chacham* who understands his obligations to his household prays not only for Torah but for the means to fulfill his domestic responsibilities.

In both readings, the prayer ends the same way: *im atah oseh ken mutav* — if You do this, it is good, and if not, *hareini holech benisisi lishol* — I will go down in anguish to the depths. The passion here is remarkable. This is not a polite petition; it is a declaration that the life he has committed to is everything — and without divine support to sustain it, there is nothing.

*Vayavei Elohim et asher sha'al* — God gave him what he asked.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּיוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: "רָשׁ וְאִישׁ תְּכָכִים נִפְגָּשׁוּ מֵאִיר עֵינֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם ה׳", בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָנִי הוֹלֵךְ אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְאָמַר: פַּרְנְסֵנִי, אִם מְפַרְנְסוֹ — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — "עָשִׁיר וָרָשׁ נִפְגָּשׁוּ עוֹשֵׂה כֻלָּם ה׳", מִי שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ עָשִׁיר לָזֶה — עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ עָנִי, עָנִי לָזֶה — עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ עָשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, you say an interpretation with regard to the verse: "The poor man and the oppressor meet together; the Lord gives light to the eyes of both of them" (Proverbs 29:13). When a poor person goes to a homeowner and says: Provide for me, if he provides for him, that is good. But if not, then it is stated: "The rich and the poor meet together; the Lord is the maker of them all" (Proverbs 22:2). This verse indicates that He Who made this one wealthy now makes him poor, and He Who made that one poor now makes him wealthy.

The Gemara closes this passage with a parallel *pasuk*: *rash ve'ish tichachim nifgashu, me'ir einei sheneihem Hashem* — a poor man and a man of substance encounter one another; God illumines the eyes of both. In the first reading, this refers to a student who comes to his *rebbi* and says: *lamdeni Torah* — teach me Torah. If the *rebbi* teaches him, *me'ir einei sheneihem Hashem* — God illumines both. If he refuses, *ashir varash nifgashu ose chulam Hashem* — God can reverse their fates: *mi she'asa chacham laze oseh oto tipeish, tipeish laze oseh oto chacham* — whoever was made wise will become foolish, and whoever was not will take his place. In the second reading, the *pasuk* applies to a poor person who insists on fulfilling his duty to provide for his household and seeks help from the wealthy — if he is helped, good; if refused, God can reverse their fortunes. In both cases the moral is the same: insist on your proper priorities, press them from God and from people, and do not waver.

## Five Deaths Debate

### Five Deaths Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה מָצִינוּ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that **Rabbi Shimon says:** Just as we found with regard to the offspring of a sin offering, and the substitute for a sin offering, and a sin offering whose owner died, that these matters apply to an individual sin offering but not to a communal sin offering, so too, with regard to a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another sin offering, and a sin offering whose year has passed, these matters are stated with regard to an individual sin offering but not with regard to a communal sin offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חָמֵשׁ חַטָּאוֹת מֵתוֹת, וְלַד חַטָּאת, וּתְמוּרַת חַטָּאת, וְחַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ, וְחַטָּאת שֶׁכִּפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ, וְחַטָּאת שֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: Five** types of **sin offerings** are left to **die,** and one may not sacrifice them, and they are: **An offspring** born **to** a **sin offering,** i.e., if a female animal that was consecrated as a sin offering gave birth, its offspring is sacred but cannot be brought as an offering itself; **and the substitution of a sin offering,** if one substituted another animal for a sin offering, the same sanctity applies to it, but it cannot be sacrificed; **and a sin offering whose owner has died; and a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement** by sacrificing another offering; **and a sin offering whose** first **year has passed,** as a sin offering must be within its first year.

The Gemara now returns to the *halacha* that lies at the center of the *Mishnah*. Rabbi Shimon states: *chamesh chata'ot metot* — there are five types of *chatat* that must be left to die when they arise in the case of an individual: *velad chatat* — the offspring of a *chatat*; *tmurat chatat* — a *temura*, an exchange made on a *chatat*; *chatat shemetu ba'aleha* — whose owners died before it was offered; *chatat shenitkapru ba'aleha* — whose owners achieved atonement through another offering; and *chatat she'avra shnata* — that has passed its first year.

### Three Don't Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר וְלַד חַטָּאת בְּצִיבּוּר — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין חַטָּאת נְקֵבָה בְּצִיבּוּר, וְאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר תְּמוּרַת חַטָּאת בְּצִיבּוּר — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין צִיבּוּר עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר חַטָּאת שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ בְּצִיבּוּר — לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַצִּיבּוּר מֵתִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon continues: **You cannot say** that there could be **an offspring of a sin offering in the case of a community, because there** are **no female sin offerings** separated **by the community. And** likewise **you cannot say** that there could be **a substitution for a sin offering in the case of a community, because a community cannot render** a non-sacred animal that is exchanged for its consecrated one as **a substitute. And** furthermore, **you cannot say** that there could be **a sin offering whose owners have died in the case of a community, because a community cannot die.**

Three of these five cannot arise in the case of a *tzibur*. A communal *chatat* is never female, so there can be no *velad chatat* of a *tzibur*'s offering. The *tzibur* cannot perform *temura*, as we have established. And the *tzibur* does not die — so *chatat shemetu ba'aleha* has no communal application. These three are therefore exclusively *yachid* scenarios.

### Group Unity Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ, וְשֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ — לֹא מָצִינוּ. יָכוֹל יְהוּ נוֹהֲגוֹת בֵּין בְּיָחִיד בֵּין בְּצִיבּוּר?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to communal offerings **whose owners** already **achieved atonement and** a communal offering **whose** first **year has passed, we have not found** a similarly clear indication of the *halakha*. Therefore, one **might** have thought **that** these two cases **are in effect both for** the offering of **an individual and a communal** offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: יִלְמַד אָדָם סָתוּם מִמְּפוֹרָשׁ, מָה מְפוֹרָשׁ בְּיָחִיד וְלֹא בְּצִבּוּר, אַף בְּשֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ וְשֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתָהּ בְּיָחִיד דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים וְלֹא בְּצִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** But **you** must **say: A person can learn** with regard to a case in which certain details are **not specified from** a similar case where these details **are specified. Just as** with regard to those offerings whose details are **specified,** i.e., an offspring of a sin offering, a substitution for a sin offering, and a sin offering whose owner has died, the *halakha* that it dies applies only **to** an offering of **an individual and not to a communal** offering, **so too,** with regard to a sin offering **whose owner achieved atonement, and** a sin offering **whose** first **year has passed, these matters are said with regard to** the offering of **an individual, but not with regard to a communal** offering.

Given that three of the five apply only to the *yachid*, Rabbi Shimon argues that the other two — *chatat shenitkapru ba'aleha* and *chatat she'avra shnata* — must likewise apply only to the *yachid*. The logic is *yilamed satum min hameforash*: the unexplicated cases are learned from the explicated ones. Since the five form a unified group, and three of them can only operate in the context of an individual, the entire group is understood to apply only to the *yachid* and not to the *tzibur*.

### Rav Natan: One Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּחַד מָקוֹם גְּמִיר לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But can one derive** the **possible from** the **impossible?** Those other cases include offerings that by definition do not apply to a community; how can one learn from them with regard to cases where they are possible? The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Shimon learns this tradition as one unit,** i.e., all the cases of sin offerings left to die are given as one *halakha*, and therefore there is no difference in their application.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אַרְבָּעָה נִתְּנוּ לָהֶן, וְהֶעֱמִידוּם עַל חָמֵשׁ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בְּצִיבּוּר — הָנָךְ מִי אִיתַנְהוּ בְּצִיבּוּר? אֶלָּא עַל כׇּרְחָיךְ — יִלְמַד סָתוּם מִמְּפוֹרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says** in explanation of this matter: When Moses received this tradition at Sinai, the *halakhot* of **four** sin offerings that must be left to die **were given to** the Jewish people, whereas the fifth sin offering is left to graze until it develops a blemish. But as they did not know which of the sin offerings was the one that should be left to graze, they **established these** *halakhot* **with regard to** all **five** sin offerings, that they are all left to die. However, given that the four sin offerings were said together, either all four are communal sin offerings, or all four are sin offerings of the individual. **And if it enters your mind** that the four cases of sin offerings that must be left to die, as stated to Moses, referred **to communal** offerings, **are** all of **these** four sin offerings possible **as communal** offerings? Rather, the tradition is with regard to sin offerings of the individual. **Rather, perforce** one must **derive** the cases that are **unspecified** in terms of their *halakhot* **from** those cases that are **specified.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אַחַת נִתְּנָה לָהֶן, וְהֶעֱמִידוּהָ עַל חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan says:** Only **one** case of a sin offering that must be left to die **was given to them** at Sinai, while the other four sin offerings are left to graze until they develop a blemish. But as they did not know which was the sin offering that must be left to die, the Jewish people **established it** as applying **to** all **five** kinds of sin offering.

Reish Lakish complicates this further by introducing the dimension of transmission. He suggests that the *mesorah* was actually for four *chataot* to die, not five. The fifth was uncertain — they knew there were four, they knew one could graze, but they did not know which was which. Rather than leave one unassigned, they extended the ruling to all five. The point about five forming a unified group still holds: since those four that had to die could only have been stated in the context of a *yachid* — the communal scenarios simply do not exist for enough of them — the entire *halacha* was said *beyachid*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחְזֵי בְּהֵי סִידְרָא גְּמִירִי לְהוּ, אִי בְּיָחִיד אִי בְּצִבּוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interrupts Rabbi Natan's statement to raise a difficulty with regard to his explanation: **But** why didn't the Jewish people first **see with regard to which category they learned** that a sin offering must be left to die, **whether** in reference **to** one of the three cases that are found only in the offering of **an individual, or to** one of the two cases that are also found in the offering **of a community?** If they knew which group of sin offerings must be left to die, they could have kept the other group in accordance with its original *halakha*, that it is left to graze.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי שִׁכְחָיוֹת שָׁכְחוּ, וְקַשְׁיָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **They forgot two** matters. They did not remember which of the five sin offerings must be left to die, and they also forgot to which category it applied, whether to a communal offering or an offering of an individual. And for this reason it was difficult for them, and they were forced to rule that all of these sin offerings must be left to die.

Rav Natan goes further still, to the opposite extreme: the *mesorah* was in fact for only one *chatat* to die. The rest were to graze. Four were lost, and since they did not know which of the five was the one, they extended the ruling to all of them.

### Double Forgetting

The Gemara then asks: even granting Rav Natan's position, might they not have at least remembered whether the one that dies was in the category that applies only to a *yachid*, or in the category that applies to both *yachid* and *tzibur*? The answer is: *trei shchiach shchu* — they forgot two things. Not only did they forget which specific *chatat* had to die; they also forgot whether it belonged to the group that applies exclusively to a *yachid* or the group that applies to both. Two layers of forgetting compounded one another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בְּצִבּוּר, הָנָךְ מִי אִיתַנְהוּ בְּצִיבּוּר? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: יִלְמַד סָתוּם מִמְּפוֹרָשׁ — מַה מְפוֹרָשׁ בְּיָחִיד וְלֹא בְּצִיבּוּר, אַף סָתוּם בְּיָחִיד וְלֹא בְּצִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its citation of the statement of Rabbi Natan, who proceeds to explain Rabbi Shimon's opinion in the mishna in a similar manner to Reish Lakish: **And if it enters your mind that** the four cases of sin offerings that are left to graze, which were stated to Moses as one unit, involved **communal** offerings, **are** all of **these** five sin offerings applicable in the case of **communal** offerings? **Rather, conclude from it** that one **learns** the cases that are **unspecified from** those cases that are **specified: Just as** the **specified** cases apply only **to** an offering of **an individual and not to a communal** offering, **so too,** the cases that are **not specified** apply only **to** an offering of **an individual, but not to a communal** offering.

Nevertheless, a resolution is still available. The *mesorah* that four had to graze — whatever its relationship to the five — was at minimum stated in a context where not all four could exist for a *tzibur*. At most two of the four could apply *betzibur*. Since those four form a unified group, and some of them apply only *beyachid*, the entire group of four must have been stated *beyachid*. And once we establish that the four that graze are a *yachid* matter, it follows that the one that dies is also a *yachid* matter. Therefore the entire *halacha* of *chamesh chataot meitot* applies only to the *yachid* and not to the *tzibur*.

### Transmission Problem

The full force of this *sugya* now comes into view. The *halacha* of *chamesh chataot meitot* is generally cited as a paradigmatic case of *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — a law transmitted directly from Sinai. Yet the Gemara has now shown that even this *halacha* was subject to the problems of transmission and forgetting. Whether the number was five, four, or one; whether it applied to *yachid* alone or also to *tzibur* — these were not questions about application to new cases, but questions about the transmission itself. The Gemara that began with the glory of perfect transmission and the *Eshkolot* who sustained it ends by demonstrating, case after case, that transmission was in fact vulnerable — that forgetting occurred, that debates arose from that forgetting, and that even foundational *halachot l'Moshe miSinai* were among the casualties.

This is, to put it directly, powerful evidence against the Rambam's position that disputes in *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* never occurred. The entire trajectory of this daf — from the mourning for Moshe through Yehoshua's loss, through the 1,700 forgotten hermeneutical traditions, through Osniel's recovery, and finally through the uncertainty at the heart of *chamesh chataot meitot* — is a sustained meditation on the fragility of transmission and the centrality of that fragility to the nature of *Torah shebe'al peh*.
