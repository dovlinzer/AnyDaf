# Nazir 59b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Partner Dies—Marketplace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: יְבַקֵּשׁ אֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּק שֶׁיִּדּוֹר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בְּנָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna described how two nazirites sacrifice offerings of impurity and purity, in a situation in which one of them has become impure but they do not know which one. This mishna discusses what must be done if **one of them dies** before bringing his offerings. **Rabbi Yehoshua said:** The surviving nazirite **asks someone in the marketplace,** a non-nazirite, **to vow to be a nazirite corresponding to him,** i.e., under the same conditions as his own naziriteship, so that he can bring offerings together with him.

### Intro: One Dies

The *mishnah* at the top of 59b introduces a scenario that bears a family resemblance to cases we have encountered before, though with a crucial new complication. Previously, the *mishnah* dealt with two *nezirim* who did not know which of them had become *tamei*. Their solution was to enter a kind of partnership, pool their offerings, and stipulate that each *korban* should count for whoever genuinely required it. They would proceed through two rounds of offerings — first bringing a *korban tumah* and a *korban tahara* with the appropriate stipulations, then, after another thirty days, completing the remaining *korban tahara*. The reason for the thirty-day wait between rounds was that the *tahor* partner could not shave before completing thirty days of *nezirus*, and so that constraint governed the entire sequence.

The new *mishnah* asks: what happens when one of the two partners dies? The surviving partner is left without anyone to stipulate with, and the question becomes what he must do on his own.

### Single Nazir Safek

Before examining the *mishnah*'s answer, it is worth establishing what the straightforward expectation would be. A single *nazir* who is uncertain whether he became *tamei* — a *safek tamei* — follows a well-defined procedure. Because he cannot shave in fewer than thirty days (lest he turn out to have been *tahor* all along and thereby cut short a valid *nezirus tahara*), he waits the full thirty days. At that point, he must bring something that could serve as his *korban tumah* if he was indeed *tamei*, and something that could serve as his *korban tahara* if he was *tahor*.

For the *tumah* side, he brings a *chatas ha-of b'safek* — the bird *chatas* — since halacha permits bringing a *chatas ha-of* under conditions of doubt. For the *tahara* side, he brings an *olas behemah b'tenai*: an animal *olah* with a conditional stipulation, because an *olah* can be offered as a voluntary *nedavah* even by someone who is *tamei* and therefore cannot validly complete a full *korban tahara*. He stipulates: if I am *tahor*, this *olah* fulfills part of my *korban tahara* obligation; if I am *tamei*, it is merely a *nedavah*. He cannot bring a full *chatas* of an animal on a conditional basis because an animal *chatas* may not be brought under conditions of doubt — only the bird *chatas* has that dispensation.

After this initial round, he waits another thirty days and brings the remainder. If he was *tamei*, his *olah* at the first stage was a *nedavah* and he has not yet brought any component of his *korban tahara*, so he must now bring the complete set — *olah*, *chatas*, and *shelamim* (the *korban tahara* does include a *shelamim*). If he was *tahor*, he already brought his *olah* at the first stage, so he need only bring the *chatas* and *shelamim* now, with the second *olah* offered *b'tenai*: if I was *tamei* previously, this *olah* is now obligatory as part of my *korban tahara*; if I was *tahor*, then my obligation has already been discharged and this one is a *nedavah*.

This is what one would expect the *mishnah* to say about a person left alone after his partner dies. Yet the *mishnah* takes a more surprising path.

### Marketplace Partner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: אִם טָמֵא הָיִיתִי — הֲרֵי אַתָּה נָזִיר מִיָּד, וְאִם טָהוֹר הָיִיתִי — הֲרֵי אַתָּה נָזִיר אַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וְסוֹפְרִין שְׁלֹשִׁים, וּמְבִיאִין קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה וְקׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה. וְאוֹמֵר: אִם אֲנִי הוּא הַטָּמֵא — קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלְּךָ. וְאִם אֲנִי הוּא הַטָּהוֹר — קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה בְּסָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **And he says** to him as follows: **If I was impure, you are hereby a nazirite immediately; and if I was pure, you are hereby a nazirite after thirty days. And they** both proceed to **count thirty days and bring an offering of impurity and an offering of purity. And** the nazirite who was defined as having uncertain impurity **says: If I am the impure** one, **the offering of impurity is mine and the offering of purity is yours; and if I am the pure one, the offering of purity is mine and the offering of impurity** we brought shall be **of uncertain** status.

Instead of treating the surviving *nazir* as a simple *safek tamei*, Rabbi Yehoshua proposes that he go out and find a stranger in the marketplace — *yevakesh lo echad min hashuk* — and recruit him to enter into the same kind of stipulated *nezirus* partnership that would have existed with the original partner. The practical mechanics of this, however, are considerably more intricate than what the original two partners had to do, precisely because the stranger is not himself *tamei* in any way.

### If I Am Tamei

After thirty days of counting together, the original *nazir* declares to the stranger: *im tamei hayiti* — if I was the *tamei* one, then your *nezirus* began immediately from the moment we made this arrangement. We are now at the end of thirty days. In that scenario, the *korban tumah* brought now is mine, and the *korban tahara* is yours. The stranger, having completed thirty days of *nezirus tahara*, may shave and is done. The original *nazir*, having now brought his *korban tumah*, will then count thirty more days of *nezirus tahara* and bring his *korban tahara*.

### If I Am Tahor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסוֹפְרִים שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּמְבִיאִין קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה, וְאוֹמֵר: אִם אֲנִי הַטָּמֵא — קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה — שֶׁלְּךָ, וָזֶה קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָתִי. וְאִם אֲנִי הוּא הַטָּהוֹר — קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלִּי, וְקׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה בְּסָפֵק, וְזֶהוּ קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָתְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And they** subsequently **count** another **thirty days and bring an offering of purity, and** the first nazirite **says: If I** was **the impure** one, **the offering of impurity** that we sacrificed at the end of the first thirty days was **mine, and the offering of purity** we brought then was **yours; and this** offering I am bringing now is **my offering of purity. And if I** was **the pure** one, and the deceased nazirite was impure, **the offering of purity** we brought thirty days ago was **mine, and the offering of impurity** we brought earlier was **of uncertain** status, **and this is your offering of purity.**

The stipulation continues: *v'im tahor hayisi* — if I was the *tahor* one, then your *nezirus* only began at this moment, not thirty days ago. In that case, the *korban tahara* being brought now is mine — I was *tahor* all along and have completed my thirty days. The *korban tumah* in this scenario is brought in a state of doubt; it counts for nobody definitively, but since we bring only what can be validly offered *b'safek*, we proceed accordingly. The stranger's *nezirus* having just started, he will wait another thirty days and bring his own *korban tahara* at that point. And in that second round, the original *nazir* makes a further declaration: if I was the *tamei* one, then the earlier *korban tumah* was mine and the earlier *korban tahara* was yours — and this final *korban tahara* is mine. If I was the *tahor* one, then the earlier *korban tahara* was mine, the *korban tumah* was a *safek* offering belonging to no one, and this *korban tahara* now concludes your *nezirus*.

### Why Wait 30 Days

One might well ask: why does the original *nazir* wait thirty days before even approaching the stranger with this arrangement? Why not find the stranger immediately and stipulate from the outset?

The answer becomes clear when one considers what happens at the end of thirty days. Not only must the *korbanos* be brought, but both partners must shave. And the shaving is the crux of the problem. If I was *tamei*, then the stranger completed thirty days of *nezirus tahara* and may shave legitimately following his *korban tahara*. But if I was *tahor*, then in the scenario where the stranger's *nezirus* had already begun thirty days earlier, the stranger now needs to shave as well — yet in that scenario, the offering brought was my *korban tahara*, not his. His *korban tahara* is only coming in the next round. How, then, can he shave now?

One solution would be to introduce yet another animal — an *olah b'tenai* — specifically to cover the stranger's potential need for a *korban tahara* at this stage. But Rabbi Yehoshua's approach avoids this complication elegantly: by stipulating that if I was *tahor*, your *nezirus* did not begin until this very moment, the stranger's *nezirus tahara* has not yet run its full course when the first shaving takes place. He is therefore not shaving at the conclusion of a *nezirus* at all — his *nezirus* has not yet ended — and there is no violation. The shaving at the thirty-day mark does not terminate a *nezirus* he is still in; it simply is a haircut that precedes the start of the relevant thirty-day period of his *nezirus tahara*.

To be precise about the function of this hair-cutting: it is not a positive ritual act concluding a *nezirus*; rather, the entire purpose of the stipulation is to ensure that the haircut does not *violate* a *nezirus*. If the stranger's *nezirus* had already started and he shaved without a valid *korban*, it would be a violation. By deferring the start of his *nezirus* in that scenario, the haircut remains permissible even without a *korban* — and then his thirty-day *nezirus tahara* begins fresh afterward. This is why the stipulation must be constructed so carefully, and why the full thirty-day waiting period before approaching the stranger is an integral part of Rabbi Yehoshua's design.

## Ben Zoma Objection

### Objection: Who Agrees?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ בֶּן זוֹמָא: וּמִי שׁוֹמֵעַ לוֹ שֶׁיִּדּוֹר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בְּנָזִיר?
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Zoma said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: **And who will listen to him to vow to be a nazirite corresponding to him?** How can one design a *halakha* on the assumption that a non-nazirite will agree to be a nazirite for a lengthy term?

Ben Zoma challenges Rabbi Yehoshua's proposal at its most basic level: *u'mi sheshomeia lo sheyidor kenegdo b'nazir* — who in the world is going to agree to this arrangement? Who will volunteer to take on *nezirus* and go through this elaborate conditional protocol simply as a favor to a stranger? The practical objection is decisive on its own. But Ben Zoma goes further and argues that the entire mechanism is unnecessary.

### Simpler Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מֵבִיא חַטַּאת הָעוֹף וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה, וְאוֹמֵר: אִם טָמֵא הָיִיתִי — הַחַטָּאת מֵחוֹבָתִי, וְהָעוֹלָה נְדָבָה. וְאִם טָהוֹר הָיִיתִי — הָעוֹלָה מֵחוֹבָתִי, וְהַחַטָּאת מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** a different procedure is available: After thirty days of naziriteship **he brings a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering, and says: If I was impure, the sin-offering is for my obligation** as an impure nazirite, **and the burnt-offering** is a regular **gift** offering. **And if I was pure, the burnt-offering is for my obligation** as a pure nazirite, **and the sin-offering** is **of uncertain** status.

Forget about finding a partner, says Ben Zoma. This person is simply a *safek tamei nazir*. Treat him as such.

### First 30 Days

He brings a *chatas ha-of* and an *olas behemah*, just as we described above for the standard *safek tamei nazir*. He stipulates: *im tamei hayiti*, the *chatas* is my obligatory *korban tumah* and the *olah* is a *nedavah*; *v'im tahor hayiti*, the *olah* is my obligatory *korban tahara* offering and the *chatas* is a *safek* — I was not obligated, so we dispose of it accordingly. This allows him to shave at the end of thirty days in either scenario.

### Second 30 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסוֹפֵר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּמֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה, וְאוֹמֵר: אִם טָמֵא הָיִיתִי — הָעוֹלָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה נְדָבָה, וְזוֹ חוֹבָה. וְאִם טָהוֹר הָיִיתִי — הָעוֹלָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה חוֹבָה, וְזוֹ נְדָבָה, וְזוֹ שְׁאָר קׇרְבָּנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And he counts** another **thirty days, and brings an offering of purity, and says: If I was impure, the first burnt-offering** I brought should be considered **a gift** offering, **and this** one I am bringing now is for my **obligation. And if I was pure, the first burnt-offering** I brought is for my **obligation** as a pure nazirite, **and this** one I am bringing now is **a gift** offering. **And these,** i.e., the sin-offering and peace-offering I am sacrificing now, comprises **the rest of my offerings** that I was obligated to bring earlier.

He then waits another thirty days and brings the full *korban tahara* — *olah*, *chatas*, and *shelamim*. He stipulates again: if I was *tamei*, then the first *olah* was a *nedavah* and this entire set is now my obligatory *korban tahara*; if I was *tahor*, then the first *olah* was my obligatory offering, and this final *olah* is a *nedavah* while the *chatas* and *shelamim* complete my *korban tahara* obligation.

It is straightforward, it avoids the need for any stranger from the marketplace, and it achieves the same result.

## Splitting Problem

### Split Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: נִמְצָא זֶה מֵבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו לַחֲצָאִים. אֲבָל הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים לְבֶן זוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said:** According to your opinion, **it turns out** that **this** nazirite **brings his offerings in halves,** i.e., in stages. If he was pure, he brings his burnt-offering thirty days before the rest of his offerings. **However, the Rabbis agreed with ben Zoma,** and disregarded the concern about splitting up the offerings.

Rabbi Yehoshua responds to Ben Zoma's simpler method with the objection: *nimtza ze mevi korbanotav lachatza'in* — you are causing this person to split his *korban tahara* into two stages. In Ben Zoma's scheme, the *olah* of the *korban tahara* comes at thirty days, while the *chatas* and *shelamim* come only at sixty days. The *korban tahara* is thus delivered in two installments rather than as a complete unit. Rabbi Yehoshua's marketplace arrangement ensures that each person brings a full, undivided *korban tahara* at their respective stage.

### Objection Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלַיְיתֵי! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶלָּא לְחַדֵּד בָּהּ אֶת הַתַּלְמִידִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that Rabbi Yehoshua countered ben Zoma's opinion by pointing out that his solution would cause the nazirite to bring his offerings in stages. The Gemara asks: What is wrong with that? **And let him bring** the offerings in stages. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Rabbi Yehoshua said** this comment **only to sharpen** the minds of **the students.** He did not really maintain that it is prohibited to act in this manner; rather, he wanted to test his disciples to see if they were aware of the *halakha*.

The Gemara, however, is unimpressed with this response. The objection that one's *korban* is split into two stages does not seem like a compelling enough reason to impose the entire complicated apparatus of finding a stranger, negotiating a conditional *nezirus*, and drafting elaborate stipulations. The sages agreed with Ben Zoma — *hodu lo chachamim l'ven Zoma* — and this is not a close call. The Gemara treats Rabbi Yehoshua's objection as weak.

### Sharpen Students

This leads Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel to offer a remarkable interpretation: *lo amar Rabbi Yehoshua ela lechaded bo et hatalmidim* — Rabbi Yehoshua only said it to sharpen his students. He was not actually advocating for the marketplace-stranger approach as normative halacha; he presented it as an intellectual exercise to stimulate discussion and sharpen the analytical capacities of his disciples.

This is, as the Gemara itself implies, a somewhat strange use of that phrase. Typically, *lechaded et hatalmidim* refers to a rhetorical device — presenting a position to see how students respond, not actually ruling that way. But here Rabbi Yehoshua laid out a detailed, fully-articulated halachic ruling with specific stipulations and scenarios. It is not obvious how an elaborate *psak* constitutes merely sharpening the students, and the Gemara leaves the precise meaning somewhat open.

### Fat Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מַאי לֶיעְבֵּיד לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְדַקַּיָּה דְּלָא לֵיסְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said,** in a light-hearted vein: **What will Rabbi Yehoshua do with the intestines** of **his** animals so **that they will not spoil?** If he insists that the offerings of purity must all be brought at the same time, the only way to do so is to wait thirty days after slaughtering the burnt-offering before burning its intestines, which is certainly impractical.

Rav Nachman then adds a further criticism of Rabbi Yehoshua's position with the cryptic remark: *mai avidei leh Rabbi Yehoshua ledakei delo lissrach* — what should Rabbi Yehoshua do for his intestines that they should not spoil?

This phrase is opaque. *Tosafot* notes that it is unexplained — *lo ipparesh* — and then attempts an interpretation involving the fat of the offerings. The idea, as the *Rishonim* develop it, is that when two *nezirim* share a *korban* and must perform *tenufa* together, they must synchronize their shavings, and the delays involved in waiting for both partners to complete their respective stages could cause the fat of the offerings to sit unburned for an inappropriately long time, creating its own set of problems. Whatever advantage Rabbi Yehoshua thought he gained by avoiding the splitting of the *korban tahara*, he loses because the shared arrangement between the original *nazir* and the marketplace stranger generates delays that compromise the timely burning of the fat.

Others read the phrase as simply a colorful expression of dismissal — something to the effect that Rabbi Yehoshua's position is so untenable that one cannot even fathom what motivated it. The precise intent of the remark remains genuinely unclear among the *Rishonim*, and one should not pretend otherwise.

## Safek Tumah & Metzora

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה טָמֵא בְּסָפֵק וּמוּחְלָט בְּסָפֵק — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים אַחַר שִׁשִּׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a nazirite who,** on the first day of his naziriteship, **was impure** from a corpse as a matter of **uncertainty and** was also **a confirmed leper** as a matter of **uncertainty,** i.e., it was uncertain whether or not he had leprosy, how can he fulfill the shaving obligations of a pure nazirite and an impure leper? The problem facing this nazirite is that a leper must shave both when he begins his purification process and at the close of it, one week later. However, a leper may not partake of sacrificial food, but a nazirite may. Therefore, **he may partake of sacrificial food sixty days after** he may have become impure, when the uncertainty with regard to leprosy will have been clarified. He shaves for the first time for his leprosy after thirty days, and for the second time thirty days later, the shaving of the end of the purification process; at which point he brings the offerings of a purified leper and may partake of sacrificial food.

The next *mishnah* introduces a compound case of uncertainty: a *nazir* who does not know whether he became *tamei meis* — ritually impure through contact with a corpse — or whether he is a *metzora*, a person afflicted with the skin condition whose *tumah* and subsequent purification process follow an entirely different track.

### Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן, וּמִטַּמֵּא לַמֵּתִים אַחַר מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **But** he **may drink wine and become impure from the dead,** effectively ending his naziriteship, only **after 120 days.** This is because he might have been a full-fledged leper, which means that his shavings count toward his leprosy, not his naziriteship. Consequently, after the first sixty days he must observe another thirty days of naziriteship and shave again. Even then he has yet to fulfill all his obligations, as he might have been impure from a corpse, which means his shaving after ninety days was for his impurity. He must therefore remain a nazirite for another thirty days, before shaving one final time at the end of 120 days to fulfill his naziriteship obligation.

The *mishnah* states that such a person may eat *kodashim* after sixty days, may drink wine after sixty days, and may contract impurity from the dead after one hundred and twenty days. These numbers emerge from a careful analysis of all the possible scenarios he might be in and the constraints each one imposes.

### Four Stages

The key insight driving the entire analysis is the following: regardless of which status this person actually has, the possibility that he might be a standard *nazir tahor* — one who is neither *tamei* nor a *metzora* — prevents him from shaving until he has completed at least thirty days of *nezirus*. A *nazir tahor* may not cut his hair until his *nezirus* is finished, and his *nezirus* requires at minimum thirty days. This thirty-day constraint, imposed by the mere possibility that he might be a *nazir tahor*, cascades through the entire sequence and forces the entire analysis into thirty-day (or year-long, as we will see) increments.

### Tahor Scenario

If this person turns out to be purely *tahor* — neither *tamei* nor a *metzora* — he simply waits thirty days, shaves, and brings the full *korban tahara*. That is his complete *nezirus* obligation.

### Nazir Tamei

If he turns out to be a *nazir tamei*, the process is: seven days of purification through the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes, then shaving at the conclusion of that purification, and then thirty days of *nezirus tahara* leading to a second shaving and the *korban tahara*. His *tumah* ceased at the halachic level after seven days regardless of offerings; the *korbanos* are what trigger the start of the *nezirus tahara*.

### Metzora Scenario

If he turns out to be a definite *metzora* who is also a *nazir*, the procedure for a *nazir metzora* would involve shaving immediately upon confirmation of the *metzora* status, then a seven-day wait, then a second shaving as part of the *metzora* purification, followed by the resumption of the *nezirus* with its own waiting period and final shaving.

### 30-Day Constraint

Since we do not know which of these scenarios he is actually in, and since the *nazir tahor* possibility prevents any shaving before thirty days, the entire analysis gets shifted. We cannot shave immediately even if he might be a *metzora*, because we must account for the possibility that he is a *nazir tahor*, for whom a premature haircut would constitute a violation of his *nezirus*. This single constraint — the *nazir tahor* possibility — forces the first shaving to be deferred to day thirty.

### Four 30-Day Periods

Here is how the full timeline unfolds. Because we must wait until day thirty for the first shaving, that first shaving at day thirty can serve double duty: it counts as the shaving of a *nazir tamei* (if that is his status) and simultaneously as the first shaving of a *metzora* (if that is his status). After that first shaving, one might think we could proceed to the second shaving after just seven more days — seven days being the waiting period between the first and second shaving of a *metzora*, and also sufficient time for the *tamei meis* purification. But we cannot: if he was a *nazir tamei*, then his first shaving now obligates him to begin thirty days of *nezirus tahara*, and he cannot shave again until that thirty-day period is complete.

So the second shaving is deferred to day sixty. At day sixty, a person who was purely *tahor* has long since completed his *nezirus* — he finished at day thirty. A person who was purely *tamei meis* finished his *nezirus tahara* at day sixty and is now done. A person who was a *metzora* but not *tamei meis* has now completed both his *metzora* shavings (the first at day thirty, the second at day sixty), and his *nezirus*, which was suspended during his *metzora* status, now resumes. Since the resumed *nezirus* might itself be a *nezirus tahara* of thirty days (corresponding to the scenario where he was a *tahor nazir* who also happened to be a *metzora*), he must wait yet another thirty days before the third shaving. And finally, if he was both *tamei meis* and a *metzora*, then his *nezirus tahara* only begins after all the *metzora* processing is complete — which means it only begins at day sixty, and he will not finish until day ninety.

This yields four consecutive thirty-day periods and, effectively, four shavings or stages at days thirty, sixty, ninety, and one hundred and twenty. By day sixty he is definitively clear of *metzora* status and may therefore eat *kodashim* (since the *kodashim* prohibition flows from his *tumah* statuses, not his *nezirus* obligations). And by day one hundred and twenty he has definitively completed his *nezirus* in all possible scenarios and may drink wine and contract impurity from the dead.

The driving logic of the four stages corresponds to four possible scenarios: (1) purely *tahor* — *nezirus tahara* concludes at stage one; (2) purely *tamei meis* — *nezirus tahara* concludes at stage two; (3) *tahor meis* but *metzora* — *nezirus tahara* concludes at stage three; (4) *tamei meis* and *metzora* — *nezirus tahara* concludes at stage four. Each scenario places the genuine *nezirus tahara* at a different stage, and the uncertainty forces us to wait through all four.

### Year-Long Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁתִּגְלַחַת הַנֶּגַע דּוֹחֶה תִּגְלַחַת הַנָּזִיר בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא וַדַּאי. אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא סָפֵק — אֵינוֹ דּוֹחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna notes: The reason that he cannot shave for his leprosy after seven days and perform the second shaving of a leper seven days later is **because the shaving of leprosy overrides** the prohibition of **the shaving of a nazirite** only **when his** status as **a leper** is **definite. However, when his** status as **a leper** is **uncertain,** the shaving **does not override** his naziriteship, and therefore he must wait thirty days before each of his shavings for leprosy.

The *mishnah* then notes, and a *baraita* elaborates: all of the above applies when the *nezirus* is of the standard minimum duration — thirty days. But if this *nazir* had accepted upon himself a *nezirus* of one year, then each thirty-day unit in the above analysis expands to a full year. He may eat *kodashim* after two years, and may drink wine and become *tamei* for the dead after four years.

The reason is intrinsic to the structure: the reason each unit had to be thirty days was that the possible start of a *nezirus tahara* at each stage required waiting out its full length. If the *nezirus tahara* is one year long rather than thirty days, then each waiting period becomes one year. A *nazir tamei* must wait, after his first shaving, the full length of his *nezirus* before his next shaving; if his *nezirus* is a year long, he waits a year. A *metzora nazir* whose *metzora* processing concludes at a given stage and who then resumes his *nezirus tahara* must wait the full year before his next shaving. Thus each of the four stages now spans one year, yielding a total timeline of four years.

## Four Haircuts & Offers

### Baraita Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּנְזִירוּת מוּעֶטֶת, אֲבָל בִּנְזִירוּת בַּת שָׁנָה — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים לְאַחַר שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים לְאַחַר אַרְבַּע שָׁנִים. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: וּמְגַלֵּחַ אַרְבַּע תִּגְלָחִיּוֹת. תִּגְלַחַת רִאשׁוֹנָה — מֵבִיא צִפּוֹרִים, וְחַטַּאת הָעוֹף, וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sage **taught: In what** case **is this statement** of the mishna **said? With regard to a short naziriteship** of thirty days. **However, with regard to a naziriteship of a year, he partakes of sacrificial food** only **after two years,** and **consequently he may drink wine and become impure from the dead** only **after four years. And it is taught with regard to** the *halakha* of the mishna (*Tosefta* 6:1): **And he shaves** with **four** separate acts of **shaving,** each time for its own reason. How so? For his **first shaving,** after the thirty days of an unspecified naziriteship or at the end of the period he vowed to observe naziriteship, he **brings** two **birds** of a leper: One is slaughtered over spring water and the other is sent away (Leviticus 14:4–7). **And** he also brings **a bird sin-offering** for his shaving of impure naziriteship, as he may have contracted impurity from a corpse, **and an animal burnt-offering** for his shaving of purity, as he may not have contracted impurity from a corpse.

Having established the structure of the four stages, the Gemara now presents a *baraita* that specifies precisely which *korbanos* are brought at each of the four shavings. This requires care, because at each stage the person might be in a different halachic status, and only offerings that can be validly brought under conditions of uncertainty are permissible. The *baraita* states: at the first shaving, one brings *tzipporim*, a *chatas ha-of*, and an *olas behemah*; at the second shaving, a *chatas ha-of* and an *olas behemah*; at the third shaving, a *chatas ha-of* and an *olas behemah*; and at the fourth shaving, the full *korban tahara*.

### First Haircut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: תִּגְלַחַת רִאשׁוֹן מֵבִיא וְכוּ׳. מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ שַׁפִּיר קָמַיְיתֵי, דְּאִי וַדַּאי מְצוֹרָע הוּא וְלָאו טָמֵא הוּא — צִיפּוֹרִין חוֹבָתוֹ, וְחַטַּאת הָעוֹף סָפֵק, אָזְלָא לִקְבוּרָה, וְעוֹלָה הָוְיָא נְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the details and reasons for this *halakha*: **You said** in this *baraita* that for the **first shaving** he **brings** the two birds of a leper, as well as a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering. The Gemara explains: In this manner **he brings** the **proper** offerings **whichever way you** look at it. **As, if his** status as **a leper** is **definite and he is not impure,** the **birds** he brings are for **his obligation** to purify himself from leprosy. **And** as **the bird sin-offering** in case he was impure was brought due to **uncertainty,** it **goes for burial,** like all bird sin-offerings sacrificed in a case of uncertainty, which cannot be eaten. **And** the bird **burnt-offering** that he brought in case he was an impure nazirite **is a gift** offering.

At the first shaving — which occurs at day thirty — the person might be in any of three possible halachic states. He might be a *nazir tahor* completing his *nezirus*, for which the *olah* component of the *korban tahara* applies; an *olas behemah b'tenai* can be brought because even if he turns out to be *tamei*, the *olah* is valid as a *nedavah*. He might be a *nazir tamei* who has just concluded his purification and is initiating his *nezirus tahara*, for which the *chatas ha-of* applies; a *chatas ha-of* may be brought under conditions of doubt, and it serves as the *korban tumah* of a *nazir tamei*. And he might be a *metzora* at stage one of his purification process, for which the *tzipporim* — the birds used in the *metzora* purification ritual — apply.

### Birds & Chatas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְגַלְּחוֹ תּוֹךְ שִׁבְעָה אִי אֶפְשָׁר, דְּדִילְמָא לָאו מוּחְלָט הוּא, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״תַּעַר לֹא יַעֲבֹר עַל רֹאשׁוֹ עַד מְלֹאת״. וְאִי לָאו מְצוֹרָע וַדַּאי הָוֵי, וְהוּא טָמֵא — חַטַּאת הָעוֹף חוֹבָתוֹ, וְצִיפּוֹרִין אַבָּרַאי קָא מִתְעַבְדִין, וְלָא הָוֵי חוּלִּין בַּעֲזָרָה. וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה הָוְיָא נְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further explains: **But** it **is not possible to shave him** right **after seven days,** as one usually does when purified from leprosy, **as perhaps he is not a confirmed** leper and has no obligation to shave but is bound by the naziriteship vow, **and the Merciful One says: "No razor shall come upon his head; until** the days are **fulfilled** in which he consecrated himself to the Lord" (Numbers 6:5). This verse teaches that he cannot shave until the end of his naziriteship. Therefore, he shaves only after thirty days. **And if he is not a definite leper but he is impure** from a corpse, **the bird sin-offering** is for **his obligation** as an impure nazirite, **and** there is no problem with the unnecessary rituals of a leper's **birds,** as **they are performed outside** the Temple, **and are not** subject to the prohibition against bringing **non-sacred** animals **into the** Temple **courtyard.** Therefore, the slaughter of one of the birds outside the Temple is not a transgression. **And** the **animal burnt-offering is** considered **a gift** offering.

It is important to distinguish carefully between the *tzipporim* and the *chatas ha-of*, as they can be confused. The *tzipporim* of the *metzora* purification are not a *korban* at all. They are a purification ritual performed outside the *beit hamikdash*: one bird is slaughtered, its blood collected in a vessel, the living bird is dipped into the blood, and the living bird is released into the field. It is analogous, in its nature as a non-*korban* purification act, to the ashes of the *parah adumah* — it is a *tahara* process, not a sacrificial offering. The *chatas ha-of*, by contrast, is an actual *korban* brought within the *beit hamikdash*, offered on the altar. It is one of the *korbanos* required of a *metzora* at the second stage of his purification, and it is also one of the *korbanos* of a *nazir tamei*. The halachic dispensation permitting a *chatas ha-of* to be brought under conditions of *safek* makes it valid for both purposes.

The *tzipporim* are brought at the first shaving because at that stage one might be a *metzora* at stage one of purification, and that is when the *tzipporim* ritual takes place. The *chatas ha-of* is not a *metzora* stage-one requirement but is brought at the first shaving because of the possibility of being a *nazir tamei*, for whom it is required as part of the *korban tumah* obligation.

### Second Haircut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָאו מְצוֹרָע הוּא וְלָאו טָמֵא הוּא — צִיפּוֹרִין אַבָּרַאי קָא מִתְעַבְדִין, חַטַּאת הָעוֹף לִקְבוּרָה, עוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה הָוְיָא חוֹבָתוֹ. וְהָא בָּעֵי אָשָׁם! רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מַיְיתֵי וּמַתְנֵי. תִּגְלַחַת שְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית צִיפּוֹרִין לָא צְרִיךְ, דְּהָא עֲבַיד, מַאי אִיכָּא: דִּילְמָא וַדַּאי מוּחְלָט הוּא, חֲדָא — לִסְפֵק סְפָרוֹ, וּלְגַלְּחוֹ תּוֹךְ שִׁבְעָה אִי אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he is neither a leper nor impure** he has also acted correctly, as **the birds are performed outside** the Temple, which means no transgression is committed, while the **bird sin-offering** of uncertain necessity goes **to burial,** and the **animal burnt-offering is** for **his obligation,** like any nazirite who completes his naziriteship. The Gemara asks: **But** isn't a leper **required** to bring **a guilt-offering** to complete his atonement and permit him to partake of sacrificial food? The Gemara answers: **This** ruling is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that one **may bring** a guilt-offering **and stipulate** as follows: If I am obligated to bring this offering then it is for my obligation; and if not, it shall be a voluntary peace-offering. For the **second and third shavings** he does **not need** to bring **birds** again, **as he has** already **performed** this ritual. **What is** the potential concern? That **perhaps he** was previously **a confirmed** leper of **definite** status. For this reason, one of the bird sin-offerings he brings at the second shaving is **for** the days of **his counting** when his status was **uncertain.** He cannot shave him right **after seven days,** as we would for a regular leper.

At the second shaving — day sixty — the *baraita* states that one brings a *chatas ha-of* and an *olas behemah*, but not *tzipporim*. The *tzipporim* are absent because, by day sixty, any *metzora* scenario has advanced beyond stage one — the *tzipporim* ritual was already performed at day thirty. The *chatas ha-of* and *olas behemah* continue to be brought because at the second shaving there are still multiple possible statuses the person might be in, and these two offerings cover the relevant possibilities in the same way they did at the first stage. The full analysis of what each offering covers at the second and subsequent shavings will be taken up in the continuation of the *baraita*.
