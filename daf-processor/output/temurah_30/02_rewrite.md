# Temurah 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Defining Zonah

### Esnan Zonah Overview

The daf continues a fascinating discussion about the Torah's category of *esnan zonah* — an animal given as payment to a prostitute that cannot be used as a *korban*. The specific details regarding what objects are included, and why money is treated differently from an animal, will be addressed later; the present focus is on defining the precise circumstances that give something the status of *esnan zonah*, and in particular, on what constitutes a *zonah* for these purposes.

A striking feature of the *Gemara*'s analysis up to this point is that the category of *zonah* is not understood in its colloquial sense — a common prostitute — but refers specifically to a woman with whom this man is forbidden to have relations under any circumstances. The operative category is *arayos*: a close relative, a married woman, and so on. The discussion has included even the case of a man paying another man. Two scenarios have been at the center of debate: first, a husband paying his wife to have relations while she is a *niddah*, which was contested — the question being whether the marital context precludes the label of *zonah*, though one approach emphasized not the word *zonah* but the word *toeva*, shifting focus from the person's status to the character of the act itself. Second, a woman paying a man was excluded, with the understanding that the *pasuk* addresses only a man paying a woman, though payment by a man to another man was also included.

### Rav's Position

The *Gemara* now asks: what does *Rav* do with the word *toeva*? Since *Rav* excluded the case of *ishto niddah* from the definition of *zonah* — and it was *Levi* who included it based on *toeva* — if *Rav* does not use *toeva* to broaden the category as *Levi* did, what purpose does he assign that word? The answer is: *mibaya lei k'Abaye* — he uses it to teach a ruling in the manner of *Abaye*.

### Abaye's Position

*Abaye* stated that *zona ovedet kochavim esnanah assur* — if the woman in question is a non-Jewish woman, her *esnan* is forbidden. This holds even when the relationship does not fall under the classic *arayos* prohibition in its most severe form, because there can be no *kiddushin* with a non-Jewish woman, and that very fact defines the relationship as *zenus*. The reasoning proceeds by way of a *gezerah shavah*: the word *toeva* appears here in the context of *esnan zonah*, and the same word appears elsewhere — *kol asher yaaseh mikol hatoevos ha'eil* — in the context of *arayos*. Just as there, *arayos* are defined as relationships in which *kiddushin* does not take effect, so too here: the category encompasses any relationship in which *kiddushin* cannot take effect. This includes relations with a non-Jewish woman.

### Non-Jewish Woman

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* establishes that all parties agree the category is limited to relationships that constitute *arayos*, and includes a Jewish man with a non-Jewish woman because no binding *kiddushin* is possible. The remaining debate concerns *ishto niddah*, where *kiddushin* is in fact operative and the couple is genuinely married — whether that situation can even be characterized as *zenus* remains contested.

## Zonah for Kohanim

### Kohen vs. Esnan

The *Gemara* now introduces a striking complication: the word *zonah* carries a different meaning in the context of *kehunah*. The Torah states *challala zona lo yikachu* — a *kohen* may not marry a *challala* or a *zonah*. Who qualifies as a *zonah* for a *kohen*? Counterintuitively, the answer excludes the non-Jewish woman. The reason is rooted in a different verse: *velo yechalel zaro* — "he shall not profane his seed." This phrase requires that the offspring be genealogically attributed to him: *mi shezaro meyuchas achrav*. Since the child of a Jewish man and a non-Jewish woman is considered her child and is not even Jewish, the concept of *zaro meyuchas achrav* does not apply. The category of *zonah* for a *kohen* therefore does not include a non-Jewish woman — not because the relationship is acceptable, but because it falls outside the specific parameters the Torah is addressing.

There is a *machlokes* between the *Rambam* and the *Ra'avad* as to whether the *issurei kehunah* attach through the act of relations or only in the context of marriage, given that the verse says *lo yikachu*. Though the *Gemara* itself does not resolve this explicitly, one could apply the same reasoning the *Gemara* uses elsewhere: since *kiddushin* cannot take effect with a non-Jewish woman, the *issur zona* in the context of *kehunah* does not apply to her.

To clarify the three tiers of *kiddushin*: where the prohibition carries *kares* or *misa* — as in the classic *arayos* of married women and close relatives — *kiddushin* does not take effect at all, and these constitute the core cases of *esnan zonah*. Where the prohibition is merely a *lav* — such as a *mamzer* or the *issurim* specific to a *kohen*, like marrying a *grusha* — *kiddushin* does take effect, and at this stage of the discussion these appear not to qualify as *esnan zonah*. A third tier consists of relations with a non-Jewish woman, where *kiddushin* similarly does not take effect, yet the prohibition is not the classic *issur arayos*; nevertheless, since *kiddushin* is not binding, such a case is included in *esnan zonah*.

### Jewish vs. Non-Jewish

The *Gemara* then presents the reverse case. *Zonah Yisraelis esnanah mutar* — if a man pays a Jewish woman who is simply a prostitute, the *esnan* is permitted. Why? *De-kiddushin tafsi bah* — because *kiddushin* would be binding with her. Yet: *ve-Kohen she-ba aleha lokeh mishum zona* — a *kohen* who has relations with her receives lashes for the violation of *zonah*. Why? *De-zaro meyuchas achrav* — the child is considered his. This defines *zonah* for the purposes of *kehunah*: it corresponds to the literal meaning of the word — a prostitute — but requires that the offspring be attributed to him, which excludes a non-Jewish woman.

The result is a striking inversion: the woman who is forbidden to a *kohen* as a *zonah* — the Jewish prostitute — is not subject to the *esnan zonah* prohibition; and the woman whose *esnan* is forbidden — the non-Jewish woman — does not qualify as *zonah* for the *kohen*. For *esnan zonah*, the operative criterion is that *kiddushin* cannot be binding; for the *kohen*'s prohibition, the operative criterion is paternal attribution of offspring.

### Two Definitions

To state this clearly: for *esnan zonah*, the category requires a woman with whom *kiddushin* is not binding — that is, cases of *arayos* with *kares* or *misa*, or a non-Jewish woman. A normal prostitute and a woman in a *kiddushei lav* situation — such as a *mamzeres* or a *grusha* for a *kohen* — are excluded at this stage. For the *kohen*'s prohibition, *zonah* means a woman with whom he has the kind of relationship in which his child would be attributed to him — which is precisely the Jewish case, including the literal prostitute, and which excludes the non-Jewish woman. Why should the same word mean two different things? Because in one context it is interpreted through the lens of *toeva*, which focuses on the character of the act, while in the other it is not.

## Rava's Position

### Rava's Inclusivity

*Rava* now diverges from some of *Abaye*'s conclusions. *Ve-Rava amar echad zeh ve-echad zeh esnanah assur* — according to *Rava*, both a non-Jewish woman *and* a Jewish woman who is a prostitute render the *esnan* forbidden. This is a significant development: for the first time in the discussion, a straightforward case of a Jewish prostitute is brought squarely into the *esnan zonah* category, without requiring her to be an *ervah* or the *kiddushin* to be invalid. *Rava* further states that *ve-Kohen ha-ba aleha lokeh mishum zona* — the reverse also applies: a *kohen* who has relations with a non-Jewish woman receives lashes for violating *zonah*. The two definitions are thus applied across the board.

### Kal Vachomer

What is *Rava*'s logic? *Yalpi me-hadadi* — we learn one from the other. Just as the prohibition of *zonah* for a *kohen* applies to a Jewish *zonah* because of a *lav*, so too does it apply to a non-Jewish woman by means of a *lav*. And correspondingly, just as *esnan zonah* applies to a non-Jewish woman because *kiddushin* is not binding, so too does *esnan zonah* apply to a Jewish prostitute. *Rava* thus draws both the *kohen*'s prohibition and the *esnan zonah* prohibition across both the Jewish and non-Jewish cases. The details of exactly which non-Jewish cases are covered — whether it requires the woman to be an active prostitute or whether the mere absence of *kiddushin* suffices — require further analysis, but in principle *Rava* applies the categories broadly.

## Baraita Challenges

### Baraita's Threshold

A challenge is raised against *Abaye*: the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* that states, *echad zona ovedet kochavim ve-echad zona Yisraelis esnanah assur* — whether the *zonah* is non-Jewish or Jewish, the *esnan* is forbidden. This appears to directly contradict *Abaye*'s position that a Jewish prostitute without *ervah* status does not render the *esnan* forbidden. The *baraita* presents a strikingly lower threshold than what *Abaye* maintained: it includes ordinary cases of prostitution and even, at the end of the *baraita*, cases involving prohibitions that are mere *lavim*, comparing the *zonah* to an *almanah* for a *Kohen Gadol*.

### Abaye's Concession

*Abaye* responds: *ha mani Rabbi Akiva hi* — this *baraita* follows the opinion of *Rabbi Akiva*, who holds that *kiddushin* does not take effect even in cases of *chayavei lavin*, negative prohibitions. If *kiddushin* is not binding even by a *lav* — which in this context would mean, for instance, a *kohen* with a *zonah* — then the threshold for *esnan zonah* becomes correspondingly lower and could encompass such cases.

The *baraita*'s comparison to *almanah le-Kohen Gadol* also introduces an important limiting principle that *Rava* must address: *mah almanah lo lakya ad de-masri bah* — just as lashes are not administered for relations with an *almanah* unless the act is properly designated and warned, so too by *zonah*, *ad de-amri hei lach* — the payment must specifically be identified as compensation for the act. This implies that the *esnan* status does not arise automatically but requires that the payment be explicitly for the purpose of the sexual act.

This connects to a teaching attributed to *Rabbi Eliezer*: *panui ha-ba al ha-pnuya shelo le-shem ishus asa-ah zona* — if a single man has relations with a single woman outside the context of marriage, he renders her a *zonah* for purposes of *kehunah*. According to *Abaye*, this *baraita* with its low threshold is attributable to *Rabbi Eliezer*, who applies a similar principle to *esnan zonah*. The *Gemara* probes this attribution: if the *baraita* follows *Rabbi Eliezer*, why does it specifically give the example of an *almanah* rather than simply saying *pnuyah*? The *Gemara* answers: *almanah itztreich* — the case of the *almanah* needed to be stated. One might have thought that since we begin from the concept of a classic prostitute, a woman who is *also* subject to a *lav* prohibition should perhaps be excluded — that the *isur* of *zonah* applies only when the sole problem is the prostitution and not when there is an additional prohibition. The *baraita* therefore teaches that even if she is *also* forbidden by a *lav*, the *esnan* is still forbidden.

### Zonah Status

The alternate version of this passage sharpens the central question: does *zonah* status for purposes of *esnan zonah* depend on the act itself, or must it precede the act? The comparison to *almanah le-Kohen Gadol* is read by *Rava* as conveying that a woman does not become a *zonah* merely because the act was extramarital. Rather, she must already have had that status beforehand — like an *almanah* whose *almanah* status precedes her relations with the *Kohen Gadol*. This means that for *esnan zonah* to apply, the woman must already be a *zonah* before the act, which in practice means she was already a prostitute, not merely that the act was outside of marriage. Thus, the extramarital act alone — *panui ha-ba al ha-pnuya* — does not in itself constitute *zenus* for these purposes.

To summarize the resulting positions: *Abaye* maintains that *esnan zonah* requires *arayos* — cases where *kiddushin* cannot take effect — and does not extend to *kiddushei lav* situations or ordinary prostitution. The *baraita* that includes these lower categories is attributed by *Abaye* to *Rabbi Akiva* (because *kiddushin* is not binding even by *lavin*) or to *Rabbi Eliezer* (because of his broader definition of *zonah* via premarital relations). *Rava* disagrees and includes both the Jewish prostitute and, by extension, the non-Jewish woman, across all applications. The *Rambam* rules that *issurei lav* are included — so, for instance, a *kohen* married to a *grusha* who pays her for relations within the marriage would create an *esnan zonah*. This is a notable ruling given that in the earlier discussion marriage seemed to preclude the *zonah* definition entirely, but the *Rambam*'s analysis draws from the contours of this *Gemara*.

The question of whether the status of the person paying makes a difference — a non-Jew paying a Jewish woman, or a non-Jew paying a non-Jewish woman — is also relevant. Presumably a non-Jew paying a Jewish woman should always generate *esnan zonah* since *kiddushin* would not be binding. As for a non-Jew paying a non-Jewish woman, the category likely requires a Jew to be involved, though the *Rambam* does not appear to address this case explicitly.

## Slave Case Study

### Slave Context

The next *mishnah* presents the following case: *omer la-chaveiro heilach seh zu* — a man says to another: here, take this sheep, in exchange for allowing his female slave to cohabit with the first man's male slave, for the purpose of producing offspring. The *Chachamim* ruled this constitutes *esnan zonah*; *Rabbi* disagreed.

The *Gemara* notes a preliminary difficulty: *ve-shifcha le-eved ivri mishtarya* — cohabitation between a female Canaanite slave and a Hebrew slave is actually permitted. The Torah itself speaks of a master giving a slave wife: *im isha yeleid lo adoneha* — the master can provide his Hebrew male slave with a non-Jewish female slave to produce children. If the act is entirely permitted, how can the payment for arranging it be *esnan zonah*?

The *Gemara*'s first answer (*Rav Huna*) is that the man was actually contracting for himself — he wanted the female slave, and his reference to his own slave was a euphemism, *lishna ma'alya*. But if so, why does *Rabbi* rule it is not *esnan zonah*? That would seem to be a straightforward case: a Jewish man, a non-Jewish woman, no binding *kiddushin*. This leads to the *Gemara*'s second and preferred answer.

### Marriage Prerequisite

*Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak* maintains: *le-olam avdo* — take the case at face value; it is indeed about the male slave. The *Gemara* then asks: but the arrangement is permitted, so why do the *Chachamim* consider it *esnan zonah*? The answer: *hacha be-mai askinan ke-gon de-lait lei isha u-vanim* — the case involves a Hebrew slave who did not already have a wife and children. A *baraita* teaches: if he had no wife and children, his master cannot provide him with a female Canaanite slave; if he did have a wife and children, he may. The reasoning appears to be that servitude should not create new opportunities — that a man who entered slavery without a family should not acquire a sexual life he did not have before. When the slave lacks the prerequisite of wife and children, the arrangement is forbidden, and the payment for facilitating it therefore falls within the parameters of *esnan zonah*.

### Rabbi Eliezer

*Rabbi* disagrees with this qualification. According to *Rabbi*, a master may always provide his Hebrew slave with a female slave, regardless of whether the slave had previously been married with children. Consequently, since the arrangement is entirely permitted, the payment cannot constitute *esnan zonah*. The debate between the *Chachamim* and *Rabbi* is thus not primarily about the parameters of *esnan zonah* itself, but about the *halacha* governing when a master may arrange this cohabitation — and the ruling on *esnan zonah* follows automatically from that determination. Once the act is forbidden, it retains the character of *zenus* and the payment remains *esnan zonah*, even though in other circumstances the very same act would be permitted.

## Mechir Kelev

### Mechir Kelev Intro

The *mishnah* turns to the second category mentioned in the *pasuk*: *ve-eizehu mechir kelev* — what is *mechir kelev*, an animal exchanged for a dog? *Ha-omer la-chaveiro holeich li zeh tachat kelev* — if someone says to a friend: bring me this animal in exchange for my dog. The animal that was swapped for the dog cannot be brought as a *korban*. Notably, if the dog were sold for money and the money used to purchase a sheep, that would be permitted; it is specifically the direct exchange of an animal for a dog that creates the prohibition. Money is not subject to this restriction; only the object itself.

### Partnership Division

The *mishnah* extends this to a less direct scenario: *ve-chen shenei shutafin she-chalaku* — partners who divide up a shared flock. If one partner takes ten sheep and the other takes nine sheep and a dog, then the sheep in the group of ten are forbidden, because one of them effectively corresponds to the dog. The nine sheep that are in the same group as the dog are permitted, since they were not given in exchange for it. Even though no single specific sheep was formally identified as the one exchanged for the dog, the ten-sheep group is treated as *mechir kelev* because one of those sheep was the implicit counterpart.

### Category Swapping

The *mishnah* introduces a related ruling: *etnan kelev u-mechir zona harei elu mutarim* — if one switches the categories, combining *etnan* with dog and *mechir* with *zona*, the result is permitted. That is, if someone pays for relations with a dog (*etnan kelev*), or if someone pays not for the act but for the *zonah* herself — purchasing her as a slave for purposes of relations (*mechir zona*) — the payment is not subject to the prohibitions of *esnan zonah* or *mechir kelev*. The Torah forbids specifically *esnan zonah* and *mechir kelev*; hybrid categories are not included. The source for this is the word *sheneihem* — "the two of them": *shnayim velo arba*, only two, not four.

### Offspring Permitted

The *mishnah* further states: *ve-vladoteihen mutarin* — the offspring of animals in these forbidden categories are permitted. The source is the suffix *hem* in the word *sheneihem*: *hem velo vladoteihen* — they themselves are forbidden, not their offspring. The prohibition attaches to the specific objects, not to what derives from them.

## Mechir vs. Etnan

### Exchange Not Sale

The *Gemara* explains the word *mechir kelev*: *zehu chalifei kelev* — *mechir kelev* means something exchanged for a dog, not something sold. This is derived from a verse in which *mechir* is used to denote exchange: *ki machar amcha belo hon* — your nation is sold without wealth, without monetary value, like an exchange transaction. Although *mechir* with a *kaf* typically means a monetary sale, *mechir* here (with a *chet*) denotes exchange. Moreover, this confirms the general principle that only the object itself is forbidden — not money derived from it, not proceeds. If a dog is sold for money and that money used to buy a sheep, the sheep is perfectly permitted. The prohibition is specific to the object received directly in exchange.

### Etnan Kelev Query

The *Gemara* then raises a question: *eima etnan kelev* — why not read *mechir kelev* as also encompassing *etnan kelev*, payment for relations with a dog? If one pays a dog for sexual purposes (as the *mishnah* already clarified is not *zenut* in the formal sense), should not such a payment also be forbidden? According to some textual variants, the question arises from the conjunction *vav* in the *pasuk* — *esnan zona u-mechir kelev* — suggesting that *mechir kelev* might subsume both the exchange concept and a payment concept. The *Gemara* further asks: why not simply read *mechir kelev* as meaning payment for sex with the dog, in parallel to *esnan zona* meaning payment for sex with a *zonah*? If so, the *pasuk* would read: payment for a *zonah* or for a dog.

### Separate Categories

The *Gemara* rejects this. *Im ken neima kra lo tavi esnan zona ve-chelev* — if the *pasuk* meant to address payment for sex with a dog, it would simply have said *esnan zona ve-chelev*, payment for a *zonah* and for a dog. Since the *pasuk* uses the distinct formulation *mechir kelev* — a separate term — *shma mina* that it is an entirely distinct category: one is payment for sex with a *zonah*, the other is an animal exchanged for a dog. The two categories have independent definitions and cannot be conflated.

This also addresses the question about the partnership division. When the ten sheep are opposite the dog, the *Gemara* asks why they are all forbidden: *nipik chada le-hai kalba ve-hanach kulehu lishtero* — one sheep corresponds to the dog, and the other nine should be permitted. *Tosafot* addresses this through the lens of *yesh breira*: the principle that a retroactive designation can clarify which item was always the relevant one. Where there was never any particular sheep that was specifically designated as corresponding to the dog, one could later designate one sheep as that item — *yesh breira* — and let the other nine remain permitted. However, the *Gemara* resolves the case differently. *Hacha be-mai askinan ke-gon de-tafei dmei kelev mi-chad mineihu* — the case is one where the dog is worth more than any single sheep. If the dog is worth more than any individual sheep in the ten-sheep group, then no one sheep is a precise equivalent of the dog; an overage remains, and that overage must be assumed distributed across all the sheep in the group. Since no single sheep can fully account for the dog's value, the entire group is tainted and all ten are forbidden as *mechir kelev*.

### No Zenut Animals

The *Gemara* records a question posed by *Rava mi-Parzikya* to *Rav Ashi*: *ein zenut be-vehema* — there is no *zenut* with an animal. The act is certainly forbidden and carries capital liability, but one does not apply the designation of *zenut* or *zonah* to a human being who has relations with an animal. The practical consequence is that payment for such an act is not *esnan zonah*. How do we know not to characterize it as *zenut*? Because the *pasuk* never uses the concept of *esnan* together with a dog in the sense of payment for sexual relations — it only speaks of *mechir kelev*, an exchange for a dog. Since the Torah never formulated *etnan kelev* as a prohibited category, this confirms that relations with an animal do not bear the characterization of *zenut*. A *baraita* similarly teaches: *minayin le-etnan kelev u-mechir zona she-hutran* — how do we know that the switched categories are permitted? *Shene'emar shnayim, shnayim velo arba* — only two, not four.

A note on the *pasuk* itself: *lo tavi esnan zona u-mechir kelev beit Hashem Elokecha le-chol neder ki to'avat Hashem Elokecha gam sheneihem* — "You shall not bring the payment of a *zonah* or the price of a dog to the house of the Lord your God for any vow, for both of them are an abomination to the Lord your God." The prohibition applies to bringing these as offerings or vows in the *Beit HaMikdash*.

## Offspring & Transform

### Offspring Rule

*Kol ha-asurim le-gabei mizbei'ach veladoseihem mutarim* — all animals forbidden to be brought as offerings, their offspring are permitted. This is the general rule. This encompasses not only *esnan zonah* and *mechir kelev* but also the other categories with which the *perek* opened: an animal born by caesarean section, an *androgynos*, a *ne'evad* (an animal worshipped as an idol), a *nirva* (an animal used in forbidden relations) — in all these cases, the offspring are permitted.

*Rava* adds a set of important qualifications. *Vlad nirva'at asura* — if an animal that was pregnant when the forbidden act occurred was used in relations, the fetus is also forbidden, based on the principle *ubar yerech imo* — the fetus is considered a limb of the mother. The act was done to the mother and thus, simultaneously, to the fetus. Similarly, *hi ve-valada nirbe'u* — the act was as though performed on both. *Nogach asura* — likewise, if a pregnant animal gored and incurred the *din* of *shor ha-niskal*, the fetus that was in its womb at the time is also forbidden, since it was a limb of the goring animal.

However, *vlad muktze ve-ne'evad mutar* — if an animal was worshipped as an idol or designated as *muktze* for idolatry and was pregnant at the time, the offspring is nonetheless permitted. The reason: *le-imei aktzei, le-imei palchei* — when the person worshipped or designated the animal, they had in mind only the mother, not the fetus. Unlike the physical act of relations or goring, which is done to the animal as a whole and therefore includes the fetus as its limb, the act of worship is a mental act directed at the animal itself; the worshipper did not contemplate the fetus, and therefore the fetus does not acquire the forbidden status.

*Ika de-amri* — some say the opposite: *vlad muktze ve-ne'evad assur*. Why? *De-nicha lei be-nefichei* — because a person actively wants the animal to be large and full when it is worshipped or presented as an offering to an idol; the pregnancy itself makes the animal more impressive, and it is therefore part of what is being worshipped. Thus the fetus is included.

The *Gemara* adds a related ruling from *Rav Acha de-vei Bar Ami* in the name of *Rav*: one who betroths a woman with the dung of a *shor ha-niskal* — an ox condemned to be stoned — she is *mekudeshet*, because the dung is not included in the prohibition of *issur hana'ah* that applies to the ox itself. But one who betroths a woman with the dung of animals worshipped as idols — *eina mekudeshet*, she is not betrothed, because those are *issurei hana'ah*. The *sevara*: by *avoda zara* one wants the animal to appear large and well-fed — its digesting food, which will become dung, is part of what the worshipper values — and therefore even the dung carries the *issur hana'ah* status. By *shor ha-niskal*, no one has any interest in the contents of the animal's stomach, and the dung is therefore not part of the forbidden category. The *pasuk*-based derivation reinforces this: by *avoda zara* it is written *ve-hayita cherem kamo-hu* — anything that derives from it has its same status; by *shor ha-niskal* it is written *lo ye'achel et besaro* — only the meat is forbidden, and *besaro assur persho mutar* — the meat is forbidden, the dung is permitted.

### Rabbi Eliezer Treifa

*Rabbi Eliezer* registers a disagreement with the general rule that offspring of forbidden animals are permitted: *vlad treifa lo yikrav le-gabei mizbei'ach* — the offspring of a *treifa* animal may not be brought as a *korban*. Whether such an animal may be eaten is a separate question that the *Gemara* will address.

### Nursing Treifa

*Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos* extends the stringency further: *kesheira she-yanaka min ha-treifa pesulah me'al gabei mizbei'ach* — if a healthy, *kesheira* animal nursed from a *treifa* animal, it becomes *pasul* for the altar. The milk of a *treifa* is itself forbidden, and an animal that was raised on *treifa* milk is thereby disqualified from being brought as a *korban*. This is not merely about the milk currently in the animal's stomach at a given moment, but about the animal having been raised on such milk at any point.

### Beit Shammai/Hillel

The *mishnah* also addresses transformations of the payment itself. *Nasan la chitin va-asan soles* — if the *esnan* was grain and she turned it into flour; *zeitim va-asan shemen* — olives she made into oil; *anavim va-asan yayin* — grapes she made into wine. One *baraita* says such transformed products are forbidden; another says permitted. These are commodities that would themselves be brought on the *mizbei'ach* — wine, oil, and flour — but they have now undergone a transformation from their original form.

*Rav Yosef* reports a tradition in the name of *Guryan from Asporak*: this dispute reflects the views of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*. *Beit Shammai osrin u-Beit Hillel matirin* — *Beit Shammai* forbids, *Beit Hillel* permits. The reasoning: *Beit Hillel savri hem* — the verse says "they," *velo vladeihem* — not their offspring, *velo shinuyeihem* — and not their transformations. The word *hem* excludes offspring and equally excludes a transformed product; the thing has become something new. *Beit Shammai* counters: *hem velo vladeihem* I agree; but the word *gam* in *gam sheneihem* — "even both of them" — is an inclusion: *gam le-rabbot shinuyeihem* — to include even their transformed versions. The thing itself in a different form remains forbidden. *Beit Hillel*'s difficulty with the word *gam* is noted but left unresolved. This is a classic *sugyah* cited also in *Bava Kamma* in the broader discussion of whether *shinuy* — transformation — converts a stolen or prohibited object into something new.

### Temple Structure

A further question concerns using the *esnan* not as a *korban* but as a structural addition to the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *baraita* states: *beit Hashem Elokecha* — to the house of God. *Prat le-parah she-eina ba'ah la-bayit divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — this comes to exclude a *Parah Adumah*, which is not brought into the *Beit HaMikdash* proper. Thus according to *Rabbi Eliezer*, an animal that was *esnan zonah* but was designated as a *Parah Adumah* would not be subject to the prohibition.

The *Chachamim* disagree, reading the phrase to include *le-rabbot et ha-vituyin* — to extend the prohibition to *vituyin*, structural additions to the *Beit HaMikdash* such as gold plating or other decorative construction. Even if an object is not brought as a *korban* but is used to add to the physical structure of the *Beit HaMikdash*, it would still be subject to the prohibition.

*Rav Chisda* identifies the *Chachamim* in this position as specifically *Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehudah* — this is not the majority opinion, and it is not the view of our *mishnah*, which emphasized only a *korban*. A *baraita* specifies: *natan lah zahav — Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehudah omer ein osim vituyin* — if he gave her gold, one may not use it as any form of gold covering or plating, *afilu achorei Beit ha-Kapporet* — even the back area behind the *Kodesh ha-Kodashim*, which is comparatively less sanctified. Anywhere in the structure of the Temple is included in his view. Giving the proceeds to the Temple treasury for operating expenses (*bedek ha-bayit* in the general sense) is permitted; it is only structural incorporation of the object itself that *Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehudah* extends the prohibition to.

## Sanctified Objects

### Pre-Sanctified

The *mishnah* rules: *natan la mukdashim harei hem mutarin* — if a man gave a woman a consecrated animal as payment, the *korban* is not thereby tainted with *esnan* status. The *Gemara* will ask how such a transaction was even possible, since a *korban* does not belong to its owner to give away freely; nonetheless the principle is established that an already-sanctified object cannot acquire the status of *esnan* or *mechir*.

The *mishnah* then observes that birds, by contrast, become forbidden as *esnan*. The *mishnah* itself acknowledges that one might have argued the opposite: *ma im mukdashim she-ha-mum posel bahen ein esnan u-mechir chal aleihem* — if consecrated animals, where a *mum* invalidates them, nevertheless cannot become *esnan* or *mechir*, then birds, where a *mum* does not invalidate them — *of she-ein ha-mum posel bo* — should certainly not become *esnan*. The *kal va-chomer* seems compelling, since birds lack the concept of blemish-based disqualification entirely. The answer is derived from the words *le-chol neder* — "for any vow" — *le-havi et ha-of*, to include birds. The prohibition is not coextensive with the categories of *mum*; it extends to any object that can be vowed as a *korban*, including birds.

### Korban Pesach Payment

The *Gemara* addresses the question of how one might have ever thought a sanctified animal could become *esnan*. *Ve-lav mamono hu* — it is not his property to give. *Rav Oshaya* provides the answer: *bi-maneh al picho* — in the case of a *Korban Pesach*. Even after a *Korban Pesach* has been sanctified, a person may add additional participants to it; the group assigned to eat the *Pesach* can be expanded. A man could therefore go to a *zonah* and say: if you have relations with me, I will include you in my *Korban Pesach*. She would thereby acquire a share in a consecrated object, and one might have thought that her ownership share constitutes an *esnan* that transforms the *korban* into a *korban* she received as payment for her services. The verse *le-chol neder* excludes this: *lehotzi et ha-nadur* — to exclude that which is already vowed. A pre-existing *korban* cannot become *esnan* or *mechir*.

### Business Permission

The *Gemara* identifies the opinion underlying this ruling as that of *Rabbi*. A *baraita* is cited: *ve-im yimat ha-bayit mi-hyot mi-seh* — if the household is too small for the sheep, one may add more members. The *Gemara* derives from this that one may add people to a *Korban Pesach* for a fee if needed to support the *avodah* itself — *mi-chdei achila* — for the purpose of eating the *Pesach*. *Rabbi* goes further: *af mi-chdei mekach* — even simply for business purposes, for cash needed for a commercial opportunity, one may add participants to one's *Korban Pesach* and to one's *Korban Chagiga* and keep the proceeds as *chulin*. *She-ha-maneh et ki-sh'yisrael pischeihem* — when one sanctifies a *Korban Pesach*, there is an implicit understanding that the owner retains the right to add participants; this is part of the nature of the *Pesach*, and the proceeds remain ordinary unconsecrated money. Accordingly, one could in principle make a *zonah* a participant in one's *Korban Pesach* in exchange for her services — the transaction being valid from the *Pesach* perspective because *Rabbi* permits adding participants for business purposes. Nevertheless, the already-sanctified *korban* does not thereby become *esnan zonah*, since the verse excludes objects that were already consecrated before the transaction.
