# Temurah 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nikbarim Intro

### Kodshim Comparison

The final daf of Maseches Temurah — daf 34 — begins with the Mishnah found at the bottom of 33b. Throughout the preceding discussion, we have been occupied with comparisons between *kodshei mizbe'ach* and *kodshei bedek ha-bayit*. This concluding Mishnah likewise has the character of a summary, yet its scope is considerably broader than the *kedushah*-centered distinctions that preceded it. Rather than focusing narrowly on issues of sanctity, it introduces a comprehensive taxonomy of forbidden objects and the prescribed method for their destruction.

### Miscarriage & Placenta

The Mishnah opens: *ve-elu hen ha-nikbarim* — the following are things that must be buried. The context flows naturally from the preceding Mishnah, which discussed which *kodshim* animals that have died may be redeemed and which may not. When redemption is unavailable, burial is required. Accordingly, the first case is *kodshim she-hipilu* — a *korban* animal that miscarried. Such an animal is treated like a *korban* that died: it cannot be redeemed and must therefore be buried. Underlying this ruling is an implicit assumption about the *kedushah* of a fetus in the womb. If a fetus acquires *kedushah* only upon birth, then a miscarriage would not generate this obligation. The Mishnah may therefore reflect the view that the fetus is *kadosh* while still in the mother's womb, or alternatively it addresses a case where the owner sanctified the animal after it was already pregnant, thereby directly conferring *kedushah* upon the fetus. In any case, the operative principle is *ein podi'in et ha-kodshim le-ha'achilam la-klavim* — one does not redeem *kodshim* in order to feed them to the dogs — and since the animal cannot be made alive again, burial is mandated. Similarly, *hipilah shilya tikaver*: if a consecrated animal produced only a placenta and no visible fetus, the placenta must also be buried, since we presume the presence of a fetus.

### Asurim be-Hana'ah

From the transition case of a dead *korban*, the Mishnah moves to the broader and more general category: things that are *asurim be-hana'ah* — forbidden in benefit. The question with such items is not merely that they cannot be eaten; because one may not derive any benefit from them at all, they cannot simply be left lying around. They must be disposed of. The Mishnah's answer, at least as a default, is burial.

### Forbidden Items List

The Mishnah enumerates: *shor ha-niskal* — an ox that was stoned because it killed a person; *eglah arufah* — the calf whose neck was broken in the ritual performed when a murder victim's killer is unknown; *tzipporei metzora* — the birds used in the purification ritual of a *metzora*. Regarding the birds: only the one that was slaughtered is *asur be-hana'ah*, not the one that was sent free, since the Gemara elsewhere establishes that the Torah would never *shillach le-takalah* — send away an animal into a situation of danger or prohibition. Next: *se'ar nazir* — the hair of a *nazir* that is shaved at the conclusion of his period of *nezirus*, which is also *asur be-hana'ah*; *peter chamor* — the firstborn donkey, which, if the owner refuses to redeem it with a sheep, becomes *asur be-hana'ah* (as this Mishnah assumes, though this was itself a subject of discussion in Maseches Bechoros); *basar be-chalav* — meat cooked with milk, a classic case of *issurei hana'ah*; and finally, *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah* — non-consecrated animals slaughtered in the Temple courtyard.

### Chullin ba-Azarah

This last case, *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah*, is the most legally complex entry in the list, and it is worth dwelling on it. There is an extensive discussion in the Gemara about whether the prohibition of *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah* is of biblical or rabbinic origin. Even if one accepts that it is *de-oraisa*, that likely refers to the prohibition of performing such slaughter, not necessarily to the resulting object being intrinsically forbidden. The question of why the *object itself* becomes *asur be-hana'ah* is therefore far from obvious.

Rashi explains: *mishum de-michlefi behu kodshim she-ira behu pasul* — because someone who sees an animal slaughtered within the *azarah* and realizes it is not part of a *korban* will naturally assume that it must be an invalid *korban*. And the general rule for disqualified *korbanot* is that they must be burned. If this *chullin* animal is treated as freely consumable, it will be confused with *psulei kodshim*, leading to improper conduct.

The Mishnah then records a dispute: *Rabbi Shimon omer chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah yisarefu* — Rabbi Shimon holds that such animals must be burned precisely because they will be confused with invalid *korbanot*, and invalid *korbanot* are burned. The first opinion, the *tanna kamma*, holds only that burial is required, not burning. The Gemara even extends Rabbi Shimon's position: *ve-chen chayah she-nishchatah ba-azarah* — even a non-domesticated animal, or indeed a pig or camel slaughtered in the *azarah*, would according to Rabbi Shimon receive this treatment. Since the governing concern is the act of slaughter in the *azarah*, we treat anything slaughtered there as though it were a *psul korban*, regardless of whether it could ever actually have been a valid *korban*.

A practical question arises: does the obligation of burial mean that one is literally required to dig a hole? The answer is that burial in this context means one should not simply leave the object available; other forms of destruction would be permissible as well, as the Mishnah will clarify shortly.

## Nisrafin Overview

### Things Burned Intro

The Mishnah continues: *ve-elu hen ha-nisrafin* — the following are things that must be burned. This is an important counterpoint to the preceding list. While *issurei hana'ah* in general are disposed of by burial, there are specific cases where the Torah mandates burning as the mode of destruction. The Mishnah proceeds to enumerate them.

### Chametz be-Pesach

The first item is *chametz be-Pesach* — leavened bread on Passover — which must be burned: *yisaref*. This reflects the opinion of one school of thought; as discussed in Maseches Pesachim, there is actually a dispute about whether *tashbitu be-chol davar* — "you shall eliminate" leaven by any means — or whether burning specifically is required, learned by analogy from *notar*. The *stam* of this Mishnah follows the view that burning is mandatory.

### Trumah Teme'ah

The next case is *trumah teme'ah* — *terumah* that has become ritually impure. This is a notably unusual category. Unlike the other items on the list, *trumah teme'ah* is not technically *asur be-hana'ah* in the classic sense. What exists here is a *mitzvah* to destroy it by burning, combined with a special allowance: one may derive *hana'ah* — benefit — from the heat and light *as it burns*. One might ask whether a *kohen* could simply feed *trumah teme'ah* to his animals, which would also constitute a form of disposal. The Gemara's position is that feeding it to animals is considered an act of deriving benefit from it rather than an act of destroying it. The technical violation is not the classic *issur hana'ah* but rather a failure to fulfill the *mitzvah* of destruction in the proper manner. The question of why using it as a fuel source — *or* as a type of air freshener, as the Gemara elsewhere establishes regarding *trumah teme'ah* wine — is categorically different from feeding it to animals is admittedly a subtle point that merits further investigation.

### Orlah & Kil'ei

The Mishnah adds *orlah* and *kil'ei ha-kerem* — produce of the first three years of a fruit tree and produce of mixed species planted in a vineyard, respectively — as further instances where the Torah mandates burning. The derivation for *kil'ei ha-kerem* comes from the verse *pen tikdash ha-mele'ah ha-zera asher tizra*, read through the homiletical lens of *tikdash tukad esh* — "it becomes holy" meaning "it must be set ablaze." The connection to *orlah* is derived by the Gemara through a principle that *orlah* and *kil'ayim* are learned from one another — *gamri me-hadadi* — though Rashi notes this linkage.

### Solid vs Liquid

The Mishnah then states: *et she-darkan li-saref yisaref, et she-darkan le-kaver yikaver* — that which is normally burned shall be burned, that which is normally buried shall be buried. One might wonder what practical work this rule performs given what the Mishnah has already stated. The answer is that this clause addresses liquids. One cannot practically burn a liquid that is not flammable. If one has wine of *orlah*, for example, or other non-oil liquid *issurei hana'ah*, burning is not a viable option. Such items are therefore buried. Oil, being flammable, would still be burned. The rule of *et she-darkan* maps the abstract obligation onto the physical reality of what can and cannot be incinerated.

### Bread & Oil Usage

The Mishnah concludes this section: *umadlikin be-fas be-shemen shel terumah* — one may use bread or oil of *trumah teme'ah* as fuel. This is the direct application of the principle just stated: since bread and oil are items that can in practice be burned, they are burned, and by the earlier rule, one may derive benefit from the heat and light produced as they are being consumed. The precise reason the Mishnah adds the case of bread (*pas*) alongside oil is not entirely clear, but the practical teaching is explicit.

## Psulei Kodshim

### Chutz mi-Zman

The Mishnah continues: *kol ha-kodashim she-nishchatu chutz mi-zmanam ve-chutz mi-mkomaum* — all sacrifices that were slaughtered with improper intention regarding time or place must be burned. This is somewhat implicit in what was discussed regarding *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah*, but here it is stated explicitly as a general rule. The case here is not *shechutei chutz* — animals slaughtered outside the Temple precincts entirely — but rather *korbanot* slaughtered within the *azarah* while the officiant had in mind to eat the sacrifice after its permitted time (*chutz mi-zmano*, the basis of *pigul*) or outside its designated location (*chutz mi-mkomo*). The broader principle, established by the Gemara in Pesachim, is *kol davar she-psulo be-hekdesh nisraf* — anything whose disqualification occurs within the realm of the sacred is burned. The Torah states explicitly about *notar*, *ve-ha-notar ba-esh yisarefu*, and from this the rule is extended to all *psulei korbanot*.

### Asham Talui

The Mishnah proceeds to a more complex and striking case: *asham talui yisaref*. The *asham talui* is a guilt offering brought by one who is uncertain whether he committed a sin for which a *chatat* would normally be required — for example, someone who may have accidentally consumed forbidden *chelev* but is not certain. This offering provides provisional atonement while the matter remains unresolved.

Now suppose this person brought the *asham talui*, the animal was slaughtered and offered, but before the *kohanim* could eat the meat, it became clear that he had *not* in fact sinned. The entire offering was therefore unnecessary. What is the status of the meat? Since there was no sin it corresponded to, retroactively this is *chullin* — non-consecrated meat slaughtered in the *azarah*, the paradigm of *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah*.

Yet there is a crucial difference from ordinary *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah*: at the time of slaughter, this animal genuinely possessed the *kedushah* of a *korban*. It was only retroactively, upon the clarification of facts, that its necessity evaporated. This proximity to genuine *psulei kodshim* means that even the *tanna kamma*, who in the standard case of *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah* required only burial, agrees here that the meat must be burned: it truly resembles a *korban* that became *pasul*. Nevertheless, *Rabbi Yehuda omer yikaver*: Rabbi Yehuda maintains that since it is ultimately *chullin* — the sin never having been committed — it should be buried, not burned.

### Chatat ha-Of

The Mishnah then addresses *chatat ha-of ha-ba'ah al ha-safek* — a bird *chatat* brought on the basis of uncertainty. The case involves a woman who is uncertain whether she gave birth to a viable child that obligates the bringing of a *korban* to permit her re-entry into the Temple and consumption of *kodshim*. Failing to bring the offering would leave her permanently excluded. Bringing it risks the category of *chullin ba-azarah* if no obligation existed. The Gemara learns from a *pasuk* that one may bring such a *korban* under conditions of doubt.

After *melikah* — the ritual nipping of the bird's neck — is performed, if the offering was in fact not obligatory, the bird is a *neveilah* and cannot be eaten. What is to be done with it? The Mishnah rules *tisaref*: it is burned, like a *korban pasul*. Rabbi Yehuda, however, says *yatilena la-amah* — cast it into the *amah*, the water channel that ran through the Temple courtyard, and cut it up limb by limb so that it flows out into Nachal Kidron. The reasoning behind Rabbi Yehuda's position here may be nuanced: since there remains a possibility that this *was* a genuine *korban*, actively destroying it in the manner one would treat *psulei kodshim* might itself be problematic. Disposing of it through the *amah*, a more passive mode of removal, avoids this concern. If the offering was indeed a valid *korban*, one has not actively violated it; if it was not, it has been removed. The question of why Rabbi Yehuda does not simply say *yikaver* here, as he did in the case of the *asham talui*, is left by the Gemara without explicit treatment, though it seems plausible that the *amah* disposal functions as a variant form of burial well-suited to the Temple setting.

## Burial/Burning Rule

### Don't Burn Buried

The Mishnah now states the overarching rule: *kol ha-nikbarim lo yisarefu* — everything that must be buried must not be burned. The Gemara asks the reason. The answer that will emerge is: *mishum de-nikbarin afran assur* — because items that must be buried retain forbidden status even after they are reduced to ashes. If one were permitted to burn such items, observers might reason that since they have now been burned, their ashes are surely permissible, applying the leniency that pertains to items that were *required* to be burned. But that leniency does not apply here. Hence: do not burn things that must be buried, lest someone mistakenly treat the resulting ashes as permissible.

### Don't Bury Burned

The converse rule — *ve-kol ha-nisrafin lo yikaveru* — everything that must be burned must not be buried — is, at first glance, more straightforward. If there is a *mitzvah* to burn something, one is obligated to fulfill that *mitzvah*; burying it instead would simply constitute a failure to perform the requisite act of destruction. Rashi, somewhat surprisingly, does not invoke this simple reasoning. Instead, Rashi says: *dilma ati inish ve-ashkechei ve-achlei* — lest someone happen upon the buried item, uncover it, and eat it. Burning is more thorough a mode of destruction than burial, and we are therefore concerned that a buried item will not be fully destroyed and will be misused. This Rashi is admittedly a difficult one, and the *acharonim* find it challenging: if the concern is insufficiency of destruction, why is that concern not equally applicable to items that must be buried? The straightforward reason — there is a *mitzvah* to burn — would seem sufficient.

The underlying logic that emerges from the Mishnah is therefore: items that must be buried have no Torah-mandated act of destruction, so virtually any form of elimination is acceptable, but do not burn them, because then their ashes would be mistakenly treated as *mutar*. Items that must be burned have a specific *mitzvah* to burn, so burning is required and one may not substitute another method.

### Rabbi Yehuda

*Rabbi Yehuda omer: im ratza le-hachmir al atzmo lisrof et ha-nikbar rashai* — Rabbi Yehuda rules that if one wishes to be stringent and subject things designated for burial to the more thorough destruction of burning, one is permitted to do so. The more complete physical destruction eliminates all possibility of misuse. *Amru lo: ein mutar le-shanot* — the Sages respond: it is not permitted to deviate from the established categories. Maintaining clear categorical boundaries prevents confusion between the two regimes. If one blurs the line in one direction, one risks blurring it in the other as well.

## Se'ar Nazir Debate

### Contradiction Raised

The Gemara now turns to the Mishnah's ruling that *se'ar nazir* must be buried, and Tavi raises a sharp contradiction before Rav Nachman. The Mishnah teaches *se'ar nazir yikaver* — the *nazir*'s hair is buried. Yet a Mishnah in Avodah Zarah states: *ha-oreg melo ha-sit mi-tzemer bechor be-veged yidaleik ha-veged* — if one weaves into a garment even a *melo ha-sit* (the span from thumbnail to forefinger) of wool from a *bechor* — a firstborn animal whose shearing is forbidden and whose wool is *asur be-hana'ah* — the garment must be burned. The Mishnah in Avodah Zarah then continues: *mi-se'ar ha-nazir u-feter chamor be-sak, yidaleik ha-sak* — if one weaves the hair of a *nazir* or the hair of a firstborn donkey into a sackcloth, the sackcloth must be burned. Here the text says *burn*, not *bury*. Why the discrepancy?

### Nazir Tahor/Tamei

The first answer offered is: *kan be-nazir tamei, kan be-nazir tahor* — the two sources are addressing different cases. When a *nazir* is *tahor* and completes his *nezirus*, the Torah explicitly prescribes what is done with his shaved hair: it is burned beneath the meat of the *korban* being cooked, as part of the ritual in the *Beit HaMikdash*. This means that for a *nazir tahor*, the Torah has positively commanded *sherifah* — burning — of the hair. The *nazir*'s hair therefore possesses an intrinsic obligation of burning. This point is made by Rav Velvel (the Griz), who argues that the *mitzvah* of burning the *nazir tahor*'s hair is not merely a procedural component of the ritual but reflects a *din* of the object itself: the Torah declares this hair to be subject to *sherifah*, even if for some reason it was not burned in the Temple context. When the hair is thus intrinsically obligated in burning, the Mishnah in Avodah Zarah's ruling to burn it makes sense. When, by contrast, the *nazir* is *tamei* and must shave but there is no ritual of burning prescribed by the Torah, the hair falls under the default category of *issurei hana'ah* designated for burial, and that is the case our Mishnah addresses.

### Peter Chamor Issue

The answer regarding *nazir* is acknowledged as a valid distinction. But the questioner presses: *peter chamor a-peter chamor kashya* — what about the firstborn donkey? Our Mishnah says bury it; the Mishnah in Avodah Zarah says burn the sackcloth woven from its hair. There is no *mitzvas sherifah* by *peter chamor* analogous to that of *nazir tahor*. The prohibition on *peter chamor* is simply *issurei hana'ah*, with no Torah-mandated burning. Rav Nachman has no response. He was silent.

### Hair vs Sack

Then Rav Nachman asked Tavi whether he had heard an answer to this, and Tavi reported what Rav Sheshes had told him: *kan be-sak, kan be-se'ar* — the distinction is not about which type of *nazir* or which *issurei hana'ah*, but about whether one is dealing with raw hair or a finished *sak*. If the hair is simply buried as hair, even if it were accidentally uncovered, the likelihood that anyone would derive benefit from loose, forbidden hair is low. But if it has been woven into a usable sackcloth and is then buried, the risk is substantial: someone will uncover a perfectly serviceable sack and use it. Under those circumstances we set aside the normal rule against burning items designated for burial and require burning to ensure genuine destruction. This answer applies to *peter chamor* as much as to *nazir*: in both cases, when the hair has been woven into a *sak*, we require burning.

### Rabbi Yose & Eliezer

This answer is corroborated: *amar Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina* — Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina independently said the same: when it is a *sak*, burn it; when it is mere hair, bury it. Rabbi Eliezer, however, reverts to the first answer — burn when it is *nazir tahor*, bury when it is *nazir tamei* — an answer that, as was noted, leaves the *peter chamor* case unresolved.

### Bitul be-Rov Q

The Gemara then raises a fresh question: *libatel se'ar be-rubba* — why is the forbidden hair not nullified (*batel*) by the majority of permitted material into which it is woven? If one strand of a *nazir*'s hair or a *peter chamor*'s hair is woven among a great many strands of permitted hair, it should be *batel be-rov* and the entire sackcloth should be permitted. The Gra notes that the word *sak* in this passage is textually awkward and removes it; the question is simply why the hair is not nullified in the majority.

### Recognition Pattern

Rav Pappa answers: *be-tzipporta* — because it was woven into a deliberate decorative pattern, such as a bird design. When the forbidden strand is incorporated into a recognizable design, its location is known; it is *nikkar* — identifiable — and therefore cannot be *batel*. The Gemara then asks: if you know exactly where it is, just remove that section — *tzipporta lishlefinhu* — and then only that piece need be destroyed. Why destroy the entire *sak*?

### Rabbi Yirmiya

Rabbi Yirmiya responds: *hani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this ruling reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who said that if one wishes to be stringent and burn items designated for burial, one may do so. The Gemara immediately challenges this: the prior question was simply why one need destroy the whole sack when one can remove the specific section — invoking Rabbi Yehuda's general *kulah* does not address that. Rabbi Yirmiya clarifies: if it is possible to remove the design, that is indeed the preferred option. But if it is not possible, then on the authority of Rabbi Yehuda one may burn the whole.

This leaves us with three distinct answers to the original question of why the Mishnah in Avodah Zarah prescribes burning rather than burial: first, that it is invoking Rabbi Yehuda's permission to burn rather than a strict obligation; second — and this is the strongest answer applicable to both *nazir* and *peter chamor* — that when forbidden hair has been woven into a functional object like a *sak*, the danger of misuse after burial is severe enough to require burning; and third, applicable only to the *nazir* case, that when a *nazir tahor*'s hair carries an intrinsic *mitzvas sherifah*, burning is mandated.

## Nisrafin List

### Chametz Burning

The Gemara now revisits the Mishnah's enumeration of *nisrafin*. The Mishnah's statement that *chametz be-Pesach yisaref* is identified as following the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who rules *ein biur chametz ela sherifah* — the destruction of chametz can only be accomplished through burning, learned by analogy from the laws of *notar*. This reflects a dispute about *biur chametz*, and the *stam* of the Mishnah adopts Rabbi Yehuda's view.

### Trumah Benefit

The Mishnah's inclusion of *trumah teme'ah* among things to be burned is revisited. As established earlier, this is a distinctive category: it is not *asur be-hana'ah* in the classic sense, but there is a *mitzvah* to destroy it by fire, and one may benefit from it in the course of that destruction.

### Orlah Burning

The cases of *orlah* and *kil'ei ha-kerem* are confirmed as instances where the Torah itself mandates burning, drawn from specific scriptural derivations as outlined in the Mishnah.

### Solids & Liquids

The Gemara spells out the practical meaning of *et she-darkan li-saref yisaref, et she-darkan le-kaver yikaver*: *ochlin bi-sreifah* — foods are burned; *mashkin bi-kvurah* — liquids that cannot practically be burned are buried. The Gemara notes that *trumah teme'ah* follows different rules from classic *issurei hana'ah*: wine of *trumah teme'ah* is not buried but rather dispersed as a kind of air freshener, a permitted form of benefit-in-destruction unique to its category.

## Ashes Status

### Reason for Rule

The Gemara now investigates the underlying principle stated in the Mishnah: *kol ha-nikbarim lo yisarefu*, and asks *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason?

### Ashes Principle

The Gemara's answer: *mishum de-nikbarin afran assur* — because items that must be buried have forbidden ashes, while items that must be burned have permitted ashes. This is the operative distinction. If one burns something that was designated for burial, observers will mistake the resulting ashes for those of items designated for burning — whose ashes are permitted — and will come to benefit from them improperly. Items that must be burned, once properly burned in fulfillment of the *mitzvah*, produce ashes that are *mutar*; items that must be buried, even if burned, produce ashes that remain *assur*. Hence the rule: do not cross categories.

## Mitzvah Principle

### Tosafot Mitzvah

Why should the status of ashes differ based on whether a *mitzvah* of destruction existed? Tosafot addresses this explicitly in the last Tosafot on 33b. Tosafot asks: *tzarich iyun ta'ama mai* — the reason requires investigation. Why should burning produce permitted ashes in the case of *nisrafin* but not in the case of *nikbarim*? Tosafot answers by invoking the concept of *na'asit mitzvato*: *keivan she-tzivah ha-katuv ve-serafan, achar she-asah ke-ilu na'asit mitzvato, ve-ein davar she-na'asit mitzvato mo'alin bo* — since the Torah commanded that it be burned, after the act is performed it is as though the *mitzvah* has been fulfilled, and one does not have the prohibition of *me'ilah* regarding something whose *mitzvah* has been completed.

### Torah Acknowledgment

The conceptual foundation is this: when the Torah prescribes a specific act of destruction — burn this object — the Torah is implicitly declaring that once that prescribed act is performed, the object is legally considered to have been eliminated. The Torah's prescription of the act constitutes a recognition that after the act, *this thing is done with*. What remains — the ashes — is therefore legally a new entity, not a transformed version of the original forbidden object. Conversely, when the Torah issues no command of destruction but merely says "do not derive benefit," it offers no mechanism for the object's legal elimination. If one on one's own initiative burns such an object, the Torah has never acknowledged that act as constitutive of the object's termination. The ashes are therefore treated as a modified form of the original object, and since that original object was *assur be-hana'ah*, the ashes remain so.

### Chemical Change

This conceptual framework connects to a broader question in halachah: how much physical transformation is needed before an object is considered legally distinct from what it was? This question arises, for example, in discussions about gelatin derived from pig bones, where chemical processing may render the substance physically unrecognizable. The question is always whether the degree of change is sufficient to constitute a different object. The principle here adds a further dimension: even radical physical transformation — reduction to ashes — may not be sufficient to create a new legal status unless the Torah itself has endorsed the act of transformation as constitutive. When there is a *mitzvah* of *sherifah*, the Torah endorses the burning as an act of legal termination; when there is not, no physical transformation achieves that result by itself. Some hold that even in the case of *nikbarim*, the prohibition on their ashes is only rabbinic — that biblically, ashes are indeed a different object — but the Gemara's operative framework is as described.

## Ashes Status…

### Drying Question

The Gemara probes the principle from several angles. It cites a *beraita* regarding *dam ha-niddah* and *basar ha-meit* — the blood of a *niddah* and the flesh of a corpse. Though these items remain *tamei* even if dried, the *beraita* states that if they become so desiccated that they crumble at the touch (*she-nifrechu*), they are *tehorim* — they no longer convey *tumah*. The Gemara asks: does *tehorim* mean they are now considered entirely a different object, such that they would also be *mutar be-hana'ah*? The Gemara responds: no — *tehorim va-asurin*. The law of *tumah* has a threshold based on the physical state of the object, and once that threshold is crossed, the *tum'ah* does not apply. But the prohibition of *hana'ah* is a separate matter; there, the object retains its status regardless of physical change, and it remains *assur be-hana'ah*.

### Sacrifice Remnants

A further challenge comes from the case of *olat ha-of she-nitmatzta dama* — a bird *olah* from which the blood has been fully squeezed out in the proper ritual manner. After this is done, the crop and feathers, which the Torah designates for removal to the side of the *mizbe'ach*, are stated to have gone *midei me'ilah* — out of the category of *me'ilah*. The Gemara asks: does *yatzu midei me'ilah* mean they are now fully permitted? The Gemara answers: *lo yatzu midei me'ilah va-asurin* — they have exited the prohibition of *me'ilah* but remain forbidden in benefit. The completion of the ritual act removes *me'ilah* specifically, but does not render them *mutar*. These remain two distinct legal categories.

### Ashes Exceptions

The Gemara then turns to examine the principle from the opposite direction: is it really true that when there is a *mitzvah* to burn something, the ashes are *mutar*? A *beraita* is cited: *kol ha-nisrafin afran mutar* — all things that are burned, their ashes are permitted. But then exceptions follow: *chutz me-efer asherah ve-efer de-hekdesh le-olam assur* — except for the ashes of an *asherah* and the ashes of *hekdesh*, which are forever forbidden. This contradicts our Mishnah, which addressed *hekdesh* cases where burning was required and whose ashes should accordingly be *mutar*.

The Gemara pauses to ask a parenthetical question: why does the *beraita* list *asherah* and *hekdesh* as two separate entries rather than combining them in a single phrase? The answer: *mishum de-asherah yesh lah bitulah bi-vnei kochavim, hekdesh ein lo bitulah olamit* — because the laws differ. An *asherah* can be nullified if the gentiles who worshipped it formally renounce its status as *avodah zarah*, whereas *hekdesh* that became *pasul* can never be permissibilized through such nullification. Since their legal contours differ, they must be listed separately.

### Hekdesh Accident

The Gemara resolves the contradiction between the *beraita* and our Mishnah through Rami bar Chama: *kegon de-nafla deleika be-heichalei hekdesh me-eleha* — the *beraita*'s ruling that *hekdesh* ashes are forever forbidden refers specifically to a case where a fire broke out accidentally in the Temple treasury. The *hekdesh* was burned, but no human being performed the *mitzvah* of burning it. In such a case, the ashes remain forbidden, because the Torah's transformation-principle — *na'asit mitzvato* — never came into effect. The prescribed act of destruction was not performed; the burning happened on its own. Without the deliberate fulfillment of the *mitzvah* of *sherifah*, the ashes are treated as a modified form of the original object and remain *assur*.

The Gemara frames this with reference to *me'ilah* as well: since nobody performed an act of *me'ilah* — unauthorized benefit — the property did not become *chulin* through that mechanism either. There are thus two pathways by which *hekdesh* could become *mutar*: the transgression of *me'ilah*, which converts it to *chulin*, and the fulfillment of a *mitzvah* of *sherifah*, which renders the resulting ashes *mutar*. When the fire broke out by itself, neither pathway operated. Hence the ashes remain forbidden.

### Special Cases Only

Rav Shemaya offers an alternative resolution: *ki tanya ha-hi be-trumat ha-deshen tanya* — when the *beraita* says that certain *hekdesh* ashes are forever forbidden, it is not speaking about *korbanot* that became *pasul* and were burned in fulfillment of a *mitzvas sherifah*. Those ashes are indeed *mutar*. The reference is specifically to *trumat ha-deshen* — the daily removal of ash from atop the *mizbe'ach*, performed each morning by the *kohen*.

### Trumat Deshen

The ashes of *trumat ha-deshen* are forbidden by their own distinct law. The Torah commands *ve-samo* — he shall place it — referring to the placement of the removed ashes beside the ramp, and the Gemara derives from this verse that the ashes of *trumat ha-deshen* must remain in their designated place and may not be used for benefit. This is categorically unrelated to the general principle about *mitzvas sherifah* and its resulting ashes being *mutar*. *Trumat ha-deshen* represents an independent Torah prohibition on those particular ashes, and this is the case the *beraita*'s exception refers to.

One question left without explicit discussion in the Gemara is why the ashes of an *asherah* remain forbidden even though there is a *mitzvah* to destroy it. One of the *acharonim* offers an explanation based on the principle of *chalifei avodah zarah assur* — that the prohibition of *avodah zarah* extends to objects derived from or exchanged for the idol, not merely the idol itself. Just as money paid for an idol becomes forbidden, so too ashes derived from it — even if not the original object — carry the extended prohibition.

The upshot of the entire sugya: when the Torah mandates an act of destruction and that act is duly performed, the resulting ashes are considered a new entity and are *mutar*. When no such Torah-mandated act exists — whether because the item is simply *assur be-hana'ah* with no prescribed destruction, because the burning occurred without human agency, or because a special law uniquely governs the resulting ashes — the prohibition persists.
