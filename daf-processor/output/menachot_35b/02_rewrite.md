# Menachot 35b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Broken Strap & Tashmishe…

### Knot vs. Sewing

The daf opens with a scene involving Abaye and Rav Yosef. Abaye was seated before Rav Yosef when his *retzuah* broke. He asked Rav Yosef: may he repair it by knotting the two broken halves together? Rav Yosef replied with a verse: *ukshartem* — "you shall bind" — which carries the implication of *she-tehei keshira tama*, that the binding must be whole and unbroken. A strap repaired with an additional knot does not meet that standard, and therefore one may not fix a broken *retzuah* by knotting the broken halves.

### Interior Stitching

Rav Acha, son of Rav Yosef, then posed a related question to Rav Ashi: rather than knotting, what if one were to sew the break, placing the stitches on the inside of the strap so that they would be invisible from the outside? This would constitute a far more complete repair aesthetically and structurally.

### Puk Chazi Principle

Rav Ashi replied with the principle *puk chazi mai ama devar* — go out and see what the people do. Rashi understands this to mean that since people do not do this, it must be forbidden. Tosafot, by contrast, reads this principle as operating in the direction of leniency, since it is typically invoked to permit something based on widespread practice. Tosafot therefore concludes that since people do sew straps in this manner, it is permitted.

Tosafot's reading reflects his general assumption that *puk chazi mai ama devar* is a *kula* formulation. One might ask, however, whether the principle is even applicable in a situation that rarely arises in practice — if it never occurs, there is no observable custom to invoke in either direction. Rashi's position is similarly complicated: if the reason nobody does it is simply that the situation never arises, the absence of practice cannot serve as evidence of prohibition. It is worth noting as well that these discussions apply most acutely to thin straps that might tear easily; the thick leather used today makes such breaks far less common.

### Two Categories

The *gemara* now introduces a structural principle that has broad halakhic significance. Rav Papa states that *gardumei retzuot kesherot* — remnants of tefillin straps remain valid. The concept of *gardumim* assumes that there is a requisite minimum length to begin with, but if the strap started at the right length and subsequently broke or tore, leaving a shorter remnant, it is still acceptable — analogous to the rule regarding *tzitzit*, where a string that began at the proper length and later tore may still be used.

However, the *gemara* rejects Rav Papa's position. It invokes the teaching of the sons of Rabbi Chiyya that *gardumei techelet* and *gardumei ezov* are valid — the remnant of a *tzitzit* string or a hyssop bundle used for *Parah Adumah* or the purification of a *metzora* remains acceptable even if it has been reduced. But the *gemara* draws a distinction: *hatam hu detashmishei mitzvah ninhu* — those cases involve objects that are categorized as *tashmishei mitzvah*, things that serve a *mitzvah*, whereas *aval hacha detashmishei kedusha ninhu lo* — here, where tefillin straps are categorized as *tashmishei kedusha*, the lenient rule of *gardumim* does not apply.

This introduces a fundamental distinction between two categories of *mitzvah*-related objects. *Tashmishei mitzvah* are what we might call *mitzvah* objects — a *lulav*, a *shofar*, *tzitzit* strings — items that serve the performance of a *mitzvah* but do not possess intrinsic *kedusha*. Their significance derives from their use in the *mitzvah*, and once that use is complete, they are no longer governed by strict rules of sanctity. One may not discard them disrespectfully, and ideally one repurposes them for another *mitzvah* — using a *lulav* for fuel when burning *chametz*, for example — but there is no ongoing, inherent sanctity.

*Tashmishei kedusha*, by contrast, are objects associated with *kisei hakodesh* or, more specifically, with *shem Hashem*. Their sanctity is not contingent on their current use; it is intrinsic. That is why they require *sheimes* — storage in a geniza — once they can no longer be used, whereas one would never place a *lulav* in *sheimes*. Tefillin straps are *tashmishei kedusha* because they serve the *klafim*, the parchments inside the batim. And since they are *tashmishei kedusha*, a higher standard of completeness is demanded — the full length of the *retzuah* is required, and a mere *gardum* is not sufficient.

### Tefillin Status

The *gemara*'s application of this distinction here is, admittedly, not entirely intuitive. The question of whether *gardumim* suffice is, at its core, a question about *yetziat yedei mitzvah* — whether one fulfills the *mitzvah* of tefillin — which would seem to address tefillin in their dimension as *tashmishei mitzvah*, not as *tashmishei kedusha*. Why should the category of *tashmishei kedusha* determine whether a broken strap is still *kasher* for the *mitzvah*?

One way to understand the *gemara*'s reasoning is that the concept of *tashmishei kedusha* creates a heightened sense of belonging: the strap belongs to the tefillin in a stronger way than a *lulav* leaf belongs to the *lulav*. When something breaks off a *tashmishei mitzvah* object, it simply ceases to be part of it. But for a *tashmishei kedusha* object, the severed piece retains a stronger conceptual attachment, and the loss of that portion is correspondingly more significant. Under this reading, *gardumim* are problematic not because of a formal rule about length, but because the strap's sanctity demands wholeness. The *gemara*'s conclusion, in any case, is clear: for tefillin, *gardumim* are not acceptable, and the full minimum length of the *retzuah* is required.

It should be noted that tefillin are in fact both *tashmishei mitzvah* and *tashmishei kedusha* simultaneously — they are *mitzvah* objects in their own right, and they also serve the sacred *klafim* inside. This dual status will be relevant to subsequent discussions, including the question raised by Tosafot regarding the *bayit* of the *shel rosh* and its *shin*, which may occupy yet a third, higher level — closer to *kedusha* itself.

## Strap Length Requirements

### Minimum Shiur

The *gemara* draws an inference from the discussion of *gardumim*: since the entire topic presupposes that the strap began at a required length, it follows that there is indeed a minimum *shiur* — a minimum length — for the *retzuah*. The *gemara* then asks: what is that minimum?

### Finger Length Measure

Rabbi Bar Chama in the name of Resh Lakish answers: *ad etzba tzerada* — until the snapping finger. Rashi identifies *etzba tzerada* as the forefinger, while Tosafot objects that one does not snap with the forefinger and identifies it instead as the middle finger.

### Bent vs. Straight

Two Amoraim demonstrate different configurations. Rav Kahana demonstrated the measurement in a *kaphuf* position — bent — while Rav Ashi demonstrated it in a *pashut* position — straightened out.

### Two Interpretations

There are two distinct ways of understanding this measurement. The first explanation, accepted by both Rashi and Tosafot (though they debate which finger is intended), is that *ad etzba tzerada* refers simply to a unit of length — the span between the thumb and the forefinger according to Rashi, or the thumb and the middle finger according to Tosafot. On this reading, *kaphuf* means the finger is fully spread, yielding a longer measurement, while *pashut* means not fully spread, yielding roughly half that length. The *gemara* on this reading is discussing how long the strap must extend below the knot of the *shel rosh* or past the fastening on the arm.

The second explanation — which is how we rule in practice — is that the *gemara* is discussing the *shel yad*, and the requirement is that the strap reach from the fastening point all the way to the middle finger. On this reading, the debate between *kaphuf* and *pashut* concerns whether the finger is bent downward, so that reaching the palm suffices, or extended, so that the strap must reach out to the fingertip. Our practice follows the extended reading: the strap must be long enough to wrap and ultimately reach the middle finger. The familiar custom of making seven wraps on the arm is not a formal *halacha* requirement in itself, but the length of strap needed for that is a practical expression of the *shiur*.

### Shel Rosh Length

If the *gemara* is indeed discussing the *shel yad*, it leaves open the question of how long the *shel rosh* straps must be. The *Shulchan Aruch* is not particularly explicit on this point. Tosafot, in the discussion beginning with *puk chazi*, cites the *Shimusha Rabba* — a Geonic work frequently cited in *hilchos tefillin* that appears to have been an authoritative handbook on the subject — which provides the following: one strap of the *shel rosh* must reach down to the point of *mila*, i.e., below the waist, on the right side, and the other must reach to the heart on the left. Tosafot elsewhere also cites an opinion that the right strap need only reach approximately to the navel.

### Directional Requirements

The directional requirements — right side toward *mila*, left side toward the heart — derive from the *Shimusha Rabba* and reflect the practical concern about strap length when tefillin is actually worn. This distinction becomes practically significant in situations where the *retzuah* breaks or where the black coloring wears off, since only the portion of the strap within the minimum *shiur* must meet these standards. Beyond the *shiur*, the strap is not subject to the same requirements.

### Strap Width

Tosafot also records a tradition regarding the width of the straps: ideally they should be wider than a grain of wheat but narrower than a grain of barley — *yoser mi-chita u-fachos mi-se'ora*. If the strap is wider, it remains acceptable. Our straps are considerably wider than this, which the sources indicate is not a problem.

A notable feature of this entire discussion is that the *gemara* itself is not explicit about whether it is addressing the *shel yad* or the *shel rosh*, and much of what governs our practice in this area derives not from the *gemara* directly but from works like the *Shimusha Rabba*. This also illuminates the broader question — discussed at length in the context of Rashi's and Rabbeinu Tam's tefillin — of how halakhic practice in areas where the *gemara* is sparse was shaped by these written handbooks. The Rambam in a famous *teshuva* describes having worn tefillin according to the order of *havayot b'emtza* — the Rabbeinu Tam arrangement — while living in Spain, influenced by a work on tefillin laws by a Rav Moshe of Cordoba. He writes: *oso chibur hu shehatani v'heta kol anshei hamaarav milfanai* — it was that work that misled me and led astray all the people of the West. He was ultimately moved to abandon that position by consulting older manuscripts, finding that Rav Hai Gaon's own tefillin followed the arrangement he now maintained, and by a version of the *gemara* that explicitly listed the *parshiyos* in their Torah order: *Kadesh Li*, *V'haya Ki Yeviacha*, *Shema*, *V'haya Im Shamoa*. He notes that this version of the *gemara* was not found in the manuscripts in Spain, but that he found it in all the older manuscripts he subsequently consulted — and that the tradition of *Eretz Yisrael*, where people were more exacting about tefillin, supported the same arrangement. The power of a written code to shape practice — and the role of manuscript evidence in reshaping it — is vividly illustrated by this episode.

## Strap Placement Customs

### Raba's Method

The *gemara* records several practices regarding what one does with the hanging straps of the *shel rosh* after tying. Raba would tie his tefillin and then fling the straps behind his back.

### Rav Acha's Method

Rav Acha bar Yaakov, by contrast, would hang the straps in front of him after tying.

### Current Practice

Mar brei deRavina followed the practice common in his time, which Rashi identifies as hanging the straps in front. What is notable about this *sugya* is that what one might assume to be a settled and straightforward halakhic requirement — that the straps hang in front — is here presented simply as varying practice among Amoraim, without any of these options being characterized as definitively wrong. That the straps should fall in front has a certain naturalness to it as a requirement, but equally, there is something natural about a headdress flowing behind. The *gemara* leaves the matter as one of custom.

## The Kesher Knot

### Halacha LeMoshe

Rav Yehuda son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat in the name of Rav states that the *kesher shel tefillin* — the knot of tefillin — is *halacha leMoshe miSinai*. The form of the knot is thus rooted in the oldest and most authoritative source in *halacha*.

### Rashi's View

Rashi explains that what was transmitted as *halacha leMoshe miSinai* is specifically this: the knot of the *shel rosh* is shaped like a *dalet*, and the knot of the *shel yad* is shaped like a *yud*, and together with the *shin* on the *bayit* of the *shel rosh*, they spell the divine name *Shakai*. This shaping of the knots to form letters of *shem Hashem* is, in Rashi's view, the substance of what was transmitted from Sinai.

### Tosafot's Objection

Tosafot questions this. The concern is whether the *retzuot* shaped into a *dalet* and a *yud* actually constitute letters of *shem Hashem* in any meaningful halakhic sense. This is not merely an aesthetic objection; it has structural implications. Tosafot points out that if the knots truly formed *shem Hashem*, then the tefillin would not merely be *tashmishei kedusha* — objects serving the sacred *klafim* — they would themselves be in the category of *kedusha atzmo*, actual sanctity comparable to *kisei hakodesh*, objects upon which *shem Hashem* is literally written. Since the halakhic classification of tefillin is *tashmishei kedusha* rather than that higher category, Tosafot is reluctant to affirm that these formed letters constitute a genuine *shem Hashem*. Whatever the substance of the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* is, it establishes that there must be a *kesher* and governs certain features of how it appears — but Tosafot does not concede that it rises to the level of *shem Hashem* inscribed on the tefillin itself.

There is also a significant *machlokes* regarding the form of the *dalet* on the back of the head — whether the standard form suffices or whether one uses what is called a double *dalet*, a square shape formed by two *dalet*s joined together. The double *dalet* has limited basis in *halacha* and is more characteristic of certain Chassidic custom. The underlying question — which precise features of the *kesher* are the substance of the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* and which are later elaboration — remains unresolved.

## Black Exterior

### Black Outward

Rav Nachman rules that the attractive side of the strap — which means the black side — must face outward. This is a formal requirement, not merely aesthetic guidance.

### Inverted Strap

Rav Ashi was seated before Mar Zutra when his *retzuah* flipped, exposing the inside. Mar Zutra pointed out to him: do you not hold that the attractive side must face outward? Rav Ashi replied that he had simply not been paying attention. The incident illustrates how easily the strap can become inverted, particularly on the *shel rosh* where the straps are more exposed to movement.

### Bathroom Concerns

Tosafot raises a question attributed to the Rebbe Yaakov of Orleans regarding entry into the bathroom: is it necessary to remove the *shel yad* before entering *beit hakisei*? His reasoning in favor of possibly permitting it is that the parchment — which is the true object of concern — is covered by the leather of the *bayit*, and it is the parchment that one may not bring into the bathroom. *Tashmishei mitzvah* objects such as *tzitzit* may be brought into the bathroom without restriction; one does so routinely every time one enters while wearing a *tallit katan*. The question is whether the same latitude applies to the *shel yad* whose *klafim* are covered.

The reason the *shel rosh* poses a different problem is, Tosafot suggests, *shekashur shin ba-or* — the *shin* is bound into the leather of the *bayit* itself. The *shin* on the *shel rosh* may give the *bayit* a status approaching *kedusha* in its own right — not merely *tashmishei kedusha* as a vessel serving the *klafim*, but something closer to an object bearing *shem Hashem* directly. Even Tosafot, who elsewhere is cautious about calling the formed knots genuine letters of *shem Hashem*, acknowledges here that at minimum the *shin* lends a degree of sacred weight to the *shel rosh* that the *shel yad* does not possess in the same way.

This analysis reveals the layered structure of tefillin's sanctity. The *klafim* inside are *kisei hakodesh* in the fullest sense. The tefillin themselves — *shel yad* and *shel rosh* — are *tashmishei kedusha* because they house those *klafim*, and simultaneously *tashmishei mitzvah* because the *mitzvah* is performed through them. The *shel rosh* may occupy an intermediate zone above ordinary *tashmishei kedusha* by virtue of its *shin*. The case or cover of the tefillin — analogous to the mantle of a *Sefer Torah* — would be *tashmish detashmish*, serving the tefillin that serve the *klafim*, and thus further removed.

## The Kesher Knot…

### God's Tefillin

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa*: Rabbi Eliezer Hagadol interprets the verse in which God says to Moshe *va-hasirosai et kapi ve-ra'ita et achorai* — "I will remove my hand and you shall see my back" — as referring to what Moshe was shown. Rav Hana bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida teaches: this means that the Holy One showed Moshe the *kesher shel tefillin* — God's own tefillin knot — because God, as the *gemara* in Berakhot establishes, wears tefillin. The back of God's head is where one sees the *kesher*. The *gemara* is not incidentally noting the geography of the scene; there is clearly something significant about the *kesher shel tefillin* specifically being what Moshe is granted to see, though the full depth of that meaning is not elaborated here.

### Kesher Position

Rav Yehuda rules that the *kesher shel tefillin* must be *lemala* — above — meaning positioned high at the back of the head, not low toward the neck or below the hairline. In practice, the *kesher* is placed where one can feel the base of the skull before the slight depression at the nape — high, not low.

### Symbolic Meaning

The reason given is *kedei she-yisrael yehei lemala ve-lo lemata* — so that Israel should be above and not below. This is a striking *drash*: the positioning of a physical knot is made to carry a statement about the standing of the Jewish people. Whether this is a purely symbolic teaching or a formal halakhic requirement with consequences for the validity of the tefillin is left unclear. 

The statement connects meaningfully, however, to the content of God's own tefillin as described in Berakhot: *mi ke-amcha Yisrael goy echad ba-aretz* — who is like Your people Israel, a unique nation on earth. Just as our tefillin express the elevation of God in our consciousness, God's tefillin express His relationship with Israel. The *kesher* positioned *lemala* — with Israel above — is thus in direct dialogue with the theme of God's own *tefillin* and with the vision Moshe was granted: the very back of God's head, where the *kesher shel tefillin* sits, was what he was privileged to see. As Rambam explains regarding this passage, seeing God's "back" means perceiving God's ways in the world — the echo of the divine presence as it operates in history — rather than God's essence directly. If God's tefillin proclaim *mi ke-amcha Yisrael*, then perhaps the *kesher shel tefillin* that Moshe saw was a vision of how God's relationship with Israel is, so to speak, bound into creation.

### Shin Design

Regarding the *shin* on the *shel rosh*: the *gemara* itself mentions only the *shin* without specifying its form. Tosafot, citing the *Shimusha Rabba*, records the practice of placing a three-armed *shin* on one side of the *bayit* and a four-armed *shin* on the other — a practice we follow today, even though it is not found in the *gemara* itself but in this Geonic work. Some have suggested that the four-armed *shin* alludes to the four *parshiyos* within the tefillin. Others note that the negative space within a four-armed *shin* contains the mirror image of a three-armed *shin*, and that this inversion has significance: the white space and the black, the letters formed by absence and by presence, reflect a deeper visual theology of *esh shechora al gabei esh levana* — black fire upon white fire — which is how the Torah is described in its primordial form.

The placement of the *shin* facing outward is also connected to the broader requirement that the straps face properly. The verse cited is *ve-tzarich she-yisyimu ke-lapei panim* — they must face forward — so that Israel should be *lifnim* and not *le-achor*. Rashi understands this to mean that the black must face outward and the white remain inward, and that this orientation must be maintained.

## When to Bless

### Time of Placement

The *gemara* asks: when does one make the *beracha* on tefillin? The initial answer is *mi-sha'as hanachasam* — from the moment one places them on.

### Placement to Tightening

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: the rule of *kol ha-mitzvos kulam mevarech aleihen over la-asiyasan* requires that a *beracha* be recited before the act, not after it has been completed. *Mi-sha'as hanachasam* sounds as though the tefillin have already been placed, making the *beracha* retrospective. Abaye and Rava resolve this: the *beracha* is recited from the moment of placing, meaning between the initial placement of the tefillin on one's body and the final *keshira* — the tightening that completes the act. One places the *shel yad*, recites the *beracha*, and then tightens; then places the *shel rosh*, recites the *beracha*, and then tightens. The *beracha* is thus made *over la-asiya* — preceding the completion of the act.

### Literal Knot Making

Tosafot finds a remarkable implication in the phrase *ad she'as keshira*. Citing Rabbeinu Eliyahu, Tosafot suggests that *keshira* here should be taken literally: one must actually make the knot each day — not merely tighten an existing one, but insert the strap, form the loop, and complete the full act of knotting. Tosafot notes that this probably applies to the *shel yad* rather than the *shel rosh*, whose knot is more elaborate. The supporting verse is *uk'shartam le-os al yadecha* — you shall bind — which Tosafot reads as mandating an active, daily act of binding, not merely the static wearing of tefillin already knotted.

Tosafot himself then hedges, acknowledging that *keshira* in this context may simply mean tightening — *lav davka keshira* — and that the plain sense of *uk'shartam* is the act of attaching the tefillin to one's arm, which one does by tightening, not necessarily by constructing the knot from scratch. Yet the interest of the position lies in how it foregrounds the active verbal dimension of the *mitzvah* — *uk'shartam*, you shall bind — as against the static dimension of *ve-hayu*, they shall be. One might observe that if Rabbeinu Eliyahu's position were correct, the natural *nusach* of the *beracha* would be *le-kashair tefillin* rather than *le-haniach tefillin*, a consideration that presents a real challenge to that reading.

## One or Two Blessings

### Speech Requires New Bles…

Rav Chisda rules: *sach bein tefillah le-tefillah chozer mevarech* — if one spoke between the *shel yad* and the *shel rosh*, one must return and make another *beracha* on the *shel rosh*. Implicitly, if one did not speak, a second *beracha* is not necessary.

### Yochanan's Nusach

A letter sent in the name of Rabbi Yochanan from *Eretz Yisrael* reports a different *nusach* for the two *berachos*: on the *shel yad* one says *baruch asher kideshanu be-mitzvosav ve-tzivanu le-haniach tefillin*, and on the *shel rosh* one says *baruch asher kideshanu be-mitzvosav ve-tzivanu al mitzvas tefillin*. The two formulations differ meaningfully.

### Rashi's Logic

Rashi explains the difference in grammatical terms: *le-haniach* is an infinitive expressing an action in progress — you are still in the act of putting on tefillin, since the *shel rosh* has not yet been placed — while *al mitzvas tefillin* is a phrase denoting completion, used once the full *mitzvah* has been accomplished.

### Pesuk-Based Logic

A more structurally grounded explanation observes that the two formulations directly mirror the *pesukim*. The verse governing the *shel yad* — *uk'shartam le-os al yadecha* — uses an active verb of binding and doing, which is reflected in the active infinitive *le-haniach*. The verse governing the *shel rosh* — *ve-hayu le-totafos bein einecha* — uses a static verb of being, a state rather than an action, which is reflected in *al mitzvas tefillin*, denoting the status of wearing rather than the act of placing.

### Abaye & Rava Rule

Abaye and Rava formalize the rule: *lo sach mevarech achas, sach mevarech shtayim* — if one did not speak between the two, one makes one *beracha*; if one spoke, one makes two.

### Sefardi Custom

The straightforward reading of Abaye and Rava — and the position adopted by Rashi — is that without a *hefsek*, a single *beracha* covers both. This is *minhag Sefarad*: Sefardim recite only one *beracha* on tefillin — *le-haniach tefillin* — unless a *hefsek* occurred between the *shel yad* and the *shel rosh*, in which case a second *beracha* is added.

### Ashkenazi Custom

*Minhag Ashkenaz* is to always recite two separate *berachos* — *le-haniach tefillin* on the *shel yad* and *al mitzvas tefillin* on the *shel rosh* — regardless of whether any speech intervened. The additional *beracha* is always made on the *shel rosh*. If, however, a *hefsek* also occurred, the Ashkenazi practice is to add yet another *beracha* on the *shel rosh*, meaning two *berachos* are recited on the *shel rosh* alone in that case: first *le-haniach* and then *al mitzvas tefillin*. If one cannot put on the *shel yad* at all — due to injury, a cast, or any other reason — both *berachos* are recited on the *shel rosh* alone, since each represents an independent *mitzvah* dimension.

Tosafot raises the analogy of other *mitzvos* involving extended processes: *bedikas chametz*, which is clearly a unified process on which one *beracha* covers the entire search regardless of how long it takes; *shechitah* of many animals, where one *beracha* covers all the individual acts of *shechitah* performed sequentially. Tefillin occupies a different position: *shel yad* and *shel rosh* are in some sense independent (*ein me'akvin zeh et zeh* — one does not prevent the other, as the *gemara* will establish), yet when performed together they form a larger, unified *mitzvah*. What constitutes a *hefsek* significant enough to disrupt that unity may be different in each of these three models.

### Process vs. Separate

The deeper issue underlying the Ashkenazi–Sefardi dispute is how to conceptualize the relationship between the two *mitzvos*. For Sefardim, the dominant framework is unity: when no *hefsek* occurs, the *beracha* of *le-haniach* covers both, treating the donning of both *shel yad* and *shel rosh* as one continuous process. The *shel rosh* is acknowledged as distinct only when something has interrupted the process.

For Ashkenazim, each of the two retains its distinct identity even within the unified process. The *shel yad* involves the act of placing — *le-haniach* — and the *shel rosh* involves the state of having tefillin on — *al mitzvas tefillin* — and both dimensions deserve their own *beracha* regardless of continuity. This may reflect the view that there are two genuinely distinct aspects of the *mitzvah*: the act of binding (*uk'shartam*) and the state of bearing (*ve-hayu*), and that each demands its own acknowledgment.

A difficulty with the Ashkenazi position deserves mention: it seems to involve making two *birchos ha-mitzvah* — both of the *asher kideshanu* form — on what is arguably one *ma'aseh mitzvah*. This is not a familiar category; typically a complex *mitzvah* receives a single *birkas ha-mitzvah*. The Sefardi reluctance to adopt the Ashkenazi approach may reflect precisely this unease. Yet the Ashkenazi position may be understood as asserting that the act-dimension and the state-dimension of tefillin are genuinely distinct enough to each carry their own *beracha* — a claim that has textual grounding in the two different verbs used by the *pesukim* governing each.

## Talking Between Them

### Aveira & Remedy

A *baraisa* teaches: *sach bein tefillah le-tefillah aveira hi be-yado* — one who speaks between the two tefillin has committed a transgression. The *baraisa* then connects this to the verse about returning from battle — *mi ha-ish ha-yarei ve-rach ha-leivav* — suggesting that even one who has spoken between his tefillin and thereby committed this *aveira* may return from the front on that account. Whether this is meant to impress upon us the severity of the transgression, or whether it reflects the more modest point that even the smallest sin provides grounds for the returning soldier provision, is not entirely clear from the context — though the former reading seems more natural.

### Nature of Problem

Rashi understands the problem of speaking between the two tefillin specifically in terms of the *beracha*: one spoke and thereby failed to make the proper *beracha* at the right moment. This frames the *aveira* as rooted in the *beracha* violation. Tosafot, however, probes further and notes that on this reading, one encounters the problem of *bracha she-eina tzricha* — by now needing to make another *beracha*, one has caused an unnecessary *beracha* to be required, which is itself a transgression. The *aveira* thus ramifies in both directions.

There is, however, a deeper question about the nature of the problem that goes beyond the *beracha* entirely. Given that the *beracha* of *le-haniach* on the *shel yad* normally extends to and covers the *shel rosh* as well — treating the process as unified — the act of speaking between them may constitute an *aveira* not merely because of the *beracha* consequences, but because it breaks apart what is meant to be a unified act. The goal is a single, uninterrupted process of donning both tefillin together as one larger *mitzvah*. Even if one were somehow to solve the *beracha* problem, the separation itself — treating the *shel yad* and *shel rosh* as two independent acts rather than parts of one whole — may remain problematic. The *gemara* does not entirely resolve whether the *aveira* is solely about the *beracha* or also about the fragmentation of the *mitzvah* process.

## Donning & Doffing Order

### Putting On Order

A *baraisa* establishes: *k'shehu meniach meniach shel yad ve-achar kach meniach shel rosh* — when one puts on tefillin, one places the *shel yad* first and then the *shel rosh*.

### Pesuk Order

The source is straightforward: *uk'shartam le-os al yadecha*, the verse about the *shel yad*, precedes *ve-hayu le-totafos bein einecha*, the verse about the *shel rosh*. The order of the *pesukim* governs the order of the acts. Notably, this framing treats the donning of both as a single process unfolding in a defined sequence — not as two independent acts each with their own order, but as a unified *mitzvah* with a beginning and a progression.

### Taking Off Order

The *baraisa* continues: *uk'shehu choletz choletz shel rosh ve-achar kach choletz shel yad* — when removing, one first takes off the *shel rosh* and then the *shel yad*. This is the reverse order.

### Rabba's Logic

The source for reversing the order upon removal is explained by Rabba bar Rav Huna: the verse *ve-hayu le-totafos bein einecha* uses the word *ve-hayu*, which is plural and refers to both tefillin together. This teaches that *kol zman she-bein einecha yehu shtayim* — for as long as the *shel rosh* is between your eyes, both tefillin must be on you simultaneously. The *shel rosh* presupposes the presence of the *shel yad*; it builds upon it as its foundation. Therefore, one may not remove the *shel yad* while the *shel rosh* is still in place, for that would eliminate the foundation before the structure above it. The *shel rosh* must come off first.

### Action vs. State

This distinction illuminates a recurring theme throughout the *sugya*. The order of putting on derives from the *pesukim*' sequential verbs — *uk'shartam* (bind) and then *ve-hayu* (they shall be) — which govern the *action* of placing. The order of removal, however, derives from *ve-hayu* as expressing a required *state*: as long as the *shel rosh* is present, the *shel yad* must also be present, because *ve-hayu* demands that both exist simultaneously on the wearer.

Thus the donning order reflects the logic of action — you perform the acts in the order the *pesukim* prescribe. The doffing order reflects the logic of state — you must preserve the required state of simultaneous wearing for as long as either item remains on you. The two orders are governed by different halakhic principles derived from the same verse, depending on which dimension of the *mitzvah* is in view.

## Timing of Wearing

### Before Dawn

The *gemara* asks: from what point may one recite the *beracha* on tefillin, and how does that interact with wearing them before daybreak? A case is presented: one woke early before dawn and wished to travel, fearing that tefillin carried in a bag might be lost. So one placed them on even before the proper time.

### Blessing After Dawn

The ruling: *u'ch'she-yagia zman memashmesh bahen u-mevarech aleihen* — when the proper time arrives, one feels the tefillin on one's body and then recites the *beracha*. The act of touching and feeling the tefillin functions as a kind of renewed placement, sufficient to serve as the basis for the *beracha* even though the physical act of putting them on occurred earlier. This is consistent with the ruling for *tzitzit* worn before the proper time: when dawn arrives, one touches the *tzitzit* and recites the *beracha*.

What is theologically and halakhically significant here is the implication that the *beracha* is not exclusively tied to the moment of the initial physical act. The state of wearing tefillin is itself a continuous reality, and *kol zman she-manichan* — for as long as one is wearing them — the *beracha* has a basis. This reframes the principle of *mi-sha'as hanachasam*: it refers not merely to the moment of placement but to the ongoing condition of having tefillin on one's body. The act of feeling them at daybreak is a moment of re-recognition of that state, and it is in that moment that the *beracha* is most naturally made. This parallels the tension throughout the *sugya* between the action-dimension and the state-dimension of the *mitzvah*.

### Sunset Limit

*Ve-ad masai menichan? Ad she-tishka ha-chama* — how long may one wear tefillin? Until sunset.

### Two Rationales

Two distinct reasons are given for not wearing tefillin at night. The first is *layla lav zman tefillin* — night is formally not the time for tefillin, derived from the *pesukim* in a manner analogous to other *mitzvos* that apply only by day. This is a categorical halakhic statement about the scope of the *mitzvah* itself. The second reason is practical concern: one may fall asleep while wearing tefillin and, in that state, pass gas, which is incompatible with the *kedusha* of tefillin.

### Yaakov's Extension

Rabbi Yaakov holds that one may continue wearing tefillin beyond sunset until *ad she-tichleh regel min ha-shuk* — until the foot traffic in the marketplace has ceased and people have gone home. This is analogous to the time limit for *ner Chanukah*. On this view, the operative concern is the practical one of falling asleep; as long as people are still active in the street, there is no realistic concern that one will fall asleep inadvertently, and so wearing tefillin remains permissible.

### Bedtime Limit

The *Chachamim* extend this further: one may continue wearing tefillin until *zman sheina* — until it is normally bedtime, whenever that is for the individual or community. Like Rabbi Yaakov, the *Chachamim* are evidently operating from the concern about sleep rather than a categorical exclusion of nighttime, but they set the limit at the point when sleep actually becomes likely.

### Bathroom Exception

The *Chachamim* agree with Rabbi Yaakov on one point: if one removed tefillin after sunset — for example, to enter a bathroom or a bathhouse — and sunset then fully passed during the interim, one does not put them back on. The *Chachamim*'s permission to wear tefillin into the evening is a concession to continuity; it applies when one has kept them on continuously, but it does not sanction *lechatchila* putting them back on once nightfall has arrived.

### Rav Nachman Rules

Rav Nachman rules *halacha ke-Rabbi Yaakov* — we follow Rabbi Yaakov's position, which means that the governing concern is falling asleep, and one may continue wearing tefillin after sunset until the streets empty of traffic. This implies that *layla lav zman tefillin* is not a categorical exclusion — it is a concern of bodily propriety that becomes pressing only when sleep is a realistic prospect.

### Night Prayer Dispute

The *gemara* records that Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna would pray — presumably *Maariv* — while wearing tefillin at night. One version understands this as a corroboration of Rabbi Yaakov's position, since they clearly continued wearing tefillin into the evening. But the *gemara* then introduces a contrary view: *ein halacha ke-Rabbi Yaakov* — we do not rule like Rabbi Yaakov — which would mean that tefillin must be removed at sunset. This creates an apparent difficulty: what then were Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna doing wearing tefillin at night? The *gemara* answers that they were among those who disputed this and held that night-time wearing is in fact permissible. The question of whether *layla lav zman tefillin* constitutes a formal halakhic exclusion demanding removal, or only a practical concern that governs when one *lechatchila* puts tefillin on, will be the subject of continued discussion on the next daf.
