# Zevachim 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiyor Capacity

### Minimum Basin Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כׇּל כִּיּוֹר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְקַדֵּשׁ אַרְבָּעָה כֹּהֲנִים מִמֶּנּוּ – אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרָחֲצוּ מִמֶּנּוּ מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned a *halakha* earlier (19b), the Gemara addresses **the** matter **itself: Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** With regard to **any Basin that does not contain enough** water **for four priests to sanctify** their hands and feet **from it,** the priests **may not sanctify** their hands and feet **with it, as it is stated: "That Moses and Aaron and his sons might wash** their hands and their feet **from it"** (Exodus 40:31).

The *gemara* opens with a *gufa* — returning to an earlier statement to examine it closely. The passage in question appears four lines from the bottom of 21b, where Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina rules: *kol kiyor she'ein bo kidei lekadesh arba kohanim mimenu ein mikadshin bo* — any basin that does not contain sufficient water for four *kohanim* to wash their hands cannot be used for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*. The proof text is the *pasuk*: *v'rachatzu mimenu Moshe v'Aharon u'vanav* — Moshe, Aharon, and his sons washed from it. Though in practice they did not all serve simultaneously, the verse is read to indicate that the basin must be capable of accommodating four *kohanim* at once.

It is worth noting the phrasing *kol kiyor* — "any basin." Since there was only one *kiyor* in the *Beis HaMikdash*, the formulation implies a general principle: whether describing the historical requirement of that vessel or establishing criteria for any future one, the minimum capacity is four *kohanim*. This also raises an immediate question: since a *kli shares* may also be used for the washing, does that requirement apply there as well? That question will unfold shortly.

### Kli Shares Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל הַכֵּלִים מְקַדְּשִׁין – בֵּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן רְבִיעִית, בֵּין שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן רְבִיעִית; וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: The priests may **sanctify** their hands and feet from **all vessels, whether they can hold a quarter***-log* of water **or they cannot hold a quarter***-log* of water, **provided that they are service vessels.**

The *gemara* immediately raises a *meitivei*: we have been taught that *kol hakelim mikadshin, bein sheyeish bahem revi'is bein she'ein bahem revi'is, u'vilvad sheyehu kli shares* — all vessels may be used for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, whether they contain a *revi'is* or not, provided they are *kli shares*. This is quite striking. We already know that the washing must be performed on the Temple grounds with a sanctified service vessel, but here the *braisa* states explicitly that the vessel's capacity is irrelevant — it may even hold less than a *revi'is*. Rashi notes that even for ordinary hand-washing performed today for *tumah* purposes, a *revi'is* is required. That a vessel holding less than that could be sufficient for the Temple's *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* is genuinely surprising. And of course it appears to contradict the requirement that the basin hold enough for four *kohanim*.

### Rashi's Kodach View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַחָא: בְּקוֹדֵחַ בְּתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Aḥa says:** The *baraita* is referring to a case **where one drills** a hole **in** the Basin and places a much smaller vessel at the hole as a conduit for the water. Even if that vessel is very small, the priest may sanctify his hands and feet from it, provided that there is enough water in the Basin for four priests.

Rav Adda bar Ahava resolves the tension: *bikode'ach b'tocho* — the case is one where a hole is carved into the basin. According to Rashi, the procedure is as follows: one takes a large basin containing four *log* of water, carves a hole in its side, and inserts the *kli shares* into that opening. The *kli shares* then functions essentially as a spout — water flows from the basin, through the *kli*, and onto the hands of the *kohanim*. The vessel itself may be tiny, but because it draws from a source containing the requisite quantity, the washing is valid.

Admittedly, this construction is conceptually unusual. If the water is flowing from the basin through the *kli* like a spout, one might argue that the *kohen* is effectively washing from the basin, not the *kli*. But that, Rashi seems to suggest, is precisely the *chiddush*: the sanctified status of the *kli* remains conceptually relevant even in this configuration. Because the water emerges through the *kli*, one is considered to be washing from the *kli* — and since four *kohanim* could in principle wash from this arrangement given the water source behind it, the requirement is satisfied.

### Tosafos on Water Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא! ״יִרְחָצוּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת כְּלִי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't the Merciful One state** that the priests must wash their hands and feet **"from it,"** i.e., from the Basin and not from another vessel? The Gemara responds: The following verse repeats the phrase **"they should wash," to include** any **service vessel.**

Tosafos reads the case very differently. In their comments on *kode'ach m'tocho* (the third Tosafos on the page), they explain that *kode'ach* does not mean literally carving a hole in the basin. Rather, it means that one removes water *from* the basin — one takes a smaller vessel, dips it into the larger basin, and then washes one's hands from that smaller vessel. There is no physical alteration of the basin; it is simply a matter of sourcing.

Under Tosafos's reading, the key criterion is not the vessel's capacity but the status of the water it contains. Water drawn from a source that held the requisite amount for four *kohanim* carries a kind of valid status with it. It is not the *kiyor* itself that must hold the full quantity in every configuration — rather, the water must originate from something that held the correct amount, whether that is the *kiyor* proper or some other sanctified vessel.

This also changes how Tosafos reads the *gemara*'s challenge from the word *mimenu* — "from it." Rashi understands the challenge as: how can we use a *kli shares* at all, given that the *pasuk* specifies *mimenu*, from the basin itself? Tosafos notes that we already know independently that the *Kohen Gadol* washed from a *kli shares* on *Yom Kippur*, so the ability to use a *kli shares* is not really in question. The challenge for Tosafos is instead this: since we know a *kli shares* may be used, must it replicate the *kiyor*'s requirements exactly? If the *kiyor* needed to hold enough for four *kohanim*, should not the *kli shares* be held to the same standard?

### Kal v'Chomer Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כְּלִי חוֹל נָמֵי! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּלִי חוֹל לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר מִכַּנּוֹ; וּמָה כַּנּוֹ, שֶׁנִּמְשַׁח עִמּוֹ – אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ; כְּלִי חוֹל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִמְשָׁח עִמּוֹ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then it should be permitted for a priest to use **a non-sacred vessel as well. Abaye says: You cannot say** that a priest may sanctify his hands and feet from **a non-sacred vessel,** since the matter may be derived **by *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* concerning **the base of** the Basin: **And just as its base, which was anointed along with it** (see Exodus 40:11), **is** still **not** fit for a priest to **sanctify** his hands and feet with water from it, then with regard to **a non-sacred vessel, which was not anointed along with** the Basin, **is it not right that it is not** fit for a priest to **sanctify** his hands and feet with water from it?

The *gemara* proceeds to explain why a non-sanctified vessel (*kli chol*) cannot be used, even though the word *yirchatzu* — the repeated mention of washing — appears to broaden the permissible instruments beyond the *kiyor* alone. Abaye provides the exclusion: *k'lei chol lo masis amart* — you cannot possibly say a non-sanctified vessel is valid — *mikkal vachomer mikkanno* — by means of a *kal v'chomer* derived from the pedestal (*kano*) of the basin.

The *pasuk* states: *va'asita kiyor nechoshet v'kanno nechoshet lerachtza* — make a copper basin and its copper pedestal for washing. The Torah explicitly mandates both the basin and its stand. Now consider the *kal v'chomer*: the pedestal was fashioned together with the basin as a single unit, was anointed with *shemen hamishcha*, and was consecrated as *kli shares* — if anything should function as a substitute for the basin, it would be this. Yet the pedestal cannot be used for *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*. (Tosafos explains the physical scenario: imagine the pedestal as an inverted dome or hollowed base upon which the basin rested; in theory its hollow could be reversed and filled with water, yet this is prohibited.)

How do we know the pedestal is excluded? A *braisa* records this explicitly: Rabbi Yehuda raises the possibility that the pedestal might be valid just as the basin is, and then derives from the doubled word *nechoshet* — copper appearing twice — that the only common ground between the two is their material. They are not functionally equivalent and the pedestal may not be used for washing.

From the pedestal, then, the *kal v'chomer* runs: if the pedestal — which was co-consecrated with the basin — cannot be used, surely an ordinary non-sanctified vessel cannot be used either.

### Mar Zutra Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: מָה לְכַנּוֹ – שֶׁאֵין עָשׂוּי לְתוֹכוֹ; תֹּאמַר בִּכְלִי חוֹל – שֶׁעָשׂוּי לְתוֹכוֹ?! אֶלָּא ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי כְּלִי חוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina:** The *a fortiori* inference can be refuted: **What** is notable **about its base?** It is notable **in that its inside is not made for** use, as it is to be used only as a stand. Will **you say** that the *halakha* is the same **for a non-sacred vessel,** concerning **which its inside is made for** use? **Rather,** one must say that the term **"from it"** serves **to exclude a non-sacred vessel.**

Mar Zutra son of Rav Mari raises an objection to Ravina: *mah l'khanno she-ken ein asui l'tokho* — there is a distinguishing feature of the pedestal: its hollow is not designed to contain anything. The pedestal, understood as an upturned dome, has no functional capacity as a receptacle. One might therefore argue that it belongs to an entirely different category from a vessel specifically constructed to hold liquid. A non-sanctified vessel designed as a basin — *asui l'tokho*, made to hold contents — may be categorically superior to the pedestal and cannot be excluded on the basis of this *kal v'chomer*.

### Two Limiting Factors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כְּלִי שָׁרֵת נָמֵי! הָא רַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא ״יִרְחָצוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** "from it" should exclude **a service vessel as well.** The Gemara responds: **But the Merciful One amplifies** the *halakha* with the term **"they should wash,"** to include a service vessel.

The *gemara* therefore turns to a different resolution. We have two competing words in the verse: *yirchatzu* (the repeated form of washing) and *mimenu* (from it). The word *yirchatzu* broadens the scope — there are additional means by which one may wash, beyond the *kiyor* alone, including a *kli shares*. The word *mimenu* limits — suggesting only from the basin itself. This tension, rather than collapsing one word against the other, places us in the middle: a *kli shares*, which is anointed and consecrated like the basin and is fashioned as a receptacle, is close enough to the *kiyor* to be included by *yirchatzu*. A *kli chol*, however, falls outside that inclusion. As the *gemara* puts it: *mimenu l'ma'utei kli chol* — *mimenu* comes to exclude non-sanctified vessels.

When the *gemara* asks whether *mimenu* should then exclude *kli shares* as well, the answer is *ha ribbi rachmana yirchatzu* — the Torah's broadening term *yirchatzu* rescues *kli shares*. And when pressed — why is *kli shares* the beneficiary of the broadening and not *kli chol*? — the answer is: *zeh ta'un meshichah v'zeh eino ta'un meshichah kemoto* — a *kli shares* is also anointed with *shemen hamishcha*, making it sufficiently analogous to the *kiyor* that the broadening applies to it. A non-sanctified vessel, by contrast, lacks this quality and is excluded by *mimenu*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ? זֶה טָעוּן מְשִׁיחָה כָּמוֹהוּ, וְזֶה אֵין טָעוּן מְשִׁיחָה כָּמוֹהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see** that led you to include a service vessel and exclude a non-sacred vessel, rather than the reverse? The Gemara responds: **This,** a service vessel, **requires anointing like** the Basin when the Tabernacle is raised (see Exodus, chapter 40), **and that,** a non-sacred vessel, **does not require anointing like** the Basin.

## Liquids for Mikveh

### Reish Lakish Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַמַּשְׁלִים לְמֵי מִקְוֶה – מַשְׁלִים לְמֵי כִיּוֹר. לִרְבִיעִית – אֵינוֹ מַשְׁלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish says: Any** liquid **that** may be used to **complete** the requisite measure of **water for a ritual bath may complete** the requisite measure of **water for** the **Basin.** But **it may not complete** the **quarter***-log* required for ritual washing of the hands.

The discussion now turns from the act of washing to the water contained within the *kiyor*. Reish Lakish formulates a general principle: *kol ha-mashlimin l'mei mikveh mashlimin l'mei kiyor* — anything that can complete (*mashlim*) the forty *se'ah* of a *mikveh* can also serve to complete the required quantity in the *kiyor*. The concept of *mashlim* refers to substances that, while not themselves water and insufficient to render a *mikveh* valid on their own, can supplement a *mikveh* that is already mostly water and bring it to the required forty *se'ah*.

### Revi'is Exception

Reish Lakish immediately qualifies his principle: *l'revi'is eino mashlim* — such substances cannot complete the measure of a *revi'is*. When the requirement is that small — the measure used for ordinary hand-washing — it must consist entirely of pure water; the supplementing substances that work for the mikveh's forty *se'ah* do not apply here.

Tosafos, as we will see, understands the reference to *revi'is* somewhat differently, but the basic contour of the ruling is clear: there is an asymmetry between the two contexts. The large quantity of water required in the *kiyor* admits of the same supplementing substances as a *mikveh*, while the small measure for hand-washing does not.

### Gemara Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי טִיט הַנִדּוֹק – הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּפָרָה שׁוֹחָה וְשׁוֹתָה מִמֶּנּוּ, אֲפִילּוּ לִרְבִיעִית נָמֵי! וְאִי אֵין פָּרָה שׁוֹחָה וְשׁוֹתָה מִמֶּנּוּ, אֲפִילּוּ לְמִקְוֶה נָמֵי אֵין מַשְׁלִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This statement means **to exclude what** as unfit for completing the requisite measure for ritual washing of the hands? **If we say** that it means **to exclude liquefied clay,** there is a difficulty, as **what is** this clay **like? If** it is such **that a cow can kneel and drink from it,** it is considered water and should be fit **even to** complete the measure of **a quarter***-log* for ritual washing of the hands. **And if a cow cannot kneel and drink from it, it should not even** be fit to **complete** the requisite measure for **a ritual bath.**

The *gemara* immediately asks: *limutei mai* — what exactly is being excluded? Which specific substance does Reish Lakish have in mind when he says that something works for a *mikveh* but not for the *revi'is*? The *gemara* begins its search with *tit ha-nerok* — pourable plaster, a thin watery mixture — which appears in *Mishnah Mikva'ot* as something that can supplement a *mikveh*. But before confirming whether this is the case in point, the *gemara* tests the limits.

### Cow Drinkability Test

The *gemara* introduces a criterion: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? *I d'parah shochah v'shotah mimenu* — if a cow could bend down and drink from this substance, then it is sufficiently water-like that it works even for the *revi'is* of hand-washing. *V'i ein parah shotah v'shochah mimenu* — and if a cow could not drink from it, then it is not water-like enough to serve even as a *mashlim* for the forty *se'ah* of a *mikveh*.

This is a striking move. Tosafos points out that the tractate *Mikva'ot* provides various definitions for the consistency of *tit ha-nerok*, and none of them employ this "cow drinkability" standard. It is puzzling that the *gemara* treats this criterion as obvious, and equally puzzling that it assumes no middle ground exists — no consistency that would qualify for the *mikveh* but not for the *revi'is*. The *gemara* appears to take for granted that the line is binary: either it qualifies for everything, or it qualifies for nothing. Nevertheless, this is the framework the *gemara* is working with, so it concludes that pourable plaster cannot be what Reish Lakish had in mind.

### Water-Origin Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי יַבְחוּשִׁין אֲדוּמִּין – אֲפִילּוּ בְּעֵינַיְיהוּ נָמֵי! דְּהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁתְּחִילַּת בְּרִיָּיתוֹ מִן הַמַּיִם – מַטְבִּילִין בּוֹ; וְאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: מַטְבִּילִין בְּעֵינוֹ שֶׁל דָּג!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say that it means **to exclude red gnats** that originate and grow in water. This, too, is difficult, as **even by themselves** they may constitute the entire measure of the ritual bath, not only to complete a deficient measure of water; **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** With regard to **anything that originates in the water, one may immerse in it,** since it is considered as though it were water. **And Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi says: One may immerse** even **in** liquefied **fish eye** of sufficient volume.

The *gemara* then suggests: *ela limutei yavshushim adumim* — perhaps the excluded category is little red water bugs. These insects, which the *gemara* understands to originate in or from water, might be thought to carry enough water-status to count toward the forty *se'ah* of a *mikveh*, yet not enough to serve for ordinary hand-washing. But the *gemara* challenges this as well: *afilu beinhu nami* — even these should count fully as water. For indeed, a *braisa* records that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: *kol she-techilas bri'aso min hamayim matbilin bo* — anything whose original formation was from water, one may immerse in it. And Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi adds that one may even immerse in the liquefied contents of a fish's eye. If the water bugs are treated as fully water-equivalent to the point of permitting *tevilah* directly in them, they should work even for the *revi'is* — and so they, too, cannot be what Reish Lakish meant.

### Natan/Natal Se'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְמַעוֹטֵי נָתַן סְאָה וְנָטַל סְאָה. דִּתְנַן: מִקְוֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה מְכוָּּונוֹת; נָתַן סְאָה וְנָטַל סְאָה – הֲרֵי זֶה כָּשֵׁר. וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר שֵׁילָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד רוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** The statement of Reish Lakish means **to exclude** a case where **one added a *se'a* and removed a *se'a*, as we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 7:2): In the case of **a ritual bath that contains** the minimum measure of **exactly forty *se'a*** of water, and **one added a *se'a*** of liquid other than water, **and** then **removed a *se'a*** of the mixture, the ritual bath remains **fit.** The added liquid is considered nullified in the forty *se'a* of water, so that when one *se'a* of the mixture is removed, there still remain the requisite forty *se'a* of fit water. **And Rav Yehuda bar Sheila says** that **Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that this *halakha* applies **up to the greater part** of the measure of the ritual bath. Adding and removing such a liquid to the requisite measure for washing of the hands renders the water unfit.

Rav Pappa resolves the question: *limutei natan sa'ah v'natal sa'ah* — Reish Lakish is referring to the case of adding and removing from a *mikveh*. The relevant *mishnah* in *Mikva'ot* establishes: *mikveh sheyesh bo mem se'ah mechuvvanot, natan sa'ah v'natal sa'ah, harei zeh kasher* — a *mikveh* containing exactly forty *se'ah* into which one adds a unit of some non-water liquid and then removes a unit of the mixture remains valid.

The classic illustration is fruit juice — apple juice or orange juice. Such a liquid would not on its own bring a *mikveh* up to the required forty *se'ah*; it is too unlike water for that purpose. But if a *mikveh* that already contains forty *se'ah* of valid water has a unit of fruit juice fall into it, and then a unit is removed, the *mikveh* remains *kasher*. Rabbi Yehuda bar Shila citing Rav Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan extends this: one may repeat this process again and again — adding a unit of juice and removing a unit — up to the point where the *mikveh* physically contains a majority of water. So long as the water remains the majority, the *mikveh* is valid. Each time juice is added to an already-valid *mikveh*, it becomes assimilated into the *mikveh*'s status and is treated as part of it.

This is the type of *mashlim* that Reish Lakish has in mind. Fruit juice does not render a *mikveh* valid on its own, and it does not get you to forty *se'ah* from scratch — it only maintains the validity of a *mikveh* already established. When the amount in question is as small as a *revi'is*, there is no pre-existing valid *mikveh* into which the juice can be absorbed; at that scale, everything must be pure water.

### Mikveh Crevice Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִם קָדַח בּוֹ רְבִיעִית – מַטְבִּילִין בּוֹ מְחָטִין וְצִינּוֹרִיּוֹת, הוֹאִיל וּמֵהֶכְשֵׁירָא דְּמִקְוֶה אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: If one drilled in** the wall of a ritual bath, and the sides of the hole can hold **a quarter***-log* of liquid, **one may immerse** small vessels such as **needles and hooks in** the hole, even though it constitutes a space separate from the ritual bath, **since** the fitness of the water in the hole **derives from the fitness of** the water in **the ritual bath.** This *halakha* is parallel to the one discussed with regard to the Basin.

Rav Pappa then introduces a closely related ruling that illuminates the earlier discussion of *kodach*. He states: *im kodach bo revi'is matbilin bo machten v'tzinoryot hoilei me'achsherei d'mikveh atya* — if one carves a small crevice in the wall of a *mikveh* and a *revi'is* of water flows into that crevice, one may immerse small objects such as needles and fine instruments in it, since that *revi'is* derives from the waters of a *kasher* mikveh of forty *se'ah*.

This use of *kodach* — carving a small space connected to a valid *mikveh* — illuminates Tosafos's reading of the earlier *gemara* about the *kiyor*. Just as water drawn from or physically continuous with a valid *mikveh* retains a valid status even in a tiny volume, so too water drawn from a valid *kiyor* containing the requisite quantity is valid for the *kohen*'s washing even if the immediate vessel is small. The structural parallel makes Tosafos's position considerably clearer.

## Water Sources

### Mikveh Waters Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מֵי מִקְוֶה כְּשֵׁירִים לְמֵי כִיּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yirmeya says** that **Reish Lakish says: Water of a ritual bath is fit to** be used as **water of** the **Basin.**

Rabbi Yirmiah citing Reish Lakish now addresses a different question about the *kiyor*: from where did the basin draw its water? We know that the *kiyor* was lowered into a well each night so that its water would not become *pasul* through *lina* — overnight standing outside the Temple service. But what type of water was used? Rabbi Yirmiah in the name of Reish Lakish states: *mei mikveh k'sheirim l'mei kiyor* — the waters of a *mikveh* are valid for the *kiyor*.

### Mayim Chayim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּלָא מַיִם חַיִּים נִינְהוּ?! וְהָתַנְיָא: ״בְּמַיִם״ – וְלֹא בְּיַיִן, ״בְּמַיִם״ – וְלֹא בְּמָזוּג, ״בְּמַיִם״ – לְרַבּוֹת שְׁאָר מַיִם; וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לְמֵי כִיּוֹר. מַאי קַל וָחוֹמֶר לְמֵי כִיּוֹר? לָאו דְּמַיִם חַיִּים נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** the water in the Basin **does not** need to be **flowing water,** i.e., spring water? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse indicates that the innards of an offering must be washed: **"With water"** (Leviticus 1:9), **and not with wine; "with water," and not with** wine **mixed** with water; **"with water," to include other** types of **water,** even those that are not spring water. **And** it can be inferred ***a fortiori*** that **the water** used **for** the **Basin** may be used to wash the innards. **What** is the basis of the ***a fortiori*** inference that **the water** used **for the Basin** may also be used? Is it **not that it is** from a source of **flowing water** and therefore obviously fit for washing the innards?

This ruling carries an immediate implication: *l'maimra d'lo mayim chayim ninhu* — it asserts that *mayim chayim*, "living water" — water from a natural underground spring, flowing river, or ocean — is not required for the *kiyor*. *Mikveh* waters are typically rainwater collected in a pool, not living spring water, and if such water qualifies for the *kiyor*, then *mayim chayim* is not the criterion.

The *gemara* challenges this from a *braisa*: the verse *v'hakerev v'hakra'ayim yirchatz b'mayim* — the innards and the legs of the animal shall be washed in water — is expounded as follows: *b'mayim v'lo b'yayin* — water, not wine; *b'mayim v'lo b'mazag* — water, not a wine-water mixture; *b'mayim l'rabot she'ar mayim* — but "water" in the broader sense to include other types of water. The *braisa* then adds: *v'kal v'chomer l'mei kiyor* — and this is a *kal v'chomer* to the waters of the *kiyor*.

Rashi explains that "other types of water" here means rainwater. The verse is saying: rainwater may be used for washing the animal's limbs — and certainly it may be used for the *kiyor*. This *kal v'chomer* seems to presuppose that the *kiyor*'s water is held to a higher standard than rainwater — which would mean the *kiyor* requires *mayim chayim*, from a spring. If rainwater were sufficient for the *kiyor*, what would the *kal v'chomer* even mean?

### No Modifying Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, (לִקְדּוֹשׁ) דְּקַדִּישׁ וְקַדִּישֵׁי מְעַלְּיוּתָא הִיא. וְהָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי שְׁמוּאֵל: ״מַיִם״ – שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם שֵׁם לְוַוי, יָצְאוּ מֵי כִיּוֹר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם שֵׁם לְוַוי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** the *a fortiori* inference is based on the fact **that** the water used for the Basin is **sanctified** for the service. The Gemara asks: **But is sanctified** water **preferable,** given that the Torah stipulates simply "water"? **But didn't Rabbi Shmuel teach:** The word **"water"** used in the verse denotes specifically water **whose** name **has no modifier;** **excluded is water of** the **Basin, which has a modifier** in its name?

The *gemara* then considers a different way to read the *kal v'chomer*: perhaps "the waters of the *kiyor*" are valid for washing the animal's limbs because they have been sanctified — and the *kal v'chomer* is from the sanctification status, not the water type. If rainwater works, surely water that has been consecrated through the *kiyor* works all the more. But the *gemara* immediately challenges this: *v'kadishim alyusa hi* — is the sanctified status actually a benefit in this context?

A *braisa* is cited in which Rabbi Yishmael rules: *mayim she'ein bahem shem l'vay* — the verse requires water that carries no modifying name. Water called by a special designation — *mei kiyor*, sanctified waters, or any other appellation — is excluded. *Yatzu mei kiyor sheyesh lahem shem l'vay* — specifically the waters of the *kiyor* are excluded because they carry such a name. On this view, the *kal v'chomer* cannot be: "if rainwater, then certainly *kiyor* water" — because *kiyor* water itself is disqualified.

The *gemara* therefore reinterprets: the *kal v'chomer* means not that the actual *kiyor* water may be used, but that water of the *type* used for the *kiyor* may be used — namely, *mayim chayim*, spring water. The argument runs: if rainwater can wash the animal's limbs, spring water certainly can — and spring water is indeed the type appropriate for the *kiyor*. This reading, in turn, implies that the *kiyor* requires *mayim chayim*.

### Tannaim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דִּרְאוּיִין לְמֵי כִיּוֹר? אַלְמָא מַיִם חַיִּים נִינְהוּ! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵי כִיּוֹר – רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: מֵי מַעְיָן הֵן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שְׁאָר מֵימוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** since the *baraita* cannot be referring to the water of the Basin itself, as it is unfit for washing the innards, **is it not** referring to water **that is fit to** be **water of** the **Basin? Apparently,** the only reason such water could be preferable is because it **is flowing water.** The Gemara responds: The issue **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **the water of** the **Basin, Rabbi Yishmael says: It** must be **spring water,** i.e., flowing water, **and the Rabbis say: It** may be **another** type of **water.**

The *gemara* concedes the point: *alma mayim chayim ninhu* — the *braisa* does seem to imply that the *kiyor* requires *mayim chayim*. But rather than overturning Reish Lakish's ruling, the *gemara* responds: *tanai hi* — this is a dispute between *tannaim*. Rabbi Yochanan records: *mei kiyor, Rabbi Yishmael omer mei mayim hein* — Rabbi Yishmael holds that the *kiyor* requires true spring water (*mayim chayim*); *v'chachamim omrim she'ar meimot hein* — but the *chachamim* hold that any water is valid. The *Rambam* rules in accordance with the *chachamim*, permitting any water for the *kiyor*.

In summary, the picture that emerges from this *sugya* is as follows: the *kiyor* must contain enough water for four *kohanim*; a *kli shares* may also be used, provided it is sourced from a valid supply; any water is sufficient (according to the *chachamim* and the *Rambam*), and the supplementing principles analogous to the *mikveh* apply to the quantity required in the *kiyor* as well.

## Arel Kohen

### Arel Definition

The *gemara* now moves to a new topic: *arel* — the uncircumcised *kohen*. After the lengthy analysis of *bigdei kehunah* and *rechitzas yadayim v'raglayim*, and the conclusion that performing the *avodah* without them renders the service *mechulal* — derived from the word *chalal* — the *gemara* turns to the next disqualifying condition.

Who exactly is this uncircumcised *kohen*? Rashi explains that we are dealing with someone who could not undergo *bris milah* for medical reasons — a family with a history of hemophilia, for instance — not someone who has principled objections to the *mitzvah*. Tosafos extends this to include someone who failed to be circumcised due to fear of pain, provided the omission is not an ideological rejection of the *mitzvah*. A *kohen* who actively rejects *bris milah* as a matter of principle would be in the category of *mumar* or *min* — and as we will see shortly, such a person is independently excluded by the category of *arel lev*. The *arel* under discussion here is someone whose physical status is the disqualifying factor, not his beliefs.

### Source in Yechezkel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָרֵל מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּבָר זֶה מִתּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא לָמַדְנוּ, מִדִּבְרֵי יְחֶזְקֵאל בֶּן בּוּזִי לָמַדְנוּ: ״כׇּל בֶּן נֵכָר עֶרֶל לֵב וְעֶרֶל בָּשָׂר לֹא יָבֹא אֶל מִקְדָּשִׁי לְשָׁרְתֵנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a priest who is **uncircumcised** disqualifies sacrificial rites he performs. The Gemara elaborates: **From where do we** derive this? **Rav Ḥisda says: We did not learn this matter from the Torah of Moses, our teacher;** rather, **we learned** it **from the words of** the prophet **Ezekiel, son of Buzi: "No stranger, uncircumcised in heart or uncircumcised in flesh, shall enter into My Sanctuary to serve Me"** (Ezekiel 44:9).

The *gemara* asks the expected question: *menalan* — from where do we derive that an uncircumcised *kohen* may not perform the *avodah*, and that if he does, it is *mechulal*? The answer is striking. Rav Chisda states: *davar zeh miTorat Moshe Rabbeinu lo lamadnu, midivrei Yechezkel ben Buzi lamadnu* — this law we did not learn from the Torah of Moshe our teacher; we derived it from the words of Yechezkel son of Buzi. This is a genuinely surprising declaration. The general principle is that the Torah of Moshe is the ultimate source of law, and prophetic books are not normally the origin of halachic obligations. The *gemara* here is prepared to say outright that this particular law does not originate in the *Chamisha Chumshei Torah*, a position the *gemara* will address only obliquely.

### Yechezkel's Text

The relevant verse in Yechezkel reads: *kol ben neichar arel lev v'arel basar lo yavo el mikdashi* — any foreigner who is of uncircumcised heart or of uncircumcised flesh shall not enter My sanctuary.

### Lechalel = Mechulal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּמַחֲלִי עֲבוֹדָה? דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּהֲבִיאֲכֶם אֶת בְּנֵי נֵכָר עַרְלֵי לֵב וְעַרְלֵי בָשָׂר לִהְיוֹת בְּמִקְדָּשִׁי לְחַלֵּל אֶת בֵּיתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do we** derive **that he desecrates** the **service** after the fact? **As it is written: "In that you have brought in strangers, uncircumcised in heart or uncircumcised in flesh, to be in My Sanctuary, to profane My house"** (Ezekiel 44:7).

The *gemara* then establishes not just the prohibition but the invalidating effect: *umenalan demachli avoda* — from where do we know that such a *kohen*'s service is *mechulal*, rendered invalid? From the continuation of Yechezkel's prophecy: *behavi'achem benei neichar arelei lev v'arelei basar lihyot bemikdashi l'chalel et beiti* — when you brought foreigners of uncircumcised heart and uncircumcised flesh to be in My sanctuary *to desecrate* My house. The word *l'chalel* establishes that the service is *mechulal* — the same verbal root used in the earlier analysis of priestly garments and hand-washing.

### Ben Neichar Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בֶּן נֵכָר״ – יָכוֹל בֶּן נֵכָר מַמָּשׁ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֶרֶל לֵב״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בֶּן נֵכָר״? שֶׁנִּתְנַכְּרוּ מַעֲשָׂיו לְאָבִיו שֶׁבְּשָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states **"stranger";** one **might** have thought that this is referring to **an actual stranger,** i.e., a gentile. Therefore, **the verse states: "Uncircumcised in heart,"** to indicate that it is referring to a priest rather than a gentile. **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Stranger"?** It is referring to one **whose actions are** considered **estranged from his Father in Heaven,** i.e., an apostate, who sins regularly.

The *gemara* now parses the terms. A *braisa* is cited: *ben neichar* — one might think this refers literally to a non-Jew. But a non-Jew cannot serve as a *kohen* in the first place; there would be no *hava amina*. The *gemara* therefore explains that the term is glossed by the very next phrase: *arel lev*. The *ben neichar* is not a gentile but a Jew who has made himself foreign — *shenitnachru ma'asav l'aviv shebashamayim* — whose deeds have become estranged from his Father in Heaven, who has alienated himself from *Torah* and *mitzvos*. Rashi explains further: *nechshavim l'nochrim l'makom* — such a person is regarded as if he were a non-Jew in relation to God. This connects to the broader halachic concept — invoked frequently by the *Rambam* — of treating a *mechalel Shabbat* or similar transgressor as though he stands entirely outside the communal fold.

### Two Arel Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא עֶרֶל לֵב; עֶרֶל בָּשָׂר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעֶרֶל בָּשָׂר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And I have** derived **only** that **one uncircumcised in heart** is unfit to serve; **from where** is it derived that **one uncircumcised in flesh** is unfit as well? **The verse states: "Or uncircumcised in flesh."**

The *gemara* then asks: *v'ein li ela arel lev* — I have only derived the exclusion of someone who has rejected *Torah* and *mitzvos*; from where do I know that *arel basar* — someone physically uncircumcised — is also excluded? The answer: *talmud lomar v'arel basar* — the text continues with this phrase to exclude him as well.

The *gemara* then explains why both exclusions are necessary. *V'tzrichi* — both are needed, because one could not be derived from the other.

### Aesthetic vs. Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״עֶרֶל בָּשָׂר״ – מִשּׁוּם דִּמְאִיס; אֲבָל עֶרֶל לֵב, דְּלָא מְאִיס – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן עֶרֶל לֵב – מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין לִבּוֹ לַשָּׁמַיִם; אֲבָל עֶרֶל בָּשָׂר, דְּלִבּוֹ לַשָּׁמַיִם – אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** both phrases in the verse **are necessary. As, had the Merciful One written** only: **"Uncircumcised in flesh,"** one might think that only he is unfit **because he is disgusting** in that he possesses a foreskin, **but** concerning **one uncircumcised in heart, who is not disgusting, I will say** that he is **not** unfit. **And had** the verse **taught us** only that one **uncircumcised in heart** is unfit, one might think that only he is unfit, **because his heart is not** directed **toward Heaven, but one uncircumcised in flesh, whose heart is** directed **toward Heaven, I will say** that he is **not** unfit. Therefore, both phrases **are necessary.**

If the Torah had only excluded *arel basar*, one might have explained the reason as aesthetic concern — *mishum de'ma'is*, because it is repugnant. The Temple's standards of propriety extend to appearances even where no human observer is present; *mumin* that are invisible disqualify a *kohen* all the same. An uncircumcised *kohen* — particularly one from a family of hemophiliacs who bears no personal blame — is excluded not because of any spiritual deficiency but because the *Beis HaMikdash* demands a certain aesthetic wholeness. One might then have thought that *arel lev* — someone who has rejected God's will but whose body is physically whole — presents no aesthetic problem and should not be excluded. Hence the Torah must say both.

Conversely, if only *arel lev* were stated, the exclusion would be explained as *mishum d'ein libo lishmayim* — because his heart is not directed toward Heaven. One might then have thought that *arel basar*, whose heart is entirely *lishmayim* and whose omission of *bris milah* is medical or rooted in fear rather than ideology, should be valid. Hence again, both must be stated explicitly.

The *gemara* does not in this passage resolve the difficulty raised by Rav Chisda — that this law originates in Yechezkel rather than in the Torah of Moshe — beyond what was addressed earlier in the *sugya*, where the *gemara* indicated that the law was always a *masorah*, a tradition transmitted orally, and Yechezkel's prophecy made explicit what was already known from *Torah she'be'al peh*. Yet it is notable that the *gemara* is willing to state plainly that we cannot find the source in the five books of Moshe. The *gemara* will proceed in the next session to the category of *tamei*.
