# Yoma 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Separation Models

### Intro: Two Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְלָל דְּתַרְוַויְיהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ מִלּוּאִים כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן. דְּאִיתְּמַר: מִלּוּאִים, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, חַד אָמַר: כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן, וְחַד אָמַר: דָּבָר הַמְעַכֵּב לְדוֹרוֹת — מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן, שֶׁאֵין מְעַכֵּב לְדוֹרוֹת — אֵין מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the question Reish Lakish addressed to Rabbi Yoḥanan and the fact that Rabbi Yoḥanan accepted the premise of that question, we learn **by inference** that **both maintain** that with regard to the **inauguration,** failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration. **As it is stated: Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Ḥanina** disagree. **One said:** Failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration. **And one said: A matter that invalidates** offerings **throughout the generations invalidates** the inauguration; **a matter that does not invalidate** offerings **throughout the generations does not invalidate** the inauguration.

We pick up in the middle of daf 4b, approximately twelve lines from the bottom, in the midst of the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding the two models for the *kohen gadol*'s days of separation. Rabbi Yochanan holds that we learn the requirement from the *miluim* — the process of consecrating Aharon, his sons, and the *kohen gadol* for the *avodah* — and accordingly the separation is oriented around preparation for *avodah*. Reish Lakish, by contrast, holds that the source is the six days Moshe spent in the *anan* at the outset of his forty days following Har Sinai, before he entered the *anan* to be with God; accordingly, the focus is on the *kohen gadol*'s entry into the *kodesh kodashim* and is less *avodah*-centered. We also noted previously the position associated with Rav, that even Rabbi Yochanan's model — preparation for *avodah* — can be understood as elevating the *kohen gadol* to a higher status, perhaps even as a representative of Aharon himself, which would explain why the process applies only to the *kohen gadol* and not to the designated stand-in.

### Vayikra Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה וַיְדַבֵּר״, לָמָּה הִקְדִּים קְרִיאָה לְדִיבּוּר? לִימְּדָה תּוֹרָה דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמַר אָדָם דָּבָר לַחֲבֵירוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קוֹרֵהוּ. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לֹא יֹאמַר אָדָם דָּבָר לַחֲבֵירוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קוֹרֵהוּ. ״לֵאמֹר״, אָמַר רַבִּי (מוּסְיָא בַּר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מַסְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מוּסְיָא) רַבָּה: מִנַּיִן לָאוֹמֵר דָּבָר לַחֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁהוּא בְּבַל יֹאמַר עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ אֱמוֹר — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֵלָיו מֵאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֵאמֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse says: **"And He called unto Moses, and** the Lord **spoke** unto him from within the Tent of Meeting, saying" (Leviticus 1:1). **Why** does the verse mention **calling before speaking,** and God did not speak to him at the outset? **The Torah is teaching etiquette: A person** should **not say anything to another unless he calls him** first. This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina, as Rabbi Ḥanina said: A person should not say anything to another unless he calls him** first. With regard to the term concluding the verse: **"Saying," Rabbi Musya, grandson of Rabbi Masya, said in the name of Rabbi Musya the Great: From where** is it derived with regard **to one who tells another** some **matter, that** it is incumbent upon the latter **not** to **say it** to others **until** the former explicitly **says to him: Go and tell others? As it is stated: "And the Lord spoke to him from** within **the Tent of Meeting, saying [*lemor*]."** *Lemor* is a contraction of *lo emor*, meaning: Do not say. One must be given permission before transmitting information.

Within that broader discussion of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, the *gemara* engages with the *pesukim* describing God calling to Moshe *mitoch ha'anan*. The question is: what was the purpose of *vayikra el Moshe* at Har Sinai, before the *aseret hadibrot*? The *gemara* answers: *lachlok lo kavod* — to bestow honor upon Moshe. The *gemara* then pivots to the *vayikra el Moshe* at the opening of *Vayikra*, where it is similarly followed by *vayedaber Hashem elav*. The question: why does *kriya* precede *dibur* — why does God first call to Moshe before speaking? The answer is *lilmedcha derech eretz shelo yomar adam davar lachavero ela im ken kora'o*: it teaches proper conduct — one does not simply begin speaking to someone without first addressing them by name and inviting them into the conversation. God called to Moshe, in effect saying, "Moshe, I wish to speak with you," and only then began. This teaching is identified as supporting the principle of Rabbi Chanina, who stated the same rule as a general norm of human conduct.

### Moshe Distance

As we noted at the close of the previous session, the opening *pasuk* of *Vayikra* must be read in juxtaposition with the final *pasuk* of *Shemot*: Moshe could not enter the *ohel moed* because the *anan* rested upon it — *ki shachan alav ha'anan* — and so God had to call out to Moshe, drawing him into at least some form of encounter. A striking *midrash* addresses the apparent asymmetry: at Har Sinai, Moshe entered into the *anan*, yet at the *ohel moed* he did not. Two answers are offered — that at Har Sinai God compelled Moshe into the *anan*, and that God carved a path for him through it. Both responses grapple with the profound question that the *gemara* returns to repeatedly in the context of Har Sinai: the nature and very possibility of a genuine human-divine encounter. These questions are alive precisely because entering the *anan* is not something a human being simply does of his own accord.

## Calling Before Speaking

### Derech Eretz Principle

The *gemara* identifies the principle underlying *vayikra el Moshe* as *derech eretz*: one does not speak to another person without first calling to them. The formulation is *shelo yomar adam davar lachavero ela im ken kora'o* — a person should not address something to his fellow unless he has first called to him. This is basic social conduct: before launching into substance, one acknowledges the other person, says their name, invites them into the exchange. The *gemara* cites this as supporting the teaching of Rabbi Chanina, who articulated the same principle independently.

### Privacy Assumption

The *gemara* proceeds to the word *lemor* at the close of the opening *pasuk* of *Vayikra*: *vayedaber Hashem elav me'ohel moed lemor*. The question becomes: what does *lemor* add? Rav Menashya Rabba derives from here a fundamental principle of confidentiality — *minayin la'omer davar lachavero shehu be'bal tomar ad she'yomar lo lech emor*: from where do we know that if someone tells you something, you are prohibited from repeating it until they explicitly tell you to go and say it? The source, as the *gemara* reads it, is this very *pasuk*. Rashi understands it as a play on *lemor* — *lav emor*, do not say — meaning one may not repeat what was told to him unless explicitly authorized. The Maharsha understands it more directly from the *pshat*: since the *pasuk* continues with *daber el Bnei Yisrael*, God had to explicitly instruct Moshe to transmit the communication outward. Had that instruction not been given, Moshe would have had no license to repeat what he heard, even to others, even if the content itself was not negative. This is a critically important principle, and one that deserves more emphasis in contemporary teaching. The prohibition of *rekhilut* is understood as the prohibition against being a gossip — carrying information from person to person — and this applies even when the content is not negative. The deeper point is that one must assume all verbal communication is private unless explicitly released.

### Speech as Relational

The deeper insight is that every verbal communication is relational — it belongs to the relationship between the two parties involved, not to the world at large. It is not simply information that happens to have been transmitted; it is an exchange between two people, and treating it as freely shareable public information violates that relational dimension. This is not only about the other person's privacy but about the fundamental nature of speech as a bond between speaker and listener.

One small curiosity in passing: by the *vayikra* to Avraham, the text reads *vayikra el Avraham Avraham* — his name is said twice. Here, it reads only *vayikra el Moshe*, without the doubling. This is a good question and is left open.

## Miluim Essentials

### Both Hold Essentials

The *gemara* now returns to the core issue. It observes: *mikhlal de-tarvaihu sevira lehu miluim kol ha-katuv ba-hen me-akkeiv ba-hen* — it is implicit from the structure of the debate that both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree that in the *miluim* itself, every written component was indispensable. The logic is as follows: Reish Lakish challenged Rabbi Yochanan by saying that if the *miluim* is the model, one should conclude that just as in the *miluim* every requirement is essential, so too the seven-day separation of the *kohen gadol* should be essential *bedi'avad*. Rabbi Yochanan did not dismiss the premise — he did not say "who told you everything in the *miluim* is *me'akev*?" His silence on that point confirms that both disputants share that assumption.

### Miluim vs. Future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הוּא דְּאָמַר כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן. מִדְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִי מָה מִלּוּאִים כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן, וְלָא קָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי. תִּסְתַּיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Conclude** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan is the one who said:** Failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration. This may be concluded **from** the fact **that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says to Rabbi Yoḥanan: Just as** with regard to the **inauguration,** failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration, so too is the *halakha* with regard to Yom Kippur, **and** Rabbi Yoḥanan **did not respond** and did **not** say **anything,** indicating that he agreed. The Gemara states: **Conclude** that this indeed is the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *gemara* then introduces a related dispute, explicitly attributed: *de-itmar miluim* — there is a recorded debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Chanina. One holds *kol ha-katuv ba-hen me-akkeiv ba-hen* — every single element written in the *miluim*, if not performed, invalidates. The other holds *davar ha-me-akkeiv le-dorot me-akkeiv ba-hen she-eino me-akkeiv le-dorot ein me-akkeiv ba-hen* — only those elements of the *miluim* that are generally essential in future generations are essential in the *miluim* itself; elements that are not *me'akev* in other contexts are likewise not *me'akev* in the *miluim*.

The *gemara* then resolves the attribution: *tistayeem* — we can conclude that Rabbi Yochanan is the one who holds everything is *me'akev*, since Reish Lakish challenged him on precisely that basis and he did not reject the premise. Rabbi Chanina, then, is the one holding the more limited position.

The question becomes: *mai beinaihu* — what are the concrete cases in dispute? That is, which components of the *miluim* are not generally *me'akev* in other contexts, and would therefore be at the center of this debate?

### Semicha Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: סְמִיכָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — סְמִיכָה מְעַכְּבָא, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מְעַכֵּב לְדוֹרוֹת אֵין מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — סְמִיכָה לָא מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** The practical difference **between them** relates to the question of **placing hands** on the head of an animal brought as an offering. According **to the one who said:** Failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard, including details that do not invalidate offerings throughout the generations, **invalidates** the inauguration, failure to perform the **placing** of **hands** on the head of the animal **also invalidates** the inauguration. According **to the one who said: A matter that does not invalidate** offerings **throughout the generations does not invalidate** the inauguration, failure to perform the **placing** of **hands** on the head of the animal **does not invalidate** the inauguration.

Rav Yosef identifies *semikhah* as the first point of disagreement. The *miluim* involved *semikhah* on the *korbanot* — Aharon and his sons leaned their hands upon the offerings. And yet in general, while *semikhah* is required on *korbanot*, failure to perform it does not invalidate the offering *bedi'avad*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that everything in the *miluim* is *me'akev*, even the *semikhah* on the *korbanot* of the *miluim* would have been indispensable. According to Rabbi Chanina, since *semikhah* is not *me'akev* in other contexts, it would not have been *me'akev* in the *miluim* either.

### Blood and Kapara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְדוֹרוֹת מְנָא לַן דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְסָמַךְ״ ״וְנִרְצָה״. וְכִי סְמִיכָה מְכַפֶּרֶת? וַהֲלֹא אֵין כַּפָּרָה אֶלָּא בְּדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַדָּם הוּא בַּנֶּפֶשׁ יְכַפֵּר״! וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְסָמַךְ״ ״וְנִרְצָה״? שֶׁאִם עֲשָׂאָהּ לִסְמִיכָה שְׁיָרֵי מִצְוָה — מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ לֹא כִּפֵּר, וְכִפֵּר!
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the *halakhot* of offerings that apply **throughout the generations** the Gemara asks: **From where do we derive** that failure to place hands on the head of the animal **does not invalidate** the offering? The Gemara answers: **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states: **"And he shall place** his hand on the head of the burnt-offering, **and it shall be accepted** for him to atone on his behalf" (Leviticus 1:4). **Does** the **placing** of **hands atone** for one's sins? **Isn't atonement** accomplished **only by** the sprinkling of **the blood, as it is stated: "For it is the blood that makes atonement by reason of the life"** (Leviticus 17:11)? **And** for **what** purpose, then, **does the verse state: "And he shall place…and it shall be accepted"?** It teaches that if **one deemed** the ritual of **placing hands** to be **a peripheral aspect of the mitzva** and consequently failed to perform it, **the verse ascribes to him** status **as though he did not achieve** optimal **atonement; and** nevertheless, the offering **atones** for his sins. Apparently, failure to lay hands on the offering does not invalidate the offering throughout the generations, as atonement can be achieved without it. Nevertheless, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, failure to lay hands on the offering invalidates the offerings brought during the inauguration.

The *gemara* then asks: *ul'dorot minalan delo me'akkeva* — from where do we know that *semikhah* is not *me'akev* in future generations? The answer is a well-known *braisa*: *v'chi semikhah mechaperes* — does *semikhah* actually achieve *kapara*? *V'halo ein kapara ela b'dam* — ultimately, *kapara* is achieved only through the blood, as the *pasuk* states: *ki hadam hu banefesh yechaper*. The *gemara* understands this *pasuk* to mean that the blood, and the blood alone, determines whether a *korban* is valid *bedi'avad*. This encompasses the *shechita* and the *zerikat hadam*; everything else, while required, does not ultimately prevent *kapara* if omitted. The word *ela* is decisive here — it is not merely that blood is *part* of *kapara*, but that it is the *only* determinant of *kapara*. The *gemara* then presses: *u'mah talmud lomar v'samach v'nirtza* — if blood alone achieves *kapara*, why does the Torah juxtapose *semikhah* with *v'nirtza*, implying that the offering is accepted on account of the *semikhah*?

### Semicha Paradox

The *braisa* resolves this with a striking formulation: *she'im asa l'semikhah shiyurei mitzvah ma'aleh alav hakatuv k'ilu lo chiper v'chiper* — if one treated *semikhah* as a marginal detail, as something optional, and accordingly omitted it, the Torah regards it as though he did not achieve *kapara* — and yet he did achieve *kapara*. This is a genuine paradox. Technically, *bedi'avad*, as long as the *dam* was handled correctly, the person has fulfilled his obligation. But *semikhah* is not merely a procedural nicety — it is part of the *kapara* process itself. Its absence does not prevent *kapara* from taking effect, but it means the *kapara* is incomplete in some meaningful sense. The Torah therefore treats the person as though he failed to achieve *kapara*, even while acknowledging that he did. This formulation is applied specifically to *semikhah* here, and as we will see, it is applied in an analogous way to *tenuphah* as well.

The key distinction is: *semikhah* is not *me'akev* in the technical sense that prevents one from being *yotzei*, but it is part of the *kapara* process. Its omission does not invalidate; it impoverishes.

## Non-Essential Components

### Waving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: תְּנוּפָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — מְעַכְּבָא. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מְעַכֵּב לְדוֹרוֹת אֵין מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — לָא מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The issue of **waving** the offering is the practical difference **between** the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Ḥanina. According **to the one who said:** Failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration, failure to wave the offering also **invalidates** the inauguration. **And** according **to the one who said: A matter that does not invalidate** offerings **throughout the generations does not invalidate** the inauguration, failure to wave the offering **does not invalidate** the inauguration.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak identifies *tenuphah* — the waving of portions of the *korbanot* and the bread — as the next point of disagreement. *Tenuphah* is a standard feature of certain *korbanot*, such as *shelamim*, where the *shok hayamin* and *chazeh hatenuphah* are waved. The question is whether failure to perform *tenuphah* in the *miluim* would have been *me'akev*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, yes; according to Rabbi Chanina, since *tenuphah* is not *me'akev* in future generations, it would not have been *me'akev* in the *miluim* either.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְדוֹרוֹת מְנָא לַן דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״לִתְנוּפָה לְכַפֵּר״. וְכִי תְּנוּפָה מְכַפֶּרֶת? וַהֲלֹא אֵין כַּפָּרָה אֶלָּא בְּדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַדָּם הוּא בַּנֶּפֶשׁ יְכַפֵּר״! וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לִתְנוּפָה לְכַפֵּר״? שֶׁאִם עֲשָׂאָהּ לִתְנוּפָה שְׁיָרֵי מִצְוָה — מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ לֹא כִּפֵּר, וְכִפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the *halakhot* of offerings **throughout the generations,** the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that failure to wave the offering **does not invalidate** the offering? The Gemara answers: **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that the verse says: "He shall take one male lamb as a guilt-offering **to be waved to make atonement** for him" (Leviticus 14:21). **Does waving** the offering **atone** for one's sins? **Isn't atonement** accomplished **only by** the sprinkling of **the blood, as it is stated: "For it is the blood that makes atonement by reason of the life"** (Leviticus 17:11)? **And** for **what** purpose, then, **does the verse state: To be waved to make atonement?** It teaches that if **one deemed** the ritual of **waving** to be **a peripheral aspect of the mitzva** and therefore failed to perform it, **the verse ascribes to him** status **as though he did not achieve** optimal **atonement; and** nevertheless, the offering **atones** for his sins on his behalf.

The *gemara* then asks: *ul'dorot minalan delo me'akkeva*? How do we know the separation is not *me'akev* in future generations? The answer is derived from our own *mishnah*: the *mishnah* speaks of preparing a stand-in *kohen gadol* for Yom Kippur, yet it does not say that the stand-in must undergo a seven-day separation. This indicates that while separation may be required, its absence does not invalidate. One can say either that the concept of separation never applies in future generations at all, or — more instructively — that even where it does apply, it is not *me'akev*. The *mishnah*'s silence regarding the stand-in's separation supports the latter reading.

### Seven-Day Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: פְּרִישַׁת שִׁבְעָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — מְעַכְּבָא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב לְדוֹרוֹת אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — לָא מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The issue of **sequestering** the priest for **seven** days is the practical difference **between** the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Ḥanina. According **to the one who said:** Failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration, failure to sequester the priest for seven days also **invalidates** the inauguration. **And** according **to the one who said: A matter that does not invalidate** offerings **throughout the generations does not invalidate** the inauguration, failure to sequester the priest for seven days **does not invalidate** the inauguration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְדוֹרוֹת מְנָא לַן דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא? מִדְּקָא תָנֵי ״מַתְקִינִין״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״מַפְרִישִׁין״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the *halakhot* of offerings **throughout the generations,** the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that failure to sequester the priest for seven days **does not invalidate** the offering? The Gemara answers: It is derived **from the fact that it is taught** in the mishna: And **they** would **designate** another priest in his stead, **and it is not taught:** The Sages **remove** the designated priest from his house, despite the possibility that ultimately he might replace the High Priest and perform the Yom Kippur service. Apparently, sequestering is not essential.

Rav Papa identifies *prisha shiva* — the seven-day separation — as the next item. This is, of course, the very issue that Reish Lakish used to challenge Rabbi Yochanan: if the *miluim* is the model, why should the seven-day separation of the *kohen gadol* not be *me'akev bedi'avad*? Rabbi Yochanan had no answer, confirming that he holds it was *me'akev* in the *miluim*. Rabbi Chanina, by contrast, would hold that since we do not find the separation to be *me'akev* in future generations, it would not have been *me'akev* in the *miluim* either.

One must be careful about what *me'akev* means in this context. By *semikhah* and *tenuphah*, the question is whether a particular act during the *avodah* of the *korban* was indispensable. Here, *me'akev* means something broader: would the failure to maintain the seven-day separation in the *mishkan* have prevented the consecration — of the *mishkan* itself and of the *kohanim* — from taking effect? The goal of the *miluim* was not just the offering of *korbanot* but the inauguration of the *mishkan* and the installation of the *kohanim* into their *avodah*. So *me'akev* here refers to *me'akev* the *miluim* as a whole, not merely a specific *korban*.

### Dressing & Anointing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: רִיבּוּי שִׁבְעָה וּמְשִׁיחָה שִׁבְעָה, אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — מְעַכְּבָא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מְעַכֵּב לְדוֹרוֹת אֵין מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן — לָא מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The issue of the priest performing the service with the **multiple** garments of the High Priest for **seven** days and serving with **anointment** for **seven** days is the practical difference **between** the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Ḥanina. According **to the one who said:** Failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration, failure to serve with multiple garments and anointment for seven days also **invalidates** the inauguration. **And** according **to the one who said: A matter that does not invalidate** offerings **throughout the generations does not invalidate** the inauguration, failure to serve with multiple garments and anointment for seven days **does not invalidate** the inauguration.

Ravina identifies *ribui shiva u'meshicha shiva* — the seven days of dressing and the seven days of anointing — as the next and most far-reaching item on the list. During the *miluim*, Moshe dressed Aharon and his sons in the *bigdei kehunah* day after day, and anointed Aharon and the garments. This was the very act of consecration. But in future generations, we do not find that the failure to repeat this process for all seven days is *me'akev*.

This is a striking escalation. If one could still accept that failing to do *semikhah* or *tenuphah* does not invalidate, since those are relatively peripheral acts during the *avodah*, the idea that the entire dressing and anointing process could be dispensed with seems radical. After all, the dressing and anointing were not incidental features of the *miluim* — they were its very substance, the mechanism of consecration. And yet, says Rabbi Chanina, since this level of detail is not *me'akev le-dorot*, it would not have been *me'akev* in the *miluim* either.

### Tetzaveh vs. Acharei Mot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְדוֹרוֹת מְנָא לַן דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְכִפֵּר הַכֹּהֵן אֲשֶׁר יִמְשַׁח אוֹתוֹ וַאֲשֶׁר יְמַלֵּא אֶת יָדוֹ לְכַהֵן תַּחַת אָבִיו״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the *halakhot* of offerings **throughout the generations,** the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that failure to serve with multiple garments and anointment for seven days **does not invalidate** the offering? The Gemara answers: **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: For **what** purpose **does the verse state: "And the priest who shall be anointed and who shall be consecrated to serve in his father's stead shall make the atonement"** (Leviticus 16:32)? If it comes to teach that all service must be performed by the High Priest, it is already written with regard to the Yom Kippur service that it must be performed by Aaron, the High Priest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁבְעַת יָמִים יִלְבָּשָׁם הַכֹּהֵן תַּחְתָּיו מִבָּנָיו״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נִתְרַבָּה שִׁבְעָה וְנִמְשַׁח שִׁבְעָה. נִתְרַבָּה שִׁבְעָה וְנִמְשַׁח יוֹם אֶחָד, נִתְרַבָּה יוֹם אֶחָד וְנִמְשַׁח שִׁבְעָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר יִמְשַׁח אוֹתוֹ וַאֲשֶׁר יְמַלֵּא אֶת יָדוֹ״ — מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Since it is stated: "Seven days shall the son that is priest in his stead don them"** (Exodus 29:30), **I** derive **only** that one who donned the **multiple** garments of the High Priest for **seven** days **and was anointed seven** days assumes the position of High Priest and may perform the service on Yom Kippur. However, with regard to whether one who donned the **multiple** garments for **seven** days **and was anointed** for **one** day, or one who donned the **multiple** garments for **one** day **and was anointed** for **seven** days is thereby inaugurated as High Priest, **from where** are those cases derived? Therefore, **the verse states: "Who shall be anointed and who shall be consecrated"; in any case** he is appointed High Priest, even if either anointment or donning the garments did not continue for seven days.

To understand what is and is not *me'akev le-dorot* regarding dressing and anointing, the *gemara* turns to the relevant *pesukim*. In *parshat Tetzaveh*, where the command of the *miluim* is given, it says: *uvigdei hakodesh asher le'aharon yihyu levanav acharav lemoshcha vahem ulemalei vam et yadam* — the sacred garments of Aharon will belong to his sons after him, to anoint them through the garments and to install them into office. Then the next *pasuk* says *shivat yamim yilbasham hakohen tachtav mibanav asher yavo el ohel moed lisharet bakodesh* — for seven days the *kohen* who succeeds him shall wear them. On the face of it, this seems to require seven days of wearing the garments for any future *kohen* who will enter the *ohel moed* — which Rashi identifies as referring to the *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur.

Yet the *parsha* at the end of *Acharei Mot*, when describing the future *kohen gadol* who will perform the Yom Kippur *avodah*, says only: *asher yimshach oto va'asher yemale et yado* — the one who was anointed and whose hands were filled, that is, who was dressed in the garments. No mention of seven days.

### Minimum Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן רִיבּוּי שִׁבְעָה לְכַתְּחִלָּה, מְשִׁיחָה שִׁבְעָה לְכַתְּחִלָּה מְנָא לַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the fact that when the High Priest is appointed, there is a requirement of donning **multiple** garments for **seven** days ***ab initio*;** however, **from where do we** derive the requirement of **anointment** for **seven** days ***ab initio*?** According to Ravina, there is a requirement to anoint the priest on each of the seven days *ab initio*, even though failure to do so does not invalidate the offering throughout the generations. From where is that requirement derived?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטַהּ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבִגְדֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר לְאַהֲרֹן יִהְיוּ לְבָנָיו אַחֲרָיו לְמׇשְׁחָה בָהֶם וּלְמַלֵּא בָם אֶת יָדָם״, אִיתַּקַּשׁ מְשִׁיחָה לְרִיבּוּי: מָה רִיבּוּי שִׁבְעָה, אַף מְשִׁיחָה שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say:** It is derived **from** the fact **that the verse:** "And the priest who shall be anointed and who shall be consecrated to serve in his father's stead shall make the atonement," **is necessary to exclude** requirements derived from other sources, i.e., that both donning multiple garments and anointment must be for seven days. Apparently, anointment for seven days is required *ab initio*. **And if you wish, say** instead that it is derived from **that** which **the verse states: "And the sacred garments of Aaron shall be for his sons after him, to be anointed in them and to be consecrated in them"** (Exodus 29:29). **Anointment is juxtaposed** in this verse **to** donning **multiple** garments: **Just as** donning **multiple** garments is required **for seven** days *ab initio*, **so too, anointment** is required **for seven** days *ab initio*.

A *braisa* is cited that resolves this apparent contradiction: *ein li ela nitraba shiva venimshach shiva* — I know only that if one was dressed for seven days and anointed for seven days, the new *kohen gadol* has achieved his status. But what about *nitraba shiva venimshach yom echad* — dressed seven days but anointed only one day — or the reverse? *Minayin*? From where do I know that *bedi'avad* even that suffices? The answer is *talmud lomar asher yimshach oto va'asher yemale et yado mikol makom* — because *Acharei Mot* uses the simple formulation without specifying seven days, *bedi'avad* even a single instance of each suffices.

This is the basis for the halachic ruling codified by the Rambam in *Kelei Hamikdash*. The Rambam states that in *Bayit Sheni*, where there was no *shemen hamishcha*, a *kohen gadol* was consecrated through *ribuy begadim* alone — wearing the eight garments repeatedly for seven days. He describes the process: the *kohen* would dress in the eight garments, remove them, and replace them each day for seven days, as the *pasuk* says *shivat yamim yilbasham hakohen tachtav mibanav*. Similarly, just as dressing is seven days, so too anointing is seven days — *ukesheim sheribuy begadim shiva kach meshicha shiva*. Yet *bedi'avad*, if he performed the *avodah* before completing all seven days of dressing or all seven days of anointing, the *avodah* is valid, *ho'il venistarbeh o nimshach pa'am achat* — since he was dressed or anointed even once.

The Ra'avad adds a fascinating qualification: this requirement of seven days applied specifically to prepare the *kohen gadol* for *avodat Yom HaKippurim* — for entering the *kodesh kodashim* — not for the ordinary status of *kohen gadol*. The *pasuk* in *Tetzaveh* itself, with its phrase *asher yavo el ohel moed*, supports this: Rashi reads it as referring to Yom Kippur entry. This nicely accords with the Soloveitchik reading that the seven-day process creates a distinct *kohen gadol* status specifically oriented toward *avodat Yom HaKippurim*, over and above ordinary *kehuna gedolah*.

The upshot for our debate: Rabbi Chanina holds that the minimum for future generations — even a single day of dressing and a single day of anointing — establishes the baseline, and therefore had the *miluim* been conducted with only that minimum, it would have been valid. Rabbi Yochanan holds that the first time around, every detail of the seven-day process was absolutely essential.

## Kakah and Textual Proof

### Kakah Exactness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בִּיסְנָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו כָּכָה״. ״כָּכָה״ עִיכּוּבָא הוּא. תִּינַח כׇּל מִילְּתָא דִּכְתִיבָא בְּהַאי עִנְיָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § After ascertaining the halakhic distinctions between the opinions of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Ḥanina with regard to the inauguration, the Gemara proceeds to analyze the rationales for those opinions. **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the one who said:** Failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the inauguration? **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Bisna said** that **the verse states: "And so shall you do to Aaron and to his sons** according to all that I have commanded you, seven days shall you consecrate them" (Exodus 29:35). The term: **So,** teaches that failure to perform the ritual precisely in this manner **invalidates** the inauguration. The Gemara asks: That works out **well** as a source that **all** matters that **are written in** the context of **this topic** of inauguration **in the book of Exodus invalidate** the inauguration.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Bisna cites the *pasuk* from *Ve'ata Tetzaveh*, at the close of the *miluim* command: *ve'asita le'aharon ulevanav kakha kechol asher tziviti otacha* — you shall do for Aharon and his sons *kakha*, exactly like this, as all that I have commanded you. *Kakha ikuva hu* — the word *kakha* signals that the requirement is exact. Every detail specified must be performed precisely as stated; nothing may be omitted. This is the textual anchor for the position that all is *me'akev*.

### Pesach Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילְּתָא דְּלָא כְּתִיבָא בְּהַאי עִנְיָנָא מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: יָלֵיף ״פֶּתַח״ ״פֶּתַח״. רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא אָמַר: ״וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם אֶת מִשְׁמֶרֶת ה׳״ — עִכּוּבָא. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: ״כִּי כֵן צֻוֵּיתִי״ — עִכּוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** However, with regard to **matters that are not written in that context,** but are written in the portion of the inauguration in Leviticus, **from where do we derive** that they invalidate the inauguration? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** One **derives a** verbal analogy between the word opening that appears in the two portions of the inauguration. It is written in the command concerning the inauguration: "The **opening** of the Tent of Meeting" (Exodus 29:32), and in its fulfillment it is written: "The **opening** of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 8:31). Failure to perform the matters written in both portions invalidates the inauguration. **Rav Mesharshiyya said:** The verbal analogy is unnecessary, as the conclusion can be derived directly from the verse written with regard to the implementation of the inauguration: **"And keep the charge of the Lord,** that you not die, for so I am commanded" (Leviticus 8:35). The emphasis on this being the charge of the Lord comes to teach that failure to perform all the details mentioned in the implementation of the command **invalidates** the inauguration.  **Rav Ashi says:** The phrase: **For so I am commanded,** is the source from which it is derived that all the details written in both portions **invalidate** the inauguration.

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty: *teinach* — that works well for everything written in *Tetzaveh*. But what about things performed in *Tzav* — where the *miluim* was actually enacted — that do not appear in *Tetzaveh*? Rashi identifies the *urim ve'tumim* as one such element: it is mentioned in *Tzav* but not in *Tetzaveh*. If *kakha* in *Tetzaveh* is the source for *me'akev*, how do we extend that to elements appearing only in *Tzav*?

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak answers: *yalif pesach pesach* — a *gezeirah shavah* is made on the word *pesach* (*petach ohel moed*), which appears repeatedly and somewhat superfluously in both *Tetzaveh* and *Tzav*. Through this *gezeirah shavah*, whatever is *me'akev* in *Tetzaveh* is equally *me'akev* in *Tzav*.

### Three Proofs

Two additional proofs are offered. Rav Mesharshiya cites *veshamrtem et mishmeret Hashem* from *Tzav* — the injunction to guard the charge of God — which in itself underscores that everything recorded there must be scrupulously observed, and carries the force of *ikuva*. Rav Ashi cites the phrase *ki chein tziveisi* — "because thus I was commanded" — which Moshe invokes in the context of the *miluim*; this too signals that every element is *me'akev*, since it frames the entire undertaking as an exacting divine command.

## Eighth Day Emphasis

### Nadav Avihu Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי כֵן צֻוֵּיתִי״, ״כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוֵּיתִי״, ״כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳״. ״כִּי כֵן צֻוֵּיתִי״ — בַּאֲנִינוּת יֹאכְלוּהָ. ״כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוֵּיתִי״ — בִּשְׁעַת מַעֲשֶׂה אָמַר לָהֶם. ״כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳״ — וְלֹא מֵאֵלַי אֲנִי אוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos that phrase, the Gemara cites a related halakhic midrash. **The Sages taught:** In the context of the implementation of the inauguration, three variations of the phrase appear: **"For so I am commanded"** (Leviticus 8:35); **"as I commanded"** (Leviticus 10:18); and **"as God has commanded"** (Leviticus 10:15). What does this repetition teach? From the phrase: **"For so I am commanded,"** it is derived that even in a state of **acute mourning,** on the first day after the death of a relative, one must **eat** the offering. God stated the verse: **"As I commanded," at the time of the incident** just after the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, when Aaron and his sons were in a state of acute mourning. And when Moses states: **"As God commanded,"** he is saying: The command is from God **and** it is **not from my** own initiative that **I am saying** it.

Since we concluded with the phrase *ki chein tziveisi*, the *gemara* pursues a digression: the *baraisa* records that this phrase — along with its variants *ka'asher tziveisi* and *ka'asher tziva Hashem* — appears three times on Yom HaShmini, the eighth day. Why this threefold repetition? The context is important: after seven days of the *miluim* ritual, the eighth day arrives as the culmination. Aharon brings his first *korban* as *kohen gadol*, the glory of God appears, and then Nadav and Avihu, sons of Aharon, bring an unauthorized offering and die. In the aftermath of this tragedy, Moshe continues to instruct Aharon and his surviving sons to carry on with the *avodah*.

### Mincha Eating

The first instance of *ki chein tziveisi* appears when Moshe instructs Aharon and his sons to eat the remaining *mincha*: *kechu et hamincha hanoseres me'ishei Hashem ve'ochuha matzot etzel hamizbe'ach ki chein tziveisi*. Despite the fact that Aharon and his sons are *onanim* — in the state of acute mourning for their sons and brothers who have just died — Moshe tells them they must eat the *mincha* anyway, and grounds this in the fact that he was commanded to say so.

### Chattas Question

The second instance — *ka'asher tziveisi* — comes when Moshe observes that the *se'ir hachattas* has been burned rather than eaten. He challenges Aharon: *achol tochlu osa bakodesh ka'asher tziveisi* — you should have eaten it in the sacred precinct, as I commanded. Here again the phrase appears in the context of insisting that the *avodah* must continue despite the mourning, with Moshe pointing to his own *tzivui* as the mandate.

### Shlomim Permission

The third instance — *ka'asher tziva Hashem* — precedes the episode of the *chattas* in the sequence of *pesukim*. Here Moshe instructs them to eat the meat of the *shelamim* — the *shok haterumah* and the *chazeh hatenuphah* — even while they are *onanim*: *velach ulevane'icha ito lechok olam ka'asher tziva Hashem*. This too grounds the instruction in divine command rather than Moshe's own authority.

### Rashi's Reading

Rashi explains the significance of the third instance — *ka'asher tziva Hashem*, "as God commanded" — by noting that here Moshe is explicitly distinguishing his words from his own authority: *velo me'elai ani omer* — "I am not saying this on my own." After the tragedy of Nadav and Avihu, and after the difficult instruction to eat the *mincha* and *shelamim* in a state of acute mourning, Moshe underscores that all of this comes directly from God. He is not improvising. Rashi notes that this statement is made in the context of reassuring them after they had already burned the *chattas*: even now, continue to follow the divine directive, because I am only transmitting what God has commanded. Whether the precise sequence of *pesukim* fully supports Rashi's reading is a matter for consideration, but the essential point is clear: Moshe's three invocations of divine command on the eighth day serve to underscore the non-negotiable, God-given character of everything he has instructed.

## Missing Components

### Michnasayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: מִכְנָסַיִם אֵין כְּתוּבִין בַּפָּרָשָׁה, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְזֶה הַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה לָהֶם לְקַדֵּשׁ אֹתָם לְכַהֵן״ — לְהָבִיא הַמִּכְנָסַיִם וַעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the matters mentioned that are not explicit in the portion, the Gemara cites that which **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: Trousers** are one of the priestly vestments worn during the inauguration, but they **are not written in the** Torah **portion. When** the verse **says: "And this is the matter that you shall do for them to sanctify them for** My **service"** (Exodus 29:1), the superfluous word: And, which appears at the beginning of the verse, comes to add to that which was written previously and **to include trousers and the tenth of an ephah** offered by a priest on the day that he begins his service.

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina raises a difficulty: the *michnasayim* — the priestly breeches — are never mentioned in the *parsha* of the *miluim* command when it speaks of dressing Aharon and his sons. Yet they are clearly part of the *bigdei kehunah*. One view holds that the phrase *vezeh hadavar asher ta'aseh lahem lekadeish osam lekahen li* — "this is the thing you shall do for them to sanctify them to serve Me" — includes the *michnasayim*, since *zeh* points back to everything previously described in *Tetzaveh*, where the *michnasayim* do appear among the priestly garments. The *gemara* accepts this: since the *parsha* commanding the *miluim* is at the close of *Tetzaveh*, and *Tetzaveh* opens with a full account of all the priestly garments including the *michnasayim*, the word *vezeh* contextually draws all of those garments into the *miluim* requirement.

### Esheris Ha'eiphah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מִכְנָסַיִם כְּתִיבִי בְּעִנְיָנָא דִבְגָדִים, אֶלָּא עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה מְנָא לַן? אָתְיָא ״זֶה״ ״זֶה״ מִ״וְּזֶה קׇרְבַּן אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, trousers** can be derived, as the verse **is written** in the context **of the matter of** priestly **garments** detailed adjacent to the portion of the inauguration. **However,** with regard to **the tenth of an ephah, from where do we** derive that there is an obligation to offer it during the inauguration? The Gemara answers: It is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the word **this** that appears in one verse and the word **this** that appears in another. It is written: **"This is the offering of Aaron and of his sons, which they shall offer to the Lord** in the day when he is anointed, **a tenth of an ephah"** (Leviticus 6:13). And in the verse cited above in the context of the inauguration it says: "And this is the matter that you shall do for them," which teaches that there is an obligation to offer a tenth of an ephah during the inauguration.

The same phrase *vezeh hadavar* is also used to derive the inclusion of the *asiris ha'eiphah* — the daily meal-offering — which a new *kohen* brings upon first entering the *avodah*, and which the *kohen gadol* brings in halves each day. This offering is not mentioned explicitly in the *miluim* parsha either. The *gemara* derives its inclusion through a *gezeirah shavah* of *zeh zeh*: the *parsha* elsewhere that introduces the *asiris ha'eiphah* also begins with the word *zeh*, and the word *vezeh* in the *miluim* context therefore draws it in.

The *gemara* notes a distinction between the two derivations: the *michnasayim* fits naturally under *vezeh* because it appears in the same contextual unit — the *inyana de-bigadim* at the opening of *Tetzaveh* — and is therefore easily read as included by implication. The *asiris ha'eiphah*, by contrast, belongs to a different *parsha* entirely, and cannot be included by contextual reading alone. It requires the independent *gezeirah shavah* of *zeh zeh* to bring it in.

A further observation: toward the close of this discussion, the *gemara* cites Rav Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai — *minayin she'af mikra parsha me'akev* — from where do we know that even the formal announcement of the *parsha* is *me'akev*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: מִנַּיִין שֶׁאַף מִקְרָא פָּרָשָׁה מְעַכֵּב, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר מֹשֶׁה אֶל הָעֵדָה זֶה הַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר צִוָּה ה׳״ — אֲפִילּוּ דִּיבּוּר מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: From where** is it derived **that even** failure to **read the** Torah **portion** of the inauguration **invalidates** the inauguration? **The verse states: "And Moses said to the assembly: This is the matter [*davar*] that God has commanded** to be done" (Leviticus 8:5), teaching that **even** failure to perform the **recitation [*dibbur*]** of the Torah portion to the people **invalidates** the inauguration.

At the beginning of *Tzav*, before the *miluim* begins, Moshe assembles the entire congregation and says: *ze hadavar asher tziva Hashem* — this is the thing God has commanded. If Moshe had not made this announcement, says Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, they would not have been *yotzei*. The derivation: *afilu dibur me'akev* — even the verbal announcement is *me'akev*, since *ze hadavar asher tziva Hashem* — the very *word* (*davar*) is included in what God commanded (*asher tziva Hashem*), and therefore even that carries the force of *ikuva*.

## History to Halacha

### Contradiction Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הִלְבִּישָׁן? כֵּיצַד הִלְבִּישָׁן?! מַאי דַהֲוָה הֲוָה! אֶלָּא: כֵּיצַד מַלְבִּישָׁן לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא? לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא נָמֵי, לִכְשֶׁיָּבוֹאוּ אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו וּמֹשֶׁה עִמָּהֶם!
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the inauguration of the priests, the Gemara asks: **How,** i.e., in what order, did Moses **dress** Aaron and his sons in the priestly vestments? The Gemara wonders: **In what order did he dress them?** That is an irrelevant question, as **what was, was.** The order in which Moses dressed the priests has no practical ramifications. **Rather,** the question must be: **How** will Moses **dress** the priests **in the future,** following the resurrection of the dead, when the Temple service will be restored? The Gemara rejects this question as well: **In the future, too, when Aaron and his sons will come and Moses** will be **with them,** he will know the proper sequence, and there is no point to raising the question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: כֵּיצַד הִלְבִּישָׁן לְמִיסְבַּר קְרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the question is: **How** did Moses **dress them?** The Gemara seeks **to explain the verses** on this topic, as they appear somewhat contradictory.

With these discussions complete, the *gemara* raises a new question: *keitzad hilbishan* — how did Moshe dress Aharon and his sons? Before engaging, the *gemara* itself objects: *mai de-heva heva* — what was, was. Why do we care how Moshe dressed them? That is mere history, and the *gemara* does not traffic in idle historical speculation. So the question is reformulated: *ela keitzad malbishin le-atid lavo* — how will we dress the *kohanim* in the future, when the *beit hamikdash* is rebuilt and a new *miluim* process takes place? By reframing the question as a halachic one — what are the requirements going forward — the inquiry becomes relevant.

But even this reframing invites a question: why not simply say what was required at the time of the original *miluim* and derive the future requirement from that? The *gemara*'s deeper answer is that the question is not idle historical speculation at all, even in its original form. The real motivation is *lemisbar karai* — to resolve contradictions between *pesukim*. The *pesukim* in *Tetzaveh*, where the *miluim* is commanded, and the *pesukim* in *Tzav*, where it is enacted, appear to describe the dressing process differently. Resolving the contradiction requires understanding precisely how Moshe dressed Aharon and his sons. That is not history for its own sake; it is textual analysis driven by a genuine *kashya* in the *pesukim*.

### Future World Query

The *gemara* adds a further observation: *le'atid lavo nami lechsheyavo Aharon uvanav uMoshe imahem* — in the future world, when the resurrection occurs, we will be able to ask Moshe and Aharon directly what they did, and they will provide the historical testimony. This is not *lo bashamayim hi* — it is not turning to heaven for halachic rulings. Resurrected figures who were present and can report what actually occurred are providing factual information, not halachic *pesak*. Their testimony will then resolve the question definitively.

### Order Contradictions

The specific contradiction in the *pesukim* is this: in *Tetzaveh*, the dressing of Aharon and his sons is described in one way, and in *Tzav*, it is described differently. The question of the order — whether Aharon was fully dressed first and then his sons, or whether they were all dressed together, garment by garment — is not resolvable without carefully working through what each set of *pesukim* implies. The *gemara* now proceeds to analyze precisely this.

## Dressing Order Dispute

### Two Dressing Orders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בָּהּ בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. חַד אָמַר: אַהֲרֹן וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּנָיו, וְחַד אָמַר: אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו בְּבַת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan disagree with regard to** this matter. **One said:** Moses dressed **Aaron** first **and afterward** Moses dressed **his sons; and one said:** Moses dressed **Aaron and his sons simultaneously,** i.e., consecutively from one to the next, without interruption, to avoid changing the order prescribed in the verses.

The dispute is between the sons of Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Yochanan. One holds *Aharon ve'achar kach banav* — Aharon was fully dressed first in all his garments, and only then were his sons dressed. The other holds *Aharon u-vanav be-vat achat* — Aharon and his sons were dressed together, meaning garment by garment, each person receiving one garment before the next garment was applied to anyone.

### Cloak/Hat Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּכֻתּוֹנֶת וּמִצְנֶפֶת כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאַהֲרֹן וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּנָיו. דְּבֵין בְּצַוּוֹאָה וּבֵין בַּעֲשִׂיָּה אַהֲרֹן קָדֵים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** With regard to the **tunic and mitre everyone agrees that** Moses dressed **Aaron and afterward his sons, as both in** the portion of **the command** concerning the inauguration **and in** the portion of **the implementation,** mention of **Aaron precedes** mention of his sons.

Abaye notes that on one point there is no dispute: regarding the *kutonet* (the cloak) and the *mitznefet* (the turban/hat), everyone agrees that *Aharon kodem* — Aharon was dressed in these garments before the sons. This is because both in *Tetzaveh* (the command) and in *Tzav* (the execution), the *pesukim* begin with Aharon receiving the *kutonet* and the *mitznefet* before the sons are mentioned at all. So *bein betzava'ah bein be'asiyah*, whether in the command or in the fulfillment, Aharon receives the *kutonet* and the *mitznefet* first, and this is uncontested.

### Belt Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּאַבְנֵט. מַאן דְּאָמַר אַהֲרֹן וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּנָיו, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּחְגּוֹר אוֹתוֹ בָּאַבְנֵט״, וַהֲדַר כְּתִיב: ״וַיַּחְגּוֹר אוֹתָם אַבְנֵט״. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו בְּבַת אַחַת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְחָגַרְתָּ אוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree,** it is **with regard to the belt.** The Gemara elaborates. The **one who said:** Moses dressed **Aaron and afterward his sons** derives it from that **which is written: "And he girded him with the belt"** (Leviticus 8:7), **and then it is written: "And he girded them with belts"** (Leviticus 8:13). Moses first dressed Aaron in all of the garments, including the belt, and then Moses dressed Aaron's sons. **And** the **one who said:** Moses dressed **Aaron and his sons, simultaneously** derives it from that **which is** subsequently **written: "And gird them** with belts, Aaron and his sons" (Exodus 29:9), indicating that Moses girded them all with belts simultaneously.

The dispute centers on the *avnet* — the belt. The *avnet* was applied last, after the *kutonet* and the *mitznefet*. At this point the *pesukim* diverge. In *Tzav*, it says *vayachgor oto ba'avnet* — he belted *him* (Aharon) — and then *vayachgor otam* — he belted *them* (the sons), suggesting a sequential process: Aharon was fully completed, belt and all, before Moshe turned to dress the sons at all. In *Tetzaveh*, by contrast, it says *ve'chagarta otam avnet* — and you shall gird *them* with the belt — combining Aharon and the sons in a single act, which suggests that all were dressed together up to the belt stage and then belted as a unit.

The one who holds *Aharon ve'achar kach banav* — first Aharon, then the sons — relies on *Tzav*: *oto* and then *otam*, a sequential completion. The one who holds *Aharon u-vanav be-vat achat* — all together — relies on *Tetzaveh*: *ve'chagarta otam*, a unified act.

### Belt Material Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו בְּבַת אַחַת, הָכְתִיב: ״וַיַּחְגּוֹר אוֹתוֹ בְּאַבְנֵט״ וַהֲדַר כְּתִיב: ״וַיַּחְגּוֹר אוֹתָם אַבְנֵט״! אָמַר לָךְ: הַהוּא אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לֹא זֶה הוּא אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to the one who says:** Moses dressed **Aaron and his sons simultaneously, isn't it written: He girded him with the belt, and then it is written: He girded them with belts,** clearly indicating that he dressed Aaron and then his sons? The Gemara answers that he could have **said to you: That** verse comes to teach that **the belt of the High Priest is not the belt of the common priest.** It is explicit in the Torah that the belt of the High Priest is made of fine blue and purple linen. On the other hand, the Torah does not state the materials used in the belt of the common priest, which was in fact linen like the rest of the garments of the common priest. And still one can say that Aaron and his sons were dressed simultaneously.

Each position must then account for the other's proof-text. The one who holds *Aharon ve'achar kach banav* — first Aharon, then the sons — relies on *Tzav*: *oto* and then *otam*, a sequential completion. The one who holds *Aharon u-vanav be-vat achat* — all together — relies on *Tetzaveh*: *ve'chagarta otam*, a unified act.

The one who holds they were dressed together explains the separation in *Tzav* (*oto* and then *otam*) as conveying not a difference in process but a difference in garment: *avneto shel kohen gadol lo zehu avneto shel kohen hedyot* — the *avnet* of the *kohen gadol* is not the same as the *avnet* of the ordinary *kohen*. The *avnet* of the *kohen gadol* is composed of four strands — three wool and one linen — making it *shaatnez*. The Torah does not specify the material of the ordinary *kohen*'s *avnet*, and there is a dispute about whether it matches the *kohen gadol*'s or is a simpler linen belt. According to this view, the reason *Tzav* separates *oto* from *otam* is precisely to indicate that the two belts are different — not that the process was sequential.

### Sequential Uninterrupted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר אַהֲרֹן וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּנָיו, וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְחָגַרְתָּ אוֹתָם אַבְנֵט״! אָמַר לָךְ: הַהוּא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל זֶהוּ אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to the one who said** that Moses dressed **Aaron** first **and then his sons, is it not written: "And you will gird them with belts,"** indicating that they were girded simultaneously? The Gemara responds that he could have **said to you: That** verse **teaches us** that **the belt of the High Priest is** identical to **the belt of the common priest.** Both of them were from fine blue and purple linen. Therefore, although the Torah distinguishes between the girding of the belts, as Moses dressed Aaron before he dressed Aaron's sons, there was a common command to make both belts, indicating that they were made of the same material.

Conversely, the one who holds *Aharon ve'achar kach banav* — the sequential reading — must explain the unifying language of *Tetzaveh*'s *ve'chagarta otam*. He explains that *otam* is brought together to convey that the *avnet* of the *kohen gadol* and the *avnet* of the ordinary *kohanim* are the same.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּחְגּוֹר אוֹתוֹ אַבְנֵט״ ״וַיַּחְגּוֹר אוֹתָם״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַהֲרֹן וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּנָיו. וּבְבַת אַחַת מִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאַקְדֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If they are identical, **why do I** need both the verse: **"And he girded him with a belt,"** and the verse: **"And he girded them"?** The Gemara explains: **Learn from it** that Moses dressed **Aaron** first **and then** dressed **his sons.** The Gemara asks: **And can you find** a situation where Moses could have girded Aaron and his sons **simultaneously?** The Gemara explains: **It is necessary only** to state that Moses girded Aaron **first** and then proceeded to gird his sons in order of their significance. After girding Aaron he did not dress Aaron in any other garments before girding his sons.

The *gemara* then raises the obvious question on the *be-vat achat* position: *u-ve-vat achat mi meshakhachat lah* — how is it even physically possible to belt four people simultaneously? The answer is *lo tzrikha de-akdim* — it does not mean literally simultaneous. Rather, the belting of all of them was done as a single uninterrupted process. One after another the belts were applied, but without any interruption between them — not completing Aharon's belt and then proceeding to dress the sons in their other garments before returning to belt them. The belts were applied in immediate succession, and in that sense it was *be-vat achat* — at one time, as a unified act.
