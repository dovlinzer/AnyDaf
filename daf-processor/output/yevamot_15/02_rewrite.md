# Yevamot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beit Shammai Hold Positi…

### Opening Question

The Gemara on this amud continues to investigate a question that animated much of the preceding discussion: did *beit shammai* ultimately adopt the rulings of *beit hillel*, or did they persist in maintaining their own positions? This question matters enormously in practical terms — particularly regarding *tzarat ha-bat* and *tzarat ervah*, where the stakes involve the permissibility of *yibum* and *chalitzah*, the status of children, and the eligibility of women to marry *kohanim*. As was noted in the prior discussion, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri had suggested a safe-harbor approach of always requiring *chalitzah*, but the Gemara pointed out the considerable costs of that position: it would render women forbidden to *kohanim*, and would impose severe emotional and relational burdens on couples already bound together in marriage. Those concerns — the human cost of reflexive stringency — remain very much in the background as the Gemara now marshals a series of proofs.

### Rabbi Tarfon Case

The Gemara opens with a *tashma*: "Come and hear, for Rabbi Tarfon said, *te-avni* — how I desire that I should have a case of *tzarat ha-bat* come before me, so that I may act upon it." In context, this means that Rabbi Tarfon was eager for a scenario in which his brother died childless while having two wives, one of whom was Rabbi Tarfon's daughter — and Rabbi Tarfon wished to perform *yibum* with the co-wife, following the position of *beit shammai* that she is permitted for *yibum*. Since Rabbi Tarfon was from the school of *beit shammai*, this seems to demonstrate that *beit shammai* adherents maintained their own *psak*. The Gemara, however, pushes back: perhaps *va-asena* should be read causatively — not "I will marry her" but "I will have her marry out," meaning he wished to demonstrate his allegiance to *beit hillel*, who holds the *tzarat ha-bat* is entirely free from *chalitzah* and may simply marry without it. He would be eager to show he goes with *beit hillel*, rejecting Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's cautious middle position.

The Gemara asks: but the language *te-avni* expresses personal longing — why would he be so emotionally invested in this if the point was merely to reject Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position? The answer given is that it was precisely that — *le-apuki me-Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — he wanted to repudiate Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's suggestion that we simply always require *chalitzah* as a safeguard. He wished to demonstrate a clear ruling like *beit hillel*, without any such hedging. Of course, it remains somewhat awkward that a figure of *beit shammai*'s school would be so animated about ruling purely like *beit hillel*. The reading is possible but strained.

### Rabban Gamliel Illanit

The Gemara then brings another *tashma*: there was a story involving the daughter of Rabban Gamliel, who had been married to his brother Abba, and that brother died childless. Rabban Gamliel then performed *yibum* with the co-wife of his daughter rather than with his daughter herself. On its face, this looks like Rabban Gamliel acting according to *beit shammai* — that the *tzarat ha-bat* is permitted for *yibum*. The Gemara immediately objects: *ve-tisbarah* — does this make sense? Rabban Gamliel was a *nasi*, a descendant of Hillel himself. He would not be ruling like *beit shammai*. 

The Gemara therefore explains: *ela shani bito shel Rabban Gamliel de-illanit havai* — the reason Rabban Gamliel could do *yibum* with the co-wife was that his daughter was an *illanit*, a woman constitutionally incapable of bearing children. Because of her status as an *illanit*, the case never presented a genuine *tzarat ervah*, and therefore there was no prohibition on performing *yibum* with the co-wife. The *chiddush* of the story was not that Rabban Gamliel was ruling like *beit shammai*, but that the *illanit* status dissolved any potential problem of *tzarat ervah* in the first place.

### Illanit Status Debate

But the Gemara presses further. The *baraita* ends with the statement: *acherim omrim bito shel Rabban Gamliel illanit hayeta* — "Others say his daughter was an *illanit*." The fact that *acherim* add this detail implies that the *tanna kamma* told the story without emphasizing the *illanit* status — meaning the original *tanna* apparently did not regard the *illanit* as the operative factor. So what is the dispute between *tanna kamma* and *acherim*? The Gemara proposes that they agree the daughter was an *illanit*, but disagree about whether the husband knew this at the outset. If he did not know, the marriage was a *mekach ta'os* — a transaction entered into under a false premise — and the marriage never took effect. That would be an obvious case in which there is no *tzarat ervah*. If he did know and married her anyway, then the marriage is valid, yet arguably the *mitzvat yibum* never applied because an *illanit* cannot fulfill the purpose underlying *yibum*. The *tanna kamma* may be speaking of the case where the *illanit* status was discovered afterward — rendering the marriage void — while *acherim* are addressing the case where the *illanit* status was known from the outset and the valid marriage simply did not create a *tzarat ervah*.

### Tenai Bikiddushin Issue

The Gemara then entertains two further possibilities of what might be at stake between *tanna kamma* and *acherim*. One is whether the case involved a *t'nai bikiddushin* — a conditional stipulation in the marriage — and whether such a condition remains operative even after the couple has lived together and consummated the marriage. The *tanna kamma* might hold that the condition persists and invalidated the marriage retroactively, such that there was never a *tzarat ervah*. *Acherim* might hold that the condition was nullified by their subsequent life together, and therefore the only way to resolve the case is through the *illanit* factor itself. This question has obvious contemporary relevance: proposals to address the *agunah* crisis through conditional *kiddushin* often founder on precisely this issue — can a stipulation survive cohabitation and physical union?

### Rabbi Akiva Ma'aserot

The Gemara shifts away from *tzarat ervah* and examines whether *beit shammai* maintained their own rulings more broadly. Rav Mesharshiya raises a *tashma* from the case of Rabbi Akiva, who harvested an *esrog* on the first of *Shevat* — a date that falls between the competing positions of *beit shammai* and *beit hillel* regarding the new year for *ma'aser*. *Beit shammai* holds the new year is the first of *Shevat*; *beit hillel* holds it is the fifteenth. If the *esrog* was harvested in the second year of the *ma'aser* cycle and the new year for *ma'aser* had just turned, then whether it requires *maaser sheni* or *maaser ani* depends on which position one follows. The story relates that Rabban Gamliel — it appears the original text means Rabban Gamliel, though the attribution appears via Rabbi Akiva in the tradition — performed *shnei isurin*, two separate tithings: one according to *beit shammai* and one according to *beit hillel*. This involved a creative series of stipulations: he took *maaser sheni*, redeemed it on money, purchased food with it in Jerusalem, and then, in case the *esrog* was actually from year three, he gave the food he had redeemed to the poor — thereby satisfying *maaser ani* as well.

The Gemara adduces this as proof that *beit shammai* held their position, since the double tithing practice was evidently still a live necessity in Rabbi Akiva's time. *Shema mina asu* — you see that they maintained their position. However, the Gemara rejects this inference: *gamrei istapka lei* — Rabban Gamliel had genuine uncertainty about the tradition, not knowing which position was actually *beit hillel*'s. He was not trying to hedge between *beit shammai* and *beit hillel*; he simply did not know which date *beit hillel* had endorsed. He doubled the tithing out of uncertainty about *beit hillel*'s view, not as an endorsement of *beit shammai*'s continued authority.

### Gamliel's Uncertainty

This rebuttal — that the double *ma'aser* practice reflected Rabban Gamliel's uncertainty about *beit hillel*'s position rather than any concession to *beit shammai* — is characteristic of the Gemara's pattern throughout this discussion. Every apparent proof that *beit shammai* adhered to their own rulings is deflected by an alternative explanation. The Gemara has not yet conceded the point, though the evidence is accumulating.

### Shammai Sukkah

Mar Zutra then raises a proof from a striking story: Shammai the Elder's daughter-in-law gave birth, and Shammai himself removed part of the ceiling directly above the crib and placed *sechach* over that opening, so that the newborn infant would be dwelling, at least notionally, under a valid *sukkah*. The *mishnah* in *Sukkah* states that minors who still require their mothers — certainly newborns — are exempt from the obligation of *sukkah*. Shammai, however, was stringent even for infants. The Gemara asks: *shema mina asu* — does this not prove that *beit shammai* held their position? The Gemara deflects: *ha-ro'eh omer le-afukei avira avad* — an observer would simply assume he was ventilating the space, not making a *sukkah*. This is an acknowledged thin response; there is no violation of *agudot agudot* — the prohibition on creating factional displays — because it looks like a home improvement, not a public legal statement. But as the *mesivot* in the room observe, the rejoinder is hardly satisfying.

### Yehu's Trough Story

Mar Zutra continues with another *tashma*: the story of *shokat Yehu*, a large trough in Jerusalem that collected water and had a small hole connecting it to a valid *mikvah*. All the purifications performed in Jerusalem were done in this *shokat Yehu*, whose validity depended on that connecting hole. The relevance is that *beit shammai* requires a majority of the dividing wall to be removed for two *mikvaot* to be considered joined, while *beit hillel* requires only an opening the width and hollow of a tube inserted into a flask — approximately two finger-widths in diameter. The story relates that *beit shammai* sent word and had the hole in the *shokat Yehu* widened to satisfy their own standard. This was not merely an internal community practice but an intervention into a public *mikvah* used by all of Jerusalem. The Gemara again attempts to deflect — people who watched would assume they were simply trying to let more water in — but again this answer is weak, and the pattern is clear: *beit shammai* actively enforced their positions.

### Mikvaot Connection Law

The *mishnah* records the operative *halacha*: the connection between *mikvaot* requires an opening of *shfoferet ha-nod* — the width of the tube of a flask — measured both in terms of the tube's outer width and its hollow interior, amounting to roughly two finger-widths. This is the position of *beit hillel*, and it is the ruling that has been followed in practice. This contextualizes the significance of the *beit shammai* intervention in the *shokat Yehu* story: not only were they holding to their own position, they were acting upon it in communal space.

### Rabbi Elazar Bar Tzadok

The Gemara now brings a *tashma* that carries more weight and begins to shift the discussion toward a concession. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok testifies: "When I was learning Torah by Rabbi Yochanan Hachorni, there were times when he ate only dry bread with salt" — this during years of famine, when even that meager sustenance was all he had. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok's father sent olives for the teacher. When Rabbi Yochanan Hachorni saw the olives were wet, he declined to eat them — he was scrupulous about *chullin betuma*, avoiding food that might have been made susceptible to *tumah* through contact with liquid. Wetness from the olives' own juice would constitute *hechsher* — making the food receptive to *tumah* — if the liquid had been desired by the owner.

Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok's father then sent word explaining that the barrel had a hole in it — *chavis nekuva hayita* — but leaves and sediment had stopped up the hole. Since the owner had clearly not wanted the liquid (having put the olives in a barrel with a drainage hole), the liquid that collected was not considered *machshir* and the olives remained *tahor*. The *mishnah* confirms: if a barrel of pressed olives had a hole and it was stopped up by sediment, the olives are *tehora*. More significantly, the Gemara adds: *ve-af al pi she-talmid Shammai haya, kol ma'asav lo asa ela ke-divrei Beit Hillel* — even though Rabbi Yochanan Hachorni was a student of Shammai, all of his practices followed *beit hillel*. The fact that he was concerned about the wetness of the olives — a concern only meaningful on *beit hillel*'s view that the liquid of olives is *machshir* — demonstrates that in practice he ruled like *beit hillel*.

### Novelty Analysis

This testimony is different in character from the earlier proofs. Here, the Gemara finds the argument genuinely compelling: *i amart bishlama asu* — if in general the students of *beit shammai* maintained their own positions, then there is a genuine *chiddush* in reporting that Rabbi Yochanan Hachorni was an exception who practiced like *beit hillel*. But *i amart lo asu* — if *beit shammai* never maintained their positions and everyone followed *beit hillel* universally, then there is no novelty whatsoever in reporting that Rabbi Yochanan Hachorni did the same. The testimony would be utterly unremarkable. The Gemara does not answer this challenge, and while it does not formally say *shema mina*, it is clearly a powerful inference. The evidence that *beit shammai* held their rulings is now very strong.

## Rabbi Yehoshua Refuses

### Standard Answer

The Gemara now turns to a series of inquiries directed at Rabbi Yehoshua regarding the specific case of *tzarat ha-bat*. Someone asks: *tzarat ha-bat mahu* — what is the *halacha* about the co-wife of a daughter? Rabbi Yehoshua gives the standard, safe response: *machloket beit shammai u-veit hillel* — it is a dispute between *beit shammai* and *beit hillel*. This is, as the discussion notes with a certain wryness, almost always a correct answer for any difficult question.

### Pasken Like Whom

The questioner is not satisfied with the identification of the dispute, however, and presses: *halacha ke-divrei mi* — whose position do we rule like? This second question is the harder one, and it is to this that Rabbi Yehoshua refuses to respond directly.

### Head Between Mountains

Rabbi Yehoshua's reply is one of the most memorable in this sugya: *mipnei mah atem machnisin roshi bein shenei harim gedolim* — "Why are you forcing me to put my head between two great mountains?" He elaborates: *beit shammai* and *beit hillel* are two enormous factions — *shenei machloket gedolot* — and *misyarei ani shema yarutzu gulgelet roshi*, he fears that his head will be crushed between them. The image is vivid: if he were to rule definitively like *beit hillel*, he would be declaring retroactively that all those who followed *beit shammai* on this question committed a *issur karet* by engaging in *yibum* with a *tzarat ervah* — and that all children born of such unions are *mamzerim* or at minimum *pesulin* for *kehuna*. The consequences of a definitive ruling are devastating for real people, real families, real lives. This is why he refuses.

The phrase has resonated throughout the *teshuva* literature: *poskim* who are asked to adjudicate a matter already debated by great earlier authorities often write *mi ani le-hachnis et roshi bein harim gedolim* — who am I to thrust my head between these great mountains — though they sometimes do so anyway.

### Two Great Families

Despite refusing to rule on the underlying question, Rabbi Yehoshua does offer testimony: *ani meid lachem al shtei mishpachot gedolot she-hayu bi-Yerushalayim* — "I testify regarding two great families in Jerusalem." These were *mishpachat beit tzvu'im mi-beit Achmai* and *mishpachat beit Kufa'i mi-beit Mkoshesh*. Both families were *b'nei tzarot* — descended from women who had been in the position of *tzarat ervah* and had married out without *chalitzah*. Despite this, members of these families became *kohanim gedolim* and served at the altar. Rabbi Yehoshua presents this not as a direct *psak* but as a factual testimony: here are families, here is their provenance, here is their standing.

### Explains the Fear

The Gemara now uses this testimony to reinforce the inference that *beit shammai* had indeed been maintaining their position. *I amart bishlama asu* — if *beit shammai* held by their rulings, then Rabbi Yehoshua's fear is entirely intelligible. He would be saying that all those *beit shammai* families who did *yibum* in *tzarat ha-bat* cases were committing *issur karet* and their children might be *pesulin*. *Ella i amart lo asu* — but if *beit shammai* was already following *beit hillel*, what was there to fear? Everyone agreed. Once again the Gemara does not formally concede *shema mina*, but the inference is nearly irresistible.

### Consequence Concern

Even granting that *beit shammai* held its position, the Gemara asks: but *ani meid lachem ein mamzer ella mi-chayavei misos beit din* — Rabbi Yehoshua himself holds that a *mamzer* results only from a union that carries a penalty of death by *beit din*! Relations with a *tzarat ervah* carry *karet*, not court-administered death. So even if *beit shammai* was wrong and those unions were *issur karet*, the children would not be *mamzerim* by Rabbi Yehoshua's own ruling. Why then is he afraid to give a *psak*?

### Pagum Not Mamzer

The Gemara answers: *venei nami de-mamzer lo havi, pagum meha havi* — true, the child would not be a *mamzer*, but the child would at minimum be *pagum* — blemished and forbidden to *kehuna* — and this is established through a *kal vachomer* from the case of an *almana* and a *kohen gadol*. The marriage of a widow to the *kohen gadol* is forbidden only to a *kohen gadol*, not to all people — it is a limited prohibition — and yet the child of such a union is *pagum* and forbidden to all of *kehuna*. How much more so in the case of a *tzarat ervah*, whose prohibition is *shaveh ba-kol* — applying equally to all people — should the child be *pagum*? Even without a *mamzer* ruling, the families of *beit shammai* who performed *yibum* in *tzarat ha-bat* cases would have children who are *pesulin le-kehuna* — and those children's children, and so on through generations.

### Kal Vachomer

The *kal vachomer* is structured as follows: in the case of an *almana* and a *kohen gadol*, the prohibition does not apply universally — she may marry ordinary *kohanim* — yet the child is *pagum*. The mechanism appears tied to the specific *issurei kehuna* context: the forbidden union makes the woman a *chalala* and the child a *chalal* or *chalala*, since that category of prohibition uniquely creates the status of *chalalah*. The question then arises whether the same logic extends to *yibum* violations or to *machzir gerushato* — a man who remarries a woman he divorced after she had married someone else — unions that are *issur lav* but are not *issurei kehuna* and do not carry the mechanism of *chalalah*. Perhaps in those cases the child is not *pagum*. This is what the Gemara is exploring when it discusses the consequences for *benei tzarat* specifically.

### Evasion Strategy

The Gemara explains the structure of Rabbi Yehoshua's testimony more precisely. They asked him two questions: first, what is the *halacha* about the *tzarot* themselves — do they need *chalitzah* or may they simply marry? Second, according to *beit shammai*'s view, what would be the status of children born from those who followed *beit hillel* and married without *chalitzah*? The answer to the second question is relevant not only to *tzarat ervah* cases but also to understanding whether children of *issur lav* unions — like *machzir gerushato* — are *pagum* to *kohanim*. Rabbi Yehoshua refuses to rule on the first question — *tzarot mitzaer ani*, I will not touch the question of the co-wives themselves — but on the second he offers testimony: the *benei tzarot* from those two great families served as *kohanim gedolim*, so their status was evidently not *pagum*. This testimony is carefully calibrated: he is not ruling that the *tzarot* were permitted, but that their children were not considered disqualified, which is itself the lesser of the two explosive questions. By testifying about the children without ruling on the wives, Rabbi Yehoshua threads the needle — or, as the Gemara reads it, strategically evades the most dangerous part of the inquiry.

## Dosa & Yonatan

### Tashma Dosa Time

The Gemara brings its most conclusive piece of evidence yet: *bimei Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas, hutru tzarat ha-bat la-achim* — in the time of Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas, they had permitted the *tzarat ha-bat* to the brothers for *yibum*, following the position of *beit shammai*. This is a clear, unambiguous statement. The Gemara responds with *shema mina* — hear from this — and concedes the point: yes, *beit shammai* held their position and acted upon it. The long series of attempted refutations has finally been resolved. The question of how *beit hillel* and *beit shammai* could have intermarried despite their dispute must therefore be explained on other grounds — such as the practice of informing the other side about relevant status issues so that the absence of such notice could be relied upon as an indication that no problem existed, or other such solutions discussed earlier in the *sugya*.

### Sages' Distress

Now the Gemara turns to the story itself. *Va-haya ha-davar kasha la-chachamim* — the matter was deeply distressing to the sages, for they understood that someone had issued a *psak* permitting *tzarat ha-bat* for *yibum* under the authority of Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas, and Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas was *chacham gadol haya* — a great sage, elderly and venerable, to whom deference was owed. Compounding matters, *einav kamu mi-lavo le-veit ha-midrash* — his eyes had grown dim, he had become blind, and he no longer came to the *beit ha-midrash*. The sages could not easily engage him in the normal collegial setting. There is something pointed in this detail: this great elder had been, in a sense, cut off from the community of scholars, and now a ruling had emerged under his name that alarmed his colleagues. If he had been more present, more visited, perhaps the situation would have developed differently.

### Delegation Visits

The sages deliberated: *mi yelech ve-yodi'o* — who will go to inform him of their opposition? Rabbi Yehoshua volunteered: *ani eilech*. Then Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah joined as the second, and Rabbi Akiva as the third. The delegation was thus composed of three of the leading figures of the generation.

### Honors Distributed

They went and stood at the entrance of his home. His maidservant entered and informed him: *chachmei Yisrael ba'im etzlecha* — the sages of Israel are coming to you. He instructed her: *yikansu* — let them enter. The text then describes a curious detail: they *tafsu* — seized or pressed — Rabbi Yehoshua and seated him on a golden couch. Then Rabbi Dosa asked Rabbi Yehoshua to invite his other colleague to sit as well. This prompts the question: who is the next visitor? — and the answer reveals a remarkable generational gap in Rabbi Dosa's awareness.

### Dosa Realizes Generations

When told the second sage is Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, Rabbi Dosa responds with astonishment: *veyesh lo ben le-Azaryah chaveireinu* — does our friend Azaryah have a son? He had been so long removed from communal life that he did not even know of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's existence, let alone his stature. He recites the verse: *naar hayiti gam zakanti* — "I was young and now I am old" — and then the verse *ve-lo ra'iti tzaddik ne'ezav ve-zaro mevakesh lachem* — "I have never seen a righteous man abandoned, with his children seeking bread." The *Maharsha* notes that Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah was in fact very wealthy and from the *nasi*'s family, and this is the fulfillment of that verse: Azaryah's son is not seeking bread. There is also, the *Maharsha* implies, an ironic double meaning: *lo ra'iti* — I have not seen him, literally, both because he cannot see and because he was unaware of him. They had Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah seated on the golden couch as well.

### Indirect Approach

Rabbi Yehoshua then mentioned the third member of the party. Asked who the next visitor was, he said: *Akiva ben Yosef*. Rabbi Dosa responded with genuine warmth and wonder: *ata hu Akiva ben Yosef she-shimcha holech mi-sof ha-olam ve-ad sofo* — "You are Akiva ben Yosef, whose name goes from one end of the world to the other!" He invited him to sit: *shev beni shev, ke-motecha yirbu be-Yisrael* — sit, my son, sit; may your like multiply in Israel. The *Maharsha* notes that Rabbi Akiva was not placed on a golden couch, as he lacked the lineage or official position of the other two, yet he was warmly welcomed. The delegation then did not immediately raise the pressing question but instead began engaging Rabbi Dosa in broad halachic discussion — *hitchilu mesavvevim oto be-halachot* — surrounding him with topics of *halachot*, drawing him gently into conversation before broaching the sensitive matter.

### Main Question Asked

After a period of *halachic* discussion, they eventually arrived at the heart of the matter: *tzarat ha-bat mahu* — what is the *halacha* regarding the co-wife of a daughter?

### Dosa's Answer

Rabbi Dosa's response was unambiguous: *machloket beit shammai u-veit hillel. Halacha ke-veit hillel* — it is a dispute between *beit shammai* and *beit hillel*, and we rule like *beit hillel*. There was no hesitation, no hedging. His *psak* was clearly with *beit hillel*.

### Sages Object

The sages then pressed him: *halo mi-shimcha amru halacha ke-veit shammai* — but we heard that under your name they ruled like *beit shammai*, permitting *tzarat ha-bat* for *yibum*. The confrontation was now direct.

### Brother Yonatan Revealed

Rabbi Dosa responded with a clarifying question: in the report you heard, did it say *Dosa* permitted this, or did it say *ben Horkinas*? The sages admitted: *stam shamanu* — we heard it attributed simply to *ben Horkinas*, without the first name. Rabbi Dosa then explained: *ach katan yesh li* — I have a younger brother. This brother is *bechor satan hu* — *Rashi* explains the phrase as describing someone utterly headstrong, indifferent to others' opinions, a provocateur of the first order — and his name is *Yonatan*. And it is *Yonatan*, not *Dosa*, who issued the permissive ruling. *Ve-hu mi-talmidei Shammai* — and he is a student of Shammai, committed to the *beit shammai* position.

### Three Hundred Proofs

Rabbi Dosa issued a serious caution: *ve-hizzaharu bo she-lo yikapach etchem be-halachot* — be careful when you engage with him, because he will overwhelm you with *halachot*. *Lefi she-immo shalosh me'ot teshuvot be-tzarat ha-bat she-hi mutteret* — he has three hundred proofs that the *tzarat ha-bat* is permitted for *yibum*. Do not underestimate what you are walking into.

### Chagai's Tradition

Nevertheless, Rabbi Dosa affirmed: *aval me'id ani alai shamayim* — I testify, calling heaven and earth upon me — *she-al maducha zo yashav Chagai ha-Navi* — that on this very grinding stone, this seat, sat Chagai the prophet — *ve-eid al shelosha devarim* — and he testified to three things. The first: *tzarat ha-bat asurah* — the co-wife of a daughter is forbidden, she may not be taken in *yibum*. Whatever proofs *Yonatan* might marshal through logical argumentation, there is a *mesorah* — a received tradition — going back to *Chagai* the prophet himself that *tzarat ha-bat* is forbidden. This is the crux of the matter: the debate between tradition and logical derivation. *Yonatan* can construct three hundred arguments; the tradition that supersedes them goes back through *Chagai* to the chain of transmission that connects the prophets to Sinai. The prophets in this context are not transmitting *nevuah* — they are transmitting *mesorah*, the chain of practice and ruling that passed through them as links: *Moshe kibel Torah mi-Sinai u-Mesarah le-Yehoshua, ve-Yehoshua le-Zkenim, ve-Zkenim li-Neviim* — and it is in this capacity, as transmitters of tradition rather than receivers of prophecy, that *Chagai* testifies.

### Chagai's Three Points

The second point of *Chagai*'s testimony: *Ammoni u-Mo'avi measrin ma'aser ani bi-shevi'it* — Ammon and Moab tithe *ma'aser ani* in the *shmita* year. Ammon and Moab, situated across the Jordan, were not part of *Eretz Yisrael* proper, but had a quasi-sanctified status rabbinically. The ordinance was that during *shmita*, when agricultural production in *Eretz Yisrael* ceased and the poor had nothing to live from, Ammon and Moab — which continued to farm — would designate their second tithe not as *ma'aser sheni* (which would be taken to Jerusalem) but as *ma'aser ani*, so that the poor would have sustenance. The irony is acute: *shmita* is meant to be liberating, yet in practice it could devastate the poor; the rabbinic solution was to leverage the produce of neighboring territories.

The third point: *u-mekablim geirim min ha-Karduyim u-min ha-Tarmudim* — converts are accepted from the Karduyim and the Tarmudim. These nations were sometimes suspected of having intermixed Jewish lineage compromised by *mamzerut*, but Chagai's testimony affirmed that they are in fact simply non-Jews, and their *geirim* are accepted without concern.

### Leaving Separately

A *baraita* adds: when the three sages entered Rabbi Dosa's home, they came through a single door, but when they left, *yatzu bi-shlosha petachim* — they departed through three different doors. *Tosafot* explains this was deliberate strategy: they feared encountering *Yonatan* all together. If *Yonatan* were to defeat all three of them in *halachic* argument simultaneously, there would be enormous pressure to concede the *psak* in his direction. But if he could only engage one at a time, the other two could each say they had not been present and would not feel bound by the outcome of that particular exchange.

### Akiva Stumped

The plan did not fully succeed. *Yonatan* encountered Rabbi Akiva — *akshi lei* — and challenged him with his proofs. *Ve-ukmei* — Rabbi Akiva was, in the expression of the Gemara, left standing; he had no response. He was not persuaded, but he could not refute the arguments. This is a remarkable moment: Rabbi Akiva, who routinely emerges victorious in dialectical combat throughout the Talmud, was intellectually outmaneuvered. *Yonatan*'s three hundred proofs were not easily dismissed.

### Yonatan's Insult

*Yonatan*'s response to Rabbi Akiva's silence was withering: *ata hu Akiva she-shimcha holech mi-sof ha-olam ve-ad sofo* — you are the great Akiva whose name goes from one end of the world to the other? *Ashreikha she-zachita le-shem ve-adayin lo higata le-ro'eh* — you should be blessed that you earned such a name, yet you have not even attained the level of a simple shepherd. This is a deliberate echo — and inversion — of his brother Rabbi Dosa's greeting. Rabbi Dosa had said *ke-motecha yirbu be-Yisrael* and *shev beni shev*; *Yonatan* greeted the same man's reputation with contempt and told him he was not worthy even of a shepherd's work.

### Akiva's Response

Rabbi Akiva replied: *va-afilu ro'eh tzon* — yes, I am not even worthy of a shepherd. The *Maharsha* attempts to read this as a subtle *vort*: the *ro'eh tzon* — shepherd — alludes to Moshe Rabbeinu, who was also a shepherd, and Rabbi Akiva is saying that this *halacha* rests on a *mesorah* going back to Moshe. Whether or not that reading is in the text, it is certainly true that the Gemara's resolution of this confrontation is not through Rabbi Akiva's logical refutation of *Yonatan*'s proofs — it is through the invocation of *Chagai*'s tradition. The three hundred proofs may stand logically, but the tradition supersedes them.

## Chagai's Traditions

### Ammon Moav Ma'aser

The Gemara now turns to examine the second and third of *Chagai*'s three traditions in greater detail. Regarding *Ammoni u-Mo'avi measrin ma'aser ani bi-shevi'it*: the practice of tithing *ma'aser ani* during the *shmita* year by Ammon and Moab is explained by the principle of *harbeh krachim kibshu olei Mitzrayim ve-lo kibshu olei Bavel* — many towns that were conquered in the time of the exodus from Egypt and the original entry into the land under Yehoshua were not reconquered by the returnees from Babylon in the time of Ezra. The *kedusha* of the first conquest — the *kedusha rishona* — did not endure: *kidsha le-sha'tah ve-lo kidsha le-atid lavo*, it sanctified for its time but not for the future. The operative sanctity for *halachic* purposes follows the second entry and the second sanctification, and those towns that were not resanctified in the time of Ezra do not carry the full *kedushah* of *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Cities for Poor

Crucially, some of those towns were deliberately left unsanctified. *Ve-henichum k'dei she-yismachu aleihem aniyim bi-shevi'it* — they were intentionally left so that the poor could rely on them during the *shmita* year. In those towns, farming continued during *shmita*, and *Rashi* notes that the poor could also be hired to work those fields, providing livelihood. The same logic applied to Ammon and Moab: they had a quasi-*eretz Yisrael* status rabbinically and therefore incurred *ma'aser* obligations, but the *ma'aser* designated there in *shmita* was *ma'aser ani* rather than *ma'aser sheni*, channeling resources to the poor precisely when they were most needed.

### Accepting Converts

The third tradition of *Chagai* — that *geirim* are accepted from the *Karduyim* and the *Tarmudim* — prompts the Gemara's extended investigation into which nations are meant and what the precise concern was regarding each.

### Kartuyim vs Karduyim

A *baraita* of Rami bar Yechezkel seems to contradict the tradition: *ein mekablim geirim min ha-Karduyim* — we do not accept *geirim* from the *Karduyim*. Rav Ashi resolves this by distinguishing between *Kartuyim* and *Karduyim*: these are different nations. The prohibition applies to *Kartuyim*, not *Karduyim*. A popular folk saying apparently supported this distinction: *Kartuyim psulin* — "the *Kartuyim* are *pasul*" — and this was a recognized phrase that rhymed and circulated widely, indicating that the problematic group was specifically the *Kartuyim*.

### Tarmodim Debate

The question about the *Tarmudim* is more involved. Both *Chagai*'s tradition and certain other sources seem to accept *geirim* from *Tarmod*, while other traditions attributed to the *amora* Rabbi Yochanan reject it.

### Rekem Stains

The Gemara introduces a *mishnah* from the laws of *niddah*-related stains: *kol ha-ktamim ha-ba'im min ha-Rekem tehorim* — all stains coming from *Rekem* are *tahor*, because *Rekem* is a non-Jewish locality and stains that originate with non-Jewish women do not carry the *tumah* of *niddah* stains. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, declaring such stains *tamei* — because *Rekem*, in his view, is inhabited by converts who observe some Jewish law but err in their practice, and therefore their stains are treated as those of Jews. The general ruling of the *tanna kamma*, however, is that stains from non-Jews — explicitly *bein ovdei kochavim* — are *tahor*, with no exception carved out for *Tarmod*.

### Rabbi Yochanan

The Gemara then points out that the *mishnah* is categorical in declaring stains from non-Jews universally *tahor* — *ka pasik ve-tanei* — without distinguishing *Tarmod* from any other non-Jewish locale. This implies that even *Tarmod* is treated as straightforwardly non-Jewish, and therefore *geirim* from there should be accepted. Rabbi Yochanan himself is cited as having drawn this conclusion explicitly: *zot omeret mekablim geirim mi-Tarmod* — this *mishnah* implies that we accept *geirim* from *Tarmod*. Yet elsewhere, Rabbi Yochanan and *Savya* are both reported as saying the opposite: *ein mekablim geirim min ha-Tarmudim*.

### Resolution Amoraim

The Gemara resolves this apparent contradiction: *amorai ninhu ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yochanan* — these are *amoraim* who disagree about what Rabbi Yochanan actually held. One *amora* understood Rabbi Yochanan to be inferring from the *mishnah* that *Tarmod* is simply non-Jewish and its *geirim* are acceptable; another *amora* understood Rabbi Yochanan as holding that the *Tarmudim* are suspect and their *geirim* are not accepted. The *mishnah*'s categorical statement — treating all non-Jewish localities as *tahor* without exception — seems to favor the view that *Tarmod* is genuinely non-Jewish. The full resolution of what distinguishes the *Tarmudim* from other nations, and why the question of their lineage was debated at all, will be taken up in the continuation of the discussion.
