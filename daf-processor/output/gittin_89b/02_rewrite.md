# Gittin 89b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kol & Substantiation

### Kol Requires Beit Din

We pick up on 89b in Gittin, approximately twenty lines down from where *Tosfos* cites the statement of *Amar Rav Huna*, and about five lines above that point, at the line beginning with the word *rabbanan*. At this stage in the sugya, the Gemara has already established several principles governing the *kol* — the rumor or public report that a woman has contracted a marriage. The *mishna* held that such a rumor requires us to be concerned, preventing the woman from marrying another man without receiving a *get* from the one she was rumored to have married. The Gemara's preceding discussion explored the degree of substantiating circumstances required for a *kol* to be treated as actionable, and whether *beit din* may proactively negate — *mevatlin* — a *kol* once it is determined to have been unfounded.

Into this discussion steps Rav Ashi with a foundational principle: *kol kola d'lo it'chazeik b'vei dina lav kola havei* — any *kol* that *beit din* has not examined and established as a matter warranting concern does not attain the status of a *kol*. This is consistent with the earlier requirement for substantiating evidentiary circumstances. As Rashi explains: we are not concerned about a rumor unless the local *beit din* has investigated — in accordance with the standards discussed earlier in the Gemara — and has formally declared this particular *kol* to be one we must heed.

### Kol After Marriage

Rav Ashi adds a second ruling: *kol kol d'basar nissuin lo chashinan lah* — any *kol* that arises after a woman is already married, we do not act upon. That is, a rumor emerging after *nissuin* — full cohabitation — that would impugn her status (for example, that she had previously been married to another man, or had committed an act rendering her forbidden to a *kohen*) is simply not heeded, since acting upon it would have a direct impact on the man to whom she is currently married. The Gemara then infers the converse: *ha d'basar eirusin chashinan lah* — a *kol* that arose after *eirusin* but before *nissuin* would still be heeded, since at that point only *kiddushin* has taken place and they are not yet living together as husband and wife.

### Rav Chaviva Ruling

Rav Chaviva disagrees even with that limited scope: *afilu d'basar eirusin nami lo chashinan lah*. Even a *kol* that arose after *eirusin* is not heeded, because here too there is already a relationship being directly impacted. The *halakha* follows Rav Chaviva: once a woman is in any stage of marriage — whether *eirusin* or *nissuin* — we disregard rumors about her prior status.

## Kol & Kiddushin

### Query Setup

The Gemara now records that a query was sent from the house of Rav to Shmuel: *yelamdenu rabbeinu* — let our master teach us. The case is as follows: a *kol* had gone forth that a woman was married to Reuven — a *kol* of the type and strength that would ordinarily require concern. Then Shimon came along and married her through explicit *kiddushei Torah*, in full public view. He did not merely create another *kol*; people actually witnessed him perform *kiddushin* with her. What is the ruling?

### Shmuel's Response

Shmuel's response is threefold: *taitzei* — she must leave Shimon; *v'amidu hadavar al buryo* — investigate and establish the facts; and *ve'odiuoni* — report the findings back to me.

The Gemara then unpacks what "clarifying the matter" means in practice, proceeding in two stages. The first possibility: the investigation reveals that the first marriage — the one underlying the *kol* — was in fact invalid. For instance, perhaps there were all the social trappings of a wedding (torches, candles, public celebration) sufficient to generate a strong *kol*, but no actual valid *kiddushin* — say, because the ring was not worth a *perutah*. In that case, *mevatlinan kala* — we would negate the *kol* and permit the woman to remain married to Shimon without requiring a *get*. The investigation has shown the *kol* to be baseless.

But this first resolution is immediately rejected: *ve'ha Nahardea atra d'Shmuel hu, v'lo mevatlinan kala* — Nahardea was Shmuel's own city, and Nahardea does not negate a *kol*. This is a striking point. Even if *beit din* determines through investigation that the *kol* was factually false, the *beit din* of Nahardea will not publicly annul it, because at a certain point, once a *kol* has achieved sufficient traction, it cannot simply be overridden by a contrary ruling without appearing to sanction what the public perceives as a real marriage. As one might put it: the only difference between reality and perception is that it is often easier to change reality than perception. And so even if the facts exonerate the first marriage, the second husband will still be required to give a *get* — because the community cannot be expected to simply disregard what it believes it knows.

The second possibility: the investigation reveals not that the first marriage was void, but that it was definitively valid. That is, we move from the realm of *kol* — substantiated rumor — to actual *edut*, direct testimony that she was truly married to Reuven. In that case, *lo tzricha get mishinei* — she does not need a *get* from Shimon at all. She is simply married to Reuven; Shimon's *kiddushin* was an act of no legal consequence, and she returns to Reuven directly.

To summarize: regardless of what the investigation yields, Shimon cannot remain married to her — *taitzei* applies in all scenarios. Whether she requires a *get* from Shimon depends on the outcome: if the investigation produces only a negated *kol* (Nahardea: she still needs a *get* from Shimon), or if the investigation yields actual *edut* establishing the first marriage as real (no *get* from Shimon needed).

### Rav Huna Dispute

The Gemara notes that this ruling stands in tension with the position of Rav Huna: *eishet ish she'pashat yadah ve'kiblah kiddushin mei'acher mekudeshet, mi'din d'Rav Hamnuna* — a married woman who extends her hand and accepts *kiddushin* from another man thereby becomes betrothed to him, based on the principle of Rav Hamnuna.

Rav Hamnuna had ruled: *ha'isha she'amra l'ba'alah geirashtani ne'emenet, chazakah ein isha me'izah paneha bifnei ba'alah* — a woman who says directly to her husband's face "you have divorced me" is believed, because we invoke the principle that a woman would not be so brazen as to make such a declaration in her husband's presence unless it were true. Rav Huna derives from this that a married woman who accepts *kiddushin* from another man is in effect making precisely such a declaration — she is treating herself as divorced — and therefore she is considered betrothed to the second man as well. Were we to rule in accordance with Rav Huna's principle, it would follow that even when we know with certainty that she was married to Reuven, her acceptance of *kiddushin* from Shimon carries evidentiary weight, and she would at minimum need a *get* from Shimon. But the Gemara above concluded that if we have actual *edut* of the first marriage, she needs no *get* from Shimon. This is therefore a dispute with Rav Huna.

### Rav Hamnuna Presence

The Gemara resolves the apparent tension by clarifying the precise scope of Rav Hamnuna's teaching: *ki m'deRav Hamnuna bifanav — shelo bifanav, mizeh umizeh* — Rav Hamnuna's principle applies only when the woman accepts *kiddushin* from the second man in the presence of her husband, or declares her divorce directly to him face to face. It is specifically in that context — in the first husband's presence — that we say a woman would not be so brazen unless it were true. But in our case, the woman accepted *kiddushin* from Shimon without Reuven being present. Therefore Rav Hamnuna's principle does not apply, and the acceptance of *kiddushin* from Shimon carries no evidentiary weight regarding her status vis-à-vis Reuven.

*Tosfos* notes that this point — that the evidentiary force of accepting *kiddushin* from another man applies only when done in the first husband's presence — was never actually in dispute. The Gemara's point here, then, is narrower: even granting that argument, one might still have thought that accepting *kiddushin* from Shimon outside Reuven's presence should at least require a *get* from Shimon as a matter of caution. The answer is no: if we know as a fact that she was married to Reuven, and Shimon's *kiddushin* was performed outside Reuven's presence, that act is entirely without legal significance, she needs no *get* from Shimon, and she simply returns to being married to Reuven.

## Rumor Unresolved

### Rav Huna Option

The Gemara now turns to the scenario where the investigation yields no clear resolution. There was a strong *kol* that she was married to Reuven. Shimon then performed *kiddushin* with her. The investigation is conducted, but the facts remain indeterminate — neither the truth nor the falsity of the first marriage can be established. We are left precisely where we began.

Rav Huna rules: *megaresh rishon venosay sheni — megaresh sheni venosay rishon lo*. Contrary to what the word *taitzei* might initially suggest — that she must leave the first arrangement — Rav Huna explains that *taitzei* means she must leave Reuven's sphere. Reuven is to be the one who divorces her, and she may then, if she chooses, marry Shimon. The reverse — Shimon divorcing her so she can return to Reuven — is not permitted. Why? *Asya l'maimar machzir grushaso min ha'erusin*: people will reconstruct the narrative as follows: the *kol* was true, she was married to Reuven, Reuven then divorced her, she married Shimon, Shimon divorced her, and now Reuven is remarrying his divorcée. This appearance of *machzir grushaso* is something Rav Huna wishes to avoid, even at the cost of foreclosing an otherwise valid legal option. This is an uncomfortable concession to perception over reality — we are closing off a permissible avenue simply to avoid what the community might think.

### Ameimar Resolution

Ameimar offers a different resolution: *aynei rabanan b'kidushei v'kidushei ta'ut havei* — the Rabbis examined the *kiddushin* and determined that it was a mistake from the outset. Rather than framing the situation as a chain of marriages and divorces — which would produce the appearance of *machzir grushaso* — the Rabbis retroactively characterize Shimon's *kiddushin* as an error, a marriage that was never valid. When the woman returns to Reuven, the community's understanding will simply be that she was always married to Reuven and that the marriage to Shimon never took legal effect.

According to *Tosfos*, this is the ruling we follow. It is, as noted, still somewhat unfortunate that we must accommodate the perception of reality at all — but Ameimar's approach at least avoids artificially restricting the available options by nullifying the second marriage conceptually rather than through a second *get*.

## Competing Rumors

### Rav Papa Initial

A new scenario: the case of two competing *kolot*. On one day, a strong *kol* emerged that the woman had married Reuven; on another day, an equally strong *kol* arose that she had married Shimon. These are pure rumors — there was no witnessed act of *kiddushin* as there was in the previous case — and we assume that the investigation has not resolved either rumor's truth or falsity.

Rav Papa's initial position, following Rav Huna's logic, is that even here the same restrictions apply: *af zu megaresh rishon venosay sheni* — even in this case, she is limited to the option of Reuven divorcing her and her then marrying Shimon. She may not have Shimon divorce her and return to Reuven, for the same concern about the appearance of *machzir grushaso*.

### Ameimar Two Rumors

Ameimar disagrees: *mutteres lishneihem* — she is permitted to either of them. With two competing rumors, the concern for *machzir grushaso* does not apply. Since both marriages rest entirely on the level of *kol*, the community will not construct a definitive narrative of married-divorced-married-divorced. One of the rumors must by necessity be false for any coherent story to emerge, and therefore no perception of *machzir grushaso* can credibly take hold.

### Two Rumors Ruling

The *halakha* follows Ameimar: *mutteres lishneihem* — when there are two competing rumors that remain unresolved, she is permitted to either man. We do not give both rumors such absolute weight that we impose the limitation of *machzir grushaso* on her options. She may receive a *get* from Shimon and remain with Reuven, or vice versa, as circumstances warrant. All of this, it bears noting, remains unfortunate to whatever degree — particularly according to the position that does not negate a *kol* even when the facts are established — that we must at all accommodate the perception of reality alongside the reality itself.

## Grounds for Divorce

### Beit Shammai

We now turn to the last *daf* of *Gittin*, 90a, and arrive at a *mishna* that forms a fitting capstone to the entire *maseches*: after an entire tractate devoted to the technicalities and legal mechanics of the *get* — its writing, delivery, agency, and conditions — we finally reach the question of which circumstances actually warrant a divorce in the first place.

The *mishna* records the famous three-way dispute. Beit Shammai states: *lo yegaresh adam et ishto ela im ken matza bah devar ervah* — a man should not divorce his wife unless he has found in her a matter of *ervah*, sexual sin. The scriptural basis is the verse in Deuteronomy that serves as the Torah's primary reference to divorce: *ki matza bah ervat davar*. The entire Torah's treatment of *get* is embedded within the laws of *machzir grushaso* — the prohibition on a man remarrying his divorced wife after she has wed another. In framing the initial divorce, the Torah describes two conditions: *ve-haya im lo timtza chen be-einav* — if she no longer finds favor in his eyes — and *ki matza bah ervat davar* — because he has found in her a matter of sexual impropriety. Beit Shammai focuses exclusively on the latter: actual adultery, or the equivalent thereof. Only when the woman has committed an act that renders it halachically forbidden for them to remain together — when there is real evidentiary basis for sexual transgression — is divorce truly warranted.

It is important to observe that Beit Shammai is not saying that a *get* given for lesser reasons is legally void. The entire framing of the *mishna* is one of guidelines — of what is appropriate — not of legal validity. A *get* does not require court approval; it is in essence an act between the two parties. It does not need to be judicially warranted for it to take effect. What Beit Shammai is articulating is a moral and normative standard: *lo yegaresh*, a man ought not divorce his wife except under these circumstances.

### Beit Hillel Leniency

Beit Hillel permits divorce for any matter that displeases the husband — *afilu hikdicha tavshilo* — even if she merely burned his soup or over-spiced his stew. Rashi notes that this can mean either that the food was actually scorched or simply that it was seasoned in a way he did not like. Either way, the most trivial domestic misstep suffices. The scriptural hook, as we will see in the Gemara's analysis, is the word *davar* in *ki matza bah ervat davar*: *matza bah davar* — he found something, anything, that he did not like.

One might note that the characterization of Beit Hillel as *le-kula* here requires some nuance. If the dynamic is one in which the wife wishes to remain married and the husband acts capriciously, Beit Hillel's ruling is hardly kind to the wife. At the same time, it is *le-kula* in the formal sense of operating with fewer restrictions — and of course the dynamic is not always as described, for it may be the wife who wishes the marriage to end.

### Rabbi Akiva Permissive

Rabbi Akiva extends the grounds for divorce even further: *afilu matza acheret na'ah heimena* — even if he has simply found another woman more beautiful, or more pleasing to him. His scriptural basis is the opening clause of the verse — *ve-haya im lo timtza chen be-einav*, if she no longer finds favor in his eyes — without requiring that she has done anything wrong at all. The wife may be entirely blameless and a model spouse; if her husband has become more attracted to another woman, that is sufficient grounds.

This is a genuinely difficult position to read with equanimity. Some have sought to connect Rabbi Akiva's view with what we know of his biographical relationship with his own wife, his famous deep and passionate love — reading him perhaps as a romantic who believes one must always follow one's heart and remain passionately devoted to one's spouse, so that a loss of that passion is itself grounds for dissolution. But that is a generous reading, and it is worth acknowledging the difficulty. Rabbi Akiva's position here also connects to his rulings in other areas — for instance, regarding a woman's appearance during periods when physical intimacy is restricted — which reflect an orientation that the wife bears a significant responsibility to ensure she remains attractive and pleasing to her husband. A position that allows a man to remarry anyone who pleases him more places a correspondingly heavy burden on the wife to remain perpetually desirable. These are matters that invite serious reflection in light of contemporary sensibilities.

## Textual Analysis

### Beit Hillel Exegesis

The Gemara now records a *baraita* that traces the hermeneutical exchange between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Beit Hillel opens: *v'lo kvar neemar davar?* — does the verse not already say *davar*, which denotes any matter? Beit Shammai responds: *v'lo kvar neemar ervat?* — but does it not also say *ervat*, which denotes sexual sin?

Beit Hillel then offers the fuller argument. If the Torah had written only *ervat* and not *davar*, one might have thought: a man may divorce his wife on account of sexual sin, but not for any ordinary matter. And if the Torah had written only *davar* and not *ervat*, one might have thought: if he divorces her for some ordinary matter, she may go and remarry another man — but if he divorces her because she has committed adultery, perhaps she is not entitled to remarry at all. For the continuation of the verse reads *ve-halchah ve-haytah le-ish acher* — and she went and became the wife of another man. One might have supposed that this permission to remarry applies only to a woman divorced for trivial reasons, not for adultery. Therefore, says Beit Hillel, both terms are necessary: *ervat* teaches that even a woman divorced for adultery may remarry (any man other than the adulterer), and *davar* teaches that even the slightest matter suffices as a ground for divorce.

### Beit Shammai Witnesses

For Beit Shammai, the challenge is to account for the word *davar* without conceding Beit Hillel's reading. The Gemara asks: *Beit Shammai, hai davar mai avid lei?* — what do Beit Shammai do with the word *davar*? Their answer is a *gezerah shavah*: the word *davar* appears here, and the same word appears elsewhere — *al pi shnayim edim o al pi shlosha edim yakum davar* — a matter is established by the testimony of two or three witnesses. Just as in that context *davar* requires two witnesses, so too here: the *davar* of *ervat davar* teaches that adultery must be established through the testimony of two witnesses. The word *davar* is not a second independent ground for divorce; it is a procedural requirement attached to the sole ground, *ervah*.

### One vs. Two Grounds

The debate then sharpens into a linguistic question about the phrase *ervat davar* itself. Beit Hillel presses: *mi ktiv ervah b'davar?* — the text does not say *ervah* was found "through" *davar*, through testimony. It says *ervat davar* as a construct phrase. Therefore *davar* cannot be merely the mechanism for establishing *ervah*; it must be a separate item. Beit Shammai replies: *mi ktiv o ervah o davar?* — but the text does not say "either *ervah* or *davar*." The phrase is *ervat davar*, a single unified construct, not two alternatives.

Beit Hillel's final response: *hachi ktiv ervat davar, d'mashma hachi u'mashma hachi* — yes, it is written as a single phrase, but it is precisely this dual resonance that is the point. The phrase simultaneously conveys: *ervah* alone (adultery as grounds) and *davar* alone (any matter as grounds), with the construct form serving to indicate that even in the case of *ervah*, the subsequent permission to remarry applies. The phrase thus carries both meanings at once.

For Beit Shammai, this linguistic analysis reinforces that *davar* is not an independent ground at all: the requirement for *edut* — two witnesses — to establish adultery is the very content the word *davar* imports. Their reading is that *ervat davar* is a single concept: an actual, evidenced act of adultery. Only under those circumstances does the Torah envision divorce as truly warranted.

## Get Validity Issues

### Unjustified Divorce

Having established that the grounds for divorce are debated — *ervah* alone (Beit Shammai), any *davar* (Beit Hillel), or even mere loss of attraction (Rabbi Akiva) — the Gemara now addresses the threshold question underlying the entire *mishna*: what if a man divorces his wife with none of these justifications? There was no *ervah*, no *davar*, nothing at all by even Beit Hillel's standards. Does the *get* take effect, or is the man required to remarry her? Rav Papa poses this question to Rava.

### Get Effective

Rava responds by invoking the law of the rapist: the Torah states *lo yuchal leshalcha kol yamav* — the man who raped a woman and was compelled to marry her may never divorce her. If he does give a *get*, he is required to remarry her — *kol yamav b'omed ve-hachzir*. Rava's reasoning: the Torah had to state explicitly that the rapist cannot divorce his wife. This implies that in any other case, even if the divorce transgresses the guidelines articulated in the *mishna*, *mai de'avad avad* — what he has done is done. The *get* is valid. The *mishna* deals entirely with normative guidelines; it does not define the conditions under which a *get* legally functions.

### Secret Divorce Plan

Rav Mesharshiya then raises a related concern — not a legal one, but an ethical one. He poses the following to Rava: if a man has decided in his heart that he intends to divorce his wife, but conceals this from her — while she continues to live in his home, serve him, prepare his meals, share his bed — what is the moral status of that conduct?

Rava's response is a scriptural citation: *al tacharosh al re'acha ra ve-hu yoshev lavetach itach* — do not plot evil against your friend when he dwells with you in trust. The word *re'im* — friends — evokes *re'im ha-ahuvim*, the beloved companions of the marriage bond. The betrayal of plotting divorce while one's wife tends to the home in full trust constitutes a profound breach of the foundational human relationship. It is not a *halakhic* violation — the *get* will still work — but the moral condemnation is severe.

## Food & Wife Analogy

### Analogy Framework

A *baraita* is then cited: *haya Rabbi Meir omer, kashem she'hade'ot b'ma'achal kach de'ot banashim* — Rabbi Meir would say: just as people differ in their attitudes toward food, so too they differ in their attitudes toward wives. The comparison of food and marital intimacy as analogous areas of human desire is not uncommon in rabbinic literature, though here the analogy focuses less on desire per se and more on fastidiousness and standards of tolerance. Three types of men are described.

### Fastidious Type

The first is the extremely fastidious man. A fly falls into his cup and he throws out the entire cup — a minor contamination renders the whole thing unacceptable. The *baraita* identifies this with the behavior of Pappos ben Yehuda: when he left his house, he would lock the door and keep his wife confined inside. The analogy to extreme fastidiousness is clear — he could not bear the thought of his wife having any interaction with the outside world, anyone who might constitute, in his mind, any form of contamination. As Rashi notes at the end of his commentary on this passage: *umida she'eina hogenet hee zo shemitoch kach eiva nichneset beineihem umezana tachtav* — this is an unseemly trait, for from such conduct hatred and enmity enter between them, and it actually leads to the very outcome he feared most. By imprisoning his wife in jealousy and suspicion, he generates the conditions for what he dreads. And beyond that consequence, the controlling and abusive nature of such a relationship requires no commentary.

### Normal & Permissive

The second type is the ordinary person — *midat kol adam*. The fly falls into his cup; he removes the fly and drinks the cup. In the context of marriage: his wife speaks with acquaintances, neighbors, and relatives, and he is not troubled by it. He does not allow groundless jealousy to poison the relationship. This is presented as the normative standard.

The third type is the morally deficient man. The fly falls into the stew — the *tamchuy* — and he wrings the fly dry to extract all the juices absorbed from the stew, and then eats the stew. He is wholly indifferent to contamination, even when the contamination is substantial. In the marital context: his wife goes out with her head uncovered, spins in the marketplace in a way that draws attention and exposes her arms, wears clothing slit up the sides, and bathes in places shared with men — and he is unbothered. The Gemara clarifies: bathing literally together with men? Rather, she bathes in the same river or area where men bathe, overlapping in proximity in a way that is far too intimate. The *baraita* concludes: *zu mitzvah min hatorah le-garesha* — in such a case it is a *mitzvah* ordained by the Torah to divorce her.

## Immodest Conduct

### Improper Behaviors

The behaviors enumerated in the preceding *baraita* — going out with her head uncovered, spinning in the marketplace, wearing garments slit up the sides, bathing in shared spaces — constitute the category of *ervat davar* in a broad sense. This is the same rubric discussed in *Ketubot* under the categories of *dat Moshe* and *dat Yehudit*: behaviors that violate either Mosaic law or the standards of Jewish communal norms as they govern the conduct expected within marriage. It should be noted that while the *mishna* and this *baraita* address women's behavior exclusively, the same logic applies to conduct by husbands that draws inappropriate attention or constitutes disrespect toward the marital bond.

### Mitzva to Divorce

When a woman engages in the behaviors enumerated above, the *baraita* states that it is a *mitzvah min hatorah* to divorce her — *she-garsha, she-ne'emar ki matza bah ervat davar*. The verse *ki matza bah ervat davar* here is read not necessarily as evidence of actual adultery, but as encompassing the broader category of sexually immodest or inappropriate conduct that undermines the integrity of the marriage.

### Second Husband Fate

The *baraita* continues with a *midrashic* reading of the continuation of the verse: *ve-halchah ve-haytah le-ish acher* — she goes and becomes the wife of another man. *Hakatuv kera'o acher* — the Torah calls him *acher*, different or "other," to indicate that he is not of the same quality as the first husband. The first husband did the right thing — *hotzi rishah mibeito*, he removed this woman whose conduct was shameful from his house. The second man — the *acher* — *hichnis rishah le-toch beito*, brings her into his house. If the second man merits it, he will eventually also send her away — *she-ne'emar ve-saneiah ha-ish ha-acharon*. And if he does not merit it: *k'vartahu* — she will bury him, as the verse says *o ki yamut ha-ish ha-acharon*, or the last husband dies. *Kedai hu be-mitah*, he is deserving of death, for the first man expelled this wicked woman from his home, and this man brought her back in.

## Malachi Dispute

### Rabbi Yehuda

The Gemara now turns to a *pasuk* in *Malachi* — *ki sanei shalach* — which is read as bearing on the question of when divorce is appropriate. Rabbi Yehuda reads the verse permissively: *im saneisah shalach* — if you hate her, send her away. Rashi understands this as aligned with Rabbi Akiva — or perhaps Beit Hillel — that subjective aversion, even without any wrongdoing on the wife's part, suffices as grounds.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan reads the same verse in the opposite direction: *sanui ha-meshaleiach* — the one who sends her away is himself to be despised. On this reading, the act of divorce is condemned; a man who divorces his wife is hateful. This aligns with a Beit Shammai ethos: divorce without genuine justification is not merely inadvisable but morally contemptible.

### God's Hate Standard

An alternative textual reading (*girsa*) of the verse is: *snuah hi lifnei ha-makom shalach* — only if she is hated before God — i.e., if she has committed adultery, a sin that makes her hateful in God's eyes — should you send her away. This is the most restrictive reading, corresponding most closely to Beit Shammai's position.

### Marriage Distinction

The Gemara then offers a resolution: *v'lo pligi* — the *tannaim* do not ultimately disagree. Rather, their statements apply to different situations. *Ha b'zug rishon, ha b'zug sheini* — one refers to the first marriage, the other to the second. The permissive reading (Rabbi Yehuda) may be applied in the case of a second marriage; the restrictive reading (Rabbi Yochanan), which treats the one who divorces as despicable, is specifically addressed to the first marriage. This resolution flows naturally into the teaching of Rabbi Elazar that follows.

## First Marriage Sanctity

### Altar's Tears

Rabbi Elazar teaches: *kol ha-megaresh ishto rishonah afilu mizbe'ach morid alav d'maos* — whoever divorces his first wife, even the altar itself sheds tears over him. The image is striking: the physical altar, the site of sacrifice and divine service, mourns the dissolution of a first marriage.

### Malachi Midrash

The scriptural basis is the verse in *Malachi* (2): *ve-zos sheinis ta'asu, ksos d'mah et mizbe'ach Hashem b'chi va-anakah, mei-ein od penos el ha-minchah v'lakchas ratzon mi-yedchem* — this second thing you do, covering the altar of God with weeping and wailing, so that He no longer turns to the offering or accepts it with favor from your hands. And when the people ask *al mah*, on what account, the verse answers: *al ki Hashem he'id beinecha u-vein eishet neurecha asher atah bagadtah bah* — because God has testified between you and the wife of your youth, against whom you have betrayed.

### Covenant Rupture

The theological weight of this verse is profound. The rupture of the marital bond — specifically the betrayal of the wife of one's youth — disrupts one's relationship with God. The altar weeps; God turns away from the offerings. The verse then identifies the wife with two terms: *v'hi chavertecha v'eishet britecha* — she is your friend, your companion, and the woman of your covenant. The marriage to the first wife is cast as a covenant — *brit* — a foundational relationship of partnership and commitment. Betraying that covenant sends ripples outward, all the way to the altar of God.

The resolution the Gemara offers — that Rabbi Yehuda speaks of a second marriage and Rabbi Yochanan of a first — is precisely calibrated to this teaching. When we speak of a second marriage, there is greater latitude; the standards of Beit Hillel or even Rabbi Akiva might apply. But when it is the first marriage — the *zug rishon*, the foundational relationship, the *eishet brit* — the bar is incomparably higher, and to dissolve it for trivial reasons is to be considered despicable, *sanui ha-meshaleiach*.

### Modern Balance

This is a beautiful and powerful teaching, and it is worth sitting with its imagery. At the same time, the principle must be handled with care in its practical application. The concern that divorce not be undertaken trivially must not become a cudgel used against people who have arrived at that painful decision through a long and responsible process. There is a real phenomenon — reflected in personal testimonies and public discourse, including in the Orthodox world — of people who have carefully deliberated and sought guidance over extended periods, who finally appear before a *beit din*, and are made to feel contemptible for being there at all. Sometimes divorce is the right and even necessary decision. The tradition's emphasis on the gravity of first-marriage dissolution must be balanced against sensitivity to the human beings who stand before the *dayan*, and against the reality that prolonging an irreparably broken marriage serves no one.

### Partnership Ideal

It is fitting to close on the phrase with which *Malachi* describes the wife: *chavertecha v'eishet britecha* — your friend and the wife of your covenant. Despite everything in this final chapter — the unilateral power of the husband to initiate divorce, the analogies to food and property, the male gaze that pervades so much of this discussion — this phrase articulates an ideal of marriage as equal partnership, as friendship, as covenant. Remarkably, *ketubot* discovered in the Cairo *Genizah* employ this very language to describe the marital bond: *ishti ve'eishet briti, chaverti ve'eishet briti* — my wife and the wife of my covenant, my companion and the wife of my covenant. Even in an era when many of the legal structures of marriage were heavily asymmetric, the covenantal ideal of *chevratcha v'eishet britecha* persisted as the animating vision of what marriage is meant to be. It is a fitting note on which to conclude *maseches Gittin*.
