# Rosh Hashanah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ninth Takkanah Debate

### Ninth Takkanah Intro

The daf opens at the bottom of 31b, continuing the discussion of the nine *takkanot* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai. Eight have already been enumerated: six in the current *perek*, one in the previous *perek*, and one concerning a *ger* who converts nowadays and should not set aside money for a *korban* — all of them, in various ways, reflecting the transition away from the *Mikdash*. The identity of the ninth *takkanah* is the subject of a dispute between Rav Pappa and Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak.

### Rav Pappa: Kerem Reva'i

Rav Pappa holds that the ninth *takkanah* concerns *kerem reva'i* — the produce of a vineyard in its fourth year. As taught in the *Mishnah*, *kerem reva'i haya oleh l'Yerushalayim mahalach yom l'chol tzad*: fourth-year fruit was to be brought to Jerusalem and consumed there. Although one could technically redeem the fruit on money even just outside Jerusalem's limits, the Rabbis instituted that within a day's travel of Jerusalem, one must bring the fruit itself, so as to adorn the marketplaces of Jerusalem with produce. The cities marking this boundary were: *Eilat* in the north, *Akravat* in the south, Lud in the west, and the Jordan in the east. Ulla — and some say, Rabba bar bar Ulla, citing Rabbi Yochanan — explains the rationale: *k'dei l'ateir shukei Yerushalayim b'peirot*, so that the markets of Jerusalem would be adorned with fruit.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Concerns

A *braita* illustrates the practical impact of this *takkanah*. Rabbi Eliezer had *kerem reva'i* to the east of Lud, near the village of Kfar Tavi — that is, in the direction of Jerusalem, within the prescribed boundary. Not wishing to bring the fruit himself, he was on the verge of declaring it *hefker* for the poor, hoping they would take it. His students stopped him: *kvar nimnu chaveirecha alav v'hitiruhu* — your colleagues already voted and permitted it, meaning they annulled the original *takkanah*. The reference to "colleagues" (*chaveirim*) is, according to Rav Pappa, a reference to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai himself, who — post-*Churban* — recognized that the purpose of the original enactment no longer applied, Jerusalem being destroyed and under Roman rule, and accordingly annulled it. This fits squarely within the recognized genre of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's *takkanot*: an acceptance of post-*Churban* reality and an adjustment of the law accordingly.

### Rav Nachman: Red String

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak disagrees. He identifies the ninth *takkanah* with the *lashon shel zehorit* — the red string used in the Yom Kippur *avodah* in connection with the *seir l'azazel*. This is, notably, not a post-*Churban* enactment at all, but rather a *takkanah* rooted in the period of the *Beit HaMikdash* and its *korbanot*.

### Red String Stages

A *braita* describes the three stages of this practice. Originally, the red string was tied at the entrance of the *Ulam*, the hallway to the *Heichal*, on the outside. If it turned white after the *korban* was brought, the people would rejoice; if it did not turn white and remained red, they would be anguished, sensing that their sins had not been atoned. This public visibility of the result created distress, so the Rabbis enacted that the string be tied instead on the inside of the doorway — but people would still peer through and look. Finally, the authorities enacted that half the string be tied to the rock at the site where the goat was thrown over and the other half between the horns of the goat itself, entirely removing it from public view. According to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, it was this final stage — concealing the string from public sight — that was the *takkanah* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai.

### Chronological Proof

The *gemara* proceeds to examine why each sage rejects the other's position. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains why he does not follow Rav Pappa: if the one who annulled the *kerem reva'i takkanah* were Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, the students would not have said to Rabbi Eliezer "your *chaveirim* annulled it," since Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was not Rabbi Eliezer's colleague but his *rebbe* — a generation above him. The *gemara* responds on behalf of Rav Pappa: since it was the students speaking to Rabbi Eliezer, it would have been disrespectful for them to say "your *rebbe* annulled it," as if invoking a higher authority to override him. As a matter of *derech eretz* they said "colleagues" when they meant the *rebbe*. That is a reasonable resolution.

The more formidable challenge, however, runs in the opposite direction — against Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak. The *gemara* asks: in the days of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, was the red string actually turning white? A *braita* teaches that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai lived 120 years — forty years devoted to business, forty to learning, and forty to teaching. Any act of communal leadership, then, belongs to his final forty years. But a second *braita* teaches that *arba'im shanah kodem shenechrav habayit* — for the forty years preceding the *Churban*, the red string did not turn white; it remained red. And we know from the *Mishnah* that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai made his *takkanot mishcharav habayit* — after the *Churban*. Working backwards: if the *Churban* is year zero, then Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was teaching for the forty years prior, going back no more than thirty-nine years before the *Churban*. But the string had already ceased to turn white forty years before the *Churban*. The entire *takkanah* of the red string — which presupposes that the string *sometimes* turned white and *sometimes* did not — must therefore have been instituted before Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's tenure of leadership. His active role post-dates the period in which the *takkanah* could have been made.

### Resolution: Young Yochan…

The *gemara* offers a resolution: during those forty years that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was a *talmid yoshev lifnei rabo* — a student sitting before his *rebbe* — he was not merely learning independently. He was present in the context of active *halachic* life and occasionally offered a suggestion: *amar milta*, he said something. *V'istaber tameih* — they found it reasonable, *v'karu rabo mishmeih* — and his *rebbe* attributed the ruling to him by name. The analogy is almost reversed from academic convention: rather than the *rebbe* taking credit for the student's insight, the *rebbe* attributed it to the student. So the *takkanah* could technically be ascribed to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai even from his years as a student.

That said, this resolution is clearly a stretch. The *takkanah* of the red string fits none of the recognizable characteristics of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's enactments: it is not post-*Churban*, it is not a response to the transition away from the *Mikdash*, and it requires a convoluted chronological reconstruction. The primary case for the ninth *takkanah* remains, with greater naturalness, Rav Pappa's position of *kerem reva'i*.

One participant noted the striking correspondence between the 120-year life of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai and that of Moshe Rabbeinu — similarly divided into thirds, with the final forty years constituting the period of public leadership. The parallel may be deliberately drawn by *Chazal* to suggest that just as Moshe led the Jewish people through the transformative rupture of leaving *Mitzrayim*, so too Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai guided Israel through the no less radical rupture of the *Churban* — a transition from one mode of national and religious existence to another.

## Malchuyos Structure

### Overview of Debate

We turn now to the top of 32a and the *Mishnah* that introduces the liturgical structure of *musaf* on Rosh Hashanah — specifically the placement of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* within the *Shemoneh Esrei*. The *Ba'al HaMaor* holds that *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* are recited in every *tefillah* of *Shemoneh Esrei* on Rosh Hashanah, but the standard ruling, and the assumption of the *gemara*, is that we are speaking exclusively about *musaf*.

The normal structure of *musaf* consists of seven *brachot*: *Avos*, *Gevuros*, *Kedushat Hashem* (culminating in *Ha-El HaKadosh* or *HaMelech HaKadosh*), *Kedushat HaYom* (ending with *Mekadesh HaShabbat v'Yisrael v'HaZmanim*), *Avodah* (*Retzei*), *Modim*, and *Birkat Kohanim* (*Sim Shalom*). To incorporate the three additional *brachot* of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* while maintaining a total of nine rather than ten, two elements must be combined. The dispute is over which ones to combine and where.

### Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri

The *Mishnah* presents the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri: *omer Avos u'Gevuros u'Kedushat Hashem v'kolel malchuyos imahem v'eino sokeia* — one recites *Avos*, *Gevuros*, and *Kedushat Hashem*, incorporating *malchuyos* together with them, without sounding the *shofar* at that point. Then, in the center of the *Shemoneh Esrei*, one says *Kedushat HaYom* together with *zichronos* and sounds the *shofar*; then *shofros* with the *shofar*; and finally *Avodah*, *Modim*, and *Birkat Kohanim*. According to this position, *malchuyos* is placed early — subsumed within *Kedushat Hashem* — and the *tekiyot* are sounded only in connection with *zichronos* and *shofros*, both of which fall in the center of the prayer.

### Rabbi Akiva's Objection

Rabbi Akiva objects: *im eino tokeia l'malchuyos lama hu mazkir?* If one is not going to sound the *shofar* with *malchuyos*, why mention *malchuyos* at all? This reflects a clear understanding of the intimate relationship between *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* on the one hand, and the *tekiyot* on the other. One can understand this relationship in either direction: either the *tekiyot* are the vehicle through which the prayers ascend — as the post-*tekiyah* formula *tikabel b'rachamim u'v'ratzon seder shofroteinu* suggests — or conversely, the verbal themes give meaning and content to the *tekiyot*, characterizing what the blasts are about. In either case, Rabbi Akiva insists, the *tekiyot* and the three liturgical themes are inextricably bound together, and to detach the *shofar* from *malchuyos* is to undermine the coherence of the entire structure.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva's own position, then, is: *omer Avos u'Gevuros u'Kedushat Hashem v'kolel malchuyos im Kedushat HaYom v'tokea* — one includes *malchuyos* together with *Kedushat HaYom* and sounds the *shofar* at that point. Then *zichronos* with the *shofar*, then *shofros* with the *shofar*, and then the final three *brachot*. All three themes — *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* — come in the center of the *Shemoneh Esrei*, each accompanied by *tekiyot*, and *malchuyos* is merged with *Kedushat HaYom* rather than with *Kedushat Hashem*.

This is, of course, the position we follow. Its traces are visible throughout the *musaf* liturgy. The *chatimah* of *Kedushat HaYom* in *musaf* reads *Baruch Atah Hashem mekadesh Yisrael v'Yom HaZikaron*, but with the addition of *Melech al kol ha'aretz* — a four-word *malchuyos* phrase that becomes the *chatimah* of *malchuyos* merged with *Kedushat HaYom*. Significantly, this combined *chatimah* is preserved in all the *Shemoneh Esrei* prayers of Rosh Hashanah, not only *musaf*, even in those *tefillos* where the full *piyutim* of *malchuyos* are not recited.

### Gemara's Response

Yet the *gemara* does not simply affirm Rabbi Akiva's stated logic. The *gemara* deflects his rhetorical challenge — *lama hu mazkir?* — by responding that *malchuyos* is said because *the Torah requires it*: *Rachmana amar*. The obligation to recite *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* exists independently of any connection to the *tekiyot*; they are a required liturgical element in their own right. The *gemara* thus strips Rabbi Akiva's argument of its full force. Rather than reading him as making a fundamental claim about the inseparability of these themes from the *shofar*, the *gemara* reduces his point to something more modest: if you are not sounding the *shofar* with *malchuyos*, at least say nine *pesukim* rather than ten — *shinui*, do something to mark the difference. The argument becomes considerably weaker when interpreted this way. The *gemara* clearly understands that *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* can in principle function as an independent liturgical form, decoupled from the *tekiyot*.

### Conceptual Analysis

This debate points toward a deeper question about the meaning of Rosh Hashanah itself. The position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, in pushing *malchuyos* to an earlier, less central position and withholding the *tekiyah* from it, implicitly decentralizes *malchuyos* as a theme and places *zichronos* and *shofros* at the center. This may reflect a conception of Rosh Hashanah primarily as a *yom hadin* — a day of judgment. *Zichronos* (God's remembrance of His creatures) and *shofros* (the *shofar* as a cry before the King) are the operative themes; *malchuyos* provides necessary background context — God as King explains why there is judgment — but it is not the focus.

Rabbi Akiva's position, by contrast, places all three themes in the center with equal weight, each receiving its own *tekiyah*. This allows *malchuyos* to function as a genuinely central theme, not merely a backdrop. One can read Rosh Hashanah as a *yom shel malchus* — a day of proclaiming God's kingship over creation — with *zichronos* and *shofros* as expressions of that sovereignty rather than as the primary substance of the day.

Interestingly, our received liturgy reflects traces of both positions. The *ub'chen* paragraphs — *ub'chen ten pachdecha*, *ub'chen ten kavod*, *ub'chen tzadikim*, culminating in *v'timloch Atah Hashem levadecha* — which appear after *Gevuros* and before *Kedushat Hashem*, suggest a *malchuyos*-themed liturgy associated with *Kedushat Hashem* that may preserve a strand of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's approach. The *chatimah* of *HaMelech HaKadosh* serves as a *malchuyos* conclusion to this section. And the *chatimah* of *Melech al kol ha'aretz* is preserved in all the *tefillos* of Rosh Hashanah even outside of *musaf*, as a remnant of the *malchuyos* theme integrated into *Kedushat HaYom*. In practice, as often observed in *halachic* liturgical disputes, both positions leave their mark on the final *nusach*.

## Biblical Sources

### Rabbi Eliezer Sources

The *gemara* now asks: from where do we derive that one recites *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* at all? A *braita* presents the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who derives all three from a single verse in Leviticus 23: *shabbaton zichron teruah mikra kodesh*. He reads *shabbaton* as a reference to *Kedushat HaYom* — the sanctity of the day as expressed in the liturgy. *Zichron* yields *zichronos*. *Teruah* yields *shofros*. And the concluding phrase *mikra kodesh* is understood as an instruction to sanctify the day through the prohibition of *melacha*.

The notable absence from Rabbi Eliezer's derivation is *malchuyos*. This is consistent with Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's framework — where *malchuyos* occupies an earlier, less central position — rather than Rabbi Akiva's. The verse as Rabbi Eliezer reads it maps onto precisely the liturgical order Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri prescribes: *Kedushat HaYom*, *zichronos*, *shofros*, with *malchuyos* elsewhere.

### Rabbi Akiva Reading

Rabbi Akiva offers a minor textual objection to Rabbi Eliezer's reading: why did he place the *mikra kodesh* prohibition of *melacha* at the end, when the word *shabbaton* — which he uses for *Kedushat HaYom* — appears first in the verse? One should begin by noting that *shabbaton* refers to the prohibition of *melacha*, and then proceed to the liturgical themes of *zichron* and *teruah*. Rabbi Akiva therefore reads the verse as: *shabbaton* — sanctify the day by refraining from *melacha*; *zichron* — *zichronos*; *teruah* — *shofros*; *mikra kodesh* — *Kedushat HaYom*. The substantive outcome is largely the same, with *zichronos* and *shofros* still derived from *zichron teruah*, but Rabbi Akiva places *Kedushat HaYom* at the end — a curious inversion, given that in his own liturgical arrangement, *Kedushat HaYom* comes before *zichronos* and *shofros*.

### Rabbi Yosi's Derivation

On the question of *malchuyos*, the *gemara* presents a *braita* in which Rabbi Yosi bar Yehudah offers an alternative derivation. He argues that *malchuyos* need not be derived from the phrase *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* adjacent to the Rosh Hashanah passage in Leviticus (as *Rebbi* suggests elsewhere). Rather, a derivation is available from the passage in Numbers 10 concerning the *chatzotzros* — the silver trumpets. The verse reads: *u'tekatem b'chatzotzros al olosaychem v'al zivchei shalmaychem vehayu lachem le'zicharon lifnei Elokeichem Ani Hashem Elokeichem*. From this passage, *u'tekatem b'chatzotzros* yields *shofros* (by loose association of blasts); *vehayu lachem le'zicharon* yields *zichronos*; and *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* yields *malchuyos*.

### Binyan Av Logic

What makes Rabbi Yosi's derivation structurally significant — and this is where the *gemara* identifies a *binyan av* — is not simply that it provides a source for *malchuyos*, but that it wraps all three themes together in a single passage, and crucially, together with *tekiyah*. The verse in Numbers 10 links *teki'at chatzotzros*, *zichronos*, and *malchuyos* in one breath. By contrast, the derivation from Leviticus 23 (*Ani Hashem Elokeichem u'vachodesh hashvi'i*) places *malchuyos* prior to the verse describing *shabbaton zichron teruah*, thereby separating it from *zichronos* and *shofros* and from the *tekiyah*.

The Numbers passage, in other words, supports precisely the Rabbi Akiva position: all three themes come together, all three are associated with *tekiyah*, and *malchuyos* is not pushed earlier and removed from center stage. As Rashi in his commentary on the Torah emphasizes, it is the *chatzotzros* passage that provides the textual basis for the entire structure of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* as a unified liturgical category. The *binyan av* principle then extends: wherever *zichronos* appear in the liturgy, *malchuyos* accompanies them — they are not separable themes.

This also clarifies the underlying logic of the earlier dispute. The derivation that places *malchuyos* adjacent to *Kedushat Hashem* (from *Ani Hashem Elokeichem u'vachodesh hashvi'i*) has *malchuyos* introduced before the characterization of the day in *shabbaton zichron teruah*, leaving *tekiyah* disconnected from *malchuyos*. The derivation from the *chatzotzros* passage has all three bound together with *teki'ah* from the outset. The textual choices, in other words, are not merely academic; they directly generate the competing liturgical arrangements.

## Yavneh to Usha

### Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka

The *gemara* reports an episode that confirms which position became normative. When the *Beit Din* sanctified Rosh Hashanah in Usha, Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka descended before the *amud* and led the prayer in the manner of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri — incorporating *malchuyos* with *Kedushat Hashem*, away from the center and without *tekiyah*.

### Yavneh Practice

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel responded immediately: *lo hayu nohagin ken b'Yavneh* — this is not how things were done in Yavneh. Whatever the theoretical debate between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and Rabbi Akiva, the practice that had been established in Yavneh did not follow Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's arrangement.

### Rabbi Akiva Adopted

The following year — or, as Rav Chisda clarifies, *l'yom sheni l'shanah haba'ah*, on a different occasion — Rabbi Chanina, son of Rabbi Yose HaGlili, descended and led the prayer in the manner of Rabbi Akiva, placing *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* in the center, each accompanied by *tekiyot*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel affirmed: *kach hayu nohagin b'Yavneh* — that is precisely how it was done in Yavneh. This confirmed that the practice follows Rabbi Akiva.

The *gemara* notes incidentally that these are not merely hypothetical positions argued in the study hall. Different communities maintained different liturgical traditions, and the Rabbis were working to determine which tradition to make authoritative. The survival of variant traditions — visible even in the Cairo *Genizah* — reflects the genuine diversity of early liturgical practice.

### Shimon's Position

A subtlety requires clarification. The *gemara* raises the question: does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel hold entirely like Rabbi Akiva? On one point he does not. Rabbi Akiva holds that *Kedushat HaYom* comes as the fourth *bracha* — immediately after *Kedushat Hashem* — because *mah matzinu b'chol makom bi-revias*, wherever the sanctity of the day is mentioned it comes in the fourth *bracha*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, by contrast, holds like the position stated elsewhere — *mah matzinu b'chol makom ba'emtza*, the sanctity of the day always falls in the center of the *Shemoneh Esrei*. He therefore places *Kedushat HaYom* with *zichronos* (the middle of the three *malchuyos-zichronos-shofros* grouping) rather than with *malchuyos*. As Rabbi Zeira clarifies, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel agrees with Rabbi Akiva that *malchuyos* belongs in the center of the *Shemoneh Esrei* and that *tekiyot* accompany all three themes; his disagreement concerns only where *Kedushat HaYom* is attached within that center group.

This question of placement is not merely technical. If *Kedushat HaYom* is attached to *malchuyos*, the *chatimah* — *Mekadesh Yisrael v'Yom HaZikaron* — frames the day primarily as a *yom shel malchus*. If it is attached to *zichronos*, the same *chatimah* frames it primarily as a *yom hadin* focused on divine remembrance. Notably, the final *nusach* — *Baruch Atah Hashem Melech al kol ha'aretz mekadesh Yisrael v'Yom HaZikaron* — manages to fold both emphases into a single *chatimah*: the kingship language of *malchuyos* together with the *Yom HaZikaron* of *zichronos*.

## Number of Verses

### Ten Verses Required

The *Mishnah* states: *ein pochtin me'asarah malchuyos me'asarah zichronos me'asarah shofros* — one must not recite fewer than ten *malchuyos*, ten *zichronos*, and ten *shofros*. The *gemara* asks what the ten *malchuyos* correspond to. Rabbi Levi answers: *k'neged asarah hillulim she'amar David b'sefer Tehillim* — the ten praises of God found in Psalm 150, *halleluhu b'teka shofar*. There the word *hallelu* appears in connection with various musical instruments, and counting yields ten instances — hence ten *malchuyos*, consonant with the theme of praising the divine king. Rabbi Yochanan offers a different correspondence: the ten *malchuyos* correspond to the *asarah ma'amarot* — the ten utterances by which the world was created — connecting the theme of God's kingship on Rosh Hashanah directly to the creation of the world.

### Rabbi Yochanan: Three Ve…

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's lenient position is then stated: *im amar shalosh shalosh mi-kullan yatza* — if one recites three from each category, one has fulfilled the obligation.

### Debate Clarified

The *gemara* examines what "three from each category" means. One possibility is that Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri means three *pesukim* from *Torah*, three from *Nevi'im*, and three from *Ketuvim* — for each of the three liturgical themes — yielding nine *pesukim* per theme. If so, the debate is between the *Tanna Kamma*'s requirement of ten and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position of nine, a modest dispute of one *pasuk*. Alternatively, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri may mean one *pasuk* from *Torah*, one from *Nevi'im*, and one from *Ketuvim* — three *pesukim* total per theme, one from each of the three sections of *Tanach*. On this reading the debate between ten and three is substantial.

### Seven Verses Option

A *braita* introduces a further position: *ein pochtin me'asarah malchuyos me'asarah zichronos me'asarah shofros; v'im amar sheva mi-kullan yatza, kenegid shiva reki'im* — ten is the ideal, but *be-diavad* seven suffices, corresponding to the seven firmaments. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's own formulation in this *braita* is: *ha-poches lo yifchos mi-sheva* — one should not recite fewer than seven; *v'im amar shalosh mi-kullan yatza, kenegid Torah Nevi'im v'Kesuvim*. The final phrase clarifies decisively that when he speaks of "three," he means one *pasuk* from each of the three sections of *Tanach* — not three *pesukim* from each section. The debate, then, is indeed between ten and three, with seven as a middle position.

### Rav Huna's Ruling

Rav Huna rules in the name of Shmuel: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's lenient position, meaning that even *be-diavad*, three *pesukim* per theme — one from *Torah*, one from *Nevi'im*, one from *Ketuvim* — constitutes a valid fulfillment.

## Verse Selection Rules

### Excluding Puranus

Once it is established how many *pesukim* are required, the *gemara* turns to which *pesukim* may and may not be selected. The first rule: *ein mazkirin* — one does not recite *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, or *shofros* that relate to *puranus* — suffering and punishment. The liturgy of Rosh Hashanah is oriented toward hope and redemption, and evoking divine wrath directed at Israel is inappropriate on this day.

### Torah-Ketuvim-Nevi'im

The prescribed order for the *pesukim* is: one begins with *Torah*, proceeds through *Ketuvim*, and concludes with *Nevi'im*. This is counterintuitive, since the standard order of *Tanach* is *Torah*, *Nevi'im*, *Ketuvim*, and one might expect the *nusach* to follow suit. Several explanations have been offered by the *Rishonim*, but the most illuminating frames the order in liturgical-temporal terms: the *pesukim* from *Torah* look backward — to foundational moments of creation, covenant, and election. The *pesukim* from *Ketuvim* (predominantly from *Tehillim*) speak to the present — how we praise and encounter God now, how the themes of *malchus*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* express themselves in our ongoing life. The *pesukim* from *Nevi'im* look forward — toward the Messianic redemption and the ultimate realization of these themes. The order thus reflects a temporal arc from past through present to future.

### Examples of Bad Verses

The *gemara* provides examples of *pesukim* that are prohibited because of their association with *puranus*. For *malchuyos*: *Chai ani neum Hashem Elokim im lo b'yad chazaka u'vizroa netuya u'vichema shfucha emloch aleichem* — "As I live, says God, with an outstretched hand and poured-forth wrath I will rule over you." Although Rav Nachman is quoted as saying that even this severe expression of divine anger would be acceptable — for if God vents His anger on us and ultimately redeems us, so be it — nevertheless, since the verse is stated *b'ritcha*, in heat and anger, we do not invoke it at the beginning of the year: *b'ritcha b'reisha d'shata lo madchrinan*.

For *zichronos*: *vayizkor ki basar hema ruach holech v'lo yashuv* (Psalm 78:39) — "He remembered that they were flesh, a wind that passes and does not return" — which, in context, is preceded by verses describing Israel's rebellions in the wilderness.

For *shofros*: *tiku shofar ba'Giva chatzotzra ba'Rama* (Hoshea 5:8) — a passage associated, in its context, with the punishment of Benjamin.

### Allowed: Non-Jews

An important qualification follows: *aval im bau lomar malchuyos, zichronos, u'shofros shel puranus shel ovdei kochavim, omer* — if the *pesukim* of punishment describe calamity befalling the nations rather than Israel, they may be recited. Examples are given. For *malchuyos*: *Hashem malach yirgezu amim* — "God reigns; let the nations tremble"; and *Hashem melech olam va'ed avdu goyim m'artzo* — "God reigns forever; nations will be driven from His land." For *zichronos*: *Zechor Hashem livnei Edom es yom Yerushalayim ha'omrim aru aru* — "Remember, God, against the children of Edom the day of Jerusalem, who said, 'Raze it, raze it.'" For *shofros*: *v'Hashem Elokim b'shofar yitka v'halach b'sa'aros Teiman* — God will blow the *shofar* and go in the storms of the south — followed by the continuation, *Hashem Tzva'os yagen aleihem*, God of hosts will protect them (the Jewish people). These are permitted. The universalist dimension of Rosh Hashanah as a day of divine sovereignty over all nations does not preclude — and in some sense accommodates — the hope for divine protection of Israel and judgment of her enemies.

### No Individual Memory

The *gemara* further rules: *ein mazkirin zichron shel yachid* — one does not recite *zichronos* referring to God's remembrance of an individual, even a positive remembrance. The themes of the *Shemoneh Esrei* are communal and national, and the *zichronos* of Rosh Hashanah must reflect that character. Examples of prohibited individual-remembrance verses: *zachreini Hashem birtzon amecha* — "Remember me, God, in the goodwill of Your people" (Psalm 106:4); and *zachrah li Elokai l'tovah* — "Remember me, my God, for good" (Nehemiah 13:31).

### Zichronos vs. Pikdonos

The *gemara* then introduces a related question: does *pikdon* — God's fixed-time remembrance of someone — count as *zichron*? Rabbi Yosi holds that *pikdonos harei hem k'zichronos* — instances of the root *pakad* are equivalent to *zichronos*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: only the root *zachar* qualifies. The distinction is conceptual. *Zachar* implies constant, ongoing awareness — keeping something continually in mind. *Pakad*, by contrast, implies a fixed-time remembrance or a scheduled visitation — remembering something at an appointed moment. *V'Hashem pakad et Sarah*, for instance, describes God's visit to Sarah at a specific, pre-designated time. The *gemara* in the name of the *Maharsha* connects this to the broader distinction: *zichronos* connotes continuous divine attention, whereas *pikdonos* connotes episodic, time-bound intervention.

### Sarah Exception

Even granting Rabbi Yosi's position that *pikdonos* count as *zichronos*, the verse *v'Hashem pakad et Sarah* would seem to be the remembrance of an individual — and thus prohibited on the grounds stated above. The *gemara* addresses this: *keivan d'atu rabbim mimena* — since the many came from her, since all of *Klal Yisrael* descended from Sarah, the remembrance of Sarah is effectively the remembrance of the entire people. *K'rabbim damya* — it is treated as if it were the remembrance of the collective.
