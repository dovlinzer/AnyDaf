# Ketubot 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Agency Boundaries

### Agent Mandate

The *gemara* on 99a opens with a question that sits at the heart of agency law: under what conditions is a *shaliach* considered to be operating within the terms of his mandate, and at what point has he gone rogue and acted independently? The practical stakes are significant. When an agent buys or sells property on behalf of the person who appointed him, the question of whether those transactions are binding depends entirely on whether he was acting as an agent or on his own account. If he was told to sell land worth a hundred and he sold land worth fifty, or conversely he was told to sell fifty and he sold a hundred, do we say there is at least a zone of overlap in which he was genuinely functioning as an agent? Or do we say that because he deviated from his instructions, the entire transaction is void and he was acting only for himself?

The question matters not only for the validity of commercial transactions but also for the parties' exposure to liability. The person who appointed the agent expects that what was done in his name is binding on him — but only to the extent he authorized it. The agent expects that his actions carry legal weight as an extension of his principal. When those expectations diverge, the *gemara* must draw lines.

It is worth noting that related discussions appear in tractate *Kiddushin*, where a *shaliach* is sent to betroth a woman for a specified sum of money and a dispute arises over whether the agent was authorized to act in a general or a precisely specified capacity. The underlying conceptual tension is the same, though the *sugyot* are scattered.

### Meila & Agency

The *gemara* draws in the laws of *meila* — misuse of *hekdesh*, sanctified property — as a productive test case for the limits of agency. Normally, the rule is *ein shaliach l'dvar aveira*, that an agent cannot be used to perform a transgression on one's behalf. But *meila* is an exception: the law of *yesh shaliach l'dvar aveira* applies here, at least in cases of unwitting misuse. If both the sender and the agent are unaware that the object is *hekdesh*, then the person who sent the agent is the one deemed to have committed *meila*.

This makes *meila* cases a particularly sharp testing ground: who bears the culpability depends directly on whose agency was operative at the moment of the misuse. If the agent deviated from his instructions, the question of whether he was still acting for the sender — or had stepped outside the bounds of agency entirely — determines which of them, or both, has transgressed.

### Overselling/Underselling

The concrete scenario that sets up the discussion involves an agent authorized to sell (or buy) a specific quantity or value and who either exceeds or falls short of that authorization. You tell your agent: sell a half-acre field. He sells an acre. Or: go buy fifty *dinar* worth of goods, and he buys a hundred. In each direction, the question is the same — is there a binding overlap, or is the deviation total? The *gemara* will work through these variations systematically, using both the case of the woman collecting her *ketuba* and the *meila* cases to probe the answer.

## Woman's Selling Power

### Woman as Agent

The *gemara* turns to the *mishna* of our *daf*, which concerns a woman who sells from the estate of her deceased husband's heirs in order to collect the outstanding balance of her *ketuba*. She was not formally appointed by anyone, yet she is treated as a kind of implicit agent — appointed, as it were, by rabbinic authority or by the logic of the *beit din* — acting on behalf of the orphans, even though they may not wish her to be doing so. The *mishna*'s case involves her selling property, and the question is whether her sale is binding.

One might ask why the *gemara* frames her role in terms of *shlichut* at all, since a creditor does not act as agent but simply seizes collateral through the court. The answer lies in the language the *mishna* uses: she sells *shelo b'beit din*, outside of court — language that is inapplicable to a creditor who exercises a lien through formal judicial process. Moreover, if she sells a piece of land worth two hundred for three hundred, the excess goes toward satisfying her debt. A creditor who seizes property and then sells it at a profit does not thereby reduce the underlying debt. The proceeds of her sale go to pay off what she is owed. That structure is characteristic of agency, not of simple debt-collection. The *gemara* therefore conceptualizes her as a *shaliach* of the heirs — a somewhat paradoxical framing, given that the heirs certainly did not appoint her — but that is the conceptual framework the *gemara* adopts.

This arrangement is particularly associated with *mezonot*, the ongoing obligation to provide for one's widow from the estate. Rather than going to court once to collect a fixed sum, she may periodically need to sell off small portions of the estate to cover her living expenses. This recurring character of the transaction explains why the *mishna* thinks in terms of agency rather than one-time judicial seizure.

### Overselling vs Underpric…

The *mishna* contains a case that the *gemara* initially understands as involving a sale of too much land: she was only authorized to sell enough to satisfy one hundred *maneh* of outstanding debt, yet she sold property worth one hundred and twenty-five for one hundred and twenty-five. The *mishna* declares this void. The *gemara*'s first reading is that this proves something about the rule for *shlichut*: that a *shaliach* who oversteps her mandate is treated as having acted entirely on her own — not merely for the excess, but for the whole transaction.

Rav Huna son of Rav Natan then reframes the case entirely. The *mishna* is not talking about overselling in terms of quantity; rather, she sold property worth one hundred and twenty-five for only one hundred. The problem is not that she sold too much — it is that she undersold. She had no right to sell at a loss that would be borne by the estate, and that is why the sale is void. This reading changes the evidentiary import of the case dramatically.

### Two Mishna Cases

Once the end of the *mishna* is read carefully, Rav Huna's reframing becomes not only plausible but necessary. The *mishna* closes with an explicit case: her *ketuba* was four hundred *zuz*; she sold to each of three buyers for one hundred *maneh* each; and to the last buyer she sold property worth one hundred and twenty-five for one hundred. The *mishna* declares: the last sale is void, the earlier ones are binding. That closing case is unambiguously a case of underselling. The *gemara* therefore reasons: *mid'seifa bid'ozil ha'rei reisha bid'lo ozil* — since the end of the *mishna* is clearly about underselling, the beginning need not be about underselling at all. The beginning must therefore be about a different problem: she sold more land than she was entitled to sell — namely, property worth one hundred and twenty-five when her outstanding debt was only one hundred. The two cases are thus distinguished: the first case is about exceeding the scope of her authorization by quantity, the second is about underselling at a price that shifts the loss onto the estate.

## Underpricing Principle

### Woman Takes Loss

Having established that the *mishna*'s first and last cases are distinct, the *gemara* now confronts an apparent difficulty. The first case of the *mishna* includes the scenario where her *ketuba* was two hundred and she sold property worth two hundred for one hundred — that is, she undersold it — and the *mishna* declares her *ketuba* satisfied. How can this be valid if, as the last case shows, underselling is void?

The answer is a fundamental distinction between who bears the loss. In the first case, the outstanding debt is two hundred, the land was worth two hundred, and she sold it for one hundred. She undersold, but there is no injustice to the estate: she simply takes the hit herself. The estate does not suffer. The property she sold was worth what she was owed, and the shortfall in the sale price is simply deducted from the debt as if she had been paid the full value. She pocketed one hundred but we consider her *ketuba* as having been fully paid. She hurt herself, not the heirs, and therefore the sale stands.

This principle works because there is no *ona'ah* by *karka* — no law of overcharging or undercharging that voids a land transaction on the basis of price discrepancy alone. Land has no fixed market price in the way that movable goods do, and therefore the normal *ona'ah* rules that can void a sale of chattel have no application here. If a sale of land is going to be voided, it must be for a different reason — such as the agent acting beyond the scope of her authority, or causing injury to a party who did not consent to the loss.

Here, no party is being injured against their will. The heirs receive what they would have received had she sold at full value: the debt is paid. The woman absorbs the difference. The sale is therefore binding.

### Estate Takes Loss

The last case of the *mishna* presents the contrast. Her *ketuba* was four hundred *zuz*. She has already sold three parcels at one hundred each, leaving one hundred outstanding. She then sells a parcel worth one hundred and twenty-five for one hundred. This is underselling — but now there is no room for her to absorb the loss. Her remaining debt is only one hundred; there is no excess debt against which the extra twenty-five can be offset. The estate — meaning the orphans and heirs — must take the loss. They receive property worth one hundred and twenty-five, and in exchange they have satisfied only one hundred of her debt. The remaining twenty-five in value has simply been taken from them.

Because the estate bears the loss rather than the woman, the sale is void. The heirs can say: you were empowered to act as our agent in selling property to satisfy your debt; you were not empowered to cause us financial injury in the process. That is the principle: *litkunei shidartich velo la'avutei* — I sent you for my benefit, not to harm me — a formulation the *gemara* will deploy again later in the context of a general agent. Since the estate takes the hit, the sale is void.

### Gezeira Explained

The *gemara* now pushes further and asks: why does the *mishna* need to tell us the last case separately? Is the principle not already implicit in the first case? The *gemara* answers that there is an important *chiddush* embedded in the *mishna*'s structure, and it involves a *gezeira* — a protective rabbinic safeguard.

Consider what would happen if the *mishna* only told us the first case and the last case, without implicitly authorizing the middle cases — that is, sales made when her *ketuba* is partially outstanding, say a parcel worth one hundred and twenty-five sold for one hundred, where she still has, say, two hundred in remaining debt. In that middle scenario, she could take the hit: the extra twenty-five would simply be deducted from her outstanding balance. The sale would be valid and she would absorb the shortfall.

One might have thought we should void even the middle sales, on the grounds that allowing them would *eventually* lead to the final scenario — where there is no longer any remaining debt against which to offset the loss. If we permit middle-case underselling on the theory that she will take the hit, buyers may come to rely on this principle; and when she finally reaches the last sale, where there is no hit for her to take, someone might mistakenly think that sale is valid too. To prevent that confusion, perhaps we should void all undersales uniformly.

The *gemara* rejects this reasoning: *nigzor mana rishon atu mana acharon* — do we make a *gezeira* voiding the first-hundred case because it might lead to the last-hundred case? No. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* is precisely that we do *not* make this *gezeira*. The middle sales are valid because in those cases she genuinely takes the loss, and the estate is not harmed. Only when the estate would bear the loss — in the final sale — does the transaction become void. The *mishna* is teaching us that we do not collapse all undersales into a single category; we assess each on its own terms.

## Mosif Doctrine

### Doing More

The *gemara* now abstracts to the general principle governing an agent who does *more* than he was authorized to do. The term is *mosif al devarav* — he adds beyond his mandate. The example: you told your agent to go sell a half-acre field, and he went and sold a full acre. In this case the *gemara* treats the agent's action as divisible: the half-acre that was within the scope of the mandate is a valid sale; the additional half-acre, which exceeded the mandate, was his own independent action. He was *mosif*, not *maavir* — adding on top of the authorized act, rather than going entirely rogue. The base transaction is therefore binding.

This is importantly different from *maavir al devarav*, where the agent steps entirely outside his authorization such that the entire act is his own and none of it is binding as agency. When he merely adds, there is genuine overlap between what was authorized and what he did; that overlap portion retains its status as *shlichut*.

Returning to the woman and her *ketuba*: if she sold one hundred and twenty-five for one hundred and twenty-five, the part of the sale up to her authorized one hundred would be valid under this *mosif* principle. It is only the extra twenty-five that was done on her own. The reason the first case of the *mishna* is void is not, therefore, that the *mosif* principle fails — it is that the *mishna* as Rav Huna reads it is not a *mosif* case at all, but an underselling case, as discussed above.

### Doing Less

The harder question is the reverse: what happens when the agent does *less* than he was asked? You told him to sell an acre and he sold a half-acre. There is no clean overlap in the sense of inclusion: he did not do part of what you asked and add something else. He simply did less. Is the half-acre sale binding as *shlichut* for that amount?

The *gemara* frames this as a genuine question with competing considerations. On one hand, the agent might say to the sender: I did you a favor. If you had needed all the cash right away, you would have needed to sell the whole acre; but by selling only half, I left you the option to come into funds another way and retain the rest of your land. Land, after all, is the primary source of wealth; no one sells it unless in genuine financial distress. To sell half is to preserve the other half against future necessity.

On the other hand, the sender might respond: *lo nicha li d'lifush shetarei ilavai* — I do not want to accumulate a history of multiple sales documents against me. To sell piecemeal is to create a record of repeated transactions, which in the context of this society signals a pattern of financial difficulty, a kind of bad credit history. The owner may well have preferred a single large transaction to multiple small ones — whether for reputational reasons, for the practical advantage of a better price on a larger parcel, or simply because he specified what he wanted and wants his instructions followed. The *gemara* leaves the tension explicit: when an agent does less than authorized, and there is a real consequential difference between what was done and what was asked — not merely a question of quantity — the question of whether the agent was acting within *shlichut* is genuinely unresolved.

## Shirt Purchase Case

### Meila Shirt Example

Rabbi Chanina of Sura introduces a *meila* case that places the question of underdoing in sharp relief. A man gives his *shaliach* a golden *dinar* and says: go buy me a shirt. Unbeknownst to either of them, the *dinar* was *hekdesh* — consecrated property. The agent takes the *dinar*, goes to the market, and with three *sela* — half the *dinar* — buys a shirt; with the other three *sela* he buys a *talit*, a cloak, on his own initiative.

The ruling: *shneihem ma'alu* — both of them committed *meila*. The sender committed *meila* in connection with the shirt, because the agent was acting on his behalf when he bought it. The agent committed *meila* in connection with the *talit*, because that purchase was entirely his own — the sender never authorized it. For the *talit*, the agent was operating outside *shlichut*, so the sender bears no culpability there; the agent does. For the shirt, the agent was operating within *shlichut*, so the sender is implicated.

But notice: the sender said to buy a shirt worth six *sela*, and the agent bought a shirt worth only three *sela*. He did less than he was asked. And yet he is still considered to have been acting as *shaliach* for that purchase. The *gemara* will now examine what exactly this case proves.

### Shirt Dispute

Reading the case straightforwardly: the sender asked for a six-*sela* shirt and received a three-*sela* shirt. Tanna Kamma holds that the agent was nevertheless acting within *shlichut* for the shirt purchase — the sender committed *meila*. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *chaluka di-veina lei sagi u-ma'alya ve-ayteit li chaluka katina ve-ra* — I wanted a large, superior garment and you brought me a small, inferior one. Since what was bought was categorically different from what was requested — not just a lesser quantity of the same thing, but an inferior object — Rabbi Yehuda holds the agent was not acting on behalf of the sender at all. The sender did not commit *meila*; only the agent did.

This maps directly onto the question of whether doing less constitutes valid *shlichut*. If I ask for a six-*sela* shirt and I receive a three-*sela* shirt, have I received what I asked for in diminished form, or have I received something different? Tanna Kamma says the former; Rabbi Yehuda says the latter.

### Sale vs Inferiority

The *gemara* then reframes the debate. Perhaps the dispute between Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehuda is not actually about whether a cheaper shirt counts as doing less of what you were asked. Perhaps everyone agrees that doing less of the same thing does not break *shlichut*. The actual scenario is: the shirt was on sale. The agent went to the market, found a six-*sela* shirt available for three *sela*, bought it at that bargain price, and then used the remaining three *sela* to buy a *talit* for himself.

Under this reading, Tanna Kamma says the agent did exactly what he was asked — he obtained the requested shirt — and did so efficiently, with funds left over. He was acting within *shlichut* for the shirt, and on his own for the *talit*. Rabbi Yehuda dissents: even if the shirt was on sale, the agent should not have pocketed the savings or spent them on a *talit*. Instead, he should have used the full six *sela* to obtain an even finer shirt — since if the shirt that cost three *sela* was worth six *sela* at full price, spending six *sela* could have obtained something worth twelve. The agent was therefore not fully operating as *shaliach* even for the shirt; he should have maximized the sender's benefit with the full sum entrusted to him.

Under this reframing, the underlying question about *doing less* — asking an agent to do one hundred and having him do fifty — is not actually resolved by this *beraita*. Both Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehuda might agree that when an agent does less of the same thing, the *shlichut* for the portion he did accomplish is binding. Their dispute is about a more specific scenario: whether buying a shirt at a bargain price and saving the rest (rather than spending the remainder on a better shirt) constitutes faithful execution of the mandate.

### Legumes Case

Rabbi Yehuda himself concedes the point in a different scenario: *modeh Rabbi Yehuda b'kitnit she-shneihem ma'alu* — Rabbi Yehuda agrees that both committed *meila* in the case of legumes. If the agent was told to buy legumes and bought half the quantity, he agrees the sender is implicated. Why? Because with legumes, it is purely a question of quantity. There is no question of inferior quality or missed opportunity to buy a better product. The agent bought legumes — the right commodity — just less of them. In that case, even Rabbi Yehuda concedes that the portion he did purchase was done within *shlichut*, and the sender therefore committed *meila* for that amount.

This distinction is then examined further. The *gemara* asks: what exactly is the case with the legumes? If produce is sold by rough estimate rather than by precise measure, then presumably buying more would have yielded a better deal — and one could ask why Rabbi Yehuda does not say here too that the agent should have spent the full sum to maximize the benefit. Rav Pappa answers: the case is in a place where legumes are sold by precise, individual measure — *de'amar leh kaneh kaneh b'peruta*, each unit costs a fixed *peruta*, with no bulk discount and no estimation. There is therefore no possible advantage to be gained by spending more, and no argument that the agent should have done otherwise. In that context, there is no ground for Rabbi Yehuda to dispute, and the half-purchase is clearly within *shlichut*.

The upshot of all this is: when the deviation from instructions is purely quantitative and there is no meaningful difference between the partial act and the full act (other than scale), *shlichut* applies to the portion accomplished. When the deviation is qualitative — a different object, a different grade, a different kind of transaction — the question is harder, and that is where the debate between Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehuda, as the *gemara* ultimately understands it, is located.

## Agent Discretion

### One vs Many Buyers

The *gemara* raises a new scenario that shifts the focus from quantity and quality to the manner of executing a mandate. You tell your agent: sell this piece of land to someone. You do not specify *not* to sell it to multiple buyers — you simply say to find a buyer. The agent proceeds to sell it to two or even more buyers, dividing the parcel. Is the sale binding?

Rav Huna holds: *amar l'echad v'lo l'shnayim* — when I say "one," I mean one and not two. Saying "find someone to buy this" implicitly limits the agent to a single transaction. Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna disagree: *afilu l'echad v'afilu l'me'ah* — even one hundred buyers would be acceptable. Saying "one" does not exclude others; it is simply an indefinite reference, not a restriction.

### Mistakes vs Latitude

When Rav Nachman visited Sura and Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna came before him, they posed this very question. Rav Nachman responded in line with the more permissive position: *l'echad v'afilu l'shnayim, l'echad v'afilu l'me'ah* — one is not a limitation, and selling to multiple buyers is within the agent's discretion.

They then pressed him: so you are giving the agent broad latitude — would you therefore also say that if the agent made an outright mistake, such as underselling the land significantly, that too would be binding? Rav Nachman drew a sharp line: *d'b'ta'ei shaliach lo ka'amina* — I am not talking about mistakes. Discretion about the manner of execution — one buyer or several, a particular day or another — is one thing. An outright financial error that injures the sender is quite another. Latitude in execution does not extend to mistakes that fall outside any reasonable range of what the sender could have contemplated.

### Owner vs Agent

The questioners then note a seeming tension: you said that if the agent makes a mistake it is void — but we know that there is no *ona'ah* by *karka*! Real estate has no fixed price, and therefore the rules of *ona'ah* — which void a transaction when the price deviates by more than one-sixth from the fair market value — simply do not apply to land. If there is no fixed price, what does it mean for an agent to have made a "mistake"?

The *gemara* answers: *hani mili heicha de-ta'ah ba'al ha-bayit* — the *ona'ah* exemption applies when the *owner* himself buys or sells. Whatever price the owner accepts, that is a legitimate price for the land; there is nothing to void. But *aval shaliach* — an agent is different. When the owner sends a *shaliach*, he sends him with the instruction *litkunei shidartich velo la'avutei* — I sent you to benefit me, not to harm me. The *shaliach* therefore must operate within a reasonable range of the owner's intent. What the owner could do freely on his own account, the agent cannot do if it amounts to acting against the owner's interests.

### Trumah Range

The *gemara* now cites a proof from *trumah* — the priestly portion separated from grain — to demonstrate that acting within the owner's range is the standard of valid *shlichut*, while acting outside it is not. The principle is established in a *tanna'itic* source: if the owner tells his *shaliach* to separate *trumah* from his grain, the agent should ideally follow the owner's usual practice. If he does not know the owner's practice, he defaults to the middle standard of one in fifty — that is, two percent.

If the agent takes one in forty (slightly more generous) or one in sixty (slightly more conservative), his *trumah* is still valid — those figures represent the edges of the normal range: a generous person gives one in forty, a stingy person gives one in sixty. The agent has deviated somewhat from the middle, but he is still within the normal range of practice, and therefore the *trumah* is binding.

By contrast, if the *owner himself* separated *trumah* at one in ten — five percent, well above the normal range — his *trumah* is nevertheless valid: *taram ba'al ha-bayit afilu echad me'asara, trumato trumah*. The owner acting for himself is not constrained to the normal range; whatever he takes is a legitimate exercise of his ownership. There is no *ona'ah* standard that governs his own action.

This distinction is precisely the parallel to land sales: the owner acting on his own is not limited by a "normal range" of acceptable price; whatever he agrees to is binding. The *shaliach*, however, must operate within the range of what the owner would have sanctioned. Deviating beyond that range — whether in the context of *trumah* or in the context of land sales — takes the act outside the bounds of *shlichut* and renders it void.

### Conclusion

The *gemara* concludes by returning to the *mishna*'s scenario: the woman who sold four hundred *zuz* worth of land in pieces — three parcels for one hundred each and a final parcel worth one hundred and twenty-five for one hundred. The earlier sales are binding; the last is void. But one might ask: if she was supposed to sell on behalf of the estate, why is it permissible for her to sell in multiple transactions, piecemeal, when we have seen that multiple sales may not be in the interest of the seller?

Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi answers: *b'katinei* — these are small, scattered plots of land. There is no single large parcel to sell as a unit. Under those conditions, the only available method of collection is to sell the small plots as they come, and the concern about multiple sales documents and the cumulative record of transactions simply does not arise as a practical matter.

The broader principle that emerges from the *gemara*'s full discussion is this: agency requires operating within reasonable bounds of the owner's intent. An agent who does more than asked may still be valid for the overlapping portion of the mandate. An agent who does less raises a harder question — with the answer depending on whether the deviation is merely quantitative or qualitative, whether the agent's judgment can plausibly be said to serve the owner's interests, and whether the act falls within a normal range of what the owner would have countenanced. What the owner may do freely for himself, the agent may not do arbitrarily; the agent is always constrained by the implicit instruction: I sent you to benefit me, not to harm me.
