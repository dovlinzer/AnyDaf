# Bava Batra 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chazaka Overview

### Opening

We pick up in the top third of 35b, having worked through a series of cases in which one party confronted another with the challenge "what are you doing on my land?" and received in reply the claim "I have been here three years." That extended discussion gave us the opportunity to explore a wide range of conceptual *halachic* issues surrounding the concept of *chazaka*. We now move on to the next phrase of the *mishnah*, working our way through the text now that much of the groundwork has been laid.

### Three Years Day-by-Day

The *mishnah* states: *chazakatan shalosh shanim yom l'yom* — *chazaka* is established by three years of possession from day to day, and applies to something used on an ongoing basis. The best example is a house. The *gemara* has also discussed an irrigated field, and earlier sugyot addressed questions of daytime and nighttime — whether one must testify to nighttime use, and whether one must literally be present every single day.

## Basket of Fruit

### Rav Abba Immediate

Rav Abba states: *idari lei ihu gufei tana d'peirei la'altar havei chazaka* — if the original owner was himself witnessed carrying a basket of fruit from the land alongside the possessor, this creates an immediate presumption of *chazaka* without any requirement of three years. The logic is straightforward: what conceivable reason would I have for helping someone haul a basket of fruit from a field unless he had purchased it from me? Witnesses to such behavior constitute immediate evidence of a completed sale.

### Rav Zevid Exception

Rav Zevid qualifies this: *im ta'an v'amar l'peiros horaditiv* — if the original owner claims that he brought the possessor onto the land specifically for the fruit, meaning the possessor was a sharecropper and not a buyer, then the act of carrying the basket no longer undermines the owner's claim. The owner is believed to say the possessor had rights to the fruit under a sharecropping arrangement without ever having purchased the land. Naturally, this defense requires that the owner advance it from the outset, since reversing course after first denying all knowledge of the possessor's presence would create a problem of *to'en v'chozer v'to'en*.

### Three-Year Cutoff

The *gemara* then establishes: *v'hani milei b'tokh shalosh, aval achar shalosh lo* — the sharecropper defense is available only within the first three years of possession. After three years have elapsed, the owner is no longer believed to say the possessor was merely a sharecropper. The reason is clear: if the sharecropper defense remained available indefinitely, it would hollow out the entire institution of *chazaka*. No matter how many years of uncontested possession a claimant could demonstrate, the original owner could always explain it away as a sharecropping arrangement. Therefore the *halacha* forecloses that defense once three years have passed.

### Mechah Protection

This immediately raises the next question: if the owner is not believed after three years to claim sharecropping, what can a genuine landowner who has legitimately placed a sharecropper on his land do to protect his own interests?

## Mechah Institution

### Sharecropper Problem

Rav Ashi poses the question directly to Rav Kahana: *i l'peira nachit, mai havei lei l'me'evad?* — if the owner genuinely brought someone onto the land only for the fruit, what was he supposed to do? How was he to protect himself?

### Mechah Distinction

The *gemara* answers that the owner can and must make a *mecha'ah*, a formal public protest — but one of a different character than the ordinary *mecha'ah*. The ordinary *mecha'ah* consists of appearing before two witnesses and declaring that the possessor is a *gazlan*, occupying the land without any permission. That formulation is clearly inapplicable to a sharecropper. Instead, the owner must appear before two witnesses and announce: be aware that this person is on my land as a sharecropper, not as a legitimate owner. If the possessor is in fact the legitimate owner, he will hear of this announcement and will know to guard his *shtar* carefully — just as a buyer would protect his deed in response to any *mecha'ah* calling him a thief.

### Mechah Limits

This is a significant *chiddush*: it reveals that *mecha'ah* is not an institution reserved for cases of illegitimate occupation. Any time land is legitimately rented or sharecropped — which must have been exceedingly common — the owner had to make regular *mecha'ot* to protect his interests and prevent the possessor from acquiring a *chazaka* claim. One observes, however, that this places the genuine buyer in a more precarious position. When the *mecha'ah* is of the ordinary kind, calling the possessor a thief, word travels quickly. But an announcement that someone is "merely a sharecropper" does not carry the same social urgency — it is not the kind of thing people necessarily gossip about. This means a legitimate buyer must now keep his ears open not only for a *mecha'ah* declaring him a *gazlan*, but also for a *mecha'ah* declaring him a sharecropper or a renter. Any kind of ruling here will favor one side at the expense of the other, which is why all the more so people must be extremely careful with their *shtaros*.

### Mashkanta Problem

The *gemara* reinforces this conclusion with a compelling case: *hanei mashkanta desura* — the institution of the *mashkanta desura*. In this arrangement, Charlie borrows ten thousand dollars from the lender and transfers his field to the lender for, say, ten years. The lender holds the field, harvests the produce, and applies that produce toward the debt; at the end of ten years, the debt is discharged and the field returns to Charlie. The contract explicitly states: *bemishlam shanya ilein tipok ara bela chaspa* — at the end of these years, the land goes out without payment, meaning the debt is fully settled. This arrangement is structured to avoid a straightforward *ribbit* problem by recasting what happens as a conditional purchase of ten years of fruit rather than an interest-bearing loan.

Now: in a *mashkanta desura*, who holds the document? The lender, because he needs to prove that Charlie owes him money. Charlie has no copy. So if the lender after three years simply chooses to conceal the *shtar* and claim he purchased the field outright, who is going to stop him? He has three years of uncontested possession, no one else holds documentation of the arrangement. This is a gaping avenue for abuse.

### Mechah Conclusion

*Ela ibayei lei lemichuyei* — the answer is that Charlie must make a *mecha'ah*, announcing that the lender is on his land pursuant to a *mashkanta* arrangement and not as a genuine owner. This both protects Charlie and ensures that the lender, if he is acting legitimately, is on notice to preserve his documentation. *Hacha nami ibayei lei lemichuyei* — the same principle applies to the sharecropping case. The possibility of a *mecha'ah* that identifies legitimate use arrangements is what makes the entire rabbinic institution of *mashkanta desura* viable in the first place. Without it, no creditor would be safe from a lender simply claiming ownership after three years.

## Non-Jew Chain

### Non-Jew Blocks

Rav Yehuda rules: *Yisrael haba machmat akum harei hu ke'akum* — a Jew whose chain of title runs through a non-Jew stands in the same position as the non-Jew himself. If Charlie says he bought the field from Christopher, who purchased it from the original Jewish owner, Charlie's *chazaka* claim is only as strong as Christopher's would have been.

### Non-Jew Exception

*Ma akum ein lo chazaka ella beshtar* — a non-Jew cannot acquire land through *chazaka*; he can prove ownership only by producing a *shtar*. *Af Yisrael haba machmat akum ein lo chazaka ella beshtar* — similarly, a Jew who traces his title through a non-Jew cannot rely on *chazaka* and must produce documentary evidence. The reason a non-Jew cannot establish *chazaka* is rooted in the historical reality of power imbalances: non-Jews were frequently in a position of dominance, capable of seizing fields from Jewish owners who dared not protest. The silence of the Jewish owner cannot be taken as *mechila* or acquiescence when the realistic alternative was fear. Since there is a ready explanation for the owner's silence other than acceptance of the transfer, the three years of uncontested possession prove nothing. Charlie, by basing his claim on Christopher's tenure, is building on an unsubstantiated foundation — *chazaka she'eina mitzad ta'ana* — a *chazaka* that lacks a valid underlying claim.

### Rav Yosef Query

Rav Yosef challenges: *lididi amar li akum de-minach zavnah* — what if Charlie says the non-Jew told him directly that he had purchased the field from the original owner? Should that testimony not be sufficient? The *gemara* immediately presses back: *mi ika midi d'ilu akum amar lo meheman, ve'ilu amar Yisrael mishmeih d'akum meheman?* Is there anything where had the non-Jew himself made the claim he would not have been believed, yet a Jew repeating that same claim on the non-Jew's behalf is believed? Charlie's report of what Christopher said does not transform Christopher's legally insufficient claim into a valid one.

### Rava Resolution

Rava clarifies: *ei amar Yisrael kamei didi zavnah minach v'zavnah nihali* — if Charlie says he has direct knowledge that the non-Jew acquired the field legitimately from the original owner — either because he himself witnessed the transaction or because he saw the *shtar* — then *meheman*, he is believed. The key is direct, personal knowledge of the non-Jew's legitimate acquisition, not merely repeating the non-Jew's assertion.

### Miggo Principle

The *gemara* grounds this in a *miggo* argument: *migo d'ei ba'ei amar lei ana zevintah minach* — since Charlie could have simply claimed that he himself purchased the field directly from the original owner, and that claim would have been believed, his alternative claim that he purchased it from the non-Jew who legitimately acquired it from the original owner is equally credible. We believe him not only that he bought from the non-Jew but — given his testimony of direct knowledge of the non-Jew's acquisition — that the non-Jew was a genuine owner.

## Non-Chazaka Cases

### Bold Claim

Rav Yehuda states: *hai man d'nakit migla v'tuvila v'amar eizil egzeiyeh l'dikla d'planya d'zavnteih mineih* — if someone picks up a scythe and a basket and announces that he is going to go harvest dates from a specific palm tree because he purchased it, he is immediately believed. *Lo chatzif inish l'migzar dikla d'lav dileih* — people are not brazen enough to go harvest a palm tree that is not theirs. The act has not yet occurred, so the fruit is still *karka* and not *metaltelin*, but the bold, public declaration of intent constitutes evidence equivalent to a *chazaka*, because no one would make such a claim falsely.

### Buffer Zone

Rav Yehuda also rules: *hai man d'achzik migudi d'arudai l'var lo havei chazaka* — possession of produce from the *gudi d'arudai*, the narrow strip of land outside the fence of a field where seeds fall and animals graze, does not constitute *chazaka*. The reason: *kol d'zarna nami avuda achli lei* — the owner reasons that whatever sprouts in that buffer strip will be eaten by animals regardless. He has no realistic expectation of harvesting that produce himself, so he has no motivation to protest when someone else takes it. The absence of protest therefore proves nothing about ownership.

### Orlah Produce

Rav Yehuda further rules: *achal orla eina chazaka* — eating *orla* fruit does not constitute *chazaka*. Since the owner is forbidden from eating it anyway, he has no reason to protest another's consumption. There is an alternative *girsa* that reads *havei chazaka*, but the accepted version holds it is not. This means that a year in which all the produce is *orla* does not count toward the three-year *chazaka* period.

### Shevi'it Kilayim

A *braita* extends and confirms: *achal orla, shevi'it, v'kilayim — eina chazaka*. Eating *orla* and *kilayim* does not count because the owner has no personal interest in those fruits in any case, and *shevi'it* produce does not count because all have rights to it — the owner cannot meaningfully protest another's access to what is publicly available.

### Sachis Fodder

Rav Yosef rules: *achal shachas lo havei chazaka* — harvesting *shachas*, which is fodder produced by cutting grain before it reaches full growth and then allowing it to regrow, does not constitute *chazaka*. The reasoning: a genuine owner would wait for the crop to reach full maturity and harvest it at its peak value. Someone who harvests only the *shachas* and takes nothing more reveals furtive behavior — he is sneaking in to take what he can quickly, behavior more consistent with a thief than an owner. One might also note that if the person harvested only the *shachas* and the crop nonetheless regrew fully, someone else must have harvested the full growth, which would directly challenge any ownership claim.

### Sachis Exception

Rava qualifies: *ve-i b'tzurei Mechuza ka-amar, havei chazaka* — if this ruling was stated with reference to the valleys of Mechuza, where cultivating fodder was the normal agricultural practice, then harvesting *shachas* does count as *chazaka*, since doing so is consistent with how a genuine owner of such land would behave.

### Tafticha Barren

Rav Nachman rules: *tafticha lo havei chazaka* — possession of *tafticha*, cracked and sun-baked land, does not constitute *chazaka*. Either because the land is so unproductive that virtually nothing grows there, giving the owner no motivation to protest someone else's negligible use of it — or because the cracked surface requires no plowing before planting, so the process of working it is not the normal ongoing agricultural activity that would signal ownership.

### No-Profit Land

Similarly: *apei kura v'ayeil kura lo havei chazaka* — land so infertile that one recovers exactly as much grain as one planted, yielding zero profit, does not support a *chazaka* claim. The owner can credibly say he had no reason to protest because he had no real interest in what the possessor was doing on land of such negligible productivity. One might ask: what if the land was productive in principle but a bad harvest — due to blight, drought, or locusts — happened to yield only a break-even result that year? The *gemara* seems to be speaking specifically about land whose intrinsic quality makes it perpetually unprofitable — what might be called hardscrabble land — as opposed to a temporary circumstantial failure of yield.

## Power Asymmetry

### Resh Galuta

The *gemara* rules: *hanei de-vei reish galuta* — with respect to land involving the household of the *reish galuta* (the exilarch), different rules apply.

### Fear Prevents

*Lo machazikinan behu* — if the *reish galuta*'s household has been occupying one's land, no *chazaka* is established in their favor, because the Jewish minority population might well have been afraid to protest an occupation by those in political power. The absence of *mecha'ah* therefore does not constitute acquiescence.

### Reciprocal

The rule is symmetrical: *ve-lo machazikinan behu* — if Jews have been occupying land belonging to the *reish galuta*'s household, no *chazaka* is established in their favor either.

### Force Override

The Rashbam explains: those in power have no need to make a formal *mecha'ah* — they can simply reclaim by force at any time. Their silence is therefore not evidence of consent. The principle is thus not specifically about Jews versus non-Jews but about power asymmetry in general: whoever is in a position to seize land without legal recourse creates a situation in which neither their occupation of another's land nor another's occupation of their land can produce a valid *chazaka*.

## Animals & Movables

### Animate Property

The *mishnah* includes slaves among the categories for which *chazaka* operates. Unlike ordinary *metaltelin* — where mere physical possession is itself evidence of ownership — slaves are not inanimate objects whose presence in one's domain conclusively proves title. Their status, as the *gemara* will explain, is more complex.

### Roaming Flocks

The *gemara* invokes Reish Lakish's ruling: *ha-godrot ein lahem chazaka* — flocks have no *chazaka*. The word *godrot* refers to *gidrot tzon*, flocks of sheep. By this Reish Lakish does not mean there is no three-year *chazaka*, but rather that immediate physical possession does not constitute *chazaka* the way it does for ordinary *metaltelin*. The reason is that flocks move on their own volition: a lamb wandering into one's field or following someone down the street does not thereby become that person's property. Since animals move independently, the fact that one is currently holding an animal proves nothing about how it came to be there.

### Three Years Needed

Rava clarifies: *ein lahem chazaka le'altar, aval yesh lahem chazaka le'achar gimmel shanim* — immediate possession does not establish ownership, but three years of uncontested possession does. After three years, the silence of the original owner becomes meaningful evidence. Strikingly, this places slaves and flocks in the same legal category as land — not for the formal reason that slaves are *karka*, but because they are living things that move on their own volition, making their physical presence in someone's domain inconclusive as immediate proof of ownership.

### Volition Logic

For slaves, the three-year framework fits well in a second respect as well: slaves are typically sold with a *shtar*, just as land is, and one can apply the same logic of "if you really bought this slave, produce your deed; after three years your inability to do so no longer defeats your claim." For flocks, however, there would normally be no *shtar* accompanying a sale of sheep, which makes the three-year standard feel somewhat less intuitively grounded than it does for land or slaves. Nonetheless, the principle that things moving on their own volition require this extended period appears to operate as a categorical rule. It is worth noting that this creates an apparent gap: between immediate possession — which proves nothing — and three years — which proves ownership. There does not appear to be an intermediate position.

### Infant Exception

Rava adds: *im haya katan mutal ba'arisa, yesh lo chazaka le'altar* — if the slave in question is an infant lying in a cradle, then immediate possession does establish ownership. The *gemara* asks: is this not obvious? An infant in a cradle cannot possibly have walked there on its own — so the concern about independent movement simply does not apply.

### Mother Logic

The *gemara* pushes back: one might have thought we should be concerned that the infant was brought there by its mother, who then left it temporarily — perhaps she stepped away on an errand and the possessor is now falsely claiming ownership. The *gemara* therefore teaches: *ima lo minashya breih* — a mother does not forget her child. A slave mother would not abandon her infant with a stranger, particularly in the context of a slave society where separation from one's child would be a devastating and permanent loss. Therefore, if an infant slave is found in someone's possession, it could not have arrived through the mother's voluntary temporary deposit, and immediate possession proves acquisition.

## Goats Case

### Seize for Claim

The *gemara* presents a practical case from Nehardea. Some goats belonging to Charlie entered a field and ate *chushla* — husk barley — belonging to the field's owner. The owner (the plaintiff) now needs to recover compensation. The difficulty is evidentiary: the plaintiff claims the goats ate a thousand dollars' worth of grain, while Charlie will insist the amount was trivial. Under the rule of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*, the burden of proof lies on the claimant. To improve his position, the plaintiff seizes Charlie's goats. Now the question becomes how much the plaintiff can claim while holding them.

### Value Limit

The father of Shmuel rules: *yelchu l'itun ad k'dai demeihen* — the plaintiff is believed in his damage claim up to the full value of the goats themselves.

### Miggo Applied

The basis is *miggo*: *migo d'ei ba'ei amar lekuchim heim b'yadi* — since the plaintiff could simply have said "I bought these goats from Charlie," and thereby kept them entirely, he is also believed to say their full value was consumed in damages. The *miggo* supports his claim up to the threshold of the strongest claim he could have made — namely, that the goats are simply his.

### Flocks Paradox

The *gemara* raises an apparent contradiction: did not Reish Lakish rule that flocks have no *chazaka*? If that is so, how does the plaintiff's seizure of the goats give him any standing to claim ownership — even fictively for purposes of a *miggo*?

### Shepherd Supervision

The *gemara* resolves: *shani izzei de'masrinan leroya* — goats are different from sheep because they are handed over to a dedicated shepherd. Goats, apparently, are prone to wandering or running off, and therefore their owners maintain constant active supervision through a shepherd who is specifically in charge of them at all times. Unlike sheep, which might be left to roam somewhat more freely, goats are under a tighter supervisory regime, making their presence in someone's domain more probative of ownership. Accordingly, when someone is holding goats, the logic of independent-volition movement applies with less force.

### Supervision Gaps

The *gemara* qualifies: *v'ika tzipra u'panya* — but there are gaps in the morning and evening when the goats are not under continuous supervision. In the morning, the owner releases them and they make their way out to where the shepherd is waiting in the field, and in the evening the shepherd releases them and they find their way back to their owner independently. During these periods they are unsupervised.

The *gemara* responds that the case concerns Nehardea, where *kayla shechicha* — nomads or bandits were prevalent — and therefore the goats would be handed over directly from owner to shepherd and back again, *mi'yada l'yada meshallmi*, without any gap in supervision. This underscores the broader principle that *chazaka* for animate property is not determined by rigid categories but by the actual circumstances of supervision and movement — exactly as with the infant slave, whose physical immobility places it firmly in the category of property for which immediate possession is conclusive.

## Partial-Year Rules

### Seasonal Cycles

We now turn to the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. The cases we have been discussing involved land used year-round — irrigated fields and homes. But a *sde habaal*, a rain-watered field, follows seasonal agricultural cycles. For such a field, Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva both hold that one need not demonstrate three complete consecutive twelve-month years, but something representative of three years: a full middle year plus partial usage at the end of the first year and the beginning of the third.

### Yishmael Approach

Rabbi Yishmael requires three months at the end of year one, a full middle year, and three months at the beginning of year three.

### Akiva Approach

Rabbi Akiva requires only one month at the end of year one, a full middle year, and one month at the beginning of year three. The *gemara* will clarify what drives this difference.

### Multiple Harvests

The *mishnah* further provides that in certain cases — where a field has multiple distinct harvest cycles within a single year, such as grapes, dates, and figs — three separate harvests within a single calendar year can constitute a three-year *chazaka*, since each harvest represents a distinct cycle of use.

### Nir Debate

One might have thought the difference between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva — three months versus one month for the partial years — hinges on the question of *nir*, plowing or tilling. *Rabbi Yishmael savar nir lo havei chazaka* — Rabbi Yishmael holds plowing alone does not count, so he requires three months to ensure some actual harvest. *Rabbi Akiva savar nir havei chazaka* — Rabbi Akiva holds plowing does count, so one month suffices. But the *gemara* rejects this: if tilling alone were sufficient for Rabbi Akiva, then one month would be far more than necessary — one could tile in a single day. *Ela dechulei alma nir lo havei chazaka* — rather, all agree that plowing alone does not constitute *chazaka*. The difference between them is rather about the minimum harvest required: Rabbi Yishmael requires substantial produce demanding a full three-month growing cycle, while Rabbi Akiva accepts even minimal produce attainable within a single month — perhaps even the kind of *shachas*-level fodder that can be cut quickly.

## Nir Chazaka

### Tannaitic View

The *gemara* now takes up the *nir* question directly. A *braita* states: *nir einu chazaka* — plowing is not *chazaka*. Then immediately: *v'yesh omrim harei zeh chazaka* — and some say it is.

### Pattern Rule

The *braita*'s illustration of when it is *not* *chazaka* is: *neira shana u'zra'ah shtayim* — one tilled for one year and planted for two, or *neira shtayim u'zra'ah shana* — tilled for two years and planted for one. None of these combinations constitute *chazaka*.

### Acha Position

Rav Chisda identifies the *yesh omrim* as Rabbi Acha. According to Tosafot, even Rabbi Acha would not recognize two consecutive years of tilling alone as *chazaka*. What Rabbi Acha does recognize is a pattern such as: till year one, plant year two, till year three. In that context, the years of tilling are set within a framework of genuine agricultural ownership — tilling and planting in alternating years is exactly what a real owner does — and the tilling years are thereby given meaning by the planting year that contextualizes them. This is the *chiddush* of Rabbi Acha: even a single year of actual planting, constituting one-third of the three-year period, provides enough of an ownership context to make the tilling years count.

### Majority View

The *chachamim* disagree. One year of planting is insufficient provocation to compel the original owner to protest the other years of tilling. The *chazaka* must rest on something more substantial than that.

### Authority List

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds to an invocation of "all the *gedolei hador*" — the great scholars of the generation — in favor of *nir* constituting *chazaka*, by countering with his own list of authorities: *ha Rav u-Shmuel be-Vavel, u-Rabbi Yishmael v'Rabbi Akiva be-Eretz Yisrael* — Rav and Shmuel in Babylonia, and Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva in the Land of Israel — all hold that *nir* does not work. The invocation of names is not itself an argument; what matters is who can marshal the stronger analysis.

### Mishnah Proof

That Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva hold *nir* insufficient is demonstrated from the *mishnah* itself. Both require at minimum one month or three months for the partial years flanking the full middle year. If tilling alone were sufficient, neither would need any minimum time at all — a single day of plowing would qualify. The fact that they require a month or three months demonstrates they demand an actual harvest, and therefore both implicitly reject *nir* as a standalone basis for *chazaka*.

### Rav Position

Rav Yehuda citing Rav states: the one-month and three-month approaches belong to Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva, but *aval Chachamim omrim, chazaka gimmel shanim mi-yom le-yom* — the *chachamim* require three full years from day to day. The phrase *mi-yom le-yom* serves to exclude *nir*: if three full years are required and tilling counted, then the possessor could spend three years doing nothing but intermittent plowing. But no one would imagine the requirement is to plow continuously for three years — that is not how anyone farms. Three full years must therefore mean three years of genuine agricultural use — planting and harvesting — not merely overturning soil.

### Shmuel Position

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel similarly identifies the partial-year approaches as belonging to Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. But Shmuel frames the *chachamim*'s standard differently: *ad she-yigdor shalosh gedirot, v'yivtzor gimmel betzirot, v'yimsok gimmel mesikot* — one must complete three full date harvests, three full grape harvests, or three full olive harvests. The partial harvest of a month or three months at the flanking years is not sufficient; three genuinely complete harvest cycles are required. Critically, Shmuel's formulation counts *harvests*, not *calendar years*.

### Young Palm

What is the practical difference between Rav and Shmuel? Rav says three full calendar years; Shmuel says three full harvests. The *gemara* identifies the case where this matters: *dekel na'ara* — a young palm tree that can yield multiple harvests within a single calendar year. According to Shmuel, three full harvests from such a tree, even if all obtained within less than three calendar years, would constitute *chazaka*. According to Rav, three complete calendar years are always required regardless of harvest frequency.

### Mechanics TBD

At this point the question of the precise mechanics of *chazaka* — whether it requires three full calendar years as Rav holds, or three full harvests as Shmuel holds — remains open for further analysis. One thing is clear: both Rav and Shmuel, together with Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva, all agree that *nir* alone is insufficient. The discussion of what exactly constitutes the required three-year measure, and how the *chachamim*'s standard operates in practice, will continue in the next session.
