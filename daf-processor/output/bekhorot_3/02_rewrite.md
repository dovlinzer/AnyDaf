# Bekhorot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tosafot: Non-Jews & Oaths

### Partnership Prohibition

The Gemara at the end of the previous daf cited a statement of Avuha DeShmuel that a person is not permitted to enter into a business partnership with a non-Jew. The concern, as the Gemara frames it, is that disputes over financial honesty would naturally be resolved by having the parties swear an oath. If a non-Jew were compelled to swear, he would do so in the name of his god, and the Jewish partner would thereby have caused the invocation of an idolatrous deity in a context expressing recognition and respect — transgressing the verse *lo yishama al picha*, "the name of other gods shall not be heard on your lips." The rabbis understand this verse to prohibit not merely the personal utterance of a foreign deity's name in a reverential context, but even causing others to utter such a name in that fashion. It is worth noting that this prohibition does not bar the mere mention of other gods' names in ordinary discourse — one may say "Zeus" without transgressing — but it does prohibit invoking them in any way that acknowledges them as genuine divine powers.

### Oath Problem

This prohibition creates a striking practical problem. The Tosafot on 70b, beginning with the word *shema*, takes up precisely this difficulty: if entering into business partnerships with non-Jews is forbidden due to the oath concern, how have Jews throughout the medieval period routinely formed such partnerships with Christians — and indeed, how have they allowed Christians to swear oaths in the course of those partnerships, oaths that typically invoke the names of saints and, alongside that, the name of God?

### Tosafot's Solution

Rabbeinu Tam, cited approximately ten lines from the bottom of the Tosafot in the passage beginning *lo hayu nizharin bahen*, first makes the general observation that other Gemarot suggest this prohibition was not rigorously observed even in the Talmudic period itself — raising the broader question of how a stated *halacha* interacts with widespread contrary practice. But the more consequential contribution follows immediately: *bizman hazeh kulam nishba'in bekadeshim* — nowadays, when Christians take oaths, they swear in the name of their saints, not in the name of Jesus. Since saints are not being accorded divinity — *ein tofsim bahem elohut*, their sanctity is acknowledged but they are not treated as gods — swearing in a saint's name is not swearing in the name of *elohim acherim*, another god.

A further complication arises from the fact that oaths typically combine the saint's name with the name of God. Rabbeinu Tam acknowledges this: *af al pi shemazkirim imahem shem shamayim*, even when they also say "God," *vekavanatam ledavar acher* — their intention when they say God differs from ours. Yet *mikol makom ein zeh shem avodat kochavim*, it is not the name of another god, *ki da'atam le'oseh shamayim va'aretz* — because when Christians say "God," they mean the creator of heaven and earth. Whatever the theological complications surrounding Jesus, when they appeal to God the Father in an oath, they are directing themselves to what is, in essence, the same concept of God as the creator that we ourselves invoke. So far, then, there is no problem of causing them to swear in the name of an idolatrous god.

A further issue remains: the oath combines God's name with another entity — the saint — and this constitutes a form of *shituf*, linking God's name with something else. A Jew cannot take an oath in this manner, because it places something created on the same level as God. Does causing the non-Jew to take such an oath violate the prohibition of *lifnei iver lo titein michshol*? Rabbeinu Tam answers: *debnei Noach lo huzharu akach* — non-Jews are not prohibited from this form of combined oath. And *lo ashkechan issur begram shituf* — there is no prohibition on us causing them to take an oath in that form. The prohibition of combining God's name with something else in an oath applies to Jews; we do not violate any prohibition by allowing a non-Jew to do it.

Read correctly, this Tosafot does no more than solve the specific practical problem of oaths and partnerships. In doing so, it also implicitly acknowledges a significant theological point: that within the Christian concept of God the Father, there is a genuine overlap with the Jewish understanding of the creator of heaven and earth. Christianity is still characterized as *avodah zarah* throughout the *rishonim*, but this Tosafot acknowledges the important domain of shared monotheistic conviction.

### Misinterpretation Issue

The historical influence of this Tosafot, however, derives largely from a misreading of it. When Rabbeinu Tam writes that there is no prohibition on non-Jews combining God's name with something else *in an oath*, if one drops the limiting phrase "in an oath," the statement transforms into a general theological dispensation: non-Jews are not prohibited from combining God and other concepts in their religious belief. Under this reading, the Trinity itself becomes permissible for non-Jews — not for Jews, for whom it remains *avodah zarah*, but for non-Jews, for whom a faith that combines an ultimate creator God with other divine figures presents no violation.

### Historical Impact

This misinterpretation was already institutionalized by the time of the Rema, whose rulings carry authoritative weight for Ashkenazic communities in the *Shulchan Aruch*, and it introduced what amounts to a dual standard for defining *avodah zarah*: for Jews, Christianity is *avodah zarah*; for non-Jews, it is not. This shift carried profound practical and attitudinal consequences. It allowed certain *halachot* to be applied less restrictively to Christians, and it fostered what might be called a quasi-pluralistic orientation — not a genuine theological pluralism, but at minimum a non-judgmental posture toward Christian belief. One can maintain the boundary — it is still *avodah zarah* for a Jew — without having to characterize Christians as *ovedei avodah zarah*. The misinterpretation of this Tosafot has entered so thoroughly into *halachic* discourse that the majority of *acharonim* today cite it without awareness that it represents a misreading of the original argument.

### Student Questions

One might ask why the oath concern ever arose at all, since parties could simply give their word without invoking any deity. The answer is that in the world in which Tosafot operated, an oath taken in the name of one's God was the operative mechanism for ensuring honesty in financial disputes. Where suspicions of fraud or dishonest bookkeeping arose, a sworn oath in the name of the relevant deity was the accepted instrument of resolution, and virtually no one in that era was in a position to substitute a purely secular affirmation. One might further ask whether the prohibition of partnership with non-Jews effectively became obsolete once sworn oaths ceased to be the standard mechanism for enforcing commercial honesty. This is a reasonable inference: if the oath concern dissolves, the prohibition grounded in it would logically fall away as well. What can be said is that by the time of the Tosafot the law had already been incorporated into the tradition, and by the time the oath mechanism itself began to lose its social force, the Tosafot's resolution was already deeply embedded in *halachic* literature — especially in its misread form.

## Non-Jewish Ownership

### Raising Animals Case

The Gemara now returns to the central topic of the *daf* — whether and how non-Jewish ownership of an animal affects the animal's status as a *bechor*. A *baraita* introduces the position of Rabbi Yehuda: *hamakabel behemah min ha'oved kochavim veyaldah* — if a person receives an animal from a non-Jew under a *kablanut* arrangement (taking the animal to raise, with the offspring to be split between them), and the animal gives birth, *ma'alin oto beshom venoten chatzi damav lakohen* — the animal is appraised and half its value is given to the *kohen* on account of the firstborn. This is Rabbi Yehuda's position: non-Jewish co-ownership does not exempt the animal from *bechor* status, so one must pay out one's share of the *bechor*'s value to the *kohen*.

The *baraita* adds a further ruling: if one gave the animal to the non-Jew under *kablanut* — transferring it to him to raise and take half the offspring — even though this is prohibited, *konsin oto ad asar bedamav* — we fine the person up to ten times the animal's value in order to compel the buyback of the firstborn, and *venoten kol damav lakohen* — because the person has transgressed, he must give the entire value of the firstborn to the *kohen*, not merely half. The Sages, by contrast, rule: *kol zman sheyad oved kochavim be'emtza, patur min habechorah* — as long as a non-Jew has a hand in it, meaning any co-ownership, the animal is fully exempt from *bechorah*.

The Gemara attempts to use this passage to resolve an open question from the previous daf: is selling a mother animal for the sake of her future offspring included in the prohibition against selling animals to non-Jews? Since the *baraita* describes a case in which the arrangement presumably transfers some right to future offspring, perhaps it proves that such an arrangement is forbidden. The Gemara rejects this: *lo, uvar* — the prohibition here concerns a case where the animal was already pregnant and the fetus itself was sold, not a sale of the mother contingent on future offspring. The question of selling a mother for her future offspring thus remains unresolved. What is confirmed, however, is that selling an existing fetus is included in the prohibition.

### Fine Amounts

The *baraita*'s mention of a fine — up to ten times the value — for one who has transgressed the prohibition of giving an animal to a non-Jew under *kablanut* sets the stage for a broader discussion about the nature and scope of these fines. It is also worth recalling the background prohibition here: the concern is that large working animals (*behemah gassah*) — cattle, horses, and similar — will be used for labor on *Shabbat*. Even if the non-Jew ultimately becomes the owner, there may be a period during which the animal is still technically the Jew's while already being worked, or it may continue to appear as a Jewish-owned animal to outside observers. For these reasons, the sale of large animals to non-Jews is prohibited.

### Reish Lakish Position

The Gemara cites a statement of Reish Lakish directly: *hamokher behemah gassah le'ovdei kokhavim konsin oto ad asarah be'dameha* — one who sells a large animal to a non-Jew is fined up to ten times its value in order to compel the buyback.

### Literal vs Exaggeration

The Gemara raises the question: *davka o lo davka* — is the figure of ten meant literally, or is it an exaggeration? Does it mean precisely ten, or does it mean "a very large amount, up to some high limit that we are rounding off as ten"? To resolve this, the Gemara appeals to a parallel teaching: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi ruled that one who sells a slave to a non-Jew is fined *ad me'ah be'dameha* — up to one hundred times the slave's value in order to compel buyback. The concern with selling a slave is distinct: a slave in Jewish ownership who has undergone *brit milah* and *mikveh* is obligated in certain *mitzvot*, and transferring him to non-Jewish ownership removes that obligation.

### Slave vs Animal Fines

Rashi's argument is that if ten and a hundred were both mere exaggerations — rough ways of saying "a very high amount" — one would not use two different exaggerated numbers in two different contexts. The fact that one text says ten and another says one hundred indicates that they are both meant literally, and that there is a real and considered distinction between the two cases.

### Rarity Principle

The Gemara then explores what justifies the different amounts. One approach: the slave's case is more serious because *kol yoma ve'yoma mevatlei lei mi'mitzvot* — every single day the slave is being prevented from performing the *mitzvot* he is obligated in. That severity could justify a literally higher fine of one hundred. But perhaps "ten" by the animal is still merely an exaggeration.

A second version of the tradition presents the figures in reverse: Reish Lakish says a hundred for selling the animal, and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says ten for selling the slave. The Gemara then draws a distinction to explain why the animal's fine is higher: *mekhirah paskah mineih* — when one fully sells the animal, one has completely divested oneself of it; the severance is total. In *kablanut*, by contrast, *lo paskah mineih* — the owner has not entirely separated himself from the animal, and so the fine is lesser. This resolves the contradiction between the hundred in Reish Lakish's ruling and the ten in the *mishnah* regarding *kablanut*.

When the fine for the animal is set at one hundred and the fine for the slave is set at ten, the Gemara offers yet another explanation for the gap: *shani eved delo hadar lei* — the case of the slave is different because the slave, once bought back, does not return to the owner's possession; as the Mishnah in Gittin teaches, once sold to a non-Jew, the slave must be freed upon buyback. Since the owner will ultimately lose the slave, the rabbis did not impose as heavy a fine. With the animal, which the owner recovers and keeps, one might think the fine should simply be one unit more than the slave's — you lose ten times its value and on the last payment you also receive the animal back. Why jump to a hundred? The Gemara answers: *eved milta delo shechicha hi* — selling one's slave to a non-Jew is an uncommon occurrence, and *kol milta delo shechicha lo gazru bah rabanan* — where something is uncommon, the rabbis did not press as hard with their legislative response. Selling large animals to non-Jews, by contrast, was a frequent and prevalent transgression, and the rabbis therefore imposed a much heavier deterrent.

## Ownership Threshold

### Scriptural Basis

Having established that the Sages exempt an animal from *bechorah* whenever a non-Jew holds any co-ownership, the Gemara turns to the underlying dispute: both the Sages and Rabbi Yehuda derive their positions from the same verse. The relevant text is *hikdashti li kol bechor b'Yisrael* — "I have sanctified for Me all the firstborn in Israel." The word *Yisrael* emphasizes Jewish ownership, and the word *kol*, "all," becomes the crux of the debate.

### Yehuda vs Sages

The Sages reason as follows: the word *bechor* by itself, without any modifier, would already have implied that even partial ownership (*miktzat bechor*) suffices to create the obligation. Therefore, when the verse adds *kol* — "all" — it must be coming to require that *the entirety* of the *bechor* be owned by a Jew before the obligation attaches. Any non-Jewish co-ownership therefore exempts.

Rabbi Yehuda reasons differently: *bechor* by itself would have suggested that the entire animal must be Jewish-owned, since the word *bechor* in its natural sense denotes the full animal. Therefore, when the verse adds *kol*, it cannot be adding something already implied. Rather, *kol* here must mean *afillu kol shehu* — even the slightest portion. That is, even if only a small fraction is Jewish-owned, the obligation attaches.

An alternative formulation proposes that everyone agrees *bechor* on its own implies the majority. Then the debate centers on what *kol* adds: one opinion holds that *kol* comes to expand — meaning the *entirety* must be Jewish-owned — while the other holds that *kol* comes to reduce the threshold — even a minimal Jewish portion creates the obligation. Either way, the practical outcome of the dispute is the same.

### Rav Huna: Ear Test

The question then follows: according to the Sages, who hold that any non-Jewish co-ownership exempts, how minimal can that ownership be? Rav Huna says: *afilu ozno* — even an ear. Even if the non-Jew owns only something as seemingly trivial as the animal's ear, that suffices for exemption. The implicit qualification is that the non-Jewish share must be at least enough that, were the non-Jew to take his portion, the animal would be left with a *ba'al mum* — a blemish. But even an ear satisfies that threshold.

It is worth noting the apparent curiosity here: even if an animal were genuinely born with a severed ear, it would still be given to a *kohen* as a *bechor* — a *ba'al mum* is still a *bechor*, simply one that cannot be brought to the altar. The exemption in the co-ownership case therefore does not derive from the fact that the animal is missing a part; it derives from the fact of non-Jewish ownership. The significance of the "ear" threshold is simply that the ownership must be of some meaningful, non-trivial portion.

### Nachman Objection

Rav Nachman objects to Rav Huna's position. If the non-Jew owns only a specific, identifiable part of the animal — say, the ear — why can one not simply say to him: *shkol oznecha v'zil* — take your ear and go? The Jew would own the entire remainder of the animal, and that remainder would still be obligated in *bechorah*. Since the co-ownership is a specific anatomical portion that can in principle be separated, the Jew's remaining share ought still to be obligated. Rav Nachman's implicit conclusion is that so trivial a portion does not suffice for exemption, and this objection is left without a direct answer — signaling that in Rav Nachman's view, the "ear" standard does not hold.

### Chisda: Neveila

The Gemara now records two alternative positions. Rav Chisda holds: the non-Jew must own a part *she'oseh otah neveilah* — a part whose removal would render the animal *neveilah*. This means a vital organ whose removal would cause the animal's immediate death, or at minimum a part whose absence would make *shechitah* impossible, such as the trachea or esophagus. The reasoning is that only if the non-Jew owns a part so central to the animal's life — or its ability to be slaughtered — does his ownership rise to the level of genuine, significant co-ownership.

### Rava: Tereifa

Rava holds a more lenient standard: the non-Jew need only own a part *she'oseh otah tereifa* — a part whose removal would render the animal a *tereifah*, a fatally injured animal. This does not require the animal to die immediately, only that it sustain the kind of wound that would eventually be fatal.

### Tereifa Viability

The Gemara identifies the underlying disagreement between Rav Chisda and Rava as turning on the question of *tereifah chaya* — whether an animal with a *tereifah*-level injury can survive. According to Rava, *tereifah einah chaya* — such an animal will eventually die — and therefore if the non-Jew owns a part whose removal would cause a *tereifah*-level wound, he is a sufficiently significant owner that his co-ownership creates the exemption. According to Rav Chisda, *aval tereifah chaya* — an animal can survive such an injury — and therefore ownership of such a part is not considered sufficiently significant. Only ownership of a part whose removal would directly cause death or prevent *shechitah* altogether constitutes real co-ownership.

It bears emphasis that this debate applies specifically to the scenario where the non-Jew owns a *specific part* of the animal. Where the non-Jew simply owns a *percentage* of the whole animal — not any identified limb or organ, but a fractional interest in the entity as a whole — the discussion is largely uncontested: even a minute fractional co-ownership would seem to suffice.

### Reconciliation Attempt

Students in the academy attempted to reconcile the sweeping gap between Rav Huna's "ear" standard and the positions of Rav Chisda and Rava. Perhaps, they suggested, these camps do not actually disagree — *lav pligi* — and the different standards apply to different animals: the stringent standard of Rav Chisda and Rava may apply to the *bechor* offspring itself, while Rav Huna's lenient "ear" standard applies only to the ownership of the *mother* before birth. For the mother, even trivial co-ownership suffices to prevent the *bechorah* from taking effect.

### Pappa Refutation

Rav Pappa rejects this attempted reconciliation. By the offspring, if one claims that even an ear exempts, one must reckon with the verse's language: the Torah says *kol bechor* — the entirety of the *bechor* must be under Jewish ownership. If even an ear of the *bechor* is non-Jewishly owned, then by definition the *entirety* of the *bechor* is not Jewish-owned, and yet Rav Huna says it is exempt. But conversely, if one were to say that by the mother, the ear test applies — one can point to the verse *kol miknecha tizachar* — "all of your cattle, the males shall [be sacred]," where the word *kol* also appears. If *kol* means that even the slightest non-Jewish ownership of the mother exempts, then that logic applies equally to the offspring. There is no basis for splitting the two cases. Rav Huna's position therefore applies equally to both mother and offspring, and the attempted reconciliation fails.

This ruling is significant for practice: since we *pasken* essentially like Rav Huna, even a trivial ownership — such as selling the ear of the mother — suffices to exempt the offspring from *bechorah*, regardless of whether it is the mother or the *bechor* itself in question.

## Yochanan Rulings

### Slight Blemish Rule

Rabbi Elazar was once unable to attend the *beis medrash* and asked Rav Assi what had been taught there. Rav Assi reported two rulings in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. The first: *afilu mum kal* — even a slight blemish suffices to exempt an animal from the obligation of *bechorah*. This ruling essentially *paskens* like Rav Huna, with a clarifying qualification: the non-Jew's co-ownership must be of a portion that would leave the animal with at least some blemish — even a minor, permanent one. A hair or a toenail would not suffice, but anything that rises to the level of a recognized *mum*, however minor, does. This definitively sets aside the positions of Rav Chisda and Rava, which required ownership of a vital or life-threatening portion.

### Hybrid Offspring

Rabbi Yochanan's second ruling pertains to the *mishnah*'s teaching that a sheep which gives birth to an animal resembling a goat, or a goat that gives birth to an animal resembling a sheep, is exempt from *bechorah* since the offspring does not look like its mother. The *mishnah* adds, however, that if the offspring has *miktzat simanim* — some characteristics of its mother alongside characteristics of the other species — it is still *chayav*, still obligated as a *bechor*. Rabbi Yochanan adds his own *chiddush*: *mum kavua hu lishahet alav* — the hybrid nature of the animal itself constitutes a permanent blemish. It is still a *bechor*, it still has *kedushat bechor*, but its abnormal appearance is a *mum* — and therefore the *kohen* who receives it may slaughter and eat it without waiting for another blemish to develop.

### Abnormality Definition

The Gemara asks: is the novelty here really significant? Is it not already obvious from elsewhere that abnormality constitutes a *mum*? A *mishnah* already teaches that an animal whose mouth resembles that of a pig has a *mum*. Clearly something that looks like a different species is a blemish. So what did Rabbi Yochanan add?

The Gemara attempts a distinction. The case of the pig's mouth, one might argue, is understood as a blemish specifically because a pig is an animal that is *not* in the category of *bechorah* — it has a characteristic of a species entirely outside the *bechor* framework. But when a sheep has characteristics of a goat, and goats are also in the *bechorah* category, perhaps a mixed appearance between two *bechor*-eligible species is not considered a deformity at all. Rabbi Yochanan therefore comes to teach that even when the "foreign" characteristic belongs to a species also subject to *bechorah*, the abnormality relative to this animal's own species still constitutes a *mum*. This is the genuine *chiddush*.

### Animal vs Human Blemishes

A further difficulty arises from the parallel *mishnah* teaching about eye blemishes, which states that *eino achas gedolah ve'achas ketanah* — one eye significantly larger than the other — constitutes a *mum*. The Gemara analyzes what "significantly large or small" means: an eye as large as that of a calf, or as small as that of a goose. The case of the goose is understandable: a goose is not subject to *bechorah*, so an eye resembling a goose's eye represents a characteristic of a non-*bechor* species. But the case of the calf is more challenging: a calf *is* subject to *bechorah*. And yet, having one eye the size of a calf's eye is still classified as a *mum*. This confirms that even a characteristic drawn from a *bechor*-eligible species can constitute a blemish, vindicating Rabbi Yochanan's ruling.

But this seems to eliminate the need for Rabbi Yochanan altogether — the calf-eye case already established the principle. The Gemara therefore refines: perhaps the different-sized-eye case is not an instance of a general principle about "abnormality," but rather a specific, named *mum* known as *sarua* — disproportionate limbs. The Torah, in listing the blemishes that disqualify a *kohen* from serving in the *Beis HaMikdash*, mentions *sarua*, which is understood to refer to limbs of unequal size or proportion. Perhaps the only reason different-sized eyes constitute a *mum* in animals is that *sarua* is a named blemish — not because of any general principle that abnormality equals *mum*.

### Abnormality Principle

The Gemara finds support for this interpretation in a crucial distinction between the blemish rules for animals and those for *kohanim*. For *kohanim*, the *mishnah* adds a category beyond what applies to animals: *shtaim gedolot shtaim ketanot* — both eyes abnormally large or both eyes abnormally small. For human *kohanim*, this disqualifies; for animals, it does not. Now: if the operative principle were simply "abnormality," then both-eyes-big and both-eyes-small would also be abnormal for an animal, and should similarly constitute a *mum*. The fact that they do not suggests that the principle is not general abnormality, but the specific *mum* of *sarua* — unequal, mismatched limbs. Two eyes both too big or both too small are not mismatched; *sarua* requires *disparity*.

For *kohanim*, the verse *ish ish mizerah Aharon* — any man from the seed of Aaron — is interpreted to require that the *kohen* be *shaveh* in his characteristics to other members of Aaron's line; even symmetric abnormality therefore disqualifies him. For animals, absent that verse and absent the specific *mum* of *sarua*, two symmetrically abnormal eyes do not constitute a *mum*.

The Gemara resolves the tension by maintaining that abnormality *is* in principle a category of *mum*, but with an important qualification. When both eyes are excessively large, one might attribute this to unusual good health: *im machmat bri'uta yitarta, travaihu ba'u lemevei* — if the animal were exceptionally healthy, both eyes would naturally be large. When both are too small: *im machmat kachishuta yitarta, travaihu ba'u lemichshei* — if the animal were malnourished or weak, both eyes would naturally be small. In either case, the symmetry allows one to say that this is still within the natural variation of the species. Only when one eye is large and the other small can one no longer attribute the disproportion to any natural cause affecting the entire animal; only then is it unambiguously characteristic of a different species, and therefore a genuine *mum*. This is what Rabbi Yochanan's ruling establishes as a principle, and it is a real *chiddush*: having characteristics that are genuinely abnormal for one's species — and that cannot be explained by within-species natural variation — constitutes a *mum* even when those characteristics are drawn from another species that also falls within the *bechorah* category.

## Rav Mari Case

### Mari's Strategy

The Gemara now introduces the case of Rav Mari bar Rachel. He owned animals and employed the following strategy: *hava maknei le'udnaihu le'oved kochavim* — he sold the ear of each animal to a non-Jew. Yet notwithstanding this sale, *ve'asar lehu begiza va'avoda* — he treated the firstborn offspring as if they were *bechoros*, forbidding their shearing and use for labor, *veyahiv lehu lekhahanei* — and giving them to *kohanim*. In the end, *vechalya chayuta deRav Mari bar Rachel* — all of Rav Mari bar Rachel's animals died. He was punished.

### Protective Motive

The Gemara asks the obvious question: if he was prepared to treat the animals as *bechoros*, forbid shearing, and give them to *kohanim*, why did he bother selling the ear to the non-Jew at all? His answer, as the Gemara explains, was *dilma ati lidei takala* — he feared that the *kohanim* might not be careful. He was willing to honor the *kedushah* of the *bechor*, but he worried that once the animal was in a *kohen*'s hands, the *kohen* might use it improperly — shearing it, working it, or slaughtering it before a proper *mum* had appeared. By selling the ear to the non-Jew, Rav Mari ensured that if a *kohen* were to misuse the animal, the *kohen* would technically not be transgressing, since the animal was not fully a *bechor*. His intent was protective — a safeguard against inadvertent transgression by others.

### Divine Punishment

Why, then, was he punished? The Gemara explains: *mishum demapkei lehu mikedushatayhu* — because he was removing these animals from their *kedushah* entirely. The act of selling the ear to a non-Jew, even with protective intent, stripped the animals of their status as *bechoros* in a way that denied them the *kedushah* they were entitled to bear.

### Blemish vs Kedushah

But this raises a challenge. Rabbi Yehuda rules that a person may insert his hand into the womb of a pregnant cow before birth and create a blemish in the *bechor* while it is still in utero — before *kedushah* attaches — so that it will emerge as a *ba'al mum*, exempt from the altar. If that is permitted, how is Rav Mari's strategy any different? He too acted before the *bechor* was born, preventing the full *kedushah* from attaching. The Gemara answers with a crucial distinction: *hatam mikedushat mizbe'ach kamapkei lei, mikedushat kohen lo mapkei lei* — when one creates a *mum* in utero, one exempts the animal only from the *kedushah* of the altar; the animal is still a *bechor* with the sanctity appropriate to a *kohen*'s property. It still may not be shorn or worked; it is still given to the *kohen* and still carries the full status of a *bechor* in every sense except sacrificial eligibility. In Rav Mari's case, however, *afilu mikedushat kohen kamafkei lei* — the sale of the ear excluded the animal entirely, removing it from all dimensions of *bechorah*, including its status as the *kohen*'s sanctified property.

### Misinterpretation Effect

A second explanation for the punishment is offered: Rav Mari bar Rachel *yadav laknuyei kinyan gamur* — he knew how to execute a transaction that would be fully and legally binding. His sale of the ear to the non-Jew was technically valid. But *vechazu lei inshei achrinei ve'azlu ve'avdu* — others observed what he was doing and imitated him, without knowing how to perform the transaction properly. They did it as a verbal transfer or in some other legally insufficient manner, meaning they had not genuinely transferred ownership to the non-Jew, yet they proceeded to treat the *bechor* as exempt. Rav Mari saw that people were executing these transactions sloppily, *ve'asi bohu lidei takala* — and this was leading them to transgression: they thought they had avoided *bechorah* when they had not. Because his practice was the catalyst for widespread error, Rav Mari was held responsible.

## Modern Exemption

### Shulchan Aruch Rule

The story of Rav Mari bar Rachel and his punishment might seem to foreclose the strategy of selling a portion of the animal to a non-Jew as a means of avoiding *bechorah*. In striking contrast, the *Shulchan Aruch* rules precisely the opposite: *bazman hazeh mitzvah leshatef im akum kodem sheyeitzei la'avir ha'olam* — in our time it is a *mitzvah* to make a non-Jew a co-owner in the mother before the firstborn emerges into the world, *kedei lifto'o min habechorah* — in order to exempt it from *bechorah*. And this remains true, the *Shulchan Aruch* continues, *af al pi shemafki'a kedushato* — even though doing so prevents the animal from ever attaining *kedushat bechor*.

### Modern Rationale

The reasoning represents a deliberate inversion of what might otherwise be expected. Rav Mari was punished in a context where the preferred religious path was to allow the *bechor* to attain its *kedushah*, wait for a *mum* to develop, and then give it to a *kohen* who would slaughter and eat it with appropriate care. In that world, one had the halakhic knowledge to assess blemishes properly, and the religious framework was intact enough to handle the obligations correctly. Today, the risks run in the opposite direction: lack of expertise in evaluating *mumim*, unfamiliarity with the detailed laws of *bechor*, and the likelihood that animals will be used improperly all combine to make the *kedushah* a source of transgression rather than sanctity. Under these circumstances, preventing the *kedushah* from taking effect is not a circumvention of the *mitzvah* — it *is* the *mitzvah*. It is better, the *Shulchan Aruch* rules, to remove the animal from *bechorah* entirely than to risk widespread violations of its laws.

### Transaction Mechanics

Nonetheless, the story of Rav Mari bar Rachel leaves a critical lesson intact: the transaction must be done properly. Those who copied Rav Mari incorrectly without executing a legally binding sale were led into transgression precisely because they assumed their informal or defective transactions had achieved the desired legal result. This concern is directly parallel to the laws of selling *chametz* before Pesach: in both cases the question of what form of transaction constitutes a legally binding *kinyan* between a Jew and a non-Jew — sufficient to transfer actual ownership in a *halachically* effective manner — is a matter of considerable complexity. The responsa literature on both topics is extensive, and the pages of the *Shulchan Aruch* in this area are surrounded by commentary running to dense columns of small print, all devoted to working through the conditions under which such a sale achieves genuine and binding effect. It is through this mechanism — a properly executed sale of co-ownership to a non-Jew before the *bechor* is born — that the obligation of *bechor* is avoided in practice today.
