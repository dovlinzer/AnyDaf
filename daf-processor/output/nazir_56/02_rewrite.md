# Nazir 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nezir & Mikdash Entry

### Intro: Exceptions

The final section of this *perek* opens with a *mishnah* that draws a striking parallel between two distinct bodies of law: the rules governing when a *nazir* must shave his head as a result of *tumas hameis*, and the rules governing liability for entering the *Beit Hamikdash* in a state of *tumah*. Throughout the preceding *perek*, we encountered a series of cases in which a *nazir* who contracted a form of *tumas hameis* was nonetheless exempt from the obligation of *nezir megaleach* — cases such as the *metzora* and certain other categories where, despite the *tumah* being biblical in nature, the *mesorah* indicates that it does not trigger the shaving requirement or nullify the *nezirut*. The *mishnah* now establishes a general principle: these two domains — *nezir megaleach* and *biat mikdash* — track each other precisely.

The rule, stated by Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua, runs as follows: *kol tumah min hameis shehanazir megaleach aleha chayavim aleha al biat mikdash* — any *tumah* from the dead that causes a *nazir* to shave also renders one liable for entering the *Beit Hamikdash* while bearing that *tumah*. Conversely, *kol tumah min hameis she'ein hanazir megaleach aleha ein chayavim aleha al biat mikdash* — any *tumah* from the dead that does not obligate a *nazir* to shave similarly carries no liability for *biat mikdash*.

It is worth noting the practical scope of this rule. *Tumas meis* ordinarily permits entry into the *Machaneh Leviyah* and even into the *Ezrat Nashim*, right up to the *Azarah* itself. What is at stake here is therefore entry into the temple precinct proper. The *mishnah* thus tells us that those categories of *tumas meis* that are exceptional with respect to *nezir megaleach* are equally exceptional with respect to *biat mikdash* — they carry no liability even at the biblical level.

### Meir's Objection

Rabbi Meir registers a sharp objection: *lo tehei zu kallah min hasheretz* — surely this *tumah* should not be treated more leniently than touching a dead rodent. His point is intuitive. Even if a particular variety of *tumas meis* does not activate the *nazir*'s obligation to shave — perhaps because it is categorized as a lesser or anomalous form — it is still *tumas meis*. And any *tumah* whatsoever, even touching a *sheretz*, renders a person liable for entering the *Beit Hamikdash* in that state. The cases under discussion are not rabbinic enactments like *sechochot u'praot* or *Beit Hapras*, where no one would argue the point. These are assumed to be *d'oraita tumot*. How, then, can one say that a person bearing such *tumah* is exempt from liability for *biat mikdash*?

### Gemara Source

The *gemara* is not primarily interested in the substance of this dispute, but in the transmission of the tradition itself. It notes that the principle attributed in the *mishnah* to Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua — the *stam* Rabbi Yehoshua being Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya — was in fact received through an intermediate link: Rabbi Yehoshua bar Mama. A *beraita* fills in the background.

Rabbi Eliezer relates that he once traveled to Ardaskaya, where he encountered Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peter Rosh seated and presenting his analysis before Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peter Rosh articulated the same rule: *tumah* from the dead that causes a *nazir* to shave also entails liability for *biat mikdash*, and *tumah* that does not obligate shaving likewise entails no such liability. Rabbi Meir pushed back with precisely the argument recorded in the *mishnah* — that this *tumah* should at minimum be no lighter than *tumas sheretz*.

At that point, Rabbi Eliezer intervened, asking Rabbi Meir whether he was acquainted with the authority of Rabbi Yehoshua bar Mama. Rabbi Meir confirmed that he was. Rabbi Eliezer then reported: *kach amar li Rabbi Yehoshua bar Mama mishum Rabbi Yehoshua* — this is what Rabbi Yehoshua bar Mama told me in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua, that he had received the tradition *shamati* — I heard — that any *tumah* from the dead that obligates a *nazir* to shave also carries liability for *biat mikdash*, and any that does not obligate shaving carries no such liability. Rabbi Eliezer thereby backed the position of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peter Rosh, establishing that it was a received tradition from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya himself.

### Chain of Tradition

This background raises an obvious question: if Rabbi Eliezer's immediate source was Rabbi Yehoshua bar Mama, who in turn received it from Rabbi Yehoshua, why does our *mishnah* cite Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua directly, skipping the intermediate link?

The *gemara* derives from this a general principle about the reporting of traditions: *kol shma'teta d'mitamra b'bei tlata* — any tradition that was transmitted through three stages, *kadmaya uvatraya amrinan* — we cite the first and the last, *metzaya la amrinan* — but the middle link need not always be mentioned. It is not required to enumerate every step in the chain of transmission.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak illustrates this with a further example. Nachum HaLavlur declares his tradition going back *miRabbi Mayasha shekibel me'abba shekibel min hazugot shekiblu min hanevi'im* — from Rabbi Mayasha who received from his father who received from the *zugot* who received from the prophets — a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* concerning one who plants dill and mustard seed in two or three different locations in a field, that *pe'ah* is given from each separate location. What is instructive here is not the content of this *halacha* but the structure of its citation: it proceeds from Nachum HaLavlur all the way back to the prophets, yet it omits Yehoshua and the *zekenim* who, according to *Pirkei Avot*, stood between Moshe and the prophets in the chain of tradition. The *gemara* concludes *shema mina*: it is indeed not necessary to cite every intermediate link.

One might reasonably ask — and the *gemara* invites this observation — that Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua were in fact contemporaries, both teachers of Rabbi Akiva. How peculiar, then, that Rabbi Eliezer would receive this tradition three removes away rather than directly from his colleague. Perhaps this reflects circumstances surrounding Rabbi Eliezer's *cherem*, or some other reason they did not communicate directly on this matter. The question is a natural one, and it was noted.

---

The same *halacha* has a parallel formulation in *Masechet Smachot* that is considerably more relevant in practical terms: *kol tumah she'ein hanazir megaleach aleha ein kohein muzhar alav* — any *tumah* that does not obligate a *nazir* to shave likewise does not constitute a biblical violation for a *kohein*. This raises the broader question — touched upon throughout the *perek* — of whether certain anomalous categories of *tumas meis*, those associated with *cherev k'chalal* or *keilim hanoge'im b'meit*, also create exceptions in the laws of *tumas kohanim*.

## Logic vs. Tradition

### Akiva's Argument

The next *mishnah* introduces a fascinating exchange between Rabbi Akiva and his teacher Rabbi Eliezer, followed by a contrasting encounter with Rabbi Yehoshua. The discussion touches on a theme that runs through much of the *perek*: many of the laws of *nezirut* resist logical explanation and appear almost arbitrary. Rabbi Yochanan, as we have seen elsewhere, would often say simply *halacha hi b'Nazir* — it is a law that applies to the *nazir*, with no further derivation offered.

Rabbi Akiva opens by presenting an argument before Rabbi Eliezer. He begins with two established facts. First, a bone the size of a barley grain — *etzem k'se'orah* — does not cause *tumah* through *ohel*, i.e., being in the same enclosed space as a corpse. It lacks the minimum mass to produce *tumas ohel*. And yet, a *nazir* who touches or carries such a bone must shave and loses his accumulated *nezirut*. Second, a *revi'it* of blood — *revi'it dam* — does cause *tumas ohel* at the biblical level. A person who is in the same space as a *revi'it* of blood from a corpse becomes *tamei*.

From these two facts, Rabbi Akiva constructs a *kal vachomer*. If a tiny chip of bone that cannot even produce *tumas ohel* is nonetheless sufficient to obligate a *nazir* to shave when he touches or carries it, then certainly a *revi'it* of blood — which does produce *tumas ohel* — should at minimum obligate a *nazir* to shave when he touches or carries it. It is important to note that Rabbi Akiva carefully limits his argument to *maga* and *masa* — touch and carrying — not *ohel*, because the bone only causes liability through those two modes, and a *kal vachomer* cannot extend beyond its premise.

The practical implication is significant. The established rule requires a *chatzi log* of blood — half a *log* — before a *nazir* is obligated to shave due to blood. Rabbi Akiva argues this is too high a threshold, at least for touch and carrying. A *revi'it* of blood should suffice for those modes, given that a *revi'it* produces *tumas ohel* while the tiny bone does not produce *tumas ohel* at all.

### Eliezer Dismisses

Rabbi Eliezer's response is blunt: *ma zeh Akiva, ein danim kan mi-kal vachomer* — what are you doing, Akiva? There is no place for a *kal vachomer* here. This amounts to a flat dismissal: these laws are arbitrary, they do not respond to logic, and Rabbi Akiva's reasoning, however clever, is simply beside the point.

### Yehoshua Validates

Unsatisfied with this response, Rabbi Akiva brought his argument to his other teacher, Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua's reaction was strikingly different: *yafeh amarta* — you argued well. The argument is valid. And yet, he continued, *ela kach amru halacha* — nonetheless, this is the received tradition, and the tradition stands.

The difference in approach between the two teachers is pedagogically significant. Rabbi Eliezer simply rejected the argument without engaging it. Rabbi Yehoshua validated the argument intellectually while explaining why it ultimately cannot prevail: the domain in question is governed not by logical inference but by *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — a tradition transmitted from Sinai. A good argument does not override a received tradition; it simply illuminates why logic alone is insufficient here.

### Eliezer vs. Akiva

This exchange is not merely a disagreement about a specific *shiur* of blood. It reflects a deeper tension about the nature of *Torah she-ba'al peh*. Rabbi Eliezer, by his own example and by the famous characterization of him in rabbinic literature, never transmitted anything that he had not received from his teacher. His entire orientation is toward received tradition. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, represents the power of human intellect and logical derivation — the idea that law can and should be reasoned out from principles, not merely inherited.

This tension has an older precedent. When the question arose whether a *korban Pesach* overrides *Shabbat* when *Erev Pesach* falls on *Shabbat*, Hillel HaBavli spent the entire day presenting *kal vachomers* and analogies to the assembled authorities, and they would not accept his conclusion. It was only when he said *kach mikublani miShemaya v'Avtalyon* — I have received this as a tradition from Shemaya and Avtalyon — that they accepted the ruling. The implication is that tradition remains the ultimate authority, even when logic independently arrives at the same conclusion.

It is in this spirit that Rabbi Eliezer was more dismissive of Rabbi Akiva's *kal vachomer*. For Rabbi Eliezer, the very act of deploying a logical argument in a domain governed by tradition was a category error. Rabbi Yehoshua, perhaps more generous in temperament, acknowledged the logical force of the argument before setting it aside. And Rabbi Akiva — who elsewhere is identified as the one who first established that a *revi'it* of blood constitutes the minimum *shiur* for a *nazir* with respect to touch and carrying — evidently continued to press this position based on his *kal vachomer*, a position grounded in his distinctive approach to legal derivation.

## Halacha Exception

### Which is Exception

The *gemara* now raises a precise question: *ibaya lehu* — the following problem was raised. When Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua said that the *kal vachomer* is inapplicable because this domain is governed by *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, which rule exactly is the *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*? Is it the rule governing the bone — that even a tiny *etzem k'se'orah* is sufficient to obligate a *nazir* to shave through touch and carrying — or is it the rule governing the blood — that even a *revi'it* of blood causes *tumas ohel*?

This question is not as simple as it first appears. One might have assumed that anything constituting *tumas meis* would obviously obligate a *nazir* to shave, and that the exceptions are what require *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. But as *Tosafot* notes, the *pesukim* concerning the *nazir* emphasize *tumas ohel*: *al kol nafshos meis lo yavo* — he shall not come near any dead person. The straightforward reading might suggest that a *nazir* is only obligated when exposed to something that produces *tumas ohel*, or at minimum to something powerful enough to produce that category of *tumah*. If so, the rule that even a tiny chip of bone — which lacks the mass for *tumas ohel* — suffices to obligate a *nazir* to shave is itself a *chiddush*, a departure from the plain meaning of the *pesukim*. That departure could be the *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*.

Alternatively, the *chiddush* could be on the side of the blood. The *pesukim* concerning the *kohein* use the phrase *al kol nafshos meis* as well, and *Tosafot* suggests that reading *nafshos* in the plural implies the minimum blood of two people, which would amount to a *chatzi log*. On that reading, the *pesukim* would support requiring a *chatzi log* of blood to produce *tumas ohel*, making the rule that a *revi'it* is sufficient the *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — a transmitted tradition that goes beyond what the *pesukim* alone would support. If so, it is the *revi'it* of blood that is the anomalous exception, and Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua were telling Rabbi Akiva: do not build a *kal vachomer* beginning from the *revi'it*, because the *revi'it* itself is the exception.

### Beraita Answer

The *gemara* resolves this through a *beraita* that states the matter explicitly: *etzem k'se'orah halacha v'revi'it dam kal vachomer* — the rule governing the tiny bone is the *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, while the rule that a *revi'it* of blood causes *tumas ohel* is derived through *kal vachomer*. In other words, the anomaly is on the side of the bone; the *revi'it* of blood is the logically derived rule, and the bone is the exceptional tradition.

### No KV from Exception

This immediately clarifies why Rabbi Akiva's *kal vachomer* cannot stand. He sought to argue from the bone — which obligates a *nazir* to shave through touch and carrying despite producing no *tumas ohel* — to the *revi'it* of blood, which does produce *tumas ohel*. But the bone is itself an exception, a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* that departs from what logic would dictate. *Ein danim kal vachomer me-halacha* — one cannot build a *kal vachomer* from a premise that is itself a received anomaly. A *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* is *sui generis*; it stands on its own and is not meant to serve as a logical premise from which further laws are inferred. The bone's rule is true, but it tells us nothing about what the law should be elsewhere, precisely because it exists outside the domain of rational derivation.

This raises a broader conceptual point. One might have thought that a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, being the most authoritative form of tradition, would constitute the strongest possible foundation for a *kal vachomer*. In fact, the opposite is the case. The force of logical inference depends on its premises being part of a coherent, rationally accessible system. A *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* that represents an exception to what logic would otherwise dictate is precisely not such a premise; it is a standalone received tradition, valid within its own scope, but incapable of generating further inferences.

## Two Nzirim Safek

### Mishnah Setup

We now turn to an entirely different topic, though one that touches on realities of *tumas meis* in a very concrete way. The *mishnah* presents the following scenario: *shnei nezirim* — two *nezirim* — to whom one person says: *ra'isi echad mikem she-nitma* — I saw one of you become *tamei*, perhaps by entering an area with a grave, *v'eini yodeia eizeh mikem* — but I do not know which of you it was. Each *nazir* is now a *safek nazir tamei*. Neither knows with certainty whether he is the one who contracted *tumas meis*.

This creates a complex halachic predicament. A *nazir tamei* must go through the process of bringing a *korban tumah* and then counting new days of *nezirut*, culminating in the *korban tahara*. But one cannot simply bring a *korban* if one is not certain one is obligated. Nor can one simply shave without being certain of one's status. The solution for a single individual in this situation would involve waiting out the minimum period of *nezirut* — thirty days — then shaving and bringing minimal *korbanot* with appropriate stipulations. The *olah*, for instance, could be brought with the condition that if one is obligated as a *nazir tamei* it counts as the obligatory *olah*, and if not it is a voluntary *olat nedavah*. The *chatat ha'of* — a bird offering — may be brought even in a state of doubt, this being a special *halacha*. The *asham*, however, cannot be brought conditionally or on the basis of doubt. So one never fully satisfies that component of the *korban tumah* in a state of *safek*.

## Two Nzirim Safek…

### Safek Tumah Rules

Before addressing the practical solution for the two *nezirim*, the *gemara* raises a fundamental question: why are they *safek tamei* at all?

The principle governing doubtful *tumah* is derived from the laws of *sotah*. The Torah describes the *sotah* as having gone into seclusion — *venistara* — in a private space. From this, the *gemara* derives the general principle: *safek tumah birshut hayachid sefeko tamei* — doubtful *tumah* that occurred in a private domain is treated as *tamei*, while *safek tumah birshut harabim sefeko tahor* — doubtful *tumah* in a public domain is treated as *tahor*. This applies even at the biblical level, *d'oraita*.

### Domain Question

The *gemara* notes, however, that the analogy to *sotah* requires that only two people be present in the private space — the woman and the man with whom she secluded herself. The rule of *safek tumah birshut hayachid* applies when there were only two people in the relevant space, paralleling the *sotah* scenario. Here, however, there are three people: the two *nezirim* and the witness who observed them. With three people present, this should constitute a *reshut harabim*, and *safek tumah birshut harabim sefeko tahor*. If so, both *nezirim* should simply be presumed *tahor* — yet the *mishnah* treats them as *safek tamei* and requires them to act accordingly.

### Reshut Hayachid

The *gemara* resolves this through Rabbah bar bar Chana, who explains that the case involves a witness who saw the two *nezirim* from a distance — *be'omer ra'isi tumah shenizreka beineichem* — who observed from afar and could not make out which of them was involved. The witness was not physically present in the same space as the two *nezirim*; he observed from outside. The space the two *nezirim* occupied was therefore a *reshut hayachid*, and the witness's presence at a distance does not convert it into a *reshut harabim*.

Rav Ashi adds that this reading is supported by the language of the *mishnah* itself: *v'eini yodeia eizeh mikem* — I do not know which of you — implies not that the witness forgot, but that he never knew, which fits precisely the scenario of someone watching at a distance who could not identify which individual was which.

Importantly, throughout this entire analysis the *gemara* assumes that the *safek* retains its status as a genuine doubt, treating it as *safek tamei* rather than *vadai tamei*. *Tosafot* raises the point that when we say *safek tumah birshut hayachid* is *tamei*, the normal rule is that we treat the person as *vadai tamei*, not merely as *safek tamei* — mirroring the *sotah*, where the presumption becomes essentially a certainty for legal purposes. However, since it is impossible for both *nezirim* to be *vadai tamei* — only one of them actually became *tamei* — the *gemara* here retains the language and substance of *safek*, operating within a framework where both individuals carry genuine uncertainty.

### Paired Offerings

The *mishnah*'s solution for the two *nezirim* is elegant. Between the two of them, there must be one *korban tumah* and one *korban tahara*. Each *nazir* shaves and they bring together both a complete *korban tumah* and a complete *korban tahara*. The stipulation each makes is: *im ani hu hatamei korban tumah sheli v'korban tahara shelcha* — if I am the one who was *tamei*, the *korban tumah* is mine and the *korban tahara* is yours; *v'im ani hu hatahor korban tahara sheli v'korban tumah shelcha* — and if I am the one who was *tahor*, the *korban tahara* is mine and the *korban tumah* is yours.

After the first thirty days, the *nezirim* count an additional thirty days. At the end of those sixty days, one of them has definitively completed a full *nezirut taharah* following an episode of *tumah*, and the other has completed a standard *nezirut taharah*. Each brings a complete set of *korbanot*, again with appropriate stipulation: the *korban* of the second thirty days belongs to whichever of them was indeed the one who was *tamei* and has now completed his *taharah*. The attribution flows to the person for whom it is obligatory.

### Two-Person Advantage

The advantage of having two *nezirim* in this situation rather than one is substantial. When a single *nazir* faces this uncertainty alone, he is limited to bringing minimal *korbanot* — primarily an *olah* on a conditional basis — because he cannot bring the full *korban tumah* when he does not know himself to be obligated. He can never bring the *asham* at all. With two *nezirim*, however, the situation is different: since one of them is definitively obligated in the *korban tumah* and the other is definitively obligated in the *korban tahara*, both complete sets may be brought without any conditional framing. The only stipulation required is to direct each set toward the person for whom it applies. *Tosafot* notes that in this configuration the stipulation is almost formulaic — it is simply an attribution, not a genuine conditional offering. No *olat nedavah* is necessary, no incomplete *korban* is brought; each person brings a full set and declares to whom it belongs.

## Double Shaving

### Hakafah Problem

Having described the practical solution for the two *nezirim*, the *gemara* identifies a difficulty that had been lurking beneath the surface. The *mishnah* envisions each *nazir* shaving twice: once at the end of the first thirty-day period and once at the end of the second. But if one of them was in fact *tahor* all along, then his second shaving — undertaken so that he can fulfill the *mitzvah* of *nazir megaleach* in the event that he was *tamei* — is a shaving that he does not need and that he may not need halachically to perform. And the act of shaving all the hair of one's head implicates the prohibition of *hakafat rosh* — the prohibition of *lo takifu*, shaving the sideburns in a manner that makes a circuit of the head.

The *gemara* is thus asking: even granting that the shaving itself is not *me'akev* — that *nezirut* is formally complete without it — we still have to account for the fact that we are permitting a *nazir* to shave in circumstances where the shaving may constitute a violation of *hakafat rosh*. Why do we permit that?

### Shmuel: Woman/Minor

Shmuel answers that the *mishnah* is speaking of a woman or a minor. A woman is excluded from the prohibition of *lo takifu* because the prohibition against shaving the beard — *lo yashchitu pe'at zekanam* — does not apply to women, who ordinarily have no beard. And since there is an assumption that whoever falls outside the prohibition of shaving the beard also falls outside the prohibition of shaving the sideburns, a woman is entirely exempt from *hakafat rosh*. A minor likewise falls outside these prohibitions in a way that eliminates the concern. Therefore the *mishnah* is addressing a case where the two *nezirim* are women, or one of them is a minor, such that the double shaving creates no violation.

### Inference: Hakafah

The *gemara* immediately draws an inference from Shmuel's answer. One might have suggested a far simpler resolution to the problem: leave the *nezirim* as adult males and simply hold that *hakafat kol harosh lo shemah hakafah* — that shaving all the hair of the head does not constitute the prohibited form of *hakafah*. The prohibition of *lo takifu*, on this reading, refers specifically to making a defined perimeter cut around the head, leaving a circle of hair — like shaving around a circuit so that the remaining hair describes a line. When one shaves the entire head smooth, there is no circling, no defined sideburn — there is simply baldness. Perhaps that does not implicate the prohibition at all.

Had Shmuel accepted this position, he could have resolved the *gemara*'s difficulty without invoking the special status of women and minors. He did not. *Midela mokim lah hachi* — since he does not explain it this way — *shema mina kasavar Shmuel hakafat kol harosh shemah hakafah* — we infer that Shmuel holds that shaving all the hair of the head does constitute the prohibited *hakafah*. This is a clear inference. For Shmuel, an adult male who shaves his entire head violates the prohibition of *lo takifu* just as surely as one who makes a circular perimeter cut. The only way to permit the double shaving of the *mishnah* is therefore to situate the case among those who are outside the prohibition entirely — women and minors.

## Shaver/Shavee

### Huna: Shaver Independent

The *gemara* then cites a further teaching: *amar Rav Huna hamakif et hakatan harei hu chayav* — Rav Huna rules that one who shaves a minor is liable. This challenges the apparent implication of Shmuel's answer, which seemed to suggest that when the *shavee* — the person whose hair is being cut — is a minor, the act is entirely permitted. Rav Huna disagrees, or at least introduces a critical distinction.

The underlying conceptual question is how to read the *Torah*'s formulation of the prohibition: *lo takifu pe'at roshchem*. To whom does the *Torah* speak? *Pe'at roshchem* — the sideburns of your head — seems addressed to the person whose sideburns are being cut, i.e., the *shavee*. But *lo takifu* — do not make a circuit — seems addressed to the one who performs the cutting, i.e., the *shaver*. Are these two aspects of a single integrated prohibition, or are they independent obligations that can be separated?

Rav Huna holds that the *shaver* bears an independent obligation. If the *shaver* is an adult male, he is liable regardless of who the *shavee* is. Even if the *shavee* is a minor — who is himself outside the prohibition — the adult male performing the shaving violates the prohibition from the *shaver*'s side.

### Adda Challenges

Rav Adda bar Ahava challenged Rav Huna directly: *vedidach man megaleach lehu* — your own children, who do you have shaving them? Presumably Rav Huna's children had their sideburns cut, yet Rav Huna had just ruled that an adult male shaver is liable regardless of the shavee's status. If an adult male shaved Rav Huna's children, that adult male would be violating the prohibition. Who, then, was doing the cutting?

### Huna Response

Rav Huna answered simply: *Chovah* — his wife. Since a woman is exempt from the prohibition — as Shmuel established, and as the logic of the *Torah*'s formulation confirms — a woman acting as the *shaver* violates nothing. The shavee is a minor, and the shaver is a woman. Neither occupies the position of an adult male in the chain of the act.

This generates a coherent model. The prohibition operates along two independent axes: the *shaver* axis and the *shavee* axis. An adult male cannot occupy either position without violating the prohibition — he cannot be the one who cuts and he cannot be the one whose sideburns are cut. But women and minors are outside the prohibition on both axes. A woman can serve as the *shaver*, and a minor can serve as the *shavee*, without creating any violation. The only constraint is that an adult male must not appear in either role.

### The Curse

The exchange, however, did not end peacefully. Rav Adda bar Ahava responded to the name *Chovah* — Rav Huna's wife — by invoking it ominously: *tekavrinehu Chovah livneih* — may Chovah bury her children. Whether this was intended as a genuine curse or as an exclamation of disbelief — the *Rosh* understands it as rhetorical, something like "does she want to bury her children? How can you allow this?" — the words carried consequence. The *gemara* records that for all the years of Rav Adda bar Ahava's life, Rav Huna had no further children. The *gemara* does not say that Chovah had no further children — pointedly, it focuses the consequence on Rav Huna himself. The power of such utterances, even when not intended as a curse, appears repeatedly in the *gemara*, and this episode is a striking illustration.

### Huna's Model

To summarize Rav Huna's position as it emerges from this discussion: the prohibition of *lo takifu* operates independently on the *shaver* and the *shavee*. An adult male cannot serve as the *shaver* — even if the *shavee* is a minor or a woman — and an adult male cannot serve as the *shavee* — even if the *shaver* is a woman or a minor. Women and minors, however, may occupy either role freely. Thus a woman shaving a minor creates no violation on either side, and Rav Huna's own practice — having his wife Chovah cut his children's sideburns — was entirely consistent with his ruling.
