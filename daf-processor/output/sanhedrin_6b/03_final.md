# Sanhedrin 6b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Peshara & Kinyan

### Rav Ashi: No Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ פְּשָׁרָה אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה קִנְיָן, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ צְרִיכָה קִנְיָן, לְמָר דְּאָמַר צְרִיכָה – תְּלָתָא לְמָה לִי? תִּסְגֵּי בִּתְרֵי וְלִיקְנֵי מִינֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: Learn from** that discussion that **compromise does not require** a formal act of **acquisition,** which would legally transfer rights to the settlement payment to the other party. **As if it enters your mind** that compromise **requires** an act of **acquisition** to finalize its terms, **according to the one who says** it **requires** an act of acquisition, **why do I** need a formal court of **three** judges? **Let it suffice with two** judges, **and let** one litigant **perform an act of acquisition with** the other litigant to signify their commitment to abide by the compromise.

The daf opens mid-discussion, picking up from the debate over *peshara* versus *din* — whether compromise, understood as binding arbitration, constitutes a form of judicial rendering or merely a contractual arrangement. That underlying question determines whether *peshara* requires three judges or one. It is in this context that Rav Ashi offers his inference: *shema mina peshara eina tzricha kinyan* — from the very fact that there is a debate between one and three judges, we may conclude that *peshara* does not require a *kinyan*.

The logic runs as follows. Were *peshara* to require a *kinyan* — the formal act of acquisition by which one obligates oneself to a decision — then the entire debate over requiring three judges would collapse. Once a *kinyan* is performed, the parties have already contractually bound themselves to accept whatever the arbitrator decides. In that case, even two witnesses should suffice to validate the arrangement, and no one would argue that three judges are necessary. The fact that an opinion exists requiring three demonstrates that we are not operating within a purely contractual framework; something more judicial is at play.

### Hilcheta: Kinyan Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: פְּשָׁרָה צְרִיכָה קִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **But** nevertheless, **the *halakha*** is that **a compromise requires** an act of **acquisition** to finalize its terms.

The *Gemara* then reaches a surprising conclusion. Despite the apparent force of Rav Ashi's inference, the *halacha* is stated plainly: *v'hilcheta peshara tzricha kinyan* — *peshara* does in fact require a *kinyan*. The question then reasserts itself: if a *kinyan* is performed, why would anyone still require three judges?

Tosafot addresses this directly. The requirement for three judges persists even when a *kinyan* is performed because a *kinyan* made before an illegitimate panel would be tantamount to a *kinyan* made in error. The parties are not agreeing to submit themselves to an arbitrary result; they are committing themselves to a genuine and responsible *peshara*. Anything less than three judges risks producing a decision that lacks the requisite legitimacy, and therefore the agreement itself would be built on a flawed foundation. Hence, three judges remain necessary even in the presence of a *kinyan* — not to enforce the contractual obligation, but to ensure that what the parties are obligating themselves to constitutes a real and defensible form of compromise.

## Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

### Baraita: 3 Judges, No Po…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַדִּין בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, כָּךְ בִּיצּוּעַ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה. נִגְמַר הַדִּין, אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לִבְצוֹעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Just as judgment** is performed **by three** judges, **so** too, **mediation** is performed **by three** judges. Once **the** verdict of the **judgment has been issued,** it **is not permitted** for **you to mediate** a dispute.

A *baraita* is introduced: just as *din* requires three judges, so too *bitzua* — compromise — requires three. This confirms that *peshara* is understood, at least procedurally, as a form of adjudication rather than a purely private arrangement. The *baraita* further establishes that once *gmar din* — the finalized judgment — has been pronounced, *bitzua* is no longer permitted.

### R. Eliezer: Assur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סרמ״ש בנק״ש סִימָן. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אָסוּר לִבְצוֹעַ, וְכׇל הַבּוֹצֵעַ – הֲרֵי זֶה חוֹטֵא, וְכׇל הַמְבָרֵךְ אֶת הַבּוֹצֵעַ – הֲרֵי זֶה מְנָאֵץ. וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״בֹּצֵעַ בֵּרֵךְ נִאֵץ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, says:** It is **prohibited to mediate** a dispute; **and anyone who mediates [*habotze'a*]** a dispute **is a sinner; and anyone who blesses the mediator is cursing** God. **And of this, it is stated:** "And **the covetous [*botze'a*] blesses himself, though he despises the Lord"** (Psalms 10:3).

Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yose HaGalili goes further. In his view, *assur livtzoa* — it is forbidden to compromise — and one who does so is a sinner, perverting the truth. More strikingly, *kol hamevarech et habotzea harei zeh menaetz* — anyone who praises the one who arranged the compromise is as one who blasphemes God. The verse invoked is *botzea berech ni'etz Hashem* — the one who blesses the *botzea* blasphemes God — which the *Gemara* reads not in its plain sense of ill-gotten gain but as a condemnation of judicial compromise. The imperative that follows is *yikov hadin et hahar* — let the law pierce the mountain. No obstacle is large enough to justify bending the truth of justice. The basis is *ki hamishpat lElokim hu* — the judgment belongs to God, and to distort it is to act against God Himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא יִקּוֹב הַדִּין אֶת הָהָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַמִּשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהִים הוּא״. וְכֵן מֹשֶׁה הָיָה אוֹמֵר: יִקּוֹב הַדִּין אֶת הָהָר. אֲבָל אַהֲרֹן אוֹהֵב שָׁלוֹם וְרוֹדֵף שָׁלוֹם, וּמֵשִׂים שָׁלוֹם בֵּין אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תּוֹרַת אֱמֶת הָיְתָה בְּפִיהוּ וְעַוְלָה לֹא נִמְצָא בִשְׂפָתָיו בְּשָׁלוֹם וּבְמִישׁוֹר הָלַךְ אִתִּי וְרַבִּים הֵשִׁיב מֵעָוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the judge must assure that **the** true **judgment will** prevail at all costs and metaphorically **pierce the mountain, as it is stated: "For the judgment is God's"** (Deuteronomy 1:17). **And similarly, Moses would say: Let the judgment pierce the mountain. But** by contrast, **Aaron,** whose role was not that of a judge, was **a lover of peace and a pursuer of peace, and** he **would apply peace between** one **person and the other, as it is stated: "The law of truth was in his mouth, and unrighteousness was not found in his lips; he walked with Me in peace and uprightness, and turned many away from iniquity"** (Malachi 2:6).

This position is reinforced by contrasting Moshe and Aharon. Moshe is associated with *yikov hadin et hahar* — the unbending administration of divine law. Aharon, by contrast, is described as *ohev shalom verodef shalom*, one who loved peace and pursued it, making peace between people, with truth on his lips and uprightness in his conduct. The verse cited is from the passage beginning *ki siftei kohen yishmeru da'at*, affirming Aharon's prophetic role.

One might ask: if compromise is *assur*, how is Aharon's conduct praiseworthy? The very qualities attributed to Aharon — making peace, speaking truth — sound like exactly the behavior Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yose HaGalili condemns.

### Rashi: Before Court

The prohibition of *livtzoa*, in Rashi's reading, applies specifically *mi sheba ledin* — once the parties have come before the court seeking judgment. At that point, the judges are obligated to administer justice and may not redirect the proceedings toward compromise. Aharon's conduct was entirely different: he pursued peace *before* disputes ever came before him as a judge, *kodem sheyavo'u lefanav ledin*. When he heard of a conflict, he would run after the parties and establish peace between them before they reached the stage of formal litigation. This is the essential distinction — Aharon's peacemaking was pre-litigation, and it is precisely that which is praiseworthy. Once the parties stand before the court, a different standard applies.

### Stolen Goods & Judah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי שֶׁגָּזַל סְאָה שֶׁל חִטִּים, וּטְחָנָהּ וַאֲפָאָהּ וְהִפְרִישׁ מִמֶּנָּה חַלָּה, כֵּיצַד מְבָרֵךְ? אֵין זֶה מְבָרֵךְ אֶלָּא מְנָאֵץ. וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״וּבֹצֵעַ בֵּרֵךְ נִאֵץ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If **one stole a *se'a* of wheat and ground it and baked it and separated *ḥalla* from it,** i.e., separated the portion of the dough that must be given to the priests, **how can he** possibly **recite the blessing** on the mitzva of *ḥalla*? **He is not blessing; rather,** he is **cursing** God. **And of this** offense **it is stated: "And the covetous [*uvotze'a*] blesses himself, though he despises the Lord,"** interpreted homiletically as: And whoever blesses upon breaking [*botze'a*] the bread despises the Lord.

The *Gemara* then digresses briefly from the main discussion. A teaching of Rabbi Eliezer considers the case of one who stole grain, ground it, baked it, and then separated *challah* from the loaf. How can such a person recite a blessing? The answer is that he cannot — *ein zeh mevarech ela menaetz*, it is not a blessing but a blasphemy, a case of *mitzvah haba'ah be'aveirah*, the performance of a commandment through sin. The verse *uvotzea berech ni'etz Hashem* is again invoked, here reading *botzea* in its literal sense of one who breaks bread.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֱמַר ״בּוֹצֵעַ״ אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד יְהוּדָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוּדָה אֶל אֶחָיו מַה בֶּצַע כִּי נַהֲרֹג אֶת אָחִינוּ״. וְכׇל הַמְבָרֵךְ אֶת יְהוּדָה – הֲרֵי זֶה מְנָאֵץ, וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״וּבֹצֵעַ בֵּרֵךְ נִאֵץ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says:** The term ***botze'a*** employed in that verse **was stated only with regard to Judah, as it is stated: "And Judah said to his brothers: What profit [*betza*] is it if we slay our brother** and conceal his blood? Come, and let us sell him to the Ishmaelites" (Genesis 37:26–27). **And** consequently, **anyone who blesses Judah** for this act **is cursing** God, **and of this it is stated: "And the covetous [*uvotze'a*] blesses himself, though he despises the Lord,"** interpreted homiletically as: "And whoever blesses the profiteer [*botze'a*] despises the Lord."

Rabbi Meir then offers another reading of the verse, applying *botzea* specifically to Yehuda's proposal regarding Yosef. When Yehuda said to his brothers *mah betza ki naharog et achinu*, "what profit is it that we kill our brother?" — he was not the hero some might make him. Yehuda had it in his power to call for Yosef's release and return to their father. Instead he settled for a half-measure, selling him to the Ishmaelites. Rabbi Meir insists that praising Yehuda for this falls into the same category of *botzea berech ni'etz Hashem* — one who blesses a compromise that fell far short of what was possible is blaspheming. The comparison is pointed: a position of moral authority carries with it the obligation to act on it fully, not to negotiate it away.

### R. Yehoshua: Mitzva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: מִצְוָה לִבְצוֹעַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱמֶת וּמִשְׁפַּט שָׁלוֹם שִׁפְטוּ בְּשַׁעֲרֵיכֶם״. וַהֲלֹא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מִשְׁפָּט – אֵין שָׁלוֹם, וּבִמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁלוֹם – אֵין מִשְׁפָּט? אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ מִשְׁפָּט שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שָׁלוֹם? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה בִּיצּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** It is **a mitzva to mediate** a dispute, **as it is stated: "Execute the judgment of truth and peace in your gates"** (Zechariah 8:16). **Is it not that in the place where there is** strict **judgment there is no** true **peace, and in a place where there is** true **peace, there is no** strict **judgment? Rather, which is the judgment that has peace within it? You must say: This is mediation,** as both sides are satisfied with the result.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha takes the opposite position. Not only is *peshara* permitted even after the parties have come before the court, but *mitzva livtzoa* — it is a positive obligation to seek compromise. His proof is the verse *emet umishpat shalom shiftu beshaareichem* — judge truth and a judgment of peace in your gates. The apparent tension between *mishpat* and *shalom* — between strict law and peace — is itself the key. If you apply strict law, you do not necessarily achieve peace; if you pursue peace, you may compromise justice. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha resolves this by saying that *bitzua* is precisely the form of judgment that contains peace within it. It is not a departure from justice but a form of it — a *din berachamim*, judgment infused with compassion — which is why it properly requires three judges just as *din* does.

### David's Tzedakah Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּדָוִד הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיְהִי דָוִד עֹשֶׂה מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה״. וַהֲלֹא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מִשְׁפָּט – אֵין צְדָקָה, וּצְדָקָה – אֵין מִשְׁפָּט? אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ מִשְׁפָּט שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ צְדָקָה? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה בִּיצּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, with regard to David, it says: "And David executed justice and charity** to all his people" (II Samuel 8:15). **And is it not** that **wherever there is** strict **justice, there is no charity, and** wherever there is **charity, there is no** strict **justice? Rather, which is the justice that has within it charity? You must say: This is mediation.**

The *Gemara* cites the verse describing David: *vayehi David oseh mishpat utzedakah lechol ammo* — David performed *mishpat* and *tzedakah* for all his people. The parallel tension arises: where there is *mishpat*, how can there be *tzedakah* in the charitable sense, and vice versa?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָאן לְתַנָּא קַמָּא. דָּן אֶת הַדִּין, זִיכָּה אֶת הַזַּכַּאי וְחִיֵּיב אֶת הַחַיָּיב. וְרָאָה שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב עָנִי מָמוֹן, וְשִׁלֵּם לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – זֶה מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an alternative interpretation of David's method of judgment, in which **we come to** the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** i.e., Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says that it is prohibited to mediate a dispute: If a judge **adjudicated** a case of monetary **law,** and he correctly **exonerated the** party who was **exempt** from payment **and deemed liable the** party who was **liable** to pay, if he then **saw that** due to his ruling **a poor person became liable** to pay an amount of **money** that is beyond his means **and** therefore the judge himself **paid for him from his** own **house, this is justice and** also **charity.**

One reading would see this as analogous to the *mishpat shalom* formulation — a judgment that incorporates *tzedakah*, understood as *chesed* and contextual sensitivity. The *Gemara*, however, reads *tzedakah* more literally, in the rabbinic sense of actual charity, and aligns the verse with the position of *assur livtzoa*: David would render strict justice, and then, if the poor party suffered financially as a result, David would personally compensate him from his own resources. The judgment was pure *mishpat*, and the subsequent payment from his own pocket was the *tzedakah*. These are two distinct acts, not one integrated ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה: מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה – שֶׁהֶחְזִיר לוֹ מָמוֹן, וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה – שֶׁשִּׁילֵּם לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ. וְכֵן בְּדָוִד הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיְהִי דָוִד עֹשֶׂה מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה לְכׇל עַמּוֹ״. מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה – שֶׁהֶחְזִיר לוֹ אֶת מָמוֹנוֹ, וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה – שֶׁשִּׁילֵּם לוֹ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: It is **justice for this** one **and charity for that** one: It is **justice for this** one, **because** the judge **restored** his **money to him; and** it is **charity for that** poor person, **because** the judge **paid for him from his** own **house. And similarly, with regard to David, it says: "And David executed justice and charity to all his people"** (II Samuel 8:15). He executed **justice for this** one, **because** he **restored his money to him, and charity for that** one, **because** he **paid for him from his** own **house.**

The *Gemara* makes this explicit: *dan et hadin, zikah et hazakai, vechiyav et hachayav* — he rendered the judgment, acquitting the innocent and obligating the liable — *vera'ah shenitchayev ani mamon, veshilam lo mitoch beito* — and when he saw that a poor person was financially ruined by the outcome, he paid him from his own household. *Zehu mishpat utzedakah* — that is both *mishpat* and *tzedakah*: *mishpat* for the winning party who received justice, and *tzedakah* for the poor party who received personal assistance from the judge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי: הַאי ״לְכׇל עַמּוֹ״ – לַעֲנִיִּים מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא שִׁילֵּם מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, זֶהוּ מִשְׁפָּט וּצְדָקָה. מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה: מִשְׁפָּט לָזֶה – שֶׁהֶחְזִיר לוֹ מָמוֹנוֹ, וּצְדָקָה לָזֶה – שֶׁהוֹצִיא גְּזֵילָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This interpretation of the verse is **difficult for Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. If the word "charity" is meant to demonstrate that David supported the poor defendants, **this** term: **"To all his people,"** is incorrect. If the interpretation is correct, **it should have** stated: Charity **to the poor people. Rather, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Although** he **did not pay from his** own **house, it is** still **justice and charity.** It is **justice for this** one **and charity for that** one. It is **justice for this** one, **because** the judge **restored his money to him, and charity for that** one, **because** the judge **removed** the **stolen item from his possession.** By adjudicating the case correctly and compelling the liable party to pay his debt, the judge thereby ensures that the liable party does not illegitimately maintain property to which he is not entitled.

A difficulty is then raised: if the *tzedakah* component only applied when the losing party happened to be poor, how does the verse say David acted this way *lechol ammo* — for all his people? In response, Rebbi offers a reading that does not require the losing party to be impoverished. Even without any financial payment from the judge's pocket, the outcome itself constitutes both *mishpat* and *tzedakah*: *mishpat* for the winning party, who had his property restored, and *tzedakah* for the losing party — *she'hotzi gezeila mitachat yado* — because the judgment removed from his hands money or property that was not rightfully his. Being compelled to return what one wrongfully holds is itself a form of *tzedakah*, a benefit to the one who is corrected, since he is no longer in possession of ill-gotten goods. In this way, every litigant receives *mishpat* and *tzedakah*, which accounts for *lechol ammo*.

### R. Shimon: Before Clarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁבָּאוּ לְפָנֶיךָ לַדִּין, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן, אוֹ מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאִי אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן דִּין נוֹטֶה – אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶן: ״צְאוּ וּבִצְעוּ״. מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן הַדִּין נוֹטֶה – אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶן: ״צְאוּ וּבִצְעוּ״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן וְלִפְנֵי הִתְגַּלַּע הָרִיב נְטוֹשׁ״. קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּתְגַּלַּע הָרִיב – אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְנׇטְשׁוֹ; מִשֶּׁנִּתְגַּלַּע הָרִיב – אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְנׇטְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says:** If **two** litigants **come before you for a judgment, before you hear their** respective **statements** and claims; **or after you hear their statements but you do not** yet **know where the judgment is leaning,** meaning that it is not yet clear to the judge which party is in the right, **you** are **permitted to say to them: Go out and mediate.** But **after you hear their statements and you know where the judgment is leaning, you are not permitted to say to them: Go out and mediate, as it is stated: "The beginning of strife is as when one releases water; therefore leave off contention before the quarrel breaks out"** (Proverbs 17:14). Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya interprets the verse to mean: **Before** the resolution of **the contention is revealed, you can cast it off. Once** the resolution of **the contention is revealed, you cannot cast it off.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya introduces a third position. Two parties come before you. Either before you have heard their arguments at all, or after hearing them but before it is clear to you which way the judgment is likely to go — in both of these situations, *ata rashai lomar lahem tze'u u'bitzu*, you may tell them to go and work out a compromise. However, once you have heard the claims and you *do* have a sense of which way the judgment will lean, *i ata rashai lomar lahem tze'u u'bitzu* — you may no longer direct them toward compromise.

The proof text is *poter mayim reshit madon* — one who releases water is the beginning of strife. Rashi reads this as: if you wish to exempt yourself from the waters of judgment, from the controversy, you may do so *reshit madon* — at the beginning, before the dispute has been laid open before you. *V'lifnei hitgala hariv* — before the conflict is revealed, you may *nitosh*, abandon it. Once *nitgala hariv* — once it becomes clear — *i ata yachol lintosho*, you can no longer walk away from it.

The language here is notable. This formulation does not frame *bitzua* as an alternative form of judgment the court is exercising; rather, it speaks of exempting oneself, stepping back. This is more consistent with the idea that at a certain stage, a judge simply cannot abandon his responsibility. Once the trajectory of the case is visible, justice demands that he see it through. The result is three positions: Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yose HaGalili forbids *bitzua* once the parties come before the court at all; Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha permits and even requires it to be offered even at late stages, understanding it as a legitimate form of *din*; and Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya permits it until the judgment's direction becomes clear, after which it is foreclosed.

## Recusal & Responsibility

### Reish Lakish: Before Cla…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁבָּאוּ לַדִּין, אֶחָד רַךְ וְאֶחָד קָשֶׁה, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶם, אוֹ מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאֵין אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן דִּין נוֹטֶה – אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶם: ״אֵין אֲנִי נִזְקָק לָכֶם״, שֶׁמָּא נִתְחַיֵּיב חָזָק וְנִמְצָא חָזָק רוֹדְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says:** If **two** litigants **come for a judgment,** and **one** is **flexible** and agreeable **and one** is **rigid** and contentious, **before you hear their** respective **statements, or after you hear their statements but you do not** yet **know where the judgment is leaning,** it **is permitted** for **you to say to them: I will not submit to** your request to judge **you.** The judge may refuse the case out of fear that **perhaps the strong** and contentious one will **be** found **liable, and** it will **turn out** that **the strong** one will **pursue** the judge with intent to harm **him.**

Reish Lakish — some have the reading *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Lakish* — applies a closely related analysis to the question of judicial recusal. Two parties come before the court: one is *rach*, gentle and accommodating, and the other is *kasheh*, firm and potentially aggressive. The judge is concerned that whichever way he rules, the firm party will make his life difficult if he loses. The question is whether the judge may recuse himself from the case entirely.

Reish Lakish rules that before the judge has engaged the parties, or after hearing them but before the direction of the judgment is apparent, *ata rashai lomar lahem eini nizkak lachem* — the judge may say, "I am not taking on this case." The concern motivating this is explicit: *shema yitchayev chazak venimtza chazak rodfo* — lest the firm party be found liable and proceed to pursue the judge who ruled against him. This is a real human concern, and Reish Lakish acknowledges it by permitting early recusal.

### 'Lo Taguru' Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן הַדִּין נוֹטֶה – אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לָהֶן: ״אֵינִי נִזְקָק לָכֶם״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** But **once you hear their statements and you know where the judgment is leaning, you may not say to them: I will not submit to** your request to judge **you, as it is stated: "You shall not be afraid before any man"** (Deuteronomy 1:17).

However, once the judge has heard the parties and knows where the judgment is heading, that option is foreclosed. *Mishetishma divreihen ve'ata yode'a leheichan hadin noteh, i ata rashai lomar lahem eini nizkak lachem.* The proof is the verse *lo taguru mipnei ish ki hamishpat lElokim hu* — do not be afraid of any man, for the judgment belongs to God. The *chiddush* of Reish Lakish is therefore not that judges must be fearless at every stage, but that early recusal — before the case is engaged — is permissible. Once engagement occurs and the trajectory is clear, fear is no longer a valid reason to step away.

## Witnesses & Judges

### Student Must Speak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִין לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ וְרָאָה זְכוּת לֶעָנִי וְחוֹבָה לֶעָשִׁיר, מִנַּיִין שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁתּוֹק? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ״. רַבִּי חָנִין אוֹמֵר: לֹא תַּכְנִיס דְּבָרֶיךָ מִפְּנֵי אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: From where** is it derived **that a student who is sitting before his teacher and** he **sees** a point of **merit for a poor person or liability for a wealthy person, from where** is it derived **that he should not be silent? As it is stated: "You shall not be afraid before any man";** he should fear neither his teacher nor the wealthy litigant. **Rabbi Ḥanin says:** The verse intimates: **Do not suppress your statement before** any **man.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha extends the responsibility of speaking truth in the courtroom beyond the judges themselves. If a student is sitting before his *Rebbe* and observes the proceeding — and he sees a point of merit in favor of the poor party, or something that would weigh against the rich party — *minayin shelo yishtok*, how do we know he may not remain silent? The source is *lo taguru mipnei ish* — do not be afraid of any person. Here *taguru* is read as a form of *agur*, to gather or hold in. Do not gather in your words; do not withhold them out of deference to the *Rebbe* presiding over the court or out of fear of the powerful litigant.

### Don't Withhold Words

Rabbi Chanina formulates the same principle more directly: *lo tachnis devarecha mipnei ish* — do not cause your words to be held back because of any person. The obligation to speak the truth in a judicial context falls on everyone present who has relevant knowledge or insight, not only on the judges themselves.

### Witness Awareness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיהוּ עֵדִים יוֹדְעִים אֶת מִי הֵן מְעִידִין, וְלִפְנֵי מִי הֵן מְעִידִין, וּמִי עָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מֵהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָמְדוּ שְׁנֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר לָהֶם הָרִיב לִפְנֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the witnesses should know about whom they are testifying, and before Whom they are testifying, and Who will ultimately exact payment from them, as it is stated: "Then both the men, between whom the controversy is, shall stand before the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 19:17).

The *Gemara* then turns to witnesses. *V'hayu ha'edim yodim et mi hem mei'idim v'lifnei mi hem mei'idim umi atid lipara mehem* — the witnesses must know before whom they testify, against whom they testify, and who will exact payment from them. The verse cited is *v'amdu shnei ha'anashim asher lahem hariv lifnei Hashem* — the two people who have the dispute shall stand before God. This is read not as referring to the litigants but to the witnesses, who are presenting themselves before God Himself.

Rashi explains the phrase *et mi hem mei'idim* — against whom they testify — in a striking way. False witnesses are, in a sense, testifying against God. By testifying falsely, they cause an unjust outcome that God would then have to correct through other means, compelling the wronged party to receive back what is his through divine intervention rather than through the court. The witnesses are therefore, as it were, making God Himself work harder, and their false testimony acts as a kind of affront to the divine justice they have circumvented.

### Judge's Fear & Assurance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיהוּ הַדַּיָּינִין יוֹדְעִין אֶת מִי הֵן דָּנִין, וְלִפְנֵי מִי הֵן דָּנִין, וּמִי עָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מֵהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת אֵל״. וְכֵן בִּיהוֹשָׁפָט הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֶל הַשֹּׁפְטִים רְאוּ מָה אַתֶּם עֹשִׂים כִּי לֹא לְאָדָם תִּשְׁפְּטוּ כִּי אִם לַה׳״. שֶׁמָּא יֹאמַר הַדַּיָּין: מָה לִי בְּצַעַר הַזֶּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עִמָּכֶם בִּדְבַר מִשְׁפָּט״. אֵין לוֹ לַדַּיָּין אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁעֵינָיו רוֹאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And the judges should know whom they are judging, and before Whom they are judging, and Who will ultimately exact payment from them, as it is stated: "God stands in the congregation of God; in the midst of the judges He judges"** (Psalms 82:1). **And similarly, with regard to Jehoshaphat it says: "And he said to the judges: Consider what you do; for you judge not for man, but for the Lord"** (II Chronicles 19:6). And **lest the judge say: What** value is there **for me with this suffering?** Why should I engage in such a burdensome and difficult task? **The verse states: "He is with you in giving judgment,"** from which it is derived that in rendering his decision, **a judge has only that which his eyes see.** He is enjoined to render the best judgment possible based on the information he has available, and he is not accountable for anything else.

The same framework applies to the judges. *V'hayu hadayanim yodim et mi hem danim v'lifnei mi hem danim umi atid lipara mehem* — judges must know before whom they judge and who will exact from them. The verse *Elokim nitzav ba'adat El* — God stands in the congregation of the judges — establishes that divine presence is real and active in the court. The same teaching is found in Yehoshaphat's address to the judges: *re'u mah atem osim ki lo l'adam tishpetu ki laShem* — see what you are doing, for you judge not for man but for God.

Lest this awareness become paralyzing — lest a judge say *mah li betzaar zeh*, why should I take on this burden — the *Gemara* offers the counterbalancing assurance: *imachem bidvar mishpat*, God is with you in the matter of judgment. *Ein lo l'dayan ella mah she'einav ro'ot* — a judge is accountable only for what his eyes can see. He is not expected to transcend his human limitations; he is expected to bring integrity, rigor, and honesty to the process. If he does, God accompanies him, and his ruling carries legitimacy even if it is not perfect. This principle finds an echo in the teaching of Rav Moshe Feinstein regarding *psak halacha*: when a decisor goes through the process with genuine integrity, whatever he concludes is a legitimate ruling.

## Gmar Din & Ruling

### Gmar Din Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי גְּמַר דִּין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אַתָּה חַיָּיב, אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אַתָּה זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** Earlier, the *Tosefta* stated that once the verdict has been issued, it is not permitted for the judge to arrange a compromise. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **a verdict,** i.e., what is the formal action that signifies the conclusion of the case? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It is when the judge says: **So-and-so, you are liable; so-and-so, you are exonerated.**

Returning to the threshold at which all opinions agree compromise is foreclosed, the *Gemara* defines *gmar din*. Rav Yehuda citing Rav states: *ish ploni atah chayav, ish ploni atah zakai* — when the court declares, "So-and-so, you are liable; so-and-so, you are exempt" — that is *gmar din*. At that moment, no further *peshara* is possible under any opinion.

### Halacha: Equal Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa,** who said it is a mitzva to mediate a dispute.

The *halacha* is ruled like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who said *mitzva livtzoa*. However, the *Gemara* clarifies the precise scope of this ruling. The *mitzva* does not mean the judge is obligated to actively encourage compromise or steer the parties toward it. Rather, *mitzva lemeimar lehu ee dina bayisu ee pshara bayisu* — it is a *mitzva* to present both options equally: do you want *din* or do you want *pshara*?

### Rav Huna's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא רַב הוּנָא תַּלְמִידֵיהּ דְּרַב הֲוָה! כִּי הֲוָה אָתוּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא אָמַר לְהוּ: אִי דִּינָא בָּעֵיתוּ, אִי פְּשָׁרָה בָּעֵיתוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? And was it** not that **Rav Huna was a student of Rav,** and **when** litigants **would come before Rav Huna he would say to them: Do you want** a strict **judgment,** or **do you want a compromise?**

This was the actual practice of Rav Huna, a student of Rav. When litigants came before him, he would ask: *ee dina be'itu ee pshara be'itu* — do you want judgment or do you want compromise? Both were offered as equal possibilities, neither promoted over the other. This reflects the ruling *le'ma'aseh*: the judge fulfills his obligation by making the option known, not by advocating for it.

### Mitzvah vs. Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מִצְוָה״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה? מִצְוָה לְמֵימְרָא לְהוּ: אִי דִּינָא בָּעֵיתוּ, אִי פְּשָׁרָה בָּעֵיתוּ? הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מִצְוָה: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה סָבַר מִצְוָה, תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** He means that it is **a mitzva to say to them: Do you want** a strict **judgment,** or **do you want a compromise?** The Gemara objects: Since this opinion **is** the same as that of **the first *tanna*,** who also allows compromise, it is redundant to teach it. The Gemara answers: There is a difference **between** them with regard to the question of whether it is **a mitzva** to arrange a compromise. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa holds** that it is **a mitzva** to offer them the option of compromise, and **the first *tanna* holds** that it is merely **permitted.**

The *Gemara* then asks whether this ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha is not simply identical to the *tanna kamma* who opened the *baraita*. After all, the *tanna kamma* said that *bitzua* requires three judges as *din* does, and that it is forbidden after *gmar din* — implying it is permitted beforehand. Is that not what Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha is saying?

The *Gemara* answers: *ika beina'ihu mitzva* — the difference is precisely the characterization of *bitzua* as a *mitzva*. The *tanna kamma* treats *bitzua* as *reshut* — permissible if the parties request it, but not something the judge needs to raise. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha holds that the judge has an affirmative obligation to present the option proactively, even if he must not push it as the preferred path.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ: מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁמַע דִּבְרֵיהֶן וְאַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ לְהֵיכָן הַדִּין נוֹטֶה, אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לוֹמַר לָהֶן ״צְאוּ וּבִצְעוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: If so, the opinion of the first *tanna* **is** the same as that **of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya.** The Gemara answers that there is a difference **between them** with regard to the principle: **After you hear their statements and you know where the judgment is leaning,** it **is not permitted** for **you to say to them: Go out and mediate.** In that instance, the first *tanna* holds that it is still not too late to suggest mediation.

A further comparison is drawn to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya. Is the *tanna kamma* not equivalent to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, who allowed *bitzua* until the direction of the judgment was clear? The *Gemara* responds: *ika beina'ihu mishetishma divreihem ve'ata yode'a heichan hadin noteh*. According to the *tanna kamma*, the option of *bitzua* remains available — if requested — right up until *gmar din*, even after the direction is apparent. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya closes that window earlier, once the judge perceives which way the case will go. So the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya differ on timing, and the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha differ on whether proactive suggestion is obligatory or merely permitted.

## Golden Calf Compromise

### Tanchum: Egel Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי: לֹא נֶאֱמַר מִקְרָא זֶה אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד מַעֲשֵׂה הָעֵגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְא אַהֲרֹן וַיִּבֶן מִזְבֵּחַ לְפָנָיו״. מָה רָאָה? אָמַר רַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין בַּר יֶפֶת אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: רָאָה חוּר שֶׁזָּבוּחַ לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the various Sages who offered interpretations of the verse: "And the covetous blesses himself, though he despises the Lord" (Psalms 10:3), **disagree with** the explanation **of Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai. As Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai says: This verse was stated only with regard to the incident of the** Golden **Calf, as it is stated: "And Aaron saw this, and he built [*vayyiven*] an altar [*mizbe'aḥ*] before it…**and said: Tomorrow shall be a feast to the Lord" (Exodus 32:5). **What** did Aaron **see? Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: He saw Hur, who** had been appointed together with Aaron by Moses to lead the people during Moses' absence (see Exodus 24:14), **slaughtered before him,** as he had protested the plan to fashion a calf and had been murdered by the people as a result.

The *Gemara* now introduces a dissenting interpretive voice. Rabbi Tanchum bar Chanilai argues that the verse *botzea berech ni'etz Hashem* — which has been used throughout this discussion to anchor the debate about judicial compromise — was never about *peshara* in court at all. *Lo ne'emar mikra zeh ella keneged ma'aseh ha'egel* — this verse was said only in reference to the sin of the golden calf. Rashi explains: Aharon made a *peshara* with his own conscience, permitting himself to build the *eigel*, and it is that compromise — internal, private, a *horeh heter le'atzmo* — to which the verse refers.

### Aharon's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: אִי לָא שָׁמַעְנָא לְהוּ הַשְׁתָּא, עָבְדוּ לִי כְּדַעֲבַדוּ בְּחוּר, וּמִיקַּיַים בִּי ״אִם יֵהָרֵג בְּמִקְדַּשׁ ה׳ כֹּהֵן וְנָבִיא״, וְלָא הָוְיָא לְהוּ תַּקַּנְתָּא לְעוֹלָם. מוּטָב דְּלִיעְבְּדוּ לְעֵגֶל, אֶפְשָׁר הָוְיָא לְהוּ תַּקַּנְתָּא בִּתְשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Aaron **said** to himself: **If I do not listen to them now, they will do to me as they did to Hur,** and the verse: **"Shall the priest and the prophet be slain in the sanctuary of the Lord?"** (Lamentations 2:20), **will be fulfilled through me, and they will never have a remedy** for such a sin. It is **better** for them **to worship the calf,** as it is **possible they will have a remedy through repentance.** Nevertheless, according to Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai, whoever praises Aaron for this compromise is provoking God.

The *Gemara* cites a *midrashic* reading of the verse *vayar Aharon vayiven mizbeiach lefanav* — and Aharon saw and built an altar before it. What did Aharon see? Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet citing Rabbi Elazar: *ra'ah Chur shezevuach lefanav* — he saw that Chur had been slaughtered before him. Moshe had left Aharon and Chur in charge. When Moshe returns, Chur is gone. The *midrash* understands that the people killed Chur when he refused to cooperate. Seeing this, Aharon reasoned: *ilu shama'u lehu, hashta avidu li kedavdu veChur* — if I refuse them as Chur did, they will do to me what they did to Chur.

But Aharon's reasoning was more than simple self-preservation. He calculated that if *he* — a *kohen* and a *navi* — were slaughtered at what would become the site of the *Mikdash*, the sin would be so catastrophic, so beyond repair, that *lo havi lehu takana le'olam* — they would have no possibility of atonement ever. Whereas if he built the *eigel*, grievous as that was, *efshar havi lehu takana be'teshuva* — there remained the possibility of *teshuva*. It was, in Aharon's calculus, a compromise between two terrible outcomes, choosing the one that left the door to repentance open.

The *Gemara* does not absolve Aharon of responsibility for this reasoning. The language of *peshara beino levein atzmo* — compromise with his own conscience — signals that this was a rationalization, or at best a morally fraught calculation, not a clear justification. The parallel to Yehuda's half-measure regarding Yosef, raised earlier in the *sugya*, hovers in the background: is a partial intervention that averts the worst still praiseworthy, or is it a failure to do what was fully required?

### Water = Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן״, מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? כִּדְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: אֵין תְּחִילַּת דִּינוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם נִידּוֹן אֶלָּא עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **those *tanna'im*** who did not interpret the verse: **"The beginning of strife is as when one releases water"** (Proverbs 17:14), with regard to compromise, **what do they derive from** this verse? They understand the verse **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Hamnuna, as Rav Hamnuna says: The beginning of a person's judgment** after he dies is that he is **judged only concerning matters of Torah, as it is stated: "The beginning of strife is as when one releases water."** Based on this verse, the *tanna'im* who apply the verse this way are not ignoring the *peshara* discussion; they simply read this particular *pasuk* as pointing elsewhere.

The *Gemara* then addresses the other *tanna'im* who interpreted the verse *poter mayim reshit madon* not in terms of judicial withdrawal but in a different context entirely. They read it according to the principle of Rav Hamnuna: *ein techilat dino shel adam nidon ella al divrei Torah* — a person is first judged in the heavenly court on the basis of his relationship to Torah. *Mayim* — water — represents Torah, as in the verse *hoi kol tzame lechu la-mayim*. Therefore, *poter mayim reshit madon* means that exempting oneself from Torah study is the *reshit*, the beginning, of one's judgment. The *tanna'im* who apply the verse this way are not ignoring the *peshara* discussion; they simply read this particular *pasuk* as pointing elsewhere.

## Folk Sayings & Verses

### Aphorisms Cited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: טוּבֵיהּ דְּשָׁמַע וְאָדֵישׁ, חַלְפוּהּ בִּישָׁתֵיהּ מְאָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן״ – רֵישׁ מְאָה דִּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked: It is good** for a person **who hears** statements said against him **and** yet **remains silent,** as **a hundred misfortunes pass him by** as a result. Upon hearing this, **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"The beginning of strife is as when one releases water"** (Proverbs 17:14). The words "beginning [*poter*] of strife [*reishit madon*]" allude to: **The beginning of one hundred litigations [*reish me'a dinei*].** Troubles are avoided if one overlooks and excuses [*poter*] an offense.

The *Gemara* now presents a series of folk sayings, each matched by Shmuel to a biblical verse, as a way of showing that common wisdom is already embedded in *pesukim*. These exchanges are framed as Shmuel speaking to Rav Yehuda.

The first aphorism: *tuvya de-shama ve-adish, chafua bei shtei me'ah* — it is good for one who hears insults hurled at him and remains silent; a hundred misfortunes will not befall him. Shmuel responds that the verse says *poter mayim reshit madon* — releasing the waters of strife is the beginning of a hundred judgments. By reading *madon* in its *gematria* value — Rashi notes that *madon* equals one hundred — one who releases strife frees himself from a hundred litigations. The aphorism and the verse, differently approached, point to the same wisdom: letting conflict go spares enormous consequence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: אַתַּרְתֵּי תְּלָת גַּנָּבָא לָא מִיקְּטַל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה פִּשְׁעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְעַל אַרְבָּעָה לֹא אֲשִׁיבֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked** by: **For** only **two** or **three** thefts, **the thief** is **not executed** by the heavenly court. **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"So says the Lord: For three transgressions of Israel, or for four, I will not repay it"** (Amos 2:6). Shmuel interprets the verse rhetorically, as if saying: Will I not repay the fourth offense? Accordingly, before the fourth offense, it is still possible to rectify the sins.

The second: a person who commits a sin two or three times without evident consequence begins to think he will escape punishment. Shmuel cites the verse *al shlosha pishei Edom ve'al arba lo ashivenu* — for three transgressions of Edom and for four I will not turn back the judgment. Beyond a certain point, accumulating sins will bring their reckoning; repeated transgression does not signal impunity but accumulating debt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: שַׁב בֵּירֵי לִשְׁלָמָנָא, וַחֲדָא לְעָבֵיד בִּישׁ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״כִּי שֶׁבַע יִפּוֹל צַדִּיק וָקָם וְרָשָׁע יִפּוֹל בְּאַחַת״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked: Seven pits** are dug **for the man of peace,** and he escapes all of them, **and one** pit is dug **for the evildoer,** and he cannot escape it. **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"For a righteous man falls seven times, and rises up again, but the wicked shall fall at once"** (see Proverbs 24:16, 28:18).

The third: seven pits are laid for a man of peace and he avoids them all, while for a wicked person a single pit suffices to bring him down. Shmuel cites *ki sheva yipol tzadik ve-kam* — the righteous man falls seven times and rises, while the wicked stumble in evil. The verse as it actually reads, noted in the margin, has *ve'rasha'im yikashlu be'ra'ah*, not quite the formulation given in the *Gemara* — a small textual discrepancy, but the point holds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: דְּאָזֵיל מִבֵּי דִינָא שְׁקִל גְּלִימָא – לִיזַמַּר זְמָר וְלֵיזִיל בְּאוֹרְחָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״וְגַם כׇּל הָעָם הַזֶּה עַל מְקֹמוֹ יָבֹא בְשָׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked:** With regard to one **who goes from the court,** and his **cloak** has been **taken** from him in the course of the proceedings, i.e., he lost all his money due to a ruling against him, **let him sing a song and go** happily **on the way.** Although he lost the case, he has benefited from justice being served. **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** with regard to Yitro's advice for judiciary reforms that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"And all these people shall also go to their place in peace"** (Exodus 18:23).

The fourth: one who leaves court having had his cloak taken from him should sing and go on his way. Shmuel cites *ve'gam kol ha'am hazeh al mekomo yavo ve-shalom* — all this people will go to their place in peace. The idea echoes the earlier discussion of *tzedakah* to the losing party: being compelled to return what does not belong to you is not merely a loss, but a restoration of right order. Justice brings peace even to those who are ruled against.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: הִיא נָיְימָא, וְדִיקּוּלָא שָׁפֵיל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״בַּעֲצַלְתַּיִם יִמַּךְ הַמְּקָרֶה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked:** If a woman is carrying a basket on her head, when **she is dozing the reed basket falls. Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"By laziness the rafters sink in;** and through idleness of the hands the house leaks" (Ecclesiastes 10:18).

The fifth: a woman carrying a basket on her head who dozes off will drop it — inattention leads to ruin. Shmuel cites *be-atzaltaim yemach ha-mikre* — through laziness the rafters will collapse. Negligence, whether physical or spiritual, produces structural failure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: גַּבְרָא דִּרְחִיצְנָא עֲלֵיהּ, אַדְיֵיהּ לִגְזִיזֵיהּ וְקָם. אָמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, קְרָא כְּתִיב: ״גַּם אִישׁ שְׁלוֹמִי אֲשֶׁר בָּטַחְתִּי בוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying as he walked: The man upon whom I relied** has **lifted his fist [*ligzizeih*] and stood** against me. **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: A verse** is **written** that conveys the message of this aphorism: **"Indeed, my own familiar friend, in whom I trusted,** who did eat of my bread, has lifted up his heel against me" (Psalms 41:10).

The sixth: the person I relied upon raised his fist against me — the pain of betrayal by someone trusted. Shmuel cites *ve'gam ki ish shlomi asher batachti bo* — even the man of my peace in whom I trusted — the verse goes on to describe how that person turned against the speaker. The aphorism has its parallel in the *pasuk*: betrayal by the trusted is an ancient and recurring human experience.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָאָמַר וְאָזֵיל: כִּי רְחִימְתִּין הֲוָה עַזִּיזָא, אַפּוּתְיָא דְּסַפְסֵירָא שְׁכִיבַן. הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא עַזִּיזָא רְחִימְתִּין, פּוּרְיָא בַּר שִׁיתִּין גַּרְמִידֵי לָא סַגִּי לַן.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** man **who was saying** about his marriage **as he walked: When our love was strong, we** could have **slept on** a bed that was **the width of a sword. Now that our love is not strong, a bed of sixty cubits** is **not sufficient for us.**

The seventh and final aphorism is the most poignant: when our love was strong, we could sleep on the edge of a sword — the narrowest space felt spacious. Now that our love has weakened, a bed of sixty *amos* is not wide enough for us. The quality of a relationship transforms the conditions within it.

### Shechina Withdrawal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי. מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּתִיב: ״וְנוֹעַדְתִּי לְךָ שָׁם וְדִבַּרְתִּי אִתְּךָ מֵעַל הַכַּפֹּרֶת״, וְתַנְיָא: אָרוֹן תִּשְׁעָה וְכַפּוֹרֶת טֶפַח, הֲרֵי כָּאן עֲשָׂרָה. וּכְתִיב: ״וְהַבַּיִת אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה הַמֶּלֶךְ שְׁלֹמֹה לַה׳ שִׁשִּׁים אַמָּה אׇרְכּוֹ וְעֶשְׂרִים רׇחְבּוֹ וּשְׁלֹשִׁים אַמָּה קוֹמָתוֹ״. וּלְבַסּוֹף כְּתִיב: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ הַשָּׁמַיִם כִּסְאִי וְהָאָרֶץ הֲדֹם רַגְלָי אֵיזֶה בַיִת אֲשֶׁר תִּבְנוּ לִי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: Verses** are **written** that convey these sentiments. **Initially, it was written: "I will meet with you there and I will speak with you from above the Ark Cover"** (Exodus 25:22), **and it is taught** in a *baraita*: The **Ark** of the Covenant was itself **nine** handbreadths high, **and** the **Ark Cover** was one **handbreadth** thick. **There** is a total height of **ten** handbreadths **here.** At first, when God had great affection for Israel, the Divine Presence was revealed within the confines of this limited space. **And it is written: "And the house that King Solomon built for the Lord, its length was sixty cubits, and its breadth twenty cubits, and its height thirty cubits"** (I Kings 6:2). **And at the end,** when Israel sinned, the whole of the space of the Temple was not expansive enough for the Divine Presence to rest within it, as **it is written: "Thus says the Lord: The heaven is My throne, and the earth is My footstool; where is the house that you may build for Me?** And where is the place that may be My resting place?" (Isaiah 66:1).

Rav Huna responds to this last aphorism with a *pasuk*. Originally, God said *ve-no'adti lecha sham ve-dibarti itcha me'al ha-kaporet* — I will meet you there and speak with you from above the *kaporet*. A *baraita* establishes that the *aron* was nine *tefachim* tall and the *kaporet* one *tefach*, making ten *tefachim* total. God's presence was contracted, as it were, to within ten *tefachim* of the ground — intimate, immediate, pressing close to the human space.

Later, however, the verse describes the *Beit Hamikdash* that Shlomo built: sixty *amos* long, twenty *amos* wide, thirty *amos* high. And after Israel sinned, God declared *ko amar Hashem ha-shamayim kis'i ve-ha'aretz hadom raglai, ei zeh ha-bayit asher tivnu li* — the heavens are My throne and the earth My footstool; what house will you build for Me? God, who had once compressed His presence into ten *tefachim* alongside His people, could no longer dwell even in a thirty-*amah* sanctuary when the relationship had deteriorated. The analogy to the aphorism is precise: when love is strong, the narrowest space suffices; when it is not, no space is adequate.

### 'Lo Taguru' Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּכַנּוֹשֵׁי הוּא? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיַיִן לֹא תִשְׁתֶּה וְלֹא תֶאֱגֹר״. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״תָּכִין בַּקַּיִץ לַחְמָהּ אָגְרָה בַקָּצִיר מַאֲכָלָהּ״. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״אֹגֵר בַּקַּיִץ בֵּן מַשְׂכִּיל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to analyzing the *Tosefta*. **From where may** it **be inferred that this** expression: **"You shall not be afraid [*taguru*]"** (Deuteronomy 1:17), **is a term for gathering in,** so that the term may be interpreted to mean that a judge may not keep his ruling to himself? **Rav Naḥman said: The verse states:** "You shalt plant vineyards and dress them, **but you shall neither drink of the wine, nor gather [*te'egor*]"** (Deuteronomy 28:39). **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says** it is derived **from here: "She provides her bread in the summer, and gathers [*agra*] her food in the harvest"** (Proverbs 6:8). **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, says** it is derived **from here: "A wise son gathers [*oger*] in the summer"** (Proverbs 10:5).

The *Gemara* concludes by returning to the phrase *lo taguru mipnei ish*, which has been used several times to derive the obligation not to withhold one's words in a judicial context. The question is the *lashon*: what does *taguru* mean such that it can be read as "do not hold back your words"?

Three sources are offered. Rav Nachman derives it from the verse *ve-yayin lo tishteh ve-lo te'egor* — wine you shall not drink and you shall not gather in — where *te'egor* means to gather or store up. Rav Acha bar Yaakov derives it from the verse about the ant, *tachin ba-kayitz lachmah, agra ba-katzir ma'achalah* — she prepares her food in summer, she gathers her stores in the harvest. And Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika derives it from *oger ba-kayitz ben maskil* — a wise son gathers in summer. In all three cases, *oger* or *agar* means to gather, to store up, to hold in. The prohibition of *lo taguru* is therefore a prohibition against gathering in one's words, against withholding them. The one who possesses truth in a court of justice — witness or bystander alike — must not hoard it.
