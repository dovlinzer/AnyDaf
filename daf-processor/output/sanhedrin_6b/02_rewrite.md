# Sanhedrin 6b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Peshara & Kinyan

### Rav Ashi: No Kinyan

The daf opens mid-discussion, picking up from the debate over *peshara* versus *din* — whether compromise, understood as binding arbitration, constitutes a form of judicial rendering or merely a contractual arrangement. That underlying question determines whether *peshara* requires three judges or one. It is in this context that Rav Ashi offers his inference: *shema mina peshara eina tzricha kinyan* — from the very fact that there is a debate between one and three judges, we may conclude that *peshara* does not require a *kinyan*.

The logic runs as follows. Were *peshara* to require a *kinyan* — the formal act of acquisition by which one obligates oneself to a decision — then the entire debate over requiring three judges would collapse. Once a *kinyan* is performed, the parties have already contractually bound themselves to accept whatever the arbitrator decides. In that case, even two witnesses should suffice to validate the arrangement, and no one would argue that three judges are necessary. The fact that an opinion exists requiring three demonstrates that we are not operating within a purely contractual framework; something more judicial is at play.

### Hilcheta: Kinyan Required

The *Gemara* then reaches a surprising conclusion. Despite the apparent force of Rav Ashi's inference, the *halacha* is stated plainly: *v'hilcheta peshara tzricha kinyan* — *peshara* does in fact require a *kinyan*. The question then reasserts itself: if a *kinyan* is performed, why would anyone still require three judges?

Tosafot addresses this directly. The requirement for three judges persists even when a *kinyan* is performed because a *kinyan* made before an illegitimate panel would be tantamount to a *kinyan* made in error. The parties are not agreeing to submit themselves to an arbitrary result; they are committing themselves to a genuine and responsible *peshara*. Anything less than three judges risks producing a decision that lacks the requisite legitimacy, and therefore the agreement itself would be built on a flawed foundation. Hence, three judges remain necessary even in the presence of a *kinyan* — not to enforce the contractual obligation, but to ensure that what the parties are obligating themselves to constitutes a real and defensible form of compromise.

## Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

### Baraita: 3 Judges, No Po…

A *baraita* is introduced: just as *din* requires three judges, so too *bitzua* — compromise — requires three. This confirms that *peshara* is understood, at least procedurally, as a form of adjudication rather than a purely private arrangement. The *baraita* further establishes that once *gmar din* — the finalized judgment — has been pronounced, *bitzua* is no longer permitted.

### R. Eliezer: Assur

Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yose HaGalili goes further. In his view, *assur livtzoa* — it is forbidden to compromise — and one who does so is a sinner, perverting the truth. More strikingly, *kol hamevarech et habotzea harei zeh menaetz* — anyone who praises the one who arranged the compromise is as one who blasphemes God. The verse invoked is *botzea berech ni'etz Hashem* — the one who blesses the *botzea* blasphemes God — which the *Gemara* reads not in its plain sense of ill-gotten gain but as a condemnation of judicial compromise. The imperative that follows is *yikov hadin et hahar* — let the law pierce the mountain. No obstacle is large enough to justify bending the truth of justice. The basis is *ki hamishpat lElokim hu* — the judgment belongs to God, and to distort it is to act against God Himself.

This position is reinforced by contrasting Moshe and Aharon. Moshe is associated with *yikov hadin et hahar* — the unbending administration of divine law. Aharon, by contrast, is described as *ohev shalom verodef shalom*, one who loved peace and pursued it, making peace between people, with truth on his lips and uprightness in his conduct. The verse cited is from the passage beginning *ki siftei kohen yishmeru da'at*, affirming Aharon's prophetic role.

One might ask: if compromise is *assur*, how is Aharon's conduct praiseworthy? The very qualities attributed to Aharon — making peace, speaking truth — sound like exactly the behavior Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yose HaGalili condemns.

### Rashi: Before Court

Rashi resolves this tension precisely. The prohibition of *livtzoa*, in Rashi's reading, applies specifically *mi sheba ledin* — once the parties have come before the court seeking judgment. At that point, the judges are obligated to administer justice and may not redirect the proceedings toward compromise. Aharon's conduct was entirely different: he pursued peace *before* disputes ever came before him as a judge, *kodem sheyavo'u lefanav ledin*. When he heard of a conflict, he would run after the parties and establish peace between them before they reached the stage of formal litigation. This is the essential distinction — Aharon's peacemaking was pre-litigation, and it is precisely that which is praiseworthy. Once the parties stand before the court, a different standard applies.

### Stolen Goods & Judah

The *Gemara* then digresses briefly from the main discussion. A teaching of Rabbi Eliezer considers the case of one who stole grain, ground it, baked it, and then separated *challah* from the loaf. How can such a person recite a blessing? The answer is that he cannot — *ein zeh mevarech ela menaetz*, it is not a blessing but a blasphemy, a case of *mitzvah haba'ah be'aveirah*, the performance of a commandment through sin. The verse *uvotzea berech ni'etz Hashem* is again invoked, here reading *botzea* in its literal sense of one who breaks bread.

Rabbi Meir then offers another reading of the verse, applying *botzea* specifically to Yehuda's proposal regarding Yosef. When Yehuda said to his brothers *mah betza ki naharog et achinu*, "what profit is it that we kill our brother?" — he was not the hero some might make him. Yehuda had it in his power to call for Yosef's release and return to their father. Instead he settled for a half-measure, selling him to the Ishmaelites. Rabbi Meir insists that praising Yehuda for this falls into the same category of *botzea berech ni'etz Hashem* — one who blesses a compromise that fell far short of what was possible is blaspheming. The comparison is pointed: a position of moral authority carries with it the obligation to act on it fully, not to negotiate it away.

### R. Yehoshua: Mitzva

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha takes the opposite position. Not only is *peshara* permitted even after the parties have come before the court, but *mitzva livtzoa* — it is a positive obligation to seek compromise. His proof is the verse *emet umishpat shalom shiftu beshaareichem* — judge truth and a judgment of peace in your gates. The apparent tension between *mishpat* and *shalom* — between strict law and peace — is itself the key. If you apply strict law, you do not necessarily achieve peace; if you pursue peace, you may compromise justice. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha resolves this by saying that *bitzua* is precisely the form of judgment that contains peace within it. It is not a departure from justice but a form of it — a *din berachamim*, judgment infused with compassion — which is why it properly requires three judges just as *din* does.

### David's Tzedakah Model

The *Gemara* cites the verse describing David: *vayehi David oseh mishpat utzedakah lechol ammo* — David performed *mishpat* and *tzedakah* for all his people. The parallel tension arises: where there is *mishpat*, how can there be *tzedakah* in the charitable sense, and vice versa?

One reading would see this as analogous to the *mishpat shalom* formulation — a judgment that incorporates *tzedakah*, understood as *chesed* and contextual sensitivity. The *Gemara*, however, reads *tzedakah* more literally, in the rabbinic sense of actual charity, and aligns the verse with the position of *assur livtzoa*: David would render strict justice, and then, if the poor party suffered financially as a result, David would personally compensate him from his own resources. The judgment was pure *mishpat*, and the subsequent payment from his own pocket was the *tzedakah*. These are two distinct acts, not one integrated ruling.

The *Gemara* makes this explicit: *dan et hadin, zikah et hazakai, vechiyav et hachayav* — he rendered the judgment, acquitting the innocent and obligating the liable — *vera'ah shenitchayev ani mamon, veshilam lo mitoch beito* — and when he saw that a poor person was financially ruined by the outcome, he paid him from his own household. *Zehu mishpat utzedakah* — that is both *mishpat* and *tzedakah*: *mishpat* for the winning party who received justice, and *tzedakah* for the poor party who received personal assistance from the judge.

A difficulty is then raised: if the *tzedakah* component only applied when the losing party happened to be poor, how does the verse say David acted this way *lechol ammo* — for all his people? In response, Rebbi offers a reading that does not require the losing party to be impoverished. Even without any financial payment from the judge's pocket, the outcome itself constitutes both *mishpat* and *tzedakah*: *mishpat* for the winning party, who had his property restored, and *tzedakah* for the losing party — *she'hotzi gezeila mitachat yado* — because the judgment removed from his hands money or property that was not rightfully his. Being compelled to return what one wrongfully holds is itself a form of *tzedakah*, a benefit to the one who is corrected, since he is no longer in possession of ill-gotten goods. In this way, every litigant receives *mishpat* and *tzedakah*, which accounts for *lechol ammo*.

### R. Shimon: Before Clarity

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya introduces a third position. Two parties come before you. Either before you have heard their arguments at all, or after hearing them but before it is clear to you which way the judgment is likely to go — in both of these situations, *ata rashai lomar lahem tze'u u'bitzu*, you may tell them to go and work out a compromise. However, once you have heard the claims and you *do* have a sense of which way the judgment will lean, *i ata rashai lomar lahem tze'u u'bitzu* — you may no longer direct them toward compromise.

The proof text is *poter mayim reshit madon* — one who releases water is the beginning of strife. Rashi reads this as: if you wish to exempt yourself from the waters of judgment, from the controversy, you may do so *reshit madon* — at the beginning, before the dispute has been laid open before you. *V'lifnei hitgala hariv* — before the conflict is revealed, you may *nitosh*, abandon it. Once *nitgala hariv* — once it becomes clear — *i ata yachol lintosho*, you can no longer walk away from it.

The language here is notable. This formulation does not frame *bitzua* as an alternative form of judgment the court is exercising; rather, it speaks of exempting oneself, stepping back. This is more consistent with the idea that at a certain stage, a judge simply cannot abandon his responsibility. Once the trajectory of the case is visible, justice demands that he see it through. The result is three positions: Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yose HaGalili forbids *bitzua* once the parties come before the court at all; Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha permits and even requires it to be offered even at late stages, understanding it as a legitimate form of *din*; and Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya permits it until the judgment's direction becomes clear, after which it is foreclosed.

## Recusal & Responsibility

### Reish Lakish: Before Cla…

Reish Lakish — some have the reading *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Lakish* — applies a closely related analysis to the question of judicial recusal. Two parties come before the court: one is *rach*, gentle and accommodating, and the other is *kasheh*, firm and potentially aggressive. The judge is concerned that whichever way he rules, the firm party will make his life difficult if he loses. The question is whether the judge may recuse himself from the case entirely.

Reish Lakish rules that before the judge has engaged the parties, or after hearing them but before the direction of the judgment is apparent, *ata rashai lomar lahem eini nizkak lachem* — the judge may say, "I am not taking on this case." The concern motivating this is explicit: *shema yitchayev chazak venimtza chazak rodfo* — lest the firm party be found liable and proceed to pursue the judge who ruled against him. This is a real human concern, and Reish Lakish acknowledges it by permitting early recusal.

### 'Lo Taguru' Obligation

However, once the judge has heard the parties and knows where the judgment is heading, that option is foreclosed. *Mishetishma divreihen ve'ata yode'a leheichan hadin noteh, i ata rashai lomar lahem eini nizkak lachem.* The proof is the verse *lo taguru mipnei ish ki hamishpat lElokim hu* — do not be afraid of any man, for the judgment belongs to God. The *chiddush* of Reish Lakish is therefore not that judges must be fearless at every stage, but that early recusal — before the case is engaged — is permissible. Once engagement occurs and the trajectory is clear, fear is no longer a valid reason to step away.

## Witnesses & Judges

### Student Must Speak

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha extends the responsibility of speaking truth in the courtroom beyond the judges themselves. If a student is sitting before his *Rebbe* and observes the proceeding — and he sees a point of merit in favor of the poor party, or something that would weigh against the rich party — *minayin shelo yishtok*, how do we know he may not remain silent? The source is *lo taguru mipnei ish* — do not be afraid of any person. Here *taguru* is read as a form of *agur*, to gather or hold in. Do not gather in your words; do not withhold them out of deference to the *Rebbe* presiding over the court or out of fear of the powerful litigant.

### Don't Withhold Words

Rabbi Chanina formulates the same principle more directly: *lo tachnis devarecha mipnei ish* — do not cause your words to be held back because of any person. The obligation to speak the truth in a judicial context falls on everyone present who has relevant knowledge or insight, not only on the judges themselves.

### Witness Awareness

The *Gemara* then turns to witnesses. *V'hayu ha'edim yodim et mi hem mei'idim v'lifnei mi hem mei'idim umi atid lipara mehem* — the witnesses must know before whom they testify, against whom they testify, and who will exact payment from them. The verse cited is *v'amdu shnei ha'anashim asher lahem hariv lifnei Hashem* — the two people who have the dispute shall stand before God. This is read not as referring to the litigants but to the witnesses, who are presenting themselves before God Himself.

Rashi explains the phrase *et mi hem mei'idim* — against whom they testify — in a striking way. False witnesses are, in a sense, testifying against God. By testifying falsely, they cause an unjust outcome that God would then have to correct through other means, compelling the wronged party to receive back what is his through divine intervention rather than through the court. The witnesses are therefore, as it were, making God Himself work harder, and their false testimony acts as a kind of affront to the divine justice they have circumvented.

### Judge's Fear & Assurance

The same framework applies to the judges. *V'hayu hadayanim yodim et mi hem danim v'lifnei mi hem danim umi atid lipara mehem* — judges must know before whom they judge and who will exact from them. The verse *Elokim nitzav ba'adat El* — God stands in the congregation of the judges — establishes that divine presence is real and active in the court. The same teaching is found in Yehoshaphat's address to the judges: *re'u mah atem osim ki lo l'adam tishpetu ki laShem* — see what you are doing, for you judge not for man but for God.

Lest this awareness become paralyzing — lest a judge say *mah li betzaar zeh*, why should I take on this burden — the *Gemara* offers the counterbalancing assurance: *imachem bidvar mishpat*, God is with you in the matter of judgment. *Ein lo l'dayan ela mah she'einav ro'ot* — a judge is accountable only for what his eyes can see. He is not expected to transcend his human limitations; he is expected to bring integrity, rigor, and honesty to the process. If he does, God accompanies him, and his ruling carries legitimacy even if it is not perfect. This principle finds an echo in the teaching of Rav Moshe Feinstein regarding *psak halacha*: when a decisor goes through the process with genuine integrity, whatever he concludes is a legitimate ruling.

## Gmar Din & Ruling

### Gmar Din Definition

Returning to the threshold at which all opinions agree compromise is foreclosed, the *Gemara* defines *gmar din*. Rav Yehuda citing Rav states: *ish ploni atah chayav, ish ploni atah zakai* — when the court declares, "So-and-so, you are liable; so-and-so, you are exempt" — that is *gmar din*. At that moment, no further *peshara* is possible under any opinion.

### Halacha: Equal Option

The *halacha* is ruled like Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who said *mitzva livtzoa*. However, the *Gemara* clarifies the precise scope of this ruling. The *mitzva* does not mean the judge is obligated to actively encourage compromise or steer the parties toward it. Rather, *mitzva lemeimar lehu ee dina bayisu ee pshara bayisu* — it is a *mitzva* to present both options equally: do you want *din* or do you want *pshara*?

### Rav Huna's Practice

This was the actual practice of Rav Huna, a student of Rav. When litigants came before him, he would ask: *ee dina be'itu ee pshara be'itu* — do you want judgment or do you want compromise? Both were offered as equal possibilities, neither promoted over the other. This reflects the ruling *le'ma'aseh*: the judge fulfills his obligation by making the option known, not by advocating for it.

### Mitzvah vs. Permitted

The *Gemara* then asks whether this ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha is not simply identical to the *tanna kamma* who opened the *baraita*. After all, the *tanna kamma* said that *bitzua* requires three judges as *din* does, and that it is forbidden after *gmar din* — implying it is permitted beforehand. Is that not what Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha is saying?

The *Gemara* answers: *ika beina'ihu mitzva* — the difference is precisely the characterization of *bitzua* as a *mitzva*. The *tanna kamma* treats *bitzua* as *reshut* — permissible if the parties request it, but not something the judge needs to raise. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha holds that the judge has an affirmative obligation to present the option proactively, even if he must not push it as the preferred path.

A further comparison is drawn to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya. Is the *tanna kamma* not equivalent to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, who allowed *bitzua* until the direction of the judgment was clear? The *Gemara* responds: *ika beina'ihu mishetishma divreihem ve'ata yode'a heichan hadin noteh*. According to the *tanna kamma*, the option of *bitzua* remains available — if requested — right up until *gmar din*, even after the direction is apparent. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya closes that window earlier, once the judge perceives which way the case will go. So the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya differ on timing, and the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha differ on whether proactive suggestion is obligatory or merely permitted.

## Golden Calf Compromise

### Tanchum: Egel Context

The *Gemara* now introduces a dissenting interpretive voice. Rabbi Tanchum bar Chanilai argues that the verse *botzea berech ni'etz Hashem* — which has been used throughout this discussion to anchor the debate about judicial compromise — was never about *peshara* in court at all. *Lo ne'emar mikra zeh ella keneged ma'aseh ha'egel* — this verse was said only in reference to the sin of the golden calf. Rashi explains: Aharon made a *peshara* with his own conscience, permitting himself to build the *eigel*, and it is that compromise — internal, private, a *horeh heter le'atzmo* — to which the verse refers.

### Aharon's Reasoning

The *Gemara* cites a *midrashic* reading of the verse *vayar Aharon vayiven mizbeiach lefanav* — and Aharon saw and built an altar before it. What did Aharon see? Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet citing Rabbi Elazar: *ra'ah Chur shezevuach lefanav* — he saw that Chur had been slaughtered before him. Moshe had left Aharon and Chur in charge. When Moshe returns, Chur is gone. The *midrash* understands that the people killed Chur when he refused to cooperate. Seeing this, Aharon reasoned: *ilu shama'nu lehu, hashta avidu li kedavdu veChur* — if I refuse them as Chur did, they will do to me what they did to Chur.

But Aharon's reasoning was more than simple self-preservation. He calculated that if *he* — a *kohen* and a *navi* — were slaughtered at what would become the site of the *Mikdash*, the sin would be so catastrophic, so beyond repair, that *lo havi lehu takana le'olam* — they would have no possibility of atonement ever. Whereas if he built the *eigel*, grievous as that was, *efshar havi lehu takana be'teshuva* — there remained the possibility of *teshuva*. It was, in Aharon's calculus, a compromise between two terrible outcomes, choosing the one that left the door to repentance open.

The *Gemara* does not absolve Aharon of responsibility for this reasoning. The language of *peshara beino levein atzmo* — compromise with his own conscience — signals that this was a rationalization, or at best a morally fraught calculation, not a clear justification. The parallel to Yehuda's half-measure regarding Yosef, raised earlier in the *sugya*, hovers in the background: is a partial intervention that averts the worst still praiseworthy, or is it a failure to do what was fully required?

### Water = Torah

The *Gemara* then addresses the other *tanna'im* who interpreted the verse *poter mayim reshit madon* not in terms of judicial withdrawal but in a different context entirely. They read it according to the principle of Rav Hamnuna: *ein techilat dino shel adam nidon ella al divrei Torah* — a person is first judged in the heavenly court on the basis of his relationship to Torah. *Mayim* — water — represents Torah, as in the verse *hoi kol tzame lechu la-mayim*. Therefore, *poter mayim reshit madon* means that exempting oneself from Torah study is the *reshit*, the beginning, of one's judgment. The *tanna'im* who apply the verse this way are not ignoring the *peshara* discussion; they simply read this particular *pasuk* as pointing elsewhere.

## Folk Sayings & Verses

### Aphorisms Cited

The *Gemara* now presents a series of folk sayings, each matched by Shmuel to a biblical verse, as a way of showing that common wisdom is already embedded in *pesukim*. These exchanges are framed as Shmuel speaking to Rav Yehuda.

The first aphorism: *tuvya de-shama ve-adish, chafua bei shtei me'ah* — it is good for one who hears insults hurled at him and remains silent; a hundred misfortunes will not befall him. Shmuel responds that the verse says *poter mayim reshit madon* — releasing the waters of strife is the beginning of a hundred judgments. By reading *madon* in its *gematria* value — Rashi notes that *madon* equals one hundred — one who releases strife frees himself from a hundred litigations. The aphorism and the verse, differently approached, point to the same wisdom: letting conflict go spares enormous consequence.

The second: a person who commits a sin two or three times without evident consequence begins to think he will escape punishment. Shmuel cites the verse *al shlosha pishei Edom ve'al arba lo ashivenu* — for three transgressions of Edom and for four I will not turn back the judgment. Beyond a certain point, accumulating sins will bring their reckoning; repeated transgression does not signal impunity but accumulating debt.

The third: seven pits are laid for a man of peace and he avoids them all, while for a wicked person a single pit suffices to bring him down. Shmuel cites *ki sheva yipol tzadik ve-kam* — the righteous man falls seven times and rises, while the wicked stumble in evil. The verse as it actually reads, noted in the margin, has *ve'rasha'im yikashlu be-ra'ah*, not quite the formulation given in the *Gemara* — a small textual discrepancy, but the point holds.

The fourth: one who leaves court having had his cloak taken from him should sing and go on his way. Shmuel cites *ve'gam kol ha'am hazeh al mekomo yavo ve-shalom* — all this people will go to their place in peace. The idea echoes the earlier discussion of *tzedakah* to the losing party: being compelled to return what does not belong to you is not merely a loss, but a restoration of right order. Justice brings peace even to those who are ruled against.

The fifth: a woman carrying a basket on her head who dozes off will drop it — inattention leads to ruin. Shmuel cites *be-atzaltaim yemach ha-mikre* — through laziness the rafters will collapse. Negligence, whether physical or spiritual, produces structural failure.

The sixth: the person I relied upon raised his fist against me — the pain of betrayal by someone trusted. Shmuel cites *ve-gam ki ish shlomi asher batachti bo* — even the man of my peace in whom I trusted — the verse goes on to describe how that person turned against the speaker. The aphorism has its parallel in the *pasuk*: betrayal by the trusted is an ancient and recurring human experience.

The seventh and final aphorism is the most poignant: when our love was strong, we could sleep on the edge of a sword — the narrowest space felt spacious. Now that our love has weakened, a bed of sixty *amos* is not wide enough for us. The quality of a relationship transforms the conditions within it.

### Shechina Withdrawal

Rav Huna responds to this last aphorism with a *pasuk*. Originally, God said *ve-no'adti lecha sham ve-dibarti itcha me'al ha-kaporet* — I will meet you there and speak with you from above the *kaporet*. A *baraita* establishes that the *aron* was nine *tefachim* tall and the *kaporet* one *tefach*, making ten *tefachim* total. God's presence was contracted, as it were, to within ten *tefachim* of the ground — intimate, immediate, pressing close to the human space.

Later, however, the verse describes the *Beit Hamikdash* that Shlomo built: sixty *amos* long, twenty *amos* wide, thirty *amos* high. And after Israel sinned, God declared *ko amar Hashem ha-shamayim kis'i ve-ha'aretz hadom raglai, ei zeh ha-bayit asher tivnu li* — the heavens are My throne and the earth My footstool; what house will you build for Me? God, who had once compressed His presence into ten *tefachim* alongside His people, could no longer dwell even in a thirty-*amah* sanctuary when the relationship had deteriorated. The analogy to the aphorism is precise: when love is strong, the narrowest space suffices; when it is not, no space is adequate.

### 'Lo Taguru' Meaning

The *Gemara* concludes by returning to the phrase *lo taguru mipnei ish*, which has been used several times to derive the obligation not to withhold one's words in a judicial context. The question is the *lashon*: what does *taguru* mean such that it can be read as "do not hold back your words"?

Three sources are offered. Rav Nachman derives it from the verse *ve-yayin lo tishteh ve-lo te'egor* — wine you shall not drink and you shall not gather in — where *te'egor* means to gather or store up. Rav Acha bar Yaakov derives it from the verse about the ant, *tachin ba-kayitz lachmah, agra ba-katzir ma'achalah* — she prepares her food in summer, she gathers her stores in the harvest. And Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika derives it from *oger ba-kayitz ben maskil* — a wise son gathers in summer. In all three cases, *oger* or *agar* means to gather, to store up, to hold in. The prohibition of *lo taguru* is therefore a prohibition against gathering in one's words, against withholding them. The one who possesses truth in a court of justice — witness or bystander alike — must not hoard it.
