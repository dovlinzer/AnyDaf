# Shabbat 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Egged Kli Shemi Egged

### Basket & Fruit Intro

Today's daf is 92a, and we pick up with a question arising from yesterday's *mishnah*. That *mishnah* established that if one has a basket filled with fruit and carries it out from a *reshus hayachid* — a private domain such as a house — into the street, a *reshus harabbim*, even if most of the basket is on the outside, as long as part of it remains on the inside, the act of carrying is not yet complete. The simple reading of this ruling reflects the idea that the basket renders everything within it a single unit; one does not look at the fruit individually but rather treats the whole as a fruit basket. Even if an entire piece of fruit has crossed into the public domain, it does not matter — it is defined as part of the fruit basket and treated accordingly. This is the position known as *egged kli shemi egged*: being bound within a vessel constitutes a genuine form of binding that creates a single unit. There are, however, other readings of this *mishnah* that downplay the role of the basket — readings that understand the case as involving unusually long fruits of which most of the fruit is outside, minimizing the *chiddush* of the ruling considerably.

### Rava: Gourds & Seeds

The *gemara* continues this debate at the top of 92a, four lines from the top. *V'chein amar Rava* — and Rava said similarly: *lo shanu ela b'kuppah meleiah kishu'in v'dulu'in* — the *mishnah* is speaking only of a basket filled with gourds and cucumbers, meaning fruits so large that even when part of the basket extends outside, no single complete fruit is fully on the outside. *Aval meleiah chardal* — but if the basket is filled with mustard seeds, small enough that whole fruits are certainly outside when part of the basket is, then *chayiv* — one is liable. In that case, Rava holds, one looks at the individual fruits rather than the basket as a whole. *Alma kasavar egged kli lo shemi egged* — this reveals that Rava's view is that being bound in a vessel does not constitute binding, that it does not create a unified unit, and one must therefore assess the individual fruits. This is a demanding reading of the *mishnah*.

### Abaye: All Seeds Exempt

Abaye, by contrast, says: *afilu meleiah chardal* — even if the basket is filled with mustard seeds, *patur* — one is exempt. Even though a thousand mustard seeds are fully outside the threshold, as long as part of the basket itself remains inside, one is exempt. *Alma kasavar egged kli shemi egged* — this reveals that Abaye holds that being bound by a basket is a genuine form of binding, that it creates a single unit, and the basket is assessed as a whole.

### Position Reversal

The *gemara* then introduces a tradition: *kam Abaye b'shitasei d'Rava, kam Rava b'shitasei d'Abaye* — each of them reversed his position, so that Abaye wound up adopting Rava's original stance and Rava wound up adopting Abaye's. They are still in disagreement, but now on the opposite sides of the argument. This is significant for *halacha*, since we almost always rule like Rava against Abaye — and now Rava is the one who holds *egged kli shemi egged*, the straightforward reading of the *mishnah* that the basket creates a single unit.

The *gemara* then states: *v'remi d'Abaye ad'Abaye, v'remi d'Rava ad'Rava* — it will now demonstrate a contradiction in Abaye's own statements and in Rava's own statements.

### Tosafot on Reversal

Tosafot raises an immediate methodological difficulty: since Rava and Abaye each held both sides of this argument at different points in their careers, how does one know that any given statement actually contradicts their final position? It might simply reflect an earlier view since abandoned. Tosafot resolves this by stating directly: *kim lehu l'shas d'hai milsa d'maisi itmar basar d'hadar behu* — the tradition of the *shas* is that the statement which will now be cited was made after they had already reversed themselves, reflecting their later, settled positions. It is worth noting how much of the analysis at this stage rests not on direct textual evidence but on received tradition — the assertion of the reversal itself is not supported by any explicit quotation, and the claim that the next statement dates from after the reversal is likewise simply known. That is the nature of the tradition here.

### Hand vs. Vessel Test

Now the *gemara* proceeds to the apparent contradiction. According to the revised positions — where Rava holds *egged kli shemi egged* and Abaye holds the opposite — what do we make of the following teaching? *D'itmar: hamotzi peiros l'reshus harabbim* — if one carries fruit out into the public domain. *Abaye amar beyad chayiv* — Abaye says, if you did so with your hand, you are liable. *Bikli patur* — but if it was in a vessel, you are exempt. Read simply and without context this seems puzzling: is one liable for carrying an apple in hand yet exempt for carrying it in a basket? The scenario being described, however, is not simple. The case involves an object that is partly inside and partly outside. Carrying "with the hand" means one is standing inside the house while one's hand — holding the fruit — extends into the public domain. The vessel case is our familiar scenario: part of the basket is outside, part remains inside. The question in both cases is identical: does one assess location by what is inside or by what is outside?

In the vessel case: does one go by where the basket is, or by where the fruit is? In the hand case: does one go by where the fruit is, or by where the body is? These are structurally parallel questions, and both hinge on whether one treats the containing unit — the basket, or the person's body together with the hand — as a single entity. Of course, the interesting cases are those where there is meaningful divergence between where the container is and where the contents are; a basket just barely larger than its fruit would not generate much of a question.

Rava, in this cited teaching, takes the opposite position: *beyad patur* — if you extend your hand, you are exempt, *bikli chayiv* — but in a vessel you are liable. According to Rava as quoted here, even though part of the basket is inside, since some of the fruit is outside, one is liable — which is the position that *egged kli lo shemi egged*. This is the reverse of Rava's stated later position. The *gemara* resolves this by saying *ipcha* — reverse the names. Either the citation reflects their earlier views before the reversal, or one simply exchanges the names of Abaye and Rava in this passage. In the end, the *gemara* arrives at two halachic conclusions now attributed to Rava: first, *egged kli shemi egged* — one looks at the basket as a single unit and goes by where the basket as a whole is; and second, that standing inside and extending one's hand outside renders one liable.

This second point raises a problem. The very first *mishnah* of this *masechta* teaches *pashat baal habayit yado lachutz* — if the homeowner extended his hand outside with bread and the poor person took from it, or the reverse, *sheneihem peturin* — both are exempt. Is that not precisely our case — standing inside, hand outside — and yet the *mishnah* says exempt?

### Rashi: Apple vs. Body

The *gemara* resolves this: *hatam lemala migimmel, hacha lemata migimmel* — there, the hand was above three *tefachim*; here, below three. Rashi explains the distinction beautifully. In the case of the first *mishnah*, the reason for the exemption is not that we regard the fruit as still "inside" because the person's body is inside. The fruit, when held in an outstretched hand, is one hundred percent outside. The exemption comes from a different consideration entirely: the fruit has not come to rest — *lo nach*. It has not been deposited. Rashi states this explicitly: *vetaama mishum d'lo nach, velo mishum eged gufeih* — the reason is the absence of *nichah*, of rest, not the connection to the carrier's body. When the hand is below three *tefachim*, the principle of *lavud* applies and anything within three *tefachim* of the ground is treated as resting on the ground, rendering one liable. When the hand is above three, the fruit has not come to rest, and the poor person must take it from the hand before liability can attach.

### Object Unity Resolved

This distinction clarifies why the basket and the apple in the hand belong to entirely different analytical categories. The basket is an *object* — and as a single object, one can coherently say it is partly inside and partly outside, and assess its location accordingly. The fruit and the person, however, are not a single object. The apple is always distinct from the person holding it. When the apple is in an outstretched hand, no one would say the apple is "one unit" with the person such that the apple's location is partly inside because the body is inside. The apple is simply outside. The separate question — whether it has come to rest — is an entirely distinct inquiry. Rashi's formulation makes this precise, and the distinction between the two cases is clean and elegant.

## Carrying Methods

### Methods of Carrying

The *mishnah* now turns to a different but closely related topic. *Hamotzi bein b'yimino bein b'smolo* — whether one carries an object in the right hand or the left hand, *b'toch heiko* — held against the chest within one's garment, *o al kteifav* — or on the shoulders, *chayiv* — one is liable in all these cases. *Shekein masa b'nei Kehat* — for this is how the *B'nei Kehat* carried the *mishkan*. The *B'nei Kehat* were responsible for transporting the primary sacred vessels; the *mishkan* was disassembled and they bore the *aron*, the *shulchan*, and the other major vessels on their shoulders and persons.

### Rashi on Methods

Rashi quotes and references a *Yerushalmi* tradition to account for why the *mishnah* enumerates multiple methods — right hand, left hand, chest, and shoulder. The shoulder is explicitly mentioned in the verse *uv'kateif yisau*. As for right hand, left hand, and chest, Rashi notes: *orah ar'a* — that is simply the natural, ordinary way things are carried; no special proof text is required. Rashi further cites, in the name of Rabbeinu Yitzchak bar Yehuda who heard it from Rav Hai, a tradition that it is explicit in the *Yerushalmi*: the verse regarding Elazar ben Aharon ha-Kohen's responsibilities — *u'fkudat Elazar ben Aharon ha-Kohen shemen ha-ma'or u'ktoret ha-samim u'minhat ha-tamid v'shemen ha-mishha* — lists multiple items under his care, and the *gemara* reads this not merely as administrative responsibility but as physical carrying. One flask of oil in his right hand, one in his left; the incense somehow against his chest; the *menahot* on his shoulder. This is how one carries seven distinct items simultaneously — by distributing them across different parts of the body.

### Unusual Methods Listed

The *mishnah* then proceeds to enumerate cases of unusual carrying: *k'l'ahar yado* — by the back of his hand; *u'v'raglo* — on his leg; *b'piv* — in his mouth; *u'v'mafreko* — Rashi understands this as the underarm, while Tosafot argues it means the elbow, and notes that the term in the vernacular of Ashkenaz for the elbow (*elbogen*) corresponds to their reading, whereas the underarm (*axlen*) corresponds to Rashi's. The *mishnah* continues: *b'ozno* — in his ear; *bi's'aro* — in his hair; *u'v'fundato* — in his belt; *u'fih l'mata* — with the opening of the belt facing downward, so that it is a poor container since things would fall out; *bein fundato l'haluko* — between one's belt and one's garment, which is actually a reasonable way to store certain items; *u'vi'sfat haluko* — at the hem of one's garment; *bi'mnalo* — in one's shoe; *bi'sandalo* — in one's sandal.

### Exemption Formula

*Patur* — one is exempt in all these cases — *she-lo hotzi k'derech ha-motzi'in* — because one has not carried in the way that people normally carry. This phrase begins to lay the foundation for the principle of *shinui* — doing something in an unusual manner — and its sister formulation *k'l'ahar yad*, named after the first example given: the back of the hand.

### Shinuy Concept

The term *k'l'ahar yad* is instructive. Literally *l'ahar yado* means the back of his hand. When the *mishnah* uses *k'l'ahar yado* — with the *kaf* of comparison — it is initially describing an actual method of carrying: on the back of one's hand. But this concrete example then becomes the paradigm for an entire conceptual category. Once we understand that carrying on the back of the hand is the prime exemplar of performing something in an unusual way, the phrase *k'l'ahar yad* is generalized to describe that whole category of *shinui*. Tosafot notes that in versions of the text that read *k'l'ahar yado* — with the *kaf* — some understand *b'raglo* (on his leg) as itself an explanation of *k'l'ahar yado*: the leg carrying is the concrete referent and what follows is the broader category.

It is worth noting that one could have read this entire *mishnah* as speaking specifically and exclusively about carrying, since carrying is a *melacha* that is unusually focused on process. Tosafot makes a point that has been discussed at length: unlike *melachot* that produce something — cooking, baking, dyeing — which are broadly defined by their result, carrying produces nothing and is therefore defined entirely by its process. Because carrying focuses on process, every variation in process may be analytically distinct and must be traced back to the *mishkan* individually. That is why the *mishnah* says: this is how *B'nei Kehat* did it. The normal ways are *chayiv* because they reflect the *mishkan* prototype; the unusual ways are exempt because they deviate from it.

### Shinuy Parameters

One might have concluded, then, that *shinui* is a principle unique to carrying. We will see, however, from subsequent *mishnayot*, that *shinui* extends across *melachot* of *Shabbat* more broadly. This generalization raises a series of important practical questions.

First: must *shinui* affect the end result? From the cases in our *mishnah* the answer is clearly no. Whether one carries an object in one's elbow, in one's belt, or in one's hand, the object arrives at its destination equally well. The end result is identical. The same is true of other *melachot*: if one turns on a light switch with one's elbow, the light burns just as brightly. *Shinui* does not require that the end result be compromised.

Second: must *shinui* make the act harder to perform? Or is any unusual method sufficient even if it is just as easy? Flipping a light switch with one's elbow, for example, may not be appreciably harder than doing so with a finger. From a number of cases it appears that something can constitute a *shinui* even when it does not make the act more difficult, simply because it is an unusual way of performing it.

Third: does *shinui* apply outside of *Shabbat*? This is a broader question that touches on *halacha* beyond the laws of *Shabbat*. The *Eglei Tal* — the well-known work on the thirty-nine *melachot* of *Shabbat*, so named because *tal* spelled backwards renders the letters for thirty-nine — addresses this directly. In the context of the laws of writing, there is a discussion of *derech ketivah*, and whether writing with the wrong hand might be invalid. The *Eglei Tal* initially considers the argument that *shinui* is relevant only to *Shabbat* because of the principle of *melechet machshevet* — the requirement that a prohibited labor on *Shabbat* be performed with the full deliberateness of skilled, purposive work. *Melechet machshevet* imposes rigorous standards on how an act must be carried out, and by its very nature, *shinui* undermines the compliance with those standards. One might then conclude that the concept of *shinui* as an exemption is peculiar to *Shabbat* and does not operate in other areas of *halacha*.

The *Eglei Tal* then demonstrates, however, that this cannot be entirely correct. In other areas of *halacha*, performing a prohibited act in an unusual manner also generates exemption: if one eats forbidden food in a *lav derech achilah* — not in the normal manner of eating — one is similarly exempt. So the *shinui* exemption cannot be entirely Shabbat-specific.

### Aglei Tal Framework

His resolution is that there are two distinct types of *shinui*. Some forms of *shinui* are significant enough — so fundamentally different from the normal performance of an act — that they apply across the board, in all areas of *halacha*, because the act is simply not the same act when performed in that manner. Other forms of *shinui* are comparatively trivial: they represent a variation that is unusual but not so dramatically different as to transform the nature of the act. This second category generates an exemption specifically in the context of *Shabbat* — because of *melechet machshevet* and its demanding standards — but would not generate an exemption in other areas of *halacha* where those particular standards do not operate.

### Two Shinuy Aspects

The conclusion, then, is twofold. On one hand, *shinui* in this *mishnah* is introduced in the context of carrying, and one could have emphasized that connection — carrying is hyper-focused on process, and *shinui* disrupts the process as defined by the *mishkan* paradigm. On the other hand, *shinui* proves to be a broader principle, extending to other *melachot* of *Shabbat*, and in certain forms extending even beyond *Shabbat* to other areas of *halacha*. What distinguishes the categories is the degree of deviation from the norm: some deviations are so substantial that they operate universally; others are significant only within the framework of *melechet machshevet*.

## Height Above 10 Tefachim

### Rabbi Elazar Rule

Rabbi Elazar states: *hamotzi masa lemala mi-asarah tefachim chayav, shekein masa B'nei Kehat* — one who carries a burden above ten *tefachim* is liable, for that is the manner in which *B'nei Kehat* carried. This ruling returns to the foundational principle that the *mishkan* defines the parameters of *chiyuv* in carrying. The relevance is as follows: the airspace of the *reshus harabbim* extends only up to ten *tefachim*, approximately three feet. If one were to carry an object in the *reshus harabbim* and place it against a wall or on a ledge higher than ten *tefachim*, one would ordinarily be exempt, since the object was never within the airspace of the *reshus harabbim*. Similarly, if one were carrying four *amos* in the *reshus harabbim* — not transitioning from domain to domain but simply traversing the public space — and carried the object above the ten-*tefach* airspace for the entire transit, one might argue there should be exemption.

Rabbi Elazar says: one is nevertheless liable, because that is how *B'nei Kehat* carried — above ten *tefachim*. Once again, the *mishkan* defines what constitutes a valid act of *hotza'ah*, even where ordinary reasoning might suggest exemption.

### Mishkan Heights

The *gemara* then asks: *u-masa B'nei Kehat minalan* — how do we know that *B'nei Kehat* carried above ten *tefachim*? The verse states: *al ha-mishkan ve-al ha-mizbeiach saviv* — regarding the curtains of the *chatzer* of the *mishkan*, it says they encompassed the *mishkan* and the *mizbeiach*. From this, the *gemara* establishes a *hekesh*: *makish mizbeiach le-mishkan* — the *mizbeiach* is linked to the *mishkan*. Just as the *mishkan* was ten *amos* tall, so too the *mizbeiach* was ten *amos* tall. This is not obvious from the plain reading of the Torah, which gives the altar's height as three *amos*; there is a debate among the *tanna'im*, with some understanding the three *amos* as measuring from a ledge surrounding the altar, making the full height of the altar ten *amos*.

And how do we know the *mishkan* itself was ten *amos*? *De-chsiv eser amos orech ha-keresh* — the verse states the length of each board was ten *amos*.

### Moshe 10 Amot

The *gemara* then cites: *ve-chsiv vayifros es ha-ohel al ha-mishkan* — and it is written that Moses spread the tent covering over the *mishkan*. Rav taught: *Moshe Rabbeinu pirso* — when the verse says *vayifros*, it means Moses personally spread it, not merely that he supervised the work. The *gemara* derives from this: *mikan atah lamed govhan shel Leviyim* — from here we learn the height of the Levites. For how could one person spread a covering over a structure ten *amos* tall unless that person was at least as tall as the structure? The *gemara* takes from this that Moses was ten *amos* tall — and moreover, that this was true of all the Levites, not only Moses. The extension to all Levites follows from several considerations: if Moses alone were ten *amos* and the others were not, he would appear as a *baal mum*, freakishly distinguished; and in any case, the task of assembling and disassembling the *mishkan* was performed repeatedly by all the Levites, so Moses's stature would not have been sufficient alone.

With this established, the *gemara* invokes a received tradition: *dekhol tuna demidlei be-motot, tilta mil'eil u-trei tiltei milra* — any burden carried on a pole is positioned one third above the pole and two thirds below. Applying this to the *mizbeiach*: the *mizbeiach* was ten *amos* tall; two thirds of ten *amos* is six and two thirds *amos* below the pole. The Levites, standing ten *amos* tall (measuring to their shoulders, perhaps nine *amos*), would have had the bottom of the *mizbeiach* several *amos* above the ground — clearly more than ten *tefachim*. This constitutes a proof, albeit one that rests on several assumptions stacked together: the height of the *mizbeiach*, the height of the Levites, and the one-third/two-thirds tradition of pole carrying.

### Aron Proof

The *gemara* offers an alternative, somewhat simpler proof: *me-Aron* — from the *aron* itself. *De-amar mar: aron tishah, ve-kaporet tefach* — we have taught that the *aron* was nine *tefachim* tall and the *kaporet* placed atop it was one *tefach*, making the total height ten *tefachim*. Applying the same pole-carrying tradition: two thirds of ten *tefachim* is six and two thirds *tefachim* below the pole. The normal height of a person from the ground to his shoulder, as given in another *gemara*, is three *amos* — eighteen *tefachim*. Eighteen *tefachim* at the shoulder, minus six and two thirds *tefachim* of the *aron* hanging below the pole, leaves roughly eleven and a third *tefachim* from the ground to the bottom of the *aron*. The *aron* was thus carried more than ten *tefachim* off the ground. This calculation is more direct, relying on the standard human proportion for shoulder height rather than the exceptional stature of Moses.

### Divine Presence Criteria

Why, then, does the *gemara* not simply use the proof from Moses? *Dilma Moshe shani* — perhaps Moses was exceptional, uniquely ten *amos* tall, and one cannot generalize from him to the other Levites. The basis for this concern is the teaching: *ein ha-shekhinah shoreh ella al chakham, gibbor, ve-ashir, u-vaal komah* — the divine presence rests only upon one who is wise, mighty, rich, and of great stature, with stature understood here in a quite literal, physical sense. If that is so, Moses's extraordinary height is explained by his unique status as the recipient of the *shekhinah*, and it cannot simply be extended to all the Levites. Therefore the *gemara* prefers the proof from the *aron*, which requires only the ordinary human proportion of shoulder height — a standard that applies universally rather than depending on the singular greatness of Moses.

## Normal Carrying Standard

### Hutzal Practice

We now turn to the second dimension of what constitutes normal carrying. Rav, in the name of Rabbi Chiya, states: *hamotzi masoi b'Shabbat al rosho chayav chatat* — one who carries a load on his head on *Shabbat* is liable, *shekein anshei Hutzal osin ken* — for that is the practice of the people of Hutzal. In some societies, carrying on the head is standard — as with women who carry baskets balanced on their heads. Rav is suggesting that because this practice exists in Hutzal, it constitutes a normal manner of carrying and renders one liable.

### Gemara Objection

The *gemara* immediately objects: *v'anshei Hutzal ruba d'alma* — are the people of Hutzal the majority of the world, that we should define universal norms based on their practice?

### City Custom

The *gemara* reframes the statement: perhaps what was actually taught is that if one of the people of Hutzal carries a load on his head on *Shabbat*, he is liable — *shekein bnei iro osin ken* — because in his city, that is the established norm. The ruling, in other words, is localized: one is liable in Hutzal because in Hutzal that is how things are carried, not because it is the universal norm.

### Universal Override

But the *gemara* goes further: *utbatel da'ato etzel kol adam* — even within Hutzal, the local practice may be of no legal consequence, since we go by what is universally normal. The local practice is simply overridden. One might well find this surprising: not only does Hutzal's practice not define the global norm, it does not even carry weight within its own territory. The *gemara* then reverses the original ruling entirely: *hamotzi masoi al rosho patur* — one who carries on the head is exempt. And even if one acknowledges that the people of Hutzal practice it, *batlah da'ato etzel kol adam* — their view is nullified by the universal standard.

### Tosafot Nuance

Tosafot raises a complication. Other *gemarot* appear to take a different approach, suggesting that regional norms can and do matter in defining what is considered normal. Rather than a pure universalism, those passages seem to allow a sufficiently large geographic region's practice to define the norm for the people of that region. Tosafot therefore attempts to delineate when local or regional norms carry weight and when the universal standard overrides them — though the precise line is not always easy to draw. The practical implication of the principle as stated here, in any event, leans toward exemption: more behaviors fall outside the universal norm and into the category of *shinui* when the standard is global rather than local, which expands the scope of what counts as an unusual manner and therefore what is exempt.

## Intent vs. Outcome

### Intent Reversal Cases

The *mishnah* now addresses cases where the manner of carrying was normal but did not unfold exactly as intended. *Hamiskavein lehotzi lefanav uva lo leacharav* — if one intended to carry an object in front of oneself — say, in a pouch or purse attached to one's belt — but in the course of carrying it slipped around behind, *patur* — one is exempt. The act was not performed exactly as intended, and though the end result — the object was taken out — is the same, the discrepancy in process suffices to exempt. Conversely, *leacharav uva lo lefanav* — if one intended to carry it behind and it moved to the front, *chayiv* — one is liable. The *gemara* will explain that in front provides better protection and visibility than behind, so when the outcome exceeds the intent in terms of safeguarding, that is not sufficient to exempt. When the outcome was worse than what was intended, the deficit in the process exempts.

This is a remarkable *chiddush*. We are familiar with *eino miskavein* — unintended consequences — but the classic cases involve genuinely unintended results: dragging a chair that unintentionally cuts a furrow, or throwing an object four *amos* that rolls eight. Here, the object arrived exactly where it was meant to go, placed precisely where intended. The only discrepancy is in the *process*: the intermediate manner of carrying was not quite as planned. That a process-level deviation — with no divergence in result — can generate exemption is a profound point, and one whose extension to other *melachot* is a significant question. If, for instance, one intended to turn on the front burner of a stove and accidentally turned on the back burner, one might ask whether this process-level error — though the end result is the same type of act — is sufficient to exempt. At the very least, by carrying, the *mishnah* establishes that it is.

### Apron Exception

*Be'emes amru ha'ishah ha-chogeret b'sinar, bein lefanav bein leacharav, chayeves* — in truth they said: a woman wearing an apron in which something is being carried, whether the apron is in front of her or behind her, is liable. It makes no difference that it moved from front to back or vice versa, *shekein raui lihayos chozer* — because that is what normally happens; the apron naturally shifts around. Since reversal is expected and normal, one cannot claim that the process deviated from intent in any meaningful way. The intent was never so rigidly fixed to a specific position when movement was foreseeable.

### Receipt-Keepers

*Rabbi Yehuda omer: af mekabblei petakim* — Rabbi Yehuda extends the same principle to those who accept receipts, certain officials or scribes who kept various documents in belts positioned in front of them, which would regularly shift to the back. The reversal of such a belt was an ordinary, expected occurrence. Tosafot explains the nature of these individuals — *lavlarei malchut* — scribes of the royal court who worked with belts containing various documents, whose belts characteristically moved around. Since reversal is the norm for their belts, even if the belt ended up in the back rather than the front, they cannot claim an unintended process.

### Gemara Confusion

The *gemara* asks: *mai shna lefanav uva lo leacharav depatur* — why is the first case, when it starts in front and moves behind, an exemption? *Delo isavid machshavto* — because it did not happen the way one planned. But *leacharav uva lo lefanav* — when it starts behind and moves in front — *nami lo isavid machshavto* — that also did not happen the way one planned. Both cases involve a process that diverged from intent; why is one exempt and the other liable?

### Rava: Protection Logic

Rava responds: *u'mai kushya* — there is no difficulty here at all. *Lefanav uva lo leacharav* — when it starts in front and moves behind, *deniskavein lishmirah me'ulah ve'alsah beyado shmirah pechusa* — one intended a higher level of protection and ended up with a lesser one. *Leacharav uva lo lefanav, deniskavein lishmirah pechusa ve'alsah beyado shmirah me'ulah* — one intended a lesser level of protection and ended up with a better one. When the outcome is inferior to the intent, one is exempt; when the outcome is superior, one is liable. The *mishnah* makes obvious sense, says Rava — there is no difficulty to resolve.

### Rav Elazar Split

The *gemara* then reveals that despite Rava's dismissal of any difficulty, there exists a tradition that a problem was perceived. The *gemara* reconstructs the source of confusion. The *mishnah* states: *hamiskavein lehotzi lefanav uva lo leacharav patur* — intended front, went behind, exempt. The inference seems to be: *ha leacharav uva lo leacharav chayav* — if one intended behind and succeeded in carrying it behind, then of course one is liable. The second half of the *mishnah* then says: *leacharav uva lo lefanav chayav* — intended behind, ended up in front, liable. But reading the *mishnah* with a forced implication: *ha leacharav uva lo leacharav patur* — if it remained behind, exempt? That would mean carrying behind you is itself not a normal act that makes one liable. With one part of the *mishnah* seemingly assuming that behind is liable and another part seemingly implying it is not, Rabbi Elazar concluded: *tavra — mi sheshana zu lo shana zu* — break it in half; these two clauses come from two different sources expressing contradictory views, combined into a single *mishnah*.

### Rav Ashi Resolves

Rav Ashi provides the definitive resolution: *u'mai kushya* — there is no difficulty. The *mishnah* is not ambiguous; it employs the rhetorical structure of *lo mibaya*. *Lo mibaya leacharav uva lo leacharav d'chayav* — it goes without saying that if one intended to carry behind and succeeded, one is liable; *d'avad machshavto* — one did exactly what one intended in a normal enough manner. *Ela afilu leacharav uva lo lefanav itztricha lei* — the novel ruling is that even when it started behind and moved to the front, one is still liable. *Salka datach amina* — one might have thought that since the intent was not fulfilled precisely, one should be exempt. *Ka mashma lan d'mitkavein lishmirah pechusa ve'alta b'yado shmira me'ulah d'chayav* — the *mishnah* teaches that when one intended lesser protection and the act resulted in greater protection, one is still liable.

The *gemara* thus closes with a striking observation: the tradition of difficulty with this *mishnah* — strong enough that Rabbi Elazar attributed it to two contradictory sources — cannot be located in any actual textual inconsistency, because the *mishnah* makes perfect sense. Yet the tradition existed. The *gemara* salvages it: *v'eima leacharav uva lo leacharav tannai hi* — perhaps the question of whether carrying behind you is considered normal at all is itself a debate among *tanna'im*. That debate surfaces in the following *baraita*.

### Money Belt Debate

*Hamotzi ma'ot b'fundato piha l'ma'ala chayav* — if one carries money in a belt with the opening facing up, one is liable; that is clearly the normal way to use such a belt. *Piha l'mata* — if the opening is facing downward: *Rabbi Yehuda mechayev v'chachamim potrin* — Rabbi Yehuda says liable; the Sages say exempt.

Rabbi Yehuda argues: *ee atem modim b'leacharav uva lo leacharav she'hu chayav* — do you not agree that carrying something behind you intentionally makes one liable? That is, the range of what counts as normal is broad enough to include carrying behind oneself. If so, says Rabbi Yehuda, carrying a money belt with the opening facing down should similarly fall within that range of normal, and one should be liable. The Sages respond: *ee atah modeh k'l'achar yado v'raglo she'hu patur* — do you not agree that carrying *k'l'ahar yad*, in the manner of the back of the hand, renders one exempt? The question is whether the belt facing down is more like the case of carrying behind — within the range of normal — or more like *k'l'ahar yad* — sufficiently unusual to be exempt. Rabbi Yehuda says it falls in the former category; the Sages say it falls in the latter.

Rabbi Yehuda then concludes: *ani amarti davar echad v'hen amru davar echad, ani lo matzati tshuva l'divreihem, v'hem lo matzu tshuva l'divrai* — I made my argument and they made theirs. I cannot refute their proof, and they cannot refute mine. The case of the belt with the opening facing downward is genuinely borderline, and neither side can conclusively establish which category it belongs to.

### Belt Category

The *gemara* then steps back and analyzes the structure of the debate. Since Rabbi Yehuda says to the Sages, "do you not admit that carrying behind is normal and makes one liable," the implication would be that the Sages deny this — that in their view, carrying behind you is also exempt. But that cannot be right either, because when the Sages say to Rabbi Yehuda, "do you not admit that *k'l'ahar yad* is exempt," the implication is that Rabbi Yehuda agrees — that even Rabbi Yehuda holds the back of the hand is exempt. And a *baraita* confirms: *k'l'aher yado v'raglo divrei hakol patur* — the back of the hand and the foot, everyone agrees, is exempt. So Rabbi Yehuda agrees that *k'l'ahar yad* is exempt. Does the *gemara* then suggest the Sages think even carrying behind you is exempt? That too would be confirmed nowhere.

### Normal Range

The *gemara* resolves this cleanly: *ela leacharav uva lo leacharav divrei hakol chayav* — carrying behind you, when that is one's intention, is universally agreed to render one liable. *K'l'aher yado v'raglo divrei hakol patur* — the back of the hand and foot render one universally exempt. *Ki pligi, b'fundato u'fiha l'mata* — the debate is exclusively about the money belt with the opening facing down. *Mar medami lei l'acharyav uva lo l'acharyav* — one compares it to the carrying-behind case, placing it within the range of normal. *U'mar medami lei l'ke'lacher yado v'raglo* — the other compares it to *k'l'ahar yad*, placing it in the unusual category.

The upshot is clear: normal carrying is not defined narrowly. It is not limited to only the most classic, optimal manner of carrying. It includes a range — and carrying behind oneself, though not the most protected or obvious method, falls within that range. At a certain point, deviation becomes sufficient to qualify as *k'l'ahar yad* and generate exemption, but that threshold is not reached by simply carrying behind. The money belt with the opening facing down sits precisely at the border, and no proof is sufficient to determine which side of the line it falls on. This elaboration of the concept of *shinui* — a range of normal with a disputed boundary — is among the most important practical frameworks established in these sugiyot.

## Two People Carrying

### Two Person Exemption

The *mishnah* states: *hamotzi kikar lereshus harabim chayav* — one who carries a loaf of bread into the *reshus harabbim* is liable. *Hotziu shenayim peturin* — if two people carried it, they are exempt. This is the exemption of *shenayim she'asu* — two people performing a single act together. Where one person could have done the act alone, having two people do it constitutes a departure from the normal manner of performing the *melacha* and therefore exempts.

### Sofa Exception

However: *lo yachol echad lehotzio vehotziu shenayim* — if one person could not have carried it alone — the case of a heavy sofa that requires two people — then if two people carry it, *shenayim chayavin* — they are both liable. That is the normal manner of moving such an object, and therefore fully constitutes the *melacha*.

### Rabbi Shimon View

*Rabbi Shimon poter* — Rabbi Shimon exempts even in the sofa case. His position is that a *melacha* is by definition an act performed by one person. When two people are required and two people carry, that is still not a *melacha* attributable to a single individual in the manner the Torah contemplates. Rabbi Shimon thus exempts universally whenever two people perform an act together.

### Three Positions

The three-way structure of the debate is as follows. *Zeh yachol vezeh yachol* — when each person is individually capable and both carry together: *Rabbi Meir mechayev* — Rabbi Meir holds them liable; *Rabbi Yehuda veRabbi Shimon potrin* — Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon both exempt. Rabbi Meir does not accept the principle of *shenayim she'asu* as a basis for exemption at all: however many people perform it, the act is the act. *Zeh eino yachol vezeh eino yachol* — when neither could do it alone and two are required: *Rabbi Yehuda veRabbi Meir mechayvim* — here Rabbi Yehuda joins Rabbi Meir in holding them liable, because the two-person carry is the normal and necessary method. *Rabbi Shimon poter* — Rabbi Shimon exempts even here, consistent with his general position.

### Asymmetric Ability

*Zeh yachol vezeh eino yachol* — the asymmetric case: one person is able to carry it alone, the other is not, and they carry it together. *Divrei hakol chayav* — everyone agrees the able-bodied person is liable. The one who was capable of performing the act alone remains the effective agent; the second person's contribution is trivial. The capable individual's participation is indistinguishable from having done it alone, and therefore constitutes a full *melacha* on his part. The principle of *shenayim she'asu* does not help him, because it is genuinely his *melacha* — the other person's assistance does not diminish his ownership of the act.

This framework has significant practical application. There are circumstances — the absence of an *eruv*, for example, and some pressing need — where having two people perform an act together can be invoked as a basis for leniency. Authorities including Rav Ovadia Yosef have applied the principle in such contexts. But every case requires careful analysis: the exemption applies only when both parties are of equal ability to perform the act alone (generating a genuine *shenayim she'asu*) or when neither can do so alone and Rabbi Yehuda's position does not apply. Any asymmetry in ability dissolves the exemption entirely.

### Baraita Summary

The *gemara* confirms: *tanya nami hachi* — a *baraita* teaches similarly. *Hamotzi kikar lereshus harabim chayav* — one who takes out a loaf is liable. *Hotziu shenayim* — if two people took it out together, *Rabbi Meir mechayev* — Rabbi Meir says liable; Rabbi Meir does not accept *shenayim she'asu*. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: im lo yachol echad lehotzio vehotziu shenayim, chayavin* — Rabbi Yehuda says: when would two people who carried together be liable? When one alone could not have done it. *Ve'im lav, peturin* — if each could have done it alone, they are exempt. *Rabbi Shimon poter* — Rabbi Shimon exempts even the sofa case, consistent with his universal exemption of *shenayim she'asu*. The source of this principle and its fuller development will be taken up in the next session.
