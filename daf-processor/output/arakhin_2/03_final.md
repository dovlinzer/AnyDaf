# Arakhin 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Arakhin

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכֹּל מַעֲרִיכִין, וְנֶעֱרָכִין, נוֹדְרִין וְנִידָּרִין — כֹּהֲנִים, לֹוִים וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים, נָשִׁים וַעֲבָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Everyone takes** vows of **valuation** and is thereby obligated to donate to the Temple treasury the value fixed by the Torah (see Leviticus 27:3–7) for the age and sex of the person valuated. **And** similarly, everyone **is valuated,** and therefore one who vowed to donate his fixed value is obligated to pay. Likewise, everyone **vows** to donate to the Temple treasury the assessment of a person, based on his market value to be sold as a slave, and is thereby obligated to pay; **and** everyone is the object of **a vow** if others vowed to donate his assessment. This includes **priests, Levites and Israelites, women, and** Canaanite **slaves.**

We begin a new *masechet*: *Masechet Arachin*, sometimes pronounced *Erchin*. The tractate takes its name and subject matter from the *psukim* at the end of *Parshat Bechukotai*, specifically *Perek* 27, which opens: *vayedaber Hashem el Moshe lemor, daber el bnei Yisrael ve'amarta aleihem, ish ki yaflie neder be'erkecha nefashot l'Hashem* — if a person enunciates a vow of the evaluation of souls to God.

### Vow of Evaluation

The Torah then proceeds to lay out a graduated chart of fixed monetary values. For a male between the ages of 20 and 60, the value is fifty *shekel* of silver by the *shekel hakodesh* — fifty *shekel*, which is ten times the value of a *bechor*. For a female in the same age bracket, it is thirty *shekel*. The values differ across age categories: from one month to five years, from five to twenty, and from sixty and above. The smallest category begins at one month of age; there is no fixed *erech* assigned to a child less than a month old. The Torah thus divides life into distinct stages, each carrying its own assigned valuation.

### Arachin & Temurah

It is worth noting that the very next *parsha* in the Torah immediately following the laws of *arachin* is the *parsha* of *temurah* — the prohibition and legal effect of exchanging a consecrated animal — which begins *ve'im behema asher yakrivu mimenu korban l'Hashem... lo yachalifenu velo yamir oto tov bera o ra betov, ve'im hamer yamir behema bivehema vehaya hu utmurato yihiye kodesh*. This is not merely a textual coincidence. The order of *Masechet Arachin* followed by *Masechet Temurah* in *Seder Kodashim* mirrors exactly the order of these topics as they appear in the *psukim* of *Bechukotai*, and it works out very neatly despite the general organizing principle of tractates being arranged by the number of *prakim*.

### Kodashim Classification

*Masechet Arachin* is placed within *Seder Kodashim* because it concerns the sanctification of value and one's obligations toward the *Beit HaMikdash*. It should be noted, however, that the vow of *erech* does not render any object itself *kadosh*; rather, the person accepts upon himself an obligation to pay a fixed sum of money to the *Beit HaMikdash*. Nevertheless, since the subject matter relates centrally to the *Beit HaMikdash* and to acts of sanctification in that sphere, it belongs within this *seder*.

## Hakol Maarichin

### General Principle

The *Mishnah* opens: *hakol maarichin vene'erachin* — all people can be *maarich*, meaning they can perform the act of *erech*, can make a vow to obligate themselves to pay the value of another person according to those Torah-fixed charts, and *vene'erachin*, they can be the object of such a vow, meaning any person can be the one about whom another says, "I will pay that person's *erech*." The *Mishnah* continues: *nodrin venidrin* — anyone can make a *neder* and anyone can be the object of a *neder*.

### Neder vs. Erech

This requires some unpacking, since the *Mishnah* is working with two distinct categories. The term *erech* refers specifically to the Torah's fixed values — those graduated charts based on age and sex. The term *neder*, in this context (often called *damim*), refers to a vow obligating oneself to pay the market value of a particular person. How does one determine a person's market value? The only legal framework in *halacha* that assigns a monetary value to a human being is the slave market. The vow of *damim* therefore obligates the one who vows to pay whatever the slave market would assign to that individual.

This is worth contrasting with other contexts in which human value is assessed — for instance, in cases of bodily injury, where *halacha* recognizes *tza'ar* (pain and suffering), *boshet* (embarrassment), *ripuy* (medical costs), and *shevet* (loss of work during recuperation). Even in injury cases, the slave market valuation is used only for one narrow component: the diminution in value of the limb itself, which is far from the only factor. In the context of *damim*, however, the slave market is the operative measure. The formal term *erech*, by contrast, is triggered only when one uses that specific word; it then invokes the Torah's predetermined fixed values rather than any market assessment.

### Included Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס, נוֹדְרִין וְנִידָּרִין וּמַעֲרִיכִין, אֲבָל לֹא נֶעֱרָכִין, שֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶעֱרָךְ אֶלָּא הַזָּכָר וַדַּאי וּנְקֵבָה וַדָּאִית.
>
> **Translation:** **A *tumtum*,** whose sexual organs are concealed, **and a hermaphrodite [*androginos*], vow, and** are the object of **a vow, and take** vows of **valuation, but they are not valuated.** Consequently, if one says, with regard to a *tumtum*: The valuation of so-and-so is incumbent upon me to donate to the Temple treasury, he is not obligated to pay anything, **as only a definite male or a definite female are valuated.**

The *Mishnah* lists: *Kohanim, Levi'im, Yisre'elim, venashim va'avadim* — whether *kohen*, *levi*, or *yisrael*, and including women and slaves. None of these categories are excluded from either making or being the object of these vows.

### Tumtum & Androgynos

One must, however, read the *Mishnah* carefully to identify where one sentence ends and the next begins. After listing the included categories, the *Mishnah* introduces the exceptional cases. A *tumtum* — a person whose genitalia are covered or indeterminate, so that one cannot know whether the individual is male or female — and an *androgynos* — a person who presents with both male and female genitalia — fall into a distinct category. They *nodrin venidrin*: they can make vows and be the object of vows of *damim*, since they have a market value regardless of gender. They can also be *maarichin*: they can obligate themselves to pay the fixed *erech* of another person. However, *aval lo ne'erachin* — they cannot be the object of an *erech*. The reason: *she'eino ne'erach ela hazachar vadai ve-hanekeivah vadait* — the Torah's chart specifies values for a definite male and a definite female. Since we do not know which column applies, we cannot assign the fixed value. One might suggest simply paying the higher amount to be safe, or, conversely, that one should at least be obligated to pay the lower. The *Mishnah* rejects both approaches: the chart applies only to those clearly identified as male or female, and a *tumtum* or *androgynos* simply does not appear on that chart. It bears noting that while the *androgynos* might be said to fit neither category cleanly, the *tumtum* presents a philosophically distinct problem: the category may exist, we simply lack the knowledge to determine it. This tension will surface again later in the *masechet*.

### Cheresh, Shoteh, Katan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן נִידָּרִין וְנֶעֱרָכִין, אֲבָל לֹא נוֹדְרִין וְלֹא מַעֲרִיכִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶם דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **A deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor** are the object of **a vow and are valuated, but neither vow** to donate the assessment of a person **nor take** a vow of **valuation, because they lack the** presumed **mental competence** to make a commitment.

A *cheresh* (deaf-mute), *shoteh* (one who is not of sound mind), and *katan* (minor) are *nidrin vene'erachin*: they can be the object of both a *neder* and an *erech*, since they have market value and they fall on the Torah's age-based charts as definite males or females. However, *aval lo nodrin velo ma'arikin* — they cannot be the subjects, cannot make a *neder*, and cannot make an *erech* vow. The reason: *mipnei she'ein bahem da'at* — they lack the legal capacity. This is the classic trio in *halacha* that cannot perform legally binding acts. Just as they cannot engage in financial transactions or fulfill obligations that require full legal standing, they similarly cannot undertake these vows.

### Eved Kanaani

A *Canaanite slave* — an *eved Kenaani* — presents yet another case. Such a slave may be the object of a vow, but if he undertakes a vow himself, it becomes an outstanding obligation rather than an immediately dischargeable one. Since the slave possesses no independent assets, the debt remains outstanding. It would be paid either upon the slave's eventual emancipation, should that occur, or if the master transfers property to the slave on terms stipulated to exclude the master's ownership — a legally recognized arrangement. If neither situation materializes, the debt simply remains on the books.

## Hakol Analysis

### Hakol Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״הַכֹּל מַעֲרִיכִין״ — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי מוּפְלָא סָמוּךְ לְאִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the mishna's statement: **Everyone [*hakol*] takes** vows of **valuation?** When a principle is stated in a mishna, it serves to include a particular case that it does not mention explicitly in its *halakha*. Which case is included by the broad statement here? The Gemara answers: The mishna teaches it **to add a discriminating** minor **on** the **brink of adulthood [*mufla samukh le'ish*],** i.e., during the year before a minor reaches majority.

The *Gemara* now turns to analyze the word *hakol* in the opening *Mishnah*. The *Gemara* assumes that *hakol* is never merely stylistic; it must be coming to include something beyond what has already been explicitly stated. The structure of the *Mishnah* provides four categories: *maarich* (the subject who vows an *erech*), *ne'erach* (the object of an *erech* vow), *noder* (the subject who vows *damim*), and *nidar* (the object of a *damim* vow). The *Gemara* will work through each of these four categories and determine what additional case *hakol* is coming to include in each.

### Concept of Haflaa

Before proceeding, it is worth establishing the conceptual framework that governs the discussion. The answer to the first category — *hakol maarichin le'atuyei mufla samuch l'ish* — involves a concept the *Rambam* categorizes under the heading of *hafla'ah*. This is one of the *Rambam*'s fourteen books, and *hafla'ah* designates a specific type of speech act: a verbal declaration that creates not merely a social or civil obligation but an actual religious or sanctified status. It is qualitatively different from a simple verbal promise, which is not legally binding in *halacha*. A *hafla'ah* act operates in the realm of the sacred. Examples include: my words can sanctify an animal; my words declaring *hareini nazir* make me a *nazir*; my words can constitute a *neder* that renders food or benefit forbidden to me. These acts do not merely record an intention — they create a *halachic* reality.

The term itself derives from the *pasuk* relating to *nezirus*: *ish ki yafli lindor neder nazir lehazir l'Hashem* — when a man *enunciates* a vow of *nezirus*. The root *hafla'ah* thus denotes the verbal act that brings about this changed status or obligation. The *erech* vow belongs within this category of *hafla'ah*: when one declares that the *erech* of a person is upon oneself, one has performed a *hafla'ah* that creates a religious obligation to pay to the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Mufla Samuch L'Ish

Within the laws of *hafla'ah*, the *halacha* establishes a category known as *mufla samuch l'ish* — a minor who is close to maturity. The rule is this: once a boy has turned twelve years old and one day — meaning he is in his thirteenth year — or a girl has turned eleven years old and one day, and we determine that the individual understands the meaning of the vow, understands that it relates to God and the religious sphere, then the *hafla'ah* act is binding upon that person even though they have not yet reached the formal age of majority (thirteen for boys, twelve for girls). This is learned from the word *yafli* in the *pasuk*, as a *d'Oraita* derivation, and carries real *halachic* consequences: if such a minor sanctifies an animal, the *kedushah* takes effect; if the minor declares *nezirus*, the *nezirus* is binding; the acts have consequences in the real world independent of whether the minor can be held legally liable.

This is what the *Gemara* means when it says *hakol maarichin le'atuyei mufla samuch l'ish*. The word *hakol* in the *Mishnah* is coming to include this category: a minor within the year before *bar* or *bat mitzvah* who already understands the nature of *hafla'ah* can be the subject of an *erech* vow.

### Minor's Legal Capacity

One might ask: if this depends on the minor's *da'at* and understanding, why should the rule not apply even earlier, whenever the individual demonstrably understands the vow? The answer is that this is not purely contextual, not purely a function of individual cognitive capacity. It reflects the same structure as the age of majority itself: the *halacha* does not test each individual to determine whether they happen to be mature. Rather, it works with some combination of an objective threshold — at this age, one is presumed to be largely developed — and a subjective assessment of understanding. The category of *mufla samuch l'ish* is a *halacha* that balances these considerations; it is not a purely subjective standard.

Returning to the four categories of *hakol* in the *Mishnah*: for *ne'erachin* — being the object of an *erech* — the *Gemara* says *le'atuyei menuval u'mukei shachin*, someone utterly repulsive and covered with blisters, meaning a person who has no market value whatsoever as a slave. One might have thought that since such a person has no value under the *neder-damim* framework (zero market value), the verse's juxtaposition of *neder* and *erech* — *neder be'erkecha nefashot l'Hashem* — would suggest that only those who carry market value under *neder* are also placed on the *erech* chart: *kol sheyeshno b'damim yeshno b'erchin, v'chol she'eino b'damim eino b'erchin*. The *Mishnah* teaches otherwise: the word *nefashot* — "souls" — means any person, regardless of market value. The Torah's fixed chart covers every identifiable male or female, whether or not the market assigns them a value.

### Four Hakol Categories Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נֶעֱרָכִין״ — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי מְנוָּּול וּמוּכֵּה שְׁחִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara similarly asks: **What is added** by the statement: And everyone **is valuated?** The Gemara answers: The mishna serves **to add a repulsive** man **and one afflicted with boils,** who have no market value.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״נֶדֶר בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״ כְּתִיב, כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּדָמִים — יֶשְׁנוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּדָמִים — אֵינוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why this addition is necessary: It might **enter your mind to say** that as **it is written** in the verse: **"A vow** of persons to the Lord, **according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:2), which juxtaposes one who is valuated to one who is the object of a vow, **anyone who is** included **in** the category of **assessments,** i.e., if he vows to pay his assessment he must pay it to the Temple, **is** also included **in** the category of **valuations. But anyone who is not** included **in** the category of **assessments is not** included **in** the category of **valuations.** Since these people, a repulsive man and one afflicted with boils, are not subject to assessment, as they have no market value, perhaps they are also not subject to valuation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן ״נְפָשֹׁת״, כָּל דְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that these too are subject to valuation, as the same verse also states: **"Persons [*nefashot*],"** indicating anyone who has **any amount** of life [*nefesh*] is subject to valuation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נוֹדְרִין״ — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? נִידָּרִין אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **What is added** by the mishna's statement: Everyone **vows** to donate the assessment of a person? The Gemara answers: Actually, this statement is not necessary, but it is mentioned because the continuation: And everyone is the object of **a vow, was necessary.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נִידָּרִין״ — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אִי לְאֵתוֹיֵי טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לְהוּ! וְאִי לְאֵתוֹיֵי חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן — בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What,** then, **is added** by the clause: And everyone is the object of **a vow? If** one were to suggest that this serves **to add a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite,** that cannot be correct, as they **are explicitly taught** in the mishna itself. **And if** one were to suggest that it serves **to add a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** they too **are explicitly taught** in the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לְאֵתוֹיֵי פָּחוֹת מִבֶּן חוֹדֶשׁ, בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לְהוּ! וְאִי לְאֵתוֹיֵי נָכְרִי, בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לְהוּ! לְעוֹלָם לְאֵתוֹיֵי פָּחוֹת מִבֶּן חוֹדֶשׁ, וְתָנֵי וַהֲדַר מְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** And **if** you say that this clause serves **to add** the *halakha* that a child who is **less than a month old,** who is not subject to valuation, is nevertheless subject to assessment, this too **is explicitly taught** in a mishna (5a). And **if** one were to say that it serves **to add a gentile,** that **is** also **explicitly taught** in a mishna (5b). The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the phrase: And everyone is the object of a vow, is mentioned in the mishna in order **to add** a child who is **less than a month old, and** the mishna **teaches** this *halakha* in general terms **and then explains** it in detail later.

For the category of *nodrin* (the subject making a *damim* vow), the *Gemara* concludes there is nothing novel to include; the *hakol* there was stated because the *Mishnah* needed to make a general statement.

For *nidrin* (the object of a *damim* vow), the *Gemara* considers several candidates: *tumtum* and *androgynos* — but the *Mishnah* states their status explicitly; *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* — also explicit; a non-Jew — also discussed in a later *Mishnah*. The *Gemara* concludes that the *hakol* here comes *le'atuyei pachot miben chodesh* — to include an infant less than a month old. Such an infant does not appear on the Torah's fixed *erech* chart, which begins at one month. One might have thought that just as they are off the *erech* chart, so too a vow of *damim* would carry no obligation with respect to them. The *Gemara* teaches that even for an infant less than a month old, the market value — whatever it may be — is assessed and paid. This point is made explicitly in a later *Mishnah* in the tractate, but the *hakol* here establishes the principle in advance. *Rashi* notes that this category was selected because it is the one that has some surrounding debate and nuance in the later *Mishnah*, making it worth flagging with a general *hakol* at the outset.

## Hakol Survey

### Hakol Somchin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל סוֹמְכִין״ — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי יוֹרֵשׁ, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara inquires about similar general expressions that appear in other *mishnayot*. **What is added** by the mishna (*Menaḥot* 93a): **Everyone** who brings an offering **places hands** on the head of the animal? The Gemara answers: This clause serves **to add** that **an heir** places hands on the offering of the deceased, **and** the mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** that an heir does not place his hands on an offering he inherited.

Having addressed the four categories of *hakol* within the *Mishnah* of *Arachin* itself, the *Gemara* embarks on a broader survey of places throughout *Shas* where a *Mishnah* employs the word *hakol*, in each case asking what the word is coming to include. The first example comes from the beginning of *Masechet Chullin* (and also *Menachot*): *hakol somchin* — everyone may perform *semichah*, the act of laying hands on a sacrifice. *Le'atuyei yoresh* — to include an heir. The question is as follows: *semichah* is performed exclusively by the owner of the *korban*. If you encounter a *korban* wandering through *Yerushalayim* that belongs to someone else, you do not perform *semichah* on it even if you bring it to the Temple, because it is not yours. What, then, about an heir? A person dies, and the son brings the *korban* — does he perform *semichah*? He did not personally accept the obligation; he inherited it. Yet to some degree he stands in his father's place.

### Heir Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל מְמִירִין״ — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי יוֹרֵשׁ, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the ruling of the mishna (*Temura* 2a): **Everyone substitutes** a non-sacred animal for a consecrated animal? The Gemara answers: Here too, the mishna serves **to add** that **an heir** substitutes a non-sacred animal for his father's consecrated animal, i.e., the non-sacred animal also becomes sanctified. **And** this mishna is also **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** as he maintains that an heir cannot substitute a non-sacred animal for the consecrated animal he inherited.

The *Mishnah* resolves that an heir does perform *semichah*, *ud'lo k'Rabbi Yehuda* — contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who says an heir is excluded.

Immediately alongside this, the *Gemara* cites *hakol memirin* — everyone may perform *temurah*, the exchange of a consecrated animal. *Temurah* is prohibited, but if performed it is halachically effective: the *kedushah* extends to the second animal as well. Here too, only the owner can perform *temurah* — if one person attempts *temurah* on another's *korban*, the act has no effect. The question of whether the heir can perform *temurah* is parallel to that of *semichah*: is the heir considered the new owner of the *korban*, or not? The *Mishnah* again says the heir can do it, *ud'lo k'Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Ownership Concepts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: יוֹרֵשׁ סוֹמֵךְ, יוֹרֵשׁ מֵימֵר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יוֹרֵשׁ אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ, יוֹרֵשׁ אֵינוֹ מֵימֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the source for these two opinions of Rabbi Yehuda. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **An heir places hands** on his father's offering, and **an heir can effect substitution** for an offering inherited from his father. **Rabbi Yehuda says: An heir does not place hands** and **an heir cannot effect substitution.**

These two questions — *semichah* and *temurah* — are obviously linked. Both hinge on the same underlying question: to what degree does the heir inherit not merely the physical property but the personal *halachic* standing of the one who originally consecrated the animal?

### R. Yehuda's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ וְלֹא קׇרְבַּן אָבִיו, וְיָלֵיף תְּחִלַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ מִסּוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ — מָה סוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ יוֹרֵשׁ אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ, אַף תְּחִלַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ יוֹרֵשׁ אֵינוֹ מֵימֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda?** With regard to placing of hands, he expounds the term: **"His offering"** (Leviticus 3:2), as teaching that one places his hands only on his own offering, **but not** on **his father's offering. And** with regard to the ruling that an heir cannot effect substitution, Rabbi Yehuda **derives** the *halakha* of **the initial stage of consecration,** i.e., substitution, in which a previously non-sacred animal is consecrated, **from the final stage of consecration,** the act of placing hands, which is performed upon an already-consecrated animal immediately before it is slaughtered: **Just as** with regard to **the final stage of consecration, an heir does not place hands, so too,** with regard to **the initial stage of consecration, an heir cannot effect substitution.**

A *beraita* spells out the debate: *yoresh somech yoresh memir* — an heir performs *semichah* and, if he does *temurah*, it is effective. *Rabbi Yehuda omer yoresh eino somech yoresh eino memir* — Rabbi Yehuda says neither applies to the heir. What is Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning? By *semichah*, the verse states *ve'samach yado al rosh korbano* — he shall place his hand on the head of *his* sacrifice. The emphasis on "his" excludes even the father's *korban*: it must be entirely and personally one's own. And Rabbi Yehuda derives the beginning of the process from the end: *temurah* — the act of creating *kedushah* in the second animal — is the beginning of sanctification, while *semichah* is performed at the end of the process before the *shechitah*. Since we have established from *semichah* that an heir is not treated as the full owner, the same principle applies to *temurah*: *mah sof hekdesh, yoresh eino somech, af techilas hekdesh, yoresh eino memir*.

### Rabbanan's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הָמֵר יָמִיר — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַיּוֹרֵשׁ, וְיָלְפִינַן סוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ מִתְּחִלַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ: מָה תְּחִלַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ — יוֹרֵשׁ מֵימֵר, אַף סוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ — יוֹרֵשׁ סוֹמֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** from where do **the Rabbis** derive their opinion? The verse states: "If **he shall substitute [*hamer yamir*]** animal for animal" (Leviticus 27:10), with the doubled form of *hamer yamir* serving **to include the heir** as one capable of effecting substitution. **And** the Rabbis **derive the final stage of consecration,** i.e., the placing of hands, **from the initial stage of consecration,** i.e., substitution: **Just as** with regard to **the initial stage of consecration an heir can effect substitution, so too,** with regard to **the final stage of consecration, an heir can place hands.**

The *Rabbanan* counter that the double language *hamir yamir* — the redundant formulation in the *parsha* of *temurah* — is understood as inclusive: *l'rabot et hayoresh*, to include the heir. And they apply the principle in the opposite direction: since at the beginning of the process — *temurah* — the heir is considered enough of an owner to instill *kedushah*, so too at the end — *semichah* — *yoresh somech*. The reasoning is a mirror image of Rabbi Yehuda's.

### Joint Offerings Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ — וְלֹא קׇרְבַּן גּוֹי, ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ — וְלֹא קׇרְבַּן חֲבֵירוֹ, ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ — לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי חוֹבְרִין לִסְמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis, what do they do with this** term: **"His offering,"** from which Rabbi Yehuda derives that an heir does not place his hands? The Gemara explains how the Rabbis expound each mention of the term, which appears three times (Leviticus 3:2, 7, 12). One instance of **"his offering"** teaches that one places hands only on one's own offering, **but not** on the **offering of a gentile.** Another instance of **"his offering"** teaches that one places hands only on one's own offering, **but not** on the **offering of another** person. The third instance of **"his offering"** serves **to include all** the **owners of** a jointly owned **offering in** the requirement of **placing hands,** i.e., they are all required to place their hands on the offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי חוֹבְרִין לִסְמִיכָה לֵית לֵיהּ, וְאִי נָמֵי אִית לֵיהּ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **And** how does Rabbi Yehuda respond to this claim? The Gemara explains that **Rabbi Yehuda does not hold** that one of the mentions serves **to include all owners** of a jointly owned **offering in** the requirement of **placing hands.** Rather, one of the owners places his hands on the offering on behalf of the entire group. Consequently, he is left with one spare mention of "his offering," from which he derives that an heir does not place his hands. The Gemara adds: **Alternatively,** one can say that Rabbi Yehuda **holds** that one of the mentions serves to include owners of a jointly owned offering,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוֹי וַחֲבֵירוֹ מֵחַד קְרָא נָפְקִי, אִיַּיתַּרִי לְהוּ תְּרֵי: חַד ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ וְלֹא קׇרְבַּן אָבִיו, וְאִידָּךְ לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי חוֹבְרִין לִסְמִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** but he maintains that the exclusion of **a gentile and** the exclusion of the offering of **another** person from the requirement of placing hands are **derived from** the same **one** mention of "his offering" in the **verse.** This **leaves two** mentions of "his offering" **for** Rabbi Yehuda. **One** he expounds to teach that he places hands on **"his offering," but not** on **his father's offering** that he inherited, **and the other** mention remains **to include all** the **owners of** a jointly owned **offering in** the requirement of **placing hands.**

The underlying question is a recurring one in *halacha*: to what degree is an heir a new and independent entity who merely has received transferred property, and to what degree is the heir a continuation of the deceased? The phrase *bra k'ra d'avuha* — the son is like the legs of the father — captures this ambiguity. Is this *korban* considered "his" or his father's?

The *Gemara* continues to work through the *psukim*. The *Rabbanan* use the phrase *korbano* (appearing multiple times in the *parsha*) to exclude a non-Jew and exclude bringing *semichah* on behalf of a friend. These derive from the same basic principle: *semichah* requires that the *korban* be one's own. One cannot designate a *shaliach* to perform *semichah* — even an authorized agent cannot do so. And when a non-Jew brings a *korban* (which is halachically permitted), there is no *semichah* — not by the non-Jew and not by a *kohen* on his behalf. This last point is the contribution of *Tosafot*: one might have thought that since the non-Jew cannot personally perform *semichah*, a *kohen* might act in his stead; the exclusion of *korbano* teaches otherwise.

An additional application of *korbano* is *l'rabot ba'alei chovarim l'semichah*: if multiple partners jointly own a *korban*, every single one of them must personally perform *semichah*. One individual cannot act for the group. Each person for whom it is "*his* sacrifice" must lay his own hands on it.

Rabbi Yehuda, who uses one *korbano* to exclude the heir, does not have the extra derivation available to include all partners in a joint offering. Accordingly, in his view one member of the group may act on behalf of all. The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, exclude the non-Jew and the friend from a single *pasuk* (since both derive from the same principle of "not yours"), freeing up the additional *korbano* to require *semichah* by every member of a partnership — while also agreeing with Rabbi Yehuda that an heir does perform *semichah*.

## Daily Ritual Mitzvot

### Hakol B'Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בְּסוּכָּה״ — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קָטָן שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ, דִּתְנַן: קָטָן שֶׁאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְאִמּוֹ חַיָּיב בְּסוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the statement of the following *baraita*: **Everyone is obligated in** the mitzva of ***sukka*?** The Gemara answers: This serves **to add a minor who does not need his mother** when he awakes in the middle of the night. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Sukka* 28a): **A minor who does not need his mother is obligated in** the mitzva of ***sukka*.**

Turning from the realm of *korbanot* to the domain of daily and seasonal *mitzvot*, the *Gemara* surveys further instances of *hakol* throughout *Shas*. *Hakol chayavin basukkah* — everyone is obligated in *sukkah*. *Le'atuyei katan she'eino tzarich le'imo* — to include a minor who no longer needs his mother. The practical measure: when he wakes up, the first thing he says is not "mother." If a child has reached the stage of independence where he does not immediately call out for his mother, he is obligated — at least from the standpoint of *chinuch*. *Detnan: katan she'eino tzarich le'imo chayav basukkah*.

### Hakol B'Lulav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בְּלוּלָב״ — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קָטָן הַיּוֹדֵעַ לְנַעְנֵעַ, דִּתְנַן: קָטָן הַיּוֹדֵעַ לְנַעְנֵעַ חַיָּיב בְּלוּלָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **What is added** by the ruling of the following *baraita*: **Everyone is obligated in** the mitzva of ***lulav*?** The Gemara answers: This clause serves **to add a minor who knows** how **to wave** the *lulav*. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Sukka* 42a): **A minor who knows** how **to wave** the *lulav* **is obligated in** the mitzva of ***lulav*.**

*Hakol chayavin belulav* — everyone is obligated in *lulav*. *Le'atuyei katan hayode'a lena'ane'a* — from the time a minor knows how to shake the *lulav* properly, performing the correct motion. *Detnan: katan hayode'a lena'ane'a chayav belulav*. Once the minor can do this correctly, he is obligated, at least as a Rabbinic obligation of *chinuch*.

### Chinuch Education

This series of rulings raises a fundamental question about the nature of *chinuch*: who exactly is obligated? The conventional understanding is that *chinuch* is an obligation on the parents — the father, with debate among the *acharonim* about whether the mother shares this obligation. Yet the phrasing used consistently in these *Mishnaiot* — *katan chayav* — suggests that it is the minor himself who is obligated, which raises a conceptual paradox: how can one who is excluded from obligation be Rabbinically obligated? Some nonetheless maintain that the *chinuch* obligation is framed as applying to the minor, not only to the parent, though this remains a point of terminological and conceptual interest.

### Hakol B'Tzitzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בְּצִיצִית״ — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קָטָן הַיּוֹדֵעַ לְהִתְעַטֵּף, דְּתַנְיָא: קָטָן הַיּוֹדֵעַ לְהִתְעַטֵּף — חַיָּיב בְּצִיצִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask similar questions: **What is added** by the statement of a *baraita*: **Everyone is obligated in** the mitzva of **ritual fringes?** The Gemara explains that this serves **to add a minor who knows** how **to wrap himself** in a garment. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A minor who knows** how **to wrap himself** in a garment **is obligated in** the mitzva of **ritual fringes.**

*Hakol chayavin betzitzit* — everyone is obligated in *tzitzit*. *Le'atuyei katan hayode'a lehit'atef* — from the time a minor knows how to wrap himself properly in the garment. *Detanya: katan hayode'a lehit'atef chayav betzitzit*.

### Hakol B'Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בִּתְפִילִּין״ — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קָטָן הַיּוֹדֵעַ לִשְׁמוֹר תְּפִלִּין, דְּתַנְיָא: קָטָן הַיּוֹדֵעַ לִשְׁמוֹר תְּפִלִּין — אָבִיו לוֹקֵחַ לוֹ תְּפִלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the ruling of a *baraita*: **Everyone is obligated in** the mitzva of **phylacteries?** The Gemara answers that it serves **to add a minor who knows** how **to preserve** the sanctity of **phylacteries** by maintaining a state of bodily cleanliness. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a minor who knows** how **to preserve** the sanctity of **phylacteries** in a state of cleanliness, **his father purchases phylacteries for him.**

*Hakol chayavin bitefillin* — everyone is obligated in *tefillin*. *Le'atuyei katan hayode'a lishmor tefillin* — from the time a minor knows how to guard his *tefillin* properly, meaning he can be relied upon not to wear them in a place or state that would be disrespectful, and to maintain appropriate bodily conduct while wearing them. The *beraita* formulates this distinctively: *katan hayode'a lishmor tefilav, aviv lokeach lo tefillin* — his father must purchase *tefillin* for him.

### Nature of Mitzvot

The different formulations across these cases are striking, and the *Gemara*'s statements about the age of *chinuch* are frequently cited as reflecting something important about the nature of each *mitzvah* itself. The *lulav* ruling — that a minor is not obligated until he can perform the shaking — indicates that the *na'anu'im*, the act of waving and shaking the *lulav*, is not a mere embellishment but a central component of the *mitzvah*. This is why some *Rishonim* hold that even after one has already taken the *lulav*, if one forgot to recite the *bracha*, one may still do so before the shaking — because the shaking is considered a core act of the *mitzvah*, not an addition to it.

### Tzitzit Wrapping

Similarly, for *tzitzit*, the criterion is not merely wearing a four-cornered garment but *lehit'atef* — the act of wrapping. According to many *Geonim* and *Rishonim*, a garment that is simply worn without any element of *atifah* (wrapping) does not fully constitute the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*. The exact parameters of what constitutes *atifah* — whether the garment must cover the head, drape around the face, or simply envelop the body — are not fully defined. But the principle is that *tzitzit* as a *mitzvah* inheres not just in the wearing of a fringed garment but in wearing it as a wrapped garment. This is reflected in the criterion for a minor's *chinuch* obligation, which speaks not of wearing but of wrapping.

### Tefillin Awareness

The *tefillin* criterion raises yet another question of interpretation. When the *beraita* says *hayode'a lishmor tefilav*, does it mean merely that the child is reliably house-trained — that he will not create a situation of *bizayon* (disrespect) to the *tefillin* by wearing them inappropriately? Or does this *shmirah* reflect something deeper about the *mitzvah* itself — that the consciousness of wearing *tefillin*, the ongoing awareness that God's name, as it were, is upon you, is actually constitutive of the *mitzvah*? There is support for this broader reading: the *Gemara* elsewhere compares *tefillin* to *tzitzit* through the verse *ki shem Hashem nikra alecha*, and there was even a practice of reciting a *bracha* of *lishmor chukav* upon removing the *tefillin*, deriving from *ve'shamarta et hachukkah hazot*, suggesting that the *shmirah* — the ongoing mindfulness — is part of the *kiyum* of the *mitzvah* itself.

The *sukkah* criterion — that the child no longer needs his mother — may be more straightforwardly practical (since women are exempt, a child totally dependent on his mother arguably belongs in the domestic sphere), but it may also reflect the nature of *sukkah* as a *mitzvah* of *teshvu k'ein taduru*, dwelling as one actually dwells. For someone wholly dependent on another, the sense of independently "living in" the *sukkah* is not fully present. The autonomy required by the *sukkah* obligation parallels the *mitzvah*'s conceptual demand for genuine residence.

As for why the *tefillin* criterion shifts formulation — from "he is obligated" to "his father must purchase for him" — *Tosafot* explains this through the economics: *tzitzit* required no separate purchase (one only added fringes to an existing four-cornered garment), and *lulav* and *etrog* could be borrowed (except on the first day) and were relatively affordable. *Tefillin*, by contrast, are expensive, required a personal set since they were worn throughout the day (making borrowing impractical), and therefore the *beraita* needed to specifically instruct the father to make the expenditure. This formulation also reinforces the sense that *chinuch* carries an obligation on the parent, not only on the child.

## Hakol B'Re'iyah

### Re'iyah Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בִּרְאִיָּיה״ — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara further inquires: **What is added** by the statement of the mishna (*Ḥagiga* 2a): **Everyone is obligated in** the mitzva of **appearance,** i.e., the obligation to appear in the Temple and to sacrifice an offering on the three pilgrimage Festivals. The Gemara answers: The mishna serves **to add one who is a half-slave half-freeman,** e.g., a Canaanite slave who was owned jointly, and only one of his owners freed him.

*Hakol chayavin bire'iyah* — everyone is obligated in *re'iyah*, the obligation to appear at the *Beit HaMikdash* on the *regalim* with an *olat re'iyah*. As with the earlier cases, the *hakol* is not truly universal — women are exempt, as are those unable to walk — and so the *Gemara* asks what additional category *hakol* is coming to include.

### Half-Slave Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבִינָא דְּאָמַר: מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין פָּטוּר מִן הָרְאִיָּיה, לְאֵיתוֹיֵי חִיגֵּר בְּיוֹם רִאשׁוֹן וְנִתְפַּשֵּׁט בְּיוֹם שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And according to** the opinion of **Ravina, who said: One who is half-slave half-freeman is exempt from the** mitzva of **appearance** in the Temple, that clause serves **to add** one who was **lame on** the **first day** of the Festival and was unable to travel, and was therefore exempt at the time, **but** who **was healed on** the **second day** of the Festival. This man is obligated to appear in the Temple before the end of the Festival.

A slave is exempt from *re'iyah*, derived from the verse *lifnei ha'adon Hashem* — before your Master, God — which implies that one who has no other master, whose only master is God, is the one obligated. A person who is owned in part has an earthly master as well. Yet the *hakol* includes such a person, because the aspect of freedom in them creates an obligation in *re'iyah*. This is according to the view that holds a half-slave half-free person is obligated.

The *Gemara* then considers: according to Ravina, who holds that the half-slave half-free person is actually exempt from *re'iyah*, what would *hakol* be coming to include? The answer: *le'atuyei chiger beyom rishon venispashet beyom sheni* — a person who was lame on the first day of *Yom Tov* and whose condition healed by the second day. A lame person is exempt from *re'iyah* because the verse speaks of *regalim* — walking feet. But if the person recovered during the festival, are they now obligated to bring an *olat re'iyah* on the remaining days?

### Lame Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּן תַּשְׁלוּמִין זֶה לָזֶה, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: כּוּלָּן תַּשְׁלוּמִין לָרִאשׁוֹן — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **all** seven days of a Festival **rectify one another,** i.e., the obligation to appear applies equally to all days of the Festival, not just the first. Consequently, one who was exempt on the first day is nevertheless obligated on the second day. **But according to the one who says** that the main obligation is on the first day and that **all** the remaining days merely **rectify the first** day, a person who was lame on the first day of the Festival remains exempt throughout the rest of the Festival. If so, **what** does the statement of the mishna in *Ḥagiga* 2a serve **to add?**

This question intersects with a broader debate: are the remaining days of *Yom Tov* each an independent obligation for *re'iyah*, or are they all *tashlumin* — makeups — for day one? If the latter, then someone who was exempt on day one has nothing to make up. If the remaining days are each independently obligating, then a person who became capable on day two would be obligated from that point. The inclusion in *hakol* suggests this person is obligated — but only according to the view that each day carries its own independent *re'iyah* obligation.

### Blind in One Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאֵיתוֹיֵי סוּמָא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו, וּדְלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It serves **to add** one who is **blind in one of his eyes,** and teaches that he is obligated to appear in the Temple, whereas one who is entirely blind is exempt. The Gemara notes: **And** this ruling is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*,** Rabbi Yehuda.

For the view that all subsequent days are only makeups for day one, the *Gemara* suggests *le'atuyei suma be'achat mei'einav* — to include a person blind in one eye. It is established that a fully blind person is exempt from *re'iyah*. The question is whether blindness in one eye also exempts.

### Vision Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, יוֹחָנָן בֶּן דַּהֲבַאי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: סוּמָא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו פָּטוּר מִן הָרְאִיָּיה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִרְאֶה״ ״יֵרָאֶה״, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁבָּא לִרְאוֹת כָּךְ בָּא לֵירָאוֹת, מַה לִּרְאוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו — אַף לֵירָאוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Yoḥanan ben Dahavai says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda:** One who is **blind in one of his eyes is exempt from the** mitzva of **appearance, as it is stated:** "Three times in the year all your males shall appear [*yera'eh*] before the Lord God" (Exodus 23:17). According to the way in which the verse is written, without vocalization, it can be read as ***yireh*,** meaning: **Shall see,** instead of ***yera'eh*,** meaning: **Shall appear.** This teaches that **in the** same **manner that one comes to see, so he comes to appear,** i.e., to be seen: **Just as** the usual way **to see** is **with both of one's eyes, so too,** the obligation **to appear** applies only to one who comes **with** the sight of **both his eyes.** This is one possible explanation for what is added by the general statement of the mishna in *Ḥagiga* 2a, according to Ravina.

The source for exempting a blind person is a *drasha* on the word *yeira'eh* in the verse *shalosh pe'amim bashanah yeira'eh kol zechurcha et pnei Adonai Hashem*. The grammatical structure of the verse is notable: *et pnei Adonai* is the object of the verb, meaning the verse literally reads "all your males shall see the face of God" — *yireh*, active, not *yeira'eh*, passive. The *mesorah* vocalizes it in the passive (*yeira'eh* — shall be seen), but the *midrash* does not abandon the active reading. On the basis of this, the *drasha* connects seeing with being seen: *keshem sheba lirot bishtei einav, af leira'ot bishtei einav* — just as one appears before God and is fully "seen," one must come with full sight. The underlying *peshat* of the verse is about the person seeing God, and the *halacha* derives that full binocular vision is required.

Rabbi Yehuda accordingly holds that even someone blind in one eye is exempt, since the *mitzvah* requires appearing with the fullness of sight. The *hakol* comes to include the contrary position: that sight in one eye is sufficient to establish the *re'iyah* obligation. Alternatively, the *Gemara* offers, *le'olam l'atuyei mi shechetzyo eved v'chetzyo ben chorin* — we return to the half-slave case — and the apparent difficulty from Ravina is resolved differently.

### Tikkun Olam Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ דְּרָבִינָא — לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּמִשְׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, כָּאן בְּמִשְׁנָה אַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually,** that statement serves **to include one who is half-slave and half-freeman. And** with regard to **what was difficult** for you **according to** the opinion **of Ravina,** that he exempts such a person from the obligation of appearance, it is **not difficult: Here** the ruling is **in** accordance with **the initial** version of the **mishna,** whereas **there** it is **in** accordance with **the ultimate** version of the **mishna.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, עוֹבֵד אֶת רַבּוֹ יוֹם אֶחָד, וְאֶת עַצְמוֹ יוֹם אֶחָד, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 88a): **One who is half-slave and half-freeman serves his master one day,** as he is half a slave, **and** works for **himself one day,** since he is half free. This is **the statement of Beit Hillel.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: תִּיקַּנְתֶּם אֶת רַבּוֹ, וְאֶת עַצְמוֹ לֹא תִּיקַּנְתֶּם? לִישָּׂא שִׁפְחָה אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל, בַּת חוֹרִין אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל, יִבָּטֵל? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם אֶלָּא לִפְרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֹהוּ בְרָאָהּ לָשֶׁבֶת יְצָרָהּ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai said to them: You have** thereby **remedied** the situation of **his master,** who fully derives benefit from all his rights to the slave, **but you have not remedied his own** situation. How so? **He cannot marry a maidservant,** as half of him is free, and a free Jew may not marry a Canaanite maidservant. **He is** also **unable** to marry **a free woman,** as half of him is still a slave, and a Jewish woman may not marry a Canaanite slave. And if you say **he should be idle,** i.e., refrain from marrying, **but isn't it** true that **the world was created only for procreation, as it is stated:** "For so said the Lord that created the heavens…Who formed the earth and made it, He established it. **He did not create it to be a waste; He formed it to be inhabited"** (Isaiah 45:18)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם, כּוֹפִין אֶת רַבּוֹ וְעוֹשֶׂה אוֹתוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, וְכוֹתֵב שְׁטָר עַל חֲצִי דָּמָיו, וְחָזְרוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל לְהוֹרוֹת כְּדִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, for the betterment of the world,** i.e., so that the slave will be able to engage in procreation, the court **forces his master to make him a freeman** by emancipating the half that he owns. **And** the slave **writes a bill to** his master accepting responsibility to pay **half his value** to him over time, as currently he has no property with which to redeem himself. **And Beit Hillel** ultimately **retracted** their opinion, **to rule in accordance with the statement of Beit Shammai** that a half-slave must be emancipated. The ruling of the mishna that a half-slave must appear in the Temple is in accordance with this opinion, which holds that the master must free him. Ravina's statement that he is not obligated to appear in the Temple is in accordance with the initial mishna, according to which Beit Hillel held that the master is not forced to free the half-slave.

The *Gemara* explains: *kan b'mishnah rishonah, kan b'mishnah acharonah* — the two positions about the half-slave half-free person reflect an earlier and later stage of *halacha*. The *Mishnah* records a dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*. *Beit Hillel*'s original view was that the half-slave half-free person simply alternates — working for the master one day and for himself the next. *Beit Shammai* objected: *tikantam et rabo* — you have been very good to the master, *v'et atzmo lo tikantam* — but you have done nothing for the slave. The problem: such a person can marry neither a slave woman (since he is half-free) nor a free woman (since he is half-slave), and *v'halo lo nivra ha'olam ela l'piryah v'rivyah* — the world was created for *pru u'rvu*, for procreation and settlement. The verse *lo tohu bera'ah, lashevet yetzarah* — God did not create the world for desolation, He created it to be settled — mandates that this untenable situation be corrected. *Kofin et rabbo ve'oseh oto ben chorin* — the master is compelled to free the slave; the slave will write an IOU for half his value. And *Beit Hillel* reversed themselves and ruled in accordance with *Beit Shammai* — which is itself striking, since it is usually *Beit Hillel* who champions the human dimension.

The practical consequence for *re'iyah*: in the earlier stage, when we permitted the half-and-half status to continue, such a person was exempt from *re'iyah*. In the later stage, once it is established that the master must be compelled to free the slave, the person is considered effectively free even before formal emancipation — and therefore obligated in *re'iyah*.

## Hakol B'Shofar

### Shofar Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִין בִּתְקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר״ — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קָטָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְחִינּוּךְ, דִּתְנַן: אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַקָּטָן מִלִּתְקוֹעַ בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the statement of the *baraita*: **Everyone is obligated to sound the shofar?** The Gemara answers: This serves **to add a minor who reached** the age of **training** in mitzvot. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Rosh HaShana* 32b): **One** need **not prevent minors from sounding** the shofar **on** the **festival** of Rosh HaShana, despite the fact that they are not obligated in mitzvot.

*Hakol chayavin bitkiat shofar* — everyone is obligated in the blowing of the *shofar*. It is somewhat unexpected that this appears here rather than alongside the other daily and seasonal ritual *mitzvot* discussed earlier, but the *Gemara* proceeds in its own order. *Le'atuyei katan shehigia lechinuch* — to include a minor who has reached the stage of *chinuch*.

### Chinuch for Shofar

What is notable here is that the *Gemara* does not give a specific criterion — it does not say "one who can blow" or "one who can understand the sound," as earlier cases specified the operative ability. It simply says *shehigia lechinuch* in a general, abstract way. The *Mishnah* teaches: *ein me'akvin et hakatan mitlkoa beyom tov* — one does not prevent a minor from blowing *shofar* on *Yom Tov*. And *Tosafot* notes that another *Mishnah* speaks of actively blowing for minors on *Yom Tov*, and the *Gemara* in *Rosh Hashanah* reconciles the two by limiting the active practice to minors who have reached the age of *chinuch*. The full resolution of those two *Mishnaiot* is treated in *Rosh Hashanah*, and the discussion here is merely an allusion to that broader debate.

### Individual Mitzvah Standards

*Rashi* cites a *Gemara* suggesting that the age of *chinuch* is around eight or nine, or nine or ten. *Tosafot* objects sharply: *velo nehira dehatam bechinuch katan lehit'anot ka'amar* — that *Gemara* speaks of *chinuch* in the context of fasting, not of other *mitzvot*. *Aval bishe'ar mitzvot kol echad ve'echad lefi darko* — for other *mitzvot*, each one is measured by its own standard, *kedashkachan le'eil gabei lulav vesukkah utfillin* — as we have seen above with *lulav*, *sukkah*, and *tefillin*, each of which has its own operative criterion. The age of *chinuch* is not one uniform threshold but a *mitzvah*-specific judgment about when the child can perform the act meaningfully and properly.

Why, then, does *shofar* not specify a concrete criterion? Because *shofar* — at least in the sense of the obligation to hear it — is passive. Hearing requires no physical skill that can be tested and defined the way shaking, wrapping, or guarding can be. The capacity for *chinuch* in hearing *shofar* is therefore a more abstract question of awareness and understanding, harder to pin to a concrete behavioral marker. The *Gemara* accordingly leaves it at the general *shehigia lechinuch* without further specification, noting that the full discussion belongs in *Masechet Rosh Hashanah*.
