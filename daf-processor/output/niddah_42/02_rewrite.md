# Niddah 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Poletet Shichvat Zera

### Interior vs. Exterior

The daf opens at the very bottom of 41b and moves into a halakhically rich discussion concerning the *tumah* of a *poletet shichvat zera* — a woman from whose body semen is flowing. The conceptual backdrop is the fundamental distinction between how *tumah* attaches to men and women respectively in the domain of *re'iyah*. For a woman, the paradigmatic flow-based *tumah* operates *be-vasara* — blood flows internally, within her body. For a man, it is precisely the opposite: the Torah specifies *asher tetze mimenu shichvat zera* — the semen must exit from him. The case of *poletet shichvat zera* sits uncomfortably between these two poles. A man has deposited semen in the woman's body; that semen is now flowing within her and eventually exiting her body. The question is which model governs.

### Chachamim vs. R. Shimon

The *Chachamim* hold that this case follows the paradigm of a woman's *tumah*: even the internal flow is sufficient to render her *teme'ah*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He maintains that since the *tumah* here is fundamentally the *tumah* of semen, it follows the male paradigm — *ad she-tetze tumat-ha lachutz* — she is not *teme'ah* until the *tumah* exits her body. This produces a hybrid category: a woman carrying a *tumah* of semen. The debate is whether it tracks the woman's model (interior flow suffices) or the man's model (external exit is required).

### Tosafot on Flow Path

Tosafot adds an important qualification. Even according to the *Chachamim*, the relevant flow is not merely any movement of semen within the vaginal canal. It must replicate the reality of blood-flow — that is, the semen must have first entered the uterus and then flowed from the uterus into the vaginal canal. It is that trajectory, and not simple presence in the vaginal canal, which triggers the *tumah* according to the *Chachamim*. Tosafot appears to presuppose the same for Rabbi Shimon's framework as well, even as Rabbi Shimon requires the additional step of external exit.

### Gemara's Question

The *Gemara* now raises an internal tension. We have a *baraita* in which Rabbi Shimon himself teaches that the *chiddush* of the Torah is that this woman is *teme'ah* even internally — even though under ordinary rules, contact via *beit hastarim* (an interior, hidden surface) does not transmit *tumah*. How, then, can Rabbi Shimon simultaneously say that the *tumah* requires external exit? If he concedes internal *tumah*, why insist on exterior flow?

### Meshameshet vs. Poletet

The *Gemara* resolves this with a clean distinction: *lo kashya — kan bi-meshameshet, kan be-poletet*. There are two separate categories of *tumah* here. The Torah's explicit *tumah* of a woman who has had intercourse — the *meshameshet* — is a *tumah* that attaches to her through the act of sex itself, not through subsequent flow. Even where nothing has yet exited her body, the fact of having had relations while the man ejaculated creates *tumah*. It is regarding *this* category that Rabbi Shimon affirms interior *tumah*: this is not a flow-*tumah*, it is a status-*tumah* stemming from the act itself. The requirement that the *tumah* exit the body applies only to the second category — *poletet* — where, a day or more after intercourse, semen is still flowing from her body. That is a different *tumah*, not the explicit Torah *tumah* of *meshameshet*, and it is here that Rabbi Shimon insists on the male model of external exit.

## Three Days Emission

### Rava's Three Days

The *Gemara* then presses: if Rabbi Shimon says that a *meshameshet* completes her *tumah* by nightfall after immersing in the *mikvah*, does that mean she could eat *terumah* that evening? The *Gemara* objects from a ruling of Rava: *meshameshet kol sheloshah yamim assurah le-echol bi-terumah* — a woman who has had intercourse cannot eat *terumah* for three days. The reason is *she-efshar lah she-lo tiflot* — it is impossible that semen will not be flowing from her body at some point over those three days. Why three days specifically? Because semen is considered halakhically potent for approximately seventy-two hours — the outer limit discussed in *Shabbat* and elsewhere, combining biological viability and halakhic significance. Therefore, Rava holds that she is perpetually at risk of emitting semen during that window, and she can never be assured of being *tehorah* long enough to eat *terumah*.

### Mikvah Problem

This raises a practical difficulty. If semen is always flowing from her body throughout the three-day period, how could there ever be a scenario in which she immerses in the *mikvah* and the semen has *not* yet come out — which is the scenario Rabbi Shimon seemingly requires to distinguish the two categories?

### Lying in Bed Case

The *Gemara* answers: *hacha be-mai askinan — de-tavluha be-mitah*. The case under discussion is one where she was immersed in the *mikvah* while lying horizontal in her bed, never having stood up after intercourse. When a woman remains lying flat, gravity does not assist in the expulsion of semen; it is the act of standing and walking that causes emission. If she was immersed without ever rising, the semen has remained within her body, and she emerges from the *mikvah tehorah* with respect to the *meshameshet tumah* — even as the semen has not yet exited. Rabbi Shimon's second category (*poletet*) would then only apply when she subsequently stands up and the semen begins to exit.

### Turning vs. Walking

The *Gemara* then draws out the implications of Rava's ruling: *mi-khlal de-chi ka-amar Rava — de-azla be-char'ah* — Rava's statement that emission is inevitable must be referring to a woman who walks upright. But then a further difficulty arises: *dilma be-hadi de-ka azla shaditah* — perhaps when she walks, the semen exits all at once immediately, and by the time she reaches the *mikvah* she is already clear. Why does Rava assume it continues to flow over three full days? The *Gemara* considers whether Rava might mean only that emission is *possible* — but then objects that Rava's language (*she-efshar lah she-lo tiflot*, it is impossible she will not emit) is too absolute for that reading. The *Gemara* resolves: *ela le-Rava nami de-tavluha be-mitah* — Rava too is speaking of the horizontal immersion case. The three-day emission occurs when she is horizontal but *turning over* — *mit'hafechet*.

### Rashi's Analysis

Rashi explains the distinction as follows. If she lies completely still and horizontal throughout, it is possible that nothing flows at all for three days — making the *mikvah* effective. If she walks, everything may exit at once immediately. Rava's scenario — where semen continues to flow for the full three days — describes the case where she was horizontal during the *mikvah* immersion but then spent those three days turning from side to side in bed while remaining horizontal. In that case, the semen exits gradually over the full three days, not all at once, and she can never be confident she is clear. Rashi also records an alternative reading involving whether or not she was moving *during* intercourse itself, which would affect whether the semen lodges in the uterus or not — but the primary reading follows the analysis above.

### Torah's Case

The *Gemara* adds that Rava is not presenting this as practical halakha for common situations. Rather, *le-Rava akra kai* — Rava is explaining a *pasuk*. When the Torah says *ve-rachatz besaro ba-mayim ve-taher le-et erev* — that they may immerse and be *tahor* by nightfall — it is describing a case where she is *she-einah mit'hafechet*, not turning over while horizontal. That specific condition is when the Torah's promise of *taharah* by evening applies. *Aval be-mit'hafechet* — if she is turning while horizontal — then for the full three days she cannot eat *terumah*, since emission is certain. If she is walking, the situation falls somewhere between these poles, though the *Gemara* does not fully address that intermediate case.

## Ro'ah vs. Noga'at

### Defining Status

Having established the basic framework of *poletet shichvat zera*, the *Gemara* turns to a penetrating question posed by Rav Shmuel bar Bisna to Abaye. We know she is *teme'ah* — that is established. But what is the conceptual basis of that *tumah*? *Ro'ah havya o noga'at havya* — is she *teme'ah* as a *ro'ah*, or as a *noga'at*? A *ro'ah* is one whose *tumah* derives from an internal physiological occurrence — the paradigm of blood, which attaches to her personal status. A *noga'at* is one who becomes *teme'ah* by contacting an external *tamei* substance and conducting its *tumah*. In the case of *poletet*, the semen is external material — the man's — that has entered her body and is now flowing out. Is her *tumah* about *her* body and what is happening to *it*, or is it about the external substance she is in contact with?

### Three Implications

The *Gemara* delineates three practical ramifications of this question. The first is *listor* — whether the emission of semen interrupts her count of *shiv'ah neki'im* (seven clean days). If she is a *ro'ah*, then this is a bodily occurrence akin to a flow of blood, and it would interrupt her clean days just as a drop of blood would. If she is a *noga'at*, touching an external substance does not interrupt the clean count, even if it causes one-day *tumah*. The second is *bi-mashehu* — minimum quantity. If her *tumah* is categorized as *ro'ah*, there is no minimum threshold; any amount of the relevant substance suffices, just as any blood causes *tumah*. If she is a *noga'at*, the external substance must meet a minimum halakhic quantity to transmit *tumah*. The third is *bifnim ki-vachutz* — interior equivalence to exterior. The *tumah* of a *ro'ah* operates even when the flow is internal, replicating the female paradigm of blood. If she is a *noga'at*, the *tumah* of an external substance requires external contact with the body.

### Baraita Answer

The *Gemara* observes that if Rav Shmuel bar Bisna was familiar with the *baraita* recording the debate between *Chachamim* and Rabbi Shimon, the answer should have been immediately apparent. According to the *Chachamim*, who say she is *teme'ah* even when the flow is internal, she is plainly a *ro'ah* — the semen behaves like white blood, an internal bodily occurrence. According to Rabbi Shimon, who insists on external exit, the implication is that she is a *noga'at* — it is about contact with an external substance, which is why external exit is required. The *baraita* thus appears to present the *ro'ah/noga'at* debate as the very substance of the *Chachamim/Rabbi Shimon* dispute.

### Logical Conclusion

If, on the other hand, Rav Shmuel bar Bisna was reasoning without the text, the *Gemara* concludes: *mistanbra noga'at havya* — logically, she ought to be a *noga'at*. The semen is not her body's own production; it is an external substance introduced from without. It does not stem from her own physiological processes. One would naturally categorize her *tumah* as conducting *tumah* from an external source rather than as an intrinsic bodily occurrence. The question then is: if both routes — text and logic — point toward answers (though different ones for *Chachamim* and Rabbi Shimon on the textual side, and toward *noga'at* on the logical side), what was Rav Shmuel bar Bisna actually asking?

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between the *versions* of the question. In one version (*ika d'amri*), he knew the *baraita* but was asking specifically according to Rabbi Shimon. He conceded that according to Rabbi Shimon the *tumah* requires external exit — that much settles the *bifnim ki-vachutz* question in Rabbi Shimon's view. But he still wanted to know: does Rabbi Shimon's position also determine the other two implications — *listor* and *bi-mashehu*? Perhaps Rabbi Shimon holds that she is still a *ro'ah* in those respects, because the language *dayo ke-vo'alah* (she is equivalent to the man she had relations with) means she follows the male model of *ro'eh*, not the model of *noga'at*. A man who emits semen is a *ro'eh* — his *tumah* is intrinsic and bodily, not merely because he contacted an external substance. So too, perhaps, the woman according to Rabbi Shimon remains a *ro'ah* even though the *tumah* requires external exit, which would preserve the implications of smallest quantity and interruption of clean days.

In the second version (*ika d'amri*), he did not know the *baraita* and was reasoning from logic. His question then was: logically, she should be a *noga'at* — but could there be a reason to say *ro'ah*? The *Gemara* presents the argument from *Ma'amad Har Sinai* as the possible counter-consideration, which is explored in the next section.

Rashi adds an illuminating note: the *Chachamim* derive the *tumah* of a woman through whom semen flows from the *pasuk* describing blood-flow — *ve-dam yihyeh zovah bi-vsharah*. They learn it from the word *yihyeh*, embedding the semen's *tumah* directly within the *parsha* of a woman's blood-flow. This textual grounding firmly positions the *Chachamim*'s view within the *ro'ah* framework.

## Ma'amad Har Sinai

### Three Days Before Torah

The *Gemara* now raises the episode of *Ma'amad Har Sinai* as a possible proof that a *poletet shichvat zera* is a *ro'ah*. Before the giving of the Torah, Moshe was commanded *al tigshu el isha* — the nation was to abstain from marital relations for three days. The question naturally arises: why three days? One day would have sufficed to purify the men from *ba'al keri*.

### Three Days Explained

*Chazal* answer that the three-day period was not primarily for the men's benefit but for the women's. If couples had relations up to three days before *Ma'amad Har Sinai*, the women would still be emitting semen at the moment of the revelation. The three-day interval was required to ensure that all the women would have finished *poletet shichvat zera* by the time they stood at Sinai.

### Women Must Be Tahor

The significance of this, the *Gemara* suggests, is in the nature of the disqualification. The Torah's concern was not simply *tumah* in the abstract. A *zav* or a *metzora*, who are *ro'im* in the most intense sense, were apparently permitted at *Har Sinai*. The issue at *Ma'amad Har Sinai* was not halakhic *tumah* per se but rather the experiential dimension of the *ro'ah* — the rootedness in the physical, sexual reality. A *poletet* who is merely a *noga'at* — someone who has incidentally contacted an external *tamei* substance — would carry no such experiential weight. The Torah's concern for the *poletet* at *Har Sinai* would then imply that she is a *ro'ah* in the fuller sense, because it is the *ro'ah* status that carries the experiential, personal significance that was deemed incompatible with standing at Sinai to receive the Torah.

### Special Case Objection

However, the *Gemara* resists drawing a firm halakhic conclusion from this. *O dilma lo gemarinan mi-Sinai — de-chiddush hu*. The entire requirement at *Ma'amad Har Sinai* may have been a *chiddush* — a unique, unrepeatable context — and not a model for ordinary halakhic categorization. Proof: *ba-zavim u-metzora'im de-chamiri ve-lo achmir behu Rachmana* — *zavim* and *metzora'im* are *ro'im* of the most serious order, and yet the Torah did not exclude them from *Har Sinai*. This shows that the disqualification at *Har Sinai* was not about the formal halakhic category of *ro'ah* at all, but rather about the specific experience of recent sexual contact. Whether one is formally classified as a *ro'ah* or a *noga'at* may therefore be entirely untouched by the *Har Sinai* evidence.

## Consensus Ro'ah

### Abaye Ruling

Rav Shmuel bar Bisna brought his question to Abaye, who answered simply: *ro'eh havya* — she is a *ro'ah*.

### Rava Ruling

Unsatisfied with a single opinion, he sought a second. He asked Rava (some texts read *Rabbah*, Abaye's own teacher), who likewise ruled: *ro'eh havya* — she is a *ro'ah*.

### Rav Yosef Ruling

He then went to Rav Yosef, of the previous generation, who gave the same ruling: *ro'eh havya*.

### Repetitive Answers

Rav Shmuel bar Bisna returned to Abaye in frustration. *Kulchu be-ruka chada tepitu* — you have all spat the same spit. Every authority gave him the identical answer with no elaboration, no divergence, nothing of substance. He wanted either different opinions or at least an explanation. *Shapir amri lach*, Abaye replied — we told you well. The answer is unanimous, and that unanimity itself is the message.

### Abaye Clarifies

Abaye then articulates the reasoning. *Ad kan lo ka-amar Rabbi Shimon dayah ke-va'alah ela le-tamei mi-bifnim ke-mi-vachutz* — Rabbi Shimon's ruling that she is like the man she had intercourse with applies solely to the question of *bifnim ki-vachutz*: the *tumah* requires external exit, as it does for a man. That one halakhic detail follows the male paradigm. But it does not follow that she is a *noga'at* in the broader sense. The mechanism of assimilating her to the man — *dayo ke-vo'alah* — is itself the language of *ro'ah*: a man's seminal emission is a *ro'ah* experience, and applying that model to the woman makes her a *ro'ah* as well. Therefore, even according to Rabbi Shimon, with respect to *listor* (interrupting clean days) and *bi-mashehu* (smallest quantity), she is a *ro'ah*, not a *noga'at*. All three *Amoraim* assumed this as self-evident.

## Same Spit Metaphor

### Spit Metaphor

The metaphor *kulchu be-ruka chada tepitu* — you have all spat the same spit — is striking and, in this context, more than coincidentally apt. The image describes giving back what you received from someone else rather than generating an original teaching of your own. But here, the very *sugya* is about external liquid originating in another person flowing back out through the body of the one who received it. The metaphor of shared spit — liquid from an external source being expelled — resonates with the substance of the halakhic discussion itself. There may be a deliberate subtextual resonance in the *Gemara*'s choice of this particular idiom at this juncture.

### Day Six Rule

The halakhic stakes of this conclusion are significant for contemporary practice. If a *poletet shichvat zera* is a *ro'ah*, then the emission of semen interrupts the *shiv'ah neki'im*. This means that a woman cannot begin counting her seven clean days until she is confident that semen is no longer being emitted. Given Rava's ruling that for a woman who is ambulatory and turning about, emission continues over three full days, she cannot begin counting until after that three-day period has passed. Since the bleeding itself typically begins to be counted from day one, and three days of potential semen emission are added, the earliest she could begin counting — under this framework — becomes day five after the onset of bleeding, and with additional considerations, day six.

### Ashkenazi vs. Sefardi

There is a practical divergence between *Ashkenazi* and *Sefardi* custom on this point. According to many *Sefardi Rishonim*, including the Ramban, if a woman walks after intercourse the semen exits all at once and she need not wait the full three days; some hold that a thorough internal washing — as mentioned in a *Mishna* in *Mikva'ot* — is sufficient to ensure the semen has been fully expelled. On this basis, *Sefardi* practice does not require waiting until day six in all cases. *Ashkenazi* practice, however, maintains a more stringent posture: we are always concerned that some semen may remain, and through a combination of *lo plug* (not distinguishing between cases) and the concern for error in calculating the onset of bleeding vis-à-vis *bein hashmashot*, the standard practice is that *Ashkenazi* women do not begin counting until day six. All of these practical rulings trace their origin to the very discussions of this *Gemara*.

## Yoledet Tumah

### Four Types Listed

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita*: *ha-niddah ve-ha-zavah ve-ha-shomeret yom ke-neged yom ve-ha-yoledet* — the *niddah*, the *zavah*, the *shomeret yom ke-neged yom*, and the *yoledet* — all of them cause *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz*, meaning that their *tumah* operates even when the flow is internal, just as it does when the flow exits the body.

### Yoledet Question

The first three are readily understood: they are all defined by flows of blood, and the principle that interior flow causes *tumah* follows directly from the analysis developed above. But the *yoledet* presents a conceptual difficulty. A *yoledet* is a woman after childbirth — her *tumah* status arises from the act of giving birth, not from a contemporaneous flow. What does it mean to say that a *yoledet* causes *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz*? What interior flow is in question?

### Mikvah Entry

The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzricha di-leides she-yareda litbol mi-tumah le-taharah* — the case under discussion is a *yoledet* who descends to the *mikvah* at the conclusion of her *tumah* period (night seven after the birth of a son, or night fourteen after the birth of a daughter) in order to transition from her days of *tumah* to her days of *taharah*. The statement that a *yoledet* causes *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz* is about what happens in the course of that immersion. As Rabbi Zeira taught in the name of Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav:

### Blood Timing

If, as the *yoledet* descends into the *mikvah*, blood flows from her uterus into her vaginal canal — *be-yerida* — before she has completed the immersion, then even after she emerges she is *teme'ah*, because that blood predated her *taharah* and was not purified by the *mikvah*. If, however, the blood emerged only *after* she ascended from the *mikvah* — *va-aliya tehorah* — she is *tehorah*, since the immersion preceded the flow. This reflects the position of *Beit Hillel*, who hold that blood does not become *tahor* simply because the *tumah* days have concluded; it requires immersion in a *mikvah*. On that view, blood that flowed during the *tumah* period and was still present in the vaginal canal at the time of immersion was not purified by that immersion, because the *mikvah* takes effect going forward, not retroactively on blood already present in the body.

## Blood Inside Body

### Yirmiyah's Query

Rabbi Yirmiyah now poses a sharp question to Rabbi Zeira regarding this teaching. The case is one of blood that has flowed from the uterus into the vaginal canal but has not yet exited the body. Rabbi Yirmiyah asks: why does this blood make her *teme'ah*? It is *tumah beluah* — swallowed *tumah*, absorbed within the body — and such *tumah* is halakhically inert.

### Beluah Concept

Two distinct principles limit the transmission of *tumah* through internal contact. The first is *beit hastarim*: contact with an interior, hidden surface of the body does not transmit *tumah* in the way that external contact does. The second, and more radical, is *beluah*: when a *tamei* substance is so thoroughly absorbed or enclosed within a body that it has effectively ceased to exist as an independent object. The paradigmatic case is a dead fetus still enclosed in its mother's womb — it does not cause *tum'at ohel* to those in the house, because it is *balua*, swallowed within her body. Similarly, Rabbi Yirmiyah argues, blood sitting in the vaginal canal prior to immersion is both *beit hastarim* (interior, hidden from contact) and *beluah* (absorbed within the body). If it is *beluah*, it should not cause *tumah* at all — so why does its presence before the *mikvah* matter?

### Rabbi Abin's Answer

Rabbi Zeira directed Rabbi Yirmiyah to Rabbi Abin, with whom Rabbi Zeira had previously worked through this question. Rabbi Abin's answer: *asuha ke-neveilas of tahor she-metamah begadim be-veit ha-blia* — we treat it analogously to the carcass of a *tahor* bird, which causes *tumah* to garments in *beit ha-blia*, the throat. The dead body of a kosher bird, unlike that of an animal, causes *tumah* specifically in the act of being swallowed — while it is in the throat. This is an instance of Torah-mandated *tumah* from something that is, by all logic, *beluah* and yet still transmits *tumah*. Similarly, the *Rabbanan* may have imposed *tumah* on the blood in the vaginal canal even though it is *beluah*.

Rabbi Yirmiyah pushes back on this comparison: *hasam ein lah tumah ba-chutz* — there is no *tumah* for the bird carcass when it is *outside* the body. Its only *tumah* is in the throat, while being swallowed. *Hacha ki nafik le-vara metamei* — here, the blood *does* cause *tumah* when it exits the body. Why would the *Rabbanan* need to establish *tumah* already while it is inside, when it will cause *tumah* on its own once it exits? The bird carcass analogy fails precisely because the bird's *tumah* is *only* internal, whereas the blood's *tumah* will be fully operative externally. There is no halakhic vacuum that would motivate the Sages to extend the blood's *tumah* inward.

### Exits Body Tamei

The *Gemara* resolves by reconceiving the entire teaching: *hacha nami ke-she-yatza la-chutz* — the blood in the *yoledet*'s vaginal canal is *tamei* only when it exits the body. The statement that the *yoledet* causes *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz* does not mean that the blood inside her body makes her *teme'ah* while it is still inside. Rather, it means that the *mikvah* she immersed in did not purify that blood. When that blood eventually exits her body, it will still be *tamei* — as though it had never been purified — because immersion in the *mikvah* does not reach and purify blood that is already lodged in the vaginal canal. Inside her body, the blood is indeed *beluah*, and causes no *tumah*. But the *mikvah* does not render it *tahor* either.

## Mikvah Effect

### Mikvah Purification

A natural question follows: if the *mikvah* purifies the *yoledet* and transitions her to her *taharah* days, why does it not simultaneously purify the blood that is already present in her vaginal canal? After all, the *mikvah* evidently does purify the blood sitting in her uterus — for blood that flows during the subsequent thirty-three days of *taharah* is *tahor* blood, which presupposes that the *mikvah* somehow acted on the blood already in the uterus.

### Mikvah Limits

The *Gemara* addresses this asymmetry. The *mikvah* acts prospectively on the woman's status and on blood that will subsequently flow from the uterus; it does not reach back to purify blood that is already in the vaginal canal. The blood in the uterus becomes *tahor* going forward because the woman's transition to *taharah* through the *mikvah* resets the status of all future uterine output. But the blood already present in the vaginal canal at the moment of immersion occupies a different position: it is not blood that flows *after* the *mikvah*, it is pre-existing blood that the *mikvah* water could not affect.

### Baraita Meaning

The *baraita*'s statement that a *yoledet* causes *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz* is therefore not a teaching about blood making her *teme'ah* while inside her body. It is a teaching about the limits of the *mikvah*: the immersion does not purify pre-existing blood in the vaginal canal, and that blood, when it exits, will cause *tumah*. The *baraita* is telling us something about the *mikvah*'s reach and limits, not about active *tumah* from interior blood.

## Fetus Birth Status

### Dry Birth Question

Having concluded the analysis of the *yoledet* in the blood context, the *Gemara* turns back to the original challenge: the *baraita* says a *yoledet* causes *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz*, but we have just shown that interior blood does not make her *teme'ah* — and if she bleeds, the *tumah* is either *niddah* or *zavah tumah*, not *yoledet tumah* specifically. So for what halakhic scenario does the *baraita*'s statement about *yoledet* and *bifnim ki-vachutz* actually apply? The *Gemara* introduces the case of *leida yevishta* — a dry childbirth, one without blood. In such a case, no blood is at issue. The question of *bifnim ki-vachutz* must therefore be about the fetus itself: at what point does the fetus's emergence render the woman a *yoledet*, and does that status attach even before the fetus has fully exited the body?

### Head Exits Prozdor

The *Gemara* answers: *eika ke-gon she-hotzi vlad rosho chutz la-prozdor* — the relevant moment is when the fetus's head exits the *prozdor*, the anatomical antechamber. Once the head has passed the *prozdor* — even though the remainder of the fetus is still inside the body — she becomes a *yoledet* and the *tumah* attaches. This is the meaning of *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz* in the case of the *yoledet*: the fetus triggers her status as a *yoledet* even while still largely interior, because the head's emergence from the *prozdor* is the operative moment of birth.

### Rav Oshaya

This ruling connects to a teaching of Rav Oshaya cited in *Chullin*, in the context of a midwife assisting in a stillbirth. Even if no one observed the fetus's head exit, we are concerned — *gezeirah shema yotzi vlad rosho chutz la-prozdor* — that the head may have crossed the threshold of the *prozdor* without being noticed. Once that moment occurs, the child is considered born, the *yoledet* has touched it, and she becomes *tuma'at meit*. Rav Oshaya's ruling anchors the principle: the *prozdor* crossing is the definitive moment of birth for purposes of *tumah*.

### Shabbos Birth Story

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a story. A man came before Rava and asked whether he could perform a *bris milah* on *Shabbos* — apparently a straightforward question, since a *bris* on day eight overrides *Shabbos*. Rava said yes. But after the man left, Rava reconsidered: surely any man knows that a *bris* on day eight overrides *Shabbos* — why would he even ask? Something in the question did not fit. Rava ran after the man to hear the full story. The man explained: *shama'is velad de-tzavyat apanya de-mallei shabsa* — he had heard the fetus crying in utero as it was being born on *erev Shabbos*, but the actual birth — in the conventional sense — did not occur until *Shabbos*. He wanted to know which day counted as the day of birth.

### Rava's Conclusion

Rava ruled decisively: *hai hotzi rosho chutz la-prozdor hu* — the fact that the fetus could be heard crying demonstrates that its head had already exited the *prozdor* on *erev Shabbos*. That is the moment of birth. The *bris milah* would therefore fall on day nine, after *Shabbos*, and *va-hava milah she-lo bi-zmana ein mechalelin aleha et ha-Shabbos* — a *bris* performed past its proper time does not override *Shabbos*. This ruling confirms that exit from the *prozdor*, not emergence from the body, is the operative moment of *yoledet* status and of birth for purposes of *milah* timing.

## Prozdor Status

### Interior Exterior

This ruling raises a substantial question that occupies the *Acharonim* at length. All of our analysis assumes that exiting the *prozdor* is a *bifnim* event — interior — which is why the *baraita* states that a *yoledet* causes *tumah bifnim ki-vachutz*. But if the *prozdor* is already interior, what exactly is its anatomical location, and where does it stand relative to the external opening of the body?

### Conflicting Sources

The difficulty is that virtually every other source in *Shas* that discusses when a child is considered born — whether in the context of inheritance, *Shabbos*, or elsewhere — speaks in terms of the head *exiting the body*: *hotzi ha-vlad rosho*, or *rubbo*, or *pudachto*, the forehead. In all those contexts, the standard assumption is that birth is determined by emergence from the body, not by an internal anatomical threshold. Yet this *Gemara* seems to say that the *prozdor* crossing — which it treats as a *bifnim* event — is the operative moment.

### Acharonim View

Some *Acharonim* resolve this by proposing that the *prozdor* must be located very close to the external opening of the body — so close that once the head has exited the *prozdor*, it is effectively at or near the bodily opening, with perhaps only the very top of the head still not fully emerged. On this reading, the *Gemara*'s statement that the *prozdor* crossing causes *bifnim* tumah means only that some part of the fetus remains inside the body — the rest of the body, or even just the neck — while the head is essentially out. The word *bifnim* refers to the remainder of the fetus, not to the head itself.

### Rav Shlomo Zalman

Rav Shlomo Zalman Auerbach addressed this directly. He acknowledged the extensive *Acharonim* literature that effectively limits this *Gemara* to the conventional understanding that birth is determined by the head fully exiting the body. However, he expressed personal stringency: if it were known that a fetus had descended out of the uterus into the vaginal canal on *erev Shabbos* but emerged from the body only after *Shabbos* began, he would be *machmir* and not perform the *bris milah* on *Shabbos*. His reasoning followed the implication of this *Gemara* directly. At the same time, he noted that in practice no one has ever distinguished between these two moments, implying that the interval between exiting the uterus and exiting the body is ordinarily negligible — instantaneous, or close to it. Where complications arise and the interval is prolonged, however, genuine *halakhic* questions about defining birth may surface.

## Beluah Debate

### Canal Status

The *Gemara* now turns to a foundational question about the anatomical and halakhic status of the vaginal canal itself. Is it *beluah* — absorbed, swallowed within the body, such that its contents are halakhically invisible — or is it *beit hastarim* — a hidden interior space, but not so deeply internal as to count as *beluah*?

### Dead Tissue Case

The practical question is posed as follows: if someone placed a *ke-zayit* of *neveilah* — the carcass of an animal not properly slaughtered, which causes *tumah* through *massa* (carrying) — inside the vaginal canal, is the woman *teme'ah*? Touching it is not the question: that place is certainly at least *beit hastarim*, which precludes *tumah* through touch. But *massa* is different — even interior carrying can transmit *tumah* if the space is only *beit hastarim*. If the vaginal canal is *beluah*, however, the *neveilah* is as if it does not exist, and she is not *teme'ah* even through *massa*.

### Abaye Beluah

Abaye rules: the vaginal canal is *beluah*.

### Rava Beit Hastarim

Rava rules: it is *beit hastarim*, not *beluah*.

### Baraita Proof

Rava supports his position from the *baraita* cited earlier in the *sugya* concerning semen flowing in a woman's body. That *baraita* explains why the Torah needed to explicitly render a woman *teme'ah* for this: because the semen flows in a space that is *beit hastarim*, and ordinarily *beit hastarim* contact does not transmit *tumah*. The *baraita* thus uses the term *beit hastarim* to describe the vaginal canal, which Rava takes as explicit textual evidence for his position.

### Abaye Counter

Abaye responds with a sophisticated counter-reading. He argues that the *baraita* is making a compound point: *chada ve-od ka-amar* — two overlapping reasons why the semen would not normally cause *tumah*. First, because it is *beluah* — swallowed. Second, even if one were to say it is merely *beit hastarim* rather than *beluah*, it still would not cause *tumah* through *beit hastarim* contact. Both reasons independently prevent *tumah*, and together they heighten the force of the *chiddush* that the Torah renders her *teme'ah* nonetheless. The *baraita*'s invocation of *beit hastarim* is therefore not a ruling that the vaginal canal is *only* *beit hastarim*; it is raising the *beit hastarim* point as one of two obstacles the Torah's ruling overcomes. Abaye's position — that the canal is *beluah* — is unaffected.

## Throat Beluah

### Esophagus Status

An analogous question is then posed concerning the esophagus — *makom nivul*, the place of swallowing. Is the throat *beluah* or *beit hastarim*? Intuitively, one might assume the throat is even more clearly *beluah* than the vaginal canal: it is deep within the body, entirely invisible, and its very function is to convey matter downward into the body. Yet the same conceptual distinction applies: *beit hastarim* is a hidden place, while *beluah* is something that has been absorbed to the point of legal nonexistence.

### Throat Case

The practical case: if someone forced a *ke-zayit* of the carcass of an animal — *neveilah* — down into a person's throat, does it cause *tumah* through *massa*? If the throat is *beit hastarim*, the *neveilah* is present in a hidden but still legally cognizable space, and *massa tumah* applies. If it is *beluah*, it is as if the *neveilah* is not there, and no *tumah* results.

### Abaye Throat

Abaye rules: the throat is *beluah*.

### Rava Throat

Rava, by implication, holds that even the throat is only *beit hastarim* — a stringent position that requires something to be very deeply and thoroughly absorbed before it rises to the level of *beluah*.

### Dead Bird Proof

Abaye marshals proof from a *baraita*. One might have thought that just as the carcass of a *tahor* bird causes *tumah* in the throat — while being swallowed — so too the carcass of an animal (*neveilat behemah*) should cause *tumah* to one's garments when being swallowed down the throat. The Torah says: *neveilah u-tereifah lo yochal le-tamei bah* — do not eat *neveilah* and *tereifah* to become *tamei* through it. Rabbinic tradition identifies this verse as referring specifically to the *neveilat of tahor*, because only that *tumah* is exclusively in the act of eating. The *neveilat behemah*, by contrast, causes *tumah* before it is eaten — through touch and carrying in the world. It is therefore *excluded* from causing *tumah* in the throat. The *Gemara* then considers whether a *kal va-chomer* could restore that *tumah*: if a bird carcass, which causes no *tumah* outside the body, causes *tumah* inside the throat — how much more should an animal carcass, which *does* cause *tumah* outside, cause *tumah* inside? The *Gemara* rebuts: *bah* — the verse restricts: only the bird carcass causes *tumah* in that context, not any other.

The key point for Abaye is that the *baraita* explicitly states that *neveilat behemah* does *not* cause *tumah* in the throat. But if the throat were merely *beit hastarim*, the *neveilah* should still cause *tumah* through *massa*! The fact that it does not cause *tumah* there demonstrates that the throat is *beluah* — and *tumah beluah lo metamei*. Rava does not have a direct refutation of this argument.

### Tosafot Degrees

Tosafot offers a synthesis that resolves the tension between Abaye and Rava. Rather than a binary distinction, Tosafot proposes degrees of depth. The higher, more accessible portions of the throat — closer to the mouth — may be *beit hastarim*. The deeper portions — closer to the stomach — may be *beluah*. Rava's ruling that the throat is *beit hastarim* would then refer to the upper portion, while the *baraita* that disqualifies *tumah* there refers to the deeper portion, which Abaye correctly categorizes as *beluah*.

### Application Women

Translating this to the question about the vaginal canal: the debate between Abaye and Rava may similarly reflect gradations of depth. The portion of the vaginal canal closer to the external opening may be *beit hastarim* — as Rava holds — while the portion higher up, closer to the uterus, may be *beluah* even in Rava's view. The *machlokes* between them may therefore be about the outer portion of the canal, with possible agreement that the deeper interior is *beluah*.

### Chatzitzah

This distinction carries enormous practical consequence for the laws of *chatzitzah*. A *chatzitzah* — an interposition between the body and the *mikvah* water — is problematic even in a *beit hastarim* space. Teeth, for example, are *beit hastarim*; they are not normally visible, but the *mikvah* water must reach that space, and any interposition there is a *chatzitzah* concern. If the vaginal canal is *beit hastarim*, then any object placed in it — whether for medical or contraceptive purposes, such as certain rings or devices inserted up to the cervix — would potentially constitute a *chatzitzah* and require removal before immersion. If, however, that space is *beluah*, or if the device is positioned high enough that everyone agrees it is *beluah*, then no *chatzitzah* concern would apply. The question of how far up the canal the *beluah* designation begins is therefore directly relevant to *pesak halakha* regarding contraceptive and medical devices in contemporary *hilchos mikvah*. This will be developed further in the continuation of the *sugya*.
