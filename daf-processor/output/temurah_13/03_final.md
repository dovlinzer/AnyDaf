# Temurah 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Terumah Achar Terumah

### Intro to Two Terumot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין תְּרוּמָה אַחַר תְּרוּמָה כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § **And there is no *teruma* after *teruma*.** Once one designates produce from his crop as *teruma*, if he then designates additional produce from that crop as *teruma*, it is not *teruma*.

The *mishna* on 13a introduces a rule that has a superficially similar structure to several laws discussed earlier in the tractate but is actually making a distinct point: *ve'ein trumah achar trumah* — one cannot take a second *terumah* after the first has already been taken from the same produce.

### Terumah vs. Temurah

This rule is importantly different from the prohibition against daisy-chaining *temurah*. The *temurah* prohibition is that one cannot take a *temurah* from a *temurah* — that is, one cannot take A, make it a *temurah* of B, and then take C as a *temurah* of that derived B. The chain cannot extend beyond one remove from the original sanctified animal. But *temurah* does permit making multiple exchanges from the same original source: one may take A, designate B as its *temurah*, and then designate C as a *temurah* as well, as long as each exchange starts from A. That is not daisy-chaining — it is always returning to the original. *Terumah*, by contrast, prohibits doing the act twice even from the same original source: once *terumah* has been taken from a pile of grain, it cannot be taken again.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַשּׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁתָּרְמוּ זֶה אַחַר זֶה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: תְּרוּמַת שְׁנֵיהֶם תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna? It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 3:3): In the case of **joint** owners of produce **who separated *teruma* one after the other, Rabbi Eliezer says: The *teruma* of both of them is *teruma*,** as each is considered to have separated from his share of the produce.

The *gemara* identifies the source for this rule in a debate recorded in a *mishna* elsewhere. The case involves *shutfim* — partners in a field — who take *terumah* from their jointly owned produce sequentially, without coordinating with one another. This is the interesting test case: a single person taking *terumah* twice from his own produce is so obvious as not to need discussion. But partners acting independently create a genuine ambiguity. Rabbi Eliezer rules that *trumat shneihem trumah* — both *terumot* are valid. His logic is apparently that we interpret each partner as having taken *terumah* on his respective half of the shared produce.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין תְּרוּמַת שְׁנֵיהֶם תְּרוּמָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אִם תָּרַם הָרִאשׁוֹן כַּשִּׁיעוּר — אֵין תְּרוּמַת הַשֵּׁנִי תְּרוּמָה, וְאִם לֹא תָּרַם כַּשִּׁיעוּר — תְּרוּמַת הַשֵּׁנִי תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: The *teruma* of neither of them is *teruma*.** Since each separated *teruma* independently, it is clear that neither accepts the separation of the other, and therefore neither is valid. **And the Rabbis say: If the first one separated *teruma* of** the correct **measure,** i.e., one-fiftieth of the produce, the produce is thereby tithed, and therefore **the *teruma* of the second is not *teruma*; but if** the first **did not separate *teruma* of** the correct **measure,** and he separated too little, **the *teruma* of the second is *teruma*.**

Rabbi Akiva rules *ein trumat shneihem trumah* — neither works. This is the *mishna*'s position, and the *gemara* therefore attributes our *mishna* to Rabbi Akiva rather than to the *tanna kamma*, since it disagrees with Rabbi Eliezer on this point.

### Rashi Interpretation

Rashi explains Rabbi Akiva's view as follows: when the second partner goes ahead and takes *terumah* again after the first already did, this demonstrates retroactively that he was not satisfied with what his partner had done. And if the second partner did not want the first partner taking *terumah* on his behalf, then by symmetry we can assume the same was true in reverse. Both of them, it turns out, did not consent to the other's act. The mutual presumption of rejection invalidates both.

### Tosafot Interpretation

Tosafot disagrees with Rashi's reading and offers what is in some ways the simpler interpretation of Rabbi Akiva: the first *terumah* is valid, and the second is void. This is consistent with the plain reading of our *mishna*, which says *ein trumah achar trumah* — there is no *terumah* after *terumah*, implying the first stands and the second accomplishes nothing. On Tosafot's reading, the first partner's act is presumed to have been done with the other's agreement, and once it is done, any subsequent attempt by the second partner is simply meaningless.

### Chachamim Position

The *chachamim* offer a third position that introduces the concept of proper measure. *Im taram harishon kashiur* — if the first partner took the standard amount (roughly two percent, the average that the Rabbis identify as appropriate), then *ein trumat hasheni trumah* — the second one's act has no effect. The first was done correctly, it stands, and the second adds nothing. But *ve'im lo taram kashiur* — if the first partner did not take the appropriate measure, taking instead the minimum permissible amount of one sixtieth, then *trumat hasheni trumah* — the second one's act is effective. According to Rashi, this means that taking a stingy amount signals dissatisfaction with the first *terumah*, and the second partner's subsequent act then validates itself. Whether this means the first is entirely nullified or merely supplemented is somewhat unclear, but the underlying logic seems to be that insufficient performance by the first creates the opening for the second.

## Temurah Osah Temurah

### Temurah Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין תְּמוּרָה עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּתְמוּרָתוֹ״, וְלֹא תְּמוּרַת תְּמוּרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **And a substitute** animal that was consecrated when it was substituted for a consecrated animal **does not render** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute;** rather, it remains non-sacred. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason,** i.e., the source for this *halakha* in the Torah? **The verse states:** "And if he substitutes an animal for an animal, then both it **and that for which it is substituted** shall be sacred" (Leviticus 27:10). The verse teaches that the *halakha* of substitution applies only to a consecrated animal and its substitute, **but not the substitute of its substitute.**

After the digression about *terumah*, the *mishna* returns to the direct discussion of *temurah*: *ve'ein temurah osah temurah* — a *temurah* cannot generate a further *temurah*. The *gemara* derives this from the verse, which says *be'temurato* — its exchange, meaning the exchange of the original sanctified animal — *velo temurat temurato* — not the exchange of the *temurah* itself. The Torah permits *temurah* only with respect to the original sanctified animal, not with respect to something whose *kedushah* is itself already derivative.

### Vlad Cannot Temurah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הַוָּלָד עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה. דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא״ — הוּא וְלֹא וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** § **And the offspring** born of a consecrated animal that was not consecrated itself **does not render** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute.** The Gemara explains: The reason for this is **that the verse states:** "Then both **it** and that for which it is substituted shall be sacred" (Leviticus 27:10), which teaches that only **it,** a consecrated animal, **but not** its **offspring,** renders a non-sacred animal exchanged for it a substitute.

The *mishna* links this to a parallel rule: *ve'ein ha-vlad oseh temurah* — the offspring of a *korban* cannot generate a *temurah* either. The common thread between the two cases is the distinction between primary and derivative *kedushah*. A *temurah* has derivative *kedushah* because it acquired its sanctity by being exchanged for the original. A *vlad* has derivative *kedushah* because it was born to a sanctified mother. In both cases, the chain of sanctity has moved one step away from the original act of consecration, and the Torah does not permit *temurah* to be performed from that derived position.

### Rabbi Yehuda Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַוָּלָד עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה. דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״יִהְיֶה״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַוָּלָד. וְרַבָּנַן: לְרַבּוֹת שׁוֹגֵג כְּמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yehuda says: The offspring renders** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute.** The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yehuda reasons **that** when **the verse states: "Shall be** sacred," this serves **to include the offspring.** The Gemara adds: **And** as for **the Rabbis,** who hold that one cannot substitute for the offspring of a consecrated animal, they maintain that this phrase serves **to include** one who substitutes **unwittingly,** so that the substitution is valid **as** if he had done so **intentionally.**

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees specifically with respect to *vlad*, drawing on the verse *yihiyeh* — it shall be — which he reads as expanding the category of *temurah* to include offspring. In his view, one cannot make a *temurah* from a *temurah*, but one can make a *temurah* from a *vlad*, since the *vlad* possesses a legitimate form of *kedushah* even if it is derivative. The Rabbis, by contrast, read *yihiyeh* as a source for a different expansion: *lerabot shogeg ke-mezid* — to include even an unintentional act of *temurah*.

### Temurah Binding Force

This introduces a broader theme that recurs throughout the tractate: *temurah* is more binding in certain respects than an original act of consecration. If one accidentally designates the wrong animal — say, one intended to designate a black cow but mistakenly referred to a white one — an original *hekdesh* would be *ta'os* and would not bind. But *temurah* takes hold even where there is a certain element of error, because the verse *yihiyeh* comes to ensure its binding force even in cases of *shogeg*. This is one of several contexts in which *temurah*, paradoxically, is more forceful than a deliberate primary consecration.

## Excluded Objects

### Birds Menachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוֹפוֹת וְהַמְּנָחוֹת אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא בְּהֵמָה. הַצִּיבּוּר וְהַשּׁוּתָּפִין אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא יַחֲלִיפֶנּוּ וְלֹא יָמִיר״. יָחִיד עוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה, וְאֵין הַצִּבּוּר וְהַשּׁוּתָּפִין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה. קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת אֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The birds** sacrificed as offerings, i.e., doves and pigeons, **and the meal offerings do not render** non-sacred items exchanged for them **substitutes, as only** the term **"an animal" is stated** with regard to substitution, in the verse: "And if he substitutes an animal for an animal" (Leviticus 27:10). A consecrated animal belonging to **the community or** to **partners does not render** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute, as it is stated** in the same verse: **"He shall neither exchange it nor substitute** it." One derives from the singular pronoun in the verse that **an individual renders** a non-sacred animal **a substitute, but the community and partners do not render** a non-sacred animal **a substitute.** Items **consecrated for Temple maintenance do not render** non-sacred items exchanged for them **substitutes.**

The next and final *mishna* of this *perek* turns to a systematic catalog of what is excluded from *temurah*. The *mishna* opens: *ha-ofot veha-menachot ein osin temurah* — bird sacrifices and *menachot* cannot perform *temurah*. The reason is that the Torah uses the word *behemah* — it says *behemah bivehemah*, specifically restricting *temurah* to domesticated livestock. A bird is not a *behemah* in the relevant sense, and *menachot* are certainly not livestock. Furthermore, since the verse says *behemah bivehemah*, both the original sanctified animal and the object of exchange must be a *behemah*. This presumably excludes birds from being either side of the transaction.

### Tzibur Shutfim

The *mishna* continues: *ha-tzibur veha-shutfim einam osim temurah* — communal sacrifices and jointly owned sacrifices cannot perform *temurah*. The *mishna* derives this from the verse *lo yachalifenu velo yamir* — you shall not exchange it or substitute it — where the verb is in the singular. *Yachid oseh temurah* — only an individual can perform *temurah*; *ve'ein tzibur ve-shutfim osim temurah* — not the community and not partners. A *korban tamid* or *musaf*, brought on behalf of the entire community, cannot be subject to *temurah*, nor can a *korban* that two individuals own jointly.

### Bedek Habayit

The *mishna* adds: *kodshei bedek ha-bayit einam osim temurah* — items sanctified for the Temple treasury do not perform *temurah*. The underlying principle, as has been noted throughout this chapter, is that *temurah* requires *kedushat haguf* — genuine intrinsic *korban*-sanctity, the kind that attaches to an animal designated for the altar. An animal consecrated for the treasury has only *kedushat damim*, and even in the middle cases — such as a blemished animal whose proceeds are designated to purchase a *korban* — the status is insufficient for *temurah*.

### Rabbi Shimon Maaser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וַהֲרֵי מַעֲשֵׂר בַּכְּלָל הָיָה, וְלָמָּה יָצָא? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר קׇרְבַּן יָחִיד וְקׇרְבַּן מִזְבֵּחַ, יָצְאוּ קׇרְבְּנוֹת צִבּוּר; וּמָה מַעֲשֵׂר קׇרְבַּן מִזְבֵּחַ, יָצְאוּ קׇרְבָּנוֹת בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said:** The fact that animals belonging to the community or partners do not render animals exchanged for them substitutes is derived as follows: The **animal tithe was** included **in** the **category** of all offerings, **and why was it** singled **out** in the verse: "And all the tithe of the herd or the flock, whatever passes under the rod, the tenth shall be sacred unto the Lord. He shall not inquire whether it is good or bad, neither shall he substitute for it; and if he substitutes it, then both it and that for which it is substituted shall be sacred" (Leviticus 27:32–33)? Rabbi Shimon explains: It was singled out to juxtapose substitution to the animal tithe, **to tell you: Just as** the animal **tithe** is brought exclusively as **an individual offering,** so too, all offerings that render their substitutes sacred are individual offerings, **excluding communal offerings** and the offerings of partners from the *halakha* of substitution. **And just as** the animal **tithe** is **an offering** sacrificed on the **altar,** so too, all offerings that render their substitutes sacred are offerings sacrificed on the altar, **excluding** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance** from the *halakha* of substitution.

Rabbi Shimon adds a distinctive methodological point. He does not dispute the conclusions just stated, but he derives them differently. The Torah, in *Bechukosai*, states the prohibition of *temurah* once generally, and then, a few verses later, repeats the prohibition specifically in the context of *ma'aser behemah*. *V'harei ma'aser bichlal haya* — *ma'aser* was already included in the general prohibition, so why was it singled out? *Lomar lecha* — to serve as a paradigm. *Mah ma'aser korban yachid* — just as *ma'aser* is a private individual's *korban*, *yatzu korbanos tzibur* — communal sacrifices are excluded. *U'mah ma'aser korban mizbe'ach* — and just as *ma'aser* is an altar sacrifice, *yatzu korbanos bedek ha-bayit* — gifts to the treasury are excluded. Rabbi Shimon thus reads the Torah's repetition of *temurah* by *ma'aser* not as redundant but as providing the halachic model for all subsequent exclusions.

## Maaser Paradigm

### Korban Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל יְהוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״קׇרְבָּן״ — מִי שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ ״קׇרְבָּן״, יָצְאוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁלֹּא נִקְרְאוּ ״קׇרְבָּן״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: According to the mishna, items consecrated for Temple maintenance do not render non-sacred items exchanged for them substitutes. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that items **consecrated for Temple maintenance render** non-sacred items exchanged for them **substitutes.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And if it is an animal of which men bring **an offering** to the Lord…he shall not alter it, nor substitute it, good for bad" (Leviticus 27:9–10). This teaches that the *halakha* of substitution applies to **that which is called an offering, excluding** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance, which are not called an offering.**

The *gemara* opens with a *braita* that presses the *tanna kamma*'s position: *yachol yehu kodshei bedek ha-bayit osim temurah* — one might have thought that items dedicated to the Temple treasury would also be subject to *temurah*. The *braita* derives their exclusion from the word *korban*: since the Torah links the prohibition of *temurah* to a *korban*, and items dedicated to the treasury are not called a *korban*, they are excluded. But the *gemara* immediately challenges this: is it really true that treasury items are not called *korban*?

### Shechutei Chutz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: אִי ״קׇרְבָּן״ — שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ ״קׇרְבָּן״, כָּעִנְיָן שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַנַּקְרֵב אֶת קׇרְבַּן ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ״ — כׇּל הַבָּא אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם שְׁחוּטֵי חוּץ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּא לְפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם שְׁחוּטֵי חוּץ. אַלְמָא אִיקְּרוּ ״קׇרְבָּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** are items consecrated for Temple maintenance **not** called offerings? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to sacrificial animals: "Or who slaughters it outside the camp, and has not brought it to the entrance to the Tent of Meeting to present it as an offering to the Lord before the Tabernacle of the Lord" (Leviticus 17:3–4). **If** the verse had mentioned just the word **"offering," I** would **derive** that the prohibition against slaughtering outside the Temple applies **even** to items **consecrated for Temple maintenance, as** they too **are called offerings, as is stated in the matter** of the spoils of the war against Midian: **"And we have brought the Lord's offering,** what every man has gotten, of jewels of gold, armlets, and bracelets, signet rings, earrings, and girdles, to make atonement for our souls before the Lord" (Numbers 31:50). These were certainly not items consecrated for the altar. Therefore, **the verse states: "And to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting he did not bring it"** (Leviticus 17:4), to teach that **for any** item **that** is fit to **come to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting,** i.e., that is fit to be sacrificed, **one is liable for it due to** the prohibition against **slaughtering offerings outside** of the Temple courtyard. **And** by contrast, **for any** animal **that** is **not** fit to **come to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, one is not liable for it due to** the prohibition against **slaughtering offerings outside** of the Temple courtyard. **Evidently,** as a verse is necessary to exclude items consecrated for Temple maintenance, such items **are** generally **called an offering,** contrary to the first *baraita* cited.

Another *braita*, in an entirely different context, shows that the word *korban* can indeed encompass treasury items. That *braita* concerns the prohibition of *shechutei chutz* — slaughtering a *korban* outside the Temple precincts, which incurs *karet*. The verse formulates the prohibition in terms of one who failed to bring his animal *el pesach ohel mo'ed*. The *braita* notes that one might have thought the prohibition extends even to animals sanctified for the treasury, since such animals are also called *korban*, citing the verse *vanakrev es korban Hashem* — and we brought the offering of God — a passage from after the war with Midian where the officers' gift to the treasury is explicitly called *korban Hashem*. However, the *braita* concludes that the actual exclusion of treasury items from *shechutei chutz* comes not from the word *korban* but from the phrase *lo hevi'o el pesach ohel mo'ed* — since treasury items would not normally come to the *ohel mo'ed* in the first place (either because they have a blemish or because they are not animals fit for the altar at all), one is not liable for slaughtering them outside the Temple.

The upshot of citing this *braita* is clear: if the word *korban* were on its own insufficient to exclude treasury items in the context of *shechutei chutz* — requiring the *gemara* to find another source — how can the *tanna kamma* of the *temurah* discussion use the word *korban* to exclude them there?

### Korban Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָא רַבָּנַן. לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִיקְּרִי ״קׇרְבָּן״, לְרַבָּנַן לָא אִיקְּרִי ״קׇרְבָּן״. וְלָא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַנַּקְרֵב אֶת קׇרְבַּן ה׳״! ״קׇרְבַּן ה׳״ אִיקְּרִי, ״קׇרְבָּן לַה׳״ לָא אִיקְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** This is **not difficult. This** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** whereas **that** first *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** items consecrated for Temple maintenance **are** in fact **called an offering,** and therefore in the mishna he derives that items consecrated for Temple maintenance are excluded from the *halakhot* of substitution from the juxtaposition with the animal tithe, not from word "offering." **According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** items consecrated for Temple maintenance **are not called an offering,** and they therefore derive the *halakha* from the word "offering." The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of the Rabbis, are items consecrated for Temple maintenance **not** called an offering? **But isn't it written: "And we have brought the Lord's offering"** (Numbers 31:50)? The Gemara answers: These items **are called: "The Lord's offering,"** but **they are not called: "An offering to the Lord,"** which is used only with regard to offerings sacrificed upon the altar.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by observing that it is not *korban* simpliciter that includes treasury items, but specifically *korban Hashem* — with the divine name attached as a modifier. *Korban Hashem ikri* — when it appears with that qualifier, it may refer to a gift. *Korban l'Hashem lo ikri* — but plain *korban*, or even *korban l'Hashem*, does not extend to treasury gifts. The occurrence in the Midian passage uses the distinctive formulation *korban Hashem*, which the *gemara* treats as an exceptional usage. The standard term *korban*, on its own, does not encompass *bedek ha-bayit*.

### Resolution

With this in hand, the *gemara* identifies the underlying dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *rabanan*. *Ha Rabbi Shimon ha rabanan* — the two *braitot* represent two positions. The *rabanan* hold that *korban stam* does not include treasury items, and therefore the word *korban* in the *temurah* verse is sufficient to exclude them. Rabbi Shimon holds that *korban* could in principle encompass *bedek ha-bayit* and is therefore insufficiently precise to serve as the basis of exclusion. For that reason, Rabbi Shimon must derive the exclusion of treasury items from the paradigm of *ma'aser*, and cannot simply rely on the word *korban*.

## Maaser Repeated

### Rabbi Shimon Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא יְבַקֵּר בֵּין טוֹב לָרַע וְלֹא יְמִירֶנּוּ״, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יַחֲלִיפֶנּוּ וְלֹא יָמִיר אוֹתוֹ טוֹב בְּרָעוְגוֹ׳״. לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא יַחֲלִיפֶנּוּ וְלֹא יָמִיר אוֹתוֹ״, מַשְׁמַע קׇרְבַּן יָחִיד, קׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר, וְקׇרְבַּן מִזְבֵּחַ וְקׇרְבַּן בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא יְבַקֵּר״. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וַהֲרֵי מַעֲשֵׂר בַּכְּלָל הָיָה, וְלָמָּה יָצָא? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה מַעֲשֵׂר קׇרְבַּן יָחִיד, וְקׇרְבַּן מִזְבֵּחַ, וְדָבָר שֶׁבָּא בְּחוֹבָה, וְדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת — אַף כֹּל קׇרְבַּן יָחִיד, וְקׇרְבַּן מִזְבֵּחַ, וְדָבָר שֶׁבָּא בְּחוֹבָה, וְדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּא בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the animal tithe: **"He shall not inquire whether it is good or bad, neither shall he substitute it;** and if he substitute it at all, then both it and that for which it is substituted shall be sacred; it shall not be redeemed" (Leviticus 27:33). **Why is** the issue of substitution **stated** here in connection to the animal tithe? **Isn't it already stated** earlier: **"He shall not alter it, nor substitute it, good for bad,** or bad for good, and if he substitutes an animal for an animal, then both it and that for which it is substituted shall be sacred" (Leviticus 27:10)? **Since it is stated: "He shall not alter it, nor substitute it,"** the verse apparently **indicates** that all types of offering are included, whether **an individual offering** or **a communal offering, and** whether it is **an offering** sacrificed on the **altar or an offering consecrated for Temple maintenance.** Therefore, **the verse states** with regard to the animal tithe: **"He shall not inquire."** **Rabbi Shimon said** in explanation: The animal **tithe was** included **in** the **category** of all offerings, **and why was it** singled **out?** It is **to tell you: Just as** the animal **tithe** is **an individual offering, and an offering** sacrificed on the **altar, and** it is **an item that comes** only **as an obligation,** not as a gift offering, **and** it is **an item that is not** brought **in partnership,** but only by an individual, **so too, all** offerings that render their substitutes sacred must be **an individual offering, and an offering** sacrificed on the **altar, and** it must be **an item that comes** only **as an obligation,**

The *gemara* now cites a *braita* that spells out Rabbi Shimon's position fully. The *braita* asks: *lama ne'emar* — why does the Torah need to state the prohibition of *temurah* a second time by *ma'aser*? *Ve'halo kvar ne'emar lo yachalifenu velo yamir oso tov be-ra* — the general prohibition was already stated earlier. *Lefi she-ne'emar lo yachalifenu* — since the general statement might have been read to apply whether the *korban* is individual or communal, whether it is a sacrifice for the altar or a gift to the treasury, therefore the Torah singles out *ma'aser* as the paradigm. *Mah ma'aser korban yachid vekorban mizbe'ach* — since *ma'aser* is a private individual's sacrifice and is specifically an altar offering — *ve-davar she-ba be-chovah* — and it is something that comes through an obligation, a point we shall return to shortly — *ve-davar she-eino be-shutfus* — and it is not jointly owned — so too all *temurah* must meet all of these conditions. This is Rabbi Shimon's full derivation.

The *rabanan* who do not use *ma'aser* as their paradigm derive the same exclusions from different sources: the singular verb excludes communal and partnership sacrifices, and the word *korban* excludes treasury gifts. What remains unclear is whether the *rabanan* agree with the additional requirement of *ba be-chovah* — that the *korban* must come through an obligation — or whether that exclusion is unique to Rabbi Shimon's paradigm.

### Rebbi Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: לָמָּה יָצָא מַעֲשֵׂר מֵעַתָּה? לִידּוֹן בִּ״תְמוּרַת שְׁמוֹ״ וּבִ״תְמוּרַת גּוּפוֹ״. לוֹמַר לָךְ: תְּמוּרַת שְׁמוֹ — קְרֵיבָה, תְּמוּרַת גּוּפוֹ — אֵינָהּ קְרֵיבָה; תְּמוּרַת שְׁמוֹ — נִגְאֶלֶת, תְּמוּרַת גּוּפוֹ — אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Why was** the animal **tithe** singled **out** of all offerings as subject to substitution **now,** after the *halakha* of substitution was stated in general? It serves **to discuss** a special *halakha* **with regard to** the animal tithe, that of **substitution of its name.** If, when the animals emerge from the pen to be tithed, the one counting them errs and calls the tenth animal the ninth and the eleventh the tenth, they are both sanctified. The animal that actually emerges tenth is the animal tithe, while the eleventh animal is consecrated as a peace offering. **And** since this *halakha* of a substitution of its name applies only to the animal tithe, it is necessary **to** teach that the general *halakha* of **the substitution of its body,** i.e., regular substitution, applies to it as well. Furthermore, the verse **tells you** other *halakhot* unique to the animal tithe: An animal that is **the substitute of the name of** an animal tithe is **sacrificed** upon the altar as a peace offering, whereas **the substitute of its body is not sacrificed** at all. But for all other offerings, substitutes hold the same status as the animal for which they were substituted. Another difference is that **the substitute of the name of** an animal tithe is **redeemed** when it develops a blemish, like a peace offering, and the proceeds of the sale belong to the Temple treasury, whereas **the substitute of its body is not redeemed,** as it is stated with regard to the animal tithe: "Then both it and that for which it is substituted shall be sacred; it shall not be redeemed" (Leviticus 27:33).

Rebbi offers an alternative explanation for why the Torah repeats the *temurah* prohibition by *ma'aser*. His position is introduced with the phrase *lama yatzas ma'aser min ha-klal* — why was *ma'aser* singled out from the general rule? His answer: *lidon bi-tmurat shemo uvi-tmurat gufo* — because *ma'aser* has a special *halakha* that involves two distinct types of exchange, and the Torah repeats the *temurah* verse by *ma'aser* in order to clarify that both apply.

The reference is to a well-known *halakha* in *ma'aser behemah*, discussed at length in *Bechoros*. When an animal owner counts his animals through a gate to separate the tenth as *ma'aser*, if he miscalls the count and declares the ninth "ten" or the eleventh "ten," those animals also acquire *kedushah*. This is *tmurat shemo* — not an exchange of the body of the animal, but a misapplication of the name. The animal called "ten" in error acquires a status similar to *temurah*, but through the mechanism of naming rather than through the physical act of exchange. Normal *temurah*, by contrast, is *tmurat gufo* — an exchange of the body itself.

Rebbi's point is that since *ma'aser* has this distinctive feature of *tmurat shemo*, one might have thought that the introduction of this new category displaces the older category: perhaps *ma'aser* is now governed exclusively by the rules of *tmurat shemo* and is no longer subject to the standard rules of *tmurat gufo*. The Torah therefore repeats the *temurah* prohibition by *ma'aser* to clarify that *ma'aser* retains *tmurat gufo* as well. Both regimes apply simultaneously.

### Shemo vs. Gufo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּמוּרַת גּוּפוֹ — חָלָה עַל דָּבָר הָרָאוּי וְעַל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי, וּתְמוּרַת שְׁמוֹ — אֵינָהּ חָלָה אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר הָרָאוּי בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, the sanctity of **the substitution of the body of** an animal tithe **takes effect upon** both **an item that is fit** for sacrifice upon the altar **and upon an item that is unfit** for sacrifice, e.g., a blemished animal, as the sanctity of the animal tithe can apply even to a blemished animal, **but the substitution of its name takes effect only upon an item that is fit** for sacrifice. If the animal that was mistakenly called the tenth is blemished, it is not consecrated.

The *gemara* then elaborates the practical differences between *tmurat shemo* and *tmurat gufo*, and the analysis is worth following carefully.

*Tmurat shemo kriva, tmurat gufo einah kriva* — if one performs *tmurat shemo*, calling eleven "ten," that animal becomes a *shelamim* and is brought as a *korban*. If one performs *tmurat gufo*, a standard *temurah* of *ma'aser*, the resulting animal is not brought as a *korban*. The reason connects to the previously learned principle that *tmurat ma'aser* and *tmurat bechor* are uniquely not brought as *korbanot*. What makes *bechor* and *ma'aser* different from other *korbanot*? Their identity is defined not by an act of consecration but by a reality — this animal was the firstborn, this was the tenth out of the gate. When *temurah* is performed with such an animal, the resulting *temurah* inherits the *kedushah* of *ma'aser* or *bechor* without actually being the tenth or the firstborn. It possesses the *kedushah* without the defining reality, and consequently it is not brought as a *korban*. *Tmurat shemo*, by contrast, produces a *shelamim*, not a *ma'aser*, and a *shelamim* is of course brought as a *korban*.

### Eligibility Diff

The *gemara* now cites a *braita* regarding further distinctions. Further differences follow. *Tmurat shemo nigelet* — the animal that arose from *tmurat shemo* can be redeemed if it acquires a *mum*. Since it became a *shelamim*, the ordinary rules of *shelamim* apply, and a blemished *shelamim* may be redeemed. *Tmurat gufo einah nigelet* — the animal that arose from *tmurat gufo* cannot be redeemed. Since it is *kedushat ma'aser*, and the Torah says *lo yiga'el* — *ma'aser* cannot be redeemed — a *temurah* of *ma'aser* retains that prohibition. One waits for it to acquire a *mum* and then eats it outside the *beit ha-mikdash*, rather than redeeming it.

A further distinction: *tmurat gufo chalah al davar ha-ra'uy ve-al davar she-eino ra'uy* — a proper *temurah* takes effect whether the substitute animal is blemished or unblemished. The *halakha* of *temurah* generally is that even if you attempt to exchange a sanctified animal for a blemished one, the exchange takes real effect: the blemished animal acquires genuine *kedushat mizbe'ach*, even though it cannot actually be brought as a *korban*. *Tmurat shemo einah chalah ela al davar ha-ra'uy bilvad* — the exchange of name, calling the eleventh "ten," only takes full effect when that animal is unblemished. If the animal designated by the erroneous count has a *mum*, it is not treated as a proper *shelamim* with intrinsic *kedushat ha-guf*; it has only *kedushat damim*, like any blemished animal that is consecrated to the altar. Rashi clarifies that this does not mean the animal acquires no sanctity at all, only that it never achieves real intrinsic altar-sanctity and must be sold with the proceeds going toward a *korban*.

The elegance of Rebbi's formulation lies in lining up these two parallel categories — calling eleven "ten" versus exchanging bodies — and showing precisely where the analogy holds and where the two regimes diverge. In the case of *tmurat shemo*, one has effectively created an original act of consecration as a *shelamim*; all the *halakhot* of an original *shelamim* designation follow from that. In the case of *tmurat gufo*, one has the special and peculiar category of *temurah* with all its distinctive stringencies and limitations.

## Ba'ah Bechovah

### Ba'ah Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּא בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **and** it must be **an item that** is **not** brought **in partnership.**

The *braita* that spells out Rabbi Shimon's paradigm introduces a requirement that has not been previously explained: the *korban* must be *davar she-ba be-chovah* — something that comes through obligation. Only when a *korban* is an obligatory offering — something that one has taken upon oneself as an obligation — is it subject to *temurah*.

### Leftover Money

One might wonder what is excluded by this criterion, since most *korbanot* are either fixed obligations or voluntary pledges. The *gemara* explains: the case excluded is *olat ha-ba'ah min ha-motarot* — an *olah* that arises from leftover money. When one sets aside funds for a *chatat* and the *chatat* ends up costing less than anticipated, the remaining money must be used for an *olah*. Similarly, when an *asham* is rendered unnecessary because the owner brings a different *asham* to atone, the original *asham* transforms into an *olah*. In both cases, the *olah* was not the result of any deliberate, intentional act of consecration; it arose as a byproduct of an accidental circumstance. Because this *olah* was never consciously undertaken as an obligation, it is considered *lo ba be-chovah* and is therefore excluded from *temurah*.

## Ba'ah Bechovah…

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְסָכִים מִי קָרְבִי בַּלַּיְלָה? וְהָתְנַן: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לִיקְרַב בַּלַּיְלָה, כְּגוֹן אֵבָרִים וּפְדָרִים, שֶׁמַּקְרִיבִין מִבּוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וּמִתְעַכְּלִין וְהוֹלְכִין כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And libations, may they be sacrificed at night? Didn't we learn** in a *baraita*: **I have** derived **only** with regard to **items that are normally sacrificed at night, for example, the limbs** of a burnt offering **and the fats** of burnt offerings and other offerings, **that one sacrifices** them **after sunset and they are consumed throughout the entire night.** This is derived from the verse: "This is the law of the burnt offering: It is that which goes up on its firewood upon the altar all night unto the morning" (Leviticus 6:2).

The *gemara* then notes an apparent tension. The criterion of *ba be-chovah* — that it requires an individual's *korban* — leads us to ask which opinion holds that leftover money produces an individual *korban* in the first place. The answer is Rabbi Eliezer, who rules that *motarot* go toward a *nidvat yachid*, a personal voluntary *korban*. However, the *gemara* points out that a *braita* explicitly records Rabbi Eliezer himself as ruling *oseh temurah* — that this *olah* from leftover money *can* perform *temurah*.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר לַהּ כְּוָתֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא, דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: עוֹלָה הַבָּאָה מִן הַמּוֹתָרוֹת עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה, וְאִיהוּ סָבַר: אֵין עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer **with regard to one** matter, that surplus funds go toward an individual voluntary burnt offering, **and disagrees with him with regard to** another matter, **as Rabbi Eliezer maintains** that **a burnt offering that came from surplus** funds **renders** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute, and he,** Rabbi Shimon, **maintains** that it **does not render** it **a substitute.**

Rabbi Shimon, therefore, agrees with Rabbi Eliezer on one point and disagrees on another. *Savar lah kavateih be-chada u-falig aleih be-chada* — he agrees that the leftover money goes toward an individual *korban*, but he disagrees that such an *olah* can perform *temurah*. Rabbi Shimon holds that because the *olah* was not consciously and intentionally undertaken, it is excluded from *temurah* by virtue of the *ma'aser* paradigm, which requires *ba be-chovah*. This is also where the practical significance of Rabbi Shimon's paradigm approach becomes manifest: until this point, the exclusions Rabbi Shimon derived from *ma'aser* were identical to those the *rabanan* derived from other verses. But the requirement of *ba be-chovah* represents a novel *halakha* — one that the *rabanan* may not share, and one that only emerges because Rabbi Shimon is reading *ma'aser* as the governing model.

## Asham to Olah

### Setup Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, דְּבָעֵי רַבִּי אָבִין: הִפְרִישׁ אָשָׁם לְהִתְכַּפֵּר בּוֹ, וְהֵמִיר בּוֹ, וְנִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאָשָׁם אַחֵר, וְנִיתַּק זֶה לְעוֹלָה — מַהוּ שֶׁיַּחְזוֹר וְיָמִיר בּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** consider **that dilemma raised by Rabbi Avin:** If one **separated** an animal as **a guilt offering by which to achieve atonement, and he effected substitution for it, and** then that original guilt offering developed a blemish and he redeemed it with another animal, which he subsequently lost, and the owner **achieved atonement by** bringing yet **another** animal as **a guilt offering; and** then **this** lost animal was found and **consigned to** be sacrificed as **a burnt offering, what is** the *halakha* as **to** whether **he can again effect substitution for it?** In this case, the animal in question is a burnt offering that came from surplus funds.

With this discussion in place, the *gemara* revisits a conceptual puzzle raised on an earlier *daf* — a scenario involving an *asham* whose *temurah* eventually transforms into an *olah*. The case: one sets aside an *asham*, performs *temurah* with it, and then separately brings another *asham* to fulfill the obligation, at which point the original *asham* transitions into an *olah* according to the relevant *halakha*.

### Double Temurah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — הָא אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ: עוֹלָה הַבָּאָה מִן הַמּוֹתָרוֹת אֵין עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **In accordance with whose** opinion was this dilemma raised? **If we say** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, you said** that **Rabbi Shimon maintains** that **a burnt offering that came from surplus** funds **does not render** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute.** There would therefore be no dilemma at all. This is problematic, because the dilemma assumes that one cannot effect substitution twice for the same animal, which is the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

The question now reframed: can one perform a second *temurah* with that animal after its transformation? This was the question that generated extended discussion on the earlier *daf*, based on Rabbi Yochanan's reading of Rabbi Shimon that one may not perform *temurah* twice with the same animal. But the *gemara* now notes a complication: according to Rabbi Shimon himself, this *olah* that arose from an *asham* should not be eligible for *temurah* at all, since it is an *olah ha-ba'ah min ha-motarot* and is excluded as *lo ba be-chovah*. The earlier discussion, therefore, presupposed a position — that this *olah* can perform *temurah* — that is actually Rabbi Eliezer's position, not Rabbi Shimon's.

### Same Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אָבִין הָכִי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: אִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ תַּנָּא דְּקָאֵי כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר אֵין מְמִירִין וְחוֹזְרִין וּמְמִירִין, וּסְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר עוֹלָה הַבָּאָה מִן הַמּוֹתָרוֹת עוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּחְזוֹר וְיָמִיר בּוֹ? בִּשְׁנֵי גוּפִין וּקְדוּשָּׁה אַחַת, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **this is the dilemma that Rabbi Avin was raising: If a *tanna* is found who holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that **one cannot effect substitution** once **and again effect substitution** for the same consecrated animal, **and he** also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who said** that **a burnt offering that came from surplus** funds **renders** a non-sacred animal exchanged for it **a substitute, what is** his opinion as to **whether one can again effect substitution with** the animal in question? As explained earlier (9b), Rabbi Avin's dilemma was first posed **with regard to two bodies,** i.e., two different animals, **and one** type of **sanctity,** e.g., in a case where one separated an animal as a guilt offering, and he effected substitution for it, and the animal he separated as a guilt offering developed a blemish and he redeemed it with another animal, which assumed the same status of a guilt offering. **What** is the *halakha* as to whether one can substitute for this replacement? Do we say that since it is a different animal from the one for which he initially effected substitution, the second substitution is effective? Or perhaps, since it possesses the same sanctity as the original animal, one cannot effect substitution for it.

The *gemara* therefore reframes the question: *aliba d'man* — according to whom is this question even relevant? One would need to find a *tanna* who holds both that one cannot perform *temurah* twice with the same animal (Rabbi Shimon according to Rabbi Yochanan) and that an *olah* arising from an *asham* can perform *temurah* (Rabbi Eliezer's position). Rabbi Avin's question thus becomes a hypothetical: if such an opinion were to exist, what would the answer be? *Be-shnei gufin bi-kedushah achas mai* — if the *kedushah* is the same but the body changes — that is, if the animal acquires a *mum* and the *kedushah* is transferred to a replacement — is performing *temurah* on that replacement considered a second *temurah* or a first?

### Different Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּימְצָא לוֹמַר: קְדוּשָּׁה אַחַת (אוֹ לָא), אֶלָּא שְׁתֵּי קְדוּשּׁוֹת וְגוּף אֶחָד, מַאי? תִּיבְּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** then Rabbi Avin further asked: **If you say** that in the above case one cannot effect substitution for the animal, perhaps this is only because the two animals possess **one sanctity. But** in a case of **two sanctities and one body, what** is the *halakha*? For example, if one consecrated a guilt offering and effected substitution for it, and he subsequently lost it and atoned using another animal, and he then found it again, such that the original animal must now be consigned to be sacrificed as a burnt offering, does one say that since the animal now possesses a different sanctity he can effect further substitution for it? The Gemara concludes: According to that *tanna*, **the dilemma remains** unresolved **[*tiba'ei*].**

*Ve-im timtzo lomar* — and if you were to say that scenario is limited (because it is the same *kedushah* passing through a different body, which counts as "twice"), the question extends further: what if the *kedushah* itself changes? In our case, the animal began as an *asham* and transformed into an *olah* — two distinct categories of *kedushah* in a single body. Would performing *temurah* after such a transformation be considered a second *temurah* on the same animal, or a first *temurah* on what is effectively a newly categorized entity?

### Unresolved Tivai

The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *tibai*. This term functions identically to the more familiar *teiku* — the question stands unanswered. It is worth noting, as the *gemara* makes clear, that there may be no *tanna* who actually holds both of the presuppositions necessary to make this question practically relevant. Yet the *gemara* pursues it anyway — a striking illustration of how the *gemara* sometimes presses conceptual questions for their own intrinsic interest, independent of whether any concrete *halakha* turns on them. With this *tibai*, the chapter concludes: *Hadran alach ha-kol memirin* — we complete the first *perek* of Temurah.
