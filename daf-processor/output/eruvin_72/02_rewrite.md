# Eruvin 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shituf vs. Eruv Debate

### Intro: Shituf & Eruv

Eruvin 72 — the two-thirds mark of the tractate — opens in the midst of a crucial *halacha l'maiseh* discussion. The question at stake is whether a *shituf mevo'ot*, the combining together of the various courtyards that open into a shared street, obviates the need for a separate *eruv chatzeirot* — or, conversely, whether courtyards that collectively perform an *eruv* still require a *shituf mevo'ot* in addition.

The structural distinction between the two institutions is as follows. An *eruv* is performed specifically with bread, whereas a *shituf* may be performed with virtually anything — wine being the paradigmatic alternative discussed here. Furthermore, an *eruv* is placed in a house within the courtyard, while a *shituf* is placed in the courtyard itself. The courtyard stands in relation to the *mavoi* as the house stands in relation to the courtyard. Rashi holds that the *shituf* may also be placed in a house, whereas Tosafot maintains it may only be placed in the courtyard — a distinction that matters for Tosafot because it provides the only unambiguous indicator of whether a collective *eruv* of all the courtyards is being labeled an *eruv* or a *shituf*. According to Rashi, where it is placed does not always resolve that question.

The overarching dispute is between Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim. Rabbi Meir holds that one must always perform both — the *eruv chatzeirot* and the *shituf mevo'ot* — while the Chachamim rule that one may serve as a substitute for the other.

### Bread vs. Wine

Picking up from the bottom of 71b, the Gemara records a dispute between Rav Nechumi and Rava about precisely when Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim disagree.

*Pligi bah Rav Nechumi v'Rava* — one of them says: *b'pat d'chulei alma lo pligi d'bachada sagi* — when bread is used for the *shituf mevo'ot*, everyone agrees that one suffices. By choosing bread — a medium that would have been valid for the *eruv chatzeirot* as well — one has already done something that signals the higher standard, and there is therefore no debate. *Ki pligi b'yayin* — the dispute arises only when wine is used for the *shituf*. Wine could not have served for an *eruv chatzeirot*, and so the question is whether such a *shituf* can nonetheless substitute for one.

The other opinion inverts this entirely: *b'yayin d'chulei alma lo pligi d'beina tartei* — when wine is used, everyone agrees that it is insufficient and both are required. *Ki pligi b'pat* — the debate is about bread. Even there, Rabbi Meir insists that both must be performed — though the Gemara noted earlier that his reason is not a conceptual one but a secondary concern, lest the institution of *eruv chatzeirot* be forgotten if *shituf* can always substitute for it.

### Resolving the Dispute

The Gemara then challenges whichever reading places the debate in the case of wine, by citing the language of the Chachamim: *o meiarvin o mishtatfin* — either one performs an *eruv* or a *shituf*. The apparent implication is that each institution operates according to its normal parameters — *eruv* with bread in the courtyards, *shituf* with wine in the *mavoi* — and the Chachamim hold that either one suffices. This would suggest that the wine case is precisely what they are permitting, and what Rabbi Meir disputes.

### Rav Giddel's Reading

Rav Giddel, citing Rav, offers a reinterpretation: *hachi ka'amar* — this is what the Mishnah means. Either one performs an *eruv* in the courtyard with bread, and one is then permitted both there and in the street, or one performs a *shituf* in the *mavoi* — but also with bread — and one is permitted both there and in the individual courtyards. On this reading, the phrase *o meiarvin o mishtatfin* does not invoke wine at all; the word *shituf* here refers to an act performed with bread. Consequently, it remains unclear from the Mishnah whether the dispute concerns wine or bread.

## Practical Rulings

### Three Levels

The Gemara now turns to the practical ruling. Rav Yehuda, citing Rav, declares: *halacha k'Rabbi Meir* — the law follows Rabbi Meir, and one must perform both. Rav Huna says: *minhag k'Rabbi Meir* — the accepted practice follows Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Yochanan says: *nahagu am k'Rabbi Meir* — the people conduct themselves in accordance with Rabbi Meir.

These three formulations represent distinct levels of legal force, a distinction the Gemara has addressed earlier in the tractate. *Halacha* means the ruling is publicly declared and everyone should know it. *Minhag* means the position is accepted but need not be actively imposed; if someone comes with a *shaila*, one rules accordingly, but one does not make a public announcement to correct those who act otherwise. *Nahagu* is the most attenuated: it means that the underlying *halacha* does not actually follow Rabbi Meir, but the people have adopted the practice, and one need not go out of one's way to correct them.

### Tosafot's Challenge

This third level raises an obvious difficulty, which Tosafot addresses directly. If Rabbi Meir's position is merely a *chumra*, why would one ever imagine correcting someone who follows it? The entire concept of *nahagu* implies that there exists a level below it — practices that people adopt but that must be corrected. What possible harm could come from requiring both an *eruv* and a *shituf*?

Tosafot, in the first wide line of his commentary, states: *v'im tomar mah mecha'ah shayach kan im machmirim k'Rabbi Meir* — if people are simply being stringent, why would we stop them? He then gives two answers. First, *d'lo havei bracha l'vatala* — one recites a *bracha* when performing the *eruv* and when performing the *shituf*; if in fact only one is required, making a *bracha* on the unnecessary second act constitutes a *bracha l'vatala*. Second, *im nimna'im al kach l'havi sakin v'chamin l'tzorech mila* — if one mistakenly believes both are required, one may refrain from carrying something urgently needed, such as a knife or hot water for a *bris mila*, because one incorrectly concludes that without both an *eruv* and a *shituf* one cannot carry.

### Tradeoffs of Chumra

This Tosafot captures a broader principle of considerable importance: there is almost no such thing as a purely unadulterated *chumra* with no downside. Every stringency entails a leniency somewhere else. Here, acting like Rabbi Meir when the *halacha* does not require it leads to *brachos l'vatala* on one side, and to failing to fulfill genuine obligations on the other. In this particular case the tradeoff is not simply between *bein adam l'chavero* and *bein adam la'Makom* — it is largely within *bein adam la'Makom* itself, since performing a *bris mila* in its proper time is also a religious imperative. The lesson is that trade-offs of this kind must always be considered when adopting a stringent position.

### How We Pasken

Given that the Gemara concludes *halacha k'Rabbi Meir*, the further question is which version of Rabbi Meir's position we adopt. The dispute in the *rishonim* turns on whether Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim debate the case of wine or the case of bread. If the debate concerns only wine, then performing the *shituf* with bread resolves the issue and one need not perform both separately. Many *rishonim* rule this way.

The *Shulchan Aruch* in *Orach Chaim* 387 states: *hamishtatfin bamavoi tzrichin le'arev bechatzerot* — those who perform a *shituf mevo'ot* must also perform an *eruv chatzeirot*, so that children not forget the institution of *eruv*, since, as Rabbi Meir's language has it, people do not pay attention to what is done in the *mavoi* and may not realize that the *mavoi shituf* was intended to substitute for the courtyard *eruv*. However: *lefikach im nishtatfu bamavoi bepat somchin alav ve'ein tzrichin le'arev bechatzerot* — if the *shituf mevo'ot* was performed with bread, one may rely on it and is not required to also perform an *eruv chatzeirot*. Bread carries the combined significance of both institutions and satisfies both requirements.

### Box of Matzah

This ruling explains the widespread contemporary practice in city-wide *eruvin*. The rabbi takes a single box of *matzah*, transfers ownership through a *kinyan* on behalf of the entire city, and thereby makes everyone partners in that *pat*. Because it is bread — *bepat* — it functions as both *shituf* and *eruv*, and there is no need to perform a separate *eruv* for every individual street or courtyard. As for the quantity of bread required: once one has two meals' worth, the quantity suffices regardless of how many people are involved — even a city of a hundred thousand. The box of *matzah* thus covers the entire population.

One practical consequence is that if one forgot to purchase bread for *Shabbos*, one could in principle obtain a couple of pieces of *matzah* from the rabbi's box, since one is already a partner in it. This also illustrates that the question of *hilchata k'divrei hameikel* — whether we always rule leniently in cases of doubt — is not absolute. The Gemara's last three rulings here all trend toward the stringent position, though within that there is a push to say that at least everyone agrees the bread case is resolved, which is indeed what we rule.

## Apartment Buildings

### Mishna: Five Groups

The next Mishnah introduces a scenario that is, in essence, the question of an apartment building. Five groups (*chamishah chavurot*) all spent *Shabbos* in one large building (*traklin echad*) — one great hall or castle, with different groups in different sections. Rashi notes that the case assumes each group also has its own individual exit into the courtyard, which makes even clearer that from the courtyard's perspective these are separate units. But even apart from the question of the courtyard, the question of the shared internal space — the hallways and common areas — is the apartment building question in its essential form.

### Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

*Beit Shammai* rules: *eruv lechol chavurah vechavurah* — each group must contribute separately to the *eruv*. Each is a separate unit, both for purposes of participating in the courtyard *eruv* and for using the common internal space. *Beit Hillel* rules: *eruv echad lechulam* — one *eruv* suffices for all. The reason one suffices, according to *Beit Hillel*, is that internally they are seen as a single coherent unit. If they are one unit internally, they need no *eruv* among themselves to use the common space; and when contributing to the courtyard's *eruv*, one contribution represents them all.

The Mishnah then records agreement: *umodim bizman shemiktzasan sheruyin bachadarim o va'aliyot shetzrichin eruv lechol chavurah vechavurah* — both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree that if they are in truly separate rooms, or if some are upstairs and some down, each group requires its own separate *eruv*. This signals that there is a degree of physical separation which even *Beit Hillel* concedes renders them distinct units — and the Gemara must now determine what that threshold is.

For the practical question of apartment buildings: the last line of the Mishnah, describing fully separate rooms, represents our case. When people live in genuinely separate units, they are distinct, and if they wish to use common space, they must make an *eruv*. One need not knock on every door; one may take a box of *matzah* and effect a *kinyan* on behalf of all residents — though the presence of non-Jewish residents raises a separate complication to be addressed below.

### Agreement: Separate Units

The distinction established by the Mishnah — that some forms of division render the groups entirely separate even in *Beit Hillel*'s view — is the key to understanding where the two schools' debate is located. The Gemara proceeds to analyze what type of physical division triggers universal agreement, and what type falls into the zone of dispute.

## Walls & Barriers

### Low Barriers Debate

Rav Nachman offers one account of the dispute. *Machloket bemespis* — the debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* concerns a *mespis*, a low or flimsy barrier. *Aval bemechitza asara* — if a genuine halachic wall of ten *tefachim* divides them, then *divrei hakol eruv lechol chavurah vechavurah* — everyone agrees they are separate units. On this reading, *Beit Hillel*'s leniency applies only in the marginal case of minimal separation, while any proper *mechitza* produces agreement that the groups are distinct.

### Both Types Debated

A second version of Rav Nachman's position: *af bemespis machloket* — even the *mespis* case is debated, and the debate extends to the *mechitza* of ten *tefachim* as well. On this reading, *Beit Hillel* holds that even a full ten-*tefach* wall that does not reach the ceiling leaves the groups as one unit, while *Beit Shammai* considers them separate in both cases. This version leaves open the question of what would finally lead *Beit Hillel* to concede that the groups are genuinely distinct.

---

## Wall Height Dispute

### Reaching Ceiling

Bar Rabbi Chiya and Bar Rabbi Shimon bar Rebbe disagree about how to frame the debate. One says: *machlokes bemechitzot hamigi'ot letikra* — the dispute concerns walls that reach all the way to the ceiling. When walls do not reach the ceiling, *divrei hakol eruv echad lechulam* — everyone, including *Beit Shammai*, agrees it is one unit. The debate is only when the wall goes all the way up, where *Beit Hillel* says it remains one unit and *Beit Shammai* says it is separate.

### Not Reaching Ceiling

The other says the opposite: *machlokes bemechitzot she'ein migiot letikra* — the debate is specifically when the wall does not reach the ceiling. When walls do reach the ceiling, *divrei hakol tzrichim eruv lechol chaburah vechaburah* — everyone agrees these are separate units requiring individual participation.

### Analysis & Reconciliation

To lay out the full range of scenarios: at one extreme is the *mespis*, the low flimsy barrier; next is a *mechitza* of ten *tefachim* that does not reach the ceiling; then a wall that approaches but does not quite reach the ceiling; and finally a wall that fully reaches the ceiling (*magias latikra*). The question is where along this spectrum *Beit Hillel* acknowledges separation and where *Beit Shammai* might acknowledge unity.

On the question of what distinguishes "separate rooms" (*chadarim mamash*) in the Mishnah from the cases under debate, Rashi and Tosafot differ. Rashi holds that separate rooms means units originally constructed as distinct, as opposed to one large space subsequently divided. Tosafot holds the distinction is between permanent real walls and non-permanent or improvised partitions. Either way, if the rooms are genuinely separate — whether by original construction or by full permanent walls — everyone agrees they are distinct units, and that is the apartment building case. The debate falls somewhere in the intermediate range of ad hoc or non-permanent divisions that do not quite constitute full separate rooms.

## Rav Yehuda Resolution

### Ceiling Rule

Rav Yehuda Hassavar — a name Rashi explains as connoting analytical sharpness, while Tosafot suggests it may indicate his profession, a place name, or possibly a connection to hat-making — issues an authoritative ruling that resolves the competing versions: *lo nechleku Beit Shammai u'Veit Hillel al mechitzot hamigi'ot latikra shetzrichin eruv lechol chaburah vechaburah* — both schools agree that walls reaching the ceiling create separate groups. *Al mah nechleku? Al mechitzot she'ein migiot latikra* — the dispute is about walls that do not reach the ceiling. *Beit Shammai* says those too make them separate groups requiring individual *eruv* participation; *Beit Hillel* says that where the wall does not reach the ceiling they remain one group.

### Testing Interpretations

This statement of Rav Yehuda Hassavar constitutes a challenge — *tiyuvta* — to the version that holds *Beit Hillel* maintains the groups are one even when the wall reaches the ceiling. If Rav Yehuda Hassavar rules that everyone agrees walls to the ceiling produce separate groups, then the version claiming *Beit Hillel* permits even that case is directly contradicted.

Conversely, it provides support — *siyata* — to the version that locates the debate specifically in walls that do not reach the ceiling.

As for the first version of Rav Nachman — that the debate concerns only the *mespis* while any *mechitza* of ten *tefachim* produces universal agreement on separation — this too is contradicted. Rav Yehuda Hassavar's ruling shows that even a genuine *mechitza* that does not reach the ceiling is still debated; *Beit Hillel* says even that scenario is one unit.

For the second version of Rav Nachman — that the debate encompasses both the *mespis* and the full *mechitza* — the Gemara raises the question of whether this also is contradicted.

### Rav Nachman's Response

Rav Nachman responds: *pligi bimchitza vehu hadin bimispes* — they debate the *mechitza* case, and the same law applies by logical extension to the *mespis* case. The reason the ruling cites a *mechitza* rather than a *mespis* is *lehodiacha kochan deBeit Hillel* — to demonstrate the reach of *Beit Hillel*'s leniency. It is easy to be stringent; it is the leniency that is the *chidush*. By showing that even in the case of a tall wall that nearly reaches the ceiling *Beit Hillel* still considers the space unified, the ruling reveals how far *Beit Hillel* is willing to extend the principle of shared domain. To have debated only the *mespis* case would have shown *Beit Shammai*'s stringency — even a tiny barrier suffices for *Beit Shammai* to call them separate — but the Gemara's principle is *koach deheteira adif*: it is always a greater *chidush* to reveal the extent of the leniency.

Thus, on this reading, the two cases — *mespis* and *mechitza* that does not reach the ceiling — are halachically equivalent, both falling within the zone of debate. In both cases *Beit Hillel* holds one unit, *Beit Shammai* holds two. Only when the wall reaches the ceiling does universal agreement emerge: it is then two groups.

The version holding that even walls to the ceiling leave *Beit Hillel* saying one unit remains flatly contradicted and does not survive.

### Mishnah's Language

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds that this resolution can also be inferred from a close reading of the Mishnah itself. The Mishnah says *umodim* — both schools agree — when they are in *chadarim* or *aliyot*, separate rooms or upper stories, that each requires its own *eruv*. If *chadarim mamash* meant simply rooms with any walls at all, the ruling would be obvious and the Mishnah would be saying nothing new. It must therefore mean something *ke'ein chadarim* — resembling separate rooms — and what does that mean? *Mechitza hamaga'at latikra* — an after-the-fact or non-permanent wall that nonetheless reaches all the way to the ceiling. Since that case produces universal agreement, one can infer that the debate applies to the range of partitions below that threshold. *Shma mina* — this is a valid inference from the Mishnah's precise language.

The upshot for the apartment building: once there are full walls reaching the ceiling — the standard case of any real apartment — both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree these are separate units. Each unit must independently participate in the *eruv* of the courtyard, and if the residents wish to carry in the common hallways, they must create an *eruv* among themselves. One practical solution is a box of *matzah* with a *kinyan* effected on behalf of all Jewish residents, though the presence of non-Jewish residents raises a distinct challenge.

## Receive vs. Contribute

### Contributing Separately

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* that draws an important distinction based on whether the groups in the large building are contributing to an *eruv* or receiving one. *B'meh devarim amurim k'she'molichin et eruvam le'makom acher* — when is the rule that each group must contribute separately? When they are bringing their *eruv* out to another location — that is, when they are the ones contributing to the unified *eruv* of the courtyard, placing their food in someone else's house outside the group.

### Receiving Exception

*Aval im hayah eruvam ba etzlan dei'hen eruv echad le'kulan* — but if the *eruv* comes to them — if they are the ones receiving the *eruv*, meaning the communal food is placed in one of their spaces — then even according to the more stringent opinions, one *eruv* suffices for all of them. Their shared space, however imperfectly unified, is coherent enough to receive as one.

### Two Interpretations

The Gemara offers two ways of understanding this asymmetry. The first version holds that *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* fundamentally agree that these groups are somewhat distinct yet have been unified — like separate units in a courtyard that have combined through a shared *eruv*. They are, as it were, five that have become one. The debate between the schools is not about their fundamental status but about what happens when this unified group wants to contribute outward: *Beit Hillel* says one representative can contribute on behalf of all, while *Beit Shammai* says that even a group that has become internally unified must contribute separately when linking with others, because the act of contributing outward expresses their individual identities.

When receiving, however, even *Beit Shammai* agrees. Since when the *eruv* is received it is simply placed in the space and no active contribution is required from each unit individually, even *Beit Shammai* accepts that the unified group receives as one.

The Gemara challenges this reading from a *baraita*: five houses in a courtyard that collectively made an *eruv* — when they send their *eruv* to link with another courtyard, one person contributing on their behalf suffices. The Gemara asks: whose position does this reflect? On the first version, it follows *Beit Hillel*, since *Beit Shammai* would require separate contributions even when giving. That much is consistent.

The second version of the interpretation holds that the fundamental debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* is precisely about whether these groups constitute a single unit at all — and the asymmetry between giving and receiving is total. When receiving, *Beit Hillel* says they are one unit and one reception suffices; *Beit Shammai* says they remain separate and each must receive individually. When contributing outward, even *Beit Hillel* concedes that the individual identities re-emerge and each must contribute separately. On this reading, the *baraita* about five houses contributing as one goes like no one's position — it is consistent with neither *Beit Shammai* (who requires separate contributions even when receiving) nor *Beit Hillel* (who, on this version, would require separate contributions when giving).

On the broader question of apartment buildings and non-Jewish residents: the concept of *tfisas yad* — the owner's presence in each unit through ownership of major appliances or infrastructure — can unify the building into a single domain, obviating the need for an *eruv* altogether. If the building owner retains ownership of the stove or refrigerator in each unit, the owner's presence creates a unified domain. In cooperative buildings where residents own only shares rather than discrete units, the analysis may similarly support a unified domain, since no resident has exclusive ownership of a particular space. Condominium arrangements require separate analysis, turning on whether the condominium association retains ownership of elements within each individual unit.

## Brothers Scenario

### Mishna: Brothers

The next Mishnah presents a related scenario. *Ha'achim shehayu ochlim al shulchan avihem* — brothers who all eat at their father's table, being supported by and taking their meals with their father, *veshenim bevateihen* — but who sleep each in his own home. The Mishnah rules: *tzrichin eruv lechol echad ve'echad* — they are considered separate units, and each must contribute independently to the *eruv*.

### Separate Contributions

The practical consequence follows: *ulefikach im shachach echad mehem velo eirev mevatel es reshuso* — if one of the brothers forgot to participate in the *eruv*, he must relinquish his rights in the courtyard to allow the others to carry. The asymmetry noted in the previous discussion appears here as well. *Bizman shemolichin eruvam bemakom acher* — this stringency applies when they are contributing their *eruv* elsewhere, meaning the *eruv* is placed in another household in the courtyard. *Aval im hayah eruv ba'etzlan* — but if the *eruv* comes to them, or if there are no other residents sharing the courtyard, *einam tzrichin le'arev* — they need not each contribute separately. The same logic applies: their individual identities become more salient when they are actively contributing outward, while their shared identity is sufficient when they are merely receiving or when there is no external group to interact with.

### Place of Sleeping

The Gemara draws a significant inference from this Mishnah: *shema mina makom lina gorem* — one learns that it is the place of sleeping, not the place of eating, that determines one's halachic domicile. The brothers all eat at their father's table, yet they are treated as separate units because each sleeps in his own home. This principle recurs throughout the halachic literature — for questions of *ner Chanukah*, for yeshiva students who eat communally but sleep in separate dormitory rooms, the operative question is where one sleeps.

Rav Yehuda, citing Rav, qualifies this inference: *be-mikablei pras shanu* — the Mishnah refers to brothers who receive a portion from their father, meaning the father sends them food to eat in their own homes; they are not literally eating at the father's table in the father's house. On this reading, the Mishnah does not actually prove that eating together in one place is irrelevant; perhaps if they ate at the father's house itself, the ruling might differ. The Gemara thus leaves open the theoretical question of whether shared meals in a shared location could define domicile, even as the practical ruling from the Mishnah applies to the case of separately dwelling brothers who receive their food portions separately.

## Valid Domicile

### Gatehouses & Porches

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *mi she-yesh lo beit sha'ar achsadra u-mirpeset be-chatzer chaveiro* — if someone has a gatehouse, a covered porch, or an open colonnade in another person's courtyard, *harei zeh eino oser alav* — this does not create an obligation; such a person need not participate in the *eruv*. These structures are not domiciles, and even if one happens to spend *Shabbos* there, one's presence does not generate the *eruv* obligation.

### Granaries & Stables

However, *beit ha-teven beit ha-bakar beit ha-etzim u-veit ha-otzarot* — a granary, a stable, a woodshed, or a storehouse — *harei zeh oser alav*: these do create an obligation. Although one does not typically reside in such structures, they have four walls, a ceiling, and a certain fitness for use that generates halachic significance. Their owner must therefore participate in the *eruv* of the courtyard they open into.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *eino oser alav ela makom dira bilvad* — only a genuine place of domicile creates an obligation. A structure that is merely four walls and a ceiling, without being actually fit for human habitation, does not suffice.

### Ben Napcha Case

Rav Yehuda cites a case in support: *ma'aseh be-ven Napcha she-hayu lo chamesh chatzerot be-Usha* — a certain man, Ben Napcha, owned parcels of property in five different courtyards in Usha, and the case came before the Sages. They ruled: *eino oser ela beit dira bilvad* — he does not create an obligation in those courtyards, because the property he holds in each is not a domicile. The Gemara notes that the formulation should be corrected: rather than *beit dira* — a house of dwelling — the proper phrase is *makom dira* — a place of dwelling. The distinction points toward the question, taken up in the following discussion, of what precisely qualifies as a *makom dira*: whether it is defined by sleeping, by eating, or by some other indicator of genuine habitation.
