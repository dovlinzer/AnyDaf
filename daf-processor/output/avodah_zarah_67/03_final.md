# Avodah Zarah 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reicha & Ta'am K'Ikar

### Reicha Recap

Today's daf is 67, and we pick up at the top of 67a. Yesterday we covered a major topic in *halakha*: the issue of *reicha* — taste transfer through smell, or more precisely, the halachic status of smell itself. The question was to what degree ingesting smell through the nose or the mouth (for one also smells through the mouth, which is why smell figures into the definition of *mino* or *eino mino*) constitutes actual ingestion of the object itself. We have the principle of *ta'am k'ikar* — the taste of something is like the thing itself — but the question is to what degree we say *reicha k'ikar*.

This was the substance of the debate between *Abaye* and *Rava*, with *Rava* concluding that *reicha lav miltha hi* — smell is not halachically significant. *Tosafot* works through this carefully, noting that a parallel debate appears in the *Gemara* in Pesachim between *Rav* and *Levi* regarding *reicha miltha*, and that based on the standard *klalei pesak*, the conclusion there would seem to favor *reicha miltha* — that smell is a problem. *Rashi* essentially rules according to our *Gemara* against that *Gemara* and holds *reicha lav miltha*. *Tosafot* attempts to reconcile all the *sugyot* by constructing a hierarchy: there may be cases where *reicha* is *miltha*, but those cases are limited.

### Ovens & Communal Use

The cases *Tosafot* identifies as potentially problematic involve very condensed, closed environments — specifically, cooking two items simultaneously in a sealed oven while uncovered, generating substantial steam and transfer. *Tosafot* therefore arrives at the conclusion that one may not cook uncovered *milchigs* and *fleishigs* in the same oven at the same time. However, the extent of *Tosafot*'s concern is precisely this and no more. Cooking one after another — even *treif* after *kosher* — was never in question. Cooking covered items simultaneously was never in question. Even cooking uncovered items simultaneously, *Tosafot* permits in large ovens, and certainly in the ventilated ovens of today, the concern effectively disappears. The one residual stringency *Tosafot* acknowledges is in the *Pesach* context, where *reicha* may not reach the threshold of *ta'am*, but might still constitute a *mashehu*, and by *Pesach* items baked on *Pesach* itself there would be an *issur mashehu*.

Today, to the extent there is any discussion about the status of ovens, it follows *Tosafot*'s framework. The *Rosh*, however, introduces an additional concern — not *reicha* but *zeia*, the actual evaporated liquid, the condensed steam. This is notable precisely because the *Gemara* itself focuses only on *reicha*, and if *zeia* were a problem, one would have expected the *Gemarot* to discuss it. Some *poskim* therefore reject the *zeia* concern entirely; others accept it in principle but rule that in large and ventilated modern ovens it is not practically operative, and certainly not when items are covered.

One additional consideration *Tosafot* raises is that in his time people used communal ovens — if you baked *cholent* you would take it to the baker — which made individual supervision of these conditions impossible. This connects to a broader point about how affluence shapes *halachic* concern. The contemporary *hechsher* industry has trained us to assume that nothing is permissible without full supervision and certification. *Tosafot*, writing in a context where communal ovens were the norm, operated on reasonable presumptions about what the default facts were. Today, where two or more ovens are affordable and common, the practical question re-emerges — but *Tosafot*'s underlying analysis remains the baseline.

### Ta'am K'Ikar Intro

With the *reicha* discussion complete, we continue to topics that are foundational to anyone learning *kashrut*. The *Mishnah* states the governing principle — *ze haklal*, this is the rule — which takes us from *reicha* into the domain of *ta'am* proper, and specifically the principle of *ta'am k'ikar*.

## Ta'am Lifgam

### Positive vs. Bad Taste

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל, כֹּל שֶׁבַּהֲנָאָתוֹ בְּנוֹתֵן טַעַם כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָכִי הִלְכְתָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the mishna: **This is the principle: Anything that benefits** from a forbidden item **imparting flavor** to it, i.e., the forbidden item contributes a positive taste to it, is forbidden, and anything that does not benefit from a forbidden item imparting flavor to it is permitted, e.g., forbidden vinegar that fell onto split beans, as the flavor imparted by the vinegar does not enhance the taste of the beans. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: This is the *halakha*.**

The *Mishnah* establishes: *kol shebehana'ato benoten ta'am* — anything whose taste transfer is a positive taste is forbidden; if it gives a *ta'am lifgam*, a negative or off-putting taste, it is permissible. There is an interesting discussion among the *Rishonim* about a neutral taste; the working assumption is that neutral and positive are treated equivalently, such that only a genuinely negative taste carries the leniency.

### Hot vs. Cold Grits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ גְּרִיסִין רוֹתְחִין, אֲבָל נָפַל לְתוֹךְ גְּרִיסִין צוֹנְנִין וְהִרְתִּיחָן, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּגַם, וְאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The Sages **taught** this **only** with regard to a case **where** the vinegar **fell into hot split beans,** imparting flavor to their detriment. **But** if the vinegar **fell into cold split beans,** the vinegar enhances the flavor, **and** if **one** subsequently **heated them, it becomes like** a dish **that** some added ingredient first **enhanced** its flavor **and ultimately detracted** from it, **and** it is rendered **forbidden,** as the initial flavor that was imparted was beneficial.

*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel* affirms: *hachi hilchata* — this is the ruling. But he immediately adds a qualification. *Lo shanu ela shenafal letoch grisin rotchin* — the case in the *Mishnah* where the vinegar is deemed to give a bad taste applies only when the grain — the oatmeal or grits — was already hot. *Aval nafal letoch grisin tzonenin ve'hirtichan* — but if the vinegar fell into cold grits and the mixture was subsequently heated, *na'aseh kemi she'hivshiach ulevasof pagam ve'asur* — it is treated as a case where the taste initially improved the mixture and only later became off-putting, and remains forbidden.

The logic is as follows. When the non-kosher vinegar — *yayin nesech* vinegar — is mixed into cold grits, vinegar with cold grits apparently produces a pleasing taste. The grits thereby become forbidden, having absorbed a positive taste from a non-kosher substance. When the mixture is then heated and the taste turns slightly unpleasant, the grits remain forbidden: having once acquired forbidden status through a positive taste, that status does not depart. *Na'aseh kemi she'hivshiach ulevasof pagam ve'asur.*

### Tzippori Grits Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ גְּרִיסִין רוֹתְחִין, אֲבָל נָפַל לְתוֹךְ גְּרִיסִין צוֹנְנִין וְהִרְתִּיחָן, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּגַם, וְאָסוּר. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי כּוּ׳. וְכָךְ הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בְּעַרְבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, וְקוֹרְאִין אוֹתָם שַׁחֲלַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he reported that **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The Sages **taught** this **only** with regard to the case **where** the vinegar **fell into hot split beans. But** if the vinegar **fell into cold split beans and one** subsequently **heated** the mixture, **it becomes like** a dish **that** some added ingredient first **enhanced** its flavor **and ultimately detracted** from it, **and** it is rendered **forbidden. And similarly, when Rav Dimi came,** he also reported this in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan and added: **And they would prepare this** dish of split beans and vinegar **on the eves of Shabbat in Tzippori, and they would call it cress dish.**

This same ruling is transmitted twice more — once by *Ravin* in the name of *Rabba bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*, and once by *Rav Dimi* (the *Gemara* does not repeat the formulation a third time in full). The *Gemara* then notes: *vechach hayu osim be'arvei shabbatos beTzippori* — this is what they used to do on *Erev Shabbat* in Tzippori. It was apparently a classic *Shabbat* dish, mixing vinegar with cold grits — referred to as *shachlayim*. One might ask why they would mix it cold if heating it would produce an off-putting taste. *Tosafot* explains that they would mix it cold, heat it up, and then let it cool again; once cold again, the off-taste was no longer present.

### Reish Lakish Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם שֶׁאָמְרוּ, לֹא שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ: קְדֵירָה זוֹ חֲסֵירָה מֶלַח, יְתֵירָה מֶלַח, חֲסֵירָה תַּבְלִין, יְתֵירָה תַּבְלִין, אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁאֵין חֲסֵירָה כְּלוּם, וְאֵינָהּ נֶאֱכֶלֶת מִפְּנֵי זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** With regard to the principle **that** the Sages **said,** that if a forbidden food **imparts flavor** to a permitted food **to** the **detriment** of the mixture it remains permitted, the criterion is **not that** people **would say: This dish is lacking in salt** or **is overabundant in salt, is lacking in spices** or **is overabundant in spices,** and that is why its flavor was detracted by the forbidden food. **Rather,** it is referring to **any** dish **that is not lacking in anything, but will not be eaten** only **because of this** forbidden substance that fell into it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם שֶׁאָמְרוּ, אֵין אוֹמְרִין: קְדֵירָה זוֹ חֲסֵירָה מֶלַח, יְתֵירָה מֶלַח, חֲסֵירָה תַּבְלִין, יְתֵירָה תַּבְלִין, אֶלָּא הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא הָא פָּגְמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that **Reish Lakish states** a lenient interpretation of the principle: With regard to that **which** Sages **said** that if a forbidden food **imparts flavor** to a permitted food **to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it remains permitted, **one does not say** that a certain food is forbidden because its flavor was not actually detracted by the forbidden substance, as **this dish is lacking in salt** or **is overabundant in salt, is lacking in spices** or **is overabundant in spices,** and it is for that reason that the forbidden substance detracted from its flavor. **Rather,** since **now, in any event,** the forbidden substance **detracted** from its flavor, it is permitted.

*Reish Lakish* now introduces a further nuance about the scope of *noten ta'am lifgam*. His statement — *lo she'yomru kedira zo chasera melach yetira melach chasera tavlin yetira tavlin* — defines when *ta'am lifgam* does not apply. The passage is admittedly difficult to parse, and two readings are presented.

According to the first, more stringent reading: *noten ta'am lifgam* as a leniency is unavailable in any case where the bad taste can be attributed to a contributing factor other than the forbidden additive — for instance, if the spices in the pot are slightly off. If one can say, "the reason this tastes bad is that the seasoning was already unbalanced, and had the seasoning been correct this additive would have improved the dish," then one may not invoke *ta'am lifgam*. *Ta'am lifgam* applies only when the spices are perfectly balanced and the forbidden additive alone is the source of the off-taste. This is the narrow, stringent reading — *ela kol she'eina chasera klum ve'eina ne'echelet mipnei zeh*: only when there is nothing else deficient and the bad taste is attributable solely to this additive.

The second, more lenient reading: we do not theorize about hypothetical circumstances. The relevant question is not what the taste would have been under ideal conditions, but what the taste actually is right now. If at this moment the additive contributed to an off-putting taste, that is sufficient for *ta'am lifgam* — regardless of whether, had the spices been better, the same additive would have enhanced the dish. *Ela hashta mei'ha pagma* — right now there is a bad taste, and that is what matters.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא: אִם נָתַן טַעַם לִפְגָם — מוּתָּר! הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא מִילֵּי אַחְרָנְיָיתָא דְּפַגְמַהּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּלִישָּׁנָא בָּתְרָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** then **let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **say: If** the forbidden food **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it is **permitted.** Why does he use the term: Amplified? The Gemara answers that **this** is what Rabbi Yoḥanan **teaches us: That even if there are other substances that detracted** from the flavor of the mixture **along with** the forbidden food, e.g., insufficient salt or excessive seasoning, this is not taken into consideration; since the forbidden food detracted from its flavor, the mixture is permitted. The Gemara comments: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with the last version** of the statement **of Reish Lakish.**

*Rav Ami* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Abahu* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* both affirm the practical ruling. The *halakha* follows the second, lenient position. One need not speculate about hypotheticals; as long as in the actual present circumstance the additive contributed to an off-putting taste, it is permissible. This is further confirmed by the *Gemara*'s language: *ve'hilchata k'lishna batra de'Reish Lakish* — we rule like the latter formulation of *Reish Lakish*.

One additional textual point: the *Gemara* uses the phrasing *riba* ("increased") rather than simply *natan* ("gave") a bad taste. This is intentional — *haka mashma lan d'af agav d'ika milei achrinaysa d'pogma bahadei* — even if there are other contributing factors also making the dish unpleasant (like imbalanced spices), if this additive increased the off-putting quality, it is still *ta'am lifgam* and therefore permissible. The forbidden substance need not be the sole source of the bad taste.

A further note on the phrase *einah ne'echelet mipnei zeh* — "it is not eaten because of this." *Rashi* immediately clarifies: *lav davka*. This is not literal inedibility. Any practitioner of *kashrut* knows that food cooked in a *treif keli* after twenty-four hours is not actually inedible — it simply tastes slightly less good. *Noten ta'am lifgam* requires only that the taste be rendered somewhat less desirable, not that the food become impossible to eat. Nevertheless, as we will see when we arrive at the source from *neveilah*, some *Rishonim* were drawn to read the *Gemara*'s cases as requiring actual inedibility, and the language here and the paradigm case together provide them a foothold. The *Ra'ah* offers a creative resolution: if a food is rendered off-putting, that is evidence that the *issur* taste, were it extractable in isolation, would be inedible. The logical connection is admittedly strained, but it allows the *Ra'ah* to honor the *Gemara*'s language while preserving the practical *psak* that mere off-putting taste is sufficient.

## Taste vs. Essence

### Three Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כֹּל שֶׁטַּעְמוֹ וּמַמָּשׁוֹ אָסוּר, לוֹקִין עָלָיו, וְזֶהוּ כְּזַיִת בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס.
>
> **Translation:** § Furthermore, with regard to a forbidden food that became mixed with a permitted food, **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In **any** case **where the flavor and substance of** the forbidden food are perceptible in the mixture, the mixture is **forbidden,** and **one is flogged for** consuming **it. And** it is a tradition that **this is** the measure for such a case: One who eats **an olive-bulk** of the forbidden element in the mixture **in** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread is liable for eating the forbidden food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמוֹ וְלֹא מַמָּשׁוֹ — אָסוּר, וְאֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו, וְאִם רִיבָּה טַעַם לִפְגָם — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** But if only **the flavor of** the forbidden food is recognizable in the mixture, **but not its substance,** as it was completely dissolved into the permitted food, the mixture **is forbidden, but one is not flogged for** consuming **it. And if** the forbidden food **amplified the flavor** of the permitted food **to its detriment,** it is **permitted.**

*Rabbi Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* articulates three levels. First: *kol sheta'amo umamasho assur v'lokin alav* — any case where one has both the taste and the essence of a forbidden substance is forbidden, and one receives lashes. *V'zehu k'zayit b'khdei akhilat pras* — this is when the quantity of forbidden substance in the mixture is sufficient that one consumes a *k'zayit* of the forbidden item within the time it takes to eat a *pras* (half a loaf of bread). The debate between *Rashi* and *Rambam* regarding the size of a *pras* — eight *k'zeisim* or nine — yields the practical implication that if the forbidden substance constitutes roughly one-eighth to one-ninth of the mixture, and one eats the mixture at a normal rate, one will have ingested a *k'zayit* of the forbidden substance within the *achilat pras* window and will be liable to lashes.

Second: *ta'amo v'lo mamasho assur v'ein lokin alav* — if one has the taste of the forbidden substance but not its essence, it is forbidden but does not incur lashes. Third: *v'im riba ta'am lifgam mutar* — and if the taste is off-putting, it is permissible.

### Rashi's Distinction

What is the distinction between *ta'amo umamasho* and *ta'amo v'lo mamasho*? *Rashi* identifies a physical difference. *Ta'amo umamasho* — taste and essence — describes a case where the forbidden and permitted substances are physically of the same type and intimately commingled: *chazir* soup dissolved into chicken soup, or a piece of *chazir* that fell into *cholent* and cannot be located. In such cases, the forbidden substance is present as substance, not merely as flavor. By contrast, if a drop of *chazir* soup — liquid — is absorbed into a solid, such as a steak, *Rashi* considers that *ta'amo v'lo mamasho*: only the taste has been transferred, not the thing itself. The boundary between these categories is admittedly imprecise, but the distinction is a physical one.

For *Rashi*, this determines the scope of *ta'am k'ikar* as a question. The principle — "the taste of something is like the thing itself" — is only in question when dealing with *ta'amo v'lo mamasho*, the absorbed-flavor scenario. *Ta'amo umamasho*, like ground *chazir* mixed into ground beef, is not a *ta'am k'ikar* question at all; it is simply the thing itself, and everyone agrees it is *d'oraisa*. The question of *ta'am k'ikar* — is the taste treated like the substance? — only arises in the liquid-into-solid scenario. And since the *Gemara* concludes *ein lokin alav* in that case, *Rashi* derives that *ta'am k'ikar* is *lav d'oraisa* — not a biblical prohibition. All the concerns of pots and absorbed flavors are, according to *Rashi*, rabbinic in origin.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

*Tosafot* presents the matter differently and offers several alternative readings. The first alternative is that even if *ta'am k'ikar* is *d'oraisa*, it does not follow that one receives lashes. The letter *kaf* in *ta'am k'ikar* — "taste is **like** the thing itself" — may mean not that it **is** the thing itself, but that it is forbidden in the same way. It is an *issur* derived from a different *pasuk*, a more general prohibition. Under this reading, the absence of lashes does not disprove that *ta'am k'ikar* is biblical; it simply means it is a different category of biblical *issur* for which lashes do not apply.

A second alternative: the distinction between *ta'amo umamasho* and *ta'amo v'lo mamasho* is not a physical distinction but a quantitative one. *Ta'amo umamasho* is when the mixture meets the threshold of *k'zayit bichdei achilat pras*, and one is therefore liable because one has actually consumed a *k'zayit* of the forbidden substance. *Ta'amo v'lo mamasho* — no lashes — simply describes a proportion below that threshold. Under this reading, *ta'am k'ikar* is fully *d'oraisa*, but the *Gemara*'s no-lashes ruling is a consequence of insufficient quantity, consistent with the principle that *chatzi shiur assur min ha-Torah* but does not incur lashes. This reading was noted to align well with *Rabbi Yochanan*'s own ruling on *chatzi shiur*.

*Rambam* charts a middle course: *ta'am k'ikar* is *d'oraisa* when the proportion reaches *k'zayit bichdei achilat pras*, and *derabbanan* below that threshold. Regarding *keilim* specifically — the prohibition on cooking in a *chazir* pot — *Rambam* holds that a *kli bas yoma* is forbidden *d'oraisa*, even as a separate question from how the *Torah* generally treats *ta'am*. There is a *Gemara* that suggests *keilim* may be a *chiddush*, a standalone prohibition independent of the general *ta'am* principle: even if *ta'am k'ikar* is not fully biblical, the *Torah* may separately prohibit cooking in a vessel that absorbed *chazir*, as a *lechatchila* concern. The *Gemara* nonetheless treats the two as intimately linked — *Rabbi Meir*'s argument from *giulei ovdei kochavim* assumes that the vessel laws reflect the general *ta'am* principle — and so the debate about the biblical status of *ta'am k'ikar* carries weight in this domain as well.

## Rabbi Meir vs. Shimon

### Baraita Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִדִּבְרֵי כּוּלָּם נִלְמַד נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם מוּתָּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁלָמָא מִכּוּלְּהוּ לְחַיֵּי, אֶלָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ — ״אָמְרוּ״ קָאָמַר, וְלֵיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Kahana says: From the statements of all** the *amora'im* who were cited, namely, Shmuel, Rabbi Yoḥanan, and Reish Lakish, **we learn** that if a forbidden food **imparts flavor** to a permitted food **to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it is **permitted. Abaye said to him: Granted, from all** the rest **of them** this conclusion **is very well; but** how can this be concluded from the statement **of Reish Lakish? He says** only that the Sages **said** that if a forbidden food imparts flavor to a permitted food to its detriment, the mixture is permitted. Perhaps he is only citing what others said **and he himself does not hold accordingly.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם — אָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Rav Kahana's statement: **By inference, is there one who says** that if a forbidden food **imparts flavor** to a permitted food **to** the **detriment** of the mixture, it is **forbidden?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, וְהָתַנְיָא: אֶחָד נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם וְאֶחָד נוֹתֵן טַעַם לְשֶׁבַח — אָסוּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְשֶׁבַח — אָסוּר, וְלִפְגָם — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answer: **Yes, and** this opinion **is taught** in a *baraita*: **Both** in a case where the forbidden food **imparts flavor to** the **detriment** of the flavor of the permitted food, **and** in a case where it **imparts flavor that enhances** the permitted food, the mixture is **forbidden;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Shimon says:** If it **enhances** the flavor it is **forbidden, but** if it causes it **detriment** it is **permitted.**

Having established that *noten ta'am lifgam mutar* is the ruling, the *Gemara* asks whether anyone would actually forbid bad taste. The answer: yes. *Detanya* — we taught in a *baraita*: *echad noten ta'am lifgam ve'echad noten ta'am leshevach assur* — whether the taste is positive or negative, it is forbidden. These are the words of *Rabbi Meir*. *Rabbi Shimon* says: positive taste is forbidden, bad taste is permissible — and it is like *Rabbi Shimon* that we rule.

This is, as one might note, an unusual *psak*. We do not ordinarily rule like *Rabbi Shimon* against *Rabbi Meir*.

### Rabbi Meir's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? גָּמַר מִגִּיעוּלֵי גוֹיִם, גִּיעוּלֵי גוֹיִם לָאו נוֹתֵן טַעַם לִפְגָם הוּא? וְאָסַר רַחֲמָנָא, הָכִי נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Meir? He derives** this *halakha* **from** the case of vessels of **gentiles** that require **purging,** i.e., vessels that gentiles used for cooking, which the Torah requires that one purge through fire and ritually purify before they may be used by Jews (see Numbers 31:22–23 and mishna on 75b). **Is it not** the case that vessels of **gentiles** that require **purging impart flavor** to food that is cooked in them **to** their **detriment?** Since time has passed since the gentiles cooked non-kosher food in the vessels, the flavor that the vessels transmit to food that a Jew cooks in them is certainly detrimental, **and** yet **the Merciful One deems** their use **prohibited** without purging. **So too,** the case here **is no different,** and even if the flavor imparted by the forbidden food is a detrimental one, the mixture should be forbidden.

*Mai ta'ama de'Rabbi Meir* — what is the source for *Rabbi Meir*'s position? *Gamar migiulei ovdei kochavim* — he derives it from the *giulei ovdei kochavim*, the purging of vessels taken from non-Jews. The word *giulei* relates to *hagalah* — expulsion, purging — and refers to the taste expelled from those vessels. When the *Torah* commanded the Israelites, after capturing vessels from Midian, to pass them through fire and through hot water, *Chazal* understand this not only as a *taharah* process but as *kashering* — removing the absorbed *issur* taste. Now, the taste absorbed in those vessels after use would by definition be off-putting — it is the residual, stale taste of old food. Yet the *Torah* required *kashering*. This proves, says *Rabbi Meir*, that even an off-putting taste is halachically significant. *Lav noten ta'am lifgam hu? Ve'asrah Rachmana* — was it not bad taste? Yet the *Torah* forbade using those vessels. *Hacha nami lo shena* — the same applies generally.

### Rabbi Shimon Reply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? כִּדְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִיָּיא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִיָּיא: לֹא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא קְדֵירָה בַּת יוֹמָא, דְּלָא לִפְגָם הוּא. וְאִידָּךְ? קְדֵירָה בַּת יוֹמָא נָמֵי אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא פָּגְמָה פּוּרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the opinion of **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Shimon, who deems the mixture permitted if the flavor imparted is detrimental, can be explained **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Huna, son of Rav Ḥiyya; as Rav Huna, son of Rav Ḥiyya, says:** With regard to the vessels of gentiles, **the Torah prohibits only a pot** that was used for cooking **on that** very **day, which does not** yet impart flavor **to** the **detriment** of the food cooked in it. Rather, the flavor that it imparts is not considered detrimental. **And** the opinion of **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Meir, can also be explained in accordance with this statement, as in his opinion, **even** in the case of **a pot** that was used for cooking **on that** very **day, it is not possible that it does not detract** from the flavor of food that is subsequently cooked in it even **slightly.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כׇּל נְבֵלָה לַגֵּר אֲשֶׁר בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״, כָּל הָרְאוּיָה לַגֵּר — קְרוּיָה נְבֵילָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reasoning** of **Rabbi Shimon?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that from the verse: **"You shall not eat of any unslaughtered animal carcass; you may give it to the resident alien [*la'ger*] who is within your gates,** that he may eat it" (Deuteronomy 14:21), it is derived that with regard to animal carcasses, **anything that is fit for a *ger*** *toshav* to consume **is called an unslaughtered carcass** and is forbidden,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין רְאוּיָה לְגֵר — אֵינָהּ קְרוּיָה נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** and any carcass **that is unfit** even **for a *ger*** *toshav* to consume, e.g., one that turned rancid and is unfit for consumption, **is not called an unslaughtered carcass** with regard to its prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הָהוּא לְמַעוֹטֵי סְרוּחָה מֵעִיקָּרָא, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — סְרוּחָה מֵעִיקָּרָא לָא צְרִיכָא מִיעוּטָא, עַפְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what can **Rabbi Meir** respond to this? He could say: **That** verse is written **to exclude** meat that was **rancid at the outset,** i.e., that was not fit for consumption even before the animal's death, due to a defect in the animal. By contrast, a carcass that was fit for consumption when the animal died and was consequently rendered forbidden remains forbidden even when it becomes rancid. **And Rabbi Shimon** could counter that meat that was **rancid at the outset does not need** specific **exclusion** by the verse, as **it is** considered as **mere dust** and does not fall under the category of an unslaughtered animal carcass.

*Rabbi Shimon* responds through the ruling of *Rav Huna son of Rav Chiya*: *lo asrah Torah ella kedeira bas yoma delav lifgamo hu* — the *Torah* only forbade those vessels on the day itself when the food was absorbed, because on that very day the taste is not yet off-putting. The *Torah*'s prohibition thus applied only when the taste was still good. Implicitly, had the vessels been used a day later, the *Torah* would not have forbidden them — *ta'am lifgam* being permissible. *Rabbi Meir* rejoins: *kedeira bas yoma nami ee efshar delo pagma purta* — even on the same day, the absorbed taste is already slightly off; the *Torah* forbade it even then, proving that bad taste is a problem. The dispute thus turns on an empirical question: is day-of absorption truly a good taste, or already slightly deteriorated?

A word here about the relationship between *keilim* and *ta'am k'ikar*: while *Rambam* may treat a *kli bas yoma* as a separate *chiddush* independent of the general *ta'am* principle, the *Gemara* here clearly treats the two as linked. *Rabbi Meir*'s argument works only if the vessel laws reflect the same principle as *ta'am* in general.

### Starkist Analogy

*Rabbi Shimon*'s broader position derives from a *baraita* on *neveilah*. The *pasuk* states: *lo tokhlu kol neveilah, lager asher bishe'arekha* — you shall not eat any carcass; give it to the stranger in your gates. The *baraita* derives: *kol haraui lager kriya neveilah* — only if it is fit for human consumption is it called a *neveilah* and subject to the prohibition; *she'eino raui lager eino kriya neveilah* — if it is not fit for consumption, it is not a *neveilah* and is permissible. From this *Rabbi Shimon* derives that a *ta'am* that is undesirable is not the *ta'am* the *Torah* forbade.

*Rabbi Meir* counters: *hahu l'motei srukha me'ikara* — that *pasuk* comes to exclude only an animal that was already putrid and inedible from the moment of death, never having held the status of *neveilah*. But if the animal died while edible and then became inedible, it remains *assur* — because once it acquired the status of *neveilah assur*, that status adheres. This is a general *halachic* principle: once something acquires a *halachic* status, a higher threshold is required to remove it than was required to confer it in the first place. *Rabbi Shimon* responds: *srukha me'ikara lo tzerikha mi'uta* — an animal that was never edible did not even need a *pasuk* to permit it. *Afra b'alma hu* — it is mere dirt, not food, not a *neveilah* at all. The *chiddush* of the *pasuk* must therefore be that even an animal that began edible and became inedible is permissible — teaching us that *ta'am lifgam* is permissible.

All the *Rishonim* ask the obvious question: what does an inedible *neveilah* carcass have to do with *ta'am lifgam*, which is merely slightly off-putting? As noted earlier, the *Ra'avad* attempts to read all the *Gemara*'s *ta'am lifgam* cases as involving genuinely inedible food — an approach that cannot be sustained. The *Ra'ah*'s creative solution — that off-putting taste is evidence that the extracted *issur* taste would itself be inedible — has an acknowledged logical gap. The general consensus among *Rishonim* resolves the matter by analogy, and it is worth spelling out.

For an object of food to become *mutar*, it must become something no normal person wishes to eat. For a *taste* mixed into a permitted dish to become *mutar*, the analogous standard is: it must be a taste that no normal person wishes to taste. The *Torah*'s prohibition of *ta'am* — "taste is like the thing itself" — refers only to taste that people desire. Taste that no one wants is not the *ta'am* the *Torah* meant to prohibit. The analogy holds structurally even if not physically: as the only *neveilah* that is forbidden is one fit for consumption, so the only *ta'am* that is forbidden is one fit to be tasted — a taste that is welcome and desired. The principle, as one might express it, is that the *Torah* does not want tunas with good taste; it wants tunas that taste good.

## Good Then Bad

### Ulla's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּגַם, אֲבָל פָּגַם מֵעִיקָּרָא — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla says:** This **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon is with regard to a forbidden food **that enhanced** the flavor of a dish when it first fell into it **and subsequently detracted** from its flavor. In this case Rabbi Meir deems the dish forbidden, since the forbidden food enhanced its flavor at the outset. **But** in a case of forbidden food that **detracted** from the flavor of the dish **at the outset, everyone agrees** that it is **permitted.**

The *neveilah* debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* raises a sharp question about *ta'am lifgam*. *Rabbi Meir* argues that a *neveilah* that began edible and became inedible remains *assur* — the initial status sticks. If we analogize *ta'am lifgam* to *neveilah*, it would seem that when we say *ta'am lifgam* is *mutar*, we can only mean a case where the taste was bad from the very outset. If the taste started good and then became bad — *hivshiach ulevasof pagam* — by the *neveilah* analogy it should remain *assur*. This is precisely the question.

*Ulla* says: *machlokes she'hivshiach ulevasof pagam* — the debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* concerns specifically the case where the taste started good and became bad, paralleling their debate about *neveilah*. *Aval pagam me'ikara divrei hakol mutar* — but if the taste was bad from the outset, everyone agrees it is permissible.

### Rav Chaga Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב חַגָּא לְעוּלָּא: יַיִן שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ עֲדָשִׁים, וְחוֹמֶץ שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ גְּרִיסִין — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. וְהָא הָכָא דִּפְגַם מֵעִיקָּרָא הוּא, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥagga raised an objection to** the opinion of **Ulla** from a *baraita*: Forbidden **wine that fell into lentils, or** forbidden **vinegar that fell into split beans,** renders the food **forbidden. And Rabbi Shimon deems** them **permitted. But here** it is a case **where** the forbidden food **detracted** from the flavor of the dish **at the outset, and** the *tanna'im* **disagree.**

*Rav Chaga* challenges *Ulla* from a *baraita*: *yayin she'nafal le'toch adashim ve'chometz she'nafal le'toch grisin assur, Rabbi Shimon matir* — wine falling into lentils and vinegar falling into grits is forbidden; *Rabbi Shimon* permits it. *Rav Chaga* reads this as a case of *pagam me'ikara* — the taste was bad from the start — and yet *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* still debate it, implying the debate extends to *pagam me'ikara*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: חַגָּא לָא מִידָּע יָדַע מַאי קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, תְּיוּבְתָּא קָא מוֹתֵיב? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ גְּרִיסִין צוֹנְנִין וְהִרְתִּיחָם, נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּגַם, וְאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: Ḥagga does not know what the Sages say,** yet **he raises an objection? Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the vinegar **fell into** the **cold split beans and** one subsequently **heated them,** in which case **it becomes like** a forbidden food **that enhanced** the flavor of the mixture **and subsequently detracted** from it, as vinegar enhances the flavor of cold split beans, **and it is** consequently **forbidden** according to Rabbi Meir.

*Ulla* is dismissive: *Chaga lo yada mai kamri rabbanan* — *Chaga* does not even understand the *baraita* he is citing. The case was in fact *nafal le'toch grisin tzonanin ve'hirticham* — the vinegar fell into cold grits and they were subsequently heated. As we established earlier, that is a case of *hivshiach ulevasof pagam* — the mixture first tasted good, then became bad — and it is that case which remains in dispute.

### Rabbi Yochanan Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בְּפוֹגֵם מֵעִיקָּרָא מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon is **with regard to** a case where the forbidden food **detracts** from the flavor of the permitted food **from the outset.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּפוֹגֵם מֵעִיקָּרָא מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל הִשְׁבִּיחַ וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּגַם — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר, אוֹ דִּלְמָא בֵּין בָּזוֹ וּבֵין בָּזוֹ מַחְלוֹקֶת? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does Rabbi Yoḥanan mean that the **dispute is with regard to** a case where the forbidden food **detracts** from its flavor **from the outset, but** if it **enhanced** it at first **and subsequently detracted** from it, **everyone agrees** that it is **forbidden? Or, perhaps** he means that the **dispute** is **both in this** case **and in that** case. The Gemara comments: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees with *Ulla* entirely. *Be'pogem me'ikara machlokes* — *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* debate specifically the case where the taste was bad from the very beginning. This prompts the question: does *Rabbi Yochanan* mean that when the taste *hivshiach ulevasof pagam* — started good and became bad — everyone would agree it is *assur*? Or does he mean that all cases, including *hivshiach ulevasof pagam*, are in dispute? The *Gemara* leaves this as *teiku*.

### Sourdough Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם: אֶפְשָׁר אִיתָא לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְלָא תַּנְיָא לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said: Is it possible** that **there is** substance **to this** statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan,** i.e., that there is disagreement with regard to a case where the forbidden food detracts from the flavor of the permitted food from the outset, **but it is not taught in the Mishna?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַק דָּק וְאַשְׁכַּח, דְּתַנְיָא: שְׂאוֹר שֶׁל חוּלִּין שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ הָעִיסָּה, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהַחְמִיץ וְהֶחְמִיצָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָפַל שְׂאוֹר שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה אוֹ שְׂאוֹר שֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְהַחְמִיץ — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Amram **went out, examined** the Mishna, **and discovered that** this dispute **is taught** in a mishna (*Orla* 2:8): In the case of **non-sacred leaven that fell into the dough, and there is enough of it to cause** the dough **to become leavened, and** the dough indeed **became leavened, and subsequently leaven of *teruma*, or leaven of** forbidden **diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard, fell** into this dough, **and there is enough of it to cause** the dough **to become leavened,** the dough is **forbidden,** because the forbidden leaven is considered to have contributed to the process of the leavening. **But Rabbi Shimon deems** the dough **permitted,** because the additional leaven has a negative impact on the dough, which was already leavened.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דִּפְגַם מֵעִיקָּרָא הוּא, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Amram comments: **But here it is** a case **where** the forbidden substance **detracted** from the flavor of the dough **at the outset, and** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon **disagree,** as a mishna that is not attributed explicitly to a *tanna* represents the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: שָׁאנֵי עִיסָּה, הוֹאִיל וּרְאוּיָה לְחַמֵּעַ בָּהּ כַּמָּה עִיסּוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Dough is different,** because even if the additional leaven is detrimental to its flavor, in any event the dough is enhanced, **since it is suitable for leavening several other** batches of **dough with it.** The more it is leavened, the more it is enhanced in terms of this purpose.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׂאוֹר שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה וְשֶׁל חוּלִּין שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לְתוֹךְ הָעִיסָּה, בְּזֶה כְּדֵי לְהַחְמִיץ וּבְזֶה כְּדֵי לְהַחְמִיץ וְחִימְּצוּ — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. נָפַל שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה תְּחִלָּה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר. נָפַל שֶׁל חוּלִּין וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָפַל שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה אוֹ שֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another source for the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Come** and **hear** that which is taught in a *baraita*: In the case of **leaven of *teruma* and non-sacred** leaven **that fell into the dough,** if **this one** was **sufficient to cause** the dough **to become leavened and that one** was **sufficient to cause** the dough **to become leavened, and they** both **cause** the dough **to become leavened,** it is **forbidden. Rabbi Shimon deems** it **permitted.** If the leaven **of *teruma* fell** in **first, everyone agrees** that it is **forbidden.** If the **non-sacred** leaven **fell** in first **and subsequently** the leaven **of *teruma* or of diverse kinds** that were planted **in a vineyard fell** in, this is also **forbidden; but Rabbi Shimon deems** it **permitted.**

*Rav Pappa* asks: is it possible that *Rabbi Yochanan* is correct that the dispute concerns *pagam me'ikara* and yet this is never reflected in a *Mishnah*? He investigates and finds the following *Mishnah* supporting *Rabbi Yochanan*. Sourdough (*se'or*) of *chulin* fell into dough, sufficient to cause fermentation and duly fermenting it. Subsequently, forbidden sourdough — *se'or* of *terumah* or of *kilayim* — fell in, also in sufficient quantity to ferment. Because the dough was already fully fermented, this second addition only over-ferments — which is *pagam me'ikara* — yet it is *assur*, and *Rabbi Shimon* permits.

*Rabbi Zeira* counters: this may not prove the general point. The reason *Rabbi Meir* might forbid in the sourdough case is not that he treats *pagam me'ikara* as forbidden in general, but that sourdough over-fermenting dough is not truly *pagam* at all — the excess sourdough makes the dough better as a starter or culture even if worse as bread. So *Rabbi Meir*'s stringency here might be case-specific.

The *Gemara* therefore tries a second case: *se'or* of *terumah* and *se'or* of *chulin* that fell in simultaneously, each in sufficient quantity to ferment. Together they over-ferment. This is *assur*; *Rabbi Shimon* permits. But this case is ambiguous — one cannot clearly assign the *pagam* to either component alone.

A third sub-case resolves matters. When *terumah* sourdough fell in *first* — *divrei hakol assur* — everyone agrees it is *assur*. This is a classic case of *hivshiach ulevasof pagam*: the *terumah* fell in and initially enhanced the fermentation, then the whole became over-fermented and unpleasant. Yet everyone agrees it is forbidden. This is a direct proof for *Rabbi Yochanan*: *hivshiach ulevasof pagam*, everyone agrees, is *assur*.

### Final Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דִּפְגַם מֵעִיקָּרָא, וּפְלִיגִי! וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכָא נָמֵי
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But here** it is a case **where** the forbidden substance **detracted** from the flavor of the dough **from the outset, and they disagree. And if you would say: Here too,**

The case where *chulin* sourdough fell in first and *terumah* fell in second is then the *machlokes*. From the moment the *terumah* sourdough entered the already-fermented dough, it added a bad taste — *pagam me'ikara*. That is the dispute between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon*.

Now one might still object: perhaps *Rabbi Meir*'s stringency in the sourdough cases is due to *Rabbi Zeira*'s reasoning, not a general view on *pagam me'ikara*. The *Gemara* answers by pointing to the wine-into-lentils and vinegar-into-grits cases, which are straightforwardly *pagam me'ikara* and present the same *machlokes*. And one cannot read those as *hivshiach ulevasof pagam* (the cold-grits reading *Ulla* proposed), because the sourdough *Mishnah* already established that *hivshiach ulevasof pagam* is *divrei hakol assur* — if those cases were *hivshiach ulevasof pagam*, they could not be a *machlokes*. Therefore they must be *pagam me'ikara*, and the *machlokes* is there. *Ela lav shema mina bepagam me'ikara machlokes — shema mina.*

The conclusion: *noten ta'am lifgam* is *mutar*, but only when the off-putting taste existed from the moment the forbidden substance entered the mixture — *pogem mitechila*. That is the debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon*, and we rule like *Rabbi Shimon* that *pagam me'ikara* is *mutar*. If, however, the substance initially improved the taste and only subsequently the taste became bad — *hivshiach ulevasof pagam* — then the mixture became *assur* when it tasted good, and that *issur* status does not leave it. Everyone agrees that in such a case one cannot rely on *noten ta'am lifgam*.
