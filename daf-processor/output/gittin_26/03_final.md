# Gittin 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tofes Forms Overview

### Pre-written Forms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכּוֹתֵב טוֹפְסֵי גִּיטִּין, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּנִּיחַ מְקוֹם הָאִישׁ, וּמְקוֹם הָאִשָּׁה, וּמְקוֹם הַזְּמַן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a scribe **who writes the standard** part **[*tofes*] of bills of divorce** in advance, so that when one requests a bill of divorce, he will need to add only the details unique to this case, **he must leave** empty the **place** in the bill of divorce for the name **of the man, and** the **place** for the name **of the woman, and** the **place for the date.**

The *daf* opens by situating this *mishnah* within the broader discussion of writing a *get* *lishmah*. The previous *mishnah* had clearly reflected the position of *Rabbi Elazar* — emphasizing the *lishmah* requirement in writing, focusing on the names of the specific man and woman and the *leshem gerushin*. With that framework in place, the *mishnah* before us turns to a related but distinct question: the practice of pre-writing *tofes* forms.

The *mishnah* opens with the case of *hakotev tofsei gittin* — scribes who wish to prepare the boilerplate text of a *get* in advance, ready to be filled in when a couple actually appears for a divorce. The analogy is apt: just as a *kesuvah* today is often printed with blank lines for the relevant particulars, so too a scribe might want a standardized *get* form at hand.

### Leaving Blanks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁטָרֵי מִלְוָה – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּנִּיחַ מְקוֹם הַמִּלְוָה, מְקוֹם הַלֹּוֶה, מְקוֹם הַמָּעוֹת, מְקוֹם הַזְּמַן. שְׁטָרֵי מִקָּח – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּנִּיחַ מְקוֹם הַלּוֹקֵחַ, וּמְקוֹם הַמּוֹכֵר, מָקוֹם הַמָּעוֹת, מְקוֹם הַשָּׂדֶה, וּמְקוֹם הַזְּמַן; מִפְּנֵי הַתַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** If a scribe writes the standard part of **loan documents, he must leave** empty the **place** of the name **of the lender,** the **place** of the name **of the borrower,** the **place** of the amount **of the money** being loaned, and the **place of the date.** If the scribe writes the standard part of **documents of sale** of land, **he must leave** empty the **place** for the name **of the purchaser, and** the **place** for the name **of the seller,** the **place** for the amount **of the money** for which the land is being purchased, the **place** for the description **of the field** that is being purchased, **and** the **place of the date** when the sale occurs. This is necessary **due to the ordinance,** as the Gemara will explain.

The *mishnah* rules: *tzarich she'yaniach mekom ha'ish umekom ha'ishah umekom hazman* — the scribe must leave blank the space for the husband's name, the wife's name, and the date. At first glance this seems almost self-evident: of course one leaves those spaces blank, since the details are not yet known. If that were the entire reason, the ruling would add nothing of substance — filling in incorrect information would obviously be disqualifying, and the *mishnah* would hardly need to tell us so.

### Lishmah Concern

This prompts the deeper question: is the requirement to leave blanks an expression of the *lishmah* requirement, or is it simply a practical necessity because the information is not yet available? Could a scribe who anticipates a particular divorce — hearing that a couple has been fighting for months and sensing that a *get* is imminent — go ahead and fill in the names in advance? And is there a *lishmah* dimension at all to the boilerplate portion of a *get*, as opposed to the essential part? These questions drive the *Gemara*'s analysis.

### Loan Documents

The *mishnah* then extends the same framework to other documents. *Shtarei milveh* — promissory notes recording that a borrower owes money to a lender, witnessed and signed — carry no *lishmah* requirement whatsoever. Yet here too the rule applies: *tzarich she'yaniach mekom hamilveh umekom haloveh mekom hamamon umekom hazman* — the scribe must leave blank the space for the lender, the debtor, the amount of money, and the date on which the loan was made.

### Sale Documents

Similarly, *shtarei mekach* — documents recording the sale of a plot of land — must have blanks left for the names of buyer and seller (*mekom halokeach umekom hamocher*), the price (*mekom hamamon*), the location of the field (*mekom hasadeh*), and the date (*mekom hazman*).

### Takanah Principle

The *mishnah* concludes: *mifnei hatakanah* — all of this is on account of a *takanah*. The nature of that *takanah* remains to be identified. As *Rashi* observes, the practical scenario motivating the rule is precisely the one that might seem harmless: a scribe who, upon hearing that a couple is headed for divorce, or that a real estate deal is nearly finalized, decides to prepare the document in advance — much as newspapers keep pre-written obituaries for public figures, updating them periodically so they are ready when needed. The *mishnah* evidently sees a problem with this practice; the *Gemara* will clarify exactly what that problem is and which authority's reasoning underlies it.

## Three Positions

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹסֵל בְּכוּלָּן. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַכְשִׁיר בְּכוּלָּן חוּץ מִגִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְכָתַב לָהּ״ – לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda invalidates all of** these documents if their standard parts were written in advance. **Rabbi Elazar deems all of them valid except for bills of divorce, as it is stated** in the Torah: **"And he writes for her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), indicating that he must write the bill of divorce **for her sake.** Therefore, one may not write even the standard part of the bill of divorce in advance, as that would not qualify as writing the bill of divorce for her sake.

The *mishnah* records three opinions. The *Tanna Kamma* — the first, anonymous position — holds that one may write the boilerplate portion (*tofes*) of any document but must leave blank the essential particulars (*toref*).

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

*Rabbi Yehuda* takes a stringent view: *poseil bekullan* — he invalidates all of them. His position, as the *Gemara* will make clear, is that one may not write even the boilerplate portion of any document in advance, presumably out of concern that permitting the *tofes* will lead the scribe to inadvertently write the *toref* as well.

### Rabbi Eliezer

*Rabbi Elazar* presents a striking asymmetry: *machshir bekullan chutz migittei nashim* — he validates everything, except for bills of divorce. For *gittin*, he invokes the verse *bekosav lo lishmah* — the *lishmah* requirement — as the basis for a stringency that extends even to the boilerplate. But for all other documents, he permits the scribe to proceed freely.

The question this raises is what exactly *Rabbi Elazar* means by *machshir bekullan*. Is he merely restating what was already implicit — that the *tofes* of non-*get* documents may be written? *Rashi* reads him this way: *Rabbi Elazar* is not adding anything novel about other documents; he permits only the *tofes*, not the *toref*, exactly as the *Tanna Kamma* does. His distinctive position is solely his stringency about the *get*: even the boilerplate may not be pre-written there, as a protective measure (*gezeira*) against inadvertently writing the essential part without *lishmah*.

### Tosafot Reading

*Tosafot* offers a bolder reading — one that the *Gemara*'s subsequent discussion actually supports. According to *Tosafot*, *Rabbi Elazar* is simultaneously the most stringent and the most lenient of the three opinions. He is strictest regarding the *get*, where the *lishmah* requirement is paramount, and therefore prohibits even the boilerplate. But he is most lenient regarding everything else, precisely because *lishmah* is irrelevant there. In *Tosafot*'s reading, *machshir bekullan* means that for all non-*get* documents, the scribe may fill in the entire document — names, amounts, locations, everything — without any concern at all, provided only that the witnesses do not yet sign. There is no *lishmah* issue and no reason to withhold anything. The scribe who knows a land deal is being finalized may write out the entire document in full; the witnesses will sign when the transaction closes.

This produces a clean tripartite structure. The *Tanna Kamma* treats *get* and other documents symmetrically: *tofes* permitted, *toref* not, for all document types. *Rabbi Yehuda* treats them symmetrically in the opposite direction: nothing may be pre-written for any document, out of a cascading set of protective concerns. *Rabbi Elazar* distinguishes sharply: complete leniency for other documents, maximal stringency for a *get*.

One student raised the obvious practical concern: *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position, if followed as *halacha*, would invalidate the pre-printed forms that have been in universal use for centuries. The *Gemara* will confirm that we do not rule like *Rabbi Yehuda*.

## Gemara Resolution

### Harei at Muteret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּנִּיחַ אַף מְקוֹם ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מוּתֶּרֶת לְכׇל אָדָם״; וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי, וּבָעֵינַן כְּתִיבָה לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** One writing a bill of divorce in advance **must also leave** the **place** on the standard portion of the bill of divorce to write the essential phrase **of: You are hereby permitted to** marry **any man. And** the unattributed opinion of the mishna **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says: Witnesses of the transmission** of the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce, **and we require** the **writing** of the essential part of the bill of divorce to be **for her sake.** If one holds the other opinion, that signatory witnesses on the bill of divorce effect the divorce, then the words "And he writes for her" are referring to the signing of the bill of divorce, and the writing itself need not be done for her sake.

The *Gemara* opens with a ruling from *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: the *mishnah*'s requirement to leave blanks extends beyond the names and the date — *tzarich she'yaniach af makom harei at muteret le-chol adam*. The scribe must also leave blank the space for the essential phrase of the *get*: *harei at muteret le-chol adam*, "you are hereby permitted to any man." This is the punchline of the *get*, its operative declaration.

### Toref Requirement

This teaching clarifies the *lishmah* dimension of the *mishnah*. The *Tanna Kamma*'s requirement to leave blanks does not obviously signal a *lishmah* concern, since it applies equally to *shtarei milveh* and *shtarei mekach*, where *lishmah* is irrelevant. One might have concluded that the entire framework is about something else entirely. But *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* reads the *mishnah* as a *lishmah* ruling: the blanks must be left precisely because the essential content of the *get* must be written *lishmah*. And if *lishmah* applies, it applies not merely to the names — which could be seen as incidental — but to the *toref* proper, the operative declaration that constitutes the divorce.

Indeed, one might argue that the *lishmah* requirement, taken at face value from *vechatav lah*, should demand that every single word of the *get* be written *lishmah*. The *Gemara* is therefore being innovative in restricting the *lishmah* obligation to the *toref* alone and not the *tofes* — the boilerplate is explicitly excluded from the *lishmah* requirement. But within the *toref*, *lishmah* applies in full, and that includes *harei at muteret le-chol adam*.

### Gemara Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא, בְּהַהִיא הוּא דְּאִיכָּא לְאוֹקֹמַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּקָתָנֵי ״אֵין כּוֹתְבִין״ וְקָתָנֵי ״כִּתְבוּ״; אֲבָל סֵיפָא, דְּקָתָנֵי ״שֶׁאֵין קִיּוּם הַגֵּט אֶלָּא בְּחוֹתְמָיו״, אֵימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהַהִיא, הָהִיא נָמֵי אִיכָּא לְאוֹקֹמַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; אֲבָל הָא, אֵימָא מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הָוֵי – רֵישָׁא לָאו רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it is **necessary** for Shmuel to mention that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar in this mishna, as well as Rabbi Elazar's opinion in the previous chapter with regard to the *mishnayot* that discuss writing a bill of divorce on a surface that is attached to the ground (21b) and the writing of a bill of divorce by a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor (22b). **As, if** he had **taught us** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar only with regard to **this first** mishna (21b), which discusses writing a bill of divorce on a surface that is attached to the ground, one could have said **it is** only **that** mishna **that can be interpreted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as that** mishna **teaches: One may not write** a bill of divorce on anything that is attached to the ground, **and it teaches** in the next clause: If **one wrote it** on something that is attached to the ground; and there the Gemara proves from the comparison between these two clauses that the mishna must be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar. **However,** in **the latter clause** of the next mishna (22b), **which teaches** that the reason a bill of divorce may be written by a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor is that **the ratification of a bill of divorce is only through its signatories,** one could **say** that **this is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: Signatory witnesses** on the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce, and the mishna is discussing a case where the entire bill of divorce was written by the deaf-mute, imbecile, or minor. Therefore, it was necessary for Shmuel to state that this mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar and is discussing a case where the essential part of the bill of divorce was left empty. **And if** he had **taught us with regard to that** mishna (22b), then it would be possible to say: **That** mishna **can also be interpreted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** as it is explained that the mishna is referring to the writing of the standard portion, **but** with regard to **this** mishna here, **say: Since the last clause is** explicitly attributed to **Rabbi Elazar, the first clause** must **not** be in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.** Therefore, it was **necessary** for Shmuel to note that each of three *mishnayot* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar.

The *Gemara* now makes explicit what *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* implies: the *mishnah*, including even the *Tanna Kamma*, is to be read according to *Rabbi Elazar*'s view — *k'Rabbi Elazar hi de-amar edei mesirah karti*. Since *Rabbi Elazar* holds that the witnesses of delivery (*eidei mesirah*) are what legally consummate the *get* rather than the witnesses who sign it, the essence of the *get* is its writing, not its signature. And since the writing is essential, *lishmah* in the writing is essential, which in turn explains why the blank for *harei at muteret le-chol adam* must be left unfilled: that phrase must be written *lishmah*.

### Three Cases

This reading of the *mishnah* as a *Rabbi Elazar* position is part of a broader interpretive project. *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* has made this attribution in three recent *mishnayot*, each presenting a distinct case. The first was the *mechubar* case — the rule that one should not write a *get* while the parchment is still attached to the ground. The second was the case of *hakol kesheirin lichtov et haget* — the rule that anyone, even a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan*, may write a *get*, because its validity rests on *ein kiyum haget ela bechotmav*. The third is the present *mishnah* regarding *tofsei gittin*.

In each case, *Shmuel* reads the *mishnah* as a *Rabbi Elazar* position, which requires reinterpreting certain words. In the *mechubar* case, the end of the *mishnah* uses the word *chatmu* — "they signed it" — but *Shmuel* reads this to mean they wrote the *toref*, not that they signed it. In the *hakol* case, the phrase *ein kiyum haget ela bechotmav* sounds entirely like *Rabbi Meir*'s position that the signatories are decisive — yet *Shmuel* reinterprets *bechotmav* to refer to the witnesses present at the moment of delivery rather than those who sign the document.

### Why Three Cases

The *Gemara* explains why each of the three cases was necessary to state. Had *Shmuel* attributed only the *mechubar* case to *Rabbi Elazar*, one might have accepted it: the emphasis on the writing in that *mishnah* — *ein kotvin bemechubar* — at least makes a *Rabbi Elazar* reading plausible, even if the end requires reinterpretation. But one might have insisted that the *hakol* case is genuinely a *Rabbi Meir* *mishnah*, since its explicit rationale — *ein kiyum haget ela bechotmav* — sounds precisely like *Rabbi Meir*'s view that the signatories are determinative. The *Gemara* notes the additional difficulty with the present *mishnah*: since *Rabbi Elazar* himself appears at its end — *chutz migittei nashim* — the implication is that the beginning is *not* his view. The *Tanna Kamma* permitting the *tofes* seems to contradict *Rabbi Elazar*, who at the end of the *mishnah* forbids even the *tofes* of a *get*. To assert that even the beginning of the *mishnah* reflects a *Rabbi Elazar* framework — albeit a position within that framework that countenances the *tofes* — required *Shmuel* to make the attribution explicit all three times. The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabbi Elazar*'s end-of-*mishnah* position as a case of *trei tanna'ei aliba d'Rabbi Elazar*: two *tanna'im* debating the precise implications of *Rabbi Elazar*'s view.

## Three Takanos

### Rabbeinu Tam Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי הַתַּקָּנָה: מַאי תַּקָּנָה? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת סוֹפֵר; וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי; וּבְדִין הוּא דַּאֲפִילּוּ טוֹפֶס נָמֵי לָא לִכְתּוֹב, וּמִשּׁוּם תַּקָּנַת סוֹפְרִים שָׁרוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the scribe may write the standard part of the document in advance, leaving empty the essential part, **due to the ordinance.** The Gemara asks: To **what ordinance** is the mishna referring? **Rabbi Yonatan says: Due to the ordinance** for the benefit **of a scribe,** i.e., to enable a scribe to write the standard part of a document in advance so that when a customer arrives he will not need to write the document hurriedly. **And it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says: Witnesses of the transmission** of the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce. **And** according to Rabbi Elazar's opinion, **by right it** should have been **that** the scribe **would not write even** the **standard** part of the document in advance, as that part must also be written for her sake, **but due to the ordinance** for the benefit **of scribes, the Sages permitted** it.

Before turning to the *Gemara*'s three explanations for the *takanah*, *Tosafot* raises a remarkable historical observation about the phrase *harei at muteret le-chol adam* itself. Throughout the *Gemara* this phrase is treated as the *ikar haget*, the essential declaration of the *get* — indeed, the next chapter will explicitly teach *gufo shel get harei at muteret le-chol adam*. Yet *Tosafot* notes that *Rabbeinu Tam* was the one who instituted the practice of including this language in *gittin*. The very fact that he had to institute it implies that *gittin* written prior to *Rabbeinu Tam* did not contain the phrase.

How is this possible? *Tosafot* explains that the standard boilerplate text of *gittin* before *Rabbeinu Tam*'s time did not include *harei at muteret le-chol adam* — not because the concept was absent, but because the operative language of divorce was expressed differently, often in Aramaic. The *Rif*'s text of a *get*, for instance, contains the phrase *l'hitnasva l'chol gvar di titzviyan* — "you may marry any man you wish" — which conveys the identical content in Aramaic. *Tosafot* then adds the crucial caveat: *ein l'hotzi la'az al gittin harishonim* — one must not cast aspersions on earlier *gittin*. The adoption of new stringent formulations, however well-grounded, does not retroactively invalidate prior practice, particularly when the earlier texts expressed the same substantive content through parallel Aramaic formulations. *Rabbeinu Tam* introduced the Hebrew phrase going forward, but the precedent from an earlier *Gemara* — that adopting new stringencies risks undermining the validity of what came before — remains relevant, and *Tosafot* is careful to address it.

### Takanat Sofrim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹסֵל בְּכוּלָּן – גָּזַר טוֹפֶס אַטּוּ תּוֹרֶף, גָּזַר שְׁטָרוֹת אַטּוּ גִּיטִּין. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַכְשִׁיר בְּכוּלָּן חוּץ מִגִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים – טוֹפֶס אַטּוּ תּוֹרֶף גָּזַר, שְׁטָרוֹת אַטּוּ גִּיטִּין לָא גָּזַר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Yehuda invalidates all of** these documents, not only bills of divorce, even if only their standard parts were written in advance. The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yehuda **decreed** that one may not write the **standard** part of a document in advance **due to** the concern lest the scribes eventually come to write the **essential** part of a document **[*toref*]** in advance as well. Similarly, **he decreed** that this *halakha* applies to all **documents,** which need not be written for anyone's sake, **due to bills of divorce.** The mishna continues: **And Rabbi Elazar deems all of them valid except for bills of divorce.** The Gemara explains his reasoning: Rabbi Elazar **decreed** that one may not write the **standard** part of a document in advance **due to** the concern lest the scribes eventually come to write the **essential** part of the document in advance. However, **he did not decree** that this *halakha* applies to all **documents,** which need not be written for anyone's sake, **due to bills of divorce.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the underlying rationale for the *takanah* mentioned in the *mishnah*. *Rav Yonatan* offers the first explanation: *mipnei takkanat sofer* — the *takanah* was enacted for the benefit of the scribes. The context is *Rabbi Elazar*'s position: since *Rabbi Elazar* requires *lishmah* in the writing, one might have thought that no part of a *get* — not even the boilerplate — could be prepared in advance. Writing anything before the proper moment might compromise the *lishmah* that the *get* demands. Yet pre-writing the *tofes* is extremely useful for scribes, enabling them to have forms ready for immediate use. The Rabbis therefore enacted a *takanah* permitting the *tofes* despite the general *lishmah* concern.

This framing also explains why the *mishnah* extends its requirements to other *shtarot*. Within a *Rabbi Elazar* framework, the temptation to fill in blanks by other documents might spill over into improper filling of blanks in a *get*. The *Tanna Kamma* therefore extends the restriction: just as you may not fill in the *toref* of a *get* — as that would compromise *lishmah* — so too you may not fill in the blanks of other *shtarot*, lest that practice migrate to the *get*. *Rabbi Yehuda* extends the concern further: since the *lishmah* requirement for a *get* is so demanding, he forbids even the *tofes* of a *get*, and once he forbids the *tofes* of a *get*, he forbids even the boilerplate of other *shtarot* by the same logic. *Rabbi Elazar* himself, by contrast, has no concern about other *shtarot* — there is no *lishmah* issue there — and is content to permit them entirely, while maintaining his stringency about the *get*.

Thus the three positions resolve into a coherent picture within the world of *Rabbi Elazar*. All three agree that the *get* is the locus of concern; they disagree only about how far the protective fences must extend.

### Takanat Ketata

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שַׁבְּתַי אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: מִשּׁוּם קְטָטָה; וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי, וּבְדִין הוּא דַּאֲפִילּוּ תּוֹרֶף נָמֵי לִכְתּוֹב; וְזִמְנִין דְּשָׁמְעָה לֵיהּ לְסוֹפֵר דְּקָא כָתֵיב, וְסָבְרָה אִיהוּ קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, וְהָוֵה לַהּ קְטָטָה בַּהֲדֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discussing the ordinance mentioned in the mishna. **Rabbi Shabbetai** says that **Ḥizkiyya says:** The ordinance mentioned in the mishna was not instituted for the benefit of scribes, allowing them to prepare the standard part of bills of divorce in advance. The ordinance was instituted **due to** the desire to prevent **a quarrel, and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: Signatory witnesses** on the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce, and there is no need for the bill of divorce to be written for the sake of the woman. **And by right it** should have been **that** the scribe would be permitted **to write even** the **essential** part of the bill of divorce **as well. But sometimes** there might be a wife **who hears the scribe who** by chance **is writing** in advance a bill of divorce with her name, **and she thinks that** her husband **told him** to write the bill of divorce on his behalf, **and she will have a quarrel with him.** Therefore, the Sages instituted an ordinance that the scribe may not write the essential part of the bill of divorce in advance, as it includes the names of the husband and the wife.

*Rav Shabtai* cites *Chizkiya* with an entirely different reading of the *mishnah*: *mishum ketata* — the reason for leaving blanks is to prevent domestic quarrels. On this account, the *mishnah* has nothing to do with *lishmah* at all. The *mishnah* should be read as a *Rabbi Meir* position, where the *lishmah* requirement does not apply to the writing. Why then may one not fill in the blanks? Because a woman who overhears a scribe writing a *get* with her name in it will naturally assume her husband has commissioned the document, and the result will be fights in the household. More broadly, it is simply bad policy to have documents pre-filled with sensitive personal details — whether it is a loan, a sale, or a *get* — before the underlying transaction has actually occurred. No one wants a fully written document floating around that purports to record something that has not yet happened.

On this reading, the *takanah* is not one that *enables* the writing of the *tofes* despite *lishmah* concerns; it is rather a *takanah* telling scribes not to fill in the blanks even though there would be no technical prohibition in doing so. In principle, within a *Rabbi Meir* framework, one could pre-fill the entire document right down to the last detail and there would be no legal problem, since validity depends entirely on the signing. The *takanah* is a prudential restriction, not a *lishmah*-based one.

### Takanat Agunot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּנַת עֲגוּנוֹת; אָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. אָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר – דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי, וּבְדִין הוּא דַּאֲפִילּוּ תּוֹרֶף נָמֵי לִכְתּוֹב; וְזִמְנִין דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ קְטָטָה בַּהֲדַהּ, וְרָתַח עֲלַהּ וְזָרֵיק לֵיהּ נִיהֲלַהּ, וּמְעַגֵּן וּמוֹתֵיב לַהּ. אָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – דְּאָמַר: עֵידֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי, וּבְדִין הוּא דַּאֲפִילּוּ טוֹפֶס נָמֵי לָא לִכְתּוֹב; וְזִמְנִין דְּבָעֵי לְמֵיזַל לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְלָא אַשְׁכַּח סָפְרָא, וְשָׁבֵיק לַהּ וְאָזֵיל, וּמְעַגֵּן וּמוֹתֵיב לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another understanding of the ordinance mentioned in the mishna. **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Avimi** said: The reason for the ordinance is **due to** the desire to provide **a remedy for deserted wives.** How so? **Some say** that he explained the mishna in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and some say** that he explained it in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar.** The Gemara clarifies: **Some say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: Signatory witnesses** on the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce; **and by right it** should have been **that** the scribe would be permitted **to write,** in advance, **even** the **essential** part of the bill of divorce **as well. But sometimes** there could be a situation **when** the husband **has a quarrel with** his wife **and becomes angry with her, and he** might **throw** the bill of divorce **to her and desert** her **and leave her** divorced because he possessed a bill of divorce that had been prepared in advance. The Sages therefore instituted that a complete bill of divorce may not be prepared in advance to ensure that it would take time for the husband to obtain one, in the hope that he would calm down in the interim and reconsider. **Some say** that he explains the mishna in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who said: Witnesses of the transmission** of the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce; **and by right it** should have been **that** scribes would **not** be permitted **to write** in advance **even** the **standard** part of the bill of divorce **as well,** due to a decree lest they come to write the essential part in advance. **But sometimes** the husband **needs to travel to a country overseas, and he will not find a scribe** who can write the bill of divorce for him, **and he will abandon her and go and desert** her **and leave her** in this position. The Sages instituted that scribes may write the standard part of the bill of divorce in advance, so that they can finish it quickly by simply adding the essential part.

*Rav Chisda* cites *Avimi* with a third explanation: *mishum takkanat agunot* — the *takanah* is designed to prevent the creation of *agunot*. The *Gemara* notes that this rationale can be applied within either a *Rabbi Meir* or a *Rabbi Elazar* framework.

### Agunot Application

According to the *Rabbi Meir* reading, the *takanat agunot* rationale works as follows. Since all that matters in a *Rabbi Meir* world is the signing, there is in principle nothing wrong with having an entirely pre-written *get* ready to go — including names, date, and the operative declaration. The *takanah* says: do not do this. Why? Because having a fully written *get* immediately available makes it fatally easy for a husband to act in anger. He has a fight with his wife, runs to the scribe's shop, picks up the pre-filled document, gets witnesses to sign it on the spot, and throws it at her. What was a resolvable domestic dispute becomes an irreversible divorce. *Rav Dov* raised an alternative nuance worth considering: even if the *get* is technically invalid because the signing was rushed or incomplete, the act of throwing a *get*-like document at one's wife may shatter the marriage in practice — the husband storms off and never returns, leaving the woman in a state of abandonment that the *Gemara* characterizes as a form of *agunah*, even if her formal legal status has not changed. This broader use of the term is noteworthy.

According to the *Rabbi Elazar* reading, the *takanat agunot* works in the opposite direction. Since *Rabbi Elazar* demands *lishmah* in the writing, one might conclude that scribes may not pre-write even the boilerplate of a *get*. But this creates its own *agunah* problem: a husband who must travel overseas and wishes to leave his wife a conditional *get* — divorce to take effect if he does not return within a specified period — may be unable to find a scribe to write the entire *get* from scratch on short notice. *Lo ashkach safra* — he will not find a scribe, and he will depart without leaving any protection for his wife. If he fails to return, she becomes a genuine *agunah*. The *takanah* therefore permits the pre-writing of the *tofes* precisely to ensure that a *get* can be prepared quickly when circumstances demand it.

The *Gemara* thus presents two dimensions of *takanat agunot*: one aimed at preventing precipitous divorce when a *get* is too easily available, the other aimed at enabling divorce when the inability to prepare one quickly would leave a woman permanently bound.

## Pre-dating Problems

### Get for Arusa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְקוֹם הַזְּמַן: קָפָסֵיק וְתָנֵי; לָא שְׁנָא מִן הַנִּישּׂוּאִין, וְלָא שְׁנָא מִן הָאֵירוּסִין. בִּשְׁלָמָא מִן הַנִּישּׂוּאִין; בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם בַּת אֲחוֹתוֹ – אִיכָּא, בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם פֵּירֵי – אִיכָּא; אֶלָּא מִן הָאֵירוּסִין; בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם בַּת אֲחוֹתוֹ – אִיכָּא, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם פֵּירֵי, אֲרוּסָה מִי אִית לַהּ פֵּירֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the scribe must leave a place for the names **and a place for the date.** The Gemara comments: The *tanna* **categorically teaches** this *halakha*; it **is no different** if the divorce is **from the** state of **marriage, and it is no different** if the divorce is **from the** state of **betrothal.** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted,** in the case of a divorce **from the** state of **marriage,** it is understood why the bill of divorce must be dated. **Whether according to the one who said** that the reason for writing the date on a bill of divorce is **due to** a situation where one marries **the daughter of his sister,** to prevent the husband from protecting her if she commits adultery by giving her an undated bill of divorce and claiming that they had been divorced before she committed adultery with the other man, **there is** a reason to record the date, or **whether according to the one who said** that it is **due to** a concern for her **produce,** to enable her to collect the produce of her usufruct property from the moment they are divorced, **there is** a reason to record the date. **But** in the case of a divorce **from the** state of **betrothal, granted, according to the one who said** that the date is written on a bill of divorce **due to** a situation where one marries **the daughter of his sister, there is** the same concern where she is divorced after being betrothed, as the *halakhot* of adultery take effect from the time of betrothal. **But according to the one who said** that it is **due to** a concern for her **produce, does a betrothed woman have produce?** In any case, the husband has a right to use his wife's property only after they are married. Therefore, it is not necessary to protect her rights by dating the bill of divorce to show when the husband's rights to her property terminate.

The *mishnah* rules without distinction — *lo shna min hanisuin velo shna min ha'erusin* — that the requirement to leave the date blank applies equally to a *get* given after *nisuin* and to a *get* given after *erusin* alone, before the couple has established a shared household. The *Gemara* accepts the *nisuin* case without difficulty.

For the *nisuin* case, the prohibition on pre-dating has two well-established rationales. One, associated with the concern about a *bat achoso*, is that a husband might wish to protect his wife from a death penalty for adultery by backdating the *get* to make it appear she was divorced before the act occurred. The other rationale is about *peiros nichsei melog*: the produce of the wife's property that the husband is entitled to during the marriage. Pre-dating a *get* would distort the record of when the husband's entitlement ended, potentially harming third parties who relied on the husband's lien over those assets.

The *erusin* case is more difficult. The concern about adultery and misrepresentation of the divorce date applies equally whether or not the couple had established a household. But the *peiros* concern seems irrelevant: *arusa mi is lah peiri* — an *arusa* does not yet have *nichsei melog* producing fruit over which the husband has any rights, since that entitlement begins only from *nisuin*. If the only reason to prohibit pre-dating were the *peiros* issue, there would seemingly be no problem with a pre-dated *get* for an *arusa*.

### Takanas Valad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם: הָא מִילְּתָא שְׁמַעִית מִינֵּיהּ דְּעוּלָּא דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּנַת וָלָד, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי נִיהוּ. כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁמְעִיתַהּ לְהָא דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר: ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט לַאֲרוּסָתִי, לִכְשֶׁאֶכְנְסֶנָּה אֲגָרְשֶׁנָּה״, אֵינוֹ גֵּט. וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: מָה טַעַם? גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ: גִּיטָּהּ קוֹדֵם לִבְנָהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי – גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ: גִּיטָּהּ קוֹדֵם לִבְנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said: I heard this matter from Ulla, who said** that the date is written on the bill of divorce **for the benefit of** the **child, and I did not know what this** benefit **is. Once I heard that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who says: Write a bill of divorce for my betrothed, as when I will bring her in** to my home in marriage **I will divorce her** with it, **it is not a** valid **bill of divorce. And Ulla said: What** is the **reason** that it is not a valid bill of divorce? Since once he marries her she may become pregnant, there is a rabbinic **decree** due to the concern **lest they say** that receipt of **her bill of divorce precedes** conception of **her son,** as it was written while they were betrothed. **Here too,** the reason the scribe must leave the place of the date in a bill of divorce for a betrothed woman is that the husband may marry her before divorcing her, and there is a rabbinic **decree** due to the concern **lest they say** that receipt of **her bill of divorce precedes** conception of **her son.**

*Rav Amram* reports that he heard an explanation from *Ulla*, though he initially did not understand its application: *d'amar mishum takanas valad* — the rule exists for the protection of children. The relevant *braita* involves a husband who says: write a *get* for the woman I have just betrothed, and after we consummate the marriage I will divorce her. The *halacha* rules that such a *get* — written during *erusin* but intended for use after *nisuin* — is invalid.

### Reputation Concern

*Ulla* explains the reason: *gzeira shema yomru gita kodem livno* — a decree lest people say that her *get* predates her children. If the *get* is dated from the time of *erusin*, and the couple subsequently lives together and has children before the *get* is delivered, observers will see a *get* bearing a date that appears to precede the birth of the children. Even though the children are not *mamzerim* — their status is not affected — the appearance that they were born after their mother was divorced is a *pegam mishpacha*, a blemish on the family's reputation.

### Arusa Protection

This teaching resolves the *Gemara*'s question about the *erusin* case. When the *get* will be given to the woman while she is still an *arusa*, pre-dating is not intrinsically a problem: there are no *peiros* at stake, and the date makes no practical difference between January and March if she remains an *arusa* throughout. The *Gemara* concedes this. But the reason the *mishnah* prohibits writing the date before the *get* is actually given — even for an *arusa* — is that the scribe cannot know the *get* will actually be delivered during *erusin*. The husband may hold onto it. He may not give it until after *nisuin*. And if he then gives it after *nisuin*, the earlier date will appear to predate any children born from the marriage, creating precisely the *pegam mishpacha* that the *takanas valad* is designed to prevent.

## Halacha Ruling

### Rabbi Elazar Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר שֵׁילָא, אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא סָבָא, אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. קָרֵי רַב עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: טוּבְיָנָא דְּחַכִּימֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabbi Abba bar Sheila says** that **Rav Hamnuna Sava says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** with regard to writing the standard portion of the bill of divorce in advance is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar** that such a bill of divorce is invalid. Upon hearing this ruling, **Rav proclaimed about Rabbi Elazar:** He is **the most gratified of** the **Sages,** as the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion.

The *halacha* is ruled explicitly: *amar Rabbi Zeira amar Rav Abba bar Shila amar Rav Hamnuna Saba amar Rav Adda bar Ahava amar Rav halacha k'Rabbi Elazar* — the ruling follows *Rabbi Elazar*. This is a chain of five transmitters, underscoring the solemnity of the ruling. The practical implications are significant: we require *lishmah* in the writing of a *get*, we hold *eidei mesirah karti* — the witnesses of delivery are what legally consummate the *get* — and, most stringently, we do not permit even the boilerplate of a *get* to be written in advance. *Rav* even praises *Rabbi Elazar* as *tuveina d'hakimi* — the best of the sages. A brief clarification: the *Rabbi Elazar* at issue here is *Rav Elazar*, the student of *Rabbi Akiva* and colleague of *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir*. He is not to be confused with *Rabbi Eliezer*, *Rabbi Akiva*'s own teacher, whose stringent position resulted in a *cherem* and the designation *Shmuti*. Throughout this tractate the relevant figure for the *lishmah* discussion is *Rav Elazar*, and any prior confusion between the two names should be set aside.

### Other Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁאָר שְׁטָרוֹת נָמֵי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב פַּפִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הַאי אַשַּׁרְתָּא דְּדַיָּינֵי, דְּמִיכַּתְבָּה מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִיסָהֲדֵי סָהֲדִי אַחֲתִימַת יְדַיְיהוּ – פְּסוּלָה; אַלְמָא מִיחְזֵי כְּשִׁיקְרָא, הָכָא נָמֵי מִיחְזֵי כְּשִׁיקְרָא! וְלֵיתַהּ, מִדְּרַב נַחְמָן – דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אֲפִילּוּ מְצָאוֹ בְּאַשְׁפָּה, חֲתָמוֹ וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ – כָּשֵׁר. וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן לָא פְּלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אֶלָּא בְּגִיטֵּי נָשִׁים, דְּבָעֵינַן כְּתִיבָה לִשְׁמָהּ, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר שְׁטָרוֹת – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is the *halakha* in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar **even with regard to other documents as well,** that it is permitted for scribes to write the standard portion of other documents in advance? **But didn't Rav Pappi say in the name of Rava:** With regard to **this ratification of judges,** which serves to confirm the authenticity of a promissory note **that was written before the witnesses had testified about their signatures** on the promissory note, it is **invalid,** even if the witnesses later attested that those were their signatures. **Apparently** it has the **appearance of falsehood,** because the court ratified the document before hearing the testimony. **Here too,** if one writes the documents in advance, they have the **appearance of a falsehood.** The Gemara notes: **And** Rav Pappi's statement that all documents written in advance are invalid because they have the appearance of falsehood **is not** accepted, **because** of the statement **of Rav Naḥman. As Rav Naḥman said: Rabbi Meir,** who holds that signatory witnesses on the bill of divorce effect the divorce, **would say: Even** if a husband **found** an unsigned bill of divorce **in the garbage,** and **he had it signed and gave it to** his wife, it is **valid,** as he holds that the manner of the writing of the bill of divorce does not affect whether or not it is a valid bill of divorce. **And even the Rabbis disagree with** the opinion **of Rabbi Meir only with regard to bills of divorce,** as they hold **that we require** the **writing** to be **for her sake; but with regard to other documents,** they do **not** disagree.

Having ruled like *Rabbi Elazar* regarding *gittin*, the *Gemara* raises the follow-on question: do we also rule like *Rabbi Elazar* regarding other documents, *l'kula* — in the direction of leniency? *Rabbi Elazar*'s position on other *shtarot* is that the scribe may fill in everything in advance — names, amounts, dates, all terms — so long as the witnesses have not yet signed.

### Mechezi k'Shikra

*Rav Pappa* cites *Rava* to raise a concern. The case involves a court's validation of witnesses' signatures — a form of notarization. Suppose the judges begin writing their certification before the witnesses have formally testified to their signatures; they intend to sign only after hearing the testimony. *Rava* rules that such a pre-written certification is *pesulah* — invalid — because it constitutes *mechezi k'shikra*: it has the appearance of falsehood. Writing a document that asserts as fact something that has not yet occurred, even if one intends to sign only when it becomes true, creates an inherently misleading document. By analogy, pre-filling any document before the underlying transaction occurs might be *mechezi k'shikra*.

### Non-Get Leniency

The *Gemara* resolves this concern by invoking the principle attributed to *Rabbi Meir* — that *rav nachman amar haya Rabbi Meir afilu matzo be-ashpah chasmo u-nosno lo kasher*: even a *get* found in a garbage heap, if signed and delivered, is valid. We do not rule this way for *gittin*, because we require *lishmah*. But the underlying principle — that writing a document does not itself constitute a legal act, and that validity depends on signing — applies to other *shtarot*. *Veafilu rabbanan lo pligi alei d'Rabbi Meir ela b'gitei nashim d'ba'inan ketivah lishmah* — the Sages dispute *Rabbi Meir* only regarding *gittin*, where *lishmah* in the writing is required. *B'sha'ar shtaros lo* — for other documents there is no such dispute, and the full leniency of pre-writing applies.

### Pre-writing Leniency

The *Gemara* thus confirms: for loan documents, bills of sale, and *kesuvot*, the scribe may write out the entire document in advance of the transaction, provided the witnesses do not sign until the transaction is complete. This is not *mechezi k'shikra*, because the document has no legal force until signed.

### Ketubah Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁטָר שֶׁלָּוָה בּוֹ וּפְרָעוֹ – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וְלֹוֶה בּוֹ, שֶׁכְּבָר נִמְחַל שִׁעְבּוּדוֹ. טַעְמָא דְּנִמְחַל שִׁעְבּוּדוֹ, אֲבָל מִשּׁוּם שִׁיקְרָא – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The proof of this is **that Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of a promissory **note with which one borrowed** money **and** the borrower then **repaid** the debt, **he may not borrow money again with it.** The reason is **that its lien has already been forgiven.** Once the debt has been repaid, the lien resulting from the loan is no longer in force. The witnesses did not sign the document again at the time of the second loan, so the lien will not be in effect, and the loan will have the status of one by oral agreement. The Gemara infers: **The reason** that he cannot reuse the document is **that its lien has been forgiven,** so that the document is no longer accurate. **But as** for the fact that it has the **appearance of falsehood,** as it was written prior to the second loan, **we are not concerned.**

The *Gemara* adduces further support from a ruling of *Rabbi Yitzchak* citing *Rabbi Yochanan*: a promissory note with which one borrowed money and then repaid the loan on the same day may not be reused to borrow again, because *kvar nimchal shibudo* — the lien created by the document was already discharged. Critically, the *Gemara* notes: *ta'ama d'nimchal shibudo, aval mishum shikra lo chaishinan* — the only reason the document cannot be reused is that its legal effect was already exhausted, but the fact that it was written before being needed is not itself a problem. Concern about *shikra* — falsity in the pre-writing — does not apply.

The practical upshot is illustrated by the *kesuvah*. A *kesuvah* is written with the couple's names, the date, the amount, and all the stipulated terms before the *kinyan* has been performed. The text may even include the formula *ve-kanina minei* — "we acquired from him" — even before the *kinyan* takes place. Some scribes are careful to leave that particular word incomplete until after the *kinyan*, filling it in just before signing, out of an abundance of caution regarding *mechezi k'shikra*. But the *Gemara*'s ruling makes clear that this scruple, while understandable, is not required: the pre-writing of the entire document, including all terms and even the formula of acquisition, is permissible, and only the witnesses' signatures must wait until the transaction is finalized.
