# Sanhedrin 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel: Disabled Witness…

### Severed Hands Rule

The daf opens on 45b, approximately ten lines from the bottom, with a statement of Shmuel bearing on the laws of *skilah*: *niktata yad ha'edim patur* — if the hands of the witnesses are severed after the *gmar din*, the person who was condemned to death goes free.

### Witnesses' Hands Require…

The reason is rooted in a scriptural requirement: *yad ha'edim tihyeh bo barishona* — the hands of the witnesses must be the first to cast the stone. If those hands no longer exist, the execution cannot proceed as prescribed, and the condemned is therefore released from this sentence. All the formal technical requirements of the procedure must be in place; if they are not, execution cannot be carried out.

### Pre vs Post Disability

One might immediately ask whether this principle extends to its logical extreme: what if the witnesses had amputated hands *from before the crime was committed* — *edim gidimim d'me'ikara* — would they also be disqualified? The answer is no. The verse specifies *yad ha'edim* — the hand of the witnesses — referring to a hand that *already existed* at the time of the crime. A pre-existing amputation does not create a *psul*. It is only when the witnesses possessed hands at the time of the crime, and those hands were severed afterward, that a disqualification arises. The hand that was present at the moment of witnessing is the hand that must cast the first stone, and its subsequent loss removes the possibility of fulfilling that requirement.

## Meitvei Challenge

### Court Observers Testify

The Gemara raises a *meitvei* — a challenge to Shmuel's position — from the following *braita*: if two people come to a court and testify, "We are witnesses that this person received a *gmar din* in another *beit din*, with these specific witnesses and these specific charges," and the person had fled before execution — *harei zeh yehareg* — he should be put to death by this court. The implication of the *braita* is striking: the two individuals testifying never witnessed the crime themselves; they only observed the prior court proceedings. Yet their testimony would apparently be sufficient to carry out the execution.

This creates a direct tension with Shmuel's rule. If such court-observer testimony were valid, then even in a case where the original witnesses had their hands severed, two others who had merely watched the original proceedings could walk into a different court, testify to the conviction, and bring about execution — effectively circumventing the disqualification Shmuel describes.

### Same Witnesses Required

Shmuel resolves this tension with the interpretation *b'hen hen edav* — it must be that the very same original witnesses came to testify in the second court. The *braita* is not describing a case where independent observers testify to having watched a court case; it is a case where the original witnesses themselves appeared before the new tribunal. This is not a minor technical point. Shmuel's position rests on a fundamental principle: the witnesses who execute the convicted person must be the very same witnesses who saw the crime committed. Court-observer testimony — no matter how reliable — cannot substitute for this. Even according to Shmuel, the original witnesses must come forward, and the second court would conduct at least some form of proceeding, though given that they are testifying that a *gmar din* was already reached, the process would presumably be abbreviated.

## Execution Alternatives

### Backup Execution Methods

The Gemara now turns to a broader question, prompted by a *braita*: the verse *mot yumat ha-makeh rotze'ach hu* establishes that a murderer must be put to death according to the prescribed method. But what if it is impossible to carry out the assigned form of execution? *Minayin she-im ee atah yachol le-hamito be-mitah ha-ktuvah bo* — from where do we know that if you cannot execute someone by the prescribed method, you may use any other available means? The answer: *talmud lomar mot yumat ha-makeh mikol makom* — the verse teaches that he must be executed regardless. The obligation to carry out the death sentence overrides the obligation to follow the specific prescribed form. This would suggest, for instance, that if the witnesses had their hands cut off and *skilah* could not be performed, one might still execute the person by another method — which would undermine Shmuel's ruling entirely.

### Murder Exception

The Gemara rejects this line of reasoning, however, by noting that the general principle drawn from *mot yumat* cannot be universally applied. The rule allowing execution by alternative means is derived specifically in the context of *rotze'ach* — the murderer — and *goel ha-dam* — the blood redeemer. These are two distinct contexts, and together they form *shnei ktuvim ha-ba'im k'echad* — two scriptural examples that appear to establish a common rule.

### Two Examples Rule

This brings us to a fundamental hermeneutical principle: *shnei ktuvim ha-ba'im k'echad ein melamdin* — when two verses come together to teach the same principle, we do not extrapolate a general rule from them to apply across all of Torah. The principle that one may execute by alternative means if the prescribed method is unavailable is therefore limited to the two specific cases from which it is derived: murder (*rotze'ach*) and the *goel ha-dam*. It cannot be extended to, say, a case of *chillul Shabbat* or *avodah zarah* where the witnesses' hands have been severed. For those crimes, Shmuel's rule stands: if the specific requirements cannot be met, the person goes free.

## Goel HaDam Principle

### Blood Redeemer Role

The Gemara clarifies the scriptural basis for the *goel ha-dam*'s authority to use alternative means: *goel ha-dam yamit et ha-rotze'ach* — the blood redeemer may kill the manslayer. This is framed as a *mitzvah*: it is a positive obligation for the *goel ha-dam* to pursue the manslayer. The *goel ha-dam* is a close relative of the person who was killed, and the Torah grants him a special dispensation to pursue and kill the manslayer — even without a formal court procedure — so long as the manslayer has not yet reached sanctuary in an *ir miklat*.

### Court Appoints Redeemer

A remarkable principle follows: *u-minayin she-im ein lo goel she-beit din ma'amidin lo goel* — if the victim has no *goel*, from where do we know that the *beit din* appoints one on his behalf? The verse *bifig'o bo* — "when he encounters him" — is read expansively: *mikol makom*, any person authorized to do so may act as the *goel ha-dam*, regardless of familial connection.

What is striking about this is the transformation the institution undergoes. Reading the verses in the Torah, the *goel ha-dam* appears to be a purely spontaneous, extra-legal expression of family grief and honor — *yecham levavo*, his heart burns with vengeance. It is a special dispensation granted because of the emotional bond between family members and their slain relative. And yet here the Gemara presents it as a legislated, institutionalized component of the juridical process: if no family member steps forward, the court itself appoints an official surrogate. The principle is that manslaughter law, as constructed by the Torah, requires there to be a *goel ha-dam* — otherwise the entire framework of the *ir miklat* loses its force, since without the threat of the *goel ha-dam* there is no compelling reason for the manslayer to flee to sanctuary in the first place.

## Necessary Conditions

### Ben Sorer Parents

The Gemara now addresses the broader question of whether Shmuel's principle — that missing necessary conditions nullify the execution — requires a specific scriptural source, or whether it might be derived from a more general rule. A *mishna* is cited: if either parent of a *ben sorer u'moreh* is *gidem* (has an amputated hand), *ilem* (mute), *chiger* (lame), *suma* (blind), or *cheresh* (deaf), the son cannot be executed as a *ben sorer u'moreh*. Each of these disqualifications is derived from the specific language of the relevant verses: *vetafsu bo* — "and they shall seize him" — requires functioning hands; *vehotziu oto* — "and they shall bring him out" — requires the ability to walk; *ve'amru* — "and they shall say" — requires the ability to speak; *benenu zeh* — "this is our son" — requires the ability to point and see; *einenu shome'a bekolenu* — "he does not heed our voice" — requires that the parents themselves can hear.

The question raised is: why not simply say that even if the parents are disqualified in one of these ways, the son is still deserving of death and should be executed by an alternative means? The answer is that these requirements are special additional conditions legislated specifically for *ben sorer u'moreh* based on its own *psukim*, and cannot be overridden by a general backup principle. They are necessary conditions whose absence simply prevents the application of this particular law.

### Ir HaNidachat Square

A further proof is brought from the laws of *ir ha-nidachat*: if a city condemned as an idolatrous city has no *rechov* — no public city square — it cannot be treated as an *ir ha-nidachat*, according to Rabbi Yishmael. The Torah requires that all the property of the condemned city be gathered into the *rechov* and burned there. Without such a square, the defining act of the procedure cannot be carried out.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *osin la rechov* — if there is no city square, one is created. The only dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is whether the *rechov* must have pre-existed the city's designation as *ir ha-nidachat*, or whether one constructed after the fact is equally valid. But both agree — *dechulei alma ba'inan kra kideichtiv* — that a *rechov* is an indispensable requirement, as written in the Torah. No alternative procedure exists to circumvent it. This is another demonstration that necessary conditions on capital and related laws cannot be bypassed by appealing to a general principle of alternative enforcement.

The discussion is also an occasion to note the parallel with certain questions in the laws of *eruvin*, where modern *poskim* — notably the *Aruch HaShulchan* — debate whether contemporary cities, with their many streets and no single dominant thoroughfare, possess what the Gemara considers a *reshut ha-rabbim*. Rabbi Yishmael's position here, that the city's *rechov* must be the particular kind of public square the Torah envisions, touches on related conceptual terrain.

### Metzora Requirements

The Gemara then raises a *machlokes tanna'im* that reveals the dispute over necessary conditions to be an ancient one. The purification process of the *metzora* requires the application of blood and oil to three specific points: the right thumb, the right big toe, and the right ear. If the *metzora* lacks any of these — *ein lo bohen yad bohen regel ozen yemanit* — what is to be done?

The *Mishnah* records that he has no purification available to him ever — *ein lo taharah olamit*. Rabbi Eliezer, however, says one places the blood and oil *al mekomo* — on the place where the limb once was, or where it would have been — and that fulfills the requirement. Rabbi Shimon offers a different alternative: *noten al smolo veyotze* — one places it on the left side instead. Both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon maintain that a backup procedure exists, differing only on its nature. Rabbi Eliezer's approach suggests that the essential act concerns the specific location regardless of whether the limb is present; Rabbi Shimon's approach suggests that right-sidedness is essential but that the left can serve as a substitute. Worth noting is the deep structural parallel between the purification of the *metzora* and the inauguration of the *kohanim* in the *miluim*: both involve the same application of blood and oil to thumb, big toe, and ear — reflecting the conceptual symmetry between the transition from ordinary *Yisrael* to *kohen* and the transition from *metzora* back to ordinary *Yisrael*.

## Hanging After Execution

### Eliezer: All Stoned

The next *mishna* opens a new topic: *telihah* — the display of the body after execution by hanging it on a post. This is emphatically not execution by hanging; it is a post-mortem display of the corpse. *Kol haniskalin nitlin divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that everyone executed by *skilah* must also have their body hanged for display.

### Chachamim: Two Cases

The *Chachamim* disagree: *eino nitlah ela ha-migadef ve-oved avodah zarah* — only the blasphemer (*megadef*) and the idolater are subject to post-mortem hanging. On the question of orientation, the *Mishnah* records that a man is hung facing outward toward the public — *panav klapei ha'am* — while a woman, according to Rabbi Eliezer, faces the wooden beam. The *Chachamim* go further and hold that women are not hanged at all.

### Shimon ben Shetach Case

Rabbi Eliezer challenges the *Chachamim* with a historical precedent: *vehalo Shimon ben Shetach talah nashim be'Ashkelon* — did not Shimon ben Shetach, from the period of the *Zugot*, hang women in Ashkelon? The *Chachamim* respond that he hung eighty women on a single day — *ve'ein danim shnayim beyom echad* — and one cannot adjudicate two cases on the same day. The very fact that his conduct raised this procedural objection is itself noteworthy, and the Gemara will shortly examine precisely what the prohibition against judging two cases in one day entails.

## Hanging Derasha Methods

### Kilelat Elohim Pasuk

The Gemara examines the *drashot* underlying the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim*. A *braita* opens the discussion: *vehumat vetalita* — "he shall be put to death and you shall hang him." This might suggest that every form of execution is followed by hanging — *yachol kol ha-mumatin nitlin*. The verse then provides a limiting phrase: *ki kilelat elohim taluy* — "for it is a curse of God that he hangs." The practical consequence of this prohibition is significant: the body must be buried before nightfall. In practice, the *beit din* would carry out the hanging only moments before sunset and untie the body almost immediately — the display lasting mere seconds — so as not to violate the prohibition against leaving the body overnight.

The connection between *kilelat elohim* and the *megadef* is the pivot of the *drashah*: the word *kilelat* — curse — refers to the one who cursed God, that is, the *megadef*, who is executed by *skilah*. The Gemara unpacks the logic of this linkage and its implications for how the verse is read by each side.

The *Rambam* in *Hilchot Avodah Zarah*, Perek 2, famously explains why he included the laws of *megadef* within the laws of *avodah zarah*: it is precisely because of this Gemara that connects the two. One who blasphemes God has, in the deepest sense, denied divine sovereignty — *kafar be'ikar* — just as one who worships idols has denied God. The connection is not merely formal but conceptual: both the *megadef* and the *oved avodah zarah* represent a fundamental rupture with the foundational belief in one God.

### Klal U'Prat Method

The *Chachamim* derive their position through the *klal ufrat* hermeneutical method. *Vehumat vetalita* is the *klal* — the general principle; *ki kilelat elohim taluy* is the *prat* — the specific case. In the *klal ufrat* method, the *prat* functions as an explanation and limitation of the *klal*: *ein baklal ela mah shebaprat* — the *klal* applies only to what is specified in the *prat*. Even something similar to the *prat* cannot be included, because the *prat* is extremely limiting.

However, because the *klal* and *prat* here are not in the same verse — *lo talin nivlato al ha'etz* comes with some textual distance from *vehumat vetalita* — the *Chachamim* expand slightly beyond the pure *klal ufrat* rule. Since they are separated, *ata hani leribuyei* — the general clause comes to include one additional case. *Avodat kochavim*, which is similar to *megadef* in every meaningful way — both represent *kefira be'ikar*, denial of fundamental belief — is therefore included alongside the *megadef*.

### Ribui U'Miut Method

Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, employs the *ribui umiut* method. Here, *vehumat vetalita* is a *ribui* — a broad inclusion — and *ki kilelat elohim* is a *miut* — a limitation. Rashi explains the critical difference between the two approaches: in *klal ufrat*, the *prat* uproots the *klal* from its broad application and restricts it precisely to what the *prat* specifies — nothing more, even if similar. In *ribui umiut*, the *miut* merely narrows the range of application without entirely displacing the broader principle; there remains more room for inclusion. The *miut* does not eliminate the *ribui* but limits it.

Under Rabbi Eliezer's *ribui umiut* reading, because the two clauses are separated in the text, the *miut* — which on a closer reading would have limited application only to *avodah zarah* as similar to *megadef* — is understood to expand further, reaching *kol haniskalin*, all those executed by *skilah*. Had the clauses been closer together in the text, Rabbi Eliezer would have limited the expansion to *avodah zarah* alone, as most similar to *megadef*; the textual distance licenses the broader inclusion. In general, the *ribui umiut* method is the more expansive of the two, which accounts for the characteristic difference between the positions: the *Chachamim* using the more restrictive *klal ufrat* arrive at only two cases, while Rabbi Eliezer using the more expansive *ribui umiut* arrives at all cases of *skilah*.

## Gender in Hanging

### Face Directions

The Gemara now addresses the *machlokes* regarding orientation of the hanged body. The *Chachamim* derive from the verse *vechi yihye be'ish chet* — "when there is in a man a sin" — that the verse speaks of *ish*, a man, but not *ishah*, a woman: *oto velo otah*. Rabbi Eliezer, who cannot use this derivation to exclude women (since he holds women are hanged as well, differing only on orientation), reads *oto velo ksuto* — "him but not his garment" — deriving that the hanging is performed without clothing. This too has a rationale: part of the purpose of the post-mortem display is to shame the person for their transgression, and accordingly the body is displayed without garments, consistent with the practice at the time of *skilah* when clothing is removed to facilitate the execution.

### Women Not Hanged

The *Chachamim* then derive from a separate verse — *vechi yihye be'ish chet*, *ish velo ishah* — that women are categorically exempt from *telihah* altogether. Rabbi Eliezer uses this verse differently, as will be seen below.

### Ben Sorer Exception

Reish Lakish raises the question of *ben sorer u'moreh*: according to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *kol haniskalin nitlin*, one executed by *skilah* is also hanged, does this apply to a *ben sorer u'moreh* as well? He argues that the verse *vechi yihye be'ish chet* — specifically the word *ish* — comes to exclude *ben sorer u'moreh*, who is not yet a man. Since he is a minor, the verse does not apply to him.

But a *braita* is cited that appears to attribute to Rabbi Eliezer the view that *ben sorer u'moreh niskal ve-nitla* — he is both stoned and hanged. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this by reading the verse differently: *ish velo ben* — the exclusion of *ben* is derived; but then *chet* is the operative word — *mi she-al chet neherag*, one who is killed for his sin. *Ben sorer u'moreh* is executed not for any sin he has actually committed but *al shum sofo* — on account of his projected future. He is *nidon al shem sofo*, preemptively sentenced. Since he is not killed for a *chet* in the full sense, he is excluded from the principle of *vechi yihye be'ish chet*. This produces a *miut achar miut* — a double exclusion — which, as a rule of *drashah*, comes to *lerabot*, to include: *ben sorer u'moreh* is therefore included in *telihah* even according to Rabbi Eliezer.

The discussion of *ben sorer u'moreh* being executed *al shem sofo* — preemptively, for what he will become rather than what he has done — evokes the theological principle of *ba'asher hu sham*: God judged Yishmael according to who he was at that moment, not according to what he would become. The *ben sorer u'moreh* represents a deliberate statutory exception to that principle, and its full treatment awaits *Perek Ben Sorer u'Moreh*.

## One Case Per Day Rule

### Two Cases Rule

Returning to the conclusion of the *mishna* — *ein danim shnayim beyom echad* — the Gemara examines what exactly is prohibited. The *mishna* cited Shimon ben Shetach's hanging of eighty women in Ashkelon as a case where this rule was apparently violated, or at least raised as a concern.

### Different Execution Types

Rav Chisda proposes a narrow reading: *lo shanu ela bishtei mitot* — the prohibition against judging two cases in one day applies only when the two cases involve two different types of execution. *Aval bemita achat danin* — if both cases involve the same form of execution, two cases may in fact be judged on the same day. Since Shimon ben Shetach's case involved women all subject to the same *mitah*, it would fall within what is permitted according to this reading.

The Gemara then revises even this formulation: *lo shanu ela bemita achat ke'ein shtei mitot* — the prohibition applies even to a single type of execution that is, in some respect, like two. This means two different *aveirot* that happen to carry the same capital punishment. *Aval bemita achat va'aveirah achat danin* — two entirely identical cases — same crime, same punishment — may be heard together on the same day.

### Adultery Challenge

Rav Ada bar Ahava challenges this: *ein danim shnayim beyom echad afilu benoef venoefet* — even a male and female adulterer cannot be judged on the same day. This is a powerful challenge: not only is it the same crime and the same punishment, it is literally the same act committed together. One would think this is the clearest possible case of *bemita achat va'aveirah achat*. Yet the *braita* rules that even this falls under *ein danim shnayim beyom echad*.

### Bat Kohen Resolution

Rav Chisda resolves the challenge: the case must involve a *bat kohen* and her lover. A *bat kohen* who commits adultery is executed not by *chenek* — the standard form of execution for adultery — but by *sreifah*, one of the relatively rare instances where burning applies. Her male partner, however, is executed by *chenek*. Thus even though it is the same act and technically the same crime, the two parties face different forms of execution — and that is why they cannot be judged together on the same day.

Alternatively, the case involves *bat kohen* and *zomemei zomemeha* — the false witnesses who had conspired against her being themselves exposed by a third set of witnesses. In such a case, she would be executed by *sreifah* while the exposed false witnesses would be executed by *chenek* — since the principle of *va'asitem lo ka'asher zamam* would, by some views, prescribe *sreifah* for the first set of witnesses but the second set, who were trying to discredit true witnesses, would receive the ordinary punishment of *chenek*. In either formulation, the result is two different *mitot*, which is why the prohibition applies.

## Beit Din Elastic Power

### Rabbi Elazar Statement

A *braita* records the well-known statement of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov: *shamati shebeit din makin veonshin shelo min haTorah* — I have heard that a *beit din* may administer lashes and punishments that are not prescribed by Torah law. This is a foundational principle for the concept of extra-legal judicial authority, one that has generated extensive discussion in later literature — notably in a *drashah* of the *Ran* and in a responsum of the *Rosh* arising from a case in Spain where an informant had endangered the Jewish community. The *Rosh*, drawing on this principle, argued for the power of contemporary rabbinic authority to impose punishments not found explicitly in Torah law, and reportedly suggested that the informant's tongue be cut out — a punishment with no basis in the *Written Torah* — precisely because of this extra-legal elastic authority.

### Syag LaTorah Purpose

The critical qualification is that this authority does not license acting *against* Torah law: *velo la'avor al divrei Torah* — it is not a license to transgress. The entire purpose is *kedei la'asot syag la-Torah* — to build a protective fence around Torah law, to deter violations when ordinary enforcement mechanisms are insufficient or when social conditions demand a more forceful response.

### Horse Riding Example

The first illustration: *umaseh be-echad sherachav al sus be-Shabbat bimei Yevanim* — there was a case of a man who rode a horse on *Shabbat* during the Hellenistic period. He was brought before the *beit din* and stoned to death. Now, riding a horse on *Shabbat* is a *de-rabbanan* prohibition — specifically a *shvut*, a rabbinic decree enacted as a *gezirah* lest one break off a branch to use as a whip. Stoning is, of course, a capital punishment reserved for the most severe Torah violations. By any standard measure, this man's offense carried no capital punishment at all.

The Gemara explains: *lo mipnei she-ra'uy lekach ela she-ha-sha'ah tzericha lekach* — not because this was the fitting penalty, but because the hour required it. The Hellenistic period was one of intense social and religious pressure; observance of rabbinic enactments around *Shabbat* was being eroded. The *beit din* of that generation determined that radical enforcement was necessary to impress upon the community the seriousness of even rabbinic-level *Shabbat* violations. One might imagine that the relevant *de-rabbanans* had only recently been legislated, and that without dramatic enforcement they would remain a dead letter. The principle underlying the enforcement is extra-legal authority deployed precisely because the *hora'at sha'ah* — the need of the hour — demanded it.

### Public Relations Example

The second illustration: *ma'aseh be-adam echad she-hitiach im ishto tachat ha-te'enah* — there was a case of a man who engaged in sexual relations with his wife in public, beneath a fig tree. He was brought to *beit din* and given lashes. There is, in fact, no *halacha* that prohibits marital relations per se; only the public nature of the act made it objectionable. Technically, no punishment attaches to this conduct. Yet here again, *lo mipnei she-ra'uy lekach ela she-ha-sha'ah tzericha lekach* — the generation required a strong public response to conduct that, while not halakhically prohibited in the strict sense, was socially destructive and publicly disgraceful.

## Hanging Methods

### Hanging Procedure

The *mishna* turns to the practical procedure of *telihah*. How is the hanging carried out? *Meshaki'in et ha-korah ba-aretz* — a vertical beam is planted in the ground. *Ve-ha-etz yotzei* — a horizontal board extends from it. The condemned person's two hands are bound together — *makif shtei yadav zo al gav zo* — and the body is suspended from the beam in that posture, hands crossed behind the back.

### Yosei Alternative

Rabbi Yosei offers an alternative: *ha-korah mutal al ha-kotel* — rather than planting the beam in the ground, one leans a beam against the wall. *Ve-toleh oto ke-derech she-ha-tabachim osim* — the body is then hung in the manner that butchers use when hanging an animal that is being flayed. This is a more practical method requiring less preparation, and it serves the same purpose of briefly displaying the body.

### Immediate Removal

Whatever the method employed, the requirement is unambiguous: *u-matirin oto mi-yad* — they untie him immediately. The moment the body has been hung, it is taken down. This near-instantaneous display is the fulfillment of the *mitzvah* of *telihah*; leaving the body up even briefly longer than necessary begins to encroach on the prohibition of *lo talin*. If the body is left overnight, *over alav be-lo ta'aseh* — one has violated a negative commandment of the Torah.

## Criminal Burial Laws

### No Overnight Hanging

The prohibition is stated explicitly in the verse: *lo talin nivlato al ha'etz* — you shall not leave his corpse hanging on the wood overnight. This is accompanied by a positive commandment — *ki kavor tikberenu* — you shall certainly bury him.

### Divine Curse Reason

The verse gives its own reason: *ki kilelat elohim taluy* — for it is a curse of God to leave the body hanging. The plain sense of the verse (*pshat*) is that leaving a corpse on display is repulsive and degrading — an affront to human dignity and, by extension, to God whose image humanity reflects. The *derash* adds another dimension: this person had *beirach et HaShem* — cursed God — and *nimtza Shem Shamayim mitchalel* — God's name becomes desecrated if the display of this curse becomes too public. There is a tension built into the law: the display must occur, because it serves as a deterrent and gives expression to the gravity of the crime; but it must not persist, because prolonging it risks further desecrating the divine name by publicizing the terrible act that was committed.

### Rabbi Meir Suffering

Rabbi Meir draws a profound theological teaching from this verse. When a person suffers, the divine presence responds as if in sympathy: *lashon omeret kallani me-roshi kallani me-zero'i* — the language of the *Shechinah* says, as it were, "my head aches, my arm aches." This is a striking expression of the principle that God's presence is implicated in human suffering, even the suffering of the wicked. If this is true of the suffering of a condemned criminal, *kal vachomer* — how much more so — is God pained by the spilling of the blood of the righteous.

### Honor Exception

The prohibition against leaving a body overnight is not limited to executed criminals: *kol ha-malin et meto over be-lo ta'aseh* — anyone who leaves any corpse unburied overnight transgresses a negative commandment. This applies to all the dead, not only those executed by *beit din*.

However, there is an important exception: *she-hilinhu l'chevodo* — if the body is left overnight for the *kavod* of the deceased, there is no violation. *Le-havi lo aron ve-tachrichin* — to allow time to procure a coffin or shrouds — that delay is permitted, because the delay itself constitutes an act of respect. The underlying principle is that the prohibition targets delay that reflects disregard for the dead; delay undertaken in service of the deceased's honor is not only permitted but appropriate. In contemporary practice, this principle is invoked when families need to wait for relatives to travel from afar — from overseas, from distant cities — in order to be present at the funeral. Such waiting is considered *she-hilinhu l'chevodo* and does not violate the prohibition.

### Burial Plot Types

Executed criminals were not buried in their families' ancestral burial plots: *velo hayu kovrin oto be-kivrot avotav* — he was not buried with his fathers. Instead, the *beit din* maintained two dedicated criminal cemeteries: one for those executed by *hereg* and *chenek*, and a separate plot for those executed by *skilah* and *sreifah*. The separation reflects the relative severity of the different forms of capital punishment — the more severe and the less severe are kept distinct.

### Bone Gathering

Once the flesh had decomposed — *nit'akel ha-basar* — the practice was *melaktin et ha'atzamot*, gathering the bones for secondary interment *be-mikoman*, in their place. Whether *be-mikoman* refers to reburial in the same criminal cemetery or eventual transfer to the ancestral family plot is itself a subject of debate. The practice of *likut atzamot* — secondary bone-gathering — was the standard burial practice of the *mishnaic* period, discussed extensively in *Moed Katan*: bodies were first interred in a cave or rock tomb, and after a year or so when the flesh had decomposed, the bones were gathered and placed in an ossuary for permanent burial. This two-stage burial practice, common throughout the land of Israel in the Second Temple and *Tanna'itic* periods, is not the norm today, reflecting one of the more significant shifts in Jewish burial practice over the centuries.
