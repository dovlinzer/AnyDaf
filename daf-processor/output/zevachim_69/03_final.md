# Zevachim 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Melikah & Neveilah

### Melikah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹרִין שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן, וּבְנֵי יוֹנָה שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנָּן; שֶׁיָּבְשָׁה גַּפָּהּ, שֶׁנִּסְמֵית עֵינָהּ וְשֶׁנִּקְטְעָה רַגְלָהּ – מְטַמֵּא בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** or if he pinched **doves whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has not** yet **arrived,** as they are too young to be sacrificed; **or if he** pinched **pigeons whose time** of fitness **has passed,** as they are too old; or if he pinched the nape of a fledgling **whose wing was withered,** or **whose eye was blinded, or whose leg was severed;** in all these cases, although the bird's nape was pinched, **it renders** one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.**

The daf opens by situating us within an ongoing discussion originating from the *mishnah*. The act of *melikah*—the pinching of a bird's neck performed by the *kohen* as part of the bird sacrifice—presents a striking irony: the very act that constitutes valid slaughter for the *korban* would, under virtually any other circumstance, render the bird a *neveilah*. It does not constitute proper *shechitah*, and outside the sacrificial context it would be treated as a mere killing rather than a valid act of slaughter. Yet within the proper sacrificial framework, it is precisely this act that renders the bird *kasher*.

The *Gemara*'s central inquiry is not simply when the bird becomes forbidden to eat—that might be the result of many disqualifications entirely unrelated to *neveilah* status, such as when it is an *olah*—but rather when *melikah* generates *tumah* upon ingestion, which is the distinctive hallmark of a bird that has become a *neveilah*.

### Three Determinants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה; לֹא הָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ – מְטַמֵּא בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle:** The meat of **any** bird that was initially fit for sacrifice and **whose disqualification** occurred in the course of the service **in the sacred** Temple courtyard **does not render** one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.** The meat of any bird **whose disqualification did not occur in the sacred** area, but rather was disqualified before the service began, **renders** one **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.**

The *mishnah* established that three components must align for *melikah* to avoid producing *neveilah* status: the act must be correct (*melikah* rather than *shechitah* or some other act), the object must be correct (a sanctified bird), and the place must be correct (inside the *Beit HaMikdash*). Even if other elements are invalid—the *melikah* was performed with the left hand, at night, or by someone who was otherwise *pasul*—as long as these three conditions are present, the bird does not become a *neveilah*. However, if any one of the three is missing—if the act was performed with a knife rather than the *kohen*'s thumbnail (wrong act), on a bird that was not consecrated or was otherwise physically disqualified (wrong object), or outside the *Beit HaMikdash* (wrong place)—the bird does become a *neveilah*.

There is one important caveat: if the act performed was *shechitah* rather than *melikah*, even when *shechitah* is the wrong act for that bird, the bird does not thereby become a *neveilah*. The category of *neveilah* in this context is produced by *melikah* applied to the wrong object or in the wrong place—not by *shechitah* regardless of context.

### Pesulah Bakodesh

The *mishnah*'s own language frames this not in terms of act, object, and place, but through the concept of *pesulah bakodesh*—how far out of bounds is the disqualification, and whether it is intrinsic to the world of the *Beit HaMikdash* or precedes and transcends it. This conceptual lens, while sometimes difficult to pin down precisely, shapes the *Gemara*'s subsequent analysis.

A parallel way of measuring the same question is whether something placed on the *mizbeiach* must be removed. Items that are entirely out of bounds—completely irrelevant to the sacrificial order—must be taken down from the altar; those that are merely *pasul* but not utterly foreign to the world of *korbanot* need not be removed. In the terminology of *Brisk*, the question is whether we are dealing with something that is simply not a *melikah* at all, or whether it is a *melikah pesulah*—a defective but recognizable act of *melikah*. If it is not a *melikah* at all (as when one uses a knife), then it is clearly out of bounds. If it is a *melikah pesulah*, it remains within the orbit of the sacrificial order, however imperfectly.

It is in this context that the *Gemara* introduced, at the end of the previous folio, the debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan regarding a *zar*—a non-*kohen*—who performs *melikah*. Rav holds that such a *melikah* does render the bird a *neveilah*; Rabbi Yochanan holds that it does not.

## Zar Performing Melikah

### R. Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְלָקָהּ זָר, מְלָקָהּ פָּסוּל, הַפִּיגּוּל וְהַנּוֹתָר וְהַטָּמֵא – אֵין מְטַמְּאִין אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan:** In a case where **a non-priest pinched** a bird offering, or a priest **disqualified** from the Temple service **pinched it,** or it became ***piggul*,** i.e., it was sacrificed with the intent to consume it beyond its designated time, **or** it became ***notar*,** i.e., its meat remained uneaten beyond its designated time, **or** it became **ritually impure,** in all these cases, even though the meat of these birds may not be consumed, they still **do not render** one who swallows them **ritually impure** when they are **in the throat.**

The *Gemara* opens with a *braita* that supports Rabbi Yochanan's position: *tanya k'vattei d'Rabbi Yochanan*. The *braita* lists a number of disqualified cases of *melikah*—performed by a *zar*, performed by a *pasul kohen* (which encompasses a broad range of disqualifications including an *onen*, a *kohen* who is a *ba'al mum*, and presumably others), and cases involving *piggul*, *notar*, or *tumah*—and declares regarding all of them that the bird does not cause *tumah* upon ingestion. The inclusion of *piggul* is notable, since a *piggul* thought during the service would not retroactively alter the physical nature of the bird. In all these cases, the ruling is *ein metam'in b'veit ha'bli'ah*: they do not constitute *neveilah*.

The *braita* thus confirms that a *zar* performing *melikah* does not create *neveilah* status, in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan. It also broadens the category of *pasul* to include many types of disqualified *kohanim* beyond the specific case of the *zar*.

### Two Separate Halachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: שָׁמַעְתִּי שְׁתַּיִם – אַחַת קְמִיצַת זָר, וְאַחַת מְלִיקַת זָר; אַחַת תֵּרֵד, וְאַחַת לֹא תֵּרֵד; וְלָא יָדַעְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: I heard [*shamati*] two** *halakhot*, **one** concerning **the removal of a handful** from a meal offering **by a non-priest** for burning on the altar, **and one** concerning the **pinching** of a bird offering **by a non-priest.** Although both offerings are disqualified, I heard that **one shall descend** from the altar if it ascended, **and one shall not descend; but I do not know** which *halakha* applies to which case.

Rabbi Yitzchak then introduces a different dimension of the same question. He states: *shamati shtayim halachot*—I heard two distinct rulings, one regarding *kmitzat zar* (a non-*kohen* performing *kemitzah*, the taking of the handful in a *minchah* offering) and one regarding *melikat zar*. The question being addressed in these rulings is not *neveilah* status, but the related question of *yeridah*—whether an item placed on the altar must subsequently be removed. This, too, is a symptom of how far out of bounds the act was: items that are entirely incompatible with the sacrificial order must be taken down from the altar; those that are merely imperfect need not be.

Rabbi Yitzchak reports that he heard a different ruling for each: *achat tered v'achat lo tered*—in one case removal from the altar is required, and in the other it is not—but he does not recall which ruling belongs to which case. Based on everything discussed so far, one might assume that *melikat zar* is the more disqualified act, since *melikah* is specifically the *kohen*'s act, performed with the *kohen*'s thumbnail, and using the thumb of a non-*kohen* is analogous to using a knife—a categorically wrong implement. By contrast, *kemitzat zar* seems like a more standard disqualification of a *zar* performing a *kohen*'s function. The *Gemara*, however, will argue the opposite.

### Chizkiyah Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה, מִסְתַּבְּרָא: קְמִיצָה תֵּרֵד, מְלִיקָה לֹא תֵּרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya said: It stands to reason** that in the case of the **removal of** the **handful** the offering **shall descend** and in the case of **pinching** the offering **shall not descend.**

Chizkiyah resolves the uncertainty: *kmitzah tered, melikah lo tered*—it is the *kemitzah* of a *zar* that must be removed from the altar, while the *melikah* of a *zar* need not be. The *Gemara* then challenges this resolution: what makes *melikat zar* not entirely out of bounds? The answer is that *melikah* is valid on a *bamah*, where a *zar* may perform the rite. Since a *zar* can perform *melikah* on a *bamah*, the *zar* is not entirely foreign to the act of *melikah*, and this demonstrates that his performance of *melikah* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, while *pasul*, is not utterly out of bounds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מְלִיקָה – דְּיֶשְׁנָהּ בְּבָמָה, קְמִיצָה נָמֵי – יֶשְׁנָהּ בְּבָמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **pinching** by a non-priest that would allow the bird to be sacrificed if it ascended onto the altar? If the difference is **that** for a non-priest to do so **would be** valid **on** a private **altar,** where all sacrificial rites were performed by non-priests, this does not constitute a difference, as the **removal of** the **handful** by a non-priest **would also be** valid **on** a private **altar.**

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning: the same would be true of *kemitzah*. A *zar* is also valid on a *bamah*, and if a *minchah* can be brought on a *bamah*, then *kemitzat zar* on a *bamah* is equally valid—so why does *kemitzat zar* warrant removal from the altar?

### Bamah & Kli Sharet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר יֵשׁ מִנְחָה בְּבָמָה יֵשׁ עוֹפוֹת בְּבָמָה. לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר אֵין מִנְחָה – אֵין עוֹפוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״זְבָחִים״ וְלֹא מְנָחוֹת, ״זְבָחִים״ וְלֹא עוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Sheshet says: According to the statement of the one who says** that **a meal offering was** sacrificed **on** a private **altar, birds were** sacrificed **on** a private **altar. According to the statement of the one who says** that **no meal offering** was sacrificed on a private altar, **no birds** were sacrificed there either. **What is the reason** for this? This is because the Torah, in describing the offerings brought at Mount Sinai, before the Tabernacle was built, mentions **slaughtered offerings** (see Exodus 24:5) **but not meal offerings;** it mentions **slaughtered offerings,** i.e., animal offerings, **but not birds.**

The *Gemara* notes the opinion of Rav Sheshet: *l'divrei ha'omer yesh minchah b'bamah yesh of b'bamah, l'divrei ha'omer ein minchah ein of*—whoever holds that a *minchah* can be brought on a *bamah* also holds that bird sacrifices can be brought on a *bamah*, and whoever holds there is no *minchah* on a *bamah* holds there are no bird sacrifices either. The two travel together. This means one cannot argue that *kemitzat zar* on a *bamah* is valid while *melikat zar* on a *bamah* is not, or vice versa. They are linked.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אֵין קִידּוּשׁ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת בְּמִנְחָה בְּבָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, say** that even though both pinching the nape of a bird offering and removing the handful of a meal offering by a non-priest are valid on a private altar, the *halakhot* of meal offerings sacrificed on a private altar cannot be compared to those of meal offerings sacrificed in the Temple. This is because in the case of **a meal offering** sacrificed **on** a private **altar, there is no consecration in a service vessel of** the handful removed from it. By contrast, in the Temple, the handful must always be consecrated in a service vessel.

The resolution is therefore not about the *bamah* itself, but about something else: *ein kiddush b'kli sharet*—on a *bamah*, the *minchah* is not sanctified in a *kli sharet*, a sacred vessel. Sanctification in a *kli sharet* is a necessary step in the *minchah* process within the *Beit HaMikdash*, and its absence on a *bamah* means that whatever a *zar* does there with a *minchah* is occurring in a context so fundamentally different from the *Beit HaMikdash* that it cannot serve as a meaningful precedent. The *minchah* on a *bamah* was never even brought into the full sanctified framework, and so the validity of a *zar* performing *kemitzah* there cannot demonstrate that a *zar* is "in bounds" for purposes of *kemitzah* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*.

By contrast, a bird is never sanctified through a *kli sharet*—a bird is sanctified verbally. Therefore, what occurs with a bird on a *bamah* is much closer to what occurs with a bird inside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The gap between *bamah* and *Beit HaMikdash* for the purpose of bird sacrifice is far narrower than for *minchah*. Consequently, the validity of a *zar* performing *melikah* on a *bamah* is genuinely informative about his status inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, whereas the validity of a *zar* performing *kemitzah* on a *bamah* is not, because the *minchah* there was never properly sanctified in the first place. This is why *melikat zar* does not require removal from the altar, while *kemitzat zar* does.

## Shechitah vs. Melikah

### Neveilah Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: יָכוֹל תְּהֵא מְלִיקָה שֶׁהִיא לִפְנִים, מְטַמְּאָה בְּגָדִים בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נְבֵלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that invalid **pinching that** occurs **inside** the Temple courtyard, such as pinching with the left hand or pinching at night, **would** cause the offering to **render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.** Therefore **the verse states:** "Every soul that eats **a carcass**…shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 17:15). Bird offerings whose napes were pinched inside the Temple courtyard are not considered carcasses.

Having addressed the questions of who performs the *melikah* and under what disqualifications *melikah* retains or loses its status, the *Gemara* now turns to the core principle governing when an act creates *neveilah* status. The governing rule—which both *braitot* to follow will apply—is derived from the juxtaposition of the words *neveilah* and *treifah* in the verse *v'nefesh asher tochal neveilah u'treifah* (Vayikra 17:15). Just as a *treifah* is an animal whose slaughter did not make it permissible—*she'ain materet et ha'issur*—so too the category of *neveilah* that generates *tumah* is limited to an act that similarly failed to permit the animal. An act that does render the animal permissible, even if only for the *mizbeiach* or for the *kohanim*, cannot simultaneously render it a *neveilah*.

### Melikah Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי נְבֵלָה הִיא! אֶלָּא תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״טְרֵיפָה״; מָה טְרֵיפָה – שֶׁאֵין מַתֶּרֶת אֶת הָאִיסּוּר, אַף כֹּל – שֶׁאֵין מַתֶּרֶת אֶת הָאִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** a bird offering whose nape was pinched inside the Temple courtyard **also a carcass? Rather,** the *halakha* of the mishna is derived from that which **the verse states:** "Every soul that eats a carcass or **a *tereifa***…shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 17:15). A *tereifa* is an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months. It is derived from this verse that **just as** having the status of **a *tereifa* does not render permitted** any **forbidden** bird, **so too, any** type of death **that does not render permitted** any **forbidden** bird renders the animal a carcass with regard to ritual impurity.

The first *braita* opens with a hypothetical challenge: perhaps even properly performed *melikah* of a *korban* inside the *Beit HaMikdash* should be considered a *neveilah*, since at the end of the day one did not perform valid *shechitah*. Even if the *kohanim* are permitted to eat from it, perhaps the bird should still carry *neveilah* status for purposes of *tumah*. The *braita* refutes this from the word *neveilah* in the verse, and the *Gemara* clarifies the mechanism: this cannot be the simple meaning of *neveilah* alone, since the question itself presupposes it should be a *neveilah*. Rather, the lesson comes from the word *treifah*—it must be an act that, like *shechitah* of a *treifah*, does not make the animal permissible. Since valid *melikah* of a *korban* inside the *Beit HaMikdash* does make it permissible—for burning on the *mizbeiach* and for the *kohanim* to eat in the case of a *chatat*—it cannot create *neveilah* status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא מְלִיקָה שֶׁהִיא לִפְנִים – הוֹאִיל וְהִיא מַתֶּרֶת אֶת הָאִיסּוּר, אֵין מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, invalid **pinching that is** performed **inside** the Temple courtyard **is excluded, since it renders permitted a forbidden** bird, as it is permitted to sacrifice such a disqualified offering if it ascended onto the altar, whereas it was prohibited to sacrifice such a disqualified offering if it was not pinched. The meat of such an offering therefore **does not render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.**

The *braita* then proceeds to the inverse cases: *hamolek kodashim bachutz u'molek chullin bein mibifnim bein mibachutz*—one who performs *melikah* on *kodashim* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, or on *chullin* whether inside or outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, does produce *neveilah*. The reasoning in each case is that the act of *melikah* did not make the bird permissible. *Melikah* on *kodashim* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* does not permit the bird—the *kohanim* cannot eat it, it cannot be burned as a *korban*. *Melikah* on *chullin* inside or outside the *Beit HaMikdash* similarly does not permit anything. Therefore, in all those cases, *ho'il v'ein matirin et ha'issur metamei begadim b'veit habli'ah*—since the act failed to permit, the bird retains *neveilah* status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵבִיא הַמּוֹלֵק קָדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ, וּמוֹלֵק חוּלִּין בֵּין מִבִּפְנִים בֵּין מִבַּחוּץ – הוֹאִיל וְאֵין מַתִּירִין אֶת הָאִיסּוּר, מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** This principle **includes** two other cases of invalid pinching, for which the Gemara gives a two-word **mnemonic: *Ketz*, *ḥefetz*.** These words are acronyms for the cases of **one who pinches** the napes of **sacrificial** birds **outside** the Temple courtyard, **and** one who **pinches** the napes of **non-sacred** birds **whether inside** the Temple courtyard **or outside** of it. **Since** these offerings **do not render permitted** any **forbidden** bird, as these offerings may not be sacrificed even if brought onto the altar, **they render** the **garments** of one who swallows them **ritually impure** when they are **in the throat.**

### Shechitah Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: יָכוֹל תְּהֵא שְׁחִיטַת חוּלִּין לִפְנִים, וְקָדָשִׁים בֵּין מִבִּפְנִים וּבֵין מִבַּחוּץ, מְטַמְּאָה אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נְבֵלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: One **might** have thought **that the slaughter of non-sacred** birds **inside** the Temple courtyard, **or** the slaughter of **sacrificial** birds **whether inside or outside** of it, would cause their meat to **render** the garments of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.** Therefore **the verse states:** "Every soul that eats **a carcass**…shall be impure until the evening." Birds that have been slaughtered in this manner are not considered carcasses, and so they do not impart ritual impurity.

A second *braita* addresses *shechitah*. It poses the parallel question: perhaps *shechitah* of *chullin* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, or *shechitah* of *kodashim* whether inside or outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, should generate *neveilah* status. In the case of *kodashim*, this would be a serious consideration: the *Torah* requires *melikah* for bird sacrifices, and so *shechitah* on a bird *korban* might seem to render it a *neveilah* just as using a knife would by *melikah*. The *braita* rules that none of these cases creates *neveilah*, invoking the word *neveilah* again, and the *Gemara* clarifies through the *treifah* link that a different principle is operative here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי נְבֵלָה הִיא! אֶלָּא תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״טְרֵיפָה״; מָה טְרֵיפָה – שָׁוָוה בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ, אַף כֹּל – שָׁווּת בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** a bird slaughtered in one of these manners **also a carcass? Rather,** it is derived from that which **the verse states:** "Every soul that eats a carcass or **a *tereifa***…shall be impure until the evening," as follows: **Just as** the status of **a *tereifa* is the same,** if the bird is slaughtered **inside** the Temple courtyard **as** it is if the bird is slaughtered **outside** of it, i.e., forbidden, **so too all** forbidden birds whose status **is the same** if slaughtered **inside** the Temple courtyard **as** it is if they were slaughtered **outside** of it constitute carcasses.

For *shechitah*, the relevant principle from the juxtaposition to *treifah* is: *mah treifah shaveh bifnim kevachutz, af kol shaveh bifnim kevachutz*—just as a *treifah*'s disqualification operates equally inside and outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, so too *neveilah* status can only attach to an act that is equally problematic both inside and outside. Only when an act carries the same invalidity in both domains can it be said to produce *neveilah* status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא שְׁחִיטַת חוּלִּין בִּפְנִים, וְקָדָשִׁים בֵּין מִבִּפְנִים בֵּין מִבַּחוּץ – הוֹאִיל וְלֹא שָׁווּ בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ, אֵין מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, **the slaughter of non-sacred** birds **inside** the Temple courtyard, **or** of **sacrificial** birds **whether inside or outside** of it, **is excluded, since** the status of such birds **is not the same** if they are slaughtered **inside** the Temple courtyard **as** if they are slaughtered **outside** of it. The meat of such an offering therefore **does not render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.**

### Inside-Outside Link

Applying this principle to *shechitat chullin bifnim*: *shechitah* of *chullin* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is entirely valid. It is the prescribed and correct act. Since the act is perfectly legitimate on the outside, it cannot generate *neveilah* status on the inside. A *neveilah*-producing act must be equally problematic in both domains; *shechitat chullin* is not, and therefore does not create *neveilah* even when performed inside the *Beit HaMikdash* in an improper context.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא חוּלִּין – לֹא שָׁווּ בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ; אֶלָּא קָדָשִׁים – אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי פְּסוּלִין נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Granted,** the status of **non-sacred** birds **is not the same** if slaughtered **inside** the Temple courtyard **as** if they are slaughtered **outside** of it, as if slaughtered inside the Temple courtyard they are forbidden, while if slaughtered outside of it they are permitted. **But** with regard to **sacrificial** birds, in both **this** case **and that** case, whether slaughtered inside the Temple courtyard or outside of it, they **are disqualified.**

The case of *shechitat kodashim*—whether inside or outside the *Beit HaMikdash*—presents a more difficult challenge. If *shechitah* of a bird *korban* is wrong both inside and outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, then by the inside-outside equivalence principle it would seem to satisfy the condition for *neveilah*. The status is the same in both domains—uniformly invalid—and so one might conclude it should generate *neveilah* status. Rava acknowledges this: the inside-outside principle alone does not resolve the case of *shechitat kodashim*. That ruling requires a different source.

### Rava Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם הוֹעִילָה לוֹ שְׁחִיטַת חוּץ לְחַיְּיבוֹ כָּרֵת, לֹא תּוֹעִיל לוֹ לְטַהֲרָהּ מִידֵי נְבֵילָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The *halakha* with regard to slaughtered sacrificial birds outside the Temple should not be derived from the verse at all, but rather by logic. **If the slaughter of** a sacrificial bird **outside** the Temple courtyard **has** sufficient **effect on it** as proper slaughter **to render** the one who slaughtered it **liable to excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*],** which is the punishment for slaughtering offerings outside the Temple courtyard (see 107a), could it be that **it does not have** sufficient **effect on** the bird as proper slaughter **to render it ritually pure** by preventing it **from** assuming the status of **a carcass?**

Rava explains: *im ho'ilu lo shchitas chutz lachayev kareis, lo so'ilu lo letaharu midei neveila*—if *shechitah* of a bird *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is sufficient to render one liable for *shchutei chutz* (slaughtering a sacrifice outside the sanctuary, which carries *karet*), then it cannot simultaneously make the bird a *neveilah*. The *Gemara* elsewhere establishes that performing *shechitah* on a bird *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* does generate *chiyuv karet* for *shchutei chutz*—the act is legally meaningful and recognized, even though it is invalid for the *korban*. Because it is a meaningful act, it is not an act of pure nullity, and therefore it does not produce *neveilah* status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן חוּץ, פָּנִים מְנָלַן? הוֹאִיל וְלֹא שָׁווּ בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* with regard to the slaughter of sacrificial birds **outside** the Temple courtyard; **from where do we** derive this *halakha* with regard to their slaughter **inside** the Temple courtyard? The Gemara answers that it is derived from the principle articulated above: **Since** their status **is not the same** if they are slaughtered **inside** the Temple courtyard **as** if they are slaughtered **outside** of it, as one who slaughters a sacrificial bird outside the Temple courtyard is liable to receive *karet*, the status of a carcass does not apply.

Once it is established that *shechitat kodashim bachutz* does not create *neveilah*—because it is a meaningful, consequential act—the inside-outside link comes into play for the second stage: since it is not *neveilah* on the outside, it is not *neveilah* on the inside either, by the same *treifah* equivalence.

### Proper vs. Improper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מָלַק קָדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ נָמֵי לָא – דְּלָא שָׁווּ בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** it follows that if the priest **pinched sacrificial** birds **outside** the Temple courtyard, they are **also not** considered carcasses, **as** their status **is not the same** if they are pinched **inside** the Temple courtyard **as** if they are pinched **outside** of it; if they are pinched inside, they are fit offerings, and if they are pinched outside, they are disqualified. This conclusion would contradict the mishna, which rules that sacrificial birds pinched outside the Temple courtyard are considered carcasses, and they render one who eats their meat impure.

The *Gemara* then addresses *melikat kodashim bachutz*—*melikah* performed on a *korban* bird outside the *Beit HaMikdash*—which the earlier *braita* ruled does create *neveilah*. One might question this ruling: inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, *melikat kodashim* is the correct and valid act. Why not say that since the act is valid inside, it is also not *neveilah* outside? After all, for *shechitat kodashim*, the *Gemara* used the opposite direction—valid outside informed the ruling inside.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶיכְשֵׁרוֹ מִדָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶיכְשֵׁרוֹ; וְאֵין דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶיכְשֵׁרוֹ מִדָּבָר שֶׁבְּהֶיכְשֵׁרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi says: One can derive** the *halakha* with regard to **an item that is** prepared **not in its valid manner,** i.e., sacrificial birds slaughtered outside the Temple courtyard, **from** the *halakha* with regard to another **item that is** prepared **not in its valid manner,** i.e., sacrificial birds slaughtered inside it. **But one cannot derive** the *halakha* with regard to **an item that is** prepared **not in its valid manner,** i.e., sacrificial birds pinched outside the Temple courtyard, **from** the *halakha* with regard to **an item that is** prepared **in its valid manner,** i.e., those pinched inside it. The cases of a bird pinched outside and a bird pinched inside the Temple courtyard are not subject to comparison, so one cannot derive conclusions from the differences between them.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi answers: *danim davar shelo b'hechsheiro midavar shelo b'hechsheiro, v'ein danim davar shelo b'hechsheiro midavar shebehechsheiro*—one may draw conclusions from one improper act to another improper act, but one may not draw conclusions from a case done properly to a case done improperly. The *shechitah* analysis moved from one improperly performed outside act to its parallel inside—both were cases of something done incorrectly or outside its proper domain. But one cannot take *melikat kodashim b'fnim*, which is the act done *correctly*, and use it to validate *melikat kodashim bachutz*, which is the act done improperly. The link only runs from improper to improper, not from proper to improper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְיוֹצֵא שֶׁאִם עָלָה לֹא יֵרֵד? שֶׁהֲרֵי יוֹצֵא כָּשֵׁר בְּבָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** can one **not** derive the *halakha* with regard to disqualified offerings from the *halakha* with regard to fit offerings? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **with regard to** an item that **emerged** from the Temple courtyard and was thereby disqualified, **that if** it nevertheless **ascended** upon the altar **it shall not descend?** It is derived from the fact **that** an item that **emerged is valid** for sacrifice **on** a private **altar,** i.e., that disqualification was not applicable to an offering sacrificed on a private altar, as there was no Temple. Here, the *baraita* derives the *halakha* with regard to a disqualified offering from the *halakha* with regard to a fit one.

The *Gemara* notes one potential counterexample: the principle that something taken outside the *Beit HaMikdash* and placed on the altar need not be removed, since it is valid on a *bamah*, seems to use the status of something outside the *Beit HaMikdash* to inform the ruling inside it. The *Gemara* resolves this: that principle is not derived from the *halacha* of a *bamah* per se, but is supported directly by a *pasuk*, and thus operates on a different basis.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא אַ״זֹּאת תּוֹרַת הָעוֹלָה״ סְמִיךְ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The ***tanna*** of that *baraita* **relies on** the verse: **"This is the law of the burnt offering [*ha'ola*]"** (Leviticus 6:2), a seemingly superfluous general phrase which is interpreted homiletically to **include** the *halakha* that any item that ascends [*ola*] upon the altar shall not descend from it, even if it was disqualified. The verse is the actual source for the *halakha* of the *baraita*, whereas the case of a private altar is cited merely in support of this ruling.

## Treifah Bird Dispute

### Logical Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָלַק וְנִמְצְאת טְרֵיפָה – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מְטַמֵּא בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the priest **pinched** the nape of the bird's neck properly **and** then **it was found** to be **a *tereifa*,** and it was therefore disqualified from being sacrificed and forbidden for consumption by a priest, **Rabbi Meir says:** An olive-bulk of its meat **does not render** one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat,** as the pinching prevents it from assuming the status of a carcass.

The *mishnah* now turns to a new case: *malak v'nimtzet treifah*—*melikah* was performed on a bird that was subsequently found to be a *treifah*. This raises a question that builds directly on the principles established thus far.

To understand the debate, one must first clarify the *halachic* definition of *neveilah*. In the *Torah*'s broader sense, a *neveilah* is an animal that died of illness or without any form of slaughter. In *halachic* application, a *neveilah* is any animal that did not receive a valid *shechitah*—even a technically defective *shechitah* due to a nicked blade, or *shechitah* performed by a non-Jew, renders the animal a *neveilah*. Given this, one might reason that if a bird or animal was a *treifah* and was then slaughtered, the *shechitah* did not truly make it permissible—since the animal was already *treifah*—and therefore the act should produce *neveilah* status. However, the accepted *halacha* for animals (*beheimot*) is that a valid *shechitah* on a *treifah* does not make it a *neveilah*. The physical act of *shechitah* was performed correctly, and even though the *treifah* status prevents consumption, the animal retains *treifah* rather than *neveilah* status and does not cause *tumah*.

For birds, the question becomes more complex—and generates a three-way dispute. When one performs *melikah* on a *korban* bird that turns out to be a *treifah*, one could argue that this *melikah* never truly entered the category of a valid sacrificial act, because the underlying bird was disqualified. This is analogous to the earlier analysis of *melikah* in the wrong place or on the wrong object: since the *melikah* did not make the bird permissible, it should produce *neveilah* status. Alternatively, one could argue that since *melikah* is the prescribed act for bird sacrifices, and the bird was in the proper place and was at least formally consecrated, the *melikah* should be recognized as the appropriate act and therefore not generate *neveilah*.

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; אִם נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה, שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – שְׁחִיטָתָהּ מְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתָהּ מִטּוּמְאָתָהּ; נִבְלַת הָעוֹף, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתְּהֵא שְׁחִיטָתוֹ מְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתוֹ מִטּוּמְאָתוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir said:** My opinion can be inferred ***a fortiori*. If an animal carcass transmits impurity** to a person **through touching** it **and through carrying** it, and nevertheless the **slaughter of** an animal **purifies it,** even if it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity,** i.e., its slaughter prevents it from assuming the impurity status of a carcass, then with regard to **a bird carcass, which** possesses a lesser degree of impurity, as it **does not transmit impurity** to a person **through touching** it **and through carrying** it, but only through swallowing it, **is it not logical that its slaughter should purify it,** even if it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity?**

Rabbi Meir holds the most permissive position: *shechitah* of a *treifah* bird does not create *neveilah*, and neither does *melikah* of a *treifah* bird *korban*. Both acts, when they are the designated acts for their respective subjects, protect the bird from *neveilah* status even if the bird was ultimately *treifah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה מָצִינוּ בִּשְׁחִיטָתוֹ – שֶׁהִיא מַכְשַׁרְתָּהּ לַאֲכִילָה, וּמְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתוֹ מִטּוּמְאָתוֹ; אַף מְלִיקָתוֹ, שֶׁהִיא מַכְשַׁרְתּוֹ בַּאֲכִילָה – תְּטַהֵר טְרֵיפָתוֹ מִידֵי טוּמְאָתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** And once it is established that slaughter renders a bird that is a *tereifa* pure, it can be inferred that **just as we found with regard to its slaughter that it renders** a bird **fit for consumption and purifies** a bird, even if it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity, so too its pinching, which renders** a bird offering **fit with regard to consumption, should purify it,** even if it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity.**

Rabbi Meir's argument proceeds in two stages. First, a *kal vachomer*: *neveilat beheimah* is a weighty form of *tumah*—it causes *tumah* through touch and carrying (*maga* and *masa*). Nevertheless, *shechitah* of a *treifah beheimah* does not make it a *neveilah*; all agree on this. Now, *neveilat of*—a bird *neveilah*—is a less weighty form of *tumah*, applying only upon ingestion and not through touch or carrying. If the heavier *tumah* of *neveilat beheimah* is circumvented by valid *shechitah* even of a *treifah*, then *kal vachomer* the lighter *tumah* of *neveilat of* should similarly be circumvented by valid *shechitah* of a *treifah* bird. This establishes that *shechitat of treifah* is not *neveilah*.

Second, having established this, Rabbi Meir builds to *melikah* through a *mah matzinu*: just as *shechitah* is the designated act that makes an animal permissible and therefore protects a *treifah beheimah* from *neveilah* status, so too *melikah* is the designated act that makes a bird *korban* permissible (*machshirah l'achilah* for the *kohanim* in applicable cases, and fit for the *mizbeiach*), and therefore *melikah* of a *treifah korban* bird should also not produce *neveilah* status.

### R. Yose Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּהּ כְּנִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה – שְׁחִיטָתָהּ מְטַהַרְתָּהּ, וְלֹא מְלִיקָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** Although one can derive from the case of an animal that slaughter renders even a bird that is a *tereifa* pure, that derivation cannot be extended to pinching. The same restriction that applies to every *a fortiori* inference, namely, that a *halakha* derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference is no more stringent than the source from which it is derived, applies here: **It is sufficient** for the halakhic status of the carcass of a bird that is a *tereifa* to be **like** that of **the carcass of** an **animal** that is a *tereifa*; **its slaughter renders it pure, but its pinching** does **not.**

Rabbi Yose occupies a middle position. He agrees with the *kal vachomer* that *shechitat of treifah* is not *neveilah*. However, he objects to Rabbi Meir's *mah matzinu* that extends this to *melikat of treifah*. Rabbi Yose invokes the principle of *dayyo*: *dayo lavo min hadin lihyot kenevelas beheimah—shechitasa metaheras, v'lo melikasa*. The *dayyo* principle holds that a conclusion derived through *kal vachomer* cannot exceed the original case from which it was derived. The entire chain of reasoning traces back to *neveilat beheimah*, where only *shechitah* removes *neveilah* status—there is no act analogous to *melikah* for a *beheimah* that would similarly protect it. Since the original case knows only *shechitah* as the protecting act, the *dayyo* principle limits the extension to *shechitah* for birds as well. One may not leap from *shechitah* to *melikah* via a *mah matzinu* when the entire argument rests on a *kal vachomer* that is itself limited by *dayyo*. For Rabbi Yose, then, *shechitat of treifah* is not *neveilah*, but *melikat of treifah* is.

Rabbi Yehudah, by contrast, disagrees with stage one—he holds that even *shechitat of treifah* produces *neveilah* status. He does not reject the *kal vachomer* on procedural grounds but rather on the basis of a *pasuk*.

### Pasuk Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לָא דָּרֵישׁ ״דַּיּוֹ״?! וְהָא ״דַּיּוֹ״ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In the mishna, Rabbi Yosei answers Rabbi Meir by invoking the principle that a *halakha* derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference is no more stringent than the source from which it is derived. The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Meir not require** that *a fortiori* inferences conform to the principle that **it is sufficient** for the conclusion that emerges from an *a fortiori* inference to be like its source? **But isn't** the principle: **It is sufficient,** etc., mandated **by Torah law?**

The *Gemara* asks how Rabbi Meir can dismiss Rabbi Yose's *dayyo* objection, since *dayyo d'Oraita*—the limiting principle of *dayyo*—is not a rabbinic construct but derives from the *Torah* itself, as illustrated by the case of Miriam (*Bamidbar* 12:14): God's displeasure might logically have warranted fourteen days of isolation by *kal vachomer* from a father's displeasure, yet the *Torah* limits it to seven days, because *dayyo lavo min hadin lihyot k'nidon*—the conclusion may not exceed the original case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מִדִּין קַל וְחוֹמֶר כֵּיצַד? ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה: וְאָבִיהָ יָרֹק יָרַק בְּפָנֶיהָ וְגוֹ׳״ – קַל וְחוֹמֶר לַשְּׁכִינָה אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם; אֶלָּא דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **How** is it derived from the Torah that derivation **by** means of an ***a fortiori* inference** is a valid method of biblical exegesis? The Torah states with regard to Miriam, who was reprimanded by God: **"And the Lord said to Moses: If her father had but spit in her face,** should she not hide in shame seven days? Let her be shut up outside the camp seven days" (Numbers 12:14). If one who was reprimanded by her father would hide in shame for seven days, one could infer through an ***a fortiori*** inference that one reprimanded by **the Divine Presence** should be shut up outside the camp for **fourteen days. Rather,** one must say: **It is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source.**

The *Gemara* answers by citing Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Avin: Rabbi Meir did not actually rely solely on the *mah matzinu* extended from the *kal vachomer*. Rather, *kra ashkhach v'darish*—he found a *pasuk* and expounded it. The verse is *zot torat habehemah v'ha'of* (Vayikra 11:46)—"this is the law of the *beheimah* and the *of*." The *Gemara* notes that this formulation is puzzling, since the *tumah* of *neveilat beheimah* and *neveilat of* are not merely different in degree but mutually exclusive: *beheimah* causes *tumah* through touch and carrying but not upon ingestion; *of* causes *tumah* upon ingestion (affecting even the garments of the one who swallows it) but not through touch and carrying. How can the *Torah* call this a single *torah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי אָבִין: רַבִּי מֵאִיר קְרָא אַשְׁכַּח וְקָדָרֵשׁ –
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Avin, said: Rabbi Meir** does require that *a fortiori* inferences conform to this principle. But he does not actually infer his opinion *a fortiori*; rather, **he found a verse and interpreted** it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָעוֹף״ – וְכִי בְּאֵיזוֹ תּוֹרָה שָׁוְותָה בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹף וְעוֹף לִבְהֵמָה? בְּהֵמָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, עוֹף אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא! עוֹף מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה, בְּהֵמָה אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Torah states, with regard to the impurity of unslaughtered animal carcasses: **"This is the law of the beast, and of the fowl"** (Leviticus 11:46), indicating that the two are somehow equated. **But with regard to what law is a beast equal to a fowl and a fowl** equal **to a beast?** The *halakhot* of ritual impurity governing animals and birds are not comparable; **an animal transmits impurity by touching and by carrying,** whereas **a bird does not transmit impurity by touching or by carrying.** Furthermore, **a bird renders** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat; an animal does not render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה בְּהֵמָה – דָּבָר שֶׁמַּכְשִׁירָהּ לַאֲכִילָה, מְטַהֵר טְרֵיפָתָהּ מִטּוּמְאָתָהּ; אַף עוֹף – דָּבָר שֶׁמַּכְשִׁירוֹ בַּאֲכִילָה, מְטַהֵר טְרֵיפָתוֹ מִטּוּמְאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this verse serves **to tell you** that **just as** with regard to **an animal, that which renders it fit for consumption,** i.e., slaughter, **purifies it,** even when it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity, so too** with regard to **a bird, that which renders** it **fit for consumption,** i.e., both the slaughter of a non-sacred bird and pinching the nape of a bird offering, **purifies** a bird, even if it is **a *tereifa*, from its impurity.**

The answer is that the verse is teaching a common principle: *mah beheimah davar shemakhshirah l'akhilah metahar tereifasah mitumah—af of davar shemakhshir b'akhilasoh metaher tereifasoh mitumatoh*. Just as by a *beheimah*, the act that renders it permissible for eating (*shechitah*) also protects a *treifah beheimah* from *neveilah* status, so too by a bird, whatever act renders it permissible for eating—and for a *korban* bird that is *melikah*—also protects a *treifah* bird from *neveilah* status. This is not merely a *mah matzinu* dependent on a *kal vachomer*; it is a direct *pasuk*. Therefore *dayyo* does not apply, and Rabbi Meir is not limited to *shechitah* alone.

### R. Yehudah Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַאי טַעְמָא? קְרָא אַשְׁכַּח וְקָדָרֵשׁ: ״נְבֵלָה טְרֵפָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And what is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that a bird that is a *tereifa* imparts impurity even when slaughtered? He too **found a verse and interpreted** it. The Torah states with regard to the ritual impurity of kosher bird carcasses: "And every soul that eats **a carcass,** or **a *tereifa***…he shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 17:15).

The *Gemara* turns to Rabbi Yehudah. His view—that even *shechitat of treifah* creates *neveilah* status—is also rooted in a *pasuk*. The same verse cited earlier in the discussion of *tumah* reads: *v'nefesh asher tochal neveilah u'treifah* (Vayikra 17:15)—if one eats a *neveilah* or a *treifah* one is *tamei*. Rabbi Yehudah takes this verse's mention of *treifah* literally: it tells us that eating a *treifah* bird—even one that was subjected to *shechitah*—generates *tumah*. For Rabbi Yehudah, this is a straightforward reading of the verse and constitutes a refutation of Rabbi Meir's *kal vachomer*. The *pasuk* explicitly includes *treifah* in the category of things that cause *tumah* upon ingestion, and for Rabbi Yehudah this applies even to a bird that was *shechted* but found to be *treifah*.

### Treifah Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: טְרֵיפָה לָמָּה נֶאֶמְרָה? אִם טְרֵיפָה חַיָּה – הֲרֵי נְבֵילָה אֲמוּרָה! אִם טְרֵיפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה – הֲרֵי הִיא בִּכְלַל נְבֵילָה! אֶלָּא לְהָבִיא טְרֵיפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ, שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: Why was** the case of **a *tereifa* stated? If** the verse is referring to **a live *tereifa*,** it should not be impure, as the term **"a carcass" is stated,** indicating that to impart impurity the bird must be dead. **If** it is referring to **a *tereifa*** that **is not alive,** but rather has died of its wounds, **it** falls **within the category of a carcass. Rather,** the word *tereifa* is written **to include a *tereifa* that one slaughtered** before it had the opportunity to die by itself, to teach **that it imparts ritual impurity** as would a carcass.

The *Gemara* explores how Rabbi Yehudah reads the word *treifah* in the verse. The question is: what scenario does *treifah* describe there? Rabbi Yehudah explains: *im treifah chayah harei neveilah amurah*—if a *treifah* lives out its life and then dies naturally, it is already a *neveilah* by that point and need not be separately mentioned. Conversely, *im treifah einah chayah*, if we consider a *treifah* as essentially already dead-while-living, then it would already fall under the category of *neveilah* in any case. Therefore, the only independent role *treifah* can play in the verse is *l'havi treifah she'shachtah*—to teach the case of a *treifah* that was subjected to *shechitah*. This is the *treifah* that is mentioned alongside *neveilah*: an animal that was slaughtered but was found to be a *treifah*. And the verse teaches that eating such a bird causes *tumah*. For Rabbi Yehudah, this is an explicit *pasuk* that a bird *treifah*—even when *shechted*—causes *tumah*, in direct contradiction to Rabbi Meir's *kal vachomer*.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְחֵלֶב נְבֵלָה וְחֵלֶב טְרֵפָה״ –
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi said to** the Sage who suggested this source for Rabbi Yehuda's opinion: **If that is so,** one should interpret another verse likewise, **as it is written: "And the fat of a carcass, and the fat of a *tereifa*,** may be used for any other service" (Leviticus 7:24), meaning that although the meat of a carcass imparts ritual impurity, those fats that would be forbidden even if the animal had been slaughtered do not impart impurity.

The *Gemara* challenges Rabbi Yehudah's interpretive method. There is another verse that also juxtaposes *neveilah* and *treifah*: *v'cheilev neveilah v'cheilev treifah yei'aseh l'chol melacha v'achol lo socheilehu* (Vayikra 7:24)—the fat of a *neveilah* and the fat of a *treifah* may be used for any labor, but not eaten. The standard interpretation of *yei'aseh l'chol melacha* is that the fat is *tahor*—it does not cause *tumah*. This implies that while the fat of a *neveilah* and *treifah* is *tahor*, the meat is *tamei*. But we all agree—including Rabbi Yehudah—that a *beheimah treifah* that was *shechted* is *tahor* and does not cause *tumah*. If Rabbi Yehudah's method of reading *treifah* in verses literally to mean a *treifah* that was *shechted* is applied here, one would conclude that the meat of such an animal is *tamei*—which is not the *halacha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי נֵימָא: אִם טְרֵיפָה חַיָּה – הֲרֵי נְבֵילָה אֲמוּרָה, אִם טְרֵיפָה אֵינָהּ חַיָּה – הֲרֵי הִיא בִּכְלַל נְבֵילָה! אֶלָּא לְהָבִיא טְרֵיפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ, שֶׁחֶלְבָּהּ טָהוֹר. מִכְּלָל דְּהִיא מְטַמְּאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **There too let us say,** interpreting the verse according to Rabbi Yehuda's logic: Why is the case of a *tereifa* stated? **If** it is referring to **a live *tereifa*,** the case is superfluous, as **"a carcass" is stated.** Since the forbidden fat of a carcass is pure, obviously that of a live animal is pure. **If** it is referring to **a *tereifa*** that **is not alive,** but has rather died of its wounds, **it is** included **within the category of "a carcass,"** and likewise it need not be mentioned. **Rather,** the word "*tereifa*" is written **to include a *tereifa* that one slaughtered,** to teach **that its** forbidden **fat** is **pure. By inference,** one should then conclude **that** its meat **does impart impurity.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״ – מִקְצָת בְּהֵמָה מְטַמְּאָה, מִקְצָת בְּהֵמָה אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה. וְאֵיזוֹ זוֹ? זוֹ טְרֵיפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*:** The verse concerning the impurity of carcasses states: **"And if some animal,** of which you may eat, **dies,** one who touches its carcass shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:39)? The word "some" teaches that **some animals impart impurity** and **some animals do not impart impurity. And what is** it **that** does not impart impurity? **That is a *tereifa* that one slaughtered.** And if even its meat does not impart impurity, the word *tereifa* is not needed to teach that its forbidden fat is pure.

The *Gemara* therefore suggests that in this other verse, the word *treifah* does not refer literally to a *treifah* animal but rather signals that the *halacha* applies only to *kosher* animals: *mi she'yesh b'mina treifah yatza zo she'ein b'mina treifah*—an animal to which the concept of *treifah* is relevant (i.e., a *kosher* species that could be either *kosher* or *treifah*) is included, while a non-*kosher* animal to which *treifah* is irrelevant (since it is forbidden regardless) is excluded. On this reading, *treifah* in that verse is not a description of a specific animal but rather a marker indicating the category of *kosher* animals. If this is so, the *Gemara* challenges Rabbi Yehudah: why should the word *treifah* in the verse about bird *tumah* be read literally as a *treifah* that was *shechted*, rather than similarly serving as a marker for *kosher* birds?

### Yehudah Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹף טָמֵא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – מִנְּבֵלָה נָפְקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **According to Rabbi Yehuda,** the *halakha* that the carcass of **a non-kosher bird** does not impart impurity **is derived from** the phrase **"a carcass"** as it appears elsewhere.

Rabbi Yehudah responds: in the verse about bird *tumah*, the word *neveilah* already performs the function of excluding non-*kosher* birds. He derives this from a *braita* in his own name:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל תְּהֵא נִבְלַת עוֹף טָמֵא מְטַמְּאָה בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה לֹא יֹאכַל״ – מִי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם בַּל תֹּאכַל נְבֵילָה; יָצָא זֶה, שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ מִשּׁוּם בַּל תֹּאכַל נְבֵילָה אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם בַּל תֹּאכַל טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One **might** have thought that **the carcass of a non-kosher bird renders** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.** But **the verse states,** concerning the impurity of carcasses of birds: **"A carcass, or a *tereifa*, he shall not eat"** (Leviticus 22:8). This type of impurity applies only to **those** birds **that are forbidden** specifically **due to** the prohibition: **You shall not eat** of **a carcass,** i.e., kosher birds that died without ritual slaughter. **This** carcass of a non-kosher animal **is excluded,** and is not impure, **as** it is **forbidden not due to** the prohibition: **You shall not eat** of **a carcass,** but **rather due to** the prohibition: **You shall not eat a non-kosher** bird, to render yourself impure with it. Consequently, the word *tereifa* in the aforementioned verse (Leviticus 17:15) teaches that a slaughtered *tereifa* imparts ritual impurity, as originally posited.

The basis of exclusion is that the prohibition of *neveilah* (*bal tochal neveilah*) applies only to an animal that is forbidden *because* it is a *neveilah*—its prohibition derives from its *neveilah* status. A non-*kosher* bird is not forbidden because it is a *neveilah*; it is forbidden because it is a non-*kosher* species (*bal tochal tamei*). Therefore, non-*kosher* birds are already excluded by the word *neveilah* itself.

Since *neveilah* already restricts the verse to *kosher* birds, the word *treifah* cannot be doing that same work redundantly. *Treifah* must therefore be adding something new—and what it adds is the ruling that a *kosher* bird that was *shechted* and found to be *treifah* does in fact cause *tumah* upon ingestion. This is Rabbi Yehudah's direct *pasuk* for his position, and it accounts for why he disagrees with the first stage of Rabbi Meir's argument, not merely the second.

The three-way dispute is thus clearly delineated. Rabbi Meir holds, on the basis of *zot torat habehemah v'ha'of*, that both *shechitat of treifah* and *melikat of treifah* avoid *neveilah* status. Rabbi Yehudah holds, on the basis of *neveilah u'treifah* read literally, that even *shechitat of treifah* creates *neveilah* status. And Rabbi Yose holds the middle position: *shechitat of treifah* is not *neveilah* (agreeing with Rabbi Meir's *kal vachomer*), but *melikat of treifah* is *neveilah* (since *dayyo* prevents extending the protection beyond *shechitah*).
