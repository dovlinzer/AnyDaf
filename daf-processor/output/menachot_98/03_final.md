# Menachot 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Healing Leaves Dispute

### Pasuk & Dispute Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָלֵהוּ לִתְרוּפָה״ – רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי, חַד אָמַר לְהַתִּיר פֶּה שֶׁלְּמַעְלָה, וְחַד אָמַר לְהַתִּיר פֶּה שֶׁלְּמַטָּה. אִיתְּמַר: חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר לְהַתִּיר פֶּה אִלְּמִים, בַּר קַפָּרָא אָמַר לְהַתִּיר פֶּה עֲקָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the fruit trees that in the future will grow on either side of a river that will emerge from the Temple, the verse states: "And by the river upon its bank, on this side and on that side, shall grow every tree for food, whose leaf shall not wither, neither shall its fruit fail. It shall bring forth new fruit every month, because its waters issue out of the Sanctuary, and its fruit shall be for food, **and its leaf for healing [*litrufa*]"** (Ezekiel 47:12). The Gemara interprets the term "*litrufa*" as a contraction of *lehatir peh*, meaning: To unlock the mouth, and there is a dispute between **Rav Ḥisda and Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi** with regard to the meaning of this term. **One said** the leaf shall serve **to unlock** the **mouth that is above,** i.e., in the person's head. The leaves will unlock the mouths of the mute and they will be capable of speech. **And one said** the leaf shall serve **to unlock** the **mouth that is below,** i.e., the womb, enabling barren women to give birth. **It was** likewise **stated** that **Ḥizkiyya says:** The leaf shall serve **to unlock** the **mouth of the mute,** whereas **bar Kappara says:** It shall serve **to unlock** the **mouth,** i.e., the womb, **of the barren** women.

The Gemara on this daf opens with what appears at first to come out of left field — a dispute between Rav Chisda and Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi that, while unconnected to the immediately preceding discussion, mirrors the structure of an earlier dispute between those same two Amoraim. The Gemara cites the pasuk from Yechezkel, *va-alehu li-trufa* — "and its leaves are for healing" — referring to the Messianic vision of a spring flowing out from beneath the *kodesh hakodashim*, with trees growing along its banks. This is one of those idyllic visions of the end of days, where the Mikdash serves as the source of water, healing, and renewal for the entire world.

### Upper vs. Lower Mouth


The dispute centers on the precise nature of the healing described in the pasuk. *chad amar le-hatir pe shel ma'ala* — one opinion holds that these leaves will open the mouths of those who are mute, restoring the power of speech. *ve-chad amar le-hatir pe shel mata* — the other holds that they will open the lower mouth, that is, they will cure the barrenness of women who are unable to conceive.

### Chizkiya & Bar Kappara


The Gemara then cites a parallel dispute that is more explicit in its formulation. Chizkiya says the leaves serve *le-hatir pe ilmim* — to open the mouths of the dumb. Bar Kappara says *le-hatir pe akarot* — to open the womb of barren women. These two pairs of opinions, while stated separately, clearly correspond to one another.

## Twelve Loaves Setup

### Three Details Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״וְלָקַחְתָּ סֹלֶת וְאָפִיתָ אֹתָהּ שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה חַלּוֹת וְשַׂמְתָּ אוֹתָם שְׁתַּיִם מַעֲרָכוֹת״ וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״שֵׁשׁ״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר אַחַת שֶׁל אַרְבַּע וְאַחַת שֶׁל שְׁמוֹנֶה, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״שֵׁשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Had** the Torah **stated: "And you shall take fine flour, and bake twelve cakes from it…And you shall set them in two arrangements," but** had it **not stated** the continuation **"six** in an arrangement," **I would have said** there may be **one** arrangement **of four** loaves **and one of eight** loaves, i.e., the arrangements do not have to be of equal size. **Therefore, it is stated: "Six** in an arrangement."

From here the Gemara transitions to the laws governing the arrangement of the *lechem hapanim* on the Shulchan. The Gemara cites the relevant pesukim: *ve-lakachta solet ve-afita otah shtem esrei chalot* — you shall take flour and bake twelve loaves, each made of two esronim of flour, which amounts to a remarkably large loaf. The subsequent pasuk then commands: *ve-samta otam *shtayim ma'arachot* *shesh hama'arechet* al hashulchan hatahor lifnei Hashem* — you shall place them in two stacks, six in a stack, on the pure table before God. The specification is tripartite: twelve loaves, two stacks, six in each stack.

### Why Three Details Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״שְׁתַּיִם מַעֲרָכוֹת שֵׁשׁ הַמַּעֲרָכֶת״, וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה״ – הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁל שֵׁשׁ שֵׁשׁ, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה״. וְאִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה חַלּוֹת״ וּ״מַעֲרָכוֹת״, וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״שְׁתַּיִם״ וְ״שֵׁשׁ״ – הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁל אַרְבַּע אַרְבַּע, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״שְׁתַּיִם״ וְ״שֵׁשׁ״. הָא עַד שֶׁלֹּא יֵאָמְרוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה מִקְרָאוֹת הַלָּלוּ לֹא לָמַדְנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And had** the Torah **stated: "Two arrangements, six in an arrangement," but** had it **not stated: "Twelve** cakes," **I would have said** that the total number of loaves may be more than twelve, and the phrase "six in an arrangement" teaches that one may bring another arrangement of six loaves in addition to the two mentioned in the verse, so that there are **three** arrangements **of six** loaves **each. Therefore, it is stated: "Twelve** cakes." **And had** the Torah **stated: Twelve cakes…And** you shall set them in **arrangements, but** had it **not stated: "Two" and "Six," I would have said** that the twelve loaves should be divided into **three** arrangements **of four** loaves **each. Therefore, it is stated: "Two** arrangements," **and: "Six** in an arrangement." **Therefore, until these three** phrases in the **verses were stated we could not learn** how to set the shewbread on the Table.

The Gemara proceeds to demonstrate why each of the three details is indispensable. Had the pasuk stated only that there were twelve loaves in two stacks — without specifying six in a stack — one might have thought the division could be four and eight, or any other uneven split. Therefore the Torah specifies *shesh hama'arechet*, six in a stack. Conversely, had the pasuk stated only two stacks of six without specifying a total of twelve, one might have concluded there were three stacks of six, yielding eighteen loaves. Therefore the Torah specifies twelve. And had the pasuk specified only twelve loaves in stacks, without stating that there were two stacks of six, one might have arranged them as three stacks of four. The Gemara concludes: *ha ad shelo yomeru gimel mikra'ot hallalu lo lamadnu* — had all three specifications not been stated, we would not have known the correct arrangement.

### Valid Arrangement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? נוֹתֵן שְׁתַּיִם מַעֲרָכוֹת שֶׁל שֵׁשׁ שֵׁשׁ, וְאִם נָתַן אַחַת שֶׁל אַרְבַּע וְאַחַת שֶׁל שְׁמוֹנֶה – לֹא יָצָא, שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁל שֶׁבַע שֶׁבַע, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אֶת הָעֶלְיוֹנָה כְּאִילּוּ אֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **How** are **these** arrangements actually set on the Table? The priest **places** on the Table **two arrangements of six** loaves **each. And if he placed one** arrangement **of four** loaves **and one** arrangement **of eight** loaves **he has not fulfilled** the obligation. If he added two loaves, so that there were **two** arrangements **of seven** loaves **each, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: One views the upper** loaf of each arrangement **as though it is not** there, and he has fulfilled the obligation.

The operative ruling is therefore: *noten shtei ma'arachot shel sheish sheish* — one places two stacks of six each. If one placed them four and eight, *lo asah* — the arrangement is invalid. This question is in some sense theoretical, since once the wooden frame is constructed to hold the loaves in position, there is really only one way to stack them properly.

### Seventh Loaf Question


The Gemara then raises a more interesting edge case: what if one placed two stacks of seven each, for a total of fourteen loaves? Within those fourteen, the required twelve are still present, arranged as two stacks of six, with one extra loaf resting on top of each stack. *rabbi omer ro'in et ha-elyonah ke-ilu einah* — Rabbi rules that the extra loaf on top is simply disregarded, as though it were not there. It does not invalidate the six loaves beneath it. Notably, the Gemara does not at this point raise the issue of *bal tosif* — the prohibition against adding to the commandments — which one might have expected to be relevant here. The classic cases of *bal tosif* involve something like adding a fifth parchment to one's tefillin or extending a festival by an extra day. Whether placing a seventh loaf on the Shulchan rises to that level is left unaddressed, with Rabbi's ruling apparently treating the extra loaf as simply extraneous to the mitzvah itself.

### Frankincense Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בָּעֵינַן ״וְנָתַתָּ עַל״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But don't we require** that the frankincense be placed upon the shewbread, as it is stated: **"And you shall place** pure frankincense **upon [*al*]** each arrangement, that it may be for the bread as a memorial part" (Leviticus 24:7)? If the priest places an additional, non-sacred loaf upon the arrangement, it interposes between the frankincense and the shewbread.

The Gemara challenges Rabbi's ruling, however, from a different angle. The pasuk states *venatata al hama'arechet levonah zachah* — you shall place frankincense on the stack. The Gemara's understanding is that the frankincense must rest directly on top of the mitzvah bread. If there is a seventh loaf sitting on top of the six, then the frankincense is resting on bread that is not part of the mitzvah — it is resting on the extraneous loaf, not on the six. This creates a significant problem for Rabbi's ruling.

### Al Means Near

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר ״וְנָתַתָּ עַל הַמַּעֲרֶכֶת לְבֹנָה זַכָּה״ – ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to Rav Hamnuna, and some say** that **Rav Hamnuna** said **to Rav Ḥisda: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he said** that the word **"*al*"** means that the frankincense is **adjacent to** the shewbread. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The verse states: **"And you shall place pure frankincense by [*al*] each arrangement"** (Leviticus 24:7). The preposition **"*al*"** in this verse means that the frankincense is **adjacent to** the shewbread.

The Gemara resolves this by invoking Rabbi's consistent interpretive position. Rav Chisda said to Rav Hamnuna — or according to an alternative tradition, Rav Hamnuna said to Rav Chisda — *rabbi le-ta'ameih*, that Rabbi's ruling is internally consistent, because he holds that the word *al* in the pasuk does not mean directly upon, but rather *besamuch* — in proximity to. According to Rabbi, *venatata al hama'arechet levonah zachah* means the frankincense is placed near the stack, not necessarily resting directly upon the top of the mitzvah bread.

### Paroches as Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא ״עַל״ מַמָּשׁ? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְסַכֹּתָ עַל הָאָרוֹן אֶת הַפָּרוֹכֶת״, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Do you say** that ***al*** means **adjacent to** the shewbread, **or** perhaps it means **nothing other than** that the frankincense was **literally upon** the shewbread? **When it says: "And you shall place the Curtain as a screen next to [*al*] the Ark"** (Exodus 40:3), it is evident that "*al*" does not mean upon the Ark, as the Curtain separating the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies was hung in front of the Ark and not placed on top of it. Therefore, **you must say** that the word ***al*** can also mean **adjacent to.**

The Gemara then asks: how do we know that *al* can mean near rather than directly on? Perhaps *al mamash* — it means literally on top. The Gemara answers from the pasuk regarding the Paroches: *ve-sachos al ha-aron et ha-paroches* — the curtain is to be spread *al*, upon, the Aron. Yet we know that the Paroches was not draped over the Aron; it stood upright as a curtain dividing the Heichal from the *kodesh hakodashim*. Nevertheless the pasuk uses the word *al*. We thus have proof that *al* can indicate proximity rather than direct placement, and Rabbi's position is fully coherent.

## Vessel Orientation & Ark

### All Vessels East-West

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַכֵּלִים שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ אׇרְכָּן לְאׇרְכּוֹ שֶׁל בַּיִת, חוּץ מֵאָרוֹן שֶׁאׇרְכּוֹ לְרׇחְבּוֹ שֶׁל בַּיִת, וְכָךְ הָיָה מוּנָּח, וְכָךְ הָיוּ בַּדָּיו מוּנָּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all the vessels that** were **in the Temple, their length was** placed **along the length of** the **Temple, except for** the **Ark, whose length was** placed **along the width of** the **Temple,** from north to south. **And in this manner** the Ark **was placed, and in that manner its staves were placed.**

The Gemara now turns to the question of how the vessels of the Mikdash were oriented within the space. The Mishkan — and by extension the Mikdash — was oriented along an east-west axis. The general rule established by the Gemara is: *kol ha-*keilim she-ba-mikdash* orkan l'orkan shel bayis* — all the vessels in the Mikdash were oriented in the same direction as the building itself, that is, east-west.

### Ark North-South


There is one prominent exception: *chutz mei-aron* — except for the Aron, *she-orko l'rochbo shel bayis* — whose length was oriented along the width of the building, that is, north-south. The Aron thus ran north-south within the *kodesh hakodashim*. And correspondingly, *kach hayu badav munachim* — and that is how its carrying poles were placed.

### Poles Stretched Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַדָּיו מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וַיַּאֲרִכוּ הַבַּדִּים״ – יָכוֹל לֹא הָיוּ נוֹגְעִין בַּפָּרוֹכֶת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וַיֵּרָאוּ״. אִי ״וַיֵּרָאוּ״ – יָכוֹל יְהוּ מְקָרְעִין בַּפָּרוֹכֶת וְיוֹצְאִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא יֵרָאוּ הַחוּצָה״. הָא כֵּיצַד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **its staves, from where do we** derive that they were placed along the length of the Temple, from east to west? This is derived **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And the staves were so long** that the ends of the staves were seen from the holy place before the Sanctuary, but they could not be seen outside" (I Kings 8:8). One **might** have thought that the staves **did not touch the Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies, and did not protrude at all. Therefore, **the verse states:** "The ends of the staves **were seen." If** the verse had stated only: "The ends of the staves **were seen,"** one **might** have thought that the staves **tear through the Curtain and emerge** into the Sanctuary. Therefore, **the verse states: "They could not be seen outside." How** can **these** texts be reconciled?

The Gemara asks: how do we know that the poles ran east-west? *detanya* — as we have a Braita: *vayarikhu habadim* — the poles stretched outward. One might think that they simply extended without touching the Paroches, but the Gemara cites the pasuk in Melachim Aleph 8:8: *vayera'u rashei habadim min hakodesh al pnei hadvir* — the tips of the poles were visible from the Kodesh, looking toward the Dvir, i.e., the *kodesh hakodashim*. Yet another phrase indicates they were not visible from outside. This seems contradictory.

### Poles Bulge Paroches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דּוֹחֲקִין וּבוֹלְטִין בַּפָּרוֹכֶת, וְדוֹמִין כְּמִין שְׁנֵי דַּדֵּי אִשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״צְרוֹר הַמֹּר דּוֹדִי לִי בֵּין שָׁדַי יָלִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: The staves of the Ark **pressed against the Curtain and bulged** outward. **And they appeared like two breasts of a woman** that are discernible through her clothes, **as it is stated: "My beloved is to me like a bundle of myrrh that lies between my breasts"** (Song of Songs 1:13). Since the staves pressed against the Curtain, they were evidently placed from east to west.

The resolution is: *dochakin uvotin baparochet* — they pressed against and bulged through the Paroches. The poles did not pierce or tear the curtain, but they pushed against it from behind, creating visible protrusions. From inside the Heichal, one could see where the poles were pressing against the curtain, yet they were not exposed to view from outside. The Gemara invokes the imagery of *shir hashirim*: *domim kishnei dadei isha* — they resembled two breasts. *shenem'ar tzror hamor dodi li bein shadai yalin* — "my beloved is to me like a bundle of myrrh that lies between my breasts." On Yom Kippur, the Kohen Gadol would enter the *kodesh hakodashim* with the incense, which is the *mor*, and the image of the incense resting between the twin protrusions of the poles pressing through the Paroches gives rise to this striking use of the verse from *shir hashirim*.

### Pole Placement Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּבַדָּיו לְפוּתְיָא דְּאָרוֹן הֲווֹ יָתְבִי? דִּילְמָא לְאׇרְכּוֹ דְּאָרוֹן הֲווֹ יָתְבִי! אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: תְּרֵי גַּבְרֵי בְּאַמְּתָא וּפַלְגָא לָא מִסְתַּגִּי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** the Ark's **staves were set along the width of the Ark? Perhaps they were set along the Ark's length,** in which case the Ark was placed along the length of the Temple, not along its width. **Rav Yehuda said** that this cannot be the case, as the Ark was carried by two men on each side, who stood between the two staves. If the staves were placed along its length, the two men carrying it on each side would be positioned along the width of the Ark, which was one and a half cubits. This is impossible, as **two men** standing next to each other **in** the space of only **one and a half cubits cannot walk.**

The Gemara then asks: from where do we know that the poles were on the long sides of the Aron rather than the short sides? The dimensions of the Aron were two and a half amot long by one and a half amot wide. *amar rav yehuda: trei gavrei b'amta u'palga lo mistagei* — two people cannot fit side by side in a space of only one and a half amot. The Gemara's assumption is that when the Aron was carried, two people stood at each end of the poles, walking within the space between the poles. If the poles were on the short side, only one and a half amot would be available for the carriers to stand abreast — not enough room. But on the long side, two and a half amot are available, which is sufficient for two carriers to stand side by side. Therefore the poles must have been placed on the long sides, oriented east-west.

### Four Carriers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דִּבְאַרְבְּעָה הֲווֹ דָּרוּ לְהוּ? דִּכְתִיב ״וְנָסְעוּ הַקְּהָתִים״ – תְּרֵי, ״נֹשְׂאֵי הַמִּקְדָּשׁ״ – נָמֵי תְּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** the Ark **was carried by four** men? This is derived from a verse, **as it is written** with regard to the carrying of the Ark by the descendants of Kohath: "And the Kohathites would travel, the bearers of the Holy Ark, that the Tabernacle might be set up before their coming" (Numbers 10:21). The verse uses the plural form twice. The first phrase: **"And the Kohathites would travel,"** indicates that **two** people bear the Ark, and the second phrase: **"The bearers of the Holy Ark," also** indicates that **two** people bear the Ark; the total is therefore four bearers.

Finally, the Gemara asks: how do we know that there were four carriers total — two on each side? *de'ketiv: u'nas'u ha'kehatim nosei ha'mikdash* — it is written that the Kehatim, the *nosei*, the carriers of the Mikdash — referring to the Aron as the holiest object — carried it. *nosei* is plural, implying at least two. *nosei ha'mikdash* — another plural — implies another two, yielding four carriers in total.

## Tables & Menorot

### Solomon's Ten Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲשָׂרָה שׁוּלְחָנוֹת עָשָׂה שְׁלֹמֹה הַמֶּלֶךְ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַשׂ שׁוּלְחָנוֹת עֲשָׂרָה וַיַּנַּח בַּהֵיכָל חֲמִשָּׁה מִיָּמִין וַחֲמִשָּׁה מִשְּׂמֹאול״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: King Solomon built ten** additional **tables,** modeled after the one that Moses crafted, **as it is stated** in the description of the Temple constructed by Solomon: **"He made also ten tables, and placed them in the Sanctuary, five on the right side, and five on the left"** (II Chronicles 4:8).

The Gemara now discusses an important historical development in the layout of the Mikdash. *asarah shulchanot asah shlomo haMelech* — King *shlomo made* ten tables when he built the *beit haMikdash*. The pasuk in Melachim records this explicitly. These tables were placed in the Heichal, five on the right and five on the left. One might think this means five on the right side of the building and five on the left — but that cannot be correct, because the Torah mandates that the Shulchan be placed on the north side. The phrase "right and left" therefore means: the original table of Moshe stood in the center, with five of Shlomo's tables to its right and five to its left, forming two rows flanking the original.

### Solomon's Ten Menorot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֶשֶׂר מְנוֹרוֹת עָשָׂה שְׁלֹמֹה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַשׂ אֶת הַמְּנֹרוֹת הַזָּהָב עֶשֶׂר כַּמִּשְׁפָּט וַיִּתֵּן בַּהֵיכָל חֲמִשָּׁה מִיָּמִין וַחֲמִשָּׁה מִשְּׂמֹאול״.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the Sages taught:** King **Solomon built ten** additional **candelabra,** modeled after the one that Moses crafted, **as it is stated: "And he made the ten candelabra of gold according to the ordinance concerning them; and he set them in the Sanctuary, five on the right, and five on the left"** (II Chronicles 4:7).

The same structure applies to the Menorah. *esser menorot asah shlomo* — Shlomo also made ten Menorot, as stated explicitly in the pasuk. He placed them in the Heichal, five on the right and five on the left. Again, this cannot mean five on the south side and five on the north side, because the Torah requires *ve-et ha-menorah nochach ha-shulchan* — the Menorah must be opposite the Shulchan, on the south side. The arrangement is therefore analogous: Moshe's Menorah in the center, five of Shlomo's to the right and five to the left.

### Location Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: מֵחֲצִי בַּיִת וְלִפְנִים הָיוּ מוּנָּחִין, וְתָנֵי חֲדָא: מִשְּׁלִישׁ הַבַּיִת וְלִפְנִים הָיוּ מוּנָּחִין. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, מָר קָא חָשֵׁיב בֵּית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים בַּהֲדֵי הֵיכָל, מָר לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב בֵּית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים בַּהֲדֵי הֵיכָל.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in **one** *baraita* that these tables and candelabra **were set inward of** the first **half of** the length of the structure of the **Temple,** as measured from east to west. **And it is taught** in **one** *baraita* that **they were set inward of** the first **third of** the length of the structure of the **Temple.** The Gemara explains that this is **not difficult,** as the two *baraitot* do not disagree with regard to the location of the tables and candelabra. In the second *baraita* the **Sage is reckoning** the length of **the Hall of the Holy of Holies,** which was twenty cubits, **together with** the length of the **Sanctuary,** which was forty cubits. Accordingly, the total length of the structure of the Temple was sixty cubits, and the tables and candelabra were set inward of the first third of its length, i.e., twenty cubits from the entrance. Conversely, in the first *baraita* the **Sage is not reckoning** the length of **the Hall of the Holy of Holies together with** the length of the **Sanctuary.** Since he is referring to the forty cubits of the Sanctuary itself, inward of the first half of the structure of the Temple's length means twenty cubits from the entrance.

There is a Tannaitic dispute regarding where precisely in the Heichal these tables and Menorot were placed. One Tanna says *michatzi ha-bayit u'lifnim* — they were positioned within the inner half of the building. Another says *meshlish ha-bayit velifnim* — within the inner third. The Gemara explains that this is not actually a contradiction. The Heichal was forty amot long and the *kodesh hakodashim* was twenty amot. The Tanna who says "half" is calculating half of the entire sixty-amot space — half of sixty is thirty, which puts them within the back thirty amot. The Tanna who says "third" is speaking only of the Heichal's own forty amot — a third of sixty is also twenty — placing them within the back twenty amot of the Heichal. Both opinions arrive at the same physical location.

### Rabbi East-West

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב הָיוּ מוּנָּחִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: צָפוֹן וְדָרוֹם. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? גָּמַר מִמְּנוֹרָה – מָה מְנוֹרָה מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב, אַף הָנֵי נָמֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: All the tables built by Solomon **were placed** from **east** to **west,** i.e., their length was along the length of the Sanctuary, as was the Table in the Tabernacle; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** They were placed from **north to south,** along the width of the Sanctuary. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **He derives** this *halakha* **from** a comparison of the tables to the **Candelabrum: Just as** the **Candelabrum** was placed from **east** to **west, so too, these** tables were positioned from **east** to **west.**

The question of orientation now becomes central. *mizrach u'maarav hayu munachim, divrei rabbi* — they were oriented east-west, in the opinion of Rabbi. Rabbi derives the orientation of the Shulchan from the Menorah: just as the Menorah was oriented east-west, so too were the tables. And how do we know the Menorah was east-west? *midechtiv: bener ma'aravi ya'aroch oto aharon lifnei hashem* — because the pasuk specifies a western lamp, one of the seven lamps designated as the primary one. If the Menorah were oriented north-south, all lamps would be equally close to the west, equally close to the *kodesh hakodashim*. The fact that there is specifically a *western* lamp — one that is more westerly than the others — demonstrates that the Menorah was aligned east-west, with one lamp closer to the *kodesh hakodashim* than the rest. Rashi understands the western lamp to be the westernmost one; Tosafot understands it to be the second from the east, i.e., the first one encountered as you move westward. In any case, the point is established: the Menorah ran east-west, and so did the Shulchan.

### R. Elazar North-South

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַאי טַעְמָא? גָּמַר מֵאָרוֹן: מָה אָרוֹן צָפוֹן וְדָרוֹם, אַף הָנֵי נָמֵי צָפוֹן וְדָרוֹם. וְרַבִּי נָמֵי לִיגְמַר מֵאָרוֹן? דָּנִין חוּץ מִחוּץ, וְאֵין דָּנִין חוּץ מִבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, what is the reason** for his opinion? The Gemara replies: **He derives** this *halakha* **from** a comparison of the tables to the **Ark: Just as** the **Ark** was placed from **north** to **south, so too, these** tables were positioned from **north** to **south.** The Gemara challenges: **But** as for **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **let him also derive from** the **Ark** that the tables were positioned from north to south. The Gemara explains that according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **one derives** the position of the tables, which are located in the **outer** area of the Sanctuary, **from** the Candelabrum, which is also located in the **outer** area of the Sanctuary. **And one does not derive** the position of the tables, which are located in the **outer** area of the Sanctuary, **from** the Ark, which is located in the **inner** area of the Sanctuary, the Holy of Holies.

Rabbi Elazar ben Shimon disagrees. *tzafon vedarom* — he holds both the Menorah and the tables were oriented north-south. He derives his position from the Aron: just as the Aron was oriented north-south, so too were the vessels of the Heichal. The Gemara asks: why doesn't Rabbi also learn from the Aron? The answer is that we apply the principle *danin chutz michutz ve'ein danin chutz mibifnim* — we derive the law of an item in the outer sanctum from another item in the outer sanctum, not from something in the inner sanctum. The Aron was in the *kodesh hakodashim*; the Menorah and Shulchan were in the Heichal. Rabbi therefore derives one from the other, keeping the comparison within the same space. Conversely, the Gemara asks why Rabbi Elazar ben Shimon does not learn from the Shulchan, which might appear to have been east-west. His reply is that the Menorah itself was also north-south — he does not concede the east-west orientation of either vessel.

### Wicks Lean Center

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכְתִיב ״יַעֲרֹךְ אֹתוֹ אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו״, דְּמַצְדֵּד לְהוּ אַצְדּוֹדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written** with regard to the western lamp of the Candelabrum: **"Aaron and his sons shall set it in order,** to burn from evening to morning before the Lord" (Exodus 27:21), indicating that this lamp must be in greater proximity to the Holy of Holies, which is possible only if the Candelabrum is positioned from east to west? The Gemara replies that according to Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, the term "before the Lord" means that the wick of the middle lamp is turned toward the Holy of Holies, as the priest who kindles the lamps **turns** the wicks of the other lamps slightly **to the side,** whereas the wick of the middle lamp is turned directly toward the Holy of Holies.

This raises an obvious question: if the Menorah ran north-south, how can the pasuk speak of a specifically *western* lamp? Rabbi Elazar ben Shimon answers: *mitzadadi lehu itzdudei* — the wicks were angled. Even if the body of the Menorah ran north-south, the wicks could be tilted to face toward the center, and thus the central lamp's wick could be tilted specifically toward the west, creating a functionally westernmost lamp even within a north-south orientation.

### Middle Lamp Superior

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״אֶל מוּל פְּנֵי הַמְּנוֹרָה יָאִירוּ שִׁבְעַת הַנֵּרוֹת״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיוּ מְצַדְּדִין פְּנֵיהֶם כְּלַפֵּי נֵר אֶמְצָעִי. אָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן: מִכָּאן שֶׁאֶמְצָעִי מְשׁוּבָּח.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "When you light the lamps, **the seven lamps shall give light toward the front of the Candelabrum"** (Numbers 8:2). This **teaches that** the priests **would turn the front** of each lamp **toward** the **middle lamp,** but the middle lamp was turned toward the Holy of Holies. **Rabbi Natan says:** One can infer **from here that** the **middle** position is **preeminent.**

A Braita is cited in support: *el mol pnei ha-menorah yairu shivat ha-nerot* — the seven lamps shone toward the face of the Menorah. *melamed shehayu metzadedim pneihem klapei ner emtzai* — this teaches that all the lamps had their faces, their wicks, angled toward the central lamp. *amar rabbi natan: mikan she'emtzai meshubach* — from here Rabbi Natan derives that the central lamp is the most distinguished of the seven. This accords with the structure of the Menorah, where the central shaft is the primary column from which the six branches extend.

## East-West Spacing Problem

### N-S Fits Easily

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר צָפוֹן וְדָרוֹם שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Granted, according to the one who said** the tables were positioned along the width of the Sanctuary, from **north** to **south,** it works out **well.**

Having established the two opinions, the Gemara now examines whether the physical dimensions of the Heichal actually permit each arrangement. *bishlama l'man d'amar tzafon vedarom shapir* — according to Rabbi Elazar ben Shimon's north-south orientation, there is no difficulty. The ten tables of Shlomo plus Moshe's table total eleven, arranged in two rows of five with Moshe's table in the center. The depth of the space available along the north-south axis easily accommodates five tables oriented with their width running north-south, since there is ample room in that dimension.

### E-W Space Calc

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב, מִכְּדֵי שׁוּלְחָן כַּמָּה מָשׁוּךְ מִן הַכּוֹתֶל? שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה, וְאַמָּה דִּידֵיהּ, וּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי. וְאַמָּה, וּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי, וְאַמָּה דִּידֵיהּ – אִישְׁתְּכַח דְּקָאָכֵיל שׁוּלְחָן פַּלְגָא דְּאַמְּתָא בְּדָרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who said** the tables were positioned along the length of the Sanctuary, from **east** to **west,** in which case the priests arranging and removing the shewbread would stand at the north and south of the Table, this is difficult. **Now,** consider how much space was taken up by the tables: **How far** was the **Table removed from the** northern **wall** of the Sanctuary? It was **two and a half cubits** away from the wall, as this is the space necessary for the two priests to pass. **And** to this one must add one **cubit** for the width of the northern row of tables **itself, and** another **two and a half cubits that** were **between** the northern row of five tables and Moses' Table, as space for two priests to pass is required here as well. **And** then one must add one **cubit** for the width of the Table of Moses, **and two and a half cubits that** were **between** Moses' Table and the southern row of tables, **and** another **cubit** for the width of the southern row **itself.** This totals ten and a half cubits, according to which **it is found that** the **Table occupies half a cubit in the south** of the Sanctuary, although the Table is supposed to be entirely in the north of the Sanctuary.

But a serious problem arises for Rabbi's view that the tables ran east-west. Here the challenge is not the length of the rows but the width — how much space is consumed across the north-south axis. Working through the calculation: the table had to be set two and a half amot from the wall, because two Kohanim needed to walk side by side alongside it during the exchange of the *lechem hapanim*, just as two carriers were needed for the Aron. The table itself was one amah wide. Then two and a half amot of space between that row and Moshe's central table. Another amah for Moshe's table. Another two and a half *amot between* Moshe's table and the second row. And another amah for the second row. Adding these together: 2.5 + 1 + 2.5 + 1 + 2.5 + 1 = 10.5 amot. But the available north-south space on the north side of the Heichal was only ten amot, meaning the arrangement would spill half an amah into the south side, where the Menorah was required to stand. This is a genuine spatial impossibility under Rabbi's east-west arrangement.

### Moshe Table Front

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ חַד דָּרָא הוּא?! תְּרֵי דָּרֵי נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, do you maintain** that **there was** only **one row** of tables? It was not so; **they were** set in **two rows,** with the Table of Moses between the rows. Therefore, there was room for all the tables and it was also possible for the High Priest to pass by them.

The Gemara resolves this: *mi savrat chad dara havo* — who says there was a single row arrangement with Moshe's table in the middle between two parallel rows? *trei darei havo* — there were two rows, yes, but Moshe's table was not positioned between them in the center of the north-south space. Rather, it was positioned *lifnim*, up toward the front, closer to the entrance of the Heichal, offset from the two rows so that it did not occupy the space between them. With Moshe's table moved to the front, the two rows of five could be placed close enough to each other that the total north-south width fit within the available ten amot: 2.5 + 1 + 2.5 + 1 + 2.5 = 9.5 amot for the two rows and their clearances, which fits within ten.

### Teacher-Student

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַלְמִיד הַיּוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** All the tables were therefore situated in the north of the Sanctuary. Since the Temple was built on terrain that sloped downward from west to east, this means **that** the Table of Moses **was raised** above the tables of Solomon. The Table of Moses **was placed** to the west **and** the tables of Solomon **were lowered slightly** toward the east. Solomon's tables therefore appeared in relation to Moses' Table **as a student who sits** on a lower level **before his teacher.**

The positioning of Moshe's table toward the front, with the two rows of tables extending behind it, is described evocatively: *k'talmid hayoshev lifnei rabo* — like a student sitting before his teacher. Moshe's table is the teacher; the two rows of tables extending behind it are the students arranged at his feet. The spatial and hierarchical arrangement reinforce one another.

## Functional Use

### One Table Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲשָׂרָה שׁוּלְחָנוֹת עָשָׂה שְׁלֹמֹה, וְלֹא הָיוּ מְסַדְּרִין אֶלָּא עַל שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶת הַשֻּׁלְחָן אֲשֶׁר עָלָיו לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים זָהָב״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Solomon built ten tables** that were situated in the Sanctuary, in addition to Moses' Table, **but** the priests **would arrange** the shewbread **only upon** the Table **of Moses.** This is derived from a verse, **as it is stated:** "And Solomon made all the vessels that were in the House of the Lord, the golden altar, **and the Table upon which the shewbread was, of gold"** (I Kings 7:48), indicating that the shewbread was placed on only one Table.

The Gemara now turns to the question of whether Shlomo's ten tables were actually used or were merely ornamental. The first Tanna holds: *v'lo hayu mesadrin ela al shel Moshe* — the *lechem hapanim* was arranged only on Moshe's table, not on any of the ten tables Shlomo constructed. The proof is from Melachim, which refers in the singular to *ha-shulchan asher alav lechem hapanim* — the table upon which was the *lechem hapanim*. The singular implies that only one table bore the bread. Similarly, with respect to the Menorot: *v'lo hayu madlikin ela beshel Moshe* — only Moshe's Menorah was actually lit. Again, the proof is from Melachim, which refers in the singular to *menoras hazahav* being lit in the evening.

### All Tables Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶשֶׂר מְנוֹרוֹת עָשָׂה שְׁלֹמֹה, וְלֹא הָיוּ מַדְלִיקִין אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁל מֹשֶׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת מְנוֹרַת הַזָּהָב וְנֵרֹתֶיהָ לְבָעֵר בָּעֶרֶב״. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ אוֹמֵר: עַל כּוּלָּם הָיוּ מְסַדְּרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָנוֹת וַעֲלֵיהֶם לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים״, וּבְכוּלָּן הָיוּ מַדְלִיקִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת הַמְּנֹרוֹת וְנֵרֹתֵיהֶם לְבַעֲרָם כַּמִּשְׁפָּט לִפְנֵי הַדְּבִיר זָהָב סָגוּר״.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Solomon built ten candelabra, but** the priests **would kindle** the lamps **only on** the Candelabrum **of Moses.** This is derived from a verse, **as it is stated:** "And they burn unto the Lord every morning and every evening burnt offerings and sweet incense; the shewbread also they arrange in order upon the pure Table, and **the Candelabrum of gold with its lamps, to burn** every **evening"** (II Chronicles 13:11). The singular form indicates that the lamps were kindled on only one Candelabrum. **Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua says: They would arrange** the shewbread **upon all** the tables, **as it is stated:** "And Solomon made all the vessels that were in the House of God, the golden altar as well, and **the tables upon which was the shewbread"** (II Chronicles 4:19). The plural form indicates that the shewbread was arranged on all the tables. **And they would kindle** the lamps **on all** the candelabra, **as it is stated** in the subsequent verse: **"And the candelabra with their lamps, which they should burn according to the ordinance before the Sanctuary, of pure gold"** (II Chronicles 4:20).

Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua takes the contrary position: *al kulam hayu mesadrin* — bread was arranged on all of them, and *uv'kulan hayu madlikin* — all the Menorot were lit. His proofs come from *divrei hayamim*, which uses the plural forms: *es hashulchanos va'aleihem lechem hapanim* — the tables with the *lechem hapanim* on them — and *es hamenoros v'neroseihem leva'aram* — the Menorot and their lamps that were kindled. The Gemara notes that this is one of the well-known instances where Melachim and *divrei hayamim* present differing versions of the same events.

### Three Functional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיוּ מְסַדְּרִין אֶלָּא עַל שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה, אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְאֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָנוֹת אֲשֶׁר עֲלֵיהֶם לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים זָהָב״? אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוּלְחָנוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ בְּאוּלָם מִבִּפְנִים לְפֶתַח הַבַּיִת, אֶחָד שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְאֶחָד שֶׁל זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: They would arrange** the shewbread **only upon** the Table **of Moses. Rather, how do I realize** the meaning of the verse: **"And the tables upon which was the shewbread…**of pure **gold"** (II Chronicles 4:19–20)? **These are** the **three tables that were in the Temple,** of which there were **two that were** situated **in the Entrance Hall** to the Sanctuary, **on the inside** near **to the entrance to the Temple,** i.e., near the entrance to the Sanctuary. **One** of these tables was made **of silver, and** the other **one** was made **of gold.**

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda offers a third position. He agrees with the first Tanna that the *lechem hapanim* was placed only on Moshe's table, but he offers a different explanation of the plural references in *divrei hayamim*. *elu sheloshah shulchanos shehayu ba-mikdash* — the plural refers to three functionally active tables that existed in the Mikdash. *shenayim shehayu ba-ulam mifnim lepesach ha-bayis* — two of these were located in the Ulam, the antechamber, just inside the entrance to the Heichal, where the outer altar stood. These two tables served a specific ceremonial function during the weekly exchange of the *lechem hapanim*.

### Silver & Gold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל שֶׁל כֶּסֶף נוֹתֵן לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בִּכְנִיסָתוֹ, וְעַל שֶׁל זָהָב בִּיצִיאָתוֹ, שֶׁמַּעֲלִין בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין. אֶחָד שֶׁל זָהָב בִּפְנִים, שֶׁעָלָיו לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים תָּמִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **On** the table **of silver** the priest **places the** new **shewbread** that has been baked, **before its entrance** into the Sanctuary, so that the loaves may cool a little from the heat of the oven. **And** when the old shewbread is removed from the shewbread Table it is placed **on** the table **of gold upon its exit** from the Sanctuary, until the frankincense is burned on the altar. The reason the shewbread is placed on a silver table before it is brought into the Sanctuary and on a gold one when it is removed is **that one elevates** to a higher level **in** matters of **sanctity and one does not downgrade.** Since it is set on the gold shewbread Table all week, it cannot be downgraded to a silver table upon its removal. The Gemara concludes: The third of the three tables is the **one** Table **of gold inside** the Sanctuary **upon which the shewbread** is **always** found.

One of the two Ulam tables was silver and one was gold. *al shel kesef nosein *lechem hapanim* bichnisaso* — on the silver table, the fresh bread was placed upon entering, that is, when the new loaves were brought in to replace the old ones. *v'al shel zahav biyitziaso* — and on the gold table, the removed bread was placed upon exiting. The procedure was thus: fresh bread was set on the silver table in the Ulam; the old bread was removed from Moshe's table inside the Heichal and placed on the gold table in the Ulam; then the fresh bread was brought in and placed on Moshe's table. This accounts for the plural "tables" in *divrei hayamim* — it refers not to Shlomo's ten ceremonial tables but to these two functional exchange tables together with the primary golden table inside the Heichal.

## Status Elevation

### Ascend Not Descend


The choice to place the fresh bread on the silver table rather than the gold, and the old bread on the gold rather than the silver, is governed by the principle of *ma'alin bakodesh v'lo moridin* — in matters of sanctity, we ascend rather than descend. Gold is more precious than silver. Fresh bread that is about to enter the sacred space and be placed on the golden table inside begins its journey on the less prestigious silver table, ascending from silver to gold. The bread that has already rested on the golden interior table and is being removed maintains its elevated status by being placed on the gold table, not demoted to silver. The principle operates in two directions: the fresh bread ascends, and the removed bread is not degraded.

This is a striking application of the principle. There are two dimensions to *ma'alin bakodesh v'lo moridin*: one concerns the reuse of sacred objects — a vessel that has served a holy purpose may only be used for a purpose of equal or greater sanctity. The other concerns how one treats such objects — even when they are being retired from active service, they must be treated with the same dignity they had when in use.

### Erecting Mishkan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דְּאֵין מוֹרִידִין? אָמַר רַבִּי: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיָּקֶם מֹשֶׁה אֶת הַמִּשְׁכָּן וַיִּתֵּן אֶת אֲדָנָיו וַיָּשֶׂם אֶת קְרָשָׁיו וַיִּתֵּן אֶת בְּרִיחָיו וַיָּקֶם אֶת עַמּוּדָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that one does not downgrade** in matters of sanctity? **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said:** This is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And Moses erected the Tabernacle, and he laid its sockets, and set up its boards, and put in its bars, and erected its pillars"** (Exodus 40:18). This teaches that once Moses, who was at a greater level of sanctity than the rest of the people, began the work of erecting the Tabernacle, he alone completed it.

The Gemara then asks: *u'minalan d'ma'alin bakodesh v'lo moridin* — from where do we derive this principle? One proposed source is the pasuk describing Moshe's erection of the Mishkan: *vayakem Moshe et ha-mishkan vayiten et adanav vayasem et krashav vayiten et brichav vayakem et amudav* — Moshe erected the Mishkan, placed its sockets, its planks, its bars, and its pillars. The argument, apparently, has something to do with the use of the more elevated word *vayakem* appearing at particular points in the verse, though the connection is admittedly difficult to understand clearly and the explanations offered are not fully satisfying.

### Fire Pans Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּמַעֲלִין? אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״אֵת מַחְתּוֹת הַחַטָּאִים הָאֵלֶּה בְּנַפְשֹׁתָם וְעָשׂוּ אֹתָם רִקֻּעֵי פַחִים צִפּוּי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ כִּי הִקְרִיבֻם לִפְנֵי ה׳ וַיִּקְדָּשׁוּ וְיִהְיוּ לְאוֹת לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – בַּתְּחִילָּה תַּשְׁמִישֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ, וְעַכְשָׁיו גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that one elevates** to a higher level in matters of sanctity? **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** This is derived from a verse, **as the verse states** with regard to the coal pans of the men of Korah's assembly, in which they burned incense before they were consumed by a fire: **"The coal pans of these men who have sinned at the cost of their lives, and let them be made beaten plates for a covering of the altar, for they have become sacred because they were brought before the Lord, that they may be a sign to the children of Israel"** (Numbers 17:3). **Initially** the coal pans had the status of **articles** used in the service **of** the **altar,** as they contained the incense, **and now** that they have been made into a covering for the altar their status has been elevated to that of **the altar itself.**

A far more compelling proof is offered by Rabbi Yochanan bar Yaakov. The pasuk says: *et machtot hachata'im ha-eleh b'nafshotam* — referring to the fire pans used by the 250 men who joined Korach's rebellion. Those men brought their fire pans into the Mishkan as commanded, and were destroyed. God then commanded Moshe to collect those pans and hammer them into sheets to overlay the altar: *v'asu otam riku'ei pachim tzipui lamizbeach ki hikrivum *lifnei haShem* vayikdashu* — they had been brought before God and thereby become sanctified. *b'tchilat tashmishei mizbeach* — initially these pans were accessories used in service of the altar. *v'achshav gufo shel mizbeach* — now they were elevated to become part of the altar itself, forming its outer overlay. This is *ma'alin bakodesh* made concrete: objects that had served as vessels were elevated to become the body of the sacred structure itself. The application of this principle here is powerful.

### Broken Tablets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר שִׁבַּרְתָּ וְשַׂמְתָּם בָּאָרוֹן״ – תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַלּוּחוֹת וְשִׁבְרֵי לוּחוֹת מוּנָּחִין בָּאָרוֹן. מִכָּאן לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁשָּׁכַח תַּלְמוּדוֹ מֵחֲמַת אוֹנְסוֹ, שֶׁאֵין נוֹהֲגִין בּוֹ מִנְהַג בִּזָּיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned the principle that one does not downgrade in matters of sanctity, the Gemara cites a related issue. The verse states: "At that time the Lord said to me: Hew for yourself two tablets of stone like the first…And I will write on the tablets the words that were on the first tablets, **which you broke, and you shall put them in the Ark"** (Deuteronomy 10:1–2). **Rav Yosef teaches** a *baraita*: This verse **teaches that** both **the tablets** of the Covenant **and** the **pieces of** the broken **tablets are placed in the Ark.** One should learn **from here that with regard to a Torah scholar who has forgotten his** Torah **knowledge due to** circumstances **beyond his control,** e.g., illness, **one may not behave toward him** in **a degrading manner.** Although the first tablets were broken it is prohibited to treat them with disrespect, due to their sanctity. A Torah scholar who forgot the Torah knowledge he once possessed is likened to these broken tablets.

Another proof is drawn from the Luchos. *asher shibarta v'samtem ba-aron* — the broken tablets that Moshe had shattered were placed in the Ark alongside the second, intact tablets. *tani rav yosef: melamed shehaluchot v'shivrei luchot munachim ba-aron* — it teaches that both the complete second Luchos and the broken fragments of the first Luchos were kept in the Ark. This too reflects the principle: even in their broken state, the first Luchos retained their sanctity and were not discarded but rather preserved in the holiest vessel in the Mishkan, alongside the whole tablets.

### Talmid Chacham


The Gemara concludes with an application of this principle to human beings. *mikan l'talmid chacham sheshachach talmudo machmat onso she'ein nohagim bo minhag bizayon* — from here we learn that a talmid chacham who has forgotten his learning not through negligence but through circumstances beyond his control — declining health, advancing age — must not be treated with disdain. He retains his stature and must be accorded the respect due to a scholar. The broken Luchos in the Ark serve as the paradigm: even sacred things that have lost their original form or function are not to be degraded. They ascend; they do not descend.
