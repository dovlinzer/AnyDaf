# Bava Metzia 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Money & Meshicha

### Intro & Distinction

The sugya opens at the bottom of 47b, approximately five lines from the end, with the question: *ella l'Rabbi Yochanan mai ikka bein Rabbi Shimon l'Rabbanan* — according to Rabbi Yochanan, what distinguishes Rabbi Shimon from the Rabbanan? To appreciate the force of this question, one must recall the background *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning *ma'ot konot* — whether money effects a *kinyan* at the *de'oraita* level. Rabbi Yochanan holds *devar Torah ma'ot konot*: the transfer of money is biblically sufficient to complete an acquisition of movable property. Reish Lakish disagrees on both counts: not only does money fail to effect a *kinyan* at the biblical level, but *meshicha* — the physical pulling or taking control of the object being purchased — does work at the biblical level. Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* asks: if Rabbi Yochanan holds that money operates as a full biblical *kinyan*, what exactly was the *machloket* between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbanan in our *Mishnah*? Rabbi Shimon had stated that whoever holds the money has the stronger claim when one party seeks to retract. But if everyone agrees money works, where is the disagreement?

### Rav Chisda's View

The *Gemara* answers: *ikka beina'aihu d'Rav Chisda* — the distinguishing factor is the position of Rav Chisda. Rav Chisda had ruled: *kederech shetiknu meshicha bemochrin kach tiknu meshicha belakuchot* — just as the Rabbanan instituted *meshicha* for sellers (i.e., the seller may do *meshicha* on the money paid to finalize the sale), so too did they institute *meshicha* for buyers (i.e., the purchaser may do *meshicha* on the merchandise to finalize the acquisition). According to the Rabbanan, then, both parties possess the power of *meshicha* — either the seller taking control of the money or the buyer taking control of the goods. Rabbi Shimon, however, does not hold *k'Rav Chisda*: he maintains that it goes only by whoever holds the money, rejecting the parallel *meshicha* mechanism for the purchased object. *Rabbanan it lehu d'Rav Chisda* — the Rabbanan accept Rav Chisda's extension; Rabbi Shimon does not.

### Mi Shepara Curse

The *Mishnah* then introduces the curse invoked against a party who violates the terms of a completed agreement: *mi shepara midor hamabul hu atid lehipara mimi she'eino omed bediburo* — the One who repaid the generation of the flood will repay the one who does not stand by his word. The *Gemara* notes that this formulation fits naturally into Rabbi Yochanan's framework: *im amart bishlama ma'ot konot* — if money effects a *kinyan*, then reneging after payment constitutes a genuine violation, and the curse is well placed. However, if one holds like Reish Lakish that money does not work at the biblical level, why should a party who simply retracted from a monetary exchange receive any form of *aval* — the shorthand the *Gemara* uses for the opening of the curse, *aval amru mi shepara*? The answer offered is *mishum devarim* — because the party violated their word. But the *Gemara* immediately challenges: does violating one's word alone, without any actual *kinyan* having occurred, warrant the *mi shepara* curse?

### Reish Lakish View

A *Baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Shimon articulates an asymmetrical rule: the *tallit* can purchase the gold coin — meaning *meshicha* performed on the *tallit* effects the transfer of the gold coin, since the gold coin functions as currency relative to the *tallit* — but the gold coin cannot purchase the *tallit*, because money does not serve as a *kinyan* mechanism for acquiring goods. Nevertheless, if one violates the agreement, one receives the *mi shepara*. In the *Baraita*'s expanded formulation, four generations are invoked: the generation of the flood (*dor hamabul*), the generation of the tower of Babel (*dor haflagah*), the people of Sodom and Gomorrah (*anshei Sedom va'Amorah*), and the Egyptians at the sea (*uviMitzrayim bayam*).

### Words Alone

The *Baraita* continues: *vehanosei venoten bidvarim lo kanah* — one who conducts an entire transaction by words alone, without any *kinyan*, has not effected an acquisition. *Vehachozer bo ein chachamim nochim heimenu* — if such a person retracts, the Rabbanan are displeased with him, but there is no formal penalty.

### Rava's Rule

Rava draws a precise distinction: *anu ein lanu ella ein chachamim nochim heimenu* — in a case of words alone, the most one incurs is rabbinic disapproval. However, *devarim d'ikka bahadeihu ma'ot kai be'aval* — if there was a verbal agreement accompanied by the actual transfer of money, then the party who retracts receives the *mi shepara* curse. *Devarim velo ka bahadeihu ma'ot lo kai be'aval* — but if it was words alone without any money having changed hands, there is no *mi shepara*. It is worth noting that the *Gemara* consistently refers to the curse by the word *aval*, since the full formulation opens with *aval amru mi shepara* — the word "however, they said: the One who repaid..." This usage can be briefly disorienting on a first read, since *avel* ordinarily denotes mourning.

## Reish Lakish Proofs

### Asham Gezeilot

Rava now turns to affirmative support for Reish Lakish's position, declaring *kra u-matnitin mesayeia lei l'Reish Lakish* — a biblical verse and a *mishnah* both lend support to Reish Lakish. The verse in question comes from the *parasha* of *Asham Gezeilot* at the opening of Vayikra perek 5: *ve-chi yechete be-amito be-pikadon u-ve-tsumet yad u-ve-gazel o ashak et amito* — if one sins against one's fellow regarding a deposit (*pikadon*), a *tsumet yad*, theft (*gazel*), or withholding wages (*oshek*). These four cases are the conditions under which one brings a *Korban Asham*, which is distinct from a *Korban Chatat* and applies only in limited circumstances — among the five cases known for *Asham*: *Asham Gezelot*, *Shifcha Charufa*, *Asham Nazir*, *Asham Metzora*, and *me'ila*.

### Tsumat Yad Defined

The *Gemara* asks: what exactly is *tsumet yad*? Rav Chisda explains: *kegon she-yiched lo kli le-halvato* — a case where a specific object has been designated as that from which the loan will be repaid. The borrower, for example, might say: when the loan comes due, you may sell my object and take your repayment from the proceeds. Similarly, for *oshek* — withholding wages — Rav Chisda says: *kegon she-yiched lo kli le-oshko* — one had to have designated a specific object that would serve as the source of the withheld wages. Rashi explains the underlying logic: *ve-ee lo lo mechayev a-kfira't sechar sachir korban shevuah* — without a tangible designated object, comparable to a *pikadon*, there is no liability for a *Korban Asham*. A contractual obligation alone, without some object having been physically set aside, does not suffice.

### Second Pasuk

The *Gemara* then turns to the second verse: *ve-hayah ki yechete ve-ashem ve-heshiv et ha-gezeilah asher gazal o et ha-oshek asher ashak o et ha-pikadon asher hufkad ito* — the verse enumerating what must be returned lists only three of the original four cases: *gazel*, *oshek*, and *pikadon*. *Ve-ilu tsumet yad lo hadar* — but *tsumet yad* is conspicuously absent from the return obligation. The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama lav mishum de-machsara meshichah* — is the reason not that *meshicha* was lacking? That is, the designation of the object for loan repayment (*tsumet yad*) did not involve the lender actually performing *meshicha* on that object, and therefore no completed *kinyan* occurred, and therefore there is nothing to return. This would be a direct proof for Reish Lakish that *meshicha* is the operative moment of acquisition.

### Rav Papa Object

Rav Papa challenges Rava's reading. He proposes: *eima me-oshek hu de-hadar kra* — perhaps we should reread the verse's silence differently. The Torah does require the return of *oshek*, yet *oshek* too involves wages being withheld. So perhaps, Rav Papa argues, the *oshek* case being enumerated for return should be understood as: *hacha b'mai askinan kegon she-netalo mimeno ve-chazar ve-hifkido etzlo* — the object designated for wage repayment had already been taken by the worker (i.e., *meshicha* had already occurred), and the worker then returned it to the employer to hold as a *pikadon* until the wages fell due. That is, the worker already acquired it and is now merely depositing it with the employer.

Rava responds: *hainu pikadon* — but then that is simply another form of *pikadon*, making *trei gavnei pikadon* — two varieties of *pikadon* — appear in the verse. Rav Papa replies: *ei hachi tsumat yad nami le-hadrei* — but if that is so, then *tsumet yad* should similarly be read as a case where the lender had already done *meshicha* on the designated object and then returned it to the borrower, and the verse should also require its return. Why does the verse omit *tsumet yad* from the return obligation if this reading would bring it under the same category?

The *Gemara* ultimately concludes: *kera lo tyuvta ve-lo siyuta* — the verse is neither a definitive refutation nor a definitive support for Reish Lakish. However, there is at least residual support: *hashta zu hadrei kera mesayea lei ve-tsumat yad lo hadrei kera* — insofar as the verse does not require the return of *tsumet yad*, that remains *somewhat* supportive of Reish Lakish's position, even if not probative in the face of Rav Papa's challenge.

A later verse is then adduced: *o mi-kol asher yishava alav le-shaker*, and Rav Nachman citing Rabba bar Avuha citing Rav explains: *le-rabot tsumat yad le-hishavon* — this verse comes to include *tsumet yad* among those things which must be returned, doing so *be-hedya* — in its concrete, physical form. This means, the *Gemara* concludes, that the earlier verse's silence on *tsumet yad* was intentional — *lo hadrei kra* — and its inclusion requires this later verse, confirming that *tsumet yad* was indeed treated differently from the outset.

### Bathhouse & Hekdesh

The *Gemara* now turns to the *mishnah* that Rava cited as support for Reish Lakish: *natana le-lavlan ma'al* — if one gave coins of *hekdesh* to the bathhouse attendant (*balan*) in exchange for admission to the bathhouse, one has committed *me'ila*. This raises the question of precisely when the *me'ila* is violated — a question on which Rashi and the Ritba disagree, disputing whether payment was made before or after the bathing. But the deeper question is: why is giving a coin to the bathhouse attendant *me'ila* at all?

### Rav on Me'ila

Rav clarifies the scope of this ruling: *davka balan hu delo machsara meshicha* — it is specifically the bathhouse case where no *meshicha* is lacking, because the attendant takes the coin directly and the benefit is immediately experienced. *Aval midi achrita demachsara meshicha lo maal ad demashich* — but in any other case where *meshicha* on the *hekdesh* object has not yet occurred, one does not transgress *me'ila* until *meshicha* is actually performed. This reading of the *mishnah* aligns directly with Reish Lakish's position: *meshicha*, not the transfer of money, is the operative moment of acquisition — and thus of *me'ila*.

### Non-Jew Barber

A complication then arises from another *Baraita*: *vehatanya natna lasapar* — what if one gave *hekdesh* coins to a barber? The *Gemara* notes that a barber finalizes a service agreement by *meshicha* on the scissors — that is, the taking of the scissors by one party was apparently the recognized *kinyan* act for the haircut agreement. If *meshicha* has not occurred, the transaction is not final and *me'ila* should not yet apply. This seems to contradict the bathhouse ruling. The resolution: *hacha b'mai askinan besapar nochri* — the barber case is discussing a non-Jewish barber. *Delav bat meshicha hu* — *meshicha* does not function as a *kinyan* with non-Jews.

Why not? The principle is that certain *kinyanim* operate within a particular legal system. If non-Jews do not recognize *meshicha* as a legally operative act of transfer in their own framework, there is no basis for *meshicha* to function as a *kinyan* between a Jew and a non-Jew in a transaction. This has significant practical implications: one reason the *Mishna Berura* discusses the practice of performing multiple *kinyanim* in the sale of *chametz* before Pesach is precisely this concern. Since various *rishonim* question whether any single *kinyan* — including *meshicha* — is universally operative with a non-Jew, the common practice developed to perform multiple overlapping *kinyanim* (*chalifin*, *kinyan chatzer* by leasing the storage area, *situmta* by handshake, and transfer of money) to ensure that the sale is halachically effective by at least one recognized mechanism.

A second, harmonious *Baraita* confirms: *natna lasapar o lasapan o lechol baal umanut lo maal ad demashach* — if one gave *hekdesh* money to a barber, a boatman, or any craftsman, one does not transgress *me'ila* until *meshicha* on the agreed-upon object occurs. The two *Baraitot* are thus resolved: one speaks of a non-Jewish barber (where *meshicha* does not operate), the other of a Jewish one. *Shema mina* — the lesson stands.

## Rav Nachman Agrees

### Money Koneh

At this point, Rav Nachman registers his own position: *devar Torah ma'ot konot* — at the biblical level, money effects a *kinyan* for *metaltelin*. Rav Nachman thereby aligns himself with Rabbi Yochanan against Reish Lakish.

## Reish Lakish Proofs (II)

### Levi Grocer

Levi had examined the text of the *Mishnah* in his own version and found: *ashkach natna lesiton ma'al* — in the case where one placed *hekdesh* money on the counter of the *siton* (a large-scale merchant or wholesaler), *me'ila* applies immediately upon transfer of the money, even though the buyer has not yet taken possession of the merchandise. This would support Rabbi Yochanan's position: *me'ila* kicks in at the moment money changes hands, not at the moment of *meshicha*.

---

## Rav Nachman Agrees (II)

### Siton Proof

This version of the *Mishnah* is presented as a challenge to Reish Lakish and a support for Rabbi Yochanan: if *me'ila* applies the moment money is given to the grocer, that implies money is the operative moment of acquisition — consistent with *ma'ot konot* at the biblical level.

### Reish Lakish Reply

Reish Lakish responds: *hamani Rabbi Shimon hi* — that *mishnah* must reflect the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. Since Reish Lakish already knows that Rabbi Shimon's is the minority view and is not followed, he is comfortable attributing that version of the *mishnah* to Rabbi Shimon and maintaining his own position undisturbed. Rav Nachman and Rabbi Yochanan, for their part, regard the *siton* case as legitimate support against Reish Lakish.

A related terminological note arises here: the terms *siton* and *chanvani* are used in the sources — Rashi glosses *siton* simply as *chanvani*, a shopkeeper, though a *siton* is perhaps better understood as a large-scale wholesale merchant. The practical scenario being described is one in which a buyer pays for bulk goods in advance, and the question is whether that payment alone, before taking the goods, constitutes acquisition.

The discussion of *chalifin* also surfaces briefly in this context. Rav Nachman's acceptance of *ma'ot konot* is not contradicted by his earlier ruling (on 45b–46a) that money cannot serve as *chalifin*. These are distinct mechanisms: *kinyan kesef* concerns the purchasing power of money as a medium of exchange, while *chalifin* treats the monetary object itself as a commodity being bartered. Currency attains a distinct halachic status *because* it functions as a formalized medium of exchange, not merely as an object of value — and this is, in many respects, the underlying thematic tension of this entire *perek*.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Abaye Informs

The *Gemara* returns to the nature of the *mi shepara* curse. *Itmar* — there is an *amoraic* dispute. Abaye holds: *odi modinan lei* — the court does not actually curse the party who reneges; it merely informs him of the consequences. Specifically, Rashi explains, the court tells him: *she'im tachzor sofcha l'hipara mimcha* — if you proceed to retract, you will ultimately be repaid in kind. The reason Abaye refrains from calling it an actual curse is the verse *venasi b'amecha lo taor* — one may not curse a member of one's community. The *derashah* on this verse extends the prohibition broadly: *nasi* is not taken literally as referring only to the *nasi*, but as encompassing any person in good communal standing.

### Rava Curses

Rava disagrees: *miliytnin lei* — the court does in fact curse him. The verse says *b'amecha b'oseh ma'aseh amcha* — the prohibition applies only to one who conducts himself as a member of the community, one who keeps the law. Someone who reneges on a completed agreement has forfeited that standing and is no longer protected by the prohibition of *venasi b'amecha lo taor*. He may therefore be formally cursed.

### Rabbi Chiya Story

Rava then adduces his proof: *mana amina lah — deRabbi Chiya bar Yosef havu lei zuzei amalcha* — people came to Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef intending to purchase salt, and they paid him money. Later, the price of salt rose — *aykar malcha* — and Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef sought to retract the deal to obtain a higher price. He brought the matter before Rabbi Yochanan, who told him: *zil hav lehu* — go and give them the salt they purchased. *Ve'ee lo kabil alecha Mi Shepara* — and if you do not, you will receive the *mi shepara*. Now, if Abaye were correct that the court merely informs the party of potential consequences, Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef — as a Torah scholar fully aware of the relevant *halachot* — would hardly need to be informed. *Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef bar oduei hu* — he would already know. The fact that Rabbi Yochanan invoked *mi shepara* as a formal sanction demonstrates that it is an actual curse being administered, in accordance with Rava's position. Indeed, *Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef ata l'kabulei alohi letuta deRabbanan* — Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef ultimately came forward to accept the terms and avoid the rabbinic curse.

## Down Payment

### Down Payment Intro

The *Gemara* transitions to the topic of *eravon* — a down payment. This discussion is precipitated by an alternative reading of the story of Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef: *ela Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef eravon hu dehavah lei* — perhaps what they had given him was not full payment but only a down payment. This reframing introduces the pivotal question of how much a down payment acquires.

### Rava vs. Yochanan

Two positions are immediately stated. *Hu savar kenegdo hu koneh* — Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef believed that a down payment acquires only the proportional share of the object corresponding to the amount paid. If the down payment was twenty percent of the purchase price, the buyer has acquired twenty percent of the produce. *Ve'amar lei Rabbi Yochanan keneged kulo hu koneh* — Rabbi Yochanan, however, told him that a down payment acquires the entire object: the full commitment is effected, with the remaining balance becoming an obligation owed to the seller. The down payment, on Rabbi Yochanan's view, is not a partial purchase but a complete acquisition coupled with a debt for the balance. This is also formalized in the *amoraic* statement: *Rava amar kenegdo hu koneh* — Rava holds proportional acquisition — *veRabbi Yochanan amar keneged kulo hu koneh* — and Rabbi Yochanan holds full acquisition.

### Two Formulas

A *meitivei* — a challenge from a *Baraita* — is raised. The *Baraita* describes two types of agreement between parties: *HaNoten eravon lachavero* — one who gives a down payment to his fellow. In the first formulation, the buyer says: *im ani chozer bi ervoni machul lach* — if I default, I forfeit the down payment to you. In the second formulation, the seller says: *im ani chozer bi achpol lach ervonecha* — if I default, I will double the down payment back to you. The *Baraita* rules: *im amar echad me'elu nitkaymu hatna'im* — if either party stated one of these conditions, the stipulation is upheld. *Divrei Rabbi Yosi* — this is the opinion of Rabbi Yosi.

### Asmachta Kanya

The *Gemara* explains: *Rabbi Yosi leta'amei de'amar asmachta kanyah* — Rabbi Yosi holds in accordance with his own broader principle that *asmachta kanya* — an exaggerated or contingent promise does effect a *kinyan*.

### Asmachta Defined

What is *asmachta*? Rashi defines it as *havtacha guzma* — an exaggerated promise. The phenomenon refers to a commitment made contingently, where the promisor does not genuinely intend to fulfill the stated payoff, having made the promise only to incentivize the other party to enter the agreement. The classic example is the kind of exaggerated conditional commitment found in gambling or wagering contexts: one party promises a large payoff under an unlikely condition, not actually expecting to have to pay. Rashi's formulation — *she-adam mavti'ach et chaveiro lismoach alav* — conveys this: a person exaggerates the reliability of the commitment to induce the other party to trust him and proceed.

By extension, the *ketubbah* is sometimes discussed in relation to *asmachta*: it is a guarantee that money will be paid in the event of a future contingency (death or divorce), and in that sense resembles an insurance policy — an obligation that exists only upon a future event and involves no present transfer of value. The same logic applies to financial futures and contingent instruments generally.

In the *eravon* context, the concern is this: the buyer says, "even if I default and you end up keeping the entire down payment, please proceed with the agreement." He is offering the seller a favorable contingency to induce him to sell. But in reality, if the sale does not go through, both parties would ordinarily expect the down payment to be returned — it is a strange economic arrangement to agree in advance to forfeit one's down payment. The economic logic is that the buyer may believe he is receiving a price low enough that the risk is worth accepting, or that the commitment serves to signal seriousness. Either way, *asmachta* captures the sense that the contingent payoff is exaggerated beyond what is genuinely intended. Rabbanan generally hold *asmachta lo kanya* — such a contingent commitment does not effect a *kinyan* — while Rabbi Yosi maintains *asmachta kanya*.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with Rabbi Yosi: *dayo sheyikne k'neged eravono* — it is enough that the down payment acquires precisely what it is worth. The buyer acquires only the proportion of the object corresponding to the down payment, nothing more. This is the position of *kenegdo hu koneh*: the down payment is a partial payment, not a lever of full acquisition.

### Rabban Shimon

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel introduces a further distinction: *bameh devarim amurim bizman she'amar lo eravoni yikneh* — the foregoing *machloket* between Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yehuda applies specifically when the buyer explicitly states, "let my down payment acquire the whole object." That is, when the language of the agreement invokes the *eravon* as a mechanism of full acquisition, that is when the dispute becomes relevant. However, says Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *aval machar lo bayit o sadeh b'elef zuz u'para lo mehen chamesh meot zuz kanah u'machzir lo et hashaar* — if one sold a field or a house for one thousand *zuz* and the buyer paid five hundred, the acquisition is effective for the whole, and the buyer must simply pay back the remainder — even if this takes several years.

### Kesef vs. Maot

The *Gemara* asks: does this rule for land (*karka*) apply equally to movable property (*metaltelin*)? The answer suggested is: *d'bistama lo kani* — by default, a partial payment for *metaltelin* does not acquire. And the language of the sources reflects this distinction: by land, the operative term is *kesef* — a formalized silver-currency transaction — whereas by *metaltelin* the term used is *ma'ot* — coins. This terminological distinction is not merely stylistic. *Kinyan kesef* for land is a formalized transaction in which a partial payment generates a complete obligation to pay the balance, because the legal act of buying land with money is treated as a discrete and binding commitment. With *metaltelin*, however, the concern is that the transfer of money should function more like *chalifin* — a barter of value for value — in which the full value must be exchanged now for the object to pass into the buyer's control. Accordingly, a partial payment for *metaltelin* does not acquire the whole, because *meshicha* or full payment is required.

This yields an important structural point: according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the *machloket* between Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yehuda may apply only to *metaltelin*, not to *karka*. When it comes to land, even Rabbi Yehuda may agree that a partial payment (*kinyan kesef*) acquires the entirety of the field, leaving only the debt for the balance. The dispute is narrowed to movable property, where Rabbi Yosi holds that the *eravon* acquires the whole even for *metaltelin*, while Rabbi Yehuda holds it acquires only *kenegdo*.

## Shemitah Collateral

### Mishna Shevi'it

The *Gemara* now connects this dispute to a *Mishnah* in *Masechet Shevi'it*. The *Mishnah* rules: *Hamalveh et chaveiro al hamashkon* — one who lends money on collateral — does not have the loan cancelled by the *shemitah* year. The basis is that a loan secured by a collateral is treated differently from an unsecured loan under the laws of *shemitah* cancellation.

### Gamliel vs. Yehuda

The *Mishnah* records a dispute: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that even if the *mashkon* is worth only half the loan, the loan is not cancelled — *eino meshamet*. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi rules: *im haya mashkon keneged halvato eino meshamet ve'im lav meshamet* — if the collateral equals the full value of the loan, it is not cancelled; if not, it is cancelled (at least in part).

### Resolution

The *Gemara* considers whether this is a genuine *machloket* or not. If Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel means the loan is not cancelled in full — *eino meshamet bekulo* — and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi means the uncollateralized half is cancelled — *be'ach palga dela nakit aleh mashkon meshamet* — then in fact the two opinions may not contradict each other at all. They may both agree that the collateralized portion is not cancelled and the uncollateralized portion is.

### Full vs. Proportional

The resolution is that the real *machloket* between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi mirrors the *eravon* dispute. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel savar keneged kulo hu koneh* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that collateral (or a down payment) acquires the entire object, meaning a partial collateral is treated as if it secures the entire loan, and therefore the entire loan is exempt from *shemitah* cancellation. *Ve'Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi savar kenegdo hu koneh* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that collateral acquires only proportionally, and therefore only the portion of the loan matching the value of the collateral is exempt; the remainder is cancelled by *shemitah*.
