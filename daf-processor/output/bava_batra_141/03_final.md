# Bava Batra 141 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumtum/Androgynous Heirs

### Gender Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים וּבָנוֹת, וְטוּמְטוּם; בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַנְּכָסִים מְרוּבִּין – הַזְּכָרִים דּוֹחִין אוֹתוֹ אֵצֶל נְקֵבוֹת. נְכָסִים מוּעָטִין – הַנְּקֵבוֹת דּוֹחוֹת אוֹתוֹ אֵצֶל זְכָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to one who **left** behind **sons and daughters and a *tumtum*,** whose halakhic status as male or female is indeterminate, the *halakha* is as follows: **When the estate is large the males direct** the *tumtum* **to the females** and exclude him from the inheritance, claiming that perhaps the *tumtum* is female. When the **estate is small, the females direct** the *tumtum* **to the males** and exclude him from receiving sustenance, claiming that perhaps the *tumtum* is male.

The ninth *perek* of Bava Batra, *Perek Mi Shemet*, opens with a *mishnah* addressing the inheritance rights of heirs of ambiguous gender. Because the halakhic system operates within a rigid gender binary, and because external sexual characteristics do not always conform to that binary, the tradition developed distinct legal categories for such cases. Two such categories appear here: *tumtum* and *androgynos*.

### Tumtum vs Androgynous

The *tumtum* generally refers to a person with what we might describe as ambiguous genitalia, while *androgynos* refers to a person who presents with both types of sexual characteristics. Both are classified as *safek zachar safek nekevah* — of doubtful male or female status — yet the nature of that *safek* differs in an important way. The *tumtum* is understood to be a *safek bechesron yedia*: a doubt arising from lack of information, potentially resolvable through some later event, and therefore assessed case by case. The *androgynos*, by contrast, represents a class *safek* — every individual falling into that category is by definition of doubtful gender, and that status cannot be resolved. The *sugyot* in the *gemara* and the elaborations of the *rishonim* work extensively to flesh out the practical ramifications of this distinction.

### Large Estate Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר ״אִם תֵּלֵד אִשְׁתִּי זָכָר – יִטּוֹל מָנֶה״, יָלְדָה זָכָר – יִטּוֹל מָנֶה. ״נְקֵבָה – מָאתַיִם״, יָלְדָה נְקֵבָה – נוֹטֶלֶת מָאתַיִם. ״אִם זָכָר – מָנֶה, אִם נְקֵבָה – מָאתַיִם״, וְיָלְדָה זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה – זָכָר נוֹטֵל מָנֶה, נְקֵבָה נוֹטֶלֶת מָאתַיִם. יָלְדָה טוּמְטוּם – אֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who says: If my wife gives birth to a male** the offspring **shall receive** a gift of **one hundred dinars,** if **she** in fact **gave birth to a male,** the offspring **receives one hundred dinars.** If he says: If my wife gives birth to **a female** the offspring shall receive a gift of **two hundred** dinars, if **she** in fact **gave birth to a female,** the offspring **receives two hundred** dinars. If he says: **If** my wife gives birth to **a male** the offspring shall receive a gift of **one hundred dinars** and **if** she gives birth to **a female** the offspring shall receive a gift of **two hundred** dinars, **and** in fact **she gave birth to** both **a male and a female,** the **male** offspring **receives one hundred dinars** and the **female** offspring **receives two hundred** dinars. If **she gave birth to a *tumtum*,** the *tumtum* **does not receive** anything.

The *mishnah* states: *hini'ach banim uvanot ve-tumtum* — if someone leaves both sons and daughters and a *tumtum* child. The first case concerns *nechasim merubin*, a large estate: when there is sufficient property both to provide *mizonot* for the daughters and to leave an inheritance for the sons, *ha-zekarim dochin oto etzel nekevot* — the sons may push the *tumtum* off toward the daughters.

### Small Estate Case

Conversely, *nechasim muatin hanekevot dochot oto etzel zekarim* — when there is a small estate, such that the property will be consumed providing *mizonot* for the daughters with nothing left for the sons, the daughters push the *tumtum* off toward the sons.

### Safek Dynamics

The logic underlying both cases is the same: because the *tumtum* is a *safek*, neither the sons nor the daughters wish the *tumtum* to be counted among their number, as doing so would reduce their own respective shares. The ambiguous gender status of the *tumtum* thus creates a situation where the child is caught between two groups, each of which seeks to exclude it — a direct consequence of the rules governing *mamon ha-mutal be-safek*, money in a situation of doubt.

### Eino Notel Rule

The second half of the *mishnah* turns to a different case: *ha-omer*, where a father declares that if his wife gives birth to a son, a certain sum should be paid, and if to a daughter, a different sum. Here the *mishnah* states that if a *tumtum* is born, *eino notel* — the child cannot claim anything — because *tumtum* is *safek zachar safek nekevah* and cannot make a positive claim to be either male or female.

## Abaye vs Rava

### Abaye Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ דּוֹחִין אוֹתוֹ – וְשָׁקֵיל כְּבַת? הָא קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: יָלְדָה טוּמְטוּם – אֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דּוֹחִין אוֹתוֹ – וְאֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that the males direct the *tumtum* to the females. The Gemara asks: Does this mean that **they direct him, and he takes** sustenance **like a daughter? Isn't it taught** in the **latter clause** of the mishna that if one said that either his male or female child will receive a certain sum once his wife gives birth, and **she gave birth to a *tumtum*,** the *tumtum* **does not receive** anything? This indicates that the *tumtum* does not have the rights of a female. **Abaye says:** The mishna means that **they direct him** to the females, **but he has no** rights to sustenance.

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: if the sons can push the *tumtum* off toward the daughters in the *reisha*, why does the *seifa* say the *tumtum* receives nothing? Abaye resolves this by explaining that *dochin oto ve-ein lo* — the sons push him off, and he receives nothing even from the daughters. The sons say he is not male; the daughters say he is not female; and so he collects nothing from either group.

### Rava Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: דּוֹחִין אוֹתוֹ – וְיֵשׁ לוֹ, וְסֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – דְּתַנְיָא: יָלְדָה טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס – רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: They direct him** to the females **and he has** a right to sustenance. **And** with regard to **the latter clause** of the mishna, which grants the *tumtum* nothing at all, there **we arrive at** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as it is taught** in a mishna (*Temura* 24b): If one consecrates a firstborn animal while it is still a fetus, stating that if it is male it shall be a burnt-offering and if it is female it shall be a peace-offering, and the mother **gave birth to a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite [*androginos*], Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: It is not imbued with sanctity,** as it is neither male nor female. So too, in the case discussed in the mishna here, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel maintains that the *tumtum* receives nothing, as he is considered to be a distinct entity of indeterminate sex, neither male nor female.

Rava disagrees: *dochin oto ve-yeish lo* — even when pushed off, the *tumtum* should have some claim. Being pushed from one group to another does not mean receiving nothing at all.

### Mamah Nafshach

Rava's reasoning is *mamah nafshach*: one way or another, the *tumtum* should receive something, since either as a son or as a daughter he would be entitled to some portion. A doubtful case should still yield some claim, not a complete forfeiture.

### Doubtful Claim Problem

Abaye's rejoinder is that a doubtful claim can never obligate us to hand over money that belongs to another party. The rules of *mamon ha-mutal be-safek* cannot operate here to award property to someone who cannot establish a positive status.

### Priority Question

The deeper issue underlying this dispute is the question of sequence: does child status get determined first — is this person a son or a daughter — with the inheritance flowing from that determination? Or is the child's status as heir primary, with the gender classification coming second? If Abaye holds that gender determination must precede any inheritance claim, then a *tumtum* who cannot establish a clear gender simply cannot make any claim at all. Rava, by contrast, allows the *safek* itself to generate a claim, however limited.

## Rabban Shimon

### Kedusha Rule

The *gemara* suggests that the *seifa* of the *mishnah* accords with the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *ein kedusha chala aleihem* — no designation of sanctity takes effect on a *tumtum* or *androgynos*.

### Korban Analogy

The context is one of *korbanot*. Suppose a person has a pregnant animal and declares: if the offspring is male, I will bring it as a *korban olah*, and if female, as a *korban shelamim*. If the offspring turns out to be a *tumtum* or *androgynos*, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that no *kedusha* takes effect at all.

### Gender First Principle

The reasoning is that the determination of male or female must come first; only after that determination can the specific type of offering be designated. If the animal cannot be identified as either male or female, the conditional designation never attaches, and no *kedusha* operates.

### Alignment Analysis

This position fits naturally with Abaye — who holds that gender must be established before any claim can arise — but not with Rava, who allows the *safek* itself to generate entitlement. The *seifa* of the *mishnah*, supported by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling on *korbanot*, thus provides implicit support for Abaye's framework.

## Tumtum Baraita

### Contradictory Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: טוּמְטוּם יוֹרֵשׁ כְּבֵן, וְנִיזּוֹן כְּבַת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרָבָא, יוֹרֵשׁ כְּבֵן – בִּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִין, וְנִיזּוֹן כְּבַת – בִּנְכָסִים מְרוּבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **A *tumtum* inherits as a son and is sustained as a daughter. Granted, according to Rava,** the *baraita* can be explained to mean that the clause: **Inherits as a son,** is **in** the case of **a small estate,** as the daughters direct the *tumtum* to the sons, and just as there is no inheritance for the sons, there is none for the *tumtum* either. And the clause: **And is sustained as a daughter,** is **in** the case of **a large estate,** as the sons direct the *tumtum* to the daughters, and the *tumtum* receives sustenance along with them.

The *gemara* now raises a challenge from a *baraita*: *meitivei*, *tumtum yoreish ke-ven ve-nizon ke-bat* — a *tumtum* inherits like a son and receives *mezonot* like a daughter. This *baraita* appears to grant the *tumtum* both forms of entitlement simultaneously, which seems to contradict the *mishnah*'s position that the *tumtum* receives nothing at all.

### Rava's Solution

The *gemara* explains that this *baraita* fits well with Rava's position. Rava reads *yoreish ke-ven be-nechasim muatin*: the *tumtum* inherits as a son in the case of a small estate — where the male inheritance share is minimal or nonexistent. And *nizon ke-bat be-nechasim merubin*: he receives *mezonot* as a daughter in the case of a large estate, where he would otherwise stand to receive a substantial male share. In each scenario, the *tumtum* receives the lesser of the two possible claims — a kind of applied *mamah nafshach*.

### Reconciliation

This reading allows Rava to reconcile the *baraita* with the *mishnah*, by understanding the *baraita* as awarding the *tumtum* only the weaker entitlement in each situation. The approach also reflects a broader halakhic preference: we generally rule like Rava, and we generally follow Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and the goal here is to fit those authoritative positions together with both the *mishnah* and this *baraita*.

### Abaye Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, מַאי ״נִיזּוֹן כְּבַת״? וּלְטַעְמָיךְ – לְרָבָא מַאי ״יוֹרֵשׁ כְּבֵן״? אֶלָּא רָאוּי לִירַשׁ – וְאֵין לוֹ; הָכָא נָמֵי, רָאוּי לִזּוֹן – וְאֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** the opinion of **Abaye, what** does it mean that the *tumtum* **is sustained as a daughter,** since Abaye maintains that the *tumtum* does not have the rights of a daughter? The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning,** even **according to Rava, what** does it mean that a *tumtum* **inherits as a son,** since Rava concedes that the *tumtum* and sons do not actually inherit anything? **Rather,** the *baraita* means that it is **fitting** for the *tumtum* **to inherit but he does not** actually inherit. **Here too,** with regard to sustenance, according to Abaye, the *baraita* means that it is **fitting** for the *tumtum* **to be sustained, but he is not** actually sustained.

For Abaye, however, the *baraita* presents a serious problem. Abaye holds *dochin oto ve-ein lo* — the *tumtum* gets nothing. How, then, can the *baraita* say he inherits as a son and receives *mezonot* as a daughter?

### Abaye Reframe

Abaye reinterprets the *baraita* to mean *ra'ui* — suitable, in principle, but not actually receiving. *Ra'ui liresh ve-ein lo*: he is conceptually placed in the son's category — to push him out of the daughter's category — yet cannot actually inherit with the sons. Likewise, *ra'ui lazon ve-ein lo*: he is conceptually placed in the daughter's category for *mezonot* purposes, yet does not actually receive *mezonot* either. The *baraita*'s language of inheriting as a son and receiving sustenance as a daughter thus describes not actual entitlement but rather the legal mechanism by which the *tumtum* is pushed between categories — and receives nothing from either. This interpretation reads the *baraita* in a direction opposite to its plain sense, which the *gemara* acknowledges. Such re-readings of *baraitot* are a recognized mechanism: when an *amora*'s position cannot accommodate a *baraita* directly, the *baraita* may be reinterpreted rather than treated as a straightforward refutation.

## Son vs Daughter

### Lemara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: אִם יָלְדָה אִשְׁתִּי זָכָר וְכוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דְּבַת עֲדִיפָא לֵיהּ מִבֵּן? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַנִּיחַ בֵּן לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ – הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מָלֵא עָלָיו עֶבְרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַעֲבַרְתֶּם אֶת נַחֲלָתוֹ לְבִתּוֹ״ – וְאֵין ״הַעֲבָרָה״ אֶלָּא עֶבְרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יוֹם עֶבְרָה הַיּוֹם הַהוּא״!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **one who says: If my wife gives birth to a male** the offspring shall receive one hundred dinars, if she in fact gave birth to a male, the offspring receives one hundred dinars. If he says: If my wife gives birth to a female the offspring shall receive two hundred dinars, if she in fact gave birth to a female, the offspring receives two hundred dinars. The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that for him a daughter is preferable to a son? But** this seems to contradict what **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** With regard to **anyone who does not leave** behind **a son to inherit** from **him, the Holy One, Blessed be He, is filled with wrath upon him, as it is stated:** "If a man dies, and has no son, **then you shall cause his inheritance to pass [*veha'avartem*] to his daughter"** (Numbers 27:8). The term ***ha'avara*** means **nothing other** than **wrath, as it is stated: "That day is a day of wrath [*evra*]"** (Zephaniah 1:15).

Having discussed a *mishnah* that awards a larger gift for the birth of a daughter than for a son, the *gemara* raises a pointed question: does this imply that a daughter is preferable? This seems to contradict a well-known statement attributed to Rabbi Yochanan.

### God's Anger

Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, taught: *kol she-eino mani'ach ben le-yorsho ha-kadosh baruch hu malei alav evrah* — whoever does not leave a son to inherit his property, God fills him with anger. The proof-text is *ve-ha'avartem et nachalato le-vito* — and you shall transfer his inheritance to his daughter — where *ha'avara* (transferring) is read as evoking *evrah* (anger), as in the verse *yom evrah ha-yom ha-hu* from the book of Tzefanya. There is thus an apparent tension between the *mishnah*'s seemingly greater valuation of daughters and this tradition's implicit preference for sons.

### Inheritance vs Gains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעִנְיַן יְרוּשָּׁה – בֵּן עֲדִיף לֵיהּ. לְעִנְיַן הַרְוָוחָה – בִּתּוֹ עֲדִיפָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the contradiction: **With regard to the matter of inheritance, for him a son is preferable** to a daughter, as a son bears his name and retains his ancestral heritage within his father's tribe, but **with regard to the matter of** providing for his offspring's **comfort, for him his daughter is preferable** to his son, as a son is more capable of coping for himself and the daughter needs more support.

The *gemara* resolves this tension by distinguishing between two different contexts. *Ben adif le-inyan yerusha* — a son is preferable when it comes to inheritance, since the son fills the role of the father in a qualitative sense, not merely receiving property but continuing the father's presence in the world. *Bat adifa le-inyan harvacha* — a daughter is preferable when it comes to *harvacha*, some form of provision or gain. Rashbam explains this in terms of *mezonot*: the daughter's claims on the estate are a different kind of entitlement, though the precise mechanism is not entirely clear from his explanation.

### First Child Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הָכָא בִּמְבַכֶּרֶת עָסְקִינַן, וְכִדְרַב חִסְדָּא – דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּת תְּחִלָּה – סִימָן יָפֶה לְבָנִים. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: דִּמְרַבְּיָא לַאֲחָהָא, וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: דְּלָא שָׁלְטָא בֵּיהּ עֵינָא בִּישָׁא. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וּלְדִידִי – בְּנָתָן עֲדִיפָן לִי מִבְּנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: Here we are dealing with** a mother who is **giving birth for the first time, and** this is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda says:** If one gives birth to **a daughter first,** it **is a good sign for sons. There are** those **who say** that this is **because she raises her brothers,** i.e., helps in their upbringing, **and there are** those **who say** that this is **because the evil eye does not have dominion over** the father. **Rav Ḥisda said: And** as **for myself, I prefer daughters to sons.**

Shmuel offers a different resolution: our *mishnah* is dealing specifically with a *mivakeret*, a woman giving birth to her first child. In this context, the reference is to the teaching of Rav Chisda: *bat techila simanya yafeh la-banim* — a daughter born first is a good omen for the sons who will follow.

### Omen Explanations

Two explanations are offered for why a firstborn daughter is considered a good sign. One tradition holds *de-marbei le-achaha* — that an eldest daughter will be able to help raise her younger brothers, assisting the mother in bringing up the children. Another tradition holds *de-lo shaletah bah eina bisha* — that there is less *ayin hara* when a daughter is born first, whether because the arrival of a girl draws less jealous attention or because it avoids whatever concerns surround the *brit milah* procedure.

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda himself applied this principle personally: *binatai adifan li mi-banai* — my daughters are preferable to me over my sons. One explanation offered is that if a man had two wives and one gave birth to a son first, this could stir jealousy between the wives; if a daughter was born first, the lesser social premium attached to the birth would reduce such tensions. Another explanation, offered by Tosafot, is that without a clear *bechor*, there would be no subsequent quarreling among sons over the double inheritance share, making the household more peaceful overall.

## Meir vs Yehuda

### Bakol Beriyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא. הֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּ״בַכֹּל״ – דְּתַנְיָא: ״וַה׳ בֵּרַךְ אֶת אַבְרָהָם בַּכֹּל״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לוֹ בַּת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁהָיְתָה לוֹ בַּת, וּ״בַכֹּל״ שְׁמָהּ. אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – בְּרַתָּא נָמֵי לָא חַסְּרֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לְאַבְרָהָם; דַּעֲדִיפָא מִבֵּן מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And if you wish, say:** In accordance with **whose** statement is **this** mishna in which preference is given to the daughter? **It is** in accordance with the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara asks: **Which** statement of **Rabbi Yehuda** is this referring to? **If we say** it is referring to the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda with regard to** the term **"with everything [*bakkol*],"** that is difficult. The Gemara cites Rabbi Yehuda's statement. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: "And Abraham was old, well stricken in age; **and the Lord had blessed Abraham with everything [*bakkol*]"** (Genesis 24:1). **Rabbi Meir says:** The blessing was **that he did not have a daughter. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The blessing was **that he had a daughter, and her name** was **Bakkol.** Evidently, Rabbi Yehuda understands the birth of a daughter to be a blessing. The Gemara explains the difficulty: **Say that you heard Rabbi Yehuda** explain that the blessing was that **the Merciful One did not even deprive Abraham** of **a daughter,** in addition to his sons. **Did you hear him** say **that** a daughter is **preferable to a son?**

The *gemara* now considers who is the *tanna* of our *mishnah* — specifically, which *tanna* would hold that having a daughter is in some respect preferable. The candidate proposed is Rabbi Yehuda, whose position is known in connection with the verse *va-donai berach et Avraham ba-kol* — and God blessed Avraham with everything. This verse provides the basis for a *machloket* among the *tannaim*.

### Meir Position

Rabbi Meir interprets the blessing of *bakol* as the fact that Avraham did not have a daughter. On this reading, the absence of a daughter was itself the divine gift.

### Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: the blessing was that Avraham did have a daughter, and her name was *Bakol* — an unusual name, but one that explains the verse. This *machloket* maps onto a broader question of whether having a daughter is beneficial or detrimental. However, the *gemara* immediately notes that one cannot extrapolate from Rabbi Yehuda's position that having a daughter is *better* than having a son. He may regard daughters as a genuine blessing without ranking them above sons, and so Rabbi Yehuda's view on *bakol* does not directly support the *mishnah*'s apparent preference for daughters.

### Feeding Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דְּתַנְיָא: מִצְוָה לָזוּן אֶת הַבָּנוֹת, וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לַבָּנִים – דְּעָסְקִי בַּתּוֹרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִצְוָה לָזוּן אֶת הַבָּנִים, וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר לִבָּנוֹת – דְּלָא לִיתַּזְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes another of Rabbi Yehuda's statements: **Rather,** it is referring to **this** other statement of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One is not halakhically obligated to provide sustenance for his children beyond the age of six. Nevertheless, it is **a mitzva to provide sustenance for the daughters. And** one can infer ***a fortiori*** that it is certainly a mitzva to provide **for sons, who are engaged in** the study of **the Torah;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is **a mitzva to provide sustenance for the sons. And** one can infer ***a fortiori*** that it is certainly a mitzva to provide **for daughters,** so **that they not be disgraced** by having to beg for their livelihood. This indicates that with regard to providing sustenance for one's children, Rabbi Yehuda gives preference to the daughters.

A different *baraita* is then invoked to demonstrate Rabbi Yehuda's actual position more precisely. The *baraita* records a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda on the obligation of financial support for children. Rabbi Meir says: *mitzvah lizon et ha-banot ve-kal va-homer la-banim de-askei be-Torah* — there is a *mitzvah* to support one's daughters, and *kal va-homer* for sons who are engaged in Torah study. Since sons were the ones studying Torah, their claim to support is, on Rabbi Meir's account, even stronger.

### Yehuda Kal Vachomer

Rabbi Yehuda reverses the *kal va-homer*: *mitzvah lizon et ha-banim ve-kal va-homer la-banot de-lo litzalan* — there is a *mitzvah* to support the sons, and *kal va-homer* for the daughters, so that they should not become degraded by having to support themselves in ways unsuitable to their station. This Aramaic phrase *de-lo litzalan* is likely a gloss appended to the *baraita* to explain the reasoning, as such Aramaic interpolations within the Hebrew text of a *baraita* are a recognized phenomenon. Rabbi Yehuda's *kal va-homer* thus gives daughters a stronger claim to support, and it is this position — not the *bakol* *derasha* — that identifies him as the likely *tanna* behind our *mishnah*'s preference for daughters.

A *baraita* recording the same basic case as the *mishnah*, but with the son receiving six *dinarim* and the daughter only two, suggests that its *tanna* considered sons preferential. This illustrates that the *tannaim* themselves were divided on the question, with different versions of the case reflecting the underlying *machloket*.

## Birth Gift Case

### Conditional Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמַר: ״כׇּל מַה שֶּׁתֵּלֵד אִשְׁתִּי, יִטּוֹל״ – הֲרֵי זֶה יִטּוֹל. וְאִם אֵין שָׁם יוֹרֵשׁ אֶלָּא הוּא – יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **If he said: Whatever** offspring **my wife gives birth to shall receive** a gift of a certain sum, and she gave birth to a *tumtum*, the *tumtum* **receives** it. **And if there is no heir other than** the *tumtum*, the *tumtum* **inherits all** of the estate.

The *mishnah* presents the case of *ha-omer im teled ishti zachar yitol maneh* — if someone declares that if his wife gives birth to a son, that son should receive 100, and if a daughter, 200. The initial reading of the *mishnah* understands the gift as going directly to the child born.

### Child as Recipient

On this reading, the *mishnah* is a case in which the father designates property for the benefit of the child, contingent on whether that child is a son or a daughter. The larger gift for the daughter — double that for the son — is what prompted the earlier discussion about whether the father prefers to have a daughter. The complication that arises is a case of twins: if a boy and girl are born together and it is unknown which was born first, the son can claim 100 *zuz* in any case — either because he was born first and is entitled to 200, or because he was born second and is entitled to 100. The disputed 100 *zuz* may then be divided as *mamon ha-mutal be-safek*.

### Messenger Recipient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״הַמְבַשְּׂרֵנִי בַּמֶּה נִפְטַר רַחְמָהּ שֶׁל אִשְׁתִּי; אִם זָכָר – יִטּוֹל מָנֶה״, יָלְדָה זָכָר – נוֹטֵל מָנֶה. ״אִם נְקֵבָה – מָנֶה״; יָלְדָה נְקֵבָה – נוֹטֵל מָנֶה, יָלְדָה זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה – אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:5): In a case where one said: **Whoever informs me** as **to what opened my wife's womb,** i.e., what the sex of her child is, **if** it is **a male,** the one who informs me **shall receive one hundred dinars.** Therefore, if the wife **gave birth to a male,** the one who informed him **receives one hundred dinars.** If he also said: **If** it is **a female** he shall receive **one hundred dinars,** if **she gave birth to a female,** he **receives one hundred dinars.** If **she gave birth to a male and a female, he** receives **only one hundred dinars.**

Ravina offers a fundamentally different reading of the *mishnah*: the gift does not go to the child at all, but to whoever brings the father the news of the birth — the messenger. The *mishnah* is then not a case of transferring property to a fetus or newborn, but a straightforward conditional gift to an adult who brings tidings.

### Baraita Support

A *baraita* is cited in support of Ravina's reading: *ha-mevasrani be-mah niftarah rechem shel ishti* — whoever informs me of the first birth from my wife's womb, *im zachar yitol maneh* — if it is a son, he receives 100; *yalda zachar notel maneh* — and if a son was born, he gets 100. The *baraita* thus explicitly frames the recipient as the messenger, *ha-mevasrani*, whoever brings the news.

### Twins & Nefel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה״ לָא אֲמַר! דְּאָמַר נָמֵי ״אִם זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה נָמֵי – יִטּוֹל מָנֶה״. אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי נֵפֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** since **he did not say** anything about **a male and a female,** if she gave birth to a male and a female, he should not receive anything. Why does the *baraita* state that he receives one hundred dinars? The Gemara answers: This is referring to **where he also said: If** she gives birth to **a male and a female** he shall **also receive one hundred dinars.** The Gemara asks: **But** if he stated all of the possibilities, **what** did his stipulations serve **to exclude?** The Gemara answers: They serve **to exclude** a case where she gives birth to **a non-viable newborn,** in which case he receives nothing.

The *baraita* further specifies that if the wife gives birth to twins, a boy and a girl, the messenger receives only 100 — not 200. Although the father did not explicitly address the twins case, it is explained that he had indeed stated that even in the case of twins, a boy and a girl, the messenger would receive only 100. The exclusion this provision accomplishes is the case of a *nefel* — a miscarriage or non-viable birth. In such a case, the messenger would receive nothing. While a *nefel* counts as *pitrat rechem*, opening the womb for purposes of birth order, it does not qualify the messenger for the conditional payment, as there is no viable birth to celebrate.

## Shchiv Mera & Ubar

### Designation Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לַהּ לִדְבֵיתְהוּ: נִכְסַי לְהַאי דִּמְעַבְּרַתְּ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הָוֵי מְזַכֶּה לְעוּבָּר, וְהַמְזַכֶּה לְעוּבָּר לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** man **who said to his wife: My property** is given **to the one with whom you are pregnant. Rav Huna said: This is** a case of **one who transfers ownership** of an item **to a fetus, and** in the case of **one who transfers ownership** of an item **to a fetus,** the fetus **does not acquire** the item.

The *gemara* turns to a related case: *ha-hu de-amar lehu le-diviteih nechasai le-hai de-mi'avrat* — a dying man who declares to his wife that all his property shall go to the one with whom she is currently pregnant. This is a *shechiv mera* case, a dying declaration, in which the husband seeks to designate the inheritance before his death, while the child is still in utero.

### Rav Huna Position

Rav Huna rules: *havei mezakeh le-ubar ve-ha-mezakeh le-ubar lo kana* — this constitutes an attempt to transfer ownership to a fetus, and such a transfer does not take effect. An *ubar* cannot acquire property.

### No Yad Problem

The conceptual basis for Rav Huna's ruling is the absence of *yad*: there is no hand, no agent, no legal presence through which a *kinyan* can be effected on behalf of a non-existent legal person. Without someone present to receive the transfer, the *kinyan* is a nullity, regardless of the sincere intent of the dying father. One might ask whether the mother could serve as proxy — through the principle of *ubar yerech imo* or otherwise — but the *gemara* does not resolve this in Rav Huna's favor, and he maintains his position directly.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב הוּנָא: הָאוֹמֵר ״אִם יָלְדָה אִשְׁתִּי זָכָר – יִטּוֹל מָנֶה״, יָלְדָה זָכָר – נוֹטֵל מָנֶה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשְׁנָתֵינוּ – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מִי שְׁנָאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rav Huna** from the mishna, which states: With regard to **one who says: If my wife gives birth to a male** the offspring **shall receive one hundred dinars,** if **she** in fact **gave birth to a male,** the offspring **receives one hundred dinars.** This indicates that the fetus did acquire the money. Rav Huna **said to him: I do not know who taught our mishna.** It is not identifiable with a known opinion, and presumably the text has been corrupted.

Rav Nachman challenges Rav Huna from our *mishnah*: *ha-omer im yalda ishti zachar yitol maneh* — if my wife gives birth to a son, he shall receive 100. When a son was born, he received 100. This *mishnah*, on its plain reading, presupposes that the fetal son was the beneficiary of the conditional gift at the time the declaration was made — which would mean the *mishnah* assumes that *mezakeh le-ubar* works.

### Rav Huna Defense

Rav Huna defends himself: *mishnateinu eini yodea mi shan'ah* — I do not know who taught our *mishnah*. This is a recognized halakhic move: if the authorship of a *mishnah* can be identified with a rejected or minority *tanna*ic opinion, the *mishnah* loses its claim to authority as a proof. Since, as will emerge, there was already a dispute about which *tanna* authored this *mishnah* — with implications touching the disputes of Abaye and Rava, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and others — Rav Huna can plausibly claim that the *mishnah* reflects a *da'at yachid* that he does not follow.

## Mishnah Author

### Rabbi Meir Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks why Rav Huna replied in this manner: **But let him say to** Rav Naḥman: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: A person can transfer ownership** of **an entity that has not** yet **come into the world,** e.g., produce that has not yet grown. Just as he maintains that one can transfer ownership of an entity that has not yet come into the world, so too, he maintains that one can transfer ownership of an item to a fetus, who has not yet been born.

The *gemara* now works through the possible *tanna*ic authors of the *mishnah* in order to determine whether any of them can support the position that *mezakeh le-ubar* is valid. The first candidate is Rabbi Meir, whose position across *Shas* is *makneh davar shelo ba la-olam* — a person can transfer ownership of something that does not yet exist.

### Meir Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – לְדָבָר שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בָּעוֹלָם; לְדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּעוֹלָם מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **Say that you heard Rabbi Meir** express this opinion with regard to transferring ownership of an entity that has not yet come into the world **to an entity that is in the world. Did you hear him** speak of transferring ownership **to an entity that has not** yet **come into the world,** e.g., a fetus?

The *gemara* immediately rejects this: one must distinguish between *ledavar she-yeshno ba-olam* — transferring something that exists to a recipient — and *ledavar she-eino ba-olam* — transferring something to a party that does not yet exist. Rabbi Meir's principle addresses the former: he allows the transfer of future property, of something not yet in existence, to a person who does exist. But it does not follow that Rabbi Meir permits transferring existing property to a person — the *ubar* — who does not yet legally exist. There is no *yad*, no legal agent, on behalf of whom the *kinyan* can operate. Rabbi Meir's *chiddush* is about the object of the transfer, not the recipient.

### Rabbi Yose Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: עוּבָּר קָנֵי! דִּתְנַן: עוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes another resolution to Rav Naḥman's objection: **But let** Rav Huna **say to** Rav Naḥman: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says: A fetus acquires** ownership, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Yevamot* 67a): With regard to an Israelite woman who married a priest and he died and left her pregnant, the **fetus disqualifies** its father's Canaanite slaves from partaking of *teruma*, because until it is born the fetus is not considered a priest, and the slaves, who are part of its inheritance, are not the slaves of a priest. **And** the fetus **does not enable** its mother **to partake** of *teruma*, even though it is the child of a priest. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.** It is evident from this mishna that Rabbi Yosei holds that a fetus inherits its father's property.

The next candidate is Rabbi Yose, who states *ubar posel ve-eino ma'achil* — a fetus disqualifies but does not enable. The context is a *kohen*ic family and the laws of *terumah*. If a *bat kohen* married to a *Yisrael* becomes widowed while pregnant with his child, the fetal *zar* prevents her from returning to eat *terumah* at her father's house — *ubar posel*. But a *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen*, who becomes widowed while pregnant with his *kohen* fetus, cannot eat *terumah* either — *eino ma'achil*. Rabbi Yose's position is thus characterized as *ubar kani* — the fetus has sufficient legal presence to generate halakhic consequences, specifically the disqualification from *terumah*.

### Yerusha Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי יְרוּשָּׁה, הַבָּאָה מֵאֵילֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **Inheritance is different,** since, unlike a gift, **it comes** into the possession of the heir **by itself,** without a formal act of acquisition. Therefore, a fetus can acquire an inheritance, but not a gift.

The *gemara* rejects Rabbi Yose as a basis for our *mishnah*: *shani yerusha ha-ba'ah me-eleiha* — inheritance is different because it is automatic. The implication, however, cuts in either direction: perhaps an *ubar* has even more standing in the context of *yerusha* precisely because inheritance operates automatically, without the *ubar* needing to perform any act of acquisition. Or perhaps the very automaticity of *yerusha* means the *ubar*'s passive presence is not enough to trigger it. Either way, the *gemara* concludes that Rabbi Yose's principle in the context of *terumah* cannot be straightforwardly applied to *yerusha*, and so he cannot serve as the author of our *mishnah*.

### R. Yochanan b. Beroka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הִיא, דַּאֲמַר: לָא שְׁנָא יְרוּשָּׁה וְלָא שְׁנָא מַתָּנָה! דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אִם אָמַר עַל מִי שֶׁרָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes another resolution to Rav Naḥman's objection: **But let** Rav Huna **say to** Rav Naḥman: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, who says:** The case of **an inheritance is not different, and** the case of **a gift** one gives to his heir **is not different,** i.e., the same *halakhot* apply to both. **As we learned** in a mishna (130a): **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says: If one said about** a person **who is fit to inherit** from **him,** e.g., one of his sons: This person will inherit all of my property, **his statement stands.**

The next candidate is Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who holds *lo shna yerusha ve-lo shna matana* — there is no distinction between inheritance and a gift. On his view, a *shechiv mera* who designates his estate to someone who would in any case inherit it is effectively making a conditional gift, and *divrav kayamin* — his words stand. Because he treats *yerusha* as fundamentally a subspecies of *matana*, the bequeathing father retains greater control: he can set conditions, modify the distribution, and, one might argue, transfer property to a fetus, since he is the master of the giving process.

### Beroka Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה – לְדָבָר שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בָּעוֹלָם; לְדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּעוֹלָם מִי אָמַר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **Say that you heard Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka** express this opinion with regard to a gift given **to an entity that** already **exists in the world,** but **does he say** anything with regard to a gift given **to an entity that has not** yet **come into the world?**

The *gemara* limits Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka in the same way it limited Rabbi Meir: *eimor di-shamat leih le-Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka le-davar she-yeshno ba-olam* — his principle may apply only to giving a gift of something that actually exists. *Le-davar she-eino ba-olam mi amar* — but who ever heard of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka permitting a gift to a fetus? His ruling addresses a living recipient; it says nothing about transferring property to one not yet born.

### Combined Option Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הִיא, וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי! מִי יֵימַר דְּסָבַר לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes another resolution to Rav Naḥman's objection: **But let** Rav Huna **say to** Rav Naḥman: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka,** who compares a gift to an inheritance, **and he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** that a fetus is fit to inherit. The Gemara rejects this explanation: **Who says that** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka **holds** in accordance **with** the opinion of Rabbi Yosei?

One further attempt is made: perhaps the *mishnah* reflects the combined position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka operating within Rabbi Yose's framework — *ve-savar lah ke-Rabbi Yose*. The *gemara* dismisses this as unattested: there is no tradition that Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka held like Rabbi Yose in this matter, and one cannot simply compound two independent opinions into a synthetic position without evidence.

### Ravina Escapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: בִּ״מְבַשְּׂרֵנִי״! אִי הָכִי, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְאִם אֵין שָׁם יוֹרֵשׁ אֶלָּא הוּא – יוֹרֵשׁ הַכֹּל; אִי בִּ״מְבַשְּׂרֵנִי״, יוֹרֵשׁ מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes another resolution to Rav Naḥman's objection: **But let** Rav Huna **say to** Rav Naḥman that the mishna is referring not to one who gave a gift to the fetus, but **to** one who said: I shall give a certain sum to whoever **informs me.** This person is in the world. The Gemara rejects this explanation as well: **If that is so,** then concerning **that** which **the last clause** of the mishna **teaches: And if there is no heir other than** the *tumtum*, the *tumtum* **inherits all** of the estate, **if** the mishna is referring **to** one who said: I shall give a certain sum to whoever **informs me, what is the purpose of** mentioning **an heir,** since the mishna is not discussing a gift to the heir at all?

At this point the *gemara* returns to Ravina's reading of the *mishnah*: *bi-mevasreni* — the gift goes to the messenger, not to the child. On this reading, none of the difficulties about *mezakeh le-ubar* arise at all, since the recipient of the gift is an adult who is already present. Ravina's interpretation thus escapes the entire problem Rav Nachman raised against Rav Huna.

### Seifa Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר לִכְשֶׁתֵּלֵד! רַב הוּנָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אַף לִכְשֶׁתֵּלֵד – לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes another resolution to Rav Naḥman's objection: **But let** Rav Huna **say to** Rav Naḥman that the mishna is referring to one **who says** that the transfer of ownership should take effect **when she gives birth,** at which point the child already exists. The Gemara rejects this resolution: Rav Huna does not interpret the mishna in this way, because **Rav Huna** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rav Huna says: Even** if one says that the transfer of ownership should take effect **when she gives birth,** the fetus **does not acquire** it.

However, the *gemara* immediately identifies a difficulty with Ravina's reading from the *seifa* of the *mishnah*: *ve-im ein sham yoresh ela hu, yoreish ha-kol* — if there is no other *yoresh*, this child inherits everything. If the *mishnah* were talking about a messenger receiving a reward for bringing news, what does it mean to speak of the messenger as a *yoresh*? The messenger is not an heir, and *yerusha* has no relevance to his entitlement. The *seifa* thus appears to be speaking of the child as the direct recipient, which pulls the reading back toward the problem of *mezakeh le-ubar* — and leaves the question open for continuation on the following *daf*.
