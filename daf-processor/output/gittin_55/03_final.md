# Gittin 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Yochanan ben Gudguda

### Opening & Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן גּוּדְגְּדָא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת שֶׁהִשִּׂיאָהּ אָבִיהָ, שֶׁהִיא יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט; וְעַל קְטַנָּה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְכֹהֵן, שֶׁאוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאִם מֵתָה – בַּעְלָהּ יוֹרְשָׁהּ; וְעַל הַמָּרִישׁ הַגָּזוּל שֶׁבְּנָאוֹ בְּבִירָה, שֶׁיִּטּוֹל אֶת דָּמָיו, מִפְּנֵי תַּקָּנַת הַשָּׁבִים; וְעַל חַטָּאת הַגְּזוּלָה שֶׁלֹּא נוֹדְעָה לְרַבִּים – שֶׁהִיא מְכַפֶּרֶת, מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda testified** before the Sages **about** the case of **a deaf-mute woman who was married** off **by her father** when she was a minor, so that her marriage took effect by Torah law. He said **that she can be released** from her marriage **through a bill of divorce,** whether as a minor or after she reaches adulthood. Although as a deaf-mute woman she is not legally competent to give her consent, the divorce is effective because divorce does not require the woman's consent. **And** similarly, he testified **about** the case of the **minor daughter of a non-priest who** was orphaned from her father and then **married** off to **a priest** by her mother or brother, so that her marriage took effect by rabbinic law. He said **that** nevertheless **she may partake of *teruma*,** although by Torah law it is prohibited for one who is not in a priestly household to partake of *teruma*. **And** furthermore **if** this girl **dies,** then **her husband inherits her** estate. It is not said that because the validity of the marriage is by rabbinic law and not Torah law he is not entitled to inherit from her. **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda further testified **about a stolen beam that was** already **built into a large building [*bira*],** that the victim of the robbery **receives** only **the value** of the beam but not the beam itself, **due to an ordinance** instituted **for the penitent.** By Torah law, a robber is obligated to return any stolen item in his possession, provided that its form has not been altered. If one stole a beam and incorporated it into a building, then by Torah law he would have to destroy the building and return the beam. In order to encourage repentance, the Sages were lenient and allowed a robber to return the value of the beam. **And** lastly, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda testified **about a sin-offering that** was obtained **through robbery** but **that was not publicly known** to have been obtained in that manner. He said **that it effects atonement** for the robber who sacrifices it, **for the benefit of the altar,** as will be explained in the Gemara.

Daf 55 of *Gittin* opens by continuing the broader sugya of enactments made for *tikkun ha-olam* and similar *takkanot*. The *mishna* presents the testimonies of Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudguda — a *mishna* that appears as well in *Eduyos*, the tractate dedicated to collecting halachic traditions that various Sages transmitted. Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudguda testified to four distinct rulings, each of which, as we shall see, carries its own significance for the theme of rabbinic enactment.

### Chereshet & Divorce

The first testimony concerns a *chereshet* — a deaf woman. The term *chereshet* technically refers to one who is deaf-mute, though it does not necessarily mean she is entirely incapable of speech; rather, because she was born deaf and had no means of learning intelligible language in the manner of hearing people, *halacha* categorizes her as someone without sufficient *da'as* to be obligated in *mitzvos* or to execute binding legal transactions. This assumption, it should be noted, is rooted in pre-modern realities: until relatively recent centuries, when schools for the deaf were developed and sign language emerged, it was widely believed that deafness precluded the development of full intellect. *Halacha* operated within that understanding, and the reassessment of that assumption is itself a significant discussion of the past few hundred years.

In the case before us, the girl is both a minor and a *chereshet*, meaning she has two reasons why she cannot independently transact. However, a father has the authority to betroth his minor daughter, and that betrothal is binding *d'oraisa*. Now the husband wishes to divorce her, and the question arises: since we assume she lacks full *da'as*, does she have sufficient awareness to receive a *get* properly? Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudguda testified that yes, *yotzei b'get* — the delivery of a *get* does work. The reasoning: since a woman can be divorced even against her will, it follows that she can be divorced even without full cognizance of what is occurring. That conclusion is striking — not being willing and not being fully aware are not necessarily the same thing — yet that is the *halacha* as received.

### Ketana & Teruma

The second testimony involves a *ketana bat yisrael* married to a *kohen*. When a minor girl is married not by her father but through her mother and brothers, that marriage is only *d'rabbanan* — the Torah does not recognize it as a fully binding betrothal, but the rabbis established it to protect the interests of girls in a society where they could not generally provide for themselves independently. Despite being only a rabbinic marriage, Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudguda testified that *ocheles biterumah* — she may eat *teruma*. A *kohen*'s wife is permitted to eat *teruma*, and even though this marriage is not Torah-binding, it was treated with sufficient weight that she was allowed to eat it. The reasoning behind this, and how it is possible given the rabbinic nature of the marriage, is developed at length in the *gemara*.

The *mishna* also states *ve'im mesa ba'ala yorshah* — if she dies, her husband inherits her. Even for a *d'rabbanan* marriage, the inheritance follows the same rule as a Torah marriage.

### Marish Gazul

The third testimony concerns a stolen beam — *marish gazul* — that has been incorporated into a building. In principle, if a stolen object retains its identity and can still be identified, the owner has a right to reclaim it. In some cases, the beam might be embedded deep within a structure and yet technically still be the same beam it always was, with no sufficient *shinui* to sever the owner's claim. The *halacha* established was nevertheless that *shekinitahu* — the owner is simply paid the monetary value of the beam and cannot demand its physical return. The reason given is *mishum takanas hashavim* — because we wish to enable and encourage repentance. We do not want the practical consequences of returning stolen property to become so burdensome that they deter people from owning up to their wrongdoing.

### Chatas Gazula

The fourth testimony concerns *chatat hagzulah shelo nodah larabim* — a stolen sin-offering that was not publicly known to be stolen. The case in the *gemara* is specific: Reuven stole Shimon's unsanctified animal and then sanctified it himself as his own *korban*. The question is whether this *korban* — stolen and then sanctified by the thief — can function as a valid *chatat* for Reuven. The *mishna* rules that if the theft was not public knowledge, *hi mechaperes* — it does atone — *mipnei tikkun hamizbe'ach*, for the sake of the *mizbeach*. The precise import of this phrase, and the underlying question of what the *d'oraisa* rule actually is, occupies the *gemara* at considerable length.

### Mnemonic Pattern

It is worth noting that while these four testimonies may appear random, there is a mnemonic logic connecting them. The first case begins with *hisia aviha* — her father married her off — the *chereshet*. The next case flows naturally: *niset l'kohen* — she married a *kohen*, with the husband ultimately inheriting her. Then comes *hamarish hagazul*, the stolen beam. And finally *chatat hagzulah shelo nodah larabim*. Each testimony flows into the next through shared language or shared conceptual links, allowing one to remember the full sequence.

## Chereshet Divorce

### Undisclosed Get Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: מֵעֵדוּתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן גּוּדְגְּדָא – אָמַר לְעֵדִים: רָאוּ גֵּט זֶה שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לָהּ, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר לָהּ: כִּנְסִי שְׁטַר חוֹב זֶה – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. מִי לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן גּוּדְגְּדָא לָא בָּעֵינַן דַּעְתַּהּ? הָכָא נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵינַן דַּעְתַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava says:** Learn **from the testimony of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda** in the mishna that if the husband secretly **says to witnesses: See this bill of divorce that I** am about to **give to** my wife, **and then he says** to his wife: **Take this promissory note,** then **she is divorced** even when she herself does not know that the document in her hand is a bill of divorce. **Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda say** that **we do not require** the woman's **consent** for a bill of divorce, as the divorce takes effect even when she is a deaf-mute, who is not legally competent to give her consent? **Here too,** one should say that **we do not require** the woman's **consent.**

The *gemara* opens its analysis with a ruling from Rava. From the testimony of Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudguda, Rava derives an additional *halacha*: if a man said to witnesses, "See this *get* — I am about to give it to my wife," and then folded it up and handed it to her while telling her it was a *shtar chov* — a promissory note — without disclosing it was a *get*, and she took possession of it, *harei zo megureshet* — she is in fact divorced. The delivery worked, even though she was entirely unaware that she was receiving a *get*.

### No Cognizance Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר: ״כִּנְסִי שְׁטַר חוֹב זֶה״, בַּטּוֹלֵי בַּטְּלֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, אִם אִיתָא דְּבַטְּלֵיהּ – לְעֵדִים הֲוָה אָמַר לְהוּ. וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר הָכִי – מִשּׁוּם כִּיסּוּפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious?** Why would the divorce not be valid? The Gemara explains: **Lest you say: Since he said** to his wife: **Take this promissory note,** after talking to the witnesses, he meant to **cancel** the bill of divorce with these words, Rava therefore **teaches us: If it is so that** he meant to **cancel** the bill of divorce, **he would have told** the **witnesses** that this was his intention. The fact that he did not do so indicates that he had no intention of canceling it. **And the reason he said** to his wife that he was handing her a promissory note is **due to embarrassment,** as he was ashamed to tell her that he was giving her a bill of divorce. Consequently, he gave it to her in such a way that she did not immediately know that it was a bill of divorce that she received.

The source for this ruling is the principle derived from Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudguda: *lo bainan da'atah* — we do not require her full awareness. Just as the *chereshet*, who we assume lacks full *da'as*, can nonetheless receive a *get*, so too a woman who takes possession of a *get* without knowing it is a *get* is divorced. The *gemara* anticipates the objection that this should be obvious once the *chereshet* principle is established, but then raises a genuine concern.

### Nullification Question

One might argue that the two cases are not analogous. In the *chereshet* case, the husband simply did not inform her of the meaning of what was happening — a passive absence of communication. But here, when the husband says "take this *shtar chov*," he is actively calling it something other than a *get*. Perhaps this constitutes a *bittul* — a nullification — of the *get*'s status. Perhaps by reframing the act of giving, he has effectively undone the *get*.

### Witness Declaration

The *gemara* responds: *im ita d'vatleih, leadurei hava amar lehu* — if he truly intended to nullify the *get*, he would have gone back and told the witnesses. He had already declared to the witnesses that he was divorcing his wife. The fact that he did not update them demonstrates that the statement "take this *shtar chov*" was not meant as a *bittul*. *V'hai d'kamar hachi mishum kisufa* — he called it a *shtar chov* out of embarrassment, or perhaps simply to be particularly unkind, but it was not a formal reframing of the act. The *get* therefore stands.

## Ketana Teruma

### Why Ketana?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל קְטַנָּה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל: וְאִילּוּ חֵרֶשֶׁת – לָא אָכְלָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יַאֲכִיל חֵרֵשׁ בְּחֵרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda testified **about the** case of **a minor daughter of a non-priest** who was married to a priest, and said that she may partake of *teruma*. The Gemara comments: This indicates that only the minor daughter can partake of *teruma*, **while** one can infer from this that **a deaf-mute woman** who was married to a priest **may not partake** of *teruma*. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? The Sages **decreed** that a deaf-mute woman married to a priest may not partake of *teruma* **lest a deaf-mute** priest come to **feed *teruma* to** his **deaf-mute** wife, as it is common for deaf-mute men to marry deaf-mute women, but their marriage is not effective by Torah law.

The *gemara* turns to the second testimony — the *ketana* married to a *kohen* who eats *teruma* — and immediately asks a pointed question. If the goal of this testimony was to establish that a *d'rabbanan* marriage to a *kohen* is sufficient to allow the wife to eat *teruma*, why did the *mishna* move to the case of a *ketana*? We had just been discussing the *chereshet*, whose marriage is also *d'rabbanan*. Why not remain with the *chereshet* and use her as the example? The implicit answer, which the *gemara* explores, is that *v'ilu hereshet lo achla* — the *mishna* is implying that a *chereshet* could not eat *teruma* in that situation. And the *gemara* sets out to explain why.

### Cheresh Gezeira

The concern is a *gezeira*: *shema ya'achil cheresh b'chereshet* — we are afraid that allowing a *chereshet* married to a hearing *kohen* to eat *teruma* will lead people to allow a *cheresh* — a deaf male *kohen* — married to a hearing, fully obligated woman to similarly feed her *teruma*. And why is this such a problem? The *gemara* clarifies: a *chereshet* married to a hearing *kohen* — the person eating is the *chereshet*, who is not halachically obligated, so one might argue the violation is tolerable given the practical need to validate the *d'rabbanan* marriage. But the mirror image — a *cheresh kohen* married to a *pikachat*, a hearing and fully obligated woman — in that case, *she* is eating *teruma*, and she is fully obligated, and her eating genuinely prohibited *teruma* is a real violation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹיכֵול – קָטָן אוֹכֵל נְבֵלוֹת הוּא! גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יַאֲכִיל חֵרֵשׁ בְּפִיקַּחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why does this matter? **And let him feed** her *teruma*. Isn't she like **a minor** who **eats** forbidden **animal carcasses?** Since the deaf-mute woman is not considered to be legally competent, she is not subject to the prohibition against partaking of *teruma*. As in the case of a minor who is eating forbidden food, there is no requirement to prevent her from doing so. The Gemara answers: Rather, the Sages **decreed** that a deaf-mute woman married to a priest may not partake of *teruma* **lest a deaf-mute** priest come to **feed *teruma* to** his halakhically **competent** wife. Since the validity of their marriage is by rabbinic law, it is therefore prohibited for the woman to partake of *teruma*, as by Torah law, she is not the wife of a priest. There is a concern that a distinction will not be made between the marriage of a halakhically competent man and deaf-mute woman, in which case the woman is permitted to partake of *teruma*, and the marriage of a deaf-mute man and a halakhically competent woman, in which case the woman is prohibited from partaking of *teruma*. Owing to this error, a deaf-mute man might come to feed his wife something that is forbidden to her.

### Mirror Image Problem

Here lies the structural problem. The *chereshet* case and the *cheresh* case are natural mirror images of each other. If we permit the *chereshet* to eat, it will be easy to allow the analogous *cheresh* case as well — and in that case, a fully obligated woman would be eating *teruma* she is not permitted to eat. Since we cannot permit the *cheresh* case, we must also prohibit the *chereshet* case, to prevent one from leading to the other. The *ketana* case, by contrast, does not have this mirror-image problem. *Halacha* recognizes the marriage of an adult male to an underage female, both *d'oraisa* through the father and *d'rabbanan* through the mother and brothers, but it does not recognize the marriage of a minor male to an adult female — neither *d'oraisa* nor *d'rabbanan*. Since the reverse scenario does not exist, the fear of a problematic mirror image is eliminated, and we can permit the *ketana* to eat *teruma* without concern that this will lead to any genuine violation.

### Teruma Allowance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹאכֵול בִּתְרוּמָה דְּרַבָּנַן! גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא אָתֵי לְאוֹכוֹלֵי בִּתְרוּמָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let her partake of *teruma*** that is defined as such **by rabbinic law,** as marriage that is valid by rabbinic law should suffice to permit partaking of such *teruma*. The Gemara answers: The Sages **decreed** that he may not feed her *teruma* even by rabbinic law, **lest he come to feed her *teruma* by Torah law.**

The *gemara* then asks: even if we concede that the *chereshet* case cannot work for *teruma d'oraisa*, why not allow her at least to eat *teruma d'rabbanan* — rabbinic-level *teruma*, applicable, for example, to produce not ordinarily subject to the full Torah prohibition? Then, even if the *cheresh* case were to arise in its mirror image, the woman eating would only be violating a rabbinic prohibition. The *gemara* responds: *gezeira shema ati l'meichal b'teruma d'oraisa* — we are nonetheless concerned that permitting *teruma d'rabbanan* in the *chereshet* case will ultimately lead to eating *teruma d'oraisa* in the *cheresh* case. Even though this requires two things to go wrong, sometimes two layers of protection are warranted, and here the rabbis chose to apply that stricter approach.

Rashi adds that even in the *ketana* case, we are likely dealing with *teruma d'rabbanan*, though the *gemara* itself seems comfortable suggesting that even *teruma d'oraisa* would be permitted in the *ketana* case, since as a minor she is not obligated, and there is no mirror-image scenario to fear.

## Marish Gazul Dispute

### Demolition vs. Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל הַמָּרִישׁ הַגָּזוּל שֶׁבְּנָאוֹ: תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גָּזַל מָרִישׁ וּבְנָאוֹ בְּבִירָה – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְקַעְקֵעַ כׇּל הַבִּירָה כּוּלָּהּ וּמַחְזִיר מָרִישׁ לִבְעָלָיו; וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא דְּמֵי מָרִישׁ בִּלְבַד, מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּנַת הַשָּׁבִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda further testified **about a stolen beam that was** already **built** into a building and said that the injured party receives the value of the beam but not the beam itself. With regard to this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 10:5): If **one robbed** another of **a beam and built it into a building, Beit Shammai say:** He must **destroy the entire building and return the beam to its owners. And Beit Hillel say:** The injured party receives **only the value of the beam** but not the beam itself, **due to an ordinance instituted for the sake of the penitent.** In order to encourage repentance, the Sages were lenient and required the robber to return only the value of the beam. The mishna was taught in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel.

A *baraita* presents the dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel on the question of the stolen beam. If one stole a beam and incorporated it into a building, Beis Shammai rules *meka'ke'a kol habira kula u'machazir marish l'ba'alav* — he must demolish the entire building and return the actual beam to its owner. Beis Shammai, characteristically uncompromising in upholding strict legal entitlement, holds that the thief's practical difficulty is irrelevant. The owner's right to his property cannot be subordinated to the thief's interest in keeping his building intact.

### Takanas Hashavim

Beis Hillel, by contrast, rules *ein lo ela d'mei marish bilvad* — the owner is entitled only to the monetary value of the beam. The reason: *mishum takanas hashavim* — to encourage repentance. If the *halacha* demanded complete demolition, many thieves would be deterred from ever admitting their wrongdoing or seeking to make amends, calculating that the cost of restitution is too catastrophic. By limiting the obligation to a monetary payment, the rabbis removed that obstacle and made *teshuva* a realistic and accessible path.

## Chatas Gazula

### Ulla's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל חַטָּאת הַגְּזוּלָה כּוּ׳: אָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, בֵּין נוֹדְעָה וּבֵין לֹא נוֹדְעָה – אֵינָהּ מְכַפֶּרֶת; מַאי טַעְמָא? יֵאוּשׁ כְּדִי לָא קָנֵי; וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ: לֹא נוֹדְעָה מְכַפֶּרֶת? שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ כֹּהֲנִים עֲצֵבִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda testified **about a sin-offering** that was obtained **through robbery,** and said that provided that it was not publicly known to have been obtained in that manner, it effects atonement for the robber. **Ulla says: By Torah law,** the *halakha* is as follows: **Whether it is known or** whether **it is not known** that the sin-offering was obtained through robbery, **it does not effect atonement** for the robber who sacrifices it. **What is the reason** for this? The owner's **despair** of recovering an article that was stolen from him **does not by itself** enable the robber to **acquire** the stolen item. Since the stolen animal was not altered in any way, it does not belong to the robber, and he cannot sacrifice it as an offering and achieve atonement through it. **And what is the reason** that the Sages **said** that if **it was not** publicly **known** that the sin-offering was obtained through robbery **it effects atonement?** It is **so that the priests not be distraught** about having sacrificed an animal unfit for the altar.

The *gemara* now presents two competing views on the underlying *d'oraisa* status of the stolen *korban*. Ulla holds: *devar Torah bein noda bein lo noda einah mechaperes* — by Torah law, whether the theft was publicly known or not, the stolen *korban* does not atone. His reasoning is *yeiush k'dei lo kani* — *yeiush* alone, without *shinui reshus*, does not transfer ownership to the thief.

To understand this, one must recall the general debate about *yeiush*. When an owner despairs of recovering a lost or stolen object, does that despair itself confer ownership on whoever holds it? In the case of a lost object found by a third party, *yeiush* combined with *shinui reshus* — a change of domain — does effect a transfer. But the *gazlan* is different. He was holding the object at the very moment the owner gave up hope. He cannot claim that the owner's despair freed up the object for him to acquire, since he took it while it was fully under the owner's control and he has an independent obligation to return it. So *yeiush* alone, while the object remains in the *gazlan*'s hands, does not work.

If Reuven's *yeiush* did not transfer ownership to him, then when Reuven sanctified the animal, he was sanctifying something that was not his. One could argue that the act of sanctification itself constitutes a *shinui reshus* — a transfer into the domain of *hekdesh* — or a *shinui hashem*, a change of identity, such that the animal becomes genuinely sanctified. The *gemara* entertains both possibilities. It is possible that the animal does become a valid *korban* but remains one that cannot atone for Reuven, since at the moment he sanctified it, it was still stolen — applying the principle of *mitzvah haba'ah b'aveirah*. Either way, according to Ulla, the *d'oraisa* baseline is that this *korban* does not atone.

### Kohanim Anxiety

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְעוּלָּא: וְהָאֲנַן – ״מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ תְּנַן? אָמַר לָהֶם: כֵּיוָן דְּכֹהֲנִים עֲצֵבִין, נִמְצָא מִזְבֵּחַ בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said to Ulla:** How can you explain the issue in this manner? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: It effects atonement **for the benefit of the altar,** which indicates that the *halakha* was enacted for the benefit of the altar, not for the benefit of the priests? Ulla **said to them: When the priests are distraught, the altar is found idle.** The priests will not sacrifice all of the offerings when they are distraught.

Given that this is Ulla's position, the *gemara* asks: *uma ta'am amru lo noda mechaperes?* — why then did the rabbis say that if the theft was not publicly known, it does atone? The answer: *shelo yehu kohanim atzeivim* — so that the *kohanim* should not be filled with anguish. The *kohanim* eat from the *korban*, and if they are always anxious that they might have consumed from a stolen offering that was not truly valid — and therefore may not have been truly a *korban*, leaving them in a state of having eaten what was in effect *chullin she-nishchatu ba-azarah* — they would become paralyzed with worry. Their anxiety would disrupt the entire operation of the *mikdash*.

The *gemara* notes that the language of the *mishna* says *mipnei tikkun hamizbe'ach*, not *mipnei kohanim atzeivim*. Ulla responds: *keivan de-kohanim atzeivim nimtza mizbe'ach batel* — since if the *kohanim* are anguished, the *mizbeach* itself will be impaired. The two are inseparable: the functioning of the *mizbeach* depends on the *kohanim* being able to perform their service with confidence and wholeness. Therefore, when it is not publicly known, we declare the *korban* valid — even though *d'oraisa* it should not be — so that the *kohanim* will not be consumed by worry. However, when the theft is public knowledge, we cannot extend that protection, and the *korban* will not be accepted.

### Rav Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, בֵּין נוֹדְעָה בֵּין לֹא נוֹדְעָה – מְכַפֶּרֶת; מַאי טַעְמָא? יֵאוּשׁ כְּדִי קָנֵי; וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ נוֹדְעָה אֵינָהּ מְכַפֶּרֶת? שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ מִזְבֵּחַ אוֹכֵל גְּזֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** This is one explanation, **but Rav Yehuda says: By Torah law, whether it is known or it is not known** that the sin-offering was obtained through robbery, **it effects atonement** for the robber who sacrifices it. **What is the reason** for this? The owner's **despair** of recovering an article that was stolen from him **by itself** enables the robber to **acquire** the stolen item. Once the owner despairs of regaining possession, the stolen item becomes the robber's property and he can consecrate it. Therefore, the offering was sacrificed in a fitting manner, and it effects atonement for the robber. **And what is the reason** that the Sages **said** that if **it is known** that the sin-offering was obtained through robbery, **it does not effect atonement?** It is so that people **not say** that **the altar consumes stolen property.**

Rav Yehuda takes the opposite *d'oraisa* position: *bein noda bein lo noda mechapper* — by Torah law, it atones regardless of whether the theft was known. His reasoning: *yeiush k'dei kani* — *yeiush* does work to transfer ownership. The moment Shimon despaired of recovering his animal, Reuven became the owner. When Reuven then sanctified it, it was already his, and therefore it is a completely valid *korban* that should work in every case.

### Mizbeach & Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְעוּלָּא – הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי חַטָּאת. אֶלָּא לְרַב יְהוּדָה – מַאי אִירְיָא חַטָּאת, אֲפִילּוּ עוֹלָה נָמֵי! לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר – לָא מִיבַּעְיָא עוֹלָה, דְּכָלִיל הִיא; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חַטָּאת נָמֵי, דְּחֵלֶב וָדָם הוּא דְּסָלֵיק לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, וְאִידַּךְ כֹּהֲנִים אָכְלִי לֵיהּ; אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי גְּזוּר, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ מִזְבֵּחַ אוֹכֵל גְּזֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to clarify the two explanations. **Granted,** according to the opinion of **Ulla,** that the concern stems from the fact that the priests will be distraught, **this is the reason that** the *tanna* **teaches** the *halakha* with regard to **a sin-offering:** The priests partake of the meat of a sin-offering. If they find out that they ate an animal that was forbidden to them, i.e., an offering slaughtered counter to *halakha*, they are likely to become distraught. **But according to** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** that the concern is about the honor of the altar, **why** does the mishna mention **specifically** the case of **a sin-offering;** shouldn't the same concern apply to **a burnt-offering, as well,** as it too is burned on the altar? The Gemara answers: The mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** and the mishna should be understood as follows: **It is not necessary** to teach the *halakha* in the case of **a burnt-offering, which is entirely** consumed on the altar. In that case, people will certainly say that the altar consumes stolen property. **But even** in the case of **a sin-offering, where** only **the fat and the blood go up** to be consumed **on the altar and the rest is consumed by the priests, even so they issued a decree** and said that the stolen sin-offering does not effect atonement, **so** that people **should not say** that **the altar consumes stolen property.**

According to Rav Yehuda, the *takana* runs in the other direction. The *takana* is not to expand the circumstances in which the *korban* works, but to restrict it: *shelo yomru mizbe'ach ochel gezeilos* — so that people should not say that the *mizbe'ach* is complicit in theft. If a thief can steal an animal and then bring it publicly as a *korban* and have it accepted, it creates a horrific impression: that the *mizbe'ach* is complicit in theft, that one can steal and atone through that very stolen object. The *pasuk* states *sonei gezel b'olah* — Hashem despises theft in a burnt offering — and this concern extends even to a *chatat*, where only the blood and fats go on the *mizbeach* while the *kohanim* eat the meat. Even in that case, the *gemara* concludes, it still models the *mizbeach* consuming stolen property, and we must not allow that when the theft is publicly known.

The *gemara* then asks: according to Rav Yehuda, why did the *mishna* specifically mention a *chatat*? If the concern is that the *mizbeach* appears to consume stolen goods, surely an *olah* — which is entirely consumed on the *mizbeach* — would be even more problematic. The *gemara* answers *lo mibaya ka'amar*: it goes without saying that a publicly stolen *olah* would not be accepted. The *mishna* chooses the *chatat* precisely because one might have thought it less objectionable — only the blood and fats go on the *mizbeach*, and the meat is eaten by the *kohanim* — and one might argue this does not really look like the *mizbeach* consuming stolen goods. The *mishna* therefore teaches that even so, the rabbis prohibited it, because it still gives the impression of the *mizbeach* endorsing theft.

## Slaughter Outside Mikdash

### Kereis Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: עַל חַטָּאת הַגְּזוּלָה שֶׁלֹּא נוֹדְעָה לְרַבִּים, שֶׁהִיא מְכַפֶּרֶת – מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְעוּלָּא – נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְרַב יְהוּדָה – אִיפְּכָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further clarifies the two understandings: **We learned** in the mishna: Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Gudgeda testified **about a sin-offering that** had been obtained **through robbery** but **that is not publicly known** to have been obtained in that manner, and said **that it effects atonement** for the robber who sacrifices it, **for the benefit of the altar. Granted, according to** the opinion of **Ulla,** it **works out well,** as he understands that the Sages instituted that if it was not publicly known that the sin-offering was obtained through robbery, it does effect atonement. **But according to** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda, it should have** stated just **the opposite,** namely, that if it was publicly known that the sin-offering was obtained through robbery, it does not effect atonement.

Rava now raises a powerful challenge against Ulla's position from a *mishna* elsewhere. The *mishna* states: *ganav vehikdish v'achar kach tavach umachar* — one stole, sanctified, and then slaughtered or sold the animal. He pays double (*tashlumei kefel*) for the theft, but does not pay the fourfold or fivefold payment (*tashlumei arba va-chamisha*) for slaughtering or selling, because by the time he slaughtered or sold it, it had already passed into *hekdesh* and was no longer in Shimon's ownership — therefore Shimon no longer has that claim. This much Ulla can accommodate.

But the *mishna* continues: *vetani alah, bachutz kehai gavna anush kares* — if he took this sanctified animal and slaughtered it outside the *beit hamikdash*, he is liable for *kares*, the penalty for *shichutei chutz* — offering a sanctified animal outside the *mikdash*. This is deeply problematic for Ulla. One is only liable for *kares* for slaughtering outside the *mikdash* something that would have been a valid *korban* inside the *mikdash*. If the animal is valid enough to generate *kares* when slaughtered outside, it implies that it would have worked as a valid *korban* inside — which is exactly what Ulla denies.

### Kereis Midvreihem

Rav Shizbi attempted to resolve this by suggesting that the *kares* here is *midivreihem* — a rabbinic enactment that treats it as if *kares* applies, even though it would not apply *d'oraisa*. 

### Kereis Cannot Be Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: גַּבְרָא רַבָּה אָמַר מִילְּתָא, לָא תְּחוּכוּ עֲלַהּ; כָּרֵת שֶׁעַל יְדֵי דִּבְרֵיהֶן בָּאתָה לוֹ – אוֹקְמוּהָ רַבָּנַן בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיחַיַּיב עֲלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said to them: A great man has spoken, do not laugh at him.** What Rav Sheizevi means is ***karet* that comes to him through the words** of the Sages, who declared that the thief's consecration is valid. It is **the Sages** who **placed** the animal **in his possession, so that he would become liable for it.**

This answer was met with laughter in the *beit midrash*, and Rava rebuked that response: *gavra rabba amar milta, lo techechu alei* — a great Sage said something; do not laugh at it. There must be something to it. But the substantive rejection stands: there is no such thing as *kares midivrehem*. Rabbinic enactments can prohibit actions, can impose lashes, but *kares* is a metaphysical reality — premature death at the hands of Heaven. The rabbis cannot create that. When the *mishna* says *kares*, it means *kares d'oraisa*.

Rava therefore reframes what Rav Shizbi must have meant: *kares al yedei divreihem ba lo* — the *kares* comes to him through the medium of a rabbinic enactment. It is a genuine *d'oraisa kares*, but it arrives as a consequence of what the rabbis did.

## Retroactive Ownership

### Before Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָא וַודַּאי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי – כִּי אוֹקְמוּהָ רַבָּנַן בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ, מִשְּׁעַת גְּנֵיבָה אוֹ מִשְּׁעַת הֶקְדֵּישָׁהּ? לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְגִיזּוֹתֶיהָ וּוַלְדוֹתֶיהָ. מַאי? הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא – מִשְּׁעַת הֶקְדֵּישָׁהּ, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא חוֹטֵא נִשְׂכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Although I agree with Rav Sheizevi, **this** matter **is certainly a dilemma for me. When the Sages placed** the animal **in his possession,** did they do so **from the time of the theft or from the time of the consecration? What is the difference** between these possibilities? There is a difference **with regard to its wool and with regard to its offspring.** If the animal was placed in his possession from the time of the theft, the wool that it grows and the offspring that it births are his, and he is not required to return them to the animal's owner. But if the animal becomes his only when he consecrates it, he is required to return them. **What** is the *halakha*? **Rava then said,** in answer to his own question: **It stands to reason** that the Sages placed the animal in his possession **from the time of the consecration.** This is **so that the sinner not profit** from his crime. Otherwise, the thief would benefit from the rabbinic decree that was instituted to increase his liability.

Tosafos provides the clearest explanation of what this means. When Ulla said that the *takana* is that a stolen *korban* will atone if the theft is not publicly known, the *takana* did not merely declare that we will *treat* it as if it atoned while secretly acknowledging it did not. Rather, the *takana* actually conferred ownership. At the moment Reuven sanctified this animal, the rabbis assigned him ownership — *d'rabbanan*, the rabbis have the authority to assign ownership — so that at the instant of sanctification, the animal was genuinely his. Now it becomes a fully valid *korban*: valid to atone inside the *mikdash*, and generating genuine *d'oraisa kares* if slaughtered outside. The *kares* is real and *d'oraisa*; it came to exist through the rabbinic enactment of assigning ownership.

### Timing Question

Rava now raises a further question: *de'okmuha rabbanan birshusei mishsha geneiva o mishsha hekdesh?* — when the rabbis assigned him ownership, did they do so from the moment of the theft, or only from the moment of sanctification? The practical difference: *ligizozeha ulvladoteha* — what happens to the wool shorn from the animal and the offspring born to it during the intervening period, while Reuven held the animal between stealing it and sanctifying it? If he owned it from the moment of theft, all of that belongs to him. If he owned it only from the moment of sanctification, then all of that remains Shimon's, and Reuven must return it when he does *teshuva*.

### At Sanctification

Rava himself resolves this: *mistabra mishsha hekdesh* — it is logical that the ownership was assigned from the moment of sanctification, and not retroactively from the moment of theft. The reason: *shelo yehei chotei niskar* — we will not allow the sinner to benefit. If we need to assign him ownership in order to make the *korban* valid, we will do so — but only for as long as necessary. There is no reason to reach further back and grant him ownership of the wool and offspring from the moment of theft. We assign him ownership only at the moment of sanctification, which is precisely when it is needed.

## Sikrikon Law

### Sikrikon Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא הָיָה סִיקָרִיקוֹן בִּיהוּדָה בַּהֲרוּגֵי מִלְחָמָה. מֵהֲרוּגֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה וְאֵילָךְ, יֵשׁ בָּהּ סִיקָרִיקוֹן. כֵּיצַד? לָקַח מִסִּיקָרִיקוֹן, וְחָזַר וְלָקַח מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת, מִקָּחוֹ בָּטֵל. מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְחָזַר וְלָקַח מִסִּיקָרִיקוֹן – מִקָּחוֹ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The law of **Sicarii [*Sikarikon*] did not** apply **in Judea in the** time that **people were being killed in the war. From** the time that **people were being killed in the war and onward,** the law of **Sicarii did** apply **there. What** is this law of Sicarii? If **one** first **purchased** land **from a Sicarius,** who extorted the field from its prior owners with threats, **and** afterward the buyer **returned and purchased** the same field a second time **from the** prior **landowner, his purchase is void.** The prior owner of the field can say that he did not actually mean to sell him the field. By contrast, if he first acquired the field **from the** prior **owner and** afterward **he returned and purchased** the same field **from a Sicarius, his purchase stands.**

The *mishna* now introduces the law of the *sikrikon*. *Sikrikon* were non-Jewish extortionists — Romans or others — who threatened Jews with death unless they sold their land. A Jew faced with such a threat would sell under extreme duress, almost certainly at a significant discount, simply to preserve his life. The *mishna* establishes that special rules apply to property that passed through the hands of such extortionists. The *gemara* notes that during the immediate period of active warfare and mass slaughter — *harugei milchama* — the law of *sikrikon* did not apply at all. Afterwards, once the killing subsided but the extortion continued, the rabbis ruled *yesh ba-sikrikon* — the law of *sikrikon* is now operative.

### Original Owner Retains

The original ruling — the *mishna rishona* — was that the original Jewish owner, call him Reuven, retained true ownership rights even after the forced sale. The *sikrikon* could not acquire the land in any meaningful halachic sense through coercion. In practice, though, anyone wishing to actually possess the land would have to deal with both parties: Reuven, the true owner, and the *sikrikon*, who physically controls it. The original ruling therefore focused on the order in which a third-party purchaser went about acquiring it.

### Sikrikon Then Owner

If one first paid the *sikrikon* and then went to Reuven to obtain his consent, the purchase is void — *mikcho batel*. The reason: Reuven's agreement in that scenario is not freely given. The *sikrikon* has already been paid; Reuven knows that if he creates difficulties, the *sikrikon*, who already received his payment and has a stake in the deal's completion, will make Reuven's life miserable. Reuven's ostensible consent is therefore coerced, and we do not treat it as genuine.

### Owner Then Sikrikon

By contrast, if one first approached Reuven — before any dealing with the *sikrikon* — and Reuven freely agreed, calculating that he may never recover the land and that at least he can salvage some value by selling his rights, then Reuven's consent is genuine and uncoerced. When the purchaser then goes to the *sikrikon* to pay him off and gain physical control, *mikcho kayam* — the purchase stands.

### Husband-Wife Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָקַח מִן הָאִישׁ וְחָזַר וְלָקַח מִן הָאִשָּׁה – מִקָּחוֹ בָּטֵל. מִן הָאִשָּׁה וְחָזַר וְלָקַח מִן הָאִישׁ – מִקָּחוֹ קַיָּים. זוֹ מִשְׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if **one** first **purchased from the husband** the rights to use a field belonging to his wife, **and** afterward **he returned and purchased** the same field **from the wife,** so that if the husband were to predecease or divorce her, the purchaser would then own it fully, **his purchase is void.** The woman can claim that she did not wish to quarrel with her husband and to object to the transaction but that in truth she did not agree to the sale. By contrast, if he first acquired the field **from the wife, and** afterward **he returned and purchased** the same field **from the husband, his purchase stands. This** is the **initial** version of this **mishna.**

The *mishna* draws an analogous case from property within a marriage. Where the wife has rights to certain property — whether brought as a dowry or secured through her *ketuba* — but the husband has practical control over it, the structure mirrors the *sikrikon* situation. The wife is like Reuven, the true holder of rights; the husband is like the *sikrikon*, with practical control. If a purchaser first bought from the husband and then sought the wife's consent, the purchase is void — her apparent agreement is suspect, as she would fear the consequences of opposing her husband. If he first approached the wife, who freely agreed, and then dealt with the husband, the purchase stands.

## Later Beit Din Ruling

### Sikrikon as Owner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל אַחֲרֵיהֶם אָמְרוּ: הַלּוֹקֵחַ מִסִּיקָרִיקוֹן, נוֹתֵן לַבְּעָלִים רְבִיעַ. אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בְּיָדָן לִיקַּח, אֲבָל יֵשׁ בְּיָדָן לִיקַּח – הֵן קוֹדְמִין לְכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** Later, **the court of those** who came **after** the Sages who composed that mishna **said:** With regard to **one who purchased** a field **from a Sicarius, he must give the** prior **owner one-fourth** of the field's value. **When** does this apply? **At a time when** the prior owner **is unable to purchase** the field himself. **But if he is able to purchase** it himself, **he precedes anyone** else.

*Zo mishna rishona* — that was the first ruling. A later *beit din* took a more pragmatic approach. Rather than treating Reuven as the true owner and requiring buyers to navigate the order-of-purchase rules, this later court ruled: *halokeach misikrikon nosein libaalim revia* — one who buys from the *sikrikon* must give the original owner a quarter of the value. In practice, the *sikrikon* is treated as the owner for purposes of the transaction, and the buyer deals only with the *sikrikon* — but must then compensate Reuven with one quarter of the purchase price, corresponding to the discount Reuven presumably suffered when he was forced to sell under duress. The assumption, somewhat remarkable, is that even with a gun to his head, the *sikrikon* only extracted a twenty-five percent discount.

The logic of this pragmatic shift is significant. The original ruling, while legally principled, created enormous practical obstacles to returning land to Jewish control. Requiring buyers to navigate competing claims, worry about the order of purchase, and secure Reuven's genuinely free consent may have been theoretically correct but was functionally paralyzing. The later *beit din* chose to treat the facts on the ground as given and devise a workable framework: acknowledge the *sikrikon*'s de facto control, compensate the original owner for his loss, and make it feasible for the land to change hands in an orderly way.

### Unable to Repurchase

The new rule applies specifically *bizman she'ein beyadam likach* — when the original owner does not have the wherewithal to repurchase the property himself.

### Right of First Refusal

If the original owner does have the means to buy it back, *heim kodmin lechol adam* — he retains the right of first refusal before any other purchaser. The later court thus preserved Reuven's priority where it was realistic, while removing it where it was not.

## Twelve-Month Rule

### 12-Month Cutoff

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי הוֹשִׁיב בֵּית דִּין וְנִמְנוּ, שֶׁאִם שָׁהֲתָה בִּפְנֵי סִיקָרִיקוֹן שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם לִיקַּח – זָכָה; אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לַבְּעָלִים רְבִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi later **convened a court, and they counted** their votes and determined **that if** the field **remained before,** i.e., in the possession of, **the Sicarius** for **twelve months, whoever first purchases** the field **acquires possession** of it, **but he must give the** prior **owner one-fourth** of the field's value.

Rabbi convened a *beit din* and they voted on a further refinement: *shehasah bifnei sikrikon shenayim asar chodesh, kol hakodem likach zachah* — if the property remained in the hands of the *sikrikon* for twelve months, it is simply first come, first serve. Reuven's right of first refusal, which the later *beit din* had preserved where he had the means to buy, applies only within that twelve-month window. After twelve months, we no longer require any buyer to seek out Reuven and offer him priority.

### Quarter Payment

However, the obligation to pay Reuven one quarter of the transaction price remains in force even after the twelve months. The financial compensation to the original owner is not contingent on the time elapsed; it is a standing obligation.

### Practical Rationale

The rationale behind this further enactment is twofold: practical administration and the broader goal of returning property to Jewish control. Requiring buyers to locate Reuven and offer him priority indefinitely would create an impossible situation. At some point the process must be regularized. Rabbi's *beit din* drew the line at twelve months — a full year — and declared that beyond that point, the property may pass freely to whoever comes first, provided the quarter-payment obligation to the original owner is honored. This balance between principled protection of original ownership and practical functionality represents the culminating refinement of the *sikrikon* law on this daf.
