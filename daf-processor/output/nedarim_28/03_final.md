# Nedarim 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vows Under Duress

### Mishna Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נוֹדְרִין לֶהָרָגִין וְלֶחָרָמִין וְלַמּוֹכְסִין. שֶׁהִיא תְּרוּמָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ תְּרוּמָה. שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָן שֶׁל בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: בַּכֹּל נוֹדְרִין,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may take a vow to murderers,** i.e., people suspected of killing others over monetary matters; **or to robbers [*ḥaramin*]; or to tax collectors** who wish to collect tax, **that** the produce in his possession **is *teruma* although it is not *teruma*.** One may also take a vow to them **that** the produce in his possession **belongs to the house of the king, although it does not belong to the house of the king.** One may take a false vow to save himself or his possessions, as a statement of this sort does not have the status of a vow. **Beit Shammai say: One may vow** in such a case, although he has no intention that his words be true, **using every** means of taking a vow or making a prohibition in order to mislead those people,

We open on daf 28 by returning to the *mishnah* that was introduced at the close of 27b, since it forms the primary focus of today's discussion. The *mishnah* belongs to a series of categories of *nidarim* that are not binding, and this particular category is *nidrei onasin* — vows made under duress.

### Nidrei Onasin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִבִּשְׁבוּעָה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף בִּשְׁבוּעָה. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִפְתַּח לוֹ בְּנֶדֶר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף יִפְתַּח לוֹ. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: בַּמֶּה שֶׁהוּא מַדִּירוֹ. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף בַּמֶּה שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַדִּירוֹ. כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: אֱמוֹר ״קֻוֽנָּם אִשְׁתִּי נֶהֱנֵית לִי״, וְאָמַר: ״קֻוֽנָּם אִשְׁתִּי וּבָנַי נֶהֱנִין לִי״, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אִשְׁתּוֹ מוּתֶּרֶת וּבָנָיו אֲסוּרִין. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **except for by** taking of **an oath,** due to its more stringent nature. **And Beit Hillel say:** One may mislead them **even by** taking **an oath. Beit Shammai say:** When negotiating with a robber, **one should not initiate by** taking **a vow for him** unless the robber does not believe his claim, in which case he may take a vow to reinforce his words. **And Beit Hillel say: He may even initiate** by taking a vow **to him. Beit Shammai say:** One may take a vow only **about that which** the robber **compels him to take a vow** but may not add to it. **And Beit Hillel say:** One may take a vow **even about that which he does not compel him to take a vow. How** so? If the extortionist **said to him** that he should **say: Benefiting from me is *konam* for my wife** if the vow is not true, **and he said: Benefiting from me is *konam* for my wife and my children, Beit Shammai say: His wife is permitted** to benefit from him, since the extortionist demanded that he take that vow, **but his children,** whom he added of his own accord, **are prohibited** from benefiting from their father. **And Beit Hillel say: Both these and those are permitted** to benefit from him.

The broader context of the chapter has already explored the first category, which concerned *onus* in the sense of circumstances beyond one's control that prevent a stipulation from being fulfilled — a general question applicable to conditional formulations in any setting. The present *mishnah*, however, treats a more specific case: a *neder* made under a degree of compulsion, not absolute coercion, but pressure exerted to protect oneself from robbery or violence.

The *mishnah* states: *nodrim le-hargin u-le-charamin u-le-mochsin* — one may make a *neder* to those who would kill for money, to *charamin* (a similar category of extortionists), and to *mochsin*, tax collectors. The inclusion of tax collectors raises an immediate question: why are they treated as illegitimately taking one's money, given that taxes are ostensibly a legal obligation? The *Gemara* will address this directly.

The *mishnah* continues: *she-hi terumah* — one may vow that the produce is *terumah*, thereby deterring Jewish bandits who would not dare touch sanctified priestly portions. As the *Tosafos* observes with some wryness, there is a certain consistency to the moral hierarchy at play: people who had no compunction about robbery drew the line at consuming *terumah*. To make the claim credible, one takes a *neder* to back it up — *af al pi she-eino shel terumah*, even though it is not actually *terumah*. Similarly, one may claim the goods belong to the royal house, *she-hen shel beit ha-melech*, and swear accordingly, *af al pi she-einam shel beit ha-melech*, even though they do not.

### Shammai/Hillel Debate

The *mishnah* then records the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai rules: *be-chol nodrim chutz mi-bishvua* — one may use any form of *neder* except a *shvua*. Beit Hillel rules: *af bishvua* — even a *shvua* is permitted. A further layer of the dispute concerns whether one may initiate the vow or must wait to be prompted. Beit Shammai holds *lo yiftach lo beneder* — do not open with a *neder* on your own initiative; wait for the extortioner to demand it. Beit Hillel rules *af yiftach lo* — one may even take the initiative. And regarding scope: if the extortioner says "vow that your wife will receive no benefit from you," may one expand it to include one's children as well? Beit Shammai rules that the wife is then permitted and the sons remain forbidden, while Beit Hillel holds *elu va-elu mutarin* — both are permitted.

### Shammai Stringency

Beit Shammai's general stringency regarding *shvua* reflects their more exacting treatment of oath-taking. At one level, this is a technical matter: a *shvua* invokes, explicitly or implicitly, the divine name, and carries with it the weight of *lo yenakeh Hashem* — God does not hold blameless one who takes His name in vain. But there is also a broader conceptual dimension. Beit Shammai's characteristic position — evident in the famous debate about how one praises a bride, where Beit Shammai insists one describe her only as she actually is, *kallah ka-mot she-hi* — reflects a thoroughgoing commitment to the alignment of word and truth. A *shvua*, whose very purpose is to affirm the veracity of one's statement, is for Beit Shammai not merely a formal halachic act but a claim to truthfulness. To deploy it in a context of deliberate misrepresentation, even under duress, compromises that integrity in a way that a *neder* — which functions more indirectly — does not.

This framework also illuminates the dispute about initiation and expansion. One reading of Beit Shammai's position is purely quantitative: whatever you do not say under direct compulsion is not compelled. If the extortioner did not demand a *neder* and you offered one, or if he demanded a *neder* about X and you added Y, those additional words are freely chosen and therefore binding, even if the overall circumstances were coercive. Beit Hillel, by contrast, holds that the entire situation compels you — without initiating or elaborating, you may not be believed, and so all of it falls within the scope of *onus*.

But Beit Shammai may also be making a deeper conceptual point. If the extortioner is essentially feeding you a script — demanding that you say precisely X — then in a sense those are not your words at all; you are merely voicing what he dictated. The compulsion is total and the speech-act is his, not yours, and therefore it carries no binding force. Any degree of initiative or elaboration on your part, however, reintroduces your own agency, and once those are your words, the duress of the surrounding circumstances is insufficient to sever you from your *neder*.

### Student Q&A

A question naturally arises about the structure of the *machlokes*: is the debate about the *neder* formulation a question of whether the *neder* is binding, or a question of whether one is permitted to make it in the first place? The answer differs depending on whether one is dealing with a *neder* or a *shvua*. With a *neder* — the formulation of *konam* making something forbidden — no one disputes that one is *permitted* to say it. There is no category of *neder sheker* or *neder shav*. The entire dispute concerns whether it is *binding*: the *neder* was never sincerely meant, and the question is whether the duress is sufficient to disassociate the speaker from those words. The *shvua*, by contrast, raises a prior question: are you *permitted* to make it at all? When you swear that this produce is *terumah*, you are asserting the truth of a false claim. That is where the concern of *shvuat sheker* or *shvuat shav* enters, and that is precisely what Beit Shammai forbids.

### Gemara Begins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דִּינָא דְמַלְכוּתָא דִּינָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks, concerning the mishna's statement that one may take a vow to tax collectors: **But didn't Shmuel say: The law of the kingdom is the law,** i.e., there is a halakhic principle that Jews must obey the laws of the state in which they live? Since one must pay the tax determined by the kingdom, how did the Sages permit one to lie in order to avoid paying?

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* opens its analysis by pressing on the classification of tax collectors. If *dina d'malchusa dina* — the halachic principle articulated by Shmuel that the law of the government is the law — governs our relationship to state authority, why does the *mishnah* treat the tax collector as an illegitimate extortioner rather than a lawful agent? That question launches the *Gemara's* extended discussion.

## Dina D'Malchusa

### Shmuel/Tax Collector

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּמוֹכֵס שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קִצְבָה. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: בְּמוֹכֵס הָעוֹמֵד מֵאֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥinnana said** that **Rav Kahana said** that **Shmuel said:** The mishna is referring **to a tax collector who has no fixed amount** for collection established by the kingdom, but rather collects the tax arbitrarily. Therefore, this case is not included in the law of the kingdom. A Sage **of the school of Rabbi Yannai said:** The mishna is referring **to a tax collector who establishes himself** as such **independently** and was not appointed by the kingdom.

The *Gemara* raises the challenge directly: Shmuel's principle of *dina d'malchusa dina* holds that we are halachically bound by the laws of the governing authority. If so, why is the tax collector treated as someone from whom one may legitimately protect oneself through misrepresentation? The *mishnah* seems to assume that such taxation is unjust — but how can that be?

### Illegitimate Tax

The *Gemara* answers with two distinct explanations. The first, transmitted in the name of Shmuel himself through a chain of *amoraim*: *b'mochs she'ein lo kitzva* — we are speaking of a tax collector who operates without a fixed rate. The amount demanded is entirely arbitrary, governed by nothing more than his whim at the moment of collection. The second explanation, from the school of Rabbi Yannai: *b'mochs ha'omeid me'eilav* — a tax collector who is self-appointed, meaning one who simply positions himself and claims the right to collect by virtue of his own presence, not any governmental authorization. In this case, it is not a legitimate government whose laws are being enforced; it is, in effect, a private individual with a weapon.

### Ran Definition

The *Ran* clarifies the conceptual distinction between the two cases precisely. In the first case, the problem lies in the *din* component: *lav dina hu ela chamsanusa* — it is not law but robbery. An arbitrary, uncapped demand is not a *din*, a genuine legal norm with some objective standard; it is mere extortion dressed in official garb. In the second case, the problem lies in the *malchusa* component: there is no legitimate governmental authority standing behind the collector. Thus, *dina d'malchusa dina* requires both elements — a genuine *din* that meets some objective standard of lawfulness, and a genuine *malchusa* that constitutes a legitimate governing power. Where either element is absent, the principle does not apply.

### Roman/Babylon

Understanding why the *mishnah* does not articulate these qualifications explicitly requires attention to historical context. Throughout the *mishnayos*, tax collectors are treated as presumptively illegitimate — a stance that reflects the reality of Roman rule in the Land of Israel. Roman taxation in that period was often implemented through the practice of *tax farming*: the government sold the right to collect taxes in a given province to a private purchaser, who would then extract as much as he could, keeping the proceeds above whatever he had paid for the franchise. This arrangement is precisely the model of *mochs she'ein lo kitzva* — and explains why the figure of the tax collector appears alongside prostitutes and bandits in the literature of the period as emblematic of social degradation.

The statement of *dina d'malchusa dina*, by contrast, originates not from the *mishnah* but from Shmuel, who operated in Babylonia under the Sassanid Persian government — a regime that treated the Jewish community fairly and extended it considerable autonomy. Shmuel himself had a close personal relationship with the Persian king Shapur. It is therefore entirely coherent that a positive, binding relationship to civil authority would be articulated in that context, and that this principle would then be applied *retroactively* to explain the limiting conditions of the *mishnah*: when the *mishnah* treats tax collectors as illegitimate, it must be because they fall into one of the two categories — uncapped assessment or self-appointment — that fall outside the scope of genuine *dina d'malchusa*.

### Dina Scope

This discussion opens onto a broader set of questions about what precisely *dina d'malchusa dina* obligates. It clearly does not extend to ritual matters: a civil government cannot redefine the parameters of Jewish marriage, divorce, or any domain of religious law. That much goes without saying. A somewhat more contested question concerns the laws of inheritance. Civil inheritance law differs significantly from *halachic* inheritance law, and the overwhelming consensus among *poskim* has been that secular inheritance law does not override *din Torah* in this domain — hence the halachic importance of properly structured *halachic* wills. But even in the monetary domain where *dina d'malchusa* is most clearly applicable, an important distinction must be drawn.

### Debt vs. Payment

The critical distinction is between the *creation of obligations* and the *obligation to discharge them*. The principle of *dina d'malchusa dina* operates, based on the *sugya* in Bava Kamma, primarily in the domain of *dinei mamonos* — it determines questions of ownership and establishes who owes what to whom. What it does not do is override the separate halachic rules governing whether one is obligated to *pay* a debt one owes.

To illustrate: when a government levies a tax, *dina d'malchusa* determines that one genuinely *owes* that sum. The money the government collects in taxes is not considered stolen, and public infrastructure built with tax revenue may be used without concern. Similarly, when the government grants someone — say, through the mechanism of tax farming — the right to collect those taxes, one owes that individual the money, and failing to pay constitutes theft. But if the government itself is the direct creditor, *dina d'malchusa* establishes the debt without necessarily imposing the full halachic obligation to discharge it. The *Ran* makes this explicit in the passage found in the portion of his commentary preserved in the *Chisronos HaShas*: the appointed collector — one who collects on behalf of the government rather than having purchased the franchise — cannot *compel* a fellow Jew to pay, *im lo miyiras hamalchus*, unless the fear of governmental consequences comes into play. Because *dina d'malchusa dina*, in the end, creates no more than a debt, and halachic tradition recognizes that *hafka'as halva'ah* — extinguishing a debt without payment — is in principle permitted.

The practical and moral stakes of this position are considerable. There is a reported view — circulated privately and always disavowed publicly by its author — that on this basis one need not pay one's taxes. The halachic foundation invoked was this very principle: *dina d'malchusa* creates the obligation to owe but not necessarily to pay, and *hafka'as halva'ah* is permissible. The profound problem with this position, leaving aside its halachic merits, is the *chilul Hashem* it creates — a concern that the *Gemara* itself treats as dispositive even in discussions where some technical halachic latitude might otherwise exist.

### Tax Evasion Ethics

The *Gemara* in Bava Kamma that deals with related questions about obligations toward non-Jews introduced the issue of tax evasion in precisely these terms. The discussion there was sufficiently uncomfortable that the text was modified: references to *gezel goy* — taking from a non-Jew — were altered in many printed editions to *gezel Kena'ani*, so that the church, should it read the text, could be told that the discussion pertained only to the ancient Canaanites. Whatever one makes of the underlying halachic categories, the *Gemara* consistently inserts the *chilul Hashem* consideration as an override. When one's conduct — even if arguably within a technical halachic framework — creates the public impression that Jews cheat on their taxes or dishonor their financial obligations, that alone is sufficient grounds for prohibition. The recurring news stories of prominent figures prosecuted for financial crimes make this concern anything but theoretical. More fundamentally, it is ethically problematic to reduce the entire question to *chilul Hashem* as if the sole concern is the risk of being caught; the *rishonim* and *acharonim* increasingly recognize that the differential legal treatment of obligations toward non-Jews in earlier sources was predicated on specific historical conditions of oppressive and hostile regimes, conditions that do not characterize the contemporary context.

### Israel State

The *Ran*'s discussion of *dina d'malchusa dina* carries significant implications for the question of the halachic legitimacy of the modern State of Israel — a matter of ongoing controversy. The *Ran* quotes *Tosafos* to the effect that *dina d'malchusa dina* applies only to non-Jewish kings, and explains the underlying rationale: the basis for the king's authority to legislate is that he owns the land, and those who live on it are subject to his rules as a condition of residence — a feudal theory of sovereignty. By this logic, the principle does not apply in the Land of Israel, because *Eretz Yisrael kol Yisrael shutafim bah* — the entire Jewish people holds a share in the Land of Israel, and no individual sovereign can invoke the landowner's prerogative.

This *Ran* has been invoked by those who wish to deny the halachic legitimacy of the secular State of Israel: since *dina d'malchusa dina* does not apply in Israel, and the state does not operate through a recognized framework of *din melech* or *din Torah*, its legislative authority lacks halachic grounding. Others ground the state's legitimacy differently — either by arguing that the *Knesset* exercises something analogous to the authority of a *melech* in its contemporary democratic form, or by arguing that a democratically elected government represents a form of collective consent that generates its own binding norms. Each of these positions engages differently with the *Ran*. It should be noted that the *Ran* himself, in his *Drashos HaRan*, articulates the concept of *din hamelech* — the king's independent legislative authority to govern society beyond the parameters of *Torah* law — which suggests that even within the Land of Israel, some form of governing authority with real legislative power is recognized, even if it does not operate under the rubric of *dina d'malchusa dina*.

### Ran Tax Types

The *Ran* draws a further distinction between two types of tax collection that bears directly on the original *mishnah*. If an individual — including a Jew — *purchased* the right to collect taxes (the tax farming model), then one is fully obligated to pay him, because *dina d'malchusa* has legitimately created that right and the purchaser holds it. Attempting to misrepresent one's goods as *terumah* in order to avoid payment to such a collector would not be permitted. If, on the other hand, the collector has been *appointed* by the government to collect on its behalf — collecting for the king rather than for his own account — then one may not swear falsely, *deleika unsa*, because the situation is not one in which you are being forced to surrender what you do not legitimately owe. The debt is real.

### Ran Payment Force

But — and this is the *Ran*'s striking final point, preserved in the *Chisronos HaShas* passage — even in the case of a legitimately appointed government collector, the collector cannot *compel* payment. *Mihu ein memuneh zeh rashai lehachriach Yisrael chaveiro lifro'a lo et hameches* — this appointee does not have the right to force a Jew to pay him the tax. The reason: *dina d'malchusa dina lo yehei ela halva'ah* — *dina d'malchusa* creates no more than a debt, and *hafka'as halva'ah muteres* — extinguishing a debt without payment is halachically permissible. Therefore, one cannot swear falsely (since the debt is real), but one is not obligated to pay it either (since the duty to pay debts to non-Jews follows its own halachic logic). The *Ran*'s position thus creates an unusual asymmetry: *dina d'malchusa* generates genuine obligations of *ownership* and *debt-creation*, but leaves the question of actual payment discharge to separate halachic principles.

## Words vs. Intent

### Hidden Reservation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָן שֶׁל בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ. הֵיכִי נָדַר? אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַב: בְּאוֹמֵר ״יֵאָסְרוּ פֵּירוֹת הָעוֹלָם עָלַי אִם אֵינָן שֶׁל בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** He may also take a vow to them **that** his produce **belongs to the house of the king, although it does not belong to the house of the king.** The Gemara asks: **How** does he **take a vow** in this way? **Rav Amram said** that **Rav said:** This is a case **where he said: The produce of the world should be forbidden to me if** this produce **does not belong to the house of the king.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the question of precisely how the vow described in the *mishnah* functions — that is, what formulation one actually uses when taking a *neder* to deflect bandits or tax collectors, and whether a purely mental reservation suffices to prevent it from being binding.

### Piv Ve-Libo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״יֵאָסְרוּ״ — אִיתְּסַרוּ עֲלֵיהּ כׇּל פֵּירֵי עָלְמָא! בְּאוֹמֵר ״הַיּוֹם״. אִי דְּאָמַר ״הַיּוֹם״ לָא מְקַבֵּל מִינֵּיהּ מוֹכֵס! בְּאוֹמֵר בְּלִבּוֹ ״הַיּוֹם״, וּמוֹצִיא בִּשְׂפָתָיו סְתָם. וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּסְבִירָא לַן דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב אֵינָן דְּבָרִים — לְגַבֵּי אוֹנָסִין שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Since he said** that the produce of the world **shall be forbidden** to him, shouldn't **all the produce of the world be forbidden to him,** as this produce did not belong to the house of the king? The Gemara answers: This is a case **where he says:** They shall be forbidden to me only **today.** The Gemara wonders: **If he says: Today, the tax collector will not accept it** as a vow, since it is not difficult to avoid eating produce for one day. Therefore, he may still be suspected of lying. The Gemara answers: This is a case **where he says: Today, in his heart but verbalizes** the vow in **an unspecified** manner. **And although we hold** that unspoken **matters that** remain **in the heart are not** significant **matters** and are not taken into consideration, **with regard to circumstances beyond** one's **control it is different,** and he is permitted to rely on the mental stipulation that he added in order to limit the duration of the prohibition effected by the vow.

The *Gemara* cites Rav Amram in the name of Rav: the formulation is *ya'asru peirot olam alai im einam shel beit ha-melech* — let all the fruit of the world be forbidden to me if these do not belong to the king. The implication is that in one's heart, one does not mean it. This would appear to be a case of *ein piv ve-libo shavin* — one's mouth and heart are not in agreement. But the *Gemara* rejects this immediately: *keivan de-amar ya'asru, yetasru lei kol peirei alma* — once you have said "let them be forbidden," they are in fact forbidden. The Gemara will not allow a simple disclaimer of intent to dissolve the *neder*.

### Vow Modifiers

The *Gemara* therefore resolves the matter differently: *be-omer be-libo hayom* — one mentally qualifies the vow as applying only *today*, while the words spoken aloud are unqualified. Although in general *devarim she-be-lev einam devarim* — thoughts in one's heart do not constitute speech-acts with legal force — *legabei onsin shani*, in cases of compulsion the rule is different. The mental qualification is sufficient to limit the scope of the *neder* to the present day. Crucially, however, the *neder* is still operative for that day; one cannot eat the fruit today. The mental reservation narrows the *neder*; it does not nullify it.

### Zeruzin/Havai

This resolution raises a deeper question. At first glance, the *mishnah* appears to permit something stronger: a *neder* declared without any genuine intent, with the understanding that the duress alone prevents it from being binding. The *Gemara*'s limitation — that one may only *qualify* the vow, not entirely disclaim it — calls for explanation, particularly in light of the earlier categories of *nidarim* that are not binding. Consider *nidrei zeruzin*: if a seller says "I will not take less than ten *zuz* for this," the *neder* is not binding because the words patently do not reflect actual intent. Or *nidrei havai*: exaggerated speech like "I saw a snake as large as a house" is simply not treated as a sincere speech-act, regardless of what the words literally say. In both of those cases, the person's words do not match their intent — and we accept that the intent controls.

### Qualification Allowed

Why does the *Gemara* not permit the same in the case of a vow to bandits? The distinguishing factor appears to lie in the *context* in which the *neder* is made. In *nidrei zeruzin* and *nidrei havai*, the surrounding social context makes it immediately apparent — to everyone present, including the other party — that the words are not to be taken as a sincere vow. The vow is, as it were, transparent to all. But in the bandit scenario, the entire purpose of the *neder* is precisely to *project* sincerity — to convince the extortioner that the speaker genuinely means what he says. The *neder* is made within a context in which its literal import is the entire point; the other party is demanding it as a truth-verification mechanism. In that setting, one cannot invoke a purely private intention to dissolve what has been publicly performed as a sincere vow. The *beit din* cannot look past the context of the *neder* and treat it as though the speaker might simply not have meant it.

What one *can* do is reinterpret the vow's scope — the principle of *perushen lehakel*, that one may offer a limiting interpretation of what one's words meant. This is a legitimate move even in normal circumstances, though in some contexts it is limited to a *talmid chacham* capable of articulating such distinctions. In the *onus* context, it may be that even an ordinary person may invoke this interpretive approach. But the category of complete non-intent — *lo kavanti* in the fullest sense — is not available when the *neder* was made in a context where sincerity of intent was, by design, the entire point.

## Vows vs. Oaths

### Mishna/Braisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תָּנָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִפְתַּח לוֹ בִּשְׁבוּעָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף יִפְתַּח לוֹ בִּשְׁבוּעָה. לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי בִּשְׁבוּעָה הוּא דְּלֹא יִפְתַּח לוֹ, הָא בְּנֶדֶר יִפְתַּח לוֹ. וְהָא תְּנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִפְתַּח לוֹ בַּנֶּדֶר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that a Sage **taught: Beit Shammai say** that **one may not initiate by** taking **an oath to him** unless the extortionist does not believe his claim, **and Beit Hillel say: He may even initiate by** taking **an oath to him.** The Gemara asks: A precise analysis of the wording indicates that **according to Beit Shammai it is** only **by** taking **an oath to him that one may not initiate, but one may initiate by** taking **a vow to him.** Rav Huna asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **Beit Shammai say: He may not initiate by** taking **a vow to him?**

The *Gemara* now addresses an apparent tension between the *mishnah* and a *braisa* on the question of what one may offer under duress. Our *mishnah* frames the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel in terms of a *neder*: Beit Shammai rules *lo yiftach lo be-neder* — do not initiate a *neder*; Beit Hillel rules *af yiftach lo* — one may initiate. Rav Huna then cites a *braisa* that frames the debate differently: Beit Shammai rules *lo yiftach lo bishvua* — do not open with a *shvua*; Beit Hillel rules *af yiftach lo bishvua* — one may even offer a *shvua*. At first reading, these two formulations seem contradictory. The *braisa* implies that Beit Shammai permits initiating a *neder*, since only the *shvua* is restricted; the *mishnah*, however, implies that Beit Shammai restricts even the *neder*. And the *mishnah* implies that even according to Beit Shammai one may not respond with a *shvua* when the extortioner demands it, whereas the *braisa* seems to limit the prohibition only to *initiating* one.

### False Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ: מִיפְתָּח הוּא דְּלָא יִפְתַּח לוֹ בִּשְׁבוּעָה, הָא מִידָּר נָדַר בִּשְׁבוּעָה. וְהָתְנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: בַּכֹּל נוֹדְרִין חוּץ מִבִּשְׁבוּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna asks another question: **And furthermore,** a precise analysis of the wording indicates that **he may not initiate by** taking **an oath to him, but he may certainly vow with an oath** if the tax collector insists on it; **but didn't we learn** in the mishna that **Beit Shammai say: They may take a vow** in such a case **using every** means of vowing in order to mislead them **except for by taking an oath,** which indicates that one may not take an oath even if he does not initiate with one?

The discussion raises a serious ethical question that the *Gemara* does not entirely sidestep. If Beit Hillel permits offering a *shvua* to deflect a bandit — and one swears, say, that these goods are *terumah* — is one not swearing a *shvuat sheker*? The *Gemara*'s answer, developed through the mechanism of *be-omer be-libo hayom*, suggests that the *shvua* too must be structured in a way that involves a qualifying mental reservation, not a wholesale false assertion. Whether swearing with *shem Hashem* that something is *terumah* — even with a mental qualifier in the form of a time-limitation — constitutes a truly permissible *shvua*, or whether some formulation short of directly swearing to the falsehood is required, remains an underlying tension in the analysis.

### Shammai Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא מַתְנִיתִין בְּנֶדֶר, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחָן דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי. תַּנָּא בָּרַיְיתָא בִּשְׁבוּעָה, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחָן דְּבֵית הִלֵּל. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, הָכִי קָתָנֵי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין שְׁאֵלָה בִּשְׁבוּעָה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ שְׁאֵלָה בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the contradiction: **The mishna taught** the *halakha* that **pertains to a vow to convey to you the far-reaching nature of** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** who say that one may not initiate even with a vow. However, **the *baraita* taught** the *halakha* that **pertains to an oath to convey to you the far-reaching nature of** the opinion of **Beit Hillel,** who maintain that initiating even with an oath is permitted. It is apparent that according to Beit Shammai one may not initiate with a vow and may not take an oath at all. Therefore, the *baraita* cannot be used to infer Beit Shammai's opinion concerning oaths. **Rav Ashi said** the following to resolve the contradiction: **This is what** it **is teaching:** The *baraita* does not refer to a vow taken in the case of robbers or tax collectors. Rather, the dispute focuses on an entirely different topic: **Beit Shammai say** that **there is no** allowance for **a request** for dissolution **of an oath,** and the statement: He may not initiate, relates to a halakhic authority who seeks an opening to dissolve an oath. **And Beit Hillel say there is** an allowance for **a request** for dissolution **of an oath.**

The *Gemara* resolves this by recognizing that the *mishnah* and the *braisa* are each designed to teach a different *chiddush*. The *mishnah* addresses the *neder* case in order to convey the full stringency of Beit Shammai: Beit Shammai does not even permit *initiating* a *neder*. But when it comes to a *shvua*, Beit Shammai goes even further — one may not even *respond* with a *shvua* when it is demanded. The *braisa*, in turn, addresses the *shvua* case in order to convey the full permissiveness of Beit Hillel: Beit Hillel permits not merely responding with a *shvua* but initiating one. Thus: *tana mateisein be-neder lehodiah kocha de-Beit Shammai* — the *mishnah* teaches through the *neder* to show how far Beit Shammai restricts (no initiation even of a *neder*); *tana braisa bishvua lehodiah kocha de-Beit Hillel* — the *braisa* teaches through the *shvua* to show how far Beit Hillel permits (even initiating a *shvua*).

### Hillel Oaths

Beit Hillel, by contrast, rules *yesh she'elah bishvua* — the same process of halachic release available for a *neder* applies equally to a *shvua*. The full apparatus of *petach* and *charata* is available to someone who has taken a *shvua* and now seeks release from it. This is of a piece with Beit Hillel's broader approach throughout this chapter: the comprehensive circumstances of duress or regret are sufficient to engage the mechanisms of release, whether the original speech-act was a *neder* or a *shvua*.

## Kodashim Status

### Kadosh Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵי נְטִיעוֹת הָאֵלּוּ קׇרְבָּן אִם אֵינָן נִקְצָצוֹת״, ״טַלִּית זוֹ קׇרְבָּן אִם אֵינָהּ נִשְׂרֶפֶת״ — יֵשׁ לָהֶן פִּדְיוֹן. ״הֲרֵי נְטִיעוֹת הָאֵלּוּ קׇרְבָּן עַד שֶׁיִּקָּצְצוּ״, ״טַלִּית זוֹ קׇרְבָּן עַד שֶׁתִּשָּׂרֵף״ — אֵין לָהֶם פִּדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one sees his property in danger of being destroyed, and takes a vow stating, for example: **These saplings are** like **an offering if they are not cut** down, or: **This garment is** like **an offering if it is not burned,** these items are consecrated if the saplings remain standing or if the garment is not burned. In addition, **they are subject to** the possibility of **redemption** just as other items consecrated for maintenance of the Temple may be redeemed. But if one said: **These saplings are** like **an offering until they are cut** down, or: **This garment is** like **an offering until it is burned,** then **they are not subject to** the possibility of **redemption.**

With the analysis of *nidrei onasin* substantially complete, the *Gemara* transitions to a new *mishnah* that appears at first to be entirely unrelated. The four categories of non-binding *nidarim* — *havai*, *shegagos*, *onsin*, and the opening category of conditional *nedarim* — have all been addressed. Now the *mishnah* introduces a different conceptual dimension: the question of whether a *neder* can acquire a status analogous to *hekdesh*, sanctified property dedicated to the *Mikdash*.

### Rabbi Meir

This question arises from the very linguistic foundation of the *neder*. When one says *konam*, one is invoking a term drawn from the world of *korbanos* — *konam* is a cognate of *korban* — to render something forbidden. Rabbi Meir holds that this is not merely a formal parallel: when one says *konam* and renders something forbidden to all benefit, one has effectively instilled upon the object a status that draws genuinely from the world of *hekdesh*. Consequently, two concepts from *hilchos kodashim* apply: *me'ilah* and *pidyon*. *Me'ilah* is the transgression of deriving benefit from sanctified property, and according to Rabbi Meir, one who benefits from an object declared *konam* transgresses *me'ilah*, not merely the prohibition of *lo yachel devaro*. And *pidyon* — redemption — is the mechanism by which the sanctified status of an object may be transferred to a monetary substitute, after which the original object becomes permitted.

This is a conceptually remarkable position. *Me'ilah* in its classical sense involves misappropriating property that belongs to the *Mikdash*; but an object declared *konam* does not belong to the *Mikdash*. The operative concept in Rabbi Meir's view must be that *me'ilah* is not primarily about ownership but about violation of sanctified status — and that status can exist in the domain of *nedarim* just as it does in the domain of *hekdesh*. An analogy might be found in *ma'al ba-cherem*: the violation of *cherem* is not a theft from any party but an abuse of the object's declared status of inviolability.

*Pidyon* is even more conceptually challenging in this context. When one redeems a sanctified animal that has developed a *mum* (blemish), the money paid transfers the sanctified status from the animal to the coins, the animal becomes permitted, and the coins go to the *Mikdash*. In the *konam* scenario, who receives the redemption money? The answer is that the mechanism here is less about ownership transfer and more about transfer of prohibitory status. One substitutes another object, which now carries the *issur*, in place of the original, which is then released.

### Konam Status

The use of the word *konam* specifically — rather than a more generic formulation of *issur* — appears to be what activates these *kodashim*-like consequences. *Konam* is the *neder* term most directly rooted in the language of *korban*, and it is this linguistic connection that provides the basis for applying the categories of *me'ilah* and *pidyon* to the *neder*-world. Whether one says *konam* in a formulation that restricts the object to oneself alone, or to all people, also matters: the *korban*-like quality of universal prohibition (*assur le-kulam*) is more fully realized when no one at all may benefit, which aligns more closely with the status of *hekdesh*.

## Trees/Garments

### Unlimited Pidyon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלִיתְנֵי ״קְדוֹשׁוֹת וְאֵין קְדוֹשׁוֹת״! אַיְּידֵי דְבָעֵי לְמִיתְנָא סֵיפָא ״אֵין לָהֶם פִּדְיוֹן״, תְּנָא נָמֵי רֵישָׁא ״יֵשׁ לָהֶם פִּדְיוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the language of the mishna: Why does the mishna utilize the wording: They have redemption and they do not have redemption? **Let** the mishna **teach: They are consecrated and they are not consecrated,** since the primary novelty is that they are consecrated, but not completely. The Gemara answers: **Since it wanted to teach** in **the latter clause** the phrase: **They are not subject to** the possibility of **redemption,** which cannot be expressed as: They are not consecrated, as they are consecrated, **it taught also the first clause** using the language: **They are subject to** the possibility of **redemption.**

The *mishnah* now presents two sets of cases to illustrate when *pidyon* applies and when it does not. The first clause states: *harei netios elu korban im einan niktzatzos* — these saplings are a *korban* if they are not chopped down; *tallis zo korban im eina nisrefes* — this garment is a *korban* if it does not get burned. In both cases, *yesh lahen pidyon* — there is *pidyon*. The formulation uses the word *korban* explicitly, which could suggest either genuine *kedushas korban* or *konam*-style *neder*. The preferred reading, given the context of *Nedarim*, is the latter: these are *konam* declarations that have acquired, per Rabbi Meir's position, the halachic attributes of *hekdesh*, including the availability of *pidyon*.

### Limited No Pidyon

The second clause states: *harei netios elu korban ad she-yikatzatzu* — these are *korban* until they are chopped down; *tallis zo korban ad she-tisaref* — this garment is *korban* until it is burned. In these cases, *ein lahen pidyon* — there is no *pidyon*. The difference appears to lie in the temporal scope of the *neder*. When one sanctifies something without qualification, the status applies permanently, which is analogous to genuine *hekdesh*, and the apparatus of *pidyon* is therefore available. When one gives the sanctification an expiration point — "until it is chopped down" or "until it burns" — one has imposed a time limit that is fundamentally inconsistent with genuine *hekdesh*. You cannot sanctify something as an *olah* for ten hours; the sanctified status either applies absolutely or it is not genuine *kedushas hekdesh*. A time-limited sanctification therefore lacks the full character of *hekdesh* and consequently does not trigger the mechanism of *pidyon*.

### Konam/Korban

The question of whether the *mishnah* is speaking of genuine *kedushas korban* or of *konam* declarations runs as a structural theme through the *Ran*'s commentary, where he develops two parallel explanations: one treating the *mishnah* as referring to actual *hekdesh be-damim* (sanctification of the object's monetary value for the purpose of purchasing a *korban*), and the other treating it as referring to *konam*-style *nedarim* that have acquired the attributes of *hekdesh* through Rabbi Meir's principle. Given that we are in *Maseches Nedarim* and that the language of *konam* pervades the surrounding discussion, the latter reading has much to recommend it.

## Time-Bound Vows

### Condition Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי נָדַר? אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: בְּאוֹמֵר ״אִם אֵינָן נִקְצָצוֹת הַיּוֹם״, וְעָבַר הַיּוֹם וְלֹא נִקְצְצוּ. אִם כֵּן לְמָה לִי לְמֵימַר? פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא זִיקָא נְפִישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **How did he take a vow?** What was the precise language that he used? **Ameimar said: Where he says:** These saplings are like an offering **if they are not cut** down **today, and the day passed and they were not cut** down. The Gemara asks: **If so, why do I** need **to say** that they are consecrated? Isn't it **obvious** that his vow takes effect? The Gemara answers: **No, it is necessary** in a case **where there is a great** strong **wind** and he thought that they would be uprooted by the wind.

The *Gemara* now returns to the specific *mishnayos* of the previous section to analyze them more carefully. The first point of inquiry is why the *mishnah* found it necessary to state the condition explicitly — *im einan niktzatzos* — in the first clause, when the real *chiddush* of the *mishnah* appears to be the second clause's ruling that there is no *pidyon*. The answer is that the first clause's condition is stated precisely to contrast it with the second, and also because the first clause itself contains a *chiddush* that is not self-evident.

### Ameimar

Ameimar explains: the first clause is speaking of a case where one says "let these trees be *korban* if they are not chopped down *today*" — and then the day passes and they are not chopped down. One might have thought that because the person *expected* them to be chopped down — that expectation being the implicit motivation for the vow — the *neder* was never entirely sincere, and therefore not binding when the condition happened to be satisfied. Ameimar's ruling is that this is incorrect: once the condition stipulated in the *neder* is satisfied, the *neder* is binding, regardless of what the person anticipated.

### Strong Wind

The *Gemara* presses further: if the *chiddush* is simply that a satisfied condition binds, that seems obvious. The *Gemara* responds that the case is specifically one where there is *zika nefisha* — a strong wind — such that destruction of the saplings seemed near-certain. The vow thus had the character of a conditional deal: "If somehow, against all odds, these saplings survive the day, they shall be *korban*." In such circumstances, one might argue that the person never truly intended to sanctify the trees — the condition was formulated as a virtual impossibility. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* rules that since the condition was formally stated and formally satisfied, the *neder* is binding.

### Fire Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ גַּבֵּי טַלִּית, וְטַלִּית לִשְׂרֵיפָה קָיְימָא? [אִין] כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא דְּלֵיקָה. הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּאִיכָּא זִיקָא נְפִישָׁא, וְסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ דְּמַסֵּיק אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּלָא מִיתְנַצְלָן וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָא נָדַר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't this taught together with** the case of **a garment,** indicating that the two are equivalent, **and** is **a garment ready for burning,** i.e., is it assumed that it will burn? The Gemara answers: **Yes,** in a case **where there is a fire.** The Gemara explains: **Here also there is a great** strong **wind, and** it **enters your mind that one raised in his mind** the possibility **that** the saplings **will not be saved, and due to that** reason **he took a vow.** Since in any event he assumes he will lose the saplings, perhaps he did not really intend to consecrate them. The mishna **teaches us** that in spite of this it is still considered a vow.

A parallel scenario is applied to the garment case. A garment declared *korban* "if it is not burned" draws its meaning from a situation where there is *deleika* — a fire in the vicinity — making destruction of the garment a near-certainty. Again, one might characterize the vow as a practical impossibility and question whether genuine intent is present; and again, the *Gemara* rules that once the condition is stipulated and satisfied, the *neder* binds.

The underlying logic connects to the earlier discussion of *nidrei zeruzin*: the question of whether a *neder* reflects sincere intent is contextually determined, and a condition-based vow, even one predicated on an expected outcome that did not materialize, does not fall into the category of obviously-insincere speech.

### Pidyon Preview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי נְטִיעוֹת הָאֵלּוּ קׇרְבָּן כּוּ׳. וּלְעוֹלָם? אָמַר בַּר פְּדָא: פְּדָאָן — חוֹזְרוֹת וְקוֹדְשׁוֹת, פְּדָאָן — חוֹזְרוֹת וְקוֹדְשׁוֹת, עַד שֶׁיִּקָּצְצוּ. נִקְצְצוּ — פּוֹדָן פַּעַם אַחַת וְדַיּוֹ. וְעוּלָּא אָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּקְצְצוּ — שׁוּב אֵין פּוֹדָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that if he said: **These saplings are** like **an offering** until they are cut down, they are not subject to the possibility of redemption. The Gemara asks: **And** are they not subject to redemption **forever? Bar Padda said:** If he **redeemed them, they become consecrated again,** as they have not yet been cut down. **If** he **redeemed them** again, **they become consecrated again, until they are cut** down. Once **they are cut** down, **he redeems them once and it is sufficient. And Ulla said: Once they are cut** down one does **not** need to **redeem them again** because they are no longer consecrated.

Having established the binding character of the first clause, the *Gemara* has confirmed that these *konam* declarations — made without fixed time limits, or with conditions that happen to be satisfied — carry the attributes of *hekdesh* and therefore have *pidyon*. The remaining and more complex question — why the time-limited formulations in the second clause of the *mishnah* result in *ein lahen pidyon* — is where the analysis will continue. The assumption going forward is that we are dealing with *konam*-style *nedarim*, not literal *kedushas korban*, and that the *chiddush* of the second clause concerns the relationship between a time-bound *neder* and the preconditions for the mechanism of *pidyon* to apply at all.
