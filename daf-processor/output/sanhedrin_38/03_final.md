# Sanhedrin 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Yerushalmi

### Civil vs. Capital

We are situated in *Sanhedrin*, *perek* 4, *echad dinei mamonot*, which deals with the ten differences between *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot* — civil monetary cases and capital cases. The *mishnah* on the previous page taught how witnesses in capital cases are warned and, in effect, guilt-tripped: they are reminded of the weight of their testimony and the justice and righteousness that demands they speak the truth. The *aggadot* that follow shift the register entirely, moving from legal procedure to the cosmic stature of the human being — because if we are dealing with life and death, we must first understand what is at stake when the life of even one person hangs in the balance.

### Yerushalmi: Adam

Before entering the *Bavli* proper, it is worth surveying the *Yerushalmi*, which compresses into five and a half lines much of what the *Bavli* will expand at length. We are looking at *Yerushalmi Sanhedrin* 23b, *perek* 4, *halacha* 9, beginning with the words *keitzad me'umad*.

The *Yerushalmi* opens with the statement *adam nivra be'erev Shabbat* — the human being was created on *erev Shabbat*, Friday afternoon — *kedei sheyikanes techila lemitzvah*, so that he would immediately be able to perform a *mitzvah*. That is one reason. A second reason is offered in the form of a *mashal*: *mashal lemelech she'asa se'udah* — this is like a king who made a meal. *Keshematkin hase'udah mazmin ha'orchim* — first he sets out the meal, and only then does he invite the guests, rather than allowing the guests to witness the disorder of the preparations. The *Bavli* will expand this parable.

The *Yerushalmi* then brings a *pasuk* from *Mishlei*: *chochmot banta beita* — wisdom built her house. The *Yerushalmi*'s treatment is cryptic and fragmented, but the identification is clear: this is like God, who built the world *be-chochma*, with wisdom, as another *pasuk* confirms — *bechochma yasad aretz*. The *Yerushalmi* then reads the continuation of the *Mishlei* passage: *chatzva amudeiha shiva* — she carved her seven pillars — which alludes to *shivat yemei bereishit*, the seven days of creation. The preparations of the meal and the mixing of wine allude to *yamim veneharot ve-chol tzorchei ha'olam*, the seas, the rivers, and all the needs of the world. When the *pasuk* continues with *mi peti yasur henna* — who is the fool who will come here — that alludes to *Adam ve-Chava*.

### God's Joy

The *Yerushalmi* then takes a surprising turn and brings a *pasuk* from *Melachim* in the context of the death of *Achav*, king of *Yisrael*: *vaya'avor harina bamachane* — and the *rinah* passed through the camp. The question posed is *mahu harina* — what is the nature of this *rinah*? *Rinah* can mean joy, and the implication is that God rejoiced at the downfall of an evildoer. But the *Yerushalmi* immediately contrasts this with a *pasuk* from *Divrei HaYamim*, where *hodu la'Hashem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo* is said — *chesed* is invoked but not *tov*, joy, in the full sense. The tension between these two verses drives the conclusion: *l'lamdecha she'af mapalat ha-resha'im eina simcha lifnei ha-makom* — to teach us that even the downfall of evildoers is not truly joyful before God. This is a tension that will appear again in our *daf*.

## Sons of R. Chiya

### Wine & Secrets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה וְחִזְקִיָּה בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא הֲווֹ יָתְבִי בִּסְעוּדְתָּא קַמֵּי רַבִּי, וְלָא הֲווֹ קָא אָמְרִי וְלָא מִידֵּי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַגְבַּרוּ חַמְרָא אַדַּרְדְּקֵי כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֵימְרוּ מִילְּתָא. כֵּיוָן דְּאִיבַּסּוּם, פָּתְחוּ וַאֲמַרוּ: אֵין בֶּן דָּוִד בָּא עַד שֶׁיִּכְלוּ שְׁנֵי בָּתֵּי אָבוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: רֹאשׁ גּוֹלָה שֶׁבְּבָבֶל וְנָשִׂיא שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה לְמִקְדָּשׁ וּלְאֶבֶן נֶגֶף וּלְצוּר מִכְשׁוֹל לִשְׁנֵי בָתֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Yehuda and Ḥizkiyya, sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya, were sitting at a meal before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and they were not saying anything.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** his servants: **Add more wine for the young men, so that they will say something. Once they were inebriated, they loosened** their tongues **and said: The son of David,** i.e., the Messiah, **will not come until two fathers' houses are destroyed from Israel,** as those two families are preventing the redemption. **And they are the head of the exile who is in Babylonia,** i.e., the family of the Exilarch, **and the *Nasi* who is in Eretz Yisrael,** i.e., the family of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi (see 5a), **as it is stated** in reference to the Messiah: **"And he shall be for a sanctuary; but for a stone of stumbling and for a rock of offense to both the houses of Israel"** (Isaiah 8:14).

We now turn to the *Bavli*, beginning a few lines from the top of 38b. The discussion of the sons of *Rabbi Chiya* connects directly to the preceding material about the weight placed on testimony and the significance of a single human being. We are told about Yehuda and Chizkiya, the sons of *Rabbi Chiya*, who were sitting at a meal in the presence of *Rebbi*. They did not speak a word, so *Rebbi* instructed his servants to give them wine — *ki heichi de-limru milta* — so that they would say something. As the *Gemara* will shortly make explicit, wine has the effect of loosening speech and drawing out what lies within.

### Two Institutions

Once the wine took effect — when they became inebriated — they opened with a startling teaching: the *Mashiach* will not come until two institutions cease from the Jewish people. *Ve-elu hen*: the *resh gola she-be-Bavel* — the head of the diaspora in *Bavel* — and the *nasi she-be-Eretz Yisrael* — the *nasi* in the Land of *Yisrael*.

Now consider the situation: the *nasi* in *Eretz Yisrael* is *Rebbi* himself, seated right before them. These two young scholars, in their father's presence, with the servants and the wine, declare that the coming of *Mashiach* depends on the cessation of the very institution their host represents. The teaching is supported with a *pasuk* from *Yeshayahu* 8: *ve-haya le-mikdash u-le-even negef u-le-tzur michshol li-shnei batei Yisrael* — he will be a *mikdash*, but also an *even negef*, something that causes tripping, and a *tzur michshol*, a stumbling block, for the two houses of *Yisrael*.

The underlying tension is striking: we are waiting for the *Mashiach*, and yet the very institutions that sustain communal life in the interim — the *nasi* and the *resh gola* — are, according to this teaching, among the conditions that delay his coming. As long as these institutions are in place, the *Mashiach*'s role is, in a sense, already occupied. And yet we cannot simply relinquish what we have. The tension between living within existing structures and anticipating their transcendence is real and unresolved.

### Gematria Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: בָּנַיי, קוֹצִים אַתֶּם מְטִילִין לִי בְּעֵינַיי? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: רַבִּי, אַל יֵרַע בְּעֵינֶיךָ. ״יַיִן״ נִיתַּן בְּשִׁבְעִים אוֹתִיּוֹת, וְ״סוֹד״ נִיתַּן בְּשִׁבְעִים אוֹתִיּוֹת. נִכְנַס יַיִן – יָצָא סוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to them: My children, do you throw thorns in my eyes?** How can you say this in the presence of the *Nasi* himself? **Rabbi Ḥiyya said to him: My teacher, do not view** their behavior **in a negative** light. **Wine [*yayin*] is given in letters of seventy,** i.e., the numerical value of the letters in the word *yayin* is seventy, **and secret [*sod* ] is given in letters of seventy,** i.e., the numerical value of the letters in the word *sod* is seventy. When **wine enters, secrets emerge.**

*Rebbi* responds — and the text is ambiguous as to whether his words are a statement or a question: *banai, kotzim atem matilin li bein einai* — my sons, you are throwing thorns between my eyes. Whether he intends a declaration of pain or an incredulous challenge, the thrust is clear: this teaching stings.

*Rabbi Chiya* then intercedes on behalf of his sons with an elegant defense. *Yayin* — *yud* (10), *yud* (10), *nun* (50) — equals 70. *Sod* — *samech* (60), *vav* (6), *dalet* (4) — also equals 70. *Nichnas yayin yatza sod* — when wine enters, secrets emerge. The image is of a narrow pipe: when wine flows in, it pushes the secrets out, because there is no room for both. The defense is elegant — you gave them the wine, and the wine produced the truth. Notably, *Rebbi* does not dispute the content of the teaching. He does not argue against the statement itself; he simply receives *Rabbi Chiya*'s explanation. The *sod* of the *sod*, one might say, has been confirmed.

## God's Righteousness

### Hastening Evil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּרֵשׁ מָרִי בַּר מָר, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁקֹד ה׳ עַל הָרָעָה וַיְבִיאֶהָ עָלֵינוּ כִּי צַדִּיק ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ״? מִשּׁוּם דְּצַדִּיק ה׳ – ״וַיִּשְׁקֹד ה׳ עַל הָרָעָה וַיְבִיאֶהָ עָלֵינוּ״? אִין, צְדָקָה עָשָׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהִקְדִּים גָּלוּת צִדְקִיָּהוּ וְעוֹד גָּלוּת יְכׇנְיָה קַיֶּימֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the discussion of exile, **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Mar Ukva says, and some say** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Mari bar Mar taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And so the Lord has hastened the evil, and brought it upon us; for the Lord our God is righteous** in all His works He has done" (Daniel 9:14)? Is it **because "the Lord** our God **is righteous"** that **"the Lord has hastened the evil, and brought it upon us"?** He explains: **Yes,** because it was **a righteous** act that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, performed for the Jewish people by hastening the exile of Zedekiah while** the Jews who had been exiled in **the exile of Jeconiah,** which preceded it, **were still alive,** as the wise people among those exiled with Jeconiah were able to instruct those exiled with Zedekiah.

*Rav Chisda* cites either *Mar Ukva* or *Mari bar Mar* — the transmission is uncertain — raising the following question: *Mai de-chtiv, vayishkod Hashem al hara'ah vayevi'eha aleinu ki tzaddik Hashem Elokeinu* — what is the meaning of this verse in *Daniel*, which says that God was diligent to bring evil upon us because He is righteous? The question the *Gemara* asks is pointed: is it because of God's righteousness that He hastened evil upon us? That is a deeply counterintuitive claim — that divine justice itself is the mechanism by which suffering is accelerated. The *Gemara* answers: *in* — yes — and proceeds to illustrate with examples.

### Yechanya Exile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּגָלוּת יְכׇנְיָה: ״הֶחָרָשׁ וְהַמַּסְגֵּר אֶלֶף״. חָרָשׁ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁפּוֹתְחִין, הַכֹּל נַעֲשׂוּ כְּחֵרְשִׁין. מַסְגֵּר – כֵּיוָן שֶׁסּוֹגְרִין בַּהֲלָכָה, שׁוּב אֵין פּוֹתְחִין. וְכַמָּה הָיוּ? אֶלֶף.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is written concerning the exile of Jeconiah:** "And all the men of might, seven thousand, and **the craftsmen [*heḥarash*] and the smiths [*vehammasger*] one thousand"** (II Kings 24:16). The Gemara interprets: The term **"*ḥarash*,"** which can be read as *ḥeresh*, deaf-mute, is referring to Torah scholars, as **when they open** their mouths to teach Torah, **all** the others **become as though** they were **deaf-mutes,** as they would listen quietly to the one teaching Torah. The term **"*masger*,"** which is related to *saggur*, meaning closed, teaches that **when they close,** i.e., finish, teaching **the *halakha*, they do not reopen** the discussion, as all has been clarified. **And how many were** these Torah scholars? **One thousand.**

The first example concerns the exile of *Yechanya*. The setting is the period of the first *Beit Hamikdash*. The destruction did not occur all at once; there were three stages of exile, *galuyot*, before the final one. *Yechanya* was a king of *Yehuda* who was exiled first by Nevuchadnezzar and the Neo-Babylonians. *Tzidkiyahu* was then installed in his place, and only later was he exiled as well. This means that when the later exiles arrived in *Bavel*, the earlier ones were already there — a welcome committee, in effect. God's "hastening" of the first exile was therefore an act of *tzedakah* toward those who came later, for they arrived to find an established community.

The proof text is the *pasuk*: *hecharash vehamasger elef* — the craftsmen and the smiths, a thousand. The *Gemara* reads *charash* not as craftsman but through the root of *cheresh*, deaf: *keivan shepotchin hakol na'asu ke-chershin* — when this group opened a *Torah* text, everyone around them fell silent like the deaf, having nothing to add. *Masger*, similarly, is read through the root *sagur*, closed: *keivan she-sogrin be-halacha shuv ein potchin* — when they closed a halachic matter, no one could reopen it. And they numbered a thousand — a substantial, learned community already established in *Bavel* to receive the later exiles.

### 852 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: שֶׁהִקְדִּים שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים לִ״וְנוֹשַׁנְתֶּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says:** The righteous act that the Lord performed, referred to in the verse in Daniel, was **that He hastened** the exile by **two years of *venoshantem*.** Moses taught: "When you shall have children, and children's children, and you shall have remained long [*venoshantem*] in the land, and shall deal corruptly, and make a carved idol, the likeness of anything, and shall do evil in the sight of the Lord your God, to provoke him to anger, I call heaven and earth to witness against you this day, that you shall soon utterly perish from off the land into which you go over the Jorden to possess it; you shall not prolong your days upon it, but shall utterly be destroyed" (Deuteronomy 4:25–26). The numerical value of the letters of the word "*venoshantem*" is 852, and the Jewish people were exiled in the 850th year after entering Eretz Yisrael. Since they were exiled early, not all of the curses enumerated in the verse would be fulfilled.

*Ulla* provides a second illustration. He says: *she-hikdim shtei shanim le-noshantem* — that God subtracted two years from the calculation implied by the word *venoshantem*. The *gematria* of *venoshantem* — *tav* (400), *shin* (300), *nun* (50), *tav* (400) — wait, reckoned as the *Gemara* reckons it — yields 852. The *pasuk* in *Devarim* uses the word *venoshantem*, and the continuation of the verse implies that if Israel remained in the land for 852 years and then acted wickedly, *va-avadetem* — they would be lost. According to *Ulla*, God in His mercy brought the exile after only 850 years, so that the full 852-year threshold stipulated in the verse would never be reached, and the threatened consequence would therefore not apply. God shortened the tenure so that the curse attached to its completion could not be triggered. Lengthy discussions exist as to how exactly the 850 years are calculated — whether *Mitzrayim* and the forty years in the desert are included or not — and we will not resolve them here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, ״מְהֵרָה״ דְּמָרֵי עָלְמָא תַּמְנֵי מְאָה וְחַמְשִׁין וְתַרְתֵּין הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says: Learn from it** that **for the Master of the World,** the term **soon** is referring to **852** years, as the verse states: "You shall soon utterly perish," and the intended time until the exile was 852 years.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* adds a further point: the next *pasuk* in *Devarim* uses the word *maher* — quickly. From this, *shema mina mehera* — we understand that for *Hashem*, 852 years qualifies as quickly, as the verse teaches: *ki elef shanim be-einecha ke-yom etmol* — for God, a thousand years is like yesterday. In divine reckoning, 852 years passes in no time at all.

## Adam Created Alone

### No Partnership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָדָם יְחִידִי נִבְרָא, וּמִפְּנֵי מָה? שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מִינִים אוֹמְרִין ״הַרְבֵּה רְשׁוּיוֹת בַּשָּׁמַיִם״. דָּבָר אַחֵר: מִפְּנֵי הַצַּדִּיקִים וּמִפְּנֵי הָרְשָׁעִים, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ הַצַּדִּיקִים אוֹמְרִים ״אָנוּ בְּנֵי צַדִּיק״, וּרְשָׁעִים אוֹמְרִים ״אָנוּ בְּנֵי רָשָׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Therefore,** Adam the first man was created alone. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Adam was created alone, and for what** reason? **So that the heretics will not say: There are many authorities in Heaven,** and each created a different person. **Alternatively, Adam was created alone due to the righteous and due to the wicked.** It was **so that the righteous will not say: We are the children of the righteous,** and righteousness is natural for us, so there is no need for us to exert ourselves to be righteous, **and** so that **the wicked** will not **say: We are the children of the wicked** and cannot change our ways.

Returning now to the *mishnah* on 37a — *lefikach nivra adam yechidi* — we turn to the *midrashim* and explanations for why the human being was created alone. *Tanu rabanan*: *adam yechidi nivra* — the human was created as a single individual. *Mifnei mah?* Why? *She-lo yehu ha-minim omrim, harbeh rashuyot ba-shamayim* — so that the *apikorsim* would not say there are many powers in the heavens. If there had been two first humans, one might claim that two divine entities created them respectively.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: מִפְּנֵי הַמִּשְׁפָּחוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מִשְׁפָּחוֹת מִתְגָּרוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ. וּמָה עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁנִּבְרָא יָחִיד מִתְגָּרוֹת, נִבְרְאוּ שְׁנַיִם – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה! דָּבָר אַחֵר: מִפְּנֵי הַגַּזְלָנִין וּמִפְּנֵי הַחַמְסָנִין. וּמָה עַכְשָׁו שֶׁנִּבְרָא יְחִידִי גּוֹזְלִין וְחוֹמְסִין, נִבְרְאוּ שְׁנַיִם – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Alternatively,** he was created alone **due to the families, so that the families will not quarrel with each other,** each one boasting of the heritage of their progenitor. **And if now that** Adam **was created alone,** families still **quarrel** and each family claims superiority, if there were **two** people **created** initially, **all the more so** would they do this. **Alternatively,** he was created alone **due to the robbers and due to those who take by force** that which is not theirs, as the feeling of fraternity among all people, having descended from the same forefather, will limit crime. **And if now that** Adam **was created alone,** criminals still **rob and take by force** that which is not theirs, if there were **two** people **created** initially, **all the more so** would this be the case.

Additional reasons follow. Another explanation: because of the righteous and the evildoers. *She-lo yehu ha-tzadikim omrim anu bnei tzadik ve-reshaim omrim anu bnei rasha* — so that the righteous would not say "we are descended from the righteous one" and the wicked would not say "we are descended from the wicked one." All are descended from a single common ancestor. Another explanation: *mifnei ha-mishpachot* — because of family rivalries. *She-lo yehu mishpachot mitgarot zo be-zo* — so that families would not provoke one another. And the *Gemara* notes pointedly: even now that only one Adam was created, families still quarrel; had there been two, all the more so. Similarly: *mifnei ha-gazlanim u-mifnei ha-chamsanim* — because of thieves and robbers. Even now that Adam was created alone, people steal; in a sense, we are stealing from ourselves. Had there been two, this would have been far worse.

### Stamp Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַגִּיד גְּדוּלָּתוֹ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, לְהַגִּיד גְּדוּלָּתוֹ שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁאָדָם טוֹבֵעַ כַּמָּה מַטְבְּעוֹת בְּחוֹתָם אֶחָד וְכוּלָּן דּוֹמִין זֶה לְזֶה, אֲבָל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא טוֹבֵעַ כׇּל אָדָם בְּחוֹתָמוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן וְאֵין אֶחָד מֵהֶן דּוֹמֶה לַחֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּתְהַפֵּךְ כְּחֹמֶר חוֹתָם וְיִתְיַצְּבוּ כְּמוֹ לְבוּשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And** this serves **to tell of the greatness of** the Holy One, Blessed be He, as when a person stamps several coins with one seal, they are all similar to each other. But the supreme King of kings, the Holy One, Blessed be He, stamped all people with the seal of Adam the first man, as all are his offspring, and not one of them is similar to another. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:5): The fact that Adam the first man was created alone serves **to declare the greatness of the supreme King of kings, the Holy One, Blessed be He, as a person stamps several coins with one seal, and they are all similar to each other. But the Holy One, Blessed be He, stamps all people with the seal of Adam the first** man, **and not one of them** is **similar to another. As it is stated: "It is changed like clay under the seal and they stand as a garment"** (Job 38:14). The verse describes people as being created "under the seal," but their external appearance is different, just as garments can differ in appearance.

These reasons all concern human benefit. There is another dimension entirely — the greatness of God. *Tanu rabanan*: *le-hagid gdulato shel melech malchei ha-mlachim, ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — to proclaim the greatness of the King of kings, the Holy One, Blessed be He. *She-adam tovea kamah matbe'ot be-chotam echad ve-kulam domim zeh la-zeh* — a human being mints many coins in a single stamp and they all look identical. *Aval ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu tovea kol adam be-chotamo shel Adam ha-Rishon ve-ein echad me-hem domeh le-chavero* — but the Holy One, Blessed be He, stamps every human being from the mold of the first Adam, and yet no two are alike. The prooftext, remarkably, comes from *Iyov*: *titehafech ke-chomer chotam ve-yityatzvu ke-mo levush* — the material turns like a stamp and yet they are each clothed differently, each bearing a distinct exterior.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין פַּרְצוּפֵיהֶן דּוֹמִין זֶה לָזֶה? שֶׁלֹּא יִרְאֶה אָדָם דִּירָה נָאָה וְאִשָּׁה נָאָה וְיֹאמַר: ״שֶׁלִּי הִיא״. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיִמָּנַע מֵרְשָׁעִים אוֹרָם וּזְרוֹעַ רָמָה תִּשָּׁבֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **And for what** reason **are their faces not similar to one another?** The *baraita* answers: It is so **that a man will not see a beautiful home or a beautiful woman and say: She is mine.** If all people looked the same, no one could contradict him. **As it is stated** in the following verse: **"And from the wicked their light is withheld and the high arm shall be broken"** (Job 38:15), indicating that the reason people look different from one another is to prevent the wicked from succeeding in their plans.

One might then ask: why, specifically, do faces differ? *U-mifnei mah ein partzufin domim zeh la-zeh?* The answer: *she-lo yireh adam dirah na'ah ve-ishah na'ah ve-yomar sheli* — so that a person will not see a beautiful home or a beautiful woman and say "those are mine," mistaking another's possessions or spouse for his own. The differences in appearance are a structural safeguard against moral disorder.

### Three Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים אָדָם מִשְׁתַּנֶּה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ: בְּקוֹל, בְּמַרְאֶה, וּבְדַעַת. בְּקוֹל וּבְמַרְאֶה – מִשּׁוּם עֶרְוָה, וּבְדַעַת – מִפְּנֵי הַגַּזְלָנִין וְהַחַמְסָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir would say:** One **person is different from another in three ways: In voice, in appearance, and in thought.** The differences **in voice and appearance** are **due to a woman forbidden to him,** so that people will not exchange spouses one with another. **And** the differences **in thought** are **due to the robbers and those who take by force** that which is not theirs, as, if everyone thought in a similar way, criminals could take advantage of others because they would understand where they keep their valuables.

*Rabbi Meir* teaches: *bishlosha devarim adam nishtaneh me-chavero* — in three things a person differs from his fellow. *Bekol u-ve-mareh u-ve-da'at* — in voice, in appearance, and in mind. *Bekol u-ve-mareh mishum ervah* — voice and appearance differ so that people will not come to immorality: at night, one can identify one's spouse by voice; by day, by sight. *Rashi* notes this prevents confusion and the moral violations that could result. *U-ve-da'at mipnei ha-gazlanim ve-ha-chamsanim* — and minds differ, *Rashi* explains, because each person conceals their valuables differently and cannot read another's mind. This limitation of mutual mental transparency is, as the lecturer notes, disappointing but important.

### Gnat Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָדָם נִבְרָא בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, וּמִפְּנֵי מָה? שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מִינִים אוֹמְרִים: שׁוּתָּף הָיָה לוֹ לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּמַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית. דָּבָר אַחֵר: שֶׁאִם תָּזוּחַ דַּעְתּוֹ עָלָיו, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: יַתּוּשׁ קְדָמְךָ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית. דָּבָר אַחֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס לַמִּצְוָה מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:7): **Adam** the first man **was created on Shabbat eve** at the close of the six days of Creation. **And for what** reason was this so? **So that the heretics will not** be able to **say** that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, had a partner,** i.e., Adam, **in the acts of Creation. Alternatively,** he was created on Shabbat eve so **that if** a person **becomes haughty,** God can **say to him: The mosquito preceded you in the acts of Creation,** as you were created last. **Alternatively,** he was created on Shabbat eve **in order that he enter into the mitzva** of observing Shabbat **immediately.**

*Tanu rabanan*: *adam nivra be-erev Shabbat* — the human was created on *erev Shabbat*. *She-lo yihyu ha-minim omrim, shuttaf haya lo le-Hakadosh Baruch Hu be-ma'aseh bereishit* — so that the *apikorsim* would not say God had a partner in creation. By creating Adam last, there was no entity present to serve as a co-creator. Another reason: *she-im tazuach da'ato alav, omer lo, yatush k'damcha be-ma'aseh bereishit* — if Adam becomes *zachuach*, overly self-important, God can say to him: even the mosquito preceded you in the order of creation. The smallest creature was here before you.

### King's Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס לַסְּעוּדָה מִיָּד. מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁבְּנָהּ פַּלְטֵרִין וְשִׁיכְלְלָן, וְהִתְקִין סְעוּדָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִכְנִיס אוֹרְחִין. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חׇכְמוֹת בָּנְתָה בֵיתָהּ חָצְבָה עַמּוּדֶיהָ שִׁבְעָה. טָבְחָה טִבְחָהּ מָסְכָה יֵינָהּ אַף עָרְכָה שֻׁלְחָנָהּ. שָׁלְחָה נַעֲרֹתֶיהָ תִקְרָא עַל גַּפֵּי מְרֹמֵי קָרֶת.״
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** he was created on Shabbat eve, after all of the other creations, **in order that he enter into a feast immediately,** as the whole world was prepared for him. This is **comparable to a king of flesh and blood, who** first **built palaces [*palterin*] and improved them, and prepared a feast and afterward brought in his guests. As it is stated: "Wisdom has built her house, and she has hewn out her seven pillars. She has killed her beasts; she has mingled her wine; she has also furnished her table. She has sent forth her maidens; she calls upon the top of the highest places of the city"** (Proverbs 9:1–3).

Two further reasons parallel what we saw in the *Yerushalmi*: *k'dei sheyikanes le-mitzvah miyad* — so he would immediately be able to perform a *mitzvah* (which *Rashi* identifies as *Shabbat*); and *k'dei sheyikanes le-seudah miyad* — so he would immediately enter into a prepared meal, finding everything ready. And now the *mashal* expands: *mashal le-melech basar va-dam she-bana paltarin ve-shichlalan ve-hitkin seudah, ve-achar kach hichnis orchim* — like a human king who built a palace, improved it, set out a meal, and only then brought the guests. The world was made ready before the guest of honor arrived.

## Wisdom's House

### Seven Pillars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חׇכְמוֹת בָּנְתָה בֵיתָהּ״ – זוֹ מִידָּתוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁבָּרָא אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ בְּחׇכְמָה. ״חָצְבָה עַמּוּדֶיהָ שִׁבְעָה״ – אֵלּוּ שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי בְּרֵאשִׁית. ״טָבְחָה טִבְחָהּ מָסְכָה יֵינָהּ אַף עָרְכָה שֻׁלְחָנָהּ״ – אֵלּוּ יַמִּים וּנְהָרוֹת וְכׇל צוֹרְכֵי עוֹלָם. ״שָׁלְחָה נַעֲרֹתֶיהָ תִקְרָא״ – זֶה אָדָם וְחַוָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: **"Wisdom has built her house"; this** is referring to **the attribute of the Holy One, Blessed be He, Who created the entire world with wisdom. "She has hewn out her seven pillars"; these** pillars are referring to **the seven days of Creation,** in which the world was established. **"She has killed her beasts; she has mingled her wine; she has also furnished her table"; these** are referring to **the seas and rivers and all the necessities of the world** that were created. **"She has sent forth her maidens; she calls"; this** is referring to **Adam and Eve,** who were created at the end of Creation.

The *mashal* is now grounded in the *psukim* of *Mishlei*. The *Gemara* brings three consecutive verses: *chochmot banta beita chatzva amudeiha shiva* — wisdom built her house, she carved her seven pillars. *Tavcha tivcha mascha yeina af archa shulchana* — she slaughtered the meat, mixed the wine, and set the table. *Ve-shalcha naaroteiha tikra al gappei meromei karet* — she sent her maidens to call, from the heights of the city. Now each element is decoded. *Chochmot banta beita* — this is the wisdom with which the Holy One, Blessed be He, built the entire world: *zu midato shel ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu she-bana et kol ha'olam kulo be-chochma*. It is striking, and worth noting, that the divine act of creation is here modeled on the image of a woman who builds her house.

*Chatzva amudeiha shiva* — she carved her seven pillars — *elu shiva yemei bereishit*, these are the seven days of creation.

### Meal & Rivers

*Tavcha tivcha mascha yeina af archa shulchana* — the preparation of meat and wine and the setting of the table — *elu yamim ve-neharot ve-chol tzorchei ha'olam* — these are the seas and rivers and all the needs of the world, everything laid out in advance for the arriving guest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל גַּפֵּי מְרֹמֵי קָרֶת״ – רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״עַל גַּפֵּי״, וּכְתִיב ״עַל כִּסֵּא״. בַּתְּחִלָּה – עַל גַּפֵּי, וּלְבַסּוֹף – עַל כִּסֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** The verses continue: **"Upon the top of the highest places of the city." Rabba bar bar Ḥana raises a contradiction:** It **is written: "Upon the top of** the highest places," **and** it **is written** afterward: "For she sits at the door of her house, **on a seat** in the high places of the city" (Proverbs 9:14). Is she at the top or on a seat? He explains: **Initially,** Adam was alone **upon the top** of a high place, **and ultimately,** Adam was **on a seat** that is set for a bridegroom, when Eve was paired with him.

*Ve-shalcha naaroteiha tikra* — she sent her maidens to call — *zeh Adam ve-Chava*, this refers to *Adam ve-Chava*, the guests who are now invited into the prepared world. *Al gappei meromei karet* — from the heights of the city.

*Rabba bar Chana* then raises a challenge: one verse says *al gabei* — on high — and another says *al kisse* — on the seat or throne. The *Gemara* resolves: *be-techila al gabei u-le-vasof al kisse* — initially Adam was on high, but ultimately he ended up on the seat. Some understand the seat as the wedding seat when *Chava* is brought to him.

### Fool Tempted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִי פֶתִי יָסֻר הֵנָּה חֲסַר לֵב אָמְרָה לּוֹ״. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מִי פִּיתָּהוּ לָזֶה? אִשָּׁה אָמְרָה לוֹ. דִּכְתִיב: ״נֹאֵף אִשָּׁה חֲסַר לֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states in that passage: **"Whoever is thoughtless, let him turn in here; as for him that lacks understanding, she tells him"** (Proverbs 9:4). The Gemara explains: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Who lured this** man to sin? **The woman told him** to sin. An allusion to the interpretation that one who is lured to sin by a woman is called one "that lacks understanding" is **as it is written: "He who commits adultery with a woman lacks understanding"** (Proverbs 6:32).

The introduction of *Chava* immediately evokes a difficult *pasuk* from *Mishlei*: *mi peti yasur henna chasar lev* — who is the fool, or the one susceptible to enticement, who will come here? He lacks a heart, lacks understanding. The *Gemara* applies this to Adam: *amar ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — what tempted him? *Isha amrah lo* — the woman told him. A further verse about the *no'ef*, the adulterer, who is *chasar lev* — lacking a heart — is then invoked in comparison with Adam. The section is terse and difficult; we note it and move on.

## Adam's Composition

### Dust Gathered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן מִכׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ הוּצְבַּר עֲפָרוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גׇּלְמִי רָאוּ עֵינֶיךָ״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי ה׳ עֵינָיו מְשֹׁטְטוֹת בְּכׇל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: The dust** that served to form **Adam the first** man **was gathered from the entire world, as it is stated:** "When I was made in secret and wrought in the lowest places of the earth, **Your eyes did see my unshaped flesh"** (Psalms 139:15–16), **and** it **is written: "For the eyes of the Lord run to and fro throughout the whole earth"** (II Chronicles 16:9), indicating that this figure was formed from the whole earth, the place within the view of the Lord's eyes.

*Rabbi Meir* teaches: *adam ha-Rishon mi-kol ha'olam kulo hutzbar afaro* — the first human being's dust was gathered from the entire world. The name *adam* itself shares a root with *adamah*, earth, and the imagery here is that the very material substance of the first person was drawn from everywhere on earth. The supporting *pasuk*: *golmi rau einecha* — your eyes saw my raw material. The parenthetical note in the *Gemara* adds: *ki Hashem einav meshotetvot be-chol ha'aretz* — because God's eyes sweep over the entire world, and from the choicest places He gathered the raw material for Adam's body.

### Body & Head

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן גּוּפוֹ מִבָּבֶל, וְרֹאשׁוֹ מֵאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֵבָרָיו מִשְּׁאָר אֲרָצוֹת. עַגְבוֹתָיו, אָמַר רַב אַחָא: מֵאַקְרָא דְאַגְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Oshaya says in the name of Rav:** With regard to **Adam the first** man, **his torso** was fashioned from dust taken **from Babylonia, and his head** was fashioned from dust taken **from Eretz Yisrael,** the most important land, **and his limbs** were fashioned from dust taken **from the rest of the lands** in the world. With regard to **his buttocks, Rav Aḥa says:** They were fashioned from dust taken **from Akra De'agma,** on the outskirts of Babylonia.

*Rav Oshaya* in the name of *Rav* provides a geographic breakdown: *Adam ha-Rishon gufo mi-Bavel ve-rosho me-Eretz Yisrael ve-eivarav mi-she'ar ha-aratzot* — his body was from *Bavel*, his head from *Eretz Yisrael*, and his limbs from the other lands. *Rashi* explains the head's origin in *Eretz Yisrael* because it is the highest and most esteemed of all lands — *she-hi gvohah ve-chashuva mi-kol ha-aratzot*. The body, the material dimension, comes from *Bavel*; the head, the spiritual apex, from *Eretz Yisrael*. *Rav Acha* adds that *agvotav* — the buttocks — come from *Akra d'Agma*, one of the lakes of *Bavel*, a location mentioned in other passages as well. The human being is literally composed from the whole world.

## Twelve Hours

### Hours 1-4

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בַּר חֲנִינָא: שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁעוֹת הָוֵי הַיּוֹם. שָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה – הוּצְבַּר עֲפָרוֹ, שְׁנִיָּה – נַעֲשָׂה גּוֹלֶם, שְׁלִישִׁית – נִמְתְּחוּ אֵבָרָיו, רְבִיעִית – נִזְרְקָה בּוֹ נְשָׁמָה, חֲמִישִׁית – עָמַד עַל רַגְלָיו, שִׁשִּׁית – קָרָא שֵׁמוֹת, שְׁבִיעִית – נִזְדַּוְּוגָה לוֹ חַוָּה, שְׁמִינִית – עָלוּ לַמִּטָּה שְׁנַיִם וְיָרְדוּ אַרְבָּעָה, תְּשִׁיעִית – נִצְטַוָּוה שֶׁלֹּא לֶאֱכוֹל מִן הָאִילָן, עֲשִׂירִית – סָרַח, אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה – נִידּוֹן, שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה – נִטְרַד וְהָלַךְ לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָדָם בִּיקָר בַּל יָלִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan bar Ḥanina says: Daytime is twelve hours** long, and the day Adam the first man was created was divided as follows: In the **first hour** of the day, **his dust was gathered.** In the **second,** an undefined **figure was fashioned.** In the **third, his limbs were extended.** In the **fourth, a soul was cast into him.** In the **fifth, he stood on his legs.** In the **sixth, he called** the creatures by the **names** he gave them. In the **seventh, Eve was paired with him.** In the **eighth, they arose to the bed two, and descended four,** i.e., Cain and Abel were immediately born. In the **ninth, he was commanded not to eat of the Tree** of Knowledge. In the **tenth, he sinned.** In the **eleventh, he was judged.** In the **twelfth, he was expelled and left** the Garden of Eden, **as it is stated: "But man abides not in honor;** he is like the beasts that perish" (Psalms 49:13). Adam did not abide, i.e., sleep, in a place of honor for even one night.

*Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina* teaches: *shtem-esrei sha'ot havei ha-yom* — the day has twelve hours. The first day of Adam's existence was divided as follows. In the first hour, *hutzbar afaro* — his dust was collected. In the second, *na'asa golem* — he became a *golem*, a shapeless mass, like the stage before the creature takes final form, like dough that has received its yeast but has not yet risen. In the third hour, *nimtechu eivarav* — his limbs stretched out. In the fourth, *nizreka bo neshama* — the soul was, as it were, injected into him; the word *mazrek* in modern Hebrew means a syringe, and the image is of the soul being shot or thrown into the prepared body.

### Hours 5-8

In the fifth hour, *amad al raglav* — he stood on his feet. In the sixth, *kara shemot* — he named the animals. In the seventh, *nizdavga lo Chava* — *Chava* was paired with him. In the eighth, *alu la-mita shnayim ve-yardu arba'ah* — they went to bed as two and came down as four. Some understand this as referring to *Kayin* and his twin sister, others to *Kayin* and *Hevel*.

### Hours 9-12

In the ninth hour, *nitztave shelo le-echol min ha-ilan* — he was commanded not to eat from the tree. In the tenth, *sarach* — he sinned. In the eleventh, *nidon* — he was judged. In the twelfth, *nitrad ve-halach lo* — he was expelled and departed. The prooftext: *adam bi-yekarot bal yalin* — Adam did not sleep even one night in his place of glory. He did not remain in the Garden of Eden long enough to see a night's sleep.

## Adam Epicurus

### Adam Heretic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן מִין הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקְרָא ה׳ אֱלֹהִים אֶל הָאָדָם וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ אַיֶּכָּה״ – אָן נָטָה לִבֶּךָ? רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: מוֹשֵׁךְ בְּעׇרְלָתוֹ הָיָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְהֵמָּה כְּאָדָם עָבְרוּ בְרִית״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״אֶת בְּרִיתִי הֵפַר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Adam the first** man **was a heretic, as it is stated: "And the Lord called to the man and said to him: Where are you"?** (Genesis 3:9), meaning, to **where has your heart turned,** indicating that Adam turned from the path of truth. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: He was** one who **drew his foreskin** forward, so as to remove any indication that he was circumcised. It **is written here: "And they like men [*adam*] have transgressed the covenant"** (Hosea 6:7), **and** it **is written there:** "And the uncircumcised male who is not circumcised in the flesh of his foreskin, that soul shall be cut off from his people; **he has broken My covenant"** (Genesis 17:14).

*Rav Yehuda* says in the name of *Rav*: *adam ha-Rishon min haya* — the first human being was an *epikoros*, a heretic. How is this known?

The proof is from the verse: *vayikra Hashem Elohim el ha-Adam vayomer lo ayeka* — God called to the human and said to him, *ayeka* — where are you? One might wonder what it means for God to ask Adam where he is, since God surely knows. The *Gemara* explains that the real content of the divine question is: *an nata libcha* — where has your heart wandered? It is not a question about physical location but about attention, orientation, inner life. The fact that God had to stop Adam and ask "where are you?" implies that Adam's mind had already drifted, that the wholeness of his awareness had fractured. Already there was a split — he no longer apprehended the oneness of God in an undivided way. Something had leaked in.

*Rabbi Yitzchak* adds a further indication that Adam was a *min*: *moshech be-orlato haya* — he was pulling at his foreskin. The *Gemara*'s reasoning: Adam was created perfect, which the tradition understands as meaning he was created already circumcised. The act of pulling at the foreskin to conceal the *brit* is a gesture associated with those who sought to mask their Jewish identity. The *pasuk*: *ve-hema ke-adam avru brit* — and they, like Adam, transgressed the covenant — is paired with another verse, *et briti hefer* — he broke my covenant.

### Denied Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: כּוֹפֵר בָּעִיקָּר הָיָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״עָבְרוּ בְרִית״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״אֶת בְּרִיתִי הֵפַר״, וְאָמְרוּ: ״עַל אֲשֶׁר עָזְבוּ אֶת בְּרִית ה׳ אֱלֹהֵי אֲבוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: He was a denier of the fundamental principle** of belief in God. It **is written here:** "And they like men [*adam*] **have transgressed the covenant," and** it **is written there: "He has broken My covenant,"** and it is written in a third verse: **"And then they shall answer: Because they have forsaken the covenant of the Lord their God** and worshipped other gods and served them" (Jeremiah 22:9).

*Rav Nachman* goes further: *kofer be-ikar haya* — he denied the fundamental principle of faith. The same *psukim* are adduced, connecting the language of transgressing the covenant with the word *adam*, from which the sages infer that Adam had already harbored doubts about his covenant relationship with God.

## Heretics

### Learn Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הֱוֵי שָׁקוּד לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, וְדַע מָה שֶׁתָּשִׁיב לָאֶפִּיקוֹרוֹס. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא אֶפִּיקוֹרוֹס נׇכְרִי, אֲבָל אֶפִּיקוֹרוֹס יִשְׂרָאֵל – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּפָקַר טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there** (Avot 2:14): **Rabbi Eliezer says: Be persistent to learn Torah, and know what you will answer to the heretic [*la'apikoros*]. Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This was **taught only** with regard to **a gentile heretic, but** not with regard to **a Jewish heretic,** as one should not respond to him. **All the more so,** if one does respond **he will become more heretical.** His heresy is assumed to be intentional, and any attempt to rebut it will only cause him to reinforce his position.

This discussion of Adam's heresy opens naturally into a broader discussion of how one should respond to *apikorsim*. The *Gemara* cites a teaching from *Pirkei Avot*: *Rabbi Elazar omer, hevei shakud lilmod Torah ve-da ma she-tashiv le-apikores* — be diligent in the study of *Torah* and know what you will answer to the *apikores*. The emphasis on *Torah* is deliberate: the *apikores* does not accept the *Torah sheba'al peh*, the oral tradition, so there is no point engaging him on that ground. Instead, master the written *Torah* so thoroughly that you can meet him on the terrain he acknowledges.

### Internal Heretics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁפָּקְרוּ הַמִּינִים, תְּשׁוּבָתָן בְּצִידָּן. ״נַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם בְּצַלְמֵנוּ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּבְרָא אֱלֹהִים אֶת הָאָדָם בְּצַלְמוֹ״. ״הָבָה נֵרְדָה וְנָבְלָה שָׁם שְׂפָתָם״ – ״וַיֵּרֶד ה׳ לִרְאֹת אֶת הָעִיר וְאֶת הַמִּגְדָּל״. ״כִּי שָׁם נִגְלוּ אֵלָיו הָאֱלֹהִים״ – ״לָאֵל הָעֹנֶה אֹתִי בְּיוֹם צָרָתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Any place** in the Bible from **where the heretics** attempt to **prove their heresy,** i.e., that there is more than one god, **the response to their** claim is **alongside them,** i.e., in the immediate vicinity of the verses they cite. The verse states that God said: **"Let us make man in our image"** (Genesis 1:26), employing the plural, **but it** then **states: "And God created man in His image"** (Genesis 1:27), employing the singular. The verse states that God said: **"Come, let us go down and there confound their language"** (Genesis 11:7), but it also states: **"And the Lord came down to see the city and the tower"** (Genesis 11:5). The verse states in the plural: **"There God was revealed [*niglu*] to him** when he fled from the face of his brother" (Genesis 35:7), but it also states in the singular: **"To God Who answers [*haoneh*] me in the day of my distress"** (Genesis 35:3).

*Rabbi Yochanan* qualifies: *lo shanu ela apikores shel ovdei kochavim, aval apikores shel Yisrael, kol she-ken de-pakir tfei* — this applies to the *apikores* among the idolaters, but the *apikores* from within the Jewish people is far more argumentative. Why? Because he sat in the same classes, studied the same material, and arrived at his conclusions knowing all the answers in advance. You must therefore be genuinely prepared, not merely armed with standard responses.

*Rabbi Yochanan* adds a broader principle: *kol makom she-pakru ha-minim, teshuvatan be-tzidam* — in every place where the *minim* launch their challenges, the answer is right next to the challenge in the text itself.

### Answer Beside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי מִי גוֹי גָּדוֹל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ אֱלֹהִים קְרֹבִים אֵלָיו כַּה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ בְּכׇל קׇרְאֵנוּ אֵלָיו״. ״וּמִי כְעַמְּךָ כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹי אֶחָד בָּאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר הָלְכוּ אֱלֹהִים לִפְדּוֹת לוֹ לְעָם״. ״עַד דִּי כׇרְסָוָן רְמִיו, וְעַתִּיק יוֹמִין יְתִיב״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan cites several examples where the counterclaim is in the same verse as the claim of the heretics. The verse states: **"For what nation is there so great that has God so near to them as the Lord our God is whenever we call upon Him?"** (Deuteronomy 4:7), where the term "near" is written in plural, *kerovim*, but the term "upon Him" is written in singular. Another verse states: **"And who is like Your people, like Israel, a nation one in the earth, whom God went to redeem unto Himself for a people?"** (II Samuel 7:23), where the term "went" is written in plural, *halekhu*, but the term "Himself" is written in singular. Another verse states: "I beheld **till thrones were placed, and one that was ancient of days did sit"** (Daniel 7:9); where the term "thrones" is written in plural, *kharsavan*, but the term "sit" is written in singular.

The examples: *na'aseh adam be-tzalmenu* — let us make man in our image — the plural. Immediately the text continues: *vayivra Elohim et ha-adam be-tzalmo* — and God created the human in *his* image, singular. The challenge and the response sit side by side. Similarly: *hava nerda ve-navla sham sfatam* — let us go down and mix their language — again plural; but: *vayered Hashem lirot et ha-ir ve-et ha-migdal* — and God went down, singular. And: *ki sham niglu elav ha-Elohim* — for there *Elohim* was revealed to him, a plural construction for God. The same tension appears in *Daniel*: *ad di chorsevan* — until thrones, plural, were set — but *ve-atik yomin yetiv* — and the Ancient of Days sat, singular.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנָךְ לְמָה לִי? כִּדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עוֹשֶׂה דָּבָר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נִמְלָךְ בְּפָמַלְיָא שֶׁל מַעְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בִּגְזֵרַת עִירִין פִּתְגָמָא וּבְמֵאמַר קַדִּישִׁין שְׁאֵלְתָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **these** instances of plural words? Why does the verse employ the plural at all when referring to God? The Gemara explains: This is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, does not act unless He consults with the entourage of Above,** i.e., the angels, **as it is stated: "The matter is by the decree of the watchers, and the sentence by the word of the holy ones"** (Daniel 4:14).

The *Gemara* addresses this last case: *hanach lama li* — but why do I need this? The answer is given in the spirit of *Rabbi Yochanan*, who taught: *ein ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu oseh davar ela im ken nimlach be-famalya shel ma'ala* — God does not do anything without consulting the heavenly court, as the *pasuk* in *Daniel* states: *be-gezirat irin pitgama u-ve-ma'amar kadishin she'elta*. All the plural formulations reflect this dynamic of divine consultation, not a plurality of divine beings. This resolves the challenge in each case regarding divine actions. But the case of the thrones in *Daniel* presents a different kind of problem — not about actions but about the physical thrones themselves. How can there be two?

## Two Thrones

### Akiva: Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתִינַח כּוּלְּהִי, ״עַד דִּי כׇרְסָוָן רְמִיו״ מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לְדָוִד, דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לְדָוִד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: עֲקִיבָא, עַד מָתַי אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה שְׁכִינָה חוֹל? אֶלָּא אֶחָד לְדִין וְאֶחָד לִצְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: This **works out well for** almost **all** the verses, as they describe an action taken by God, but **what is there to say** concerning the verse: "I beheld **till thrones were placed"?** The Gemara answers: **One** throne is **for Him and one** throne is **for David,** i.e., the messiah, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One** throne is **for Him and one** throne is **for David;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yosei said to him: Akiva! Until when will you desacralize the Divine Presence** by equating God with a person? **Rather,** the correct interpretation is that both thrones are for God, as **one** throne is **for judgment and one** throne is **for righteousness.**

The *Gemara* provides the answer: *echad lo ve-echad le-David* — one throne for God and one for David. This is *divrei Rabbi Akiva* — the opinion of *Rabbi Akiva*, who holds that the two thrones mentioned in *Daniel* are one for God and one for the *Mashiach*, identified here as David. It is worth bearing in mind *Rabbi Akiva*'s well-known association with *Bar Kochba* as a messianic figure; for *Rabbi Akiva*, a throne for the *Mashiach* is not merely homiletical but carries deep practical weight.

### Yosi Challenge

*Rabbi Yosi* objects: *Akiva, ad matai ata oseh shechina chol* — Akiva, how long will you make the *shechina* mundane, *chol*? That interpretation is not acceptable. Rather, *echad le-din ve-echad le-tzedaka* — one throne is for justice and one is for righteousness. The *Gemara* then asks whether *Rabbi Akiva* accepted this rebuke or did not. It finds that elsewhere *Rabbi Akiva* is cited as agreeing with the formulation *echad le-din ve-echad le-tzedaka*, suggesting that he did in fact accept the correction.

### Footstool

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ אוֹ לָא קַבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד לְדִין וְאֶחָד לִצְדָקָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: עֲקִיבָא, מָה לְךָ אֵצֶל הַגָּדָה? כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל נְגָעִים וְאֹהָלוֹת! אֶלָּא, אֶחָד לְכִסֵּא וְאֶחָד לִשְׁרַפְרַף: כִּסֵּא לֵישֵׁב עָלָיו, שְׁרַפְרַף לַהֲדוֹם רַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Did Rabbi Akiva **accept** this explanation **from** Rabbi Yosei **or** did he **not accept it from him?** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof to the matter from what was taught in another *baraita*, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One** throne is **for judgment and one** throne is **for righteousness;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said to him: Akiva! What are you doing near,** i.e., discussing, matters of ***aggada*? Go near** tractates ***Nega'im* and *Oholot*,** which examine the complex *halakhot* of ritual purity, where your knowledge is unparalleled. **Rather,** the correct interpretation is that while both thrones are for God, **one** is **for a throne and one** is **for a stool.** There is **a throne for God to sit upon, and a stool** that serves **as His footstool.**

*Rabbi Elazar ben Azaria* then offers a third resolution: *echad le-kise ve-echad le-shrafraf* — one is the throne and one is the footstool. When God, as it were, sits at the end of the day, He sits on the chair — *kise la-shevet alav* — and rests His feet on the stool — *shrafraf le-hadom raglav*. *Rabbi Elazar ben Azaria* even addresses *Rabbi Akiva* directly: *Akiva, ma lecha etzel aggada? Kelach etzel nega'im ve-ohalot* — what business do you have with *aggadata*? Go back to your specialty in *nega'im* and *ohalot*, purity and impurity law, where you are a master. The *aggadata* is not your strong suit.

## R. Idi Expert

### Only Rav Idi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הַאי מַאן דְּיָדַע לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי לְמִינֵי כְּרַב אִידִית – לַיהְדַּר, וְאִי לָא – לָא לַיהְדַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: This one,** i.e., any person, **who knows** how **to respond to the heretics** as effectively **as Rav Idit should respond** to them, **but if** he does **not** know, he **should not respond** to them.

*Rav Nachman* now introduces a principle for the extended discussion of responses to *minim* that follows: *hai man de-yada le-ahadori le-minim ke-Rav Idi leihadar, ve-i lo, lo leihadar* — whoever knows how to respond to the *minim* as *Rav Idi* did, let him do so; and if not, let him not try. *Rav Idi* knew not merely what to answer but how to answer — in a manner that was both textually grounded and rhetorically measured. The implication is that an ill-prepared response can do more harm than silence.

## Heretic Challenges

### Metatron Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַב אִידִית, כְּתִיב: ״וְאֶל מֹשֶׁה אָמַר עֲלֵה אֶל ה׳״. ״עֲלֵה אֵלַי״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זֶהוּ מְטַטְרוֹן שֶׁשְּׁמוֹ כְּשֵׁם רַבּוֹ, דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי שְׁמִי בְּקִרְבּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **A certain heretic said to Rav Idit:** It **is written** in the verse concerning God: **"And to Moses He said: Come up to the Lord"** (Exodus 24:1). The heretic raised a question: **It should have** stated: **Come up to Me.** Rav Idit **said to him: This** term, "the Lord," in that verse **is** referring to the angel **Metatron, whose name is like the name of his Master, as it is written:** "Behold I send an angel before you to keep you in the way and to bring you to the place that I have prepared. Take heed of him and obey his voice; do not defy him; for he will not pardon your transgression, **for My name is in him"** (Exodus 23:20–21).

A *min* poses a challenge to *Rav Idi*: there is a verse, *ve-el Moshe amar, aleh el Hashem* — and to Moshe He said, go up to God. The challenge: if God is speaking, He should have said *alei elai*, come up to me. Why does God speak of Himself in the third person, saying "go up to *Hashem*"? The implication is that the entity speaking to Moshe is distinct from the entity called *Hashem* — raising the possibility of two powers.

### Metatron A

*Rav Idi* responds: *zehu Metatron she-shmo ke-shem rabo* — this is *Metatron*, whose name is like the name of his Master. The *pasuk* affirms this: *ki shmi be-kirbo* — my name is within him. *Metatron*'s *gematria* is 314, which equals the *gematria* of *Shaddai* — *Shin* (300), *Dalet* (4), *Yud* (10) — and this is how *Metatron* bears God's name within him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נִיפְלְחוּ לֵיהּ! כְּתִיב: ״אַל תַּמֵּר בּוֹ״ – אַל תְּמִירֵנִי בּוֹ. אִם כֵּן, ״לֹא יִשָּׂא לְפִשְׁעֲכֶם״ לְמָה לִּי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵימָנוּתָא בִּידַן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּפַרְוָונְקָא נָמֵי לָא קַבֵּילְנֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו אִם אֵין פָּנֶיךָ הֹלְכִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The heretic said to him: **If so,** if this angel is equated with God, **we should worship him** as we worship God. Rav Idit said to him: It **is written: "Do not defy [*tammer*] him,"** which alludes to: **Do not replace Me [*temireni*] with him.** The heretic said to him: **If so, why do I** need the clause **"For he will not pardon your transgression"?** Rav Idit **said to him: We believe that we did not accept** the angel **even as a guide [*befarvanka*]** for the journey, **as it is written: "And he said to him: If Your Presence go not with me** raise us not up from here" (Exodus 33:15). Moses told God that if God Himself does not accompany the Jewish people they do not want to travel to Eretz Yisrael.

The *min* presses: if so, *niflahu leh* — perhaps we should worship him? *Rav Idi* responds: *ktiv al tamer bo* — the verse says do not defy him. The Rabbis read the verb *tamer* not only as "defy" but as *al temireni bo* — do not replace me with him. And: *im ken lo yissa le-fish'achem lama li* — why then does the verse continue to say that he will not carry your sins? If *Metatron* were a valid intermediary, he would forgive sins. But he cannot. The conclusion: *haymanuta be-yadan ve-afilu be-farvanka nami lo kablinei* — we reject *Metatron* entirely. Not only do we not accept him as a divine being who carries our sins, but even as a guide along the way we do not accept him. As Moshe himself said: *im ein panecha holchim* — if You yourself do not go with us, we are not going. We want *Hashem* directly, not an intermediary.

### Sodom Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כְּתִיב ״וַה׳ הִמְטִיר עַל סְדֹם וְעַל עֲמֹרָה גׇּפְרִית וָאֵשׁ מֵאֵת ה׳״, ״מֵאִתּוֹ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא כּוֹבֵס: שִׁבְקֵיהּ, אֲנָא מַהְדַּרְנָא לֵיהּ. דִּכְתִיב ״וַיֹּאמֶר לֶמֶךְ לְנָשָׁיו עָדָה וְצִלָּה שְׁמַעַן קוֹלִי נְשֵׁי לֶמֶךְ״, נָשַׁיי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא מִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא הָכִי, הָכָא נָמֵי מִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא הָכִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? מִפִּירְקֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר שְׁמִיעַ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei:** It **is written: "And the Lord rained upon Sodom and upon Gomorrah brimstone and fire from the Lord** out of heaven" (Genesis 19:24). The heretic raised the question: **It should have** stated: **From Him** out of heaven. **A certain launderer said to** Rabbi Yishmael: **Leave him be; I will respond to him.** This is **as it is written: "And Lemech said to his wives: Adah and Zillah, hear my voice; wives of Lemech,** hearken to my speech" (Genesis 4:23). One can raise the question: **It should have** been written: **My wives,** and not: "Wives of Lemech." **Rather, it is** the style of **the verse** to **speak in this** manner. **Here too, it is** the style of **the verse** to **speak in this** manner. Rabbi Yishmael **said to** the launderer: **From where did you** hear **this** interpretation? The launderer **said to him: I heard it at the lecture of Rabbi Meir.**

A *min* poses a challenge to *Rabbi Yishmael* from *Rabbi Yosi*: the verse reads, *ve-Hashem himtir al Sedom ve-al Amora gofrit va-esh me-et Hashem* — and God rained down fire and brimstone on *Sedom* and *Amora* from God. The challenge is parallel to the previous one: if God is the one raining, the verse should say *me-ito* — from Him. The phrase *me-et Hashem* again implies two distinct entities.

A *koves* — a launderer, remarkably — says to *Rabbi Yishmael*: leave it, I will respond to him. And he answers with a verse from *Bereishit*: *vayomer Lemech le-nashav Ada ve-Tzila, sheman koli neshei Lemech* — Lemech said to his wives Ada and Tzila, listen to my voice, wives of Lemech. If Lemech is speaking, he should have said "my wives," *neshai*. Instead he refers to them in the third person as "wives of Lemech." *Ela mishtaye kra hachi* — this is simply how the text speaks. And the same principle applies to the verse about *Sedom*: *hacha nami mishtaye kra hachi* — here too the text speaks in this manner. That is a recognized literary pattern in *Tanach* — speaking of oneself in the third person — and it resolves the challenge.

### R. Meir Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? מִפִּירְקֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר שְׁמִיעַ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **said to** the launderer: **From where did you** hear **this** interpretation? The launderer **said to him: I heard it at the lecture of Rabbi Meir.**

*Rabbi Yishmael* is impressed that the *koves* can answer this way and asks: *minalach* — where did you learn this? The *koves* replies: *ha mi-pirkei de-Rabbi Meir shmiya li* — I have heard this from the teachings of *Rabbi Meir*. This sets up the final passage of the *daf*.

## R. Meir Parables

### Teaching Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי הֲוָה דָּרֵישׁ רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּפִירְקֵיהּ, הֲוָה דָּרֵישׁ תִּילְתָּא שְׁמַעְתָּא, תִּילְתָּא אַגָּדְתָּא, תִּילְתָּא מַתְלֵי. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת מִשְׁלוֹת שׁוּעָלִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְאָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This is **as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: When Rabbi Meir would teach his lecture he would expound one-third *halakha*, one-third *aggada*,** and **one-third parables. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Meir had,** i.e., taught, **three hundred parables of foxes, and we have only three.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* teaches: *ki hava darish Rabbi Meir be-pirkei, hava darish tilta shamata* — when *Rabbi Meir* would deliver his public discourses, a third was *halakha*, a third was *aggadata*, and a third was *meshalim* — parables.

### Fox Parables

*Rabbi Yochanan* further teaches: *shlosh me'ot mashal shu'alim hayu lo le-Rabbi Meir* — *Rabbi Meir* had 300 fox parables. He was extraordinarily rich in parabolic wisdom. And yet: *ve-anu, ein lanu ela shalosh* — and we, we possess only three of them. Three fox parables from that vast treasury of 300 have survived to us. Those three parables await us at the opening of the next *daf*, and they are, by all accounts, worth the anticipation.
