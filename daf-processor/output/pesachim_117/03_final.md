# Pesachim 117 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Suma Patur Haggadah

### Overview & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים אֵלּוּ בַּפֶּסַח לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: פֶּסַח, מַצָּה, וּמָרוֹר. פֶּסַח — עַל שׁוּם שֶׁפָּסַח הַמָּקוֹם עַל בָּתֵּי אֲבוֹתֵינוּ בְּמִצְרַיִם, [שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַאֲמַרְתֶּם זֶבַח פֶּסַח הוּא לַה׳ אֲשֶׁר פָּסַח וְגוֹ׳״].
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **these three matters on Passover has not fulfilled his obligation:** The **Paschal lamb, *matza*, and bitter herbs.** When one mentions these matters, he must elaborate and explain them: The **Paschal lamb** is brought **because the Omnipresent passed over [*pasaḥ*] the houses of our forefathers in Egypt, as it is stated: "That you shall say: It is the sacrifice of the Lord's Paschal offering for He passed over** the houses of the children of Israel in Egypt, when he smote the Egyptians, and delivered our houses" (Exodus 12:27).

Daf 117b picks up near the conclusion of the *haggadah* section of the *seder*, in the passage preceding *motzi matzah*. The immediate context is the discussion flowing from Rabban Gamliel's famous ruling — *kol shelo amar sheloshah devarim eilu b'Pesach* — that whoever has not articulated the meaning of the three ritual objects has not fulfilled his obligation. The Gemara has established that the *seder* plate is placed before every participant, the foods integrating themselves into the very structure of the narrative. The child asks *ki yishalcha bincha machar l'amor mah zot*, questions rooted in the ritual foods, and the response culminates in *pesach, matzah, maror*, tying the foods and their symbolism into the act of telling. With this backdrop firmly in place, the Gemara opens a fascinating discussion about the precise contours of the *mitzvah* of *haggadah*.

### Suma Patur Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: סוֹמֵא פָּטוּר מִלּוֹמַר הַגָּדָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״בַּעֲבוּר זֶה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״בְּנֵנוּ זֶה״. מַה לְּהַלָּן — פְּרָט לְסוֹמֵא, אַף כָּאן — פְּרָט לְסוֹמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: A blind** person **is exempt from reciting** the **Haggadah.** The proof is that **it is written here,** with regard to the Paschal lamb: "And you shall tell your son on that day saying, **it is because of this** which the Lord did for me when I came forth out of Egypt" (Exodus 13:8), **and it was written there,** with regard to the stubborn and rebellious son, that his parents say: **"This son of ours** is stubborn and rebellious, he does not listen to our voice; he is a glutton, and a drunkard" (Deuteronomy 21:20). The Gemara explains the verbal analogy of the word "this": **Just as there,** in the case of the rebellious son, the Sages expound that the verse **excludes a blind** person, as a blind parent cannot say: This son of ours, for he cannot point to him; **so too here,** in the case of the recitation of the Passover Haggadah, the word "this" **excludes blind** people.

Approximately three lines into the Gemara's new sugya — on 117b, beginning with the words *ella she'nireh koach kadshecha bachutz* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov makes a striking statement: *suma patur mi'lomar haggadah*, a blind person is exempt from reciting the *haggadah*.

### Ba'avur Zeh Tziyun

The textual basis for this ruling is the phrase *ba'avur zeh*, which appears in the verse *v'higadta l'vincha bayom hahu leimor ba'avur zeh asah Hashem li b'tzeisi miMitzrayim*. Chazal understand this phrase as the foundation of the entire *haggadah* obligation: *ba'avur zeh lo amarti ella b'shaas she'yesh matzah u'maror munachim lefanecha* — one recites the *haggadah* only when *matzah* and *maror* lie before him. The telling of the story is understood to be constitutively tied to the ritual foods themselves.

### Ben Sorer Comparison

The derivation proceeds by means of a *gezerah shavah*-like comparison to the laws of the *ben soreir u'moreh*. In that context, the verse says *bneinu zeh* — "this our son." Had the Torah wished simply to say "our son," it could have written *baneinu hu*. The extra word *zeh* teaches, as an accepted *drasha*, that both parents must be able to directly point to and see the child; blind parents are therefore excluded from bringing the case of the *ben soreir u'moreh*. The word *zeh* implies a direct, visual identification. Rav Acha bar Yaakov imports this logic: just as *zeh* in that context excludes the blind because they cannot directly perceive the referent, so too *ba'avur zeh* in the *haggadah* context requires a direct relationship to the ritual foods — and although a blind person relates to *matzah* and *maror* through touch and taste, the *zeh* of *ba'avur zeh* is understood to require visual engagement with the objects. Hence, *suma patur*.

It is worth noting, as the Gemara itself implicitly signals, that the motivation in the *ben soreir u'moreh* case was clearly to limit and ultimately eliminate the category. No such motivation operates here — the comparison is purely textual.

### Mareimar Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר מָרִימָר, שְׁאֵלְתִּינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאן דְּאָמַר אַגָּדְתָּא בֵּי רַב יוֹסֵף? אֲמַרוּ: רַב יוֹסֵף. מַאן דְּאָמַר אַגָּדְתָּא בֵּי רַב שֵׁשֶׁת? אָמְרוּ: רַב שֵׁשֶׁת. קָסָבְרִי רַבָּנַן, מַצָּה בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Mareimar say: I asked the Sages from the school of Rav Yosef,** who was blind: **Who recited** the **Haggadah in the house of Rav Yosef? They said** to him: **Rav Yosef** himself recited it. Mareimar subsequently asked: **Who recited** the **Haggadah in the house of Rav Sheshet,** who was also blind? **They said** to him: **Rav Sheshet** himself recited it. This indicates that a blind person is obligated to recite the Haggadah. The Gemara answers: **These Sages,** Rav Yosef and Rav Sheshet, **maintain** that **nowadays** the *halakhot* of eating ***matza*** and the recitation of the Haggadah that accompanies it apply **by rabbinic** law. For this reason, blind people can recite the Haggadah for others.

The Gemara immediately challenges this ruling: *inei?* Is it really true? Mareimar reports that he asked the students in the academy of Rav Yosef — *man de'amar haggadah bei Rav Yosef* — who was it that actually recited the *haggadah* there? The answer: Rav Yosef himself. And Rav Yosef was blind. Similarly, in the academy of Rav Sheshet — also blind — it was Rav Sheshet himself who recited the *haggadah*. How, then, can the ruling of Rav Acha bar Yaakov stand?

### One Reciter Model

What this account incidentally reveals is that at these *sederim* there was a single reciter of the *haggadah*. This is notable and worth pausing on. While we have discussed previously how the *seder* is structured as a dialogue — a child asks and a father answers, a wife asks and a husband answers — the Gemara makes clear that one person was effectively the narrator. Everyone else plays their part by asking and by listening, while one figure carries the *sippur yetzias Mitzrayim*.

This raises a genuine question about the nature of the *mitzvah*. If I am the one asking and you are the one telling me the answer, am I participating in *sippur yetzias Mitzrayim*? The fuller model suggested by the Gemara is not simply that each person contributes their part to a collective telling, but rather that the principle of *shomea k'oneh* — that listening is halachically equivalent to speaking — operates here. The reciter recites; the listeners hear and are thereby considered as if they themselves have recited. That appears to be the structural understanding the Gemara is operating with.

The difficulty, then, is sharp: if a blind person is exempt from reciting the *haggadah*, how could Rav Yosef and Rav Sheshet — who were blind — have served as the *motzi* for an entire household of sighted people who were themselves obligated? One who is exempt cannot discharge the obligation of one who is obligated.

### Matzah Status Debate

The Gemara introduces a resolution by way of Rav Sheshet's own view: *kasavar rabanan matzah bizman hazeh d'rabanan*. Those sages held that the obligation to eat *matzah* in the present era is only rabbinic in force, since *matzah* and *maror* are in the Torah linked to the *Korban Pesach*, and in the absence of the *korban* some hold the obligation reduces to a rabbinic one. If *matzah* nowadays is rabbinic, then *haggadah* — which is pegged to *matzah* through *ba'avur zeh* — is likewise rabbinic. Following this reasoning, virtually everything done on *seder* night in our era would be rabbinic in origin; the only candidates for biblical standing might be *kiddush* and *birchas hamazon*.

### Rav Acha's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב סָבַר מַצָּה בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? וְהָא רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב הוּא דְּאָמַר: מַצָּה בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה דְּרַבָּנַן! קָסָבַר: כֹּל דְּתַקּוּן רַבָּנַן — כְּעֵין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא תַּיקּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does this prove **by inference** that **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov maintains** that eating ***matza* nowadays** applies **by Torah** law? **But isn't Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov the one who said** that eating ***matza* nowadays** applies **by rabbinic** law? Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov apparently contradicts himself. The Gemara answers: Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov **maintains** that **everything the Sages instituted** through their decrees, **they instituted similar** to the model established **by Torah** law. In other words, although the obligations to eat *matza* and recite the Haggadah are rabbinic, the stringencies and restrictions that apply to Torah mitzvot apply here as well. Therefore, a blind person is exempt from reciting the Haggadah.

The Gemara then turns to a sharp observation: the implication of Rav Sheshet's response is that Rav Acha bar Yaakov, who holds a blind person is *patur*, must hold that *matzah* nowadays is biblical — since his entire *derasha* only works if *haggadah* has biblical standing. But the Gemara notes a complication: *veha Rav Achai bar Yaakov hu d'amar matzah bizman hazeh d'rabanan* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov himself is on record holding that *matzah* nowadays is rabbinic! How then can he maintain that a blind person is exempt?

The resolution: *kasavar kol d'tikon rabanan k'ein d'oraisa tikun*. Even if the obligation is rabbinic, the rabbis patterned their rabbinic institutions after the structure of the biblical ones. Since biblically a blind person would be exempt, the rabbis extended that exemption to their rabbinic legislation as well. Rav Acha bar Yaakov's position is internally consistent: he holds *matzah* is rabbinic, yet the rabbinic framework replicates the biblical structure, and therefore a blind person remains exempt.

The Gemara then asks whether Rav Sheshet and Rav Yosef also accept this principle of *kol d'tikon rabanan k'ein d'oraisa tikun*. If they do, then even on a rabbinic level, a blind person should have been exempt — and they should not have served as the *motzi* for their households. The Gemara's answer is that they in fact reject the entire *derasha* of Rav Acha bar Yaakov.

### Ba'avur Zeh Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, מִדַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִיכְתַּב ״בְּנֵנוּ הוּא״, וּכְתִיב ״בְּנֵנוּ זֶה״ — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: פְּרָט לְסוֹמִין הוּא דַּאֲתָא. אֲבָל הָכָא, אִי לָאו ״בַּעֲבוּר זֶה״, מַאי לִכְתּוֹב? אֶלָּא בַּעֲבוּור מַצָּה וּמָרוֹר הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this difficulty: **How can** these cases, the verses dealing with rebellious son and the Passover Haggadah, **be compared? Granted, there,** in the case of the rebellious son, **as** the verse **could have written: He is our son, and** instead **it is written: "This son of ours,"** I can **learn from it** that the parents must point with a finger to their son, **which comes to exclude blind** parents. **However, here,** if the verse did **not** use the phrase **"because of this," what** could it have **written** in reference to *matzot* and bitter herbs? **Rather,** this verse **comes because of the *matza* and bitter herbs.** Consequently, there is no need to actually point with one's finger in this instance, and therefore the blind are also obligated to recite the Haggadah.

The Gemara proceeds to dismantle the comparison to *ben soreir u'moreh*. *Bishlama hasam* — in the case of *ben soreir u'moreh* there is good reason to read the extra *zeh*: the verse could have written simply *baneinu hu*, yet it chose *baneinu zeh*. The superfluous *zeh* teaches that the parents must be able to point directly, which implies visual capacity. *Shema mina prat le'sumin* — the extra word comes specifically to exclude the blind.

But *aval hacha* — here, by *ba'avur zeh*, what else could the verse have written? The sentence wishes to express that the telling is occasioned by, and connected to, the ritual foods. The word *zeh* is not superfluous at all; it is required to identify the referent of *ba'avur*. One might not even know what *zeh* refers to without Chazal's interpretation — *v'higadta l'vincha bayom hahu leimor ba'avur zeh asah Hashem li b'tzeisi miMitzrayim* — what is the *zeh*? Chazal tell us: the *matzah* and the *maror*. But the word *zeh* was absolutely necessary to point to *something*; it was not added to exclude anyone. Therefore *ela ba'avur matzah u'maror d'asit* — the verse comes to link the *haggadah* to the *matzah* and *maror*, and the word *zeh* carries no exclusionary implication. The comparison to *ben soreir u'moreh* collapses, and with it the ruling that *suma patur*.

One might ask: why doesn't *ba'avur zeh* refer to the *Korban Pesach* rather than to *matzah* and *maror*? In the context of the surrounding *pesukim* — which speak of eating *matzah* for seven days and the prohibition of *chametz* — the *ba'avur zeh* is most naturally read in connection with *matzah*, and Chazal's reading reflects that contextual anchor.

### Haggadah Food Link

What emerges from this entire discussion is a set of important conclusions. First, there is near-universal agreement that if *matzah* were merely rabbinic, *haggadah* would be rabbinic as well. The obligation to recite *haggadah* on that particular night derives entirely from *ba'avur zeh*, which ties the telling to the ritual foods being present. Without a biblical obligation in *matzah*, there is no biblical *haggadah*. The entire reason *haggadah* is *d'oraisa* is because *matzah* is *d'oraisa*.

Second, Rabban Gamliel's statement — *kol shelo amar sheloshah devarim eilu b'Pesach, lo yatza yedei chovato* — reflects a tight, constitutive integration of *haggadah* with the ritual foods. Some *rishonim* take this literally: if one does not articulate the significance of the *pesach*, *matzah*, and *maror*, one has not fulfilled the obligation. Others say that while *b'di'eved* one may have fulfilled it, the fundamental conception of the *mitzvah* demands this integration. The *she'eilos* of the *seder* are questions about the foods; the answers are about the foods; the very timing of the obligation is derived from the presence of the foods. The Gemara considers and then rejects the possibility that a blind person — unable to see those foods — cannot fulfill the obligation, but the tight linkage between *haggadah* and *achilah* remains firmly established.

Third, the structure of the *seder* as one person reciting while others listen — with *shomea k'oneh* discharging the obligation of the listeners — is clearly reflected in the practice of Rav Yosef and Rav Sheshet.

## Hallelujah Etymology

### Hallel Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ אֲנַחְנוּ חַיָּיבִים לְהוֹדוֹת, לְהַלֵּל, לְשַׁבֵּחַ, לְפָאֵר, לְרוֹמֵם, לְהַדֵּר, לְבָרֵךְ, לְעַלֵּה וּלְקַלֵּס לְמִי שֶׁעָשָׂה לַאֲבוֹתֵינוּ וְלָנוּ אֶת כׇּל הַנִּסִּים הָאֵלּוּ. הוֹצִיאָנוּ מֵעַבְדוּת לְחֵרוּת, מִיָּגוֹן לְשִׂמְחָה, וּמֵאֵבֶל לְיוֹם טוֹב, וּמֵאֲפֵלָה לְאוֹר גָּדוֹל, וּמִשִּׁעְבּוּד לִגְאוּלָּה. וְנֹאמַר לְפָנָיו הַלְלוּיָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues with the text of the Haggadah. **Therefore we are obligated to thank, praise, glorify, extol, exalt, honor, bless, revere, and laud [*lekales*] the One who performed for our forefathers and for us all these miracles: He took us out from slavery to freedom, from sorrow to joy, from mourning to a Festival, from darkness to a great light, and from enslavement to redemption. And we will say before Him: *Halleluya*.** At this point one recites the *hallel* that is said on all joyous days.

The Mishnah proceeds to the phrase *v'nomar lefanav shira chadasha Hallelujah*, and the word *hallelujah* itself becomes the occasion for a brief but analytically rich digression into etymology and textual structure.

### One Word Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ וְ״כֵסְיָהּ״ וִ״ידִידְיָה״ — אַחַת הֵן. רַב אָמַר: ״כֵּסְיָהּ״ וּ״מֶרְחַבְיָהּ״ — אַחַת הֵן. רַבָּה אָמַר: ״מֶרְחַבְיָהּ״ בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the meaning of this term. **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The word ***halleluya*** and the word ***kesya*** (Exodus 17:16) and the name **Yedidya** (II Samuel 12:25) **are** each regarded as **a single** word, not an amalgamation of two smaller words, i.e., *Hallelu*-*ya*. **Rav said** that ***kesya* and *merḥavya*** (Psalms 118:5) **are single** words. **Rabba said: Only *merḥavya*** is a single word; the others are two words.

Rav Yochanan's question concerns whether *hallelujah* and related compound expressions — *kes Yah* (from *ki yad al kes Yah milchama l'Hashem b'Amalek*), and *Yedidyah* (one of the names of Shlomo) — are each one word or two. The question has practical halakhic implications: if *Yah* stands alone as a separate word, it constitutes a divine name and is therefore *kodesh*, affecting how such texts may be handled and erased.

### Rav Rabba Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״מֶרְחָב יָהּ״ לְרַב חִסְדָּא, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״יְדִידְיָהּ״ לְרַב, מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב: ״יְדִידְיָהּ״ נֶחְלָק לִשְׁנַיִם, לְפִיכָךְ: יְדִיד חוֹל, יָהּ קוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: According to the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda, what is** the status of ***merḥavya*?** Is it counted as two words or one? This dilemma was raised because Rav Ḥisda himself did not mention this term. No answer was found for this dilemma, and therefore the Gemara states: **Let it stand** unresolved. Another **dilemma was raised before** the Sages: According to the opinion of **Rav, what is** the status of the name **Yedidya?** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear, as Rav said** explicitly: **Yedidya is divided into two** separate names. **Therefore, *yedid*** is **a mundane** word, whereas ***ya*** is **a sacred** name, which must be treated respectfully like the other sacred names of God.

Rav holds that *kes Yah* and *Merchavyah* are each a single word, leaving *hallelujah* and *Yedidyah* unaddressed. Rabba takes the more restrictive position that only *Merchavyah* is a single word; all the others are two words, each with *Yah* as an independent divine name. The Gemara then notes that Rav Chisda's position on *Merchavyah* is left unresolved — *teiku*. As for what Rav would say about *Yedidyah*, the Gemara brings a proof: Rav explicitly states that *Yedidyah* is divided into two — *yedid chol, Yah kodesh* — meaning the *Yah* component is a separate, sacred divine name. The implication for our *davening* is clear: *aneini b'merchav Yah* in *Hallel* — whether *Merchavyah* there is one word or two has direct consequences for how the word is treated.

### Intensifier Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ לְרַב, מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב: חֲזֵינָא תִּילֵּי דְּבֵי חַבִּיבָא דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ ״הַלְלוּ״ בְּחַד גִּיסָא, וְ״יָהּ״ בְּחַד גִּיסָא. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַאי ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״? הַלְּלוּהוּ בְּהִלּוּלִים הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **According to** the opinion of **Rav, what is** the status of ***halleluya*?** Is it one word or two? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear, as Rav said: I saw a book of Psalms in the study hall of my uncle,** Rabbi Ḥiyya, **in** which the word ***hallelu* is written on one side,** at the end of a line, **and *ya*** was written **on one side,** at the beginning of the next line. This shows that the word *halleluya* can indeed be split in two. The Gemara comments: This opinion **disputes** that **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: What is** the meaning of the word ***halleluya*?** It means **praise Him [*halleluhu*] with many praises [*hillulim*].** According to this opinion, the *ya* at the end of the word is a superlative, not a divine name.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi takes a different analytical approach entirely. He asks *mai Hallelujah?* and answers: *hallulhu b'hillulim harbeh* — praise God with many praises. On this reading, the *Yah* suffix is not functioning as the divine name at all but rather as an intensifier, denoting abundance or superlative degree. Even if *hallelujah* is formally one word, its *Yah* component might simply amplify the praise rather than explicitly invoking God's name.

### God's Name Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ופְלִיגָא דִּידֵיהּ אַדִּידֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בַּעֲשָׂרָה מַאֲמָרוֹת שֶׁל שֶׁבַח נֶאֱמַר סֵפֶר תְּהִלִּים: בְּ״נִיצּוּחַ״, בְּ״נִגּוּן״, בְּ״מַשְׂכִּיל״, בְּ״מִזְמוֹר״, בְּ״שִׁיר״, בְּ״אַשְׁרֵי״, בִּ״תְהִלָּה״, בִּ״תְפִלָּה״, בְּ״הוֹדָאָה״, בְּ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״. גָּדוֹל מִכּוּלָּן ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״, שֶׁכּוֹלֵל שֵׁם וָשֶׁבַח בְּבַת אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: This statement **of** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **disagrees with** another ruling that he **himself** issued, **as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: The book of Psalms is said by** means of **ten expressions of praise: By *nitzuaḥ*, *niggun*, *maskil*, *mizmor*, *shir*, *ashrei*, *tehilla*, *tefilla*, *hoda'a*,** and ***halleluya*.** He continues: **The greatest of them all** is ***halleluya*, as it includes** God's **name and praise at one time.** This statement indicates that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi considers *halleluya* to be a combination of two words, one of which is the name of God.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi then adds a further observation that argues on his own first position. He teaches that *Sefer Tehillim* employs ten distinct expressions of praise — among them *nitzuach*, *nigun*, *maskil*, *mizmor*, *shir*, *ashrei*, *tehillah*, *tefillah*, *hodaah*, and *hallelujah*. Of all these, *hallelujah* is the greatest: *shekolel shem v'shevach b'vas achas* — it contains both God's name and the act of praise within a single word. On this reading, *hallelujah* is one word, but the *Yah* within it does mean God — it is not merely an intensifier. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi thus appears to argue with himself: in one statement the *Yah* is an intensifier; in this statement it is the divine name embedded within praise.

## Hallel Origins

### Prophets Tikun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שִׁיר שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, מֹשֶׁה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָלוּ מִן הַיָּם. וְהַלֵּל זֶה מִי אֲמָרוֹ? נְבִיאִים שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶן תִּקְּנוּ לָהֶן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיְּהוּ אוֹמְרִין אוֹתוֹ עַל כׇּל פֶּרֶק וּפֶרֶק, וְעַל כׇּל צָרָה וְצָרָה שֶׁלֹּא תָּבֹא עֲלֵיהֶן. וְלִכְשֶׁנִּגְאָלִין, אוֹמְרִים אוֹתוֹ עַל גְּאוּלָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The song in the Torah,** i.e., the Song at the Sea (Exodus 15:1–19), **Moses and the Jewish people recited it when they ascended from the sea.** The Gemara asks: **And who said this *hallel*** mentioned in the mishna, Psalms 113–118? The Gemara answers: The **Prophets among them established** this hallel **for the Jewish people, that they** should **recite it on every** appropriate **occasion; and for every trouble, may it not come upon them,** they recite the supplications included in *hallel*. **When they are redeemed, they recite it over their redemption,** as *hallel* includes expressions of gratitude for the redemption.

The Gemara now addresses the historical origins of *Hallel* as a liturgical institution. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel states that the prophets among the people established for Israel that they would recite *Hallel* on the occasions appropriate to it.

### Three Contexts

Three distinct contexts are identified: *al kol perek v'perek* — at every designated festival season, which the Rashbam interprets as *yom tov*; *al kol tzarah v'tzarah shelo tavo aleihem* — God willing it should never happen, but at every time of affliction and danger; and *u'kshe'nigalin, omrim oso al geulasan* — when they were redeemed, they would say it upon their redemption. The parenthetical *shelo tavo aleihem* is exactly that — parenthetical, expressing the hope that such affliction never occur; the operative point is that at the moment of redemption from affliction, this is what was recited. This framework would encompass events like *Chanukah*, and perhaps other moments of national deliverance.

### David Authorship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל תּוּשְׁבָּחוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בְּסֵפֶר תְּהִלִּים כֻּלָּן דָּוִד אֲמָרָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּלּוּ תְפִלּוֹת דָּוִד בֶּן יִשָׁי״ — אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״כׇּלּוּ״, אֶלָּא ״כׇּל אֵלּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: All the praises stated in the book of Psalms were recited by David, as it is stated: "The prayers of David, son of Yishai, are ended [*kalu*]"** (Psalms 72:20). **Do not read *kalu*; rather,** read ***kol elu*, all of these,** which indicates that the entire book of Psalms consists of the prayers of King David.

A *baraisa* records Rabbi Meir's view: *kol tushbachos ha'amuros b'sefer Tehillim, kulam David amram* — every single psalm in the Book of Psalms was composed by David. The basis is a *drasha* on the verse *kalu tefillos David ben Yishai*: read not *kalu* (ceased) but *kol elu* (all these), meaning everything in the book — even those psalms headed by other names such as *tefillah le'Moshe* or *livnei Korach mizmor* — was composed by David, though in the voices or names of those figures.

### Moshe at Sea

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַלֵּל זֶה מִי אֲמָרוֹ? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר, אֶלְעָזָר בְּנִי אוֹמֵר: מֹשֶׁה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָלוּ מִן הַיָּם. וַחֲלוּקִין עָלָיו חֲבֵירָיו לוֹמַר שֶׁדָּוִד אֲמָרוֹ. וְנִרְאִין דְּבָרָיו מִדִּבְרֵיהֶן. אֶפְשָׁר יִשְׂרָאֵל שָׁחֲטוּ אֶת פִּסְחֵיהֶם וְנָטְלוּ לוּלְבֵיהֶם וְלֹא אָמְרוּ שִׁירָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: According to those who dispute Rabbi Meir's claim that the entire book of Psalms was composed by King David, **who recited this *hallel*? Rabbi Yosei says: My son Elazar says** that **Moses and the Jewish people recited it when they ascended from the sea. And his colleagues dispute him, saying that it was recited by** King **David. And the statement of** my son, Elazar, **appears** more accurate **than their statement.** The reason is as follows: Is it **possible that the Jewish people slaughtered their Paschal lambs and took** and waved **their *lulavim*** all those generations **without reciting a song?** Rather, the Jews must have recited a song each year. Since it is the custom to sing *hallel* nowadays, it is evidently an ancient institution.

Given that attribution, the Gemara presses: *hallel zeh mi amaro?* Who actually composed and first recited *Hallel*? Rabbi Yose reports in the name of his son Elazar that it was *Moshe v'Yisrael* who said it *b'sha'ah she'alu min ha'yam* — when they came up from the Sea of Reeds. This stands somewhat in tension with Rabbi Meir's view, but establishes the crucial point that *Hallel* predates David. His colleagues challenge this, preferring the simpler assumption that David composed everything — but Rabbi Yose defends the antiquity of *Hallel* with two arguments.

### Necessity Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: פִּסְלוֹ שֶׁל מִיכָה עוֹמֵד בִּבְכִי, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹמְרִים אֶת הַהַלֵּל?
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** is it possible that **Micah's idol stood in tears, and the Jewish people** were **reciting *hallel*** before it? The reference is to the idol of Micah, which was still standing in the days of David (see Judges 17). The Gemara states that the idol was crying, as a euphemism for its laughter, to avoid shaming the Jewish people (*ge'onim*). The point is that the Jews would not have chanted: "They who make them shall be like them" (Psalms 115:8) at a time that they were worshipping idols. Rather, *hallel* must be older than that, and it dates back to the Song at the Sea.

The first argument is a logical one: *efshar Yisrael shachtu et pischeihen v'natlu lulaveihen v'lo amru shirah?* Is it conceivable that Israel, throughout all the centuries before David, slaughtered their *Korban Pesach* and took their *lulav* and uttered no song of praise? Obviously, they must have been praising God at those moments, and if so, they must have been doing so with some established text — and this is evidently the text of *Hallel*. Hence *Hallel* must predate David.

A second, more striking, argument is cited: the idol of Michah was present and active — the *Rashbam* explains this as a euphemistic way of saying it stood in the period leading up to and overlapping with David's time — yet Israel was simultaneously reciting *Hallel*, which itself contains the verse *einayim lahem v'lo yiru, oznayim lahem v'lo yishme'u* — mocking those who have eyes but cannot see, ears but cannot hear. This implies *Hallel* was composed at a time when *avodah zarah* was a live reality, perhaps in the brief interval between *yetziat Mitzrayim* and the sin of the *eigel*. The text must have been ancient. These arguments are striking, though it is worth noting that they do not strictly *require* this specific text — they establish that *some* form of praise must have been ancient, and the *Hallel* text we possess is then assumed to be that ancient form.

### Ramban Defense

This discussion about the antiquity and nature of *Hallel* connects to a significant dispute among the *rishonim* regarding whether *Hallel* is *d'oraisa* or *d'rabanan*. The Rambam opens his *Sefer Hamitzvos* with fourteen principles for determining which commandments count as biblical. The first is that only biblical commandments are to be enumerated, not rabbinic ones. Among his critiques of the Ba'hag's list is the inclusion of *Hallel*: since the psalms that comprise it were written by David, how can *Hallel* be biblical?

The Ramban, in his *Hassagos* on *Sefer Hamitzvos*, mounts a sustained defense of the Ba'hag. He first challenges the Rambam's logic: the Rambam himself holds that *tefillah* — daily prayer — is a biblical obligation, yet the specific text of *tefillah* was not composed until the Men of the Great Assembly, long after the biblical period. Similarly, *benshen* is *d'oraisa* even though its particular text came much later. One can therefore have a biblical obligation without that obligation having been expressed in its current textual form from Sinai. The same argument applies to *Hallel*: the obligation could be biblical even if the specific psalms were composed by David.

The Ramban then adds that the Gemara in *Pesachim* itself attests to multiple traditions holding *Hallel* to be ancient — indeed, the Gemara's reasoning that Israel *must* have been praising God at *Pesach* and *lulav* supports the idea that this was never merely an institution invented late in the rabbinic period.

The Ramban proposes that the biblical basis for *Hallel* might be either a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* or — more suggestively — that it is subsumed within the biblical *mitzvah* of *simchas yom tov*. The verse *v'samachta l'fnei Hashem* implies that *simchah* before God is not merely physical — not just eating *basar u'shlamim* — but includes the spiritual dimension of praise and song in God's presence. On this reading, saying *Hallel* on *yom tov* is part of the fulfillment of *simchas yom tov* itself. This aligns with the earlier Gemara's framework connecting *Hallel* to *yom tov*, to redemption, and to the specific acts of taking *lulav* and slaughtering the *Korban Pesach* — all moments of heightened encounter with the divine presence.

## Inspiration Mood

### Simcha Shel Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְדָוִד מִזְמוֹר״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁשָּׁרְתָה עָלָיו שְׁכִינָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ אָמַר שִׁירָה. ״מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאָמַר שִׁירָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁרְתָה עָלָיו שְׁכִינָה. לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁאֵין הַשְּׁכִינָה שׁוֹרָה, לֹא מִתּוֹךְ עַצְלוּת וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ עַצְבוּת וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ שְׂחוֹק וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ קַלּוּת רֹאשׁ וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ דְּבָרִים בְּטֵלִים, אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ דְּבַר שִׂמְחָה שֶׁל מִצְוָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעַתָּה קְחוּ לִי מְנַגֵּן וְהָיָה כְּנַגֵּן הַמְנַגֵּן וַתְּהִי עָלָיו יַד ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the book of Psalms. If a psalm begins: **Of David a psalm,** this **teaches that the Divine Presence rested upon him** first **and afterward he recited** the **song.** However, if a psalm opens with: **A psalm of David,** this **teaches that he** first **recited** the **song, and afterward the Divine Presence rested upon him.** The Gemara adds: Incidentally, this serves **to teach you that the Divine Presence rests** upon an individual **neither from** an atmosphere of **laziness,** nor **from** an atmosphere of **sadness, nor from** an atmosphere of **laughter, nor from** an atmosphere of **frivolity, nor from** an atmosphere of **idle chatter, but rather from** an atmosphere imbued with **the joy of a mitzva. As it is stated** with regard to Elisha, after he became angry at the king of Israel, his prophetic spirit left him until he requested: **"But now bring me a minstrel; and it came to pass when the minstrel played, that the hand of the Lord came upon him"** (II Kings 3:15).

The Gemara derives from the contrast between *mizmor l'David* and *l'David mizmor* a fundamental principle about the conditions for divine inspiration. When a psalm is headed *l'David mizmor* — David's name precedes the song — this teaches that the *shechina* rested upon him first, and he then sang. When it is *mizmor l'David* — the song precedes — this teaches that he sang first, and the *shechina* rested upon him afterward.

Why would God wait? *She'eina shechina shora lo mitoch atzlut v'lo mitoch atzvut* — the *shechina* does not rest upon a person when they are lazy, nor when they are sad, nor when they are frivolous (*sechok*), nor when they are engaged in empty levity (*kalut rosh*), nor when they are occupied with trivial matters (*devarim betelim*). Rather, divine inspiration comes *ela mitoch devar simcha shel mitzvah* — specifically from a state of joyful engagement in *mitzvah*. The additional verse *ve'atah kchu li menagen, vehaya kenagen hamenagen vatehi alav yad Hashem* reinforces the point: it was the playing of the musician — an act of joyful engagement — that brought the divine inspiration upon Elisha.

### Mood for Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: וְכֵן לִדְבַר הֲלָכָה. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וְכֵן לַחֲלוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: And, so** too, one should be joyful before stating **a matter of *halakha*. Rav Naḥman said: And, so** too, one should be joyful before going to sleep, **to** ensure he will have **a good dream.**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav extends this principle: the same requirement of a good and joyful state applies *vechen ledvar halacha* — to the rendering of halachic rulings as well. Rav Nachman adds a parallel application: the same is true for receiving a *chalom tov*, a good dream — one should enter sleep in a state of *simcha shel mitzvah*.

### Dreams Joy

These three applications — divine inspiration, *halacha*, and good dreams — are presented as structurally parallel, all requiring the same preparatory inner disposition of joy rooted in *mitzvah* engagement.

### Trembling Lips

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הַיּוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ וְאֵין שִׂפְתוֹתָיו נוֹטְפוֹת מַר — תִּכָּוֶינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׂפְתוֹתָיו שׁוֹשַׁנִּים נוֹטְפוֹת מוֹר עוֹבֵר״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״שׁוֹשַׁנִּים״, אֶלָּא ״שֶׁשּׁוֹנִים״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״מוֹר עוֹבֵר״, אֶלָּא ״מַר עוֹבֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** that one should introduce matters of *halakha* joyfully? **Didn't Rav Giddel say** that **Rav said: Any Torah scholar who sits before his teacher and his lips are not dripping with bitterness** due to fear of his teacher, those lips **shall be burned, as it is stated: "His lips are as lilies [*shoshanim*] dripping with flowing myrrh [*notefot mor over*]"** (Song of Songs 5:13). He interpreted homiletically: **Do not read it** as ***shoshanim*, lilies; rather,** read it as ***sheshonim*, who are studying.** Likewise, **do not read it** as ***mor over*, flowing myrrh; rather,** read it as ***mar over*, flowing bitterness.** In other words, lips that are studying Torah must be full of bitterness.

A challenge is immediately raised. Rav Gidel in the name of Rav states: *kol talmid chacham hayoshev lifnei rabbo v'ein siftotav notfot mar tichzena* — any Torah scholar who sits before his teacher and whose lips are not dripping with bitterness, trembling in awe, his lips should be scorched. The proof text is from *Shir Hashirim*: *siftotav shoshanim notfot mor over* — his lips are like roses dripping myrrh. Read not *shoshanim* (roses) but *sheshonim* (those who learn); read not *mor over* (passing myrrh) but *mar avar* (bitterness that passes over). A student must come to his *rebbi* in a state of trembling awe — the opposite of the joyful ease suggested above. This is the same paradox invoked when explaining why one does not study Torah on *Tisha B'Av*: the joy of Torah study is itself an expression of the soul's delight, incompatible with mourning.

### Rebbe vs Student

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּרַבָּה, הָא — בְּתַלְמִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This is **not difficult;** there is no contradiction here, as **this** statement, which teaches that one should introduce matters of *halakha* joyfully, is referring **to a rabbi, and that** statement, which teaches that one must be filled with bitterness, is referring **to a student,** who must listen to his teacher with trepidation.

The Gemara resolves the tension: *lo kashya — ha b'rabba, ha b'talmida*. The two descriptions apply to different parties. The *rebbi* may and should be in a state of *simcha* — the joy of *mitzvah* engagement that allows him to teach, to reach *halachic* conclusions, and to access deeper levels of understanding. The student, by contrast, must be in a state of trembling reverence before his teacher. The two postures are not contradictory; they characterize different positions in the relationship.

### Rabba's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא בְּרַבָּה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — מִקַּמֵּי דְּפָתַח, וְהָא — לְבָתַר דְּפָתַח. כִּי הָא דְּרַבָּה, מִקַּמֵּי דְּפָתַח לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן, אָמַר מִילְּתָא דִּבְדִיחוּתָא וּבָדְחוּ רַבָּנַן, וּלְבַסּוֹף יָתֵיב בְּאֵימְתָא, וּפָתַח בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **this and that** are referring **to a rabbi, and** it is **not difficult. This** statement, where it is taught that one must be joyful, is **before** one **begins** teaching; **that** statement, where it is taught that he must be filled with bitterness and trepidation, is **after** he already **began** teaching *halakha*. The Gemara adds: That explanation is **like that** practice **of Rabba's. Before he began** teaching *halakha* **to the Sages, he would say some humorous** comment, **and the Sages would be cheered. Ultimately, he sat in trepidation and began** teaching **the *halakha*.**

Alternatively, the Gemara suggests that both descriptions apply to the *rebbi* himself, but at different moments. *Hachi hashta: ha mikmei depatach, ha levatar depatach* — one disposition belongs to before one begins to teach, the other to after. This is illustrated by the practice of Rabba: *mikmei d'patach l'rabbanan, amar milta d'bidichuta* — before he would open his lesson to the rabbinic students he would say something light, something humorous, *u'badu rabbanan* — and the students would laugh, relaxing into a receptive frame of mind. *U'levasof yativ be'eimta u'patach b'shmata* — and then he would sit with a sense of solemnity and open the lesson itself. The humor served not as a distraction but as a vehicle to shift the students from their ordinary state into readiness for learning, whereupon the *rebbi* himself entered into the awe-filled space of Torah. One might ask whether these two states are truly contradictory: perhaps *gilu b're'adah* — joy and trembling combined — represents the ideal in which one engages Torah with both love and awe simultaneously. But the Gemara is here navigating a practical balance, and the model of Rabba suggests a temporal sequencing: levity to open, then gravity to sustain.

## Hallel Crises

### Moshe Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַלֵּל זֶה מִי אֲמָרוֹ? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מֹשֶׁה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמְדוּ עַל הַיָּם. הֵם אָמְרוּ: ״לֹא לָנוּ ה׳ לֹא לָנוּ״, מְשִׁיבָה רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ וְאָמְרָה לָהֶן: ״לְמַעֲנִי לְמַעֲנִי אֶעֱשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: This *hallel*, who** initially **recited it? Rabbi Eliezer says: Moses and the Jewish people recited it when they stood by the sea. They said: "Not to us, God, not to us,** but to Your name give glory" (Psalms 115:1). **The Divine Spirit responded and said to them: "For My own sake, for My own sake, will I do it"** (Isaiah 48:11).

The Gemara returns to the question of *Hallel*'s origins with a *baraisa* that presents multiple competing Tannaitic positions, each locating the first recitation of *Hallel* at a different historical moment of crisis and redemption. The structure is uniform throughout: at each crisis, the protagonists recited *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu* — "not for us, God, not for us" — a form of petition acknowledging unworthiness while pleading for divine intervention, and the divine spirit responded *l'ma'ani l'ma'ani e'eseh* — "for My sake, for My sake I will act" (from Yeshayahu). This is striking: *Hallel* is here understood not merely as a post-redemption song of thanksgiving but as containing, at its culmination, a genuine *bakashah* — a petition for salvation — and a divine response promising redemption for God's own sake rather than Israel's.

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *Moshe v'Yisrael amru b'sha'ah she'amdu al ha'yam* — Moshe and Israel said *Hallel* while they were standing at the sea, before they crossed, during the moment of danger itself.

### Yehoshua Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמְדוּ עֲלֵיהֶם מַלְכֵי כְנַעַן. הֵם אָמְרוּ: ״לֹא לָנוּ״, וּמְשִׁיבָה וְכוּ׳:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Joshua and the Jewish people recited it when** they defeated **the kings of Canaan** who **stood against them** (see Joshua 12:7–24). **They said: Not to us, and** the Divine Spirit **responded:** For My own sake.

Rabbi Yehuda holds that it was *Yehoshua v'Yisrael* who said it *b'sha'ah she'amdu aleihem malchei Kena'an* — when the kings of Canaan stood against them in battle.

### Deborah Barak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַמּוֹדָעִי אוֹמֵר: דְּבוֹרָה וּבָרָק אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמַד עֲלֵיהֶם סִיסְרָא. הֵם אָמְרוּ: ״לֹא לָנוּ״, וְרוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ מְשִׁיבָה וְאוֹמֶרֶת לָהֶם: ״לְמַעֲנִי לְמַעֲנִי אֶעֱשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i says: Deborah and Barak recited it when Sisera stood against them** (see Judges 4–5). **They said: Not to us, and the Divine Spirit responded and said to them: For My own sake, for My own sake, will I do it.**

Rabbi Elazar HaModai holds that it was *Devorah u'Barak* who said it *b'sha'ah she'amad aleihem Sisra* — when Sisra threatened them.

### Chizkiyahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: חִזְקִיָּה וְסִייעָתוֹ אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמַד עֲלֵיהֶם סַנְחֵרִיב. הֵם אָמְרוּ: ״לֹא לָנוּ״, וּמְשִׁיבָה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: Hezekiah and his company recited it when Sennacherib stood against them** (see II Kings 18–19). **They said: Not to us and** the Divine Spirit **responded:** For My own sake.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds that it was *Chizkiyahu v'siato* — Chizkiyahu and his circle — who said it *b'sha'ah she'amad aleihem Sancheriv* — when Sancheriv besieged Jerusalem.

### Three Friends

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמַד עֲלֵיהֶם נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָרָשָׁע. הֵם אָמְרוּ: ״לֹא לָנוּ״, וּמְשִׁיבָה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah recited it when the wicked Nebuchadnezzar stood against them** (see Daniel 3). **They said: Not to us, and** the Divine Spirit **responded:** For My own sake.

Rabbi Akiva holds that it was *Chananyah, Mishael v'Azaryah* who said it *b'sha'ah she'amad aleihem Nevuchadnetzar harasha* — when the wicked Nevuchadnetzar threatened to cast them into the fiery furnace.

### Mordechai Esther

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: מָרְדְּכַי וְאֶסְתֵּר אֲמָרוּהוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָמַד עֲלֵיהֶם הָמָן הָרָשָׁע. הֵם אָמְרוּ: ״לֹא לָנוּ״, וּמְשִׁיבָה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: Mordecai and Esther recited it when the wicked Haman stood against them. They said: Not to us, and** the Divine Spirit **responded:** For My own sake (see the book of Esther).

Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds that it was *Mordechai v'Esther* who said it *b'sha'ah she'amad aleihem Haman harasha* — when Haman threatened the entire people. The list proceeds in historical order — from the Sea of Reeds through the Persian period — suggesting a cumulative argument: *Hallel* was not composed at one moment; it was always the prayer and song of Israel in its moments of greatest peril and greatest redemption.

The Rashbam notes that one reading of these positions holds they are not actually in dispute — each sage is pointing to a different historical instantiation of the same practice, collectively demonstrating that *Hallel* has always been recited at such moments. The structure is reminiscent of the ancient *selichot* and *ta'anit* liturgy — *mi she'anah l'Avraham Avinu... mi she'anah... mi she'anah* — where the catalog of divine responses to crisis becomes itself a form of prayer and praise.

What is particularly powerful about this *baraisa* is the location of *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu* — "not for us God, not for us" — as the pivot point of each account. This verse, which appears near the end of the *Hallel* we recite, carries a petitionary, even supplicatory quality. The *baraisa* is pointing out that *Hallel* contains within itself not only thanks for redemption already received but the cry of those still standing at the threshold of danger, pleading for divine intervention. The narrative arc of *lo lanu* followed by divine response *l'ma'ani e'eseh* captures the full movement from petition to promise.

### Chachamim View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נְבִיאִים שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶן תִּיקְּנוּ לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיְּהוּ אוֹמְרִים אוֹתוֹ עַל כׇּל פֶּרֶק וּפֶרֶק וְעַל כׇּל צָרָה וְצָרָה שֶׁלֹּא תָּבֹא עֲלֵיהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל. וְלִכְשֶׁנִּגְאָלִין, אוֹמְרִים אוֹתוֹ עַל גְּאוּלָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say** that *hallel* was not established for any specific event, but **the Prophets among them instituted that the Jewish people** should **recite it on every** appropriate **occasion, and for every trouble, may it not come upon the Jewish people. When they are redeemed, they recite it over their redemption.**

The *Chachamim* offer a formulation that distinguishes itself from the individual Tannaitic positions. They reiterate the formula that *nevi'im shebeineihem tiknu l'Yisrael sheyihu omrim oso al kol perek u'perek v'al kol tzarah shelo tavo aleihem u'kshe'nigalin omrim oso al geulasan*. The key distinction in the *Chachamim*'s version appears to be the emphasis on recitation *al geulatan* — upon redemption — rather than specifically during the moment of threat. Where the individual Tannaim emphasize the crisis itself as the occasion for *Hallel*, the *Chachamim* stress the moment of deliverance. Both dimensions are present in the full *Hallel*, but the *Chachamim* place the center of gravity on thanksgiving for redemption rather than petition during danger.

## Hallelujah Perek

### Ends Perek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ — סוֹף פִּירְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the term *halleluya*. **Rav Ḥisda said:** The ***halleluya*** stated in the final verse in several Psalms signifies **the end of a chapter.**

Rav Chisda rules that whenever *hallelujah* appears at a point of ambiguity in *Sefer Tehillim* — between the end of one *perek* and the beginning of another — it should be understood as *sof perek*, the concluding word of the preceding section.

### Begins Perek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ — רֵישׁ פִּירְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Rav Huna said: *Halleluya*** marks **the start of** a new **chapter,** the beginning of the next psalm.

Rava bar Rav Huna disagrees: *hallelujah reish pirka* — *hallelujah* is the opening word of the following section, not the conclusion of the preceding one.

### Between Sections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: חֲזֵינָא לְהוּ לְתִילֵּי דְּבֵי רַב חָנִין בַּר רַב דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ בְּאֶמְצַע פִּירְקָא. אַלְמָא מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: I saw a book of Psalms in the study hall of Rav Ḥanin bar Rav, in which it is written *halleluya* in the middle of the chapter,** i.e., between the chapters, neither at the start of one psalm nor at the end of the next. **Apparently,** Rav Ḥanin bar Rav **was uncertain** where the word belonged.

Rav Chisda reports having seen the *Sefer Tehillim* in the possession of Rav Chanin bar Rav, in which *hallelujah* was written precisely between two sections — *b'emtza pirka* — clearly because its owner was uncertain (*misafka lei*) whether it belonged to the end of one or the beginning of the other, and so placed it exactly in between as a way of expressing that uncertainty in the physical layout of the scroll.

### Consensus Begin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנִין בַּר רָבָא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּ״תְהִלַּת ה׳ יְדַבֶּר פִּי וִיבָרֵךְ כׇּל בָּשָׂר שֵׁם קׇדְשׁוֹ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד (הַלְלוּיָהּ)״, ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ דְּבָתְרֵיהּ — רֵישׁ פִּירְקָא. ״רָשָׁע יִרְאֶה וְכָעָס שִׁנָּיו יַחֲרֹק וְנָמָס תַּאֲוַת רְשָׁעִים תֹּאבֵד״, ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ דְּבָתְרֵיהּ — רֵישׁ פִּירְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanin bar Rava said: Everyone concedes** with regard to the verse: **"My mouth shall speak the praise of the Lord; and let all flesh bless His holy name forever and ever"** (Psalms 145:21), that the ***halleluya* that follows,** the opening word of the subsequent psalm, marks **the start of** the next **chapter,** not the conclusion of the previous one. Likewise, with regard to the verse: **"The wicked shall see and be vexed; he shall gnash with his teeth, and melt away; the desire of the wicked shall perish"** (Psalms 112:10), the ***halleluya* that follows it,** is **the start of** the next **chapter.**

Rav Chanin bar Rava — distinct from Rav Chanin bar Rav cited just above — states that in fact *hakol modin*, everyone agrees, that the *hallelujah* following the verse *tehillas Hashem yedaber pi viyvarech kol basar shem kodsho l'olam va'ed* is *reish pirka*, the opening of the next section. This verse concludes one psalm; the *hallelujah* that follows it opens the next. Similarly, the *hallelujah* that follows *rasha yireh v'ka'as, shinav yichrok v'namas, ta'avas resha'im toveid* is likewise *reish pirka* — the opening of the next psalm, not the closing of that one.

### Ashrei Practice

The practical implication emerges in our *davening*. In *Sefer Tehillim* itself, psalm 145 (*ashrei*) concludes with *tehillas Hashem yedaber pi...* and does not end with *hallelujah*. Psalm 146 then opens *Hallelujah, halleli nafshi es Hashem*. When we recite *ashrei* in our prayers, however, we conclude with *hallelujah* — reflecting the liturgical practice rather than the strict *Tehillim* division. In a *Sefer Tehillim*, there is only one *hallelujah* at that juncture; in the printed *siddur*, it appears twice, because the liturgical tradition influenced the printed text. The Gemara's discussion clarifies that structurally that *hallelujah* is properly the beginning of the next *perek*, not the ending of *ashrei* — and indeed, several of the psalms that begin with *hallelujah* continue immediately with *halelu es Hashem* or similar language, which further supports reading *hallelujah* as an opening formula that flows into the body of the new section.

## Hallel Length

### Shammai Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד הֵיכָן הוּא אוֹמֵר? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: עַד ״אֵם הַבָּנִים שְׂמֵחָה״, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד ״חַלָּמִישׁ לְמַעְיְנוֹ מָיִם״. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ, עַד הֵיכָן הוּא אוֹמֵר? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: עַד ״בְּצֵאת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרָיִם״. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד ״לֹא לָנוּ ה׳ לֹא לָנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** it is **parallel to** a dispute between the ***tanna'im*,** as we learned in the mishna: **Until where does one recite** *hallel*? **Beit Shammai say: Until "A joyful mother of children,** *halleluya*" (Psalms 113:9). **And Beit Hillel say: Until "The flint into a fountain of waters"** (Psalms 114:8). **And it was taught** in **another** source, a *baraita*: **Until where does one recite** *hallel*? **Beit Shammai say: Until "When Israel came forth out of Egypt"** (Psalms 114:1), the beginning of the first passage after "A joyful mother of children, *halleluya*." **And Beit Hillel say: Until "Not to us, God, not to us"** (Psalms 115:1), which follows "the flint into a fountain of waters."

The Gemara now reconnects to the *Mishnah*'s dispute about how far one recites *Hallel* before the *berachah* that concludes the *haggadah* — that is, before *motzi matzah* and the main meal. Two *beraisos* record the dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel, and the two versions seem to differ on how each position is formulated.

### Version One

Two *beraisos* record the dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel, and the two versions seem to differ on how each position is formulated.

In the first *baraisa*: Beis Shammai says one recites until *eim habanim simchah*, the final phrase of the first *perek* of *Hallel* (psalm 113). Beis Hillel says until *chalamish l'mayno mayim*, the final phrase of the second *perek* (psalm 114).

### Version Two

In the second *baraisa*: Beis Shammai says until *b'tzeis Yisrael miMitzrayim*, the opening phrase of the second *perek*. Beis Hillel says until *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu*, the opening phrase of the third *perek* (psalm 115).

At first glance these two versions seem simply to disagree about whether the stopping point is quoted as the last phrase of the *perek* one does say or the first phrase of the *perek* one does not say — an inclusive versus exclusive formulation. But the Gemara notices something significant: neither version mentions *hallelujah*, even though *hallelujah* appears between the two *perakim* at issue.

### Chisda Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עַד ״אֵם הַבָּנִים שְׂמֵחָה״, סָבַר ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ — רֵישׁ פִּירְקָא, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר עַד ״בְּצֵאת יִשְׂרָאֵל״, סָבַר ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ — סוֹף פִּירְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** the case **that the** mishna and the *baraita* **disagree** concerning **the following** matter: **According to the one who says** that one must recite **until "A joyful mother of children,"** he **maintains** that the subsequent ***halleluya*** is **the start of a chapter. And the one who said** that one must recite **until "When Israel came forth" maintains** that ***halleluya*** is **the end of the** previous **chapter.** The mishna and the *baraita* disagree only with regard to when the word *halleluya* should be recited, at this point in the seder or when *hallel* is resumed after the meal.

Rav Chisda resolves the apparent discrepancy between the two versions of the dispute according to his own position that *hallelujah* is *sof perek*. On this reading, the full text of the first *perek* ends: *moshivi akkeres habayis, eim habanim simchah, Hallelujah*. And the second *perek* begins: *b'tzeis Yisrael miMitzrayim*. The one who says "until *eim habanim simchah*" holds *hallelujah* is *reish pirka* and therefore does not include it — stopping before the word *hallelujah*, which opens the next section. The one who says "until *b'tzeis Yisrael miMitzrayim*" holds *hallelujah* is *sof pirka*, meaning it was already included as part of the first *perek*, and one recites up until (but not including) *b'tzeis Yisrael miMitzrayim*.

### Rabba Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ — רֵישׁ פִּירְקָא, מַאן דְּאָמַר עַד ״אֵם הַבָּנִים שְׂמֵחָה״ — שַׁפִּיר, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר עַד ״בְּצֵאת יִשְׂרָאֵל״, סָבַר: עַד — וְלֹא עַד בַּכְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rabba bar Rav Huna explains** the difference between the mishna and the *baraita* **in accordance with his reasoning, that everyone agrees** that ***halleluya*** signifies **the start of a chapter. The one who said** that one must recite **until "A joyful mother of children"** spoke **well, and the one who said** that one must recite **until "When Israel came forth" maintains** that the term means **until and not including,** as one does not conclude with the word *halleluya* after "A joyful mother of children."

Rabba bar Rav Huna resolves the same discrepancy according to his position that *hallelujah* is *reish pirka*. On this reading, the second *perek* properly opens *Hallelujah b'tzeis Yisrael miMitzrayim*. The one who says "until *eim habanim simchah*" is citing the last phrase of the first *perek* inclusively — *ad v'ad bichlal*, up to and including — meaning one does say *eim habanim simchah*. The one who says "until *b'tzeis Yisrael miMitzrayim*" means up to but not including — *ad v'lo ad bichlal* — so the second *perek*, which begins with *hallelujah*, is not recited before the meal.

### Kashia Unresolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא: עַד ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״, וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּלָא יָדְעִינַן הֵי ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ — וְנֵימָא עַד ״הַלְלוּיָהּ״ שֶׁ״בְּצֵאת יִשְׂרָאֵל״? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a similar question with regard to the opinion of Rabba bar Rav Huna: If so, **let** the *tanna* **say: Until *halleluya*. And if you say that we** would **not know which *halleluya*** he meant, **let** the *tanna* **say:** The ***halleluya* of "When Israel came forth."** The Gemara comments: This is indeed **difficult** for Rabba bar Rav Huna's opinion.

Both resolutions, however, run into the same difficulty: if the word *hallelujah* is the crux of the debate — if the entire point of the dispute between the two versions is whether *hallelujah* belongs to the end of one *perek* or the beginning of another — then why doesn't either version of the dispute simply say "until *hallelujah*" or "until the *hallelujah* of *eim habanim simchah*"? The omission of *hallelujah* from both formulations is conspicuous. The Gemara raises this challenge — *v'neima Hallelujah shel eim habanim simchah*, why not simply say until *that* *hallelujah*? — and leaves it unresolved: *kashia*.

The halacha, the Gemara notes, is that *hallelujah* is *reish pirka* — the opening of the following section. The overall discussion leaves us with a richly detailed portrait of *Hallel*'s structure, its ancient origins, its function as both petition and thanksgiving, and the careful attention Chazal paid to its textual divisions and their practical implications for the *seder* night and for all *yamim tovim*.
