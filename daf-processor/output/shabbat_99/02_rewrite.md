# Shabbat 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beams & Sockets

### Carving Technique

The daf opens with a *baraita* describing the construction of the *mishkan*'s structural elements: *charutzim hayu kerashim vachallulim hayu adanim* — the beams were carved, and the sockets were hollow. What this means practically is that each beam, which was essentially a rectangular solid, had to terminate at its base in two small pegs that would fit into the hollow of the sockets below. To produce those pegs, the craftsmen had to cut away the surrounding material — removing everything that was not the peg — leaving two protruding feet at the bottom of each beam. The sockets, in turn, were square blocks with a hollow cavity, into which those feet would be inserted. That is the entirety of the mechanism described: carved pegs and hollow sockets, fitted together.

### Assembly Method

The visual logic is straightforward. One begins with a full rectangular beam and cuts inward on all sides near the base, producing two narrow pegs. The sockets receive those pegs, and the whole structure locks into place. *Rashi* includes a diagram that clarifies the geometry. The broader halachic significance of this description will become apparent as the *gemara* returns to questions of carrying and domain.

## Curtains & Skill

### Hooks as Stars

The *gemara* turns to a entirely different aspect of the *mishkan* — the curtains that formed the roof. The Torah describes two sets of five curtains each, sewn together along their respective lengths and then joined to one another by means of rings (*lula'ot*) and hooks (*krasim*). The *gemara* notes: *venirim krasim belula'ot kekochavim barakia* — the hooks visible through the rings appeared like stars in the sky. From beneath, looking up at this ceiling of curtains, the gold-toned copper fittings would glimmer through, creating the impression of a starlit sky. Whether that visual impression was intentional — whether the *mishkan*'s designers were consciously evoking the heavens — is left open. The curtains themselves were not uniformly blue, so this was not a simple imitation of sky. Yet the image is a striking one: the very structure of the joinery producing an unplanned, or at least unspecified, celestial effect.

### Curtain Materials

A *baraita* then specifies the materials of the two layers of curtains. The lower curtains (*yeri'ot tachtonot*) were woven from *techeiles* (blue), *argaman* (royal purple), *tola'as shani* (scarlet), and *shesh* (linen) — a rich, multicolored fabric. The upper curtains (*elyonot*), by contrast, were *ma'aseh izim*, made entirely from goat hair. On the surface, this suggests the upper curtains were simpler: a single, plain material versus the elaborate combination below.

### Goat Hair Spinning

The *baraita* immediately overturns that assumption: *ugdolah chochmah shene'emrah be'elyonot yoter mimas shene'emrah betachtonot* — the skill required for the upper curtains exceeded that required for the lower ones. The proof is textual. Regarding the lower curtains, the verse says that women of skilled heart (*chachmat lev*) spun with their hands. Regarding the upper curtains, the verse says that women whose hearts *lifted them up* with wisdom spun *et ha'izim* — they spun the goats. The *gemara* cites a *tannaitic* teaching in the name of Rabbi Nechemiah, already encountered a few weeks earlier in the course of study: *shatuf ba'izim vetavui min ha'izim* — the goats were washed in the river while still bearing their hair, and the thread was spun directly from the backs of the goats while they stood there. That is the meaning of "they spun the goats" rather than simply "they spun the goat hair." Spinning directly from a living animal, rather than from sheared and prepared fiber, demanded a level of technical mastery that surpassed even the intricate multicolor weaving of the lower curtains. This passage was cited earlier in the tractate in a halachic context: the *gemara* there raised the possibility that since this technique required extraordinary skill (*chochmah yetirah*), a normal person performing it might not be liable for a *melachah* on *Shabbat*, as it would constitute a *shinuy* — a deviation from standard practice.

## Wagons Layout

### Wagon Positioning

With the discussion of curtains concluded, the *gemara* returns to the theme of the *levi'im* and carrying — specifically, the question of *reshut harabbim* and how its dimensions are derived from the *mishkan*. The context is a teaching of Rav in the name of Rabbi Chiya: *agalot tachteihen beneihen vetzideihen reshut harabbim* — underneath the wagons, between the wagons, and at their sides was all *reshut harabbim*. This was noted the previous day in the discussion of whether the overhead beams converted the space into a covered *reshut harabbim*.

### Wagon Length

Abaye states: *bein agalah le'agalah kemlo orech agalah* — the gap between one wagon and the next was equal to the length of one wagon. And the length of a wagon was five *amot*.

### Rashi's Model

*Rashi* explains this as follows. Each wagon was five *amot* long. Two wagons moved side by side, and each was also five *amot* wide — two and a half *amot* for the base of the wagon, and then one and a quarter *amot* on each side for the thickness of the wheels. The full outer width of each wagon, from wheel edge to wheel edge, was therefore five *amot*.

According to *Rashi*, the beams of the *mishkan* were laid across the tops of two side-by-side wagons, overhanging each one. From the outer edge of one wagon to the outer edge of the other was fifteen *amot*: five for each wagon plus five between them, and the beams extended across the entire span with overhangs on the outer edges.

The *gemara* asks about the length: why does the wagon need to be a full five *amot*? The calculation based on the beam widths — whether one and a half *amot* or one *amah* across — yields either four and a half or some similar measure that falls short of five. The *gemara* answers: *ki heichi dela litzdu kerashim* — to prevent the beams from rubbing against one another. A small cushion of space was necessary so that the columns of beams would not wear each other down through friction.

### Base Width

The *gemara* then turns to the width of the wagon's base. Rava states that the sides of each wagon (*tzidei agalah*) were equal to the width of the wagon — two and a half *amot*. So the base of each wagon was two and a half *amot*, with the outer wheel adding one and a quarter *amot* on each side, for a total of five *amot* per wagon.

The *gemara* asks: why does the base need to be two and a half *amot*? Would not one and a half suffice? The answer is: *ki heichi d'lo lidudi krashim* — again, to give the beams sufficient stability so they would not sway. A narrower base would leave too much beam overhanging on each side without adequate support.

One should appreciate just how much overhang is involved here in *Rashi*'s model. The beam extends from beyond the outer wheel on one side, across the full wagon, and out over the wheel on the other side. If the inner support is only two and a half *amot* and the overhang on each side extends considerably further, the structure is not particularly stable — a point *Tosafot* does not let pass.

### Width Derived

The *gemara* now addresses the derivation of the standard width of *reshut harabbim*: *d'kaima lan derech reshut harabbim shesh esrei amah* — we hold that a *reshut harabbim* must be sixteen *amot* wide, and we learn this from the *mishkan*. The *gemara* says that if we learn it from the *mishkan*, the *mishkan* structure itself only yields fifteen: five for each of the two wagons and five between them. How do we arrive at sixteen?

The *gemara* answers: *amta yetirta havai d'hava kai bein Levi* — there was an additional *amah*, the space where the *levi* walked alongside the wagon. If the beams began to slip, a *levi* positioned at the side could catch and adjust them. This guard-walking *levi* occupied an extra *amah* — either a half-*amah* on each side, or a full *amah* on one side — bringing the total to sixteen. That is *Rashi*'s explanation.

### Tosafot's Model

*Tosafot* raises several objections to *Rashi*'s model. First, if two wagons travel side by side and the beams overhang from both, the beams will press against one another in the middle, rubbing away at each other. Second, even accepting the two-and-a-half *amah* base, the overhang on each side is so great relative to the support that the structure is structurally implausible.

*Tosafot* therefore proposes an entirely different understanding. In *Tosafot*'s model, what the *gemara* calls the "two wagons" is in fact one unified structure. Each "wheel" was itself two and a half *amot* thick — not one and a quarter as in *Rashi*. You thus have one large structural unit: two and a half *amot* for the outer wall and wheel on one side, ten *amot* of interior space, and two and a half *amot* for the outer wall and wheel on the other side — a total of fifteen *amot* from outer edge to outer edge. The ten-*amah* interior space is exactly the length of each *mishkan* beam, so no overhang was needed or intended: the beam lay perfectly across the interior of the structure.

This single unified structure had four wheels underneath — two outer wheels and two inner wheels — and interior walls or supports at the points where the inner wheels were located, so that the beam rested on those interior walls rather than spanning freely. The distinction *Tosafot* draws is that what *Rashi* calls "two wagons" and what the *gemara* calls each *agalah* in the context of width refers to half of this single structure — from the outer wall to the first interior support, which was two and a half *amot*, plus the gap to the next half, which was five *amot*. The entire unit read as a unified structure rather than two independent vehicles.

*Tosafot*'s model avoids the problems of opposing overhangs and structural instability. It also allows a single ten-*amah* beam to rest cleanly across the interior without conflict. The *levi* with his extra *amah* still accounts for the sixteenth *amah* on the outside. The *gemara*'s insistence on five *amot* as the measure of a wagon is interpreted by *Tosafot* as referring to each half of the structure, not a separate independent vehicle, with the full structure spanning fifteen.

## Mound & Rock

### Dimensions

The *gemara* moves to the next *mishnah*: *chulyat habor vehasela* — the mound of earth (*chulya*) surrounding a well and a rock. The *mishnah* establishes that if either of these is ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* in its base dimensions, it constitutes a *reshut hayachid*. One who takes an object from the top of such a mound or rock, or places something upon it while standing in *reshut harabbim*, is liable.

This ruling contains an important conceptual point. A *reshut hayachid* is not limited to the classic model of a cavity surrounded by four walls. Even a solid elevation — a mound of earth or a natural rock — with the requisite dimensions qualifies. The logic is that once you have walls of ten *tefachim* on all sides, those walls are understood to continue upward indefinitely, making the space above them — including the top surface of the mound or rock — part of the *reshut hayachid*. The analogy is to the roof of a building: even if the entire interior were filled with earth, the roof would still be a *reshut hayachid* because the walls define the perimeter and their legal effect extends upward.

The *mishnah* specifies that if the dimensions fall short of ten high or four by four wide, one is exempt. The four-by-four requirement means exactly four in each direction; sixteen square *tefachim* arranged in a shape lacking four in any one direction would not suffice.

### Combination Debate

The *gemara* asks: why does the *mishnah* need to mention the mound around the well (*chulyat habor*) separately from the rock? The mound, as a solid elevated mass with the requisite dimensions, is simply a case of the same *halacha* as the rock — what novel point does it add? The *gemara* answers: *mesaya lei le-Rabbi Yochanan* — it supports the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan, who said that *bor vechuliyasa mitztarfin l'asara* — the well and its surrounding mound combine to reach the measure of ten.

The question is: in which dimension do they combine? Here *Rashi* and *Tosafot* diverge.

*Rashi* understands the combination to be in height. Imagine a well that is only five *tefachim* deep, with a mound of five *tefachim* built up around its opening. For a person standing at the bottom of the well, the total wall surrounding them is ten *tefachim*: five from the depth of the well and five from the height of the mound above ground. The interior of the well thus constitutes a *reshut hayachid*, even though neither element alone reaches ten. The difficulty with *Rashi*'s interpretation is that the *mishnah* speaks of placing objects *on top of* the mound (*al gaban*) and being liable — but on *Rashi*'s model, the top of the mound is only five *tefachim* high and would not itself be a *reshut hayachid*. The liability would attach only to placing something *inside* the well, not on top of the mound.

*Tosafot* therefore reads the combination differently — as combining in width rather than height. On this reading, the mound itself is ten *tefachim* high, so height is not the issue. The problem is that the mound consists of two raised sides flanking the opening of the well, and each side may be only a *tefach* or two wide, with the gap of the well's opening — perhaps two *tefachim* — between them. Taken separately, neither side has the required four *tefachim* in width. But Rabbi Yochanan's principle allows the mound and the well opening together to be treated as a combined unit of four *tefachim*. The basis for this combination is that one could in theory lay a plank across the gap, and since the well and its mound are understood as a single functional apparatus, the gap is treated as bridged even without an actual plank, rendering the entire surface — including the top of the mound — a *reshut hayachid*. On this reading, the liability described in the *mishnah* for placing something *on top* fits perfectly.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* do not dispute that both types of combination are legally valid. The question is only which scenario Rabbi Yochanan and the *mishnah* are addressing. *Tosafot* argues that since the *mishnah* explicitly discusses placing on top of the mound and being liable, the combination must be one that makes the top of the mound itself a *reshut hayachid*, which only works on the width-combination reading.

## Mound & Rock (II)

### Application

The *gemara* then cites a supporting *baraita*: a well in *reshut harabbim* that is ten *tefachim* deep and four wide — one may not draw water from it on *Shabbat*, because doing so transfers from a *reshut hayachid* into a *reshut harabbim* or at minimum into a *karmelis*. The *baraita* confirms that *bor vechuliyasa mitztarfin l'asara*, and the phrasing here — combining to reach ten — aligns textually with *Rashi*'s height-combination reading. *Tosafot* agrees that the *baraita* and Rabbi Yochanan's statement refer to the *Rashi* scenario, but maintains that when Rabbi Yochanan's principle is applied to explain the *mishnah*'s case, the same logic extends to width-combination as well.

## Well Usage

### Drawing Prohibition

The *baraita* establishes that one may not draw water from a ten-deep, four-wide well situated in *reshut harabbim* on *Shabbat*, because the act of drawing transfers an object from a *reshut hayachid* into the surrounding space, whether that space is a *reshut harabbim* or a *karmelis*. This is the practical problem that led to the rabbinic practice of constructing enclosures around public wells, or at minimum symbolic corner-post structures, to define the surrounding space as *reshut hayachid* and permit water-drawing.

### Wall Requirement

The *baraita* continues: *ela im kein asu lo mechitzah gevohah asara tefachim* — unless one builds a wall ten *tefachim* high around the well. Only then is water-drawing permitted, because the surrounding area has been converted into a *reshut hayachid* and one is simply moving within that domain.

### Drinking Rule

Even absent a proper wall, the *baraita* allows drinking from the well under one strict condition: *ela im kein hichnis l'tocho rosho ve-rubo* — unless one inserts into the well one's head and the majority of one's body. The concern is that if a person merely leans over the well to draw water — even if the bucket or vessel remains within the airspace of the well — there is a risk that the person will stand upright while still holding the vessel, thereby transferring it from *reshut hayachid* into whatever domain the person is standing in. Only if the majority of the body is actually inside the well can we be confident that the person and the water are effectively together within the *reshut hayachid* and that drinking constitutes movement within that domain rather than a transfer across domain boundaries. This is, practically speaking, a highly precarious requirement, demanding that one suspend the majority of one's body over a pit ten *tefachim* deep in order to drink.

## Throwing Clarifications

### Rav Mordechai

The *gemara* records a question posed by Rav Mordechai to Rabbah. There is a post (*amud*) standing in *reshut harabbim* that is ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide — in other words, a classic *reshut hayachid* exactly like the rock of the *mishnah*. One throws an object that lands on top of the post. The question: is one liable?

On the surface this appears trivially answered by the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* says one is liable for taking from or placing upon a ten-high, four-wide rock in *reshut harabbim*. But Rav Mordechai raises a consideration: *keivan d'mimakom patur ka-asya* — since the object came from a *makom patur*. In order to land on something that is ten *tefachim* high, the object must have traveled through the airspace above ten *tefachim* in *reshut harabbim*, which is a *makom patur*. Perhaps the trajectory from *reshut harabbim* through a *makom patur* and then down onto *reshut hayachid* interrupts the act, making it a movement from *makom patur* into *reshut hayachid* rather than from *reshut harabbim* into *reshut hayachid* — and thereby exempting the thrower.

### Mishnah Res.

Rabbah, Rav Yosef, and Abaye all respond identically: *matnisin hi* — it is answered by the *mishnah*. The very *mishnah* under discussion describes placing something on a rock of exactly these dimensions in *reshut harabbim* and holds one liable; clearly passage through the higher airspace does not exempt.

Rav Mordechai is unimpressed, accusing all three of repeating each other without engaging the question. He points out that the *mishnah* might not actually address the precise scenario he raised. Perhaps the *mishnah* refers to placing an object directly — without the object ascending above ten *tefachim* first. For instance: a needle thrown into a crack in the rock (*charitza*) near the base, which never rises above ten *tefachim* at all. It enters a fissure that, as part of the floor of a *reshut hayachid*, is itself *reshut hayachid*, without the object ever having passed through the higher airspace. That would resolve the *mishnah* without answering Rav Mordechai's question about an object that does pass through *makom patur*.

The *gemara* notes that even a needle must rise slightly (*ee efshar de-lo midalya purta*), and the crevice scenario is an attempt to minimize that elevation as much as possible. Admittedly, reading the entire *mishnah* as discussing needle-in-crevice is a forced interpretation, and *Tosafot* takes a different view of what Rav Mordechai was really asking.

### Tosafot Theory

*Tosafot* argues that the question is not simply about an object passing through *makom patur* as a way-point in flight. That scenario — an object passing through an intermediate domain in the course of being carried — has already been discussed elsewhere, and transit through a *makom patur* does not exempt. *Tosafot* understands that Rav Mordechai's question is more specific: it concerns the physics of a thrown object that reaches an apex and then falls.

When one throws an object upward, the object travels from *reshut harabbim* upward into *makom patur*, reaches its apex, and then changes direction — gravity takes over and the object falls back down. *Tosafot* suggests that because this change of direction, this cessation of the thrower's energy and transfer to gravitational force, occurs precisely in the *makom patur*, perhaps the act is thereby divided into two separate movements: one from *reshut harabbim* into *makom patur*, and a second from *makom patur* into *reshut hayachid*. If so, the second segment — from *makom patur* into *reshut hayachid* — does not constitute a prohibited carrying act, and one would be exempt.

This is a far more compelling and nuanced question. The needle-in-crevice scenario was an attempt to resolve the *mishnah* under any possible reading, but *Tosafot*'s framing clarifies why the three *amoraim* who simply said "it's a *mishnah*" were failing to engage the real issue: the *mishnah* may address direct placement, whereas Rav Mordechai is asking specifically about the dynamic of a thrown arc that reverses direction in *makom patur*.

## Wall Status

### Wall Question

The *gemara* records that Rav Mesha transmitted a question of Rabbi Yochanan. There is a wall in *reshut harabbim* that is ten *tefachim* high but not four *tefachim* wide. Because it lacks the four-*tefach* width, the wall itself is not a *reshut hayachid* in its own right. However, this wall surrounds a *karmelis* — some open, intermediate-domain space — and in doing so converts that enclosed area into a *reshut hayachid*. Now, someone throws an object that lands not inside the enclosed yard but on top of the wall itself — on the wall's upper surface, which is less than four *tefachim* wide. What is the status of that surface?

One might say: the wall lacks four *tefachim* of width and is therefore a *makom patur* — exempt space — neither *reshut harabbim* nor *reshut hayachid*. Or one might say: since the wall has created a *reshut hayachid* in the backyard, it should be considered part of that *reshut hayachid*, as if the entire enclosed space — yard plus wall — is one filled unit (*keman d'malya*), making the wall's upper surface itself *reshut hayachid*.

### Ulla's Logic

Ulla resolves the question by means of a *kal vachomer*: if this wall creates *reshut hayachid* for the backyard it encloses, it certainly — *kol she-kein* — creates *reshut hayachid* for its own thickness. The force of the *kal vachomer* is that it would be incongruous for a structure to extend *reshut hayachid* status to others while being denied that status for itself. The wall is, as it were, more integral to the *reshut hayachid* it creates than the yard it encloses; surely its own surface partakes of that status.

### Precedent

A confirming tradition is then cited: Rav Chisda in the name of Rav, and similarly in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, state that any wall ten *tefachim* high — even if not four wide — that surrounds a *karmelis* and converts it to *reshut hayachid*, renders one who throws an object onto the top of that wall liable. The *kal vachomer* of Ulla is thus formally endorsed as *halacha*: the wall's own thickness shares in the *reshut hayachid* it creates.

## Becoming R.H.

### Digging Q.

Rabbi Yochanan poses a striking question. Suppose there is a well that is only nine *tefachim* deep — one *tefach* short of the depth required to constitute a *reshut hayachid*. On *Shabbat*, someone shovels a *chulya* of earth from the bottom of that well, deepening it to ten *tefachim*, and throws the dirt backward out of the well into *reshut harabbim*. Setting aside whatever liability attaches to the digging itself (*boneh* or *choreish*), has this person also committed the *melachah* of carrying? That is: did the person take an object from *reshut hayachid* and deposit it in *reshut harabbim*?

### Simultaneous Acts

The question is: *akirat chefetz ve'asiyas mechitzah bahadi hadadi ka'atu* — the uprooting of the object (the dirt) and the creation of the *mechitza* (the additional *tefach* of depth that makes it a *reshut hayachid*) occurred simultaneously. Perhaps we say that since they occurred together, we treat the well as if it were already a *reshut hayachid* at the moment of uprooting, and the person is liable. Or perhaps we say that since the well was *not* a *reshut hayachid* at the moment the act began, there is no liability: the *akirah* — the uprooting — did not take place from a *reshut hayachid*, and the fact that it became one during the act is insufficient.

### Inverse Q.

Rabbi Yochanan then poses the inverse question. Suppose the well is already ten *tefachim* deep — a proper *reshut hayachid*. One takes a mound of earth from *reshut harabbim* and throws it into the well, and it lands on the bottom, reducing the depth from ten to nine *tefachim*. Has one carried from *reshut harabbim* into *reshut hayachid*? The act began with a valid *reshut hayachid*, but by the time the dirt settled at the bottom, the well no longer qualified. Do we say that the *hanachah* — the placing — and the elimination of the *mechitza* occurred simultaneously, and therefore one is liable because the *reshut hayachid* still existed when the act was completed? Or do we say that since the *mechitza* was removed in the very act of placement, there is no valid *hanachah* in *reshut hayachid* and one is exempt? One might invoke the principle of *kluta k'mi she-huncha damya* — that the moment an airborne object enters a domain it is treated as having been placed there — but that principle's applicability here is itself subject to debate.

### Dates on Wall

To potentially resolve these questions, the *gemara* cites a *mishnah* involving a different case. One throws an object ארבע *amot* in *reshut harabbim* and it strikes a wall: if it hits at a height above ten *tefachim* it is like throwing in the air and one is exempt; if it hits below ten *tefachim* it is like throwing on the ground and one is liable for *hazarak arba amot b'reshut harabbim*. The *gemara* had previously questioned this *mishnah* — the object presumably bounced off the wall rather than coming to rest — and Rabbi Yochanan resolved it by explaining that the *mishnah* speaks of *dveila shemena*, a sticky mass of compressed dates, which adheres to the wall upon impact.

The *gemara* then asks: why is one liable in this case? *Ha kamivtzar me'arba amot* — the mass of dates, once stuck to the wall, reduces the effective traversed distance to slightly less than four *amot* (since the object's resting position is on the wall, not at the full four *amot* of open ground). *Tosafot* rejects *Rashi*'s reading of this objection (that the back of the date rests short of four *amot*), arguing that if that were the case one would be straightforwardly exempt because the four *amot* were simply not completed, comparable to the case of a basket straddling domains. *Tosafot* instead understands the question as follows: a clump of dates protruding from the wall at a height below ten *tefachim* creates a structural protrusion that, if it were a true building element, would interrupt the *reshut harabbim* beneath it. The question is whether that protrusion — created by the act of throwing — has now altered the domain, such that the object did not land in *reshut harabbim* after all.

### Architecture Change

The *gemara* resolves the matter with a crucial distinction. *Hasam lo mevatlei* — in the case of the dates on the wall, the structure is not truly altered. A sticky mass of dates attached to a wall is a temporary accretion; no one considers the fundamental architecture of the space to have changed. The dates will be removed, the wall remains what it is, and the surrounding space remains *reshut harabbim*. Therefore one is liable, and the objection about architectural change does not apply.

*Hacha mevatlei* — in the case of the well, however, the situation is genuinely different. When one throws earth into the well and fills it up, or digs earth out and deepens it, one is truly and permanently altering the physical structure of the space. The *mechitza* is genuinely eliminated or created. The *gemara* therefore declines to derive an answer to Rabbi Yochanan's questions from the dates case: they are structurally dissimilar. Rabbi Yochanan's questions — about both digging and filling a well — remain unresolved.

## Plank & Pegs

### Rava Q.

Rava poses a further question. Suppose there are two pegs (*yesedot*) standing in some space, each ten *tefachim* high, but neither is four *tefachim* wide, and they are not enclosing any space that would independently combine their dimensions. By themselves, they have no *reshut hayachid* status — unlike the mound and the well, which are understood as part of a single apparatus. One then throws a plank (*daf*) and it lands across the tops of the two pegs. The plank, now resting atop the two ten-*tefach* supports, spans the gap and creates a surface that constitutes a *reshut hayachid*. What is the *halacha*?

### Simultaneity

The *gemara* asks: is this simply the same question Rabbi Yochanan already posed — whether the simultaneous creation of a *mechitza* and the placement of an object creates liability? *Hainu de'Rabbi Yochanan* — that is precisely what Rabbi Yochanan asked. The well case and this case would seem to be identical in structure, and since we were inclined to say there is no liability when the *akirah* does not begin from an established *reshut hayachid*, the same reasoning would apply here.

### Separation Theory

The *gemara* clarifies that Rava's question is in fact distinct. Rava's scenario involves throwing not just the plank but also an additional object resting on the plank — both thrown together. The plank is the vehicle that creates the *reshut hayachid*; the other object is the one whose carrying we wish to evaluate. Now, it is physically impossible for an object resting on a plank to remain perfectly stationary during flight: *ee efshar de-lo midalya purta* — inevitably, the object rises slightly off the plank at some point during the arc. There is therefore a momentary interval of separation between plank and object. If that separation means the plank lands first — even for an instant — and creates the *reshut hayachid*, and then the object completes its descent and lands within that newly-created *reshut hayachid*, then the act is in fact sequentially ordered: first the *reshut hayachid* came into being, and then the *hanachah* occurred. Under that analysis, one would be liable.

### Unresolved (Teiku)

The *gemara* leaves Rava's question unresolved: *teiku*. However, the framing of the question implies something significant. It appears that if the plank genuinely lands first — if the *reshut hayachid* actually exists, even momentarily, before the second object comes to rest — then liability does attach. One was in control of the entire process; one initiated the throw knowing that the plank would create the *reshut hayachid* and that the object would land within it. The fact that when the act began no *reshut hayachid* yet existed does not automatically exempt, as long as by the time the *hanachah* is completed the *reshut hayachid* is in place. The unresolved question is whether the in-flight separation is sufficient to establish that temporal sequence, or whether the simultaneous nature of the throw collapses the two events into a single undifferentiated act, foreclosing liability.

These questions — of domains coming into existence during the carrying act itself — will continue to be explored in the material ahead.
