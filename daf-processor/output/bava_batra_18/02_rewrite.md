# Bava Batra 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Cistern Debate

### Perek Overview

The second *perek* of Bava Batra is occupied with a central question: what are you permitted to do within your own property, and how far must you remain from the boundary line in order to avoid indirectly causing damage or disruption to your neighbor's property? The specific focus at the opening of our daf is the *mishna* governing cisterns. When one digs a cistern in one's property, the act can weaken the surrounding ground. Accordingly, if a neighbor already has a cistern, one may not construct a similar pit, ditch, or water-containing structure in a way that will compromise the neighbor's cistern — one must remain *shlosha tefachim* away from the boundary line, and one must also plaster the walls of the excavation.

### Abaye's Position

The plain reading of the *mishna* seems to limit this obligation to situations where the neighbor has already built a cistern. If nothing yet exists on the other side of the boundary line, one would seemingly be permitted to construct right up to that line. This is the position of Abaye: the distancing requirement is triggered only when there is an existing structure that one's project actually threatens.

### Rava's Position

Rava disagrees. In his view, the *mishna* is not to be read as restricted to cases where a cistern already stands on the neighbor's side. Even if nothing has yet been built there, one must still remain *shlosha tefachim* from the boundary line, so as not to compromise the neighbor's future ability to build. The distancing obligation, on Rava's reading, is categorical and anticipatory.

---

## Soft Ground Case

### Soft Ground Setup

The Gemara attempts to resolve this dispute by examining a *braita* regarding what is called *sela haba'ah b'yadayim* — ground so soft that it can essentially be dug by hand. The *braita* states: when two neighbors each wish to dig a cistern, each must remain *shlosha tefachim* from the boundary line and plaster the walls. This appears to support Rava, since the rule is stated without requiring that either party's cistern already exist — each is simply told to stay distant.

### Soft Ground Exception

The Gemara rejects this proof. *HaBa'ah b'yadayim shani* — soft ground is different. Because the ground is so unstable, special precautions are inherently necessary regardless of the general rule. The *braita* may therefore reflect a specific stringency applicable only to that unusual case, and need not indicate anything about ordinary firm ground.

### Three Tefachim Rule

The Gemara then explains what *chiddush* the *braita* actually comes to teach. One might have thought that precisely because the ground is so soft, *shlosha tefachim* would be insufficient — that perhaps a far greater distance, say *asara tefachim* or *arba amot*, would be required. The *braita* therefore teaches that even in the soft-ground case, *shlosha tefachim* of distance with proper plastering is adequate. But the underlying assumption of this *chiddush* — namely, that *shlosha tefachim* is required from the boundary line even absent an existing cistern on the other side — was taken for granted by the Gemara. The *braita* thus does not resolve the Abaye-Rava dispute.

## Heat-Based Damages

### Heat Materials Mishna

The Gemara turns to a series of *mishnayot* and attempts to extract a proof from each. The first *mishna* addressed states: one must distance *gefet* (the residue from an olive press), dung, salt, plaster, and certain types of rocks from a neighbor's wall by *shlosha tefachim* — or alternatively, plaster the containing area to prevent damage to the wall. All of these materials generate heat and can damage a wall.

### Boundary Line Reading

The apparent implication of the *mishna* is straightforward: *ta'ama d'ika kotel* — the reason for the distancing requirement is that there is a wall present. *Ha leit ka kotel* — if there were no wall, one could presumably place these materials right up against the boundary line. This would support Abaye's position.

### Heat as Damage

The Gemara rejects this inference. Even without a wall, one cannot bring these materials flush against the boundary line. Rather, the *mishna* is telling us something about the nature of the problem: *d'hani kashu l'kotel* — these materials are damaging to walls. That is the source of the legal concern. Because the underlying problem is their capacity to damage a wall, the obligation to distance applies even when no wall yet stands — so as not to compromise the neighbor's ability to build one. The *mishna* illustrates the concrete mechanism of harm; that mechanism then generates an obligation that applies categorically.

---

## Moisture Damages

### Moisture Mishna

The same pattern repeats with a second *mishna*: seeds, plowing, and urinating must be kept *shlosha tefachim* from a neighbor's wall. Again, the apparent reading is that the obligation is predicated on the presence of a wall.

### Moisture Problem

The Gemara gives the identical response: even without a wall, one cannot position these activities against the boundary line. The *mishna* is teaching that *matunta kashya l'kotel* — moisture is damaging to walls. That is the underlying problem, and it is what generates the distancing obligation in all cases. Once we know that moisture is damaging, we apply the rule even absent a concrete wall on the other side.

---

## Vibration Damages

### Millstone Mishna

The pattern continues with millstones: the lower stone (*shechev*) must be kept three *tefachim* from the wall, and the upper stone (*rechev*) four *tefachim*. The *mishna* phrases this in terms of distance from the neighbor's wall, suggesting again that a wall is a precondition.

### Vibration Source

The Gemara responds identically. Even without a wall, one cannot place a mill against the boundary line. The *mishna* comes to tell us that *tiraya kashya l'kotel* — the trembling and vibrations of the mill are harmful to walls. That is the source of the problem, and it applies regardless of whether a wall currently stands. An additional benefit of identifying the concrete harm in each case — heat, moisture, vibration — is that it allows us to extend these rules to analogous situations not explicitly covered by the *mishna*.

---

## Oven Heat Damage

### Oven Mishna

The same analysis is applied to ovens. An oven must be kept three *tefachim* from the lower part of the plastered area (*kilya*), or four *tefachim* from the narrower upper rim (*safa*) — these measurements following Rashi and Tosafot respectively. The distance is, again, stated in terms of proximity to a neighbor's wall.

### Heat Problem Inference

*Ki leit ka kotel nami lo someich* — even without a wall, one may not place an oven against the boundary line. The *mishna* teaches that *havla kashya l'kotel* — heat is damaging to walls. That is the underlying harm; the distancing obligation follows from it universally.

## Dira Exception

### Bakery/Stable Mishna

The Gemara now examines a *mishna* that appears to introduce an important qualification. One may not open a bakery (*chanut shel nechtom*) or a dyer's shop (*tzavaim*) directly beneath a neighbor's storehouse (*otzar*), because the heat (according to Tosafot) or smoke will rise and damage the stored grain and other goods. Similarly, one may not operate a cattle stable (*refet bakar*) beneath a neighbor's storehouse, as the smell will damage what is stored above.

### Dira Exception

Crucially, the Gemara here does not give the same response it has given to every prior *mishna*. In all the earlier cases, the Gemara said: even without a concrete structure on the other side, you cannot go up to the boundary line. Here, however, the Gemara says: *dira shani* — a *dira*, a domicile or basic structure, is different. In this case, if there is no storehouse actually present on the other side, you are permitted to build your bakery or stable right up against the boundary line. The obligation to distance oneself is only triggered when there is an actual, concrete enterprise being directly threatened.

### Rashi on Dira

Rashi explains the principle as follows: *chanut v'refet bakar diratam shel adam hem v'ein lanu l'esor dirato alav ella im ken hizik muchach* — a shop and a cattle stable are basic human domiciles and structures, and we do not restrict a person's ability to build such structures unless there is demonstrated, concrete damage. The category of *dira* functions something like a zoning principle: while one can restrict where a person digs a cistern or places a pile of plaster, basic living and working structures may be erected freely up to the boundary line, so long as no existing structure is being directly harmed. If a neighbor does have a storehouse there, then of course the bakery or stable cannot be built so as to damage it. But in the absence of such a concrete threat, the *dira* category permits full use of one's property up to the line.

### Stable First Rule

The Gemara notes that the *mishna*'s special status as a *dira* case can also be inferred from a *braita* that elaborates upon it: *im hayta refet bakar kodemet l'otzar mutar* — if the stable was built before the storehouse was constructed, it is permitted. This "first in time" rule is mentioned only in connection with the *dira* case and not with respect to cisterns or the other categories discussed earlier. Why? Because the *braita* is emphasizing that in the *dira* case, even building right up to the boundary line was permitted from the outset. In other categories, being "first" might not protect you if you violated the underlying distancing rule — you could have arrived first but still built improperly, and you might still be required to relocate. Here, because the structure was permissible at the boundary line to begin with, arriving first is fully protective.

## Vineyard Work Space

### Tree Distance Mishna

The Gemara continues examining *mishnayot*. One may not plant a tree adjacent to a neighbor's grain field unless one has distanced it by *arba amot*. This is a notably larger distance than the *shlosha tefachim* that has appeared throughout the earlier cases.

### Vineyard Labor Space

A *braita* explains: *arba amot she'amru k'dei avodat hakerem* — the four *amot* mentioned is the amount of space needed to perform labor around a vineyard. Tosafot notes that while the tree in the *mishna* is not literally a vineyard, the standard measure of four *amot* derives from vineyard work and applies equally to an orchard. The reason for the distance is practical: one can plow a wheat field by working from the center outward to one's own edge, without needing to enter the neighbor's field. But tending a vineyard or orchard requires working from the outside in — pruning, picking, weeding — and if the trees or vines extend right to the neighbor's field, the orchard owner will inevitably have to step into the neighbor's grain field to perform that work. The four *amot* provides the buffer space needed for *avodat hakerem*.

### Distance Without Field

This again raises the question central to the Abaye-Rava debate: the distancing requirement appears conditioned on a grain field actually existing on the other side. If there is no grain field, it seems the tree could be planted right up to the boundary line. The Gemara observes this implication: *ta'ima mishum k'dei avodat hakerem ha lav mishum k'dei avodat hakerem samuch* — if the reason is the need for working space, then absent that reason, one could apparently go right up to the edge. And strikingly, this seems to mean that even the concern for roots spreading underground into the neighbor's field would not, by itself, mandate distancing. The roots concern is apparently subordinated to the *avodat hakerem* concern.

### Rock Barrier Case

The Gemara resolves this: *hacha b'mai askinan d'mafsik tzunma* — the case here assumes a rock formation running along the boundary line, which extends underground as well. Because the rock prevents roots from spreading, there is no root damage to worry about, and the only remaining concern is the need for working space. That is why, in this particular scenario, proximity to a grain field is what triggers the distancing obligation: it is the *avodat hakerem* concern alone that is operative.

### Wall Between Properties

The Gemara further notes the following scenario: if there is a wall between the two properties, one may place one's vineyard against the wall on one side and the neighbor's grain field against the wall on the other side. The same question arises — what about the roots? The Gemara resolves this the same way: the case involves a rock formation, so root spread is not a concern.

### Root Removal Right

The Gemara then considers the end of the *mishna*: if roots did nevertheless spread into a neighbor's property (*hayu shoreshav yotsim b'tokh shel chaveiro*), the neighbor is entitled to dig three *tefachim* into the ground and sever all roots that might obstruct plowing. The Gemara asks: if we are dealing with a case involving a rock barrier, how could roots have gotten through? The answer: the *mishna* is addressing an alternative scenario. Either there was no rock and roots spread naturally, or the rock was present but the roots somehow traversed it. In either such case, the neighbor may dig three *tefachim* down and remove them.

## Long-Distance Roots

### Tree-Cistern Mishna

The Gemara examines a *mishna* requiring that a tree be kept *esrim v'chamesh ama* — twenty-five *amot* — from a neighbor's cistern. The concern is that tree roots can spread that great a distance underground and ultimately crack or undermine the cistern. Twenty-five *amot* is roughly forty feet, a substantial distance.

### Distance Without Cistern

The Gemara raises the question directly relevant to Rava's position: *taama d'ika bor* — the *mishna* frames the obligation around the existence of an actual cistern. If there is no cistern, it sounds as if one could plant a tree even within twenty-five *amot* of the boundary line. But according to Rava, should one not be required to stay twenty-five *amot* from the boundary line even without a cistern, so as not to compromise the neighbor's ability to build one? The Gemara accepts this consequence: *ki leika bor nami lo samikh* — even without a cistern, you cannot plant the tree adjacent to the boundary line. The *mishna* comes to tell us that roots spread up to twenty-five *amot* and can damage a cistern — *that* is the source of the problem — and because that is the underlying harm, the twenty-five *amot* distance applies even in the absence of an existing cistern.

### Purchaser Exception

The Gemara then asks about the end of the *mishna*: *v'im ilan kadam lo yakots* — if the tree was already there first, it need not be cut down. But if Rava holds that even without a cistern one may not plant within twenty-five *amot* of the boundary line, how could a tree ever have been legitimately planted within that zone, such that it could now claim priority? The Gemara resolves this with a principle stated by Rav Pappa in another context: *b'lokayach* — we are speaking of a purchaser. The scenario is one in which the tree was originally planted in the middle of a single undivided field, where it was entirely legitimate. The owner subsequently divided the field and sold part of it to another party. Now, by virtue of the new boundary line, the tree happens to sit within twenty-five *amot* of the division. No violation ever occurred: the tree was planted before the boundary existed. In that case, the new neighbor cannot compel the tree to be cut down. The *lokayach* doctrine thus carves out an exception for trees (or other structures) that were legitimate at the time of planting and became "problematic" only because of a subsequent division of property. If, by contrast, a boundary already existed and the tree was planted within twenty-five *amot* of it, Rava would hold that the planting was a violation from the outset, and the tree would be subject to removal.

## Smell & Reciprocal

### Smell Damages

The Gemara examines several additional *mishnayot* in the same vein. One must keep the *misreh* — a place where flax is soaked, which generates powerful and unpleasant odors — away from a vegetable garden. One must keep *krashim* (cress) away from *betzalim* (onions). And one must keep mustard seed away from bees: because bees take nectar from surrounding plants and incorporate it into the honey, mustard in proximity to a beehive will produce undesirable honey, damaging the beekeeper's enterprise.

### Problem Pattern

Following the now-familiar pattern: *ta'ama d'ika yerek* — it sounds as though the flax-soaking restriction applies only when vegetables are actually present. *Ki leika yerek nami lo samikh* — but even without vegetables present, one cannot place the *misreh* against the boundary line. *D'hani kashu ahadadi* — the *mishna* is identifying that these things damage one another, and that underlying relationship generates the distancing obligation regardless of whether the threatened crop currently exists.

### Rabbi Yossi Bees

Rabbi Yossi introduces an exception specifically for mustard and bees: he permits mustard in proximity to bees, because the mustard grower can say to the beekeeper — *ad she'ata omer li harchaik chardalcha midvorai, harchaik dvoraycha michardali* — before you tell me to move my mustard away from your bees, move your bees away from my mustard, because they come and eat (*gilgulei chardali*) the mustard. The logic is one of reciprocal damage: you are damaging me no less than I am damaging you, and therefore I have no greater obligation to distance myself than you do.

This is striking for two reasons. First, Rabbi Yossi is not here invoking his general principle that it is the *nizak*'s responsibility to protect himself. Rather, he is making a specific claim that in the mustard-bee case the damage runs symmetrically in both directions. It is a limited argument confined to this particular case of mutual harm. Second, the question of who arrived first would seem to be highly relevant — if the bees were established first and the mustard grower is the newcomer, it would seem the mustard grower is the one causing disruption. Tosafot notes that enterprises like beehives and mustard crops are perhaps more readily relocated than a planted tree, which might explain why Rabbi Yossi is willing to entertain the argument that even a latecomer need not defer.

### Boundary Existence

This leads to a difficulty: if Rava holds that no one is permitted to establish any enterprise right at the boundary line — because one must always anticipate the neighbor's future use — then how does the mustard-bee scenario ever arise? Who is this mustard grower arguing with? If both parties must remain distant from the boundary line in anticipation of the other, neither would ever be in a position to claim that the other's enterprise is crowding them.

The Gemara works through this carefully. Rav Pappa's answer — *b'lokayach* — again provides the resolution. The scenario involves someone who originally planted or established an enterprise in the middle of a unified field and then divided it, with the result that the enterprise now sits close to the new boundary. The bee enterprise, for instance, was legitimately established; then the field was sold or divided, bringing a new neighbor into proximity. The Rabbanan say: now that the mustard grower is present, the mustard grower must distance himself from the bees. Rabbi Yossi says: no — since my bees damage your mustard as much as your mustard damages my bees, you are no more entitled to demand I move than I am entitled to demand you move.

But this reading, the Gemara notes, makes the Rabbanan's position difficult to understand. If the mustard was already there first — legitimately, by virtue of the *lokayach* scenario in the opposite direction — why would the Rabbanan require the mustard grower to uproot and move? It is at this point that Tosafot offers a fundamentally different reading of the entire sugya.

## Tosafot Reframe

### Damager Responsible

Tosafot reads the conclusion of the Gemara — introduced with the word *ella* — as a wholesale revision of how we understood the Abaye-Rava debate. Ravina states: *kasavar Rabbanan al ha-mazik leharchaik et atzmo* — the Rabbanan who dispute Rabbi Yossi hold that it is the *mazik*'s responsibility to distance himself from whatever he is damaging. On Tosafot's reading, this is not just a statement about the mustard-bee case — it retroactively reframes what Rava was saying all along.

### Cistern Direct Damage

Tosafot understands the debate between Abaye and Rava as follows: the *only* case where the distancing obligation applies even without a concrete existing structure on the other side is the case of a cistern. And why there? Because digging a cistern is an act of *direct* damage (*hezek karov*) to the neighboring ground. The very act of excavation weakens the surrounding earth, and that physical effect extends immediately to the neighbor's property — regardless of whether the neighbor has done anything there. In that case, even Rava requires distancing even without an existing cistern on the other side, because the harm is present and direct from the moment of digging.

### Concrete Structure

All other cases are different. In every other scenario examined — heat from plaster or ovens, moisture from seeds or urination, vibration from millstones, smell from flax — the obligation to distance arises only when there is an actual, concrete structure or enterprise on the other side that is being threatened. If nothing is there, one may go right up to the boundary line. The Rabbanan's position is not that one must anticipate the neighbor's future plans; it is simply that one may not be a *mazik* to something that actually exists. Absent any existing target of damage, one is not a *mazik* and there is no obligation to pull back. Rava, on Tosafot's reading, effectively retracts his broader position and is left holding only the narrow rule about cisterns.

## Nizak vs Mazik

### Responsibility Debate

The Gemara now addresses the foundational debate explicitly: is the responsibility to prevent harm that of the *mazik* (the one causing damage) or the *nizak* (the one being damaged)? The Rabbanan hold *al ha-mazik leharchaik et atzmo* — the damager must distance himself. Rabbi Yossi holds *al ha-nizak leharchaik et atzmo* — the damaged party must protect himself.

### Rabbi Yossi Nizak

Evidence for Rabbi Yossi's general position comes from elsewhere in the *mishna*: *af al pi she-bor kodemet l'ilan lo yakotz* — even though the cistern preceded the tree, the tree need not be cut down, because *ze chofer b'tokh shelo v'ze noteia b'tokh shelo* — this one digs within his own property and that one plants within his own property. This is a striking ruling. Even if the neighbor's cistern was there first, and even if one is clearly the *mazik* who arrived second and whose tree roots will certainly spread and damage the cistern, Rabbi Yossi says: you are master of your property. If the cistern owner does not want his cistern damaged, it is the cistern owner's responsibility to address that — not the tree planter's. This clearly reflects a categorical position: *al ha-nizak leharchaik et atzmo*.

If that is Rabbi Yossi's general principle, why did he argue the mustard-bee case specifically on the grounds of mutual damage? He should simply have said: even if my mustard damages your bees, it is your responsibility as the *nizak* to move your bees — full stop. Why introduce the mutual-damage argument at all?

### Rabbanan Mazik

The Gemara resolves this: Rabbi Yossi's mustard-bee argument was constructed *l'divreihem d'Rabbanan* — as an argument within the Rabbanan's own framework, accepting their premise. Rabbi Yossi is effectively saying: on my own view, *al ha-nizak leharchaik*, and even the flax grower need not distance himself from the vegetable garden — it is always the damaged party's problem. But I know you do not accept that. So let me argue within your framework. Even granting your position that the *mazik* must distance himself, you must concede that in the mustard-bee case you cannot identify a single *mazik*, because the damage is reciprocal. Therefore, even on your own terms, you cannot require one party alone to move.

### Mutual Damage

Rabbi Yossi's specific claim about the mustard-bee case: *tarvayhu mazkei ahadadi* — they both damage one another. The bees damage the mustard, and the mustard damages the honey. Therefore, even within the Rabbanan's framework of *al ha-mazik leharchaik*, there is no basis to require the mustard grower alone to distance himself, since the beekeeper is equally a *mazik*.

### Damage Threshold

The Rabbanan respond by disputing the factual premise: *devorim b'chardal lo mazkei lei* — the bees do not actually damage the mustard in any meaningful way. *Ei b'zinta lo mishkachat lah* — if the supposed damage is that bees eat the mustard seeds, that does not happen; bees do not eat seeds. *Ei b'tarpa hadri parchi* — if the damage is to the leaves, the leaves grow back. The Rabbanan are therefore denying that the damage to mustard meets the threshold of legally cognizable harm, and thus there is no symmetry. The mustard damages the bees' honey in a real and irreversible way; the bees do not damage the mustard in any equivalent sense. The *tarvayhu mazkei ahadadi* argument therefore fails, and the mustard grower remains the sole *mazik*, who must accordingly distance himself.

## Summary & Tensions

### First in Time

One tension that runs throughout the *sugya* and remains incompletely resolved is the question of priority: does it matter who established their enterprise first? The *lokayach* doctrine provides one clean answer — if an enterprise was established before any boundary existed, and a boundary was subsequently created through division or sale, that enterprise has full legitimacy even if it now sits in a position that would otherwise be problematic. And the observation that, on Tosafot's reading, one may in general build up to the boundary line when nothing yet exists on the other side, means that a first-established enterprise is broadly protected: if you planted your mustard before any neighboring beehive existed, you did nothing wrong, and the newcomer with the bees has to find a solution.

### Core Debate

The daf ultimately leaves us with several interlocking tensions. The Abaye-Rava debate — on Tosafot's reading of the *maskanah* — is narrow: it applies only to the cistern, where direct physical damage is caused by the act of digging itself, and Rava requires distancing even without an existing neighboring cistern. In all other cases, the Rabbanan's rule of *al ha-mazik leharchaik* applies, but only when there is a concrete, existing enterprise being threatened. Absent that, one is not yet a *mazik* and may use one's property freely up to the boundary line.

Against this, Rabbi Yossi takes the position that *al ha-nizak leharchaik et atzmo* in virtually all cases — the damaged party, not the damager, bears the responsibility of self-protection — with the exception of direct physical damage to the neighbor's property, where even Rabbi Yossi would concede. His argument against the Rabbanan in the mustard-bee context was purely internal to their framework: even granting their premise of *mazik* responsibility, the symmetry of harm in that specific case removes the basis for singling out the mustard grower.

The balance the Gemara strikes is a nuanced one: strong protection for the freedom to use one's own property; a meaningful but carefully bounded obligation not to damage what actually exists on the other side; and an ongoing conversation about how anticipatory obligations, temporal priority, and the nature of reciprocal harm interact.
