# Bava Batra 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishna: Mida vs. Chaseir…

### Mishna Setup

The *mishna* at the bottom of 104b introduces a case that will animate the entire discussion on our daf: a seller uses contradictory language in describing a land transaction. He says, "I am selling you a *beit kor* — an acre — by the measuring stick, more or less." On its face, the phrase *mida bechevel* ("by the measuring stick") suggests precision, while *hen chaseir hen yeter* ("more or less") suggests approximation. Both appear in the same contractual statement. How does the law treat this conflict?

### Chaseir Yeter Negates Mi…

The *mishna* rules that one goes by the last phrase stated. In the first scenario, the seller says *mida bechevel ani mocher lach, hen chaseir hen yeter* — he begins with the precise language and concludes with the approximate. The result: *batel mida bechevel* — the earlier phrase, "by the measuring stick," is nullified by the closing phrase, "more or less." The sale therefore follows the rules of approximation, as elaborated in the preceding *mishna*, meaning the measurement may vary by as much as a *rova* per *beit sa'ah*.

### Mida Negates Chaseir Yet…

In the second scenario the order is reversed: *hen chaseir hen yeter, mida bechevel* — he begins with the approximate language and concludes with the precise. Here too the *mishna* rules by the final phrase: *batel hen chaseir hen yeter* — the "more or less" opening is nullified by the closing "by the measuring stick," and the sale must conform to the rules of precision.

### Ben Nanas Attribution

The *mishna* attributes this ruling — that the last language stated always governs — to Ben Nanas: *divrei Ben Nanas*. What is striking is that the *mishna* presents only this single opinion, without recording any dissent. It is unusual for a *mishna* to stand alone with one named view and no counterposition. The *gemara* will supply the missing voice.

## Gemara: Rental Proof

### Chachamim Disagree

The *gemara* opens by noting, in the name of Rav Abba bar Mamal citing Rav, that *vecholkin alav chaverav al Ben Nanas* — Ben Nanas's colleagues in fact disagreed with him. The *mishna* therefore does not represent a consensus, even if it presents no alternative.

### Tzipori Bathhouse Case

The *gemara* then asks: is this information new? We might already know from another *mishna* that *tannaim* disputed Ben Nanas's position. The *gemara* points to a case in Tzipori: *maaseh beTzipori be'echad shesachar merchatz mechaveiro bishneim asar zehuvim leshanah* — a man rented a bathhouse from his fellow for twelve gold *dinarim* per year, followed by the phrase "one *dinar* of gold per month." At first glance these seem equivalent, but they are not. In a leap year containing thirteen months, the annual figure of twelve *dinarim* and the monthly rate of one *dinar* per month produce different totals. Which phrase controls? The case is structurally similar to ours: an opening formula and a closing formula with different legal implications.

### Split Difference Ruling

The *mishna* there records that the case came before Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yossi, who ruled *yachloku et chodesh ha'ibur* — split the leap month. Unable to determine whether the first or the second phrase should govern, they treated the dispute as a *safek* and divided the ambiguous month between landlord and tenant.

### Mammon Hamutal Besafek

This ruling reflects the principle of *mammon hamutal besafek*: when there is a genuine doubt over money, and one cannot resolve it through *muchzak* — the party currently in possession — one option is to divide it. The *gemara* cites this *mishna* to demonstrate that at least some *tannaim* did not follow Ben Nanas's rule of going by the last language; instead they held both phrases in tension and split the difference.

---

## Clarification vs. Revers…

### Reversal/Explication

The *gemara* presses further: can we really derive from the rental case that the debate applies equally to our *mishna* about land sales? Not necessarily. *Hava amina hatam hu de'ika leimar mehadar ka hadar beih ve'ika leimar peirushei ka mefaraish* — in that case, the second phrase could be understood either as a reversal of the first, or as an explication of it. The distinction initially seems unclear. If it is a reversal, one goes by the second phrase — the landlord declared one *dinar* per month, overriding his earlier statement. If it is an explication, it would also seem to favor the second phrase: what did he mean by "twelve *dinarim* per year"? He was simply clarifying that he assumed a normal twelve-month year, meaning one *dinar* per month. Whether characterized as reversal or explication, the outcome appears identical — the second phrase wins.

### Rashbam Explication

The Rashbam resolves this difficulty by explaining that "explication" here does not mean a reinterpretation of the total price. Rather, the second phrase — one *dinar* per month — may have been intended to clarify the *structure* of the rental arrangement, not its total cost. The tenant was saying: I intend this to be a monthly lease rather than an annual lease, governing questions of when payment is due and when either party may exit the arrangement. Under this reading, the twelve-*dinar* annual total stands, and the second phrase addresses only the periodicity of payment, not the question of the leap month. Since the second phrase may therefore be read as consistent with the first rather than contradicting it, the two phrases do not stand in outright opposition, and the doubt is genuine — hence the ruling to split the difference.

### Clear Contradiction

Given that ambiguity, one might have thought the *tannaitic* debate over splitting applies only where the second phrase can be read as mere explication — leaving open the possibility that where phrases are in clear contradiction, all would agree to go by the last phrase, as Ben Nanas holds. It is to forestall this inference that Rav Abba bar Mamal's statement is necessary: even in cases of clear, unambiguous contradiction — where approximation and precision cannot be harmonized — there are those who maintain that the phrases remain in tension, the matter is a *safek*, and one does not simply defer to the last language stated.

---

## Shmuel: Pachot Shebalesh…

### Ben Nanas vs. Chachamim

A parallel tradition is then introduced: Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel says, *zu divrei Ben Nanas* — this is Ben Nanas's view — *vechachamim omrim halach achar pachot shebilshono* — but the Sages say: go by the minimum of the two formulations.

### Pachot Shebaleshonot

The principle of *pachot shebaleshonot* — follow the lesser of the stated terms — is here articulated as the position of the *Chachamim* against Ben Nanas. The minimum in question, as the Rashbam explains, is minimum from the perspective of the buyer: one goes by the formulation that gives the purchaser the least, and thus favors the seller, who remains in possession of his property.

### Muchzak Principle

The reason for this disposition, as the *gemara* makes clear, is the principle of *muchzak*: the owner of the land is currently in possession, and when two contractual formulations stand in contradiction, the party seeking to wrest assets from the *muchzak* must make his case unambiguously. Since the buyer cannot point to a clear, uncontested formulation supporting his claim to the larger quantity, he receives only the minimum the language unambiguously supports. This is distinct from splitting the difference — rather than halving the disputed amount, the law simply leaves the seller holding what the language cannot definitively transfer.

## Kor Beshloshim Case

### Kor for 30 / Se'ah per S…

When Shmuel says "these are the words of Ben Nanas" without explicitly endorsing them, the *gemara* infers that Shmuel rejects Ben Nanas and holds instead like the *Chachamim* — that contradictory language creates a *safek* resolved by *muchzak*. But this inference is immediately challenged. The *gemara* notes: *veha Rav u'Shmuel d'amri tarvaihu* — Rav and Shmuel both said — *kor bishloshim ani mocher lach yachol lachzor bo afilu bese'ah achronah* — "I am selling you a *kor* for thirty": until the very last *se'ah* is transferred, either party may retract, because the entire *kor* is treated as a single unit. However, if one says *kor bishloshim, se'ah besela ani mocher lach* — "a *kor* for thirty, each *se'ah* for one *sela*" — then the second formulation governs: the transaction is structured as thirty separate unit sales, and *rishon rishon kana* — each *se'ah* as it is measured out becomes a completed, irrevocable sale.

### Last Language Wins

The *chiddush* of this ruling is not merely that a *kor* for thirty differs structurally from a *se'ah* per *sela*. The novelty is that when both formulations are stated together, the second determines the structure of the transaction — the bulk pricing of the *kor* gives way to the unit pricing of the *se'ah per sela*. The later language wins.

### Shmuel & Ben Nanas

This appears to place Shmuel squarely in Ben Nanas's camp: when two phrases conflict, go by the second. Why, then, did Shmuel seem to dismiss Ben Nanas in favor of *pachot shebaleshonot*?

---

## Resolution: Muchzak Rules

### Consistent Muchzak

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ela le'olam zu velo sevira lei* — in truth, Shmuel does reject Ben Nanas. When Shmuel says "these are the words of Ben Nanas," he consistently means he does not hold that way. Shmuel's own position is that contradictory formulations are held in tension, the matter becomes a *safek*, and one goes by the *muchzak*. This is coherent with *pachot shebaleshonot*: the *chachamim's* rule of taking the minimum is simply the application of *muchzak* to the specific context of a land sale.

### Rental Case Timing

The *gemara* then revisits the rental case to confirm this consistency. Shmuel himself explained the *mishna* in Tzipori as a case where the dispute arrived before the court in the middle of the leap month — *beva be'emtza chodesh askinan*. In that scenario the ruling to split the month follows directly from *muchzak*.

### Middle/Beginning/End

Three distinct scenarios emerge depending on when the case comes to court. If the dispute arises in the middle of the leap month (*Adar Sheni*), the tenant has already lived there for half the month without paying, so he is in possession of that half; the landlord, meanwhile, retains possession of the remaining half of the month's rental value. Each holds the *muchzak* in half, and therefore they split it. If the case arises at the *beginning* of the leap month — before the tenant has dwelled there at all — then the landlord is fully *muchzak* in the month's rent, and *kula lemashkir*: the tenant must pay the full *dinar* for that month if he wishes to remain. If the case arises at the *end* of the month — after the tenant has already lived out the entire month — then the tenant is *muchzak*: he has already resided there and has not yet been made to pay, and so *kula lesocher*, the landlord cannot compel payment for that month. In every scenario, the outcome tracks who is in possession at the moment of adjudication. This is entirely consistent with Shmuel's principle, and not with Ben Nanas's rule of automatically following the later language.

### Kor Muchzak Logic

But if Shmuel consistently applies *muchzak*, what of the *kor* case, where Shmuel appeared to rule by the second language and not by *muchzak*? The *gemara* answers: *de-hatam taama mai? mishum de-tafis, hacha nami ha-tafis* — the reason there is precisely *muchzak*, and the same logic applies here. When both formulations appear together, the two understandings of the transaction stand in contradiction — is this a single bulk sale or thirty unit sales? Since the language does not clearly resolve the question, the law defaults to *muchzak*. As each *se'ah* is measured out and transferred, the buyer takes physical possession of it. At that moment he is *muchzak* in that unit, and *rishon rishon kana* — each *se'ah* is a finalized sale. The *gemara* acknowledges a certain complexity here: if the buyer is simultaneously *muchzak* in the object and in his money, he could arguably claim both that the sale is final (because he holds the *se'ah*) and that it is not (because he intended the whole *kor* as a unit). The *gemara* does not pursue this further, but the principle it invokes is consistent throughout: Shmuel's ruling on the *kor* case is not an endorsement of Ben Nanas but another application of *muchzak* to a case of genuinely ambiguous contractual language.

## Istira Case

### Istira Definition

The *gemara* then turns to a new teaching attributed to Rav Huna in the name of Rav. The case involves the *istira*, a coin whose nominal name suggests a round figure but whose actual value is ninety-six *ma'im*, not one hundred. So if a seller says *istira, me'ah ma'ei* — "an *istira*, one hundred *ma'im*" — he has combined an approximate term (the *istira*, which implies ninety-six) with a precise figure (one hundred). The situation mirrors our *mishna*: approximate and precise language appear together.

### Rav: Last Language

Rav rules: *tfos lashon acharon* — go by the last language stated. If he said *istira, me'ah ma'ei*, the buyer owes one hundred *ma'im*, because that is the final formulation. If he said *me'ah ma'ei, istira*, the buyer owes ninety-six, the value of an *istira*. This is the Ben Nanas position applied to a monetary transaction.

### Apparent Repetition

The *gemara* immediately challenges: did Rav not already say this? *Ha amrah Rav chada zimna* — Rav said it once before. In the context of the bathhouse rental, the *gemara* records Rav as saying: *ee havei hatam havei yahivna kula lemashkir* — had he been present at the rental adjudication, he would have awarded the entire disputed month to the landlord. Since "one *dinar* per month" is the final phrase, and that formulation favors the landlord, Rav would have ruled entirely in his favor. That too reflects the principle of *tfos lashon acharon*. Why, then, does the *gemara* need Rav to state it again in the *istira* case?

---

## Explication Caveat

### Explication vs. Contradi…

The answer lies in the potential to distinguish the rental case. One might have concluded that Rav's ruling in the rental context rested not on the general principle that the second language always wins, but on the specific feature that in the rental the two formulations could plausibly be read as consistent — twelve per year, meaning a normal year, meaning one per month. Under that reading, the second phrase does not override the first but rather explicates it, clarifying what the speaker intended all along. If so, Rav's ruling there would apply only when explication is possible — when the second phrase can be understood as commentary on the first rather than as a contradiction of it. In a case of genuine contradiction, like nine-ninety-five versus ten dollars, where no harmonization is available, perhaps even Rav would treat it as a *safek*.

### Second Language Wins

The *istira* case forecloses this interpretation. *Ma'u detema perusha kimefaresh kamashmalan* — one might have thought that in the rental Rav was merely ruling based on possible explication. The *istira* case makes clear that Rav's principle operates even where the two formulations flatly contradict one another and no harmonization is possible. In that setting too, the second language wins outright.

### Rav Consistent

This confirms that Rav holds consistently with Ben Nanas across all cases: whether the language can be explicated or whether it is a true contradiction, the final formulation stated is the one that governs the transaction.

---

## Three Positions Summary

### Ben Nanas/Rav Camp

Three distinct positions now emerge from the *sugya*. The first is that of Ben Nanas, endorsed by Rav: *tfos lashon acharon* — in all cases of contradictory contractual language, one follows the last phrase stated. Applied to the land sale, whichever term appears at the end of the formulation — whether precise or approximate — is the operative one. Applied to the rental, Rav would give the entire disputed month to the landlord because "one *dinar* per month" was the closing phrase. Applied to the *istira* case, the final stated price is the one binding on buyer and seller alike.

### Tannaim: Split

The second position is that of the *tannaim* in Bava Metzia, reflected in the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yossi in the Tzipori case: the two formulations remain in genuine tension, creating a state of *mammon hamutal besafek*, and one therefore *cholkin* — divides the disputed amount between the parties.

### Shmuel: Muchzak

The third position is Shmuel's: the formulations are likewise held in contradiction and create a *safek*, but rather than splitting the difference, one resolves the doubt by identifying the *muchzak* — the party currently in possession — and ruling in his favor.

### Apply to Cases

Each position yields distinct outcomes across the cases examined on this daf. In the land sale, Ben Nanas and Rav follow whichever phrase came last; the *tannaim* would split the disputed portion of land; and Shmuel would apply *pachot shebaleshonot*, awarding the seller — the *muchzak* in the land — the benefit of the contradiction, leaving the buyer with only the minimum the language unambiguously supports. In the rental, Ben Nanas and Rav award the leap month entirely to whichever party is favored by the final phrase; the *tannaim* split the month; and Shmuel goes by *muchzak*, producing the three-way split depending on whether the case comes to court at the beginning, middle, or end of the disputed month. In the *kor bishloshim* case, what appears to be a ruling by the final language is, under Shmuel's analysis, actually an application of *muchzak* as well — each *se'ah* transferred finalizes as a completed sale at the moment the buyer takes possession of it.
