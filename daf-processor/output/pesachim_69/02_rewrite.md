# Pesachim 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Preparatory Acts

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

The daf picks up in the middle of 69b with a dispute found in the *mishnah* between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua concerning *machshirei mitzvah* — preparatory acts — for the *korban Pesach* when *erev Pesach* falls on Shabbat. Rabbi Eliezer holds that one may perform even *d'rabanans* — rabbinic prohibitions — that could have been done beforehand, in order to bring the *korban Pesach*, even on Shabbat. Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees.

### Firewood & Reshus

Rabbi Yehoshua draws a proof from the prohibition against chopping firewood on *Yom Tov* to make a fire for cooking: even though cooking on *Yom Tov* is permitted, the preparatory act of chopping wood that could have been done from beforehand is not. To this, Rabbi Eliezer responds that chopping wood for cooking is a *reshus* — an optional act — whereas the *korban Pesach* is a *mitzvah*, and there is therefore no valid comparison. Notably, in taking this position, Rabbi Eliezer implicitly characterizes having good food on *Yom Tov* as a *reshus* rather than a *mitzvah*.

### Simchat Yom Tov

The *Gemara* connects this to a broader dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that *simchat Yom Tov* — the physical pleasure of the holiday — is itself a *mitzvah*. Accordingly, preparing food for *Yom Tov* is comparable to preparatory acts for the *korban Pesach*, and the analogy is apt. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, holds that one may devote the entire *Yom Tov* to *l'Hashem* — to purely spiritual *simcha* — and therefore physical enjoyment is not necessarily a *mitzvah* at all. The *Gemara* reads back into the first dispute: what made it obvious that Rabbi Yehoshua's proof was legitimate was precisely *his own position* that *simchat Yom Tov* carries the force of a *mitzvah*.

## Atzeret Division

### All or Nothing

A *baraita* elaborates on the underlying dispute. Rabbi Eliezer says: a person has nothing on *Yom Tov* except one of two choices — either *ochel v'shoteh*, eating and drinking, or *yoshev v'shoneh*, sitting and learning. There is no hybrid. The dedication must be total: either fully physical or fully spiritual.

### Half and Half

Rabbi Yehoshua says *chelkeihu* — divide it. Half should go to *achila v'shtiya*, eating and drinking, and half to the *beit hamidrash*. This is not simply a pragmatic compromise; it reflects a principled view about the nature of *Yom Tov* and what is demanded of a person on such days. One might observe that *t'filla* is conspicuously absent from the discussion — this is consistent with the rabbinic orientation in which the primary mode of religious expression is *talmud Torah*, not prayer.

### Samach Lifnei

Rabbi Yochanan teaches that both *tannaim* derive their positions from a single juxtaposition of verses: one verse reads *Atzeret l'Hashem Elokecha* — the *atzeret* is for Hashem — while another reads *Atzeret tihyeh lachem* — it is for you. Rabbi Eliezer reads the two verses as presenting a choice: *o kulo l'Hashem o kulo lachem*, either entirely for God or entirely for yourself. Rabbi Yehoshua reads them as complementary: *chetzyo l'Hashem v'chetzyo lachem*, half for God and half for yourself.

It is worth noting that the Torah's description of *simchat Yom Tov* frequently emphasizes *sameach lifnei Hashem* — rejoicing in the presence of God. The *Gemara* elsewhere teaches *ein simcha ela b'vasar shelamim* — there is no *simcha* without the meat of a *shelamim*. The physical pleasure and the act of bringing a sacrifice were once integrated experiences, with the physical *simcha* serving as a manifestation of one's joy in the *mikdash*. In the post-*mikdash* reality, however, that integrated experience has fractured, and what was once a unified expression of *simcha lifnei Hashem* now requires some deliberate allocation between the spiritual and the physical.

### Shavuot Agreement

Rabbi Elazar — an *amora* — adds a striking qualification: *hakol modim b'Atzeret d'ba'inan nami lachem* — everybody agrees that on *Shavuot*, even Rabbi Eliezer concedes that we require an element of *lachem*, of physical pleasure. This is notable precisely because one would have expected the opposite: on the day the *Torah* was given, one might assume that everyone would agree we need *l'Hashem* — an expression of purely spiritual engagement, especially since, as noted, the primary expression of *l'Hashem* for the rabbis is *talmud Torah*, not *t'filla*. Rashi explains that the day carries such intense joy that it must find expression in *achila u'shtiya*.

A different approach suggests itself. On other days of the year, one may express one's religious life through purely spiritual pursuits without any risk of misrepresenting the nature of *Torah*. But on the day the *Torah* was given, to engage exclusively in spiritual *limud* would risk conveying the wrong message — that the *Torah* belongs to heaven, that it is the exclusive domain of the spiritual, as the *malachim* argued it should remain. It was Moshe Rabbeinu who replied to the angels: does the *Torah*'s prohibition of *kibbud av va'em* apply to you? Does *lo tirtzach* speak to your situation? The *Torah* was given to human beings living in the physical world. On *Yom shenitna bo Torah*, we must demonstrate that the *Torah* finds its fullest expression precisely in how we live our lives in this world — that even *achila u'shtiya* can become the expression of a *mitzvah*. On other days, there is no risk of this message being lost; on *Shavuot*, there is.

## Oneg Shabbat

### Shabbat Lachem

Rava extends the principle further: *hakol modim be'Shabbat d'ba'inan nami lachem* — everyone agrees, as a regular weekly matter applying far more broadly than the once-or-twice-yearly *Yomim Tovim*, that Shabbat requires *lachem*, physical pleasure. The source is the verse *vekarata l'Shabbat Oneg* — you shall call Shabbat a delight, a day of *oneg*.

### Oneg vs. Simcha

There is an important distinction between *oneg* and *simcha*. *Simcha* — joy — is a capacious category: it can arise from a fine meal, but equally from an enriching conversation, an inspiring experience, or an elevated *limud*. Joy is an inner state of elevation and completeness that can be reached through many paths, and it is therefore plausible that for some people *kulo l'Hashem* is itself a path to *simcha*. *Oneg*, by contrast, connotes specifically physical pleasure and enjoyment. The Torah does not say *vekarata l'Shabbat simcha*; it says *oneg*. This is why, on *Yom Tov*, it is *simcha* that is emphasized, whereas on Shabbat, the concept foregrounded is *oneg* — and why the specific *halachot* of Shabbat, from *shalosh seudot* to the preparation of the Shabbat meal, are so precisely oriented around this quality of physical delight.

### Yom Kippur Shabbaton

Why must Shabbat in particular emphasize physical *oneg*? One consideration emerges from a comparison with *Yom Kippur*. The Rambam explains that we fast on *Yom Kippur* not primarily as a form of self-affliction for purposes of *teshuva*, but because *Yom Kippur* is *Shabbat Shabbaton* — a complete *Shabbat*, meaning *Shabbat mimlacha u'Shabbat mei'achila v'shtiya*. It is a full cessation of our physical engagement with the world, both in terms of creative action and in terms of sustaining our bodies. The ideal of *Yom Kippur* is a day of total withdrawal from the physical dimension of existence. That is the meaning of *Shabbat Shabbaton*.

### Shabbat Rest

The other *Shabbatot*, by contrast, must ensure that *oneg* is present — and this is important on at least two levels. First, one must not overextend the idea that the highest Jewish religious ideal is the complete renunciation of the physical world. But second, and equally, one must not allow Shabbat to become associated in the mind — particularly in the minds of children — with deprivation. When a day defined by cessation from weekday activities becomes experienced primarily as a day of *what you can't do*, it invites dread rather than delight. The antidote is precisely the *lachem*: the beautiful Shabbat table, the fine food, the warmth of the experience — these ensure that the day of rest from the world is associated with *oneg* rather than with its absence. On Shabbat there is a special imperative to cultivate that association.

## Purim & Erev YK

### Purim Celebration

Rav Yosef adds that *hakol modin b'Purim d'ba'inan nami lachem* — everyone agrees that *Purim* requires physical pleasure. The source is explicit in the *megilla*: *yemei mishteh v'simcha* — days of feasting and rejoicing — where the *mishteh*, the drinking and feasting, is written directly into the text.

### Fasting Exceptions

The *Gemara* then relates that Mar bar Ravina would fast the entire year — presumably maintaining a pattern of daytime fasting with eating at night, a kind of year-round regimen of ascetic practice. The three exceptions to his fasting were *Atzarta*, *Shavuot*; *Puraya*, *Purim*; and *ma'alei Yoma d'Kippuri*, *erev Yom Kippur*. Reading this carefully, the *Gemara* seems to indicate that even *Yom Tov* and Shabbat were included in his year-round fast — though there is a known concept of a *ta'anit chalom* on Shabbat, the plain reading here does seem to extend further. In any event, three days stood apart: *Shavuot*, because it is *yom shenitna bo Torah*; *Purim*, because *yemei mishteh v'simcha* is written explicitly; and *erev Yom Kippur*, as the *baraita* of Chiya bar Rav mi-Diftei explains.

### Erev YK Eating

That *baraita* teaches: *v'innitem et nafshoteichem b'tish'a lachodesh* — you shall afflict your souls on the ninth of the month. But surely we fast on the tenth, not the ninth? The *Gemara* derives: *kol ha'ochel v'shoteh b'tish'a bo*, one who eats and drinks on the ninth — on *erev Yom Kippur* — it is counted as though one had fasted both the ninth and the tenth.

Several explanations are offered for this striking formulation. One approach: since one cannot observe *simchat Yom Tov* and *kavod Yom Tov* on *Yom Kippur* itself, those observances are displaced to *erev Yom Kippur*, which is why people set a festive table and make a proper meal on that day. A second approach, consistent with the Rambam's understanding of the purpose of the *Yom Kippur* fast — that it is not meant as self-affliction but as a day of complete separation from the physical — suggests that one eats on the ninth precisely so that the fast does not become an act of undue hardship; the fasting would then be done for the right reasons rather than as a form of self-flagellation.

A third approach parallels the argument made regarding *Shavuot*: on the day that is entirely devoted to divorcing oneself from the physical world, it is equally important, on the day before, to demonstrate that serving God through *achila u'shtiya* is no less an act of *avodah*. It is genuinely challenging to experience eating as *avodas Hashem* — but *davka* on *erev Yom Kippur*, when the following day will be entirely about cessation from the physical, we are called upon to demonstrate that serving God in our embodied, eating-and-drinking lives is also *mitzvah*. There is a story of an elderly rabbi, a *talmid* of the Chofetz Chaim who had survived the *Mir Yeshiva*'s years in Shanghai, who in his nineties was told by his doctor that he could no longer fast on *Yom Kippur*. He was devastated. The doctor said to him: the same way you served God all these years by fasting on *Yom Kippur*, now you will serve God by eating on *Yom Kippur*. A profound formulation of the same principle.

### Ninth vs. Tenth

The Rav offers an additional explanation for the emphasis on eating on the ninth: it serves to make a demonstrative distinction between the ninth and the tenth. Just as we do not blow *shofar* on *erev Rosh Hashana* so as to mark the distinction between the day before and the *Yom Tov* itself, similarly eating robustly on the ninth creates a clear demarcation from the fast of the tenth. This connects as well to the *Rashi*-influenced reading that just as by *Atzeret* we demonstrate our joy at having received the *Torah*, so on *erev Yom Kippur* the eating expresses the *rishuma de'chapara* — the imprint of the atonement that is coming. Indeed, the *Gemara* elsewhere teaches *lo hayu yamim tovim b'Yisrael k'chamisha asar b'Av u'k'Yom HaKippurim* — no days were as joyous for Israel as the fifteenth of Av and *Yom Kippur*, which is *yom slicha u'mechila u'chapara*. Since that joy cannot be expressed on *Yom Kippur* itself, it finds its expression on *erev Yom Kippur*.

## Egla Talita

### Yosef's Feast

The *Gemara* relates that Rav Yosef, on *Shavuot*, would instruct his household: *avdi li egla tlata* — make for me a choice calf. The *egla tlata* was a particularly fine and luxurious dish, reserved for occasions of genuine celebration. And he would explain his reasoning: *ei lav hai yoma d'ka garim, kama Yosef ika b'shuka* — were it not for this day that brought about everything it brought about, there would be plenty of Josephs in the marketplace. Meaning: without *yom shenitna bo Torah*, without the day the *Torah* was given and the world it created, he would be just another unremarkable person. It is the *Torah* and the day it was given that gives his life its entire meaning and religious purpose. Therefore, on this day above all, he expresses profound *simcha*.

### Sheshet's Joy

A parallel story is told of Rav Sheshet, whose regimen was to review all of his learning every thirty days — *kol taltin yomin mahader lei letalmudei* — and then go stand by the river bank and say: *chadai nafshai chadai nafshai* — my soul rejoices, my soul is glad. *Lach karai, lach tanai* — for you have I studied *mikra*, for you have I learned *mishna* and the rabbinic corpus. What Rav Sheshet is expressing is the profound personal pleasure he takes in his *Torah* learning — a joy so complete that it finds expression in this outburst of personal delight.

### Torah's Role

The *Gemara* challenges this: *ini*? Is that really so — that one takes personal pleasure in one's learning as if for oneself? Did not Rav Elazar teach: *ilmalei Torah lo nitkaymu shamayim va'aretz* — were it not for *Torah*, the heavens and earth would not be sustained? As the verse says, *im lo briti yomam valaila, chukot shamayim va'aretz lo shamti* — were it not for my covenant of day and night, I would not have established the laws of heaven and earth. Torah keeps the world going. It is not about personal pleasure; it is about God and the cosmos. So what is this sense of private joy and personal satisfaction?

### Personal Investment

The *Gemara* responds: *meikara ki avid inish ada'ata denafshei ka'avid* — initially, when a person throws himself into something, he does it based on his own *da'at*, his own mind, his own personal investment. What drives people initially into a life of *Torah* is not a philosophical understanding of *chukot shamayim va'aretz*; it is personal resonance, personal pleasure, the sense that this speaks to me. The joy of a *rebbe* who approaches learning with delight and enthusiasm; the experience of *Torah sha'ashua* — Torah as a source of pleasure and play — these are what generate the initial love and investment. Obviously one comes to understand, over time, that there is an infinitely larger dimension — *chukot shamayim va'aretz lo shamti* — but the personal investment is what motivates and sustains the engagement from the outset.

### Joy Expression

These two stories together illustrate something important: the greatest *talmidei chachamim* experienced their *Torah* learning as a source of profound personal joy, and that joy translated into the full expression of *simcha* — including its physical dimension. The *ba'inan nami lachem*, the requirement that even on *Shavuot* there be physical celebration, is not merely a concession to human nature. It is the full expression of what *simcha* looks like when a person's *Torah* life is genuinely one of joy and love.

## Eliezer's Position

### Machshirei Argument

The *Gemara* now returns to the substantive halachic dispute with which the daf opened. Rav Ashi clarifies: even according to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that cooking on *Yom Tov* is a *reshut*, one could challenge his position from a different direction. Even granting that cooking is a *reshut*, on *Yom Tov* one may perform *melacha* for the *reshut* purpose of cooking — and yet *d'rabanans* that could have been done beforehand are not permitted even to enable that cooking. If so: on Shabbat, where only *melacha* of *mitzvah* — such as the *korban Pesach* — is permitted, should it not be *a fortiori* that even the preparatory *d'rabanans* are prohibited? Rabbi Eliezer's response is that this *kal v'chomer* fails: it is true that Shabbat is more restrictive than *Yom Tov*, but what makes the *shevut* permissible here is that it is a *shevut d'mitzvah* — a rabbinic prohibition being overridden for the sake of a *mitzvah*. And *shevus d'mitzvah adif lei* — the weight of a *shevut* being performed for the sake of a *mitzvah* is sufficiently powerful to override even Shabbat.

A *baraita* then elaborates Rabbi Eliezer's position directly. Rabbi Eliezer reasons: *uma im docha machshirei mitzvah she-le'achar shechita et ha-Shabbat* — if preparatory acts performed *after* the *shechita* override Shabbat, then certainly preparatory acts *before* the *shechita* should override Shabbat.

### Burning Fats

What are the *machshirei mitzvah* performed after *shechita* that override Shabbat? According to Tosafot, this refers to burning the *chalavim* — the fats — of the *korban Pesach* on Shabbat itself. One could theoretically wait until *motzei Shabbat* to burn the fats — the *korban* has already been brought, the *mitzvah* in its essential form is done — and yet we permit burning the fats on Shabbat. If even these post-*shechita* acts override Shabbat despite being deferrable, then *a fortiori*, the pre-*shechita* acts that are necessary to *enable* the bringing of the *korban Pesach* — removing a *mum*, carrying the animal — should certainly override. This is the logical structure of Rabbi Eliezer's argument.

## Haza'ah Proof

### Haza'ah Prohibited

Rabbi Akiva responds with a counter-proof. He invokes *haza'ah* — the sprinkling of purification waters — for a *tamei meit* whose seventh day of *tumah* falls on Shabbat which is *erev Pesach*. This is a person who, without *haza'ah*, cannot become *tahor* in time to bring the *korban Pesach*. The *haza'ah* itself is only *d'rabanan* — it is a *shevut*. And yet everyone agrees: *haza'ah* is not performed on Shabbat even to enable the *korban Pesach*.

### Act vs. Preparation

Here is Rabbi Akiva's logic. Rabbi Eliezer argues that things enabling the *korban* should be permitted because they remove obstacles to the *mitzvah*. But Rabbi Akiva draws a categorical distinction: it is the *shechita* itself — the *hakrava* of the *korban* — that overrides Shabbat, not the enabling of one's personal obligation to bring it. Once the *shechita* has overridden Shabbat, everything flowing from that act — the post-*shechita* activities — is carried along in the same *hetter*. But before the *shechita* has overridden Shabbat, there is no basis to override preparatory acts, because it is not your personal need to be in a position to bring the *korban* that does the overriding — it is the act of *hakrava* itself. One should not confuse the two.

There is something genuinely attractive about Rabbi Eliezer's position — the idea that if the *mitzvah* obligation overrides, everything standing in the way of its fulfillment should yield. Nevertheless, we do not *pasken* like him.

### Psul Concern

A second counter-argument is introduced: *shema yimatzei zevach pasul v'nimtza mechallel Shabbat l'mafrei'a* — perhaps the *shechita* will turn out to be invalid, and then everything done beforehand to enable it will retroactively be a *chilul Shabbat* without justification. The *Gemara* immediately rejects this concern on logical grounds: by that reasoning, one should not perform the *shechita* at all, since it too might be *pasul*. Clearly, we do not void a *hetter* due to the mere possibility of *psul*. The *Gemara* notes that this weaker argument was recorded first, and the stronger argument — Rabbi Akiva's categorical distinction — came second, even though in the actual exchange it was the first argument given. The written version records the more powerful reasoning first.

### Eliezer's Response

Rabbi Eliezer responds to Rabbi Akiva: the case of *haza'ah* is different from all the other *machshirei mitzvah*. He acknowledges the question but maintains that *haza'ah* falls into a separate category — it is not simply a *shevut d'mitzvah* comparable to carrying the *korban* or removing a *mum*. As we will see, his exact reasoning for distinguishing *haza'ah* becomes the subject of sustained *Gemara* analysis.

## Akiva Reminds

### Inverse Logic

A *baraita* records the following exchange in greater detail. Rabbi Akiva offers what sounds at first like a foolish argument: just as *haza'ah* does not override Shabbat, so too *shechita* should not override Shabbat. Rabbi Eliezer responds indignantly: obviously *shechita* overrides — it says *bemo'ado*, implying even on Shabbat. He dismisses the comparison in sharp terms: *bishchita heshivatani, bishchita tehei mitato* — you challenge me with *shechita*? Your argument will be slain by *shechita*. The response is mock-contemptuous: it is self-evident that *shechita* overrides; the argument is absurd.

### Akiva's Tradition

Rabbi Akiva then reveals what he was actually doing. He says to Rabbi Eliezer: *Rebbi*, do not dismiss me in the middle of a legal argument — *al tikpacheini bishat hadin*. Do you really want to engage me head-on? Because *kach mekublani mimcha* — this is a tradition I received *from you*: *haza'ah shevut v'einah dochah et ha-Shabbat* — *haza'ah* is a *shevut* and it does not override Shabbat.

The structure of Rabbi Akiva's argument is now clear. By saying something apparently foolish — that *shechita* should not override just as *haza'ah* does not — he was not actually making a logical argument. He was signaling that he was treating the prohibition of *haza'ah* on Shabbat as axiomatic. What you hear when he makes that argument is: I am taking it as given — as a first principle — that *haza'ah* does not override. So certain was he of this that he built it into the premise of an *a fortiori* argument, not as a conclusion to be argued, but as something too obvious to require proof. And the reason he was so certain is that Rabbi Eliezer himself had transmitted this tradition to him.

### Eliezer Forgot

The *Gemara* asks: but if Rabbi Eliezer himself taught Rabbi Akiva that *haza'ah* does not override Shabbat, why was Rabbi Eliezer now backtracking, challenging the principle, and suggesting that perhaps one *would* do *haza'ah* for the *korban Pesach*? Ulla initially offers a reinterpretation: perhaps the tradition Rabbi Eliezer had transmitted concerned only *haza'ah* for the purpose of eating *terumah*, not *haza'ah* for the *korban Pesach* — and Rabbi Akiva had misunderstood. On this reading, they were simply talking past each other.

But Rava rejects this reading. He cites the *baraita* explicitly: *hazat tmei meis tochaich, shechal shvi'i shelo lihyot be'Shabbat u've'erev Pesach* — the *haza'ah* for a *tamei meit*, whose seventh day falls on Shabbat which is *erev Pesach* — that is the explicit case in which, despite it being a *mitzvah* and only a *shevut*, it does not override Shabbat. This is unambiguously about the *korban Pesach*, not about *terumah*. So *ella vadai haza'ah d'Pesach agmrei* — clearly the tradition Rabbi Eliezer transmitted to Rabbi Akiva was precisely this case. And yet Rabbi Eliezer was now challenging it.

The *Gemara* concludes: *Rabbi Eliezer gmara itaker leh* — Rabbi Eliezer forgot. He was ill or aged, and the tradition had slipped from him. *V'ata Rabbi Akiva l'azkurei gmareh* — Rabbi Akiva came to remind him of his own teaching. But he could not do so directly, for *lav orach ara* — it is not *derech eretz* to tell one's teacher to his face that he has forgotten. So Rabbi Akiva reminded him indirectly, by making a statement so bizarre in its apparent logic — you can't do *shechita*! — that Rabbi Eliezer would hear from it: this student is treating the non-overriding of *haza'ah* as so axiomatic that he is prepared to make an absurd argument from it. And perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would then recall: yes, I did teach him that.

### Mesorah Irony

There is a striking and beautiful irony here. Rabbi Eliezer is described elsewhere as a *bor sud she'eino me'abeid tipah* — a plastered cistern that loses not a drop. He is the consummate guardian of received tradition; his entire *Torah* persona is organized around the faithful transmission of the *mesorah*. And it is precisely him who forgets — and it is Rabbi Akiva, the master of analysis and intellectual creativity, who restores the lost tradition to its rightful owner. The one who prided himself on never having said a word he did not receive from his teachers needed his student to give him back his own *Torah*.

## Obligation Status

### Tiltul Question

Having established that *haza'ah* does not override Shabbat even for the *korban Pesach*, the *Gemara* asks a more fundamental question: why can't one simply do *haza'ah* on Shabbat in general? After all, *haza'ah* is *tiltul be'alma* — it is merely moving water from one place to another. What makes it a *shevut* of such weight that it cannot be overridden for the *korban Pesach*?

### Carrying Concern

Rava provides his standard answer: *gezeirah shema yitlena v'ya'virena arba amot bi'reshut ha'rabim* — a rabbinic decree lest one pick up the water and carry it four *amot* in the public domain. This is the same reasoning Rava applies to explain why one does not blow *shofar* on Shabbat and why one does not take *lulav* on Shabbat — practices already codified in the *Mishnah* whose original reasoning may not have been this concern about carrying, but which Rava consistently explains through this lens. The fear of incidental carrying thus explains why *haza'ah* was given the status of a significant *shevut*.

But this raises the question for Rabbi Eliezer's position: if carrying would itself be *mutar* to enable the *korban Pesach* according to Rabbi Eliezer — since *machshirei mitzvah dochin et ha-Shabbat* — how could *Rava's* concern about carrying explain why even Rabbi Eliezer would prohibit *haza'ah*? The *Gemara* therefore must look for a different reason why *haza'ah* stands apart even for Rabbi Eliezer.

### Obligation Override

The answer emerges from a conceptual distinction: only those things that place a person in a state of *chiyuv* can override Shabbat. The *mitzvat korban Pesach* overrides Shabbat because one is *obligated* — and everything that is an obstacle to that obligated person performing the *mitzvah* can yield. But in the case of a *tamei meit* waiting for his seventh-day *haza'ah*, the person is not currently obligated to bring the *korban Pesach*. He could, by performing the *haza'ah*, put himself into the category of obligation — but right now, *gufei lo chazi*, he is not fit, and therefore *lo rami chiyuva alei*, the obligation is not upon him. Since his current status places him outside the sphere of *chiyuv*, he cannot invoke the principle that a *mitzvah* obligation overrides preparatory obstacles — because the obstacle is not in the way of his *chiyuv*; the obstacle is what stands between him and *having* a *chiyuv* at all.

### Infant Milah

Rabba draws out the logical implications of this principle with an application to *bris milah*. For a healthy infant on the eighth day, one may heat water to prepare him for the *milah* on Shabbat — *mechaminin lo chamim lehavroso u'lemolo be'Shabbat* — because *de'chazi lei katan*, the child is fit and the obligation is upon him; the hot water is merely an obstacle-removal for someone who is already obligated. For a sick infant, however, one may not heat up water to nurse him to health so that he can then receive the *milah* on Shabbat — *de'ha lo chazi lei*, right now he is not fit, and one cannot perform a *Shabbat* violation to bring someone *into* the category of obligation.

Rabba then challenges this distinction. In the case of the healthy infant, he asks: if he is so healthy, why does he need the hot water prepared for him at all — *lamah lei chamim lehavroso*? Rabba then resolves the difficulty: *akol cholim heim etzel milah* — all infants are considered ill relative to *milah*; even a healthy baby needs some preparation before he is genuinely fit for *bris milah*. But this actually *collapses* the distinction rather than preserving it. If even the healthy baby requires preparation before he is fit, then even the healthy baby is currently not fit — and by our principle, one cannot perform Shabbat violations to bring him into fitness. The conclusion is surprising: *echad katan bari v'echad choleh, ein mechaminin lo chamim lehavroso u'lemolo be'Shabbat* — whether the infant is healthy or sick, one does not heat water on Shabbat to prepare him for *milah*. One must prepare the water beforehand. This is a significant limitation even within Rabbi Eliezer's permissive approach: the permission to override Shabbat applies only when one is already obligated; it does not extend to actions that *generate* the obligation.

---

## Arel vs. Tamei

### Arel Karet

Abaye challenges this framework with a sharp question. Rabbi Eliezer holds that an *arel* — an uncircumcised man who was capable of receiving a *bris milah* — is liable for *karet* if he fails to bring the *korban Pesach* on *erev Pesach*. He could have found a *mohel*, undergone *bris milah* that day, and brought his *korban*. His failure to do so is culpable, and he receives *karet*. But one can only be liable for *karet* if one is *obligated* to bring the *korban Pesach*. If the principle we established says that someone who is currently not fit — like the *tamei meit* before *haza'ah* — is not considered obligated, then the *arel*, who currently cannot eat the *korban* and is therefore not fully fit, should also not be considered obligated. How then can he be liable for *karet*?

### Tumah Hutra

Rava responds: *arlus* and *tumah* are fundamentally different categories, and the difference is rooted in the principle of *tumah hutra b'tzibur*. For *arlus*, there is no such principle. If the entire *tzibur* were *arelim*, we would not say that they may bring the *korban* in a state of *arlus* — we would say *kumu mehul*, go circumcise yourselves. There is no *arlus hutra b'tzibur*. Therefore, our basic approach to *arlus* is: you are obligated, and *arlus* is an obstacle — go remove the obstacle. The obligation is upon you, the *arlus* stands in the way, and you must deal with it. This is why an *arel* who fails to address the obstacle is *chayav karet*.

For *tumah*, however, the principle of *tumah hutra b'tzibur* means that our fundamental approach to the *tzibur* in a state of *tumah* is not to insist they become *tahor* — it is to permit them to bring the *korban* in their current state. This fundamentally reframes the nature of *tumah* relative to the *korban*: it is not simply an obstacle to be removed before one can be obligated; it is a state that may in some circumstances be accommodated.

### Tzibur Principle

The *tzibur* principle reshapes the *yachid* case as well. *V'chol milsa d'leisa b'tzibur leisa b'yachid* — whatever is not demanded of the *tzibur* is not demanded of the *yachid* either. Since we would not insist that the *tzibur* become *tahor* if they are all *tamei* — we would invoke *tumah hutra b'tzibur* and let them bring the *korban* in their state — we similarly do not insist that the *yachid* who is *tamei* become *tahor* before bringing his *korban*. His *tumah* is, in effect, accommodated. He is redirected to *Pesach Sheni*, but he is not considered presently obligated in the same way the *arel* is. And since he is not presently obligated in the same categorical way, there is no basis for overriding Shabbat to enable his *haza'ah*.

---

## Braitah Proof

### Both Liable

A *baraita* is cited to challenge this framework: *yachol lo yei'einosh karet ella she'hayah tahor v'she'lo hayah b'derech rechokah* — one might think that only someone who was *tahor* and not far away is subject to *karet* for failing to bring the *korban Pesach*. *Arel u'tmei sheretz u'she'ar kol ha'tmei'im minayin* — from where do we know that even an *arel*, even someone *tamei* from contact with a *sheretz*, and all other *tmei'im* who could have corrected their status — are liable? The *baraita* derives it from a verse — *ve'ha'ish* — which broadens the category. The implication is clear: both the *arel* and the *tmei sheretz* are considered *obligated*, and their inability to currently bring the *korban* is treated as an obstacle they were required to remove.

### Status Fix

This *baraita* appears to demonstrate that even someone in a state of *tumah* — someone who could have jumped into the *mikvah* that day and become *tahor* — is considered *chayav* and must fix his status. This challenges the claim that *tumah* places a person outside the category of obligation. If the *tmei sheretz* is considered obligated and subject to *karet*, he seems to be in the same position as the *arel* — obligated, with an obstacle, required to address it.

---

## Braitah Proof (II)

### Sheretz Alternative

Rava, however, reinterprets the *baraita*. He argues that the *baraita* actually presupposes that one cannot bring the *korban Pesach* for a *tmei sheretz* in his current state — *ein shochtin v'zorkin al tmei sheretz* — meaning that the *baraita* is treating his situation as parallel to the *arel*: he is obligated, he has an obstacle, and he must remove it. The *baraita* is not describing the generally accepted *halacha* for *tumah*; rather, it is establishing that even in this case where his state prevents the bringing of the *korban*, the obligation still rests upon him.

---

## Final Answer

### Tamei Yotzei

Rava now articulates a complete reorientation. Rabbi Eliezer actually holds *shochtin v'zorkin al tmei sheretz* — one *does* bring the *korban Pesach* on behalf of a person who is *tamei* from *sheretz*, even before he has gone to the *mikvah*. And similarly, *ve'hu ha'din le'tmei meit be'shivii shelo* — the same applies to a *tamei meit* on his seventh day. Even without having done *haza'ah*, the *korban* may be brought for him.

### Eating Optional

This completely inverts the earlier analysis. The earlier framework assumed that *haza'ah* is prohibited because the person is too far outside the category of obligation. Rava now says the opposite: the reason *haza'ah* is not done is that it is *unnecessary*. The person is so thoroughly within the category — the *korban* can be brought for him even in his current state — that the only reason he would ever need *haza'ah* is so that he can *eat* the *korban* that night. And on this point, *achilat pesachim lo me'akva* — eating the *korban Pesach* is not indispensable. One can be *yotzei* the *mitzvah* of *korban Pesach* without eating it, at least in circumstances such as this. Therefore, since *haza'ah* is needed only to enable eating, and eating is not strictly required, there is no basis to permit *haza'ah* on Shabbat even according to Rabbi Eliezer. Not because he is too *outside* the obligation — but because he is already so *inside* it that *haza'ah* adds nothing to the core *mitzvah*.

### Shelo Lochlav

Rav Adda bar Ahava raises an objection to Rava: if a person cannot eat the *korban* — because he has not yet done *haza'ah* and remains *tamei* — then the *shechita* was performed *shelo le-ochlav*, for one who cannot eat it, which is a *psul* that invalidates the *korban*.

### Theoretical Fitness

Rava responds: *shelo le-ochlav* refers to cases like a *choleh* or *zaken* — someone who is truly unable to eat, whose incapacity is fixed or at least not immediately remediable. *Aval hai*, this *tamei* person — *mechazi chazi*, he is theoretically fit. His inability to eat is not an intrinsic incapacity; it is merely the result of his not having taken the available steps to make himself fit — *tikunei hu delo metaken*. Since in principle he is capable of eating, he retains his status as a theoretical *ochal*, and the *shechita* is not *shelo le-ochlav*. The *korban* is valid.

The same reasoning would presumably apply to an *arel* as well — since he could theoretically have had a *bris milah*, he too would be considered a theoretical *ochal* rather than someone who falls into the category of *shelo le-ochlav*. This final resolution thus redraws the entire landscape of who is considered *fit* and who is not: fitness is determined not by one's current state but by one's theoretical capacity. And it is this theoretical fitness — not any achieved status — that keeps a person within the frame of the *mitzvah*.
