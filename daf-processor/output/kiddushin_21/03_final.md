# Kiddushin 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Partial Redemption

### Rav Huna's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: עֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְנׇכְרִי, נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין, אוֹ אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין? ״גְּאֻלָּתוֹ״ ״גְּאֻלָּתוֹ״ מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה גָּמַר: מָה שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין – אַף הַאי נָמֵי אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְקוּלָּא אָמְרִינַן, לְחוּמְרָא לָא אָמְרִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: With regard to **a Hebrew slave who is sold to a gentile,** can he **be partially redeemed, or can he not be partially redeemed?** Does this slave have the option of paying part of his value and thereby reducing his remaining period of service? With regard to a Hebrew slave sold to a gentile, the verse states: "According to his years he shall give back the price of **his redemption [*ge'ullato*]"** (Leviticus 25:52), and the meaning of the term "*ge'ullato*" can be **derived from** the same term stated with regard to the redemption of an ancestral field: "And he becomes rich and finds sufficient means to **redeem it [*ge'ullato*]"** (Leviticus 25:26). Accordingly, **just as an ancestral field cannot be partially redeemed,** but is either fully redeemed or not at all, **so too, this** slave **cannot be partially redeemed. Or perhaps we say** that he cannot be partially redeemed only if that leads **to a leniency,** but **we do not say** that he cannot be redeemed if it leads **to a stringency.**

The daf opens on 21b, picking up mid-discussion of the *eved Ivri*. The most recent topic had established that when redeeming a Hebrew slave sold to a non-Jew, the redemption amount is calculated based on years of service remaining, priced at the lesser of the original purchase price or the current market value. The Gemara had also articulated, in terms that are partly *aggadata* and partly *halacha*, that one who purchases an *eved Ivri* must treat him at least as well as himself — a pointed disincentive. The *parsha* of Behar makes clear that selling oneself into slavery is a consequence of the most desperate circumstances, and yet even when a person has fallen so low as to sell himself to *ovdei avodah zarah* — to a household of idolatry — the obligation to redeem him remains.

It is worth pausing here to note the precise scope of the Torah's explicit provisions. The Torah specifies that relatives bear an obligation to redeem in only two cases: where a person sells a *sdeh achuza* (an ancestral field), and where a person sells himself into slavery to a non-Jew. The cases of a house sold in a walled city and of a Hebrew slave sold to a fellow Jew are not addressed explicitly. These gaps will drive much of the Gemara's subsequent inquiry.

The daf begins with a question posed by Rav Huna bar Hinena to Rav Sheshet: *eved Ivri hanimkar le-ovdei kochavim* — a Hebrew slave sold to a non-Jew — can he be redeemed *lachatza'in*, partially, or not? The question is grounded in a potential *gezerah shavah*: the word *ge'ulato* appears both in the context of the *sdeh achuza* and in the context of the *eved Ivri* sold to a non-Jew. By the *sdeh achuza*, the verse says *u-matza k'dei ge'ulato* — he must find the sufficient funds for full redemption — implying that only the complete amount suffices, and partial redemption is ineffective. Should we apply this rule via *gezerah shavah* to the *eved Ivri* as well, or does the *gezerah shavah* function only to import leniencies and not stringencies?

### Rashi's View

Before the Gemara develops its own scenario, it is worth noting that Rashi understands *nigal lachatza'in* in a rather striking way. Rashi explains that the question is whether, if a slave has managed to accumulate funds sufficient to buy back only half his remaining value, he can pay that amount and thereby reduce his remaining term of service by half. In other words, if four years remain and he can pay the equivalent of two years, can he simply buy back two years and go free after two years rather than four? That is Rashi's reading — and it is quite radical. As we will see, the Gemara's actual scenario operates differently.

### Price Fluctuation Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לְקוּלָּא וּלְחוּמְרָא. לְקוּלָּא: זַבְנֵיהּ בִּמְאָה, יְהַב לֵיהּ חַמְשִׁין, פַּלְגָא דְּדָמֵי, וְאַשְׁבַּח וְקָם עַל מָאתַן, אִי אָמְרַתְּ נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין – יָהֵיב לֵיהּ מְאָה וְנָפֵיק, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵין נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין – יָהֵיב לֵיהּ מְאָה וְחַמְשִׁין וְנָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: If you say** that **he is partially redeemed, you find** elements **of leniency and stringency.** This ruling can lead **to a leniency** in the following case: A master **purchased** a Hebrew slave **for one hundred** and the redeemer **gives him fifty,** which is **half of his value, and** afterward the slave **increased** in value **and stood at two hundred. If you say** that **he can be partially redeemed** he is already half-redeemed. Consequently, the redeemer **gives him one hundred,** half of his current value, **and** the slave **goes out,** i.e., is redeemed. **And if you say** that **he cannot be partially redeemed,** the redeemer **gives** the owner an additional **one hundred and fifty and** the slave **goes out,** as the initial fifty serves merely as the first payment, and he is now worth two hundred.

The scenario the Gemara itself develops is as follows. Suppose a slave has four years of service remaining, currently valued at 100. He pays 50 — half the current value. Then the market rises and he is now worth 200. How much must he pay to complete his redemption? If the initial payment of 50 constituted a genuine partial buyback — if *nigal lachatza'in* applies — then he has already purchased back half of himself. The remaining half is now worth 100 (half of 200), and that is all he must pay. His total outlay is 150. If, however, partial redemption is invalid and that initial payment of 50 had no legal effect, he must now pay the full 200 to redeem himself — less the 50 already advanced, meaning an additional 150, for a total of 200.

### Abaye's Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרַתְּ נִתְרַבָּה כַּסְפּוֹ – ״מִכֶּסֶף מִקְנָתוֹ״! כְּגוֹן דְּאוֹקִיר וְזָל וְאוֹקִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But you said** that if the **money** of **his** value **increased,** one always pays **"out of the money that he was bought for"** (Leviticus 25:51), i.e., one calculates the value of the slave in accordance with his value at the time when he was acquired, even if that is less than his present value. Why, then, should one have to pay the full two hundred? The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case **where** he initially, before being sold, **increased in value,** was then sold for two hundred, **and** afterward **decreased** in value to one hundred, **and** subsequently he again **increased in value** so that he was worth the same amount as he was originally. In this case, his value of two hundred remains determinative.

Abaye then develops both the lenient and stringent dimensions of *nigal lachatza'in*. Consider the lenient case: the slave was purchased for 100. He pays 50 (half the value), and then the market rises so that he is now worth 200. If the initial payment counted as a partial buyback, he only owes an additional 100 to complete his redemption. If it did not, he must pay an additional 150.

The Gemara, however, immediately raises a difficulty with this scenario: we have already established that one always calculates the redemption price at the *lesser* of the original purchase price and the current market value. So even without the partial buyback concept, if the market has risen to 200 but the purchase price was only 100, you go by the purchase price of 100 — the partial payment question would be irrelevant.

### Abaye's Analysis (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לְחוּמְרָא: זַבְנֵיהּ בְּמָאתַן, יְהַיב מְאָה, פַּלְגֵי דִּדְמֵי, וְאִיכְּסִף וְקָם עַל מְאָה, אִי אָמְרַתְּ נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין – יָהֵיב לֵיהּ חַמְשִׁין וְנָפֵיק, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵין נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין – הָנָךְ מְאָה פִּקָּדוֹן נִינְהוּ גַּבֵּיהּ, יָהֵיב לְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ וְנָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye proceeds to describe how **you find** a case that leads **to a stringency.** A master **purchased** a Hebrew slave **for two hundred** and the redeemer **gave one hundred,** which is **half of his value, and** the slave **decreased** in value **and stood at** a value of **one hundred. If you say** that **he can be partially redeemed,** the redeemer **gives fifty and** the slave **goes out,** i.e., is redeemed. **And if you say** that **he cannot be partially redeemed, these one hundred are a deposit with him.** Therefore, the redeemer **gives it to him** at the time of the redemption **and** the slave **goes out.** There is no reason to pay him more money, as he is now worth one hundred.

The Gemara therefore refines the scenario to make the question meaningful: the slave was originally purchased at 200 (the market was high), then the market dropped and he paid 50 when his value had fallen to 100, and now the market has recovered to 200 again. In this situation, both the original purchase price and the current market value are 200 — so the lesser-of-two-values rule provides no relief. Now the question of partial redemption becomes decisive. If his earlier payment of 50 constituted a valid partial buyback, he has already paid for a portion of himself at the favorable lower price, and the remaining amount owed is correspondingly reduced. If that payment had no effect, he faces the full 200 (less what he deposited). This is the scenario — analogized to a mortgage — where the partial redemption question carries real halachic weight.

### Lesser Price Rule

This explains the Gemara's point that the lesser-of-two-values rule, while important, does not resolve the *nigal lachatza'in* question in every case. Where the market has fluctuated and returned to the original sale price, both benchmarks converge and the slave must look to whether his installment payment constituted a meaningful partial buyback.

### Complex Scenario

The structure also explains what the Gemara means by the *stringent* side of *nigal lachatza'in*. Suppose the slave was purchased for 200 and pays 100 immediately (half his value at the time). Then the market falls and he is worth only 100. If the initial payment of 100 counted as a partial buyback, he has bought back exactly half of himself — but at the then-current price. The remaining half is now worth only 50, so he pays another 50 and goes free, for a total outlay of 150. If, however, the partial payment had no legal effect, the 100 he gave was simply held in escrow, and since his full value has now dropped to 100, that 100 is sufficient to redeem him entirely. He ends up paying exactly what he gave — 100 total — which is *less* than the 150 he would pay under the partial buyback model. So here, *nigal lachatza'in* would actually work to his detriment.

### Market Timing

The Gemara's point is that if partial redemption is valid, a slave can engage in strategic market timing: accumulate funds, monitor his own market value, and purchase back portions of himself when prices are favorable. The Gemara does not, however, resolve the underlying question — whether *nigal lachatza'in* is valid for an *eved Ivri* sold to a non-Jew. What it establishes is that the question is real and consequential, and that Rashi's understanding (that partial payment reduces one's remaining years of service) differs from the Gemara's own scenario (which frames the question as one of financial accounting for the redemption sum).

## Walled Cities Halakha

### Extending Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַמּוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין, אוֹ אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין? ״גְּאֻלָּתוֹ״ ״גְּאֻלָּתוֹ״ מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה גָּמַר: מָה שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת לַחֲצָאִין – אַף הַאי נָמֵי אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet** with regard to a similar topic: In the case of **one who sells a house from among the houses of walled cities,** which can be redeemed only during the first year after the sale, can it **be partially redeemed, or can it not be partially redeemed?** The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: Does he **derive** the verbal analogy of **"*ge'ullato*"** (Leviticus 25:29) and **"*ge'ullato*"** (Leviticus 25:26) **from** the case of **an ancestral field** and say that **just as ancestral land cannot be partially redeemed, so too, this** house **cannot be partially redeemed?**

The same question — *nigal lachatza'in* or not — is then posed with respect to a house sold in a walled city: *ha-mocher bayit be-vatei arei chomah*, can it be redeemed partially? Again the *gezerah shavah* of *ge'ulato ge'ulato* from the *sdeh achuza* is invoked, arguing that just as the ancestral field cannot be redeemed halfway, so too the walled-city house cannot be. Or perhaps the *gezerah shavah* imports the rule only in one direction?

### Rabbi Shimon Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִמִּדְרָשׁוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נִשְׁמַע, שֶׁלֹּוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין, דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִם גָּאֹל יִגְאַל״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to him: From Rabbi Shimon's interpretation one learns that** with regard to houses in walled cities, **one can borrow** money **and redeem** them, **and** similarly that one **can partially redeem** them. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a verse that describes one who consecrates his field: **"And if he will redeem [*ga'ol yigal*]** the field" (Leviticus 27:19). The repetition of the verb **teaches that one can borrow** money **and redeem** houses in walled cities **and** that **one can** also **partially redeem** them.

Rav Sheshet answers that the matter can be resolved from the *drasha* of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that one may borrow money to redeem (*loveh ve-goel*) and that one may redeem halfway (*goel lachatza'in*) — two distinct rules — in the case of *makdish sadeh*, a field one has consecrated to the Temple treasury.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם, לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ בְּמוֹכֵר שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאִם הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה – חוֹזֶרֶת לִבְעָלֶיהָ בַּיּוֹבֵל, הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ – שֶׁאֵינוֹ לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** for this *halakha*? This is **because we find with regard to one who sells his ancestral field that his power is enhanced, as, if the Jubilee** Year **arrives and it is not redeemed,** the ancestral field **returns to its owners in the Jubilee** Year without them having to pay for it. Therefore, **the power** of the seller **is diminished in** that **he cannot borrow** money **and redeem** the field but must have the money to do so on his own, **and** he cannot **partially redeem** it.

The Gemara cites the *baraita*: from the verse *im gaol yigal ish* (Vayikra 27) — the doubled language — we learn *melamed she-loveh ve-goel ve-goel lachatza'in*: one may borrow money and redeem, and one may redeem in installments. This stands in contrast to the rule for *sdeh achuza*, where *u-matza k'dei ge'ulato* teaches that you may not borrow (the money must be one's own, newly acquired) and you may not redeem partially.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַקְדִּישׁ, שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאִם הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה – יוֹצְאָה לְכֹהֲנִים בַּיּוֹבֵל, יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ – שֶׁלֹּוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if **one consecrates** his field **his power is diminished, as, if the Jubilee** Year **arrives and he did not redeem it** in the meantime, and the Temple treasurer sold it to someone else, the ancestral field **goes out** and becomes the property **of the priests in the Jubilee** Year and does not return to its original owners. Consequently, in order to offset this stringency **his power is enhanced** in **that he may borrow** money **and redeem** the field, **and partially redeem** it.

Rabbi Shimon then provides the underlying rationale. By the *sdeh achuza*, the seller is in a position of strength: *im higia yovel velo nig'ala chozeret le-ba'alah ba-yovel* — if the field is not redeemed by the *Yovel*, it reverts to the owner anyway. Because the seller will recover the property regardless, the Torah does not need to be lenient with him beforehand — it can afford to make redemption harder (only full amount, only own money). By contrast, by *makdish sadeh*, if the field is not redeemed before *Yovel*, it goes to the *Kohanim* permanently. The seller's position is weak. And it is precisely because his position is weak — *hura kocho* — that the Torah compensates by making redemption easier: *she-loveh ve-goel ve-goel lachatza'in*.

### Strength Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה נָמֵי, הוֹאִיל וְהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאִם מָלְאָה לוֹ שָׁנָה תְּמִימָה וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה נֶחְלָט – יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁלֹּוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** According to this explanation, with regard to **this one who sells a house from among the houses of walled cities, since his power is also diminished, as, if a full year passes and** the house **has not been redeemed** it remains the **permanent** property of the buyer, in order to offset this stringency, **his power** should be **enhanced in** that **he may borrow** money **and redeem** the house, **and partially redeem** it.

The Gemara then applies this logic to *batei arei chomah*: a house in a walled city that is not redeemed within the first year is permanently forfeited — *nechlat* — to the purchaser. The seller's position is extremely weak. Therefore, following Rabbi Shimon's logic of counterbalancing: since his position is so weak, the Torah should grant him the rights of partial redemption and borrowing. This is the power of *darshinan ta'ama de-kra* — reasoning from the underlying principle of a verse to apply it to cases not explicitly mentioned.

The comparison across categories is illuminating. By *sdeh achuza*, the buyback window runs from year 3 until *Yovel* (the first two years being closed to buyback). By *batei arei chomah*, the buyback window is only year 1. By *makdish sadeh*, the buyback window runs from the moment of consecration all the way through *Yovel* — the largest possible window. Rabbi Shimon's logic runs: a larger buyback window means a stronger position, hence more stringent conditions; a smaller window means a weaker position, hence more generous conditions.

### Baraita's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״אִם גָּאֹל יִגְאַל״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין. שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה מוֹכֵר שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאִם הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה חוֹזֶרֶת לִבְעָלֶיהָ בַּיּוֹבֵל – הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאֵין לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין, מַקְדִּישׁ, שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאִם הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה יוֹצְאָה לְכֹהֲנִים בַּיּוֹבֵל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ שֶׁאֵין לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין?
>
> **Translation:** Rava bar Ḥinnana **raised an objection to** this opinion from a *baraita*. The verse states with regard to one who consecrates his field: **"And if he will redeem [*ga'ol yigal*]** the field" (Leviticus 27:19). This **teaches that he may borrow** money **and redeem** it, **and partially redeem** it. The *baraita* continues: **As** one **might** have thought: Could this **not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference: Just as** with regard to **one who sells an ancestral field that his power is enhanced, as if the Jubilee** Year **arrives and it is not redeemed, it returns to its owners in the Jubilee** Year, and yet **his power is diminished** in **that he cannot borrow** money **and redeem** the field **and** he cannot **partially redeem** it; with regard to **one who consecrates** a field, where **his power is diminished, for if the Jubilee** Year **arrives and it was not redeemed it goes out to the priests in the Jubilee** Year, **is it not logical that his power is diminished** in **that he cannot borrow** money **and redeem** the field **and** he cannot **partially redeem** it?

A *baraita*, however, presents the opposite logic. By *sdeh achuza*, the seller has strength — the field returns at *Yovel* — and yet the halacha is stringent (full amount, own money). By *makdish sadeh*, the seller's position is weak — he loses it at *Yovel* — so one might have argued by *kal va-chomer* that the rules should be even stricter. But the doubled language *gaol yigal* teaches that in fact the rules are more lenient. This *baraita*'s logic is the mirror image of Rabbi Shimon's: it reasons that where the category is already strong, the accompanying rules are strict; where it is weak, one might expect still stricter rules — but the verse intervenes to grant leniency.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְמוֹכֵר שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, שֶׁכֵּן הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל מִיָּד, תֹּאמַר בְּמַקְדִּישׁ שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל מִיָּד. מוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל מִיָּד וְאֵין לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין! לָא קַשְׁיָא:
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* raises a difficulty against this *a fortiori* inference: **What** is unique **about** one who **sells his ancestral field** is that **his power is diminished with regard to redeeming** it **immediately,** as he cannot redeem his field right away but must wait at least two years. Shall **you say** that the same *halakhot* apply **with regard to one who consecrates** his field, **as his power is enhanced with regard to redeeming** it **immediately?** One who has consecrated his field can redeem it as soon as he has the money to do so. If so, the *a fortiori* inference is not valid. The case of one who **sells a house from among** the **houses of walled cities can prove** otherwise, **as his power is enhanced with regard to redeeming** it **immediately,** and yet **he cannot borrow** money **and redeem** the house **and** he cannot **partially redeem** it. In any case, with regard to the issue at hand, the *baraita* indicates the opposite of the previous conclusion: One who sells a house in a walled city cannot partially redeem it. Rav Sheshet answers: This is **not difficult.**

The *baraita* also invokes *batei arei chomah* as part of its argument structure: even though one can redeem a house in a walled city within the first year (which sounds like strength), nevertheless the halacha is stringent there (no borrowing, no partial redemption). This shows that the ability to buy back does not automatically generate the favorable rules — and therefore even the stronger case of *makdish sadeh* would not logically derive those rules without a verse.

### Rabbanan vs. Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא רַבָּנַן, הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **This** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and **that** *baraita*, which teaches that one can borrow money and redeem the house and partially redeem it, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction simply: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. One *baraita* represents the position of the Rabbis, the other reflects Rabbi Shimon. The Rabbis hold that strength goes with strength and weakness goes with weakness — a category that is already weak in one respect will also be weak in terms of redemption rights. Rabbi Shimon holds a counterbalancing logic: a category that is weak in one respect deserves to be given *more* redemption rights, to offset the disadvantage. Accordingly, under Rabbi Shimon, *batei arei chomah* — whose one-year window is a severe limitation — is precisely the category where borrowing and partial redemption are permitted. Under the Rabbis, that same weakness means those rights are withheld. Two *beraitot*, two opinions.

## Four Categories Analysis

### Rav Acha Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְמוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה – שֶׁכֵּן הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל לְעוֹלָם, תֹּאמַר בְּמַקְדִּישׁ – שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל לְעוֹלָם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** The comparison detailed earlier (20b) between one who sells a house and one who consecrates a field, **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is unique about **one who sells a house among the houses of walled cities** is that **his power is diminished,** in that he is not able **to redeem** it **forever,** since he cannot redeem it after a year has passed (see Leviticus 25:30). Shall **you say** the same **with regard to one who consecrates** his field, **as his power is enhanced** in that he is able **to redeem forever?**

Rav Acha son of Rava challenges the *baraita*'s argument to Rav Ashi. The *baraita* had used *batei arei chomah* as a proof-case showing that even a category with buyback rights (the one-year window) lacks the special privileges of borrowing and partial redemption. Rav Acha's challenge: *mah le-mocher bayit be-vatei arei chomah she-ken hura kocho le-goel le-olam* — the walled-city house is categorically weak because after year 1, the owner can *never* buy it back. That is a uniquely severe weakness that does not characterize *makdish sadeh* at all. By *makdish sadeh*, the owner can redeem at any point up to *Yovel* — *yafeh kocho le-goel le-olam* — so its redemption window is the strongest of all three categories. One should not need a verse to tell me that the strongest category has the most generous rules; it should follow logically.

The implication: the *baraita*'s use of *batei arei chomah* as a baseline for weakness is flawed, because *batei arei chomah* is uniquely weak in a way that *makdish sadeh* is not. And *sdeh achuza* has its own distinct weakness — it cannot be redeemed in the first two years. Neither weakness of the other two applies to *makdish sadeh*, which is strong across all dimensions.

### Weakness Elimination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר נֶיהְדַּר דִּינָא, תֵּיתֵי בְּמָה הַצַּד: מוֹכֵר שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל לְעוֹלָם וְאֵין לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין. מָה לְמוֹכֵר שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, שֶׁכֵּן הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל מִיָּד. מוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה יוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa Sava said to Rav Ashi:** The comparison is valid, **because it can be said** that **the argument returns,** as one can learn in a different manner. **Let** this matter **be derived by** an analogy derived from **the common factor** of two sources: The *halakha* of **one who sells an ancestral field can prove it, as his power is enhanced** in that he is able **to redeem** the field **forever, and he cannot borrow** money **and redeem** it, **and** he cannot **partially redeem** it. And if you say: **What** is unique about **one who sells an ancestral field is that his power is diminished** in that he is not able **to redeem** it **immediately,** the case of **one who sells a house from among houses of walled cities can prove** otherwise. Although one can redeem the house immediately, he cannot borrow money and redeem it, nor partially redeem it.

Rav Acha the Elder responds to Rav Ashi: *nihadar dina ve-teiti mi-ma'atzad* — cycle through all the cases. *Sdeh achuza* proves that even where the category is strong (returns at *Yovel*), the rules are strict. Ah, but perhaps the *sdeh achuza*'s rule derives from its own weakness — that it cannot be redeemed immediately. And *batei arei chomah* proves that even when there is an immediate buyback right, the strict rule applies. But perhaps that case's strictness derives from the weakness of permanent forfeiture after year 1. Each case has its own distinct weakness, and neither weakness applies to the other case. *Lo re'i zeh ke-re'i zeh* — the unique characteristics of each case differ from the other.

### Generic Halakha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁנִּגְאָלִין, וְאֵין לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין, אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא מַקְדִּישׁ – שֶׁנִּגְאָל, וְאֵין לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And the derivation has reverted** to its starting point, as one could alternate the explanations for each side with their respective refutations. Ultimately, the **aspect of this** case, an ancestral field, **is not like the aspect of that** case, a house from among houses of walled cities. **Their common denominator is that they can be redeemed, and one cannot borrow** money **and redeem** them, **and** likewise, one cannot **partially redeem** them. **I will also bring** the case of **one who consecrates** his field and rule **that it may be redeemed, but one cannot borrow** money **and redeem** it, **and** one cannot **partially redeem** it.

The Gemara therefore proposes a *tzad ha-shaveh*: the common denominator between *sdeh achuza* and *batei arei chomah* is that both appear in the Torah's *parsha* of forced redemptions (*she-nig'alin*), and both require the full amount from one's own funds. Perhaps this is simply a generic rule — whenever something is subject to a forced redemption framework, it cannot be done with borrowed money and cannot be done partially. From this common denominator, one might have derived the same rule for *makdish sadeh* without a verse. The verse *im gaol yigal* is therefore needed to override this inference and teach that *makdish sadeh* is the exception.

### Year Two Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁכֵּן הוֹרַע כֹּחָם לִיגָּאֵל בְּשָׁנָה שְׁנִיָּה, תֹּאמַר בְּמַקְדִּישׁ שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל בְּשָׁנָה שְׁנִיָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina:** This derivation, based on the common denominator, **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is unique **to their common denominator is that their power is diminished** in that they are unable **to redeem** the respective properties **during the second year,** as in neither case can they be redeemed in the second year. One who sells a house in a walled city cannot redeem it beyond the first year, while one who sells an ancestral field cannot redeem it during the first two years. Shall **you say** the same **with regard to one who consecrated** his field, **as his power is enhanced** in that he is able **to redeem** the field **in the second year?**

Mar Zutra son of Rav Mari presses further against the *tzad ha-shaveh*: *mah le-hatzad ha-shaveh she-bahen she-ken ein kocho le-goel be-shana shniya* — what is common to both of those cases is that neither can be redeemed in the second year. The *sdeh achuza* cannot be redeemed in years 1 and 2; *batei arei chomah* can only be redeemed in year 1, so year 2 is also closed. *Makdish sadeh*, however, can be redeemed in year 2 — in fact, in every year. That is its strength. And it is that strength — the openness of year 2 — that is the common weakness of the other two, and which would logically entitle *makdish sadeh* to the lenient rules. So once again, one should not need a verse.

### Ravina's Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: עֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְנׇכְרִי יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ לִיגָּאֵל בְּשָׁנָה שְׁנִיָּה וְאֵין לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to him** that the derivation is valid **because it can be said** that the case of **a Hebrew slave sold to a gentile can prove** otherwise, **as his power enhanced** in that he is able **to redeem** himself **in the second year, and** nevertheless **he cannot borrow** money **and redeem** himself, **and** he cannot **partially redeem** himself.

Ravina responds: *Eved Ivri yokhiach* — the Hebrew slave sold to a non-Jew is the counterexample that collapses this argument. An *eved Ivri* sold to a non-Jew can be redeemed throughout the full term of his service, including in year 2 — and yet, the halacha (as established earlier in the Gemara via *gezerah shavah* or via the language of *v'nigal kulo*) is that he cannot be redeemed partially and cannot be redeemed with borrowed money. So here is a fourth category — one that *can* be redeemed in year 2, whose window is as broad as *makdish sadeh*'s — and yet it carries the stringent rule. The proposed common denominator (inability to redeem in year 2) therefore cannot be the rationale for the strict rule, because this category lacks that characteristic and yet is equally strict. The strict rule is thus a general feature of all forced redemption frameworks, *makdish sadeh* is the lone exception, and the verse *im gaol yigal* is genuinely necessary to establish that exception.

## Relatives' Redemptio (II)

### Relatives Redeem Houses?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַמּוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, נִגְאָל לִקְרוֹבִים אוֹ אֵין נִגְאָל לִקְרוֹבִים? ״גְּאֻלָּתוֹ״ ״גְּאֻלָּתוֹ״ מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה גָּמַר: מָה שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת לַחֲצָאִין וְנִגְאֶלֶת לִקְרוֹבִים – אַף הַאי נָמֵי אֵין נִגְאָל לַחֲצָאִין וְנִגְאָל לִקְרוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a similar matter. **Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet:** In the case of **one who sells a house from among the houses of walled cities, can** it **be redeemed by relatives or can** it **not be redeemed by relatives?** Is the ability to redeem a house limited to the seller himself? The Gemara presents the two sides of the dilemma: Does one **derive** the verbal analogy of **"*ge'ullato*"** (Leviticus 25:29) and **"*ge'ullato*"** (Leviticus 25:26) **from an ancestral field** in this manner: **Just as an ancestral field cannot be partially redeemed and** yet it **can be redeemed by relatives, so too, this** house **also cannot be partially redeemed and** yet **it can be redeemed by relatives?**

Rav Huna bar Hinena poses yet another question to Rav Sheshet: *ha-mocher bayit be-vatei arei chomah* — a house in a walled city — *nigal le-krovim o ein nigal le-krovim*? Can it be redeemed by relatives, or not?

To appreciate the question, one must recall what *ge'ulas krovim* means in practice. The Torah's provision that relatives may redeem is not merely about the family's right to offer money; it is about the legal compulsion placed on the purchaser to accept that money and release the property. A stranger's offer can be declined, but a relative's redemption is binding. The question is: does this power of *ge'ulas krovim* extend beyond the two cases where the Torah mentions it explicitly — *sdeh achuza* and *eved Ivri* sold to a non-Jew — to include *batei arei chomah*?

Again, the *gezerah shavah* of *ge'ulato ge'ulato* from *sdeh achuza* is invoked: just as an ancestral field is not redeemed halfway but *is* redeemed by relatives, so too might we say that the walled-city house is not redeemed halfway and *is* redeemed by relatives. Alternatively, perhaps the *gezerah shavah* was only meant to carry over the *lachatza'in* rule, not the *krovim* rule.

### Gezerah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא: כִּי כְּתִיבָא ״גְּאוּלָּה״ – בַּחֲצָאִין הוּא דִּכְתִיבָ[א], בִּקְרוֹבִים לָא כְּתִיבָ[א]? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps, when** the word ***ge'ula* is written** in the case of an ancestral field, **it is written with regard to partial** redemption; but the word redemption **is not written with regard to relatives.** If so, the verbal analogy does not apply to the *halakha* of redemption by relatives. Rav Sheshet **said to him:** The house **cannot be redeemed** by relatives.

Rav Sheshet answers: the house in a walled city is *not* redeemed by relatives. Without an explicit verse establishing *ge'ulas krovim* for this category, it does not apply.

### Inclusive Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״בְּכֹל גְּאֻלָּה תִּתְּנוּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת בָּתִּים וְעֶבֶד עִבְרִי. מַאי לָאו בָּתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה? לָא, בָּתֵּי חֲצֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana **raised an objection to** the ruling of Rav Sheshet from a *baraita* that deals with the verse: "And **in all** of your ancestral land **you shall grant redemption** for the land" (Leviticus 25:24). This verse serves **to include houses and a Hebrew slave. What, is it not** correct to say that the term houses is referring to **houses of walled cities,** indicating that they can also be redeemed by relatives? The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** this is referring to **houses in open areas** without walls.

The Gemara immediately challenges this ruling from the verse *u-vekhol ge'ulah titnu la-aretz* — "give redemption in everything." What new law does this teach? The proposal: *le-rabot battim ve-eved Ivri* — to include houses and the *eved Ivri* (sold to a Jew) within the framework of *ge'ulas krovim*.

### Scope vs. Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתֵּי חֲצֵרִים בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״עַל שְׂדֵה הָאָרֶץ יֵחָשֵׁב״! הָהוּא לְקוֹבְעוֹ חוֹבָה, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **houses in open areas** without walls, **it is explicitly written: "Shall be reckoned with the field of the country"** (Leviticus 25:31), meaning that they are like ancestral fields. If so, there is no need for another verse to teach this *halakha*. The Gemara answers: **That** verse: "And in all of your ancestral land you shall grant redemption for the land" (Leviticus 25:24), serves **to establish** that redemption by relatives is **an obligation, and** this is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer.**

The Gemara deflects: *lo* — the verse is not extending the scope to new categories. *Batei chatzer* — houses in unwalled cities — are already explicitly comparable to fields by the verse *al sdei ha-aretz yeichashev*, so they do not need a new derivation. And the verse *u-vekhol ge'ulah titnu* is not extending *ge'ulas krovim* to *batei arei chomah* at all; rather, it is deepening the *obligation*. It establishes not merely that relatives *may* redeem (a *reshut*), but that they *must* redeem (a *chovah*). The scope remains limited to *sdeh achuza* and *eved Ivri* sold to a non-Jew; the verse adds obligatory force within those categories.

## Obligation & Hebrew (II)

### Right vs. Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְגָאַל אֵת מִמְכַּר אָחִיו״ – רְשׁוּת. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר רְשׁוּת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא חוֹבָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִישׁ כִּי לֹא יִהְיֶה לּוֹ גֹּאֵל״ – וְכִי יֵשׁ אָדָם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ גּוֹאֲלִים? אֶלָּא זֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ וְאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה לִיקַּח, שֶׁהָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to a verse that deals with one who sells ancestral land: **"And he shall redeem that which his brother has sold"** (Leviticus 25:25), this redemption is **optional,** i.e., if he wishes to do so he may redeem the land. Do **you say** that it is **optional or is it nothing other** than **an obligation? The verse states** concerning an ancestral field that was sold: **"And if a man has no one to redeem it"** (Leviticus 25:26). This verse is puzzling: **But is there a man in Israel who does not have redeemers?** Every Jew has a relative of some sort, even if he must trace his family tree back all the way to Jacob. **Rather,** this is referring to **one who has** a relative **who does not want to purchase,** i.e., redeem, the land, **as he has permission** to refrain from doing so, since this redemption is optional. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua.**

A *baraita* directly addresses whether relatives' redemption is a *reshut* (a right, discretionary) or a *chovah* (an obligation). The verse *ve-ga'al et mimchar achiv* — "and he shall redeem his brother's sale" — might indicate an obligation. Or perhaps it confers only a right?

### Rabbi Yehoshua

Rabbi Yehoshua derives from the verse *ve-ish ki lo yihiyeh lo go'el* — "a man who has no redeemer" — that relatives may choose not to redeem. This verse contemplates a scenario where a man has relatives but none of them step forward. Since the Torah acknowledges this possibility and addresses what happens in that case (the slave redeems himself when he accumulates sufficient funds), it implicitly confirms that relatives are not obligated — they have a *reshut*, a right to redeem but not an obligation. *She-hareshut be-yado* — the choice is theirs.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״וְגָאַל אֵת מִמְכַּר אָחִיו״ – חוֹבָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹבָה אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא רְשׁוּת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּכֹל... גְּאֻלָּה תִּתְּנוּ״ – הַכָּתוּב קְבָעוֹ חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says** that when the verse states: **"And he shall redeem that which his brother has sold,"** this redemption is **an obligation.** Do **you say** that it is **an obligation, or is it only optional? The verse states:** "And **in all** of your ancestral land **you shall grant redemption** for the land" (Leviticus 25:24). Here **the verse established it as obligatory.** This is the interpretation of the verses according to Rabbi Eliezer.

Rabbi Eliezer reads *ve-ga'al et mimchar achiv* as establishing a *chovah* — an actual obligation. Then what does *ve-ish ki lo yihiyeh lo go'el* mean? The Gemara resolves: it does not mean he literally has no relatives, since virtually everyone has relatives (*ve-chi yesh adam be-Yisrael she-ein lo go'alim?*). Rather, it describes a situation where no relative is willing to step up — *zeh she-yesh lo ve-eino rotzeh likach* — i.e., one whose relatives are shirking their obligation. And the verse then tells us that even in such a case, the slave himself can eventually redeem himself. Rabbi Eliezer uses *u-vekhol ge'ulah titnu* not to extend scope but to establish that the obligation of redemption is universal across the enumerated cases: *ha-katuv kva'o chovah*.

## Relatives' Redemptio (II)

### Yigalenu Repetitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יִגְאָלֶנּוּ״ ״יִגְאָלֶנּוּ״ ״יִגְאָלֶנּוּ״ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים? לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל הַגְּאוּלּוֹת שֶׁנִּגְאָלוֹת כַּסֵּדֶר הַזֶּה. מַאי לָאו בָּתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, וְעֶבֶד עִבְרִי? לָא, בָּתֵּי חֲצֵרִים וּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "May redeem him," "may redeem him,"** and **"may redeem him"** (Leviticus 25:48–49), **three times?** This serves **to include all redemptions,** and **that they are redeemed in this order. What, is it not** correct to say that this inclusion is referring both to **houses of walled cities and a Hebrew slave sold to a Jew,** that they can be redeemed by relatives? The Gemara rejects this interpretation: **No,** it includes **houses in open areas and an ancestral field.** The Gemara asks: **Concerning houses in open areas and an ancestral field, it is written explicitly: "Shall be reckoned with the field of the country"** (Leviticus 25:31). **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This teaches that **the closer** of a relative one is to the seller, the **earlier** one is in the order of redeeming the house or the field. This is the context of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak's statement.

The Gemara turns to another potential source for extending the scope of *ge'ulas krovim*. In the *parsha* dealing with a Jew who sells himself to a non-Jew, the word *yigalenu* appears three times: *o dodo o ven dodo yigalenu*, *o mi-she'ar besaro yigalenu*, *o hisiga yado ve-nigal*. Why three repetitions? The *baraita* proposes: *le-rabot kol ha-ge'ulot she-nig'alu ke-seder ha-zeh* — to include all redemptions that follow this order, i.e., to extend the framework of *ge'ulas krovim* to other categories as well.

The Gemara asks: *mai lav* — doesn't this include *batei arei chomah* and *eved Ivri* sold to a Jew? The Gemara deflects: no, the repetitions are not extending the scope. *Batei chatzerim* and *sdeh achuza* are already explicitly covered. Then why the three repetitions of *yigalenu*? As Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains: *karov karov kodem* — to establish the order of priority among relatives. The Torah is teaching that closer relatives take precedence in the obligation to redeem. The verse *ha-karov elav yigalenu* in the *sdeh achuza* section already indicates that a closer relative is preferred, but it does not specify the detailed order. The threefold repetition of *yigalenu* by the case of the *eved Ivri* sold to a non-Jew — *o dodo o ven dodo yigalenu, o mi-she'ar besaro yigalenu* — establishes the specific hierarchical order of which relatives redeem first.

So both potential scope-extensions — *u-vekhol ge'ulah titnu* and the triple *yigalenu* — are reinterpreted as doing other work: one establishes the degree of obligation, the other establishes the order of relatives. Neither actually extends *ge'ulas krovim* to the two unaddressed categories.

## Obligation & Hebrew (II)

### Rabbi's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק? אַהָא, דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל נִגְאָל לִקְרוֹבִים אוֹ אֵינוֹ נִגְאָל לִקְרוֹבִים? אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי לָא תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּאָמַר: מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִגְאָל בְּאֵלֶּה – נִגְאָל בְּשֵׁשׁ, אַלְמָא לָא מִיפְּרֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Where was** this opinion **of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak** originally **stated?** The Gemara answers that it was stated **with regard to this** issue, **as a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Can **a Hebrew slave sold to a Jew be redeemed by** his **relatives or can he not be redeemed by** his **relatives?** The Gemara comments: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **you should not raise the dilemma, as he says: One who is not redeemed by these** relatives **is redeemed by six** years of service. **Evidently,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that a Hebrew slave sold to a gentile **cannot be redeemed** by his relatives.

The question of *ge'ulas krovim* for an *eved Ivri* sold to a Jew is taken up next. According to Rabbi (Rebbi), the answer is clear and explicit. Rebbi had taught: *mi she-eino nigal be-eleh nigal be-shesh* — only one who is not redeemed by relatives is released in six years. The converse is equally true: an *eved Ivri* sold to a Jew, who does go out after six years, does *not* go out via *ge'ulas krovim*. Rebbi thus explicitly excludes the Hebrew slave sold to a Jew from the framework of *ge'ulas krovim*. The ruling is: *lo miphrik* — he is not redeemed by relatives.

### Slaves to Jews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן מַאי? יָלְפִינַן ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, וְלָא דָּרְשִׁי ״יִגְאָלֶנּוּ״, אוֹ דִילְמָא ״יִגְאָלֶנּוּ״ – לָזֶה וְלֹא לְאַחֵר?
>
> **Translation:** **When you raise the dilemma,** it is **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis. What** is the *halakha*? Does one **derive** the verbal analogy comparing **"hired worker"** (Leviticus 25:40) and **"hired worker"** (Leviticus 25:53) from the case of a Jew sold to a gentile, and say that just as one can be redeemed by one's relatives if sold to a gentile, so too, one can be redeemed if sold to a Jew? **And** at the same time **one does not interpret homiletically** the phrase **"may redeem him"** (Leviticus 25:48), stated with regard to one sold to a gentile, as a restricting term. **Or perhaps** the phrase **"may redeem him"** is in fact a restricting term, which indicates that this *halakha* applies only **to this** slave, i.e., only one sold to a gentile can be redeemed by relatives, **and** it does **not** apply **to another** slave, one sold to a Jew.

The question of *ge'ulas krovim* for an *eved Ivri* sold to a Jew is, however, a live dispute among the Rabbis who have not articulated Rebbi's position. The Gemara asks: do the Rabbis apply a *gezerah shavah* of *sachir sachir* — linking the *eved Ivri* sold to a Jew to the one sold to a non-Jew — and thereby extend *ge'ulas krovim* to the former? Or does *yigalenu* (stated regarding the slave sold to a non-Jew) function exclusively: *le-zeh ve-lo le-acher* — only for this category and not for the other?

### Rashi's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״בְּכֹל... גְּאוּלָּה תִּתְּנוּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת בָּתִּים וְעֶבֶד עִבְרִי. מַאי לָאו בָּתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה וְעֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל? לָא, עֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְנׇכְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: "And **in all** of your ancestral land **you shall grant redemption** for the land" (Leviticus 25:24). The inclusive expression "in all" serves **to include houses and a Hebrew slave. What, is it not** correct to say that this includes **houses of walled cities and a Hebrew slave sold to a Jew?** The Gemara rejects this interpretation: **No,** one can say that this is referring to **a Hebrew slave sold to a gentile.**

The Gemara once again cycles through *u-vekhol ge'ulah titnu* and the triple *yigalenu* as potential sources for extending the scope. And once again the Gemara pushes back: *batei chatzerim ve-sdeh achuza be-hedya ksiv* — these are already explicit, and the proposed scope-extensions are doing other work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶבֶד עִבְרִי הַנִּמְכָּר לְנׇכְרִי בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״אוֹ דֹדוֹ אוֹ בֶן דֹּדוֹ יִגְאָלֶנּוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: It is **explicitly written with regard to a Hebrew slave sold to a gentile: "Either his uncle or his uncle's son may redeem him"** (Leviticus 25:49). If so, it is unnecessary to derive the option of redemption by a relative from an inclusive expression in a different verse.

Rashi clarifies the final move. The verse *u-vekhol ge'ulah titnu* establishes — according to the Gemara's conclusion — that by *eved Ivri* sold to a non-Jew, the relatives' redemption is a *chovah*, not merely a *reshut*, and this ruling holds *even according to Rabbi Yehoshua*. That is a notable shift: Rabbi Yehoshua holds that *ge'ulas krovim* is generally a *reshut* (as in the case of *sdeh achuza*), yet even he agrees that by an *eved Ivri* sold to *ovdei kochavim* it rises to the level of *chovah*. As Rashi explains: *de-lo yitma be-ovdei kochavim* — so that he not become assimilated among the gentiles. The urgency of that concern generates an obligation that the less urgent cases do not.

### Final Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא לְקוֹבְעוֹ חוֹבָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **That** verse serves **to establish** that redeeming him is **an obligation. And even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** who says that there is no requirement to redeem land, this case is different, as it is proper to redeem him to prevent him from being assimilated by gentiles.

The Gemara's final resolution: the two potential scope-extending derivations — *u-vekhol ge'ulah titnu* and *yigalenu yigalenu yigalenu* — are both reinterpreted as doing internal work within the two categories that the Torah already mentions. The *bechol* verse deepens the obligation in those categories; the triple *yigalenu* establishes the hierarchical order of *karov karov kodem* — the closer relative redeems first. Neither verse extends *ge'ulas krovim* to *batei arei chomah* or to an *eved Ivri* sold to a Jew. According to the conclusion of the Gemara, those two categories remain outside the scope of *ge'ulas krovim*.
