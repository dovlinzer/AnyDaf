# Shabbat 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Selling to Non-Jews

### Beis Shammai's Sale Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִמְכּוֹ אָדָם חֶפְצוֹ לְגוֹי, וְלֹא יַשְׁאִילֶנּוּ, וְלֹא יַלְוֶנּוּ, וְלֹא יִתֵּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְבֵיתוֹ. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לַבַּיִת הַסָּמוּךְ לַחוֹמָה. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הֵן הֵן דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הֵן הֵן דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל — לֹא בָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶלָּא לְפָרֵשׁ דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai say: A person may not sell his object to a gentile, and lend it to him, and loan him money, and give him an object as a gift on Shabbat eve, unless there is sufficient time for him to reach his house while it is still day. And Beit Hillel say:** He is permitted to do this if there is sufficient time **for him to reach a house adjacent to the wall** of the place where he is going. **Rabbi Akiva says:** It is permitted to give an object to a gentile on Shabbat eve if there is **sufficient time for him to exit the entrance of** the Jewish person's **house.** What the gentile does afterward is irrelevant. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said: That is the statement of Rabbi Akiva; that is the statement of Beit Hillel. Rabbi Akiva came only to explain the statement of Beit Hillel.** The *tanna* whose version of Beit Hillel's statement was: Until he reaches the house adjacent to the wall, held that Beit Hillel's opinion was similar to Beit Shammai's opinion. Rabbi Akiva came to elucidate the actual opinion of Beit Hillel.

The daf opens at the bottom of 18b, where a *Tanu Rabbanan* introduces a series of restrictions attributed to Beis Shammai concerning transactions with non-Jews on *erev Shabbos*. This discussion follows naturally from the preceding material about beginning a *melacha* before Shabbos and having it continue — whether on the basis of *shevisas keilim* or some form of rabbinic decree — and the various exceptions that Beis Hillel and even Beis Shammai themselves acknowledged.

The *Baraisa* states: *Beis Shammai omrim v'lo yimkor adam cheftzo l'nochri* — Beis Shammai prohibits selling one's objects to a non-Jew on *erev Shabbos*, and similarly forbids lending to him or giving him a gift, *ella k'dei she-yagi'a l'beiso* — unless there is sufficient time for the non-Jew to reach his home before Shabbos begins.

### Baraisa Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לַבַּיִת הַסָּמוּךְ לַחוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say:** He is permitted to do this if there is sufficient time **for him to reach a house adjacent to the wall** of the place where he is going.

The *Mishnah* had used the phrase *l'makom karov* — to a nearby place — which the *Baraisa* now clarifies more precisely: the non-Jew must have enough time to reach his home. Beis Hillel, for its part, requires only that he reach *bayis hasamuch lachoma* — the house at the wall at the edge of the city. This formulation assumes that the non-Jew is traveling to a different city to bring the sold item home, which reflects a social reality in which cities tended to be either predominantly Jewish or predominantly non-Jewish, making it quite natural to assume that a non-Jewish buyer would be departing for a different location.

### Rabbi Akiva Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** It is permitted to give an object to a gentile on Shabbat eve if there is **sufficient time for him to exit the entrance of** the Jewish person's **house.**

Rabbi Akiva presents a more lenient position: *k'dei she-yeitzei mi-pesach beiso* — it is sufficient that the non-Jew departs from the seller's doorway. Rabbi Yosi bar Yehuda clarifies that Rabbi Akiva is not introducing a third position but rather explaining what Beis Hillel's position actually was: *lo ba Rabbi Akiva ella l'faresh divrei Beis Hillel* — he came only to articulate Beis Hillel's view. In that reading, Beis Hillel was always as lenient as Rabbi Akiva describes, requiring no more than that the non-Jew leave the house. This coheres with the *Mishnah*'s own formulation, where Beis Hillel simply permits the transaction without specifying distance — a rendering far closer to Rabbi Akiva's version than to the *Tosefta*'s stricter language requiring the non-Jew to reach his home.

### Maris Ayin vs Process

What is the underlying concern driving these restrictions? The situation here is not analogous to *shevisas keilim* in any straightforward sense, since the object has been sold — it now belongs to the non-Jew, and what he does with his own property is no longer the seller's concern. Rather, the concern appears to be one of appearances: a non-Jew departing on *erev Shabbos* carrying an object that formerly belonged to a Jew creates the impression, for any onlooker, that he is handling and transporting it on behalf of the Jew on Shabbos.

This is the thrust of *maris ayin* — not that we distrust any particular individual, but that perceptions are a social reality, and people naturally draw conclusions from appearances without always extending the benefit of the doubt. As one might observe, *maris ayin* assumes precisely that people will form natural inferences and that the experience of Shabbos is shaped in part by those visible realities around us. Beis Shammai is concerned about this; Beis Hillel is considerably more lenient, and it is Beis Hillel's view that we rule by in practice.

### Chametz Sale Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִמְכּוֹ אָדָם חֲמֵצוֹ לְגוֹי אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יוֹדֵעַ בּוֹ שֶׁיִּכְלֶה קוֹדֶם הַפֶּסַח, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמּוּתָּר לְאוֹכְלוֹ — מוּתָּר לְמוֹכְרוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כּוּתָּח הַבַּבְלִי וְכׇל מִינֵי כּוּתָּח אָסוּר לִמְכּוֹר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם קוֹדֶם הַפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a similar principle in a *baraita* with regard to another tannaitic dispute. **Beit Shammai say: A person may not sell his leaven to a gentile** on Passover eve **unless he knows that** the leaven **will be finished before Passover. And Beit Hillel say: As long as it is permitted** for the Jew **to eat** leaven, **it is** also **permitted for him to sell it** to a gentile. The Jew ceases to be responsible for the leaven sold to a gentile from the moment it is sold. **And Rabbi Yehuda says: With regard to Babylonian *kutaḥ,*** a spice that contains leavened bread crumbs, **and all kinds of *kutaḥ*, it is prohibited to sell** it to a gentile **thirty days before Passover.**

A parallel *Baraisa* makes the concern more precise. Beis Shammai prohibits selling one's *chametz* to a non-Jew before Pesach unless one knows that he has enough time to finish it before Pesach arrives: *Beis Shammai omrim lo yimkor adam chametzo lenachri ela im kein yodeia bo sheyichleh kodem ha-Pesach*. Beis Hillel, by contrast, permits the sale for as long as a Jew himself may eat the *chametz*: *kol zman she-mutar le-echol mutar limkor*. Rabbi Yehuda further specifies that Babylonian *kutach* — a dip containing breadcrumbs — may not be sold for thirty days before Pesach, since such a spread is consumed gradually over time, consistent with Beis Shammai's position.

This *chametz* case is telling, because it is difficult to sustain a pure *maris ayin* argument here. When a non-Jew eats *chametz* in his own home, there is no obvious visual impression that the Jew is involved — the *chametz* belongs to the non-Jew, sits in the non-Jew's home, and is consumed by the non-Jew. Yet Beis Shammai still objects.

Combining the two cases — the sale of objects and the sale of *chametz* — suggests that Beis Shammai's concern is not primarily one of appearances but of process responsibility. When I sell you something on *erev Shabbos*, I have initiated a process that culminates in you carrying it through Shabbos. When I sell you *chametz* before Pesach, I have initiated a process that culminates in your eating *chametz* — which is mine — on Pesach. Even though you are a non-Jew who bears no personal prohibition, and even though the object now belongs to you, I set this in motion. This reasoning closely parallels the concern about beginning a *melacha* on *erev Shabbos* that continues into Shabbos — whether the dyeing, the ink-making, or the other processes discussed earlier in the *masechta*. There too the underlying concern is not merely one of appearances but of being the initiating party in a process that unfolds across Shabbos.

### Feeding in Courtyard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נוֹתְנִין מְזוֹנוֹת לִפְנֵי הַכֶּלֶב בֶּחָצֵר. נְטָלוֹ וְיָצָא — אֵין נִזְקָקִין לוֹ. כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ: נוֹתְנִין מְזוֹנוֹת לִפְנֵי הַגּוֹי בֶּחָצֵר. נְטָלוֹ וְיָצָא — אֵין נִזְקָקִין לוֹ. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי, הַיְינוּ הָךְ. מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: הַאי רְמֵי עֲלֵיהּ, וְהַאי לָא רְמֵי עֲלֵיהּ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a different *baraita*: **One may,** *ab initio*, **put food before the dog in the courtyard** on Shabbat, and we are not concerned that the dog may lift it and carry it out to the public domain. If the dog **lifted it and exited** the courtyard, one need **not attend to him**, as he is not required to ensure that the dog will eat it specifically in that courtyard. **On a similar note,** the *baraita* continued: **One may place food before the gentile in the courtyard** on Shabbat. If the gentile **lifted it and exited, one need not attend to him.** The Gemara asks: **Why do I need this as well? This** case **is the same as that** case. The *halakhot* with regard to the dog and the gentile are identical, as Shabbat prohibitions do not apply to either of them. The Gemara answers: There is a distinction. **Lest you say** that in **this** case, the case of the dog, responsibility for its food **is incumbent upon** the owner of the courtyard who owns the dog. **And** in **this** case, the case of the gentile, responsibility for his food **is not incumbent upon** the owner of the courtyard. Therefore, in a situation where there is concern that Shabbat will be desecrated, there is room to say that one may not give the gentile his food. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that in that case, it is also permitted.

The *Gemara* at the top of 19a adds a case that illuminates the limits of this process-responsibility principle. *Tanu Rabbanan*: one may place food before a dog in the courtyard on Shabbos itself. If the dog picks it up and carries it out, one is not responsible — *ein nizkakim lo*. Similarly, one may place food before a non-Jew in the courtyard — a *reshus hayachid* — and if he takes it and leaves, one bears no further responsibility.

Tosafos notes that this ruling holds even according to Beis Shammai. Why? Because the critical distinction is the nature of the process one has initiated. When one sells a non-Jew an object on *erev Shabbos* and he does not live nearby, the natural, foreseeable, and intended endpoint of that transaction is the non-Jew carrying the object home — including through Shabbos. The process one began leads directly and obviously into Shabbos. But when one invites a non-Jew into one's courtyard and places food before him, one has begun a process directed entirely toward eating in place, right there. If the non-Jew then takes the food and carries it home, that is a separate, secondary act — not the culmination of what one initiated. Responsibility ends with the first process.

The *Gemara* questions why both cases — the dog and the non-Jew — are needed, since they seem identical. The answer: one might have thought that because you bear an obligation to feed your dog, perhaps leniency applies to your pet uniquely, and the ruling by the non-Jew, where you have no such obligation, would be stricter. The *Gemara* teaches that this makes no difference — in both cases, since the process you initiated was intended to culminate within the courtyard, what happens afterward is not your responsibility.

Taken together, these rulings support a consistent theme running through Beis Shammai's positions throughout this section of the *masechta*: beginning a process that continues into Shabbos — even when the continuation is carried out by a non-Jew acting on his own — implicates the one who began it. The exceptions arise either when the continuation on Shabbos would not itself constitute a biblical violation, or when what happens on Shabbos is genuinely disconnected from what one initiated beforehand.

## Renting Objects

### Rental Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יַשְׂכִּיר אָדָם כֵּלָיו לְגוֹי בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, בִּרְבִיעִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **A person may not rent his utensils to a gentile on Shabbat eve,** as it appears that the Jew is receiving payment for work performed on Shabbat. However, **on the fourth and on the fifth** days of the week **it is permitted.**

The *Gemara* continues: *lo yaskir adam keilav lenachri be'erev Shabbos* — one may not rent one's utensils to a non-Jew on *erev Shabbos*. On Wednesday or Thursday, however, this is permissible. This prohibition is presumably attributed to Beis Shammai.

### Sechar Shabbat Issue

Tosafos raises an important analytic point: one cannot explain this prohibition on the basis of *shevisas keilim* as Beis Shammai understands it. Were *shevisas keilim* the operative concern, renting one's utensils on Wednesday or Thursday would be equally prohibited, since the vessels remain one's own property regardless of when the rental begins, and one's vessel performing labor on Shabbos would be just as problematic whether the rental started Friday or Wednesday. Tosafos adds that the same logic would apply to renting an animal — which everyone agrees implicates *shevisas behema* — but that concern applies equally no matter how far in advance the animal is rented.

Tosafos concludes that this is a ruling consistent with Beis Hillel, which does not hold of *shevisas keilim* for utensils — or alternatively that the discussion concerns garments, for which *shevisas keilim* would not be applicable. Given this, Tosafos identifies the operative concern as *sechar Shabbat*: the prohibition against earning income for work done on Shabbos. If one rents a utensil to a non-Jew immediately before Shabbos, the rent received is payment specifically for Shabbos use, making one a beneficiary of Shabbos labor — which is problematic. If the rental begins on Wednesday or Thursday, the payment encompasses work performed across multiple days, and the Shabbos component is absorbed into a broader arrangement that does not constitute *sechar Shabbat*.

Rashi, for his part, explains the prohibition in terms of appearances: renting a utensil on Friday afternoon creates the impression that one is lending it specifically for use on Shabbos. Even so, since it is the non-Jew doing the work for himself and not for the Jewish owner, this is most naturally understood as a Beis Shammai concern — not based on *shevisas keilim*, but rather on the principle of beginning a process that leads into Shabbos. If one rents a hoe to a non-Jew on Friday afternoon and he uses it on Shabbos, one has initiated a process whose natural continuation is Shabbos labor. If the rental began on Wednesday or Thursday, that causal link to Shabbos is sufficiently attenuated that one is no longer understood as having begun the process that culminates in Shabbos work.

## Sending Letters

### Letter Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ, אֵין מְשַׁלְּחִין אִיגְּרוֹת בְּיַד גּוֹי בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, בִּרְבִיעִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי — מוּתָּר. אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַכֹּהֵן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ עַל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הֶחָסִיד, שֶׁלֹּא נִמְצָא כְּתַב יָדוֹ בְּיַד גּוֹי מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **On a similar note, one may not send letters in the hand of a gentile on Shabbat eve.** However, **on the fourth and on the fifth** days of the week **it is permitted.** Nevertheless, **they said about Rabbi Yosei the priest, and some say** that they said this **about Rabbi Yosei the Ḥasid, that** a document in **his handwriting was never found in the hand of a gentile,** so that a gentile would not carry his letter on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* continues: *ein meshalechin igeret beyad nochri be'erev Shabbat* — one may not send a letter via a non-Jew on *erev Shabbos*. On Wednesday or Thursday it is permissible.

This case is more immediately obvious than the cases of selling or renting, because here the non-Jew is carrying the letter explicitly for the sender. Even if one did not explicitly instruct him to carry it on Shabbos — and even if the non-Jew theoretically had the option to complete delivery before Shabbos — the practical reality is that one is directly commissioning an act of *melacha* on one's own behalf.

The *Gemara* adds: *amru alav al Rabbi Yose HaKohen — v'amrei lah Rabbi Yose HaChassid — shelo nimtza ktav yado beyad nochri me'olam* — it was said of him, referred to as Rabbi Yose HaChassid, that his handwriting was never found in the hands of a non-Jew. He maintained this stringency absolutely, even when giving a letter well before *erev Shabbos*, because there was always the possibility that the non-Jew might still be carrying his letter on Shabbos. The language here is significant: *ktav yado* — his handwriting — highlighting that one's personal script is in some sense an extension of oneself, making one's participation in any *melacha* performed while carrying the letter especially palpable. This gives the letter case a qualitative dimension beyond the selling and renting cases: not only is the non-Jew acting on the sender's behalf, but the sender's own identity is physically present in the process being carried out on Shabbos.

A practical question arises: does this apply to placing mail in a postal box on Friday? The analysis of such cases is typically conducted through the framework of *amira le-goy* — the prohibition against instructing a non-Jew to perform *melacha* on Shabbos — rather than through the specifically Beis Shammai framework of process-initiation that governs much of this section of the *Gemara*. Depositing mail in a postal box on Friday raises questions about whether the carrier is performing work for the individual sender or for the postal service as an institution, and whether the sender has truly "instructed" anyone to do anything. Sending express mail for next-day delivery on Friday would clearly be more problematic. These remain subjects of significant halachic discussion.

## Renting Objects (II)

### Fixed Price Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מְשַׁלְּחִין אִיגֶּרֶת בְּיַד גּוֹי עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קוֹצֵץ לוֹ דָּמִים. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְבֵיתוֹ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לַבַּיִת הַסָּמוּךְ לַחוֹמָה. וַהֲלֹא קָצַץ? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְאִם לֹא קָצַץ — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְבֵיתוֹ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לַבַּיִת הַסָּמוּךְ לַחוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not send a letter in the hand of a gentile** on **Shabbat eve unless he stipulates a set sum of money for him.** In that case, anything the gentile does with this letter is not in service of the Jew, but rather on his own, since his payment is stipulated in advance. **Beit Shammai say:** One may only give a letter to a gentile on Shabbat eve if there is sufficient time **for** the gentile **to reach his house** before dark. **And Beit Hillel say:** If there is sufficient time **for him to reach the house adjacent to the wall** of the city to which he was sent. **Didn't he stipulate a set** price? What difference does it make whether he reaches the city on Shabbat eve or on Shabbat? **Rav Sheshet said,** the *baraita* **is saying as follows: And if he did not stipulate a set** price for the task, **Beit Shammai say:** One may only give a letter to a gentile on Shabbat eve if there is sufficient time **for** the gentile **to reach his house** before dark. **And Beit Hillel say:** If there is sufficient time **for him to reach the house adjacent to the wall** of the city to which he was sent.

Returning to the framework of the *Mishnah*'s earlier discussion of giving items to a non-Jewish launderer, the *Gemara* makes explicit two criteria that permit such arrangements: first, the fee must be fixed — *katzatz damim* — rather than calculated hourly; second, the non-Jew must have a theoretical ability to complete the work at some time other than Shabbos. Both conditions must be met.

The rationale for the fixed-price requirement is that if one pays by the hour, then to the extent that the non-Jew works on Shabbos and one compensates him for that specific Shabbos hour, one is effectively paying for — and thereby participating in — Shabbos labor. A fixed fee for the job, by contrast, severs this direct linkage: the non-Jew is doing the work on his own account, for an agreed price, and the question of when he chooses to do it is, in principle, his own.

The requirement that there exist theoretical ability to perform the work outside of Shabbos is equally important, though its application can be subtle. If one drops off a car at a mechanic on Friday and picks it up Saturday night, with no realistic window for the work to have been done other than during Shabbos, that raises concern regardless of the fixed-price arrangement. What counts as adequate theoretical availability is a genuine question — whether the mechanic could, in principle, have rearranged his schedule to complete the work Saturday night after Shabbos matters, even if practically he would not.

These same principles apply, with heightened scrutiny, when the work is performed on one's property. Because construction on a home is visible and public — passersby see workers on the property on Shabbos — there is an additional concern that it appears as though they are doing labor for the Jewish owner. Tosafos addresses this and notes that even Rabbeinu Tam, who maintained in theory that construction work subject to the standard fixed-price criteria could be permitted, did not rely on his own ruling when it came to building his own home. He was more *machmir* in practice. The practical complications are also significant: workers are commonly paid hourly at least at the individual laborer level, even when a contractor charges a fixed fee for the overall project.

The interaction between these two criteria invites close analysis. One might argue, for instance, that a first-class postage stamp constitutes a fixed price for the service of letter delivery. However, the second criterion — that the carrier have realistic ability to deliver other than on Shabbos — is more doubtful, especially if the relevant pickup or delivery cycle falls exclusively within Shabbos. Additionally, the relationship between the sender and the individual carrier is mediated through a large institution, raising the further question of whether the non-Jewish worker performing the actual delivery is doing so for the sender or for the postal service as his employer.

## Sending Letters (II)

### Fixed Price & Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא אֵין מְשַׁלְּחִין? לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִּקְבִיעַ בֵּי דַוָּאר בְּמָתָא, וְהָא דְּלָא קְבִיעַ בֵּי דַוָּאר בְּמָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't you say in the first clause** of the *baraita*, that **one may not send** a letter unless he stipulated a set price? Without stipulating a set price, one may not send a letter at all. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as it is possible to explain that **this,** where we learned that one is permitted to give a letter to a gentile on Shabbat eve even if he did not stipulate a set price, is in a case **where the house of the mail carrier [*bei doar*] is permanently located in the city. And this,** where it is permitted to give a letter to a gentile only if he stipulated a set price, is in a case **where the house of the mail carrier is not permanently located in the city.**

The *Baraisa* states explicitly: *ein meshalchin igros beyad nochri erev Shabbos ela im kein katzatz lo damim* — one may not send letters via a non-Jew on *erev Shabbos* unless a fixed price has been set. The *Gemara* then asks how to read the additional requirement — found in the positions of Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel — that the non-Jew must have time to reach either his home or the house at the edge of the next city. Is this an independent requirement, or does it apply only when no fixed price has been set?

Rav Sheshes clarifies: when no fixed price has been set — that is, when the non-Jew is effectively being paid by the hour — then the additional time requirement applies, because without a fixed price, the Jew bears a more direct responsibility for the Shabbos labor being performed on his behalf. Beis Shammai requires that the non-Jew have time to reach his home, and Beis Hillel requires that he reach the house at the edge of the next city. With a fixed price, these distance requirements do not apply in the same way, since the non-Jew carries the letter on his own account.

The *Gemara* then addresses a tension with a *Tosefta* that appears to prohibit sending letters even beyond the distance threshold. The resolution is: *ha d'kviei doar b'masa, ha d'la kviei doar b'masa* — one case involves a city with a fixed postal office, and the other does not. In a city with a postal station, the carrier need only deposit the letter at the central office, and recipients will collect their mail from there. In that case, having time to reach the next city suffices. Where there is no such postal office, the carrier must search out the addressee individually, meaning that even arriving in the next city does not end his active labor on the sender's behalf — he continues carrying the letter for the Jew throughout his search. In that case, more time is required.

Notably, even Beis Hillel in this context acknowledges some concern about sending letters on *erev Shabbos*, particularly because here the non-Jew is unambiguously performing a service for the Jew — carrying his correspondence — which makes the Shabbos labor more direct than in the cases of selling objects or feeding a non-Jew in one's courtyard. This places the letter case at the intersection of the Beis Shammai process-initiation concern and the more universally applicable prohibition of *amira le-goy*.

## Boats & Sieges

### Three-Day Boat Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מַפְלִיגִין בִּסְפִינָה פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים קוֹדֶם לַשַּׁבָּת. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת, אֲבָל לִדְבַר מִצְוָה — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. וּפוֹסֵק עִמּוֹ עַל מְנָת לִשְׁבּוֹת, וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹבֵת — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ. וּמִצּוֹר לְצִידֹן — אֲפִילּוּ בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One may not set sail on a ship fewer than three days before Shabbat,** to avoid appearances that the Jew is performing a prohibited labor on Shabbat. **In what case is this** statement **said?** In a case where he set sail **for a voluntary matter; however,** if he sailed **for a matter** involving a **mitzva,** he may **well** do so. **And,** even then, **he** must **stipulate with** the gentile ship captain that this is **on the condition that he rests,** i.e., stops the ship, **and** even if the gentile **does not rest. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He need not** stipulate. **And** sailing on a ship that is traveling **from Tyre to Sidon,** a short journey by sea, **is permitted even on Shabbat eve.**

A new *Baraisa* introduces the rule concerning sea travel: *ein mafligin bisefina pachos mi-gimmel yamim kodem le-Shabbos* — one may not set out on a boat journey fewer than three days before Shabbos. This is a notably stricter standard than the Wednesday-Thursday threshold that governed the preceding cases of selling, renting, and sending letters.

The reason for the heightened threshold reflects the different nature of the process. A transatlantic or extended sea voyage is not an activity that is "started" in any proximate sense on *erev Shabbos*; it is an ongoing multi-day process, and if one embarks within three days of Shabbos, one's departure is sufficiently close in time that the voyage as a whole is conceptually connected to the Shabbos it will span. One has begun a journey that will be continuing on Shabbos, and because it is fundamentally a much more extended and continuous process than renting a hoe or selling an object, the window of temporal responsibility is correspondingly wider. Some *Rishonim* offer additional explanations: that it takes several days for a traveler to become acclimated to the sea and avoid seasickness that would ruin *oneg Shabbos*; or that embarking too close to Shabbos risks placing oneself in a *makom sakanah* — a situation of physical danger — which would then necessitate *chillul Shabbos*. The three-day threshold corresponds to the principle that more than three days before Shabbos, one is not yet within the conceptual orbit of the coming Shabbos and need not be anticipating its implications.

### Mitzvah & Local Trips

The restriction applies only to discretionary travel: *bameh devarim amurim — l'dvar harashus*, but for a *d'var mitzvah* — a journey undertaken for a religious obligation — one may set out even on *erev Shabbos* itself. The fact that a *d'var mitzvah* overrides this concern demonstrates that the underlying prohibition is rabbinic in nature, since biblical prohibitions are not set aside for the sake of a *mitzvah* in this way, or at minimum that whatever concern animates the rule yields to the weight of religious obligation.

Rabbi, speaking in the *Baraisa*, holds that even in the case of a *d'var mitzvah*, one should stipulate with the captain that he will not navigate the vessel on Shabbos — understanding full well that this is something of a nominal arrangement, since the captain is not actually bound to honor it and the boat will in practice continue sailing. Rabban Gamliel disagrees and holds that no such stipulation is necessary. As for short trips — the *Baraisa* cites the example of sailing from Tzor to Tzidan, a brief journey that would typically occur in connection with the *erev Shabbos* market — these are permitted even on Friday, since they do not constitute the kind of extended process that raises the concern of being bound to a Shabbos-spanning voyage.

The underlying logic of the three-day rule is that it is only when an ongoing process begins within what one might call the Shabbos-adjacent period — the three days immediately preceding Shabbos — that one bears responsibility for what that process entails over Shabbos itself.

### Siege Warfare

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין צָרִין עַל עֲיָירוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים קוֹדֶם לַשַּׁבָּת. וְאִם הִתְחִילוּ — אֵין מַפְסִיקִין. וְכֵן הָיָה שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: ״עַד רִדְתָּהּ״, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **One may not lay siege to cities of gentiles fewer than three days before Shabbat,** to avoid the need to desecrate Shabbat in establishing the siege. **And if they** already **began** establishing the siege fewer than three days before Shabbat, **they need not stop** all war-related actions even on Shabbat. **And so Shammai would say:** From that which is written: "And you should build a siege against the city that is waging war with you **until it falls"** (Deuteronomy 20:20), it is derived that the siege should be sustained "until it falls." Consequently, the siege must continue **even on Shabbat.**

The *Gemara* draws a parallel case: *ein tzarin al ayarot shel nochrim pachos mi-gimmel yamim kodem l'Shabbos* — one may not lay siege to a non-Jewish city fewer than three days before Shabbos. The same three-day rule applies, and for a closely related reason: once a siege begins, military exigency will require continuing operations on Shabbos. Rather than creating a situation in which *chillul Shabbos* becomes necessary, one should not initiate the siege within three days of Shabbos.

However — and this is a crucial qualification — *v'im hitchilu ein mafsikin*: once the siege has begun, one does not interrupt it, and it continues even through Shabbos. Shammai himself is cited as deriving this from the verse *ki tatzur el ir yamim rabbim* — "when you besiege a city many days" — *ad ridetah*, until it falls: *afilu b'Shabbat* — even on Shabbos. Military operations once underway implicate *pikuach nefesh* throughout, and so continuation through Shabbos is not merely permitted but required. This parallel illuminates the logic of the boat case: the concern in both instances is not about what happens on Shabbos itself — war and genuine danger override Shabbos entirely — but about deliberately placing oneself in a situation where those overrides will be necessary.

### Garment Practices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: נוֹהֲגִין הָיוּ וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי צָדוֹק: כָּךְ הָיָה מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, שֶׁהָיוּ נוֹתְנִין כְּלֵי לָבָן לְכוֹבֵס שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים קוֹדֶם לַשַּׁבָּת, וּצְבוּעִים אֲפִילּוּ בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת. וּמִדִּבְרֵיהֶם לָמַדְנוּ שֶׁהַלְּבָנִים קָשִׁים לְכַבְּסָן יוֹתֵר מִן הַצְּבוּעִין.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said:** The ancestral house of my father, the dynasty of *Nesi'im* from the house of Hillel, **was accustomed** to give its white clothes to a gentile launderer no fewer than three days before Shabbat. **It was taught** in the *Tosefta* that **Rabbi Tzadok said: This was the custom of the house of Rabban Gamliel: They would give white clothes to the** gentile **launderer three days before Shabbat, and** they would give him **colored** clothes **even on Shabbat eve.** The Gemara comments: **And from their statement we learned that white** garments **are more difficult to launder than colored ones,** as in white garments every stain is more conspicuous.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is cited in the *Mishnah*: the house of Rabban Gamliel would give white garments to the launderer three days before Shabbos, while colored garments could be given even on *erev Shabbos* itself, since they require less effort to clean. This stringent practice aligned with the Beis Shammai position, though the house of Rabban Gamliel identified with Beis Hillel — illustrating that personal piety sometimes led individuals to adopt the stricter view even when they did not rule that way for others.

The *Tanya* cites Rabbi Tzadok confirming this as the practice of Rabban Gamliel's household. The *Gemara* derives from this practice the principle that white garments are harder to launder than colored ones — a practical reality that becomes explicit halachic reasoning. The *Gemara* then relates that Abaye, when bringing a colored garment to a launderer who quoted him the same price as for white garments, invoked this very teaching: since the Rabbis had already established that white garments require more effort, the launderer was not entitled to charge the same rate for colored garments.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה יָהֵיב לֵיהּ הַהוּא מָנָא דִצְבִיעָא לְקַצָּרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּמָּה בָּעֵית עִילָּוֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּדְחִיוָּרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּבָר קַדְמוּךָ רַבָּנַן. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַאן דְּיָהֵיב מָנָא לְקַצָּרָא, בְּמִשְׁחָא נִיתֵּיב לֵיהּ וּבְמִשְׁחָא נִשְׁקוֹל מִינֵּיהּ. דְּאִי טְפֵי — אַפְסְדֵיהּ דְּמַתְחֵיהּ. וְאִי בְּצִיר — אַפְסְדֵיהּ דְּכַוְּוצֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, the Gemara relates that **Abaye gave this dyed garment to the launderer.** Abaye **said to** the launderer: **How much do you want** as payment **to** wash **it?** The launderer **said to** Abaye: **Same as for a white** garment. Abaye **said to him:** You cannot deceive me in this matter, **as the Sages already preceded you,** as it was taught in the *baraita* which garment is more difficult to wash. On this topic, **Abaye said: One who gives clothing to the launderer, he should give** it to **him by measure and he should take** it back **from him by measure.** In **that** way, **if it is longer,** it is an indication that the launderer **caused him a loss** because **he stretched** the garment. **And if it is shorter, he** certainly **caused him a loss** because **he shrunk it.**

The *Gemara* appends a practical piece of advice from Abaye: when giving a garment to a launderer, measure it first and measure it again upon return. If it is larger than when given, the launderer stretched and damaged it; if smaller, he shrank it. The competence and honesty of the craftsman matter, and one should protect oneself accordingly.

## Continuing Processes

### Beis Shammai Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ, שֶׁטּוֹעֲנִין כּוּ׳: מַאי שְׁנָא כּוּלְּהוּ דִּגְזַרוּ בְּהוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא קוֹרוֹת בֵּית הַבַּד וְעִיגּוּלֵי הַגַּת דְּלָא גְּזַרוּ? הָנָךְ דְּאִי עָבֵיד לְהוּ בְּשַׁבָּת מִיחַיַּיב חַטָּאת — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת עִם חֲשֵׁכָה. קוֹרוֹת בֵּית הַבַּד וְעִיגּוּלֵי הַגַּת דְּאִי עָבֵיד לְהוּ בְּשַׁבָּת לָא מִיחַיַּיב חַטָּאת — לָא גְּזַרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **these,** Beit Shammai, **and those,** Beit Hillel, **agree** that **one may load** the beam of the olive press and the circular wine press. The Gemara asks: **What is different about all** of the cases in the mishna, **where Beit Shammai issued a decree** prohibiting **them, and what is different about the beams of the olive press and the circular wine press that** Beit Shammai **did not issue a decree** prohibiting them? The Gemara answers: **Those** cases, **where if he performed them on Shabbat he is rendered liable** to bring **a sin-offering, Beit Shammai issued a decree** prohibiting **them** on **Shabbat eve at nightfall.** However, in the cases of **the beams of the olive press and the circular wine press,** where even **if he performed them on Shabbat he is not rendered liable** to bring **a sin-offering,** Beit Shammai **did not issue a decree.**

The *Gemara* now addresses the *Mishnah*'s ruling that both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree that one may place a beam on olives or grapes before Shabbos and allow the press to continue operating through Shabbos — *uve-shavin elu va-elu sheto'anin*. This is noteworthy because Beis Shammai is generally the one prohibiting the initiation of such processes. Why does Beis Shammai agree here?

The *Gemara* poses the question directly: what is the reason Beis Shammai issued a *gezeira* against all the other processes — dyeing, ink-making, and the like — but not against placing the beam on olives and grapes? Tosafos flags that the *Gemara*'s formulation here is significant: it says *gezeira*, not *shevisas keilim*. This confirms that Beis Shammai's concern throughout this section is properly understood as a rabbinic decree against beginning processes on *erev Shabbos* that will continue into Shabbos — not as the more far-reaching principle of *shevisas keilim* — and the *gezeira* is grounded in the concern that either one will come to fiddle with the process on Shabbos itself and commit a *melacha*, or that one will reason from the permissibility of a process begun just before sunset to the permissibility of beginning it on Shabbos directly.

The answer the *Gemara* gives is that the *gezeira* was issued only for processes that, were one to perform them on Shabbos directly, would constitute a biblical *melacha* requiring a *chatat*. Where the act of doing it oneself on Shabbos would not rise to the level of a *melacha de'orayta*, Beis Shammai saw no basis for the decree.

### Olives & Grapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא דְּכֹל מִידֵּי דְּאָתֵי מִמֵּילָא, שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי (בַּר) [בְּרַבִּי] חֲנִינָא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַשּׁוּם וְהַבּוֹסֶר וְהַמְּלִילוֹת שֶׁרִסְּקָן מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: יִגְמוֹר מִשֶּׁתֶּחְשַׁךְ, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִגְמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who holds that **anything that comes on its own,** and not as the result of an action, it may **well** be done on Shabbat? **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **the garlic and the unripe** grapes, **and the stalks** of wheat **that he crushed while it was still day, Rabbi Yishmael says: He may** continue tending to them and **finish after it gets dark,** as after the crushing is completed these items are placed beneath a weight, so that the liquids will continue to seep out. **And Rabbi Akiva says: He may not finish.**

This leads to the question: why would squeezing olives and grapes not be a *melacha de'orayta*? Squeezing — *sechita* — is a classic biblical prohibition, and olives and grapes are its paradigm cases. The answer lies in the prior crushing. Before the olives or grapes are placed in the press, they have already been crushed — stamped, milled, or otherwise broken down. At that point, the juices will emerge on their own. Placing a beam on them merely accelerates a process that is already underway naturally. The *Gemara* frames the question: who is the *Tanna* who holds that anything that comes about by itself — *de'ati mimmela* — is acceptable?

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina answers: it is Rabbi Yishmael, based on the following *Mishnah*: if one has crushed garlic, unripe grapes (*boser*), or unripe wheat stalks (*malilot*) before Shabbos, Rabbi Yishmael says one may finish the process after dark on Saturday night, while Rabbi Akiva says one may not. The permissibility of consuming the juices that run off naturally after crushing, in Rabbi Yishmael's view, reflects the underlying position that were one to squeeze them oneself at that stage, it would not constitute a biblical violation — because the crushing has already fundamentally completed the productive work, and what remains is merely the natural emergence of liquid.

### Honey Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא. דִּתְנַן: חַלּוֹת דְּבַשׁ שֶׁרִיסְּקָן בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת וְיָצְאוּ מֵעַצְמָן — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַתִּיר. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? אָמַר לָךְ, הָתָם הוּא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא אוֹכֶל וּלְבַסּוֹף אוֹכֶל, הָכָא מֵעִיקָּרָא אוֹכֶל וְהַשְׁתָּא מַשְׁקֶה. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר לָךְ: הָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דַּאֲפִילּוּ זֵיתִים וַעֲנָבִים נָמֵי שָׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *amora* **Rabbi Elazar said:** Our mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar** the *tanna*. **As we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **honeycombs that he crushed on Shabbat eve and** the honey **came out on its own** on Shabbat day, **it is prohibited to eat** the honey, like anything that was prepared on Shabbat. **And Rabbi Elazar permits** eating it on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina, what is the reason he did not say** in accordance with the explanation of **Rabbi Elazar?** Apparently, Rabbi Elazar's explanation in the mishna is more accurate. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yosei could have **said to you** that **there,** in the case of the honeycombs, **it is food from the beginning and it is food at the end,** as honey is considered food. Therefore, there was no squeezing of liquid from food at all. However, **here,** in all of the cases in the mishna, **from the beginning** they were **food and now** they became **liquid,** and that is the definition of squeezing. **And Rabbi Elazar** could have **said to you** in response to this assertion: **We heard** that **Rabbi Elazar permitted olives and grapes as well.**

Rabbi Ami, however, suggests the ruling derives instead from Rabbi Elazar, based on a different case: *challot devash she-riskan mibe'od yom* — loaves of honeycomb crushed before Shabbos, from which honey runs off on Shabbos itself. The *Mishnah* there rules that the honey is forbidden; Rabbi Elazar permits it. Rabbi Elazar's leniency implies that even were one to squeeze the honeycomb directly, it would not be a biblical *melacha*, and therefore one need not prohibit benefiting from honey that flows on its own.

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina, however, declines to derive from the honey case. His reasoning: *hatam hu di-me'ikara ochla u-lesof ochla* — honey begins as a food and remains a food even as a liquid; its halachic classification does not change. But in the case of olives and grapes: *me-ikara ochla ve-hashta mashka* — what begins as food ends as a liquid. This transformation is halachically significant. The prohibition of *sechita* is defined in terms of extracting liquid from food. When the endpoint is liquid, the act of squeezing is more clearly a full *melacha*; when the endpoint is still categorized as food — as with honey, which retains the status of a food despite its liquid consistency — the prohibition applies differently. Moreover, squeezing honey from honeycomb is structurally different from squeezing juice from olives: the honey sits interspersed within the wax rather than being absorbed inside a cellular structure, and the act of pressing it out is less clearly analogous to classic *sechita*.

Therefore, Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina maintains that one cannot prove from the honey case that squeezing olives and grapes after crushing would be biblically exempt. A proof must come from a harder case — one where the endpoint is liquid.

### Crushing Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָא כִּי אֲתָא רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא, אֲתָא וְאַיְיתִי מַתְנִיתָא בִּידֵיהּ: זֵיתִים וַעֲנָבִים שֶׁרִיסְּקָן מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת וְיָצְאוּ מֵעַצְמָן — אֲסוּרִין. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּירִין. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא בָּרַיְיתָא לָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As when Rav Hoshaya from Neharde'a came, he came and brought a *baraita* with him,** in which it was taught: **Olives and grapes that he crushed from Shabbat eve and** the liquids **seeped out on their own,** the liquids **are prohibited. Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon permit** those liquids. The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Ḥanina did not know** this ***baraita*.**

That harder case is provided: Rav Hoshaia arrived from Nahardea with a *Baraisa* in hand. The *Baraisa* states that olives and grapes crushed before Shabbos, whose juices run off on Shabbos, are forbidden — but Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon permit it. Their permissive ruling, allowing one to drink those juices, reflects the position that even were one to squeeze the crushed olives and grapes directly, it would not be a *melacha de'orayta*. This is the explicit proof from the hardest case — olives and grapes, *sechita* in its most classic form — that the crushing threshold matters.

The *Gemara* then notes that Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Chanina had not heard this *Baraisa* and therefore relied on the *Mishnah* with Rabbi Yishmael's ruling about garlic and unripe grapes. Rabbi Elazar, for his part, did not rely on the Rabbi Yishmael source because of a qualification articulated by Rabbi Yochanan:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא? אָמַר לָךְ: לָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ אָמַר רָבָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּמְחוּסָּרִין דִּיכָה — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּמְחוּסָּרִין שְׁחִיקָה. וְהָנֵי נָמֵי כִּמְחוּסָּרִין דִּיכָה דָּמוּ. הוֹרָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** On the other hand, the Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Elazar, what is the reason he did not say** in accordance with the explanation of **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina,** that our mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Elazar could have **said to you: Wasn't it stated** that **Rava bar Ḥanina said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** Here it is referring **to** items that **lack grinding,** i.e., when the garlic and the unripe grapes were not ground in a pestle at all, everyone agrees that it is prohibited to place them in a manner that causes their liquids to come out on their own on Shabbat. The case **where they disagreed** was where they were already completely ground, but they were still **lacking** additional **pounding; and these** cases in our mishna **are also considered as** if they were **lacking grinding.** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina issued a** practical **ruling in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** and permitted a person to finish tending to them even after dark.

*be'mechusrin dikah kulei alma lo pligi* — where the produce has not yet been crushed, no one argues; squeezing would obviously be a *melacha de'orayta*. The argument only arises, *ki pligi be'mechusrin shechika* — in cases where the produce has been crushed but not yet fully pressed out. And the *Mishnah* with the beam, Rabbi Elazar holds, may not clearly involve produce that has been crushed to a sufficient degree, making it a less reliable proof. He therefore prefers to rest his position on the explicit *Baraisa* about olives and grapes.

The upshot is this: Beis Shammai agrees that one may place the beam on olives and grapes before Shabbos because, once the produce has been sufficiently crushed, even direct squeezing on Shabbos would not constitute a *melacha de'orayta*. The *gezeira* against beginning processes on *erev Shabbos* was limited to activities that would be biblical violations if performed on Shabbos itself. Where Shabbos performance would not be a biblical violation — because the crushing has already done the essential work — Beis Shammai had no basis for the decree, and indeed all authorities agreed it was permitted.

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina ruled in practice like Rabbi Yishmael, permitting one to benefit from juices that ran off naturally after the produce was well crushed before Shabbos.

## Muktzeh Debates

### Rav vs Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל בַּדָּדִין וּמַחְצָלוֹת שֶׁל בַּדָּדִין — רַב אָסַר וּשְׁמוּאֵל שָׁרֵי. הָנֵי כְּרָכֵי דְזוּזֵי — רַב אָסַר וּשְׁמוּאֵל שָׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: עֵז לַחֲלָבָהּ, וְרָחֵל לְגִיזָּתָהּ, וְתַרְנְגוֹלֶת לְבֵיצָתָהּ, וְתוֹרֵי דְרִידְיָא, וְתַמְרֵי דְעִיסְקָא — רַב אָסַר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מוּתָּר. וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara raised issues related to the olive press, it cites other connected matters: **Oil of olive pressers and mats of olive pressers,** which they use in their work, **Rav prohibited** moving them on Shabbat since they are set aside for a specific purpose, and it is prohibited to move an item set aside and designated for a defined purpose on Shabbat. **And Shmuel permitted** doing so, as according to Shmuel, the legal status of set-aside [*muktze*] does not apply in most cases. Along the same lines, they disagreed with regard to **those mats** used to cover merchandise transported **on a ship. Rav prohibited** using them because they are set aside **and Shmuel permitted** using them. Similarly, **Rav Naḥman said: A goat** raised **for its milk, and a ewe** that is raised **for shearing its** wool, **and a chicken** raised **for its egg, and oxen** used **for plowing,** all of which are designated for purposes other than eating, **as well as dates used for commerce;** in all of these **Rav prohibited** using them for food, or slaughtering them even on a Festival due to the prohibition of set-aside. The reason for this is that during the day, before Shabbat, he had no intention of eating them, as he set them aside for a different purpose. **And Shmuel said: They are permitted,** as in his opinion there is no prohibition of set-aside. The Gemara comments that **they disagree in the dispute of** the *tanna'im* **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon** with regard to the issue of *muktze*.

The discussion of the oil press and its byproducts introduces two new halachic categories: *shemen shel badadin* — oil that dripped off during the pressing process on Shabbos itself — and *machatza'os shel badadin* — the mats used in the press. The oil may raise concerns either because of *nolad* — it is a new substance that came into existence on Shabbos — or because permitting one to drink it might lead one to squeeze the olives directly. The mats may be subject to *muktzeh* because they are designated exclusively for a particular professional or commercial function.

Rav prohibits both; Shmuel permits both. Their dispute reflects a fundamental disagreement about the scope of *muktzeh*: Rav holds a broad definition, aligned with Rabbi Yehuda's position, while Shmuel holds a narrow definition, aligned with Rabbi Shimon's position.

### Business-Use Items

Rabbi Nachman extends the same framework to a series of analogous cases: a goat set aside for its milk, a sheep for shearing, a chicken for its eggs, an ox designated for plowing, and dates set aside to be sold at market. These are all items that were not designated for Shabbos or *Yom Tov* use — they were earmarked for commercial or productive purposes. If one finds oneself in need on Shabbos or *Yom Tov* — wanting to eat from the dates, or wanting to *shecht* the animal — may one do so?

Rav prohibits this: if the item was not designated for Shabbos use, it is *muktzeh* and unavailable. Shmuel permits it: the category of *muktzeh* is narrow, and unless an item is fundamentally set aside in a way that makes it off-limits for any ordinary use, it remains available on Shabbos and *Yom Tov* even if it was not actively designated for that purpose.

### Rabbi Yehuda vs Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא תַּלְמִידָא דְּאוֹרִי בְּחַרְתָּא דְאַרְגֵּיז כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, שַׁמְתֵּיהּ רַב הַמְנוּנָא. וְהָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לַן? בְּאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַב הֲוָה לָא אִיבְּעַי לֵיהּ לְמִיעְבַּד הָכִי. הָנֵי תְּרֵי תַלְמִידֵי, חַד מַצִּיל בְּחַד מָנָא, וְחַד מַצִּיל בְּאַרְבַּע וַחֲמֵשׁ מָאנֵי — וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר זַבְדָּא וְרַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **this student who issued a ruling in** the city of **Ḥarta De'argiz** that items that are set aside are permitted, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** and **Rav Hamnuna excommunicated him.** The Gemara asks: **Don't we hold** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon?** Why, then, did Rav Hamnuna excommunicate him? The Gemara answers: **This** incident **was in the place of Rav** and the student **should not have done this;** even if the accepted ruling is lenient, the city was under Rav's jurisdiction, and the student's public ruling, contrary to Rav's opinion, was a blatant display of disrespect. Incidentally, the Gemara relates a story involving **these two students: One would rescue** from a fire **with one vessel and one would rescue with four and five vessels,** as it is permitted to rescue one's belongings from a fire on Shabbat. They disagreed with regard to whether it is preferable to carry just one vessel and go back and forth several times, or to carry several vessels and go back and forth fewer times. **And they disagree with regard to the** same issue that was the subject of the **dispute of Rabba bar Zavda and Rav Huna** elsewhere.

The *Gemara* identifies this as the classic dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon on the scope of *muktzeh*. Broadly stated, Rabbi Yehuda holds of *muktzeh* — items not designated for Shabbos use are set aside and unavailable — while Rabbi Shimon does not hold of *muktzeh*, meaning he recognizes only a very narrow set of cases in which *muktzeh* applies. The practical *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon, which is why Shmuel's more permissive ruling aligns with accepted practice. However, this is not to say that Rabbi Shimon recognizes no *muktzeh* at all; rather, he limits it to exceptional cases where items are truly and fundamentally inaccessible for Shabbos use.

A student who ruled publicly in the town of Chartah of Arguiz in accordance with Rabbi Shimon — permitting items that Rav, following Rabbi Yehuda, would have declared *muktzeh* — was placed into *niddui* by Rav Hamnuna. The *Gemara* expresses surprise: do we not rule like Rabbi Shimon? The answer is that Chartah of Arguiz was Rav's own locale, and one does not issue rulings contrary to the local *posek* within his own jurisdiction, even if in principle one would agree with the position being advanced.

The *Gemara* adds a final illustrative dispute: two students, when permitted to salvage items from a fire on Shabbos, differed in approach — one saved a large quantity in a single large vessel, while the other made four or five trips with smaller baskets. Their debate parallels another dispute among *Amora'im*, between Rabba bar Zavda and Rav Huna, and is brought here in parallel to the ongoing discussion about Rav and Shmuel's differing approaches.

Regarding *nolad* more broadly: the *Gemara* and Tosafos here suggest that most cases involving a change of form would not qualify as *nolad* at all. Tosafos understands *nolad* as applying only to a genuine transformation — such as food becoming juice — not to ordinary changes of state. This has significant practical implications: the question of whether ice or water formed on Shabbos constitutes *nolad* would, on this reading, generally not be a concern. Even in the oil-press case, the *nolad* problem is debated and ultimately resolved through the dispute between Rav and Shmuel. With this, the *Gemara* concludes its extended treatment of Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel's debate about processes that begin on *erev Shabbos* and continue through Shabbos.
