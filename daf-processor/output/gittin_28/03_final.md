# Gittin 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get Delivery & Chazaka

### Opening & Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט, וְהִנִּיחוֹ זָקֵן אוֹ חוֹלֶה – נוֹתֵן לָהּ בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהוּא קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of an agent **who brings a bill of divorce** to a woman, **and** when **he had left** the husband was **elderly or sick,** the agent **gives her** the bill of divorce **based on** the **presumption that** the husband **is** still **alive,** and there is no concern that in the meantime he has died, thereby canceling the bill of divorce.

Today's *daf* is dedicated to the learning in memory of Goldie, *Goldie bas Golda bas Yitzchak Yehuda*, on her third *yahrtzeit*. May it be in her memory and in her *zechus*.

We pick up with the *mishna* on 28a. The *mishna* opens: *hamavei get* — someone is delivering a *get*. The entire first *masechta* opens with the concept of *shelichus*, and that framework allows the Gemara to explore some genuinely interesting scenarios. We already dealt with what happens if the *get* is lost by the *shaliach*; now we confront a different problem.

### Chazaka & Status Quo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל הַנְּשׂוּאָה לְכֹהֵן, וְהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהוּא קַיָּים. הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ חַטָּאתוֹ מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם – מַקְרִיבִין אוֹתָהּ בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהוּא קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to **an Israelite woman who is married to a priest** and may therefore partake of *teruma*, **and her husband went to a country overseas, she may** continue to **partake of *teruma* based on** the **presumption that** her husband **is** still **alive.** Similarly, in the case of **one who sends his sin**-**offering from a country overseas,** the priests may **offer it** on the altar **based on** the **presumption that** the one who sent it **is** still **alive.**

The *mishna* states: *hamavei get vehenicho zaken o choleh* — if the *shaliach* left the husband and at that time he was old or he was sick, *notno lah bechezkas shehu chayim* — the *get* may be delivered on the presumption that he is still alive. The wife is treated as divorced, with all the relevant consequences. Presumably the most immediate concern is *yibum* — but it is worth noting that even if one later discovers that the husband died, there may be no way to precisely date events, and the matter may simply never be determined. The operative principle is *chazaka*: he was alive then, and we presume he remains alive now.

### Wife of Kohen Overseas

The *mishna* then introduces a second case: *bas Yisrael hanesua leKohen vehalach baalah limdinas hayam* — an Israelite woman married to a *Kohen* whose husband travels overseas. *Ocheles baterumah bechezkas shehu chayim* — she continues eating *terumah* on the presumption that he is alive. The question naturally arises: for how long? A hundred years? The Gemara will address this to some extent, but the baseline ruling is that she continues indefinitely without being required to stop.

### Chattat from Overseas

The *mishna* adds a third case: *hasholeiach chataso mimdinas hayam* — someone who sends a *chatas* from overseas. *Makrivim osah bechezkas shehu chayim* — the offering is brought on the presumption that he is alive. Were he not alive, it would be a *chatas shemeisu baaleha* and would have to go *limisa*; it could not be brought as a *korban*. And yet the *chazaka* permits it.

### Terumah as Chidush

Of all these cases, *terumah* stands out as the most remarkable *chidush*. One might say that *gittin* is enormously weighty — the prohibition of *eishes ish* is at stake — though it bears noting that either way the woman is permitted, whether as a *gerusha* or an *almana*. And *terumah* consumed by a *zar* carries a penalty of *chiyuv misa bidei shamayim*, which is severe. But the deeper reason *terumah* is the greatest *chidush* is that it is ongoing. In the other cases, the *chazaka* is invoked at one moment in time — a single determination that he was alive when the *get* was sent or when the *korban* was dispatched. The *terumah* case, by contrast, requires an indefinite, open-ended presumption: she eats *terumah* continuously for years, without any terminus, on the basis of nothing more than the status quo.

## Rava on Age & Illness

### Rava's Limitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא זָקֵן שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לִגְבוּרוֹת; וְחוֹלֶה – שֶׁרוֹב חוֹלִים לְחַיִּים; אֲבָל זָקֵן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לִגְבוּרוֹת, וְגוֹסֵס – שֶׁרוֹב גּוֹסְסִין לְמִיתָה; לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava says: They taught** that this presumptive status exists **only** concerning **an elderly man who has not reached** his years of **strength,** i.e., the age of eighty, **and** an ordinary **sick person, as** the **majority of sick** people continue **to live** and recover from their illnesses. **But** if the husband was **an elderly man who had reached** his years of **strength, or** if he was **moribund,** then, **as the majority of moribund** people proceed **to die,** he does **not** have this presumptive status.

The Gemara opens with a qualification from Rava: *lo shanu ela zaken* — the *mishna*'s ruling applies only to someone who is merely old in the ordinary sense, *shelo higia ligvurot*, meaning he has not yet reached the age of eighty. Similarly it applies to one who is merely sick, *sherov cholim lachayim* — because most sick people recover.

### Exceptions: Elderly & Goses

Rava continues: *aval zaken shehigia ligvurot* — if the man has already reached eighty years of age — *v'goses* — or if he is a *goses*, meaning he is actively in the process of dying — *sherov gosesim lemisa* — the majority of such people do die, and therefore one cannot invoke *chezkat chayim* on their behalf. In such cases the *chazaka* may be understood as *chazakah asuyah lehishtanot* — a status quo that is already in the process of changing.

### Braita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט וְהִנִּיחוֹ זָקֵן – אֲפִילּוּ בֶּן מֵאָה שָׁנָה, נוֹתֵן לָהּ בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהוּא קַיָּים! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rava's statement from a *baraita*: With regard to an agent **who brings a bill of divorce and left** the husband when he was **old, even one hundred years old, he gives** the bill of divorce **to** the wife, **based on** the **presumption that** her husband **is** still **alive.** The Gemara concludes: This is **a conclusive refutation,** and Rava's statement is rejected.

Abaye challenges Rava from a *braita*: *hamavei get vehenicho zaken afilu ben me'ah shanah* — even if the husband is a hundred years old, *notno lo bechezkas shehu kayam* — the *get* is delivered on the presumption that he is alive. This appears to directly contradict Rava's position that an elderly man who has reached *gevurot* cannot be presumed alive.

### Plateau Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיפְּלִיג – אִיפְּלִיג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And if you wish, say** that this is not a conclusive refutation. In the case of the *baraita*, **since** it is so **that** the man **reached an exceptionally** old age, one cannot apply the general presumptions to him and must instead apply presumptions that are for one who has **reached an exceptionally** old age. However, in a case where someone has not demonstrated that he is an exception to the rule, once he reaches the age of eighty there is a concern that perhaps he has died in the interim.

Rava responds: *keivan de'ifrak ifrak* — once someone has made it that far, he has demonstrated genuine longevity. The concern with most people after eighty is that the probability of death is increasing; but once a person reaches an extremely advanced age — Rashi suggests around ninety — the risk plateaus. He is no longer at escalating risk of death on any given day, and therefore we can once again presume he remains alive.

### Gosses Status

There is, however, an important asymmetry between the case of old age and the case of a *goses*. Old age, even at eighty, does not carry a distinct *halachic* status as such — Rava creates the category of *gevurot*, but it is not a formal *halachic* classification. A *goses*, by contrast, carries a clearly defined *halachic* status: there is an explicit *halacha* that one may not move or handle a *goses*, out of concern that the very act of touching him will hasten his death. Because *goses* is a recognized *halachic* category in its own right, the argument that a *goses* has no *chezkat chayim* stands on considerably firmer ground than the argument about an old man.

## Get vs. Terumah

### The Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַבָּה, תְּנַן: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט, וְהִנִּיחוֹ זָקֵן אוֹ חוֹלֶה – נוֹתֵן לָהּ בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהוּא קַיָּים. וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ״ – שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם לְמִיתָתוֹ, אֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה מִיָּד!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raises a contradiction before Rabba: We learned** in the mishna that in the case of an agent **who brings a bill of divorce** to a woman **and left** the husband when he was **elderly or sick,** the agent **gives her** the bill of divorce **based on** the **presumption that** the husband **is** still **alive. And** one can **raise a contradiction** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 6:11) that states: If one is leaving his place of residence, and in order to preclude a situation where his wife would have the status of a deserted wife he gives her a conditional bill of divorce and stipulates: **This is your bill of divorce** that will take effect **one hour prior to my death, it is immediately prohibited for her to partake of *teruma*,** due to the concern that he may die immediately after an hour has passed. Apparently, there is a concern that one may die at any point.

Abaye now presents Rava with a pointed internal contradiction. The *mishna* states that we deliver a *get* on the presumption that an old or sick husband is alive. Yet there is another ruling: if a husband says *harei zeh gitech sha'ah achas kodem lemisasi* — this is your *get*, to take effect one hour before I die — the wife is immediately prohibited from eating *terumah*. She cannot eat *terumah* because he might die in the next moment. This appears to be a direct conflict: in one case we are unconcerned about the husband's death, and in the other we are immediately concerned.

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּרוּמָה אַגִּיטִּין קָא רָמֵית?! תְּרוּמָה אֶפְשָׁר, גֵּט לָא אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him:** Do **you raise a contradiction** from the *halakhot* of ***teruma* to** the *halakhot* of **bills of divorce?** Concerning ***teruma*,** it is **possible** for the wife to eat only non-sacred produce in order to be stringent due to the possibility that her husband may die. However, concerning **a bill of divorce it is not possible** to take into account the possibility that her husband may die, as there would then be no way for a husband to send a bill of divorce to her with an agent.

Rava responds: *terumah agittin karamis?* — why are you challenging a *terumah* ruling from a *gittin* ruling? These are not comparable cases. *Terumah efshar efshar velo efshar* — when it comes to *terumah*, she has a choice; she can simply refrain from eating *terumah* if there is any uncertainty. But the *get* — this woman needs to get on with her life. Just because we invoke the *chazaka* for purposes of *gittin* does not mean we apply the same standard to *terumah*.

### Tosafot Reframes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי תְּרוּמָה אַתְּרוּמָה; תְּנַן: בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל הַנְּשׂוּאָה לְכֹהֵן, וְהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁהוּא קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the *halakhot* of ***teruma*** in the mishna **to** the *halakhot* of ***teruma*** in a *baraita*. **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to **an Israelite woman who is married to a priest** and may therefore partake of *teruma*, **and her husband went to a country overseas, she may** continue to **partake of *teruma* based on** the **presumption that** her husband **is** still **alive.**

Tosafot notes, however, that this response does not go far enough, because the very next line of the *mishna* itself applies the *chazaka* to *terumah*. It is therefore better to compare *terumah* against *terumah*. The *mishna* says the wife of a *Kohen* who has gone overseas eats *terumah* on the presumption he is alive. Yet the ruling about *harei zeh gitech sha'ah achas kodem lemisasi* says she immediately cannot eat *terumah*. That is a direct contradiction within *terumah* itself, and it requires an answer.

### Rav Ada's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ״ – שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם מִיתָתוֹ, אֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה מִיָּד!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: If one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce** that will take effect **one hour prior to my death, it is immediately prohibited for her to partake of *teruma*** due to the concern that he may die immediately after an hour has passed. These two *halakhot* appear to contradict one another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יִצְחָק: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, שֶׁהֲרֵי אֲסָרָהּ עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם מִיתָתוֹ. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: מִמַּאי דְּאִיהוּ מָיֵית בְּרֵישָׁא? דִּלְמָא אִיהִי מָיְיתָא בְּרֵישָׁא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda, son of Rav Yitzḥak, said:** It **is different there, as he rendered her forbidden to himself one hour prior to his death,** meaning that she will definitely be prohibited from partaking of *teruma* at some point. Therefore, it is considered to be an uncertainty immediately. **Rav Pappa objects to this** answer: **From where** is it known **that he will die first** and she will be prohibited from partaking of *teruma*? **Perhaps she will die first,** and the bill of divorce will never take effect.

Rav Ada bar Abba offers a response: *shani hasam sheharei asar aleha sha'ah achas kodem lemisaso* — the case of one hour before death is different, because in that case the husband has explicitly created a prohibition that will apply to her at some moment during his lifetime. Rashi acknowledges that this is more of a descriptive distinction than an explanatory one — the Gemara has identified that the two cases differ, but has not yet fully explained why the difference matters. As will become clear, the real distinction lies between a concern about whether he is already dead (*shema met*) versus a concern about whether he will die (*shema yamut*), but the Gemara at this stage has not yet articulated that distinction with full clarity.

### Rav Papa's Challenge

Rav Papa challenges: *midi de'ihu meiyas barishah dilma ihi meisa barishah* — how do you know he will die first? Perhaps she will die before him. The implicit reasoning behind the earlier analysis had been that since there will inevitably be a moment in this woman's life when she is prohibited from eating *terumah* — namely, that one hour before his death — we are always concerned that this might be that moment. Rav Papa's challenge dismantles this: if she dies before him, there will never be such a moment at all. The argument that *she must always be on guard because such a moment is coming* fails, because no such moment may ever arrive. This challenge lays the groundwork for the distinction the Gemara will draw between a concern about the present (*shema met*) and a concern about the future (*shema yamut*).

## Tannaitic Dispute

### Abaye's Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר – דְּלָא חָיֵישׁ לְמִיתָה, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that this is **not difficult: This** mishna, which permits the woman to partake of *teruma*, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who is not concerned about** potential **death. That** *baraita*, which prohibits the woman from partaking of *teruma*, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who is concerned about** potential **death.**

Abaye proposes a different resolution: the apparent contradiction may simply reflect a *tannaitic* dispute. *Ha Rabbi Meir delo chaish lemisa, ha Rabbi Yehuda dechaish lemisa* — the permissive ruling of the *mishna* follows Rabbi Meir, who is not concerned about the possibility of death, while the stringent position about *sha'ah achas kodem lemisasi* follows Rabbi Yehuda and others who are concerned about such a possibility. This is somewhat ironic because, as a rule, Rabbi Meir is known for being *chaish lemi'uta* — attentive to minority cases — and yet here he is the lenient position.

### Barrel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ יַיִן מִבֵּין הַכּוּתִים, אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין שֶׁאֲנִי עָתִיד לְהַפְרִישׁ – הֲרֵי הֵן תְּרוּמָה; עֲשָׂרָה – מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן; תִּשְׁעָה – מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי; וּמֵיחֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה מִיָּד – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a *baraita* in the *Tosefta* (*Demai* 8:7): In the case of **one who purchases wine from among the Samaritans,** about whom it is assumed that they did not separate *teruma* and tithes, and he is not in a position to separate *teruma*, he acts as follows: If there are, for example, one hundred *log* of wine in the barrels, **he says: Two *log* that I will separate in the future are *teruma*,** as the mandated average measure of *teruma* is one-fiftieth; **ten** *log* are **first tithe;** and one-tenth of the remainder, which is approximately **nine** *log*, are **second tithe. And he deconsecrates** the second tithe that he will separate in the future, transferring its sanctity to money, **and** he may **drink** the wine **immediately,** relying on the separation that he will perform later, which will clarify retroactively what he designated for the tithes and for *teruma*. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

This brings Abaye to cite the relevant *mishna*: someone who purchases wine from Samaritans just before *Shabbat*, when there is no time to properly separate *terumah* and *maaser*, may say: two *lugin* of this wine shall be designated as *terumah*, ten of the remaining hundred as *maaser rishon*, and nine of the remaining ninety as *maaser sheni*. He may then immediately proceed to drink from the wine. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. After *Shabbat*, he designates from the remaining wine what corresponds to the designated portions. Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yosi, and Rabbi Shimon all prohibit this arrangement.

### Rabbi Meir Unconcerned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹסְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: However, **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon prohibit** this practice. These Sages, Rabbi Yehuda among them, were concerned that perhaps the barrel would break before *Shabbat* ends — leaving no wine to be designated later, which would mean he had drunk *tevel* — whereas Rabbi Meir is not concerned about this occurring. Similarly, it is said that Rabbi Meir is not concerned about potential death, and Rabbi Yehuda is concerned.

The Gemara elsewhere explains the basis for this dispute. When asked whether he is not afraid that the barrel might break before *Shabbat* ends — leaving no wine to be designated later, which would mean he had drunk *tevel* — Rabbi Meir responds: when it breaks, I will worry about it. He is not concerned the barrel will break. This parallels a concern about death or some other catastrophic change in circumstances: is one concerned that some tragedy will occur and transform the current state of affairs?

### Parallel to Death

The Gemara understands the case of the barrel as a close parallel to the concern about death — whether conceptually or because both reduce to the same question: are we concerned that some accident or unforeseen tragedy will upset the current status? According to this reading, the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda about the barrel maps onto the question of whether we worry that the husband might die.

## Shema Met vs. Yamut

### The Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא מֵת לָא חָיְישִׁינַן, שֶׁמָּא יָמוּת חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There is a different distinction: **We are not concerned** that **perhaps he** has already **died,** which is why the mishna rules that she may continue to partake of *teruma*. However, in the case of one who stipulates that the bill of divorce will take effect one hour before his death, **we are concerned** that **perhaps he will die** at an unknown moment in the future and she will no longer be permitted to partake of *teruma*.

Rava, however, offers a sharper analysis that goes beyond attributing the matter to a *tannaitic* dispute. Rava says: *shema met lo chayshinan, shema yamut chayshinan* — whether he is already dead, that we do not worry about; whether he will die, that we do worry about. This is a fundamental and practically significant distinction.

### Chazaka & Future

A *chazaka* speaks to the present status: it asserts that the status quo we knew to obtain continues to obtain now. It does not address the future. If we knew he was alive and have heard nothing to the contrary, we presume he remains alive — the *chazaka* resolves the question of his present status. But the *chazaka* cannot resolve a question about what will happen going forward.

### Present vs. Future

In the case of the wife eating *terumah* while her husband is overseas: the question is whether he is alive right now. We knew he was alive when he left; we presume he is still alive. The *chazaka* answers this question directly. In the case of *harei zeh gitech sha'ah achas kodem lemisasi*: the husband is sitting right there, alive. The question is not whether he is alive now — that is not in dispute — but whether he will die within the next hour. That is a question about the future, and the *chazaka* that he is presently alive says nothing about it. Since the *chazaka* provides no answer to the future question, one must remain concerned, and she may not eat *terumah*. This is *shema yamut*, a future concern, and the *chazaka* does not address it.

### Rav Ada Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה לְרָבָא: וְהָא נוֹד דִּכְשֶׁמָּא יָמוּת הוּא, וּפְלִיגִי! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה מִדִּסְקַרְתָּא: שָׁאנֵי נוֹד דְּאֶפְשָׁר דְּמָסַר לֵיהּ לְשׁוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Mattana said to Rava: But** the concern in the case of the **wineskin** is that it might burst before one is able to separate the *teruma* and tithes, **which is similar** to the concern that **perhaps he will die,** as both are concerns with respect to the future, **and** Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir **disagree. Rav Yehuda of Diskarta said: A wineskin is different, as it is possible to give it to a watchman** who will protect it from bursting, which is why Rabbi Meir is not concerned that it might burst. This reasoning does not apply to the concern that the husband might die.

Rav Ada bar Matna challenges Rava's formulation: if *shema met* is universally not a concern and *shema yamut* is universally a concern, then why does the barrel case appear as a *tannaitic* dispute? If both matters are agreed upon by all, neither the leniency nor the stringency should be a point of disagreement. This challenge presses Rava to refine his position further.

---

## Rava's Resolution

### Barrel vs. Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: עָרְבָיךְ עָרְבָא צְרִיךְ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁמָּא מֵת – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן, שֶׁמָּא יָמוּת – תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya objects to this** answer: **Your guarantor** himself **needs a guarantor,** as there is no way of knowing that the guardian will not be negligent, so the wineskin may burst. **Rather, Rava said:** Everyone agrees that **we are not concerned** that **perhaps he** has already **died,** in accordance with the mishna here. As to the issue of whether there is a concern that **perhaps he will die,** as in the case of the *baraita*, this **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.**

Rava begins to respond by noting a factual distinction between barrels and people: not every barrel breaks, and one can be careful to prevent it from breaking. Every person, by contrast, will eventually die — death is inevitable. This asymmetry might explain why the barrel case is disputed while the inevitability of death is not.

### Guardian Needs Guardian

Rav Mesharshiya pushes back: *shomer tzarich shomer* — your guardian himself needs a guardian. You cannot rely on someone's carefulness to guarantee that the barrel will never break; even the most careful guardian cannot be fully trusted to prevent all accidents. This undercuts the proposed distinction between barrels and human mortality.

### All Agree: No Shema Met

Rava therefore clarifies: *shema met lo chayshinan* — the concern that someone is already dead, that we are not concerned about, according to everyone. The *mishna*'s ruling that we deliver the *get* and permit the eating of *terumah* is agreed upon by all *tannaim*. The *chazaka* that a person remains in the status we last knew him to be in applies universally.

### Shema Yamut Debate

*Shema yamut* — the concern that some accident or tragedy will occur in the near term and cause a change in status — that is a *tannaitic* dispute. Rabbi Meir is not concerned that such events will occur imminently; Rabbi Yehuda and the other *tannaim* are. This is the correct framing of where the disagreement lies.

---

## Tosafot on Time

### Rabbi Meir & Long Term

Tosafot refines this picture significantly. Even Rabbi Meir, who is not concerned that the barrel will break before *Shabbat* is over, would still be concerned if asked whether something might go wrong over a span of ten years. Rabbi Meir's leniency is not absolute: it applies to short-term concerns, but not to the possibility of death or catastrophe over a genuinely long period of time.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Concern

Conversely, Rabbi Yehuda, who is concerned that the barrel might break in the short term — before *Shabbat* ends — is not concerned about *shema met le'altar*, that the husband dropped dead the very instant he walked out the door.

### Absurd Consequence

Tosafot demonstrates why this must be so with a reductio ad absurdum: if a wife of a *Kohen* were required to worry that her husband died the moment he stepped out of the house, she could never eat *terumah* once he left her presence — not even when he went to the synagogue. She would have to be watching him at all times. This is plainly unworkable and cannot be the *halacha*.

### Time-Qualified Dispute

Tosafot therefore concludes that the real dispute is more precisely framed as follows: the disagreement is not between *shema met* in the absolute and *shema yamut* in the absolute, but between *shema met lezman merubah* — the concern that after a substantial period of time he may be dead — and *shema yamut lezman mu'at* — the concern that he will die imminently or in the near term. The former is universally not a concern, the latter is universally a concern, and the *tannaitic* dispute occupies the middle ground: whether we worry about accidents or death occurring over the short-to-medium term. These are critical distinctions in *halacha* that find application in a number of areas, including *hilchos niddah*.

---

## Chattat & Smicha

### The Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ חַטָּאתוֹ מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וְכוּ׳: וְהָא בָּעֵינָא סְמִיכָה! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּקׇרְבַּן נָשִׁים. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּחַטַּאת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in the case of **one who sends his sin-offering from a country overseas,** the priests may sacrifice it on the altar, based on the presumption that the one who sent it is still alive. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** an offering **require** the **placing** of **hands** on the head of an offering by the one who brings the offering? In this case the owner of the offering is elsewhere and cannot place his hands on the offering. **Rav Yosef says:** The mishna states this ruling **with regard to the offering of women,** as women are not required to place their hands on the head of their offerings. **Rav Pappa says:** The mishna states this ruling **with regard to a bird** sacrificed as **a sin-offering,** where it is not required that the owner place his hands on the head of the offering.

The Gemara now turns to a challenge against the *mishna*'s third case — the *chatas* sent from overseas. If we presume the sender is alive, should we not require *smicha* — the laying of hands on the *korban* — which demands the owner's physical presence? The fact that the *korban* is brought without *smicha* seems inconsistent with truly treating the owner as alive.

---

## Chattat & Smicha (continued)

### Rav Yosef's Answer

Rav Yosef answers: the case involves a *korban nashim* — an offering brought by a woman. Women are not obligated to perform *smicha* on their offerings, and therefore the absence of *smicha* raises no difficulty.

### Rav Pappa's Answer

Rav Pappa offers an alternative: the case involves a *chatas ha'of* — a bird sacrifice — and *smicha* is simply not performed on bird offerings at all.

### Three Cases Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גֵּט – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה דְּאֶפְשָׁר – אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Noting that the mishna states the same *halakha*, that the presumption is that one remains alive, in three different contexts, the Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** for the mishna to state this in all three cases, **as, if it** had **taught us** only the case of **a bill of divorce,** one might have said that it is **due to** the fact that it is **not possible** to take into account the possible death of the husband, as there would then be no way for the husband to send a bill of divorce to his wife with an agent. That is why the presumption that the husband is alive is relied upon. **But** with regard to ***teruma*, where** it is **possible** for the wife to partake of non-sacred produce, **say** the presumption is **not** relied upon, and there is a concern that he died.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן תְּרוּמָה – דְּזִמְנִין דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר; אֲבָל חַטַּאת הָעוֹף – מִסְּפֵיקָא לָא לֵיעוּל חוּלִּין לָעֲזָרָה, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it** had **taught us** also the case of ***teruma*,** one might have said **that** there are **times that** it is **not possible** for the wife to avoid partaking of *teruma*, e.g., if she does not have sufficient non-sacred produce. **But** with regard to **a bird** sacrificed as **a sin-offering,** one might say that **for** a situation of **uncertainty** as to whether the sin-offering may be sacrificed, since its owners might not be alive, **one should not bring non-sacred** animals, i.e., an animal that may not be sacrificed, **into the** Temple **courtyard.** Therefore, it is **necessary** for the mishna to mention all of these cases.

The Gemara then explains why all three cases in the *mishna* — *gittin*, *terumah*, and *chattat* — are necessary. Each one teaches something the others do not. The *get* case might have been explained by the urgency of the woman's situation — she has no alternative but to rely on the *chazaka*. If only *gittin* had been mentioned, one might have said that *terumah* is different because she could simply refrain from eating *terumah* and eat ordinary *chulin* instead. The *terumah* case is therefore needed to teach that even where an alternative technically exists, the *chazaka* permits her to eat. And the *chattat* case is needed to teach that even where there is no urgency — she can simply wait until the owner next comes to the *Beis HaMikdash* — we still permit the *korban* to be brought on the basis of *chezkat chayim*.

## Three Cases of Life

### Three Hazakas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים, וְקִיְּימוּ אֶת דְּבָרָיו: עַל עִיר שֶׁהִקִּיפָהּ כַּרְקוֹם, וְעַל הַסְּפִינָה הַמּוּטְרֶפֶת בַּיָּם, וְעַל הַיּוֹצֵא לִידּוֹן – שֶׁהֵן בְּחֶזְקַת קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Elazar ben Perata said three statements before the Sages** as testimony from previous generations, **and they upheld his statements:** He spoke **concerning** the residents of **a town that was surrounded** by **a camp of besiegers [*karkom*]; and concerning the** travelers in **a ship that is cast about in the sea; and concerning one who is going out to be judged** in a capital case; **that they are** all **presumed to be alive.**

The Gemara presents three cases introduced by Rabbi Elazar ben Parta, which the Sages affirmed. First, a city surrounded by a siege (*karkom*). Second, a ship caught in a storm at sea. Third, someone brought before a court on a capital charge. In all three of these cases, the person is presumed to still be alive — *bechezkat kayamin* — and one may proceed accordingly with respect to a *get*, *terumah*, and related matters.

### When Hazaka Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל עִיר שֶׁכְּבָשָׁהּ כַּרְקוֹם, וּסְפִינָה שֶׁאָבְדָה בַּיָּם, וְהַיּוֹצֵא לֵיהָרֵג – נוֹתְנִין עֲלֵיהֶן חוּמְרֵי חַיִּים וְחוּמְרֵי מֵתִים – בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן, וּבַת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, לֹא תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** concerning the residents of **a town that was conquered** by **a camp of besiegers; and** the travelers on **a ship that was lost at sea; and one who is going out to be executed** after receiving his verdict; in these cases **one applies to them the stringencies of** the **living and the stringencies of** the **dead.** How so? **An Israelite woman** married **to a priest** in one of these situations **or a daughter of a priest** married **to an Israelite** in one of these situations **may not partake of *teruma*.** The first woman may not do so because she may partake of *teruma* only while her husband is alive, and the second may not do so because she may partake of *teruma* only if he has died.

However, once the situation has been resolved in the worst direction — the city has actually been conquered, the ship has been lost at sea, the death sentence has been carried out — then we can no longer simply presume the person is alive. In these cases, we apply *chumrei chayim ve'chumrei meisim* — the stringencies of both life and death simultaneously — because there is genuine uncertainty as to whether the person is alive or dead.

### Consequences for Terumah

This dual stringency has concrete implications. Consider a *bas Yisrael* married to a *Kohen*: if her husband's fate is now uncertain — his city has been conquered, or he has been sentenced and the execution is imminent — she cannot eat *terumah*, because we must entertain the possibility that he is dead and she is no longer part of the *Kohen's* household. At the same time, she cannot remarry, because we must also entertain the possibility that he remains alive. Similarly, a *bas Kohen* married to an Israelite: if he might be dead, she might revert to her father's household and regain the right to eat *terumah*, but since he might be alive, she cannot eat *terumah* either. Either way, the uncertainty produces maximum stringency.

### Time Limits & Witnesses

As for time limits — whether there is any point at which the uncertainty is resolved — there is no simple rule. There is an entire chapter in Yevamos dealing with testimony about death, including the application of *eid echad* and *masiach lepi tumo* (innocent speech by a single witness). Those mechanisms allow for a lower evidentiary threshold in certain circumstances, enabling a determination that the husband has died. Until such testimony is available, however, the full dual stringency applies, and the most severe consequence is of course that she cannot remarry.

## Jewish vs. Non-Jewish Courts

### Rav Yosef's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל; אֲבָל בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, כֵּיוָן דִּגְמִיר לֵיהּ דִּינָא לִקְטָלָא – מִיקְטָל קָטְלִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef says: They taught** that one applies the stringencies of the living **only** to one who is being taken to be executed **in a Jewish court,** where even once he is being taken to be executed, he will be released if the court finds evidence for his innocence; **but in a court of the nations of the world, once he is sentenced to death, he is killed** in any case. Therefore, he should be considered as a dead man in every regard.

Rav Yosef qualifies the third case of Rabbi Elazar ben Parta — the person brought to trial on a capital charge. The uncertainty as to whether such a person has actually been executed applies only when the trial was conducted before a Jewish court (*Beis Din shel Yisrael*), which retains the theoretical possibility of retracting even after judgment has been passed. A non-Jewish court, by contrast, once it has passed judgment, will not retract.

### Abaye Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם נָמֵי, דִּמְקַבְּלִי שׁוּחְדָּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי שָׁקְלִי – מִקַּמֵּי דְּלַחְתּוֹם פּוּרְסִי שְׁנָמַג; לְבָתַר דְּמִיחְתַּם פּוּרְסִי שְׁנָמַג – לָא שָׁקְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** In **a court of the nations of the world as well,** perhaps they will not execute him, **as they accept bribes.** Rav Yosef **said to him: When they take** a bribe, it is only **before the verdict [*puresei shenmag*] has been sealed;** but **after the verdict has been sealed, they do not take** bribes.

Abaye challenges: even a non-Jewish court can be bribed. How can one be certain they will not reverse the judgment in exchange for payment?

### Sealed Judgment

Rav Yosef responds: they are willing to accept bribes only before the judgment has been formally sealed — before the *pursishnamag* (whether this refers to a particular official or the act of affixing a formal seal, Tosafot explores the possibilities). Once the final seal has been affixed, they will not retract even for a bribe, because to do so at that stage would be too obvious and would undermine their credibility entirely.

### Baraita on Courts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיַּעַמְדוּ שְׁנַיִם וְיֹאמְרוּ: ״מְעִידִין אָנוּ אֶת אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ בְּבֵית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי, וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי עֵדָיו״ – הֲרֵי זֶה יֵהָרֵג! דִּלְמָא בּוֹרֵחַ שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** According to Rav Yosef, in a Jewish court, even after one's verdict is complete the presumption is that he is alive. The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a mishna (*Makkot* 7a) concerning one who fled from the court after his verdict had been issued: **Any place where two** witnesses **arise and say: We testify about so-and-so that his judgment was finalized in the court of so-and-so, and so-and-so and so-and-so** were **his witnesses,** the *halakha* is that **this** person **should be killed.** It is evident from the mishna in tractate *Makkot* that there is no concern that the court may have later found a reason to release him. The Gemara answers: **Perhaps one who flees is different,** as the court will not reconsider his verdict once he has fled.

A *baraita* is cited: if two witnesses come forward in any location and testify that they were present in a particular city, in a particular court, and they witnessed the formal sentencing of a named individual — that testimony is accepted and the man is executed on its basis. This shows that we are not concerned that a Jewish court's judgment may have been subsequently overturned.

### Met vs. Neherag

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁמַע מִבֵּית דִּין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מֵת״; ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי נֶהֱרָג״ – יַשִּׂיאוּ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. מֵקוֹמֶנְטָרִיסִין שֶׁל גּוֹיִם ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מֵת״; ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי נֶהֱרַג״ – אַל יַשִּׂיאוּ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof: **Come** and **hear:** If one **heard from a Jewish court that they were saying:** The **man so-and-so died,** or: The **man so-and-so was killed,** then the court allows **his wife to marry.** If he heard **from a gentile judicial registrar [*komentirisin*]:** The **man so-and-so died,** or: The **man so-and-so was killed,** then the court does **not** allow **his wife to marry.**

The question now arises: when the *baraita* uses the words *met* (died) and *neherag* (was killed), does it mean that the man has literally already died or been executed, or does it mean that he is in the process of being led out to die?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מֵת״, וּמַאי ״נֶהֱרַג״? אִילֵימָא ״מֵת״ – מֵת מַמָּשׁ, וְ״נֶהֱרַג״ – נֶהֱרַג מַמָּשׁ; דִּכְווֹתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי גּוֹיִם, אַמַּאי אַל יַשִּׂיאוּ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ? הָא קַיְימָא לַן דְּכֹל מֵסִיחַ לְפִי תּוּמּוֹ הֵימוֹנֵי מְהֵימְנִי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What** does it mean when it says: **Died,** and **what** does it mean when it says: **Was killed? If we say** that: **Died,** means that he **actually died, and: Was killed,** means that he **was actually killed,** such **that** the case **concerning the gentiles** is stated in **a similar manner,** i.e., that he heard from the gentile registrar that the person was actually dead, **why** may the court **not** allow **his wife to marry? Don't we maintain that** with regard to **any** gentile who **speaks offhandedly,** the Sages **deemed him credible?** Therefore, the gentile should be deemed credible when he says that someone died or was killed.

If the *baraita* refers to a literal announcement of death or completed execution, a difficulty arises: we have an established principle that *kol masiach lefi tumo heimnuhu rabanan* — witnesses who speak in innocent, uncontrived speech are believed by the Rabbis for purposes of allowing a wife to remarry, and this applies even to non-Jewish witnesses. Why, then, would we not accept the announcement of a non-Jewish court that the man has been executed?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו ״מֵת״ – יוֹצֵא לָמוּת, ״נֶהֱרַג״ – יוֹצֵא לֵיהָרֵג? וְקָתָנֵי: בְּבֵית דִּין יִשְׂרָאֵל יַשִּׂיאוּ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** necessary to explain that when it says: **Died,** it means that he is **going out to die,** and when it says: **Was killed,** it means **going out to be executed. And** it **teaches** that if it occurred **in a Jewish court** then the court allows **his wife to marry,** as it is assumed that he was already executed, contrary to the statement of Rav Yosef.

The resolution suggested is that *met* does not mean the man has literally died, but rather *yotzei lamus* — he is being led out to be left to die. Similarly, *neherag* means *yotzei le'hareig* — he is being led out to be executed, not that the execution has already occurred. They are announcing their intention, not a completed fact. Under this reading, the *baraita* is consistent with Rav Yosef's original formulation: when a Jewish court announces that a person is being brought out for execution, we are confident the execution will be carried out; when a non-Jewish court makes the same announcement, we are not equally certain.

### Alternative View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם מֵת מַמָּשׁ וְנֶהֱרַג מַמָּשׁ, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: דִּכְווֹתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי גּוֹיִם אַמַּאי לָא, וְהָא קַיְימָא לַן דְּכֹל מֵסִיחַ לְפִי תּוּמּוֹ הֵימוֹנֵי מְהֵימְנִי; הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּמִילְּתָא דְּלָא שָׁיְיכִי בָּהּ, אֲבָל בְּמִילְּתָא דְּשָׁיְיכִי בַּהּ, עָבְדִי לְאַחְזוֹקֵי שִׁיקְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it can be explained that he **actually died, and actually was killed. And** with regard to **that which you said:** Such **that** the case **concerning the gentiles** is stated in **a similar manner, why** may the court **not** allow his wife to marry? **Don't we maintain that** with regard to **any** gentile who **speaks offhandedly,** the Sages **deemed him credible?** The answer is that **this** credibility **applies** only **in a matter that is not relevant to** the gentiles; **but in a matter that is relevant to** the gentiles, such as here, where they desire to publicize that they carried out their verdict, it is **common for them to reinforce their false** verdict, i.e., once they reach a verdict they will say that the accused was killed. Therefore, one cannot rely on their statements.

An alternative interpretation holds that *met* and *neherag* do mean literally dead and executed. Under this reading, the question becomes: why do we not accept the testimony of the non-Jewish court? The answer would have to be that the principle of *masiach lefi tumo* — believing witnesses who speak without a vested interest — does not apply here, because the non-Jewish court is not a disinterested party.

## Vested Interest

### Mesiach Lefi Tumo

The principle of *masiach lefi tumo* — believing those who speak innocently and without a stake in the matter — applies precisely because the speaker has no vested interest in the outcome. The reason such testimony is trusted even for so weighty a matter as allowing a woman to remarry is that someone speaking casually, without knowing the import of their words, has no reason to fabricate. Once a party has a vested interest, that reliability evaporates.

### Court's Vested Interest

The non-Jewish court is in exactly such a position. They announced that they would execute a particular individual. If they did not actually carry it out, they have failed to deliver on their formal judgment — a significant loss of institutional credibility. To avoid this embarrassment, they will claim they executed the person whether or not they actually did so. They are therefore not a disinterested party, and the principle of *masiach lefi tumo* cannot apply to their announcement.

### Jewish Court Finality

The Jewish court, by contrast, while technically capable of reopening a case even after *gmar din* — final sentencing — does so only in rare and exceptional circumstances in practice. Before *gmar din*, evidence and merit (*zchuta*) are actively considered; after the sentence is sealed, the case is effectively closed. It is this practical finality that allows us to rely on the testimony of witnesses who report that a Jewish court passed judgment, and that permits a wife to remarry on the basis of such testimony.
