# Bava Kamma 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tam vs. Muad

### Opening

We begin today's daf, 17b, by picking up with the *mishna* in the middle of the page. The first *perek* of *Bava Kamma* has, up to this point, been remarkably well organized: it opened with concrete cases from the *Torah*, abstracted them into general principles, identified the categories of *tamim* and *muadim*, and now turns to the *halachic* implications of that distinction. Compared to other *masechtot*, this one is extraordinarily well laid out, and that architectural clarity is worth appreciating before diving into the substance.

### Mishna on Tam/Muad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָה בֵּין תָּם לְמוּעָד? אֶלָּא שֶׁהַתָּם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק מִגּוּפוֹ, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **What** is the difference **between** the liability incurred for damage caused by an ox that is considered **innocuous and** the liability incurred for damage caused by an ox that is **forewarned?** The **only** differences are **that** for damage caused by **an innocuous** ox, the owner **pays half** the cost of the **damage** exclusively **from** proceeds of the sale **of the body** of the ox, **and** for **a forewarned** ox **he pays the full** cost of the **damage from** his **higher** property.

*Ma bein tam lemuad?* — What is the difference between a *tam* and a *muad*? The *mishna* answers: *ella shehatam mishalem chatzi nezek migupo* — a *tam* pays half damages, and that payment comes from the body of the goring animal itself. The cost cannot exceed the value of the damaging animal. The full significance of this rule — including the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon over whether the injured party automatically acquires a share in the animal's body — will be treated later in the tractate. *Umuad mishalem nezek shalem min ha-aliya* — a *muad*, by contrast, pays full damages from *aliya*, from "the best." The *Gemara* will immediately address what *aliya* means.

### Meaning of Aliya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״עֲלִיָּיה״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בִּמְעוּלֶּה שֶׁבִּנְכָסָיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** does the mishna mean by saying he pays from his **higher [*aliyya*]** property? **Rabbi Elazar says:** It means that he pays **with the superior-quality [*bame'ulla*]** items **of his property.**

*Mai aliya?* — What does *aliya* mean? *Amar Rabbi Elazar bimeula shebinichasav* — the best of one's property. This does not necessarily mean *meitav*, the finest-quality land, in the technical sense developed elsewhere in the tractate. Rather, the essential point is that payment is not limited to the body of the damaging animal; the owner must pay from his own assets. If he pays willingly, he may choose what to pay — whether land or *metaltelin*. If *beis din* must seize assets, the *Gemara* earlier suggested that *beis din* would make that determination, and there are relevant *mishnayot* in *Erchin* on those procedures. The running theme in the *Gemara* of *afilu suvin* — that one must pay even with bran, i.e., even the least valuable property — comes from a *braita* that the *Gemara* treats as authoritative, though one could ask whether an alternative reading of *meula min hanechasim* might yield a different approach.

### Chizkiyahu Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּשְׁכַּב חִזְקִיָּהוּ עִם אֲבֹתָיו, וַיִּקְבְּרֻהוּ בְּמַעֲלֵה קִבְרֵי בְנֵי דָוִיד״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״בְּמַעֲלֵה״ – אֵצֶל מְעוּלִּים שֶׁבַּמִּשְׁפָּחָה, וּמַאן נִינְהוּ – דָּוִד וּשְׁלֹמֹה.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** the fact that the word *aliyya* is referring to superior-quality property is indicated by the verse that **states: "And Hezekiah lay with his ancestors, and they buried him in the best [*bema'ale*] of the sepulchers of the descendants of David"** (II Chronicles 32:33), **and Rabbi Elazar says:** The term ***bema'ale*** means **beside the best of the family. And who are they? David and Solomon.**

To support his reading of *aliya*, Rabbi Elazar cites the verse: *vayishkav Chizkiyahu im avosav vayikberuhu bemaaleh kivrei bnei David* — Chizkiyahu lay down with his fathers and they buried him in the *maaleh* of the graves of the sons of David. *Veamar Rabbi Elazar bemaaleh etzel meulim shebamishpacha* — he was buried near the most prestigious members of the family, that is, near David and Solomon. The word *maaleh* thus denotes elevation and excellence, and that same sense of *meula* is what characterizes *aliya* in our *mishna*: payment comes not from the animal but from the owner's own assets, with no upper limit tied to the animal's value.

## Aggadic Digression

### Zanim & Spices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקְבְּרֻהוּ בְקִבְרֹתָיו אֲשֶׁר כָּרָה לוֹ בְּעִיר דָּוִד, וַיַּשְׁכִּיבֻהוּ בַּמִּשְׁכָּב אֲשֶׁר מִלֵּא בְּשָׂמִים וּזְנִים״ – מַאי ״בְּשָׂמִים וּזְנִים״? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: זְינֵי זְינֵי. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: בְּשָׂמִים שֶׁכׇּל הַמֵּרִיחַ בָּהֶן בָּא לִידֵי זִימָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers an interpretation of another verse about the burial of a king of Judea, King Asa: **"And they buried him in his own sepulchers, which he had hewn out for himself in the city of David, and laid him in the resting place, which was filled with perfumes and spices [*zenim*]** prepared by the perfumers' art" (II Chronicles 16:14). **What** is meant by **"perfumes and spices"? Rabbi Elazar says:** It means **many** different **types [*zinei*]** of perfumes. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** The word *zenim* sounds similar to the word *zenut*, licentiousness, and should therefore be understood about types of **perfumes that anyone who smells them is led to licentiousness.**

Since the *Gemara* cited a verse about Chizkiyahu's burial, it now transitions — by means of an adjacent verse — into an extended *aggadic* excursus that will carry us through to the end of the *perek*. The connecting verse describes the burial of Asa, king of Yehuda: *vayashkivuhu bemishkav asher maleh besamim uzeinim* — they laid him in his resting place which was filled with spices and *zeinim*. The *Gemara* asks: *mai besamim uzeinim* — what does this mean?

### Fragrances Dispute

Rabbi Eliezer interprets *zeinim* as *zinei zinei* — many different types of spices, all different kinds of fragrances. *Rashi* explains that *zanohei* is the Aramaic equivalent of *min*, a species or kind, so the verse means an abundance of varied fragrances. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani offers a strikingly different reading: *kebesamim shekol hamariach bahem ba lidei zimah* — these were erotic fragrances of such a nature that anyone who smelled them would be driven to an act of sexual impropriety. This leads into a question about what exactly Asa's attendants suspected him of.

### Zona & Pit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי כָרוּ שׁוּחָה לְלׇכְדֵנִי, וּפַחִים טָמְנוּ לְרַגְלָי״ – רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: שֶׁחֲשָׁדוּהוּ מִזּוֹנָה. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: שֶׁחֲשָׁדוּהוּ מֵאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another dispute between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani: Jeremiah requested of God to punish those who hounded him: **"For they have dug a pit to trap me and they have set snares for my feet"** (Jeremiah 18:22). In what way did they dig a pit to trap him? **Rabbi Elazar says: They cast suspicion upon him of** engaging in intercourse with **a woman who had engaged in sexual intercourse with a man forbidden to her by the Torah [*zona*]. Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says: They cast suspicion upon him of** engaging in intercourse with **a married woman.**

Rabbi Elazar says: *shechashdu mizona* — they suspected him of consorting with a prostitute. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says: *shechashdu meishet ish* — they suspected him of adultery. The word *zeinim* triggers an associative connection to *zenut* and *zona*, and the *Gemara* then draws in a verse from Yirmiyahu: *ki shucha lachadeni* — for they have dug a pit to trap me — *upachim tamnu leragli* — and traps were laid for my feet.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁחֲשָׁדוּהוּ מִזּוֹנָה – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי שׁוּחָה עֲמֻקָּה זוֹנָה״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁחֲשָׁדוּהוּ מֵאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, מַאי ״שׁוּחָה״? אַטּוּ אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ מִי נָפְקָא מִכְּלַל זוֹנָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says that they cast suspicion upon him of** engaging in intercourse with **a *zona*, this is** because the word "pit" is used as a metaphor for a *zona*, **as it is written: "For a *zona* is a deep pit"** (Proverbs 23:27). **But according to the one who says that they cast suspicion upon him** of engaging in intercourse with **a married woman,** for **what** reason did Jeremiah use the word **"pit"?** The Gemara answers: **Due to** the fact that she is **a married woman, should she be excluded from the category of a *zona*?** If she commits adultery she is also termed a *zona*, and the term "pit" can justifiably be applied to her.

*Bishlama* according to the opinion that they suspected him of a prostitute, the verse works well: *ki shucha amuka zona* — a prostitute is like a deep pit, a trap that ensnares. But according to the opinion that they suspected him of adultery, *mai shucha* — what is the pit? The *Gemara* resolves this: *atu eishet ish minafka miklal zona* — does a married woman fall outside the category of *zona* in this metaphorical sense? The image of a pit captures any forbidden sexual entanglement, and so the verse applies to adultery as well. Tosafot further notes that Yirmiyahu was a *Kohen*, and thus the suspicion of consorting with a *zona* carried an additional dimension: a *zona* is forbidden to a *Kohen* in marriage — though whether the prohibition extends beyond marriage was itself a question discussed at the end of *Kiddushin*. By contrast, *eishet ish* is the more severe sin of adultery, which is why Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani preferred that reading.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁחֲשָׁדוּהוּ מֵאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַתָּה ה׳ יָדַעְתָּ אֶת כׇּל עֲצָתָם עָלַי לַמָּוֶת״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁחֲשָׁדוּהוּ מִזּוֹנָה, מַאי ״לַמָּוֶת״? שֶׁהִשְׁלִיכוּהוּ לִבְאֵר טִיט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says that they cast suspicion upon him of** engaging in intercourse with **a married woman, this is** consistent with **that which is written: "You, Lord, know all their plans to kill me"** (Jeremiah 18:23). By claiming he engaged in intercourse with a married woman, they implicated him in a prohibition punishable by the death penalty. **But according to the one who says that they suspected him of** engaging in intercourse with **a *zona*, what** did he mean by saying **"to kill** me" when there is no court-imposed capital punishment for doing so? The Gemara answers: Jeremiah was referring to the fact **that they cast him into a pit of mire.**

The *Gemara* continues: *bishlama* according to the opinion that they suspected him of adultery, the verse *ve'ata Hashem yadata et kol atzatam alai lamut* — You, God, know all their plots against me to kill me — fits naturally, since adultery is a capital offense. But according to the opinion that they suspected him of a prostitute, *mai lamut* — what does "to kill me" mean? There is no capital punishment for that. The answer is *dehishlichuhu lebor hatit* — they literally threw him into a pit filled with mud, as recounted explicitly in the *pesukim*. So there are also literal pits in the world that people construct against one another.

### Stumbling Blocks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״יִהְיוּ מֻכְשָׁלִים לְפָנֶיךָ, בְּעֵת אַפְּךָ עֲשֵׂה בָּהֶם״? אָמַר יִרְמְיָה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹשִׂין צְדָקָה – הַכְשִׁילֵם בִּבְנֵי אָדָם שֶׁאֵינָן מְהוּגָּנִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְקַבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶן שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava interpreted** a verse **homiletically: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Let them be made to stumble before You; deal with them in the time of Your anger"** (Jeremiah 18:23)? It means that **Jeremiah said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, even at a time when they** try to **perform** acts of **charity, make them stumble** by ensuring that they do so **with people who are unworthy** of charity **in order that they should not receive** the **reward for** helping **them.**

*Darash Rava* — Rava expounded: *maid dechtiv veyehyu muchshalim lefanecha* — what is the meaning of the verse, "Let them stumble before You; at the time of Your anger, act against them"? Yirmiyahu said before the Holy One, blessed be He: Master of the Universe, *afilu besha'at she'osim tzedakah hachshilem bivnei adam she'einam mehuganim* — even when they are performing *tzedakah*, cause them to stumble by directing their gifts toward people who are not of proper character, *kedai shelo yekablu aleihem sachar* — so that they receive no reward for it, since those recipients will use the money for improper purposes.

This raises an interesting *halachic* question: the *halachot* about vetting recipients of *tzedakah* are largely designed to protect the giver from an obligation that may not be genuine. But if one chooses not to vet and the money is misused, does that mean the *mitzvah* was not fulfilled? Presumably one did fulfill the *mitzvah* — at least when the scam was not obviously apparent. The *Gemara* here suggests, at minimum, that the quantum of *sachar* one receives when a recipient uses the money for drugs is less than when it is used for food. This is left as an open *halachic* question. On *Purim*, the practice of *matnos aniyim* is deliberately structured so that one does not vet — that is its characteristic quality.

The aggadic role this passage plays in the broader context of the *perek* is also worth noting. Just as one can dig a pit that is not a literal excavation — through slander, allegations, and plots, as in the case of Yirmiyahu — one can also create *michsholim*, stumbling blocks, through the people one encounters or enables. A *michshol* is not only a physical obstacle in the street; it can also be the moral environment one creates around others.

### Sefer Torah Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכָבוֹד עָשׂוּ לוֹ בְמוֹתוֹ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהוֹשִׁיבוּ יְשִׁיבָה עַל קִבְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara, above, cited a verse concerning King Hezekiah's burial. The Gemara cites the continuation of that verse: **"And they afforded him honor in his death"** (II Chronicles 32:33). This **teaches that they established a yeshiva at his grave** to study Torah there.

*Ukvodo asu lo bemoso* — and they did honor for him in his death. This verse refers to Chizkiyahu. *Milamed shehoshivu yeshiva al kivro* — this teaches that a *yeshiva* studied Torah at his grave. Tosafot notes that one of Chizkiyahu's central achievements — attested in other *Gemarot* — was spreading Torah throughout Israel to an extraordinarily high level of knowledge. The practice of Torah study near the graves of great rabbis continues to this day.

### Grave Learning Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד אָמַר שִׁבְעָה. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: שְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the other **one said:** They studied there for **seven** days. **And some say** they studied there for **thirty** days.

*Pligi Rabbi Natan verabanan* — Rabbi Natan and the Sages dispute the duration of this *yeshiva* at the grave: one opinion says three days, another says seven, and a third says thirty. These correspond to stages of mourning: *shiva* and *shloshim* are familiar, and the tractate *Moed Katan* establishes that even three days constitute a primary unit within *shiva* for certain *halachot*.

### Chizkiyahu Procession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְכָבוֹד עָשׂוּ לוֹ בְמוֹתוֹ״ – זֶה חִזְקִיָּה מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה, שֶׁיָּצְאוּ לְפָנָיו שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה אֶלֶף חֲלוּצֵי כָתֵף, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita* that offers another interpretation of the verse cited: **"And afforded him [*lo*] honor in his death"** (II Chronicles 32:33). **This** is referring to the honor given to **Hezekiah, king of Judea, that** at his burial **36,000** men with **bared shoulders went out before him.** They removed their robes from their shoulders as a sign of mourning. The number 36,000 is alluded to by the numerical value of the word *lo*, which is thirty-six. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

*Tano rabanan* — the Sages taught: *zeh Chizkiyahu melech Yehuda sheyatzu lefanav shloshim veshisha elef chalutzei katef* — this was Chizkiyahu king of Yehuda, before whom went 36,000 people with their shoulders uncovered. Rashi understands this as a reference to *kriya*, such that the garment was torn and the shoulder exposed. Tosafot reads the number 36,000 as encoded in the words *asu lo* — *lamed vav* equals 36, multiplied by a thousand for rhetorical weight.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: וַהֲלֹא לִפְנֵי אַחְאָב עָשׂוּ כֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥemya said to him: But didn't they** also **do this before Ahab?** Apparently, if they did this for the wicked king Ahab, it is an honor shown to all kings, and it is was not a unique show of honor for the righteous Hezekiah.

This is the view of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Nechemiah objects: *velo le'achiv asu kein?* — did they not do the same for his brothers? A king always receives an enormous funeral procession, and Tosafot references another *Gemara* that reads the verse *kemisped Hadad-rimmon ben Tabrimmon* as an allusion to Achav's enormous funeral. So what was distinctive about Chizkiyahu's honor?

### Sefer Torah Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שֶׁהִנִּיחוּ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה עַל מִטָּתוֹ, וְאָמְרוּ: קִיֵּים זֶה מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the honor that was done for Hezekiah was **that they laid a Torah scroll upon his bier and they said: This one,** i.e., Hezekiah, **fulfilled that which is written in this,** i.e., the Torah scroll.

*Ella* — the distinctive honor was that they placed a *sefer Torah* on his funeral bier and declared in the procession: "This one fulfilled what was written in that." He was being honored as an embodiment of the *sefer Torah* itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִידָּנָא נָמֵי עָבְדִינַן הָכִי! אַפּוֹקֵי מַפְּקִינַן, אַנּוֹחֵי לָא מַנְּחִינַן. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אַנּוֹחֵי נָמֵי מַנְּחִינַן, ״קִיֵּים״ לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But nowadays as well, we do this** for any great Torah scholar that dies, so what is unique about what was done to honor Hezekiah? The Gemara answers: Nowadays, **we take** a Torah scroll **out** but **we do not lay** it on the bier of the deceased. **And if you wish, say** instead that nowadays **we also lay** a Torah scroll on the bier of the deceased; but **we do not say:** This one **fulfilled** that which is written in this.

The *Gemara* asks: *ha'idna nami avdinan hachi* — do we not do this today for great rabbis? What is uniquely remarkable about Chizkiyahu? The *Gemara* answers: *apukei mapkinan anuchet lo manchinan* — perhaps today we carry a *sefer Torah* in the procession, but we do not actually place it on the bier. And if you prefer, perhaps we do place it on the bier — *kiyam lo amrinan* — but we do not make the explicit verbal declaration. What was distinctive about Chizkiyahu was that they articulated it: they said outright that this man fulfilled everything written in it. That explicit declaration of fulfilled embodiment was the singular honor.

### R. Yochanan Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָא: הֲוָה אָזֵילְנָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְמִשְׁאַל שְׁמַעְתָּא, כִּי הֲוָה עָיֵיל לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא וַהֲוָה בָּעֵינָא מִינֵּיהּ מִלְּתָא, לָא פָּשֵׁיט לַן עַד דְּמָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ וּמַנַּח תְּפִילִּין וּמְבָרֵךְ, וַהֲדַר אֲמַר לַן: אֲפִילּוּ ״קִיֵּים״ אָמְרִינַן; ״לִימֵּד״ לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: I was** once **walking together with Rabbi Yoḥanan to ask** him about this **statement. Whenever he would enter a lavatory,** upon his exit **I would ask him** to explain **a matter,** and **he would not answer us until he had washed his hands and donned his phylacteries and made** the **blessing, and** only **then would he answer us.** With regard to the honor given to King Hezekiah, he said: Nowadays, **we even say:** This one **fulfilled** that which is written in this, but **we do not say: He taught** that which is written in this, which was a unique honor performed at the burial of the righteous King Hezekiah.

*Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana*: I was once following Rabbi Yochanan in order to ask him *halachic* questions. When Rabbi Yochanan would emerge from the bathroom, he would not give us a *psak* until he had first washed his hands, put on *tefillin*, and recited the *beracha* — and only then would he answer. The *Gemara* notes the interesting detail that the *beracha* is mentioned after the putting on of the *tefillin*, though that question is left aside. After performing all of this, Rabbi Yochanan then taught them Torah.

### Learning vs. Doing

At first glance this story seems parenthetical — a colorful anecdote whose real purpose is to convey what Rabbi Yochanan told them regarding Chizkiyahu. One reading, however, understands the story as substantive in itself, and we will return to that shortly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר מָר: גָּדוֹל תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה, שֶׁהַתַּלְמוּד מֵבִיא לִידֵי מַעֲשֶׂה! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא לְמִיגְמַר, הָא לְאַגְמוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't the Master say: Torah study is great because** the **study** of Torah **leads to** one's **performance** of the mitzvot? This indicates that the performance of mitzvot is considered of greater value than Torah study. If so, once Hezekiah had been praised with the fact that he fulfilled the mitzvot of the Torah, why mention that he studied it? The Gemara explains: This is **not difficult: This** statement of the Master is **about studying** the Torah for one's own knowledge, and **that** unique praise given to King Hezekiah was **about teaching** the Torah to others.

What Rabbi Yochanan told them was: *afilu kiyam amrinan* — even today we would be prepared to say that someone fulfilled the *sefer Torah*. So what was uniquely honorable about Chizkiyahu? *Limadei lo amrinan* — that he taught others Torah, that is something we do not say today. The distinctive achievement being commemorated was not merely that Chizkiyahu embodied the *sefer Torah* himself, but that he spread Torah to others, making him a teacher of the entire nation.

The *Gemara* then introduces: *ve'amar mar gadol limud Torah shelimud meivi lidei ma'aseh* — great is learning, for learning brings about action. Now the *Gemara* does not initially distinguish between learning and teaching. The question it raises is: why is saying that someone taught Torah a higher honor than saying he fulfilled it? Based on the principle that learning brings about action, one might conclude that doing is the greatest achievement. If so, saying he did is better than saying he learned or taught. Why, then, is it treated as a greater honor to say he taught?

Rashi reads the *Gemara* accordingly: the hierarchy of religious value, from lowest to highest, is learning, then doing, then teaching. Learning is celebrated because it leads to action, but that means action itself is the higher value. Teaching is highest of all because it spreads action to others. This raises a profound question that lies at the heart of how we define religious achievement: is the doctor who spends his life saving people and performing practical *mitzvot* of greater religious worth than the *talmid chacham* who sits and learns? Rashi would seem to say yes — doing ultimately surpasses learning.

### Teaching Hierarchy

Tosafot disputes Rashi's reading of the *Gemara* in *Kiddushin*. For Tosafot, the conclusion of that *sugya* is not that doing is greater, but that learning is greater — precisely because it brings about action. Learning subsumes doing; it is the superior category. On this reading, the question here is different: if learning is already the greatest value, why is it a higher honor to say that Chizkiyahu taught rather than that he learned? Tosafot answers: because saying that someone did *mitzvot* already implies that he learned — *ein am ha'aretz chasid*, one who has not learned cannot truly fulfill a life of doing. So if we were praising his doing, we would already be implying his learning. What Chizkiyahu was honored for was not just learning or doing but *teaching* — spreading Torah to others. In the end, both Rashi and Tosafot agree on the conclusion: teaching stands at the top of the hierarchy. But their disagreement about the relationship between learning and doing is a genuinely important question about how to read the *Gemara* in *Kiddushin* and about which achievement our tradition ultimately prizes more.

The *Sheiltos* offers an entirely different reading of the passage about Rabbah bar bar Chana. On his reading, the story of Rabbi Yochanan coming out of the bathroom is not parenthetical at all. The point is that Rabbi Yochanan first washed his hands, then put on *tefillin*, then taught Torah — and the *Gemara* asks why he did it in that order. If *limud mevi lidei ma'aseh* — learning brings about action — then should he not have taught Torah before performing the action of putting on *tefillin*? The *Gemara* answers: *lo kashya, ha lemigmar ha le'agmorei* — there is no contradiction; one distinction applies to learning for oneself and another to teaching others. If it is between learning and doing, you learn first. If it is between teaching and doing, you do first — because by doing first you are modeling the hierarchy. You learn first, then act, then teach, thereby demonstrating through your own conduct the correct sequence of religious life. This reading transforms the entire passage: it is not about what was said at Chizkiyahu's funeral but about the order in which Rabbi Yochanan structured his own actions.

### Torah & Chasadim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אַשְׁרֵיכֶם זֹרְעֵי עַל כׇּל מָיִם, מְשַׁלְּחֵי רֶגֶל הַשּׁוֹר וְהַחֲמוֹר״? כׇּל הָעוֹסֵק בְּתוֹרָה וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, זוֹכֶה לְנַחֲלַת שְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** § A verse that was cited at the beginning of this chapter (2b) as part of a halakhic exposition is now explained homiletically: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Happy are you that sow beside all waters, that send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey"** (Isaiah 32:20)? It teaches that **whoever engages in** the study of **Torah and in** the performance of **acts of kindness merits** reward equal to **the portion of two tribes,** Joseph and Issachar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַשְׁרֵיכֶם זֹרְעֵי״ – וְאֵין ״זְרִיעָה״ אֶלָּא צְדָקָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זִרְעוּ לָכֶם לִצְדָקָה וְקִצְרוּ לְפִי חֶסֶד״; וְאֵין ״מַיִם״ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how this is derived from the verse: **As it is stated: "Happy are you that sow." And** the reference to **sowing** refers **only** to acts of **charity, as it is stated: "Sow for yourselves for charity, reap according to kindness"** (Hosea 10:12). **And** the reference to **water** refers **only** to the study of **Torah, as it is stated** with regard to Torah study: **"Ho, all who are thirsty, go to water"** (Isaiah 55:1).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹכֶה לְנַחֲלַת שְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים – זוֹכֶה לְכִילָה כְּיוֹסֵף, דִּכְתִיב: ״בֵּן פֹּרָת יוֹסֵף, בָּנוֹת צָעֲדָה עֲלֵי שׁוּר״; וְזוֹכֶה לְנַחֲלַת יִשָּׂשכָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״יִשָּׂשכָר חֲמֹר גָּרֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the fact that **he merits** reward equal to **the portion of two tribes** is derived as follows: The reference to the ox in the verse is an allusion to Joseph, who is described as an ox (Deuteronomy 33:17), and one's reward is that **he merits to** receive **a canopy** of honor, **as** did **Joseph, as it is written: "Joseph is a fruitful vine,** a fruitful vine by a fountain; **its branches run over the wall."** The branches over the wall in this verse allude to the canopy. **And** the reference to the donkey is an allusion to the fact that **he merits** to receive **the portion of Issachar,** who is described as a donkey, **as it is stated: "Issachar is a large-boned donkey"** (Genesis 49:14).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִית דְּאָמְרִי: אוֹיְבָיו נוֹפְלִים לְפָנָיו כְּיוֹסֵף – דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּהֶם עַמִּים יְנַגַּח יַחְדָּו אַפְסֵי אָרֶץ״; וְזוֹכֶה לְבִינָה כְּיִשָּׂשכָר – דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמִבְּנֵי יִשָּׂשכָר יוֹדְעֵי בִינָה לַעִתִּים, לָדַעַת מַה יַּעֲשֶׂה יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** some **who say** that the comparison should be understood in a different manner. **His enemies will fall before him** just **like** in the blessing given by Moses to the tribe of **Joseph, as it is written** in the blessing bestowed by Moses to the tribe of Joseph: "His horns are the horns of the wild-ox; **with them he shall gore the nations, even to the ends of the earth"** (Deuteronomy 33:17). **And he merits the understanding of Issachar, as it is written: "And of the children of Issachar, men that had understanding of the times, to know what Israel ought to do"** (I Chronicles 12:33).

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*: What is the meaning of the verse *ashreichem zor'ei al kol mayim meshalechei regel hashor vehachamor* — praised are you who scatter seeds on all waters, who send forth the foot of the ox and the donkey? *Kol ha'osek baTorah uvigmilus chasadim* — anyone who engages in Torah and *gmilus chasadim*, *zocheh lenachalas shnei shevatim* — merits the inheritance of two tribes. The verse's imagery is decoded: *zeri'ah* means *tzedakah*, as in the verse *zir'u lachem letzedakah vekitzru lefi chesed* — sow for yourselves in righteousness and reap according to kindness. And *mayim* means Torah, as in *hoi kol tzameh lechu lamayim* — all who are thirsty, come to the waters.

One who plants seeds — acts of *tzedakah* — and draws on living water — Torah — *zocheh lechilah keYosef* — merits a bridal canopy like Joseph, derived from *ben porat Yosef banot tza'adah alei shur*, where young women ascended upon the wall to gaze at him. *Vezocher lenachalas Yissachar* — and merits the inheritance of Issachar, *Yissachar chamor garem* — Issachar is a bony donkey, understood as one who bears the weight of Torah. Another reading: *oyevav noflim lefanav keYosef* — his enemies fall before him like Joseph's, from *bechor shoro hadar lo vekarnei re'em karnav bahem amim yenagach yachdav afsei aretz* — the honor of the firstborn ox is his, with the horns of a wild ox he gores nations to the ends of the earth. And *vezocher levinah keYissachar* — and he merits wisdom like Issachar, *umivnei Yissachar yodei vinah le'itim lada'at mah ya'aseh Yisrael* — for the sons of Issachar knew how to read the times and knew what Israel should do.

The connection back to the opening of the *perek* is worth drawing out: *meshalechei regel hashor vehachamor* — sending forth the foot of the ox and the donkey — is the *pasuk* of *regel*, of *nezikin*. But as this *aggadic* passage suggests, sending forth one's foot can be an act of destruction or an act of building. Negligence allows one's animals to go out and damage; intentional devotion to Torah and *gmilus chasadim* sends forth forces that benefit and uplift. *Hadran alach arba'ah avos* — we conclude the first *perek*.

## Perek Two: Regel

### Perek Structure

The second *perek* is devoted specifically to *shein* and *regel*. If the first *perek* offered a presentation of primary principles — and it is hard to find another *masechet* that opens with such clarity — the second *perek* moves to *pratim*, to the specific cases. The structure is consistent throughout: each category is introduced with a *keitzad*, a "how so?", followed by the general rule, and then a set of exceptions that clarify the boundaries of liability.

### Regel Muad Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד הָרֶגֶל מוּעֶדֶת? לִשְׁבֹּר בְּדֶרֶךְ הִלּוּכָהּ. הַבְּהֵמָה מוּעֶדֶת לְהַלֵּךְ כְּדַרְכָּהּ וּלְשַׁבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna in the previous chapter (15b) teaches that the owner of an animal is always forewarned with regard to the category of Trampling. The mishna elaborates: For **what** damage caused with **the hoof** is the animal deemed **forewarned?** It is deemed forewarned with regard **to** trampling objects and **breaking** them **in the course of its walking. An animal is** deemed **forewarned with regard to walking in its** typical **manner and,** by doing so, **breaking** objects as it proceeds.

*Keitzad haregel mu'edet leshabber bederech hilucha?* — How is the *regel* *mu'edet*? In what way is *regel* considered *muad*, and in what situations does it pay full damages? The answer: when the damage is caused in the normal course of the animal's walking, that constitutes the *muad* of *regel*, and full damages are owed. The second line of the *mishna* restates this: *ha-behema mu'edet lalechet k'darkah u'l'shaber* — the *behema* is *muad* to go normally and to break. The *Gemara* will later address the apparent redundancy of these two formulations.

### Regel Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה מְבַעֶטֶת, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ צְרוֹרוֹת מְנַתְּזִין מִתַּחַת רַגְלֶיהָ – וְשָׁבְרָה אֶת הַכֵּלִים, מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק. דָּרְסָה עַל הַכְּלִי וּשְׁבָרַתּוּ, וְנָפַל עַל כְּלִי וּשְׁבָרוֹ – עַל הָרִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְעַל הָאַחֲרוֹן מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if the animal **was kicking** while it was walking, **or** it transpired **that pebbles were** inadvertently **propelled from under its feet and** those pebbles **broke vessels,** cases of that kind do not fit precisely into the primary category of Trampling. In both of these cases the owner of the animal **pays half** the cost of **the damage.** If an animal **trod upon a vessel and broke it and** then a shard of that vessel **fell upon** a second **vessel and broke it,** the owner **pays** the **full** cost of the **damage for the first** vessel, as its action is classified under the primary category of Trampling, **and he pays half** the cost of **the damage for the latter** vessel, as the damage caused by the shard is tantamount to damage caused by pebbles inadvertently propelled by the foot of an animal.

The *mishna* then offers examples of departures from the basic rule. *Hayetah meva'etet* — if the animal was kicking, that is not *regel* but *keren*, and *chatzi nezek* is paid. *O she'hayu tzerorot menatzin mitachat ragleiha* — or if pebbles were scattering from under its legs, that too pays half damages — but for a different reason: *tzrorot* is the indirect force of *regel*, a *kocho* rather than a *gufo*, and is therefore reduced to half. *Darsah al hakli veshibarto*, *venafal hakli veshibaro* — if it trampled on a vessel and broke it, and the broken vessel then struck another vessel and broke that too, the first vessel pays full (classic *regel* in the *reshut hanizak*), and the second pays half, since the second damage was caused through *tzrorot*, indirect force.

### Behema & Chickens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַתַּרְנְגוֹלִין מוּעָדִין לְהַלֵּךְ כְּדַרְכָּן וּלְשַׁבֵּר. הָיָה דְּלִיל קָשׁוּר בְּרַגְלָיו, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה מְהַדֵּס וּמְשַׁבֵּר אֶת הַכֵּלִים – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **Chickens are** deemed **forewarned** with regard **to walking in their** typical **manner and breaking** objects, and therefore, the owner of a chicken pays the full restitution for the damage done to any objects broken by his chicken. If there **was a string [*delil*] tied to** a chicken's **leg** as an indication of ownership and it wrapped around a vessel and broke it, **or** if the chicken **was hopping** in an atypical manner **and breaking vessels,** its owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage.**

*Hatarnagolim mu'adin lalechet k'darkan u'l'shaber* — chickens are *muad* to walk normally and break. The *mishna* adds an exception: *haya d'li kashur beraglav* — if a bucket was tied to its leg and was bouncing around and breaking vessels, that pays half damages, as another case of *tzrorot*. Strikingly, the language used for chickens is essentially identical to the language used for the general *regel* and the *behema*. The apparent redundancy among all three formulations will be the subject of the *Gemara's* opening question on this *mishna*.

## Av & Toladah Debate

### Redundancy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: הַיְינוּ רֶגֶל הַיְינוּ בְּהֵמָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנָא אָבוֹת, וְקָתָנֵי תּוֹלָדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara analyzes the mishna: **Ravina said to Rava:** In the context of the category of Trampling, damage caused by an animal trampling an object with its **foot is the same as** damage caused by **an animal** breaking an object with its body. Why does the mishna repeat the same *halakha* twice? Rava **said to him:** First, the *tanna* **teaches** the **primary categories** of damage, namely the category of Trampling with an animal's foot, which is mentioned explicitly in the Torah, **and** then **he teaches** the **subcategories** of those primary categories, i.e., the animal is deemed forewarned with regard to causing damage with other parts of its body in the course of its walking.

*Amar lei Ravina l'Rava*: *Hainu regel hainu behema* — what did the second line add to the first? You said *regel* is *muad* to break in walking, and then you said *behema* is *muad* to walk and break. They are the same thing.

### Av vs. Toladah

Rava answers: the *Tanna* teaches an *av* and a *toladah*. Rashi explains: the *av* is *regel mamash*, the leg itself; the *toladah* is *lalechet k'darkah*, which includes any part of the animal's body — its tail, its side, anything that bumps into something in the normal course of its movement. The first line establishes the leg as the primary category; the second line generalizes to the whole animal as a derivative category.

### Follow-up Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי: ״הַשֵּׁן מוּעֶדֶת״ ״הַבְּהֵמָה מוּעֶדֶת״, מַאי אָבוֹת וּמַאי תּוֹלָדוֹת אִיכָּא? הֲוָה קָמַהְדַּר לֵיהּ בִּבְדִיחוּתָא, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא שַׁנַּאי חֲדָא, וְאַתְּ שַׁנִּי חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina challenges this explanation: **If that is so, the latter clause** of the mishna (19b), **which teaches,** concerning the primary category of Eating: For what damage caused by **Eating** is the animal deemed **forewarned?** It is deemed forewarned with regard to eating food items fit for its consumption, i.e., **the animal is** deemed **forewarned** with regard to eating fruits and vegetables. Ravina asks: **What primary categories and what subcategories are there** in that context? No subcategories of Eating are enumerated there, and nevertheless, the phrase: The animal is deemed forewarned, is repeated. Rava **responded to** Ravina **with a humorous** response **and said to him: I resolved** the difficulty in **one** mishna, now **you resolve** the difficulty in **one** mishna.

The *Gemara* presses further: if that is the structure — *av* and *toladah* — then look at the equivalent structure for *shein* in the next *mishna* (19b): *keitzad hashen mu'edet le'echol et hara'ui lah*, followed by *ha-behema mu'edet le'echol peirot veyerakot*. There the second line does not introduce a new part of the animal — it simply specifies that *ra'ui lah* means fruits and vegetables. The exceptions there are *ksut vekelim*. So what is the *av* and *toladah* in the *shein* structure? The *Gemara* notes that Rava's answer does not obviously extend to *shein*.

### Chaya vs. Behema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: תַּנָּא שֵׁן דְּחַיָּה, וְקָתָנֵי שֵׁן דִּבְהֵמָה. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״וְשִׁלַּח אֶת בְּעִירֹה״ כְּתִיב, בְּהֵמָה – אִין, חַיָּה – לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּחַיָּה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is the reason** for the apparent redundancy in the mishna with regard to Eating? **Rav Ashi said:** First the *tanna* **teaches** the *halakha* of **Eating by an undomesticated animal and** then **he teaches** the *halakha* of **Eating by a domesticated animal.** And it was necessary to state both, as **it enters your mind to say** that since **it is written: "And he set his animal loose"** (Exodus 22:4), with regard to **domesticated animals, yes,** the primary category of Eating applies, but with regard to **undomesticated animals, no,** the primary category of Eating does not apply. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us that undomesticated animals** are included **in the category of domesticated animals** in this regard and the owner of an undomesticated animal is liable for damage caused by his animal eating another person's produce.

*Amar Rav Ashi*: the solution is different for *shein*. There, the first line concerns *chaya*, an untamed animal, and the second line concerns *behema*, a domesticated one. One might have thought that the rule of *uvi'er biseh acher* — the verse from which *regel* liability is derived — applies only to *behema*, as the word *behema* appears there explicitly, and not to *chaya*. Therefore the *mishna* teaches: *ka mashma lan de-chaya bichlal behema* — the category of *behema* in the verse includes *chaya* as well, so both are liable.

### Literary Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָא מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמִיתְנֵי בְּרֵישָׁא! הַאי דְּאָתְיָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרָשָׁא – חֲבִיבָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the *tanna* **should have taught** the *halakha* with regard to a domesticated animal **first** and only afterward taught the *halakha* with regard to an undomesticated animal, as the latter is included in the category of the former. The Gemara answers: On the contrary, **that** *halakha*, **which is derived from an interpretation, is dear to him,** and therefore the *tanna* preferred to begin with the derived *halakha* and only then proceed to the explicit *halakha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי – לִיתְנֵי הָהִיא דְּלָא כְּתִיבָא בְּרֵישָׁא! הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי אָבוֹת נִינְהוּ, הָךְ דְּאָתְיָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרָשָׁא – חֲבִיבָא לֵיהּ. הָכָא – שָׁבֵיק אָב, וְתָנֵי תּוֹלָדָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then with regard to the **first clause,** in this mishna **too, let** the *tanna* **teach that which is not** explicitly **written first,** as that which is derived from an interpretation is dear to him. The Gemara distinguishes between the cases: **How can** they **be compared? There,** with regard to Eating, **this** damage caused by a domesticated animal **and that** damage caused by an undomesticated animal **are primary categories** of damage; therefore, the *tanna* taught first **that** *halakha*, **which is derived from an interpretation, is dear to him.** But **here,** with regard to Trampling, would **he leave aside the primary category** of Trampling, done with the animal's foot, **and teach a subcategory** first?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אַיְּידֵי דְּסָלֵיק מֵרֶגֶל, פָּתַח בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that the reason that in this mishna the *tanna* begins with that which is written explicitly in the Torah and only then proceeds to teach that which is derived through an interpretation is as follows: **Since** the *tanna* **concluded** the discussion in the final mishna in the previous chapter (15b) **with** the primary category of **Trampling** by teaching the clause: The foot of an animal is deemed forewarned with regard to trampling objects and breaking them in the course of its walking, therefore, **he began** the first mishna in the second chapter **with** the primary category of **Trampling.**

This raises a further question about ordering. If that is so, the first line concerns *chaya* and the second concerns *behema*, which would mean the *toladah* — inferred from *drasha* — is placed before the *av*. Why lead with the derived case? The *Gemara* answers: *hai de-atya mi-drasha chaviva leh* — the *Tanna* prefers to open with what must be derived, because it is the more novel teaching. But then why not apply the same logic to *regel*, leading with the *toladah* (any body part) before the *av* (the leg itself)? The *Gemara* responds: *hatam eidi de-avot ninhu* — there, *chaya* and *behema* are not in a true *av*-*toladah* hierarchy with respect to each other; they are parallel instances of the same *av*, and so the derived case can lead. But when there is a genuine hierarchy of *av* and *toladah*, you begin with the *av*. Alternatively: *eidi de-salik me-regel patach be-regel* — since the preceding *mishna* concluded with the word *regel*, this *mishna* naturally opens with *regel* as well, even though it would otherwise prefer to begin with the derived case. None of these answers is entirely satisfying as *pshat* in the *mishna*, but that is the *Gemara's* resolution.

## Gufo vs. Tzrorot

### Direct Body Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּהֵמָה מוּעֶדֶת לְהַלֵּךְ כְּדַרְכָּהּ וּלְשַׁבֵּר. כֵּיצַד? בְּהֵמָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לַחֲצַר הַנִּיזָּק – וְהִזִּיקָה בְּגוּפָהּ דֶּרֶךְ הִלּוּכָהּ, וּבִשְׂעָרָהּ דֶּרֶךְ הִלּוּכָהּ; בָּאוּכָּף שֶׁעָלֶיהָ, וּבִשְׁלִיף שֶׁעָלֶיהָ, וּבִפְרוּמְבְּיָא שֶׁבְּפִיהָ, וּבְזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארָהּ; וַחֲמוֹר בְּמַשָּׂאוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: צְרוֹרוֹת, וַחֲזִיר שֶׁהָיָה נוֹבֵר בָּאַשְׁפָּה וְהִזִּיק – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: An animal is** deemed **forewarned** with regard **to walking in its** typical **manner and,** while doing so, **breaking** objects in the course of its walking. **How so?** If **an animal entered into the courtyard of the injured** party **and it damaged** an object **with its body** in **the course of its walking; or** if it caused damage **with its hair** in **the course of its walking; or with the saddle that is on it; or with the** packed **saddlebag [*shalif* ] that is on it; or with the halter in its mouth; or with the bell [*zog*] around its neck; or** if it was **a donkey** that caused damage **with its burden;** in all these cases the owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage. Sumakhos says:** In the case of **pebbles** propelled by an animal in the course of its walking, **or** in the case of **a pig that was burrowing in the garbage heap and caused damage,** the owner of the animal **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

*Tano rabanan* — the Sages taught: *behema mu'edet lalechet k'darka lishabber*. The *braita* then specifies in detail which components of an animal's movement are classified as *gufo* — direct body damage — and which are *tzrorot*. If a *behema* entered the *chatzer hanizak* and caused damage *begufa derech hilucha*, through its body in the normal course of walking, that is *gufo*. The list of components that count as *gufo* is extensive: the animal's body itself, its hair or tail (*sa'ara*), the saddle on its back (*ukaf*), the saddlebags (*shlif*), the bit in its mouth (*pumbia*), and the bell around its neck (*zug*). The logic is consistent throughout: so long as the damaging element has not left the animal's body and continues to be directly moved by the animal, it remains *gufo* and not *tzrorot*.

The bell (*zug*) might appear to raise a difficulty. A bell swings when the animal moves, and one might think that its oscillation resembles the chicken's bucket (*d'li*) tied to its leg, which the *mishna* classifies as *tzrorot*. The distinction will become clearer when the *Gemara* examines the chicken cases: the bucket, unlike the bell, is not in continuous contact with the animal and does not move in direct proportion to the animal's body motion. The *braita* lists all the above items as *gufo* unambiguously.

### Donkey Burden

*Vechamor bemasso'o* — and a donkey bearing its burden — *meshallem nezek shalem*, pays full damages. The burden on the donkey's back is treated as part of its body for purposes of liability, not as *tzrorot*.

### Sumchus Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִזִּיק – פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הִתִּיז וְהִזִּיק – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If a pig **caused damage,** it **is obvious** that its owner must pay the full cost of the damage. What novel element is Sumakhos introducing? The Gemara answers: **Rather, say:** If **it propelled** pebbles **and** thereby **caused damage,** the owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרוֹרוֹת מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמַיְהוּ? חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: צְרוֹרוֹת כִּי אוֹרְחַיְיהוּ – חֲצִי נֶזֶק. וַחֲזִיר שֶׁהָיָה נוֹבֵר בָּאַשְׁפָּה, וְהִתִּיז וְהִזִּיק – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק. סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: צְרוֹרוֹת, וַחֲזִיר שֶׁהָיָה נוֹבֵר בָּאַשְׁפָּה וְהִתִּיז וְהִזִּיק – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Pebbles, who mentioned anything about it?** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching:** For damage caused **by pebbles** that were propelled by animals walking **in their typical manner,** the owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage, and** for damage caused by **a pig that was burrowing in the garbage heap and propelled** pebbles **and** that **damaged** an object, its owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage. Sumakhos says:** Both in the case of **pebbles** that were propelled **and** in the case of **a pig that was burrowing in the garbage heap and propelled** pebbles **and damaged** an object, the owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.** Sumakhos does not accept the *halakha* that the owner pays only half the cost of the damage caused by pebbles inadvertently propelled by the foot of an animal in the course of its walking, i.e., in its typical manner. He holds that since the damage results from the animal's typical behavior, the owner pays the full cost of the damage.

*Sumchus omer tzroros vechazir shehaya nover be'ashpah vehitiz vehizik meshallem nezek shalem* — Sumchus says: in the case of *tzrorot* and in the case of a pig rooting in a dung heap and scattering material that causes damage, one pays full damages. The *Gemara* initially asks: but if the pig directly damaged, of course it pays full — that is obvious. The *Gemara* therefore corrects the reading: the case of the pig is specifically one where it was rooting and that caused things to scatter and strike another object — it is a case of *tzrorot*. The pig is rooting presumably in search of food; its motion scatters debris that then causes damage, making this a *regel* case. Whether it should be classified as *tzrorot deshein* or *tzrorot deregel* is an interesting question: the act of rooting for food would suggest *shein*, but its incidental scattering of material is more naturally classified as a *regel* derivative.

### Tzrorot Payment

The *Gemara* reads the *braita* as requiring a clarification: *chissorei mechassra v'hachi katani* — something is missing and the *braita* should be read as follows: *tzrorot k'orchaya chatzi nezek* — *tzrorot* in the normal manner of *regel* pays half damages. And a pig rooting in a dung heap and scattering material is likewise a case of *tzrorot* and pays half. *Sumchus omer* — Sumchus disputes this and holds that *tzrorot* pays full in both cases.

One might object that the rule of *chatzi nezek tzrorot hilcheta gemiri lah* — that half damages for *tzrorot* is an ancient tradition — should be beyond dispute. The *Netziv* notes, however, that the phrase *hilcheta gemiri lah* need not mean *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* in the strict sense; it may refer to an ancient tradition whose precise rationale has been lost. Furthermore, the rationale is not far to seek: *regel* is itself the category of damage most characterized by *mit'asek*, unintentional action — the animal does not aim to damage but simply moves through the world. When the damage is additionally *kocho* rather than *gufo* — not the animal's body but its indirect force — there is a compelling logic to reduced liability. If one's framework is not pure negligence but rather that "my animal did it and therefore I pay," then *tzrorot* represents a case where that causal connection is attenuated, justifying half. And while Rambam holds that there are no disputes about *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, the *Gemarot* contain several instances of precisely such debates.

## Regel Motion Cases

### Chicken Wings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תַּרְנְגוֹלִין שֶׁהָיוּ מַפְרִיחִין מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, וְשָׁבְרוּ כֵּלִים בְּכַנְפֵיהֶן – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. בְּרוּחַ שֶׁבְּכַנְפֵיהֶן – מְשַׁלְּמִין חֲצִי נֶזֶק. סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: תַּרְנְגוֹלִין שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַדְּסִין עַל גַּבֵּי עִיסָּה וְעַל גַּבֵּי פֵּירוֹת, וְטִינְּפוּ אוֹ נִיקְּרוּ – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. הֶעֱלוּ עָפָר אוֹ צְרוֹרוֹת – מְשַׁלְּמִין חֲצִי נֶזֶק. סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 2:1): If **chickens were flying from place to place and broke vessels with their wings,** their owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage** as this is a subcategory of Trampling. By contrast, if the damage was caused **by the wind** generated **by their wings,** the owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage,** in accordance with the *halakha* in the case of pebbles. **Sumakhos says:** The owner pays **the full** cost of the **damage. It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If **chickens were hopping upon dough or upon produce and they sullied** it with their feet, **or** if **they pecked** at it and caused damage, their owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage. If** in the course of their hopping **they stirred up dust or** propelled **pebbles,** the owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage. Sumakhos says:** He pays **the full** cost of the **damage.**

*Tarnagolim shehayvu mafrichim mimakom lemakom veshibru kelim bechanfeihem* — chickens flying from place to place that broke vessels with their wings — *meshalmim nezek shalem*, full damages, because the wings directly struck the vessels. *Beruach shebechanfeihem* — if, however, their wing-motion created wind that knocked vessels over — *meshalmim chatzi nezek*, half damages. Wind is a case of *tzrorot* without any physical object: it is pure, disembodied *koach*, the animal's force entirely separated from its body. *Sumchus omer nezek shalem* — Sumchus maintains full damages even here.

### Jumping Chickens

A second *braita*: chickens that were jumping on dough or fruit — *mehadesin al gabei issa* — and soiled it or pecked at it — *meshalmim nezek shalem*, full damages. If they were jumping around and the motion raised dust or scattered pebbles that dirtied the dough — *he'elu afar o tzroros* — *meshalmim chatzi nezek*, half damages. *Sumchus omer nezek shalem* — full damages.

### Wind Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: תַּרְנְגוֹל שֶׁהָיָה מַפְרִיחַ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, וְיָצְתָה רוּחַ מִתַּחַת כְּנָפָיו וְשִׁיבְּרָה אֶת הַכֵּלִים – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק. סְתָמָא כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If **a chicken was flying from place to place and wind emerged from beneath its wings and** the wind **caused vessels to break,** the owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage.** The Gemara notes: This **unattributed** *baraita* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Sumakhos and hold that the owner pays half the cost of the damage in these cases, just as he does in the case of pebbles propelled by the feet of an animal.

A third *braita* states: a chicken flying from place to place, where wind issued from beneath its wings and broke vessels — *meshalem chatzi nezek*. This *braita* is *stama kerabanan*, anonymous and consistent with the majority position, and does not include Sumchus's objection.

## Kocho vs. Gufo

### Zav Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא סוֹמְכוֹס – קָסָבַר: כֹּחוֹ כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי. אֶלָּא רַבָּנַן, אִי כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי – כּוּלֵּיהּ נֶזֶק בָּעֵי לְשַׁלֵּם; וְאִי לָאו כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי, חֲצִי נֶזֶק נָמֵי לָא לְשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** analyzed the *baraita* and **said: Granted,** the opinion of **Sumakhos** is understood, as **he holds** that the status of damage resulting from **a force** generated by the animal's action **is like** that of damage directly caused by the animal **itself,** and therefore the owner pays the full cost of the damage in both cases. **But** the opinion of **the Rabbis** is difficult, as **if** they hold that the status of damage resulting from **a force** generated by the animal's action **is like** that of damage directly caused by the animal **itself,** the owner should **be required to pay the full** cost of the **damage. And if** they hold that the status of damage resulting from **a force** generated by the animal's action **is not like** that of damage directly caused by the animal **itself,** and is not attributed to the animal, then the owner should not be required **to pay even half** the cost of **the damage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי, וַחֲצִי נֶזֶק צְרוֹרוֹת – הִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Then Rava said: Actually,** the Rabbis hold that the status of damage resulting from a force generated by the animal's action **is like** that of damage directly caused by the animal **itself,** and in principle the owner should be required to pay the full cost of the damage. **And** the liability to pay for **half** the cost of **the damage** caused by **pebbles** is because the Rabbis **learned this *halakha* through tradition,** and it does not correspond to the standard *halakhot* of damages.

This principle deserves careful attention. The underlying logic is that the laws of *tum'ah* pertaining to a *zav* track what is truly attributable to a person's body versus what is merely a remote effect of his force. If *tum'ah* law considers a given transmission as genuinely connected to the *zav's* body, then *nezikin* law should similarly treat it as *gufo*. If *tum'ah* law considers it too attenuated for transmission, *nezikin* law correspondingly treats it as *kocho*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל שֶׁבַּזָּב טָמֵא – בְּנִזָּקִין מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. כֹּל שֶׁבַּזָּב טָהוֹר – בְּנִזָּקִין מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** With regard to **any** type of action **which,** when **a *zav*** interacts with a vessel in that manner, he renders it **ritually impure,** if the same type of action was done by an animal, resulting **in damage,** the owner of the animal is liable to **pay the full** cost of the **damage.** Conversely, with regard to **any** type of action **which,** when **a *zav*** interacts with a vessel in that manner, he leaves it **ritually pure,** e.g., where the contact is indirect, if the same type of action was done by an animal, resulting **in damage,** the owner of the animal is liable to **pay** only **half** the cost of the **damage.**

Before the *Gemara* applies the principle, it raises a difficulty with Rava's earlier position. Rava had stated that *kocho kegufo dami* — an animal's force is like its body. But if that is so, *tzrorot* should pay full, not half. And if *kocho* is *not* like the animal's body, then *tzrorot* should pay nothing. Rava then retracts and says: *le'olam kgufo dami* — it is always considered like the body. The half payment of *chatzi nezek tzrorot* is *hilcheta gemiri lah* — an ancient transmitted rule that does not follow naturally from either logical extreme. One can interpret this practically: *tzrorot* sits in a grey zone. Whether one says the animal "really" did it is genuinely ambiguous — it is unintentional action that caused indirect force. The *halakha* does not resolve the ambiguity in either direction; it assigns half.

### Calf & Wagon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא, צְרוֹרוֹת אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן?! לֹא; רָבָא – עֶגְלָה מוֹשֶׁכֶת בַּקָּרוֹן קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rava, did he come to teach us** the *halakha* of **pebbles?** The substance of Rava's statement is that for damage caused by the indirect action of an animal, its owner pays half the cost of the damage. That is identical to the *halakha* of pebbles articulated in the *baraitot* cited earlier. The Gemara answers: **No, Rava teaches us** the *halakha* in the case of **a calf pulling a cart [*karon*].** Just as with regard to a *zav*, the status of a vessel that a *zav* moves is like that of a vessel with which he came into contact, so too, the status of damage caused by the cart pulled by the animal is like that of damage caused by the animal's body, and it is not considered indirect damage like in the case of pebbles.

*Egla moshacha b'karon ka mashma lan* — Rava's novel teaching is about a calf drawing a wagon over vessels. Is this *gufo* or *kocho*? Rashi explains: by a *zav*, if a wagon bearing a *zav* passed over a vessel, the vessel would be *tamei*, because the principle of *midras* — the *zav's* resting place — extends through the wagon to whatever the wagon presses upon. The wagon is an extension of the *zav's* body for purposes of *midras*. So too in *nezikin*: the wagon is tightly connected to the calf, like the saddle or the saddlebag, and is therefore *gufo*. *Meshallem nezek shalem* — full damages.

### Scattered Pebbles

*V'lo k'tzoros*: if pebbles scattered from under the wagon and broke a vessel, that is *tzrorot*. By a *zav*, pebbles scattered from beneath his wagon would not transmit *tum'ah* to a struck vessel — the connection is too remote. So too in *nezikin*: *tzrorot ninhu*, it is *tzrorot*, and half damages apply.

### Supporting Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: בְּהֵמָה מוּעֶדֶת לְשַׁבֵּר בְּדֶרֶךְ הִלּוּכָהּ. כֵּיצַד? בְּהֵמָה שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לַחֲצַר הַנִּיזָּק – וְהִזִּיקָה בְּגוּפָהּ דֶּרֶךְ הִלּוּכָהּ, וּבִשְׂעָרָהּ דֶּרֶךְ הִלּוּכָהּ; בָּאוּכָּף שֶׁעָלֶיהָ, וּבִשְׁלִיף שֶׁעָלֶיהָ, וּבִפְרוּמְבְּיָא שֶׁבְּפִיהָ, וּבְזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארָהּ; וַחֲמוֹר בְּמַשָּׂאוֹ, וְעֶגְלָה מוֹשֶׁכֶת בְּקָרוֹן – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava: An animal is** deemed **forewarned with regard to breaking** objects **in the course of its walking. How so?** If **an animal entered into the courtyard of the injured** party, **and it damaged** an object **with its body** in **the course of its walking; or with its hair** in **the course of its walking; or with the saddle that is on it; or with the** packed **saddlebag that is on it; or with the halter that is in its mouth; or with the bell that is around its neck; or** if **a donkey** caused damage **with its burden; or** if **a calf** caused damage while **pulling a cart;** in all these cases the owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

*Tanya k'vashei d'Rava* — a *braita* is taught in alignment with Rava's principle. The *braita* enumerates: *behema mu'edet lishabber bederech hilucha, keitzad, behema she-nichnesa l'chatzer ha-nizak v'hizika b'gufa derech hilucha, o b'sa'ara, o b'zanava, derech hilucha, b'ukaf she-aleha, b'shlif she-aleha, b'fumiya she-befiha, b'zug she-b'tzavaro, v'chamor b'masso'o* — all pay full. The *braita* adds: *ve'ha'egla moshacha b'karon meshallem nezek shalem* — and a calf drawing a wagon, full damages. This confirms Rava's ruling.

### Bucket Rope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תַּרְנְגוֹלִים שֶׁהָיוּ מְחַטְּטִין בְּחֶבֶל דְּלִי, וְנִפְסַק הַחֶבֶל וְנִשְׁבַּר הַדְּלִי – מְשַׁלְּמִין נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** In a case where **chickens were pecking at the rope** tied to **a bucket and the rope was severed and the bucket** fell and **broke,** their owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

*Tanu rabanan*: *tarnagolim shehayvu mechat'tetin b'chevel d'li* — chickens that were pecking away at the rope of a bucket — *venifsakha hachevel venishbar had'li*, the rope snapped and the bucket fell and broke — *meshallmim nezek shalem*, full damages. This is a *chiddush*: one might have thought that because the bucket fell as a result of the rope breaking — the rope being an intermediary — this should be considered *kocho* with respect to the bucket. The *Gemara* resolves it: the rope and the bucket are treated as one connected object. The chicken's beak directly engaged the rope, which is part of the bucket-rope unit; what subsequently happens to the bucket is a continuation of that direct act. This is analogous to an animal that bumps a wall on which a vessel is sitting — one does not say "the wall breaking the vessel is *kocho*"; the animal's encounter with the connected system makes the resulting damage *gufo*. Because the rope was tied to the bucket, the pecking of the rope is a direct act against the bucket-rope system, and full damages follow.

### Pending Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: דָּרְסָה עַל כְּלִי וְלֹא שְׁבָרַתּוּ, וְנִתְגַּלְגֵּל לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר וְנִשְׁבַּר, מַהוּ? בָּתַר מֵעִיקָּרָא אָזְלִינַן – וְגוּפֵיהּ הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא בָּתַר תְּבַר מָנָא אָזְלִינָא – וּצְרוֹרוֹת נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** If an animal **trod upon a vessel but did not break it and** the vessel then **rolled to another place and broke** there, **what is** the *halakha*? Rava elaborates: In determining one's liability for causing damage, **do we follow the initial** action that ultimately led to the damage **and this** case is regarded as damage caused by the animal **itself?** Accordingly, the owner would pay the full cost of the damage as he would in any case classified under the primary category of Trampling. **Or perhaps** do **we follow the breaking of the vessel and** therefore **this** case is regarded as similar to the case of **pebbles** propelled by the foot of an animal in the course of its walking, since the vessel was not broken by a direct action of the animal, but rather, as an indirect result of the animal's action?

*Ba'ei Rava* — Rava raises an unresolved question about a similar case. We will take up that question in the next session.
