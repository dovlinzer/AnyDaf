# Menachot 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamidim & Musafim

### Ein Me'akvin Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם שִׁבְעָה הָווּ! אֶלָּא, תַּנָּא בְּעָלְמָא קָאֵי, וּמַאי כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת וּשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה? סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In any case,** the question stands: Why does the mishna say six lambs when, in fact the number required **is seven?** The Gemara answers: **Rather, the *tanna* is** speaking **generally,** referring to how many inspected lambs are required throughout the year. **And what** did he mean by his statement: **Sufficient for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana?** It is **merely** intended as **a mnemonic,** to help one remember that there must be enough lambs for the daily offering of three days.

The overarching theme of this section of Menachot concerns the principle of *ein me'akvin* — that certain ritual components do not hold one another back — now applied to the world of *korbanot*. Having already examined the tight relationship between the *kivsei atzeret* and the *shtei halechem* within a single *korban*, the Mishna turns to broader groupings of communal offerings.

The Mishna states: *hatmidim ein me'akvin et hamussafim* — the *tamidim* do not prevent the *musafim*, and conversely, *velo hamussafim me'akvin et hatmidim* — if one has animals for *musafim* but not *tamidim*, the *musaf* may be brought even without the *tamid*, and vice versa.

### Musafim Independence

The principle extends further: *velo hamussafim me'akvin zeh et zeh* — even among the *musafim* themselves, they do not hold one another back. On a day such as *Shabbos Rosh Chodesh*, when there are two separate *musaf* obligations, if one cannot bring both, one may bring whichever is available. Even a partial complement of the *musaf* animals — which typically comprises a large group of bulls, a ram, and seven lambs — may be brought without the remainder.

### Two Tamidim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבּוֹקֶר וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, שֶׁאֵין מְחַנְּכִין אֶת מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב אֶלָּא בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים. חִינּוּךְ מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the priests **did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, nevertheless, they should sacrifice a lamb in the afternoon as the daily offering. If they did not burn the half-measure of incense in the morning, they should burn the half-measure in the afternoon. **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon, as** the daily service on a new **golden altar is initiated only with** the burning of **the incense of the spices** in the afternoon, at which time they would burn a full measure. The Gemara asks: **Who mentioned anything about** the **initiation** of the Temple vessels, i.e., what is its relevance to the mishna?

The Mishna continues: *lo hikrivu keves baboker yakrivu bein ha'arbayim* — if the morning lamb was not brought, the afternoon lamb may still be brought. The two *tamidim* are thus not mutually dependent, just as the individual animals of the *musaf* are not mutually dependent. One might have assumed that since they come as a natural pair, it should be all or nothing — but the Mishna rules otherwise.

Rabbi Shimon, however, qualifies this: *eimatai? bizman shehayu anusim o shogegim* — when is this permitted? Only when the failure to bring the morning *tamid* was beyond one's control (*onus*) or inadvertent (*shogeg*). But *aval im hayu mezidim* — if it was a willful choice — *lo yakrivu bein ha'arbayim* — they may not bring it in the afternoon.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבֹּקֶר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ אֲנוּסִים אוֹ שׁוֹגְגִין, אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ מְזִידִין – לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבֹּקֶר, לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the mishna **is incomplete, and this is** what **it is teaching:** If **they did not sacrifice a lamb** for the daily offering **in the morning, they should not sacrifice** a lamb in **the afternoon. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the service of a new **altar had not** yet **been initiated,** since it must first be initiated with the daily offering of the morning. **But** if the service of **the altar had** already **been initiated,** then **they should sacrifice** the lamb of the daily offering in **the afternoon** even though they did not sacrifice the lamb of the morning offering. **Rabbi Shimon said: When** does this *halakha* apply? It applies **at a time when** the failure to sacrifice the daily morning offering was because **they were** prevented from sacrificing it due to **circumstances beyond their control or** they failed to sacrifice it **unwittingly. But if** the priests acted **intentionally and did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, **they should not sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering.

Rabbi Shimon's position here reflects a broader principle visible elsewhere in his rulings. Recall his earlier ruling that if one must choose between bringing some of the *musafim* with their *nesachim* or all of them without their *nesachim*, it is better to bring fewer but bring them properly. There is a consistent theme: do it right, or do not do it at all. Here too, Rabbi Shimon holds that one should not volitionally choose to do only one of the *tamidim*. If forced by circumstance, perhaps; but as a free choice, one may not elect the afternoon *tamid* while foregoing the morning one. The relationship between the two *tamidim*, in his view, is too significant to allow a deliberate half-measure.

This also connects to what the Mishna implies through its phrasing *ve'et hakeves hasheni* — the emphasis on "the second" presupposes a "first." Rabbi Shimon reads this language as establishing an interdependence, while the *chachamim* are not persuaded.

## Mizbeach Inauguration

### Altar Inauguration Ways

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְלֹא אֶת הַשֻּׁלְחָן אֶלָּא בְּלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בְּשַׁבָּת, וְלֹא אֶת הַמְּנוֹרָה אֶלָּא בְּשִׁבְעָה נֵרוֹתֶיהָ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** And use of a new **Table** was initiated **only with** the arrangement of **the shewbread on Shabbat,** and use of a new **Candelabrum** was initiated **only with** the kindling **of its seven lamps in the afternoon.**

The Mishna then introduces a series of rules about how the various sacred vessels are inaugurated: *she'ein mitchaneket mizbach hazahav ela biktoret hasamim* — the golden altar is inaugurated through the burning of the *ktoret*; *v'lo mizbeach ha'olah ela b'tamid shel shachar* — the outer altar is inaugurated only with the morning *tamid*. The *shulchan* is inaugurated with the *lechem hapanim* on *Shabbat*, and the *menorah* is inaugurated by lighting its seven candles in the afternoon. The principle is that each vessel is inaugurated by performing its characteristic *avodah* upon it for the first time.

As the Gemara will later add, the *mizbach hazahav* is specifically inaugurated with the *ktoret hasamim shel bein ha'arbayim* — the afternoon incense — a point whose significance will be discussed.

### Outer Altar & Tamid

The Mishna's statement that the outer *mizbeach* is inaugurated specifically with the *tamid shel shachar* is not incidental. It underscores the foundational status of the morning *tamid* in relation to the outer altar. This provides at least a contextual basis for Rabbi Shimon's position: since the *tamid shel shachar* serves as the anchor and beginning of the altar's service — indeed, as its very act of inauguration — one should not make a deliberate choice to skip it and perform only the *tamid shel bein ha'arbayim*.

## Today vs Tomorrow

### Limited Resources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשִּׁשָּׁה טְלָאִים הַמְבוּקָּרִין בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים, כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת וְלִשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin's dilemma based upon a mishna (*Arakhin* 13a): One maintains **no fewer than six lambs that** have been **inspected** and found to be unblemished **in the Chamber of the Lambs, sufficient for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana** that in some years occur adjacent to it.

The Gemara opens its analysis with a question posed by Rav Chiya bar Avin to Rav Chisda: *tzibur she'ein lahem tmidim u-musafim eizeh me-hem kodem* — if a community does not have animals for both *tamidim* and *musafim*, which takes priority?

The question requires careful framing. *Kodem* here does not mean which is performed first in sequence, but rather which is preferred when only one can be brought. The easy case is clear: if one must choose between today's *tamid* and today's *musaf* — say, on *Shabbos*, with only two lambs — *pshita tmidim adifei*, the *tamidim* take precedence, because of the principle *tadir v'she-eino tadir tadir kodem*: the more frequent offering precedes the less frequent one. Moreover, the *tamid* of *Shabbat* is no less *mikudash* than the *musaf* of *Shabbat*, so there is no competing consideration of greater sanctity.

The genuinely difficult question is: *tmidim d'limachar u-musafim d'ha-idna* — what if one must choose between today's *musaf* and tomorrow's *tamidim*? This could arise, for instance, on *Shabbos* with four lambs and no prospect of acquiring more before the next morning — given that animals must be inspected four days in advance, even a supplier arriving that evening could not provide valid animals for tomorrow's *tamid*. Do you address today's needs and bring the *musaf*, or do you plan ahead and preserve animals for tomorrow's *tamid*?

The Gemara frames the tension as: *tmidim adifei she-ken tadir* — perhaps the *tamidim* are preferred because they are more frequent — versus *musafim adif de-havu le-mikudash* — perhaps the *musafim* take precedence because they are of greater sanctity, or because, in one reading of Rashi, *mikudash* does not refer to greater intrinsic holiness but rather to the immediacy of today's obligation. Rashi offers two approaches: one in which a *korban* brought on a sacred day like *Shabbat* is considered to carry a heightened quality by virtue of its association with that day's *kedusha*; and one in which *mikudash* simply means "the pressing matter of today" — the question being whether one's obligation is to address the present moment or to plan for the future.

### Mishna Proof Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ תְּמִידִין וּמוּסָפִין – טוּבָא הֲווֹ! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּמִידִין עֲדִיפִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that** it is referring to a case where the Temple treasury **has** enough animals for all of the offerings that should be brought, six lambs are not enough, as **there are many** lambs sacrificed for **daily offerings and additional offerings** on those three days. In total, twenty-two lambs are required: Two each day for the daily offerings, two for the additional offering of Shabbat, seven for the additional offering of Rosh HaShana, and seven for the additional offering of the New Moon. **Rather, is it not that** the mishna is referring to a case where the Temple treasury **does not have** enough animals for all of the offerings, and it is teaching that the animals that he does have are utilized for the daily offerings of all three days rather than for the additional offering of Shabbat on the first of the three days? Consequently, one may **conclude from it** that the **daily offerings** of tomorrow **are given preference** over the additional offerings of today.

To resolve the question, the Gemara cites the Mishna itself: *hatmidim ein me'akvin et hamussafim velo hamussafim me'akvin et hatmidim velo hamussafim me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the *tamidim* and *musafim* do not prevent one another, and the *musafim* do not prevent each other.

The Gemara asks: what precisely does this Mishna mean? If it means that one has all the animals available and the Mishna is speaking about order — i.e., if one performed them out of sequence it is still valid — that cannot be its meaning, because we know from a *braita* that nothing may precede the *tamid shel shachar*: *minayin shelo yehe davar kodem letamid shel shachar — talmud lomar ve-arach aleha ha-olah*, and Rava states explicitly that *ha-olah olah rishona* — the *tamid* must be the first offering on the fire, with nothing preceding it.

The Gemara therefore concludes that the Mishna must be discussing a case of *leis lei* — where one does not have all the animals and must choose which to bring. And since the Mishna seems to permit either choice — bringing the *tamidim* without the *musafim*, or bringing the *musafim* without the *tamidim* — as a volitional *le'chatchila* decision (since later in the Mishna Rabbi Shimon is discussing deliberate choices), it appears to treat both options as equivalent. Choosing today's *musaf* over tomorrow's *tamid* would be fine; choosing tomorrow's *tamid* over today's *musaf* would also be fine. This would suggest that neither is categorically preferred.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית לְהוּ, וּלְקַדֵּם. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר קוֹדֵם – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Ḥisda: That is not a valid resolution of the dilemma, as one can claim that **actually,** the mishna is referring to a case **where they have** enough animals for all the offerings, **and** the mishna is referring to the issue of sacrificing one **before** the other. **And** with regard to **that which** poses **a difficulty for you** based upon the *baraita* that states that **no** sacrifice **should precede** the sacrifice of the daily offering of the morning, that *baraita* **is merely** stating **the mitzva** *ab initio*.

Abaye objects: *le-olam de-is lehu* — the Mishna is in fact discussing a case where one has all the animals available. *U-lekadem* — the point of the Mishna is indeed about performing them out of sequence. The *braita* that says nothing may precede the *tamid shel shachar* is only a *le'chatchila* requirement; *be-di'avad*, if one reversed the order, it is still valid. Abaye thus reads the Mishna as a *be-di'avad* statement, not a *le'chatchila* prescription about which to choose. The Mishna is not offering guidance on priority; it is simply stating what happens when things have gone differently than planned. No proof can therefore be drawn from it regarding today's *musaf* versus tomorrow's *tamid*.

## Reserve Sheep Supply

### Six Sheep Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית לֵיהּ, וְהָכִי קָא אָמַר: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשִּׁשָּׁה טְלָאִים הַמְבוּקָּרִין בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, וּמַנִּי? בֶּן בַּג בַּג הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **No,** this is not a valid proof, as one can claim that **actually,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the Temple treasury **has** enough animals for all of the offerings of the three days, **and this is what** the mishna **is saying** when it mentions six lambs: One maintains for use as daily offerings **no fewer than six lambs that** have been **inspected** and found to be unblemished **in the Chamber of the Lambs four days prior to** their **slaughter.** Lambs sacrificed as other offerings do not have to be inspected four days prior to being slaughtered. **And** in accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **ben Bag Bag.**

The Gemara continues its search for a resolution and cites a Mishna: *ein pochsin mi-shisha t'layim ha-mevukarim be-lishkas ha-t'layim kedei le-shabbat u-lishnei yamim tovim shel Rosh Hashana* — there may be no fewer than six inspected lambs kept in the chamber of the lambs (*lishkat ha-telayim*), enough to supply *Shabbat* and the two days of *Rosh Hashana* — the only two-day *yom tov* observed in Eretz Yisrael.

The idea of a reserve makes practical sense: during *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana*, there is no opportunity to go out and acquire new animals. The reserve ensures that the *tamidim* can be brought even under those constrained circumstances.

### Reserve Interpretation

The Gemara initially assumes this Mishna is discussing a case where one does not have animals for both *tamidim* and *musafim*, and notes that the reserve is calibrated specifically for the *tamidim* — two lambs for *Shabbat* and two lambs for each of the two days of *Rosh Hashana*, for a total of six. The *musafim* are not provided for in this reserve. This would indicate that when planning in advance, the community's primary concern is for the *tamidim*, not the *musafim* — suggesting that *tamidim* are prioritized even over a *Shabbat* or *Rosh Hashana* *musaf*.

Rashi sharpens this further: if *Shabbat* falls immediately before *Rosh Hashana*, then on *Shabbat* itself, one would bring only two of the six lambs — for the *tamid* — deliberately preserving the remaining four for the *tamidim* of the two days of *Rosh Hashana*, even though one could theoretically bring the *musaf shel Shabbat* today. This would represent an active, live choice of tomorrow's *tamid* over today's *musaf*.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading: *le-olam de-is lei* — in truth, the reserve is not premised on a shortage of animals. If it were talking about providing the full complement for *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana*, including *musafim*, the number would be far greater than six. *Rosh Hashana* alone requires seven lambs for its *musaf*, in addition to bulls and a ram. The number six therefore cannot be about fully provisioning all *korbanot* for these days.

### Ben Bag Bag Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, בֶּן בַּג בַּג אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְתָמִיד שֶׁטָּעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תִּשְׁמְרוּ לְהַקְרִיב לִי בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה לָכֶם לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת עַד אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם״. מָה לְהַלָּן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה, אַף כָּאן טָעוּן בִּיקּוּר אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **ben Bag Bag says: From where** is it derived that **the daily offering requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter? The verse states** with regard to the daily offering: **"You shall observe [*tishmeru*] to sacrifice to Me in its due season"** (Numbers 28:2), **and elsewhere,** with regard to the Paschal offering, the verse **states:** "On the tenth day of this month they shall take for them every man a lamb…**and you shall keep it [*mishmeret*] until the fourteenth day** of this month" (Exodus 12:3–6). Consequently, it is derived by means of a verbal analogy that **just as** in the verse **there,** the Paschal offering **requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter,** from the tenth of the month to the fourteenth, **so too here,** the daily offering **requires inspection four days prior to** its **slaughter.**

The Gemara therefore reinterprets the Mishna entirely: *ein pochtin me-shishah telayim ha-mevukarin be-lishkas ha-telayim arba'ah yamim kodem shechitah* — there must be no fewer than six lambs that have been inspected for four days prior to their slaughter. The mention of *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana* was not the reason for the reserve; it was merely a mnemonic for the number six.

The actual rule is grounded in a *braita* attributed to Ben Bag Bag: *minayin le-tamid she-ta'un bikkur arba'ah yamim kodem shechitah?* — whence do we know that the *tamid* requires four days of prior inspection before it is slaughtered? He derives it through a *gezerah shavah*: the Torah says *tishmeru le-hakriv li be-mo'ado* regarding the *tamid*, and separately states regarding the *korban Pesach* that it must be kept *ad arba'ah asar yom* — the *korban Pesach* was designated on the tenth of Nisan and slaughtered on the fourteenth, a four-day period of inspection. Just as the *Pesach* requires four days of checking, so too does the *tamid*.

Critically, Ben Bag Bag specifies that this requirement applies specifically to the *tamid*. Not every *korban* requires four days of advance inspection — only the *tamid*. The *musafim*, by contrast, have no such requirement. This explains why the reserve chamber holds specifically *tamid* animals: they require extended advance preparation that *musafim* do not. The existence of a dedicated reserve for *tamid* lambs thus reflects a technical *halachic* demand, not a normative judgment that *tamidim* take priority over *musafim*.

This point also carries a striking conceptual resonance. The two offerings that require four days of advance preparation are the *korban Pesach* — the singular once-a-year event — and the *tamid* — the most daily and routine of all *korbanot*. These two extremes, the most exceptional and the most regular, require equal degrees of preparation and care. The *tamid*, which is brought every single day without exception, demands the same level of attention as the most celebrated offering of the year. This parallels the theme articulated by Rashi at the beginning of *Parshat Tzav*, where the instruction to the *kohanim* concerning the daily removal of ashes — *zeiruz miyad kan u-ledoros* — comes precisely because daily repetition breeds complacency. The things most in need of safeguarding are often those done every day.

### Ravina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָנֵי שִׁשָּׁה? שִׁבְעָה הָווּ, דְּהָא אִיכָּא דְּצַפְרָא דִּתְלָתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא! וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תְּמָנְיָא הָווּ, דְּהָאִיכָּא דְּפַנְיָא דְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, דִּלְבָתַר דְּאַקְרֵיב קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a discussion pertaining to the mishna cited earlier. **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** With regard to **these six** inspected lambs that must always be kept in the Chamber of Lambs, there ought to **be seven, as there is** a need for another lamb for the daily offering **of the morning of the third** day **of the week,** since it cannot be inspected on Shabbat or the subsequent festival days of Rosh HaShana, and therefore should be inspected and kept beforehand. Rav Ashi responded to Ravina: **According to your reasoning** the correct number is not seven, but rather **it is eight; as there is** also the daily offering **of the afternoon of Shabbat eve** that should also be counted. Ravina replied: **That** is **not difficult,** because the mishna **is saying that after he sacrificed** the daily offering of the afternoon there must be six inspected lambs.

Ravina raises a challenge to Rav Ashi: if the six lambs are indeed intended to cover the *tamidim* for *Shabbat* and two days of *Rosh Hashana*, the number is actually insufficient. Consider the scenario where *Shabbat* comes first and is immediately followed by two days of *Rosh Hashana*. Six lambs would cover the three days — two for *Shabbat* and four for the two days of *Rosh Hashana*. But the morning after *Rosh Hashana* ends, the *tamid* must be brought, and there will be no inspected animals available. Since four days of prior checking are required, there is no way to have a valid animal ready first thing the next morning without some reserve remaining. So why is six sufficient?

Ravina further objects: one could equally argue the reserve should be eight, because the afternoon *tamid* on *erev Shabbat* — just before *Shabbat* begins — also depletes the supply, and one has no opportunity to replenish it before *Shabbat* enters. The Gemara deflects this second challenge: the count of six refers to what remains *after* the day's *korbanot* have been brought, not before. But the challenge about the morning after *Rosh Hashana* still stands.

### Mnemonic Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם שִׁבְעָה הָווּ! אֶלָּא, תַּנָּא בְּעָלְמָא קָאֵי, וּמַאי כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת וּשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה? סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״כְּדֵי לַשַּׁבָּת״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״לְשַׁבָּת וְלִשְׁנֵי יָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁל רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In any case,** the question stands: Why does the mishna say six lambs when, in fact the number required **is seven?** The Gemara answers: **Rather, the *tanna* is** speaking **generally,** referring to how many inspected lambs are required throughout the year. **And what** did he mean by his statement: **Sufficient for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana?** It is **merely** intended as **a mnemonic,** to help one remember that there must be enough lambs for the daily offering of three days. According to this the language of the mishna **is also precise, as** it **teaches: Sufficient for Shabbat** and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana, **and it does not teach** that there must be six lambs **for Shabbat and for the two festival days of Rosh HaShana,** which would have indicated that these lambs are meant to actually be sacrificed on those days. The Gemara concludes that in fact one can **learn from** the language of the mishna that this is the correct interpretation.

The Gemara's response is decisive: *ela tanna b'alma ka'ei* — the reference to *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana* is simply a mnemonic (*simna b'alma*), not a literal prescription for those specific days. The Mishna is not saying that six lambs are designed to cover exactly *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana*. Rather, six is the standing minimum reserve that must be maintained at all times. When *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana* are actually approaching, one would increase the reserve accordingly. *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana* are invoked only as a memorable reference point for the number six, not as the operational purpose of the reserve.

This is also inferred from the Mishna's precise language: it says *kedei le-Shabbat* — "enough as for *Shabbat*" — using *kedei* rather than *le-Shabbat*, which would mean explicitly "for *Shabbat*." The phrasing suggests the number is calibrated by analogy to the *Shabbat* and *Rosh Hashana* scenario, not that it is literally meant to serve those days.

### Rotation System

The Shittah Mekubetzet attempts to explain the logic of six more precisely by describing the rotation system. If a new *mizbeach* is being inaugurated on a Wednesday, one would begin designating and checking lambs on the *Shabbat* before — two lambs checked on *Shabbat* for use on Wednesday, two checked on Sunday for use on Thursday, and two checked on Monday for use on Friday. That gives six animals in the chamber, all in various stages of their four-day inspection cycle. On Tuesday, two more lambs would be added for the following *Shabbat*.

This continuous rotation — two animals added each day, two drawn upon each day — keeps the *lishka* in constant operation. Technically, the system requires eight animals to maintain perfectly, since at any given moment there must be a full four-day queue. But the Shittah Mekubetzet explains that when the two oldest animals are drawn out for today's use and before new ones have been introduced, there are exactly six remaining. The number six thus represents the minimum point in the rotation cycle, the moment between drawing on the reserve and replenishing it.

This also raises the question of who performs the inspection. From a comment in Tosafot on 50a, it emerges that the *Beit Hamikdash* could acquire animals from external suppliers who had themselves conducted the four-day inspection and could certify that the animals were *mevukarim*. The problem Ravina identifies — that there would be no inspected animals available first thing the morning after *Rosh Hashana* — is resolved in practice because suppliers could be contacted in advance. The one scenario that cannot be resolved this way is if one needs an animal in the very first hours of the morning without any opportunity to have arranged it the night before.

In any case, the conclusion of the Gemara is that no proof can be derived from this Mishna for the original question of priority between today's *musaf* and tomorrow's *tamid*. The reserve system has nothing to do with choosing between *tamidim* and *musafim*; it exists solely to meet the unique technical requirement that the *tamid* must be inspected four days in advance.

## Afternoon Service

### Afternoon Tamid Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאֵת הַכֶּבֶשׂ הַשֵּׁנִי תַּעֲשֶׂה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״ – שֵׁנִי בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, וְלֹא רִאשׁוֹן בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, that if the lamb of the daily offering of the morning was not sacrificed and the service of a new altar had not yet been initiated, then the lamb of the afternoon is also not sacrificed? The Gemara explains that it is derived **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: "The one lamb you shall offer in the morning; **and the second lamb you shall offer in the afternoon"** (Exodus 29:39). This verse teaches that **the second** lamb of the daily offering is sacrificed **in the afternoon, but** if it is **the first** one to be sacrificed, it may **not** be sacrificed **in the afternoon.**

The Gemara now turns to a direct examination of the Mishna's ruling regarding the afternoon *tamid*: *lo hikrivu keves baboker lo yakrivu bein ha'arbayim* — if they did not bring the morning lamb, they may not bring it in the afternoon. The question is how to properly contextualize this ruling.

### Shimon Mezid Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אֲבָל נִתְחַנֵּךְ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹן בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ אֲנוּסִים אוֹ שׁוֹגְגִין, אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ מְזִידִין – לֹא הִקְרִיבוּ כֶּבֶשׂ בַּבֹּקֶר, לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the service of a new **altar had not** yet **been initiated,** since it must first be initiated with the offering of the morning. When the verse refers to the first or second offering, it means the first or second offering ever sacrificed on the altar. **But** if the service of **the altar had** already **been initiated,** then **even** if it is **the first** to be sacrificed that day, it should be sacrificed **in the afternoon.** **Rabbi Shimon said: When** does this *halakha* apply? It applies **at a time when** the failure to sacrifice the daily morning offering was because **they were** prevented from sacrificing it due to **circumstances beyond their control or** they failed to sacrifice it **unwittingly. But if** the priests acted **intentionally and did not sacrifice a lamb in the morning** as the daily offering, **they should not sacrifice** a lamb **in the afternoon** as the daily offering.

Rabbi Shimon's position, as stated, is that if the failure to bring the morning *tamid* was due to *onus* or *shogeg*, the afternoon *tamid* may be brought; but if it was *mezid* — willful — they may not bring the afternoon one. When it comes to the *ktoret*, however, Rabbi Shimon permits bringing only the afternoon *ktoret* even when it is *mezid*. This asymmetry demands explanation.

The difficulty with Rabbi Shimon's position, as the Gemara notes, is that if his reasoning were purely principled — that the two *tamidim* form a necessary pair and one cannot stand without the other — then the distinction between *shogeg* and *mezid* would be irrelevant. A principled pairing requirement would apply regardless of intent. The fact that Rabbi Shimon distinguishes between inadvertent and willful failure suggests that his concern is not about the intrinsic necessity of the pair but rather about something else entirely.

### Distinction Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְכִי כֹהֲנִים חָטְאוּ, מִזְבֵּחַ בָּטֵל?! אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר – לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ הֵן, אֲבָל אֲחֵרִים יַקְרִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does it make sense that **because the priests sinned** by intentionally failing to sacrifice the morning daily offering, **the altar** should be entirely **idle? Rava said** that **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying: They,** the priests who deliberately failed to sacrifice the morning daily offering, **should not sacrifice** the afternoon daily offering; **but other** priests **should sacrifice** it.

The Gemara develops this by reading the Mishna in context. The end of the Mishna states that the outer *mizbeach* is inaugurated only with the *tamid shel shachar*, which implies that the morning *tamid* carries foundational significance. The Gemara suggests reading the Mishna's ruling as follows: *bameh devarim amurim she-lo nitchanech hamizbe'ach* — the rule that you cannot bring the afternoon *tamid* without the morning one applies when the *mizbeach* has not yet been inaugurated. The *chachamim* agree that when establishing the service of the *mizbeach* for the first time, you must begin with the morning *tamid* — you cannot inaugurate it beginning in the afternoon. But *aval nitchanech hamizbe'ach* — once the altar has been inaugurated — *afilu rishon bein ha'arbayim* — even if the afternoon *tamid* is the first offering of the day, it is acceptable, because the foundational act of inauguration has already been performed.

To this, Rabbi Shimon adds: *eimatai?* — when is this permission to bring only the afternoon *tamid* granted? *Bizman shehayu anusim o shogegim* — only when the failure to bring the morning one was not the *kohanim*'s fault. *Aval im hayu mezidim* — if they willfully skipped the morning *tamid* — *lo hikrivu baboker lo yakrivu bein ha'arbayim* — they forfeit their right to bring the afternoon one.

This reading integrates the inauguration discussion naturally into the Mishna. The *chachamim* acknowledge the primacy of the morning *tamid* as the inaugurating act, but hold that once the *mizbeach* is inaugurated, the afternoon one stands independently. Rabbi Shimon agrees with the premise but adds a rabbinic safeguard for the case of willful neglect.

### Ketores Different

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִקְטִירוּ קְטֹרֶת בַּבֹּקֶר, יַקְטִירוּ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא, וּמְעַתְּרָא, חֲבִיבָא לְהוּ וְלָא פָּשְׁעִי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if the priests acted intentionally and **did not burn** the **incense in the morning,** even those same priests **may burn** it in the **afternoon.** The reason for this is **that since** burning the incense **is uncommon and causes** those who do so to become **wealthy,** it is **dear to the** priests, **and they will not be negligent** in the performance of this rite.

The Gemara then addresses the *ktoret*. The Mishna states: *lo hiktiru ketoret baboker yaktiru bein ha'arbayim* — if the *ktoret* was not burned in the morning, it may be burned in the afternoon. Unlike the *tamid*, Rabbi Shimon does not restrict this to cases of *onus* or *shogeg*. Why is the *ktoret* treated differently?

The Gemara seeks the source for the basic rule. A *braita* cites the verse: *et hakeves ha'echad ta'aseh baboker ve'et hakeves hasheni ta'aseh bein ha'arbayim* — the second lamb is specifically designated for the afternoon, and not the first. This teaches: *sheni bein ha'arbayim velo rishon bein ha'arbayim* — what may stand in the afternoon is only "the second," only what was paired with a first. If there was no morning offering, then the afternoon offering cannot be the "second" — it would be the first, which is not its proper designation for the afternoon. This is what prevents doing only the afternoon *tamid* — it cannot be called *hasheni* if there was no *ha'echad*.

However, this restriction applies only to inaugurating the *mizbeach* for the first time, as explained above. Once the *mizbeach* is inaugurated, the *pessukim* in *Pinchas* — which govern the ongoing *tamid* — are read as not imposing the same pairing requirement as a mandatory matter, and the afternoon *tamid* may be brought independently in cases of *onus* or *shogeg*.

The association of the *tamid* with the *mizbeach ha'olah* runs deep throughout the Torah's language. The outer *mizbeach* is consistently called the *mizbach ha'olah*, and the *tamid* is described as *olat tamid hasuya behar sinai* — linked to the very inauguration of the service at Sinai. These *pessukim* establish the morning *tamid* as the defining act of the *mizbeach*'s identity and its service.

## Shimon Rabbinic

### Rava Interpretation

The Gemara cites Rava as clarifying the fundamental nature of Rabbi Shimon's ruling: *lo hikrivu heim aval acherim yakrivu* — when Rabbi Shimon says that if the morning *tamid* was not brought, the afternoon one may not be brought by those same *kohanim*, he does not mean that no afternoon *tamid* is brought at all. Rather, the *kohanim* who were negligent in the morning are disqualified from performing the afternoon service; other *kohanim* may step in and bring it. The altar does not suffer for the priests' misconduct. This framing confirms that Rabbi Shimon's restriction is not a principled ruling about the necessary pairing of the two *tamidim*, but a disciplinary measure directed at the *kohanim* who were at fault.

This also accounts for why it is described in terms of *mezid* — willful conduct — rather than as a categorical requirement. If the pairing were essential to the *korban* itself, it would be irrelevant whether the failure was deliberate or inadvertent; the *korban* would be invalid either way. Since Rabbi Shimon distinguishes between *shogeg* and *mezid*, the ruling is necessarily about accountability, not about the intrinsic structure of the service.

### Ketores Exemption

With this framework in place, the Gemara explains why the *ktoret* is treated differently from the *tamid*. The reason is captured in the phrase *de-lo shechichi u-ma'ashra* — the *ktoret* is both rare and enriching. In contrast to the *tamid*, where a large team of *kohanim* was involved each morning, the *ktoret* was performed by a single *kohen*, and was so rare an opportunity that most *kohanim* never had the chance to perform it even once. Moreover, there was a tradition that performing the *ktoret* brought the *kohen* wealth. Given how prized and infrequent the opportunity was, *chaviva lehu velo pashei* — they cherish it and will not be negligent. There is simply no need to establish a rabbinic safeguard against negligence in a domain where negligence is almost inconceivable.

### Tamid Needs Protection

The *tamid*, by contrast, is brought every single day. Its very regularity creates the risk of complacency. The *kohanim* assigned to bring it might begin to treat it as routine and allow it to slip. It is precisely because it is performed daily — *tadir* in every sense — that it requires the protection of a rabbinic deterrent. If *kohanim* know that deliberate neglect in the morning forfeits their right to bring the afternoon *tamid*, they will be more careful to attend to the morning service properly.

This connects directly to the theme of Ben Bag Bag's four-day inspection requirement: the *tamid* is the *korban* most in need of structural safeguards and careful preparation, not because it is more sacred than others, but precisely because its regularity makes it vulnerable to neglect. The things that are done every day are the things that need *chizuk* — reinforcement and vigilance — most of all.

## Afternoon Ketores Full

### Full Ketores Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכוּלָּהּ הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, שֶׁאֵין מְחַנְּכִין אֶת מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב אֶלָּא בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם וְכוּ׳. וְהָתַנְיָא: בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if they did not burn the half-measure of incense in the morning, they should burn the half-measure in the afternoon. **Rabbi Shimon said: And** in such a case, **the entire** measure **was sacrificed in the afternoon.** The reason for the difference between the daily offerings and the incense is **that** the daily service on a new **golden altar is initiated only with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the afternoon,** at which time they would burn a full measure. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The service of a new golden altar is initiated **with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning?** The Gemara answers: The question of whether the incense of the morning or the afternoon initiates the service of a new golden altar **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.**

The Mishna records a further ruling of Rabbi Shimon: *vekula hayata kreva bein ha'arbayim* — if the afternoon *ktoret* is brought without a morning *ktoret* having been offered, the full measure is brought in the afternoon, not merely the half. The standard practice divided the *maneh* of *ktoret* between morning and afternoon, half at each time. If one missed the morning, Rabbi Shimon's ruling is not that one brings only the afternoon's half; rather, the full measure is brought in the afternoon.

The Gemara introduces this by noting that the version of the Mishna connected to Rabbi Shimon includes the phrase *she'ein mitchanchin et mizbach hazahav ela biktoret hasamim shel bein ha'arbayim* — you only inaugurate the *mizbach hazahav* with the afternoon *ktoret*, not the morning one. This is the Gemara's version of Rabbi Shimon, which differs from the plain text of the Mishna, and leads into the next set of discussions.

### Ketores-Menorah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִסְתַּבְּרָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּבֹּקֶר בַּבֹּקֶר בְּהֵיטִיבוֹ אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת יַקְטִירֶנָּה״. אִי לָאו דַּעֲבַד הַדְלָקָה מֵאוּרְתָּא, הֲטָבָה בְּצַפְרָא מֵהֵיכָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It **stands to reason** that the *halakha* should be **in accordance with the one who says** that it was initiated **with the incense of the spices of the afternoon, as it is written** with regard to the golden altar: "And Aaron shall burn thereon incense of sweet spices; **every morning, when he dresses the lamps, he shall burn it.** And when Aaron lights the lamps at dusk, he shall burn it, a perpetual incense before the Lord throughout your generations" (Exodus 30:7–8). The fact that ashes are removed from the lamps of the Candelabrum in the morning indicates that the lamps had been lit previously, since **if** the priest had **not performed the lighting** of the lamps the previous **evening, from where** would the ashes be **removed in the morning?** This proves that the Candelabrum must have been lit for the first time in the evening. Since the verse states: "When Aaron lights the lamps at dusk, he shall burn it, a perpetual incense before the Lord," it must be that the incense was burned for the first time in the evening.

The Gemara raises an immediate challenge from a *braita*: *biktoret hasamim shel shachar* — another source indicates that the *mizbach hazahav* is inaugurated with the morning *ktoret*, not the afternoon one. The Gemara resolves this as a *tannaitic* dispute: it is a debate among the *tannaim* as to which *ktoret* service — morning or afternoon — is more fundamental, and accordingly which one inaugurates the *mizbach hazahav*.

The significance of this question for Rabbi Shimon's ruling about the full measure is as follows: he permits bringing only the afternoon *ktoret* without the morning, and mandates that the full amount be brought in that case. If the afternoon *ktoret* is the more fundamental service — as demonstrated by its role in the inauguration of the *mizbach hazahav* — then it is coherent to say that one may bring only the afternoon, and moreover, that one should bring the full measure when doing so alone.

Abaye is cited as supporting the view that the afternoon *ktoret* is indeed the more fundamental: *mistabra k'man d'amar biktoret hasamim shel bein ha'arbayim*. His reasoning is drawn from the verse *baboker baboker betivo et hanerot yaktirena* — every morning, when the *kohen* cleans out (*heitiv*) the lamps of the *menorah*, he also burns the *ktoret*. But for there to be a morning cleaning of the *menorah*, there must have been a nighttime lighting. The *menorah* is lit at night and cleaned in the morning; the *ktoret* accompanies both. This means the *ktoret* is organically linked to the *menorah*'s cycle, and the *menorah*'s cycle begins at night — with the afternoon/evening lighting — not in the morning. Therefore the primary orientation of the *ktoret* also begins in the afternoon, making the afternoon *ktoret* the more foundational of the two.

### Parallel to Tamid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר, גָּמַר מִמִּזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה: מָה לְהַלָּן בְּתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר – אַף כָּאן בִּקְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים שֶׁל שַׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that the service of a new golden altar is initiated **with** the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning,** from where is this *halakha* derived? The Gemara answers: He **derives** it **from** the initiation of **the altar of the burnt offering. Just as there,** the service of a new altar of the burnt offering is initiated **by** means of the **daily offering of the morning** rather than the afternoon, **so too here,** the service of a new golden altar is initiated **by** means of the burning of **the incense of the spices of the morning.**

Abaye's position is contested by those who hold that the morning *ktoret* is the inaugurating act. Their reasoning: *gmar mi-mizbach ha'olah* — just as the outer *mizbeach* follows the model of morning as foundational (the *mizbeach ha'olah* is inaugurated with the *tamid shel shachar*), so too the *mizbach hazahav* should be understood as following the same morning-first model. Just as the outer *mizbeach* is characterized by the morning *tamid*, the inner *mizbeach* should be characterized by the morning *ktoret*.

In both cases — whether one emphasizes the morning or the afternoon — the underlying principle is to identify which service is most fundamental to the altar's identity and function.

### Mizbeach Identity

This debate crystallizes a deeper question about the nature of the *mizbach hazahav* itself: is it best understood as a parallel structure to the *mizbeach ha'olah*, with a morning-first orientation, or is it more properly categorized with the other vessels of the *heichal* — particularly the *menorah* — which operate on a night-morning-night cycle? 

The *mizbeach ha'olah* and its *tamid* operate on a morning-to-afternoon rhythm, with the morning as the inaugurating and fundamental moment. The *menorah*, by contrast, begins its cycle at night: *Shabbat* evening, morning, the following evening, and so on. Since the *ktoret* accompanies the *menorah* at both its moments, the question of whether the *mizbach hazahav* belongs to the *mizbeach ha'olah* family or the *menorah* family determines whether its foundational service is in the morning or the afternoon.

What remains implicit in Rabbi Shimon's position — though the Gemara does not spell it out fully — is that by identifying the afternoon as the fundamental *avodah* of the *mizbach hazahav*, Rabbi Shimon can coherently (a) permit bringing only the afternoon *ktoret* without the morning, and (b) require that when it is brought alone, the full measure be used. If the afternoon is primary, then bringing it alone is not a deficient substitute for the morning; it is the essential act, now performed in its fullness.
