# Pesachim 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Work on Erev Pesach

### Intro & Chatzos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מוֹשִׁיבִין שׁוֹבָכִין לְתַרְנְגוֹלִים בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר. וְתַרְנְגוֹלֶת שֶׁבָּרְחָה — מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתָהּ לִמְקוֹמָהּ, וְאִם מֵתָה — מוֹשִׁיבִין אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ. גּוֹרְפִין מִתַּחַת רַגְלֵי בְּהֵמָה בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, וּבַמּוֹעֵד מְסַלְּקִין לִצְדָדִין. מוֹלִיכִין כֵּלִים וּמְבִיאִין מִבֵּית הָאוּמָּן — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָם לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna continues the discussion of the *halakhot* of Passover eve. **One may place eggs under hens** on the **fourteenth** of Nisan so that the birds will brood until the eggs hatch. **And** if a **hen fled** from brooding, **one** may **restore it to its place. And if** a brooding hen **died, one** may **place another in its stead.** Similarly, **one may sweep** dung **from beneath the legs of an animal** on **the fourteenth** of Nisan. **And during** the intermediate days of the **Festival one may clear** it **to the sides.** Similarly, **one** may **take vessels to the craftsman's house** for repair **and bring** others from there **even though they are not for the purpose of the Festival.**

The daf begins mid-*mishna*, picking up from 55b, in the context of the prohibition of *melachah* on *Erev Pesach*. The discussion is transitioning from the realm of *minhag* — regional custom — into more formally defined *halachic* territory. It is possible that the *mishna* is also addressing the period after *chatzos*, when the prohibition is universally agreed upon and no longer merely a matter of custom.

The previous *mishna* had dealt with the concept of *tzorech hamoed* and completing *melachah* in ways that warranted leniency. This *mishna* turns to analogous activities that are permitted, presumably on account of *hefsed* — the concern for financial loss.

### Hens on Eggs

The *mishna* states: *moshivin shovchin letarnigolim b'yod daled*, that one may have hens sit on eggs on the fourteenth of Nisan, and if a hen ran away, *vetarnigolet shebarchah machzirin otah limkomah* — one may return it to its nest. Rashi offers two readings of *moshivin shovchin*: either it is a single phrase referring to setting hens upon eggs, or it refers to two distinct acts — settling doves in a dovecote and having chickens sit on eggs. In either case, the practical point is that one may arrange for hens to sit on eggs on the fourteenth so that the eggs will be fertilized and hatch, thereby preventing loss. The *mishna* adds that if a hen dies, one may substitute another in its place — *ve-im metah moshivin acheret tachteiah*. All of these acts are permitted in order to guard against possible loss.

### Sweeping Dung

The *mishna* continues: *gorfin mitachat raglei behemah b'yod daled ubamoed mesalkin litzedadin* — one may sweep the dung from beneath the legs of animals on the fourteenth, removing it entirely from the barn, while on *Chol HaMoed* one may only sweep it to the sides. One observes here that the *melachah* under discussion is emphatically not *melachah* in the sense of the thirty-nine prohibited labors; it refers, rather, to the kind of ordinary physical work one would associate with going about one's daily occupation.

### Vessels from Uman

The *mishna* states further: *molichin kelim umevi'im mibeit ha-uman af al pi she'einam letzorech hamoed* — one may bring vessels to and take them back from the craftsman's house, even if they are not needed for the festival. The precise basis for this leniency is less immediately clear. It does not appear to involve *hefsed* in the same straightforward sense as the preceding cases, and the *mishna* itself emphasizes that it applies even when the vessels are not required for the *moed*.

## Returning Hens Debate

### Abaye on Returns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַשְׁתָּא אוֹתוֹבֵי מוֹתְבִינַן, אַהְדּוֹרֵי מִיבַּעְיָא?! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Now,** the mishna stated that **placing** a brooding hen to sit on eggs is permitted; **is it necessary** to mention that **restoring** a hen to its brooding place is permitted? **Abaye said: In the last clause** of the mishna **we have arrived at** the *halakhot* of the **intermediate days** of the Festival, when placing a hen to sit on eggs is prohibited, yet one may nevertheless restore a hen that fled, as failure to do so will cause him to incur a loss. On the fourteenth of Nisan, one may even place a hen to brood *ab initio*.

The *Gemara* opens with a pointed question: *hashta otuvei motvinan* — the *mishna* permits one to set hens upon eggs in the first place, so *ahadorei mibaiya*? Is it even a question that one may return them once they have already been sitting? Returning them would seem to involve an even clearer concern for *hefsed*. Abaye resolves this by explaining that *seifa atan l'cholo shel moed* — the latter clause of the *mishna* is in fact addressing *Chol HaMoed*. On the fourteenth, one may even place hens on eggs *lechatchilah*; on *Chol HaMoed*, one may not do so *lechatchilah*, but one may return a hen that has wandered.

### Rav Huna 3 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא תּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה לְמִרְדָּהּ — דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא פְּרַח צִימְרָא מִינָּה, וְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה לִישִׁיבָתָהּ — דְּפָסְדָא לַהּ בֵּיעֵי לִגְמָרֵי. אֲבָל לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה לְמִרְדָּהּ, דִּפְרַח לַהּ צִימְרָא מִינָּה, וְתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה לִישִׁיבָתָהּ, דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא פְּסִידִי בֵּיעֵי לִגְמָרֵי — לָא מַהְדְּרִינַן. רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה לִישִׁיבָתָהּ מַהְדְּרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: They taught** this *halakha* that one may restore the hen to the eggs **only** when it is within **three days of its rebellion,** when the hen fled from its place, **as the heat has not yet** completely **dissipated** from the hen, so that restoring the hen to its place to resume its brooding will be effective; furthermore, this *halakha* applies only if it is also at least **three** days **after** the hen began **sitting, when** failure to restore the hen to sit on the eggs will cause **the eggs** to be **totally ruined,** as on the one hand they are no longer edible, and on the other hand the chick in the egg is only partially formed. **However,** if it is more than **three** days **after its rebellion, when its heat has** completely **dissipated, or within three** days **from when it** began **sitting, when** failure to restore the hen to sit on the eggs will **not** yet cause **the eggs** to be **totally ruined, one** may **not restore** the hen to sit on the eggs. **Rabbi Ami said: Even within three** days **from when it began sitting, one** may **restore** the hen to sit on the eggs.

Rav Huna then qualifies the scope of the allowance to return a hen on *Chol HaMoed*: *lo shanu ela betoch sheloshah l'mirdah* — the permission applies only within three days of the hen's rebellion and flight, because *dekarir uparash chamimuta minah* — within this window, its warmth has not yet entirely dissipated and there remains a natural inclination for it to return to the eggs. Beyond this, the hen will neither be willing nor useful. In addition, Rav Huna restricts the allowance to *betoch sheloshah l'yeshivatah* — only after the hen has already been sitting for three days, *de-pseida de-bei'ei*, because by that point the eggs have begun to fertilize and their loss would be total if the hen does not continue. If the hen rebelled more than three days ago, *deparach uparash chamimuta minah*, its warmth has fled entirely. Or if it is within the first three days of sitting, the eggs have not yet substantially fertilized and one may still simply use them as ordinary eggs — in either such case, *lo mahadrinan*, one may not force the hen to return, since the justification of *hefsed* is insufficient.

### Hefsed Merubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: לְהֶפְסֵד מְרוּבֶּה חָשְׁשׁוּ, לְהֶפְסֵד מוּעָט — לֹא חָשְׁשׁוּ, וּמָר סָבַר: לְהֶפְסֵד מוּעָט נָמֵי חָשְׁשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle do Rav Huna and Rabbi Ami **disagree?** The Gemara explains: One **Sage,** Rav Huna, **holds** that with regard **to a major loss,** the Sages **were concerned** about the eggs being completely ruined, and therefore one may restore the hen to its place only if it had already sat on the eggs for three or more days. With regard **to a minor loss** of being forced to sell at a discount the unhatched eggs that were incubated for less than three days, **they were not concerned. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Ami, **holds** that with regard **to a minor loss, they were also concerned,** and therefore the Sages permitted one to restore the hen to its place during the intermediate days of the Festival.

Rabbi Ami disagrees: *afilu betoch shlosha liyeshivata mahadrinan* — even within the first three days of the hen's sitting, when the eggs have only begun to fertilize, one may nevertheless return the hen. That degree of loss is already sufficient justification. The *Gemara* identifies the crux of their dispute: *mar savar hefsed merubeh chashashu, hefsed muat lo chashashu* — one authority holds that the rabbis permitted intervention only for major loss, not minor loss, while the other holds *hefsed muat nami chashashu* — even minor loss is sufficient grounds. It is worth noting the relative scale involved: we are speaking of the difference in value between a fertilized egg that fails to hatch and one that does not — sums that in absolute terms are quite small, yet they are nonetheless treated as meaningful gradations of *hefsed merubeh* and *hefsed muat* relative to the value of the object at stake.

## Dung Removal

### Chatzer Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוֹרְפִין מִתַּחַת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַזֶּבֶל שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר מְסַלְּקִין אוֹתוֹ לִצְדָדִין, שֶׁבָּרֶפֶת וְשֶׁבֶּחָצֵר מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לָאַשְׁפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna: **One may sweep** the dung **from beneath** the legs of an animal on the fourteenth of Nisan. **The Sages taught** in greater detail in the *Tosefta*: With regard to **the dung in a courtyard, one** may **clear it to the sides,** and with regard to the dung **that is in the barn and in a courtyard, one may take it out to the garbage** dump.

The *Gemara* turns to the second clause of the *mishna* regarding sweeping dung. A *baraita* is cited: *hazeval shebachatzer mesalkin oto latzedadim* — dung in the *chatzer* (the shared courtyard, familiar from the discussions in Eruvin) may be swept to the sides. The *baraita* then states *shebarefet ushevachatzer motzi'in oto le'ashpah* — dung in the barn or in the *chatzer* may actually be carried out to the garbage dump entirely. The internal contradiction regarding the *chatzer* is immediately apparent: *hagufa kashya* — does one sweep it to the sides, or remove it to the dump? The initial distinction between barn and courtyard — that a barn contains far more dung and thus warrants greater leniency — does not resolve the problem, since the *baraita* states contradictory things about the *chatzer* itself.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ: זֶבֶל שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר מְסַלְּקִין אוֹתוֹ לִצְדָדִין, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: שֶׁבָּרֶפֶת וְשֶׁבֶּחָצֵר מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לָאַשְׁפָּה! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, כָּאן בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** matter **itself is difficult.** On the one hand, **you said:** With regard to **the dung in a courtyard, one** may **clear it to the sides,** from which it may be inferred that one may not take it out to the garbage dump. **And then it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: With regard to the dung **that is in the barn and in a courtyard, one may take it out to the garbage** dump. **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** where the Sages permitted taking out the dung to the garbage dump, it refers to the **fourteenth** of Nisan. **There,** where they permitted only moving it to the sides, it refers **to** the **intermediate days of the Festival.**

Abaye resolves the contradiction by applying the same principle he articulated earlier: *kan beyod-daled vekan becholo shel moed* — one clause addresses the fourteenth and the other *Chol HaMoed*. In this case the order is inverted from what might be expected: the first statement — that one may only sweep to the sides — applies to *Chol HaMoed*, while the second — that one may remove entirely to the dump — is the more expansive allowance granted on the fourteenth. The same logic operates throughout: the fourteenth permits more than *Chol HaMoed*.

### Rava Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם נַעֲשָׂה חָצֵר כְּרֶפֶת — מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לָאַשְׁפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Both **this** case **and that** case refer **to** the **intermediate days, and this is** what **it is saying:** In an ordinary courtyard one may only move the dung to the sides; however, **if the courtyard becomes** as filthy **as a barn, one** may **take out** the dung **to the garbage** dump.

Rava offers an alternative resolution: *ha veha becholo shel moed* — both statements may be referring to *Chol HaMoed*, and there is no contradiction. *Ve-hakhi ka'amar*: the general rule for the *chatzer* is that one may only sweep to the sides. However, *ei na'aseh chatzer kerefet* — if the quantity of dung in the *chatzer* has grown so great that it resembles a barn, then *motzi'in oto le'ashpah* — one may remove it entirely to the dump. The allowance thus tracks not the time period but the quantity of the dung. One notes also the parallel to *geraf shel rei* in the laws of *muktzeh*, where the presence of excrement creates exceptional leniency because of the profound discomfort it causes.

## Vessels Trust

### Rav Papa Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹלִיכִין כֵּלִים וּמְבִיאִין מִבֵּית הָאוּמָּן. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בָּדֵיק לַן רָבָא. תְּנַן: מוֹלִיכִין וּמְבִיאִין כֵּלִים מִבֵּית הָאוּמָּן, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָן לְצוֹרֶךְ הַמּוֹעֵד. וּרְמִינְהוּ: אֵין מְבִיאִין כֵּלִים מִבֵּית הָאוּמָּן, וְאִם חוֹשֵׁשׁ לָהֶן שֶׁמָּא יִגָּנְבוּ — מְפַנָּן לְחָצֵר אַחֶרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna: **One** may **take vessels to the craftsman's house** for repair **and bring** others from there. **Rav Pappa said: Rava tested us. We learned** in the mishna: **One** may **take vessels** to **the craftsman's house** for repair **and bring** others **from** there **even if they are not for the purpose of the Festival.** He **raised a contradiction** from that which we learned: **One** may **not bring vessels from a craftsman's house, and if he is concerned lest they be stolen, he may move them to another courtyard** belonging to the craftsman, but not to his own house.

Rav Papa reports that Rava once tested his students with the following difficulty. The *mishna* states *molichin umevi'im kelim mibeit ha-uman af al pi she'eino letzorech hamoed* — one may bring vessels to and from the craftsman even when they are not needed for the festival. Against this, a *baraita* states *ein mevi'in kelim mibeit ha-uman* — one may not retrieve vessels from the craftsman. The *baraita* adds that *ve-im choshesh lahen shema yignavu* — if one fears that the vessels may be stolen, one may place them in another nearby courtyard, but one may not bring them home directly. The tension with the *mishna* is clear: one source permits retrieval outright, the other forbids it even when there is concern for the safety of the vessels.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁנֵּינַן, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, כָּאן — בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא וְהָא בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּמַאֲמִינוֹ, כָּאן — בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מַאֲמִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And we answered:** This is **not difficult. Here,** where one may bring vessels from the craftsman's house, it is referring to the **fourteenth** of Nisan. **There,** where one may not bring them, it is referring **to the intermediate days** of the Festival. **And if you wish, say** instead that **this and that** are referring **to the intermediate days, and** nevertheless, this is **not difficult. Here,** where one may not bring them home, it is referring to a case where **he trusts** that the craftsman will return the items; **there,** where it is permitted to take the vessels home, it is referring to a case where **he does not trust him.**

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction in the standard manner: *kan bearba'ah asar vekan becholo shel moed* — the blanket permission belongs to the fourteenth, the restricted allowance applies on *Chol HaMoed*. Alternatively, *ve-i ba'it eima*, both may be addressing *Chol HaMoed*, and the distinction is *kan bema'amino vekan beshe'eino ma'amino* — in one case one trusts the craftsman, in the other one does not. Rashi explains that "trusting him" means one is not concerned that the craftsman will either demand re-payment or sell the vessels if they are not promptly retrieved. When one does trust him in this sense, one is afforded less latitude, since the urgency is lower. When one does not trust him — fearing either that he will sell the item or demand additional payment — a broader allowance is granted.

### Zilzul Yom Tov?

A question arises as to whether the underlying concern is *zilzul yom tov* — the concept known from Moed Katan, whereby one is pressed to complete such errands before the festival so as not to treat *Chol HaMoed* as an opportunity to catch up on ordinary business. The issue of *melachah* on *Chol HaMoed* encompasses two distinct categories: certain prohibitions, such as those involving haircuts and laundering, are specifically designed to ensure that matters are attended to before the festival; others reflect the principle that *Chol HaMoed* is *mikzat yom tov* and thus certain labors are simply forbidden. Which category governs the case of vessels from the craftsman is not entirely clear. It is possible that the entire framework here is about ensuring one takes care of these matters beforehand — which would explain why the fourteenth carries a blanket allowance, a structure parallel to the allowances regarding haircuts already discussed in prior material.

### Baraita Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מְבִיאִין כֵּלִים מִבֵּית הָאוּמָּן, כְּגוֹן הַכַּד מִבֵּית הַכַּדָּר, וְהַכּוֹס מִבֵּית הַזַּגָּג. אֲבָל לֹא צֶמֶר מִבֵּית הַצַּבָּע, וְלֹא כֵּלִים מִבֵּית הָאוּמָּן, וְאִם אֵין לוֹ מַה יֹּאכַל — נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ וּמַנִּיחוֹ אֶצְלוֹ. וְאִם אֵינוֹ מַאֲמִינוֹ — מַנִּיחָן בְּבַיִת הַסָּמוּךְ לוֹ. וְאִם חוֹשֵׁשׁ שֶׁמָּא יִגָּנְבוּ — מְבִיאָן בְּצִינְעָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it** was **taught** in the following *baraita*: **One** may **bring vessels from the house of a craftsman, such as a jug from the house of a potter, or a cup from the house of a glassmaker, but** one may bring **neither wool from the house of a dyer nor** other **vessels from the house of a craftsman** that are not for the purpose of the Festival. **And if** the craftsman **has nothing to eat, one gives** the craftsman **his payment** on the intermediate days of the Festival **and leaves** the item **with him. And if he does not trust** that the craftsman will deliver the items, **he leaves them in the adjacent house. And if he is concerned lest** the item **be stolen, he** may **bring them to his house surreptitiously.**

A *baraita* is cited in support of these distinctions. It permits one to retrieve items like a pitcher from the potter or a glass vessel from a glassblower — things that have immediate *tzorech hamoed* utility — but not dyed wool or ordinary vessels that have no particular use during the festival. If the craftsman needs his fee and has no other means of support, one may go and pay him, but still may not take the vessels home. If one does not trust the craftsman to hold the item securely, one may transfer the vessels to a nearby house. If one fears even that location is vulnerable to theft, *mivi'an betzina betoch beito* — one may bring them home quietly. The *Gemara* then notes that this *baraita* addresses the case of retrieval but does not resolve the question of *molichin* — dropping the vessels off. The *mishna* permits this while the *baraita*'s formulation implies it is forbidden, and explaining one direction of the transaction does not automatically account for the other.

## Jericho Practices

### Jericho Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים עָשׂוּ אַנְשֵׁי יְרִיחוֹ, עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה מִיחוּ בְּיָדָם וְעַל שְׁלֹשָׁה לֹא מִיחוּ בְּיָדָם. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן שֶׁלֹּא מִיחוּ בְּיָדָם: מַרְכִּיבִין דְּקָלִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם, וְכוֹרְכִין אֶת שְׁמַע, וְקוֹצְרִין וְגוֹדְשִׁין לִפְנֵי הָעוֹמֶר. וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁמִּיחוּ בְּיָדָם: מַתִּירִין גַּמְזִיּוֹת שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna continues the discussion of the *halakhot* of Passover eve, along with other local customs. **Six actions were performed by the** Jewish **residents of Jericho,** contrary to common practice. With regard **to three,** the Sages **reprimanded them, and** with regard **to three,** the Sages **did not reprimand them. And these are the ones** with regard to **which they did not reprimand them:** The residents of Jericho would **graft palm trees the entire day** on the fourteenth of Nisan; **and** they would **bundle *Shema*,** as explained in the Gemara; **and** they would **harvest and pile** grain **before the *omer*** offering was brought. **And these are the ones** with regard to which the Sages **reprimanded them:** They would **permit** the use of **consecrated branches** of carob or sycamore trees. This refers to trees whose branches were cut and consecrated for Temple upkeep, which subsequently sprouted new branches;

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* describing six practices of the people of Jericho (*anshei Yericho*): three regarding which the Sages did not protest, and three which they did protest. The thematic connection to the preceding discussion is twofold: one of the practices relates to *melachah* on the fourteenth, and the broader subject — variation in local practice and the rabbinic response thereto — continues the discussion of *minhag* and deviation from standard norms.

### No Protest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹכְלִין מִתַּחַת הַנְּשָׁרִים בְּשַׁבָּת, וְנוֹתְנִין פֵּאָה לַיָּרָק. וּמִיחוּ בְּיָדָם חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **and** they would **eat** fallen fruit **from beneath** palm **trees that shed fruit** that had fallen **on Shabbat; and they** would **designate** the produce in the **corner** for the poor in a field of **vegetables,** which is exempt from this obligation even by rabbinic law. **And the Sages reprimanded** the people of Jericho for doing these three things.

The three practices regarding which the Sages did not protest are as follows. First, *markivin dekalim kol hayom* — they would graft palm trees the entire day of the fourteenth, even in the afternoon when *melachah* would ordinarily be forbidden. This was apparently tolerated because of *hefsed* concerns. Second, *vekorchin et shema* — they would "wrap" the *Shema*, reading straight through without the appropriate separation; the meaning of this will be elaborated below. Third, *vekotzrin vegodshin lifnei ha'omer* — they would cut their grain and bind it into sheaves before the *Omer* offering was brought. This would seem to violate the principle of *reishit ketzirchem*, the biblical prohibition on harvesting before the *Omer*. However, the grain of Jericho — growing as it did in the valley — was not considered of sufficient quality for use as the *Omer*, which required grain from higher ground. Since it could not be brought for the *Omer*, there was no biblical prohibition on harvesting it beforehand. There might have been a rabbinic concern that one could inadvertently come to eat from it, but the Sages did not protest.

### With Protest

The three practices against which the Sages did protest are as follows. First, *matirin gamziyot shel hekdesh* — they would permit the additional growths that sprouted from sycamore trees after these trees had been consecrated as *hekdesh*. Once a tree is sanctified, the later growth that emerges after one trims it and new shoots proliferate should retain its sanctified status; the people of Jericho held that this subsequent growth was permitted. Second, *ve'ochlin mitachat hanesharim beShabbat* — they would eat on *Shabbat* from produce that had fallen off trees, which raises a *muktzeh* concern or another *Shabbat* problem. Third, *venotnin pe'ah leyarak* — they would designate *pe'ah*, the corner of the field left for the poor, from vegetables. The difficulty with this seemingly generous practice is that *pe'ah* is exempt from *terumot u'ma'asrot*. Since vegetables are ordinarily not subject to the obligation of *pe'ah*, the people of Jericho's designation of *pe'ah* from vegetables did not actually exempt the produce from tithes — meaning the poor were consuming *tevel*, untithed produce. This is why the Sages protested.

## Hezekiah Actions

### Actions Approved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁשָּׁה דְבָרִים עָשָׂה חִזְקִיָּה הַמֶּלֶךְ, עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה הוֹדוּ לוֹ, וְעַל שְׁלֹשָׁה לֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ: גֵּירַר עַצְמוֹת אָבִיו עַל מִטָּה שֶׁל חֲבָלִים — וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ. כִּיתַּת נְחַשׁ הַנְּחשֶׁת — וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ. גָּנַז סֵפֶר רְפוּאוֹת — וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Apropos the people of Jericho, who were reprimanded for some of their actions and not reprimanded for others, the Gemara cites a similar *baraita*. **The Sages taught: King Hezekiah performed six actions. With regard to three** of them, the Sages of his generation **conceded to him; and with regard to three** of them, the Sages **did not concede to him.** Due to King Hezekiah's father's wickedness, **he dragged the bones of his father** Ahaz **on a bier of ropes** and did not afford him the respect due to a king, **and** the Sages **conceded to him. He ground the copper snake** that Moses fashioned in the desert because Israel worshipped it, **and** the Sages **conceded to him. He suppressed the Book of Cures, and they conceded to him.**

The *Gemara* continues with a parallel *baraita* concerning King Hezekiah (*Chizkiyahu HaMelech*): *shisha devarim asah Chizkiyahu haMelech, al shlosha hodu lo ve'al shlosha lo hodu lo* — six things Hezekiah did, three of which the Sages approved and three of which they did not. The three they approved: first, *girer atzmot aviv al mita shel chavalim* — he dragged his father's bones in the street on a bier of ropes rather than carrying them in a coffin with proper honor. His father was Achaz, who had led the people into idolatry. Despite the obligations of *kibbud av va'em* and respect for the dead, this act of public dishonor was considered necessary for *Kiddush Hashem* — to express condemnation of Achaz's idolatrous reign and to help restore the people to the proper religious path. The Sages agreed. Second, *ve-khitet nekhash ha-nekhoshet* — he ground up the copper serpent that Moses had made, because people had begun to worship it or employ it in healing rituals, treating it as a magical object. Destroying it was similarly an act of religious restoration. Third, *ganaz Sefer Refuot* — he hid the book of healing, and the Sages agreed with this as well.

### Rambam & Medicine

The nature of the *Sefer Refuot* is a matter of significant debate. One view is that it was simply a straightforward medical text, and Hezekiah hid it because he wished to encourage people to pray to God when ill, rather than turning to human remedies. The Rambam famously and forcefully rejects this interpretation. He argues that the use of rational faculties to address illness and physical hardship is not only permitted but expected. In his view, one thanks God for healing not because one expects miraculous intervention but because God has endowed the human intellect with the capacity to develop medicine, and has given the physician the skill to heal — and this is itself *yad Hashem*. One need not forgo natural means of healing in order to acknowledge divine providence. The Rambam therefore suggests that the *Sefer Refuot*, analogous to the copper serpent, contained magical or incantation-based healing methods — the type of quasi-idolatrous practice that warranted suppression — and that it was this, not legitimate medicine, that Hezekiah sought to eradicate.

### Actions Disapproved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל שְׁלֹשָׁה לֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ: קִיצֵּץ דְּלָתוֹת שֶׁל הֵיכָל וְשִׁיגְּרָן לְמֶלֶךְ אַשּׁוּר — וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ. סָתַם מֵי גִיחוֹן הָעֶלְיוֹן — וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ. עִיבֵּר נִיסָן בְּנִיסָן — וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to three** actions, the Sages **did not concede to him. He cut off the doors of the Sanctuary and sent them to the King of Assyria, and they did not concede to him** because he thereby demeaned the Temple. **He sealed the waters of the upper Gihon** stream, diverting its water into the city by means of a tunnel, **and they did not concede to him,** because he harmed the local populace in the process and should have relied upon God (*Me'iri*). **He intercalated** the year, delaying the advent of the month **of Nisan during Nisan, and they did not concede to him.** The Gemara explains that he declared the first of Nisan to be the thirtieth of Adar and only then intercalated the year (see II Chronicles 30:2).

The three actions the Sages did not approve: first, *kitzetz daltot shel Heikhal ve-shigar le-melekh Ashur* — he cut off the golden doors of the *Heikhal* and sent them to the king of Assyria as tribute. The Sages held that he should have placed greater trust in God. Second, *setam mei Gichon ha-elyon* — he sealed the upper waters of the Gichon spring, so that a besieging army could not benefit from them. Again, the Sages felt this reflected insufficient faith. Third, *ve-ibar Nisan be-Nisan* — he intercalated the year and declared an additional month of Adar while Nisan had already begun. The halacha requires that a leap year be declared before Nisan commences; a *leap year* intercalation cannot be effected once Nisan has already begun. The Sages did not agree with this action. The Talmud notes that while all three disapproved actions find some textual basis in *pesukim*, the first two are more explicitly grounded there.

## Palm Grafting

### Rav Yehuda Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַרְכִּיבִין דְּקָלִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי עָבְדִי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מַיְיתֵי אָסָא דָּרָא וְשִׁיכְרָא דְּדַפְנָא וְקִימְחָא דִשְׂעָרֵי דִּרְמֵי בְּמָנָא דְּלָא חָלְפִי עֲלֵיהּ אַרְבְּעִין יוֹמִין, וּמַרְתְּחִי לְהוּ, וְשָׁדוּ לְהוּ לְדִיקְלָא בְּלִיבֵּיהּ, וְכׇל דְּקָאֵי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דִּידֵיהּ, אִי לָא עָבְדִי לֵיהּ הָכִי — צָאוֵי לְאַלְתַּר. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: מַנְּחִי כּוּפְרָא דִּיכְרָא לְנוּקְבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **They** would **graft palm trees the entire day** of the fourteenth of Nisan. The Gemara asks: **How did they perform** this grafting? **Rabbi Yehuda said: They brought fresh myrtle, strong beer** made **from the** fruit of the **laurel tree, and barley flour that was cast into a vessel, and forty days has not passed** since it was ground. **They boiled them** together **and poured** the mixture **into the core of the palm tree.** And for **any** tree **standing within four cubits of that** tree, **if they did not perform this** treatment with it, **it would immediately wither** because the tree that received the treatment would grow faster at the expense of the surrounding trees. **Rav Aḥa, son** of **Rava, said: They placed a branch of a male palm tree on the female,** and by doing so the female tree would yield fruit.

Having introduced the practice of *markivin dekalim* in the Jericho *baraita*, the *Gemara* now inquires *heikhi avid* — what exactly does this grafting procedure involve? Rav Yehuda describes the following method: one takes a moist myrtle branch (*asa dara*), combines it with a resinous sap from a particular tree (*shikhla de-dafna*), and barley flour that has been sitting in a vessel for fewer than forty days since grinding (*kimcha de-sa'arei de-rami be-mana de-lo khalfei aleih arba'in yomin*). This mixture is then heated and poured into the heart of the palm tree. Rav Yehuda adds that any palm trees standing within four *amot* of the treated tree that do not receive a similar treatment will immediately wither. This procedure, then, is less a conventional graft than a medicinal or restorative treatment applied to the tree.

### Rav Acha Method

Rav Acha the son of Rava offers a different description: *manchi kufra dikhra le-nukveta* — one takes a branch from a male palm and inserts it into a corresponding hole bored into a branch of a female palm. This is a genuine graft in the more familiar sense of the term. One might ask whether this constitutes *kilayim* — the forbidden crossbreeding of species — but since both trees are palm trees, no such prohibition applies.

## Wrapping Shema

### Shema V'ahavta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוֹרְכִין אֶת שְׁמַע. הֵיכִי עָבְדִי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אוֹמְרִים ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד״, וְלֹא הָיוּ מַפְסִיקִין. רָבָא אָמַר: מַפְסִיקִין הָיוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״הַיּוֹם עַל לְבָבֶךָ״, דְּמַשְׁמַע: הַיּוֹם עַל לְבָבֶךָ, וְלֹא מָחָר עַל לְבָבֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that the residents of Jericho would **bundle *Shema*.** The Gemara asks: What does it mean that they bundled *Shema*? **How did they do** so? **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **they recited: "Hear Israel: The Lord is our God, the Lord is One**" (Deuteronomy 6:4), **and they would not pause** between words. **Rava said: They** would **pause** between words, **but** instead of reciting this verse in the proper manner: "That which I command you today, shall be on your heart" (Deuteronomy 6:5), pausing after the word today; **they would say: Today shall be on your heart,** inferring: **Today it will be on your heart, and tomorrow it will not be on your heart.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the second practice of the people of Jericho: *korchin et shema* — wrapping the *Shema*. The concern is the transition between *Shema Yisrael* and *Ve'ahavta*. These two passages are fundamentally different in character: the *Shema* is a declaration of faith, an assertion, while *Ve'ahavta* is a commandment — not a statement of belief but a statement of obligation. A break between them is therefore required. The practice of the people of Jericho was to run from one directly into the other without such a break.

### Rashi Break

Rashi explains that the break is necessary because the two sections are qualitatively distinct. The *Shema* functions as a kind of *ani maamin*, an affirmation addressed to oneself and to one's fellow Jews. *Ve'ahavta*, by contrast, returns to the standard language of Torah obligation. The transition from one mode to the other demands a pause.

### Rava Pause

Rava offers a different reading of the *korchin* practice. He maintains that the people of Jericho did in fact pause between *Shema* and *Ve'ahavta* — *mafsikin hayu*. Their error, however, lay elsewhere: *ella shehayu omrim hayom al levavecha* — they combined the phrase *asher anochi metzavecha hayom al levavecha* in a way that implied "today it shall be upon your heart, but not tomorrow," distorting its meaning by an irregular break in the verse.

### Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד הָיוּ כּוֹרְכִין אֶת שְׁמַע? אוֹמְרִים ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד״, וְלֹא הָיוּ מַפְסִיקִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מַפְסִיקִין הָיוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״בָּרוּךְ שֵׁם כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **How would they bundle *Shema*? They recited: "Hear Israel, the Lord is our God the Lord is One," without pausing;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: They paused, but they would not recite: Blessed be the name of His glorious kingdom for ever and ever.**

A *baraita* is cited that maps this dispute back to a *tannaic* debate. Rabbi Meir holds that the practice of *korchin* meant *velo hayu mafsikin* — they would recite *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* and flow straight into *Ve'ahavta* without any pause whatsoever. Rabbi Yehuda holds *mafsikin hayu* — they did pause — *ella shelo hayu omrim Baruch Shem kevod malchuto l'olam va'ed* — but they did not insert the *Baruch Shem* as the break. Both *tannaim* agree that *korchin* denotes an insufficient separation between *Shema* and *Ve'ahavta*; they differ on whether the deficiency was a complete absence of any break or merely the omission of *Baruch Shem*.

### Jacob Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנַן, מַאי טַעְמָא אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ? כִּדְדָרֵישׁ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: ״וַיִּקְרָא יַעֲקֹב אֶל בָּנָיו וַיֹּאמֶר הֵאָסְפוּ וְאַגִּידָה לָכֶם״. בִּיקֵּשׁ יַעֲקֹב לְגַלּוֹת לְבָנָיו קֵץ הַיָּמִין, וְנִסְתַּלְּקָה מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁכִינָה. אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא חַס וְשָׁלוֹם יֵשׁ בְּמִטָּתִי פְּסוּל, כְּאַבְרָהָם שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנּוּ יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְאָבִי יִצְחָק שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנּוּ עֵשָׂו? אָמְרוּ לוֹ בָּנָיו: ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל, ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד״. אָמְרוּ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין בְּלִבְּךָ אֶלָּא אֶחָד, כָּךְ אֵין בְּלִבֵּנוּ אֶלָּא אֶחָד. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה פָּתַח יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ וְאָמַר: ״בָּרוּךְ שֵׁם כְּבוֹד מַלְכוּתוֹ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason that we recite** that passage: Blessed be the name of His glorious kingdom for ever and ever, even though it does not appear in the Torah? The Gemara answers: We recite it **in accordance with** that **which Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish interpreted homiletically. As Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said** that it is written: **"And Jacob called his sons and said, Gather around and I will tell you** what will occur to you in the end of days" (Genesis 49:1). **Jacob wanted to reveal to his sons** when the complete redemption would arrive at **the end of days** (see Daniel 12:13), **but the Divine Presence abandoned him,** rendering him unable to prophesy. **He said: Perhaps** the Divine Presence has abandoned me because, **Heaven forfend, one of my descendants is unfit, as** was the case with my grandfather **Abraham, from whom Ishmael emerged, and** like **my father Isaac, from whom Esau emerged. His sons said to him: Hear Israel,** our father, **the Lord is our God, the Lord is One. They said: Just as there is only one** God **in your heart, so too, there is only one in our hearts. At that moment Jacob our father said** in praise: **Blessed be the name of His glorious kingdom for ever and ever,** as all his children were righteous.

The *Gemara* then asks why we ourselves recite *Baruch Shem kevod malchuto l'olam va'ed* at all, given that it does not appear in the text of the Torah. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish expounds the following *midrash*: the verse states *vayikra Yaakov el banav vayomer he'asvu ve'agidah lachem et asher yikra etchem be'acharit hayamim* — Jacob called his sons and said, "Gather, and I will tell you what will befall you at the end of days." Yet what follows in the Torah is not a prophecy of the end of days but a series of blessings, descriptions, and pronouncements. The *Gemara* understands this gap by saying that *bikeish Yaakov legalot le-vanav keitz hayamin* — Jacob sought to reveal the end of days to his sons, but *venistalkah mimenu Shechinah* — the divine presence departed from him at that moment, preventing the revelation.

Jacob was seized by anxiety: *shema chas ve-shalom yesh be-mitati pasul* — perhaps, God forbid, one among his descendants is unworthy, as had been the case with Abraham, from whom Ishmael had come forth, and with his father Isaac, from whom Esau had emerged. In response, his sons declared: *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* — "Hear, O Israel" — addressing Jacob by his name Israel — "the Lord is our God, the Lord is one." They were assuring him: just as for you there is only one God, so too for us — we are entirely faithful. Upon hearing this, Jacob responded: *Baruch Shem kevod malchuto l'olam va'ed* — blessed is the name of His glorious kingdom forever, an expression of relief and gratitude that all his sons were true believers.

### Resolution Softly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן: הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? נֵאמְרֵיהּ, לֹא אֲמָרוֹ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ. לָא נֵאמְרֵיהּ, אֲמָרוֹ יַעֲקֹב. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ אוֹמְרִים אוֹתוֹ בַּחֲשַׁאי. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מָשָׁל לְבַת מֶלֶךְ שֶׁהֵרִיחָה צִיקֵי קְדֵירָה. אִם תֹּאמַר — יֵשׁ לָהּ גְּנַאי. לֹא תֹּאמַר — יֵשׁ לָהּ צַעַר. הִתְחִילוּ עֲבָדֶיהָ לְהָבִיא בַּחֲשַׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said: What should we do? Shall we recite** this verse? But **Moses our teacher did not say it** in the Torah as part of *Shema*. **Shall we not recite it?** But **Jacob said it.** In order to resolve this dilemma **they established that** this passage **should be recited surreptitiously. Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **the school of Rabbi Ami said:** This is **analogous to the daughter of a king who smelled** the fragrance of the dried **spices** stuck to the bottom of **the pot** and craved to eat them. What can she do? **If she tells** her servants to give it to her, **she will be disgraced,** as the dried spices are a contemptible food. However, if **she does not say** she wants to eat them, **she will** endure **suffering. Her servants began to bring** them to her **surreptitiously.** One should conduct himself in that manner in similar cases of uncertainty.

The Sages then faced a practical dilemma: *heikhi na'avid* — what should we do with *Baruch Shem*? If we recite it, *lo amaro Moshe Rabbeinu* — Moses did not include it in the Torah text. If we omit it, *amaro Yaakov* — Jacob said it, and it has this powerful responsive function in the context of the *Shema*. The resolution was *hitkinu sheyehu omrim oto bechashai* — they ordained that it be recited quietly. In this way, it fulfills its function as a break and a response to the declaration of *Shema*, while maintaining the integrity of the Torah text as it flows from *Shema* to *Ve'ahavta*. A *mashal* is offered: it is like a princess who caught the scent of a pot of humble stew and desired it. To give it to her openly would be beneath her dignity; to refuse her would cause her distress. So her servants brought it to her quietly. Similarly, *Baruch Shem* is recited — because it belongs there and serves an important purpose — but it is said in a whisper, so as not to visibly disrupt the flow of the Torah's text.

### Minim Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ אוֹמְרִים אוֹתוֹ בְּקוֹל רָם, מִפְּנֵי תַּרְעוֹמֶת הַמִּינִין. וּבִנְהַרְדְּעָא, דְּלֵיכָּא מִינִין, עַד הַשְׁתָּא אָמְרִי לַהּ בַּחֲשַׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said:** The Sages **instituted that** the people **should recite it aloud due to the grievance of the heretics.** It was instituted to prevent the heretics from claiming that the Jews are surreptitiously reciting inappropriate statements. The Gemara adds: **In Neharde'a, where there are no heretics, they recite it surreptitiously even now.**

Rabbi Abbahu adds a further historical note: *hitkinu sheyihyu omrim oto bekol ram mipnei taromet ha-minim* — at some point an ordinance was issued to recite *Baruch Shem* aloud, on account of the provocations of the *minim*, the heretics. Rashi explains that the *minim* would mock the practice of whispering, accusing the Sages of inserting words into the liturgy and then being embarrassed by them. To counter this charge, the practice in some communities was to say *Baruch Shem* loudly, demonstrating that there was no shame in the recitation. The *Gemara* notes that in Nehardea, where there were no *minim*, the practice remained to say it quietly. Over time, of course, the universal practice returned to reciting it in a whisper, as we do today — except on *Yom Kippur*, when it is said aloud.

## Jericho Mishna

### Mishna No Protest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים עָשׂוּ אַנְשֵׁי יְרִיחוֹ, שְׁלֹשָׁה בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים. וְאֵלּוּ בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים: מַרְכִּיבִין דְּקָלִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם, וְכוֹרְכִין אֶת שְׁמַע, וְקוֹצְרִין לִפְנֵי הָעוֹמֶר. וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים: גּוֹדְשִׁין לִפְנֵי הָעוֹמֶר, וּפוֹרְצִין פְּרָצוֹת בְּגַנּוֹתֵיהֶן וּבְפַרְדְּסוֹתֵיהֶן לְהַאֲכִיל נֶשֶׁר לַעֲנִיִּים בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת, וּמַתִּירִין גַּמְזִיּוֹת שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁל חָרוּב וְשֶׁל שִׁקְמָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a related matter in the *Tosefta*: **The people of Jericho performed six actions, three** in keeping **with the will of the Sages and three against the will of the Sages. And these** are what they did in keeping **with the will of the Sages: They** would **graft palm trees the entire day** of the fourteenth of Nisan; **and they** would **bundle *Shema*; and they** would **harvest** grain **before the *omer*** offering was brought. **And these** are what they did **against the will of the Sages: They** would **pile** the harvest **before the *omer*; and they** would **make breaches in** the walls of **their gardens and their orchards to feed fallen** fruit **to the poor during drought years,** so that the poor could take the fruit that had fallen **on Shabbat and Festivals; and they** would **permit** the use of **consecrated branches of carob and of sycamore** trees. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *Gemara* now presents a parallel *baraita* that restates the Jericho practices with some variation from the *mishna*, and the *mishna* itself then provides a summary. Both agree on the three practices against which the Sages did not protest: *markivin dekalim kol hayom* — attending to the palm trees the entire day; *korchin et shema* — wrapping the *Shema*; and *vekotzrin vegodshin lifnei ha'omer* — both the cutting and the bundling of grain before the *Omer*. The *baraita* separates the cutting from the bundling and treats only the cutting as unprotested; the bundling (*godshin*) is listed among the disputed practices, on the grounds that binding grain into sheaves creates greater concern that one will come to eat from it before the *Omer* is brought. The *mishna*, however, groups both cutting and bundling together among the practices the Sages did not protest.

### Mishna Protest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אִם בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין, יְהוּ כׇּל אָדָם עוֹשִׂין כֵּן. אֶלָּא: אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן חֲכָמִים הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין, עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה מִיחוּ בְּיָדָם, וְעַל שְׁלֹשָׁה לֹא מִיחוּ בְּיָדָם. וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁלֹּא מִיחוּ בְּיָדָם: מַרְכִּיבִין דְּקָלִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם, וְכוֹרְכִין אֶת שְׁמַע, וְקוֹצְרִין וְגוֹדְשִׁין לִפְנֵי הָעוֹמֶר. וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁמִּיחוּ בְּיָדָם: מַתִּירִין גַּמְזִיּוֹת שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁל חָרוּב וְשֶׁל שִׁקְמָה, וּפוֹרְצִין פְּרָצוֹת בְּגַנּוֹתֵיהֶן וּפַרְדְּסֵיהֶן לְהַאֲכִיל נֶשֶׁר לַעֲנִיִּים בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים בִּשְׁנֵי בַצּוֹרֶת, נוֹתְנִין פֵּיאָה לַיָּרָק, וּמִיחוּ בְּיָדָם חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said to him:** It is inaccurate to formulate it in that manner, as **if they acted** in keeping **with the will of the Sages, all people would do so,** not only the residents of Jericho. **Rather,** formulate it in this manner: Both **these** three acts **and those** three **were performed against the will of the Sages. With regard to three** the Sages **reprimanded them; and with regard to three** the Sages **did not reprimand them.** Since one could contend that the latter are permitted and the people of Jericho had already performed them, the Sages chose not to reprimand them. **And these** are what they did for which the Sages **did not reprimand them: They** would **graft palm trees the entire day; and they** would **bundle *Shema*; and they** would **harvest and pile** grain **before the *omer*** offering was brought. **And these** are what they did for which the Sages **reprimanded them: They** would **permit** the use of **consecrated branches of carob and of sycamore** trees; **they** would **make breaches in** the walls of **their gardens and orchards on Shabbat and Festivals, in order to feed the poor fallen fruit during drought years; and they** would **designate** for the poor **the** produce in the **corner** in a field of **vegetables. And the Sages reprimanded them** for those actions.

Rabbi Yehuda raises a structural objection to the *baraita*'s formulation: if the Sages truly acquiesced in these practices, *kol adam osim ken* — why was it only the people of Jericho who followed them? If the Sages endorsed the practice, everyone should have adopted it. He therefore maintains *ella eilu ve'eilu shelo birtzon chachamim hayu osim* — in truth, the Sages disapproved of all six practices. The distinction in the *mishna* is not between approval and disapproval, but between active protest and silence: *al sheloshah miche b'yadam ve'al sheloshah lo miche* — three they formally protested, three they did not.

The *mishna* lists the three practices the Sages did formally protest. First, *matirin gamziyot shel hekdesh shel charuv veshel shikma* — permitting the new growths from sanctified carob and sycamore trees; these should remain *hekdesh* and may not simply be declared permitted. Second, *portzin pratzot begannotayhen u'fardesayhen le'ha'achil nesher la'aniyim bishabbatot ve'yamim tovim bishnei batzoret* — breaching the fences of their gardens and orchards so that the poor could enter on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* in years of famine to eat from produce that had fallen. While the motivation was charitable, consuming fallen produce on *Shabbat* raises concerns of *muktzeh* or other *Shabbat* prohibitions, and accordingly the Sages protested. Third, *notnim pe'ah leyarak* — designating *pe'ah* from vegetables. As discussed, vegetables are not biblically subject to *pe'ah*, and the unilateral designation of *pe'ah* from them does not exempt the produce from *terumot u'ma'asrot*; the poor thus consume *tevel*, and for this reason the Sages protested.
