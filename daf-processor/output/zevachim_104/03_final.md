# Zevachim 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Skin & Meat Separation

### Intro: Physical Unity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁאֵירַע בָּהֶן פְּסוּל קוֹדֶם לְהֶפְשֵׁיטָן – אֵין עוֹרוֹתֵיהֶן לַכֹּהֲנִים. לְאַחַר הֶפְשֵׁיטָן – עוֹרוֹתֵיהֶן לְכֹהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **any offerings** of the most sacred order **were disqualified prior to their flaying, their hides do not** go **to the priests;** rather, they are burned together with the flesh in the place of burning. If they were disqualified **after their flaying, their hides** go **to the priests.**

The *mishnah* we are studying appears on 103b, and it shifts our focus from the question of which *kohanim* receive a portion of the meat to the question of who receives the *or* — the skin. The earlier *mishnah* had established that if the *mizbeach* is not *zocheh* in the *basar*, the *kohanim* do not receive the skin. The operative moment for the *mizbeach*'s entitlement to the meat of an *olah* is *zrikat hadam*: if the *korban* became invalid before the blood was sprinkled — as in the case of *pigul* — the *kohanim* do not receive the skin; if it became invalid afterward — for instance, the meat became *tamei* — they do, since the *mizbeach* had already acquired its entitlement to the meat.

Our *mishnah*, however, takes a strikingly different approach. It states: *kol hakodashim she'eira bahen pasul kodem lehefshetan ein oroteihem lakohanim* — all *kodashim* that became invalid before they were flayed, their skins do not go to the *kohanim*. The determining factor here is not *zrikat hadam* but physical unity: whether the skin and the meat still constitute a single, undivided entity. If the animal became invalid while still whole and unflayed, the skin follows the meat and is not given to the *kohanim*. If it became invalid after flaying — for example, the meat became *tamei* or was removed from the *Beit Hamikdash* — the skin and meat are already separate units, and *oroteihem lakohanim*: the *kohanim* do receive the skin. There is no mention of *zrikat hadam*; the sole criterion is whether the two are physically combined or apart.

This apparent contradiction with the earlier *mishnah* is what the *gemara* will now work to resolve.

### R. Chanina: No Separate…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים: מִיָּמַי לֹא רָאִיתִי עוֹר שֶׁיּוֹצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, said: In** all **my days, I never saw a hide going out to the place of burning.**

The *mishnah* then cites a testimony from *Rebbe Chanina sgan hakohanim* — the deputy *kohen gadol*, a figure of considerable authority who was regularly present in the *Beit Hamikdash*. He states: *miyamai lo ra'iti or sheyotzei leveit hasreifa* — never in all my days did I see a skin taken out to the *beit hasreifa*. This is a remarkable testimony. It implies that in situations where the *korban* became invalid and the *kohanim* could not receive the skin, that skin would need to be ritually burned — yet he says he never witnessed this occurring with the skin alone, separately from the meat. As the *gemara* will emphasize, *zrikat hadam* is what permits the skin, and a skin belonging to an invalid *korban* retains its *kedusha* and must be burned rather than discarded or sold.

The *gemara* understands *Rebbe Chanina* as reaffirming the position of the *Tanna Kamma*: once the animal has been flayed, the skin will never thereafter become invalid on its own. He never saw the skin taken out separately to the *beit hasreifa* — though of course the skin was sometimes taken out still attached to the meat before flaying — because once flaying has occurred, the skin is already secure. This is a positive affirmation that the moment of flaying is the determining factor.

### R. Akiva's Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִדְּבָרָיו לָמַדְנוּ, שֶׁהַמַּפְשִׁיט אֶת הַבְּכוֹר וְנִמְצָא טְרֵיפָה – שֶׁיֵּאוֹתוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים בְּעוֹרוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין ״לֹא רָאִינוּ״ רְאָיָה, אֶלָּא יֵצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said: From the statement of** Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, **we learned that** in a case where **one flays the firstborn** offering, **and** the animal is later **discovered to have a wound that would have caused it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** the *halakha* is **that the priests may derive benefit [*sheye'otu*] from its hide. And the Rabbis say:** The claim: **We did not see, is no proof; rather,** if after flaying it is discovered that the animal was unfit before it was flayed, the hide **goes out to the place of burning.**

*Amar Rebbe Akiva*: from *Rebbe Chanina*'s words we can learn that if one flays a *bechor* and it is then discovered to be a *treifa*, *she'ye'outu hakohanim be'oro* — the *kohanim* are entitled to its skin. *Rebbe Akiva* takes one critical step further: what happens when the invalidity preceded the flaying, but was only discovered after the fact?

Consider the typical case: an animal is slaughtered, flayed, and then, while the *kohen* is eviscerating it and arranging the entrails for the *mizbeach*, he discovers a hole in the lung — a *treifa*. Most *treifot* are internal and invisible from the outside. The invalidity existed from before the animal was flayed. Yet since it was only discovered after flaying, does the skin still go to the *kohanim*?

*Rebbe Akiva* reasons from *Rebbe Chanina*'s statement: if *Rebbe Chanina* says the skin was never taken out separately to be burned, this must have encompassed even such cases. Because it is entirely possible — indeed, likely — that animals were slaughtered, blood was sprinkled, they were flayed, and then found to be *treifot*. Yet *Rebbe Chanina* says the skin was never taken out separately. The only coherent explanation is that the status of the skin is determined not by when the invalidity came into existence, but by when it was discovered. Since the *treifa* was discovered after flaying, the *kohanim* retain the skin. This is, as is frankly acknowledged, a counterintuitive *halacha*: since when does the moment of discovery govern? Nevertheless, this principle appears to apply specifically to the skin, which has an ambiguous status relative to the *korban* itself — the meat's invalidity does not automatically and retroactively divest the *kohanim* of what has already, in terms of timing, become theirs. One finds a partial analogue in the principle, discussed by the *Rosh*, that *bitul* in the laws of *kashrus* can in certain respects be conditioned on the moment of awareness.

Importantly, this discovery-based rule does not extend to the meat. If the blood was sprinkled and the animal was then found to be a *treifa*, one would not declare the *korban* valid on the grounds that the *treifa* was discovered only afterward. The rule applies solely to the skin, a further indication of the skin's unique, partly detached status vis-à-vis the *korban*.

### Sages Reject Proof

The *chachamim* respond with a pointed methodological objection — *lo reinu re'ayah*. The fact that *Rebbe Chanina* never witnessed a skin being burned separately proves nothing. Perhaps such cases were simply too infrequent, or occurred when he was not present. A man of great seniority and authority he may have been — the *sgan hakohanim* was unlike an ordinary *kohen* who served only two weeks a year, and so his testimony carries particular weight — yet the *chachamim* insist: absence of evidence is not evidence. One is reminded of the methodological dispute between the *Ba'al HaMaor* and the *Ramban*, where a similar argument was made that *lo ra'inu* proves nothing, to which the response was *kan ra'inu* — here, at least, we have seen it. The *chachamim* are not dismissing *mesora* as a category; they are simply refusing to draw halachic inferences from a negative observation. The skin, they maintain, goes to be burned whenever the invalidity existed before flaying — regardless of when it was discovered.

## Two Mishnayot Reconciled

### First Mishnah: Blood Sta…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֶׁלֹּא זָכָה הַמִּזְבֵּחַ בִּבְשָׂרָהּ – לֹא זָכוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים בְּעוֹרָהּ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאַפְּשָׁטֵיהּ לְעוֹר קוֹדֶם זְרִיקָה. מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין הַדָּם מְרַצֶּה עַל הָעוֹר בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The previous mishna (103a) teaches: In the case of **any** burnt offering **for which the altar did not acquire its flesh,** e.g., if it was disqualified prior to the sprinkling of its blood, **the priests did not acquire its hide.** The mishna does not state any qualification, indicating that this is the *halakha* **even if** the priest **flayed the hide before** the **sprinkling** of the blood on the altar. The Gemara posits: **Whose** opinion is this? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who says: The blood does not effect acceptance of the hide by itself;** i.e., it effects acceptance of the hide only together with the flesh. Since the flesh is disqualified and the sprinkling does not effect its acceptance, the sprinkling does not effect acceptance for the hide either.

The *gemara* now turns to the explicit tension between the two *mishnayot*. The earlier *mishnah* states: *kol shelo zachah mizbeach bvesarah lo zachu kohanim beorah* — wherever the *mizbeach* was not *zocheh* in the meat, the *kohanim* are not *zocheh* in the skin. And it adds: *ve-af al gav de'ifashtah le-or kodem zrikah* — even if the skin was flayed before *zrikah*, that is insufficient; the entitlement depends on *zrikat hadam*, not on flaying.

The *gemara* asks: whose opinion is that? It is *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* — and we will recall from earlier in the *masechet* that he is the one who said *ein adam meratzeh al ha-or bifnei atzmo*: one does not sprinkle the blood for the sake of the skin alone. By an *olah*, the purpose of the blood is to permit the meat to go on the *mizbeach*. If the meat became *tamei* and all that remains is the skin, one would not sprinkle the blood — and if one did, that sprinkling would not render the skin permissible. For *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*, the *mizbeach*'s entitlement and the *kohanim*'s entitlement are linked through *zrikat hadam*: *mishulchan gavoah zachu* — the *kohanim* receive only what derives from the *shulchan gavoah*'s own entitlement.

Now the *gemara* asks — *eima seifa* — let us look at the end of our *mishnah*. It states: *kol hakodashim she-hayu bahem p'sul kodem hefshetan eino osam lakohanim* — invalidity before flaying means no skin for the *kohanim*. And conversely, *le'achar hefshetan oram lakohanim* — after flaying, the *kohanim* are entitled to the skin. Importantly, this appears to apply even before the blood has been sprinkled: you flayed the animal, the meat became invalid, and even though *zrikat hadam* has not yet occurred — the *kohanim* are going to receive the skin. This is because, presumably, *zrikat hadam* will then be performed, and the skin's entitlement is secured. That sounds like *atya k'Rebbi* — it follows the view of *Rebbi*, who holds that *ha-dam meratzeh al ha-or bifnei atzmo*: the blood can work for the skin even when the meat is no longer present. So the *seifa* sounds like *Rebbi* and the *reisha* sounds like *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* — which creates a contradiction within the structure of the two *mishnayot*.

### Abaye's Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי הִיא, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי רַבִּי הִיא; וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי שֶׁאֵין הֶפְשֵׁט קוֹדֶם זְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Since the latter clause** is the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, it must be that **the former clause is also** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And** although Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that in principle the priests should acquire the hides if they are removed before the flesh is disqualified, in any case **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes that** the **flaying is not** done **before** the **sprinkling.** Since the offering cannot be disqualified before the hide is removed, practically speaking, the priests will never acquire the hides unless the altar acquires the flesh, as taught in the former clause.

*Amar Abaye*: since the *seifa* of the second *mishnah* follows *Rebbi*, the *reisha* — that is, the first *mishnah* — is also *Rebbi*. And *Rebbi* concedes: *she'ein hafshait kodem zrikah* — in normal practice, flaying does not precede *zrikah*. The procedure in the *Beit Hamikdash* was: slaughter, collect the blood, perform *zrikat hadam*, and only then process the meat, disembember the carcass, and flay the skin. In practice, the *kohanim* would never be entitled to the skin before *zrikat hadam* — not because *zrikat hadam* is the conceptual trigger, but because flaying always occurred after it. Thus when the first *mishnah* articulates the rule in terms of *zrikat hadam*, it is not establishing *zrikah* as the principled criterion; it is simply reflecting the practical reality that flaying always comes after *zrikat hadam*. The true principle is the moment of flaying, as stated in our *mishnah*, and the first *mishnah* is accurate only as a description of regular practice. Had someone flayed the animal before *zrikat hadam* — which never happened — our *mishnah*'s rule would govern, and the *kohanim* would receive the skin.

This reconciliation, it must be said, is somewhat counterintuitive. The first *mishnah* reads as though it is articulating a substantive principle about *zricat hadam*, not merely describing a procedural sequence. But that is how *Abaye* reads it.

### Rava's Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִדְּרֵישָׁא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, סֵיפָא נָמֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן; מַאי קוֹדֶם הֶפְשֵׁט – קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּרְאוּ לְהֶפְשֵׁט, אַחַר שֶׁנִּרְאוּ לְהֶפְשֵׁט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** On the contrary, **since the former clause** is the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** it must be that **the latter clause is also** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.** When the mishna states that the priests acquire the hides if the flesh was disqualified after the flaying, it must mean that the flesh was disqualified after the sprinkling. Therefore, **what** does the mishna mean by the phrase: **Before flaying?** It means **before** the moment the offerings **became fit for flaying,** and **after** the moment **they became fit for flaying,** i.e., before and after the sprinkling of the blood.

*Rava amar*: the opposite approach. Since the *reisha* of the two *mishnayot* reflects *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*, the *seifa* is also *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*. For him, *zrikat hadam* is the true criterion — and when our *mishnah* speaks of "before or after flaying," it does not mean the physical act of flaying literally. Rather, *kodem sheniru lehafshait, achar sheniru lehafshait* — "before or after it was appropriate to flay." And it is appropriate to flay only after *zricat hadam*. Indeed, *Rava* goes further: according to *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*, one would never flay the skin prematurely precisely because the skin only acquires its independent status through the *basar* — it has no separate significance prior to *zricat hadam*. Therefore, "before flaying" in our *mishnah* simply means before *zricat hadam*, because that is the moment after which flaying becomes appropriate.

On this reading, both *mishnayot* articulate the same principle — *zricat hadam* is determinative — and "before or after flaying" is merely an alternative formulation of the same idea. In the end, the two *mishnayot* appear to present genuinely different principles, and the *gemara* is working to read them in harmony with one opinion — though the most natural reading is that they represent different *tannaitic* positions: one that goes by *zricat hadam* and one that goes by *hefshait*.

## Rebbi vs. R. Eliezer b.…

### Rebbi: Blood Works for S…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: הַדָּם מְרַצֶּה עַל הָעוֹר בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. וּכְשֶׁהוּא עִם הַבָּשָׂר, נוֹלַד בּוֹ פְּסוּל בֵּין קוֹדֶם זְרִיקָה בֵּין לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – הֲרֵי הוּא כְּיוֹצֵא בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What** is the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and what** is the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon?** Their opinions are **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: The blood effects acceptance of the hide by itself,** after it has been flayed, even if the flesh is disqualified. **And** if, **when** the hide **is** still **with the flesh, a disqualification appears on** the flesh, **whether before** the **sprinkling** of the blood **or after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, then the *halakha* with regard to the hide **is parallel to** the *halakha* with regard to the flesh: Both are burned.

*De-tanya*: what exactly is the debate between *Rebbi* and *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*? *Rebbi omer*: *ha-dam meratzeh al ha-or bifnei atzmo* — the blood atones for, meaning renders permissible for the *kohanim*, the skin even when it stands alone. That is: after the skin has been flayed and is physically separate from the meat, if the meat became *tamei* or otherwise invalid, one may still sprinkle the blood and the *kohanim* receive the skin. Moreover — *u-k'shehu im habasar* — when the skin is still physically attached to the meat and not yet flayed, *nolad bo pasul bein kodem zrikah bein l'achar zrikah harei hu k'yotzei bo*: whether the invalidity arose before or after *zrikah*, as long as the skin was still joined to the meat, its status follows the meat's status entirely. This is a beautifully clear articulation of our *mishnah*'s principle: physical separation is everything. If the skin is a separate unit, the blood can work for it independently regardless of the meat's condition. If the skin is still part of the meat, it travels with the meat in every direction.

### R. E.b.S.: Blood Fails S…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין הַדָּם מְרַצֶּה עַל הָעוֹר בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. וּכְשֶׁהוּא עִם הַבָּשָׂר – נוֹלַד בּוֹ פְּסוּל קוֹדֶם זְרִיקָה, הֲרֵי הוּא כְּיוֹצֵא בּוֹ. אַחַר זְרִיקָה, הוּרְצָה בָּשָׂר שָׁעָה אַחַת; יַפְשִׁיטֶנּוּ, וְעוֹרוֹ לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: The blood does not effect acceptance of the hide by itself. And** if, **when** the hide **is** still **with the flesh, a disqualification appears on** the flesh **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, then the *halakha* with regard to the hide **is parallel to** the *halakha* with regard to the flesh: Both are burned. If a disqualification develops on the flesh **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, the **flesh was** already **accepted for a time.** Therefore, even though the flesh is disqualified, the priest **may flay** the animal before it is burned, **and its hide** goes **to the priests.**

*Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon omer*: *ein hadam meratzeh al ha-or bifnei atzmo* — the blood does not work for the skin by itself. Even when the skin has been physically separated through flaying, if the meat was already *tamei* or invalid at the time of *zricat hadam*, the sprinkling does not help the skin. The reason is structural: by an *olah*, the function of *zricat hadam* is to permit the meat to go on the *mizbeach*. If the meat is invalid, the blood is not being thrown for the sake of anything meaningful, and whatever status the sprinkling might confer on the skin is parasitic on the meat's validity. For *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*, the physical separation of skin from meat is irrelevant; what governs is the status of the meat at the moment of *zricat hadam*. If the meat was valid when the blood was sprinkled — *hurtza basar sha'ah achat* — then even if the meat subsequently became invalid, *yafshitenu ve'oro lakohanim*: you flay it and the skin goes to the *kohanim*. If the meat was already invalid at the time of sprinkling, the skin does not go to the *kohanim* regardless of whether it has been flayed.

### Invalid Meat Application

In application, then: according to *Rebbi*, the question is whether the skin was physically separated from the meat at the point of invalidity. According to *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*, the question is whether the meat was valid at the moment of *zricat hadam*. These are genuinely different principles, and each author, as the *gemara* notes, is the natural author of one of the two *mishnayot*. The most straightforward reading is that the two *mishnayot* simply represent these two positions, and the *gemara*'s attempts to read them as consistent — through *Abaye*'s and *Rava*'s reconciliations — are exercises in harmonization rather than reflections of the plain sense.

## R. Eliezer & R. Yehoshua

### R. Yehoshua: Interdepend…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּדְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ קָמִיפַּלְגִי? ״וְעָשִׂיתָ עֹלֹתֶיךָ הַבָּשָׂר וְהַדָּם״ – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אִם אֵין דָּם אֵין בָּשָׂר, אִם אֵין בָּשָׂר אֵין דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Elazar **disagree about** the same principle as do **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua?** As it is taught in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall offer your burnt offerings, the flesh and the blood,** upon the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:27). **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The verse teaches that **if there is no blood** sprinkled on the altar, **no flesh** may be burned on the altar, and **if there is no flesh** to be burned on the altar, **no blood** may be sprinkled on the altar.

The *gemara* now asks whether the debate of *Rebbi* and *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* is rooted in an earlier dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua*, which concerns the validity of the *korban* itself when the meat cannot be offered. The connecting *pasuk* is: *va'asita olotecha ha-basar veha-dam* — you shall perform your *olot*, the meat and the blood. *Rabbi Yehoshua* reads this as linking the two: *im ein dam ein basar, im ein basar ein dam*. Evidently if there is no blood there is no valid *korban*, since *zricat hadam* is the essential act. But *Rabbi Yehoshua* goes further: if there is no meat, there is also no blood — that is, there is no point in sprinkling the blood of an *olah* if the meat cannot be placed on the *mizbeach*. Just as by a *pesach* the eating is so central that one would not bring it if unable to eat, so too by an *olah* there is a fundamental weight on the meat reaching the *mizbeach*, and if it cannot, one does not sprinkle the blood. The consequence, presumably, is that the *korban* is invalid, and the *kohanim* certainly do not receive the skin.

### R. Eliezer: Blood Indepe…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: דָּם – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בָּשָׂר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ עֹלֹתֶיךָ הַבָּשָׂר וְהַדָּם״? לוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה דָּם בִּזְרִיקָה, אַף בָּשָׂר בִּזְרִיקָה. הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁרֶיוַח יֵשׁ בֵּין כֶּבֶשׁ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The **blood** must be sprinkled **even if there is no flesh, as it is stated** in the continuation of the verse: **"And the blood of your sacrifices shall be poured out** against the altar of the Lord your God, and you shall eat the flesh." **If so, what** is taught when **the verse states: "And you shall offer your burnt offerings, the flesh and the blood"?** It is stated **to tell you: Just as blood** is placed on the altar **by sprinkling, so too,** the **flesh** is placed on the altar **by tossing. Consequently, you learn that there is a space between** the **ramp and the altar,** such that the priest must toss the flesh from the edge of the ramp.

*Rabbi Eliezer omer*: *dam af al pi she'ein basar* — blood is independent of meat; you may and should sprinkle the blood even if the meat has become invalid. His source is *ve-dam zevachecha yishafech* — the blood of your sacrifices shall be poured — emphasizing the blood as an autonomous focus. The difficulty is that *zevachecha* normally does not refer to the *olah*, and the full verse continues *ve-habasar tochel*, which does connect the two — but *Rabbi Eliezer* reads the blood as having independent standing even for the *olah*. *Im kein ma talmud lomar va'asita olotecha ha-basar veha-dam*? That *pasuk* teaches something else: *mah dam bi-zrikah af basar bi-zrikah* — just as the blood is thrown upon the *mizbeach*, so too the meat is thrown. This teaches that there is a *revach* — a gap — between the *kevesh* and the *mizbeach*, from which the *kohen* would throw the pieces of meat.

## R. Eliezer & R. Yehosh…

### Gemara Equations Debates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַאן דְּאָמַר הוּרְצָה – כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא הוּרְצָה – כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ? אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי; כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Shall we say** that **the one who says** that the hide **is accepted** independent of the flesh holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** that the blood is sprinkled independent of the flesh, **and the one who says** that the hide **is not accepted** independent of the flesh holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** that if there is no flesh then the blood is not sprinkled? The Gemara rejects this: **According to** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that the blood may be sprinkled even if the flesh is disqualified, **everyone agrees** that this sprinkling effects acceptance of the hide. **When they disagree,** it is **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua.**

The *gemara* now proposes the equation: *leima*, let us say that *man d'amar hurtza* — the view that sprinkling the blood works for the skin even when the meat is invalid — follows *Rabbi Eliezer*, who holds the *korban* valid even without the meat; and *man d'amar lo hurtza* — the view that the *kohanim* do not receive the skin when the meat was invalid at the time of sprinkling — follows *Rabbi Yehoshua*, who says that without valid meat there is no valid blood. There is, however, an important caveat: the debate of *Rebbi* and *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* is not a debate about whether the *korban* is *kasher* — it simply assumes the *korban* is valid and asks who is entitled to the skin. The debate of *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* is about whether the *korban* itself is valid when the meat cannot be offered. These are different questions, and the *gemara* is really drawing a *kal vachomer* or a parallel rather than asserting strict identity: if the *korban* is invalid (*Rabbi Yehoshua*), then certainly the *kohanim* lose the skin; if it is valid (*Rabbi Eliezer*), then presumably the *kohanim* receive the skin — though the *gemara* acknowledges that even this is not ironclad, since there could be a technical rule relating skin entitlement to meat validity even within a *kasher korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא הוּרְצָה – כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. מַאן דְּאָמַר הוּרְצָה אָמַר לָךְ: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָתָם – אֶלָּא בְּבָשָׂר, דְּלֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא לַכֹּהֲנִים; אֲבָל עוֹר, דְּאִיכָּא פְּסֵידָא לַכֹּהֲנִים – אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The one who says** that the hide **is not accepted** independently, i.e., Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds **in accordance with** the straightforward meaning of the statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua;** once the flesh is disqualified, the blood cannot be sprinkled and does not effect acceptance of the hide. **The one who says** that the hide **is accepted,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, could **say to you: Rabbi Yehoshua says only there** that the blood may not be sprinkled **in** a case where nothing but the **flesh** was at stake, **where there is no loss for the priests,** who never receive meat from burnt offerings. **But** in cases where the **hide** would go to waste, **where there is a loss for the priests,** perhaps **even Rabbi Yehoshua concedes** that the blood effects acceptance.

According to *Rabbi Eliezer*, the *gemara* says *kuli alma lo pligi* — all agree that the consequence of his position is that the *kohanim* receive the skin. The debate in the earlier *machlokes* only bears on what *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s position implies for the skin question.

## R. Yehoshua: L'chatchila…

### Rashi: L'chatchila/Bedia…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵי דְּהָוֵה אַדִּיעֲבַד; דִּתְנַן: נִטְמָא בָּשָׂר אוֹ נִפְסַל, אוֹ שֶׁיָּצָא חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר יִזְרוֹק, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר לֹא יִזְרוֹק. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שֶׁאִם זָרַק – הוּרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** This latter interpretation of Rabbi Yehoshua's opinion with regard to the hides is **just as it is with regard to** disqualified flesh **after the fact. As we learned** in a *baraita*: If the **flesh contracted ritual impurity or was disqualified, or** if it **emerged beyond the curtains** delineating its designated area, **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The priest must nevertheless **sprinkle** the blood on the altar. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The priest **may not sprinkle** the blood on the altar. **And Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that if** the priest nevertheless **sprinkled** the blood, the offering **is accepted** after the fact.

The *gemara* then addresses the crucial question: how could anyone hold that the *kohanim* receive the skin if *Rabbi Yehoshua* says the *korban* is invalid when there is no meat? The answer given is that *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s position must be understood as *lechatchila* rather than as an absolute invalidation. *Rashi* reads it this way: *lechatchila*, do not sprinkle the blood. Why would you? If the meat is already *tamei*, you cannot put it on the *mizbeach*. What would motivate the sprinkling? One might answer: to enable the *kohanim* to receive the skin. But *Rashi* reads the *gemara* as assuming that even if the blood is sprinkled *bediavad*, it works only to make the *korban kasher* in a technical sense, not to allow the owners to fulfill their *korban* obligation — a distinction introduced at the very opening of the *masechet*. Therefore, since there is nothing to be gained by the sprinkling — neither meat on the *mizbeach* nor fulfillment of the owner's obligation — *Rabbi Yehoshua* says *lechatchila* do not sprinkle.

### Meat Becomes Invalid Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים כּוּ׳. וְלֹא?! הֲרֵי פָּרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים וּשְׂעִירִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, said:** In all my days, I never saw a hide going out to the place of burning. The Gemara challenges: **And** is it so that he did **not** see? **Aren't there bulls that are burned and goats that are burned** together with their hides as a matter of course?

The *gemara* states that *Rabbi Yehoshua* says not to sprinkle the blood specifically *ela bevasar deleka pseda le-Kohanim* — in a case where the only thing at stake is the meat, and there is no prospective loss to the *kohanim*, since they have not yet acquired the skin. But *aval or de-ika pseda le-Kohanim* — when there is already a potential loss to the *kohanim* regarding the skin, meaning circumstances where if the blood is not sprinkled they will lose what might have been theirs — *afilu Rabbi Yehoshua modeh*, even *Rabbi Yehoshua* would concede. This is, frankly, a very strained reading of the *sugya*, and both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* acknowledge the difficulty. The plain sense of *ein basar ein dam* sounds like a categorical invalidation, not a *lechatchila* prohibition.

### R. Yehoshua Agrees Bedia…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמַחֲתָא לְאַרְעָא – לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ; דְּנָקֵיט בְּפוּמֵּיהּ – לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ; כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ – דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ: מְחוּסַּר קְרִיבָה כִּמְחוּסַּר מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Do not raise the dilemma** in a case **where** the carcass **lies on the ground,** as in that case it certainly must be of the volume of an egg-bulk, like any other impure food. And **do not raise the dilemma** in a case **where** a person **holds** the bird's flesh **in his mouth,** as it may be swallowed and transmit impurity to him even if it is of the volume of only an olive-bulk; in this case it certainly transmits impurity to food in the same measure. **When you raise the dilemma,** raise it in a case **where he holds** the bird's flesh **in his hand.** When the flesh **has not yet been brought close** to being swallowed, is it **considered** to be **like** an item for which a necessary **action has not yet** been performed, in which case it is considered a normal food and must be of the volume of an egg-bulk, **or** perhaps **not?**

That *Rabbi Yehoshua* does hold *bediavad* it works is established directly from a *mishnah*: *nitma basar o nifsul o sheyatza chutz lekla'im* — if the meat became *tamei*, or was otherwise invalidated, or left the *kla'im*: *Rabbi Eliezer omer yizrok* — *Rabbi Eliezer* says still sprinkle the blood. *Rabbi Yehoshua omer lo yizrok* — *Rabbi Yehoshua* says do not sprinkle. *U-modeh Rabbi Yehoshua she'im zarak hurtza* — but *Rabbi Yehoshua* concedes that if one did sprinkle, *bediavad* it worked. The language *hurtza* even suggests more than mere technical validity — it implies that the owners may even fulfill their obligation — which makes the earlier *gemara*'s reading even more complex. The *rishonim* resolve this by distinguishing between cases where the meat is present but invalid versus cases where it has been destroyed or lost entirely; *bediavad* validity applies only in the former category. The fundamental thrust, in any case, is that *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s position is a *lechatchila* prohibition, such that even his view can be reconciled with the position that the *kohanim* receive the skin when the blood was sprinkled under those circumstances.

## R. Chanina Reconsidered

### Burned Offerings Objecti…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לְמִצְוָתָן לָא קָאָמְרִינַן. הֲרֵי קוֹדֶם הֶפְשֵׁט וְקוֹדֶם זְרִיקָה! חָלוּץ קָאָמְרִינַן. וְהָאִיכָּא אַחַר הֶפְשֵׁט וְקוֹדֶם זְרִיקָה לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: אֵין הַדָּם מְרַצֶּה עַל הָעוֹר בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We are not saying** that Rabbi Ḥanina never saw hides go out to be burned **in accordance with their mitzva;** he certainly did. Rather, he never saw hides being burned because the offering was disqualified. The Gemara challenges: But **isn't there** a case where an offering is disqualified **before flaying and before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, in which case all agree that the animal is burned with its hide? The Gemara answers: **We are saying** that Rabbi Ḥanina never saw a hide go out **stripped** from its flesh. The Gemara challenges: **But isn't there** a case where an offering is disqualified **after** the **flaying** of the hide **and before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, in which case the hide is burned **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who says: The blood does not effect acceptance of the hide by itself?**

### Separate Burning Clarifi…

### Treifa Discovery Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רַבִּי חֲנִינָא כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אֲפִילּוּ תּוֹקְמַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שֶׁאֵין הֶפְשֵׁט קוֹדֶם זְרִיקָה. וְהָאִיכָּא נִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים! קָסָבַר: נִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים – מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ḥanina holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, that the blood does effect acceptance of the hide in such a case, and so it would not be burned. **And if you wish, say** instead that you can **even interpret** the opinion of Rabbi Ḥanina **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** as **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes that** the **flaying is not** done **before** the **sprinkling,** and so in practice Rabbi Ḥanina never saw a hide that was flayed before the offering was disqualified. The Gemara challenges: **But isn't there** the case of an animal that, after the hide was flayed and the blood was sprinkled, **was found** to have a wound **in** its **intestines** rendering it **a *tereifa*,** in which case the offering was already disqualified when the blood was sprinkled? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ḥanina **holds** that in the case of an animal that **was found** to be **a *tereifa*** due to a wound **in** its **intestines,** the sprinkling of the blood nevertheless **effects acceptance,** because the wound was unknown at the time of the sprinkling.

### Chanina = Rebbi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמִי – דְּקָתָנֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִדְּבָרָיו לָמַדְנוּ, שֶׁהַמַּפְשִׁיט אֶת הַבְּכוֹר וְנִמְצָא טְרֵיפָה – שֶׁיֵּאוֹתוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים בְּעוֹרוֹ! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. וְאֶלָּא מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: אֲפִילּוּ בִּגְבוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches: Rabbi Akiva said: From the statement of** Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, **we learned that** in a case where **one flays the firstborn** offering, **and** the animal is later **discovered** to be **a *tereifa*,** the *halakha* is **that the priests may derive benefit from its hide.** This indicates that the sprinkling of the blood effects acceptance if the wound was unknown. The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **learn from** the mishna that this is so. The Gemara challenges: **But** if this is what Rabbi Ḥanina meant, then **what is Rabbi Akiva teaching us?** His statement seems unnecessary. The Gemara answers: **This** is what Rabbi Akiva is **teaching us:** This *halakha* applies not just in the Temple but **even in the outlying areas,** e.g., with regard to a blemished firstborn animal, which is slaughtered outside the Temple. If it is discovered to be a *tereifa* before its slaughter, it must be buried with its hide, but if it is slaughtered and later discovered to be a *tereifa*, then its slaughter renders the hide permitted to the priests, just as the sprinkling of the blood renders the hide permitted in the Temple.

The conclusion is stated explicitly: *Rabbi Chanina k'Rebbi svira lei* — *Rebbe Chanina* holds like *Rebbi*. His testimony that the skin was never taken out separately to be burned reflects *Rebbi*'s position that once flayed, any problem with the meat does not affect the skin. Since *Rebbi* holds that flaying is the critical factor and that the skin is thereafter independent, there would indeed never be a case where a separately flayed skin needs to be burned. *K'Rebbi svira lei*.

The *gemara* adds: *v'ibait ayma afilu k'Rabbi Elazar BeRebbi Shimon* — alternatively, one could read *Rebbe Chanina*'s testimony as consistent even with *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon*, who holds that *zrikah* is the critical factor. The reason: *modeh Rebbi she'ein hafshait kodem zrikah* — in practice, flaying never preceded *zricat hadam*. Therefore, if the *korban* became invalid before *zrikah*, it became invalid when the skin was still attached to the meat, and the skin was taken out together with the meat — not separately. And if it became invalid after *zrikah*, that means it also became invalid after flaying in normal practice, at which point even *Rabbi Elazar beRabbi Shimon* agrees the skin goes to the *kohanim*. On this reading, *Rebbe Chanina*'s testimony is less halachically revelatory — it reflects the practical reality that the two critical moments, *zrikah* and *hefshait*, effectively coincide in practice — and the interesting *chiddush* is limited to the discovery rule in the case of an internal *treifa*.

## Bechor Application

### Why Bechor Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וְאַף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לֹא אָמַר אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהִתִּירוֹ מוּמְחֶה, אֲבָל לֹא הִתִּירוֹ מוּמְחֶה – לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** The Gemara adds: **And even Rabbi Akiva said** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where an expert** verified the firstborn animal's blemish and **permitted it** to be slaughtered. **But** if **an expert did not permit it,** then its slaughter does **not** render the hide permitted to the priest.

This brings us directly to *Rebbe Akiva*'s use of the *bechor* example, which the *gemara* now explains. *Ve-lamai kamashmalan Rabbi Akiva* — what exactly is *Rebbe Akiva* teaching us with the case of a *bechor*? *Ha-kamashmalan afilu bigvulin* — he is teaching us that this rule applies even outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, in the provinces, in the case of *kodshim* that exist and are managed outside the Temple context. *Rebbe Akiva* lived after the *churban* and was addressing practical *halachot* relevant to his time.

### Bechor with Blemish Stat…

The *halacha* of a *bechor* with a permanent *mum* is as follows. A *bechor* must be given to a *kohen*, but in the absence of the *Beit Hamikdash* there is nothing the *kohen* can do with it as a *korban*. Once it acquires a permanent *mum*, the *kohen* is permitted to slaughter it and eat it outside the *Beit Hamikdash*. However, because it retains its *kedusha*, it is not a freely disposable animal — the *kedusha* limits what may be done with it to slaughter and consumption. It cannot be sold, it cannot be used as animal fodder, and the meat cannot be treated as *chulin*. Its *kedusha* functions more like an *issur* — a prohibition that circumscribes permitted use — than like a transferable sanctity. And because the entitlement is limited to eating, if the meat cannot be eaten, the *kedusha* has no vehicle for expression through the meat.

### Bechor Treifa Skin Quest…

The *halachic* question: the *kohen* slaughters his *bechor* with a *mum* outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, and in the course of processing the animal discovers that it is a *treifa*. He cannot eat the meat — it is *assur* as a *treifa* — and he cannot derive any other benefit from it because of its *kedusha*. What happens to the skin? Must the skin follow the meat and be subject to the same restrictions? Or does the moment of discovery, relative to flaying, determine that the skin is separate?

There is no *zricat hadam* in this case — this is an animal slaughtered outside the *Beit Hamikdash* with no *mizbeach* and no *kohen* sprinkling blood on an altar. And yet *Rebbe Akiva* applies to it the same principle derived from *Rebbe Chanina*: the moment of discovery governs. If the *treifa* was discovered after flaying, the *kohanim* — or in this case, the *kohen* who owns the *bechor* — retain the skin. The principle of discovery-based determination of the skin's status, and the centrality of the moment of flaying, applies *bigvulin* just as it does within the *Beit Hamikdash*.

### Halacha: Sages on Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים; בָּשָׂר בִּקְבוּרָה, וְהָעוֹר בִּשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis,** not Rabbi Akiva. Therefore, the **flesh** is discarded **by burial and the hide by burning.**

Finally, *ve-hilchesa ke-divrei chachamim* — the *halacha* also follows the *chachamim* with respect to a *korban* proper, meaning that for *kodshim* brought in the *Beit Hamikdash* context, the *halacha* does not follow the discovery rule but rather determines the skin's status based on when the invalidity actually existed. This appears to be in tension with the preceding ruling in favor of *Rebbe Akiva*, and *Rashi* resolves this by drawing a distinction: for a *bechor bigvulin*, we follow *Rebbe Akiva* and go by flaying and discovery; for a *korban* in the proper sense, we follow the *chachamim* and go by when the invalidity was present, not when it was discovered. We thus arrive at a divided ruling — *Rebbe Akiva* for the *bechor* outside the *Beit Hamikdash*, and the *chachamim* for *korbano* inside it.
