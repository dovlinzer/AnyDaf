# Bava Metzia 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishkeno, Asmachta

### Mishkeno Definition

The daf opens at a point roughly mid-page on *amud bet*, at the passage beginning *mishkeno lo bayit*. The word *mishkeno* is vocalized as *mishken*, derived from *mashkon* — collateral. The form is *binyan pi'el*, meaning the act of collateralizing. The broader context of the discussion concerns the practice associated with Baitus ben Zonin, and a major theme running through the entire daf will be the concept of *asmachta* — a conditional verbal undertaking whose legal force is the central question.

### Baitus ben Zonin

The *mishnah* describes a scenario in which a borrower pledges a field worth far more than the loan as collateral — a *mashkon*. The lender, knowing full well that repayment may be difficult, agrees to accept the field. Because the *mashkon* is worth significantly more than the loan, the structure of the transaction raises immediate concern: the borrower is not simply giving security equal to the debt, but potentially surrendering an asset of substantially greater value. The *mishnah* notes that the field should not be acquired except at a fixed price — *ve-lo tehe niknit lo ela bidamim hallalu* — and the discussion turns on what that price is and what legal consequences flow from it.

### Fixed Price, Ribit

The problem the *gemara* identifies is that the price fixed for the field is *less* than the market value of the field — less, in fact, than even the loan amount relative to the field's worth. If the borrower fails to repay and the lender takes the field at a below-market price, the lender effectively gains more than the face value of the loan, which is functionally equivalent to interest. If, on the other hand, the parties agree on the actual market value of the field — say, the field is worth one hundred and fifty and is sold for one hundred and fifty, with one hundred applied toward repayment of the loan and fifty returned to the borrower — then while the transaction may not be entirely at arm's length, it does not generate an *issur* of *ribit*. The rule articulated is: at market value, *mutar*; at a fixed reduced price below market value, *assur*.

The *gemara* asks: *man tana bedamim hallalu assur* — who is the *tanna* that holds that a fixed reduced price is forbidden? Rav Huna bar Yehuda answers in the name of Rav Yehoshua: this ruling is not consistent with Rabbi Yehuda, *de-lo ke-Rabbi Yehuda*, because if it were Rabbi Yehuda's ruling, it would be permitted — since Rabbi Yehuda holds that *tzad echad beribit mutar*.

### Tzad Echad Beribit

*Tzad echad beribit* — literally, one side of *ribit* — refers to a transaction where it is not inevitable that interest will result. There is a *machloket* among the *tanna'im* as to whether such uncertainty saves a transaction from the prohibition. Rabbi Yehuda holds that so long as there is no certainty that the arrangement will result in *ribit*, it is permissible. The rationale here is that the borrower retains the option to repay the loan in full. If he does so, no below-market sale occurs, no field changes hands at a discount, and no benefit accrues to the lender beyond the principal. Since the scenario where *ribit* materializes is only one possible outcome, Rabbi Yehuda would call this *tzad echad beribit* and permit it.

### Likshe'Tirtze Condition

The key phrase introduced at this point is *likshe'tirtze* — "if you decide to sell." The arrangement is not an irrevocable obligation to sell the field at a reduced price; rather, it is structured as: if you ever decide to sell, you will sell only to me, at this price. Since the borrower retains the option not to sell at all — to simply repay the loan and reclaim the field — it is not a foregone conclusion that the below-market arrangement will ever be triggered. This is precisely what makes it *tzad echad beribit* in Rabbi Yehuda's framework. The current ruling, however, which declares it *assur*, reflects the position of the Sages who do not permit even *tzad echad beribit*.

## Sale/Repurchase Cases

### Seller Repurchase Clause

The *gemara* turns to a series of cases involving sale with a repurchase arrangement. The *mishnah* presents: *machar lo bayit, machar lo sadeh* — someone sells a house or a field. In the first case, the seller says to the buyer, *kesheyihyu li ma'ot, hahzirem li* — when I have money, return it to me. That is, the seller, having sold the property, explicitly stipulates at the time of the transaction that the buyer must sell it back once the seller has the funds. This case is *assur*.

The key to understanding why this is forbidden is that the transaction looks structurally like a loan masquerading as a sale. The seller parts with the property only because he needs the money now, and the stipulation that the buyer must reverse the transaction once the seller is solvent exposes the arrangement for what it really is. The buyer holds the property for an extended period — perhaps a year or more — enjoys the *peirot* (the produce and use of the land), and then when the seller comes up with the money, returns it at the original price. The seller gets back exactly what he paid; the buyer gets the property and all of its benefits over the intervening period. This is functionally equivalent to interest on the original sum, which is why it is *assur*.

### Buyer Repurchase Offer

The second case reverses the direction of the stipulation: *kesheyihyu lecha ma'ot, achzirem lach* — the buyer says to the seller, "when you have money, I will return it to you." Here, it is the buyer who voluntarily offers a repurchase right to the seller. This case is *mutar*. The contrast is striking, and the *gemara* immediately turns to analyzing what distinguishes the two.

### Peirot Problem

Why is the first case *assur*? The answer is that the seller's stipulation at the time of the sale that the buyer must return it creates what amounts to a loan structure. The buyer holds the property throughout — collecting the *peirot*, the produce and benefits of the land — and then simply hands it back when the seller repays the original sum. The buyer has thus received exactly what a lender receives from interest: use of the property over the loan period, returned to him at the end. This is the *peirot* problem — the buyer's enjoyment of the field's yield during the interval is the equivalent of *ribit*.

The analysis also touches on whether the repurchase price matters — whether it would be worse if the repurchase were at the original price rather than the current market price. If the repurchase price is the same as the original sale price, the arrangement is even more transparently loan-like, since neither party bears any market risk. If the repurchase price is the current market value, the buyer at least in principle absorbs fluctuation in the land's worth. Either way, the problem of *peirot* during the intervening period remains, since the buyer has had the use of the property throughout and returns it at some fixed price.

The Ramban, noted in the commentary, holds that such a transaction does not go through at all — it is void — which is consistent with the ruling that it is *assur* not merely on a rabbinic level but structurally invalid.

### Distinction Analysis

*Mai shna reisha u-mai shna seifa* — what distinguishes the first case from the second? Rabbah formulates the distinction as follows: *reisha de-lo amar lada'atei, aval seifa de-amar lei lada'atei* — in the first case, the buyer did not say the return would be at his discretion (*leda'atei*), while in the second case, the buyer did say it would be at his discretion.

The word *achzirem* — "I will return it to you" — as spoken by the buyer in the second case, carries an implicit qualification of voluntariness. The buyer is not binding himself irrevocably; he is saying, in effect, *likshe'irtze* — if I choose to. This is in contrast to the first case, *hahzirem li* — "return it to me" — which is a demand or stipulation embedded in the sale itself, from which there is no escape. One side is locked in; the other is not. That asymmetry is legally decisive.

### Leda'atei Voluntary

Rabbah clarifies: the permissibility of the second case rests on the word *leda'atei* — at his discretion, if he wants to. *Leda'atei* imports *ratzon*, will or desire. *Achzirem* is understood by the *gemara* to imply this voluntariness: I will return it to you, if I so choose. *Hahziram li*, by contrast, is a stipulation, a condition of the sale itself, that admits no discretion.

The principle being developed here is that where a party is genuinely locked in — where the commitment is binding and inescapable — the arrangement becomes functionally a loan and triggers the *ribit* prohibition. Where the commitment is discretionary, it remains a true sale with an independent and optional future transaction.

## No Warranty Sale

### Achrayut Assumption

The *gemara* now introduces a case involving *achrayut* — warranty or guarantee — in the sale of land. As a background principle, *karka* — real property — is normally assumed to carry *achrayut*. There is a concept of *achrayut ta'ut sofer*: if a land deed omits the standard warranty clause, we treat it as a scribal error and read the guarantee in anyway. This is in fact the ruling of Shmuel, and it is the normative *halacha*. Therefore, for the following case to even be legally coherent, it must involve a contract that explicitly stipulates there is no *achrayut* — because otherwise the warranty would be implied by law.

### Seller's Promise

A man sold land to another party *shelo be-achrayut* — explicitly without warranty. This means the seller made clear: if a creditor of mine comes and seizes this land from you, I will not compensate you. Having sold the land on these terms, the seller later saw the buyer looking distressed — *chazayah de-havah ka atziv* — visibly upset about the exposure he had taken on. The seller then approached the buyer and said, in effect: don't worry. *I metzarfu lah minach magvinah lach shufra ve-shivcha u-feirei* — if it is seized from you, I will make you whole: I will compensate you for the enhanced value of the property, for any improvements you made, and for the produce you would have received. He promised to cover everything.

### Pitumei Mili

Ameimar rules on this promise: *pitumei mili be'alma hu* — it is nothing more than empty words. The term *pitumei* means to fatten up, as one fattens livestock; the image is of words that puff someone up without substance. Rashi explains: *ein elu ela divrei tanchumim be'alma lifayeso* — these are merely words of comfort to appease him, without legal effect. The contract already stipulated explicitly that there would be no warranty. The seller's subsequent verbal reassurance cannot override what was written, because it was never intended as a binding commitment.

### Wrong Side Stipulation

Rav Ashi challenges Ameimar: *ta'ama mai* — what is the reason for saying *pitumei mili*? He frames the question analytically: *mishum de-lo'keach ba'ei le-itnuyei* — normally it is the buyer who stipulates the protective conditions in a transaction. The buyer is the one spending money, and the buyer has every incentive to demand guarantees. The seller, by contrast, wants to minimize his post-sale obligations. Therefore, when we see the *seller* making these generous promises, we know something is off — it is not the natural role of the seller to volunteer such guarantees, and that very unnaturalness is evidence that he didn't really mean it.

### Seller's Incentive

Rav Ashi's reasoning sharpens the point: the seller is the *wrong* party to be making these stipulations. A buyer entering a real estate transaction demands title guarantees, freedom from liens, and protection against every contingency — that is the buyer's natural behavior. The seller's natural interest is to shed responsibility as quickly as possible and hand over the property free of ongoing obligation. When the seller spontaneously volunteers expansive guarantees after the sale has already been closed on explicitly warranty-free terms, it is precisely because this is out of character that we can infer he didn't truly mean it. Hence: *ve-hacha mochar ka matni* — here it is the seller making the stipulations — and that is why it is *pitumei mili be'alma*.

### Promise as Leda'atei

The *gemara* ties this back to the principle established earlier. Rabbah's explanation — that the permissible repurchase case works because the buyer said *leda'atei*, at his discretion — is applied here analogously. In the case of Ameimar, the seller made his promise after the sale, not as part of the original contractual terms. Since it was done after the sale, the *gemara* treats it as if the seller had said *likshe'irtze* — "if I choose to." Even though he did not literally use that language, the fact that the promise came after the transaction had already concluded, under terms that specified the contrary, means we read it as though he said "I will consider it." And since it is discretionary and unenforceable, it carries no legal weight. *Na'aseh ke-man de-amar lei leda'atei* — it is treated as if he said it would be at his discretion, and therefore it is indeed *pitumei mili*.

The underlying principle being crystallized is: a commitment made after the fact, particularly when it runs contrary to the written terms of the original agreement, is presumed to be non-binding, because the very timing signals that it was not meant seriously. Had the seller said *leda'atei* at the time of the sale — and had he truly meant it — it would have been a valid, enforceable commitment. But where the promise post-dates the sale and contradicts the written terms, it is structurally equivalent to saying "if I want to" — and is therefore empty.

## Get, Pitumei Mili

### Deathbed Get

A *shechiv mera* — a man on his deathbed — gives his wife a *get*. The reason a dying man would give a *get* at this point is to spare his wife from *yibum*: were she to be left a *yevamah*, she would need to undergo *chalitzah* or *yibum* with her husband's brother. To preempt this, he divorces her on his deathbed so that she dies as a divorcée, not as a widow. The man, however, groans — *angid ve-itnach* — and sighs as he hands over the *get*, expressing his reluctance. His wife, moved by his hesitation, says to him: *i kaymet ana didach* — if you live, I will remain your wife. She is offering a kind of comfort: do not be so distressed; if by some miracle you recover, I am yours.

### Wife's Words Insignifica…

Zeiri rules: *pitumei mili de-amrah* — what she said is mere comforting words, legally empty. Just as in the previous cases, the post-hoc verbal declaration carries no binding force.

### Wife Cannot Condition Get

Rav Acha of Difti challenges Zeiri before Ravina: *ve-i lav pitumei mili, mai?* — but even setting aside *pitumei mili*, what would the problem be? The underlying question is: does she even have the legal power to impose conditions on a *get*? A *get* is the husband's instrument to give; it is he who sets its terms and conditions. *Biyada kayma le-mishdei tna'ah be-gitta?* — is it in her power to attach conditions to the *get*? The answer is categorically no. She has no standing to condition the validity of the *get* on anything. If that is true, why invoke *pitumei mili* at all? The *get* is valid on its own terms regardless of what she says; her statement cannot undo it in any case.

### His Da'at Governs

The *gemara* answers: the reason we invoke *pitumei mili* despite the fact that she lacks the power to condition the *get* is as follows. One might have thought: *hu gufeih ada'ata de-didah ka gamar ve-yahiv gitta* — he himself completed the act of giving the *get* with her statement in mind; that is, perhaps his own *da'at* — his intention — was contingent on her declaration, such that he gave the *get* on the assumption that her statement constituted a condition. If his *da'at* were thus contingent, the *get* might be compromised. Therefore, *ka mashma lan* — the ruling teaches us that her statement is *pitumei mili*: since her words carry no legal force, his *da'at* cannot have been genuinely contingent upon them, and the *get* is entirely valid. The operative principle here is not about her capacity but about *his* intention: we rule that he could not have understood her words as a binding condition, and therefore his *da'at* in delivering the *get* was unconditional.

This case thus represents a further step in the development of the concept. Earlier, we saw *pitumei mili* applied to verbal promises that were made after the fact. Here, the ruling applies not even to a verbal promise but to the effect that verbal words might have had on the inner intention of the other party — *da'at* in the most subjective sense. The conclusion is that since the words themselves have no legal substance, they cannot infect the *da'at* of the one who acts.

## Baitus Field Case

### Loan Terms

The *gemara* returns to the case of Baitus ben Zonin, now analyzed at greater length. The basic structure is: a lender extends a loan of one hundred and the borrower pledges a field worth three hundred as *mashkon*. The agreement is that if the borrower does not repay within three years, the field becomes the lender's — *im lo natati lecha mikan ve-ad shalosh shanim harei hi sheli*. The lender, under this arrangement, would receive a three-hundred-dollar asset in satisfaction of a one-hundred-dollar loan. This is the core tension: why is this not *ribit*?

### Timing Distinction

Rav Huna introduces a critical distinction based on timing: *be-sha'at mattan ma'ot* versus *achar mattan ma'ot* — whether this condition was established at the time the money changed hands or afterward.

### At Loan Time

If the condition was stipulated *be-sha'at mattan ma'ot* — at the moment the loan was extended — Rav Huna holds that *kanah ha-kol*: the lender acquires everything. The rationale is that a condition made contemporaneously with the transaction is part of the deal itself. The borrower, at that moment, is making a genuine agreement, and the full value of the field is what he has put on the line.

### After Loan Time

If the condition was established *achar mattan ma'ot* — after the money was already lent — then *lo kanah ella ke-neged ma'otav*: the lender acquires only what corresponds to the actual value of the loan, not the full field. A post-hoc verbal condition, as we have seen throughout the daf, carries the character of *pitumei mili* or at least of a non-binding declaration. The borrower, having already received the money, is in no position to make a serious pledge of his entire field; the statement is dismissed as insufficiently grounded.

### Document Torn, Reversal

Rav Nachman, consistent with his own ruling, drafted documents in accordance with the principle that *be-sha'at mattan ma'ot kanah ha-kol*. Rav Yehuda — presumably Rav Yehuda bar Yechezkel — tore up one of these documents. When Reish Galuta heard that his document had been torn, he reported this to Rav Nachman. In one version of the exchange, Rav Nachman asks: *dardaka karaye?* — did a child tear it up? — meaning: can I dismiss the concern? The answer comes back: *gavra rabbah karaye* — a great scholar tore it up. *Chaza bah ta'ama ve-karaye* — he must have seen a reason to do so, and I must reconsider. In the alternate version, Rav Nachman says *dardaka karaye* as a statement, dismissively — a child tore it up and knows nothing — and then proceeds to justify this with the general principle that *halacha ke-Rav Nachman be-dinei*, that in monetary law we rule with Rav Nachman. On this version he adds: *de-kulei alma le-gabei didi be-dinei dardakei ninhu* — compared to me in monetary law, everyone else is a child.

### Rav Nachman Reversal

Regardless of which version one follows for the exchange with Reish Galuta, the *gemara* records that Rav Nachman ultimately reversed his position entirely: *afilu be-sha'at mattan ma'ot lo kanah ve-lo klum* — even when the condition was stipulated at the time the money was lent, the lender acquires nothing. This is a complete *hadar*, a reversal, which at minimum follows from the first version of the story where Rav Nachman acknowledged that Rav Yehuda must have had a compelling reason.

The logic of the reversal is rooted in the concept of *asmachta*. The borrower pledges a three-hundred-dollar field for a hundred-dollar loan and says: if I don't pay, it's yours. The question is whether this verbal commitment — however solemn it sounds — reflects a genuine, serious *da'at* to transfer the field. Rav Nachman concludes that it does not. The borrower says this because he wants the money now and is not thinking realistically about losing his field; he never genuinely intends to forfeit an asset worth three times the loan. Therefore the condition is an *asmachta*, and *asmachta lo kanya* — an *asmachta* does not effect an acquisition. The consideration of *meshichah* — physical taking — as a possible way to validate the acquisition is also noted; the issue of whether an act of *meshichah* could have been used to effectuate the *asmachta* arises in the surrounding discussion.

## Asmachta Validity

### Asmachta Lo Kanya

Having arrived at Rav Nachman's final position, the *gemara* now addresses it directly. Rav Nachman holds: *asmachta lo kanya* — an *asmachta* does not effect a valid acquisition under any circumstances. This is a categorical ruling: verbal undertakings predicated on future contingencies, where the obligor does not truly intend to be bound, have no legal force.

### Mishnah Position

But this creates a problem, because our *mishnah* appears to say otherwise. *Matnitin* — the *mishnah* — through the position of Rabbi Yose, states: *asmachta kanya* — such a condition does effect an acquisition. The Baitus ben Zonin practice, which the *mishnah* records approvingly, appears to validate exactly the kind of contingent pledge Rav Nachman is rejecting.

Rav Nachman responds: *ani amar asmachta kanya, u-Minyumi amar asmachta lo kanya* — I used to hold that *asmachta kanya*, and it was Minyumi who convinced me to change my position to *asmachta lo kanya*. So the question about the *mishnah* should be addressed to Minyumi, not to me. And the *gemara* asks accordingly: *Minyumi, kashya mi-mishna* — how does Minyumi answer the *mishnah*?

### Me'achshav Timing

Two answers are offered. The first: *matnitin Rabbi Yose de-amar asmachta kanya* — the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yose, who holds that *asmachta kanya*, but that is not the accepted ruling. Minyumi does not hold like Rabbi Yose.

The second answer: *iba'it eima amar lei kani me'achshav* — one can say that the *mishnah* involves a case where the borrower said the acquisition takes effect *me'achshav*, from now, in the event that he does not repay. This formulation is different in kind from a standard *asmachta*. When the lender receives the field *me'achshav* — immediately, at the time of the loan — the forfeiture is not a distant, abstract contingency three years in the future. It is a present reality: the field belongs to the lender now, subject to being reclaimed upon repayment. That concreteness makes the borrower's commitment serious; he is not dealing in hypotheticals. The three-year deadline is the window within which he may buy it back, not the moment at which the transfer occurs. This is why *me'achshav* removes the arrangement from the category of *asmachta*: the transfer is immediate and real, not contingent and speculative.

### Neharde'i Rule

The *gemara* records a version of Rav Nachman's ruling attributed to the scholars of Nehardea: *hachi amri Neharde'ei mishmeih de-Rav Nachman: asmachta bizmanei kanya, ve-lo bizmanei lo kanya* — an *asmachta* acquires within its time period, but does not acquire outside its time period. This is understood to mean: if the borrower reaffirms or invokes the condition *within* the period of the loan — before the due date — the *asmachta* takes effect; if he does so after the time has elapsed, it does not.

The sons of Rav Chisda — one called *yanuka* (the younger) and one called *kashisha* (the older) — press on this formulation: *ke-midi bizmanei kanya ve-lo bizmanei kanya* — what does "in its time" even mean? Everything is either at its time or not; the formulation seems to need clarification. The *gemara* explains: *dilma hachi ka-amar lei, ashkacheih be-go zimneih ve-amar lei kani* — perhaps what the Nehardea rule means is that if the lender encounters the borrower *within* the loan period and the borrower at that point says, "go ahead, take it, the field is yours," this declaration is effective and the lender *koneh* the field. The idea is that within the loan's active period, the borrower is still in a posture of voluntarily offering the pledge; the relationship is ongoing, the commitment is still fresh and alive.

### Seriousness Analysis

Rashi explains the Nehardea position with characteristic precision: *de-gemar ve-akni de-i lo amar lei, lema amar lei, halo einecha achshav be-tovah* — he clearly means what he says, for why else would he say it? If the lender is currently holding him to nothing — the loan is not yet due, there is no crisis — and the borrower voluntarily says "take the field," this is a sign of seriousness. He doesn't *have* to say it. There is no pressure on him. The fact that he volunteers this information, when his eyes are currently enjoying a good situation, demonstrates genuine intent. Therefore, within the loan period, the declaration is effective and the *asmachta* acquires.

### Borrower Intent

The *gemara* ultimately rejects even this: *ve-lo hi* — this is not so. *De-afilu be-go zimneih nami lo kani* — even within the loan period, the *asmachta* does not acquire. The reason: *ve-hai de-ka-amar lei kani, kasavar ki mati zimneih lo leite litardeni* — when the borrower says "take it" during the loan period, his motivation is simply to avoid being pestered. He knows the due date is coming; if he does not pay, the lender will appear at his door every day demanding the money. By preemptively saying "if I don't pay, the field is yours," he is buying himself peace of mind until the due date. He doesn't actually intend to forfeit the field — he just doesn't want to be bothered. The moment after the due date arrives and he has not paid, the lender starts coming to collect, and then the embarrassment (*kissufa*) of the post-deadline encounter is what drives any further declarations.

In neither case — neither within the loan period nor after — does the borrower truly, seriously intend to transfer the field. The *asmachta* reflects an aspiration or a social gesture, not a genuine act of *kinyan*. Rav Nachman's final position is therefore unequivocal: *asmachta lo kanya* — categorically, an *asmachta* does not effect an acquisition. The daf ends with this ruling in place, with the full halachic analysis of when it applies and what distinguishes it from valid conditional transfers yet to be developed further.
