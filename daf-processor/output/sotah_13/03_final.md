# Sotah 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Moshe's Birth & Miriam

### 21st Nissan Dating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא אָמַר: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן הָיָה, אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִי שֶׁעָתִיד לוֹמַר שִׁירָה עַל הַיָּם בְּיוֹם זֶה — יִלְקֶה בְּיוֹם זֶה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa says: That day** that Moses was placed in the river **was the twenty-first** day of the month **of Nisan. The ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe,** should the **one who in the future will say** the **Song at the** Red **Sea on this day be stricken on this day?** As this was also the date on which the Red Sea would be parted during the salvation of the Exodus.

The daf opens in the midst of a series of *midrashim* surrounding *yetziat mitzrayim*, specifically the birth of Moshe Rabbeinu. We pick up with Rabbi Hanina bar Pappa, who derives from the word *zeh* in the *pasuk* a precise date for when Moshe was discovered by *Bat Pharaoh*. His position is that *oto yom esrim ve'echad b'Nissan haya* — that day was the 21st of Nissan. On that basis, the heavenly angels said before God: *Ribbono shel olam*, the one who in the future will sing the *shira al hayam* on this very day — should he be smitten on this very same day?

### 6th Sivan Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שִׁשָּׁה בְּסִיוָן הָיָה, אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִי שֶׁעָתִיד לְקַבֵּל תּוֹרָה מֵהַר סִינַי בְּיוֹם זֶה — יִלְקֶה בְּיוֹם זֶה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says: That day was** actually **the sixth** day of the month **of Sivan. The ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe,** should the **one who in the future will receive** the **Torah on Mount Sinai on this day be stricken on this day?** As this was also the date on which the Torah was received.

Rabbi Akiva bar Rabbi Hanina disagrees. According to him, *oto yom shisha b'Sivan haya* — the day Moshe was discovered was not the 21st of Nissan but the 6th of Sivan, the very day the Torah would later be given at Har Sinai. The angels' protestation is framed accordingly: the one who in the future will receive Torah from Har Sinai on this day — should he be smitten on this day? Both opinions derive their respective dates from the emphatic use of *zeh* in the *pasuk* — *miyaldei ha'ivrim zeh* — understanding the word as pointing toward a day of particular cosmic significance.

### 7th Adar Birth/Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּשִׁשָּׁה בְּסִיוָן, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ תְּלָתָא יַרְחֵי. דְּאָמַר מָר: בְּשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר מֵת, וּבְשִׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר נוֹלָד מֹשֶׁה, וּמִשִּׁבְעָה בַּאֲדָר וְעַד שִׁשָּׁה בְּסִיוָן תְּלָתָא יַרְחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that Moses was placed in the water **on the sixth of Sivan, you find** that there can be **three months** during which Moses was hidden after his birth; **as the Master said**: Moses **died on the seventh of Adar, and Moses was born on the seventh of Adar. And** based on this, **from the seventh of Adar until the sixth of Sivan** there are **three months,** which correspond to the three months Moses was hidden before being placed in the water.

The *Gemara* now examines which opinion is internally coherent with the tradition that Moshe was hidden for three months before his discovery. The underlying datum is the teaching: *beshiva b'Adar met uveshiva b'Adar nolad* — Moshe died on the 7th of Adar and was born on the 7th of Adar. His death date on the 7th of Adar is derived from the account in *Sefer Yehoshua*: Bnei Yisrael mourned Moshe for thirty days, after which God told Yehoshua to cross the Jordan, and that crossing, as the *pesukim* in Yehoshua make clear, occurred on the 10th of Nissan — counting back thirty days yields the 7th of Adar. The birth date on the same day is derived from the principle that *tzaddikim* live out complete years, dying on the anniversary of their birth, along with Moshe's own declaration *ben me'ah ve'esrim shana anochi hayom* — "today I am exactly 120 years old" — implying that the day of his death was precisely the day of his birth.

### Leap Year Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת הָיְתָה: רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן, וְרוּבּוֹ שֶׁל אַחֲרוֹן, וְאֶמְצָעִי שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that it was **on the twenty-first of Nisan, how can you find** that he was hidden for three months? The Gemara answers: **That year was a leap year** in which there were two months of Adar. Moses was hidden **most of the first** month of the three, from the seventh day of the first Adar when he was born, **and most of the last** month of the three, i.e., all of Nisan until the twenty-first, **and the entire middle one.** All of this together is considered as three months.

The *Gemara* asks: *bishlamah* for the opinion that Moshe was discovered on the 6th of Sivan, the arithmetic works out elegantly — three months from the 7th of Adar yields Adar, Nissan, Iyar, Sivan, arriving at the 6th or 7th of Sivan, aligning with *Matan Torah*. But for the opinion that he was discovered on the 21st of Nissan, how can three months from the 7th of Adar reach only the 21st of Nissan? The *Gemara* resolves this: *osa shana me'uberet hayita* — that year was a leap year. Moshe was born on the 7th of Adar Rishon; he had the remainder of Adar Rishon, the entirety of Adar Sheni, and 21 days of Nissan — most of the first month, all of the middle month, and most of the third month, satisfying the requirement of *rubo shel rishon*, *rubo shel acharon*, and *emtza'ei shalem*.

### Nursing Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֹּאמֶר אֲחוֹתוֹ אֶל בַּת פַּרְעֹה הַאֵלֵךְ וְקָרָאתִי לָךְ אִשָּׁה מֵינֶקֶת מִן הָעִבְרִיּוֹת״. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵעִבְרִיּוֹת? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֶחְזִירוּהוּ לְמֹשֶׁה עַל כׇּל הַמִּצְרִיּוֹת כּוּלָּן, וְלֹא יָנַק, אָמַר: פֶּה שֶׁעָתִיד לְדַבֵּר עִם הַשְּׁכִינָה יִינַק דָּבָר טָמֵא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now discusses the next verse in Exodus: **"Then said his sister to Pharaoh's daughter: Shall I go and call you a nurse of the Hebrew women,** that she may nurse the child for you?" (Exodus 2:7). The Gemara asks: **And what is different** that Pharaoh's daughter would specifically want a nurse **of the Hebrew women?** This **teaches that** prior to this, **they took Moses around to all the Egyptian** wet nurses **and he did not** agree to **nurse** from any of them, as **he said:** Shall **a mouth that in the future** will **speak with the Divine Presence** actually **nurse something impure?**

The *Gemara* teaches: *melamed she-hechziru l'Moshe al kol hamitzriyot* — they had already attempted to have Moshe nurse from Egyptian women, and he refused from every one of them. The reasoning given is: *peh she-atid ledaber im haShechina yanik davar tamei* — the mouth that would one day speak directly with the Divine Presence, should it suckle something impure? The impurity referred to is not the person of the Egyptian woman herself, but the non-kosher food she consumed, the effects of which pass into a nursing mother's milk. This *aggadic* passage carries real halakhic resonances among the *poskim* regarding whether the food of a nursing mother must meet kosher standards, given that the infant, who is below the age of *chinuch*, would still be receiving some spiritual or physical effect of *metamtem et halev* — the heart-dulling quality that the *Gemara* elsewhere attributes to non-kosher food.

### Knowledge Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶת מִי יוֹרֶה דֵעָה וְגוֹ׳״. לְמִי יוֹרֶה דֵּעָה וּלְמִי יָבִין שְׁמוּעָה — ״לִגְמוּלֵי מֵחָלָב וּלְעַתִּיקֵי מִשָּׁדַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** And **this is as it is written: "Whom shall one teach knowledge?** And whom shall one make understand the message?" (Isaiah 28:9). The prophet is asking: **To whom shall** God **teach** the **knowledge** of the Torah, **and to whom shall** God **make to understand the message** of the Torah? The answer is as the verse continues: **"Them that are weaned from the milk, them that are drawn from the breasts"** (Isaiah 28:9). The conclusion of the verse indicates that the Torah should be taught to the one who did not want to nurse from the milk of a gentile woman, i.e., Moses.

The *Gemara* connects Moshe's refusal to nurse from Egyptian women to the verse: *l'mi yoreh de'ah v'et mi yavin shemu'ah gemulei mechalav atikei mishadayim* — "To whom shall one teach knowledge, and who shall understand the tradition? One who weans himself from milk and removes himself from the breast." This verse is now understood as a reference to Moshe, who as an infant refused the milk of Egyptian women, thereby preserving the sanctity of the mouth destined to speak before God.

### Miriam's Role and Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֹּאמֶר אֲחוֹתוֹ אֶל בַּת פַּרְעֹה לֵכִי וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָלְכָה בִּזְרִיזוּת כְּעַלְמָה. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: ״הָעַלְמָה״ — שֶׁהֶעֱלִימָה אֶת דְּבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And Pharaoh's daughter said to her: Go.** And the maiden [*ha'alma*] went and called the child's mother" (Exodus 2:8). **Rabbi Elazar says:** This **teaches that she went quickly like a maiden,** i.e., with the strength of one of marriageable age, and not as the young child that she was. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** The word ***ha'alma*** is related to the word meaning to hide [*le'alem*], **for she hid her words** and didn't tell Pharaoh's daughter that she was bringing the baby's mother.

The *Gemara* then addresses the verse *vattomer lah Bat Pharaoh lechi*, in which *Bat Pharaoh* tells Miriam to go. Rabbi Elazar derives from the subsequent *vattelech ha'almah* — "and the young woman went" — that she went *bizrizut k'almah*, with the alacrity of a young woman. The *Gemara* then notes *vattelech ha'almah vatikra et em hayeled*, and Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani explains why she is called *ha'almah*: *she-he'elimah et dvareha* — she concealed her words, presenting Yocheved as merely a Hebrew nursing woman without revealing that she was Moshe's own mother.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֹּאמֶר לָהּ בַּת פַּרְעֹה הֵילִיכִי אֶת הַיֶּלֶד הַזֶּה״. אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִתְנַבְּאָה וְאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת מָה מִתְנַבְּאָה, ״הֵילִיכִי״ — הָא שֶׁלִּיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states what Pharaoh's daughter said to Jochebed: **"And Pharaoh's daughter said to her: Take this [*heilikhi*] child away,** and nurse it for me, and I will give you your wages. And the woman took the child, and nursed it" (Exodus 2:9). **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** Pharaoh's daughter **is prophesying and she does not know what she is prophesying,** as the word ***heilikhi*** means: **This is yours [*ha shellikhi*],** i.e., this is your child.

The verse then continues: *vattomer lah Bat Pharaoh heichi et hayeled hazeh* — here, take this child. Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina observes: *misnave'ah ve'einah yoda'at mah misnave'ah* — she prophesied without knowing what she was prophesying. The word *heichi*, parsed as *hei* and *sheleichi*, carries the meaning "here is what is yours" — *Bat Pharaoh* unknowingly declared that this was Yocheved's own child. Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina adds a broader principle: *lo dayam latzaddikim she-machzirim lahem avedatan* — it is not sufficient for the righteous that their lost objects are returned to them; *ela she-notnim lahem scharam* — they are also paid for retrieving what was already theirs to begin with.

### Miriam's Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּקַּח מִרְיָם הַנְּבִיאָה אֲחוֹת אַהֲרֹן וְגוֹ׳״. אֲחוֹת אַהֲרֹן וְלֹא אֲחוֹת מֹשֶׁה? אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיְתָה מִתְנַבְּאָה כְּשֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אַהֲרֹן, וְאוֹמֶרֶת: עֲתִידָה אִמִּי שֶׁתֵּלֵד בֵּן שֶׁמּוֹשִׁיעַ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Elsewhere, the verse states with regard to Miriam: **"And Miriam the prophetess, the sister of Aaron, took** a timbrel in her hand; and all the women went out after her with timbrels and with dances" (Exodus 15:20). The Gemara asks: Why is Miriam referred to as **"the sister of Aaron," and not the sister of Moses? Rav Amram says** that **Rav says, and some say** that **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rav says:** This **teaches that** Miriam already **prophesied when she was** still **the sister of** only **Aaron,** i.e., before Moses was born. **And** as a child Miriam **would say: In the future, my mother will give birth to a son who will save the Jewish people.**

The *Gemara* transitions to the verse at *Shirat Hayam*: *vattikach Miriam hanevia achot Aharon*. The question is posed: why does it say *achot Aharon* — Aaron's sister — and not Moshe's sister? The answer, cited in the name of Rav Amram citing Rav, and some say Rav Nachman citing Rav: *melamed she-nitnave'ah k'she-hi achot Aharon* — this teaches that she had already attained the status of *nevia* while she was only Aaron's sister, before Moshe was born. This implies that her prophecy concerned Moshe's birth: *atida imi sheteleid ben* — my mother will give birth to a son. Her father kissed her on her head and said, *bitti, nitkayma nevuatecha* — my daughter, your prophecy has been fulfilled — and, as a result, he restored his wife, bringing Yocheved back.

### Miriam's Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן שֶׁנּוֹלַד מֹשֶׁה נִתְמַלֵּא כָּל הַבַּיִת כּוּלּוֹ אוֹר. עָמַד אָבִיהָ וּנְשָׁקָהּ עַל רֹאשָׁהּ, אָמַר לָהּ: בִּתִּי, נִתְקַיְּימָה נְבוּאָתִיךְ. וְכֵיוָן שֶׁהִטִּילוּהוּ לַיְאוֹר, עָמַד אָבִיהָ וּטְפָחָהּ עַל רֹאשָׁהּ, אָמַר לָהּ: בִּתִּי הֵיכָן נְבוּאָתִיךְ? וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתֵּתַצַּב אֲחוֹתוֹ מֵרָחוֹק לְדֵעָה מַה יֵּעָשֶׂה לוֹ״ — לֵידַע מָה יְהֵא בְּסוֹף נְבוּאָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And once Moses was born, the entire house was filled with light. Her father arose and kissed her on her head. He said to her: My daughter, your prophecy has been fulfilled. And once they put him into the river, her father arose and hit her on her head. He said to her: My daughter, where is your prophecy? And this is as it is written: "And his sister stood afar off, to know what would be done to him"** (Exodus 2:4), i.e., **to know what will be the ultimate** resolution **of her prophecy.**

Yet once Moshe was placed into the Nile, the father struck Miriam on her head and said: *bitti, heichan nevuatecha* — my daughter, where is your prophecy now? This explains the verse *vatitatzav achoto merachok lada'at mah yei'aseh lo*: Miriam stood at a distance not merely to watch over her brother, but *lada'at mah yihyeh b'sof nevuatah* — to see what would ultimately become of her prophecy, whether it would in fact be fulfilled. The contrast between Miriam's long-term prophetic perspective and Amram's immediate, reactive responses — first to Pharaoh's decree by divorcing Yocheved, then to Moshe's placement in the Nile by striking Miriam — runs as a recurring thread through this section of the *Gemara*.

## Yosef Buries Yaakov

### Entourage Precedence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מֵעִיקָּרָא דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַל יוֹסֵף לִקְבּוֹר אֶת אָבִיו וַיַּעֲלוּ אִתּוֹ כׇּל עַבְדֵי פַּרְעֹה וְגוֹ׳״, וַהֲדַר: ״וְכׇל בֵּית יוֹסֵף וְאֶחָיו וּבֵית אָבִיו״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא לְבַסּוֹף דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּשַׁב יוֹסֵף מִצְרַיְמָה הוּא וְאֶחָיו״, וַהֲדַר: ״וְכׇּל הָעוֹלִים אִתּוֹ לִקְבּוֹר אֶת אָבִיו״?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Joseph merited** to bury his father, resulting in a display of great honor to his father. The Gemara begins its discussion of the burial of Jacob by asking: **What is different initially that it is written: "And Joseph went up to bury his father; and with him went up all the servants of Pharaoh,** the elders of his house, and all the elders of the land of Egypt" (Genesis 50:7), **and afterward** it says in the following verse: **"And all the house of Joseph, and his brethren, and his father's house;** only their little ones, and their flocks, and their herds, they left in the land of Goshen" (Genesis 50:8), indicating that the brothers of Joseph were second in importance to the Egyptians? **And what is different at the end that it is written: "And Joseph returned into Egypt, he, and his brethren," and afterward** it states: **"And all that went up with him to bury his father,** after he had buried his father" (Genesis 50:14), placing the brothers before the Egyptians?

The *Gemara* now moves to the burial of Yaakov, examining the *pasuk*: *vaya'al Yosef likbor et aviv vaya'alu ito kol avdei Pharaoh* — Yosef went up to bury his father, accompanied by all the servants of Pharaoh. The *Gemara* notes that on the way out, Pharaoh's entourage preceded Yosef's own family — *kol beit Yosef ve'echav u-veit aviv* — yet on the return journey, the order was reversed: *vayashov Yosef Mitzraima hu ve'echav v'hadar kol ha-olim ito likbor et aviv*, where Yosef and his family are mentioned first. Rabbi Yochanan explains: *batchila shelo ra'u bikhvodan shel Yisrael* — initially the Egyptians had not yet witnessed the honor afforded to Israel, so they did not show them honor; *ul'vasof she-ra'u bikhvodan, nahagu bahem kavod* — in the end, once they witnessed that honor firsthand, they treated them accordingly. The *Gemara* now turns to where this transformation of honor occurred.

### Atad & Crowns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִילָּה עַד שֶׁלֹּא רָאוּ בִּכְבוֹדָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל — לֹא נָהֲגוּ בָּהֶן כָּבוֹד, וּלְבַסּוֹף שֶׁרָאוּ בִּכְבוֹדָן — נָהֲגוּ בָּהֶן כָּבוֹד. דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּבֹאוּ עַד גּוֹרֶן הָאָטָד״, וְכִי גוֹרֶן יֵשׁ לוֹ לָאָטָד? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִקִּיפוּהוּ כְּתָרִים לַאֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יַעֲקֹב כְּגוֹרֶן זֶה שֶׁמַּקִּיפִים לוֹ אָטָד, שֶׁבָּאוּ בְּנֵי עֵשָׂו וּבְנֵי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וּבְנֵי קְטוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Initially, before** the Egyptians **saw the honor of the Jewish people,** as the Gemara will soon explain, **they did not treat them** with **honor,** so the brothers were behind the servants of Pharaoh. **And in the end, when they saw their honor, they treated** the brothers with **honor.** The Gemara explains what honor was accorded to the family of Jacob: **As it is written: "And they came to the threshing floor of Atad,** which is beyond the Jordan, and there they wailed with a very great and sore wailing; and he made a mourning for his father seven days" (Genesis 50:10). The word atad is the name of the boxthorn bush. **And does a boxthorn** bush **have a threshing floor?** Thorns are not collected and eaten. **Rabbi Abbahu says:** This **teaches that they surrounded the casket of Jacob with crowns, like this threshing floor that is surrounded with boxthorns, because the children of Esau and the children of Ishmael and the children of Keturah** all **came** to the burial of Jacob.

The *Gemara* cites the verse *vayavo'u ad goren ha-Atad* — they came to the threshing floor of the Atad, meaning thornbushes or thistles. The *Gemara* asks: *v'chi goren yesh l'atad* — who makes a threshing floor for thorns? Rabbi Abbahu explains: *melamed she-hikifuhu ktarim l'arono shel Yaakov* — what the verse is describing is that they surrounded Yaakov's coffin with crowns, the various kings who came to pay their respects each placing a crown there. The word *atad*, with its connotation of sharp thorns, thus evokes the image of a *goren* — a threshing floor — surrounded by a fence of thorns, which Rashi understands as a barrier to keep animals out. But the deeper image is of Yaakov's coffin encircled by the pointed crowns of kings.

### Descendants' Arrival

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: כּוּלָּם לַמִּלְחָמָה בָּאוּ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאוּ כִּתְרוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף תָּלוּי בַּאֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יַעֲקֹב, נָטְלוּ כּוּלָּן כִּתְרֵיהֶן וּתְלָאוּם בַּאֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught:** Initially, **they all came to** wage **war** with the family of Jacob, but **once they saw the crown of Joseph,** the viceroy of Egypt, **hanging on the casket of Jacob, they all took their crowns and hung them on the casket of Jacob.**

A *Braitah* is cited: *kulam lemilchamah ba'u* — they had not come to pay honor but to make war. Bnei Esav, Bnei Yishmael, and Bnei Keturah — all the descendants of Avraham outside of the line of Yaakov — came with the intention of attacking the Israelite family as they returned with Yaakov's coffin into the land they had been occupying.

### Yosef's Crown

Yet when they saw Yosef's crown suspended over Yaakov's coffin — *kivan she-ra'u kitro shel Yosef taluy be'arono shel Yaakov* — Yosef being the viceroy of Egypt and the most powerful figure present — their intention shifted. *Natlu kulam kitreihem u-tla'um be'arono shel Yaakov* — they all took their own crowns and hung them on Yaakov's coffin as well. The irony is apparent: the Egyptians in Pharaoh's entourage came to show honor to Yosef as an Egyptian dignitary, not specifically to honor Bnei Yisrael — and yet the net effect was a massive public demonstration of honor to Yaakov.

### 36 Crowns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה כְּתָרִים נִתְלוּ בַּאֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught: Thirty-six crowns were hung on the casket of Jacob.** This was the great honor accorded to the family of Jacob.

A *Braitah* teaches: *sheloshim ve-shishah ktarim nitlu be'arono shel Yaakov* — thirty-six crowns were ultimately hung on Yaakov's coffin. Rashi notes that one can roughly arrive at this number by tallying the princes listed in the genealogies of Esav and Yishmael, arriving close to thirty-six even if the count does not come out exactly.

### Esav's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (מֵאֵילּוּ מֵאֵילּוּ) כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעוּ לִמְעָרַת הַמַּכְפֵּלָה, אֲתָא עֵשָׂו קָא מְעַכֵּב, אָמַר לָהֶן: ״מַמְרֵא קִרְיַת הָאַרְבַּע הִיא חֶבְרוֹן״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: קִרְיַת אַרְבַּע — אַרְבַּע זוּגוֹת הָיוּ: אָדָם וְחַוָּה, אַבְרָהָם וְשָׂרָה, יִצְחָק וְרִבְקָה, יַעֲקֹב וְלֵאָה. אִיהוּ קַבְרַהּ לְלֵאָה בְּדִידֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּפָיֵישׁ — דִּידִי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Once they reached the Cave of Machpelah, Esau came and was preventing them** from burying Jacob there. **He said to them:** It says: "And Jacob came unto Isaac his father to **Mamre, to Kiryat Arba, the same is Hebron,** where Abraham and Isaac sojourned" (Genesis 35:27). **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** It is called **Kiryat Arba** because there **were four couples** buried there: **Adam and Eve, Abraham and Sarah, Isaac and Rebecca, Jacob and Leah.** Esau said: Jacob **buried Leah in his** spot, **and the** spot **that is remaining is mine.**

When they arrived at *Me'arat haMachpelah*, Esav came to obstruct the burial. The *Gemara* provides background via the teaching of Rav Yitzchak: *Kiryat Arba, arba zugot hayu* — the name Kiryat Arba, the city of the four, refers to the four pairs buried there: Adam and Chavah, Avraham and Sarah, Yitzchak and Rivkah, and Yaakov and Leah. Three and a half pairs were already interred. Esav argued: Yaakov buried Leah in his portion, and the remaining niche is mine. Even if I sold the privileges of the firstborn, *peshitosah mi zvini* — I did not sell my basic rights as a regular son, and therefore my share of the cave remains mine.

### Document Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: זַבֵּינְתֵּהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: נְהִי דְּזַבֵּינִי בְּכֵירוּתָא, פְּשִׁיטוּתָא מִי זַבֵּינִי? אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: אִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּקִבְרִי אֲשֶׁר כָּרִיתִי לִי״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: אֵין ״כִּירָה״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן מְכִירָה, שֶׁכֵּן בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם קוֹרִין לִמְכִירָה ״כִּירָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The children of Jacob **said to** Esau: **You sold** your rights **to** Jacob. Esau **said to them: Though I sold the birthright, did I** also **sell** my rights to the burial site as **an ordinary** brother? The brothers **said to him: Yes,** you also sold to Jacob those rights, **as it is written** that Joseph stated: "My father made me swear, saying: Behold, I die; **in my grave that I have dug [*kariti*] for me** in the land of Canaan, there shall you bury me" (Genesis 50:5). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak:** The word ***kira*** in the verse **is nothing other** than **a term of a sale [*mekhira*]** sharing a similar root, **because in the cities overseas they call a sale *kira*.**

Bnei Yaakov responded that he had in fact sold all rights, citing Yaakov's own declaration *bekivri asher kariti li* — "my grave that I have acquired for myself." Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak explains: *ein kirah ela leshon mekhirah* — the word *kira* in this *pasuk* means not to dig but to purchase, as on the islands (*karkei hayam*) the word for purchase is *kira*. Thus Yaakov's insistence on *kariti li* — I acquired this for myself — strongly implies that his ownership of the specific niche was not merely ancestral but was actively established and transferred to him. Esav demanded proof: *havu li igarta* — show me the document. Bnei Yaakov replied that the document had been left behind in Egypt.

### Naftali's Mission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לִי אִיגַּרְתָּא! אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: אִיגַּרְתָּא בְּאַרְעָא דְמִצְרַיִם הִיא. וּמַאן נֵיזִיל — נֵיזִיל נַפְתָּלִי דְּקַלִּיל כִּי אַיָּילְתָּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״נַפְתָּלִי אַיָּלָה שְׁלוּחָה הַנּוֹתֵן אִמְרֵי שָׁפֶר״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״אִמְרֵי שָׁפֶר״, אֶלָּא ״אִמְרֵי סֵפֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** Esau **said to them: Bring the bill** of sale **to me,** i.e., you can't prove your claims. **They said to him: The bill** of sale **is in the land of Egypt.** They said: **And who will go** to bring it? **Naphtali will go, for he is as fast as a doe, as it is written: "Naphtali is a doe let loose, he gives goodly words"** (Genesis 49:21). **Rabbi Abbahu says: Do not read** it as "**goodly words [*imrei shafer*]"; rather,** read it as ***imrei sefer*,** i.e., the words of the book, as he returned to Egypt to retrieve the bill of sale.

There was no difficulty in retrieving it quickly: *nizil Naftali* — let us send Naftali, *dekal ke'ayalta* — who is as swift as a hind, as the *pasuk* says *Naftali ayalah shelucha ha-noten imrei shefer* — Naftali is a swift deer who delivers beautiful words. Rabbi Abbahu applies *al tikri* here: *al tikri imrei shefer ela imrei sefer* — do not read "beautiful words" but "words of the document," for Naftali was being sent to fetch the written proof.

### Chushim Kills Esav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּשִׁים בְּרֵיהּ דְּדָן תַּמָּן הֲוָה, וְיַקִּירָן לֵיהּ אוּדְנֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי הַאי? וְאָמְרוּ לֵיהּ: קָא מְעַכֵּב הַאי עַד דְּאָתֵי נַפְתָּלִי מֵאַרְעָא דְּמִצְרַיִם. אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְעַד דְּאָתֵי נַפְתָּלִי מֵאַרְעָא דְּמִצְרַיִם, יְהֵא אֲבִי אַבָּא מוּטָל בְּבִזָּיוֹן? שְׁקַל קוּלְפָא מַחְיֵיהּ אַרֵישֵׁיהּ, נָתְרָן עֵינֵיהּ וּנְפַלוּ אַכַּרְעָא דְיַעֲקֹב, פַּתְחִינְהוּ יַעֲקֹב לְעֵינֵיהּ וְאַחֵיךְ. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״יִשְׂמַח צַדִּיק כִּי חָזָה נָקָם פְּעָמָיו יִרְחַץ בְּדַם הָרָשָׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Hushim, the son of Dan, was there and his ears were heavy,** i.e., he was hard of hearing. **He said to them: What** is **this** that is delaying the burial? **And they said to him: This one,** Esau, **is preventing** us from burying Jacob **until Naphtali comes** back **from the land of Egypt** with the bill of sale. **He said to them: And until Naphtali comes** back **from the land of Egypt will** our **father's father lie in degradation? He took a club [*kulepa*] and hit** Esau **on the head,** and Esau's **eyes fell out and they fell on the legs of Jacob. Jacob opened his eyes and smiled. And this is that which is written: "The righteous shall rejoice when he sees the vengeance; he shall wash his feet in the blood of the wicked"** (Psalms 58:11).

While everyone waited for Naftali to return from Egypt, Chushim son of Dan was present. He was *yakiran leh udneih* — hard of hearing, literally his ears were heavy. He asked what was happening, and was told that Esav was holding up the burial until Naftali returned from Egypt with the document. Chushim's reaction was immediate: *ve'ad de'ati Naftali me'ara demitzrayim yehei avi abba mutal be-vizayon?* — Until Naftali returns from Egypt, my father's father should lie there in disgrace? He took a rod, struck Esav on the head, and Esav's eyes fell out and landed on Yaakov's lap — the coffin being evidently open. *Patchinhu Yaakov le'einei* — Yaakov opened his eyes and smiled. The *Gemara* cites the verse *yismach tzaddik ki chaza nakam* — the righteous will rejoice when he sees retribution done, *pe'amav yirchatz bedam harasha* — and will wash his feet in the blood of the wicked.

One might note a difficulty: the *pesukim* seem to record a genuine reconciliation between Yaakov and Esav. Why is this narrative, with its violent resolution, the satisfying endpoint? The *Gemara*'s presentation here likely reflects the perspective of *Chazal* who, living under Roman — and later other — oppressive powers, understood Esav as a recurring historical antagonist to Bnei Yisrael. What read as personal reconciliation in the *pesukim* becomes, in the broader sweep of history, an unresolved tension that demands ultimate resolution. There is something genuinely comforting, from within that framework, in the image of the righteous smiling at the sight of justice.

### Yaakov Laughs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נִתְקַיְּימָה נְבוּאָתָהּ שֶׁל רִבְקָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לָמָּה אֶשְׁכַּל גַּם שְׁנֵיכֶם יוֹם אֶחָד״. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמִיתָתָן לֹא בְּיוֹם אֶחָד הֲוַאי — קְבוּרָתָן מִיהָא בְּיוֹם אֶחָד הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **At that moment the prophecy of Rebecca was fulfilled, as it is written** that Rebecca said of Jacob and Esau: **"Why should I be bereaved of you both in one day?"** (Genesis 27:45), as Rebecca foresaw that the future bereavement for both her sons would be on the same day. The Gemara comments: **And although their deaths were not on the same day, in any event their burials were on the same day,** as Esau was killed and buried on the same day that Jacob was buried.

*Be'otah sha'ah nitkayma nevo'atah shel Rivkah* — at that moment the prophecy of Rivkah was fulfilled. She had said *lamah eshkal gam shneichem yom echad* — why should I be bereaved of both of you in one day? Although Yaakov and Esav did not die on precisely the same day, *kevuratam miha beyom echad hava* — their burials occurred on the same day, and in that sense her prophecy found its fulfillment.

## Yosef-Moshe Parallel

### Yosef's Leadership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא [אִי]עֲסַק בֵּיהּ יוֹסֵף, אֶחָיו לָא הֲווֹ מִעַסְּקִי בֵּיהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׂאוּ אוֹתוֹ בָנָיו אַרְצָה כְּנַעַן״? אָמְרוּ: הַנִּיחוּ לוֹ, כְּבוֹדוֹ בִּמְלָכִים יוֹתֵר מִבְּהֶדְיוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss the involvement of Joseph and his brothers in the burial of their father: **And if Joseph would not have dealt with** the burial of Jacob, **would his brothers not have dealt with it? But isn't it written: "For his sons carried him into the land of Canaan,** and buried him in the cave of the field of Machpelah" (Genesis 50:13)? Since it is evident that the brothers were involved in the burial, why did they not deal with Jacob's burial needs from the outset? The Gemara answers: **They said: Allow** Joseph to take care of it, because there is **more honor** for our father to be prepared for burial **by royalty than by common** people **[*hedyotot*].**

The *Gemara* raises a question about the earlier *pasuk*: if Yosef's brothers were also involved in carrying Yaakov up to Canaan — as the verse *vayisu oto vanav artza Kena'an* indicates — why does the primary *pasuk* credit Yosef alone? The *Gemara* explains: *amru hanichu lo* — the brothers said, let Yosef take the lead, *kevodo bi-mlachim yoter mibedyotot* — it is greater honor for Yaakov to be buried by kings than by ordinary people. They did not remove themselves from the burial; they deferred to Yosef's royal prominence to maximize the honor afforded to their father.

## Yosef-Moshe Parallel (II)

### Moshe's Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה חֲבִיבוֹת מִצְוֹת עַל מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, שֶׁכׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל כּוּלָּן נִתְעַסְּקוּ בַּבִּיזָּה, וְהוּא נִתְעַסֵּק בְּמִצְוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חֲכַם לֵב יִקַּח מִצְוֹת וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § It states further in the mishna: **Who, to us,** had **a greater** burial **than Joseph,** as it was none other than Moses who involved himself in transporting his coffin. **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta* (4:6–7): **Come and see how beloved mitzvot are to Moses our teacher. As,** at the time of the Exodus, **all the Jewish people were involved in** taking **the plunder** from Egypt, **and he was involved in** the performance of **mitzvot, as it is stated: "The wise in heart will take mitzvot"** (Proverbs 10:8).

Just as Yosef was the most prominent figure of his generation and took the lead in burying Yaakov, so too Moshe — the greatest of his generation — took it upon himself to carry Yosef's bones out of Egypt. The *Gemara* draws this as an explicit and intentional parallel, underscoring a principle of reciprocal care for the dead across generations. The narrative of Yosef carrying Yaakov's bones from Egypt to Canaan is thus mirrored in Moshe carrying Yosef's bones from Egypt to Canaan. There is moreover a deep sense of closure in this: the long arc of Yosef's oath to the brothers, the period of enslavement during which the hope of redemption may have seemed forgotten, and the ultimate fulfillment of the return to the land — all are gathered together in the act of Moshe lifting Yosef's bones.

## Moshe Gets Yosef

### Moshe & Mitzvot

The essay continues directly from where the previous blockquote ended, with no new Sefaria text requiring insertion. The *Gemara* has already established Moshe's devotion to mitzvot as shown in the teaching of the Sages.

### Serach bat Asher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ הֵיכָן יוֹסֵף קָבוּר? אָמְרוּ: סֶרַח בַּת אָשֵׁר נִשְׁתַּיְּירָה מֵאוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר. הָלַךְ מֹשֶׁה אֶצְלָהּ, אָמַר לָהּ: כְּלוּם אַתְּ יוֹדַעַת הֵיכָן יוֹסֵף קָבוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where did Moses our teacher know where Joseph was buried?** The Sages **said: Serah, the daughter of Asher, remained from that generation** that initially descended to Egypt with Jacob. **Moses went to her** and **said to her: Do you know anything** about **where Joseph is buried?**

How did Moshe know where Yosef was buried? *Amru: Serach bat Asher nishtayra me'oto hador* — Serach daughter of Asher was the sole survivor still living from the generation of Yosef. Moshe went to her and asked: *klum at yoda'at heichan Yosef kavur?* The *Gemara* frames this detail with quiet pathos: if Bnei Yisrael had maintained the hope of redemption alive during the years of enslavement, one would expect them to have preserved the tradition of Yosef's burial place. That only this one elderly woman knew suggests how completely *yisrael* had despaired. It is also notable, as has been observed throughout this *sugya*, that it is women — Miriam, Serach — who serve as the keepers of tradition and the carriers of hope.

### Metal Casket

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לוֹ: אָרוֹן שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת עָשׂוּ לוֹ מִצְרִים וּקְבָעוּהוּ בְּנִילוּס הַנָּהָר, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְבָּרְכוּ מֵימָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **She said to him: The Egyptians fashioned a metal casket for him and set it in the Nile [*Nilus*] River** as an augury **so that its water would be blessed.**

Serach told Moshe: *Aron shel matechet asu lo Mitzrayim* — the Egyptians had made Yosef a metal casket, *ukeva'uhu baNilus* — and they had sunk it in the Nile, *kedei sheyitbarchu meimav* — so that the waters of the Nile would be blessed by Yosef's presence. Yosef's capacity for sustaining Egypt through the famine had left such an impression that even in death the Egyptians sought to harness his blessing for their river.

### Moshe Calls Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָלַךְ מֹשֶׁה וְעָמַד עַל שְׂפַת נִילוּס, אָמַר לוֹ: יוֹסֵף יוֹסֵף! הִגִּיעַ הָעֵת שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁאֲנִי גּוֹאֵל אֶתְכֶם, וְהִגִּיעָה הַשְּׁבוּעָה שֶׁהִשְׁבַּעְתָּ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִם אַתָּה מַרְאֶה עַצְמְךָ מוּטָב, אִם לָאו — הֲרֵי אָנוּ מְנוּקִּין מִשְּׁבוּעָתֶךָ. מִיָּד צָף אֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Moses went and stood on the bank of the Nile.** He **said to** Joseph: **Joseph, Joseph, the time has arrived** about **which the Holy One, Blessed be He, took an oath** saying **that I,** i.e., God, **will redeem you. And** the time for fulfillment of **the oath that you administered to the Jewish people** that they will bury you in Eretz Yisrael **has arrived. If you show yourself,** it is **good,** but **if not, we are clear from your oath. Immediately, the casket of Joseph floated** to the top of the water.

*Halach Moshe ve'amad al sfat Nilus* — Moshe went and stood at the bank of the Nile and called out: *Yosef, Yosef, higa zman she-nishba HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — the time has come that God swore to redeem, *ve-higi'ah ha-shvu'ah she-hishbata Yisrael* — and the oath that you made Israel swear has also arrived. *Im ata mareh atzmacha mutav; ve'im lav, harei anu menukim mi-shvu'atecha* — if you show yourself, good; if not, we are absolved of your oath. We have done everything we can. Immediately, *miyad tzaf arono shel Yosef* — Yosef's coffin floated to the surface.

### Elisha Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַל תִּתְמַהּ הֵיאַךְ בַּרְזֶל צָף, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּתִיב: ״וַיְהִי הָאֶחָד מַפִּיל הַקּוֹרָה וְאֶת הַבַּרְזֶל נָפַל אֶל הַמָּיִם וְגוֹ׳ אֲהָהּ אֲדוֹנִי וְהוּא שָׁאוּל. וַיֹּאמֶר אִישׁ הָאֱלֹהִים אָנָה נָפָל וַיַּרְאֵהוּ אֶת הַמָּקוֹם וַיִּקְצׇב עֵץ וַיַּשְׁלֶךְ שָׁמָּה וַיָּצֶף הַבַּרְזֶל״. וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֱלִישָׁע תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל אֵלִיָּהוּ, וְאֵלִיָּהוּ תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה — צָף בַּרְזֶל מִפָּנָיו, מִפְּנֵי מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And do not wonder how iron** can **float, as it is written** in the verses describing how Elisha was able to cause iron to float: **"But as one was felling a beam, the ax head fell into the water;** and he cried, and said: **Alas, my master! For it was borrowed. And the man of God said: Where did it fall? And he showed him the place. And he cut down a stick, and cast it in there, and the iron floated up"** (II Kings 6:5–6). **And are** these **matters not** inferred ***a fortiori*: And just as Elisha,** who was a mere **student of Elijah, and Elijah** was a mere **student of Moses,** as Elijah studied the Torah of Moses, was able to cause the **iron** to **float before him, all the more so** would it float **before Moses our teacher** himself.

The *Gemara* anticipates the obvious question: how does iron float? It cites the episode from *Sefer Melachim* in which Elisha caused a borrowed iron axe-head that had fallen into the water to float by throwing a piece of wood into the spot where it had sunk. From this a *kal vachomer* is constructed: if the iron floated for Elisha, who was a student of Eliyahu who was a student of Moshe, *bifnei Moshe Rabbeinu al achat kamah vechamah* — how much more readily would iron float for Moshe himself.

### Crypt Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: בְּקַבַּרְנִיט שֶׁל מְלָכִים הָיָה קָבוּר. הָלַךְ מֹשֶׁה וְעָמַד עַל קַבַּרְנִיט שֶׁל מְלָכִים, אָמַר: יוֹסֵף, הִגִּיעַ עֵת שֶׁנִּשְׁבַּע הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁאֲנִי גּוֹאֵל אֶתְכֶם, וְהִגִּיעָה שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁהִשְׁבַּעְתָּ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִם אַתָּה מַרְאֶה עַצְמְךָ — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — הֲרֵי אָנוּ מְנוּקִּין מִשְּׁבוּעָתֶךָ. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נִזְדַּעְזַע אֲרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף, נְטָלוֹ מֹשֶׁה, וֶהֱבִיאוֹ אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now presents a different version of where Joseph was buried. **Rabbi Natan says:** Joseph **was buried in the crypt [*kabbarnit*] of kings. Moses went and stood by the crypt of kings** and **said: Joseph, the time has arrived** about **which the Holy One, Blessed be He, took an oath** saying **that: I will redeem you. And** the time for fulfillment of **the oath that you administered to the Jewish people** that they will bury you in Eretz Yisrael **has arrived. If you show yourself,** it is **good, but if not, we are clear from your oath. At that moment, the casket of Joseph shook** among the caskets. **Moses took it and brought it** over **to himself.**

Rabbi Natan offers an alternative tradition: Yosef was not buried in the Nile at all but *bikvarnit shel melachim* — in the royal crypt, the catacombs of the kings of Egypt. According to this view, Moshe went to the royal crypt, made the same declaration — *Yosef, higi'a et she-nishba HaKadosh Baruch Hu she'ani go'el etchem* — and at that moment *nizdaze'ah arono shel Yosef*, Yosef's coffin shook free from its niche, and Moshe removed it.

### Two Arks Travel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל אוֹתָן שָׁנִים שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר, הָיוּ שְׁנֵי אֲרוֹנוֹת הַלָּלוּ, אֶחָד שֶׁל מֵת וְאֶחָד שֶׁל שְׁכִינָה, מְהַלְּכִין זֶה עִם זֶה, וְהָיוּ עוֹבְרִין וְשָׁבִין אוֹמְרִים: מָה טִיבָן שֶׁל שְׁנֵי אֲרוֹנוֹת הַלָּלוּ? אָמְרוּ: אֶחָד שֶׁל מֵת וְאֶחָד שֶׁל שְׁכִינָה. וְכִי מָה דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל מֵת לְהַלֵּךְ עִם שְׁכִינָה? אָמְרוּ: קִיֵּים זֶה כׇּל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And all those years that the Jewish people were in the wilderness, these two arks, one** a casket **of a dead man,** Joseph, **and one** the Ark **of the Divine Presence,** i.e., the Ark of the Covenant, **were traveling together, and passersby would say: What is the nature of these two arks? They said** to them: **One** is **of a dead person and one** is **of the Divine Presence.** The passersby would ask: **And** in **what** way is it **the manner of a dead person to travel with the Divine Presence? They said** in response: **This** one, i.e., the deceased Joseph, **fulfilled all that is written in this,** i.e., the Torah.

*Vechol otan shanim she-hayu Yisrael bamidbar hayu shnei aronot halalu mehalchim zeh im zeh* — all those years that Israel traveled in the wilderness, these two arks traveled together: *echad shel met* — one containing a dead body, Yosef, *ve'echad shel Shechinah* — and the other the *aron* of God's presence containing the *Luchot HaBrit*. They were not side by side in the strict sense — the *Gemara* elsewhere derives from Yosef that a corpse may enter the *machaneh Leviyah* but not the *machaneh Shechinah* — but they traveled in close proximity.

### Passersby Question

*Vehayu ovrim u-shavim omrim: mah tivan shel shnei aronot halalu* — passersby would ask: what are these two *aronot*? They would be told: one contains a dead body and the other contains the divine presence. *Vechi mah darko shel met le-halech im Shechinah* — what right does a dead body have to travel alongside the divine presence? The image is genuinely startling — and, as one might observe, carries a visual resonance that would have seemed deeply disturbing from the outside. The *Gemara* does not shy away from this tension.

### Yosef Fulfilled

The answer given: *kiyem zeh kol mah she-katuv bazeh* — this one, Yosef, fulfilled everything written in that one, the *luchot*. The *Gemara* connects this to the practice, known from other contexts, of placing a *sefer Torah* in the *aron* of a great *talmid chacham* during his *levayah*, and saying *ki ein zeh mah she-katuv bazeh* — for this one fulfilled what is written in that one. Yosef is thus presented as the original paradigm of one whose life so completely embodied the Torah that his *aron* properly accompanies the *aron* of the *Shechinah*.

### Israel's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא עֲסִיק בֵּיהּ מֹשֶׁה — יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא הֲווֹ מִיעַסְקִי בֵּיהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת עַצְמוֹת יוֹסֵף אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱלוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרַיִם קָבְרוּ בִשְׁכֶם״! וְתוּ: אִי לָא אִיעֲסַקוּ בֵּיהּ יִשְׂרָאֵל — בָּנָיו לָא הֲווֹ מִיעַסְקִי בֵּיהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיִּהְיוּ לִבְנֵי יוֹסֵף לְנַחֲלָה״?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if Moses had not dealt with** the burial of Joseph, **would the Jewish people not have dealt with it? But isn't it written** that after Moses died: **"And the bones of Joseph, which the children of Israel brought up out of Egypt, they buried in Shechem"** (Joshua 24:32), which indicates that the Jewish people completed the burial of Joseph? **And furthermore, if the Jewish people had not dealt with** Joseph's burial, **would his children not have dealt with it? But isn't it written** in that same verse: **"And they became the inheritance of the children of Joseph,"** as Joseph was buried in Shechem, which was then given to his descendants? Therefore, the question arises: Why did Joseph's descendants initially leave the task of his burial to the Jewish people and Moses?

The *Gemara* then poses the same structural question it raised earlier regarding Yosef and Yaakov: *ve-illu asik beh Moshe, Yisrael lo havu ma'aski beh?* If Moshe had not taken the initiative, would Israel not have done it themselves? Indeed, the *pasuk* says *et atzmot Yosef asher he'elu Bnei Yisrael mi-Mitzrayim kavru be-Shechem* — and Bnei Yisrael buried the bones that *they* brought up. A further question: and if all of Israel would not have done it, would not Yosef's own children have seen to it? For another *pasuk* links the land of Shechem specifically to *bnei Yosef* as an inheritance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ: הַנִּיחוּ לוֹ, כְּבוֹדוֹ בִּמְרוּבִּים יוֹתֵר מִבְּמוּעָטִין. וְתוּ אָמְרוּ: הַנִּיחוּ לוֹ, כְּבוֹדוֹ בִּגְדוֹלִים יוֹתֵר מִבִּקְטַנִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **They said: Leave** Joseph for others. **It is more of an honor for** Joseph to be buried **by the many than by the few,** and therefore it is better that the Jewish people be involved in the burial. **And furthermore, they said: Leave** Joseph for others. **It is more of an honor for** Joseph to be buried **by** one of **the great** men like Moses **than by lesser ones** like us.

The answer follows the same logic as before: *amru hanichu lo*. Bnei Yosef said, let Bnei Yisrael do it, *kevodo bi-mrubeh yotze mi-mi'ut* — the honor is greater when the multitude carries it out than when only a few do. And Bnei Yisrael in turn said, let Moshe do it, *kevodo bigdolim yoter mibiketanim* — it is greater honor when a great one does it than when lesser ones do.

### Completion Gets Credit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָשׁוּ קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח מֹשֶׁה אֶת עַצְמוֹת יוֹסֵף עִמּוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת עַצְמוֹת יוֹסֵף אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱלוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳״! אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כָּל הָעוֹשֶׂה דָּבָר וְלֹא גְּמָרוֹ, וּבָא אַחֵר וּגְמָרוֹ — מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב עַל שֶׁגְּמָרוֹ כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The verses contradict each other,** as **it is written: "And Moses took the bones of Joseph with him"** (Exodus 13:19), **and it is written** elsewhere: **"And the bones of Joseph, which the children of Israel brought up** out of Egypt" (Joshua 24:32). Who in fact took Joseph's bones? **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Anyone who performs a matter but does not complete it, and then another comes and completes it, the verse ascribes credit to the one who completed it as if he had** actually **performed** the entire act. Due to the fact that the children of Israel completed Joseph's burial, the Torah ascribes them credit as if they had performed the entire act.

But then there is a direct contradiction in the *pesukim* — one verse says *vayikach Moshe et atzmot Yosef imo*, Moshe took the bones, and another says *asher he'elu Bnei Yisrael* — which Bnei Yisrael brought up. Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina resolves this with a general principle: *kol oseh davar velo gmaro, u-va acher u-gmaro, ma'aleh alav ha-katuv al she-gmaro ke-illu asao* — whoever begins something and does not complete it, and another comes and completes it, the credit goes entirely to the one who finishes. A *mitzvah* is called by the name of the one who completes it — *ein ha-mitzvah nikret ela al shem gomrah*. Since it was Bnei Yisrael who ultimately buried Yosef in Shechem, the *pasuk* attributes the entire act to them.

### Brought Down & Demoted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף מוֹרִידִין אוֹתוֹ מִגְּדוּלָּתוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי בָּעֵת הַהִיא וַיֵּרֶד יְהוּדָה״. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: אַף קוֹבֵר אִשְׁתּוֹ וּבָנָיו, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַתָּמׇת בַּת שׁוּעַ אֵשֶׁת יְהוּדָה וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּמׇת עֵר וְאוֹנָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** with regard to one who initiates performance of a mitzva but does not complete it when capable of doing so: **He is also demoted [*moridin*] from his** position of **greatness, as it is written: "And it came to pass at that time, that Judah went down [*vayyered*]** from his brethren, and turned in to a certain Adullamite, whose name was Hirah" (Genesis 38:1). Usage of the term "went down" indicates that the rest of Judah's brothers had demoted him from his position of greatness because he began the process of saving Joseph, but he did not complete it. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** The episode with regard to Judah also indicates that one who initiates performance of a mitzva but does not complete it will **also bury his wife and children** as Judah did, **as it is written: "And** in process of time **Shua's daughter, the wife of Judah, died"** (Genesis 38:12), **and it is written** further: "And the sons of Judah: Er, and Onan, and Shelah, and Perez, and Zerah; **but Er and Onan died** in the land of Canaan" (Genesis 46:12).

Rabbi Elazar adds a sobering corollary: *af moridin oto migdulato* — one who begins and fails to complete is not merely passed over but is brought down from his greatness. The *pasuk* records *vayehi ba'eit vayered Yehuda* — Yehuda went down — because he set in motion the effort to save Yosef but did not see it through. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani extends this even further: such a person may even bury his wife and children, as indeed Yehuda did — *vatamat bat Shua eishet Yehuda*, and *vayehi vayamat Er ve-Onan*. The *Gemara* presents this as *mussar* — pointed moral instruction — rather than strict legal analysis.

### Bones Self-Reference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מִפְּנֵי מָה נִקְרָא יוֹסֵף עֲצָמוֹת בְּחַיָּיו — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא מִיחָה בִּכְבוֹד אָבִיו, דְּקָאָמְרִי לֵיהּ ״עַבְדְּךָ אָבִינוּ״, וְלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: For what** reason **was Joseph called: Bones,** even **during his lifetime,** as he had his brothers take an oath that "God will surely remember you, and you shall carry up my bones from here" (Genesis 50:25)? **Because he did not protest for the honor of his father, as** the brothers **said to** Joseph while unaware of his true identity: **"Your servant our father"** (Genesis 43:28, 44:31), **and** Joseph **said nothing to them** in protest that they referred to his father Jacob as Joseph's servant.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav asks: why did Yosef refer to himself with the word *atzmotai* — my bones — during his own lifetime, saying *ve-ha'alitem et atzmotai mizeh itchem*? The answer: *mipnei she-lo micha bikhvod aviv* — because he failed to protest on behalf of his father's honor. When his brothers stood before him without yet knowing who he was, they referred to Yaakov as *avdecha avinu* — your servant our father — and Yosef said nothing to correct the disrespectful formulation. Because he did not defend his father's dignity, his own honor was diminished, and he spoke of himself as *bones* — a term of lowered status — even in life.

### Early Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה מֵת יוֹסֵף קוֹדֶם לְאֶחָיו — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִנְהִיג עַצְמוֹ בְּרַבָּנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says, and some say** that this was said by **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina: For what** reason **did Joseph predecease his brothers,** as is indicated from his requesting of them to take care of his burial needs? **Because** Joseph **acted authoritatively,** and such behavior can reduce one's life span.

Rav Yehuda citing Rav — and some say Rav Chama bar Rabbi Chanina — asks further: *mipnei mah meit Yosef kodem le'echav* — why did Yosef die before his brothers? *Mipnei she-inhig atzmo be-rabbanus* — because he conducted himself with an air of authority. The word *hinhig atzmo* implies that he gave himself superior airs, throwing his considerable weight around, asserting his status and position. This — rather than simply holding authority, which was unavoidable given his position as viceroy — is the fault attributed to him.

### Brought Down Egypt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיוֹסֵף הוּרַד מִצְרָיְמָה״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״הוּרַד״ אֶלָּא ״הוֹרִיד״, שֶׁהוֹרִיד אִיצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה מִגְּדוּלָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** After describing that Judah "went down" from his greatness, the Gemara discusses a similar term employed with regard to Joseph, as the verse states: **"And Joseph was brought down [*hurad*] to Egypt"** (Genesis 39:1). **Rabbi Elazar says: Do not read** the word as **"*hurad*,"** meaning that he was passively brought down, **but** rather read it as ***horid*,** meaning: He, Joseph, brought down others, **as** Joseph **brought down the astrologers [*itztagninei*] of Pharaoh from their** position of **eminence** because he knew the interpretation of Pharaoh's dreams when they did not.

The *Gemara* returns to the verse *Yosef hu hurad Mitzraima* — Yosef was brought down to Egypt. Rabbi Elazar applies *al tikri*: *al tikri hurad ela horid* — do not read "was brought down" but "brought down," for Yosef ultimately brought down *itztigninei Pharaoh migdulatan* — the astrologers of Pharaoh from their greatness. He alone could interpret the dreams that confounded them all, and in doing so he effectively displaced them.

### Potiphar & Castration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּקְנֵהוּ פּוֹטִיפַר סְרִיס פַּרְעֹה״, אָמַר רַב: שֶׁקְּנָאוֹ לְעַצְמוֹ. בָּא (גַּבְרִיאֵל) [מִיכָאֵל] וְסֵירְסוֹ, בָּא גַּבְרִיאֵל וּפֵירְעוֹ. מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּתִיב ״פּוֹטִיפַר״, וּלְבַסּוֹף ״פּוֹטִיפֶרַע״.
>
> **Translation:** The continuation of that verse states: **"And Potiphar, an officer [*seris*] of Pharaoh's,** the captain of the guard, an Egyptian, **bought him** from the hand of the Ishmaelites, who had brought him down there" (Genesis 39:1). **Rav says: He purchased** the handsome Joseph **for himself,** for the intended purpose of homosexual intercourse, but was unable to fulfill his desires, as the angel **Gabriel came and castrated** Potiphar **[*seireso*].** Then **Gabriel came** again **and** further **mutilated** him **[*fero*]** in the same part of his body. This is alluded to in the verses that write Potiphar's name differently: **Initially, it is written "Potiphar"** (Genesis 39:1) **and in the end it is written "Potiphera"** (Genesis 41:45). The change in his name indicates that a part of himself was mutilated.

The verse *vayiknehu Potifar saris Pharaoh* raises a question: what is a *saris* — a eunuch — doing with a wife? Rav explains: *sheknaho le'atzmo* — he bought Yosef for his own personal use, meaning his intentions toward Yosef were sexual in nature, and he was not yet a *saris* at the time of purchase. Thereupon *ba Gavriel ve-sirso* — the angel Gavriel came and castrated him. The *Gemara* also notes a variant reading *ufara'o*, which Rashi explains as referring to the *peri'ah* — a more complete form of mutilation, analogous to the second stage of *milah* in which the thin membrane beneath the foreskin is pulled back. The linguistic evidence is the shift in the text: the name first appears as *Potifar* and subsequently as *Potifera*, the added syllable reflecting the act of *peri'ah* performed upon him.

## Moshe's Final Days

### Enough for You

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָנוּ גָּדוֹל מִמֹּשֶׁה וְכוּ׳. ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֵלַי רַב לָךְ״, אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: בְּ״רַב״ בִּישֵּׂר, בְּ״רַב״ בִּישְּׂרוּהוּ. בְּ״רַב״ בִּישֵּׂר — ״רַב לָכֶם״. בְּ״רַב״ בִּישְּׂרוּהוּ — ״רַב לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Who, to us,** had **a greater** burial **than Moses,** as no one involved himself in his burial other than the Omnipresent Himself. The Gemara teaches: When Moses relates how God responded to him when denying his request to enter Eretz Yisrael, he states: **"And the Lord said to me: Let it suffice for you [*rav lakh*];** speak no more to Me of this matter" (Deuteronomy 3:26). **Rabbi Levi says:** Moses **proclaimed** to the Jewish people when rebuking them **with** the term **"*rav*,"** and therefore **it was proclaimed to him with** the term **"*rav*"** that he would not enter Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara explains: **He proclaimed with** the term **"*rav*"** when speaking with the congregation of Korah: **"You take too much upon you [*rav lakhem*],** you sons of Levi" (Numbers 16:7), and **it was proclaimed to him with** the term **"*rav*,"** as God denied his request and said: **"Let it suffice for you [*rav lakh*]."**

The *Gemara* now turns to God's response to Moshe's repeated entreaties to be permitted to enter the land: *Rav lach* — it is enough for you, or more precisely, *rav lach* — it is too much for you. Rav Levi observes a kind of measure-for-measure quality in the language: *be-rav bisar, be-rav bisru* — Moshe informed others with the word *rav* — *rav lachem bnei Levi* — and he himself was informed with the word *rav*: *rav lach*.

### Rav Lach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״רַב לָךְ״ — רַב יֵשׁ לְךָ, וּמַנּוּ — יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״רַב לָךְ״, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ: הָרַב כַּמָּה קָשֶׁה, וְתַלְמִיד כַּמָּה סָרְבָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively:** God's telling Moses **"*rav lakh*"** was intended to mean: **You** now **have a *rav*,** a master, **and who is it?** It is **Joshua,** who has been chosen to lead the Jewish people. **Alternatively:** God's telling Moses **"*rav lakh*"** was intended to mean: You have a *rav*, i.e., God, Who says that you may not enter Eretz Yisrael. You must not importune Me anymore, **so that** people **should not say: How difficult is the Master and how obstinate is the student.** The Gemara asks: **And why** was Moses punished **so much** in that he was not allowed to enter Eretz Yisrael, despite being so righteous?

The *Gemara* offers multiple interpretations of *rav lach*. One: *rav yesh lach* — Moshe, there is already a *rav* here, a leader in the wings, and that is Yehoshua. Rashi adds that this also carries the implication that it is no longer Moshe's turn — Yehoshua's time has come. Another interpretation: *she-lo yomru ha-rav kama kasheh ve-hatalmid kama sarvan* — if Moshe continues petitioning, people will say: how intransigent is the master, and how pressuring is the student. Neither God's reputation nor Moshe's is served by the continued back-and-forth.

### High Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: לְפוּם גַּמְלָא שִׁיחְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why** was Moses punished **so much** in that he was not allowed to enter Eretz Yisrael, despite being so righteous? **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that the reason is based on the common aphorism: **Based on the camel is the burden.** In other words, a person is judged in accordance with his stature, and therefore a righteous individual will be punished greatly due to any sins he committed.

Why was God so exacting with Moshe to begin with? *Tana devei Rabbi Yishmael: lefum gamla shichna* — based on the camel is the load. The load placed on a camel corresponds to its capacity to bear it. Because Moshe was on such an elevated level, he was held to account for things that would not be reckoned against a lesser person. The great are measured by a higher standard.

### 120 Years Old

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵיהֶם בֶּן מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה אָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַיּוֹם״: הַיּוֹם מָלְאוּ יָמַי וּשְׁנוֹתַי.
>
> **Translation:** The verse relates what Moses said to the Jewish people at the end of his life: **"And he said to them: I am a hundred and twenty years old this day;** I can no longer go out and come in; and the Lord has said to me: You shall not go over this Jordan" (Deuteronomy 31:2). The wording is problematic, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** the term **"this day."** Moses said it in order to indicate: On **this day, my days and years have been completed** to be precisely one hundred and twenty, in order **to teach you that the Holy One, Blessed be He, completes the years of the righteous from day to day and from month to month, as it is written: "The number of your days I will fill"** (Exodus 23:26), indicating that the righteous will live out their years fully.

Moshe declared to Israel: *ben me'ah ve'esrim shana anochi hayom* — I am 120 years old today. The *Gemara* asks: why does it say *hayom* — today — unnecessarily? *Hayom malu yamai u-shnotai* — to tell you that today, this very day, my years are complete. This is also the proof that *tzaddikim* die on the anniversary of their birth: *God* fulfills *mispar yamecha amalei* — the number of your days I will fill — precisely, from day to day and from month to month.

### Gates of Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא אוּכַל עוֹד לָצֵאת וְלָבוֹא״. מַאי ״לָצֵאת וְלָבוֹא״? אִילֵימָא לָצֵאת וְלָבֹא מַמָּשׁ, וְהָכְתִיב: ״וּמֹשֶׁה בֶּן מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה בְּמוֹתוֹ לֹא נָס לֵחֹה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַל מֹשֶׁה מֵעַרְבֹת מוֹאָב אֶל הַר נְבוֹ״, וְתַנְיָא: שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲלוֹת הָיוּ שָׁם, וּפְסָעָן מֹשֶׁה בִּפְסִיעָה אַחַת. אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: לָצֵאת וְלָבוֹא בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּסְתַּתְּמוּ מִמֶּנּוּ שַׁעֲרֵי חׇכְמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"I can no longer go out and come in"** (Deuteronomy 31:2). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **"go out and come in"? If we say** it means literally that Moses was **actually** physically restricted from **going out and coming in, but isn't it written: "And Moses was a hundred and twenty years old when he died,** his eye was not dim, **nor his natural force abated"** (Deuteronomy 34:7), indicating that he was at full physical strength? **And it is written** further: **"And Moses went up from the plains of Moab to Mount Nebo"** (Deuteronomy 34:1). **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **There were twelve steps there** to ascend the mountain, **and Moses stepped over them** all in **one step,** also indicating that he was at full physical strength. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** The verse means that he could no longer **go out and come in with words of Torah.** This **teaches that the gates of wisdom were closed off to him.**

Moshe then says *lo uchal od latzet velavo* — I can no longer go out and come in. One might assume this refers to physical movement, but the *Gemara* objects: *u-Moshe ben me'ah ve'esrim shana bemoto lo chahat eino velo nas lecho* — at his death his eyes had not dimmed and his strength had not left him. Indeed, a *Braitah* teaches that when God told Moshe to ascend Har Nevo, *shtem esrei ma'alot hayu sham, u-psa'an Moshe be-psi'ah achat* — there were twelve elevations from the plains of Moav to the mountain, and Moshe traversed them all in a single leap. He clearly retained his full physical vigor.

### Leap to Nebo

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan therefore explains: *latzet velavo be-divrei Torah* — I can no longer go out and come in through words of Torah. *Melamed she-nistatmu mimenu sha'arei chochmah* — the gates of wisdom had been closed to him. He had lost his capacity for the kind of vigorous intellectual engagement with Torah — the going in and out, the argumentation and analysis — that had defined his entire life. There is something at once poignant and, perhaps, merciful in this: Moshe may have found it more possible to accept his death once he recognized that the faculty most precious to him was already departing.

## God Buries Moshe

### Shared Sabbath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּלֶךְ מֹשֶׁה וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ וַיִּתְיַצְּבוּ בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״. תָּנָא: אוֹתָהּ שַׁבָּת שֶׁל דְּיוֹ זוּגֵי הָיְתָה, נִיטְּלָה רְשׁוּת מִזֶּה, וְנִיתְּנָה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse discussing when Joshua was appointed to be the successor of Moses states: **"And Moses and Joshua went, and presented themselves in the Tent of Meeting"** (Deuteronomy 31:14). A Sage **taught: That Sabbath** when Moses died **was** a day **of two pairs [*deyo zugei*],** i.e., two wise men, Moses and Joshua, serving together in one place. **Authority was taken from one and given to the other.**

The *Gemara* notes the verse *vayeilech Moshe ve-Yehoshua ve-yityatzevu be-Ohel Moed* — Moshe and Yehoshua went and stood in the *Ohel Moed*. A *Tanna* teaches: *otah Shabbat shel dyo-zugi hayta* — that *Shabbat*, that particular period, was one of shared pair-leadership. *Nitlah me-zeh ve-nitnah le-zeh* — authority was being taken from Moshe and transferred to Yehoshua. The use of the word *Shabbat* here deliberately echoes the rabbinic institution of *Shabbat shel mi hayta* — whose week was it? — referring to the practice of rotating authority between two leaders, a phenomenon the rabbis were familiar with in their own institutional life. The *Gemara* uses that resonance to say that here, uniquely, the two were briefly able to share the moment of transition.

### Death & Burial Sites

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אִילְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאוֹמְרוֹ. הֵיכָן מֹשֶׁה מֵת — בְּחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל רְאוּבֵן, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַל מֹשֶׁה מֵעַרְבֹת מוֹאָב אֶל הַר נְבוֹ״, וּנְבוֹ בְּחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל רְאוּבֵן קָיְימָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן בָּנוּ וְגוֹ׳ וְאֶת נְבוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: If not for** an explicitly **written verse, one could not say** what is written with regard to the death and burial of Moses. **Where did Moses die? In the portion of Reuben, as it is written: "And Moses went up from the plains of Moab to Mount Nebo"** (Deuteronomy 34:1), **and** it is known from elsewhere that **Nebo is situated in the portion of Reuben, as it is written: "And the children of Reuben built** Heshbon, and Elealeh, and Kiriathaim, **and Nebo"** (Numbers 32:37–38).

*Amar Rabbi Yehuda: ilmalei mikra katuv iy efshar le-omro* — if it had not been written in the verse, one could not say such a thing. The verse *vaya'al Moshe me-arvot Moav el Har Nevo* places Moshe's death at Mount Nevo, which lies in the portion of Reuven, as Bnei Reuven are recorded as having built *et Nevo*. Yet Moshe was buried in Gad's portion, as the verse *vayar reshit lo ki sham chelkat mechokek safun* — "for there is the hidden portion of the lawgiver" — is understood to refer to Moshe's burial place in Gad's territory. Between the site of his death in Reuven and his burial in Gad, the distance was four *mil* — approximately four kilometers. A considerable distance to traverse, and the question is: who transported him?

### Divine Transport

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָן מֹשֶׁה קָבוּר — בְּחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל גָּד, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּרְא רֵאשִׁית לוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. וּמֵחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל רְאוּבֵן עַד חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל גָּד כַּמָּה הָוֵי — אַרְבָּעָה מִילִין. אוֹתָן אַרְבָּעָה מִילִין מִי הוֹלִיכוֹ? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיָה מֹשֶׁה מוּטָּל בְּכַנְפֵי שְׁכִינָה, וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אוֹמְרִים: ״צִדְקַת ה׳ עָשָׂה וּמִשְׁפָּטָיו עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״מִי יָקוּם לִי עִם מְרֵעִים מִי יִתְיַצֵּב לִי עִם פּוֹעֲלֵי אָוֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda continues: **And where is Moses buried? In the portion of Gad, as it is written** in the blessing of Moses to the tribe of Gad: **"And he chose a first part for himself,** for there a portion of a ruler was reserved" (Deuteronomy 33:21), indicating that Moses, the ruler, is buried in the portion of Gad. **And how much is** the distance **from the portion of Reuben to the portion of Gad? Four *mil*.** Rabbi Yehuda asks: For **those four *mil*** from Mount Nebo in the portion of Reuben to the burial place of Moses in the portion of Gad, **who transported him?** He answers: The contradiction between the two verses **teaches that Moses was lying in the wings of the Divine Presence,** as Moses was carried out by God Himself, **and the ministering angels were saying: "He executed the righteousness of the Lord, and His ordinances with Israel"** (Deuteronomy 33:21). **And the Holy One, Blessed be He, was saying: "Who will rise up for Me against the evildoers? Who will stand up for Me against the workers of iniquity?"** (Psalms 94:16). In other words, God asked: Who will now defend the Jewish people against its accusers?

*Melamed she-hayah Moshe mutal be-chanfei Shechinah* — Moshe lay cradled in the wings of the divine presence, which carried him the four *mil* to his burial place. This image closes the circle opened by our *Mishnah*: Moshe carried Yosef's *aron*, and God carried Moshe's body. The parallel is exact and intentional.

### Angels' Eulogy

The heavenly angels accompanied Moshe and declared: *tzidkat Hashem asah u-mishpatav im Yisrael* — he performed the righteousness of God and God's laws with Israel. This verse from the end of *Devarim* serves as the angelic eulogy at Moshe's burial.

### God's Lament

God's own expression of mourning: *mi yakum li im mere'im, mi yityatzeiv li im po'alei aven* — who will rise with me against the wicked? Who will stand firm with me against those who do iniquity? The death of Moshe is experienced, as it were, as a loss of an irreplaceable ally in the ongoing work of justice in the world.

### Sages' Responses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: ״מִי כְּהֶחָכָם וּמִי יוֹדֵעַ פֵּשֶׁר דָּבָר״, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״הַחׇכְמָה מֵאַיִן תִּמָּצֵא״, וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: ״וַיָּמׇת שָׁם מֹשֶׁה וְגוֹ׳״, סְמַלְיוֹן אָמַר: ״וַיָּמׇת שָׁם מֹשֶׁה סָפְרָא רַבָּה דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says** that God was saying the verse: **"Who is as the wise man and who knows the interpretation [*pesher*] of a matter?"** (Ecclesiastes 8:1), referring to the greatness of Moses, who was able to forge compromises, *pesharim*, between God and the Jewish people. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that God was saying the verse: **"Wisdom, where can it be found?"** (Job 28:12). **And Rav Naḥman says** that God was saying the verse: **"And Moses,** the servant of God, **died there"** (Deuteronomy 34:5). **Semalyon says** that God was saying: **And Moses, the great scribe of Israel, died there.**

The *Gemara* then records several Amoraic responses, each apparently citing a verse that captures what was lost. Shmuel cites *mi ke-he-chacham u-mi yodea pesher davar* — who is the wise one who knows the explanation of things? Rabbi Yochanan cites *ve-hachokhmah me-ayin timatzei* — and from where now shall wisdom be found? Rav Nachman simply states *vayamot sham Moshe* — and there Moshe died. A further voice, *Samayon*, declares: *vayamot sham Moshe safra rabba de-Yisrael* — the great scribe of Israel has died.

Whether these Amoraim are understood as voicing what the angels said, what God said, or as themselves participating virtually in the *levayah* of Moshe — the *vav* connecting them (*ve-Shmuel amar*, *ve-Rav Nachman amar*) gives the passage something of the quality of a gathering of mourners, each adding their eulogy in turn, until the last voice simply names the fact: the great scribe of Israel is gone.
