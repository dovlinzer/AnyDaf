# Taanit 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Rashi & Tosafot

### Rashi/Tosafot Issues

Before turning to the daf itself, a preliminary scholarly note is in order. The printed *Rashi* on Maseches Taanit is, by the assessment of current scholarship, not genuinely *Rashi*. The length and style of many comments, as well as the manner in which subjects are introduced, depart markedly from Rashi's characteristic method. Similarly, the *Tosafot* on this *masechet* contains none of the classic analytical exchanges one expects from *Ba'alei HaTosafot*; it reads more as a running commentary and, as Orbach has demonstrated, dates no earlier than the fourteenth century. These are helpful commentators, and they will be referenced where useful, but the reader should bear in mind that neither attribution is secure.

### Daf 16 Overview

We are picking up in the middle of 15b and continuing into daf 16. The *mishnah* has been working through the graduated series of public fast days declared in times of drought, and we now arrive at several additional topics treated within that same *mishnah*.

## Fasting & Mikdash

### Fasting Impacts Mikdash

One of the animating themes of this entire section is how profoundly the communal fast penetrates even into the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *Rambam* cites, apparently from a *Tosefta*, the practice of placing ashes on the *Sifrei Torah* — as if the distress reaches into God's own domain and affects the sacred vessels themselves. Running parallel to this is the question of whether the *kohanim* serving in the *Mikdash* participate in the communal fast.

### Three Phases of Fasts

The *mishnah* establishes a crucial distinction among the three sets of fast days. The first three fasts are declared because rain has not fallen — *avru eilu velo yardu geshamim*. These are a response to an objective absence, not yet to a sense of divine withdrawal. It is only with the middle three and the final seven, framed by the language *avru eilu velo na'anu* — they prayed and were not answered — that a qualitative shift occurs. The phrase *velo na'anu* conveys not merely the lack of rain but a felt distance between the people and God. It is precisely from this middle stage onward that the fast begins to penetrate into the *Beit HaMikdash* itself, with even the *mishmar* participating. That progression is theologically coherent: the *kohanim* do not fast during the first three because those fasts reflect a practical deficit; they fast during the later stages because those fasts reflect a relational rupture.

## Mishmar & Ma'amad

### Wine & Service

The *mishnah* now turns to the *mishmar* and the *beit av*, which at first appears to be a digression. The weekly priestly group — the *mishmar* — is permitted to drink wine at night but not during the day, since they may be called upon to perform the *avodah* at any time during daylight hours, and performing the *avodah* while intoxicated carries the penalty of *chayav mitah bidei shamayim*. The daily group — the *beit av* — may drink neither during the day nor on the preceding night, since one cannot report to the *avodah* impaired in any degree.

### Grooming Restrictions

The *mishnah* continues: *anshei mishmar ve'anshei ma'amad asurim misaper umelakhabes* — both the *mishmar* and the *ma'amad* are prohibited from cutting their hair or laundering their clothes during the week of their service. The *ma'amad* requires a brief explanation: since the *korbanot tzibur* are brought on behalf of the entire community yet the community is not physically present, a rotating delegation of *kohanim*, *Levi'im*, and *Yisraelim* would stand by the *korbanot* in Yerushalayim, with parallel groups assembled in the cities throughout the land. These restrictions on grooming resemble in spirit the prohibitions of *Chol HaMoed* — just as one enters *Yom Tov* having already attended to such matters, so too one enters the week of service already having done so, so that the week itself is undistracted and dignified. On Thursday, however, an exception is permitted: *mutarim mipnei kvod haShabbat*, the honor of Shabbat justifies attending to these matters, with Thursday chosen practically because Friday is consumed with Shabbat preparations.

### Connection to Community

What appears to be a digression in fact carries a pointed structural logic, and here the work of Avraham Walfish on the *mishnayot* of Taanit has been illuminating. The *mishnah* has just established that the *kohanim* share in the communal fast — the *Mikdash* is connected to the people's distress. The introduction of the *ma'amad* makes the inverse point: on any normal day, the people are connected to the *Mikdash* through their standing representatives. There is, in ordinary times, an organic bond flowing in both directions between the community and the Temple service. The fast days in question are days when that bond is felt to be strained, when the sense of divine distance is palpable. Presenting the *ma'amad* here is not a tangent; it is the other face of the same coin.

## Megillat Ta'anit

### Eulogies Forbidden

Having concluded the laws governing the fast days themselves, the *mishnah* turns to days on which fasting may not be imposed. The governing framework is *Megillat Ta'anit*, the ancient scroll that catalogues days of historical celebration on which mourning practices are prohibited. For days listed in *Megillat Ta'anit* on which even eulogies are forbidden — the higher category of commemoration — the prohibition extends to the preceding day as well: *l'fanav assur l'acharav muttar*. The day before carries the character of an *erev Yom Tov*, a minor anticipatory sanctity. The day after, however, is permitted — once the occasion has passed, there is no residual force.

### Before/After Yom Tov

*Rabbi Yosei* disagrees: *l'fanav u'l'acharav assur* — even the following day is forbidden. This position is strikingly parallel to *Rabbi Yosei*'s ruling in the opening *mishnah* of Avodah Zarah, where he holds that the restrictions on commercial dealings with non-Jews before their festivals apply equally to the three days following — which, in the case of Christians, would render such dealings perpetually forbidden. The structural logic appears to be the same *Rabbi Yosei* in both places: the festival's influence radiates in both temporal directions. Where the *mishnah* describes the lesser category — days on which fasting alone is prohibited, without the additional restriction on eulogies — *Rabbi Yosei* holds *l'fanav assur l'acharav muttar*: the preceding day is still affected, but the following day is not.

### Lesser Holidays

An important background question lurks here. The *gemara* elsewhere raises the issue of whether *Megillat Ta'anit* was effectively nullified — *batla Megillat Ta'anit* — after the *churban*. The *mishnah*, which features *Rabbi Yosei* who lived after the *churban*, clearly proceeds on the assumption that *lo batla* — it remains in force. That debate, however, continues in the *gemara* and is worth keeping in mind throughout.

## Fast Day Rules

### Thursday Rule

Another general restriction on the declaration of fast days: *ein gozrin ta'aniyot al hatzibur betchila bechamishi* — one does not initiate a fast series on a Thursday. The reason is *shelo leafkia shearim* — so as not to cause market prices to spike. When the entire community fasts on Thursday, people rush to purchase food for breaking the fast Thursday night and then again for Shabbat. Those unfamiliar with the fast might interpret the unusual market activity as a sign of shortage, causing prices to rise artificially. The practical calculus is sound: wait until Monday, so that by Thursday people already understand the context.

### Fast Sequences

The prescribed sequence for the first three fasts is Monday, Thursday, Monday — *sheni vechamishi vesheni*. By the time the second Thursday arrives, the situation is publicly known. The second set of three runs Thursday, Monday, Thursday — *chamishi sheni vechamishi* — since at that point the community is fully aware. Notably, this sequence means there is no break at all between the two sets of three: completing Monday-Thursday-Monday and immediately resuming Thursday-Monday-Thursday produces a nearly continuous fast cycle.

### Yosei: Intermittency

*Rabbi Yosei* holds that just as the first series does not begin on a Thursday, neither do the second or third series: *keshem she-ein harishonot bechamishi kach lo shniyot velo achronot*. This could simply reflect a preference for maintaining a consistent pattern — Monday, Thursday, Monday throughout — so the rhythm is easier to remember. But there may be a deeper point. The intermittent character of the fast series, with built-in intervals, preserves the psychological and spiritual power of each new stage. When something strikes intermittently rather than continuously, the renewed impact is sharper. If the community moves without pause from one series to the next, the sense that *this* is a new and more serious escalation is blunted. A brief respite also, as it were, gives time for God to respond before the community presses further.

### Exempt Days

Specific days exempt from *ta'anit tzibur* regardless of circumstance: *Rosh Chodesh*, *Chanukah*, and *Purim* — *beroshei chodashim beChanukah uvePurim*. The *mishnah* notes that this remains in force even according to those who hold *batla Megillat Ta'anit*, since these three days — the non-*Yom Tov* occasions of rabbinic significance — retain their special status by independent authority.

### Gamliel vs. Meir

A further complication: what happens if a fast series is already underway when one of these exempt days arrives? *Rabban Gamliel* rules *ein mafsikin* — the series is not interrupted; one continues fasting. *Rabbi Meir* qualifies this: even *Rabban Gamliel* would concede that one does not fast the entire day on *Chanukah* or *Purim* — *modeh hayah she-ein mashlimin* — so that while the fast is technically not broken, it is not completed either, since *Chanukah* and *Purim* must win out over a full fast. A parallel case is noted: if *Tisha B'Av* falls on a Friday, one does not fast through to nightfall, so as not to enter *Shabbat* in a state of hunger and deprivation.

## Order of Fasting

### Special Practices: When?

Having stated the framework of the *seder ta'aniyot* — the *mishnah*'s description of how one brings out the ark, places ashes, and undertakes the special liturgy — the *gemara* raises a pointed question: does all of this apply from the very first set of three fasts, or only later in the progression?

### Ark/Ashes: Last Seven

A *baraita* provides the answer with precision. During the first two sets of three fasts — six fasts in all — *nichnasin l'veit haknesset umitpalelim kederech she-mitpalelim kol hashana kulah*: one enters the synagogue and prays as one does throughout the year. It is only for the last seven, *uvesheva acharonot*, that the ark is taken out to the public square, that ashes are placed upon the ark and upon the heads of the *nasi* and the *av beit din*, and that each individual places ashes upon his own head. *Rabbi Natan* adds that the ashes used are specifically *eifer mikleihem* — true ashes, from burning — not merely dust or dirt. *Rav Pappa* confirms: *ki tenan nami matnitin sheva acharonot tenan* — our *mishnah* too is speaking only of the last seven.

One might ask whether the *mishnah*'s silence on this limitation means it intended the practices to apply from the beginning, with the *baraita* then standing in tension with the *mishnah* rather than contextualizing it. This is genuinely not resolved. The *gemara* often uses a *baraita* or *Tosefta* to supply a context for the *mishnah*, but it is equally possible that *Rebbi* deliberately left the matter unrestricted, understanding the special practices as appropriate from the outset. A parallel instance arises shortly regarding the *shofar* blowing, where a *baraita* will recontextualize what the *mishnah* apparently says about practice outside Yerushalayim — and there too the question remains whether to read the *baraita* as reshaping or contradicting the *mishnah*.

### Escalation Logic

The theological rationale for reserving these dramatic practices for the last seven is plain: if the community begins at maximum intensity, there is nowhere left to go, and the practices lose their force. The accumulated weight of fasts that have gone unanswered, the felt depth of divine distance — these give the extraordinary practices their meaning. Arriving at the public square, the ark in tow, ashes on leaders and laypeople alike, only after six previous fasts have failed to bring rain — that is when the gesture carries its full gravity.

## Ashes on Leaders

### Honor vs. Curse

The *baraita* specifies that ashes are placed first upon the head of the *nasi* and the *av beit din*, and then each individual places ashes upon himself. The *gemara* pauses on this ordering, invoking a principle: *bigdulah matchilim min hagadol, uviklalah matchilim min hakatan* — in matters of honor, one begins with the greater; in matters of curse, one begins with the lesser.

Two prooftexts anchor this rule. For *gedulah*: the verse in which Moshe addresses Aharon, then Elazar, then Itamar — in descending order of rank — even in the painful aftermath of the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, when he instructs them not to engage in mourning practices, their priestly status is implicitly honored through the order of address. For *klalah*: after the sin in Gan Eden, the serpent was cursed first, then Chava, then Adam — proceeding from the one most responsible upward, both to apportion blame accurately and to spare the dignity of the more honored parties as long as possible.

### Leaders' Role

Given this principle, one might expect the *gemara* to question why the *nasi* and *av beit din* are singled out *first* for the ashes, rather than last — since the ashes ostensibly carry the character of *klalah*, a degrading practice. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *hai chashivuta lidiidhu* — placing ashes on the leaders first is actually an honor to them. The message conveyed is, *atun chashivuta l'mibaya alan rachamei akulei alma* — you are people of sufficient stature that your prayer on behalf of the entire world carries special weight. Beginning with them signals that they are the ones most capable of effecting intercession.

One might equally have read the sequence as an implicit statement of responsibility — you are the leaders, and if the people have sinned, the ultimate accountability begins with you. The *gemara* does not go in that direction; it reads the priority as honor, not reproach. Yet the tension is worth noting.

### Self vs. External Shame

A second distinction then emerges. Although the *nasi* and *av beit din* receive ashes first as a sign of their status, the *baraita* specifies that someone else places the ashes on their heads — whereas every other individual places the ashes on his own head. Why the difference? *Amar Rabbi Nachman bar Abba de-min Kisari: eino domeh mitbayesh mi-atzmo le-mitbayesh me-acherim* — the shame of being embarrassed by another person is qualitatively greater than the shame of embarrassing oneself. Having someone else perform the act upon you is more humbling, not less.

This is a striking reversal of what one might have assumed. One could have argued that having an attendant place the ashes on the leader's head is a mark of elevated status — the leader does not dress himself, someone does it for him. The *gemara* inverts the logic entirely: precisely because it is more embarrassing to be subjected to this act by another, the leaders undergo it that way, ensuring that they feel the full weight of the moment.

### Ashes at Tefillin Place

Where precisely on the head are the ashes placed? *Amar Yitzchak: bimkom tefillin* — in the place of the *tefillin*. The proof is from the verse in Yeshayahu: *lasum le'avelei Tziyon latet lahem pe'er tachat efer* — to give the mourners of Tziyon glory in place of ashes. Since *tefillin* are called *pe'er* — glory — in a verse in Yechezkel, the counterpart to *pe'er* in mourning is *efer* — ashes — placed precisely where the *tefillin* would rest. The crown of the head, where God's name rests upon you through the *tefillin*, is now marked with ash. The visual and theological resonance with the ashes placed atop the *aron* is unmistakable: both are objects that bear the imprint of the divine presence, and both are now covered in ash.

## Ark Out, Sackcloth On

### Public Square: No Respon…

Having established the *what* of the special practices, the *gemara* now turns to *why*. Why does the community leave the synagogue and move to the public square? *Amar Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba*: to declare — *za'aknu betzina velo ne'aninu* — we cried out in private and were not answered. Now, *nivzeh atzmenu befarhesya* — let us degrade ourselves publicly. Perhaps public self-humiliation will succeed where private prayer has not. The implicit theory is one of divine compassion: if God sees how utterly miserable and humbled the community has made itself in the open square, perhaps that sight will move God to respond.

### Resh Lakish: Exile

*Reish Lakish* offers an alternative: *galinu* — we have exiled ourselves from the *beit knesset*. *Galutenu mechaperet aleinu* — our exile atones for us. The resonance with *galut* as a theological reality is unmistakable: exile is not merely a political punishment, it is a condition of distance from the place where one belongs, distance from God. By voluntarily departing from the *beit knesset*, the community enacts, on a small scale, the existential structure of exile — and lets that self-imposed displacement function as *kapparah*.

The *gemara* probes the practical difference between these two explanations: *ika beinaihu de-gali mi-be knishta le-be knishta* — would it be sufficient to move from one synagogue to another? Under Reish Lakish's view, the act of leaving one's own *shul* is what matters, since the exile is the point; under Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba's view, the public exposure — the *befarhesya* — is what matters, and going to a different synagogue accomplishes nothing particularly public. There is also a substantive theological difference between the two positions: the first is an appeal to divine compassion — *look how we have humbled ourselves, answer us*; the second is a mechanism of atonement — *the self-imposed punishment atones for the sin*. The tension between these two frames — prayer and empathy versus sin and *kapparah* — runs throughout these *sugyot*.

### Ark Degraded

Why bring the ark out to the public square alongside the community? *Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: kli tzanua hayah lanu ve-nitbazeh ba-avonoteinu* — we had a hidden, private vessel, and it has been degraded through our sins. The ark, kept in the innermost sanctum, in the *Kadshei Kadashim*, is a vessel of intimacy and concealment. Our sins have worked their way inward until even the ark has been displaced, brought out of its proper place.

This is not merely a rhetorical flourish. It reflects the theology of the *Mikdash* as treated in tractate Yoma: sin accumulates within the *Mikdash* as *tumah*, and if it is not cleansed — as it was annually through the *Yom Kippur* service — eventually the *Shechinah* itself is forced to withdraw. The ten stages of the *Shechinah*'s departure described in the sources are precisely this process drawn out to its conclusion. The bringing of the ark to the public square during a communal fast dramatizes that same dynamic in miniature: *beavonoteinu* — our sins have pushed even the sacred vessel out of its place.

### Sackcloth Symbolism

Why does the community wear sackcloth? *Amar Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba: lomar harei anu hashuvim ke-vehemah* — to declare, we consider ourselves as animals. Sackcloth was made from coarse goat hair; wearing it instead of human clothing dissolves something of the dignity that distinguishes a person from a beast. Clothing is one of the markers of human status, and replacing it with the raw material of animals reduces, at least symbolically, one's self-perception to the animal level. This gesture of radical self-degradation speaks in the same register as the ashes and the public square: the community presents itself before God as utterly stripped of pretension, of dignity, of the standing it would normally claim.

### Divine Empathy

Why are ashes placed specifically upon the ark? Two answers are offered. *Amar Rabbi Yehuda ben Pazi: ke-lomar imo anochi be-tzarah* — it is as if to say, I, God, am with you in distress. God, as it were, shares in the practice of mourning. *Reish Lakish* offers the formulation: *be-chol tzaratam lo tzar* — in all their afflictions, to God it is also an affliction.

There is a subtle difference between these readings. *Imo anochi be-tzarah* — I am with you in your distress — suggests divine presence and solidarity; even amid estrangement, God is here to support. This could be read as: you may fear we are distant, yet I am still with you. *Be-chol tzaratam lo tzar* conveys more that God feels the people's pain, is grieved by it — but does not necessarily signal closeness. The one emphasizes accompaniment, the other empathy at a distance. Both ground the act of placing ashes on the ark in a theology of divine pathos — God is not indifferent to Israel's suffering — but they locate God somewhat differently in relation to the community's distress.

### Zeira's Reaction

*Amar Rabbi Zeira*: from the time I was young, when I would see the scholars place ashes upon the ark, *mizdazei li kulei gufai* — my entire body would tremble. There is something about encountering this practice not as a legal abstraction but as a visual reality that overwhelms whatever distance discussion might create. The sight of ashes resting on the ark — that symbol at once of the covenant and of divine presence — strikes with a visceral power that no amount of intellectual engagement can fully anticipate.

## Individual Ashes

### Dust vs. Akedah

The *gemara* returns to the question of why individuals place ashes on their own heads. Here *Rabbi Levi bar Chama* and *Rabbi Chanina* dispute. One holds: *harei anu hashuvim lefanecha ke-efer* — we are as ashes and dust before You, a declaration of radical self-nullification, of how degraded the community has become in its own eyes and before God. The other holds: *kedei sheyizkor lanu afro shel Yitzchak* — so that God will remember the ashes of Yitzchak, invoking the *Akeidat Yitzchak* as a source of *zechus*, merit, that might move God to respond.

### Akedah Complexity

The reference to Yitzchak's ashes raises a complex question, since in the plain sense of the narrative, Yitzchak was not burned. Yet there is a significant strand within *Chazal* — developed more fully in various *midrashim* — that holds either that Avraham did in fact offer Yitzchak, followed by a miraculous resurrection through the ashes, or at minimum that since Avraham was wholly prepared to do so, it is considered *ke'ilu* the ashes were before God. There is also a more literal reading: even without the *shechitah*, once Yitzchak was lying on the *mizbe'ach* and the fire was lit, the ashes that would have resulted from the offering are real in some theological sense. The *gemara* does not adjudicate the full range of these traditions here, but the invocation of *afro shel Yitzchak* presupposes at least the *ke'ilu* principle — that the readiness to sacrifice constitutes a kind of actuality before God, and that invoking it in prayer draws on accumulated merit.

### Dirt vs. Ashes

The practical difference between the two opinions: *ika beinaihu afar stam* — can one use ordinary dirt or dust, or must one use true ashes? If the purpose of the ashes is self-degradation — we are like dust — then ordinary dirt serves equally well. If the purpose is to invoke the *Akeidat Yitzchak*, then only *eifer* proper, ashes from burning, carries the necessary resonance. This distinction also maps onto a broader thematic difference that has been running through these *sugyot*: is the fast primarily an expression of misery and appeal to divine compassion, or is it primarily a mechanism of *teshuvah*, *kapparah*, and the invocation of ancestral merit? The two positions on ashes embody precisely that tension.

## Cemetery & Dead

### Like the Dead

The *baraita* also references a practice of going to the cemetery during the fast. Again *Rabbi Levi bar Chama* and *Rabbi Chanina* dispute. One position: *harei anu hashuvim lefanecha ke-meitim* — we consider ourselves before You as the dead. This follows the same logic as the ashes — a declaration of utter degradation, of having lost one's standing as the living. The other: *kedei sheyivakshu aleinu meitim rachamim* — so that the dead will pray on our behalf. Since the prayers of the living community have gone unanswered, one seeks additional intercessors; the righteous dead, whose relationship to God transcends the limitations of earthly life, are asked to plead on the community's behalf. The *Rambam*, characteristically, was uncomfortable with this approach, which he viewed as incompatible with a rationalist theology.

### Jewish Cemetery Only

The practical difference between the two opinions: *ika beinaihu kivrei akum* — if no Jewish cemetery is nearby, may one go to a non-Jewish cemetery? If the point is purely symbolic — to feel as the dead — then any cemetery would serve. But if the point is to have the dead intercede, then a non-Jewish cemetery is insufficient, since non-Jews lack the power of intercession, or as *Rashi* (or the pseudo-*Rashi*) notes, God would not listen to their prayers on Israel's behalf any more readily than to Israel's own prayers. The Jewish cemetery is therefore required specifically under the intercessory rationale.

## Sermon & Teshuvah

### Who Preaches

The *mishnah* describes the delivery of a *drasha* — a sermon — addressed to the assembled community during the fast. A *baraita* elaborates on who is to deliver it: *im yesh zakein omer zakein* — if there is an elder, the elder speaks. If not, a *chacham* speaks. If not, a person of recognized status — *adam shetzurah* — delivers the address. The *gemara* presses the obvious question: surely one would not appoint an elder who lacks wisdom simply on account of his age? *Abaye* clarifies: the intended sequence is that the ideal is someone who combines both — an elder who is also a sage. Absent such a person, a sage who is younger will serve. And absent that, a person of general communal standing.

### Not Ritual, Action

The substance of the address: *acheinu, lo sak ve-ta'anis gormim ela teshuvah u'ma'asim tovim gormim* — our brethren, it is not the sackcloth and the fasting that cause the desired effect; it is *teshuvah* and good deeds. This is the *drasha*'s central thesis, and it is striking for what it says about the relationship between the practices and their purpose. Everything that has been described — the ark, the ashes, the sackcloth, the public humiliation — is not what actually produces the result. These practices are means of awakening the community to what is truly required, but they are not the operative cause of divine response.

### Nineveh Evidence

The proof is drawn from the people of Ninveh. The verse does not say *vayar Elokim es sakam ve-es ta'anisam* — God saw their sackcloth and their fast. It says *vayar Elokim es ma'aseihem ki shavu midarko hara'ah* — God saw their deeds, that they had turned from their evil path. What God perceived and responded to was not the ritual garb but the behavioral transformation.

### Nineveh Model

The *gemara* proceeds to expound the extraordinary character of the *teshuvah* in Ninveh. The verse states: *vayiskasu sakim ha'adam vehabehemah* — both the people and the animals were covered in sackcloth. The *gemara*, perhaps sensing the comic dimension of animals in sackcloth, redirects the interpretation entirely: what the people of Ninveh actually did was *asru habehemos lachud ve-es havelados lachud* — they separated the mother animals from their young. This had nothing to do with sackcloth; the verse's reference to the *behemah* is read through an entirely different lens.

### Mothers & Calves

The community then declared before God: *Ribono shel Olam, im ein attah merachem aleinu ein anu merachamim al elu* — Master of the universe, if You will not have compassion upon us, we will not have compassion upon these. The implicit appeal is to divine *rachamim*: God, who is characterized throughout scripture as One whose *rachamim* extend over all creatures — *rachamav al kol ma'asav* — and who has prohibited, for example, slaughtering an animal and its offspring on the same day (*oto ve-et bno lo tishchatu beyom echad*), surely cannot witness the distress of mothers separated from their young without being moved. And if God's compassion is stirred for these animals, how much more for His own people, who stand before Him as children before a parent. The imagery, notably, is maternal: it is the nursing mothers separated from their calves, and it is that image — the denial of nurture and closeness — that is pressed upon God.

The *gemara* then notes: *vayasuvu ish midarko hara'ah u-min ha-chamas asher be-kapeihem* — and each person turned from his evil way and from the *chamas* that was in their hands. *Shmuel* explains: *afilu gazal marish* — even one who had stolen a beam and incorporated it into the structure of his castle — *mekake'a kol ha-birah kulah u-machazir marish li-ve'alav* — would tear down the entire building in order to return the beam to its owner. The rabbis had in fact enacted a leniency for such cases in the interest of facilitating *teshuvah* — a thief who builds a stolen beam into his home need only pay its value rather than demolish the structure. The people of Ninveh required no such accommodation; their *teshuvah* was absolute, going beyond any legal obligation. The parallel that suggests itself is *Dama ben Netinah* and the stories of *kibbud av va-em* that exceed the letter of the law — in both cases, a non-Jewish example is adduced to model an extremity of virtue beyond what *halachah* technically demands, and in both cases the implicit challenge to the Jewish community is pointed.

## Confession Empty

### Dead Rodent Parable

*Rabbah bar Abba* distills the principle into a vivid image: someone who confesses a sin *ve-eino chozer bah* — without genuinely returning from it — is comparable to a person holding a *sheretz* — a dead crawling creature that causes *tumah* — in his hand while immersing himself. *She'afilu tovel bechol meimei sheba'olam, lo alta lo tevila* — even if one immerses in every body of water in the world, the immersion accomplishes nothing, because the source of *tumah* is still in hand.

### Cast Away Sin

The resolution: *zarko miyado, keivan shetaval bearba'im se'ah, miyad alta lo tevila* — the moment one casts it away, a single valid immersion in a *mikvah* of forty *se'ah* is immediately effective. The ritual works, but only once the underlying condition is addressed. Confession is the ritual of *teshuvah*, and like *tevilah*, it functions only when the sin itself has been relinquished. The verse confirms: *umodeh ve'ozev yerucham* — the one who confesses *and leaves* the sin behind is the one whom God will show compassion. A further verse — *nisa levaveinu el kapayim el el bashamayim* — we will lift up our hearts with clean *hands* to God in heaven — underscores that the *kapayim*, the hands, must be free of what they have wrongfully held. *Rashi* (or pseudo-*Rashi*) identifies *aveira beyado* specifically as *gezel* — theft — so that the paradigmatic case of holding onto sin is the case of the stolen property that has not been returned.

### Modern Application

This principle has immediate and serious application to cases of grave interpersonal harm — including, most urgently, cases of abuse. When someone who has committed such wrongs comes forward claiming *teshuvah*, expressing remorse, and requesting reinstatement into community life, the operative question is not whether there is regret or whether the verbal confession has been made. The question is whether the hands are clean — whether concrete, real-world efforts have been made to approach every victim, to right the wrongs, to make genuine amends. Without that, the confession is the man holding the *sheretz* and asking why the *mikvah* is not working.

### Interpersonal Wrongs

The broader principle is one of *bein adam lachaveiro*: wrongs between people cannot be resolved purely on a metaphysical or theological plane. It is not sufficient to feel *teshuvah* in one's heart, to confess before God, or to perform ritual acts of contrition. Where real harm has been done to another person, the corrective must be equally real and equally interpersonal. The fasting, the sackcloth, the ashes — all of this can only work if the *kapayim* are genuinely clean.

## Shliach Tzibur

### Prayer Leader Rules

When the community stands for prayer — *amdu bitfilla* — the *gemara* establishes that the *shliach tzibur* is not chosen on the basis of age or wisdom alone. *Af al pi sheyesh sham zakein ve-chacham, ein moridin lifnei hateiva ella adam harugul* — even if there is someone older and wiser present, the one who leads the prayer must be someone *ragil*, someone practiced and fitting for the role. The *gemara* then proceeds to define what *ragil* actually means, and the answer goes far beyond liturgical competence.

### Full Qualifications

A *baraita* sets out the qualifications in detail. He must be *metupal ve-ein lo* — someone with many dependents and insufficient means, so that he viscerally understands what it is to carry the weight of responsibility for others without adequate resources. He must have worked hard in the field — *yegia basadeh* — yet his household remains materially poor. He must have a good reputation — *pirko na'eh*. He must be humble — *ushfal berech*. He must be well-regarded by the community — *merutze la'am*, so that people feel genuinely connected to him. He must have a pleasant voice — *veyesh lo ne'ima, vekolo arev*. He must be literate in *Torah*, *Nevi'im*, and *Ketuvim*, capable of reading and interpreting them. He must be versed in *midrash*, *halachah*, and *aggadah*. And he must know all the *berachot* — *uvaki bechol haberachot kulan* — so that he can competently discharge the liturgical obligations of the role. This is, in short, a description of someone at a very high *madrega* — not merely a capable cantor, but a person of integrated spiritual, moral, and communal stature.

### Yitzchak bar Ami

When the *gemara* finished reciting this list, *yahivu beh rabanan einaihu b'Rabbi Yitzchak bar Ami* — all the rabbis turned and looked at *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Ami*. The glance conveyed recognition: here is the person this description is talking about.

### Clarifications

Two terms in the *baraita* require clarification. *Beito reikam* — his household is empty — is interpreted by *Rav Chisda* not as material poverty (which has already been captured by *ein lo*) but as *zehu shebeito reikam min ha'aveira* — his home is free of transgression. The household itself carries a clean record. And *pirko na'eh* — his chapter is good — is explained by *Abaye* as *zeh shelo yatza alav shem ra b'yalduto* — he never had a bad reputation even in his youth. His entire life, from the beginning, has been untarnished.

### Lion Parable

A final verse frames the consequences of appointing an unworthy *shliach tzibur*: *haitah li nachalati k'aryeh baya'ar, natna alai b'kola al kein sneitiha* — my inheritance has been to me like a lion in the forest; it has raised its voice against me, and therefore I have despised it. Applied here: *zeh shliach tzibur she'eino hagun hayored lifnei hateivah* — this is a *shliach tzibur* who is not fitting yet descends before the ark. The voice that God will hear from such a person is one God will despise. The point is not merely aesthetic — that an unqualified cantor sounds bad — but theological: the *shliach tzibur* is the community's representative before God. He must authentically embody the community's need, carry its suffering, and speak from a place of genuine identification with the people he serves. A beautiful voice without that inner substance is noise; only someone who truly represents the *tzibur* — who has felt what they feel, who carries what they carry — can bring their prayer before God in a way that will be received.
