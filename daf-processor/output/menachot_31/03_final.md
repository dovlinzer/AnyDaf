# Menachot 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Shimon Shezuri

### Ulla's Independence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִין בַּר חִינָּנָא, אָמַר עוּלָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravin bar Ḥinnana says** that **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri; and moreover,** not only is the *halakha* in accordance with his opinion with regard to this matter, but in **any place where Rabbi Shimon Shezuri taught** a *halakha*, the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with his** opinion.

The daf begins at the bottom of 30b with a recapitulation of why the ruling of Ulla in the name of Rabbi Chanina cannot be anchored in the case of the grain mixture discussed previously. The principle at stake is straightforward: Ulla's statement that "the *halachah* is like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri — and not only here, but everywhere Rabbi Shimon Shezuri rules, the *halachah* follows him" must be grounded in some specific case of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri about which no other *amora* had already weighed in. The reason is simple: if an *amora* had already ruled on that very case, then Ulla, by voicing his own ruling, would have been entering into dialogue with that *amora*, and the statement would have been formulated differently.

This is precisely the problem with the grain mixture case. The Gemara notes: *ha itmar alah amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yochanan, halachah k'Rabbi Shimon Shezuri* — Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the *halachah* follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri. Since Rabbi Yochanan had already ruled on that case, if Ulla's anchor had been this case, he would have framed his statement in relation to Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, not independently. The fact that Ulla speaks without reference to any other *amora* means we must still identify a case of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri on which no *amora* had commented. Two candidates are proposed: Rav Papa says the anchor is the case of the *shida* (a large box), while Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak says it is the case of wine.

### Shida Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשִּׁידָּה, דִּתְנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים נִמְדֶּדֶת מִבִּפְנִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים מִבַּחוּץ, וּמוֹדִים אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין עוֹבִי הָרַגְלַיִם וְעוֹבִי הַלְּבִזְבְּזִין נִמְדָּד.
>
> **Translation:** that Rabbi Ḥanina's statement was **with regard to a chest, as we learned** in a mishna (*Kelim* 18:1): A wooden chest that is large enough to contain forty *se'a* is not susceptible to contracting ritual impurity, since it is no longer considered a vessel. In determining its capacity, **Beit Shammai say** that **it is measured on the inside, and Beit Hillel say** that **it is measured on the outside** so that the volume of the walls of the chest itself is included in the measurement. **And** both Beit Shammai **and** Beit Hillel **concede that the volume of the legs and the volume of the rims [*halevazbazin*]** are **not measured.**

The *Mishnah* in Keilim teaches that a very large vessel — one capable of holding two *kor* of dry measure — is classified more like furniture than like a vessel and therefore does not become *tamei*. The *shida*, a large storage chest, is the paradigm case. The question is: how do you measure whether this vessel meets the two-*kor* threshold?

Beis Shammai rules *nimdedes mibifnim* — you measure the interior volume. This is eminently logical: the whole point is whether the vessel is large enough to contain two *kor*, so you measure what it actually contains. Beis Hillel rules *mibachutz* — you measure from the outside. The thickness of the walls is treated as if it too constitutes contained volume. This is a *kula*, a leniency: the more volume we can attribute to the vessel, the more readily we can say it has crossed the threshold and therefore does not become *tamei*.

Both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree, however, that the thickness of the legs and the thickness of the decorative metalwork or grating around the top are not measured toward the volume — these structural elements do not define any enclosed space.

### Legs & Space

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מוֹדִים שֶׁעוֹבִי הָרַגְלַיִם וְעוֹבִי הַלְּבִזְבְּזִין נִמְדָּד, וּבֵינֵיהֶן אֵין נִמְדָּד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיוּ רַגְלַיִם גְּבוֹהוֹת טֶפַח – אֵין בֵּינֵיהֶן נִמְדָּד, וְאִם לָאו – בֵּינֵיהֶן נִמְדָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: They concede that the volume of the legs and the volume of the rims** are **measured, but** the space enclosed **between** the rims and the legs **is not measured. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says: If** the **legs were** one **handbreadth high** then the space **between** the legs **is not measured,** since the area has an independent significance, **but if** the space is **not** one handbreadth high, the space **between** the legs **is measured** as part of the chest. It is with regard to this statement that Rabbi Ḥanina said the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri.

Rabbi Yosi introduces a surprising position. He says both Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree that the thickness of the legs *and* the metalwork *is* measured, but the space *between* the legs is not. The Shita Mekubetzet notes a different textual reading of this *mishnah*. That the space between the legs is not measured makes obvious sense — they enclose nothing. But that the thickness of the legs themselves should be counted toward the volume, while the walls are not, is quite strange. This is nevertheless Rabbi Yosi's position.

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri goes further still with an even more lenient position: *im hayu raglayim gevohot tefach, ein beinehem nimdad; ve-im lav, beinehem nimdad* — if the legs raise the box a full *tefach* or more off the ground, the space between the legs is not counted; but if the legs are less than a *tefach* high, the space between them *is* counted toward the volume. The reasoning is that short legs, being insignificant in their own right, are treated as mere extensions of the walls of the box — the walls are understood to continue downward through them, and the enclosed space between them is therefore attributed to the vessel itself. This is a substantial leniency, and it presumably operates according to Beis Hillel's approach.

Here, then, is a ruling of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri on which no *amora* on record voiced an opinion — making it a plausible candidate for the anchor case of Ulla's general statement.

### Solidified Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: אַיַּיִן, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: שֶׁמֶן תְּחִלָּה לְעוֹלָם, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַף הַדְּבָשׁ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַיָּיִן. מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר יַיִן לָא? אֵימָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר: יָיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that Rabbi Ḥanina's statement was **with regard to wine, as we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 3:2): **Rabbi Meir says: Oil,** an example of a liquid, that contracted impurity, is **always** considered to have **first**-degree ritual impurity, even if it came into contact with an item that was impure with second-degree ritual impurity, which, according to the standard *halakhot* of ritual impurity, should result in it having third-degree ritual impurity. **And the Rabbis say** that this is the *halakha* **even** with regard to **honey. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says:** This is the *halakha* **even** with regard to **wine.** The Gemara asks: **By inference,** is that to say **that the first *tanna* holds** that **wine** is **not** considered a liquid? Rather, **say** as follows: **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says: Wine** is considered a liquid, but oil and honey are not.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes the case of wine instead. The *Mishnah* in Taharot deals with a principle from Rabbinic law: whenever *tumah* touches a liquid — regardless of whether the *tumah* is an *av*, a *rishon*, or a *sheni* — that liquid always becomes a *rishon le-tumah*. Normally, *tumah* is transmitted in degrees: an *av* makes something a *rishon*, a *rishon* makes something a *sheni*, and so on. But liquids are always elevated immediately to *rishon* status. The question taken up in the *Mishnah* is what happens when the classic liquids — oil, honey, and wine — solidify. Are they still considered liquids for the purposes of these *tumah* laws?

Tosafot clarifies that nobody disputes that oil, honey, and wine constitute liquids in their natural state; the dispute concerns only their solidified forms. Rabbi Meir rules that oil (*shemen*), even when solidified, retains its status as a liquid and therefore still becomes *rishon le-tumah* regardless of what degree of *tumah* touches it. The Sages add that honey (*dvash*) in solidified form likewise retains liquid status, which is somewhat surprising since people eat solid honey and might expect it to be classified as food rather than liquid. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri adds: *af hayayin* — even wine, when solidified, is still considered a liquid.

This raises a difficulty: if the *tanna kamma* (the earlier opinion) holds that solidified oil and honey are still liquids, how could solidified wine be any less so? Wine is the paradigmatic liquid; surely it would be classified as a liquid even more readily than oil or honey. The Gemara therefore reinterprets Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's statement. Rather than *adding* wine to the list, he is *restricting* it: oil and honey, when solidified, lose their liquid status and become solids for purposes of *tumah*. Only wine, as the quintessential liquid, retains its liquid status even after solidifying. This is thus a ruling of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri that no *amora* had addressed — the second plausible anchor for Ulla's statement.

### Mixed Tevel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי: פַּעַם אַחַת נִתְעָרֵב לִי טֶבֶל בְּחוּלִּין, וּבָאתִי וְשָׁאַלְתִּי אֶת רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, וְאָמַר לִי: לֵךְ קַח לְךָ מִן הַשּׁוּק וְעַשֵּׂר עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates another statement of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri said: Once, my untithed produce became mixed together with** a greater quantity of **non-sacred,** i.e., tithed, **produce, and I came and asked Rabbi Tarfon** how I should separate tithes from the untithed produce that was mixed with the tithed produce. **And he said to me: Go and take from the market** doubtfully tithed produce, which requires the removal of tithes by rabbinic law, **and separate tithes from it on behalf of** the untithed produce that is mixed with the tithed produce.

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* featuring yet another case involving Rabbi Shimon Shezuri: *amar Rabbi Shimon Shezuri: paam achat nisarev li tevel b'chulin u'vati v'shaalti et Rabbi Tarfon* — "One time *tevel* became mixed with *chulin* and I came and asked Rabbi Tarfon." Rashi clarifies that this is *tevel de'oraita* — grain from which *terumos u'maasros* had not been separated, with a full Biblical obligation — which had become mixed with *chulin*, grain already properly tithed. The challenge is that *tevel* is a *davar she'yesh lo mattirin* (something that has a permitted state awaiting it), and for such things *bittul* does not apply even Rabbinically. How, then, can one separate *terumos u'maasros* for *tevel* buried in a mixture?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בְּרוּבָּא בָּטֵל, וְרוֹב עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ מְעַשְּׂרִים הֵן, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּתוֹרֵם מִן הַפְּטוּר עַל הַפְּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rabbi Tarfon **holds** that **by Torah law** the minority of untithed produce is **nullified in the majority** of tithed produce and is therefore exempt from tithes; it is by rabbinic law that it is not nullified and one is obligated to separate tithes from it. **And** additionally, he holds that **the majority of those who are unreliable with regard to tithes [*amei ha'aretz*] do separate tithes,** in which case by Torah law one is not obligated to separate tithes from produce purchased from the market. **And** therefore, if Rabbi Shimon Shezuri receives produce from an *am ha'aretz*, **he is considered** by Torah law **to be separating tithes from exempt** produce **on behalf of exempt** produce, while all of it is obligated in tithes by rabbinic law.

Rabbi Tarfon's solution: go to the marketplace and buy grain of the same type. Marketplace grain purchased from an *am ha'aretz* has the status of *demai* — doubly tithed produce that is Rabbinically obligated but Biblically exempt. Then separate *terumos u'maasros* from the purchased *demai* in proportions that also cover the *tevel* mixed into the *chulin*.

The elegance of this solution lies in the symmetry of obligations. The *tevel* in the mixture is, *de'oraita*, rendered *batel* by the majority of *chulin* — Rabbi Tarfon holds that *bittul* operates Biblically even for *tevel*. It thus carries only a Rabbinic obligation. The *demai*, meanwhile, is Biblically exempt because Rabbi Tarfon holds that the majority of *amei ha'aretz* do tithe (*rov amei ha'aretz me'asrim*), so one may rely on that majority Biblically, yet Rabbinically the obligation remains. Both are therefore Biblically exempt and Rabbinically obligated, and separating from one works for the other: *havei lei k'torem min ha'patur al ha'patur*, it is like separating from the exempt for the exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ קַח מִן הַגּוֹי״, קָסָבַר: אֵין קִנְיָן לְגוֹי בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַפְקִיעַ מִיַּד מַעֲשֵׂר, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִן הַחִיּוּב עַל הַפְּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But let** Rabbi Tarfon **say to him: Go and take** produce **from a gentile.** Since it is exempt from tithes by Torah law but requires tithing by rabbinic law, he could then separate tithes from this produce on behalf of the untithed produce that is nullified by the tithed produce. The Gemara explains: Rabbi Tarfon **holds** that **a gentile has no acquisition** of land **in Eretz Yisrael to abrogate** the sanctity of the land, thereby removing it **from** the obligation to **tithe** its produce. **And** therefore, if Rabbi Shimon Shezuri were to take produce from a gentile, he would be **considered to be** separating tithes **from** produce **that is obligated** in tithes by Torah law **on behalf of exempt** produce, which one may not do.

One might ask why Rabbi Tarfon did not simply separate *terumos u'maasros* from within the mixture itself — taking, say, ten percent of the combined mixture as the portion for the *tevel* mixed within it. Rashi addresses this: since the *chulin* component is entirely exempt, it is not meaningful to designate a separation that is predominantly *chulin* as serving the *tevel*. We cannot take *terumos u'maasros* from within the mixture; an external, equivalently-obligated source must be used instead.

The Gemara then asks: why specifically *demai*? Why not buy grain from a non-Jew? Grain owned by a non-Jew in Eretz Yisrael is also, according to some opinions, Rabbinically obligated but Biblically exempt. The answer is that Rabbi Tarfon holds *ein kinyan l'oveid kochavim b'eretz Yisrael l'hafkiya miyad maaser* — a non-Jew's ownership in Eretz Yisrael does not suffice to exempt his produce Biblically. Grain from a non-Jew thus carries a full Biblical obligation, making it incompatible with the Rabbinically-obligated mixture.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ קַח מִן הַגּוֹי״, קָסָבַר יֵשׁ קִנְיָן לְגוֹי בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַפְקִיעַ מִיַּד מַעֲשֵׂר, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִן הַפְּטוּר עַל הַפְּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that Rabbi Tarfon **said to him: Go and take** produce **from a gentile** and separate tithes from it on behalf of the untithed produce that is intermingled in the majority of tithed produce. Accordingly, Rabbi Tarfon **holds** that **a gentile has acquisition** of land **in Eretz Yisrael to abrogate** the sanctity of the land, thereby removing it **from** the obligation to **tithe** its produce. **And** therefore, if Rabbi Shimon Shezuri takes produce from a gentile, **he is considered,** by Torah law, **to be** separating tithes **from exempt** produce **on behalf of exempt** produce, while all of it is obligated in tithes by rabbinic law.

An alternative version of the discussion reverses the scenario: Rabbi Tarfon actually told Rabbi Shimon Shezuri to buy from a non-Jew, because Rabbi Tarfon holds *yesh kinyan l'oveid kochavim* — non-Jewish ownership *does* suffice to create Biblical exemption — so the non-Jew's grain parallels the *tevel*-in-*chulin* mixture perfectly. In that version the Gemara asks: why not use *demai* instead of grain from a non-Jew? The answer: Rabbi Tarfon holds *ein rov amei ha'aretz me'asrim* — he does not rely on the majority of *amei ha'aretz* tithing — and therefore *demai* is not Biblically exempt at all but rather a *safek de'oraita*, a Biblical doubt, which would make it incompatible with the mixture that is fully exempt Biblically.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״קַח מֵהַשּׁוּק״, קָסָבַר: אֵין רוֹב עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ מְעַשְּׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But let** Rabbi Tarfon **say to him:** Go and **take** produce **from** an *am ha'aretz* in **the market** and separate tithes from it on behalf of the mixed untithed produce. The Gemara explains: Rabbi Tarfon **holds** that **the majority of *amei ha'aretz* do not separate tithes,** in which case he is considered to be separating tithes from produce that is obligated in tithes by Torah law on behalf of exempt produce.

## Universal Application

### Mixed Case Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב יֵימַר בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא לְרַב פָּפָּא: הָא דְּאָמַר רָבִין בַּר חִינָּנָא אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ, אַף בְּנִתְעָרֵב לֵיהּ טֶבֶל בְּחוּלִּין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar bar Shelamya sent** the following question **to Rav Pappa: That which Ravin bar Ḥinnana said** that **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri, and moreover, any place where Rabbi Shimon Shezuri taught** a *halakha*, the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with his** opinion, was that said **even with regard to** the case of **one whose untithed produce became mixed together with non-sacred,** i.e., tithed, **produce,** or was Rabbi Ḥanina referring only to cases where Rabbi Shimon Shezuri stated his opinion in the Mishna, but not in a *baraita*?

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Shmuel sent a question to Rav Pappa: Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Ulla in the name of Rabbi Chanina that the *halachah* follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri — and moreover, *v'lo od ela kol makom sheshana Rabbi Shimon Shezuri, halachah k'moaso* — not only in that case but everywhere Rabbi Shimon Shezuri rules, the *halachah* follows him. Does this sweeping statement extend even to the case just discussed, where *tevel* became mixed with *chulin* and the solution involved separating from *demai* or from grain of a non-Jew?

### Mishnah & Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲמַר לִי מָר זוּטְרָא, קָשֵׁי בַּהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא: פְּשִׁיטָא! מִי קָאָמַר בְּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ? ״כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה״ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa **said to him: Yes,** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri even with regard to untithed produce that was mixed together with tithed produce. **Rav Ashi said: Mar Zutra said to me: Rabbi Ḥanina of Sura raised a difficulty with this:** Isn't it **obvious?** **Does** Rabbi Ḥanina **say** that wherever Rabbi Shimon Shezuri taught a *halakha* **in our Mishna** the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion? Rather, **he says** that **any place where he taught** a *halakha* the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion, and this applies even to *baraitot*.

Rav Pappa replied: yes, *amar lei in* — it applies there as well. Rav Ashi then reported that Mar Zutra told him that Rabbi Chanina of Sura was in fact bothered that the question was even raised. *Pshita* — it is obvious! When Ulla said "everywhere Rabbi Shimon Shezuri rules," he did not say "everywhere Rabbi Shimon Shezuri rules in the *Mishnah*." The formulation *kol makom sheshana* means everywhere he teaches — whether in a *Mishnah* or in a *baraita*. The grain mixture case, which appears in a *baraita*, is therefore included without any question.

With that, the extended discussion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's rulings — spanning *tumah*, vessel measurement, solidified wine, and *terumos u'maasros* — reaches its conclusion. The anchor case for Ulla's general ruling is identified as either the *shida* measurement or the solidified wine, both being rulings of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri on which no *amora* had previously commented.

## Tears & Sewing

### Two-Line Tears

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְעֵירָא, אָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל, אָמַר רַב: קֶרַע הַבָּא בִּשְׁנֵי שִׁיטִין – יִתְפּוֹר, בְּשָׁלֹשׁ – אַל יִתְפּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ze'eira says** that **Rav Ḥananel says** that **Rav says:** If **a tear** in the parchment of a Torah scroll **extends into two lines, one can sew** the parchment to render the scroll fit, but if it extends **into three** lines then **one cannot sew** it to render it fit.

The Gemara turns to a new topic: tears in the *klaf* of a *Sefer Torah*. Rav Zeira said in the name of Rav Chananel in the name of Rav: *kera haba bishtei shitin yitpor* — a vertical tear that passes through two lines of text may be sewn. Even if a letter in one of those lines is broken by the tear, the *klaf* may be sewn and the letter then rewritten, since one cannot form a letter through the act of sewing — the letter must be written on the *klaf* directly. Subject to that condition, however, the *Sefer Torah* remains *kosher* with a repaired two-line tear.

### Three-Line Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה זוּטֵי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, הָא דְּאָמְרִינַן ״בְּשָׁלֹושׁ אַל יִתְפּוֹר״ – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּעַתִּיקָתָא, אֲבָל חַדְתָּתָא – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba Zuti said to Rav Ashi: This** is what **Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti said in the name of Rava: That which we say,** that if the tear extends **into three** lines **one cannot sew** it to render it fit, **we say only with regard to old** sheets of parchment. **But** in the case of **new** sheets of parchment, **we have no** problem **with it.**

*Bishalosh lo yitpor* — if the tear passes through three lines, it may not be sewn. A tear of that magnitude, if patched together with thread, makes the *Sefer Torah* look like patchwork, which is unseemly. In such a case, the entire *klaf* must effectively be replaced, which is a significant requirement. Rav Zutra reported to Rav Ashi that Rav Yirmeyah of Difti said in the name of Rava: the ruling that a three-line tear may not be sewn applies only to an old *Sefer Torah* (*atikta*). An old scroll that is patched in multiple places reaches a point of genuine unsightliness. But a new *Sefer Torah* (*chadta*) — *leit lan bah* — we have no objection to sewing even a three-line tear.

### Gall Nut Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא עַתִּיקָתָא עַתִּיקָתָא מַמַּשׁ, וְלָא חַדְתָּתָא חַדְתָּתָא מַמַּשׁ, אֶלָּא הָא דְּלָא אֲפִיצָן, הָא דַּאֲפִיצָן, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּגִידִין, אֲבָל בִּגְרָדִין – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And old** does **not** mean **literally old, and new** does **not** mean **literally new. Rather, those** sheets of parchment **that are not processed with gall** are labeled as old and cannot be sewn, whereas **those** sheets of parchment **that are processed with gall** are labeled as new and can be sewn. **And this statement,** that one can sew the parchment and render it fit, applies to sewing it **with sinew; but** if one sews the parchment **with thread [*bigradin*],** it is **not** rendered fit.

"Old" and "new" in this context are not literal chronological designations. *Lo atikta atikta mamash, velo chadta chadta mamash* — old does not literally mean old, and new does not literally mean new. Rather, *ela ha de-afitzan ha de-lo afitzan*: "old" means a *klaf* that has been treated with gall nut, and "new" means one that has not. Gall nut treatment was applied to *klaf* to darken its surface, giving it the appearance of age and making any scraping of the ink visible. The practical effect is that a *klaf* treated with gall nut has a distinctive dark, aged appearance — and it is *that* appearance which triggers the concern for unsightliness when multiple sections are sewn together. A *klaf* without such treatment, regardless of its actual age, is considered "new" for purposes of this *halachah* and may be sewn even where three lines are torn.

### Catgut Requirement

All of the above, regarding when sewing is permitted, applies only when catgut is used: thread made from animal intestine. *Aval be-gardin* — but with regular thread, sewing is not permissible. There are two ways to understand this restriction. Some hold that there is a *halachah le-Moshe mi-Sinai* requiring catgut for the sewing of *klafim*, and this extends to repairs as well. Others maintain that the *halachah le-Moshe mi-Sinai* applies only to the sewing of *klafim* to one another (joining columns), not to repairing a tear; the objection to regular thread is purely aesthetic — regular thread stands out too prominently against the *klaf* and makes the repair visually conspicuous.

### Between Lines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר אַבָּא: בֵּין דַּף לְדַף, בֵּין שִׁיטָה לְשִׁיטָה, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda bar Abba asks:** If the tear occurred in the space **between** one **column and** another **column** but it was of the length that had it occurred inside a column it would have extended more than three lines, and similarly, if the tear occurred **between** one **line and** another **line** horizontally, but not tearing through any letters, **what** is the *halakha*? No answer was found, and therefore the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Yehuda bar Abba posed an unresolved question: what if the tear runs three lines vertically but falls entirely *bein daf le-daf* — in the blank space between columns — without cutting into any text? Or *bein shita le-shita* — what if it is a horizontal tear running between two lines of text, cutting only the blank space between them? In these cases the tear does not visually disrupt the written text in the same way a tear through lines of writing would. Does the three-line restriction still apply, or may such tears be sewn regardless of their extent? The Gemara leaves this as an unresolved question.

## Mezuza Format

### Two Per Line

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זְעֵירִי, אָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל, אָמַר רַב: מְזוּזָה שֶׁכְּתָבָהּ שְׁתַּיִם שְׁתַּיִם, כְּשֵׁרָה. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ וְאַחַת, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ze'eiri says** that **Rav Ḥananel says** that **Rav says: A *mezuza* that one wrote two** by **two,** i.e., two words on each line, is **fit. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If one wrote **two** words on one line, **and three** words on the following line, **and one** word on the line after that, **what is** the *halakha*?

A *mezuza* written with only two words per line — a very long, narrow text — is *keshera*, valid. This is Rashi's reading of the formulation *shtayim shtayim*.

### 2-3-1 Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ כְּשִׁירָה. מֵיתִיבִי: עֲשָׂאָהּ כְּשִׁירָה, אוֹ שִׁירָה כְּמוֹתָהּ – פְּסוּלָה! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: All the more so** that it is fit, **as he prepared it as** one writes **a poem** in the Torah scroll. The song sung by the Jewish people at the sea after the Exodus is written in lines whose length is not uniform. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **one wrote it as** one writes **a poem** in the Torah, **or if one wrote a poem** in the Torah **as** one writes it, it is **unfit.** The Gemara answers: **When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is referring **to a Torah scroll,** not a *mezuza*.

*Iba'ya lehu: shtayim ve-shalosh ve-achat mahu?* — the question was posed: what if the *mezuza* is written with two words on one line, three on the next, and one on the following, in a repeating pattern? For a visual illustration, the Shita Mekubetzet provides examples: *vehayu hadevarim* on the first line, *ha'eileh asher anochi* on the second, *metzavecha* on the third, and so on. Is such a format *kosher*?

One might have assumed that a *mezuza*, like a *Sefer Torah*, requires uniform columns with consistent line lengths. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's answer is striking: *kol shekein she'as'ah keshira* — not only is it valid, it is particularly fine. The staggered 2-3-1 pattern resembles the formatting of *shira* in the Torah — specifically the format described by the Gemara as *ariach al gabei leveinah*, "a brick over a space," like the song *Az Yashir*, in which text and space alternate in a staggered pattern across lines. This type of arrangement is treated as an especially appropriate form for the *mezuza* text, though Rashi and Tosafot do not offer an explanation of *why* this should be so.

The Gemara then raises an apparent difficulty from a *baraita*: if one wrote a section that should be formatted as normal columns in the style of *shira*, or wrote a *shira* section (such as *Shirat Hayam* or *Ha'azinu*) in the format of normal columns, it is *pesulah* — invalid. Does this not contradict the ruling that a *shira*-like format is acceptable?

The Gemara resolves this immediately: *ki tanya hahi b'Sefer Torah* — that teaching refers to a *Sefer Torah*. In a *Sefer Torah*, the *shira* sections (*Az Yashir*, *Ha'azinu*) must be written in their distinctive staggered format, and the regular text must be written as standard columns. Mixing the formats in a *Sefer Torah* is *pesulah*. A *mezuza*, however, does not operate under the same constraints, and its text may be written in a *shira*-like pattern without invalidation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי: אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְזוּזָה שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ וְאַחַת – כְּשֵׁרָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂנָּה כְּקוּבָּה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂנָּה כְּזָנָב.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** by *amora'im* **as well: Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and some say** it was **Rav Aḥa bar bar Ḥana** who **says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **a *mezuza* that one prepared** with **two** words on one line, **and three** words on the following line, **and one** word on the line after that, it is **fit, provided that he does not prepare it like** the shape of **a tent,** i.e., progressively widening the lines, starting with a line of one word, then a line of two words and a line of three, **and provided that he does not prepare it like** the shape of **a tail,** progressively shortening the lines, from three words to two to one.

This is further confirmed by a parallel tradition: *amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan, mezuza she'as'ah shtayim ve-shalosh ve-achat kesheira* — a *mezuza* written in the 2-3-1 pattern is valid.

### Avoid Tent/Tail

Two conditions attach to this leniency. *Shelo ya'asennah ke-kuvah* — one must not write it in the shape of a tent, meaning a progressively widening pattern of one word, then two, then three (1-2-3). And *shelo ya'asennah ke-zanav* — one must not write it like a tail, meaning a progressively narrowing pattern of three, two, one (3-2-1). The Gemara's reasoning, implicit in the discussion, is that the staggered 2-3-1 pattern is recognized as echoing the structure of *shira* — short, long, short, long — a pattern that repeats with balance and symmetry. A simple triangle (tent) or inverted triangle (tail) has no analog in any recognized Torah text format and is therefore seen as visually incoherent or aesthetically disqualifying.

### Al Ha'aretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: ״עַל הָאָרֶץ״ בְּשִׁיטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה, אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּסוֹף שִׁיטָה, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בִּתְחִלַּת שִׁיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says:** One writes the last two words of a *mezuza*, ***al ha'aretz*,** meaning "above the earth" (Deuteronomy 11:21), by themselves **on the final line,** without the preceding word. The Sages disagreed as to how this is done. **Some say** that one writes this phrase **at the end of** the final **line, and some say** that one writes it **at the beginning of** the final **line.**

Rav Chisda rules: *al ha'aretz be-shita acharona* — the words *al ha'aretz* should appear on the final line of the *mezuza*. This is indeed the practice.

### Positioning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר בְּסוֹף שִׁיטָה, כִ״גְבֹהַּ שָׁמַיִם עַל הָאָרֶץ״, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר בִּתְחִילַּת שִׁיטָה, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּמְרַחֲקָא שָׁמַיִם מֵאֶרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their dispute: **The one who says** that one writes it **at the end of** the final **line** interprets the verse: "That your days may be multiplied, and the days of your children, upon the land which the Lord swore unto your fathers to give them, as the days of the heaven above the earth," in a similar manner to the verse: **"For as the heaven is high above the earth"** (Psalms 103:11). Consequently, if one writes "above the earth" at the end of the final line, it will appropriately be below the term "the heaven" at the end of the previous line. **And the one who says** that one writes it **at the beginning of** the final **line** explains the phrase "as the days of the heaven above the earth" as meaning: **Just as** the **heaven is far from the earth.** Consequently, if one writes "above the earth" at the beginning of the final line, it is far from the term "the heaven" at the end of the previous line.

Two opinions are cited regarding exactly where on that final line *al ha'aretz* should appear. *Ika de'amrei besof shita* — some say it is left-justified, appearing at the end of the line with blank space before it. *Ve-ika de'amrei be-tichlas shita* — others say it is right-justified, appearing at the beginning of the line. The Shita Mekubetzet provides visual depictions of both options.

Each position carries a visual rationale. The one who says *besof shita* — left-justified — draws on the phrase *kegova shamayim al ha'aretz*: the heavens are positioned directly above the earth, visually, with *shamayim* in the line above *aretz*, and *aretz* below it. The one who says *be-tichlas shita* — right-justified — points to the verse *ki heichi d'merachka shamayim mei-aretz*: the point is that heaven is *distant* from earth, and placing *aretz* as far as possible from where *shamayim* appears makes that separation visually manifest.

In current practice, *al ha'aretz* does appear on the last line of the *mezuza*, right-justified. The *mezuza* itself is written in standard justified columns rather than in a *shira* pattern. The question of how to format the break between the two paragraphs — *Shema* and *Ve-haya im shamo'a* — will be addressed in the next shiur.
