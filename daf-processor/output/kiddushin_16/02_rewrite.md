# Kiddushin 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinyanim Overview

### Progression Overview

Today's daf is 16 and we have moved on from the discussion of how a woman is acquired or acquires herself, and how a *yevama* represents a similar type of scenario. We now turn to other acquisitions, and this *perek* is really about *kinyanim* broadly. The progression moves in stages: we begin with the *kinyan* of a woman through marriage, which, as we have discussed, may have evolved from something more purely *kinyan*-based to something more centered on personal status. From there we turn to the *kinyan* related to a Jewish slave, the *eved Ivri*, and then we will proceed to the *eved Kena'ani*, and eventually to animals and inanimate objects.

### Eved Ivri Concept

The *kinyan* of a woman through marriage is followed by the *kinyan* of the *eved Ivri*, which is emphatically not an ownership of the person as property, but rather an ownership of the rights to the person's labor — *ma'asei yedeihem*. The *eved Kena'ani*, by contrast, does involve ownership of the person as property, though even there the Torah's innovation is that the slave retains recognition as a human being — a point not to be taken for granted in the ancient world. The *eved Ivri* thus occupies a middle position: the master acquires the right to compel labor, but does not acquire the person as an object of property.

## Shtar Kinyan Source

### Mishna Teaching

The *Mishna* already established *kesef* as a mode of acquiring an *eved Ivri*, and the Gemara derived the source for that. We now turn to the second mode: *shtar*. The Gemara on 16a, five lines from the top, asks: *uv'shtar — minalan?* Where do we derive that an *eved Ivri* or *amah Ivriyah* can be acquired through a document?

*Amar Ulla amar kra: im acheres yikach lo* — the verse, speaking of the *amah Ivriyah* whose master takes an additional wife, says that the master may not diminish the first wife's *she'era kesuta ve'onata*, the conjugal and material obligations due to her. The Gemara uses this verse to make a comparison: *hekisha hakatuv le'acheres* — the Torah links the original acquisition of the girl as a slave to the subsequent taking of a wife. Just as a wife can be acquired through a *shtar* — *mah acheres maknya b'shtar* — so too an *amah Ivriyah* is acquired through a *shtar* — *af amah Ivriyah maknya b'shtar*.

### Marriage vs Slave

This analogy immediately raises a question. The comparison is between acquiring an *amah Ivriyah* and acquiring a wife through *kiddushin*. But who writes the *shtar*? This is not a trivial matter. Consider the scenario: I am purchasing a young girl from her father. Do I write a *shtar* saying *bitcha knuyah li* — your daughter is acquired by me — and hand it to the father? Or does the father write *biti mechura lach* — my daughter is sold to you — and give it to me?

By *kinyan karka*, the seller writes the *shtar*. The logic is clear: a *shtar* can serve as proof of a transaction, and the purchaser is the one who needs that proof. So when land is sold, the seller writes *sadi mechura lach*. By contrast, in *kiddushin*, it is the husband who writes the document — *harei at mekudeshet li*, or in the context of the father's daughter, *harei bitcha mekudeshet li*.

### Two Shtar Models

There are thus two distinct models. The first is the *shtar mechira* model, where the seller writes the document, as by the sale of land. The second is the *shtar kiddushin* model, where the acquirer writes it. These are not merely formal differences — they reflect two fundamentally different conceptions of what the transaction is. If the *amah Ivriyah* acquisition is modeled on *kiddushei ishah*, it should be the master writing the *shtar*. If it is modeled on the sale of property, it should be the father writing it.

The relevance of this distinction to the derivation is direct: the verse *im acheres yikach lo* compares the sale of the daughter to the taking of a wife through *kiddushin*. This comparison is compelling only if the same party writes the *shtar* in both cases — namely, the acquirer. If the father writes the *shtar*, the analogy to *kiddushin* collapses, and the basis for deriving *kinyan shtar* from *im acheres yikach lo* disappears entirely.

### Rav Huna vs Chisda

The Gemara records a dispute on precisely this point. *Shtar amah Ivriyah — mi kotvo?* Rav Huna says: *adon kotvo*, the master writes it. Rav Chisda says: *av kotvo*, the father writes it.

According to Rav Huna, who holds that the master writes the *shtar*, the comparison to *kiddushei ishah* is apt and the derivation works cleanly. According to Rav Chisda, who holds that the father writes it — reflecting the property-sale model — the derivation from *im acheres yikach lo* cannot stand. A different source must be found. It is to supply that alternative source that Rav Acha bar Yaakov introduces the verse *lo tetzei k'tzeit ha'avadim*.

## Lo Tetzei Derivation

### Lo Tetzei Meaning

*Lo tetzei k'tzeit ha'avadim* — she shall not go free like slaves go free. The plain sense of this verse, as Chazal understand it, is that a Jewish slave — whether male or female — does not go free when the master knocks out an eye or destroys a limb, as an *eved Kena'ani* does. For an *eved Kena'ani*, loss of an eye or a tooth triggers freedom. For an *eved Ivri*, it does not.

This may seem unjust, but there is an important halakhic consideration. If the master knocks out the eye of a Jewish slave, the slave does not go free, but the master does owe substantial monetary compensation for the injury. The slave may then use that compensation to purchase their own freedom. The Torah's arrangement may therefore actually benefit the slave: rather than losing a few remaining years of service, they receive significant compensation for the injury — and can use part of it to redeem themselves.

One might ask: how do we know the verse is speaking about *eved Kena'ani* when it says "like slaves go free"? This requires some discussion, but the Gemara proceeds on this assumption.

### Drasha Logic

The *drasha* built on this verse is elegant in structure: *lo tetzei k'tzeit ha'avadim — aval nikneit hi k'kinyan avadim*. She does not go *out* like slaves go out, but she is *acquired* like slaves are acquired. Since the verse is specifically about going free, the phrase "like slaves" must be doing additional work — pointing us toward the manner of acquisition. And the manner of acquiring an *eved Kena'ani* is, as will be detailed, through *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*.

### Three Kinyan Methods

An *eved Kena'ani*, treated as property, is acquired by the same three modes as land: *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*. A woman is acquired by *kesef*, *shtar*, and *biah*. An *eved Ivri* is acquired by — as we will see — *kesef* and *shtar* only. Notably, there is no third mode parallel to *chazakah*. The *drasha* from *lo tetzei k'tzeit ha'avadim* is that the *amah Ivriyah* is acquired *like* an *eved Kena'ani*, which includes *shtar*. But *kesef* we already know from other sources, so this verse comes to teach us *shtar*. The Gemara then raises an objection: perhaps the verse is teaching *chazakah*, not *shtar*? After all, those are both modes of acquiring an *eved Kena'ani* and we already have *kesef*.

What is *chazakah* in the context of a slave? It is demonstrating mastery through some form of service rendered to the acquirer. If the slave ties my shoe or helps me on with my coat, that act of service constitutes *chazakah*. Interestingly, there is a slight asymmetry between *chazakah* over a slave and *chazakah* over land: with land, the classic form of *chazakah* is improving the property (e.g., repairing a breach in a fence), rather than extracting benefit from it, whereas with a slave it is precisely rendering service — a form of benefit — that establishes mastery. One could attempt to construct a parallel to *biah* as a form of *chazakah*, but that discussion leads into complex territory.

### No Chazakah Principle

The Gemara's answer for why the verse teaches *shtar* and not *chazakah* emerges from a different verse. For the *eved Kena'ani*, the Torah says *v'hisnachaltem osam livneichem achareichem* — you shall bequeath them to your children after you. The Gemara reads from this: *osam b'chazakah v'lo acher b'chazakah* — it is they, the *eved Kena'ani*, who are acquired by *chazakah*, and not another. The *chazakah* mode of acquisition is reserved for *eved Kena'ani* alone, because *chazakah* expresses full possessory ownership — something that applies only where the person is treated entirely as property.

The Gemara anticipates a counter-reading: perhaps the verse is telling us that the *eved Kena'ani* is acquired with *shtar* and not another — i.e., that *shtar* is for *eved Kena'ani* exclusively, and the *eved Ivri* gets *chazakah* instead. But the Gemara responds: *haktiv lo tetzei k'tzeit ha'avadim* — the verse establishes that there is *some* mode of acquisition shared between *eved Ivri* and *eved Kena'ani*. The question is only which one.

### Rashi on Chazakah

Rashi makes this point sharply. The full verse reads *v'hisnachaltem osam livneichem achareichem lareshes achuzah* — to inherit as a possession. Rashi explains: *b'eved Kena'ani achsiv osam k'achuzah* — the Torah treats the *eved Kena'ani* like a real estate holding, like *achuzah*. It is precisely this property-possession framing — the *achuzah* dimension — that makes *chazakah* appropriate for *eved Kena'ani* and limits it to that category. An *eved Kena'ani* is like a parcel of land: fully possessed, fully owned, transmissible. That is the conceptual home of *chazakah*.

## Ownership Concept

### Michael's Question

A closely related question arises regarding *shtar* and the *amah Ivriyah*. If the master wishes to perform *yi'ud* — to marry the girl — we established earlier that the *kesef mechira*, the money of the sale, transforms into *kesef kiddushin*. But what happens if the *amah Ivriyah* was acquired through a *shtar* rather than money? If no money changed hands at the moment of acquisition — or if money was not yet paid — is there any *kesef* available for *yi'ud*? And would a new *shtar* be required for *kiddushin*, given that a *shtar mechira* is too specific an instrument to double as a *shtar kiddushin*? Unlike money, which has no intrinsic identity — *tiv'a chad hu* — a *shtar* is a specific document with specific content, and it cannot simply change its legal character. This question remains to be investigated further.

---

## Chazakah vs Shtar

### Shtar Preference

Returning to the central question: of the two remaining candidates — *shtar* and *chazakah* — which one applies to *eved Ivri*? The Gemara offers a principled reason to prefer *shtar*: *shtar havei lei leribuyei, sheken motzi bebat Yisrael* — we should derive that *shtar* is the operative *kinyan* because *shtar* is found to work in the context of transactions involving a Jewish person who is not being treated as property. Specifically, *shtar* works in the domain of marriage — *kiddushin* and *geirushin*. Tosafot notes a slight oddity: the Gemara says *motzi bebat Yisrael*, emphasizing the *get*, rather than *machnisa bebat Yisrael*, the *shtar kiddushin*, which would be the more direct parallel. Tosafot explains that the *shtar* of divorce is more explicit in the Torah and therefore is the preferred reference. But the underlying point is the same: *shtar* operates in contexts of personal status, not only property.

### Chazakah Power

The Gemara considers the counter-argument: perhaps *chazakah* should be preferred, because *chazakah* is the more powerful *kinyan* in at least one critical respect. It works even where there is no seller — specifically, by *nichsei ha-ger*, the property of a convert who dies without heirs. Since a convert who dies without children has no relatives, his estate is *hefker*, ownerless. One cannot write a *shtar* and one cannot pay *kesef* to a seller, since there is none. But one can acquire the property through *chazakah* alone. This makes *chazakah* the stronger *kinyan* in certain respects. Perhaps then it should be the one that extends to *eved Ivri*.

### Shtar Works Persons

The Gemara's decisive response: *be'ishah miha lo ashkechan* — we have never found *chazakah* working in a context of marriage, of personal status involving a human being who is not treated as property. The force of this response is not merely formal. The deeper point is that *chazakah* is conceptually rooted in the idea of taking physical possession of an object — of property. It works wherever ownership is of that possessory, property-holding character. *Shtar*, by contrast, has demonstrated applicability to human beings in non-property contexts: *kiddushin* and *geirushin*. Since an *eved Ivri* is not owned as property but rather under a form of personal-status obligation, the *kinyan* that resonates with personal status — *shtar* — is the appropriate one.

### Combined Approach

The Gemara ultimately offers a combined approach: *lehachi ahani im acheres*. The verse *im acheres yikach lo* pointed toward *shtar*, but was not conclusive on its own — because it depended on the contested question of who writes the *shtar*, and if it is the father who writes it, the comparison to *kiddushin* does not hold. But taken together with the *lo tetzei* derivation, which independently tells us that one of the *kinyanim* of *eved Kena'ani* extends to *eved Ivri*, the two sources work in concert: *im acheres* establishes the *shtar* direction, and *lo tetzei*, by excluding *chazakah*, confirms that among the available *kinyanim*, it is *shtar* that applies to *eved Ivri*.

The overarching conclusion is significant: an *eved Ivri* has *kesef* and *shtar* — but not *chazakah*. That absence is not accidental. *Chazakah* is the *kinyan* most expressive of full possessory ownership, of treating something as property. Its exclusion from *eved Ivri* is the Gemara's way of encoding, at the level of *kinyan*, that the *eved Ivri* is not property. He retains his fundamental human status even while his labor has been acquired.

## Ownership Concept (II)

### Braita Sources

The *Mishna* teaches that an *eved Ivri* gains his freedom through *shanim* (six years of service), *yovel*, and *gir'on kesef* (pro-rated redemption). A *braita* supplements the *Mishna* by adding that he can also acquire his own freedom — *v'koneh atzmo b'kessef u'v'shaveh kessef u'v'shtar*. Not only money but the equivalent of money, and additionally a *shtar*.

The Gemara traces the sources for each. *Kesef* derives from *mikessef miknato* in Vayikra, which refers to the scenario of a Jew who sells himself into servitude — the redemption price can be paid back from the money of his acquisition. *Shaveh kesef* — the equivalent of money, not cash — derives from *yashiv geulato* in that same *parsha* in Behar, where the general formulation "shall give back his redemption" without specifying *kesef* indicates that any form of equivalent value suffices.

### Shtar Admei IOU

The *braita*'s addition of *shtar* raises immediate difficulties. *Ela hai shtar heichi dami?* What is the scenario of this *shtar*? If the *eved Ivri* wrote an IOU — a *shtar admei* — to the master, and the master accepted it as payment for the slave's freedom, is that not simply *kesef*? The Gemara says: *hainu kessef* — that is money; it is already subsumed under the *kesef* category.

This assumption deserves pause. Normally, halakha does not treat a *shtar chov*, a promissory note, as *shaveh kesef*. A check or an IOU has no intrinsic value — no object of value changes hands; there is merely a statement of debt. The Gemara's casual equation of a *shtar admei* with *kesef* is in fact quite surprising. One could rationalize it by saying the master is effectively agreeing to treat the debt as if already paid — as though he has lent the slave the money and been immediately repaid — but this is far from self-evident, and the Gemara does not press the point. This is also why the scenario is genuinely anomalous in comparison to *kesef kiddushin*, where a check for ten thousand dollars would not work but a coin worth a few cents would — because *kiddushin* requires intrinsic value, not mere obligation.

If the *shtar* is not an IOU, then it must refer to a *shtar shichrur* — a document written by the master declaring the slave free, *harei ata ben chorin*. And here the Gemara raises a deeper question: *shtar lamma li?* Why would you need a *shtar* for that? Why could the master not simply say in front of two witnesses — *zil*, go, you are free — or declare it before a *beis din*? If releasing the slave is simply a matter of waiving a legal claim, a verbal waiver should suffice.

### Rava's Principle

This question leads to one of the most conceptually significant statements in the *sugya*. *Amar Rava: zos omeres eved Ivri gufo kanui* — this teaches that even an *eved Ivri*'s body is considered owned, acquired by the master. The proof: *rav she-machal al gir'ono, ein gir'ono machul* — a master who simply waives his financial claim against the slave does not thereby free the slave. The waiver does not work.

This requires careful unpacking. One might have understood the *eved Ivri* relationship as purely contractual: the slave owes a debt, has agreed to pay it off through labor, and the master has a contractual right to demand that labor. If so, the master could simply waive his contractual rights and the slave would be free. But Rava is saying that this is wrong. The relationship is not merely contractual. The master genuinely *owns* the *eved Ivri* — not as property in the full sense, but with a real form of ownership. And ownership cannot be extinguished by mere waiver of rights. You cannot free yourself from ownership of a car by simply saying "I waive my rights to it." You must either sell it or perform a positive act of renunciation — *hafka'ah*, making it ownerless.

### Waiving vs Renouncing

Tosafot draws the crucial distinction. While verbal waiver of rights does not work, a verbal renunciation of *ownership* — being *mafkir* — can work. The master could declare: "I renounce my ownership of you," and that would be effective, because it is not a mere waiver but a positive relinquishment of the ownership itself. The difference is between saying "I no longer want to enforce my claim" (which changes nothing about the underlying ownership relationship) and "I declare this person ownerless" (which actively dissolves the ownership). The former fails; the latter would succeed.

The *shtar shichrur* that says *harei ata ben chorin* works precisely because it is read not as a waiver of rights but as a declaration that dissolves the status — an act of renouncing ownership, not merely forgoing its exercise. This is why the *shtar* is conceptually effective where the verbal claim of *mochel* is not: the *shtar* effects a status change, which can also be understood as the equivalent of declaring the slave ownerless.

The conceptual parallel here is to a husband's relationship to his wife's property. A husband does not merely have contractual rights to the *peiros* of his wife's property; he has a form of ownership — *guf l'peiros* — over it. Similarly, the master's entitlement to the *eved Ivri*'s labor is not merely a contractual claim but a form of genuine, if limited, ownership. That is why *mechilah* alone cannot dissolve it.

## Amah Freedom Sources

### Mishnah Teaching

The *Mishna* teaches that an *amah Ivriyah* is in one way greater — *yeseira* — than a male *eved Ivri*: she gains her freedom through *simanim*, the physical signs of physical maturity. When she develops the signs of puberty, she is released from her master's control even if the six years are not complete.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish adds a mode of freedom not listed in the *Mishna*: *amah Ivriyah konah atzma b'mitat ha'av meirshat adon m'kal vachomer* — an *amah Ivriyah* acquires her freedom from the master's domain through the death of her father, derived by *kal vachomer*.

### Ironic Comparison

The *kal vachomer* runs as follows: *simanim*, signs of puberty, do not remove a *na'ara* from her father's control — a girl only leaves her father's authority when she becomes a *bogeret*, and *simanim* alone do not accomplish that. Yet *simanim* do free her from the master's control. If so, the death of the father — which does remove her from the father's control entirely — should certainly free her from the master's control as well. The irony is built into the argument: the event that removes her from one domain (her father's) is precisely the one Reish Lakish claims also removes her from the other.

---

## Defense Reish Lakish

### Hoshaya Challenge

Rav Hoshaya raises an immediate challenge to Reish Lakish. The *Mishna* lists *simanim* as the way in which an *amah Ivriyah* is *yeseira* over the male slave. If the death of the father also frees her, why doesn't the *Mishna* mention that as well? The omission appears to refute Reish Lakish's claim.

### Abbreviated List

The Gemara's first answer is *tana v'shayer* — it is an abbreviated list; the *Mishna* left things out. This answer, however, is only acceptable if something *else* was also omitted. Otherwise the charge that the *Mishna* left off exactly one item, and that item happens to be Reish Lakish's novel addition, is damning. The Gemara therefore proposes that the *Mishna* also omitted the case of *mitat ha'adon* — the death of the master. By a normal *eved Ivri*, if the master dies, the slave continues working for the sons. For an *amah Ivriyah*, the master's death frees her. That is an additional omission, and so the *tana v'shayer* response has at least two items to point to.

However, the Gemara immediately questions this. In the case of a *nirtza* — a slave whose ear has been pierced and who continues working until *yovel* — the master's death also frees him; he does not continue to work for the sons. This is itself a notable irony: the *nirtza*, who is bound more permanently, is in this respect treated *less* like property than a regular *eved Ivri*, who does continue to work for the sons. The logic, as the Gemara implies, is that the regular six-year slave's situation is more contractual — the obligation to render six years of service survives the master and is owed to the heirs — while the *nirtza*, who is more like property, is specifically *not* passed on to the sons, because the Torah is making the point that even a *nirtza* is not property. And property does pass to the sons. Since *mitat ha'adon* also applies to the male *nirtza*, it is not exclusive to the woman, and thus does not qualify as an additional omission that distinguishes the *amah Ivriyah*.

### Fixed vs Unfixed

The Gemara therefore pivots to a different answer. *Tana davar she'yesh lo kitzva kateni, davar she'ein lo kitzva lo kateni* — the *Mishna* taught things that have a fixed or predictable time of occurrence, and did not teach things that have no such fixed time. Six years, *yovel*, and *gir'on kesef* are all knowable in advance or within a defined framework. The death of the father, by contrast, has no predictable timing. It may happen before the girl becomes an adult; it may never happen during her period of servitude. The *Mishna*'s omission is therefore principled, not substantive — it is not denying that *mitat ha'av* frees her; it is simply that this event falls outside the *Mishna*'s organizing criterion.

### Safra Refinement

This answer raises a challenge: *simanim* also have no fixed time. You cannot know when a girl will develop the signs of puberty. The Gemara therefore invokes Rav Safra's refinement: *ein lahem kitzva l'ma'ala* — there is no fixed upper limit; you cannot know when *simanim* will appear. *Aval yesh lahem kitzva l'mata* — but there is a fixed lower limit. The signs cannot appear before a certain minimum age, before the age of 12 for a girl. A *braita* confirms this in the context of a boy: hairs that grow before age 9 are treated as a *shuma*, a mere blemish, of no legal significance. Between 9 and the onset of the thirteenth year the question is disputed, but all agree that hairs appearing after the boy has entered his thirteenth year are unambiguous *simanim*. The point for our purposes is that there is a floor — a defined minimum age — giving *simanim* a form of temporal anchoring even without an upper limit. The death of the father has no such floor. It is entirely unpredictable in both directions.

## Four Hanakah Scenarios

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon teaches that there are four scenarios in which the parting gift of *hanakah* applies — *ha'anek ta'anik lo* — and three of those four apply to a male slave, and three to a female slave, with neither having all four.

### Sheshet Challenge

Rav Sheshet uses this *braita* to challenge Reish Lakish. If the death of the father frees the *amah Ivriyah*, it ought to appear among the enumerated scenarios. And one cannot simply invoke *tana v'shayer* — the abbreviated list defense — when the *braita* gives an explicit count of four. When a fixed number is stated, the enumeration is meant to be exhaustive.

Nor can one fall back on the *kitzva* principle — that only events with fixed timing are enumerated — because *simanim* already appears in the list despite lacking a fully fixed time. And Rav Safra's refinement about a lower limit is already in play.

### Exclusion Rationale

The Gemara therefore presses harder on what is and is not included. The initial assumption was that one of the four scenarios for the male slave was *mitat ha'adon* — the death of the master, as found in the case of the *nirtza*. If so, one might ask why *mitat ha'av* for the woman is not listed alongside it. But the Gemara now clarifies: *mitat ha'adon* is also *not* in this list. The enumeration does not include events with no predictable timing, and *mitat ha'adon*, like *mitat ha'av*, has no *kitzva*. Both are therefore excluded on the same principled basis.

### Four Scenarios

What then are the four scenarios? *Shanim v'yovel v'yovel shel retziah v'amah ha'Ivriyah b'simanim*. Two are shared between man and woman: *shanim* (the six years) and *yovel* (the Jubilee year during the six-year period). The third — *yovel shel retziah* — is the Jubilee year that frees the *nirtza*. This is not *mitat ha'adon* but rather the *yovel* itself catching up with the *nirtza*. The fourth is *simanim*, exclusive to the *amah Ivriyah*.

This identification is confirmed by the closing statement of the *braita*: *i attah yakhol lomar arba'ah b'echad mehem* — you cannot attribute all four to any single individual, because *simanim* does not apply to a man, and *retziah* does not apply to a woman. Had one of the four been *mitat ha'adon*, that would apply to the *amah Ivriyah* through the *nirtza* case as well, and then the woman would have all four — which the *braita* explicitly denies. This confirms that *mitat ha'adon* is not among the four, and provides the parallel that justifies the absence of *mitat ha'av* as well: both are excluded because neither has a *kitzva*.

### Reish Lakish Defended

The conclusion is that Reish Lakish's position is defended. The *Mishna*'s failure to mention *mitat ha'av* as a mode of freedom for the *amah Ivriyah* does not refute his *kal vachomer*. There is a principled reason for the omission: the *Mishna* and the *braita* confine themselves to modes of freedom that have at least a lower temporal bound. *Mitat ha'av*, which has no such bound, is excluded on those grounds — not because it does not free her, but because it does not meet the organizing criterion of the enumeration.
