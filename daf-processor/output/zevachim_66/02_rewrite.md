# Zevachim 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatas v. Olah Bird

### Core Differences

The daf opens by situating us within the broader contrast between the bird *chatas* and the bird *olah*. Both the location of the service and the handling of the blood differ fundamentally between the two. The *chatas* bird is performed below and the *olah* above — a reversal of the animal *korbanot*, where the *chatas* is the one performed at the top. More centrally for the discussion that follows, the *Torah* specifies distinct procedures for each. By the bird *chatas* the verse states *lo yavdil* — you do not sever the head — and this is understood to mean not only that you do not cut all the way through, but that you cut only one of the *simanim*, either the trachea or the esophagus. By the bird *olah*, the understanding is that you do fully sever, separating the head entirely. The blood procedures likewise differ: by the *chatas* the verse says *v'hizah* — the blood is first squirted, then the remainder is squeezed out (*venimtzah damo el yesod hamizbe'ach*); by the *olah* there is no *v'hizah*, only *venimtzah damo*, the squeezing alone.

### R. Eliezer bar R. Shimon

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* had been engaged with the position of Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon, who ruled that if one performs the *chatas* bird in the manner of an *olah* — that is, severs both *simanim* — the offering nevertheless remains *kasher*. One might have thought that the very violation of *lo yavdil* should disqualify the offering by force of the negative command, but the *Gemara* does not focus on that line of argument. Instead, the question it pursues is whether severing both *simanim* constitutes performing the *chatas* in the manner of the *olah*, and it is that equivalence — doing it *like* an *olah* — that would be the operative basis for invalidation.

### Like an Olah?

The *Gemara* then raises the complicating consideration: even if you sever both *simanim* in a *chatas*, would that action actually be comparable to a valid *olah* procedure? This is where the requirement of *rov basar* — cutting the majority of the flesh of the neck — becomes critical. Once you have cut through to the first *siman* and severed it, you must complete the cutting of the majority of the flesh in that same act. By the time you would proceed to the second *siman*, there would necessarily have been a *shehiya*, a pause, between the two. The question then arises whether such a pause between *siman* one and *siman* two would have invalidated the service had this been an actual *olah*. If so, then what was done in the *chatas* would not have constituted a valid *olah*-style *melikah* in the first place, and on that basis Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon would say it does not invalidate the *chatas* — because you have not genuinely "done it like an *olah*."

## Rov Basar Rule

### Inference Rov Basar

The preceding discussion contains an implicit inference: the requirement to cut the majority of the flesh of the neck (*rov basar*) is a necessary component of the *melikah* of a *chatas* bird. The *Gemara* makes this explicit: *michlal de-ba'inan rov basar bit'chilah* — do we not conclude from all of this that it is indeed required to cut through the majority of the flesh, and that this must occur in coordination with the severing of the *siman*?

### Baraita Rules

The *Gemara* confirms this with a *baraita*: How does one perform the *melikah* of a bird *chatas*? One cuts through the spinal cord and the neckbone — but without cutting away the majority of the flesh (*belo rov basar*). The reason is straightforward: were you to sever the majority of the flesh before reaching the *siman*, the bird would in effect already be considered as having been killed before the valid ritual act was performed. The *baraita* therefore specifies that you continue cutting until you reach either the *veshet* or the *kaneh* — the esophagus or the trachea. Once you have reached one of them (*higia la-veshet o la-kaneh*), you sever that one *siman* or the majority of it — and in that same act you complete the cutting of the majority of the flesh (*v'rov basar imo*). The head remains attached, in fulfillment of *lo yavdil*.

### Chatas v. Olah Timing

By an *olah*, the *baraita* continues, you must sever both *simanim* or the majority of both. The structural difference is now clarified: for a *chatas*, you sever one *siman* and the *rov basar* is completed simultaneously in that act. For an *olah*, you must sever both *simanim* — and both must be severed before the *rov basar* is cut. The reason is the same as above: cutting through the majority of the flesh before completing the required *simanim* would constitute killing the bird prior to the valid ritual act.

### Shehiya Issue

This raises the *shehiya* question: if one pauses between the severing of the first and second *siman*, would that pause — after *rov basar* of the first stage has already been cut — constitute an invalidating interruption for the *olah*? Beyond the *shehiya* concern, there is a deeper conceptual question here. When the *Torah* requires two *simanim* by the *olah*, is it redefining the act of *melikah* such that no valid act has been performed until both are severed? Or does severing the first *siman* constitute a complete act of *melikah*, with the severing of the second being an additional requirement? This conceptual question bears directly on whether pausing between the two would be problematic — and indeed on whether proceeding from one *siman* to a second, after the *rov basar* has already been completed in the first act, is even possible within the structure of a valid *olah melikah*.

## R. Yirmiyah's Reading

### Tradition Intro

The entire preceding analysis — the debate over whether Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon's leniency is grounded in the claim that the dual *siman* procedure would not have been valid by an *olah* in any event — is now challenged by Rabbi Yirmiyah. He reports that this analysis has missed the actual reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon entirely. The tradition he invokes runs through Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Das, who cited it in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Shamuah — a chain of two *Elazars*, ultimately transmitting the stated position of Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon.

### Lo Yavdil = Optional

Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon's actual reasoning, according to this tradition, is that *lo yavdil* does not mean *prohibited* — it means *not required*. The verse is not telling you that severing the head is forbidden; it is telling you that it is unnecessary. In his words: *shamati be-chattasot she-mavdilin* — I have received a tradition that severing is permissible for *chatas* birds. And the *lo yavdil* of the verse is to be read as *ein tzarich le-havdil* — there is no requirement to sever.

This is therefore an entirely different basis for his ruling than what the *Gemara* had previously been exploring. The earlier analysis assumed that *lo yavdil* carried a prohibitory force and sought to explain why the violation did not invalidate; Rabbi Yirmiyah's tradition says the premise was wrong — there is no prohibition in *lo yavdil* at all.

### Context Matters

Rav Achai the son of Rava raised a challenge to Rabbi Yirmiyah before Rav Ashi: does this reading not run into the same problem as reading *velo yechasenu* — "and he shall not cover it" — regarding the uncovered pit? Surely we would not read that verse to mean there is no *requirement* to cover a pit? The *Gemara* distinguishes the cases: there, the verse states explicitly that *ba'al ha-bor yeshalem* — the owner of the pit must pay — and it is precisely because he did not cover it that liability arises. The context makes unmistakably clear that the absence of covering generates a monetary obligation. Here, by contrast, the context supports a different reading, and the surrounding verses provide the interpretive framework.

### Olah First

The critical textual point is that the *olah* is mentioned first in the passage. And by the *olah*, the verse does not explicitly say *yavdil* — the requirement to sever is derived only by inference. Given that the *Torah* already distinguishes the *olah* procedure (*v'hikrivo* implies this one is different from that one), the *lo yavdil* by the *chatas* is best read as saying: severing is not required — not as a prohibition. Moreover, since the *olah*, which is discussed first, already carries an implicit inference that one *does* sever, the *lo yavdil* of the *chatas* can naturally be read as the contrasting permissive statement: you need not sever. Reading it as a prohibition, by contrast, would drive a more extreme contrast between the two, placing them at diametrically opposite poles — and since the *Torah* does not explicitly state what is done by the *olah*, the more modest interpretive move is to say that *lo yavdil* means it is optional, not forbidden. As the principle goes, *tafasta merubeh lo tafasta* — if you overreach, you lose the inference. We can move the distinction one degree; we are not entitled to flip the verses to complete opposites. The conclusion, then, is *shma mina ein tzarich lehavdil* — it is simply not required.

## Olah Blood Essence

### Body Blood Only

A *baraita* addresses a question about the blood procedure of the bird *olah*: even if the *kohen* squeezes out only the blood of the body (*dam ha-guf*) and does not squeeze out the blood of the head, the *olah* remains valid.

### Head or Body?

One might have reasoned the opposite — that the blood of the head should be the more critical component. The *baraita* addresses this by invoking the word *hu*: the verse says *olah hu* or *isheh reiach nichoach*, and the word *hu* indicates that only one of the two components is necessary. But the question remains: why is it specifically the body's blood that is indispensable, and not the head's?

### Ravina's Logic

Ravina provides a logical resolution: *mistavra de-rov damim be-guf shechichi* — it stands to reason that the majority of blood is found in the body, not in the head. Since most of the blood is in the body, it is self-evident that when the *Torah* speaks of squeezing out the blood of the *olah* as the essential act, it refers to the body's blood. The head's blood is a secondary quantity, and its omission does not invalidate.

## Chatas/Olah Swap

### Chatas Like Olah

With the *halacha* of *hadran* marking the conclusion of the previous chapter, the *Gemara* opens a new *perek* — though it is striking how seamlessly the new *mishnah* continues the very discussion that immediately preceded it. The *mishnah* takes up precisely the question we have been analyzing: what happens when one performs a *chatas* bird in the manner of an *olah*, or an *olah* bird in the manner of a *chatas*?

The *mishnah* first addresses the bird *chatas* done in a manner departing from its proper service. If it was performed below in the manner of a *chatas* but with *olah* intent (*leshem olah*), it is *pesulah*. If it was performed like an *olah* with *olah* intent (*kema'aseh olah leshem olah*), it is equally invalid — one might have thought that matching the method and intent to the *olah* would effectively redefine the offering, but that reasoning is rejected. The *chatas* cannot be redefined into an *olah* by intent or procedure.

### Olah Correct

If the *chatas* was performed *above* — in the domain of the *olah* — then regardless of whether one intended it as a *chatas* or as an *olah*, it is *pesulah*. The *mishnah* closes off both potential arguments: one might have said that doing everything precisely in the manner of a *chatas* with *chatas* intent should suffice despite the wrong location; or one might have said that performing it above with *olah* intent and *olah* procedure effectively transforms it into an *olah*. Both arguments are rejected. The correct procedure must be followed in the correct place.

Conversely, the bird *olah* done above (*lema'alah*), in the manner of an *olah* (*kema'aseh olah*), with *olah* intent (*leshem olah*) — when all three variables are correct — is *keshera*.

### Olah Like Chatas

Any deviation from those three variables, however, creates problems. If the *olah* was done in the manner of a *chatas*, the most obvious meaning of that is that only one *siman* was severed rather than two. That is clearly invalid — not because it is "like a *chatas*," but because the required *melikah* of the *olah* was simply not performed. Alternatively, "like a *chatas*" could refer to the blood procedure. If the *olah* was done with *chatas* intent (*leshem chatas*) but by an *olah* method — two *simanim* — it is similar to the case of *shelo lishmo*: the *olah* done with the wrong intent, which under ordinary rules would be *keshera* but not credited to the owner's obligation. The *mishnah* is nuanced on this point, and the *Gemara* will explore its details further.

### Wrong Location

The *mishnah* concludes: if the *olah* was performed below (*asah lematah kema'aseh kulan*), it is *pesulah* regardless of how it was done or what intent was held. One might have thought that doing it in the correct *olah* manner with correct *olah* intent would save it despite the wrong location. One might alternatively have thought that performing it below with *chatas* intent and *chatas* procedure effectively reconstitutes it as a *chatas*, which properly belongs below. Both arguments are rejected.

## Like What?

### Melikah or Blood?

The *Gemara* now analyzes what it means for the *mishnah* to say the *chatas* was done "like an *olah*." The two candidates are: (1) *melikah* — that both *simanim* were severed, as in an *olah*; or (2) *haza'ah* — that the blood was handled in the manner of an *olah*, meaning it was squeezed out without the prior squirting that the *chatas* requires.

If the *mishnah* means the *melikah* was changed — that both *simanim* were severed — then the *mishnah* takes a position in the very debate we analyzed earlier: against Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon, who holds that severing both *simanim* does not invalidate the *chatas*. That is not necessarily a problem — the *Gemara* has already established that the *mishnah* does not follow Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon on this point. But the *Gemara* seeks a reading that is, if possible, not the subject of dispute.

### Haza'ah Focus

A cleaner reading is that the *mishnah* means the *haza'ah* was changed — that instead of squirting the blood as the *chatas* requires, the *kohen* only squeezed it. This is an obvious basis for invalidation: the required blood procedure of the *chatas* was not performed. On this reading, the invalidation is not because one "did it like an *olah*" in some abstract way, but because one failed to fulfill what the *Torah* positively requires of the *chatas* — the *v'hizah*.

### From Seifa Logic

The *Gemara* supports this reading by examining the *seifa* — the end of the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states that performing the *chatas* bird *above* (*lema'alah*) invalidates it, regardless of intent or method. When the *mishnah* says you did "it" above, the "it" refers to something that can be placed above or below. The *melikah*, however, can be performed anywhere on the *mizbe'ach* — as the principle states, *melikah be-khol makom bamizbe'ach kesherah*. Since the location of the *melikah* is irrelevant to validity, the invalidating factor of performing the service "above" must refer to the blood — specifically, where the blood was squirted. It was squirted against the upper portion of the *mizbe'ach* rather than the lower, and that is what disqualifies the *chatas*.

### Both Blood Focused

Since the *seifa*'s reference to performing "it" in the wrong location demonstrably refers to the blood, the *Gemara* argues that the *reisha*'s reference to performing "it" like an *olah* should similarly refer to the blood. Doing the *chatas* "like an *olah*" thus means treating the blood like an *olah* — squeezing rather than squirting. This reading is internally consistent and avoids embroiling the *mishnah* in an unresolved dispute.

The *Gemara* ultimately responds that this argument is not a formal proof — *ha-kedei'ita v'ha-kedei'ita*, each phrase means what it means in its own context. The *seifa* necessarily speaks about blood placement; that does not compel the *reisha* to do so as well. Both remain possible: if the *mishnah* speaks of *melikah*, the *chiddush* is that doing something formally extra — a second *siman* — invalidates because of the prohibition implied by *lo yavdil* or because of the principle of doing it "like the other *korban*," even though the *Gemara* never explicitly provides a textual basis for this conclusion. If the *mishnah* speaks of the blood, the invalidation is more transparent — the required act was simply not performed.

## Neveilah/Me'ilah

### Not Neveilah

A new *mishnah* addresses the status of the invalid bird offerings just discussed. Despite their invalidity, all of these bird offerings — whether a *chatas* done like an *olah* or an *olah* done like a *chatas* — do not convey *tum'ah* as *neveilah* would. This is a genuine *chiddush*: since *melikah* is performed from the back of the neck rather than through a proper *shechita*, one might have expected an invalid *melikah* to render the bird a *neveilah*, subject to the impurity of a carcass improperly slaughtered. The *mishnah* teaches otherwise. Even in the case of an *olah* performed like a *chatas* in which only one *siman* was severed — and thus the *melikah* was deficient — the bird is not treated as a *neveilah*. The reasoning is that since the act would have constituted a valid *melikah* for a *chatas*, it retains sufficient halakhic standing to preclude *neveilah* status.

Furthermore, the *mishnah* states that *me'ilah* applies to all of these invalid offerings. Since none of them ever became permissible — neither through valid consumption by *kohanim* in the case of the *chatas*, nor through a proper *olah* service — they remain in the domain of *hekdesh* and subject to the laws of misappropriation.

### Exception Debate

The *mishnah* records one exception: if the *chatas* bird was done below, in the manner of a *chatas*, with *chatas* intent — that is, in every respect as a proper *chatas* — then the offering is simply valid, and after the blood service the bird becomes accessible to the *kohanim*. In that case there is no *me'ilah* because the *hekdesh* has been properly discharged. This, however, is not the exceptional case; it is the standard valid scenario.

The genuine exception the *mishnah* introduces involves the bird *olah* done below, in the manner of a *chatas*, with *chatas* intent — where a dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua applies.

### R. Eliezer: Me'ilah

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *me'ilah* does apply even in this case. The offering was and remains an *olah*; it was never properly discharged; and the fact that everything was done in the manner of a *chatas* does not transform it into a *chatas*. Since the *olah* is never permissible for human benefit even when performed correctly, it certainly retains *me'ilah* when performed invalidly.

### R. Yehoshua: No Me'ilah

Rabbi Yehoshua holds the striking position that *me'ilah* does *not* apply. When one performs the bird *olah* entirely in the manner of a *chatas* — below, with *chatas* intent, with *chatas melikah* — the offering is so thoroughly recategorized in practice that it steps out of the domain of *me'ilah*. It does not become *kasher* as a *chatas*; it remains *pasul*. But it is sufficiently reconstituted in character that *me'ilah* no longer attaches to it.

---

## Recategorization

### Chatas Le-Shem Olah

Rabbi Eliezer challenges Rabbi Yehoshua's position with a pointed argument. Consider the *chatas* bird itself: when a *chatas* is performed correctly, *me'ilah* does not apply after the blood is presented, because the offering is then consumed by the *kohanim*. That is precisely the one category that emerges from *me'ilah* through proper service. Now, if someone performs that *chatas* with *olah* intent (*leshem olah*) — attempting to recategorize it the other direction — the offering is invalid. And in that case, Rabbi Eliezer argues, *me'ilah* clearly applies, because the offering never became permissible. If you allow the logic of recategorization when an *olah* is treated as a *chatas* (Rabbi Yehoshua's position), you must accept the parallel: a *chatas* treated as an *olah* equally becomes *me'ilah*-liable because of its recategorization — now as an *olah*, which carries *me'ilah*. But that parallel only strengthens Rabbi Eliezer's case, not Rabbi Yehoshua's: the recategorization works against you either way.

### Reductio Case

Rabbi Eliezer presses further with a *reductio ad absurdum*. Suppose one takes *kodshei kodashim* — an *olah*, *asham*, or *chatas* — which are required to be slaughtered in the north, and slaughters them in the south (*badarom*) with the intent of *kodashim kalim*, specifically as a *shelamim*. This case attempts to recategorize an offering of the highest sanctity into an offering of lesser sanctity, one that — crucially — does not carry *me'ilah* for its meat. Should we say, following Rabbi Yehoshua's logic, that the recategorization succeeds, and *me'ilah* no longer applies? Surely not. *Me'ilah* applies in that case regardless. Yet here too, the intent and procedure were directed toward something without *me'ilah*. If recategorization does not help there, how can Rabbi Yehoshua maintain it helps by the bird *olah*?

### Reductio Refuted

Rabbi Yehoshua rejects the *reductio*. He concedes that in principle the logic of recategorization should apply to the animal case as well — were the *shelamim* an offering entirely exempt from *me'ilah*, he would indeed say that treating a *kodshei kodashim* entirely like a *shelamim* would remove it from *me'ilah*. But a *shelamim* is not completely exempt from *me'ilah*. The *emurim* — the fats and entrails — are burnt on the *mizbe'ach*, and *me'ilah* applies to them. Only the meat of the *shelamim* is accessible to human benefit; the *mizbe'ach* portions retain *me'ilah*. Therefore, recategorizing an *olah* as a *shelamim* does not place it into a category fully free of *me'ilah*, and the recategorization yields no benefit.

### Complete Exemption

The key distinction for Rabbi Yehoshua is the bird *chatas*. As the *mishnah* elsewhere states, *ein l'mizbe'ach bo klum* — the *mizbe'ach* has no portion in the bird *chatas*. After the blood service, the entire bird is consumed by the *kohanim*. There is no component that goes to the *mizbe'ach* and no portion that remains subject to *me'ilah*. The bird *chatas* is therefore the singular case of an offering *completely* exempt from *me'ilah* once its blood has been presented.

It is precisely this complete exemption that makes recategorization effective, in Rabbi Yehoshua's view. When one takes a bird *olah* and performs it in every respect as a bird *chatas*, one has recategorized it into the only category that is entirely free of *me'ilah*. This does not render the offering *kasher* — it remains *pasul*. But it is sufficient to remove the *me'ilah* that would otherwise apply. The discussion of this remarkable debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua will continue in the next session.
