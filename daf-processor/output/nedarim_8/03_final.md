# Nedarim 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Niddui & Cherem

### Previous Discussion

Daf 8 continues from the preceding discussion of *niddui* and *cherem*. The *mishnah*'s language of *mudrani mimcha* and *menudani mimcha* had drawn the *gemara* into an extended treatment of these forms of excommunication. On the one hand, there are clear parallels between *niddui* and *cherem* and the world of *nedarim* — the *gemara* had discussed performing *hatara* to annul a *cherem*, and there was a question whether certain formulations might constitute *lashon neder*, which is Rabbi Akiva's position. The *gemara*, however, rules that we do not *pasken* accordingly.

### Power of Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנִין אָמַר רַב: הַשּׁוֹמֵעַ הַזְכָּרַת הַשֵּׁם מִפִּי חֲבֵירוֹ צָרִיךְ לְנַדּוֹתוֹ, וְאִם לֹא נִידָּהוּ — הוּא עַצְמוֹ יְהֵא בְּנִידּוּי. שֶׁכׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהַזְכָּרַת הַשֵּׁם מְצוּיָה, שָׁם עֲנִיּוּת מְצוּיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanin said** that **Rav said: One who hears mention of the name** of God in vain **by another** individual **must ostracize him** for doing so. **And if he did not ostracize him, he himself,** the listener, **shall be ostracized, as wherever mention of God's name** in vain **is common, poverty is** also **common there.**

What lends this entire discussion its coherence within *Masechet Nedarim* is the centrality of speech. The preceding *daf* addressed the case of someone who uses God's name loosely — *levatala* — and is consequently placed in *cherem*, itself an act of speech, which is then released through another act of speech. This cycle of verbal creation, verbal sanction, and verbal release speaks directly to the animating theme of the *masechet*. It is in this context that the *gemara* now introduces another statement of Giddel in the name of Rav, one that bears even more directly on the concerns of *Masechet Nedarim*.

## Oath & Mitzvah

### Oath Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן שֶׁנִּשְׁבָּעִין לְקַיֵּים אֶת הַמִּצְוָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי וָאֲקַיֵּמָה לִשְׁמֹר מִשְׁפְּטֵי צִדְקֶךָ".
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived **that one may take an oath to fulfill a mitzva?** It is **as it is stated: "I have sworn and I have confirmed it, to observe Your righteous ordinances"** (Psalms 119:106).

At the top of 8a, Giddel in the name of Rav asks: *m'nayin she'nishba'im l'kayem et hamitzvah* — from where do we know that one may take a *shevuah*, an oath, to fulfill a *mitzvah*? The source is the verse *nishbati va'akayemah lishmor mishpatei tzidkecha* — "I have sworn and I will fulfill, to observe Your righteous edicts." The *gemara* understands this to mean either that it is a praiseworthy thing to do — not a concern of *shem shamayim levatala*, since the intent is to spur oneself to action — or that such a *shevuah* is actually binding. The *gemara* focuses on the second possibility.

### Double Oath Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא מוּשְׁבָּע וְעוֹמֵד מֵהַר סִינַי הוּא. אֶלָּא הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּשָׁרֵי לֵיהּ לְאִינִישׁ לְזָרוֹזֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is he not already under oath from** when each Jew took an oath at **Mount Sinai** to fulfill all the mitzvot? An oath cannot take effect if one is already bound by a different oath. **Rather,** it **teaches us this: It is permitted for a man to motivate himself** to fulfill the mitzvot in this manner, although the oath is not technically valid.

The difficulty is immediately apparent: *v'halo mushba ve'omed meHar Sinai* — is the person not already foresworn from Sinai to observe the commandments? Two related principles apply. If one takes a *shevuah* to *batel* a *mitzvah* — saying, for instance, that he will not sit in the *sukkah* — it is non-binding because he is pre-foresworn to observe it. And conversely, if one takes a *shevuah* that he *will* sit in the *sukkah* and then fails to do so, he has not transgressed the violation of that *shevuah*, because *ein shevuah chala al shevuah* — a second oath cannot take effect upon an existing oath. The *gemara* is thus pressed to explain how an oath to perform a *mitzvah* could have any legal force at all.

### Oath as Zeruzon

The *gemara* resolves this by saying *lo* — technically it is not binding — but this teaches us *l'zeruzon*, *mishum l'ziruzei nafshei*: one is permitted to take such an oath even without binding force, because it serves as a powerful means of encouraging oneself to fulfill an existing obligation. The point is pragmatic: we know that having obligations does not guarantee their fulfillment, and finding ways to motivate ourselves to perform what we are already supposed to do is entirely legitimate.

### Ran's Position

The *Ran* is not satisfied with this simple reading and advances an alternative. He questions whether *ein shevuah chala al shevuah* necessarily means the second oath has no effect whatsoever. Perhaps, the *Ran* suggests, it only means one does not bring a *korban* for it — *de'ha lo mechayev korban* — but there may still be a transgression of *bal yachel*, the prohibition against violating one's oath, *afilu bedvar mitzvah*. He proceeds to argue from a comparison between *shevuah* and *neder* found elsewhere in the *gemara*, concluding that the principle of *ein shevuah chala al shevuah* pertains only to liability for a *korban*, while liability for the *lav* of *bal yachel* — and thus *malkos* — remains in force.

The *Ran*'s reading means that the *chiddush* of Giddel in the name of Rav is not to tell us that the oath takes effect — that is already assumed — but rather to tell us it is an affirmatively good thing to do as a way of encouraging fulfillment of one's obligations. The *Sifsei Avraham* at the bottom of the *daf* cites the *Ramban* in *Parshas Chukas* who takes the opposite position from the *Ran*: if one takes a *shevuah* to perform a *mitzvah* and then fails to do so, he incurs neither a *korban* nor *malkos* — the oath has no effect at all. On this reading, the point of Giddel's statement is simply that it is a legitimate and even meritorious practice to take such an oath to encourage oneself, even though it carries no halachic consequence.

## Neder Mitzva

### Ashne Perek Zeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: הָאוֹמֵר "אַשְׁכִּים וְאֶשְׁנֶה פֶּרֶק זֶה" "אֶשְׁנֶה מַסֶּכְתָּא זוֹ" — נֶדֶר גָּדוֹל נָדַר לֵאלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. וַהֲלֹא מוּשְׁבָּע וְעוֹמֵד הוּא, וְאֵין שְׁבוּעָה חָלָה עַל שְׁבוּעָה! מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ זָרוֹזֵי בְּעָלְמָא — הַיְינוּ דְּרַב גִּידֵּל קַמַּיְיתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: One who says: I will rise early and study this chapter,** or: **I will study this tractate, has taken a great vow to the God of Israel.** This clearly indicates that the vow takes effect. The Gemara asks: **Is he not already under oath** due to the general obligation to study Torah? **And an oath does not take effect upon a** preexisting **oath. What is he teaching us?** If one claims that Rav Giddel is teaching **that** such a vow may be made **even merely** to **motivate oneself** to study, **this is** the same as **the first** statement **of Rav Giddel.**

The *gemara* now introduces a second and closely related statement of Giddel in the name of Rav: *ha'omer eshne perek zeh* — one who says "I will study this *perek* today," or "I am studying this *masechet*" — *neder gadol nadar l'Elokei Yisrael*, he has made a great vow to the God of Israel. This is a striking *gemara*. The person used no *lashon neder* and no *lashon shevuah* — he simply said he was going to do something. How, then, does any form of *shevuah* or *neder* take effect?

### Binding Mechanism

The *gemara* treats the statement *eshne* — "I will study" — as *lashon shevuah*, as a verbal commitment to perform an act. That immediately raises the same problem as before: *v'halo mushba ve'omed hu*, is he not already foresworn from Sinai? *V'ein shevuah chala al shevuah* — how can a second oath take effect here? Nor can we say this is simply another formulation of the first principle, that it is permitted as an act of *zeruzon* to encourage oneself — if that were the whole point, it would be identical to what was already taught in the first statement of Giddel and would add nothing.

### Optional Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי פָּטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בִּקְרִיַּת שְׁמַע שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי חָיֵיל שְׁבוּעָה עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **teaches us this: Since if he desires he can exempt himself** from the obligation to study Torah **with the recitation** of ***Shema*** in the **morning and evening, due to that** reason **the oath takes effect upon him** and he is obligated to study the chapter or tractate that he specified.

The *gemara* therefore resolves the difficulty differently here: *d'ei bei patar nafshei bikrias Shema shacharis ve'arvis* — a person can actually discharge his basic obligation of *Talmud Torah* through recitation of *Shema* morning and evening. The minimum threshold of *Talmud Torah* is derived from the verse *v'shinantam l'vanecha*, together with *uvshochbecha uvkumecha*, from which we learn that the explicit biblical mandate of *Talmud Torah* is fulfilled through *Krias Shema*. This is not a coincidence of formulation: the *psukim* from which we derive the *halacha* of *Krias Shema* are themselves, at the *pshat* level, *psukim* about *Talmud Torah* — which is why there are serious positions among the *Tannaim* that *Krias Shema* is itself only *m'derabanan*, since those *psukim* in their plain sense are about *Talmud Torah*, not about *Shema* as such. This also explains why the *brachos* of *Krias Shema* discharge the *brachos* of *Talmud Torah*: the recitation of *Shema* is understood as a fulfillment of *uvshochbecha uvkumecha*, which at the *pshat* level is the *mitzvah* of *Talmud Torah*.

Since the bare minimum of *Talmud Torah* can be discharged through *Krias Shema*, a person is not obligated to learn any particular *perek* or *masechet*. He could learn all day and satisfy his obligation by reviewing material he has already covered, without ever getting to this specific *perek*. Therefore, he is not *mushba ve'omed* with respect to this particular study, and a *shevuah* — or, as the *gemara* calls it, a *neder* — can take effect on it. *Mishum hachi chala shevuah aleiya.*

### Talmud Torah Scope

One might ask whether *Krias Shema* really constitutes *Talmud Torah* in the full sense, given that it functions primarily as *kabalas ol malchus shamayim*. The broader question of the extent of the *mitzvah* of *Talmud Torah* is significant: the plain sense of this *gemara* appears to be that the minimum is *Krias Shema shacharis ve'arvis*, which is consistent with a number of other *gemaras*. The *gemara*'s formulation here — that one can exempt oneself through *Krias Shema* — is the *pshat* of the passage, and has been used as a source for discussions about the scope of *mitzvas Talmud Torah* ever since, making it an apt text for a *shiur* on *Shavuos* night.

### Ran on Explicit

The *Ran*, however, is uncomfortable with the *gemara*'s apparent implication that a person is not obligated to learn *Torah* all day. In his view — citing the *gemara* in *Menachos*, *lo nitnu divrei Torah ella l'ochlei haman*, and the principle of *v'hagita bo yomam valayla* — every person is required to learn *Torah* to the maximum of his ability, *yomam valayla*, using every available moment. The *Ran* therefore resolves the *gemara* differently: *d'hacha d'mishtava l'kayeim mitzvah she'eina meforeshes b'hedya* — a *shevuah* to fulfill a *mitzvah* takes effect when the obligation is not stated explicitly in the *Torah*. Since the only *explicitly* stated obligation is *Krias Shema shacharis ve'arvis*, all learning beyond that — though obligatory — is not explicit, and therefore a *shevuah* does take effect upon it.

This alternative reading preserves both the *Ran*'s view of maximal *Talmud Torah* obligation and the *gemara*'s conclusion that the *shevuah* is effective. One could also propose a simpler resolution even granting maximal obligation: even if one is obligated to learn every available moment, one is not obligated to learn specifically *this masechet*. One could spend every moment in *chazara* on *Bava Kamma* and never arrive at *Bava Metzia*, and have discharged one's obligation entirely. Therefore there is no *mushba ve'omed* with respect to this specific *perek*.

## Neder Framework

### Shevuah vs Neder

Having established that a statement of intent to perform a *mitzvah* creates some form of binding obligation, the *gemara* calls it a *neder gadol* — a great *neder*. This creates two interrelated difficulties. First, the person used neither *lashon shevuah* nor *lashon neder* — he simply said he would do something. Second, and more conceptually significant, is the question of what the *gemara* means by calling this a *neder* at all, since the *gemara* simultaneously treats it as raising the problem of *ein shevuah chala al shevuah*, which is the language of *shevuah*.

The conceptual distinction between *shevuah* and *neder* is fundamental. A *shevuah* is a sworn declaration in the name of God: if one fails to fulfill it, one has violated an oath and has, in a sense, desecrated God's name by invoking Him as witness. A *neder*, by contrast, is not a promise in this sense — it is an act of creation. When one says *harei zeh asur*, one is not making a promise; one is bringing about a change in the metaphysical status of an object. The wrongness of violating a *neder* lies not in breaking a sworn promise but in failing to live up to the obligation one has constructed for oneself.

### Yaakov's Vow

The biblical model of this phenomenon is *vayidar Yaakov neder lemor* — and Jacob made a vow. Yet when one reads the actual words Jacob spoke — if God protects me and provides for me, then whatever You give me I will tithe to You — there is no formal invocation of an oath, no declaration in God's name. Jacob spoke with a quality of personal commitment and religious conviction, and the *Torah* characterizes the result as a *neder*. This is precisely the phenomenon the *gemara* is describing: a person who says *eshne perek zeh* with genuine commitment and the sense of creating an obligation is engaging in the same kind of act as Jacob's vow.

### Lashon Neder

This also clarifies why no particular formula is required for a *neder*. We have already seen in the *mishnah* that *harei zeh asur alai* — "this is forbidden to me" — constitutes *lashon neder* even without the word *konam* or any other technical term. Similarly, when one says "I am committed to learning this *perek*," that can function as an act of *kabbalah* — of accepting upon oneself an obligation — without any magic words. The line between an ordinary expression of intent and a genuine *neder* may not always be obvious, but the concept itself is entirely grounded in the *Torah*'s own use of the term: *vayidar Yaakov neder*.

What makes this a *neder* conceptually rather than a *shevuah* is precisely that it creates a metaphysical reality — a personal obligation — rather than merely invoking God's name as witness to a future act. When one says "I am accepting upon myself the obligation to do this," the wrong in failing to fulfill it lies in having failed to live up to the status one constructed for oneself, not in having violated a sworn oath. The *gemara*'s language of *neder gadol nadar l'Elokei Yisrael* thus captures something real: this is the Torah's own category of *neder mitzva*, a religious commitment to perform a specific act of divine service, modeled on the biblical narrative of Jacob's vow.

The question that then arises is where *Masechet Nedarim* has until now recognized anything of this kind. The entire *masechet* has been built around *nedarim* as a mechanism for forbidding objects — *konam*, *harei zeh asur*, objects rendered prohibited by one's declaration. Here we are encountering something different: a *neder* that creates a personal obligation to *do* something rather than making something forbidden. And notably, the *gemara* still raises the language of *ein shevuah chala al shevuah* in connection with this *neder*, suggesting that when a *neder* creates a personal obligation it is treated, for certain purposes, as equivalent to a *shevuah*.

### Rosh: Neder Tzedaka

The *Rosh* addresses this directly. In the first wide line of his commentary, he writes: *velav haynu neder mamash* — this is not a *neder* in the strict sense of *matfis bedavar hanadur*, making something forbidden by reference to a prohibited object. Rather, *ela mechashvinan lei ke'ilu nadar litzdaka* — we treat it as though one made a *neder tzedaka*. The source he invokes is *neder beficha zu tzedaka*: a commitment to give *tzedakah* creates a genuine binding obligation. The *Rosh* thus uses *neder tzedaka* as the halakhic model for *neder mitzva* — one can similarly obligate oneself to perform other *mitzvos* by adopting the same form of commitment.

The implication is that *neder tzedaka* itself represents a recognized halachic category in which one's words create a personal obligation, not merely a change in an object's status. Whether this category is *d'oraisa* or *d'rabbanan* is not entirely clear from the *Rosh*, but it functions as a real obligation with real consequences.

### Neder on Mitzvah

This raises an important question: can a *neder* take effect upon an existing *mitzvah* obligation? The *gemara* elsewhere — in the *perek* of *elu nedarim* — rules that a *neder* can indeed take effect on a *dvar mitzvah*, treating it like a *dvar hareshus*. A *shevuah* to not sit in the *sukkah* is ineffective because it directly countermands the *mitzvah*. But a *neder* that renders the *sukkah asur* is an indirect mechanism: the *sukkah* becomes forbidden and then when the *mitzvah* time arrives, there is a collision — this is treated differently, as the *neder* itself is effective even as it conflicts with the *mitzvah*.

However, the *Ran* draws a distinction. A regular *neder* — the classic *matfis bedavar assur* mechanism — is one thing. But a *neder* of the *tzedakah* type, which creates a personal obligation rather than forbidding an object, is effectively functioning like a *shevuah*: both obligate the person to do something. Therefore, the *Ran* argues, such a *neder* follows the rules of a *shevuah* with respect to existing obligations — it cannot take effect on something one is already obligated to perform. *V'gamrinan mishvua d'einah chala al devar mitzvah.* The *chiddush*, then, is precisely that it *can* take effect on a *mitzvah* one is not specifically obligated to fulfill in this particular form.

### Neder Obligations

This leads to a broader conceptual point. Until now the world of *nedarim* in the *masechet* has been the world of forbidden objects. Here we enter a different realm: the *neder* as a mechanism for creating personal religious obligations. This realm is rooted in the *Torah*'s own narratives — *vayidar Yaakov neder*, and the use of *neder* language in connection with *cherem* and *tzedakah* throughout *Tanach*. In all these cases, the underlying act is a commitment made to God in a religious context.

One might ask: why does *neder tzedakah* work at all, given that the standard *neder* mechanism operates on objects? A possible answer — one attributed to the *Ramban* — is that a *neder tzedakah* effectively creates a kind of lien on one's property, even if no specific property is identified. The money ultimately becomes a physical object upon which the *neder* rests, even if indirectly. If so, one might use *neder tzedakah* as a model for *neder mitzva* in other contexts: the *neder* to learn a specific *perek* is similarly treated as creating an object-like obligation in the sphere of religious life, modeled on the *tzedakah* paradigm.

Crucially, there is no suggestion in any of these discussions that one can use this form of *neder mitzva* to create an obligation for a purely secular act. The entire framework operates only in the religious sphere — *vayidar Yaakov neder*, one is *noder* to God. One cannot use a *neder* to obligate oneself to mow one's lawn. The category is specifically that of making a religious commitment to God, in the space where the act is not explicitly required in this specific form, and where one's words can construct a genuine personal obligation of divine service.

Also closely related is the well-known principle that performing a *davar tov* three times creates the equivalent of a *neder* — *ke'ilu kibel al atzmo beneder*. This again points to the same underlying category: through repeated action, one can implicitly construct a personal religious obligation, just as through explicit verbal commitment. This is also the framework invoked in *hatarat nedarim*, where one declares *lo mitzad shevuah velo mitzad neder* while seeking release from *chovoteinu* — the accumulated commitments, verbal or behavioral, that have created personal religious obligations. All of this is ultimately rooted in the same biblical *psukim*.

## Vow to Travel

### Nesak V'Niul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ "נַשְׁכִּים וְנִשְׁנֶה פֶּרֶק זֶה" — עָלָיו לְהַשְׁכִּים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי (בֶּן אָדָם) קוּם צֵא אֶל הַבִּקְעָה וְשָׁם אֲדַבֵּר אוֹתָךְ וָאֵצֵא אֶל הַבִּקְעָה וְהִנֵּה שָׁם כְּבוֹד ה׳ עֹמֵד".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **one who says to another: Let us rise early and study this chapter,** and they agree to do so, **it is** incumbent **upon him to rise early** and be the first to arrive, because it was his initiative. There is an allusion to this in a verse, **as it is stated: "And He said to me: Arise, go forth into the plain, and there I will speak with you.** Then I arose **and went forth into the plain; and behold, the glory of the Lord stood there"** (Ezekiel 3:22–23).

The *gemara* continues: *ha'omer lechavero neissak v'niul l'bei Tarmodei* — one who says to his friend, "Let's get up and go to such-and-such a place." Even if the friend does not comply, the one who made the statement is personally obligated to go. Moreover, the implication is that he must make sure to arrive before his friend — to be there first.

### Ezekiel Source

God invited Ezekiel to that place, and His glory arrived before Ezekiel did. The one who initiates the arrangement has an obligation not only to follow through, but to be present and ready before those he has invited.

## Niddui Dreams

### Dream Niddui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: נִידּוּהוּ בַּחֲלוֹם, צָרִיךְ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם לְהַתִּיר לוֹ, וְהוּא דְּתָנוּ הִלְכְתָא, אֲבָל מַתְנוּ וְלָא תָּנוּ — לָא. וְאִי לֵיכָּא דְּתָנוּ הִלְכְתָא — אֲפִילּוּ מַתְנוּ וְלָא תָּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yosef said: One** who **was ostracized in a dream requires ten people to dissolve** the ostracism **for him. And** the ostracism can be dissolved **only** by those **who have studied *halakha*,** i.e., by Torah scholars. **However,** if they have **recited Mishna and have not studied** *halakha*, **no,** they are not fit to dissolve the ostracism. **And if there are not** ten people **who have studied *halakha*,** then **even** those **who have recited Mishna and have not studied** *halakha* are fit to dissolve the ostracism.

Rav Yosef teaches: if a person has a dream in which he is placed in *cherem*, he must take it seriously as a possible sign of divine displeasure — *kdeniddin min haMakom*. This concept, that a person can be *niduya l'Shamayim*, appears prominently in *Masechet Taanit* in the context of drought, where the community is called upon to act *kdeniddin min haMakom* in response to the apparent withdrawal of divine favor. A dream of being placed in *cherem* is treated as a potential signal of such heavenly censure.

### Ten People

To be released from such a *niddui*, the person requires ten people — *tzrichah asarah b'nei adam l'hatirah*. The *gemara* specifies further: *v'hu d'tani hilchata* — only if these ten have actually studied *halachos*, meaning Talmudic learning, not merely *Mishnah* alone. The reason ten specifically are needed is that ten constitute a *minyan*, in the presence of whom there is *Shechinah sheryuyah* — divine presence. If God's presence rests among ten people, they can act as divine representatives to release the person from the heavenly *niddui*.

### Crossroads Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לֵיכָּא — לֵיזִיל וְלִיתֵּב אַפָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים וְיָהֵיב שְׁלָמָא לְבֵי עַשְׂרָה, עַד דְּמִקַּלְעִי לֵיהּ עַשְׂרָה דְּגָמְרִי הִלְכְתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if there are not** even ten people who have studied Mishna, **let him go and sit at the crossroads and extend** a greeting of ***shalom*,** meaning peace, **to ten people,** who will respond with a similar greeting, **until ten** individuals **who have studied *halakha* happen to come to him.**

If ten people who have studied *halachos* cannot be assembled, the *gemara* offers an alternative: *neizil v'nisvei a'parashat drachim* — go sit at a crossroads, and keep offering *Shalom aleichem* to passersby until one has encountered ten people who have learned *halachos*. The *rishonim* debate the precise mechanism here. Some understand it to mean one continues greeting people until ten *talmidei chachamim* reciprocate and say *Shalom aleichem* back, and this somehow functions as a form of release. The majority of *rishonim*, however, incline toward reading this as a form of protection — greeting and being greeted by ten learned people provides some measure of divine protection until a proper *beis din* can be convened to effect a full release.

### Releaser Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: יָדַע מַאן שַׁמְתֵּיהּ, מַהוּ דְּלִישְׁרֵי לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְשַׁמּוֹתֵיהּ שַׁוְּיוּהּ שָׁלִיחַ, לְמִישְׁרֵי לֵיהּ לָא שַׁוְּיוּהּ שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** If **he knows who excommunicated him** in his dream, **what is** the *halakha*? Can that individual **dissolve** the excommunication **for him?** Rav Ashi **said to him:** It is possible that **he was appointed** by Heaven **as an agent to excommunicate him,** but **he was not appointed as an agent to dissolve** the excommunication **for him.** Consequently, the excommunication can be dissolved only by ten people.

Rav Acha posed a question to Rav Ashi: if one knows in real life who it was that placed him in *cherem* in the dream, can that person release him from it? Rav Ashi responds: *dilma shlicha hu d'shadruhu mishmeih d'Shamaya* — perhaps in the dream that person was acting as a divine agent, sent from Heaven to execute the *niddui*. If so, going to him in real life accomplishes nothing: he was authorized to impose the *niddui* on Heaven's behalf, but he has no authority to rescind what Heaven decreed.

### Dream Cancellation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: שַׁמְּתֻיהּ וּשְׁרוֹ לֵיהּ בְּחֶלְמֵיהּ, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לַבָּר בְּלֹא תֶּבֶן,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa said to Rav Ashi:** If **he was excommunicated and** the excommunication was **dissolved for him in his dream, what is** the *halakha*? Rav Ashi **said to him: Just as it is impossible for the grain** to grow **without straw,**

A further question was posed: what if, within the same dream, the very same person who placed one in *niddui* subsequently released him from it? The dream would appear to cancel itself out, leaving no remaining obligation.

### Dream Nonsense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּךְ אִי אֶפְשָׁר לַחֲלוֹם בְּלֹא דְּבָרִים בְּטֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **so too, it is impossible to dream without idle matter.** It is possible that the excommunication was real and the dissolution was not real, and he must have the ostracism dissolved by ten people.

Rav Papa responds with a striking observation: *keshem she-ee efshar l'bar belo teven, kach ee efshar lachalom belo devarim betelim* — just as it is impossible for grain to exist without straw, so it is impossible for a dream to exist without some nonsense in it. Not every element of a dream carries prophetic weight. One must therefore remain *choshesh* that the part of the dream in which one was placed in *niddui* was the true, meaningful element — and the part in which one was released was the meaningless straw. The obligation to seek proper release therefore remains.

## Husband as Agent

### Wife's Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא הֲוָה לַהּ נִדְרָא לִדְבֵיתְהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, אָמַר לֵיהּ: בַּעַל מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּעָשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ לַחֲרָטַת אִשְׁתּוֹ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מְכַנְּפִין — אִין, אִי לָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Ravina's wife had** taken **a vow** that he had not immediately nullified as a husband is entitled to do, and she wished to have it dissolved. Ravina **came before Rav Ashi** and **said to him: What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **a husband can become an agent to** express **his wife's regret** to a court so that they can dissolve her vow? Rav Ashi **said to him: If** three people who can dissolve the vow **were** already **assembled, yes; if** they are **not** already assembled, **no,** a husband cannot serve as an agent to express his wife's regret to the court.

The *gemara* presents a case: Ravina had a *neder* that his wife had made upon herself — a *neder* of a type that was either beyond the window for a husband's unilateral annulment, or was not the kind that a husband can annul through *hafara* alone. He came before Rav Ashi seeking guidance.

### Representation Q

Ravina asked: *ba'al mahu she'ye'aseh shaliach l'charatat ishto* — can a husband serve as an agent representing his wife in the process of *charata*, the expression of regret that is the basis for *hatarat nedarim*? The scenario is that the wife has explained the circumstances to her husband, who would then present the grounds for *charata* before the *beis din* on her behalf.

### Already Gathered

Rav Ashi answered: *ee michnafim in, ee lav lav* — if the *beis din* is already assembled, yes; if not, no.

### Three Inferences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בַּעַל נַעֲשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ לַחֲרָטַת אִשְׁתּוֹ. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לָא שְׁרֵי לְמִישְׁרֵי נִדְרָא בְּאַתְרָא דְרַבֵּיהּ. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כִּי מְכַנְּפִין שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Learn from this** incident **three** *halakhot*: **Learn from this** that **a husband can become an agent to** express **his wife's regret. And learn from this** that a Sage **is not permitted to dissolve a vow in the location of his teacher,** which is why Ravina, a distinguished Sage, did not dissolve the vow without consulting Rav Ashi. **And learn from this** that **when** three people **were** already **assembled,** it **seems well** for a husband to serve as an agent to express his wife's regret, but if the three people were not yet assembled he cannot serve as her agent.

The *gemara* extracts three inferences from this exchange. First: *ba'al na'aseh shaliach l'charatat ishto* — a husband can indeed represent his wife. Second: *lo sharei l'mishrei nidra b'atra d'rabeih* — it is not appropriate to annul a *neder* in the location of one's *rebbe*, which explains why Ravina did not convene his own *beis din* and instead came to Rav Ashi — raising the larger question of whether a man can annul his own wife's vow, and the broader principle of deferring to one's *rebbe*'s authority. Third: *ki michnafim shapir dami* — if the *beis din* is already gathered, it is entirely proper to proceed.

### Rambam Tosafot

There is an important dispute among the *rishonim* about the underlying principles here. The *Rambam* understands the husband's representation of his wife as a unique exception grounded in *ishto k'gufo* — wife and husband are treated as a single legal unit in certain respects — and not as an instance of a general rule permitting representation before *beis din*. For the *Rambam*, the fundamental requirement is that every individual appear personally before the *beis din*. The condition that the *beis din* already be assembled is, on his reading, a further constraint on this already exceptional leniency: since we are permitting something inherently unusual, we do not extend it to cases where it would require specially convening a *beis din* for this purpose.

*Tosafot* disagrees sharply. *Tosafot* holds that representation before *beis din* is permissible as a general matter — one may even send a letter, and the *beis din* may annul a *neder* without the person's physical presence. The specific problem in the case of a husband representing his wife is not representation per se, but the husband's vested interest in obtaining the *hatara*. Because he benefits from his wife's release, he may unconsciously — or even consciously — present the circumstances in a slanted manner to make the case for *charata* seem stronger than it truly is. Therefore, the requirement that the *beis din* already be assembled serves as a check: if he is not going to all the effort and expense of specially convening a *beis din*, his investment in the outcome is less conspicuous and the concern about biased representation is accordingly reduced.

## Shamta in Rebbe

### Shamta Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁמְתָּא — אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַתְרָא דְרַבֵּיהּ, וְיָחִיד מוּמְחֶה שָׁרֵי שַׁמְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And** one may dissolve **excommunication even in the location of his teacher. And an individual** Sage who is an **expert** can **dissolve excommunication** even without two other judges.

The *gemara* continues: *v'shamta afilu b'asrei d'rabbai* — in a case of *niddui*, even in the location of one's *rebbe*, one may release the person from the *shamta*. This is a surprising ruling. One would have thought the opposite: releasing someone from *cherem* is a more significant assertion of rabbinic authority than annulling a *neder*, since *hatarat cherem* involves representing communal standards and accepting someone back from a state of religious censure — it is more public, more institutional, more clearly an exercise of *rabbinic* power. It would therefore seem that performing *hatarat cherem* in one's *rebbe*'s presence would be a greater usurpation of authority than performing *hatarat nedarim*.

### Kavod HaRav

*Tosafot* addresses this tension directly. *Tosafot* explains: *shamta afilu b'asrei d'rabbai shapir dami lehatir hashamta d'pe'amim shelo yehei rav mzuman* — sometimes the *rebbe* is not available or is otherwise occupied, *v'ein l'akev heter hashamta* — and it is not fair to delay the release of a *shamta*. Leaving a person in a state of *niddui* is an ongoing harm that cannot simply wait for a more convenient moment. The principle is analogous to what the *gemara* says elsewhere — *b'makom sheyesh chilul Hashem ein cholkin kavod l'rav* — when the honor of Heaven demands action, the honor due to a *rebbe* yields. Here the pressing need of the person standing before you demands that one act immediately, even if doing so involves assuming an authority that would normally be deferred to one's *rebbe*.

### Yachid Mumche

Furthermore: *v'yachid mumche sharei shamta* — even an individual expert, without a full *beis din*, may release someone from a *shamta*. This principle applies even in the location of one's *rebbe*, combining both of the preceding points into a single halakhic ruling.

## Sun of Righteousness

### Fear of God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר זְבִיד, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר טַבְלָא, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא אֲרִיכָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי אַחָא, אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, אָמַר רַבִּי מְיָאשָׁה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִילְעַאי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "וְזָרְחָה לָכֶם יִרְאֵי שְׁמִי (שֶׁמֶשׁ צְדָקָה וְגוֹ׳)" אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁהֵן יְרֵאִין לְהוֹצִיא שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם לְבַטָּלָה. "שֶׁמֶשׁ צְדָקָה וּמַרְפֵּא". אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ חִרְגָּא דְיוֹמָא מַסֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Shimon bar Zevid said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Tavla said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya Arikha,** the tall, **of the school of Rabbi Aḥa said** that **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said** that **Rabbi Meyasha said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "But to you that fear My name shall the sun of righteousness arise** with healing in its wings; and you shall go forth and leap as calves of the stall" (Malachi 3:20)? "You that fear My name"; **these are people who are afraid to mention the name of Heaven in vain.** The verse states that **a sun of righteousness with healing** will arise to heal them. **Abaye said: Learn from this** verse that **the dust [*ḥirga*]** that is illuminated by the rays of the sun during **the day heals,** as it states: "With healing in its wings."

The *gemara* now offers a closing teaching on the theme that has run through this entire section. A chain of seven *Amoraim* — Rabbi Shimon ben Zavid citing Rabbi Yitzchak bar Tavla, citing Rabbi Chiya Aricha of Rabbi Acha's academy, citing Rabbi Zeira, citing Rabbi Elazar, citing Rabbi Chanina, citing Rabbi Mayasha in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai — transmit an interpretation of the verse *v'zarcha lachem yirei shmi shemesh tzedakah u'marpe*: to those who fear My name, the sun of righteousness will shine with healing. The *gemara* identifies *yirei shmi* as *bnei adam shehen yere'in l'hotzi shem shamayim l'vatala* — people who are careful not to pronounce God's name loosely. This circles back neatly to the discussion that preceded this *daf*, where a person who uses God's name *levatala* is placed in *cherem*: those who fear God's name precisely avoid that misuse of speech.

### Olam Haba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר: אֵין גֵּיהִנָּם לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מוֹצִיא חַמָּה מִנַּרְתִּיקָהּ, צַדִּיקִים מִתְרַפְּאִים בָּהּ וּרְשָׁעִים נִידּוֹנִים בָּהּ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְזָרְחָה לָכֶם יִרְאֵי שְׁמִי שֶׁמֶשׁ וְגוֹ׳". וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁמִּתְעַדְּנִין בָּהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וִיצָאתֶם וּפִשְׁתֶּם כְּעֶגְלֵי מַרְבֵּק".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Abaye **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** who **said: There is no Gehenna in the World-to-Come. Rather, the Holy One, Blessed be He,** will **remove the sun from its sheath [*minartika*],** and **the righteous will be healed by it and the wicked will be punished by it.** The righteous will be healed by it, **as it is stated: "But to you that fear My name shall the sun** of righteousness **arise** with healing in its wings"; **and moreover,** not only will they be healed by it **but they will** even **be rejuvenated by it, as it is stated: "And you shall go forth and leap as calves of the stall."**

*Reish Lakish* reads the verse very differently, applying it to *olam haba*: *ein gehenom l'olam haba* — there is no *gehenom* in the world to come in the conventional sense. Instead, *hakadosh baruch hu motzi chamah m'nartika* — God will draw the sun out of its sheath, and the same sun will serve two opposite purposes simultaneously.

### Righteous Wicked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָרְשָׁעִים נִידּוֹנִים בָּהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "הִנֵּה הַיּוֹם בָּא בֹּעֵר כַּתַּנּוּר וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** **And the wicked will be punished by** the same sun, **as it is stated: "For behold, the day is coming, it burns as a furnace;** and all the proud, and all that work wickedness, shall be stubble; and the day that comes shall set them ablaze" (Malachi 3:19).

*Tzadikim mitrap'in bah u'rshaim nidonin bah* — the righteous will be healed by that sun, and the wicked will be judged and burned by it. Not only will the righteous be healed, but *shemitangin bah* — they will derive actual pleasure from it, as the verse continues: *v'yatzatem u'fishtem k'eglei marbek*, you will go forth and frolic like calves released from the stall. The wicked, by the same sun, will be punished. The same reality, the same light — experienced entirely differently depending on who one is.

## Mumche Requirements

### Smicha Question

The *gemara*'s ruling that *yachid mumche* may release from *shamta* raises an important halakhic question: what precisely qualifies one as a *yachid mumche* for this purpose? Does it require *smicha* in the classical sense — ordination traceable back to *Moshe Rabbeinu*?

### Taz View

The *Taz* addresses this question. He rules that *yachid mumche* for purposes of *shamta* does not necessarily require classical *smicha*. Rather, what is required is that the individual be a genuine *chacham* who is *baki b'hilchos nidduyin* — expert in the laws of *niddui*. Knowledge and expertise in the relevant halachic domain is the defining criterion, not formal ordination in the chain from Sinai.

### Neder Shamta Diff

There is, however, a meaningful distinction between the requirements for *hatarat nedarim* and the requirements for releasing from *shamta*. For *hatarat nedarim*, the *gemara* elsewhere and *Tosafot* raise the question of whether *parshas elohim* — the scriptural basis for the authority of those who annul *nedarim* — requires *semuchim*, formally ordained judges. The *Taz* discusses the difference between these two domains: the requirements for *hatarat nedarim* and the requirements for *shamta* may not be identical, and although both topics have been treated in parallel throughout this *daf*, they remain distinct areas with their own rules. This remains a matter of ongoing halakhic discussion.
