# Yevamot 66b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slaves & Chalalah

### Setup: Property Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, הִכְנִיסָה לוֹ עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג וְעַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל, עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה, עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל — יֹאכֵלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A widow** married **to a High Priest,** and **a divorcée or a *yevama* who performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*]** married **to a common priest** are all unions prohibited by Torah law. If one of these women **brought** with her **into** the marriage **slaves of usufruct [*melog*]** property **or slaves of guaranteed investment,** then the **slaves of usufruct** property **do not partake of *teruma*** but the **slaves of guaranteed investment do partake** of *teruma*.

The *mishnah* on this *daf* addresses the question of what happens to the slaves of a woman married to a *kohen* with respect to the eating of *terumah*. Two categories of property require distinction from the outset. The first is *nichsei milug*: property that the wife retains legal title to, though the husband enjoys its use. The second is *nichsei tzon barzel*: property whose title transfers to the husband, in exchange for which he bears all losses and retains all gains, while remaining obligated to return the assessed value upon divorce or death.

The *mishnah* focuses on the more conceptually interesting category, *nichsei milug*, the slaves she owns. The case involves a woman who, by virtue of her marriage to a *kohen* — whether as a *gerusha* or *chalutzah* married to an ordinary *kohen*, or as an *almanah* married to a *kohen gadol* — becomes a *chalalah*. The very act of marital relations in a forbidden union renders her a *chalalah*, disqualified from eating *terumah*. The question is: what about her slaves? They are, in some sense, connected to the *kohen* household. May they eat *terumah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג: אִם מֵתוּ — מֵתוּ לָהּ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ — הוֹתִירוּ לָהּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹתָן הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל: אִם מֵתוּ — מֵתוּ לוֹ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ — הוֹתִירוּ לוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְהוּא חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are slaves of usufruct** property: They are those with regard to whom the couple stipulated that **if** the slaves **die, their death** is **her** loss, **and if they increase** in value, **their increase** is **her** gain. **Although** the husband **is obligated in their sustenance, they do not partake of *teruma*,** as they belong to her, not to him. He owns only the right of their use while he is married to her. **And these are slaves of guaranteed investment:** They are those with regard to whom the couple stipulated that **if they die, their death** is **his** loss, **and if they increase** in value, **their increase** is **his** gain. **Since he bears** financial **responsibility** for compensating her in the event of **their** loss, **they partake of *teruma*,** as they are considered his property.

The *mishnah* rules that they may not. The *gemara* explains by invoking the principle *kinyano shekanah kinyan* — a formulation that, while uncomfortable in its description of the wife as a *kinyan*, refers to the husband's legal relationship to her and through her to what she owns. The slaves, in turn, are eating by virtue of her, and if she cannot eat — being a *chalalah* — then she cannot, as it were, confer that right upon her slaves.

### Kinyan Ochel Maachil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַבְדֵי מְלוֹג לֹא יֹאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה, אַמַּאי? לֶהֱוֵי כְּקִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁקָּנָה קִנְיָן! דְּתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְכֹהֵן שֶׁנָּשָׂא אִשָּׁה וְקָנָה עֲבָדִים שֶׁיֹּאכְלוּ בִּתְרוּמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכֹהֵן כִּי יִקְנֶה נֶפֶשׁ קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ הוּא יֹאכַל בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that if a priest married a woman forbidden to him, his wife's **slaves of usufruct** property **do not partake of *teruma*.** The Gemara asks: **Why** is this so? **Let** this case **be like** that of **his acquisition who acquired an acquisition, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived with regard **to a priest who married a woman and acquired slaves that they partake of *teruma*? As it is stated: "But if a priest buys any soul, the purchase of his money, he may eat of it"** (Leviticus 22:11).

The *gemara* challenges this straightforward explanation. If the reason the slaves can eat *terumah* is traced back through the wife, consider the case of a *mamzer* who cannot himself eat *terumah* yet can enable his grandmother to eat. The scenario is this: a *kohen* dies leaving a daughter, who has since married a *mamzer* or even an ordinary *Yisrael*. Their child, not being a *kohen*, cannot eat *terumah*. Yet as long as that grandchild is alive, the grandmother — the original wife of the *kohen* — may continue eating *terumah* by virtue of the connection maintained through her child or grandchild, even if that descendant could never eat *terumah* himself. Here, then, is someone who cannot eat yet enables another to eat. Does the same logic not apply to the wife's slaves?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָאוֹכֵל — מַאֲכִיל, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין אוֹכֵל — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The principle is that **anyone who** is fit to **partake** of *teruma* can **enable** others **to partake** of *teruma*, and **anyone who does not partake** of *teruma* **cannot enable** others **to partake.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי מַמְזֵר, שֶׁאֵין אוֹכֵל, וּמַאֲכִיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** there the case of **a son born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzer*]**, **who does not partake** of *teruma* yet **enables** others to partake?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: קִנְיָן אוֹכֵל קָאָמַר, קִנְיָן אוֹכֵל — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** that the above principle **is referring** to the case of **an acquisition who partakes** of *teruma*. If the **acquisition** of a priest **partakes** of *teruma*, he **enables** others **to partake,** whereas an acquisition **who does not partake,** e.g., his forbidden wife, **cannot enable** others **to partake.**

Ravina replies that these two cases are categorically different. The grandmother eats by virtue of a descendant who traces back to the *kohen* lineage — a separate and independent principle. The question in the *mishnah* concerns eating by virtue of a *kinyan*, specifically the rule *kinyan ochel maachil*: only a *kinyan* that eats can feed. A *kinyan* that cannot itself eat — here, the wife who is a *chalalah* — cannot enable those she owns to eat. That is the first explanation of the *mishnah*.

### Rava's Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מֵיכָל אָכְלִי, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דְּגָזְרִי בְּהוּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁתֹּאמַר: אֲנִי אֵינִי אוֹכֶלֶת, עֲבָדַי אֵינָן אוֹכְלִין — זוֹנָה הִיא אֶצְלוֹ?! הִלְכָּךְ אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said** a different solution. **By Torah** law, the forbidden wife's slaves indeed **partake** of *teruma*, as they are included in the category of: His acquisition who acquired an acquisition. **And it was the Sages who issued a decree** prohibiting **them** from partaking of *teruma*, **so that** the woman unlawfully married to a priest **would say: I do not partake** of his *teruma* and **my slaves do not partake** of it either, so that she will realize that she is not a valid wife, but rather **she is** like **a prostitute to him.** Her husband will **therefore come to divorce her,** which is the desired outcome.

Rava offers a second approach. In truth, *mid'raisa* — on the level of Torah law — the slaves could eat. They are connected to the *kohen* and, independent of the wife's personal disqualification, they retain a basis for eating *terumah* through him. However, the rabbis instituted a *gezeira* preventing the slaves from eating in this case. The reasoning is social and corrective: the *kohen* finds himself in a forbidden union, married to a woman who should not have entered into this marriage with him. By disallowing the slaves from eating, the rabbis create pressure. The wife says: I cannot eat *terumah*, my slaves cannot eat *terumah* — *zona hi etzlo*, I am like a stranger to him, excluded from the very benefits of his priestly status. This discontent, the rabbis reasoned, would motivate the couple to dissolve the marriage.

### Rav Ashi: Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא תַּאֲכִיל לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different reason for the prohibition: It is a rabbinic **decree lest she have** those slaves **partake** of *teruma* even **after** the **death** of her husband the priest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּשֵּׂאת לְכֹהֵן לֹא תַּאֲכִיל, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא תַּאֲכִיל לְאַחַר מִיתָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, if** that is **so,** that the decree is lest she have those slaves partake of *teruma* after her husband's death, any **Israelite woman who marries a priest should not enable** her slaves of usufruct property **to partake** of *teruma* either, due to the same rabbinic **decree, lest she have them partake** of *teruma* **after** her husband's **death.**

Rav Ashi also agrees in principle that the slaves could eat, but offers a different rationale for the rabbinic prohibition. The *gezeira* is *shema ta'achil achar misa* — lest she continue feeding the slaves *terumah* even after the *kohen* dies. Having become accustomed to feeding them during the marriage, she might simply continue the practice after his death, when she is no longer entitled to do so.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: by that logic, why should any *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen* be permitted to feed her slaves *terumah*? She, too, might continue after his death. Should we not decree against her as well? This objection effectively forces Rav Ashi to refine his position.

### Almanah Kohenet Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּאַלְמָנָה כֹּהֶנֶת, דְּאָתְיָא לְאוֹרוֹיֵי: מֵעִיקָּרָא אֲכַלִי בִּתְרוּמָה דְּבֵי נָשָׁא, אִינְּסֵבִי לֵיהּ לְהַאי — אֲכַלִי בִּתְרוּמָה דְגַבְרַאי, וְהַשְׁתָּה הֲדַרִי לִי לְמִילְּתַאי קַמַּיְיתָא. וְלָא יָדְעָה דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא שַׁוֵּיתַהּ לְנַפְשַׁהּ חֲלָלָה, הַשְׁתָּה שַׁוֵּיתַהּ לְנַפְשַׁהּ חֲלָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said** that the decree is dealing **with a widowed priestess,** the daughter of a priest, who then married the High Priest, **as she is likely to rationalize** enabling her slaves to partake of *teruma* after the death of the High Priest as follows: **Initially,** my slaves **partook of the *teruma* of my father's house. I** then **married this** man, **and they partook of the *teruma* of my husband. And now** that my husband died, **I have returned to the original circumstance,** and therefore they may once again partake of my father's *teruma*. **And she does not realize that** this is not so, as **initially she did not render herself a woman disqualified from marrying a priest [*ḥalala*],** but **now,** by marrying a High Priest unlawfully, **she rendered herself a *ḥalala*,** and both she and her slaves do not partake of *teruma* even upon returning to her father's house.

The concern, says Rav Ashi, is specific to the case of an *almanah* married to a *kohen gadol* who is herself a *bat kohen*. Here is where the *gezeira* finds its most compelling foothold. Such a woman, upon the *kohen gadol*'s death, could reason as follows: *me'ikara achli bitruma devei nasha* — originally, my slaves ate *terumah* by virtue of my father's household, because I was a *bat kohen*. Then, when I married this *kohen gadol*, they continued eating by virtue of my husband. Now that he has died, they should revert to their original status and continue eating on account of my being a *bat kohen*.

What she fails to realize is that by entering into the forbidden union — as an *almanah* who had relations with a *kohen gadol* — she became a *chalalah*. She is no longer a *bat kohen* in the relevant legal sense. Her slaves cannot eat by virtue of her father's household anymore, because that status has been forfeited. The fear is that she will not appreciate this transformation and will permit the slaves to eat *terumah* erroneously after his death. The *gezeira* addresses precisely this case.

### Uniform Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח אַלְמָנָה כֹּהֶנֶת, אַלְמָנָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? בְּאַלְמְנוּתַהּ לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This **works out well** as an explanation of the mishna, with regard to **a widowed priestess.** However, if that **widow** who married a High Priest was **an Israelite woman, what can be said?** There is no reason for the decree in that case. The Gemara answers: **With regard to widowhood, the Sages did not distinguish** between one type of widow and another.

The *gemara* then asks: very well, the concern makes sense when the wife is an *almanah kohenet*, but why does the *mishnah* apply this ruling even when she is a *bat Yisrael* — an ordinary Jewess who has no such parallel status to return to? The *gemara* answers with the principle *lo plug rabanan* — the rabbis did not differentiate within a single category. Since the *gezeira* was necessary for the case of the *almanah kohenet*, the rabbis applied it uniformly to all cases of *almanah*, without distinguishing between those who are daughters of *kohanim* and those who are not. This avoids confusion and ensures consistent application of the rule.

To summarize: two explanatory frameworks emerge. The first, Ravina's, holds that the slaves eat only by virtue of the wife, and since she is a *chalalah* and cannot eat, she cannot extend that right — *kinyan ochel maachil*. The second, shared by Rava and Rav Ashi, holds that in principle the slaves could eat through the *kohen*, but a rabbinic *gezeira* overrides this, whether to discourage the forbidden marriage or to prevent erroneous feeding after the husband's death.

## Tzon Barzel Ownership

### The Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּכְנֶסֶת שׁוּם לְבַעְלָהּ, הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: כֵּלַי אֲנִי נוֹטֶלֶת, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: דָּמִים אֲנִי נוֹתֵן, הַדִּין עִם מִי? רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: הַדִּין עִמָּהּ, וְרַב אַמֵּי אָמַר: הַדִּין עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** With regard to a woman **who brings appraised,** guaranteed **property into her** marital contract with her **husband,** he is obligated to return it at the conclusion of the marriage. Upon collection of her marriage contract, e.g., following divorce, if **she says: I am taking my belongings, and he says: I am** willing to **give you** only their **monetary** value, **the *halakha*** favors **whom? Rav Yehuda said: The *halakha*** favors **her;** she may take the belongings. **And Rav Ami said: The *halakha*** favors **him;** he may retain the items and return their value.

The *gemara* now takes up a distinct but related question about *nichsei tzon barzel*. When a woman brings assessed property into the marriage — the husband enters the value into the *ketubah*, accepting full financial responsibility — and the marriage subsequently ends through divorce or death, what exactly is she entitled to recover? She assessed, say, a set of vessels at one thousand *zuz*. Those vessels are still extant. Does she retrieve the vessels themselves, or only their assessed monetary value?

The wife says: *keli ani notelet* — I take my vessels. The husband says: *damim ani noten* — I owe you money, not objects. The dispute is whether title fundamentally transferred to the husband upon the bringing in of the dowry, leaving her only a creditor's claim, or whether she retained some form of ownership such that she may demand the objects back.

### Rav Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר הַדִּין עִמָּהּ — מִשּׁוּם שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ דִּידַהּ הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rav Yehuda said** that **the *halakha*** favors **her because** they are **the assets of her paternal family,** whose prestige will suffer if they aren't returned. Therefore, **they are hers.**

Rav Yehuda rules *hadin imah* — the law favors the wife. She is entitled to retrieve the actual objects. His reasoning is *mishum shevach beit aviha* — on account of the benefit, or honor, of her father's house. This is the property she received from her family, and she is entitled to reclaim it. It is admittedly not a purely technical legal argument — it is an appeal to equitable principle — but it is the position Rav Yehuda maintains.

### Rav Ami Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר: הַדִּין עִמּוֹ — כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: אִם מֵתוּ — מֵתוּ לוֹ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ — הוֹתִירוּ לוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן — יֹאכֵלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said that the *halakha*** favors **him, since the Master said** in the mishna, with regard to guaranteed property: **If they die, their death** is **his** loss, **and if they increase** in value, **their increase** is **his** gain. **Since he bears** financial **responsibility for their** loss, **they partake** of *teruma*.

Rav Ami rules *hadin imo* — the law favors the husband. His reasoning flows from the language of the *mishnah* itself: *im mesu mesu lo v'im hosiru hosiru lo* — if the property dies or is lost, the loss is his; if it increases in value, the gain is his. This, says Rav Ami, demonstrates conclusively that title has transferred. He bears all risk and receives all benefit. The property is therefore his, and she is merely a creditor for its assessed value.

### Renting Analogy Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: מִי קָתָנֵי ״וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ״? ״הוֹאִיל וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן״ קָתָנֵי, וּלְעוֹלָם לָאו דִּידֵיהּ נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra said** in rejection of Rabbi Ami's reasoning: **Does** the mishna **teach** that **they are his?** It **teaches** only that **he bears** financial **responsibility for their** loss, **but actually they are not his.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הֵיכָא דְּחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן אָכְלִי בִּתְרוּמָה? וְהָתְנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשָּׂכַר פָּרָה מִכֹּהֵן — הֲרֵי זֶה יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה. כֹּהֵן שֶׁשָּׂכַר פָּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ עָלָיו — לָא יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do they partake of *teruma* wherever he bears** financial **responsibility for their** loss, even if they are not his actual possession? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Terumot* 11:9): **An Israelite who rented a cow from a priest may feed it vetches [*karshinin*] of *teruma*,** since the animal belongs to a priest? With regard to **a priest who rented a cow from an Israelite, although** its feed **is** incumbent **upon him, he may not feed it vetches of *teruma*,** as it does not belong to him. This indicates that the requirement for enabling an acquisition to eat *teruma* is possession, not responsibility.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? נְהִי נָמֵי דִּמְחַיֵּיב בִּגְנֵבָה וַאֲבֵדָה, בְּאוֹנְסֶיהָ בְּכַחְשָׁהּ וּבְנִפְחַת דָּמֶיהָ — מִי מִיחַיַּיְב? הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לְסֵיפָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשָּׁם פָּרָה מִכֹּהֵן, לָא יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה. אֲבָל כֹּהֵן שֶׁשָּׁם פָּרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל — יַאֲכִילֶנָּה כַּרְשִׁינֵּי תְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **And** how can **you understand** that the case of the rented cow is parallel to the case of guaranteed property? **Though** the renter **is indeed liable for theft and loss, is he liable for unavoidable accidents, for emaciation,** i.e., the cow became thinner or weaker for any reason, **or for** any other **decrease in its value?** Certainly he is not. In fact, the case of guaranteed property **is similar only to the latter clause** of that mishna: In the case of **an Israelite who appraised a cow** upon renting it **from a priest** under an arrangement where he guaranteed its value to the owner, he **may not feed it vetches of *teruma*,** as it is considered his own. **However, a priest who appraised a cow** upon renting it **from an Israelite may feed it vetches of *teruma*.** This indicates that guaranteed property is considered the acquisition of its recipient with regard to enabling it to eat *teruma*.

The *gemara* attempts to probe the logic by noting the *mishnah*'s further language: *v'hoil v'chayav b'achrayusan yochalu* — and since he is responsible for them, the slaves eat *terumah*. This seems to confirm both that the slaves belong to him and that his responsibility is characteristic of ownership. But does mere responsibility, *achrayus*, suffice to allow slaves to eat *terumah* even if they are not technically owned by the *kohen*?

A *baraita* is introduced: a *Yisrael* who rents a cow from a *kohen* — the cow remains the *kohen*'s, and the renter may feed it animal fodder of *terumah*. Conversely, a *kohen* who rents a cow from a *Yisrael*, even though he is responsible for feeding it and bears the liability of a renter, may not feed it *terumah*, because it remains the *Yisrael*'s cow. Mere *achrayus* of the renter's level does not confer the right to eat *terumah*.

The *gemara* responds: there are degrees of *achrayus*, and rental is a low degree. A renter is liable for theft and loss, but not for unavoidable accident, not for the animal becoming thinner through natural attrition, and not for a decline in market value. By contrast, the husband who takes in *nichsei tzon barzel* is responsible for the full assessed value regardless of fault, market changes, or any other cause. If the slaves were worth one thousand when they entered the marriage and the market drops so they are now worth five hundred at divorce, he must nonetheless restore the full thousand. This degree of *achrayus* is qualitatively different — it resembles ownership far more than rental does.

### Responsibility vs. Owner…

The *gemara* is therefore pressed: perhaps this extraordinary level of responsibility is tantamount to ownership after all, which would support Rav Ami. But it resists this conclusion. The *baraita* itself offers a parallel case that illuminates the middle ground. If a *Yisrael* assesses a cow from a *kohen* and takes full responsibility to return its assessed value regardless of circumstance, he may not feed it *terumah* — the cow remains the *kohen*'s. Conversely, if a *kohen* does the same from a *Yisrael* — undertakes full assessed-value responsibility — he may feed it *terumah*, even though technically speaking the cow has not been transferred in title. This suggests that a sufficiently high level of *achrayus* can ground the right to eat *terumah* without constituting full legal ownership. Applying this to the dowry slaves: the husband's extraordinary responsibility may suffice for the slaves to eat *terumah*, while the wife technically retains her entitlement to reclaim the objects themselves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף בְּשִׁילְהֵי פִּרְקֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן וְיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְתַנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי. תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי: עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל יוֹצְאִין בְּשֵׁן וָעַיִן. לָאִישׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba and Rav Yosef sat at the conclusion of Rav Naḥman's sermon, and they sat and said: It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda, and it is taught** in another *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami. It is taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami** in the following *baraita*: If a slave's owner strikes him and knocks out his tooth or blinds him in an eye, he is set free. **Slaves of guaranteed investment go** free **at** the loss of **a tooth or an eye** caused **by the husband, but not** at such loss caused **by the wife.** This indicates that they belong to the husband.

Rabbah and Rav Yosef were present at the end of one of Rav Nachman's public lectures and remarked that there exists *baraita* support for both positions. In favor of Rav Ami: *avdei tzon barzel yotzin b'shen v'ayin la'ish aval lo l'isha* — if the husband knocks out the eye or tooth of a *tzon barzel* slave, the slave goes free, because the husband is treated as the slave's master; but if the wife does the same, the slave does not go free, suggesting the husband holds full title. In favor of Rav Yehuda: if the husband wishes to sell the dowry property, he may not. This is a powerful indication that she retains rights over the property that transcend a mere creditor's claim. Indeed, even property that the husband himself contributed to her dowry cannot be sold by him unilaterally. This case came before Rabban Gamliel, who voided the sale and ordered restitution from the buyers.

### Baraita: No Sale Right

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: הַמַּכְנֶסֶת שׁוּם לְבַעְלָהּ, אִם רָצָה הַבַּעַל לִמְכּוֹר — לֹא יִמְכּוֹר. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ הִכְנִיס לָהּ שׁוּם מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, אִם רָצָה הַבַּעַל לִמְכּוֹר — לֹא יִמְכּוֹר. מָכְרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם לְפַרְנָסָה, זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְאָמַר: הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda:** When a woman **brings appraised,** guaranteed **property** into her marriage **to her husband, if the husband wishes to sell** it, **he may not sell** it, as it belongs to her. **And not only** that, but **even** when her husband **brought** property **into** the marriage and added it to **her** dowry as **an appraised,** guaranteed gift **of his own,** even **if the husband wishes to sell** that gift **he may not sell** it. With regard to a case in which **either** the husband or wife unlawfully **sold** this property **for subsistence,** there **was an incident** like **this** that came **before Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and he said:** Although **the husband** executed the sale, **he may repossess** the property **from the purchasers,** as the sale is void.

The force of this *baraita* — that neither party may sell the dowry property without mutual consent, and that Rabban Gamliel enforced this rule against buyers — strongly supports Rav Yehuda's position. If he held full title and merely owed a monetary debt, there would be no legal basis to void his sale.

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַב יְהוּדָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: וְהָתַנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי! אַף עַל גַּב דְּתַנְיָא כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, מִסְתַּבְּרָא טַעְמָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Naḥman said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda. Rava said to Rav Naḥman: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami?** He replied: **Although it is taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami, Rav Yehuda's rationale,** that the wife may take the objects in question **because** they are **assets of her paternal family** and their complete removal from her domain would hurt the family's prestige, **is** more **reasonable.**

Rava rules in accordance with Rav Yehuda: *halacha k'Rav Yehuda* — she is entitled to retrieve the actual property she brought in. The *baraita* concerning the prohibition on sale was evidently persuasive, and Rava adds that the reasoning *mishum shevach beit aviha* — the property of her father's house — supports giving her the right to reclaim what she brought in, even though the *baraita* supporting Rav Ami regarding *shen v'ayin* presented a counterweight. As Tosafot notes, even if the slaves technically belong to her, it is conceivable that the *kohen*'s extensive rights over them — rights sufficient for them to eat *terumah* on his account — could also ground the *shen v'ayin* rule without establishing full title in him. Rava thus dismisses that *baraita* as a conclusive proof for Rav Ami and rules for Rav Yehuda.

### Shroud Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּעַיִּילָה לֵיהּ לְגַבְרַאּ אִיצְטְלָא דְמֵילָתָא בִּכְתוּבְּתַהּ. שְׁכֵיב, שַׁקְלוּהָ יַתְמֵי וּפַרְסוּהָ אַמִּיתָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an incident: **A certain woman brought into** her marriage to her **husband a robe [*itztela*] of fine wool [*meileta*],** which was deemed guaranteed property **by her marriage contract.** Her husband subsequently **died,** and **the orphans took** that robe **and spread it over the corpse** as a shroud.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רָבָא: קַנְיַיהּ מִיתָנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ נָאנַאי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: וְהָאָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַב יְהוּדָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי לָא מוֹדֶה רַב יְהוּדָה דִּמְחוּסַּר גּוּבְיָינָא. וְכֵיוָן דִּמְחוּסַּר גּוּבְיָינָא — בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The dead has acquired it,** as deriving benefit from anything consecrated for the dead is prohibited. **Nanai, son of Rav Yosef, son of Rava, said to Rav Kahana: But didn't Rava say** that **Rav Naḥman said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda?** Accordingly, the woman's robe must be returned. **He said to him: Doesn't Rav Yehuda admit that** the robe **has not yet been collected? And since it has not yet been collected, it remains in his possession,** and his inheritors can render its use as a burial shroud prohibited.

A practical story follows. A woman brought a fine wool cloak into her marriage as part of her *tzon barzel* dowry. The husband died. Before she had an opportunity to act, the orphans — the husband's heirs — took the cloak and used it as a shroud for the deceased. Something designated and used as a shroud for a corpse is *asur b'hana'ah* — forbidden for any benefit.

Rava rules: *kanye misna* — the dead body has, as it were, acquired it. Its status has been permanently transformed. While the wife retains her monetary claim against the estate for the assessed value of the cloak, she loses any right to reclaim this particular object.

### Collection Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, חָמֵץ, וְשִׁחְרוּר — מַפְקִיעִין מִידֵי שִׁעְבּוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that in this regard **Rava** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rava said: Consecration** of property, the prohibition against benefiting from **leavened bread** on Passover, **and** the **manumission** of a slave **release** the property **from a lien.**

This ruling prompted a sharp question from Nemchi, the grandson of Rav Yosef the son of Rava, to Rav Kahana: but do we not rule like Rav Yehuda that she is entitled to retrieve the objects? If so, the cloak was hers. The orphans had no right to use her property as a shroud, and their doing so should not affect her claim.

Rav Kahana responded: *mi lo modei Rabbi Yehuda demchusar guvaina* — even Rav Yehuda concedes that her entitlement requires actual collection. The right to reclaim exists, but it has not yet been exercised. Until she actively retrieves the object, it remains under the husband's — or now the heirs' — control. *Dekeivan demchusar guvaina birshuseih kai* — since it has not yet been collected, it stands in his domain. The heirs are therefore in a position to use it, and their doing so — transforming it into a shroud, rendering it *asur b'hana'ah* — removes her practical rights in this object, even if she is technically owed its value.

This connects to a broader principle that Rava articulates: *hekdesh chametz veshichrur motzi'in miyedei shibbud* — sanctification, *chametz* on Passover, and manumission of a slave are three situations where a change in the object's essential identity dissolves an existing lien or claim. Here, the transformation of the cloak into a *tachrichim* — a burial shroud that is *asur b'hana'ah* — operates analogously to *chametz* becoming prohibited on Passover, severing the wife's rights in the object itself. This illustrates that even where Rav Yehuda's ruling grants her the right to retrieve, that right must be actively exercised; it does not automatically transfer control.

### Two Vessels Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הִכְנִיסָה לוֹ שְׁנֵי כֵלִים בְּאֶלֶף זוּז, וְשָׁבְחוּ וְעָמְדוּ עַל שְׁנֵי אֲלָפִים. אֶחָד נוֹטַלְתּוֹ בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, וְאֶחָד נוֹתֶנֶת דָּמִים וְנוֹטַלְתּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** If the wife **brought** with her **into** the marriage **two belongings** of guaranteed investment worth **one thousand dinars, and they appreciated** until **they stood at two thousand, one** of them **she collects as** payment of **her marriage contract,** as it is now worth her dowry of one thousand dinars. **And** as for the other **one, she pays its monetary** value **and takes it** from her husband **because** it is **an asset of her paternal family.**

Rav Yehuda then presents a concrete scenario that bears on the practical implications of his own ruling. A woman brings two vessels worth a total of one thousand *zuz* into the marriage; by the time of divorce or the husband's death, they are worth two thousand *zuz*. What does she retrieve?

The ruling is: she takes one vessel as part of her *ketubah* entitlement — that is, within the original assessed value — and for the second vessel, she pays the difference in value and retrieves it as well, *mipnei shevach beit aviha*. Even though the vessels have doubled in value, she is entitled to take both back and simply compensate for the increase.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — שֶׁבַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ דִּידַהּ הָוֵי? הָא אַמְרַהּ רַב יְהוּדָה חֲדָא זִימְנָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּמָטְיָא לְמִשְׁקַל בִּכְתוּבְּתַהּ, אֲבָל מִיתָּן דְּמֵי וּמִישְׁקָל — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is he teaching us?** Is it that **assets of her paternal family are hers? Rav Yehuda** already **said this once,** in his previous statement. The Gemara answers: The latter statement was necessary as well, **lest you say** that **this applies** only **where she comes to collect her marriage contract,** which is rightfully hers, **but to give money and take** assets that are worth beyond what her husband owes her, you might say that she may **not** do so, although the property in question is an asset of her paternal family. Rav Yehuda therefore **teaches us** that she may take all of the assets of her paternal family and pay for what they are worth beyond her husband's debt to her.

The *gemara* asks: is this ruling necessary? Has Rav Yehuda not already stated that she is entitled to retrieve the property? The answer is that one might have thought her entitlement to retrieve extends only up to the original assessed value — meaning she could take back property worth up to a thousand, but not beyond that threshold. The ruling teaches that even when the property has appreciated beyond its original value, she may still retrieve all of it by paying the increment. The principle *shevach beit aviha* — the special consideration given to property that she brought from her father's house — extends to allow her to reclaim the full quantity of objects she originally brought in, with the increase in value paid out to the estate.

## Fetus & Slave Terumah

### Ubar Posel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּיסֵּת לְכֹהֵן וּמֵת, וְהִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ עֲבָדֶיהָ בִּתְרוּמָה, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר, שֶׁהָעוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל. וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an Israelite woman who married a priest and he died and left her pregnant, her slaves** of guaranteed investment **may not partake of *teruma*** during her pregnancy, **due to the share of the fetus,** as an inheritor of his father, in the ownership of the slaves. In the opposite case, where **the fetus disqualifies** her from partaking of *teruma*. However, in the current case, the fetus **does not enable** its mother or the slaves **to partake** of *teruma*, despite the fact that it is the child of a priest. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

The *mishnah* now turns from the question of forbidden marriage and its effect on the wife's status to a different dynamic entirely: the role of an unborn fetus in determining whether the slaves of a deceased *kohen* may eat *terumah*. The case is as follows. A *bat Yisrael* marries a *kohen*, the *kohen* dies, and she is left pregnant. What is the status of the slaves?

As a general rule, when a *kohen* dies and leaves a child through his wife, that wife may continue eating *terumah* on account of her child's connection to the priestly lineage. But that applies to a born child. Here, only an unborn fetus remains. The *mishnah* rules: *lo yochlu avadeha biterumah mipnei chelko shel ubar* — the slaves may not eat *terumah* on account of the fetus's portion. That is, we assume the fetus will be a son and will therefore inherit a portion of the estate, including a portion of the slaves. Because he is a partial owner, the slaves cannot eat *terumah* by virtue of him — not yet, since he is unborn.

### Rabbi Yossi's Principle

The *mishnah* encapsulates the rule: *she'ha'ubar posel ve'eino ma'achil* — the fetus invalidates and does not feed. He is in the worst of both positions simultaneously. On the one hand, his partial ownership of the estate — specifically, of the slaves — invalidates them from eating *terumah* through that portion he owns. On the other hand, he is not yet sufficient of a person to enable his mother to eat *terumah*, and cannot cause his own portion of the slaves to eat through him. Were he fully nonexistent in legal terms, the other heirs' portions of the slaves would eat unimpeded. Were he fully born, his mother would eat and all the slaves would eat. In his intermediate state, he creates problems without providing any benefit. This is the position of Rabbi Yossi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מֵאַחַר שֶׁהֵעַדְתָּ לָנוּ עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — אַף בַּת כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן וּמֵת, וְהִנִּיחָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ עֲבָדֶיהָ בִּתְרוּמָה, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him: Since you testified before us about** the case of **an Israelite woman** who was married **to a priest,** in the case of **the daughter of a priest** who was married **to a priest and he died and left her pregnant, her slaves should not partake of *teruma* either, due to the fetus's share.** The same *halakha* should apply whether the woman is an Israelite or the daughter of a priest.

The sages in the *mishnah* respond by pointing out that this logic should apply equally when the mother is a *bat kohen* married to a *kohen*. If the *kohen* dies and she is pregnant, by the same reasoning the slaves should not eat on account of the fetus's portion. Rabbi Yossi's response, and whether the sages are genuinely disagreeing or merely drawing out the implications, remains to be developed in the *gemara*.

### Reason A: Non-Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר עוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי זָרָה — זָר הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא: יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָלוּד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars: Is **the reason for** the ruling of **Rabbi Yosei because he holds that a fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest,** as it is considered part of its mother's body and it becomes a priest only upon birth, and therefore the slaves in which it owns a share will be allowed to eat *teruma* only at that stage? **Or,** is Rabbi Yosei **perhaps** of the opinion that only **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake,** although it is considered a priest?

The *gemara* raises the question of what exactly underlies Rabbi Yossi's ruling. Two possible reasons are entertained, and the difference between them has a practical *nafka mina*. The first possibility: *ubar b'mei zara zar hu* — an unborn fetus takes on the legal identity of his mother. Before birth, the fetus is considered an extension of his mother — *ubar yerech imo*. Since his mother in the *mishnah*'s case is a *bat Yisrael*, a non-*kohen*, the fetus is also considered a non-*kohen*. If a partial owner of the slaves is a non-*kohen*, those slaves cannot eat *terumah* through that portion, because a *zar* — a non-priest — cannot confer the right to eat *terumah*.

### Reason B: Yalud Maachil

The second possibility: *yalud machil she'ein yalud eino machil* — only a born person can enable others to eat *terumah* through him; an unborn person cannot. On this reading, the fetus's *kohen* status is not in question — once born, he will be a *kohen* — but his being unborn disqualifies him from serving as the basis for others' eating of *terumah*. The incapacity is not about identity but about actualized legal personhood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְעוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי כֹהֶנֶת, מַאי? אָמַר רַבָּה, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: דְּקָסָבַר עוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי זָרָה זָר הוּא. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵין יָלוּד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the **practical difference** between the two possible reasons? It is the case of **a fetus in the womb of the priestess,** the daughter of a priest. If Rabbi Yosei's rationale is that the fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest, that is not the case here, and therefore the slaves should partake of *teruma*. **What** is the *halakha* in this case? **Rabba said** that **this is Rabbi Yosei's reasoning: He holds** that **a fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest.** **Rav Yosef said:** His rationale is that only **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake.**

The *nafka mina* between these two reasons is stark: what if the mother is a *bat kohen*? If the reason is *ubar b'mei zara zar hu* — that the fetus takes on his mother's non-*kohen* identity — then when his mother is a *bat kohen*, that concern disappears. The fetus's mother is a *kohen*, and therefore the fetus is not a *zar*. In that case, the slaves would eat. But if the reason is *yalud machil she'ein yalud eino machil*, it makes no difference whether his mother is a *bat kohen* or a *bat Yisrael* — the problem is solely that he has not yet been born.

### Sages: No Fetus Share

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מֵאַחַר שֶׁהֵעַדְתָּ לָנוּ עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן, בַּת כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן מַהוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְזוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Yosef's opinion from a *baraita* that continues the last clause of the mishna: The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Yosei: Since you testified before us about** the case of **an Israelite woman** married **to a priest,** in the case of **the daughter of a priest** married **to a priest, what is** the *halakha*? **He said to them:** With regard to the former case, **I heard** from my teachers that the slaves do not partake of *teruma*, **but** with regard to **this** one, **I did not hear** such a thing.

Rabbah identifies reason A as Rabbi Yossi's actual position: *ubar b'mei zara zar hu* — the fetus, while still in a non-priestly mother, takes her identity and is therefore a *zar* whose portion of the slaves cannot eat *terumah*. Rav Yosef identifies reason B: *yalud machil she'ein yalud eino machil*, independent of the mother's status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא: עוּבָּר בִּמְעֵי זָרָה זָר הוּא — הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ: זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְזוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵין יָלוּד — אֵין מַאֲכִיל, מַאי זוֹ שָׁמַעְתִּי וְזוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי? אִיהִי הִיא קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that Rabbi Yosei's reasoning is that **a fetus in the womb of a non-priest is a non-priest, this is** the reason **that he said to them: This** case **I heard but this** case **I did not hear.** There is a logical distinction between the two cases, as in the latter case the fetus is not in the womb of a non-priest. **However, if you say** that his rationale is that only **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake, what** does he mean by saying: **This** case **I heard but this** case **I did not hear? It is the same** case with regard to this principle. The Gemara concludes: This is **a difficult** objection.

The *gemara* then presents the disagreement of the sages and unpacks it. The sages of the *mishnah* — the *chachamim* who respond to Rabbi Yossi — hold the following position. If there are born children of the deceased *kohen*, they eat *terumah* by virtue of those children. If there are no children, others eat *terumah* by virtue of the *kohen*'s brothers or by virtue of any other living member of the priestly family. The presence of the unborn fetus does not alter these arrangements.

### Two Assumptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּנִים — אוֹכְלִים מִשּׁוּם בָּנִים. אֵין לוֹ בָּנִים — אוֹכְלִים מִשּׁוּם אַחִים. אֵין לוֹ אַחִים — אוֹכְלִים מִשּׁוּם מִשְׁפָּחָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: This is the statement of Rabbi Yosei. However, the Rabbis say** that if the dead husband **has children,** the slaves **partake** of *teruma* **due to** the **children,** as they inherit the slaves. If **he does not have children, they partake** of *teruma* **due to** his **brothers,** who inherit his property. If **he does not have brothers** either, **they partake due to** the **entire family,** which inherits his property. The fetus does not disqualify them, as it does not yet own its share of the inheritance.

What drives the sages' disagreement? Rabbi Yossi's ruling rests on two assumptions. Assumption one: the fetus already acquires a portion of the estate, even before birth. Assumption two: since he is unborn, no one may eat *terumah* by virtue of that portion. The *chachamim* reject assumption one outright. Until the fetus is born, he has no title to the estate whatsoever. He is not a legal heir in the present moment. The other relatives — sons, brothers, or extended family — are the current heirs, and the slaves eat *terumah* through them without any interference from an unborn fetus who holds no current legal claim.

It is worth noting the important implication: even the *chachamim* do not necessarily deny that once born the child will inherit. Their claim is merely temporal — until birth, he holds nothing.

### Shmuel's Stance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זוֹ״ וְלָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. הָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב חָנָא בַּגְדָּתָאָה: פּוֹק אַיְיתִי לִי בֵּי עַשְׂרָה, דְּאֵימָא לָךְ בְּאַנְפַּיְיהוּ: הַמְזַכֶּה לְעוּבָּר — קָנָה. אֶלָּא: ״זוֹ״ וּסְבִירָא לֵיהּ. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — דִּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: By saying that **this** is only Rabbi Yosei's stance, Shmuel seemingly indicates that **he** himself **does not maintain** that opinion. However, **Shmuel said to Rav Ḥana of Baghdad: Go and bring me an assembly of ten** men **and I will say to you before them** a *halakha* that I seek to disseminate: **One who transfers ownership** of an object **to a fetus,** the fetus **acquires** it. Consequently, according to Shmuel, a fetus can own property, which is the premise of Rabbi Yosei's stance that a fetus shares the inheritance even before he is born. The Gemara answers: **Rather,** although Shmuel said that **this** is only Rabbi Yosei's stance, **he holds** likewise. **What is** Shmuel **teaching us** by saying so? He is teaching us **that the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Yosei.**

Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Shmuel: *zo divrei Rabbi Yossi* — this, the ruling of the *mishnah*, is the position of Rabbi Yossi. The phrasing suggests that Shmuel is presenting Rabbi Yossi's ruling as a particular opinion, implying he himself may not hold it. This leads the *gemara* to question whether Shmuel actually rejects Rabbi Yossi's ruling.

The *gemara* challenges this impression: did Shmuel not say to Rav Chana Bagdatna — *puk aytei li bei asara ve-eima lachu be-anpaihu ha-mezakeh le-ubar kanah* — go gather me ten people, and I will declare publicly before them that one who transfers an asset to a fetus acquires it on the fetus's behalf? If Shmuel holds that a fetus can receive a transfer of title through someone acting on his behalf, then presumably he also holds that a fetus inherits from his father — which is precisely assumption one of Rabbi Yossi. How then can Shmuel be understood as rejecting Rabbi Yossi?

### Fetus Can Inherit

The *gemara* resolves this: *ela zu ve-sevira leh* — Shmuel is not dismissing Rabbi Yossi's ruling. He agrees with it, and by saying "this is the position of Rabbi Yossi," he is not distancing himself but rather identifying and affirming it. He is informing his audience that there is a competing opinion — that of the *chachamim* who deny assumption one — of which they should be aware. But Shmuel himself sides with Rabbi Yossi, consistent with his broader principle that a fetus can acquire title.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי פְּלִיגִי? מֵתִיב רַבִּי זַכַּאי: זוֹ עֵדוּת הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִפִּי שְׁמַעְיָה וְאַבְטַלְיוֹן, וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִי קָתָנֵי ״וְקִבְּלוּ״? ״וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ״ קָתָנֵי, דְּמִסְתַּבַּר טַעְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But do they** really **disagree? Rabbi Zakkai raised an objection** to this statement from a *baraita*: **This** was **a testimony** that **Rabbi Yosei testified** that he heard **from the mouths of Shemaya and Avtalyon, and** the Rabbis **acknowledged** his testimony. Apparently, they accepted his opinion. **Rav Ashi said: Does** that *baraita* **state: And** the Rabbis **accepted** his testimony? **It states: And they acknowledged** his testimony, which indicates **that his opinion is reasonable.** However, they did not accept his ruling.

The *gemara* then asks: *mahu mashma lan* — what is the novelty in Shmuel's statement? The answer is that Shmuel is letting us know that the *chachamim* do in fact disagree with Rabbi Yossi, even though their formulation in the *mishnah* may have sounded like they were merely drawing out his logic rather than disputing him.

A further complication is introduced. Rabbi Zakai reports that Rabbi Yossi gave this testimony in the name of Shemaya and Avtalyon, and the sages conceded to him — *ve-hodu lo*. Does this mean the ruling achieved consensus? Rav Ashi responds: *mi katani ve-kibbelu* — it does not say they accepted it. *Ve-hodu lo katani* — they conceded, meaning *de-mistabar tameh* — they acknowledged the reasoning was cogent, but this falls short of a formal ruling that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yossi. The tradition from Shemaya and Avtalyon confers weight to Rabbi Yossi's position, but does not settle the dispute.

### All Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אֵין אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים, וְהִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג אוֹכְלִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִיא אוֹכֶלֶת, עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר. שֶׁהָעוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the priest who was married to an Israelite woman and died **left children,** both the slaves of usufruct property **and** the slaves of guaranteed investment **may partake** of *teruma*. The slaves of guaranteed investment are owned by the children, who are priests, and the slaves of usufruct property are owned by the woman, who partakes of *teruma* due to her children. If **he left** his wife **pregnant** and did not leave children, both **these** slaves **and those** slaves **may not partake** of *teruma*. If **he left children and left her pregnant,** the **slaves of usufruct** property who belong to her **partake** of *teruma* **just as she partakes** due to her children. However, the **slaves of guaranteed investment,** who are inherited by the children, **may not partake, due to the fetus's share,** as it too inherits them, **as a fetus can disqualify** one from partaking of *teruma* **but it cannot not enable** one **to partake.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

A *baraita* now plays out the full range of scenarios according to Rabbi Yossi's position. If the *kohen* dies leaving sons — and no pregnancy — then *elu va'elu ochlin*: all the slaves eat. The *nichsei tzon barzel* slaves eat because they form part of the estate now in the sons' possession. The *nichsei milug* slaves eat because, with living children from the marriage, the wife continues to eat *terumah* and therefore her slaves eat through her. If the *kohen* dies leaving his wife pregnant and no other children — *elu va'elu ein ochlin*: none of the slaves eat. She cannot eat on account of an unborn child, and both categories of slaves are blocked.

If he leaves both sons and a pregnant wife — a mixed case — then *avdei melog ochlin kederech shehi ochelet*: the *milug* slaves eat, because the presence of born children entitles the wife to eat, and therefore her slaves eat through her. But *avdei tzon barzel lo yochlu mipnei chelko shel ubar*: the estate's slaves cannot eat because the fetus holds a partial ownership in them, and that partial ownership — *she'ha'ubar posel ve'eino ma'achil* — invalidates their eating without itself enabling them to eat through the fetus. The born sons' portions would normally suffice, but the fetus's portion creates an insoluble impediment for that share of the slaves.

### R. Yishmael Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: הַבַּת — מַאֲכֶלֶת, הַבֵּן — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in the name of his father:** If the priest left behind **a daughter,** she **enables** the slaves **to partake** of *teruma*; however, **a son does not enable** them **to partake.**

Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yossi adds a ruling in his father's name: *ha'bat ma'achelet, ha'ben eino ma'achil* — a daughter enables the mother to eat, but a son does not. The precise meaning of this statement is not immediately clear and the *gemara* will unpack it in subsequent discussion.

### R. Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים יֹאכְלוּ כּוּלָּן, נְקֵבוֹת לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ, שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם הַבֵּן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** If among the priest's children there are **males,** the slaves **partake** of *teruma*. But if they are **all females, they do not partake, lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters do not** receive **any** of the inheritance **where** there is **a son.** The male fetus would be the sole inheritor, and it does not enable slaves to partake of *teruma*.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai adds yet another nuance: *zecharim yochlu kulam, nekevot lo yochlu* — all males eat, but females do not. His reasoning appears to hinge on a concern about the possibility of finding a male fetus — *shema yimtza ubar zachar* — but the full elaboration of his position, along with the full analysis of Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yossi's ruling, will be taken up in the continuation of the *gemara*.
