# Berakhot 20b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Women & Kiddush

### Women obligated Kiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת בְּקִדּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם דְּבַר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: Women are obligated to** recite the sanctification of the Shabbat day [***kiddush*]by Torah law.**

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rav Adda bar Ahava: *nashim chayavot bekiddush hayom d'oraita* — women are obligated in *kiddush* on *Shabbat* biblically. Our form of *kiddush* is recited over a cup of wine and includes the declaration of *mekadesh hashabbat*, but the wine itself is generally understood to be only *miderabbanan*. At its core, however, there is a biblical *mitzvah* of *zachor et yom hashabbat lekadesho* — to declare the sanctity of *Shabbat*. And that biblical obligation, Rav Adda bar Ahava maintains, applies to women as well.

### Time-bound exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הוּא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Why?** *Kiddush* is a **time-bound, positive mitzva, and** the halakhic principle is: **Women are exempt from any time-bound, positive mitzva.** The Gemara replies: **Abaye said:** Indeed, women are obligated to recite *kiddush* by **rabbinic,** but not by Torah **law.**

The immediate challenge is obvious: *kiddush* is a *mitzvat aseh shehazman grama* — a positive time-bound commandment — and the principle is that women are exempt from all such commandments. Abaye attempts to resolve this by saying the obligation is only rabbinic (*miderabbanan*), not biblical. Rava, however, objects on two grounds. First, the statement explicitly says *d'oraita*. Second, and more fundamentally, if we are going to say the rabbis independently obligated women in *kiddush* even though it is time-bound, then we should have obligated them in every time-bound *mitzvah* — which we clearly have not done. There must therefore be something uniquely compelling about *kiddush*. The question is what.

### Zachor v'Shamor link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא ״דְּבַר תּוֹרָה״ קָאָמַר. וְעוֹד, כׇּל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה נְחַיְּיבִינְהוּ מִדְּרַבָּנַן! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״זָכוֹר וְשָׁמוֹר״ — כׇּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בִּשְׁמִירָה יֶשְׁנוֹ בִּזְכִירָה. וְהָנֵי נְשֵׁי הוֹאִיל וְאִיתַנְהוּ בִּשְׁמִירָה, אִיתַנְהוּ בִּזְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: There are two refutations to your explanation. First, Rav Adda bar Ahava said that women are obligated to recite *kiddush* **by Torah law, and, furthermore,** the very explanation is difficult to understand. If the Sages do indeed institute ordinances in these circumstances, **let us obligate them** to fulfill **all** time-bound, **positive mitzvot by rabbinic law.** **Rather, Rava said:** This has a unique explanation. In the Ten Commandments in the book of Exodus, **the verse said: "Remember** Shabbat and sanctify it" (Exodus 20:8), while in the book of Deuteronomy it is said: **"Observe** Shabbat and sanctify it" (Deuteronomy 5:12). From these two variants we can deduce that **anyone included in** the obligation to **observe** Shabbat by avoiding its desecration, **is** also **included in** the mitzva to **remember** Shabbat by reciting *kiddush*. **Since these women are included in** the mitzva **to observe** Shabbat, as there is no distinction between men and women in the obligation to observe prohibitions in general and to refrain from the desecration of Shabbat in particular, so too **are they included in** the mitzva of **remembering** Shabbat.

Rava answers by citing the verse: *zachor ve-shamor* — remember and observe. These two words, appearing in the two versions of the *Aseret Hadiberot*, were spoken simultaneously (*b'dibbur echad ne'emru*), establishing an intrinsic link between *shmirah* (the negative prohibition of *Shabbat*) and *zkhirah* (the positive declaration of *Shabbat*). The principle Rava derives is: *kol she-yeshno bi-shmirah yeshno bi-zkirah* — anyone bound by the obligation to observe *Shabbat*, meaning to refrain from *melacha*, is equally bound in the obligation of *zikira*, meaning *kiddush*. Since women are unquestionably obligated in *shmirat Shabbat*, they are correspondingly obligated in *kiddush*. The practical implication is significant: because men and women share an equal biblical obligation in *kiddush*, a woman can serve as *motzi* for a man. Any objections that have been raised in later authorities are secondary considerations — matters of *zila milta* and the like — but at the level of principle, the equal obligation is accepted.

### Drasha mechanics

One might ask what kind of *drasha* this is. It is not a *kal v'chomer*, nor quite a *gzeira shava* in the technical sense, yet it functions similarly — treating the two usages of the same concept in parallel texts as definitionally linked. The nature of the derivation is that because *zachor* and *shamor* were said simultaneously, their subjects are coextensive: whoever falls within one falls within the other.

A parallel question arises regarding *havdalah*, which many authorities understand to also be biblical, linked to the same *zkhirah* obligation — declaring the boundaries of *Shabbat* both at its beginning and at its end. On this analysis, women would be equally obligated in *havdalah* as in *kiddush*, and could certainly perform it for themselves and potentially serve as *motzi* for a man. There are, however, certain *rishonim* and *poskim* who hold that women's obligation in *havdalah* is only *miderabbanan*, and some go so far as to maintain that a woman cannot even perform *havdalah* for herself — she must hear it from a man. This is a striking position, since it is exceedingly rare in *halacha* for someone to be unable to perform even for themselves a *mitzvah* from which they are partially or fully exempt. The straightforward reading of the relevant *sugyot*, and the position of most *poskim*, is that women are equally obligated in *havdalah* and may at minimum perform it for themselves.

## Birkat Hamazon status

### Biblical vs Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: נָשִׁים בְּבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן, דְאוֹרָיְיתָא אוֹ דְּרַבָּנַן? לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְאַפּוֹקֵי רַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, אָתֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וּמַפֵּיק דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּרַבָּנַן, הָוֵי ״שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּיָּיב בַּדָּבָר״, וְכׇל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּיָּיב בַּדָּבָר אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים יְדֵי חוֹבָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava:** We learned in the mishna that **women** are obligated in the mitzva of **Grace after Meals.** However, are they obligated **by Torah law** or merely **by rabbinic law? What difference does it make** whether it is by Torah or rabbinic law? The difference is regarding her ability **to fulfill the obligation of others** when reciting the blessing on their behalf. **Granted, if you say that** their obligation **is by Torah law,** one whose obligation **is by Torah law can come and fulfill the obligation** of others who are obligated **by Torah law. However, if you say** that their obligation is **by rabbinic law,** then from the perspective of Torah law, women **are** considered to be **one who is not obligated, and** the general principle is that **one who is not obligated** to fulfill a particular mitzva **cannot fulfill the obligations of the many** in that mitzva.

The *gemara* now turns to *birkat hamazon*. The *mishna* has already stated that women are obligated, and we have noted that *benching* is not time-bound. Yet Ravina poses a sharper question to Rava: *nashim b'Birkat Hamazon d'oraita o d'rabbanan* — is women's obligation in *birkat hamazon* biblical or merely rabbinic? The *nafka mina* — the practical difference — is whether a woman can serve as *motzi* for others. If her obligation is biblical, then *d'oraita* can discharge *d'oraita*. If it is only rabbinic, then she is in the category of *she'eino mechuyav b'davar*, one not fully obligated, and cannot discharge the obligation of those who are biblically bound.

### Tosefta teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ בֵּן מְבָרֵךְ לְאָבִיו וְעֶבֶד מְבָרֵךְ לְרַבּוֹ וְאִשָּׁה מְבָרֶכֶת לְבַעֲלָהּ, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: תָּבֹא מְאֵרָה לְאָדָם שֶׁאִשְׁתּוֹ וּבָנָיו מְבָרְכִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** from what was taught in a *baraita*: **Actually they said** that **a son may recite a blessing** on behalf of **his father, and a slave may recite a blessing** on behalf of **his master, and a woman may recite a blessing** on behalf of **her husband, but the Sages said: May a curse come to a man** who, due to his ignorance, requires **his wife and children to recite a blessing on his behalf.**

The *gemara* brings a *tosefta* from *Sukkah* as potential evidence. The *tosefta* states: *ben mevarech l'aviv, ve'eved mevarech l'rabbo, v'ishah mevareches l'baalah* — a son can perform *birkat hamazon* for his father, a slave for his master, a woman for her husband. This would seem to indicate that all three can discharge the obligation of the men, which in turn suggests their own obligation is biblical and equal. However, the *tosefta* immediately adds that even though this is technically true in principle, *tavo me'eirah l'adam she'ishah u'vanav mevarchin lo* — a curse should befall a man whose wife and children must bench on his behalf — since this could only reflect that the man was insufficiently learned or capable. The cultural context is important here: this was a world in which egalitarianism was not a governing value, and in which the general expectation was that men would be more steeped in learning and practice. In such a context, dependence on one's wife for *birkat hamazon* was understood as a social and personal embarrassment.

### Obligation levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אָתֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וּמַפֵּיק דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּרַבָּנַן, אָתֵי דְּרַבָּנַן וּמַפֵּיק דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא?! וּלְטַעְמָיךְ קָטָן בַּר חִיּוּבָא הוּא? אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל שִׁיעוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן, דְּאָתֵי דְּרַבָּנַן וּמַפֵּיק דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** From here we may infer: **Granted, if you say that** their obligation **is by Torah law,** one whose obligation **is by Torah law can come and fulfill the obligation** of others who are obligated **by Torah law. However, if you say** that their obligation is **by rabbinic law,** can one who is obligated **by rabbinic law, come and fulfill the obligation** of one whose obligation is **by Torah law?** The Gemara challenges this proof: **And according to your reasoning,** is **a minor obligated** by Torah law to perform mitzvot? Everyone agrees that a minor is exempt by Torah law, yet here the *baraita* said that he may recite a blessing on behalf of his father. There must be another way to explain the *baraita*. **With what we are dealing here? With a case where** his father **ate** a quantity of food that did not satisfy his hunger, a **measure** for which one is only obligated **by rabbinic law** to recite Grace after Meals. In that case, one whose obligation **is by rabbinic law can come and fulfill the obligation** of another whose obligation **is by rabbinic law.**

The *gemara* tries to read the *tosefta* as proof that women's obligation is biblical: *bishlama de'oraita, ati de'oraita umapik de'oraita* — it makes sense that a woman could serve as *motzi* her husband only if both share the same biblical obligation. But if her obligation were merely rabbinic, how could *d'rabbanan* discharge *d'oraita*?

The *gemara* immediately deflects this argument. The *tosefta* also lists a minor (*katan*) as someone who can bench for his father. But a minor is not biblically obligated in *birkat hamazon* — so how can he discharge his father's obligation? Clearly the *tosefta* is not making a claim about equal levels of obligation. The *gemara* resolves this: *kegon she'achal shiur derabbanan* — the case is where the father ate only a rabbinic-sized amount, meaning less than the quantity of *saveta*, true satiety, that triggers a biblical obligation. The Torah says *ve'achalta ve-savata u'veirachta* — only when one is truly sated is the obligation biblical. When one eats only a *ke'zayit* or a *ke'beitzah*, the obligation is rabbinic. So the father in this case is a biblical-level person who ate only a rabbinic-sized amount, making his current obligation only rabbinic. And a *d'rabbanan* can discharge a *d'rabbanan*, even if the two parties reach that level of obligation through different routes.

This resolution dissolves the proof from the *tosefta*: the case may be entirely within the realm of rabbinic obligation on both sides, and therefore we cannot infer anything about women's biblical status from it. It also opens a broader discussion in the *rishonim* about levels of obligation: what happens when someone is a "double *derabbanan*" — doubly distanced from biblical obligation — such as a minor performing *megillah* for an adult? The minor's obligation is rabbinic because of his age; the adult's obligation in *megillah* is also rabbinic; but are these the same level of *derabbanan*, or is the minor's a *derabbanan* of a *derabbanan*? These questions ramify in later contexts.

It is also worth noting that the *tosefta*'s phrasing — *ben mevarech*, *ve'eved mevarech*, *v'ishah mevareches* — may not necessarily imply *motzi* in the fullest sense. One could read *mevarech* as the son or wife leading the *birkat hamazon* aloud while the others repeat after, rather than truly discharging their obligation on their behalf. On this reading, the *tosefta* would not be addressing *yeitziah* at all, and the whole proof collapses from a different direction. In any case, the *gemara* concludes that the question of whether women's obligation in *birkat hamazon* is biblical or rabbinic remains unresolved.

### Rashi's approach

This raises the underlying question: given that *birkat hamazon* is not time-bound, why would women's obligation be less than men's in the first place? The *mishna* states simply that women are obligated — why should that be anything other than fully biblical?

Rashi explains that women may be excluded from the biblical obligation because of the content of *birkat hamazon* itself. The Torah's formulation is *ve'achalta ve-savata u'veirachta et Hashem Elohecha al ha'aretz hatovah asher natan lach* — you shall bless God for the good land that He has given you. The blessing is not merely a response to eating food; it is an expression of gratitude for the Land of Israel. The second *bracha* of *birkat hamazon* — *al ha'aretz ve'al hamazon* — has this as its core. And the Land of Israel, in biblical law, is apportioned through inheritance — a domain from which women are generally excluded as direct heirs. A woman never inherits the land directly as a primary heir; at most she might inherit in the absence of male heirs. Therefore Rashi suggests that the verse's phrase *asher natan lach* — which He gave *you* — does not apply to women in the same way, and their obligation may therefore be only rabbinic.

### Tosafot's view

Tosafot rejects Rashi's approach. If the concern is about who receives the land, then we should also question the obligation of *kohanim*, *levi'im*, *geirim*, and others who do not hold portions of the land. Collectively, the Jewish people received the land, and that collective relationship is what generates the obligation — not one's individual *nachalah*. Rashi's distinction, Tosafot argues, does not hold.

Tosafot instead points to a later *gemara* which states that if one omits the phrases *al brischa shechatamta bivsareinu* and *al torascha shelimaditanu* from *birkat hamazon*, one has not fulfilled the obligation (*lo yatza*). Tosafot takes this *gemara* literally — these phrases are part of the biblical obligation of *birkat hamazon*. Women who do not have *bris milah* in the literal sense, and who are not obligated in *talmud Torah*, are therefore missing essential elements of the biblical text, and on that basis their biblical obligation may not fully apply. Tosafot acknowledges the difficulty: these themes do not appear in the Torah's own formulation of *birkat hamazon*, so it is hard to understand how they can be part of the biblical obligation. But this is Tosafot's attempt to explain why the *gemara* would entertain the possibility of a reduced obligation for women.

### Pshat conclusion

Both Rashi and Tosafot struggle to explain the *gemara*'s *hava amina*. The simple *pshat* of the *mishna* — that women are obligated in *birkat hamazon* — would suggest their obligation is fully biblical, identical to men's. Given that the underlying question is left open by the *gemara*, and given that the *hava amina* behind a lesser obligation is difficult to ground clearly, the straightforward reading remains that women's obligation in *birkat hamazon* is equal to men's.

The question of *zimun* is a separate matter. *Zimun* is a distinct rabbinic institution — the formal introduction to *birkat hamazon* when three or more have eaten together. Whether women participate in *zimun* with men, and under what conditions, involves additional considerations that go beyond the basic question of whether a woman can discharge a man's obligation in *benching* itself. That discussion belongs to a later *perek* and will be addressed in its proper place.

## Benching & Divine favor

### Angels' challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְרַשׁ רַב עַוִּירָא, זִמְנִין אָמַר לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי וְזִמְנִין אָמַר לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי: אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, כָּתוּב בְּתוֹרָתֶךָ ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִשָּׂא פָנִים וְלֹא יִקַּח שֹׁחַד״, וַהֲלֹא אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא פָּנִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״יִשָּׂא ה׳ פָּנָיו אֵלֶיךָ״?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Avira taught, sometimes he said it in the name** of **Rabbi Ami, and sometimes he said it in the name** of **Rabbi Asi: The ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, in Your Torah it is written:** "The great, mighty and awesome God **who favors no one and takes no bribe"** (Deuteronomy 10:17), **yet You,** nevertheless, **show favor to Israel, as it is written: "The Lord shall show favor to you** and give you peace" (Numbers 6:26).

Before moving on, the *gemara* closes with a *midrash* attributed to Rav Avira — sometimes in the name of Rav Ammi, sometimes in the name of Rav Assi. The *malachei hasharet*, the heavenly angels, address God with a challenge: *kasuv b'torascha asher lo yissa panim v'lo yikach shochad* — it is written in Your Torah that You do not show favor and do not accept bribes. Yet You show special favor to Israel, as it is written *yissa Hashem panav eilecha* — God will lift His countenance to you. How can this be? Tosafot notes that the *pasuk* about God lifting His countenance does not in *pshat* mean favoritism over others — it simply describes a warm and close relationship. Nevertheless, the *midrash* takes up the apparent tension.

### Divine reciprocity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: וְכִי לֹא אֶשָּׂא פָּנִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁכָּתַבְתִּי לָהֶם בַּתּוֹרָה ״וְאָכַלְתָּ וְשָׂבָעְתָּ וּבֵרַכְתָּ אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, וְהֵם מְדַקְדְּקִים [עַל] עַצְמָם עַד כְּזַיִת וְעַד כְּבֵיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He replied to them: And how can I not show favor to Israel, as I wrote for them in the Torah: "And you shall eat and be satisfied, and bless the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 8:10), meaning that there is no obligation to bless the Lord until one is satiated; **yet they are exacting with themselves** to recite Grace after Meals even if they have eaten **as much as an olive-bulk or an egg-bulk.**

God responds: *v'chi lo essa panim l'Yisrael* — shall I not show them favor? For I wrote in the Torah *ve'achalta ve-savata u'veirachta* — that the obligation to bench is only when one is truly sated. And yet *ve'hem medakdekin al atzman ad ke'zayit ve'ad ke'beitzah* — they are so careful upon themselves that they obligate themselves rabbinically even for as little as an olive-sized or egg-sized amount of food. Because Israel does more than is strictly demanded of them — going beyond the letter of biblical law in their punctiliousness — God in turn acts toward them beyond the strict *shurat hadin*, the line of justice. There is a reciprocity here: Israel's self-imposed stringency in *benching* is met with divine favor that exceeds what strict judgment would require.

## Ba'al Keri rules

### Ba'al Keri practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּעַל קֶרִי — מְהַרְהֵר בְּלִבּוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ, לֹא לְפָנֶיהָ וְלֹא לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. וְעַל הַמָּזוֹן מְבָרֵךְ לְאַחֲרָיו וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ לְפָנָיו. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְבָרֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the time for the recitation of *Shema* arrived and **one** is impure due to a **seminal emission,** he may **contemplate** *Shema* **in his heart, but neither recites the blessings preceding** *Shema*, **nor the blessings following it. Over food** which, after partaking, one is obligated by Torah law to recite a blessing, **one recites a blessing afterward, but one does not recite a blessing beforehand,** because the blessing recited prior to eating is a requirement by rabbinic law. **And** in all of these instances **Rabbi Yehuda says: He recites a blessing beforehand and thereafter** in both the case of *Shema* and in the case of food.

The *gemara* now shifts abruptly to a different category of person: the *ba'al keri*, a man who has experienced a seminal emission and remains in a state of *tumah* until he immerses in a *mikvah*. The *mishna* states: *ba'al keri meharher belibo ve'eino mevarech lo lefanav ve'lo le'acharav* — a *ba'al keri* thinks the *shema* in his heart, but he does not recite the *brachos* before or after it. Conversely, *ve'al hamazon mevarech le'acharav ve'eino mevarech lefanav* — for *benching*, he recites the blessing after the meal (the biblical obligation), but not the blessing before eating (which is only rabbinic).

The operative principle at work in the *mishna* is that a *ba'al keri* fulfills his biblical obligations but not his rabbinic ones. The *bracha* of *shema* itself — the core *pasuk* — is biblical, so he does it, though only by thinking it. The surrounding *brachos* of *kriat shema* are rabbinic, so he omits them. The *birkat hamazon* after eating is biblical, so he recites it. The *bracha* over bread before eating is rabbinic, so he omits it.

### Rabbi Yehuda disagrees

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *mevarech lefanav u'le'acharav* — he may recite the blessings both before and after. Rabbi Yehuda is not troubled by the *ba'al keri*'s status when it comes to *brachos*.

### Thought vs. speech

Before the *gemara* addresses the underlying reason for these restrictions, a striking asymmetry in the *mishna* itself demands attention. The *shema* is performed through *hirhur* — silent thought. The *birkat hamazon* afterward, by contrast, appears to be recited aloud. Why the difference?

Some *rishonim* — the *talmidei Rabbeinu Yona* — resolve this by reading the *mishna* uniformly: even when it says the *ba'al keri* "says" *birkat hamazon*, it means he thinks it, just as with *shema*. Both are done only in the mind.

A second approach draws on the particular character of *shema*. Throughout the earlier discussions in *Berakhot*, there is a strong emphasis on *kavana* in *shema* — on the orientation of the heart. Some earlier opinions even framed *shema* primarily as a *mitzvat kavana* rather than a *mitzvat kriya*. On this reading, the threshold for fulfilling *shema* may be lower than for *birkat hamazon* — perhaps one can be *yotzei shema* through thought alone in a way that does not apply to *benching*.

A third approach — raised by a student — inverts the analysis: perhaps the issue is not about the lower threshold for *shema* but about the higher *problem* of articulating it. *Shema* consists of direct words of *Torah* — words that, when spoken aloud, create a more acute tension with the *ba'al keri*'s state. *Birkat hamazon*, by contrast, is a rabbinic formulation, not words of *Torah* in the same direct sense. Therefore the *gemara* may be telling us not that thinking *shema* is sufficient, but that speaking *shema* is specifically more problematic, while speaking *birkat hamazon* — when the biblical obligation demands it — is more permissible. This is an insightful distinction that the *gemara* will partially address in what follows.

## Hirhur k'dibbur debate

### Does thinking = speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת הִרְהוּר כְּדִבּוּר דָּמֵי. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ לָאו כְּדִבּוּר דָּמֵי — לָמָּה מְהַרְהֵר?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: That is to say,** from the mishna that **contemplation is tantamount to speech. As if it would enter your mind** that **it is not tantamount to speech,** then **why** does one who is impure because of a seminal emission **contemplate?** It must be that it is tantamount to speech.

Ravina now sharpens the conceptual question latent in the *mishna*: *zot omeret hirhur kedibbur dami* — the fact that the *mishna* instructs the *ba'al keri* to think the *shema* tells us that thinking counts as speech. For if thinking did not count as speech — if *hirhur lav kedibbur dami* — why would the *mishna* instruct him to think *shema* at all? If thinking accomplishes nothing, there would be no point.

### Hirhur k'dibbur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי — הִרְהוּר כְּדִבּוּר דָּמֵי, יוֹצִיא בִּשְׂפָתָיו?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **But what** are you saying, that **contemplation is tantamount to speech?** Then, if one who is impure because of a seminal emission is permitted to contemplate, why does he not **utter** the words **with his lips?**

The *gemara* entertains this first position: *hirhur kedibbur dami* — thinking is halachically equivalent to speech. But then an obvious objection arises: if thinking counts as speech, then the *ba'al keri* should simply say *shema* aloud — *yotzi bisftav* — since the result is the same. Why go through the intermediate step of thinking?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאַשְׁכְּחַן בְּסִינַי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **As we found at** Mount **Sinai.**

The *gemara* answers: *kede'atan beSinai* — it is like the situation at Sinai. What this means requires unpacking. The reason a *ba'al keri* cannot recite *shema* or *tefilla* or *birkat hamazon* aloud is rooted in the experience of *Maamad Har Sinai*. Before receiving the Torah, the men were commanded *al tigshu el isha* — do not approach a woman. The *gemara* understands this not merely as a prohibition against sexual relations, but as relating to the state of seminal emission. Indeed, the *gemara* points out that the three-day requirement was calculated with the women in mind as well: semen might still be present in a woman's body for up to three days after relations. So the concern at *Har Sinai* was not about the act of intimacy per se — intimacy with one's wife is a *mitzvah* — but about the physical state that follows, which is incompatible with the direct, enunciated encounter with the word of God.

This is not a statement that physical life is problematic. The point is more subtle: receiving the Torah, or any reenactment of that encounter — and every learning of Torah can be understood as a microcosmic *Ma'amad Har Sinai* — demands a particular frame of mind. A state of *ba'al keri* represents an immersion in the purely physical that sits in tension with this spiritual encounter. Crucially, the concern at Sinai was about the *spoken* word of God being received. It was not merely that people were thinking Torah — the word was proclaimed aloud and encountered directly. Therefore the restriction on the *ba'al keri* applies specifically to the articulated word: speaking Torah, saying *shema* aloud, is the problem. Thinking it does not carry the same weight.

Tosafot adds a nuance: at *Har Sinai* the people were not themselves speaking — they were listening. Yet the restriction still applied, because the spoken word was present, active, being proclaimed. The word was "out there," not merely internal. This connects to the student's earlier observation: even reading *shema* from a *klaf*, directly encountering the written words of Torah, may present a heightened problem for the *ba'al keri* compared to reciting the rabbinic text of *birkat hamazon*.

The parallel deepens further: the human being reciting *shema* aloud is in some way a microcosmic reenactment of God proclaiming the *Aseret Hadiberot*. Rashi notes that every engagement with Torah can be seen as a reenactment of *Maamad Har Sinai*, grounded in the verse *yom asher amadeta lifnei Hashem Elokecha be-Chorev*. The act of *kriat shema* in particular carries this quality of direct encounter, and that is precisely what the *ba'al keri*'s state renders inappropriate.

So on the first view: thinking *shema* renders one *yotzei*, and the reason not to say it aloud is specifically the *ba'al keri* problem with articulated Torah — but thinking it is both efficacious and unproblematic.

### Hirhur lav k'dibbur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר, הִרְהוּר לָאו כְּדִבּוּר דָּמֵי, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ הִרְהוּר כְּדִבּוּר דָּמֵי — יוֹצִיא בִּשְׂפָתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said** that the opposite conclusion should be drawn from the mishna: **Contemplation is not tantamount to speech, as if it would enter your mind** that **contemplation is tantamount to speech,** then one who is impure because of a seminal emission should *ab initio*, **utter** *Shema* **with his lips.**

Rav Chisda takes the opposing view: *hirhur lav kedibbur dami* — thinking does not count as speech, and the *ba'al keri* is therefore not *yotzei shema* by thinking it. His reasoning: if thinking were equivalent to speech, then *yotzi bisftav* — the *ba'al keri* should just say it aloud. And if you say he cannot say it because of the *ba'al keri* problem while thinking is equivalent to speech — Rav Chisda does not accept this middle position. He holds that the two cannot be decoupled: if thinking counts, say it; if you cannot say it, thinking does not count. Therefore, according to Rav Chisda, the *ba'al keri* cannot be *yotzei shema* at all until he immerses. He simply cannot fulfill the obligation in his current state.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי הִרְהוּר לָאו כְּדִבּוּר דָּמֵי, לָמָּה מְהַרְהֵר? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ כׇּל הָעוֹלָם עוֹסְקִין בּוֹ וְהוּא יוֹשֵׁב וּבָטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this argument: **But what** are you saying, that **contemplation is not tantamount to speech?** If so, **why does he contemplate? Rabbi Elazar said: So that** a situation **will not** arise **where everyone is engaged in** reciting *Shema* **and he sits idly** by.

This leaves the *gemara* with a question: on Rav Chisda's view, why does the *mishna* instruct the *ba'al keri* to think *shema* at all? If he is not *yotzei*, what is the point?

### Participation purpose

Rabbi Elazar provides the answer: *kedei shelo yehei kol ha'olam oskim bo vehu yoshev u'vatel* — so that he should not be in a situation where the entire community is engaged in *shema* and he sits there doing nothing. This is not merely a social concern about appearances. *Shema* is the central act of *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim* — the acceptance of the yoke of heaven, the declaration of faith in God's sovereignty. It is not something one simply sits out when the community is doing it. Even if the *ba'al keri* cannot be *yotzei*, even if his thinking does not count as speech and does not discharge his obligation, there is a distinct value in participating in this act of communal faith. He thinks along, he orients himself toward the same declaration, even though technically he is not fulfilling the *mitzvah*.

This principle has broader application. When one is in the middle of *Psukei d'Zimra* and the congregation reaches *shema*, at the appropriate pause points one should say *shema* along with the congregation — even though one will arrive at *shema* in one's own davening shortly thereafter. The point is not *yeitziah*; it is participation in a communal act of declaring God's sovereignty. One should not be visibly standing aside from that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִגְרוֹס בְּפִרְקָא אַחֲרִינָא! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: בְּדָבָר שֶׁהַצִּבּוּר עוֹסְקִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If that is the only purpose, **let him study another chapter** and not specifically *Shema* or one of the blessings. **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** It is fitting that one engage **in a matter in which the community is engaged.**

The *gemara* then asks a follow-up question: *venigras be-pirkeih* — why must he specifically think *shema*? Why not simply study his regular learning while everyone else says *shema*? The answer provided: *be-davar she-hatzibur oskim bo* — the point is to be doing specifically what the community is doing. It is not merely about avoiding idleness or doing something unrelated; it is about participating in the same shared act.

### Malchut shamayim focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי תְּפִלָּה דְּלֵית בַּהּ מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Prayer is different** in **that it does not contain** the acceptance of the yoke of **the kingdom of Heaven.**

Having established this principle, the *gemara* asks why it does not apply equally to *tefilla*. The *mishna*'s case of someone who begins *Shmoneh Esrei* and then remembers he is a *ba'al keri* — *lo yafsik ela yekatzer*, he should not interrupt but should abbreviate — implies that if he has not yet begun, he should not begin at all. Why then does he not at least silently participate in *Shmoneh Esrei* the way he thinks *shema*?

The *gemara* answers: *shani tefilla d'leit bah malchut shamayim* — *tefilla* is different, because it does not carry the same dimension of *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim*. The act of *shema* is intrinsically the act of accepting God's sovereignty — *Shema Yisrael*, *ve'ahavta*, the declaration of faith. That is so central a communal and theological act that even a *ba'al keri* who cannot fulfill it must participate in some way. *Tefilla* — *Shmoneh Esrei* — is not that act in the same sense. And here Rashi adds a philological note: the standard formula *Eloheinu Melech ha'olam* does not appear in the *brachos* of *Shmoneh Esrei*, which instead close with *haMelech haKadosh* and similar formulations. More fundamentally, it is not that *Shmoneh Esrei* never invokes God's kingship; it is that *shema* itself *is* the act of *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim* in a way that *tefilla* is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן לְאַחֲרָיו דְּלֵית בַּהּ מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם, וּתְנַן עַל הַמָּזוֹן מְבָרֵךְ לְאַחֲרָיו וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ לְפָנָיו! אֶלָּא: קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וּתְפִלָּה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And Grace after Meals does not contain** the acceptance of the yoke of **the kingdom of Heaven, and** yet **we learned** in the mishna: **Over food, one recites a blessing afterward, but does not recite a blessing beforehand. Rather,** the differences must be explained otherwise: **The recitation of *Shema* and Grace after Meals** are both mitzvot **by Torah law, while prayer** is only **by rabbinic law.**

The *gemara* then turns back to *birkat hamazon*: the *mishna* says the *ba'al keri* does recite *birkat hamazon* after eating. This is not *malchut shamayim*, so why does he recite it? The answer: *krias shema u'virchas hamazon d'oraita u'tefilla d'rabbanan* — the dividing line is the biblical versus the rabbinic. The *ba'al keri* performs his biblical obligations; he does not perform the rabbinic ones. *Krias shema* is biblical (and additionally has the *malchut shamayim* dimension); *birkat hamazon* afterward is biblical; *tefilla* in its structured form is rabbinic — and so he does not participate in it.

There is an important alternate *girsa* here: on that reading, *shema* gets special treatment because of *malchut shamayim*, *birkat hamazon* because it is biblical, and *tefilla* is set aside because it has neither dimension — it is neither biblically mandated in its specific form nor is it the primary act of *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim*. Even those who hold, like the Rambam, that there is a biblical *mitzvah* of prayer, agree that the specific structure of *Shmoneh Esrei* as we know it is rabbinic. So the demands surrounding *tefilla* are correspondingly less.

It is worth noting here an important historical footnote: concerns similar to those surrounding the *ba'al keri* were applied in certain Ashkenazic communities of the Middle Ages to women who were *niddah*. The idea that a particular physical state renders certain articulations of *shem Hashem* problematic — that one cannot make *brachos*, cannot say *Shmoneh Esrei*, and so on — was extended by some to the *niddah* context, though this was never a uniform ruling but rather specific *minhagim*. The *gemara* here makes clear that the core concern is the articulation of *shem Hashem* in a state that is spiritually incongruent with that encounter, whatever form that state takes.

The *gemara* does not explicitly address why *shema* is done through *hirhur* while *birkat hamazon* is articulated. The *talmidei Rabbeinu Yona* maintain that even *birkat hamazon* is only thought, not said aloud, so there is no asymmetry. The other approach — that *shema*, as direct words of Torah, is more problematic to enunciate, while *birkat hamazon*, as a rabbinic formulation, can be recited when biblically demanded — has much to recommend it, especially in light of the student's observation connecting this to the Sinai parallel.

## Biblical sources

### Benching source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מִנַּיִן לְבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן לְאַחֲרֶיהָ מִן הַתּוֹרָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ וְשָׂבָעְתָּ וּבֵרַכְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: From where is** the mitzva **by Torah law** to recite **Grace after Meals,** derived? **As it is stated: "And you shall eat and be satisfied and bless the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 8:10).

The *gemara* now moves to the biblical derivation of *birkat hamazon*. Rav Yehuda asks: *menalan le'Birkat HaMazon le'achareiha min haTorah* — from where do we know that *birkat hamazon* afterward is biblical? The source is the verse *ve'achalta ve-savata u'veirachta et Hashem Elohecha* — you shall eat, be sated, and bless God. The word *ve-savata* — be sated — implies that the biblical obligation applies specifically when one has eaten enough to be truly full. This is what generates the entire distinction between the biblical obligation (requiring *seva*, satiety) and the rabbinic extension to smaller amounts such as a *ke'zayit* or *ke'beitzah*.

### Torah blessing source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לְבִרְכַּת הַתּוֹרָה לְפָנֶיהָ מִן הַתּוֹרָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי שֵׁם ה׳ אֶקְרָא הָבוּ גֹדֶל לֵאלֹהֵינוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** And **from where is** the mitzva **by Torah law** to recite **the blessing over the Torah before it** is read, derived? **As it is stated: "When I proclaim the Lord's name, give glory to our God"** (Deuteronomy 32:3), meaning that before one proclaims the Lord's name by reading the Torah, he must give glory to God.

Rav Yehuda then asks: *menalan le'Birkat HaTorah lefaneha min haTorah* — from where do we know that *Birkat HaTorah* before Torah study is itself biblical? The source is the verse *ki shem Hashem ekra havu godel l'Eloheinu* — when I call out in the name of God, you shall ascribe greatness to our God. Rashi understands this as Moshe declaring that before he teaches Torah — before *kriat* the name of God — he will make a *Birkat HaTorah* and the people respond *amen* with *havu godel l'Eloheinu*. But more simply, the verse can be read as saying that whenever one is about to "call out" — *ekra* — in God's name, meaning engage with and read the words of Torah, the response and the prerequisite is *havu godel*, to bless and praise God. The *bracha* of *Birkat HaTorah* is therefore not merely a *birkat hamitzvah* in the technical sense, but primarily an act of *shevach* — praise and thanksgiving for God's gift of Torah to Israel. This is the point the Ramban emphasizes: *asher bachar banu mikol ha'amim ve-natan lanu et Torato* — the selection and the gift are what call forth the blessing.

One student notes that *havu godel* may also be the textual basis for *Yehei Shmei Rabba* — a compelling parallel that connects the congregational response to Torah with the central declaration of *Kaddish*.

### Kal v'chomer logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמַדְנוּ בִּרְכַּת הַתּוֹרָה לְאַחֲרֶיהָ מִן בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, וּבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן לְפָנֶיהָ מִן בִּרְכַּת הַתּוֹרָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר. בִּרְכַּת הַתּוֹרָה לְאַחֲרֶיהָ מִן בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מָזוֹן שֶׁאֵין טָעוּן לְפָנָיו, טָעוּן לְאַחֲרָיו, תּוֹרָה שֶׁטְּעוּנָה לְפָנֶיהָ, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטְּעוּנָה לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. וּבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן לְפָנֶיהָ מִן בִּרְכַּת הַתּוֹרָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה תּוֹרָה שֶׁאֵין טְעוּנָה לְאַחֲרֶיהָ, טְעוּנָה לְפָנֶיהָ, מָזוֹן שֶׁהוּא טָעוּן לְאַחֲרָיו, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא טָעוּן לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: We derived** that one must recite **the blessing over the Torah after** it is read **from Grace after Meals** by means of an ***a fortiori* inference. And** we derive the obligation to recite **a blessing before** partaking of **food** from **the blessing over the Torah** by means of an ***a fortiori* inference. The blessing over the Torah after** it is read **from Grace after Meals** by means of an ***a fortiori* inference: Food, which does not require a blessing beforehand** by Torah law, **requires a blessing afterward; Torah, which requires** a blessing **beforehand, is it not right that it requires** a blessing **afterward? And** similarly: **The blessing before** partaking of **food** from **the blessing over the Torah** by means of an ***a fortiori* inference: Torah, which requires no** blessing **afterward** by Torah law, **requires** a blessing **beforehand; food, which requires** a blessing **afterward, is it not right that it requires** a blessing **beforehand?**

Rabbi Yochanan then proposes that these two biblical obligations can be derived from one another through a *kal v'chomer*. The reasoning works in both directions.

To derive *Birkat HaTorah* afterward from *birkat hamazon*: *u'ma mazon she'ein ta'un lefanav ta'un le'acharav* — for food, where there is no biblical requirement to bless before, there is nevertheless a biblical requirement to bless after. *Torah she'te'unah lefaneha* — for Torah, where you do make a *bracha* before (and it is more demanding than food in that respect), *eino din she'te'unah le'achareiha* — how much more so should there be a requirement to bless after.

To derive *birkat hamazon* beforehand from *Birkat HaTorah*: *u'ma Torah she'ein te'unah le'achareiha te'unah lefaneha* — for Torah, where there is no biblical requirement to bless after, there is a requirement to bless before. *Mazon she'hu ta'un le'acharav* — for food, where you must bless after (and it is thus more demanding than Torah in that respect), *eino din she'ta'un lefanav* — how much more so should you bless before.

### Challenge to logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִפְרַךְ: מָה לְמָזוֹן שֶׁכֵּן נֶהֱנֶה. וּמָה לְתוֹרָה שֶׁכֵּן חַיֵּי עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The logic of this *a fortiori* inference **can be refuted: What** is true with regard **to food, where one derives pleasure** from eating, is not true with regard to matters which offer no bodily pleasure. Therefore, the blessing over the Torah cannot be derived from the blessing over food. **And** similarly: **What** is true with regard to **Torah, that** provides **eternal life** to those who engage in its study, is not true with regard to matters that do not provide eternal life.

This *kal v'chomer* is problematic on two levels. First, it is circular in a particularly acute way: once you use the *kal v'chomer* to establish one conclusion, you have destroyed the asymmetry that made the *kal v'chomer* possible in the first place. The entire argument depends on one being more demanding than the other in a particular direction; proving symmetry collapses the basis for the inference in the other direction.

Second, and more substantively, the *gemara* challenges both *kal v'chomer* arguments by noting that neither domain is simply "more demanding" than the other — each has its own distinctive quality that may explain the arrangement. *Ma lemazon sheken nehene* — food involves physical benefit, derived here and now. *U'ma la-Torah sheken chayei olam* — Torah leads to *olam haba*, to eternal life. These are qualitatively different, and one cannot simply treat one as a *kal* relative to the other.

These differences may in fact explain the substantive asymmetry of *before* and *after*. For *birkat hamazon*, the blessing comes after because the sense of gratitude and appreciation is most naturally expressed in response to physical benefit already received — *ve-savata u'veirachta*, you ate and were sated, and then you blessed. One could argue the reverse — that hunger and anticipation are themselves occasions for gratitude — but there is a logic to saying that the experience of benefit is what most calls forth the response of blessing. For Torah, by contrast, the benefit is not received during or immediately after the learning in any tangible sense — the reward is *ad mea ve'esrim*, extending to a future that is not experienced at the moment of study. There is therefore no natural logic of responding to benefit already received. Instead, the very act of preparing to encounter Torah — to involve oneself in something of such spiritual magnitude — is itself the moment of heightened awareness, and that is when the *bracha* belongs.

So the *gemara*'s challenge to the *kal v'chomer* is not merely formalistic. The differences between *nehene* and *chayei olam* go to the heart of why the obligations are structured as they are, and that structural difference undermines the attempt to derive one from the other.

### Mishna refutes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, תְּנַן עַל הַמָּזוֹן מְבָרֵךְ לְאַחֲרָיו וְאֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ לְפָנָיו. תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Furthermore, we learned** in the mishna: **Over food,** one who is impure due to a seminal emission **recites a blessing afterward, but does not recite a blessing beforehand.** The mishna does not derive the blessing recited before a meal from the blessing recited over Torah. Consequently, this is a **conclusive refutation** of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement.

The *gemara* closes this discussion by pointing to the *mishna* itself as a *tiyuvta* — a refutation of the position that *birkat hamazon* beforehand is biblical. The *mishna* states: *al hamazon mevarech le'acharav ve'eino mevarech lefanav* — the *ba'al keri* recites *birkat hamazon* after eating but not the *bracha* before. Since the *ba'al keri* performs biblical obligations and omits rabbinic ones, the *mishna*'s ruling demonstrates that only *birkat hamazon* afterward is biblical. The *bracha* over bread before eating — *birkat ha-nehenin* — is only rabbinic. This decisively rules out the possibility that Rabbi Yochanan's *kal v'chomer* succeeded in establishing a biblical *bracha* before food.

The *gemara*'s position at this juncture is thus: *birkat hamazon* afterward is biblical, established by the verse *ve'achalta ve-savata u'veirachta*. *Birkat HaTorah* beforehand is biblical, established by *ki shem Hashem ekra*. The *bracha* over food before eating is rabbinic. And the *bracha* over Torah after — the *bracha* recited at the conclusion of an *aliyah* — is established through Rabbi Yochanan's *kal v'chomer* from *birkat hamazon*, even if that *kal v'chomer* cannot be cleanly run in reverse.

The further question — which parts of *kriat shema* are biblical and which are rabbinic — remains for the *gemara* to address in what follows.

The discussion of *hirhur k'dibbur* that runs through this entire *sugya* has one further application worth noting before concluding. Tosafot addresses the case of someone in the middle of *Shmoneh Esrei* who hears the congregation reaching *Kaddish* and *Yehei Shmei Rabba*. Rashi (elsewhere) rules that the person should be silent and is *yotzei* through *shomea k'oneh* — hearing counts as answering. Tosafot objects: if *shomea k'oneh* means one has effectively spoken, then one has interrupted *Shmoneh Esrei*. Tosafot ultimately accepts Rashi's position, but the resolution lies precisely in the nuanced middle ground explored throughout this *sugya*: *hirhur* — thinking, or even hearing — can be sufficient for *yeitziah* without being treated as literal speech in every halachic context. It is sufficient to be *yotzei* without being considered an interruption of *Shmoneh Esrei*.

This same question surfaces in the debate between the *Gra* and the *Shulchan Aruch* about whether one must recite *Birkat HaTorah* before engaging in Torah study conducted entirely through thought, without any articulation. The *Gra*'s position turns on whether *Birkat HaTorah* is primarily a *birkat hamitzvah* on the *mitzvah* of *talmud Torah* — in which case it would apply even to silent thought, since one is certainly fulfilling the *mitzvah* thereby — or whether it is fundamentally tied to the encounter with the spoken or written word of Torah, the *ki shem Hashem ekra* moment of calling out God's name. On the latter understanding, *Birkat HaTorah* is demanded only when the words are enunciated, just as the *ba'al keri*'s restrictions are triggered specifically by articulation. The same conceptual tension between *hirhur* and *dibbur* that governs the *ba'al keri*'s *shema* runs through the *Birkat HaTorah* debate as well.
