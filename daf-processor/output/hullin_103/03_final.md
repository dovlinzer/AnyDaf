# Hullin 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sources: Ever/Basar

### R. Yochanan's Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״לֹא תֹאכַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ עִם הַבָּשָׂר״ – זֶה אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי, ״וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״ – זֶה בָּשָׂר מִן הַחַי וּבָשָׂר מִן הַטְּרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: "You shall not eat the life with the flesh"** (Deuteronomy 12:23); **this** is the source for the prohibition of eating **a limb from a living** animal. And the verse: **"And you shall not eat any flesh that is torn in the field"** (Exodus 22:30); **this** is the source for the prohibition of eating **flesh** severed **from the living and flesh** severed **from a *tereifa*,** even if it is not an entire limb.

The Gemara opens near the bottom of 102b, with a debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning the scriptural sources for two related prohibitions: *ever min hachai* (a limb from a living animal) and *basar min hachai* (flesh from a living animal).

Rabbi Yochanan identifies each prohibition with a distinct verse. The source for *ever min hachai* is the verse *lo tochal hanefesh im habasar* — "you shall not eat the soul with the meat" (Devarim 12:23). Rashi explains that *nefesh* here signifies the limb, something that possesses a kind of independent vitality, a piece that, once severed, does not regenerate. Hence, do not eat the limb while the *nefesh chayah* is still animating the flesh. The source for *basar min hachai*, by contrast, is a separate verse: *uvasar basadeh treifah lo tochelu* — "do not eat flesh torn in the field" (Shemot 22:30). Although this verse is, of course, the primary source for *treifah*, Rabbi Yochanan reads it simultaneously as a prohibition against flesh torn from a living animal, since *basar treifah* denotes flesh that has been violently ripped.

The practical distinction between the two prohibitions is also worth noting. For *ever min hachai*, one transgresses even by consuming less than a *kezayis* — certainly if the limb includes bones that together constitute a *kezayis* — whereas *basar min hachai* is only transgressed upon eating a *kezayis* of flesh.

### Reish Lakish's Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: ״לֹא תֹאכַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ עִם הַבָּשָׂר״ – זֶה אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי וּבָשָׂר מִן הַחַי, ״וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״ – זֶה בָּשָׂר מִן הַטְּרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: "You shall not eat the life with the flesh"** (Deuteronomy 12:23); **this** is the source for the prohibitions of eating **a limb from a living** animal **and** of eating **flesh** severed **from the living.** And the verse: **"And you shall not eat any flesh that is torn in the field"** (Exodus 22:30); **this** is the source for the prohibition of eating **flesh** severed **from a *tereifa*.**

Reish Lakish agrees that *lo tochal hanefesh im habasar* is the source for *ever min hachai*, but he departs from Rabbi Yochanan regarding *basar min hachai*. For Reish Lakish, the very same verse — *lo tochal hanefesh im habasar* — also prohibits *basar min hachai*. The reading is natural: do not eat the meat while the soul is still within the animal, meaning eat meat only after the animal has been slaughtered and its soul taken. By this reading, the verse *uvasar basadeh treifah lo tochelu* refers exclusively to the laws of *treifah*, with no additional derivation for *basar min hachai*.

### Debate Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכַל אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי וּבָשָׂר מִן הַחַי – לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָכַל בָּשָׂר מִן הַחַי וּבָשָׂר מִן הַטְּרֵפָה – לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָכַל אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי וּבָשָׂר מִן הַטְּרֵפָה – לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the difference between these two opinions. If one **ate a limb from a living** animal **and flesh** severed **from the living,** then **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan he is liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes but **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. Conversely, if one **ate flesh** severed **from the living and flesh** severed **from a *tereifa*,** then **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish he is liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes but **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan he is liable to** receive **only one** set of lashes. If one **ate a limb from a living** animal **and flesh** severed **from a *tereifa*, everyone agrees** that he is **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes.

The practical consequences of this dispute come into focus when one examines the rules governing multiple sets of lashes (*malkot*). The Gemara operates on the assumption that transgressions learned from the same verse — even if they are conceptually distinct — yield only a single set of lashes when committed simultaneously in a single act.

If one ate *ever min hachai* and *basar min hachai* together with a single warning (*azharah*), Rabbi Yochanan would hold him liable for two sets of lashes, since they derive from separate verses. Reish Lakish would hold him liable for only one set, as both are derived from a single verse. The Gemara notes that this is not the same as the principle of *lav shebichlalus* — a prohibition whose verse is so broad and nonspecific as to preclude lashes entirely. Here, the verse is specific; it is simply interpreted in two ways. Still, simultaneous transgression of two applications of the same verse yields only one set of lashes.

A more striking implication involves eating *basar min hachai* together with *basar min hatreifah* — undeniably distinct prohibitions, one concerning meat ripped from a living animal and the other concerning a slaughtered *treifah*. Yet according to Rabbi Yochanan, who derives *basar min hachai* from the same verse as *treifah* (*uvasar basadeh treifah*), if both were eaten simultaneously, only one set of lashes would result. For Reish Lakish, who assigns *basar min hachai* to a different verse, two sets of lashes would apply. Finally, if one ate *ever min hachai* together with *basar min hatreifah* — the two extremes of the spectrum — everyone agrees these derive from separate verses, and two sets of lashes apply regardless of the debate.

## Ever + Treifah Case

### Live Treifah Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכַל אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי מִן הַטְּרֵפָה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who **ate a limb from a living** animal that is **a *tereifa*, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He is **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes, **and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: He is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes.

The Gemara now opens 103a with a new and more intricate case: *achal ever min hachai min hatreifah* — one ate a limb taken from an animal that is both alive and a *treifah*. Rabbi Yochanan holds him liable for two transgressions; Reish Lakish holds him liable for only one.

At first glance, the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan poses no difficulty: *ever min hachai* and *treifah* are derived from universally agreed-upon separate verses, so two sets of lashes are expected. The difficulty lies with Reish Lakish. Since those two prohibitions come from distinct verses — something everyone concedes — why does Reish Lakish say only one set of lashes?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** this **works out well** because the prohibitions of eating a limb from a living animal and of eating flesh severed from a *tereifa* are derived from two different verses. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** it is **difficult;** why does he hold that the individual receives only one set of lashes?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בִּבְהֵמָה אַחַת, כָּאן בִּשְׁתֵּי בְּהֵמוֹת. בִּשְׁתֵּי בְּהֵמוֹת מִיחַיַּיב שְׁתַּיִם, בִּבְהֵמָה אַחַת פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This is **not difficult. Here** it is referring **to one animal,** but **there** it is referring **to two animals.** Rav Yosef clarifies: **In** a case of **two animals,** e.g., where one ate a limb from a living animal and flesh severed from a different animal that was a *tereifa*, everyone agrees that **he is liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes. But **in** a case where he ate from **one animal,** e.g., he ate a limb severed from a live *tereifa* animal, Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **disagree.**

The resolution offered by Rav Yosef is that the earlier discussion concerned two separate animals — one forbidden as *ever min hachai*, the other as *treifah* — eaten simultaneously. With two animals and two verses, everyone agrees on two sets of lashes. But the present case concerns a single animal, and here the complications of *issur chal al issur* — one prohibition taking effect on top of another — come into play. When the same piece of meat must be doubly categorized, the question is no longer merely which verse applies, but whether both prohibitions can simultaneously be in force on the very same object.

### Born Treifah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּבְהֵמָה אַחַת, בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה עִם יְצִיאַת רוּבָּהּ, מָר סָבַר: בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וְאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה וְאִיסּוּר אֵבֶר בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to** the case of **one animal, in what** case **do they disagree? Abaye said:** They disagree, **for example,** in a case where the animal **became a *tereifa* as the majority of it emerged** from its mother's womb. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **an animal,** even **during its life, stands to** be divided into **limbs,** and therefore each of its limbs is considered a separate entity; **and** here **the prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa* and the prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal **come** into effect **at the same time.** Consequently, both prohibitions apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָר סָבַר, בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לָאו לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וְלָא אָתֵי אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר חָיֵיל אַאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, **holds** that **during its life an animal does not stand** to be divided into **limbs.** Consequently, although the prohibition of eating a *tereifa* comes into effect when it is born, the prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal does not take effect until the limb is actually severed from the animal, **and** at that point **the prohibition of a limb** from a living animal **does not come and take effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of a *tereifa*.**

Abaye proposes the first specific scenario to illustrate what the debate might actually be about. The animal was born as a *treifah* — it emerged from the womb already carrying the defect that renders it mortally ill. At the moment of birth, both the prohibition of *treifah* and potentially the prohibition of *ever min hachai* came into being simultaneously.

This scenario depends on a further halachic question we have already encountered: does the principle of *beheimah bechayeha le'eivarim omedet* apply — is a living animal already considered, in halachic terms, as subdivided into its limbs? If it is, then the prohibition of *ever min hachai* begins the moment the animal is born, since it is already "seen" as constituent limbs. If so, both *treifah* and *ever min hachai* take effect simultaneously at birth, and there is no prior prohibition to block the other.

According to this reading, Rabbi Yochanan holds that *beheimah bechayeha le'eivarim omedet* — the animal is considered subdivided from the moment it lives — and therefore both prohibitions arose at the same time, both take full effect, and two sets of lashes apply. Reish Lakish holds the opposite: the animal is not considered subdivided into limbs while alive. The prohibition of *ever min hachai* only arises at the moment the limb is actually severed. Since the animal began its life already as a *treifah*, the *issur treifah* was already in place when the limb was torn off, and the subsequently arising *issur ever min hachai* cannot take effect upon it — *ein issur chal al issur*.

Tosafot raises the natural objection: is not *ever min hachai* an *issur mosif*, a prohibition of larger scope — since it applies even to non-Jews (*bnei Noach*)? And does not larger scope allow a later prohibition to take effect on a pre-existing one? Tosafot acknowledges that not everyone accepts the principle of *issur mosif* in this way. At least one position simply does not grant the later-arising prohibition the power to take effect, even when its scope is broader.

### Larger Scope Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לָאו לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וּבְמֵיתֵי אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר מֵיחַל אַאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר אָתֵי אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר חָיֵיל אַאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה, וּמָר סָבַר לָא אָתֵי אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר חָיֵיל אַאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **during its life an animal does not stand** to be divided into **limbs, and they disagree with regard to** whether **the prohibition of a limb** from a living animal **comes and takes effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of a *tereifa*.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **the prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal, which applies to gentiles as well as to Jews, **comes and takes effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa*. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, **holds** that **the prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal **does not come and take effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa*.**

The Gemara then offers a second way of understanding the debate. In this reading, everyone would agree that *beheimah bechayeha lav le'eivarim omedet* — the animal is not considered subdivided into limbs from the moment of birth. There is no dispute on that question. The dispute instead concerns whether the *issur ever min hachai*, which arises later (when the limb is severed from an animal already established as a *treifah*), can nonetheless take effect by virtue of its larger scope.

The argument for allowing it to take effect is straightforward: *ever min hachai* is an *issur mosif* — it applies to a broader category of persons, namely *bnei Noach* — and therefore it ought to be able to take hold even on top of a pre-existing *issur treifah*. One opinion accepts this; the other does not, perhaps questioning whether the fact that non-Jews are additionally prohibited constitutes a sufficient basis for *issur mosif*, given that non-Jews exist in an entirely separate legal framework.

### Reverse Order Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וּכְגוֹן שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה לְאַחַר מִכָּאן, וּבְמֵיתֵי אִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה חָיֵיל אַאִיסּוּר אֵבֶר קָא מִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** And **if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **an animal,** even **during its life, stands to** be divided into **limbs, and** the dispute is about a case **where** the animal **became a *tereifa* afterward,** i.e., after it was born, **and they disagree** with regard to whether **the prohibition of a *tereifa* comes and takes effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of a limb** from a living animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר אָתֵי וְחָיֵיל, וּמַר סָבַר לָא אָתֵי וְחָיֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that the prohibition of eating a *tereifa* **comes and takes effect** in addition to the already existing prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, **holds** that the prohibition of eating a *tereifa* **does not come and take effect** in addition to the already existing prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal.

The Gemara entertains a third possibility. Here, everyone agrees that *beheimah bechayeha le'eivarim omedet* — the animal is considered subdivided from birth — but the temporal sequence of the prohibitions is reversed. The animal was born healthy; from the moment of birth, it was already subject to the prohibition of *ever min hachai*. Only later did it develop the defect that rendered it a *treifah*. Now the question is inverted: can *treifah* take effect on top of a pre-existing *ever min hachai*?

This is a far harder case to justify. *Treifah* has no claim to larger scope in this context; if anything, it has a narrower or equal scope relative to *ever min hachai*. How then could it take effect on top of a pre-existing prohibition? The Gemara states the debate: one opinion holds that *issur treifah* can indeed come and take effect (*ati vichayela*); the other holds it cannot.

The possibility that *treifah* can take effect on pre-existing prohibitions is not without precedent, and the Gemara will spell this out later in connection with *chelev* — but it is previewed here as the possible rationale for the opinion that allows *treifah* to override.

### Single Act Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁתָּלַשׁ מִמֶּנָּה אֵבֶר וּטְרָפָהּ בּוֹ. מָר סָבַר: בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לְאֵבָרִים אֵינָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת, אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר וְאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** an alternative explanation: This is referring to a case **where he severed a limb from** the animal **and** thereby **rendered** the animal **a *tereifa*.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **during its life, an animal does not stand to** be divided into **limbs.** Consequently, **the prohibition of a limb** from a living animal **and the prohibition of a *tereifa* come** into effect **at the same time.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָר סָבַר, בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וְלָא אָתֵי אִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה חָיֵיל אַאִיסּוּר אֵבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, **holds** that even **during its life, an animal stands to** be divided into **limbs,** and therefore the prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal takes effect when the animal is born. Consequently, **the prohibition of a *tereifa* does not come and take effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of a limb** from a living animal.

Rava presents a fourth and particularly elegant scenario. The animal was healthy, and the act of tearing off the limb was itself the event that rendered the animal a *treifah* — for example, the hind leg was torn off, and this very act created the *treifah* condition. In this case, the question is not about which prohibition arose first, since both *ever min hachai* and *treifah* would seem to arise at the identical moment of tearing.

One might think this eliminates the problem of competing prohibitions entirely — if they arise simultaneously, both should take effect, and two sets of lashes should result. But the Gemara shows that even here, the debate depends on the question of *beheimah bechayeha le'eivarim omedet*. The opinion that holds the animal is not considered subdivided into limbs while alive would say that *ever min hachai* only begins at the moment of tearing — and therefore, at that same moment, both *issur ever* and *issur treifah* arise together (*bahadi hadadi kasu*), both take effect, and two transgressions result. The opinion that holds the animal is already considered subdivided while alive would say that *issur ever* preceded the tearing — the limb was already subject to *ever min hachai* while the animal was walking around — and when the tearing now creates *treifah*, that prohibition is trying to take effect on an already-forbidden object, which it cannot do. Hence only one transgression.

## Chelev Cases

### Chelev as Eiver?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָכַל חֵלֶב מִן הַחַי מִן הַטְּרֵפָה – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי: וְלֵימָא מָר שָׁלֹשׁ, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָכַל חֵלֶב מִן הַחַי מִן הַטְּרֵפָה – חַיָּיב שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **ate** forbidden **fat from a living** animal that is **a *tereifa*** he is **liable** to receive **two** sets of lashes. **Rabbi Ami said to him: But let the Master say** that he is liable to **three** sets of lashes, **because I say** that the correct version of Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement is that he is liable to **three** sets of lashes. It **was also stated: Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **ate** forbidden **fat from a living** animal that is **a *tereifa*** he is **liable** to **three** sets of lashes.

The Gemara now brings a statement of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: one who ate *chelev* (forbidden fat) from a live *treifah* animal is liable for two transgressions. The Gemara pauses on this formulation, because it apparently treats the *chelev* as an instance of *ever min hachai* — but since when is *chelev* considered a limb?

The underlying question is significant. The prohibition of *ever min hachai* normally applies to a limb — an *eiver* — which is conventionally understood as a structure that includes bone, flesh, and sinew. *Chelev* is fat; it has none of these components in the ordinary sense. A number of *Rishonim* grapple with this. The Ramban establishes that if a limb naturally has a bone, the bone must be present for the *ever* designation; but if a limb by its nature has no bone — such as the ear or nose — then it can constitute an *ever* without one. From here, the Rashba extends the argument: if *ever min hachai* can apply to internal organs (which also have no bones), and if the *chelev* is associated with an internal organ such as the kidney — lying in its crevice, in some sense belonging to it — then the *chelev* might itself be categorized as an *eiver*. The Rashba goes further to suggest that *chelev*, considered on its own terms as a discrete anatomical unit, could constitute an *eiver*. This is admittedly difficult, since the precise boundaries of a mass of fat are not self-evident, but that is the direction of the analysis.

Given this, in the scenario under discussion, you have a *treifah* animal from which you have removed *chelev* while it is still alive. Three prohibitions are in play: *chelev*, *ever min hachai*, and *treifah*. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba quotes Rabbi Yochanan as saying two transgressions — and immediately Rabbi Ammi challenges him: why only two? I myself say three. And indeed, another tradition is cited in which Rabbi Abbahu quotes Rabbi Yochanan as saying three. So there are now competing traditions about Rabbi Yochanan's own ruling.

### Two vs. Three Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה עִם יְצִיאַת רוּבָּהּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר שָׁלֹשׁ, קָסָבַר: בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, דְּאִיסּוּר חֵלֶב וְאִיסּוּר אֵבֶר וְאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba and Rabbi Ami **disagree?** They disagree in a case **where** the animal **became a *tereifa* as the majority of it emerged** from its mother's womb; **the one who said** that he is liable to **three** sets of lashes **holds** that even **during its life an animal stands to** be divided into **limbs,** and each of its limbs is considered as a separate entity, **so that the prohibition of** eating forbidden **fat, the prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal, **and the prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa* come** into effect **at the same time.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁתַּיִם, קָסָבַר בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לָאו לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וְאִיסּוּר חֵלֶב וְאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה – אִיכָּא, אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר – לָא אָתֵי חָיֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And the one who said** that he is liable to **two** sets of lashes **holds** that **during its life, an animal does not stand to** be divided into **limbs,** i.e., its limbs are not considered separate entities while the animal is alive. Consequently, **the prohibition of** eating forbidden **fat and the prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa*** animal **apply,** as they came into effect at the same time, when the animal was born. By contrast, **the prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal **does not come and take effect,** due to the fact that other prohibitions already apply.

The Gemara directly confronts these competing traditions of Rabbi Yochanan — two lashes versus three. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba transmits a tradition of two; Rabbi Abbahu transmits a tradition of three. Rabbi Ammi, pressing on behalf of the three-lash tradition, challenges Rabbi Chiya's report. The Gemara asks: *bimai kamifligi* — what is this debate actually about?

### Timing Resolution

The Gemara explains the two versions by applying the framework already developed. The scenario involves an animal that became a *treifah* at the moment of birth (*kegon shenitrefa im yetziat rubba*).

The tradition of three lashes relies on the principle of *beheimah bechayeha le'eivarim omedet*. If the animal is considered subdivided into limbs from the moment of birth, then at the very instant this animal emerged, the prohibition of *chelev* began (since *chelev* of a fetus does not apply, it begins at birth), the prohibition of *ever min hachai* began, and the prohibition of *treifah* began — all simultaneously. When all three prohibitions take effect at precisely the same moment, all three apply in full, yielding three sets of lashes.

The tradition of two lashes applies the opposite principle: *beheimah bechayeha lav le'eivarim omedet*. The moment the animal was born, *chelev* and *treifah* took effect, but *ever min hachai* did not — it only arises when the fat is actually torn out. At that later moment, *issur ever* arrives and attempts to take effect on a piece of fat already doubly prohibited. Even granting that *ever min hachai* has larger scope as an *issur mosif*, this opinion does not allow the later-arising prohibition to take effect. Hence only two — *chelev* and *treifah*.

### Larger Scope Alt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לָאו לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וּבְמֵיתֵי אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר וְחָיֵיל אַאִיסּוּר חֵלֶב וְאַאִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר אָתֵי חָיֵיל, וּמַר סָבַר לָא אָתֵי חָיֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **during its life an animal does not stand to** be divided into **limbs,** and each of its limbs is not considered as a separate entity. **But they disagree with regard to** whether **the prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal **comes and takes effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of eating** forbidden **fat and the** already existing **prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa*.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Ami, **holds** that it does **come and take effect, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, **holds** that **it does not come and take effect.**

The Gemara again offers an alternative reading. In this version, everyone agrees that *beheimah bechayeha lav le'eivarim omedet* — the animal is not considered subdivided from birth. At the moment of birth, both *chelev* and *treifah* took effect. The prohibition of *ever min hachai* only came into being when the fat was ripped out, meaning it arrived last, on top of two pre-existing prohibitions. Can it take effect by virtue of being an *issur mosif*? One tradition of Rabbi Yochanan says yes, it can take effect — yielding three transgressions. The other says no — yielding two.

### Treifah's Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וּכְגוֹן שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה לְאַחַר מִכָּאן, וּבְמֵיתֵי אִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה מֵיחַל אַאִיסּוּר אֵבֶר קָא מִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **during its life an animal stands to** be divided into **limbs, and** it is a case **where** the animal **became a *tereifa* afterward,** i.e., after it was born; **and they disagree with regard to** whether **the prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa* comes and takes effect upon the** already existing **prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: אָתֵי חָיֵיל, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַחֵלֶב, דְּאָמַר מָר: הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה יָבֹא אִיסּוּר נְבֵלָה יָחוּל עַל אִיסּוּר חֵלֶב, וְיָבֹא אִיסּוּר טְרֵפָה יָחוּל עַל אִיסּוּר חֵלֶב.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Ami, **holds** that it does **come and take effect, just as is** the *halakha* **with** forbidden **fat. As the Master said** that in the verse: "And the fat of a carcass, and the fat of a *tereifa* may be used for any other service; but you shall in no way eat of it" (Leviticus 7:24), **the Torah said: Let the prohibition of** eating **a carcass come and take effect upon the prohibition of** eating forbidden **fat,** despite the fact that the prohibition of forbidden fat came into effect first. **And** similarly, the word "*tereifa*" teaches: **Let the prohibition of** eating **a *tereifa* come and take effect upon the prohibition of** eating forbidden **fat.** Consequently, one who eats forbidden fat from a *tereifa* is liable to receive two sets of lashes. Rabbi Ami holds that just as the prohibition of eating a *tereifa* takes effect in addition to the prohibition of eating forbidden fat, it also takes effect in addition to the prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, אַחֵלֶב הוּא דְּחַיָּיב, דְּהוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ, אֲבָל אֵבֶר דְּלֹא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, holds **that it is** only the prohibition of eating forbidden **fat for which he is liable** in addition to being liable for the prohibition of eating a *tereifa*. The prohibition of eating a *tereifa* takes effect in addition to the prohibition of eating forbidden fat **because** with regard to the latter, there are **permitted** circumstances that serve as exceptions **to its general prohibition,** as the fat of an undomesticated animal is permitted. **But** with regard to **a limb** from a living animal, **where** there are **no permitted** circumstances to **its general prohibition,** the prohibition of consuming a *tereifa* does **not** take effect.

The Gemara next considers the third permutation: everyone agrees *beheimah bechayeha le'eivarim omedet*, so *chelev* and *ever min hachai* both began at birth. The animal later developed the condition that made it a *treifah*. Can *treifah*, arriving last and without obvious larger scope, take effect on top of the pre-existing prohibitions of *chelev* and *ever min hachai*?

This is where the Gemara invokes a remarkable principle. The Torah itself teaches — derived from the verse *vechelev neveila vechelev treifah ye'aseh lechol melacha, ve'achol lo tocheluhu* (Vayikra 7:24) — that the fat of a *neveila* and a *treifah* is doubly prohibited: not only as *chelev*, but also as *neveila* or *treifah*. Why does the Torah need to say this, since we already know not to eat *chelev*, and we already know not to eat *neveila* or *treifah*? Precisely to teach that the prohibition of *treifah* takes effect on top of the pre-existing prohibition of *chelev*. We thus have an explicit textual basis for the principle that *treifah* can override a pre-existing *issur* — at least in the case of *chelev*. From this, one opinion extends the same principle to *ever min hachai*: just as *treifah* can take effect on *chelev* despite the prior prohibition, perhaps *treifah* can similarly take effect on the pre-existing prohibition of *ever min hachai*.

The rebuttal, however, is sharp. The reason *treifah* can take effect on *chelev* is not that *treifah* possesses some general power to override prior prohibitions. Rather, it is because *chelev* has a structural weakness: *dehutir miklalo* — it was permitted in a certain category. Specifically, *chelev* of a *chayah* (wild animal) is permitted. Since *chelev* is not a prohibition of universal scope — there are circumstances under which it does not apply — the prohibition of *treifah* is, in a relevant sense, of larger scope relative to *chelev*, and can take effect. By contrast, *ever min hachai* has no such exception — *aval ever d'lo hutra miklalo*. There is no animal or circumstance where *ever min hachai* does not prohibit; it applies universally, including to *bnei Noach*. Therefore *treifah* cannot take effect on top of *ever min hachai*.

An additional dimension of the *chelev* case is worth noting. When an animal becomes a *treifah*, the prohibition of *treifah* now covers the meat, the organs, and the *chelev* together — expanding to apply to parts of the animal that were previously only subject to *chelev*, not *treifah*. This constitutes a kind of *issur mosif* in its own right: the *treifah* prohibition adds to what was previously prohibited. In the case of *ever min hachai* applied to the whole living animal, by contrast, the entire animal was already subject to *ever min hachai* — there is no expansion in scope when *treifah* later arrives. The Gemara does not fully explore whether *treifah* might have a slightly larger scope in the sense that it applies to parts of the animal that are not *eivarim* and therefore not covered by *ever min hachai*, but this line of reasoning is not pressed.

## Breaking Limb

### Throat vs. Stomach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חִלְּקוֹ מִבַּחוּץ, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish inquired of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If one took from a living animal a limb that was an olive-bulk and **divided it** into two pieces **when it was outside** his mouth and ate each piece separately, **what is** the *halakha*? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** He is **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבִּפְנִים, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish then asked Rabbi Yoḥanan: If he placed an olive-bulk of a limb from a living animal **inside** his mouth and then divided it and swallowed the two parts separately, **what** is the *halakha*? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** He is **liable** to receive lashes.

The Gemara now shifts entirely away from the question of competing prohibitions and turns to the nature of the act of eating itself — with far-reaching implications that extend, as we will see, to Yom Kippur. The anchor debate is again that of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, here on the question of how eating (*achilah*) is defined.

Rabbi Yochanan's position is: *harei neheneh grono bikezayis* — the act of eating is defined by the throat deriving benefit from a *kezayis*. Reish Lakish's position is: *achilah beme'ayim ba'inan* — eating requires that the food be consumed and reach the stomach. In conceptual terms, the debate can be framed as: does eating mean ingestion (what happens in the throat) or something closer to the endpoint of digestion (what happens in the stomach)?

### Outside Mouth Exempt

The opening question is posed by Reish Lakish to Rabbi Yochanan: *chilkehu ve'he'achilo bachutz mahu* — if one divided the limb outside the mouth, broke it in two pieces before bringing it to the mouth, and then ate both pieces, has one transgressed the prohibition of *ever min hachai*? The answer is that one is exempt. For normal prohibited foods, the *kezayis* measure can be accumulated over a period of time (*kedei achilat pras*), and one need not eat it in a single gulp. But *ever min hachai* involves eating the limb as a limb — as a cohesive *eiver* — and once it has been split, it may no longer carry that identity. The wholeness of the *eiver* is a precondition, and dividing it outside the mouth, before eating begins, eliminates that precondition. This is the position both sages accept.

### Inside Mouth Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר: חִלְּקוֹ מִבַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר, מִבְּפָנִים – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר חַיָּיב, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he **said** an alternative version of this discussion. If one took from a living animal a limb that was an olive-bulk and **divided it** into two pieces **when it was outside** his mouth, and he then ate each piece separately, he is **exempt.** If he divided the limb into two parts **inside** his mouth, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that he is **liable, and Reish Lakish says** that he is **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, הֲרֵי נֶהֱנָה גְּרוֹנוֹ בִּכְזַיִת. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פָּטוּר, אֲכִילָה בְּמֵעָיו בָּעֵינַן וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** he is **liable** because his **throat derives pleasure from an olive-bulk** of a limb from a living animal. **And Reish Lakish says** that he is **exempt** because in order to be liable **we require** an act of **eating** that contains the requisite amount, i.e., an olive-bulk, when it enters **his stomach, and** in this case **there is not** a full olive-bulk that enters his stomach at one time.

The more contentious question is: what if one divided the limb inside the mouth — put it in whole, and then broke it apart while chewing? Rabbi Yochanan holds one liable; Reish Lakish holds one exempt. The rationale follows directly from their respective definitions of eating. For Rabbi Yochanan, since eating is defined by the throat's benefit, and since the limb entered the mouth as a whole *eiver*, the act of eating had already begun when it was whole. The subsequent division within the mouth does not undo the fact that the eating commenced with an intact limb. For Reish Lakish, since eating is defined by what reaches the stomach, and since the food arrived in the stomach as two separate pieces, one never ate a whole *eiver* — the definitive moment of eating reveals only fragments, not a limb.

### Ravin's Version

When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted the positions in slightly different form. The ruling on dividing outside the mouth — exempt — is confirmed. On dividing inside the mouth: Rabbi Yochanan holds liable, Reish Lakish holds exempt. This is consistent with what we have said.

One might then ask: according to Reish Lakish, who holds one exempt whenever the limb is divided before swallowing — whether outside or inside the mouth — how can one ever transgress *ever min hachai*? The Gemara's answer, brought by Rav Kahana, is: *begarmiza ze'irata* — a small bone or bony piece, something that by its nature one swallows whole without chewing. A piece of that sort one would naturally ingest intact, sending it whole into the stomach. If one swallowed it whole, one transgresses according to Reish Lakish; if one divided it inside the mouth and swallowed the halves separately, one is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דִּמְחַיֵּיב? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: בִּגְרוֹמִיתָא זְעֵירְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan it is clear how one can be liable for eating an olive-bulk of a limb from a living animal. **But according to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, how can you find** a case **where** one will **be liable** for eating a limb from a living animal, since the food is generally broken up before he swallows it? **Rav Kahana said:** One would be liable **in** a case where he eats **a small bone** that contains an olive-bulk of meat, bone and sinew all together, and that he can swallow whole.

### R. Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חִלְּקוֹ מִבַּחוּץ נָמֵי חַיָּיב, מְחוּסַּר קְרִיבָה לָאו כִּמְחוּסַּר מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** As quoted above, Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish agree that if one divides a limb from a living animal before placing it in his mouth, he is not liable for eating it. The Gemara adds: **But Rabbi Elazar says: Even** if one **divided** the limb **outside** his mouth he is **liable.** This is because the fact that the two pieces are **lacking** in **proximity** to each other as they are placed in one's mouth is **not comparable to lacking an action,** i.e., it is not comparable to a case where he ate only half an olive-bulk. Since he ate an entire olive-bulk, he is liable.

Rabbi Eliezer takes an even more permissive position than the two principals: *afilu chilko mibachutz nami chayav* — even if one divided the limb outside the mouth, one is still liable. Rabbi Eliezer does not accept the premise that dividing the limb before eating is a meaningful interruption. His principle is *mechussar keriva lav kimechussar ma'aseh dami* — the fact that the food still needs to be brought to the mouth (is *mechussar keriva*, lacking proximity) does not constitute a lack of an act. Cutting the limb on one's plate, in the process of preparing to eat it, is part of the eating process itself. Whether Rabbi Eliezer would extend this to a case where the limb was divided much earlier, not as part of the current act of eating, is not addressed.

## Combining Amounts

### Food Stuck

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כְּזַיִת שֶׁאָמְרוּ – חוּץ מִשֶּׁל בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף עִם בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** The **olive-bulk** of **which** the Sages **spoke** with regard to prohibitions involving eating is measured by the food one actually swallows, **aside from** the food that remains stuck **between the teeth. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it includes **even** the food that remains stuck **between the teeth.**

The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish generates further implications for the question of how a *kezayis* is measured. Reish Lakish states a general principle: the *kezayis* that the Torah requires for the prohibition of eating excludes food that is stuck between one's teeth (*bein hashinayim*). One cannot count food lodged in one's teeth toward the *kezayis* measure of eating. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: even food stuck between the teeth counts.

### Palate Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּשֶׁל בֵּין שִׁינַּיִם – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בֵּין הַחֲנִיכַיִם. מָר סָבַר: הֲרֵי נֶהֱנָה גְּרוֹנוֹ בִּכְזַיִת, וּמָר סָבַר: אֲכִילָה בְּמֵעָיו בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** In explanation of this dispute, **Rav Pappa says:** With regard to food **that** remains stuck **between** the **teeth, everyone agrees** that it is not included in measuring an olive-bulk that would render one liable to receive lashes. **When they disagree** it is with regard to food that remains **on the palate,** which one tastes but does not swallow. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that since **his throat derives pleasure from an olive-bulk,** i.e., he tastes the full olive-bulk, he is liable. **And** one **Sage,** Reish Lakish, **holds** that in order to be liable, **we require** an act of **eating** that contains the requisite amount, i.e., an olive-bulk, when it enters **his stomach.**

Rav Pappa intervenes to clarify the precise terms of the dispute. When food is actually lodged and stuck in one's teeth — truly caught between them — everyone agrees it does not count toward the *kezayis*. No one argues that chewing food in one's mouth, as such, constitutes the act of eating.

The real dispute, clarifies the Gemara, is about food between the *chanichayim* — a term referring to the region at the back of the mouth near the throat, where Rashi glosses that it is *samuch leveit habeli'ah*, close to the opening of the throat, and the place where *gerono neheneh mimmennu* — the throat derives its benefit from the food. When food reaches this region, Rabbi Yochanan holds that the throat has already received a *kezayis* of benefit and the act of eating is considered accomplished. Reish Lakish continues to maintain that *achilah beme'ayim ba'inan* — the food must reach the stomach.

### Half + Half Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָכַל חֲצִי זַיִת וֶהֱקִיאוֹ, וְחָזַר וְאָכַל חֲצִי זַיִת אַחֵר – חַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? הֲרֵי נֶהֱנָה גְּרוֹנוֹ בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one **ate half an olive-bulk** of a forbidden food **and vomited it, and then ate another half an olive-bulk,** he is **liable. What is the reason?** It is **because his throat derives pleasure from an olive-bulk** of the forbidden food, even though the full olive-bulk did not actually enter his stomach.

The practical question now becomes acute. A statement is cited: Rabbi Assi said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — one who ate half a *kezayis*, regurgitated it, and then ate another half *kezayis*, transgresses the prohibition, even though no full *kezayis* ever remained in the stomach. The basis of this ruling is *harei neheneh grono bikezayis* — a full *kezayis* passed through the throat, providing the throat its benefit, even though what reached the stomach at any given moment was never a full *kezayis*.

### Rav Assi Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַבִּי אַסִּי: אָכַל חֲצִי זַיִת וֶהֱקִיאוֹ וְחָזַר וַאֲכָלוֹ, מַהוּ? מַאי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אִי הָוֵי עִיכּוּל אִי לָא הָוֵי עִיכּוּל, וְתִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ כְּזַיִת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma before Rabbi Asi:** If one **ate half an olive-bulk** of forbidden food **and vomited it, and then ate it** again, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara clarifies: **What is the dilemma he is raising? If** it is about whether the half-olive-bulk that he ate and vomited up **is** considered to have been **digested,** in which case it is no longer considered food, **or whether it is not** considered to have been **digested, let him raise the dilemma** with regard to an entire **olive-bulk.** If one eats an entire olive-bulk and vomits it and then eats it again, if the food is considered not to have been digested the first time, he is liable to be flogged twice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִי בָּתַר גְּרוֹנוֹ אָזְלִינַן, אִי בָּתַר מֵעָיו אָזְלִינַן. וְתִפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי אַסִּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** his dilemma must be about **whether we follow the throat or whether we follow the stomach** in measuring how much forbidden food one has swallowed. That being the case, **let him resolve** the dilemma **from** that **which Rabbi Asi** said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said, which indicates that we follow the throat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַסִּי גְּמָרֵיהּ אִיעֲקַר לֵיהּ, וַאֲתָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְאַדְכּוֹרֵיהּ, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: לְמָה לִי חֲצִי זַיִת אַחֵר? לֵימָא מָר בְּדִידֵיהּ, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי: שָׁמְעִינַן מִינַּהּ דְּלָא הָוֵי עִיכּוּל, וְשָׁמְעִינַן מִינַּהּ דַּהֲרֵי נֶהֱנָה גְּרוֹנוֹ בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that Rabbi Elazar knew the answer to his question, but **Rabbi Asi forgot the statement** that he had learned from Rabbi Yoḥanan, **and Rabbi Elazar came to remind him** of what he had known previously. **And this is what** Rabbi Elazar was **saying to him: Why do I** need the case where he swallows **another half an olive-bulk? Let the Master** teach this ruling **in** a case where he swallows **the same** half-olive-bulk he had swallowed previously and vomited, **as two** principles **can be derived from** the ruling in **that** case: **We** can **learn from it that** the food **was not** considered to have been **digested** the first time he swallowed it, **and we** can **learn from it that** since **his throat derives pleasure from** a full **olive-bulk,** he is liable.

This statement attributed to Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan makes a striking point. It implies that the act of eating is not defined by what the stomach retains, but by what the throat processes. At the same time, one should not misread this as Rabbi Yochanan maintaining that food need never reach the stomach at all. The nuanced reading of Rabbi Yochanan's position is that the process of eating begins — and its character is determined — in the mouth and throat, but it still requires that the food actually travel down the throat toward and into the stomach. Rabbi Yochanan is not saying that chewing and spitting out would suffice. What he is saying is that the stomach need not retain the food; the act is complete once the throat has processed a *kezayis* on its way to the stomach.

### R. Elazar Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתִּיק וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מוֹפֵת הַדּוֹר, לָא זִימְנִין סַגִּיאִין אָמְרַתְּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמַר לָךְ: הֲרֵי נֶהֱנָה גְּרוֹנוֹ בִּכְזַיִת?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Asi **was silent and did not say anything.** Rabbi Elazar **said to him: Wonder of the generation, did you not say** this case **many times before Rabbi Yoḥanan, and he said to you:** This person is liable **because his throat derives pleasure from** a full **olive-bulk?**

Rabbi Elazar posed a question to Rav Assi: one who ate half a *kezayis*, regurgitated it, and then ate that same half *kezayis* again — does one transgress? At first glance, the Gemara asks what exactly this question is probing: is the issue whether food that hits the stomach is halachically considered already digested — so that regurgitated food is no longer food — or is it the throat-versus-stomach debate itself? The Gemara notes that if the sole question were whether regurgitated food retains its status as food, one could have asked a simpler case involving a full *kezayis* rather than using the more complex half-and-half scenario.

The Gemara therefore concludes that Rabbi Elazar was not genuinely inquiring but was gently reminding Rav Assi of a teaching Rav Assi himself had once said — and apparently forgotten due to age. Rav Assi's own original formulation had used the case of the same half *kezayis* re-eaten, because that version contains two distinct *chiddushim* simultaneously: first, that regurgitated food is not considered halachically digested and retains its status as food; and second, that one may transgress based on what the throat processes, even without a full *kezayis* remaining in the stomach. The version transmitted by Rav Assi using a different second half *kezayis* makes only one of these points. Rabbi Elazar was prompting him to recall that his own teaching captured both points in a single case.

Rav Assi fell silent. Rabbi Elazar then addressed him: *mofle hador* — wonder of our generation — have you not many times said this very thing before Rabbi Yochanan? And Rabbi Yochanan responded to you: *harei neheneh grono bikezayis* — the throat's receipt of a *kezayis* is what matters, and one transgresses.

## Yom Kippur Relevance

### Both Require Stomach

The *perek* concludes, and the concluding words of *hadran alach Gid Hanasheh* are recited. Before moving on, however, several practical implications for Yom Kippur deserve attention.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** [Conclusion of the tractate]

The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is not, ultimately, one of stark opposites. The more nuanced reading of Rabbi Yochanan is that eating is defined by what the throat processes and the benefit the throat derives — but it nonetheless requires that the food travel through the throat to the stomach. Rabbi Yochanan is not claiming that the act of eating is complete the moment food touches the palate, if the food never reaches the stomach at all. The half *kezayis* case confirms this: the food did reach the stomach; it simply did not stay there. Similarly, Reish Lakish, for whom eating is focused on the stomach, still requires that the food reach the stomach via the throat. It is not sufficient, even for Reish Lakish, for food to arrive in the stomach by some other route.

### Chasam Sofer

The Chasam Sofer raises an additional consideration specific to Yom Kippur. The prohibition of Yom Kippur is not formulated as a simple prohibition of eating; it is expressed as *ve'inisem et nafshoteichem* — you shall afflict your souls. This phrasing means that the relevant standard is not merely the act of ingestion but the deprivation of nourishment and the satisfaction that eating provides. Accordingly, the measure for transgression on Yom Kippur is not a *kezayis* but a *kekoseves* — a larger quantity, the size of a large date — reflecting that the prohibition requires an amount sufficient to constitute genuine satisfaction.

The Chasam Sofer argues that on Yom Kippur even Rabbi Yochanan would concede that the food must end up in the stomach. The ruling follows Rabbi Yochanan's position, but on Yom Kippur, unlike for standard food prohibitions, it is not enough that the throat processed the food; the food must actually provide nourishment by reaching and remaining in the stomach. Eating half a *kezayis*, regurgitating it, and eating another half — while this would transgress a standard food prohibition under Rabbi Yochanan's ruling — would not constitute the affliction-negating act of eating for Yom Kippur, since no full measure of nourishing food reached and remained in the stomach.

### IV/Tubes

A final and practically significant application concerns intravenous nutrition and feeding tubes. What if a person receives nutrients directly into the bloodstream through an IV, or receives food through a tube that bypasses the throat and delivers it directly to the stomach? According to virtually all *poskim*, neither modality constitutes eating for purposes of Yom Kippur — or, for that matter, for any food prohibition.

This conclusion holds across both positions. For Reish Lakish, who focuses on the stomach, one might have thought that direct delivery to the stomach would suffice. But the consensus of the authorities is that even Reish Lakish requires the food to reach the stomach *through the throat* — the act of eating is not merely about the destination but about the process, specifically the act of swallowing through the throat. An IV that delivers nutrients directly to the bloodstream, without ever passing through the digestive tract, clearly involves no act of eating. A feeding tube that bypasses the throat likewise does not constitute eating. In the end, both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish require the throat's involvement — they differ only on whether the throat's benefit alone suffices (Rabbi Yochanan) or whether the food must ultimately settle in the stomach (Reish Lakish, and Rabbi Yochanan on Yom Kippur per the Chasam Sofer). On the common ground — that the throat must be traversed — all agree, and from that ground, the conclusion regarding IV feeding and tubes follows directly.
