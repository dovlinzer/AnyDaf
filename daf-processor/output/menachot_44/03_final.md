# Menachot 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lavan vs. Techelet

### White String Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: גָּדוֹל עוֹנְשׁוֹ שֶׁל לָבָן יוֹתֵר מֵעוֹנְשׁוֹ שֶׁל תְּכֵלֶת, מָשָׁל לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁאָמַר לִשְׁנֵי עֲבָדָיו, לְאֶחָד אָמַר: ״הָבֵא לִי חוֹתָם שֶׁל טִיט״, וּלְאֶחָד אָמַר: ״הָבֵא לִי חוֹתָם שֶׁל זָהָב״, וּפָשְׁעוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם וְלֹא הֵבִיאוּ, אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן עוֹנְשׁוֹ מְרוּבֶּה? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ ״הָבֵא לִי חוֹתָם שֶׁל טִיט״ וְלֹא הֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught in a baraita that Rabbi Meir would say:** The **punishment for** not attaching **white** strings is **greater than the punishment for** not attaching **sky-blue** strings, despite the fact that the sky-blue strings are more important. Rabbi Meir illustrates this with **a parable: To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a king of flesh and blood who said to his two subjects** that they must bring him a seal. The king **said to one** of them: **Bring me a seal of clay, and he said to** the other **one: Bring me a seal of gold. And both of them were negligent and did not bring** the seals. **Which of them** will have **a greater punishment? You must say** that it is **this** one **to whom he said: Bring me a seal of clay, and** despite its availability and low cost, he **did not bring** it.

The daf opens with a *tanya*: *hayah Rabbi Meir omer gadol onsho shel lavan yoter me'onsho shel techelet* — the punishment for neglecting the white strings of *tzitzit* is greater than the punishment for neglecting the blue strings. The reasoning is straightforward: the white strings are far easier to obtain and affix than the *techelet*, and therefore one's blameworthiness for neglecting them is correspondingly greater. When transgression requires no effort at all to avoid, negligence is all the more culpable.

### King's Seal Parable

To what can this be compared? *L'melech basar v'dam* — to a human king who instructs two of his servants to bring him a seal. To one he says: bring me a seal of plaster. To the other: bring me a seal of gold. Both are negligent and fail to deliver. Who deserves the greater punishment? Clearly the one who was asked for the plaster seal, since his task was so much simpler. The *mashal* may not be strictly necessary to grasp the underlying logic — that *techelet* is harder and thus its neglect is more understandable — but it contributes something beyond mere illustration.

### Identity & Belonging

These seals, as the parable makes vivid, were worn by animals or slaves to indicate to whom they belonged. The *tzitzit*, the Gemara thus implies, function similarly: they are markers of identity, signs of belonging to God. The slave bears the king's seal; the Jew wears *tzitzit*. This transforms *tzitzit* from a ritual garment detail into a statement of identity and allegiance.

## Hundred Blessings

### Hundred Blessings Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְבָרֵךְ מֵאָה בְּרָכוֹת בְּכׇל יוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְעַתָּה יִשְׂרָאֵל מָה ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ שֹׁאֵל מֵעִמָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught in a baraita that Rabbi Meir would say: A person is obligated to recite one hundred blessings every day, as it is stated** in the verse: **"And now, Israel, what [*ma*] does the Lord your God require of you"** (Deuteronomy 10:12). Rabbi Meir interprets the verse as though it said one hundred [*me'a*], rather than *ma*.

The Gemara continues with another *tanya hayah Rabbi Meir omer*, a second teaching of Rabbi Meir in a series: *chayav adam l'varech me'ah berachos bechol yom* — a person is obligated to recite one hundred *berachos* every day.

### Me'ah Reinterpretation

The source is a *pasuk* in Devarim: *ve'ata Yisrael mah Hashem Elokecha sho'el me'imach* — "And now, Israel, what does the Lord your God ask of you?" Rashi explains, and this is the most widely accepted reading, that one should not read *mah* — "what" — but *me'ah* — "one hundred." God asks of you one hundred *berachos*.

What justifies such a reinterpretation of the *pasuk*? The Maharsha offers a beautiful explanation. The Gemara elsewhere struggles with the apparent triviality implied by *mah* — "what does God ask of you?" — as if it were a small thing, when the continuation of the verse lists a demanding program: *ki im l'yirah et Hashem Elokecha*, to fear God, to walk in His ways, to love Him, to observe all His commandments. That is an enormous ask, not a modest one.

### Maharsha's Teaching

This tension pushes Rabbi Meir to read the verse differently. And the Maharsha adds a second, equally beautiful dimension: by reading *me'ah* — one hundred *berachos* — the verse itself supplies the answer to how one achieves *ki im l'yirah et Hashem Elokecha*. It is through reciting *berachos* throughout the day — in the *Shemoneh Esrei* petitioning God for our needs, over food, over the performance of *mitzvos* — that we bring God into our consciousness at every turn. The hundred *berachos* are not an arbitrary number but the very mechanism by which *yirat Shamayim* is cultivated. *Tosafos* adds a further observation: the word *sho'el* spelled with a *vav* yields exactly one hundred letters in the *pasuk* — a striking textual corroboration.

### Blessings on Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַוְיָא, בְּשַׁבְּתָא וּבְיוֹמֵי טָבֵי, טָרַח וּמְמַלֵּי לְהוּ בְּאִיסְפַּרְמָקֵי וּמַגְדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **on Shabbat and Festivals,** when the prayers contain fewer blessings, **Rav Ḥiyya, son of Rav Avya, made an effort to fill** this quota of blessings **with** blessings on **spices [*be'isparmakei*] and sweet fruit,** of which he would partake in order to recite extra blessings.

On a regular weekday, reaching one hundred *berachos* is not difficult. Three *Shemoneh Esreis* of nineteen *berachos* each yields fifty-seven; add *Birchas HaTorah*, *berachos hashachar*, *birchas krias shema*, *bentching*, and the number is readily reached. The challenge arises on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, when the *Shemoneh Esrei* has only seven *berachos* rather than nineteen. The Gemara records that Rabbi Chiya the son of Rav Avya, on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* — *b'Shabso u'vyomei Tovei* — would go to the effort of filling up the hundred *berachos* with fragrances and delicacies: eating many different fruits, smelling various *besamim*, each generating its own *bracha*. This practice of smelling snuff on *Shabbos* to accumulate *berachos* was well known in later generations, and *Yom Kippur* presents a similar challenge since one cannot even rely on food *berachos*.

## Three Identity Blessings

### Identity Blessings List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְבָרֵךְ שָׁלֹשׁ בְּרָכוֹת בְּכׇל יוֹם, אֵלוּ הֵן: ״שֶׁלֹּא עֲשָׂאַנִי גּוֹי״, ״שֶׁלֹּא עֲשָׂאַנִי אִשָּׁה״, ״שֶׁלֹּא עֲשָׂאַנִי בּוּר״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught in a baraita that Rabbi Meir would say: A man is obligated to recite three blessings every day** praising God for His kindnesses, and **these** blessings **are: Who did not make me a gentile; Who did not make me a woman;** and **Who did not make me an ignoramus.**

A third *tanya hayah Rabbi Meir omer* follows — though it should be noted that in the *Tosefta* the text reads *Rabbi Yehuda omer*; it is cited here in the sequence of Rabbi Meir's teachings: *chayav adam l'varech shalosh berachos bechol yom* — a person is obligated to recite three *berachos* of identity each day. The Gemara then enumerates them.

### Censorship & Phrasing

The first blessing, as the footnote to our text makes clear, was originally *shelo asani goy* — "who did not make me a non-Jew." The formulation *she'asani Yisrael* — "who made me a Jew" — is a product of censorship, adopted in times when communities engaged in self-censorship of the Talmud to avoid provoking Christian authorities who might take offense at an explicit negative formulation about non-Jews.

This raises a natural question: why not simply say it in the positive permanently? There are two difficulties. First, the three *berachos* form a descending gradation — *shelo asani goy*, *shelo asani eved*, *shelo asani isha* — and the negative formulation allows each to remain meaningful in relation to the preceding one. A *goy* is not a slave, but one can still be grateful not to be a slave even after being grateful not to be a *goy*. If one says *she'asani Yisrael*, it renders the next *bracha* — *she'asani ben chorin* — superfluous, since being Jewish already implies freedom; the order would need to be reversed. Second, and more substantively, there is a reason the *shelo asani* formulation was chosen in the first place. We often come to appreciate the gifts we have by reflecting on what it would mean not to possess them. The negative phrasing focuses attention on what one might have lacked and thereby deepens the gratitude.

It is also worth noting that these *berachos* closely parallel a sentiment attributed to Thales, the Greek philosopher, who expressed gratitude to Fortune for three things: that he was born human and not animal, man and not woman, and Greek and not barbarian. It is notable that a similar triad of identity-gratitude already existed in pagan Hellenic culture.

### Women & Mitzvot

The second *bracha* is *shelo asani isha* — "who did not make me a woman." This reflects the general rabbinic assumption — which need not come as a surprise to anyone — that men bear a greater obligation in *mitzvos* than women, and that this difference in obligation was understood in the *Gemara* as constituting a kind of hierarchy of religious status. Rav Moshe Feinstein addresses this directly in a well-known *teshuvah*, making clear that this difference in function carries no implication of a different level of *kedushah* — *chas v'shalom* — and that the *kedushah* of every Jew is equal. Nevertheless, the *pshat* assumption of the *Gemara* that men have a higher degree of obligation is plainly present in the text.

### Modern Interpretations

There is a great deal of contemporary literature on the *bracha* of *shelo asani isha* and how communities relate to it today. Some *shuls* have shifted to a positive formulation, though as noted this creates its own structural difficulties with the sequence of blessings. Others have chosen not to recite these *berachos* aloud in communal prayer, out of sensitivity to the pain they can cause. One interpretive approach that has been offered is to read the *bracha* not as an expression of gratitude for social advantage, but as a form of social critique: a recognition that even today men generally navigate society with fewer obstacles than women, and that rather than simply feeling grateful for those advantages, the *bracha* ought to sensitize us to the unfairness that persists and call upon us to address it. A parallel reading might apply to *shelo asani eved* — a recognition that slavery still exists in various forms in the world, and that the *bracha* should mobilize us to eradicate it. There is, needless to say, an extensive literature on all of this.

### Ignoramus Blessing Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב שַׁמְעֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא מְבָרֵךְ ״שֶׁלֹּא עֲשָׂאַנִי בּוּר״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כּוּלֵּי הַאי נָמֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאֶלָּא מַאי מְבָרֵךְ? ״שֶׁלֹּא עֲשָׂאַנִי עֶבֶד״? הַיְינוּ אִשָּׁה! עֶבֶד זִיל טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov heard his son reciting the blessing: Who did not make me an ignoramus.** Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov **said to him:** Is it **in fact** proper to go **this far** in reciting blessings? Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov's son **said to him: Rather, what blessing** should one **recite?** If you will say that one should recite: **Who did not make me a slave, that is** the same as **a woman;** why should one recite two blessings about the same matter? Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov answered: Nevertheless, **a slave** is **more lowly** than a woman, and therefore it is appropriate to recite an additional blessing on not having been born a slave.

The third *bracha*, as it appears in the original *baraita*, is *shelo asani bor* — "who did not make me an ignoramus." The Gemara then records: Rav Acha bar Yaakov heard his son reciting this *bracha* and challenged him: *kulei hai nami?* — you're going that far? Rashi offers one reading of the challenge: who says you're not a *bor*? Don't be so certain of your own learning. But a more incisive explanation is also possible. The *shelo asani* framework is predicated on circumstances of birth — conditions into which one is born and which are entirely outside one's control. Being a non-Jew, a woman, or a slave are statuses one is born into. But whether one becomes a *talmid chacham* or remains an ignoramus is, to a significant degree, within one's own control. One cannot appropriately thank God for the result of one's own choices — or failures — in the same way one thanks God for the natural circumstances of one's birth.

### Slave Blessing

Rav Acha bar Yaakov's son then asks: so what *bracha* should I say instead? The answer given is *shelo asani eved* — "who did not make me a slave." A person may be born into slavery or captured and enslaved against their will; either way, it is not a condition they chose. The Gemara then anticipates an objection: *hainu isha* — is that not already covered by *shelo asani isha*, since the reason we recite that *bracha* is the lesser degree of *mitzvah* obligation, and a slave's obligations are similarly limited? The Gemara responds: *eved zil tefei* — a slave's social condition is nonetheless lower than that of a woman. The obligations may overlap, but the statuses are distinct, and the *bracha* over being free from slavery speaks to a different dimension of existence than the *bracha* over being a man rather than a woman.

## Chilazon Identification

### Chilazon Properties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חִלָּזוֹן זֶה, גּוּפוֹ דּוֹמֶה לַיָּם, וּבְרִיָּיתוֹ דּוֹמָה לְדָג, וְעוֹלֶה אֶחָד לְשִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, וּבְדָמוֹ צוֹבְעִין תְּכֵלֶת, לְפִיכָךְ דָּמָיו יְקָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: This *ḥilazon*,** which **is** the source of the sky-blue dye used in ritual fringes, has the following characteristics: **Its body resembles the sea, its form resembles** that of **a fish, it emerges once in seventy years, and with its blood one dyes** wool **sky-blue** for ritual fringes. It is scarce, and **therefore it is expensive.**

At the top of 44a, the Gemara turns to the source of the *techelet* dye. *Tanu Rabbanan*: the *chilazon* — *chilazon zehu* — *gufo domeh layam*: its body resembles the sea. The *chilazon* is understood today to be a type of snail, though the animal itself is not blue. The description *gufo domeh layam* is therefore understood to refer to the color of the dye extracted from it, which resembles the color of the ocean. *U'vriato domeh ledag* — it is created similarly to a fish, a description that led the Radziner Rebbe to conclude it was a type of fish rather than a snail; the phrase may refer to the creature living inside the shell. *V'oleh achas leshivim shanah* — it surfaces or washes ashore only once every seventy years.

### Techelet Expense

*U'bidamo tzovin techelet* — its blood is used to produce the *techelet* dye. *Lefichach damav yekarim* — and therefore, because of its extreme rarity and the difficulty of obtaining it, it is extraordinarily expensive. This contextualizes the earlier ruling of Rabbi Meir: the inaccessibility of *techelet* diminishes, though does not eliminate, culpability for its neglect; the ready availability of the white strings makes neglecting them far less excusable.

## Reward for Mitzvot

### Rabbi Natan's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן: אֵין לָךְ כׇּל מִצְוָה קַלָּה שֶׁכְּתוּבָה בְּתוֹרָה, שֶׁאֵין מַתַּן שְׂכָרָהּ בְּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וְלָעוֹלָם הַבָּא אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ כַּמָּה. צֵא וּלְמַד מִמִּצְוַת צִיצִית.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught in a baraita that Rabbi Natan says: There is no mitzva,** however **minor, that is written in the Torah, for which there is no reward given in this world; and in the World-to-Come I do not know how much** reward is given. **Go and learn from** the following incident concerning **the mitzva of ritual fringes.**

*Tanya amar Rabbi Natan*: Rabbi Natan teaches the governing principle that frames what follows. *Ein lecha kol mitzvah kalah shebatorah she'ein matan secharah ba'olam hazeh ule'olam haba* — there is no *mitzvah* in the Torah, even the most trivial and easiest to perform, that does not carry reward both in this world and in the world to come. The precise formulation varies across manuscript traditions: one reading emphasizes that there is both *olam hazeh* reward and *olam haba* reward, while another reading — which appears to be the more correct one — emphasizes that we know there is reward in this world, *kamah*, some quantity, but as for the world to come, *eini yodea kamah*, the reward is so vast it cannot even be estimated.

### Tzitzit Story Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה זָהִיר בְּמִצְוַת צִיצִית, שָׁמַע שֶׁיֵּשׁ זוֹנָה בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם שֶׁנּוֹטֶלֶת אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זְהוּבִים בִּשְׂכָרָהּ, שִׁיגֵּר לָהּ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זְהוּבִים וְקָבַע לָהּ זְמַן, כְּשֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנּוֹ, בָּא וְיָשַׁב עַל הַפֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** There was **an incident involving a certain man who was diligent about the mitzva of ritual fringes.** This man **heard that there was a prostitute in** one of **the cities overseas who took four hundred gold coins as her payment. He sent her four hundred gold coins and fixed a time to** meet with **her. When his time came, he came and sat at the entrance** to her house.

*Tze ulemad mimitzvas tzitzit* — go and learn from the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit*. There follows one of the most famous *aggadic* passages in the Talmud. *Ma'aseh be'adam echad shehayah zahir bemitzvas tzitzit* — there was a man who was scrupulous in the performance of *tzitzit*, though he was not without other weaknesses. He heard that there was a certain prostitute in the distant isles of the sea who charged four hundred gold coins for her services.

### Seven Beds Scene

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְנְסָה שִׁפְחָתָהּ וְאָמְרָה לָהּ: אוֹתוֹ אָדָם שֶׁשִּׁיגֵּר לִיךְ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זְהוּבִים בָּא וְיָשַׁב עַל הַפֶּתַח. אָמְרָה הִיא: יִכָּנֵס. נִכְנַס, הִצִּיעָה לוֹ שֶׁבַע מִטּוֹת, שֵׁשׁ שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְאַחַת שֶׁל זָהָב, וּבֵין כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת סוּלָּם שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, וְעֶלְיוֹנָה שֶׁל זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** **The maidservant of** that prostitute **entered and said to her: That man who sent you four hundred gold coins came and sat at the entrance. She said: Let him enter. He entered. She arranged seven beds for him, six of silver and one of gold. Between each and every one** of them there was **a ladder** made **of silver, and the top** bed was the one that was made **of gold.**

He sent her the four hundred *zuzim* and arranged an appointment. When the appointed day arrived, he went and waited at her door. Her maidservant entered and informed her that the man who had sent the four hundred *zuzim* had arrived. The prostitute said: let him enter. He entered. She had prepared for him seven beds — *shesh shel kesef ve'achas shel zahav* — six of silver and one of gold, arranged in ascending order with silver ladders between each level and a golden ladder leading to the uppermost bed, building anticipation and effort step by step. It is hard to avoid noticing that the seven beds and the six spaces between them echo, in an inverted fashion, the seven heavenly *rekiyos* and the six spaces between them — the same numbers that inform the thirteen wrappings of the *tzitzit*. The parallel seems intentional, a dark reflection of the sacred structure in a context of transgression.

### Tzitziot Slap Him

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלְתָה וְיָשְׁבָה עַל גַּבֵּי עֶלְיוֹנָה כְּשֶׁהִיא עֲרוּמָּה, וְאַף הוּא עָלָה לֵישֵׁב עָרוֹם כְּנֶגְדָּהּ, בָּאוּ אַרְבַּע צִיצִיּוֹתָיו וְטָפְחוּ לוֹ עַל פָּנָיו, נִשְׁמַט וְיָשַׁב לוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, וְאַף הִיא נִשְׁמְטָה וְיָשְׁבָה עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **She went up and sat naked on the top** bed, **and he too went up** in order **to sit naked facing her.** In the meantime, **his four ritual fringes came and slapped him on his face. He dropped down and sat himself on the ground, and she also dropped down and sat on the ground.**

She ascended to the topmost golden bed and waited for him, unclothed. He too began to climb and was in the process of disrobing when — *bau arba tzitziosav vetapchu lo al panav* — his four *tzitziyos* came and struck him in the face. Whether this occurred as the *tzitzit* swung free as he was climbing, or as he removed the garment, the effect was the same. He was overcome: the spell of his desire was broken, his more rational and responsible self reasserted itself. *Nishmat veyashav lo al gabi karka* — he slipped down and sat on the floor. She, seeing he was not ascending, descended as well and sat beside him on the ground.

### Man Explains Tzitzit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לוֹ: גַּפָּהּ שֶׁל רוֹמִי, שֶׁאֵינִי מַנִּיחָתְךָ עַד שֶׁתֹּאמַר לִי מָה מוּם רָאִיתָ בִּי. אָמַר לָהּ: הָעֲבוֹדָה, שֶׁלֹּא רָאִיתִי אִשָּׁה יָפֶה כְּמוֹתֵךְ, אֶלָּא מִצְוָה אַחַת צִיוָּנוּ ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ וְצִיצִית שְׁמָהּ, וּכְתִיב בָּהּ: ״אֲנִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ שְׁתֵּי פְעָמִים – אֲנִי הוּא שֶׁעָתִיד לִיפָּרַע, וַאֲנִי הוּא שֶׁעָתִיד לְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר. עַכְשָׁיו נִדְמוּ עָלַי כְּאַרְבָּעָה עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **She said to him:** I take an oath by the ***gappa* of Rome that I will not allow you** to go **until you tell me what defect you saw in me.** **He said to her:** I take an oath by **the Temple** service **that I never saw a woman as beautiful as you. But** there is **one mitzva** that **the Lord, our God, commanded us, and its name is ritual fringes, and in** the passage where **it** is commanded, **it is written twice: "I am the Lord your God"** (Numbers 15:41). The doubling of this phrase indicates: **I am the one who will punish** those who transgress My mitzvot, **and I am the one who will reward** those who fulfill them. **Now,** said the man, the four sets of ritual fringes **appeared to me as** if they were **four witnesses** who will testify against me.

She said to him: *gapah shel Romi* — by the god of Rome (as Rashi understands *gapah*) — I will not release you until you tell me what blemish you found in me that caused you to reject me. He said to her: *ha'avodah* — in truth, I have never seen a woman as beautiful as you. But God has commanded us in one *mitzvah*, and it is called *tzitzit*, and it says twice in that *mitzvah*, *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* — in the final verse, *Ani Hashem Elokeichem asher hotzeiti etchem me'eretz Mitzrayim lihyot lachem l'Elokim Ani Hashem Elokeichem*. The repeated invocation of God's name underscores that *tzitzit* is meant to keep us cognizant of God and our obligations to Him.

It is worth noting — though the man himself does not quote it — that the very *parasha* of *tzitzit* contains the phrase *v'lo taturu acharei levavchem v'acharei eineichem asher atem zonim achareihem* — you shall not stray after your hearts and after your eyes after which you go astray. The words *zonim*, desire, fornication, vision, longing — are woven directly into the text of *tzitzit*. The *mitzvah* is explicitly designed to prevent precisely the situation this man found himself in.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לוֹ: אֵינִי (מניחך) [מַנִּיחָתְךָ] עַד שֶׁתֹּאמַר לִי מָה שִׁמְךָ, וּמָה שֵׁם עִירְךָ, וּמָה שֵׁם רַבְּךָ, וּמָה שֵׁם מִדְרָשְׁךָ שֶׁאַתָּה לָמֵד בּוֹ תּוֹרָה. כָּתַב וְנָתַן בְּיָדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **She said to him: I will not allow you** to go **until you tell me: What is your name, and what is the name of your city, and what is the name of your teacher, and what is the name of the study hall in which you studied Torah? He wrote** the information **and placed** it **in her hand.**

He continued: the words *Ani Hashem Elokeichem* serve as a reminder. *Ani hu she'atid l'hipara* — I am the One who will exact punishment from transgressors. *Ani hu she'atid l'shalem sachar* — and I am the One who will reward those who fulfill the commandments. And now, he said, these four *tzitziyos* appeared to me as four witnesses — two to testify regarding punishment for transgression, two to testify regarding reward for fulfillment.

### Woman Converts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמְדָה וְחִילְּקָהּ כׇּל נְכָסֶיהָ, שְׁלִישׁ לַמַּלְכוּת, וּשְׁלִישׁ לַעֲנִיִּים, וּשְׁלִישׁ נָטְלָה בְּיָדָהּ, חוּץ מֵאוֹתָן מַצָּעוֹת. וּבָאת לְבֵית מִדְרָשׁוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי חִיָּיא. אָמְרָה לוֹ: רַבִּי, צַוֵּה עָלַי וְיַעֲשׂוּנִי גִּיּוֹרֶת. אָמַר לָהּ: בִּתִּי, שֶׁמָּא עֵינַיִךְ נָתַתְּ בְּאֶחָד מִן הַתַּלְמִידִים. הוֹצִיאָהּ כְּתָב מִיָּדָהּ וְנָתְנָה לוֹ. אָמַר לָהּ: לְכִי זְכִי בְּמִקָּחִךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **She arose and divided all of her property,** giving **one-third** as a bribe **to the government, one-third to the poor, and she took one-third** with her **in her possession, in addition to those beds** of gold and silver. **She came to the study hall of Rabbi Ḥiyya** and **said to him: My teacher, instruct** your students **concerning me and** have them **make me a convert.** Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to her: My daughter, perhaps you set your sights on one of the students** and that is why you want to convert? **She took** the **note** the student had given her **from her hand and gave it to** Rabbi Ḥiyya. **He said to her: Go take possession of your purchase.**

She was so overwhelmed by this encounter, by the moral clarity and religious conviction it revealed, that she said: I will not leave you until you tell me your name, the name of your city, the name of your teacher, and the name of your *beit midrash* where you study Torah. He wrote out all of this information and placed it in her hand.

### Bedspreads Redeemed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתָן מַצָּעוֹת שֶׁהִצִּיעָה לוֹ בְּאִיסּוּר, הִצִּיעָה לוֹ בְּהֶיתֵּר. זֶה מַתַּן שְׂכָרוֹ בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וְלָעוֹלָם הַבָּא אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ כַּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Those beds that she had arranged for him in a prohibited** fashion, **she** now **arranged for him in a permitted** fashion. The Gemara completes its point about the reward of mitzvot and points out how this story illustrates the concept: **This is the reward given to him in this world, and** with regard **to the World-to-Come, I do not know how much** reward he will be given.

She then arose and distributed her property: a third to the government — Rashi explains this was necessary to secure permission to convert — a third to the poor, and a third she retained for herself. The one additional thing she did not distribute was those very bedspreads, *matza'ot*, that she had laid out for him. She came to the *beit midrash* of Rabbi Chiya and said: *Rebbi, tzavei alai v'ya'asuni giyoret* — appoint students to instruct me and make me a convert. Rabbi Chiya asked whether perhaps her eyes were set on one of the students and she was seeking conversion for the sake of marriage — a disqualifying motivation. She produced the note that the student had written and gave it to him, explaining everything that had occurred. Rabbi Chiya said to her: *lechi zechi b'mikchech* — go and take possession of your acquisition. Yes, he would convert her, and she could also marry the very man whose righteousness had so moved her.

*Otan matza'ot she-hetzi'ah lo b'issur hetzi'ah lo b'heter* — those very same bedspreads that she had spread out for him in a context of prohibition, she now spread out for him in a context of permissibility and sanctity, within the bounds of marriage. There is something resonant here in the image of the cloth itself transformed — the same bedspreads, the same physical objects, now sanctified by the changed relationship between the two people.

### Reward Fulfillment

*Zeh matan sachar ba'olam hazeh* — this is the reward in this world. The man who restrained himself from transgression was granted that same opportunity, with the same woman, in a manner fully permitted and sanctified. And *ule'olam haba eini yodea* — as for the reward in the world to come, it is simply beyond reckoning.

## Thirty Days Rule

### Borrowed Tallit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: טַלִּית שְׁאוּלָה, כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם פְּטוּרָה מִן הַצִּיצִית, מִיכָּן וְאֵילָךְ חַיֶּיבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says:** In the case of **a borrowed cloak, for** the first **thirty days it is exempt from ritual fringes; from then on** it is **obligated.**

Rav Yehuda teaches: *talit she-ulah kol shloshim yom patur min ha-tzitzit* — a borrowed *talit* is exempt from the obligation of *tzitzit* for the first thirty days. *Mi-kan ve-eilech chayav bah* — beyond thirty days, it is obligated. *Tosafos* explains that in truth a borrowed garment is always exempt *de-Oraita*, since the verse *al arba kanfot kesutcha* — upon the four corners of your garment — implies that you must own the garment; a borrowed one does not belong to you and thus falls outside the Torah obligation. Nevertheless, after thirty days of continuous use, people will assume the garment is yours, and for that reason the Rabbis obligated you to affix *tzitzit* at that point. Alternatively, after thirty days one develops a degree of relationship to the garment that approximates ownership, and the obligation attaches for that reason — even though *de-Oraita* one is still technically exempt.

### Rented House Mezuzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַדָּר בְּפוּנְדְּקִי בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ – כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם פָּטוּר מִן הַמְּזוּזָה, מִיכָּן וְאֵילָךְ חַיָּיב. אֲבָל הַשּׂוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – עוֹשֶׂה מְזוּזָה לְאַלְתַּר, מִשּׁוּם יִישּׁוּב דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **That** distinction **is also taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who resides in a guesthouse [*pundaki*] in Eretz Yisrael, or one who rents a house outside of Eretz Yisrael, for** the first **thirty days** he is **exempt from the** mitzva of ***mezuza*; from then on** he is **obligated. But one who rents a house in Eretz Yisrael** must **affix a *mezuza* immediately, due to** the **settlement of Eretz Yisrael.**

A parallel rule applies to *mezuzah*: *ha-dar be-pundak be-Eretz Yisrael ve-hasocher bayit be-chutz la-aretz kol shloshim yom patur min ha-mezuzah* — one staying at an inn in Israel or renting a house outside the land of Israel is exempt from *mezuzah* for the first thirty days; *mi-kan ve-eilech chayav*, beyond that one is obligated.

The underlying principle parallels the borrowed *talit* discussion. One view holds that renting, like borrowing, creates no *de-Oraita* obligation, since the verse says *mezuzot beitecha* — the doorposts of your house — implying ownership. After thirty days the obligation is rabbinic, based either on the appearance of ownership or on the relationship that develops to the dwelling. A second view holds that renting does in fact create a *de-Oraita* obligation, but that a rented dwelling does not become a *beit dirah* — a genuine domicile, a home — until one has settled into it, and that process of settling takes thirty days. On this view, the obligation is biblical in principle but only activates once the threshold of genuine habitation is reached. An interesting question that flows from this: if one signs a lease from the outset for more than thirty days, is one obligated immediately, or must one wait the full thirty days regardless? May one affix a *mezuzah* with a *bracha* from the first day? These questions are debated, but the simple language of the Gemara implies no obligation — and no *bracha* — until thirty days have elapsed, whatever the length of the original lease.

### Eretz Yisrael Exception

*Aval hasocher bayit be-Eretz Yisrael oseh mezuzah la-altar mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael* — but one who rents a house in the land of Israel must affix a *mezuzah* immediately, on account of settling the land of Israel. Rashi explains this as an incentive structure: requiring immediate *mezuzah* makes a person more likely to remain in the house, since one is reluctant to leave behind a *mezuzah*, and the presence of a *mezuzah* may encourage others to move in. Alternatively, *mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael* is not merely a practical incentive but a substantive halachic principle: because of the obligation to settle and inhabit the land, even a brief stay there constitutes "coming home," and the full status of dwelling attaches immediately, without any thirty-day threshold. The phrase *mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael* more naturally reads as an incentive rationale, but the substantive reading is also present in the *rishonim*.

## Tefillin Interdependence

### Hand & Head Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל יָד אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ, אֲבָל אֵין לוֹ – מְעַכֶּבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Absence of the **phylacteries of** the **arm does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of the phylacteries of the head, and absence of the phylacteries of the head does not prevent fulfillment of the mitzva of the phylacteries of the arm. **Rav Ḥisda said: They taught** this **only** in a case **where one has** the other phylacteries, but they are not with him or he is unable to wear them for some reason. **But** if **he does not have** the other phylacteries at all, then their absence **does prevent** the fulfillment of the mitzva to don the phylacteries that he has.

The Gemara moves to a brief but analytically significant discussion of *tefillin*. The *Mishna* states: *tefillah shel yad einah me'akevet* — the *tefillin shel yad* does not prevent the *tefillin shel rosh*, and vice versa. They are two separate *mitzvos*, and the absence of one does not negate the other.

### Chisda's Restriction

Rav Chisda qualifies this: *lo shanu ela she-yesh lo* — this rule that each *tefillin* stands independently applies only when one possesses both but is currently wearing only one (for whatever reason). *Aval ein lo me'akevet* — but if one does not own both, then failing to have one does prevent one from fulfilling the *mitzvah* of the other. This is a striking position. If *tefillin shel yad* and *tefillin shel rosh* are two separate *mitzvos*, why should the fact that one does not own one of them affect whether one has fulfilled the other?

### Chisda Reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אָמַרְתָּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא, אֶלָּא מַאן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ תְּרֵי מִצְוֹת, חַד מִצְוָה נָמֵי לָא לֶיעְבֵּיד?! וּמֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי סְבַר? גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִפְשַׁע.
>
> **Translation:** Later on, the students **said to him:** Do **you** still **say** that? Rav Ḥisda **said to them: No, rather** I would say the opposite: Concerning **one who does not have** the ability to fulfill **two mitzvot, should he also not perform** the **one mitzva** that he does have the ability to fulfill? The Gemara asks: **And what did he hold initially** when he said not to don one of the phylacteries in the absence of the other? The Gemara answers: He held that it was due to a rabbinic **decree, lest he be negligent** and not try to acquire the phylacteries that he lacks.

The Gemara records that when challenged — *amru lo, amarta?* — Rav Chisda reversed himself: *lo*, he denied having said it. The Gemara understands this not as a denial of having uttered the words, but as a reversal of his position. *Ela man d'leit lei trei mitzvos chada mitzvah nami lo le'avid?* — what, if someone does not have both, should he not perform even one *mitzvah*? Of course, even if one lacks the other, one is *yotzei* the *mitzvah* of whichever one does possess and wear.

### Gezerah Incentive

The Gemara then asks: *ume'ikara mai savar* — what was Rav Chisda's original reasoning? The answer is *gezeirah shema yifsha* — he initially held that position as a rabbinic *gezeirah*: if one tells people that owning only one *tefillin* suffices to fulfill at least that one *mitzvah*, there is no incentive for people to acquire the other. By telling them that without both they fulfill neither, one creates a strong incentive for full acquisition. In the end, however, the Gemara rejects this reasoning: the goal of incentivizing complete observance is not sufficient justification for telling a person not to perform the *mitzvah* available to him. The *mitzvah* at hand must be performed even without the other.

## Transgression Counts

### Eight Tefillin Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין עוֹבֵר בִּשְׁמוֹנָה עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says: Anyone who does not don phylacteries violates eight positive mitzvot.** This is referring to the mitzva to don phylacteries of the arm and head, each of which is mentioned in four different passages (Exodus 13:9; Exodus 13:16; Deuteronomy 6:8; Deuteronomy 11:18).

Rav Sheshet teaches: *kol she'eino mani'ach tefillin over bi-shmonah asei* — whoever does not put on *tefillin* transgresses eight positive commandments. The four *parshiyos* of *tefillin* each contain verses commanding their donning — *u'kshartem l'ot*, *v'hayu l'totafot*, *v'hayu l'zikaron*, *u'kshartem*, and *v'shamarta et ha-chukah ha-zot* — and when tallied, there are eight imperative verbs, four directed primarily at the *shel yad* and four at the *shel rosh*, yielding eight distinct positive commandments violated by a failure to wear *tefillin*.

### Five Tzitzit Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ צִיצִית בְּבִגְדוֹ עוֹבֵר בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And anyone who does not have ritual fringes on his garments violates five positive mitzvot.** This is because the mitzva of ritual fringes is stated four times in the primary passage concerning ritual fringes in Numbers: "That they prepare for themselves strings…and they shall put on the fringe of the corner a sky-blue thread. And it shall be to you for a fringe that you may look upon it and remember all the commandments of the Lord" (Numbers 15:38–39). An additional command appears in the verse: "You shall prepare yourself twisted cords" (Deuteronomy 22:12).

*V'chol she'ein lo tzitzit b'vigdo over b'chamisha asei* — one who has a garment that is obligated in *tzitzit* but fails to affix them transgresses five positive commandments. Rashi, in the final comment on the page, lists them: *v'asu* — they shall make *tzitzit*; *v'natnu* — they shall place the *techelet*; *v'haya lachem l'tzitzit* — it shall be *tzitzit* for you; *u're'item* — you shall see them; and *u'gdilim ta'aseh lach* — you shall make twisted cords. Five *aseis* violated by the failure to attach *tzitzit* to an obligated garment.

### Two Mezuzah Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹלֶה לַדּוּכָן, עוֹבֵר בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And any priest who does not ascend the platform** to recite the Priestly Benediction **violates three positive mitzvot** expressed in the verses: "So you shall bless the children of Israel; you shall say to them" (Numbers 6:23), and: "And they shall put My name upon the children of Israel" (Numbers 6:27).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מְזוּזָה בְּפִתְחוֹ עוֹבֵר בִּשְׁנֵי עֲשֵׂה, ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Anyone who does not have a *mezuza* in his doorway violates two positive mitzvot,** stated in the verses: **"And you shall write them** on the doorposts of your house" (Deuteronomy 6:9), and: **"And you shall write them** on the doorposts of your house" (Deuteronomy 11:20).

*Kol she'ein lo mezuzah b'fitcho over b'shnei asei* — one who lacks a *mezuzah* on his doorpost transgresses two positive commandments: *u'chtavtam* from the *parasha* of *Shema*, and *u'chtavtam* again from the *parasha* of *v'haya im shamo'a*, each commanding the writing of a *mezuzah*.

### Priestly Blessing Violation

*V'chol kohen she'eino oleh la-duchan over b'gimmel asei* — any *kohen* who fails to ascend to the *duchan* to bless the congregation transgresses three positive commandments: *amor lahem* — say to them; *koh tevarachu* — thus shall you bless; and *v'samu et shmi* — and they shall place My name upon them. This *halacha* regarding the priestly blessing does not fit neatly into the surrounding discussion of *tefillin*, *mezuzah*, and *tzitzit*, but it is included here as a related enumeration of positive commandments that are violated by inaction.

## Tefillin & Long Life

### Extended Days Promise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַמַּנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין מַאֲרִיךְ יָמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ עֲלֵיהֶם יִחְיוּ וּלְכׇל בָּהֶן חַיֵּי רוּחִי וְתַחֲלִימֵנִי וְהַחֲיֵנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says: Anyone who dons phylacteries lives** a **long life, as it is stated:** **"The Lord is upon them, they will live, and altogether therein is the life of my spirit; and have me recover, and make me to live"** (Isaiah 38:16). This is interpreted as referring to those who don phylacteries, which contain the name of the Lord, on their heads; as a result, they will live, be healed and merit long life.

Reish Lakish offers a teaching on the reward for *tefillin*: *kol hamani'ach tefillin ma'arich yamim* — whoever puts on *tefillin* will have extended days.

### Head & Heart Verse

The source is the verse at the top of 44b: *Hashem aleihem yichyu* — "God upon them they shall live." The Maharsha explains the *derashah*: *Hashem aleihem* refers to the *tefillin shel rosh*, placed upon the head; and *u'lechol bahen chiyei ruchi* — "and all those in whom is the spirit of my life" — refers to the *tefillin shel yad*, placed opposite the heart, speaking to the *ruach*, the life-spirit within us. *V'tachlimeni v'hachayeni* — and it healed me and gave me life. The one who wears *tefillin* thus brings divine vitality to bear on both head and heart, and is promised extended days as a result.

## Mincha vs. Nesachim

### Mincha First Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַסּוֹלֶת וְהַשֶּׁמֶן אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַיַּיִן, וְלֹא הַיַּיִן מְעַכְּבָן. הַמַּתָּנוֹת שֶׁעַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן אֵין מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna returns to discussing the *halakhot* of meal offerings, which are the central theme of this tractate. The absence of **the fine flour and the oil** for the meal offering accompanying burnt offerings and peace offerings **does not prevent** libation of **the wine, and** the absence of **the wine** for libation **does not prevent** sacrifice of the flour and the oil. Failure to perform some of **the placements** of blood **on the external altar does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva with **the other** placements, as even if the priest performed only one placement of blood, the offering effects atonement after the fact.

With this the extended discussion of *tefillin*, *mezuzah*, and *tzitzit* concludes, and the Gemara returns to matters directly relevant to *Menachos*. The *Mishna* had noted that the *solet* and the *shemen* do not prevent the wine, and the wine does not prevent them; they are brought together with the *korban* but are not mutually interdependent. The Gemara now takes up the question of order: when bringing the *mincha* and the *nesachim* — the flour-and-oil offering and the wine libation — that accompany a *korban*, which is offered first?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּמִנְחָתָם וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם״ – הָבֵא מִנְחָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הָבֵא נְסָכִים. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״זֶבַח וּנְסָכִים״ – הָבֵא זֶבַח, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הָבֵא נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse concerning the additional offerings sacrificed on *Sukkot* and the Eighth Day of Assembly states: **"And their meal offering and their libations** for the bulls, for the rams and for the lambs" (Numbers 29:18). This indicates that after the animal is sacrificed, one must **bring** the **meal offering and then bring** the **libations. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that since the verse states: "To bring an offering made by fire to the Lord, a burnt offering, and a meal offering, **an animal offering, and libations,** each on its own day" (Leviticus 23:37), one must **bring** the **animal offering and then bring** the **libations,** and only then bring the meal offering.

A *baraita* addresses this: *u'minchasam v'niskeihem* — the Torah phrases it: their *mincha* and then their *nesachim*. *Havei mincha v'achar kach nisachim* — the *mincha* comes first, then the libations.

### Rebbi's Order

Rebbi disagrees, citing a different verse: *zevach u'nisachim* — the sacrifice and the libations. *Havei zevach v'achar kach nisachim* — after the sacrifice comes the libation, and the *mincha* comes only afterward. So there is a dispute: the *Rabbanan* hold that *mincha* precedes the *nesachim*, while Rebbi holds that after the animal *korban* come the *nesachim* and only then the *mincha*.

### Verse Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי נָמֵי הָכְתִיב ״וּמִנְחָתָם וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם״? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לְ״מִנְחָתָם וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם״ בַּלַּיְלָה, וּ״מִנְחָתָם וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם״ אֲפִילּוּ לְמָחָר. וְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״זֶבַח וּנְסָכִים״? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִכְדִזְעֵירִי, דְּאָמַר זְעֵירִי: אֵין נְסָכִים מִתְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁחִיטַת הַזֶּבַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **also, isn't it written: "And their meal offering and their libations,"** indicating that the meal offering precedes the libations? The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is necessary** in order **to** teach that once the animals have been sacrificed during the day, **their meal offering and their libations** may be offered even **at night, and** similarly, **their meal offering and their libations** may be offered even **the next day.** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **the Rabbis also, isn't it written: "An animal offering, and libations,"** indicating that the libations immediately follow the animal offering and precede the meal offering? The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is necessary to** teach that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the statement **of Ze'eiri, as Ze'eiri says:** The **libations** that accompany animal offerings **are consecrated only through the slaughter of the animal offering.** This means that once the animal is slaughtered, the libations that were set aside to be brought with that animal offering cannot be diverted to accompany a different animal offering.

The Gemara now asks: if Rebbi bases himself on *zevach u'nisachim* to argue that libations come after the sacrifice, what does Rebbi do with the verse *u'minchasam v'niskeihem* that the *Rabbanan* used? And conversely, if the *Rabbanan* base themselves on *u'minchasam v'niskeihem* for the order of *mincha* before *nesachim*, what do they do with *zevach u'nisachim*?

The Gemara resolves this by pointing out that these verses are already needed for entirely different *drashos*. The verse *u'minchasam v'niskeihem* — appearing multiple times in the *psukim* in various forms — is needed to teach that the *mincha* and *nesachim* may be brought separately from the *korban*: they can be brought at night, or even on a different day, when the *korban* itself was offered during the day. The verse is thus teaching not order but separability: the *mincha* and *nesachim* need not be offered simultaneously with the *korban*, though when brought together they must be.

### Zeira's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, בְּבָאִים עִם הַזֶּבַח דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמִנְחָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נְסָכִים, דְּהָכְתִיב ״עֹלָה וּמִנְחָה״. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּבָאִין בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מִדְּבָאִין עִם הַזֶּבַח – מִנְחָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נְסָכִים, בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן נָמֵי – מִנְחָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נְסָכִים. וְרַבִּי, הָתָם הוּא דְּאַיְּידֵי דְּאַתְחֵיל בַּאֲכִילָה, גָּמַר לַהּ לְכוֹלָּא מִילְּתָא דַּאֲכִילָה, אֲבָל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן – נְסָכִים עֲדִיפִי, הוֹאִיל דְּמִיתְאַמְרָא שִׁירָה עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, with regard to** libations that **accompany an animal offering, everyone agrees that** the **meal offering** should be brought **and afterward** the **libations** should be brought, **as it is written: "A burnt offering, and a meal offering." When they disagree** it is **with regard to** meal offerings and libations that are **brought by themselves. The Rabbis hold** that **from** the fact **that** when meal offerings and libations **accompany offerings,** the **meal offerings** are brought **and then** the **libations** are brought, it can be derived that when they are brought **by themselves also,** first the **meal offering** is brought **and then** the **libations** are brought. **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi holds that **it is** specifically **there,** in the case of meal offerings and libations that accompany an animal offering, that the meal offering is burned on the altar before the wine libation is poured on the altar. This is **because since** the altar **has started** to **eat,** i.e., consume, the animal offering, one must first **complete the entire matter of** the altar's **eating,** including the meal offering. The pouring of the wine on the altar is likened more to drinking than to eating. **But** when the meal offering and the libations are brought **by themselves,** the **libations are** considered **preferable, because** the **song** of the Levites **is recited over them.** Consequently, the pouring of the libations precedes the burning of the meal offering on the altar.

The verse *zevach u'nisachim*, meanwhile, is needed for a completely different principle, that of Zeira: *ein nisachim miskadshim ela bishchitas ha-zevach* — the *nesachim* are not consecrated except through the *shechitah* of the *korban* they accompany. The verse *zevach u'nisachim* conveys that they come as a package: the wine and flour that accompany a *korban* become linked to and sanctified by that specific *korban* only at the moment of its slaughter. Until *shechitah* they can be reassigned to a different *korban*; after *shechitah* they are bound to this one.

There is a dispute among the *rishonim* as to the precise meaning of this consecration. One view holds that it refers to *kedushah* in the sense of *kedushas ha-guf*: after *shechitah* the *nesachim* become fully sacred such that removing them from the *Beis HaMikdash* renders them *pasul*. Another view holds that the consecration refers specifically to their attachment to a particular *korban*: before *shechitah* they remain fungible; after, they are dedicated to this offering alone.

### Separate Order Logic

Since neither *u'minchasam v'niskeihem* nor *zevach u'nisachim* can serve as the basis of the dispute — both verses are required for other *drashos* — the Gemara establishes the actual source of agreement and dispute. When the *mincha* and *nesachim* are brought together with the *korban* at the same time, the Gemara concludes that everyone agrees: after the *korban*, one first brings the *mincha* and only then the *nesachim*. This is derived from the phrase *olah u'mincha* — after the *olah* comes the *mincha*, and the wine follows. That much is uncontested.

The dispute pertains only to the case where the *mincha* and *nesachim* are brought separately, on a different occasion from the *korban* itself. In that case, what is the correct order?

The *Rabbanan* argue: since when they are brought together with the *korban* the order is *mincha* then *nesachim*, the same order should apply when they are brought separately. Order established in one context carries over to the other.

### Wine & Levite Song

Rebbi's reasoning runs differently. When the *mincha* and *nesachim* come together with the *korban*, there is an internal logic: one has begun with the animal, which is food, and therefore continues with the *mincha*, which is also food — something that is eaten — before proceeding to the wine. *Hatchil be'achilah gemar lah lechulah miltah de'achilah* — having started with the food, complete all the food-related offerings before moving to the drink. But when they are brought separately, that logic does not apply. In that independent context, *nesachim adifi* — the wine takes priority. Why? *Hu demis'amra shirah aleihu* — because the *shirah* of the *Levi'im* is sung at the pouring of the wine. The *nesachim* are the occasion for *Levitical* song, and *ein omrim shirah ela al ha-yayin* — song is said only over wine. The importance of the wine as the vehicle for *shirah* elevates it, and therefore when brought independently, the *nesachim* come first.

## Altar Placements

### Single Placement Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּתָּנוֹת שֶׁעַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן אֵין מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Failure to perform some of **the placements** of blood **on the external altar does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva **with the other** placements, as even if the priest performed only one placement, the offering effects atonement after the fact.

The Gemara returns to the *Mishna's* ruling: *ha-matanot she'al ha-mizbeach ha-chitzon ein me'akvot zu et zu* — the placements of blood on the outer altar do not prevent one another. Whether one is speaking of the four applications of the blood of a *chatat* or the two *zerikos* of other *korbanos*, all the placements on the outer altar are non-indispensable: even if only one placement is performed, *be-di'eved* atonement is achieved. This principle was established earlier in Zevachim.

### Scriptural Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לְכׇל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן שֶׁנְּתָנָן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת שֶׁכִּיפֵּר? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ עַל מִזְבַּח ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos this statement, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived with regard **to all** offerings **whose** blood is to be **placed on the external altar that** if **one placed their** blood **with one** act of **placement,** as opposed to the two or four that are required depending upon the offering, **that** the offering has nevertheless **effected atonement** after the fact, i.e., one has fulfilled his obligation to bring the offering? **As it is stated: "And the blood of your offerings shall be poured out against the altar of the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 12:27).

The Gemara asks: from where is this derived? *Tanu Rabbanan: minayin le-chol ha-nisanim al ha-mizbeach ha-chitzon she-nisnu matanah achas she-kiper?* — how do we know that for all blood applications on the outer altar, a single placement suffices? *She-ne'emar: ve-dam zevacheicha yishapech al mizbeach Hashem Elokecha* — as the verse says, the blood of your sacrifices shall be poured upon the altar of the Lord your God. The verse speaks of a single pouring — *yishapech*, in the singular — indicating that one placement, one act of applying the blood to the altar, is sufficient to achieve atonement *be-di'eved*, even where multiple placements would ideally be performed.
