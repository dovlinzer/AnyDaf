# Nedarim 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kol Nidrei & Stipulation

### Kol Nidrei Recap

Today's daf is 24. Yesterday's shiur, delivered by a substitute, covered the central issues surrounding *kol nidrei* and the mechanism of making a *tnai* — a stipulation — to prevent one's vows from taking effect. By way of review: the *mishnah* presented the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who introduced the category of *nidrei zeruzin*, vows that are not genuinely intended to be binding.

### Nidrei Zeruzin

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov identified two related phenomena. The first and more transparent example involved two people haggling over the price of something, each making a *neder* in the course of the back-and-forth — a process that was obviously a kind of game, with no real intent that the vows should take effect. The second, somewhat less obvious case involved using a vow as leverage or pressure to get someone to do something — for instance, vowing that a friend's food will be forbidden unless he agrees to come for dinner. This calls to mind the story of Abaye and his wife, where a vow was deployed as an ultimatum; the inherent problem with ultimatums is that one must ultimately make good on them. The question is whether one can truly claim retroactively that the vow was never intended to be binding. That is precisely Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position: in such cases, where the person was using the vow purely as a form of pressure or encouragement and never genuinely wanted it to take effect, the vow is categorized as *nidrei zeruzin* and is not binding.

### Stipulation Mechanism

Alongside the *nidrei zeruzin* discussion, the *gemara* identifies a separate and independent mechanism: making an explicit stipulation at the outset — at the beginning of the year, for instance — declaring that any future vow one may take should not be considered binding if one does not remember having made it. The *gemara* raises an internal tension here: if you remember the stipulation and you go ahead and make a vow anyway, you presumably want the vow to be binding. Conversely, if your intent was always that it not be binding, you shouldn't be making the vow in the first place. The stipulation therefore helps only in cases where one genuinely forgets about it. This mechanism is analogous to, though not identical with, the institution of *moda'ah* in *dinei mamonot*: if someone is pressured into selling a plot of land, he may tell witnesses in advance that even though he will sign the document, he does not truly consent to the sale. Similarly here, one can declare in advance that any future vow should not be considered sincere. Rava offered a nuanced answer addressing certain intermediate cases where the person half-remembers the stipulation, but he did not, in principle, dispute Abaye's basic framework.

### Hatarat Nedarim

The practical upshot is that one may make such a declaration at the beginning of the year — at *Rosh Hashanah* — stipulating that any vows taken during the coming year, if forgotten, should not be binding. This is precisely the formula recited at the conclusion of the *hatarat nedarim* ceremony on *erev Rosh Hashanah*: *hareini mosar moda'ah lifneichem* — "I hereby make a proclamation before you" — followed by the declaration that any vow made from this day forward without being remembered shall be annulled from now. Of course, one observes that if this declaration truly worked comprehensively, one would not need to repeat *hatarat nedarim* every year; a single *moda'ah* would suffice indefinitely. But that, in any case, is the *gemara*'s mechanism.

The Ran then takes up the question of how all this bears on *kol nidrei*. The *hatarat nedarim* ceremony performed on *erev Rosh Hashanah* combines both dimensions explicitly: it annuls past vows through a generic expression of *charatah* — regret — and takes a stipulation for the future. The question of whether *charatah* alone, without specifying a *petach*, suffices for a generic annulment is one we have addressed previously; a generalized declaration of regret on behalf of the entire *tzibur* is somewhat unusual, since ordinarily the person seeking release must present himself and indicate *mischarait* — that he regrets the vow.

### Kol Nidrei Meaning

*Kol nidrei* presents its own complications. Unlike the formal *hatarat nedarim* ceremony, one is not presenting oneself individually, the formula combines past and future in an ambiguous way, and the halachic mechanism through which it operates is not entirely clear. The simple reading of the text suggests it is past-oriented, though Rabbeinu Tam, apparently for reasons related to this sensibility, proposed reorienting it toward the future — requiring certain grammatical adjustments to the text. Some *machzorim* preserve both the past-oriented and future-oriented *nuscha'os*, presenting them as alternate versions. An article on the grammar of *kol nidrei* by Dr. Richard Steiner offers a thorough analysis of this question for those interested in pursuing it.

It is worth noting that this question had significant real-world stakes. For much of Jewish history, the integrity of a business transaction with non-Jews was backed by a sworn vow. The perception that Jews would publicly release themselves from their vows on *Yom Kippur* night — effectively nullifying all commitments made over the preceding year — created serious problems and was used as grounds to question Jewish trustworthiness in commerce. This was part of the reason Rav Hirsch removed *kol nidrei* from the *Yom Kippur* liturgy in his community. The Ran's position that *kol nidrei* cannot coherently be understood as future-oriented — given the *gemara*'s concern, discussed below, about not publicizing the stipulation mechanism too broadly — also bears on how one understands the practical halachic meaning of the text.

## Rav Huna & Rava Dispute

### Rav Huna's View

The *gemara* now raises a question: should this mechanism of stipulation have been publicly announced? Rav Huna bar Chinana's position was that it ought to have been publicized broadly — at a *pirka*, a public gathering — so that everyone would know they could make a stipulation at the beginning of the year and thereby protect themselves from inadvertently transgressing their vows.

### Rava's Objection

Rava disagreed sharply. He pointed out that the *tana* had deliberately concealed this information — *mistam lei satumei* — by weaving the stipulation mechanism subtly into the *mishnah*'s case about *nidrei zeruzin*, rather than stating it as a separate and explicit principle. The reason: *kedei shelo yinhagu kalos rosh benedarim* — so that people should not treat their vows lightly. If everyone knew that a simple advance stipulation could nullify any future vow, there would be a strong temptation to take vows casually, confident that no real obligation was being incurred. Rava therefore challenges Rav Huna bar Chinana: the *tana* took care to obscure this information — and you propose to announce it in a public forum?

This passage is striking for two reasons. First, it offers a window into how the *gemara* sometimes understands its own hermeneutic activity. When the *gemara* reads a *mishnah* and extracts two distinct legal principles from what appears on its surface to be a single unified case, one might wonder whether the *gemara* truly believes this to be the *pshat* of the text or whether it is simply offering a legal reformulation that need not be taken as a reading of the *mishnah* itself. Here, remarkably, the *gemara* seems to be affirming that this is genuinely *pshat*: the very fact that the information was buried in the *mishnah*'s formulation, rather than stated explicitly, is itself legally significant — it was hidden intentionally.

### Sin vs Sloppiness

The second noteworthy dimension of this passage is the broader tension it reflects. This tension has run throughout *masechet Nedarim*: on one hand, you want to prevent people from actually transgressing their vows; on the other hand, if you make it too easy to avoid transgression — by providing ready-made mechanisms for annulment or by assuring people that certain vows are not really binding — you risk encouraging sloppiness in how people take vows in the first place. We have seen this tension elsewhere in the tractate: sometimes a *neder* is not technically binding, yet we require the person to go through the process of *hatarat nedarim* precisely so that the gravity of vow-taking is not undermined. Similarly, the *gemara* elsewhere formulates the principle of *halacha v'ein morin kein* — a ruling exists but is not publicized — and some halachic handbooks reflect this by presenting the public-facing ruling in the main text while reserving more nuanced leniencies for footnotes in Hebrew accessible only to *talmidei chachamim*.

Rav Huna bar Chinana's position, it should be noted, was presumably motivated by genuine concern: people were stumbling over vows they did not fully intend, and a publicly known mechanism could spare them that sin. Rava's response is not that the mechanism is invalid, but that broadcasting it too widely creates different harms. The *mishnah*'s deliberate obliqueness was thus not a failure of transparency but a considered halachic policy.

## Rabbis vs R. Eliezer

### Opening Question

The *gemara* now poses a new question: did the *rabbanan* disagree with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's categorization of *nidrei zeruzin*, or did they accept it? The stipulation mechanism, the *gemara* notes, would presumably be agreed upon by everyone — the debate about the *tnai* at the beginning of the year seems unrelated to the question of *nidrei zeruzin* per se. The real point of contention is whether the *rabbanan* accepted Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's broader category: when someone makes a vow not in the context of transparent haggling but as a form of one-sided pressure to get a result — "if you don't come to dinner, my food will be forbidden to you" — is that truly *nidrei zeruzin* and therefore not binding? One can imagine someone who genuinely would be offended at being refused, and who would sincerely intend the vow. Perhaps the *rabbanan* maintain that when the situation is not transparently a game, the vow stands. And a secondary question: if the *rabbanan* do disagree, how do we rule — like them or like Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov?

### Gift Acceptance Case

The *gemara* adduces evidence from a *mishnah*. The case: someone says to his friend, *konam she'ani neheneh lecha im i attah notel livnecha kor shel chitin ushtei chaviyot shel yayin* — "my benefit from you shall be *konam* if you do not accept for your son this measure of wheat and these two barrels of wine." The speaker is trying to give a gift — perhaps a wedding present — and the intended recipient keeps refusing. In the middle of this friendly exchange, the giver declares that if the gift is not accepted, all benefit from the recipient will be forbidden to him. The *mishnah* rules: *harei ze yachol lehatir nidro shelo pi chacham* — he can get out of the vow without consulting a *chacham*, meaning the vow is not binding.

### Honor Satisfaction

The reasoning is as follows: *sheyachol lomar lo klum amarta ela bishvil kvodi zehu kvodi* — the recipient could say, "You only made that vow for the sake of my honor. Well, this is my honor — that you made me such an offer and I had the standing to decline it." In other words, the underlying purpose of the vow — to confer honor — has been achieved even without the gift being accepted. The person who refused the gift can claim he was honored by the very offer and by his ability to graciously decline. Therefore, it is as though the condition of the vow was satisfied, and the vow does not take effect.

One might observe that there is something not entirely gracious about this response. By insisting that the honor lies in declining, the recipient in some sense dishonors the giver by denying him the satisfaction of giving. The *Yerushalmi* addresses this directly: it says that if the giver responds, "I did not make the vow for your honor — I made it for mine, because it meant something to me to give this to you," then the vow is binding. The *Yerushalmi* thus contemplates the possibility that *lechvodi*, not *lechvodecha*, was the animating motivation, in which case the honorific logic fails. When the motivation is ambiguous, the question of whose honor drove the vow becomes determinative.

There is also a broader cultural dimension here. In the Roman world of patronage, the acceptance of a gift from a social inferior was itself a powerful statement of status. A *mishnah* in *Kiddushin* reflects something similar: one of the modes of *kiddushin* is the woman giving a gift to the man, with his agreement to accept it serving as the act of acquisition. In a world structured around patronage dynamics, the willingness of a more prominent figure to accept a gift carried real social weight, and the entire exchange could not be reduced to simple friendship.

### Case Classification

Having laid out the *mishnah*'s ruling, the *gemara* draws an inference. The *mishnah* says the vow is not binding because *zehu kevodi* — it is as though the honor was received. But this implies: were it not for that reasoning, the vow *would* be binding. So this is a genuine *neder*, not *nidrei zeruzin*. One might therefore conclude that the *rabbanan* argue with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — that a one-sided vow used to pressure someone into accepting a gift is a real vow, not mere *zeruzin*.

The *gemara* rebuffs this inference: *le'olam Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov hu* — this *mishnah* can still be according to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. But here, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov would concede that the vow is serious: *modeh Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov be'hai denidra havai*. Why? Because the giver says to the recipient: *lo kalba ana d'mit'hanina minech v'lo mit'hanita minai* — "I am not a dog, that I would be deriving benefit from you while you get nothing from me." Here the dynamic is reciprocal: the giver is saying, "I give things to you all the time; if you will not accept even this, then it is simply not a relationship where I can continue to take from you either." The vow runs in the opposite direction — if you do not accept from me, I will not take from you — and that introduces an element of genuine sincerity that distinguishes it from the simpler case.

## Reciprocal Vows

### Giver's Vow

The *gemara* now introduces the inverse case. *Ha'omer l'chavero konam she'ata nehenit li im ee ata noten livni kor shel chitin ushtei chavyot shel yayin* — "Your benefit from me shall be *konam* if you do not give my son this measure of wheat and these two barrels of wine." Here, instead of the giver making a vow about his own benefit from the recipient, he makes a vow about the recipient's benefit from him. Rabbi Meir says: *ad sheyiten* — until the recipient actually gives the gift, he cannot derive benefit from the giver. The *chachamim* say: *af zeh yachol lehatir et nidro b'lo pi chacham* — even here the vow is not binding without consulting a *chacham*. Why? *Sheyachol lomar hareni k'ilu hitkabalti* — because the giver can say, "It is as though I received the gift." This is similar in structure to the previous case: though the condition was not met, the giver declares it as effectively satisfied.

### Reciprocity Argument

Before returning to the question of *nidrei zeruzin*, the Ran raises an important question about the meaning of *ke'ilu hitkabalti*. One intelligible understanding is as follows: the giver wanted to receive an object; he could imagine a scenario in which Michael had given him the barrels of wine and he had then given them back. In that case, saying *ke'ilu hitkabalti* — "it is as though I received them" — has a plausible legal logic, because there is a concrete transaction one can imagine having occurred and been reversed. But what if the condition was not about a physical object? Suppose instead the condition was: "This is forbidden to you unless you go to the gym twice a week." In that case, *ke'ilu hitkabalti* is much harder to parse — what exactly is being treated as though it happened? The Ran raises this difficulty. He suggests that perhaps in all cases, the underlying logic is simply that the giver set a condition, the purpose of the condition was to satisfy a desire, and the giver can always declare that desire satisfied — which amounts to waiving the condition. Whether this is a conceptually clean legal mechanism or more of a pragmatic escape valve is a question the Ran leaves somewhat open.

In any case, the thrust of the *mishnah* — before the *gemara* addresses the question of *nidrei zeruzin* — is that this is a genuine *neder*, but one from which the giver can extract himself by invoking *ke'ilu hitkabalti*. The logical consequence: were that not available, the *neder* would be binding. So this seems to point toward the *rabbanan* holding that these pressure-vows are real vows.

### Ran's Analysis

The *gemara* again deflects: *le'olam Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov hu* — and the Ran's explanation makes this intelligible in terms of the *gemara*'s language. When the direction of the vow runs one way — "if you don't come to dinner, my food will be forbidden to *you*" — one might argue this is just an empty threat, a form of *zeruzin*. The person may be using the vow to encourage or pressure, but perhaps it is so hollow that even Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's category cannot make it binding. By contrast, when the vow runs in the reciprocal direction — "if you don't accept this from me, I won't get any benefit from *you*" — that reflects a genuine concern about the nature of the relationship. One is saying: what kind of relationship would it be if I give to you and you give me nothing in return? If this dynamic is allowed to continue, I simply do not want that relationship. Someone could sincerely feel this way, and therefore even Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov would concede that such a vow is binding.

The Rashba reads the distinction differently: he argues that what makes the case more serious is the weight of the gift involved — a larger, more significant gift warrants a more sincere vow, whereas an invitation to dinner is trivially easy to dismiss. However, this reading is difficult to reconcile with the actual language of the *gemara*, which never focuses on the size of the gift. The *gemara*'s language — *rav malka ana de-mahnina lach v'at la mahnit li* — points squarely to the reciprocity concern, not to the monetary value of the gift. When someone says, in effect, "Do you think I'm some kind of king who gives and expects nothing back?", the issue is relational symmetry, not the magnitude of any particular item. As anyone who has experienced that hollow feeling when the same person keeps arriving at *Shabbat* meals without ever hosting in return will recognize — that relational dynamic is a real source of grievance, and a vow expressing it could well be sincere.

### Initiated Vows & Ones

Mar Kashisha son of Rav Chisda raises yet another attempted proof from the *mishnah* dealing with *nidrei onsin* — vows that became impossible to fulfill due to circumstances beyond one's control. The case: I pressure someone to come eat at my house, and if he does not come, my food is forbidden to him. He was on his way but fell ill, or his son fell ill, or a river flooded and he could not cross. The *mishnah* holds that the vow is not binding, because I surely never intended the vow to apply in a case of genuine *ones*. What I wanted was for him to make his best effort to come; had he done so and been thwarted, I would never have wanted the prohibition to take hold.

This case sounds like *nidrei zeruzin*, since it is a one-directional vow used to encourage attendance at dinner. Yet the *mishnah* appears to treat it as a real vow that happens to be suspended due to *ones*. If it were truly *nidrei zeruzin*, the question of *ones* would be irrelevant — the vow would simply not be binding at all. This seems to imply that the *rabbanan* hold such a vow is genuinely binding when the condition is willfully not met.

The *gemara* once again deflects by reframing who initiated the vow. The case is not one where the inviter made the vow against the invitee; rather, *dizamina adrei l'mizamna* — the invitee initiated the vow. Michael had been wanting to come to dinner and felt that his host was not sincere about the invitation. So Michael pressed: "Come on, do you really want me to come? Make a vow to show me." The host, thus challenged, made the vow. In this scenario, the vow was not a *zeruzin* — it was not deployed to push Michael into doing something he was reluctant to do; Michael was already eager to come. The host made the vow to demonstrate sincerity in response to Michael's prompt. Therefore, the vow is a genuine *neder*, and when Michael cannot come due to *ones*, the *gemara* holds the vow does not take effect — precisely because it was not a vow of pressure but a vow of sincere commitment that circumstances rendered impossible to fulfill. This, the *gemara* argues, is still consistent with Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov; it does not prove the existence of a *rabbanan* who dispute him.

After all this, the *gemara* has provided an answer to each attempted proof, but the weight of the evidence continues to suggest that there is indeed a stricter position in the background — one that treats these one-directional pressure-vows as genuine *nedarim*. The *gemara* has managed each time to distinguish the specific case, either through reciprocity, initiation, or *ones*, but the category of *nidrei zeruzin* as applied to simple one-sided encouragement-vows remains contested.

### R. Eliezer Extended

At this point, the *gemara* introduces a further statement: *yeter al ken amar Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* — Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov went further. He said: *ha'omer l'chavero konam she'ani neheneh lecha im i attah mit'areach etzli v'tokhal immi pat chamah v'tishteh immi kos chamim* — "My benefit from you shall be *konam* if you do not come and stay with me as a guest, eat hot bread with me, and drink a cup of tea with me." And if the other party then responds in kind — saying similarly that he will derive no benefit from the host if the host does not reciprocate — *af elu nidrei zeruzin* — even these are *nidrei zeruzin*, says Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. The *chachamim* did not agree.

This statement is significant. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov is here extending his leniency to include even the reciprocal case — the very case the *gemara* had been treating as more sincere throughout the preceding discussion. Not only do the *rabbanan* refuse to follow him in this extension, but the *gemara* now asks what exactly the *rabbanan*'s disagreement covers: *lav afillu b'kammaita* — do the *rabbanan* not agree even with the original, non-reciprocal case? *U-shema mina pligi rabbanan alei* — from the phrasing here, it seems the *rabbanan* reject Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position across the board.

The *gemara* acknowledges, however, that there is an alternate *girsa* of the text according to which the *rabbanan*'s disagreement is limited to the reciprocal case alone; on the non-reciprocal case they might agree. What is clear in all *girsaos* is that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov himself holds that both cases are *nidrei zeruzin*.

## Ruling & Nidrei Havai

### Halakha Decision

The *gemara* concludes: *mai havya alah* — what is the practical ruling? *D'amar Rav Huna halakha k'Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, v'khen amar Rav Adda bar Ahava halakha k'Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* — Rav Huna ruled like Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, and Rav Adda bar Ahava said the same. We rule like Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov.

The Ran observes that this ruling effectively renders the first question — whether the *rabbanan* actually disputed Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — moot. The entire investigation was driven by a *halachic* interest: does Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov stand alone or does he have opponents, and if opponents, who do we follow? Once we have the ruling, the prior question loses its urgency. The Ran notes that this is analogous to the Rambam's approach of presenting the final ruling without the surrounding dialectic — if the only interest is knowing what the *halacha* is, all the preceding discussion becomes academic. Of course, one might respond that learning the *gemara* has value beyond the bottom-line ruling, and the reasoning behind a position matters for understanding edge cases and borderlines. This also connects to the broader question of the principle of *yachid v'rabim halacha k'rabim* — ordinarily we follow the majority — and why in this case we deviate from it to follow Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. The considerations that go into such a ruling — the logic of the positions, the accepted practice in the field, the authority of the *Shulchan Aruch*, the clarity of the *gemara*'s own *pshat* — are the same considerations any *posek* must weigh, and there is no fixed hierarchy among them.

The practical scope of *nidrei zeruzin* as now defined is quite broad: not just the transparent mutual haggling case, but even one-sided pressure-vows and even reciprocal vows of the form "if you don't take from me, I won't take from you." All of these Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — and now the *halakha* — considers *nidrei zeruzin*. The difficulty that remains is knowing precisely where the line falls. Abaye's own case, after all — where he made a vow as an ultimatum and still required a *hatarat neder* — seems to fall in the *zeruzin* category on its face. Yet it was treated as a genuine vow that needed formal release. Knowing which pressure-vows are mere *zeruzin* and which are sincere commitments that a person genuinely entertained — even if reluctantly — and which are the middle category of Abaye where the person was sincere but assumed the other party would comply, is a question that the *gemara* leaves without a fully explicit answer.

### Nidrei Havai Intro

The *mishnah* introduces the second category: *nidrei havai* — vain or empty vows. *Amar konam im lo ra'iti baderech hazeh k'olei Mitzrayim* — "This loaf is *konam* to me if I did not see on this road as many people as the Jews who came out of Egypt." Or: *im lo ra'iti k'nachash k'korat beit ha-bad* — "if I did not see a snake as thick as the beam of an olive press."

### Exaggeration vs Falsehood

The Ran distinguishes two different phenomena at play in the *mishnah*'s examples. The first case — seeing as many people as *olei Mitzrayim* — the Ran actually treats as a form of *guzma*, rhetorical exaggeration. When someone says "there were as many people as the Jews who came out of Egypt," everyone understands this as hyperbole meaning there were enormous crowds. On this reading, the person is actually telling the truth — he really did see massive throngs of people, and his vow condition was genuinely satisfied, expressed in the conventional idiom of exaggeration. The vow is therefore not binding, but for a different reason than one might expect: the *tnai* was actually fulfilled. The Ran's point here is really a principle about how to interpret exaggerated language in vows: *guzma* is read in its figurative meaning, not its literal one, so a vow conditioned on a patently hyperbolic statement is assessed according to what the speaker clearly meant, not the literal words.

The second case — a snake the size of an olive-press beam — is more interesting and more clearly within the *nidrei havai* category. This is not rhetorical exaggeration but an outright fantastical claim. The person is making a vow conditional on something everyone knows to be false. If you genuinely wanted to make the bread forbidden, you would simply say "this bread is forbidden to me." Why attach it to a condition you know to be false? The only sensible answer is that you are not actually trying to make the bread forbidden at all. What you are doing is using the vow to add rhetorical weight and colorfulness to your fish story — you want people to appreciate how enormous the snake was, and the vow formula serves as a rhetorical intensifier.

### Purpose Principle

This leads to the governing principle of *nidrei havai*: a *neder* is an instrument designed to make an object forbidden. When a vow is conditioned on something patently false — something everyone present knows to be untrue — the speaker clearly has no interest in making the object forbidden. He is using the vow for an entirely different purpose: to add drama and emphasis to a boastful or exaggerated statement. Since the vow is not being deployed for its intended function, it is not a *neder* at all, and nothing is forbidden.

## Nidrei vs Shevuot

### Shevuot Shav

The *gemara* introduces a *braita* that contrasts *nidrei havai* with *shevuot havai*. The version of the text before us reads: *nidrei havai mutarim shvuos havai asurim* — vows of the *havai* type are permitted, but oaths of the *havai* type are forbidden. An alternate *girsa* cited in the margin reads that both are permitted. To understand which is correct, the *gemara* points to the *mishnah* in *Shevuos* 29, which enumerates examples of *shvuat shav* — a false or vain oath. *Nishba l'shanot et hayadua la'adam* — swearing to contradict something universally known. For example: swearing that a stone pillar is made of gold, that a man is a woman, or that one did not see a snake like the beam of an olive press, or a flying camel. This is called *shvuat shav*. The *mishnah* in *Shevuos* thus categorizes these precisely as the type of oath the *gemara* is calling *shvuat havai*.

### Neder vs Shevua

The conceptual distinction between a *neder* and a *shevua* is essential here. The purpose of a *neder* is to make an object forbidden. The purpose of a *shevua* — particularly an oath about the past or present — is to back up one's word, to assert the truth of a statement with the weight of a divine invocation. These are fundamentally different instruments.

### Permitted vs Forbidden

When a person makes a *neder havai* — attaching a vow condition to a patently false or absurd statement — he has no interest in making any object forbidden. He is not using the *neder* for its proper purpose at all; he is co-opting the vow formula to add rhetorical color to a story. Therefore the vow is null, and nothing is forbidden. The *neder* simply does not function as one here.

When a person makes a *shevuat havai*, however — invoking an oath to back up a patently false claim — he is using the *shevua* for *exactly its intended purpose*: to lend credibility and weight to a statement. The fact that the statement is false does not mean the *shevua* is being misused; quite the contrary. Taking a *shevua* in order to back up one's word, even a false word, is precisely what a *shevua* does. And when it is invoked to back up a patently false claim, that is *shvuat shav* — forbidden by the Torah. So the asymmetry is principled: *nidrei havai* are permitted because the *neder* is not functioning as a *neder*; *shevuot havai* are forbidden because the *shevua* is functioning exactly as a *shevua*, and a *shevua* used to assert a falsehood — even an obvious one — is *shvuat shav*.

### Shevuat Havai Refined

The *gemara* then asks: what exactly would constitute a *shevuat havai*? If one says *shvua im lo ra'iti* — "an oath if I did not see such-and-such" — *midi ka'amar*? What kind of oath is that? An oath *if* you saw something is incoherent; the oath needs to be that you saw something. Abaye offers the straightforward formulation: *d'amar shvua she'ra'iti* — "a *shevua* that I saw a snake of such a size." That is indeed *shvuat havai* — you are invoking an oath to back up a patently false claim.

Rava objects, however, that this formulation is too obvious: it is simply the well-known category of *shvuat shav*, not a new principle. And furthermore, *dumiya d'neder katani* — the *shvuat havai* should be structurally parallel to the *nidrei havai* case. The case of *nidrei havai* involved a conditional structure: "this bread is forbidden to me if I did not see..." Rather than a direct assertion of the oath, Rava therefore reformulates: *b'omer ye'asru peros ha'olam alay b'shvua im lo ra'iti baderech hazeh k'olei Mitzrayim* — one says "by oath, all the fruit of the world is forbidden to me if I did not see on this road as many people as came out of Egypt." This is the genuinely novel case of *shvuat havai*: it is not a direct false assertion backed by an oath, but a conditional oath attached to a fantastical or exaggerated claim. That formulation is less obviously a simple *shvuat shav*, and it is here that Rava locates the real content of the category. Here too, one might observe intuitively, the oath is being used to give *oomph* to one's statement — the speaker is trying to convey the magnitude of what he witnessed, and the oath-formula is the intensifier he reaches for.
