# Bava Kamma 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tziddei Reshus Harabim

### Gray Zone Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָלְמָא – בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ פָּטוּר; וְשָׁנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר: לָאו כֹּל כְּמִינָךְ דִּמְקָרְבַתְּ לְהוּ לְפֵירוֹתָךְ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּמְחַיְּיבַתְּ לְהוּ לְתוֹרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** that **usually,** if one digs **a pit within his** own **property** and he then declares his property to be ownerless, he is **exempt,** as he dug the pit when the property was his. **But here** it **is different, because** here the owner of the animal can **say** to the owner of the produce: **It is not all in your power to bring your produce close to the public domain and to** also **hold my ox liable for** eating **it.**

We pick up at the very bottom of 21a, dealing with the question of *tziddei reshus harabim* — the edges of *reshus harabim*. If an animal eats from that space, is it considered *reshus harabim* or not? The question is not simply about where the animal is standing versus where it is eating. If the food were clearly in *reshus hayachid*, that would straightforwardly be *reshus hanizak*. The point is that *tziddei reshus harabim* is a grey zone — a space closely associated with *reshus harabim* — and the question is whether it retains that character or not.

### Incursion vs. Head-Turn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּעָלְמָא – בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ חַיָּיב; דְּבִשְׁלָמָא בּוֹר, אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר לָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ; אֶלָּא פֵּירוֹת, מִי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר לָאו אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ?! הָא חָזֵי לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** can **say: Usually,** if one digs **a pit** or creates an obstacle that can cause damage **within his** own **property** and he then declares his property to be ownerless, he is **liable** for any damage that is caused by the pit. **As** with regard to the **pit, granted, it is possible to say** that **he was not aware,** meaning the injured party was not thinking about the possibility that there may be a pit there that could cause him harm, and therefore the one who dug the pit is liable. **But** with regard to **produce** that is spread out over the ground, **can it be said that** the animal **was not aware** of it? It cannot be, because the animal **sees** the produce.

As discussed previously, this was a debate between Rav and Shmuel. Everyone agreed that if the animal walked directly into *reshus harabim* and made a real incursion into that space, it would not be considered *reshus hanizak*. But if the animal merely turned its head and ate from the side — accessing food while still stationed in *reshus harabim* — that might itself be considered part of *reshus harabim*. That was the first phase of the discussion.

### Owner Permits Public Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַחְזֶרֶת תַּנָּאֵי הִיא – דְּתַנְיָא: אָכְלָה מִתּוֹךְ הָרְחָבָה – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית, מִצִּידֵּי הָרְחָבָה – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמְרִים: אֵין דַּרְכָּהּ לֶאֱכוֹל, אֶלָּא לְהַלֵּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that actually, the amoraic dispute about an animal that **turns** its head and eats produce at the side of the road **is** the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If an animal **ate** produce **from the** public **square,** the owner of the animal **pays** for **the benefit that** the animal **derived;** but if it ate **from the sides of the** public **square,** he **pays** for that **which it damaged.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda.** But **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Elazar say: It is not typical** for an animal **to eat** in the public domain **but only to walk** there. Consequently, the owner is liable.

The Gemara now offers a second interpretation, picking up from the bottom of 21a. *Vehachi itmar* — and so it was stated: *de'ika d'amri, bemachzeres kula alma lo pligi dechayaves* — in the case where the animal turns its head to eat from the side, everyone agrees it is *chayaves*, meaning that space is considered *reshus hanizak*. Since the animal must turn its head, the food is not directly in front of it during normal passage through *reshus harabim*, and so it falls outside the functional scope of *reshus harabim*. This represents a very narrow definition of *reshus harabim* — or equivalently, a broad definition of *reshus hanizak*.

The real dispute, then, is *ki pligi bemuktzeh mimakom reshuso lereshus harabim* — in a case where a property owner has set aside a portion of his private domain adjoining *reshus harabim* and permitted the public to use it. He retains legal title, but the space is functionally incorporated into the main thoroughfare — people actually walk through it regularly. Physically it may be "off to the side," but in terms of actual usage it is part of *reshus harabim*. The term *muktzeh* here means "set aside." The question is whether technical title or functional use governs its classification.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! אֶלָּא מַחְזֶרֶת אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: מַחְזֶרֶת נָמֵי – מְשַׁלֵּם מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** Initially, the Gemara understands Rabbi Yosei's opinion as also referring to a situation in which the animal eats from the sides of the public square, and since this is so, it seems that the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei is** the same as that of **the first *tanna*,** Rabbi Meir, and the mishna presents their opinions as differing. **Rather,** it is clear that **there is** a difference **between them** with regard to a case in which the animal **turns** its head to eat. **The first *tanna* holds** that with regard to an animal that **turns** its head, the owner of the animal **also pays** for **the benefit that** the animal **derived, while Rabbi Yosei holds** that he **pays** for that **which it damaged.**

*Vehachi itmar* — and it was stated thus: *amar Rav, lo shanu ela bemachzeres* — Rav says the rule that the animal is *chayav* applies only where the animal turns its head and the space is not functionally part of *reshus harabim*. But *aval bemuktzeh mimakom reshuso lereshus harabim* — if the owner has given his space over to be used by the public and it is functionally *reshus harabim* — *ptura* — the animal owner is exempt. Here, interestingly, Rav takes the more exempting position and Shmuel the more obligating one. *U'Shmuel amar, afilu bemuktzeh mimakom reshuso lereshus harabim chayaves* — even in that case it is *chayaves*, because technically it remains private property, not *reshus harabim*.

### Bor Analogy Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, מַחְזֶרֶת – אִי כְּרַב, אִי כִּשְׁמוּאֵל. וְהָכָא בְּ״בִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – מָר סָבַר: ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ – וְלֹא בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; וּמָר סָבַר: ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ – וְלֹא בִּרְשׁוּת הַמַּזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it is possible to say **that everyone** agrees about the *halakha* in the case of an animal that **turns** its head, **either in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav or in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel. And here they disagree** about the exemption inferred from the verse: **"It consumed in the field of another"** (Exodus 22:4), which renders the owner of an animal liable for damage classified as Eating. One **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that only if it ate from private property is its owner liable, as the verse stating the owner's liability means: **"And it consumed in the field of another," but not** when it eats **in the public domain. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** it means that even if it ate from the public domain its owner is liable, as the verse stating the owner's liability means: **"And it consumed in the field of another," but not** when it eats **in the domain of the one responsible for the damage;** only if the animal ate produce of another that was on the property of the animal's owner, is its owner exempt.

At this point the Gemara raises the possibility of mapping this debate onto a different dispute: *veneima bebor birshuso kemifligi* — perhaps Rav and Shmuel are debating the same question underlying the case of a *bor* dug in one's own domain. The scenario is: I dig a well on my property adjoining *reshus harabim*, then make the surrounding space *hefker* so that the public effectively uses it as an extension of the street. But I retain title to the *bor* itself. This differs from the classic *bor*, which is dug in *reshus harabim* — where one had no right to dig and owns nothing. Here I can say: you have no right to use my *bor*, I retained title.

The Gemara connects this to our case as follows: if I place fruit in the space I have opened to the public, and an animal trips on it, this is analogous to the *bor* — I own the fruit, I allowed the space to be used, but I still hold title. If one would be *chayav* for a *bor* of this kind, the fruit is seen as a violation, and the *bor* principle allows others to seize it. There is a later Gemara discussing how placing a hazard in *reshus harabim* gives passersby a right to seize it as their own — a kind of *knas*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּרְשׁוּת הַמַּזִּיק – לֵימָא: פֵּירָךְ בִּרְשׁוּתִי מַאי בָּעֵי? אֶלָּא דְּאִילְפָא וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: But not when it eats **in the domain of the one responsible for the damage?** What is the novelty in this statement? **Let** the owner of the animal **say** to the owner of the produce: **What is your produce doing on my property?** The owner would certainly not be liable if the produce is damaged in that case. **Rather,** it must be that the difference **between them** is with regard to the dispute **between Ilfa and Rabbi Oshaya** concerning an animal that ate produce from the back of another animal or the like. Rabbi Meir holds that one is never liable for damage classified as Eating in the public domain, even if his animal ate from the back of another, and Rabbi Yosei holds that if it ate off the back of another animal, this is equivalent to Eating from the property of the injured party.

The Gemara then attempts to work out the positions: *Rav d'amar patur, kasavar bor birshuto chayav* — Rav, who exempts the animal owner in the case of public use, holds that a *bor* in one's domain renders one *chayav*, and therefore since placing the fruit there constitutes a kind of violation, the animal is not liable for eating it. *U'Shmuel amar chayav, kasavar bor birshuto patur* — Shmuel, who holds the animal owner *chayav*, holds that a *bor* in one's domain is *patur*, meaning the placement of fruit was not a violation, and therefore the animal that eats from it is *chayav*.

But the Gemara immediately complicates this mapping. Rav, the Gemara says, *amar lach le'olam eima lach, be'alma bor birshuto patur* — Rav might actually hold that a *bor* in one's own domain is normally *patur*. If so, why does Rav still exempt the animal here? *V'shani hacha d'amar lav kol kaminach d'makrevas lah l'peirosecha l'reshus harabbim u'mechayavas lah l'sorai* — because the animal owner can say to the fruit owner: you had no right to place your fruit right at the edge of *reshus harabim* and then hold me liable.

### Functional Definition

The Gemara ultimately concludes that this entire *bor* detour was a distraction. Either direction one holds about *bor birshuto*, the question of *tziddei reshus harabim* stands on its own. The conclusion, as assumed from the outset, is simply a functional one: if the owner has made a space functionally part of *reshus harabim*, then it is *reshus harabim* for these purposes. It is not *sdeh acher* — it does not have a distinct functional use separate from the public thoroughfare. I have a right to pass through it with my animals; if you leave fruit there, you are taking your own risk. This is entirely analogous to leaving fruit in a genuine *reshus harabim*, and the animal owner is *patur*.

Shmuel, meanwhile, who holds you are *chayav* even in the *muktzeh* case, could also sidestep the *bor* mapping: even if *bor birshuto* would normally make one *chayav*, a *bor* is dangerous because it is flush with the ground and easy to miss. *Ela peiros mi ika lemeimar lav adateih ha chazi lehu* — fruit, by contrast, is clearly visible. So the fruit cannot constitute a *bor* that triggers a right to seize it. Since there is no such right, the animal owner remains *chayav* because, technically, this is still the owner's private domain.

The Gemara's conclusion, then, is to set the *bor* question entirely aside. The real debate is over how to define *reshus harabim* — whether functionally or technically — and that definition alone governs the ruling. The *bor* question neither exempts nor obligates in a way that affects this discussion.

This raises a genuinely interesting conceptual question. One way to frame the entire dispute of *tziddei reshus harabim* is as follows: is our fundamental principle that wherever it is *reshus hanizak*, one is *chayav*, with everything else *patur* — meaning the exemption category is the narrow one? Or is the fundamental principle that in *reshus harabim* one is *patur*, with the *chiyuv* reserved for *reshus hanizak* — meaning the obligation category is the narrow one? When you define the primary category narrowly, most intermediate cases fall into the "not A" space. Whether we frame this as defining *reshus hanizak* narrowly or defining *reshus harabim* narrowly determines how we treat the many intermediate spaces — *tziddei reshus harabim*, *chatzer shel shneihem*, and so on. The debate about how functional versus formalistic to be in defining *reshus harabim* is really a debate about which of these two conceptual orientations governs.

## Mechazeret Debate

### Beraita Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַכֶּלֶב וְהַגְּדִי שֶׁדִּלְּגוּ מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה – פְּטוּרִין. מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה – חַיָּיבִין. אָדָם וְתַרְנְגוֹל שֶׁדִּלְּגוּ; בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה, בֵּין מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה – חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **If a dog or a goat jumped from below to** something that was **above** them and thereby caused damage, their owners are **exempt,** as this is atypical behavior. But if they jumped **from above to below,** their owners are **liable** to pay the full cost of any damage they cause, as this is typical behavior. **If a person or a chicken jumped** and broke something, **regardless of whether they jumped from above to below or from below to above, they are liable.**

The Gemara now asks: *leima mechazeres tanna'ei hi* — perhaps the question of *mechazeres* (whether an animal that turns its head to eat from the side triggers liability) is itself a Tannaitic dispute. The Gemara cites a *beraita*: *achla mitoch harachavah meshalemes mah shehenis, mitzidai harachavah meshalemes mah shehezika, divrei Rabbi Meir* — if it ate from the street itself it pays *mah shehenis* (as in *reshus harabim*), while if it ate from the sides of the street it pays *mah shehezika* (as in *reshus hanizak*). These are the words of Rabbi Meir. The *beraita* then gives this position as the view of both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Elazar say: *ein darkah le'echol ela lehalech* — an animal does not eat, it only walks. It should keep moving; it should not be eating from the side of the street at all.

### Universal Agreement

The Gemara then says: *ela mechazeres ika beiniyahu* — perhaps the difference between the positions is specifically the case of *mechazeres*, where the animal turns its head. According to the *Tanna Kamma*, if the animal physically stands on the side of the street, that is *chayav*, but if it merely turns its head to eat from the side, it is *patur*. Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Elazar, however, would say that even turning its head renders one liable — an animal should be walking straight, and any eating it does from the side is in a space it has no business accessing, which is *reshus hanizak*.

But the Gemara pushes back: *dekulei alma mechazeres ikar heizeka shamur* — one might say that actually everyone agrees on *mechazeres*, whether treating it as always *chayav* (because animals do not normally turn their heads, so it falls outside *reshus harabim*) or always *patur* (because animals sometimes do turn their heads and the space is associated with *reshus harabim*). Either position could be held by any of the Tannaim.

### Sdeh Acher Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא בְּ״בִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – מָר סָבַר: ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ – וְלֹא בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; וּמָר סָבַר: ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ – וְלֹא בִּרְשׁוּת הַמַּזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** And here they disagree** about the exemption inferred from the verse: **"It consumed in the field of another"** (Exodus 22:4), which renders the owner of an animal liable for damage classified as Eating. One **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that only if it ate from private property is its owner liable, as the verse stating the owner's liability means: **"And it consumed in the field of another," but not** when it eats **in the public domain. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** it means that even if it ate from the public domain its owner is liable, as the verse stating the owner's liability means: **"And it consumed in the field of another," but not** when it eats **in the domain of the one responsible for the damage;** only if the animal ate produce of another that was on the property of the animal's owner, is its owner exempt.

The Gemara therefore reframes the dispute as a debate over the meaning of *uv-ve-ar bi-sdeh acher* — "and it grazes in another's field" (Shemos 22:4). *Mar savar, uv-ve-ar bi-sdeh acher velo b'reshus harabim* — one opinion holds the verse is excluding *reshus harabim*, meaning as long as it is not *reshus harabim*, liability attaches; thus *tzidei rechava* is not *reshus harabim* and one is *chayav*. *U'mar savar, uv-ve-ar bi-sdeh acher velo b'reshus hamazik* — the other opinion holds the verse only excludes the tortfeasor's own domain; everywhere else, one is *chayav*.

### Conceptual Framework

This is a striking conceptual observation. We have a continuum: *reshus harabim* on one end, *reshus hanizak* on the other, and many intermediate spaces in between — *tzidei rechava*, *reshus shel shneihem*, and others. Until now the question was: how do we define *sdeh acher*? Does liability require the victim's field specifically — a space the tortfeasor entirely violated by entering — or does it apply to any space that is not *reshus harabim*?

There are two ways to frame this. On one reading, the verse is telling us that one is *chayav* in *reshus hanizak* — making *reshus hanizak* the relevant category, and everything else is *patur*. On another reading, the verse is telling us one is *patur* in *reshus harabim* — making *reshus harabim* the relevant exemption category, and everything else generates *chiyuv*. This is a classic definitional dynamic: whichever category you define as primary becomes the narrow one, because all the borderline cases fall into the "not A" space. If the *chiyuv* category is primary (narrowly defined as *reshus hanizak*), many intermediate cases are *patur*. If the *patur* category is primary (narrowly defined as *reshus harabim*), many intermediate cases are *chayav*.

### Hamazik-Only Theory

The Gemara now entertains a far more radical suggestion. *U'mar savar, uv-ve-ar bi-sdeh acher velo b'reshus hamazik* — the verse may be excluding only *reshus hamazik*, the tortfeasor's own domain. On this reading, one would be *chayav* everywhere else — including in *reshus harabim* itself. This is genuinely surprising. If this were true, how would it explain our *beraita*? The language *ein darkah le'echol ela lehalech* would be saying: an animal should only be walking in *reshus harabim*, and anything it does beyond walking — including eating — generates liability. The only exempt space would be the tortfeasor's own property. The Gemara itself acknowledges the difficulty: *lama torcha b'reshuti mai bayit* — one does not need a verse to tell you that if you bring your fruit into my domain, I am not liable. *Reshus hamazik* would not need to be excluded by a biblical verse, since it is self-evident.

### Gemara Rejects Theory

The Gemara therefore abandons the *reshus hamazik* theory and suggests instead that the *beraita*'s dispute may correspond to a different disagreement — that of Ilfa and Rabbi Oshaya, concerning an animal standing in *reshus harabim* and eating off the back of another animal. The *Tanna Kamma* may hold that any eating in *reshus harabim* makes one *patur*, while Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Elazar may hold that an animal jumping onto another animal in *reshus harabim* — since it should have been walking — renders one *chayav*. The phrase *ein darkah le'echol ela lehalech* would mean: you cannot prove from normal eating in *reshus harabim* that one is *patur*, because there are things an animal does in *reshus harabim* that it should not be doing, such as jumping onto another animal's back.

The Gemara does not formally resolve the debate of *tziddei reshus harabim*, but the conceptual stakes are clear. Whether we understand the fundamental exemption as rooted in *reshus harabim* (and everything outside it creates *chiyuv*) or as rooted in *reshus hanizak* (and everything outside it creates *patur*) determines how we handle every intermediate case. These marginal situations are always illuminating precisely because they force us to examine what the underlying principle really is.

## Regel Cases

### Roof Jump Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכֶּלֶב וְהַגְּדִי שֶׁקָּפְצוּ מֵרֹאשׁ הַגָּג, וְשָׁבְרוּ אֶת הַכֵּלִים – מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן מוּעָדִין. הַכֶּלֶב שֶׁנָּטַל חֲרָרָה וְהָלַךְ לְגָדִישׁ, אָכַל הַחֲרָרָה וְהִדְלִיק הַגָּדִישׁ – עַל הַחֲרָרָה מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְעַל הַגָּדִישׁ מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a dog or a goat that jumped from a rooftop and broke vessels** while doing so, their owners **must pay the full** cost of the **damage** to the vessels **because** these animals **are** deemed **forewarned** concerning to jumping. With regard to **a dog that took a cake** that had been baked directly on hot coals, **and went to a stack** of grain to eat it, **and it ate the cake** and at the same time **ignited the stack** of grain with a coal that it had taken along with the cake, the owner of the dog must **pay the full** cost of the **damage for the cake, and** he must **pay for half** the cost of **the damage to the stack** of grain.

The Gemara now doubles back to the category of *regel*. The *mishna* states: *hakelav vehagidi shekaftzu merosh hagag veshivru et hakelim meshalem nezek shalem mipnei shehen muadin* — a dog or a goat that jumped from the roof and broke vessels below pays *nezek shalem*, full damages, because they are *muad* for this. It is essentially a category of *regel* — it is foreseeable and therefore not a *shinuy*. The *mishna* then introduces the case that will occupy much of the subsequent discussion: *hakelav she-natal chararah* — a dog that took a biscuit, went to a *gadish* (a wheat stack — considerably more valuable than a simple haystack), ate the biscuit, and lit the wheat stack on fire. *Al hacharara meshalem nezek shalem ve'al hagadish meshalem chatzi nezek* — for the biscuit it pays full damages, for the wheat stack it pays half. This is presumably a form of *tzroros* — the damage to the stack was incidental, not caused directly by the body of the animal.

### Jumping Down Natural

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּקָפְצוּ, הָא נָפְלוּ – פָּטוּר; אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: תְּחִלָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה וְסוֹפוֹ בְּאוֹנֶס – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the mishna: **The reason** the owners must pay the full cost of the damage is **because** the animals **jumped** off the rooftop. This indicates that if **they fell** off the roof, they would be **exempt** from all liability despite his obligation to keep them from climbing onto the roof and jumping down from there. **Apparently,** the *tanna* **holds** that in an incident that **begins with negligence,** meaning carelessness or even an intention to cause damage, **and ends in an accident,** the one who caused the damage is **exempt,** as in this case the owner was careless in allowing the animals to go to the rooftop, but since they did not jump off the roof but rather fell accidentally, he is exempt.

The *mishna*'s logic regarding the roof jump is that *ta'ama d'kaftzu* — the reason liability attaches is that they jumped. *Ha naflu patur* — had they merely fallen, the owner would be exempt. This implies a principle: *alma kasavar techilato bifshiya vesofo be-ones patur* — the Gemara infers that the *mishna* holds that if one began with negligence but the damage was ultimately completed by an unforeseeable event (*ones*), one is *patur*.

### Negligence + Oness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן דִּמְקָרְבִי כֵּלִים לְגַבֵּי כוֹתֶל – דְּכִי קָפְצִי בִּקְפִיצָה, לָא נָפְלִי עֲלַיְיהוּ; וַאֲפִילּוּ תְּחִלָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The case in the *baraita* is a case **where the vessels were brought close to the wall** by their owners, **so** that **when** the animals **jump** off the rooftop **in** an ordinary **leap they do not fall on top of them,** and the vessels broke because the animal fell and didn't jump. **And** since in an ordinary circumstance no damage should occur, **this** case does **not even begin with negligence.** Since the damage was caused by falling, the entire case is ruled an accident.

The Gemara asks: this works well according to the opinion that *techilato bifshiya vesofo be-ones patur* — but what of the opinion that one is *chayav* in such a case, since were it not for the initial negligence, the unforeseeable event would not have led to damage? The Gemara answers: *kegon she-keriv kelim legabi kotel* — the case is where the vessels were right up against the wall. *De-ki kaftzu lo nafli aleihu* — when the animal jumps, it carries forward momentum and would not have landed on them. The vessel owner placed the vessels where they would only be damaged by a fall, not by a jump. Therefore, there was never any *peshiya* with respect to those vessels in the first place — even the initial act was not negligent in relation to them. This resolves the difficulty even for the opinion that *techilato bifshiya vesofo be-ones* generates *chiyuv*, because here there was no *techilato bifshiya* regarding these specific vessels at all.

### Narrow Wall Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ נָפְלוּ נָמֵי חַיָּיב – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּכוֹתֶל רָעוּעַ. מַאי נִיהוּ – דְּאִבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאַסּוֹקֵי דַּעְתָּא דְּנָפְילִ[י] אַרְחֵי? סוֹף סוֹף, לָא נְפוּל אַרְחֵי – וּנְפוּל אִינְהוּ; תְּחִלָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה וְסוֹפוֹ בְּאוֹנֶס הוּא! לָא צְרִיכָא, בְּכוֹתֶל צַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava: Sometimes** one can be **obligated** to pay **even** in a case where the animals **fell** from the wall of their owner's house. **You find** such a case **where the wall was unstable,** and the owner was negligent in allowing the animals to go up to the roof due to the danger of the wall collapsing. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? Is it **because it should have occurred to him that bricks may fall** from the wall and break the vessels? But **ultimately, no bricks fell and** instead **the** animals **fell,** so **this is** actually a case that **begins with negligence and ends in an accident.** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* **with regard to** a case of **a narrow wall,** in which case it is clear that if they climb up there they will fall, and that is why he is liable for the damage they cause by falling.

*Amar Rav Zevid mishmeih d'Rava* — Rav Zevid in the name of Rava adds: *pe'amim she-afilu naflu nami chayav* — there are times when even a fall generates *chiyuv*, even according to the opinion that *techilato bifshiya vesofo be-ones patur*. This is because in certain circumstances, the falling itself is foreseeable — making the owner *poshea* with respect to the fall as well. When would that be? *Mashkachat lah bekotel reuah* — a shaky wall, where one should have anticipated the animal might fall.

The Gemara probes this: would we really say that placing an animal on a shaky wall makes the owner negligent with respect to falling? Perhaps he anticipated a brick falling, not the animal. *Sof sof lo nafal archa ve-nafal inhu* — in the end it was not the brick that fell but the animal itself, which remains an *ones*. The Gemara's response is direct: *lo, tzericha be-kosel tzar* — the case is a narrow wall, where the animal could clearly be expected to fall off. There one was genuinely negligent about the falling itself, and *chiyuv* follows even for the fall.

### Up vs. Down Jump

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הַכֶּלֶב וְהַגְּדִי שֶׁדִּילְּגוּ; בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה, בֵּין מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה – פְּטוּרִין! תַּרְגְּמַאּ רַב פָּפָּא: דְּאָפֵיךְ מֵיפָךְ – כַּלְבָּא בִּזְקִירָא וְגַדְיָא בִּסְרִיכָא. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי פְּטוּרִים? פָּטוּר מִנֶּזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְחַיָּיבִין בַּחֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **If a dog or a goat jumped,** regardless of **whether** they jumped **from above to below or from below to above,** their owners are **exempt** from all liability? **Rav Pappa interpreted** it in the following way: Their manners of movement **were changed** from the typical manner of movement for their species. The **dog** moved **by leaping [*bizkira*],** while the **goat** moved **by climbing [*bisrikha*].** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, why** are the owners **exempt** from liability? After all, they still caused damage. The Gemara answers: The *baraita* does not intend to say that they are completely exempt, but that they are **exempt from** paying **the full** cost of the **damage; they are,** however, **liable** to pay **half** the cost of **the damage,** as is the *halakha* in any case of damage caused by atypical behavior, as such acts are classified as Goring.

A *beraita* is cited: *hakelav vehagidi she-dilgu mi-mata le-ma'ala* — a dog or goat that jumped upward — *pturim*, they are exempt. *Mi-lemala le-mata chayavin* — jumping downward, they are *chayavin*. The assumption apparently embedded here is that jumping down is the natural direction for these animals, while jumping up is unusual. The Gemara then cites a conflicting *beraita* which exempts in both directions — whether the animal jumps up or down. Rav Papa resolves this: *de-afich mi-pach* — the reason the second *beraita* exempts both directions is not about the direction itself but about the manner of movement. *Kalba be-zikira* — the dog performs some kind of flip in midair; *ve-gadayya be-sericha* — the goat does some kind of climbing maneuver along the wall. Because of these unusual movements, the question is not simply whether it jumped up or down, but whether it moved in an unexpected, irregular fashion.

### Half Payment Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֶּלֶב שֶׁנָּטַל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **a dog that took** a cake that had been baked directly on hot coals, and went to a stack of grain to eat it, and it ate the cake and at the same time ignited the stack of grain with a coal that it had taken along with the cake, the owner of the dog must pay the full cost of the damage for the cake, and he must pay for half the cost of the damage to the stack of grain.

The Gemara then asks: *ei hachi amai pturin* — if the unusual movement makes it a *shinuy*, why are they entirely exempt? A *shinuy* should produce half-payment, not complete exemption. The Gemara answers: *patur min ha-nezek shalem ve-chayav be-chatzi nezek* — they are exempt from full damages but *chayav* for half. The manner of movement constitutes a *shinuy*, placing the case in the *keren* category and generating *chatzi nezek*. This is consistent with the general principle that extraordinary or unexpected acts of damage that fall short of complete *ones* warrant partial payment rather than full liability or full exemption.

## Fire Concept Debate

### Arrow vs. Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אִשּׁוֹ – מִשּׁוּם חִצָּיו. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אִשּׁוֹ – מִשּׁוּם מָמוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to damage caused by a fire lit by one person spreading to a location other than where it was lit, the Gemara cites a dispute among the *amora'im*: **It was stated: Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** His liability for damage caused by **his fire is due to** its similarity to **his arrows,** meaning that damage caused by a fire in a location other than where it was lit is comparable to damage caused by an arrow shot at a distant target. **And Reish Lakish says:** His liability for the damage caused by **his fire is due to** its similarity to **his property;** he is responsible for this damage just as he is responsible for damage caused elsewhere by any of his possessions, e.g., one of his animals.

We now turn to what is perhaps the most conceptually central *sugya* of our *daf*. *Itmar* — it was stated: the Gemara poses the fundamental question about the nature of the Torah's obligation for fire damage. When one's fire spreads and causes damage, what is the conceptual basis of *chiyuv*?

### Rav Yochanan Position

*Rabbi Yochanan amar eisho mishum chitzav* — Rav Yochanan holds that fire is categorized as *chitzav*, an arrow. When you light a fire and the wind carries it to cause damage, the entire sequence — lighting, spreading, impact — is seen as a continuation of your initial act, just as shooting an arrow is one integrated action from release to impact. The fire is your arrow; you did it.

### Reish Lakish Position

*Reish Lakish amar eisho mishum mamono* — Reish Lakish holds that fire is categorized as *mamono*, property. You created a fire, it escaped and did damage — you are liable for what your property did, not because you are seen as having personally committed the act of destruction. This is analogous to an animal that escapes and causes damage: one is liable not because one "did" the damage but because it was one's *mamon* that did it.

Before proceeding, several significant questions merit attention. First, what kind of fire does this *sugya* address? Classic *eish* involves negligence without intent. If one carefully tracks the wind direction, knows it will intensify, places the fire strategically, and steps aside — at what point does the intentionality and foreseeability turn this into something all would agree is equivalent to a direct act, rather than *mamon* or *chitzav*? This question is bracketed but worth noting.

Second, is this debate confined to the laws of *nezikin* — specifically, which of the two categories of *nezikin* (direct act or escaping property) does fire belong to — or does it extend to *kol haTorah kullah*? The *Nimukei Yosef* raises the question: if we say *eisho mishum chitzav*, when one lights *Shabbat* candles just before *Shabbat*, the continued burning on *Shabbat* should be like igniting fire on *Shabbat*. His answer is that even if fire is seen as one's arrow, the act is attributed entirely to the moment of lighting — so the act occurred before *Shabbat*. The practical *nafka mina* of this: if someone lights a fire, drops dead ten minutes later, and the fire then burns down a neighbor's house, can one collect from the heirs? If the act is attributed entirely to the moment of lighting, then yes — the tortfeasor caused the damage before his death. But the more fundamental question remains whether this categorization is a general legal principle or is specific to the laws of damages.

### Why Not Chitzav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? אָמַר לָךְ: חִצָּיו – מִכֹּחוֹ קָאָזְלִי, הַאי – לָא מִכֹּחוֹ קָאָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Reish Lakish did not state** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan?** The Gemara answers: He could have **said to you** that fire is not comparable to an arrow, as **an arrow proceeds** as a result **of his** direct **force,** while **this** fire **does not proceed** as a result **of his** direct **force,** but requires a wind to carry it from the location where it was lit to the location of the damage.

*V'Reish Lakish mai ta'ama la amar k'Rabbi Yochanan?* — Why does Reish Lakish not say like Rav Yochanan? *Chitzav mikocho ka azlei* — with a normal arrow, your physical force propels it; the energy is your own, communicated through the arrow from the moment of release to the moment of impact. *Hai lav mikocho ka azil* — but with fire, your act was the lighting, and then the wind came and did the spreading. You originated the process, but it was not your energy communicated through the flame. The connection between your act and the eventual damage is not tight enough to constitute *chitzav*.

### Why Not Mamono

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ? אָמַר לָךְ: מָמוֹנָא – אִית בֵּיהּ מַמָּשָׁא, הָא – לֵית בֵּיהּ מַמָּשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about the other opinion: **And what is the reason** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan did not state** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish?** The Gemara answers: He could have **said to you** that one's fire is not comparable to his possessions, as **property is** a tangible **substance** but **this** fire **is not** a tangible **substance.**

*V'Rabbi Yochanan mai ta'ama la amar k'Reish Lakish mishum mamono?* — Why does Rav Yochanan not agree that it is *mamono*? *Mamona it bei mamasha, ha leit bei mamasha* — because property has substance, and fire has no substance. One cannot truly "own" fire the way one owns an animal or an object. Even if there is some sense in which a fire "belongs" to the one who lit it, fire is pure energy — it is not the kind of thing that can be categorized as owned property causing damage.

### Tosafot Nuance

Two important *Tosafos* sharpen the conceptual picture. On *eish mishum chitzav*, *Tosafos* writes: *lo she'ya'avir b'atzmo ha'eish ela kol makom she'pasha v'lo shamar gechalto chitzav ninhu* — it is not required that one personally transmitted the fire; rather, anywhere one was negligent and failed to guard one's coals, that neglected fire becomes one's *chitzav*. This is a significant abstraction: even if one did not personally light the fire — say, someone else lit it and one was made responsible as a *shomer* who then neglected it — that neglected fire is still one's "arrow," because one's negligence made it so.

The parallel *Tosafos* on *eish mishum mamono* adds the inverse: *kol omer chiyuv mamona yesh bah v'lo she'yesh shelo d'afilu hidlik b'eish shel acher chayav* — it does not matter who owns the fire; even if one lights a fire using someone else's flame to ignite another's wheat stack, one is *chayav*. Ownership is not what triggers the *chiyuv*; it is the negligence and responsibility that determine it. Both *Tosafos* are pointing to the same underlying insight: whether the basis is *chitzav* or *mamono*, what associates the fire with a particular person is not merely the act of lighting or the fact of ownership, but one's assumption of responsibility and negligence in relation to it. This is arguably a greater *chiddush* in the case of *chitzav*, since there one is saying that even without personally performing the act of lighting, one's negligence alone makes the fire one's arrow.

## Burning Biscuit Case

### Biscuit Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַכֶּלֶב שֶׁנָּטַל חֲרָרָה כּוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִשּׁוֹ מִשּׁוּם חִצָּיו – חִצָּיו דְּכֶלֶב הוּא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to settle the dispute: **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to **a dog that took a cake** that had been baked directly on hot coals, and went to a stack of grain to eat it, and it ate the cake and at the same time ignited the stack of grain with a coal that it had taken along with the cake, the owner of the dog must pay the full cost of the damage for the cake, and he must pay for half the cost of the damage to the stack of grain. **Granted, according to the one who says** that one's liability for damage caused by **his fire is due to** its similarity to **his arrows,** this fire is similar to **the arrows of the dog,** and therefore he must pay for half the cost of the damage caused by the dog's force, as in a case of pebbles.

The Gemara now applies the positions of Rav Yochanan and Reish Lakish to the *mishna* about the dog that took a biscuit, went to the *gadish*, and lit it on fire. The *mishna* rules: full payment for the biscuit, half payment for the *gadish*. Each position faces a difficulty.

### Rav Yochanan's Problem

*Bishlama le-man de-amar eish mishum chitzav, chitzav d'kalba hu* — according to Rav Yochanan that fire is like an arrow, *chitzav d'kalba hu* — the dog's fire-spreading is like the dog's arrow. Just as I light a fire and it spreads, and that spreading is treated as my arrow (full *chiyuv*), so too when the dog creates the fire and it spreads to the *gadish*, that is the dog's arrow — but an indirect one, more akin to *tzroros*, which generates half-payment. This explains why the *mishna* rules *chatzi nezek* on the *gadish*: direct impact is full, indirect spreading is *tzroros*-like and therefore half.

### Reish Lakish's Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִשּׁוֹ מִשּׁוּם מָמוֹנוֹ – הַאי אֵשׁ, לָאו מָמוֹנוֹ דְּבַעַל כֶּלֶב הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that one's liability for damage caused by **his fire is due to** its similarity to **his property, this fire** is **not the property of the owner of the dog;** rather, it is the property of the owner of the cake, so why is the owner of the dog liable?

*Ella le-man de-amar eish mishum mamono* — but according to Reish Lakish, who says fire is categorized as *mamono*, there is a problem. *Hai eish lav mamono d'ba'al kalba hu* — this fire is not the property of the dog's owner. The dog created the fire; the fire is, as it were, the dog's fire, not the owner's fire. And one is not liable for damage done by property belonging to one's animal. The Torah makes one liable for what one's animals do and for what one's property does — but not for what property owned by one's animal does. A dog cannot own property in a legally meaningful sense, and so the fire the dog created is outside the scope of the owner's *chiyuv*.

The intuitive objection here is: isn't this all ultimately about negligence, and if one is responsible for one's dog generally, shouldn't one be responsible for whatever chain of events the dog sets in motion? The answer is that the first half of *Bava Kamma* is precisely about the balance between pure negligence-based liability and the specific categories of obligation involving one's property. One is obligated to control one's own property — not property that one's property effectively "acquired." The analogy that emerges from the discussion is apt: if a child picks up a stray animal that has not yet been brought home, the parent is not yet responsible for what that animal does. The stray animal is not the parent's property, and no assumed responsibility has yet attached. So too here — the dog's fire is not the owner's fire, and since it is not the owner's *mamono*, there is no liability under *mishum mamono*.

One might further note that *Rashi* makes the discussion somewhat easier by pointing out that the fire actually belongs to whoever lit the original flame — perhaps a *hefker* fire or a fire lit by someone else from which the dog took the ember. That person may be an *ones* (exempt because the dog took it without his ability to intervene), but the fire is still that person's, not the dog owner's. Even setting *Rashi*'s reading aside, the essential point stands: the dog owner did not make this fire, does not own it, and cannot be held liable under the *mamono* framework.

### Direct vs. Spread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּאַדְּיֵיהּ אַדּוֹיֵי; דְּעַל חֲרָרָה מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וְעַל מָקוֹם גַּחֶלֶת מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק, וְעַל גָּדִישׁ כּוּלֵּהּ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Reish Lakish** could have **said to you: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case where the dog took the coal and did not place the cake on the stack, rather **it threw it** onto the stack. The ruling of the mishna is: **For the cake** which the dog ate, the owner **must pay the full** cost of the **damage,** and **for the** specific **spot** on the stack of grain that was damaged by the throwing of the **coal** there, the owner must **pay for half** the cost of **the damage** as in a case of pebbles. **And for the stack** of grain **as a whole,** he is **exempt,** because his liability for damage caused by the spread of the fire is due to its similarity to his property, and in this case it was not his property.

The resolution requires distinguishing between two different phases of the damage. *Mekom gechelet* — the immediate point of direct contact between the ember and whatever it touches — is conceptually different from the subsequent spreading of fire. Direct contact, where the coal or ember itself burns the surface it strikes, is a case of *kocho* — the power or force exerted by the animal. This is not yet the concept of *eish* as understood in the context of the *chitzav/mamono* debate. When one takes a torch and holds it directly against something, everyone agrees that is the actor's direct act or *kocho* — not a question of whether fire is *chitzav* or *mamono*. Similarly, when the dog throws or deposits the burning biscuit and it burns the immediate point of contact, that is *kocho d'kalba* — the dog's direct force.

### Reish Lakish Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אִשּׁוֹ – מִשּׁוּם חִצָּיו – חִצָּיו דְּגָמָל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rabbi Yoḥanan** can explain the mishna in a more straightforward manner. It is discussing a case **where** the dog **placed** the cake with the coal directly on the stack of grain in order to eat the cake. Accordingly, the owner of the dog must **pay the full** cost of the **damage for the cake and for the** specific **spot** where the **coal** was put down, as these cases of damage were caused directly by the dog. **And for** the rest of **the stack** of grain, he must **pay for half** the cost of **the damage,** because in Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion one is liable for the damage caused by fire due to its similarity to arrows shot by his force. The damage to the rest of the stack of grain is an indirect result of the dog's force, and therefore his liability is according the *halakhot* of pebbles.

Reish Lakish therefore resolves his difficulty as follows: the half-payment for the *gadish* is not for fire that spread. It is for the immediate point where the ember came in direct contact and burned — *mekom gechelet* — which is *kocho*, analogous to *tzroros*, and generates *chatzi nezek*. The fire that subsequently spread beyond that contact point is not the owner's *mamono*, and for that he pays nothing. The *mishna*'s language — *al hagadish meshalem chatzi nezek* — refers to this immediate contact point, not to the entire *gadish* consumed by spreading fire.

One might object that the *peshat* of the *mishna* sounds as though one is paying half for the entire *gadish* that was burned. Reish Lakish must read it more narrowly: payment is only for the immediate place of contact, and the rest is *patur*. The broader spreading is categorized as the dog's fire — *mamono d'kalba* — for which the owner has no *chiyuv*.

## Camel & Flax Case

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: גָּמָל טָעוּן פִּשְׁתָּן וְעָבַר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; נִכְנְסָה פִּשְׁתָּנוֹ לְתוֹךְ הַחֲנוּת (וְדָלְקוּ), [וְדָלְקָה] בְּנֵרוֹ שֶׁל חֶנְווֹנִי, וְהִדְלִיק אֶת הַבִּירָה – בַּעַל גָּמָל חַיָּיב. הִנִּיחַ חֶנְווֹנִי נֵרוֹ מִבַּחוּץ – חֶנְווֹנִי חַיָּיב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּנֵר חֲנוּכָּה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (62b): In the case of **a camel** that was **laden with flax and was passing through the public domain,** and **its flax extended into a store** at the edge of the public domain, **and** the flax **caught fire from a lamp** in the store **belonging to the storekeeper, and** as a result of the burning flax the camel **set fire to the building** together with all its contents, **the owner of the camel** is **liable** for the damage. But if **the storekeeper placed his lamp outside** in the public domain, thereby causing the flax on the camel to catch fire, and consequently the building was set on fire, **the storekeeper** is **liable. Rabbi Yehuda says: In** a case where the lamp placed outside was **a Hanukkah lamp,** the storekeeper is **exempt** from liability, as there is a mitzva to place a Hanukkah lamp outside.

The Gemara brings a new case: *gamal ta'un pishtan ve-over bireshus harabim* — a camel carrying flax is passing through *reshus harabim*. *Nichnas pishtano letoch chanut* — the flax extends into a store. *Vedalka benero shel chanvani* — it is ignited by the storekeeper's candle. *Vehidlik es habirah* — and the fire then ignites the entire building. The straightforward ruling: *ba'al hagamal chayav* — the camel's owner is *chayav*. But if the *chanvani* left his candle outside the store in a place where passing animals would be likely to brush against it — *chanvani chayav* — the storekeeper is *chayav*, since it was effectively his fire, his negligence with his candle having set the whole sequence in motion. Rabbi Yehuda adds: *be-ner Chanukah patur* — if it was a *Chanukah* light, the storekeeper is *patur*, because he had a right to light his *Chanukah* candles outdoors. That case will be addressed later in a separate *mishna*.

The question the Gemara focuses on is the first scenario — why is the *ba'al hagamal chayav*, and what is the nature of that *chiyuv*?

### Rav Yochanan Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אִשּׁוֹ מִשּׁוּם חִצָּיו – חִצָּיו דְּגָמָל הוּא. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם מָמוֹנוֹ – הַאי אֵשׁ, לָאו מָמוֹנָא דְּבַעַל גָּמָל הוּא! אָמַר לָךְ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּמְסַכְסֶכֶת כׇּל הַבִּירָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says** that one's liability for the damage caused by **his fire is due to** its similarity to **his arrows,** the fire in the store **is** similar to **the arrows of the camel,** and that is why the camel's owner is liable. **But according to the one who says** that liability for the damage **is due to** its similarity to **his property, this fire** is **not the property of the owner of the camel.** The Gemara answers: **Reish Lakish** could have **said to you: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the camel moved the burning flax around and **ignited the entire building,** lighting one spot after another. The fire did not need to spread since the camel directly set the building on fire in each of those spots, and therefore this case is comparable to that of a dog who moved the coal around from place to place.

*Bishlama le-man de-amar mishum chitzav, chitzav de-gamal hu* — according to Rav Yochanan, the camel's action is like its arrow: its flax caught fire and the fire spread, so this is the camel's *chitzav*, and the owner is *chayav*. But here too the difficulty immediately presents itself: this should then be *chatzi nezek*, since the camel's spreading fire is analogous to *tzroros*. The *mishna* says *chayav* without qualification, which sounds like full payment — a difficulty for Rav Yochanan's position as it stood in the earlier case.

One might also wonder whether the analysis can be pushed further: I have flax, I know people have fires in their homes, I know my camel is overloaded with flax — does this knowledge make the flax itself into something like "my fire" heading toward an anticipated flame? This would be a way of treating the camel owner as directly negligent with respect to fire. But the Gemara does not pursue this line. The assumed framework is that the owner's negligence was in overloading the camel, and whatever *chiyuv* flows from that negligence is at most *tzroros*-level — *chatzi nezek*.

### Reish Lakish Problem

*Ella le-man de-amar mishum mamono, hai eish lav mamono de-ba'al gamal hu* — according to Reish Lakish, the same problem as in the dog case arises: this is not the camel owner's fire. The *chanvani* lit the candle; it is the *chanvani*'s fire. The camel owner was negligent with the flax, but once the flax caught fire, he had no ability to intervene — he is an *ones* at that point. He is not liable for the *chanvani*'s fire, and it never becomes his own *mamono*. He should therefore be exempt.

### Direct Contact Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אִם הִנִּיחַ חֶנְווֹנִי נֵרוֹ מִבַּחוּץ – חֶנְווֹנִי חַיָּיב. וְאִי בִּמְסַכְסְכֶת – אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? בְּשֶׁעָמְדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, say the latter clause** of the mishna: If **the storekeeper placed his lamp outside, the storekeeper** is **liable. And if** the case is one **where** the camel **ignited** the entire building, spot by spot, **why** is the storekeeper **liable?** His fire did not spread through the building; it was the camel that moved it around from one place to another. The Gemara answers: The case is one **where** the camel did not move around, but rather **stood** still, but since there was an extremely large load of flax on its back, once it caught fire it ignited the entire building simultaneously.

But then the Gemara confronts a new difficulty: if the animal is directly causing the burning by moving through the building — *mistachseches* — why is the *chanvani* the sole liable party when he places his candle outside? If it is the camel's direct contact that matters, then even when the *chanvani* was negligent with his candle, the camel owner should share liability, since it is the camel's direct presence that is burning the building.

### Oness via Urination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמְדָה וְסִכְסְכָה – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּחֶנְווֹנִי פָּטוּר וּבַעַל גָּמָל חַיָּיב! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָמְדָה לְהַטִּיל מֵימֶיהָ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the camel **stood** without moving from place to place, but did shift the load on its back and thereby **ignited** the entire building, **all the more so** it should be **that the storekeeper** should be **exempt** from any liability **and the owner of** the **camel** should be **liable,** as it was his responsibility to move the camel away from there. **Rav Huna bar Manoah said in the name of Rav Ika: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the camel **stood** still in order **to urinate** and while doing so set the building ablaze; in that case the incident is deemed an accident as the owner could not have moved the camel from the spot.

The Gemara's answer: *beshe'amdah* — the animal had stopped. *Amar Rav Huna bar Manoach mishmeih de-Rav Ika hacha bemai askinan kegon she-amdah lehatil mayim* — the specific scenario is one where the animal stopped to urinate. Once an animal is urinating, it cannot be moved. This is the key: the owner is now an *ones* with respect to his ability to intervene. He cannot control the animal's position at this moment.

### Negligence + Oness Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא – בַּעַל גָּמָל חַיָּיב, דְּלָא אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאַפּוֹשֵׁי בִּטְעִינָה. סֵיפָא – חֶנְווֹנִי חַיָּיב, דְּלָא אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאַנּוֹחֵי נֵרוֹ מֵאַבָּרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, in **the first clause,** the **owner of the camel** is **liable,** as **he should not have overloaded** the camel with flax to such a degree that it extended out so far from the sides that it could catch fire and ignite the store. But in **the latter clause,** the **storekeeper** is **liable, as he should not have placed** the **lamp outside.**

This allows the Gemara to account for both halves of the ruling. *Reisha ba'al gamal chayav delo ibai lei le'afushei bete'inah* — in the first scenario, the owner is *chayav* because he should not have overloaded the camel with flax. His negligence was the overloading; everything that followed — the flax entering the store, catching fire, and directly burning the building while the camel stood and urinated — is a direct consequence of that negligence. The burning is by direct contact, not spreading, so it falls within the scope of *chiyuv* even for Reish Lakish.

*Seifa chanvani chayav delo ibai lei le'anuchei nero mibara'i* — in the second scenario, the storekeeper is *chayav* because he should not have left his candle outside. His negligence began the sequence. The camel owner, meanwhile, was not negligent in overloading — the scenario posits that the flax entered the store because of the *chanvani*'s negligently placed candle, not because of any excess load on the camel. By the time the fire starts, the camel owner cannot intervene because the animal is urinating. His *ones* is established: he did not begin negligently, and now he physically cannot stop it. The *chanvani*'s negligence started the chain; the camel owner bears no *chiyuv*.

The overall picture for Reish Lakish is now coherent: one can never be *chayav* under the *mamono* framework when one's animal ignites a fire and the fire spreads on its own — that spread is the animal's fire, not the owner's *mamono*. The only basis for *chiyuv* is when the animal itself is in direct contact — *mistachseches* — with what it burns. And even then, if one cannot intervene because of an *ones* (here, the animal urinating), the liability shifts to whoever was negligent at the outset of the causal chain.
