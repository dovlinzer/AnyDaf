# Bava Kamma 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hekdesh Exemptions

### Overview & Context

Daf 38 is a particularly central page of Talmud, devoted in large part to the discussion of legal obligations between Jews and non-Jews. But the daf opens — on 37b — with a *mishna* concerning *hekdesh*, Temple property, and its relationship to the ordinary laws of goring. What follows is a daf that moves from technical hermeneutics to broad questions of theology, universalism, and the moral complexity of particularistic law.

### Mishna: Hekdesh Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְשֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁנָּגַח לְשׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹר רֵעֵהוּ״ – וְלֹא שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an ox of a Jew that gored a consecrated ox, and** conversely, **a consecrated** ox **that gored a non-sacred ox,** i.e., an ox owned by a Jew, the owner of the ox is **exempt** from paying compensation, **as it is stated:** "And if one man's ox hurts **the ox of another"** (Exodus 21:35). It is derived from the phrase "the ox of another" that one is liable only if it is a non-sacred ox, **but not** if it is **a consecrated ox,** which belongs to the Temple treasury, regardless of whether the latter was the ox that gored or the ox that was gored.

The *mishna* states: if the ox of a layperson (*hedyot*) gores an ox belonging to *hekdesh*, or if an ox of *hekdesh* gores that of a layperson, both parties are exempt from payment — *patur* in both directions. The verse cited is *shor re'ehu* — "the ox of his neighbor" (Shemot 21:35) — which the *mishna* reads as excluding *hekdesh*: the Temple is not your *re'ehu*, not your fellow, and therefore the entire *parasha* of liability does not apply. One might have assumed that *hekdesh* would always enjoy a privileged position and be entitled to compensation. Nevertheless, on the basis of this *pasuk*, the exemption runs in both directions.

A practical question presents itself: what does the *beit hamikdash* do with an ox that has been killed? It sells it — the ox passes out of *hekdesh* status, is redeemed into *chulin*, and the proceeds go to the Temple treasury. Not every ox belonging to the Temple is a *korban*; oxen might be donated or held as an asset of the Temple's estate.

### Censorship & Terminology

Before proceeding to the *mishna*'s second case, a note on textual history is essential. The printed *girsa* of the *mishna* uses the word *kena'ani* — "Canaanite" — where the original text almost certainly read *nochri* or *goy*. The standard term for non-Jew in the Talmud is either *nochri*, drawn from the *Torah* itself (*m'chor l'nochri*), or *goy*, a more rabbinic term, both of which are simply neutral words meaning "foreigner."

The word *akum* — an acronym for *ovdei kochavim u'mazalot*, worshippers of stars and constellations — entered the Talmud through a process of self-censorship. In the Middle Ages, when Jewish converts to Christianity informed the Church about passages in the Talmud that were unfavorable to non-Jews, the Church began censoring and burning the Talmud. To preempt further persecution, Jewish communities replaced sensitive terms like *nochri* and *goy* with *akum*, allowing the argument that such passages referred only to ancient pagan star-worshippers and not to contemporary Christians. The Church, of course, eventually reinterpreted *akum* as a reference to their own sacred figures and did not accept the explanation.

Crucially, the word *kena'ani* — "Canaanite" — is a signal of an even deeper layer of censorship. If a passage uses *kena'ani* rather than *akum*, it marks a text that was considered especially problematic. The original sources, as attested throughout the *Rishonim*, consistently have *nochri* or *goy*. What is printed is a medieval gloss. This context matters enormously for understanding the *sugya* that follows.

### Non-Jew Cases in Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹר שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּגַח לְשׁוֹר שֶׁל גּוֹי – פָּטוּר. וְשֶׁל גּוֹי שֶׁנָּגַח לְשׁוֹר שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל – בֵּין תָּם בֵּין מוּעָד, מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **an ox of a Jew that gored the ox of a gentile,** the owner of the belligerent ox is **exempt** from liability. **But** with regard to an ox **of a gentile that gored the ox of a Jew,** regardless of **whether** the goring ox was **innocuous or forewarned,** the owner of the ox **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

This asymmetry is striking and troubling in two respects. First, it is simply unfair: one party pays nothing, the other pays in full. Second, it departs from the ordinary graduated rule under which a *tam* ox pays only half-damages. The non-Jew pays full in either case. Both of these anomalies will receive sustained attention in the *Gemara*.

## Hekdesh Gemara Analysis

### Tanna Kamma vs. R. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּמ׳ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא, דְּתַנְיָא: שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְשֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹר רֵעֵהוּ״ – וְלֹא שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט – פָּטוּר; וְשֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – בֵּין תָּם בֵּין מוּעָד, מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a non-sacred ox that gored a consecrated ox, or a consecrated** ox **that gored a non-sacred ox,** the owner of the ox is **exempt** from liability, **as it is stated: "The ox of another,"** indicating: **But not a consecrated ox. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says:** With regard to **a consecrated ox that gored a non-sacred ox,** the Temple treasury is **exempt** from liability; **but** with regard to **a non-sacred** ox **that gored a consecrated ox, whether** it was **innocuous or forewarned,** the owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

The *Gemara* opens by noting that the *mishna*'s position — full mutual exemption between *hedyot* and *hekdesh* — is not in accordance with Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya. A *beraita* records the dispute explicitly. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that both are *patur* in both directions, derived from *shor re'ehu* — the ox of your fellow — which excludes *hekdesh* categorically. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya disagrees: if *hekdesh* gores a layperson's ox, *hekdesh* is exempt; but if a layperson's ox gores that of *hekdesh*, the layperson pays full damages — *nezek shalem* — whether the ox is *tam* or *mu'ad*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מַאי קָא סָבַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אִי ״רֵעֵהוּ״ דַּוְקָא – אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁנָּגַח שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ לִיפְּטַר! וְאִי ״רֵעֵהוּ״ לָאו דַּוְקָא – אֲפִילּוּ דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ נָמֵי, כִּי נָגַח דְּהֶדְיוֹט לִיחַיַּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: What does Rabbi Shimon** ben Menasya **hold?** Why does he distinguish between a consecrated ox that gored a non-sacred ox and a non-sacred ox that gored a consecrated ox? **If** the phrase **"of another"** is meant in **a precise** manner, then **even** with regard to **a non-sacred** ox **that gored a consecrated ox** the owner of the belligerant ox **should be exempt** from liability, as the victim is not the ox of another, but belongs to the Temple treasury. **And if** the phrase **"of another"** is **not** meant in **a precise** manner, but rather, includes all oxen, then **a consecrated** ox **that gored a non-sacred** ox **should render** the Temple treasury **liable as well.**

The *Gemara* immediately raises the logical problem: *mai kasavar Rabbi Shimon* — what exactly is Rabbi Shimon's position? The difficulty is one of internal consistency. If the word *re'ehu* is being read in a precise, limiting sense (*davka*) — such that only transactions involving a fellow layperson generate liability — then both directions should be *patur*. This is exactly the *Tanna Kamma*'s position. If, on the other hand, *re'ehu* is *lav davka* — not technically precise, merely a general expression — then liability should flow in both directions.

This is worth pausing over. We are trained to read words like *re'echa*, *amitecha*, and *re'ehu* as technically limiting terms that exclude non-members of one's legal community — typically understood to mean non-Jews, or here, *hekdesh*. But in the *peshat* of the *pesukim*, this is far from obvious. The word *achicha* — "your brother" — is clearly used in contrast to *nochri* in some places (as in *lanachri tashich u'le'achicha lo tashich*), but *re'echa* and *amitecha* in their plain meaning appear simply to mean "another person." Indeed, *ve'ahavta l're'echa k'mocha* — love your neighbor as yourself — clearly includes everyone. The *Gemara*'s willingness here to raise the possibility that *re'ehu* is *lav davka* is therefore significant: it acknowledges that sometimes such terms are not meant in a technically exclusive sense, even while the halakhic tradition generally reads them as exclusive.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר ״רֵעֵהוּ״ דַּוְקָא, וּמִיהוּ דְּהֶדְיוֹט כִּי נָגַח דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּמִיחַיַּיב – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר דְּהֶדְיוֹט: וּמָה הֶדְיוֹט שֶׁנָּגַח שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט – חַיָּיב, כִּי נָגַח דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּמִיחַיַּיב? דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין – לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן! מָה לְהַלָּן – תָּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק, הָכָא נָמֵי חֲצִי נֶזֶק!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **actually** Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya **holds** that **"of another"** is meant in **a precise** manner, and accordingly, if a consecrated ox gores a non-sacred ox the Temple treasury is exempt from liability; **but nevertheless, when a non-sacred** ox **gores a consecrated** ox, **this is the reason** its owner **is liable: Because** Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya **infers it *a fortiori* from** the case of **a non-sacred** ox, as follows: **If** in the case of **a non-sacred** ox **that gores** another **non-sacred** ox the owner of the belligerent ox **is liable,** is it **not** clear **all the more so that when it gores a consecrated** ox the owner of the ox **is liable?** If this is Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's reasoning, then his ruling that the owner of the ox pays the Temple treasury the full cost of the damage, whether his ox was innocuous or forewarned, is problematic, as **it is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source.** In other words, a *halakha* derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference cannot be more stringent than the *halakha* of the source from which it is derived. Therefore, **just as there,** in a case where an individual's **innocuous** non-sacred ox gores another non-sacred ox, the owner pays only **half** the cost of **the damage, here too,** if an innocuous non-sacred ox gores a consecrated ox, its owner should be liable to pay only **half** the cost of **the damage.**

The *Gemara* presses the logical problem further. The *Tanna Kamma* has a coherent position: *re'ehu* excludes *hekdesh* in both directions. But Rabbi Shimon reads it asymmetrically. The *Gemara* proposes that even if *re'ehu* is *davka* — and thus *hekdesh* is excluded from the *parasha* entirely, making *hekdesh* exempt when it gores — one might still derive a *chiyuv* on the layperson who gores *hekdesh* through a *kal vachomer*: if a layperson's ox is liable when it gores another layperson's ox, how much more so when it gores the property of *hekdesh*. The *Gemara* then invokes the rule of *dayo* — it is sufficient for the law derived by inference to be equivalent to its source — meaning that such a *kal vachomer* would yield only the same measure of liability as applies in the ordinary *hedyot*-to-*hedyot* case, namely half-damages for a *tam*. This does not produce "full damages always," which is what Rabbi Shimon requires.

### Reish Lakish Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַכֹּל הָיוּ בִּכְלַל נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם; כְּשֶׁפָּרַט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב: ״רֵעֵהוּ״ גַּבֵּי תָם – רֵעֵהוּ הוּא דְּתָם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק; מִכְּלָל דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ – בֵּין תָּם בֵּין מוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Reish Lakish said** that Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's reasoning is as follows: In principle, **all** cases of damage **were included** among those **in** which the owner pays the **full** cost of the **damage.** The *halakha* that in a case of an innocuous ox the owner pays only half the cost of the damage is an exception to the rule, and **when the verse specified** the term **"of another" with regard to an innocuous** ox, it intended **that** it **is** specifically when one's **innocuous** ox gores the ox of **another** that the owner **pays** only **half** the cost of **the damage.** And **by inference,** if it gores **a consecrated** ox, **whether** the belligerent ox **is innocuous or forewarned** its owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

The *Gemara*'s resolution comes from Reish Lakish: *hakol hayu bichlal nezek shalem* — the original default is that everyone always pays full damages. This requires understanding the structure of the *pesukim*. In the verse dealing with the *tam* ox (*ki yigach shor ish et shor re'ehu*), the word *re'ehu* appears — "his neighbor's ox." The *pasuk* for *mu'ad*, by contrast, does not contain *re'ehu*; it reads simply that if the ox is known to be a gorer, the owner pays in full (*shaleim yeshalem*).

Reish Lakish argues as follows: the baseline rule, before any scriptural qualification, is full damages for everyone in every case. The *Torah* then introduced the word *re'ehu* specifically in the context of the *tam* — to teach that when you gore *your neighbor's* ox and it is a first offense, you pay only half. This qualification applies only to *re'ehu*: only when goring the ox of a fellow layperson do you receive the concession of half-damages. When you gore *hekdesh* — which is not your *re'ehu* — you revert to the default of full damages, even for a *tam*. Similarly, because *hekdesh* is not included in the *re'ehu* framework at all, it never benefits from the reduction to half — it always starts and stays at full.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא לְהַאי ״רֵעֵהוּ״ גַּבֵּי מוּעָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Because if so,** if one whose ox gores a consecrated ox is exempt from liability, **let the verse write this** phrase: **"Of another," with regard to** the case of **a forewarned** ox. One could then infer that the owner is exempt from liability in the case of an innocuous ox as well, as the liability with regard to an innocuous ox is less severe than with regard to a forewarned ox. The stating of this exemption specifically in the context of an innocuous ox indicates that the exemption is only concerning the leniency stated in the verse, that if the gored ox belongs to another person, the owner of the belligerent ox is liable to pay only half the cost of the damage.

The difficulty that remains, however, is this: if the logic is that one reverts to full damages when dealing with *hekdesh*, why doesn't *hekdesh* also pay full when it gores a layperson? Reish Lakish's argument, as the *Gemara* frames it, explains the asymmetry in the direction of the layperson paying *hekdesh*, but not in the reverse. The *Gemara* notes that if the *Torah* had wanted to exclude liability entirely — including full damages — it should have written *re'ehu* by the *mu'ad* rather than by the *tam*. Since it placed *re'ehu* only by the reduced liability case, the implication is that the default of full payment remains operative for all cases not covered by *re'ehu*.

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi's answer addresses the remaining difficulty — why *hekdesh* does not pay full when it gores a layperson — by focusing on the word *ish* at the opening of the *parasha*: *ki yigach shor ish*. The verse says "if a man's ox gores." When a layperson's ox gores *hekdesh*, the ox belongs to an *ish* — a person — and it has not gored *shor re'ehu*, since *hekdesh* is not *re'ehu*. Because the ox has not gored *re'ehu*, the layperson does not receive the reduction to half-damages and must pay full. Now consider the reverse: when *hekdesh*'s ox gores, the verse's opening condition — *shor ish*, the ox of a person — is not even met, since *hekdesh* is not an *ish*. If the ox does not even enter the threshold of the *parasha* through its opening clause, then the entire framework of liability simply does not apply. There is nothing to start from — not full, not half — and therefore *hekdesh* pays nothing at all. Rashi's reading is somewhat compressed, and the asymmetry it produces is admittedly difficult. Nevertheless, the logic is that a layperson's ox can enter the *parasha* (as a *shor ish*) and then be denied the half-damages reduction (because it gored *hekdesh*, not *re'ehu*), whereas *hekdesh*'s ox cannot enter the *parasha* at all.

## Non-Jew Liability Rules

### Rav Yosef's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ, אִי ״רֵעֵהוּ״ דַּוְקָא – דְּגוֹי כִּי נָגַח דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי לִיפְּטַר! וְאִי ״רֵעֵהוּ״ לָאו דַּוְקָא – אֲפִילּוּ דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל כִּי נָגַח דְּגוֹי נִחַיַּיב!
>
> **Translation:** This statement is difficult **whichever way you** look at it. **If** the phrase **"of another"** is meant in **a precise** manner, and therefore the liability applies only if his ox gores the ox of another Jew, **when a gentile's** ox **gores that of a Jew he should also be exempt** from liability. **And if** the phrase **"of another"** is **not** meant in **a precise** manner, then **even when a Jew's** ox **gores that of a gentile** the owner of the belligerent ox **should be liable.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the parallel problem regarding the non-Jew, and the same structural difficulty arises. If *re'ehu* is *davka*, excluding the non-Jew, then the non-Jew's ox goring a Jew's should also be exempt. If *re'ehu* is *lav davka* — a general term — then a Jew who gores a non-Jew should also be liable. The pure logic of the *pasuk* cannot yield the asymmetric result the *mishna* states. Rav Yosef, therefore, abandons the attempt to derive the asymmetry from *re'ehu* alone and instead brings a different verse entirely to ground the rule. The *pasuk* in Habakkuk (3:6) reads: *amad vayimoded eretz ra'ah vayater goyim* — God stood and measured the earth, He saw and scattered the nations. Rav Yosef reads *vayater goyim* as meaning that God rendered the property of the nations permissible to Israel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״עָמַד וַיְמֹדֶד אֶרֶץ, רָאָה וַיַּתֵּר גּוֹיִם״ – רָאָה שֶׁבַע מִצְוֹת שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם בְּנֵי נֹחַ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁלֹּא קִיְּימוּ – עָמַד וְהִתִּיר מָמוֹנָן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said** that the reason for this ruling is that **the verse states: "He stood and shook the earth; He beheld, and made the nations tremble [*vayyatter*]"** (Habakkuk 3:6). This is homiletically interpreted to mean that God **saw the seven mitzvot that the descendants of Noah accepted upon themselves** to fulfill, and **since they did not fulfill** them, **He arose and permitted [*vehittir*] their money to the Jewish people,** so that in certain cases Jews are not liable for damage caused to gentiles.

The reading is as follows: God measured the earth — meaning He assessed the moral state of the nations — saw that they had accepted upon themselves the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* and had not fulfilled them, and therefore declared their property free, in effect imposing a divine fine. Since they forfeited their obligations under the moral law they had accepted, Israel is not required to pay compensation when their animals cause damage to non-Jewish property.

This raises an important question about the scope of *hitir mamonam l'Yisrael* — rendering their money permissible. Does this mean outright license to steal from non-Jews? Tosafot is explicit that the *peshat* of this *sugya* gives no such license. The permission operates specifically within the domain of damage law — the question of whether one is obligated to pay or entitled to demand compensation — not as a general authorization for theft. Indeed, Tosafot notes that according to the position that *gezel nochri assur* — stealing from a non-Jew is forbidden — one understands immediately why a special *pasuk* is needed here and why its application is limited to these damage cases. According to the opposing position that theft from non-Jews is permitted, the *pasuk* would be superfluous — which is itself a difficulty.

### Rav Abbahu & Sheva Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״הוֹפִיעַ מֵהַר פָּארָן״ – מִפָּארָן הוֹפִיעַ מָמוֹנָם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the source for this *halakha* is **from here:** It is stated in reference to the giving of the Torah: "The Lord came from Sinai and rose from Seir unto them; **He appeared from Mount Paran"** (Deuteronomy 33:2), which is homiletically interpreted to mean: **From** the time God came from Mount **Paran,** when giving the Torah, **the money of** the gentile nations **appeared,** i.e., it was revealed and granted **to the Jewish people.**

Rav Abbahu derives the same rule from a different verse: *v'hofia me-Har Paran* — God revealed Himself from Mount Paran (Devarim 33:2). On Rashi's reading, this refers to the tradition that God offered the *Torah* to the nations at *Har Paran* and they refused — specifically because it contained prohibitions against violence and theft. The nations' refusal to accept the *Torah* demonstrates not merely that they declined additional obligations, but that they were unwilling to accept even the basic moral law. It is this that rendered their property permissible.

There is a question of what the underlying logic of *hitir mamonam* actually is. Is it simply a divine fine — a punishment, with Israel as the incidental beneficiary — or is there a deeper rational justification? A more substantive reading understands the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* as something like the Jewish conception of natural law, the foundational conditions of civilized society. If the nations are not fulfilling those obligations, they are, in effect, not participating in the social order that generates mutual legal obligations. The *Gemara* was written under Roman rule in *Eretz Yisrael*, and the context of oppression and lawlessness is not incidental. From this perspective, the rule is not an arbitrary ethnic privilege but a response to the practical reality that those who do not participate in civil society cannot claim its protections. This reading — that the nations are not careful (*zehirim*) about causing damage and do not maintain the systems of accountability that make civil compensation meaningful — appears to inform the Rambam's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: שׁוֹר שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל גּוֹי – פָּטוּר. שׁוֹר שֶׁל גּוֹי שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל – בֵּין תָּם בֵּין מוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עָמַד וַיְמֹדֶד אֶרֶץ, רָאָה וַיַּתֵּר גּוֹיִם״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״הוֹפִיעַ מֵהַר פָּארָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an ox of a Jew that gored the ox of a gentile,** the owner of the belligerent ox is **exempt** from liability. By contrast, with regard to **an ox of a gentile that gored the ox of a Jew, whether** it was **innocuous or forewarned,** the owner of the belligerent ox **pays the full** cost of the **damage, as it is stated: "He stood and shook the earth; He beheld, and made the nations tremble." And** another verse **states: "He appeared from Mount Paran."**

### Rambam's Rationale

The Rambam, in *Hilkhot Nizkei Mamon* (chapter 8, *halacha* 5), provides a marginal note on the lower right side of 37b: since non-Jews do not hold a person liable for damage caused by his animal, we follow their law with them — *harei anu danim imahem kedineihem* — and this is sourced in the Yerushalmi. This grounds the exemption from paying them in a principle of reciprocity: they themselves do not impose this form of liability, so we need not pay them under it.

The Rambam then adds, in the same location, that the reason non-Jews pay full damages to Jews — the fine imposed upon them — is because *einam zehirim b'mitzvot v'einam mesalkim hanezek*: they are not careful about their obligations and do not remove the source of damage. This is not merely a statement about ritual noncompliance with *sheva mitzvot*; the Rambam is saying that their failure to maintain orderly protection of others' property and persons — their broader disregard for the conditions of civil society — is what justifies the imposition of full liability against them. This appears to be a rationalization of Rav Abbahu's position, framing the asymmetric rule not as pure ethnic privilege but as a response to a structural failure in how the non-Jewish nations govern civil life.

### Later Qualifications

The discomfort this *sugya* generated is visible in subsequent *Rishonim*. Looking at the *Shita Mekubetzet* on 38, lower left, the following is cited: *ra'ah sheva mitzvot* — the *pasuk* says God saw the *sheva mitzvot* — *mashma dechol shemekaymim sheva mitzvot dinam etzlenu kedinenu etzlam*: this implies that any nation that does observe the *sheva mitzvot* would be treated by us as we are treated by them, with full legal parity. From this it follows that for nations governed by religious and ethical law — *omot hagedurot bedarkhei datot venimusim*, in the language of the Meiri — these distinctions would not apply. The Meiri's position, that nations which are bound by civilized norms of law and ethics are outside the scope of these discriminatory rules, finds its justification here.

The *Shita Mekubetzet* further explains the phrase *vayater goyim* as referring specifically to those who are cruel and wicked and who pay no regard to the suffering of others — *achzarim velev ra lahem* — and who are suspect with respect to all seven Noahide commandments. The point is not generic: it is specifically their disregard for causing damage and their indifference to others' welfare that justifies the fine. *Lefikach knasam hakatuv kedei sheyishmeru shoroteihem* — the *Torah* fined them in order that they should guard their animals. The *Shita Mekubetzet* then adds that *vechol zeh lo dibru ela al sheva umot* — all this was said only regarding the seven Canaanite nations — which is, of course, a further attempt to distance the rule from any general application to contemporary non-Jews. That this gloss ultimately found its way into the printed text itself, replacing *nochri* with *kena'ani*, reflects how deeply uncomfortable later authorities were with the rule's apparent scope.

Two things are significant here. First, there is a clear apologetic motivation — an awareness of how this rule would appear to outsiders and perhaps also an internal moral discomfort. But second, and more importantly, what these authorities are attempting is not merely apologetics but a genuine rational reconstruction: the rule is not simply "they are *reshaim*, so we can take advantage of them." Rather, it is that their failure to participate in the structures of civil and legal accountability is what generates the asymmetry. This makes the rule comprehensible in a way that pure ethnic privilege does not.

## Rabbi Meir's Universalism

### Tanya on Non-Jew Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ גּוֹי וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה – שֶׁהוּא כְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה אֹתָם הָאָדָם, וָחַי בָּהֶם״ – ״כֹּהֲנִים וּלְוִיִּים וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״אָדָם״; הָא לָמַדְתָּ, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ גּוֹי וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה – הֲרֵי הוּא כְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** do they **not** receive reward for fulfilling those mitzvot? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir says: From where** is it derived **that even a gentile who engages in Torah is** considered **like a High Priest? The verse states** with regard to the mitzvot: **"Which if a person does, he shall live by them"** (Leviticus 18:5). It **is not stated:** Which if **priests and Levites and Israelites** do, they shall live by them, **but rather: A person,** indicating that all people are included. **You have therefore learned that even a gentile who engages in Torah** study **is** considered **like a High Priest.**

A *beraita* records the rule regarding the non-Jew's ox in precisely the form stated in the *mishna*: *shor shel nochri shenagach shor shel Yisrael bein tam bein mu'ad meshalem nezek shalem* — whether *tam* or *mu'ad*, full damages. Two *pesukim* are cited as the source: *amad vayimoded eretz ra'ah vayater goyim* (Rav Yosef) and *v'hofia me-Har Paran* (Rav Abbahu / Rabbi Yochanan). The *Gemara* asks: why are two verses needed? If the first is sufficient, why cite the second?

The answer is that the first *pasuk* — *amad vayimoded eretz* — is needed for two other entirely different teachings, attributed to Rav Matna and Rav Yosef respectively, and thus cannot also serve as the source for the damage law. Therefore a second *pasuk* is required.

### Rav Matna & Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דְּרַב מַתְנָה? דְּאָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: ״עָמַד וַיְמֹדֶד אֶרֶץ, רָאָה וְכוּ׳״. מָה רָאָה – רָאָה שֶׁבַע מִצְוֹת שֶׁנִּצְטַוּוּ עֲלֵיהֶן בְּנֵי נֹחַ וְלֹא קִיְּימוּם, עָמַד וְהִגְלָה אוֹתָם מֵעַל אַדְמָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **What is Rav Mattana's** exposition? It is **as Rav Mattana says: "He stood and shook the earth." What did He see? He saw the seven mitzvot that the descendants of Noah were commanded but did not fulfill,** and **He arose and exiled them from their land** on account of their transgressions.

What does Rav Matna read from *amad vayimoded eretz ra'ah vayater goyim*? He reads it as a statement of divine punishment through exile. God saw the *sheva mitzvot* they had been commanded (*sheva mitzvot shenitzvu aleihen Bnei Noach*), saw that they did not fulfill them (*v'lo kiyemum*), and therefore exiled them from their land (*amad v'higlah osam me-al admasam*). On this reading, *vayater goyim* means not "made their money permissible" but "scattered them," "sent them into exile" — the same way Israel is exiled for its sins, the nations are exiled for theirs. The verbal support is from a *gezerah shavah*: the root *n-t-r* appears elsewhere in connection with jumping or leaping across the land (as with the locust, *l'nater bahen al ha-aretz*, rendered by the Targum as "to leap upon the land"), implying movement from place to place.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״וַיַּתֵּר״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאִגַּלּוֹיֵי הוּא? כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיַּתֵּר גּוֹיִם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״לְנַתֵּר בָּהֵן עַל הָאָרֶץ״ – וּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן: לְקַפָּצָא בְּהוֹן עַל אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where may** it **be inferred that this** term ***vayyatter* is a term of exile? It is written here: "And made the nations tremble [*vayyatter*]"** (Habakkuk 3:6), **and it is written there: "*Lenatter* upon the earth"** (Leviticus 11:21), **which is translated** into Aramaic as: **"To leap upon the earth."** Apparently, the root *nun*, *tav*, *reish*, common to both words, indicates uprooting from one place to another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דְּרַב יוֹסֵף? דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: ״עָמַד וַיְמֹדֶד אֶרֶץ, רָאָה וְכוּ׳״; מָה רָאָה? רָאָה שֶׁבַע מִצְוֹת שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם בְּנֵי נֹחַ וְלֹא קִיְּימוּם, עָמַד וְהִתִּירָן לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **What is Rav Yosef's** exposition? It is **as Rav Yosef says: "He stood and shook the earth; He beheld." What did He see? He saw the seven mitzvot that the descendants of Noah accepted upon themselves and did not fulfill,** so **He arose and permitted** their prohibitions **to them.**

Rav Yosef offers a still more surprising reading of the same *pasuk*. God saw the *sheva mitzvot* the nations had accepted (*sheva mitzvot shekiblu aleihem Bnei Noach*), saw that they were not fulfilling them, and therefore *amad v'hitiram lahem* — released them from their obligation entirely. He revoked their commandment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּגוֹרֵי אִתְּגוּר?! אִם כֵּן, מָצִינוּ חוֹטֵא נִשְׂכָּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Did they** thereby **profit,** in that their prohibitions became permitted to them? **If so, we have found a transgressor** who **is rewarded.**

The *Gemara* immediately objects: *ishtari ishtra* — are you serious? *Im kein matzinu choteh niskar* — if that is so, the sinner is rewarded! The more flagrantly you violate, the more you are released from your obligations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבְנָא: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ מְקַיְּימִין אוֹתָן – אֵין מְקַבְּלִין עֲלֵיהֶן שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Rabbana, says:** This is not to say that for them to transgress their mitzvot is no longer a sin; rather, it is **to say that even if they fulfill them, they do not receive reward for** fulfilling **them.**

Mar, son of Rav Avina, resolves the paradox: the release does not mean freedom from punishment when they violate. It means that even when they do fulfill the *mitzvot*, they no longer receive reward. Having demonstrated such categorical disregard for their commandments, they have severed the relationship of *metzuveh v'oseh* — the status of being commanded and acting accordingly — and now, when they happen to do the right thing, they do so without the orientation of commandedness. They therefore receive only the lesser reward of *eino metzuveh v'oseh*. But violation is still punished.

### Reward for Non-Jews

The resolution through the category of *aino metzuveh v'oseh* raises a deeper question: do non-Jews receive any reward at all for doing *mitzvot*? And Rabbi Meir's statement provides an answer.

### Rabbi Meir on Goy & Torah

*Minayin she'afilu nochri v'osek baTorah harei hu k'kohen gadol* — how do we know that even a non-Jew who engages in Torah is like a *kohen gadol*? From the verse *asher ya'aseh otam ha'adam v'chai bahem* (Vayikra 18:5): it does not say *kohanim*, *leviyim*, or *Yisraelim* shall do them and live — it says *ha'adam*, any human being. The universalist implication is explicit: any person (*adam*), regardless of people or station, who performs the commandments of the *Torah*, attains life through them — and the measure of reward is that of a *kohen gadol*.

It is important to note that *v'osek baTorah* here, in its original Tannaitic context, means performing the *mitzvot* of the *Torah*, not studying *Torah*. Although Tosafot later applies this to *Talmud Torah*, connecting it to a different *drasha* in *Sotah* about Torah being more precious than pearls (*yekarah hi mipninim m'kohen gadol hanichnas lifnai v'lifnim*), that reading reflects Tosafot's own framework and is not the *peshat* of this *Gemara*.

What is remarkable here is that Rabbi Meir's statement appears to go even further than the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*: it seems to say that a non-Jew who performs *mitzvot* from the full *Torah* of Israel receives reward commensurate with a *kohen gadol*. The Rambam, elsewhere, states that a non-Jew who performs any *mitzvah* from the *Torah* receives reward like an *eino metzuveh v'oseh*, and he applies this principle even to *bris milah*.

### Aino Metzuveh Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אֵין מְקַבְּלִים עֲלֵיהֶן שָׂכָר כִּמְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה, אֶלָּא כְּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: גָּדוֹל הַמְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה יוֹתֵר מִמִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** in response: Rav Yosef meant that **they do not receive the reward as** does **one who is commanded** to perform a mitzva **and performs** it, **but as** does **one who is not commanded** to perform a mitzva **and performs** it anyway. **As Rabbi Ḥanina says:** One who is **commanded and performs** a mitzva **is greater than** one who **is not commanded and performs** it.

The *Gemara* reconciles the apparent contradiction with Rav Yosef's reading — which seemed to imply no reward — as follows: *ein mekablim aleihem sechar mitzvah k'metzuveh v'oseh ela k'mi she'eino metzuveh v'oseh* — they do receive reward, but not at the level of one who is commanded and fulfills. They receive reward only at the reduced level of *eino metzuveh v'oseh*. And *Rabbi Chanina* taught: *gadol metzuveh v'oseh yoter m'mi she'eino metzuveh v'oseh* — it is greater to act under the compulsion of commandment than out of pure volition.

This resolution applies on two levels. With respect to the *taryag mitzvot* of Israel, non-Jews are genuinely *eino metzuveh v'oseh* — they were never commanded in those *mitzvot* — so their reward is naturally at the lower level. But with respect to the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*, the additional element introduced by Rav Yosef's reading is that even these commandments, which they *were* obligated in, they have severed themselves from through persistent violation. So even when they do fulfill them, they no longer experience them as the acts of a *metzuveh*, and their reward reflects that diminished orientation.

What is striking about this entire unit is the way the *Bavli* has structured it. Immediately after establishing the harsh asymmetric rule — non-Jews pay full damages and receive nothing — the *Gemara* introduces Rabbi Meir's universalist teaching: *asher ya'aseh otam ha'adam*, any human being who performs Torah is rewarded like a *kohen gadol*. The principle that humanity as such (*adam*) is the relevant category, and that no specification of *kohanim*, *leviyim*, or *Yisraelim* was written, directly counterweights the particularism of the preceding rule. The *Gemara* does not abandon the *halakha*, but it complicates the picture immediately and forcefully.

A related universalist passage appears in the *Sifra*, cited by Tosafot in the first wide line of the *tosafot* on this *daf*. There an extended passage notes multiple *pesukim* that use non-particularistic language — *pitchu shearim veyavo goy tzaddik* ("open the gates, let the righteous nation enter," Yeshayahu 26:2), read as referring to any righteous nation; *hetiva Hashem latovim*, God does good to the good, where it does not say *kohanim, leviyim, v'Yisraelim* but *latovim*, to the good — all converging on the same principle: *afilu nochri v'osek baTorah harei hu k'kohen gadol*. The *Sifra* extends this across numerous verses, building a sustained universalist counterweight.

Tosafot discusses this passage because other *gemarot* create the opposite hermeneutical move — reading the word *adam* specifically as *excluding* non-Jews. This is, as the *Gemara* here implicitly acknowledges, the inverse of the thrust of this passage. Tosafot works through the tension; it may reflect a genuine dispute among *Tana'im* over how to read those terms. But the juxtaposition within our *daf* itself is deliberately constructed: the *Bavli* places this universalist counterweight immediately adjacent to the most exclusionary-seeming *halakha*, not merely to qualify it but to fundamentally complicate the picture of how one ought to view the non-Jew.

## Roman Officers Story

### Roman Officers & Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, וּכְבָר שָׁלְחָה מַלְכוּת רוֹמִי שְׁנֵי סְרַדְיוֹטוֹת אֵצֶל חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל: לַמְּדוּנוּ תּוֹרַתְכֶם. קָרְאוּ וְשָׁנוּ וְשִׁלֵּשׁוּ. בִּשְׁעַת פְּטִירָתָן אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: דִּקְדַּקְנוּ בְּכׇל תּוֹרַתְכֶם – וֶאֱמֶת הוּא; חוּץ מִדָּבָר זֶה שֶׁאַתֶּם אוֹמְרִים: שׁוֹר שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל גּוֹי – פָּטוּר. שֶׁל גּוֹי שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל – בֵּין תָּם בֵּין מוּעָד, מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following story in the context of the aforementioned *halakha*: **And the Roman kingdom once sent two military officials [*sardeyotot*] to the Sages of Israel,** and ordered them in the name of the king: **Teach us your Torah.** The officials **read** the Torah, **and repeated** it, **and** repeated it again, reading it for the **third** time. **At the time of their departure, they said to** the Sages: **We have examined your entire Torah and it is true, except for this** one **matter that you state,** i.e., that with regard to **an ox of a Jew that gored the ox of a gentile,** the owner is **exempt** from liability, whereas with regard to the ox **of a gentile that gored the ox of a Jew, whether** it was **innocuous or forewarned,** the owner **pays the full** cost of the **damage.**

The *Gemara* records that the Roman government — the original *girsa* was *malkhut harasha*, "the evil government," subsequently altered — had already sent two *sardiytot* to the sages of Israel. Saul Lieberman notes evidence that Rome had a practice of sending legal scholars to study the laws of subject nations, and he understands *sardiytot* in that sense, though the term is more commonly understood to mean officers. The officers studied *Torah* with the sages of Israel thoroughly: *kara veshanu veshilshu* — they read, reviewed, and mastered the material three times over.

### Officers' Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ, אִי ״רֵעֵהוּ״ דַּוְקָא – אֲפִילּוּ דְּגוֹי כִּי נָגַח דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לִיפְּטַר; וְאִי ״רֵעֵהוּ״ לָאו דַּוְקָא – אֲפִילּוּ דְיִשְׂרָאֵל כִּי נָגַח דְּגוֹי לִחַיַּיב! וְדָבָר זֶה אֵין אָנוּ מוֹדִיעִים אוֹתוֹ לַמַּלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The officials' reasoning was that this *halakha* is difficult **whichever way you** look at it. **If** the phrase **"of another"** is meant in **a precise** manner, that the owners of both oxen must both be Jewish, then **even when** the ox **of a gentile gores the ox of a Jew** the owner of the ox **should be exempt** from liability. **And if** the phrase **"of another"** is **not** meant in **a precise** manner, and the oxen of all are included, then **even when** the ox **of a Jew gores the ox of a gentile** the owner **should be liable.** They added: **But we will not inform this matter to the kingdom;** having acknowledged that the entire Torah is true, we will not reveal this ruling, as it will displease the kingdom.

When they came to depart, they told the sages: *dikdaknu bechol Torat-chem ve'emet hu* — we have gone through your entire *Torah* with great precision, and it is true — *chutz midavar zeh*, with one exception. That exception is precisely the rule at issue: the Jew's ox that gores the non-Jew's ox is *patur*, while the non-Jew's ox that gores the Jew's pays full damages whether *tam* or *mu'ad*. Their objection is framed in terms of the *mimah nafshach* argument: if *re'ehu* is *davka*, exemption should run in both directions; if *re'ehu* is *lav davka*, liability should run in both directions. The rule as stated is internally inconsistent.

Having stated their critique, the officers add: *vedavar zeh einu magidim oso lamalchut* — this thing we will not report to the government. They chose not to inform Rome of this discriminatory rule.

### Bavli vs. Yerushalmi

Several observations about this story deserve attention. First, the *mimah nafshach* argument — the logical inconsistency framing — is a later Aramaic gloss, borrowed from the earlier discussion in this very *sugya*. The original text of the story almost certainly said only: here is the one thing we cannot accept, the asymmetric damage rule. And the reason they could not accept it was not logical inconsistency but moral offense — it is discriminatory. The *Bavli* by inserting the *mimah nafshach* argument transforms their objection from a moral one into a merely logical one, thereby understating the depth of their critique. The rule is framed as *all your Torah is true* (*ve'emet hu*) — a logical problem, not an ethical scandal.

The Yerushalmi's version of the story is instructive by contrast. There, the officers describe the *Torah* as *na'eh umeshubachat* — praiseworthy and beautiful — and say this one thing is *not* praiseworthy. That is a moral objection, not merely a logical puzzle. Furthermore, in the Yerushalmi's version, the officers had disguised themselves as Jews in order to study *Torah*. The *Bavli*'s version has them come openly as Romans — consistent with the immediately preceding statement of Rabbi Meir that the *Torah* is open to all; they can come from Rome and we will teach them *Torah*. The Yerushalmi is more protective of *Torah* as specifically Israel's, while the *Bavli* has deliberately contextualized the story within a universalist frame. The Yerushalmi also adds that on their return journey, God made them forget everything they had learned, so they could report nothing to Rome. The *Bavli* contains no such epilogue — the officers are left in possession of their knowledge and choose, on their own, not to report.

The *Maharsha* and the *Yam Shel Shlomo* raise the question: why did the sages not simply misrepresent the *halakha* to the officers, or simply decline to teach this rule? Given that disclosure might lead to Roman reprisal, was there not a *pikuach nefesh* justification for concealment? The *Maharsha*'s answer: from this story we learn that it is forbidden to misrepresent *Torah* even where one might argue that doing so could prevent danger to life. One may decline to teach; one may not falsify.

## Divine Compassion Stories

### Ulla's Theology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה שְׁכִיבָא לֵיהּ בְּרַתָּא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְעוּלָּא: קוּם נֵיזִל נִינַחֲמֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי אִית לִי גַּבֵּי נֶחָמְתָּא דְבַבְלָאֵי – דְּגִידּוּפָא הוּא? דְּאָמְרִי: ״מַאי אֶפְשָׁר לְמִיעְבַּד״; הָא אֶפְשָׁר לְמִיעְבַּד – עָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** § Incidentally, it is related that **the daughter** of **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda died. The Sages said to Ulla: Arise; let us go console him.** Ulla **said to them: What** business **do I have with the consolation of Babylonians, which is** actually **heresy? As, they say** while consoling mourners: **What can be done?** This seems to suggest that **if it were possible to do** something, acting against the Almighty's decree, **they would do** so, which is tantamount to heresy. Therefore, Ulla declined to accompany the Babylonian Sages.

The *Gemara* introduces Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda, whose daughter had died. The rabbis said to Ulla: let us go and comfort him, let us pay a *shiva* call. Ulla declined to join them, saying: *mai li gabei nichuma d'Bavla'ei* — what do I want with the Babylonian style of *nichum aveilim*? *D'gidufa hu* — it is blasphemy. His reasoning: when Babylonians offer condolences, they say *mai efshar l'me'evad* — what could have been done? And the implication of that phrase is that if something *could* have been done, one would have tried to do it — thereby suggesting a readiness to subvert the divine decree. Ulla went alone. His approach to *nichum aveilim* reflects a theology that is, by any measure, harsh: complete submission to divine judgment, with no comfort offered beyond that submission.

### Kal V'Chomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל הוּא לְחוֹדֵיהּ גַּבֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ (אֶל מֹשֶׁה) אַל תָּצַר אֶת מוֹאָב וְאַל תִּתְגָּר בָּם מִלְחָמָה״; וְכִי מָה עָלָה עַל דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה, לַעֲשׂוֹת מִלְחָמָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת?! אֶלָּא נָשָׂא מֹשֶׁה קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמוֹ – אָמַר: וּמָה מִדְיָנִים שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ אֶלָּא לַעֲזוֹר אֶת מוֹאָב, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״צָרוֹר אֶת הַמִּדְיָנִים וְהִכִּיתֶם אוֹתָם״, מוֹאָבִים עַצְמָן לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** Ulla therefore **went to** console Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda **by himself,** and **said to him:** The verse states: **"And the Lord said to me, do not be at enmity with Moab, neither contend with them in battle"** (Deuteronomy 2:9). **What entered Moses's mind,** that God had to warn him not to undertake a particular action? Did it enter his mind **to wage war** with the Moabites **without permission? Rather, Moses reasoned an *a fortiori*** inference **by himself, saying: And if** with regard to **the Midianites, who came only to help the Moabites** harm the Jewish people (see Numbers, chapter 22), **the Torah said: "Harass the Midianites and smite them"** (Numbers 25:17), with regard to the **Moabites themselves,** is it **not** clear **all the more so** that they should be attacked?

Ulla went to Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda and offered the following comfort. He cited the verse from Bamidbar (21:16): *al tatzar et Moav v'al titgar bam milchama* — do not distress Moav and do not wage war against them. Ulla frames this through a *kal vachomer* that Moshe himself had reasoned: if regarding Midyan — who came only to assist Moav in hiring Bilam and were the secondary party to the hostile design — the *Torah* commands *tzor et haMidyanim v'hikitem osam*, to fight and strike them — then regarding Moav itself, which instigated the entire plot, *lo kal vachomer* that we should wage war? Yet God told Moshe: *lo k'sh'alta datcha al datchi* — My thinking is not like yours. God had a reason to preserve Moav: *shtei preidot tovot yesh li l'hotzi mehem* — I have two beautiful doves to bring forth from them — Ruth the Moabite and Naamah the Ammonite.

### Ruth & Na'amah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: לֹא כְּשֶׁעָלְתָה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ עָלְתָה עַל דַּעְתִּי, שְׁתֵּי פְּרִידוֹת טוֹבוֹת יֵשׁ לִי לְהוֹצִיא מֵהֶן – רוּת הַמּוֹאֲבִיָּה וְנַעֲמָה הָעַמּוֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** To counter this, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: That which has entered your mind has not entered Mine,** because **I have two virtuous fledglings [*feridot*],** i.e., girls, **to extract from them: Ruth the Moabite,** who will be the foremother of the dynasty of David, **and Naamah the Ammonite,** Solomon's wife, from whom the continuation of that dynasty will emerge. For the sake of these women, the Moabites and Ammonites must not be destroyed.

The logic Ulla applies to the bereaved father is this: if, on account of two righteous descendants, God had compassion over two entire wicked nations and did not destroy them — *v'lo hechrivam* — then your daughter, *im keshera hi v'rauya hi l'atzeit mimena davar tov*, if she was righteous and worthy for some good to descend from her, *al achat kama v'khama d'hava chaya* — how much more so would she have lived. Therefore, since she died, one may conclude that nothing good would have come from her progeny in any case. Accept God's decree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה בִּשְׁבִיל שְׁתֵּי פְּרִידוֹת טוֹבוֹת – חָס הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל שְׁתֵּי אוּמּוֹת גְּדוֹלוֹת וְלֹא הֶחֱרִיבָן; בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי – אִם כְּשֵׁרָה הִיא וּרְאוּיָה הִיא לָצֵאת מִמֶּנָּה דָּבָר טוֹב, עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה דַּהֲוָה חַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla continued: **And are** these **matters not** inferred ***a fortiori*? If for the sake of two virtuous fledglings the Holy One, Blessed be He, had pity on two large nations and did not destroy them,** then **if the daughter of my teacher,** Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda, **was righteous, and she had** the **potential for something good to emerge from her,** it is **all the more so** clear **that she would have lived.**

This is, to put it plainly, not pastoral counseling. The logic is as brutal as it is tidy. What is theologically significant, however, is what the story establishes about divine providence: even a completely wicked nation — one that actively tried to destroy Israel — is preserved by God on account of the potential for righteous descendants. The calculus of divine compassion is entirely indifferent to present wickedness when future goodness is possible.

### God's Reward System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְקַפֵּחַ שְׂכַר כׇּל בְּרִיָּה, אֲפִילּוּ שְׂכַר שִׂיחָה נָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned the Moabites and Ammonites, the Gemara cites that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, does not deprive any creature of** its **reward.** He rewards every person for his good deeds, and provides **reward even for** using **pleasant speech** by using euphemisms.

The *Gemara* continues with Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ein Hakadosh Baruch Hu mekappea sechar kol berya* — God does not withhold the reward of any creature, however small the good deed. *Afilu sechar sicha na'ah* — even for a pleasant manner of speech, reward is given.

### Daughters' Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִילּוּ בְּכִירָה דְּקָאָמְרָה ״מוֹאָב״, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה ״אַל תָּצַר אֶת מוֹאָב וְאַל תִּתְגָּר בָם מִלְחָמָה״ – מִלְחָמָה הוּא דְּלָא, הָא אַנְגַּרְיָא עֲבֵיד בְּהוּ;
>
> **Translation:** **As** with regard to the descendants of the **elder** of the two daughters of Lot, **who said** that the name of her son, whom she conceived with her father, would be **Moab,** meaning: From father, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: "Do not be at enmity with Moab, neither contend with them in battle,"** indicating that **specifically** a full-fledged **battle was not** authorized **but** that the Jewish people could **impose forced labor [*angarya*] on them.**

The illustration comes from the daughters of Lot. The older daughter, who slept with her father and named her son Moav — *me-av*, from the father — was explicit and perhaps immodest in her language. God's instruction to Moshe was *al tatzar et Moav v'al titgar bam milchama* — do not afflict Moav and do not wage war, with the added nuance that even *angarya*, forced labor or tribute, is permitted against Moav. The younger daughter, who named her son Ben Ammi — "son of my nation" — was less explicit and more *tzanua*, more modest in her choice of words. For this modest speech — a single small *mitzvah* — she received a greater reward from God: *al tatzar* and *al titgar* with no qualification, meaning not even *angarya* may be imposed upon the Ammonites.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְעִירָה דְּקָאָמְרָה ״בֶּן עַמִּי״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: ״וְקָרַבְתָּ מוּל בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן, אַל תְּצֻרֵם וְאַל תִּתְגָּר בָּם״ – כְּלָל, דַּאֲפִילּוּ אַנְגַּרְיָא לָא תַּעֲבֵיד בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, with regard to the descendants of the **younger** daughter, **who said** her son's name would be **ben Ami,** meaning: Son of my nation, merely alluding to the fact she conceived him through an incestuous union, **the Holy One, Blessed be He said to Moses: "And when you come near against the children of Ammon, do not harass them, nor contend with them"** (Deuteronomy 2:19). In other words, do not contend with them **at all; do not even impose forced labor on them.** This additional prohibition was a reward for her employing a euphemism when naming her son.

The *Gemara*'s handling of this narrative is significant. In many *midrashim*, the story of Lot's daughters is deployed as evidence of the licentiousness and depravity of the nations that descended from them. Here it is reframed entirely: the daughters are credited with performing a *mitzvah* — indeed a great one — and their *tzniut* is highlighted as meritorious. The very story that elsewhere serves as a negative exemplar is turned into a story of righteousness, divine reward, and the value of modesty.

### Genealogical Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: לְעוֹלָם יַקְדִּים אָדָם לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, שֶׁבִּשְׁבִיל לַיְלָה אַחַת שֶׁקְּדָמַתָּה בְּכִירָה לִצְעִירָה – קְדָמַתָּה אַרְבַּע דּוֹרוֹת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: עוֹבֵד, יִשַׁי, וְדָוִד, וּשְׁלֹמֹה; וְאִילּוּ צְעִירָה – עַד רְחַבְעָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשֵׁם אִמּוֹ נַעֲמָה הָעַמֹּנִית״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the daughters of Lot, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: A person should always hasten to perform a mitzva, as due to** the **one night by which the elder** daughter of Lot **preceded the younger** daughter, with the intention of performing a mitzva by bringing children into the world, **she preceded her by four generations** in having her descendants enter **into the Jewish people.** They are: **Obed,** son of Ruth the Moabite, **Yishai, David, and Solomon. Whereas,** the descendants of **the younger** daughter did not join the Jewish people **until Rehoboam,** Solomon's son, was born, **as it is written: "And his mother's name was Naamah the Ammonite"** (I Kings 14:31).

The final word of the *sugya* is Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: *le'olam yakdim adam l'dvar mitzvah* — a person should always hasten to perform a *mitzvah*. The illustration again comes from the daughters of Lot. The older daughter was the first to act — she preceded her sister by one night in the incestuous act undertaken (in their understanding) to preserve humanity. As a result of that one night's priority in performing the *mitzvah*, her progeny entered the genealogy of Israel four generations earlier. The line from Moav runs: Ruth married Boaz, and their line continues through Oved, Yishai, David, and Shlomo — four generations of the royal lineage. Naamah the Ammonite married Shlomo, and so the younger daughter's lineage entered only at that later point, through Rechavam her son, four generations after Ruth. *Kadmasa arba dorot b'Yisrael* — the older daughter preceded in the Jewish royal line by four generations — all because she was one night quicker to the *mitzvah*.

The *sugya* closes, then, in a remarkable place. Beginning with one of the most legally and morally challenging rules in the entire tractate — a rule that is discriminatory, asymmetric, and defended through *pesukim* that require considerable hermeneutical work — the *Bavli* has built a sustained counterweight. Rabbi Meir's universalism (*asher ya'aseh otam ha'adam*, any human who performs Torah is like a *kohen gadol*), the *Sifra*'s extended universalist passages, the story of the Roman officers who acknowledge *kol Torat-chem emet*, the divine preservation of wicked nations for the sake of two righteous descendants, and finally the reframing of the mothers of Moav and Ammon as exemplars of *mitzvah* and *tzniut* — all of this sits deliberately adjacent to, and in tension with, the asymmetric *halakha* that opens the discussion. The *Bavli* does not retract the *halakha*. But it will not allow it to stand without complication.
