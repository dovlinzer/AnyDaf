# Bekhorot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bechor Shor Chesev E (II)

### Intro: Mother-Child Match

The sugya opens at the very bottom of 5b, where the Gemara is working through the principle that firstborn animals must match their mother's species in appearance. As part of that discussion, one derivation proceeds from the triple formulation *bechor shor bechor chesev bechor eiz* — the firstborn of ox, sheep, and goat — while a competing *tanna* holds that this triple formulation does not teach the mother-child matching rule at all, but rather that all three species' fats are placed on the *mizbeach*.

### Klal U'Prat Method

The Gemara explains why the Torah had to mention the *korban bechor* context by all three animals separately. One might ask: could we not have derived the fat-offering rule for one species from the other two? The Gemara works through each possibility systematically. We cannot omit the mention by ox and derive it from sheep and goat, because those two share a special status — they are both brought as a *korban pesach* — and that distinguishing feature might explain the fat-on-the-*mizbeach* rule, rendering it inapplicable to ox. We cannot omit the mention by sheep and derive it from ox and goat, because ox and goat are both brought as special *korbanot* for *avodah zarah* by the community, a status sheep does not share. And we cannot omit the mention by goat and derive it from sheep and ox, because both sheep and ox possess their own forms of additional *mizbeach* contribution — the sheep has the fat tail, the ox receives a larger measure of wine — which goat lacks. *Hilkach tzrichi*: all three had to be stated explicitly.

### R. Yose: Double Bechor

This brings us to Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position. If all three species must be stated in order to teach the fat-offering rule, why does the Torah write the word *bechor* separately before each animal? Why not simply write *ach bechor shor chesev va-eiz* without repeating *bechor* three times? The answer, Rabbi Yose HaGlili maintains, is that the repetition of *bechor* — *bechor shor, bechor chesev, bechor eiz* — is itself the teaching: *k'shehu shor bechoro shor*, when the firstborn is an ox its mother must also be an ox, and so on. The double *bechor* thus conveys the mother-child appearance-matching rule for kosher animals. Rabbi Yose HaGlili then applies the reverse logic to the *peter chamor*: the Rabbis derive the mother-child rule from the repeated *peter chamor* and apply it back to kosher animals; Rabbi Yose HaGlili begins with kosher animals and applies it outward to the donkey.

## Donkey Firstborn Lim (II)

### Broad to Narrow

This raises a question about the plain sense of the verses. One *pasuk* speaks broadly — redeem the firstborn of non-kosher animals generally — while another specifies the *peter chamor* redeemed with a sheep. How do these coexist? One could read the broad verse as applying universally while the sheep-redemption clause is limited to donkeys. But Rabbi Yose HaGlili asks: perhaps we should take the general verse at face value, and the firstborn of horses and camels would also require redemption?

The *baraita* records his reasoning: we hear from the verse *bechor behemah hatmeiah tifdeh* that one might include even *pitrei susim v'gamalim* — firstborn horses and camels. The Torah therefore says *u'peter chamor tifdeh vaseh*: the firstborn donkey you redeem with a sheep — *peter chamor amarti lecha v'lo pitrei susim u'gamalim*, only the firstborn donkey, not those of horses or camels. Still, one might think the restriction is only regarding the sheep: horses and camels cannot be redeemed *with a sheep*, but the underlying *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn non-kosher animals still applies to them, albeit with money or other value. At the top of 6a, the Torah's repetition of *peter chamor* a second time forecloses this: *pitrei chamorim amarti lecha v'lo pitrei susim u'gamalim* — not merely the sheep mechanism, but the entire *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn non-kosher animals is restricted to donkeys alone. Why the broad opening verse states the *mitzvah* in such generic terms without the restriction remains, as the lecturer notes, an open question.

## Bechor Shor Chesev E (II)

### Rav Achai's Challenge

At this point Rav Achai raises a challenge. As noted previously, *Tosafot* understands this Rav Achai to be Rav Achai Gaon of the *She'iltot*, whose questions are consistently more tangential in character and were likely inserted into the text as a later gloss. His objection here is that deriving the exclusion of horses and camels from the double mention of *peter chamor* does not work, because there is a competing way to read the first occurrence. There is a principle that something which belonged to a general category and was then singled out — *davar shehayah bichlal veyatza min haklal lilamed* — is not to be read as a personal exception but rather as teaching about the entire category. Accordingly, the first time the Torah specifies *peter chamor* one would read it not as a limitation but as an example: all non-kosher animals are redeemed, and it says *peter chamor* as an instance, extending the sheep-redemption mechanism to everything. The second occurrence would then limit the sheep mechanism specifically to donkeys — *mi'atinu mi'seh* — but that says nothing about excluding other non-kosher animals from the underlying *mitzvah*. So the double mention cannot achieve what Rabbi Yose HaGlili claimed.

### Gemara Response

The Gemara responds: if that were the intent, the Torah need not have written the word *peter* twice. The context of firstborn animals is already established; the Torah could have written simply *chamor tifdeh vaseh* and again *chamor tifdeh vaseh*. The fact that it writes *peter chamor* twice — reiterating the firstborn framing — shows that the doubling is purposeful: *pitrei chamorim amarti lecha v'lo pitrei susim u'gamalim*, to teach that the entire *mitzvah* applies only to donkeys. Whether or not Rav Achai's challenge holds, the basic *pshat* of the dual *peter chamor* is that it narrows the seemingly broad opening verse to apply exclusively to donkeys.

## Donkey Firstborn Lim (II)

### Peter Chamor Twice

With that established, the Gemara turns to the *tanna didan*, the author of our *Mishnah*, who uses the double *peter chamor* to derive the mother-child appearance rule. If he uses that doubling for a different purpose, where does he learn that horses and camels are excluded from the *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn non-kosher animals?

### Klal U'Prat: All Animals

Rav Pappa answers from a different verse: *kol miknecha tizkar peter shor vaseh u'peter chamor tifdeh vaseh* — all your animals, then ox, flock, and donkey. This is a case of *klal u'frat*: "all your animals" is the general (*klal*), and ox, flock, and donkey are the specific (*prat*). The rule of *klal u'frat ein baklal ela mah shebaprat* means the general is confined to what appears in the particular. Only ox, flock, and donkey — *midei achrinei lo*, nothing else.

### R. Yose: Verse Break

Rabbi Yose HaGlili objects that this *klal u'frat* reading does not hold. The verse *peter shor vaseh* and the following *u'peter chamor tifdeh vaseh* are in fact separate statements. The word *peter* that begins the second clause — *peter hifsik hainyan* — marks a new sentence and breaks the connection to the preceding general statement. You cannot construct a *klal u'frat* across a verse break.

### Rabbis: Different Kedusha

The Rabbis respond on two levels. First, the *vav* at the opening of the second clause — *u'peter chamor* — connects it back to the preceding statement and restores the *klal u'frat* structure; *vav hadar arvei kra*, the *vav* reconnects them. Rabbi Yose HaGlili counters that if the Torah wanted them as one sentence it would not have written *peter* at all — the *vav* alone would have sufficed; writing *peter* signals a deliberate break.

The Rabbis respond with a second argument: *aidei deha kedushat haguf v'ha kedushat damim* — the two halves of the verse describe fundamentally different categories of sanctity. The first half, *peter shor vaseh*, concerns animals brought as *korbanot*, carrying *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity. The second half, *u'peter chamor tifdeh*, concerns an animal redeemed through monetary value given to a *kohen*, *kedushat damim* alone. The two halves also carry different verbs: the first uses *tizkar*, the second *tifdeh*. Because these are such different types of *bechor* obligation, the Torah needed to mark them as separate clauses — *pasik lehu*, it had to break them. But because it still wished to maintain the overarching *klal u'frat* principle limiting the *mitzvah* to these species alone, *hadar arvei lehu*, it then reconnected them with the *vav*. The student question arises as to why human firstborns, who also appear in this context, are grouped with *peter chamor* rather than with the first clause. The answer is that humans, like donkeys, are redeemed — they are not brought as *korbanot* — and so they share the *kedushat damim* character of the second half of the verse. In sum: the Rabbis derive the exclusion of horses and camels from the *klal u'frat*, while Rabbi Yose HaGlili derives it from the double *peter chamor*, and both arrive at the same conclusion.

## Partial Simanim Rules

### Cow-Donkey Hybrid

The Gemara now raises a new set of questions. It has already been established that if a sheep gives birth to an animal that looks like a goat, or vice versa, there is no *kedushat bechor*. It was also established that if the offspring has *some* characteristics of the right species — some sheep-like traits in a mostly goat-looking animal born of a sheep — it remains obligated in *bechor*, albeit as a *baal mum*. The Gemara now asks: does this principle of partial *simanim* transfer to cross-category cases? The opening case: a cow (*parah*) gives birth to something that looks like a donkey but retains some cow-like characteristics. Is the partial resemblance to the mother sufficient to impose *kedushat bechor*?

### Sheep-Goat Parallel

The ease of the sheep-goat ruling is attributable to several compounding factors: both animals appear in the same *parsha*, both are kosher (*de'ha tahor ve'ha tahor*), and both carry *kedushat haguf* (*ha kedushat haguf ve'ha kedushat haguf*). When all relevant dimensions align, partial *simanim* suffices. But the present case is more complex.

### Different Categories

The cow-donkey case differs on multiple axes: one is kosher, one is non-kosher (*de'ha tahor ve'ha tamei*), and one carries *kedushat haguf* while the other carries only *kedushat damim* (*ha kedushat haguf v'ha kedushat damim*). Despite these differences, one might still reason that since both fall under the general category of *bechor* — one as a *bechor* of a kosher animal and one as a *peter chamor* — their shared *bechor* status is sufficient basis to say that partial *simanim* still obligates. The Gemara then extends the question: if we grant that the cow-donkey case is obligated with partial *simanim*, what about a donkey that gives birth to something resembling a horse, where not only are the categories different but the horse is entirely outside the *bechor* framework? And further: what if a cow gives birth to something resembling a horse, where the offspring's non-mother half is both of a different kosher status *and* entirely outside the *bechor* category? Is the principle *simanim milta hi* — that partial characteristics are inherently sufficient — broad enough to cover even those cases?

### Baraita: Simanim Enough

The Gemara cites a *baraita* in an attempt to resolve the question: a kosher animal (*behemah tehorah*) that gives birth to something resembling a non-kosher animal (*kemin behemah teme'ah*) is exempt from *bechorah*, but if it has some of the right characteristics (*im yesh bah miktzat simanim*), it is obligated. The Gemara asks whether this *baraita* resolves even the most extreme case — a cow giving birth to something that looks like a horse — but concludes it does not. The word *teme'ah* in the *baraita* may refer specifically to a donkey, not any non-kosher animal, so only the first and least extreme case — the cow-donkey with partial cow characteristics — is resolved.

A second *baraita* is then cited that adds the case of *chamor sheyalda kemin sus* — a donkey giving birth to something resembling a horse. Again, partial *simanim* obligates, and again the Gemara attempts to read this as resolving the intermediate question. The Gemara declines: perhaps the *baraita*'s second case is mentioned only to tell us it is *patur* without partial *simanim*, in which case why even mention it, since a donkey-horse would obviously be exempt once we know a cow-donkey is exempt? *Itzterich* — it was necessary. One might have thought that a donkey and horse, being more physically similar than a cow and donkey — neither has horns, both have single hooves (*parsotav klutot*) — are perhaps not really different species at all, and the horse-looking animal is merely what one might call a reddish donkey (*chamor adom be'alma hu*). The *baraita* therefore tells us it is still *patur*. The upside of this reading, however, is that the *baraita* mentions partial *simanim* obligating even in the donkey-horse case, which would resolve the intermediate question. But the Gemara once more deflects: the partial *simanim* clause in the *baraita* refers only to the cow-donkey case. The donkey-horse question, and the more extreme cow-horse question, remain unresolved.

### Donkey-Horse Example

The reason the *baraita* bothers to state the donkey-horse case at all is to forestall the inference that because donkey and horse are physically so similar, one might mistakenly conclude the horse-resembling offspring is simply a variety of donkey and is therefore obligated. The *baraita* tells us it is not — it is a distinct enough type that without partial *simanim* of the mother, it is exempt.

### Unresolved Questions

Two questions remain open: whether partial *simanim* suffice in the donkey-horse case, and whether partial *simanim* suffice in the cow-horse case. The underlying question cutting across all of these is what the Gemara actually envisions biologically — whether it contemplates a genuine interspecies offspring, or simply an animal that looks like another species. This is never fully resolved. On a related note, the Gemara has not yet addressed the role of the father's species in any of these determinations; that belongs to a later *Mishnah*.

## Mother vs. Appearance

### Yotzei Min HaTamei

The *Mishnah* now states the foundational principle: *yotzei min hatamei tamei, yotzei min hatahor tahor* — what issues from a non-kosher animal is non-kosher, and what issues from a kosher animal is kosher.

### Mother Determines Status

The Gemara asks what the practical import of this statement is, given the context. The *Mishnah* seems to be stating a rule about *kedushat bechor*, but one might wonder whether the principle also applies to eating. That is indeed the extension: *umah hen ba'achilah* — what is the rule regarding consumption? The answer is that the mother determines the status of the offspring for eating purposes as well. The *Mishnah* is not merely saying something about *bechor* sanctity; it is stating a general rule about species classification. Critically, the principle cuts against following the appearance of the offspring: even if an animal is born from a kosher mother but looks like a non-kosher animal, it does not thereby become non-kosher. And even if an animal looks perfectly kosher but was born of a non-kosher mother, it does not thereby become permitted.

The Gemara establishes the source for this rule from a *baraita*: the verse *ach et zeh lo tochlu mi'ma'alei hageirah u'mi'mafrisei haparsa* — "but this you shall not eat, from among those that chew the cud and have split hooves" — implies there exists an animal that chews the cud and has split hooves yet you may not eat it. What is that? *Zeh tahor shenolad min hatamei* — a kosher-appearing animal born of a non-kosher mother. Alternatively, the verse could be read as referring to a non-kosher animal born of a kosher mother. The *baraita* explains that the verse (*hachi ka'amar*) speaks of *davar haba mi'ma'aleh geirah u'mi'mafris haparsa* — something that comes from a cud-chewer and hoof-splitter — you may not eat. Then the Torah says *gamal tamei hu* — the camel, it is non-kosher — teaching: *hu tamei*, the camel itself is non-kosher, *v'ein tamei hanolad min hatahor tamei* — but a non-kosher-looking animal born of a kosher mother is not non-kosher. Rather, it is *tahor*. The principle is thus bidirectional: a kosher-looking animal born of a non-kosher mother is non-kosher; a non-kosher-looking animal born of a kosher mother is kosher. What determines status is the mother, not the appearance.

### R. Gamliel: Appearance

Rabban Gamliel disagrees. He reads the doubled appearance of *gamal* in the Torah — once in Vayikra and once in Devarim — as teaching that even a camel born of a cow is non-kosher: *echad gamal hanolad min hagamala v'echad gamal hanolad min hapara*, whether the mother is a camel or a cow, if it looks like a camel it is forbidden. Rabban Gamliel thus holds that appearance alone determines kosher status. Presumably this position is intended to be stringent rather than lenient — he does not extend it to permit a non-kosher-looking animal born of a kosher mother; he says that if it looks non-kosher, that appearance is sufficient to forbid it even if the mother is kosher. The contemporary application is striking: if, as one might ask, an animal were produced in a laboratory without a kosher mother, Rabban Gamliel's view would suggest it is non-kosher based on appearance alone, with *simanim* being determinative.

### Rabbis: Mother Status

The Rabbis, who do not follow Rabban Gamliel's appearance-based ruling, are left with the doubled *gamal* in need of interpretation. If the doubling does not teach about appearance, what does it teach? *Chad le'asor atzmo v'chad le'asor chalavo* — one mention forbids the camel itself, the other forbids camel's milk. This moves the discussion into the realm of non-kosher byproducts.

### Camel Milk Forbidden

The Rabbis' reading establishes that the Torah forbids not only the non-kosher animal but also its milk — a direct expression of the principle *yotzei min hatamei tamei*. Even products of non-kosher animals, not only the animals themselves, fall under the prohibition.

### R. Shimon: Byproducts

Rabbi Shimon, however, derives the prohibition of camel milk differently — from the word *et* in *et hagamal*: the *et* extends the prohibition to something connected to but distinct from the camel itself, namely its milk. The Rabbis, who do not typically expound the word *et*, use the doubled *gamal* instead.

This gives the Gemara occasion to cite the well-known *baraita* about Shimon Ha'amsuni, who made a career of expounding every instance of *et* in the Torah to include something adjacent. When he reached *et Hashem Elokecha tira* — you shall fear *et* God — he stopped, unable to find anything comparable to the fear of God that could be included by the *et*, and accordingly retracted his entire enterprise. When his students asked what would become of all his prior *drashos*, he replied: just as I received reward for the exposition, so I receive reward for the retraction — *keshem shekibalti sachar al hadrisha kach ani mekabel sachar al haprisha*. Subsequently Rabbi Akiva came and resolved the verse: *et Hashem Elokecha tira* — *lerabbot talmidei chachamim*, to include Torah scholars in the requirement of *mora*. A beautiful interpretation is offered: Shimon Ha'amsuni himself could not imagine any human object of reverence remotely comparable to *mora shamayim*. It was precisely Rabbi Akiva's witness of Shimon Ha'amsuni's intellectual integrity — his willingness to sacrifice a life's work for the sake of *emet* in Torah interpretation — that led Rabbi Akiva to say: there exist *talmidei chachamim* whose stature can be, as it were, placed alongside *mora shamayim*.

The question naturally arises whether *drashos* of this sort require a received tradition (*mesorah*) or may be generated independently. The Gemara requires a *mesorah* for *gezeira shava*, though the Ramban in his *hassagot* on the Rambam's *Sefer HaMitzvot* qualifies this significantly: what it means to have a *mesorah* for a *gezeira shava* is that you have a tradition that the *halacha* is learnable from a *gezeira shava*, but you must work out the precise derivation yourself. A *kal va'chomer*, by contrast, the Gemara characterizes as *adam doresh me'atzmo* — something a person may derive independently — though the Gemara's own use of *kal va'chomer* is not always a purely formal logical construction. Rashi and *Tosafot* disagree on what lies in between: Rashi holds that virtually everything other than *kal va'chomer* requires a *mesorah*; *Tosafot* holds that everything other than *gezeira shava* may be derived independently.

## Byproducts Forbidden

### Milk as Novelty

Having established that the Rabbis derive the prohibition of camel milk from the doubled *gamal*, Rav Acha son of Rav Ashi raises a difficulty: why was a special verse necessary at all? We already know from the *hei* in *ha'teme'im* — the non-kosher animals — that their *tziran v'rotban v'kipa shelahem* (their brine, juices, and residue) are all forbidden. Non-kosher byproducts are already prohibited by that derivation. Why did the Torah need a separate verse to prohibit camel milk?

The Gemara answers: the prohibition of milk of a kosher animal is itself a *chiddush*. Since even kosher-animal milk required special permission, one might have thought that a similar logic applies to non-kosher animals — that milk, being a *chiddush* in any case, might be permitted even there. Therefore a special verse was needed.

### Blood Becomes Milk

Why is milk of a kosher animal a *chiddush*? The Gemara in Niddah discusses why nursing mothers typically do not menstruate, offering two explanations. The first holds that menstrual blood transforms into milk to nurse the child — *dam ne'char v'na'aseh chalav*. According to this view, milk is essentially transformed blood. One might then ask: if milk is transformed blood, why is it permitted? That question itself demonstrates that milk's permissibility is a *chiddush*, and by the same logic, one might think that non-kosher animals' milk, being similarly transformed from their blood, might also be permitted. Hence a specific verse is required to forbid it.

### Not Eiver Min Hachai

There is a second and independent reason milk of a kosher animal is a *chiddush*. Since normally nothing that comes from a living animal is permitted — *leika midi de'ati michay ve'shari*, there is nothing that issues from a living creature and is permitted — one might have thought that milk, which comes from a live animal, should be treated as a form of *ever min hachai* or at least *basar min hachai*. The Gemara clarifies that it is neither: it is not a limb (*ever*) and it is not flesh (*basar*). Yet the Gemara still frames the permissibility of milk as surprising: since there is truly nothing produced by a living animal that is permitted, I might have placed milk roughly in that category and said *vehai chalav ke'ever min hachay hu* — and therefore it should be forbidden. Once the Torah tells me that milk of a kosher animal is permitted, I might extend that reasoning and say non-kosher animals' milk is equally permitted. The double *gamal* or the *et* is therefore required to teach otherwise.

The point can be sharpened: milk is a *product* of the animal rather than a *part* of the animal. Unlike the brine or juices of non-kosher meat — which are, in a sense, suffused throughout the flesh — milk is externally produced. It does not taste like the animal. And unlike *eiver min hachai*, milk is not something you sever from the body; it is expressed. All of these factors might have led to a more lenient conclusion, which is precisely why a special verse was necessary to forbid it.

### Redemption Milk Rule

The Gemara then asks the prior question: granted that we need a verse to address non-kosher milk, but how do we know that the milk of a *kosher* animal is even permitted in the first place? The obvious answer — that the Torah forbids *basar b'chalav*, implying each is individually permitted — is rejected: perhaps milk itself is forbidden to eat but merely permitted in benefit, and the prohibition of *basar b'chalav* adds the further dimension of forbidding even benefit when cooked together. The Gemara attempts to derive kosher-milk permissibility from the ruling that milk of a redeemed *korban* is forbidden — if it were already forbidden as general milk, the verse about redeemed sacrifices would be unnecessary — but this too is deflected on the same logic: perhaps normal milk is forbidden for eating but not for benefit, and the *korban* case is stricter.

### Biblical Milk Examples

The Gemara ultimately grounds the permissibility of milk in several *Nach* sources. The verse in Mishlei, *vedei chalav izim lelachmecha* — sufficient goats' milk for your bread — is cited. One might deflect: perhaps the goats are milked and the milk is sold, and the proceeds buy bread, rather than the milk being eaten directly. The *pasuk* in Shemuel about David being sent to bring *aseret charitzei hachalav* — ten portions of cheese — to his brothers at the battlefield is then cited: surely one does not bring cheese to sell at a battlefield. The verse *eretz zavat chalav u'devash* is also invoked: the Torah praises the land with its milk and honey; would the Torah laud a land for something that is forbidden? And finally, the verse from Yeshayahu — *lechu shivru ve'ocholu... yayin ve'chalav* — go purchase and eat wine and milk — provides direct evidence of milk consumption.

### Non-Kosher Byproducts

Once the permissibility of kosher-animal milk is established, the circle closes. The very fact that milk is permitted — despite being transformed blood, despite resembling a form of *ever min hachai*, despite being a product of the living animal — constitutes the *chiddush* that would lead one to think non-kosher animal milk might similarly be permitted. The double *gamal* of the Rabbis, or the *et hagamal* of Rabbi Shimon, teaches that this permissiveness does not extend to non-kosher animals. *Yotzei min hatamei tamei* applies even to products that bear no resemblance in taste or appearance to the animal from which they come. This is the conclusion of the *sugya*, and the question of bee honey — which is produced by a non-kosher creature and yet is permitted — will be taken up in the continuation.
