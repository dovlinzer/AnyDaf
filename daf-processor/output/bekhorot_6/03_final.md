# Bekhorot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bechor Shor Chesev E (II)

### Intro: Mother-Child Match

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנַן הָתָם: רָחֵל שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין עֵז, וְעֵז שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין רָחֵל — פָּטוּר מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, וְאִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִקְצָת סִימָנִין — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the issue of a kosher animal that gave birth to a donkey of sorts and its firstborn status, the Gemara comments: **We learned** in a mishna **there** (16b): **A ewe that gave birth to a goat of sorts and a goat that gave birth to a ewe of sorts are exempt** from the mitzva of the **firstborn. And if** the offspring **has some of the characteristics** of its mother, it is **obligated,** i.e., subject to the obligations of firstborn status.

The sugya opens at the very bottom of 5b, where the Gemara is working through the principle that firstborn animals must match their mother's species in appearance. As part of that discussion, one derivation proceeds from the triple formulation *bechor shor bechor chesev bechor eiz* — the firstborn of ox, sheep, and goat — while a competing *tanna* holds that this triple formulation does not teach the mother-child matching rule at all, but rather that all three species' fats are placed on the *mizbeach*.

### Klal U'Prat Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אַךְ בְּכוֹר שׁוֹר״ — שֶׁיְּהֵא הוּא שׁוֹר וּבְכוֹרוֹ שׁוֹר, ״בְּכוֹר כֶּשֶׂב״ — שֶׁיְּהֵא הוּא כֶּשֶׂב וּבְכוֹרוֹ כֶּשֶׂב, ״בְּכוֹר עֵז״ — שֶׁיְּהֵא הוּא עֵז וּבְכוֹרוֹ עֵז.
>
> **Translation:** **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Yehuda says** they are derived from a verse, **as the verse states** with regard to a firstborn kosher animal: **"But the firstborn of an ox,** or the firstborn of a lamb, or the firstborn of a goat, you shall not redeem, they are holy" (Numbers 18:17). The word firstborn seems superfluous in this verse, as it is clear from the context that it is referring to a firstborn. Therefore, the phrase "the firstborn of an ox" is interpreted as teaching **that it must be an ox and its firstborn** must be **an ox** in order for firstborn status to apply. Likewise, the phrase **"the firstborn of a lamb"** teaches **that it must be a lamb and its firstborn a lamb,** and not another animal. Similarly, the phrase **"the firstborn of a goat"** teaches **that it must be a goat and its firstborn a goat.**

The Gemara explains why the Torah had to mention the *korban bechor* context by all three animals separately. One might ask: could we not have derived the fat-offering rule for one species from the other two? The Gemara works through each possibility systematically. We cannot omit the mention by ox and derive it from sheep and goat, because those two share a special status — they are both brought as a *korban pesach* — and that distinguishing feature might explain the fat-on-the-*mizbeach* rule, rendering it inapplicable to ox. We cannot omit the mention by sheep and derive it from ox and goat, because ox and goat are both brought as special *korbanot* for *avodah zarah* by the community, a status sheep does not share. And we cannot omit the mention by goat and derive it from sheep and ox, because both sheep and ox possess their own forms of additional *mizbeach* contribution — the sheep has the fat tail, the ox receives a larger measure of wine — which goat lacks. *Hilkach tzrichi*: all three had to be stated explicitly.

### R. Yose: Double Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: גַּלִּי רַחֲמָנָא בִּקְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, וְהוּא הַדִּין בִּקְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים, וְגָמַר קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים מִקְּדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei** HaGelili **holds** that **the Merciful One revealed** that a kosher firstborn animal, which is subject **to inherent sanctity,** does not have firstborn status unless it resembles its mother, **and the same is true with regard to** a donkey, which has **sanctity** that inheres in its **value, and he derives** the *halakha* of **sanctity** that inheres in its **value from** the *halakha* of **inherent sanctity.**

This brings us to Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position. If all three species must be stated in order to teach the fat-offering rule, why does the Torah write the word *bechor* separately before each animal? Why not simply write *ach bechor shor chesev va-eiz* without repeating *bechor* three times? The answer, Rabbi Yose HaGlili maintains, is that the repetition of *bechor* — *bechor shor, bechor chesev, bechor eiz* — is itself the teaching: *k'shehu shor bechoro shor*, when the firstborn is an ox its mother must also be an ox, and so on. The double *bechor* thus conveys the mother-child appearance-matching rule for kosher animals. Rabbi Yose HaGlili then applies the reverse logic to the *peter chamor*: the Rabbis derive the mother-child rule from the repeated *peter chamor* and apply it back to kosher animals; Rabbi Yose HaGlili begins with kosher animals and applies it outward to the donkey.

## Donkey Firstborn Lim (II)

### Broad to Narrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״אַךְ פָּדֹה תִפְדֶּה אֶת בְּכוֹר הָאָדָם וְאֵת בְּכוֹר הַבְּהֵמָה הַטְּמֵאָה תִּפְדֶּה״, שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ פִּטְרֵי סוּסִים וּגְמַלִּים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר״ — פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ, וְלֹא פִּטְרֵי סוּסִים וּגְמַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says** that **since it is stated: "Yet you shall redeem the firstborn of man, and the firstborn of non-kosher animals you shall redeem"** (Numbers 18:15), **I** would **derive** that **even firstborn horses and camels** must be redeemed, as they too are non-kosher animals. Therefore, **the verse states: "Firstborn of a donkey,"** which indicates **I said to you** that firstborn status applies to **a firstborn donkey, but not** to **firstborn horses or camels.**

This raises a question about the plain sense of the verses. One *pasuk* speaks broadly — redeem the firstborn of non-kosher animals generally — while another specifies the *peter chamor* redeemed with a sheep. How do these coexist? One could read the broad verse as applying universally while the sheep-redemption clause is limited to donkeys. But Rabbi Yose HaGlili asks: perhaps we should take the general verse at face value, and the firstborn of horses and camels would also require redemption?

The *baraita* records his reasoning: we hear from the verse *bechor behemah hatmeiah tifdeh* that one might include even *pitrei susim v'gamalim* — firstborn horses and camels. The Torah therefore says *u'peter chamor tifdeh vaseh*: the firstborn donkey you redeem with a sheep — *peter chamor amarti lecha v'lo pitrei susim u'gamalim*, only the firstborn donkey, not those of horses or camels. Still, one might think the restriction is only regarding the sheep: horses and camels cannot be redeemed *with a sheep*, but the underlying *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn non-kosher animals still applies to them, albeit with money or other value. At the top of 6a, the Torah's repetition of *peter chamor* a second time forecloses this: *pitrei chamorim amarti lecha v'lo pitrei susim u'gamalim* — not merely the sheep mechanism, but the entire *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn non-kosher animals is restricted to donkeys alone. Why the broad opening verse states the *mitzvah* in such generic terms without the restriction remains, as the lecturer notes, an open question.

## Bechor Shor Chesev E (II)

### Rav Achai's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר״ ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים — פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹרִים אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ, וְלֹא פִּטְרֵי סוּסִים וּגְמַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states** the phrase: **"Firstborn of a donkey"** (Exodus 13:13), **"firstborn of a donkey"** (Exodus 34:20), mentioning it a total of **two times.** The additional usage of the term indicates **I said to you** that firstborn status applies only to **firstborns of donkeys, but not** to **firstborns of horses or camels.**

At this point Rav Achai raises a challenge. As noted previously, *Tosafot* understands this Rav Achai to be Rav Achai Gaon of the *She'iltot*, whose questions are consistently more tangential in character and were likely inserted into the text as a later gloss. His objection here is that deriving the exclusion of horses and camels from the double mention of *peter chamor* does not work, because there is a competing way to read the first occurrence. There is a principle that something which belonged to a general category and was then singled out — *davar shehayah bichlal veyatza min haklal lilamed* — is not to be read as a personal exception but rather as teaching about the entire category. Accordingly, the first time the Torah specifies *peter chamor* one would read it not as a limitation but as an example: all non-kosher animals are redeemed, and it says *peter chamor* as an instance, extending the sheep-redemption mechanism to everything. The second occurrence would then limit the sheep mechanism specifically to donkeys — *mi'atinu mi'seh* — but that says nothing about excluding other non-kosher animals from the underlying *mitzvah*. So the double mention cannot achieve what Rabbi Yose HaGlili claimed.

### Gemara Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרֵיךְ רַב אַחַאי: אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא חַד, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא דָּבָר שֶׁהָיָה בַּכְּלָל, וְיָצָא מִן הַכְּלָל לְלַמֵּד, לֹא לְלַמֵּד עַל עַצְמוֹ יָצָא אֶלָּא לְלַמֵּד עַל הַכְּלָל כּוּלּוֹ יָצָא, וּלְעוֹלָם בְּשֶׂה. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר״ אַחֲרִינָא, פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹרִים וְלֹא פִּטְרֵי סוּסִים וּגְמַלִּים, וְאֵימָא: מַעֲטִינְהוּ מֹשֶׁה, וּלְעוֹלָם בְּכׇל דָּבָר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥai refutes** this claim: How can the extra verse alone indicate that other firstborn non-kosher animals do not have firstborn status? **If the Merciful One had written** the term "firstborn of a donkey" **once, I would say** a firstborn donkey is **an item that was included in a generalization** of all non-kosher animals, **but emerged from the generalization to teach** that it must be redeemed by means of a lamb. According to the principles of exegesis, it **emerged** from the generalization **to teach not** just **about itself, but emerged to teach about the entire generalization. And** accordingly, all non-kosher animals should **actually** be redeemed **by means of a lamb** as well. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "Firstborn of a donkey," another** time, to teach that only **firstborn donkeys** must be redeemed by means of a lamb, **but not firstborn horses or camels. And** therefore **I can say** that perhaps the earlier suggestion is correct: The Torah **excluded** firstborn horses and camels **from** being redeemed by means of **a lamb, but actually,** they must still be redeemed **by means of any** other **item.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר תִּפְדֶּה בְשֶׂה״ וַ״חֲמוֹר תִּפְדֶּה בְשֶׂה״. ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר תִּפְדֶּה בְשֶׂה״, ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר תִּפְדֶּה בְשֶׂה״ לְמָה לִי? פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹרִים אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ, וְלֹא פִּטְרֵי סוּסִים וּגְמַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** it is **so** that the Torah excludes horses and camels only from redemption by means of a lamb but not from redemption in general, **let the Merciful One write: Firstborn of a donkey you shall redeem with a lamb, and: A donkey you shall redeem with a lamb,** which would suffice. **Why do I** need the verse to state: **"A firstborn of a donkey you shall redeem with a lamb," "a firstborn of a donkey you shall redeem with a lamb,"** using the same phrase both times? It is necessary to teach that **I said to you** that firstborn status applies only to **firstborns of donkeys, but not** to **firstborns of horses or camels.**

## Donkey Firstborn Lim (II)

### Peter Chamor Twice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא דִידַן, לְמַעוֹטֵי סוּסִים וּגְמַלִּים, מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the *tanna* of our** mishna, who derived from the repetition of the phrase "firstborn of a donkey" that the mitzva of redeeming a firstborn donkey applies only if the mother and offspring are both donkeys, **from where does he** derive that the Torah **excludes horses and camels** from firstborn status?

With that established, the Gemara turns to the *tanna didan*, the author of our *Mishnah*, who uses the double *peter chamor* to derive the mother-child appearance rule. If he uses that doubling for a different purpose, where does he learn that horses and camels are excluded from the *mitzvah* of redeeming firstborn non-kosher animals?

### Klal U'Prat: All Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כׇּל מִקְנְךָ תִּזָּכָר — כָּלַל, שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה וַחֲמוֹר — פָּרַט, כְּלָל וּפְרָט — אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט, שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה וַחֲמוֹר — אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** it is derived from the verse: "From all your livestock you shall take the males, the firstborn of the ox and lamb. And the firstborn of a donkey you shall redeem with a lamb" (Exodus 34:19–20). The phrase **"From all your livestock you shall take the males"** is **a generalization,** and the reference to **an ox and a lamb and a donkey** is **a detail.** According to the principles of exegesis, in the case of **a generalization and a detail, the generalization** is referring **only** to **that which** is specified **in the detail.**Therefore, **an ox and a lamb and a donkey, yes,** their offspring have firstborn status, but **anything else, no,** it does not have firstborn status.

Rav Pappa answers from a different verse: *kol miknecha tizkar peter shor vaseh u'peter chamor tifdeh vaseh* — all your animals, then ox, flock, and donkey. This is a case of *klal u'frat*: "all your animals" is the general (*klal*), and ox, flock, and donkey are the specific (*prat*). The rule of *klal u'frat ein baklal ela mah shebaprat* means the general is confined to what appears in the particular. Only ox, flock, and donkey — *midei achrinei lo*, nothing else.

### R. Yose: Verse Break

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? ״פֶּטֶר״ הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן, וְרַבָּנַן? וָי״ו הֲדַר עָרְבֵיהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** how does he respond to this claim? The Gemara answers: He holds that a donkey is not included in the generalization "From all your livestock you shall take the males," as the word **"firstborn"** in the phrase "and the firstborn of a donkey," indicates that the Torah **interrupts the matter,** i.e., the list of details. The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis,** how do they respond to this claim? The Gemara answers: The letter ***vav*,** meaning "and," in the phrase "and the firstborn of a donkey," indicates that **the verse returns** and **combines** the donkey with the other animals as having firstborn status.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili objects that this *klal u'frat* reading does not hold. The verse *peter shor vaseh* and the following *u'peter chamor tifdeh vaseh* are in fact separate statements. The word *peter* that begins the second clause — *peter hifsik hainyan* — marks a new sentence and breaks the connection to the preceding general statement. You cannot construct a *klal u'frat* across a verse break.

### Rabbis: Different Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי? לָא לִכְתּוֹב לָא וָי״ו וְלָא ״פֶּטֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** how does he respond? The Gemara answers: He holds that if the donkey was indeed included in the delineation of the other animals that follows the generalization, the Torah **would write neither** the letter ***vav* nor** the word **"firstborn,"** and it still would be included.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? אַיְּידֵי דְּהָא קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים, וְהָא קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף — פָּסֵיק לְהוּ וַהֲדַר עָרֵיב לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis,** how do they respond to this claim? The Gemara answers: **Since this,** a firstborn donkey, has **sanctity** that inheres in its **value** and must be redeemed by means of a lamb, **and that,** a firstborn ox and lamb, possess **inherent sanctity** and are sacrificed upon the altar, the word "firstborn" must **interpose** between **them, and** the verse must **combine them again** with the letter *vav* for "and," which indicates that a donkey is also included in the delineation that defines the generalization "From all your livestock you shall take the males."

The Rabbis respond on two levels. First, the *vav* at the opening of the second clause — *u'peter chamor* — connects it back to the preceding statement and restores the *klal u'frat* structure; *vav hadar arvei kra*, the *vav* reconnects them. Rabbi Yose HaGlili counters that if the Torah wanted them as one sentence it would not have written *peter* at all — the *vav* alone would have sufficed; writing *peter* signals a deliberate break.

The Rabbis respond with a second argument: *aidei deha kedushat haguf v'ha kedushat damim* — the two halves of the verse describe fundamentally different categories of sanctity. The first half, *peter shor vaseh*, concerns animals brought as *korbanot*, carrying *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity. The second half, *u'peter chamor tifdeh*, concerns an animal redeemed through monetary value given to a *kohen*, *kedushat damim* alone. The two halves also carry different verbs: the first uses *tizkar*, the second *tifdeh*. Because these are such different types of *bechor* obligation, the Torah needed to mark them as separate clauses — *pasik lehu*, it had to break them. But because it still wished to maintain the overarching *klal u'frat* principle limiting the *mitzvah* to these species alone, *hadar arvei lehu*, it then reconnected them with the *vav*. The student question arises as to why human firstborns, who also appear in this context, are grouped with *peter chamor* rather than with the first clause. The answer is that humans, like donkeys, are redeemed — they are not brought as *korbanot* — and so they share the *kedushat damim* character of the second half of the verse. In sum: the Rabbis derive the exclusion of horses and camels from the *klal u'frat*, while Rabbi Yose HaGlili derives it from the double *peter chamor*, and both arrive at the same conclusion.

## Partial Simanim Rules

### Cow-Donkey Hybrid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין חֲמוֹר, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ מִקְצָת סִימָנִין — מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara previously cited the mishna (16b) that rules that a ewe that gave birth to a goat of sorts and a goat that gave birth to a ewe of sorts are exempt from the mitzva of the firstborn. And if the offspring has some of the characteristics of its mother, it is subject to the obligations of firstborn status. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages about a related matter: In the case of a **cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts,** where the mishna (5b) ruled that the offspring does not have firstborn status, **and it has some of the characteristics** of the mother, **what is** the *halakha*?

The Gemara now raises a new set of questions. It has already been established that if a sheep gives birth to an animal that looks like a goat, or vice versa, there is no *kedushat bechor*. It was also established that if the offspring has *some* characteristics of the right species — some sheep-like traits in a mostly goat-looking animal born of a sheep — it remains obligated in *bechor*, albeit as a *baal mum*. The Gemara now asks: does this principle of partial *simanim* transfer to cross-category cases? The opening case: a cow (*parah*) gives birth to something that looks like a donkey but retains some cow-like characteristics. Is the partial resemblance to the mother sufficient to impose *kedushat bechor*?

### Sheep-Goat Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵז שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין רָחֵל, וְרָחֵל שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין עֵז, דְּכִי יֵשׁ מִקְצָת סִימָנִין — חַיֶּיבֶת בִּבְכוֹרָה, דְּהָא טְהוֹרָה וְהָא טְהוֹרָה, הָא קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף וְהָא קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dilemma: When the mishna states with regard to **a goat that gave birth to a ewe of sorts and a ewe that gave birth to a goat of sorts, that when it has some of the characteristics** of the mother, it **is obligated,** i.e., subject to the obligations of **firstborn** status, perhaps this is because a ewe and a goat are somewhat similar; **as this** one is **a kosher** animal **and that** one is **a kosher** animal. Likewise, **this** one has **inherent sanctity** and may be sacrificed on the altar, **and that** one has **inherent sanctity.**

The ease of the sheep-goat ruling is attributable to several compounding factors: both animals appear in the same *parsha*, both are kosher (*de'ha tahor ve'ha tahor*), and both carry *kedushat haguf* (*ha kedushat haguf ve'ha kedushat haguf*). When all relevant dimensions align, partial *simanim* suffices. But the present case is more complex.

### Different Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא, דְּהָא טְמֵאָה וְהָא טְהוֹרָה, הָא קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף וְהָא קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים — לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּנֵי מִיקְדָּשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה — קָדוֹשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** But **here,** in the case of a cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts, perhaps the *halakha* would be different, **as this,** the offspring, resembles **a non-kosher** animal, **and that,** the mother, is **a kosher** animal. Likewise, the firstborns of **this** species, the cow, possess **inherent sanctity, and** the firstborns of **that** species, the donkey, have **sanctity** that inheres in its **value.** Therefore, perhaps the offspring does **not** have firstborn status, even if it has some of the characteristics of the mother. **Or perhaps** it can be reasoned that **since** both **this** cow **and that** donkey are **sanctified with firstborn** status, the offspring is **sanctified** with firstborn status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי מִיקְדָּשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה קָדוֹשׁ, חֲמוֹר שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין סוּס מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי לָא בַּת מִיקְדָּשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּמִין טְמֵאָה הוּא קָדוֹשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **since** both **this** cow **and that** donkey are **sanctified with firstborn** status, the offspring is also **sanctified** with firstborn status if it has some characteristics of the mother, then **what is** the *halakha* in a case of **a donkey that gave birth to a horse of sorts?** Perhaps **here** the offspring **certainly** does not have firstborn status, as a horse **is not sanctified with firstborn** status. **Or perhaps** one can reason that **since** the mother **is** a member of **a non-kosher species,** as is the offspring, the offspring should be **sanctified** with firstborn status, as it has some characteristics of the mother, which is a donkey.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּמִין טְמֵאָה הוּא קָדוֹשׁ, פָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין סוּס מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי הָא טְהוֹרָה וְהָא טְמֵאָה, הָא בַּת מִיקְדָּשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה וְהָא לָא בַּת מִיקְדָּשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה, אוֹ דִלְמָא סִימָנִין מִילְּתָא הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an additional dilemma about a related case: **If you say** in the previous case that **since it is a non-kosher species** it is **sanctified,** then **what is** the *halakha* in the case of **a cow that gave birth to a horse of sorts? Here,** one can say there **certainly** is no reason for the offspring to have firstborn status, as **this** cow is **kosher and that** horse is **non-kosher.** And in addition, **this** cow is **sanctified with firstborn** status **and that** horse is **not sanctified with firstborn** status. **Or perhaps** one can reason that having some of the **characteristics** of the mother **is** a significant **matter** and the offspring does have firstborn sanctity.

The cow-donkey case differs on multiple axes: one is kosher, one is non-kosher (*de'ha tahor ve'ha tamei*), and one carries *kedushat haguf* while the other carries only *kedushat damim* (*ha kedushat haguf v'ha kedushat damim*). Despite these differences, one might still reason that since both fall under the general category of *bechor* — one as a *bechor* of a kosher animal and one as a *peter chamor* — their shared *bechor* status is sufficient basis to say that partial *simanim* still obligates. The Gemara then extends the question: if we grant that the cow-donkey case is obligated with partial *simanim*, what about a donkey that gives birth to something resembling a horse, where not only are the categories different but the horse is entirely outside the *bechor* framework? And further: what if a cow gives birth to something resembling a horse, where the offspring's non-mother half is both of a different kosher status *and* entirely outside the *bechor* category? Is the principle *simanim milta hi* — that partial characteristics are inherently sufficient — broad enough to cover even those cases?

### Baraita: Simanim Enough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה — פְּטוּרָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִקְצָת סִימָנִין — חַיֶּיבֶת בִּבְכוֹרָה. מַאי לָאו אֲפִילּוּ פָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין סוּס? לֹא, פָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **A kosher animal that gave birth to a non-kosher animal of sorts is exempt from** its offspring being counted **a firstborn. If** the offspring **has some of the characteristics** of the mother it **is obligated,** i.e., subject to the obligations of **firstborn** status. **What,** does this **not** include **even** the case of **a cow that gave birth to a horse of sorts? No,** it is referring to a case such as where **a cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts,** where both species are subject to the sanctity of the firstborn.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין חֲמוֹר, וַחֲמוֹר שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין סוּס — פְּטוּרָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִקְצָת סִימָנִין — חַיֶּיבֶת בִּבְכוֹרָה. מַאי לָאו אַתַּרְוַיְיהוּ? לָא, אַפָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from a *baraita*: In the case of **a cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts or a donkey that gave birth to a horse of sorts,** each of the mothers is **exempt from** their offspring being counted **a firstborn. If** the offspring **has some of the characteristics** of the mother, it is **obligated,** i.e., subject **to** the obligations of **firstborn** status. **What,** is the last clause **not** referring **to both** cases of the *baraita*? If so, this resolves two of the dilemmas about offspring that have some characteristics of the mother. The Gemara responds: **No,** it is referring only **to** the case of **a cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts,** so only that dilemma is resolved.

The Gemara cites a *baraita* in an attempt to resolve the question: a kosher animal (*behemah tehorah*) that gives birth to something resembling a non-kosher animal (*kemin behemah teme'ah*) is exempt from *bechorah*, but if it has some of the right characteristics (*im yesh bah miktzat simanim*), it is obligated. The Gemara asks whether this *baraita* resolves even the most extreme case — a cow giving birth to something that looks like a horse — but concludes it does not. The word *teme'ah* in the *baraita* may refer specifically to a donkey, not any non-kosher animal, so only the first and least extreme case — the cow-donkey with partial cow characteristics — is resolved.

A second *baraita* is then cited that adds the case of *chamor sheyalda kemin sus* — a donkey giving birth to something resembling a horse. Again, partial *simanim* obligates, and again the Gemara attempts to read this as resolving the intermediate question. The Gemara declines: perhaps the *baraita*'s second case is mentioned only to tell us it is *patur* without partial *simanim*, in which case why even mention it, since a donkey-horse would obviously be exempt once we know a cow-donkey is exempt? *Itzterich* — it was necessary. One might have thought that a donkey and horse, being more physically similar than a cow and donkey — neither has horns, both have single hooves (*parsotav klutot*) — are perhaps not really different species at all, and the horse-looking animal is merely what one might call a reddish donkey (*chamor adom be'alma hu*). The *baraita* therefore tells us it is still *patur*. The upside of this reading, however, is that the *baraita* mentions partial *simanim* obligating even in the donkey-horse case, which would resolve the intermediate question. But the Gemara once more deflects: the partial *simanim* clause in the *baraita* refers only to the cow-donkey case. The donkey-horse question, and the more extreme cow-horse question, remain unresolved.

### Donkey-Horse Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל חֲמוֹר שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין סוּס, לְמַאי קָתָנֵי לַהּ? לִפְטוּרָא? פְּשִׁיטָא! הַשְׁתָּה, וּמָה פָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין חֲמוֹר, דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּנֵי מִיקְדָּשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה נִינְהוּ, אָמְרַתְּ: אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִקְצָת סִימָנִין — אִין, וְאִי לָא — לָא, חֲמוֹר שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין סוּס מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **for what** purpose does the *baraita* **teach** the case of **a donkey that gave birth to a horse of sorts?** If it is **to exempt** the offspring from being accounted firstborn status when it does not have some of the characteristics of its mother, that *halakha* is **obvious** for the following reason: **And now,** if in the case of **a cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts, where** both **this** species **and that** species **are sanctified with firstborn** status, **you said** that **if** the offspring **has some of the characteristics** of its mother then **yes,** it has firstborn status, **and if** it does **not** have such characteristics, then **no,** the offspring does not have firstborn status, then in the case of **a donkey that gave birth to a horse of sorts,** which generally is not sanctified with firstborn status, **is** it **necessary** to state that the horse does not have firstborn status unless it has some of the characteristics of its mother?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ: הָתָם הוּא דְּיֵשׁ לָהּ קַרְנַיִם, וְהָא אֵין לָהּ קַרְנַיִם; הָא פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ סְדוּקוֹת, וְהָא פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ קְלוּטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** to teach that case in addition to that of a cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts, as it might **enter your mind** that **there,** in the case of the cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts, the offspring does not have firstborn status because a cow and donkey are entirely different from one another, **as** a cow **has horns, and this** animal, a donkey, **does not have horns.** Moreover, with regard to **that** animal, a cow, **its hooves are split, but** with regard to **this** animal, a donkey, **its hooves are closed [*kelutot*].**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי אֵין לָהּ קַרְנַיִם, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ קְלוּטוֹת, אֵימָא חֲמוֹר אָדוֹם בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But here,** in the case of a donkey that gave birth to a horse of sorts, where a similarity does exist between the mother and its offspring, **as this** one **and that** one both **do not have horns,** and with regard to both **this** one **and that** one **its hooves are closed, I can say** that this offspring **is merely a red donkey,** i.e., a donkey with the coloring of a horse, and has firstborn status. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that it is a different species from that of the mother, and does not have firstborn status.

The reason the *baraita* bothers to state the donkey-horse case at all is to forestall the inference that because donkey and horse are physically so similar, one might mistakenly conclude the horse-resembling offspring is simply a variety of donkey and is therefore obligated. The *baraita* tells us it is not — it is a distinct enough type that without partial *simanim* of the mother, it is exempt.

### Unresolved Questions

Two questions remain open: whether partial *simanim* suffice in the donkey-horse case, and whether partial *simanim* suffice in the cow-horse case. The underlying question cutting across all of these is what the Gemara actually envisions biologically — whether it contemplates a genuine interspecies offspring, or simply an animal that looks like another species. This is never fully resolved. On a related note, the Gemara has not yet addressed the role of the father's species in any of these determinations; that belongs to a later *Mishnah*.

## Mother vs. Appearance

### Yotzei Min HaTamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פָּרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה מִין חֲמוֹר, וַחֲמוֹר שֶׁיָּלְדָה כְּמִין סוּס — פְּטוּרָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר״ ״פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא הַיּוֹלֵד חֲמוֹר וְהַנּוֹלָד חֲמוֹר. וּמַה הֵם בַּאֲכִילָה? בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה כְּמִין בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה — מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה, וּטְמֵאָה שֶׁיָּלְדָה כְּמִין בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה — אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה, שֶׁהַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַטָּמֵא — טָמֵא, וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַטָּהוֹר — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A cow that gave birth to a donkey of sorts and a donkey that gave birth to a horse of sorts are exempt from** their offspring being counted **a firstborn, as it is stated:** "And every **firstborn of a donkey** you shall redeem with a lamb" (Exodus 13:13); "and the **firstborn of a donkey** you shall redeem with a lamb" (Exodus 34:20). The Torah states this *halakha* **twice,** indicating that one is not obligated **unless** both **the birth mother is a donkey and the** animal **born is a donkey. And what** is the offspring's halakhic status **with regard to** its **consumption?** In the case of **a kosher animal that gave birth to a non-kosher animal of sorts,** its **consumption is permitted. And** in the case of **a non-kosher** animal **that gave birth to a kosher animal of sorts,** its **consumption is prohibited.** This is **because that which emerges from the non-kosher** animal **is non-kosher and that which emerges from the kosher** animal **is kosher.**

The *Mishnah* now states the foundational principle: *yotzei min hatamei tamei, yotzei min hatahor tahor* — what issues from a non-kosher animal is non-kosher, and what issues from a kosher animal is kosher.

### Mother Determines Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גְּמָ׳ לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵי ״שֶׁהַיּוֹצֵא״?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Why do I** need the mishna **to teach** the concluding phrase: This is **because that which emerges** from the non-kosher animal is non-kosher and that which emerges from the kosher animal is kosher?

The Gemara asks what the practical import of this statement is, given the context. The *Mishnah* seems to be stating a rule about *kedushat bechor*, but one might wonder whether the principle also applies to eating. That is indeed the extension: *umah hen ba'achilah* — what is the rule regarding consumption? The answer is that the mother determines the status of the offspring for eating purposes as well. The *Mishnah* is not merely saying something about *bechor* sanctity; it is stating a general rule about species classification. Critically, the principle cuts against following the appearance of the offspring: even if an animal is born from a kosher mother but looks like a non-kosher animal, it does not thereby become non-kosher. And even if an animal looks perfectly kosher but was born of a non-kosher mother, it does not thereby become permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, דְּלָא תִּיחַלַּף לָךְ, דְּלָא תֵּימָא: זִיל בָּתְרֵיהּ דִּידֵיהּ, וְהַאי טָהוֹר מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, וְהַאי טָמֵא מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, אֶלָּא זִיל בָּתַר אִימֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It **is merely a mnemonic** to ensure **that you do not** mistakenly **reverse** the *halakha* in these two cases, so **that you do not say: Follow its** appearance, **and** therefore **this** offspring that looks like a kosher animal is **a full-fledged kosher** animal, despite being born to a non-kosher animal; **and that** one that looks like a non-kosher animal **is a full-fledged non-kosher** animal, despite its mother being a kosher animal. **Rather,** the mishna teaches that one must **follow** the status of **its mother.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אַךְ אֶת זֶה לֹא תֹאכְלוּ מִמַּעֲלֵי הַגֵּרָה וּמִמַּפְרִיסֵי הַפַּרְסָה״ — יֵשׁ לְךָ שֶׁהוּא מַעֲלֵה גֵרָה וּמַפְרִיס פַּרְסָה וְאִי אַתָּה אוֹכְלוֹ, וְאֵיזֶה זֶה? טָהוֹר שֶׁנּוֹלַד מִן הַטָּמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** that when the verse states: **"Nevertheless these shall you not eat, of them that chew the cud, or of them that split the hoof"** (Leviticus 11:4), this verse indicates that **you have** an animal **that chews the cud and has split hooves, but** nevertheless **you may not eat it. And which is this?** It is **a kosher** animal **that was born from a non-kosher** animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: דָּבָר הַבָּא מִמַּעֲלֵי הַגֵּרָה וּמִמַּפְרִיסֵי הַפַּרְסָה לָא תֵּיכוּל, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״גָּמָל... טָמֵא הוּא״ — הוּא טָמֵא, וְאֵין טָמֵא הַנּוֹלָד מִן הַטָּהוֹר טָמֵא אֶלָּא טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **This** is what it **is saying: You shall not eat a being that comes from** one of those animals that **chew their cud and have split hooves** if it itself does not have the signs of being kosher. To counter this, the continuation of **the verse states: "The camel…is impure** for you" (Leviticus 11:4). This indicates that a camel itself **is non-kosher, but a non-kosher** animal **that is born from a kosher** animal **is not non-kosher; rather,** it is **kosher.**

The Gemara establishes the source for this rule from a *baraita*: the verse *ach et zeh lo tochlu mi'ma'alei hageirah u'mi'mafrisei haparsa* — "but this you shall not eat, from among those that chew the cud and have split hooves" — implies there exists an animal that chews the cud and has split hooves yet you may not eat it. What is that? *Zeh tahor shenolad min hatamei* — a kosher-appearing animal born of a non-kosher mother. Alternatively, the verse could be read as referring to a non-kosher animal born of a kosher mother. The *baraita* explains that the verse (*hachi ka'amar*) speaks of *davar haba mi'ma'aleh geirah u'mi'mafris haparsa* — something that comes from a cud-chewer and hoof-splitter — you may not eat. Then the Torah says *gamal tamei hu* — the camel, it is non-kosher — teaching: *hu tamei*, the camel itself is non-kosher, *v'ein tamei hanolad min hatahor tamei* — but a non-kosher-looking animal born of a kosher mother is not non-kosher. Rather, it is *tahor*. The principle is thus bidirectional: a kosher-looking animal born of a non-kosher mother is non-kosher; a non-kosher-looking animal born of a kosher mother is kosher. What determines status is the mother, not the appearance.

### R. Gamliel: Appearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״גָּמָל״ ״גָּמָל״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים, אֶחָד גָּמָל הַנּוֹלָד מִן הַגְּמַלָּה, וְאֶחָד גָּמָל הַנּוֹלָד מִן הַפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** it is stated: **"Camel"** (Leviticus 11:4), **"camel"** (Deuteronomy 14:7), teaching the term **two times** with regard to the prohibition of consumption. This teaches that **both a camel that is born from a camel and a camel that is born from a cow** are non-kosher. According to Rabbi Shimon, a non-kosher animal born to a kosher animal is forbidden for consumption, in contrast to the ruling in the mishna.

Rabban Gamliel disagrees. He reads the doubled appearance of *gamal* in the Torah — once in Vayikra and once in Devarim — as teaching that even a camel born of a cow is non-kosher: *echad gamal hanolad min hagamala v'echad gamal hanolad min hapara*, whether the mother is a camel or a cow, if it looks like a camel it is forbidden. Rabban Gamliel thus holds that appearance alone determines kosher status. Presumably this position is intended to be stringent rather than lenient — he does not extend it to permit a non-kosher-looking animal born of a kosher mother; he says that if it looks non-kosher, that appearance is sufficient to forbid it even if the mother is kosher. The contemporary application is striking: if, as one might ask, an animal were produced in a laboratory without a kosher mother, Rabban Gamliel's view would suggest it is non-kosher based on appearance alone, with *simanim* being determinative.

### Rabbis: Mother Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״גָּמָל״ ״גָּמָל״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? חַד לֶאֱסוֹר עַצְמוֹ, וְחַד לֶאֱסוֹר חֲלָבוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis** in the *baraita*, who disagree with Rabbi Shimon, **what do they do with this** repetition of **"camel," "camel"?** The Gemara answers that **one** of the verses is written **to prohibit** the camel **itself and one** is written **to prohibit** the female camel's **milk.**

The Rabbis, who do not follow Rabban Gamliel's appearance-based ruling, are left with the doubled *gamal* in need of interpretation. If the doubling does not teach about appearance, what does it teach? *Chad le'asor atzmo v'chad le'asor chalavo* — one mention forbids the camel itself, the other forbids camel's milk. This moves the discussion into the realm of non-kosher byproducts.

### Camel Milk Forbidden

Rabban Gamliel's reading establishes that the Torah forbids not only the non-kosher animal but also its milk — a direct expression of the principle *yotzei min hatamei tamei*. Even products of non-kosher animals, not only the animals themselves, fall under the prohibition.

### R. Shimon: Byproducts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, לֶאֱסוֹר חֲלָבוֹ מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אֶת הַגָּמָל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Shimon, from where does he** derive that a camel's **milk is forbidden?** The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the phrase **"the camel [*et hagamal*]."** The verse could have stated just the term "*hagamal*." The addition of the word "*et*" teaches that the prohibition applies also to its milk.

Rabbi Shimon, however, derives the prohibition of camel milk differently — from the word *et* in *et hagamal*: the *et* extends the prohibition to something connected to but distinct from the camel itself, namely its milk. The Rabbis, who do not typically expound the word *et*, use the doubled *gamal* instead.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: שִׁמְעוֹן הָעַמְסוֹנִי הָיָה דּוֹרֵשׁ כׇּל ״אֵת״ וָ״אֵת״ שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא״ פֵּירַשׁ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: רַבִּי, כׇּל ״אֶתִּין״ שֶׁדָּרַשְׁתָּ מָה תְּהֵא עֲלֵיהֶם? אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁקִּבׇּלְתִּי שָׂכָר עַל הַדְּרִישָׁה, כָּךְ אֲנִי מְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר עַל הַפְּרִישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Shimon HaAmasoni would interpret each and every** occurrence of the word **"*et*" in the Torah,** deriving additional *halakhot* with regard to the particular subject matter. **Once he reached** the verse: **"You shall fear the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 6:13), which is written with the added word "*et*," **he withdrew** from this method of exposition, as whose fear could be an extension of the fear of God? **His students said to him: Our teacher, what will be with all** the occurrences of **"***et***" that you interpreted** until now? Shimon HaAmasoni **said to them: Just as I received reward for the exposition, so I receive reward for** my **withdrawal** from using this method of exposition.

This gives the Gemara occasion to cite the well-known *baraita* about Shimon Ha'amsuni, who made a career of expounding every instance of *et* in the Torah to include something adjacent. When he reached *et Hashem Elokecha tira* — you shall fear *et* God — he stopped, unable to find anything comparable to the fear of God that could be included by the *et*, and accordingly retracted his entire enterprise. When his students asked what would become of all his prior *drashos*, he replied: just as I received reward for the exposition, so I receive reward for the retraction — *keshem shekibalti sachar al hadrisha kach ani mekabel sachar al haprisha*. Subsequently Rabbi Akiva came and resolved the verse: *et Hashem Elokecha tira* — *lerabbot talmidei chachamim*, to include Torah scholars in the requirement of *mora*. A beautiful interpretation is offered: Shimon Ha'amsuni himself could not imagine any human object of reverence remotely comparable to *mora shamayim*. It was precisely Rabbi Akiva's witness of Shimon Ha'amsuni's intellectual integrity — his willingness to sacrifice a life's work for the sake of *emet* in Torah interpretation — that led Rabbi Akiva to say: there exist *talmidei chachamim* whose stature can be, as it were, placed alongside *mora shamayim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְלִימֵּד: ״אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא״ — לְרַבּוֹת תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The word "*et*" in this verse was not explained **until Rabbi Akiva came and taught: "You shall fear the Lord your God";** the word "*et*" in the verse serves **to include Torah scholars,** i.e., that one is commanded to fear them just as one fears God.

The question naturally arises whether *drashos* of this sort require a received tradition (*mesorah*) or may be generated independently. The Gemara requires a *mesorah* for *gezeira shava*, though the Ramban in his *hassagot* on the Rambam's *Sefer HaMitzvot* qualifies this significantly: what it means to have a *mesorah* for a *gezeira shava* is that you have a tradition that the *halacha* is learnable from a *gezeira shava*, but you must work out the precise derivation yourself. A *kal va'chomer*, by contrast, the Gemara characterizes as *adam doresh me'atzmo* — something a person may derive independently — though the Gemara's own use of *kal va'chomer* is not always a purely formal logical construction. Rashi and *Tosafot* disagree on what lies in between: Rashi holds that virtually everything other than *kal va'chomer* requires a *mesorah*; *Tosafot* holds that everything other than *gezeira shava* may be derived independently.

## Byproducts Forbidden

### Milk as Novelty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֲבָל מֵעַתָּה, טַעְמָא דְרַבָּנַן מִ״גָּמָל״ ״גָּמָל״, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מֵ״אֶת הַגָּמָל״, הָא לָאו הָכִי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא חָלָב דִּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה שְׁרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: If that is so,** that **the reason of the Rabbis** for the *halakha* that a camel's milk is forbidden is that it is derived **from** the words **"camel," "camel,"** written twice, **and Rabbi Shimon** derives it **from "the camel [*et hagamal*],"** then could it be reasoned **that** if **not** for **this** derivation **I would say** that **milk of a non-kosher animal is permitted?**

Having established that the Rabbis derive the prohibition of camel milk from the doubled *gamal*, Rav Acha son of Rav Ashi raises a difficulty: why was a special verse necessary at all? We already know from the *hei* in *ha'teme'im* — the non-kosher animals — that their *tziran v'rotban v'kipa shelahem* (their brine, juices, and residue) are all forbidden. Non-kosher byproducts are already prohibited by that derivation. Why did the Torah need a separate verse to prohibit camel milk?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: ״הַטְּמֵאִים״ — לֶאֱסוֹר צִירָן, וְרוֹטְבָן, וְקֵיפֶה שֶׁלָּהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** In **what** way **is** this case **different from that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah states with regard to the prohibition against eating creeping animals: "These are they **that are impure [*hateme'im*]** to you among all the creeping animals" (Leviticus 11:31). The Sages interpret the letter *heh* in the term "that are impure [*hateme'im*]" **to prohibit their juice** that oozes from their body **and their gravy** that is produced when they are cooked, **and sediments** of **their** flesh that congeal at the bottom of the dish when cooked. Evidently, any liquid that emerges from a non-kosher animal is forbidden.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל דִּבְהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה נָמֵי חִידּוּשׁ הוּא, דְּאָמַר מַר: דָּם נֶעְכָּר וְנַעֲשֶׂה חָלָב, וְכֵיוָן דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא — בִּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה נָמֵי לִישְׁתְּרֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi responded: The additional exposition to derive that camel's milk is forbidden **was necessary,** because it might **enter your mind to say** it may be permitted, **since** the permissibility of consumption of the milk **of a kosher animal is also a novelty.** The milk originates from the blood, which is forbidden for consumption, **as the Master said** that the reason a nursing woman does not experience menstruation is because **the blood is spoiled and becomes milk. Since it is a novelty** that the Torah permits milk despite its origins, it might enter your mind to say that **with regard to a non-kosher animal it should also be permitted.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that it is forbidden.

The Gemara answers: the prohibition of milk of a kosher animal is itself a *chiddush*. Since even kosher-animal milk required special permission, one might have thought that a similar logic applies to non-kosher animals — that milk, being a *chiddush* in any case, might be permitted even there. Therefore a special verse was needed.

### Blood Becomes Milk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דָּם נֶעְכָּר וְנַעֲשֶׂה חָלָב, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵיבָרֶיהָ מִתְפָּרְקִין הֵימֶנָּה וְאֵין נַפְשָׁהּ חוֹזֶרֶת עָלֶיהָ עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה חֹדֶשׁ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **This works out well according to the one who says** that a nursing woman does not menstruate because **the blood is spoiled and becomes milk. But according to the one who says** that the milk does not originate from blood, but rather the reason she does not menstruate is because **her limbs become disjointed and her soul,** i.e., her health, **does not return to her until twenty-four months** later, **what is there to say?** Why does the Torah employ a verse to render forbidden the milk of a non-kosher animal?

Why is milk of a kosher animal a *chiddush*? The Gemara in Niddah discusses why nursing mothers typically do not menstruate, offering two explanations. The first holds that menstrual blood transforms into milk to nurse the child — *dam ne'char v'na'aseh chalav*. According to this view, milk is essentially transformed blood. One might then ask: if milk is transformed blood, why is it permitted? That question itself demonstrates that milk's permissibility is a *chiddush*, and by the same logic, one might think that non-kosher animals' milk, being similarly transformed from their blood, might also be permitted. Hence a specific verse is required to forbid it.

### Not Eiver Min Hachai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְלֵיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּאָתֵי מֵחַי וְשַׁרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי חָלָב כִּי אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי הוּא, וּשְׁרֵי, וְהִילְכָּךְ אֲפִילּוּ בִּבְהֵמָה טְמֵאָה לִישְׁתְּרֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary,** because it might **enter your mind to say** it may be permitted; **since** generally speaking **there is nothing that comes from a living** animal **that the Merciful One permits, and** yet **this milk** of a kosher animal **is similar to a limb** severed **from the living and is** nevertheless **permitted,** the permissibility of milk is a novelty of the Torah. **And consequently** it might enter your mind to say that **even** the milk **of a non-kosher animalshould be permitted** in line with that novelty. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that it is forbidden.

There is a second and independent reason milk of a kosher animal is a *chiddush*. Since normally nothing that comes from a living animal is permitted — *leika midi de'ati michay ve'shari*, there is nothing that issues from a living creature and is permitted — one might have thought that milk, which comes from a live animal, should be treated as a form of *ever min hachai* or at least *basar min hachai*. The Gemara clarifies that it is neither: it is not a limb (*ever*) and it is not flesh (*basar*). Yet the Gemara still frames the permissibility of milk as surprising: since there is truly nothing produced by a living animal that is permitted, I might have placed milk roughly in that category and said *vehai chalav ke'ever min hachay hu* — and therefore it should be forbidden. Once the Torah tells me that milk of a kosher animal is permitted, I might extend that reasoning and say non-kosher animals' milk is equally permitted. The double *gamal* or the *et* is therefore required to teach otherwise.

The point can be sharpened: milk is a *product* of the animal rather than a *part* of the animal. Unlike the brine or juices of non-kosher meat — which are, in a sense, suffused throughout the flesh — milk is externally produced. It does not taste like the animal. And unlike *eiver min hachai*, milk is not something you sever from the body; it is expressed. All of these factors might have led to a more lenient conclusion, which is precisely why a special verse was necessary to forbid it.

### Redemption Milk Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָלָב דִּבְהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה מְנָלַן דִּשְׁרֵי? אִילֵימָא מִדַּאֲסַר רַחֲמָנָא ״בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב״, הָא לְחוּדֵיהּ שְׁרֵי, וְאֵימָא: חָלָב לְחוּדֵיהּ אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב — בַּהֲנָאָה נָמֵי אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that the milk of a kosher animal is** in fact **permitted? If we say** that it is derived **from** the fact **that the Merciful One prohibits** eating **meat** that was cooked **in milk,** indicating **that** milk **by itself is permitted,** one can reject this proof **and say** that **milk alone is forbidden** only **for consumption but permitted with regard to** deriving **benefit** from it. By contrast, **meat** that was cooked **in milk is forbidden with regard to** deriving **benefit** from it **as well.**

The Gemara then asks the prior question: granted that we need a verse to address non-kosher milk, but how do we know that the milk of a *kosher* animal is even permitted in the first place? The obvious answer — that the Torah forbids *basar b'chalav*, implying each is individually permitted — is rejected: perhaps milk itself is forbidden to eat but merely permitted in benefit, and the prohibition of *basar b'chalav* adds the further dimension of forbidding even benefit when cooked together. The Gemara attempts to derive kosher-milk permissibility from the ruling that milk of a redeemed *korban* is forbidden — if it were already forbidden as general milk, the verse about redeemed sacrifices would be unnecessary — but this too is deflected on the same logic: perhaps normal milk is forbidden for eating but not for benefit, and the *korban* case is stricter.

### Biblical Milk Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִדִּכְתִיב: ״וְדֵי חֲלֵב עִזִּים לְלַחְמְךָ לְלֶחֶם בֵּיתְךָ וְחַיִּים לְנַעֲרוֹתֶיךָ״. וְדִילְמָא לִסְחוֹרָה? אֶלָּא מִדִּכְתִיב ״וְאֵת עֲשֶׂרֶת חֲרִיצֵי הֶחָלָב״. וְדִלְמָא לִסְחוֹרָה? אַטּוּ דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל מִלְחָמָה לִסְחוֹרָה?! וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״אֶרֶץ זָבַת חָלָב וּדְבָשׁ״, וְאִי לָא דִּשְׁרֵי, מִשְׁתַּבַּח לַן קְרָא בְּמִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי? וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״לְכוּ שִׁבְרוּ וֶאֱכֹלוּ, וּלְכוּ שִׁבְרוּ בְּלֹא כֶסֶף וּבְלֹא מְחִיר יַיִן וְחָלָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** proof may be brought that milk is permitted **from** the fact **that it is written: "And there will be goats' milk enough for your food, for the food of your household, and maintenance for your maidens"** (Proverbs 27:27). According to the verse, goats' milk serves as food, and therefore must be kosher. The Gemara rejects the proof: **But perhaps** the verse is referring **to** selling the milk as **merchandise** and using the money to buy food, and not to consuming the milk itself. **Rather,** proof may be brought **from** the fact **that it is written** with regard to Jesse's instructions to his son David upon sending him with provisions for his brothers, who were at war against the Philistines: **"And these ten cheeses** you shall bring to the captain of their thousand" (I Samuel 17:18), which indicates they ate dairy products. The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **But perhaps** this verse too means the captain can sell the cheese **as merchandise.** The Gemara responds: **Is that to say** that it is **the norm during war to** engage in **commerce?** Clearly the cheese was meant for consumption, which proves it is permitted to eat dairy products. **And if you wish, say** instead that proof may be brought **from here:** The Torah praises Eretz Yisrael as: **"A land flowing with milk and honey"** (Exodus 3:17), **and if** milk **was not permitted, would the verse praise** the land **to us with an item that is not suitable** for consumption? **And if you wish, say** instead a proof **from here: "Come, buy, and eat; and come, buy wine and milk without money and without price"** (Isaiah 55:1).

The Gemara ultimately grounds the permissibility of milk in several *Nach* sources. The verse in Mishlei, *vedei chalav izim lelachmecha* — sufficient goats' milk for your bread — is cited. One might deflect: perhaps the goats are milked and the milk is sold, and the proceeds buy bread, rather than the milk being eaten directly. The *pasuk* in Shemuel about David being sent to bring *aseret charitzei hachalav* — ten portions of cheese — to his brothers at the battlefield is then cited: surely one does not bring cheese to sell at a battlefield. The verse *eretz zavat chalav u'devash* is also invoked: the Torah praises the land with its milk and honey; would the Torah laud a land for something that is forbidden? And finally, the verse from Yeshayahu — *lechu shivru ve'ochlu... yayin ve'chalav* — provides direct evidence of milk consumption.

### Non-Kosher Byproducts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״שָׁפָן״ ״שָׁפָן״, ״אַרְנֶבֶת״ ״אַרְנֶבֶת״, ״חֲזִיר״ ״חֲזִיר״, לְהָנֵי הוּא דְּאָתוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis, who interpret the repetition of the word "camel" as indicating the prohibitions against eating a camel born from a cow and consuming its milk, respectively, and asks: **But if that is so,** that the repetition indicates these *halakhot*, then with regard to the words: **"Hare," "hare," "rock badger," "rock badger," "swine," "swine"** (see Leviticus 11:5–7; Deuteronomy 14:7–8), which are all mentioned twice as being forbidden, **do** these words **come to** teach **these** *halakhot* as well?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְכִדְתַנְיָא: לָמָּה נִשְׁנוּ בַּבְּהֵמָה — מִפְּנֵי ״הַשְּׁסוּעָה״, וּבָעוֹפוֹת — מִפְּנֵי ״הָרָאָה״. ״גָּמָל״ ״גָּמָל״ נָמֵי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא! כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא לְמִידְרָשׁ דָּרְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: They are necessary **only for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Why were** all of the non-kosher animals and birds **repeated** in Deuteronomy (14:7–20), after having been mentioned already in Leviticus (11:10–20)? **With regard to the animals,** it is **because of the *shesua*** (see Deuteronomy 14:7), an animal with two backs and two spines, which is not mentioned in Leviticus, **and with regard to the birds,** it is **because of the *ra'a*** (see Deuteronomy 14:13). The Gemara challenges: If so, then the double reference of **"camel," "camel," also comes for this** purpose of teaching about the *shesua*, and not to teach a separate *halakha* about the camel. The Gemara explains: Nevertheless, **anywhere that it is** possible **to interpret** the verse as teaching an additional *halakha*, **we interpret** it in that manner. Only where there is no possibility of such an interpretation is the concept of repeating the passage merely to introduce one additional detail invoked.

Once the permissibility of kosher-animal milk is established, the circle closes. The very fact that milk is permitted — despite being transformed blood, despite resembling a form of *ever min hachai*, despite being a product of the living animal — constitutes the *chiddush* that would lead one to think non-kosher animal milk might similarly be permitted. The double *gamal* of the Rabbis, or the *et hagamal* of Rabbi Shimon, teaches that this permissiveness does not extend to non-kosher animals. *Yotzei min hatamei tamei* applies even to products that bear no resemblance in taste or appearance to the animal from which they come. This is the conclusion of the *sugya*, and the question of bee honey — which is produced by a non-kosher creature and yet is permitted — will be taken up in the continuation.
