# Nazir 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Son Born Before Day 70

### Conditional Nazirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ "הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר לִכְשֶׁיְּהֵא לִי בֵּן" וְ"נָזִיר מֵאָה יוֹם", נוֹלַד לוֹ בֵּן עַד שִׁבְעִים — לֹא הִפְסִיד כְּלוּם. לְאַחַר שִׁבְעִים — סוֹתֵר שִׁבְעִים. שֶׁאֵין תִּגְלַחַת פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of one who said: I am hereby a nazirite when I will have a son, and he added: I am hereby a nazirite from now for one hundred days, and he then began observing the one hundred days of his naziriteship, if a son is born to him up to seventy days from the start of his naziriteship he has not lost anything. He pauses from the observance of the naziriteship of one hundred days and observes the thirty-day term for his son. He then completes the thirty or more days left of his initial naziriteship. However, if his son is born after seventy days, this negates the first seventy days, and he must observe a full hundred days after he completes the naziriteship for his son. The reason is that here, he is unable to merely complete the remaining days of his initial naziriteship after shaving at the completion of the naziriteship for his son, since shaving cannot be performed after a period of less than thirty days.

The Mishnah on this daf continues the themes developed over the preceding days. The case is one of conditional and sequential *nezirut*: a person declares *hareini nazir k'sheyihiyeh li ben* — I will be a *nazir* when I have a son — and simultaneously declares a hundred-day *nezirut*. Because the son's *nezirut* cannot yet take effect (the son has not been born), the person begins observing the hundred-day *nezirut* immediately. When the son is subsequently born, as we learned previously, the person interrupts the *nezirut* he is currently observing and shifts into the *nezirut* vowed for the son, since that vow takes precedence.

### Double Counting Principle

The Mishnah then states: *im ad shivim lo hifsid klum* — if the son is born up to and including day seventy, the person loses nothing. As Tosafot understands this, the operative principle is that if the son's *nezirut* — a thirty-day *nezirut* at minimum — will conclude before or by the end of the hundred-day *nezirut* already underway, and is thus completely contained within it, the person is permitted to double-count the two periods. This is a striking idea: rather than interrupting, pausing, and resuming, the two periods of *nezirut* simply overlap and are counted simultaneously.

### Day 70 Boundary

If, however, the son is born *l'achar shivim* — after day seventy — the Mishnah states *soter shivim*. Tosafot brackets the word *shivim* as not carrying its usual meaning here, reading *soter* not as an outright nullification of the preceding seventy days but as an interruption: since the son's *nezirut* will now extend beyond the hundred-day period and therefore possesses a distinct identity, it creates a suspension. The person must complete the son's *nezirut* and then resume the hundred-day *nezirut* from where he left off.

### Tosafot's Reading

The Mishnah adds the phrase *ein tiglachas pachos mishloshim yom* — there is no shaving in fewer than thirty days. Tosafot explains that the word *tiglachas* (shaving) is not itself the point; what the Mishnah is conveying is that *ein nezirut pachos mishloshim yom* — no *nezirut* is fewer than thirty days. Since the son's *nezirut* is at minimum thirty days, it will inevitably extend beyond the hundred-day period if begun after day seventy, thereby acquiring a distinct identity and triggering the suspension. This requires considerable reading into the Mishnah's language — bracketing *shivim* in one place and reinterpreting *tiglachas* in another — but Tosafot's reading provides the coherent framework within which the subsequent Gemara is discussed.

The fundamental principle that emerges is: if the son's *nezirut* is fully contained within the hundred-day *nezirut*, double-counting is permitted and nothing is lost; if it is not contained, suspension and resumption are required.

## Rav's Double Counting

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: יוֹם שִׁבְעִים עוֹלֶה לְכָאן וּלְכָאן. תְּנַן: נוֹלַד לוֹ עַד שִׁבְעִים — לֹא הִפְסִיד כְּלוּם. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ עוֹלָה לְכָאן וּלְכָאן, אִיתְּגוֹרֵי מִיתְּגַר! אֶלָּא בְּדִין הוּא דְּלָא לִיתְנֵי "עַד שִׁבְעִים", וּמִשּׁוּם דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא "אַחַר שִׁבְעִים סוֹתֵר שִׁבְעִים", קָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא "שִׁבְעִים".
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Rav said: The seventieth day itself counts for here and for there, as the last of the seventy days of his term of naziriteship as well as the first day of the naziriteship for his son. The Gemara questions Rav's statement. We learned in the mishna: If a son is born to him up to seventy, he has not lost anything. This seems to include the seventieth day as well. And if it enters your mind that it counts for here and for there, not only has he not lost anything, but he even gains a day, so why would the mishna state: He has not lost anything? The Gemara answers: Rather, by right the mishna should not have taught: Up to seventy he has not lost anything, since if the son is born on the seventieth day he actually gains, as stated above, but due to the fact that it is taught in the last clause of the mishna: After seventy it negates seventy, in which case he does lose, the *tanna* therefore teaches the first clause with the contrasting expression: Up to seventy he has not lost anything.

The Gemara introduces Rav's position: *yom shivim oleh l'kan ul'kan* — day seventy itself double-counts. If the son is born on day seventy, that day counts simultaneously as the seventieth day of the father's hundred-day *nezirut* and as day one of the son's thirty-day *nezirut*. The son's *nezirut* then concludes on day ninety-nine, and the two periods together account for the full hundred days.

One might ask: what practical difference does this double-counting make? After all, if you simply count from day seventy as day one for the son, the son's *nezirut* still concludes by day ninety-nine, and one still needs to complete the remaining day of the hundred-day period. The Gemara does not spell out the significance explicitly, but Tosafot addresses this directly.

### Bringing Korban Early

Tosafot explains that the double-counting would, in theory, allow the person to bring the *korban* on day one hundred rather than having to wait until day one hundred and one. The logic runs as follows: the person completed seventy days of *nezirut* up through day seventy; starting from that same day, thirty days were counted for the son, concluding on day ninety-nine; the total is therefore seventy plus thirty, equaling one hundred. The hundred-day *nezirut* is complete, and the *korban* may be brought on the following day — day one hundred.

Tosafot immediately flags how strange this is: not only are we permitting double-counting in general, but by virtue of the son's *nezirut* beginning on day seventy, the father somehow gets to count an extra day toward his own hundred. Tosafot resolves this by noting that, in principle, one could always bring a *korban* on the last day itself due to the principle of *miktzas hayom k'kulo* — a partial day counts as a full day, much as a mourner rises from *shiva* on the seventh day. The practical benefit of Rav's position is therefore not that one gains an entirely new day, but that one may bring the *korban* on day one hundred rather than waiting for day one hundred and one. Tosafot notes that this leniency is in any case *derabbanan*.

### Challenge to Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע מִסֵּיפָא: נוֹלַד אַחַר שִׁבְעִים — סוֹתֵר שִׁבְעִים. מַאי "אַחַר"? אַחַר אַחַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: Come and hear a statement that contradicts Rav's opinion from the last clause of the mishna: If the son is born after seventy days, it negates the first seventy days. If, as Rav stated, one day can count for both terms, then the final day of the thirty-day term for his son also counts toward his hundred-day term, meaning that there will be thirty days remaining for a full hair growth; in that case, why should he forfeit the first days? The Gemara answers this: What is the meaning of: After? It means after, after. The mishna is referring to the second day after the seventieth, the seventy-second day, so that there do not remain thirty days for his hair to grow.

The more pointed implication of Rav's position — and the one the Gemara really targets — concerns day seventy-one. If day seventy double-counts, then by the same logic, even if the son is born on day seventy-one, that day should count as day one for the son, and the son's *nezirut* would conclude by day one hundred. If so, the two *neziruyot* would still be coterminous, and the Mishnah's distinction between "up to seventy" and "after seventy" would be inexplicable. Why should day seventy-one be treated any differently than day seventy? That is the question the Gemara now turns to press.

## Refutation via Mishnah

### Gains vs. Loses Nothing

The Gemara challenges Rav from the Mishnah's own language. The Mishnah says that if the son is born by day seventy, *lo hifsid klum* — the person loses nothing. But according to Rav's position, this formulation is not merely accurate — it actually understates the case. If day seventy double-counts and allows the *korban* to be brought a day earlier, then the person has not merely "lost nothing" — he has actually *gained* something. The Gemara captures this with the idiom *itgurei mitgar* — he gains. The Mishnah, by saying only that nothing is lost, implies that there is no gain, which directly contradicts Rav's position.

### 'Achar' Means Day 72

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אַחַר מַמָּשׁ מַאי — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא סָתַר? אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי נוֹלַד עַד שִׁבְעִים לֹא הִפְסִיד כְּלוּם? אֲפִילּוּ אַחַר שִׁבְעִים נָמֵי, הָא אָמְרַתְּ לָא סָתַר! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ "אַחַר" מַמָּשׁ. וְכֵן מַתְנִיתִין לְרַב, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But according to this explanation, what would be the *halakha* if a son is born on the day that actually comes after the seventieth, the seventy-first day; so too, would Rav say that it does not negate the previous days, because he has thirty days remaining to grow his hair? If so, why does the *tanna* specifically teach: If he is born up to seventy he has not lost anything? The same would hold true even for a case where he was born on the day after seventy as well, as didn't you say that it does not negate? Rather, one must learn from this that: After, means the actual day after, the seventy-first day, and likewise conclude that the mishna is difficult for Rav. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, learn from this that the *tanna* of the mishna disagrees with Rav.

The Gemara then turns to the end of the Mishnah: *nolad achar shivim soter* — if born after seventy, it causes a hiatus. According to Rav, even day seventy-one should not be problematic, since counting from day seventy-one as day one, the son's *nezirut* concludes by day one hundred. The Gemara therefore proposes: perhaps *achar shivim* does not mean "after day seventy" (i.e., day seventy-one) but rather "after the after of seventy" — meaning day seventy-two. The Mishnah would then be teaching that up through day seventy-one nothing is lost, and only from day seventy-two onward is there a problem.

But the Gemara immediately rejects this reading: if that were the Mishnah's intent, it should simply have said "up to day seventy-one" rather than "up to day seventy." The precision of the Mishnah's language demonstrates that day seventy-one is genuinely a problem, which means the son's *nezirut* does not begin on the day he is born. It must begin on the following day.

### Day of Birth Conclusion

The Gemara concludes: *achar mamash* — "after seventy" means literally day seventy-one, and day seventy-one is indeed a problem. The son's first day of *nezirut* is not the day of his birth but the day after. Consequently, if born on day seventy-one, his thirty-day *nezirut* begins on day seventy-two and concludes after day one hundred — it is not contained within the hundred-day period, a suspension is triggered, and the Mishnah's ruling is fully coherent. The Gemara thus demonstrates that the Mishnah does not support Rav's principle of *oleh l'kan ul'kan*.

## Partial Day Clarification

### General Principle

This conclusion requires clarification, lest it be misread as a rejection of the general principle that a partial day counts in *nezirut*. Under ordinary circumstances, if a person declares *nezirut* today with only an hour remaining in the day, that hour counts as day one. The partial day suffices. This is not the principle being rejected.

### Why Day 71 Fails

What is being rejected is something more specific: the double-counting of a day that has already begun as part of a different counting. When day seventy-one breaks — at the start of that calendar day — it is already day seventy-one of the person's own hundred-day *nezirut*. The day does not begin as a neutral day; it begins already assigned to a pre-existing *nezirut*. When the son is subsequently born in the middle of that day, the day cannot be reassigned to serve simultaneously as the son's day one. The problem is not that a partial day is insufficient — a partial day is generally sufficient. The problem is specifically *oleh l'kan ul'kan*: double-counting a day that belongs to one *nezirut* so that it also serves as the first day of another. The Mishnah's ruling that day seventy-one creates a problem proves that this kind of double-counting is not permitted.

## Rav & Abba Shaul

### Miktzas Hayom Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב כְּמַאן אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ? אִילֵּימָא כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, דִּתְנַן: הַקּוֹבֵר אֶת מֵתוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים קוֹדֶם לָרֶגֶל — בָּטְלָה מִמֶּנּוּ גְּזֵירַת שִׁבְעָה. שְׁמֹנָה יָמִים קוֹדֶם לָרֶגֶל — בָּטְלָה מִמֶּנּוּ גְּזֵירַת שְׁלֹשִׁים. וּמוּתָּר לְסַפֵּר עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל, וְאִם לֹא סִיפֵּר עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל — אָסוּר לְסַפֵּר אַחַר הָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And Rav, in accordance with whose opinion did he say his *halakha*? Since the *tanna* of the mishna disagrees with him, which *tanna* does he follow in ruling that one day may be counted for two different observances? If we say that he stated the ruling in accordance with the opinion of Abba Shaul, as we learned in the *Tosefta* (*Mo'ed Katan* 2:9): With regard to one who buries his dead three days before a pilgrimage Festival, the rabbinic decree of seven days of mourning is voided for him, i.e., once the Festival begins, he no longer observes the prohibitions and customs of the first seven days of mourning. Since he has mourned for three days, the Festival voids the remainder of the seven days. The *baraita* continues: If he buried his dead eight days before a pilgrimage Festival, the rabbinic decree of the restrictions of thirty days of mourning is voided for him as well. Since he already observed one day of this type of mourning he need not complete the entire period, and it is therefore permitted for him to cut his hair on the eve of the pilgrimage Festival in honor of the Festival. But if he did not cut his hair on the eve of the pilgrimage Festival, it is prohibited for him to cut his hair after the pilgrimage Festival, until thirty days of mourning have passed.

Having demonstrated that the Mishnah does not support Rav's view, the Gemara asks: *v'Rav keman amar l'shmaisa* — according to whom does Rav hold? The Gemara suggests: *eima k'Abba Shaul* — perhaps Rav follows the view of Abba Shaul. This introduces the principle of *miktzas hayom k'kulo* — a partial day counts as a whole — in its most familiar context: the laws of *aveilus*.

### Moed Katan Case

The Gemara cites the Mishnah from Moed Katan. One who buries a close relative fewer than three days before *Yom Tov* — the arrival of the festival does not end the *shiva*. The festival can only interrupt *shiva* if the mourner has observed at minimum three days. But if the burial occurred eight days before *Yom Tov*, such that *erev Yom Tov* already falls within the *shloshim* period rather than the *shiva* period, then the festival ends the *shloshim*. Furthermore, in that case, because *shloshim* will be interrupted by *Yom Tov*, the person is permitted to take a haircut on *erev Yom Tov* in deference to *kevod ha-regel*. However — and here the Mishnah adds an important caveat — *v'im lo siper b'erev ha-regel, asur l'saper achar ha-regel*: if the person did not actually take the haircut before *Yom Tov*, the interruption is deemed not to have occurred and the *shloshim* must be completed after the festival.

This Mishnah is not the ruling we follow, but it provides the framework within which Abba Shaul's view is presented.

### Abba Shaul's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא סִיפֵּר קוֹדֶם הָרֶגֶל — מוּתָּר לְסַפֵּר אַחַר הָרֶגֶל, שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּצְוַת שְׁלֹשָׁה מְבַטֶּלֶת גְּזֵירַת שִׁבְעָה — כָּךְ מִצְוַת שִׁבְעָה מְבַטֶּלֶת גְּזֵירַת שְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** Abba Shaul says: Even if he did not cut his hair before the pilgrimage Festival, it is permitted for him to cut his hair after the pilgrimage Festival. His reasoning is that just as the mitzva of three voids the rabbinic decree of seven, as was taught before; so the mitzva of seven voids the rabbinic decree of thirty. Since he completed the observance of the seven days of mourning before the Festival, he need not observe the thirty days of mourning.

Abba Shaul disagrees on two points. First, he maintains that even if the mourner did not take the haircut before *Yom Tov*, the festival still interrupts — *mutar l'saper achar ha-regel*. The haircut is not a condition for the interruption. Second, and more fundamentally, Abba Shaul holds that a mourner need not wait until *the eighth day* to be considered in *shloshim* at the onset of *Yom Tov*; it suffices to have completed *seven days*. His ruling is expressed as a parallelism: just as the obligation of three days terminates *shiva* when the festival arrives, so the obligation of seven days terminates *shloshim* when the festival arrives. The key question is what Abba Shaul means by "seven days."

### Parallel Principles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר שְׁבִיעִי עוֹלֶה לְכָאן וּלְכָאן? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אֶלָּא בַּאֲבֵילוּת שִׁבְעָה דְּרַבָּנַן, אֲבָל בְּנָזִיר דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What is the rationale of Abba Shaul for his opinion that if he observed seven days of mourning before the Festival commenced the thirty-day mourning period is voided? Isn't it because he holds that the seventh day counts for here and there, i.e., the seventh day is considered both the end of the seven days and the start of the thirty days of mourning, so he had already begun observing his thirty days of mourning before the start of the Festival? This ruling may be the basis for the ruling of Rav. The Gemara rejects this: This does not support Rav, since perhaps Abba Shaul was saying his ruling that the same day counts for both here and there only with regard to the mourning of seven days, which is by rabbinic law, but with regard to a nazirite, whose obligation is by Torah law, he would not say so.

The answer lies in the principle of *miktzas hayom k'kulo*. On the morning of the seventh day of *shiva*, the mourner rises — that partial day counts as the completion of the seventh day. From that morning onward, the mourner is no longer in *shiva*; he has already entered the *shloshim* period. When *Yom Tov* then arrives the following day, it encounters a mourner who is observing *shloshim*, not *shiva*, and the festival is therefore *mevatel* the *shloshim*. Abba Shaul's principle is thus precisely analogous to Rav's: day seven double-counts — it is simultaneously the last day of *shiva* and, from the morning onward, the first day of *shloshim*. The seventh day is *oleh l'kan ul'kan*. This is exactly the structure of Rav's position regarding the son's *nezirut*: the day functions as the conclusion of one period and simultaneously as the beginning of another.

## Rabbinic vs. Torah

### Rabbinic Extension

Despite the conceptual elegance of the parallel, the Gemara raises an objection. *Dilma* — perhaps Abba Shaul's principle of double-counting applies only *b'aveilus shiva derabbanan* — in the context of *aveilus*, which is rabbinic (at least from the second day onward, and certainly with respect to the *shloshim* period and the festival's power to interrupt it). In rabbinic law there may be greater flexibility to permit such double-counting. But *b'nazir de'oraita* — in the context of *nezirut*, which is a Torah-level obligation — who says the same leniency would apply? The parallelism is intellectually compelling but legally inconclusive: one cannot automatically extend a principle developed in the context of a rabbinic institution to a biblical one. The Gemara therefore sets aside Abba Shaul and looks for a *tanna* who applies the equivalent of *miktzas hayom k'kulo* in a double-counting sense within a biblical context.

### Rabbi Yosi's Zava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַב דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וְזָרְקוּ עָלֶיהָ בַּשֵּׁנִי שֶׁלָּהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאֲתָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת, וּפְטוּרָה מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rav spoke in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, as it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yosei says: A woman who observes a clean day for each day she experiences a discharge is a woman who discharges blood for one or two days at a time when she does not expect her menstrual period. The case under discussion is one where she experienced a discharge for one day and they slaughtered a Paschal offering and sprinkled the blood for her on her second day, after she immersed in a ritual bath. At that point, it is unclear whether she will remain clean of discharges for the remainder of the day, in which case she is retroactively pure from the time she immersed and may eat the Paschal offering at night, or whether she will experience a discharge of blood during the day, in which case her immersion is retroactively invalid and she was impure the entire time. And after that, she saw blood, thereby retroactively clarifying that at the time the Paschal offering was slaughtered she was unfit to partake of it. The *halakha* is that she may not eat from the Paschal lamb due to her ritual impurity, but she is exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ*, which is observed by those who did not sacrifice the Paschal offering on the first Pesaḥ. The reason is that since she was pure when they slaughtered the Paschal offering on her behalf, she has fulfilled the obligation of the offering, despite the fact that she became impure and was unable to eat the offering.

The Gemara proposes: *ella Rav d'amar k'Rabbi Yosi* — Rav holds in accordance with Rabbi Yosi. The relevant *baraita* concerns the laws of *zivah*. The Gemara will now develop Rabbi Yosi's position in detail through the case of the *shomeret yom keneged yom*.

## Rabbi Yosi Zava Case

### Zava Setup

When a woman bleeds outside her normal menstrual cycle, she is governed by the laws of *zivah*. If she bleeds on a single day, the *halacha* requires her to observe the following day in *tahara* — she becomes a *shomeret yom keneged yom*, observing one clean day corresponding to the day she saw blood. If she observes that day without any bleeding, she immerses in the *mikvah* and becomes *tehora*.

The case Rabbi Yosi addresses is as follows. A woman saw blood on Sunday and is therefore *shomeret yom* on Monday — she must observe Monday in *tahara*. It happens that Monday is *erev Pesach*. The hope is that Monday will pass without bleeding, she will immerse in the *mikvah* during the day (relying on *miktzas hayom k'kulo*), and she will be *tehora* in time to partake of the *korban Pesach* that evening. Because of this expectation, they go ahead and bring a *korban Pesach* on her behalf on Monday afternoon, at which time she has not yet bled and is *tehora* — having already immersed in the *mikvah* earlier in the day.

### Retroactivity Problem

At 3:00 PM on Monday — after the *korban Pesach* has been slaughtered and its blood sprinkled on her behalf — the woman unfortunately bleeds again. She will clearly be unable to eat the *korban Pesach* that evening, as she is now *teme'ah*. The more serious question is whether the *korban Pesach* itself counts. The natural analysis would be: the only reason she was permitted to immerse in the *mikvah* mid-day was because we applied *miktzas hayom k'kulo* — we treated the partial day as though the remainder of the day had already passed cleanly. If it turns out that by the end of the day she was not clean, then retroactively we must say she was *teme'ah* all along, the *mikvah* immersion was invalid, and the *korban Pesach* did not count. This is the problematic implication of understanding *miktzas hayom* as working in anticipation of the rest of the day.

### Rabbi Yosi's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ? מִמַּאי? וְדִלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this: What is the rationale of Rabbi Yosei for his opinion that she is exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ*? Isn't it because he holds that the legal status of part of the day is like that of an entire day? Since she was pure for part of the day, it is considered as though she was pure the entire day. The Gemara rejects this: From where do you know that this is the rationale? Perhaps it is because Rabbi Yosei holds that one becomes impure from now and onward. He holds that the impurity of a *zava* starts only from the moment she had a discharge of blood, but she is ritually pure up to that point, so she was ritually pure when they slaughtered the offering for her.

Rabbi Yosi rules otherwise. The *korban Pesach* counts — she is *petur* from *Pesach Sheni* — because she was genuinely *tehora* when it was brought. The bleeding at 3:00 PM is a new *tumah*, not a retroactive disqualification of the *tahara* she enjoyed earlier. This ruling reflects a fundamentally different conception of *miktzas hayom*: not that the partial day works because we fold the remainder of the day into it, but rather that a partial day is *sufficient in itself*. The woman's *tahara* from the morning until the time of the *korban* is real and complete. What happens afterward is independent. This is directly analogous to Rav's principle: if a partial day is sufficient in itself, then the first hours of a day can count for one purpose and the remaining hours for an entirely different one.

## Two Models Miktzas

### Day 71 Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: זָב בַּעַל שְׁתֵּי רְאִיּוֹת שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וְזָרְקוּ עָלָיו בַּשְּׁבִיעִי, וְכֵן שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וְזָרְקוּ עָלֶיהָ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאוּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּטַמְּאִין מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב לְמַפְרֵעַ — פְּטוּרִין מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** But isn't it taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yosei says: With regard to a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*] who has had two sightings of discharge, for whom they slaughtered a Paschal offering and sprinkled its blood on the seventh, and final, day of impurity, and similarly, with regard to a woman who observes a clean day for a day, for whom they slaughtered a Paschal offering and sprinkled its blood, and afterward they saw their respective discharges, although they render objects designed for lying and sitting impure retroactively, they are exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ*. This is proof that Rabbi Yosei holds that their ritual impurity applies retroactively, rather than from the moment of discharge onward. It must be that the reason why they are nevertheless exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ* is that part of the day is like the entire day, and the part of the day before they became impure, during which the blood of the Paschal offering was sprinkled for them, is considered a whole clean day.

The Gemara now makes explicit the connection between Rabbi Yosi's ruling and Rav's position in *nezirut*. The structural parallel is as follows. On day seventy-one, the son is born at 10:00 AM. According to Rav's principle of *oleh l'kan ul'kan*, the early hours of that day count toward the father's hundred-day *nezirut*, and the hours following the son's birth count as day one of the son's *nezirut*. How can a single day be split between two different *neziruyot*? Only if *miktzas hayom* means that a partial period is independently sufficient — not that the partial period works because we project the remainder of the day.

### Sufficiency Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לְמַפְרֵעַ — מִדְּרַבָּנַן. הָכִי נָמֵי מִיסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אַמַּאי פְּטוּרִין מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי? לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ טוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, תְּהוֹם דְּזִיבָה הִתִּירוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds to this attempted proof: What is the meaning of Rabbi Yosei's statement that the ritual impurity applies retroactively? It means that the ritual impurity applies retroactively by rabbinic law. However, by Torah law, the *zav* or *zava* is impure only from the time of the new sighting and onward. The Gemara adds: So too, it is reasonable that this is Rabbi Yosei's opinion, as, if it enters your mind that they become ritually impure retroactively by Torah law, why are they exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ* if by Torah law they were impure at the time the offering was slaughtered? The Gemara responds: This is no proof, since actually, I could say to you that this retroactive impurity is by Torah law, and the reason they are exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ* is that the Sages permitted impurity of the deep of *ziva*. The *halakha* is that in the case of a Paschal offering, an unknown impurity of the dead, called the impurity of the deep, is permitted. In a case where one brought his offering and later became aware that he had contracted impurity from a corpse, if this source of impurity was unknown to everyone, he is not required to observe the second *Pesaḥ*. The Gemara suggests that Rabbi Yosei may hold that the same *halakha* applies to the impurity of a *zava* as well: Since the owner of the offering was pure when it was brought and could not have known that she would become impure due to discharge later that day, it is similarly considered an impurity of the deep, and she has therefore fulfilled her obligation.

This yields Model Two: *miktzas hayom* means a partial day is sufficient in itself. Under this model, what happens later in the day is entirely separate from and independent of what occurred earlier. The woman immerses in the *mikvah* mid-day: the hours of *tahara* up to that point constitute a complete, self-contained unit. The bleeding at 3:00 PM is a new *tumah* because the day is not a monolithic unit — it can be divided, with earlier portions standing on their own. Similarly, the first hours of day seventy-one belong to the father's *nezirut*, and the later hours, from the moment of the son's birth, begin a new and independent counting for the son. Neither half requires the other to be valid.

### Anticipation Model

Model One operates entirely differently: a partial day counts only in anticipation that the rest of the day will unfold as expected. Under this model, the *mikvah* immersion mid-Monday works only because we are projecting forward and treating the whole of Monday as already having passed without incident. If bleeding occurs later in the day, it retroactively reveals that this anticipatory projection was incorrect, and the *tahara* of the morning is undone. By the same logic, under Model One, if day seventy-one begins as day seventy-one of the father's *nezirut*, the entire day belongs to that *nezirut* — we cannot split it, because the day was already "claimed" from the moment it began. The son's *nezirut* cannot begin until the following day, which is why day seventy-one creates a problem in the Mishnah.

## Retroactive Impurity

### Retroactivity Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא סָבַר לְמַפְרֵעַ מִדְּרַבָּנַן, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: (אֲבָל) הָרוֹאֶה זָב בַּשְּׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ — סוֹתֵר אֶת שֶׁלְּפָנָיו. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא נִסְתּוֹר אֶלָּא יוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: And even Rabbi Oshaya holds that, according to Rabbi Yosei, the fact that a *zav* renders objects designed for sitting or lying impure retroactively is only by rabbinic law in this circumstance. As it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Oshaya says: In a case of one who sees gonorrhea-like discharge on his seventh clean day, this negates the clean days that preceded it and starts his seven days anew. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him that it should negate only its own day, i.e., he should require only one additional clean day.

The Gemara now examines Rabbi Yosi's position more carefully, because a complication arises. A different *baraita* appears to attribute to Rabbi Yosi the view that when the *zava* bleeds later in the day, she is retroactively *teme'ah* — and that if she sat on an object earlier in the day (even after her *mikvah* immersion), that object is rendered *tamei*. This seems to contradict the ruling that the *korban Pesach* counts: if she was retroactively *teme'ah* the whole time, how can the *korban* have been brought validly?

The Gemara's first resolution: *le'mafrea miderabbanan* — the retroactive *tumah* is only rabbinic. Biblically, her *tahara* at the time of the *korban* was real, and the later bleeding constitutes a new *tumah*. Rabbinically, we treat the bleeding as retroactively disqualifying the day, which is why objects she sat upon are rendered *tamei* — but this is a rabbinic stringency overlaid on a biblical status of *tahara*. The *korban Pesach*, which operates at the biblical level, is therefore unaffected.

### Special Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי סָתַר, כּוּלְּהוּ סָתַר. אִי לָא סָתַר — לָא נִסְתּוֹר וְלֹא יוֹמוֹ! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: לָא נִסְתּוֹר וְלָא יוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: Whichever way you look at Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, it is difficult. If this discharge negates clean days just as the case of a *zav* who had a discharge during his seven days, it should negate all his clean days, not only the last one. If it does not negate clean days, since it is not considered a discharge within his seven days but rather, as the first sighting of a new *ziva*, it should not negate any of it, and not even its own day. What, then, is the logic of Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling that it negates a single day? Rather, say instead that Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows: It does not negate at all, and not even its own day, since this discharge is considered to be the first of a new series.

The Gemara offers an alternative resolution: *le'olam eima lach* — perhaps Rabbi Yosi genuinely holds that the retroactive *tumah* is biblical. Even so, the *korban Pesach* counts for a separate reason.

### Tumah of Depths

The principle at work is *tumas tehom* — the *tumah* of the depths, meaning an *tumah* that was unknowable at the time the offering was brought. The classical example is a person who brings a *korban Pesach* while unknowingly standing above a buried grave, rendering him biblically *tamei* — yet because the *tumah* was entirely hidden and unknowable to all parties at the time, the *korban* counts. The Gemara applies the same logic here: when the *korban Pesach* was brought for this woman, she had observed part of the day in *tahara*, immersed in the *mikvah*, and all evidence indicated she was *tehora*. The subsequent bleeding was not foreseeable or knowable at that moment. This constitutes a *tumah* of the depths with respect to *zivah*, and the *korban* therefore counts despite the fact that she was, retroactively, biblically *teme'ah*. Under this second approach, Rabbi Yosi does not rely on the *sufficiency model* of *miktzas hayom*, and consequently cannot serve as support for Rav's position.

## Rabbi Yoshiya View

### Zav Day 7 Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, מִכְּדִי סָבַר מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ, זָבָה גְּמוּרָה דְּמַיְיתָא קׇרְבָּן הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לָהּ? כֵּיוָן דְּחָזְיָא בְּפַלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא, אִידַּךְ פַּלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא סָלֵיק לַהּ לְשִׁימּוּר! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא דְּקָא שָׁפְעָה תְּלָתָא תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּחָזְיָא תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה, דְּלָא הֲוַאי שְׁהוּת דְּסָלֵיק לֵיהּ לְמִנְיָינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question with regard to Rabbi Yosei's statement: And as to Rabbi Yosei, now, he holds that the status of part of the day is like that of an entire day. How can you find a full-fledged *zava* who brings an offering? Since she sees a discharge at the midpoint of the day, the other half of the day counts for her as a full day of observing, as part of the day is considered to be like all of a day. The Gemara answers: If you wish, say that she continuously discharges blood for three days consecutively. And if you wish, say that she saw a discharge on three consecutive days close to sunset. In that case she is impure on each occasion at the end of one day and the beginning of the next, so that there was not any time to become pure on the following day so as to be included for her counting of a clean day corresponding to an impure one.

The Gemara continues by citing Rabbi Yoshiya's view in a *baraita*: a *zav* — a man who has experienced two or more abnormal seminal discharges — must count seven clean days before immersing in the *mikvah* and becoming *tahor*. Rabbi Yoshiya rules: if on day seven the *zav* has another discharge, *soter et shelefanav* — he loses not merely that day but all seven preceding days and must begin the count again from scratch. This is the *anticipation model* operating to its most extreme conclusion: the partial day seven was counted only in anticipation of the rest of the day passing cleanly; if a discharge occurs, retroactively the entire preceding period is undermined.

### Yochanan Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא נִסְתּוֹר אֶלָּא יוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: It should negate only its own day.

Rabbi Yochanan, upon hearing Rabbi Yoshiya teach this, responded: *lo nistar ela yomo* — he loses only that day, not the full seven. The discharge on day seven does not retroactively destroy the days that preceded it.

### Logical Problem

The Gemara immediately presses: *mah nafshach* — either way you look at it, Rabbi Yochanan's position is logically unstable. If we apply the *anticipation model* — if the partial day seven counted only because we projected the rest of the day passing cleanly — then when the discharge occurs, *kulu sater*: all seven days should be lost, because day seven itself is retroactively disqualified, and the count cannot be complete without it. If, on the other hand, we apply the *sufficiency model* — if the partial day seven counted independently of what happens later — then *lo nistar ela yomo* doesn't even go far enough: the discharge on day seven shouldn't even cost him *one* day, because a single *re'iyah* on its own does not destroy a day of *tahara* for a *zav*. How then can Rabbi Yochanan say he loses exactly one day?

## Yochanan Support

### No Retroactive Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא סָבַר לְמַפְרֵעַ מִדְּרַבָּנַן, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: (אֲבָל) הָרוֹאֶה זָב בַּשְּׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ — סוֹתֵר אֶת שֶׁלְּפָנָיו. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא נִסְתּוֹר אֶלָּא יוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: And even Rabbi Oshaya holds that, according to Rabbi Yosei, the fact that a *zav* renders objects designed for sitting or lying impure retroactively is only by rabbinic law in this circumstance. As it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Oshaya says: In a case of one who sees gonorrhea-like discharge on his seventh clean day, this negates the clean days that preceded it and starts his seven days anew. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: It should negate only its own day.

The Gemara resolves this by clarifying what Rabbi Yochanan actually means. The formulation *lo nistar ela yomo* should be read as: there is no disruption at all beyond that day itself — meaning the discharge later on day seven has no retroactive effect whatsoever on the days that preceded it. The man immersed in the *mikvah* in the morning of day seven; the later discharge does not undo that immersion or undermine the previous six days. He is *tahor* from the *mikvah* forward; the discharge constitutes a new *re'iyah* from this point onward.

### From Now Onward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי קָאֵי כְּוָתָיךְ, דְּאָמַר מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא מְטַמֵּא. וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְמַפְרֵעַ הוּא דְּאָמַר! מַאי לְמַפְרֵעַ — מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabbi Oshaya said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: Rabbi Yosei holds in accordance with your opinion, as he too says that in this case one becomes impure from now and onward, and any impurity after the start of the seventh day does not cause him to forfeit the previous clean days. The Gemara analyzes Rabbi Oshaya's statement: But didn't Rabbi Yosei say that the impurity is retroactive, as stated above? Rather, Rabbi Oshaya must certainly hold as follows: What is the meaning of Rabbi Yosei's ruling that he is impure retroactively? It means that he is rendered impure only by rabbinic law, but not by Torah law.

This is precisely the principle of *mikan ul'haba metamei* — from now onward he is *tamei*, not retroactively. Rabbi Yochanan's position aligns entirely with the *sufficiency model* of *miktzas hayom*: the partial day is complete in itself, and what happens later is independent. The *zav* who went to the *mikvah* on the morning of day seven is genuinely *tahor* at that moment. The later discharge begins a new count of *zivah* and does not reach backward to disqualify what was already established.

### Oshaya Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, מִכְּדִי סָבַר מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ, זָבָה גְּמוּרָה דְּמַיְיתָא קׇרְבָּן הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לָהּ? כֵּיוָן דְּחָזְיָא בְּפַלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא, אִידַּךְ פַּלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא סָלֵיק לַהּ לְשִׁימּוּר! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא דְּקָא שָׁפְעָה תְּלָתָא תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּחָזְיָא תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה, דְּלָא הֲוַאי שְׁהוּת דְּסָלֵיק לֵיהּ לְמִנְיָינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question with regard to Rabbi Yosei's statement: And as to Rabbi Yosei, now, he holds that the status of part of the day is like that of an entire day. How can you find a full-fledged *zava* who brings an offering? Since she sees a discharge at the midpoint of the day, the other half of the day counts for her as a full day of observing, as part of the day is considered to be like all of a day. The Gemara answers: If you wish, say that she continuously discharges blood for three days consecutively. And if you wish, say that she saw a discharge on three consecutive days close to sunset. In that case she is impure on each occasion at the end of one day and the beginning of the next, so that there was not any time to become pure on the following day so as to be included for her counting of a clean day corresponding to an impure one.

Rabbi Oshaya draws the explicit conclusion: *Reb Yosi kai ke-vathakh* — Rabbi Yosi stands together with Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Yosi's ruling that the *korban Pesach* counts for the *shomeres yom* who bleeds later in the day is grounded in the same *sufficiency model* of *miktzas hayom*: the earlier hours stand on their own, the later bleeding is *mikan ul'haba*, and the two portions of the day are independent. The Gemara's note that Rabbi Yosi also says the retroactive *tumah* applies — *ve-ha Reb Yosi le-mafrea hu de-amar* — is resolved, as before, by limiting that ruling to the rabbinic level: *u-mai le-mafrea miderabbanan*. Biblically, Rabbi Yosi holds with Rabbi Yochanan that *miktzas hayom* is sufficient in itself, and the later events do not reflect back on the earlier portion.

## Full Zava Problem

### Consecutive Days Problem

A final difficulty arises from Rabbi Yosi's *sufficiency model* of *miktzas hayom*. A woman becomes a *zavah gemurah* — a full *zava* requiring seven clean days and a *korban* — only by bleeding on three consecutive days. Under the normal understanding, "three consecutive days" means she bled on Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday without interruption. But under Rabbi Yosi's model, since a partial period at the start of any day is independently sufficient as a unit of *tahara*, it would seem that any day on which she does not bleed *at the very beginning* would constitute a break — a *shomeret yom keneged yom* interlude — that resets the count. The question is therefore: how does a woman ever accumulate three truly consecutive days of *zivah* bleeding under Rabbi Yosi's framework? Every day that begins without bleeding would, it seems, be counted as a clean unit and interrupt the sequence.

### Continuous Bleeding

The Gemara offers two answers. The first is direct: *dechazya tlata yomei behadi hadadi* — she bled continuously for three full days without any break whatsoever, not even for a moment. Every minute of all three days involved active bleeding, leaving no opening for *miktzas hayom* to establish a partial unit of *tahara*. This is the straightforward solution, however biologically demanding it may be.

### Sunset Straddling

The second answer is more conceptually precise. The three consecutive days of bleeding need not be entirely continuous; rather, the bleeding straddles *shkiah* — sunset — each day. That is, each day she begins bleeding just before sunset and the bleeding continues past sunset into the next day. Consider the structure: the bleeding begins Sunday just before 6:00 PM and continues past 6:00 PM into Monday; it continues into Monday and again extends past 6:00 PM into Tuesday; and so through Tuesday into the night. Under this pattern, each calendar day *begins* with the bleeding already in progress (since it carried over from the previous evening) and *ends* with the bleeding still ongoing (since it will carry past sunset). There is therefore no window — neither at the beginning of the day nor at its end — that could serve as a clean partial unit.

### Miktzas Hayom Requirement

This second answer clarifies the precise scope of *miktzas hayom*: it requires that either the beginning or the end of the day be clean. A clean period sandwiched in the middle of the day — with bleeding before and bleeding after — does not constitute a valid unit of *tahara* under the *sufficiency model*. The phrase *delo hava shehus lesalukei leminyana* — there is no time that counts toward the clean-day count — captures this precisely. *Miktzas hayom k'kulo*, in its most sophisticated form, means that as long as a day genuinely opens or closes with a period of *tahara*, that period stands independently and is sufficient. But a clean interlude embedded within a day that begins and ends in *tumah* cannot serve this function.

With this, the Gemara concludes its analysis of the interlocking themes of *nezirut*, *miktzas hayom*, and *zivah* that run through daf 15. *Hadran alach hareini nazir*.
