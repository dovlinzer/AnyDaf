# Bava Batra 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Storekeeper Jug Liability

### Mishnah: Child Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ אֶת בְּנוֹ אֵצֶל חֶנְווֹנִי וּפוֹנְדְּיוֹן בְּיָדוֹ, וּמָדַד לוֹ בְּאִיסָּר שֶׁמֶן וְנָתַן לוֹ אֶת הָאִיסָּר; שָׁבַר אֶת הַצְּלוֹחִית וְאִבֵּד אֶת הָאִיסָּר – חֶנְווֹנִי חַיָּיב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן שְׁלָחוֹ. וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַצְּלוֹחִית בְּיַד הַתִּינוֹק וּמָדַד חֶנְווֹנִי לְתוֹכָהּ – שֶׁחֶנְווֹנִי פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who sends his son to a storekeeper with a *pundeyon*,** a coin worth two *issar*, **in his hand, and** the storekeeper **measured oil for him for** one ***issar* and gave him the** second ***issar*** as change, and the son **broke the jug and lost the *issar*, the storekeeper** must compensate the father, as he gave the jug and coin to one who is not halakhically competent. **Rabbi Yehuda exempts** him from liability, **as** he holds that the father **sent** his son **in order to** do **this,** i.e., to bring back the jug and coin. **And the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Yehuda** with regard to a case **when the jug is in the hand of the child and** the **storekeeper measured** the oil **into it that** the **storekeeper** is **exempt** if the child breaks the jug.

The daf opens by resuming from the final line of 87b, which sets up the *mishnah*'s central debate. When a father sends his minor son to a storekeeper to purchase oil, the storekeeper sends back the oil in a jug along with change — and then everything is lost or broken. According to the *Chachamim*, liability falls on the storekeeper. Since the child lacks the legal capacity to effect a *kinyan*, the transaction was never completed from the storekeeper's perspective; sending the child should be understood merely as placing an order, leaving the storekeeper responsible for ensuring safe delivery to the father. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: the father's act of sending the child implicitly authorized that mode of delivery, and the father thereby assumed the risk.

### Jug Ownership Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּאִיסָּר וָשֶׁמֶן, בְּהָא פְּלִיגִי – דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לְאוֹדוֹעֵי שַׁדְּרֵיהּ, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: לְשַׁדּוֹרֵי לֵיהּ שַׁדְּרֵיהּ; אֶלָּא שָׁבַר צְלוֹחִית – אֲבֵדָה מִדַּעַת הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Granted, with regard to** the ***issar* and** the **oil,** one can explain that **they disagree over this** matter: **As the Rabbis hold** that the father **sent** his son **to inform** the storekeeper that he needed oil but did not intend for the storekeeper to send the oil with the boy. For this reason, if the storekeeper gave the child the oil he is liable for its loss. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **he sent** his son so that the storekeeper would **send him** back with the oil, and therefore the storekeeper is exempt from liability. **But** if the child **broke the jug,** why do the Rabbis hold that the storekeeper is responsible for it? **It is a deliberate loss** on the part of the father, as he entrusted the jug to his young son, who is not responsible enough to care for it.

This framework works cleanly for the oil and the change, but raises a pointed difficulty regarding the jug. If the jug belonged to the father, why should the storekeeper bear liability for it at all? The *Chachamim*'s reasoning — that the *kinyan* never transferred — does not obviously extend to an object the storekeeper never owned.

### Rav Yosef: Storekeeper's Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הָכָא בְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת מוֹכֵר צְלוֹחִיּוֹת עָסְקִינַן, וּכְגוֹן שֶׁנְּטָלָהּ חֶנְווֹנִי עַל מְנָת לְבַקְּרָהּ; וּכְדִשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַנּוֹטֵל כְּלִי מִן הָאוּמָּן עַל מְנָת לְבַקְּרוֹ, וְנֶאֱנַס בְּיָדוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hoshaya said: Here we are dealing with a proprietor who sells jugs,** and the father sent his son with a jug in case the store owner might want to buy it. **And** this is a case **where the storekeeper took** the jug **in order to examine it, and** the ruling is **in accordance with** a statement **of Shmuel. As Shmuel says:** With regard to **one who takes a vessel from a craftsman in order to examine it** and buy it if he chooses, **and an accident occurred** while it was **in his possession** and it broke, he is **liable** to pay restitution for the vessel. He has the halakhic status of a borrower, and therefore he bears financial responsibility for the loss.

The best answer offered thus far is that of Rav and Rav Yosef, who maintain that the jug too belonged to the storekeeper. On this reading, the jug, oil, and change are all the storekeeper's property, and the child was simply the vehicle of delivery. The *mishnah*'s concluding clause — that if the jug is in the child's hand the storekeeper is exempt — would then be read simply as: if it is the father's jug, the storekeeper is exempt on that jug.

### Gemara Rejects Simple Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל תַּנָּאֵי הִיא?! אֶלָּא רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הָכָא בְּחֶנְווֹנִי מוֹכֵר צְלוֹחִיּוֹת עָסְקִינַן; וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that the opinion **of Shmuel is** subject to a dispute between ***tanna'im*?** Since Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with the Rabbis, his opinion evidently differs from that of Shmuel. **Rather, Rabba and Rav Yosef both say** that the disagreement in the mishna should be explained as follows: **Here, we are dealing with a storekeeper who sells jugs,** and the father sent his son to buy from him a jug filled with oil. **And Rabbi Yehuda follows his** line of **reasoning,** as explained above, that the father sent his son to bring back the merchandise, **and the Rabbis** follow **their** line of **reasoning,** that the father sent the son to inform the storekeeper what he needed, but not to carry it back.

The *Gemara*, however, declines this straightforward reading of the *mishnah*'s conclusion, preferring instead to maintain that the jug remains the storekeeper's throughout, which creates a different interpretive challenge: if the jug always belongs to the storekeeper, why should the *mishnah*'s emphasis on whether the jug was in the child's hands matter at all? The *Gemara* may simply be exploring alternative frameworks rather than responding to a strictly logical compulsion — and it is worth acknowledging that the simple reading favored by Rav and Rav Yosef remains the more intuitive one.

## Shoel Shelo Midat Model

### Storekeeper Borrows Jug

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא – מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַצְּלוֹחִית בְּיַד הַתִּינוֹק, וּמָדַד חֶנְווֹנִי לְתוֹכָהּ – שֶׁחֶנְווֹנִי פָּטוּר. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ לְאוֹדוֹעֵי שַׁדְּרֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אַבָּיֵי בַּר אָבִין וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר אָבִין דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנְּטָלָהּ לָמוֹד בָּהּ, וְכִדְרַבָּה – דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: הִכִּישָׁהּ – נִתְחַיֵּיב בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, say the last clause: The Rabbis concede to Rabbi Yehuda** in a case **when the jug was in the hand of the child, and the storekeeper measured** the oil **into it, that the storekeeper** is **exempt.** Why would the Rabbis rule that the storekeeper is exempt? **But you said** that the father **sent** his son only **to inform** the storekeeper of his order, but he did not intend for the storekeeper to give anything to his son. **Rather, Abaye bar Avin and Rabbi Ḥanina bar Avin both say** that the disagreement in the mishna should be explained as follows: **With what are we dealing here?** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the storekeeper **took** the jug from the child in order **to measure with it** for him, **and** because he took it in his hands, he is liable in the event of an accident, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba. As Rabba says:** With regard to the obligation to return lost animals, even in a case where the finder is exempt from caring for the animal and returning it to its owner, e.g., if he is an elderly person and it is beneath his dignity, if **he struck** the animal in order to lead it, then **he becomes obligated** to return **it.** Likewise, the Rabbis here maintain that the storekeeper's action renders him liable for the jug.

The *Gemara* therefore introduces a third approach, attributed to Abaye bar Avin and Rabbi Chanina bar Avin, who both say: *hacha b'mai askinan kegon de-natla limod bah* — the case concerns a storekeeper who took the jug without permission in order to use it for measuring out oil, not for the customer in question, but for other customers. The child arrives with the father's jug, and the storekeeper, before filling the order, appropriates the jug for his own commercial purposes. This renders the storekeeper a *shoel shelo midat* — a borrower without permission — and that status creates his liability.

On this reading, the *mishnah*'s final clause becomes coherent: if the storekeeper never removed the jug from the child's hands — never borrowing it for his own use — he is exempt on the jug. But if he took it from the child for his own purposes, he becomes liable as a *shoel shelo midat*.

### Rava: Animal Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַבָּה – בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים, דְּאַנְקְטִינְהוּ נִיגְרָא בָּרָיָיתָא; כִּי הַאי גַּוְנָא מִי אָמַר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: You can **say that Rabba says** his statement **with regard to animals, as** the finder **taught them** to **take steps away,** i.e., he worsened the situation, as he causes them to stray even further from their owner. Nevertheless, in **a case like this,** where the storekeeper took the jug from the child, **did** Rabba **say** that the storekeeper is liable? The storekeeper's action does not make it any more likely that the jug will break.

The *Gemara* introduces a parallel from Rava: *hikeishah ve-chayav bah* — if a person who has no obligation of *hashavat aveidah* (because returning a lost animal would be beneath his dignity) nevertheless strikes the animal to set it moving, he thereby assumes liability for it. The *Gemara* initially suggests that this principle might apply to the storekeeper as well: even if he takes the jug at the owner's behest — using it to measure oil for the very child who brought it — the mere act of taking it into his hands makes him responsible.

The *Gemara* then rejects this application. The logic of Rava's animal case rests on a specific mechanism: *deha ankiteihu nigra brayta* — once the animal has been set in motion, it will no longer remain grazing in the meadow where it was safe. Having disturbed its natural resting place, the person who struck it has created a new vulnerability, and negligence now attaches if he fails to follow through. That is why liability arises even absent a positive obligation of *hashavat aveidah*. But where the storekeeper takes the jug purely to serve the owner's own order — using it for the very customer who brought it — there is no analogous negligence. The *Gemara* therefore asks: *ki hai gavna mi amar* — would Rava's principle really apply in such a case?

### Refining Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אֲנִי וַאֲרִי שֶׁבַּחֲבוּרָה תַּרְגֵּימְנוּהָ – וּמַנּוּ? רַבִּי זֵירָא; הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנְּטָלָהּ לָמוֹד בָּהּ לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: I and the lion of the group explained it. And who is** this great Sage, referred to as a lion? It is **Rabbi Zeira,** and the explanation is as follows: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the storekeeper **took** the jug from the child in order **to measure with it for others,** without the knowledge of the father.

This leads the *Gemara* to distinguish: Rava's principle generating liability without a prior obligation applies specifically because the actor's intervention destabilizes a previously stable situation. Taking an object at the owner's direction, for the owner's benefit, creates no such destabilization and thus no independent liability.

### Rava's Position: Unauthorized Borrowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשׁוֹאֵל שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – מָר סָבַר: שׁוֹאֵל הָוֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: גַּזְלָן הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda **disagree with regard to a borrower** who takes an item **without** the owner's **knowledge.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that the storekeeper **is** considered like any other **borrower** and once he returns the jug to the child, he is no longer responsible for it. **And** one **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that someone who borrows without the owner's knowledge **is a robber** and is obligated to return the item to its owner. Therefore, the storekeeper must pay for the jug that the child broke before it reached the father.

Rava accordingly clarifies — noting that he and Rav Zeira had already worked through this together: *hacha b'mai askinan kegon shentalo limod bo l'acherim* — the storekeeper took the jug without permission to measure out for *other* customers entirely. And the underlying dispute between the *Tanna'im* is precisely *u'v'sho'el shelo mida'at kamifligi* — whether one who borrows without permission is classified as a *shoel* or a *gazlan*.

## Borrower vs. Thief Debate

### Shoel vs. Gazlan

The question now becomes: what practical difference does it make whether the unauthorized borrower is classified as a *shoel* or a *gazlan*? In either case he is liable — a *shoel* bears liability even for *ones* (unavoidable accident). The jug is returned to the child; if it breaks, the storekeeper is liable regardless. So what turns on the classification?

### Return Obligations Differ

The answer lies in the nature of the return obligation. A *shoel* discharges his responsibility by restoring the object to the state from which he took it. He took the jug from the child's hands; returning it to the child's hands is therefore a complete return, even though the child's hands constitute an unprotected place. The *shoel*'s liability is a liability framework — it is not that the object has been alienated from the owner; it is simply that the borrower bears risk. Once the object is returned to the point of departure, the liability ends.

A *gazlan*, by contrast, has effectively dispossessed the owner. The object is no longer in the owner's domain; something has been taken away. Restoration therefore requires more than returning to the point of departure — it requires restoring the object to a genuinely protected state, placing it back under the owner's actual control. Returning to the child, an unprotected intermediary, does not accomplish this.

### Tosafot: Protected Return

Tosafot raises a complication: we have a general principle that *gazlan shelo midat* — someone who stole without the owner's knowledge — may effect return without the owner's knowledge. If I pickpocket someone and later slip the wallet back without their noticing, I have discharged my obligation of return. But — and this is the critical point — that principle applies only when the return is to a *protected* place. The child's hands are not a protected place. Even though the jug was initially taken from an unprotected situation, restoring it to an unprotected situation does not make the *gazlan* whole. That, then, is the operative dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Chachamim* regarding the jug — not a question about who owns it, but about what standard of return the storekeeper must meet after having borrowed it without permission.

## Shmuel: Inspection Model

### Shmuel: Inspection Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַנּוֹטֵל כְּלִי מִן הָאוּמָּן לְבַקְּרוֹ, וְנֶאֱנַס בְּיָדוֹ – חַיָּיב. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הוּא דְּקַיְצִי דְּמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself. Shmuel says:** With regard to **one who takes a vessel from a craftsman** in order **to examine it, and an accident occurred** while it was **in his possession** and it broke, he is **liable** to pay restitution for the vessel. The Gemara explains: **And this statement** applies only in a case **where the monetary** value of the vessel **is fixed,** because he examines the vessel merely to ensure there is nothing wrong with it, and it is assumed that if he finds no defect he will buy it.

Having resolved the *mishnah*'s framework, the *Gemara* turns *gufa* — to examine on its own terms the earlier statement of Shmuel: *hanotel keli min hauman levakaro* — one who takes a vessel from an artisan in order to inspect it becomes liable if an accident befalls it in his hands. The *Gemara*'s interest is in what legal category this creates. Is the inspector a *shoel* — a borrower — who has temporarily assumed the object under his control for his own benefit (assessing whether he wants it)? Or is he already something more like a *lokeach*, a purchaser, whose acquisition is merely conditional on the inspection's outcome?

### Price Question: Lokeach Status

This question has practical stakes beyond the *ones* liability they share. Tosafot and the Rashbam both engage the question, and the *Gemara* itself supplies a clue: *ve-hani mili dekaitzi dameha* — this liability obtains only where the price is fixed. This condition is telling. If the inspector were simply a *shoel* — borrowing the object to determine whether he wishes to buy it — it would be puzzling why a fixed price should be required. The price is irrelevant to a borrower's liability. The need for a fixed price suggests instead that the inspector functions as a *lokeach*: he has effectively purchased the item subject to a right of return if unsatisfied. The fixed price is necessary precisely because a purchase, even a conditional one, requires a determinate price to be operative.

### Butcher's Meat Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּעָל לְבֵי טַבָּחָא, אַגְבַּהּ אַטְמָא דְבִישְׂרָא. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא אַגְבַּהּ, אֲתָא פָּרָשָׁא מִרְמֵא מִינֵּיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יֵימַר, חַיְּיבֵיהּ לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי דְּמָיהּ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הוּא דְּקַיְצִי דְּמָיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who entered a butcher shop and lifted a thigh of meat** to examine it. **While he was lifting it, a horseman came** and **seized** it **from him.** The buyer **came before Rav Yeimar,** who **deemed him liable to pay its monetary** value to the seller, because it was seized from him after he had lifted it. The Gemara again notes: **And this matter,** i.e., *halakha*, applies only in a place **where the monetary** value of the item is **fixed,** and therefore lifting it is equivalent to finalizing the sale.

A concrete illustration follows. A man entered a butcher's shop, lifted a side of meat (*atma de-visra*) to inspect it — checking freshness, fat content, and quality — and while he was holding it up, a horseman came and snatched it away. Rav Yeimar made the inspector liable to pay for it, applying Shmuel's principle directly. The man was doing nothing more than inspecting; he was not test-driving the meat or using it in any meaningful way. Yet the liability attached. And, as the *Gemara* reiterates, this is specifically *dekaitzi dameha* — where the price was already fixed.

## Gourds: Kedush Case

### Gourds at Market

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַיְיתִי קָארֵי לְפוּם נַהֲרָא. אֲתוֹ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, שְׁקוּל קָרָא קָרָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הֲרֵי הֵן מוּקְדָּשִׁין לַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: There was **a certain man who brought pumpkins to** the city of **Pum Nahara. Everyone came** and **took a pumpkin** with the intention of buying it, but had yet to pay for it. The seller was angry, and since he did not know from whom to demand payment, **he said to them:** The pumpkins **are hereby consecrated to Heaven.**

A further case: a man brought gourds to *Pum Nahara* — the mouth of the river, a marketplace — and the entire crowd descended on his stock, each person grabbing a gourd to inspect it. The owner, overwhelmed and unable to reassert control, feared he would never recover his merchandise. In desperation he announced: *harei hen mukdashim la-shamayim* — these are hereby consecrated to Heaven, to the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Rav Kahana: No Kedush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֵין אָדָם מַקְדִּישׁ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הוּא דְּקַיְצִי דְּמַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל לָא קַיְצִי דְּמַיְיהוּ – בִּרְשׁוּת מָרַיְיהוּ קָיְימִי, וְשַׁפִּיר אַקְדֵּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **They came before Rav Kahana** to inquire about the halakhic status of the pumpkins. Rav Kahana **said to them:** This statement has no effect, as **a person cannot consecrate an item that is not his,** and since the buyers had lifted the pumpkins, they no longer belonged to the seller. Once again the Gemara points out: **And this matter,** i.e., *halakha*, applies only in a place **where** the item's **monetary** value is **fixed,** and therefore lifting it is akin to finalizing the sale. **But** if the item's **monetary** value is **not fixed, it remains in the jurisdiction of its owner, and** the seller **did well,** i.e., was successful, in **that he consecrated** it.

When the matter came before Rav Kahana, he ruled: *ein adam makdish davar she-eino shelo* — one cannot consecrate something that does not belong to him. The implication is striking: the gourds were no longer his to consecrate, because the crowd of inspectors had already acquired them in some fashion.

### Tosafot: Konayim Model

Tosafot draws an important inference from Rav Kahana's ruling. If the liability of inspection arose from *shoel* status, the owner could certainly have consecrated the gourds, because one may consecrate an object one owns even while someone else has borrowed it — the *shoel*'s status does not divest the owner of ownership. But Rav Kahana said he could not consecrate. This proves that the inspectors are not *shoelim* but *konim* — they are deemed to have acquired the gourds, however temporarily. The inspection model, at least when a price is fixed, places the inspector in the position of a conditional purchaser, not a borrower.

### Tentative Purchase Logic

This conclusion raises a puzzling logical problem. If the inspectors are temporary *konim* who may nonetheless choose to return the goods, can the owner equally choose to void the transaction on his side? A *kinyan* normally requires the full assent of both parties — a fixed price, an act of acquisition, and *da'as* on both sides. Is the mere act of a stranger lifting a gourd sufficient to constitute the owner's *da'as* to sell? And if both parties retain a right to back out, why could the owner not simply treat his declaration of *hekdesh* as an exercise of that right — backing out of the tentative sale and consecrating the item? The *Gemara* provides a partial resolution: *aval lo kaitzi dameihu birshus mareihu kaimei* — if the price was not fixed, the gourds remain in the owner's domain and his *hekdesh* is valid. But where the price was fixed, Rav Kahana's ruling stands, and the full logic of why the owner cannot simultaneously back out and consecrate remains, as acknowledged, not entirely transparent.

## Chavér Demai Problem

### Mishnah: No Purchase, No Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ יָרָק מִן הַשּׁוּק, וּבֵירַר וְהִנִּיחַ – אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, לֹא קָנָה וְלֹא נִתְחַיֵּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** In the case of **one who** wishes to **buy vegetables from the market** and the seller is an *am ha'aretz*, i.e., one who is unreliable with regard to tithes, in which case the buyer is required to separate tithes after his purchase, **and he selected** superior-quality vegetables from inferior-quality vegetables **and placed** them to the side, as he was considering purchasing them, **even** if he did this **all day,** if he does not decide to buy the vegetables **he does not acquire** them **and does not become obligated in** separating **tithes.**

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* that introduces the question of *terumot u-ma'asrot* into the inspection model. A *chavér* — an observant Jew scrupulous in the separation of tithes — purchases vegetables from an *am ha-aretz*, a seller not trusted to have separated properly. If one buys from such a seller and brings the produce home, one must separate *demai* — the rabbinic tier of uncertain tithes — because the *am ha-aretz* may have failed to do so. The *baraita* states: *ha-lokeach yarak min ha-shuk u-viyeir ve-heniach afilu kol ha-yom kulo lo kanah ve-lo nitchayev be-ma'aser* — one who picks up vegetables in the market, inspects them, and sets them down has not acquired them and incurs no obligation of *ma'aser*, even if he spent the entire day doing this.

### Intent Creates Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּמַר בְּלִבּוֹ לִקְנוֹתוֹ – קָנָה וְנִתְחַיֵּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. לְהַחְזִירוֹ אִי אֶפְשָׁר – שֶׁכְּבָר נִתְחַיֵּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, וּלְעַשְּׂרוֹ אִי אֶפְשָׁר – שֶׁכְּבָר מַפְחִיתָן בְּדָמִים. הָא כֵּיצַד? מְעַשְּׂרוֹ, וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** If **he had determined to buy** the vegetables he selected, **he acquires** them **and becomes obligated in** separating **tithes.** In such a case, **it is not possible** for him to decide to not buy the vegetables and **to return** them to the seller in their present state, **as they have already became obligated in tithes, and,** likewise, **it is not possible to tithe them** and return them to the seller, **as he would then lower their monetary** value. **How** should he proceed? **He** should **tithe** the produce, give the tithes to an appropriate recipient, **and give** the seller **money** in exchange for the **tithes** that he separated from it.

Immediately, however, the *baraita* introduces a contrasting rule: *gamar be-libo liknoso, kanah ve-nitchayev be-ma'aser* — if, at the moment of picking it up, he had formed the definitive intention to purchase it, then he has acquired it and is immediately obligated in *ma'aser*. This is a striking development. The inspector has not yet paid; the seller has not yet accepted; no formal *kinyan* has been completed. Yet intention alone — *g'mar da'as* to purchase — creates both acquisition and its attendant obligations.

The *baraita* then confronts the consequences directly: *le-hachziro ee efshar, shekvar nitchayev be-ma'aser* — he cannot simply hand it back, because he is already obligated to tithe it. *Ve-le'asro ee efshar, shekvar mafchis be-damim* — nor can he tithe it in place and return the diminished produce, because he would be reducing the value of what he returns. The resolution: *maasro ve-noten lo d'mei ma'aser* — he separates the *ma'aser* and pays the seller for that portion he has taken.

Tosafot notes that this is not as complex as it sounds with respect to most tithes. For *demai*, once *ma'aser rishon* is separated, it may be eaten even by a non-*Levi*, since the underlying obligation is uncertain; the *Levi* who wants to claim it must prove it was not already taken — *ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'ayah*. Similarly with *ma'aser ani*. The genuinely difficult cases are *ma'aser sheni* — which is *kadosh* and must be eaten in Jerusalem or redeemed — and *terumas ma'aser* within the *ma'aser rishon*, which may only be consumed by a *Kohen*. For those portions, separation and compensation are the only path.

### Rav Hoshaya: Yarei Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ מִשּׁוּם דְּגָמַר בְּלִבּוֹ לִקְנוֹת, קָנָה וְנִתְחַיֵּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר?! אָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הָכָא בִּירֵא שָׁמַיִם עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן רַב סָפְרָא, דְּקַיֵּים בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ ״וְדֹבֵר אֱמֶת בִּלְבָבוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say** that **because one determined to buy** the produce, even though he says nothing and performs no act of acquisition, **he acquires** the produce **and becomes obligated in tithes? Rav Hoshaya said: Here, we are dealing with** a buyer who is **God-fearing, such as Rav Safra,** who **himself fulfilled** the verse: **"And speaks truth in his heart"** (Psalms 15:2). With regard to business matters Rav Safra considered himself bound by his thoughts at the time of negotiations, even if he did not express them verbally or perform any action (see *Makkot* 24a). A person on the ethical level of Rav Safra acquires the produce and becomes obligated to separate tithes from the moment that he decides to buy it.

At this point the *Gemara* raises a sharp objection: *atu mishum de-gamar be-libo liknoso kanah ve-nitchayev be-ma'aser?* — does the mere fact that he made up his mind to buy it mean he has acquired it? This seems to contradict everything the *Gemara* has labored to establish about the requirements of a *kinyan*. The *Gemara* answers with Rav Hoshaya: *hacha be-yarei shamayim askinan* — the *baraita* is speaking specifically about a *yarei shamayim*, a person of exceptional scrupulousness, one who holds himself to a higher standard than strict law requires.

### Rav Safra Story

The paradigm of such a person is Rav Safra, cited immediately: *kegon Rav Safra de-kayem be-nafsheih ve-doveir emes be-levavo* — he upheld in his own conduct the verse "who speaks truth in his heart." The well-known story: Rav Safra was reciting *Shema* when a merchant approached wishing to sell him something. The merchant, receiving no response, kept raising his price. When Rav Safra finished *Shema*, he said: I had already agreed in my heart to buy it at the original price — I could not interrupt my prayer to say so — and therefore I will pay only that original price. A *yarei shamayim* honors the intent formed in his heart as though it were a completed transaction.

### Rash'bam: Intent as Purchase

The Rashbam articulates the principle directly: *ve-hachi nami gabei mekach, keivan de-yarei shamayim hu, keivan she-gamar be-libo zacha ba-mekach ve-nitchayev ba-mechir, ve-chi machzir ke-chozer u-mocher lo dami* — since this person is a *yarei shamayim*, once he formed his intention to buy, he treats himself as though he has acquired the produce and incurred the price. If he subsequently returns it, he treats this as a resale back to the original seller, and compensates accordingly.

This resolution, however, produces a certain tension with the earlier cases. In those cases — the butcher's meat, the gourds — the *Gemara* appeared to treat inspection-with-intent as acquisition for everyone, not merely for the especially pious. The *Gemara* here seems to be saying that, in principle, a tentative inspection does not constitute acquisition, and one who changes his mind was never truly *koneh*. The liability in cases of *ones* arises specifically when the inspector cannot restore the object to its original state — at that point his conditional purchase is locked in. But if he simply puts it down undamaged, he was retroactively never the owner. The *yarei shamayim* holds himself to a stricter standard: even when he could legally walk away, he honors the intention he formed. It is for such a person that the *baraita*'s *ma'aser* obligation applies.

## Weights & Measures

### Mishnah: Cleaning Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַסִּיטוֹן מְקַנֵּחַ מִדּוֹתָיו אֶחָד לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם. וּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, אֶחָד לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A wholesaler [*hassiton*] must clean his measuring** vessels, which are used for measuring liquids such as oil and wine, **once every thirty days,** because the residue of the liquids sticks to the measure and reduces its capacity. **And a homeowner** who sells his goods must clean his measuring vessels only **once every twelve months. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: The matters are reversed.** In the case of one who is constantly using his vessels for selling merchandise the residue does not adhere to the measuring vessel, and therefore a wholesaler must clean his measures only once a year. But in the case of a homeowner, who does not sell as often, the residue adheres to the measuring vessel; therefore, he must clean them every thirty days.

The *Gemara* now turns to a new *mishnah*, continuing the theme of commercial integrity but shifting from the act of purchasing to the instruments of commerce themselves: weights, measures, and scales. The *mishnah* introduces three categories of seller, each with different cleaning obligations for their measuring vessels.

The *siton* — a wholesaler, as the Rashbam explains, *hu chanvani gadol she-koneh harbeh yachad u-mocher le-chanvanim ketanim*, one who buys in bulk from producers and sells to smaller retailers (the word itself, as Steinsaltz notes, derives from the Greek *sitones*, a commissary for grain) — must clean his measuring vessels once every thirty days. The *ba'al habayit* — the homeowner who sells only occasionally from domestic stock — must clean only once a year. The underlying logic of the *tanna kamma* is that heavier use produces more residue: if I am constantly filling and emptying my liter vessel, residue will accumulate faster and must be cleaned more often. If I barely use it, residue builds up slowly.

The practical stakes are significant: residue lining a measuring vessel reduces its effective capacity. Even without any intent to deceive, the buyer will not receive the full amount purchased. This is the *halakhic* analogue to *eifa va-eifa* — using different standards for buying and selling — but produced by negligence rather than malice.

### Rabban Shimon: Inverse Reasoning

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel reverses the reasoning entirely: *chiluf ha-devarim* — it is the opposite. Where a vessel is used rarely, whatever residue adheres to it clings and hardens, demanding more frequent cleaning. Heavy use, paradoxically, keeps vessels cleaner through the constant flow of material. On this view, the *ba'al habayit* whose vessel sits idle must clean more often, not less.

### Storekeeper Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶנְווֹנִי, מְקַנֵּחַ מִדּוֹתָיו פַּעֲמַיִם בְּשַׁבָּת, וּמְמַחֶה מִשְׁקְלוֹתָיו פַּעַם אַחַת בְּשַׁבָּת, וּמְקַנֵּח מֹאזְנַיִם עַל כׇּל מִשְׁקָל וּמִשְׁקָל. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּלַח, אֲבָל בְּיָבֵשׁ – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **A storekeeper,** who constantly sells merchandise in small quantities, **cleans his measuring** vessels **twice a week and cleans his weights once a week; and he cleans** the pans of **his scales after each and every weighing,** to ensure that no merchandise has adhered to the pans, thereby increasing their weight. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement,** that it is necessary to clean a measuring vessel, **said? With regard to moist** items, which are likely to adhere to the measuring vessels. **But with regard to dry** goods, which do not adhere to the measuring vessels, one **does not need** to clean his measuring vessels.

The *mishnah* then addresses the *chanvani* — the regular storekeeper — who occupies the middle position: he must clean his measuring vessels twice a week, reflecting their constant daily use. He must also wipe down his weights once a week. This is less frequent than the vessel cleaning because the concern with weights is different: not internal residue from repeated filling, but external accumulation — dust, dirt, or debris adhering to the surface that incrementally alters the weight. 

It is worth noting that this works asymmetrically: if the dirt makes a selling weight slightly heavier, the buyer actually benefits, receiving slightly more than purchased. But if it afflicts the purchasing weights, the seller benefits at the supplier's expense. Someone, in either scenario, is being shortchanged, and so regular cleaning is required.

Beyond the weights themselves, the *mishnah* adds: *u-mekaniach moznayim al kol mishkal u-mishkal* — he must wipe down the balance pans between every single weighing. The balance pan is the surface on which the goods themselves are placed. With adhesive substances — meat, wine, oil — material clings to the pan and adds to the apparent weight of subsequent goods, systematically cheating buyers. This must be addressed after every transaction.

### Liquid vs. Dry Measures

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies this last requirement: *bameh devarim amurim, be-lach* — this applies specifically to *lach*, to wet or liquid-adjacent goods, which adhere. *Aval be-yavesh eino tzarich* — for dry goods, the pan need not be wiped between each weighing, since dry material does not stick. The Rashbam clarifies: *lach* encompasses meat, wine, and oil — goods that carry moisture and leave residue on the pan. Apples, for instance, would generally not raise this concern; fine flour might approach the *lach* category depending on how it adheres.

### Tefach & Geirumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּיב לְהַכְרִיעַ לוֹ טֶפַח. הָיָה שׁוֹקֵל לוֹ עַיִן בְּעַיִן, נוֹתֵן לוֹ גֵּירוּמִין. אֶחָד לַעֲשָׂרָה בַּלַּח, וְאֶחָד לְעֶשְׂרִים בַּיָּבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** before adding the weights and merchandise the seller is **obligated to let** the pans of the scale that will hold the merchandise **tilt** an extra **handbreadth for** the buyer by adding a weight to that side. If the seller **weighed for him exactly,** i.e., with the scales equally balanced initially, instead of allowing the scales to tilt an extra handbreadth, **he must give** the buyer **additional amounts [*geirumin*],** an additional **one-tenth in** the case of **liquids** sold by weight, **and** an additional **one-twentieth in** the case of **dry** goods.

Beyond the question of accurate measurement lies a further obligation: *ve-chayav le-hachria lo tefach* — the storekeeper is obligated to tip the scale in the buyer's favor by a *tefach*. That is, after placing the purchased goods on one side and the weights on the other, one must add enough additional goods to cause the buyer's side of the scale to descend by a full *tefach* — approximately three to four finger-breadths. This is a remarkably large margin. The Rashbam specifies that this is the standard for a *litra* (a pound-weight). For such a small unit, a full *tefach* depression of the scale seems substantial, and the requirement is genuinely surprising even if a highly sensitive scale might require less actual material to achieve that deflection.

The *mishnah* then addresses a different commercial custom: *ha-shokel ayin be-ayin, notnin lo geirumin* — where the local practice is to weigh precisely, eye to eye, with an exactly even balance, the storekeeper gives the buyer *geirumin*. The Rashbam connects the word *geirumin* to *hagramah* as in *shechitah* — a tipping or deflection — which is a linguistically interesting suggestion. Functionally, *geirumin* refers to a small extra measure thrown in after the weighing: not tipping the scale during measurement, but tossing in something additional afterward, like a baker's dozen.

The *mishnah* specifies the amount as *echad le-asarah be-lach ve-echad le-esrim be-yavesh* — one in ten for liquid, one in twenty for dry. The *Gemara* will significantly reinterpret these numbers, reducing them from what appears to be ten percent and five percent to something far smaller.

### Custom Determines Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לָמוֹד בְּדַקָּה – לֹא יָמוֹד בְּגַסָּה. בְּגַסָּה – לֹא יָמוֹד בְּדַקָּה. לִמְחוֹק – לֹא יִגְדּוֹשׁ. לִגְדּוֹשׁ – לֹא יִמְחוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues to discuss the correct method of weighing: In **a place where they were accustomed to measure** merchandise in several stages **with a small** measuring vessel, **one may not measure** all the items at once **with** a single **large** measuring vessel. In a place where they measure **with** one **large** measuring vessel, **one may not measure with** several **small** measuring vessels. In a place where the custom is **to level** the top of the measuring vessel to remove substances heaped above its edges, **one may not heap** it, and where the custom is **to heap** it, **one may not level** it.

The *mishnah* concludes with the principle that local custom governs commercial practice: where it is the custom to measure with small vessels, one may not switch to large ones; where large vessels are the norm, small ones are prohibited. The reason is that the practice of small measures customarily involves giving a small additional amount with each unit, benefiting the buyer incrementally; switching to a single large measure removes that accumulated benefit. Someone is disadvantaged whenever the established practice changes, whether seller or buyer, and therefore custom must be respected.

Similarly: *limchok o ligdosh, ligdosh lo yimchok* — if the custom is to smooth the top of the measuring vessel (giving a level, flat measure), one may not heap it up; if the custom is to heap (*ligdosh* — to pile), one may not smooth it down. Changing in either direction disrupts the equilibrium of local commercial expectations.

## Source of Extra Measures

### Reish Lakish: Even Sheleima

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אֶבֶן שְׁלֵמָה וָצֶדֶק״ – צַדֵּק מִשֶּׁלְּךָ וְתֵן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that the seller must initially let the scales tilt an extra handbreadth, **derived? Reish Lakish said:** The source is **that the verse states** that one should have: **"A perfect and just [*tzedek*] weight"** (Deuteronomy 25:15), which is interpreted as an instruction to the seller: **Be righteous [*tzaddek*] with that which is yours and give it to** the buyer. The Gemara asks: **If that is so, say the latter clause:** If the seller **weighed for him exactly, he gives** the buyer **additional amounts. But if letting** the scales **tilt** is obligatory **by Torah law, how can he** originally **give him** by weighing **exactly?**

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the scriptural source for the requirement to give extra? Reish Lakish answers from the verse: *even sheleima va-tzedek* — a complete and just stone — which he expounds as *tzedek mi-shelcha ve-ten lo*: be righteous from your own — give something of your own to ensure the buyer receives everything owed. This establishes the general principle that one must give a small increment beyond the precise weight.

### Tipping vs. Adding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הָיָה שׁוֹקֵל לוֹ עַיִן בְּעַיִן, נוֹתֵן לוֹ גֵּירוּמִין. וְאִי הַכְרָעָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, הֵיכִי יָהֵיב לֵיהּ עַיִן בְּעַיִן? אֶלָּא רֵישָׁא בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ; וְאִי אִיתְּמַר דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – אַסֵּיפָא אִיתְּמַר: הָיָה שׁוֹקֵל לוֹ עַיִן בְּעַיִן – נוֹתֵן לוֹ גֵּירוּמִין. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וָצֶדֶק״ – צַדֵּק מִשֶּׁלְּךָ וְתֵן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is not obligatory to let the scales tilt, and **the first clause** is referring **to a place where they are accustomed** to let the scales tilt an extra handbreadth. **And if** the statement **of Reish Lakish was stated,** it **was stated with regard to the latter clause:** If the seller **weighed for him exactly, he gives** the buyer **additional amounts. From where is this matter** derived? **Reish Lakish said** that this is **as the verse states: "And just [*tzedek*],"** which indicates: **Be righteous [*tzaddek*] with that which is yours and give it to** the buyer. The Gemara asks: **And how much** are the **additional amounts** that are given? **Rabbi Abba bar Memel says** that **Rav says: In** the case of **liquids, one-tenth of a *litra* for** every **ten *litra*,** i.e., one-hundredth.

The *Gemara* then notices a tension. If *even sheleima* establishes a requirement to give extra *in the act of weighing* — by tipping the scale — then the *mishnah*'s practice of *ayin be-ayin* (precise weighing followed by a separate *geirumin*) seems to violate a *d'Oraita* obligation. How can precise, exactly-even weighing ever be permitted?

The *Gemara* resolves this: *ela reisha be-makom she-nahagu* — the *mishnah* is describing local customs. The *d'Oraita* principle requires giving something extra, but does not mandate the specific form — whether by tipping the scale during weighing or by adding afterward. Both methods satisfy the underlying requirement; local convention determines which form is used.

Accordingly, Reish Lakish's derivation is relocated: *ve-chi itmar d'Reish Lakish, a-seifa itmar* — his teaching applies to the latter clause, where one weighs precisely and then gives *geirumin* afterward. The verse *ve-tzedek, tzedek mi-shelcha ve-ten lo* supports adding something from one's own stock *after* the precise measurement, not necessarily during it. The general obligation to give something extra — in one form or another — is thus grounded in scripture.

### Rav Abba: One of Ten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה גֵּירוּמִין? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל אָמַר רַב: אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה בַּלִּיטְרָא בַּלַּח – לַעֲשָׂרָה לִיטְרִין. אֶחָד לַעֲשָׂרָה בַּלַּח, וְאֶחָד לְעֶשְׂרִים בְּיָבֵשׁ וְכוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֵיכִי קָאָמַר? אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה בַּלַּח – לַעֲשָׂרָה דְלַח, וְאֶחָד מֵעֶשְׂרִים בְּיָבֵשׁ – לְעֶשְׂרִים דְּיָבֵשׁ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה – לַעֲשָׂרָה דְּלַח וּלְעֶשְׂרִים דְּיָבֵשׁ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the seller adds **one-tenth in** the case of **liquids, and one-twentieth for dry** goods. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **what** case **is** the *tanna* of the mishna **speaking?** Does he mean **one-tenth in** the case of **liquids for** every **ten** units **of liquid, and** similarly **one-twentieth in** the case of **dry** goods **for** every **twenty** units **of dry** goods, i.e., one four-hundredth? **Or perhaps,** he means **one-tenth for** every **ten** units **of liquid, and** similarly one-tenth **for** every **twenty** units **of dry** goods, i.e., one two-hundredth? The Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *Gemara* turns to quantification. The *mishnah* stated *echad le-asarah be-lach* — one in ten for liquids — which, read literally, would mean ten percent. This seems far too large. Rav Abba bar Mamal citing Rav introduces a creative re-reading: *echad me-asarah be-litra be-lach le-asarah litrin* — one tenth of a single unit when purchasing ten units. That is, the extra measure is one tenth of one unit given when buying ten units — which is one percent, not ten percent. The *mishnah* is read as compressing two layered fractions.

### Dry Measures: Percentage Ambiguity

The corresponding figure for dry measures — *echad le-esrim be-yavesh* — is then subjected to the same question. The *Gemara* poses a *be-ayah*: does it mean one twentieth of a unit for every twenty units of dry measure (*echad me-esrim be-yavesh le-esrim de-yavesh*), yielding one four-hundredth of a percent — a quarter of a percent? Or does it mean one twentieth of a unit for every ten units, as in the liquid case (*echad me-asarah le-asarah de-lach, u-le-esrim be-yavesh*), yielding one two-hundredth — a half of a percent? The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teku*. As for why dry measures should require a smaller *geirumin* than liquid measures, the *Gemara* does not explain, and the question remains open.

## Severity of Fraud

### Rav Levi: Measure Fraud vs. Sexual Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: קָשֶׁה עוֹנְשָׁן שֶׁל מִדּוֹת יוֹתֵר מֵעוֹנְשָׁן שֶׁל עֲרָיוֹת, שֶׁזֶּה נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״אֵל״, וְזֶה נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״אֵלֶּה״. וּמַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״אֵל״ – קָשֶׁה הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת אֵילֵי הָאָרֶץ לָקָח״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Levi says: The punishment for** using false **measures is more severe than the punishment for** transgressing the prohibition of **forbidden sexual relations. As** in **that** case, forbidden relations, it **is stated with regard to them** a shortened term for the word "these": **"*El*,"** in the verse: "For all these [*el* ] abominations" (Leviticus 18:27). **And** in **this** case, false measures, it **is stated** an expanded term for the word "these": **"*Elleh*,"** in the verse: "For all that do these [*elleh*] things, even all that do unrighteously, are an abomination unto the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 25:16). **And from where may it be inferred that this** expression **"*el*"** indicates that the prohibition **is severe,** based on which it is understood that the form this word takes indicates a level of severity? **As it is written: "And the mighty [*eilei*] of the land he took away"** (Ezekiel 17:13).

The *Gemara* now steps back from technical analysis to deliver a striking moral statement. Rav Levi says: *kasheh onsham shel middos yoter me-onsham shel arayos* — the punishment for weights-and-measures fraud is more severe than the punishment for *arayos*, the gravest sexual prohibitions. This is a deliberately provocative formulation, precisely because it inverts the intuitive hierarchy: we tend to regard *arayos* as among the most serious of all transgressions. The *Gemara* supports the comparison with a textual argument: the verse concerning *arayos* uses the formulation *eil* (singular), while the verse concerning measures uses *eileh* (the fuller form), which connotes greater gravity.

### Impossible Restitution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי עֲרָיוֹת נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״אֵלֶּה״! הָהוּא לְמַעוֹטֵי מִדּוֹת מִכָּרֵת. וְאִילּוּ אִי הָכִי אֵימָא סֵיפָא וְאֶלָּא מַאי עוּדְפַּיְיהוּ? דְּהָתָם אֶפְשָׁר בִּתְשׁוּבָה, וְהָכָא לָא אֶפְשָׁר בִּתְשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But with regard to forbidden relations isn't it also written:** "For whosoever shall do any of **these [*elleh*]** abominations" (Leviticus 18:29)? If so, why is the punishment for using false measures considered harsher? The Gemara answers: **That** expression of "*elleh*" (Leviticus 18:29) in the context of forbidden relations does not serve to emphasize its severity. Rather, it serves **to exclude** one who uses deception in **measures from** the penalty of **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*].** The Gemara asks: **But** if the punishment is in fact less severe, **what is the advantage,** i.e., the greater severity, in the case of false measures? The Gemara answers **that there,** in the case of one who engages in forbidden relations, **he has the possibility of repentance. But here,** in the case of one who uses false measures, **there is no possibility of repentance** because he has no way of knowing whom he cheated, and is therefore unable to return the stolen money.

The *Gemara* then challenges its own reading: does the *arayos* passage not also contain the word *eileh* — *ki kol asher ya'aseh mi-kol ha-to'eivos ha-eileh ve-nichretu*? Yes — and that is precisely the point. The *eileh* in the *arayos* passage specifies that *karet* applies *only* to *arayos*, not to measure fraud. So while measures are described in graver terms, the concrete punishment of *karet* is reserved for *arayos* alone.

The *Gemara* then asks: in what sense, then, is the punishment for measure fraud more severe? *De-hasam efshar bi-teshuvah ve-hacha lo efshar bi-teshuvah* — because in the case of *arayos*, repentance is possible, while for measure fraud, full repentance is structurally impossible. The merchant who has systematically shortchanged customers has stolen fractionally from every person who ever entered his shop. To do *teshuvah* properly for theft requires returning what was stolen — *ve-hashiv et ha-gezeila* — but he cannot identify all his victims. He does not know who they are, how many there were, or how much each lost. Even if he contributes to communal needs — *tzorchei rabbim* — that is not complete *teshuvah*. The case of Crazy Eddie's was raised as a contemporary illustration: one of those involved spent the remainder of his life lecturing students on business ethics, but acknowledged he could never locate all those he had defrauded.

### Theft Comparison: Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: קָשָׁה גֶּזֶל הֶדְיוֹט יוֹתֵר מִגֶּזֶל גָּבוֹהַּ, שֶׁזֶּה הִקְדִּים ״חֵטְא״ לִ״מְּעִילָה״, וְזֶה הִקְדִּים ״מְעִילָה״ לְ״חֵטְא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Levi says: Robbing an ordinary** person **is more severe than robbing the Most High,** i.e., taking consecrated property. **As** with regard to **this** regular robber, the verse states **"sin" before "*me'ila*":** "If any one sin, and commit a trespass [*me'ila*] against the Lord, and deal falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit, or of pledge, or of robbery, or have oppressed his neighbor" (Leviticus 5:21). **And** with regard to **that** one who misuses consecrated items, the verse states ***me'ila* before sin:** "If any one engages in misuse [*timol ma'al*] and sins unwittingly" (Leviticus 5:15).

Rav Levi extends this theme with a second statement: *kasha gezel hedyot yoter mi-gezel gavoha* — stealing from an ordinary person is worse than stealing from God (i.e., *me'ila*, misappropriating consecrated property). The proof is structural: when the Torah describes stealing from a person, it says first *ve-chata u-ma'ala ma'al ba-Hashem* — "he has sinned and committed a *ma'al* against God" — and only then describes the human theft. The *chet* against the person comes first in the sequence, suggesting its primacy. By contrast, when the Torah describes *me'ila* with consecrated property, it says *ki timol ma'al mi-kodshei Hashem* — the *me'ila* is named first, the *chet* only later. 

The Rashbam's reading is that *ma'al* in the theft context means *deriving benefit* — so in the human-theft case, the sin itself (*chet*) precedes the benefit derived, suggesting a more immediate transgression. In the *me'ila* case, benefit from the sacred comes first; the formal *chet* designation follows. This sequencing — the *chet* arriving earlier in the theft-from-person case — indicates that stealing from a person carries a more urgent moral charge. This point reinforces the broader *mussar* lesson that the hierarchy of *bein adam la-Makom* and *bein adam la-chaveiro* is not what the intuitive religious sensibility typically assumes.

Tosafot addresses the obvious objection: can one really say that *teshuvah* is possible for *arayos* but not for measure fraud? Many *arayos* violations produce lasting, irreparable damage — children who are *mamzerim*, devastated marriages, profound psychological harm. Tosafot answers by distinguishing: where *mamzerus* does not result and the harm is less concrete, *teshuvah* to God remains achievable even if the human damage is hard to reverse. Rav Chaim adds a further clarification: between the sinner and God, *teshuvah* is always available for any *aveirah* — God commanded repentance precisely because it is possible. But for theft, the *teshuvah* process is defined by *hashavat gezeila*, the physical return of stolen property. Where that return is structurally impossible because the victims cannot be identified, the *teshuvah* process itself is incomplete.

### Blessings vs. Curses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: בּוֹא וּרְאֵה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִדַּת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא – בֵּרַךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם, וְקִלְּלָן בִּשְׁמֹנֶה. בֵּרְכָן בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם – מֵ״אִם בְּחֻקֹּתַי״ עַד ״קוֹמְמִיּוּת״;
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Levi says: Come and see that the attribute of flesh and blood is unlike the attribute of the Holy One, Blessed be He. The Holy One, Blessed be He, blessed the Jewish people with twenty-two, and cursed them with** only **eight.** Rabbi Levi explains: **He blessed them with** the **twenty-two** letters of the Hebrew alphabet, **from** the first letter, *alef*, that begins the verse: **"If [*im*] you** walk **in My statutes"** (Leviticus 26:3), **until "upright [*komemiyyut*]"** (Leviticus 26:13), which ends with the letter *tav*, the last letter of the Hebrew alphabet.

Rav Levi closes with a reflection on the asymmetry between divine and human communication: *shelo ke-middat HaKadosh Baruch Hu middat basar va-dam* — God's ways are not the ways of flesh and blood. God blessed Israel with twenty-two letters — from *alef* to *taf*, encompassing the whole alphabet — and cursed them with only eight. The *berachot* in *Vayikra* begin with *Im be-chukotai telechu* (opening letter *alef*) and end with *komemiyut* (closing letter *taf*); the *kelalot* begin with *im be-chukotai tim'asu* (opening letter *vav*) and end at *mem* — only a third of the alphabet.

Moshe Rabbeinu, a human being, reversed this proportion: he blessed Israel with only eight letters — from *vav* to *mem* (*Ve-haya im shamo'a tishme'u* through *la-avdam*) — and cursed them with twenty-two, from *vav* in *Ve-haya im lo tishme'u* all the way around to *hei* in *ve-ein koneh*. This observation connects to the well-known *Gemara* in other contexts that the *kelalot* of *Devarim* are longer and more intense than those of *Vayikra*, since Moshe spoke them in the first person without the softening of divine mediation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקִלְּלָן בִּשְׁמוֹנָה – מִ״וְּאִם בְּחֻקֹּתַי תִּמְאָסוּ״ עַד ״וְאֶת חֻקֹּתַי גָּעֲלָה נַפְשָׁם״. וְאִילּוּ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ – בֵּרְכָן בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה, וְקִלְּלָן בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And He cursed them with eight** letters, **from** the letter *vav* that begins the verse: **"And if [*ve'im*] you shall reject My statutes"** (Leviticus 26:15), **until: "And My statutes were abhorred by their soul [*nafsham*]"** (Leviticus 26:43), which ends with the letter *mem*. There are eight letters in the Hebrew alphabet from the letter *vav* to the letter *mem*, inclusive. **And yet Moses, our teacher,** who is flesh and blood, **blessed them with eight** letters, **and cursed them with twenty-two.**

## Parshiyot Timing

### Vayeilech & Nitzavim

Tosafot takes note of the *Gemara*'s derivation — that the *kelalot* of *Devarim* span from *Ve-haya im lo tishme'u* through *ve-ein koneh* — and uses it to address a practical question of the annual Torah reading cycle: why do we sometimes separate *Nitzavim* and *Vayeilech* into two distinct *parshiyot*, even though *Vayeilech* is exceptionally short and would more naturally be combined with *Nitzavim*, while we routinely combine the much longer double *parasha* of *Matot-Masei*?

The common explanation is that we need *Nitzavim* to fall before *Rosh Hashanah*, ensuring a *parasha* of relative comfort and gathering (*atem nitzavim* — "you stand together") immediately before the new year, and that separating *Nitzavim* from *Vayeilech* creates enough additional weeks to achieve this. Implicit in this is a desire not to read the *tochacha* — the curses — immediately before the new year.

### Rabbeinu Nissim: Buffer

Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Nissim for a refined version: *le-pi peirush Rabbeinu Nissim*. He explains that we always read *Nitzavim* before *Rosh Hashanah* in order to insert a *parasha* as a buffer between the *kelalot* of *Ki Tavo* and the beginning of the new year — *k'dei le-hafsik be-parsha bein klalos d'shana she-avra de-Ki Tavo le-shana ha-ba'ah*. We want the *kelalot* to belong entirely to the expiring year, and we want a neutral intervening *parasha* to mark the separation. Simply finishing *Ki Tavo* and beginning the new year the very next week would feel too abrupt; *Nitzavim* provides the necessary pause.

Tosafot then engages the current *Gemara*'s implication: since the *Gemara* here derives that the *kelalot* of Moshe's *tochacha* span from *Ve-haya im lo tishme'u* through *ve-ein koneh*, the *kelalot* effectively conclude at the end of *Ki Tavo*, not within *Nitzavim*. On this reading, *Nitzavim* itself is not truly a *parasha* of *kelalot* — so there would be no textual reason from this *Gemara* to require it before *Rosh Hashanah* on account of the curses. Rabbeinu Nissim's point is therefore not that *Nitzavim* itself contains unacceptable *kelalot*, but rather that it functions as a buffer between *Ki Tavo*'s curses and the new year — a clean structural separation between the old year's judgment and the new one's beginning.

### Bamidbar Before Shavuot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִ״וְּהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ תִּשְׁמַע״, וְעַד ״לְעׇבְדָם״; וְקִלְּלָן בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם – מִ״וְּהָיָה אִם לֹא תִשְׁמַע״ עַד ״וְאֵין קֹנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **from** the letter *vav* that begins the verse: **"And it shall come to pass [*vehaya*], if you shall hearken diligently"** (Deuteronomy 28:1), **until: "To serve them [*le'ovdam*]"** (Deuteronomy 28:14), which ends with the letter *mem*. **And he cursed them with twenty-two** letters, **from** the letter *vav* that begins the verse: **"But it shall come to pass [*vehaya*], if you will not hearken** to the voice of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 28:15), **until: "And no man shall buy [*koneh*]"** (Deuteronomy 28:68). This verse ends with a letter *heh*, which comes just before *vav* in the alphabet. Starting with a *vav* and ending with a *heh* encompasses the twenty-two letters of the alphabet.

The same logic applies, Tosafot continues, to the *parasha* of *Bamidbar*: *le-olam anu korim Bamidbar lifnei Atzeret* — we always read *Bamidbar* immediately before *Shavuot*. *Shavuot* is *Rosh Hashanah le-peirot ha-ilan* — the day of judgment for tree fruits — and is therefore a form of *yom ha-din* for the new fruit of the year. Just as we separate the *kelalot* from the new year of *Rosh Hashanah*, so too we insert *Bamidbar* — a *parasha* of census and tribal order, without *tochacha* — as a buffer between the *kelalot* at the end of *Vayikra* (*Im be-chukotai*) and the new year of fruits. *Bamidbar* thus functions as a structural pause, ensuring that the curses of *Vayikra* do not flow directly into the judgment of *Shavuot*.

Tosafot cites in support the *Gemara* in Megilla, which records that Ezra established the reading of the *kelalot she-be-Mishneh Torah* — the curses of *Devarim* — before *Shavuot/Atzeret*, for this same reason of temporal separation between curse and judgment.
