# Gittin 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eliezer vs. Meir: Get De…

### Get Nature Intro

The present daf opens with a review of the foundational debate that runs throughout *masechet Gittin*: what is the essential nature of the *get*, and which component — the writing or the signatures — is the operative act of divorce?

### Chatima vs. Ketiva

The Talmudic dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Meir turns on a single conceptual pivot. Rabbi Meir holds *edei chatima karsi* — the signing witnesses are what "cut" the *get*, making the *get* operative through its signatures. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, locates the operative act in the *ketiva*, the writing itself, and emphasizes the witnesses present at the moment of *mesira*, the physical handover of the *get*.

### Mechubar Problem

This distinction generates practical consequences throughout the tractate. For Rabbi Meir, who focuses on the signatures, the prohibition against writing a *get* on something *mechubar* — still attached to the ground — is a problem located in the signing, not in the writing. For Rabbi Eliezer, who focuses on the writing, the *mechubar* problem arises at the moment the text is inscribed.

### Forgeable Background

Similarly, the earlier discussion about a *get* written on a forgeable background — parchment that had been erased and could be erased again without detection — generated different outcomes under the two positions. For Rabbi Meir, the *get* is defined partly by its function as written testimony: the signed *get* must genuinely testify to what occurred. If the document is written on a forgeable background, the testimony is compromised and it can no longer serve as a *get*, even if witnesses were present at the *mesira* and even if there is no actual doubt about what transpired. The documentary testimony itself has become meaningless, and that disqualifies it. For Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, the testimonial function is separable from the instrument of divorce.

### Get Definition

This brings us to a conceptually crucial distinction. For Rabbi Meir, part of the very definition of a *get* is that it constitutes written testimony — a *shtar raya* — and if it fails to genuinely testify, it is not a *get* regardless of independent proof of the underlying facts. For Rabbi Eliezer, the testimony aspect is entirely separate from the *get*. One can ask the *edim* who witnessed the handover what occurred; how one knows the facts is independent of whether the document functions as an instrument of divorce.

### Shtar Kinyan/Raya

This distinction maps onto the broader categories of *shtar kinyan* — a document that itself effects a change in status — and *shtar raya* — a document that serves as evidence of what occurred. For Rabbi Eliezer, these two identities can be fully separated. A *get* can be a *shtar kinyan* without being a *shtar raya*, and therefore may even be written on a forgeable background without invalidation. For Rabbi Meir, the *get* as written testimony is bound up in its very definition as an instrument of divorce.

### Other Kinyan Cases

The *gemara* raises the question of whether Rabbi Eliezer's permissive position extends to other instruments of *kinyan*, such as a *shtar shichrur* for a slave or a deed of sale for a field. While the *gemara*'s simple reading is that the unique nature of a *get* permits separation of the two identities — *shtar kinyan* without *shtar raya* — the question of whether Rabbi Eliezer would allow the same leniency in other *shtarei kinyan* is left open. *Tosafot* inclines toward the view that only by *get* does Rabbi Eliezer permit the separation; for other instruments, even a *shtar kinyan* may require simultaneously functioning as a *shtar raya*.

### Fundamental Debate

The deepest level of the dispute, then, is not merely a technical question of whether *vechatav lah* focuses on writing or signing. It is a question about the very character of the *get* as a legal instrument: to what degree must a *get* be written testimony? For Rabbi Meir, it must be — and a forgeable document fails categorically. For Rabbi Eliezer, the *get* is purely a *shtar kinyan* and need not serve as written testimony at all. And this distinction ramifies into discussions of *shichrur* and the sale of land, where the degree to which the *shtar kinyan* must also serve as *shtar raya* remains a live question.

## Student Q&A

### Eliezer Challenge

One might ask: what recourse does Rabbi Eliezer offer when a *get* is challenged in court? Since Rabbi Eliezer does not rely on the document's written signatures as operative testimony, the answer is that one must go and locate the *edim* who were physically present at the *mesira*. The signed document cannot itself serve as proof of the transaction under Rabbi Eliezer's framework.

That said, the picture is more complicated than a clean binary. Some *rishonim* hold that even for Rabbi Eliezer, where the *get* was written on a non-forgeable background, the document can in practice also function as a *shtar raya*. If you have it, you may use the written signatures without needing to locate the physical witnesses. Some *rishonim* go further, holding that when written signatures are present, Rabbi Eliezer would not require physical witnesses to be present at the handover at all — those written witnesses can serve both testimonial functions. Yet even according to these views, the key point stands: it need not function that way. It is a *get* independently of whether it serves as written testimony.

### Mesira Role

The question of *mesira* and the role of external witnesses draws out the further ramifications of the debate. For Rabbi Meir, the signed witnesses function as the witnesses to the *mesira* itself — no external witnesses are required at the handover, because the signatures accomplish everything. For Rabbi Eliezer, since the emphasis falls on the *mesira*, external witnesses must be physically present. The signed text is not what constitutes the divorce; the witnessed handover is.

Further complexity arises from Rabbeinu Tam, who holds that even for Rabbi Meir — who demands the signatures — this requirement pertains only to what turns the document into a *get*, but when it comes to the actual giving over, physical witnesses are required externally. This creates a striking inversion: Rabbi Meir's position ends up, in this view, resembling Rabbi Eliezer's in practice with respect to *mesira*. The most unambiguous point that emerges is that for Rabbi Eliezer, the demands of *vechatav* — whatever the *Torah* requires in how the *get* is written — focus on the *ketiva*; for Rabbi Meir, they focus on the *chatima*.

## Mishna: Who Can Write

### Hakol Kosherim

The *mishna* at the bottom of 24b states: *hakol kesherim lichtov et haget* — everyone is eligible to write a *get* — *afilu cheresh shoteh vekatan* — even a deaf-mute, a person who is mentally incompetent, and a minor. These three categories — *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* — are the paradigmatic cases of people who cannot be made agents, cannot execute legally binding acts, and are not considered *bnei da'at*. The fact that they can write a *get* is, at first glance, astonishing.

### Mishna Simple Sense

The simplest reading of this *mishna* — and the one confirmed by the *gemara*'s eventual resolution — is that it follows the position of Rabbi Meir. *Stam mishna* is Rabbi Meir, and the previous *mishna* established the same principle: a *get* written *mechubar lakarka* is valid so long as it is signed after detachment. The consistent implication of both *mishnayot* is that what matters is the signing, not the writing. If the signing is all that counts, one could in principle print a *get* and simply have the correct witnesses sign it — it does not matter who produced the text.

### Woman/Man Writing

The *mishna* continues: *ha'isha kotevet et gitah veha'ish kotev et shovro* — a woman can write her own *get*, and a man can write the receipt acknowledging that the *ketuba* was paid. The apparent problem — that one might misrepresent facts by writing one's own document — is dissolved by what follows.

### Kiyum Haget

The *mishna* closes: *she'ein kiyum haget ela b'chotamav* — the validation of the *get* rests exclusively on its signatures. This is the operative principle. Writing without signatures is nothing — it is equivalent to printing a document without authorization. It becomes a *get* only when witnesses sign it, and witnesses will not sign unless the act is genuinely occurring.

### Misrepresentation Preven…

This is why there is no concern about misrepresentation. A woman cannot falsely claim she received a *get* by writing the text herself, because without proper signatures the document has no standing whatsoever. Similarly, a man cannot fabricate a receipt for the *ketuba* by writing it himself; the witnesses must sign, and they will only sign if the *ketuba* was actually paid. The entire structure of protection against fraud is built into the requirement of witness signatures, not into any restriction on who may write the text.

## Gemara: Intent Problem

### Bnei Daas Problem

The *gemara* initially approaches the *mishna* as though it might represent the position of Rabbi Eliezer — a surprising assumption, though one generated in part by the fact that the previous discussion about *mechubar* had been associated with Rabbi Eliezer. On that reading, the *gemara* presses a challenge: *vehala lav bnei de'ah ninhu* — how can a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* write a *get* if they are not considered *bnei de'ah*? Their intent cannot constitute *lishma*, and they cannot execute legal acts. So how does the *mishna* permit this?

### Gadol Omed Al Gavo

Rav Huna answers: *vehu shehaya gadol omed al gabav* — the case is where an adult stands over them and supervises the writing.

### Tosafot: Instruction

*Tosafot* immediately observes that *gadol omed al gabav* can mean different things in different contexts. By *shechita*, where the only requirement is technical correctness in the performance of the act, the adult's presence is primarily one of observation — ensuring the mechanics were executed properly. Here, where the issue is writing with the correct intent of *lishma*, *gadol omed al gabav* means something more active: the adult is directly instructing the writer as the writing proceeds. This explains why the *gemara* presents *gadol omed al gabav* as a solution even for the case of a *shoteh*, which would seem to be the hardest case. By standing over and continuously directing the act, the adult creates the context and content of the intent, which is then attributed to or channeled through the person writing.

*Tosafot* also notes a related conceptual framework: since the person writing is acting entirely in accordance with the adult's instructions, and this is observable from their actions — write the name *Leah bat Rachel*, and they write *Leah bat Rachel* — there is concrete evidence that the writer is being shaped by the correct intent. One might even conceptualize the relationship as the adult writing through the other person as a vehicle, though *Tosafot* does not explicitly adopt this framing.

### Shlichut Not Required

*Tosafot* raises a further question. If we are operating within Rabbi Eliezer's framework (as the *gemara* initially assumes), doesn't the writer need to be a *shaliach* of the husband? The husband presumably instructs the *sofer*: write this *get* for my wife. Isn't this a formal act of *shlichut*? *Tosafot* answers: no. The proof is in the *gemara* itself — since a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* cannot be made *shelichim* at all, and yet the *gemara* considers them valid writers, it follows that *shlichut* is not a requirement for *ketiva* of a *get*. The reason the husband speaks to the *sofer* and instructs him to write it for his wife is not to create a formal *shlichut* relationship, but solely to establish the proper context of *lishma*. Without hearing from the husband that this *get* is for his specific wife, the *sofer* cannot genuinely claim to be writing it for the sake of divorcing this particular woman. The instruction creates the mindset of *lishma*; it does not create *shlichut*.

### Bnei Krisus Debate

*Tosafot* raises a third problem. The *gemara* states in multiple places that those who write and sign a *get* must be *bnei krisus* — persons within the legal domain of the laws of *gittin*. Does this not exclude the *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan*? *Tosafot* addresses this in two ways. First, one might argue that *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* are theoretically within the *parsha* — a *katan* will eventually become an adult, and a *cheresh* or *shoteh* might recover. But the stronger argument emerges from the *gemara*'s next move: the *gemara* considers whether even a *nochri* could write the *get*. A *nochri* is certainly not *bnei krisus* within the framework of our laws of *gittin vekiddushin*. Yet the *gemara* entertains the possibility. This forces the conclusion that the requirement of *bnei krisus* is contingent on *shlichut*: one must be in the *parsha* to be a *shaliach*, but if *shlichut* is not required for *ketiva* of a *get*, then even a *nochri* could in principle write it.

### Intent via Instruction

The picture that *Tosafot* constructs, then, is coherent and radical simultaneously. For Rabbi Eliezer, who cares very much about the *ketiva*: (1) *lishma* in writing can be achieved through *gadol omed al gabav*, even for a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan*; (2) *shlichut* is not required — the husband's instruction creates *lishma*, not agency; and (3) even *bnei krisus* is not required for the writer, since that requirement is a function of *shlichut* which is itself not required for writing.

## Intent/Mechanics Q&A

### Right Intent

A close examination of what "right intent" means in the context of *lishma* for writing a *get* raises genuinely difficult questions. The requirement is that the *get* be written for the sake of this particular woman's divorce — for this husband, for this wife.

### Observable Intent

The principle at work here is something like *kavanatam nikeret mitoch maaseihem* — the intent of the writer is observable from their actions. The adult instructs: write for the divorce of this man's wife, her name is *Leah bat Rachel*, and write these words. When the person writes those specific words, there is concrete, observable evidence that they are being guided by the instructions. The assumption is that whatever ideas occupy their mind are the ideas placed there by the supervising adult.

### Gadol Intent Role

Several questions arise about the precise mechanism of *gadol omed al gabav*. If the person's intent is inferred from their faithful execution of instructions, one might ask whether the *gadol*'s presence is strictly necessary in all cases — could there be scenarios where the correct intent is independently evident? Conversely, one might conceive of the *gadol* as the true agent, with the *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan* functioning as the *gadol*'s instrument or extended "pen." *Tosafot* does not take this direction explicitly, and the two framings — the writer as having their intent shaped by instruction, versus the writer as the vehicle of the *gadol*'s intent — carry different implications. If the emphasis falls on the writer as having independent, observable intent, then in principle one might imagine cases where *gadol omed al gabav* is unnecessary. If the emphasis falls on the *gadol* as the true writer, then the *gadol*'s active presence is indispensable.

### Cheresh Instruction

One further difficulty presents itself in the case of the *cheresh* specifically: if the mechanism of *gadol omed al gabav* is the communication of instructions, how does one instruct someone who cannot hear? In those times, written placards or other forms of communication were not readily available, and the *gemara* does not appear to unpack this question. It remains a genuine difficulty left in the text.

## Non-Jew Exclusion

### Non-Jew Challenge

Rav Nachman poses a sharp challenge to the *gadol omed al gabav* framework: *oved kochavim veYisrael omed al gabav, hachi nami dekasher* — if the principle is that an adult supervising creates the proper intent for the writing, why would the same not apply to a non-Jew with an adult Jew standing over him? The logic of *gadol omed al gabav* seems to make no distinction between a *cheresh* or *katan* and an adult non-Jew.

### Adaata Denafshei

The *gemara* answers: *oved kochavim ada'ata d'nafsheih avid* — a non-Jew is always considered to be acting on his own initiative. He is a full adult, and one cannot assume that what occupies his mind is entirely determined by the instructions given to him.

### Control Distinction

This answer illuminates the deeper logic of *gadol omed al gabav*. The reason it works for a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* is that these persons — by virtue of being not fully *bnei de'ah* — can be seen as having their mental content entirely shaped by the supervising adult. What they think and intend is what the adult places in their minds. Whether one frames this as the writer being the *gadol*'s instrument or as the writer's observable actions demonstrating that the correct intent was instilled, the underlying assumption is that they are fully malleable in this respect. A non-Jew, by contrast, is a complete adult, acting on his own initiative. However faithfully he may follow instructions and write the correct words, one cannot assume that his intent has been wholly determined by those instructions. He may write *Leah bat Rachel* for entirely his own reasons, or with no particular intent at all — and crucially, one cannot project the correct *lishma* onto him.

*Tosafot* presses on the precise nature of the problem with the non-Jew's intent. It would be strange to say that the non-Jew is positively thinking something contrary to *lishma* — writing *l'hachis*, so to speak. The real issue is the opposite: by a *get*, the default assumption is *stam lav lishmah* — an unstated, undirected act of writing does not carry the presumption of *lishma*. This is unlike *mila*, for example, where *stam lishmah kai* — the default assumption for circumcision is that it is performed with the appropriate intent. For a *get*, one needs positive evidence of the correct intent, and by the non-Jew, even with instructions, one cannot assume those ideas were genuinely operative in his mind. The *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* will have in their minds the ideas put there; the non-Jew may follow the words and have nothing in mind at all.

### Rav Nachman Retracts

Having posed the challenge and received the answer, Rav Nachman then retracts his original concern entirely: *lav milta hi de'amri* — what I said was incorrect.

### Mesira Exclusion

His reasoning: *dimichdi kapaslei oved kochavim min hamuva* — the upcoming *mishna* will exclude a non-Jew specifically from the *shlichut* of *mesira*, from serving as an agent to deliver the *get*. *Michlal de'le'inyan ketiva kasher* — from the fact that the exclusion is limited to the *mesira*, one can infer that a non-Jew is valid for the writing. The structure of the next *mishna* thus implies that a non-Jew's ineligibility is confined to acting as a *shaliach*, not to writing the text.

### Non-Jew Good

How then does one account for the *braita* that explicitly says *oved kochavim pasul* — a non-Jew is invalid? Rav Nachman answers: *hahi Rabbi Eliezer hi* — that *braita* follows Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *edei mesirah karsei* — the witnesses at the handover are the operative ones — and who therefore cares very much about the *ketiva* being done properly and *lishma*. For Rabbi Eliezer, the non-Jew's *ada'ata d'nafsheih* problem is fatal. But our *mishna* follows Rabbi Meir, for whom the writing is entirely immaterial.

## Final Position: Rabbi Me…

### Braiata Problem

The *gemara* now confronts the *braita* that states *oved kochavim pasul* — a non-Jew is disqualified from writing a *get* — against the *mishna*'s blanket inclusion of all writers.

### Braiata vs. Mishna

The resolution is clean: *hahi Rabbi Eliezer hi* — the *braita* is Rabbi Eliezer; the *mishna* is Rabbi Meir. Under Rabbi Eliezer, who focuses on the *ketiva* and demands it be written *lishma*, the non-Jew's *ada'ata d'nafsheih* is disqualifying and even a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* would presumably be invalid, even with *gadol omed al gabav*. Under Rabbi Meir, neither the non-Jew nor anyone else is an obstacle, because writing is simply immaterial.

### Meir: Signing Only

With this resolution, the entire elaborate construction of *gadol omed al gabav* for Rabbi Eliezer is set aside as the *gemara*'s preferred reading of the *mishna*. For Rabbi Meir, the *vechatav* of the *pasuk* — *vechatav lah sefer keritut* — means *vechatam*: signing is the operative act. Writing is nothing until signed, and it is only the signing that matters.

### Meir: Garbage Get

Rabbi Meir's position is rendered vivid by an illustration: *afilu metza'o ba'ashpah* — even if one finds a *get* in the garbage, written originally for a different woman, *chasmo venatno lo kasher* — have it signed with the correct intent and give it over, and it is valid. Before the signature, it is a piece of paper. The signature transforms it into a *get*, and it is only the signature that matters.

### Two Explanations

The *gemara* thus preserves two readings of the *mishna*. The first: the *mishna* follows Rabbi Eliezer, with the mechanism of *gadol omed al gabav* creating the necessary *lishma* — a position that generates the remarkable *chidush* that for Rabbi Eliezer, writing a *get* requires neither *shlichut* nor *bnei krisus* on the part of the writer. The second, and ultimately stronger: the *mishna* follows Rabbi Meir, writing is categorically immaterial, and none of these concerns apply at all.

## Challenges: Leshmah

### Rava Challenge

The *gemara* now subjects Rabbi Meir's position to a series of challenges from other texts that appear to demand *lishma* specifically in the *writing* of the *get*. Rava opens: the *pasuk* states *vechatav lah* — and he should write it for her — *lishma*. Does this not refer to the writing of the *get*? How can Rabbi Meir claim that *lishma* in writing is irrelevant?

### Vechatav=Signing

Rav Nachman responds: *lo, chasimas edim* — no, "writing" in the *pasuk* refers to the signing. *Vechatav lah lishma* means: have it signed for her sake. The word *vechatav* encompasses signing.

### Rava Second

Rava presses a second challenge: the *mishna* states *kol get shenichtav shelo lishum ishah pasul* — any *get* that was written not for the sake of the woman is invalid. The word *nichtav* — written — seems to refer unambiguously to the text of the *get*.

### Nichtav=Signed

The answer is parallel: *eima shenichtam shelo lishum ishah* — read *nichtav* as meaning "signed." Writing does not mean writing; it means signing. Again, what was "written" invalidly means what was "signed" without the correct intent.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye raises the strongest textual challenge. A later *mishna* states *keshu kotevo lishma* — when one writes part of it *lishma*, it is as though the whole was written *lishma*. Does this not explicitly indicate that *lishma* applies to the writing — specifically the key written portion?

### Signing=Writing

The answer: read *keshu kotevo* as *keshu chasmo* — when it is signed *lishma*, that is as if the whole was written *lishma*. Signing is a form of writing in the language of the text, and the entire challenge dissolves on this reading. The objection to this reading is that it departs from the plain sense of the word *kotevo*, which most naturally refers to the textual content of the *get*. Nevertheless, this is the *gemara*'s answer.

### Third Answer

An alternative resolution is also offered: *iy ba'it eima, hani masnitin mani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — if you prefer, one may simply say that those *mishnayot* requiring *lishma* in the writing follow Rabbi Eliezer, not Rabbi Meir. Under Rabbi Eliezer, the writing must be *lishma* — or at least the *toref*, the essential particulars, must be written *lishma*. Under Rabbi Meir, these requirements simply do not apply to the writing at all. There is no need to reinterpret the word *kotevo*; one need only recognize that the texts speaking of *lishma* in writing are Rabbi Eliezer's, and the texts under discussion follow Rabbi Meir.

## Strongest Reading

### Abba: Blanket

Rabbi Abba draws the decisive conclusion from the structure of the *mishna* itself. If the *mishna* intended to say that these ineligible writers could write a *get* only under specific conditions — such as leaving the *toref* blank for a competent adult to fill in, or requiring *gadol omed al gabav* — it should have said so. The *mishna*'s blanket statement that *hakol kesherim lichtov*, everyone is eligible to write, with no qualifications, is itself the message. The *mishna* is teaching that the writing of a *get* is immaterial.

### Yochanan: Immaterial

This reading is confirmed by the tradition in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, transmitted by Rav Zeira: the *mishna* demonstrates conclusively that the writing of a *get* does not matter for Rabbi Meir.

### Ein Koach Leshmah

*Kan hodi'ach she'ein koach lishma* — the *mishna* is informing us that there is no force to the *lishma* requirement in the writing of a *get*, according to Rabbi Meir, *de'amar edei chatimah karsei*. All that matters is who signs, not who writes. The *mishna*'s sweeping formulation is its proof: it is stating that the writing is entirely immaterial, and the only reading consistent with that sweep is Rabbi Meir's.

## Next Mishna: Bringing Get

### Hakol Kosherim

The next *mishna* shifts from writing to delivery: *hakol kesherim l'havi et haget chutz m'cheresh shoteh vekatan* — everyone is eligible to bring a *get*, meaning to serve as a *shaliach* for its delivery, except for a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan*. Here, unlike the previous *mishna*, we are dealing explicitly with *shlichut* — the formal appointment of an agent — and the exclusions reflect the requirements of agency.

### Blind Excluded

The *mishna* adds: *vesuma* — and also a blind person is excluded from serving as *shaliach*. The reason for this exclusion is not immediately self-evident and will be explored by the *gemara*.

### Non-Jew Excluded

*Ve'oved kochavim* — a non-Jew is also excluded from *shlichut* for delivering a *get*.

### Status Change Cases

The *mishna* then proceeds to address cases of status change during the course of the *shlichut*: *kiblo katan vehigdil* — if the *get* was received by a minor who subsequently became an adult; *cheresh venispa'ach* — a deaf-mute who recovered; *suma venitpateach* — a blind person who regained sight; *shoteh venishtapeh* — a *shoteh* who regained competence; *oved kochavim venisgayyer* — a non-Jew who converted — in all these cases, *pasul*. The appointment of *shlichut* must occur when the person already has the status that qualifies them as a *shaliach*. If they were appointed before acquiring that status, the *shlichut* is invalid even if they subsequently attain it.

However, the reverse sequence — beginning in a qualified state, lapsing temporarily, and returning — is treated differently. *Pikeach venitcharesh vechazar venitpakeach* — sighted, then deaf-mute, then again hearing; *patuach venistama vechazar venitpateach* — sighted, blind, and sighted again; *shafui venishtateh vechazar venishtapeh* — competent, then a *shoteh*, then competent again: *kasher* in all these cases.

### Zeh Haklal

The *mishna* concludes with a general principle: *zeh haklal, kol shetechilato vesofo beda'at kasher* — this is the principle: anyone whose beginning and end are with *da'at* is valid. The *mishna* does not require *kashrus* at every intermediate stage; what matters is that the *shaliach* received the *get* from the husband and delivered it to the wife in the requisite state of *da'at* and eligibility.

## Shlichut Requirements

### Bnei Daas

The *gemara* begins unpacking the exclusions. The exclusion of *cheresh*, *shoteh*, and *katan* is readily explained: *d'lav bnei deiah ninhu* — they are not *bnei da'at*, and the formal legal relationship of *shlichut* requires a person capable of genuine legal cognition and intent.

### Bnei Krisus

The exclusion of the non-Jew is explained by a different principle: *lav bar heiteira hu* — sometimes expressed as *lav bnei krisus* — he is not within the legal framework of *gittin vekiddushin*, our laws of divorce and marriage. He cannot represent a Jewish husband in an act governed by a legal framework that does not apply to him.

### Blind Exclusion

The exclusion of the blind person requires more explanation, since a blind person is generally considered a *bar da'at*. Rav Sheshet answers: *lefi she'eino yode'a miman notlah ulemi notnah lo* — because the blind person does not know from whom he is taking it and to whom he is giving it. Without visual identification, the precise execution of the *shlichut* — taking the *get* from the specific husband and delivering it to the specific wife — is compromised.

### Tziuta Dekala

Rav Yosef challenges Rav Sheshet's answer: *bitzi'uta dekala* — one can recognize by voice. He extends the analogy: how can a blind man have relations with his wife if he cannot see whom he is with? How can anyone have relations with a spouse in the dark at night? In all these cases, voice recognition suffices for personal identification. The same should apply to the blind *shaliach*.

### Bifanay Nichtav

Rav Yosef then offers his own answer: *hacha bechutz la'aretz askinan* — the case is specifically in *chutz la'aretz*, outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, where the *shaliach* must declare *bifanay nichtav uvifanay nechtam* — it was written and signed before me. This declaration requires that the *shaliach* have actually seen the writing and signing. Voice recognition of the scratching of the pen is insufficient for this purpose; *bifanay* requires actual visual witness. A blind person therefore cannot make this declaration and cannot serve as *shaliach* in *chutz la'aretz*.

### Patuach V'Nistama

Abaye raises a challenge to Rav Yosef's reasoning: if the only problem is the inability to say *bifanay nichtav*, then a person who was sighted when the *get* was written and signed — and therefore can truthfully make the declaration — but subsequently became blind before delivering the *get*, should be perfectly valid. He saw the writing; he can say *bifanay nichtav*. If so, the *mishna*'s requirement that sighted-then-blind-then-sighted cases require restoration of sight at the end seems to exclude the case where the person ends blind — but on Rav Yosef's reasoning, there should be no problem even if the person remains blind at delivery, as long as he saw the writing. The *gemara* resolves this: even if the person ends blind, it is in fact valid. The reason the *mishna* formulated the case as *patuach venistama vechazar venitpateach* — sighted, blind, and sighted again — was for literary parallelism with the other cases involving *shoteh*, where the ABA pattern genuinely is required. For the blind case specifically, even the AB pattern would suffice.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi derives the same conclusion from a close reading of the *mishna*'s general principle: *zeh haklal kol shetechilato vesofo beda'at kasher* — the principle specifies *beda'at*, in a state of cognitive competence, at beginning and end. It does not say *kol shetechilato vesofo bekashrus* — in a state of *kashrus* or eligibility in every respect at beginning and end. Blindness is not a *da'at* problem; it is a different kind of *kashrus* issue. Therefore the *zeh haklal* does not require restoration of sight at the end, only *da'at* at beginning and end.

## Eved as Shaliach

### Eved Question

The *gemara* poses a question to Rabbi Ami: *eved mahu she-ye'aseh shaliach lekabbel get isha miyad ba'ala* — can a slave, not the slave of either party, serve as a *shaliach* to receive or bring a *get*? A slave owned by the woman would be treated as an extension of her domain — receipt by her slave would be receipt by her. But what of an independent slave serving as a formal *shaliach*? The slave is partially obligated in *mitzvot*; is this sufficient?

### Eved Inference

An initial argument from the structure of the *mishna* favors the slave's eligibility: *midkatanei pasul ve'akum bechada, michlal de'eved kasher* — since the *mishna* lists only a non-Jew among those excluded, one might infer that a slave is included among the valid *shelichim*.

### Rav Assi Answer

Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan rules against this: *ein eved na'aseh shaliach lekabbel get le'isha miyad ba'alah* — a slave cannot serve as *shaliach* to receive a *get* on behalf of a woman from her husband. The reason: *lefi she'eino betoras gittin vekiddushin* — because the laws of *gittin* and *kiddushin* do not apply to slaves. Marriage, as a legal institution, does not pertain to slaves; and therefore they are not within the *parsha* that would qualify them to serve as agents in matters governed by that *parsha*.

### Elazar Counter

Rabbi Elazar challenges this conclusion from a different direction. He does not dispute the ruling but questions whether the reasoning is internally consistent. If the criterion for *shlichut* is being within the relevant *parsha*, then a non-Jew should be able to serve as *shaliach* in areas of law where the non-Jew is within the *parsha*. And indeed, there are areas of *halacha* where non-Jews are within the *parsha* — yet they cannot be *shelichim* even there. This suggests that being within the *parsha* is not sufficient on its own; there must be an additional criterion.

### Non-Jew Teruma

The case in point is *teruma*. A non-Jew who owns land in *Eretz Yisrael* and separates *teruma* from his own grain — *akum vekuti shetaramu mishelahen terumasam terumah* — the *teruma* has legal status. The non-Jew is within the *parsha* of *teruma* in this sense. Yet if a non-Jew takes *teruma* on behalf of a Jew — *akum shetaram shel Yisrael afilu birshus ein terumasam terumah* — even if he was formally appointed, it does not work. So the non-Jew is in the *parsha* but cannot be a *shaliach*.

### Gam Atem

What is the additional criterion? The *gemara* derives it from the *pasuk*: *kein tarimu gam atem* — even you should take *teruma*. The *gam* expands to include the *shaliach*; the *atem* restricts the *shaliach* to be like the principal. *Mah atem Yisrael af sheluchachem Yisrael* — just as you are Jewish, so must your agent be Jewish. This requirement — that the *shaliach* be like the principal in relevant respects — is the second criterion.

### Two Criteria

There are thus two independent requirements for valid *shlichut*: first, the *shaliach* must be a *ben brit*, within the covenantal framework of obligated persons; second, the *shaliach* must be within the *parsha* — the specific domain of law governing the transaction. A non-Jew fails the first criterion and therefore cannot be a *shaliach* for anything involving Jewish law, regardless of whether the non-Jew is within the *parsha*. A slave satisfies the first criterion but may fail the second, depending on the transaction.

### Bnei Brit Key

The school of Rabbi Yannai — *d'bei Rabbi Yannai* — refines the formulation: *mah atem bnei brit af sheluchachem bnei brit* — it is not *Yisrael* that is the criterion, but *bnei brit*, members of the covenant. This is a somewhat broader category. A slave, male at least, has undergone *berit milah* and is bound by *mitzvot* in the relevant ways.

### Eved Bnei Brit

The implication is that slaves are *bnei brit*: they are circumcised and bound by the covenant of *mitzvot*. *Rashi* on this passage emphasizes both elements — *mehulin uvo'ei bivrit mitzvot*, circumcised and having entered into the covenant of *mitzvot*. And support is found from the *pasuk* in *Bamidbar* 18: the *meichotev etzecha ad sho'ev meimecha* — your hewers of wood and drawers of water — are your slaves, and the continuation is *la'avarcha bivrit Hashem Elokecha* — to bring you into the covenant of the Lord your God. The slaves are part of the *brit*. This raises, in passing, an interesting question about *areivut* and the degree to which slaves are part of the *am* in an extended sense.

With this understanding: a non-Jew can never be a *shaliach* because the *gam atem* requirement — *bnei brit* — excludes him. A slave can be a *shaliach* for matters within his *parsha*, such as *terumot* and *ma'asrot*, because he satisfies the *bnei brit* requirement and is within that *parsha*. But a slave cannot be a *shaliach* for *gittin vekiddushin*, because the laws of *gittin* and *kiddushin* do not apply to him — he lacks *kiddushin* entirely — and he therefore fails the second criterion.

### Rabbi Chiya

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states the ruling explicitly: *ein eved na'aseh shaliach lekabbel get isha* — a slave cannot be a *shaliach* to receive a *get* for a woman. The reason coheres with the two-criteria framework: while the slave satisfies *bnei brit*, he is not *betoras gittin vekiddushin* — not within the domain of the laws of divorce and marriage — and therefore cannot represent a principal in a transaction governed by those laws.

The daf closes with this ruling in place. The subsequent sugya, to be continued, addresses a further point arising from the slave's distinct status — an aspect of the discussion that belongs to the next session.
