## Gilui & Filtering

### Spilled Liquid Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״אַצִּיל אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ, וְאַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִי דְּמֵי שֶׁלִּי״ – חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But if** the owner of the wine **said to** the owner of the honey: **I will salvage your** honey **and you will pay me the value of my** wine, the owner of the honey is **obligated to pay him** compensation for the wine.

The daf begins, technically speaking, on 115b, approximately halfway down the page, at the word *bishlema*. The broader discussion concerns a case where a barrel or pitcher breaks and its contents — honey, wine, or oil — are going to waste. A *braita* has been cited holding that such liquid cannot serve as *terumat ma'aser*, because once liquid is flowing out and cannot be recovered, it is effectively *hefker*. The *Gemara* then struggled to reconcile this with several sources that appear to treat such property as still belonging to its owner: a *Mishnah* holding that the honey of a breaking barrel still belongs to the owner; another source permitting the transfer of *kedushah* of *ma'aser sheni* onto money being seized by a robber; and a *braita* permitting *terumat ma'aser* from *tamei* wine or oil that is in the process of spilling, the only limiting factor being whether designating it would cause a loss to the *kohein*.

The *Gemara* resolved all of these cases by explaining that in each instance the liquid is not yet entirely lost — the barrel may have netting that slows the breakage, or the robber's seizure may still be reversible — and therefore none of those cases involves true *hefker*.

### Gilui & Snake Poison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא חַזְיָא לְזִילּוּף, וְהָתַנְיָא: מַיִם שֶׁנִּתְגַּלּוּ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִשְׁפְּכֵם בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; וְלֹא יְגַבֵּל בָּהֶן אֶת הַטִּיט; וְלֹא יְרַבֵּץ בָּהֶן אֶת הַבַּיִת; וְלֹא יַשְׁקֶה מֵהֶם אֶת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ, וְלֹא בֶּהֱמַת חֲבֵירוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the exposed wine is **fit for sprinkling,** in order to spread its pleasant aroma, **isn't it taught** otherwise in the following *baraita*? With regard to **water that was exposed, one may not pour it** out **in the public domain, nor mix clay with it, nor settle** dust **with it** by sprinkling it in **a house, nor give it to his animal to drink, nor** give it **to the animal of another.** This proves that exposed liquids may not be used even for sprinkling.

With that framework established, the *Gemara* turns to a new difficulty. The case of *tamei* wine that is spilling has been permitted *lechatchila* — because *tamei terumah* wine, usable only for air freshening (*ziluf*), is of little enough value that designating it as *terumat ma'aser* causes no meaningful loss to the *kohein*. But what about wine that has been left uncovered? The *Gemara* raises the problem of *gilui*: the concern, accepted in the rabbinic period, that a snake may have drunk from uncovered liquid and deposited venom in it, rendering it dangerous. If the wine was *nisgalta* — left exposed — *lemai chazya*? What use is it at all?

One might answer that even after *gilui* the wine retains its value for *ziluf*, since that was the use of *tamei terumah* wine to begin with. But a *braita* closes off this possibility. Uncovered water (*mayim shenitgalu*) is subject to a series of prohibitions: one may not spill it in the public domain lest a barefoot person walk through it and absorb the venom through a wound; one may not use it to mix plaster; one may not sprinkle it to settle dust in a house; one may not give it to one's own animal or another's animal to drink, lest the animal be harmed or lest one later eat the animal's flesh. Given that even vapor contact is prohibited, the wine would seem to have no value whatsoever — and therefore could not become *terumat ma'aser*.

### Sieve Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּעַבַּר לָהּ בִּמְסַנֶּנֶת, כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – דְּתַנְיָא: מְסַנֶּנֶת, יֵשׁ בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי. אָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַתַּחְתּוֹנָה מְגוּלָּה, אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַתַּחְתּוֹנָה מְכוּסָּה – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהָעֶלְיוֹנָה מְגוּלָּה, אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי; לְפִי שֶׁאֶרֶס שֶׁל נָחָשׁ דּוֹמֶה לִסְפוֹג, וְצָף וְעוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The wine is fit for use **because he passed it through a strainer,** and it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A vessel covered with **a strainer is subject to** the *halakha* of **exposure** if the vessel is left unsupervised. **Rabbi Neḥemya said: When** is this so? It is so **when the lower vessel,** in which the liquid collects after passing through the strainer, **is exposed. But when the lower vessel is covered, even if the upper vessel is exposed, it is not subject to** the *halakha* of **exposure, because the venom of a snake is like a sponge** in that **it floats and stays in place.**

The *Gemara* resolves this by explaining that the wine is passed through a *mistanena* — a sieve or filter, similar in concept to two vessels stacked one atop the other with a screen between them. The *Gemara* specifies that this follows the position of *Rabbi Nechemya*.

A *braita* is cited that discusses this very apparatus: ordinarily, having such a sieve setup does not solve the problem of *gilui*, because the sieve itself does not eliminate the venom. *Rabbi Nechemya* limits this ruling. When is the sieve system still problematic? Only when the bottom vessel is exposed — in which case the snake could drink from that vessel directly. But if the bottom vessel is covered, even though the top vessel is exposed, there is no concern of *gilui*. The reason: *le'fi she'eres shel nachash dome lisfog* — the venom of a snake behaves like a sponge; it floats at the top of the liquid and does not sink through. Therefore, the filter will catch the venom along with the other surface material, and the liquid that passes through is safe. This is why the wine, even after *gilui*, can still have value and can serve as *terumat ma'aser*.

### Pouring Pressure Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי סִימוֹן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא טְרָקוֹ, אֲבָל טְרָקוֹ – אָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **Wasn't it stated with regard to** Rabbi Neḥemya's ruling that **Rabbi Simon says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: They taught** Rabbi Neḥemya's ruling **only where one did not mix it, but** if **one mixed** the wine, any venom found in it becomes capable of penetrating the strainer? Consequently, the wine remains **forbidden** even if it is passed through a strainer.

A complication is immediately raised. It was taught in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*, transmitted by *Rabbi Simon*, that the leniency of the *mistanena* applies only when the liquid was not agitated or poured forcefully — *shelo tarfo*. If it was poured with force (*tarfo*), the velocity of the liquid would drive the venom through the filter even if it would otherwise float at the top. In that case, *assur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי, אֶפְשָׁר דְּמַנַּח מִידֵּי אַפּוּמָּא דְחָבִיתָא, (דְּשָׁפֵי) [וְשָׁפֵי] לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There, too, it is possible** to prevent the venom from mixing with the wine **by placing something upon the mouth of a barrel that will filter it** slowly so that the venom will not penetrate the strainer, and the wine may therefore be used.

The *Gemara* resolves that this is not a problem in the case before us. Just as one ordinarily sets up the *mistanena* system in a gradual and gentle manner — letting the liquid seep slowly through the filter rather than pouring it with force — so too here, one can arrange the barrel so that its contents flow out gently across the mouth of the vessel. The answer is thus: *hacha nami d'efshar demaniach midi apuma dechavisa* — one can place something at the mouth of the barrel so that the wine trickles out softly, without the pressure that would push the venom through the screen.

With that, the wine remains usable and the designation as *terumat ma'aser* is valid. The remaining question is why this process — simple enough in principle — disqualifies oil from being designated *lechatchila*. The answer, as we shall see, is that passing the oil through a sieve is genuinely burdensome. Giving the *kohein* oil that he must first strain carefully through such an apparatus is a real imposition, and therefore it constitutes a *hefsed kohein* that forbids the designation *lechatchila*, though it remains valid *bedieved*.

### Demai Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – (מִטָּמֵא אַטָּמֵא) מִי תָּרְמִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** It has now been established that the *baraita*, which states that if one's barrel of untithed, impure wine is breaking, he may separate it as *teruma* of the tithe for other barrels of untithed, impure wine, may be explained in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya. The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Neḥemya, may we separate *teruma*** from impure produce for other impure produce?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: תּוֹרְמִין מִן הַטָּמֵא עַל הַטָּמֵא, וּמִן הַטָּהוֹר עַל הַטָּהוֹר, וּמִן הַטָּהוֹר עַל הַטָּמֵא; אֲבָל לֹא מִן הַטָּמֵא עַל הַטָּהוֹר. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: אַף מִן הַטָּמֵא עַל הַטָּמֵא לֹא הִתִּירוּ לִתְרוֹם, אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁל דְּמַאי! הָכָא נָמֵי בְּשֶׁל דְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **One may separate *teruma* from** produce that is **impure for** other produce that is **impure, and from** produce that is **pure for** other produce that is **pure, and from** produce that is **pure for** produce that is **impure, but not from** produce that is **impure for** produce that is **pure. Rabbi Neḥemya says: Even** with regard to separating **from** produce that is **impure for** produce that is **impure,** the Sages **permitted one to separate *teruma*** in this manner **only in the case of doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*].** Consequently, how can the aforementioned *baraita* unequivocally permit one to separate impure wine as *teruma* of the tithe for other impure wine? The Gemara answers: **Here too,** in the *baraita*, it is discussing **a case of *demai*.**

Now the *Gemara* notes an internal tension. The solution invoking *Rabbi Nechemya* and the *mistanena* addresses how the wine retains value. But it creates a different problem: according to *Rabbi Nechemya*, one cannot separate *tamei* wine as *terumat ma'aser* on *tamei* grain even in ordinary circumstances, since he holds that even from *tamei* to *tamei*, one must still give the *kohein* *trumah tehorah* wherever possible. The entire *braita* permitting *terumat ma'aser* from *tamei* wine on *tamei* grain — which is what we have here — seems to contradict *Rabbi Nechemya*'s own stated position.

The *braita* on this topic reads: *tormin min hatame al hatame* — one may separate *tamei* terumah on *tamei* grain; *min hatahor al hatahor* — and *tahor* terumah on *tahor* grain; *min hatahor al hatame* — and *tahor* terumah on *tamei* grain. But *lo min hatame al hatahor* — not *tamei* on *tahor*, since that would deprive the *kohein* of something of superior value. *Rabbi Nechemya* goes further: even from *tamei* to *tamei*, separation is not generally permitted — the only exception is *demai*, produce purchased from an *am haaretz* whose tithes are of uncertain status.

The *Gemara* resolves the conflict cleanly: *hacha nami beshel demai* — if one wishes the entire discussion to follow *Rabbi Nechemya*, then read the *braita* as referring specifically to *demai*. *Rabbi Nechemya* agrees that *tamei* may be taken on *tamei* in the case of *demai*, and it is precisely that case that the *braita* addresses. The resolution is consistent and the attribution holds.

## Oil vs. Wine Loss

### Comparative Values

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שֶׁנִּשְׁבְּרָה – חַזְיָא, אֶלָּא נִתְגַּלְּתָה – לְמַאי חַזְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** Granted, this rationale applies to a barrel **that was broken,** as the remaining contents are still **fit** for use, **but** with regard to a barrel that was **exposed, for what** use **is it fit?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וּבְשֶׁמֶן לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן, מִפְּנֵי הֶפְסֵד כֹּהֵן. מַאי שְׁנָא שֶׁמֶן – דְּרָאוּי לְהַדְלִיק? יַיִן נָמֵי רָאוּי לְזִילּוּף! וְכִי תֵּימָא זִילּוּף לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: שׁוֹתִין מִלּוֹג בְּסֶלַע, וּמְזַלְּפִין מִלּוֹג בִּשְׁתַּיִם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the continuation of the *baraita* cited previously: **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **But with regard to oil, he should not do so, due to the priest's loss.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about a leaking barrel of impure **oil,** which one may not separate as *teruma*, because if he does so the priest will suffer a loss? That case is different **because** the impure *teruma* oil **is fit** to be used **to kindle** a fire, and the priest will now not receive that oil. The Gemara challenges: But **wine is also fit** to be used, as the priest is able **to sprinkle** it in order to impart a pleasant aroma. Why is there is no concern that the priest will suffer a loss? **And if you would say** that **sprinkling is nothing,** i.e., it is insignificant, **doesn't Shmuel say in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya: One drinks from a *log*** of wine worth one ***sela*, and one sprinkles from a *log*** of wine worth **two** *sela*, which indicates that there is greater benefit in sprinkling wine than in drinking it?

With the theoretical framework established, the *Gemara* turns to articulate why the oil case is categorically different from the wine case. The *braita* stated: *uvshemen lo yaaseh kein mipnei hefsed kohein* — regarding oil, one may not do this because it causes a loss to the *kohein*. The *Gemara* asks: *mai shna shemen*? What is distinctive about oil? The answer: *dechazi l'hadlaka* — even *tamei* oil can be used by the *kohein* for illumination, for his oil lamps. That is a genuine and significant use. Therefore, receiving damaged, exposed oil that must be filtered is a real loss.

But this raises an immediate challenge. *Yayin nami chazi l'ziluf* — wine is also usable, as an air freshener. Is that not also a real benefit? One might dismiss this, noting that *ziluf* sounds like a trivial use. But the *Gemara* cites *Shmuel* in the name of *Rabbi Chiyya*: for drinking purposes, one should be prepared to pay one *sela* per *log*; for *ziluf* purposes, one should buy higher-quality wine and pay two *slaim* per *log*. So *ziluf* is not a trivial use — it is actually valued more highly than ordinary drinking wine in terms of the quality one seeks. Why then is there no *hefsed kohein* even in the wine case?

### Takalah Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּחָדָשׁ. וְהָא רָאוּי לְיַשְּׁנוֹ! אָתֵי בֵּיהּ לִידֵי תַּקָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with new** wine that has not yet developed an aroma and is therefore not fit for sprinkling. The Gemara counters: **But isn't it fit to** allow it to **age** so that it will become fit for sprinkling? The Gemara responds: If one waits for it to age, he might **come to experience a mishap with it,** as he will forget that it is impure *teruma*, which is forbidden for consumption.

The *Gemara* answers: *hacha bemai askinan bechadash* — we are speaking of new wine. New wine is not yet aromatic enough to serve effectively as *ziluf*, and therefore has minimal value for that purpose. But the *Gemara* immediately presses further: *v'ha chazi l'yashon* — but new wine can become old wine! If the *kohein* simply holds onto it, it will age and become valuable. Surely the *kohein* is being deprived of something of genuine future worth.

The *Gemara* responds: *ati bah lidei takalah* — we would not permit the *kohein* to hold onto *tamei* wine for an extended period, because of the concern that he might eventually forget it is *tamei* and come to drink from it accidentally. Therefore, we would require the *kohein* to use it immediately. A wine that must be used immediately and cannot be stored has very little market value, and so there is no meaningful *hefsed kohein* in the wine case.

### Vessel Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמֶן נָמֵי אָתֵי בֵּיהּ לִידֵי תַּקָּלָה! דְּמַנַּח [לֵיהּ] בִּכְלִי מָאוּס. יַיִן נָמֵי מַנַּח לֵיהּ בִּכְלִי מָאוּס! הַשְׁתָּה לְזִילּוּף קָא בָּעֵי לֵיהּ, בִּכְלִי מָאוּס קָא מַנַּח לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Oil** should **also** be subject to the concern that one might **come to experience a mishap with it,** as he might forget that it is forbidden and mistakenly drink it. The Gemara explains: The halakha is **that** the priest must **place** the oil **in a foul vessel,** and there is no concern that he will consume it, as such oil is used only for kindling. The Gemara asks: **Let the wine also be placed in a foul vessel,** in which case there would be no concern that he would drink it. The Gemara responds: **Now that he desires** to use **it for sprinkling, would he place it in a foul vessel?** This would ruin its aroma. Consequently, the wine may not be kept due to concern over a possible mishap, while the oil may be kept.

The *Gemara* then asks: *shemen nami ati lidei takalah* — but the same concern of *takalah* should apply to oil as well, should it not? If the *kohein* stores the *tamei* oil for a long time, he might forget and come to eat with it.

The *Gemara* answers: *demanach bichli maus* — oil can be stored in a repulsive vessel. Since *tamei* oil is used only for fuel, the *kohein* would naturally store it in a disgusting container, one that he would never use for eating. The repulsive nature of the vessel itself serves as a constant reminder and removes any concern of accidental consumption.

But could one not do the same with wine — store it in a repulsive vessel and thereby allow it to age? The *Gemara* dismisses this: *hashta l'ziluf kabaei lei bichli maus kamanach lei*? If you want to use the wine for air freshening because of its pleasant aroma, you are simply not going to store it in a disgusting vessel. The very purpose for which the wine is being preserved — its fragrance — makes it impossible to use the solution that works for oil. Therefore, wine cannot be stored safely as *tamei terumah*, must be used immediately, has minimal value, and thus presents no *hefsed kohein*.

## Shammai/Hillel Debate

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַקָּלָה עַצְמָהּ תַּנָּאֵי הִיא – דְּתַנְיָא: חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה, שֶׁנִּטְמֵאת – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: תִּשָּׁפֵךְ הַכֹּל, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: תֵּעָשֶׂה זִילּוּף. אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֲנִי אַכְרִיעַ; בַּבַּיִת – תֵּעָשֶׂה זִילּוּף, וּבַשָּׂדֶה – תִּשָּׁפֵךְ הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** the concern with regard to **a mishap itself is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a barrel of *teruma* wine that became impure** and is therefore forbidden for consumption, **Beit Shammai say:** The wine must **all be poured out** in order to prevent a possible mishap. **And Beit Hillel say:** Let the wine **be used** for **sprinkling. Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: I will decide** the *halakha* by proposing an intermediate opinion: If the wine became impure **in a house, it may be used** for **sprinkling,** as it can be used immediately, **but** if it became impure **in the field, it must all be poured out,** as it is possible that by the time it is brought to the house and used for sprinkling, someone may drink it.

The *Gemara* observes that the question of *takalah* — the concern of accidental consumption of *tamei terumah* — is itself the subject of a *tannaitic* debate, which takes us to the top of 116a. The *braita*: *chavis shel yayin shel trumah shenitmeis* — a barrel of *terumah* wine that became *tamei*. *Beit Shammai* says *tishafeich hakol*: it must all be spilled out, for we fear the *kohein* will come to drink it. *Beit Hillel* says *taaseh ziluf*: it may be used for *ziluf*, as an air freshener.

*Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yose* then offers a nuanced position, saying *ani achriya* — I will adjudicate. His first version of the ruling: if the wine is in the house, use it for *ziluf*, because it is readily at hand and one will use it right away without risk of forgetting. But if it is out in the field, it must be spilled, because by the time one schleps it to the house, there is a risk of forgetting it is *tamei* along the way. A second version of his ruling, reported as *ika d'amri*, drew the line differently: *beyashan taaseh ziluf, bechadash tishpocheihu hakol* — old wine may be used for *ziluf*, but new wine must be spilled. New wine is too weak as an air freshener and might be left to age; by the time it is old enough to use, the *kohein* may have forgotten its status and come to drink it. Old wine, used immediately, presents no such concern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּיָשָׁן – תֵּעָשֶׂה זִילּוּף, וּבְחָדָשׁ – תִּשָּׁפֵךְ הַכֹּל. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין הַכְרָעָה שְׁלִישִׁית מַכְרַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that he decided the *halakha* as follows: **With regard to aged** wine, **it may be used** for **sprinkling,** as it is fit to be sprinkled immediately, **but with regard to new** wine, which does not yet have a pleasant aroma, **it must all be poured out** in order to prevent a possible mishap. **They said to** Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei: **A decision** by **a third** party **does not** conclusively **decide** the *halakha*.

### Third Opinion Rule

*Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* respond to *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yose*: *ein hachra'a shelishit machra'at* — a third opinion does not adjudicate between the original two. This requires explanation, since his position seems to be a reasonable middle path that accounts for real distinctions between cases.

*Rashi* explains that a third opinion carries adjudicatory weight only when it operates within a framework implicitly recognized by both of the original disputants. That is, if *Beit Shammai* had said "spill it in all cases — in the house and in the field, old and new" and *Beit Hillel* had said "use it for *ziluf* in all cases," this would imply that both schools recognized the distinction between house and field, or between old and new, but each chose to rule consistently in one direction regardless. In that scenario, *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yose* would be selecting from the very categories the disputants had already considered, ruling like *Beit Hillel* in some cases and like *Beit Shammai* in others — and that constitutes a genuine *hachra'a*.

But here, *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* issued categorical rulings without reference to any contextual distinctions. Neither school acknowledged that house versus field, or new versus old, should affect the outcome. *Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yose* is not choosing between their positions in context-sensitive cases — he is introducing an entirely new framework that neither school considered. That is not adjudication between two positions; it is a third independent position, and as such, it cannot serve as the *hachra'a* between them.

## Stipulated Payment

### Active Loss Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וּנְטָלוּהָ מַסִּיקִין, אִם מַכַּת מְדִינָה הִיא – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״; אִם מֵחֲמַת הַגַּזְלָן – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ שָׂדֶה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who robs a field from another and thugs [*massikin*]** subsequently **take** the field from the robber, the *halakha* is dependent upon the circumstances: **If it is a regional disaster** in which the thugs seize all the property in the region, the robber **says to** the owner of the field: **That which is yours is before you,** i.e., it is your prerogative to try to reclaim it from the thugs. No compensation is required since the thugs would have seized the property in any event. But **if** the thugs took that field alone **due to the robber,** the robber is **obligated to provide** the owner with **a different field.**

We return now to the *Mishnah*. The case: *im amar lo atzil et shelcha* — if the person explicitly stipulates, "I will save yours if you agree to compensate me for mine," then that agreement is binding, and the owner of the honey must pay for the wine that was spilled.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״אַצִּיל אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ וְכוּ׳״. אַמַּאי? וְנֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״מְשַׁטֶּה אֲנִי בָּךְ״!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **if** the owner of the wine **said to** the owner of the honey: **I will salvage your** honey and you will pay me the value of my wine, the owner of the honey is obligated to give him compensation for the wine. The Gemara asks: **Why** is the stipulation binding? **Let** the owner of the honey **say to him: I was** merely **fooling with you** when I accepted your condition, and I did not agree to it at all.

The *Gemara* asks: *amai*? Why should this be so? Why can the honey owner not respond afterward with *meshahta ani bach* — I was just speaking loosely, making an exaggerated promise under duress? One might simply say: because the agreement was made before the act, and the other party acted in reliance on it. But the *Gemara* frames the question more precisely by comparing it to a *braita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא תַּנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה בּוֹרֵחַ מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין וְהָיְתָה מַעְבּוֹרֶת לְפָנָיו, אָמַר לוֹ: ״טוֹל דִּינָר, וְהַעֲבִירֵנִי״ – אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׂכָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In a case **where one was fleeing from prison and there was a ferry before him,** and **he said to** the ferryman: **Take** this entire **dinar and take me across** the river, the ferryman **has** the right to collect **only his** usual **wage,** but not the entire dinar.

The *braita*: a prisoner was escaping from captivity — presumably a just prisoner, not a criminal one would wish returned — and there was a boat at the river's edge. He said to the ferryman: *tol dinar vehaavireni* — take a *dinar* and bring me across. The ferryman did so. The *halacha* is that afterward, when they come before a *beit din*, the prisoner need only pay the standard ferry fare. He can in effect say he was speaking under duress and only owes the market rate.

Why then, in our *Mishnah*, can the honey owner *not* say the same?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא דָּמֵי אֶלָּא לְסֵיפָא – וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״טוֹל דִּינָר זֶה בִּשְׂכָרְךָ, וְהַעֲבִירֵנִי״ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ מִשָּׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** case in the mishna **is not comparable** to the case cited above; **rather,** it is comparable **to the latter clause** of that same *baraita*, which states: **And if he said to** the ferryman: **Take this dinar as your wage and take me across** the river, the prisoner must **give him his wage in the full amount,** i.e., the entire dinar.

The *Gemara* answers: our case is not analogous to the opening clause (*reisha*) of the *braita* but to its concluding clause (*seifa*). The *seifa* states: *v'im amar lo tol dinar zeh besakharkha veha'avirani* — if he said "take this *dinar* as your fee and bring me across," then the full stipulated amount must be paid. The *Gemara* then probes what distinguishes *reisha* from *seifa*, since in both cases the ferryman simply ferried the man.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: בְּצַיָּיד הַשּׁוֹלֶה דָּגִים מִן הַיָּם, וְאָמַר לֵיהּ: אַפְסֵדְתַּנִי כְּווֹרֵי בְּזוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is different** in **the first clause,** which states that the ferryman receives only his regular wage, **and what is different** in **the latter clause,** which states that he receives the full dinar? **Rami bar Ḥama said:** The latter clause deals **with a trapper who scoops fish from the sea,** i.e., a fisherman, **and he** can **say to** the prisoner: **You have caused me a loss** of **fish [*kavrei*]** worth **a dinar,** which I would have caught had I not taken you across the river. Similarly, in the case in the mishna, the owner of the wine sustains a financial loss in order to save the honey, and therefore he is entitled to the compensation that the owner of the honey agreed to pay.

*Rav Bar Chama* explains the distinction in substantive terms: in the *seifa* case, the ferryman was actually a fisherman who was in the middle of catching fish. He said: *afsadeitani kavari bezzuza* — you caused me to lose my fish for that *zuz*. That is, he was incurring a real opportunity cost or actual financial loss by agreeing to ferry the man. When there is a genuine loss that the party is absorbing in reliance on the agreement, the stipulated price is binding. When there is no actual loss — merely a service that the other party happened to perform — the promise of an inflated payment does not obligate beyond the fair market value of the service.

This is quite a significant *chiddush*. One might have thought the distinction was merely formal — whether the payment was framed as a "reward" versus a "fee." But the *Gemara* roots the obligation in the substance of the transaction: if a real loss was incurred in reliance on the agreement, the stipulated amount is enforceable. If no real loss was incurred, even a formal promise of a large sum does not bind beyond the value of the service rendered. One might add that exposure to genuine risk would similarly support an elevated agreed-upon price — the point being that the law will not permit exploitative windfalls, but it will enforce agreements that reflect real financial sacrifice.

### Passive Loss Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁטַף נַחַל חֲמוֹרוֹ וַחֲמוֹר חֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁלּוֹ יָפֶה מָנֶה וְכוּ׳. וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן קַמַּיְיתָא, הָתָם הוּא דְּכִי פֵּירֵשׁ יָהֵיב לֵיהּ דְּמֵי כּוּלֵּיהּ, מִשּׁוּם דִּבְיָדַיִם קָא פָסֵיד; אֲבָל הָכָא דְּמִמֵּילָא, נֵימָא: אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׂכָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **a river washed away his donkey and the donkey of the other** man, and **his donkey is worth one hundred dinars,** etc. The Gemara notes: **And it** is **necessary** to teach both the case of the barrels of wine and honey and the case of the donkeys, **as if it** had **taught us** only **the first** case, that of the wine and honey, it might be said that **it is** only **there,** in that case, **that when** the owner of the wine **states** his stipulation, the owner of the honey **gives him the value of** the **entire** loss. Why is this? It is **because** the wine **is lost through** the direct **action** of its owner. **But here,** in the case of the donkeys, **since** the donkey is lost **by itself** in the river, **let us say:** The one who salvages the donkey of his fellow **has** the right to **only his wage,** i.e., compensation for his labor.

The *Gemara* then notes that both the wine-and-honey case and the two-donkey case were necessary to teach. If the *Mishnah* had presented only the wine case, one might have thought: *mishum deveyada kapasidei* — the stipulation is binding because I actively spilled out my wine based on your commitment. The active loss, directly incurred by my own hand at your behest, is what makes the agreement enforceable. But in the donkey case, the loss is passive — I simply refrained from saving my own donkey while I went to save yours. Perhaps in such a case, even with a prior stipulation, you should only owe me the market rate for my service, since you did not instruct me to take any active step that caused me loss. The *Mishnah* therefore teaches that passive loss too, when it is a genuine financial loss that occurred in the context of an agreed-upon arrangement, obligates payment of the stipulated sum.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן סֵיפָא, הָכָא הוּא דְּבִסְתָמָא אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׂכָרוֹ, מִשּׁוּם דְּמִמֵּילָא; אֲבָל הָתָם, דִּבְיָדַיִם, אֵימָא אֲפִילּוּ בִּסְתָמָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ דְּמֵי כּוּלֵּהּ; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the mishna had **taught us** only the case of **the latter clause,** it might be said: **It is here,** in the case of the donkeys, **that** if the intention of the one who saved the donkey was **unspecified,** i.e., he did not stipulate that the owner of the other donkey would compensate him for the loss of his own donkey, he **has** the right to **only his wage, because** the donkey is lost **by itself. But there,** where the barrel of wine is lost **through** the direct **action** of its owner, one might **say: Even in** a case where the intention of the owner of the wine was **unspecified,** the owner of the honey must **give him the value of** the **entire** barrel of wine. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, both cases are **necessary.**

Conversely, had the *Mishnah* presented only the donkey case, one might have thought: even in the wine case, without a prior stipulation, the person who actively spilled his wine should be compensated in full by the honey owner, since the loss was directly and visibly caused by the act of saving. The *Mishnah* teaches that this too is incorrect — without a stipulation, even actively incurred losses in the course of saving another's property yield only the standard labor fee. Both cases are therefore necessary.

### Lucky Outcome

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא מֵרַב: יָרַד לְהַצִּיל, וְעָלָה שֶׁלּוֹ מֵאֵלָיו, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשְּׁמַיָּא רְחִימוּ עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a relevant discussion. **Rav Kahana raised a dilemma before Rav:** If one **descended** into the river **to rescue** another's donkey instead of his own after stipulating that he would be compensated for the loss of his own donkey, **and his** own donkey **emerged** from the river **by itself, what is** the *halakha*? Is the rescuer still entitled to payment from the owner of the donkey that he saved, despite the fact that he did not suffer a monetary loss? Rav **said to him:** The rescuer is still compensated because it was **from Heaven** that **mercy was bestowed upon him,** and his good fortune does not affect the stipulation.

*Rav Kahana* posed a question to *Rav*: *yarad l'hatzil v'ala shelo me'elav mahu* — what if the person descended to save the other's donkey, succeeded in doing so, and then his own donkey happened to survive on its own? Is the honey owner still obligated to pay the full stipulated amount — including the value of the first person's donkey — even though that donkey was never actually lost?

*Rav* answered: yes. *Mishmaya rachimu alei* — from heaven they had mercy on him, and he simply got lucky. The stipulated price was agreed upon against the background of a foreseeable and genuine risk of loss. The agreement was not conditioned on the donkey actually dying; it was entered into because the risk was real and the sacrifice was undertaken. The honey owner's obligation was created at the moment of the agreement and the act of rescue. The fact that in the end no loss materialized does not retroactively dissolve the binding commitment.

### Rav Safra Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב סָפְרָא הֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל בִּשְׁיָירְתָּא, לַוִּינְהוּ הָהוּא אֲרִי. כֹּל לֵילְיָא קָא (שַׁדַּר) [שָׁדוּ] לֵיהּ חֲמָרָא דְּחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ, וְקָא אָכֵיל. כִּי מְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ דְּרַב סָפְרָא, (שַׁדַּר) [שְׁדָא] לֵיהּ חֲמָרֵאּ וְלָא אַכְלֵיהּ. קְדֵים רַב סָפְרָא וּזְכָה בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This **is like that** case where **Rav Safra was traveling in a caravan,** and **a certain lion** followed them, **accompanying them** along the way. **Every night,** the travelers **would send one of their donkeys to** the lion, **and** the lion **would eat** it. **When Rav Safra's time arrived** to provide the lion with prey, **he sent it** his **donkey but** the lion **did not eat it. Rav Safra went ahead and acquired it** back, as his donkey had become ownerless when he presented it to the lion. Although Rav Safra did not lose his donkey, he had fulfilled his obligation to provide the lion with a donkey, and his good fortune did not affect his obligation vis-à-vis the other travelers.

This principle is illustrated by a story involving *Rav Safra*, who was traveling with a caravan. A trained lion was accompanying and guarding the group, and each night the travelers would take turns providing the lion with a donkey to eat. When *Rav Safra*'s turn arrived, they sent his donkey to the lion — but the lion did not eat it. *Rav Safra* immediately went and re-took possession of his donkey.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִיזְכֵּי בֵּיהּ? נְהִי דְּכִי אַפְקְרֵיהּ – אַדַּעְתָּא דְאַרְיָה אַפְקְרֵיהּ, אַדַּעְתָּא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא אַפְקְרֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this episode, **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: Why** was it necessary **for him to acquire** the donkey again? **Although** Rav Safra **made it ownerless** by sending it to the lion, it was **made ownerless** only **with the intention that the lion** would consume it, but **it was not made ownerless with the intention that everyone** else could acquire it. Accordingly, no one else could have acquired the donkey.

*Rav Acha mi-Difti* asked *Ravina*: *lama li l'mizka bei*? Why did *Rav Safra* need to re-take possession? If he only gave the donkey up on the assumption the lion would eat it — *ada'ata d'arya afkrei, ada'ata d'kulei alma lo afkrei* — he had relinquished it with the expectation that the lion would consume it, not as a blanket renunciation of ownership to the world at large. If the lion did not eat it, his conditional relinquishment simply did not take effect. He should still own it automatically.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַב סָפְרָא לְרַוְוחָא דְמִילְּתָא הוּא דַּעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him: Rav Safra did** so **as an added precaution.**

*Rav Acha* answers: *d'milta hu d'avid* — it was a pragmatic act. Technically, *Rav Safra* remained the owner, since his giving-up of the donkey was conditional on the lion eating it. *Tosafot* notes that it was always understood this was not a certainty. But *Rav Safra* re-took formal possession so that no one else could claim a share or dispute his ownership. The practical act of *zechiyah* — re-acquisition — foreclosed any argument.

*Rashi* connects this back to the *Mishnah*: just as *Rav Safra* retained his ownership because his relinquishment was conditional, and still collected the benefit of his night's obligation being fulfilled even though his donkey survived, so too in the *Mishnah* case — the agreement stands and the stipulated sum is owed even when the anticipated loss did not materialize. It should be noted, however, that there is a complication: the scenario of abandoning one's own donkey to save another's may trigger the principle of *avudah mimenu umikol adam* — a loss so total that the property becomes *hefker* in everyone's eyes. *Rashi* understands the case in the *Mishnah* this way, meaning that the rescuer's donkey does in fact become *hefker*, and if his donkey survives he re-acquires it as ownerless property — while still collecting the stipulated amount for having taken on that risk.

### Failed Rescue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב מֵרַבִּי: יָרַד לְהַצִּיל וְלֹא הִצִּיל, מַהוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: וְזוֹ שְׁאֵילָה, אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׂכָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav raised a dilemma before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: If one **descended** into the river in order **to rescue** another's donkey instead of his own, **but** he **did not** manage to **rescue** it, **what is** the *halakha*? Does the stipulation still apply so that the owner of the donkey he attempted to rescue must compensate him for the loss of his own donkey? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: And is this a question?** It is clear that **he has** the right to collect **only his wage,** but he is not entitled to the value of his own donkey.

*Rav* addressed a further question posed to him by *Rebbe*: *yarad l'hatzil v'lo hitzil mahu* — what if the person went to save the other's donkey and failed? Does he still collect the stipulated amount — including the reimbursement for his own lost donkey — in addition to his basic labor fee?

*Rebbe* responded: *vezo she'eilah*? Is this even a question? *Ein lo ela secharo* — he gets only the standard labor fee for the effort expended. The stipulated compensation for the loss of his own donkey was understood to be contingent on actually succeeding in the rescue, not merely on making the attempt. The agreement was: "I will save yours, and in return you will compensate me for mine." If he did not save the other's donkey, he did not fulfill his end of the bargain, and the enhanced compensation does not apply.

## Cabbage Plums Case

### Unnecessary Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל לְהָבִיא כְּרוּב וְדוּרְמַסְקָנִין לַחוֹלֶה, וְהָלַךְ וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁמֵּת אוֹ שֶׁהִבְרִיא – נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ מִשָּׁלֵם!
>
> **Translation:** Rav **raised an objection to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling from a *baraita*: In a case of **one who hires a laborer to bring cabbage and plums to an ill** person, **and he went and found that** the patient had already **died or recovered,** the employer nevertheless **gives** the worker **his entire wage.** Evidently, one is paid for his efforts even if the desired result is not accomplished. Accordingly, the rescuer's stipulation should apply, and one should be obligated to pay another for attempting to salvage his donkey even if he did not actually succeed in doing so.

A challenge is raised from a *braita*. If someone hired a worker to bring him cabbage and plums for a sick person (*choleh*), at an agreed-upon premium because these ingredients were difficult to obtain, and the worker went and retrieved them — but upon returning found that the patient had died or had already recovered — *noten lo secharo mishalem*: the full stipulated wage must be paid. Here too, the worker's efforts turned out to be unnecessary, just as the failed rescue was unnecessary. Yet full payment is owed. Does this not contradict *Rebbe*'s ruling?

### Success Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם עָבֵיד שָׁלִיחַ שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ, הָכָא לָא עָבֵיד שָׁלִיחַ שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Are** those two cases **comparable? There,** in the case of the ill person, **the agent performed his assignment,** as he procured and delivered the foods to the ill person, but **here,** in the case of the donkeys, **the agent did not perform his assignment,** as he was unable to rescue the animal from the river.

The distinction is immediate and clear. *Hatam avad shelichuteih, hacha lo avad shelichuteih* — in the case of the cabbage and plums, the worker did exactly what he was commissioned to do. He brought the cabbage and plums. The fact that the patient subsequently died or recovered is entirely outside the worker's control and entirely unrelated to his performance. He completed his *shlichut* — his assigned task — and is entitled to the full agreed price.

In the case of the failed rescue, the worker did not complete the task he had agreed to. The stipulated sum was not merely compensation for effort or for the risk assumed; it was compensation premised on actually retrieving the other's donkey. Attempting and failing to do so does not fulfill the agreement, and therefore only the standard labor fee applies.

## Ransom & Guild Rules

### Ransom Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁיָירָא שֶׁהָיְתָה מְהַלֶּכֶת בַּמִּדְבָּר, וְעָמַד עָלֶיהָ גַּיִיס לְטוֹרְפָהּ – מְחַשְּׁבִין לְפִי מָמוֹן, וְאֵין מְחַשְּׁבִין לְפִי נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the episode involving Rav Safra, the Gemara discusses the division of expenses among travelers in a caravan. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a caravan that was traveling in the desert and a troop** of bandits **stood over it** in order **to** attack and **pillage** it, and the members of the caravan agreed to pay ransom to the bandits, they **calculate** each traveler's contribution **according to** the amount of **money** he is carrying, **and** they **do not calculate according to** the number of **souls** in the caravan, i.e., the expense is not divided equally among the travelers.

The *Gemara* continues with a series of related *beraitot* concerning communal distribution of losses. A caravan traveling through the desert was set upon by troops demanding ransom. *Mechashevin lefi mamon v'ein mechashevin lefi nefashot* — the ransom is divided proportionally according to wealth, not per capita. This is logical: the brigands attacked for money, and in the absence of a ransom they would have taken money proportional to what each person was carrying or owned. The payout should mirror the exposure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם שָׂכְרוּ תַּיָּיר הַהוֹלֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם – מְחַשְּׁבִין אַף לְפִי נְפָשׁוֹת; וְלֹא יְשַׁנּוּ מִמִּנְהַג הַחַמָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **But if they hired a scout who travels in front of them** and leads them safely through the desert, the travelers **calculate** each one's contribution to paying the scout's wages **even according to** the number of **souls. And they should not differ from the** usual **custom of donkey drivers,** who often have set procedures for such situations.

If, however, the caravan had hired a guide or scout to lead them through the desert — protecting them both from getting lost and from attack — then *mechashevin af lefi nefashot*: the cost of the guide is divided both by wealth and by person. The guide is performing a dual service: protecting money and protecting lives. The fee should therefore be split into two components, one allocated by wealth and one allocated equally per capita, since each traveler derives equal benefit from the protection of his life regardless of how much money he carries. The precise arithmetic is left implicit, but the principle is that both factors must enter the calculation.

All of this, the *braita* adds, applies in the absence of an explicit stipulation or an established local custom. *V'lo yeshano miminhag hachamarim* — one does not deviate from the practice of the donkey drivers' guild. If there is a standard practice for how such costs are divided, that governs.

### Donkey Guild

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַשָּׁאִין הַחַמָּרִין לְהַתְנוֹת שֶׁכׇּל מִי שֶׁיֹּאבַד לוֹ חֲמוֹרוֹ נַעֲמִיד לוֹ חֲמוֹר אַחֵר. בְּכוּסְיָא – אֵין מַעֲמִידִין, שֶׁלֹּא בְּכוּסְיָא – מַעֲמִידִין לוֹ. וְאִם אָמַר: ״תְּנוּ לִי, וַאֲנִי אֶשְׁמוֹר״ – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The donkey drivers are permitted to stipulate that** with regard to **anyone whose donkey becomes lost,** the caravan **will provide him** with **a different donkey.** If the donkey was lost **through negligence, they do not provide him** with another donkey, but if it was lost **not through negligence, they provide him** with another donkey. **And if** the individual whose donkey became lost **said: Give me** money instead of the donkey **and I will guard** the caravan along with everyone else, **they do not listen to him.**

Similarly, *rashaim hachamarim l'hatnot shekol mi sheyovad lo chamor yaamid lo chamor acher* — donkey drivers are permitted to stipulate as a group that if any member loses his donkey in the ordinary course of business, the others will provide him with a replacement. This is a form of mutual insurance, and once the guild has agreed to it, the stipulation is binding on all members.

However, *b'chusia ein maamidim lo* — if the donkey was lost through the negligence (*chusia*) of its owner, the others are not required to replace it. Only non-negligent losses trigger the mutual obligation. *Shelo b'chusia maamidim lo* — where there was no negligence, they must provide the replacement.

### Shared Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִית לֵיהּ חֲמָרָא אַחֲרִינָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָא קָא מְינַטַּר לֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – שָׁאנֵי נְטִירוּתָא דְחַד מִנְּטִירוּתָא דְבֵי תְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious? No,** it is **necessary** to teach this for a case **where he** already **has another donkey, lest you say** that **he will guard** the caravan **for its** sake. Since he still owns one donkey, he never lost his incentive to guard the caravan, and the other drivers should therefore give him money if he wishes. The *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that **guarding one** donkey **is different from guarding two** donkeys, and the driver will be more likely to guard the caravan properly if they supply him with another donkey.

If the member whose donkey was lost then says: "Don't give me a replacement donkey — just give me the cash value and I will continue watching the herd," the guild need not agree to this. *Pshita*, says the *Gemara* — obvious. But then a subtlety: what if this person already owns another donkey? Perhaps he has skin in the game regardless, and one might think the cash equivalent is sufficient. The *Gemara* rejects this: *shinui netiruta d'chad minetiruta d'bei terei* — the quality of watching one donkey is different from watching two. The more assets a person has at stake, the more diligent he will be. The guild's insistence on providing a replacement donkey — not cash — is specifically designed to maximize each member's personal stake in protecting the herd. Giving cash removes one unit of self-interest and diminishes the collective security of the arrangement.

## Boat Jettison

### Weight Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: סְפִינָה שֶׁהָיְתָה מְהַלֶּכֶת בַּיָּם; עָמַד עָלֶיהָ נַחְשׁוֹל לְטוֹבְעָהּ, וְהֵקֵילּוּ מִמַּשָּׂאָהּ – מְחַשְּׁבִין לְפִי מַשּׂאוֹי, וְאֵין מְחַשְּׁבִין לְפִי מָמוֹן; וְלֹא יְשַׁנּוּ מִמִּנְהַג הַסַּפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another *baraita* that discusses the division of expenses among a group. **The Sages taught:** In a case of **a boat that was traveling on the sea** when **a gale arose** and threatened **to sink it, and** the sailors **lightened its load** by throwing some of their belongings overboard, the sailors **calculate** the amount that each must throw out **according to weight, but** they **do not calculate** it **according to** how much **money** the items are worth. **And they should not differ from the** usual **custom of sailors.**

A parallel *braita* addresses sea travel. A ship was at sea when a large wave threatened to capsize it, and cargo had to be thrown overboard to stabilize the vessel — an echo of the *Yona* narrative. How is the loss distributed among the passengers? *Mechashevin lefi masa v'ein mechashevin lefi mamon* — the jettisoned cargo is allocated by weight (*masa*), not by value (*mamon*). If one person's cargo weighs ten times as much as another's, ten times as much of his cargo should be thrown overboard, regardless of the relative monetary values involved. The reason is straightforward: it is weight, not value, that endangers the vessel. The distribution of sacrifice should correspond to the distribution of the danger created.

### Seafarer Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַשָּׁאִין הַסַּפָּנִים לְהַתְנוֹת, שֶׁכׇּל מִי שֶׁאָבְדָה לוֹ סְפִינָה נַעֲמִיד לוֹ סְפִינָה אַחֶרֶת. אָבְדָה לוֹ בְּכוּסְיָא – אֵין מַעֲמִידִין, שֶׁלֹּא בְּכוּסְיָא – מַעֲמִידִין לוֹ. וְאִי פֵּירַשׁ לְמָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַסְּפִינוֹת הוֹלְכִין – אֵין מַעֲמִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if a number of boats are traveling together, **the sailors are permitted to stipulate that** with regard to **anyone whose boat was lost,** the others **will provide him** with **a different boat.** If **his** boat **was lost through negligence,** the other sailors **do not provide** him with another boat, but if it was lost **not through negligence,** the sailors **provide him** with another boat. **And if** the boat **sailed to a place where the boats do not travel,** the other sailors **do not provide** him with a different boat.

Here too, *rashaim hasapanim l'hatnot shekol mi shetoavad lo sfina yaamid lo sfina acheret* — seafarers may stipulate as a guild that if any member's boat is lost, the others will replace it. The insurance here covers the boat itself, presumably not the cargo.

*Avda lo b'chusia ein maamidin* — if the boat was lost through the negligence of its captain or owner, the guild is not obligated to replace it. *Shelo b'chusia maamidin lo* — if the loss was not due to negligence, they must replace it. An additional exception applies in the maritime context that has no direct parallel in the donkey case: *v'i peirash limakom she'ein sfinos holchos ein maamidin* — if the captain sailed his boat to a location that boats do not normally travel, venturing beyond the standard routes, that deviation is treated as a form of negligence and the guild is similarly not obligated to replace the vessel.
