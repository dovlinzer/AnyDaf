# Bava Metzia 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ribbit Overview

### Introduction

With daf 71, we finally turn from the purely technical halakhic discussions — the distinctions between *Torah ribbit* and *d'rabbanan ribbit*, the gradations of *avak ribbit* — toward the broader, practical prohibitions associated with lending at interest. The gemara picks up at the bottom of 71a.

### Mishlei Verse

The gemara opens by quoting a verse from Mishlei (22:16): *marbe hono beneshech uvetarbit, lechonin dallim yikabtzenu* — one who increases his wealth through *neshech* and *tarbit*, through charging interest, will ultimately gather it for one who is gracious to the poor. The meaning is that even if you accumulate wealth through *ribbit*, in the end God will see to it that the money passes on to those who will give it to the poor.

The gemara asks: who exactly is this *chonin dallim*, the one gracious to the poor? Rav answers: like Shvor Malka, the Persian ruler. Rashi explains that the Persian king would tax the Jews and redistribute that wealth to the non-Jewish poor — precisely the irony of amassing wealth through interest. The money does not remain within the Jewish people; it is taxed away and redistributed to outsiders.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman then cites a teaching he received from Rav Huna: this applies not only to *ribbit* charged to Jews, but even to *ribbit* charged to non-Jews — an activity that the Torah seems at minimum to permit, and possibly even to encourage, as the verse states *lanachri tashich*. Nevertheless, the principle of divine redistribution applies even there.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava immediately challenges Rav Nachman: how can you suggest there is a problem with lending to non-Jews at interest when the Torah explicitly says *lanachri tashich* — you shall lend to the non-Jew with interest?

### Tashich Interpretation

At this point the gemara revisits an interpretive move that appeared earlier in the tractate: the word *tashich* is read not as "you shall lend" but as "you shall borrow." The gemara proposes that the verse is not condoning lending to the non-Jew with interest, but permitting borrowing from the non-Jew with interest.

The gemara then asks: if so, why does the Torah need to say this at all? We already know from the explicit prohibition *v'lachicha lo tashich* — do not borrow from your fellow Jew with interest — that borrowing from Jews is forbidden. So why state that you may borrow from a non-Jew? The answer is *la'avor b'lav aseh v'lo ta'aseh*: in order to add a positive commandment into the prohibition, so that one who borrows from a fellow Jew at interest transgresses both the negative prohibition and the positive commandment to borrow at interest only from a non-Jew.

This interpretive move, it must be said, is not the *pshat* of the verse in any straightforward sense. But the gemara is clearly motivated to read the *psukim* in a way that avoids the implication that the Torah openly endorses lending to non-Jews with interest. Why?

### Explicit Permission

The gemara is forced to confront an explicit teaching: *lovin mehen umalvin otan beribbit* — one may borrow from non-Jews and lend to non-Jews with interest. And moreover: *vechen beger toshav* — this applies even to a *ger toshav*, a resident alien who has accepted the seven Noahide commandments. The permission is unambiguous.

### Sustenance Limit

Given this explicit permission, Rav Chiya the son of Rav Huna draws a distinction: the permission is not unlimited. *Lo nitzrecha ella bichdei chayav* — the rabbis permitted it only to the extent of basic sustenance. You may lend to non-Jews with interest to earn a basic *parnassah*, but not to build great wealth through it. Critically, no one is claiming this is a *Torah* prohibition — the distinctions being drawn here are clearly rabbinic in nature.

## Rabbinic Concerns

### Rashi's Rationale

Rashi spells out the rabbinic concern. He writes: *bichdei chayav hittiru lo* — only to the extent of his sustenance did the rabbis permit, and *yoter mikan asur miderabbanan* — more than that is rabbinically prohibited. Why? *Dilma ati lemisrach* — perhaps one will become "dragged after" this behavior. There are two possible readings of this concern. One is that once you build a full-fledged business of lending at interest to non-Jews, the psychological and commercial logic of that enterprise will begin to erode the internal barrier against doing the same with Jews. You want to maintain a sense of hesitation, of limits, so that the prohibition against lending to Jews with interest remains felt as a genuine *issur* and not merely a technicality.

### Ravina's View

Ravina offers a different framing: the permission applies specifically to *talmidei chachamim* — Torah scholars. Why?

### Yelmed Mimaaasav

*Taama mai gazaru Rabbanan shema yilmad mimaasav* — the reason the rabbis restricted this practice is the fear of *yelmed mimaasav*, that one will learn from the non-Jew's ways. When a person's entire business consists of lending to non-Jews — where he cannot engage Jews as clients at all — his customer base is exclusively non-Jewish, which draws him socially into the non-Jewish world and risks pulling him away from Jewish observance and ethics. This concern is more specific than simple commerce with non-Jews; it is the situation where the entire orientation of one's livelihood points exclusively outward. Since a *talmid chacham* is grounded enough not to be pulled after those practices, the restriction does not apply to him.

### Tosafot Medieval

This raises a pressing question for the medieval period. The primary economic activity of Jews in Christian Europe — lending money with interest to non-Jews — was not a choice but a necessity. Jews were barred from owning land and excluded from most other professions. The Christian prohibition on lending to fellow Christians (a prohibition the Christians did take seriously, even post-Jesus) left Jews as the only available source of credit. How could the rabbinic restrictions discussed here be squared with the lived reality of European Jewish communities?

### Tosafot First Answer

Tosafot addresses this directly in his comments on *tashich* at the bottom of 71b. He presents several answers. His first answer is that we do not rule in accordance with this restriction at all. The statement of Rav Nachman citing Rav Huna — *afilu ribbit shel oved kochavim* — we will shortly see the gemara reread in an entirely different context. According to the second version (*lishon*) of that teaching that the gemara goes on to present, it was never said in the context of restricting lending to non-Jews with interest. Therefore, that restriction simply does not constitute the operative ruling.

### Tosafot Sustenance

Tosafot's second answer: even accepting the first *lishon* of the gemara, which does apply the restriction, there is still room to permit. *Le-fi she-yesh aleinu mas ha-melech ve-ha-sarim* — we bear heavy taxes from the king and the nobles. *Ve-ha-kol havei kedei chayeinu* — therefore, any money we can earn counts as basic sustenance. Given the crushing fiscal burdens on Jewish communities, even significant earnings are considered *kedei chayav*, the basic minimum needed to sustain the community. Perhaps the logic is that income flowing into any part of the community ultimately circulates and benefits the whole.

### Tosafot Exile

Tosafot's third answer cuts more deeply: *ve-od she-anu sheruyin bein ha-umot ve-ee efshar lanu le-histaker be-shum davar im lo nisa ve-nitan imahem* — we live among the nations and have no way of earning a livelihood through anything other than this commerce with them. The original restriction, Tosafot argues, was aimed at a situation where lending to non-Jews with interest was an active choice among available options — where a Jew could have conducted business within the Jewish community and instead chose to focus exclusively on non-Jews. In that situation, the concern about *yelmed mimaasav* is genuine, because you are choosing to orient yourself outward. But when there are no other options — when the entire structure of medieval society has foreclosed every other avenue — the restriction cannot apply. *Hilkach ein le-asor ribbit* — therefore there is no basis to prohibit it. All three of Tosafot's answers may operate together: we do not rule like the restriction; all income qualifies as basic sustenance; and in any case, the restriction only applied when equivalent alternatives existed.

## Lending Priorities

### Rav Yosef Hierarchy

The gemara now introduces a second *lishon* — an alternative reading of where Rav Huna's statement belongs. *Ika de-matni le-ha de-Rav Huna a-ha de-tani Rav Yosef* — some teach the statement of Rav Huna not on the context we just discussed, but on the following teaching of Rav Yosef, which is one of the most important sources in the laws of *tzedakah* and lending priorities.

The verse (Shemot 22:24) states: *im kesef talveh es ami es he'ani imach* — if you lend money to my nation, to the poor among you. Rav Yosef derives from the structure of this verse a hierarchy of obligations.

It is worth pausing here to note something significant about the Torah's approach to charity. Nowhere in the Torah is there an explicit commandment to give money outright to the poor. The Torah's language — *pasoach tiftach* and related verses — is consistently about lending, not giving. This preserves the borrower's dignity: he receives the funds in the framework of a loan, as an equal member of society who intends to repay. The Torah addresses the obvious problems with this structure — that loans create debt and generate interest — by instituting *shemitah* and the prohibition of *ribbit*. After seven years, unpaid loans are cancelled; interest is never charged. It is the ideal system: the dignity of borrowing, without the burdens typically associated with debt. This is why the rabbis interpret the Torah's lending verses as also encompassing obligations of *tzedakah* — the two are deeply intertwined.

### Nation Priority

From the phrase *ami* — my nation — the gemara derives: *ami venochri, ami kodem* — between my nation and the foreigner, my nation comes first.

### Need Priority

From *es he'ani* — the poor among you — the gemara derives: *ani ve'ashir, ani kodem* — between a poor person and a wealthy person, the poor person takes priority.

### Closeness Priority

From *imach* — with you — the gemara derives: *aniyecha ve'aniyei ircha, aniyecha kodmim* — your own poor before the poor of your city; *aniyei ircha ve'aniyei ir acheres, aniyei ircha kodmim* — the poor of your city before the poor of another city.

Two distinct criteria are operating simultaneously. One is degree of closeness — geographic proximity, relational proximity, and shared identity as coreligionists. The other is degree of need — poor versus wealthy. The *poskim* debate extensively how to weigh these two criteria against one another when they point in different directions. If a desperately poor person in another city is competing with a less-needy relative in your own family, which criterion governs? The gemara leaves this genuinely open, and different *poskim* take different positions.

One might also ask: does the priority framework imply any residual obligation to the non-Jew at all, or does *ami kodem* categorically exclude non-Jews from the calculus? The plain language of the *braita* suggests they are lower on the priority list, not entirely excluded. Ha'Meiri takes this seriously and says explicitly that there is some degree of obligation toward non-Jews — or at minimum an ethical obligation — though he qualifies this in different places. Most authorities, however, read the exclusion of non-Jews here as categorical rather than as a mere lower priority, given that the fund of available *tzedakah* resources is never exhausted: there will always be poor Jews whose needs are more pressing before one reaches the level of non-Jewish recipients.

These priorities are better understood as ethical guidelines than as the kind of precisely calibrated *halakha* one finds in, say, the laws of *kashrut*. This is deliberate. In the domain of *bein adam lachaveiro*, an excess of quantification risks converting what should be an exercise of ethical judgment into mere rule-following. The Shulchan Aruch cannot fully specify the obligations of compassion without evacuating them of their moral character.

### Ethics Philosophy

This raises a broader philosophical question: in areas of ethics and interpersonal obligation, the degree of specificity found in *halakhic* literature is markedly lower than in ritual domains. There are two reasons for this. One is simply that the complexity of human circumstances cannot be reduced to a finite set of criteria — context matters in ways that rules cannot anticipate. The other, more fundamental reason is that we want these *mitzvot* to remain genuine ethical acts. If the entirety of *tzedakah* behavior were specified in the Shulchan Aruch and one simply executed the prescribed formula, it would cease to be an exercise of moral judgment. The partial indeterminacy in this domain is not a failure of *halakhic* development but a feature of it.

### Rav Nachman Reinterpreted

The gemara returns to the statement of Rav Nachman citing Rav Huna and now relocates it entirely. *Lo nitzrecha deafilu lenochri beribbit uleyisrael bechinam* — the statement is not saying that lending to a non-Jew with interest is somehow problematic. Rather, it is saying that if one has the choice between lending to a non-Jew with interest — from which one will profit — and lending to a Jew for free, one must lend to the Jew. Even financial gain does not override the priority obligation. This is presumably not a mandate to drain one's entire lending portfolio in favor of interest-free Jewish loans; it addresses the ordinary individual who holds funds and must choose between a profitable deployment to a non-Jew and a free loan to a Jew. The Jew comes first. And this is the context — the *Rav Yosef* hierarchy of lending — in which Tosafot's preferred reading places the Rav Huna statement, removing it entirely from any restriction on lending to non-Jews with interest.

## Moral Blindness

### Rabbi Yosi Critique

Rabbi Yosi offers a sharp moral critique: *bo u'rei sumyos eineihem shel malvei b'ribis* — go and see the blindness of those who lend with *ribbit*. If someone were to call another person a *rasha*, the accused would be so outraged he would make the other's life miserable — no one wants that label. Yet the very same person is about to commit a flagrant *aveira* of lending with *ribbit*.

### Public Documentation

And not only is he committing this sin, but he makes a public production of it: he brings witnesses, a scribe, a quill, and ink — *heim mevi'im eidim v'lavlar v'kulmos v'dyo* — and writes and signs a formal document.

### Shtar Declaration

What he does not realize is what he is actually signing. The *shtar* amounts to a declaration: *ploni zeh kafar b'Elohei Yisrael* — this person denies the God of Israel. By publicly documenting his violation of an explicit Torah prohibition, he announces his own *resha'ut* in permanent written form before witnesses. Most sins, when committed, are at least done privately. The *ribbit* lender advertises his.

### Noda B'Yehuda

This calls to mind a story — attributed here to the Noda B'Yehuda, though the exact attribution is uncertain — about a man in a certain town who had lent money with interest to fellow Jews and accumulated great wealth at their expense. When he died, the *chevra kadisha* sought to charge his heirs an exorbitant price for a burial plot, on the theory that the community might at least recover something of what had been taken from them over the years. The heirs brought the matter to secular court, arguing that the standard price should apply. The Noda B'Yehuda was called to testify. His argument: the standard price is appropriate for ordinary Jews because Jews believe in *techiyas hameisim* — resurrection of the dead — and therefore a burial plot is essentially a rental, since the occupant will eventually depart. But, he continued, the gemara teaches that one who lends with *ribbit* has no share in *olam haba*. For this man, it is a permanent sale — and a permanent sale commands a higher price.

## Ribbit Consequences

### Rabbi Shimon

A *braita* is cited: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches — whoever has money and lends it without *ribbit*, of him the verse says (Tehillim 15:5): *kaspo lo nasan b'neshech* — he did not give his money with interest, *v'shochad al naki lo lakach* — and did not take a bribe against the innocent. *Oseh eileh lo yimmot l'olam* — one who acts this way will never stumble. The implication runs in the other direction as well: *kol hamalveh b'ribbit, nechasav mismotetin* — the property of one who lends with *ribbit* will ultimately collapse.

### Wealth Failure

The rhetorical purpose of this teaching is clear: if people are lending with *ribbit* because they believe it will make them wealthy, the appropriate response is to demonstrate that it will in fact destroy their wealth.

### Objection Raised

The gemara raises a challenge — interestingly, not the obvious one. One might have expected the gemara to challenge the claim by pointing to *ribbit* lenders who prosper. Perhaps that challenge was too dangerous to put in writing, since admitting the phenomenon openly might undermine the deterrent. Instead, the gemara challenges the converse: *veha chazinan delo mozfi beribis veka mismotetin* — we see people who do not lend with *ribbit* and yet they lose their money. If not lending with *ribbit* guaranteed financial stability, how do we account for innocent people who suffer financial ruin?

### Rabbi Elazar Reply

Rabbi Elazar answers: *halalu mismotetin veolin vehalalu mismotetin veeinan olin* — these cases are not symmetric. Those who do not lend with *ribbit* may experience financial setbacks, but their fortunes recover. Those who lend with *ribbit* suffer declines from which they never recover. The downturns are different in kind.

### Tzaddik Gamur

The gemara then cites a verse: *lama tabit bogdim, tacharish bevala rasha tzaddik mimennu* — why do You look on while the treacherous prosper; why are You silent when the wicked swallows one more righteous than he? Rav Huna comments: *tzaddik mimennu bolea, tzaddik gamur eino bolea* — the wicked can swallow someone who is righteous on a relative scale, but he cannot destroy someone who is entirely and objectively righteous. Rashi reads this as reinforcing the earlier principle: the truly righteous person's wealth may decline temporarily but will always recover. By limiting the guarantee to a *tzaddik gamur*, the teaching becomes both more defensible empirically and more demanding spiritually.

## Converts and Aliens

### Tannaitic Statement

A *braita* is now introduced: *Rebbi omer* — when the Torah mentions a *ger tzedek*, a righteous convert, it does so in the context of sale; when it mentions a *ger toshav*, a resident alien, it does so in the context of *ribbit*. Rebbi says he does not know how to read these properly into the relevant verses. The gemara proceeds to unpack this.

### Slave Sale Hierarchy

The verse (Vayikra 25:39) states: *vechi yamuch achicha imach venimkar lach* — when your brother becomes poor and is sold to you. This *parasha* follows immediately after the *parasha* of *shemita*, and the moral teaching the rabbis draw from the juxtaposition is this: if you violate *shemita* in order to get rich through forbidden means, you will ultimately be ruined — you will sell your field, then your house, then eventually yourself. The *parasha* traces an accelerating downward spiral.

Within this context, the verse describes successive stages of to whom a person may be sold: to a fellow Jew, to a *ger tzedek*, to a *ger toshav*, and finally — through the phrase *mishpachat ger* — to an outright non-Jew. And the phrase *o leiker mishpachat ger*, "or to the root of a foreigner's family," is understood by some to refer to being sold into the service of a pagan temple itself — not necessarily to perform idolatrous worship, but to serve in a menial capacity, as one who chops the wood and cleans the floors, as Rashi explains. The escalating stages reflect an escalating spiritual and social alienation.

The gemara now focuses on the stage of being sold to a *ger*: does this mean a *ger tzedek* can actually acquire a Jewish slave? This is the question of Rebbi — he does not know how to understand that.

### Convert Slave

The gemara cites a ruling: *ein hager nikneh beeved Ivri* — a *ger* cannot be sold as a *Hebrew slave* — and *ein isha veger konim eved Ivri* — a woman and a *ger* cannot purchase a Hebrew slave. Why can a *ger* not be sold as a *Hebrew slave*? Because the institution of the *eved Ivri* is bound up with the verse *veshavsata el mishpachto* — and he shall return to his family — meaning, the *eved Ivri* goes free at the *Yovel* and is restored to his ancestral family and ancestral land. The entire structure presupposes a family to which one returns, a sense of belonging that is temporarily suspended but never dissolved.

A *ger*, however, has no such family in the halachic sense. He has left his prior identity, and the Jewish family he now belongs to does not carry the same ancestral continuity that the institution of *eved Ivri* was designed to protect. To sell a *ger* as a Hebrew slave — stripping him further of status, marking him as not fully one of us — compounds an alienation that is already inherent in the convert's situation. The degree of belonging necessary to make the institution bearable and ultimately restorative simply is not present.

### Purchase Principle

Why can a *ger* not purchase a Hebrew slave? *Gemiri* — there is a received tradition: *d'maknei kanei, v'dela maknei lo kanei* — one who can be purchased can purchase; one who cannot be purchased cannot purchase. Since a *ger* himself cannot be sold as an *eved Ivri*, he correspondingly cannot own one.

### Convert Ownership

But this creates a difficulty: the verse clearly implies that a *ger* can acquire a Jewish person in some slave-like capacity. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves it: a *ger* cannot own an *eved Ivri ke-din Yisrael* — in the full legal capacity of a Jewish owner — but *aval koneh ke-din nochri* — he can own one with the legal status of a non-Jew who owns an *eved Ivri*. What is the difference? If sold to a non-Jew (or, by extension, to a *ger* with non-Jewish legal status), the slave does not serve the owner's children after the owner's death — *eino oved lo et haben velo et habat*. This contrasts with a standard six-year Jewish slave, whose contractual obligation continues with the estate if the owner dies. When the ownership is of the more complete variety (*nirtza* or sold to a non-Jew), the relationship is more personal — tied specifically to the original purchaser, not transferable to his heirs. A *ger*, then, can own an *eved Ivri*, but only in this modified form, which is why the verse could mention it.

### Women Converts Purchasing

The gemara then addresses why a woman and a *ger* are grouped together in the ruling that they cannot purchase a *Hebrew slave*. The gemara considers whether this position is at odds with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who rules that a woman *can* purchase male slaves. The resolution: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling applies to non-Jewish male slaves (*eved Kena'ani*), while everyone agrees a woman cannot purchase a Jewish male slave (*eved Ivri*).

### Discretion Distinction

The explanation hinges on an assessment of discretion. *Eved Ivri tznia lah* — a Jewish male slave is discreet. If a woman were to engage in improper behavior with a male Jewish slave, he would keep quiet about it. This very discretion is what makes the situation dangerous: there would be no public indicator that anything had gone wrong, so we are concerned that improprieties might actually occur, or at minimum that appearances of impropriety could arise. By contrast, *eved Kena'ani paritz leh* — a non-Jewish slave will not be discreet. Because the misconduct would become public, the concern is considered less acute; the anticipated consequences serve as a natural deterrent. It is a counterintuitive logic: precisely because the Jewish slave can be trusted to keep a secret, we are more worried about what might happen behind closed doors; the non-Jewish slave's indiscretion paradoxically provides a measure of protection.

## Widow Restrictions

### Widow Teaching

Rav Yosef teaches: *armala lo tarbi kalba* — a widow should not raise a dog; *v'lo tishri bar bei rav b'ushpiza* — and she should not host a yeshiva student as a lodger. In both cases, the concern is either actual impropriety or the appearance of it.

### Dog Question

The dog case seems puzzling. If a widow were actually engaging in improper conduct with the animal, the animal would follow her around obsessively — surely people would notice? Why should we be concerned about something that would be so visibly apparent?

### Dog Answer

The gemara resolves this: *keivan d'ki shadya leh umtza misrach bah* — since she is always throwing it juicy pieces of meat, the dog will follow her around regardless. People will simply say the animal is devoted to her because she feeds it so well. The behavior that would otherwise be a visible signal of misconduct is indistinguishable from innocent care for an animal. Therefore, *amri inshi, mishum umtza d'shadya leh hu d'misrach* — people will attribute the dog's attachment to the food, and the appearance of impropriety cannot be dispelled by observing the animal's behavior. The concern is sustained.

## Resident Alien Ribbit

### Resident Alien Verse

The gemara returns to Rebbi's statement that he does not understand the role of the *ger toshav* in the context of *ribbit*. The relevant verse (Vayikra 25:35–36) states: *vechi yamuch achicha umata yado imach, vehechezakta bo ger vetoshav vechai imach* — if your brother becomes poor and his means fail with you, you shall sustain him, whether a stranger or a resident alien, that he may live with you. The verse then continues: *al tikach me'ito neshech vetarbit* — do not take *neshech* or *tarbit* from him.

Reading these verses together, one would naturally conclude that lending to a *ger toshav* with interest is prohibited.

### Resident Alien Contradic…

Yet the *braita* cited earlier explicitly states: *lovin mehen umalvin otan beribbit, vechen beger toshav* — you may borrow from non-Jews and lend to non-Jews with interest, and the same applies to a *ger toshav*. How can both be true simultaneously?

### Rav Nachman Resolution

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the apparent contradiction through a careful reading of the verse's pronouns. *Mi ketiv al tikach mei-itam?* — the verse does not say "do not take from *them*." It says *al tikach meito* — do not take from *him*. The "him" does not refer back to the most proximate antecedent, the *ger toshav*. Rather, it refers back to the earlier subject: *achicha*, your fellow Jew. The verse's structure is: sustain both your poor brother and any *ger toshav* living among you; but do not take *ribbit* from *him* — meaning from the Jew who has fallen on hard times. The *ger toshav* is mentioned in the sustaining obligation but not in the prohibition of interest. This reading, admittedly, runs against the natural *pshat* of the verse, but it is necessary to harmonize the *psukim* with the established *halacha*.

## Co-Signing Arrangements

### Guarantor Teaching

The gemara cites a further teaching: *al tikach me'ito neshech vetarbit, aval ata na'aseh lo arev* — even though you may not take interest from a non-Jew in certain framings, you may become a guarantor (*arev*) for a non-Jewish loan bearing interest. To illustrate: a non-Jewish borrower takes an interest-bearing loan from a Jewish lender, and a Jew co-signs as guarantor.

### Guarantor Problem

The problem is immediately apparent. Under non-Jewish law, the creditor may proceed directly against the guarantor without first pursuing the primary borrower. If that is the case, then the Jewish guarantor effectively becomes the borrower from the perspective of the transaction: the lender skips the non-Jew entirely and collects from the Jew — including interest. The Jewish guarantor has thus paid *ribbit* to a Jewish lender, which is precisely what is forbidden. As the gemara puts it: *ihu nihu d'kashkil minei ribita* — it is effectively this Jew from whom the *ribbit* is extracted.

### Rav Sheshet Solution

Rav Sheshet provides the solution: *shekibel alav ladun b'dinei Yisrael* — the parties agreed in advance that any dispute arising from this arrangement would be adjudicated in a Jewish court rather than under non-Jewish law.

### Jewish Court Logic

In Jewish law, a creditor must pursue the primary borrower first before turning to the guarantor. Since the lender must go first to the non-Jewish borrower, the Jew's guarantee is only triggered in the event the borrower actually defaults. The relationship between the Jewish lender and the Jewish guarantor therefore never takes the form of a direct loan. At no point did the guarantor borrow a sum and then accumulate a growing debt with interest. Rather, the debt was accumulating entirely within the relationship between the lender and the non-Jewish borrower. Only when that borrower has defaulted and now owes, say, $1,500, does the guarantor's obligation crystallize — and at that moment, the guarantor's debt to the lender begins at $1,500. It is not a case of borrowing $1,000 and paying back $1,500 after interest has accrued; it is a guarantee that has been called in for a pre-existing amount. There is no *ribbit* in the technical sense.

The gemara then asks: if the parties agreed to follow Jewish law, should that not also mean that no interest at all may be collected (since Jewish law would prohibit even the original interest charged to the non-Jewish borrower under the restrictive views discussed earlier)? Rav Sheshet clarifies: they agreed to adjudicate *this particular aspect* — the order of collection — under Jewish law, while the interest arrangement itself was accepted as binding under whatever terms were originally set. The agreement to use Jewish court adjudication was targeted, not global.

## Debt Transfer

### Jew Lends Non-Jew

The gemara now extends the analysis to cases where debts are transferred between parties. The opening principle: a Jew may lend out a non-Jew's money on the non-Jew's behalf, with the non-Jew's awareness.

### Transfer Scenario

The scenario: a Jew has borrowed money from a non-Jew at interest and now wishes to repay. As he is carrying the funds — let us say $1,500 (the $1,000 principal plus $500 in interest) — a second Jew encounters him and proposes: give me that $1,500 and I will continue paying the interest to the non-Jew in your place, effectively assuming your debt.

### Forbidden Direct

If the non-Jew is not brought into this arrangement, it is forbidden. The second Jew would be paying interest to the first Jew — Jew to Jew — which is a straightforward prohibition of *ribbit*. The first Jew becomes the effective creditor in the arrangement, and the second Jew becomes the interest-paying borrower.

### Permitted via Non-Jew

However, if they go to the non-Jew and restructure the transaction with his participation — the first Jew transfers the $1,500 to the second Jew in the presence of the non-Jew, and the second Jew now has a direct relationship with the non-Jew as the new borrower — this is permitted. The crucial feature is that the original creditor, the non-Jew, remains the active party in the loan relationship. The money flows through in a way that maintains the non-Jew's position as lender. The first Jew is out of the picture, and the second Jew's obligation runs to the non-Jew, not to the first Jew.

### Reverse Scenario

The gemara then considers the reverse: a non-Jew who borrowed money from a Jew at interest is now coming to repay. A second Jew encounters him and says: give me that money, and I will continue paying interest to the original Jewish lender. In this case, the second Jew's interest payments run to the first Jew — a Jewish lender — but this is permitted, because the second Jew's relationship is with the non-Jew who transferred the funds, and his obligation to the first Jewish lender is construed as continuous with the non-Jew's original obligation.

### Non-Jew Middleman

However — and this is where the gemara points toward a significant structural loophole — if the parties bring the non-Jew in and formally restructure the arrangement so that the non-Jew drops out entirely and the second Jew now owes money directly to the first Jew with interest, this is forbidden. That is *ribbit* between Jews with no permissible intermediary relationship remaining.

The gemara ends here for today, but the shape of what is coming is already visible: the use of a non-Jewish party as an intermediary — a cut-out — to effectuate what is functionally a Jew-to-Jew interest-bearing loan while maintaining the formal appearance of each party transacting only with the non-Jew. This will be the major structural mechanism, the great loophole, that the gemara will continue to analyze in the next session.
