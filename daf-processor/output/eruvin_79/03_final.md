# Eruvin 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ditches & Separation

### Ditch Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָרִיץ שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת, עָמוֹק עֲשָׂרָה וְרוֹחַב אַרְבָּעָה — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a ditch between** two **courtyards** that is **ten** handbreadths **deep and four** handbreadths **wide,** it is considered a full-fledged partition, and the residents of the courtyard **establish two *eiruvin*,** one for each courtyard, **but they** may **not establish one *eiruv*.**

The mishna that opens our daf deals with the halachic status of a *charitz* — a ditch — situated between two courtyards. The governing dimensions are the inverse of those that apply to a wall: a ditch must be ten *tefachim* deep and four *tefachim* wide to function as a dividing barrier. The width requirement is conceptually distinct from the analogous requirement for walls. A wall serves as a divider regardless of its width; the question of a four-*tefach* width arose there only in the context of whether the wall itself constituted an independent domain from which carrying would be separately governed. By contrast, a ditch that is less than four *tefachim* wide is simply not a divider at all — one steps over it without difficulty, and it imposes no separation. Once a ditch reaches four *tefachim* in width, however, it functions as a wall, and the two courtyards on either side must maintain separate *eruvin* and may not be treated as one: *me'arvin shtayim ve'ein me'arvin achat*.

### Filled Ditches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ מָלֵא קַשׁ אוֹ תֶּבֶן. מָלֵא עָפָר אוֹ צְרוֹרוֹת — מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Even** if the ditch is **filled** with **straw or hay,** it is not regarded as sealed and is therefore not nullified. However, if the ditch is **filled with dirt or pebbles,** the residents **establish one *eiruv*, but they** may **not establish two *eiruvin*,** as the ditch is nullified and considered nonexistent.

The mishna then addresses the case of a filled ditch. If the ditch is filled with straw or stubble (*kash o teven*), this does not alter its status — the ditch retains its separating character even though it is currently passable. The reasoning is that straw is inherently temporary; one looks to the fundamental configuration of the space rather than to its transient condition. Water filling the ditch would be even less of a solution, and presumably something as ephemeral as snow or ice would be treated similarly.

The case of dirt or rocks (*afar o tzerorot*) is fundamentally different. If the ditch is filled with dirt or pebbles, it is treated as though the ditch no longer exists, and the two courtyards are considered a single unified space: *me'arvin achat ve'ein me'arvin shtayim*. Here there is no option — the courtyards are combined, full stop. This creates a structural asymmetry: with a genuine ditch, the two courtyards are necessarily separate; with a fully filled ditch, they are necessarily combined. The intermediate case — where there is a choice — is addressed by the next clause of the mishna.

### Plank Across

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן עָלָיו נֶסֶר שֶׁרָחָב אַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים, וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי גְזוּזְטְרָאוֹת זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, וְאִם רָצוּ מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** If **one placed a board four handbreadths wide across** the ditch so that he could cross it, **and similarly,** if **two balconies [*gezuztraot*]** in two different courtyards are **opposite one another,** and one placed a board four handbreadths wide between them, the residents of the courtyards or balconies **establish two *eiruvin*, and if they desire, they** may **establish one,** as the board serves as an opening and a passageway between them. If the width of the plank is **less than** four handbreadths, the residents **establish two *eiruvin*, but they** may **not establish one *eiruv*.**

The mishna introduces the case of a plank (*neser*) placed across the ditch. The plank must be four *tefachim* wide — approximately one foot — for it to be considered genuinely traversable. Note that the four-*tefach* requirement here applies to the width of the plank itself, not to the span of the ditch, which could be far wider. If the plank satisfies this width requirement, it creates an option: *me'arvin shtayim ve'im ratza me'arvin achat*. One may treat the courtyards as separate — because the ditch remains — or as unified — because there is a means of passage. This is analogous to a wall that has a doorway: the presence of passage allows for the possibility of combination without requiring it. If the plank is less than four *tefachim* wide, it does nothing, and the courtyards are necessarily separate.

### Balconies

The mishna concludes with the case of *shtei gezuztraot zu keneged zu* — two balconies facing one another. Rashi and Tosafot read this clause differently. According to Rashi, the balconies case is a continuation of the plank discussion: two balconies that are far apart may be connected by a plank placed across them, with the same four-*tefach* width requirement. According to Tosafot, the case of the balconies is an independent one, parallel to the case of a ditch that is less than four *tefachim* wide. Just as a ditch narrower than four *tefachim* is not considered a true separation — and thus allows the option of treating the two courtyards as either separate or unified — so too if the gap between two facing balconies is less than four *tefachim*, they are not considered truly separate, and one has the option of combining them. The reason Tosafot reads the mishna this way will become clear from the gemara's later discussion.

## Straw Dividers

### Straw Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתֶבֶן לָא חָיֵיץ? וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: מַתְבֵּן שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** Does **hay not** constitute **a** proper **filling** to seal the ditch? **Didn't we learn** in the following mishna: With regard to **a haystack ten handbreadths high** that stands **between two courtyards,** the residents of the two courtyards **establish two *eiruvin*, but they** may **not establish one *eiruv*.** This indicates that hay can create a valid partition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעִנְיַן מְחִיצָה — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּהָוְיָא מְחִיצָה, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן חֲצִיצָה, אִי בַּטְּלֵיהּ — חָיֵיץ, וְאִי לָא בַּטְּלֵיהּ — לָא חָיֵיץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that the matter should be understood as follows: **With regard to a partition, everyone agrees that** hay **is a partition** and that it divides between the courtyards as long as it is placed there. **But with regard to filling** the ditch so that it is considered sealed, one must distinguish between two cases: **If one** explicitly **nullified** the hay and decided to leave it there, **it fills** and seals the ditch; **however, if he did not nullify it** but intends to remove the hay from the ditch, **it does not fill** it, and the ditch is not considered sealed.

The gemara opens the analysis with an apparent internal contradiction. The mishna has just stated that straw filling a ditch does not count as filling it — the ditch retains its status as a divider. Yet the very next mishna, which we will also learn today, states that a *matben* — a large mound or wall of straw — standing ten *tefachim* high between two courtyards does function as a wall and forces them to be treated as separate. If straw is considered effectively absent and non-functional, how can it simultaneously constitute a valid *mechitza*?

Abaye resolves the tension by drawing a conceptual distinction between two different halachic roles that a physical substance can play. *Leinyan mechitza kulei alma lo peligi dehavya mechitza* — when it comes to forming a partition, everyone agrees that straw qualifies. The question is whether straw also serves as a *chatzitza* — an obstruction that fills and thereby neutralizes an existing spatial configuration. Here, says Abaye, the answer depends on whether one has designated the straw as permanent. *Iy batlei chaitz* — if one has performed *bitul*, treating the straw as a permanent fixture not intended for removal, then it fills the ditch and removes its status as a divider. *Ve'iy lo batlei lo chaitz* — if one has not performed *bitul*, the straw does not fill the ditch. The mishna's statement about straw not counting assumes the default case where no such designation has been made.

The underlying logic seems to be that when the underlying configuration is a ditch — something that by its nature divides — one needs something more than a temporary fill to redefine that configuration. A wall of straw, by contrast, is not fighting against a pre-existing dividing structure; it is simply asserting itself as a new partition, and for that purpose straw is sufficient.

### Dirt & Rocks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָלֵא עָפָר. וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּסְתָמָא? וְהָתְנַן: בַּיִת שֶׁמִּילְּאָהוּ תֶּבֶן אוֹ צְרוֹרוֹת וּבִיטְּלוֹ — בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** It is written in the mishna: If the ditch is **filled with dirt** or pebbles, it is considered sealed. The Gemara asks: Does this apply **even if one did not specify** his intention to leave it there? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna with regard to the ritual impurity of a corpse: If there is **a house that one filled with hay or pebbles, and he nullified** the hay or pebbles and decided to leave them in the house, then the house (Rambam) **is nullified** and is no longer considered a house with partitions? Generally, a house containing a corpse is ritually impure on the inside but does not impart impurity to the surrounding area. However, in this case, the house is considered an enclosed grave that imparts ritual impurity to its surroundings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּיטְּלוֹ — אִין, לֹא בִּיטְּלוֹ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** And one can infer from the mishna: **If he nullified** the hay or pebbles, **yes,** the house is nullified and considered sealed. However, if **he did not nullify it, no,** the house retains the status of a house, although it is filled with hay.

The gemara now presses further on the case of dirt and rocks. Our mishna implies that dirt filling a ditch suffices without any special designation — it is, in the default (*stam*) case, enough to neutralize the ditch. But the gemara cites a contradiction from the tractate of Oholot. That mishna states: if a house is filled with straw or rocks (*teven o tzerorot*) and one has been *mevatel* them — designated them as permanently remaining — then they are *batel* and alter the halachic configuration of the house for purposes of the laws of *tumah*. The concern there is whether a corpse buried under such fill is treated as buried in the ground (in which case the *tumah* passes upward through the fill directly rather than spreading throughout the house via the intervening airspace beneath the ceiling). The implication of *uvitlo batel* — that it is only effective when one has performed *bitul* — is that even rocks, without such designation, do not suffice on their own. This appears to contradict our mishna, which treats dirt as automatically effective.

### Rabbi Yose View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: תֶּבֶן וְאֵין עָתִיד לְפַנּוֹתוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא כִּסְתַם עָפָר, וּבָטֵל. עָפָר וְעָתִיד לְפַנּוֹתוֹ — הֲרֵי הוּא כִּסְתַם תֶּבֶן, וְלֹא בָּטֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** that mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** there is a difficulty, since **we heard him** say **the opposite, as it was taught** in the *Tosefta* that **Rabbi Yosei says:** In a case where there is a house full of **hay and** the owner **does not intend to remove, it is considered as though** it were filled with **indeterminate dirt, and it is** therefore **nullified.** However, if the house was full of **dirt** that **he intends to remove, it is considered as though** it were filled with **indeterminate hay, and it is** therefore **not nullified.** Apparently, the decisive factor for Rabbi Yosei is not the specific material in the house, but whether or not the owner intends to remove it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מַאן תְּנָא עֵירוּבִין — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Asi said: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** tractate *Eiruvin*? **It is Rabbi Yosei,** who does not accept the opinion of the *tanna* of tractate *Oholot*.

Rav Huna suggests that the author of the mishna in Oholot is Rabbi Yose, whose position is more demanding. The gemara immediately objects: from what we know of Rabbi Yose's actual view, he demands *less*, not more. The gemara then cites a *baraita* that articulates Rabbi Yose's position with precision and elegance. Rabbi Yose says: *teven de-ein asuy le-pnoto harei hu ke-stam afar u-vattel* — straw that one does not intend to remove is treated like ordinary undesignated dirt, and is *batel*, altering the configuration. Conversely, *afar ve-asuy le-pnoto harei hu ke-stam teven ve-lo battel* — dirt that one intends to remove is treated like ordinary undesignated straw, and does not alter the configuration.

The key to remembering this position is that Rabbi Yose makes intent the decisive variable. The default (*stam*) case for dirt is that it alters the landscape — one does not need *bitul*; the only exception is if one explicitly plans to remove it. The default case for straw is that it does not alter the landscape — it is assumed to be temporary and removable; the only exception is if one has explicitly designated it as permanent. This three-way matrix — *bitul*, *stam*, and *asuy le-pnoto* — maps neatly onto the cases in our mishna, which describes the undesignated (*stam*) case: straw does nothing, dirt does everything.

Thus our mishna is entirely consistent with Rabbi Yose. The initial suggestion — that Rabbi Yose is the author of the ruling in Oholot that demands *bitul* even for dirt — was mistaken. Rav Assi corrects the attribution: *man tanna Eruvin Rabbi Yose hi* — Rabbi Yose is the author of our mishna in Eruvin, not of the more stringent position in Oholot. As Tosafot observes, what this exchange reveals is that the amora'im knew that Rabbi Yose was the key figure in resolving the contradiction between Eruvin and Oholot, but initially got confused about which mishna he authored.

## Domains

### Tumah/Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: טוּמְאָה אַשַּׁבָּת קָרָמֵית?! הַנַּח אִיסּוּר שַׁבָּת, דַּאֲפִילּוּ אַרְנְקִי נָמֵי מְבַטֵּל אִינִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: Are you raising a contradiction** between the *halakhot* of ritual **impurity** and the *halakhot* of **Shabbat?** These two areas of *halakha* cannot be compared. **Leave** aside the **prohibition of Shabbat.** With regard to Shabbat, **a person nullifies even a pouch** full of money. The pouch may not be moved on Shabbat and is therefore considered fixed in place. However, hay, which may be moved even on Shabbat, is not considered to be fixed in the ditch. With regard to ritual impurity, by contrast, the nullification must be permanent.

Setting aside the dispute between *tanna'im*, Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua offers a structural resolution that does not require attributing the two mishnas to different authorities: *tuma Shabbat karamsi* — *tumah* and *Shabbat* are simply different halachic categories, and different standards legitimately apply to each. Why, specifically, does dirt not require *bitul* in the context of Shabbat? Because on Shabbat, one would never remove the dirt anyway — dirt is *muktzeh*, and one does not handle *muktzeh* on Shabbat. Therefore, even if the standard halachic rule requires *bitul* to treat dirt as permanently neutralizing a space, on Shabbat the dirt is effectively treated as *batel* by default, since no one will disturb it. *Afilu arneki nami mivatel inshi* — even something as valuable as a wallet, if it happened to be sitting in the dirt, one would leave it there on Shabbat rather than move the dirt.

### House vs Ditch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בַּיִת אַחָרִיץ קָא רָמֵית?! בִּשְׁלָמָא חָרִיץ לְמִיטְיְימֵיהּ קָאֵי. אֶלָּא בַּיִת, לְמִיטְיְימֵיהּ קָאֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: And are you raising a contradiction** between the *halakha* that governs **a house** and that which governs **a ditch? Granted** in the case of **a ditch,** it typically **stands to be** permanently **filled.** As there is no doubt that one's intention is to fill the ditch, the assumption is that anything placed inside a ditch will remain there. **However,** does **a house stand to be** permanently **filled?** Of course it does not. Presumably, the hay and dirt will be removed. Consequently, additional proof is necessary in order to conclude that the owner of the house intends to seal it permanently.

Rav Ashi offers yet another angle of resolution, one that returns to the contrast between a house and a ditch rather than invoking the distinction between *tumah* and Shabbat. *Bishlama charitz limitamei khei* — a ditch exists in order to be filled; that is, one naturally fills a ditch to restore level ground, and therefore dirt in a ditch is naturally read as having restored the ground to its unfilled state. *Aval bayis* — but a house? Who fills a house with dirt and leaves it that way? The default assumption when dirt is found in a house is that it will be removed, and so without explicit *bitul*, one does not assume the configuration has been permanently altered. The question, in other words, is not just about the substance filling the space but about what we naturally assume regarding that substance given the nature of the space it is filling.

## Plank Mechanics

### Rava Plank

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן עָלָיו נֶסֶר שֶׁרוֹחַב אַרְבָּעָה. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּתַן לְרׇחְבּוֹ, אֲבָל לְאׇרְכּוֹ — אֲפִילּוּ כָּל שֶׁהוּא נָמֵי, שֶׁהֲרֵי מִיעֲטוֹ מֵאַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: If **one placed a plank that is four** handbreadths **wide** across a ditch that separates two courtyards, the plank is considered an entrance. **Rava said: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where one placed** the plank **along the width** of the ditch. **But** if one positioned a plank **along its length, even** if the plank is of **minimum** width, it is **also** considered an entrance and reduces the ditch, **as he reduced** the opening **to less than four** handbreadths. The ditch was originally only four handbreadths wide. Therefore, if one places a plank of any width along its length, it becomes less than four handbreadths wide and no longer constitutes a partition.

Having established the mishna's general framework, the gemara turns to the specifics of the plank. Rava clarifies: *lo shanu ella shenasan le-rochvo* — the requirement that the plank be four *tefachim* wide applies specifically when the plank is laid across the width of the ditch — that is, the four *tefachim* of the plank span the gap from one bank to the other. In that configuration, the plank must be wide enough to walk across, hence the four-*tefach* minimum. *Aval le-orkho afilu kol shehu nami sharei* — but if one lays the plank lengthwise along the ditch, so that it runs along the edge rather than bridging the gap, even a very thin plank may suffice — provided it reduces the actual gap to less than four *tefachim*.

### Gap Options

This raises a conceptual question: if one lays the plank lengthwise, must it run the entire length of the ditch? The gemara's discussion suggests that what matters is whether there exists some point at which the gap is reduced below four *tefachim*. If the ditch is itself only four *tefachim* from end to end, then a lengthwise plank — even a narrow one — placed along its edge reduces the crossing distance to less than four *tefachim* at that point, which may be sufficient to consider it traversable and thereby give the two courtyards the option of combining. The logic is that just as a natural narrowing of the ditch below four *tefachim* creates an optional connection, an artificial narrowing achieved by placing a plank can serve the same function. This is, admittedly, a somewhat counterintuitive result — that a lengthwise plank of minimal width could accomplish what a crosswise plank of less than four *tefachim* cannot — and the precise mechanics of the scenario invite further analysis.

## Balconies Revisited

### Opposition/Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי גְזוּזְטְרָאוֹת זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ. אָמַר רָבָא: הָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ זוֹ כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ — אִין, זוֹ שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד זוֹ — לֹא, וְזוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ נָמֵי, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים, אֲבָל אֵין בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה — גְּזוּזְטְרָא עֲקוּמָּה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And similarly,** if **two balconies** are **opposite each other,** and one placed a plank four handbreadths wide between them, the residents of the two courtyards may establish a single *eiruv*, as the plank is considered an entrance. **Rava said: That which you said:** If the two balconies are **opposite each other, yes,** carrying between them is permitted by means of a plank; by inference, if they are **not opposite each other, no,** carrying is not permitted in this manner, **and** in a case where **one** balcony **is above the other as well,** it is prohibited, as it is not an entrance because it is too dangerous to walk from one to the other by means of the plank. **We said** the prohibition in these cases **only where there is** a disparity of at least **three** handbreadths **between this** balcony and **that** balcony. **However,** if **there is** a difference of **less than three** handbreadths **between this** balcony and **that** balcony, **it** is considered a single **crooked balcony.** Two balconies separated by a gap of less than three handbreadths are considered joined, based on the principle of *lavud*.

Returning to the case of the two balconies, Rava establishes several limiting conditions. *Ha de'amrat zu keneged zu in zu shelo keneged zu lo* — the leniency of treating two balconies as potentially connected applies only if they directly face one another. If one balcony is offset horizontally from the other, they are not considered accessible to one another even if the gap between them is less than four *tefachim*. Similarly, *vezu lemala mizu nami* — if one balcony is at a higher elevation than the other, that too disqualifies the connection, even if the horizontal distance between them is minimal.

### Three Tefach

Rava then adds: *lo amran ela sheyesh bein zeh lezeh shelosha tefachim* — even the requirement of direct opposition and equal height applies only when the gap between the balconies is more than three *tefachim*. *Aval ein bein zeh lezeh shelosha, gezustra akuma hi* — but if the gap is less than three *tefachim*, the two balconies are considered a single bent balcony. In that case, the principle of *lavud* — the halachic rule that treats a gap of less than three *tefachim* as though it were closed — applies automatically, rendering them connected regardless of whether they are at exactly the same height or in perfect alignment. The interplay here is subtle: when the gap is between three and four *tefachim*, the balconies are considered disconnected, and the conditions of direct opposition and equal height are necessary for the optional connection to apply; when the gap is less than three *tefachim*, *lavud* makes the question of traversability moot, and they are considered joined in any event.

## Straw Walls

### Wall Specs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַתְבֵּן שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד. אֵלּוּ מַאֲכִילִין מִכָּאן, וְאֵלּוּ מַאֲכִילִין מִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a haystack that is** positioned **between two courtyards** and is **ten handbreadths high,** it has the status of a partition, **and** therefore the residents of the courtyards may **establish two *eiruvin*, and they** may **not establish one *eiruv*. These,** the inhabitants of one courtyard, may **feed** their animals **from here,** from one side of the haystack, **and those,** the inhabitants of the other courtyard, may **feed** their animals **from there,** from the other side of the haystack. There is no concern that the haystack might become too small to serve as a partition.

The next mishna introduces the case of a *matben* — a large mound of straw — serving as a wall between two courtyards. Unlike the ditch scenario, the mishna does not specify that this wall must have any particular width; it need only be ten *tefachim* high. This reflects the general principle that a wall, however thin, serves as a divider — the width requirements that arose earlier concerned the ditch itself, not the wall. *Gavoha asarah tefachim — me'arvin shnayim ve'ein me'arvin echad*: the straw wall at this height functions as a full halachic partition, and the two courtyards are necessarily treated as separate. There is no option to combine them.

Importantly, this is straw that is designated as animal fodder, not the construction straw we encountered in an earlier discussion of *muktzeh*. Because it is intended for feeding animals, it is not *muktzeh*, and one may draw from it on Shabbat.

### Rav Huna Restrict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְמַעֵט הַתֶּבֶן מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, — מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** If the height of **the hay was reduced** to less **than ten handbreadths** across its entire length, its legal status is no longer that of a partition. Consequently, the residents of both courtyards **establish one *eiruv*, and they do not establish two *eiruvin*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתֵּן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ וְיַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's statement that the inhabitants of the two courtyards are permitted to place their animals next to the haystack and feed them, **Rav Huna said: And** this is the *halakha* **provided that one does not** actually **put** hay **into his basket and feed** his animals. In that case, there is concern that one might inadvertently reduce the height of the partition to less than ten handbreadths, which would constitute a breach between the courtyards and invalidate both *eruvin*.

Having established that the straw wall is a legitimate *mechitza* and the straw is not *muktzeh*, one might conclude that one may freely draw from this wall to fill a basket and then feed one's animals from the basket. Rav Huna rules otherwise: *uvilvad shelo yiten le-toch kupato ve-yachil* — one may not directly fill a basket from the straw wall. The concern is practical: if one stands there filling a basket, one is liable to remove large quantities without carefully monitoring the wall's height. The wall may drop below ten *tefachim* on Shabbat itself, at which point the two courtyards — which had separate *eruvin* — become merged into one without a unified *eruv*, creating a violation.

Rashi adds a second rationale: since the straw is designated as a wall, it has the character of *muktzeh* in the sense that one has mentally removed it from the category of animal fodder for Shabbat purposes. It has been assigned a structural role, and treating it as simply available fodder conflicts with that designation. This second rationale, as we will see, explains why the gemara requires the same caution even when one's animal is feeding directly from the wall.

### Animals Feed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאוֹקֹמֵי שְׁרֵי? וְהָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַעֲמִיד אָדָם אֶת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי עֲשָׂבִים בְּשַׁבָּת. וְאֵין מַעֲמִיד אָדָם אֶת בְּהֶמְתּוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי מוּקְצֶה בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if the actual handling of the hay is prohibited, is it **permitted to stand** one's animal next to the haystack and let it eat? **Didn't Rav Huna say** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said: A person may stand his animal on** a patch of **grass on Shabbat,** as he will certainly be careful not to pull out grass for the animal, due to the severity of the Torah prohibition involved. However, **a person may not stand his animal on set-aside** items **on Shabbat.** As the prohibition of set-aside is rabbinic in origin, he might forget and move the set-aside objects himself. The same reasoning should apply in the case of the haystack. If it is prohibited by rabbinic decree to remove hay from the stack manually, it should likewise be prohibited to position one's animal alongside the stack.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָאֵים לַהּ בְּאַפַּהּ וְאָזְלָה וְאָכְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna is not referring to a case where one directly brings the animal and places it alongside the haystack. Rather, it is dealing with a situation **where one stands in front of** the animal so that it cannot go elsewhere, **and it goes and eats** from the haystack of its own accord. In that case, the rabbinic decree does not apply.

One might assume that since one cannot personally fill a basket from the straw, at least one's animals may feed directly from the wall. The gemara probes this. It cites a ruling of Rav Huna in the name of Rav Chinana: *mamid adam et behemto al gabei asabim be-Shabbat* — one may cause one's animal to stand over grass and eat it on Shabbat, by walking behind the animal and guiding it there. However, *ve-ein mamid adam et behemto al gabei muktzeh be-Shabbat* — one may not cause one's animal to stand over and eat *muktzeh*, because one might come to pick up the *muktzeh* oneself and feed it to the animal.

Why the asymmetry between grass and *muktzeh*? Picking up grass would involve a *melachah de-oraita*, and we are not concerned that someone will inadvertently perform a biblical violation. But picking up *muktzeh* is only a rabbinic prohibition, and since the animal is already eating from it — which somewhat diminishes the sense that it is off-limits — one might come to take it with one's own hands. This is exactly the concern with the straw wall: if you are standing there while your animal eats, you might yourself reach in and take some.

The gemara therefore limits the permissibility of animal feeding from the straw wall. *De-kaim lei be-apa ve-azla ve-achla* — what is permitted is to guide the animal toward the wall and let it go eat on its own, without standing over it. One may not personally supervise the animal's feeding at the wall, lest one come to fill a basket directly.

## Oholot Case

### Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִתֵּן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ תֶּבֶן? וְהָתַנְיָא: בַּיִת שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת וּמִילְּאָהוּ תֶּבֶן — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד. זֶה נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ וְיַאֲכִיל, וְזֶה נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ וְיַאֲכִיל. נִתְמַעֵט הַתֶּבֶן מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to Rav Huna's statement itself: **And may one not put hay into his basket** and feed his animal? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a house that is** positioned **between two courtyards and** the residents **filled it with hay,** they **establish two *eiruvin*, but** they **do not establish one *eiruv*,** as the hay is considered a partition that divides the house. The resident of **this** courtyard **puts** hay **into his basket and feeds** his animal, **and** the resident of **that** courtyard **puts** hay **into his basket and feeds** his animal. **If the hay was reduced** to a height less **than ten handbreadths,** it is **prohibited** for residents of **both** to carry in their respective courtyards.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? נוֹעֵל אֶת בֵּיתוֹ וּמְבַטֵּל אֶת רְשׁוּתוֹ, הוּא אָסוּר וַחֲבֵירוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **How,** then, **does** the resident of one of the courtyards **act** if he seeks to permit use of the other courtyard to its resident? He **locks his house and renounces his right** to carry in the courtyard in favor of the other person. Consequently, **it is prohibited** for him to carry from his house into the courtyard, **and it is permitted** for **the other** resident to do so.

The gemara now confronts Rav Huna's restriction with a *baraita* that appears to contradict it directly. The *baraita* describes a house situated between two courtyards, serving as the connective passage from one to the other, and filled with straw. *Me'arvin shenayim ve'ein me'arvin echad* — like the straw wall in the mishna, this configuration forces them to be separate. But then: *ve-noten le-toch kupato ve-ochel* — one may go ahead and directly fill one's basket from this straw house and feed one's animals. This is precisely what Rav Huna forbade. The *baraita* not only permits direct basket-filling but seems to take it for granted as the normal mode of use.

Furthermore, the *baraita* proceeds: *nismat ha-teven me-asara tefachim — shenehem asurin* — if the straw drops below ten *tefachim*, both courtyards become forbidden, since they are now merged without a unified *eruv*. And it then describes the remedy: *no'el beito u-mevatel et reshuso* — one locks one's house and performs *bitul reshut*, renouncing one's rights in the courtyard.

## Wall Collapses

### Collapse Problem

The scenario of a straw wall dropping below ten *tefachim* on Shabbat raises a fundamental problem. If the two courtyards had been set up with separate *eruvin* for the very reason that the straw wall divided them, and the straw wall then collapses — whether gradually through animal feeding or otherwise — the two courtyards become merged into a single space that has no valid *eruv* at all. Both courtyards are now forbidden for carrying, since each had established its eruv in anticipation of the continued existence of the wall.

The gemara also raises whether this change in halachic status takes effect on Shabbat itself — that is, whether a wall that falls on Shabbat immediately alters the *eruv* status of the courtyards for that very Shabbat — or whether the status established at the onset of Shabbat persists throughout, with the problem only manifesting the following Shabbat. This question has a bearing on how urgent the situation is and what remedies are available.

### Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְכֵן אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגוֹב שֶׁל תֶּבֶן שֶׁבֵּין שְׁנֵי תְּחוּמֵי שַׁבָּת. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: זֶה נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ וְיַאֲכִיל, וְזֶה נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ וְיַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And you say likewise with regard to a pit [*gov*] of hay that** is positioned **between two Shabbat limits.** The residents of each area may feed their animals from the common hay, as there is no concern lest the animals go beyond the limit. **In any case,** the *baraita* **teaches:** The resident of **this** courtyard **puts** hay **into his basket and feeds** his animal, **and** the resident of **that** courtyard **puts** hay **into his basket and feeds** his animal. This *halakha* poses a difficulty to Rav Huna's opinion.

The *baraita*'s proposed remedy is twofold: *no'el beiso u-mevatel reshuso* — one locks one's house and performs *bitul reshut*. The scenario as described involves three houses: house one, house two, and the straw-filled house in the middle. Once the straw wall falls below ten *tefachim*, the courtyards merge. A resident of one house, wishing to help the other courtyard maintain usability, locks the door connecting his house to the now-merged space, thereby effectively reconstituting a barrier, and simultaneously performs *bitul reshut*, renouncing his own rights to the courtyard so that the others may freely use it. He himself is then forbidden — the one who performs *bitul reshut* loses his own carrying rights — but his neighbors are permitted.

## Oholot Case…

### House Interior

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: בַּיִת, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא (מְחִיצּוֹת וְ)תִּקְרָה, כִּי מִיפְּחִית — מִינַּכְרָא לֵיהּ מִלְּתָא. הָכָא — לָא מִינַּכְרָא לֵיהּ מִלְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We** can **say** that in the case of **a house, since it has walls and a ceiling, when** the height of the haystack **is reduced the matter is conspicuous.** The height disparity between the haystack and the ceiling is obvious. Consequently, when the haystack is reduced to less than ten handbreadths, people will stop carrying in the courtyard. **Here,** however, with regard to the hay in the pit, the difference in height **is not conspicuous.** The height of the hay in the pit could become diminished to the extent that the partition is nullified without anyone noticing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְמַעֵט הַתֶּבֶן מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים שְׁנֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין. הָא עֲשָׂרָה — שְׁרֵי, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמִידַּלְיָא תִּקְרָה טוּבָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מְחִיצּוֹת שֶׁאֵין מַגִּיעוֹת לַתִּקְרָה — שְׁמָן מְחִיצּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the *baraita*: **If** the height of **the hay was reduced** to less **than ten handbreadths** it is **prohibited** to carry in **both** courtyards. The Gemara infers from the phrasing of the *baraita*: If the hay was at least **ten** handbreadths high, it is **permitted** to carry there, even though the **ceiling is much higher** than the hay. **Conclude from it** that the legal **status of** ten-handbreadth **partitions that do not reach the ceiling is that of** standard **partitions,** which was the subject of a dispute elsewhere. Apparently, this *baraita* proves that they have the status of partitions in all respects.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּבַיִת שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר חָסֵר מַשֶּׁהוּ עָסְקִינַן, וְתֶבֶן עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Here, we are dealing with** the case of a **house** that is **slightly less than thirteen** handbreadths high **and the hay** is **ten** handbreadths high. The haystack is less than three handbreadths from the ceiling, and based on the principle of *lavud*, they are considered joined as though the partitions reach the ceiling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּבֵית עֲשָׂרָה וְתֶבֶן שִׁבְעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ, דְּכׇל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: Even** if **you say** that the *baraita* is dealing **with a house ten** handbreadths high, **and** the **hay** in the house is **slightly more than seven** handbreadths high it is considered a full-fledged partition that reaches the ceiling, as objects separated by **any** gap of **less than three** handbreadths **are considered joined,** based on the principle of *lavud*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְאַבָּיֵי, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי "מֵעֲשָׂרָה", אֶלָּא לְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מַאי "מֵעֲשָׂרָה"? מִתּוֹרַת עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments. **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Abaye, that is why** the *baraita* **teaches:** If the height of the haystack was reduced to less **than ten** handbreadths. **However, according to Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, what is** the meaning of: Less **than ten?** Even at the outset it was never as high as ten handbreadths. The Gemara answers: It means that it was reduced to less **than the law of ten** handbreadths. As long as the hay is slightly more than seven handbreadths high, it is regarded as ten handbreadths high, in accordance with the principle of *lavud*. Once its height is reduced to less than seven handbreadths, the *halakha* of a partition ten handbreadths high is no longer in effect.

The gemara now explains why the *baraita* permits direct basket-filling from the straw-filled house, while the mishna's straw wall — Rav Huna's case — does not permit it. The difference lies in the visual cue provided by an enclosed space. *Bayit, keivan de-ika tikrah, ki mifchat, minkara lei milta* — in a house, because there is a ceiling, when the pile of straw diminishes, the growing gap between the top of the pile and the ceiling makes the shrinkage immediately apparent. One will notice that the pile is getting lower before it drops below ten *tefachim*. *Hacha lo minkara milta* — in the open field, with no ceiling overhead, the gradual reduction from eleven *tefachim* to ten to nine is far less perceptible. There is no visual reference point to alert one to the danger. Accordingly, when filling one's basket from an enclosed straw house, one can trust that one will notice the diminution in time; when filling from an outdoor straw wall, one cannot.

This resolution raises a further halachic complication. The *baraita* states that as long as the pile remains at ten *tefachim*, both courtyards are permitted — *ha asara shari* — even if the ceiling is very high above the pile. This might seem to suggest that a ten-*tefach* partition inside a house is sufficient even when it does not approach the ceiling. But this is in tension with a principle established elsewhere that partitions within an enclosed structure must reach the ceiling to be effective; a ten-*tefach* partition in a room is not the same as a partition between two fully separate spaces.

Abaye resolves this by saying that the case in the *baraita* involves a very low ceiling: specifically, a house that is thirteen *tefachim* and a fraction high — *beiveit shelosha asar chasar mashehu*. The pile of straw at ten *tefachim* is then within three *tefachim* of the ceiling, and by the principle of *lavud*, the gap is treated as closed. The partition is thus considered to extend all the way to the ceiling, making it a full *mechitza*.

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua offers an even more compressed scenario: the entire house is only ten *tefachim* high — perhaps it is really more of a storage space than a dwelling — and the pile of straw is seven *tefachim* and a fraction. Since there is less than three *tefachim* between the top of the straw and the ceiling, *lavud* completes the partition and it is treated as a full wall of ten.

The gemara notes the asymmetry between these two resolutions: according to Abaye, "less than ten" means the pile has dropped below the threshold where *lavud* can still bridge the gap; according to Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua, "less than ten" is measured *mi-toras asarah* — it means the pile is no longer functioning as the equivalent of a ten-*tefach* partition once the *lavud* contribution is subtracted.

This discussion has a fascinating practical ramification noted in a *teshuvah* of Rav Henkin. There is longstanding debate among the *poskim* about whether an *ezrat nashim* — a women's section — possesses the same *kedusha* as the men's section of a synagogue, a question that arises, among other contexts, when considering whether one may reconfigure a synagogue by converting the men's section to women's use. Many of those responsa were written in the context of synagogues where the women's section was a genuinely separate room, with the dividing *mechitza* reaching the ceiling. Rav Henkin argues that as soon as the *mechitza* does not reach the ceiling, the space is halachically a single room regardless, and the entire framework of those discussions becomes moot. Whether or not one accepts his conclusion — and there are certainly *poskim* who would not — the underlying principle is directly rooted in discussions such as this one, where a partition that fails to reach the ceiling, even if ten *tefachim* high, does not genuinely divide the space it occupies.

## Wall Collapses…

### Lock vs Bitul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דָּיוֹרִין הַבָּאִים בְּשַׁבָּת אֲסוּרִין? דִּלְמָא דְּאִימְּעַט מֵאֶתְמוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The same *baraita* taught that if the height of the hay was reduced to less than ten handbreadths, it is **prohibited** to carry in **both.** The Gemara comments: Should we **learn from this** that **residents who arrive on Shabbat prohibit** the other residents from carrying? At the onset of Shabbat, both sets of residents were permitted to use the hay and the house, but once the hay was reduced on Shabbat it is as though new residents had been added to each of the courtyards, and it is prohibited for all of them to carry. Why not say that since at the beginning of Shabbat it was permitted to carry in the domain, they are permitted to do so for its duration? The Gemara rejects this contention: **Perhaps** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the hay **was** already **reduced on the previous day,** before Shabbat began. In that case, it was never permitted to carry at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? נוֹעֵל אֶת בֵּיתוֹ וּמְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּתוֹ. תַּרְתֵּי?! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אוֹ נוֹעֵל אֶת בֵּיתוֹ אוֹ מְבַטֵּל אֶת רְשׁוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **How,** then, **does** the resident of one of the courtyards **act** if he seeks to permit use of the other courtyard to its resident? He **locks his house and renounces his right** to carry in the courtyard in favor of the other person. The Gemara is surprised by this ruling: Does he require these **two** steps? One should suffice. The Gemara answers: **This is what** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **is saying: Either he locks his house or he renounces his right** to the courtyard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם תַּרְתֵּי, כֵּיוָן דְּדָשׁ בֵּיהּ — אָתֵי לְטַלְטוֹלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** a different explanation instead. **Actually, both** actions are required in this case, even though one of them would ordinarily suffice. The reason is: **Since he is accustomed to** using the courtyard, **he will come to carry.** Consequently, the Sages were stringent with a person in this position and obligated him to implement an additional change so that he will not forget and come to carry when it is prohibited.

The gemara now returns to the question of whether both remedies — locking the door and performing *bitul reshut* — are required, or whether either one alone suffices. *Tarti?* the gemara asks: why both? Normally, *bitul reshut* is effective on its own to resolve the problem of an unjoined resident in a courtyard. And locking the door should, at least in principle, reconstitute a barrier. The gemara resolves: *o no'el beiso o mevatel reshuso* — it is one or the other; either remedy independently suffices. Locking the door is presumably most useful before Shabbat, while *bitul reshut* is the available solution once Shabbat has begun. Alternatively, the gemara entertains the possibility that both really are required: *le'olam tarti, keivan dedash bah asi letaltulei* — since one is accustomed to traversing between the spaces, one is liable to forget and carry, and therefore a double measure is needed.

### Rashi/Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא אָסוּר וַחֲבֵירוֹ מוּתָּר. פְּשִׁיטָא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּהֲדַר אִידַּךְ וּבַטֵּיל לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ, וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּאֵין מְבַטְּלִין וְחוֹזְרִין וּמְבַטְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita*: If one locks his house and renounces his rights to the courtyard, **it is prohibited** for him to carry, **and it is permitted** for **the other** person to carry. The Gemara raises a difficulty: Isn't this **obvious?** Why was it necessary to state this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary only** in a case **where the other** person **then renounced** his right in favor **of the first** person. **And** the *baraita* **teaches us that** one **may not renounce** his rights in favor of the other, **and then** have the latter **renounce** his own rights in favor of the former.

Rashi explains the requirement for both as follows: locking the door is for the resident's own benefit — as a *heikera*, a reminder — so that he does not accidentally carry from his house into the now-merged courtyard. The *bitul reshut* alone makes the other courtyard's residents permitted, but without physically locking the door, the resident who performed *bitul* might forget his own restrictions and violate Shabbat. Rashi's position implies that the locking requirement is ongoing throughout Shabbat: when one leaves the house, one locks it behind oneself; when one re-enters, one locks the door again. One need not remain inside the house all of Shabbat, but the door must be locked whenever one is inside.

Tosafot is dissatisfied with this explanation. Tosafot argues that the double requirement of locking and *bitul* applies only to this particular unusual case — where everything was fine at the start of Shabbat and an unexpected event midday forced a change in status. In such a circumstance, because one has not mentally prepared for a changed halachic reality, one requires a double safeguard to prevent accidental violation. Under ordinary circumstances, a single remedy would be sufficient.

## Grain Techumim

### Grain Pile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגוֹב שֶׁל תֶּבֶן שֶׁבֵּין שְׁנֵי תְּחוּמֵי שַׁבָּת. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: זֶה נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ וְיַאֲכִיל, וְזֶה נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ קוּפָּתוֹ וְיַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And you** can **say likewise with regard to a pit of hay that** is situated **between two Shabbat limits.** The inhabitants of each area may feed their animals from the common hay.

The mishna adds a parallel case: *gov shel teven she-bein shtei techumei Shabbat* — a pile of grain straddling two Shabbat boundaries. Each city has a *techum* of two thousand *amot*, and if a pile of grain lies exactly at the boundary between two communities' *techumim*, each side may take from its own portion of the pile — *zeh noten le-toch kupato ve-zeh noten le-toch kupato* — but must be careful not to take from the other side's portion, which would constitute carrying beyond one's *techum*.

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר: תְּחוּמִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִיגְזוֹר דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was necessary** to state this *halakha* only **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said** that the principle of Shabbat **boundaries** is **by Torah law. Lest you say** that **let us issue a decree** and prohibit it, **lest** people **come to exchange** objects from one boundary to another, which would violate a Torah prohibition; therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that no distinction is made between the cases, and no decree of this kind is issued.

The gemara notes that this ruling requires explicit statement specifically according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *techumim* is a biblical prohibition. One might have thought that since the risk of reaching across and taking from the wrong side is a biblical violation, Rabbi Akiva would require a protective rabbinic *gezeira* — forbidding one from taking from one's own side of the pile at all, lest one come to mistakenly take from the other side as well. *Kamashma lan* — the ruling teaches that no such gezeira is imposed. Each person may take from his own portion, and we do not add a preventive layer to guard against biblical transgression in this context.
