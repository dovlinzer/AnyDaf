# Sukkah 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ikar Shira: Song or Music

### Opening Debate

The daf begins at the very bottom of 50b, picking up a discussion about whether one may play musical instruments — specifically the *chalil*, the flute — on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*. There is a three-way dispute among *Tannaim*: Rabbi Yose, Rabbi Yehuda, and the *Chachamim*. The initial framing attributed to Rabbi Yose is that the debate concerns the musical instruments played in accompaniment to the *korban* — the daily sacrificial service — and whether this music may be performed even on *Shabbos*. The crux of that question is how central the instrumental music is to the *avodah* of the *Levi'im* relative to their vocal singing. By contrast, when it comes to the musical instruments played during the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* — the nocturnal water-drawing celebration of *Sukkot* — everyone agrees that this constitutes mere *simcha* and may not be performed on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*. The use of the phrase *docheh es haShabbos* is itself somewhat striking, since what is at stake is a rabbinic prohibition against playing musical instruments; the language of overriding *Shabbos* in such a context is notable and worth bearing in mind.

### Rav Papa's Theory

Rav Papa, whose statement spans the last three words of 50b and continues to the top of 51a, reframes the question. He says *k'tannai* — this dispute between *Amora'im* about whether *ikar shirah b'peh* or *ikar shirah b'kli*, whether the primary *shira* accompanying the *korban* is the vocal singing or the instrumental music, is itself mirrored in a prior *Tannaitic* debate. His reading invites us to look at a *Mishnah* that asks not who sang but who actually played the instruments.

### Who Played Music

The *Mishnah* records three opinions. Rabbi Meir says the instrument players were *avdei Kohanim* — slaves of *Kohanim*. Rabbi Yose says they were from two named families, *mishpachat Beit Hapgaram* and *mishpachat Beit Tzipparya*, from a place called Ammaum — families of distinguished *Yisraelim* who, though not *Kohanim* themselves, were of such good *yichus* that *Kohanim* would seek to marry their widowed women. Rashi notes that these were *mishpachot meyuchasot* of *Yisrael*. Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus says they were *Levi'im*. This last position is the one most people naturally assume when they picture the Temple service — yet it is only one of three opinions.

Rav Papa suggests these opinions map onto the halakhic dispute: the one who says they were slaves (*avdim*) presumably holds that *ikar shirah b'peh* — since if instrumental music were the primary *avodah*, one could hardly permit slaves to perform it, and so the music was secondary enough to allow for that. The one who says they were *Levi'im* holds *ikar shirah b'kli* — instrumental music is itself the core Levitical service and therefore could only be performed by *Levi'im*.

### Consensus on Vocal Prima…

The *Gemara* challenges this neat alignment. It asks what Rabbi Yose's position — that they were distinguished families of *Yisraelim* — actually implies about *ikar shirah*. If he holds *ikar shirah b'peh*, then he should have no reason to object to slaves performing the music; if he holds *ikar shirah b'kli*, then he should require *Levi'im*, not merely distinguished *Yisraelim*. Neither option explains his middle position. The *Gemara* concludes that the three opinions cannot be a debate about halakhic principle at all: *tisbra* — does that really make sense?

Rather, says the *Gemara* — *ela* — the debate is one of historical fact. And if that is so, the very possibility of the debate reveals something important: for it to be factually possible that the instrument players were slaves or *Yisraelim*, everyone must agree that *ikar shirah b'peh* — the primary Levitical *avodah* was the singing, not the instruments. Only under that assumption could someone credibly report that non-*Levi'im* played the instruments. Hence: *de-kulei alma ikar shira b'peh* — all *Tannaim* concede that the primary service of the *Levi'im* was vocal. What they dispute is purely factual — *de-mar savar hachi havei ma'aseh u-mar savar hachi havei ma'aseh* — one says it happened this way, another says it happened that way.

This leads naturally to a wry observation. Talmudic *Tannaim* were not historians. Why do we care about a factual dispute? The answer is that facts carry halakhic weight.

One might further note how quickly historical memory can erode. Rabbi Meir was a student of Rabbi Akiva, meaning these discussions took place roughly a hundred years after the destruction. The instrument players' identities were already a matter of competing oral traditions. Whatever one's grandfather reported, another's grandfather may have reported something different.

### Nafka Mina: Status

The *Gemara* asks: *le-mai nafka mina* — what practical difference does this factual dispute make? The answer is: *le-ma'alin mi-duchan le-yuchasin u-le-ma'aser* — the implications concern whether one may draw conclusions from the fact that someone stood on the *duchan*. The *duchan* was the platform where the *Levi'im* performed; here the question is not about singing but about playing instruments. If one witnessed someone playing a musical instrument on the *duchan*, can one conclude anything about his status — either in terms of *yuchasin* (eligibility to marry into his family) or *ma'aser* (whether one may give him tithes on the assumption that he is a *Levi*)?

The three positions yield three different answers. The one who says they were slaves (*avdim*) holds *ein ma'alin mi-duchan le-yuchasin ve-lo le-ma'aser* — you cannot infer anything from having seen someone play the instrument; he may well have been a slave. The one who says they were distinguished *Yisraelim* holds *ma'alin mi-duchan le-yuchasin aval lo le-ma'aser* — you may infer that he is of good lineage and suitable for marriage, but you may not give him *ma'aser*, as he is not necessarily a *Levi*. The one who says they were *Levi'im* holds *ma'alin mi-duchan bein le-yuchasin bein le-ma'aser* — you may infer both *yuchasin* and *Levi* status from his having played on the *duchan*.

This is a useful reminder that knowing facts matters even when the underlying *halakhah* is clear. The *halakhah* — that anyone could play the instruments, since *ikar shirah b'peh* — is agreed upon. But who actually stood there historically determines what status we attribute to a person seen performing. The *Rambam* rules accordingly: *ikar shirah b'peh*, and non-*Levi'im* could indeed have played the instruments.

## Shir Sho'eva vs. Shir Ko…

### Yirmiya's Distinction

The *Gemara* now presents a different approach attributed to Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba. Rather than framing the debate about playing instruments on *Shabbos* as a question about the *korban*'s music, Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba says: *machloket b'shir shel sho'eva* — the entire dispute is about the music accompanying the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva*, not about the music that accompanies the *korban* itself.

### Simcha Yeterah Debate

On this reading, Rabbi Yosef bar Yehuda holds *simcha yeteira nami docha et haShabbos* — even the "extra rejoicing" of the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva*, rooted in the verse *ushavtem mayim b'sasson*, is of sufficient religious weight that it may be practiced on *Shabbos* within the *Beit Hamikdash*. The *Rabbanan* disagree: *simcha yeteira eino docha et haShabbos* — additional joy of this kind does not permit the playing of musical instruments on *Shabbos*.

### Korban Override

On one point, however, all agree: *aval b'shir shel korban divrei hakol avoda hi v'docha et haShabbos* — when it comes to the music that accompanies the actual *korbanot*, everyone agrees that this constitutes *avodah* and therefore overrides *Shabbos*. The musical instruments may be played together with the *korbanot* even on *Shabbos*, according to all opinions.

### Reconciling Premises

A careful reader will immediately notice a tension. Just moments ago the *Gemara* seemed to firmly conclude that *ikar shirah b'peh* — the primary service was vocal, and instrumental music was secondary enough that even slaves or *Yisraelim* could perform it. Now the *Gemara* asserts that everyone agrees the instrumental music accompanying the *korban* constitutes *avodah* and overrides *Shabbos*. If the instruments were so peripheral that their performance was entrusted to slaves, how can they simultaneously be an *avodah* that overrides *Shabbos*?

### Three Explanations

Three approaches address this tension.

The first explanation is that the language *avoda hi* should not be taken too literally. Since playing musical instruments on *Shabbos* is in any case only a rabbinic prohibition, there is no great jurisprudential obstacle to permitting it when it accompanies a *korban*. It is not so much that the instruments constitute a formal *avodah* of the highest order; rather, their connection to the *korban* service is close enough that the rabbinic restriction yields.

The second explanation, a variation on the first, draws a distinction between incorporation and primacy. Even if the instruments are not the *ikar* of the service, when they are actually performed at the time of the *korban* they become absorbed into the *avodah* in a meaningful way — unlike the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* music, which, however joyful, is preparatory or collateral to the *avodah*. The temporal and functional proximity to the *korban* lends the instrumental music enough *avodah* character to override the rabbinic restriction.

The third explanation is the most intriguing and is implicit in the *Rambam*. The *Rambam* distinguishes between two categories of Temple music. The ordinary orchestral instruments accompanying the daily *korbanot* are not themselves an *avodah* and need not be performed by *Levi'im* — this is precisely what flows from *ikar shirah b'peh*. However, on *Yamim Tovim* and *Rosh Chodesh* there is a biblical *mitzvah* to sound the *chatzotzrot*, the trumpets, and that *mitzvah* can only be performed by *Kohanim*. The *Rambam* draws on this to explain a further category: specifically on *Yom Tov* — not on *Rosh Chodesh* — the *chalil* was added to the Temple orchestra. This *chalil* was not part of the ordinary daily music; it was a special instrument used only on *Yom Tov*, and the *Rambam* rules that it constituted an *avodah* proper to the *Levi'im* alone, modeled on the logic of the *chatzotzrot*.

This reading of the *Rambam* raises a fascinating corollary. The name of the entire *perek* is *Hechalil*, and the *chalil* surfaces throughout as an instrument of special significance. If the *Rambam*'s position is followed strictly, it may mean not merely that the *chalil* was added to an existing orchestra on *Yom Tov*, but that on *Yom Tov* only the *chalil* — with its special *avodah* status — was played, while the ordinary orchestral instruments that required no particular *avodah* status were not used on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* at all. That would be a rather different image of the Temple service on sacred days. Thus when the *Gemara* says that the instrumental music accompanying the *korban* overrides *Shabbos*, it may specifically mean this *Yom Tov chalil* with its full *avodah* status, not the general orchestra of the daily service.

The *Gemara* then records a *baraita* stating explicitly: *shir shel sho'eva docha et haShabbos divrei Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda — ve-Chachamim omrim af Yom Tov eino doche* — confirming that the *Tannaitic* debate was indeed about the music of the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva*. This directly contradicts Rav Yosef's earlier framing that the debate was about the music of the *korban*. The *Gemara* declares *tiyuvta de-Rav Yosef tiyuvta* — a decisive refutation of Rav Yosef on both counts. The *Mishnah*'s language *zehu chalil shel beit hashoeva she'eino doche lo et haShabbos ve-lo et haYom Tov* — "this is the flute of the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* that overrides neither *Shabbos* nor *Yom Tov*" — implies a contrast: *aval de-korban doche* — the flute of the *korban* does override *Shabbos*. And the attribution of this *Mishnah* to the *Rabbanan* — who hold that the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* music does not override *Shabbos* — shows that even the *Rabbanan* agree the *korban* music overrides *Shabbos*, contradicting Rav Yosef's claim that they debated that question too.

Rav Yosef's response would have been that the *Tannaim* debate both: they focused their *machloket* on the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* in order to show the strength of Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda's position — *lehodia kocho* — that he permits instrument playing even for the *sho'eva*, not only for the *korban*. But the *Gemara* reads the *Mishnah* as not supporting this reconciliation, and the *tiyuvta* stands.

In summary: the three explanations for how *ikar shirah b'peh* coexists with the permissibility of playing instruments with the *korban* on *Shabbos* remain on the table — the *d'rabanan* leniency argument, the incorporation argument, and the *Rambam*'s unique status for the *Yom Tov chalil*.

## Simchat Beit Hasho'eva

### Unsurpassable Joy

The *Mishnah* opens with a sweeping declaration: *mi shelo ra'ah simchat beit hasho'eva lo ra'ah simcha miyamav* — one who never witnessed the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* has never in his life truly seen joy. What follows is a vivid account of a nightlong celebration of extraordinary sensory and spiritual intensity.

One detail in the opening merits passing attention. The *Mishnah* states that the celebration took place *b'motzei Yom Tov ha-rishon shel chag* — following the conclusion of the first day of *Yom Tov*. This is consistent with the earlier discussion: since playing musical instruments on *Yom Tov* was contested, and since even carrying the water on *Shabbos* would be impermissible, the celebration as a full event — with music and procession — could not begin until the first *Yom Tov* had ended. The *Mishnah* makes clear that without the music, there was no party. The celebration began only once the instruments could be brought out.

### Golden Candelabras

The celebration was set in the *Ezrat Nashim* — the Women's Courtyard — which functioned as something of a great hall, the gathering space before entry into the inner *azara*. The *Mishnah* describes a *tikkun gadol* — a major installation — prepared there for the occasion.

*Menorot shel zahav hayu sham* — there were great golden candelabras set up in the courtyard. These were not the *Menorah* of the *Heichal*, but large candelabras clearly evocative of it. At the top of each were four golden bowls — *arba'ah sefalim shel zahav b'rosheihem* — and leading up to each bowl were four ladders — *arba'ah sulamot l'chol echad ve-echad*. Young *Kohanim* — described as *yeladim mipirchei kehunah*, whether boys or young men — would ascend with pitchers of oil. The *Mishnah* states each pitcher held 120 *log*. A *log* is approximately 12 to 14 ounces, which puts each pitcher at somewhere around 13 to 15 gallons — a figure that strains credulity as a literal description of what a young person could carry up a ladder. It is best understood as a stylized or aggregate figure for the total oil consumed per bowl over the course of the night.

The wicks for these lamps were made *mi-blaei michnesei Kohanim u-mehemeihem* — from the worn-out trousers and belts of the *Kohanim*, from which strips were torn and fashioned into wicks. From these they would kindle the great bowls of oil.

### City Lit Up

The result of this illumination was dramatic: *v'lo hayah chatzer bi-Yerushalayim she-eina me'ira me-or beit hasho'eva* — there was not a single courtyard in all of Jerusalem that was not lit up by the light emanating from the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva*. The scale of the light, emanating from the Temple Mount outward across the entire city, must have been breathtaking.

### Dance and Music

The *Mishnah* then describes the celebration itself. *Chasidim va-anshei ma'aseh hayu merakdim bifneihem* — pious men and men of great deeds would dance before the assembled crowds, *va-avukot shel or she-b'yadeihem* — with torches of fire in their hands, *ve-omrim lifneihem divrei tishbachot* — singing praises and songs as they performed.

Rashi offers a charming aside on the phrase *avukot shel or*, describing in detail what juggling with torches looks like: you throw them up into the air and catch them, and those skilled in the art could do so with four or eight torches simultaneously, throwing one while catching another. The *chasidim* and *anshei ma'aseh* who led this performance were thus engaged in a physically impressive display of fire-juggling while simultaneously singing praises.

The *Levi'im* accompanied them with every manner of instrument: *b'chinorot u-vinvalim u-v'metziltayim u-v'chatzotzrot u-v'chelei shir v'lo mispar* — with harps, lyres, cymbals, trumpets, and instruments beyond counting. They stood *al chamisha asar ma'alot ha-yordot me-Ezrat Yisrael l'Ezrat Nashim* — on the fifteen steps descending from the *Ezrat Yisrael* to the *Ezrat Nashim*, steps corresponding to the fifteen *Shirei Hama'alot*, the Songs of Ascent in *Tehillim*.

A clarification is in order about the spatial arrangement. The *Levi'im* standing on these fifteen steps for the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* were not standing on the *duchan* — the platform from which they would sing and play during the *korban* service. Rashi is explicit on this point: the place where the *Levi'im* stood for the *korban* was a different location. One should not conflate the festive nocturnal celebration in the *Ezrat Nashim* with the formal *avodah* of the *korban* that took place in the inner courtyard.

### Trumpet Proclamation

At daybreak — when the rooster crowed — two *Kohanim* stationed at the upper gate leading from the *Ezrat Yisrael* down to the *Ezrat Nashim* held trumpets in their hands. *Kara ha-gever* — the cock crowed — and they sounded *teki'ah teru'ah teki'ah*. The sequence of *teki'ah teru'ah teki'ah* had already appeared earlier in the tractate in multiple contexts: accompanying the pouring of the water libation, and accompanying the *aravah* ceremony on *Hoshana Rabba*. Here it marked the transition from the nightlong celebration to the morning procession — the moment when the assembled crowd began to move from the *Ezrat Nashim* toward the Shiloach spring to draw the water that would be poured on the *mizbe'ach*.

### Procession Declaration

The procession moved downward, with the trumpeters sounding *teki'ah teru'ah teki'ah* again at the tenth step, and again upon reaching the floor of the *Ezrat Nashim*. They then continued — still sounding the trumpet sequence — until they reached *sha'ar ha-yotzei mi-mizrach*, the easternmost gate of the *Ezrat Nashim*, from which they would descend off the Temple Mount and make their way to the Shiloach.

At that eastern gate, they turned. *Hafchu pneihem mi-mizrach le-ma'arav* — they turned their faces from east back toward the west, facing the *Beit Hamikdash*.

### Avoteinu Statement

Standing at the eastern gate and facing the Temple, they made the following declaration: *avoteinu she-hayu ba-makom ha-zeh achoreihem el ha-Heichal u-fneihem kedma ve-hem mishtachavim kedma la-shemesh* — our ancestors who stood in this very place during the era of the First Temple had their backs to the *Heichal* and their faces to the east, bowing down to the sun. This is an allusion to the sin of the *ovdei avodah zarah* described in *Tanach*, who would worship the sun by turning away from the Temple. *Va-anu l'Yah eineinu* — but we, our eyes are to God.

Rabbi Yehuda adds that they would repeat this declaration: *anu l'Yah u-l'Yah eineinu* — we are to God, and to God are our eyes.

This moment is striking. Why, in the middle of a joyous water-drawing procession, would the participants evoke the sin of their ancestors? It is not a simple declaration of faith; it is a pointed narrative contrast — we are not what our ancestors once were. The structure has a parallel to the *Haggadah*, which similarly brings the shame of ancestors into a moment of celebration: *mitchilah ovdei avodah zarah hayu avoteinu*. Here too, a narrative of contrast is embedded in the ritual itself, making the joy all the more conscious of what it is rejecting.

## Comparative Splendor

### Marble Like Waves

Having described the glory of the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva*, the *Gemara* transitions into a broader meditation on things of unsurpassable beauty. A *Baraita* extends the earlier declaration of the *Mishnah*: just as one who never saw the *Simchat Beit Hasho'eva* has never seen real joy, so too one who never saw Jerusalem in its glory — *lo ra'ah krach nechmad me'olam* — has never seen a truly magnificent city. And one who never saw the *Beit Hamikdash be-vinyano* — in its built state — has never seen a truly magnificent building.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai hi* — what specifically was so beautiful? The answer, attributed to Abaye or alternatively Rav Chisda, is that the reference is to the Second Temple as renovated by Herod: *zeh binyan Hordus*. And what materials did Herod use? *Be-avnei shaisha u-marmara* — with marble, alternating in and out, jutting and recessed in such a way that the surface could accept plaster where needed. Herod apparently considered then gilding the façade with gold. The rabbis who were consulted — an interesting detail in itself, that the rabbis were advising Herod directly — told him: *shabkieh*, leave it. *De-hachi shapir tfei* — it is more beautiful this way. *De-michzei ke-advata de-yama* — it looks like the waves of the sea. The alternating white and darker marble, jutting in and out, produced a visual effect of undulating waves, at once beautiful and serene. According to the tradition, that was apparently the one time Herod followed rabbinic advice.

### Alexandria Synagogue

Rabbi Yehuda in the *Baraita* adds a comparable institution to the list: *mi shelo ra'ah dyoplostin shel Alexandriya shel Mitzrayim lo ra'ah be-chivodan shel Yisrael* — one who never saw the great *dyoplostin* of Alexandria in Egypt has never seen the glory of Israel. The word *dyoplostin* is a Greek term indicating a double colonnade — Rashi explains it as some form of vast coliseum or double-porticoed structure. What is notable about Rabbi Yehuda's formulation is that he does not say "you have never seen a magnificent building" but "you have never seen the *kivod* of Israel" — the grandeur, the collective honor, of the Jewish people. The building was impressive, but the statement is about what it represented as a gathering of Jews.

The *Gemara* describes it: *ke-min basilki gedola hayta* — it was like a great Basilica, *stav lifnim mi-stav* — a double colonnade or stoa. The population figures given are hyperbolic in the extreme — the *girsa* of *shishim ribo al shishim ribo* yields an impossibly vast number, and the more plausible reading is *kiflayim ke-yotzei Mitzrayim*, double the number of those who left Egypt — that is, approximately 1.2 million Jews in the synagogue. Even this figure is remarkable, and one should read it as expressing the sense of an incomprehensibly vast gathering rather than a precise census.

Within this structure were seventy-one golden thrones — *ayin-alef katedra'ot shel zahav* — corresponding to the seventy-one members of the *Sanhedrin*, each throne reportedly not less than twenty-one *kikarim* (talents) of gold, a figure that the *Gemara* likewise reads as hyperbolic. The architecture of the institution thus mirrored the architecture of Jewish governance: the community of Israel, represented by its scholars, seated in golden dignity.

### Flag Signaling

At the center of this enormous structure stood a wooden *bimah* — *bimah shel etz be-emtzayita* — and the *chazzan ha-knesset*, the sexton or attendant of the synagogue, stood upon it. Here *chazzan* does not mean the prayer leader but the functionary responsible for the logistics of the service. He held flags — *sudarim* — in his hands. The problem he was solving was straightforward: in a building so vast that one could not hear the *chazan* leading the prayers, how would the congregation know when to respond *amen*? His solution was to wave the flags at the appropriate moment — *hala menif be-sudar* — and upon seeing the signal, *ve-chol ha-am onim amen*, the entire assembly would respond in unison. The architecture demanded innovation; the innovation allowed the community to function as one despite the distances involved.

### Occupational Seating

The seating arrangement in this enormous synagogue was not random. *Ve-lo hayu yoshvim me'uravim* — they did not sit mixed together. Rather, each trade sat in its own section: *zehavim bifnei atzman* — goldsmiths together, *kesafim bifnei atzman* — silversmiths together, *nafachim bifnei atzman* — ironsmiths together, *tarsiyim bifnei atzman* — coppersmiths together, *gardiyim bifnei atzman* — weavers together.

The practical purpose was economic networking. A craftsman newly arrived in Alexandria — or a craftsman's son seeking an apprenticeship or a business connection — could enter the synagogue, identify the section of his trade, and find the people he needed. *Ve-chol ha-am onim amen* gave way to a social function: *misham parnasato u-farnasat anshei beito* — from there came his livelihood and the livelihood of his household. The synagogue was simultaneously a house of prayer, a gathering of the community, and a center of commerce and *parnasah*. This is not a modern innovation; it is described here as an ancient and celebrated feature of one of the greatest Jewish communities in the ancient world. The *beit knesset* — the gathering place — was conceived as a place of gathering in every dimension.

## Alexandria's Destruction

### Community Wiped Out

Abaye then delivers the devastating coda: *ve-kulhu katlinu* — they were all wiped out. The text as it stands names Alexander of Macedonia (*Alexandros Mokdon*), but this is clearly a scribal difficulty: Alexander the Great had no role in massacring Egyptian Jews, and chronologically the dates do not align by several centuries. Marginal notes in manuscripts cross out *Mokdon*, and various authorities, including Steinsaltz, identify the Roman general responsible as Trajan. The *Gra*'s *girsa* reads Trajan as well. Others suggest Severus. What is not in dispute is that the community — however vast and glorious — was destroyed by Roman imperial violence.

### Returning to Egypt

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama i'enshu* — why were they punished? One might expect the answer to invoke the community's well-documented Hellenization or religious laxity. What the *Gemara* actually says is more pointed: *mishum de-avru a-hai kra* — they transgressed the verse: *lo tosifun lashuv baderech hazeh od* — you shall not return this way again. By settling in Egypt following the Exodus, they violated the Torah's explicit prohibition. *Ve-innu hadur* — and yet they returned. The punishment, on this reading, was a direct consequence of ignoring the Torah's warning against Egyptian settlement — a warning addressed to Israel at the moment of its founding as a people.

### Verse Omen

The account closes with a striking anecdote. When the Roman general arrived — ahead of schedule, his ten-day sea journey having taken only five days due to favorable winds — he encountered a child in the community reading aloud from a *sefer*. The verse the child happened to be reading was: *yisa Hashem alecha goy merachok* — God will bring upon you a nation from afar. The general reflected: *hahu gavra* — this man, meaning himself, *ba'ei l'meiti sefinta b'asara yomei, ve-dalyei zika ve-ati sefinta b'chamsha yomei* — was supposed to arrive in ten days, and yet favorable wind brought him in five. He understood this as a *siman min ha-shamayim* — a heavenly sign — that God was directing him to this place to fulfill the verse's warning. *Nafal alaive ve-katleinu* — he fell upon them and slaughtered them.

The juxtaposition is deeply unsettling: a child's voice reading scripture serves as the omen that seals the community's fate. The same Torah that had warned them not to return to Egypt now, in the mouth of one of their own children, announces the arrival of the instrument of their destruction.
