# Bava Metzia 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomer Liability Review

### Mishnah Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם אֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע וְכוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ״ – כְּלָל, ״כֶּסֶף אוֹ כֵּלִים״ – פְּרָט, ״וְגֻנַּב מִבֵּית הָאִישׁ״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **An unpaid bailee does not take an oath** if these items were stolen or lost. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse that discusses an unpaid bailee: "When a man delivers to his neighbor money or vessels to safeguard, and it is stolen out of the man's house; if the thief is found, he shall pay double. If the thief is not found, then the master of the house shall approach the judge" (Exodus 22:6–7). **"When a man delivers to his neighbor"** is **a generalization; "money or vessels"** is **a detail.** And when the verse states: **"And it is stolen out of the man's house," it then generalizes** again.

The daf opens at the bottom of 57b, continuing the discussion of the *mishnah* enumerating categories excluded from various areas of *halacha* — from *ona'ah*, from *shvuah*, and from the laws of *shomrim*. The list includes land, slaves, documents, and *hekdesh*.

### Shomer Exemptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם וְכוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ״ – כְּלָל, ״חֲמוֹר אוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה״ – פָּרַט, ״וְכׇל בְּהֵמָה לִשְׁמוֹר״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **A paid bailee does not pay** if these items were stolen or lost. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse that discusses a paid bailee: "When a man delivers to his neighbor a donkey, or an ox, or a sheep, or any beast to safeguard, and it dies, or is hurt, or is driven away, no man seeing it, the oath of the Lord shall be between them both…but if it be stolen from him, he shall make restitution to its owner" (Exodus 22:9–11). **"When a man delivers to his neighbor"** is **a generalization; "a donkey, or an ox, or a sheep"** is **a detail.** And when the verse states: **"Or any beast to safeguard," it then generalizes** again.

Two baseline rules are established. A *shomer chinam* — one who watches an object without compensation — is not required to take an oath that he was not negligent. A *shomer sachar* — one who is paid for his watching — is exempt from paying in cases of *gneiva va-aveida*, theft or loss.

### Negligent Admission

One case conspicuously left unaddressed is that of a *shomer* who was actually negligent and admits to his negligence. Negligence may carry a higher level of liability than *gneiva va-aveida*, and there is a view that such conduct rises to the level of *mazid*, intentional wrongdoing, which would carry its own liability. That case remains open. What the *mishnah* has established is that with respect to an oath, a *shomer* is not liable for these things; and with respect to paying for *gneiva va-aveida*, a *shomer* similarly is not liable.

## Shekalim & Ownership

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ, בְּנֵי הָעִיר שֶׁשָּׁלְחוּ אֶת שִׁקְלֵיהֶן וְנִגְנְבוּ אוֹ שֶׁאָבְדוּ, אִם מִשֶּׁנִּתְרְמָה הַתְּרוּמָה –
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna. Half-shekels were donated by the people during the month of Adar and placed in a special chamber in the Temple, from where, as needed, some of the shekels were taken for use in purchasing public offerings in the coming months. This was done in order to give all of the Jewish people a share in those public offerings. Additionally, from the moment that the money is taken from there, both those shekels that arrived at the Temple and those that did not yet arrive become Temple property. The mishna teaches (*Shekalim* 5a): With regard to **residents of the city who sent their shekels** to the Temple, **and** the coins **were stolen** from the agent **or were lost** en route, the *halakha* depends on the circumstances. **If** this occurred after the **contributions** of the chamber **had** already **been collected** from the chamber,

The *gemara* raises a challenge — *u'remi'nei* — from a *mishnah* in *Shekalim*. Each year, the people of a town would pool their obligatory annual *shekel* contribution — the *machatzis ha-shekel* — so that the communal sacrifices (*korban tzibur*) would be purchased from shared funds. They would dispatch a *shaliach* to carry the coins to the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *mishnah* addresses what happens when, along the way, the money is stolen by highway bandits or simply lost.

### Terumat HaLishka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁבָּעִין לַגִּזְבָּרִין, וְאִם לָאו – נִשְׁבָּעִין לִבְנֵי הָעִיר, וּבְנֵי הָעִיר שׁוֹקְלִין אֲחֵרִים תַּחְתֵּיהֶן. נִמְצְאוּ, אוֹ שֶׁהֶחְזִירוּם הַגַּנָּבִים – אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ שְׁקָלִים הֵם, וְאֵין עוֹלִין לָהֶם לַשָּׁנָה הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** the agents must **take the oath** of a bailee **to the treasurers** that they did not misappropriate the coins. **If** the collection of the chamber was **not** yet performed, the lost or stolen shekels remain the property of the residents of the city. Therefore, the agents **take an oath to the residents of the city. The residents of the city** then **contribute other** shekels **in place** of the original shekels. **If** the original shekels **were** then **found or the thieves returned them,** both **these,** the original shekels, **and those,** the replacement shekels, assume the status of consecrated **shekels** and belong to the Temple. **But** the two half-shekels that they contributed this year **do not count** to absolve them from their obligation to contribute the shekels **for the following year.** This mishna teaches that an unpaid bailee does take an oath concerning consecrated property, contrary to the mishna here.

The *mishnah* distinguishes between two scenarios based on whether the theft or loss occurred before or after *terumat ha-lishka* was taken. To understand the significance of this, one must appreciate how the *lishka* functioned. All the *shekalim* contributed from across the land were deposited into a chamber in the *Beit HaMikdash*. Three times a year, the *kohanim* would enter with baskets and scoop out coins, which would then be used to fund the next cycle of communal sacrifices. This act of scooping — separating coins from the chamber — is called *terumat ha-lishka*, analogous to *teruma* as an act of separation.

The crucial legal consequence is that this *teruma* is considered to take effect not only on the coins physically present in the chamber, but also on coins that are still in transit — already collected from the towns but not yet physically deposited. Just as *teruma* can be taken from produce in one location and declared to apply to produce in another location (*bedie'avad*), so too the *terumat ha-lishka* applies conceptually to the coins being carried by the *shaliach*. Once this conceptual application occurs, those coins are treated as if they have already been deposited in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The ownership — or at least the operative control — has transferred.

### Before/After Teruma

The distinction between the two scenarios is therefore as follows. If the money was stolen or lost before *terumat ha-lishka* was taken, the coins conceptually still belong to the townspeople. They never made it to the *Beit HaMikdash* in any operative sense. In that case, *nishba'im livnei ha'ir* — the messengers swear to the townspeople — and *shoklin acheirim tachteichem* — the townspeople must give their *shekel* a second time, since the first never reached the *Beit HaMikdash*. If the money was stolen or lost after *terumat ha-lishka* was taken, however, it is as though it was stolen from the *Beit HaMikdash* itself. In that case, *nishba'im la-gizbarin* — the messengers swear to the Temple treasurers — and the townspeople are not required to contribute again.

It is worth noting the distinction between *kedusha* as a status of an object and *kedusha* in the sense of ownership. Even before *terumat ha-lishka*, the coins may well have been sanctified from the moment they were designated and handed to the *shaliach*. Yet sanctification as a status does not automatically transfer ownership or operational control to the *Beit HaMikdash*. Compare the case of one who vows *harei alai olah* — I obligate myself to bring a burnt-offering — and the animal subsequently dies. The animal's sanctity does not dissolve his personal obligation, because the *Beit HaMikdash* never took effective possession. Similarly here, the coins were *hekdesh* in status, but the ownership dimension — the question of who bears responsibility and who has control — shifts only upon *terumat ha-lishka* or actual delivery.

### Eilu V'Eilu Shekalim

The *mishnah* continues: if the money that was lost is subsequently found, or the thieves are apprehended and the coins recovered, the rule is *eilu v'eilu shekalim* — both the original coins and the replacement coins are consecrated. The original coins remain *shekalim* because nothing ever deconsecrated them. The replacement coins are *shekalim* because they were freshly contributed and there is nothing to prevent their consecration. One might ask: why couldn't the townspeople, at the time they gave the replacement coins, simply declare the original coins — wherever they were — deconsecrated, *mechul*, on the new ones? The answer invokes *Rabbi Yochanan*'s principle that one cannot be *mekadesh* or *mechallel* something not under one's control. Since the original coins were neither physically accessible nor within the townspeople's operative domain, they could not be deconsecrated. Once recovered, there is similarly no mechanism to unravel the double consecration after the fact. The *Beit HaMikdash* therefore receives both. And one more point: *eilu v'eilu shekalim hem v'ein olin lahem l'shanah haba'ah* — they cannot be applied as a prepayment credit toward next year's obligation. The double contribution does not carry forward.

### Shvuah on Hekdesh

All of this is fascinating on its own terms, but the immediate relevance to our *sugya* is the following: in both scenarios — whether the messengers swear to the townspeople or to the *gizbarin* — the coins in question are *hekdesh*, sanctified property. We have just established that one does not make a *shvuah* on *hekdesh*. So what is the *shvuah* being taken on here? The *gemara* therefore asks: what is the basis for these oaths?

## Shmuel's Fee Theory

### Fee Collection Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָכָא בְּנוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר עָסְקִינַן, וְנִשְׁבָּעִין לִיטּוֹל שְׂכָרָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: Here we are dealing with paid bailees,** and the purpose of the oath is not to exempt them from their liability to pay for the theft. Rather, **they take an oath** that they performed their task properly, in order **to collect their wages.**

*Shmuel* offers a reading that is admittedly not the straightforward *pshat* of the *mishnah*. He says: *hacha b'netilat sachar askinan* — we are dealing here with the messengers collecting their fee. The messengers are not swearing in order to exempt themselves from liability — they have no such liability, since the object is *hekdesh*. Rather, they want to be paid for their service. Having undertaken to transport the coins and now presenting themselves after a theft or loss, they must take an oath to demonstrate that they were not negligent, not to avoid a payment obligation, but in order to establish their entitlement to receive payment. If they were content to forfeit their fee and simply walk away, no *shvuah* would be required at all.

### Swearing to Gizbarin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נִשְׁבָּעִין לַגִּזְבָּרִין? לִבְנֵי הָעִיר מִבְּעֵי לֵהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** is this phrase accurate: **They take an oath to the treasurers?** The *tanna* **should have** taught: They take an oath **to the residents of the city,** from whom they are claiming their wages.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this. In the scenario where *terumat ha-lishka* was already taken — when it is the *Beit HaMikdash* that sustains the loss — the *mishnah* says the messengers swear to the *gizbarin*, the Temple treasurers. But according to *Shmuel*'s reading, they are swearing in order to collect their fee from the townspeople who hired them. Why then are they swearing to the *gizbarin*? They should be swearing to the townspeople — *l'vnei ha'ir miba'ei leh*.

### Rabbah's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: נִשְׁבָּעִין לִבְנֵי הָעִיר בְּמַעֲמַד גִּזְבָּרִין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִחְשְׁדִינְהוּ, וְאִי נָמֵי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִקְרוֹ לְהוּ ״פּוֹשְׁעִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: They take an oath to the residents of the city in the presence of the treasurers, so that** the treasurers **will not suspect** that the residents of the city did not contribute their shekels at all. **Alternatively,** the agents take an oath that they executed their mission properly, **so that** the Temple treasurers **will not call them negligent.**

*Rabbah* resolves this: *nishba'im l'vnei ha'ir b'ma'amad gizbarin k'dei shelo yachshidohu*. They do indeed swear to the townspeople, since the townspeople are their employers and the ones who owe them the fee. But in the first scenario — where the loss falls upon the *Beit HaMikdash* — the *gizbarin* are brought in as witnesses, so that they too can satisfy themselves that the messengers did not steal or misappropriate the funds. Alternatively, so that the *gizbarin* will not accuse the messengers of negligence. Since the messengers are in any case taking an oath to collect their fee, the *gizbarin* are invited to be present so that suspicion is cleared on both fronts — neither theft nor negligence — at least in the eyes of the Temple treasury.

## Negligence & Fee

### Negligence Forfeits Pay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא נִגְנְבוּ אוֹ שֶׁאָבְדוּ קָתָנֵי, וְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה חַיּוֹבֵי מִיחַיַּיב! וְהָכָא נָמֵי, נְהִי דְּשַׁלּוֹמֵי לָא מְשַׁלְּמִי, אַגְרַיְיהוּ מִיהָא לַפְסֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the mishna: **And** the coins **were stolen or were lost** en route, **and paid bailees are liable** to pay **in** cases of **theft and loss? And here too,** when safeguarding consecrated property, **although** paid bailees **do not pay** for the theft, as a paid bailee is exempt, **let them at least forfeit their wages,** as their responsibility to safeguard the money includes preventing theft and loss.

The *gemara* is not satisfied with *Shmuel*'s approach and raises a further difficulty. The *mishnah* says the money was stolen or lost. If the messenger was being paid — i.e., he is a *shomer sachar* — then normally a *shomer sachar* is liable for *gneiva va-aveida*. True, the *hekdesh* exemption means he will not actually pay. But the *gemara* argues: *nehi de-shilumei lo meshalmei* — granted he does not pay — *agrai'u mia lifsedu* — at least he should forfeit his fee. If the circumstances were such that a paid *shomer* would normally be liable, that is because his duty of care required him to prevent exactly these outcomes. Even where *hekdesh* relieves him of the payment, it does not mean he fulfilled his duty of care. Since he failed in his *chiyuv shmirah*, he is not entitled to his compensation.

### Duty of Care

This is an important distinction. The question of whether a *shomer* is exempt from *shvuah* and *tashlumin* is separate from the question of whether a *shomer* has any underlying *chiyuv shmirah* at all. Even where there is no formal obligation to pay and no oath to take, the *shomer* presumably still has a responsibility to watch. The *gemara* is pointing out that when a *shomer sachar* fails in that responsibility — when loss occurs under circumstances for which he would normally bear liability — we may conclude that he did not do his job, and consequently he forfeits his fee. An oath cannot change that underlying reality.

### Oath Insufficient

The implication is clear: according to the *gemara*'s objection, a *shvuah* alone is insufficient to entitle the messengers to their fee when the loss is of the type for which a *shomer sachar* would ordinarily bear responsibility. The *gemara* thus rejects *Shmuel*'s rationale as inadequate to explain the *mishnah*.

## Rava: Force Majeure

### Unavoidable Disasters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: נִגְנְבוּ – בְּלִסְטִים מְזוּיָּין, אָבְדוּ – שֶׁטָּבְעָה סְפִינָתוֹ בַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** When the *tanna* says that **they were stolen,** the reference is to a case where the item was stolen **by armed bandits.** When he said that **they were lost,** the reference is to a case where the agent's **ship sank at sea.** Since the shekels left his possession through circumstances beyond his control, this is considered an unavoidable accident, for which a paid bailee is exempt.

*Rava* rescues *Shmuel*'s basic framework by recharacterizing the facts. He says: *nignevu be-listim mezuyan avdu she-ta'ah sefinato ba-yam* — the cases in the *mishnah* involve not ordinary theft or loss, but armed bandits and a sinking ship. These are *oneis* — situations of absolute unavoidable compulsion, force majeure.

### Oneis Logic

With this clarification, the *gemara*'s objection dissolves. A *shomer sachar*'s liability for *gneiva va-aveida* rests on the premise that he had a heightened duty of care and failed to prevent a preventable loss. But when the loss is an *oneis* — when nothing could have protected the coins against armed robbers or a shipwreck — one cannot say that the messenger shirked his *chiyuv shmirah*. He was not negligent; he was overwhelmed by forces entirely beyond his control. He therefore retains his entitlement to his fee, takes an oath to demonstrate that the loss was indeed an *oneis*, and collects his pay. *Rava* thus preserves *Shmuel*'s reading: the *shvuah* is to collect the fee, the scenarios are ones of *oneis*, and the oath is appropriate.

## Rabbi Yochanan View

### Rabbi Shimon Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: קָדָשִׁים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן – יֵשׁ לָהֶן אוֹנָאָה, וְנִשְׁבָּעִין עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says: Consecrated** items **for which one bears responsibility** to replace **them are** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation, and** therefore **one takes an oath concerning them.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* takes a different approach entirely, one grounded in the position of *Rabbi Shimon*. He says the *mishnah* reflects *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle: *kodashim she-chayav be-achrayutan yesh lahem ona'ah ve-nishba'in aleihen* — sanctified property for which a person bears financial responsibility is treated, for purposes of *ona'ah* and *shvuah*, as though it were his own. Since the messengers are carrying coins for which the townspeople remain financially responsible — specifically, before *terumat ha-lishka* was taken, the townspeople must contribute again if the money is lost — it is as though the coins are effectively the townspeople's property. The oath is therefore not on *hekdesh* in the exempting sense, because the liability-nexus makes it *amito*, the property of a private party.

### Principle Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתִינַח עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִתְרְמָה הַתְּרוּמָה. מִשֶּׁנִּתְרְמָה הַתְּרוּמָה, קָדָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן נִינְהוּ, דְּתַנְיָא: תּוֹרְמִין עַל הָאָבוּד, וְעַל הַגָּבוּי, וְעַל הֶעָתִיד לִגָּבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well** if the **contribution** of the chamber **had not** yet **been collected** into the baskets. At that stage, residents of the city bear responsibility to replace the shekels, and that is why Rabbi Shimon deems the agent liable to take an oath. But if the shekels were lost or stolen **once the contribution** of the chamber **had been collected,** the shekels are tantamount to **sacrificial animals for which one does not bear responsibility,** and the residents of the city should be exempt, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One collects** shekels in the Temple chamber with the intention of fulfilling the obligation **for** those coins **that are lost** on the way, **and for** those **that are collected** but did not yet arrive, **and for** those **that are destined to be collected** in the future. Apparently, the residents of the city no longer bear responsibility for the shekels.

The *gemara* immediately identifies the limitation of this approach. It works elegantly for the scenario *before* *terumat ha-lishka* was taken, when the townspeople are still responsible for the loss and must re-contribute. But once *terumat ha-lishka* has been taken, the *mishnah* itself says the townspeople are no longer required to give again — the loss falls on the *Beit HaMikdash*. At that point, the coins are *kodashim she-eino chayav be-achrayutan* — sanctified property for which the townspeople bear no responsibility. *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle would then provide no basis for an oath. Why does the *mishnah* still require one?

### Braita: Teruma Scope

The *gemara* cites a *braita* to address this: *tormin al ha-avud v'al ha-gavuy v'al ha-atid ligvot* — the *teruma* of the *lishka* takes effect upon money that is lost, money that has been collected and is in transit, and money that has not yet been collected at all. *Tosafot* explains the first clause: even if coins were deposited into the *lishka* and subsequently lost before physical use, the *teruma* is treated as having applied to them. The second clause — *al ha-gavuy* — covers the scenario of our *mishnah*: coins collected from a town and being carried by a messenger. The *teruma* applies to those as well, rendering them as though already deposited.

### L'Asid Ligvos

The third clause — *v'al ha-atid ligvot*, on money not yet collected — is the most remarkable. When *terumat ha-lishka* is taken and *korbanot* are purchased, the communal sacrifices are brought on behalf of the entire Jewish people, including towns that have not yet remitted their contribution for that year. The *teruma* conceptually encompasses even those future contributions. This ensures that the *korbanot* are genuinely *tzibur*, communal, even where some contributions come in later. However, this conceptual inclusion does not transfer ownership or discharge liability. The townspeople whose coins have not yet been collected remain responsible for their *shekel*. Only once their coins have been collected, dispatched, and the *hafrasha* has occurred — placing those specific coins within the conceptual orbit of the *lishka*'s *teruma* — are they discharged from responsibility. The distinction between *kedusha* as status and operative ownership remains critical throughout.

## Takana on Hekdesh

### Takana Justification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שְׁבוּעָה זוֹ תַּקָּנַת חֲכָמִים הִיא, שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם מְזַלְזְלִים בַּהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Rather, Rabbi Elazar said: This oath** of the agents **is a rabbinic ordinance,** instituted **so that people will not treat consecrated property with contempt.** If they knew that there is no oath, they would neglect to safeguard the shekels properly.

Having pressed *Rabbi Yochanan*'s approach to its limits, the *gemara* ultimately concludes with a different resolution. *Rabbi Elazar* states: *shvua zo takanas chachamim hi shelo yei kol bnei adam mezalzelin be-hekdeshos* — this oath is a rabbinic enactment, instituted so that people would not treat *hekdesh* negligently.

### Policy Rationale

The policy logic is straightforward. If it were publicly known that one who deals with *hekdesh* faces no oath and no payment consequences — neither a *shvuat ha-shomrim* nor *tashlumin* — people would be far less careful with sanctified property. The *takana* imposes an oath requirement as a check on carelessness, even where there is no *ikar din* basis for one. The parallel to the first *perek*'s ruling — that an oath is taken when splitting a disputed *tallis*, explained there too as *shvua zo takanas chachamim* — is apt.

### Scope of Takana

Whether this *takana* is limited to the specific context of *shekalim* — the most financially significant and logistically common category of *hekdesh* transport — or extends as a general principle to all *hekdesh* interactions is left open. What is clear is that the *gemara* is acknowledging a real gap: the Torah formally exempts *hekdesh* from the ordinary *shvuah* framework, yet the practical consequence of that exemption — unchecked carelessness toward sacred property — demands a corrective measure.

## Shomer Sachar Cases

### Payment Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם. רָמֵי לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא לְרַבָּה: תְּנַן, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל לִשְׁמוֹר אֶת הַפָּרָה, לִשְׁמוֹר אֶת הַתִּינוֹק, לִשְׁמוֹר אֶת הַזְּרָעִים – אֵין נוֹתְנִים לוֹ שְׂכַר שַׁבָּת, לְפִיכָךְ אֵין אַחְרָיוּת שַׁבָּת עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **A paid bailee does not pay** if these items were stolen or lost. **Rav Yosef bar Ḥama raises a contradiction before Rabba. We learned** in the mishna: **A paid bailee does not pay. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **One who hires** a day **laborer to watch the** red **heifer** to ensure that it is not disqualified, **to watch the child** to ensure that he remains ritually pure from birth in order to draw the water mixed with the ashes of the heifer, or **to safeguard the seeds** for the barley that will be used in the *omer* offering, **does not give him wages for Shabbat. Therefore, if** the items that the day laborer was entrusted to watch **were lost** on Shabbat, financial **responsibility for** their loss on **Shabbat is not** incumbent **upon him,** since he is not a paid bailee on that day.

The *gemara* now raises a fresh challenge. *Rav Yosef bar Chama* confronts *Rava* with a contradiction between our *mishnah*'s ruling that a *shomer sachar* is exempt from *gneiva va-aveida* when the object is *hekdesh*, and a *braita* that discusses paid watchmen whose employer is the *Beit HaMikdash* itself. The *braita* discusses three cases: one hired to guard the *parah adumah* — ensuring that no burden is placed upon it that would render it *passul* — one hired to be a babysitter for the children being raised in ritual purity in isolation to assist with the *parah adumah* ceremony, and one hired to guard seeds designated for the *omer* or for produce during *shmitta*.

In each case, the worker is paid by the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *braita* states: *ein notnim lo sechar Shabbat* — if payment is computed on a daily basis, the worker is not paid for *Shabbat*, because one may not pay wages specifically designated for *Shabbat* work. Consequently, *ein achrayut Shabbat alav* — he bears no liability as a *shomer sachar* on *Shabbat*, being at most a *shomer chinam* on that day.

### Havla'ah Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה שְׂכִיר שַׁבָּת, שְׂכִיר חֹדֶשׁ, שְׂכִיר שָׁנָה, שְׂכִיר שָׁבוּעַ – נוֹתְנִין לוֹ שְׂכַר שַׁבָּת, לְפִיכָךְ אַחְרָיוּת שַׁבָּת עָלָיו. מַאי לָאו לְשַׁלֵּם?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: But if he was a laborer **hired for a week, hired for a month, hired for a year,** or **hired for seven years,** the one who hired him **gives him wages** for labor performed on **Shabbat** as well. **Therefore,** if the items were lost on Shabbat, financial **responsibility for** their loss on **Shabbat is** incumbent **upon him. What, is it not** that he is liable **to pay** for the loss he caused? As the list of the items being safeguarded includes consecrated items, this *baraita* apparently rules that a paid bailee bears responsibility for consecrated property.

The *braita* then presents the countercase: if the worker is paid on a weekly, monthly, annual, or seven-year cycle, the *Shabbat* wages are included — *notnim lo sechar Shabbat* — and correspondingly, *achrayut Shabbat alav* — he bears *shomer sachar* liability on *Shabbat* as well. This is the principle of *havla'ah*: when one is paid for the whole week as a unit rather than day by day, the *Shabbat* component is absorbed into the general wage and is not singled out as payment for *Shabbat*. Crucially, this does not mean we conceptualize the arrangement as paying for six days with the seventh day thrown in free. The worker is genuinely considered paid for *Shabbat* — which is why he bears *shomer sachar* liability on *Shabbat* — but because the payment is not specifically designated for *Shabbat* work, it is halakhically acceptable. The prohibition is against directly paying for *Shabbat*; aggregated weekly pay does not violate that prohibition.

### Liability Problem

The deeper problem this *braita* poses is not about *Shabbat* but about the nature of the *shomer*'s liability altogether. In these cases, the objects being guarded — children, seeds, the *parah adumah* — are not themselves *hekdesh* in the ordinary sense. The exemption from *shomer* liability established by the *mishnah* derives from the verse *re'ehu*, "his neighbor," which the *gemara* reads as excluding *hekdesh* — *re'ehu v'lo hekdesh*. But that exclusion, strictly read, focuses on the nature of the contractual relationship and not exclusively on the sanctity of the object. Here the object may not be *hekdesh*, but the *shomer*'s contract is with the *Beit HaMikdash*. If the exclusion applies to this relationship, the *shomer* bears no liability — yet the *braita* treats him as a *shomer sachar* with standard liability. How?

### Kinyan Sudar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ בְּהָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to** Rav Yosef bar Ḥama: **Have you heard anything with regard to this?** Rav Yosef bar Ḥama **said to him** that **this** is what **Rav Sheshet says:** The ruling of the latter clause is stated **with regard to** a case **where** the laborers made a commitment to the one who hired them to take responsibility for the items, and **they performed** an act of **acquisition with** the one who hired them to reinforce that commitment. **And likewise Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ruling of the latter clause is stated **with regard to** a case **where** the laborers made a commitment to the one who hired them to take responsibility for the items, and **they performed** an act of **acquisition with him.**

The *gemara* presents the question, and after an initial silence, reports the answer in the name of *Rav Sheshet* and *Rabbi Yochanan*: *be-shekanu miyado* — the arrangement was effected through *kinyan sudar*, a formal act of contractual acquisition. When the *Beit HaMikdash* hired the watchman, both parties knew that the ordinary laws of *shomrim* would exempt him from liability. To create a binding obligation nonetheless, a separate *kinyan sudar* was performed — the watchman grasped a *sudar*, a garment or cloth, and formally accepted liability through this special contractual act. This obligation operates outside the default framework of *dinei shomrim* altogether, or alternatively, within it by the mechanism analogous to *Rabbi Meir*'s principle — *ha-matneh al mah shekatuv ba-Torah b'davar shebe-mamon, tna'o kayam* — that stipulations modifying monetary obligations in Torah law are valid. Either way, the watchman has voluntarily assumed a level of liability beyond what the *halacha* would otherwise impose.

### Kinyan Sudar Effects

This solution, taken together with the *takana* discussed in the previous section, effectively closes the gaps that the Torah's exemptions created. On the one hand, *Rabbi Elazar*'s *takana* ensures that even absent any other arrangement, people will not treat *hekdesh* negligently because an oath is required. On the other hand, *kinyan sudar* enables the *Beit HaMikdash* to contract directly for full *shomer* liability when it hires workers. The result is that one cannot easily escape responsibility when dealing with sacred property — despite the Torah's formal exemptions, the system of rabbinic enactment and private contractual mechanism fills the practical void. There is something striking in this: the Torah exempts *hekdesh* as a formal legal category, and the rabbinic and contractual response says, in effect, that the policy implications of such an exemption are unacceptable, and systematically corrects for them.

## Rabbi Shimon Kodashim

### Liability Determines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: קָדָשִׁים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן – יֵשׁ לָהֶן אוֹנָאָה. וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן – אֵין לָהֶן אוֹנָאָה. תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַבָּא:
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says: Sacrificial** animals **for which one bears responsibility** to replace **them are** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation, and** those **for which one does not bear responsibility** to replace **them are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation.** The Gemara relates that **the *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall **taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Abba** with regard to the source of the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. It is derived from the verse: "If anyone sins, and commits a trespass against the Lord, and deals falsely with his colleague in a matter of deposit, or of pledge, or of robbery, or has oppressed his neighbor" (Leviticus 5:21).

A *tanna* taught before *Rabbi Yitzchak*: *kodshim she-chayav be-achrayus chayav ve-she-eino chayav be-achrayus patur* — with respect to sanctified property that one bears financial responsibility for, one is liable; with respect to sanctified property for which one bears no responsibility, one is exempt. This reiterates *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle, applying it here to the laws of *shvuah* and *chiyuv* more broadly.

### Braita Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדָשִׁים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן, חַיָּיב, שֶׁאֲנִי קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן: ״בַּה׳ וְכִחֵשׁ״. וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן, פָּטוּר, שֶׁאֲנִי קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן: ״בַּעֲמִיתוֹ וְכִחֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** In that *baraita*, it is taught: For an oath concerning **sacrificial** animals **for which one bears responsibility,** one is **liable** to bring an offering for taking a false oath, **as I apply** the phrase **"against the Lord, and deals falsely." And** for an oath concerning sacrificial animals **for which one does not bear responsibility,** one is **exempt, as I read in their regard: With his neighbor, and deals falsely.** That reading indicates that even one who deals falsely in matters related to the Lord, e.g., sacrificial animals, is liable to bring an offering for a false oath.

The *gemara* analyzes the *braita* and initially notes that the reasoning seems backwards. *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle, as stated, derives the *chiyuv* from the words *ba-Hashem u-kichesh* — "and he betrayed God and he denied" — and the *patur* from *be-amito u-kichesh* — "and he denied to his neighbor." But one might have expected the logic to run in the opposite direction: when something is more closely associated with *Hashem* (*hekdesh*), it should be *patur*, and when associated with *amito* (a private party), it should be *chayav*. The *tanna* before *Rabbi Yitzchak* initially has no answer, and when asked, reports the following.

### Word Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak **said to him: Isn't it the opposite** [***kelapei layya***]?

The *pasuk* in question combines *ba-Hashem v'kichesh* with *be-amito v'kichesh*, effectively bracketing the word *kichesh* — "he denied" — between a reference to God and a reference to one's neighbor. The verse (*Vayikra* 5) lists the wrongs one may commit — *pikadon*, *tesumas yad*, *gazel*, *oshek* — and specifies that one who swears falsely about them is liable for the fifth and the *asham*.

### Amito Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶיסְמְיַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. הָכִי קָאָמַר: קָדָשִׁים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן – חַיָּיב, דְּאִיתְרַבּוּ מִ״בַּה׳ וְכִחֵשׁ״. וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן – פָּטוּר, דְּאִמְּעִיט מִ״בַּעֲמִיתוֹ וְכִחֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The opposite is reasonable.** An oath concerning sacrificial animals for which one does not bear responsibility is considered to be a matter related to the Lord even more than an oath concerning a sacrificial animal for which one bears responsibility, as in the latter case it is owned by the person in some respects. The *tanna* **said to him: Should I delete** this *baraita* because it is corrupted? Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Abba **said to him: No, this is what** the *baraita* is **saying:** For an oath taken concerning **sacrificial** animals **for which one bears responsibility,** one is **liable** to bring an offering for a false oath, **as it is included** due to the phrase **"against the Lord, and deals falsely."** It is derived from this that one is liable for taking a false oath even with regard to an item which belongs, to a certain degree, to the Lord. **And** with regard to **sacrificial** animals **for which one does not bear responsibility,** one is **exempt, as it is excluded** by the phrase: **With his neighbor and deals falsely.** It is derived from this that one is liable to bring an offering for a false oath only if it pertained to property that belongs to a layman, i.e., his neighbor, but not for an item that belongs completely to God, as is the case with regard to sacrificial animals for which one does not bear responsibility.

The interpretive key is that *ba-Hashem* teaches that there exist categories of *hekdesh* for which one can still be *chayav*, and *be-amito* defines the condition: only when there is an *amito* element — a component of private-party liability and ownership — is one *chayav* even on *hekdesh*. When that *amito* element is entirely absent — when the property is wholly the *Beit HaMikdash*'s responsibility with no private-party dimension — one is *patur*, excluded by the word *amito* itself. The principle therefore runs as follows: if the *hekdesh* item has an *amito* component (i.e., the person bears financial responsibility for it), the *chiyuv* applies — *kodshim she-chayav be-achrayus, chayav*. If there is no *amito* component, *de-itma'atu mi-ba-amito* — the word *amito* excludes it — and one is *patur*.

## Rabbi Yehudah Ona'ah

### Sefer Torah Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמּוֹכֵר סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, מַרְגָּלִית וּבְהֵמָה, אֵין לָהֶם אוֹנָאָה. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמּוֹכֵר סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, אֵין לָהּ אוֹנָאָה – לְפִי שֶׁאֵין קֵץ לְדָמֶיהָ. בְּהֵמָה וּמַרְגָּלִית, אֵין לָהֶם אוֹנָאָה – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָדָם רוֹצֶה לְזַוְּוגָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** in the case of **one who sells a Torah scroll, a pearl, or an animal,** those items **are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation. It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** in the case of **one who sells a Torah scroll,** it **is not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation, as there is no limit to its value.** It is the Torah of God, which is priceless. **An animal and a pearl are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation because a person seeks to pair them.** An animal is paired with an animal of similar strength so that they can be yoked together to work in the field. A pearl is paired with a similar pearl to fashion jewelry. Since there is a need to obtain a specific variant of these items, one is not particular about the price.

*Rabbi Yehudah* offers his own list of items excluded from *ona'ah*, distinct from the formal exemptions of the *mishnah* (land, slaves, documents, *hekdesh*). His list consists of a *sefer Torah*, a *margalit* (precious gem), and *behemah* (large animal). The *gemara* cites a *braita* explaining each. Regarding the *sefer Torah*: *ein ketz li-dameha* — there is no limit to its value. Whether this is an empirical observation about the variability of the market for *sifrei Torah*, or a theological statement that one cannot declare any price excessive for a *Torah* scroll, is a genuine question. Either way, the implication is that no transaction involving a *sefer Torah* can be attacked on the grounds that the buyer paid more than the item is worth.

### Matching Premium

For *margalit* and *behemah*, the reasoning is different: *mishum d'amar rotzeh l'zavgan* — because the buyer may say he wanted to match it. A precious gem that perfectly completes a matched set of jewelry commands a premium far above the ordinary market price for a stone of that size and quality. An ox whose physique and temperament are a precise match for another ox already in one's possession will plow far more efficiently, and one may reasonably pay far above the standard market rate to acquire it. Because these items are routinely purchased for their matching value, one cannot claim *ona'ah* simply because the price exceeded what a standalone item would fetch.

### Matching Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא הַכֹּל אָדָם רוֹצֶה לְזַוְּוגָן? וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָנֵי חֲשִׁיבִי לֵיהּ, וְהָנֵי לָא חֲשִׁיבִי לֵיהּ. וְעַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: עַד כְּדֵי דְּמֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The Rabbis **said to him: But isn't** it the case that with regard to **every item, a person seeks to pair** them with similar items under certain circumstances? According to your explanation, the *halakhot* of exploitation would never apply. The Gemara asks: **And** what does **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to that question? He claims that **these are significant to** a person, **but those are not significant to him.** In other words, it is particularly important to find a precise match for an animal and a pearl. The Gemara continues to analyze Rabbi Yehuda's opinion. **And up to how much** can one deviate from the value of items for which exploitation does not apply, as Rabbi Yehuda is clearly not saying that any deviation is acceptable? **Ameimar said:** One can deviate **up to** double **their value.**

The *gemara* pushes back: there are many items for which a buyer might seek a precise match and pay a premium accordingly. Fabric for a quilt, thread of a particular color, all manner of goods might attract a matching premium in particular circumstances. What distinguishes the *sefer Torah*, *margalit*, and *behemah* from everything else?

### Particular Value

*Rabbi Yehudah*'s distinction is that these items are *chashivei* — they are particularly and weighty enough in their importance that people regularly pay disproportionately for them. Other goods for which a matching premium sometimes applies are not sufficiently weighty that the market consistently accommodates such extreme departures from ordinary price.

### Doubling Limit

Even for these items, however, there is a threshold. *Amemar* rules: *ad kedei dmeihem* — up to double the standard value. If the buyer pays more than double the ordinary market price, the premium has exceeded what even the matching rationale can justify, and an *ona'ah* claim would lie. This is noteworthy: *Tosafot* had observed that even for the formally excluded categories — land, slaves, documents, *hekdesh* — there is *bitul mekach*, nullification of the sale, if the price is double or more than double. It is precisely this same threshold — double value — that applies here to *Rabbi Yehudah*'s categories, which are excluded not by formal rule but by the logic of the marketplace.

### War Goods Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמּוֹכֵר סוּס וְסַיִיף וַחֲטִיטוֹם בַּמִּלְחָמָה – אֵין לָהֶם אוֹנָאָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶם חַיֵּי נֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: Even** in the case **one who sells a horse, or a sword, or a helmet [*veḥatitom*] during wartime,** these items **are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation, because they** then **have** the capacity to preserve **life,** and a person is willing to pay any price for them.

A *tanna* taught before *Rav Nachman*: *Rabbi Yehudah ben Beteira* adds another category — one who sells a horse, a sword, or a shield during wartime: *af ha-mocher sus v'sayaf v'taris b'milchama ein lahem ona'ah*. No *ona'ah* claim lies against the seller of war materiel during war.

### Desperate Market

The rationale: *mipnei she-yesh ba-hen chayei nefesh* — because these items are matters of life and death. A soldier facing combat will pay whatever is necessary to equip himself. The market for these goods during wartime is fundamentally different from the peacetime market. The price that prevails under those conditions is the market price for those conditions. One cannot then invoke a peacetime benchmark and cry *ona'ah*. It is worth noting what this ruling does and does not say. The laws of *ona'ah* prohibit selling above the prevailing market rate but do not themselves set that market rate. The *gemara* elsewhere identifies a separate prohibition against price gouging on staple goods — *chayei nefesh* in the ordinary sense, bread and oil — but that is a different *issur* from *ona'ah*. *Rabbi Yehudah ben Beteira*'s ruling is simply that the wartime market for combat equipment is its own market, and the seller who charges accordingly has not violated *ona'ah*.

## Ona'at Devarim

### Price Without Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאוֹנָאָה בְּמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר, כָּךְ אוֹנָאָה בִּדְבָרִים. לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ: בְּכַמָּה חֵפֶץ זֶה? וְהוּא אֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה לִיקַּח. אִם הָיָה בַּעַל תְּשׁוּבָה, לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ: זְכוֹר מַעֲשֶׂיךָ הָרִאשׁוֹנִים. אִם הוּא בֶּן גֵּרִים, לֹא יֹאמַר לוֹ: זְכוֹר מַעֲשֵׂה אֲבוֹתֶיךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְגֵר לֹא תוֹנֶה וְלֹא תִלְחָצֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Just as** there is a prohibition against **exploitation [*ona'a*] in buying and selling, so is there *ona'a* in statements,** i.e., verbal mistreatment. The mishna proceeds to cite examples of verbal mistreatment. **One may not say to** a seller: **For how much** are you selling **this item, if he does not wish to purchase** it. He thereby upsets the seller when the deal fails to materialize. The mishna lists other examples: **If one is a penitent,** another **may not say to him: Remember your earlier deeds. If one is the child of converts,** another **may not say to him: Remember the deeds of your ancestors, as it is stated: "And a convert shall you neither mistreat, nor shall you oppress him"** (Exodus 22:20).

The *mishnah* now introduces the concept of *ona'at devarim* — the prohibition against afflicting another person with words. The first case: *lo yomar lo b'kama chefetz zeh v'hu eino rotzeh likach* — do not ask a storekeeper the price of an item if you have no intention of buying. This is a serious issue, and not merely a historical one. In the contemporary context, the problem is acute: entering a brick-and-mortar store, trying on merchandise, determining the right size and style, and then purchasing online at a reduced price exploits the physical retailer's services without any intention of patronizing his business. This is categorically *assur* as *ona'at devarim*.

### Modern Application

The prohibition is real and applies broadly. When employees work on commission, the injury is more direct — the salesperson invests time and energy expecting a sale that never comes. But even absent commission, the storekeeper's attention and investment of service are being taken advantage of. One simple mitigation: clearly stating at the outset that one is only browsing and has no present intent to buy, or that one is planning to purchase elsewhere, removes the deception and changes the interaction materially.

### Component Types

This first case sits at the intersection of business and interpersonal harm. The person asking the price leads the merchant to believe a transaction is possible, toys with his expectations and hopes, and then disappoints him — which has both a financial dimension (wasted time, lost opportunity to attend another customer) and a deeply personal emotional dimension. The cases that follow, however, are purely interpersonal, with no business component whatsoever.

### Past Deeds Cases

The *mishnah* continues: *im haya ba'al teshuva, lo yomru lo zechor ma'asecha ha-rishonim* — if someone is a *ba'al teshuva*, do not say to him, "Remember how you used to behave." *Im hu ben gerim, lo yomar lo zechor ma'aseh avotecha* — if he is the descendant of converts, do not say, "Remember what your fathers used to do." You need not even be attacking the person himself — reminding him that his father was a gentile is equally prohibited.

### Torah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא תוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ״ – בְּאוֹנָאַת דְּבָרִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּאוֹנָאַת דְּבָרִים, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאוֹנָאַת מָמוֹן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְכִי תִמְכְּרוּ מִמְכָּר לַעֲמִיתֶךָ אוֹ קָנֹה מִיַּד עֲמִיתֶךָ״, הֲרֵי אוֹנָאַת מָמוֹן אָמוּר! הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״לֹא תוֹנוּ אִישׁ אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ״? בְּאוֹנָאַת דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** It is written: **"And you shall not mistreat [*tonu*] one man his colleague;** and you shall fear your God, for I am the Lord your God" (Leviticus 25:17). The *tanna* explains: **The verse is speaking with regard to verbal mistreatment.** The *baraita* proceeds: Do **you say** that it is speaking of **verbal mistreatment [*be'ona'at devarim*], or** perhaps **it is** speaking **only with regard to monetary exploitation [*be'ona'at mammon*]? When it says** in a previous verse: **"And if you sell to your colleague an item that is sold, or acquire from your colleague's hand,** you shall not exploit [*tonu*] his brother" (Leviticus 25:14), **monetary exploitation is** explicitly **stated. How** then **do I realize** the meaning of the verse: **"And you shall not mistreat one man his colleague"?** It is **with regard to verbal mistreatment.**

The *mishnah* cites the verse *veger lo toneh velo tilchatzenu* as a source. It is significant that the *mishnah* shifts to the *ger* case, and that the verse cited speaks of afflicting and oppressing. This brings to the fore the core of *ona'at devarim*: unlike *ona'at mamon*, which is fundamentally about a financial transaction, *ona'at devarim* is about inflicting harm upon a person — injuring their dignity, their feelings, their sense of self. The verse's language of *ona'ah* and *lachatz* (oppression) communicates that we are dealing with something that reaches beyond commercial wrong into the domain of interpersonal injury.

The *gemara* returns to the Torah sources. *Vayikra* 25 contains two verses: verse 14 — *vechi timkru mimkar l'amitecha o kano miyad amitecha al tonu ish et achiv* — and verse 17 — *velo tonu ish et amito*. The first verse occurs in the context of *mimkar*, commerce, and addresses *ona'at mamon*. The second verse, which does not mention commerce, addresses *ona'at devarim*. The doubling of the prohibition is thus not redundant — each verse targets a distinct category of wrong.

### Braita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? אִם הָיָה בַּעַל תְּשׁוּבָה, אַל יֹאמַר לוֹ: ״זְכוֹר מַעֲשֶׂיךָ הָרִאשׁוֹנִים״. אִם הָיָה בֶּן גֵּרִים, אַל יֹאמַר לוֹ: ״זְכוֹר מַעֲשֵׂה אֲבוֹתֶיךָ״. אִם הָיָה גֵּר וּבָא לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, אַל יֹאמַר לוֹ: ״פֶּה שֶׁאָכַל נְבֵילוֹת וּטְרֵיפוֹת, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים, בָּא לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרָה מִפִּי הַגְּבוּרָה״?
>
> **Translation:** **How so? If one is a penitent,** another **may not say to him: Remember your earlier deeds. If one is the child of converts,** another **may not say to him: Remember the deed of your ancestors. If one is a convert and** he **came to study Torah,** one **may not say to him:** Does the **mouth that ate unslaughtered carcasses and animals that had wounds that would have caused them to die within twelve months [*tereifot*],** and **repugnant creatures, and creeping animals, comes to study Torah that was stated from the mouth of the Almighty?**

The *braita* elaborates: *im hayah ba'al teshuva, al yomar lo zechor ma'asecha ha-rishonim* — do not recall a returnee's past. *Im hayah ben gerim, al yomar lo zechor ma'aseh avotecha* — do not recall a convert's ancestry. And perhaps most striking: *im hayah ger u'va lilmod Torah* — if the person is himself a *ger* who has come to study *Torah* — *al yomar lo: peh she'achal neveilot u'treifot shekatzim u'remasim, ba lilmod Torah?* — do not say to him: "This mouth that ate forbidden foods comes to study *Torah*?" The injury here is not the recollection of a factual past but the weaponizing of that past to delegitimize the person's present aspirations and spiritual standing.

### Suffering Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיוּ יִסּוּרִין בָּאִין עָלָיו, אִם הָיוּ חֳלָאִים בָּאִין עָלָיו, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה מְקַבֵּר אֶת בָּנָיו, אַל יֹאמַר לוֹ כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאָמְרוּ לוֹ חֲבֵירָיו לְאִיּוֹב: ״הֲלֹא יִרְאָתְךָ כִּסְלָתֶךָ תִּקְוָתְךָ וְתֹם דְּרָכֶיךָ. זְכׇר נָא מִי הוּא נָקִי אָבָד״.
>
> **Translation:** **If torments are afflicting** a person, **if illnesses are afflicting him, or if he is burying his children,** one **may not speak to him in the manner that** the **friends of Job spoke to him: "Is not your fear of God your confidence, and your hope the integrity of your ways? Remember, I beseech you, whoever perished, being innocent?"** (Job 4:6–7). Certainly you sinned, as otherwise you would not have suffered misfortune.

The *braita* continues: *im hayu yissurin ba'in alav, o shehayah chola'im ba'in alav, o shehayah mekaber banav* — if afflictions come upon someone, or sickness, or God forbid he is burying his children — *al yomar lo kederech she'amru lo chaveirav le-Iyov* — do not say to him what *Iyov*'s so-called friends said. The friends suggested to *Iyov*: *halo yiratecha kislatecha* — was not your fear your confidence? *Tikvatecha v'tom derachecha* — your hope and the pureness of your ways? Meaning: you thought you were so righteous and upright — yet look what is happening to you. It must be punishment for some hidden sin. This pastoral catastrophe — attributing suffering to secret guilt, compounding a person's pain with accusation — is among the gravest forms of *ona'at devarim*.

### Fool's Errand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיוּ חַמָּרִים מְבַקְּשִׁין תְּבוּאָה מִמֶּנּוּ, לֹא יֹאמַר לָהֶם: ״לְכוּ אֵצֶל פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהוּא מוֹכֵר תְּבוּאָה״, וְיוֹדֵעַ בּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מָכַר מֵעוֹלָם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף לֹא יִתְלֶה עֵינָיו עַל הַמִּקָּח, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאֵין לוֹ דָּמִים, שֶׁהֲרֵי הַדָּבָר מָסוּר לַלֵּב, וְכׇל דָּבָר הַמָּסוּר לַלֵּב נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ ״וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **if donkey drivers are asking** to purchase **grain from** someone, and he has none, **he may not say to them: Go to so-and-so, as he sells grain, if he knows about him that he never sold** grain at all. He thereby causes the donkey drivers and the would-be seller anguish. **Rabbi Yehuda says: One may not even cast his eyes on the merchandise** for sale, creating the impression that he is interested, **at a time when he does not have money** to purchase it. Verbal mistreatment is not typically obvious, and it is difficult to ascertain the intent of the offender, **as the matter is given to the heart** of each individual, as only he knows what his intention was when he spoke. **And with regard to any matter given to the heart, it is stated: "And you shall fear your God"** (Leviticus 25:17), as God is privy to the intent of the heart.

Another case from the *braita*: *im hayu chamarin mevakshim tvu'ah mimenu, lo yomar lahem lechu eitzel ploni she-hu mocher tvu'ah, v'yodeia bo shelo machar me'olam* — if donkey drivers come asking where to buy grain, do not send them to someone you know has never sold grain in his life, just to get these unwanted visitors away from you. This case is notable for being a different kind of injury. The person being harmed is not insulted or shamed — he is tricked and sent on a fool's errand. His feelings are not exactly hurt in the way a *ba'al teshuva*'s are hurt. He wastes time, effort, and perhaps money, and eventually feels the sting of having been misled. The scope of *ona'at devarim* is thus broader than shaming or emotional injury in the narrow sense — it encompasses any use of words to cause harm or disadvantage to another person.

### Non-Verbal Ona'ah

*Rabbi Yehudah* extends the prohibition even beyond words: *afilu yitle einav al ha-mekach bi-sha'ah she-ein lo damim* — do not even fix your gaze upon merchandise when you have no money to purchase it, giving the merchant the impression that a sale is imminent. The word *devarim* does not appear in the *pasuk* itself — *lo tonu* alone — which perhaps licenses *Rabbi Yehudah*'s extension to non-verbal conduct. The underlying wrong in all these cases is the same: creating false impressions, manipulating expectations, and thereby injuring another.

### Davar Masur L'Lev

The *gemara* notes: *harei hu davar masur la-lev* — this is a matter given over to the heart, i.e., the inner intention is the determining factor. A person might claim, "I really was thinking of buying," or "I honestly thought that other person sold grain." Since only the individual knows his true intent, and no court can verify it, the *pasuk* appends *ve-yareta me-Elohecha* — you shall fear your God. God knows what was in the heart. It is the awareness that one cannot hide inner intention from the Divine that is meant to restrain behavior in cases where external scrutiny cannot reach.

## Ona'ah Severity

### Yochanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: גָּדוֹל אוֹנָאַת דְּבָרִים מֵאוֹנָאַת מָמוֹן, שֶׁזֶּה נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ ״וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ״, וְזֶה לֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ ״וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ״. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: זֶה בְּגוּפוֹ וְזֶה בְּמָמוֹנוֹ. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: זֶה נִיתַּן לְהִישָּׁבוֹן, וְזֶה לֹא נִיתַּן לְהִישָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: Greater is** the transgression of **verbal mistreatment than** the transgression of **monetary exploitation, as with regard to this,** verbal mistreatment, **it is stated: "And you shall fear your God." But with regard to that,** monetary exploitation, **it is not stated: "And you shall fear your God." And Rabbi Elazar said** this explanation: **This,** verbal mistreatment, affects **one's body; but that,** monetary exploitation, affects **one's money. Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says: This,** monetary exploitation, **is given to restitution; but that,** verbal mistreatment, **is not given to restitution.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai* states: *gadol ona'at devarim mi-ona'at mamon* — *ona'at devarim* is a graver sin than financial *ona'ah*.

### Yirat Elohim

The proof: *she-ze ne'emar bo ve-yareta me-Elohecha ve-ze lo ne'emar bo ve-yareta me-Elohecha* — the verse addressing *ona'at devarim* includes the phrase *ve-yareta me-Elohecha*, fear your God, whereas the verse addressing *ona'at mamon* does not. As established earlier in the *sugya*, *ve-yareta me-Elohecha* signals not merely that intention is hidden and left to God's knowledge, but that Divine punishment is imminent and deserved. The presence of that phrase by *ona'at devarim* and its absence by *ona'at mamon* thus indicates that *ona'at devarim* carries a more severe Divine response.

### Elazar: Person vs. Pocket

*Rabbi Elazar* articulates the substantive reason behind the severity: *ze be-gufo ve-ze be-mamono* — one injures the person, the other injures the pocket. *Ona'at devarim* strikes at a human being's dignity, sense of self, emotional wellbeing, and inner life. *Ona'at mamon* strikes at one's financial position. The financial injury may feel deeply personal — one who has been cheated feels taken advantage of and violated — but there remains a categorical difference between an injury to the self and an injury to the wallet. When words cut, they wound something that cannot be fully separated from the person who was wounded.

### Nachmani: Irreversibility

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* adds a further dimension: *ze nitan le-hishavon ve-ze lo nitan le-hishavon* — financial loss is correctable; verbal harm is not. Money stolen or overcharged can be returned. The wronged party can be made financially whole. Words, once spoken, cannot be taken back. An apology can be offered, restitution of dignity attempted, but the words remain — people carry them for decades, and no sum of money neutralizes what was said. It is the irreversibility of verbal injury that makes it, in this dimension, categorically more serious than financial wrong.
