# Shevuot 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamidim Redemption

### Ulla's Statement

The *daf* opens at the very first line of 11a, with the statement transmitted by Ulla in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: when the *tamidim* — the daily offerings — were set aside at the start of the year, four animals would be kept in reserve so that there would always be replacements available. When the new year arrived, any animals that remained unused from the previous year's supply could be redeemed even without a blemish. This is a striking ruling, since the general principle is that a consecrated animal cannot be redeemed unless it develops a blemish. How, then, does this work?

### Kedusha Disappearance

The challenge is articulated sharply: *keddusha she-hen l'heichan halcha* — where does the *kedusha* go? If these animals have been sanctified, how can that sanctity simply dissolve without the standard mechanism of a physical blemish? As a preview of where the *Gemara* will ultimately arrive, the resolution will be the principle of *lev beis din matneh aleihem* — since these animals were sanctified with communal funds, and it was always understood that there would be surplus, the *beis din* implicitly stipulated from the outset that leftover animals would not carry full *kedushat haguf*. That answer, however, is developed later; for now, the *Gemara* is working through the preliminary difficulties.

### Ketores Proof

As an initial proof that redeeming such items without a blemish is possible, the *Gemara* appeals to the *ketores*. The *ketores* presents an analogous situation: it was prepared in bulk at the beginning of the year, and at year's end there would inevitably be a remainder. That remainder could be redeemed despite the fact that the *ketores* presumably possesses *kedushat haguf* — the mortar in which it is ground is considered a *kli shareis*, and once it has been placed into a *kli shareis* and rendered fit for use, it should have *kedushat haguf*. Yet it can nonetheless be redeemed. This, the argument goes, is a precedent for the same principle applying to the *tamidim*.

The response to this proof, which appears at the end of 11b, is: *amar lei — ktores kamarta? Sha'ani ktores d'kedushat damim hi* — the *ketores* is different, because the *ketores* only has *kedushat damim*, monetary sanctity. According to this response, the mortar — the *machteshet* — is not in fact a *kli shareis*. Therefore, even though the *ketores* has been verbally consecrated, it has not yet been elevated to the status of readiness for offering, and consequently it retains only *kedushat damim*, not *kedushat haguf*. Anything possessing only *kedushat damim* can be redeemed straightforwardly — not through the mechanism of a blemish, but simply because it was never intrinsically sanctified for use on the *mizbeach*.

## Damim vs HaGuf

### Ketores Kedushat Damim

The *Gemara* now picks up at 11a to examine whether the *ketores* can really be dismissed as a case of mere *kedushat damim* after it has been ground. This is where the real analytical work of the *sugya* begins.

### Tvul Yom Challenge

The challenge is direct: *lo tipasel b'tvul yom* — if the *ketores* only has *kedushat damim* even after grinding, why would it become *pasul* if touched by a *tvul yom*? A *tvul yom* is someone who is in a state of residual *tumah* — they have immersed in a *mikveh* but the sun has not yet set, and they therefore retain the status of a *sheini l'tumah*. Such a person can render something *kodesh* into *pasul* — but only if that item has *kedushat haguf*. If the *ketores* were truly only *kedushat damim*, a *tvul yom* should have no power to disqualify it.

### Braita on Ketores

The *Gemara* cites a *braita* explicitly: *nittal m'machteshet v'nifseles b'tvul yom* — once the *ketores* is removed from the mortar, a *tvul yom* can render it *pasul*. This indicates that the *ketores* acquires *kedushat haguf* at the moment of grinding, even before it is placed into any subsequent *kli shareis*. The proposed dismissal of the *ketores* as a case of mere *kedushat damim* is therefore untenable.

### Meilah & Pasul

One might attempt to resist this conclusion by arguing that even *kedushat damim* renders an item susceptible to disqualification by a *tvul yom*. The *Gemara* forecloses this by citing the tractate *Me'ilah*: *ha-minchot mo'alin bahen misheyukdashu* — the laws of *meilah* apply to *menachot* from the moment of verbal sanctification. But the text then adds: *kidshu bikeli huchsharu lipasel b'tvul yom u-vi-mechusar kipurim u-vi-lina* — only once the *mincha* is sanctified within a *kli shareis* does it become susceptible to disqualification by a *tvul yom*, by a *mechusar kipurim* (one who has completed immersion but has not yet brought the final purification offering — such as a woman after childbirth, a *zav*, or a *metzora*), and by *lina* (overnight disqualification, which applies once an item is fit to be offered). The implication is unambiguous: verbal sanctification alone — *kedushat damim* — does not suffice to make an item susceptible to these forms of *pesul*. Only *kedushat haguf*, conferred through placement in a *kli shareis*, creates that susceptibility. Consequently, the fact that the *ketores* becomes *pasul* through contact with a *tvul yom* once it has been ground demonstrates that it does possess *kedushat haguf* from that moment onward — and yet it can be redeemed at year's end. The proof stands.

## Lina Discrepancy

### Braita-Mishna Gap

Having established that the *ketores* carries *kedushat haguf* from the moment of grinding, the *Gemara* raises a further difficulty. The same *mishna* in *Me'ilah* that listed the effects of *kedushat haguf* included not only *tvul yom* and *mechusar kipurim* but also *lina* — overnight disqualification. The logic of *lina* is that once an item is fit to be offered, it must be offered that same day; if it remains overnight, it becomes *pasul*. By the same token, if the *ketores* has *kedushat haguf* from the moment it is ground, it should also be susceptible to *lina* from that same moment.

The *Gemara* presses this point: *ela me'ata tipasel b'lina* — if you claim *kedushat haguf* from the time of grinding, then the *ketores* should become *pasul* with *lina* immediately after grinding. But we know that the *ketores* is ground at the beginning of the year and sits for the entire year without any issue of *lina*. How can this be reconciled?

A closer look reveals that there is an apparent contradiction between two sources. The *braita* states that the *ketores* is susceptible to *tvul yom* once it is removed from the mortar — implying that the *machteshet* is a *kli shareis* and that *kedushat haguf* is acquired there. But the *mishna* in *Me'ilah* formulates the rule as: once sanctified in a *kli*, it becomes susceptible to *tvul yom* and *lina* together. If the *mishna*'s *kli* is a later vessel — not the *machteshet* — then the two sources locate the onset of *kedushat haguf* at different points. If the *mishna*'s reference to *lina* represents a later stage, the mention of *tvul yom* in that same context must be understood as non-exclusive, bundled together with other items in a list. The fundamental question remains: at which precise moment does the *ketores* acquire *kedushat haguf*, and how do we reconcile the apparent gap between the two sources on the question of *lina*?

### Rashi: No Lina

The *Gemara* offers an initial answer: *sha'ani ktoret ho'il v'tzurata b'chol hashana kula* — the *ketores* is different because it maintains its appearance throughout the entire year. Rashi understands this to mean that the concept of *lina* simply does not apply to *ketores* at all. The underlying rationale of *lina* is that something begins to "go stale" or deteriorate once it has been prepared and then left overnight. The *ketores*, however, remains as fresh a year later as it was when first ground. Since *lina* as a concept presupposes some form of degradation over time, it is inapplicable to *ketores*. According to Rashi, the *ketores* therefore has full *kedushat haguf* from the moment of grinding, and the *mishna*'s mention of *lina* for the later stage is merely *derabanan*. There is no *lina* issue at any point in the year.

### Tosafot: Lina Timing

Tosafot offers what is described as a superior explanation. Rather than eliminating *lina* entirely from the *ketores*, Tosafot argues that the *lina* clock does not begin at the moment of initial grinding. Because the *ketores* is prepared in bulk at the start of the year and is understood from the outset to be designated for use throughout the entire year, it is not yet considered to be "at the cusp of being offered." The clock for *lina* begins only when the *ketores* has been transferred into the vessel from which it will actually be offered — not merely the *machteshet* which confers *kedushat haguf*, but the subsequent *kli* that represents readiness for immediate offering. The *machteshet* confers *kedushat haguf*, but *lina* — which tracks the transition from readiness to offering — is measured from the later stage of preparation, when a specific portion is taken for use on that day. This explanation elegantly accounts for why the *braita* and the *mishna* can both be accurate, each speaking of a different halakhic threshold.

### Ketores Full Kedusha

With this resolution in place, the *Gemara* returns to its original point: the *ketores* does indeed have *kedushat haguf* from the moment of grinding, and yet it can be redeemed at the end of the year. This constitutes solid support for Rabbi Yochanan's position regarding the surplus *tamidim*. Just as the *ketores* — an item bought with communal funds, possessing *kedushat haguf* — can be redeemed at year's end, so too the leftover *tamidim* can be redeemed without a blemish. But the *Gemara* immediately presses further: *v'chi kdusha shebahem l'heichan halcha?* — the fact of the *ketores* precedent does not yet explain the *mechanism* by which the *kedusha* can be dissolved. That explanation is what must now be supplied.

## Lev Beis Din

### Rabba's Answer

Rabba provides the central resolution of the *sugya*: *lev beis din matneh aleihem* — the *beis din* implicitly stipulates conditional sanctity. The *beis din* that represents the Jewish public and that purchases these items with the funds of the *terumat halishka* understands from the outset that there will be surplus. Their implicit stipulation is: *im hutzrechu hutzrechu* — if the items purchased turn out to be needed and used on the *mizbeach*, then they are fully sanctified with *kedushat haguf*. *V'im lav yehiyu dmeihem* — but if they turn out not to be used, then they were never intended to carry *kedushat haguf*; they were intended only as *kedushat damim* for the monetary value they represent. At year's end, the leftover *ketores* or unused animals are therefore in the same category as someone who sanctified a non-sacred object for its monetary value — they carry only *kedushat damim* and can be redeemed straightforwardly, without any need for a blemish.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye objects: did not Rabba himself rule that if one sanctifies a male unblemished animal for *kedushat damim* — intending only that the proceeds be used by the *Beis HaMikdash* — the sanctity nonetheless automatically upgrades to *kedushat haguf*? The logic is that if the animal itself could serve as an *olah*, it is pointless to speak of merely selling it and using the money; the animal simply becomes the *olah* directly, eliminating the middleman. If so, the same reasoning should apply here: even if the *beis din* stipulates *kedushat damim*, the moment the stipulation lapses and the animal is deemed unused, it stands there as a perfectly fit animal — and should automatically be elevated to *kedushat haguf*.

### Earmarking Answer

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between different varieties of *kedushat damim*: *ha de-amar lidmei olah v'ha de-amar lidmei nesachim* — the automatic upgrade to *kedushat haguf* operates only when the *kedushat damim* is specifically earmarked for the purpose of an *olah*. If someone sanctifies a sheep and declares that its proceeds should be used for an *olah*, then since the *kedushat damim* is targeted toward acquiring something to be offered on the *mizbeach*, and this animal itself could serve in that role, the upgrade happens automatically. But if the *kedushat damim* is earmarked for wine offerings, or for repairs to the *Beis HaMikdash*, or for any other purpose besides an *olah* to be offered on the *mizbeach* itself, the upgrade does not occur. The sanctity remains *kedushat damim* directed toward its declared purpose, and the animal is sold accordingly.

### Application

The application to the present case follows naturally. The *beis din*'s implicit stipulation earmarks any surplus not for use on the *mizbeach* but for other *Beis HaMikdash* needs — specifically, the funds remaining from the *terumat halishka* at year's end were known to be redirected toward communal *nedava* offerings or *Beis HaMikdash* maintenance. Since the *kedushat damim* of the surplus is thus earmarked for non-*mizbeach* purposes, it does not trigger the automatic upgrade to *kedushat haguf*, and the animals or *ketores* can be redeemed without a blemish.

## Uncommon Cases

### Lost YK Animals

Abaye is still not satisfied and raises a further challenge from a *mishna*: *paro v'se'iro shel Yom HaKippurim she-avedu* — if the bull and goat of Yom Kippur that were designated for the *Kodesh HaKodashim* service were lost, and replacements were set aside in their stead, and then the originals were found, what is to be done with them? Similarly, the goats brought when the entire community sinned through *avodah zarah* on the basis of a *beis din* ruling — if those designated animals were lost and replacements brought, and the originals were then found, what then? The *mishna* states: *kullan yamutu* — they must all be left to die. This is the view of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that a *chatas* whose owners have achieved atonement through a replacement animal — a *chatas shekapru be'alav b'acher* — must be left to die. This applies even to a communal *chatas*.

Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon disagree: *yiru ad sheyista'avu v'yimkeru v'yiplu dmeihem l'nedava* — they should be left to graze until they develop a blemish, at which point they can be redeemed, and the proceeds used as an *olah* donation. This is because the principle that a *chatas* must be left to die applies only to an individual's *chatas*, not to a communal one. The question then becomes: according to Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon, why must one wait for a blemish? Why not simply invoke *lev beis din matneh aleihem* — these are communal animals, implicitly stipulated to carry only *kedushat damim* if they cannot be used for their designated purpose? And according to Rabbi Yehuda, who says they must be left to die, why does *lev beis din* not rescue the situation at all?

### Rare vs Common

The answer is straightforward: *sha'ani avudim d'lo shechichi* — the case of an animal that gets lost is uncommon. Rashi adds that these particular *korbanot* themselves are not common occurrences. The principle of *lev beis din matneh aleihem* operates only where surplus or non-use is anticipated and standard. When the *beis din* purchases *ketores* in bulk at the start of the year, it is known with certainty that there will be leftover. When four extra *tamidim* are kept in reserve, it is known that some will remain. The *beis din* makes its implicit stipulation precisely because it is thinking about what will typically occur. An animal that gets lost is a freak occurrence that no one anticipates, and the *beis din* does not formulate its stipulation with such outlier scenarios in mind.

### Parah Adumah

No sooner is this distinction established than the *Gemara* introduces a counter-case: *v'harei parah d'lo shechicha* — but consider the *parah adumah*, which is also uncommon. Yet a *braita* teaches that a *parah adumah* can be redeemed in a wide range of circumstances: if any defect is found (*parah nifdes al kol psul shebah*), if it died before *shechita*, if it was slaughtered in the wrong location, or even if a more beautiful specimen was found (*matza acheres na'ah himenu tipadeh*). In all of these situations, the *parah* can be redeemed — and the case of finding a more beautiful animal explicitly involves no blemish whatsoever. The only exception is after it has been slaughtered in the proper location on the proper pyre, at which point it can no longer be redeemed — not because redemption is legally impossible, but, as Rashi explains, because it would be demeaning to the sanctified item at that stage. The question stands: if the lost Yom Kippur animals cannot be rescued by *lev beis din* because their loss is uncommon, how can the *parah adumah* be redeemed in these scenarios if the *parah adumah* is itself an uncommon occurrence?

### Parah as Bedek

The *Gemara* answers: *sha'ani parah d'kodshei vedek habayis hi* — the *parah adumah* is categorically different because it belongs to *kodshei bedek habayis*, not *kodshei mizbeach*. This is a distinction worth pausing over. There are two orthogonal axes in the laws of sanctity: *kedushat haguf* versus *kedushat damim* (intrinsic sanctity versus monetary sanctity), and *kodshei mizbeach* versus *kodshei bedek habayis* (altar sanctity versus Temple-maintenance sanctity). These are not synonymous. One could have something that is *kedushat haguf l'bedek habayis* — a beam designated for construction of the *Beis HaMikdash*, for instance, is intrinsically sanctified but not for the *mizbeach*. The *parah adumah* occupies this category: it is used — not sold — so it is not simply *kedushat damim*, but the purpose of its use is not as a *korban* on the *mizbeach*. It is slaughtered outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, its blood is sprinkled facing the *Beis HaMikdash* but not within it, and its body is burned. It is therefore *kedushat haguf l'bedek habayis*, not *kodshei mizbeach*.

Because it is *kodshei bedek habayis* rather than *kodshei mizbeach*, it can be redeemed without a blemish — this is the standard rule for *kodshei bedek habayis* and requires no special invocation of *lev beis din*. This explains why finding a more beautiful *parah* allows redemption of the original: from the outset, its sanctity was of the *bedek habayis* variety, which carries no prohibition against redemption for a wholesome animal.

The *Gemara* confirms this by identifying the ruling with Rabbi Shimon specifically, who holds that *kodshei mizbeach* requires *ha'amada v'ha'aracha* — the animal must be presented alive before a *kohen* for evaluation in order to be redeemed — whereas *kodshei bedek habayis* does not require this. That is why the *parah adumah* can be redeemed even if it died: *kodshei bedek habayis* has no requirement of *ha'amada v'ha'aracha*.

A difficulty then arises: the *braita* states that Rabbi Shimon holds the *parah* has a *sha'as hakosher* — a moment when it could theoretically have been eaten — and this is the basis on which *parah* is considered susceptible to the *tumah* of food. Reish Lakish explains that this *sha'as hakosher* refers to the moment immediately after *shechita*, because Rabbi Shimon holds that the *parah* could have been redeemed even at that point. If Rabbi Shimon would allow redemption even after proper *shechita*, then the *braita* that prohibits redemption once the *parah* is slaughtered on its pyre cannot be Rabbi Shimon's view. This leaves open the question of who authored that *braita*, and whether the ruling permitting redemption of a dead *parah* can still be attributed to Rabbi Shimon's reasoning about *kodshei bedek habayis*.

### Parah Cost Clause

The *Gemara* therefore retreats to an alternative explanation: *ela sha'ani parah ho'il v'dameha yekarin* — the *parah adumah* is different because its price is very high. Since the *parah adumah* is extraordinarily costly, there is always an implicit understanding on the part of *beis din* that it must be possible to redeem it if circumstances require — and this implicit stipulation constitutes a form of *lev beis din matneh aleihem* that operates even in uncommon cases.

This explanation is admittedly difficult to fully comprehend in terms of its practical logic. If the animal has died or been replaced, what precisely is being salvaged by redemption? The answer offered by the *Gemara* via Rav Kahana is that even if all one can salvage is the leather — like the ear of a camel that died in one's care — people will want to feel that something was recouped from a very costly investment. The *beis din* therefore built its stipulation around that psychological and financial reality, allowing at minimum the salvage value to be realized. This may be the practical content of *dameha yekarin* as a basis for *lev beis din*.

The upshot of the entire discussion is therefore as follows. The *ketores* and the surplus *tamidim* — anticipated surpluses from communal purchases — can be redeemed without a blemish because *lev beis din matneh aleihem* applies in standard, foreseeable cases. The lost bull and goat of Yom Kippur — a freak occurrence — receive no such benefit; one must either let them die (Rabbi Yehuda) or wait for a blemish (Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon). The *parah adumah* presents its own sui generis analysis: it is *kodshei bedek habayis*, not *kodshei mizbeach*, and can be redeemed without a blemish on that basis; and additionally, because of its great cost, *lev beis din* furnishes a supplementary basis for flexibility in its redemption.

## Regalim Substitution

### Mishna Position

The *Gemara* now turns back to our *mishna*, where the Rabbis challenge Rabbi Shimon: *mahu sheyakrivu zeh b'zeh* — can the communal *korbanot* of one *regel* substitute for the *musaf korbanot* of another? Rabbi Shimon answers: *yikrevu* — yes, they may. When pressed as to how, given that each set of *korbanot* atones for slightly different dimensions of *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*, Rabbi Shimon replies: *kulam ba'in l'chaper al tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — all of them come to atone for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*, and that is sufficient commonality to allow substitution among them.

### Why Not Lev Beis Din

The *Gemara* then asks: *v'amai? leima lev beis din matneh aleihem* — why did Rabbi Shimon need to invoke the reasoning that all these *korbanot* share the same atonement category? Why could he not have simply said that since these animals were purchased with communal funds, the *beis din* implicitly stipulated from the outset that if one set cannot be used for its original designation, it may be applied toward a related need? This would be a simpler and perhaps more powerful argument. Tosafot acknowledges the difficulty of why this argument is not deployed here, given that we have been somewhat restricting the scope of *lev beis din* to anticipated surpluses — and the scenario of *korbanot* being lost and then recovered is arguably more like the uncommon lost-animal cases. Nevertheless, the question stands as to why Rabbi Shimon does not reach for *lev beis din* as a basis.

### School Dispute

The *Gemara*'s answer is decisive: *Rabbi Shimon ka'amart? Rabbi Shimon leit lei lev beis din matneh aleihem* — Rabbi Shimon simply does not accept the principle of *lev beis din matneh aleihem*. He rejects it entirely. Therefore he cannot invoke it; it is not part of his halakhic framework.

### Rabbanan vs Shimon

The proof is brought by a chain of tradition: *amar Rav Idi bar Abin amar Rav Amram amar Rabbi Yochanan* — the very statement with which this *daf* opened, in Rabbi Yochanan's name via Ulla, is now quoted again with its full attribution. Regarding the surplus *tamidim*, *l'divrei Rabbi Shimon ein nifdin temimim* — according to Rabbi Shimon, the surplus *tamidim* cannot be redeemed while still unblemished; one must wait for a blemish. *U-l'divrei Chachamim nifdin temimim* — but according to the Sages who disagree with Rabbi Shimon, the surplus *tamidim* can be redeemed even while unblemished.

The entire principle of *lev beis din matneh aleihem* that the *Gemara* has been developing across this *daf* — the implicit stipulation of the *beis din* that governs how communal sanctity operates with respect to surplus — is thus the position of the Rabbanan, not of Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon holds that once *kedushat haguf* has adhered in an animal, no stipulation can pre-empt it, and a blemish is required for redemption. The Rabbanan hold that the *beis din*'s implicit prior stipulation can qualify the nature of the *kedusha* itself, such that anticipated surplus never fully acquires *kedushat haguf* and can therefore be redeemed without a blemish. This is the fundamental *machloket* that will be elaborated further in the *daf* that follows.
