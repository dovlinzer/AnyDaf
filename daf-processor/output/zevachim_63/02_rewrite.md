# Zevachim 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Menachot Overview

### Opening & Context

Today's daf is 63, and we begin with the next *mishnah*. What makes this *mishnah* particularly interesting is that we are now moving into the subject of *menachot* — a topic that, of course, has an entire *masechet* devoted to it — but which appears here precisely to provide a contrast with what came before.

The previous *perek* was organized around the major distinctions governing *korbanot*: *kodshei kodashim* were slaughtered in the north of the *azarah*, while *kodshei kalim* were not; *kodshei kodashim* were eaten only within the *azarah*, *lifnim min hake'laim*, while the *shlamim*, as a representative *kodshei kalim*, could be eaten throughout all of *Yerushalayim*. That was the dominant framework of the previous *perek*. The opening of our *perek* then raised certain variations — what happens if one slaughters *kodshei kodashim* at the top of the *mizbe'ach* — essentially wrapping up residual questions from before. Now, with this *mishnah*, we turn to the *mincha*, which provides a revealing contrast.

### Mishnah: Kemitzah Anywhe…

The contrast is immediately striking. A *mincha* is, in every conventional sense, *kodshei kodashim*: it is eaten only by male *kohanim*, and only within the *azarah*. One might therefore have expected that the primary act of the *mincha* — the *kemitzah*, the taking of the handful of flour which is the analog to *shechitah* for animal offerings — would likewise be restricted to a specific location, presumably the north. But the *mishnah* opens by declaring otherwise:

> מְנָחוֹת הָיוּ נִקְמָצוֹת בְּכָל מָקוֹם בָּעֲזָרָה

*Menachot hayu nikmatzot bechol makom ba'azarah* — the *kemitzah* of *menachot* could be performed anywhere in the *azarah*; it was not restricted to the north.

The *mishnah* then continues with language that is virtually word-for-word identical to the rules governing *kodshei kodashim* in other respects: eaten by male *kohanim*, within the *azarah*, during the day and the following night until midnight, and so forth. The purpose of this *mishnah*, then, is specifically to highlight this one surprising divergence — that the location of the *kemitzah* carries no geographic restriction within the courtyard.

### Menachot vs. Kodshei Kod…

This sets up the central analytic tension of the *perek*: in what ways does the *mincha* follow the rules of *kodshei kodashim*, and in what ways does it deviate? The *mishnah* demonstrates that despite its status as *kodshei kodashim*, the *mincha* does not carry the northern requirement for its defining sacrificial act. The *Gemara* will now explore both the outer limits — can the *kemitzah* be performed even inside the *heichal*? — and the inner logic — why does the *mincha* not require the north at all?

---

## Mincha in Heichal

### R. Elazar: Heichal Kosher

Before the *Gemara* addresses the broad question of why the *mincha* is not restricted to the north, it first considers a more extreme possibility at the other end of the spectrum. Rabbi Elazar teaches:

> מִנְחָה שֶׁנִּקְמְצָה בַּהֵיכָל כְּשֵׁרָה

*Mincha shenikmetza baheichal kesheira* — a *mincha* whose *kemitzah* was performed inside the *heichal* itself is valid. This is already a *chiddush* of a different magnitude. We have established that the *kemitzah* is not restricted to the north; but does "anywhere in the *azarah*" encompass even the inner sanctum, the chamber housing the *shulchan* and the *menorah*?

### Lechem Hapanim Proof

Rabbi Elazar's source is the practice surrounding the *lechem hapanim*. The *lechem hapanim* is itself a form of *mincha*. Its *kemitzah*-equivalent — the act that permits the bread to be consumed — was not a taking of flour from the bread itself, but rather the removal of the two small bowls of frankincense resting atop the *shulchan*, which were then burned on the outer altar. That act of removal was performed inside the *heichal*, since that is where the *shulchan* stood. From this precedent, we learn that acts constituting the *kemitzah* of a *mincha* may legitimately be performed within the *heichal*.

### Heichal Permissibility

One might object — and the point is well-taken — that the case of the *lechem hapanim* was one of *ee efshar*, where there was no alternative: the frankincense was in the *heichal* and had to be taken from there. Perhaps where a choice exists, the *heichal* would be excluded. The *Gemara* will continue to engage this question, but for the moment, the ruling stands: the *heichal* is a permissible location for *kemitzah*, even if it is not the required one.

## R. Yirmiyah Challenge

### Kemitzah Restriction

Rabbi Yirmiyah challenges this ruling from a *baraita*. The verse describing the *kemitzah* reads:

> וְקָמַץ מִשָּׁם בִּמְלֹא קֻמְצוֹ מִסֹּלֶת הַמִּנְחָה וּמִשַּׁמְנָהּ עַל כָּל לְבֹנָתָהּ

*Vekamatz misham bimlo kumtzo misolet hamincha umishmanah ve'al kol levonasah* — and he shall take from *there* his handful of the fine flour of the *mincha* and of its oil. The *baraita* reads the word *misham* — "from there" — as delimiting the zone of valid *kemitzah*: only from the place where a *zar*, a non-*kohen*, may stand. A non-*kohen* may not enter the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbe'ach*, may not enter the *ulam*, and certainly may not enter the *heichal*. If the zone of valid *kemitzah* is wherever the *zar* may stand, then the *heichal* is explicitly excluded.

### Kemitzah Redo

Parenthetically, the *baraita* includes a ruling from Ben Beteira that illuminates how the word *misham* is being used. Ben Beteira asks: if the *kohen* performed the *kemitzah* with his left hand, what is the *din*? May he replace the flour and take the *kemitzah* again with his right hand? In this he differs from the rule governing *shechitah*, where a botched act cannot be redone. Ben Beteira derives the answer from the word *misham* — "from there": meaning, from the place where the *kemitzah* was already taken, one may take the *kemitzah* again. This reading of *misham* as licensing a redo is distinct from reading it as a geographic limit.

The implicit halakhic point here is significant: if the *kemitzah* performed with the left hand were an absolute nullity, there would be nothing "there" from which to redo it. The very possibility of a redo suggests that a *kemitzah* performed improperly has some degree of standing, even if it requires correction.

### Rabbi Yaakov Resolution

The *Gemara* then reports — some say Rabbi Yirmiyah himself resolved his own question, others attribute the resolution to Rabbi Yaakov responding to Rav Yirmiyah bar Tachlifa:

> לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְהַכְשִׁיר כָּל הָעֲזָרָה כֻּלָּהּ

*Lo nitzrecha ela lehachshir kol ha'azarah kulah* — the clause "from where the *zar* stands" comes not to *restrict* the zone of valid *kemitzah*, but to *expand* it.

### Verse Expands Location

The resolution hinges on recognizing what the verse is actually teaching. One might have thought — returning now to the *mishnah*'s ruling — that since the *mincha* is *kodshei kodashim*, and since the Torah later specifies that the *kemitzah* follows the *hagashah* (the touching of the *mincha* to the altar) which must be done at the southwest corner, perhaps the *kemitzah* must also be performed specifically at the southwest corner. The verse *vekamatz misham* comes to counter this: wherever the *zar* brings the *mincha* — that is, anywhere in the *azarah* — the *kohen* takes the *kemitzah*. The phrase is thus an expansion, permitting *kemitzah* anywhere in the *azarah*. It does not function as a restriction excluding the *heichal*; that is simply not the verse's concern.

## No North Requirement

### North Requirement Questi…

With the geographic flexibility of *kemitzah* established, the *Gemara* now addresses the underlying question: why doesn't the *mincha* require the north at all? After all, it is *kodshei kodashim*, and the north is the defining feature of *kodshei kodashim* slaughter. The *Gemara* sets up what should have been a compelling *hava amina*:

> הוֹאִיל וְעוֹלָה קָדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים וּמִנְחָה קָדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים

*Ho'il ve'olah kodshei kodashim umincha kodshei kodashim* — since both the *olah* and the *mincha* are *kodshei kodashim*, perhaps just as the *olah* requires the north, so too the *mincha* requires the north. This would mean we *need* the verse *vekamatz misham* to override what would otherwise have been an obvious inference. But does this *hava amina* hold up under scrutiny?

### Failed Comparisons

The *Gemara* systematically dismantles each potential analogy. The *olah* requires the north — but the *olah* is *kalil*, entirely consumed on the altar. That is a distinctive feature the *mincha* does not share in its general form. Fine — compare instead to the *chatat*, which is also *kodshei kodashim* and also requires the north. But the *chatat* atones for sin; the *mincha* does not. Perhaps compare to the *asham* — but the *asham* involves *dam*, blood, and is a species of animal sacrifice. The *mincha*, with no blood and no slaughter, is simply not sufficiently similar to any animal *korban* to generate a compelling *hava amina* that it should require the north. And this logic extends to all the animal offerings together:

> וְכֵן לְכוּלְּהוּ מַה לְּכוּלְּהוּ שֶׁכֵּן מִין דָּמִים

*Vechein lekulhu mah lekulhu shekein min damim* — in every case, the animal offerings share the common feature of being blood offerings, which the *mincha* is not.

### Southwest Corner Logic

The *Gemara* therefore shifts its *hava amina* to an argument internal to *menachot* themselves. It is not that I would have compared the *mincha* to animal *korbanot* and required the north — that comparison was never strong enough. Rather, even within the internal logic of the *mincha*, I might have thought that the *kemitzah* must be done specifically at the southwest corner. Here is why: the Torah states *vehigisha el hamizbe'ach veheirim mimenu bikumtzo* — the *mincha* is first brought to the altar (*hagashah*) and then the *kemitzah* is performed. As we will establish shortly from two verses, the *hagashah* must be performed specifically at the southwest corner (*keren dromit maaravit*). Since the verse immediately thereafter commands the *kemitzah*, one might conclude that the *kemitzah* too must be performed at that same corner — *mah hagashah bekeren dromit maaravit, af kemitzah bekeren dromit maaravit*.

Therefore, the verse *vekamatz misham* is indeed necessary — not to dislodge a comparison to animal *korbanot*, but to clarify that despite the *kemitzah*'s proximity to the *hagashah* in the verse, it may be performed anywhere in the *azarah*.

## Shelamim in Heichal

### Shelamim Kosher

Having established that the *kemitzah* of a *mincha* is valid even inside the *heichal*, the *Gemara* turns to a parallel question regarding animal sacrifices. Rabbi Yochanan states:

> שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן בַּהֵיכָל כְּשֵׁרִים

*Shlamim sheshchatam baheichal kesherim* — *shlamim* slaughtered inside the *heichal* are valid. This is a striking ruling, and it invites comparison to the *mincha* case. Why specifically *shlamim*? Because *shlamim* are the only animal *korban* that may be slaughtered anywhere in the *azarah* — *kodshei kodashim* require the north, and therefore the question of whether "anywhere" extends into the *heichal* arises only for *shlamim*.

### Kal v'Chomer Debate

The basis for Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is a *kal vachomer*. The verse states that *shlamim* are slaughtered *petach ohel moed*, at the entrance of the *ohel moed*. The *Gemara* has established that "entrance" is understood expansively to include the entire *azarah*. If so, argues Rabbi Yochanan, *velo yihyeh tafel chamur min haikar* — the secondary reference (proximity to the *ohel moed*) should not be more stringent than the primary location (inside the *ohel moed* itself). If slaughtering in reference to the *ohel moed* is valid, certainly slaughtering within it should be valid.

But this *kal vachomer* is immediately challenged.

### Kodshei Kodashim in Heic…

The challenge comes from a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Yehudah ben Beteira. He raises the case of a siege: if *ovdei kochavim* surrounded the entire *azarah*, what is the *din* for the *kohanim* who find themselves inside the *heichal*? May they eat their *kodshei kodashim* there? Rabbi Yehudah ben Beteira derives permission from the verse *bakodesh hakodashim tochalennu* — "in the holy of holies you shall eat it" — which provides explicit textual authorization for eating *kodshei kodashim* within the *heichal*.

This *baraita* poses a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning: if a *kal vachomer* sufficed to permit *shechitah* inside the *heichal*, why did the *Gemara* need a specific verse to permit *eating* inside the *heichal*? Should not the same *kal vachomer* — if something is permitted in proximity to the *ohel moed*, it is certainly permitted within it — apply equally to eating?

### Avoda vs. Achila

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing between *avoda* and *achila*. When it comes to the *shechitah*, which is an *avoda* — an act of divine service — the *kal vachomer* is straightforward: if one may perform divine service at a certain remove from the sanctuary, one may certainly perform it in closer proximity. *Hatam avoda d'adam oved bimkom rabbo amrinan* — regarding service performed in the presence of one's master, we readily apply the logic that what is permitted further out is certainly permitted closer in.

But *achila* — eating — follows a different social logic entirely. *Ein adam ochel bimkom rabbo* — one does not eat in the presence of one's master. We would not instinctively assume that permission to eat in the *chatzer* extends to eating before the *heichal* itself. Therefore, a *kal vachomer* could not be applied to eating, and a specific verse was necessary.

The result is a somewhat counterintuitive hierarchy: the *kal vachomer* works to permit *shechitah* inside the *heichal* — which intuitively seems the most incongruous of all acts to perform in that sacred space — while eating requires an explicit verse. The logic is abstractly correct, but it does produce the odd conclusion that what is most visually incongruous is, from a *halakhic* standpoint, the most easily permitted.

## Chatat Ha'of & Mincha

### Chatat Ha'of Location

The *Gemara* now introduces the *chatat ha'of* — the bird sin-offering — as another point of comparison. Recall that with animal *korbanot*, the relationship between *chatat* and the other offerings is inverted when it comes to birds: whereas a mammal *chatat* requires the north (above the red line, *chut hasikra*), a bird *chatat* is performed below. The *mishnah* now tells us that the bird *chatat* was performed at the southwest corner (*keren dromit maaravit*) — the same location associated with the *mincha*'s *hagashah*.

### Three Things at Corner

The *mishnah* goes on to describe three acts performed at that corner below, and three above:

> בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים הָיְתָה עוֹשָׂה כְּמִין מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת מִלְּמַטָּה וּבִשְׁלֹשָׁה מִלְמַעְלָה

*Uv'shlosha dvarim hayta osa kemin meshameshet milmata uv'shlosha milmala* — this corner served three functions below and three above.

Below (*milmata*): the *hagashah* of the *mincha* (touching the *mincha* to the altar), the *shechitah* of the *chatat ha'of*, and the *shiyurei hadam* — the spilling of the remnant blood at the base of the altar. Above (*milmala*), at the corner near the horn: *nisuch hayayin vehamayim* — the wine and water libations (which flowed down into the *hashitin* below), and the *olat ha'of* — the bird *olah*. The bird *olah* was normally performed at the southeast corner, but when that location became congested, it was moved to the southwest corner. Thus a single corner served as a multi-purpose focal point for multiple aspects of the *avodah*.

### Altar Traffic Rules

The *mishnah* then describes the general traffic pattern on the altar:

> כָּל הָעוֹלִים לַמִּזְבֵּחַ עוֹלִים דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין וּמַקִּיפִין וְיוֹרְדִים דֶּרֶךְ שְׂמֹאל

*Kol ha'olim lamizbe'ach olim derech yamin umakifin veyordim derech smol* — all who ascended the altar went up on the right and descended on the left, following a counterclockwise circuit. This is consistent with the broader principle that movements in *kedushah* contexts (carrying the *Aron*, processing with a *Sefer Torah*, and so forth) always bear to the right.

The exception to this rule was made for the three acts associated with the southwest corner — *nisuch hamayim veha'yayain*, and the *olat ha'of* when relocated there. For those, one ascended and made an immediate left, proceeding directly to the corner rather than completing the full circuit. Indeed, the *mishnah* adds that for those who performed these acts, *she'yihyu olim veyordim al ha'akev* — they would ascend and then retrace their steps downward, effectively reversing course rather than continuing along the normal circuit.

## Southwest Corner Derivat…

### Text Corruption

The *Gemara* now seeks the textual basis for requiring the southwest corner for the *chatat ha'of*. It cites a verse from the context of the *mincha brought as a chatat* in the *korban oleh veyored* — the sliding-scale offering where a penitent who cannot afford an animal may bring a bird, and one who cannot afford a bird may bring a *mincha*. That *mincha* is called a *minchat chotei*, and the verse about it states: *lo yasim aleha shemen velo yiten aleha levona ki chatat hi* — do not place oil or frankincense upon it, for it is a *chatat*. From this identification — *chatat karya mincha, mincha karya chatat*, the *mincha* is called a *chatat* and the *chatat* is called a *mincha* — the *Gemara* seeks to derive: just as the *chatat* requires the north, so too the *mincha* requires the north; and conversely, just as the *mincha* is associated with the southwest, so too the bird *chatat* is associated with the southwest.

However, the first half of this derivation — that the *mincha* requires the north — is immediately problematic. We have already established at length that the *mincha* does not require the north. And a bird *chatat* likewise does not require the north.

### Rashi's Position

Rashi addresses this directly and forcefully. On the phrase *mah chatat te'una tzafon af mincha te'una tzafon*, Rashi writes:

> יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁגִּרְסָא זוֹ שִׁבּוּשׁ

*Yode'a ani shegirsa zo shibush* — I know that this textual reading is corrupted. Neither the bird *chatat* nor the *mincha* requires the north, so the derivation as it stands cannot be correct. Rashi is unable to supply the correct reading, but he suggests a possible emendation: perhaps the comparison between *mincha* and *chatat* was intended to teach that just as a *chatat* performed *shelo lishmah* is invalid, so too a *mincha* performed *shelo lishmah* is invalid — a halachic point that belongs to the same context and makes sense as a derivation from the identification between these two offerings.

One could attempt to save the north-derivation by proposing that the verse specifically singles out the *minchat chotei* as requiring the north, in distinction from ordinary *menachot*. But Rashi rejects this as well: *ule'chatat behema makish lo, havei lei lemismaich matnissin* — if the *minchat chotei* required the north like a mammal *chatat*, our *mishnayot* — which are precisely organized around specifying the required locations for all these acts — would have said so somewhere. No *mishnah* anywhere indicates that the *minchat chotei* is performed in the north. The silence is conclusive.

Tosafot, by contrast, engage with the question of whether the text should simply be emended or whether the north requirement for *minchat chotei* should be taken seriously despite the silence of the other sources. This remains a major point of discussion.

### Two Verses Rule

Setting aside the north question, the *Gemara* proceeds to establish how we know the *mincha*'s *hagashah* is performed at the southwest corner. A *baraita* cites two verses. The first:

> הַקְרֵב אֹתָהּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן לִפְנֵי ה׳

*Hikreiv otah bnei Aharon lifnei Hashem* — bring it before God. *Lifnei Hashem* points to the west of the altar — the face directed toward the *ohel moed*, toward the *heichal*. This means the western side.

The second verse says: *el pnei hamizbe'ach* — toward the face of the altar. The "face" of the altar is understood to be where the ramp ascends, namely the south face.

These two requirements point in different directions. *Ha keitzad* — how are both satisfied? By performing the *hagashah* at the southwest corner, specifically at the very point of the corner (*kineged chudah shel keren*), one simultaneously faces west (toward the *heichal*, satisfying *lifnei Hashem*) and south (facing the ramp, satisfying *al pnei hamizbe'ach*). The *Gemara* adds *vedayo* — and this is sufficient. A separate *Gemara* elsewhere explains that *vedayo* indicates one need not actually touch the flour itself to the stone of the altar; touching the vessel suffices.

### R. Eliezer Alternative

Rabbi Eliezer offers a different analysis of how the two verses should be reconciled. Rather than requiring the exact corner point, he argues that one should identify *which single face* satisfies both verses simultaneously — and do the *hagashah* there, without necessarily standing at the precise corner.

His reasoning: whenever there are two verses, one of which (*A*) satisfies its own requirement and also satisfies the requirement of the other (*B*), while the other (*B*) satisfies only itself, we set aside *B* and follow *A*. *Tofsin et ze shemekayem atzmo umekayem chaveiro* — we adopt the reading that satisfies both.

Now, which face satisfies both? If one performs the *hagashah* at the western face (*maarav*), that satisfies *lifnei Hashem* but does not satisfy *al pnei hamizbe'ach*. The western face is not the face of the altar. So the west satisfies only one verse.

But if one performs the *hagashah* at the southern face (*darom*), that clearly satisfies *al pnei hamizbe'ach* — the south is the face of the altar. And, according to Rabbi Eliezer, it also satisfies *lifnei Hashem*. How? Because the south face is directly opposite the entrance to the *heichal*, making it *lifnei Hashem* as well. Therefore, one need not stand at the precise corner point; performing the *hagashah* anywhere along the southern face suffices.

### Rav Ashi's Explanation

The *Gemara* objects: how can the southern face be *lifnei Hashem* — in front of God, facing the *heichal* — if the *heichal* is to the west?

Rav Ashi explains: *hai tana savar kulei mizbe'ach batzafon kai* — this *tanna* holds that the entire altar stands within the northern half of the *azarah*. Given this positioning, the southern face of the altar is directly opposite the doorway of the *heichal*, because the *heichal*'s entrance spans the entire width of the building, and the southern face of the altar — though facing south rather than west — is in the direct line of sight from that entrance. Therefore, the southern face simultaneously constitutes *pnei hamizbe'ach* and *lifnei Hashem*, and the *hagashah* performed there satisfies both verses without requiring the precise corner point.

This is Rabbi Eliezer's position, distinct from the *tanna kamma* who requires the exact corner. Both positions, however, agree that the southwest region is the operative location.

## Melika & Hazaya

### Melika vs. Hazaya

Returning to the *mishnah*'s somewhat cryptic language about the bird *chatat* — it is performed at the southwest corner, yet could validly be done anywhere — Rav Ashi offers the proper reading:

> בְּכָל מָקוֹם הָיְתָה כְּשֵׁרָה לִמְלִיקָתָהּ אֶלָּא זֶהוּ מְקוֹמָהּ לְהַזָּיָתָהּ

*Bechol makom hayta kesheira limlikatah, ela zehu mekoma lehazatah* — anywhere in the *azarah* was valid for the *melika* (the severing of the bird's neck), but the southwest corner was the designated location for the *hazaya* — the squirting of the blood against the altar wall.

This creates a precise and elegant parallel with the *mincha*. The *kemitzah* (analogous to *shechitah*) may be done anywhere; the *hagashah* (the touching of the offering to the altar wall) must be at the southwest corner. In the bird *chatat*: the *melika* (severing) may be done anywhere; the *hazaya* (the blood that strikes the altar wall) must be at the southwest corner. In both cases, the act that makes contact with the altar wall requires the specific corner; the act of severance or taking is geographically unrestricted.

### Baraita Editing

The *Gemara* notes that this ruling is supported by a *baraita*, but the *baraita* as it stands requires careful reading — indeed, a degree of textual editing — to align with the *mishnah*'s ruling. The *baraita* states:

> מְלִיקָה בְּכָל מָקוֹם בַּמִּזְבֵּחַ כְּשֵׁרָה, הַזָּיַת דָּמָהּ בְּכָל מָקוֹם כְּשֵׁרָה

*Melika bechol makom bamizbe'ach kesheira, hazayat damah bechol makom kesheira* — the *melika* anywhere on the altar is valid, the squirting of its blood anywhere is valid. On a plain reading, this seems to permit the *hazaya* anywhere, contrary to what we have established.

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between *hazaya* — the initial squirting of blood — and *mitzuy* — the subsequent squeezing out of the remaining blood, analogous to the *shfichat shirayim* (pouring of the blood remnants) in animal sacrifices. The *baraita* should be read as stating: *mitzuy damah bechol makom kesheira* — the squeezing out of the blood may be done anywhere. The *hazaya* proper, the initial squirting, must be at the southwest corner.

### Dam Hanefesh

The *baraita* continues: *hazaya velo mitza kesheira* — if one performed only the *hazaya* and did not perform the subsequent *mitzuy*, it is still valid. This parallels the rule for animal *korbanot* where the *shfichat shirayim* is not *me'akev*. The *baraita* then adds the qualification *uvilvad sheyiten mikeneged hasovev ulemata* — as long as the blood was applied below the *chut hasikra*, the red line.

The *Gemara* parses this carefully: *dam hanefesh* — the soul blood, the initial burst of blood that issues at the moment of *melika* — that is what constitutes the valid *hazaya* and must be applied at the southwest corner below the *chut hasikra*. The subsequent *mitzuy*, the squeezing out of the remaining blood, is not *me'akev* and may be performed anywhere on the altar.

### Hazaya Southwest Only

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is thus consistent: both the *mishnah* and the *baraita*, properly read, establish that the *melika* may be performed anywhere, and the *mitzuy* (the squeezing out, analogous to *shfichat shirayim*) may likewise be performed anywhere since it is not *me'akev*. But the *hazaya* — the actual squirting of the *dam hanefesh* against the altar wall — must be performed specifically at the southwest corner. This is the precise parallel to the *mincha*: in both cases, it is the act of contact with the altar wall that is fixed to the southwest corner, while the act of severance or taking remains geographically flexible throughout the *azarah*.
