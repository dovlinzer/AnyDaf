# Niddah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Chambers

### Parable of House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' מָשָׁל מָשְׁלוּ חֲכָמִים בָּאִשָּׁה: הַחֶדֶר, וְהַפְּרוֹזְדוֹר, וְהָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A woman's reproductive organs are composed of different parts, and the halakhic status of blood that emerges from one part differs from the halakhic status of blood that emerges from another part. **The Sages stated a parable with regard to** the structure of the sexual organs of **a woman,** based on the structure of a house: **The** inner **room** represents the uterus, **and the corridor [*perozdor*]** leading to the inner room represents the vaginal canal, **and the upper story** represents the bladder.

The Mishnah at the top of 17b presents a parable for a woman's internal anatomy: *hacheder*, *vehaprozdor*, *veha'aliyah* — the inner chamber, the antechamber, and the upper story. The use of the house as a metaphor for the woman's body appears elsewhere in rabbinic literature, though the imagery here is distinctively architectural. The Mishnah rules that *dam hacheder tamei* — blood from the inner chamber is *tamei* — while *dam ha'aliyah tahor* — blood from the upper story is *tahor*. Blood found in the *prozdor*, the antechamber, is *tamei* by doubt, and the reason given is *lefi she'chazkato min hamakor* — the presumption is that it comes from the *makor*. One notices here that *makor* is a fourth term, distinct from *cheder*, *prozdor*, and *aliyah*. The conventional assumption is that *makor* and *cheder* are simply different terms drawn from different collections of imagery — one architectural, the other biological — both referring to the same organ, the uterus.

### Anatomical ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּם הַחֶדֶר — טָמֵא, דַּם הָעֲלִיָּיה — טָהוֹר, נִמְצָא בַּפְּרוֹזְדוֹר — סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא, לְפִי שֶׁחֶזְקָתוֹ מִן הַמָּקוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Blood** from **the** inner **room is ritually impure. Blood** from **the upper story is ritually pure.** If blood **was found in the corridor,** there is uncertainty whether it came from the uterus and is impure, or from the bladder and is pure. Despite **its** state of **uncertainty,** it is deemed definitely **impure, due to** the fact **that its presumptive status** is of blood that came **from the source,** i.e., the uterus, and not from the bladder.

Working from that assumption, the *cheder* is identified with the uterus, the *prozdor* with the vaginal canal, and the *aliyah* with the urinary tract — specifically the urethra, which can on occasion be a source of blood in the urine. Blood originating from the urinary tract is *tahor*, blood from the uterus is *tamei*, and blood found in the vaginal canal is presumed *tamei* because we assume it came from the uterus and has no other natural source. That is the *peshat* of the Mishnah — clean and straightforward until the Gemara introduces a *beraita* that complicates matters considerably by positing a structural connection between the two pathways.

### Uterine Blood

Before proceeding to the Gemara's complication, Tosafot flags an important underlying principle. The word *dam niddah* on *dam ha'aliyah tahor* notes that blood from the *aliyah* is *tahor* — *afilu hu mimarei d'mei tmei'im* — even if it presents in one of the colors that would otherwise constitute *tamei* blood. The reason, as Tosafot derives, is the verse *mimkor dameha* — "from the source of her blood" — which teaches that *dam niddah* must specifically be *d'mei makor*, uterine blood. Alternatively, Tosafot notes, *damim* is written in the plural, indicating the place where multiple varieties of blood are found, a description that applies to the uterus but not to the *aliyah*, which would produce only blood from a wound of a single type. This principle — that uterine origin is a defining criterion of *dam niddah*, not merely an implicit assumption — has been operative throughout the tractate's discussions of *dam makkah* and related topics, and Tosafot here makes it explicit.

## Lul Debate

### Lul Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' רָמֵי בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל וְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה תְּנוֹ נִדָּה בֵּי רַב הוּנָא. אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: הַחֶדֶר מִבִּפְנִים, וְהַפְּרוֹזְדוֹר מִבַּחוּץ, וַעֲלִיָּיה בְּנוּיָה עַל שְׁתֵּיהֶן, וְלוּל פָּתוּחַ בֵּין עֲלִיָּיה לַפְּרוֹזְדוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rami bar Shmuel and Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, were learning** tractate ***Nidda*** in **the study hall of Rav Huna. Rabba bar Rav Huna found them sitting and saying** an interpretation of this mishna: **The room,** i.e., the uterus, is the **inner** part of the reproductive organs, **and the corridor** is the **outer** part. **And the upper story,** the bladder, **is built,** i.e., found, **above them both. And** there is **an open vestibule between** the **upper story and the corridor.**

The Gemara opens the discussion by reporting that Rami bar Shmuel and Rav Yitzchak, the son of Rav Yehuda, were studying *Niddah* in the school of Rav Huna. Rava bar Rav Huna found them sitting and saying: *hacheder mibifnim vehaprozdor mibachutz* — the *cheder* is inner and the *prozdor* is outer — *veha'aliyah benuyah al shetehen* — and the *aliyah* is built upon both of them, sitting above. Now comes the element not found in the Mishnah: *halul patuach bein aliyah leprozdor* — there is a *lul*, a tract or opening, between the *aliyah* and the *prozdor*. The term *lul* can denote a chicken coop, which is characteristically tall and narrow, and by extension refers here to a narrow channel or passage. The students were saying that this *lul* creates an opening between the urinary tract and the vaginal canal.

### Galen's Animals

This immediately presents an anatomical problem: in humans, nothing connects the urinary tract to the vaginal canal. These are separate systems opening to separate orifices, with no communicating passage between them. The source of this anatomical assumption in the Gemara may be traced to Galen, whose anatomy was based largely on dissection of cats and other animals rather than humans. In animals, the uterus opens directly to the exterior — because animals typically carry multiple young simultaneously, the uterus extends outward without the separate vaginal canal that characterizes human anatomy. Animals also lack separate orifices in the way humans have them, with the urinary tract opening into the extended uterine space and everything exiting through a common passage. This anatomical picture — in which the urinary tract communicates with the space adjacent to the uterus — is true for animals, and it is likely from this source that the Gemara's assumption about the *lul* derives. The Gemara will be read within that framework, and alternative explanations will follow.

### Initial Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא מִן הַלּוּל וְלִפְנִים — סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא, מִן הִלּוּל וְלַחוּץ — סְפֵקוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** They continued: If blood is **found from** the opening of this **vestibule and inward** toward the uterus, i.e., inside the vagina, there is uncertainty whether it came from the uterus and is impure, or from the bladder and is pure, but **its** state of **uncertainty** renders it definitely **impure.** If it is found in the area **from the** opening of this **vestibule and outward,** on the outer surface of the vulva, the blood is more likely to have come from the bladder, through the urethra, and therefore **its** state of **uncertainty** renders it **pure.**

Working with the Gemara's anatomical assumption, the students were saying: *nimtza min halul velifnim* — if blood is found from the *lul* inward, that is, between the *lul* and the *makor*, it is *safek tamei*, doubtfully *tamei* — and *min halul velachutz*, from the *lul* outward, *safek tahor*, doubtfully *tahor*. This is a striking ruling. Even setting aside the anatomical complexities, blood in the interior region toward the uterus would seem overwhelmingly likely to be uterine in origin. To call that only a *safek* seems counterintuitive. And blood outward from the *lul*, heading toward the exterior, is blood that has to pass through the vaginal canal to exit the body — again, most likely uterine blood. The designation of that blood as *safek tahor* is puzzling. These difficulties will be addressed through the alternative readings to follow, but the initial position of the students is set: both directions are treated as a *safek*, with the inward direction leaning *tamei* and the outward direction leaning *tahor*.

### Rava Corrects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ: סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא אֲמַרְתְּ לַן מָר? וְהָא אֲנַן ״שֶׁחֶזְקָתוֹ מִן הַמָּקוֹר״ תְּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **came and said to his father,** Rav Huna: With regard to the *halakha* of blood that is found from the vestibule and inward, **did the Master say to us** that **its** state of **uncertainty** renders it **impure,** as I heard in the study hall, from which it may be inferred that this is a case of uncertain impurity? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: The blood is impure **due to** the fact **that its presumptive status** is of blood that came **from the source,** i.e., the uterus? This indicates that it is a case of definite impurity. If so, the mishna cannot be referring either to blood found from the vestibule and inward or from the vestibule and outward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא — מִן הַלּוּל וְלִפְנִים וַדַּאי טָמֵא, מִן הַלּוּל וְלַחוּץ סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **said to** his son Rabba: **This is what I said:** If the blood was found **from the vestibule inward** it is **definitely impure,** as it is presumed to come from the uterus. This is the case mentioned in the mishna. If the blood was found **from the vestibule outward its** state of **uncertainty** renders it **impure.** Although it can be claimed that if this was blood from the uterus it would not have been found in this area, it is possible that when the woman bent over, the blood went from the uterus into this area. Consequently, she is impure due to the uncertainty.

Rava bar Rav Huna's son came and reported this to his father, Rav Huna. The son challenged the ruling: *safek tamei amrat lan mar?* — did you, the master, really say that the status is merely *doubtfully tamei*? After all, the Mishnah itself says *chazkato min hamakor* — the presumption is that it comes from the uterus — which sounds more like a definite ruling than a mere doubt being decided strictly. Rav Huna responded that the students had misunderstood him. What he had actually said was: *min halul velifnim vadai tamei* — from the *lul* inward, it is *definitely tamei* — of course it is uterine blood. And *mehalul velachutz sfeko tamei* — from the *lul* outward, there is an element of doubt, yet we still rule it *tamei*. This is a considerably stricter position than the students had reported: instead of doubtfully *tamei* and doubtfully *tahor* in the two directions, Rav Huna holds definitely *tamei* inward and doubtfully but still *tamei* outward. The son's challenge prompted the clarification, and the result is a more coherent, more stringent ruling.

## Abaye's Two Models

### Doubt Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַאי שְׁנָא מִן הַלּוּל וְלַחוּץ, דִּסְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא? דְּדִלְמָא שָׁחֲתָה, וּמֵחֶדֶר אֲתָא. מִן הַלּוּל וְלִפְנִים נָמֵי, אֵימָא: אִזְדְּקַרָה, וּמֵעֲלִיָּיה אֲתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: What is different** about a situation where the blood was found **from the vestibule outward, where its** state of **uncertainty** renders it **impure?** The reason for that *halakha* is **that perhaps** the woman **bent over** and leaned forward, **and** the blood **came from the room,** i.e., the uterus. If so, in the case where the blood was found **from the vestibule inward,** concerning which you ruled that she is definitely impure, you can **also say** that it is possible that the woman **staggered** backward **and** as a result the blood **came from** the **upper story** to the back of the canal. Accordingly, she should be impure merely out of uncertainty.

Abaye now enters with a sharper challenge. He asks: *mai shna min halul u'lechutz sfeiko tamei?* — why are you saying that from the *lul* outward the blood is doubtfully *tamei*? If the assumption is that blood found outward from the *lul* comes most naturally from the urinary tract and not the uterus, then it should be presumed *tahor*, not even doubtfully *tamei*. Abaye presses: perhaps the only reason to call it *safek tamei* is the concern that *dilma shachtza umechader ata* — perhaps she bent or contorted her body in some way at the time of the bleeding, so that blood from the *cheder* traveled outward in an unusual direction rather than following its normal pathway. If that is the reason for calling the outward blood doubtfully *tamei* — that we must account for abnormal body positions — then we have a problem in the other direction as well. *Min halul velifnim nami* — even blood found inward from the *lul* — *eima izdarka mei'aliyah* — perhaps she did a somersault or stood on her head, and blood from the *aliyah*, the urinary tract, traveled backward and deposited itself in the inward region. If we take seriously the possibility that the body may not have behaved normally, then every drop of blood, regardless of location, carries that element of uncertainty, and nothing can be called definitely *tamei*. The Doubt Model, as Abaye frames it: *ei batar chashasha azlat, idi ve'idi sfeiko* — if you follow the logic of concern for abnormal possibilities, then everything is a *safek*, and we should say both directions are doubtfully *tamei*.

### Normal Behavior

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִי בָּתַר חֲשָׁשָׁא אָזְלַתְּ — אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי סָפֵק הוּא, וְאִי בָּתַר חֲזָקָה אָזְלַתְּ — מִן הַלּוּל וְלִפְנִים וַדַּאי טָמֵא, מִן הַלּוּל וְלַחוּץ וַדַּאי טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye says: If you follow the concern,** i.e., if your ruling of *halakha* is based on a concern that the blood might have moved due to the woman leaning forward or backward, then in both **this** case **and that,** whether the blood is found in the inner or outer section of the canal, the source of the blood **is uncertain. And if you follow** the **presumption** based on where the blood found in a particular place is usually from, then blood found **from the vestibule inward is definitely impure,** whereas blood found **from the vestibule outward is definitely pure.**

Abaye then presents the alternative: *ei batar chazaka azlat* — if instead you follow the normal behavior of the body, the established pattern of how these structures function, then the picture is clear and clean. *Min halul velifnim vadai tamei* — from the *lul* inward is definitely *tamei*, because this is unquestionably the pathway of uterine blood. *Min halul velachutz vadai tahor* — from the *lul* outward is definitely *tahor*, because this is the pathway of the *aliyah*, not the uterus. In Abaye's Normal Behavior Model there is no middle ground and no ambiguity — location determines status definitively in each direction. But this creates an obvious difficulty: if blood that is found toward the exterior is definitely *tahor*, that seems to cut against simple anatomical intuition. Blood emerging from the body must travel outward. Why would outward location indicate *tahor*?

## Rashi Model

### Rashi's Placement

Rashi's anatomical model offers a resolution. Rashi does not identify the *cheder* with the uterus. Instead, according to Rashi, the uterus is positioned lower in the anatomical picture, and the *cheder* — perhaps to be understood as something like the cervix — is distinct from the *makor*. This explains why the Mishnah uses a separate term, *makor*, which might then refer specifically to the uterus while *cheder* designates a related but different structure. This reconception is not merely terminological; it alters the entire anatomical map.

### Separate Pathways

Rashi understands that uterine blood and urine travel along genuinely separate pathways and exit through different orifices. The blood from the uterus follows one route — moving through the *cheder* and out in one direction. The *aliyah*, the urinary tract, sends its contents in a different direction entirely, toward a different exit. Because these pathways diverge, the direction of flow becomes meaningful. Blood found moving in the direction associated with the urinary pathway is presumed *tahor*, because that pathway leads away from the uterus. Blood found in the region associated with the uterine pathway is *tamei*. With genuine separate pathways, Abaye's Normal Behavior Model becomes anatomically coherent: outward in the urinary direction is definitively *tahor*, inward toward the uterus is definitively *tamei*, without the counterintuitive results that troubled the simpler reading.

### Vestibule Model

There is another possible reading, suggested in the context of the discussion, which draws on a relevant anatomical fact mentioned in a Gemara in Chullin: animals do not have a *prozdor*. This may mean not only that animals lack a separate vaginal canal, but also that animals lack external labia. In human anatomy, the space between the labia is known as the vestibule. Under this reading, the *prozdor* of the Mishnah refers not to the vaginal canal per se, but to this external vestibular space. The *cheder* remains internal — *mibifnim* — while the *prozdor* is external — *mibachutz* — in a more literal sense than is usually understood. The *aliyah* and the vaginal opening sit adjacent to one another in this external area, and the *lul* is not an internal tube but rather the space between these two external openings. Under this reading, blood found near that *lul* in the external region would most naturally be presumed to come from the urinary tract, since uterine blood traveling normally would flow downward and outward through the vagina, not laterally across the vestibule toward the urinary opening. This vestibule model preserves the Mishnah's logic without positing an anatomical connection that does not exist in humans, and it renders the concerns about somersaults and contortions coherent: the normal posture directs uterine blood downward and away, but unusual positions could theoretically displace it. With this reading, the Mishnah's ruling about blood found in the *prozdor* — assumed *tamei* because of *chazkato min hamakor* — also follows naturally, since the *prozdor* as vestibule is a space where blood of uncertain origin is presumed uterine.

## Chiya vs Katina

### Chiya Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: דָּם הַנִּמְצָא בַּפְּרוֹזְדוֹר — חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו עַל בִּיאַת מִקְדָּשׁ, וְשׂוֹרְפִין עָלָיו אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה. וְרַב קַטִּינָא אָמַר: אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו עַל בִּיאַת מִקְדָּשׁ, וְאֵין שׂוֹרְפִין עָלָיו אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: Blood that is found in the corridor** is considered definite menstrual blood, and therefore if she engages in intercourse, both she and her partner would be **liable as a result of** this blood to receive *karet* **for entering the Temple** intentionally when ritually impure, or to bring an offering for entering unwittingly. **And one burns *teruma* due to it,** if the woman touches such produce. **And Rav Ketina says:** It is impure merely as a matter of uncertainty; therefore, the woman **is not obligated, due to** that blood, to bring an offering **for entering the Temple** when ritually impure, **and one does not burn *teruma* on its account.**

The Gemara now cites a *beraita*: *tani Rebbe Chiya* — Rabbi Chiya taught that *dam hanimtza baprozdor chayavin alav bi'as mikdash vesorfin alav es hatrumah* — blood found in the *prozdor* creates full *chiyuvim*: if the woman entered the *Beis HaMikdash* in that state, she would be liable — for in fact entry onto *Har HaBayis* itself is forbidden to a *niddah* — and *terumah* that was touched would be burned. These are the consequences of *vadai tum'ah*, definite impurity. Rabbi Chiya's position is thus that blood in the *prozdor* renders the woman definitely *tamei*, not merely doubtfully so.

### Katina Position

Rav Katina disagrees: *ein chayavin alav al bi'as mikdash ve'ein sorfin alav es hatrumah* — neither penalty applies, because the blood is only doubtfully *tamei*. When the status is only a *safek*, one does not burn *terumah* on that basis and does not incur liability for *bi'as mikdash*. Rav Katina thus holds that blood in the *prozdor* is *safek tamei* rather than *vadai tamei*.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״אִי בָּתַר חֲשָׁשָׁא אָזְלַתְּ״ — מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב קַטִּינָא, וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the relationship between this dispute and the previous statements of *amora'im*. **According to this formulation,** i.e., option, **that Abaye stated: If you follow the concern** that the blood might have moved due to the woman leaning forward or backward, there is uncertainty whether the blood was found in the inner or outer section of the canal, this **supports** the opinion **of Rav Ketina,** who likewise deems blood found in the canal impure due to uncertainty. **And** this option suggested by Abaye **contradicts** the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** who deems the blood definitely impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: ״אִי בָּתַר חֲזָקָה אָזְלַתְּ״ — מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא,
>
> **Translation:** **According to that formulation that Abaye stated: If you follow** the **presumption** that blood found in the inner section is definitely impure, while blood found in the outer section is definitely pure, this **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya,** whose ruling that the blood is definitely impure is understood as referring to blood found in the inner section.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב קַטִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this option **contradicts** the opinion **of Rav Ketina,** who deems the blood impure due to uncertainty.

The Gemara reconciles this debate through Abaye's two models. *Lehach d'amar Abbaye* — according to Abaye's first approach, the Doubt Model — *ibasya chushsha azlas*, if we follow the logic of accounting for aberrant body positions and abnormal possibilities, then everything is always a *safek*, and we rule *tamei* throughout but only with *safek* status. This *mitsayeileh leRav Katina* — supports Rav Katina, who says the blood is only doubtfully *tamei* — *upliga d'Rebbe Chiya*, and it argues against Rabbi Chiya, who calls it *vadai*. According to Abaye's second approach, the Normal Behavior Model — *ibasya chazakah azlas*, following how the body normally functions — then position within the body determines status definitively: from the *lul* inward is *vadai tamei*, from the *lul* outward is *vadai tahor*, with no *safek* at all. This *mitsayeileh leRebbe Chiya*, supports Rabbi Chiya's ruling of definite *tum'ah* for blood found in the region closer to the *makor*, and it argues against Rav Katina in that space.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַב הוּנָא, לָא פְּלִיגִי — כָּאן מִן הַלּוּל וְלִפְנִים, כָּאן מִן הַלּוּל וְלַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to** the opinion of **Rav Huna,** who said that if the blood was found in the inner section it is definitely impure, as it is presumed to come from the uterus, and if the blood was found in the outer section it is impure due to uncertainty, one can say that Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rav Ketina **do not disagree,** as they were referring to different cases. **Here,** where Rabbi Ḥiyya deems the blood definitely impure, he is speaking of blood found **from the vestibule and inward,** whereas **there,** Rav Ketina deems it impure due to uncertainty when it is found **from the vestibule and outward.**

According to Rav Huna's reading — which distinguished between blood inward from the *lul* as *vadai tamei* and blood outward from the *lul* as *safek tamei* — the Gemara explains that the two positions of Rabbi Chiya and Rav Katina need not be in conflict at all. *Rav Huna lo peligi* — they are not actually debating. *Ha min halul velifnim, ha min halul velachutz* — the one who says *vadai tamei* is speaking about blood found on the inward side of the *lul*, and the one who says *safek tamei* is speaking about blood found on the outward side. Each ruling applies to its own location.

### Rami Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרָמֵי בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל וּלְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמְרִי: מִן הַלּוּל וְלַחוּץ — סְפֵקוֹ טָהוֹר, מִן הַלּוּל וְלִפְנִים — סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא, הָנֵי בְּמַאי מִתּוֹקְמָא? מִן הַלּוּל וְלִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** the opinion of **Rami bar Shmuel and Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, who say** that if the blood is found **from the vestibule and outward, its** state of **uncertainty** renders it **pure,** whereas if it is found in the area **from the vestibule and inward, its** state of **uncertainty** renders it **impure, with regard to what** case can the dispute between **these** Sages, Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rav Ketina, be **interpreted?** It must be referring to a situation where the blood was found **from the vestibule and inward,** as according to Rami bar Shmuel and Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, if the blood is found from the vestibule and outward it is pure.

However, a difficulty remains for the position of Rami bar Shmuel and Rav Yitzchak son of Rav Yehuda, the two students whose formulation opened the discussion. They had said that blood inward from the *lul* is *safek tamei* and blood outward from the *lul* is *safek tahor* — treating both directions as *safek*, with the inward *safek* resolved *tamei* and the outward *safek* resolved *tahor*. Under their reading, there is no room for a position of *vadai tamei* anywhere. So how can Rabbi Chiya, who rules *vadai tamei*, be accommodated within their framework? The Gemara notes that Rav Katina's *safek tamei* can fit — that corresponds to the inward region under their reading — but Rabbi Chiya's *vadai* cannot find a home.

### Floor vs Ceiling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן — כְּשֶׁנִּמְצָא בַּקַּרְקַע פְּרוֹזְדוֹר, וְכָאן — שֶׁנִּמְצָא בְּגַג פְּרוֹזְדוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult.** It is possible that Rami bar Shmuel and Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, agree with the opinion of Rabbi Ḥiyya, as they maintain that there is no dispute between Rav Ketina and Rabbi Ḥiyya. Once again the reason is that Rav Ketina and Rabbi Ḥiyya might be referring to two different cases: **Here,** Rabbi Ḥiyya deems the blood definitely impure because he is speaking of a case **where it is found on the floor of** the **corridor,** in which case the blood is presumed to come from the uterus rather than the bladder. **And there,** Rav Ketina, who deems the blood impure due to uncertainty, is referring to blood **that is found on the roof of** the **corridor,** and therefore it is uncertain whether the blood came from the bladder or the uterus.

The Gemara resolves this with a distinction that is somewhat unexpected: *kan keshenimtza bekarka prozdor ve'kan keshenimtza begag prozdor* — here refers to blood found on the floor of the *prozdor*, and there refers to blood found on the ceiling of the *prozdor*. Uterine blood, flowing downward, would naturally settle on the floor of the space. Blood that might have come from the *lul* from above — from the *aliyah* — would be found higher up, on the ceiling or upper surface. So even Rami bar Shmuel and Rav Yitzchak can agree that if the blood is on the floor, where uterine blood characteristically collects, it is *vadai tamei* — consistent with Rabbi Chiya's position. The *safek* ruling applies when blood is found on the upper surface, where there is genuine ambiguity about its source. With this, the full range of positions is accounted for, and the extended discussion of the woman's anatomy and the *lul* reaches its conclusion.

## Three Cases

### Yochanan Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת הָלְכוּ בּוֹ חֲכָמִים אַחַר הָרוֹב, וַעֲשָׂאוּם כְּוַדַּאי: מָקוֹר, שִׁלְיָא, חֲתִיכָה. מָקוֹר — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן;
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: In three places** where there is uncertainty **the Sages followed the majority, and** based on that majority **they established** the *halakha* in these cases **as** though they involved **a certainty.** The three cases are as follows: The **source,** the **afterbirth [*shilya*],** and **a shaped** limb. The Gemara elaborates: The **source** is **that which we** just **said,** i.e., that blood found in the corridor from the vestibule and inward it is ritually impure, because the majority of blood found there is from the uterus.

The Gemara now introduces a statement of Rabbi Yochanan: *beshlosha mekomot halkhu chachamim achar harov va'asuum kevaday* — in three places the Sages followed the majority and treated it as definite. The emphasis here is not simply that we rule according to majority — that is a pervasive principle throughout *Shas* in matters of both *issurim* and *tumah*. The question is of a different order: when we rule by *rov*, how complete is that ruling? Do we merely follow the majority as a practical decision while acknowledging the residual minority possibility, or do we treat the case as though there is no question at all — as fully *vadai*? In these three cases, Rabbi Yochanan says, the ruling is truly *vadai*, treated as definite. All three cases fall within the domain of *tumah* and *taharah* involving women.

### Case 1: Makor

The first case is the *makor hametamei* — the uterus. As the Mishnah taught, blood that comes from at least the region from the *lul* inward is treated as definitively *tamei*. While one could always imagine some aberrant scenario in which the blood did not originate from the uterus, the ruling is not merely a strict resolution of a *safek* — it is treated as *vadai*. The practical consequence of this *vadai* status, as the Gemara notes, is that she establishes her *veset* from that day. She does not merely observe *tumah* practices stringently while acknowledging uncertainty; she is treated as having definitely seen that day, with all that follows for the counting of her clean days.

### Case 2: Shilya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלְיָא — דִּתְנַן: שִׁלְיָא בְּבַיִת, הַבַּיִת טָמֵא, וְלֹא שֶׁהַשִּׁלְיָא וָלָד, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין שִׁלְיָא בְּלֹא וָלָד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: נִמּוֹק הַוָּלָד עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָצָא;
>
> **Translation:** The **afterbirth** is **as we learned** in a mishna (26a): If a woman miscarried and **the afterbirth** is **in the house, the house is ritually impure,** in the sense that everything under the roof becomes impure due to impurity imparted by a corpse. **And** the reason is **not that the afterbirth** itself has the status of **an offspring; rather,** it is **that there is no afterbirth without an offspring.** It is clear that the afterbirth contained an offspring, which disintegrated after the miscarriage. That offspring renders the contents of the house impure. **Rabbi Shimon says:** The **offspring was squashed before it emerged** with the afterbirth. Consequently, the house is not rendered impure, because the squashed fetus is nullified by the majority of blood that accompanied the miscarriage.

The second case is the *shilya* — the embryonic sac. The Mishnah teaches that if a *shilya* is found in a house, the house is *tamei* with *tumas mes*, the impurity of a corpse. The reasoning is *she'ein shilya belo velad* — an embryonic sac does not exist without a fetus having been present. Even if the fetus itself cannot be located — perhaps it dissolved into the amniotic fluids and cannot be separately identified — the presence of the *shilya* is treated as proof that a fetus was there, creating definite *tumas mes* for the house and everything within it. The *nafka minah* of calling this *vadai* rather than *safek* is that *terumah* that was inside would be burned, a consequence that requires *vadai tum'ah*.

### Shimon Dissent

Rabbi Shimon dissents. He argues that the reasoning *ein shilya belo velad* is not airtight, because *nimok havelad ad shelo yatza* — perhaps the fetus dissolved before it emerged from the woman's body. If so, there may be no actual fetus inside the *shilya*, and the *tum'ah* of a corpse cannot be applied. Rabbi Shimon therefore holds that the *tum'ah* of a *shilya* is only *safek* rather than *vadai*. The Gemara presents this case as one of the three in which we treat *rov* as *vadai* — specifically, we follow the majority of *shilyos* which do contain fetuses — and Rabbi Shimon's dissent is the minority view against that consensus.

### Case 3: Chaticha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲתִיכָה — דִּתְנַן: הַמַּפֶּלֶת יָד חֲתוּכָה וְרֶגֶל חֲתוּכָה, אִמּוֹ טְמֵאָה לֵידָה, וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא מִגּוּף אָטוּם בָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The third case of an uncertainty where the Sages followed the majority is that of **a shaped** limb, **as we learned** in a *baraita*: In the case of a woman **who miscarries a shaped hand,** i.e., its fingers are discernible, **or a shaped foot, its mother is impure** with the impurity of a woman after **childbirth,** as it certainly came from a full-fledged fetus, **and we are not concerned** that **perhaps it came from** a fetus with **a sealed,** i.e., deficient **body,** in which case the miscarriage does not have the status of childbirth with regard to ritual impurity. The reason is that most pregnant women give birth to a fully formed fetus, and therefore it is presumed that the hand or foot came from a whole fetus that was squashed during childbirth. Once again the Sages established the impurity as a certainty, based on a majority.

The third case is the *chaticha* — a developed limb. The Mishnah teaches that if a woman miscarries and what emerges is a *yad chatucha*, a hand whose fingers are distinguished and separated, or similarly a foot with divided toes, *imo tmeiah leida* — the mother has the *tum'ah* of *yoledet*, a woman who has given birth. The question is why this requires a ruling of *vadai* at all: if only a limb emerged and not the entire fetus, one might wonder whether the rest of the fetus was normally developed. Perhaps the body from which this limb came was an *atuma*, an undifferentiated mass that never developed normally into a human form, in which case the woman would not be considered a *yoledet*. The Mishnah rules *ve'ein chosheshin shema miguf atum ba* — we do not entertain the concern that it came from an undeveloped mass. Since the limb itself shows proper development — fingers or toes clearly distinguished — we assume the entire fetus was developing normally, and the mother is a *yoledet* with full *tum'ah* consequences. This is the application of *rov*: most fetuses that produce well-developed limbs are themselves normally developing fetuses, and we treat that majority presumption as *vadai*.

It should be noted that the *tum'ah* of *yoledet* applies to miscarriages as well, not only live births, and that below forty days of gestation the question of *yoledet* status would not arise regardless of limb development. Beyond that threshold, even improperly developed fetuses can raise questions about whether the woman has the *tum'ah* of *yoledet*, which is precisely why the evidence of a properly formed limb is halachically significant.

## Rov Principle

### Meat Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָאִיכָּא תֵּשַׁע חֲנוּיוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are there no more** cases of uncertainty in which the Sages determined the *halakha* according to the status of the majority, treating the case as though it involved a certainty? **But isn't there** the case of **nine stores?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: תֵּשַׁע חֲנוּיוֹת, כּוּלָּן מוֹכְרוֹת בְּשַׂר שְׁחוּטָה, וְאַחַת מוֹכֶרֶת בְּשַׂר נְבֵלָה, וְלָקַח מֵאַחַת מֵהֶן וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן לָקַח — סְפֵקוֹ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **nine stores** in a city, **all of which sell** kosher **meat** from **slaughtered** animals, **and one** other store that **sells meat** from unslaughtered **animal carcasses, and** a person **bought** meat **from one of** the stores **and he does not know from which** store **he bought** the meat, in this case of **uncertainty,** the meat **is prohibited.** With regard to an item of uncertain status, if it separated from its fixed location it is presumed to have separated from the majority of items like it in that location, and has their halakhic status. But in the case of an item that remained in its fixed location, i.e., didn't separate, it is viewed as an uncertainty that is equally balanced, and one does not follow the majority. This ruling is based on the principle: The halakhic status of uncertainty with regard to any item fixed in its place is that of an uncertainty that is equally balanced, and one does not follow the majority.In this case, when it comes to determining whether or not this meat comes from a kosher store, since the uncertainty stems from the act of buying the meat in the store, and the stores are fixed in their places, the two types of stores are regarded as though they were equal in number.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַנִּמְצָא — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And in the** case of meat **found** in the street, outside the stores, **follow the majority** of stores. If most stores in the city sell kosher meat one can assume that the meat he found is kosher, based on the principle: Any item separated, i.e., not fixed in its place, is presumed to have been separated from the majority. Similar to the previous cases, this meat is treated as certainly kosher on the basis of a majority.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאָה קָאָמְרִינַן, אִיסּוּר לָא קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We say** that this list of cases mentioned by Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to matters of **ritual impurity,** whereas **we do not say** that the list includes cases that involve **prohibitions,** such as that of non-kosher meat.

The Gemara asks a pointed question: if Rabbi Yochanan says there are three places where we follow *rov* and treat it as *vadai*, that formulation implies that this is not universally the case. But we follow *rov* constantly — in *issurim*, in *tumah*, across *Shas*. What exactly is being singled out here? The Gemara's first response is *tuma ka'amrinan, issura lo ka'amrinan* — we are speaking specifically about the domain of *tumah v'taharah*, not about prohibitions. In matters of forbidden foods, for example, we routinely follow *rov* to permit eating a piece of meat of unknown origin, treating it as definitely permitted. But perhaps that is a separate domain with its own rules.

### Kavua Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא תֵּשַׁע צְפַרְדְּעִין וְשֶׁרֶץ אֶחָד בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְנָגַע בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן נָגַע — בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד סְפֵקוֹ טָמֵא, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים סְפֵקוֹ טָהוֹר,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But isn't there** the case where there were **nine** dead **frogs,** which do not impart ritual impurity, **and one** carcass of a **creeping animal among them,** which does impart impurity, **and** someone **touched one of** these ten dead creatures, **and he does not know which of them he touched?** The *halakha* is as follows: If this occurred **in the private domain** the item's **uncertain** impurity renders it **impure,** as it is derived from the Torah that in cases of uncertainty with regard to ritual impurity in the private domain, the item is deemed impure. If the contact occurred **in the public domain,** the item's **uncertainty** leaves it **pure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַנִּמְצָא — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And in** a case where one of these creatures was separated from the rest and **was found** elsewhere, and the person touched it there, **follow the majority.** Since most of the animals do not impart ritual impurity, this individual remains pure. This is another case involving uncertain impurity where the Sages established the *halakha* as certain based on the majority.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאָה דְּאִשָּׁה קָאָמְרִינַן, טוּמְאָה בְּעָלְמָא לָא קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **We say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan's list is referring to matters of **ritual impurity of a woman,** whereas **we do not say** that the list includes cases that involve **ritual impurity in general.**

Even within *tumah v'taharah*, however, the Gemara presses further: we go by *rov* in other cases of *tumah* as well. The classic example that underlies this discussion is the principle of *kol kavua k'mechtza al mechtza dami* — anything that is fixed in its place is treated as fifty-fifty rather than by majority. This principle governs the famous case of nine stores selling kosher meat and one selling *neveilah*: if a person went to one of the stores and does not know which, the *safek* is adjudicated as fifty-fifty — not by the nine-to-one majority — and the meat is forbidden. The reason is that when one engages with objects in their fixed location, the problematic item is a present reality that cannot be subsumed into probabilistic reasoning. One is, in effect, confronting all ten stores simultaneously, and the ratio becomes irrelevant. By contrast, *uv'nimtza halech achar harov* — if a piece of meat is found on the ground, separated from any fixed location, then we follow the majority and assume it is kosher. In that case, the question is purely abstract: what is the identity of this one item before me, divorced from context? There we apply *rov* and rule definitively.

### Frogs Rodents

The same principle applies in *tumah v'taharah*: nine frogs — which do not transmit *tum'ah* when dead — and one dead rodent, a *sheretz*, which does transmit *tum'ah*, mixed together in a pile. If one touches one of them without knowing which, the analysis depends on setting. *B'reshus hayachid sfeiko tamei* — in a private domain, a fifty-fifty doubt is ruled *tamei*. *B'reshus harabim sfeiko tahor* — in a public domain, a fifty-fifty doubt is ruled *tahor*. The pile constitutes a *kavua* situation, so it is fifty-fifty regardless of the numerical ratio. *Uv'nimtza halech achar harov* — but if one item became separated from the pile and you touched it without knowing what it was, you follow the majority: since most of the pile was frogs, the separated item is assumed to be a frog, and you are *tahor*. And critically, this ruling is *vadai tahor* — even in a private domain, where doubt would normally be decided strictly, you are lenient, because you are treating the majority presumption as definitive rather than as a *safek* resolved strictly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר: מֵתִיב רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא טוֹעָה, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this explanation: **But when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said** that **Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Ḥanina raises an objection** against the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi from a *baraita* that deals with **an unsure woman,** i.e., one who does not know when she gave birth. Ravin added: **And I do not know what his objection** was from that *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין קָאָמְרִינַן, שְׁמַעְתָּתָא לָא קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We say** that only cases taught in **the Mishna** or a *baraita* are included in this list, whereas **we do not say** that those derived from an amoraic **halakhic statement,** e.g., the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, are included.

So even outside the three cases Rabbi Yochanan cites, we find *rov* being treated as *vadai* in *tumah v'taharah*. The Gemara therefore progressively narrows what Rabbi Yochanan is excluding: first from *issurim* to *tumah*, then from general *tumah* to *tumah* cases involving women, ultimately specifying *matnitin ka'amrinan, shemata lo ka'amrinan* — we are speaking of *mishnayos*, specifically the three Mishnaic cases involving women and *tumah v'taharah* where *rov* is treated as *vadai*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling about a pregnant woman who miscarries while crossing a river — that she brings a *korban* which may be eaten, proving her status is *vadai yoledet* — is raised as a potential fourth case but is deflected: *lo tiuvta v'lo say'ata* — the related discussion does not establish that ruling as a firm, independent Mishnaic case in this series.

## Meir Minority

### Infant Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: תִּינוֹק הַנִּמְצָא בְּצַד הָעִיסָּה, וּבָצֵק בְּיָדוֹ — רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִין, שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל תִּינוֹק לְטַפֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the source for Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion in this regard. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 3:8): If a ritually impure **child is found alongside** ritually pure **started dough** that has not yet risen, **and** he has **risen dough in his hand** that may have been removed from the larger portion of started dough, **Rabbi Meir deems** the started dough **pure,** since there is no proof the child touched it, as he might have been given the piece by someone else. **And the Rabbis deem it impure,** as they assume that he touched the started dough. The child is presumed to be impure, **because it is the norm of a child to handle** items.

The Gemara then raises a further case: *tinok hanimtza b'tzad ha'issah uvatzek b'yado* — an infant who is *tamei* (because his mother recently gave birth and is a *niddah*, and she handled the infant) is found beside a pile of dough, with a piece of dough in his hand. The question is whether the infant touched and thereby rendered *tamei* the entire pile of dough. The dough had a prior *chazakah* of being *tahor*. The evidence — dough in the infant's hand, infant sitting beside the pile — suggests strongly that he touched it. But there is a minority possibility: someone may have handed the infant that piece separately, without the infant having touched the main pile.

Rabbi Meir rules the dough is *tahor*. The Chachamim rule it is *tamei*.

### Meir Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? קָסָבַר: רוֹב תִּינוֹקוֹת מְטַפְּחִין, וּמִיעוּט אֵין מְטַפְּחִין, וְעִיסָּה זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת טְהוֹרָה עוֹמֶדֶת, סְמוֹךְ מִיעוּטָא לַחֲזָקָה, וְאִיתְּרַע לֵיהּ רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And we say** with regard to this dispute: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir? He holds** that **a majority of children handle** items that are within reach, in this case the dough, **and a minority do not handle** items within reach, **and this dough retains a presumptive status of purity,** since its impurity has not been definitively determined. Therefore, one should **append** the fact that the **minority** of children do not handle items within reach **to the presumptive status** of purity of the dough, **and** the force of the **majority** of children who handle items within reach **is weakened.** Therefore, the dough is considered pure.

The Gemara asks: how can Rabbi Meir ignore the *rov*? We generally say that *rov* wins. His reasoning is as follows: *rov tinokos metap'chim* — he concedes that most infants do go about touching and patting things. *Umiyut ein metap'chim* — but there is a minority that does not explore in that way. *Ve'issah zu b'chezkat taharah omedes* — and this dough stands with a *chazakah* of being *tahor*. *V'ne'etzmech miyuto l'chazakah* — combine the minority possibility that this particular infant is among those who did not touch the dough with the *chazakah* of the dough's prior *tahor* status. *V'isar lei rov* — and the two together outweigh the *rov*. It is two considerations — the *chazakah* and the minority — against one consideration — the majority. The minority tips the balance in favor of maintaining the *chazakah*.

This is a revealing window into Rabbi Meir's well-known principle of *choshesh l'miyuta* — being concerned for minority possibilities. It would be easy to read that principle as simply a character of stringency, a reluctance to be lenient when there is any residual possibility. But here we see something more principled: Rabbi Meir holds that a minority possibility *cannot be ignored*, regardless of the direction in which it cuts. When the minority possibility supports leniency — by reinforcing the *chazakah* — Rabbi Meir employs it equally. The principle is symmetric: minority possibilities have legal standing, and that standing can be deployed either way.

### Rabbanan Ignore

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן מִיעוּטָא ״כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי״, וְרוּבָּא וַחֲזָקָה — רוּבָּא עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** contend that in a case where the majority is followed, **the minority is considered like it does not exist. And** consequently, there is a conflict between the determining factors of **the majority** of impure children who handle items within reach **and the presumptive status** of purity of the dough. Therefore, **the majority takes precedence.**

The Chachamim hold the opposite: *miuta keman d'leisa dami* — the minority is treated as though it does not exist. Once it is dismissed, you are left with *rov* against *chazakah*, and *ruba adif* — the majority wins. The dough is *tamei*.

### Lakish Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: זוֹ הִיא חֲזָקָה שֶׁשּׂוֹרְפִין עָלֶיהָ אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין זוֹ חֲזָקָה שֶׁשּׂוֹרְפִין עָלֶיהָ אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: This** *halakha* of a child **is** an example of **a presumption,** that children handle items within reach, **over which *teruma* is burned,** as the Rabbis hold that it is sufficiently certain that the dough has become impure to allow it to be burned. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is not a presumption over which *teruma* is burned.** Rather, the dough is left aside, and can be neither eaten nor burned, due to the uncertainty whether it is impure. In this context, Rabbi Yoḥanan has already stated that when a majority is contradicted by a presumption, the status of uncertainty applies. Therefore, there was no need for him to specify the three cases he mentioned in order to exclude situations of this kind.

This brings the Gemara to the question of how *tamei* this dough is — is it *vadai tamei* or *safek tamei*? When *rov* prevails against *chazakah*, does it produce a definitive ruling or a stringently-resolved doubt? This is the debate between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan. *D'amar Reish Lakish mishum Rebbi Yoshiyah: zu hi chazakah shesorfin aleha es haterumah* — Reish Lakish holds that even against a *chazakah*, when *rov* wins, the result is *vadai tamei*, and *terumah* would be burned. *V'Rebbi Yochanan omer: ein zu chazakah shesorfin aleha es haterumah* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that when *rov* overcomes a *chazakah*, the status is only *tamei* with residual uncertainty. *Terumah* would not be burned; it would be left to rot rather than consumed, but the full consequences of *vadai tum'ah* do not apply. So when Rabbi Yochanan himself declared that in three cases we go by *rov* and treat it as *vadai*, we now understand what he is excluding: the case of *rov* against *chazakah* is precisely the case that is not *vadai* according to his own view. That exclusion is already established from his ruling elsewhere and need not be the point of the three-case enumeration.

## Yehuda Chunk

### Miscarriage Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי רוּבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּתְנַן: הַמַּפֶּלֶת חֲתִיכָה — אִם יֵשׁ עִמָּהּ דָּם טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that there are only three cases in which uncertainty is treated as certainty is meant **to exclude** a specific situation involving **a majority, as** discussed by **Rabbi Yehuda. As we learned** in a mishna (21a): In the case of a woman **who miscarries** an amorphous **piece** of flesh, **if there is blood** that emerges **with it,** the woman **is ritually impure** with the impurity of a menstruating woman. **And if not, she is pure,** as she is neither a menstruating woman nor a woman after childbirth. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In **both this** case, where blood emerged, **and that** case, where no blood emerged, the woman **is impure** with the impurity of a menstruating woman, as there was certainly undetected blood that emerged with the flesh.

The Gemara concludes by identifying what Rabbi Yochanan's three-case enumeration *does* exclude. The case under examination is that of Rabbi Yehuda. The Mishnah teaches: *hamapeles chatichah* — a woman who miscarries a chunk of indeterminate form, something so amorphous that it would not on its own make her a *yoledet* — what is her status? *Im yesh imah dam, tmeiah* — if she saw blood along with it, she is *tamei* as a *niddah*. *Ve'im lav, tehorah* — if not, she is *tahor*. Rabbi Yehuda says: *bein kach uvein kach tmeiah* — either way she is *tamei*, regardless of whether blood was visible.

### Four Red Colors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא טִימֵּא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶלָּא בַּחֲתִיכָה שֶׁל אַרְבַּע מִינֵי דָמִים, אֲבָל שְׁאָר מִינֵי דָמִים — טְהוֹרָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: שֶׁל אַרְבַּע מִינֵי דָמִים — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל טְמֵאָה, וְשֶׁל שְׁאָר דָמִים — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל טְהוֹרָה. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהִפִּילָה
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Rabbi Yehuda deemed** the woman **impure,** despite the fact that no blood emerged, **only in** the case of **a piece** of flesh that has the color **of** one of the **four types of** ritually impure **blood,** as stated in the mishna below (19a). **But** if it has the color **of other types of blood,** the woman is **pure. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If a woman miscarries a piece of flesh that has the color **of** one of the **four types of** ritually impure **blood, all,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, **agree** that she **is impure. And** likewise, if the piece has the color **of other types of blood, all agree** that **she is pure.** The Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda **disagree only** with regard to a case **where** the woman **miscarried** an amorphous piece of flesh,

The basis of Rabbi Yehuda's ruling is clarified through a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *lo tima Rebbi Yehuda ela b'chatichah shel arba'ah minei damim* — Rabbi Yehuda declares her *tamei* only when this chunk has the coloring of the four types of blood that constitute *dam niddah*. The four red colors specified in the Mishnah elsewhere as constituting *tamei* blood are the standard: if the chunk presents in one of those colors, it is presumed to be congealed, dried uterine blood, and therefore she is *tamei* as a *niddah* on account of it. *Aval shear minei damim, tehorah* — but if the chunk presents in other colors, colors outside those four types, she is *tehorah*.

### Yehuda Rov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָהּ יוֹדַעַת מָה הִפִּילָה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: זִיל בָּתַר רוֹב חֲתִיכוֹת, וְרוֹב חֲתִיכוֹת שֶׁל אַרְבַּע מִינֵי דָּמִים הָוְיָין. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: זִיל בָּתַר רוֹב חֲתִיכוֹת לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **and she** herself **does not know** exactly **what** the appearance of the piece of flesh that **she miscarried** was, e.g., if it was lost. In this case **Rabbi Yehuda holds: Follow the majority of** miscarriages of amorphous **pieces** of flesh, **and the majority of pieces** of flesh have the appearance **of** one of the **four types of** impure **blood. And the Rabbis hold: We do not say: Follow the majority of** miscarriages of amorphous **pieces** of flesh. Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan's mention of three cases is meant to exclude this statement of Rabbi Yehuda, who rules that the woman is definitely impure based on a majority.

The question then is what happens when the chunk emerged but was not examined — perhaps it dissolved or was lost. Rabbi Yehuda rules she is still *tamei*. The reason: *zil batar rov chatichot* — go by the majority of such chunks. *V'rov chatichot shel arba'ah minei damim havyan* — most chunks that emerge from a woman's body in this way have the coloring of the four types of *tamei* blood. Therefore, even without examining this particular chunk, the majority presumption is that it was of those colors, and she is *tamei*.

### Rabbanan Reject

The Rabbanan disagree: *zil batar rov chatichot lo amrinan* — we do not follow the majority of chunks here. The analysis of why this *rov* fails to carry the day has two possible readings. One is that the Rabbanan dispute Rabbi Yehuda's empirical premise — perhaps they do not agree that most such chunks are in fact of the four *tamei* colors. The other, and more structurally significant, reading is that even granting the majority, it runs against the woman's *chazakah*: she was previously *tehorah*, and that presumptive status cannot be overridden by a *rov* in this manner. Rabbi Yochanan enters here to state that even according to Rabbi Yehuda, when he rules she is *tamei*, this is not *vadai tamei* — it is *safek*, a *rov* that has prevailed but without the force of certainty. And the practical ruling follows the Chachamim: we do not apply Rabbi Yehuda's *rov* here, we go by the *chazakah* that she was previously *tehorah*, and she is *tehorah*. This, then, is what Rabbi Yochanan's three-case enumeration excludes: the *rov* of Rabbi Yehuda in the case of the *chatichah* is precisely the case where we do *not* treat the majority as *vadai* — where, according to the Chachamim, we do not even follow the *rov* at all, because the *chazakah* of *taharah* holds.
