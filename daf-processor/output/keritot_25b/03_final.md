# Keritot 25b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## YK & Chatat/Asham

### Mishna: YK & Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּיבֵי חַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת וַדָּאִין שֶׁעָבַר עֲלֵיהֶן יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים – חַיָּיבִין לְהָבִיא לְאַחַר יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, וְחַיָּיבֵי אֲשָׁמוֹת תְּלוּיִין – פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Those **liable** to bring **sin offerings and definite guilt offerings for whom Yom Kippur has passed are liable to bring** them **after Yom Kippur.** By contrast, those **liable** to bring **provisional guilt offerings are exempt** from bringing them after Yom Kippur.

The *mishna* with which our *daf* opens establishes a fundamental principle about the atoning power of Yom Kippur and its relationship to outstanding *korban* obligations. The *mishna* states: *chayvei chataot ve'ashamot vadai she'avar aleihem Yom HaKippurim chayavin lehavi achar Yom HaKippurim* — those who are obligated in *chataot* and *ashamot vadai* and over whom Yom Kippur has passed are still required to bring those offerings after Yom Kippur. By contrast, *chayvei asham talui peturin* — those obligated in an *asham talui* are exempt once Yom Kippur has passed.

The *gemara* immediately asks *mina hani milei* — from where do we derive these rules? The first question is how we know that Yom Kippur does not extinguish one's *chiyuv* in a *korban*.

### Rav Dimi's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִפֶּר עַל הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִטּוּמְאֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמִפִּשְׁעֵיהֶם לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״, חֲטָאִים דּוּמְיָא דִּפְשָׁעִים, מָה פְּשָׁעִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן – אַף חֲטָאִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן – מְכַפֵּר, אֲבָל חֲטָאִים בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן – לָא מְכַפַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, he said that **Rabbi Ami says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says** that **the verse states** with regard to Yom Kippur: **"And he shall make atonement for the holy place, from the impurity of the children of Israel, and from their transgressions, for all their sins"** (Leviticus 16:16). This indicates that **sins,** i.e., violations committed unwittingly, **are similar to transgressions,** violations committed intentionally: **Just as** Yom Kippur atones for **transgressions, which are not subject to** atonement through **an offering,** as personal sin offerings are brought only for unwitting sins, **so too** with regard to **sins,** Yom Kippur **atones** only for sins **that are not subject to** atonement through **an offering, but** Yom Kippur **does not atone** for **sins that are subject to** atonement through **an offering.**

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Chanina a *drasha* anchored in the *pasuk* concerning the *se'ir hanasa bifnim* — the goat whose blood is brought inside the sanctuary on Yom Kippur: *vechiper al hakodesh mitumot bnei Yisrael umipisheihem lechol chatatam* — "he shall atone for the sanctuary from the impurities of the children of Israel and from their *pishaim* and all of their *chataim*." The key distinction embedded in this verse is that *pesha* denotes a willful sin while *cheit* denotes an unintentional sin.

The *drasha* proceeds as follows: *chataim dumya depishaim* — the *chataim* that are atoned for are analogous to the *pishaim*. Just as *pishaim* are *delav bnei korban* — not the type of sin for which one brings a *korban*, since willful sins do not generate a *korban* obligation — so too the *chataim* that Yom Kippur atones for are those that are *delav bnei korban*. But *chataim* that are *bnei korban* — those obligated in a *korban* because they involve *kares* and will eventually be accompanied by *yedia* — Yom Kippur is not *mechaper* for.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא כִּי כְּתִיב הָדֵין קָרָא בְּשָׂעִיר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים הוּא דִּכְתִיב, דְּלָא מְכַפַּר עַל עֲבֵירוֹת דְּמִצְוָה יְדוּעָה, אֲבָל שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, דִּמְכַפַּר עַל עֲבֵירוֹת דְּמִצְוָה יְדוּעָה, אֵימָא לָךְ: אֲפִילּוּ עַל חֲטָאִים דִּבְנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ מְכַפַּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Dimi: **But** how can one derive this matter from the verse? **When that verse is written, it is written with regard to the goat whose** blood presentation is **performed inside** the Sanctuary on Yom Kippur,**which does not atone for a known violation of a mitzva,** but only for a violation that is unknown. Perhaps only that offering does not atone for sins that are subject to atonement through an offering, **but** with regard to **the scapegoat** that is dispatched to Azazel, **which atones for a known violation of a mitzva, I** could **say to you** that **it atones even for those sins that are subject to** atonement through **an offering.**

Abaye challenged Rav Dimi's *drasha* on structural grounds. This *pasuk*, he pointed out, is written specifically about the *se'ir hanasa bifnim* — not the goat sent to *la'azazel*, but the goat whose blood is offered inside the *Beit HaMikdash*. And that *se'ir* is categorically limited in its atoning scope: it only atones for the sins of *tumot mikdash vekodashav* — the *tumah* of the sanctuary and its sacred objects. It does not atone for a broad array of other sins.

Abaye's objection is therefore this: since the *se'ir hanasa bifnim* already operates in such a narrow domain, perhaps one could say that even within those *chataim* it does atone for, it works even for *chataim d'vnei korban* — sins obligated in a *korban*. The limitation to *delav bnei korban* might be an artifact of the *se'ir*'s restricted scope rather than a universal principle. You cannot prove from this *se'ir* that Yom Kippur generally fails to atone for *korban*-obligated sins.

### Se'ir Types

It is worth pausing to clarify the landscape of the Yom Kippur *se'irim*. The *se'ir hanasa bifnim* — whose blood is sprinkled inside the sanctuary — atones exclusively for *tumot mikdash vekodashav*. All other sins fall under the purview of the *se'ir hanasa bachutz*, namely the *se'ir hamishtaleach*, the goat sent to *azazel*. That latter goat has a far broader, across-the-board atoning effect. Abaye's point is thus that even granting Rav Dimi's *drasha*, one cannot generalize from the inside *se'ir* — which is limited to a very narrow category of sins — to the wide-ranging atonement of the *se'ir hamishtaleach*. The *se'ir hamishtaleach* might well atone even for *chataim d'vnei korban*.

## Se'ir Hamishtaleach

### Abaye's Se'ir Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, מֵהָכָא: ״וְהִתְוַדָּה עָלָיו [אֶת] כׇּל עֲוֹנוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶת כׇּל פִּשְׁעֵיהֶם לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״, חֲטָאִים דּוּמְיָא דִּפְשָׁעִים, מָה פְּשָׁעִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן – אַף חֲטָאִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן, אֲבָל חֲטָאִים דִּבְנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ – לָא מְכַפַּר. וּמַיעֲטֵיהּ קְרָא בְּשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, לְמֵימְרָא דְּעַל חֲטָאִים דִּבְנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ לָא מְכַפַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the matter is derived **from here,** a verse that deals with the scapegoat: **"And he shall confess over it all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions, for all their sins"** (Leviticus 16:21). This indicates that **sins are similar to transgressions: Just as** the scapegoat atones for **transgressions, which are not subject to** atonement through **an offering, so too** with regard to **sins,** the scapegoat atones only for sins **that are not subject to** atonement through **an offering, but** it **does not atone** for **sins that are subject to** atonement through **an offering. And the verse limited** the scope of atonement specifically **with regard to the scapegoat,** in order **to say that** despite the scapegoat's greater capacity to achieve atonement, **it does not atone for sins that are subject to** atonement through **an offering.**

The *gemara* therefore asks: given that the *se'ir hanasa bachutz* — the *se'ir hamishtaleach* — is the more sweeping vehicle of atonement, what does one do with it? Rather, says Abaye, the source must come from the *pasuk* about the *se'ir hamishtaleach* itself: *v'hisvada alav es kol avonos bnei Yisrael v'es kol pish'eihem l'chol chatosam* — "he shall confess over it all the iniquities of the children of Israel and all their *pish'aim* and all their *chatosam*."

Here again the *drasha* is *chataim dumya d'pish'aim*: the *chataim* atoned for are analogous to *pish'aim*. Just as *pish'aim* — willful sins — are *delav bnei korban*, so the *chataim* this verse speaks of are those *delav bnei korban*. But *chataim d'vnei korban* — those obligated in a *korban* — *lo mechaper*. The verse thus excludes, and the *gemara* notes that the *pasuk* has effectively limited things: *u'mi'ateih kra b'se'ir hamishtaleach* — the verse limits the *se'ir hamishtaleach* as well, teaching that even it does not atone for *chataim d'vnei korban*.

### Ravin's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי: כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״וְהִתְוַדָּה עָלָיו אֶת כׇּל עֲוֹנוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶת כׇּל פִּשְׁעֵיהֶם לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״, חֲטָאִים דּוּמְיָא דִּפְשָׁעִים, מָה פְּשָׁעִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן מְכַפֵּר, אַף חֲטָאִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן מְכַפֵּר, אֲבָל חֲטָאִים דִּבְנֵי קׇרְבָּן – לָא מְכַפַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** with regard to this matter that **when Rabin came** from Eretz Yisrael he related a statement that **Rabbi Yosei says** that **Reish Lakish says:** The verse states: **"And he shall confess over it all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions, for all their sins"** (Leviticus 16:21). This indicates that **sins are similar to transgressions: Just as** the scapegoat **atones** for transgressions, **which are not subject to** atonement through **an offering, so too** with regard to **sins,** the scapegoat **atones** only for sins **that are not subject to** atonement through **an offering, but** it **does not atone** for **sins that are subject to** atonement through **an offering.**

Independently, when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted the same teaching in the name of Reish Lakish, likewise grounding the *drasha* in the *pasuk* of the *se'ir hamishtaleach*: *v'hisvada alav es kol avonos bnei Yisrael v'es kol pish'eihem l'chol chatosam*. The formulation was identical — *chataim dumya d'pish'aim*, and just as *pish'aim* are *delav bnei korban mechaper*, so too only *chataim delav bnei korban mechaper*, but *chataim d'vnei korban lo mechaper*.

### Ravina's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב דִּימִי: מִמַּאי דְּהָנֵי פְּשָׁעִים לָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ? דִּלְמָא הָנֵי דִּבְנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ, כְּדִתְנַן: אַרְבָּעָה מְבִיאִין עַל הַזָּדוֹן כִּשְׁגָגָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi said to** Abaye: **From where** is it known **that those transgressions** mentioned in that verse **are not subject to** atonement through **an offering? Perhaps those** transgressions **are subject to** atonement through **an offering, as we learned** in a mishna (9a): There are **four** who **bring** an offering **for an intentional** transgression **as** they do for **an unwitting** sin: One who engages in intercourse with an espoused maidservant, a nazirite who became ritually impure, one who falsely takes the oath of testimony that he does not know testimony on behalf of another, and one who falsely takes the oath on a deposit that the item belonging to another is not in his possession. The Gemara does not provide Abaye's answer to this question at this point.

Abaye, upon hearing Ravin's version, noted that he himself had tried to use that same *pasuk* — but that Ravina bar Rav Dimi had challenged him. The challenge: *mi yeimar d'hanei pish'aim lav bnei korban ninhu?* Who says that the *pish'aim* referenced in the verse are paradigmatically sins that carry no *korban* obligation? *Dilma hanei bnei korban ninhu* — perhaps these very *pish'aim* are precisely sins for which one does bring a *korban*. As the earlier *mishna* in our *masechet* taught, there are four categories that bring a *korban* even for a willful act — *arba'ah mevi'im al hazadon kish'gagah*. Thus the *pish'aim* cited in the verse might well include *korban*-obligated sins, and therefore the *chataim* linked to them would likewise include *korban*-obligated sins — undercutting the entire *drasha*.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֲנָא נָמֵי מֵהָדֵין קְרָא אָמְרִי, וְאַקְשִׁי לִי רַב דִּימִי: מִמַּאי דְּהָנֵי פְּשָׁעִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ? דִּלְמָא הָנָךְ דִּבְנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ, כְּדִתְנַן: אַרְבָּעָה מְבִיאִין עַל הַזָּדוֹן כִּשְׁגָגָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִין: רוֹב פְּשָׁעִים לָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵי רוֹב כְּתִיב? אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵרֵישֵׁיהּ דִּקְרָא: ״וְהִתְוַדָּה עָלָיו אֶת כׇּל עֲוֹנוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְתַנְיָא: ״עֲוֹנוֹת״ – אֵלּוּ זְדוֹנוֹת. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״עֲוֹנָהּ בָּהּ״. ״וְאֶת כׇּל פִּשְׁעֵיהֶם לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״ – ״כׇּל״ לְמָה לִי? לְאַקְשׁוֹיֵי לִפְשָׁעִים, מָה פְּשָׁעִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן, אַף חֲטָאִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי קׇרְבָּן, אֲבָל חֲטָאִים דִּבְנֵי קׇרְבָּן נִינְהוּ – לָא מְכַפַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: I also said** that this *halakha* was derived **from that verse, and Rav Dimi raised** the following **difficulty** before **me: From where** is it known **that those transgressions** mentioned in the verse **are not subject to** atonement through **an offering? Perhaps those** transgressions **are subject to** atonement through **an offering, as we learned** in a mishna that there are **four** who **bring** an offering **for an intentional** transgression **as** they do for **an unwitting** sin. **Ravin said to** Abaye: **The majority of transgressions are not subject to** atonement through **an offering.** Abaye **said to him: Is** the term **majority written** in that verse? **Rather, Abaye said** that an answer to Rav Dimi's question can be inferred **from the beginning of the verse: "And he shall confess over it all the iniquities of the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 16:21), **and it is taught** in a *baraita*: The term **"iniquities"** is referring to **those** sins that were performed **intentionally. And similarly,** the verse **states:** "Because he despised the word of the Lord, and has broken His commandment; that soul shall utterly be cut off, **his iniquity shall be upon him"** (Numbers 15:31). Abaye continues: If so, when it states: **"And all their transgressions, for all their sins," why do I** need the verse to mention **"all** their transgressions," which is also referring to sins that were intentionally performed? It is written **to juxtapose** sins **to transgressions: Just as** the scapegoat atones for **transgressions,** the majority of **which are not subject to** atonement through **an offering, so too** with regard to **sins,** it atones only for sins **that are not subject to** atonement through **an offering, but** it **does not atone** for **sins that are subject to** atonement through **an offering.**

Ravin responded to this challenge with the argument of *rov pish'aim lav bnei korban* — the majority of *pish'aim* as a class are sins that carry no *korban* obligation, since the overwhelming majority of willful transgressions do not generate a *korban*. The verse, he argued, must be referring to the typical case.

Abaye, however, was not satisfied. *Midi rov ksiv?* — does the verse say "most *pish'aim*"? The verse speaks of *pish'aim* without qualification, and since some *pish'aim* are indeed *bnei korban*, the argument from majority does not definitively establish the *drasha*.

Abaye therefore proposed a different resolution, drawn from the *reisha* — the opening of the same *pasuk*. A *baraita* teaches: *avonos elu hazdonos* — *avon* means willful sin, as evidenced by the usage *avonah bah*, "her sin is upon her," which refers to a willful act. Since the verse already uses *avonos* to indicate willful sins, the subsequent mention of *pish'aim* — which also denotes willful sins — is redundant. The *pish'aim* are therefore an extra word, and its extra status allows us to interpret it precisely: *pish'aim* here represents sins that are paradigmatically *delav bnei korban*, which is the core characteristic of willful sins as a class. The redundancy eliminates the ambiguity that Ravina bar Rav Dimi had raised, because we are no longer relying on the *rov* of *pish'aim* but on the exegetical force of a repeated term that must be teaching something new. The result: *chataim d'vnei korban eino mechaper* — Yom Kippur does not atone for sins obligated in a *korban*.

It is worth pausing over the underlying logic here, because it is somewhat counterintuitive. There are three categories of sin in relation to Yom Kippur's atoning power: sins so minor or ambiguous that no *korban* is generated — those Yom Kippur atones for; sins that are fully willful and serious — those Yom Kippur also atones for; but sins in the middle, where the transgressor is obligated in a *korban* — those Yom Kippur does not atone for. The reason, presumably, is not that these middle sins are worse. Rather, the principle seems to be that Yom Kippur does not override obligations and rectifications that the *Torah* has already assigned. If the *Torah* prescribes a specific atonement, Yom Kippur is not going to substitute for it. This guiding principle will resurface throughout the subsequent discussion.

## Asham Talui & YK

### Asham Talui Exempted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּיבֵי אֲשָׁמוֹת תְּלוּיִין כּוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אֲמַר קְרָא: ״מִכֹּל חַטֹּאתֵיכֶם לִפְנֵי ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״ – חֵטְא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם – יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Those **liable** to bring **provisional guilt offerings** are exempt from bringing them after Yom Kippur. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rabbi Elazar said:** With regard to Yom Kippur **the verse states:** "For on this day shall atonement be made for you, to cleanse you; **from all your sins** you shall be pure **before the Lord"** (Leviticus 16:30). This indicates that **Yom Kippur atones** for **a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware,** i.e., an uncertain sin that renders one liable to bring a provisional guilt offering.

Having established why Yom Kippur does not extinguish *chatat* and *asham vadai* obligations, the *gemara* turns to the second part of the *mishna*: why does Yom Kippur wipe away the obligation of an *asham talui*? An *asham talui*, after all, is itself a *bnei korban* — one is obligated to bring it. Why should Yom Kippur cancel it when it does not cancel a *chatat*?

### God-Known Sins

The source is derived from the *pasuk*: *mikol chatoseichem lifnei Hashem titharu* — "from all your sins before God you shall be cleansed." From this Rabbi Elazar derives: *cheit she'ein makir bo ela HaMakom Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — a sin that only God knows about, Yom Kippur atones for. The *asham talui* is brought precisely when one is not certain that one has sinned — one suspects it but does not know definitively. Since the person himself does not have definite knowledge of the sin, it is categorically a *cheit she'ein makir bo ela HaMakom*, and Yom Kippur therefore works for it.

### Unknown Sins Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב תַּחְלִיפָא אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: קַדְמָיְיתָא נָמֵי לָא תֵּיתֵי לְמִפְשְׁטַיהּ לָא מִדְּרַב דִּימִי וְלָא מִדְּאַבַּיֵּי, אֶלָּא מֵהָא: חֵטְא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם – יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר, וּמִינַּהּ: חֵטְא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם הוּא דְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר, אֲבָל דְּיָדַע בֵּהּ – לָא מְכַפַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Taḥlifa, father of Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa, said in the name of Rava:** With regard to **the first** clause in the mishna **as well:** Those liable to bring sin offerings and definite guilt offerings for whom Yom Kippur has passed are liable to bring them after Yom Kippur, **one cannot bring** a convincing proof and find a way **to explain** that *halakha*, **neither from** that statement **of Rav Dimi,** who derived it from the verse: "And from their transgressions, for all their sins" (Leviticus 16:16), **nor from that** statement **of Abaye,** who derived it from the verse: "For all their transgressions, for all their sins" (Leviticus 16:21). **Rather,** it is derived **from that** which Rabbi Elazar said: With regard to Yom Kippur the verse states: "From all your sins before the Lord" (Leviticus 16:30). This indicates that **Yom Kippur atones** for **a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware,** i.e., an uncertain sin for which one brings a provisional guilt offering. **And from that** statement it is derived that **it is** only for **a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware that Yom Kippur atones, but it does not atone** for a sin **about which** the sinner **is aware.**

Rav Tachlifa, the father of Rav Huna, said in the name of Rava: one need not derive the two halves of the ruling from separate *drashot* as the earlier Amoraim did. Rather, this single *pasuk* — *cheit she'ein makir bo ela HaMakom Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — can do double duty. It tells you that Yom Kippur works for the *asham talui* case, and it simultaneously implies that Yom Kippur does not work for a *chatat* and an *asham*, because those involve sins that the sinner himself knows about.

The *gemara* however immediately notes a problem with making "known versus unknown" the operative criterion. Yom Kippur does atone for known sins — including willful sins that one is fully aware of but for which no *korban* is brought, and ordinary inadvertent sins that do not generate a *korban* obligation. So the rule cannot simply be that known sins are excluded from Yom Kippur's atonement.

## Obligation Framework

### Shvuos Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב תַּחְלִיפָא אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: חַיָּיבֵי מַלְקִיּוֹת שֶׁעָבַר עֲלֵיהֶן יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – חַיָּיב. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַאי שְׁנָא מֵחַיָּיבֵי חַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת וַדָּאִין? סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָתָם – מָמוֹנָא הוּא, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּגוּפָא הוּא – אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Taḥlifa, father of Rav Huna,** further **says in the name of Rava: Those liable to** receive **lashes for whom Yom Kippur has passed** are **liable** to receive those lashes after Yom Kippur. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious? What is** the **difference between** this case and the case of **those liable to** bring **sin offerings and definite guilt offerings,** who must bring them after Yom Kippur? The Gemara answers: It might **enter your mind to say: There,** where the liability to bring sin offerings and definite guilt offerings **is financial** in nature, Yom Kippur does not exempt one from financial obligations. **But here, where** the liability to incur lashes applies to one's **body,** one might **say** that he is **not** flogged after Yom Kippur. Therefore, Rav Taḥlifa **teaches us** that this is not so.

The *gemara* presses further. Rav Tachlifa also said in the name of Rava: *chayavei malkiyos she'avar aleihen Yom HaKippurim chayav* — one who is obligated in *malkos* and over whom Yom Kippur passes remains obligated. The *gemara* asks *peshita* — this is obvious. Why should it be any different from one obligated in a *chatat* or *asham vadai*?

The *gemara* responds that one might have thought there is a distinction: a *korban* obligation is analogous to a monetary debt — just as Yom Kippur does not wipe away financial obligations and the need to make restitution, it does not wipe away *korban* obligations either. *Malkos*, by contrast, is a punishment imposed on one's body, and perhaps one might reason that since Yom Kippur atones for the sin, the punishment associated with it should also fall away. Therefore the ruling is needed: no, *malkos* obligations remain as well.

This framing is itself striking. One would have expected the *korban* — as an act of atonement — to be more directly comparable to what Yom Kippur accomplishes, while *malkos* as a bodily punishment would seem more readily dissolved by atonement. Yet the *gemara* frames the *korban* as a debt and the *malkos* as a punishment, making the *korban* the more obvious case of persistence and the *malkos* the case requiring explicit ruling.

### Core Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: הוֹדַע וְלֹא הוֹדַע, עֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא – דְּאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ, הָא – דְּלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can Rava say that Yom Kippur does not exempt one from the liability to incur lashes? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Shevuot* 2b): For other prohibitions that are stated in the Torah, apart from those delineated in that mishna, whether **one became aware** of them before Yom Kippur **or did not become aware** of them until after Yom Kippur, whether they are **positive** mitzvot **or prohibitions,** Yom Kippur atones for them. This is the *halakha* despite the fact that one who transgresses a prohibition is liable to incur lashes. The Gemara answers: That is **not difficult. This** statement of Rava is referring to a transgression about **which** witnesses **warned him,** and therefore he is liable to incur lashes, and Yom Kippur does not completely atone for him. **That** mishna is referring to a transgression about **which they did not warn him,** and he is not liable to incur lashes, and therefore Yom Kippur provides full atonement.

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty from the *mishna* in *Shvuos*: *hoda v'lo hoda, asei v'lo ta'asei* — Yom Kippur atones for positive and negative commandments, known and unknown. Explicitly, then, Yom Kippur does work for known sins. If so, how can Rav Tachlifa's formulation — that Yom Kippur only works for what only God knows about — exclude *chatat* and *asham vadai* on the basis that the sinner knows of his sin?

### Hasra'ah Distinction

The *gemara* resolves this: *lo kashya — ha de'asru bei, ha de'lo asru bei*. There is no contradiction. When the sin involved a *hasra'ah* — a formal warning — and the transgressor is truly obligated in *malkos*, Yom Kippur does not wipe it away. When no *hasra'ah* was given and the person has merely sinned without incurring a formal legal obligation of punishment, Yom Kippur does atone.

At the end of the day, the operative principle is not really "known versus unknown" at all. The rule is whether or not the *Torah* has assigned a specific obligation or rectification. *Malkos* and *chatat* — these are assigned obligations that Yom Kippur does not override. Everything else — regardless of whether the sin was known or unknown, willful or inadvertent — Yom Kippur does atone for, so long as it does not carry a specific assigned obligation.

The one genuine exception where "unknown" remains relevant is the *asham talui*. There, although an obligation technically exists, it is an obligation generated by doubt — by not knowing. In that particular case, Yom Kippur does dissolve the obligation. One way to understand this is that while Yom Kippur in the normative sense atones for the person — addressing guilt — when there is an objective, known sin, the *Torah* requires the *korban* to address that objective dimension of sin as well. The *asham talui*, rooted in uncertainty, has no such objective dimension to address, and so Yom Kippur can handle it entirely.

## Safek Korbanos

### Safek Yoledet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק יוֹלֶדֶת שֶׁעָבַר עָלֶיהָ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לָא תַּיְיתֵי, דְּהָא כַּפַּר עֲלֵיהּ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, דְּחֵטְא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם הוּא! אָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״ – וְלֹא לְכׇל טוּמְאֹתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If that is so, that Yom Kippur atones for a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware, **a woman who gave birth who** is **uncertain** whether her miscarriage obligated her to bring a sin offering **should not bring** her sin offering after **Yom Kippur has passed, due to** the fact **that Yom Kippur atoned for her. As this is** a case of **a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware,** since God alone knows the nature of her miscarriage. **Rav Hoshaya said:** With regard to Yom Kippur the verse states: **"For all their sins"** (Leviticus 16:21), indicating that Yom Kippur atones for all their sins **but not for all their impurity.** A woman who gives birth does not bring a sin offering for atonement, but rather in order to purify her and thereby enable her to consume sacrificial meat.

The *gemara* now turns to a series of cases involving *safek korbanos* — offerings brought in situations of doubt — and asks why Yom Kippur does not dissolve those obligations just as it dissolves the *asham talui*. The first case: *safek yoledet* — a woman who may or may not have given birth. A *mishna* states explicitly that even if Yom Kippur passes over her, she remains obligated to bring her *korban*. Why? By analogy to the *asham talui*, this is a doubt-based *korban* obligation, and the sinner is unaware of any definite sin. Should Yom Kippur not clear this away?

### Sin vs. Impurity

Rav Oshaya answers with a textual distinction: the verse says *l'chol chatosam* — for all their *sins* — *v'lo l'chol tumasam*, not for all their *tumah*. While the Yom Kippur *psukim* do mention *tumah*, that is *tumah* that flows from sin. Yom Kippur provides *taharah* for sin, but it does not substitute for the *taharah* process that rectifies issues of ritual impurity. The *yoledet*'s *korban* is part of her *taharah* process, not primarily an act of atonement.

### RSBY Yoledet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי, דְּאָמַר יוֹלֶדֶת חוֹטֵאת הִיא, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? יוֹלֶדֶת כִּי קָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן – לְאִישְׁתְּרוֹיֵי בַּאֲכִילַת קָדָשִׁים הוּא, וְלָא לְכַפָּרָה מֵתְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai, who says: A woman who gave birth is a sinner, what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: Even according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, **when a woman who gave birth brings an offering,** she brings it in order **to permit** her to **eat sacrificial** food, which is the final stage in her process of purification; **but she does not bring** the offering **for atonement.** Therefore, she must bring her offering after Yom Kippur, as Yom Kippur effects atonement but not purification.

One might ask: what about the view of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, who holds that a *yoledet* is a *chotei* — a sinner? He famously derives from the *yoledet*'s *chatat* that she sinned by swearing during childbirth never to be intimate with her husband again, and she requires atonement for that rash oath. According to this view, there is an element of genuine sin underlying the *yoledet*'s *korban*. Why does Yom Kippur not address it?

### Yoledet Purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלֶיהָ חַטַּאת הָעוֹף סָפֵק, וְעָבַר עָלֶיהָ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – חַיֶּיבֶת לְהָבִיא לְאַחַר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַכְשַׁרְתָּהּ לֶאֱכוֹל בִּזְבָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that **we learn** in the mishna (25a) **as well: A woman upon whom** it is incumbent to bring **a bird sin offering** due to **uncertainty,** e.g., uncertainty as to **whether** her miscarriage obligated her to bring the sin offering of a woman who gave birth, **for whom Yom Kippur has passed, is liable to bring** it **after Yom Kippur.** This is **because** the offering does not come as atonement for a sin. Rather, **it renders** her **eligible to partake** of the meat **of offerings.**

Rav Ashi responds: even according to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, the *korban* of a *yoledet* functions *l'ishtruyei b'achilas kodashim* — to render her fit to eat *kodshim* again. Whatever sin element may be present, the fundamental purpose of the *korban* is to complete her *taharah* and restore her status, not to achieve atonement in the primary sense. This is a striking concession: even the view that identifies a sin element in the *yoledet*'s situation acknowledges that the atoning function is not what drives the obligation. Yom Kippur, which addresses atonement, therefore cannot substitute for a *korban* whose essential role is *taharah*.

Rav Ashi further notes that this is effectively demonstrated by our own *mishna*, which states explicitly: *ha'isha sheyesh aleiha chatas ha'of safek v'avar aleiha Yom HaKippurim chayaves l'havi l'achar Yom HaKippurim* — a woman with a doubtful *korban* obligation from a possible childbirth must still bring after Yom Kippur. The reason the *mishna* gives is *mipnei shemachshartah l'echol b'zvachim* — because the *korban* makes her fit to eat sacrificial food. The *taharah* function is explicit.

### Safek Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, סְפֵק מְצוֹרָע שֶׁעָבַר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לָא מַיְיתֵי, דְּהָא עֲבַר עֲלֵיהּ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, דְּחֵטְא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם הוּא! אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״ – וְלֹא לְכׇל טוּמְאֹתָם. וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַל שִׁבְעָה דְּבָרִים נְגָעִים בָּאִים! ... מְצוֹרָע כִּי מַיְיתֵי לָאו לְכַפָּרָה מַיְיתֵי, אֶלָּא לְאִישְׁתְּרוֹיֵי בַּאֲכִילַת קָדָשִׁים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** then **an uncertain leper** who is obligated to bring an offering for purification **for** whom **Yom Kippur has passed should not bring** his offering, **due to** the fact **that Yom Kippur has passed for him, as this is** a case of **a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware,** since God knows if he is impure. **Rabbi Oshaya said** that the verse states: **"For all their sins"** (Leviticus 16:21), **but not for all their impurity,** and this offering is also brought for purification. The Gemara objects: **But doesn't Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Leprous **marks come due to seven matters,** i.e., there are seven sins that are the possible causes of leprosy. ... **When a leper brings** an offering, **he does not bring** the offering **for atonement.** Rather, it is brought in order **to permit** him to **eat sacrificial** food. Therefore, he must bring his offering after Yom Kippur.

The *gemara* continues: what about a *safek metzora* — someone whose *tzara'as* status is uncertain — who must bring *korbanos* as part of the *taharah* process at the conclusion of *tzara'as*? Why does Yom Kippur not dissolve that obligation? The answer is the same: Rav Oshaya invokes *l'chol chatosam v'lo l'chol tumasam*. The *korbanos* of a *metzora* are part of the *taharah* process, not an atonement for sin — even though Rabbi Yochanan taught in the name of Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani that *negaim* come as a result of seven specific sins. Even when sin is an underlying cause, the *korbanos* serve *taharah*, not *kaparah*, and *ki maysei lav l'chapara maysei* — the *metzora* does not bring these offerings for atonement. Their purpose is *l'ishtruyei b'achilas kodshim* — to allow him to eat *kodshim* again.

### Safek Nazir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, סְפֵק נָזִיר שֶׁעָבַר עָלָיו יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן, דְּהָא כַּפַּר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, דְּחֵטְא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם הוּא! אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: לְכׇל חַטָּאת״ – וְלֹא לְכׇל טוּמְאָה. וּלְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן הַקַּפָּר, דְּאָמַר: נָזִיר חוֹטֵא הוּא, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? נָזִיר כִּי קָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן, לָאו לְכַפָּרָה מַיְיתֵי, לְאִשְׁתְּרוֹיֵי בַּאֲכִילַת קָדָשִׁים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** that Yom Kippur atones for a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware, then in a case of **an uncertain nazirite,** i.e., when it is uncertain if the nazirite became impure through contact with the dead, which would obligate him to bring an offering for purification, **for whom Yom Kippur has passed,** he should **not bring an offering, due to** the fact **that Yom Kippur atoned** for him, **as this** too **is a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware.** Once again, **Rabbi Oshaya said** that the verse states that Yom Kippur atones **"for all their sins," but not for all their impurity.** The Gemara asks: **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben HaKappar, who says: A nazirite is a sinner,** as he has caused himself to suffer by abstaining from wine, **what is there to say?** The Gemara answers similarly: Even so, **when a nazirite brings an offering, he does not bring** the offering **for atonement.** Rather, he brings **it** in order **to permit** him to **eat sacrificial** food. Consequently, he must bring his offering after Yom Kippur.

The *gemara* moves to the case of *safek nazir* — which in context means a *nazir* who became *tamei* under uncertain circumstances, and must bring a *korban* at the conclusion of the *tumah* period. Why does Yom Kippur not dissolve this doubt-based obligation?

Again: *l'chol chatosam v'lo l'chol tumasam*. The *tumah* dimension places this outside Yom Kippur's atoning reach.

But what about the view of Rabbi Elazar Ben Hakapar, who holds that a *nazir* is a *chotei* — a sinner — precisely because he denied himself wine? Here too the question arises: given a sin element, why doesn't Yom Kippur work? The *gemara* clarifies that Rabbi Elazar Ben Hakapar's *drasha* is specifically directed at the *chatat* brought by the *nazir tamei*, and his point may be that a *nazir* who was so careless as to allow himself to become *tamei* demonstrates that his *nezirus* was not sincerely motivated — which is precisely why this *nazir* in particular is called a *chotei*.

Nevertheless, the *gemara* answers: *nazir ki mayisi korban lav l'kapara mayisi*. The *korban* of the *nazir tamei* is not primarily for atonement. Notably, the usual *taharah*-completion formula — *l'ishtruyei b'achilas kodshim* — does not quite apply here either, since the *nazir* actually achieves *taharah* on the seventh day, and the *chatat* he brings is on the eighth day. Instead, the *gemara* gives a different functional explanation: *l'meichal alei nezirus tahara hu* — the *korban* serves to initiate the next stage of *nezirus*, the *nezirus* of *taharah*. It is not closing out *tumah*; it is opening a new period. This remains fundamentally a procedural and structural function rather than an atoning one, and Yom Kippur therefore does not displace it.

### Safek Sotah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, סְפֵק סוֹטָה, שֶׁעָבַר עָלֶיהָ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לָא תַּיְיתֵי, דְּהָא כַּפַּר עֲלַהּ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, דְּחֵטְא שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם! אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״לְכׇל חַטֹּאתָם״ – וְלֹא לְכׇל טוּמְאֹתָם. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בּוֹעֵל מַכִּיר בּוֹ. רָבָא אָמַר: סוֹטָה כִּי מֵתְיָא – לְבָרֵר עָוֹן (קָא אָתְיָא).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to object: **If that is so,** that Yom Kippur atones for a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware, then in a case of **an uncertain *sota*,** i.e., a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful who then secludes herself with another man, **for whom Yom Kippur has passed,** she should **not bring** an offering, **due to** the fact **that Yom Kippur has atoned for her, as this is a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware. Rabbi Oshaya said** that the verse states: **"For all their sins," but not for all their impurity,** and the actions of a *sota* are referred to as rendering her impure (see Numbers 5:13). **Abaye said** a different answer: This is not a case of a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware, because the **paramour,** i.e., the man suspected of having had intercourse with her, **knows about** her sin. **Rava said** that the case of a *sota* is not difficult: **When a *sota* brings** an offering, **she brings** it in order **to clarify** the existence of **iniquity,** but not for atonement.

The *gemara* turns to *safek sotah* — a woman who is a *sotah*, whose very status is defined by doubt, since she may or may not have committed adultery. Her *korban* is brought under conditions of uncertainty. Why does Yom Kippur not dissolve this obligation?

One might have argued the case for Yom Kippur applying: this involves a possible trespass against God and against her husband, and the sin — if it occurred — is something only God truly knows about (*cheit she'ein makir bo ela HaMakom*). Should that not bring it within Yom Kippur's purview?

The *gemara* offers several answers. Rav Oshaya's standard formula of *l'chol chatosam v'lo l'chol tumasam* is brought, though many textual variants strike it from this case since the *sotah*'s situation is not fundamentally about *tumah* in the ritual sense, even though she is called *tamei'ah*.

Abaye responds instead: *bo'el makir bo* — the adulterer, if one exists, knows about it. This undercuts the argument that it is a sin known only to God.

Rava gives the definitive answer: *sotah ki matya levar avon ka'ati* — the *sotah*'s *korban* does not come to atone for sin. It comes to clarify — to determine through the *sotah* ritual whether she sinned or not. Since its function is clarification rather than atonement, Yom Kippur, which addresses atonement, cannot substitute for it. The process of clarification must still take place even after Yom Kippur passes.

### Eglah Arufah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה שֶׁעָבַר עָלֶיהָ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים! ... אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הוֹרֵג מַכִּיר. רָבָא אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלָאָרֶץ לֹא יְכוּפַּר לַדָּם אֲשֶׁר שֻׁפַּךְ בָּהּ וְגוֹ׳״. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא: ״כַּפֵּר לְעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳״ – רְאוּיָה כַּפָּרָה זוֹ שֶׁתְּכַפֵּר עַל יוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty in the same format as above: **If that is so,** in the case of **a heifer whose neck is broken, with regard to which Yom Kippur has passed,** the heifer should not be brought, as this is a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware, since the identity of the murderer is unknown. ... **Abaye said:** The **murderer** himself **knows** that he is the guilty party. **Rava said** that **the verse states: "And no atonement can be made for the land for the blood that is shed therein,** but by the blood of him who shed it" (Numbers 35:33).This indicates that atonement for murder can be achieved only by means of the heifer whose neck is broken or capital punishment for the murderer, not by Yom Kippur. **Rav Pappa said** another answer: **The verse states** that the priests at the ceremony of the heifer whose neck is broken should recite the following sentence: **"Forgive,** Lord, **Your people Israel,** whom You have redeemed" (Deuteronomy 21:8). The verse indicates that **this atonement,** which is achieved through the heifer whose neck is broken, **is** also **fit to atone** retroactively **for those who left Egypt,** i.e., every case of unknown murder that occurred since the Jews left Egypt. It can be inferred from here that Yom Kippur of previous years have not atoned for those past murders.

The *gemara* takes up the most challenging case: *eglah arufah* — the heifer brought when a murder victim is found between cities and the perpetrator is unknown. This too rests on a foundation of doubt regarding who is responsible. Why does Yom Kippur not dissolve the community's obligation?

Abaye answers: *horeg makir* — the murderer knows. It is therefore not a *cheit she'ein makir bo ela HaMakom*. Someone does have knowledge of the sin, even if the court does not.

Rava's answer draws on a *pasuk*: *vela'aretz lo yekhuppar ladam asher shuppakh bah* — the land cannot be atoned for the blood that was shed upon it except through the prescribed process. There is something categorically unique about murder that places it outside the ordinary reach of Yom Kippur's atonement. The verse signals that no other mechanism — including Yom Kippur — will suffice. The *eglah arufah* ritual holds the situation in abeyance until the murderer is found; only the murderer's death or the performance of this particular ceremony addresses the blood-guilt. This raises the broader implication that Yom Kippur may not atone for *retzicha* at all — a question the *gemara* leaves hovering.

Rav Papa adds yet another source: *kapper le'ammekha Yisrael asher padita* — "atone for Your people Israel whom You redeemed." From this the *gemara* derives that this atonement is fit to atone for those who left Egypt, implying that this particular form of atonement is perpetually required regardless of how many Yom Kippurs have intervened.

## Yedia Criterion

### Unknown During YK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: חֵטְא שֶׁאֵין מַכִּיר בּוֹ אֶלָּא הַמָּקוֹם יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מְכַפֵּר, אֵימָא: כִּי מִתְיְידַע לֵיהּ בָּתַר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים נָמֵי לָא מַיְיתֵי חַטָּאת! אָמַר רַבִּי זְעֵירָא: לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, דִּכְתִיב קְרָא יְדִיעָה גַּבֵּי חַטָּאת, וְגַבֵּי נְשִׂיא, וְגַבֵּי צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Now that you said** that **Yom Kippur atones for a sin of which only the Omnipresent is aware,** one can **say** that **when** his sin **becomes known to him after Yom Kippur as well, he should not bring a sin offering. Rabbi Zeira said** that **you cannot say** this, **as** a term of **knowing is written** in **a verse** three times (see Leviticus 4:14, 23, 28): **With regard to** an individual's **sin offering and with regard to** the sin offering of **a *Nasi*, and with regard to a communal** sin offering. Since it was not necessary to repeat an expression of knowing in all three instances, this teaches that anyone who knows about his sin must bring a sin offering, even if he became aware after Yom Kippur.

Having established that Yom Kippur does dissolve an *asham talui* obligation on the grounds that it is a *cheit she'ein makir bo ela HaMakom*, the *gemara* now raises a further question. Since during Yom Kippur the sin was unknown — and we said Yom Kippur works for what only God knows — should we not go further and say that if the person later discovers he definitely did sin, he still need not bring a *chatat*? Perhaps Yom Kippur fully resolved the matter at the time, and any subsequent discovery changes nothing. The *gemara* asks: *eima ki mishtakhach leh batar Yom HaKippurim nami lo mayti chatat* — perhaps when one finds out after Yom Kippur, one still need not bring a *chatat*.

### Triple Yedia

Rabbi Zeira rejects this emphatically: *lo matzit amart* — this cannot be said. The reason is that the *pasuk* mentions *yedia* — the becoming-aware criterion for obligating a *korban* — three times: once by the *chatat* of the ordinary *yachid*, once by the *nasi*, and once by the *tzibur* (when the *beis din* issues an erroneous ruling and the community acts upon it). The verse says *o hoda elav chattato asher chata bah* — "or his sin became known to him." Since this appears three times, at least one mention is extra. That extra mention teaches that whenever one becomes aware of the sin — even after Yom Kippur, even if during Yom Kippur one had no knowledge — the *chatat* obligation is triggered.

The logic follows: Yom Kippur, in the context of an *asham talui*, provides protection from liability for the period of doubt. But it does not erase the underlying sin. When knowledge later arrives, the obligation crystallizes. The *asham talui* itself never claimed to wipe away the sin — only to protect the person pending clarification. So too Yom Kippur in this context protects but does not permanently resolve.

### One Case Insufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב קְרָא גַּבֵּי יָחִיד, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: כּוּלְּהוֹן מִיָּחִיד לָא אָתְאָן, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְיָחִיד, שֶׁכׇּל קׇרְבָּנוֹ נְקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this derivation: All three instances of a term of **knowing** are **necessary** to teach that each sin offering is brought only for a known sin and not for an uncertain sin, and therefore no instance of the term is superfluous. The Gemara elaborates: **As, if the verse** had **stated** a term of knowing **with regard to** the sin offering of **an individual, I would say** that the *halakhot* of **all** sin offerings, i.e., of a *Nasi* and the community, **cannot be derived from** the sin offering of **an individual, as** this suggestion **can be refuted: What** is unique **about** the sin offering of **an individual?** It is unique in **that all of his** sin **offerings are female,** either a ewe or a female goat, whereas a *Nasi* brings a male goat, and the sin offering of the community is a bull.

The *gemara* then examines why all three mentions of *yedia* are necessary — and which one is the "extra." A single mention would not suffice to establish *yedia* as a universal criterion applicable across all three cases, because each case has a particular leniency that would allow one to argue the rule is local rather than universal.

### Yachid & Nasi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְתּוֹב גַּבֵּי נָשִׂיא, וְנַיְיתֵי הָנָךְ מִנָּשִׂיא! אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְנָשִׂיא, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין בִּשְׁמִיעַת קוֹל, תֹּאמַר בְּיָחִיד שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנוֹ בִּשְׁמִיעַת קוֹל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: And if you would say: **Let** the verse **write** a term of knowing **with regard to** the sin offering of **a *Nasi*, and let these,** the sin offerings of an individual and the community, **be derived from** that of **a *Nasi*,** this derivation can likewise be rejected. The Gemara elaborates: The sin offering of **an individual cannot be derived from** that of **a *Nasi*, as** this suggestion **can be refuted: What** is unique **about** the sin offering of **a *Nasi*?** It is unique and more lenient than the sin offering of an individual **in that** a *Nasi*, i.e., a king, **is not included in** the obligation of a sin offering for **hearing a voice,** i.e., taking a false oath that one did not witness an event (see Leviticus 5:1), as a king may not testify. Can **you say** the same **with regard to an individual, who is included in** the obligation of a sin offering for **hearing a voice?**

If the *Torah* wrote *yedia* only by the *yachid*, one could not derive the *nasi* and *tzibur* from it: *mah leyachid shekein kol korbano nekeivah* — since the *yachid* always brings a female *korban*, which is considered a lighter obligation, one might say the *Torah* goes easy on him and therefore requires *yedia* before obligating him. But the *nasi* and *tzibur*, who bring male *korbanos* — heavier obligations — one might say there the *Torah* is strict, and they would be obligated even without definite *yedia*.

Conversely, if the *Torah* wrote it only by the *nasi*, one could not derive the *yachid*: *mah lenasi shekein ein bishmias kol* — the *nasi* is exempt from the *korban* for failing to testify under oath, since a king cannot be compelled to testify. That leniency regarding the *nasi* might explain why *yedia* is required for him, while the *yachid*, who does bear that oath-testimony obligation, might be held to a stricter standard and obligated even without definite *yedia*.

### Tzibur Uniqueness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצִבּוּר מִנָּשִׂיא לָא אָתֵי, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְנָשִׂיא, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעֱלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: And if** you refute this suggestion **due to** the fact **that** an individual sin offering is more lenient because **all his** sin **offerings are female,** the sin offering of the **community, where** there **is no** case **of a female offering, proves** that this is not the key factor. Since for both of these categories of sin offering **there is no obligation** to bring a sin offering **until there is knowledge** of the sin, **why do I** need a term of **knowing that is written with regard to** the sin offering of **a *Nasi*?**

If the *Torah* wrote it only by the *tzibur*, one could not derive the others: *mah letzibur shekein ein chayav ela al he'elam davar im shigegas ma'aseh* — the *tzibur beis din* only becomes obligated when there is an erroneous *psak* followed by action in accordance with it. This more limited mechanism for incurring obligation might explain why *yedia* is required, while the *yachid* and *nasi*, who incur obligation through the sin directly without this two-stage process, might not require *yedia*.

### Two-Case Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵחֲדָא יְדִיעָה לָא אָתְיָא, תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא יְדִיעָה מִתַּרְתֵּי – לָא נִכְתּוֹב יְדִיעָה גַּבֵּי יָחִיד, וְתֵיתֵי מִנָּשִׂיא וְצִבּוּר. אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְנָשִׂיא וְצִבּוּר, שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָן בִּשְׁמִיעַת קוֹל, תֹּאמַר בְּיָחִיד שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בִּשְׁמִיעַת קוֹל! לָא נִכְתּוֹב גַּבֵּי צִבּוּר, וְתֵיתֵי מִידִיעָה דְּיָחִיד וְנָשִׂיא. אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְיָחִיד וְנָשִׂיא שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בְּקׇרְבָּ[נָ]ן נְקֵבָה, תֹּאמַר בְּצִבּוּר שֶׁאֵין בְּקׇרְבָּ[נָ]ן נְקֵבָה! לָא נִכְתּוֹב גַּבֵּי נָשִׂיא, וְתֵיתֵי מִידִיעָה דְּיָחִיד וְצִבּוּר. מַאי פָּרְכַתְּ? אִי מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵינָן בִּשְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל – יָחִיד יוֹכִיחַ, דְּאֵינוֹ בִּשְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל, וְאִי מִשּׁוּם דְּיֵשׁ בְּכׇל קׇרְבָּנוֹ נְקֵבָה – צִבּוּר יוֹכִיחַ, דְּאֵין בְּקׇרְבָּנָן נְקֵבָה, עַד דְּאִית לְהוֹן יְדִיעָה לָא מִחַיַּיב, לְמָה לִי דִּכְתִיב יְדִיעָה גַּבֵּי נָשִׂיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Granted that two instances of a term of **knowing cannot be derived from one** instance of a term of **knowing.** Nevertheless, **let one** instance of a term of **knowing be derived from** the other **two,** as follows: **Let** the Torah **not write** a term of **knowing with regard to** the sin offering of **an individual, and let it be derived from** the sin offering of **a *Nasi* and** that of the **community. What** can you say to **refute** this suggestion? **If** you refute this **due to the fact that** a communal sin offering is more lenient, as **it is not included in** the obligation of a sin offering for **hearing a voice,** the sin offering of **an individual, which is included in** the obligation of a sin offering for **hearing a voice, proves** that this is not the decisive consideration. **And if** you refute this suggestion **due to** the fact **that** an individual sin offering is more lenient because **all his** sin **offerings are female,** the sin offering of the **community, where** there **is no** case **of a female offering, proves** that this is not the key factor. Since for both of these categories of sin offering **there is no obligation** to bring a sin offering **until there is knowledge** of the sin, **why do I** need a term of **knowing that is written with regard to** the sin offering of **a *Nasi*?**

Since no single case can establish the rule, the *gemara* considers whether two cases together could yield the third by common denominator — *tzad hashaveh*. The *gemara* examines all three pairings.

Writing *yedia* by *nasi* and *tzibur* but not by *yachid*: the common leniency of *nasi* and *tzibur* — that neither is subject to the oath-of-testimony *korban* (*bishmias kol*) — does not exist by *yachid*, so one might argue the *yachid* is more strict and does not require *yedia*.

Writing *yedia* by *yachid* and *nasi* but not by *tzibur*: the common feature of *yachid* and *nasi* is that both have at least some *korban* that is female (*nekeivah*) — the *yachid* generally and the *nasi* in the case of *avodah zarah* — which represents a leniency. The *tzibur* has no female *korban*, making it stricter; perhaps the *Torah* is therefore more stringent with *tzibur* and does not require *yedia*.

### Nasi Extra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְגוּפֵיהּ, דְּהָא אָתְיָא מִיָּחִיד וְצִבּוּר, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְהֵיכָא דְּמִתְיְידַע לֵיהּ בָּתַר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, דְּמַיְיתֵי חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **If** this term of knowing **is not** necessary for the **matter of** the sin offering of a *Nasi* **itself, as it is** already **derived from** the cases of **an individual and** the **community, apply it** to the **matter of** a case **where** his sin **becomes known to him** only **after Yom Kippur,** i.e., **that** in such an instance **he brings a sin offering.**

The third combination — writing *yedia* by *yachid* and *tzibur* but not by *nasi* — succeeds. Any challenge based on the *yachid*'s leniency (*korbano nekeivah*) is answered by *tzibur*, which has no female *korban* and yet is included. Any challenge based on the *tzibur*'s leniency (*he'elam davar im shigegas ma'aseh*) is answered by *yachid*, who has no such requirement and yet is included. The leniency unique to each is not shared by the other, demonstrating that neither leniency is the operative reason for the *yedia* criterion. From *yachid* and *tzibur* together, therefore, one could have derived the rule for *nasi* as well.

The mention of *yedia* by *nasi* is therefore the extra mention. *Im eino inyan legufo* — if it is not needed to teach *yedia* for the *nasi* himself, since that can be derived from *yachid* and *tzibur* — *tanihu inyan leheicha demistayda leh batar Yom HaKippurim* — apply it to teach the case of one who discovers the sin after Yom Kippur: *demayti chatat asher chata'o* — he must bring a *chatat*. Post-Yom Kippur discovery of a sin fully obligates one in bringing a *chatat*, even though during Yom Kippur itself only God knew of the sin.

### Post-YK Discovery

This is the conclusion of the present stage of the discussion. The *yedia* principle operates independently of when awareness arrives. Yom Kippur's capacity to work for a *cheit she'ein makir bo ela HaMakom* does not permanently foreclose the *chatat* obligation — it merely holds it in suspension during the period of unknowing. Once *yedia* arrives, even if Yom Kippur has already passed, the obligation crystallizes in full. We know this specifically from the extra mention of *yedia* by *nasi*, which, having been rendered superfluous by the *tzad hashaveh* of *yachid* and *tzibur*, must be redirected to teach this law of post-Yom Kippur discovery.
