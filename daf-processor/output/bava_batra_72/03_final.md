# Bava Batra 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Huna on Trees

### Rav Huna's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אִיתָא דְּלָא שַׁיַּיר, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״עֲקוֹר אִילָנָךְ, שְׁקוֹל וְזִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** **if it is so that** the prior owner **did not retain** some of the land for himself, **let** the buyer **say to him: Uproot your trees, take** them, **and go,** as the trees draw water and nutrients from the soil, causing damage to the buyer's land. Therefore, the seller must have retained for himself the land needed for these trees.

Today's daf is a continuation of 72a, the final daf of the *perek*, picking up from yesterday's discussion. Rav Huna established that even though when one sells trees the land does not accompany them, if one *retains* ownership of trees, one retains ownership of the land beneath them. The precise meaning of this "ownership" is itself disputed. The Rashbam understands it as literal ownership of the underlying plot. Tosafot, by contrast, argues that it means something more limited: not that the seller permanently owns the soil, but that while the tree stands, he possesses sufficient rights over the ground to draw on its nutrients — and even to plant beneath it. Should the tree die, those rights expire.

### Be'ayin Ra'ah vs Yafah

The further question is whether Rav Huna's principle operates according to the Rabbanan, who hold *mocher be'ayin ra'ah* — that a seller transfers with a stingy eye — or even according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds *mocher be'ayin yafah*, that a seller transfers with a generous eye. According to the Rabbanan, the seller keeps as much as possible, so it is straightforward that retaining a tree means retaining the land beneath it, just as retaining a well means retaining the path to it. The more striking claim is that even according to Rabbi Akiva, who in principle would have the seller give away more, the retention of the tree still preserves the underlying land — because the right to draw nutrients from the soil is a practical necessity, and once that right exists by necessity, it crystallizes into full ownership or planting rights in the manner Rabbi Akiva would recognize.

## Mishnah Challenge

### Hekdesh & Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה – לֹא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶלָּא חָרוּב הַמּוּרְכָּב, וְסַדַּן הַשִּׁקְמָה. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: ״אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? הוֹאִיל וְיוֹנְקִין מִשְּׂדֵה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **We learned** in the mishna here that **Rabbi Shimon says: One who consecrates a field has not consecrated** any of the items that are ordinarily excluded from a sale **except for the grafted carob tree and the sycamore trunk. And it is taught with regard to** this in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** that it is specifically the carob tree and the sycamore trunk that are consecrated? **Since** they **draw** their **nutrients from a consecrated field,** the owner must have had in mind to consecrate them as well, as otherwise his trees would be nurtured from consecrated property.

### R. Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ שַׁיּוֹרֵי שַׁיַּיר, כִּי קָא יָנְקִי – מִדְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ קָא יָנְקִי!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind,** as Rav Huna claims, that when the seller retains certain trees for himself, he also **retains** the land around them so that they will be nurtured from soil that belongs to him, what is the reason for Rabbi Shimon's ruling? **When** these trees **draw** their **nutrients, they draw** their **nutrients from** the ground that the consecrator had retained for himself that still **belongs to him,** not from consecrated property.

Why does *hekdesh* receive the trees? The Gemara explains: since the tree is drawing its nutrients from the field of *hekdesh*, one cannot maintain private ownership of a tree that grows from sanctified land without incurring inappropriate benefit from *hekdesh*. Therefore, once the field is given to *hekdesh*, the tree follows.

The challenge to Rav Huna is now apparent: if retaining a tree means retaining the underlying land, then when the owner sanctifies the field but *retains* the tree for himself, he should retain rights to the ground beneath it — and if he retains the ground, there would be no problem of drawing from *hekdesh* land. Yet Rabbi Shimon rules that the tree *does* become *kodesh*, implying that retaining the tree does not preserve the underlying land.

### Rav Huna Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר – כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וְרַב הוּנָא דְּאָמַר – כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The assumption that Rav Huna's statement is true according to everyone must be reconsidered. Rather, **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that one who consecrates his field does not retain for himself the land around the trees, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of his teacher, **Rabbi Akiva.** According to Rabbi Akiva, one who sells, sells generously, and there is no presumption that he retained some item or right for himself unless this was stated explicitly. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon rules that one who consecrates his field has also consecrated the carob trees, as otherwise they would draw nutrients from consecrated land. **And Rav Huna, who says** that when a seller retains trees for himself he also retains the land around them, holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who say that one who sells, sells sparingly.

The Gemara resolves this by limiting Rav Huna's statement: it applies only according to the Rabbanan, who hold *be'ayin ra'ah*, and therefore when one retains a tree one retains the ground beneath. But it does not apply according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds *be'ayin yafah*. Since Rabbi Shimon follows Rabbi Akiva's position — under which even retaining the tree does not preserve the ground — the field passes entirely to *hekdesh*, and the tree, rooted in *hekdesh* land, necessarily becomes *kodesh* as well.

## R. Shimon Follow R. Akiva

### Contradiction Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבָּנַן?! פְּשִׁיטָא! נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ – דְּאִי נָפְלִי, הָדַר שָׁתֵיל לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If Rav Huna's statement is only **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** then isn't his statement **obvious?** What novel idea is he adding? The Gemara answers: The practical **difference** is **that** while one might have thought that the prior owner retains a right to the land only for the sake of trees that were there, this is not the case. Rather, he retains absolute ownership of the land, and therefore, **if** the trees **fall** or die **he can plant them again.**

At this point the Gemara asks a sharp question: how can one say that Rabbi Shimon follows Rabbi Akiva? Rabbi Akiva was Rabbi Shimon's own *rebbi* — stam "Rabbi Shimon" in the Talmud refers to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, one of the *talmidei Rabbi Akiva*. For a student to follow his teacher is unremarkable and requires no comment. But the Gemara suggests this attribution is actually problematic, and introduces a *baraita* to press the point.

### Baraita on Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: הִקְדִּישׁ שְׁלֹשָׁה אִילָנוֹת מִמַּטַּע עֲשָׂרָה לְבֵית סְאָה – הֲרֵי הִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַקַּרְקַע וְאֶת הָאִילָנוֹת שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶם; לְפִיכָךְ כְּשֶׁהוּא פּוֹדֶה – פּוֹדֶה בֵּית זֶרַע חוֹמֶר שְׂעוֹרִים, בַּחֲמִשִּׁים שֶׁקֶל כָּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But can you establish that** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** that one who sells or consecrates property does so generously? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If **one consecrates three trees** in a field **where ten** trees are **planted in an area required for sowing one *se'a* of seed [*beit se'a*], he has consecrated** not only those trees, but also **the land and the** young **trees between them? Therefore,** if this is an ancestral field of his, **when he redeems** them, **he redeems** the land and everything contained within it according to the standard rate established by the Torah, whereby **an area** fit **for the sowing of a *ḥomer*,** i.e., a *kor*, of **barley seed** is redeemed **for fifty silver shekels.**

The *baraita* states: if one sanctifies three trees that are distributed at the density of ten trees per *beis se'ah* — *hikdish shalosh ilanos mimatu asura l'beis se'ah* — then *hekdesh* receives not only the trees but the land between them and the smaller trees growing among them.

### Distribution Rules

The *beis se'ah* measures fifty *amah* by fifty *amah*, which is twenty-five hundred square *amos*. Ten trees properly distributed across a *beis se'ah* means each tree occupies a space of approximately two hundred and fifty square *amos*. This density is considered the ideal spacing: not so crowded that trees must be uprooted, and not so dispersed that they cannot be regarded as constituting a coherent grove. Three trees that are spaced at this density — meaning each occupies roughly two hundred and fifty square *amos* — together constitute what is treated as a field of trees, and the land between them is implicitly included in the sanctification.

The mention of smaller trees among them (*ilanot shebeineihem*) raises an apparent difficulty: if there are additional trees, does that not disrupt the proper distribution? Tosafot resolve this by explaining that the additional trees are non-fruit-bearing (*ilanot srak*), and since they do not produce fruit they are considered destined for uprooting and are therefore not counted when assessing the spacing of the significant trees.

The upshot is that when three trees of the proper distribution are sanctified, *hekdesh* receives the trees, the land between them, and any small trees growing in between.

### Improper Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן אוֹ יוֹתֵר עַל כֵּן, אוֹ שֶׁהִקְדִּישָׁן בָּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא הִקְדִּישׁ לֹא הַקַּרְקַע, וְלֹא אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶם. לְפִיכָךְ, כְּשֶׁהוּא פּוֹדֶה – פּוֹדֶה אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת בְּשׇׁוְויֵהֶן. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ הִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת, וְחָזַר וְהִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַקַּרְקַע; כְּשֶׁהוּא פּוֹדֶה – פּוֹדֶה אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת בְּשׇׁוְויֵהֶן, וְחוֹזֵר וּפוֹדֶה בֵּית זֶרַע חוֹמֶר שְׂעוֹרִים בַּחֲמִשִּׁים שֶׁקֶל כָּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If the ratio of land to trees was **less than this,** and the trees were planted more densely, **or** if the ratio of land to trees was **more than this,** and the trees were planted less densely, **or if he consecrated** each of the trees separately, **one after the other, this** person **has consecrated neither the land nor the** young **trees between them. Therefore, when he redeems** them, **he redeems the trees in accordance with their worth. And moreover, even if one consecrates the trees** where they are planted more densely, less densely, or one after the other, **and then** afterward **he consecrates the land,** so that everything belongs to the Temple treasury, **when he redeems** them, **he redeems the trees** separately **in accordance with their worth, and then he redeems** the land according to the standard rate, where **an area** fit **for the sowing of a *ḥomer*** of **barley seed** is redeemed **for fifty silver shekels.**

If, however, the trees are too condensed or too dispersed — so that they do not fall within the proper density — the field does not accompany them. Similarly, if one sanctified the trees individually, one by one rather than as a unit of three, the land does not transfer. In both such cases, *harei zeh lo hikdish lo et hakarka v'lo et ha'ilanot shebeineihem*: *hekdesh* receives only the three trees themselves, not the land and not the smaller trees between them.

## Redemption Values

### Fixed vs Market

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא אָמַר: מוֹכֵר בְּעַיִן יָפָה מוֹכֵר; וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן מַקְדִּישׁ! אִי רַבָּנַן, הָא אָמְרִי: מוֹכֵר הוּא דִּבְעַיִן רָעָה מוֹכֵר, אֲבָל מַקְדִּישׁ – בְּעַיִן יָפָה מַקְדִּישׁ! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Whose** opinion is expressed in the *baraita*? **If** it is the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, doesn't he say** that **one who sells, sells generously, and all the more so one who consecrates** does so generously? This being the case, even if the trees were consecrated one after the other, some of the land should be consecrated along with them. And **if** the *baraita* reflects the opinion of **the Rabbis, don't they say** in the mishna that **it is** specifically **the seller who sells sparingly, but one who consecrates, consecrates generously,** and therefore the pit and the winepress are consecrated along with the field? Therefore, the *halakha* stated here is not in accordance with their opinion either. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** in the mishna here, that even one who consecrates a field does not consecrate the cistern and the winepress along with it.

The *baraita* continues to address what happens when one comes to redeem the sanctified property. Here the key distinction is between the fixed Torah rate and market rate.

When a *sdeh achuzah* — an ancestral field — is sanctified as a single unit, the Torah sets a fixed redemption price: fifty *shekel kesef* per *chomer beis se'orim* for a field sanctified at the outset of a *yovel* cycle, prorated for the years remaining to *yovel*. This fixed rate applies when the field and its contents are treated as one unit.

When the trees and field are sanctified as separate units — whether because the trees were too condensed or dispersed, or because they were sanctified individually — then redemption proceeds separately: the trees are redeemed at their market value, and the field, if later sanctified independently, is redeemed at the Torah's fixed rate. The *baraita* further adds: even if one first sanctified the trees individually and then went back and sanctified the field — *hikdish et ha'ilanot v'chazar v'hikdish et hakarka* — they remain two conceptually distinct items. The trees are redeemed at market price and the field at the Torah's fixed rate. This is the *chiddush*: even though physically the setup might be identical to a properly sanctified grove, the sequence of sanctification determines the conceptual units, and two separate acts of *hekdesh* produce two separate redemptions.

One might ask: when the whole is redeemed as a unit under the fixed rate, does one pay anything extra for the trees? No — the Torah's fixed rate applies to the field as a whole, and the trees are subsumed within it. It is only when the trees are redeemed separately that their market value is assessed independently, potentially adding to the total cost. Whether the combined total of fixed rate plus market rate is more or less than the fixed rate alone depends on the quality of the land and the condition of the market.

### Who Is the Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – אַלִּיבָּא דְמַאן? אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא אָמַר: מוֹכֵר בְּעַיִן יָפָה מוֹכֵר; וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן מַקְדִּישׁ! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּנַן, וְקָא סָבַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמוֹכֵר בְּעַיִן רָעָה מוֹכֵר, מַקְדִּישׁ נָמֵי בְּעַיִן רָעָה מַקְדִּישׁ – וּמְשַׁיַּיר אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of whom? If** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, doesn't he say** that **one who sells, sells generously, and all the more so one who consecrates** does so generously? **Rather,** it is **obvious** that his opinion is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Akiva and say that one who sells, sells sparingly. **And Rabbi Shimon** disagrees with the Rabbis insofar as **he** himself **holds** that **just as one who sells, sells sparingly, one who consecrates also consecrates sparingly and retains the land** around the trees for himself.

The Gemara now asks: whose position does this *baraita* represent? If it is Rabbi Akiva — *hamocher be'ayin yafah mocher, vechol shekein makdish* — then when one sanctifies even a single tree, one would expect some portion of the underlying land to accompany it, since even in a sale, generosity requires giving the buyer access to what he needs. Yet the *baraita*, at least on the Rashbam's reading, seems to assume that when trees are sanctified individually, no land whatsoever accompanies them. That cannot be Rabbi Akiva.

If it is the Rabbanan, a different difficulty arises. The Rabbanan hold that while a seller acts with a stingy eye — *mocher be'ayin ra'ah* — one who gives a *matanah* or who is *makdish* acts with a generous eye. The Rabbanan in the *mishnah* say explicitly that *makdish be'ayin yafah makdish* — *hekdesh* receives everything. But this *baraita* treats *hekdesh* as receiving nothing beyond the trees when they are sanctified individually. That also cannot fit the standard Rabbanan position.

The Gemara concludes: *ela peshita Rabbi Shimon hi* — it must be Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon holds that *makdish* is like *mocher*, not like *matanah* — *hekdesh* receives only what is explicitly given. Now the secondary question arises: Rabbi Shimon's *mocher* — is it *be'ayin yafah* like Rabbi Akiva, or *be'ayin ra'ah* like the Rabbanan? If it were *be'ayin yafah*, then even *hekdesh* would receive some land together with the tree. Since the *baraita* assumes no land accompanies an individually sanctified tree, it must be that this is Rabbi Shimon following the Rabbanan's stingy model: *makdish* is like *mocher*, and *mocher* is *be'ayin ra'ah*.

## R. Shimon Stingy

### Proof R. Shimon

Having established that the *baraita* follows Rabbi Shimon operating *aliba de'Rabbanan* — with the stingy eye — the Gemara now draws the implications. According to this model, when one gives *hekdesh* a tree, one gives only the tree: no land accompanies it. Equivalently, when one retains a tree for oneself and gives *hekdesh* the field, one certainly retains the ground beneath the tree, since on the stingy model one never cedes more than explicitly given.

### Contradiction Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא ״הוֹאִיל וְיוֹנְקִין מִשְּׂדֵה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes stating the difficulty: **But** this is **difficult,** as Rabbi Shimon himself said that the grafted carob tree and the sycamore trunk are consecrated along with the field **since they draw** their **nutrients from a consecrated field,** indicating that one who consecrates acts generously and does not retain the land around the trees for himself.

This creates a new problem. If Rabbi Shimon holds that one retains the land beneath a retained tree — consistent with Rav Huna's principle as applied to the Rabbanan — then when one sanctifies the field and retains the tree, the ground under the tree also remains in private hands. But then why does the Mishnah's Rabbi Shimon say that the tree becomes *kodesh*? The Gemara's earlier explanation was that the tree becomes *kodesh* because it draws nutrients from *hekdesh* land — *yonkim mis'deh hekdesh*. But if the land under the tree still belongs to the owner, the tree is not drawing from *hekdesh* land at all. The problem has apparently come full circle.

There are two distinct sub-questions here that must be kept separate. The first is whether *hekdesh* operates like *matanah* (generous) or like *mecher* (limited in scope). The second is, within the framework of *mecher*, whether it operates *be'ayin yafah* like Rabbi Akiva or *be'ayin ra'ah* like the Rabbanan. Rabbi Shimon says *hekdesh* is like *mecher*, not *matanah* — that is his distinctive position. But within *mecher* we have now shown he must follow the Rabbanan's stingy model. The tension is: if even in that stingy model retaining the tree preserves the land, why does Rabbi Shimon's Mishnah allow the tree to become *kodesh*?

### R. Shimon Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – לְדִבְרֵיהֶם דְּרַבָּנַן קָאָמַר לְהוּ: לְדִידִי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמוֹכֵר בְּעַיִן רָעָה מוֹכֵר, מַקְדִּישׁ נָמֵי בְּעַיִן רָעָה מַקְדִּישׁ – וְשַׁיּוֹרֵי מְשַׁיַּיר; לְדִידְכוּ, אוֹדוּ לִי מִיהָא דְּלֹא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶלָּא חָרוּב הַמּוּרְכָּב וְסַדַּן הַשִּׁקְמָה! וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather,** it must be understood that when **Rabbi Shimon** stated his ruling in the mishna, he was not expressing his own opinion. Rather, **he was speaking to the Rabbis in accordance with their statement,** and he meant to say: **According to my** opinion, **just as one who sells, sells sparingly, so too, one who consecrates, consecrates sparingly and retains** for himself land to nurture the trees. Therefore, when one consecrates a field, even the sycamore and carob tree are not consecrated along with it. But **according to your** opinion, that one who consecrates does so generously, **agree with me at least that** one who consecrates a field **has consecrated only the grafted carob and the sycamore trunk,** because they draw nutrients from consecrated ground, but he has not consecrated the other items that are not integral parts of the field. **And the Rabbis said to him: There is no difference** between the two in this regard. Since one who consecrates an item does so generously, everything found in the field is consecrated.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty with a crucial distinction: *Rabbi Shimon l'divreihem de'Rabbanan ka-amar lehu* — Rabbi Shimon in the Mishnah is not stating his own position when he gives the reason about drawing nutrients from *hekdesh* land. He is arguing *within the logic of the Rabbanan*.

Rabbi Shimon's own view is as follows: *l'didi* — as far as I am concerned — just as selling is stingy, *hekdesh* is also stingy. Therefore, when you sanctify the field, you retain rights to the land under the tree, and since you retain the land, the tree remains in your private domain and does not become *kodesh* at all. That is Rabbi Shimon's genuine position: even the tree would not transfer to *hekdesh*.

But then why does the Mishnah present Rabbi Shimon as saying the tree *does* become *kodesh*? Because Rabbi Shimon is addressing the Rabbanan on their own terms: *l'didchu* — according to you, who say that *hekdesh* and *matanah* are generous, you would say that even the well and the pigeon coop transfer to *hekdesh*. Concede to me at least — *odu li mi'at* — that the specifically named items like the *charuva hamurkav* and *sadan hashikma* do not transfer, because those are so distinct as to constitute separate plots of land. And he is willing, in arguing with the Rabbanan, to say that on their generous model the tree would become *kodesh* because of the logic of drawing from *hekdesh* land.

The Rabbanan respond: *lo shna* — we draw no distinction. On our generous model, *hekdesh* receives everything — just as a full *matanah* would include everything in the field.

The ultimate implication for Rav Huna's principle is thus preserved: according to the Rabbanan's model applied to *mecher*, retaining a tree retains the land beneath it. Rabbi Shimon's own view affirms the same. What the Mishnah presents as Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is his dialectical engagement with the Rabbanan's framework, not his personal holding.

## Redemption vs Sanctify

### Two-Stage Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לַהּ – כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ הִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת וְחָזַר וְהִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַקַּרְקַע, כְּשֶׁהוּא פּוֹדֶה – פּוֹדֶה אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת בְּשׇׁוְיֵיהֶן, וְחוֹזֵר וּפוֹדֶה בֵּית זֶרַע חוֹמֶר שְׂעוֹרִים בַּחֲמִשִּׁים שֶׁקֶל כָּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the *baraita* that it had concluded was taught according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, and asks: **In accordance with which opinion did you interpret** the *baraita* discussing consecrated property? It was interpreted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** But **say the last clause: And moreover, even if one consecrates the trees and then** afterward **he consecrates the land, when he redeems** them, **he redeems the trees** separately **in accordance with their worth, and then he redeems** the land according to the standard rate, where **an area** fit **for the sowing of a *ḥomer*** of **barley seed** is redeemed **for fifty silver shekels.**

A further difficulty now emerges against the identification of the *baraita* as Rabbi Shimon. The *baraita* treats the two-stage sanctification — trees first, field second — as producing two separate redemptions, each governed by its own valuation method. But there is a well-known principle associated with Rabbi Shimon: *batar pidyon azil* — he follows the time of redemption, not the time of sanctification. If Rabbi Shimon assesses the status of sanctified property at the moment of *pidyon*, then even when trees and field were sanctified at different times, at the moment of redemption one faces a unified field with trees. That should be treated as one unit and redeemed at the single fixed Torah rate, not as two separate units with two separate redemption schemes.

### Baraita Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה מֵאָבִיו וְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָבִיו – מִנַּיִן שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִם אֶת שְׂדֵה מִקְנָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר לֹא מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחֻזָּתוֹ״ – שָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵין רְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, יָצְתָה זוֹ – שֶׁרְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that **with regard to one who purchases a field from his father and consecrates it, and afterward his father dies,** so the field would now be considered his as an inheritance, **from where** is it derived **that** with regard to its redemption **it should be** considered **before him as an ancestral field** and not a field that he purchased? **The verse states** about a field that was purchased: **"And if** he sanctifies to the Lord **a field that he has bought, which is not of his ancestral fields"** (Leviticus 27:22). The verse speaks specifically of **a field that is not fit** at the time of its consecration **to be an ancestral field,** meaning that he never could have inherited it in the future. This specification **excludes this** field **that was fit to be an ancestral field** from this *halakha*, since eventually it would have become his through inheritance, even had he not purchased it. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon.**

To demonstrate that Rabbi Shimon indeed follows the time of redemption, the Gemara cites another *baraita*. The case: Reuven purchases a field from his father Yaakov. He then sanctifies it. After the sanctification, Yaakov dies.

At the time Reuven sanctified the field, it was a *sdeh miknah* — a purchased field. It was not his ancestral field. Had Yaakov been alive when *yovel* arrived, the field would have reverted to Yaakov, since Reuven's purchase only granted him rights for the duration until *yovel*. In technical terms, Reuven held a *kinyan peiros* — usufruct rights — for those years, not full title. One who sanctifies a *sdeh miknah* redeems it at market rate, not at the Torah's fixed rate. Yet after Yaakov dies, Reuven inherits the field — had he not already purchased it, it would have come to him as a *sdeh achuzah*.

### R. Yehuda R. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה מֵאָבִיו, וּמֵת אָבִיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישׁ, מִנַּיִן שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִם אֶת שְׂדֵה מִקְנָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר לֹא מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחֻזָּתוֹ״ – שָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, יָצְתָה זוֹ – שֶׁהִיא שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Meir** learns a different *halakha* from this verse, and he **says: From where** is it derived **that** in the case of **one who purchases a field from his father, and his father dies, and afterward he consecrates** the field, **from where** is it derived **that it should be** considered **before him like an ancestral field? The verse states: "And if** he sanctifies to the Lord **a field that he has bought, which is not of his ancestral fields."** The verse refers specifically to **a field that is not** now **an ancestral field** at the time of its consecration. This specification **excludes this** field, **as** after the death of the father, **it is an ancestral field.**

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon rule: since at the time Reuven comes to redeem the field, Yaakov has already died, we treat the field as a *sdeh achuzah* — he redeems it at the Torah's fixed rate, not at market rate. The reasoning rests on the verse: *v'im et sdeh miknato asher lo mi-sdeh achuzato* — "and if it is from his purchased field, which is not from his ancestral field." The phrase *mi-sdeh achuzato* implies a field that does not even belong to the category of things that could be ancestral fields. But this field *could* have been his *sdeh achuzah* — had it not been purchased, at the moment of Yaakov's death it would have been inherited. Therefore it is excluded from the *sdeh miknah* category and treated as a *sdeh achuzah* for redemption purposes.

This is a striking ruling. Even though at the moment of sanctification the field was unambiguously a purchased field — indeed, one that would have returned to Yaakov at *yovel* had Yaakov lived — Rabbi Shimon says that because at the time of *pidyon* the conditions now resemble what would have made it a *sdeh achuzah*, we assess it accordingly.

### Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הֵיכָא דְּמֵת אָבִיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁהּ – לָא צְרִיכִי קְרָא; כִּי אִצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא – הֵיכָא דְּהִקְדִּישָׁהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **But** according to **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, a verse is not required** to teach that, in a case **where his father dies and afterward he consecrates** the field, it is considered to be an ancestral field, as this is obvious. **A verse is required** only to teach the *halakha* in a case **where he consecrates** the field after having bought it, **and afterward his father dies.**

Rabbi Meir accepts a version of this logic but draws the line differently. He agrees that if the sequence was: (1) Reuven purchases, (2) Yaakov dies, (3) Reuven sanctifies — then the field is treated as a *sdeh achuzah*, because at the moment of sanctification it genuinely *was* a *sdeh achuzah*: Yaakov had already died, Reuven had already inherited full title, and the field's status had truly changed before the act of *hekdesh*. This, Rabbi Meir says, is not a great *chiddush* — it is simply recognizing that the field's status changed before sanctification.

What Rabbi Meir refuses to accept is the reverse order: (1) purchase, (2) sanctification while Yaakov is still alive, (3) Yaakov's death. In that case, at the moment of sanctification it was a *sdeh miknah*, and Rabbi Meir holds that we assess the field based on its status at the time of *hekdesh*, not at the time of *pidyon*. The subsequent death of the father does not retroactively transform the character of the sanctification.

For Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, the case of father-dies-then-sanctifies requires no verse — it is obvious. The verse is needed for the harder case, where the sanctification preceded the father's death, and yet we still treat the redemption as that of a *sdeh achuzah*.

### Textual Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא לְהוּ? אִי מֵהַאי קְרָא, אֵימָא לְכִדְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הוּא דַּאֲתָא! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָזְלִי בָּתַר פִּדְיוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do** Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon arrive at this conclusion? **If** they derive it only **from this verse,** you can **say that** the verse **came** to be interpreted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** as opposed to the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, as there is no clear proof from the verse to support either opinion. **Rather, is it not due to** the fact **that they follow** the time of the **redemption,** and at the time of the redemption the father is dead, and the field is the son's ancestral field that is currently in the possession of the Temple treasury?

The Gemara probes further: if Rabbi Shimon simply holds as a general principle that we always assess things at the time of *pidyon*, then he did not need a specific verse — his principle would already yield this result. The fact that a verse is required suggests something more limited is happening. The Gemara concludes: in truth, one could have read the verse like Rabbi Meir. What forced Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon into their reading was the specific formulation *asher lo mi-sdeh achuzato* — "which is *not from* his ancestral field." The plain reading would be: a purchased field that simply is not an inherited field. But the phrase *mi-sdeh achuzato* — "not *from* his ancestral field" — implies a field that has no connection whatsoever to the ancestral-field category. This field does have such a connection: it came from the father's holdings and, at the time of *pidyon*, would have been inherited. Therefore it cannot be characterized as a field entirely outside the ancestral-field universe, and it is treated as a *sdeh achuzah* for redemption.

## Rav Nachman Clarifies

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לְעוֹלָם בְּעָלְמָא – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא אָזְלִי בָּתַר פִּדְיוֹן; וְהָכָא, קְרָא אַשְׁכַּחוּ וּדְרוּשׁ – אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״וְאִם אֶת שְׂדֵה מִקְנָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר לֹא אֲחוּזָּתוֹ״, אִי נָמֵי ״שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּתוֹ״, מַאי ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחֻזָּתוֹ״? אֶת שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, יָצְתָה זוֹ – שֶׁרְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: Actually,** I can say to you that **generally** speaking, **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon do not follow** the time of the **redemption,** and therefore their ruling here is not based on this premise. **But here they found a verse and interpreted it,** as, if the verse is to be understood as it was explained by Rabbi Meir, then **let the verse write: And if** he sanctifies to the Lord **a field that he has bought, which is not his ancestral estate, or** let it write: Which is not **his ancestral field. What** is meant by the expression: **"Which is not of his ancestral fields"** (Leviticus 27:22)? It means that **a field that is not fit to** ever **be an ancestral field** is considered a purchased field. That **excludes this** field, **as it is fit to be an ancestral field.** Based on this explanation, the *baraita* that addresses one who consecrates trees can, in fact, be understood to be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak now intervenes to sharpen the point. In general, he states, Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon do *not* follow the time of *pidyon* as a standing methodological principle — *l'olam b'alma Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabbi Shimon lo azli batar pidyon*. Their ordinary orientation is to assess sanctified property based on the moment of *hekdesh*. Accordingly, in the *baraita*'s case of two-stage sanctification, they would indeed treat trees and field as two separate units and require two separate redemptions, since that is how the property entered *hekdesh*.

### Text Compels

The exception in the ancestral-field case is not a general principle but a textually compelled result. *V'hacha kra ashkach v'daresh* — here they found a verse and expounded it. The phrase *asher lo mi-sdeh achuzato* carries the specific implication that we are excluding only a *sdeh miknah* with no connection to the ancestral-field category — thereby including a field that, though purchased, came from a father's holdings and would have been inherited. This is a local textual derivation, not a reflection of a broader commitment to assessing things at the time of *pidyon*.

The Gemara reinforces this: had the Torah simply written *v'im mikanato asher lo achuzato* — without the *mem* introducing *mi-sdeh* — Rabbi Meir's reading would have been natural and unforced. It was the additional *mem* — *mi-sdeh achuzato*, "not from the world of ancestral fields" — that pressed Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon into their alternative reading. Their general inclination remains to follow the time of *hekdesh*, and it is only this specific textual pressure that leads them to classify the field as a *sdeh achuzah* at the time of *pidyon*. One might ask whether this makes the ruling somewhat circular — since the father's having died after the sanctification is a future event that we are reading back into the field's character at the time of sanctification. The Gemara acknowledges this subtlety but maintains that the textual logic of *mi-sdeh achuzato* carries the force, not a general retroactive principle.

## Dual Status

### Carob Sycamore

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חָרוּב הַמּוּרְכָּב וְסַדַּן הַשִּׁקְמָה – תּוֹרַת אִילָן עָלָיו, וְתוֹרַת קַרְקַע עָלָיו. תּוֹרַת אִילָן עָלָיו, דְּהֵיכָא דְּאַקְדֵּישׁ אוֹ זַבֵּין שְׁנֵי אִילָנוֹת וְהַאי – יֵשׁ לוֹ קַרְקַע. תּוֹרַת קַרְקַע עָלָיו, דְּלָא מִזְדַּבַּן אַגַּב אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says: A grafted carob and a sycamore trunk have** both **the status of a tree and the status of land.** Each of these **has the status of a tree, so if one consecrates or buys two trees and this** carob or sycamore, **he has** also consecrated or bought **the land** between them, as the sycamore or carob joins with the other two trees to form a unit of three trees that take their land with them. And each **has the status of land, as it is not sold along with land,** as explained in the mishna, that one who sells a field has not sold a grafted carob or a sycamore trunk that is in the field.

Having concluded the extended analysis of *hekdesh*, trees, and land, the Gemara now turns to a related but distinct set of rulings from Rav Huna concerning objects that carry dual legal status simultaneously.

Rav Huna states: *charov u-murkav de-shikma torat ilan leh ve-torat karka leh* — the grafted carob tree and the sycamore stump possess the legal character of a tree and also the legal character of land. We have already established in the *mishnah* that when one sells a field with everything in it, the sycamore and carob are not included — they are treated not as trees within the field but as independent plots, a separate *karka*. That is their *torat karka*.

But they also carry *torat ilan*: when one sells or sanctifies three trees and one of the three is a carob or sycamore, it counts as one of the three trees for purposes of the rule that three properly distributed trees bring the underlying land along with them. In that context it is treated fully as a tree.

### Large Sheaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: עוֹמֶר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סָאתַיִם – תּוֹרַת עוֹמֶר עָלָיו וְתוֹרַת גָּדִישׁ עָלָיו. תּוֹרַת עוֹמֶר עָלָיו, דִּשְׁנֵי עוֹמָרִים – שִׁכְחָה, שְׁנַיִם וָהוּא – אֵינָן שִׁכְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Huna says** in a similar fashion: A large **sheaf** of grain **that contains two *se'a* has** both **the status of a sheaf and the status of a heap** with regard to the *halakhot* of forgotten sheaves that must be left for the poor. **It has the status of a sheaf, as** the principle is that **two sheaves** that were inadvertently **left in the field are** considered **forgotten** sheaves that must be left for the poor, whereas three sheaves need not be left for the poor, but rather the owner of the field may go back and take them for himself. In this regard a two-se'a sheaf is considered one sheaf, so if one forgot **two** sheaves **and** also **this** sheave that contains two *se'a*, the three together are three sheaves and **are not** considered **forgotten** sheaves that must be left for the poor.

Rav Huna adds a parallel ruling: *omer she-yesh bo sa-atayim torat omer leh ve-torat gadish leh* — a sheaf of wheat that contains two *se'ah* — an unusually large quantity — has simultaneously the legal character of a sheaf (*omer*) and the character of a pile (*gadish*). Ordinarily, a sheaf is a modest bundle; something containing two *se'ah* might seem to cross into the category of a pile, which is qualitatively different.

### Shikhchah Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹרַת גָּדִישׁ עָלָיו, דִּתְנַן: עוֹמֶר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סָאתַיִם, שְׁכָחוֹ – אֵין שִׁכְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** And **it has the status of a heap, as we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 6:6): In the case of **a sheaf that contains two *se'a*,** if **one forgets it** in a field, it is **not** considered **a forgotten** sheaf that must be left for the poor, as its size and importance grant it the status of a heap, rather than a sheaf.

The dual status becomes practically significant in the laws of *shikhchah* — forgotten produce. The Torah commands that if a farmer forgets a sheaf in the field while harvesting, he must leave it for the poor. However, this applies only to one or two forgotten sheaves; if three or more sheaves are forgotten, they are considered too substantial to be the kind of oversight the Torah addresses, and they are not *shikhchah*.

The large two-*se'ah* sheaf generates a ruling in both directions. As *torat gadish* — pile — if it stands alone forgotten in the field, it is not *shikhchah*, because a pile is never treated as forgotten produce. As *torat omer* — sheaf — if two ordinary sheaves are also forgotten alongside it, then together you have what is considered three sheaves, and none of the three constitute *shikhchah*. Had the large bundle been treated purely as a pile, it would not have combined with the two sheaves to create a count of three, and those two sheaves would each have been *shikhchah* independently. But because the large bundle also carries *torat omer*, it is counted as a third sheaf, which removes *shikhchah* status from the two ordinary sheaves as well.

The result is that this large sheaf protects against *shikhchah* in both directions: alone, it is a pile and not *shikhchah*; combined with two other sheaves, it functions as a sheaf and renders all three outside the scope of *shikhchah*.

## Rabbi Menachem

### Source Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: חָרוּב הַמּוּרְכָּב וְסַדַּן הַשִּׁקְמָה – בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בַּר יוֹסֵי וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Reish Lakish says:** With regard to whether **a grafted carob and a sycamore trunk** are consecrated along with a field that one has consecrated, **we have arrived at the dispute between Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei and the Rabbis,** as Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei rules that they are not consecrated, whereas the Rabbis rule that they are.

The Gemara closes the *perek* with a brief but pointed attribution. Rabba bar bar Chana says in the name of Reish Lakish: the debate about the grafted carob and the sycamore stump — whether they transfer when a field is sold or sanctified — comes to expression in the dispute between Rabbi Menachem berabbi Yossi and the Rabbanan.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא מַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּרַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let us say** that this is **the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis,** as it was concluded previously that according to Rabbi Shimon himself, even the carob and sycamore trees are not consecrated. The Gemara answers: Reish Lakish **teaches us this, that Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei, holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** and therefore Rabbi Shimon is not the only Sage who holds this opinion.

The Gemara immediately asks: why invoke Rabbi Menachem berabbi Yossi and the Rabbanan? Simply say this is the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbanan as recorded in our *mishnah*. The Gemara answers: *ka mashma lan de-Rabbi Menachem berabbi Yossi ke-Rabbi Shimon sevira leih* — the point is to convey that Reish Lakish possessed a tradition that Rabbi Menachem berabbi Yossi holds in accordance with Rabbi Shimon. The Rashbam notes that this must derive from a *mesorah* — an authoritative transmission — rather than from any explicit textual derivation. It is, admittedly, a somewhat unusual note on which to conclude a *perek*, but it serves to ground the anonymous ruling about carob and sycamore in a named tannaitic position.
