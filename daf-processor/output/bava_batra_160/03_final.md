# Bava Batra 160 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get Pashut Intro

### Context & Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּט פָּשׁוּט – עֵדָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ. מְקוּשָּׁר – עֵדָיו מֵאֲחוֹרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In **an ordinary document, its witnesses** are to sign **inside it,** i.e., on the written side of the paper. In a folded and **tied** document, **its witnesses** are to sign **on the back of it.**

We begin the new *perek*, *Get Pashut*, the last *perek* of *Bava Batra*, which is devoted to the writing of *shtarot*. The connection to the preceding material is that the earlier discussion of deathbed bequests touched on questions of whether the disposition was written in a *shtar* or made orally, and whether the document reflected that the person was ill or healthy at the time. That thematic thread now opens into a full *perek* dealing with how *shtarot* must be written.

### Pashut vs. Mekushar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּשׁוּט – שֶׁכָּתְבוּ עֵדָיו מֵאֲחוֹרָיו, מְקוּשָּׁר – שֶׁכָּתְבוּ עֵדָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ, שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִין. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מְקוּשָּׁר שֶׁכָּתְבוּ עֵדָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ – כָּשֵׁר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ פָּשׁוּט.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **an ordinary** document **whose witnesses wrote** their signatures **on the back of it,** and **a tied** document **whose witnesses wrote** their signatures **inside of it, both of these** are **not valid. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says: A tied** document **whose witnesses wrote** their signatures **inside of it** is **valid, because one can transform it into an ordinary** document by untying it.

The *perek* opens with the *Mishnah* on 160a: *get pashut edav mitocho* — a simple, flat document has its witnesses signing on the same side as the text itself, at the bottom of the written content. This is the format familiar from a standard *ketubah*. The term *pashut* is used here in contrast to *mekushar*. A *mekushar* is a bound document in which the text is written in sections, each of which is folded over and sewn shut. For a *mekushar*, the *Mishnah* states *edav meachorav* — the witnesses sign on the back side, side B of the document.

### Bar Kokhba & History

The *Gemara* will explain that the institution of the *get mekushar* was originally established for *kohanim* who wished to divorce their wives. Because *kohanim* could be impulsive and would sometimes write and deliver a *get* in a moment of anger — after which reconciliation was impossible, since the wife became forbidden to her *kohen* husband as a divorcée — the Rabbis instituted the *get mekushar* to slow down the process. Writing the document section by section, folding and sewing each fold, required time and a degree of expertise, giving the husband a chance to reconsider.

By the time of *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi, at the end of the *Mishnaic* period), the *get mekushar* was already becoming unfamiliar, as we will see later in the *Gemara*. By the time of the *Rishonim*, it was entirely out of practice, and its structure had to be reconstructed from the textual sources. Interestingly, actual *mekushar* documents were found among the Bar Kokhba cave documents — not specifically *gittin* for divorce, but ordinary legal documents. This suggests that in the ancient world, folding and sewing a document was a standard method of securing it against tampering.

### Mishnah: Placement Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּט פָּשׁוּט – עֵדָיו בִּשְׁנַיִם, וּמְקוּשָּׁר – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה. פָּשׁוּט שֶׁכָּתַב בּוֹ עֵד אֶחָד, וּמְקוּשָּׁר שֶׁכָּתַב בּוֹ שְׁנֵי עֵדִים – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **An ordinary document** is rendered valid **by** its having at least **two witnesses, and a tied** document is rendered valid **by** its having at least **three** witnesses. With regard to **an ordinary** document **in which a single witness wrote** his signature, **and a tied** document **in which** only **two witnesses wrote** their signatures, **they are both not valid.**

The *Mishnah* continues: *pashut she-katvu edav meachorav* — if one has a simple *pashut* document but placed the witnesses on the back as one would in a *mekushar*, or conversely, a *mekushar* in which the witnesses were placed inside alongside the text, *shehem pesulim* — both are invalid. The *Rashbam* explains that the invalidity follows from the fact that *Chachamim* established fixed parameters for each type of document, and failing to follow them renders the document invalid. *Tosafot* offers a different rationale: mixing the formats would distort the dating system, potentially creating what would be considered an antedated or postdated document — a *mukdam* or *me'uchar*. Other *Rishonim* offer additional explanations; the *Rashbam*'s position suffices as the working framework here.

### R. Chanina Leniency

Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel disagrees in one case: *mekushar she-katvu edav mitocho kasher mipnei she-yakhol la'asoto pashut* — a *mekushar* whose witnesses signed on the inside is still valid, because one can simply undo all the sewn folds and it becomes an ordinary flat document. Therefore it is not invalid, since it can be converted into a valid *pashut*. However, he does not extend this leniency to a *pashut* with witnesses on the back, because a *pashut* cannot easily be converted into a *mekushar* — and there are also concerns about the potential for forgery in such a configuration.

## Mekushar Structure

### Rashbam: Blank Lines

Before proceeding further, the *Gemara*'s discussion requires a clear picture of what a *mekushar* actually looked like. The *Rashbam* explains that one would write a line of text, leave a blank line beneath it, and then fold the written line down over the blank line, sewing that fold shut. Then one would move further down the document, write another line, leave another blank line beneath it, and fold and sew again. This produces an accordion-like structure of consecutive sealed folds — not a continuous fan from one end, but discrete separate folds, each consisting of a written line sewn against a blank one.

### Rashbam Problem

This model, however, runs into a difficulty from the *Mishnah* itself, specifically from Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel's ruling that a *mekushar* with internal witnesses is valid because it can simply be converted into a *pashut*. If the *Rashbam*'s model is correct, then undoing the folds would leave the document with a blank line between every written line — a document with blank lines interspersed throughout would be entirely unacceptable, as those blank lines could be filled in with fraudulent additions. Such a document could not serve as a valid *pashut*.

### Tosafot: No Blanks

*Tosafot* therefore rejects the *Rashbam*'s model and argues that the entire document was written continuously, with no blank lines. The accordion folds were created by folding written line one over written line two, then written line three over written line four, and so on — each fold containing two lines of actual text with nothing blank in between. When unfolded, the result would be a complete, continuously written document without gaps. *Tosafot* derives this understanding specifically from Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel's ruling, which only makes sense if undoing the folds would leave a usable, uninterrupted document.

## Scriptural Basis

### First Proof: Deut.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״שָׂדוֹת בַּכֶּסֶף יִקְנוּ, וְכָתוֹב בַּסֵּפֶר וְחָתוֹם, וְהָעֵד עֵדִים״. ״שָׂדוֹת בַּכֶּסֶף יִקְנוּ וְכָתוֹב בַּסֵּפֶר״ –
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? What biblical basis is there for the existence of these two types of documents? **Rabbi Ḥanina says: As the verse states: "They shall buy fields for money, and subscribe the deeds, and seal them, and call witnesses"** (Jeremiah 32:44). When the verse states: **"They shall buy fields for money, and subscribe the deeds,"**

The *Gemara* asks: *mina hanei milei* — from where do we derive all these laws? Rabbi Chanina cites the verse from Jeremiah's land purchase narrative: *sadot ba-kesef yiknu ve-chatuv ba-sefer ve-chatom ve-ha'ed edim* — "fields shall be purchased for money, and it shall be written in a document and sealed, and witnesses shall testify." This verse is cited in other contexts as the scriptural basis for the legal validity of a *shtar re'ayah* — a documentary attestation as opposed to a *shtar kinyan*, which effects a transaction such as marriage, divorce, or purchase. The question of where the Torah legitimizes written testimony rather than oral testimony before a court is addressed through this verse.

### Two Types Derived

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה פָּשׁוּט. ״וְחָתוֹם״ – זֶה מְקוּשָּׁר. ״וְהָעֵד״ – שְׁנַיִם, ״עֵדִים״ – שְׁלֹשָׁה. הָא כֵּיצַד? שְׁנַיִם לְפָשׁוּט, שְׁלֹשָׁה לִמְקוּשָּׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **this** is referring to **an ordinary** document. When the verse states: **"And seal them," this** is referring to **a tied** document. The next phrase, "and call witnesses [*veha'ed edim*]," which more literally would be translated: And have witnesses bear witness, is interpreted as follows: **"And have bear witness [*veha'ed*],"** this indicates the need for **two** witnesses, as the term "witness [*ed*]" in the Torah generally refers to two witnesses. As to the word **"witnesses [*edim*],"** this additional term indicates the need for **three** witnesses. **How so?** How can the verse call for both two witnesses and three witnesses? Rabbi Ḥanina explains: **Two** witnesses are required **for an ordinary** document, and **three** are required **for a tied** document.

The *Gemara* on 160b attempts to derive both types of documents from this single verse. *Ve-chatuv ba-sefer* — "written in a document" — *zeh pashut*, this refers to a simple, straightforward document written in the manner of a book, everything on one side of the page. *Ve-chatom* — "and seal it" — *zeh mekushar*, this refers to a document that is folded over and sewn shut, i.e., sealed. *Ve-ha'ed edim* — "and witnesses shall testify" — since *edim* is written in the plural, the minimum count is two; but then a second instance of the word *edim* teaches that there can be three. This gives us the distinction in the number of witnesses: two for a *pashut*, three for a *mekushar*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא! מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנִּתְרַבָּה בִּקְשָׁרָיו, נִתְרַבָּה בְּעֵדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But I** can just as well **reverse** it, requiring two witnesses for a tied document and three for an ordinary one. The Gemara answers: **Since** the tied document requires **more** to be done **with regard to its ties,** it stands to reason that it requires **more** to be done **with regard to its witnesses,** requiring three rather than two.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *ve-eifcha ana* — why not reverse the assignment and say two for the *mekushar* and three for the *pashut*? The answer is: *mitoch she-nitrabah bi-kshirah nitrabah be-adeha* — since the *mekushar* already bears greater demands in how it must be physically constructed, it stands to reason that it also bears the greater demand of requiring three witnesses rather than two.

### Second Proof: Jeremiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַפְרָם אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וָאֶקַּח אֶת סֵפֶר הַמִּקְנָה, אֶת הֶחָתוּם הַמִּצְוָה וְהַחֻקִּים, וְאֶת הַגָּלוּי״; ״וָאֶקַּח אֶת סֵפֶר הַמִּקְנָה״ – זֶה פָּשׁוּט. ״אֶת הֶחָתוּם״ – זֶה מְקוּשָּׁר. ״וְאֶת הַגָּלוּי״ – זֶה פָּשׁוּט שֶׁבַּמְקוּשָּׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rafram says** that there is a different source for two kinds of documents, **from here: "So I took the deed of the purchase, that which was sealed, the terms and conditions, and that which was open"** (Jeremiah 32:11). When the verse states: **"So I took the deed of the purchase," this** is referring to **an ordinary** document. When it states: **"That which was sealed," this** is referring to **a tied** document. When it states: **"And that which was open," this** is referring to the **ordinary,** unfolded part **of a tied** document.

Rafram cites a different verse, from Jeremiah's own account of executing the purchase he was commanded to perform: *va-ekach et sefer ha-miknah et ha-chatum et ha-mitzvah ve-ha-chukim ve-et ha-galui* — "I took the deed of purchase, the sealed one, and the laws and statutes, and the open one." Here, *sefer ha-miknah* — the deed of purchase — *zeh pashut*, that is the simple document; *et ha-chatum* — the sealed one — *zeh mekushar*, that is the sewn document.

### Pashut Shebim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַמִּצְוָה וְהַחֻקִּים״ – אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּין פָּשׁוּט לִמְקוּשָּׁר. הָא כֵּיצַד? זֶה עֵדָיו שְׁנַיִם, וְזֶה עֵדָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה; זֶה עֵדָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ, וְזֶה עֵדָיו מֵאֲחוֹרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Rafram continues: With regard to the phrase: **"The terms and conditions," these are the matters that** distinguish **an ordinary** document **from a tied** one. **How so?** What are the details that differentiate the two types of documents? **This** one, the ordinary document, has **two witnesses, and that** one, the tied document, has **three witnesses.** And in **this** one, the ordinary document, **its witnesses** are signed **inside it,** on the front side, while in **that** one, the tied document, **its witnesses** are signed **on the back of it.**

The verse then says *et ha-galui* — the open, explicit portion — and the *Gemara* identifies this as *zehu pashut she-bi-mekushar*, the flat portion of the *mekushar* document. This introduces an important concept: the *mekushar* consists of two distinct sections. The upper portion contains the *toref* — the essential particulars: the names of the parties, the amount, the date — written in folded and sewn sections. The lower portion, the *tofes*, contains the boilerplate language and is left flat and open, written in the ordinary manner. This lower flat portion is the *pashut she-bi-mekushar*. Finally, the verse adds *ha-mitzvah ve-ha-chukim* — the commandments and statutes — and the *Gemara* reads this as: *elu devarim she-bein pashut le-mekushar*, these are the detailed laws that distinguish between the two types of documents.

### Third Proof: Edim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים אוֹ עַל פִּי שְׁלֹשָׁה עֵדִים יָקוּם דָּבָר״ – אִם תִּתְקַיֵּים עֵדוּתָן בִּשְׁנַיִם, לָמָּה פָּרַט לָךְ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה? לוֹמַר לָךְ: שְׁנַיִם לְפָשׁוּט, שְׁלֹשָׁה לִמְקוּשָּׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Yeḥezkel said** that there is a different source for two sets of *halakhot* for two types of documents **from here: "At the mouth of two witnesses or at the mouth of three witnesses shall a matter be established"** (Deuteronomy 19:15). **If** witnesses' **testimony is established with two** witnesses, **why** did the verse **specify for you** that it is also established **with three,** which is self-evident? Rather, this verse serves **to tell you** that there is a requirement for **two** witnesses **for an ordinary** document, and a requirement for **three** witnesses **for a tied** document.

Rami bar Yechezkel cites yet a third source: *al pi shnayim edim o al pi shlosha edim yakum davar* — "by the testimony of two witnesses or by the testimony of three witnesses shall a matter be established." The *Gemara* asks: if two suffice to establish testimony, why does the Torah add the possibility of three? *Le-omar lecha shnayim la-pashut shlosha la-mekushar* — to teach you that while two witnesses suffice for a *pashut*, a *mekushar* requires three. Again the reversal is challenged and answered with the same logic: the greater demands of the *mekushar* in its physical construction correlate with its greater demand for witnesses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא! מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנִּתְרַבָּה בִּקְשָׁרָיו, נִתְרַבָּה בְּעֵדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But I** can just as well **reverse** it, requiring two witnesses for a tied document and three for an ordinary one. The Gemara answers: **Since** the tied document requires **more** to be done **with regard to its ties,** it stands to reason that it requires **more** to be done **with regard to its witnesses,** requiring three rather than two.

## Asmachta Disclaimer

### Rejection of Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי לְהָכִי הוּא דְּאָתוּ? כׇּל חַד וְחַד לְמִילְּתֵיהּ הוּא דַּאֲתָא – לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״שָׂדוֹת בַּכֶּסֶף יִקְנוּ, וְכָתוֹב בַּסֵּפֶר וְחָתוֹם״ – עֵצָה טוֹבָה קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. ״וָאֶקַּח אֶת סֵפֶר הַמִּקְנָה״ – הָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה. ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים אוֹ עַל פִּי שְׁלֹשָׁה עֵדִים״ – לְהַקִּישׁ שְׁלֹשָׁה לִשְׁנַיִם, בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְרַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that **these** verses **are coming for this** purpose, to teach that there are two types of documents? But **each and every one** of them **comes for its** own **purpose.** The first verse comes **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"They shall buy fields for money, and subscribe the deeds, and seal them,** and call witnesses" (Jeremiah 32:44), it is merely to **teach us good advice,** that people should carefully document their purchases in order to provide permanent proof of purchase. When the verse states: **"So I took the deed of the purchase"** (Jeremiah 32:11), **this was** merely how that **incident** occurred, and the phrase is not intended to teach any *halakhot*. When the verse states: **"At the mouth of two witnesses or at the mouth of three witnesses** shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15), this is stated in order **to juxtapose three** witnesses **with two** witnesses for several reasons, as delineated **in the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis** (*Makkot* 5b).

The *Gemara* then decisively rejects all three derivations: *ve-hanei le-hachi hu de-atu* — are these verses really coming to teach us about *pashut* and *mekushar*? No — *kol chad ve-chad le-miletei hu de-ata* — each verse comes to teach something entirely its own.

The first verse — *sadot ba-kesef yiknu ve-chatuv ba-sefer ve-chatom* — is *etzah tovah ka-mashma lan*: it is simply giving good advice. God commanded Jeremiah that, as an act of faith in Israel's return from exile, he should purchase a plot of land, write it in a document, seal it, and place it in a pottery vessel so that it should endure for many years. The verse is practical guidance for that particular act, not a source for legal categories.

The second verse — *va-ekach et sefer ha-miknah* — is *hachi havai ma'aseh*: it is simply narrating the story, reporting that Jeremiah did what he was commanded. The apparent superfluity of all the enumerated details does not carry the legal weight the *Gemara* had tried to assign to it. The *Gemara* acknowledges, in effect, that under ordinary interpretive principles one might press those extra words for legal content, but here it knows that the laws of *pashut* and *mekushar* do not actually derive from these verses.

The third verse — *al pi shnayim edim o al pi shlosha edim* — comes *le-hakish shlosha li-shnayim*, to equate a group of three witnesses with a group of two, establishing that even three witnesses constitute a single *kat* — a single testimonial unit. The practical implications of this equation are debated by Rabbi Akiva and the *Rabanan*: for instance, if three witnesses are subjected to *edim zomemim* proceedings, all three must be found conspiratorial — it does not suffice to disqualify only two of them. Similarly, if one of three is found to be *pasul*, the entire group is disqualified, rather than relying on the remaining two. These are distinct *halachot* discussed elsewhere in the *Gemara*, and the verse is fully occupied with them. It has nothing to do with *pashut* and *mekushar*.

### Kohanim Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְקוּשָּׁר מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָאֵי אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather,** the entire institution of the **tied** document is **rabbinic** in origin, **and** all these **verses** that were cited above by various *amora'im* were intended as **mere support** for the concept of a tied document, as opposed to actual sources.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *mekushar mi-derabanan* — the entire institution of the folded, sewn document is rabbinic in origin — *u-kerai asmachta be-alma* — and the verses cited above are mere *asmachta*, supports or allusions, not genuine derivations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן מְקוּשָּׁר? אַתְרָא דְכָהֲנֵי הֲווֹ, וַהֲווֹ קָפְדִי טוּבָא וּמְגָרְשִׁי נָשַׁיְיהוּ; וְעָבְדִי רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא, אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי מִיַּתְּבָא דַּעְתַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **the Sages instituted** the **tied** document? The Gemara explains: There was **a place where there were** many **priests, and they were very quick tempered, and they would** seek to **divorce their wives** impetuously. The *halakha* is that a priest may not marry a divorcée, even his own ex-wife. These priests, who acted impetuously, often regretted having divorced their wives. **And** therefore, **the Sages instituted an ordinance** that the bill of divorce for these people should be of the tied format, which is a long, drawn-out process, hoping that **meanwhile, their composure would be regained** and they would reconsider their decision to divorce.

The *Gemara* then asks: *ve-ta'ama mai tiknu rabanan mekushar* — what was the actual reason the Rabbis instituted the *mekushar*? The answer: *asra de-kahanei hava* — this arose in a locale where *kohanim* were prevalent, *ve-havu kefidei tuva* — and those *kohanim* were quick-tempered and greatly agitated by small provocations, *u-mgarshi nishayhu* — and they would divorce their wives impulsively, and then regret it and find themselves unable to remarry them. The Rabbis therefore established the *takana* of the *get mekushar* specifically for *gittin*, so that the laborious process of writing, folding, and sewing — *ve-ad hachi ve-hachi maytva da'atayhu* — would allow sufficient time for the husband's temper to subside and for reconsideration.

### Extension to All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתִינַח גִּיטִּין, שְׁטָרוֹת מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּחַלֵּק בֵּין גִּיטִּין לִשְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This **works out well** for **bills of divorce,** but **what can be said** with regard to other **documents?** Why is this procedure used for other documents as well? The Gemara answers: This was instituted **so that you should not differentiate between bills of divorce and** other **documents.**

The *Gemara* then asks: *ha-tinach gittin, shtarot mai ika le-meimar* — understandable that the Rabbis made this institution for *gittin*, but why extend it to all *shtarot*? The answer: *kedei she-lo techalek bein gittin le-shtarot* — once the format was established for *gittin*, it became a recognized mode of document-writing, and one could not simply create a partition by which the same physical format would be valid in one legal context and unrecognized in another. Presumably, as well, the evidence from the Bar Kokhba caves confirms that this method of sealing documents was used in the broader world as a security measure, so extending it to all *shtarot* had practical justification.

## Witness Placement

### Rav Huna: Folds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָן עֵדִים חוֹתְמִין? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר. וְרַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אֲחוֹרֵי הַכְּתָב – וּכְנֶגֶד הַכְּתָב מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Where do the witnesses sign** on a tied document? **Rav Huna says:** They sign **between each tied** fold. **And Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says:** They sign **on the back of the written** side, taking care that the signatures are exactly **opposite the writing, on the outside.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a specific and important practical question: *heichan edim chotmin* — where exactly did the witnesses sign on a *mekushar*? Rav Huna says: *bein kesher le-kesher* — between the folds. To understand this properly: imagining the accordion-folded document with all its folds on side A (where the text is), on side B — the back — the sealed folds appear as a series of raised ridges. Each fold, on side B, presents a strip of visible surface. Rav Huna's position is that one witness signed on each such fold-strip, so that three folds carry three witnesses, one per fold.

### R. Yirmeya: Back

Rabbi Yirmeya offers a different position: *achurei ha-ktav u-keneged ha-ktav mi-bachutz* — on the back of the document, facing the written text, on the outside. As the discussion develops, it becomes clear that Rabbi Yirmeya is referring to the *pashut she-bi-mekushar*, the flat lower portion of the document. On side B, directly behind the flat written section rather than behind the folded section, the witnesses would inscribe their names. This contrasts with Rav Huna's placement on the folded portion at the top of side B.

### R. Chama & Rabbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: לְרַב הוּנָא דְּאָמַר בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר – קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר מִגַּוַּאי – וְהָא הָהוּא מְקוּשָּׁר דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, וְאָמַר רַבִּי: אֵין זְמַן בָּזֶה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי לְרַבִּי: שֶׁמָּא בֵּין קְשָׁרָיו מוּבְלָע? פַּלְיֵיהּ, וְחַזְיֵיהּ. וְאִם אִיתָא, ״אֵין זְמַן בָּזֶה וְאֵין עֵדִים בָּזֶה״ מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said to Rav Ḥisda: According to Rav Huna, who says** that the witnesses sign **between each tied** fold, it **enters our mind** that he meant **between each tied** fold **on the inside** of the document. **But** this is difficult, as there was **a certain tied** document **that came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, not realizing it was tied, **said: There is no date on this** document, so it is not valid. Then, **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **Perhaps** the date is **hidden between the tied** folds. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **opened it and saw** that the date was in fact between the tied folds. **And if it is so** that the witnesses sign between each tied fold on the inside of the document, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **should have** had two objections, and said: **There is no date on this** document, **and there are** also **no witnesses** signed **on this** document.

Rami bar Chama poses a question to Rav Chisda concerning Rav Huna's position. He raises an assumption: *ka-salka da'atach bein kesher le-kesher mi-gavai* — one might have thought that when Rav Huna said "between the folds," he meant on the inside, on side A. Rami bar Chama adduces a proof from a story: a *shtar mekushar* was once brought before *Rebbi*, and *Rebbi* said *ein zman ba-zeh* — there is no date on this document. He was looking at the *pashut she-bi-mekushar*, the flat lower portion visible before him, and did not see the date, because the date was written in the sewn-up folds above it. Rabbi Shimon be-Rabbi said to him: *shema bein kashrav mufla* — perhaps the date is concealed in the folds. *Palyei* — he opened them — *ve-chazyei* — and the date was there. This story reveals that by the time of *Rebbi*, the *get mekushar* was already so unfamiliar that *Rebbi* himself did not recognize it when presented with one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר – מִגַּוַּאי? לָא, בֵּין קֶשֶׁר לְקֶשֶׁר – מֵאַבָּרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: Do you maintain** that Rav Huna meant that the witnesses sign **between the tied** folds **on the inside? No,** he meant **between the tied** folds **on the outside** of the document.

The *Gemara* uses this story to resolve the question about Rav Huna's position. If the witnesses had been on the inside of the folds — sewn up together with the text — then before *Rebbi* even raised the question of the missing date, he should have said: "Where are the witnesses?" A document without visible witnesses is simply not a valid *shtar*. The fact that *Rebbi* recognized it as a *shtar* and only questioned the date proves that the witnesses were visible on the outside. The *Gemara* therefore clarifies: *lo, bein kesher le-kesher mi-barai* — when Rav Huna said "between the folds," he meant on the outside, on side B, on each fold-strip as it appears externally.

## Anti-Forgery Problem

### Blank Space Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא זַיֵּיף וְכָתֵב מַאי דְּבָעֵי, וַחֲתִימִי סָהֲדִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav Huna's opinion: **But let us be concerned** that **perhaps** the party holding the document **falsified** some information **and wrote whatever he wanted. And** this is a concern, as there are already **witnesses signed** on the document. In an ordinary document the witnesses sign immediately following the text, so there is no possibility of adding to the text. A tied document has part of its text written in the folds, but also has a part written on the face of the document on the unfolded paper, before or after the text in the folded part. If the witnesses sign between the folds there is the possibility of writing additional text in the unfolded section.

With the witnesses' placement established, the *Gemara* raises a serious structural problem. In an ordinary *pashut* document — such as a *ketubah* — the witnesses sign at the bottom, immediately after the final line of text. This configuration prevents forgery: there is no space to add lines after the witnesses have signed, and the standard closing formula *ha-kol sharir ve-kayam* signals that nothing follows. But in a *mekushar*, the witnesses are on side B up at the top of the document, associated with the folded section. The *pashut she-bi-mekushar* — the flat lower portion on side A — has no witnesses beneath it. This leaves the entire lower portion of the document open for fraudulent additions. A dishonest party could write additional obligations there — "and Reuven also owes Shimon one thousand dinars" — and the witnesses, being on side B above, would appear to attest to the entire document including the added text. The *Gemara* states the problem succinctly: *ve-neichosh dilma zayif ve-chasav mai de-ba'ei ve-chasmi sahadei* — let us be concerned that one might forge and write whatever one wishes, with the witnesses still appearing to corroborate it.

### Sharir V'kayam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The case is one **where it is written in** the document: Everything is **confirmed and established.** That is, every folded document must contain this formula at the end of the text, to prevent forgery, as any writing after this formula would be disregarded.

The *Gemara*'s first answer is: *de-chasvi sharir ve-kayam* — they would write *sharir ve-kayam* at the end of the document. The formula *sharir ve-kayam*, meaning "this is established and confirmed," functions as a definitive termination marker. Anything appearing after it is automatically invalid. Writing this formula at the end of the *pashut she-bi-mekushar* would thus prevent any fraudulent additions from carrying legal weight.

### Two Sharir Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא כָּתֵב מַאי דְּבָעֵי, וַהֲדַר כָּתֵב ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״ אַחֲרִינָא! חַד ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״ כָּתְבִינַן, תְּרֵי ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״ לָא כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But let us be concerned** that **perhaps** the holder of the document **wrote whatever he wanted and afterward wrote another** time: Everything is **confirmed and established.** The Gemara explains: **We write** only **one** declaration of: Everything is **confirmed and established; we do not write two** declarations of: Everything is **confirmed and established.** Therefore, anything written after the first declaration would be rejected, even if followed by a repetition of the declaration.

The *Gemara* immediately presses further: what if the forger writes the additional content and then simply adds a second *sharir ve-kayam* after it? The answer is straightforward: *chad sharir ve-kayam kasvinan, terei sharir ve-kayam lo kasvinan* — a document contains one *sharir ve-kayam*, never two. If a document bears two such formulas, the second is immediately suspect and renders the addition invalid. The presence of a second *sharir ve-kayam* is itself a red flag that something was improperly added.

### Erasure Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא מָחֵיק לֵיהּ לְ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״, וְכָתַב מַאי דְּבָעֵי, וַהֲדַר כָּתֵב ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״! הָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תְּלוּיָה; מְקוּיֶּימֶת – כְּשֵׁרָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions further: **But let** there be **a concern** that **perhaps** the holder of the document **erased** the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established, and** then **wrote whatever he wanted** over the erasure, **and afterward wrote** the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established.** The Gemara responds: How could this happen? **Doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** A document that includes **a suspended** correction of text inserted between lines of the document, which is **verified** at the end of the document, **is valid;**

The *Gemara* escalates the challenge further: *ve-neichosh dilma makhakei lei le-sharir ve-kayam* — what if the forger simply erases the *sharir ve-kayam*, writes in the fraudulent addition, and then writes a new *sharir ve-kayam*? Now the document has only one *sharir ve-kayam* and appears entirely normal. Why should we not be concerned about this?

This question requires the *Gemara* to address the broader question of when we are concerned about document forgery through erasure. In general, an altered document can still be authenticated through a *kiyum* — a ratification written at the bottom of the document, before *sharir ve-kayam*, in which the scribes or witnesses note the correction explicitly: e.g., "in line three we inserted a *zayin*," and the witnesses sign beneath it. When they do so, the witnesses are attesting that the correction was made before they signed, not after. If a letter appears to be *taluy* — suspended or written above the line as a correction — this is acceptable if it is *mekuyam*, i.e., if the correction is explicitly noted and attested at the bottom before the signatures. Such a correction does not render the document invalid.

### Erasure Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחָק – פָּסוּל, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּקוּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** but a document with a reference to words written over **an erasure is not valid, even if it is verified** at the end of the document. At the end of a document, before the formula: Everything is confirmed and established, is written, any corrections made in the document are verified by adding to the text: On line so-and-so, such and such a word has been added, or some similar formulation. This may be done only for inserted corrections, not for erasures.

Rabbi Yochanan establishes the governing rule: *tluyah me-kuyemet kesheirah, machuk pasul* — a suspended or floating correction that has been properly ratified is valid; an erasure (*machak*) is invalid. Critically, this applies even if the erasure is accompanied by a *kiyum* — *ve-af al pi she-mekuyam*. Why? Because the erasure might have been made precisely at the location where *sharir ve-kayam* had originally appeared, meaning the forger erased the closing formula, inserted fraudulent content, and then added a new closing. One can never be confident that an erasure in the relevant location did not erase the *sharir ve-kayam* itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אָמְרוּ מְחָק פָּסוּל – אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״, וּכְשִׁיעוּר ״שָׁרִיר וְקַיָּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this statement: **And they said that an erasure** on a document renders the document **not valid only** if it is **in a place** on the document where the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established,** should have been written, **and** only if the erasure **is the measure** of space in which the declaration: Everything is **confirmed and established,** can be written. The only concern with erasures is that the crucial formula: Everything is confirmed and established, might have been erased, as this would allow for unlimited forgery. If the erasure is such that this formula could not possibly have been erased, the document is valid.

The *Gemara* qualifies this rule: *ve-lo amru machak pasul ela bi-makom sharir ve-kayam* — erasure invalidates a document only when it occurs at the location in the document where one would expect *sharir ve-kayam* to appear, *u-ke-shiur sharir ve-kayam* — and when the erasure is of sufficient size to have accommodated that formula. Outside of that context and scale, erasures may still be ratified through a *kiyum*. Within that context, however, no *kiyum* can rehabilitate an erasure, precisely because the possibility of having erased the *sharir ve-kayam* itself can never be ruled out.

In the case of the *mekushar*, where there is extensive blank space at the bottom of side A below the main text, any erasure near the end of the document falls squarely within the problematic zone. There is no surrounding text that could limit the scope of the concern, and the entire area could have accommodated a *sharir ve-kayam* that was then erased and replaced.

### Conclusion

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is that the combination of two measures provides the anti-forgery protection that the *mekushar* requires in place of the conventional witness placement at the bottom. First, *sharir ve-kayam* is written at the end of the text on side A, marking definitively where the document concludes. Second, the rule that no document may contain two *sharir ve-kayam* formulas means that any addition after the first is immediately invalid. And the rule on erasures — that an erasure in the *sharir ve-kayam* zone cannot be rehabilitated even with a *kiyum* — means that erasing and rewriting the closing formula would itself render the document invalid. Together with the witnesses signed on the folds of side B, these measures constitute the structural integrity of the *mekushar* document. The question of how Rabbi Yirmeya's alternative placement — witnesses on the flat portion of side B rather than on the folds — handles the forgery concern will be taken up in the next session.
