# Eruvin 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wall Breaches & Pi Takra

### Keivan De'hutra Hutra

We pick up on the bottom of 94b, four lines from the bottom. By way of review, the *mishnah* established that if a wall falls down on *Shabbos*, one may still carry in that zone, because the space was *mutar* when *Shabbos* began. This was the position of the *tanna kamma*, Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, insists on consistency: either the space is valid for future *Shabbosos* or it is not valid for this *Shabbos*.

This connects directly to the principle of *keivan de'hutra hutra* — once something begins *Shabbos* in a permitted state, *hutra le'oho Shabbos*, it remains permitted for the entire *Shabbos*: *hutra lechol ha'Shabbos kulo*. Things continue in the condition in which they began. There is, of course, a limit to this principle. If the situation deteriorates to the point of a genuine biblical violation — such as a true *reshus ha'rabim* — one cannot invoke mere rabbinic leniency to sustain it.

### Maintaining Initial Stat…

This is precisely why the *gemara* labored to explain that the *mishnah*'s case of two walls falling down does not really mean that the space has been reduced to an open field. Rather, we are dealing with something like two corners of a wall having crumbled, while the structure retains mostly intact walls on three sides in various configurations. The *gemara* explored why the *mishnah* needed to specify two walls when even one wall falling would suffice to compromise the *reshus ha'yachid* status and generate the scenario under discussion.

### Rav vs. Shmuel Debate

Within that broader discussion, we encountered the debate between Rav and Shmuel regarding the principle of *pi takra yored vesosem* — the notion that the edge of a roof descends conceptually and seals off that side, creating a wall. According to Rashi's reading of the *gemara*, Shmuel holds that one cannot invoke *pi takra* for all four walls, while Rav permits invoking it even for a full four walls. That debate over how broadly one may apply *pi takra* is the backdrop against which the *gemara* now examines several further parameters.

## Pi Takra Parameters

### Ten Amos Consensus

The *gemara* opens this analysis with the statement *b'eser kulei alma lo peligi* — if the breach is ten *amos* or fewer, everyone agrees that one invokes *pi takra yored vesosem*. There is no dispute in that case.

### Larger Than Ten Amos

The disagreement arises when the breach is *beyaser me'eser* — larger than ten *amos*. An opening wider than ten *amos* is not treated as a normal doorway and therefore requires correction. It is precisely here that Rav and Shmuel diverge: according to Rashi, Shmuel will not invoke *pi takra* for four walls, while Rav will do so even in that scenario.

The *gemara* then presents an alternative formulation — *ika de'amri* — which inverts the premise: *beyaser kulei alma lo peligi*, meaning that for openings larger than ten *amos*, everyone agrees that *pi takra* does not apply. The debate, on this reading, concerns openings of ten *amos* or smaller: *ki peligi b'eser*. That is, it is only within the ten-*amah* range that Rav and Shmuel differ over whether *pi takra* may be invoked.

One might ask: if the breach is ten *amos* or smaller, what is the need for *pi takra* at all? A gap that size would ordinarily be treated like an ordinary doorway. The answer is that there are scenarios where it matters — most notably where there are too many openings such that *parutz meruba al ha-omed* becomes a concern, and in the extreme case Rashi describes of a portico situated in the middle of a valley with no walls at all. In those situations, the question of whether *pi takra* can function for an opening of a given size becomes critical. One could also imagine an analogous vertical limit — whether *pi takra* applies when the roof is higher than twenty *amos*, which connects to the beginning of the *masechet*, though this specific question is not taken up explicitly here.

### Beam in Field Case

The *gemara* next cites a ruling of Rav Yehuda: *kora arba matya b'churva* — a beam four *tefachim* wide positioned in the middle of an open field creates a *reshus ha'yachid* beneath it. The mechanism is *pi takra* applied to all four sides: the edges of the beam descend and form four conceptual walls, enclosing a space four by four *tefachim*. This is an extreme application of *pi takra*, relying on it to generate all four walls simultaneously.

### Water vs. Dry Land

Rav Nachman, citing Rabba bar Avuha, qualifies this ruling: *kora arba matya b'mayim* — the beam case applies only over water, not over dry land. We have seen elsewhere that the *halachos* of walls over water involve special leniencies, such as permitting the drawing of water. The question of who this ruling follows — *mani* — is then addressed in light of the two formulations of the Rav-Shmuel debate. According to the first reading (*leima*), where everyone agrees for openings of ten *amos* or smaller, *de'eser ve'divrei hakol*, even Shmuel could accept the beam case, provided the opening is less than ten *amos*. According to the second reading (*ve'idach lishana*), where the debate extends even to ten *amos* or smaller, the beam case reflects the position of Rav alone.

### Sukkah S'chach Debate

The *gemara* then asks whether the debate between Abaye and Rava regarding *s'chach* on a portico parallels the dispute between Rav and Shmuel. The case: *sekhakha gabei achsadra she'yesh lah petzimim* — *s'chach* placed on a portico that has posts at its open edges. When such posts exist, they constitute at least two and a half walls, which suffices for a *sukkah*, and everyone agrees it is *keshera*. The difficulty arises when *ein lah petzimim* — there are no posts, meaning the portico lacks the requisite walls. Here the debate turns entirely on *pi takra*: Abaye says it remains *keshera* because *pi takra yored vesosem*, while Rava says it is *pesulah* because we do not invoke *pi takra* in this configuration.

The *gemara* asks whether this means Abaye follows Rav and Rava follows Shmuel. The answer is more nuanced: the debate of Abaye and Rava, on this reading, is *aliba d'Rav* — both are working within Rav's framework, which ordinarily permits invoking *pi takra* on all four sides. Abaye holds that this applies to the *sukkah* case as well. Rava disagrees.

### Permanent vs. Ad Hoc Roo…

Rava's position is that Rav's permission to invoke *pi takra* on four sides was stated specifically where *hani mechitzos l'achsadra avidi* — the roof was built as part of the portico structure itself, and so the roof defines and encloses the space beneath it. In that case, the logic of *pi takra* is compelling: a permanent roof suggests the existence of walls and creates a sense of enclosed space. But in the *sukkah* case, one simply set up posts and laid *s'chach* on top — an ad hoc arrangement made for a specific purpose. Rava holds that such a roof, *lav l'sukka avidi* in the relevant sense, lacks the structural character necessary to invoke *pi takra* on all four sides. The underlying logic, as one might frame it, is that *pi takra* derives its force from the way a roof naturally implies an enclosed structure beneath it; a purely temporary covering arranged for *Sukkos* does not carry that implication strongly enough to generate four conceptual walls.

## Rabbi Yosi's Position

### Past vs. Future Shabbosos

The *gemara* now returns to the central question of the *mishnah*: what is Rabbi Yosi's ruling, and in which direction does it cut? *Ibayu lehu*: Does Rabbi Yosi's principle apply *l'issura* — meaning the wall's compromise renders the space forbidden for this *Shabbos* as well as future ones — or *l'hetera* — meaning its permitted status now carries forward to future *Shabbosos*?

### Ruling Like Rabbi Yosi

Rav Sheshet rules *l'issura*, and so does Rabbi Yochanan. The logic is straightforward: it would be anomalous for an accident that occurs on *Shabbos* to generate more leniency going forward. The normal rule should govern. This is corroborated by a *beraita*: *amar Rabbi Yosi k'shem she'asura l'asid lavo kach asura l'osa Shabbos* — just as it is forbidden going forward, so too it is forbidden for this very *Shabbos*.

### Rabbi Yehuda in Eruvin

On the question of practical *psak*, Rav Chiya bar Yosef rules like Rabbi Yosi, while Shmuel rules like Rabbi Yehuda, maintaining that the space retains its permitted status. This latter ruling appears consistent with the broader principle cited in Rav Yehuda's name from Shmuel: *kol makom sheshana Rabbi Yehuda b'Eruvin halacha k'moso* — wherever Rabbi Yehuda rules in *Eruvin*, we follow him.

### Shmuel's Qualified Posit…

However, the *gemara* challenges whether Shmuel truly maintains this position across the board. Rav Chana Bagdata'ah asks Rav Yehuda: does Shmuel mean to rule like Rabbi Yehuda even in the case of our *mishnah* — where the beam or post of a *mavoi* fell down, eliminating the requisite wall configuration — and say that carrying there remains permitted? This is the case of *b'mavoi shenitla koraso o lechayav*.

### Eruvin vs. Mechitzos

Shmuel clarifies: *b'Eruvin amri lach, v'lo b'mechitzos* — his ruling in favor of Rabbi Yehuda applies to *halachos* of *eruvin* proper, not to questions of wall requirements. The distinction is significant: while we often discuss both under the heading of *Eruvin*, the laws of *mechitzos* are technically *Shabbos* laws that define the character of a *reshus ha'yachid*, while *eruvin* in the narrow sense concerns the joining together through bread or food to create communal unity permitting carrying. Shmuel will follow Rabbi Yehuda on the former, not the latter.

### Karmelis vs. Reshus Hara…

Rav Abba offers a further precision, reporting what was explained to him directly from Shmuel: the ruling depends on what domain the wall opens to. *Kan shenifretzu l'karmelis, kan shenifretzu l'reshus ha'rabim*. If the breach exposes the *chatzer* to a *karmelis*, there is less cause for concern — even if one carries from the *chatzer* into the *karmelis*, it is only a rabbinic transgression, and so the space may remain in its initial permitted status over *Shabbos*. But if the breach opens to a *reshus ha'rabim*, one risks a biblical transgression, and Shmuel would not permit the space to continue in its permitted status.

Crucially, the leniency of the *mavoi* case involving the *korah* or *lechi* that fell — distinct from our *mishnah*'s concern about the number of walls — brings in an additional dimension: the function of the *korah* or *lechi* is partly to serve as a *heker*, a reminder that one is entering a different zone. When that marker falls, the severity of the concern depends on what zone is exposed. So Shmuel's position emerges as quite narrow: he would rule like Rabbi Yehuda only when three full walls remain intact and the breach opens only to a *karmelis*.

Tosafot's conclusion on the practical *halacha* is that we rule like Rav against Shmuel in prohibitions, and therefore even in the case of a breach to a *karmelis*, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi who is stringent: *v'afilu b'nifretza l'karmelis halacha k'Rabbi Yosi d'machmir*.

## Bridge & Portico Cases

### Second Story Case

The *mishnah* with which we close this *perek* presents two related cases. The first: *haboneh aliyah al gavi sheni batim* — someone builds a second story over two houses situated on opposite sides of a *reshus ha'rabim*, connected like a bridge overhead. The practical image is vivid: two houses flanking a public thoroughfare, with a walkway or room built above spanning the gap between them, open at both ends below. Rabbi Yehuda rules that *metaltelin tachteihen b'Shabbos* — one may carry underneath the bridge on *Shabbos*. The *chachamim* forbid it.

The second case: *me'arvin l'mavoi mefulash* — Rabbi Yehuda further holds that one may make an *eruv* for an open-ended street, a *mavoi mefulash*, that has walls on its two long sides but is open at both ends. The *chachamim* forbid this as well.

### Rabba's Pi Takra View

Rabba cautions against misreading Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning in the first case. *Lo teima hainu ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda mishum d'kasvar shtei mechitzos d'oraita* — do not assume that the permission in the bridge case rests on Rabbi Yehuda's principle that two walls suffice biblically. Rather, says Rabba, *mishum d'kasvar pi tikra yored vesosem* — the bridge case relies on *pi takra*: the edges of the overhead structure descend and create four effective walls, leaving the two actual house walls on the sides and the two conceptual walls generated by *pi takra* at the open ends.

### Two Walls Sufficing

Abaye challenges this interpretation. He demonstrates from a different *sugya* that Rabbi Yehuda's principle that two walls suffice is independently established: *yeter al ken amar Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda further ruled that a person with houses on two sides of a *reshus ha'rabim* need only add a *lechi* or *korah* at each open end in order to carry in the enclosed stretch, even though it traverses an actual *reshus ha'rabim*. This is an extreme position — a minimal post or beam alongside two existing walls is sufficient to create a *reshus ha'yachid*. The *chachamim* responded that one cannot make an *eruv* in a *reshus ha'rabim* with so few structural elements. But the point for Abaye is that Rabbi Yehuda's view that two walls suffice is clearly established from this independent source.

Abaye's response to Rabba is therefore: *me'ha leka l'mishma minah* — I do not claim you are wrong about Rabbi Yehuda's position; I know full well he holds two walls suffice. I am only saying that the first case in the *mishnah* does not make that principle clear, since the bridge itself provides *pi takra* on the remaining sides. So the debate between Rabba and Abaye is largely academic.

### Two Principles Analysis

Rav Ashi, however, observes that the *mishnah* itself points toward Rabba's interpretation. Since the *mishnah* adds a second case with the words *ve'od amar Rabbi Yehuda* — *and further* Rabbi Yehuda said one may make an *eruv* in an open-ended street — this formulation implies that the second case adds something beyond the first. *Ei amart bishlama mishum de'kasvar pi tikra yored vesosem* — if the first case is about *pi takra*, then *ve'od* makes perfect sense: the second case goes further and shows that Rabbi Yehuda does not even need *pi takra*, for he relies on two walls alone. But if the first case were itself already about two walls, there would be no escalation in the second case and the *ve'od* would be superfluous. *Shma mina*.

### Chachamim Disagree

This analysis also raises a noteworthy question about the *chachamim*'s position. It is understandable that the *chachamim* reject the principle that two walls suffice — that is the second case's dispute. But since everyone, including the *chachamim*, has been working with some form of *pi takra* throughout this *sugya*, why do the *chachamim* forbid the bridge case where *pi takra* is in play? The answer, presumably, is that the bridge case involves *pi takra* being invoked in the middle of an active *reshus ha'rabim* — a space being constantly traversed by the public. Invoking invisible conceptual walls in a thoroughfare through which people are continually passing is a different matter from invoking *pi takra* over a quiet courtyard or valley. This, evidently, is where the *chachamim* draw the line.

With that, the *perek* concludes. *Hadran alach kol ha'gagos.*

## Tefillin Transport Case

### Finding Tefillin

The new *perek*, *Hamotzi Tefillin*, opens a significantly different kind of discussion. The scenario: one finds *tefillin* lying in the street on *Shabbos* — not *chutz la'tchum*, but simply outdoors with no *eruv*, no walls, no valid enclosure. What is one to do?

### Pair-by-Pair Transport

The *mishnah* rules *machnisan zug zug* — one brings them in pair by pair. One dons a complete pair — both the *shel yad* and the *shel rosh* — wears them while walking home, removes them, and returns for the next pair, repeating as needed.

### Two Pairs Option

Rabban Gamliel goes further: *shnayim shnayim* — one may wear two complete pairs simultaneously, corresponding to the view of Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam. We will analyze the basis of this debate in detail shortly.

### Used vs. New Tefillin

The *mishnah* then qualifies the first ruling. *Bameh devarim amurim b'yashanot* — this applies to old, used *tefillin*. If the *tefillin* are brand new, one is not only exempt from this procedure but actually forbidden to employ it. The concern is that at that time it was common to fashion amulets housed in containers that resembled *tefillin* and worn on the body. A new, unworn set of *tefillin* could plausibly be mistaken for such an amulet. One cannot be certain they are genuinely *halachic tefillin* and therefore may not carry them in this manner.

This raises an obvious question: how does age resolve the concern? Rashi explains that *yashanot* in practice means that the knots have been tied — the *dalet* of the *shel rosh* and the *yud* of the *shel yad* are visibly formed. Since no one would go to the trouble of creating an amulet with the proper *kesher* of *tefillin*, the presence of these knots establishes that one is dealing with genuine *halachic tefillin*. A further question arises: one might think amulets too are worn, so why would wearing not raise the same concern? The answer is that the wearing here is a device for transport, not a straightforward act of donning an amulet in the normal sense. The concern is whether the items are genuine, not how they are being used.

### Bundled Tefillin Case

If the *tefillin* are found *tzvatim o kerichot* — tied in bundles — a different regime applies. One cannot simply don them, so *machshikh aleihem u'meviyan*: one remains with them until nightfall and brings them in at *Motzaei Shabbos*. The reason one cannot simply unbundle them and wear them is not stated explicitly here and will be addressed in the *gemara*. The *mishnah* is clear, however, that guarding them is an obligation — one who encounters *tefillin* lying exposed has a duty to remain there all day if necessary to protect them from mistreatment or theft.

### Danger Override

If remaining poses a *sakkanah* — a danger to oneself, whether from human threat or simple vulnerability of being left in an exposed field until dark — then *mekhasan ve'holekh lo*: one covers the *tefillin* and is exempt from staying. At *Motzaei Shabbos* one returns, and hopefully they will still be there. The *tefillin* matter greatly, but the danger to the person overrides.

### Daisy Chain Method

Rabbi Shimon offers a practical alternative for situations where wearing the *tefillin* is not possible. *Noten le'chavero ve'chavero le'chavero ad she'magia la'chatzer ha'chitzonah* — a chain of people is formed; each person passes the *tefillin* to the next, with no individual carrying them more than *arba amos*, thereby avoiding any prohibited act even in a *reshus ha'rabim*. The *tefillin* are thus brought to an outer yard where they can be protected.

The *mishnah* extends this principle to an infant — Rashi understands this to refer to a newborn delivered in the field — and the same daisy-chain method may be employed to transport the child.

### Object Techumim Rule

The *mishnah* returns to the theme of *Eruvin* by way of Rabbi Yehuda's position, which introduces the *halacha* that objects are bound by their owner's *techumim*: *lo teileich zo yoter me'raglei be'aleha* — an object cannot travel further than its owner may go.

### Property Outside Tehum

Rabbi Yehuda extends the daisy-chain principle even further, ruling that one may employ it not only for *tefillin* and infants but even for property — *noten adam chavilo le'chavero* — and crucially, even property that is *chutz la'tchum*, beyond the owner's *techum*. This is a significant leniency: not merely permitting the transport of personal possessions but allowing one to move property across a *techum* boundary to protect it, provided one does not carry it into an enclosed *reshus ha'yachid*.

### Tosafot's Reasoning

Tosafot explains why this *perek* belongs in *Eruvin*: it is precisely this final *halacha* of *techumim* that anchors it here. One can also see a thematic connection to the preceding *perek*, which dealt with scenarios where there is no *eruv* yet carrying is still necessary — asking how one navigates a world without proper wall configurations. This *perek* continues that inquiry: when there are no walls at all and something sacred or vulnerable needs to be transported, what tools does *halacha* provide?

## One vs. Two Pairs

### Mishnah Positions

The *mishnah* establishes the base positions: the *tanna kamma* permits only one pair at a time, while Rabban Gamliel permits two. The *gemara* now proceeds to analyze these positions carefully, asking first whether our *mishnah* is simply in tension with a more permissive *tanna*.

### Rabbi Meir Comparison

Perhaps the *mishnah*'s restriction to one pair is inconsistent with Rabbi Meir, who in another context — a fire that threatens property on *Shabbos* — rules that one may wear as many layers of clothing as possible: *lovesh kama sheyachol lilvosh, ve'otef kama sheyachol la'atof*. If Rabbi Meir permits piling on garment after garment as a device for saving property, why does our *mishnah* limit the transport of *tefillin* to one pair? The *gemara* establishes that the fire *mishnah* is indeed attributed to Rabbi Meir by way of a *beraita* that makes this explicit: *lovesh umotzi ufoshet velovesh umotzi ufoshet afilu kol ha'yom kulo, divrei Rabbi Meir* — one may wear, bring out, remove, and repeat the entire day.

### Rava's Distinction

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction. Even if our *mishnah* were consistent with Rabbi Meir, *tefillin* are categorically different from clothing. In the fire case, *hatam devachol kama d'ba'ei lavish* — during the weekday one may choose to wear as many layers as one wishes; wearing many layers, however eccentric, remains within the range of normal wearing. Therefore, even for the purpose of rescue, the Rabbis permit it, since it does not constitute an abnormal or prohibited act. But with *tefillin*, the position is *hacha devachol nami zug echad in, tefei lo* — even during the week, only one pair is *halachically* acceptable; more than one is not. This is not a matter of being unusual; it is a matter of being prohibited. Therefore, *le'inyan hatzalah nami zug echad in, tefei lo* — for the purpose of rescue as well, only one pair may be worn.

### Shabbos as Zman Tefillin

The *gemara* now reaches the heart of the analysis, framing the debate as hinging on a fundamental question: is *Shabbos* a *zman tefillin* — a time when one is obligated to wear *tefillin*? The accepted practice is that *tefillin* are not worn on *Shabbos*, rooted in the understanding that *Shabbos* itself bears the character of an *ot*, a sign of the covenant, and wearing *tefillin*, which is also an *ot*, would be superfluous or even incongruous. But this is not immediately obvious from the text of the Torah, and the theoretical question of whether *Shabbos* is *zman tefillin* has genuine *halachic* implications.

### One Pair If Zman

If *Shabbos* is *zman tefillin*, then wearing *tefillin* on *Shabbos* is subject to the normal laws governing the *mitzvah*: one pair, worn properly, is the obligation. Wearing a second pair would then constitute *bal tosif* — the prohibition against adding to a commandment — and would be entirely unacceptable. The consequence for transport is stark: one pair may be worn as fulfilling or simulating the *mitzvah*; more than one renders the additional pairs forbidden, which in turn means they are not being worn in any legitimate sense, and carrying them would be prohibited.

### Tefillin as Accessory

If *Shabbos* is *not zman tefillin*, the analysis shifts. Since one is not obligated to wear *tefillin* on *Shabbos*, the *tefillin* one carries are not being viewed through the lens of the *mitzvah* at all. They function more like an accessory — an object donned on the body in a manner that happens to resemble *tefillin*-wearing but is not framed as a *mitzvah* act. In that case, multiple pairs need not be seen as an impermissible expansion of the commandment.

### Normal Wearing Location

Nevertheless, Rabban Gamliel does not permit unlimited pairs even on the view that *Shabbos* is *lav zman tefillin*. The Rabbis permitted this form of transport only when it constitutes *derech malbush bimkom tefillin* — wearing in the normal place that *tefillin* are worn. One cannot, say, tie *tefillin* around one's ankle and call it wearing. So even on the permissive view, the constraint is that the *tefillin* must be placed where *tefillin* actually go. This places an upper bound on how many may be worn simultaneously.

### Space for Two

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak provides the anatomical grounding: *makom yesh ba'rosh le'haniach bo shtei tefillin* — the head has sufficient space to accommodate two sets of *tefillin*, one behind the other. Similarly, the arm has adequate space for two *shel yad* placements. This is the basis for Rabban Gamliel's position that two pairs — no more — are acceptable.

### Head Placement

Tosafot raises the question of the precise size of *tefillin*, noting that the Ri found in a *midrash* that the minimum width of *tefillin* is two fingers. Tosafot connects this to the *tzitz* worn by the *Kohen Gadol*, which Tosafot believes was positioned in the location of the *shel rosh* rather than on the forehead. The *tzitz* was two fingers wide, and with the *Kohen*'s head covering above it, there was exactly enough room for the *tefillin* to fit in the proper space. The place of the *shel rosh* is defined by the *gemara* through the ruling of *d'vei Rav Yannai*: *makom she'mocho shel tinok rofes* — the place where an infant's skull is still soft, the fontanelle or crown of the head.

### Zman Tefillin Resolution

The *gemara* settles the matter: *dkulei alma is lehu de'Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak* — everyone would agree that there is physical space for two pairs. The debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabban Gamliel is not about anatomy but about the *zman tefillin* question: *d'Tanna Kamma savar Shabbos zman tefillin hu, v'Rabban Gamliel savar Shabbos lav zman tefillin hu*. The *tanna kamma* holds *Shabbos* is a *zman tefillin*, which means only one pair is ever acceptable; any second pair is *bal tosif*. Rabban Gamliel holds *Shabbos* is not *zman tefillin*, which means the *tefillin* are not being treated as a *mitzvah* act and so the *bal tosif* framework does not apply. Given that, the only constraint is whether one is wearing them in the normal place — which, as Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak establishes, accommodates precisely two pairs.

### Bal Tosif Principle

Tosafot adds a further dimension to this analysis that deepens the logic considerably. *Bal tosif* — the prohibition of adding to a commandment — is most easily violated when one is in the *zman* of that *mitzvah*, because then one is genuinely expanding an obligatory act. If it is not the *zman* of the *mitzvah*, adding to it is not a meaningful expansion of the commandment — it is simply doing something unnecessary. Tosafot's illustration is instructive: were one today, outside of *Sukkos*, to take a *lulav va'esrog* and shake it with the appropriate formula, one would likely not be transgressing *bal tosif*, because the *mitzvah* season has not begun and one is not expanding any active obligation. Accordingly, if *Shabbos* is *zman tefillin*, wearing a second pair falls directly within the prohibition of *bal tosif*; if *Shabbos* is not *zman tefillin*, there is no *bal tosif* concern, and the only question becomes one of what constitutes normal wearing. This is the principle that will carry the discussion forward into the next *gemara*.
