# Zevachim 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatas Pesukim

### Shechitah Source

The eighth daf of Zevachim opens by pivoting from the previous discussion — which established that most *korbanos* must be performed *lishmah* and *l'shem ba'alim* at least *lechatchila*, though failure to do so renders them *kasher* while denying the owners fulfillment of their obligation — to the exceptions enumerated in the *mishnah*: *chatas* and *pesach*. When either of these is performed *shelo lishmo*, the *korban* is entirely *pasul* and cannot be offered on the *mizbeach*. The *gemara* now undertakes the task of locating the scriptural basis for both the *lechatchila* requirement and the *me'akev* status of *lishmah* by *chatas*.

The *gemara* begins with the question *chatas minalan* — where do we learn the law of *chatas*? The relevant *pasuk* states *v'shachat osah l'chatat*, "and he shall slaughter it for a *chatas*." The phrase *l'chatat* — for the purpose of being a *chatas* — establishes that the *shechitah* must be performed *l'shem chatas*. The principle derived is *she'te'hei shechitah l'shem chatat*: the slaughter must be done for the sake of the sin offering. This covers the first of the four *avodos*.

### Kabbalah Source

At the top of 8a, the *gemara* turns to the *kabbalah* — the receiving of the blood in a vessel. The *pasuk* reads *v'lakach hakohen midam hachatat*, "and the *kohen* shall take from the blood of the *chatas*." The formulation *hachatat* — the *chatas* — is a doubling of reference that could have been expressed more simply; the Torah's choice to specify *hachatat* teaches that *she'te'hei kabala l'shem chatat*, the receiving of the blood must also be for the sake of the *chatas*. The term *lakach* is classically understood to encompass both the *kabbalah* and the *holacha*, the carrying of the blood toward the *mizbeach*.

### Zerikah Source

For the *zerikah* — the throwing of the blood on the *mizbeach* — the *gemara* cites *v'chiper alav hakohen michatato*, "and the *kohen* shall atone for him from his sin." The act of atonement is the blood service on the *mizbeach*, and the formulation teaches *she'te'hei kapara l'shem chatat* — the atonement, too, must be performed for the sake of the *chatas*.

### Shinui Kodesh

Having established from these *pesukim* that all the *avodos* must be performed *l'shem chatas* at least *lechatchila*, the *gemara* notes: *ashkachan shinui kodesh* — we have now established the requirement regarding *shinui kodesh*, the case where the *avodah* is performed with intent for a different type of *korban*.

### Shinui Ba'alim

The *gemara* then asks *shinui ba'alim minalan* — where do we learn that *lechatchila* one must also have in mind the correct owner? The answer is found in the word *alav* from the same *pasuk*: *v'chiper alav hakohen* — "and the *kohen* shall atone for him." The precision of *alav v'lo l'chavero* — for him and not for another — establishes that the *avodah* must be performed with intent for the actual owner. And the *gemara* concludes: *ashkachan l'mitzva* — all of this establishes the *lechatchila* requirement.

## Me'akev Requirements

### Shinui Kodesh Gap

Having established the *lechatchila* requirements for both *shinui kodesh* and *shinui ba'alim*, the *gemara* now confronts the harder question: *l'akev minalan?* How do we know that failure to comply renders the *korban pasul*?

The *gemara* reviews what has been established and what remains to be proven. The *pesukim* of *v'shachat osah l'chatat* and *v'lakach midam hachatat* establish *lechatchila* for *shinui kodesh* across the *avodos*. The *pasuk* of *v'chiper alav hakohen michatato* establishes both the *lechatchila* and the *me'akev* status — but only for *shinui ba'alim* by *zerikah*, because the word *michatato* ("from his *chatas*") reiterates the ownership requirement a second time in the same verse, and the doubling teaches that the *zerikah* with *shinui ba'alim* invalidates the *korban*.

The resulting picture is therefore incomplete in two respects. First, *shinui kodesh*: we know all the *avodos* must be performed *l'shem chatas*, but we do not yet know that failure to do so is *me'akev*. Second, *shinui ba'alim*: we know it is *me'akev* only by *zerikah*, but not by the other *avodos*. The question *l'akev b'chol avodos b'shinui kodesh*, and *b'she'ar avodos b'shinui ba'alim*, is therefore still open.

Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua had already made a *drasha* from the extra *vav* in the word *chatato* — the possessive suffix — arguing that the reiteration teaches that the ownership requirement is *me'akev*. The *gemara* notes that the same type of reasoning will be applied here: *hacha nami chatat chataso*, the expression *michatato* doubles down on the ownership requirement and teaches *me'akev* status. But this still only covers *zerikah*. The broader application to all *avodos* requires an additional source.

### Chatas Nazir

Rav Yona provides the answer: *atya m'chatat nazir* — one learns from the *chatas* of a *nazir*. The relevant *pasuk* states *v'hikriv hakohen lifnei Hashem v'asa et chatato v'et olato*, "and the *kohen* shall bring before God and perform his *chatas* and his *olah*." The word *v'asa* — "and he shall perform" — is generic and encompasses all the *avodos* at once. From *v'asa et chatato*, we learn *sheyaaseh kol asiyotav l'shem chatat* — all of the acts of the *avodah* must be performed for the sake of the *chatas*. This covers the *lechatchila* requirement for *shinui kodesh* across all *avodos*.

Regarding *shinui ba'alim*, the *gemara* adopts the *girsa* of Tosafot: since the *pasuk* of *v'asa et chatato* by *chatas nazir* is superfluous (the *lechatchila* requirement for *shinui kodesh* was already derived from the *pesukim* by *chatas chelev*), it is redirected — *im eino inyan l'shinui kodesh, tneihu inyan l'shinui ba'alim* — to teach that all the *avodos* must also be performed *l'shem ba'alim*.

### Doubling Teaches Me'akev

The *lechatchila* for *shinui ba'alim* across all *avodos* is now established, but *me'akev* remains elusive beyond *zerikah*. It is Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua who supplies the answer: the extra *vav* in *chatato* — appearing as *michatato* — reiterates the ownership requirement in a way that signals *me'akev* status. Once applied, this covers the *me'akev* requirement for both *shinui kodesh* and *shinui ba'alim* across all *avodos* of *chatas*.

## Me'akev Requirements…

### Chatas Chelev vs Nazir

At this point Ravina raises a sharp challenge. The entire structure rests on the assumption that one *chatas* can be learned from another — specifically, that the *pesukim* by *chatas nazir* can be applied to fill the gaps by *chatas chelev*. But this assumption does not go unchallenged. The *gemara* poses the question: *migmari mehadadi? chatas chelev mechatas nazir lo gamri* — can one really learn them from one another? Perhaps not.

---

## Chatas Metzora

### Ravina's Challenge

Ravina challenges the derivation: how can one learn *chatas chelev* from *chatas nazir*? Each *chatas* may have requirements unique to its own context. *Chatas chelev mechatas nazir lo gamri*, the *gemara* states, *sheken yesh immah damim acherim* — a *chatas nazir* comes together with other blood offerings, specifically an *olah*, and therefore one cannot assume that the demands applying to *chatas nazir* transfer to a standalone sin offering. Conversely, one cannot learn in the reverse direction either: *chatas nazir mechatas chelev lo gamar, sheken karet* — a *chatas chelev* is brought for a sin punishable by *karet*, which may entail heavier requirements. Each type of *chatas* carries a distinctive feature that might explain elevated demands, and therefore neither can be derived from the other.

### Minchah Nesachim

At this point the *gemara* notes that Ravina challenged specifically the *vav* of *olaso* — the *olah* of the *nazir* — asking: if you are going to use the *vav* in *chatato* to teach *me'akev*, what will you do with the extra *vav* in *olaso*, since the *me'akev* requirement does not apply to an *olah* the way it does to a *chatas*? Why did Ravina not similarly challenge the extra *vavs* found in *minchaso* and *nisko* — his *minchah* and wine libation?

The *gemara* explains that those *vavs* are not problematic because they are needed for a separate *drasha*. The *pasuk* by *Sukkos* teaches, from the possessive form of *minchasam veniskeihem*, that the *minchah* and *nesachim* accompanying a *korban* may be brought even at night or even on the following day. Applying the same reading to the *korban* of the *nazir*, *minchaso venisko* teaches that a *nazir*'s *minchah* and *nesachim* may similarly be brought on a subsequent night or day. Since those *vavs* serve that separate purpose, Ravina's challenge was rightly directed only at *olaso*. And the challenge about *olaso* — paired with the structural objection that one *chatas* cannot be learned from another — leaves the derivation incomplete.

### Rava's Solution

Rava proposes an alternative: *atya mechatas metzora* — learn not from *chatas nazir* but from the *chatas* of a *metzora*. The relevant *pasuk* states *ve'asah hakohen et hachatas*, "and the *kohen* shall perform the *chatas*." Once again the word *ve'asah* is generic and encompasses all the *avodos*, yielding the principle *sheyehu kol asiyosav leshem chatas* — all acts of the *avodah* must be for the sake of the *chatas*. And for *shinui ba'alim*, the *pasuk* adds *v'chiper al hamitaher* — he shall atone for the one becoming purified — from which one derives *al hamitaher zeh v'lo al mitaher chaveiro*: for this person and not for another's *metzora*. Combined with the *pesukim* already known by *chatas chelev*, the doubling would then signal *me'akev* status.

### Metzora Distinct

The *gemara* immediately raises the same objection against Rava's solution. *Chatas chelev mechatas metzora lo yalfei, sheken yesh imom damim acherim* — a *chatas metzora* comes alongside an *olah* and an *asham* and other *korbanos*, so its demands may be heavier and cannot be assumed to apply to a standalone *chatas chelev*. And in the reverse, *chatas metzora mechatas chelev lo gamar, sheken karet* — the demands of a *chatas* brought for a *karet* offense may be more stringent than those applicable to a *metzora*. Once again, the two *chatasos* are too distinct to allow learning one from the other.

## Tzad HaShaveh

### Learn One from Two

*Chada mechada lo asya* — one cannot be learned from the other alone, since each has a distinctive feature that might account for its requirements. But the *gemara* proposes a way forward: *teisi chada mitartei* — why not learn one from two? We now have three sets of *pesukim* requiring *lishmah* by *chatas*: by *chatas chelev*, by *chatas nazir*, and by *chatas metzora*. If any two of them together would suffice to establish the requirement in the third, then the *pesukim* by the third are superfluous — and that surplus teaches not merely *lechatchila* but *me'akev*.

The *gemara* tests each possible configuration. First: why not omit the *lishmah* requirement from *chatas chelev* and learn it from *chatas nazir* and *chatas metzora*? No — *mah lehanach sheken yesh imom damim acherim*, both of those come in a cluster of *korbanos*, and their combined demands might not transfer to a standalone *chatas chelev*. Second: why not omit it from *chatas nazir* and learn from *chatas chelev* and *chatas metzora*? No — *mah lehanach sheken ein lahem she'eilah*, a *nazir* can in principle approach a *chacham* to have the *nezirus* annulled, so the demands applying to the irreversible *chatas chelev* and *chatas metzora* might not apply to *chatas nazir*. Third: why not omit it from *chatas metzora* and learn from *chatas chelev* and *chatas nazir*? No — *mah lehanach sheken ein bam bedalut*, those two have no *oleh v'yored* option for the poor, while *chatas metzora* does allow a lesser offering for someone impoverished; perhaps that leniency means *chatas metzora* carries lighter requirements.

In every configuration, the third *chatas* cannot be learned from the other two, and therefore every one of the three needed its own *pesukim* to establish the *lishmah* requirement independently. This means none of the *pesukim* are superfluous, and therefore none of them can be used to teach *me'akev*.

### Shelamim Connection

The *gemara* resolves the impasse by returning to a *pasuk* cited earlier: *zos hatorah la'olah u'laminchah v'lachatat v'la'asham v'lamiluim u'lizevach hashelamim* — this is the teaching for the *olah*, the *minchah*, the *chatas*, the *asham*, the *miluim*, and the *zevach hashelamim*. *Hikishu hakasuv leshelamim* — the verse compares *chatas* to *shelamim*. Just as *shelamim* requires *lishmah* and *l'shem ba'alim* at least *lechatchila* for both *shinui kodesh* and *shinui ba'alim* — as established in earlier discussions — the same applies to *chatas*. This *hekesh* to *shelamim* is the foundation that all *korbanos*, including *chatas*, must be performed *lishmah* and *l'shem ba'alim* at least *lechatchila*. That is our starting point.

### Extra Pesukim Teach Me'a…

Once that baseline is established through the connection to *shelamim*, the individual *pesukim* by each *chatas* category — *chatas chelev*, *chatas nazir*, and *chatas metzora* — are no longer needed to teach the *lechatchila* requirement, since that is already known. Each of those sets of *pesukim* is now available to teach something additional: *hanach kra'ei l'akeiv* — those verses come to teach that the requirement is *me'akev*. If you fail to perform the *avodah* *lishmah* or *l'shem ba'alim* in any of these *chatas* categories, the *korban* is *pasul*.

### All Five Categories

The *gemara* now traces how all five categories of *chatas* are covered. *Chatas chelev* — the standard sin offering — has its own *pesukim* stating *l'chatat*, which now teach *me'akev*. *Chatas nazir* and *chatas metzora* similarly have their own *pesukim* teaching *me'akev*. *Chatas avodas kochavim* — the *chatas* brought for idolatry — can be learned directly from *chatas chelev*, since both involve a *karet*-level transgression; if it is *me'akev* by *chatas chelev*, it is likewise *me'akev* for the *chatas* of *avodas kochavim*.

The remaining category — the *chatas oleh v'yored* found at the end of *Parshas Vayikra*, covering cases such as a false oath, failure to testify, or *tumah* in the *Beis HaMikdash* — does not have its own explicit *pesukim* establishing *me'akev*. Here the *gemara* applies a *tzad hashaveh*: since across all the other *chatas* categories — *chatas chelev*, *chatas nazir*, *chatas metzora*, and *chatas avodas kochavim* — *lishmah* is *me'akev*, one can infer by logical equivalence that the same applies to the *chatas oleh v'yored* as well. *Kulhu asyan b'ma hatzad* — all of them together support the inference.

There is also an underlying logic to why *me'akev* applies specifically to *chatas*: the *chatas* is inherently bound to its owner and their particular sin. The mechanism of *lo olah l'ba'alim l'shem chovah* — which ordinarily allows a *korban* performed *shelo lishmah* to be offered even if it doesn't count toward the owner's obligation — has no meaningful application to a *chatas*, since a *chatas* disconnected from its owner and their transgression loses its essential character entirely. This is also the explanation behind the category of *chatasos meisos* — *chatasos* that must be left to die once atonement has been achieved through other means — and it explains why a *chatas* performed *shelo lishmah* is not merely unhelpful but fully *pasul*.

## Pesach Overview

### Pesach Mishnah

The *gemara* now turns to the second exception identified in the *mishnah*: the *pesach* offering. A *baraisa* is cited: *tanu rabbanan*, *pesach bizmano lishmo kasher shelo lishmo pasul* — the *pesach* slaughtered at its proper time, from *chatzos* on the fourteenth of *Nissan*, if done *lishmo* is *kasher*, and if done *shelo lishmo* it is *pasul*.

This parallels *chatas*: when the *pesach* is performed at the correct time but with the wrong intent — for a different *korban* — it is entirely invalid.

### Pesach Becomes Shelamim

But the *mishnah* introduces a further ruling that applies when it is *not* the proper time — any time other than the fourteenth from *chatzos* onward. *Uv'she'ar yemos hashanah lishmo pasul shelo lishmo kasher* — during the rest of the year, the reverse holds. If it is slaughtered as a *pesach*, it is *pasul*, since the *Torah* repeats multiple times that the *pesach* must be slaughtered on the fourteenth after *chatzos*, and bringing it as a *pesach* at the wrong time is simply invalid. But if it is slaughtered for something other than *pesach* — thereby *oker hashem*, uprooting its *pesach* status — then it is *kasher*, and it becomes a *shelamim*.

---

## Pesach to Shelamim

### V'Im Pasuk

The *gemara* asks: *minalan hanei mili* — where do we learn that when a *pesach shelo bizmano* is slaughtered *shelo lishmo* it is *kasher*? The father of Shmuel answers that the *pasuk* states *v'im min hatzon korbano l'zevach shelamim la'Hashem*, "and if from the sheep is his offering as a *zevach shelamim* to God." This appears at the beginning of *Vayikra* in the context of *shelamim* offerings. The *drasha* proceeds: *davar haba min hatzon* — a type of *korban* that comes exclusively from sheep — this is the *pesach*, which is restricted to *tzon* (sheep or goats). *Yehei l'zevach shelamim* — such a *korban* brought outside its proper time shall be a *shelamim*. Since a *pesach* cannot be a *shelamim* on the fourteenth of *Nissan* after *chatzos*, the verse must refer to a *pesach* brought at any other time of year.

### Kavana Scope

One might object: perhaps the verse means only that if one explicitly had *kavana* for *shelamim*, it becomes a *shelamim*, but not if one had *kavana* for something else entirely. If one slaughtered the *pesach* with intent for an *olah* or a *chatas*, perhaps it should be *pasul*?

This question is not merely technical. The underlying mechanism would be: intent for something other than *pesach* uproots its *pesach* status, and because of its fundamental *kedusha* as something eaten by the owners in *Yerushalayim*, it gravitates toward becoming a *shelamim*. But that mechanism is not obvious; one might assume that only explicit *kavana* for *shelamim* could produce this result.

### Grammar L'Zevach

Rav Ila in the name of Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: *l'zevach l'rabot kol zavach* — the word *l'zevach* functions as a *ribuy*, an expansion, including any type of *zevach*. The full verse then reads: if it is a *korban* of sheep (*i.e.*, a *pesach*) — *l'zevach*, for any *zevach* — *shelamim*, it becomes a *shelamim*. Any form of *shelo lishmah* intent transforms the *pesach* into a *shelamim*.

However, the *gemara* presses further. If *l'zevach* is read as a *ribuy*, one might ask: why does it become specifically a *shelamim*? Why not become whatever *korban* the *shochet* had in mind — an *olah* if intent was for *olah*, a *chatas* if intent was for *chatas*? The *gemara* considers whether *l'zevach shelamim* should be read as *klal u'prat* — a general statement (*l'zevach*) followed by a specific (*shelamim*) — in which case the rule *ein baklal ella mah shebaprat* would restrict the result to *shelamim* only when one explicitly intended *shelamim*.

### Klal U'Prat

Rav Yaakov miNehar Pekod challenges the *klal u'prat v'klal* reading on its own terms. A valid *klal u'prat v'klal* requires that the two *klalim* be of comparable scope, but here they are not. The first *klal*, *l'zevach*, encompasses only *zvachim* — slaughtered mammals. The second *klal*, *l'Hashem*, is far broader, potentially including *olos ha'of*, *menachos*, and any form of *korban* brought to God. If the two *klalim* are of unequal scope, the *klal u'prat v'klal* analysis does not function properly.

The *gemara* responds that this *tanna* follows the school of Rabbi Yishmael, which takes a looser approach to *klal u'prat* and allows the inference even when the *klalim* are not precisely matched.

### Ribuy Resolution

Ultimately, the *gemara* sets aside the *klal u'prat v'klal* analysis and returns to the reading of *l'zevach* as a *ribuy*. As Rashi explains, whether a term functions as a *klal* or as a *ribuy* depends on the linguistic context and one's hermeneutical methodology. Here, reading *l'zevach* as a *ribuy* — as simply expanding the scope of which *zevach* intent suffices — the verse teaches that any *zevach* intent transforms the *pesach*. And *shelamim* specifies what it becomes.

The *gemara* asks one final question on this reading: if *l'zevach* is a *ribuy* and no longer part of a structured *klal u'prat v'klal* sentence, how do we still know that the result is *shelamim* rather than whatever *korban* the *shochet* intended? The answer to this question is provided in the next section.

## Why Shelamim

### Kodshim Kalim

Rav Avin supplies the principle: *dochin kodshim ne'echalim etzel kodshim ne'echalim v'ein dochin kodshim ne'echalim etzel kodshim she'einam ne'echalim* — *kodshim* that are eaten are pushed toward other *kodshim* that are eaten, and they are not pushed toward *kodshim* that are not eaten. A *pesach* is eaten by its owners; an *olah* is entirely consumed on the *mizbeach* and not eaten by people. Therefore, even if one had *kavana* for an *olah*, the *pesach* will not become an *olah* — it will gravitate toward something that is eaten, namely *shelamim*.

Rabbi Yose berebi Avin states the principle more precisely: *dochin kodshim kalim etzel kodshim kalim v'ein dochin kodshim kalim etzel kodshei kodshim* — items of *kodshim kalim* status (eaten throughout *Yerushalayim*) are pushed toward other *kodshim kalim*, and not toward *kodshei kodshim*. A *pesach* is *kodshim kalim*, and *shelamim* is *kodshim kalim*, and therefore the *pesach* becomes a *shelamim*.

### Maaser Objection

Rabbi Yitzchak berebi Svara raises an objection: *eima shachatei l'sheim Maaser l'havei Maaser* — why not say that if one slaughtered the *pesach* with intent for *maaser*, it should become *maaser*? *Maaser* is also *kodshim kalim*, eaten by the owners in *Yerushalayim*. And the distinction matters practically: if treated as *maaser*, it would not require *nesachim* — the accompanying wine libations — and one would receive *malkus* for selling it, given the specific *lav* against redeeming *maaser* improperly.

### Ha'Asiri Answer

The *gemara* answers by citing the *pasuk* *ha'asiri yihiyeh kodesh* — the tenth shall be holy. The *drasha* is: *zeh Maaser v'ein acher Maaser* — only the item that is literally the tenth in a sequence acquires *maaser* status. Something like a *korban pesach*, which does not emerge from a tithing process, cannot spontaneously become *maaser* regardless of the *kavana* at the time of *shechitah*. Therefore, the only available destination for the displaced *pesach* is *shelamim*.
