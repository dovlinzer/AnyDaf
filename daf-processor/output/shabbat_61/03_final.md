# Shabbat 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shoe Order

### Single Shoe Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּיָחִיד בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בְּרַגְלוֹ מַכָּה. הָא יֵשׁ בְּרַגְלוֹ מַכָּה — נָפֵיק. בְּהֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ נָפֵיק? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ מַכָּה. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר, סַנְדָּל לְשׁוּם צַעַר עֲבִיד. וְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: בְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ מַכָּה. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר, לְשׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג עֲבִיד, וְזוֹ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ מַכָּה — מַכָּתָהּ מוֹכַחַת עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **And** he may **not** go out **with a single** sandal **when there is no wound on his foot.** By inference: **If there is a wound on his foot, he may go out** with one sandal. In that case, **with** a sandal **on which of** his feet **does he go out? Rav Huna said:** With a sandal **on the** foot **that has a wound on it. Apparently, he holds: A sandal is made for the purpose** of avoiding **pain.** Typically, a person wears sandals only in order to avoid the pain of walking on stones and the like. When he is seen with only one sandal, it is clear that he is oblivious to that pain and the only reason that he is wearing the sandal is due to the wound on his foot. Consequently, no one will suspect that he went out wearing two sandals and that if he is wearing one, he must be carrying the other one. **And Ḥiyya bar Rav said:** He goes out with a sandal **on the** foot **that does not have a wound on it. Apparently, he holds** that the sandal is **made for the purpose of** providing **comfort,** and he wears it on his healthy foot. **And** it does not arouse suspicion because, with regard to **that** foot **on which there is a wound, its wound indicates** that he is unable to wear a sandal on **that** foot, and it is clear that he left the other sandal at home.

The daf opens at the very bottom of 60b with the ruling that an individual may not wear a single shoe when there is no wound on his leg. Rashi offers two reasons: passersby will suspect him of carrying the other shoe, or they will mock him, prompting him to remove the one shoe he is wearing and carry it. The *gemara* then moves to the top of 61a and asks: when a person does have a wound on his leg and is thus permitted to go out wearing only one shoe, which foot wears it — the wounded leg or the healthy one?

Rav Huna rules that one wears the shoe on the leg with the wound. His underlying assumption is that a *sandal* is fundamentally designed to prevent pain. When a wound is present, one wants specifically to protect the painful foot from any further discomfort. Given this rationale, people readily understand why the shoe is on the wounded leg, and there is no reason to mock the wearer or to suspect him of carrying the other shoe.

Chiya bar Rav disagrees and rules that one wears the shoe on the leg without the wound. His underlying assumption is the opposite: a shoe is worn primarily for pleasure and comfort, not merely to prevent pain. When a foot is wounded, putting a shoe on it may in fact cause additional discomfort, so one naturally leaves the wounded foot bare. The wound itself (*makaso mocheches aleha*) makes the situation self-evident — people see the wound and immediately understand why the shoe is absent from that foot. There is accordingly no concern that onlookers will mock the wearer or suspect carrying.

As noted, this is a peculiar debate. It presupposes a single explanation for why people wear shoes, and a single template for how wounds interact with that explanation. In reality, different people, different types of sandals, and different types of wounds might point in very different directions. Yet the *gemara* treats it as a matter of binding *halacha*, and so it stands.

### R. Yochanan's Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַב הוּנָא. דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַב שֶׁמֶן בַּר אַבָּא: הַב לִי מְסָנַאי. יְהַב לֵיהּ דְּיָמִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ עֲשִׂיתוֹ מַכָּה. וְדִילְמָא כְּחִיָּיא בַּר רַב סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: עָשִׂיתָ שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל מַכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan also holds that** the opinion of **Rav Huna,** which maintains that one only wears sandals to avoid pain, is correct. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Rav Shemen bar Abba,** his attendant: **Give me my sandal. He gave him the right** sandal. **He said to him: You have rendered** this foot as one with **a wound.** In Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion, one must always put on his left shoe first. One who puts on the right shoe first is no longer permitted to put on the left shoe. By handing him his right sandal, he is forcing Rabbi Yoḥanan to go out with one sandal, leading onlookers to conclude that he has a wound on that foot. That incident corresponds to Rav Huna's opinion that one wears the sandal on the wounded foot. The Gemara rejects this: **And perhaps, he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥiyya bar Rav,** which maintains that one wears the sandal on the healthy foot, **and he is saying as follows:** By handing me my right shoe, **you have rendered** my left foot, on which I have no shoe, as one with **a wound.** No proof can be cited from that incident, as Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion cannot be ascertained from the exchange with his attendant.

The *gemara* then notes that Rabbi Yochanan agrees with Rav Huna's position — that a person wearing one shoe due to a wound wears it on the wounded leg. The proof is an incident: Rabbi Yochanan said to Rav Shemen bar Abba, "Give me my shoes." Rav Shemen bar Abba handed him the right shoe first. Rabbi Yochanan responded, "You have made a wound in my right leg" (*asita maka*).

The logic is as follows. The *gemara* will shortly establish that there is a prescribed order for putting on shoes. According to Rabbi Yochanan, one must put on the left shoe before the right. If Rav Shemen bar Abba hands him only the right shoe, intending for him to wear only that one, then Rabbi Yochanan cannot simply put it on — doing so would mean beginning with the right and violating the proper order. The only circumstance under which one wears a single shoe on the right foot is if the right foot bears a wound, for that would justify departing from normal practice. Hence: "You have turned my right leg into a wounded leg."

The *gemara* raises an objection: perhaps Rabbi Yochanan actually holds like Chiya bar Rav, in which case what he really meant was "you have made it as though my left leg has a wound" — i.e., you are forcing me to wear a single shoe, and depending on which opinion one follows, a single shoe signals a wound on whichever leg happens not to be wearing it. The *gemara* does not resolve which leg exactly bears the implied wound, leaving the underlying dispute technically unresolved.

### Putting On Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ. דַּאֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּתְפִילִּין כָּךְ מִנְעָלִין. מַה תְּפִילִּין בִּשְׂמֹאל, אַף מִנְעָלִין בִּשְׂמֹאל. מֵיתִיבִי: כְּשֶׁהוּא נוֹעֵל — נוֹעֵל שֶׁל יָמִין וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹעֵל שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan follows his** regular line of **reasoning. As Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** Just **as** one dons **phylacteries, so too,** one puts on **shoes. Just as phylacteries are** placed **on the left** arm, **so too,** when putting on **shoes** one begins **with the left** foot. The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion from a *baraita*: **When one puts on** his **shoes, he puts on the right shoe** first **and afterward puts on the left shoe.**

From here the *gemara* turns to the broader question of the proper order for putting on shoes. Rabbi Yochanan states explicitly: *k'tefillin kach min'alim* — just as with *tefillin*, so with shoes. Just as *tefillin* are placed on the left arm, one puts the left shoe on first. The *gemara* immediately challenges this from a *baraita*: "When one puts on shoes, one puts on the right and then the left." This directly contradicts Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that the left precedes the right. Rav Yosef proposes a simple resolution: since there is one *baraita* mandating right-first and Rabbi Yochanan mandating left-first, one may follow either approach — *d'avi hachi avid, u'd'avi hachi avid*. Tosafot frames it as follows: if one is drawn to the analogy with *tefillin*, one adopts Rabbi Yochanan's approach; if one wishes to follow the general *halakhic* principle favoring the right, one follows the *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַשְׁתָּא דְּתַנְיָא הָכִי, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי, דַּעֲבַד הָכִי — עֲבַד, וְדַעֲבַד הָכִי — עֲבַד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דִּילְמָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָא מַתְנִיתִין לָא הֲוָה שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ, וְאִי הֲוָה שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ הֲוָה הָדַר בֵּיהּ. וְאִי נָמֵי שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ, וְקָסָבַר אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּאוֹתָהּ מִשְׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Now that it was taught** in a *baraita* in **this** manner, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** the *halakha* in **that** manner, **one** who **acted this way acted** properly, **and one who acted that way acted** properly, as each custom has a basis. **Abaye said to him:** Why is the Gemara certain that Rabbi Yoḥanan disagrees with the *baraita*? **Perhaps Rabbi Yoḥanan had not heard this *baraita*, and had he heard it, he would have retracted** his opinion. **And** even **if he heard it,** perhaps he heard it **and held that the *halakha* is not in accordance with that mishna.** In any case, it is necessary to rule in accordance with one of the opinions.

Abaye, however, objects to this easy pluralism. Perhaps Rabbi Yochanan was simply unaware of the *baraita*, and had he known it he would have retracted his ruling. Conversely, perhaps he was aware of it and deliberately rejected it. If so, we cannot simply declare both equally valid — one of them may be definitively correct and we have not yet determined which.

### Mar bar Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: יְרֵא שָׁמַיִם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן. וּמַנּוּ? — מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבְנָא. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? — סָיֵים דְּיַמִּינֵיהּ וְלָא קָטַר, וְסָיֵים דִּשְׂמָאלֵיהּ וְקָטַר, וַהֲדַר קָטַר דְּיַמִּינֵיהּ. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חֲזֵינָא לְרַב כָּהֲנָא דְּלָא קָפֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: And one who fears Heaven fulfills both** opinions. **And who is this** God-fearing person? **Mar, son of Rabbana. How does he conduct** himself? **He puts on his right** shoe **and does not tie** the laces. **And** then **he puts on his left** shoe **and ties** it, **and** then afterward **ties** the laces **of his right** shoe. **Rav Ashi said: I saw that Rav Kahana was not particular** with regard to the order in which he put on his shoes.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers the resolution: a person who fears Heaven (*yere shamayim*) will satisfy both rulings simultaneously. The example given is Mar bar Ravina. What did he do? He would put on his right shoe first — but leave it untied. Then he would put on his left shoe and tie it. Only then would he return to tie the right shoe.

The logic is elegant. The *baraita* speaks of the order of putting on (*no'el*), which should be right then left. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, properly understood, is about tying (*katar*) — and the tying of the left shoe corresponds to the binding of *tefillin* on the left arm, which is performed first. Mar bar Ravina's practice therefore honors both: he puts on the right first (satisfying the *baraita*'s preference for the right), then the left, ties the left (corresponding to *tefillin*), and finally ties the right. This ruling is codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*: for shoes without laces, one puts on right then left; for shoes that are tied, one ties the left first and then the right.

### Daily Order & Consciousness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁהוּא נוֹעֵל — נוֹעֵל שֶׁל יָמִין וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹעֵל שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל. כְּשֶׁהוּא חוֹלֵץ — חוֹלֵץ שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל וְאַחַר כָּךְ חוֹלֵץ שֶׁל יָמִין. כְּשֶׁהוּא רוֹחֵץ — רוֹחֵץ שֶׁל יָמִין וְאַחַר כָּךְ רוֹחֵץ שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל. כְּשֶׁהוּא סָךְ — סָךְ שֶׁל יָמִין וְאַחַר כָּךְ שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל. וְהָרוֹצֶה לָסוּךְ כׇּל גּוּפוֹ — סָךְ רֹאשׁוֹ תְּחִילָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מֶלֶךְ עַל כׇּל אֵיבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: When one puts on** his **shoes, he puts on the right shoe** first **and afterward puts on the left shoe** because the right always takes precedence. **When he removes them, he removes the left and afterward he removes the right,** so that the right shoe will remain on the foot longer. **When one washes** his feet, **he washes the right** first **and afterward he washes the left. And one who wishes to spread oil** on his feet **spreads oil on the right** first **and afterward spreads oil on the left. And one who wishes to spread oil on his entire body, spreads oil on his head first because it is the king of all his** other **limbs.**

The *baraita* continues: when bathing, one washes the right side first and then the left. When anointing with oil, one anoints the right side first and then the left. And if one wishes to anoint the entire body, one begins with the head, because the head is the king over all the limbs (*shemelech al kol ivarav*).

At first glance this may seem trivial — is the order in which one bathes or puts on shoes genuinely a matter of *halacha*? One might reasonably experience such rulings as an excessive burden. But the Meiri frames these *halakhot* in a way that is deeply illuminating. He writes that *talmidei chachamim* direct all their actions toward a single ultimate purpose — *libo poneh lekavanat avodat Hashem* — even when attending to their most mundane bodily needs. When one puts on shoes, the *gemara* teaches that one recites *she'asa li kol tzorki* at that very moment, expressing gratitude for the capacity to stand and walk. The order of dressing, bathing, and anointing keeps one's mind attuned to God even in these moments. The right, for the Meiri, symbolizes the proper path as opposed to the wrong one, so honoring it in each action encodes a constant moral and spiritual orientation. What might feel like the burden of *halacha* can instead become a continuous, embodied God-consciousness in which even the most trivial physical acts are transformed into moments of awareness and acknowledgment.

## Tefillin on Shabbat

### Tefillin Outdoors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בִּתְפִילִּין. אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: לָא תֵּימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא. אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא — לֹא יֵצֵא, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵיתוּיֵי בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And** he may **neither** go out **with phylacteries. Rav Safra said: Do not say** that this *halakha* is only **in accordance with** the opinion of the **one who said** that **Shabbat is not** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries,** i.e., it is prohibited to don phylacteries on Shabbat, and that is the reason that one may not go out into the public domain with them. **Rather, even according to the one who said** that **Shabbat is** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries, he may not go out** with them due to the concern **lest he come to carry them** in his hand **in the public domain,** which is prohibited by Torah law.

The *gemara* turns to *tefillin* on *Shabbat*. The *mishnah* rules that one may not wear *tefillin* outdoors on *Shabbat*, and if one does, one is not liable for a *chatat* offering. Rav Safra addresses the first clause. One might think the prohibition applies only according to the opinion that *Shabbat* is not a time for *tefillin* (*Shabbat lav zman tefillin*) — since *Shabbat* itself is a sign of the covenant, one does not add the sign of *tefillin*, and indeed one should not be wearing them at all. But Rav Safra specifies: even according to the opinion that *Shabbat* is a time for *tefillin* (*Shabbat zman tefillin*), and that one does in fact wear *tefillin* on *Shabbat*, one still should not wear them outdoors. Why? Because one may come to carry them in *reshut ha-rabim*. Rashi explains: since the bathrooms in that era were located outdoors, one might step outside wearing *tefillin*, then be forced to remove them before entering the bathroom, and then fail to replace them immediately — carrying them instead through the public domain.

### Malbush Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בִּתְפִילִּין. אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: לָא תֵּימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְמַאן דְּאָמַר שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא. אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא — לֹא יֵצֵא, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵיתוּיֵי בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. וְלֹא בִּתְפִילִּין. אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: לָא תֵּימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְמַאן דְּאָמַר שַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב חַטָּאת. מַאי טַעְמָא? — דֶּרֶךְ מַלְבּוּשׁ עֲבִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And** he may **neither** go out **with phylacteries. Rav Safra said: Do not say** that this *halakha* is only **in accordance with** the opinion of the **one who said** that **Shabbat is not** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries,** i.e., it is prohibited to don phylacteries on Shabbat, and that is the reason that one may not go out into the public domain with them. **Rather, even according to the one who said** that **Shabbat is** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries, he may not go out** with them due to the concern **lest he come to carry them** in his hand **in the public domain,** which is prohibited by Torah law. There is another aspect to address. **We learned in the mishna: And** he may **neither** go out **with phylacteries. Rav Safra said: Do not say that** this *halakha* is only **in accordance with** the opinion **one who said** that **Shabbat is** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries,** and therefore he does not violate a Torah prohibition by going out into the public domain with phylacteries and is not liable to bring a sin-offering. **Rather, even according to** the opinion of **one who said that Shabbat is not** an appropriate **time** to don **phylacteries, he is not liable to bring a sin-offering. What is the reason** that he is exempt? Donning phylacteries is **performed in the manner of** wearing **a garment** or an ornament. Although one may not use phylacteries on Shabbat, there is no Torah prohibition against moving them.

Rav Safra then addresses the second clause: even one who wore *tefillin* outdoors has not incurred biblical liability. This *chiddush* extends in the other direction as well. One might think that at minimum, according to the opinion that *Shabbat lav zman tefillin* — that one is positively prohibited from wearing *tefillin* on *Shabbat* — wearing them in *reshut ha-rabim* should constitute a biblical act of carrying. Yet Rav Safra says no: even according to that opinion, one is exempt. Why? *Derech malbush avidtei* — because this is the manner of wearing, not the manner of carrying. The *tefillin* are worn on the body, not transported from one place to another.

This requires careful analysis, because we know from other *sugyot* that not everything worn on the body is automatically classified as *malbush*. The classic counterexample is the *signet ring*: a woman who carries a signet ring on her finger on *Shabbat* has committed a biblical violation, even though the ring is physically on her body. The Ritva explains the distinction as follows. What matters is how the object is regarded during the weekdays. During the week, *tefillin* are universally understood as something one *wears* — *tefillin* are donned, not carried. A signet ring worn by a woman, by contrast, was not within the societal norms of feminine dress in that era; it was understood as a means of conveying the ring from place to place on one's person, not as an adornment. Because the signet ring was perceived as a mode of conveyance during the week, it retains that character on *Shabbat* as well. *Tefillin*, perceived as a form of wearing during the week, are classified as *malbush* even on *Shabbat* when they ought not to be worn.

One can also frame the distinction this way: the defining question is not whether the object formally counts as clothing or jewelry, but whether the wearer's purpose is *conveyance* or *wearing*. When one carries a signet ring on one's finger, the point is to bring it somewhere for use; when one wears *tefillin*, the point is to have them on one's body, not to transport them. This purposive understanding of *malbush* will recur as the *gemara* continues.

## Amulet Expertise

### Three-Fold Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּקָמֵיעַ בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִן הַמּוּמְחֶה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא תֵּימָא עַד דְּמוּמְחֶה גַּבְרָא וּמוּמְחֶה קָמֵיעַ, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּמוּמְחֶה גַּבְרָא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מוּמְחֶה קָמֵיעַ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: "וְלֹא בְּקָמֵיעַ בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִן הַמּוּמְחֶה", וְלָא קָתָנֵי: "בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוּמְחֶה". שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ קָמֵיעַ מוּמְחֶה — כֹּל שֶׁרִיפֵּא, וְשָׁנָה, וְשִׁלֵּשׁ. אֶחָד קָמֵיעַ שֶׁל כְּתָב וְאֶחָד קָמֵיעַ שֶׁל עִיקָּרִין, אֶחָד חוֹלֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סַכָּנָה וְאֶחָד חוֹלֶה שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סַכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Nor with an amulet when it is not from an expert. Rav Pappa said: Do not say** that the meaning of the mishna is that one may **only** go out with an amulet **if the person** who wrote it **is an expert and the amulet** has proven **effective. Rather, if the person** who wrote it **is an expert, even though the amulet** has **not** proven **effective,** he may go out with it. The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches: Nor with an amulet when it is not from an expert, and it does not teach: When** the amulet **is not effective.** Apparently, it is sufficient if the writer of the amulet is an expert, even if the effectiveness of the amulet has not been proven. The Gemara comments: Indeed, **learn from it. The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **What is an effective amulet?** It is **any** amulet **that healed** one person once, **and** healed him **again, and** healed him **a third** time. That is the criterion for an effective amulet, and it applies to **both a written amulet and an amulet of** herbal **roots; both** if it has proven effective in healing **a sick person who is dangerously** ill, **and** if it has proven effective in healing **a sick person who is not dangerously** ill. It is permitted to go out with these types of amulets on Shabbat.

The *mishnah* rules that one may not wear a *kame'a* (*kameia*, an amulet) that is not from an expert (*mumcheh*), but if one did, one has not incurred a *chatat*; and one is permitted *lechatchila* to wear an expert amulet on *Shabbat*. Rav Papa's statement addresses how expertise is established.

First, though, the *gemara* presents the *baraita* that defines a *mumcheh kame'a*: any amulet that has healed once, twice, and three times. Whether it is an amulet of writing (*kameia shel ketav*) — containing inscriptions, incantations, or *pesukim* — or an amulet of herbs (*kameia shel ikkarin*), the criterion is the same. Whether the wearer faces a life-threatening condition (*choleh she-yesh bo sakanah*) or not, and indeed even where the person has not yet fallen ill but has reason to fear a particular condition — for instance, a family history of epilepsy (*nichpeh*) — wearing such a proven amulet is permitted. The *gemara* further specifies that one may adjust and retie the amulet even in *reshut ha-rabim*, so long as one does not attach it to a bracelet or ring and go out in that fashion, which would give the misleading appearance that it is being worn as jewelry — a category quite different from a therapeutic device.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא שֶׁנִּכְפָּה, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּפֶה. וְקוֹשֵׁר וּמַתִּיר אֲפִילּוּ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִקְשְׁרֶנּוּ בְּשֵׁיר וּבְטַבַּעַת וְיֵצֵא בּוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. מִשּׁוּם מַרְאִית הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** And an amulet was **not** only permitted in a case **where** one has already **fallen** due to epilepsy and wears the amulet in order to prevent an additional fall. **Rather,** even if one has never fallen, and he wears the amulet so that he **will not** contract the illness and **fall,** he is permitted to go out with it on Shabbat is permitted. **And he may tie and untie** it **even in the public domain, as long as he does not tie it to a bracelet or a ring and go out with it into the public domain.** The reason for the prohibition is **due to** the **appearance** of transgression, as, in that case, it appears that he is wearing the amulet strictly for ornamental purposes, which is prohibited.

The rationale for permitting the wearing of a *mumcheh kame'a* on *Shabbat* follows the same logic as for *tefillin*: it falls within the *malbush* category. One who wears such an amulet will not casually remove it, and thus there is no concern that removal will lead to carrying. This principle, moreover, extends beyond amulets: bandages and other therapeutic objects worn on the body for the sake of healing are likewise not classified as carrying on *Shabbat*. A garment protects the body; these objects preserve the body's health. Whether one calls that *malbush* strictly speaking or understands it more broadly as something worn on the body rather than conveyed through it, the halakhic result is the same.

### Writer vs. Amulet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ קָמֵיעַ מוּמְחֶה — כֹּל שֶׁרִיפֵּא שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם כְּאֶחָד! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — לִמְּחוֹיֵי גַּבְרָא, הָא — לִמְּחוֹיֵי קְמִיעָא. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי תְּלָתָא קְמֵיעֵי לִתְלָתָא גַּבְרֵי תְּלָתָא תְּלָתָא זִימְנֵי — אִיתְמַחִי גַּבְרָא וְאִתְמַחִי קָמֵיעַ. תְּלָתָא קְמֵיעֵי לִתְלָתָא גַּבְרֵי חַד חַד זִימְנָא — גַּבְרָא אִיתְמַחִי, קְמִיעָא לָא אִיתְמַחִי. חַד קָמֵיעַ לִתְלָתָא גַּבְרֵי — קְמִיעָא אִיתְמַחִי, גַּבְרָא לָא אִיתְמַחִי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the definition of an effective amulet as one which healed one person three times, the Gemara raises an objection. **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Which is an effective amulet; any** amulet **that healed three people as one?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This,** where it was taught in the *baraita* that the amulet must have healed three different people, is referring **to proving the expertise of the man** who wrote it. Once his amulets have proven themselves by healing three different people stricken with different illnesses, clearly the one who wrote them is an expert. **That,** where it was taught in the *Tosefta* that even if the amulet healed one person three times, is referring **to proving that the amulet is effective** in fulfilling its designated purpose. **Rav Pappa said:** It is **obvious to me** in a case where **three amulets** were written **for three people** and effectively healed **each three times** that both **the man** who wrote them **is proven an expert and the amulet is proven effective.** Likewise, it is obvious to me that in the case of one who writes **three amulets for three people** and healed **each one time, the man is proven** to be **an expert;** however, **the amulet is not proven effective.** Similarly, if one wrote **one amulet for three people** and it healed them, **the amulet is proven effective,** while **the man** who wrote it **is not** thereby **proven an expert.**

Rav Papa adds a key precision: one need not establish *both* that the writer is an expert *and* that the specific amulet has been proven effective. The requirement is met if *either* is established. This is inferred linguistically from the *mishnah*'s phrasing: it prohibits a *kame'a* that is not *min ha-mumcheh* — from a proven expert — rather than a *kame'a* that is not itself *mumcheh*. The focus is on the writer. So long as the writer has been validated, one may rely on his amulet even if that particular formula has not independently been tested three times.

Rav Papa then sets out the matrix of cases systematically. Where one writer has written three amulets for three different people, each for a different ailment, and has done so at three separate occasions — *tlat kmeiei litlat gavrei tlat tlata zimni* — both the writer and the specific formulas are established: the writer, because he has succeeded across three different circumstances; each formula, because it succeeded three times for its particular ailment. This is the fullest proof.

Where one writer has written three amulets for three different people but each only once — *tlat kmeiei litlat gavrei chad chad zimna* — the writer is established (*gavra itmachi*) because he has successfully treated three different conditions for three different individuals, demonstrating broad competence. But no single formula has been proven three times, so the amulet as such is not established (*kamea lo itmachi*): one could not write that same formula without this particular expert and rely on it.

Where one amulet formula was written for three different people with the same ailment — *chad kamea litlat gavrei* — the formula is established (*kamea itmachi*) because it has worked three times. But the writer is not established (*gavra lo itmachi*), since all he has demonstrated is mastery of a single formula across the same condition.

Any one of these forms of proof — writer proven or formula proven — suffices to permit wearing the amulet on *Shabbat*.

### Three Ailments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: תְּלָתָא קְמֵיעֵי לְחַד גַּבְרָא מַאי? קְמִיעָא וַדַּאי לָא אִיתְמַחִי. גַּבְרָא אִיתְמַחִי, אוֹ לָא אִיתְמַחִי? מִי אָמְרִינַן הָא אַסִּי לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא מַזָּלָא דְּהַאי גַּבְרָא הוּא דְּקָא מְקַבֵּל כְּתָבָא. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: Three amulets for one person, what** is the status of the amulet and the one who wrote it in that case? **The amulet is certainly not proven effective;** however, with regard to **the man** who wrote it, **is he proven an expert or is he not proven an expert?** This is the dilemma: **Do we say** that the person is an expert since **the amulet** that he wrote **healed** the person who was ill? **Or, perhaps** we say that **it was the fortune of that** sick **man who received** the influence of **the writing** of the amulet, but a different person would not be healed? The Gemara concludes: Let this dilemma **stand** unresolved.

Rav Papa closes with an unresolved question: what if the same person suffered three different ailments and a writer composed three different amulets for him, each of which proved effective? There is no question that no single formula has been proven (*kamea vadai lo itmachi*), since each was used only once. The question is whether the writer is considered proven. On one hand, he has successfully treated three different conditions under three different circumstances — surely this demonstrates his competence. On the other hand, perhaps the success is attributable to a particular quality of this one recipient — *mazla d'hai gavra hu d'ka mekabbel ktava* — a kind of personal receptivity to written prescription, which would tell us nothing about how his amulets would perform for other people. The *gemara* leaves this as *teiku*.

## Amulet Efficacy Debate

### Rambam's Rationalism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: קְמֵיעִין יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשָּׁה, אוֹ דִילְמָא אֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשָּׁה? לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? אִילֵּימָא לְאַצּוֹלִינְהוּ מִפְּנֵי הַדְּלֵיקָה, תָּא שְׁמַע: הַבְּרָכוֹת וְהַקְּמֵיעִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן אוֹתִיּוֹת וּמֵעִנְיָנוֹת הַרְבֵּה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה — אֵין מַצִּילִין אוֹתָן מִפְּנֵי הַדְּלֵיקָה, וְנִשְׂרָפִים בִּמְקוֹמָן! אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן גְּנִיזָה. תָּא שְׁמַע: הָיָה כָּתוּב עַל יְדוֹת הַכֵּלִים וְעַל כַּרְעֵי הַמִּטָּה — יָגוֹד וְיִגְנְזֶנּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Do amulets have an element of sanctity, or perhaps they have no element of sanctity?** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this dilemma relevant? **If you say** it is relevant **with regard to rescuing them from fire** on Shabbat, there is a clear resolution to the dilemma. **Come** and **hear** what was taught: **The blessings and the amulets, even though there are letters** of holy names **and many matters that are in the Torah** written in them, **one may not rescue them from the fire, and they burn in their place.** **Rather,** the dilemma is relevant with regard **to the matter of interment** of sacred documents. Must an amulet no longer in use be buried, or may it be discarded? However, with regard to the matter of interment as well, **come** and **hear** a resolution from what was taught: If one of the names of God **was written** even **on the handles of the vessels and** even **on legs of the bed, he must cut off** the name **and bury it,** as one must be exacting with regard to the name of God, wherever it is written.

Before engaging the theoretical debate about amulet efficacy, one preliminary clarification is necessary. Why is a *mumcheh kame'a* permitted while a non-expert one is not? The most natural explanation — at least for those who accepted efficacy — is that if the amulet is known to work, the wearer will be reluctant to remove it, and the concern that he will take it off and come to carry it is substantially diminished. Conversely, if he is uncertain of the amulet's power, he may remove it casually. The Meiri, notably, understands the entire category along psychosomatic lines: people who believe an amulet is effective will not remove it, and that belief itself is sufficient to classify the wearing as genuine *malbush* rather than carrying.

The deeper question — one that the *gemara* does not sidestep — is whether the rabbis of the *gemara* believed these amulets actually worked, and how later authorities, particularly rationalist ones, squared their own skepticism with the *gemara*'s legislation.

The Rambam addresses this in several places. In *Hilchos Shabbos* 19:13-14, he rules that one may go out wearing various therapeutic objects — including a bandage on a wound, garlic or onion peel over a sore, and, remarkably, the tooth of a fox, a nail from a crucifixion, and similar items — *u-vchol davar she-tolin oso mishum refuah*, anything worn for the purpose of healing, provided that doctors say it is effective (*v'hu she-yomru ha-rofim she-hu mo'il*). He then rules that an expert *kame'a* may be worn, and that even a non-expert one worn on *Shabbat* involves no biblical liability — *patur mipnei she-hotzio derech malbush*.

Yet elsewhere — most notably in his *Perush ha-Mishnah* — the Rambam states that remedies of the type called *segulot* may not be used even to override a *mitzvah*, such as eating on *Yom Kippur* for a dangerously ill person. Only treatments that are rationally grounded and empirically verifiable may justify overriding a Torah obligation: *she-hu davar barur she-ha-higayon ve-ha-nisayon ha-pashut mechayvim oso* — something that logic and straightforward experience confirm. *Aval ha-ripui b'sgulah lo* — but a cure based on *segulot* cannot. Such remedies, he writes, are weak in their rationale (*inyanam chalush*) and distant in their proof (*nisyono rachok*).

In the *Moreh Nevuchim*, the Rambam explains the talmudic permission to wear a nail of the gallows or a fox's tooth as follows: in those days these items were considered established by experiment, and the Torah permits as medicine everything verified by experiment, even where no rational analogy exists. They were permitted in the same way as purgatives — not because they are rationally explicable, but because they were empirically accepted in their time.

### Radvaz & Rashba

This position generates serious difficulties, noted by the Radvaz and the Rashba. The Radvaz, whose *teshuvot* address the Rambam's position at length, argues that the Rambam's permission in *Hilchos Shabbos* for such items must rest on the fact that any violation is at most rabbinic in nature — the object is worn around the body rather than carried in the hand, so at most it is a *shinui* from normal carrying, not a biblical act. On that basis, psychological benefit suffices to justify the rabbinic leniency, even without genuine medical efficacy.

The Rashba is more direct in his critique. He points out that the Rambam seems to be saying two incompatible things. On one hand, the Rambam states that remedies not grounded in rational medicine may not be used to override even a rabbinic prohibition. On the other hand, the Rambam codes as normative law that one may wear a nail from a crucifixion on *Shabbat* — which is, on its face, exactly the kind of item whose rationale is "very weak" and "distant in proof." The Rashba frames the challenge sharply: *lo yadanu eizeh nikach be-yadeinu le-ma'aseh* — we do not know which principle to act upon. Either permit the superstitious remedies and drop the rationalist requirement, or forbid the crucifixion nail as *darkei ha-Emori*. The Rashba also notes a deeper problem: on what authority does the Rambam declare that only Greek-style rational science defines what "makes sense"? If the Sages of the *gemara* attested to the efficacy of these remedies, why should Aristotelian categories of reason be the standard by which we evaluate them? The Rashba ultimately concludes that *segulot* accepted by the Sages are entirely reliable, the Rambam's rationalist critique notwithstanding.

### Gra's Response

The Vilna Gaon (*ha-Gra*) is far more blunt. He writes that while the Rambam states this position in his *Perush ha-Mishnah*, all who came after him disagreed: *kol ha-ba'im acharav chalku alav, she-harei harbeh lachashim ne'emru ba-Gemara* — the *gemara* itself contains many incantations and remedies of exactly this type, which the Sages evidently took seriously as genuinely effective. The Gra states plainly that the Rambam was led astray by philosophy — *ha-filosofia hittatu* — and that one should not, *chas ve-shalom*, be influenced by Greek rationalist categories in interpreting the *gemara*. All of these things, the Gra insists, are to be taken at face value: *kol ha-devarim hem ki-fshatam*. As someone once quipped: when the Rambam wrote that demons do not exist, all the demons disappeared; when the Gra wrote that the Rambam was wrong, they all came back.

### Modern Applications

The practical implications of this debate extend well beyond the medieval period. The *Shulchan Aruch* rules that one may recite an incantation over a scorpion bite on *Shabbat*, *ve-af al pi she-ein ha-davar mo'il klal* — even though it is not genuinely effective — *she-lo titoraf da'ato alav*, in order to prevent the victim from becoming mentally destabilized. The psychological dimension is thus explicitly invoked as a justification, though the *Shulchan Aruch* does not extend this reasoning to the wearing of amulets. The *Birkei Yosef* records a case in which someone had poisoned himself on *Shabbat* and a rabbi permitted the writing of an incantation on *Shabbat* because of *pikuach nefesh*, entering into a sustained discussion of whether a remedy that defies rational explanation may justify overriding a Torah prohibition. The *Tzitz Eliezer* similarly grapples with whether one may send a telegram to a *tzaddik* on *Shabbat* to pray on behalf of a *choleh she-yesh bo sakanah* — potentially involving a *melachah de-oraita*.

The tension becomes concrete in contemporary *shailos* involving homeopathic and Eastern medicine. These present an intermediate case: unlike amulets, which many today would regard as simply without any mechanism of efficacy, homeopathic and Eastern remedies have in some cases demonstrated effectiveness even if the mechanisms are not well understood by Western medicine. When a patient whose conventional treatments have failed wishes to pursue such a remedy — say, a preparation that involves an insect or another *davar ha-assur* in a highly attenuated form — the question of whether we trust the efficacy of an "irrational" cure becomes very real. Opinions among contemporary decisors vary, with some holding that if the patient believes it works, that suffices, while others demand a more rigorous standard of demonstrated efficacy before permitting any halakhic accommodation.

## Amulet Sanctity

### God's Name Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִיכָּנֵס בָּהֶן בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא. מַאי? יֵשׁ בָּהֶן קְדוּשָּׁה — וַאֲסִיר, אוֹ דִילְמָא אֵין בָּהֶן קְדוּשָּׁה — וּשְׁרֵי? תָּא שְׁמַע וְלֹא בְּקָמֵיעַ בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִן הַמּוּמְחֶה: הָא מִן הַמּוּמְחֶה — נָפֵיק. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ קְמֵיעִין יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשָּׁה, זִמְנִין דְּמִיצְטְרִיךְ לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, וְאָתֵי לְאֵיתוֹיִינְהוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּקָמֵיעַ שֶׁל עִיקָּרִין. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֶחָד קָמֵיעַ שֶׁל כְּתָב וְאֶחָד קָמֵיעַ שֶׁל עִיקָּרִין! אֶלָּא הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּחוֹלֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סַכָּנָה. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֶחָד חוֹלֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ סַכָּנָה וְאֶחָד חוֹלֶה שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ סַכָּנָה. אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּמַסֵּי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ — (נָמֵי) שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the dilemma was raised with regard to whether or not it is permitted **to enter the bathroom with them. What is** the *halakha*? Do **they have sanctity, and it is** therefore **prohibited? Or, perhaps they have no sanctity, and it is permitted? Come** and **hear** a resolution from that which we learned in our mishna: **Nor with an amulet, when it is not from an expert.** By inference: **If it is from an expert, he may go out** with it. **And, if you say** that **amulets have an element of sanctity, at times he will need** to go **to the bathroom,** will be required to remove the amulets, forget that he removed them, **and come to carry them four cubits in the public domain.** Since the mishna did not address these complications, apparently amulets do not have an element of sanctity in that regard and one may enter the bathroom with them. The Gemara rejects this: **With what we are dealing here? With an amulet** made **of** herbal **roots** that certainly has no sanctity. **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: This is the case with regard to **both a written amulet and an amulet of** herbal **roots,** indicating that their *halakhot* are equal? **Rather, with what we are dealing here? With a person who is dangerously ill.** Because of the life-threatening situation, he is permitted to enter the bathroom with his amulet, despite the resulting degradation of the Holy Name. **Wasn't it taught** in the same *baraita* that the *halakha* applies to **both a sick person who is dangerously** ill **and a sick person who is not dangerously** ill, indicating that they share the same status in this regard? **Rather, since the amulet heals, even though he holds it in his hand,** he may **well** go out with it **too.** In terms of healing, there is no difference whether the amulet is hanging around his neck or whether it is in his hand; just as they permitted him to wear it around his neck on Shabbat, so too they permitted him to carry it in his hand.

The *gemara* now raises a new question: *yesh bahem mishum kedushah o dilma ein bahem mishum kedushah* — do amulets carry any *kedushah*? The question is prompted by the widespread practice of inscribing *pesukim* and God's name in amulets — for instance, the verse *kol ha-machalah asher samti be-Mitzrayim lo asim alecha ki ani Hashem rofecha*. The *gemara* immediately asks: for what legal purpose is this question relevant?

The first candidate is whether one may carry amulets into a burning building on *Shabbat* — that is, whether a rabbinic carrying violation is permitted in order to save them from fire, as it would be for *kisvei kodesh*. The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: written *berachot* and *kameiot*, although they contain letters of God's name and many subjects drawn from the Torah, may not be rescued from a fire. *Ve-nisrafin bimkomam* — one lets them burn in their place. They do not carry the status of *sifrei kodesh*, and one does not perform even a rabbinic carrying violation to save them. This settles the first question: they do not have *kedushah* in the sense that confers the status of *kisvei kodesh*.

The *gemara* then turns to the second possible question: must amulets be placed in *genizah* (buried rather than disposed of) when they are no longer needed? The *gemara* cites a ruling regarding God's name written on vessel handles or bed legs: one cuts off the portion bearing God's name and buries it. This principle — that God's name written anywhere must be treated with *genizah* — applies regardless of the object on which it appears. So the *kedushah* relevant to *genizah* does not depend on the object being a *sefer kodesh*; it attaches wherever the divine name is written. Amulets containing God's name would accordingly require *genizah* when discarded.

### Written vs. Herb

With these preliminary matters established, the *gemara* arrives at its central question in this section: may one enter a bathroom (*beit ha-kisei*) while wearing an amulet that contains God's name?

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishnah* itself: since a proven amulet may be worn on *Shabbat* without restriction, and since requiring its removal before entering an outdoor bathroom would create a situation where the wearer is left holding it in *reshut ha-rabim* — *ve-ati le-asuyei arba amot bi-reshut ha-rabim* — one cannot demand removal. This implies that entering the bathroom while wearing it is permitted. The *gemara* deflects: perhaps the *mishnah* refers only to an herb amulet (*kameia shel ikkarin*), which contains no written text. The *gemara* immediately objects that the *baraita* cited earlier explicitly equates written and herb amulets: *echad kameia shel ketav ve-echad kameia shel ikkarin*.

### Leather Covering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אוֹמֵר: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יֹאחֲזֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ וְיַעֲבִירֶנּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים! [אֶלָּא] הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בִּמְחוּפֶּה עוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Oshaya says** with regard to an amulet: **As long as he does not hold it in his hand and carry it four cubits in the public domain?** Apparently, even with regard to an amulet, there is a distinction between wearing it and carrying it. **Rather, with what are we dealing here? With** an amulet that is **covered in leather.** Since the writing itself is covered, the name of God is not degraded when the amulet is brought into the bathroom with him.

A series of attempted resolutions follows. The *gemara* first proposes that the discussion concerns a person whose life is at risk (*choleh she-yesh bo sakanah*), for whom we would not demand removal even if there is normally a prohibition against entering a bathroom with God's name. The *baraita* blocks this as well: *echad choleh she-yesh bo sakanah ve-echad choleh she-ein bo sakanah* — even a patient who faces no immediate mortal danger may wear the proven amulet.

The *gemara* then takes a more radical turn: perhaps when the amulet is healing the wearer, even holding it in the hand does not constitute carrying on *Shabbat* — *af al pi de-nakit lei be-yadei nami shapir dami*. This would dissolve the problem entirely: if there is no prohibition against carrying a healing device even in one's hand, there is nothing to worry about. But the *gemara* rejects this from a *baraita* attributed to Rabbi Oshaya: *u-vilvad she-lo yochozenu be-yado ve-ya'avirenu arba amot bi-reshut ha-rabim* — one may not hold an amulet in one's hand and carry it four *amot* in *reshut ha-rabim*.

The final resolution is the narrowest and the most elegant: *bi-mechupa or* — we are dealing with an amulet covered in leather. A leather covering creates a sufficient barrier such that the presence of God's name within does not render the bathroom entry problematic.

### Tefillin Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי תְּפִילִּין דִּמְחוּפּוֹת עוֹר, וְתַנְיָא: הַנִּכְנָס לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא — חוֹלֵץ תְּפִילִּין בְּרִחוּק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְנִכְנָס. הָתָם מִשּׁוּם שִׁין. דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שִׁין שֶׁל תְּפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דָּלֶת שֶׁל תְּפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: יוֹד שֶׁל תְּפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects. There is writing on the scrolls of **phylacteries, which is covered in** the **leather** of boxes housing the scrolls, **and** nevertheless **it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One who enters a bathroom** while wearing phylacteries **must remove** the **phylacteries at a distance of four cubits and** only then **enter.** There is no halakhic difference whether or not the writing is covered. The Gemara rejects this: **There,** with regard to phylacteries, the prohibition to enter the bathroom is not due to the sacred writing on the scrolls inside the phylacteries. Rather, it is **due to the** letter ***shin*** that protrudes from the leather of the boxes housing the scroll in the phylacteries of the head, **as Abaye said:** The source of the requirement of the ***shin* of the phylacteries is a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.** It is required by Torah law. **And, Abaye said:** The knot in the shape of the letter ***dalet*** in the straps **of the phylacteries** of the head **is a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai. And, Abaye said:** The **letter *yod* of the phylacteries is a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai.** It is due to those letters that one must safeguard the sanctity of the phylacteries and refrain from taking them into the bathroom.

This raises an obvious question: *tefillin* are leather boxes encasing parchment scrolls. Yet the *baraita* rules that one must remove *tefillin* at least four *amot* before entering the bathroom (*choletz tefillin be-richok arba amot*). If leather covering suffices for an amulet, why does it not suffice for *tefillin*?

The answer is that *tefillin* are categorically different. It is not merely the parchment inside that bears *kedushah* — the *batim* themselves have *kedushah* by virtue of the *shin* inscribed on the *shel rosh*. Abaye states: *shin shel tefillin halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — the *shin* on the *tefillin shel rosh* is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. Similarly, the *dalet* in the knot at the back of the head and the *yud* in the *yad* are *halakhot le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. Rashi reads the *shin*, *dalet*, and *yud* together as constituting God's name. Tosafot, however, argues that only the *shin* carries *kedushat ha-Shem*, since it is an actual letter inscribed on the box; the *dalet* and *yud* are formed by knots rather than written letters, and Tosafot questions whether these are truly considered letters bearing *kedushah*. On Tosafot's reading, there is consequently a separate question about the removal of the *shel yad* before entering a bathroom, which does not bear the *shin*. This will require further discussion at a later point.

The upshot: an amulet, even one containing God's name on parchment, has no *kedushah* inherent to the outer covering, and a leather casing therefore isolates the holy content sufficiently. The *tefillin* box itself is endowed with *kedushah* through the inscribed *shin*, and no leather casing can neutralize that.

## Armor on Shabbat

### Three Armor Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בְּשִׁרְיוֹן וְלֹא בְּקַסְדָּא וְלֹא בְּמַגָּפַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And** he may **neither** go out **with *shiryon*, nor with a *kasda*, nor with *maggafayim*.** These terms were not understood, and therefore the Gemara explains them:

The *mishnah* closes with a ruling entirely unrelated to the foregoing discussion of amulets: one may not wear various forms of armor on *Shabbat*, though doing so constitutes only a rabbinic prohibition rather than a biblical one.

The three items named are: *shiryon* — identified as chain armor or plate armor (*shiryon zrada*); *kasta* — identified as a helmet (*snuarta*); and *magafayim* — identified as leg armor (*pazmakei*). Detailed illustrations of these items appear in the Steinsaltz edition. The continuation of the *mishnah*, and the broader discussion of why wearing armor is rabbinically prohibited on *Shabbat*, will be taken up in the next session.
