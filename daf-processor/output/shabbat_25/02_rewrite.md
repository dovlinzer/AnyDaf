# Shabbat 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prohibited Oils

### Shemen Serefah

Daf 25 begins with the *mishnah* found in the middle of 24b. The *mishnah* opens: *ein madlikin b'shemen sreyfah b'yom tov* — one may not kindle with *shemen sreyfah* on *yom tov*. Having spent considerable time on the theme of Chanukah, we now return to the major subject of which oils and fuels may be used for *Shabbat* lighting. This *mishnah*, however, begins by addressing something one may not use specifically on *yom tov*.

The preceding *mishnah* had already established that one may not use *shemen sreyfah* on *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* there explained that *shemen sreyfah* refers to *terumah tme'ah* — oil of *terumah* that has become *tamei* and which it is a *mitzvah* to burn. This *mishnah* now adds that the same prohibition applies on *yom tov*.

### Rabbi Yishmael/Itran

The *mishnah* then pivots back to *Shabbat* concerns: *Rabbi Yishmael omer ein madlikin b'itran mipnei kvod hashabat* — Rabbi Yishmael says one may not kindle with *itran*, a type of malodorous tar or pitch fuel, because of the honor of *Shabbat*, since the unpleasant smell will drive one from the room. *Vachachamim matirin* — the Sages, however, permit it.

### Permitted Oils

The *mishnah* then enumerates the oils that are permitted: *shemen shumshumin* (sesame seed oil), *shemen egozim* (nut oil), *shemen tnunot* (gourd oil), *shemen dagim* (fish oil — Rashi identifies this as made from fish innards), *shemen pakuot* (which Rashi describes as another variety of gourd), *itran* (the very tar that Rabbi Yishmael prohibits now appears on the permitted list according to the Sages), and *nefat* — naphtha, a petroleum derivative. Steinsaltz notes that this may be the earliest historical source to mention the production of white naphtha, though it merely references it rather than describing its production in any detail.

### Rabbi Tarfon

Rabbi Tarfon takes the most stringent position of all: *ein madlikin ela b'shemen zayit bilvad* — one may kindle only with olive oil, to the exclusion of all other oils.

## Invalid Sacrifices

### Kodshim Comparison

The *Gemara* now turns to *mai ta'ama* — the reason *shemen sreyfah* may not be burned on *yom tov*. As was touched upon previously, there are two possible frameworks. One is that the primary concern is actually a *Shabbat* concern: if one may use this oil on *Shabbat*, the presence of a *mitzvah* to burn it might lead one to tip the lamp to accelerate the burning. The other reading — which sits more naturally with this *mishnah*'s emphasis on *yom tov* — is that the concern is fundamentally a *yom tov* concern.

The *Gemara* states: *l'fi she'ein sorfin kodshim b'yom tov* — because one may not burn invalid *kodshim* on *yom tov*, and since *terumah tme'ah* similarly possesses sanctity and is unfit for use, it falls under the same category. The *Gemara* then turns to establish the source for this rule: *minani milei* — from where do we know that *kodshim* may not be burned on *yom tov*?

### Pesach Notar

The *Gemara* cites the teaching of Chizkiyah, which was also transmitted as a *braisa* in the school of Chizkiyah. The verse by *Pesach* states: *v'lo sosiru mimenu ad boker, v'hanotar mimenu ad boker ba'esh tisrofu* — do not leave any of it until morning, and whatever remains until morning you shall burn in fire. The verse uses the phrase *ad boker* — "until morning" — twice, which is apparently redundant. The *drashah* derives from this repetition: *boker sheni litein lo boker sheni l'sreyfato* — the second mention of "morning" comes to teach that there is a second morning designated for the burning. One does not burn *notar* on *Pesach* morning itself — on the *yom tov* of the 15th — but rather on the morning of the 16th.

This establishes that burning invalid *kodshim* is prohibited on *yom tov* itself. The reasoning is clear: burning is a *melachah*, and burning *kodshim* yields no direct physical benefit of the kind permitted on *yom tov* — it does not qualify as *ach asher ye'aseh l'chol nefesh*, an act performed for human nourishment or physical enjoyment.

A significant conceptual difficulty arises at this point, however. Both Rashi and Tosafot raise the obvious question: if one is permitted to derive benefit from the burning of *terumah tme'ah*, why can one not light with it? After all, when burning ordinary oil for light, one is clearly deriving benefit. Why should the incidental presence of a *mitzvah* to burn this oil remove the permission to use it on *yom tov*?

The *Gemara* itself does not address this directly. Two answers are offered. The simpler one, advanced by both Rashi and Tosafot, is that it is a *gzeirah*: since one may not burn invalid *kodshim* on *yom tov* in cases where no benefit is derived, the rabbis extended this prohibition to *terumah tme'ah* as well, even where benefit is incidentally obtained, in order to keep the entire category closed and prevent confusion. Tosafot offers a more conceptual answer: although benefit from light is indeed obtained when burning *terumah tme'ah*, that benefit is only incidental to the act. The reason one burns *terumah tme'ah* is to fulfill the *mitzvah* of destroying it. The *Torah* permits incidental benefit during the course of destruction, but the act itself is fundamentally one of religious obligation, not personal benefit. Tosafot draws an analogy to the debate about bringing *nedarim unedavot* on *yom tov*: according to the view that this is forbidden, even though one will eat the meat, the act of *shechitah* for a *korban* is primarily an act directed toward God, not an act of meat preparation — and so even where benefit follows, it does not transform the act into one that *yom tov* permits.

### Olas Shabbat

Abaye derives the same prohibition from a different verse: *olas Shabbos beshabbaso* — the *olah* of *Shabbat* in its *Shabbat*, which implies *velo olas chol beShabbos* — and not the weekday *olah* on *Shabbat*. Tosafot explains that an additional *drashah* extends this to *yom tov* as well: an *olas chol* may not be burned on *Shabbat*, and similarly may not be burned on *yom tov*. Crucially, note that what is being prohibited here is not *kodshim tme'im* but rather the burning of legitimate fats and portions of *korbanot* whose burning time has not yet arrived for that particular day. Even though these are valid *kodshim* and their burning is a *mitzvah*, if that burning is not specifically commanded for this *Shabbat* or *yom tov*, it may not be performed. The same logic applies, *a fortiori*, to *kodshim tme'im*.

### Food-Related Actions

Rava derives the prohibition from yet another verse: *hu levado ye'aseh lachem* — the *Torah* permits on *yom tov* only that which is performed for human eating. From *hu* — "it alone" — we learn *velo machshirin*: one may not prepare the instruments of food preparation on *yom tov*, only the act of food preparation itself. Thus one may not sharpen a *shechitah* knife on *yom tov*, as that is one stage removed from the food. And from *levado* we derive *velo milah shelo bizmano* — not even *milah* that falls outside its designated time may be performed on *yom tov*, since it is not food-related and its obligation is not specific to this day.

One might ask: why would I have assumed that *milah shelo bizmano* could be performed on *yom tov*? What grounds would there be for such a view? Rashi explains that one might have constructed a *kal vachomer*: a *kohen* with *tzara'as* may not remove the affected skin to perform the *avodah* in the *Beis HaMikdash*, meaning *tzara'as* overrides *avodah*. Yet *avodah* overrides *Shabbat*. By the transitive property, *tzara'as* is more stringent than *Shabbat*. Meanwhile, *bris milah* overrides *tzara'as* — one cuts away the *tzara'as* on the *orlah* to perform the *milah*. Therefore *bris milah* would seem to override *tzara'as*, which overrides *avodah*, which overrides *Shabbat* — and one might have concluded that *milah shelo bizmano* could likewise override *yom tov*. Hence the verse is needed to teach otherwise.

Tosafot rejects the *kal vachomer* as formally flawed: the reason a *kohen* with *tzara'as* may not perform the *avodah* is not that *tzara'as* defeats the *avodah* in some competitive sense, but rather that such a *kohen* is simply *pasul* to perform the *avodah* — the two are not directly in tension. Tosafot accordingly offers other reasons why one might have supposed *milah shelo bizmano* permissible on *yom tov*, and the verse comes to exclude it.

The overarching principle from all of these derivations is consistent: even acts that are in some sense connected to a *mitzvah*, even acts whose physical execution would normally be permitted on *yom tov* in another context, may not be performed on *yom tov* when the obligation is not intrinsically tied to that day. One cannot burn *notar*, one cannot burn the fats of *korbanot*, one cannot perform *milah shelo bizmano*. And therefore, just as one cannot burn *kodshim* on *yom tov*, one similarly cannot burn *terumah tme'ah* on *yom tov*.

### Shabbaton Override

Rav Ashi offers yet another framework. The Torah designates *yom tov* as *shabbaton* — a day of rest — and this constitutes a positive commandment, an *asei*. Combined with the negative prohibition *lo ta'aseh kol melachah*, *yom tov* carries both a positive and a negative commandment. The principle *ein asei docheh lo ta'aseh v'asei* — a positive commandment does not override a combination of a positive and negative commandment — means that the *asei* of burning invalid *kodshim* cannot override the combined positive and negative structure of *yom tov*. Therefore: *b'Yom Tov hu d'assur* — it is on *yom tov* that it is forbidden.

All of these approaches converge: burning *terumah tme'ah* is analogous to burning *kodshim*, and since one cannot burn *kodshim* on *yom tov*, one cannot burn *terumah tme'ah* on *yom tov* either. As noted, the conceptual bridge — that *terumah tme'ah* is being burned for one's benefit whereas invalid *kodshim* are not — remains unaddressed by the *Gemara* itself and is resolved only through Rashi's *gzeirah* approach or Tosafot's framework of incidental versus primary purpose.

## Terumah Benefit

### Rav's Principle

The *Gemara* now draws an inference from the foregoing: *yom tov hu de-asar* — it is *yom tov* that prohibits this. *Ha be-chol shapir dami* — on an ordinary weekday, however, it is permitted. Specifically, what is permitted on a weekday is to burn *terumah tme'ah* and derive benefit from the resulting light. This raises the question the *Gemara* immediately addresses: how do we know one may derive this benefit at all?

The fact that the *Gemara* needs to establish a source for this tells us something important: the underlying assumption had been that *terumah tme'ah* — as an item possessing *kedushah* that has become unfit — is *asur ba-hana'ah*, forbidden to derive benefit from, in the same way that other invalid sacred items are. The *Gemara* therefore asks: where does the *Torah* permit benefit from *terumah tme'ah*?

Rav teaches: *keshem she-mitzvah lisrof et ha-kodshim she-nitme'u kach mitzvah lisrof et ha-trumah she-nitme'ah* — just as there is a *mitzvah* to burn *kodshim* that became *tamei*, there is a *mitzvah* to burn *terumah* that became *tamei*. This is the first major principle: burning *terumah tme'ah* is not merely a practical measure for disposal — it is an affirmative *mitzvah*. And Rav adds: *be-sha'at bi'urah tehani mimmennah* — at the moment of its destruction, one may derive benefit from it.

### Mishmeret Source

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *hechan amrah Torah* — where exactly does the *Torah* say this? The answer is derived from what Rav Nachman bar Avuha taught in the name of Rav Nachman: the verse states *va-ani hineh natatti lecha et mishmeret trumotai* — "I have given you the guarding of my *terumot*," in the plural. The word *trumotai* implies two *terumot*: one *terumah tehorah* and one *terumah tme'ah*. How does one guard *terumah tehorah*? By ensuring it does not become *tamei*. How does one guard *terumah tme'ah*? By ensuring it is properly disposed of — not left lying around where one might accidentally eat it.

The verse then says *lecha* — "for you." *Shelcha teheh le-hasika tachat tavshilcha* — you may use it to kindle a fire beneath your cooking pot. When you are in the process of guarding the *terumah tme'ah* by destroying it, the *Torah* says *lecha* — it shall be yours — meaning that during the process of its destruction, you may derive incidental benefit from the burning.

Several important principles emerge from this analysis. First, *terumah tme'ah* is *asur ba-hana'ah* — this the *Gemara* assumes without demonstration. Second, there is a *mitzvah* to burn it, established by analogy to *kodshim*. Third, the verse of *mishmeret trumotai* and *lecha* permits incidental benefit during the process of destruction. And as Tosafot explains, because this benefit is only incidental to the primary act of destruction, it cannot be performed on *yom tov*, where one's actions must be directed toward human benefit as their primary purpose.

There is an interesting debate between Rashi and Tosafot regarding the nature of the *mitzvah* to burn *terumah tme'ah*. Rashi gives two reasons: first, it resembles *kodshim* in which there is a *mitzvah* to burn; and second, it must be disposed of to prevent accidental consumption — *mishum takalah*. The second reason sounds entirely rabbinic. The first suggests a closer biblical analogy, perhaps even a biblical obligation. Tosafot raises the question of whether there is in fact a biblical *mitzvah* to burn it specifically. Even if the verse *mishmeret trumotai* implies a biblical obligation to dispose of it, that obligation need not take the form of burning — one could bury it or destroy it by other means. Tosafot leaves open whether a specifically biblical *mitzvah* of burning exists. The *Gemara* itself elsewhere implies that other forms of destruction might also be acceptable — for example, flooding the wine, though that raises its own questions — and this leaves the precise contours of the obligation somewhat indeterminate. What is clear is that however it is destroyed, the *Torah* permits incidental benefit during the process.

### Ma'aser Contrast

An alternative derivation is offered: *ve-iba'it eima mi-Rav Avohu*, from what Rav Avohu taught in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. The verse from the *vidui* recited over *ma'aser sheni* states: *velo bi'arti mimenu be-tamei* — I did not destroy from it in a state of *tumah*. The word *mimenu* — "from it" — is taken to exclude: from *ma'aser sheni* you may not derive benefit when destroying it in a state of *tumah*, but this exclusion implies a contrast: from *terumah*, oil that became *tamei* may be burned and benefit derived during its destruction.

The *Gemara* challenges this inference: perhaps the contrast is not to *terumah* but to *shemen shel kodesh she-nitma* — oil consecrated for *korbanot* that became *tamei*. Perhaps it is oil of *korbanot* that is permitted, not *terumah*. The *Gemara* responds that this would constitute a *kal vachomer*: if *ma'aser*, which is less weighty than *kodshim*, carries the prohibition of deriving benefit during its *tamei* destruction, then *kodshim*, which are weightier, should surely be prohibited as well. Applying the same logic, *terumah*, which sits between *ma'aser* and *kodshim* in relative weight, would also be included in the prohibition. If the verse is coming to permit something, it would logically permit the least weighty option — and between *kodshim* and *terumah*, *terumah* is less weighty.

### Terumah Less Weighty

This generates a genuine debate within the *Gemara*: which is actually weightier, *terumah* or *korbanot*? The *Gemara* first argues that *kodshim* clearly should not be the permitted category — *kodshim chamur*, they are weightier. The mnemonic *Panak Akas* encapsulates six factors by which *kodshim* exceed *terumah* in stringency. *Piggul* — an improper intention during *shechitah* creates an *issur karet* — applies only to *korbanot*, not *terumah*. *Notar* — leaving over the sacrifice past its time creates a *karet* prohibition — likewise applies only to *korbanot*. The *kuf* represents the status of being called a *korban* itself, which carries an inherent gravity. *Me'ila* — the prohibition against deriving improper benefit from consecrated property, which entails a *lav* and requires bringing a *korban* — applies to *kodshim* but not *terumah*. *Karet* for eating them unlawfully. And *assur lo-onen* — a mourner on the day before burial may not eat *korbanot*, a restriction that does not apply to *terumah*. Six distinguishing features make *kodshim* weightier.

The *Gemara* then entertains the counter-argument: *adarabba, trumah lo mematina* — perhaps *terumah* should not be the permitted category either, since *terumah* also has its own stringencies. The mnemonic *Machpaz* encapsulates four of them. *Misah* — a non-*kohen* who eats *terumah* is liable to *misah bidei shamayim*, death at the hands of Heaven. *Chomesh* — one who unlawfully eats *terumah* must return it with an added fifth. *V'ein la pidyon* — *terumah* cannot be redeemed, whereas a blemished *korban* may be redeemed and its sanctity transferred. *Ve-assur le-zarim* — *terumah* is completely forbidden to non-*kohanim*, whereas at least some *korbanot*, such as *shelamim* and *todah*, may be eaten by non-*kohanim*.

The *Gemara* resolves the tension simply: *hanach nefishen* — six outweighs four. *Kodshim* remain more stringent overall. Furthermore, even setting aside the numerical count, the decisive factor is punishment: *kodshim* carry *karet*, while transgressing *terumah* carries *misah bidei shamayim*. Whichever is more severe establishes the greater weight of the category. Rashi explains that *karet* means dying before one's appointed time and childless, whereas *misah bidei shamayim* means dying before one's appointed time but not childless. Tosafot engages extensively with the question, devoting considerable space — visible from page 28 onward — to the distinctions between *karet* and *misah bidei shamayim*. He cites another *Gemara* to argue that *karet* means dying before sixty, while *misah bidei shamayim* does not necessarily specify a time of death but rather that the death is attributed by Heaven to the sin in question. Various interpretations circulate, but the *Gemara*'s conclusion is clear: *karet*, whatever its precise definition, is worse than *misah bidei shamayim*, and therefore *kodshim* remain the more weighty category. *Terumah* is thus less weighty, and it is *terumah* that is the permitted category from the contrast with *ma'aser sheni*.

### Exclusion Logic

At the opening of 25b, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a final derivation. The verse states *reishis degancha tiroshcha veyitzharecha titen lo* — you shall give him the first of your grain, wine, and oil. *Lo velo l'oro* — "to him and not for his light." The *Torah* says that when you give *terumah tehorah* to a *kohen*, the *kohen* should use it for himself, not burn it as firewood for light. If the *Torah* had to exclude burning *terumah tehorah* for light, the inference is: *mikhlal de-vas orah hu* — this implies that *terumah tme'ah* has the quality of being appropriate for burning as light. Tosafot raises the circularity here — the phrase *lo l'oro* was itself read into the verse by the rabbis, and then read back out again as a derivation — but this serves nonetheless as a final *drashah* establishing that benefit may be derived from *terumah tme'ah* during its destruction.

## Candlelighting

### Rava vs Abaye

Returning now to Rabbi Yishmael's prohibition of *itran* due to *kvod Shabbat*, the *Gemara* asks what exactly the concern is. Rava explains: *mitoch she-rei'o ra* — because its smell is so bad, *gezeirah shema yanichenah ve-yetze* — we are concerned that one will leave the room rather than tolerate the smell.

Abaye challenges: *ve-yetze* — so what if one leaves the room? According to Abaye, the entire concern regarding bad wicks and oils is that one might fiddle with the flame. If the smell is so unpleasant that one simply vacates the room, there is no risk of fiddling — one will not extinguish the lamp or interfere with it. So why does it matter if one leaves?

Rava responds: *she-ani omer, hadlakas ner bi-Shabbat hovah* — because I say that candlelighting on *Shabbat* is an obligation, a *hovah*. It is here that the *Gemara* explicitly articulates, for the first time, that there is an obligatory dimension to *Shabbat* candlelighting. If leaving the room means being without light where one needs to be, then the *hovah* is not being fulfilled.

### Chovah vs Mitzvah

Tosafot clarifies the precise *chiddush* of Abaye's statement: the point is not that lighting candles is an obligation — that, Tosafot says, was never in doubt. The *mishnah* later in this tractate teaches that women can face serious consequences for being careless about *hadlakas ha-ner*, and if there are such consequences, the *mitzvah* nature of the act is assumed. What Abaye adds is that the light must be present specifically where one is eating — *bimkom se'udah* — because *de-hovah hu she-yishe bimkom ha-ner mishum oneg*, it is obligatory that one be in the place of the light so as to enhance *oneg Shabbat*. Eating in darkness is not pleasurable, and *oneg Shabbat* requires that one eat by light.

### Rabbi Yehuda Example

The *Gemara* continues with Rav teaching that it is a *mitzvah* to bathe on *Erev Shabbat*. This goes beyond the *reshut* — mere permission — to bathe warmly before *Shabbat*, which was the formulation of Rav Nachman bar Rav Zavda. Rav says it rises to the level of *mitzvah*. What does this *mitzvah* look like?

*De-amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav kach hayah minhago shel Rabi Yehuda bar Elai* — Rav Yehuda taught in the name of Rav: this was the practice of Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai. *Erev Shabbat* they would bring him a vessel filled with hot water, and he would wash his face, his hands, and his feet in preparation for *Shabbat*. He would then wrap himself in linen garments adorned with *tzitzit*, and he appeared like an angel of God.

The image is striking: not merely hygiene, but a deliberate transformation — clean, draped in fine ritual garments, and emanating an aura of holiness. Taking a bath immediately before *Shabbat*, even when one might otherwise feel sufficiently clean, places one in an entirely different mode of consciousness. The act of preparation — being clean, wearing fine clothing, setting the table — is itself an entry into *Shabbat* holiness.

### Tzitzit Digression

This description of Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai's linen garments prompts a brief digression. *Vehayu talmidav mechabim mimeno kanfei kesutan* — his students would hide the corners of their garments from him. Why? Because Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai's practice was to wear linen garments with *tzitzit* that included *techelet* — wool strings — which technically constitutes *kilayim* or *shatnez* (mixing wool and linen). Normally *shatnez* is forbidden, but where a positive commandment such as *tzitzit* is at stake, *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* — a positive commandment overrides a negative — and the wool *techelet* may be used even in a linen garment.

The students, however, did not follow this practice. When Rabbi Yehuda asked why they were hiding their garments, he said: *lo kach shaniti lachem, sadin b'tzitzit* — have I not taught you that regarding a linen garment and *tzitzit*, *Beit Shammai* exempts and *Beit Hillel* obligates, and we rule like *Beit Hillel*?

The students' response is described as a *gezeirah mishum kesut lailah*: even though we hold in principle like *Beit Hillel*, we refrain from putting the wool strings in, lest one come to do the same with nighttime garments which are not obligated in *tzitzit*, in which case the *shatnez* would serve no *mitzvah* and would be a genuine prohibition. Tosafot explains that on this point we actually rule like *Beit Shammai*, making this one of the instances where *Beit Shammai*'s position is followed over *Beit Hillel*'s. Rashi reads the passage differently, suggesting the students had no *tzitzit* at all on their linen garments — a more striking reading.

### Bracha Debate

Returning to the nature of candlelighting, a critical terminological observation emerges. The *Gemara* used the term *hovah* for candlelighting, while for bathing it used *mitzvah*. This distinction matters. In the context of *mayim acharonim* — washing hands after eating — the *Gemara* in Chullin similarly says *hovah*, and Tosafot there explains that *hovah* can carry a sense of greater obligation than *mitzvah*, but it also suggests a non-ritual, health-based requirement — which is why one does not recite a *bracha* over *mayim acharonim*, whereas one does recite a *bracha* over *mayim rishonim*.

Applying this framework to candlelighting raises a fundamental question: when, and why, did the lighting of *Shabbat* candles become ritualized as a *ma'aseh mitzvah* requiring a *bracha*? The *bracha* itself does not appear in the *Gemara*; it emerges in the Geonic period.

Tosafot records the debate here explicitly. Some authorities argue — based on the word *hovah* — that no *bracha* should be recited over candlelighting, just as none is recited over *mayim acharonim*. *Rabbeinu Tam* forcefully rejects this view: *de-lo dami l'mayim acharonim d'lo havei ela le-hatzala b'alma* — *mayim acharonim* serves only a health purpose, whereas *hadlakas ner hi hovah shel mitzvas oneg Shabbat* — candlelighting is a *hovah* that itself constitutes a *mitzvah* of *oneg Shabbat*, and many obligations do require a *bracha*. Rabbeinu Tam goes further, engaging in a recorded exchange with Rabbeinu Meshullam — found in the responsa of *Sefer Hayashar* — on whether, if lights are already burning from before *Shabbat*, one must extinguish and relight them. Rabbeinu Meshullam holds that there is no ritual act of lighting per se; the entire obligation is simply to have light in one's home on *Shabbat*, and if it is already lit, the obligation is fulfilled. Rabbeinu Tam ultimately concludes that there is indeed an affirmative ritual act of lighting, and one should extinguish and relight — a position not explicit anywhere in the *Gemara* but one that follows naturally once the *bracha* is established and the act becomes understood as a *ma'aseh mitzvah* in its own right.

## Aggada: Peace/Wealth

### Nishiti Tova

Having concluded the legal material for this section, the *Gemara* turns to *aggadeta*, citing the verse from Lamentations: *vattiznach mishalom nafshi nishisi tova* — my soul has been abandoned from peace, I have forgotten goodness. The *Gemara* reads this verse as an allusion to several different forms of physical and domestic well-being.

Rabbi Avahu identifies *vattiznach mishalom nafshi* with *hadlakas ner b'Shabbos*: without candles, one has no peace — one stumbles in the dark, one cannot eat properly, there is no *shalom bayit*. *Nashiti tova* — I forgot goodness — is interpreted by Rabbi Yirmeyah as *beit hamerchatz*, the bathhouse, recalling the extended discussion in earlier pages about the central role of the bathhouse in ancient life. Rabbi Yochanan says it refers to *rechitzat yadayim veraglayim bechamin* — washing one's hands and feet in hot water, connected to the *Erev Shabbat* practice described above. Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha says it refers to *mitah na'ah ve-chelim na'im* — a nice bed and fine vessels in the home, the sense of peace that comes from living amid pleasant surroundings. Rabbi Abba reads it as *mitah mutza'at* — a made bed — and *ishah mekushetet letalmidei chachamim* — an adorned wife for Torah scholars. The juxtaposition of the made bed and the woman described for the benefit of scholars is acknowledged as somewhat difficult, and Rashi's gloss — suggesting the phrase connotes dignity and suitability — is noted as a careful reading of a passage that does not sit entirely smoothly.

### Who is Rich?

The *Gemara* continues with a sequence of teachings on what constitutes wealth. The mnemonic *Mem-Tav-Kuf-Shin* organizes the four opinions. Rabbi Meir says: *kol sheyesh lo nachat ruach be-oshro* — whoever has contentment with his wealth. The *Maharsha* distinguishes this from the famous line in Pirkei Avot, *hasame'ach be-chelko*, noting that the word used here is *oshro* — his wealth — rather than *chelko* — his portion. Rabbi Meir is saying that even a person who genuinely has much can fail to be rich in the meaningful sense if he lacks inner satisfaction, and another with less but a sense of contentment with what he has is truly wealthy.

Rabbi Tarfon offers an emphatically concrete answer: *kol sheyesh lo kuf kramim u-me'ah sadot ve-kuf avadim she-ovdim bahem* — rich is having a hundred vineyards, a hundred fields, and a hundred servants working them. Rabbi Tarfon was himself a man of great wealth, which may explain his frame of reference. The *Maharsha* suggests that even this statement may contain an implicit critique — the more one accumulates, the more one desires, and true satisfaction remains elusive — but this reading requires some inference.

Rabbi Akiva says: *kol sheyesh lo ishah na'ah bema'asim* — whoever has a wife of good deeds. This reflects Rabbi Akiva's own biography, where his wife's selfless devotion made possible his entire life as a Torah scholar. Notably, Rabbi Akiva also taught *marbeh nechasim marbeh de'agah* — one who increases possessions increases worry — which gives his definition of wealth a quality of inversion: real wealth is not measured in possessions at all.

Rabbi Yose says: *kol sheyesh lo beis ha-kiseh samuch le-shulchano* — whoever has his bathroom close to his table. Particularly in an era without indoor plumbing, this was genuinely significant for comfort and dignity. It is known that Rabbi Yose himself suffered from gastrointestinal difficulties, which may give this statement a personal dimension.

## Tzari Oil

### Tzari Concern

The *mishnah* lists *tzari* among the oils one may not kindle with. The *Gemara* explains: Rabbah says, *mitoch she-reicho nodef, gezeirah shema yistapek mimenu* — because the smell of *tzari* — balsam oil — is so wonderfully fragrant, we are concerned that one will be drawn to dip his bread into the oil. By dipping the bread into the vessel of oil, one removes oil from around the wick, causing the flame to be extinguished prematurely — which constitutes *mechabeh*, a prohibited act of *melachah* on *Shabbat*. Thus *tzari* is prohibited not because of unpleasantness, as with *itran*, but for the opposite reason: its fragrance is so enticing that it creates the risk of interference with the flame.
