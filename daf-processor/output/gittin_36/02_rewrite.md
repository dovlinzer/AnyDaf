# Gittin 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Widow's Vow vs. Oath

### Vow instead of oath

We begin at the very top of 36a, picking up a topic that has been developing across the preceding pages: the substitution of a *neder* (vow) for a *shvuah* (oath) when a widow comes to collect from her deceased husband's estate. The reason for the substitution is the severity of a *shvuat sheker* — a false oath — even an unintentional one carries grave consequences. By switching to a *neder*, the Rabbis hoped to give the widow a viable path to collection while protecting her from the dangers of a potentially false oath. We have already seen how that institution experienced a certain pushback at a later stage, and we will continue to see that dynamic as we proceed.

### Vow annulment problem

The *gemara* now asks: why should a *neder* suffice? Two problems immediately present themselves. First, can she make the vow at all once she is already married, given that her husband has the power to annul it — in which case the vow would not accomplish anything? Second, even for an unmarried widow, what prevents her from going to a *chakham* (sage) and having the vow released, thereby undermining the entire mechanism?

### Married woman's vow

On the question of a married woman, the *gemara* records a dispute. According to one opinion, a married woman cannot make such a vow for precisely this reason: her husband will simply annul it and it will prove nothing. According to another opinion, it is possible — but only under certain conditions.

### Be-rabbim vs. al da'at

Before examining those conditions, a preliminary distinction is essential: the difference between *be-rabbim* and *al da'at rabbim*. *Be-rabbim* means the vow was made in public — *Rashi* defines the relevant quorum as ten people. *Al da'at rabbim* means the vow was made not merely on the basis of the individual's own *da'at* (intent or understanding) but in some sense on the basis of what the community would intend — it is grounded in communal, rather than personal, *da'at*.

### Tosafot on husband annul…

*Tosafot* observes that when it comes to the husband's power of annulment (*hafarah*), *al da'at rabbim* does not help. The mechanism of *hatarah* — going to a *chakham* to have a vow released — works by identifying an error in the vower's *da'at*: had I known this or that, I never would have made the vow; my intent was mistaken, and so the vow should be dissolved. If the vow is grounded not in my subjective *da'at* but in the community's *da'at*, that avenue is closed off, because the subjective circumstances that might have constituted my personal error are no longer operative. That is the power of *al da'at rabbim* in blocking *hatarah* by a *chakham*.

But *hafarah* by a husband operates on an entirely different principle. The husband does not dissolve the vow by identifying an error in his wife's intent. He has a legal power, independent of *da'at* altogether, to declare that this vow cannot stand within the marriage. The wife's intent — whether completely certain or completely ambiguous — is irrelevant to this mechanism. And if the wife's *da'at* is irrelevant, substituting the community's *da'at* for hers changes nothing. Accordingly, *al da'at rabbim* provides no shield against a husband's *hafarah*.

### Public vs. communal dist…

*Be-rabbim*, however, may work precisely because it operates on a different logic altogether. When a vow is made in public, it is no longer a private matter between husband and wife. The vow has entered the public domain — it is a matter of public record, so to speak — and is thereby removed from the private relational sphere in which the husband's power of annulment operates. According to the opinions that *be-rabbim* functions in this way, it would block both *hafarah* by a husband and *hatarah* by a *chakham*, and it would do so without exception, since the mechanism is not about *da'at* at all.

There are, however, opinions that *be-rabbim* does not help: merely pronouncing a vow in front of others does not alter the inherent nature of the vow. According to those opinions, *al da'at rabbim* remains the operative mechanism — effective against the *chakham*, ineffective against the husband, and, as we shall see, subject to its own internal limitations.

### Three solutions explained

Returning to the question of how to block a woman (married or not) from going to a *chakham* to dissolve the vow: the *gemara* presents multiple approaches. One answer is that when the vow is made for purposes such as these, she must specify the circumstances in which it was made (*tzarikh lefareit et haneder*), and once those circumstances are explicit, the *chakham* will recognize that this is not a vow he may dissolve — it is the very instrument by which she collects. A second answer holds that ordinarily one need not detail the conditions of the vow, and therefore we are concerned she may seek release. According to this view, the vow is only protected if made either *be-rabbim* or *al da'at rabbim*. These two mechanisms, and the interplay between them, are what the *gemara* now proceeds to examine in detail.

## Al da'at rabbim mechanism

### Gemara on two concepts

The *gemara* opens this discussion with a case that clarifies how *be-rabbim* and *al da'at rabbim* function in practice. Three lines from the bottom of 35b, the *gemara* frames its question around a case analogous to the widow's: a *kohen* who made a vow not to derive benefit from a woman he is forbidden to marry — say, a *gerusha* — in order to render himself eligible to perform the *avodah*. The concern is identical: perhaps he will simply go to a *chakham* and have the vow dissolved.

The *gemara* at the top of 36a proposes: *demadirin lei be-rabbim* — we have him take the vow in public. That will close off the avenue of *hatarah*. But the *gemara* immediately complicates this: *hani-cha le-man d'amar neder she-hudur be-rabbim ein lo hafarah* — this works well for the opinion that a vow made in public cannot be annulled. *Ella le-man d'amar yesh lo hafarah* — but according to the opinion that it can still be annulled, what solution remains? The *gemara* answers: *demadirin lei al da'at rabbim* — we have him take the vow on the communal *da'at*, not merely in their presence.

### Ameimar's position

This leads to a direct ruling. *Amar Ameimar*: the *halacha* is that even according to the opinion that a vow made *be-rabbim* can still be annulled, a vow made *al da'at rabbim* cannot be annulled (*al da'at rabbim ein lo hafarah*).

A clarification is in order here. *Tosafot* has already established that *al da'at rabbim* cannot prevent a husband's *hafarah*, since the husband's power is independent of *da'at* entirely. Therefore, the *hafarah* that *Ameimar* says is blocked must refer specifically to *hatarah* — release by a *chakham* — not to a husband's *hafarah*. *Ameimar*'s ruling is thus: *be-rabbim* may or may not block *hatarah* (it is disputed); *al da'at rabbim* blocks *hatarah* according to everyone. The mechanism, as noted above, is that *hatarah* works by identifying subjective error in the vower's *da'at*. When the vow is grounded not in one's individual *da'at* but in a communal one, the personal subjective circumstances that might have constituted error are simply no longer relevant.

It is worth pausing to note the difference in scope between the two mechanisms. If *be-rabbim* does work — and that remains disputed — it is arguably broader and more absolute than *al da'at rabbim*. Making a vow in public transforms it into a matter of public record, removing it entirely from the private sphere; this would block *hafarah* by a husband as well as *hatarah* by a *chakham*, and it would permit no exceptions. *Al da'at rabbim*, by contrast, operates through *da'at* — it replaces subjective individual intent with communal intent — and therefore it is only relevant where *da'at* is operative. This means it does not help against a husband's *hafarah*, and, as the *gemara* now demonstrates, it is not absolute even against a *chakham*.

### Dardeki case study

The *gemara* introduces an important limitation: *ve-hani milei le-dvar reshut* — this rule that *al da'at rabbim* prevents *hatarah* applies only when the vow pertains to a discretionary matter. *Aval le-dvar mitzvah, yesh hafarah* — but if keeping the vow would interfere with the performance of a *mitzvah*, it can be annulled despite having been made *al da'at rabbim*.

The illustrative case is the *mikri dardeki* — an elementary school teacher. *Rav Acha* imposed a vow on him *al da'at rabbim* — binding him, under communal *da'at*, to the effect that he would no longer be permitted to teach children. The background is that he had been negligent, apparently meaning he had been striking the children — a recognized form of discipline in that era, though one that the *gemara* treats as grounds for removal. The vow was structured as *al da'at rabbim* precisely to close off any avenue of release. Nevertheless, *Ravina* subsequently restored him to his position (*ahadrei Ravina*), because *lo ishtakach de-dayek ke-votei* — no other teacher could be found who was as exacting and demanding as he was, and the children's education was therefore suffering.

### Communal intent limits

The question naturally arises: how could *Ravina* dissolve a vow made *al da'at rabbim*? The answer lies in distinguishing between subjective and objective grounds for release. The mechanism of *al da'at rabbim* closes off the avenue of *ta'ut* (error) that is personal and subjective. If the vower says, "Had I known my own personal circumstances, I would not have made this vow," that subjective claim is not available — the community's *da'at* does not share those private circumstances. But there is a category of error that is not subjective at all: had we known that this vow would come to block the performance of a *mitzvah*, no community would ever have countenanced such a vow. The *rabim* never intend their communal *da'at* to be invoked in service of obstructing a *mitzvah*. That is an objective error — one that any community would universally recognize — and it therefore qualifies as a valid basis for *hatarah* even against a vow made *al da'at rabbim*.

This refines our understanding of what *al da'at rabbim* actually does and does not accomplish. It removes the vow from the domain of individual, subjective *da'at*, preventing release on the grounds of personal error or personal circumstance. But it does not insulate the vow against objective, universal errors — specifically, circumstances that no community would ever have wished to support. A *mitzvah* being impeded is precisely such a circumstance; the community, by definition, never would have wanted their *da'at* to stand in the way of Torah obligations. So *al da'at rabbim* is more limited than it might initially appear: it works against personal error, but not against objective necessity.

The overall picture, then, is as follows. *Al da'at rabbim* works according to everyone to block *hatarah* in ordinary discretionary cases. *Be-rabbim*, if it works at all (which is disputed), is a categorically different mechanism — it transforms the vow into a public matter and is therefore broader, more absolute, and operates even against a husband's *hafarah*. And *al da'at rabbim*, despite its power, has two significant limits: it does not block a husband's *hafarah* (as *Tosafot* established), and it does not prevent *hatarah* in cases where the vow obstructs a *mitzvah*.

### Oath vs. vow nature

It is worth stepping back to recall why this entire discussion is taking place in the register of *neder* rather than *shvuah*. The window of possibility for a *chakham* to release a vow, and for all of these mechanisms of *be-rabbim* and *al da'at rabbim* to become relevant, exists precisely because we shifted from *shvuah* to *neder*. A *shvuah* regarding a past event — "I never took any money from the estate" — is determined at the moment it is uttered. Either the words are true or they constitute a *shvuat sheker*. There is nothing for a *chakham* to act upon after the fact; no release is possible because the binding obligation exists only in the past. A *neder*, by contrast, creates an ongoing obligation that can be modified or dissolved. All the analytical machinery the *gemara* deploys in this section is a consequence of that initial substitution.

## Witness signatures

### Biblical requirement

The *mishnah* now turns to a second *tikkun olam*: *ve-ha-eidim chosmin al ha-get mi-pnei tikkun olam* — the witnesses sign on the *get* for the sake of *tikkun olam*. At first glance this is surprising. Surely the requirement that witnesses sign a document is biblical in origin? The verse says *ve-chasov be-sefer ve-chasom* — "write in a scroll and sign." Is this not a *de-oraita* requirement?

### Rabbi Elazar view

*Rava* explains: this *mishnah* is needed specifically according to the position of *Rabbi Elazar*, who holds *edei mesirah karti* — that biblically, the critical witnesses are those who are physically present at the moment the *get* is handed over, not those who sign the document. According to *Rabbi Elazar*, another *gemara* teaches, the verse cited is not a mandatory requirement for documents to be valid, but rather *eitzah tovah ka-mashma lan* — sound advice. The Torah recommends that signatures be included, but does not make them a biblical prerequisite for the legal efficacy of a *get*. Biblically, what is needed is the physical presence of witnesses at the moment of transmission — *edei mesirah* — whether understood as required for the *davar she-be-ervah* dimension, or as the witnesses whose observation of the handover constitutes the validating act of the *shtar* itself. Either way, their names need not appear in writing.

### Rabbinic innovation

It was the Rabbis who established *edei chasimah* — that witnesses must actually sign their names — *mi-pnei tikkun ha-olam*. The practical concern is stated plainly: *de-zimnin de-maisi sahadei* — sometimes witnesses die, or they travel far away (*azli li-medinas ha-yam*), and the woman is left with a document that divorces her but without any means of proving that she is indeed divorced. The signed *get* solves this problem elegantly: the very document she receives to effectuate her divorce simultaneously serves as her evidence of divorce. She need not locate the original witnesses; their names are there in the document itself.

An interesting structural point follows from this. One might have imagined an alternative arrangement: the *get* itself would not bear signatures, but a subsequent rabbinic *beit din* document would separately attest that the divorce occurred. The Rabbis' solution is more elegant: the single document that functions as the instrument of divorce also bears witness to its own execution.

### Original practice

One might ask how witness signatures were verified in earlier times. A *baraita* records the history: *ba-rishonah hayah kosev ani ploni chasamti eid* — originally, witnesses would write only their first name, something like "I, Reuven, have signed as a witness." This was sufficient *im kesav yado yotzei mi-makom acher* — if the person's handwriting was recognizable from another source, one could confirm the authenticity of the signature. *Ve-im lav, pasul* — but if the handwriting was not independently recognizable, the signature could not be verified. With a common name like Reuven, there could be any number of individuals who might have signed, and without a means of identifying the particular person, the *get* could be rendered invalid.

### Rabban Gamliel takana

*Amar Rabban Gamliel: takana gedola hiskinu* — they enacted a major *takana* that witnesses must spell out their names fully in *gittin*: the full name and patronymic, as in Reuven ben Yaakov. With this information, it becomes possible to identify the specific individual, to locate other exemplars of his handwriting, and to verify the signature properly. The *baraita* presents this as the content of the *tikkun olam* referred to in the *mishnah* — indeed, *Tosafot* notes that the conjunction in the *mishnah* (*ve-ha-eidim chosmin*) might suggest this was part of *Rabban Gamliel*'s own *takana*, but the *gemara* makes clear that witness signatures predated *Rabban Gamliel*; his contribution was the standardization of full identifying names.

### Symbols as signatures

The *gemara* then raises an apparent tension. If the concern is identification, what about those sages who signed with pictorial symbols rather than names? *Ve-ha Rav tziyer kavra* — *Rav* drew a little fish. *Rav Chanina tziyer charusa* — *Rav Chanina* drew a palm tree. *Rav Chisda* drew a *samech* (presumably for *Chisda*). *Rav Oshaya* drew an *ayin*. *Rabba bar Rav Huna tziyer makusa* — he drew a sail of a ship. These symbols, then, served as valid signatures among the *tanna'im* and *amora'im*.

This raises two distinct questions: first, are such symbols distinctive enough to prevent forgery, in the way a handwritten signature is? Second, how can a symbol adequately identify the signatory when, unlike a name with a patronymic, it gives no inherent information about who the person is?

### Symbol establishment

The *gemara* addresses the second concern: *shani rabbanan de-bekiei be-simanaihu* — the rabbinic class was distinguished by the fact that their symbols were publicly known. These scholars had recognizable, known symbols, such that people could identify the symbol as belonging to a particular sage. Once a symbol is publicly recognized and associated with a known individual, it functions no differently from a fully spelled-out name for purposes of identification and verification.

This prompts a further question: *me-ikara be-mai apteinu* — but how did they begin using their symbols in the first place? There is a circular problem: you cannot use a symbol on a *shtar* before people know it, but how do they come to know it before you use it? The *gemara* answers: *be-diskei* — through personal correspondence. Before a scholar began signing *shtarot* with his symbol, he would use it extensively in private letters and communications, such that the symbol became recognizable. Only once it was publicly known could it then be employed on legal documents.

## Prozbul introduction

### Hillel's takana

The *mishnah* in *Shevi'it* is cited: *pruzbul eino meshamet* — a loan protected by a *pruzbul* is not annulled in the *shemita* year. This is identified as one of the institutions that *Hillel ha-Zaken* established. The *mishnah* explains the motivation: *she-ra'ah ha-am she-nimnu mi-lhalvot zeh la-zeh* — Hillel saw that people were refraining from lending money to one another as the *shemita* year approached, thereby transgressing what is written in the Torah: *hishamer lecha pen yihyeh davar im levavecha beliya'al* — "Beware lest there be a treacherous thought in your heart" leading you to withhold a loan from the poor because the *shemita* year is near. Hillel responded by establishing the *pruzbul*.

### Mitzvah framing

A crucial point must be made about how this institution is framed, because it is commonly misunderstood. The *pruzbul* is not presented as a pragmatic concession to economic reality — as though the Torah's law of *shemita* had become too burdensome and a workaround was needed. Rather, it is framed entirely in terms of competing *mitzvot*. The existing situation — people refusing to lend — was causing active biblical violations. People were transgressing the commandment to lend to the poor. Hillel's *takana* is thus presented as a resolution to a conflict between two Torah mandates, not as an accommodation of human weakness against a Torah institution.

This framing matters significantly. Hillel does not start by saying this *mitzvah* is economically unworkable; he says that the current state of affairs is causing the transgression of biblical commandments. There is still a genuine trade-off: is it better that people transgress the prohibition against refusing to lend but that the *shemita* annulment functions properly, or is it better to preserve the lending relationship at the cost of not fulfilling the annulment? Hillel clearly judges that the well-being of the poor — who are better served by receiving loans that must eventually be repaid than by seeing their creditors refuse to lend at all — tips the scale toward protecting the lending relationship. But the entire analysis is conducted within the framework of Torah *mitzvot*, not against it.

### Prozbul text

The *gemara* records the formula of the *pruzbul*: *mosrani lachem ploni u-ploni dayanim she-bi-makom ploni* — "I am handing over to you, Reuven and Shimon, the judges in such-and-such a place, that any debt owed to me by so-and-so I retain the right to collect at any time I wish." The judges or witnesses then sign below.

### Debt transfer mechanism

The underlying legal mechanism, clarified by the *mishnah* in *Shevi'it* and by *Rashi* here, is *moseir shtarotav le-veit din* — handing the debt over to the *beit din*. By making this declaration before the court, the creditor is understood to transfer the authority to collect the debt to the *beit din* rather than retaining it as a personal claim against his neighbor. The verse *shamot* — "you shall release" — speaks of a personal claim: you shall not exact it from your neighbor. But once the debt is in the hands of *beit din* rather than of the individual creditor, it is the communal institution of the court, not the individual, that holds the right to collect. The *shemita* annulment operates against individual creditors; it does not apply to *beit din*. *Rashi* makes this explicit: *mosrani lachem* — "I am handing it to you, you will be the ones to demand it, and I will not."

## Legal basis for prozbul

### Gemara's challenge

The *gemara* presses with a fundamental question: *u-mi ika midi de-mi-d'Oraita meshamta shevi'it, ve-hiskin Hillel de-lo meshamta* — is there any case where the Torah biblically annuls a debt, and Hillel can simply establish by *takana* that it should not be annulled? How can a rabbinic institution override a biblical law of *shemita*?

### Abaye's answer

*Amar Abaye*: the entire discussion concerns *shemita* of our time, and it follows the position of *Rebbi* — that the annulment of money debts in our era is only rabbinic (*derabbanan*), not biblical. Since the Rabbis instituted the debt annulment in the first place, they retain the authority to create exceptions to it or workarounds from it. Hillel's *pruzbul* operates against a rabbinic institution, not a biblical one.

### Rabbi's teaching

The source for this claim is a *baraita* in which *Rebbi* expounds: *ve-zeh dvar ha-shemita shamot* — "this is the manner of the *shemita*, you shall release." The verse speaks of two *shemitot* simultaneously — *shemitat karka* (the sabbatical release of the land) and *shemitat kesafim* (the annulment of debts): *bizman she-ata meshamet karka, ata meshamet kesafim; bizman she-i ata meshamet karka, i ata meshamet kesafim* — when you observe the release of the land, you observe the release of money; when you do not observe the release of the land, you do not observe the release of money. The two *shemitot* are halakhically interdependent.

### No yovel status

Since there is no *shemitat karka* in force biblically in our era, there is correspondingly no biblical *shemitat kesafim*. But why is there no biblical *shemitat karka* nowadays? *Rashi* explains that *shemitat karka* is itself dependent on *yovel* — the Jubilee year — and *yovel* requires *kol yoshveha aleha*, all twelve tribes settled on the land. This condition was not met even during the Second Temple period (*Bayit Sheni*), and certainly has not been met since. Consequently, even during the period of *Hillel*, there was no biblical *yovel*, and without biblical *yovel* there was no biblical *shemita* of land, and without biblical *shemita* of land there was no biblical *shemita* of money. The entire framework at issue was already rabbinic before *Hillel* instituted his *pruzbul*.

### Prior puzzle

The *gemara* is still not satisfied. Even if we accept that the existing debt annulment was only rabbinic, there is a prior puzzle: how did the Rabbis come to institute a rabbinic *shemitat kesafim* in the first place? Before they acted, during *Bayit Sheni*, there was no biblical *shemita* of money — debts had to be paid. By what authority did the Rabbis institute a new obligation that people's debts would be annulled, effectively taking away the creditor's biblical right to collect?

### Shev ve'al ta'aseh

*Amar Abaye*: the institution of rabbinic *shemita* was possible because it operates in the mode of *shev ve-al ta'aseh* — passive non-action rather than active violation. The Rabbis did not actively compel anyone to violate a biblical obligation; they simply instructed creditors not to collect. This is analogous to the Rabbinic practice of setting aside the *lulav* when the relevant day falls on *Shabbat* — a passive restraint from action rather than an active override of the Torah.

### Hefker beit din

*Rava* offers a stronger answer: *hefker beit din hefker* — what a *beit din* declares ownerless becomes ownerless. The Rabbis have inherent authority to control the disposition of property. When it comes to monetary matters, the court can release people from their debts, reassign ownership, or restructure financial obligations. It was by virtue of this principle that the Rabbis were able to institute a rabbinic *shemitat kesafim* — they exercised their authority over property to declare debts annulled.

### Proof texts

*Rabbi Yitzchak* provides the biblical source for *hefker beit din hefker*: the verse in *Ezra*: *ve-chol asher lo yavo li-shloshet ha-yamim ke-atzat ha-sarim ve-ha-zekenim, yocharam kol rechusho* — anyone who does not appear within three days, in accordance with the directive of the officers and elders, shall have all his property put under the ban. This demonstrates that the court has the power to divest an individual of his property. *Rabbi Elazar* adduces a different verse: *eileh ha-nachalot asher nichalu Elazar ha-Kohen vi-Yehoshua bin Nun* — these are the portions that *Elazar ha-Kohen* and *Yehoshua bin Nun* apportioned. The text mentions *rashei ha-avot* — heads of the fathers — rather than heads of the tribes, which the *gemara* reads as a comparison: just as a father may bequeath to his children whatever he wishes, so too the leaders of the nation may apportion and redistribute the wealth of the people as they see fit.

The two proof texts point in somewhat different directions. The first (*Ezra*) demonstrates the punitive dimension of the court's power — property can be seized as a sanction. The second (*Yehoshua*) points to something broader: a fundamental, non-punitive authority to restructure ownership, comparable to a father's power to bequeath. *Hefker beit din hefker*, in *Rava*'s usage, invokes this broader principle.

## Tosafot critique

### Mechanism vs. will

*Rashi* notes that once *hefker beit din hefker* is introduced, it resolves not only the question of how the Rabbis could institute rabbinic *shemitat kesafim* in the first place, but also the original challenge of how *Hillel* could institute the *pruzbul* even against a potentially biblical *shemita*. If the court can control ownership, then even if the Torah's *shemita* voided the debt, *beit din* could simply reinstate it after *shemita*, and Hillel's institution is thereby justified even on a *de-oraita* level.

### Propriety concern

*Tosafot* takes sharp issue with this. Their question is not about mechanism but about propriety. As *Tosafot* puts it: even granting that Hillel had the mechanism of *hefker beit din hefker* available to him, *lo hayah lo le-Hillel la'akor shevi'it she-hi de-oraita* — it was not appropriate for Hillel to uproot a Torah institution of *shemita* in such a blatant way. The existence of a mechanism does not, by itself, justify its use. To openly deploy rabbinic power to directly override a foundational biblical institution is a different category of act than working within or around it.

### Fig leaf approach

*Tosafot* draws an illuminating parallel to the concept of *hafka'at kiddushin* — retroactive rabbinic annulment of a marriage. The *Rashba* holds that the Rabbis have the technical power to retroactively annul a marriage, but they will not exercise that power openly. To look back at twenty years of recognized marriage and declare it never existed would be a blatant and intolerable show of rabbinic power against a biblical institution. Therefore, the Rabbis will only invoke *hafka'at kiddushin* in the presence of a *get* — even if the *get* is technically invalid (*pasul*), its existence provides a cover: it looks as though the woman was divorced by the *get*, not as though the Rabbis voided the *kiddushin* by fiat. This is what might be called the *fig leaf* approach: the mechanism used must at least appear to be a non-confrontational resolution, not a naked exercise of power against the Torah.

### Prozbul as fig leaf

The same logic applies to *pruzbul*. *Tosafot* explains why the *gemara* frames the *pruzbul* around the mechanism of *moseir shtarotav le-beit din* — handing over the debt to the court — rather than simply invoking *hefker beit din hefker* directly. The transfer-to-*beit-din* framework provides the necessary appearance of legitimacy. Rather than openly declaring that the Rabbis are overriding *shemita*, the *pruzbul* presents itself as a structural change in who holds the debt: it is now the *beit din's* debt, not the individual creditor's, and *shemita* does not apply to *beit din*. Even if the underlying *takana* is really being driven by *hefker beit din hefker*, the *pruzbul* gives it a face that avoids the appearance of direct confrontation with a Torah law.

### Transfer doesn't work

Indeed, *Tosafot* goes further and suggests that the transfer to *beit din* may not actually be legally operative in the strict sense — it may be a legal fiction rather than a real transfer of the debt. But that does not undermine its purpose; if anything, it makes the point more sharply. The function of the *moseir shtarotav* framework is not primarily mechanical but presentational. We need a structure in which the *pruzbul* does not look like a straight override of a Torah institution, even if the underlying justification is ultimately rooted in the court's broader power over property.

This provides the proper lens through which to understand *tikkun olam* in the context of *pruzbul*. Several dimensions converge. First, the problem is framed not as economic hardship but as a competition of Torah *mitzvot* — the *pruzbul* resolves a clash between the commandment to lend and the institution of *shemita* by siding with what better serves the poor. Second, even where the Rabbis have the raw mechanism to act, they do not deploy it brazenly against a Torah institution; they require a framework — the *fig leaf* of the transfer to *beit din* — that presents the resolution as something other than a naked rabbinic override. These are not mere technicalities; they reflect a deeper commitment to operating within the spirit of Torah authority even when exercising the outer limits of rabbinic power.
