# Bava Batra 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzedakah vs. Korbanot

### Tzedakah Replaces Korban…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשַׁגֵּשׁ אֹרְחָתַיהּ דְּאִימֵּיהּ״, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּלְבַּשׁ צְדָקָה כַּשִּׁרְיֹן״? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה שִׁרְיוֹן זֶה – כׇּל קְלִיפָּה וּקְלִיפָּה מִצְטָרֶפֶת לְשִׁרְיוֹן גָּדוֹל, אַף צְדָקָה – כׇּל פְּרוּטָה וּפְרוּטָה מִצְטָרֶפֶת לְחֶשְׁבּוֹן גָּדוֹל. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וּכְבֶגֶד עִדִּים כׇּל צִדְקֹתֵינוּ״; מָה בֶּגֶד זֶה – כׇּל נִימָא וְנִימָא מִצְטָרֶפֶת לְבֶגֶד גָּדוֹל, אַף צְדָקָה – כׇּל פְּרוּטָה וּפְרוּטָה מִצְטָרֶפֶת לְחֶשְׁבּוֹן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **who perverted the ways of his mother.** He said **in the name of Rabbi Elazar: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And He donned charity like a coat of mail"** (Isaiah 59:17)? This verse serves **to tell you** that **just as** with regard to **this coat of mail, each and every scale** of which it is fashioned **combines to** form **one large coat of mail, so too** with regard to **charity, each and every *peruta*** that one gives **combines to** form **a great sum. Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The same idea is derived **from here,** as it is stated: **"And all our charity is as a polluted garment"** (Isaiah 64:5). **Just as** with regard to **this garment, each and every thread** in it **combines to** form **one large garment, so too** with regard to **charity, each and every *peruta* combines to** form **a great sum.**

The daf opens at the very end of 9b, in the middle of a major *sugya* on *tzedakah*. Rabbi Elazar teaches: *bizman shebeis hamikdash kayam, adam shokeil shiklo umischaper lo* — when the Temple stood, a person would give his shekel and thereby receive atonement. And now that the Temple no longer stands, *im osim tzedakah mutav* — if one gives *tzedakah*, it is good. It is worth noting that we more commonly think of *tefillah* as the replacement for *korbanot*, yet here the *gemara* explicitly draws the parallel to *tzedakah* instead. There have been earlier comparisons as well — the concept of *tzedakah* was learned, in part, from money given to the Temple — but here the identification is direct: the money one gives to the poor serves as a kind of *korban*.

### Gov't Intervention

The passage continues: *v'im lav boin ovdei kochavim umezuyei* — if you do not take care of your own poor, the non-Jewish government will step in and do the job for you, collecting taxes and imposing that burden on you regardless. The *gemara* adds, *v'af al pi kein nechshav lahem l'tzedakah* — and even so, even when they are compelled by outside forces, it still counts as *tzedakah*, as the verse says: *v'nogsaich tzedakah* — those who oppress you will extract *tzedakah* from you, and it will still bear that character. The practical implication is worth considering: one should think carefully about how much one already gives to *tzedakah* — and how much goes to taxes whose proceeds support various social welfare programs.

### Small Gifts Accumulate

The *gemara* then quotes a teaching of Rabbi Elazar: *mai d'chsiv, v'yilbash tzedakah k'shiryon* — what is meant by the verse, "and he clothed himself in righteousness like plate armor"? While the verse speaks of righteousness in its plain sense, the *gemara* reads it through the rabbinic lens of *tzedakah* as charitable giving. The point of the metaphor is *ma shiryon zeh kol klipa u'klipa mitztarefet l'shiryon gadol* — just as every individual piece of metal plate joins together to form a great armor, so too with *tzedakah*: *kol pruta u'pruta mitztarefet l'cheshbon gadol* — every single penny combines into a great sum.

Rabbi Chanina offers a parallel from a different verse: *u'k'veged idim kol tzidkoseinu* — like a worn garment are all our acts of righteousness. The emphasis here falls on the word *beged*, garment. *Ma beged zeh kol nima u'nima mitztarefet l'veged gadol* — just as every individual thread is nothing on its own but together forms a real garment, so too *kol pruta u'pruta mitztarefet l'cheshbon gadol* — every small gift combines into something significant. Interestingly, this point is made from the perspective of the giver's reward: each small amount of *tzedakah* one gives accumulates into a great reckoning of merit.

## Rav Sheshet Digression

### Rav Sheshet's Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי קָרוּ לֵיהּ ״עוּלָּא מְשַׁגֵּשׁ אֹרְחָתַיהּ דְּאִימֵּיהּ״? דִּבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מִנַּיִן לִמְצוֹרָע בִּימֵי סְפוֹרוֹ, שֶׁמְטַמֵּא אָדָם? אָמַר לוֹ: הוֹאִיל וּמְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים, מְטַמֵּא אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Why was** this Sage **called the infant who perverted the ways of his mother?** It was because of the following incident: **Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami asked Rav Sheshet: From where** is it derived **that a leper renders a person impure** through contact **during his days of counting,** i.e., during the seven days that the leper must count from the start of his purification process, when he brings his birds, until he completes that process with the bringing of his sacrifices? Rav Sheshet **said to him: Since he renders** his **garments impure,** as it states that on the seventh day of his purification he must wash his clothes (see Leviticus 14:9), **he** also **renders people impure.**

The *gemara* introduces a passage attributed to Rav Sheshet in the name of Rabbi Elazar — the very teaching about the *shiryon* we just encountered — but opens with a curious label. The teacher is called *olel meshageish archasei d'imei* — the suckling who perverted the path of his mother. This strange epithet is applied to Rav Sheshet, and the *gemara* explains it through a story.

### Metzora Tumah Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּילְמָא טוּמְאָה בְּחִבּוּרִים שָׁאנֵי, דְּהָא הֵסִיט נְבֵילָה – דִּמְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא אָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥadvoi **said to him: Perhaps connected impurity is different,** meaning perhaps he renders his garments impure because they are connected to the source of the impurity, but this does not mean that he renders impure a person whom he touches. A proof for this distinction may be brought from the fact **that** if **one moves an animal carcass,** an action that renders him impure even if there was no actual contact with the carcass, **he renders** the **garments** that he is wearing **impure but does not render** another **person impure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאֶלָּא שֶׁרֶץ דִּמְטַמֵּא אָדָם – מְנָלַן? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּמְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁרֶץ בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״אוֹ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל שֶׁרֶץ״. אֶלָּא שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע דִּמְטַמֵּא אָדָם – מְנָלַן? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּהוֹאִיל וּמְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים, מְטַמֵּא אָדָם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע נָמֵי בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״אוֹ אִישׁ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַנּוֹגֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to him: But from where do we** derive that the carcass of **a creeping animal renders a person impure? Is it not because** we know **that it renders** his **garments impure?** Rav Aḥadvoi **said to him: It is written explicitly** with regard to the carcass of **a creeping animal: "Or a man who touches any creeping animal,** whereby he may be made unclean" (Leviticus 22:5). Rav Sheshet said to him: **But from where do we** derive that **semen renders a person impure? Is it not because** we say that **since it renders** his **garments impure** (see Leviticus 15:17) **it** also **renders a person impure?** Rav Aḥadvoi **said to him: It is also written explicitly** with regard to **semen: "Or a man** from whom semen is expelled" (Leviticus 22:4), and the Sages expound the superfluous word "or" as serving **to include** as impure **one who touches** semen.

Rav Acha bar Adda posed a series of difficult questions to Rav Sheshet: *minayin l'tzipporei yemei tzipporo she'metam'in adam* — from where do we know that a *metzora* during the period of *tzipporo* — after the initial purification process with the birds has been performed and he is awaiting re-entry into the camp — has *tumah* sufficient to render a person *tamei*? For a person to become *tamei* from another person, that person must be an *av hatumah*. What is his *tumah* status during this intermediate period?

Rav Sheshet responded: he is *metamei bgadim* — he renders his garments *tamei* — and therefore, by extension, he renders a person *tamei*. Rav Acha pushed back: perhaps there is a distinction between rendering one's own garments *tamei* while they are worn versus rendering another person or separate garments *tamei*. The case of *heses neveilah* illustrates this: one who lifts or carries a *neveilah* becomes *tamei* along with the garments he is wearing, yet that person does not thereby acquire the capacity to render other people *tamei*. So the fact that something makes one's worn garments *tamei* does not prove it can make other persons *tamei*.

Rav Sheshet then tried a different angle: *ella sheretz d'metamei adam menalan* — how do we know a dead *sheretz* renders a person *tamei*? Is it not inferred from its capacity to render garments *tamei*? Rav Acha replied: *sheretz behedya ksiv bei* — there is an explicit verse, *o ish asher yiga b'chol sheretz* — it is derived from an explicit *pasuk*, not from *bgadim* at all. Rav Sheshet then attempted the case of *shichvas zera* — semen, which renders garments *tamei* and, seemingly by the same logic, renders a person *tamei*. Rav Acha deflected this as well: *shichvas zera nami behedya ksiv bei* — there too, we have an explicit verse, *asher tetzei mimenu shichvas zera*, from which we extend even to one who merely touches it, *l'rabos es hanogea*. In each case, the *gemara* demonstrates that one cannot infer the *metzora*'s capacity to render a person *tamei* from his capacity to render garments *tamei*: either the cases involve garments being worn in different circumstances, or they rest on explicit *pesukim* rather than inference.

### Punishment & Healing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ בִּבְדִיחוּתָא. חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, אִישְׁתִּיק רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי וְאִתְיַקַּר תַּלְמוּדֵיהּ. אָתְיָא אִימֵּיהּ וְקָא בָּכְיָא קַמֵּיהּ, צָוְוחָה צָוְוחָה וְלָא אַשְׁגַּח בָּהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: חֲזִי לְהָנֵי חַדְיֵי דִּמְצֵית מִינַּיְיהוּ. בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי עֲלֵיהּ, וְאִיתַּסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that with each of his answers Rav Aḥadvoi **responded to** Rav Sheshet **in a mocking tone,** intimating that he doubted Rav Sheshet's grasp of Torah verses. **Rav Sheshet was** deeply **offended,** and as punishment, **Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami became mute and forgot his learning.** Rav Aḥadvoi's **mother came and wept before** Rav Sheshet. **She cried and cried but he ignored her.** As she had once been Rav Sheshet's nursemaid, **she said to him: Look at these breasts of mine from which you suckled.** Upon hearing that, Rav Sheshet **prayed for mercy for** Rav Aḥadvoi, **and he was healed.** And since it was Rav Aḥadvoi whose behavior led to his mother's acting in this manner, he was referred to as the infant who perverted his mother's ways.

With each answer Rav Sheshet offered, Rav Acha pushed back — and apparently did so with some amusement, laughing at what he may not have intended as mockery. *Chalash datei d'Rav Sheshet* — Rav Sheshet became deeply despondent, feeling that he was being ridiculed. As a result, Rav Acha fell mute and, worse, *ishtakach talmudei* — he forgot all of his learning. A woman then came — Rashi identifies her as Rav Sheshet's mother, while Tosafot identifies her as Rav Acha's mother who had served as Rav Sheshet's nursemaid — and wept before Rav Sheshet, imploring him on behalf of Rav Acha. She cried and cried and he did not respond. She then said: *chazi l'hanei chadi d'matzit mineihu* — look at this breast from which you suckled. Have compassion. In the end, *bei rachamei alei v'itassi* — he prayed for Rav Acha, and he was healed. This is the origin of that curious name — the suckling who perverted his mother's path. How this story connects to the *tzedakah* discussion that precedes it appears to be a genuine digression.

### Explicit Pasuk Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַאֲתָאן עֲלַהּ; מְנָא לַן? כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כִּבּוּס בְּגָדִים בִּימֵי סְפוֹרוֹ, וְנֶאֱמַר כִּבּוּס בְּגָדִים בִּימֵי חִלּוּטוֹ, מָה לְהַלָּן מְטַמֵּא אָדָם – אַף כָּאן מְטַמֵּא אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: With regard to **that which we arrived at,** i.e., this particular subject, let us clarify the matter: **From where do we** derive that a leper renders impure a person with whom he comes into contact during his days of counting? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai says: Washing garments is stated with respect to his days of counting, and washing garments is stated with respect to his days of confirmed** leprosy, after he has been declared conclusively impure by a priest. **Just as there,** when he is a confirmed leper **he renders a person impure,** as is explicitly stated in the Torah, **so too here,** during his days of counting **he renders a person impure.**

Before returning to the *tzedakah* discussion, the *gemara* resolves the underlying *halachic* question. Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai teaches through a *gezeirah shavah*: the Torah speaks of washing garments both during the *metzora*'s period of *sipuro* and during his period of *chaluto* — his full *tumah* status. *Mah lehalan metamei adam, af kan metamei adam* — just as during the period of full *tumah* the *metzora* renders a person *tamei*, so too during the *sipuro* period. The answer came not from logical inference but from an explicit scriptural parallel.

## Secret Giving > Moshe

### Greater Than Moshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: גָּדוֹל הָעוֹשֶׂה צְדָקָה בַּסֵּתֶר, יוֹתֵר מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ – דְּאִילּוּ בְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ כְּתִיב: ״כִּי יָגֹרְתִּי מִפְּנֵי הָאַף וְהַחֵמָה״, וְאִילּוּ בְּעוֹשֶׂה צְדָקָה כְּתִיב: ״מַתָּן בַּסֵּתֶר יִכְפֶּה אָף, [וְשֹׁחַד בַּחֵק חֵמָה עַזָּה]״. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אַף כּוֹפֶה, חֵמָה אֵינוֹ כּוֹפֶה; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשֹׁחַד בַּחֵק חֵמָה עַזָּה״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשּׁוֹחַד בַּחֵיק – חֵמָה עַזָּה. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל דַּיָּין שֶׁנּוֹטֵל שֹׁחַד – מֵבִיא חֵמָה עַזָּה לָעוֹלָם [שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: וְשֹׁחַד בַּחֵק וְגוֹ׳].
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes its discussion of the virtues of giving charity. **Rabbi Elazar said: One who performs** acts of **charity in secret is greater than Moses, our teacher. Whereas with regard to Moses, our teacher, it is written: "For I was afraid of the anger and the wrath"** (Deuteronomy 9:19), **with regard to one who performs** acts of **charity it is written: "A gift in secret pacifies anger, and a present in the bosom fierce wrath"** (Proverbs 21:14). The Gemara comments: **And this** statement **disagrees with** a statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** A secret gift **pacifies anger,** but it **does not pacify wrath, as it is stated: "And a present in the bosom fierce wrath,"** meaning that **although a present is in the bosom,** i.e., even if one gives charity in secret, nevertheless **fierce wrath** can still harm him. **There are** those **who say** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** as follows: **Any judge who accepts a bribe brings fierce wrath upon the world, as it is stated: "And a present in the bosom** fierce wrath."

Returning now to the main discussion, Rabbi Elazar teaches: *gadol oseh tzedakah b'seser yoser miMoshe Rabbeinu* — one who gives *tzedakah* in private is greater than Moshe Rabbeinu. This is a striking statement. Tosafot clarifies: it does not mean that such a person becomes greater than Moshe Rabbeinu as a person, but rather that the act has greater power to calm divine anger than Moshe's prayers did. *D'ilu b'Moshe Rabbeinu kesiv ki yagorti mipnei ha'af v'hacheimah* — by Moshe it is written, "I was fearful before the anger and the wrath," indicating that Moshe could not counter or stand against God's anger and wrath. *V'ilu b'oseh tzedakah kesiv matan b'seser yichpeh af* — whereas regarding *tzedakah* it is written: a gift in private covers over the anger. And *v'shochad becheik cheimah azah* — giving in the hidden, private realm calms even fierce rage. The act of secret giving thus stands where Moshe's prayer could not.

The *gemara* notes a dissent: *u'pliga d'Rabbi Yitzchak*. Rabbi Yitzchak holds that *af kofeh cheimah eino kofeh* — *tzedakah* can cover *af*, anger, but not *cheimah azah*, the fiercer rage. He reads the verse as split in two: the word *yichpeh*, "covers," applies only to the *af*, while *v'shochad becheik cheimah azah* — despite giving in private, the fierce rage remains beyond what *tzedakah* can address.

There is a second version of Rabbi Yitzchak's teaching: *kol hanosein shochad meivi cheimah azah l'olam* — any judge who takes a bribe brings fierce divine anger upon the world. On this reading the verse speaks of two entirely separate matters: one about *tzedakah* and one about bribery.

### Six vs. Eleven Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל הַנּוֹתֵן פְּרוּטָה לְעָנִי – מִתְבָּרֵךְ בְּשֵׁשׁ בְּרָכוֹת, וְהַמְפַיְּיסוֹ בִּדְבָרִים – מִתְבָּרֵךְ בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה בְּרָכוֹת. הַנּוֹתֵן פְּרוּטָה לְעָנִי מִתְבָּרֵךְ בְּשֵׁשׁ בְּרָכוֹת – דִּכְתִיב: ״הֲלֹא פָרֹשׂ וְגוֹ׳, וַעֲנִיִּים מְרוּדִים תָּבִיא בָיִת (וְגוֹ׳) כִּי תִרְאֶה עָרֹם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Anyone who gives a *peruta* to a poor person receives six blessings, and whoever consoles him with words** of comfort and encouragement **receives eleven blessings.** The Gemara explains: **One who gives a *peruta* to a poor person receives six blessings, as it is written: "Is it not to share** your bread with the hungry, **and that you shall bring the poor that are cast out to your house? When you see the naked,** that you cover him" (Isaiah 58:7). And the next verses list six blessings: "Then shall your light break forth like the dawn, and your health shall spring forth speedily, and your righteousness shall go before you, the glory of the Lord shall be your rearguard. Then, shall you call, and the Lord shall answer; you shall cry, and He shall say: Here I am" (Isaiah 58:8–9).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְפַיְּיסוֹ בִּדְבָרִים מִתְבָּרֵךְ בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה בְּרָכוֹת – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְתָפֵק לָרָעֵב נַפְשְׁךָ וְנֶפֶשׁ נַעֲנָה תַּשְׂבִּיעַ, וְזָרַח בַּחֹשֶׁךְ אוֹרֶךָ וַאֲפֵלָתְךָ כַּצׇּהֳרָיִם. וְנָחֲךָ ה׳ תָּמִיד, וְהִשְׂבִּיעַ בְּצַחְצָחוֹת נַפְשְׁךָ וְגוֹ׳, וּבָנוּ מִמְּךָ חׇרְבוֹת עוֹלָם, מוֹסְדֵי דוֹר וָדוֹר תְּקוֹמֵם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And whoever consoles** a poor person **with words** of comfort and encouragement **receives eleven blessings, as it is stated: "And if you draw out your soul to the hungry and satisfy the afflicted soul, then shall your light shine in darkness, and your gloom shall be as the noonday. And the Lord shall guide you continually, and satisfy your thirst in drought… And they that shall be of you shall build the old waste places, you shall raise up the foundations of many generations"** (Isaiah 58:10–12).

Rabbi Yitzchak continues: *kol hanosein prutah l'ani misbarech b'sheish brachos* — one who gives even a penny to a poor person is blessed with six blessings. But *v'hamifayaso bidvarim* — if one uses words to console the poor person, to actually address his spirit and make him feel better — *misbarech b'achas esrei brachos* — he is blessed with eleven blessings. Money is important, but the capacity to speak to a person's *neshamah*, to console and support him emotionally, is worth nearly double.

The six blessings come from the verse: *halo faros lara'eb lachmecha* — is it not to break your bread for the hungry — and the sequence that follows: *v'aniyim merudim tavi bayis, ki sireh arom v'chisiso, az yibaka kashachar orecha, v'halach l'fanecha tzidkecha, v'chvod Hashem ya'asfecha, az tikra v'Hashem ya'aneh, tishava v'yomar hineni* — six blessings. The eleven come from: *v'safek lara'eb nafshecha* — you extend your very soul, not merely your money, to the hungry person — *v'nefesh na'ana tasbia*, you satisfy the soul of the afflicted — and the verses that follow.

Tosafot raises a variant reading: some wish to emend the text so that the eleven blessings apply only to one who both gives *tzedakah* and consoling words together. They base this on the phrase *v'nefesh na'ana tasbia*, arguing it could refer to giving food. But the *gemara* rejects this: *nefesh na'ana tasbia* means *tasbia bidvarim* — you satiate the soul with words, and the verse speaks of pure emotional support without monetary giving. The two tracks — money and words — remain distinct, and pure consolation carries even greater reward than giving alone.

## Pursuing Tzedakah

### God Provides Means

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״רוֹדֵף צְדָקָה וָחָסֶד יִמְצָא חַיִּים צְדָקָה וְכָבוֹד״? מִשּׁוּם דְּרוֹדֵף צְדָקָה – יִמְצָא צְדָקָה?! אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: כׇּל הָרוֹדֵף אַחַר צְדָקָה – הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַמְצִיא לוֹ מָעוֹת וְעוֹשֶׂה בָּהֶן צְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "He who pursues charity and mercy finds life, charity, and honor"** (Proverbs 21:21)? Is this to say that **because one has pursued charity, he** himself **shall find charity?** That is, shall the reward of one who has always given charity be that he will eventually become poor and other people will act charitably toward him? **Rather,** the verse serves **to tell you** that with regard to **anyone who pursues charity,** giving to the poor and leading others to do so, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, furnishes him with money with which to perform** his acts of **charity.**

Rabbi Elazar raises a question about the verse: *rodeif tzedakah v'chesed yimtza chayim tzedakah v'chavod* — one who pursues *tzedakah* and *chesed* will find life, *tzedakah*, and honor. The difficulty: if *rodeif tzedakah* means you run to give charity, and the reward is *yimtza tzedakah* — you will find *tzedakah* — that means you will need to receive charity from others, which is hardly a blessing. The answer: *ella lomar lach kol harodeif achar tzedakah Hakadosh Baruch Hu mamtzi lo mammon l'asos bahen tzedakah* — if you pursue *tzedakah*, the Holy One will provide you with the financial means to keep giving. The reward for giving is the ability to give more.

### Worthy Recipients

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַמְצִיא לוֹ בְּנֵי אָדָם הַמְהוּגָּנִים לַעֲשׂוֹת לָהֶן צְדָקָה, כְּדֵי לְקַבֵּל עֲלֵיהֶם שְׂכָרוֹ. לְאַפּוֹקֵי מַאי? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּדָרֵשׁ רַבָּה – דְּדָרֵשׁ רַבָּה, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיִהְיוּ מֻכְשָׁלִים לְפָנֶיךָ, בְּעֵת אַפְּךָ עֲשֵׂה בָּהֶם״? אָמַר יִרְמְיָה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, [אֲפִילּוּ] בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכּוֹפִין אֶת יִצְרָן וּמְבַקְּשִׁין לַעֲשׂוֹת צְדָקָה לְפָנֶיךָ, הַכְשִׁילֵם בִּבְנֵי אָדָם שֶׁאֵינָן מְהוּגָּנִין, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְקַבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶן שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, sends him people who are deserving of charity, so that he will reap** his just **reward for** helping **them.** The Gemara comments: **What** does this statement serve **to exclude?** It serves **to exclude Rabba's homiletical interpretation** of a different verse, **as Rabba taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Let them be made to stumble before You; deal thus with them in the time of Your anger"** (Jeremiah 18:23)? The prophet **Jeremiah said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, even when** those wicked men who pursued me **subdue their inclinations and seek to perform** acts of **charity before You, cause them to stumble upon** dishonest **people who are not deserving** of charity, **so that they will not receive reward** for coming to their assistance.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a different reading: *Hakadosh Baruch Hu mamtzi lo bnei adam hamehuganim l'asos bahen tzedakah* — if you pursue *tzedakah*, God will ensure that the people who come to you asking for it are truly deserving, *kidei lekabel aleihem scharo* — so that you receive proper reward for your giving.

This teaching stands *l'apukei* — in contrast to — what Rava expounded from the verse in Yirmiyahu: *v'yiyu michshalim l'fanecha* — let them stumble before You. Yirmiyahu said to God: even when these people overcome their *yetzer* and try to perform *mitzvos*, *hachshilem bivnei adam she'einam mehuganim* — cause them to stumble by sending them undeserving people to give to, so that they receive no reward for their efforts, *kidei shelo yekablu aleihem schar*. Rav Nachman's point is thus the inverse: if you genuinely pursue *tzedakah*, God will help you give it to the right people, and you will receive full reward. It is worth pausing here. The framing of worthy recipients as the means by which one earns reward can make the *ani* feel like an instrument — like a *lulav* one picks up to fulfill a *mitzvah*. This tension is worth keeping in mind as the *sugya* continues.

### Promise of Good Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: כׇּל הָרָגִיל לַעֲשׂוֹת צְדָקָה – זוֹכֶה, הָוְיִין לוֹ בָּנִים בַּעֲלֵי חׇכְמָה, בַּעֲלֵי עוֹשֶׁר, בַּעֲלֵי אַגָּדָה. בַּעֲלֵי חׇכְמָה – דִּכְתִיב:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Anyone who is accustomed to performing** acts of **charity merits** blessing; **he will have sons who are masters of wisdom, masters of wealth, and masters of *aggada*.** The Gemara explains: **Masters of wisdom, as it is written:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יִמְצָא חַיִּים״. בַּעֲלֵי עוֹשֶׁר – דִּכְתִיב: ״צְדָקָה״. בַּעֲלֵי אַגָּדָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכָבוֹד״ – כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְכָבוֹד״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״כָּבוֹד חֲכָמִים יִנְחָלוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** "He who pursues charity and mercy, **finds life"** (Proverbs 21:21), and with regard to wisdom it is written: "He who finds Me, finds life" (Proverbs 8:35). **Masters of wealth, as it is written:** "He who pursues charity and mercy finds **charity,"** meaning he will be able to give charity. **Masters of *aggada*, as it is written:** "He who pursues charity and mercy, finds **honor."** And how do we know that this refers to masters of *aggada*? **It is written here "honor," and it is written there: "The wise shall inherit honor"** (Proverbs 3:35).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds: *kol haragil la'asot tzedakah zocheh sheyihyu lo banim ba'alei chochmah ba'alei osher ba'alei agadah* — one who makes a regular practice of giving *tzedakah* merits children who are wise, wealthy, and skilled in *aggadah*. This serves an important social function: in a society where rabbinic achievement is the highest form of status, *tzedakah* — accessible to all — is here presented as the path by which even the wealthy philanthropist who is not himself a *talmid chacham* can merit children who are *talmidei chachamim*, as well as children who are themselves wealthy and able to continue the tradition of giving.

The three blessings are sourced from the verse *rodeif tzedakah v'chesed yimtza chayim tzedakah v'chavod*: *chayim* corresponds to Torah wisdom, as Rashi notes from the verse *ki motzai matza chayim* — wisdom is life. *Tzedakah* here corresponds to wealth and the ongoing capacity to give. *Chavod*, honor, corresponds to skill in *aggadah*. Rashi explains this beautifully: *ba'alei agadah mitoch shehem darshanim u'moshchim et halev hakol mechabdim otam* — those who are skilled orators and can draw hearts to them receive wide public recognition and honor. It is a distinct skill from that of the *talmid chacham*, and it brings its own form of *kavod* — still the honor of sages, as the parallel verse states, *kavod chachamim yinchalu*.

## Why Poverty Exists

### Pagan Theology Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, יֵשׁ לוֹ לְבַעַל הַדִּין לַהֲשִׁיבְךָ וְלוֹמַר לְךָ: אִם אֱלֹהֵיכֶם אוֹהֵב עֲנִיִּים הוּא, מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵינוֹ מְפַרְנְסָן? אֱמוֹר לוֹ: כְּדֵי שֶׁנִּיצּוֹל אָנוּ בָּהֶן מִדִּינָהּ שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם. וְזוֹ שְׁאֵלָה שָׁאַל טוֹרָנוּסְרוּפוּס הָרָשָׁע אֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אִם אֱלֹהֵיכֶם אוֹהֵב עֲנִיִּים הוּא, מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵינוֹ מְפַרְנְסָם? אָמַר לוֹ: כְּדֵי שֶׁנִּיצּוֹל אָנוּ בָּהֶן מִדִּינָהּ שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir would say: An opponent may bring an argument against you and say to you: If your God loves the poor, for what** reason **does He not support them** Himself? In such a case, **say to him:** He commands us to act as His agents in sustaining the poor, thereby saving ourselves from *geihinnom*. **And this is the question that Turnus Rufus the wicked asked Rabbi Akiva: If your God loves the poor, for what** reason **does He not support them** Himself? Rabbi Akiva **said to him:** He commands us to sustain the poor, **so that through them** and the charity we give them **we will be saved from the judgment of Gehenna.**

A *braita* introduces a challenge the Rabbis took seriously: Rabbi Meir teaches that the opposing side — whether an internal skeptic or an external critic — can pose the following: *im elokeichem ohev aniyim hu, mipnei mah eino mefarnesam* — if your God truly loves the poor, why does He not simply provide for them? The existence of poverty, in this view, signals divine disfavor toward the poor. This was indeed a widespread theology in the ancient pagan world: wealth demonstrated divine favor, poverty reflected divine displeasure, and therefore the wealthy were righteous and the poor were sinners. While the Torah's framework of *schar v'onesh* operates at a collective level and never reaches the point of equating an individual's wealth with his righteousness, the critique has force within the pagan framework and must be answered on its own terms.

### Redemption Through Giving

The Talmudic response: *kidei shenitzol anu bahem midina shel geihinnom* — God has created poverty to give us the opportunity to perform the *mitzvah* of *tzedakah*, thereby saving ourselves from *geihinnom*. The tension implicit in this answer — that it treats the *ani* as a means by which the giver earns merit — is real and was noted. Perhaps this response is best read as an answer offered *l'shitasam*, on the terms of the questioner, rather than as a full theological account.

### Turnusrufus Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: [אַדְּרַבָּה], זוֹ שֶׁמְּחַיַּיבְתָּן לְגֵיהִנָּם! אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁכָּעַס עַל עַבְדּוֹ, וַחֲבָשׁוֹ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְצִוָּה עָלָיו שֶׁלֹּא לְהַאֲכִילוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׁקוֹתוֹ. וְהָלַךְ אָדָם אֶחָד וְהֶאֱכִילוֹ וְהִשְׁקָהוּ. כְּשֶׁשָּׁמַע הַמֶּלֶךְ, לֹא כּוֹעֵס עָלָיו? וְאַתֶּם קְרוּיִם עֲבָדִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לִי בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עֲבָדִים״!
>
> **Translation:** Turnus Rufus **said to** Rabbi Akiva: **On the contrary, it is this** charity **which condemns you,** the Jewish people, **to Gehenna** because you give it. **I will illustrate** this **to you with a parable. To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a king of flesh and blood who was angry with his slave and put him in prison and ordered that he should not be fed or given to drink. And one person went ahead and fed him and gave him to drink. If the king heard about this, would he not be angry with** that person? **And you,** after all, **are called slaves, as it is stated: "For the children of Israel are slaves to Me"** (Leviticus 25:55). If God decreed that a certain person should be impoverished, one who gives him charity defies the will of God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֶמְשׁוֹל לְךָ מָשָׁל: לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁכָּעַס עַל בְּנוֹ וַחֲבָשׁוֹ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְצִוָּה עָלָיו שֶׁלֹּא לְהַאֲכִילוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׁקוֹתוֹ. וְהָלַךְ אָדָם אֶחָד וְהֶאֱכִילוֹ וְהִשְׁקָהוּ. כְּשֶׁשָּׁמַע הַמֶּלֶךְ, לֹא דּוֹרוֹן מְשַׁגֵּר לוֹ?! וַאֲנַן קְרוּיִן בָּנִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּנִים אַתֶּם לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to** Turnus Rufus: **I will illustrate** the opposite **to you with a** different **parable. To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a king of flesh and blood who was angry with his son and put him in prison and ordered that he should not be fed or given to drink. And one person went ahead and fed him and gave him to drink. If the king heard** about this once his anger abated, **would he not** react by **sending** that person **a gift? And we are called sons, as it is written: "You are sons of the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 14:1).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אַתֶּם קְרוּיִם בָּנִים וּקְרוּיִן עֲבָדִים, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאַתֶּם עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם אַתֶּם קְרוּיִן בָּנִים, וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאֵין אַתֶּם עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם אַתֶּם קְרוּיִן עֲבָדִים; וְעַכְשָׁיו אֵין אַתֶּם עוֹשִׂים רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם! אָמַר לוֹ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הֲלֹא פָרֹס לָרָעֵב לַחְמֶךָ וַעֲנִיִּים מְרוּדִים תָּבִיא בָיִת״; אֵימָתַי ״עֲנִיִּים מְרוּדִים תָּבִיא בָיִת״ – הָאִידָּנָא, וְקָאָמַר: ״הֲלֹא פָרֹס לָרָעֵב לַחְמֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Turnus Rufus **said to him: You are called sons and you are called slaves. When you fulfill the will of the Omnipresent, you are called sons; when you do not fulfill the will of the Omnipresent, you are called slaves. And** since **now you do not fulfill the will of the Omnipresent,** the parable that I offered is more apt. Rabbi Akiva **said to him:** The verse **states: "Is it not to share your bread with the hungry, and** that **you shall bring the poor that are cast out to your house?"** (Isaiah 58:7). **When** do we **bring the poor that are cast out** into our **houses? Now,** when we have to billet the Roman soldiers in our homes; **and** about that very time, the verse **states: "Is it not to share your bread with the hungry?"**

The *gemara* notes that Turnusrufus the wicked, the Roman governor in the time of Rabbi Akiva, posed the same question — *elokeichem ohev aniyim hu, mipnei mah eino mefarnesam* — and received the same initial response. Turnusrufus then pushed back: *zu she'mechayvatan l'geihinnom* — by giving to the poor you are actually sinning. He offered a parable: a human king who was angry at his servant imprisoned him and commanded that no one give him food or drink. One who defied that order and brought him food — would the king not be furious with that person? You are called God's servants, as the verse states, *ki li venei Yisrael avadim avadai hem*. The natural order of the world reflects God's decree; by subverting it through charity, you are violating the divine will.

Rabbi Akiva responded with a counter-parable. It is not a servant the king imprisoned — it is a son. *Keshem she'ka'as al beno, chavsho be'veit ha'asurim v'tzivah alav she'lo le'hakhilo v'lo le'hashkoto* — the king grew angry at his son and imprisoned him, commanding that no one feed or give him drink. But if someone came and fed and cared for the son — *keshe'shama ha'melech lo doron meshagei'r lo* — would the king not send that person a gift in gratitude? Even if a parent must impose a punishment, what the parent truly wants is for the child to be cared for. And we are called God's children: *banim atem la'Hashem Elokeichem*.

Turnusrufus pressed the contradiction: we are called both children and servants in different verses. *Bizman she'atem osim retzono shel Makom atem keruyim banim* — when you do God's will you are His children; *bizman she'ein atem osim retzono shel Makom atem keruyim avadim* — when you do not, you are His servants. And now, under Roman subjugation, clearly you are not in God's favor — so you are servants, and the parable of the servant applies.

Rabbi Akiva responded by citing the verse explicitly: *halo faros lara'eg lachmecha v'aniyim merudim tavi bayis* — is it not to break your bread for the hungry and bring the outcast poor into your house? *Eimatai?* When is this commanded? *Ha'idna* — now, precisely in this time of difficulty and subjugation. The command to support the poor addresses us even, or especially, at the moment when we are under oppression. The theological answer is ultimately: we remain God's children, and even within a period of divine displeasure God wants us to respond with compassion to those in need, and through that compassion to merit restoration.

## Income & Expenses Decreed

### Sustenance & Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי שָׁלוֹם: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמְּזוֹנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָדָם קְצוּבִין לוֹ מֵרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, כָּךְ חֶסְרוֹנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָדָם קְצוּבִין לוֹ מֵרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה. זָכָה – ״הֲלֹא פָרֹס לָרָעֵב לַחְמֶךָ״. לֹא זָכָה – ״וַעֲנִיִּים מְרוּדִים תָּבִיא בָיִת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shalom taught: Just as a person's** entire **livelihood is allocated to him from Rosh HaShana,** when God issues His judgments for the entire year, **so too are a person's** monetary **losses allocated to him from Rosh HaShana.** If **one merits,** the following verse is applied to him: **"You shall share your bread with the hungry,"** i.e., he will spend the sum allocated to him on gifts of charity; and if **he does not merit,** the following verse is applied to him: **"You shall bring the poor that are cast out to your house,** i.e., he will be compelled by the government to billet soldiers in his house and feed them against his will.

Rav Yehuda bar Meramya teaches: *keshem she'mezonotav shel adam ketzuvim lo me'Rosh Hashanah, kach chesronotav shel adam ketzuvim lo me'Rosh Hashanah* — just as a person's income for the year is decreed on *Rosh Hashanah*, so too are his losses. Two figures are set: gross income, and total expenditure. *Zachah, halo faros lara'eg lachmecha* — if one merits, that decreed amount of loss will go to support the poor through *tzedakah*. *Lo zachah, v'aniyim merudim tavi bayit* — if one does not merit, the Roman tax collectors will enter and take that amount regardless. You will lose the money either way. The question is whether you will give it or whether it will be taken from you.

### Nephews' Tzedakah Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דִּבְנֵי אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, חֲזָא לְהוּ בְּחֶילְמָא דְּבָעוּ לְמִיחְסַר שְׁבַע מְאָה דִּינָרֵי. עַשִּׂינְהוּ שְׁקַל מִינַּיְיהוּ לִצְדָקָה, פּוּשׁ גַּבַּיְיהוּ שִׁיבְסַר דִּינָרֵי. כִּי מְטָא מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְכִיפּוּרֵי, שַׁדּוּר דְּבֵי קֵיסָר נַקְטִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is **like this** incident **involving the nephews of Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai,** who once **saw in a dream that** his nephews **were** destined **to lose seven hundred dinars** over the course of the year. **He encouraged them** and **took** money **from them for charity, and they were left with seventeen dinars** out of the seven hundred. **When Yom Kippur eve arrived, the government sent** messengers who came and **took** the remaining seventeen dinars.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: לָא תִּדְחֲלוּן, שִׁיבְסַר דִּינָרֵי גַּבַּיְיכוּ שַׁקְלִינְהוּ מִינַּיְיכוּ. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מְנָא יָדְעַתְּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: חֶלְמָא חֲזַאי לְכוּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְאַמַּאי לָא אֲמַרְתְּ לַן [דְּנִיתְּבִינְהוּ]? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אָמֵינָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתַעְבְּדוּ מִצְוָה לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said to them: Do not fear** that they will take even more from you; **they took from you** the **seventeen dinars** that were still **with you.** The nephews **said to him: How did you know?** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to them: I saw a dream about you,** and he related his dream to them. **They said to him: And why did you not tell us** about the dream? Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to them: I said,** It is better **that they perform a mitzva for its own sake.** Had you known from the start that you were fated to lose that amount of money, the mitzva would not have been performed purely for its own sake.

The *gemara* illustrates this with the story of Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai's nephews. He saw in a dream that they were destined to lose 700 dinars in the coming year. Over the course of the year, without their knowledge of the dream, he made repeated appeals to them, collecting for various causes, until he had taken 683 dinars from them for *tzedakah*. Only 17 remained of the decreed 700. On *Erev Yom Kippur* — Rashi indicates the dream came on *Motza'ei Yom Kippur* the prior year, completing a full annual cycle — agents of the Caesar came and demanded exactly 17 dinars. The full 700 had been expended.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai told his nephews: do not be afraid of further demands. *Shivasar dinarei degavaichu shaklinhu mineichu* — the 17 that were still decreed, they took from you; there will be no more. They asked: how did you know? He told them of the dream. They asked: why did you not tell us at the outset, so that we could have gladly given the full 700? *Amina ki heichi d'ta'avdu mitzvah lishmah* — because I wanted you to give the *mitzvah* for its own sake, not out of the calculated awareness that you were going to lose the money anyway.

### Giving for Its Sake

There is an inherent irony here: by teaching this story, the Rabbis introduce a degree of *shelo lishmah* into our giving. Having internalized the lesson, one is tempted to think, "I should give generously to *tzedakah* because presumably some portion of my income is decreed to be lost this year anyway — might as well give it purposefully." We don't have the dream; we don't know the exact amount. Perhaps this leaves just enough uncertainty — just the right measure of *shelo lishmah* — to encourage more generous giving, without entirely undermining the ideal of *lishmah*.

## Ignoring Poor=Avodah Zarah

### Rav Papa's Fall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא הֲוָה סָלֵיק בְּדַרְגָּא, אִישְׁתְּמִיט כַּרְעֵיהּ בָּעֵי לְמִיפַּל. אֲמַר: הַשְׁתָּא (כֵּן) [כּוּ], אִיחַיַּיב מַאן דְּסָנֵי לַן כִּמְחַלְּלֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת וּכְעוֹבְדֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ חִיָּיא בַּר רַב מִדִּפְתִּי לְרַב פָּפָּא: שֶׁמָּא עָנִי בָּא לְיָדְךָ וְלֹא פִּרְנַסְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa was** once **climbing** up **a ladder** when **his foot slipped and he almost fell. He said: Now, is the one who hates us,** a euphemism for himself, **liable like Shabbat desecrators and idol worshippers,** who are subject to death by stoning, which is similar to death by falling, the punishment that Rav Pappa narrowly escaped? **Ḥiyya bar Rav of Difti said to Rav Pappa: Perhaps a poor person** once **approached you and you did not sustain him,** and therefore you were given a glimpse of the punishment that you actually deserve.

Rav Papa was ascending some steps when his foot slipped and he nearly fell. He said: I almost died the death of one who violates *Shabbos* or worships *avodah zarah* — i.e., the death of *skilah*, being thrown from a height, which suggested some grave sin. Chiya bar Rav of Diftei said to him: *shema ani ba l'yadcha v'lo parnastato* — perhaps a poor person came to you and you did not provide for him.

### Eyes Hidden = Idolatry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַמַּעֲלִים עֵינָיו מִן הַצְּדָקָה, כְּאִילּוּ עוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ פֶּן יִהְיֶה דָבָר עִם לְבָבְךָ בְּלִיַּעַל״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״יָצְאוּ אֲנָשִׁים בְּנֵי בְלִיַּעַל״; מַה לְהַלָּן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אַף כָּאן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: Anyone who turns his eyes away from** one seeking **charity is** considered **as if he worships idols.** From where is this derived? **It is written here: "Beware that there be not a base thought in your heart…**and your eye be evil against your poor brother, and you give him nothing" (Deuteronomy 15:9). **And it is written there: "Certain base men have gone out…**and have drawn away the inhabitants of their city, saying, Let us go and serve other gods" (Deuteronomy 13:14). **Just as there,** the base men sin with **idolatry, so too here,** the base thought is treated like **idolatry.**

This leads to the *braita* of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: *kol hama'alim einav min hatzedakah ke'ilu oved avodah zarah* — anyone who hides his eyes from *tzedakah*, who ignores the *ani* before him, is as if he has worshiped *avodah zarah*. The derivation: the verse here uses the word *beliyaal* — *hishamer lecha pen yihyeh davar im l'vavcha beliyaal* — and the verse regarding *avodah zarah* also uses the word *beliyaal* — *yatze'u anashim bnei beliyaal*. The Maharsha notes that these are the only two occurrences of *beliyaal* in the five books of Moshe. *Mah halan avodah zarah, af kan avodah zarah* — just as the latter involves *avodah zarah*, so too the former.

Beyond the textual parallel, one can offer a thematic reading as well. Hiding one's eyes from *tzedakah* involves a kind of acceptance of the natural order — a fatalistic theology, the very worldview that Turnusrufus pressed upon Rabbi Akiva. In that sense, it is indeed *ke'ilu oved avodah zarah*: you are worshiping the forces of fate rather than acting on the divine imperative. Alternatively, the refusal to give reflects a worship of money itself — a sense that what you earned is entirely your own, denying God's role in providing your wealth, which is its own form of denying God's sovereignty.

## Cosmic Peace via Tzedakah

### Advocates Before God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כֹּל צְדָקָה וָחָסֶד שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, שָׁלוֹם גָּדוֹל וּפְרַקְלִיטִין גְּדוֹלִין בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לַאֲבִיהֶן שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳, אַל תָּבֹא בֵּית מַרְזֵחַ וְאַל תֵּלֵךְ לִסְפּוֹד וְאַל תָּנֹד לָהֶם, כִּי אָסַפְתִּי אֶת שְׁלוֹמִי מֵאֵת הָעָם הַזֶּה וְגוֹ׳ [אֶת] הַחֶסֶד וְאֶת הָרַחֲמִים״; ״חֶסֶד״ – זוֹ גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, ״רַחֲמִים״ – זוֹ צְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: All** acts of **charity and kindness that Jews perform in this world** make **great peace and** are **great intercessors between the Jewish people and their Father in Heaven, as it is stated: "So said the Lord, enter not into a house of mourning, neither go to lament nor bemoan them, for I have taken away My peace from this people,** says the Lord, **both kindness and mercy"** (Jeremiah 16:5). **"Kindness"; this** is referring to **acts of kindness. "Mercy"; this** is referring to acts of **charity.** This indicates that when there is kindness and mercy, God is at peace with His people.

A *braita* taught by Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yose: *kol tzedakah v'chesed she'Yisrael osim ba'olam hazeh, shalom gadol u'peraklitin gedolim bein Yisrael l'Avihem she'ba'shamayim* — all *tzedakah* and *chesed* that Israel performs in this world creates great peace and great heavenly advocates between Israel and their Father in Heaven. In the rabbinic framework, *tzedakah* refers to the giving of money and *chesed* refers to personal acts of assistance and kindness — and *chesed*, notably, is not limited to the poor.

The source verse is from Yirmiyahu: God says He has taken away His *shalom* — His peace and wellbeing — from the people, removing *et hachesed v'et harachamim*. *Chesed, zu gemilus chasadim; rachamim, zu tzedakah*. That which God withdraws — *chesed* and *rachamim* — is precisely what constitutes His *shalom* between Himself and Israel. Restoring them through acts of *tzedakah* and *chesed* is therefore the means of restoring that relationship. There is sensitivity worth noting in equating *tzedakah* with *rachamim* — compassion: giving must flow from genuine empathy, seeing the other person's full humanity, not merely treating them as an object of pity.

### Brings Redemption Closer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה צְדָקָה שֶׁמְּקָרֶבֶת אֶת הַגְּאוּלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳, שִׁמְרוּ מִשְׁפָּט וַעֲשׂוּ צְדָקָה, כִּי קְרוֹבָה יְשׁוּעָתִי לָבֹא וְצִדְקָתִי לְהִגָּלוֹת״. הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר, עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים קָשִׁים נִבְרְאוּ בָּעוֹלָם: הַר קָשֶׁה – בַּרְזֶל מְחַתְּכוֹ, בַּרְזֶל קָשֶׁה – אוּר מְפַעְפְּעוֹ, אוּר קָשֶׁה – מַיִם מְכַבִּין אוֹתוֹ, מַיִם קָשִׁים – עָבִים סוֹבְלִים אוֹתָן, עָבִים קָשִׁים – רוּחַ מְפַזַּרְתָּן, רוּחַ קָשֶׁה – גּוּף סוֹבְלוֹ, גּוּף קָשֶׁה – פַּחַד שׁוֹבְרוֹ, פַּחַד קָשֶׁה – יַיִן מְפִיגוֹ, יַיִן קָשֶׁה – שֵׁינָה מְפַכַּחְתּוֹ; וּמִיתָה – קָשָׁה מִכּוּלָּם [וּצְדָקָה מַצֶּלֶת מִן הַמִּיתָה] – דִּכְתִיב: ״וּצְדָקָה תַּצִּיל מִמָּוֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Great is charity in that it advances the redemption, as it is stated: "So said the Lord, uphold justice and do charity, for My salvation is near to come, and My righteousness to be revealed"** (Isaiah 56:1). **He would say: Ten strong entities were created in the world,** one stronger than the other. **A mountain is strong,** but **iron,** which is stronger, **cleaves it. Iron is strong,** but **fire melts it. Fire is strong,** but **water extinguishes it. Water is strong,** but **clouds bear it. Clouds are strong,** but **wind disperses them. Wind is strong,** but the human **body withstands it.** The human **body is strong,** but **fear breaks it. Fear is strong,** but **wine dispels it. Wine is strong,** but **sleep drives it off. And death is stronger than them all, but charity saves** a person **from death, as it is written: "And charity delivers from death"** (Proverbs 10:2, 11:4).

Rabbi Yose teaches: *gedolah tzedakah she'mekarevet et hage'ulah* — *tzedakah* hastens the redemption. *Ko amar Hashem: shimru mishpat va'asu tzedakah ki krovah yeshuati lavo* — thus says God: observe justice and perform *tzedakah*, for My salvation is close to coming.

Rabbi Yose further taught that ten hard things were created in the world, each one undone by the next in a chain — a structure reminiscent of *Chad Gadya*. A mountain is hard, iron cuts it. Iron is hard, fire melts it. Fire is hard, water extinguishes it. Water is hard, clouds carry it. Clouds are hard, wind scatters them. Wind is hard, the body contains it. The body is hard, fear humbles it. Fear is hard, wine dissipates it. Wine is hard, sleep dispels it. And *umitah kashah mikulam* — death is the hardest of all. Yet *utzedakah matzela min hamitah* — and *tzedakah* saves from death, as it is written, *utzedakah tatzil mimavet*.

The sequence seems discontinuous: all the prior items follow the *Chad Gadya* pattern, each destroying the previous, but the transition from sleep to death breaks the chain. One suggestion: sleep is described in the *gemara* elsewhere as one-sixtieth of death, which might serve as the conceptual bridge.

## Lender to God

### Immediate Divine Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי: בּוֹא וּרְאֵה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִדַּת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא – מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם; מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם, אָדָם מֵבִיא דּוֹרוֹן גָּדוֹל לַמֶּלֶךְ – סָפֵק מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ הֵימֶנּוּ, סָפֵק אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ הֵימֶנּוּ; [וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר מְקַבְּלִים אוֹתוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ –] סָפֵק רוֹאֶה פְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, סָפֵק אֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה פְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ. וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֵינוֹ כֵּן, אָדָם נוֹתֵן פְּרוּטָה לֶעָנִי – זוֹכֶה וּמְקַבֵּל פְּנֵי שְׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲנִי בְּצֶדֶק אֶחֱזֶה פָנֶיךָ, אֶשְׂבְּעָה בְהָקִיץ תְּמוּנָתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Dostai, son of Rabbi Yannai, taught: Come and see that the attribute of the Holy One, Blessed be He, is not like the attribute of flesh and blood.** An illustration of **the attribute of flesh and blood** is that when **a person brings a great gift to the king, it is uncertain whether** the king **will accept it from him or will not accept it from him. And if you say that** the king **will accept it from him, it is uncertain whether** the person who brought the gift **will** eventually **see the face of the king, or will not see the face of the king. But the Holy One, Blessed be He, does not** act in **this way.** Even when **a person gives a** mere ***peruta* to a poor person, he merits to receive the Divine Presence, as it is stated: "As for me, I will behold Your face through charity; I will be satisfied, when I awake, with Your likeness"** (Psalms 17:15).

Dostai bar Yannai expounds: *bo u'rei shelo kemidat Hakadosh Baruch Hu midat basar vadam* — come and see that God is unlike human beings. When you bring a great gift to a human king, you do not know whether it will be accepted, and even if accepted, you do not know whether you will gain an audience with the king. God is not so. *Adam noten pruta l'ani, zoche umekabel pnei hashchina* — one who gives a penny to a poor person immediately merits an encounter with the divine presence. *Shene'emar: ani betzedek echezeh panecha* — "I, with righteousness, will see Your face."

One can read this verse in a deeper register as well. If we truly saw the *tzelem Elokim* — the divine image — in every human being, how could we fail to give? When we allow ourselves to pass by a person with outstretched hand, it is because we have ceased to see them as fully human, as bearers of the divine image. To give *tzedakah* in response to genuinely seeing that person is itself a *re'iyat pnei HaShem*, an encounter with God's presence. This connects, too, to the earlier equation of ignoring the poor with *avodah zarah*: both involve a failure to see God where God is.

### Tzedakah Before Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר יָהֵיב פְּרוּטָה לְעָנִי, וַהֲדַר מְצַלֵּי – אָמַר, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲנִי בְּצֶדֶק אֶחֱזֶה פָנֶיךָ״. מַאי ״אֶשְׂבְּעָה בְהָקִיץ תְּמוּנָתֶךָ״? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁמְּנַדְּדִין שֵׁינָה מֵעֵינֵיהֶם בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַשְׂבִּיעָן מִזִּיו הַשְּׁכִינָה לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **Rabbi Elazar would** first **give a *peruta* to a poor person and** only **then** would he **pray. He said: As it is written** in the same verse: **"I will behold Your face through charity."** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of that which is written: **"I will be satisfied, when I awake, with your likeness"? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: These are Torah scholars,** who in pursuit of their studies **banish sleep from their eyes in this world, and the Holy One, Blessed be He, satiates them with the radiance of the Divine Presence in the World-to-Come.**

Rabbi Elazar made it his practice to give a *pruta* to an *ani* before praying. He would cite the same verse: *ani betzedek echezeh panecha* — first I give *tzedakah*, and then I stand before God. One can also read this as follows: before one feels entitled to stand before God and petition for one's own needs — which is the heart of *Shemoneh Esrei* — one must first demonstrate that one cares about others. If you want God to respond to you with compassion and care, first show that you respond that way to those around you.

### Two Types of Salvation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״מַלְוֵה ה׳ חוֹנֵן דַּל״? אִלְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאוֹמְרוֹ – כִּבְיָכוֹל ״עֶבֶד לֹוֶה לְאִישׁ מַלְוֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "He that graciously gives to the poor makes a loan to the Lord,** and that which he has given, He will pay him back" (Proverbs 19:17)? How can it be that one is considered to have granted a loan to God? **Were it not** explicitly **written in the verse, it would be impossible to say this,** that somebody who is gracious to a poor person is seen as lending to God. It would be impertinent, since **"the borrower is servant to the lender"** (Proverbs 22:7), **as it were.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״לֹא יוֹעִיל הוֹן בְּיוֹם עֶבְרָה, וּצְדָקָה תַּצִּיל מִמָּוֶת״, וּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יוֹעִילוּ אוֹצְרוֹת רֶשַׁע, וּצְדָקָה תַּצִּיל מִמָּוֶת״ – שְׁתֵּי צְדָקוֹת הַלָּלוּ לָמָּה? אַחַת שֶׁמַּצִּילָתוֹ מִמִּיתָה מְשׁוּנָּה, וְאַחַת שֶׁמַּצִּילָתוֹ מִדִּינָהּ שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם. וְאֵי זוֹ הִיא שֶׁמַּצִּילָתוֹ מִדִּינָהּ שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם? הַהוּא דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״עֶבְרָה״ – דִּכְתִיב: ״יוֹם עֶבְרָה הַיּוֹם הַהוּא״. וְאֵי זוֹ הִיא שֶׁמַּצִּילָתוֹ מִמִּיתָה מְשׁוּנָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: Rabbi Yoḥanan raises a contradiction** between two texts. In one place **it is written: "Riches profit not on the day of wrath, but charity delivers from death"** (Proverbs 11:4), **and** elsewhere **it is written: "Treasures of wickedness profit nothing, but charity delivers from death"** (Proverbs 10:2). **Why** is it necessary to have **these two** verses about **charity,** that it delivers from death? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba continues: **One** verse serves to teach **that** charity **delivers from an unnatural death** in this world, **and one** verse serves to teach **that** charity **delivers from the judgment of Gehenna** in the World-to-Come. **And** in **which** of the verses **is that** charity **which delivers from the judgment of Gehenna** mentioned? It is in **that** verse **in which "wrath" is written, as** with regard to the day of judgment **it is written: "That day is a day of wrath"** (Zephaniah 1:15). **And which** type of charity **is that which delivers from an unnatural death?**

Rabbi Yochanan then raises a question: why does the verse *utzedakah tatzil mimavet* appear twice — once as *lo yo'il hon b'yom evrah utzedakah tatzil mimavet*, and once as *lo yo'ilu otzros resha utzedakah tatzil mimavet*? If it is the same teaching, why repeat it? The answer: *shtei tzedakos hallalu* — these two mentions correspond to two distinct forms of salvation from death. One saves from *misah meshunah* — a painful or disfiguring death in this world, so that one dies peacefully rather than through suffering. The other saves from *dinat geihinnom* — the punishment worse than death, the judgment of *geihinnom*. Which *tzedakah* saves from *geihinnom*? The one associated with the word *evrah* — anger — since *yom evrah hayom hahu* refers to the day of judgment. Which saves from *misah meshunah* in this world?

### Anonymous Giving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹתְנָהּ – וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לְמִי נוֹתְנָהּ, נוֹטְלָהּ – וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מִמִּי נוֹטְלָהּ. נוֹתְנָהּ וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לְמִי נוֹתְנָהּ – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּמַר עוּקְבָא; נוֹטְלָהּ וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מִמִּי נוֹטְלָהּ – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי אַבָּא. וְאֶלָּא הֵיכִי לֶיעְבֵּיד? לִיתֵּיב לְאַרְנָקִי שֶׁל צְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is the type in which **one gives** the charity **without knowing to whom he gave it,** and the other **one takes it without knowing from whom he took it.** The Gemara explains: **One gives it without knowing to whom he gave it,** this serves **to exclude** the practice **of Mar Ukva,** who would personally give charity to poor people without their knowing he was the donor. The other one **takes it without knowing from whom he took it;** this serves **to exclude** the practice **of Rabbi Abba,** who would render his money ownerless, so that poor people would come and take it without his knowing whom he helped, although they would know from whom the money came. The Gemara asks: **Rather, how** then **should one act** to conceal his own identity and also remain ignorant of the identities of the recipients? The Gemara answers: The best method is **to put** the money **into the charity purse.**

### Mar Ukva & Rabbi Abba

*Nosenah v'eino yodea l'mi nosenah, v'notlah v'eino yodea mimi notlah* — the giver does not know to whom he gives, and the recipient does not know from whom he receives. This is the ideal form of *tzedakah*, the highest level — which the Rambam systematized in his eight stages, though the basis is clearly here in the *gemara*.

The *gemara* clarifies. *Nosenah v'eino yodea l'mi nosenah* — the giver does not know who receives — this stands *l'apukei mi'demar Ukva*, in contrast to Mar Ukva's practice, in which at least the giver did not know who received but the recipient did not know the giver's identity either. *Notlah v'eino yodea mimi notlah* — the recipient does not know who gave — stands *l'apukei m'Rabbi Abba*, in contrast to Rabbi Abba's method. How does one achieve full anonymity on both sides? *Leisiv l'arneki shel tzedakah* — one gives to a communal charity fund administered by a trustworthy person who maintains the privacy of both giver and recipient. Neither party ever learns the other's identity.

## Promise of Reward

### Male Children Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מָה יַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם וְיִהְיוּ לוֹ בָּנִים זְכָרִים? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יְפַזֵּר מְעוֹתָיו לַעֲנִיִּים. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: יְשַׂמַּח אִשְׁתּוֹ לִדְבַר מִצְוָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִתֵּן אָדָם פְּרוּטָה לְאַרְנָקִי שֶׁל צְדָקָה, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מְמוּנֶּה עָלֶיהָ כְּרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן! כִּי קָא אָמְרִינַן – דִּמְמַנֵּי עֲלַהּ כְּרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from what is taught in a *baraita*: **What should a person do to have male offspring? Rabbi Eliezer says: He should distribute his money liberally among the poor. Rabbi Yehoshua says: He should gladden his wife before** engaging in the **mitzva** of conjugal relations. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: A person should not donate a *peruta* to the charity purse unless** a great and trusted individual **like Rabbi Ḥananya ben Teradyon is appointed** as supervisor **over it.** This seems to indicate that putting money into the charity box is not always preferred. The Gemara answers: **When we say** that putting money into the charity box is the preferred way to give charity, this is referring to **when** a man **like Rabbi Ḥananya ben Teradyon is appointed** as supervisor **over it.**

The *gemara* asks: *mah ya'aseh adam veyihyu lo banim zacharim* — what should a person do to merit male children? Rabbi Eliezer says: *yefazer me'osav le'aniyim* — scatter his money to the poor, give prolifically and without excessive calculation. Rabbi Yehoshua says: *yisammach ishto b'dvar mitzvah* — he should attend to his wife's needs in the context of marital intimacy. These are strikingly different answers; at least Rabbi Yehoshua's connects directly to the question of children being conceived, while the connection between scattering *tzedakah* and male children is less immediately apparent.

### Trustworthy Middlemen

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov teaches within the same *braita*: *lo yitten adam peruta l'ani shel tzedakah ella im ken memunah alav keRebbi Chananya ben Teradyon* — a person should not give a penny to a *tzedakah* collector unless that person is as trustworthy as Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon. At first glance this seems in tension with the earlier recommendation to give through an anonymous middleman. The resolution: *ki kamina demamnu aleih keRebbi Chananya ben Teradyon* — the recommendation to give through a fund presupposes that the fund is run by someone of that caliber. One who gives responsibly should investigate: what is the overhead of the organization, how wisely is the money deployed, how trustworthy are those administering it? This is an important counterbalance to an earlier passage that cautioned against suspecting those actively engaged in *tzedakah* collection. That passage concerned not casting aspersions on those performing their role; this passage concerns the prior decision of where to direct one's giving.

### Moshe's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אָמַר מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, בַּמֶּה תָּרוּם קֶרֶן יִשְׂרָאֵל? אָמַר לוֹ: בְּ״כִי תִשָּׂא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses other matters concerning charity. **Rabbi Abbahu says: Moses said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, with what shall the horn of Israel be exalted?** God **said to him: With** the passage of **"When you raise,"** i.e., Israel will be exalted by way of the donations and charity that they will give, as it is stated: "When you raise the heads of the children of Israel…then shall they give" (Exodus 30:12).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת שְׁלֹמֹה בֶּן דָּוִד: עַד הֵיכָן כֹּחָהּ שֶׁל צְדָקָה? אָמַר לָהֶן, צְאוּ וּרְאוּ מָה פֵּירֵשׁ דָּוִד אַבָּא: ״פִּזַּר נָתַן לָאֶבְיוֹנִים – צִדְקָתוֹ עוֹמֶדֶת לָעַד, קַרְנוֹ תָּרוּם בְּכָבוֹד״. רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״הוּא מְרוֹמִים יִשְׁכֹּן, מְצָדוֹת סְלָעִים מִשְׂגַּבּוֹ, לַחְמוֹ נִתָּן, מֵימָיו נֶאֱמָנִים״; מָה טַעַם ״מְרוֹמִים יִשְׁכֹּן, מְצָדוֹת סְלָעִים מִשְׂגַּבּוֹ״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּ״לַחְמוֹ נִתָּן״ וּ״מֵימָיו נֶאֱמָנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu says: They asked** King **Solomon, son of David: How far does the power of charity extend?** King Solomon **said to them: Go out and see what my father David explained: "He has distributed freely, he has given to the poor, his righteousness endures forever, his horn shall be exalted with honor"** (Psalms 112:9). **Rabbi Abba said:** It is derived **from here** how far the power of charity extends: **"He shall dwell on high, his place of defense shall be the fortress of rocks; his bread shall be given, his water shall be sure"** (Isaiah 33:16). **What is the reason** that **"He shall dwell on high, his place of defense shall be the fortress of rocks"? Because "his bread shall be given"** to the poor, **and "his water shall be sure,"** i.e., it shall be given faithfully and he can be trusted in the matter.

Rabbi Abbahu teaches: Moshe said before the Holy One — *bameh tarum keren Yisrael*, by what means will the glory of Israel be elevated? God answered: *b'Ki Tisa* — by the portion of *Ki Tisa*, the giving of the half-shekel to the Temple. *Ki Tisa* is read here as a template: supporting the sacred causes of one's community is what raises the standing of Israel. Solomon's son and successor is then cited: when asked *ad heichan kocha shel tzedakah* — how far does the power of *tzedakah* extend — he replied, *tze'u ur'u mah peireish David abba* — go and see what my father David wrote: *pizer natan la'evyonim, tzidkato omedet la'ad, karno tarum bechavod* — he scattered his wealth to the poor, his righteousness stands forever, his horn will be raised in honor.

Rabbi Abba adds a verse: *hu meromim yishkon, metzadot sela'im misgavo, lachmo nutan, meimav ne'emanim* — he will dwell on high, in the strongholds of rocks, his bread he has given, his waters are faithful. *Mah ta'am meromim yishkon?* Why does he dwell on high? *Mishum delachmo nutan* — because he gave his bread to the poor.

### Upside-Down Worlds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת שְׁלֹמֹה: אֵיזֶהוּ בֶּן הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא? אָמַר לָהֶם: כֹּל שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד זְקֵנָיו כָּבוֹד. כִּי הָא דְּיוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ חֲלַשׁ, אִינְּגִיד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אֲבוּהּ: מַאי חָזֵית? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עוֹלָם הָפוּךְ רָאִיתִי – עֶלְיוֹנִים לְמַטָּה, וְתַחְתּוֹנִים לְמַעְלָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עוֹלָם בָּרוּר רָאִיתָ. וַאֲנַן, הֵיכִי חֲזֵיתִינַּן? [אֲמַר לֵיהּ:] כִּי הֵיכִי דַּחֲשִׁבִינַן הָכָא, חֲשִׁבִינַן הָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu says: They asked** King **Solomon: Who is one** who is destined **for the World-to-Come?** King Solomon **said to them: All** those about whom it is stated: **"And before His Elders will be His glory"** (Isaiah 24:23), referring to those who are honored in this world due to their wisdom. This is **like** the incident involving **Yosef, son of Rabbi Yehoshua, who became ill** and **fainted.** When he returned to good health, **his father said to him: What did you see** when you were not conscious? Yosef **said to him: I saw an inverted world. Those above,** i.e., those who are considered important in this world, were **below,** insignificant, while **those below,** i.e., those who are insignificant in this world, were **above.** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: You have seen a clear world.** The world you have seen is the true world, one in which one's spiritual and moral standing determines his true importance. Rabbi Yehoshua further asked him: **And how did you see us,** the Torah scholars, there? Yosef **said to him: Just as we are important here, we are important there.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אַשְׁרֵי מִי שֶׁבָּא לְכָאן – וְתַלְמוּדוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. וְשָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת – אֵין כׇּל בְּרִיָּה יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בִּמְחִיצָתָן. מַאן נִינְהוּ? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וַחֲבֵרָיו, מִשּׁוּם הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת – וְתוּ לָא?! פְּשִׁיטָא, בְּלָאו הָכִי נָמֵי! אֶלָּא הֲרוּגֵי לוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** Yosef added: **And I heard that they were saying** in that world: **Happy is the one who arrives with his studies in hand. And I** also **heard that they were saying: Those executed by the government** enjoy such exalted status that **no one can stand in their section.** The Gemara asks: **Who are these** martyrs to whom Yosef was referring? **If we say** that he was referring to **Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues,** who were killed by the Romans, this cannot be: Is their elevated status **due** only **to** the fact that **they were martyred by** the Roman **government and nothing more?** These men were exceptional in their piety and sanctity during their lives as well. Therefore **it** is **obvious that even without** their martyrdom they would be greater than other people. **Rather,** it is referring to those like **the martyrs of Lod,** who died for the sanctification of God's name but were not Torah scholars.

Rabbi Abbahu reports that Solomon was asked: *eizehu ben Olam Haba* — who merits the world to come? He answered: *kol shekeneged zekenav kavod* — one in whose old age he is honored. Rashi explains: this refers to one who is honored for the wisdom accrued through a life well lived — not for wealth or power, but for the genuine wisdom that commands respect.

The *gemara* then tells the story of Yosef the son of Rabbi Yehoshua, who fell gravely ill and, upon recovering, was asked by his father what he had seen in that state. He replied: *olam hafuch ra'iti* — I saw an inverted world. *Elyonim lemata, vetachtonim lemala* — those who are at the top here were at the bottom there, and those at the bottom here were at the top there. His father replied: *olam barur ra'ita* — that is the true world, the clear world. The one we inhabit is the inverted one.

When asked about the *talmidei chachamim* — where do the Rabbis stand? — Yosef replied: *ki heichi dechashvinan hacha, chashvinan hatam* — the same standing we have here, we have there. He did not claim the top. The point is precise: honor earned for money and worldly power will be reversed in the world to come. But honor earned for *Torah* and *chochmah* — that reflects genuine achievement, and it is consistent between the two worlds.

He added: *ashrei mi sheba lechan vetalmudo beyado* — praised is one who arrives in the world to come with his learning in his hand. The Maharsha explains: *beyado* — in his hand — means the learning he truly owns, the material he internalized because he wrote it down. This is a pedagogical point of enduring relevance.

Finally: *harugei malchus ein kol berya yechola la'amod bimchitzatam* — those martyred by the government, who gave their lives *al kiddush Hashem*, no creature can stand at their level. The *gemara* asks: are we speaking of Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues, the great Rabbis of the Roman persecutions? But *pshita delav hachi nami* — they would have been at the highest level in the world to come even without martyrdom. The reference is rather to the *harugei Lod* — those martyred in the story of Lod, where two brothers falsely confessed to a killing they did not commit in order to save an entire city from Roman collective punishment. Their martyrdom was not accompanied by decades of Torah greatness; it was an act of supreme self-sacrifice for the community. And that, the *gemara* suggests, places them at an incomparable level.
