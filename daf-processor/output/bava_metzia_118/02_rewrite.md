# Bava Metzia 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Roof Collapse Rights

### Cliff Structure

The daf opens with a *mishna* at the bottom of 117b that continues the tractate's treatment of divided properties and questions of responsibility when something breaks. The case described is the *beit habad* — a pressing house for olives — built into a cliff, with a garden (*gina*) situated on top of its roof. One can picture the structure: a cliff face with an opening leading into the pressing house, and above it, on the slope of the mountain, someone's orchard or vegetable garden whose soil rests directly atop the roof of the *beit habad* below.

The *mishna* presumably reflects a real-world scenario — such arrangements would not have been invented without precedent. The relevant backdrop is the framework of *socher u'mashkir*, landlord and tenant, which the *gemara* had applied to analogous cases earlier in the tractate, where the party below bears responsibility for the structural integrity of what supports the party above. There is, as *Rashi* and *Tosafot* discuss, some complexity about whether that framework governs here, but that appears to be the operative assumption.

### Planting Remedy

The *mishna* states: if the roof collapsed — *v'nifchas* — the owner of the garden (*ba'al hagina*) may descend and plant below, *ad she'ya'aseh l'veit bado kipin*, until the owner of the *beit habad* constructs arched vaults to restore the roof. In other words, if the person responsible for the roof fails to repair it, the garden owner may come and plant in the space below — within or around the *beit habad* — until the roof is restored. It is a curious remedy, since the *beit habad* is not obviously ideal for planting, but the *mishna* may contemplate adjacent space below, and in any case it functions as a parallel to the earlier rule that a tenant in an *aliya* (upper story) whose floor has collapsed may move into the space below until repairs are made. This is a variation on the same principle: the party who suffers the loss of use has a remedy against the party responsible for the structural failure.

## Collapsed Wall Liability

### Ones Exemption

The *mishna* introduces a new case: a wall and a tree that fell into *reshut harabim* and caused damage. The ruling is *patur milishalem* — the owner is exempt from paying. The reason is that the collapse is treated as an *ones*, an unavoidable accident. One might note that this raises an interesting *Bava Kamma* question: under which category of *nezikin* would such a case fall? Is it *shor*, *esh*, or something else? But that taxonomic question is set aside; the operative principle is that an unforeseen collapse without prior warning is an *ones*.

### Beit Din Time

The *mishna* then addresses the case where *beit din* had already put the owner on notice: *nasnu lo zman lakotzes et ha'ilan u'lisator et hakotel* — they gave him time to cut down the tree or demolish the wall, and it collapsed anyway. *Betoch hazman patur* — if it fell within the allotted period, he is still exempt, because he has not yet become negligent; he was still acting within the window granted to him and remains in the status of *ones*. *Achar hazman chayav* — after the deadline, he is liable. This is a question of negligence: once the window expires and he has not acted, any resulting damage is no longer treated as *ones*.

Rabbi Yochanan's view is that the standard *zman beit din* — the period ordinarily allotted for such matters — is thirty days. *Tosafot* draws on this *gemara* to establish a general principle: thirty days (*shloshim yom*) is the standard time *beit din* grants for getting things done, whether removing a dangerous structure, repaying a debt, or other obligations. *Tosafot* further notes that *Rabban Gamliel*'s earlier ruling — that a creditor may not sell a debtor's collateral for thirty days — reflects a similar idea: *beit din* puts you on notice, and after thirty days takes action.

At the same time, *Tosafot* qualifies the rule: thirty days is not a universal grace period for anything that might cause damage. Where the hazard is immediate — as in the classic *Bava Kamma* case of a broken jug whose shards have fallen in *reshut harabim* — one must act immediately. The thirty-day standard applies where the risk is not imminently active. Today, *Tosafot* notes, thirty days remains the standard — which, as one might observe, bears more than passing resemblance to the modern thirty-day return policy, though obviously the *halacha* is speaking of something rather different.

### Wall Removal

The *mishna* turns to a further case, one that moves from the domain of *nezikin* into territory more characteristic of *Bava Batra*: someone whose wall collapsed into a neighbor's garden. Here the issue is not active damage but interference — the rubble sits in the neighbor's yard and prevents him from using it. The *mishna* presents the following scenario: the neighbor demands that the wall owner remove the stones, and the wall owner responds *higiu'cha* — "just keep them." The *mishna* rules *ein shom'in lo* — you cannot impose this arrangement on the yard owner. If he does not wish to accept the rubble, the wall owner remains obligated to remove it.

On the other hand, *mishekiblo alav* — if the yard owner agreed to the arrangement and accepted the stones, and then the wall owner returns and says *heilech et yetziat'cha v'ani etol et sheli* — "I will pay your expenses for clearing it and I want my stones back" — *ein shom'in lo*. Once the yard owner has agreed and cleared away the rubble, the arrangement is binding and cannot be reversed. The *gemara* will probe both of these rulings in greater depth.

## Rubble Agreement

### Cannot Force

The principle underlying the first ruling — that the wall owner cannot force the yard owner to accept the rubble — flows naturally from the basic structure of the case. The yard owner has not agreed to anything; the wall owner is attempting to unilaterally resolve his own obligation to remove by imposing something of questionable value on another party. The *mishna* simply refuses to permit this.

### Binding After Work

Once the yard owner agrees and proceeds to clear the rubble, the arrangement crystallizes into a binding agreement. The *gemara* will explain the mechanism: why the agreement becomes final only upon the completion of clearing, and not earlier, raises a significant question that the *gemara* addresses directly.

## Wages in Kind

### Worker Payment

The *mishna* introduces a parallel case: *hasocher et hapoel la'asot imo beteven uvakash* — someone hired a worker to deal with straw and stubble. The worker requests his wages, and the employer offers *tol mah she'asita bischar'cha* — take what you gathered as your pay. Even if the straw is of equivalent or even greater monetary value, *ein shom'in lo* — one cannot force this on the worker. The implicit assumption of a wage arrangement is that payment will be in cash.

### Irrevocable

However, *mishekiblo alav* — if the worker agreed to accept the straw — and then the employer returns and says *heilech schar'cha v'ani etol et sheli* — I found the cash after all, return the straw — *ein shom'in lo*. The agreement, once accepted by the worker, is irrevocable.

### Shaveh Kesef

The obvious question is: why can the employer not pay in straw *lechatchila*? The general principle is *shaveh kesef k'kesef* — something of monetary value is treated as equivalent to cash. *Rashi* explains: the prohibition here is derived from the verse *bal talin scharo* — do not withhold a worker's wages. When one specifies a wage, *mai d'atni bahadei mashma* — what was contracted is implied to mean cash. Saying "I will pay you X" implies an obligation in actual currency, not merely in equivalent goods. Therefore the payment-in-kind does not satisfy the wage obligation, even if its value matches or exceeds the agreed amount.

*Tosafot* in the beginning of *Bava Kamma* elaborates on the broader question of *shaveh kesef k'kesef*, identifying wages and loan repayment as two areas where the principle may not apply in the usual way: if one borrowed cash, one must return cash; if one agreed to pay wages, cash is implicitly required.

## Collapsed Wall Liabili…

### Mafkir Defense

Before the *gemara* proceeds to analyze the *mishna*'s cases, *Tosafot* raises an important question about the wall-collapse scenario. One might think that the wall owner could simply declare himself *mafkir* — relinquish ownership of the fallen stones — and thereby escape any obligation to remove them. There is a classical *Bava Kamma* case (*perek hamaniach*) in which a person's glassware falls and shatters in *reshut harabim*, creating a public hazard. If the owner declares the shards *hefker* immediately — before having had an opportunity to clear them — he is exempt from liability: the fall itself was an *ones*, and by declaring *mafkir* before becoming negligent, he avoids the secondary liability for failing to clean up.

Could the same logic apply here? If my wall collapses into your yard and I immediately declare *mafkir*, I was not negligent in the collapse and I have now relinquished ownership before any negligence in failing to remove — so perhaps I owe you nothing?

*Tosafot* concedes the theoretical force of this argument but dismisses it in the present case: *ishamutei kamishtamei v'eino mafkir k'd'amri bigmara* — here the wall owner is not truly making a declaration of *mafkir*, he is merely trying to avoid the inconvenience of removal. When he says *higiu'cha*, he is not sincerely relinquishing ownership; he is deflecting. A genuine, immediate *mafkir* would indeed exempt him, but what the *mishna* describes is not that.

The *halacha* that emerges is significant: if a wall collapses into another's yard and the owner was an *ones*, and he genuinely declares *mafkir* before any negligence, he is exempt even without removing the rubble. The case in the *mishna* is distinguished because the purported *mafkir* is not genuine.

## Roof Collapse Rights…

### Rav vs Shmuel

The *gemara* now addresses the threshold for the garden owner's remedy: how much of the roof must have collapsed before he may move his entire planting operation downstairs? *Rav amar beruba* — Rav holds it must be the majority of the roof. *U'shmuel amar bearba* — Shmuel says four is sufficient. Worth noting is that the text here reads *arba* rather than *arba'ah*, which may reflect a difference in the units: earlier in the tractate a parallel dispute used *arba'ah tefachim* (four *tefachim*), while here it appears to mean *arba amot* (four *amot*).

Rav elaborates: even if four *amot* have collapsed, the garden owner is not entitled to move the entire operation below; he plants *chetzvo l'mata v'chetzvo l'ma'ala* — half below and half above. Only when the majority is destroyed does he move the whole operation downstairs. Shmuel counters: *ein adam zorei'ach chetzvo l'mata v'chetzvo l'ma'ala* — one cannot be compelled to split a planting operation between two levels. Even a small opening of four suffices to allow the garden owner to descend entirely.

### Field vs Dwelling

The *gemara* asks: why state both this case and the earlier case involving a domicile? *Utzricha* — both are necessary.

Had the *gemara* only taught the case of a domicile, one might have concluded that Shmuel's leniency — that even a small breach entitles the tenant to move the whole operation downstairs — applies there because *lo avid inish de'dayir portsa hacha portsa hacha*: people do not split their living quarters between two floors. One cannot ask a person to sleep upstairs and keep their belongings downstairs. So in that case, any significant breach justifies full relocation. But one might have thought that for a field or orchard, where it is not unusual for a farmer to work plots in different locations, *avid inish d'shazar hacha portsa vehacha portsa* — one might manage some planting above and some below — and therefore perhaps even Shmuel would agree with Rav that only a majority breach compels full relocation.

Conversely, had only the planting case been taught, one might have concluded that Rav's majority requirement applies there because splitting a field is manageable, but perhaps even Rav would concede that in a domicile any small breach allows full relocation. *Tzricha* — each case is necessary to establish that both Rav and Shmuel maintain their respective positions regardless of whether the space in question is a residence or an agricultural field.

## Rubble Agreement…

### Immediate Kinyan

The *gemara* probes the ruling that once the yard owner clears the rubble, the wall owner cannot reverse the agreement. *Michlal d'pinuyei askinan* — it sounds as though the act of clearing is the point at which the yard owner acquires the stones. But *amai* — why? Once the yard owner agrees to keep the stones, they are sitting in his field. *D'tikni lei sadei* — his field should acquire them for him. The principle of *chatzer koneh* should operate: a person's enclosure takes possession on his behalf even of things he has not personally handled.

### Chatzer Koneh

Indeed, *Rabbi Yoshiya bar Rabbi Chanina* states explicitly: *chatzer shel adam koneh lo shelo mida'ato* — a person's yard acquires property for him even without his direct awareness. *Tosafot* notes that the *gemara*'s reference to *shelo mida'ato* is deliberate: it addresses the question of whether *chatzer* operates when the owner is not standing nearby, establishing that it can function even in the owner's absence.

If so, why is the yard owner's clearing of the rubble the operative moment? Why does the *gemara* not say he acquires immediately upon agreement, by virtue of *chatzer*?

### Intent Test

The *gemara* answers: *hani mili heicha d'ka mechavein l'aknoyei lei* — *chatzer* operates when the transferring party genuinely intends to transfer ownership. *Aval hacha achmuyei ashaynach d'ka mishtamit lei* — but here, the wall owner is not genuinely trying to give away his stones. He is simply trying to avoid being pestered to clean them up. His *higiu'cha* is not a serious *matanah* but an evasion. Therefore, since his intent is not sincere transfer, *chatzer koneh* cannot operate on his behalf. Only once the yard owner has actually invested the labor to clear the rubble does it become untenable for the wall owner to claim he was not serious — at that point, the arrangement is treated as genuine and binding.

One might ask: why must the *entire* clearing be completed? Why does not the beginning of the work demonstrate sufficient seriousness to crystallize the agreement? The *gemara* does not address this directly, but its implication is that the completed act is the clearest possible indicator that both parties have treated the arrangement as real.

## Both Cases Needed

### No Debt Relationship

The *gemara* asks: why is it necessary for the *mishna* to teach both the rubble case and the wages case? *Utzricha.* Consider each in turn. In the rubble case, the wall owner has no pre-existing monetary obligation to the yard owner. There is no debt relationship between them. If even there we rule *ein shom'in lo* — that the wall owner cannot force the yard owner to accept the stones — one might think this is only because there is no underlying financial relationship that could make the arrangement compelling. The yard owner owes nothing and is owed nothing; imposing rubble on him is purely gratuitous.

### Debt Obligation

In the wages case, however, the employer *does* owe the worker money — *de'is lei agra gabei*. One might therefore think that because a debt exists, paying it in kind should be acceptable: *d'amri inshi, mimarei reshusach parei efra* — the folk saying goes that when someone owes you money, you should be willing to take whatever you can get, even fodder. There is a *hava amina* that since the employer has a financial obligation and the straw represents equivalent value, the rule of *shaveh kesef k'kesef* should permit him to discharge the debt in goods. The *mishna* must therefore teach that even here, *ein shom'in lo*: you cannot force a worker to accept in-kind payment.

### Forcing Acceptance

Similarly, once the arrangement is accepted, the question of revocability differs between the cases. In the wages case, one might have thought that once the worker agrees to accept the straw, the pre-existing debt structure makes this irrevocable — the employer has discharged his obligation and cannot reassert a claim to the straw. But conversely, one might have thought that without any pre-existing structure, as in the rubble case, the arrangement could always be undone.

The *mishna* teaches both directions: in the rubble case, even without a pre-existing debt, once the rubble is cleared the arrangement cannot be reversed; in the wages case, even though there is a debt that might seem to compel acceptance, the employer still cannot force the worker to take in-kind payment initially. Both rulings are necessary because the *hava amina* in each case runs in a different direction, and without both being explicitly taught, one might err in one case or the other.

## Hefker Field

### Own vs Hefker

The *gemara* returns to the wages case — specifically, the ruling that one cannot force a worker to accept payment in kind — and notes a *brayta* that appears to say *shom'in lo*, that one *can* force it. *Rav Nachman* reconciles the apparent contradiction: *kan b'shelo kan b'shel chaveiro* — it matters whether the worker is laboring in the employer's own field or in someone else's field. In the employer's own field, payment in kind cannot be forced. In another person's field, perhaps different rules apply.

*Rava* challenges this immediately: *b'shelo mai ta'ama* — why can you not force payment when it is your own field? Because *amar lei agrai alach* — the worker can say, "You are the one obligated to pay me." *B'shel chaveiro nami agrai alach* — but equally, when you sent him to work in your friend's field, the obligation to pay still falls on you, as a *brayta* confirms: *hasocher et hapo'el la'asot b'shelo v'her'ahu b'shel chaveiro* — if you hired someone ostensibly to work for you but directed him to your friend's field instead, you must pay him in full, and then you go collect from the property owner for the benefit he received. Rav Nachman's first distinction therefore collapses.

*Rav Nachman* revises: *ella lo kashya kan bishelo kan bishel hefker* — the distinction is between the employer's own field and a *hefker* field, an ownerless field. When the field is the employer's own, he clearly benefits from the work and owes the wages. When the field is *hefker*, the picture becomes more complex.

The analysis turns on the rule of *magbi'ah metzi'ah lachaveiro lo kanah chaveiro* — if a worker lifts found property on behalf of another, the other party does not thereby acquire it. If the employer sent the worker to collect straw in a *hefker* field, and the legal mechanism of *sechirus* requires that the employer actually benefit — that is, actually acquire the collected straw — then it matters enormously whether the employer acquires it at all. If the worker's collection does not vest ownership in the employer, then the employer has not received any real benefit, and the entire wage obligation is called into question.

This yields a striking principle: the structure of *sechirus* (hire) appears to require not merely that an employer ask a worker to do something and agree to pay, but that the activity constitute a genuine service — one from which the employer actually benefits. If the worker collects straw in a *hefker* field and the employer never acquires that straw, perhaps there is no proper *sechirus* at all, and consequently no enforceable wage obligation even if the employer had verbally agreed to pay.

### Rava's Challenge

*Rava* challenges *Rav Nachman* from a ruling about *metzi'as po'el* — a worker's found property. The rule is: *eimasai? bizman she'amar lo ba'al habayis nakesh imi hayom, ador imi hayom* — when does found property belong to the worker himself? When the employer hired him for a specific task such as weeding or hoeing. Then what he finds incidentally is his own, since he was not working as a general agent of the employer.

### Task vs General

However, *aval amar lo asei imi melachah hayom* — if the employer simply said "work for me today" without specifying a task, then *metzi'aso l'ba'al habayis* — what the worker finds belongs to the employer. In that case, the worker functions as an extension of the employer, and whatever he picks up he acquires on the employer's behalf. Therefore, even in a *hefker* field, if the worker is hired generally to work for the employer that day, any straw he collects would vest in the employer — the employer would acquire it and would owe the worker his fee in cash, not in kind. Rav Nachman's *hefker* distinction must therefore account for this.

### Hagbahah Habatah

*Rav Nachman* answers with a final formulation: *lo kashya, kan b'hagbahah kan b'habatah* — the distinction lies not between the employer's field and a *hefker* field as such, but between *hagbahah* (physical lifting) and *habatah* (watching or looking). If the worker was hired to *lift* and collect the straw in the *hefker* field — *hagbahah* — then by lifting it he effects a *kinyan* for the employer, the employer acquires it, a service was rendered, wages are owed, and payment in kind cannot be forced.

But if the worker was hired merely to *watch* the straw — *habatah* — and *habatah* is not a valid *kinyan*, then the employer never acquires the straw, no genuine service was rendered in the *sechirus* sense, and therefore the employer can simply direct the worker to take the straw for himself. *Rashi* elaborates: when hired to collect (*lagbahah*), the worker's lifting effects the acquisition and therefore *secharo alav* — the wage obligation stands. When hired for *habatah* — guarding or moving without lifting, *kegon lishmor o l'hashlich miyad l'yad* — no *kinyan* is effected, the employer has not acquired anything, and therefore *de'amar lei: hashta lo zachi b'hai, ana, zil at zachi beih* — the employer can tell the worker: I have not yet taken possession of this; go take possession of it for yourself.

The difficulty, as the *gemara* itself seems not to fully resolve, is why the employer does not remain obligated to pay the agreed-upon fee regardless. The worker spent the day watching the field. Even if the employer never acquired the straw, the worker performed a service as contracted — or did he? The *gemara*'s implication appears to be that *sechirus* as a legal category requires that the activity constitute a genuine service to the employer in a substantive sense, not merely the execution of an agreed-upon act. If the employer ultimately acquired nothing, the *sechirus* framework may simply not have been activated.

## Habatah Debate

### Temple Watchers

*Rava* observes that the question of whether *habatah b'hefker koneh* — whether watching *hefker* property constitutes a valid act of acquisition — is in fact a *tannaitic* dispute. This connects, remarkably, to the very first *meimra* of *Bava Metzia*, where the *gemara* had asked whether *habatah b'hefker koneh* and concluded it does not. *Rashi* notes that *habatah* in this context is not mere passive observation but active watching and guarding — exerting a form of control — which is why some *tannaim* might regard it as a type of *kinyan*.

The *mishna* cited is: *shomrei sefichi shevi'is notlin secharan mi-terumas ha-lishkah* — those who guard the wild produce of the *shevi'is* year that was intended for the public *korbanot* (*omer* and *shetei ha-lechem*) receive their wages from the Temple treasury.

### Tannaim Disagree

The debate is between the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Yose*. *Rabbi Yose* holds that someone who wishes may volunteer to guard the produce without pay. The *chachamim* respond: if the watcher is not paid, then according to our understanding, the *korban* would not be a proper *korban tzibbur*.

### Tanna Kamma

The *gemara*'s first reading is that the *tannaim* disagree about whether *habatah b'hefker* effects acquisition. The *tanna kamma* holds *habatah b'hefker kani* — watching *hefker* produces ownership. Therefore, if the watcher is not paid a fee, he acquires the grain himself through his watching. He would then donate it privately to the *beit hamikdash*, and the *omer* or *shetei ha-lechem* brought from it would be a *korban yachid*, not a *korban tzibbur*. Only by paying him a fee — making him an agent of the *tzibbur* — does the watching accrue to the *tzibbur*'s ownership. *Rabbi Yose* holds *habatah b'hefker lo kani* — watching does not effect acquisition. The watcher owns nothing; when the *tzibbur* comes to harvest, it acquires the grain at that moment, making it a proper *korban tzibbur* regardless of whether the watcher was paid.

### Rava's Resolution

*Rava* rejects this reading. *De-khuli alma habatah b'hefker kani* — everyone agrees that watching *hefker* effects acquisition. The dispute is not about *habatah* as a *kinyan* but about a different concern. According to the *rabbanan*, we pay the watcher a fee so that he is explicitly watching on behalf of the *tzibbur*, thereby ensuring that the acquisition accrues to the *tzibbur* and not to himself. *Ve-ilo* — if we do not pay him — *cheishinan shelo yimsur yafeh yafeh* — we are concerned that even if he intends to donate the grain afterwards, he will not do so wholeheartedly. He has already acquired it as private property and may not genuinely dedicate it.

*Rabbi Yose* holds that a person who volunteers to watch and then donates may be trusted to do so fully and wholeheartedly — *harotzeh minadiv hu* — whoever wishes may volunteer with full dedication, and we are not concerned about half-heartedness.

The *chachamim*'s response to *Rabbi Yose*, according to *Rava*'s reading, is: even within your framework, if we follow your practice of not paying the watcher, then within our framework — where we are concerned about wholehearted donation — the *korban* would not properly be *mishel tzibbur*. *Midvarecha lidvareinu* — granting your premise, applied to our concern, the result is unsatisfactory.

A second version of the *gemara* (*ika d'amri*) holds that *Rav* says *habatah b'hefker lo kani* and therefore the reason to pay the watcher has nothing to do with acquisition. Rather, *de-hashinan l'va'alei zeroa* — we pay him so that forceful people (*ba'alei zeroa*) who hear that the *beit hamikdash* is paying someone to watch this produce know to stay away. The fee is a deterrent, not an instrument of *kinyan*. The *tannaitic* dispute on this reading is whether this institutional deterrent is necessary.

## Public Domain Rules

### Dung Removal

The final *mishna* of the tractate addresses regulations for various activities conducted in *reshut harabim*. The first case: someone who brings out animal dung to sell as fertilizer. The arrangement contemplated is a direct transaction in the public domain: *ha-motzi motzi v'ha-mezabbel mezabbel* — I bring it out and the buyer loads it up immediately. One cannot maintain a standing pile of dung in *reshut harabim*, because if someone slips and is injured, liability follows.

It is worth appreciating the broader context: *reshut harabim* in the Talmudic period was not merely a thoroughfare but a public commercial space — a marketplace, often lined with stalls, where people conducted all manner of business. The *Cardo* and the open plazas along major city streets were venues for trade. The *halacha* therefore does not prohibit all activity in *reshut harabim*, but it imposes limits to prevent public hazard.

### Construction Limits

Similarly, *ein shurin tit b'reshut harabim* — one may not soak clay in *reshut harabim*, and *ein lovnin leveinim* — one may not make bricks there. *Rashi* describes the brick-making process: one lays out a flat expanse of wet cement, scores lines to form the brick shapes, and allows it to harden, producing a batch of bricks. Doing this in *reshut harabim* creates an unattended hazard — people will step on it, slip, or be injured.

However, *aval govlin tit b'reshut harabim* — one may knead clay in *reshut harabim*. Since one is actively present and working, the danger of an unattended hazard is reduced. The distinction seems to be between activities requiring the worker's constant attention and presence versus those that leave an unattended material obstacle.

### Building Setup

*Haboneh b'reshut harabim*: if one is engaged in authorized construction in *reshut harabim* — say, hired by the public to build a wall — one cannot stockpile large quantities of materials on the street. *Hameivi avanim meivi v'haboneh boneh* — the supplier delivers and the builder builds in coordinated fashion, so that materials do not sit idle and unattended as a hazard. *V'im hizik meshalem mah she-hizik* — if someone is damaged, the builder is liable. This is significant: even when operating within the rules, liability for damage is not automatically extinguished. The question of what one is *permitted* to do and whether one is *exempt from liability* are distinct inquiries, a point that becomes central in the *gemara*'s analysis below.

*Rabban Gamliel* adds: *af metaknin et melachtan lifnei shloshim yom* — one may set up the worksite and arrange materials in *reshut harabim* for up to thirty days before commencing construction. Whether this thirty-day allowance also confers exemption from liability, or merely establishes what is permitted *l'chatchila*, is left ambiguous by the *mishna*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

A *brayta* presents *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position: *b'sha'at hotzaat zevelim adam motzi zivlo l'reshut harabim v'tzovro kol shloshim yom* — at the season when people bring out their excrement for use as fertilizer, one may pile it in *reshut harabim* for thirty days, *kedei she-yishof b'raglei adam u-v'raglei beheima* — so that it gets trampled by pedestrians and animals, which improves its quality as fertilizer. *She-al menat kein hinchilu Yehoshua l'Yisrael et ha-aretz* — this right was among the original conditions stipulated when Yehoshua apportioned the land of Israel. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, this is a fully permitted activity, and the thirty days are not a concession but a right.

### Permission vs Liability

This creates an apparent tension with the *mishna*: the *mishna* implies that dung must be removed immediately, not left for thirty days. The *gemara* asks: *leima matnisin dela k'Rabbi Yehuda* — is our *mishna* necessarily not in accordance with *Rabbi Yehuda*?

The *gemara* responds: *afilu teima Rabbi Yehuda* — even *Rabbi Yehuda* could be consistent with the *mishna*. *Modeh Rabbi Yehuda she-im hizik chayav l'shalem* — *Rabbi Yehuda* would concede that if damage results, the person is liable to pay. The *mishna*'s apparent requirement for immediate removal may be read not as a prohibition on leaving the dung for thirty days, but as establishing the liability framework: if you do leave it and someone is hurt, you pay. You are permitted to leave it — but at your financial risk.

The *gemara* then tests whether *Rabbi Yehuda* truly maintains that permission and liability are separable. The case of *ner Chanukah* is invoked: *modeh Rabbi Yehuda b'ner Chanukah she-hu patur mipnei shehu oseh b'reshut* — even *Rabbi Yehuda* concedes that one is exempt from liability for a *Chanukah* lamp placed in the doorway if a camel with flax walks by and is ignited, because the person was acting with legal permission (*b'reshut*). If permission exempts in that case, does it not exempts always?

The *gemara* answers: perhaps *ner Chanukah* is different because it is a *mitzvah* — *rishut d'mitzvah* — and the exemption is specific to the performance of a religious obligation, not to mere legal permission generally.

A *brayta* then addresses the question directly. *Kol elu she'amru mutarin lekalkel b'reshut harabim* — all those whom the *halachah* permits to create a mess in *reshut harabim* (disposing of waste products, rainwater runoff, etc.): the *tanna kamma* holds that *im hizik chayav leshalem* — even if permitted, they are liable for damage. *Rabbi Yehuda poter* — *Rabbi Yehuda* exempts. This is the core dispute: for *Rabbi Yehuda*, permission and liability exemption go together; for the *tanna kamma*, they are entirely independent.

The *gemara* concludes: *mechvarta matnitin d'lo k'Rabbi Yehuda* — the *mishna* is most straightforwardly read as not in accordance with *Rabbi Yehuda*. Our *mishna* — which requires immediate removal (or at minimum imposes liability for delayed removal) — reflects the view that permission does not confer exemption from liability, which is the *tanna kamma*'s position. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s more expansive approach, granting both a thirty-day window and liability exemption within it, does not align with the *mishna*'s framework.
