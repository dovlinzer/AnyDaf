# Hullin 138 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah Review

### Dosa vs. Hachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳. וְכַמָּה ״כׇּל שֶׁהֵן״? אָמַר רַב: מָנֶה וּפְרָס, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהוּ מְחוּמָּשׁוֹת, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שִׁשִּׁים, וְנוֹתֵן סֶלַע אַחַת לְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas says** that the mitzva of the first sheared wool applies to five sheep, each of whose shearing weighs one hundred and fifty dinars, whereas the Rabbis say: Five sheep, each of whose shearing is any amount. The Gemara asks: **And how much** is signified by the phrase: **Any amount? Rav says:** It is a total weight of **one hundred dinars** [***maneh***] **and half** of one hundred dinars [***peras***], **provided that they are divided** equally **between the five** sheep. **And Shmuel says:** It is a total weight of **sixty** *sela*, of which he **gives** the weight of **one *sela* to the priest.**

We begin on 137b, picking up the *sugya* dealing with *reishit hagez* — the obligation to give the first shearings to the *kohen*. The *mishnah* records a dispute about the minimum amount of wool required from one's sheep. Dosa ben Harkinas holds that the threshold is one and a half *maneh* per sheep. A *maneh*, it should be noted, equals 25 *sela*, which in turn equals 100 *dinar* — roughly a pound in modern terms. The *Hachamim*, by contrast, maintain *kol shehu*: any amount suffices. The *Gemara*, however, is unwilling to accept that *kol shehu* means literally any trivial quantity, and several substantive positions emerge from the attempt to define it more precisely.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel holds that the minimum unit relevant here is one *sela* — since there are 25 *sela* to a *maneh*, this amounts to roughly 4% of a pound. His reasoning is proportional: one must give a *sixtieth* of the total wool to the *kohen*, and since the minimum gift is one *sela*, working backwards, one must begin with at least 60 *sela* of wool. This is notably less than the one and a half *maneh* (37.5 *sela*) mentioned by the first opinion.

### Six Sela Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֵׁשׁ – לְכֹהֵן חָמֵשׁ, וְאַחַת לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is a total weight of **six** *sela*, of which he gives **to the priest** the weight of **five** *sela*, **and** he leaves **one** *sela* **for himself.**

A different approach dispenses with the proportional framework entirely and works from the absolute figure prescribed by the *mishnah* elsewhere: the *kohen* receives five *sela*. If five *sela* is the absolute minimum gift, then the minimum amount of wool one must start with is six *sela* — just enough to give five and retain a nominal remainder. On this view, the measure is always flat and absolute, not proportional.

## Ulla/R. Elazar

### Kol Shehu Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״כׇּל שֶׁהֵן״ שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla** says that **Rabbi Elazar says: We learned** in the mishna that according to the Rabbis the obligation of the first sheared wool applies to **any amount,** even if the total weight is only one *sela*.

We now reach the line in the *Gemara* where Ulla reports in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *kol shehu shaninu* — when the *mishnah* says *kol shehu*, it means precisely that, any amount, however trivial. There is no minimum threshold of six, no minimum of sixty — any shearing at all triggers the obligation, and any amount may be given.

### Five Sela Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וְכַמָּה נוֹתֵן לוֹ? מִשְׁקַל חֲמֵשׁ סְלָעִים בִּיהוּדָה, שֶׁהֵן עֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים בַּגָּלִיל. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – נִיחָא, אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna (135a): **And how much does one give to** the priest? One gives him sheared wool of the **weight** of **five *sela* in Judea, which are** the equivalent of **ten *sela* in the Galilee. Granted,** according **to** the opinions of **Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan,** the mishna **works out well,** as their statements do not contradict the mishna. Rav does not discuss the amount one is required to give the priest, and Rabbi Yoḥanan states that one gives the priest five *sela*. **But** according **to Shmuel and Rabbi Elazar,** the mishna is **difficult,** as Shmuel states that one is required to give one *sela*, while Rabbi Elazar states that the obligation of the first sheared wool applies even for a weight of less than five *sela*.

This immediately raises a difficulty from the very *mishnah* under discussion. The closing clause of the *mishnah* asks how much one actually gives the *kohen*, and answers: *mishkal chamesh sela'im biYehuda shehen eser sela'im baGalil* — five *sela* by the Judean standard, which equals ten *sela* by the Galilean standard (the *sela* of Judea being heavier). This is a prescribed minimum gift, stated in absolute terms.

### Kashya on Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, וּלְרַב מִי נִיחָא? וְהָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז בְּשִׁשִּׁים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning, does it work out well** according **to** the opinion of **Rav? But Rav and Shmuel both say** that **the first sheared wool** given to the priest is one part **in sixty.** Rav holds that the weight of one hundred and fifty dinars renders one obligated in the first sheared wool, and one-sixtieth of this weight is less than one *sela*. Therefore, Rav's opinion is also not in accordance with the mishna.

The *Gemara* now formulates the objection. *Bishlama* — it is understandable — for Rav and Rabbi Yochanan, since Rabbi Yochanan explicitly said one gives a minimum of five *sela* in absolute terms, and Rav simply stated a threshold of one and a half *maneh* without specifying the gift amount. Both can accommodate a *mishnah* that prescribes five *sela* as the floor. But for Shmuel and Rabbi Elazar, there is a genuine *kashya*. Shmuel says one gives a *sixtieth*, which can be as little as a single *sela* — not five. And Rabbi Elazar, as cited by Ulla, says one may start with a *kol shehu*, in which case the gift will be trivially small — certainly not five *sela*. The *Gemara* further sharpens the problem: Rav himself, in another context, agreed to the principle that *reishit hagez* is measured as a *sixtieth*. If one begins with one and a half *maneh* — 37.5 *sela* — and gives a *sixtieth*, the result is well under one *sela*, far short of five.

The underlying tension is between two incommensurable modes of measurement: an absolute floor of five *sela*, and a proportional measure of one-sixtieth. They will not produce the same number except by coincidence, and when the proportional measure yields less than five *sela*, the two systems conflict directly.

## Resolution Approach

### Two Minimums

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ דְּהַהִיא, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ גִּיזִּין הַרְבֵּה עָסְקִינַן, וּמְבַקֵּשׁ לִיתְּנָן לְכֹהֵן, וְאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: כֹּל חַד וְחַד לָא תְּבַצַּר לֵיהּ מֵחֲמֵשֶׁת סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It was stated with regard to that** mishna that **Rav and Shmuel both say:** The five *sela* stated in the mishna is not referring to the total amount given from the sheared wool. Rather, in the mishna **we are dealing with a Jew who has a large** amount of **shearing, and he wishes to give them to the priest,** i.e., to several priests. **And we say to him:** With regard to **each and every one** of the priests, do **not** give **him less than five *sela*.** But if one has only a small amount of shearing, he gives the priest one-sixtieth of the shearing, even if it is less than five *sela*.

The *Gemara* now introduces a resolution attributed to Rav and Shmuel. The passage reads: *b'Yisrael sheyesh lo gizzin harbeh askinan* — we are dealing with a person who has a great deal of wool and intends to distribute it among multiple *kohanim*. On this reading, the five *sela* figure is not a minimum threshold for triggering the obligation. The fundamental measure of *reishit hagez* is indeed proportional — one-*sixtieth* — and that proportion could be very small or very large depending on how much wool one has. The five *sela* figure addresses a different question entirely: *kol chad v'chad lo tivtzar leh mechamisha sela'in* — when one is apportioning a large quantity among several *kohanim*, one must not give any individual *kohen* less than five *sela*. Five *sela* is thus a standard of dignified distribution, not a floor for the basic obligation.

### Giver/Receiver

This resolution is intellectually elegant and worth pausing over. Logically, one might have proposed that both minimums operate simultaneously — the obligation is a *sixtieth*, but it must never fall below five *sela*, whichever is higher. The *Gemara* does not take that route. Instead, it distinguishes the two measures by their respective functions. One governs the giver's obligation: give a proportion of what you have, so that the gift reflects the scale of the flock. The other governs the receiver's dignity: if you are subdividing a large gift, do not give any one *kohen* a disrespectfully small portion.

This framing resonates with earlier themes in the *sugya* regarding the dual perspective on these transactions. The obligation runs both ways — how appropriately one gives, and what constitutes a respectful minimum for the one receiving. A *kohen* who receives a trivial amount from someone who owns thousands of *sela* worth of wool has not been properly honored; and conversely, a *kohen* who receives less than five *sela* when a large gift is being divided has received something less than respectable. These are distinct concerns, and the *Gemara* assigns each concern its own measure.

One should also note that the *Gemara* does not say one is required to divide the gift among multiple *kohanim*. The rule is conditional: *if* one chooses to distribute the gift to several *kohanim*, then each must receive at least five *sela*. The decision to divide is the owner's; the standard of distribution, once that choice is made, is the *Gemara*'s.

## Terumah/Peah

### All Sixtieth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז בְּשִׁשִּׁים, תְּרוּמָה בְּשִׁשִּׁים, פֵּאָה בְּשִׁשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara mentioned a ruling of Rav and Shmuel with regard to the amount that one is required to give as the first sheared wool. The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself. Rav and Shmuel both say: The first sheared wool** given to the priest is one part **of sixty.** Likewise, the amount one is required to separate as ***teruma*** is one part **of sixty,** and the amount one must leave in his field as ***pe'a*** is one part **of sixty.**

The *Gemara* now reports a broader principle in the names of Rav and Shmuel: *reishit hagez beshishim, terumah beshishim, pe'ah beshishim* — all three agricultural and pastoral gifts share the same proportional minimum of one-*sixtieth*.

### Terumah Kashya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמָה בְּשִׁשִּׁים? וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: תְּרוּמָה עַיִן יָפָה – אֶחָד מֵאַרְבָּעִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is the amount one is required to separate as ***teruma*** one part **of sixty? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Terumot* 4:3): With regard to the measure one should separate as ***teruma*,** if one is of **generous disposition** he gives **one-fortieth.**

This immediately invites a challenge from a *mishnah* regarding *terumah*. The *mishnah* there states that a generous measure of *terumah* is 1/40th, an average measure is 1/50th, and a stingy measure is 1/60th. Why would Rav and Shmuel cite the stingy measure as the standard? Would one not cite at least the average, if not the generous?

### Biblical/Rabbanic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – בְּשִׁשִּׁים, דְּרַבָּנַן – בְּאַרְבָּעִים. דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בְּשִׁשִּׁים? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חִטָּה אַחַת פּוֹטֶרֶת אֶת הַכְּרִי! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּרַבָּנַן בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – אַחַת מֵאַרְבָּעִים, דְּרַבָּנַן בִּדְרַבָּנַן – בְּשִׁשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **By Torah law,** it is sufficient to give one part **of sixty; by rabbinic law** the requisite amount is one part **of forty.** The Gemara asks: Is the measure of *teruma* **by Torah law** one part **of sixty? But doesn't Shmuel say:** By Torah law, even **one** grain of **wheat** given as *teruma* **exempts the** entire **pile [*keri*]** of grain? The Gemara answers: **By Torah law** the measure is **as** stated **by Shmuel,** that even one grain of wheat is sufficient. **By rabbinic law**, **with regard to** produce that is obligated in *teruma* **by Torah law** the measure is **one-fortieth,** whereas **by rabbinic law**, **with regard to** produce obligated in *teruma* **by rabbinic law** the measure is one part **of sixty.**

The *Gemara* answers: *de'oraita beshishim* — biblically, a *sixtieth* is the required minimum. But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this as well: does Shmuel himself not say that a single grain of wheat exempts an entire granary? Biblically, *terumah* has no minimum whatsoever — even the most trivial designation removes the *tevel* status from a mound of grain. If that is so, how can one say the biblical minimum is a *sixtieth*?

The *Gemara* resolves this with a three-tier distinction. Biblically, Shmuel is correct — one grain suffices. The rabbinic minimum for biblically obligated produce (the five grains and similar crops) is 1/40th, with 1/50th as the standard and 1/60th as the floor one should not fall below. However, *derabbanan b'derabbanan* — for produce that is only rabbinically obligated in *terumah*, such as fruit and vegetables, the Rabbis were more lenient and set the standard at 1/60th. It is to this category that Rav and Shmuel refer.

This is a notable distinction that one does not often encounter explicitly: the rabbinic measure varies depending on whether the underlying obligation is biblical or rabbinic. For biblically obligated produce, the Rabbis raised the standard considerably; for rabbinically obligated produce, they kept the measure lower.

Several *Acharonim* have observed a related point about the nature of *terumah* itself. The act of designating *terumah* accomplishes two distinct things: it removes the *tevel* status from the grain, and it fulfills the *mitzvah* of giving to the *kohen*. The *Aruch HaShulchan* and others argue that while the *de'oraita* requirement for removing *tevel* is satisfied by even a single grain, the *mitzvah* of giving — *venatan*, as the verse puts it — requires an amount of genuine substance. Once, however, one has designated a trivial amount as *terumah*, that designation cannot be undone. The *kohen* receives the grain that was separated; one cannot subsequently add to it. If one wishes to fulfill the *mitzvah* of giving more ideally, one would need to separate additional *terumah* in the first instance.

### Peah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּאָה בְּשִׁשִּׁים, וְהָתְנַן: אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן שִׁיעוּר: הַפֵּאָה, וְהַבִּכּוּרִים, וְהָרֵאָיוֹן. דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר, דְּרַבָּנַן – בְּשִׁשִּׁים. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין לַפֵּאָה מִשִּׁשִּׁים, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ הַפֵּאָה אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר! הָתָם בָּאָרֶץ, הָכָא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav and Shmuel said above that the amount one is required to leave in his field as ***pe'a*** is one part out **of sixty.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 1:1): **These are** the **matters,** i.e., mitzvot, **that have no measure: *Pe'a*, and the first fruits, and the** burnt offering of **appearance** sacrificed on the pilgrimage Festivals. The Gemara answers: **By Torah law,** *pe'a* **has no** fixed **measure; by rabbinic law** the measure is one part **of sixty.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the statement of Rav and Shmuel **teaching us? We** already **learn** in the subsequent mishna (*Pe'a* 1:2): One should **not give for *pe'a* less than** one part **of sixty, even though they said** that **the** mitzva of ***pe'a* has no measure.** The Gemara answers: The mishna **there** is referring to the obligation to leave *pe'a* **in Eretz** Yisrael, whereas **here,** the statement of Rav and Shmuel is referring to the obligation of *pe'a* **outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

The *Gemara* turns to *pe'ah*. Here too the claim is that the minimum is a *sixtieth*, yet a *mishnah* in *Pe'ah* explicitly teaches that *pe'ah* has no minimum measure at all — *ein lah shiur*. The *Gemara* resolves this as it did for *terumah*: *de'oraita ein lah shiur* — biblically there is no minimum — but *derabbanan b'shishim* — rabbinically the standard is a *sixtieth*. This is itself taught in a *mishnah*: *ein pochtin l'pe'ah mishishim*, one does not give less than a *sixtieth* for *pe'ah*, even though the Torah prescribes no minimum.

One might then ask: what *chidush* is Rav and Shmuel adding if the *mishnah* already states the *sixtieth* for *pe'ah*? The *Gemara* answers: *hatam b'aretz hacha b'chutz la'aretz* — the *mishnah* speaks of *Eretz Yisrael*, while Rav and Shmuel are teaching that even in the lands adjacent to Israel where certain agricultural obligations were rabinically applied, the standard remains a *sixtieth*. The principal *chidush* in Rav and Shmuel's statement remains, however, the application of the *sixtieth* to *reishit hagez* — where one might have expected an absolute measure from the *mishnah*'s language, Rav and Shmuel establish that the fundamental obligation is proportional.

## Isi bar Hini

### Grammar Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק אִיסִי בַּר הִינִי, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ (לְרַבִּי) [רַבִּי] יוֹחָנָן דְּקָא מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ ״רְחֵלִים״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְנְיֵיהּ ״רְחֵלוֹת״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּדִכְתִיב ״רְחֵלִים מָאתַיִם״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְשׁוֹן תּוֹרָה לְעַצְמָהּ, לְשׁוֹן חֲכָמִים לְעַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** § **When Isi bar Hini ascended** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, **Rabbi Yoḥanan found him teaching** the mishna **to his son.** Isi taught that the obligation of the first sheared wool applies only in the case of **sheep [*reḥelim*],** the masculine plural form of *raḥel*, meaning a sheep. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Isi: You should **teach him** using the term ***reḥelot*,** the feminine plural form. Isi **said to him** in reply: I teach the mishna in accordance with **that which is written: "Two hundred *reḥelim*"** (Genesis 32:15). Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Isi: The **language** of the **Torah is distinct** and the **language** of the **Sages is distinct,** i.e., these are like two separate languages, and the Sages do not always use the same forms that appear in the Bible. In this case, they use *reḥelot* rather than *reḥelim*.

The *Gemara* now introduces a narrative about Isi bar Hini's visit to *Eretz Yisrael*. Rabbi Yochanan encountered Isi bar Hini while the latter was teaching his son the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* uses the word *rechelim* — female sheep — and Rabbi Yochanan corrected him: the proper form is *rachelot*, in the feminine plural as Mishnaic Hebrew employs it.

### Hebrew Standards

Isi bar Hini defended himself by citing a biblical verse: *rechelim matayim* — when Yaakov assembled gifts for Esav, the Torah uses the masculine plural form *rechelim* for female sheep. Rabbi Yochanan replied: *lashon Torah l'atzma lashon chachamim l'atzmam* — biblical Hebrew has its own grammar, and Mishnaic Hebrew has its own. The two are distinct literary registers, and one cannot import biblical usage into the Mishnaic context. Rabbi Yochanan, as a native of *Eretz Yisrael*, was fully fluent in spoken and Mishnaic Hebrew; Isi bar Hini, coming from Bavel, naturally fell back on biblical usage when uncertain — precisely as one who does not know the contemporary spoken form will reach for the classical source.

### Respect for Rebbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן רֵישׁ סִדְרָא בְּבָבֶל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַבָּא אֲרִיכָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַבָּא אֲרִיכָא קָרֵית לֵיהּ?! דְּכִירְנָא כַּד הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא אַחַר שְׁבַע עֶשְׂרֵה שׁוּרָן אֲחוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, וְנָפְקִי זִיקּוּקִין דְּנוּר מִפּוּמֵּיהּ דְּרַב לְפוּמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, וּמִפּוּמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי לְפוּמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, וְלֵית אֲנָא יָדַע מָה הֵן אָמְרִין, וְאַתְּ ״אַבָּא אֲרִיכָא״ קָרֵית לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Isi: **Who is the head of the yeshiva [*reish sidra*] in Babylonia?** Isi **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: It is **Abba the tall,** i.e., the *amora* Rav. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Isi: **You call him Abba the tall,** in such a familiar manner? **I remember when I sat seventeen rows behind Rav,** who sat **before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and fiery sparks emerged from the mouth of Rav to the mouth of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and from the mouth of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **to the mouth of Rav, and I did not know what they said,** due to the profundity of their discussion. **And** yet **you call him Abba the tall?**

Rabbi Yochanan then asked Isi bar Hini: *man reish sidra b'Vavel?* — who is the leading *rosh yeshiva* in Bavel? The question seems to carry an implicit criticism: if the head of your academy is as great as he is, why is your grammar so poor? Isi replied: *Abba Aricha* — Abba the tall one, which is Rav, whose full name was Abba.

Rabbi Yochanan was taken aback by the casual manner of address: *Abba Aricha karyat lei?* — you call him simply "Abba the tall one"? He then described his own memory of Rav: when Rav was still in *Eretz Yisrael* before departing for Bavel, he sat seventeen rows behind Rav in the academy of Rebbi. And as Rav and Rebbi exchanged words, *v'nafik zikukin d'nur mipumei d'Rav l'pumei d'Rebbi* — sparks of fire would emerge from Rav's mouth to Rebbi's mouth and back again, *v'la hava yadana mai ka'amri* — and Rabbi Yochanan could not understand a word of what they were saying. That is who Rav is. *V'at Abba Aricha karyat lei?* — and you refer to him so familiarly?

The correction here is not merely about titles. Rabbi Yochanan is making a point about how one speaks of one's teachers and of the great figures of one's generation. Twice in this exchange he corrects Isi bar Hini: once on grammar and once on respect. The way one speaks matters — in language, and in the reverence one conveys toward those who taught and shaped one's tradition.

### Reishit Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז בְּכַמָּה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּשִׁשִּׁים. וְהָאֲנַן ״בְּכׇל שֶׁהֵן״ תְּנַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, מָה בֵּין לִי וְלָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** Isi **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: With regard to **the first sheared wool, to how much** fleece does the mitzva apply? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him:** The first sheared wool applies **to** fleece weighing **sixty** *sela*. Isi asked: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna (135a) that the first sheared wool applies **to any amount** of fleece? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him** in reply: **If so,** i.e., if the *halakha* could be understood by a simple reading of the mishna, **what** is the difference **between my** knowledge **and yours?** In fact, you know only the mishna's statement, whereas I know the halakhic conclusion in this matter.

Isi bar Hini then turned the conversation to *halacha* and asked Rabbi Yochanan: *reishit hagez b'chama?* — what is the measure for *reishit hagez*? Rabbi Yochanan responded: *beshishim* — sixty, either meaning one must begin with sixty *sela* or that one gives a *sixtieth* (Rashi offers both explanations, though the reading of a *sixtieth* seems preferable in context).

Isi bar Hini challenged: but the *mishnah* says *kol shehu* — any amount! How can you impose a minimum of sixty? Rabbi Yochanan replied: *im kein ma bein li velach* — if all I can do is quote the *mishnah* at face value, then what distinguishes my role from yours? I too know how to read a *mishnah*. The point is that despite the *mishnah*'s language of *kol shehu*, there is more to be said, and Rabbi Yochanan maintains that the operative minimum is sixty, or a *sixtieth*, rather than a literal *kol shehu*.

## R. Yochanan Contradiction

### Two Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז – רַב אָמַר בְּשִׁשִּׁים, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר בְּשֵׁשׁ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב דִּימִי: אַנַּחְתְּ לַן חֲדָא וְאַקְשֵׁת לַן חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he **said:** With regard to **the first sheared wool, Rav says** that the mitzva applies **to sixty** *sela*, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan in the name of Rabbi Yannai says** that the mitzva applies **to six** *sela*. **Abaye said to Rav Dimi: You have made one** statement **work out well for us, but you have made another** statement **difficult for us.**

When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael* he reported: Rav says *beshishim* — sixty or a *sixtieth*; Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yannai says *beshesh* — six. This is the position we encountered earlier: one needs to begin with only six *sela* in order to give five as an absolute minimum.

### Abaye's Kashya

Abaye challenged Rav Dimi: *anachta lan chada v'aktasta lan chada* — one of these reports makes sense, the other is problematic. How can Rabbi Yochanan say both? In the exchange with Isi bar Hini just described, Rabbi Yochanan clearly maintained *shishim* — sixty or a *sixtieth* — yet here he is reported saying *shesh* in the name of Rabbi Yannai.

### Different Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִידֵיהּ, הָא דְרַבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye elaborates: **Granted,** you have resolved a contradiction between two statements of Rabbi Yoḥanan. Rabbi Yoḥanan said above that the amount of fleece to which the obligation of the first sheared wool applies is six *sela*, whereas in his answer to Isi he stated that the amount is sixty *sela*. According to Rav Dimi, the apparent contradiction between one statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan and** the other statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** is **not difficult,** as **this** statement **is his** and **that** statement **is his teacher's.** Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that the obligation applies to sixty *sela*, whereas his teacher Rabbi Yannai maintains that it applies to six *sela*.

The *Gemara* resolves the Rabbi Yochanan contradiction straightforwardly: *ha didei ha de-rabbei* — one statement is Rabbi Yochanan's own position (*shishim*), and one is what he transmitted in the name of his teacher Rabbi Yannai (*shesh*). There is no contradiction when the two statements belong to different authorities.

### Rav's Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּרַב אַדְּרַב קַשְׁיָא, דְּהָא אָמַר רַב מָנֶה וּפְרָס! דְּרַב אַדְּרַב נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא, מַאי מָנֶה דְּקָאָמַר? בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים סְלָעִים, דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ בְּשִׁשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **But** the contradiction between one statement **of Rav and** another statement **of Rav** poses **a difficulty, as Rav said** that fleece weighing **one hundred dinars [*maneh*] and half** of one hundred dinars [*maneh*] is obligated in the first sheared wool, whereas Rav Dimi stated that according to Rav the obligation applies to sixty *sela*, which are 240 dinars. The Gemara answers: The apparent contradiction between the one statement **of Rav and** the other statement **of Rav** is **also not difficult,** as **what** is the ***maneh*** of **which** Rav **said** that a *maneh* and a half are obligated? Rav was referring not to a *maneh* of one hundred dinars but to a *maneh* **of forty *sela*,** i.e., 160 dinars, a *maneh* and a half of **which is** equivalent to the weight **of sixty** *sela*.

But the difficulty with Rav remains. Here Rav is quoted as saying *shishim* — sixty — yet at the opening of the discussion Rav said the minimum is one and a half *maneh*, which equals 37.5 *sela*. These are not the same number, and they reflect different frameworks. How can Rav's position be internally consistent?

## Rav's Maneh

### Two Manahs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשִׁשִּׁים, וְתָנֵי תַּנָּא: מָנֶה בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים סְלָעִים. אִין! וְהָתְנַן: חֵמֶת חֲדָשָׁה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּקַבֶּלֶת רִמּוֹנִים – טְהוֹרָה. תְּפָרָהּ וְנִקְרְעָה – שִׁיעוּרָהּ כְּמוֹצִיא רִמּוֹנִים. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: כְּפַקְעִיּוֹת שֶׁל שְׁתִי, אַחַת מֵאַרְבַּע, בְּמָנֶה בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** equivalent to the weight **of sixty** *sela*, as stated by Rav Dimi. The Gemara asks: **But** does **a *tanna* teach** that **a *maneh* is of forty *sela*?** The Gemara answers: **Yes; and we learned** in the *Tosefta* (*Kelim*, *Bava Metzia* 6:2): With regard to **a new** leather **flask** that is not yet completely sewn together, **even though it can contain pomegranates,** nevertheless, because it cannot contain liquids it is considered unfinished and is **not susceptible to ritual impurity.** If one **sewed** the flask together **and it tore,** the **measure** of the tear that renders the flask no longer susceptible to impurity is a hole large enough **to enable pomegranates to go out,** as then it ceases to serve as a vessel. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** The tear must be **like** the measure of **balls of a warp,** the weight of each of which is **one-quarter of a *maneh* of forty *sela*.** This *tanna* explicitly mentions a *maneh* of forty *sela*.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction in Rav's position as follows: *mai mane de-ka'amar ben arba'im sela'im* — when Rav said one and a half *maneh*, he was not using the standard *maneh* of 25 *sela*. He was referring to a *maneh* of 40 *sela*.

### Rav Consistent

One and a half times forty equals sixty. *De-have leh b'shishim* — that comes out to sixty. Rav's statement of one and a half *maneh* and his statement of sixty are thus the same number, expressed in different terms. The *maneh* of 40 *sela* reconciles what appeared to be a contradiction.

### Shuttle Reference

One might wonder whether a *tanna* would ever use a *maneh* of 40 *sela* — it is an unusual measure. The *Gemara* confirms its existence by citing a *baraita* (preserved in the *Tosefta*) about a leather flask. The *baraita* discusses at what point a flask that has not yet been fully sewn acquires the status of a *keli* susceptible to *tumah*, and at what point a completed flask loses that status once it tears. Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov says that the relevant gauge is whether the hole is large enough that the shuttle used in weaving (*kefa'iyot shel shesi*, the implements used in the warp of a loom) would fall through. The *baraita* then specifies the size of this shuttle: *achas me-arba'ah be-mane ben arba'im sela'im* — one-quarter of a *sela* from a *maneh* that is made up of forty *sela*. This confirms that the forty-*sela* *maneh* appears in Talmudic sources, even if the speaker must explicitly clarify which *maneh* he means. Normally one assumes the standard *maneh* of twenty-five *sela*; but the forty-*sela* *maneh* exists and is referenced in authentic *tannaitic* material. With this, Rav's position is fully consistent: both his statements describe sixty *sela* as the minimum, and the apparent discrepancy dissolves.

## Five Sela Amount

### Raw Wool Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה נוֹתֵן לוֹ כּוּ׳. תָּנָא – לֹא שֶׁיְּלַבְּנֶנּוּ וְיִתְּנֶנּוּ לוֹ, אֶלָּא שֶׁיְּלַבְּנֶנּוּ כֹּהֵן וְיַעֲמוֹד עַל חֲמֵשׁ סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And how much** of the sheared wool **does one give to** the priest? One gives him the weight of five *sela* in Judea, which are ten *sela* in the Galilee, once laundered and not when sullied. The Sages **taught:** The mishna does **not** mean **that one** must **launder** the wool **and** then **give it to** the priest; **rather,** the meaning is **that** one must give him enough wool for the **priest to launder it and it will amount to five *sela*.**

The *mishnah* asks *ve-kamah noten lo* — how much does one give the *kohen*? The answer given is five *sela*. A *baraita* clarifies that this does not mean one launders the wool first and then gives it: *lo she-yilbenenu ve-yitnenu lo* — one does not launder it and then give it. One gives the wool raw.

### After Laundering

The five *sela* figure refers to the value of the wool after processing. One must give enough raw wool that, after the *kohen* launders it himself, it will amount to five *sela* worth. The obligation is thus to give sufficient raw material, not a finished product.

This connects to the broader theme noted earlier: once the wool is separated for the *kohen*, it is in some sense already his, and one should not continue to handle or process it. If one were to dye it, for example, that would constitute a misappropriation — treating as one's own something that has effectively entered the *kohen*'s domain. Laundering it before giving it, while not as severe, would similarly overstep; the *kohen* receives the raw wool and processes it himself.

### Verse Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת בֶּגֶד קָטָן. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אָמַר קְרָא ״לַעֲמֹד לְשָׁרֵת״ – דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לְשֵׁירוּת. מַאי נִיהוּ? אַבְנֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states: The measure that must be given to the priest is **enough to fashion a small garment** from it. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Yeshoshua ben Levi said:** The **verse** that follows the mention of the first sheared wool **states:** "For the Lord your God has chosen him out of all your tribes, **to stand to serve** in the name of the Lord, he and his sons forever" (Deuteronomy 18:5). The term "to serve" indicates that the first sheared wool given to the priest must be **a matter that is fitting for service** in the Temple, i.e., an amount of wool sufficient to fashion one of the priestly garments. **What is** the garment in question? It is the **belt,** which is made from wool weighing five *sela*.

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for this minimum? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi answers from a verse: *la-amod le-sharet* — God has chosen the *kohen* to stand and to serve. The gift must therefore be *davar she-hu ra'uy le-sheirut* — something fit for service in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The wool must be sufficient to produce a garment appropriate for priestly service.

### The Belt

What is the smallest priestly garment? The *Gemara* says: *avnet* — the belt. The wool that would go into the belt of a *kohen* during service amounts to five *sela*.

### Tafasta Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא מְעִיל! תָּפַשְׂתָּ מְרוּבֶּה – לֹא תָּפַשְׂתָּ, תָּפַשְׂתָּ מוּעָט – תָּפַשְׂתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is the garment in question necessarily the belt? **Say** that it is the **robe,** which is fashioned from a far greater amount of wool. The Gemara answers: This inference is based on the principle that if **you grasped a lot you did not grasp** anything; if **you grasped a little, you grasped** something. Since a belt meets the condition of "to serve," as it is one of the priestly vestments, one cannot say that the obligation is any greater than the amount of wool needed to fashion a belt.

One might suggest the standard should be a larger garment, such as the *me'il*, the priestly robe. The *Gemara* rejects this with a well-known principle: *tafasta meruba lo tafasta, tafasta mu'at tafasta* — if you grab too much, you have not grabbed; if you grab a little, you have grabbed. The intent is: do not overreach. When the verse says a garment fit for service, and we do not know exactly which garment is meant, we take the minimum. If one claims it refers to a large garment, one may not be able to sustain that claim; if one claims it refers to the smallest garment, that claim is secure. The minimum here is the belt.

## Kippah/Tzitz

### Kippah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא כִּיפָּה שֶׁל צֶמֶר, דְּתַנְיָא: כִּיפָּה שֶׁל צֶמֶר הָיְתָה מוּנַּחַת בְּרֹאשׁ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְעָלֶיהָ צִיץ נָתוּן, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשַׂמְתָּ אֹתוֹ עַל פְּתִיל תְּכֵלֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** **But say** that the garment in question is the **cap of wool** that the High Priest wears, which is smaller than the belt. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A cap of wool was placed on the High Priest's head, and** the **frontplate was placed upon it, to fulfill that which is stated** with regard to the frontplate: **"And you shall put it on a thread of sky blue,** and it shall be upon the mitre; upon the forefront of the mitre it shall be" (Exodus 28:37). The term "thread of sky blue" is referring to the cap of sky-blue wool.

The *Gemara* now raises an alternative: perhaps the standard should be a small wool *kippah* worn by the *kohen gadol*, which might require even less wool than the belt. A *baraita* describes this: a small wool *kippah* was placed on the head of the *kohen gadol*, and the *tzitz* — the golden frontplate inscribed *Kodesh L'Hashem* — rested upon it. The verse says the *tzitz* was placed *al pesil techeiles*, on a blue thread; this *baraita* reads *pesil techeiles* as referring to the small blue wool *kippah* on which the *tzitz* sat. On this reading, the *tzitz* was not simply tied around the head with a string; rather, there was a small wool garment interposed between the gold plate and the forehead of the *kohen gadol*.

It must be noted that this description is unusual. The *Rambam* does not record it, and the standard understanding of *pesil techeiles* is a cord or string securing the *tzitz*. Artscroll renders it as a "ribbon," a translation that attempts to bridge the gap between a string and a *kippah* — something wider and more substantial than a string but not as full as a *kippah*. In any case, the *baraita* describes it as a small wool head-covering worn by the *kohen gadol* beneath the *tzitz*.

### Kohen Gadol Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא ״הוּא וּבָנָיו״, דָּבָר הַשָּׁוֶה לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the **verse states:** "To stand to serve in the name of the Lord, **he and his sons"** (Deuteronomy 18:5), which indicates that the verse is referring to **a matter,** i.e., a garment, **that is equal for Aaron and for his sons.** The wool given to the priest must be of sufficient size for fashioning a garment worn both by the High Priest and by common priests, whereas the woolen cap is worn only by the High Priest.

The *Gemara* raises a further objection: the verse *hu uvanav* — he and his sons — implies that the garment in question must be something equally worn by the *kohen gadol* and by ordinary *kohanim*. The *kippah* described above was worn only by the *kohen gadol*, not by other *kohanim*. It therefore cannot serve as the standard by which to measure the gift of first-shearings, which applies to all *kohanim*.

### Belt Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבְנֵט נָמֵי לָא שָׁוֵי. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לֹא זֶהוּ אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זֶהוּ אַבְנֵטוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? שֵׁם אַבְנֵט בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But the **belt is also not equal** for all priests. The Gemara elaborates: **This works out well according to the one who said** that the linen **belt of** the **High Priest** worn on Yom Kippur **is not** the same as **the belt of an ordinary priest.** According to this opinion, both the belt of common priests and the belt worn by the High Priest during the rest of the year were fashioned from a mixture of wool and linen. This belt is therefore equal for all priests, and it works out **well.** **But according to the one who said** that the linen belt worn by the High Priest on Yom Kippur **is** the same as the **belt of an ordinary priest, what can be said?** According to this opinion, the belt fashioned from wool and linen is worn only by the High Priest during the rest of the year. The Gemara answers: Although the belts are different, the **term belt in general** applies to all priests, whereas no type of cap is worn by common priests.

The *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur wore a belt made entirely of linen. During the rest of the year, his belt was *shatnez* — a mixture of wool and linen. The belt of an ordinary *kohen* is subject to dispute: one view holds that it was the same as the *kohen gadol*'s Yom Kippur belt (all linen), while another view holds it was like the *kohen gadol*'s year-round belt (wool and linen).

### All Wear Belts

The *Gemara* resolves the issue as follows. If one holds that the ordinary *kohen*'s belt contained wool, then it was worn equally by the *kohen gadol* and by ordinary *kohanim*, and serves perfectly as the standard. If one holds that the ordinary *kohen* wore only a linen belt, then the wool element is confined to the *kohen gadol*'s garment — but all *kohanim* wear a belt of some kind. The principle is *shem avnet ba'olam* — the name "belt" applies universally. The verse requires a garment fit for priestly service, and the belt is the smallest garment worn by all *kohanim*. That at least one version of the priestly belt contains wool is sufficient to establish the wool measure. The five *sela* standard for *reishit hagez* is thus derived from the amount of wool required for the *avnet*.

## Gazaz U'Mechar

### Debate Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיתְּנוֹ וְכוּ׳. אִיתְּמַר: גָּזַז וּמָכַר רִאשׁוֹנָה, רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: חַיָּיב, רַבִּי נָתָן בַּר הוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: If the owner of the shearing **did not manage to give it** to the priest until he dyed it, he is exempt from the obligation of giving the first sheared wool. The mishna further teaches that one who purchases the fleece of the sheep of a gentile is exempt from the obligation of the first sheared wool. It **was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to one who owned five sheep and he **sheared and sold the first** sheep before shearing the second, and in this manner sold each sheep after shearing it. When he finished shearing he owned the requisite five fleeces, to which the obligation of the first sheared wool applies, but he no longer owned the sheep. **Rav Ḥisda says:** He is **obligated** in the mitzva of the first sheared wool; and **Rabbi Natan bar Hoshaya says:** He is **exempt** from the mitzva of the first sheared wool.

The *mishnah* addressed in this section deals with *gazaz umachar rishona* — one who sheared the first sheep and sold it (in context, as Rashi clarifies, one who sheared and then sold the sheep itself) before completing the shearing of all five. The question is whether such a person is still obligated in *reishit hagez*, given that by the time all five shearings are complete, not all five sheep remain in the owner's possession.

### Chisda View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אֲמַר חַיָּיב, דְּהָא גָּזַז.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the two opinions. **Rav Ḥisda says** that he is **obligated, as** he **sheared** five sheep that he owned at the time of shearing, and therefore the term: "Your flock" (Deuteronomy 18:4), applies to this case.

Rav Chisda rules: *chayav* — obligated. The fact that one sheared five sheep is the operative criterion, regardless of whether all five remained in one's possession throughout the entire process.

### Natan View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן בַּר הוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר פָּטוּר, בְּעִידָּנָא דְּקָא מָלֵא שִׁיעוּרָא בָּעֵינַן ״צֹאנְךָ״, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan bar Hoshaya says** that he is **exempt,** as **at the time that the measure** of five fleeces **is completed, we require** the term **"your flock"** to apply, since the obligation takes effect at that stage, **and** in this case it does **not** apply.

Rav Natan bar Oshaya rules: *patur* — exempt. His reasoning: *be-idna de-kamlei shiura*, at the moment the minimum threshold is completed — when all five shearings have been assembled — *be'inan tzoncha*, the flock must still be yours, *ve-leika*, and it is not, because some sheep were sold in the interim.

### Selling Sheep

Rashi's explanation confirms that the case involves selling the sheep themselves after shearing, not merely selling the shorn wool. The owner sheared each sheep and then sold it before proceeding to the next. By the time the fifth sheep was sheared, the first four had already left his possession. He holds all five shearings but no longer owns all five sheep.

### Timing Question

The question thus turns on when exactly the obligation of *reishit hagez* crystallizes. Does it attach at the moment of shearing each individual sheep — in which case the ownership condition was met for each at the time of shearing — or does it attach only when the minimum threshold (five shearings) is complete, by which time the flock has been partially dispersed? The *Gemara* leaves this as the substance of the dispute between Rav Chisda and Rav Natan bar Oshaya.

## Purchasing Wool

### Non-Jew Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ גֵּז צֹאנוֹ שֶׁל גּוֹי, פָּטוּר מֵרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז. הָא צֹאנוֹ לִגְזוֹז – חַיָּיב. אַמַּאי? כֹּל חַד וְחַד בָּתַר גִּיזָּה נָפְקָא לַהּ מֵרְשׁוּתֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a challenge: **We learned** in the mishna (135a): **One who purchases the fleece of the sheep of a gentile is exempt from** the obligation of **the first sheared wool,** as he purchased only the fleece but not the sheep. One can infer from here that if he purchased the gentile's **sheep** themselves in order **to shear** them and then return them to the gentile, he is **obligated,** because the sheep belonged to him at the time of shearing. But **why** is he obligated, according to the opinion of Rabbi Natan bar Hoshaya? **Each and every one** of the sheep, **after** the **shearing** is completed, **leaves his possession,** and when he has sheared five sheep, the term "your flock" no longer applies to them.

The *mishnah* rules that one who purchases the shearings of a non-Jew's flock is exempt from *reishit hagez*. The simple case is straightforward: the non-Jew already sheared and sold the wool; the buyer never owned the sheep, so no obligation attaches. Even if one purchased the wool while it was still on the sheep — effectively buying the shearings in advance and then performing the shearing oneself — one is still exempt, because the sheep were never *tzoncha*, one's own flock.

### Shearing Purpose

However, if one purchased the sheep themselves from a non-Jew — not for full ownership but specifically for the purpose of shearing — one would be *chayav*. The reason is that at the moment of shearing, the sheep are indeed one's own, and the act of shearing occurs under conditions of ownership.

### Friend's Residue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַלּוֹקֵחַ גֵּז צֹאנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ כּוּ׳. מַאן תַּנָּא דְּהֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא שִׁיּוּרָא גַּבֵּי מוֹכֵר, בָּתַר מוֹכֵר אָזְלִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **one who purchases the fleece of the sheep of another** Jew, if the seller kept some of the wool, then he is obligated to give the first sheared wool to the priest. If the seller did not keep any of the wool, the buyer is obligated to give it. The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught that** in a case **where there is residual** wool **in** the possession of the **seller, we follow the seller** in determining who is obligated in the mitzva of first sheared wool?

The more complex case involves purchasing the shearings of a fellow Israelite's flock — that is, buying the wool that is still on the sheep, before shearing takes place. If the seller retains even a small portion of his wool (and thus shears some himself), the obligation remains his. If he sells everything, the obligation passes to the buyer.

### Peah Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַמּוֹכֵר קִלְחֵי אִילָן בְּתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ – נוֹתֵן פֵּאָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The *tanna* who taught the mishna **is Rabbi Yehuda, as we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 3:5): With regard to **one who sells** a few fruit-bearing **tree stalks within his field,** without selling the field itself, for the buyer to uproot them and plant them in his own field, the buyer **gives** separate ***pe'a* for each and every one** of the trees. The field does not combine the trees into a single unit for *pe'a*, as the land is not owned by the buyer.

Rav Chisda initially attempts to ground this ruling in an analogy to *pe'ah*. A *mishnah* in *Pe'ah* discusses one who sells individual trees growing in his grain field. Rav Yehuda rules: if the field owner retains even one tree for himself, he bears the *pe'ah* obligation for the entire crop — including the trees he sold. The same logic would apply here: if the seller retains any wool, he bears the entire *reishit hagez* obligation.

### Rava Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר, וְהוּא שֶׁהִתְחִיל בַּעַל הַשָּׂדֶה לִקְצוֹר! וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר, וְהוּא שֶׁהִתְחִיל לִגְזוֹז – בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, ״וּבְקֻצְרְכֶם אֶת קְצִיר אַרְצְכֶם״ כְּתִיב, מֵעִידָּנָא דְּאַתְחֵיל לִקְצוֹר מִיחַיַּיב בְּכוּלַּהּ שָׂדֶה, אֶלָּא הָכָא, מֵעִידָּנָא דְּאַתְחֵיל לְמֵיגַז לָא מִיחַיַּיב בְּכוּלֵּיהּ עֶדְרֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Ḥisda: **But wasn't it** you, **Master, who said** with regard to Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that the owner gives *pe'a* for all the trees: **This is** the *halakha* only **when the owner of the field began to harvest** the fruit before he sold the trees, as the obligation to give *pe'a* had already applied to him. By contrast, with regard to the first sheared wool, the obligation came into effect only after he sold his sheep. **And if you would say** that **so too,** with regard to the first sheared wool, **this** *halakha* that the seller gives the first sheared wool applies only **if** the seller **began to shear** the sheep before he sold them, that explanation is difficult. The Gemara elaborates: **Granted, there,** with regard to *pe'a*, it **is written: "And when you reap the harvest of your land"** (Leviticus 19:9), which indicates that **from the time that he began to harvest he is obligated** in the mitzva of *pe'a* **with regard to the entire field. But here,** in the case of the first sheared wool, **he is not obligated with regard to the entire flock from the time that he began to shear** his sheep. Therefore, even if he began shearing before he sold the sheep, the obligation to give the first sheared wool should not apply to the seller.

Rava objects to the analogy. Rav Yehuda's ruling in the *pe'ah* case applies only *ve-hu she-hitchil ba'al hasadeh liktzor* — only when the field owner had already begun harvesting before he sold the trees. The verse governing *pe'ah* reads *u-vekutzrechem et ketzir artzechem* — when you harvest — establishing that the obligation falls the moment harvesting begins. Once the owner began harvesting, the *pe'ah* obligation had already crystallized over the entire field; subsequent sale of individual trees does not divest him of that obligation. But in our case, the seller had not yet begun shearing before selling the wool. And unlike *pe'ah*, which attaches at the beginning of harvesting, *reishit hagez* attaches not at the start of shearing but at its conclusion — the verse says *gez tzoncha*, referring to the completed shearings, not to the act of shearing. If the obligation does not even arise until the shearing is complete, and if the seller was not yet obligated at the time of sale, how can the analogy to *pe'ah* sustain the rule?

### Never Sold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא, דִּתְנַן: אָמַר לוֹ ״מְכוֹר לִי בְּנֵי מֵעֶיהָ שֶׁל פָּרָה זוֹ״, וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן מַתָּנוֹת – נוֹתְנָן לַכֹּהֵן, וְאֵין מְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן הַדָּמִים. לָקַח מִמֶּנּוּ בְּמִשְׁקָל – נוֹתְנָן לַכֹּהֵן וּמְנַכֶּה לוֹ מִן הַדָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: It is this *tanna*** who taught the mishna, **as we learned** in a different mishna (132a): If one **said to** a butcher: **Sell me the innards of this cow, and there were gifts** of the priesthood included **in them,** i.e., the maw, the purchaser **must give them to the priest, and** he **may not deduct** the value of the gifts **from the money** that he pays the butcher, as it is assumed that the gifts were not included in the sale. If **he purchased** the innards **from** the butcher **by weight,** the buyer **must give** the gifts **to a priest and** he **may deduct** the value of the gifts **from the money** that he pays the butcher. If the priestly gifts have not yet been separated from the animal, the price by weight includes the price of these gifts. But since the priests had the right to their gifts from the time of the slaughter, the buyer does not need to pay for them and may therefore deduct their value from his payment.

Rava therefore proposes a different foundation. He cites the principle established earlier in the tractate in the context of the stomach and other *matnot kohen*: *matnot de-kohen lo mizabin inish* — a person does not sell priestly gifts. When a butcher weighs out an animal and sells it, and the sale incidentally includes the stomach (which belongs to the *kohen*), we do not treat the stomach as genuinely sold. The butcher did not intend to sell it, and the buyer did not intend to purchase an obligation that was never his. Therefore, when the buyer gives the stomach to the *kohen*, he can recover its purchase price from the butcher.

### Butcher Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא מַתָּנוֹת דְּכֹהֵן לָא מְזַבֵּין אִינִישׁ, הָכָא נָמֵי מַתָּנוֹת דְּכֹהֵן לָא מְזַבֵּין אִינִישׁ. הִלְכָּךְ: שִׁיֵּיר הַמּוֹכֵר – מוֹכֵר חַיָּיב, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ לוֹקֵחַ: ״מַתָּנָה דְּכֹהֵן גַּבָּךְ הִיא״. לֹא שִׁיֵּיר – לוֹקֵחַ חַיָּיב, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ מוֹכֵר: ״מַתָּנָה דְּכֹהֵן לָא זַבֵּנִי לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Evidently, a person does not sell the gifts** belonging **to** the **priest,** and therefore they are not included in the sale of the innards unless they were sold by weight. **Here too,** with regard to the first sheared wool, **a person does not sell the gifts** belonging **to** the **priest. Therefore,** if **the seller left** wool in his possession, the **seller is obligated** to give the first sheared wool from the remaining wool for that which he sold, **as** the **buyer** can **say to** the seller: The **gift of** the **priest is in your** possession, since you did not sell me everything. If the seller did **not leave** any wool in his possession, **the buyer is obligated** to give the first sheared wool and he does not deduct its value from the price, **as the seller** can **say to him: I did not sell the gift of** the **priest to you,** i.e., there was no obligation to give the gifts to a priest when I sold the wool to you, and therefore the buyer is required to give the gifts to the priest.

The same interpretive principle governs the *reishit hagez* case. When the seller sold the wool on his sheep, we read his intentions generously: he never intended to transfer to the buyer the portion that would ultimately become *matnot kehunah*. The sale is real for everything else, but the *matnot* component is notionally excluded from what was transferred — even though the seller was not yet formally obligated at the time of sale.

### Final Rule

The operative rule that emerges is as follows. If the seller retains any residue of wool — even a small amount — then we say the buyer effectively told him: whatever *matnot kehunah* arise from this transaction, they come from your remaining portion. The seller remains obligated. If the seller sold everything and retained nothing, then the seller himself can say: I never sold you the *matnot* portion — meaning it was never genuinely transferred, and therefore it remains his to give to the *kohen*, even though legally the buyer now holds all the wool. In the latter case, Rava's interpretation ensures the obligation stays with the seller — the party who originally owned the flock — rather than passing entirely to the buyer who may never have contemplated taking on that responsibility.
