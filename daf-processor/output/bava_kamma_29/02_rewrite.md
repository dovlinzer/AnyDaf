# Bava Kamma 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav v. Shmuel

### Debate Setup

The discussion on 29b opens by returning to a debate that occupied much of the preceding amud: the disagreement between Rav and Shmuel concerning an object left in the *reshus harabbim* — specifically water or a broken vessel — that causes injury. The core question is whether such a hazard is classified as *shor* or *bor*. Rav holds that it retains the character of *shor*: since the owner still owns the object, it functions like an animal that goes out and damages, making the owner liable even for damage to vessels. Shmuel disagrees. Even though the owner retains ownership, the object is fundamentally a stationary hazard — a *bor* — and therefore the owner is exempt from liability for damage to vessels, just as the *mazik* of *bor* carries no liability for *keilim*.

### Tripping & Injury

A related question concerns what happens when a person trips over such an object and injures himself on the ground. Here Rav says the owner is *patur*. Even if one's ox pushed a person to the ground, since the actual damage was caused by the impact with the ground rather than by the ox itself, Rav exempts the owner. Shmuel disagrees: if a person trips over the hazard and injures himself on the ground, that ground impact is treated as an extension of the *bor*, and the owner is *chayav*.

## Elazar's Limitation

### Three Scenarios

At the bottom of 28b, the *Gemara* introduces a ruling of Rabbi Elazar. The case at hand is tripping: in what scenario is the owner of a stone left in the *reshus harabbim* liable when someone trips and is injured? Rabbi Elazar articulates three distinct scenarios.

The first is the clearest: one trips over the stone and bangs into the stone. Here, everyone agrees the owner is *chayav*. The stone was the hazard and the stone caused the injury. The second scenario — tripping over the stone and banging into the ground — is precisely the debate of Rav and Shmuel: the stone functioned as the hazard but the ground did the damage. The third scenario, however, is the one Rabbi Elazar directly addresses: one trips over the ground itself and then slams into someone else's stone. Here, the stone did not initiate the act of damage; rather, the victim stumbled independently and impacted an object that was passively present. Rabbi Elazar rules: *nitkal ba-karka ve-nishaf be-even patur* — if you tripped over the ground and banged yourself on someone's stone, the stone's owner is *patur*.

The reasoning is significant. Whether the object is classified as *shor* or *bor*, it must be the thing that initiates the damaging act. If it merely receives the impact — passively present while the victim stumbles into it — that is not a *maise hezek* attributable to the object's owner.

### Rabbi Nasan

The *Gemara* notes that this ruling of Rabbi Elazar is *d'lo k'Rabbi Nasan* — it does not follow Rabbi Nasan's position. Rabbi Nasan holds that if, for example, one person's ox pushes a victim into another person's *bor*, the owner of the *bor* bears partial responsibility even though the *bor* functioned entirely passively — it did not cause the person to fall, it merely received them. According to Rabbi Nasan, passive participation in damage is sufficient to create liability. The *Gemara* reasons: if an ox goring is the initiating act and the *bor* is still considered a participant, then surely when I trip over my own feet and fall into someone's *bor* or onto their stone, that object should also be considered a participant in the damage. That would be the implication of Rabbi Nasan's view.

### Initiating Acts

One might distinguish, however, between the two cases. When damage begins with a goring ox, there is a defined *maise hezek* already underway, and the *bor* can be viewed as participating in that act. When a person merely trips over their own feet — even if entirely blameless — there is no initiated act of damage; it is simply something that happened to the person. A passively placed stone or *bor* is therefore not a participant in any damaging act. Rabbi Elazar, as stated in the first version of his ruling, draws precisely this distinction and rules *patur*.

### Accident as Mazik

The *Gemara* then presents an alternative version of Rabbi Elazar's ruling — *ikka d'amrei* — that goes in the opposite direction. According to this version, one should not think that liability is limited to tripping on the stone and banging on the stone. Even *nitkal ba-karka ve-nishaf be-even* — tripping over the ground and injuring oneself on the stone — renders the stone's owner *chayav*, and this position follows Rabbi Nasan. This is the second and apparently concluding position of the *Gemara*. It is a striking conclusion on two levels. First, it presupposes that *nitkal* — tripping — is classified as *ones*: the victim is not negligent for having tripped, and thus there is no *peshi'as hanizak* to exempt the stone's owner. Second, it holds that even when damage begins as a pure accident, with no initiated act of *hezek*, the stone left improperly in the *reshus harabbim* is treated as a *maise mazik* sufficient to impose liability — consistent with Rabbi Nasan's broad view of passive participation in damage.

## Mishna: Pitcher Broke

### R. Meir v. R. Yehuda

The *Gemara* now takes up the *Mishna*, whose first *Tanna* — identified as Rabbi Meir — rules that if one's pitcher broke in the *reshus harabbim* and someone slipped on the water or was injured by the shards, the owner is *chayav*. Rabbi Yehuda qualifies this: one is *chayav* only if *mitkavein* — only in circumstances involving some element of intent or deliberateness.

### Mitkavein Definition

Rava clarifies what *mitkavein* means in Rabbi Yehuda's formulation. The intent in question is not an intent to break the pitcher — that would be a different matter entirely. Rather, it means one was *mitkavein l-horida l-mata mi-kateifo* — intentionally taking the pitcher down from one's shoulder — and in the course of that deliberate act of transfer, it slipped from the hands and broke. The act of deliberately lowering the pitcher introduces a degree of fault: the moment of transfer is precisely when accidents are most likely to occur, and one is expected to exercise heightened care at that moment. By contrast, if the pitcher simply fell off one's shoulder while walking — without any deliberate action to move it — that is closer to *ones*, and Rabbi Yehuda would exempt the owner.

### Ones Liability

This raises an important question, articulated by Abaye: does Rabbi Meir truly hold one *chayav* even in a case of complete *ones*? If Rabbi Yehuda requires at least the quasi-negligence of *mitkavein*, and the *Tanna Kamma* disagrees, does that mean Rabbi Meir imposes liability even when the pitcher, say, simply dissolved in one's hands through no fault at all?

### Ones Liability Affirmed

The answer given is yes: *mechayev havah Rabbi Meir afilu ozno be-yado* — Rabbi Meir would hold one liable even if the entire pitcher collapsed while one was holding only the ear. This is a vivid image: you are standing there holding the handle, the whole vessel crumbles, and yet Rabbi Meir says you are *chayav* for whatever damage the shards and water subsequently cause.

## Ones Exemption

### Blamelessness

This conclusion immediately raises a profound objection. The *Gemara* asks: *amai, ones hu* — why should one be liable? It was a complete accident. One is entirely blameless. And the Torah itself — *ve-la-na'arah lo ta'aseh davar* — explicitly exempts the blameless party, stating in the context of a woman who is attacked that nothing should be done to her, for she bears no fault. The Torah thus establishes, in a context of capital law, that blamelessness removes culpability.

The *Gemara* then entertains the possibility that this principle of *ones* exemption applies only to capital crimes — *inyanei kilkala* — but that in matters of *nezikin* there might be strict liability. The rationale for strict liability is not arbitrary: if my water caused your injury, why should *you* be the one to absorb the loss? The fact that I am blameless may be morally significant, but from a civil damages perspective, my property was the instrument of harm. This principle exists elsewhere in the *Gemara*'s framework: *adam mu'ad le'olam* — a person is always forewarned, always liable for bodily damage regardless of intent or care, establishing that strict liability is not alien to *halakha*.

### Braita Cases

The *Gemara* now brings a *braita* to probe the matter. The *braita* states: *nishberu kado ve-lo silko, naflo gemalo ve-lo he'emido* — if one's pitcher broke and one did not remove it, or one's camel fell and one did not make it stand up. Rabbi Meir says one is *chayav* for whatever damage they cause. The *chachamim* say one is *patur be-dinei adam ve-chayav be-dinei shamayim* — exempt in human courts but liable in the heavenly court.

### Extremes Agreed

The *braita* is valuable because it also establishes two poles on which all opinions agree. The *braita* teaches: the *chachamim* concede to Rabbi Meir that if one placed stones, a knife, or a bundle on a rooftop and they fell due to a *ruach metzuya* — an expected, ordinary wind — and caused damage, one is *chayav*. This is the definition of *peshi'ah*: if the wind that would blow them down is foreseeable, one should not have left them there. Conversely, Rabbi Meir concedes to the *chachamim* that if one placed pitchers on a rooftop to dry them out and they fell due to a *ruach she'eina metzuya* — an unexpected wind, a true *ones* — one is *patur*. So both extremes are settled: *ruach metzuya* is negligence and everyone agrees you are *chayav*; *ruach she'eina metzuya* is *ones* and everyone agrees you are *patur*.

### Nitkal Debate

The debate, then, is situated in the middle ground. The case of the broken pitcher — *nishberu kado* — is neither clear negligence nor clear *ones*. It is understood as a case of *nitkal*: something slipping, breaking, falling in a way that is neither fully one's fault nor a complete unforeseeable accident. The question is which side of the line this middle ground falls on. Rabbi Meir places it on the side of *peshi'ah* and imposes liability; the *chachamim* place it on the side of *ones* and exempt. Thus one cannot say Rabbi Meir holds liable for a *bor* created through pure *ones* — his position is that the middle case of *nitkal* constitutes sufficient negligence.

## Abaye: Two Debates

### Debate 1: At Falling

Abaye introduces a more complex analysis. He argues that the *Mishna* and *braita* embed not one debate but two: *bi-tartei peligi* — they disagree on two matters. The first debate concerns *sha'at nefilah* — the moment of falling. If someone was damaged at the instant the pitcher broke or in the immediately subsequent seconds before the owner had any opportunity to act, the question is: was the owner negligent at that moment of breaking? This is the classic question of *nitkal poshea* — is tripping or slipping considered negligence? *Mar savar nitkal poshea hu* — Rabbi Meir holds that tripping constitutes negligence. *U-mar savar nitkal lav poshea hu* — the *chachamim* hold it does not. Significantly, the *Gemara* formulates the opposing view not as *ones* but as *lav poshea* — not negligent — acknowledging that while there is some element of carelessness involved, it does not rise to the level of *peshi'ah*.

The debate over *sha'at nefilah* maps neatly onto the bracketed extremes: *ruach metzuya* is unambiguous *peshi'ah*, *ruach she'eina metzuya* is unambiguous *ones*, and *nitkal* is the contested middle — is it on the *poshea* side or not?

### Debate 2: After Falling

The second debate, according to Abaye, concerns *le-achar nefilah* — after the pitcher or camel has already fallen and the owner has had an opportunity to remove the hazard but has not done so. Here the question shifts entirely: it involves *mafkir nezikav* — the owner has essentially renounced the broken object, abandoned it. The question is whether renouncing ownership exempts one from liability.

Abaye's position is conceptually striking. He argues that this second debate is logically independent of the first. Even if we concede that the pitcher broke *be-peshi'ah* — with negligence — Rabbi Yehuda would still say one is *patur* if one has been *mafkir* the object and no longer owns it. The reasoning: one is only *chayav* for a *bor* that one owns. This is understood to parallel the rule regarding a *bor bi-reshus harabbim*: when one digs a pit in a public domain, one does not technically own the pit, yet the Torah imposes liability. According to the view Abaye is attributing to Rabbi Yehuda, however, ownership is prerequisite to liability for *bor*, and once one renounces the object, the *chiyuv* falls away — even if it was created through negligence.

### Ones + Mafkir

To fully understand the scope of the debate, two scenarios must be distinguished. In the first, the pitcher fell through *ones* — a complete accident, no negligence. At the immediate moment of falling, before any opportunity to act, the owner is *patur*: there is no fault whatsoever. Now, even after time has passed and the owner would ordinarily have an opportunity to remove the hazard, if the owner was *mafkir* the broken object — if, in the way of things, one simply walks away from one's shattered pitcher — then there is no fault at the time of creation and no continuing ownership. The owner remains *patur*. Everyone agrees on this: *mafkir nezikav le-achar nefilat ones* — renouncing ownership after an accidental fall — exempts the owner. There is neither negligence nor ownership to ground liability.

### Peshia + Mafkir

The second scenario is *nefilat peshi'ah* — the pitcher fell through negligence. Here, one would logically expect liability to be inescapable regardless of what happens next. The *bor* was created through one's fault. Whether one continues to own it or not should be irrelevant: when one digs a *bor* in a *reshus harabbim*, one does not own that pit — the public domain is not one's property — yet one is *chayav*. What matters is that one created the hazard through negligence, not that one owns it. So if it fell through *peshi'ah* and one is thereafter *mafkir* it, logic would dictate continued *chiyuv* regardless.

This is indeed the position of Rabbi Meir: even if you are *mafkir nezikav* after a negligent fall, you remain *chayav*. The novelty of Abaye's reading is that Rabbi Yehuda would still exempt — not because the negligence is retroactively erased, but because Rabbi Yehuda fundamentally understands *chiyuv bor* as requiring ownership. This is described as a *mamon hamazik* model rather than a pure hazard model: the *bor* is not merely a hazard you created; it is property of yours that causes damage. Without ownership, the category of liability does not apply. This is a difficult and somewhat jarring read, but Abaye insists it is the only way to account for the full range of cases across the *Mishna* and *braita* as representing two distinct debates.

The *Gemara* establishes that both cases must be read into both the pitcher and the camel scenarios of the *braita*. The pitcher lends itself naturally to both: one can imagine damage at the moment of breaking (*sha'at nefilah*) or after the shards remained on the ground (*le-achar nefilah*). The camel case presents a challenge for the *sha'at nefilah* reading: when a camel falls, that seems like *ones* by definition — animals are not careless. The *Gemara* resolves this by positing that the debate arises when the owner himself tripped and the camel then tripped on top of the owner — making the camel's fall an extension of the owner's original act of tripping, which is the very *nitkal* case under discussion.

## R. Elazar: One Debate

### Elazar on Sha'at

Rabbi Elazar offers a simpler reading than Abaye. He says *bishas nefila machloket* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* takes place at the moment of falling, revolving around the single question of whether the owner was *poshea* at that moment. This is a clean, unified explanation: one debate, one question, one moment.

### Two Debates Confirmed

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this. If the debate is only at *sha'at nefilah*, what does Rabbi Elazar say about *le-achar nefilah*? Does he hold everyone would agree the owner is *patur* after the fall, or that everyone agrees the owner is *chayav*? Neither works. Rabbi Meir clearly holds *chayav* in the *braita*'s case — the *braita* explicitly says one did not remove the pitcher or stand up the camel, emphasizing that the problem is *le-achar nefilah*. And the *chachamim* clearly hold *patur* in the same case. So the *braita* itself compels the conclusion that part of the dispute concerns what happens after the fall. Rabbi Elazar's formulation must therefore be understood as *af bishas nefila* — also at *sha'at nefilah* — meaning they dispute *that* too, in addition to *le-achar nefilah*. Understood this way, Rabbi Elazar agrees with Abaye that there are two debates, and his statement merely adds that the dispute extends even to the moment of falling.

## R. Yochanan Simple

### Yochanan: One Debate

Rabbi Yochanan offers what is presented as the simplest resolution. He says *le-achar nefilah machloket* — the entire dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* concerns what happens after the fall. At *sha'at nefilah*, when the pitcher first breaks or the camel first falls, everyone agrees the owner is an *ones* and is *patur*.

This would be an elegant explanation. The *Gemara* had earlier found it troubling that Rabbi Meir would hold someone *chayav* even if the pitcher dissolved in their hands — a pure *ones*. Under Rabbi Yochanan's reading, that problem evaporates: at the moment of breaking, everyone agrees you are *patur*. The debate is only about what happens afterwards, when you have had the opportunity to remove the hazard but have not done so.

### Yochanan Contradiction

The problem is that Rabbi Yochanan himself says something elsewhere that contradicts this reading. He states regarding a different *Mishna*: *lo teima masnitin Rabbi Meir hi de-amar nitkal poshea* — do not think that that *Mishna* represents Rabbi Meir's view that *nitkal* is *poshea*. This statement from Rabbi Yochanan presupposes that Rabbi Meir's position — that tripping constitutes negligence — is a defined opinion that belongs to him in this dispute. That means Rabbi Yochanan understands the debate of Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* to include the question of *nitkal poshea* at *sha'at nefilah*. One cannot say they only debate *le-achar nefilah* if Rabbi Yochanan himself implies that the question of whether tripping is *poshea* is the characteristic position of Rabbi Meir in this very controversy. Similarly, since Rabbi Meir articulates that position, it is clear the *chachamim* hold the opposite — that at the moment of falling one is *patur* because *nitkal lav poshea*.

### Mafkir Limitation

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by clarifying the scope of Abaye's second debate — *mafkir nezikav*. The rule that *mafkir nezikav* exempts applies only when the original fall was itself an *ones*. In that scenario, there was no fault at the time of creation, and subsequent abandonment of the object means there is neither fault nor ownership — hence *patur*. But if the original fall was *peshi'ah* — genuine negligence — then *mafkir nezikav* provides no escape, and everyone would agree the owner is *chayav*. You cannot neutralize original fault by walking away from the object.

### Unified Debate

This clarification allows Rabbi Yochanan's position to be synthesized into a coherent whole. The debate of Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* is ultimately about one question: is *nitkal poshea* or not? But this single question has ramifications at both temporal stages. At *sha'at nefilah*, it determines whether the owner is immediately liable if someone is damaged in those first moments. At *le-achar nefilah*, it determines the owner's continuing obligation: if *nitkal* was *poshea* (Rabbi Meir), the owner remains *chayav* regardless of whether they are *mafkir* the object, because the original fault is indelible. If *nitkal* was not *poshea* (the *chachamim*), then the owner begins with no fault, and if they subsequently renounce ownership, they have no responsibility to remove the hazard and remain *patur*.

What Abaye presented as two separate debates — one over *sha'at nefilah* and another, conceptually independent, over *mafkir nezikav le-achar nefilah be-peshi'ah* — Rabbi Yochanan collapses into one. The *mafkir* question in this context is not an independent debate about whether ownership is required for *bor* liability; it is simply the natural consequence of the primary debate over *nitkal*. If you were an *ones*, you remain blameless throughout; if you were a *poshea*, you remain liable throughout.

## Ownership Debate

### Mafkir Debate

Having established the positions of Abaye and Rabbi Yochanan on the structure of the *Mishna*'s debate, the *Gemara* now turns to an independent *Amora*'ic dispute. It is stated: Rav Yochanan and Rav Elazar disagree — one says *chayav* and one says *patur* — in a case of *mafkir nezikav*.

### Real Negligence

The *Gemara* immediately clarifies: this dispute does not concern the *nitkal* case discussed throughout the preceding analysis. It is not a question of whether tripping constitutes *peshi'ah*. Rather, this is a case of genuine, unambiguous negligence — one left a real hazard in the *reshus harabbim* — and then renounced ownership of that hazard. The question is pure: does liability for *bor* require ongoing ownership of the hazard?

### Elazar: No Ownership

The *Gemara* concludes that Rav Elazar is the one who holds *chayav* — meaning that one does not need to own the *bor* in order to be liable for it. The textual basis for this is a statement of Rav Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: *shnei devarim einam bi-reshuto shel adam ve-hakatuv asa'an ke-ilu hem bi-reshuto* — there are two things that are not truly in a person's domain, yet the Torah treats them as if they were. These are: a *bor bi-reshut harabbim* and *chametz* from midday of Pesach onwards. Chametz at that point is *assur be-hana'ah* — forbidden to derive benefit from — and thus cannot truly be owned; yet the Torah holds one accountable for its presence. Similarly, a *bor* dug in the public domain is not owned property — one does not own the public domain — yet the Torah imposes liability on the one who dug it. Rav Elazar thus establishes explicitly that *bor* liability does not require ownership; it is grounded in the act of creation and the resulting hazard, not in continuing proprietary interest.

### Dung Case

This position, however, seems to be contradicted by another ruling attributed to Rav Elazar himself. A *Mishna* teaches: *ha-hofech et ha-galal bi-reshut harabbim* — one who turns over dung in the *reshus harabbim*, intending to acquire it for fertilizer, and another person is injured in the process. Rav Elazar comments on this: *lo shanu ela she-nitkavein lizkot bahen* — the owner is *chayav* only if they intended to acquire the dung. If they moved the dung without intending to take possession, they are *patur*. Now, if one moves dung from place A to place B, one has created a new hazard in place B — a *bor* of sorts, positioned where it was not before. If Rav Elazar holds that ownership is irrelevant to *bor* liability, why does he require *mitkavein lizkot* — intent to acquire? Even without acquisition, surely the act of moving the dung created a hazard for which one should be responsible?

### Adda Resolution

Rav Adda bar Ahava resolves this by reconstructing the scenario. The case in question is one where the person moved the dung and then returned it to its original location. They did not leave a new hazard somewhere new; they simply picked up the dung and put it back where they found it. In that case, they did not *dig a new bor* — they merely displaced and replaced an existing hazard. Since they created nothing new, they are *chayav* only if they acquired it — only if it became theirs. But if they returned it to its place and never acquired it, they have done nothing net, and they are *patur*. Rav Elazar's requirement of *mitkavein lizkot* thus applies specifically to this peculiar scenario of displacement and return, not to the general principle.

### Pit Analogy

Ravina offers an analogy to illuminate Rav Adda's resolution. The case of returning dung to its original location is comparable to finding an uncovered pit in the *reshus harabbim*, covering it as a public service, and then uncovering it again. In such a case, one has not created a *bor* — the pit existed before and remained the original digger's *bor* even while covered. When one removes the cover, one has merely restored the status quo; the original *bor* is still the original digger's hazard. One is therefore not liable as the creator of a *bor*, and absent ownership, one is *patur*.

### Zutra Objection

Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, objects to this analogy. The cases are fundamentally different, he argues. *Hasam lo istallek lei ma'aseh rishon* — when one covered the pit, the original *bor* never ceased to exist. It was simply covered; it remained the original person's *bor* throughout. Removing the cover does nothing more than restore what was always there. The first person's act was never erased. But in the dung case: *istallek lei ma'aseh rishon* — when one physically removed the dung from its location, one completely eradicated the original hazard. There was no longer any dung there. When one then replaced it, one created a new hazard — a genuinely new *bor*. The proper analogy, Mar Zutra argues, is not covering and uncovering a pit, but filling a pit with earth and then digging it again. If someone fills in another person's pit and then re-digs it, they have unambiguously created a new *bor* — *istallek lei ma'aseh rishon*, the first person's act is gone — and they are liable for it regardless of ownership. By that logic, moving the dung and replacing it should also create a new *bor* and render the mover liable even without intent to acquire.

### Ashi Solution

Rav Ashi offers the resolution. The *Mishna*'s case of *hofech* — turning over the dung — involves lifting it less than three *tefachim* off the ground. Objects lifted less than three *tefachim* are treated halakhically as still connected to the ground — they have not been fully removed from their place. Therefore, one who *hofech* the dung in this manner has not truly moved it: it never left its original position in the operative legal sense. No new *bor* has been created. Since nothing new was created and the object was never truly relocated, one is *chayav* only if one acquired it. Had one lifted the dung fully — more than three *tefachim* — one would have genuinely moved it, created a new hazard, and been *chayav* regardless of intent to acquire, consistent with Rav Elazar's general position.

### Rava Language

The *Gemara* then asks why Rav Elazar specifically interpreted the *Mishna* as a case of less than three *tefachim*, rather than simply reading it as a case where one *did* fully remove the dung and holding *chayav* even without acquisition. Rava answers: *mah nafka mina hafach, lishneih higbih* — the language of the *Mishna* is the key. The *Mishna* says *hofech* — turning over — not *higbih* — lifting up. The word *hofech* connotes a minimal repositioning, a turning and tilting in place, rather than a full elevation and removal. To Rav Elazar, this language signaled that the dung was not significantly displaced — consistent only with a reading of less than three *tefachim*. The conclusion: *shema mina kol hafach l-mata mi-shloshah hu* — whenever the word *hafach* is used in this context, it implies a lifting of less than three *tefachim*.
