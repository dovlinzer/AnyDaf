# Shabbat 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Glassware Status

### Sand Similarity

The Gemara on this daf does not begin with the eighteen decrees of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel — we will return to those shortly — but rather with a list of earlier *takanot* that was quoted previously. Two of those *takanot* are under discussion: one establishing that glassware is *mekabel tumah*, susceptible to ritual impurity, and the other declaring the land of non-Jews *tamei*. Both were enacted in the earliest period of rabbinic legislation, attributed to Yose ben Yoezer Ish Tzereda and Yose ben Yochanan Ish Yerushalayim. The Gemara now turns its attention to glassware.

The fundamental question is: why did the Sages decide that glassware would become *tamei*? One cannot simply say that the Rabbis applied *tumah* to all vessels universally, since there are vessel categories that remain *tahor* even rabbinically — stone vessels, for instance. So what was the specific logic for glassware? The Gemara answers that glassware was considered similar to earthenware because it, too, is made from sand. The assumption, therefore, was that glass would carry a status analogous to *kli cheres*, the earthenware vessel.

One immediate difficulty, however, arises from this analogy. A defining feature of *kli cheres* is that it cannot be purified in a *mikveh* — the only path to *taharah* for earthenware is breakage. Yet it is well established that glassware can be immersed in a *mikveh*. The Gemara resolves this by treating the *mikveh*-eligible glass as a special and narrow case: a glass vessel that had developed a hole — and thereby ceased to be a *kli*, since a hole of sufficient size removes an earthenware vessel from the category of *kli cheres* — and was subsequently repaired by filling the hole with lead. In that case, because the lead repair conferred on it the character of a metal vessel, it could be immersed in a *mikveh* according to the position of Rabbi Meir. Normal glassware, however, would remain in the earthenware category and could not be immersed.

### Earthenware Model

With this analogy to *kli cheres* established, the Gemara subjects it to scrutiny. The most prominent and practically significant feature of *kli cheres* is not merely the question of *mikveh* immersion, but the distinctive manner in which it becomes *tamei* and transmits *tumah*. Ordinary vessels — wood, metal, bone — become *tamei* when *tumah* physically contacts any part of them. Earthenware, by contrast, becomes *tamei* only when something is within its airspace; external contact, even with the outer surface or base, does not render it *tamei*. Correspondingly, earthenware transmits *tumah* only from its airspace. If one suspends a *sheretz* — a dead rodent that is a primary source of *tumah* — within the airspace of a pottery vessel without it touching the walls, the vessel becomes *tamei*. Conversely, if one suspends food within that airspace, the food becomes *tamei*.

The Gemara raises the obvious objection: if glass is like *kli cheres*, then touching the outside of a glass vessel should have no halachic consequence. Yet we know from the *Mishnah* in Kelim that this is not the case. That *Mishnah* states that *kli cheres* and *kli nether* — natron, a mineral substance comparable in halachic status to pottery, identified as sodium carbonate — share the same *tumah* rules: *metameim u-mitameim u-metameim me'aviran*, they can receive *tumah* and transmit *tumah* through their airspace. The *Mishnah* then adds: *u-mitamei me'achoreihem ve-ein mitamei migabeihem* — they become *tamei* through their *achorayim* but not through their *gav*.

Rashi's interpretation of these two terms repays careful attention. *Gav* refers to the simple back or exterior surface of the vessel. *Achorayim*, by contrast, refers to what Rashi understands as a secondary receptacle — specifically, the concave underside of the base of a vessel, a surface that faces outward but still has something of the character of a receptacle. Think of a *kiddush* cup: the bowl is the primary receptacle on the inside, but the base, when inverted, reveals a hollow space on what is technically the exterior of the vessel. Rashi holds that this *achorayim* zone can receive and transmit *tumah*, though rabbinically rather than biblically. The biblical rule governs only the primary interior receptacle; the *achorayim* is an extension of that principle at the rabbinic level. The plain *gav* — the simple exterior surface — cannot receive *tumah* at all, though there may be some capacity to transmit *tumah* if contact is made there, a point that the Gemara does not address explicitly. Tosafot has a lengthy discussion engaging with and largely defending Rashi's reading before offering an alternative, though for our purposes Rashi's framework suffices.

The *Mishnah* closes with the statement *ve-shi'areihem metaharan* — the vessel is purified only through breakage. This too is the law of *kli cheres*. The Gemara then points out: the *Mishnah* in Kelim specifies that it is only pottery and natron vessels that share this identical *tumah* profile — *kli cheres ve-kli nether hu de-tumatam shavah* — and nothing else. Glass is not mentioned. If glass truly followed the pottery model, one would expect it to be listed alongside pottery. Its omission implies that glass is governed by a different set of *tumah* rules. This undermines the original rationale for treating glass like *kli cheres*.

### Metal Model

The Gemara pivots. It proposes a new rationale: *keivan de-chi nishtabru yesh lahem takanah* — since when glass breaks it can be repaired, it is therefore treated like metal vessels, *shavinhu k'cheli matachot*. When glass cracks or breaks, it can be mended by applying heat — a process analogous to how a craftsman solders or re-forms a broken metal vessel. In antiquity, glassware was expensive, and artisans had techniques to fuse broken glass back together. This capacity for heat-based repair defines it as metal-like rather than pottery-like.

Tosafot raises a subtle objection: the mere fact that glass can be *repaired* through heat would not by itself have prompted the original rabbinic decision to make it *tamei*. Fundamentally, glass does not begin its existence as a metal object. Tosafot therefore proposes a hybridized account: the Rabbis initially observed that glass was sufficiently similar to pottery — being formed from sand through intense heat — to declare it *tamei*. But having made that decision, they then faced the question of which existing halachic category to assign it to. Given the repairability through heat, they placed it in the metal category, even if the original impetus came from its resemblance to pottery.

One student raised the observation that the manufacturing process of glass is itself analogous to that of metal — both involve applying extreme heat to a raw material and fashioning it into shape — whereas pottery, though also fired at high temperatures, is shaped differently. This may in fact be closer to what the Gemara intends in this shift: not merely repairability but the entire mode of production and material constitution. Glass also possesses the fragility of pottery while sharing the fire-formed character of metal, making it in some respects a genuinely hybrid material.

### Glass vs Metal/Wood

Granting that glass is now being categorized as metal-like, the Gemara proceeds to test this against two specific halachot of metal vessels and discovers anomalies. The first anomaly concerns the requirement for a *beit kibul*, a receptacle. The general rules for susceptibility to *tumah* vary across material categories. Earthenware becomes *tamei* only through its airspace and has no need of a formal receptacle in the ordinary sense. Wood and bone vessels require a *beit kibul* — a spoon of wood becomes *tamei* only because it has a receptacle; a simple wooden board or stick does not. Metal vessels, however, are the most inclusive: even a *pashut* metal vessel — one that is perfectly flat and has no receptacle whatsoever, such as a needle — becomes *tamei*. Metal requires no *beit kibul*.

The *Mishnah* cited by the Gemara states: *klei matachot — pshuteihem u-mekableihem tme'im* — metal vessels, whether simple or receptacle-bearing, are all susceptible to *tumah*. The *Mishnah* continues: *nishbiru taharu* — if they break, they become *tahor*. And then: *chazar ve-asa mehem kelim — chazru le-tumatam yeshanah* — if they were broken and then refashioned into new vessels, they revert to their *old* *tumah*. That is, if a metal vessel was *tamei*, broken (and thereby became *tahor*), and then remade, it does not begin its renewed existence as a fresh vessel susceptible to *tumah* only going forward — it is immediately and retroactively *tamei* again with its original *tumah*. This is a special rabbinic rule applied to metal vessels.

Now contrast the *Mishnah* governing glassware, which groups it with *klei etz*, *klei or*, and *klei etzem* — wood, leather, and bone. That *Mishnah* states: *pshuteihem tehorin* — vessels in these categories that are flat and have no receptacle are *tahor* and cannot receive *tumah*. Only those with a receptacle become *tamei*, and only when any part of them is physically contacted. Furthermore: *nishbiru taharu* — they become *tahor* upon breakage — but *chazar ve-asa mehem kelim, mekablin tumah mikan u-le'aba* — if refashioned, they can receive *tumah* only going forward, not retroactively. The old *tumah* does not return.

The Gemara thus identifies two anomalies in the attempt to categorize glass as metal. First, glass requires a *beit kibul*, whereas metal does not. Second, the old *tumah* does not revert when broken glass is remade, whereas for metal it does. The wood-and-bone category would actually fit glass perfectly — it accounts for both anomalies — but there is no logical basis to place glass in that category. So the Gemara is left attempting to explain why glass, even if metal-like, diverges from the metal template in these two specific respects.

### Anomalies Solution

The Gemara addresses each anomaly in turn. On the question of why the old *tumah* does not return: *tumas klei zechuchis derabanan* — the entire *tumah* of glassware is rabbinic. And *tuma yeshanah derabanan* — the rule that old *tumah* reverts upon remaking is itself rabbinic. Biblically, once *tumah* is gone, it is gone; a remade vessel can contract *tumah* anew but does not restore prior *tumah*. When did the Rabbis invoke the rule that old *tumah* returns? Only when dealing with a vessel whose *tumah* is biblical in nature. When the vessel's *tumah* is itself only rabbinic, the Rabbis did not apply this additional rabbinic stringency — *lo achisu leh rabbanan*. Since glassware is only *derabanan*, its old *tumah* does not revert.

On the second anomaly — why glass requires a *beit kibul* even though metal does not: here the Gemara gives a different kind of answer. *Avdi rabbanan heikera* — the Rabbis deliberately made a distinguishing marker. Specifically: *ki heichi d'lo lishrifu aleih terumah ve-kodshim* — so that one would not come to burn *terumah* and *kodshim* that came in contact with glassware. The logic is elegant. If glass followed all the rules of metal precisely — including becoming *tamei* without a *beit kibul* — then a scholar thoroughly versed in the laws of *tumah va-taharah* would have no way of knowing, from the behavior of glass alone, that its *tumah* status was rabbinic rather than biblical. By ensuring that glass deviates from metal in this one respect, the Rabbis created a built-in signal: anyone who knows the laws will notice that glass requires a *beit kibul* while metal does not, and will thereby be reminded that the *tumah* of glass is rabbinic. Armed with that awareness, one will refrain from treating it with biblical stringency and will not burn *terumah* that comes into contact with it.

This leads to a broader observation. Earlier in the tractate we discussed cases where the Sages were so strict about a rabbinic *tumah* that they required burning *terumah* that contacted it — for example, *beit ha-pras*, or *yadayim* in their status as a *sheini*. In the case of glassware, they went in precisely the opposite direction, engineering the halachah to prevent such burning. The *rishonim* acknowledge that there is no uniform rule governing which rabbinic *tumot* are treated with full biblical stringency and which are not — it varies by case.

### Transparency Theory

Rav Ashi offers a fundamentally different approach at the top of 16b. He maintains: *l'olam l'cheli cheres damu* — glass is and remains analogous to pottery, not to metal. The objection that originally seemed fatal — if it is like pottery, why should touching the outside of a glass vessel render it *tamei*? — is answered by a celebrated principle: *hoil ve-tocho k'varo*, since its inside is like its outside.

The phrase *tocho k'varo* resonates with a famous earlier discussion in the tractate concerning Rabban Gamliel and the standards for admission to the *beit ha-midrash* — whether a student's inner character must match his outward presentation. Here the phrase is given a striking literal application. Glass is, by its physical nature, transparent. There is no visual distinction between inside and outside; what is on the inside can be seen from the outside, and vice versa. Because glass is inherently transparent, the Rabbis did not maintain the usual distinction between the interior and exterior of the vessel. Touching the outside is, in a sense, like touching the inside, since through the glass wall one sees the inside when viewing the outside. The ordinary rule of *kli cheres* — that only the interior airspace matters — is therefore extended to encompass the exterior as well, not because of any categorical shift but because the physical nature of glass collapses the inside-outside distinction.

Under Rav Ashi's account, the other halachic features of glass align naturally with the pottery model. The *mikveh* case is explained by the same special circumstance invoked earlier — a hole filled with lead. The fact that old *tumah* does not revert upon remaking presents no problem, since that is simply not a feature of the pottery category. And the requirement for a *beit kibul* similarly presents no difficulty. On Rav Ashi's reading, the anomalies that required explanation under the metal theory dissolve. The sole departure from standard *kli cheres* rules is the treatment of exterior contact as equivalent to interior contact, and that is fully accounted for by the physical transparency of glass itself.

The upshot is that the Gemara does not definitively resolve whether glass is fundamentally like metal (with two anomalies explained away) or fundamentally like pottery (with one anomaly explained by transparency). Both positions remain on the table.

### Kashering Glass

This sustained Talmudic discussion of glassware's halachic category carries a direct and well-known application to the laws of *kashrus*: the question of whether glassware can be *kashered*. The Gemara does not address *kashering* directly in this context, but some *poskim* have sought to derive guidance from the *tumah* categorization explored here. If glass is like pottery, then just as pottery cannot be *kashered* — because absorbed material cannot be expelled — glass similarly cannot be *kashered*. If glass is like metal, then just as metal can be *kashered*, so can glass.

Many *poskim* push back sharply on this analogical reasoning, arguing that it conflates two entirely distinct frameworks. The *tumah* categorization is a formal halachic taxonomy; the question of *kashering* is a physical one, turning on whether a material absorbs and releases food substances. These are not the same inquiry, and the formal category assigned for purposes of *tumah* does not necessarily answer the empirical question of absorption. From this perspective, glass should be evaluated on its own physical merits — and in fact, common glass does not absorb food substances at all. A student pointed out that glass manufacturers confirm this: ordinary glassware is non-porous and non-absorbent, and specialty industrial glass that does absorb is rare and prohibitively expensive.

Some *poskim* go so far as to hold that glassware does not require *kashering* at all between *milchig* and *fleishig* use, precisely because it absorbs nothing. It was reportedly common in earlier generations for families to maintain a single set of glass dishes used interchangeably, since glass was understood not to absorb taste. In more recent generations, increased wealth and increased stringency have both contributed to a shift in practice, and most contemporary *poskim* are more conservative — particularly with respect to Pesach, where the threshold of stringency is generally higher. The range of opinions among *poskim* today remains real, however, and in pressing circumstances different answers may be given.

The broader methodological point is worth noting: when the Gemara does not address a practical question directly, decisors look for parallel cases in adjacent halachic domains. Sometimes those parallels are genuinely illuminating; sometimes they are merely formal, and applying them uncritically leads to distorted conclusions. The glassware debate is a case study in that tension.

## Shimon ben Shatach

### Ketubah Takana

The Gemara now turns to the early *takanot* of Shimon ben Shatach, who predated the era of Shammai and Hillel. Two *takanot* are attributed to him. The first — that women receive a *kesubah* — is mentioned but not analyzed at length here; nonetheless, the essential background is worth laying out.

The broader question of whether *kesubah* is biblical or rabbinic in origin is debated in tractate Kesubos. A potential biblical source is found in the verse dealing with the seduction of a virgin, which states that the seducer must pay *kesef yishkol ke-mohar ha-besulos* — money equivalent to the *mohar ha-besulos*, the bride price for virgins, fixed elsewhere in the Torah at fifty *shekel*, equivalent to 200 *zuz*, the standard amount of a *kesubah*. The difficulty is that the Torah's *mohar* was paid upfront to the father, functioning either as a bride price or, in another reading, as funds held in escrow for the benefit of the wife should the marriage end. The rabbinic *kesubah*, by contrast, is paid at the time of dissolution of the marriage. Whether the Torah's framework is the true source of the rabbinic institution is therefore genuinely uncertain.

What is clear is that the original practice was to pay the *kesubah* sum upfront at the time of marriage, either to the father or held by the bride directly, as a kind of prepaid alimony or substitute for inheritance — since the wife would not inherit from her husband, but these funds would support her after his death. The *Tosefta* records the problem that arose: a husband who wished to divorce his wife could do so callously, without hesitation, because the *kesubah* had already been paid. There was no financial deterrent. The Rabbis found this unacceptable — *shelo yihei kal b'einav le-garshah*, divorce should not be a trivially easy step. Shimon ben Shatach therefore instituted that the *kesubah* would no longer be paid upfront but would remain as an obligation payable only upon dissolution of the marriage. With his assets potentially at stake, a husband would think carefully before divorcing his wife. It is this specific innovation — the deferred payment — that is attributed to Shimon ben Shatach, not the institution of *kesubah* itself.

### Metal Recontamination

The Gemara pivots immediately to Shimon ben Shatach's second *takana*, which is far more directly relevant to the *tumah* discussion: *ve-gazar tuma al klei matachot* — he decreed *tumah* upon metal vessels. At first this seems entirely superfluous: metal vessels are already *tamei* biblically. The Torah itself provides the source in the passage dealing with the vessels seized from Midian, which commands that any vessel of gold, silver, iron, bronze, or other metals that comes in contact with *tumah* must be purified — either through *kashering* in fire or, for *tumah* purposes, through immersion, together with sprinkling of the waters of the *parah adumah*. What, then, did Shimon ben Shatach add?

The Gemara answers: *lo nitzrecha ela le-tuma yeshanah* — his *takana* concerned the rule of *tuma yeshanah*, the restoration of old *tumah* upon remaking. As we established when analyzing the metal vessel *Mishnah*, this rule — that a broken and remade metal vessel regains its prior *tumah* — is itself rabbinic. It was Shimon ben Shatach who instituted it. Why?

### Sheltzion Story

The Gemara provides the historical background through a narrative: *ma'aseh be-Sheltzion ha-malka* — there is a story about Queen Sheltzion, identified with Shlomtzion, a Hasmonean queen. She made a great feast — a wedding banquet — for her son. During the feast, someone died, and as a result all of the vessels under the same roof became *tamei*, since *tumas meis* — the impurity transmitted by a corpse — extends to everything within the same enclosure. All her vessels were now *tamei*.

For those operating in a world where eating *chullin be-tahara* — ordinary food in a state of purity — was the norm, this was a genuine crisis. The standard process for purifying vessels contaminated by *tumas meis* involves sprinkling the waters of the *parah adumah*, and this process spans a week. There was no time. So she broke all the vessels, gave them to the smelter, who melted and remade them, and produced new vessels. Breaking renders a metal vessel *tahor*; once remade, it would be a fresh vessel capable of contracting *tumah* only going forward. The feast could proceed.

The Sages responded: *yachzeru le-tumasam ha-yeshanah* — the vessels revert to their original *tumah*. They enacted the rule of *tuma yeshanah* that very day, effectively closing the workaround that had been exploited.

### Parah Adumah Logic

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama* — what was the reasoning? *Mishum geder mei chatat nahagu bah* — because of the concern of undermining the institution of the waters of the *parah adumah*. If breaking and remaking a metal vessel were a straightforward path to *taharah* from *tumas meis*, no one would ever bother with the elaborate and arduous process of *parah adumah* purification for vessels. One could always simply melt them down and recast them. The Rabbis feared this would corrode the entire institution.

The Gemara then raises an internal complication: this reasoning works well according to the view that the rule of *tuma yeshanah* applies only to *tumas meis* — since *parah adumah* waters are specifically required for *tumas meis* purification, making the *tuma yeshanah* rule a targeted deterrent. But according to the view that *tuma yeshanah* applies to all categories of *tumah*, not only *tumas meis*, the *parah adumah* rationale cannot be the whole story.

### Abaye & Rava

For this broader application, Abaye and Rava each supply an alternative rationale. Abaye says: *gezeirah shema lo yishvirenu bikhdei taharaso* — the concern is that a person executing this workaround will not break the vessel sufficiently to achieve valid *taharah*. Since remelting and remaking is work, one will naturally want to make the smallest possible break — a hairline fracture rather than genuine breakage — which will not actually remove the vessel's *kli* status and therefore will not actually produce *taharah*. Forbidding the workaround entirely prevents this risk.

Rava says: *gezeirah shema yomru tevilah bas yoma oltah lah* — the concern is what observers will conclude. If they see that a vessel was *tamei* earlier in the day and is now being used in the evening of the same day, they will assume it was immersed in a *mikveh*. And if they believe that, they will draw the erroneous conclusion that immersion in a *mikveh* confers *taharah* effective immediately — the same day — rather than only at nightfall, which is the actual rule.

The Gemara asks what the practical difference (*nafka mina*) is between Abaye and Rava. It answers: *ikka beinaihu ditzraffinhu martzei* — the case where the vessel was broken into many small pieces. According to Rashi, if the concern is that one will not break it sufficiently (Abaye), then shattering it into many small fragments fully addresses that concern, and in such a case one might not apply the *gezeirah*. According to Rava, however, whose concern is what observers will think, even if the vessel is broken into many pieces the observers will still see it used the same day and draw the wrong conclusion — though Rashi also suggests an inverse reading: a vessel broken into many fragments and visibly repaired will display all its solder lines, making it obvious that it was remade, so observers will not mistake it for a vessel that was merely immersed.

Rashi notes with characteristic candor that he cannot identify the source of the debate between the view that *tuma yeshanah* applies only to *tumas meis* and the view that it applies to all *tumot*. Tosafot, for its part, attempts to locate it in the *Tosefta* of Kelim or *Masechet Kelim*, noting a position attributed there to Rabban Gamliel. The Gilyon HaShas records, in the name of Rabbi Akiva Eiger, every place in Shas where Rashi similarly acknowledges that he cannot identify the source of a Talmudic reference — a significant set of admissions that itself reveals something about how the tradition understands the limits of available sources.

## Eighteen Decrees

### Return to 18

The Gemara signals a return to the primary subject of the *yud-ches davar* — the eighteen things decreed by Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. The Gemara has been engaged with earlier *takanot* — the glassware decree, the land of non-Jews decree, the *takanot* of Shimon ben Shatach — all of which were relevant background but were not themselves among the eighteen. Now the Gemara reorients and asks: *ve-ha'idach mai hi* — what are the remaining items among the eighteen? We have already counted, from earlier in the discussion, approximately eight or nine: becoming a *sheini* through eating *ochel rishon* or *ochel sheini*, through drinking *mashkin tme'im*, through immersing one's head and greater part in drawn water (*rosho ve-rubo be-mayim shu'uvim*); hands always having the status of a *sheini*; and food and vessels that touch *mashkin* causing the *mashkin* to become a *rishon* and the food and vessels a *sheini*. The Gemara now sets out to identify the remaining items necessary to reach eighteen.

### Drainpipe Vessels

The Gemara cites a *Mishnah*: *ha-maniach keilim tachat ha-tzinor le-kabel bahen mei geshamim* — one who places vessels beneath a drainpipe in order to collect rainwater. This requires some background. The relevant halachic issue here is not *tumah* susceptibility but the laws of *mikveh*: specifically, the category of *mayim she'uvim*, drawn water, which can invalidate a *mikveh* if added before the *mikveh* contains its requisite forty *se'ah* of valid water. The core rule is that water becomes "drawn" — halachically equivalent to water poured from a bucket — if it is collected within a vessel with the owner's intent. Physical drawing is not required; it suffices that one *wanted* the water to be in the vessel, and that intent is itself treated as if one drew it.

The case of the *Mishnah* is: rainwater falls into a vessel placed under a drainpipe. No one physically carried the water; it fell in by itself. If that water then enters a *mikveh*, is it *mayim she'uvim*? The answer depends entirely on whether the owner *wanted* the water to be in the vessel when it fell.

The *Mishnah* specifies that this rule applies equally to large and small vessels. Tosafot explains the significance of mentioning large ones: a vessel so large that it cannot be lifted when full — approximately the size of a *mikveh* itself, forty *se'ah* — is not ordinarily considered a vessel for purposes of *kabbelas tumah*. One might therefore have thought that such a large vessel is not truly a *kli* and thus cannot render the water "drawn." The *Mishnah* teaches otherwise: even this oversized vessel, though it cannot receive *tumah*, is still legally a *kli* sufficient to define water collected within it as drawn. This is a significant point about how the category of *kli* operates in different legal contexts — a vessel can be a *kli* for one purpose without being a *kli* for another.

Similarly, the *Mishnah* includes vessels of stone (*klei avanim*), vessels of unformed earth (*klei adamah* — earthenware not fired in a kiln), and vessels of dried dung (*klei glalim*). All of these lack the capacity to receive *tumah*, yet all are still legally vessels for purposes of the drawn-water rule. The *chiddush* is that *kabel tumah* and *kli* status are not identical; the absence of one does not entail the absence of the other.

### Shammai Position

The *Mishnah* then presents the dispute: *achad ha-maniach ve-achad ha-shochei'ach* — whether the vessels were placed there intentionally or were forgotten there — *posalin et ha-mikveh, divrei Beit Shammai*. Beit Shammai holds that in both cases the rainwater collected is treated as drawn water. Even if one had originally placed a vessel under the drainpipe for some other purpose and then simply forgotten about it — so that when the rain came and filled it there was no active intent at that moment — Beit Shammai considers the water drawn. The prior placement under the drainpipe, even if no longer present in one's mind, is sufficient to define the water as *she'uvim*.

### Hillel Position

Beit Hillel disagrees: *u-Veit Hillel metaharin be-shocheach* — Beit Hillel considers the forgotten vessel's water *tahor* and not drawn. If one did not intend for the water to be collected when it actually fell — if one's mind was elsewhere and one had genuinely forgotten the vessel — then there is no operative intent, and the water cannot be treated as if one had drawn it. Intent at the moment of collection, or at least a sufficiently active and ongoing intent, is required.

The *Mishnah* continues: *amar Rabbi Meir, nimnu ve-rabu Beit Shammai al Beit Hillel* — they took a vote, and Beit Shammai outnumbered Beit Hillel. This, says Rabbi Meir, is what happened on the day of the eighteen decrees. This drainpipe case is therefore one of the eighteen things on which Beit Shammai's position prevailed. Rabbi Yosi, however, disputes this: *adayin machloket bimkomah omedet* — the debate remains unresolved; Beit Hillel was not outvoted. According to Rabbi Yosi, this case cannot be counted among the eighteen. The Gemara will address the implications of this disagreement for the count of eighteen below.

### Intent Continuity

The *Mishnah* also records the limits of the dispute with a refinement attributed to *d'vei Rav*: *ha-kol modin she-ke-she-nichah bi-sha'at kishur avim teme'im* — everyone agrees that if one placed the vessels under the drainpipe when the sky was overcast and rain was imminent, the water is *tamei* (i.e., treated as drawn) even if one subsequently forgot about the vessels. At the moment of placement, rain was so clearly impending that the intent to collect was already fully formed. Conversely: *bi-sha'at pizur avim* — if one placed the vessels when the sky was clear and there was no prospect of rain, everyone agrees the water is *tahor*, since whatever general intention may have existed, it was not active at any relevant time.

The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel applies to an intermediate case: one placed the vessels under the drainpipe when it was overcast, then the clouds dispersed (*ve-nitpazru*), and then the clouds gathered again (*ve-chazru ve-nitkashru*) and it rained. The question is whether the continuity of intent was broken by the interval in which the clouds separated. *Mar savar batalah machashavto* — one view holds that the dispersal of the clouds constituted a break in the process, effectively nullifying the original intent; when the rain finally fell, it did not fall within the framework of the owner's active intention, and so the water is not drawn. *U-mar savar lo batalah machashavto* — the opposing view holds that the intent was continuous: the initial placement during overcast conditions established an intent that was not voided by a temporary clearing of the sky, and when the rain eventually fell, it was received within that unbroken framework of intent. This is the core of the Shammai-Hillel debate in the intermediate case, and both sides agree that the operative question is not merely whether intent existed at the moment of placement, but whether the intent remained operative and continuous through to the moment of actual collection.

### Kutim Niddah

Since Rabbi Yosi holds that the drainpipe dispute was never resolved by a vote and therefore cannot be counted among the eighteen, the Gemara needs to identify another item to bring the total to eighteen. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak supplies it: *af bnot kutim niddot me-arisatan bo ba-yom gazru* — on that very day they also decreed that the daughters of the *Kutim* — the Samaritans — are to be considered *niddah* from the moment of birth.

The background is as follows. The *Kutim* were halachically considered *geirei emet*, genuine converts, and were therefore treated in many respects as Jews. However, their interpretation of the laws of *niddah* diverged from the rabbinic understanding. The Rabbis held that a woman can attain the status of *niddah* even as a newborn infant — any blood, regardless of the girl's age, can in principle generate *niddah* status. The *Kutim* did not accept this interpretation, and held that *niddah* begins only upon physical maturity. Because of this divergence in practice, there was a concern that *Kutim* women might be regarded as *tahor* when they were in fact halachically *tamei*. The Rabbis therefore enacted a sweeping decree: all daughters of the *Kutim*, from the moment of birth, are treated as *niddot*. This eliminated the ambiguity entirely.

This decree, enacted on the same day as the other eighteen enactments, gives us the number we need to complete the count of eighteen according to Rabbi Yosi's position — substituting for the drainpipe case which Rabbi Yosi excludes. The Gemara signals that the discussion of the eighteen decrees will continue on the following daf, where, among the remaining items, the law of *batzir* — the harvesting of grapes — will be addressed. That case concerns the rule that for food to become susceptible to *tumah*, it must be moistened by one of the seven liquids (*zayin mashkin*), and only if that moisture came *le-ratzon*, with the owner's approval. When grapes are harvested and a small amount of juice is naturally released in the process, Hillel held that this is not *le-ratzon* since the harvester has no interest in that juice; Shammai held that it is. A vote was taken, Shammai prevailed, and this too became one of the eighteen decrees.
