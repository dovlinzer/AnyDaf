# Shevuot 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Contradictory Witnesses

### Two Groups Contradict

The daf opens with an *itmar* — a reported Amoraic dispute — concerning two groups of witnesses whose testimonies directly contradict one another. Reuven and Shimon testify that at a particular time and place, Reuven borrowed a hundred dollars from Shimon; Levi and Yehudah testify that at that same time and place, no such loan occurred. These are not *edim zomemim* — the second pair is not claiming the first pair was elsewhere — they are simply offering contradictory accounts. One group is necessarily lying, but we cannot determine which.

### Rav Huna: Kosher Status

Rav Huna rules: *zo ba bifnei atzmah me'idah vezo ba bifnei atzmah me'idah* — each group comes on its own and testifies on its own. Since we cannot identify which group is invalid, each retains its *chazakot kashrus*, its presumption of being credible witnesses. Consequently, when each group appears separately before separate courts in separate cases, each case is adjudicated on its own terms and the witnesses are accepted. The one thing that cannot be permitted is for members of the two groups to combine into a single set of witnesses for one case, since we know with certainty that one of those combined witnesses is invalid. But appearing independently, in separate proceedings, each group remains fully kosher.

### Rav Chisda: Both Invalid

Rav Chisda disagrees sharply: *bahadi sahadi shikri lama li* — why should I have anything to do with lying witnesses? Since one group is definitively fabricating testimony, and since I cannot determine which, I will not accept either group in any future proceeding. Both groups are disqualified from this point forward.

### Two Lenders/Borrowers

The Gemara now works through several applications of this dispute. The first is the case of *shnei malvin u'shnei lovin u'shnei shtarot* — two lenders, two borrowers, two documents. The first lender's document is signed by Reuven and Shimon; the second lender's document is signed by Levi and Yehudah. Each case comes before the court separately. The Gemara notes that this is simply *haynu plugta* — the straightforward playing out of the dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Chisda. According to Rav Huna, each document is valid and each case is adjudicated independently; according to Rav Chisda, neither set of witnesses is accepted.

### Same Parties, Two Docs

The next case involves *malveh v'loveh u'shnei shtarot* — one lender, one borrower, but two separate documents, say one for a hundred dollars and one for two hundred. Here even Rav Huna introduces a complication. If the lender were wise, he would either bring the documents on separate days or simply drop the lower-value document and press only the larger claim. But if he presents both documents simultaneously, the court is now confronted with two testimonies about the same parties — and one of those testimonies is grounded in false witnesses. Since we cannot determine which document is valid, we can only award what both testimonies agree upon at minimum: the lower amount. The lender collects the hundred dollars, while the additional hundred remains unresolved. The Gemara further notes that complications arise with respect to lien priority — one must take the overlap of both testimonies, including the later lien date.

### Two Lenders, One Borrower

The case of *shnei malvin veloveh echad* — two lenders, one borrower, two documents — raises a different question. Can each lender come on a separate day, have his case adjudicated independently, and collect from the same borrower, even though the borrower is effectively paying out twice based on two contradictory sets of witnesses? The Gemara notes that this is *haynu masnisin* — this is effectively the same structure as the *mishnah* concerning the storekeeper and the workers, where the *ba'al habayis* pays out to both parties even though one of the oaths must be false. According to the *chachamim* of that *mishnah*, each party's claim is adjudicated independently and the owner pays twice; according to Ben Nanas, who recoils from facilitating a false oath, such an arrangement may not be permitted. The analogy holds precisely.

The final permutation — *bei lovin umalveh echad*, two borrowers, one lender, two documents — is left unresolved. The question is whether, when the single lender presents both documents against two different borrowers, we treat these as genuinely independent cases each with their own valid witnesses, or whether the lender, who pools the proceeds, cannot be permitted to collect on the strength of two mutually contradictory sets of testimony. The Gemara concludes: *teiku*.

### Moon Testimony Case

The Gemara raises a challenge from the laws of testimony about the new moon. A *beraita* states: if one witness says the moon was two *marda'ot* above the horizon and another says it was three, their testimonies are combined — we assume a small discrepancy and accept the testimony jointly. But if one says three and one says five, *eidusan betelah* — the testimony is void. Nevertheless, *umistarfim le'eidus acheres* — each witness may join with another for a different testimony.

The question is pressed: does *eidus acheres* mean a separate monetary case? If so, this is a strong support for Rav Huna — the witnesses remain individually valid for other proceedings. Rava resolves the difficulty: *eidus acheres* does not mean a different kind of case entirely. It means that within the very same dispute about the new moon, if Reuven said three and Shimon said five, and then Levi also comes and says three, the side of three now has two witnesses against one — *de-hava lei trei ke-negad chad* — and the side supported by two witnesses prevails. The witnesses are not being validated for external monetary cases; rather, within the *Rosh Chodesh* proceeding itself, a third witness can tip the balance. Rav Chisda's position — that contradicting witnesses cannot testify elsewhere — therefore remains intact.

## Possession & Oaths

### Storekeeper/Worker

The Gemara turns to a related *itmar* emerging from the *mishnah's* storekeeper-and-worker dispute. In the classic case of the *mishnah*, the storekeeper claims he distributed food to the workers, and the workers deny receiving it; both take oaths and both are paid. But the Gemara now considers a different formulation: the storekeeper has already handed over a crate of produce and the customer claims to have paid, while the storekeeper insists payment was not received. Under ordinary rules, since the customer is *kofer hakol* — denying the entire claim — he would normally go free without an oath. However, this is a context in which the Rabbis instituted a new *shevuah* — the customer must swear that he paid.

### R. Yehuda: No Oath

Rabbi Yehudah disagrees with the Rabbis. *Eimatai* — when does this *shevuah* requirement apply? Only while the produce is still sitting at the counter, contested by both parties. But if the customer has already loaded the produce into his sack and walked away — *hifshilan b'kupato l'achorav* — then the ordinary principle applies: *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*, the burden of proof rests on the claimant, and the customer need not take any oath at all.

### Money Changer Case

A parallel case involves the money changer. If a customer asks for change for a twenty, receives the singles, and the money changer then claims the twenty was never tendered, the Rabbis would require the customer to take an oath. Rabbi Yehudah again disagrees: if the customer already holds the change, he is obviously believed and requires no oath.

### Fruit Deterioration

The Gemara asks why both cases are necessary — could one not be derived from the other? The answer is *tzricha*: each case has its own direction of novel ruling. In the case of produce, one might have thought the Rabbis agree with Rabbi Yehudah because fruit deteriorates — *pirei avidi d'mirkavi* — and merchants therefore sometimes allow produce to leave before payment is confirmed, in order to prevent disputes over bruises or spoilage. On that assumption, one might think the Rabbis would agree that once the produce is in hand, no oath is needed. Conversely, in the case of money, one might think that since coins do not rot — *ma'ot lo mirkevi* — Rabbi Yehudah himself would agree that change in hand does not suffice to exempt the customer. Since each case presents a reason to think the respective position would concede, both must be stated explicitly.

## Heirs & Non-Transmissibl…

### Heirs Collecting Basics

The *mishnah* includes among its list of *shevuot de-rabbanan* the case of *yetomim* — orphans collecting a debt. The Gemara asks: from whom are these orphans collecting? If they are collecting from the borrower himself, there is a difficulty: when their father came with a *shtar* to collect, he needed no oath — a *shtar* is full proof. Why should the heirs require an oath that their father did not?

The Gemara resolves the question by reading the *mishnah* differently: *yetomim min ha-yetomim* — heirs collecting from heirs. The case is where Mordechai is the *malveh* and Levi is the *loveh*. When Mordechai comes with a *shtar* to collect from Levi's children, the *halacha* requires him to swear that the debt has not been paid — this is the standard rule protecting orphaned debtors. So when Mordechai's own children then come to collect from Levi's children, the symmetry is appropriate: just as Mordechai took the oath "I know with certainty this debt has not been paid," his children take the *shvuat yorshin* — "to the best of our knowledge this debt has not been paid" — and collect.

### Rav & Shmuel's Rule

Rav and Shmuel qualify this rule with a critical restriction: *lo shanu ela she-met malveh be-chayei loveh* — this applies only when Mordechai died while Levi was still alive. In that scenario, Mordechai himself had never yet become obligated in any oath to Levi's children, because Levi was still living. The obligation to swear to the *loveh's* heirs arises only upon the *loveh's* death. So if Mordechai died first and his children are now going to collect from Levi's children after Levi himself subsequently dies, the children can take their *shvuat yorshin* freely.

But if Levi died first — *met loveh be-chayei malveh* — then from the moment of Levi's death, Mordechai became obligated to take an oath to Levi's children. And then Mordechai dies. *Kevar nitchayav malveh livnei loveh shvua* — the *malveh* was already obligated in a *shevuah* to the *loveh's* children. *Ve-ein adam morish shvua le-vanav* — a person cannot bequeath that oath to his sons.

### 'Cannot Bequeath Oath'

The principle *ein adam morish shvua le-vanav* admits of two readings. The simpler reading is that the *halacha* had already determined that this money could only be accessed by means of a specific oath — an oath that the *malveh* personally knows the debt has not been paid — and since the children can only swear "to the best of our knowledge," they cannot fulfill what the court had already established as the necessary condition for collection. They are therefore stuck.

A more literal reading of the phrase would be that the need for an oath somehow severs the *malveh's* connection to the money itself, rendering it too remote for inheritance. If the money can only be accessed through a sworn declaration of personal knowledge, and that declaration belongs to the father alone, it is as though the asset had moved out of the father's estate before death. This reading is harder to sustain theoretically — it is not obvious why an oath requirement should be more disqualifying than the need for documentary evidence — but the Gemara's later discussion makes it relevant.

The practical consequence, as one questioner observes, is deeply troubling for commercial society. A creditor who goes to the trouble of drawing up a proper *shtar* finds that a quirk of mortality — the sequence in which parties die — can render that document uncollectable. Much of the rest of the *daf* is devoted to challenging Rav and Shmuel's ruling.

### Rav Elazar Disagrees

When the question was sent to Rav Elazar — whether, in the case where the *loveh* died first, the *malveh's* heirs could take a *shvuat yorshin* and collect — he replied: *yorshin nishba'in shvuat yorshin ve-notlin*. The heirs swear the heir's oath and collect. Even though their father was obligated in a specific *shevuah* they cannot replicate, society must continue to function, and therefore we permit the heirs to take their own variation of the oath and receive payment. Rav Elazar thus explicitly rejects the position of Rav and Shmuel.

### Rav Ami's Distinction

When the question was subsequently reviewed by Rav Ami, he acknowledged the ruling had not changed, but offered one nuance worth noting. *Amad be-din u-mes* — if Mordechai had actually stood before the court and the court had ruled, or was in the process of ruling, that he must take a specific oath, and then Mordechai died, that constitutes a formal adjudication: *kevar nitchayav malveh livnei loveh shvua* — the court's ruling concretizes the obligation. In such a case, Rav Ami would agree that the children cannot take the place of that oath and are therefore out. But where no formal proceeding had yet reached that determination, *yorshin nishba'in shvuat yorshin ve-notlin* — the heirs take their oath and collect.

### Rav Nachman Unsure

Rav Nachman challenges Rav Ami's distinction sharply. *Atu beis dina kamchaiv lishvua?* — does the court create the obligation of the oath, or merely recognize an obligation that already existed? From the moment Levi died, the *halacha* determined that Mordechai could only collect by swearing a personal oath to Levi's children. The court proceeding neither creates nor eliminates that obligation — it merely formalizes it. Therefore one cannot meaningfully distinguish between *amad be-din* and *lo amad be-din*; in both cases the obligation existed from the moment of the *loveh's* death. The question is binary: either we rule like Rav and Shmuel — in which case the children are excluded regardless of whether a formal proceeding occurred — or we do not, in which case the children may take their *shvuat yorshin* even after the court has already weighed in.

*Ai isa le-Rav u-Shmuel isa, ve-i lessa lessa* — if we hold like Rav and Shmuel, we hold like them; if we do not, we do not. Do not attempt a hybrid position. Rav Nachman thus confesses uncertainty about whether to rule like Rav and Shmuel at all, and declines to force an artificial middle ground.

The Gemara notes an apparent tension with Rav Nachman's own ruling in the case where one party was *chashud al ha-shevuah* and could not take the obligatory *shvuat modeh be-miktzat*. In that case Rav Nachman ruled like Rav Yosef — the obligation is split between the parties. Should not the same logic apply here, yielding a compromise rather than an all-or-nothing ruling? The Gemara resolves this: Rav Nachman ruled that way according to his own position, but his uncertainty here concerns the approach of Rabbi Meir, who uses the phrase *chazra shevuah li-mekomah* — the oath returns to its place. Under Rabbi Meir's framework, it is genuinely unclear whether "returning to its place" means the claimant loses entirely or whether the heirs may substitute their own oath and collect. It is specifically with respect to that unresolved ambiguity in Rabbi Meir's position that Rav Nachman says he does not know which way to rule.

## Proofs Against Rav & Shm…

### Widow/Ketubah Case

Rav Oshaya raises a series of challenges against Rav and Shmuel from cases where it appears unambiguously that heirs collect through an oath even when the *loveh* died first. The first: *meisah, yorsheha mashbi'in es ketuvasah ad esrim ve-chamesh shanim* — if a widow died without having collected her *ketubah*, her heirs may press that claim for up to twenty-five years. But to collect the *ketubah* from the husband's estate, the widow would have had to take a *shevuah* to the husband's heirs — the husband having died first. Now the widow dies, and her heirs are collecting. This is precisely the scenario Rav and Shmuel would prohibit: the *malveh* (the widow) was obligated in an oath to the heirs of the *loveh* (the husband's estate), and now her children seek to collect. The *beraita* implies they succeed. The Gemara deflects: *she-keshe-nishba'ah u-meisah* — the case must be one where the widow had already taken the required oath and only then died, so the oath obligation is already discharged before her heirs enter the picture. This reading, as the Gemara itself implies, is quite strained.

### Second Wife Scenario

A second challenge: a man married his first wife, she died, he married a second wife, and then he died. The second wife — and her heirs — have priority over the heirs of the first wife in collecting from the estate. The second wife's heirs are therefore pressing her *ketubah* claim against the husband's estate. Since the husband died first, the second wife was obligated in a *shevuah* to his heirs; she then died; her heirs now collect. Once again the Gemara answers: *she-keshe-nishba'ah u-meisah* — we are discussing a case where the second wife had already taken her oath before dying, leaving her heirs only the ministerial act of collection.

### Waived Oath Exception

A third and more elaborate challenge concerns a man who explicitly waived his right to ever demand an oath from his wife in connection with the *ketubah*. Such a waiver binds him personally: if she later claims partial payment was made and would ordinarily need to take a *shevuah*, he cannot compel it. However, the *mishnah* states: *aval yorshaiv mashbi'in otah* — his heirs may still administer an oath to her — because he waived only the oath she owed to him personally, not the oath she would owe to his heirs. And further: *ve-et yorsheha ha-ba'im bi-reshutah* — his heirs may also administer an oath to her heirs. Here we have a direct case: the husband (the *loveh* of the *ketubah* debt) died first, the wife was obligated in an oath to his heirs, and then the wife died — yet her heirs collect from the husband's heirs by taking their *shvuat yorshin*.

The Gemara invokes the principle *le-tzadin katani* — the *mishnah* is describing two separate cases in parallel. When it says "his heirs may administer an oath to her" — that describes the case of widowhood, where the husband died first. When it says "his heirs may administer an oath to her heirs" — that refers to a case of divorce, where she died first. In the divorce scenario she was never obligated in any oath to the husband's heirs, because the husband was still alive when she died; her heirs therefore take a standard *shvuat yorshin* without difficulty. The challenge to Rav and Shmuel is thus deflected, albeit once again through a reading that substantially departs from the plain sense of the text.

### Son vs. Father Power

A further challenge is raised from the principle *yafeh koach ha-ben mi-koach ha-av* — the son's power exceeds the father's. The father can collect from the *loveh's* heirs only by taking an oath; the son can collect either with a *shvuat yorshin* or, in the case where the father declared on his deathbed that the debt had not been paid, without any oath at all, in accordance with the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Since the case under discussion — where the father collected from the *loveh's* heirs — necessarily involves the *loveh* having predeceased the *malveh*, this *beraita* presents a direct case in which the son collects by *shvuat yorshin* even though the *loveh* died first and the father had been obligated in an oath.

Rav Yosef deflects: *ha mani Beis Shamai he* — this *beraita* follows the view of Beis Shamai, who holds *shtar ha-omed ligvos ke-gavuy dami* — a *shtar* that is due for collection is treated as though already collected. Under that principle, the money is conceptually already in the father's possession; the oath requirement is a technical condition for formal extraction but does not represent a barrier to the underlying ownership. Since the money is thus deemed to belong to the father, his children inherit it directly, and the question of transmitting an oath obligation does not arise. The mainstream ruling of Beis Hillel, on which Rav and Shmuel presumably rely, would not accept this premise and would therefore maintain the restriction.

## Rav Nachman's Compromise

### Rav Nachman's Visit

After all these challenges and deflections, the Gemara recounts an encounter that shaped the practical outcome. Rav Nachman visited Sura, and Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna came before him. They urged him — *lismai mar akrei de-hilchosa de-Rav ve-Shmuel* — to formally uproot the ruling of Rav and Shmuel. The verb *na'akrei* is telling: the ruling had already taken root and would require deliberate action to overturn. The implicit motivation for this request was the practical harm caused by Rav and Shmuel's position, which rendered entire categories of legitimate debts uncollectable based purely on the fortuity of who died first.

### 'Don't Add' Agreement

Rav Nachman declined. *Ikbi ve-asi kol hanei parsai le-ma'akra le-hada de-Rav ve-Shmuel?* — did I travel all this way to come and overturn Rav and Shmuel? He would not reject their authority outright. But he offered a compromise: *ahosifu lo tosifu alah* — do not add to it. Rav and Shmuel ruled on a specific configuration; their ruling applies to exactly that case and is not to be generalized. Novel cases, even those structurally similar, will be decided in favor of allowing the heirs to take their oath and collect.

## Rav Nachman's Compromi…

### Pogem Shtar Case

Rav Pappa illustrates what falls outside the scope of Rav and Shmuel's ruling. Consider the case of *pogem et shtaro* — a creditor who admits that part of the debt documented in his *shtar* has already been paid. Such a person must take an oath before collecting the balance, not because he is collecting from heirs but because his own partial concession creates an obligation equivalent to *modeh be-miktzat*. If this creditor then dies, his children wish to collect the remainder. This is not a case of heirs collecting from heirs with a *shvuat yorshin*; it is a case where the father was obligated in an oath to the *loveh* himself, not to the *loveh's* heirs. Since Rav and Shmuel's ruling addressed specifically the configuration of heirs-to-heirs, this case lies outside it. *Yorshin nishba'in shvuat yorshin ve-notlin* — the heirs take their oath and collect.

### Cosigner Scenario

A second case involves someone who died leaving a *arev* — a cosigner — as the party from whom collection would be sought. Rav Pappa considered exempting this from Rav and Shmuel's ruling: it is heirs collecting from a cosigner, not heirs collecting from the *loveh's* heirs, and therefore structurally different. Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua objected: *atu arva lav basar yasmei azil?* — does the cosigner not subsequently go after the heirs to seek reimbursement? Since the cosigner will ultimately recover from the *loveh's* heirs, the practical effect is that this is indeed a case of heirs-to-heirs, and Rav and Shmuel's ruling applies.

### Brother vs. Son

A third case involves a *malveh* who died and left a brother rather than a son as his heir. Rami bar Chama suggested that this falls outside Rav and Shmuel's ruling — they spoke of sons, not brothers. Rava rejected this as an arbitrary distinction: *mah li shelo pkadsani abba mah li shelo pkadsani achi?* — what difference does it make whether the heir's oath is "my father never told me the debt was paid" or "my brother never told me the debt was paid"? The structure is identical: an heir is testifying to the best of his knowledge about a debt the deceased was owed. Imposing a distinction based on the precise familial relationship would be nothing more than casuistry without conceptual grounding.

## Proofs Against Rav & S…

### Partial Admission Case

The *pogem shtar* case — where the creditor admits partial payment and then dies — was examined not only as a limit on Rav and Shmuel's ruling but also as a proof against it. Here the father was obligated in an oath not to the *loveh's* heirs but to the *loveh* himself. The heirs take their *shvuat yorshin* and collect. This demonstrates that the principle *ein adam morish shvua le-vanav* is not a sweeping rule barring inheritance wherever an oath was outstanding; it is a targeted rule confined, at most, to the specific configuration Rav and Shmuel addressed — and even there, as the rest of the *daf* demonstrates, its authority is contested.

## Final Judicial Discretion

### No Fixed Ruling

Rav Chama states the bottom line directly: *hashta de-lo itmar hilchasa lo ke-Rav u-Shmuel ve-lo ke-Rabbi Eliezer* — since no definitive ruling has been issued either in favor of Rav and Shmuel or in favor of Rav Elazar, *hai de-avad ke-Rav u-Shmuel avad, de-avad ke-Rabbi Eliezer avad* — any judge who rules like Rav and Shmuel has acted correctly, and any judge who rules like Rav Elazar has also acted correctly. The two positions remain formally in tension without resolution, and judicial discretion governs.

### Orphans' Document Status

Rav Pappa translates this into a practical instruction for handling a *shtar* caught in this uncertainty — a debt document where the *loveh* died first, the *malveh* then died, and the heirs now hold the *shtar*. *Lo mikra karinan lei ve-lo agvoyi magbinan bei* — we do not tear up the document, and we do not use it to collect. We do not tear it up because a judge who rules like Rav Elazar would permit collection on it, and we have no authority to preemptively destroy a potentially valid claim. We do not allow collection on it because a judge who rules like Rav and Shmuel would deny it, and we cannot ignore their weight. The document exists in a state of legal suspension, its fate determined by whichever court the heirs choose to approach.

### Judge's Discretion Prote…

A judge who ruled like Rav Elazar faced pressure when a *talmid chacham* threatened to obtain a letter from *Eretz Yisrael* — ironically, since the *Eretz Yisrael* authorities had generally been more sympathetic to the heirs' right to collect — establishing that the ruling was impermissible. The judge was told: *le-chi teisi shkayach* — come back to me when you have the letter; don't bother me until then. When the matter was brought before Rav Chama, he confirmed: *hai dayna de-avad ke-Rabbi Eliezer avad* — the judge was fully within his authority. A ruling in favor of Rav Elazar cannot be invalidated by external pressure or correspondence.

### Default Position Status

The *daf* thus concludes on a nuanced practical note. Rav and Shmuel's position carries weight and, in the absence of a contrary ruling, functions as something of a default — their authority having taken sufficient root that Rav Nachman refused to uproot it. But the default is not exclusive. Rav Nachman's compromise ensures that only the precise case Rav and Shmuel addressed falls within their ruling; analogous cases are resolved in favor of collection. And even within their core case, a *dayan* retains full discretion to rule like Rav Elazar, and such a ruling stands.
