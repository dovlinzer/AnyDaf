# Bekhorot 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nursing & Chazaka

### Lactation Proof

The daf opens with a question arising from the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who ruled in the Mishnah that if one observes a cow nursing, one may assume that the cow has given birth. The Gemara proceeds to explore exactly how far this assumption — this *chazaka* — extends. The basic point is straightforward: *ein merachemet ela im ken yoledet* — an animal will not lactate unless it has given birth. Therefore, if it is lactating, we may confidently assume it has given birth.

### Two Questions

This principle, however, raises two distinct questions. The first: does lactation prove that the nursing mother has indeed given birth? The second, and more far-reaching: does it also prove that the particular animal nursing from her is her own offspring? Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel addresses both in the Mishnah, but the Gemara wants to examine whether the second inference is truly as well-established as the first.

The *nafka mina* — the practical consequence — of the second question involves the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno*, slaughtering a mother and its young on the same day. If we see a calf nursing from a cow and subsequently *shecht* both mother and calf, do we administer lashes for violating *oto ve-et beno*? The answer depends on whether we can definitively establish a mother-child relationship from the act of nursing alone. *I amarta de-dada rachma ve-lo de-dada lo rachma* — if we hold that an animal will only nurse its own offspring and never another's, then yes, we can be certain, and lashes would apply. But if we hold that once an animal has given birth it may nurse offspring that are not its own, then we cannot be certain, and lashes would not apply.

### Purchase Case

The Gemara first turns to what appears to be an explicit ruling in the Mishnah. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel states: *halokeach behema min ha-oved kochavim* — if one purchases a nursing animal from a non-Jew — *eino choshesh shema binah shel acheret hayah* — we are not concerned that the reason it is nursing is because of some other animal's offspring. The initial reading of this passage would suggest that the Mishnah is telling us we need not worry that the calf currently nursing is actually a different animal's calf — thus resolving the second question in Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's favor.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading. *Mi katani hu* — the Mishnah does not say we are unconcerned that this particular calf is another animal's offspring. Rather, *haya katani* — it says we are unconcerned that the reason the cow is nursing is because some other animal gave birth and the cow is nursing that animal's young without having given birth herself. In other words, the first case in the Mishnah establishes only the more basic point: we may assume this animal has given birth. It does not resolve the question of whether the specific animal it is nursing is definitely its own child.

### Barn Case

The Gemara then advances to the second case in the Mishnah, which appears more directly on point. One enters a barn where multiple animals gave birth overnight, and both those giving birth for the first time (*mavkirot*) and those that had given birth previously are all nursing young. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *eino choshesh shema bnah shel zu ba eitzel zu o shema bnah shel zo ba eitzel zu* — we are not concerned that the calves have been exchanged between mothers. Accordingly, the calves nursing from the *mavkirot* are definitively *bechor*, and those nursing from the others are definitively not. Here the Mishnah seems explicitly to establish a mother-child relationship from the fact of nursing alone — precisely the question the Gemara is attempting to resolve.

### Case Comparison

But the Gemara resists this conclusion as well. The reason, as one might anticipate, is that in the barn case, each mother's own offspring *is* present in the room. The Gemara therefore explains: *bimkom didah lo shavka l'didah u-merachma ad-la didah* — when a mother's own young is present in the same space, she will not abandon it to nurse the offspring of another. Thus the certainty that the nursing animal is her own is derived not from nursing alone, but from the conjunction of nursing *and* the presence of her own young.

The Gemara then attempts to extract a ruling from the comparison the Mishnah makes between the two cases, noting the word *vechen* — "and similarly" — which links them. *Reisha dumya de-seifa* — the first case is being compared to the second. Just as in the second case we say it is *vadai didah*, definitely nursing its own, so too in the first case. But the Gemara deflects this inference as well: *hai kide-it lei ve-hai kide-it lei* — each case has its own particular context. The *vechen* does not mean the two cases are identical in their reasoning; it merely tells us that in both there is an exemption from *bechorah*.

### Unresolved Doubt

The result, then, is that a genuine doubt remains. We know with certainty that a lactating animal has given birth — this Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel establishes clearly. We also know that if a mother's own offspring is present in the barn and she is nursing a particular animal, we may assume that animal is her own. But whether nursing alone — without knowing that her own offspring is present — suffices to establish a definitive mother-child relationship, that question the Gemara leaves unresolved.

## Pig & Sheep

### Pig Case

The Gemara now introduces one of the most memorable cases in this sugya: *chazir she-koreich achar rachel* — a pig that is wrapped around and nursing from a sheep. The ruling attributed to Ami bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is twofold. First, *petura min ha-bechorah* — the sheep is exempt from *bechorah*, since its nursing demonstrates it has given birth. Second, *ve-asur ba-achilah* — the pig is forbidden for consumption.

The reasoning behind the second ruling requires unpacking. The Gemara's understanding is that although a kosher animal can in principle give birth to offspring that appears to belong to a non-kosher species — a concept the Gemara takes seriously as a real possibility — the mere fact that this pig is nursing from the sheep does not suffice to establish that it is the sheep's offspring. Since its parentage remains uncertain, and since it presents all the *simanim* of a forbidden animal, it may not be eaten. The Gemara adds that this prohibition stands *ad yavo ve-yoreh tzedek lachem* — until one who will "instruct in righteousness" arrives, a poetic allusion drawn from *Hoshea*.

### Mixed Rulings

This dual ruling immediately invites scrutiny. *Paturninu mibechorah keman* — the exemption from *bechorah* is granted in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that lactation proves birth. *Ve-asur ba-achilah ke-rabbanan* — the prohibition on eating seems to follow the Rabbis, who do not assume that a nursing animal is definitively the offspring of the nursing mother. How can a single ruling follow two opposing authorities simultaneously?

Beyond the internal contradiction, there is a further problem with this reading. If we are merely applying the Rabbis' position of uncertainty about the parentage of the pig, then the appropriate formulation would have been one expressing factual doubt — *ad she-yivadeh lecha davar* — until the facts become known. But the text uses the language of *hora'ah*, of legal instruction — *ad yavo ve-yoreh tzedek lachem* — suggesting that what remains unresolved is not a question of fact but a question of *halachah*. Moreover, the very end of the previous daf established clearly, through the rule of *Rabbah bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*, that *kol makom she-shanah Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel be-mishnateinu halacha kemoto* — wherever Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel expresses an opinion in our Mishnah, the *halachah* follows him, save for three specific exceptions (*arev*, *tzidan*, and *raya achrona*). There is therefore no basis for uncertainty about which authority we follow.

### Resolution

The resolution is that the entire statement is *within* the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and reflects an unresolved question about the *scope* of his ruling. The sheep's exemption from *bechorah* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel without difficulty — it is nursing and therefore has certainly given birth. As for the pig, the question is whether Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say that the unresolved question from the previous section — whether nursing alone establishes a definitive mother-child relationship — applies here as well. If it does, we cannot assume the pig is the sheep's offspring, and we cannot eat it. If Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say nursing *does* definitively establish the relationship, the pig would be a *nidmeh* — an unusual-looking offspring of the sheep — and would be permitted.

This is not factual uncertainty that *Mashiach* will resolve by clarifying what happened; it is halachic uncertainty about what Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position entails, and that is precisely the kind of question that calls for *hora'ah*. The formula *ad yavo ve-yoreh tzedek lachem* is therefore entirely apt.

### Why Pig?

One might ask: if the point is to test whether nursing alone establishes a mother-child relationship, why use the dramatic case of a pig nursing from a sheep? Why not simply ask the question about a sheep nursing from a sheep — a case that would seem even more obviously to establish parentage, and where denial of that assumption would be a greater *chiddush*?

The Gemara explains that each formulation yields a different inference. Had the Mishnah used a sheep nursing from a sheep, one might have reasoned as follows: even if we normally doubt the mother-child relationship when nursing alone is observed, surely a sheep will not nurse an animal not of its own kind — *demina*. Therefore, if it is nursing a fellow sheep, perhaps it did nurse another's offspring out of solidarity with its own kind, and doubt remains. But if it is nursing a pig, *delav mina* — something not of its own species — one might think the very strangeness of the situation is itself proof: why would a sheep nurse a pig unless that pig were truly its own offspring? If the Mishnah had taught only the sheep case, one might conclude that inter-species nursing is *conclusive* proof of parentage precisely because it is so unusual.

By choosing the pig case, the Gemara demonstrates that even this seemingly conclusive consideration does not settle the matter. Perhaps, one could argue the other way: even cross-species nursing may occur without a mother-child relationship, and we cannot be certain. The pig case, in other words, is not a random choice — it is calibrated to present the strongest possible argument for certainty, and to show that even there, the Gemara leaves the question open. Additionally, the pig case is unforgettable, which itself serves a pedagogical purpose.

### Testing Limits

The Gemara then introduces a *baraita* framing the question posed by *Achai b'Rabbi*: seeing a pig wrapped around a nursing sheep — what is the ruling? The Gemara clarifies what precisely is being asked. It is not simply whether we rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel that a nursing animal has given birth — that is settled. Rather, the question branches in two directions.

The first direction: even according to the Rabbis, who normally doubt whether lactation proves birth, might the pig case be different? The Rabbis generally say an animal might lactate out of *rachamim* — compassion — for another's offspring, without having given birth itself. But if that is so, one could limit this *rachamim* to animals of the same species: *demina*, it might have compassion; *delav mina lo* — it would not begin producing milk for an animal of a different species. Therefore in the pig case, the Rabbis might concede that the sheep *vadai* gave birth, since it certainly was not producing milk on account of the pig. Or perhaps even this does not help, and we say *delav mina nami merachama* — it might lactate even for a pig without having given birth.

The second direction: even according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who in principle allows us to determine parentage from nursing when the mother's own offspring is present — might the pig case be special? If he holds that once an animal has given birth it may nurse another's offspring, perhaps that is only true *demina* — within the same species. A sheep would never nurse a pig unless it were truly its own. Therefore, could one eat this pig as *mutar*? Or perhaps *delav mina nami merachama* — even cross-species nursing can occur, and parentage remains uncertain even here.

### Teiku

The Gemara concludes with *teiku* — the question is left unresolved. The practical upshot of all that has been established: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that a lactating animal has certainly given birth and is exempt from *bechorah*. If a mother's own offspring is present in the same space and she is nursing a particular animal, we may definitively assume that animal is her child. But whether nursing alone — absent knowledge that her own offspring is nearby — establishes a conclusive mother-child relationship, whether this holds for same-species or cross-species nursing, remains an open question that the Gemara does not resolve.

## Shearing Issue

### Rabbi Yossi

A new Mishnah introduces the ruling of Rabbi Yossi ben Hamishulam. *Hashochet et ha-bechor oseh makom be-kupitz mikan umikan* — one who slaughters a *bechor* clears a space with a *kupitz* on either side of the neck. The context is as follows: a *bechor* with a *mum* may be slaughtered and eaten by a *kohen*. However, the Torah explicitly prohibits shearing a *bechor*: *lo tagoz bechor shorecha*. The problem is that slaughtering through thick wool is technically difficult — the knife may slip, the cut may be impeded — and yet removing the wool to facilitate *shechitah* appears to violate *gizzah*. Rabbi Yossi ben Hamishulam rules that one may clear a space to make the *shechitah* possible.

### Hand vs. Tool

The Gemara immediately asks why this would not constitute a violation of *lo tagoz*. Its answer is that the Mishnah must be read as describing removal of the hair using one's hands, not a *kli*. The word *bikupitz* is therefore read as *makom la-kupitz* — clearing space for the *kupitz* to be used as the slaughtering instrument — rather than as using the *kupitz* itself to remove the hair. Tosafot notes the unusual choice of a *kupitz* — a cleaver — as a slaughtering instrument, but resolves that the *nafka mina* may be that a larger instrument requires a wider cleared space, and the Mishnah is therefore informing us that even clearing sufficient space for a *kupitz* is permitted.

Why is removal by hand preferable? Because using a *kli* makes the act too closely resemble *gizzah* — formal shearing — whereas using the hand is qualitatively different. Rashi explains that the concern with wool over the neck is that if the knife becomes buried beneath the hair and between the skin, it may constitute *chaladah*, a disqualifying defect in *shechitah*. Beyond that, practically speaking, the wool will impede a clean, uninterrupted cut, and removing it prevents this.

The Mishnah also records a parallel case: *vechen tolesh et ha-sa'ar lirot makom mum* — one may similarly pull out hair in order to examine the site of a blemish. In both cases, the plain reading of the Mishnah suggests that the pulling is done intentionally. The question the Gemara will now pursue is whether the Mishnah truly permits intentional removal, or whether it should be read as describing an unintentional consequence.

### Yom Tov Q

Rav rules: *halachah ke-Rabbi Yossi ben Hamishulam*. Upon this ruling, the students pose a follow-up to Rav Huna: *kenegdo be-Yom Tov mahu* — if one wishes to slaughter a sheep on Yom Tov for the sake of the festival meal, may one similarly remove wool from the neck to facilitate *shechitah*? The issue is no longer *lo tagoz bechor* — that prohibition is specific to *bechor* — but rather the *melachah* of shearing that applies on Yom Tov.

The question, as the Gemara frames it, hinges on understanding the *ta'am* — the reason — behind Rabbi Yossi ben Hamishulam's ruling. Is the Mishnah's permission based on the principle that *tolesh lav hayinu gozez* — pulling hair by hand is fundamentally a different act from shearing, and therefore is not prohibited even by *lo tagoz*? If so, the permission is specific to *bechor*, because on Yom Tov the prohibition is broader, encompassing any act of removing hair from an animal — what the Gemara describes as *oker davar mi-gidulo*, uprooting something from where it grows — regardless of whether the act is technically *gozez* in the narrow sense.

Alternatively, perhaps Rabbi Yossi ben Hamishulam holds that *tolesh hayinu gozez* — pulling is in fact formally equivalent to shearing — but the Mishnah permits it because it was done *she-eino miskaven*, without specific intention to remove the hair. If that is the reason, then the same logic would apply on Yom Tov, because *davar she-eino miskaven* is permitted on Yom Tov as well.

Rav Huna sends the questioners to Rav Chananel: if he invokes the *bechor* Mishnah to answer your Yom Tov question, you will have your answer — because applying the *bechor* ruling to Yom Tov means the rabbi considers the two cases parallel, i.e., the reason must be one that translates across contexts. Rav Chananel does exactly that: citing the ruling of Rav that the *halachah* follows Rabbi Yossi ben Hamishulam, he rules that it is permitted on Yom Tov as well. Rav Huna confirms: *kenegdo be-Yom Tov mutar*.

## Intent & Unintended

### Wine Barrel

If the Yom Tov ruling is based on the principle that *davar she-eino miskaven mutar*, a sharp question arises: did Rav really hold this position? For Rav Chiya bar Ashi quotes Rav as saying *masuchraya denazaita* — the case of the wine-barrel stopper — is prohibited. The case is well known: a wine barrel is stoppered with a rag that has absorbed wine; when one re-inserts the rag, wine inevitably gets squeezed out. Even though one does not intend to squeeze out the wine — one merely wants to stopper the barrel — Rav says it is forbidden on Yom Tov.

If Rav holds that an unintended action is prohibited, how can the Gemara justify applying the Mishnah's ruling to Yom Tov on the basis of *she-eino miskaven*?

### Psik Reisha

The Gemara answers that even Rabbi Shimon — the great proponent of permitting unintended actions — concedes in a case of *psik reisha*. As Abaye and Rava both rule: *modeh Rabbi Shimon be-psik reisha ve-lo yamut* — Rabbi Shimon agrees that if one chops off an animal's head, one cannot claim one did not intend for it to die. When a consequence is *definite* and *inevitable*, the lack of intent is legally irrelevant — it is as if one intended it.

The *masuchraya denazaita* is such a case: squeezing wine out of the rag upon re-insertion is a *definite* consequence, not a mere possibility. Therefore, even Rabbi Shimon would prohibit it, and Rav's ruling there is consistent with his view. In the case of the *bechor*, however, the Gemara reads the Mishnah as describing a situation where hair removal is not *inevitable* — one is pushing the hair aside, and it may or may not be pulled out. In that case, since the consequence is not certain, *she-eino miskaven* genuinely applies and the act is permitted.

### Rav & Yehuda

However, a further complication emerges. Rav Chiya bar Ashi quotes Rav as ruling explicitly: *halachah ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — Rav follows Rabbi Yehuda's position. And Rabbi Yehuda holds that *davar she-eino miskaven assur* — even an unintended act is prohibited if it might lead to a prohibited consequence. The classic example: one may not drag a bench through a garden lest it create a furrow, even if one has no interest in creating that furrow. If Rav holds like Rabbi Yehuda, then he cannot permit the hair-removal on grounds of *she-eino miskaven* — because even if unintended, the possibility of pulling out hair would be enough to prohibit it.

The Gemara lists the positions: Rav Chananel bar Ami in the name of Shmuel says *halachah ke-Rabbi Shimon*; Rav Chiya bar Avin, transmitting without attribution, records: *Rav amar halachah ke-Rabbi Yehuda, Shmuel amar halachah ke-Rabbi Shimon*. So Rav indeed follows Rabbi Yehuda, and the foundation of the Yom Tov ruling cannot be *she-eino miskaven*.

The Gemara therefore concludes: *le-olam savar Rav davar she-eino miskaven assur* — Rav consistently holds that unintended actions are prohibited when they may lead to a forbidden result. The reason of the Mishnah cannot be *she-eino miskaven*. We must return to the other possibility.

## Acts Defined

### Different Acts

The Gemara now asserts: *tolesh lav hayinu gozez* — pulling hair by hand is not the same act as shearing. This is the *pshat* of the Mishnah, and a far more natural reading. When one uses one's hand to remove hair, one is performing a categorically different act from *gizzah*. The prohibition of *lo tagoz bechor shorecha* applies to the act of *gozez* — shearing — and *tolesh* simply does not fall within that category. Therefore, the Mishnah is entirely coherent when read as permitting *intentional* removal of hair by hand: it is not *gizzah*, and there is no prohibition to violate.

### Yom Tov Method

On Yom Tov, however, the analysis must be more nuanced. On Yom Tov, the applicable category is not the narrow prohibition of *lo tagoz* but the broader framework of *melachot*, where what matters is not whether the act fits the precise technical definition of *gozez* but whether it accomplishes the same end result as shearing. Removing hair from an animal achieves the same result whether done with shears or with bare hands, and therefore it should in principle be subject to the Yom Tov prohibition.

Nevertheless, Yom Tov does not prohibit unusual methods of performing otherwise-forbidden acts with the same force as their normal performance. The concept of *ke-le'achar yad* — doing something in an unusual way — mitigates the prohibition at a rabbinic level. No ordinary person seeking to remove wool from an animal uses their bare hands to pull it out; that is not the normal method. Therefore, on Yom Tov, hand-removal constitutes *ke-le'achar yad* — an irregular mode of performing what would otherwise be prohibited.

### Final Allow

Ordinarily, even *ke-le'achar yad* remains rabbinically prohibited on Yom Tov. But the Rabbis did not apply this rabbinic prohibition where the unusual act is done for the purpose of enabling *shechitah* on Yom Tov. Since *shechitah* itself is permitted on Yom Tov for the purpose of food, the ancillary act needed to facilitate it — clearing the wool from the neck — is permitted as well, even though under other circumstances the rabbinic concern would apply. Thus the Yom Tov ruling is grounded not in *she-eino miskaven* but in the combination of *tolesh lav hayinu gozez* (for *bechor*) and *ke-le'achar yad* done for the sake of *shechitah* (for Yom Tov).

### Tosafot View

Tosafot offers an alternative approach, suggesting that throughout this entire discussion — including the permission of Rabbi Yossi ben Hamishulam for *bechor* — the Gemara never actually intended to permit *intentional* hair removal. Even when invoking the principle that *tolesh lav hayinu gozez*, Tosafot reads the case as *ke-le'achar yad*, an unusual action that was effectively unintentional in its character. Under Tosafot's reading, the Mishnah consistently describes an unintended consequence, and the various rulings permitting the act all rest on that unintentionality.

However, as the Gemara's plain reading makes clear, this is not the straightforward *pshat*. The simpler and more compelling reading is that the Mishnah permits intentional pulling of hair by hand for the purpose of *shechitah*, because *tolesh* is genuinely a different act from *gozez*, and therefore no prohibition of *lo tagoz* is triggered at all. The extension to Yom Tov follows, not because the act becomes unintentional, but because it is done in an unusual manner for the sake of an act — *shechitah* — that the Torah itself permits on Yom Tov.
