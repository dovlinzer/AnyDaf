# Bava Metzia 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Purpose of Shvuah

### Oath's necessity

The Gemara on 6a, roughly fifteen lines down at the word *pachot*, opens with a striking question: *ve-khi mei'achar she-zeh tofes ve-omed ve-zeh tofes ve-omed, shvuah zu lama?* If this one is grabbing onto the garment and that one is grabbing onto it, each should have a presumption of being a half-owner — why is a *shvuah* needed at all? This question differs from the inquiry at the opening of the *masechet*, which asked why in certain configurations an oath is *absent*. Here the Gemara asks the inverse: what is the purpose of imposing an oath when the facts of the case seem to speak for themselves? Each party is holding on and claiming ownership, and that physical possession should of itself create a *chazakah*. Why must they additionally be made to swear?

### Preventive takkanah

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *shvuah zo takanat chachamim hi shelo yehei kol echad ve-echad holech ve-tokef be-talito shel chaveiro ve-omer sheli hu.* The oath is not primarily an evidentiary device aimed at clarifying what actually occurred; rather, it is a *takkanat chachamim* — a preventive enactment. The Rabbis were concerned that without it, anyone could simply grab another's garment, claim ownership, and thereby obtain at least half. The knowledge that one will be required to make a formal oath is meant either to deter the act in the first place or, once in court, to induce the dishonest claimant to withdraw.

### Thief & oath

This explanation immediately raises a deeper problem, one that was introduced at the beginning of the *masechet* in a different register. The early Gemara had emphasized that a *shvuah* is effective because a person who is rationalizing — telling himself a self-serving story about why he deserves the money — will be forced to confront the falsity of his claim the moment he must state it unequivocally under oath. But here the concern is different: we are afraid not of a rationalizer but of someone who would knowingly and brazenly grab another's property. Will an oath restrain such a person?

## Oath & knowing thief

### Gemara's question

The Gemara now presses this directly: *ve-neima migo de-chashid amamona, chashid nami ashvuata* — if someone is suspect with respect to monetary matters, with respect to theft, should he not equally be suspect for swearing falsely? What good will a *shvuah* do for a person who, in his own mind, knows he is stealing?

### Migo principle

The Gemara answers: *lo amrinan migo de-chashid amamona chashid ashvuata.* We do not say that because one is suspect for monetary wrongdoing he is therefore suspect for false oath-taking. The principle is stated clearly: suspicion of monetary transgression does not carry over into suspicion regarding *shvuah*. The reasoning behind this principle is taken up immediately in the next section.

## Purpose of Shvuah…

### Tosafot reasoning

Tosafot explains the rationale for this principle with two distinct arguments. The first is that a false oath is of exceptional gravity. As Tosafot notes with reference to Yoma, when God declared *lo tisa et shem Hashem Elokecha la-shav* and followed it with *ki lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa et shemo la-shav* — that God will not cleanse the one who takes His name falsely — the whole world shook. The terror attending a false *shvuah* is of a categorically different order than the discomfort of a monetary transgression. This reflects a broader and, it must be said, somewhat troubling phenomenon: people are often far more scrupulous about ritual matters than about interpersonal and monetary ones. A person might refuse to eat without a reliable *hechsher* and yet feel no compunction about stealing. Tosafot's first answer speaks precisely to this asymmetry — the weight of a *shvuah* reaches people even when monetary prohibitions do not.

Tosafot's second answer is also significant: the *shvuah* can be effective specifically when the person has not yet fully committed to the act. We are dealing with someone who is in the process of making a dishonest claim; the requirement to take a formal oath intervenes at a decision point, before the transgression is complete, and at that stage it may induce him to back down. This is distinct from the case of someone who has already been proven to be a *gazlan* in the past — like the shepherd discussed on the previous *daf* — who is disqualified from taking oaths precisely because there is no longer a decision point at which the oath can intervene. Once a person has fully committed to being a *gazlan*, the oath provides no lever. But when the person is still, so to speak, in motion, the oath may stop him.

So to summarize: a *shvuah* can be effective for a knowing would-be thief either because people generally regard false oaths as far weightier than monetary transgressions, or more specifically because the oath reaches the person before he has fully crossed the line into confirmed *gazlanut*.

## Oath & knowing thief…

### Modeh b'miktzat

The Gemara now explores whether the effectiveness of a *shvuah* in cases of potential dishonesty can be demonstrated from established law. It raises the case of *modeh b'miktzat*: the Torah itself (*hai d'amar Rachmana modeh b'miktzas hatana yishava*) mandates an oath when a defendant admits to part of a claim — say, fifty out of a hundred — and denies the rest. If we were to say that someone who lies about money is thereby suspect for false oaths, the entire institution of *modeh b'miktzat* would collapse: why impose an oath on someone who is already lying? The Gemara deflects this, however: the case of *modeh b'miktzat* involves rationalization, not brazen theft. Rabba's famous psychological explanation applies — *ishtamutei ka mishtamit* — the defendant is not really a thief; he is buying time, hoping to gather funds. He wants to pay but cannot, so he denies part of the debt. His *shvuah* is effective precisely because he is rationalizing rather than knowingly stealing, and therefore he will not swear falsely to something he knows to be a lie.

### Loan vs. deposit

The Gemara presses on, noting that Rav Idi bar Avin said in the name of Rav Chisda: one who denies a monetary loan and is proven to be lying remains *kasher* as a witness, while one who denies a *pikadon* (deposit) and is proven to be lying is disqualified — *pasul le-edut*. This distinction is crucial. Denying a loan, even if dishonest, does not constitute a fully realized act of theft in terms of what it does to one's moral character, because the defendant can rationalize that he is only delaying payment and intends to pay eventually. Denying a *pikadon*, by contrast, is a genuine act of *gzel* — there is no analogous rationalization, because the object was entrusted to you and you have no legitimate claim to it.

This raises the question: if a *shvuah* is administered in cases involving a *pikadon* — as Rami bar Chama taught, the four types of *shomrim* require both partial denial and partial admission before an oath is taken — why should that oath be effective if we are potentially dealing with a knowing thief? The Gemara answers that even in a *pikadon* case there can be a rationalization. The *shomer* who denies having the animal may tell himself: *mishtachna leganav ve-atfisna lei* — I will find the thief who stole it and recover it. Or: *inami mishtachna lei ba-agam u-masina lei* — perhaps the animal wandered off into the marsh and I will bring it back. Rather than admit negligence, the *shomer* prefers to say the animal is gone entirely, buying time while he tries to locate it. So even in *pikadon* cases the door to rationalization is not fully closed, and the oath retains its function.

The Gemara then tightens this further: if even in *pikadon* cases there is possible rationalization, why is one who denies a *pikadon* and is proven to lie *pasul le-edut*? The answer is that the disqualification applies specifically when witnesses testify that *at the very moment* the defendant denied possessing the object, *ito pikadon be-veiteh* — it was in his house. There is no room for rationalization if witnesses establish that he knew exactly where the object was and still denied it. In all ordinary cases, however, since we cannot rule out the possibility that the defendant is rationalizing, we impose the oath because it will be effective for the rationalizer even if it would not deter a confirmed knowing thief.

The Gemara proceeds through further cases. Rav Huna rules that when a *shomer* offers payment and claims the object is no longer in his possession, we nonetheless administer an oath — *shvuah she-einah bi-reshuto* — that he is not concealing it. And Rav Nachman rules that a *shomeret* who completely denies a debt must take a *shvuat heset*. In each case the question arises: why not say *migo de-chashid amamona chashid ashvuata*? And in each case the Gemara either finds a rationalization available to the defendant or concludes with the flat ruling that the principle simply does not apply — *lo amrinan migo de-chashid amamona chashid ashvuata* — and that the effectiveness of the *shvuah* is not undermined by the possibility that the person is a knowing wrongdoer.

The case of the two workers and the storeowner (a case introduced earlier in the *masechet*) is also noted here. The employer tells the storeowner to feed his workers; the storeowner says he fed them, the workers say he did not, and both swear and collect. One of them is certainly lying. There is little obvious rationalization available — either the storeowner fed them or he did not — yet both oaths are administered and both are honored. Similarly, Rav Sheshet's ruling that a *shomer* must take three oaths — that he was not negligent, that he did not make personal use of the object, and that it is not in his possession — raises the same issue with the third oath: if it really is in his closet, he is a knowing thief, and why would the oath help? The answer comes in the Gemara's concluding declaration: the principle is that we simply do not say that suspicion of monetary wrongdoing renders a person suspect for false oath-taking.

## Abaye on rationalization

### Old debt claim

Abaye does not dispute the practical conclusion but offers a different theoretical framing. Rather than saying that a *shvuah* can work even for a knowing thief, Abaye argues that in practice people can almost always rationalize. The specific rationalization he invokes is: *chaishinan shema milva yeshana yesh lo alav* — we are concerned that perhaps the person grabbing the garment believes the other party owes him an old debt. A person sees David walking around in a fine coat and thinks: David owes me money from ten years ago; he has forgotten about it, I cannot prove it, so I am entitled to seize this coat as payment. This rationalization is always available. Whatever the context, the would-be thief can construct a story in which he is not really stealing but rather collecting what is owed to him.

### Specificity matters

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *ei hachi, nishkalei belo shvuah* — if you are only rationalizing because you genuinely believe the debt exists, then the oath should be worthless; just let him take it without an oath, since in his mind it is legitimately his. If the rationalization is sufficient to make him think the object is his, he will simply swear that it is his and proceed. The Gemara therefore refines the point: *ela chaishinan shema safek milva yeshana yesh lo alav* — we are not concerned that he is certain of an old debt, but only that he has a vague, uncertain recollection that perhaps he is owed something. The doubt is the key. Because he is not certain, he will not take a firm oath that the object is unambiguously his; the doubt in his own mind restrains him from swearing definitively.

### Rav Sheshet

But the Gemara presses again: *velo amina tafas mamona mi-safek, mishtava nami mi-safek?* If a person will grab property on the basis of a mere doubt — because he might be owed money — why will he not swear on the basis of that same doubt? The answer comes from Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi: *parishi inshi mi-safek shvuah velo parishi mi-safek mamona* — people will refrain from even a doubtful oath but will not refrain from taking doubtful money. The reason: *mamona ita be-chazarah, shvuah leita be-chazarah* — money can be returned. If I later realize I was wrong, I can give the money back and make amends. But a false oath, once spoken, cannot be unsaid or undone. The irreversibility of the spoken word — an insight one student poignantly connected to interpersonal speech as well — is what makes even a potentially false *shvuah* something people are more reluctant to risk.

This is a profound observation about human moral psychology, especially apt before Rosh Hashanah: in the realm of *bein adam la-chavero*, people routinely give themselves latitude, telling themselves that wrongs can be corrected. But when an act is inherently irreversible, people exercise greater caution. Practically speaking, there is not a large gap between the position of the anonymous Gemara and that of Abaye. Both agree that *shvuot* are effective across a very wide range of cases. The difference is theoretical: the earlier view holds that even a person who knows he is a thief will be deterred by a *shvuah*, while Abaye holds that people can nearly always construct some rationalization, making the deterrence possible without needing to posit that a knowing thief fears a false oath. In practice, the coverage is essentially the same.

## Yanking the object

### Rabbi Zeira's question

Having established the purpose and mechanics of the *shvuah* in the standard case of two people holding a garment, the Gemara now introduces a variation. Rabbi Zeira poses the question: *takpah echad befaneinu, mahu?* — if, while both are holding the *tallit* before the court, one party yanks it out of the other's hands, what is the law? Does the person who now holds the entire garment gain a superior presumption, or do we disregard the yanking?

### Silence interpretation

The Gemara initially frames this as a question about how to interpret the behavior of the party whose hands were emptied. *Ei deshatik* — if he said nothing after his companion yanked it away — perhaps *oduyei odei lei*, his silence is an implicit concession that the object is his companion's after all. But *ve-ei dekatzavach* — if he is actively protesting — then what could he have done? He was physically overpowered. The court saw the entire episode. Why should he need to say anything? He might reasonably conclude that the *beit din* witnessed what happened and there is no need to protest. The Gemara complicates this further: *lo tzricha deshatik me-ikara vehadar tzavach* — the real question is where he was initially silent and then began to protest. Does the initial silence indicate concession, with the subsequent protest too late? Or does the fact that he eventually protested make clear retroactively that the silence was not a concession — *savar ha chazu lei rabanan*, he assumed the *beit din* saw exactly what happened and he had no need to shout?

### Rav Nachman braita

Rav Nachman brings a *braita* to address the question. The *braita* states: *she-shneihem adukin bah* — the rule of splitting applies when both are holding onto it with equal grasp. But *aval hayta tallis yotzeit mi-tachat yado shel echad meihem* — if the garment is emerging from under the hand of only one of them, *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — the other must bring proof. This *braita* seems to be relevant to Rabbi Zeira's question, because the scenario where the garment is under one person's hand must be telling us something about a case where the situation was not so from the outset.

### Braita ambiguity

The Gemara immediately asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case in this *braita*? If it simply means that one person is holding the garment and a stranger walks up claiming it is his, that is obvious — *peshita* — of course the bystander must prove his claim; there is nothing novel there. The *braita* must therefore be addressing the case *she-takaf echad befaneinu* — where they were initially both holding on, but now only one is. This would seem to resolve Rabbi Zeira's question in favor of the one now holding.

### Return with possession

The Gemara deflects this reading. *Hacha b'mai askinan* — the *braita* is not discussing that scenario at all. Rather: both were holding, the court told them to go divide the garment (*zilu palgu*), they left, and when they returned, one was holding it alone. Now each tells a different story: one says the other conceded to him; the other says the first one agreed to buy his half and has not paid. In that situation, *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — the one not holding must prove his claim. But the Gemara explains why: *ad hashta chashadat lei be-gazlan, ve-hashta mograt lei be-lo sahadi* — until this point you were accusing him of theft, and yet now you are claiming you sold him your half without any witnesses? The inference from that behavior is that the holder came by the garment legitimately. This scenario, therefore, does not speak to the case of yanking in front of *beit din*.

### Edge holding

A further reading is offered: *ve-iba'is eima*, the *braita* addresses a case where from the outset only one person was holding properly while the other was gripping only *be-siruchei* — by the fringes or threads, not the garment itself. *Ve-afilu le-Sumchos de-amar mamon ha-mutal be-safek cholkin belo shvuah* — even Sumchos, who is willing to split any disputed property without an oath, *modeh Sumchos* would agree that *sirkha lav klum hi* — holding by mere threads or fringes counts for nothing. The question of Rabbi Zeira thus remains unresolved.

## Verbal vs. physical

### Dedication question

Rabbi Zeira's unresolved question generates a further inquiry through the *im timtzi lomar* framework. Were one to conclude that yanking the garment in front of *beit din* does not change the presumption — that we treat the parties as still equally *muchzak* — then, by analogy, if Reuven were to declare the *tallit* dedicated to the Temple (*makdish*) while Shimon is still holding it, that declaration should equally be without effect; one cannot consecrate property in which another party holds a *chazakah*. Conversely, were one to conclude that yanking does work — that Reuven who yanks becomes fully *muchzak* — then the question arises whether a verbal declaration of *hekdesh*, without any physical act, could achieve the same result.

### Verbal transfer

The basis for thinking it might is the principle *amirato le-gavo'ah ki-mesirato la-hedyot dami* — a verbal transfer to the Divine is halachically equivalent to physical handing over to a private person. If so, Reuven's declaration of *hekdesh* should be *ke-man de-takfah dami* — equivalent to his having physically seized the garment. And just as physical seizure would make Shimon's silence into a concession, so too his silence in the face of the verbal declaration might constitute a concession.

### Possession requirement

The counterargument is equally compelling. Whatever power words have in the realm of *hekdesh*, the verse teaches otherwise from the case of a house: *ish ki yakdish et beito kodesh, mah beito bi-reshuto, af kol bi-reshuto* — just as a man's house must be in his own possession for him to consecrate it, so too any object must be in his possession. As long as Shimon is physically holding the garment, it is under his control and Reuven's words have no purchase over it. Shimon has no obligation to respond to a verbal declaration that, by law, can have no effect on something he is holding. His silence is therefore meaningless. The question remains unresolved.

## Bathhouse case

### Dedication to Temple

The Gemara now brings a concrete case that directly engages the question of verbal *hekdesh* over disputed property. Two people were disputing ownership of a certain bathhouse. *Hai amar didi hu ve-hai amar didi hu* — each one claimed it as his own. Then one of them, apparently out of frustration at the impasse, got up and declared the entire bathhouse dedicated to the Temple: *kam chad mineihu ve-akdeshah*.

### Sacred concern

Rabbi Chanina, Rabbi Oshaya, and all the other Rabbis in the vicinity stopped using the bathhouse, reasoning that it might now be *hekdesh* — sacred property of the Temple — and using it would constitute a misappropriation of consecrated property.

### Rav Chisda query

Rabbi Oshaya said to Rabba: when you go to see Rav Chisda in Kafri, ask him what the *halacha* is with this bathhouse, for we need to know whether we may use it. As Rabba passed through Sura on his way, he encountered Rav Hamnuna and posed the question to him there.

## Safek bechor

### Taharos reference

Rav Hamnuna replied: the answer is already in a *mishnah* — not the *mishnah* of our chapter, but a *mishnah* in Taharos, in the tractate dealing with doubtful firstborn animals.

### Bechor cases

The *mishnah* discusses *safek bechoros* — animals or children whose firstborn status is uncertain. With respect to a human *bechor*: if a woman had a miscarriage and it is unknown whether forty days had passed since conception, the next child's status as *bechor* for *pidyon ha-ben* is in doubt. With respect to animals: if it is unknown whether a cow had previously given birth, the next calf might or might not be a *bechor*. The *mishnah* covers both *tahor* animals (cattle and sheep) and *tamei* animals (donkeys, which require redemption with a sheep). In all such cases, *echad bechor adam ve-echad bechor behemah, bein tehorah bein teme'ah* — the law is the same.

### Hamotzi rule

The *mishnah* rules: *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah*. If the *kohen* claims the animal is a *bechor* and belongs to him, he must prove it. The owner retains the animal by default. Whoever wishes to extract property from another bears the burden of proof.

### Priest grabbing

Now the *mishnah* goes further: even if the *kohen* grabs the animal, it must be taken back from him. The *braita* elaborates: *asurin be-gizah ve-avodah* — the owner who retains the animal may not shear it and may not put it to work. Why not? Because even though the *kohen* cannot prove his claim and therefore cannot take it, the animal might genuinely be a *bechor*, and a *bechor* may not be shorn or worked by its owner. The owner is caught in a paradox: he keeps the animal but cannot fully use it.

### Paradox of ownership

This is a striking halachic configuration: from the perspective of *dinei mamonot*, the owner wins — *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — and the *kohen* cannot take the animal. But from the perspective of *issurim*, the animal is a *safek bechor* and must be treated accordingly. The owner may wait for a blemish to develop and then slaughter and eat the animal, or bring it as a *korban* if it is unblemished — but he cannot shear it, work it, or otherwise treat it as ordinary livestock. The *mammon* question and the *issur* question are resolved on entirely separate tracks.

## Mammon vs. issur

### Rabba's answer

Rabba addresses the seeming tension. He states clearly that *tokfo kohen motziin oto mi-yado* — if a *kohen* grabs the animal, we take it back from him. The *hamotzi mechavero* principle is a definitive ruling in the owner's favor: the *kohen* cannot grab and keep. This is a full *psak* that the animal belongs to its owner for all *mammon* purposes.

### Sanctity principle

Nevertheless, *kedushah ba'ah me-eleha shani* — the sanctity of a *bechor* is categorically different. It does not derive from the *kohen*'s ownership; it inheres in the animal itself by virtue of being the firstborn. For that reason, even after a clear *psak* that the animal belongs to its owner and even after the *kohen* is turned away, the animal retains its status as a *safek bechor* and the prohibitions on shearing and working remain in force. The *issur* dimension is entirely independent of who wins the monetary dispute.

### Parallel case

This separation of *mammon* and *issur* extends further: if one were to take the animal from one owner and give it to another owner entirely, both the monetary transfer and the *kedushah* question would be resolved on their own terms. The monetary question follows whoever holds it under proper title; the *kedushah* question follows the reality of whether the animal is in fact a *bechor*, regardless of who owns it at any given moment.

## Tokfo kohen

### Ma'aser proof

Rav Chanina now offers a different proof to Rabba in support of the position that *tokfo kohen motziin oto mi-yado* — that the *kohen* cannot grab and keep. The proof comes from the laws of *ma'aser behemah*, the animal tithe. The *mishnah* states: *ha-sfekot nichnasin la-dir le-hit'aser* — doubtful animals enter the pen to be tithed. What are these doubtful animals? They are sheep that were given as redemption for a *safek bechor* donkey. A donkey whose firstborn status is uncertain must be redeemed with a sheep; that sheep then belongs to the *kohen*, unless the donkey was not actually a *bechor*, in which case the sheep belongs to its original owner. The owner has custody by default. These sheep, whose ownership is uncertain, are nonetheless counted together with the owner's other newborn animals for purposes of *ma'aser behemah*.

### Safek animals

The process of *ma'aser behemah* requires the owner to gather all animals born in a given season and have them pass single-file through a gate; every tenth one is set apart as *ma'aser*. The question is whether the *safek* animals — whose status as belonging to the owner or to the *kohen* is unresolved — may be included in this counting.

### Tithe problem

Rav Chanina's argument is: if one were to say that *tokfo kohen ein motziin oto mi-yado* — that if the *kohen* grabs such an animal he is allowed to keep it — then these animals would be genuinely uncertain in their ownership in a way that should prevent them from being included in the *ma'aser* count. If the *kohen* could legitimately seize them at any moment, their inclusion in my *ma'aser* pool means I am potentially using property that might not be mine to fulfill a religious obligation — *nimtza zeh poter mamono be-mamono shel kohen* — I am exempting my animals through another man's property.

### Exactly ten

Abaye refines the argument. He concedes the force of the concern but notes that the proof from *ma'aser* works only in a specific case: *hacha be-mai askinan ke-gon de-leit lei ela tishah ve-hu* — where the owner has exactly nine unambiguous animals plus the one doubtful *safek* animal, making ten in total. In that configuration, *mimanafshach* — either way it works: if the doubtful animal belongs to the owner, he has ten and is obligated in *ma'aser*, and the animal participates normally in the tithe; if the doubtful animal belongs to the *kohen*, the owner has only nine and is not obligated in *ma'aser* at all. There is no case, then, where he is using another's property to exempt his own. But Abaye then retracts this position.

## Jumping sheep

### Interruption case

Abaye withdraws his own defense of the *mimanafshach* argument and declares: *safeka lav bar asura hu* — a doubtful animal cannot be used for *ma'aser* even where a *mimanafshach* analysis would seem to cover all outcomes. He derives this from another *mishnah*: *kafatz echad min ha-menuyim le-tochan, kulam peturim* — if one of the animals that had already been counted jumps back into the pen and becomes mixed in with the uncounted animals, all are exempt from *ma'aser*. The counting process is disrupted because one may not count the same animal twice, and since it is unknown which animal jumped back in, no further counting can proceed.

### Either-way objection

One might object: why not continue counting and apply a *mimanafshach* argument? If the next group of ten does not include the animal that jumped back in, the count is valid and the tenth is *ma'aser*. If it does include the jumped-in animal, then by the principle of *minyan ha-ra'uy* — the animals already counted were part of a valid, fit counting process — those animals are exempt even if the process was cut short. Either way, it would seem that the owner is covered.

### Minyan hara'uy

The principle of *minyan ha-ra'uy* holds that animals which passed through a valid counting process — one that was, at the moment of counting, fit to result in a completed *ma'aser* — are exempt from future counting even if the process was interrupted. Thus, animals counted before the disruption do not need to be re-entered into a future count; they participated in a *minyan ha-ra'uy* and are released from the obligation.

### Definite tenth

The Gemara's answer is decisive: *asiri vadai amar Rachmana, velo asiri safek* — the Torah requires a definite tenth, not a doubtful tenth. Even where a *mimanafshach* analysis would seem to guarantee that the outcome is always either fully valid or fully exempt, the Torah's requirement of *asiri* — definiteness — is not satisfied by doubt. Doubt, even doubt that is covered from all directions, does not produce the definitive tenth that *ma'aser behemah* requires.

### Definitive presumption

The conclusion that flows from this is directly relevant to the *tokfo kohen* question. Since a doubtful animal cannot serve as *ma'aser behemah* under any circumstances, and yet the *mishnah* teaches that these *safek* animals do enter the pen and are tithed, it must be that they are not genuinely doubtful from a *mammon* perspective at all. The ruling of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* is not merely a procedural holding that we stand aside and see who grabs what; it is a definitive *psak* that the animal belongs to its owner. And if the *kohen* grabs it, he does not keep it — *tokfo kohen motziin oto mi-yado*. The owner's presumption is real and complete, and the *ma'aser* process can proceed on the basis of that definitive ownership, not on the basis of doubt.
