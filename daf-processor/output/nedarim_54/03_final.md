# Nedarim 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pumpkins & Vegetables

### Intro: Pumpkin Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיָּרָק — מוּתָּר בַּדִּלּוּעִין, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹסֵר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲלֹא אוֹמֵר אָדָם לִשְׁלוּחוֹ ״קַח לִי יָרָק״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא מָצָאתִי אֶלָּא דִּלּוּעִין״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: For **one who vows** that **vegetables** are forbidden **to** him, it **is permitted** for him **to** eat **gourds,** as people typically do not include gourds in the category of vegetables; **and Rabbi Akiva prohibits** him from eating gourds. The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Akiva: But doesn't a person say to his agent: Purchase vegetables for me, and** the agent, after failing to find vegetables, returns with gourds and **says: I found only gourds?** This indicates that gourds are not considered vegetables.

The *mishnah* states: *hanoder min hayerek mutar badiluim* — one who takes a *neder* not to derive benefit from vegetables is permitted to eat pumpkin. The immediate question is why pumpkin should not be included in the category of *yerek* in the first place.

### Ran's Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: כֵּן הַדָּבָר, אוֹ שֶׁמָּא אוֹמֵר הוּא ״לֹא מָצָאתִי אֶלָּא קִיטְנִית״? אֶלָּא, שֶׁהַדִּלּוּעִין בִּכְלַל יָרָק, וְקִיטְנִית אֵינוֹ בִּכְלַל יָרָק. וְאָסוּר בְּפוֹל הַמִּצְרִי לַח, וּמוּתָּר בַּיָּבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to them: The matter is so,** and that proves my opinion; **or perhaps,** does the agent return and **say: I found only legumes? Rather,** it is apparent **that gourds are included in** the category of **vegetables,** although they differ from other vegetables, and therefore, the agent purchases gourds and explains that he found only gourds. **And legumes are not included in** the category of **vegetables,** and that is why the agent dispatched to purchase vegetables would not purchase legumes at all. **And** for one who vows that vegetables are forbidden to him, it **is prohibited to** eat **the fresh cowpea**, which is considered a vegetable, **and** it **is permitted to** eat **dry** cowpea, which is not a vegetable.

The *Ran* at the opening of his commentary offers two distinct explanations. The first is: *dela havei yerek ela pri* — pumpkin is not a vegetable at all; it is a fruit. It grows on a vine and falls into the *pri* category rather than the *yerek* category. The second explanation offered by others is: *desatam yerek ne'echal kemoto shehu chai* — the ordinary meaning of *yerek* refers to something that is edible while raw. *Diluim* do not meet that criterion, and therefore fall outside the scope of the *neder*.

### Edible Raw Category

The question of whether something is *ne'echal kemoto shehu chai* — edible in its raw state — carries significant halachic weight across a range of areas. One prominent application is *bishul* on *Shabbat*. The *rishonim* discuss whether cooking a food that is already edible raw constitutes the *melacha* of *bishul*, and the *halacha* is that it does. *Tosafot* and the *Mordechai* raise the case of baked apples and similar foods where the item remains edible throughout but undergoes a qualitative transformation — becoming soft, mushy, and differently flavored — and even so, the conclusion is that such cooking is still *chayav*.

A clarifying example: boiling water. Water is edible cold and edible hot; functionally nothing has changed. Yet it remains subject to the prohibition.

The related question of partially cooked food generates a significant *machloket*. The *Rashba* holds that once a food has been cooked to the point of edibility, it is considered halachically cooked, and one is not *chayav mide'oraita* for cooking it further — for instance, returning a rare steak to the fire. The *Rambam* disagrees, holding that any improvement through further cooking, up to the point of full completion, continues to constitute the *melacha* of *bishul*. The *Shiltei Gibborim* goes even further, ruling that even fully cooked food, if further cooking would improve it — *mitztamek veyafeh lo* — remains subject to the *melacha de'oraita*, until the point at which it begins to deteriorate.

A second major application is *bishul akum*. A food that is edible in its raw state is exempt from the prohibition of *bishul akum*, since the non-Jew's involvement in its preparation is irrelevant — the food could have been eaten without any cooking at all. Carrots, for instance, are edible raw, and therefore cooked carrots present no *bishul akum* concern. The same logic has been applied to sushi, as eating raw fish has come to be regarded as normative.

### Rabbi Akiva's View

The *mishnah* continues: *v'Rabbi Akiva oser* — Rabbi Akiva prohibits eating pumpkin under such a *neder*. The *chachamim* challenge him: *vehalo omer adam lishlucho kach li yerek veomer lo matzati ela diluin* — is it not true that a person who sends a *shaliach* to buy vegetables, and the *shaliach* returns saying they had no vegetables but did have pumpkins, would report back in exactly those terms? The implication of the *chachamim* is that this return-and-report demonstrates pumpkin is *not* a vegetable — the *shaliach* came back empty-handed, distinguishing pumpkins from what was requested.

Rabbi Akiva, however, responds differently. He draws a contrast: if a *shaliach* came back and said they had no vegetables but did have *kitnit* — beans — no one would say that, because beans are too far removed from the vegetable category. The very fact that the *shaliach* specifically returns to report that pumpkins were available, and to ask whether pumpkins would be acceptable, is what interests Rabbi Akiva.

### Chachamim's Response

The *mishnah* also addresses the case of *ful hamitzri* — the Egyptian bean. *Vaasur beful hamitzri lach umutar beyavesh* — one is prohibited from eating it when fresh but permitted when dry. Fresh green beans are clearly a vegetable; dried beans are a different matter. This line supports Rabbi Akiva's position, demonstrating that even within the *kitnit* category, context determines whether a food falls within the scope of *yerek*.

### Machloket Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: כֹּל מִילְּתָא דִּצְרִיךְ שְׁלִיחָא לְאִמְּלוֹכֵי עֲלַהּ — לָאו מִינֵיהּ הוּא. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: כֹּל מִילְּתָא דְּמִימְּלִיךְ שְׁלִיחָא עֲלַהּ — מִינֵיהּ הוּא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְעִנְיַן מַלְקוֹת, שֶׁאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis **disagree?** The Gemara explains that **the Rabbis maintain: Any item with regard to which an agent must consult** the person who dispatched him before purchasing it, **is not** considered the same **type.** Since the agent must ask whether he can purchase gourds, apparently they are not a vegetable. **And Rabbi Akiva maintains: Any item with regard to which an agent** must **consult is** considered the same **type.** With regard to food of a different type, he does not consult. **Abaye said: Rabbi Akiva concedes with regard to lashes that** the one who vowed **is not flogged** if he ate gourds, as the issue of whether or not he violated his vow is not entirely clear.

The *gemara* now articulates the underlying dispute. *Rabbanan savri kol milta d'tzarich shlicha l'amlokei alei lav minei hu* — the *rabbanan* hold that anything the *shaliach* feels the need to return and clarify is thereby shown to be *outside* the requested category. If he comes back and says "they had no vegetables but they did have pumpkins," he is implicitly distinguishing pumpkin from vegetable.

*V'Rabbi Akiva savar kol milta d'mimloch shlicha alei minei hu* — Rabbi Akiva holds the opposite: anything the *shaliach* returns to specify is shown to be *within* the category. The *shaliach* is saying: the only vegetable they had was pumpkin — do you want it? The act of asking the question itself indicates that pumpkin is understood to belong to the same general category as what was requested. The *machloket* thus turns on whether the need to specify is an act of inclusion or exclusion.

## Ulla's Precision

### Ulla's Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַיָּרָק כּוּ׳. וְהָא מִן יָרָק נְדַר! אָמַר עוּלָּא: בְּאוֹמֵר ״יַרְקֵי קְדֵרָה עָלַי״, וְדִילְמָא יָרָק הַנֶּאֱכָל בִּקְדֵרָה קָאָמַר? בְּאוֹמֵר ״יָרָק הַמִּתְבַּשֵּׁל בִּקְדֵרָה עָלַי״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna: For **one who vows** that **vegetables** are forbidden **to** him, it is permitted to eat gourds, and Rabbi Akiva prohibits him from eating gourds. The Gemara questions Rabbi Akiva's ruling: **But** how can his vow include gourds, which are fruits and not vegetables; **didn't he vow** to refrain **from** eating **vegetables? Ulla said:** The mishna is referring **to one who said: Vegetables** cooked **in a pot** are forbidden **to me.** Gourds are included in the category of vegetables cooked in a pot. The Gemara asks: **And** if that is what he said, **perhaps he is saying: A vegetable that is eaten in a pot,** i.e., a vegetable that is added to flavor the food cooked in the pot, is forbidden to me? The Gemara answers: The mishna is referring **to one who said: A vegetable that is cooked in a pot** is forbidden **to me,** a statement that can include gourds.

The *gemara* questions the *mishnah*'s formulation from a different angle: *tanan hamudar min hayerek vehu min hayerek nadar* — but the *mishnah* simply says he took a *neder* from *yerek*, and *yerek* sounds inclusive enough that even Rabbi Akiva should concede that *diluim* would be included. The language seems too broad to generate the *machloket* described. *Amar Ulla beomer yarkei kedeirah alai* — Ulla therefore explains that the *mishnah* must be dealing with a case where the person used more precise language: "vegetables for the pot are forbidden to me."

### Gemara's Challenge

The *gemara* pushes back on Ulla's reading, suggesting alternative formulations: *vedilma yerek hane'echal bekedeirah ka'amar* — perhaps he said "vegetables that are eaten in a pot," or *beomer yerek hamitbashel bekedeirah alai* — "vegetables that are cooked in a pot." Any of these phrasings, while sounding more specific, would still be relatively inclusive, and Ulla's precision requirement would not meaningfully distinguish the case.

### Rashba's Extension

The *Rashba* extends the question raised here — whether *diluim* falls within the category of *yerek* — beyond the laws of *nedarim* entirely. Drawing on the *Yerushalmi*, the *Rashba* notes that the same categorical question applies in commerce and in the *halachot* of *hefker*. If a person declares all of his vegetables *hefker*, did he intend to include his pumpkins? The *Rashba*'s point is that this is a broadly applicable question about the scope of categorical language, not one confined to the specific domain of vows.

## Specification Principle

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *gemara* returns to articulate the positions more precisely. *Rabbanan savri kol milta d'tzarich shlicha l'amlokei alei lav minei hu* — the *tanna kamma* holds that anything requiring the *shaliach* to return and seek clarification is thereby shown to lie *outside* the category. The specification signals a distinction, not an inclusion.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

*V'Rabbi Akiva savar kol milta d'mimloch shlicha alei minei hu* — Rabbi Akiva maintains that the act of returning to specify is precisely what places something *within* the category. The *shaliach*'s clarification is a statement that the item is the only representative of the requested category available; it is not a concession that the item falls outside it.

### Abaye: Malkot vs. Issur

*Amar Abaye modeh Rabbi Akiva l'inyan malkot she'eino lokeh* — Abaye introduces an important distinction: even Rabbi Akiva concedes that one does not receive *malkot* for eating pumpkin in violation of a *neder* against *yerek*. Rabbi Akiva holds that it is *assur*, but he would not say that the violation of *bal yachal* is sufficiently clear-cut to impose lashes.

This is connected to the principle of *safek nedarim l'hachmir* — that in cases of doubt regarding *nedarim*, we rule stringently. Rabbi Akiva's position is essentially one of *safek l'chumra*: the item is close enough to the prohibited category to be forbidden, but the doubt is real enough that punishment cannot follow. There is also a technical dimension relating to the rules of *hatra'ah* — the warning that witnesses must deliver before an *aveirah* can result in *malkot*. The warning must be specific and unambiguous, and where there is genuine uncertainty about whether the item falls within the *neder*, the conditions for *malkot* cannot be met. The *issur* remains; the *onesh* does not.

## Shlichut Principles

### Shlichut Intro

The *mishnah*'s discussion of the *shaliach* who returns to report on the availability of certain items opens the door to a broader treatment of *shlichut*. The major *sugyot* on *shlichut* appear in *Kiddushin* and will be encountered there in due course, but several fundamental points are relevant here.

The foundational principle is *shlucho shel adam k'moto* — a person's *shaliach* stands in his stead. There are, however, different models of *shlichut*: one in which the *shaliach* is an extension of the sender, and another in which the *shaliach* is an independent actor operating on the sender's behalf. The scope of *shlichut* varies depending on the type of act being performed.

One important application of this principle, touched on tangentially in this context, concerns *chamar medina* — the locally significant beverage that may substitute for wine for *kiddush* and *havdala*. The question of whether a *shaliach* sent for wine who returns with beer parallels the *diluim* case: does the return-and-report indicate that beer is within the category of wine, or does it mark beer as distinct? Rav Moshe Feinstein addresses a related question regarding seltzer water in a well-known *teshuvah*, suggesting that the operative criterion is whether a beverage is consumed for enjoyment beyond mere thirst. Other *poskim* apply a different standard, asking whether it is something one would serve to guests.

### Ein Shaliach L'Aveira

A central principle of *shlichut* is *ein shaliach lidvar aveirah* — one cannot appoint an agent to commit a sin on one's behalf. There are three distinct ways to understand what this principle means in practice.

The first understanding focuses on the *meshaleach* — the sender. The reasoning is *divrei harav v'divrei hatalmid divrei mi shomim*: when there is a conflict between God's law and a human instruction, God's law prevails. The *shaliach* knows the act is prohibited and has no legitimate basis to comply; therefore, the act cannot be attributed back to the sender.

The second understanding focuses on the *shaliach* himself. Since this is someone else's idea entirely — the *shaliach* did not conceive the act independently — one might argue that holding the *shaliach* liable requires some affirmative basis. The third understanding draws an analogy between *shlichut* and *tnai*: the capacity to appoint a *shaliach* is equivalent to the capacity to formulate a conditional — both require that one actually controls the act in question. Since a *de'oraita* prohibition is not within one's control to authorize or modify, one cannot make a *tnai* on an *aveirah*, and therefore cannot appoint a *shaliach* to perform one. Conversely, for a *mitzvah*, the analysis differs, with a further distinction between *mitzvah she'begufu* — a *mitzvah* performed with one's own body, such as wearing *tefillin*, which cannot be performed by an agent — and other *mitzvot* that can be fulfilled through an agent.

There are three recognized exceptions to *ein shaliach lidvar aveirah*, derived from specific sources: *me'ilah*, where one sends a *shaliach* to spend *hekdesh* money; *shlichut yad*, where a *shomer* misappropriates a watched object; and the case of *dalet v'hei*, where slaughtering or selling a stolen animal can be accomplished through an agent.

## Shlichut Principles (II)

### Mishna: Meila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַשָּׁלִיחַ שֶׁעָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ — בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל. לֹא עָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ — שָׁלִיחַ מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Me'ila* 20a): With regard to **an agent who performed his mission** properly, if he was tasked to use a particular item, and the one who dispatched him forgot that it was a consecrated item, **the employer,** who dispatched him, **misused** the consecrated item and is liable, as the agent acted on his behalf. However, if the agent **did not perform his mission** properly, and the **agent misused** the consecrated item, he is liable, as once the agent deviates from his mission, he ceases to be an agent and his actions are attributable to him.

*Tnan hatam: hashaliach she'asah shelichuto, baal habayit maal* — a *mishnah* elsewhere states that if the *shaliach* performed the task he was assigned, the *baal habayit* — the owner who sent him — is liable for *me'ilah*. *Me'ilah* is the misappropriation of consecrated *hekdesh* property for personal use. This *mishnah* establishes *me'ilah* as one of the exceptions to *ein shaliach lidvar aveirah*, and the following analysis explains why.

---

## Meila Exception

### Correct Performance

If a person instructs his *shaliach* to take money — which turns out to be *hekdesh* — and purchase a watermelon, and the *shaliach* does exactly that, then *baal habayit maal* — the sender has committed *me'ilah*. The *shaliach* acted as a pure extension of the sender, executing the task precisely as assigned, and the liability flows back to the one who dispatched him.

### Deviation from Task

*Lo asah shelichuto* — if, however, the *shaliach* deviates from his instructions and purchases something other than what was requested, then *shaliach maal* — it is the *shaliach* who bears the *me'ilah* liability. By departing from the *shelichut*, the *shaliach* acts on his own volition and severs the chain of agency.

### Attribution Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דִּתְנַן: כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: ״תֵּן בָּשָׂר לָאוֹרְחִים״ וְנָתַן לָהֶם כָּבֵד, ״תֵּן כָּבֵד״ וְנָתַן לָהֶם בָּשָׂר — הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל. וְאִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כֹּל מִילְּתָא דְּמִימְּלִיךְ עֲלַהּ שָׁלִיחַ — מִינֵיהּ הוּא, לִמְעוֹל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְלָא לִמְעוֹל שָׁלִיחַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is** the ***tanna*** who **taught** this *halakha* in the mishna? **Rav Ḥisda said: The mishna** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva as we learned** in the mishna: **How so?** If the employer **said to** the agent: **Give meat to the guests, and he gave them liver;** or if he said: **Give** them **liver, and he gave them meat, the agent has misused** the consecrated item, as he deviated from his mission. **And if** this were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, didn't Rabbi Akiva say: Any matter with regard to which an agent must consult is** considered the same **type?** Since based on that criterion, liver is certainly considered meat, **let the employer** be liable **for misuse** of consecrated property **and let the agent not** be liable **for misuse** of consecrated property, as he fulfilled his mission.

*Man tana?* — the *gemara* asks which *tanna* is the author of this *mishnah* on *me'ilah*. *Amar Rav Chisda matnitin lo ke-Rabbi Akiva* — Rav Chisda suggests it does not fit Rabbi Akiva's position, citing another *mishnah*: *keitzad amar lo ten basar le'orchin venatan lahem kaved* — if the householder instructed his steward to serve meat to guests and the steward served liver instead, or vice versa, *hashaliach maal* — the *shaliach* has violated the *shelichut*. Here "maal" may not strictly denote *me'ilah* in the technical sense; it means the *shaliach* has transgressed the terms of the agency.

This case appears to cut against Rabbi Akiva's position. Under Rabbi Akiva's framework, the *shaliach*'s act of returning to clarify — "they had no meat, but they did have liver" — would indicate that liver is *within* the category of meat, making the *shaliach*'s independent substitution irrelevant. But here, serving liver when meat was requested is treated as a deviation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, מִי לָא מוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דִּצְרִיךְ אִימְּלוֹכֵי? אִיתְּמַר שְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר לְהוֹן: שַׁפִּיר אָמַר נַחְמָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, doesn't Rabbi Akiva concede that** the agent **must consult** his employer? Because he failed to do so and acted on his own, he is not considered to have performed his mission. This *halakha* **was stated before Rava. He said to** those who stated the *halakha* before him: **Naḥmani,** i.e., Abaye, **spoke well.**

The *gemara* responds: *ve'i Rabbi Akiva, ha amar Rabbi Akiva kol milta d'mimlecha hashaliach minei* — Rabbi Akiva can still maintain his position, because his principle applies only when the *shaliach* actually returns to check. In this case, the *shaliach* did not go back and ask; he simply substituted on his own initiative. The act of seeking clarification is what does the classificatory work in Rabbi Akiva's system. Without that act, no inference about categorical inclusion can be drawn.

Abaye elaborates: *modeh Rabbi Akiva de-tzarich imlucha* — Rabbi Akiva concedes that the *shaliach* must actually have gone back and inquired. The *mishnah* therefore fits Rabbi Akiva's framework after all: since the steward substituted without checking, he bears the liability. Had he returned and asked "you asked for meat — shall I give them liver, since that is all available?" and that inquiry been made and addressed, the liver would have been classified as falling within the requested category. The inquiry itself is the operative act; its absence means the *shaliach* acted unilaterally. *Amar Rava: shapir amar Nachmani* — Rava affirmed Abaye's analysis, referring to him by his alternate name *Nachmani*, a name associated with Abaye's adoptive family connection to *Rabbah bar Nachmani*.

## Meat vs. Poultry

### Rabban Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַבָּשָׂר — אָסוּר בְּכׇל מִינֵי בָשָׂר, וְאָסוּר בָּרֹאשׁ וּבָרַגְלַיִם וּבַקָּנֶה וּבַכָּבֵד וּבַלֵּב, וּבְעוֹפוֹת, וּמוּתָּר בִּבְשַׂר דָּגִים וַחֲגָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna* who disagrees with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva** in the mishna here? The Gemara answers: **It is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as it is taught** in a dispute in the *baraita*: For **one who vows** that **meat** is forbidden **to** him, it **is prohibited** to eat **all types of meat, and** it **is prohibited** for him to eat meat **of the head, and of the feet, and of the windpipe, and of the liver, and of the heart,** although people do not typically eat meat from those parts of the body. **And** it is prohibited for him to eat meat **of birds,** as it too is popularly called meat. **However,** it **is permitted** for him to eat **of the meat of fish and grasshoppers,** as their flesh is not called meat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַבָּשָׂר — אָסוּר בְּכׇל מִינֵי בָשָׂר, וּמוּתָּר בָּרֹאשׁ וּבָרַגְלַיִם וּבַקָּנֶה וּבַכָּבֵד וּבַלֵּב וּבְעוֹפוֹת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּשַׂר דָּגִים וַחֲגָבִים. וְכֵן הָיָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: קְרָבַיִם לָאו בָּשָׂר, וְאוֹכְלֵיהֶן לָאו בַּר אִינָשׁ. אוֹכְלֵיהֶן כְּבָשָׂר, לְעִנְיַן זְבִינֵי — לָאו בַּר אִינָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One who vows** that **meat** is forbidden **to** him, **is forbidden in all types of meat, and is permitted** to eat meat **of the head, and of the feet, and of the windpipe, and of the liver, and of the heart and of birds, and needless to say** he may also partake of **fish and grasshoppers. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would likewise say: Innards are not** considered **meat, and one who eats them is not a person,** meaning that the innards are not fit for human consumption. The Gemara elaborates: With regard to **one who eats them,** in terms of the *halakhot* related to their consumption, e.g., vows, they are considered **as meat.** However, **with regard to purchase,** one who purchases them **is not a person.** In any case, apparently, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees with Rabbi Akiva, as he maintains that although if an agent fails to find meat he is required to consult his employer before replacing it with liver, it is not considered meat with regard to vows.

*Man tana de'palig alei de'Rabbi Akiva?* — the *gemara* asks who is the *tanna* in our *mishnah* who disagrees with Rabbi Akiva, holding that *diluim* are excluded from a *neder* against *yerek*. The answer is Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as established by a *baraita*: *hanoder min habasar, assur be'kol minei basar* — one who takes a *neder* against meat is prohibited from all types of meat. The *baraita* enumerates: the head, the legs, the trachea (*kaneh*), the liver, the heart, poultry — all are included. Fish and locusts, however, are permitted.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position is stricter in one respect and narrower in another: *hanoder min habasar, assur be'kol minei basar, umutar be'rosh u'be'raglayim u'be'kaneh u'be'kaved u'be'lev u'be'ofot, ve'ein tzarich lomar basar dagim vachagavim* — he holds that a *neder* against *basar* applies only to red meat proper, excluding offal, poultry, and obviously fish and locusts. The word *basar*, for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, means beef or lamb — what one would colloquially call meat — and not the broader category of *fleishig*.

This *baraita* is discussed in the context of *Masechet Chullin* as well, in *perek kol habasar*, which treats the question of which meats are prohibited together with milk. The appearance of this material in that *masechet* alongside its treatment here in *Nedarim* is itself noteworthy.

### Offal Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא בְּשַׂר עוֹף לְתַנָּא קַמָּא דַּאֲסִיר — דַּעֲבִיד שְׁלִיחָא דְּמִימְּלִיךְ עֲלֵיהּ. בְּשַׂר דָּגִים נָמֵי עָבֵיד שְׁלִיחָא, דְּאִי לָא מַשְׁכַּח בִּישְׂרָא, מִימְּלִיךְ עֲלֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: אִי לָא מַשְׁכַּחְנָא בִּישְׂרָא אַיְיתֵי דָּגִים, וְלִיתַּסְרוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the meat of a bird according to the first *tanna*, that he prohibits it** since it is considered meat, due to the fact **that** when **the agent** fails to find meat, **he tends to consult** his employer **about it?** The same should be true of the **meat of fish too. If the agent does not find meat, he tends to consult** his employer **about it, as he says: If I do not find meat, should I bring fish? And** therefore, **let** fish also **be forbidden** according to the first *tanna*.

*Keravim lav basar* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel states that organ meats are not *basar* in the relevant sense. He goes further: *veochleihem lav bar inish* — those who eat them are not acting as civilized people. This reflects both a socioeconomic sensibility and a cultural one. In societies where resources are scarce, people consume organ meats out of necessity; those from more affluent backgrounds may view such consumption as beneath the standard of *bar inish*. The comment is worth reading in its social context — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel came from a prominent family, and his characterization reflects the tastes and norms of that milieu. Nonetheless, the *halachic* point stands independent of the social commentary.

The *gemara* adds: *ochleihem kevasar leinyan zvini* — those who eat them are treated as having purchased meat *for purposes of commerce*. If a *shaliach* is sent to purchase meat and returns with offal, that constitutes a departure from the *shelichut* — it is not the meat that was intended.

### Poultry vs. Fish Q

The question is then asked: *Mai shna basar of?* — why should poultry be treated differently from fish regarding a *neder* against *basar*? According to the *tanna kamma*, poultry is included within such a *neder* while fish is not. But a *shaliach* sent for meat might just as plausibly return and report the availability of fish as of poultry. If the *shaliach*'s report-back is the operative criterion for categorical inclusion, why does the *tanna kamma* draw the line between poultry and fish rather than including both or excluding both?

## Bloodletting Context

### Abaye Answer 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִקִּיז דָּם, דְּלָא אָכֵיל דָּגִים. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ עוֹפוֹת נָמֵי לָא אָכֵיל. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דִּמְסוֹכַר וְאָכֵיל בִּישְׂרָא דְצִפְּרָא — פָּרַח לִבֵּיהּ כְּצִפְּרָא! וְתַנְיָא: אֵין מַקִּיזִין לֹא עַל דָּגִים, וְלֹא עַל עוֹפוֹת, וְלֹא עַל בָּשָׂר מָלִיחַ. וְתַנְיָא: הִקִּיז דָּם — לֹא יֹאכַל לֹא חָלָב, וְלֹא גְּבִינָה, וְלֹא בֵּיצִים, וְלֹא שַׁחֲלַיִים, וְלֹא עוֹפוֹת, וְלֹא בָּשָׂר מָלִיחַ! שָׁאנֵי עוֹפוֹת, דְּאֶפְשָׁר עַל יְדֵי שְׁלִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is referring to a case **where he let** his **blood** when he vowed, **as** a person in that condition **does not eat fish.** It was common knowledge then that eating fish after bloodletting is harmful. The Gemara asks: **If so, he** would **not eat birds either, as Shmuel said:** With regard to one **who lets blood and eats the meat of a bird, his heart** rate accelerates and **flies like a bird.** Clearly, bird meat too is deleterious for his health. **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One neither lets blood before** eating **fish, nor before** eating **birds, nor before** eating **salted meat. And it is taught** in another *baraita*: If one **let blood, he may eat neither milk, nor cheese, nor eggs, nor cress, nor birds, nor salted meat.** The Gemara answers: Meat of **birds is different, as** it **is possible** to eat it safely after bloodletting **by means of** thoroughly **boiling** it.

*Amar Abaye kegon sheheikiz dam d'lo achil dagei* — Abaye's first answer is that the *mishnah* is addressing the specific context of *hakazat dam*, bloodletting. After a bloodletting, a person should not eat fish. Accordingly, if a *shaliach* were sent to procure food after a bloodletting, and he returned to report that only fish was available, no one would take that as a meaningful clarification — the person would not have wanted fish regardless. With poultry, the situation is different: a person who has had a bloodletting *can* eat poultry under certain conditions (as will be elaborated), and therefore a *shaliach*'s return to specify that poultry is available does constitute a meaningful clarification within the category.

### Shmuel's Rule

*D'amar Shmuel d'misukar v'achil bisra d'tsafra parach libei k'tsafra* — Shmuel states that a person in a certain vulnerable medical state who eats meat in the morning will find that his heart flutters like a bird. This is understood as referring to heart palpitations — a warning against eating poultry after *hakazat dam*. The *gemara* then challenges Abaye: if Shmuel says that even poultry causes heart palpitations after bloodletting, what distinguishes poultry from fish in this context? If both are medically problematic, why would the *shaliach* clarify about poultry but not fish?

### Braita: Hakazat Dam

A *baraita* provides a comprehensive list of foods to avoid after *hakazat dam*: *hekiz dam lo yochal lo chalav velo gevinah velo beitzim velo shachalayim velo ofot velo basar maliach* — after bloodletting, one should not eat milk, cheese, eggs, *shachalayim* (a small leafy vegetable, possibly cress), poultry, or salted meat. The *baraita* also teaches: *ein makizin lo al hadagim velo al haofot* — do not perform bloodletting [and then eat] fish or poultry. The general principle seems to be one of dietary restriction and care for the recovering body — eat simply, eat fresh, and avoid straining the system.

The question has been raised whether a modern blood donation carries the same *halachic* status as the *hakazat dam* discussed in the *gemara*, and whether the dietary restrictions would therefore apply. One consideration pointing toward exclusion is that a modern blood donation is not a therapeutic procedure performed for the donor's own benefit, and unlike classical bloodletting, it does not leave the body in the same depleted medical state. There is also a practical consideration: one should not discourage people from donating blood, which is of vital communal importance. In any case, the *Rambam* discusses these dietary guidelines in *Hilchot De'ot*, situating them as medical advice within a broader framework of healthful living based on the humoral medicine of his era.

### Abaye Answer 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּכָיְיבִין לֵיהּ עֵינֵיהּ, דְּדָגִים קָשִׁים לָעֵינַיִם. אִי הָכִי אָכֵיל דָּגִים, דְּהָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נוּן סָמֶךְ עַיִן — נוּנָא סַמָּא לְעֵינַיִם! הָהוּא סוֹף אוּכְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is referring to a case **where his eyes hurt him, as fish are harmful for eyes.** Therefore, meat of birds is permitted, but not fish. The Gemara asks: **If so,** and he is suffering from eye pain, he should **eat fish, as Shmuel said** an acronym: ***Nun*, *samekh*, *ayin*,** which stands for: ***Nuna samma la'einayim*,** which means: Fish is a medicine for eyes. The Gemara answers: **That** statement of Shmuel is referring to the **latter** stages **of the** eye **infection.**

Abaye now offers a second answer to the question of why *tanna kamma* distinguishes between poultry and fish. *Abaye amar kegon dechaivin lei eineih* — the context here is someone whose eyes are in pain. *Dagim kashim la'einayim* — fish is harmful for painful eyes. And therefore, if a *shaliach* is sent on behalf of someone with eye pain, and returns to report that fish is available, the person would have no interest in fish — so the clarification would be pointless. With poultry, there is no such constraint, and the *shaliach*'s report-back remains meaningful.

Furthermore, the distinction between poultry and fish in the *hakazat dam* context resolves along a similar axis: *shani ofot de'efshar al yedei shelikah* — poultry is different from fish in that if it is prepared *shaluk*, stewed or braised, it may be consumed after bloodletting. Fish, by contrast, is entirely prohibited following *hakazat dam*. This distinction explains why the *tanna kamma* draws the line where he does.

### Fish & Eyes Remedy

The *gemara* adds a striking counterpoint from Shmuel: *nuna sama l'eina* — fish is a remedy for the eyes. Shmuel derived this from the sequence of Hebrew letters *nun, samech, ayin* — which spell *nuna sama l'eina*, fish is medicine for the eye. The apparent contradiction — fish is both harmful and beneficial for the eyes — is resolved by distinguishing stages of the condition: fish is harmful when the eyes are actively in pain, but serves as a preventative or restorative once the acute phase has passed.

The *Chida* applies this *gemara* in a broader *kabbalistic* context, noting that *nuna sama l'eina* has been taken to mean that fish protects against *ayin hara*, the evil eye, and connects this to the widespread custom of eating fish on *Shabbat*. He also records a practice of reciting the eleven *pesukim* in *Tanakh* that both begin and end with the letter *nun*, as a protective measure against *ayin hara* — drawing directly on this *gemara*'s equation of *nun* with protection for the eye. With this, we leave off and will continue from here.
