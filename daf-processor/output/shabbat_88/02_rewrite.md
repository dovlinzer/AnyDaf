# Shabbat 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Days of Separation

### Intro & Debate

Daf 88 is perhaps the major daf in the Gemara dealing with *aggadata* on *Har Sinai*, and it is accordingly often studied on *Shavuot*. The daf opens by wrapping up the debate between Rabbi Yosi and the *Rabbanan* that occupied much of the previous daf. The point of departure for that debate bears restating: everyone agrees that the Torah was given on *Shabbat*, and everyone agrees that *Bnei Yisrael* arrived at *Har Sinai* on the first of *Sivan*. The dispute concerns whether they arrived on a Sunday or a Monday — and correspondingly, whether the Torah was given on the sixth or the seventh of *Sivan* — and it is bound up with the further question of whether there were two or three days of separation (*havdalah* between husbands and wives) prior to *matan Torah*.

### Two vs. Three Days

As established on the previous daf, two points are worth keeping in mind. First, the plain reading of the Torah itself supports two days of separation — *hayom umachar*, "today and tomorrow" — while Rabbi Yosi introduces the striking position that Moshe added a third day on his own initiative, so that although God had intended to give the Torah on Friday, it was ultimately given on *Shabbat* due to Moshe's decision. Second, as noted by *Tosafot* and a passage from the Maharal, both opinions actually converge on the conclusion that the Torah was given on the fifty-first day of the *omer*.

### 51st Day Omer

This follows from the shared premise that *Bnei Yisrael* left *Mitzrayim* on a Thursday, meaning that the first day of the *omer* (had they been counting) would have been a Friday. Counting from Friday: days 1, 8, 15, 22, 29, 36, 43, and 50 all fall on Friday. *Shavuot* — the fiftieth day — would therefore have been a Friday, yet both opinions agree the Torah was given on *Shabbat*, making it the fifty-first day of the *omer*. This raises the obvious question of why we celebrate *Shavuot* as *zman matan torateinu* on the fiftieth day if the Torah was actually received on the fifty-first — a question the Maharal addresses directly, as we shall see.

### Seder Olam Braita

The Gemara opens with a *ta shma* — a *braita* from *Seder Olam*, the *Tannaitic* work that establishes the chronology of events in the Torah. The *braita* states: *Nisan shebo yatzu Yisrael miMitzrayim* — *Nisan* is the month when *Bnei Yisrael* left *Mitzrayim*; they slaughtered the *Pesach* on the fourteenth and departed on the fifteenth. It then specifies: *ve'oto hayom erev Shabbat hayah* — that day, the fifteenth of *Nisan*, was a Friday. If the fifteenth of *Nisan* was a Friday, then the first of *Nisan* was also a Friday. Since *Nisan* is always *malei* (a full month of thirty days), *Rosh Chodesh Iyar* falls two days after *Rosh Chodesh Nisan*, placing it on a Sunday. *Iyar* is a *chaser* (a deficient month of twenty-nine days), so *Rosh Chodesh Sivan* falls one day after the end of *Iyar*, placing it on a Monday — precisely the position of the *Rabbanan*, who hold that *Rosh Chodesh Sivan* was a Monday, the Torah was given on *Shabbat*, and there were only two days of separation.

This constitutes a challenge to Rabbi Yosi: *kashya le-Rabbi Yosi*. The Gemara resolves it simply: Rabbi Yosi will say *ha meni Rabbanan hi* — that *braita* reflects the position of the *Rabbanan*, not his own. Rabbi Yosi holds that *Rosh Chodesh Nisan* fell on a Sunday rather than a Friday.

### Moshe's Ascents

The Gemara then brings a *ta shma* that cites Rabbi Yosi himself, giving his own account of Moshe's ascents and descents. On the second of *Sivan* — Monday, the day after they arrived — Moshe ascended and descended, and God told him *atem tihyu li mamlechet kohanim*. On Tuesday, the third, he ascended and descended again; according to the earlier Gemara, this was also when God gave him the *mitzvah* of *hagbalah*, establishing a boundary around the mountain. On the fourth — Wednesday — he ascended and descended, and this was when God commanded the separation of husbands and wives, giving Rabbi Yosi his three days of separation before the Torah was given on *Shabbat*. (The Gemara briefly interjects to correct the formulation: the text initially says he descended on the fourth without having gone up, and the Gemara amends it to read that he went up and came down on the fourth.)

### Mizbeach & Shabbat

On the fifth day — Thursday — Moshe built a *mizbeiach*. This may seem puzzling, since there is no mention of an altar in the narrative of *Parashat Yitro*. The reference is to the end of *Parashat Mishpatim*, where the Torah describes Moshe building an altar, sprinkling blood, and declaring *zeh dam ha-brit*, and where *Bnei Yisrael* proclaimed *na'aseh v'nishma*. According to many approaches in the Gemara, this entire episode — the altar, the blood, the covenant, and *na'aseh v'nishma* — took place before *matan Torah*, not after, and this *braita* is proceeding on that assumption. So on Thursday Moshe built the *mizbeiach*, brought the *korban*, and the entire ceremony of *na'aseh v'nishma* occurred.

Then: *beshishi lo haya lo pnai* — on Friday he had no time. The Gemara asks what this means: was Friday the day the Torah was given? That would be a curious way to express it. The Gemara rejects this reading: *lo mishum tarchah d'Shabta* — he was occupied with preparations for *Shabbat*. This is entirely consistent with Rabbi Yosi's position, since the Torah would then have been given the following day, on *Shabbat* itself.

### Galilean Sermon

A certain Galilean, the Gemara relates, rose to deliver a *drasha* before Rav Chisda. He opened with what appears to be a stylized homiletical formula — a kind of riddle — and declared: *brich Rachmana d'yahiv oryan tlitai* — blessed is God who gave the tripartite Torah (*Torah*, *Neviim*, and *Ketuvim*) to a tripartite people (*Kohanim*, *Leviim*, and *Yisraelim*), through a third-born — Moshe was the third among his siblings, after Aharon and Miriam — on the third day, *b'yom tlitai* — meaning the third day of separation — in the third month, *b'yarcha tlitai*, the month of *Sivan*. The Gemara then asks: *keman* — whose opinion does this reflect? *K'rabbanan* — like the *Rabbanan*, who hold that the Torah was given on the third day of separation, with only two days before it, not three days of separation followed by a fourth day. With this, the Gemara concludes its treatment of the two-day versus three-day question.

One further point deserves mention in this context. A student raises the pointed observation that the *Seder Olam* — which the Gemara cited as reflecting the position of the *Rabbanan* — was in fact authored by Rabbi Yosi himself, as the Gemara elsewhere states (*stam Seder Olam Rabbi Yosi*). This is a genuine difficulty: if the *Seder Olam* is Rabbi Yosi's own work, how can it be cited as evidence against him? The question does not admit of a simple answer, and the discussion moves on — but it serves as a fitting segue to the Maharal's treatment of the fifty-first day.

### Maharal Matan/Kabbalat

In his *Sefer Tiferet Yisrael*, the Maharal addresses both the question of the fifty-first day and the concept of *yom echad hosif Moshe mi-da'ato* — Moshe's adding of a day on his own initiative. On the latter point, the Maharal writes:

> *Vadai kol ma she-hotziu chachamim min ha-katuv al yedei drasha she-lahem nikra kol divrei chachamim ve-da'atam af she-hu nirmaz ba-Torah ve-hu min ha-Torah mi-kol makom hem hotziu ha-perush mi-chochmatam ve-da'atam.*

Certainly everything that the Sages derived from Scripture through their own *drasha* is called the words of the Sages and their understanding — even though it is hinted at in the Torah and is *min ha-Torah*, nonetheless they drew out the interpretation through their own wisdom and understanding. The Maharal is here deliberately emphasizing what is sometimes downplayed: the genuine human component of *Torah she-ba'al peh*. Even where the *Sages* derive something from *psukim* with *de-Oraita* binding force, they are exercising their own intellectual creativity and judgment — and he connects this directly to the idea of Moshe adding a day on his own initiative, with God's subsequent assent.

On the fifty-first day, the Maharal writes:

> *Ve-anu omrim be-Atzeret zman matan Toratenu u-levadai de-Rabbanan havei shapir mishum de-lehu avei yom vav be-Sivan.*

Our formula of *zman matan Toratenu* at *Atzeret* works well at least according to the *Rabbanan*, since for them the Torah was given on the sixth of *Sivan*. But according to Rabbi Yosi, who holds it was given on the seventh of *Sivan*, calling it *zman matan Torah* is problematic.

He then makes a crucial distinction. According to Rabbi Yosi, the *matan Torah* — God's giving — took place on the fiftieth day; it was only the *kabbalah*, the actual receiving, that occurred on the fifty-first. As the Maharal writes:

> *Aval ha-mikabel omer zman matan Torah be-yom nun rak she-lo higia Torah el ha-mikabel rak be-yom nun aleph u-mi-kol makom zman matan Torah hu be-yom chamishim.*

The *zman matan Torah* is the fiftieth day — that is when God intended to give it — but the Torah did not actually reach the receiver until the fifty-first day. The Maharal adds:

> *U-ve-zeh tavin divrei Rabbi Yossi she-amar yom chamishim ve-yom shishi she-ein mashmao rak shishi be-Sivan be-chinat mitzad ha-noten.*

We call it *zman matan Torah*, not *zman kabbalat ha-Torah*. God planned to give the Torah on the fiftieth day; it was Moshe who said they were not yet ready to receive it and added a day. So the *giving* is the fiftieth, while the *receiving* is the day after. This dovetails beautifully with the human component of *Moshe hosif yom echad mi-da'ato*: the Torah requires a receiver, and the partnership between the divine giving and the human capacity to receive means that the two events need not coincide. We celebrate the day God wanted to give the Torah — and this also accommodates the two days of *Yom Tov* in the *Diaspora* rather elegantly, since we were only ready to receive it the following day.

## Kafa Har Kegigit

### Barrel Over Mountain

The Gemara now turns to two famous *aggadot* concerning *kabbalat ha-Torah*. The first begins with the verse *vayityatzvu be-tachtit ha-har* — they stood at the foot of the mountain. Rav Avdimi bar Chama bar Chisda reads *be-tachtit* not merely as "at the foot of" but as *tachat* — underneath — and derives: *she-kafa ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu aleihem et ha-har ke-gigit* — God lifted the mountain over them like a barrel. They were standing underneath it, with the mountain raised above them like a cask ready to be overturned. God then said to them: *im atem mekablim et ha-Torah mutav* — if you accept the Torah, good — *ve-im lav, sham tehe kevuratchem* — and if not, this will be your burial place. You have no choice.

### Modaa Objection

To this, Rav Acha bar Yaakov raises an objection: *mikan moda'ah rabbah le-Oraita* — from here there is a great legal disclaimer against the Torah. A *moda'ah* is the mechanism by which a person who is being compelled to sign a contract takes witnesses aside beforehand and informs them of the coercion; the testimony of those witnesses can later be used to void the contract. So here: if *Bnei Yisrael* were forced to accept the Torah under duress, there is a standing basis to annul the obligation. We never willingly accepted it.

### Purim Acceptance

Rava responds: *af al pi chen hadar kibluha b'imei Achashverosh* — nonetheless, they accepted it again in the days of *Achashverosh*. As the verse says, *kimu v'kiblu ha-Yehudim* — they fulfilled and accepted. The double language — *kimu* and *kiblu* — is read as: *kimu mah shekiblu kvar* — they ratified what they had previously accepted under coercion. At *Purim*, the acceptance was fully voluntary, retrospectively validating the original covenant.

### Narrative Problem

*Tosafot* raises a narrative difficulty with Rav Avdimi's reading. Before the verse about standing at the foot of the mountain, the Torah already records *Bnei Yisrael* declaring *kol asher diber Hashem na'aseh* — we will do all that God has spoken. And according to many readings, the entire ceremony at the end of *Parashat Mishpatim* — the *Sefer Ha-Brit*, the declaration of *na'aseh v'nishma*, the *brit* — all took place before *matan Torah* as well. If so, how can the acceptance be described as coerced? They had willingly committed themselves beforehand.

### Free Will Issue

Setting *Tosafot*'s narrative question aside for a moment, the deeper issue the Gemara is grappling with is this: what does it mean to be standing directly in the presence of God's commanding voice? If one truly experiences the divine presence — not merely hears about it, but encounters it directly — where is the space for free choice? How can a person genuinely exercise their human independence and willingly say yes or no when God's commanding voice is present in full force, accompanied by the mountain and the fire and the thunder and the lightning? Even bracketing the spectacle, if they truly understood that they were experiencing God's own voice commanding them, how could they have done anything but say yes?

What the Gemara is doing, then, is giving expression to this deep problem. The *kafah aleihem har ke-gigit* is not simply a description of physical compulsion; it is a way of articulating that in the direct presence of God's commanding voice, free choice is, in a meaningful sense, extinguished. This, in fact, also gives the *Tosafot* solution its power: the *na'aseh v'nishma* at the end of *Mishpatim* preceded the moment of *matan Torah*, so their prior agreements were made before they stood in the full force of the divine presence. When they finally heard the content of the Torah — standing at the mountain with God's voice directly addressing them — what kind of choice did they truly have? It was as if the mountain had been lifted over them.

*Purim* serves as the precise counterpart. At *Har Sinai* it was impossible to deny God's commanding presence; one could not look away from the divine voice. At *Purim*, the opposite conditions prevailed: God's name does not appear anywhere in the *Megillah*, the *Beit HaMikdash* had been destroyed, and it would have been entirely plausible to conclude that God had abandoned His people. Many Jews at that time, as *Chazal* suggest, may well have believed exactly that. To assert in those conditions that they still saw God's hand, that this was a *nes*, that they remained committed to the *brit* — that is a genuine, fully voluntary act of faith, the exact diametric opposite of *matan Torah*. There, full divine presence made free choice nearly impossible. At *Purim*, apparent divine absence made denial entirely possible — and yet they chose affirmation. That is the willing acceptance that retroactively validates the original covenant.

### Necessity & Will

The Maharal, whose treatment of this passage was reviewed before the shiur, raises a further question: if God was going to compel them anyway, what was the point of the *na'aseh v'nishma*? Why do both elements — the *kafah har ke-gigit* and the willing commitment — appear side by side?

His answer operates on several levels. First, the Maharal connects this to the upcoming *midrash* — which the Gemara introduces momentarily — that the entire *ma'aseh bereishit* was contingent upon *Bnei Yisrael* accepting the Torah. The Torah preceded the creation of the world; it was part of the foundational divine plan that *Bnei Yisrael* would receive the Torah at *Har Sinai*. This was not merely one possible outcome among others; it was a necessary element of the fabric of reality. In order to express that necessity — to assert that this would happen regardless, as an inviolable part of the divine design — the *kafah har ke-gigit* was required. It manifests the non-contingent character of the *brit*.

At the same time, you want the commitment to be a genuine, willing engagement — and so the *na'aseh* is also necessary. But here another consideration enters. The Maharal points out that if the covenant were understood as purely voluntary, it would cut both ways: one might reason, "I entered it willingly, I can leave it willingly." That is precisely the mirror image of the *modaa rabbah* problem — not "I was forced, so I'm not bound," but "I chose freely, and I can unchoose freely." The *kafah har ke-gigit* forecloses that exit: regardless of what you say, God is telling you that you must do it; at some level you have no choice in the matter. The two elements together — the willing *na'aseh* and the compelled *kafah* — create a structure in which neither avenue of evasion is available. You cannot say you were coerced into something that doesn't bind you, nor can you say that because it was your choice it can be undone by your choice.

This points to the fundamental dialectic between *tzivui* and *brit* that runs through *maamad Har Sinai* and the Torah as a whole. *Lo yiheyeh lecha*, *Anochi Hashem Elokecha* — these are *mitzvot*, divine commands. The surrounding narrative of *sefer ha-brit* and *na'aseh v'nishma* is the language of *brit*, of a mutually entered covenant. The Gemara here is grappling seriously with both of these dimensions and their tension.

## Creation & Torah

### Extra Hei

The Gemara then turns to Resh Lakish's *drasha* on the verse *vayehi erev vayehi voker yom ha-shishi* — and there was evening and there was morning, the sixth day. The extra *hey* in *yom ha-shishi* — not found at the other days of creation — requires explanation. Resh Lakish teaches: *melamed she-hitnah ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu im ma'aseh bereishit* — this teaches that God stipulated with the *ma'aseh bereishit*, with all of creation, and said to them: *im Yisrael mekablim et ha-Torah atem mitkaymin* — if the Jews accept the Torah, you will endure; *ve-im lav, ani machzir etchem le-tohu va-vohu* — and if not, I will return you to waste and nothingness. The *hey* of *yom ha-shishi* refers to the sixth of *Sivan* — the day of *matan Torah*, at least according to the *Rabbanan*.

### Suspended Creation

The entire edifice of creation, then, was suspended upon *Bnei Yisrael*'s acceptance of the Torah. As noted, this ties directly back into the *kafah har ke-gigit*: because the Torah's reception was a necessary condition for the world's continued existence, it could not be left as a merely contingent event. That is why the Maharal explains that *kafah har ke-gigit* was needed — to give expression to the necessity of the Torah's acceptance as part of the very structure of reality.

It is also worth noting that the *hey* of *yom ha-shishi* — the sixth day — appears in the *Kiddush* recited on Friday night at the very beginning, and the connection to *matan Torah* on the sixth of *Sivan* gives that word a particular resonance.

## Naaseh V'Nishma

### Rabbi Simai

From the *kafah har ke-gigit* — the compelled acceptance — the Gemara now transitions to the willing acceptance, the *na'aseh v'nishma*. Rabbi Simai expounds: *be-sha'ah she-hikdimu Yisrael na'aseh le-nishma* — when Israel placed *na'aseh* before *nishma*, when they said "we will do" before "we will hear," something remarkable occurred.

### Angel Crowns

Sixty myriads of ministering angels — *shishim ribo malachei ha-sharet* — descended, one for each of the six hundred thousand men of *Bnei Yisrael*, and each angel placed two crowns upon the head of each Israelite: *echad keneged na'aseh ve-echad keneged nishma* — one crown for *na'aseh* and one for *nishma*.

### Golden Calf Loss

When *Bnei Yisrael* sinned and made the *egel*, 1.2 million angels of destruction — *malachei chabalah* — descended and removed the crowns. The disparity in numbers is explained by the principle that the *midah tovah* is more potent than the *midah pur'anuyot*: one angel of blessing could bestow two crowns, but the angels of destruction being weaker each could remove only one. The verse *vayitnatzlu Bnei Yisrael et edyam mehar Chorev* — they stripped themselves of their jewelry at *Har Chorev* — is read by the Gemara as a reference to this very event. Rabbi Chama bar Chanina explains: at *Chorev* they wore the crowns (*bechorev ta'anu*), and at *Chorev* they were removed (*uvechorev parku*). The verse about removing jewelry implies they had received it — and the Gemara establishes that they received it when they declared *na'aseh v'nishma*.

### Moshe's Inheritance

Rabbi Yochanan then teaches: *ve-chulam zachah Moshe u-netalan* — Moshe merited all of those crowns and took them for himself. The proof text cited is the verse *u-Moshe yikach et ha-ohel* — Moshe took the tent — and Rashi understands this as a reference to the shining of Moshe's face, which was the outward manifestation of these crowns. Having remained faithful while all others sinned, all the crowns accrued to him alone.

### Future Return

Resh Lakish adds: *atid ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-hachziram lanu* — in the future God will return them to us. The proof text is *u-fduyei Hashem yeshuvun u-va'u Tziyon be-rinah* — those redeemed by God will return and come to Zion with rejoicing — *ve-simchat olam al rosham* — and the joy of eternity will be upon their heads. The word *al rosham* — upon their heads — is read as an allusion to the crowns they will once again wear; the *simchah* they had from time immemorial will again rest upon their heads.

### Apple Metaphor

Rabbi Elazar adds another dimension to the glory of *na'aseh v'nishma*. A *bat kol* — a heavenly voice — came forth and declared: "Who revealed to My children this secret that the ministering angels use?" The proof text is *barchu Hashem malachav giborei koach osei devaro lishmoa bekol devaro* — *bless God, His angels, mighty of strength, who do His word, to hear the voice of His word*. The angels first *do* — *osei devaro* — and then *hear* — *lishmoa*. *Na'aseh* before *nishma* is the pattern of the angels.

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina then derives the same lesson from *Shir HaShirim*: *ke-tapuach be-atzei ha-ya'ar* — like an apple among the trees of the forest, so is my beloved. Why are *Yisrael* compared to an apple? Because *mah tapuach zeh piryo kodem le-alav* — just as an apple's fruit precedes its leaves, so too Israel placed *na'aseh* before *nishma*. (It should be noted that *Rabbeinu Tam* already pointed out that apples were not a Mediterranean fruit and that *tapuach* in *Shir HaShirim* likely refers to the *etrog*, which retains its fruit for multiple years such that the previous year's fruit is still present before the new year's leaves have emerged.)

## Heretic Challenge

### Impetuous Accusation

A certain *min* — a heretic, with the original *girsa* being *min* rather than *Tzeduki*, most likely referring to a Jewish Christian — saw Rava so deeply engrossed in study that he had placed his fingers beneath his feet and, without realizing it, was squeezing them so hard that blood was flowing. The *min* addressed him with a taunt: *ama peziza* — an impetuous nation. *De-kadmitu pumaichu le-udnaichu* — you put your mouth before your ears. You agreed to do before you even agreed to listen. *Achti bi-fzizutaichu kayimitu* — you're still with that same impetuousness, as evidenced by Rava sitting here cutting off his fingers without noticing, so caught up is he in what he is doing without attending to what is happening.

*Breisha ibei lachem lemishma, ei matzitu kablitu, ve-ei lo lo kablitu* — first you should have listened, and if you were capable of accepting it, accept it; if not, don't. Proceed rationally, deliberately; don't commit before you understand what you're committing to.

If this *min* is indeed a Jewish Christian — as is typical of the *min* stories in the Talmud — there is likely an additional subtext. The challenge would carry an implicit theological argument: the problem with *na'aseh v'nishma* is precisely that it was impetuous, unreasoned, and therefore ultimately unsustainable and misguided; you should have thought it through, and had you done so, you might have recognized that the law in its Jewish form is not the ideal.

### Rava's Defense

Rava responds: *anan de-sarginan bi-shleimuta* — we who walk in wholeness and complete faith in God. The verse about us is *tumat yesharim tanchem* — the integrity of the upright will guide them. We had complete trust in God; we didn't need to condition our acceptance on our own rational evaluation. If God said the Torah is what we need, we trusted God entirely. As for the others — *hanach inshi de-sarginan be-aliluta* — those who walk with complaints and pretexts — *ve-selef bogdim yeshadem* — the perversity of traitors will destroy them. The defense of *na'aseh v'nishma* is not that it was thoughtless; it is that it was an act of complete *emunat Hashem*, trusting God without the precondition that it make sense by our own criteria.

## Naaseh Meaning

### Two Approaches

What is the deeper significance of *na'aseh v'nishma*, and why is it celebrated as such an extraordinary achievement? One approach — reflected in the *bat kol* comparing it to the angels — is that it represents total submission to divine authority: we do not wait to decide whether the command makes sense; we accept it unconditionally, as the angels execute God's word before hearing its rationale. Read this way, the *nishma* becomes almost incidental — the leaf of the apple rather than the fruit.

But this reading needs to be held in tension with another dimension. The entire discussion of the *kafah har ke-gigit* versus the willing *brit*, the dialectic between *tzivui* and *brit*, continues to play out within *na'aseh v'nishma* itself. Even within the willing acceptance, Israel chose to bracket their own deliberative process and accept on the basis of trust — not a suspension of free will, but a deployment of it in a particular direction, toward unconditional commitment rather than conditional evaluation.

### Sfat Emet

The *Sfas Emes* offers two illuminating treatments of *na'aseh v'nishma*, both in *Parashat Mishpatim*. In the first, he writes:

> *Kshehikdimu na'aseh le-nishma, she-haya chaviv beneihem yoter la'asos retzono yisbarech mi-nishma she-hu hasagas ha-ta'amim.*

When they placed *na'aseh* before *nishma*, what was dearer to them was to do God's will rather than the *nishma*, which is the attainment of the *ta'amim* — the reasons and purposes behind the *mitzvot*. The *nishma* is not merely "let us decide whether to accept"; it is "let us understand why." *Ta'amei ha-mitzvot* — understanding the reasons for the commandments — adds depth, prevents rote observance, and allows one to participate more fully in the *mitzvah*. But the challenge of *ta'amei ha-mitzvot* is precisely that once you condition performance on understanding the reasons, you may not perform if you find the reasons insufficient, or if you think the reason can be satisfied in other ways.

The *Sfas Emes* therefore insists that the foundation must be *na'aseh*: you do it, prior to and independent of whether you know or like the reasons. Once that is your basic orientation, *vechein zachu lehassig ha-ta'amim* — then you will merit to attain the reasons. And the reasons, he notes, are in some sense even more essential than the physical performance: *ki ha-ta'amim yoter pnimiyim mi-guf ha-mitzvot* — the reasons are more inward than the act itself. Understanding the reasons allows you to participate in the *mitzvah* as a genuine partner, to understand its scope and apply it — this is the human element, echoing the theme of *Moshe hosif yom echad mi-da'ato*. But you cannot reach that level of partnership unless you begin with the prior, unconditional commitment of *na'aseh*.

### Post-Cheit Path

The *Sfas Emes* returns to this theme in his treatment of *Parashat Yitro*, where he cites a *midrash* from *Midrash Rabbah* that is rarely quoted. After the *cheit ha-eigel*, God says to *Bnei Yisrael*: *avartem na'aseh* — you have transgressed *na'aseh*, you said you would do and look what you did — *hizaru bi-nishma* — at least hold on to *nishma*.

The *Sfas Emes* understands this as opening a second pathway. Not everyone can begin with *na'aseh*; not everyone is in a position to first commit unconditionally and then discover meaning. Some people need to approach through the *nishma* — they need to first grapple with the reasons, understand the purpose, and through that understanding arrive at commitment. Indeed, there may be something to the critique that a *na'aseh* without sufficient grounding in *nishma* can prove fragile, as the episode of the *eigel* tragically demonstrated — the commitment was made before it was deeply internalized. For those in that situation, the path back runs through *nishma*: by attending to the reasons, to the meaning, to the understanding, one can work one's way back toward genuine *na'aseh*.

As the *Sfas Emes* writes: *she-yuchlu od le-hassig ha-shmiyah... nishar lahem le-hitdabek al yedei ha-shmiyah* — they can still attain the *shmiyah*, to cleave to the covenant through the *nishma*. Both paths — *na'aseh* leading to *nishma*, and *nishma* leading back to *na'aseh* — are legitimate, and both are necessary dimensions of the covenantal relationship.

## Bride Metaphor

### Shir HaShirim Eyes

On the second side of the daf (*88b*), Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan cites the verse from *Shir HaShirim*: *libavtini achoti kallah, libavtini be-achat me-einayich* — you have drawn my heart, my sister, my bride, with one of your eyes. The *drasha*: *be-techilah be-achat me-einayich* — at first, with just one eye, when you accepted the covenant at *Har Sinai* in words — *le-chashe-ta'aseh, bi-shtei einayich* — once you actually fulfill the *mitzvot* in deed, then with both eyes. The metaphor here is not simply about *na'aseh v'nishma* as declarations, but about the difference between the initial verbal commitment and its actualization through practice. The one eye of commitment is already beautiful; the two eyes of doing consolidate and deepen the relationship.

### Bride Adultery

Ulla then gives voice to the tragic irony: *aluvah kallah mezaneh be-toch chuppatah* — how disgraceful is a bride who commits adultery under her own *chuppah*. The juxtaposition is devastating: the angels bestowed crowns, a *bat kol* declared the glory of *na'aseh v'nishma*, *Shir HaShirim* speaks of eyes that drew God's heart — and the very first opportunity that arose after all of this, *Bnei Yisrael* made the *eigel*. Worshipping the golden calf is frequently described by *Chazal* in terms of *zenut* — *va-yiznu acharei elohei neichar* — as a betrayal of covenantal fidelity. The wedding had barely begun.

### Fragrance Endures

Rav Mari berei de-Bat Shmuel asks: where is this moment of betrayal alluded to in *Shir HaShirim*? He finds it in *ad she-ha-melech bi-mesibo, nirdi natan reicho* — while the king was at his banquet, my fragrance departed. The *ha-melech bi-mesibo* is the wedding feast at *Har Sinai*; *nirdi natan reicho* — the fragrance went away — is the sin of the *eigel*.

Yet Rav then observes: *adayin chavivuta hi gaban* — God's love for us endures even so. The proof: the verse says *natan reicho* — the fragrance departed — and does not say *hisriach* — it soured and spoiled. Even after the *cheit ha-eigel*, the relationship was not destroyed; the fragrance dissipated, but it did not rot. The *aron* remained in the *mishkan*, the poles protruding through the *parochet* in the image of intimate presence; God's dwelling among *Bnei Yisrael* continued. The *midrash* cited earlier — that after the *eigel*, *na'aseh* was compromised but the way back runs through *nishma* — finds its textual echo in the poetic language of *Shir HaShirim*: the fragrance is gone, but the beloved remains.

### Bearing Shame

The Gemara appends a *braita* connected thematically through the word *aluvah* — disgraced — that appeared in Ulla's formulation. The *braita* teaches: *aluvim ve-einam olvim* — those who are shamed and do not return the shame — *shomim cherpatam ve-einam meshivim* — who hear themselves reviled and do not respond — *osim me-ahavah u-smeichim be-yisurim* — who serve God out of love and rejoice even in affliction — regarding these the verse says: *ve-ohavav ke-tzet ha-shemesh bi-gevurato* — those who love God will be like the sun going forth in its strength. The connection to the surrounding discussion is subtle but real: the fragrance may depart, the crowns may be taken, the *na'aseh* may be violated — but the one who continues to love God through all of that, who bears the shame without retaliation and serves out of pure love, will ultimately shine with the greatest light.

## Seventy Languages

### Hammer & Rock

Returning to the *aggadot* of *Har Sinai*, the Gemara cites Rabbi Yochanan on the verse *Hashem yiten omer ha-mevaserot tzava rav* — God gives a word; those who bring tidings are a great host. The word *tzava rav* — a great host — is read as describing the divine word: *kol dibur v'dibur she-yatza mi-pi ha-gevura nechlak le-shivim leshonot* — every single word that came out of God's mouth divided itself into seventy languages. One divine utterance, but from it emerge seventy distinct linguistic expressions, each offering a different nuance of the ultimate speech of God. The divine word transcends human language; when it enters into human linguistic forms, each such form necessarily captures only a part of what that word contains. Translation is not merely a technical operation but a hermeneutical one: different listeners hear differently, and the seventy languages reflect the inexhaustibility of the divine word in its encounter with the multiplicity of human understanding.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael (*tana de-bei Rabbi Yishmael*) adds another image: *u-chfatish yefotzetz sela* — like a hammer that shatters a rock. Just as a hammer striking a rock sends out many sparks in every direction, so too every word from God's mouth divides into seventy languages — many possible interpretations and understandings radiating outward from a single divine utterance.

### Life & Death

Rav Chanana bar Pappa then asks: why are the words of Torah compared to a *nagid* — a ruler or prince? The verse says *shim'u ki negidim adaber* — listen, for I speak *negidim*. Because just as a ruler has the power to kill and to give life, so too the words of Torah have that power: *yesh bahem le-hamit u-le-hachayot* — they carry within them both death and life.

### Right & Left

Rava elaborates on this through the verse *bi-ymina orech yamim bi-smola osher ve-chavod* — in her right hand is length of days, in her left hand wealth and honor. *Le-mayminim bah* — those who approach Torah from the right, who study it *le-shem shamayim* with full self-sacrifice — *sama de-chayei* — for them it is a potion of life. *Le-masmilim bah* — those who approach from the left — *sama de-mota* — for them it is a potion of death. There is a distinction within *shelo lishmah*: there is the *shelo lishmah* of insufficient motivation, not studying for the ideal reason but without negative intent — that can still lead to *lishmah*. But there is a deeper *shelo lishmah* represented here by the left-hand approach, where Torah is engaged with a destructive or adversarial intent, as a means of attack or denial — and that brings death.

### Two Crowns Word

A further reading of *negidim* — plural, suggesting multiple crowns — connects back to the theme of the two crowns of *na'aseh v'nishma*: *kol dibur v'dibur she-yatza mi-pi ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu, koshrim lo shnei ketarim* — every divine word carries two crowns, presumably the doing and the understanding, the *na'aseh* and the *nishma*.

## Word Beauty

### Aromatic Field

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi then opens a series of *drashot* on the beauty and power of the divine word, drawing again from *Shir HaShirim*. The verse *lechayav ke-arugat ha-bosem* — His cheeks are like a bed of aromatic spices. *Kol dibur v'dibur she-yatza mi-pi ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu, nitmalei kol ha-olam kulo besamim* — every word that came out of God's mouth, the entire world filled with fragrance. This stands in striking contrast to the images of awe and terror — the mountain like a barrel, the hammer shattering rock, the souls departing — that surround it. Here the divine word does not shatter or compel; it suffuses the world with beauty, drawing the heart as *Shir HaShirim* describes, filling everything with fragrance.

The verse understood here is read against the background of the ceremony at *Har Sinai*: each of the Ten Commandments, each utterance of God's voice, was not merely a command but an aesthetic and spiritual event of overwhelming beauty.

### Clearing Fragrance

But this generates a problem: *ve-keivan she-mi-dibur rishon nitmalei, dibur sheini le-heichan halach?* — if after the first word the entire world was already filled with fragrance, how did the second word add anything in terms of fragrance? The answer: *hotzi ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ha-ruach me-otzrotav* — God brought forth the wind from His storehouse, *ve-hayah ma'avir rishon rishon* — and He caused the first fragrance to dissipate so that the unique fragrance of each subsequent word could be experienced freshly. The proof text is *shiftotav shoshanim notfot mor over* — His lips are like roses dripping with passing myrrh. Reading *al tikrei shoshanim ela she-shonim* — do not read "roses" (*shoshanim*) but "that teach" (*she-shonim*) — the verse becomes: from His lips, which teach, passes fragrance after fragrance, each word carrying its own unique scent that is allowed to be experienced as such. The fragrance passes — but not to spoil; it moves aside to make room for the next.

### Souls Depart/Return

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi continues: *kol dibur v'dibur she-yatza mi-pi ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu, yatz'ah nishmatan shel Yisrael* — at each word that came out of God's mouth, the souls of *Bnei Yisrael* departed. The proof is *nafshi yatz'ah be-dabro* — my soul went out at His speech. But if their souls departed at the first word, how did they receive the second? *Horid tal she-atid le-hachayot bo ha-meitim* — God brought down the dew that He will use in the future to resuscitate the dead — *ve-hechayah otam* — and He restored them to life. The proof: *geshem nedavot tanif Elohim nachalatecha ve-nil'ah atah konantah* — a gracious rain You bestowed upon Your inheritance, and when they were weary, You established them. The same dew destined for *techiyat ha-meitim* at the end of days was here used to resuscitate *Bnei Yisrael* between each of the utterances at *Har Sinai*.

### Twelve Mil Retreat

The awe overwhelmed them physically as well. *Chazru Yisrael le-achoreihem yod-bet mil* — *Bnei Yisrael* retreated backward twelve *mil* — the entire length of their camp — overwhelmed by the divine presence. *Ve-hayu malachei ha-sharet medadin otam* — the ministering angels had to come and walk them forward again, like one who guides a toddler taking first steps. The proof: *malchei tzeva'ot yedodun yedodun* — reading not *yedodun* ("they flee") but *yedaddun* ("they help toddle") — the angels pushed *Bnei Yisrael* back toward the mountain, steadying them as they made their way forward.

## Fackenheim Synthesis

### Paradox Presence

The shiur closes with a passage from Emil Fackenheim's *God's Presence in History*. Fackenheim cites a *midrash* closely parallel to the one just studied: Rabbi Azaryah and Rabbi Acha in the name of Rabbi Yochanan teach that when *Bnei Yisrael* heard at *Sinai* the word *Anochi*, their souls departed — as the verse says, "if we hear the voice anymore we will die." The divine word returned to God and said: "Sovereign of the universe, You are full of life, Your Torah is full of life, and You have sent me to the dead — they are all dead." Thereupon God sweetened the word for them.

Fackenheim draws from this the following formulation: the divine presence at *Sinai* is a commanding presence. Because it is commanding rather than merely saving, the overwhelming astonishment of the encounter turns into deadly terror. Such a presence is paradoxical in its very first instant: because it is commanding, it addresses human freedom; because it is the sole ultimate power, it destroys that freedom — since a finite human being standing in the direct presence of infinite divine command has no space in which to freely choose. Hence, the divine commanding presence can be genuinely commanding and genuinely present only if it is doubly present: as sole power it destroys human freedom, and as gracious power it restores that freedom and indeed exalts it — for human freedom is itself part of the covenant that divinity has established.

This formulation gives precise philosophical expression to the *aggadot* of the entire daf. The divine word causes souls to depart — it overwhelms and in a sense destroys human freedom and human capacity. Yet the same divine word brings the dew of resurrection; it sweetens itself; the angels walk the retreating *Bnei Yisrael* back. The divine presence that destroys freedom is the same divine presence that restores it and enlists it in the covenant. *Kafah har ke-gigit* and *na'aseh v'nishma*; compulsion and willing commitment; the hammer shattering rock and the fragrance filling the world — these are not contradictions to be resolved but the two faces of a single paradoxical encounter between the infinite divine will and the finite human freedom that is invited into covenant with it.
