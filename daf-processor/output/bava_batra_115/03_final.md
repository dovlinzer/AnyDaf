# Bava Batra 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mother Inherits Son

### Radical Position Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה – הָאָב יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת בְּנוֹ, וְאִשָּׁה יוֹרֶשֶׁת אֶת בְּנָהּ; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מַטּוֹת״ – מַקִּישׁ מַטֵּה הָאֵם לְמַטֵּה הָאָב, מָה מַטֵּה הָאָב – אָב יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת בְּנוֹ, אַף מַטֵּה הָאֵם – אִשָּׁה יוֹרֶשֶׁת אֶת בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the *halakha* that a mother inherits from her son, the Gemara notes that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Rabbi Shimon: By Torah law a father inherits** from **his son, and a woman inherits** from **her son** if the father is no longer alive, **as it is stated** with regard to a woman receiving inheritance: "And every daughter who possesses an inheritance from the **tribes** of the children of Israel" (Numbers 36:8). Since the plural term "tribes" includes both her father's tribe and her mother's tribe, the verse **juxtaposes** the **tribe of the mother to the tribe of the father,** in that **just as** with regard to **the father's tribe a father inherits** from **his son, so too,** with regard to **the mother's tribe, a woman inherits** from **her son** if the father is deceased.

The daf opens with one of the most disruptive positions in the laws of inheritance: the teaching of Rabbi Yehuda bar Rabbi Shimon, transmitted by Rabbi Yochanan, that a mother inherits from her deceased son. This opinion stands in direct conflict with the *mishna*, and its implications are sweeping. As *Tosafot* notes, the innovation of *Bnos Tzelofchad* already introduced the possibility of property passing down to daughters rather than remaining along the patrilineal line; the position of Rabbi Yehuda bar Rabbi Shimon effectively squares that innovation, allowing property to pass not only downward to daughters but upward to mothers as well, with all the attendant consequences for property leaving the father's family and tribe.

### Source & Mishna Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן: הָאִשָּׁה אֶת בְּנָהּ, וְהָאִשָּׁה אֶת בַּעְלָהּ, וַאֲחֵי הָאֵם – מַנְחִילִין וְלֹא נוֹחֲלִין! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשְׁנָתֵנוּ, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מִי שְׁנָאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Shimon** from the mishna, which teaches: **A woman** with regard to **her son, a woman** with regard to **her husband, and maternal uncles** all **bequeath** to their respective relatives **but do not inherit** from them. The mishna states explicitly that a mother does not inherit her son's property. Rabbi Yehuda ben Shimon **said to him:** With regard to **our mishna, I do not know who taught it,** i.e., I am not aware of any *tanna* who concurs with its ruling and it is not the accepted *halakha*.

The *gemara* opens by recording that Rabbi Yochanan transmitted this ruling in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bar Rabbi Shimon. Yet Rabbi Yochanan himself immediately confronts the teaching with our *mishna*, which states explicitly: *ish es benav, isha es ba'alah, v'achei ha'eim, manchilin v'lo nochlin* — a man bequeaths to his children, a woman bequeaths to her husband, and the mother's brothers may bequeath but do not inherit. The *mishna* thus explicitly excludes both a wife from inheriting her husband and a mother from inheriting her son. To this challenge, Rabbi Yehuda bar Rabbi Shimon responds: *mishnasenu eini yodeia mi shenasnah* — I do not know who taught that *mishna*; it does not reflect my view. In other words, he dismisses the *mishna* as representing some minority position without binding authority, acknowledging the conflict but declining to be governed by it.

### Rabbi Zechariah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: רַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב הִיא, דְּלָא דָּרֵישׁ ״מַטּוֹת״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **say to** Rabbi Yehuda ben Shimon that the mishna **is** written in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav, who,** as noted on page 111a, **does not derive** the *halakha* that sons precede daughters with regard to the inheritance of their mother from the word **"tribes."** Since Rabbi Yehuda ben Shimon's ruling is based on a derivation from the word "tribes," it stands to reason that Rabbi Zekharya disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda ben Shimon, and the ruling of the mishna is in accordance with Rabbi Zekharya's opinion.

The *gemara* presses further: granted that you wish to treat the *mishna* as a *da'as yachid* — the isolated opinion of a single *tanna* rather than a consensus ruling — why not identify who that *tanna* is? The *gemara* suggests: why not say the *mishna* represents the position of Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav, who does not expound the *drasha* of *matot*?

### Matot Drasha Explained

To appreciate this suggestion, it is necessary to recall the *drasha* of *matot* developed on earlier *dapim*. The *pasuk* reads: *v'chol bat yoreshet nachala mi'matot* — every daughter who inherits a portion from the tribes. The use of *matot* in the plural signals a case in which the daughter's father belongs to one tribe and her mother to another, and the verse equates the two — *mateh ha'eim* equals *mateh ha'av*. The tribe of the mother is rendered equivalent to the tribe of the father.

### Twin Implications

This equation carries two distinct implications. The first concerns what happens when the *mother* dies: just as when the father dies the sons inherit before the daughters, so too when the mother dies the sons take priority over the daughters, because the mother's tribe is equated to the father's tribe in the transmission of property downward. The second implication runs in the opposite direction: just as when a son dies his *father* inherits from him, so too — if the two tribes are truly equated — his *mother* should inherit from him alongside the father. These two implications point in different directions from the perspective of any simple analysis of whose interests are served: the first preserves the patriarchal ordering of sons over daughters even in a matrilineal transmission, while the second opens the door to property departing the father's tribal line entirely if the father has predeceased the son and the inheritance passes to the mother.

### Zechariah Rejects Equation

Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav rejects this equation altogether. He holds that *mateh ha'em* is not equated to *mateh ha'av*, and therefore when the mother dies, the sons and daughters inherit her property equally — there is no basis to impose the prioritization of sons over daughters when it is the mother's property at stake. By the same logic, when a son dies, the property passes only to his father, not to his mother, since the two parental lines are not equated.

### Nephews Before Nieces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיתּוֹקְמָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּרַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב; דְּקָתָנֵי: ״וּבְנֵי אָחוֹת״, וְתָנָא: ״בְּנֵי אָחוֹת״ – וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אָחוֹת. וְאָמְרִינַן: לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לְקַדֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The mishna cannot be interpreted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav, as it teaches: And the sons of a sister** inherit but do not bequeath, **and** a Sage **taught** that the mishna is referring only to **sons of a sister and not** to **daughters of a sister, and we say: With regard to what *halakha*** is there a distinction between the sons and daughters of a sister? **And Rav Sheshet said:** The distinction is meant **to** teach that where there are sons as well, they **precede** the daughters in inheriting from their maternal uncle.

The *gemara* now explains why the *mishna* cannot be attributed to Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav. The *mishna* includes in its discussion the inheritance of nephews — specifically the sons of a sister — and a *baraita* clarifies that the *mishna*'s intent is *bnei achot v'lo bnos achot*: nephews inherit before nieces. Rav Sheshet explains that this is not to exclude nieces entirely, but to establish that nephews take priority. Now consider the scenario: if a brother is dying and his estate passes to his sister's children, that means the sister herself has already died. The inheritance is thus flowing through a deceased woman to her children — structurally equivalent to the case of a mother dying. The *mishna*, by specifying that nephews precede nieces in this scenario, implicitly establishes that even when property passes through a woman, sons take priority over daughters. This is inconsistent with Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav's view that sons and daughters inherit equally from their mother. Therefore, the *mishna* cannot represent his position.

### Yoreshet Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא דִּידַן – מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ; אִי דָּרֵישׁ ״מַטּוֹת״, אִשָּׁה נָמֵי תִּירַשׁ אֶת בְּנָהּ; אִי לָא דָּרֵישׁ ״מַטּוֹת״, בֵּן דְּקוֹדֵם לַבַּת בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם – מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the *tanna* of our** mishna, **whichever** way **you** look at it, is inconsistent: **If he interprets** the word **"tribes,"** then **a woman also should inherit** from **her son,** as noted above (114b). And **if he does not interpret** the word **"tribes,"** then **from where is it** clear **to him** that **a son precedes a daughter with regard to** the **property of the mother?** Both *halakhot* are derived from the same source, so how can the *tanna* accept one and reject the other?

This creates an acute difficulty. Our *mishna* appears to maintain both rulings simultaneously — that when the mother dies, the sons get first (inferred from the nephews-before-nieces ruling), and that when the son dies, only the father inherits, not the mother. But these two positions seem to pull in opposite directions with respect to the *matot* equation. As the *gemara* frames it: *mi manafshach* — whichever way you turn, there is a problem. If the *mishna* does expound the *drasha* of *matot* and equates mother and father, then the mother should inherit from her son just as the father does. If the *mishna* does not expound *matot*, then how does it know to give priority to sons over daughters when the mother dies?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דָּרֵישׁ ״מַטּוֹת״; וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכׇל בַּת יֹרֶשֶׁת נַחֲלָה״ – יוֹרֶשֶׁת, וְאֵינָהּ מוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Actually,** the *tanna* of the mishna **interprets** the word **"tribes," but it is different here,** with regard to a woman inheriting from her son, **as the verse states: "And every daughter** who **possesses [*yoreshet*] an inheritance** from the tribes" (Numbers 36:8), which teaches that a daughter **inherits [*yoreshet*]** from two tribes, **but** she **does not bequeath** to two tribes. She bequeaths only to her father's tribe.

The *gemara* resolves this by pointing out that the *drasha* has two components, and the questioner had only considered one. The *gemara* says: *le-olam darish matot* — the *mishna* does indeed expound the *matot* equation, and that is how it establishes that sons precede daughters in the mother's estate. But the same *pasuk* contains another word: *yoreshet*. The full phrase is *v'chol bat yoreshet nachala mi'matot* — the *bat yoreshet*, the daughter who inherits. The *drasha* on *yoreshet* teaches: *yoreshet v'eina moreshet* — she inherits but she does not cause others to inherit. The equation of *matot* — mother's tribe to father's tribe — applies only in the downward direction, when property is passing down from parent to child and we are ordering among the heirs. In that context, mother and father are equated and sons take priority. But the equation does not apply upward, when a child dies and we ask who inherits from him. In that case, the property rises only to the father, not to the mother. The word *yoreshet* restricts the scope of the *matot* equation to the downward transmission.

### Two-Part Drasha

The structure of the resolution, then, is that the full *drasha* has two parts. *Matot* equates the mother's tribe to the father's tribe, ensuring that the patrilineal ordering of sons before daughters governs even when property descends through the mother. *Yoreshet* limits that equation to the case of descent, preventing it from operating in the upward direction when a son predeceases both parents. The *mishna* thus upholds both principles in a coherent framework, and cannot be attributed to Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav, who rejects the *matot* equation entirely.

## Seder Nachalos

### Intro to Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סֵדֶר נַחֲלוֹת כָּךְ הוּא: ״אִישׁ כִּי יָמוּת וּבֵן אֵין לוֹ, וְהַעֲבַרְתֶּם אֶת נַחֲלָתוֹ לְבִתּוֹ״ – בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת, כׇּל יוֹצְאֵי יְרֵיכוֹ שֶׁל בֵּן קוֹדְמִין לַבַּת. בַּת קוֹדֶמֶת לָאַחִין, יוֹצְאֵי יְרֵיכָהּ שֶׁל בַּת קוֹדְמִין לָאַחִין. אַחִין קוֹדְמִין לַאֲחֵי הָאָב, יוֹצְאֵי יְרֵיכָן שֶׁל אַחִין קוֹדְמִין לַאֲחֵי הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The **order** of precedence with regard to **inheritances is this:** The verse states: **"If a man dies, and has no son, then you shall pass his inheritance to his daughter"** (Numbers 27:8). This teaches that **a son precedes a daughter.** Additionally, **all descendants of a son precede a daughter. A daughter precedes the brothers** of the deceased. Additionally, the **descendants of a daughter precede the brothers** of the deceased. **Brothers** of the deceased **precede the uncles** of the deceased. Additionally, the **descendants of the brothers precede the uncles.**

Having addressed the opening controversy of the *daf*, the *gemara* turns to the *mishna*'s systematic exposition of *seder nachalos* — the ordered hierarchy of inheritance. The opening *mishna* of this *perek* established who can give and receive inheritance in terms of the categories of relatives involved, but did not lay out a priority order. The present *mishna* supplies that structure. It is worth noting that this is an unusually well-organized *perek*: it opens with a conceptual framework — *nochlin u-manchilin* — and now moves to elaborate the hierarchy from *pesukim*. The *perek* itself is quite substantial, extending to thirty *dapim* and eventually reaching the topic of rabbinic instruments for transferring property that function analogously to wills.

### Descendants Priority

The *mishna* begins with the foundational *pasuk*: *ish ki yamut u-ven ein lo v'ha'avartem et nachalato le-vito* — if a man dies and has no son, his estate passes to his daughter. From this the *mishna* derives: *ben kodem le-vas* — the son precedes the daughter. But the *mishna* immediately adds the critical refinement: *kol yotzei yerecho shel ben kodmin le-vas* — all the issue of the loins of the son precede the daughter. The phrase *yotzei yerecho* is precise and intentional: it does not refer to relatives generally but to direct lineal descendants. Every direct descendant of the son — however many generations removed — is treated as standing in the son's place and takes priority over the daughter.

### Replacement Principle

The animating principle here is that a child or grandchild is understood as a generational replacement for the person who died. When a son predeceases the deceased, all of that son's descendants — sons, daughters, grandchildren, great-grandchildren — step into his place in the inheritance hierarchy. Before the estate moves to the next category on the list, one must first exhaust every possible descendant of the prior category. *Tosafot* raises the question of whether this rests on the general principle of *bnei banim harei hem ke-vanim* — that grandchildren are treated as children — noting that this principle may be more firmly established when the *pesukim* use the word *zera*, which can connote any descendant, than when they use *ben*, which might suggest only the immediate first generation. But regardless of the precise derivation, the operative rule is clear: no matter how many generations removed, a descendant counts as a replacement.

### Gender Blindness Query

This raises a genuinely interesting question about how gender-blind the replacement principle is. The basic rule is that when a son has predeceased the testator, all of that son's descendants take his place — and the *mishna* states this without qualification, so even a great-great-granddaughter through that son comes before the testator's own daughter. Superficially this seems ironic: a daughter of the testator is closer in degree than a many-generations-removed female descendant through a son, yet the latter takes priority.

The deeper question is what happens when multiple descendants of the predeceased son are alive simultaneously. If the predeceased son had both a son and a daughter, does the grandson take priority over the granddaughter, or are they treated equally since we are already in a gender-blind "replacement" mode? The answer appears to be that if they are both alive, the grandson does take priority — we are not fully gender-blind within the replacement generation, and the male descendant would inherit before the female descendant. The question becomes more complex when asking whether, if the grandson himself predeceases his sisters, one should prefer a line through him over a line through a female sibling. This requires further verification, but the basic framework of the replacement principle is clear.

The seemingly paradoxical result — that a granddaughter through a son comes before a daughter — also raises a structural puzzle. The preference of sons over daughters was originally grounded in the goal of keeping property within the patrilineal tribe. Yet once a daughter of a predeceased son is inheriting, the property may well pass out of the tribe through her marriage. As *Tosafot* observes in connection with the earlier discussion, the introduction of female inheritance through *Bnos Tzelofchad* set in motion a logic that plays out in many directions — the "joint account" has complex downstream consequences.

### Full Hierarchy Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בַּנַּחֲלָה – יוֹצְאֵי יְרֵיכוֹ קוֹדְמִין. וְהָאָב קוֹדֵם לְכׇל יוֹצְאֵי יְרֵיכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle:** Concerning **anyone who precedes** another **with regard to inheritance, his descendants precede** the other as well, **and a father** who inherits **precedes all of his descendants.**

The *mishna* then continues down the list. After the daughter and all her descendants come the brothers — the deceased's siblings. The *mishna* then specifies *achei ha'av* — the father's brothers, i.e., the deceased's uncles — as coming after brothers. Notably, the *mishna* does not insert the father into this explicit sequence; the *gemara* supplies that omission. The full hierarchy, once the father is incorporated, is: children (and all their descendants), then the father, then brothers (and their descendants), then the father's brothers.

### Father's Position

The *mishna*'s closing rule states: *ha'av kodem l'chol yotzei yerecho* — the father precedes all of his own issue. This places the father before the deceased's brothers, since the deceased's brothers are precisely the father's children. The logic is that a sibling relationship is mediated through the father: the deceased is connected to his brother only through their common father. Therefore, the father — as the direct ancestor — takes priority over his own descendants.

This resolves what might initially seem like an omission. The *mishna* lists the order as: son, daughter, brothers, father's brothers — and the father seems to be missing. In fact, the father enters between the daughter and the brothers, based on the rule that he precedes all his own issue. So the operative order is: children (all descendants), then father, then siblings (and their descendants), then grandfather, then uncles, and so on.

### Recursive Pattern

Once this structure is grasped, the full order of inheritance follows a recursive pattern. The estate passes downward as far as possible through direct descendants; when that line is exhausted, it rises one generation; from there it again passes downward through all descendants of that generation before rising again. The movement is always: down as far as possible, then up one step, then down again. Children of the deceased first — exhausting every descendant; then the father; then the father's children (siblings), exhausting their descendants; then the grandfather; then the grandfather's children (uncles), exhausting their descendants; and so on back through the generations. The *gemara* notes that ultimately this chain leads all the way back to *Reuven ben Yaakov*, because the entire Jewish people share a common ancestry.

### Two-Rule Summary

Underlying this entire hierarchical structure are really only two rules. The first is the ordering of son before daughter — with the accompanying principle that all descendants of a son precede a daughter, and all descendants of a daughter precede the next category. The second is the rule that the father precedes all his own issue. With just these two rules, the complete *seder nachalos* follows automatically: go down through sons and daughters and all their descendants; rise to the father; go down through the father's sons and daughters and all their descendants; rise to the grandfather; repeat. Everything else is a consequence of applying these two rules recursively. The *Rashbam* notes that the formulation *kodem l'chol yotzei yerecho* in the *mishna* is slightly imprecise in the context of the father's rule, but the intent is clear.

## Descent Drasha

### Baraita on Descent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בֵּן״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בֵּן; בֶּן הַבֵּן, אוֹ בַּת הַבֵּן, אוֹ בֶּן בַּת הַבֵּן – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֵין לוֹ״ – עַיֵּין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** The verse states: "If a man dies, and has no **son,** then you shall pass his inheritance to his daughter" (Numbers 27:8). **I have** derived **only** that **a son** precedes others with regard to the inheritance of the deceased; **from where** do I derive that **a son of a son, or a daughter of a son, or a son of a daughter of a son** also precedes the deceased's other relatives? **The verse states:** "If a man dies, and **he has no [*ein lo*]** son." The word *ein* is written *aleph*, *yod*, *nun*. Therefore, the Sages read it as if it states: **Investigate with regard to him [*ayyein alav*],** to search for descendants of his son, and give the inheritance to them if they are found.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *tanu rabbanan*, the Sages taught — *ben u-ven ein lo*: the son, and if there is no son. The *baraita* asks: I know only that we go to the son; how do I know to continue down through the son's son, the son's son's son, and so forth, through all subsequent generations?

### Ayin Alav Drasha

The *drasha* is derived from the phrase *ein lo* — the *gemara* reads this as *ayen alav*, "investigate concerning him": investigate whether there are any descendants in his line before moving on to the next category. The word *ein*, which in its simple meaning signals absence ("there is no"), is read as an instruction to scrutinize (*ayen*) the line for any surviving descendants, however remote. This is admittedly a striking *drasha*, but it establishes the textual basis for the unlimited downward extension of each category in the inheritance hierarchy.

### Female Line Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּת״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּת; בַּת הַבַּת, וּבֶן הַבַּת, וּבַת בֶּן הַבַּת – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֵין לוֹ״ – עַיֵּין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: "And if he has no **daughter,** then you shall give his inheritance to his brothers" (Numbers 27:9). **I have** derived **only** that **a daughter** precedes others, except a son and his descendants, with regard to the inheritance of the deceased, **from where** then do I derive that **a daughter's daughter, or a son of a daughter, or a daughter of a son of a daughter** also precede the deceased's other relatives? **The verse states:** "And if he **has no [*ein lo*]** daughter." The word *ein* is written *aleph*, *yod*, *nun*. Therefore, the Sages read it as if it states: **Investigate with regard to him [*ayyein alav*],** to search for descendants of his daughter, and give the inheritance to them if they are found.

The same logic extends to the daughter's line. The *pasuk* reads: *v'ha'avartem et nachalato le-vito* — if he has no son, his estate passes to his daughter. Here too, the verse includes *ein lo* — "if he has no daughter" — and the same *drasha* applies: *ayen aleha*, investigate the daughter's line. Thus the granddaughter through a daughter, and the great-granddaughter, and so on, all continue the daughter's place in the hierarchy before the estate moves to the next category.

### Nachala Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? נַחֲלָה מְמַשְׁמֶשֶׁת וְהוֹלֶכֶת, עַד רְאוּבֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How so,** i.e., how is the investigation performed when he has no offspring at all? The Gemara answers: The family lineage that determines the **inheritance is successively examined up to Reuben,** son of Jacob, i.e., the heirs are determined by investigating the family genealogy, and that investigation can extend all the way to Reuben, son of our forefather Jacob.

The *baraita* captures this dynamic with a vivid image: *nachala memashmeshet ve-holechet* — the inheritance flows and travels, moving continuously down the generations and then back up through the families. The estate descends through the son's line, through the daughter's line, up to the father, through the father's children and their descendants, up to the grandfather, and so on, traveling through the entire network of relatives until an heir is found.

### Tribal Safeguard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא עַד יַעֲקֹב! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּמִירִי דְּלָא כָּלֵה שִׁבְטָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** it **say: Until Jacob** himself, rather than until Reuben, since if none of Reuben's descendants survive, one would have to examine Jacob's descendants. **Abaye said** in reply: It **is learned** as a tradition **that a tribe will not be eliminated** entirely, and some descendants will always remain.

The *baraita* says this chain extends *ad Reuven* — all the way back to Reuven son of Yaakov. But the *gemara* immediately asks: why stop at Reuven? Why not go all the way back to Yaakov himself, and then down through all the other tribes?

Abaye answers with a fundamental principle: *gmiri de-lo kalya shivta* — it is a received tradition that no tribe of Israel disappears entirely. If the inheritance were to rise above Reuben to Yaakov and then descend through another tribe, that would necessarily imply that the entire tribe of Reuben had ceased to exist — every last descendant of Reuben had died without heirs. Since we hold as a matter of received tradition that this will not occur, we do not extend the chain beyond the tribe. As long as any descendant of Reuben exists anywhere, that person is related to the deceased and will be found somewhere in the chain. Crossing over to another tribe would only become necessary if an entire tribe were extinguished, which we presume cannot happen.

### No Heirs Found

This raises a practical modern question: what happens when no heirs can actually be located? After exhaustive searching, if no heir is found, what becomes of the property? Is it *hefker*? Does it revert to some communal authority? The *gemara* does not address this directly, and it remains an interesting unresolved question, with analogues in civil law systems — where courts have grappled with similar problems involving institutions such as churches whose last surviving member dies before the property's disposition is legally settled.

## Sadducean Heresy

### Equal Inheritance Condemned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר תִּירַשׁ בַּת עִם בַּת הַבֵּן, אֲפִילּוּ נָשִׂיא שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, שֶׁאֵינָן אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂה צָדוֹקִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **anyone who says** that **a daughter** of the deceased **should inherit** the estate of her father along **with the daughter of the son** of the deceased, **even** if he is **a prince of the Jewish people,** one **should not listen to him, as this is nothing other than an act of the Sadducees,** and runs counter to the ruling of the mishna that the descendants of a son inherit before a daughter.

Rav Huna citing Rav states: *kol ha-omer tirash bat im bat ha-ben* — whoever says that a daughter should inherit together with a granddaughter through a son has articulated a position that is utterly rejected. To appreciate the force of this, recall the hierarchy established above: when a son predeceases the testator, his daughter — the testator's granddaughter — takes the son's place in the inheritance order, and therefore comes before the testator's own daughter. The position being condemned here is the suggestion that once a granddaughter is receiving inheritance, the daughter ought to receive alongside her, dividing the estate between them.

### Nasi Authority Limited

Rav Huna adds: *afilu nasi she-be-Yisrael, ein shom'in lo* — even if the *Nasi*, the supreme communal authority in Israel, were to rule this way, one does not listen to him. The *Rashbam* understands this as referring to a *nasi* who convenes a great *beis din* and issues a ruling. *Tosafot* goes further: even a *nasi* who is not claiming this is genuine *halacha* but is instead seeking to institute a *takkanah* — a rabbinic ordinance — for communal purposes, even one framed as a temporary measure responding to particular social circumstances, is not to be heeded. The principle of *hefker beis din hefker* — that a *beis din* has the power to render property ownerless and redirect it — would ordinarily permit a rabbinic body to accomplish something like this. Yet even that avenue is blocked here. The ruling is so completely off the table that it cannot serve even as the basis for a temporary institutional arrangement.

### Sadducean Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: בְּאַרְבָּעָה וְעֶשְׂרִים בְּטֵבֵת תַּבְנָא לְדִינַנָא. שֶׁהָיוּ צָדוֹקִין אוֹמְרִין: תִּירַשׁ הַבַּת עִם בַּת הַבֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* in *Megillat Ta'anit*, which describes various minor holidays on which it is forbidden to fast or eulogize: **On the twenty-fourth of Tevet, we returned to our law,** i.e., the *halakha* was reestablished in accordance with the opinion of the Sages after having been dictated by the Sadducees. **As the Sadducees would say: A daughter should inherit** the estate of her father along **with the daughter of the son** of the deceased.

The reason for this extraordinary exclusion is that the position is identified as *mi-Tzedukim* — it is a Sadducean doctrine. Because of this historical association, the ruling is not merely a legitimate minority opinion that lost; it is placed entirely outside the range of permissible positions. This is a noteworthy phenomenon: sometimes a position that might otherwise have been treated as a *da'at yachid* — a minority view worthy of consideration — is rendered categorically illegitimate because of its historical association with a heterodox movement. One might observe that the very persuasiveness of the Sadducean argument — if a granddaughter inherits, why should a daughter not share in that inheritance? — may itself be part of why the Sages felt it necessary to place it so firmly beyond discussion.

The *gemara* cites the source for this historical attribution: *de-tanya, be-esrim ve-arba be-Tevet, tavna le-dinana* — as the *baraita* teaches, on the twenty-fourth of *Tevet*, we returned to our ruling. This text is drawn from *Megillat Ta'anit*, the ancient document from the period of the Second Temple that records minor holidays on which fasting is prohibited. The document itself was written in Aramaic — an unusual feature, since typically the original authoritative text is in Hebrew and Aramaic serves as the *gemara*'s gloss, but here the situation is reversed, with the *tanna*'s elaboration serving as a commentary on an originally Aramaic source. *Megillat Ta'anit* is of great historical interest as one of the earliest pieces of non-scriptural Jewish writing, and the discussion brought here is drawn from its *skolion* — the explanatory commentary that accompanies the list of dates.

### Old Man's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְפַּל לָהֶן רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, אָמַר לָהֶם: שׁוֹטִים, מִנַּיִן זֶה לָכֶם? וְלֹא הָיָה אָדָם שֶׁהֶחְזִירוֹ דָּבָר, חוּץ מִזָּקֵן אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מְפַטְפֵּט כְּנֶגְדּוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר: וּמָה בַּת בְּנוֹ הַבָּאָה מִכֹּחַ בְּנוֹ – תִּירָשֶׁנּוּ; בִּתּוֹ הַבָּאָה מִכֹּחוֹ – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai joined them** to discuss their ruling, and **said to them: Imbeciles, from where** do **you** derive **this** ruling? **And there was no person that answered him anything, except for one old** man **who was chattering at him and saying** that it is an *a fortiori* inference: **And just as a daughter of** the deceased's **son, who comes** to claim her inheritance from her grandfather **by virtue of his son, inherits** her grandfather's property, so too, with regard to the deceased's own **daughter, who comes** to inherit **by virtue of** the deceased, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that she should inherit his property?

The *baraita* continues: *Nitpal lahem Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was dispatched to deal with them. The verb *nitpal* carries something of a dismissive tone — "go take care of them," as if the matter were being handed off as a nuisance to be managed.

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai opened by challenging them: *shotim, minayin lachem?* — Fools, where do you derive this? And *lo haya adam she-hechiziro davar* — no one was willing to articulate a defense. *Chutz mi-zaken echad she-haya mefatpet knegdo* — except for one old man who was chattering away against him, the word *mefatpet* again carrying a dismissive tone. This old man put forward the following argument: *u-mah bat bno ha-ba'ah mi-koch bno tirashenu* — a granddaughter, who comes to inherit only through the medium of her father, will inherit the testator. *Bito ha-ba'ah mi-kocho, lo kol she-ken?* — His own daughter, who comes directly from him, surely all the more so should she inherit. This is, as the *gemara* implicitly acknowledges, a genuinely strong argument. The granddaughter's claim depends on an intermediary — her father — and yet she inherits. The daughter's claim is direct and unmediated. The logical inference from the lesser to the greater seems compelling.

### Anah Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרָא עָלָיו אֶת הַמִּקְרָא הַזֶּה: ״אֵלֶּה בְנֵי שֵׂעִיר הַחֹרִי יֹשְׁבֵי הָאָרֶץ: לוֹטָן וְשׁוֹבָל וְצִבְעוֹן וַעֲנָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״אֵלֶּה בְּנֵי צִבְעוֹן, וְאַיָּה וַעֲנָה״!
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **recited this verse about him: "These are the sons of Seir the Horite, the inhabitants of the land: Lotan and Shobal and Zibeon and Anah"** (Genesis 36:20), **and it is written: "And these are the children of Zibeon: Aiah and Anah"** (Genesis 36:24).

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's response was to cite a *pasuk*: *eleh bnei Se'ir ha-Chori yoshvei ha'aretz, Lotan v'Shoval v'Tzivon v'Anah* — these are the sons of Se'ir the Chori, dwellers of the land: Lotan, Shoval, Tzivon, and Anah. And a second *pasuk* states: *v'eleh bnei Tzivon, v'Ayah v'Anah* — these are the sons of Tzivon: Ayah and Anah. The difficulty is immediate: in the first verse, Anah appears as a sibling of Tzivon, both being children of Se'ir; in the second verse, Anah appears as a child of Tzivon.

### Anah Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבָּא צִבְעוֹן עַל אִמּוֹ, וְהוֹלִיד עֲנָה. וְדִלְמָא תְּרֵי עֲנָה הֲווֹ! אָמַר רַבָּה: אָמֵינָא מִלְּתָא דְּלָא אַמְרַהּ שַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא; וּמַנּוּ? שְׁמוּאֵל. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אָמֵינָא מִלְּתָא דְּלָא אַמְרַהּ שַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא; וּמַנּוּ? רַבָּה. אָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא עֲנָה״ – הוּא עֲנָה דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this **teaches that Zibeon engaged in sexual intercourse with his mother and begot Anah,** so that he was both Anah's father and his brother. From the fact that the first verse equates Zibeon and Anah by referring to both of them as Seir's sons despite Anah being a grandson of Seir, it is clear that grandchildren are equal to children, contrary to the Sadducees' assertion. The Gemara interrupts the recounting of the *baraita* and questions Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai's inference: **But perhaps there were two** people named **Anah,** so that one Anah was Zibeon's son, and the other his brother? **Rabba said: I will state a matter** that even **King Shapur did not state. And who is** this King Shapur? This cannot be a reference to Shapur, king of Persia; rather, it must be a moniker for someone else. He is **Shmuel,** whose legal rulings were accepted by the public like the edicts of a king by his subjects. **Some state** a different version, that it was **Rav Pappa** who **said: I will state a matter** that even **King Shapur did not state. And who is** this King Shapur? He is **Rabba. The verse** goes on to **state: "This is Anah"** (Genesis 36:24), indicating that **he is** the same **Anah** mentioned **initially,** earlier in the verse. Accordingly, there was only one Anah, who was both Zibeon's brother and Zibeon's son.

The *gemara* addresses the apparent contradiction: *ela melamed she-ba Tzivon al imo v'holid Anah* — the *baraita* teaches that Tzivon committed incest with his mother and thereby produced Anah, who is thus both his child and his sibling. The *gemara* anticipates the objection — *u-dilma terei Anah havu*, perhaps there are simply two different people named Anah? — and dismisses it on the basis of the phrase *hu Anah*, "he is Anah," which appears in a third verse: *hu Anah asher matza et ha-yemim ba-midbar bi-r'oto et ha-chamorim l'Tzivon aviv* — he is the Anah who found the *yemim* in the desert while tending the donkeys of Tzivon his father. The word *hu*, "he is," signals that this is the same Anah referenced in both earlier verses. Rava (some say Rav Papa citing Rava) introduces the explanation with the remark that he is offering a reading even the great authorities of *dinei mamonot* had not articulated — a rhetorical flourish underscoring the subtlety of the interpretation.

*Tosafot* adds an important point: Anah is in fact a woman, not a man, as can be shown from various *pesukim*. With this in mind, the *drasha* becomes more meaningful. Anah is listed as an inheritor together with her siblings in the context of *yoshvei ha'aretz* — inheritance of the land. Although her father is Tzivon, she is recorded as a child of Se'ir in the inheritance context, because Tzivon is no longer alive and she stands in his place. Yet she is listed as a *daughter* of Se'ir's household, inheriting equally alongside the remaining siblings — not as if she had independent status as a child of Se'ir, but as a descendant of Tzivon taking his place. Crucially, if Tzivon had daughters — sisters at the same generation — those sisters would not be inheriting. Anah, a granddaughter, inherits while hypothetical daughters of Se'ir at that level do not. This is precisely the rule being debated: a granddaughter through a son takes the son's place and inherits in the presence of the surviving brothers, while a daughter who lacks that connection to a son's line does not share.

### Torah Superiority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, בְּכָךְ אַתָּה פּוֹטְרֵנִי?! אָמַר לוֹ: שׁוֹטֶה, וְלֹא תְּהֵא תּוֹרָה שְׁלֵמָה שֶׁלָּנוּ, כְּשִׂיחָה בְּטֵלָה שֶׁלָּכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The Sadducee **said to** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai: **My teacher, you dismiss me with this** retort? I agree that the son of a son precedes a daughter, as the verse you quoted suggests; I am asserting that a daughter inherits together with the daughter of a son, and the verse you quoted has no bearing on that claim. Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: Imbecile, but will our perfect Torah not be** as worthy **as your frivolous speech,** as your inference is fallacious:

The old man responded: *Rebbi, be-chach atah potreni?* — Master, is that your answer? That *drasha* is your proof? Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai replied: *shoteh, v'lo tehei torah sheileimah shelanu ke-sichah bateilah shelachem* — Fool. Our complete *Torah* should not even be put in conversation with your nonsense. The dismissal is sharp: the *drasha* from *Anah* was offered not as the substantive proof but as a rhetorical move, demonstrating that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai could engage on their terms while treating their position with contempt.

### Granddaughter Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְבַת בְּנוֹ – שֶׁכֵּן יִפָּה כֹּחָהּ בִּמְקוֹם הָאַחִין; תֹּאמַר בְּבִתּוֹ – שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחָהּ בִּמְקוֹם אַחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is notable **about** the inheritance of **a daughter of** the deceased's **son?** It is notable **in that her right is enhanced** in that she inherits from her paternal grandfather together **with the brothers** of her father. Would you **say** that the same applies **with regard to** the deceased's **daughter, whose right** to inherit **is diminished** in that she does not inherit from her father together **with** her **brothers?** The Sadducee's *a fortiori* inference is thereby disproved.

The actual argument — the substantive *halacha* — is then stated: *mah le-vas beno, shekein yafeh koachah be-makom ha'achim* — what distinguishes a son's daughter? The fact that her position is strengthened in the presence of brothers. A son's daughter is treated as equivalent to the brothers in the inheritance hierarchy: she takes her father's place and stands alongside the brothers as an heir at the same level, displacing those below. *Ta'omar be-vito, she-hora'ah koachah bi-makom achim* — but can you say the same of the daughter? The daughter's position is weaker in the presence of brothers; she does not take a place equivalent to a brother. The *Rashbam* explains that the actual proof for this distinction comes from *Bnos Tzelofchad*: the daughters of Tzelofchad inherited in the presence of brothers — specifically, in the presence of their uncles, the brothers of their father Tzelofchad. Conceptually, since Tzelofchad had predeceased the entry into the land, the relevant inheritance is from Chefer, Tzelofchad's father. The daughters of Tzelofchad stood in Tzelofchad's place and inherited together with Tzelofchad's brothers — his siblings — even though at that level there were no surviving daughters of Chefer receiving a share. This establishes the principle: a son's daughters, standing in the son's place, inherit in the presence of brothers; the testator's own daughters do not share when the estate has already been assigned to the son's line.

### Victory Established

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִצְּחוּם, וְאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם עֲשָׂאוּהוּ יוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** since the Sadducees had no counterargument, the Sages **were victorious over them, and they established that day,** the twenty-fourth of Tevet, as a minor **festival** to celebrate the establishment of the *halakha* in accordance with the opinion of the Sages.

*Oso ha-yom nitzchum va'asa'uhu yom tov* — on that day they prevailed over the Sadducees, and it was made into a holiday. However the Sadducees were positioned politically, on that day the rabbinic position became established as authoritative *halacha* — and the twenty-fourth of *Tevet* was commemorated as a minor holiday recorded in *Megillat Ta'anit*, a day on which fasting was prohibited.

### Tzelofchad Proof

The proof from *Bnos Tzelofchad*, as the *Rashbam* explains, is the cornerstone of the substantive *halacha*. Tzelofchad had died, leaving daughters but no sons. His father Chefer was the conceptual owner of the ancestral portion in *Eretz Yisrael*. When Chefer's estate was distributed, Tzelofchad's daughters — granddaughters of Chefer — inherited Tzelofchad's share, doing so in the presence of their uncles, who were Chefer's other surviving sons. At that same level, any daughters of Chefer who lacked a son's line standing behind them received nothing. The granddaughters through Tzelofchad displaced the testator's own daughters because they stood in the place of a son. This is the case that established the rule and refuted the Sadducean claim: a daughter inheriting in the presence of uncles, because she is occupying a son's place, is proof that the son's descendants have a strengthened position that the testator's own daughters do not share.
