# Pesachim 116 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review: Kiddush–Karpas

### Kiddush, Urchatz, Karpas

Today's daf is 116, picking up at the bottom of 115b. To review what has been covered: the seder begins with *kiddush*, followed by *urchatz* — the washing of hands before dipping. The Gemara, though not the Mishnah explicitly, took for granted that one washes twice, explaining that this is on account of the dipping of *karpas* in liquid. The question of whether this is a general requirement — whether it is because one might render the food *tamei*, and whether a *bracha* is recited — was addressed, and the conclusion is that we do not make a *bracha*, though the Rambam does require one.

### Karpas Details

In the Mishnah, the vegetable used for *karpas* was *maror*. The Gemara gave the impression that *maror* was used only because no other vegetable was available, which is itself somewhat strange. As was suggested, there may in fact be a deliberate reason to use *maror* specifically at the outset: it fits with the fact that one of the four questions concerns *maror* before the *maror* has actually been eaten in its formal context, and the symbolism of *maror* begins, in a sense, from the very opening of the *seder*. Nevertheless, because of complications surrounding the recitation of *al achilat maror* — if *maror* is used both at the front and later — the practice developed to use other vegetables for *karpas*.

Regarding how much to eat: the Ashkenazic practice is to eat less than a *kezayit* in order to avoid the question of whether a *bracha achrona* is required, since the *haggadah* constitutes a *hefsek*. The Rambam, by contrast, holds that one specifically eats a *kezayit* because there is an obligation of *achilah*, which has the practical advantage of allowing one to have some food before the seder properly begins.

As for what to dip it in — the Gemara does not specify. The Rambam understands that one dips in *charoset*, which carries the symbolism of slavery and is fitting if *maror* was the primary vegetable used for *karpas*. Tosafot and other Ashkenazic authorities hold that one dips in something else, such as salt water or vinegar. The related question of whether one eats *karpas* with or without *haseiba* (reclining) turns on the same axis: if the dipping is understood as a symbol of slavery, one eats without reclining; if it is understood as the Roman custom of dipping before a meal — a gesture of freedom — then one reclines. This question is the subject of debate.

### Removing the Seder Plate

The last topic discussed previously was the removal of the *ke'arah*, the seder plate. Although the Mishnah speaks first of bringing what will be dipped and then bringing the *matzah*, *chazeret*, and *charoset*, the Gemara explains this second bringing in terms of removing the plate so that the *tinokot* will take notice. The Rashbam at the bottom of 115b writes:

> *Mitzvah hi la'akor et hashulchan kodem l'haggadah, ki heichi d'lehavay heker l'tinokot* — it is a *mitzvah* to remove the table before the *haggadah*, so that there should be something to stimulate the curiosity of the children.

He explains that in earlier times, each person had a small individual table that would be removed entirely during the *haggadah*. In our practice, we remove the *ke'arah* — containing *matzah*, *maror*, and the two cooked items — to the side of the table. The Rashbam notes that some have the custom of lifting the *ke'arah* onto the shoulder, or removing the bread from it, but he dismisses this as unnecessary and not an effective *heker* for the child. His view is that one should remove the *ke'arah* entirely, placing it at the corner, "as if they had already eaten," so that the child will notice and ask. This is analogous to the earlier practice of removing the small table.

In practice, right after *ha lachma anya* at the beginning of *maggid*, the plate is removed from the table. The second cup is poured, and at that moment the child asks *mah nishtanah*. After the questions, the *ke'arah* is brought back — precisely the sequence the Gemara describes. Indeed, Abbaye before Rabbah — when Rabbah removed the *ke'arah* — said, "Good, now we do not have to say *mah nishtanah*," which suggests that any genuine expression of curiosity can in principle substitute for the formal recitation of the *mah nishtanah*. Tosafot, however, is uncomfortable with omitting the formal text entirely, and understands that even after the question about the plate is asked, one still proceeds to recite the *mah nishtanah*.

## Yachatz: Lechem Oni

### Lechem Oni Definition

Moving now to *yachatz*. Four lines from the bottom of the page, Shmuel states: *lechem she-onim alav devarim harbeh* — "bread over which many things are said." The *haggadah* is recited in relation to the *matzah*, which is why the *matzah* remains uncovered and exposed throughout the telling. It is the symbol around which the story is woven. The term *onim* literally suggests proclaiming, though it may equally connote responding — *v'anita v'amarta lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — since the entire structure of the evening is one of question and response: the child asks *mah nishtanah* and the father responds. Either interpretation fits: the *haggadah* is both proclaimed over the *matzah* and given in answer to the questions.

### Lechem Oni Interpretati…

The Gemara then continues: *tanya nami hachi — v'it d'amri*: another version also teaches this, and some say *lechem she-onim alav devarim harbeh*. Then comes *davar acher*: *lechem oni* — the word *ani* is written without a *vav*, and just as a poor person typically possesses only a broken piece of bread rather than a whole loaf, so too here the *haggadah* should be recited over a broken piece of bread. This is the source of *yachatz*: because *maggid* is said over *lechem oni*, which represents bread of affliction, the *matzah* must be broken before *maggid* begins. A third interpretation adds that just as a poor person has no fuel to waste — the husband lights the fire while the wife kneads the dough and gets it into the oven immediately — so too in the making of *matzah* one must work efficiently so that the dough does not become *chametz*.

### Two vs. Three Matzot

The concept of *b'prusa* — using a broken loaf — raises an important halachic question: when one recites *hamotzi*, must the loaf be whole? May one of the two *lechem mishneh* loaves be broken? The debate among the *rishonim* is significant. Most *rishonim*, including the Rif, hold that two whole loaves are required even on *yom tov*. On *leil seder*, however, the Rif permits one of the loaves to be half, in keeping with *darko shel ani b'prusa*. The Rambam follows the Rif, using one and a half *matzot*.

One might question whether two loaves are even required on *yom tov* at all. The *torah* itself does not mandate it, and the usual rationale — the double portion of *man* that fell on *shabbos* — does not straightforwardly extend to *yom tov*, since one may both carry and prepare food on *yom tov*, so the need for a double portion is less obvious. Tosafot raises this question and notes a further complication from the liturgical phrase *v'attah kidashtanu*, suggesting that the *man* did fall in a double portion on *erev yom tov* as well.

### Tosafot on Three Matzot

Tosafot's own resolution is to use three *matzot*: two whole ones to fulfill *lechem mishneh*, and one broken one to represent *lechem oni*. This is our Ashkenazic practice. Tosafot writes:

> *Nira d'gam hamotzi tzarich l'varech al haprusa* — it appears that *hamotzi* must also be recited over the broken one.

He then describes the dispute among earlier authorities. The view of Rav Menachem of Yoni and Rav Yom Tov was that one recites *hamotzi* with *kavana* directed toward the broken *matzah*. The Ri, however — and this is the practice we follow — recites *hamotzi* over the whole top *matzah*, then lowers it, recites *al achilat matzah* with *kavana* directed toward the broken middle *matzah*, and cuts from both together. The practical *kavana* is: *hamotzi* on the top whole *matzah*, *al achilat matzah* on the broken *prusa*.

Technically, the *kezayit* requirement applies only to the *mitzvah* of *achilat matzah*, which could in principle be fulfilled from the broken middle alone. Nevertheless, the practice has developed to take a *kezayit* from both the top and the middle, and since there is never quite enough to go around, those present supplement from the pieces as needed. The bottom *matzah* serves as the third of the *lechem mishneh*; the half that was broken off during *yachatz* is set aside for the *afikomen*; and the remaining portion of the broken middle *matzah* is used for *korech*. This is how the various components of the *seder* cohere — though much of this elaborated detail is not found in the Gemara itself, and alternative arrangements were certainly possible.

## Charoset Debate

### Charoset Obligation

The Mishnah continues: *af al pi she-ein charoset mitzvah* — even though *charoset* is not a *mitzvah* according to the *tanna kamma*. If it is not a *mitzvah*, why do we bother with it at all? *Mishum kapa* — Rabbi Ami explains that it is to neutralize a dangerous sap or substance found in the *maror*. According to this view, *charoset* has no intrinsic symbolic purpose at the *seder*; it is purely a practical precaution.

Abbaye then analyzes this further. The *kapa* — the pungency or dangerous sap — of *chassa* (lettuce used as *maror*) is countered by radish; the *kapa* of radish is countered by cress; the *kapa* of cress is countered by hot water; and ultimately, *kapa* of all of them is countered by hot water. The text then produces an Aramaic incantation directed at the harmful substance and its seven daughters and eight daughters-in-law, which the Gemara includes without further elaboration.

Rabbi Eliezer — or Rabbi Tzadok — then declares that *charoset* is in fact a *mitzvah*. The question becomes: what is the nature of this *mitzvah*?

### Charoset Symbolism

Rabbi Levi says: *zecher le-tapuach* — to recall the *tapuach*. This refers to the well-known *midrash* found in tractate Sotah, that the women of Israel seduced their husbands under the *tapuach* trees (Shir HaShirim: *tachat hatapuach orarticha*), because the men had despaired of having children given Pharaoh's decrees. It was this act of faith and courage that produced the children of Israel and ultimately led to the *geulah*. As an aside, the translation of *tapuach* as "apple" is almost certainly incorrect — there were no apples in the ancient Near East — and the word likely refers to some other fruit, perhaps a citrus. Some suggest it could even be an *esrog*.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different rationale: *zecher le-tit* — to recall the mortar. The *charoset* is thick and heavy, resembling the mortar the slaves worked with. Abbaye draws both concerns together: *tzarich le-kahavuye* — it must be made pungent, *zecher le-tapuach*; and *tzarich le-samchu-e* — it must be made thick, *zecher le-tit*.

A *baraita* consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's view adds: *tavlin zecher le-teven* — the spices recall the straw. There may be a wordplay here between *tavlin* and *teven*. The Rambam understands this literally, suggesting that one leaves whole pieces of spice — like cinnamon sticks — uncrushed, to more directly evoke straw. The *charoset* as a whole is described as *zecher le-tit*, recalling the mortar of Egyptian bondage.

### Charoset Preparation

Regarding the preparation: *tzarich le-kahavuye* and *tzarich le-samchu-e* — it must be pungent and thick. Tosafot adds *tzarich le-chapuyei* — it should also have a covering quality. The Yerushalmi notes: *ist avdi zecher le-dam* — there is also the symbolic recall of the blood, and this is why *charoset* is called a *mashkeh* — because wine is added, rendering it a liquid. At the time of eating, one dilutes the thick *charoset* somewhat with wine and vinegar, which is the reason it was already characterized as a *mashkeh* earlier in the discussion.

Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Tzadok reports that the merchants of *cherach* in Jerusalem would call out: "Come and take spices for the *mitzvah*" — meaning, the *mitzvah* of *charoset*. The general ruling follows Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok, and we are therefore particular about the ingredients. The Rambam in his *Perush Hamishnayot* states that according to Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok, one would even recite a *bracha — al achilat charoset* — though this is not standard practice.

The Geonim, in their responsa, prescribe preparing *charoset* from fruits to which the *Knesset Yisrael* is compared in *Shir HaShirim*: *tachat hatapuach orarticha*, *k'felach harimon*, *hate'enah chanta fageha*, *amarti eleh b'tamar*, *egoz el ginat egoz* — pomegranates, figs, dates, nuts — along with almonds, because *shekadim* evokes *shakad Hakadosh Baruch Hu al hakeitz*, God's vigilance over the moment of redemption. Hence the recipe includes nuts, wine, pomegranates, figs, dates, and so on.

A remarkable point was raised: Tosafot lived in medieval Europe at a time when Jews were accused of blood libels during Pesach, and yet the tradition includes a symbolic food that evokes blood. These historical echoes are not invented — someone heard something and misunderstood it. Such things have their tragic resonance.

## Mah Nishtanah Structure

### Four Questions

Moving now to the Mishnah on *maggid*: *mazgu lo kos sheni* — they pour the second cup of wine, after the *karpas* and *yachatz*, and now *v'kan haben sho'el* — the child asks. If the child does not know how to ask, the father prompts him. *Mah nishtanah halailah hazeh mikol haleilot?* — How is this night different from all other nights? The four distinctions enumerated in the Mishnah's version include: *chametz u'matzah* vs. *kulo matzah*; other vegetables vs. *maror*; roasted, boiled, and cooked meat vs. *kulo tzali*; and either *ein anu chayavin litbol* once or the question about dipping once vs. twice. We will see the Gemara's discussion of the dipping question shortly.

It is worth noting that not all versions of the Mishnah have four questions. The Rif has only three, omitting the *maror* question and retaining *matzah*, *tzali*, and dipping. The *tzali* question is also noteworthy: the Rashbam explains it as belonging to the time of the Beit HaMikdash. However, there is reason to think that even after the *churban*, the practice of eating specifically roasted meat on *leil seder* — *zecher la-pesach* — persisted in some communities, since an earlier Mishnah references *gufo shel pesach* being brought before the participants. That tradition was ultimately set aside under the influence of the Bavli's concern that displaying roasted meat might appear like eating *kodshim bachutz*.

### Response Structure

The Mishnah continues: *lefi da'ato shel ben aviv melamdo* — the father teaches according to the capacity of the child. *Matchil bignut u'mesayem beshevach* — he begins with denigration and ends with praise. This is the foundational structural principle of the *haggadah*: a narrative arc from darkness to light, from shame to glory.

### Dorsh Me'Arami Oved Avi

The Mishnah then states: *v'doresh me-arami oved avi ad she-yigmor kol haparsha kulo* — one expounds on *Arami oved avi* (Devarim 26:5) until he completes the entire passage. This is a remarkably open-ended, free-form structure. The basic components are: the *mah nishtanah* questions, a narrative from *avdut l'cheirut*, and the expounding of *Arami oved avi* as the textual framework.

Why use *Arami oved avi* rather than simply reading the account from *Shemot*? Because *Arami oved avi* is the one place in the *Torah* where the story of the Exodus appears in a ritual retelling context: the person bringing *bikkurim* recounts the story as his own. The distinction between *telling* and *retelling* is essential. A retelling allows the speaker to bring his own framing, to emphasize particular dimensions, to make the story genuinely personal. This is the nature of *doresh* — the intersection of the human with the divine, the *Torah she-be'al peh* enterprise applied not only to *halacha* but to the telling of the story itself. We use *Arami oved avi* as our framework in order to do a retelling of a retelling.

This also connects to the broader concept of *talmud Torah*. The *Tosefta* speaks of reciting *hilchot hapesach* as part of the *mitzvah*. The *chacham* is told *af atah emor lo ke-hilchot hapesach*. The phrase *baruch hamakom baruch hu, baruch shenatan torah le-amo Yisrael* functions as a miniature *birchat hatorah*. Thinking of *sippur yetziat mitzrayim* as a form of *talmud Torah* is a distinct theme embedded in the *seder*, and *doresh me-arami oved avi* is its classic expression — the rabbinic enterprise of *Torah she-be'al peh* applied to the telling of our story.

### Questioning Framework

The *mitzvah* of *haggadah* on *leil seder* is distinct from the daily obligation of *zecher li-yetziat mitzrayim* in several respects: it involves the symbols, it involves extended narration rather than mere mention, it involves expounding on verses. But perhaps the most significant distinction is the framework of questioning and responding. *Ki yishalcha bincha machar le-emor* — the *Torah* structures the transmission of the Exodus narrative as a response to a child's question. The entire apparatus of *kedei she-yish'alu ha-tinokot* — removing the plate, pouring the second cup — is constructed to elicit the question that the *Torah* itself expects. The type of engagement generated by genuine curiosity and responsive explanation is qualitatively different from any other mode of transmission.

### Child Asking Before Seei…

One might ask: how does the child ask questions about the *tzali* before he has seen it? He has not yet observed the roasted meat. Similarly, he has not yet experienced the second dipping — only one dipping has occurred. Tosafot's resolution is that when the plate is removed and the child asks what is happening, the father feeds him the cues: don't worry, there will be *maror*, there will be dipping, there will be more — and this prompts the child to formulate his questions in advance.

This difficulty leads to a striking interpretation found in the Rambam: *mah nishtanah halailah hazeh* is not the question — it is the beginning of the answer. The child asks whatever he asks, and the father then declares, *mah nishtanah halailah hazeh mikol haleilot* — "let me show you how different this night is" — presenting the child with the reality of the night's differences, which in turn prompts the child to ask *why*. The Rambam's formulation is analogous to *mah tovu*, which is similarly structured as a declaration rather than a genuine question. On this reading, *mah nishtanah* is what the father says — it is the beginning of the *haggadah* text rather than the child's question.

One further observation: by the time one reaches *rabban gamliel hayah omer kol shelo amar shlosha devarim eilu ba-pesach lo yatza yeday chovaso — pesach, matzah, u-maror*, three of the original four questions have already been answered through the course of the *haggadah*. The *mah nishtanah* is indeed answered — it is simply that the answer is embedded in a vast, rich narrative that intervenes before the explicit explanation of the symbols. In a sense, *rabban gamliel* is ultimately providing the direct answers that the *mah nishtanah* raised at the outset.

## Who Asks Questions

### Chacham Beno Shoalo

The Gemara: *tanu rabbanan — chacham beno shoalo*. If the son is capable of asking, the son asks. The emphasis is on *beno* — it is the son's wisdom that determines who initiates the question. *V'im eino chacham — ishto shoalto*: if the son cannot ask, the wife asks. *V'im lav — hu shoal et atzmo*: if there is no one else, he asks himself. Asking oneself is not a strange practice — it is a legitimate method of driving genuine inquiry.

### Talmidei Chachamim Asking

*V'afilu shnei talmidei chachamim she-yodi'in be-hilchot hapesach shoalin zeh la-zeh* — even two *talmidei chachamim* who are fully versed in the laws of Pesach ask one another, because the question-and-answer structure itself is essential to the *mitzvah*, independent of whether a child is present or capable. This is notable: the Gemara specifies *she-yodi'in be-hilchot hapesach* — they may know all the *halachot* and yet still be obligated to engage in questioning. Knowing the law does not exempt one from exploring the story.

### Dipping Once vs. Twice

The Gemara then takes up the dipping question from the *mah nishtanah*: *she-bechol haleilot anu matbilin paam achat halailah hazeh shtei pe'amim*. Rava challenges this formulation: is one really obligated to dip once every night? *Atu kol yoma lo sagi delo matbe'ei chada zimna?* Therefore, says Rava, the correct reading is: *shebechol haleilot ein anu chayavin litbol afilu paam achat* — on all other nights we are not obligated to dip even once, whereas tonight we dip twice.

Rav Safra then challenges even this: *chiyuva le-dardakei* — does one speak of an *obligation* to children? The framing of *chiyuv* seems out of place in something designed to arouse the children's curiosity. Rav Safra therefore proposes: *ein anu matbilin afilu paam achat* — on other nights we do not go out of our way to dip even once, whereas tonight we go out of our way to dip twice. This formulation carries the practical force of obligation without using the language of *chiyuv*. Just as the Mishnah's *anu ochlin she'ar yerakot* does not mean one is *obligated* to eat other vegetables on other nights — merely that one does not make a point of eating *maror* — so too here one does not ordinarily make a point of dipping, but tonight one does so twice.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav's Approach

*Matchil bignut u'mesayem beshevach — mai bignut?* What is the "denigration" with which the narrative begins? Rav says: *matchila ovdei avodah zarah hayu avoteinu* — at the outset our forefathers were idolaters. On this view, the story of redemption is fundamentally a story of spiritual transformation: from worshipping foreign gods to being brought close to the service of God, culminating at *ma'amad Har Sinai*.

### Shmuel's Approach

Shmuel says: *avadim hayinu* — we were slaves. On this view, the story is about physical freedom and national liberation: from slavery to autonomy, from *avdut* to *cheirut*. Both approaches are present in the *haggadah* as we have it. Immediately after *mah nishtanah*, we recite *avadim hayinu le-Pharaoh be-Mitzrayim* — Shmuel's version, as the immediate and simple answer. Then, before the fuller elaboration, we say *mitchilah ovdei avodah zarah hayu avoteinu ve-achshav kervanu HaMakom la-avodato* — Rav's version, introducing the theological framing of the narrative. The *keneged arba'ah banim* section appears between these two, perhaps because before launching into an elaborate retelling, one must first calibrate the story to the different people around the table.

### Rav Nachman Anecdote

Rav Nachman said to Darru, his slave: *avda de-mappik leih mareih le-cheirut ve-yahiv leih kaspa ve-dahava — mai ba'ei le-meimar leih?* If a master were to free his slave and give him gold and silver, what should the slave say? It is a somewhat pointed question to pose to a slave, but Darru answered: *ba'ei le-oduyei u-le-shabbuchei* — he would thank him and praise him. Rav Nachman responded: *patartan mi-lomar mah nishtanah* — you have exempted us from reciting *mah nishtanah*. He then opened and said: *avadim hayinu*.

The anecdote is illuminating on several levels. It confirms that immediately after *mah nishtanah*, the opening words are *avadim hayinu* — consistent with Shmuel's structure. It also raises the question of what exactly exempted them from *mah nishtanah*: Darru gave an *answer*, not a question. This may support the Rambam's reading that *mah nishtanah* is itself the beginning of the answer rather than the question — for here too, an answer sufficed. But it remains somewhat puzzling, since Rav Nachman is about to give the answer, *avadim hayinu*, and how Darru's response parallels *mah nishtanah* is not entirely transparent. Earlier, when Abbaye asked Rabbah about the removed table, the question was clearly a genuine expression of curiosity and so substituted for the *mah nishtanah*. Here, the analogy is less straightforward.

A question was raised regarding whether the *avodah zarah* and the *avdut* are somehow connected — whether the bondage in Egypt was in some sense a *tikkun* for the idolatry of the ancestors. This is a much larger discussion, appropriate for *seder* night itself; the lecturer noted it as a possibility without affirming it as the primary reading.

Returning to the significance of *doresh me-arami oved avi* as a framework: the Mishnah does not appear to prescribe a fixed text for how one expounds on *arami oved avi* — it is meant to be a genuine, personal retelling. The fact that *patach ve-amar avadim hayinu* sounds like a fixed text may indicate that the principals agreed on a common text that already contained both Rav's and Shmuel's components, with each simply pointing to a different part of it. But the structure of *doresh* was originally meant to be one's own engagement with the *pesukim*.

## Rabban Gamliel's Three

### Three Things Obligation

*Kol shelo amar shlosha devarim eilu ba-pesach lo yatza yeday chovaso* — whoever did not say these three things on Pesach did not fulfill his obligation. The *rishonim* debate whether this is meant literally or as rhetorical emphasis. But even granting the literal reading, one must identify which *chiyuv* has not been fulfilled. The most natural answer is the *chiyuv* of *haggadah* — the obligation to tell the story. The telling of the story requires explaining the symbolism of what one is eating. Some *rishonim*, however, suggest that not fulfilling this obligation may also mean that one has not properly discharged the *chiyuv* of *achilat matzah*, *achilat maror*, or *achilat pesach* — that the *mitzvah* of eating these foods is not complete without integrating them into the narrative and explicating their significance.

### Pesach Explanation

*Pesach al shum she-pasach HaMakom al batei avoteinu be-Mitzrayim, she-ne'emar: v'amartem zevach pesach hu la-Adonai asher pasach*. The Pesach offering is explained as recalling God's passing over — or, according to some, saving — the houses of our ancestors. Tosafot points out that the *pasuk* itself instructs *v'amartem*, "and you shall say" — on the eating of the Pesach, one must articulate its meaning. The very *pasuk* that describes the Pesach commands its verbal explanation.

### Matzah & Maror

*Matzah al shum she-nig'alu avoteinu, she-ne'emar vayofu et habatzek asher hotziu* — the *matzah* represents the moment of liberation, the dough that had no time to rise. At this point in the narrative, *matzah* emphasizes freedom rather than affliction. *Maror al shum she-mar'ru ha-mitzriyim et chayei avoteinu be-Mitzrayim* — some have *marorin* following the language of the *pasuk* *she-ne'emar vay'mar'ru et chayayhem*. The *maror* recalls the bitterness of slavery.

### Integration of Symbols

The deeper point of *rabban gamliel* is that these symbols must be *pulled into the story* — not merely eaten, not merely displayed *b'fnei kol echad ve-echad*, but actively integrated into the telling and made part of the narrative itself. What he is requiring is not just the presence of the symbols but the articulation of their meaning in the context of the story of redemption.

Looking at the *haggadah*: after *tzei u'lemad* and the full *doresh me-arami oved avi*, after *dayenu* and the unfolding narrative, one arrives at *rabban gamliel omer kol shelo amar shlosha devarim eilu* — *pesach she-hayu*, *matzah zu*, *maror zeh* — and then, holding the *kos* and the *matzah*, one says *lefikhakh anachnu chayavim lehodot*. The story has been told, the symbols have been explained and integrated, and *therefore* — *lefikhakh* — one must break into praise, thanks, and *hallel*. This is the logical and experiential culmination of the *seder*'s first half.

## Bekhol Dor VaDor

### Personal Redemption

*Bekhol dor vador chayav adam lirot et atzmo k'ilu hu yatza mi-Mitzrayim* — in every generation, a person is obligated to see himself as if he personally left Egypt. The *pasuk* cited is *she-ne'emar ve-higadeta le-vincha bayom hahu leimor ba'avur zeh asah Hashem li be-tzeiti mi-Mitzrayim* — "you shall tell your son on that day, saying: it is because of this that Hashem did for *me* when I left Egypt."

### Hagada L'Bincha

This *pasuk* is the source of the *mitzvah* of *haggadah* itself, and the word *li* — "for me" — is what makes the *mitzvah* personal. It is the directive to make the story one's own, to experience it not as ancient history but as present reality. This connects to the Darru story: only if one genuinely feels that he has been redeemed — has been a slave who was freed and given gold and silver — is he in a position to naturally express the obligation of thanks and praise.

### Lefikhakh to Hallel

It is also worth noting that at this point in the Mishnah, we appear to have transitioned from the Mishnah *speaking about* the *haggadah* to the Mishnah *containing* the text of the *haggadah* itself. The formulation *lefikhakh anachnu chayavim lehodot lehalel le-shabe'ach le-fa'er le-romem le-hadar le-varech le-aleh u-lekales le-mi she-asa la-avoteinu ve-lanu et kol hanisim ha-eilu* — with its seven expressions of praise addressed to the One who performed these miracles for our ancestors and for us — is clearly not *halachic* formulation but *haggadah* text. Similarly, *u-mimavet le-chayim, me-avdut le-cheirut, mi-yagon le-simcha, me-avel le-yom tov, me-afeila le-or gadol, u-mishiabbud li-geulah — v'nomar lefanav halleluyah* is the transition into *hallel*.

The reason there is no *bracha* recited before *hallel* on *leil seder* — unlike *hallel* in its other contexts — is that a *bracha* is appropriate when one is performing a defined ritual act. But if one has truly achieved *lefikhakh anachnu chayavim* and *chayav lirot et atzmo*, then the *hallel* is not a formalized ritual obligation but a natural expression of religious feeling. One does not frame a genuine outpouring of the heart with a *bracha* — one simply sings.

A fascinating reading was offered by one of the participants: when *rabban gamliel* says *shlosha devarim*, the three things may refer not merely to *pesach, matzah, u-maror*, but to three structural obligations: (1) explaining the symbols, (2) *chayav adam lirot et atzmo*, and (3) *le-hodot ve-le-halel*. This is a striking and original interpretation, integrating the entire sweep of the *seder*'s *maggid* into a tripartite framework — though it does not appear to have been articulated by other authorities.

## Hallel: Timing & Amount

### Hallel Extent

*Ad heikan hu omer?* — How far does one recite *hallel* in the first half of the *seder*? The remainder will be completed after the meal.

### Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

*Beit Shammai omerim ad em habanim semeichah* — Beit Shammai holds that one stops before the second psalm of *hallel*. *U-Beit Hillel omerim ad chalamish le-mayno mayim* — Beit Hillel holds that one continues through the end of the second psalm, concluding with *chalamish le-mayno mayim*.

### Immediacy vs. Narrative

What accounts for this dispute? Beit Hillel's position is the more comprehensible: the second psalm opens with *betzeit Yisrael mi-Mitzrayim*, which speaks directly of the Exodus, and it concludes with *chalamish le-mayno mayim*, a powerful image of miraculous redemption. What Beit Shammai's reasoning might be is less obvious. The best interpretation is that for Beit Shammai, reciting *betzeit Yisrael mi-Mitzrayim* takes the speaker out of the immediacy of the experience. *Betzeit Yisrael* speaks of *them*, of a past event, of *Yisrael* when *they* went out — it is a narrative of others rather than an immediate personal experience. Beit Shammai may want to preserve the first-person vividness of the moment: *halleluyah*, the spontaneous praise, without slipping into third-person historical narration. Why the *hallel* is divided into two parts at all has to do with this same tension: the first half should feel immediate and personal; the second half, recited after the meal, completes the formal ritual of *hallel*.

## Geula Blessing Debate

### Tarfon's Version

*V'chosem bi-geulah* — the Mishnah requires concluding the first half of *hallel* with the *geulah* blessing. What is that *chatimah*? *Rabbi Tarfon omer: asher ge'alanu ve-ga'al et avoteinu mi-Mitzrayim* — and he did not conclude with a *chatimah*. On Rabbi Tarfon's view, this is a short *bracha* — *baruch atah Hashem elokeinu melech ha-olam asher ge'alanu ve-ga'al et avoteinu mi-Mitzrayim* — focused entirely on the past *geulah*, with no closing formula.

### Akiva's Version

Rabbi Akiva adds: *kein Hashem elokeinu ve-elokei avoteinu yagi'einu le-moadim u-le-regalim acherim ha-ba'im likrateinu le-shalom, semeichim be-vinyan irecha ve-sasim be-avodatecha, ve-nochal sham min ha-zevachim u-min ha-pesachim* — and he closes with *baruch atah Hashem ga'al Yisrael*. Rabbi Akiva does not merely lengthen the *bracha* — he adds an entirely new dimension: the orientation toward *future* redemption, toward the rebuilding of the *Beit HaMikdash*. This is deeply characteristic of Rabbi Akiva, who throughout the destruction period — and in the context of Bar Kochba — maintained a constant focus on future *geulah*. The *haggadah* on this view is not only a story culminating in *Har Sinai* but also a story pointing forward. How much the *seder* night's narrative incorporates the theme of future redemption is itself a significant question, and Rabbi Akiva answers it emphatically.

### Rishonim on Chatimah

Tosafot and the Rashbam both clarify that the earlier line of the Mishnah — *v'chosem bi-geulah* — is not a third opinion alongside Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva, but rather the earlier, unexplicated statement that the later *tannaim* are interpreting. The Mishnah presents the phenomenon, common in rabbinic literature, where an earlier formulation — *v'chosem bi-geulah* — is stated without elaboration, and subsequent *tannaim* debate what it means. Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva represent the two positions in that debate.

### Rambam's Girsa

The Rambam's reading of the earlier Mishnah — *chayav liros et atzmo* vs. *chayav l'haros et atzmo* — presents a telling textual variant. *Chayav lirot et atzmo* means one is obligated to actually *see* oneself as if having left Egypt — a demanding standard of genuine internalization. *Chayav l'haros et atzmo* means one is obligated to *present* or *demonstrate* oneself as if — a more accessible standard that is closer to the principle of "act as if, and understanding will follow." If one cannot fully feel the experience of redemption, at minimum one should conduct oneself as though one does, and through that embodied practice, the feeling may develop.

## Lifting Objects in Seder

### Lifting Matzah & Maror

Rava states: *matzah tzarich l'hagbi'ah* — when saying *matzah zu*, one lifts the *matzah*. *Maror tzarich l'hagbi'ah* — when saying *maror zeh*, one lifts the *maror*. This is a practical form of *show and tell*: the physical objects are raised to make vivid the connection between word and symbol.

### Not Lifting Meat

*Basar ein tzarich l'hagbi'ah* — the meat, however, should not be lifted. The reason: *lo od ela she-neireh k'ochlei kodshim bachutz* — it would appear as if one were eating *kodshim* outside the *Mikdash*. Here is the opposite principle from the earlier discussion of *tzali*: while there was historically a practice of eating specifically roasted meat on *leil seder* as a *zecher la-pesach*, and while some went so far as to roast a kid goat and refer to it as the *pesach*, the Bavli's concern is that publicly indicating the meat while discussing the *pesach korban* creates the appearance of consecrating offerings outside their proper context. Therefore, though some symbolism of the *pesach* is maintained, it is deliberately downplayed — the meat is not lifted, not pointed to in the way that *matzah* and *maror* are.
