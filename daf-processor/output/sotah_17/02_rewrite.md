# Sotah 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Writing the Megillah

### Opening & Mezuzah

Daf 17 opens with a new *mishnah* that continues the procedure for the *sotah*. The preceding *mishnayot* had addressed the collection of the dirt and its placement into the vessel — the questions of where the dirt should come from, whether it should be visible on the surface of the water, and so on. Now, having established that the woman has already brought her *minchah*, the *mishnah* turns to the next stage: writing the *megillah* and scraping its contents into the water. The *kohen* comes to write the *megillah* — *ba lo lichtov et ha-megillah* — and the question immediately arises: from which point in the relevant passage does he begin writing?

### Biblical Pesukim Order

Before examining the *mishnah*'s answer, it is worth pausing to review the order of the *pesukim* in *Parshat ha-Sotah*, since the *mishnah*'s presentation does not straightforwardly follow that order.

The passage begins with the husband bringing his wife to the *kohen* along with her offering of a tenth of an *eifah* of barley flour — *lo yitzok alav shemen v'lo yitein alav levonah* — no oil or frankincense, for it is a *minchah* of jealousy, a memorial offering that recalls iniquity. That is where our *perek* began. The *kohen* then presents her before God, takes holy water in an earthen vessel, and adds dust from the floor of the *Mishkan* to the water — *v'natan el ha-mayim* — which is what the previous *mishnah* covered.

Then comes: the *kohen* stands the woman before God, dishevels her hair, places the *minchah* in her hands — and in the *kohen*'s hands are the bitter, curse-inducing waters. This passage already mentions disheveling her hair, which in fact appeared much earlier in the *mishnayot*' treatment. Next: *v'hishbia otah ha-kohen* — the *kohen* makes her take an oath, saying: "If no man has lain with you, and if you have not gone astray in impurity under your husband, *hinaki mi'mei ha-marerim ha-eileh* — be cleansed of these bitter, curse-inducing waters." Then the conditional curse: "And you, if you have gone astray under your husband, and if you have become impure, and a man has given his seed in you apart from your husband — *yitein Hashem otach l'alah v'lishvuah b'toch amech* — may the Lord make you a curse and an oath among your people, may the Lord cause your thigh to fall and your belly to swell. *Uva'u ha-mayim ha-marerim ha-eileh b'me'ayich* — and let these bitter waters enter your innards to swell the belly and cause the thigh to fall." *V'amrah ha-ishah amen amen* — and the woman shall say: amen, amen.

Then: *v'katav et ha-alot ha-eileh ha-kohen ba-sefer u-machah el mei ha-marim* — the *kohen* writes these curses in a book and scrapes them into the bitter water — and then *v'hishkah et ha-ishah*, he makes her drink.

Importantly, the *mishnah* skips entirely over the *kohen*'s oral administration of the oath and proceeds directly to the writing. This omission will prove significant later.

### Mishnah: Writing Point

The *mishnah* asks: from where does the *kohen* begin writing? The answer is: from *v'im lo shachav ish otach* — "if no man has lain with you." That is the opening of the oath formula proper, beginning with the conditional declaration of innocence. 

The *mishnah* then adds that the *kohen* does not write the instructional text embedded in the *pesukim* — *eino kotev v'hishbia ha-kohen et ha-ishah*, the narration that "the *kohen* shall make her swear." The text to be written contains, as it were, quotation marks: the quoted speech of the oath, not the stage directions. And at the conclusion, the *mishnah* states *eino kotev v'amrah ha-ishah amen amen* — the woman's response of *amen amen* is likewise not written, for that is her reaction to the oath, not the oath itself. This, the *mishnah* implies, is the position of the *tanna kamma*.

### Tanna Kamma View

The *tanna kamma*'s approach, then, is to write the full quoted text of the oath — both the conditional declaration of innocence ("if no man has lain with you, be cleansed") and the curse that follows — while omitting the narrative instructions embedded in the middle of the *pesukim* and the woman's concluding response of *amen amen*.

One might ask whether it is problematic to omit the middle section of the *pesukim*. This is precisely the concern raised by the *tanna kamma*'s formulation: you are not cutting *within* any one *pasuk*, but rather excising what is, in context, parenthetical instruction. The quoted speech continues uninterrupted; only the interposed narrative direction is dropped. That is a natural reading — you write the words in quote marks, so to speak, and skip the words outside them.

### Rabbi Yehudah View

*Rabbi Yehuda* takes a far more restrictive position. *Kol atzmo eino kotev ela* — his entire point is to write nothing other than the curse formula itself: *v'yitein Hashem otach l'alah u'lishvuah b'toch amech, uva'u ha-mayim ha-marerim ha-eileh b'me'ayich* — may the Lord make you a curse and an oath, and let the waters enter your innards. *Rashi* explains *kol atzmo* as an idiom indicating that one must be scrupulously careful to write only the curse phrase and nothing beyond it.

*Rabbi Yehuda* agrees with the *tanna kamma* that *v'amrah ha-ishah amen amen* is not written. But he goes far further: he also omits the opening conditional — the clause about being cleansed if innocent — and writes nothing of the conditional framing of guilt. Only the curse itself goes into the *megillah*.

This position is striking in light of a theme that has run throughout the *mishnayot*: the *sotah* procedure is presented throughout with a presumption of guilt. The process has consistently been framed not as a clarification of her innocence but as the administration of punishment for her transgression. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s view reflects this most sharply — the *megillah* contains only the curse; there is no trace in the written text of the possibility that she is innocent.

One might argue, as was raised in discussion, that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is not necessarily about a presumption of guilt, but about a more precise understanding of the *mechanics* of the water's power. The water is the *me'arerim* — the cursing water. What makes it cursed is the written curse scraped into it. The curse-producing element is specifically the curse text. If she is innocent, the water will not harm her regardless — so perhaps the rationale is about the function of the *megillah* rather than any presumption about her guilt.

That said, the objection remains: if one thinks about the *power of the verses entering the water*, then the water itself is also conditional — because the full *pasuk* presents a conditional reality. If she is innocent, *v'niktah v'nizre'ah zara* — she will be cleansed and will bear seed. Not including those words means, in a sense, that only the curse element is being placed into the water. This tension is not fully resolved in the *mishnah* and awaits the *Gemara*'s analysis.

## Gemara Debate

### Rabbi Meir: Implicit

The *Gemara* asks: *b'mai kamifligi* — what is the source of the disagreement? *B'hai kra kamifligi* — they dispute the reading of the following *pasuk*: *v'katav et ha-alot ha-eileh ha-kohen ba-sefer* — he shall write these curses in the book.

*Rabbi Meir* — who is identified as the *tanna kamma* — holds that *alot*, the curses, means *alot mamash*, the actual curse text. The additional *hei* at the beginning — *ha-alot* — is a *ribui*, an expansion: *l'rabot klalot ha-ba'ot machamat brachot* — to include the curses that come by way of the blessing. What does this mean? The phrase *hinaki mi'mei ha-marerim* — be cleansed from the bitter waters — is ostensibly a blessing. But it implies a curse: if you are not innocent, you will not be cleansed. The *hei* of *ha-alot* therefore includes even this implicit curse — the conditional statement of innocence — within what must be written, because it is the flip side of the curse.

Then *eileh* — "these" — serves to limit: *l'ma'utei klalot she'be-Mishneh Torah* — to exclude the curses that appear in *Parshat Ki Tavo*, which uses the same language of *alah*. Those *klalot* are not to be included. And *ha-eileh*, with the additional *hei*, further limits — *l'ma'utei tzava'ot v'kabalot amen* — to exclude the instructional portions and the woman's *amen amen*.

### Rabbi Yose: No Cutting

*Rabbi Yose* accepts the overall framework of the *derashah* but extends it in a different direction. He holds that *et ha-alot ha-eileh* — the word *et* — comes to include the instructional text of the *pesukim* and the woman's response of *amen amen*. That is, *et l'rabot tzava'ot v'kabalot* — the *et* expands what is written to include the entire *pasuk* without interruption. His concern, in effect, is that one does not chop a *pasuk* in the middle. Starting from the beginning of the oath and including the conclusion — the woman's *amen amen* — he writes the entire passage without cutting any portion out.

*Rabbi Yose*'s position is thus actually broader than the *tanna kamma* on the question of the instructional text embedded in the middle. The *tanna kamma* cuts out the narrative directions; *Rabbi Yose* does not cut anything.

### Meir Responds

*Rabbi Meir*, by contrast, is *esin lo darish* — he does not accept *et* as a *ribui*. He is simply not impressed with the word *et* as a basis for including additional content. Therefore, unlike *Rabbi Yose*, he does omit the narrative instructions and the *amen amen* response.

### Yehudah: Minimal

*Rabbi Yehuda* takes the same words — *alot*, *ha-alot*, *eileh*, *ha-eileh* — but reads all of them as *mi'utim*, limitations, rather than expansions. *Alot mamash* — curses, yes, but only the actual curse text. *Ha-alot* — the *hei* is not a *ribui* but a *mi'ut*: *l'ma'utei klalot ha-ba'ot machamat brachot* — specifically to exclude the conditional innocence clause at the beginning, which is not itself a curse. *Eileh* — to exclude the curses of *Mishneh Torah*. *Ha-eileh* — to agree that the narrative instruction and the *amen amen* are excluded. Thus *Rabbi Yehuda* uses every extra letter to narrow, narrowing the written text to only the curse formula at the end.

The *Gemara* thus presents a spectrum: write everything (*Rabbi Yose*), write the full oath but omit the instructions and *amen amen* (*tanna kamma / Rabbi Meir*), or write only the curse (*Rabbi Yehuda*).

It is worth pausing to consider whether these *derashot* reflect genuine hermeneutic differences — about when a *hei* expands versus when it limits, or whether *et* carries *ribui* — or whether each *tanna* began with a different intuition about what makes sense, and then found the hermeneutic language to support it. One position finds it logically indispensable to include both sides of the conditional; another feels that only the curse need enter the water; a third insists that one does not interrupt a *pasuk*. It is an open question whether the hermeneutic generated the conclusion or the conclusion generated the hermeneutic.

### Hei Analysis

The *Gemara* itself asks a sharp question: both *ha-alot* and *ha-eileh* contain an extra *hei* — yet *Rabbi Meir* treats *ha-alot* as expansive and *ha-eileh* as restrictive. Why does the same letter do opposite work in each location?

The *Gemara* answers: it depends on context. *Hei de-gabei de-ribuiya hi* — when the *hei* attaches to a word that is inherently expansive, as *alot* is (since you are being told to write the *alot*, which implies writing what belongs to them), the *hei* expands further, including even the implicit curses at the beginning. *Hei de-gabei de-mi'uta* — when the *hei* attaches to a word that is inherently limiting, as *eileh* ("these" already restricts), the *hei* reinforces the limitation, excluding even the narrative instruction and the *amen amen*.

In the end, the dispute distills to four positions about what gets written: the *pasuk* in full without excision (*Rabbi Yose*); the full oath text minus embedded instruction and the woman's response (*tanna kamma*); the full oath text including the innocence clause, minus instruction and *amen amen* (*Rabbi Meir*, which on one reading is the same as *tanna kamma*); or only the bare curse formula (*Rabbi Yehuda*).

## Inverse Inference

### Meir & Inference

The *Gemara* now raises a pointed challenge to *Rabbi Meir*. *Rabbi Meir* holds that the opening conditional clause — "if no man has lain with you, be cleansed" — is an *implicit curse*, and that is why the *hei* of *ha-alot* includes it. But *Rabbi Meir* is famously of the opinion that *mi-klal lav attah shomea hen* — from a negative formulation you cannot infer a positive, and from a positive you cannot infer a negative. In other words, if the *pasuk* says "if you are innocent, be cleansed," one cannot automatically infer the inverse: "if you are guilty, you will not be cleansed." Yet that inference is precisely what makes the innocence clause an *implicit curse* according to *Rabbi Meir*'s analysis.

This principle is established in the context of *tena'im* — the formal conditions that must be stated when making stipulations. *Rabbi Meir* holds that a *tenai kaful* — a double condition — is required: you must spell out explicitly both sides of the stipulation. The model is Moshe's agreement with *Bnei Gad u-Bnei Reuven*: *im ya'avru chalutzim* — if they cross in arms before their brothers and the land is subdued, they receive their portion *me-ever la-Yarden*; *v'im lo ya'avru* — and if they do not cross, they will settle among you in Canaan. Both sides must be stated, because you cannot logically derive the inverse from a single conditional statement alone.

Indeed, as anyone who has studied formal logic knows, if P then Q does not entail if not-P then not-Q. The statement "if it rains, we will not play ball" does not mean "if it does not rain, we will play ball" — it only means that if we did play ball, it cannot have rained. So "if you are innocent, be cleansed" does not strictly imply "if you are guilty, you will be punished." You would need that to be stated explicitly.

The question is whether *Rabbi Meir*'s principle that one cannot infer the inverse applies broadly and universally, or whether it is limited to formal *tena'im* — where the concern is about the strict legal sufficiency of a stipulation's formulation. If it is a broad principle, then *Rabbi Meir* cannot treat "be cleansed if innocent" as implying an inverse curse for guilt, and the whole basis of his *derashah* unravels.

### Tanchum: Chinaki

*Rabbi Tanchum* resolves this difficulty with a linguistic observation: *chinaki ktiv* — the *pasuk* reads *hinaki*, which can be parsed as *chinaki*, meaning "be strangled." If you read the conditional clause with that double valence — if you are innocent, *hinaki*, be cleansed; but embedded in the very word is also *chinaki*, be strangled — then the clause is not merely a statement of potential innocence from which a curse must be inferred inversely. The curse is, in a sense, already there within the word itself, as a play on its letters. This makes the clause less *implicitly* cursed and more directly so, giving *Rabbi Meir* firmer ground to include it as part of the *alot* that must be written.

### Akiva: Fire

At this point the *Gemara* introduces a celebrated *derashah* of *Rabbi Akiva*. *Ish v'ishah* — man and woman. Take the letters of *ish*, aleph-yud-shin, and *ishah*, aleph-shin-hei. God's name, *yud-hei*, is distributed within the words of man and woman: the *yud* in *ish*, the *hei* in *ishah*. *Zachu* — if they merit — *shechina beineihem* — the divine presence is between them. If they do not merit — *lo zachu* — *esh ochaltan* — fire consumes them. Remove the *yud* and the *hei* and what remains in both words is *aleph-shin*, *esh*, fire.

*Rashi* explains *zachu* in concrete moral terms: *lalechet be-derech yesharah* — to walk on the straight path — *shelo yehei hu no'ef v'lo hi no'efet* — that he should not be an adulterer and she not an adulteress. This is a striking formulation, because it reintroduces the man as a potential offender on equal terms with the woman. It is worth noting that technically, under *halacha*, a man commits adultery only when he sleeps with a *married woman*; sleeping with an unmarried woman, while prohibited, is not the technical offense of *ni'uf* against his own wife. Yet *Rashi* here seems to read the moral register more broadly: cheating on one's spouse — regardless of the technical legal category — tears the marriage, and then *esh ochaltan*.

The *Gemara* continues: *amar Rava, v'de-ishah adifa mi-de-ish* — *Rava* observes that the woman's fire is greater than the man's. In the word *ish*, the *aleph* and *shin* of *esh* are separated by the *yud* — God's letter stands between the fire-letters and keeps them in check. In *ishah*, the *aleph* and *shin* are adjacent — *mitzarfis* — the fire letters are already joined. *Rashi* glosses: *esh shel ishah kashah u-mi-maharet le-echol* — the woman's fire is fierce and swift to consume. Whether this reflects something about the nature of temptation, the social dynamics of the household when infidelity occurs, or some other concern, the *Gemara* does not spell out fully, and the plain meaning remains somewhat open.

The connection of this *derashah* to the *sotah* procedure is worth appreciating. The entire process takes place in the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, where God's presence dwells. God's name is being written on the parchment and then scraped into the water. If the couple's relationship has deteriorated to the point that God's name is not sufficiently present in their marriage to avert this crisis — that the fire has not been kept in check — then the procedure is an attempt to bring God's name more directly into the situation and, through it, resolve the conflict. If she is innocent, the water — containing the divine name — douses the fire. If she is guilty, the fire consumes. It is a fitting thematic arc.

## Dust & Divine Name

### Rava: Merit

*Amar Rava* — why does the *Torah* prescribe bringing *afar*, dust, for the *sotah*? *Zachtah, yotzei mimenah ben k'Avraham Avinu* — if she merits, she will bring forth a son like *Avraham Avinu*, of whom it is written *afar va-efer* — dust and ashes. *Lo zachtah, tachzor la-afarah* — if she does not merit, she returns to her dust.

The connection to *v'niktah v'nizre'ah zara* — she will be cleansed and bear seed — is thus given a more specific content: the child she will bear is compared to *Avraham Avinu*. And if not, she reverts to dust — a fitting end.

### Abraham: Merit

The *Gemara* continues with a *derashah* of *Rava*: because *Avraham Avinu* said *v'anochi afar va-efer* — and I am but dust and ashes — his descendants merited two *mitzvot* connected to *afar* and *efer*: *efer parah* — the ashes of the *parah adumah* — and *afar sotah* — the dust of the *sotah*.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: but there is also *afar kisuy ha-dam* — dust used to cover blood after *shechita* — which is also a *mitzvah* involving dust. The answer: *hasam hachsher mitzvah ika, hana'ah leka* — there it is merely the performance of a *mitzvah*, but there is no concrete benefit. Here, both the *efer parah* and the *afar sotah* produce tangible benefit — the ashes of the *parah adumah* restore *taharah*, and the dust of the *sotah* resolves strife between husband and wife.

### Derasha: Efer

The *Gemara* then records a second *derashah* of *Rava*: because *Avraham* said *im mi-chut v'ad s'roch na'al* — from a thread to a sandal strap, I will take nothing of the spoils of *Sdom* — his descendants merited two *mitzvot*: *chut shel techelet* — the thread of *techelet* in *tzitzit* — and *retzuah shel tefillin* — the strap of *tefillin*, which, like a shoelace, is made of leather.

Again the *Gemara* requires that these be *mitzvot* with concrete this-worldly benefit. *Retzuah shel tefillin* — this is established by the *pasuk*: *v'ra'u kol amei ha-aretz ki shem Hashem nikra alecha v'yare'u mimeka* — all the peoples of the earth will see that God's name is called upon you, and they will be in awe of you. A *baraita* identifies this with the *tefillin she-ba-rosh* — and *Rabbi Eliezer ha-Gadol* confirms it. Wearing *tefillin* on one's head causes others to perceive one's connection to God and generates protective awe.

### Techelet: Throne

Then the *Gemara* asks: *ela chut shel techelet mai hi* — in what way does wearing *techelet* produce concrete benefit? *Detanya, Rabbi Meir omer*: *mah nishtanah techelet mi-kol minei tzivonin* — what distinguishes *techelet* from all other colors? Because *techelet* resembles the sea, the sea resembles the sky, and the sky resembles the *kise ha-kavod* — the divine throne. This is supported by two *pesukim*: *v'tachtat raglav k'ma'aseh livnat ha-sapir u'che'etzem ha-shamayim la-tohar* — and under His feet was like the work of a sapphire brick, like the very essence of the sky in its purity — and *k'mareh even sapir dmut kise* — like the appearance of a sapphire stone is the form of the throne. The *techelet* thus draws one's gaze upward — from sea to sky to throne — and creates a genuine connection to God, a benefit of a different order than financial gain but concrete and real nonetheless.

The pattern in all these *derashot* is consistent: *Avraham*'s merit produced *mitzvot* for his descendants that carry not merely the reward of performance but a palpable this-worldly effect — purification, domestic peace, divine awe, and connection to God.

## Writing Materials

### Surfaces

A new *mishnah* addresses the physical requirements for writing the *megillah*. The *kohen* may not write on a *luach* — *Rashi* explains this as a wooden tablet — nor on *neyar*, paper, which *Rashi* describes as made from a fibrous material, presumably a papyrus-like substance. Nor may he write on *diphera*, which is untreated or incompletely treated hide. Rather he must write on the *megillah*, that is, on properly prepared parchment — *klaf*.

It is interesting that the *mishnah* uses the word *megillah* here rather than *klaf*, since the *Torah* itself uses the word *sefer*. The *mishnah* contrasts *diphera* with *megillah* rather than with *klaf* — the constructed *megillah* as an object, not merely the raw parchment. The *pasuk* cited is *she-ne'emar ba-sefer* — for so the *Torah* says: in a *sefer*.

### Ink Requirements

The *mishnah* continues: one may not write using *komos* — *Rashi* identifies this as a tree sap — nor *kankantom*, which is copper sulphate, nor with any substance that leaves a permanent mark — *kol davar she-roshem*. The concern is indelibility: if the ink is absorbed into the parchment such that even scraping leaves a residue, the substance is invalid. The ink must sit on the surface of the parchment without being absorbed, so that it can be fully scraped off and dissolved into the water.

The valid substance is *deyo* — the standard ink used for *Sifrei Torah* and the like, which sits on the surface and can be scraped off cleanly. The *pasuk* cited is *she-ne'emar u-machah* — the *Torah* says "and he erased" — requiring *ketav she-yachol le-himachot*, writing that can be erased. This implies not merely that the ink can be removed, but that when scraped it leaves nothing behind — the words must be fully dissolved into the water.

## Validity Conditions

### Nighttime Invalid

*Rava* rules: *megillat sotah she-ketavah ba-laylah pesulah* — if the *megillah* was written at night, it is invalid. The source is a *gezerah shavah*: *v'asah lah ha-kohen et kol ha-Torah ha-zot* — the *kohen* shall perform for her all this instruction — is linked to *al pi ha-Torah asher yoru-cha v'al ha-mishpat* — according to the instruction they teach you and according to the judgment. Just as judgment must take place during the day — *mishpat ba-yom* — so too *megillat sotah* must be written during the day.

### Backward Invalid

If the *megillah* was written *le-mafrea* — in reverse order, writing from the end backwards even if the final product reads correctly — it is invalid. The source: *v'katav et ha-alot ha-eileh* — write these curses — meaning write them *ke-heichi de-chtivei*, in the order they are written. The *pasuk* implies forward sequential writing, not writing that arrives at the correct text by working backwards.

### Pre-Oath Invalid

If the *megillah* was written before the woman accepted the oath — *kodem she-tikabel aleha ha-shevuah* — it is invalid. The source: *v'hishbia* — he made her swear — *v'achar kach v'katav* — and afterwards he wrote. This is the explicit sequence of the *pesukim*, and it must be followed. Notably, the *mishnah* itself skipped over the oath-taking entirely and moved directly to the writing, yet the actual *halacha* requires that the oath precede the writing. This underscores what *Tosafot* will observe: the order of the *mishnah* does not determine the *halachic* order.

### No Scoring Invalid

*Ketavah iggeret pesulah* — if it was written as a letter, it is invalid. *Rashi* explains this as writing without *shirtut* — scoring. *Sifrei kodesh* are written with scored lines on the parchment; if the *megillah* was written without this scoring, it is considered a mere letter and not a *sefer*, and therefore invalid. The derivation: *ba-sefer amrah Rachmana* — the *Torah* said it must be in a *sefer*, meaning with the formal requirements of *sifrei kodesh*.

### Two Parchments

If the *megillah* was written on two separate pieces of parchment — say, the text did not fit on one piece — it is invalid: *sefer echad amrah Rachmana, v'lo shenayim u-sheloshah sefarim* — the *Torah* required one book, not two or three.

### Intermittent Invalid

Finally, if the *kohen* wrote one letter and scraped it off, wrote the next letter and scraped it off — so that the entire text was never present on the parchment at one time — this is invalid: *d'chtiv v'asah lah ha-kohen et kol ha-Torah ha-zot* — the entire text must be present at once before it is scraped into the water.

## Tosafot Analysis

### Sefer Distinction

*Tosafot* raises an extensive question about the ruling that writing on a *luach* or *neyar* is invalid. In other halachic contexts — for example, a *get* — the *Torah* uses the language of *v'katav lah sefer keritut*, and the rabbis derive from the word *sefer* there that one may write a *get* on virtually any surface, even a cow's horn. Why, then, is the *megillat sotah* restricted to parchment?

The distinction *Tosafot* draws is the preposition *bet*. By the *sotah*, the *Torah* says *ba-sefer* — in a book — which places the emphasis on the physical object itself. The *megillah* must be a *sefer* in the full, physically realized sense. By the *get*, the *Torah* says *sefer keritut* — a document of severance — and the word *sefer* there is taken by the rabbis as *sefirat devarim be'alma*, a mere recounting of separation, emphasizing the content rather than the physical form. Hence the broad permissiveness for writing surfaces by a *get* but strict limitation to *klaf* by the *megillat sotah*.

Separately, the *Yerushalmi* raises the question of whether parchment from a non-kosher animal may be used. One consideration there is: if the woman refuses to drink and the *megillah* is not erased, you would be leaving God's name written on parchment from a non-kosher animal, which is unseemly. The *Yerushalmi* also asks whether the *megillah*, before being erased, has *kedushah* — does it convey impurity to the hands the way sacred *sifrei kodesh* do? The tentative answer is that it may not, because it was written for the purpose of being erased, which may deprive it of the standing of a sacred *sefer*. These remain interesting questions about the status of an object whose nature is fundamentally transitional.

On the matter of the nighttime invalidation, *Tosafot* addresses what type of judgment *sotah* resembles. *Dinei mamonot* — monetary cases — must begin during the day but may conclude at night. *Dinei nefashot* — capital cases — must be conducted entirely during the day, beginning and end. *Tosafot* rules that *sotah* resembles *dinei nefashot*: *kol din sotah, bein gmar bein techilah, pasul ba-laylah* — every aspect of the *sotah* process, whether beginning or completion, is invalid if performed at night. This framing of *sotah* as analogous to *dinei nefashot* is significant: it reflects the understanding that what is being adjudicated is, in effect, a capital matter. If found guilty, the woman will die — and *Chazal* understand this even though the *pesukim* do not state death explicitly. Treating it as *dinei nefashot* is therefore consistent with that thematic reading of the entire procedure.

### Scoring Requirement

*Tosafot* then turns to the requirement of *shirtut*. Why does the *pasuk* need to tell us that writing without scoring invalidates the *megillah*? There is already a rule elsewhere — derived at the opening of *Gittin* — that one may not write even three words from a *pasuk* without scoring, even in the context of ordinary correspondence. Given that this is already a general prohibition, surely the *megillat sotah* must also be written with scoring. What novel information does the *pasuk* add?

*Tosafot*'s answer: the existing requirement establishes a prohibition on writing without scoring, but a prohibition does not automatically mean that an object produced in violation of that prohibition is halachically invalid. One might have violated the rule and yet produced a valid *megillah*. The *pasuk* comes to tell us that here, the absence of scoring is not merely a procedural violation — it renders the *megillah* itself *pesulah* as an object. The formality of *ba-sefer* is *me'akev*, not merely *l'chatchilah*.

### Tosafot Question

*Tosafot* then develops an extended analysis of when *shirtut* is required and when it is not, working through the case of *tefillin*, where the *pesukim* are written without the full scoring applied to a *Sefer Torah*. *Tosafot* proposes distinguishing between two types of scoring: a perimeter score around the entire section, which satisfies the general prohibition against writing three words of a *pasuk* without any scored border, and the line-by-line scoring required for full formal *sifrei kodesh*.

At the conclusion, *Tosafot* cites a tradition in the name of *Tosafot Rabbeinu* from *Masechet Gittin*, transmitted in the name of *Rabbi Eliyahu ha-Tzarfati*: when one quotes the language of a *pasuk* in correspondence not as a direct citation of a *pasuk* but as flowery Hebrew expression — *amira be'alma lifrosh b'shalom* — using the words as literary idiom rather than as scripture, there is no obligation of *shirtut*. But if one is writing a letter and explicitly cites a *pasuk* as such — "as the *pasuk* says..." followed by the quoted text — then *ki hai gavna assur le-michtav belo shirtut*, one may not write it without scoring.

*Tosafot* then adds its own question — and notably does not resolve it: *v'lo yadana minan li-michtav be-peirushin pesukim belo shirtut* — we do not know the source permitting the writing of *pesukim* in commentaries without *shirtut*, in cases where the *pasuk* is being brought as an explicit citation. Commentaries on *Torah* and *Gemara* routinely cite *pesukim* explicitly as *pesukim* without performing *shirtut*. How is this permitted? *Tosafot* leaves the question open.

A parallel question concerns quoting partial *pesukim* — whether citing only a portion of a *pasuk*, particularly when that portion includes *shem Hashem*, is permissible. *Rav Ovadia Yosef* addresses this in a *teshuvah* and concludes that the *Gemara* itself quotes partial *pesukim* containing *shem Hashem* without concern, demonstrating that the rule of *kol pasuk de-lo paskei Moshe* — not dividing a *pasuk* — applies specifically to a ritual recitation context, not to learning, discussion, or citation in analysis. The *megillat sotah* itself, as a ritual act, might be governed by that stricter standard, while ordinary Torah discourse is not. But the question of *shirtut* in correspondence and commentary remains, as *Tosafot* acknowledges, unresolved.
