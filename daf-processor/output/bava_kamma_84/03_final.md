# Bava Kamma 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ayin Tachat Ayin Intro

### Rambam Classification

Before turning to the *gemara* itself, it is worth pausing on a striking passage from the Rambam at the opening of *Hilchot Chovel U'Mazik* in *Sefer Nezikin*. The Rambam acknowledges that the topic of *ayin tachat ayin* — how we assess the valuation of damages when one person strikes another — is deeply polemical. From the plain sense of the *pesukim*, one might conclude that the Torah actually requires inflicting an equivalent injury upon the wrongdoer. Yet the Rambam insists: *af al pi she-devarim elu nir'im me-inyan Torah she-bichtav*, even though the way the *halacha* comes out — requiring monetary restitution — is supported by *drashot* and makes a certain amount of sense based on the *pesukim*, *kulan mephorashin hen mipi Moshe mei-Har Sinai*, all of these laws are in the category of *devarim hamekubalim mipi Moshe Rabbeinu*, things transmitted orally to Moshe and passed down through every generation. The Rambam continues: *elu avoteinu danim be-veit dino shel Yehoshua, beit dino shel Shmuel HaRamati ve-chol beit din u-veit din she-amdu mi-mot Moshe ve-ad achshav* — every *beit din* in Jewish history, from the time of Yehoshua through Shmuel HaRamati and down to the present, has always understood *ayin tachat ayin* to mean monetary compensation, because that is the tradition going back to Sinai. The *drashot* exist to justify what is, at bottom, an oral tradition — one that might have a certain hint in the Torah text or make a certain amount of logical sense, but whose ultimate authority rests on transmission rather than derivation.

### Five Categories

The Rambam's use of the term *devarim hamekubalim mipi Moshe Rabbeinu* is his own formulation; the *gemara* does not use that precise language. According to the Rambam, there are five categories of *Torah She-Ba'al Peh*. The first is *devarim hamekubalim mipi Moshe Rabbeinu*: received tradition that has a hint in the text and about which there is no *machloket*. The second is *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*: tradition that has no connection to the text whatsoever, and also has no *machloket*. The difference between these two categories is significant: the first is where *drashot* appear, while the second is purely tradition with no textual anchor at all. The third category — which generates virtually all *machlokot* — consists of laws derived from *drashot*, where a certain discretion is involved in the interpretive act and disagreement can therefore arise. The remaining two categories are *gezeirot* and *takanot*, the distinction between which is a topic for another occasion.

### Perek HaChovel Setup

We pick up from the fourth line from the bottom of 83b in *Bava Kamma*, where we left off yesterday. *Perek HaChovel* opened with a *mishnah* listing five types of compensation that a tortfeasor must pay — *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, *shevet*, and *boshet* — after which the *gemara* launched into the question of how we know that monetary compensation is required at all. Doesn't *ayin tachat ayin* sound as though one who blinds another's eye should have his own eye put out? Yesterday we saw the first major attempt: a *gezeirah shavah* from *makeh makeh*, deriving from the case of injuring an animal — *makeh beheima yeshalmena* — that injury requires monetary payment. Today's *daf* continues with many further attempted proofs, along with their objections and in some cases their rejections. The broader dynamic here reflects what the Rambam was pointing to: in defending a fundamentally oral tradition against critics — including, in the medieval period, the Karaites, against whom Sa'adia Gaon polemicized extensively — it became important for *Chazal* to justify the tradition exegetically, even when what is ultimately being demonstrated is the textual support for a practice already known through received law.

## Dostai: Unequal Eyes

### Large vs Small Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״ – מָמוֹן. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר מָמוֹן, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא עַיִן מַמָּשׁ? אָמַרְתָּ, הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיְתָה עֵינוֹ שֶׁל זֶה גְּדוֹלָה וְעֵינוֹ שֶׁל זֶה קְטַנָּה, הֵיאַךְ אֲנִי קוֹרֵא בֵּיהּ ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״?
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda says:** The phrase: **"An eye for an eye"** (Leviticus 24:20), means **monetary restitution.** Do **you say** that he must pay the victim **monetary restitution, or is it only** teaching that the one who caused the injury must lose **an actual eye? You say:** There may be a case where **the eye of** the one who caused the injury **is large and the eye of** the injured party is **small. How can I read** and literally apply the phrase **"an eye for an eye" in this** case?

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda omer: ayin tachat ayin — mamon ata omer, o eino ela ayin mamash?* Is it money, or does it mean a literal eye? His argument proceeds as follows: *harei she-hayah eino shel zeh gedolah ve-eino shel zeh ketanah* — what if the *mazik*, the person who caused the injury, had a large eye, and the *nizak*, the person who was injured, had a small eye? The question of what precisely "large" and "small" means here is itself worth considering. Some *mefarshim* might read it as referring to the quality of vision rather than the physical dimensions of the eye, though the text literally says size. In any event, the thrust of the argument is that full reciprocal punishment becomes nonsensical once we acknowledge that there is virtually no case in which the equivalence will be perfectly equal.

### Equal Law Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: כָּל כִּי הַאי – שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ מָמוֹנָא; הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״מִשְׁפַּט אֶחָד יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״ – מִשְׁפָּט הַשָּׁוֶה לְכוּלְּכֶם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the derivation: **And if you would say** that **in all cases like this,** where their eyes are different sizes, the injured party **takes monetary restitution** from the one who caused him injury, but in a case where their eyes are the same size, the one who caused injury is punished by actually having his eye removed, this cannot be, as **the Torah said: "You shall have one manner of law"** (Leviticus 24:22), teaching that the **law** shall be **equal for all of you.**

*Heichi ani kore ba ayin tachat ayin?* How can I read this as meaning one eye in place of the other when they are not equal? *Ve-chi teima kol ki hai shakil minei mamona* — so perhaps then in such a case you take money from him — *ha-Torah amra: mishpat echad yihyeh lachem*, the Torah requires that the law be equally applicable. *Mishpat ha-shaveh le-chulchem* — the standard of justice must be consistent across all cases. If one person has a large eye and another has a small eye, then the law will produce an asymmetric result: blinding the *mazik*'s very large eye would be a greater loss than the injury he originally caused, or vice versa.

### Vision Not Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא נְהוֹרָא שְׁקַיל מִינֵּיהּ, נְהוֹרָא אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא נִשְׁקוֹל מִינֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages object to this derivation and **say: What** is **the difficulty** in saying that his eye should be blinded? **Perhaps,** as the one who caused the injury **took** the **sight from** the injured party's eye, **the Merciful One states** that the court should **take** the **sight from** his eye as well, no matter the size of the eye.

The *gemara* objects to Rabbi Dostai's proof: *amrei: mai kushya? Dilma nehora shakil minei nehora amar Rachmana nishkol minei* — perhaps it is only the vision that the Torah speaks of, not the physical eye itself. If so, the size of the eye is irrelevant; what matters is that you take away his vision, just as he took away the other person's vision, and the two are considered equivalent. *De-ilu* — in that case it would indeed be permissible to blind him, since it would be fully equivalent.

### Capital Case Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָטָן שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַגָּדוֹל וְגָדוֹל שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַקָּטָן, הֵיכִי קָטְלִינַן לֵיהּ? הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״מִשְׁפַּט אֶחָד יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״ – מִשְׁפָּט הַשָּׁוֶה לְכוּלְּכֶם! אֶלָּא נְשָׁמָה שְׁקַיל מִינֵּיהּ, נְשָׁמָה אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא נִשְׁקוֹל מִינֵּיהּ; הָכִי נָמֵי, נְהוֹרָא שְׁקַיל מִינֵּיהּ, נְהוֹרָא אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא נִשְׁקוֹל מִינֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** in the case of **a small** person **who killed a large** person, **or a large** person **who killed a small** person, **how do we kill** the murderer? If one suggests that in such a case a monetary penalty will be imposed, **the Torah stated: "You shall have one manner of law"** (Leviticus 24:22), teaching that the **law** shall be **equal for all of you,** so the punishment must be the same for all murderers. **Rather,** explain that since the murderer **took the life of** the victim, **the Merciful One states that** the court should likewise **take the life from him. So too,** since the one who caused the injury **took** the **sight from** the eye of the injured party, **the Merciful One states** that the court should likewise **take** the **sight from** his eye. Therefore, the Gemara does not accept the derivation of Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda, in accordance with the objection of the Sages.

This response to Rabbi Dostai leads the *gemara* to press further: *lo teima hachi — katan she-harag et ha-gadol, be-gadol she-harag et ha-katan, heichi katlinan lei?* What about a short person who killed a tall person, or a large person who killed a small person — *ha-Torah amra mishpat echad yihyeh lachem, mishpat ha-shaveh le-chulchem*? In capital cases, the life of the smaller person and the life of the larger person are considered equivalent in law. The court does not say: the life about to be taken by execution is worth more or less than the life that was taken in the crime. *Ela neshama shakil minei neshama amar Rachmana nishkol minei* — one life is equivalent to another life, because the Torah commanded that you take a life for a life. *Hacha nami nehora shakil minei nehora amar Rachmana nishkol minei* — so too here, one person's vision is equivalent to another's vision, and that is what you take. This constitutes a rejection of Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda's proof: the analogy to capital cases shows that courts routinely treat unequal things as legally equivalent, so the problem of unequal eye size does not force us to monetary compensation. However, the underlying question — whether *ayin tachat ayin* means money or actual physical injury — remains very much in dispute, and the *gemara* will press ahead with many further attempts.

## Shimon: Impossible Cases

### Blind Blinding Blind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״ – מָמוֹן. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר מָמוֹן, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא עַיִן מַמָּשׁ? הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה סוֹמֵא, וְסִימֵּא; קִיטֵּעַ, וְקִיטַּע; חִיגֵּר, וְחִיגֵּר; הֵיאַךְ אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים בָּזֶה ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״ – וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״מִשְׁפַּט אֶחָד יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״, מִשְׁפָּט הַשָּׁוֶה לְכוּלְּכֶם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another derivation: It **is taught** in **another** *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: "An eye for an eye"** (Leviticus 24:20), is referring to **monetary restitution.** Do **you say** that this is referring to **monetary restitution, or is it only** teaching that the one who caused the injury must lose **an actual eye?** There may be a case where **there was a blind person and he blinded** another, or there was **one with a severed limb and he severed** the limb of another, or there was **a lame person and he caused** another **to be lame. In this** case, **how can I fulfill "an eye for an eye"** literally, when he is already lacking the limb that must be injured? If one will suggest that in that case, a monetary penalty will be imposed, that can be refuted: **But the Torah stated: "You shall have one manner of law"** (Leviticus 24:22), which teaches that **the law** shall be **equal for all of you.**

*Tani idach*: a second suggestion, from a different *tanna*. *Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai omer: ayin tachat ayin — mamon ata omer, o eino ela ayin mamash?* His argument: *she-harei she-summa ve-simmei* — what if a blind person went and blinded someone else? There is no way to reciprocate, since there is no sight to take. Or *kiteia ve-kiteia* — a person missing a limb who went and amputated someone else's limb. *Chigger be-chigger* — a lame person who lamed another. *D'heicha ani mekayem be-zeh ayin tachat ayin?* In these cases, since the perpetrator already has the very injury he has now inflicted on another, reciprocity is physically impossible.

### Equal Law Again

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: כָּל כִּי הַאי – שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ מָמוֹנָא; הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״מִשְׁפַּט אֶחָד יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״ – מִשְׁפָּט הַשָּׁוֶה לְכוּלְּכֶם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the derivation: **And if you would say** that **in all cases like this,** where their eyes are different sizes, the injured party **takes monetary restitution** from the one who caused him injury, but in a case where their eyes are the same size, the one who caused injury is punished by actually having his eye removed, this cannot be, as **the Torah said: "You shall have one manner of law"** (Leviticus 24:22), teaching that the **law** shall be **equal for all of you.**

*Ve-ha-Torah amra: mishpat echad yihyeh lachem, mishpat ha-shaveh le-chulchem.* The same principle of equal law must apply universally. You cannot have a system in which a sighted *mazik* has his eye put out while a blind *mazik* pays money — that is an inconsistent application of the law. That inconsistency itself demonstrates that the Torah cannot have intended literal physical reciprocity in the first place.

### Conditional Reciprocity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא הֵיכָא דְּאֶפְשָׁר – אֶפְשָׁר; הֵיכָא דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר – לָא אֶפְשָׁר, וּפָטְרִינַן לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages object to this derivation and **say: And what** is **the difficulty? Perhaps** in a case **where it is possible** to render the guilty party liable according to the punishment listed in the Torah, it is **possible** and the court does so; but in a case **where it is not possible** to do so, it is **not possible, and we exempt him.**

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: *amri: u-mai kushya? Dilma heicha d'efshar — efshar, heicha d'lo efshar — lo efshar u-patrinan lei* — perhaps the system simply says: where it is possible to inflict equivalent punishment, do so; where it is impossible, either exempt him or collect money instead. This would be a nuanced rule rather than a blanket one.

### Treifa Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, טְרֵפָה שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַשָּׁלֵם – מַאי עָבְדִינַן לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **As if you do not say so,** that punishing one and exempting another is not counter to the principle of: "One manner of law," then by the same logic, in the case of **one who has a wound that will cause him to die within twelve months [*tereifa*]** and **who killed a healthy person, what do we do to him?**

The *gemara* rejects this suggestion by means of a powerful analogy. *D'ilu teima hachi — treifa she-harag et ha-shalem, mai avdinan lei?* What if a person who is *treifa* — that is, mortally wounded and therefore halachically considered already as though dead, *gavra katila* — killed a healthy person? *Ha gavra katila hu*, Rashi notes: such a person is presumed to have a limited time to live, and executing him would therefore not be equivalent to the capital crime of taking a healthy person's full life. This creates the further problem of *edut she-i ata yakhol le-hazimah*: testimony that cannot be subjected to the full protection of *edim zomemim*. If witnesses testify against the *treifa* murderer and are later found to be conspiring witnesses, they themselves would be exempt from execution, since all they were trying to do was execute a *gavra katila* — someone already considered as dead. *D'hai mishtakchei edim zomemim peturim d'gavra katila ba'ei l'miktal*: the system of *edim zomemim* requires that whatever punishment the witnesses sought to impose upon their victim could in principle be turned back upon them. If it could not be — as here — the testimony is inadmissible from the outset. This is the disqualification of *edut she-i ata yakhol le-hazimah*, discussed at length in the first chapter of *Masechet Arachin* in the context of *hayotze le-haryeg*, a person already condemned and walking to execution: *lo nerach ve-lo ma'arichin oto*, he is assessed neither by others nor by himself, since he is treated as already dead. The point is that capital law itself produces analogous situations of structural impossibility, yet we do not abandon the rule of one punishment for all. The same logic therefore does not compel us to adopt a two-track system for *ayin tachat ayin*; and the suggestion of conditional reciprocity is rejected.

## Redundancy Proofs

### Yishmael Extra Word

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״כֵּן יִנָּתֶן בּוֹ״ – וְאֵין נְתִינָה אֶלָּא מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another derivation: **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that **the verse states:** "A fracture for a fracture, an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth; as he has given a blemish to a person, **so shall it be given unto him"** (Leviticus 24:20), **and giving can** refer **only** to a payment of **money.**

*D'bei Rabbi Yishmael tana amar kra: ki yinaten bo* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives the monetary principle from a textual redundancy. The verse *ka'asher yitein mum ba-adam ken yinaten bo* — whatever one does to another shall be done to him — already states the principle of reciprocity. The word *ki yinaten bo*, appearing as a separate phrase, is therefore extra. *Ein netinah ela mamon*: the word *netinah*, giving, in its stand-alone usage refers to the giving of money, not the giving of an injury. Rabbi Yishmael's school, which is characteristically more restrictive in the types of *drashot* it is willing to make, derives from a superfluous word rather than claiming that *netinah* always means financial compensation.

### Yishmael Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן מוּם בָּאָדָם״ – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמָמוֹן הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But if that is so,** then when the same verse states: **"As he has given [*yitten*] a blemish to a person,"** does **this** word, "*yitten*," **also** refer **to money?** The word "*yitten*" means that he caused an actual injury, even though it employs a term whose literal meaning is give.

The *gemara* questions this: *ella ma'ata ka'asher yitein mum ba-adam* — the earlier clause also uses the root *natan* in describing the infliction of a physical injury (*mum*). Does that mean the injury itself is also a monetary matter? *Hach nami de-mamon hu?* Clearly not, since *mum* explicitly denotes a physical wound. The response is that *d'amri d'bei Rabbi Yishmael kra yateira darshi* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael makes *drashot* only from genuinely superfluous words.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – קְרָא יַתִּירָא דָּרְשִׁי; מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״וְאִישׁ כִּי יִתֵּן מוּם בַּעֲמִיתוֹ, כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה כֵּן יֵעָשֶׂה לוֹ״; ״כֵּן יִנָּתֶן בּוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: The rabbis **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael are interpreting a superfluous verse. Now, it is written: "And if a man gives a blemish to his neighbor; as he has done, so shall it be done to him"** (Leviticus 24:19), so **why do I** need the verse: **"So shall it be given unto him"** (Leviticus 24:20)? **Learn from** the repetition that the verse is referring to **monetary restitution.**

*Michdi ve-ish ki yitein mum ba-amito ka'asher asa ken ye'aseh lo* — one verse already covers the reciprocity principle fully. *Ki yinaten bo lama li? Shema mina mamon* — since this additional phrase is redundant, it must be teaching that the compensation takes the form of money. The parallel phrase *ka'asher yitein mum ba-adam* in turn is not itself superfluous but is needed to anchor the monetary compensation to the original act of physical injury, establishing that the financial payment is the legal equivalent of what was done.

### Chiya Hand-Hand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא תָּנָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״יָד בְּיָד״ – דָּבָר הַנִּיתָּן מִיָּד לְיָד; וּמַאי נִיהוּ? מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another derivation: **The school of Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** that **the verse states** with regard to conspiring witnesses: "And your eye shall not pity; a life for a life, an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth, **a hand for a hand,** a foot for a foot" (Deuteronomy 19:21). This teaches that the witnesses pay compensation with **that which is given from hand to hand. And what** type of compensation **is that? Monetary restitution.**

*D'bei Rabbi Chiya tana amar kra: yad be-yad* — the school of Rabbi Chiya derives the point differently. The phrase *yad be-yad*, a hand for a hand, does not mean that a hand is physically exchanged for a hand. Rather: *davar ha-nitan mi-yad le-yad* — it refers to something given from hand to hand, which is money. *U-mai nihu? Mamon.* Monetary compensation is what passes from one person's hand to the other.

### Chiya Foot Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״רֶגֶל בְּרָגֶל״ נָמֵי הָכִי הוּא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But if that is so, is** the phrase: **"A foot for a foot"** (Deuteronomy 19:21), **also like that,** i.e., is it teaching that the witnesses pay compensation with an item passed from foot to foot?

This immediately raises a question: *ella me'ata regel be-regel* — if *yad be-yad* already teaches that compensation is monetary, why does the verse also need to say *regel be-regel*, a foot for a foot?

### Chiya Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא – קְרָא יַתִּירָא קָא דָרְשִׁי; מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו״; אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַמָּשׁ, ״יָד בְּיָד״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say:** The rabbis of **the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya are interpreting a superfluous** phrase in the **verse. Now, it is written: "And you shall do to him as he purposed to do to his brother"** (Deuteronomy 19:19). **If it enters your mind** to say that the verse means this **literally, why do I** need the Torah to specify: **"A hand for a hand"** (Deuteronomy 19:21)? The punishment will be whatever he purported to do to his brother. **Learn from** the extra phrase that the punishment is **monetary restitution.**

*Nami hachi hu. Amri d'bei Rabbi Chiya kra yateira darshi* — just as with the school of Rabbi Yishmael, the school of Rabbi Chiya is also deriving from redundancy. Since the verse has already stated *yad be-yad*, the further phrase *regel be-regel* is superfluous, and the redundancy as a whole teaches that monetary compensation — *mamon* — is the mandated form of payment. One might ask whether this creates a circular argument, particularly in connection with *edim zomemim*: the verse says *ve-asitem lo ka'asher zamam la'asot le'achiv*, we do to the conspiring witnesses what they sought to do to their victim. If we assume monetary damages, then *yad be-yad* is redundant and teaches monetary compensation; but the original question is precisely whether damages are monetary. The answer is that by the time *edim zomemim* is invoked, the form of the penalty is already known: if they sought a capital conviction, they are executed; if they sought a monetary obligation, they pay. Since we know that in the damages context *edim zomemim* pay money, this provides an independent baseline from which the redundancy of *yad be-yad* can be observed and the monetary principle extracted.

## Amoraic Proofs

### Abaye Death Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אוֹמֵר: אָתְיָא מִדְּתָנֵי דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה: ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״; ״נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״ – וְלֹא נֶפֶשׁ וְעַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַמָּשׁ, זִימְנִין דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ עַיִן וָנֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת עַיִן – דְּבַהֲדֵי דְּעַוַּיר לֵיהּ, נְפַקָא לֵיהּ נִשְׁמְתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another derivation: **Abaye says** that this principle is **derived from** that which **was taught** by **the school of Ḥizkiyya, as the school of Ḥizkiyya taught** that the Torah states: **"An eye for an eye"** (Exodus 21:24), and: **"A life for a life"** (Exodus 21:23), **but not an eye and a life for an eye. And if it enters your mind** to say that the verse means this **literally,** there could be **times when you find** a case where both **an eye and a life** are taken **for an eye,** i.e., when the one who caused the damage is so weak **that as** the court **blinds** his eye, **his soul departs** from his body.

The *gemara* now moves from *tannaitic* sources to *amoraic* ones. Abaye derives from a *tanna* of the school of Chizkiyah: *ayin tachat ayin, nefesh tachat nefesh* — one pays an eye for an eye and a life for a life. The key clause is *ve-lo nefesh va-ayin tachat ayin* — you cannot take a person's life along with their eye as payment for taking out an eye. *Di salka daitach mamash* — if you thought it literally meant poking out his eye: *zimnin di-mashkachat lah ayin ve-nefesh tachat ayin, de-vahadi de-avir leh nafka leh nishmateh* — there are situations in which the attempt to take away someone's vision would end up killing them, whether from the pain, blood loss, or infection. In such a case, the literal punishment would inadvertently constitute a capital penalty for what was a non-capital offense, with no textual warrant.

### Assessment Before Injury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא מֵימָד אָמְדִינַן לֵיהּ; אִי מָצֵי מְקַבֵּל – עָבְדִינַן, וְאִי לָא מָצֵי מְקַבֵּל – לָא עָבְדִינַן;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And what** is **the difficulty? Perhaps we evaluate** the physical condition **of** the guilty party; **if he can withstand** this punishment, then **we do** blind his eye; **if he cannot withstand** this punishment, then **we do not** do so and he goes unpunished.

The *gemara* objects: *u-mai kushya? Dilma omdana omdinan leh* — perhaps we simply assess the person in advance. *Ei matzi le-kabulei, avdinan lei* — if the person can tolerate the procedure, we proceed; *ei lo matzi le-kabulei, lo avdinan lei* — if not, we refrain. Prior medical evaluation could eliminate the risk and preserve the literal punishment.

### Lashing Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְדִינַן דְּמָצֵי מְקַבֵּל, וְעָבְדִינַן בֵּיהּ וּנְפַק רוּחֵיהּ – אִי מָיֵית, לֵימוּת! מִי לָא תְּנַן גַּבֵּי מַלְקוֹת: אֲמָדוּהוּ, וּמֵת תַּחַת יָדוֹ – פָּטוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **And if we evaluate** him and determine **that** he **can withstand** this punishment, **and we do so to him** and blind his eye, **and** yet **his soul departs** his body as a result, **if he dies, he will die. Didn't we learn** in a mishna **with regard to lashes** (*Makkot* 22b): If one was sentenced to be flogged, and the court **evaluated him** and determined that he could withstand a certain number of lashes, **and he dies at the hand** of the officer tasked with administering the lashes, then the officer is **exempt,** even though the one who was flogged was not sentenced to the death penalty?

The *gemara* responds by invoking the parallel case of lashing: *tenan gabi malkus, amduhu va-met tachat yado, patur* — the *mishnah* teaches that if the court assessed how many lashes a person could tolerate and then the person died in the course of receiving them, the court representative administering the lashes is exempt. The court assesses as best it can, and if death occurs despite proper evaluation, there is no liability. The implication is that performing an assessment beforehand and then proceeding — even with the risk of death — is the accepted framework in physical punishment. This does not, however, prove that literal punishment cannot be the intended mode; it merely shows that the death risk would not automatically invalidate the system.

### Rav Zvid Petza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״פֶּצַע תַּחַת פָּצַע״ – לִיתֵּן צַעַר בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַמָּשׁ – כִּי הֵיכִי דִּלְהַאי הָוֵי לֵיהּ צַעֲרָא, לְהַאי נָמֵי אִית לֵיהּ צַעֲרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another derivation: **Rav Zevid said in the name of Rabba** that the **verse states: "A wound for a wound"** (Exodus 21:25), to teach that one who injures another must **pay** compensation for **pain,** even **in a case** where he pays compensation for **damage. And if it enters your mind** that the phrase: "An eye for an eye" (Exodus 21:24), refers to the removal of **an actual** eye from the one who injured the other, then **just as** it is so that the injured party **has pain** from the loss of his eye, the one who caused him injury **also has pain** when the court removes his eye; why then does the Torah require that he pay compensation for pain as well?

*Rav Zvid mishmeih de-Rava amar: amar kra petza tachat patza* — a burn for a burn. The phrase refers to a painful wound and is cited to establish that *tza'ar*, pain and suffering, is paid in the place of *nezek*, monetary damage. That is: *liten tza'ar bi-makom nezek* — the category of *tza'ar* is paid in a manner equivalent to how *nezek* is paid. *I salka daitach mamash* — if you thought the verse literally meant inflicting the equivalent injury: *ki heichi de-lehavei lei tza'ara* — why would the verse need to introduce *tza'ar* as a separate compensable category? If both parties undergo equivalent injuries, they will each have experienced equivalent pain; there would be no need for a separate financial payment for pain and suffering. *Ve-le-hai nami is lei tza'ara* — the *mazik* would also have the pain and suffering from the reciprocal injury. The very existence of *tza'ar* as a distinct category to be paid implies that the underlying mode of compensation is financial rather than physical.

### Sensitivity Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא אִיכָּא אִינִישׁ דִּמְפַנַּק – אִית לֵיהּ צַעֲרָא טְפֵי, וְאִיכָּא אִינִישׁ דְּלָא מְפַנַּק – לֵית לֵיהּ צַעֲרָא! לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְמִתַּבי לֵיהּ הַיְאךְ דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And what** is **the difficulty? Perhaps there is a person who is delicate,** so **he has more pain, and there is a person who is not delicate,** so **he does not have** the same amount of **pain.** Therefore, even if the court actually removes an eye for an eye, the one who caused the injury might still need to compensate the injured party for pain. **What is the** practical **difference** when the Torah states: "A wound for a wound" (Exodus 21:25)? It renders the one who caused the injury liable **to give** the injured party compensation for **the difference between** them in pain tolerance.

The *gemara* challenges this: *u-mai kushya? Dilma ika inish de-mefanak is lei tza'ara tefai, ve-ika inish de-lo mefanak leis lei tza'ara* — perhaps the category of *tza'ar* exists precisely because people feel pain differently. One person may be more sensitive and experience greater suffering from the same injury; another person may be more hardy and barely register the pain. Physical reciprocity alone cannot equalize these differences, and *tza'ar* as a financial payment serves to bridge that gap without requiring that the mode of primary compensation be monetary. *De-lo nafka minah le-meitav lei ha-hi de-veini beini* — you find the midpoint that equalizes the two extremes, paying the financial difference between the two parties' actual experiences of pain without abandoning the physical punishment itself.

### Tzaar-Nezek Link

The resolution, however, carries its own force. Once one concedes that *tza'ar* must be assessed and paid financially — because individual differences in pain sensitivity cannot be equalized by physical means alone — one has already accepted that there is a financial component to these injuries. And once *tza'ar* is established as *bi-makom nezek*, in the same legal register as *nezek*, it follows that *nezek* itself is also to be paid financially. The argument runs: if you are willing to say *tza'ar* can be entirely financial and need not take the form of causing the other person equivalent suffering, then since *tza'ar* stands in the place of *nezek*, *nezek* too is financial. At that point, the entire system of five payments is rendered financial, and the case for literal physical reciprocity collapses.

### Rav Pappa Healing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְרַפֹּא יְרַפֵּא״ – לִיתֵּן רְפוּאָה בִּמְקוֹם נֶזֶק. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַמָּשׁ – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּהַאי בָּעֵי אָסְיָיא, הַאי נָמֵי בָּעֵי אָסְיָיא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another derivation: **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava** that concerning one who was injured by another, who must pay for damage, the **verse states:** "If he rises again, and walks outside upon his staff, then he that struck him shall be absolved; only he shall pay for his loss of livelihood, **and shall cause him to be thoroughly healed"** (Exodus 21:19), which teaches that one who injures another must **pay** compensation for **medical costs** even **in a case** where he pays compensation for **damage. And if it enters your mind** that the phrase: "An eye for an eye" (Exodus 21:24), is referring to an **actual** eye, then **just as** it is so **that** the injured party **needs healing,** the one who caused him the injury **also needs healing** after the court removes his eye; why, then, does the Torah require that he pay compensation for medical costs as well?

*Rav Pappa mishmeih de-Rava amar: amar kra ve-rapo yerape* — the Torah's provision of medical expenses is itself a proof. *Liten refuah bi-makom nezek* — medical expenses are paid as a form of compensation equivalent to *nezek*. If you thought the verse meant literal physical reciprocity — *de-ei salka daitach mamash* — then since both parties undergo equivalent injuries, both would require medical care: *ve-heichi de-hai ba'ei asya, hai nami ba'ei asya*. Why would the verse single out medical expenses as something the *mazik* pays to the *nizak*, if the *mazik* himself will have suffered equivalent damage requiring equivalent care?

### Medical Care Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא אִיכָּא דְּסָלֵיק בִּשְׂרֵיהּ הַיָּיא, וְאִיכָּא דְּלָא סָלֵיק בִּשְׂרֵיהּ הַיָּיא! לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְמִיתַּב לֵיהּ הַיְאךְ דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **What** is **the difficulty? Perhaps there is** one **whose flesh heals quickly, and there is** another **whose flesh does not heal quickly.** Therefore, even if the court actually removes an eye for an eye, the one who caused the injury might still need to compensate the injured party for medical costs. **What is the** practical **difference** when the Torah states: "And shall cause him to be thoroughly healed" (Exodus 21:19)? It renders the one who caused the injury liable **to give** the injured party compensation for **the difference between** their respective medical costs.

The *gemara* counters: *salka daitach de-hai salka basar ve-hai lo salka basar* — perhaps one of the two injuries heals better than the other. Their physical conditions may differ, and the *mazik* may recover more quickly than the *nizak*, creating an asymmetry. *Heichi mehina takanta?* So there is still a need for some financial equalization between them.

### Medical Counter

The *gemara* acknowledges: *le-meitav lei hai chatzyo beini beini* — you find the middle ground and compensate the difference. This does not constitute a knockout proof that literal punishment is not the primary mode; as with the *tza'ar* case, you can accommodate the asymmetry with supplementary monetary payments without abandoning physical reciprocity as the principal rule. The argument therefore does not decisively close the question, and the *gemara* proceeds to Rav Ashi.

## Rav Ashi Shor

### Tachat Gezeira Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אָתְיָא ״תַּחַת״–״תַּחַת״ מִשּׁוֹר; כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם שׁוֹר תַּחַת הַשּׁוֹר״ – מָה לְהַלָּן מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another derivation: **Rav Ashi said** that the fact that one who injures another pays monetary restitution **is derived** from a verbal analogy of the word **"for,"** as written with regard to injuries caused to people **from** the word **"for,"** as written with regard to **an ox** that gored another ox. **It is written here: "An eye for an eye"** (Exodus 21:24), **and it is written there,** with regard to a forewarned ox that gored the ox of another: **"He shall pay an ox for an ox"** (Exodus 21:36). **Just as there,** the verse does not mean that the owner pays compensation with an actual ox, but rather pays **monetary restitution, so too here,** one who injures another pays **monetary restitution.**

*Rav Ashi amar: atya tachat tachat mi-shor* — Rav Ashi proposes a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *tachat*. *Kesiv hacha: ayin tachat ayin* — here, in the laws of human injury, the word *tachat* appears. *U-chesiv hatam: shaleim yeshaleim shor tachat ha-shor* — there, in the laws of ox damages, the same word *tachat* appears. Just as ox damages are paid in money — a point established across the first six chapters of *Bava Kamma* — so too human bodily injuries are paid in money. *Ma halan mamon, af kan mamon.*

### Why Not Capital

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חָזֵית דְּיָלְפַתְּ ״תַּחַת״–״תַּחַת״ מִשּׁוֹר? נֵילַף ״תַּחַת״–״תַּחַת״ מֵאָדָם – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָתַתָּ נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נֶפֶשׁ״; מָה לְהַלָּן מַמָּשׁ, אַף כָּאן מַמָּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did you see that** led **you** to **derive** the *halakha* from a verbal analogy of the word **"for"** as stated in the verse with regard to injuries to a person **from** the word **"for"** as stated with regard to **an ox? Let us learn** a verbal analogy of the word **"for"** as stated in the verse with regard to injuries to a person **from** the word **"for"** as stated with regard to **a person, as it is written** about one who kills another: **"You shall give a life for a life"** (Exodus 21:23). **Just as there,** the court punishes the guilty party by taking his **actual** life, **so too here,** why not say that the court should take his **actual** eye?

The *gemara* objects: *mai chazit de-yalfat tachat tachat mi-shor? Neilaf tachat tachat me-adam* — why learn the *gezeirah shavah* from the ox case? Perhaps we should learn from the human case, specifically *de-ichtiv: ve-natata nefesh tachat nefesh*, where *tachat* appears in the context of capital crimes. *Ma halan mamash, af kan mamash* — just as capital punishment is literally carried out, so too physical injury should be literally repaid.

### Damages from Damages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: דָּנִין נְזָקִין מִנְּזָקִין, וְאֵין דָּנִין נְזָקִין מִמִּיתָה. אַדְּרַבָּה! דָּנִין אָדָם מֵאָדָם, וְאֵין דָּנִין אָדָם מִבְּהֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: The *halakha* concerning **damages is derived from** a verse concerning **damages, and** the *halakha* concerning **damages is not derived from** a verse concerning **death.** The Gemara objects: **On the contrary,** why not say that the *halakha* concerning **a person is derived from** a verse concerning **a person, and** the *halakha* concerning **a person is not derived from** a verse concerning **animals?**

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking a structural principle: *amri: dayninan nezikin mi-nezikin, ve-ein dayninan nezikin mi-mitah* — we learn damages from damages, not from capital crimes. The objection is then raised in the reverse direction: *aderaba, dayninan adam me-adam ve-ein dayninan adam mi-beheimah* — perhaps we should learn human-to-human cases from other human-to-human cases, not from animal cases. If so, the analogy to the ox is inappropriate, and we should learn from *nefesh tachat nefesh* instead. This mutual objection leaves the *gezeirah shavah* from ox damages in tension with the possible *gezeirah shavah* from capital crimes.

### Rav Ashi Rape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִ״תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר עִנָּהּ״ יָלֵיף לֵיהּ – אָדָם מֵאָדָם, וּנְזִיקִין מִנְּזִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi** retracted his original statement and **said** a different derivation: The *halakha* is **derived from** a verbal analogy of the word "for" as written with regard to injuries from the word "for" as written with regard to a man who rapes a woman, who must pay monetary compensation. The verse states: "Then the man that lay with her shall give to the girl's father fifty shekels of silver, and she shall be his wife, **for he has afflicted her;** he may not send her away all his days" (Deuteronomy 22:29). Based on this verbal analogy, the *halakha* concerning **a person** is derived **from** a verse concerning **a person, and** the *halakha* concerning **damages** is derived **from** a verse concerning **damages.**

Rav Ashi therefore offers an alternative proof: *mi-tachat asher inah* — in the laws of rape, the Torah uses the word *tachat* in a context that is unambiguously monetary and involves human beings. *Yalpat lei adam me-adam* — we learn *tachat* for monetary compensation from a human-to-human case. *Ve-nezikin mi-nezikin* — and it is damages from damages, not a capital case. This satisfies both criteria simultaneously: it is a case of human beings dealing with human beings, and it is a case of monetary restitution for injury, not a capital analogue. The *gezeirah shavah* thus establishes on solid grounds that *ayin tachat ayin* refers to financial compensation.

## Rabbi Eliezer

### Eliezer Literal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״ – מַמָּשׁ. ״מַמָּשׁ״ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לֵית לֵיהּ כְּכֹל הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The verse that states: **"An eye for an eye"** (Exodus 21:24), is referring to **an actual** eye. The Gemara asks: Can it **enter your mind** that the verse is referring to **an actual** eye? **Doesn't Rabbi Eliezer** understand the verse **like all these *tanna'im*,** who explained that this verse is referring to monetary payment?

*Tanya: Rabbi Eliezer omer ayin tachat ayin mamash* — a *baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Eliezer says that *ayin tachat ayin* means literally, actually, physically blinding the person's eye. This is a striking *gemara*: Rabbi Eliezer appears to stand alone against the consensus of all the *tannaim* surveyed above. The *gemara* asks with evident unease: *mamash salka datach? Rabbi Eliezer leit lei kulei hanei tanna'ei?* Does Rabbi Eliezer really disagree with all of these *tannaim*? That would undermine not only this entire *sugya* but also the Rambam's claim that *ayin tachat ayin* as monetary compensation is a *davar hamekubal mi-pi Moshe Rabbeinu* — an undisputed transmitted tradition.

### Rabbah Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין שָׁמִין אוֹתוֹ כְּעֶבֶד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** in response: Rabbi Eliezer means **to say that** the court **does not appraise** the injured party **as a slave** to assess the compensation for the injury.

*Amar Rabbah: lomar she-ein shamin oto ke-eved* — Rabbah resolves this by offering a different reading of Rabbi Eliezer's position. Rabbi Eliezer is not truly advocating for poking out people's eyes. Rather, his point is about the method of assessment. The standard *mishnah* formula assesses the *nizak* as a slave — how much would this person sell for before and after the injury — and the difference is the *nezek* payment. Rabbi Eliezer says: we do not use the slave-valuation method. Instead we assess the actual eye itself.

### Abaye Free Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא כְּמַאן, כְּבֶן חוֹרִין?! בֶּן חוֹרִין מִי אִית לֵיהּ דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: **Rather, like whom** does the court appraise the injured party? If you say that the court appraises him **like a freeman, does a freeman have monetary** value?

Abaye objects: *ela ke-man? Ke-ven chorin?* If not assessed like a slave, then you would assess him like a free person — *ben chorin mi is lei dmei?* But a free person has no market monetary value. There is no mechanism by which one assesses the worth of a free person's bodily integrity outside the slave-market framework.

### Rav Ashi Mazik

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין שָׁמִין אוֹתוֹ בְּנִיזָּק, אֶלָּא בְּמַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** Rabbi Eliezer means **to say that** the court **does not appraise the injured** party as if he were going to be sold as a slave, but **rather,** they appraise **the one who caused** him **damage.** The court appraises how much the latter's value would be reduced were he to sustain the same injury he caused to the injured party, and he pays this amount as indemnity.

*Ela amar Rav Ashi: lomar she-ein shamin oto ba-nizak ela ba-mazik* — Rav Ashi's resolution is elegant. Rabbi Eliezer means that we do not assess how much the *nizak*'s eye was worth — that is, we do not calculate the damage suffered by the injured party in the standard *nezikin* framework. Instead, we assess how much the *mazik*'s eye is worth. The *mazik* pays the value of his own eye, not the *nizak*'s loss. This makes it structurally more like *kofer* — payment based on the value of the injurer — than like *nezek*, which is payment calibrated to the damage sustained by the victim. The distinction between *dmei nizak* and *dmei mazik* was already discussed in connection with *kofer* earlier in the tractate.

## Assessment Without Market

### Donkey Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא חַמְרָא דְּקַטַּע יְדָא דְיָנוֹקָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ שׁוּמוּ לֵיהּ אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים. אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא אֲנַן חֲמִשָּׁה תְּנַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְבַר מִנֶּזֶק קָאָמֵינָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא חֲמוֹר הוּא, וַחֲמוֹר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא נֶזֶק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain donkey that severed the hand of a child.** The case **came before Rav Pappa bar Shmuel. He said** to the officers of the court: **Go appraise the four types** of indemnity **for** the child. **Rava said to him: But didn't we learn** in the mishna that there are **five** types of indemnity? Rav Pappa bar Shmuel **said to him: I was saying** to include the indemnities the responsible party is liable to pay **other than damage. Abaye said to him: But** was **this** not **a donkey** that caused this injury, **and** the owner of **a donkey** that causes injury **pays only for** the **damage?**

*Hahu chamra de-kata yadei de-yinoka* — there was a donkey that bit off a child's hand. The case came before *Rav Papa bar Shmuel*, who instructed: *zilu shimu lei arba'a devarim* — go collect the four categories of compensation. Rava objected: *ve-ha anan chamsha tenan* — don't we teach five categories? *Amar lei: le-var mi-nezek* — Rav Papa bar Shmuel meant four categories in addition to, or exclusive of, *nezek*. The underlying issue is that the case involves a donkey: an animal that causes injury is governed by the *shor* framework, not by *adam ha-mazik*, the laws of human bodily harm. A donkey pays only *nezek* and not the other four categories. Abaye then makes this explicit: *ve-ha chamor, chamor hu? Ve-chamor eino meshalem ella nezek* — it is a donkey, and a donkey only pays the damage, not pain, medical expenses, lost work, or embarrassment. The confusion arose because in Aramaic, *chamor* (donkey) and *chammar* (donkey driver) are easily conflated, and Rav Papa bar Shmuel had apparently thought it was a human being who caused the injury.

### No Authority Bavel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ שַׁיְימוּהּו כְּעַבְדָּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ אֲבוּהּ דְּיָנוֹקָא: לָא בָּעֵינָא, דְּזִילָא בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְהָא קָא מְחַיְּיבַתְּ לֵיהּ לְיָנוֹקָא! אֲמַר לְהוּ: לְכִי גָדֵיל, מְפַיֵּיסְנָא לֵיהּ מִדִּידִי.
>
> **Translation:** The court **said to** the officers of the court: **Go appraise him as a slave.** **The father of the child said to them: I do not want** my child to be appraised as a slave, **because this matter** would **demean him. They said to** the father: **But** you are **acting to the detriment of the child,** as he will not receive compensation for his injury. **He said to them: When he matures, I will appease him with my own** money, rather than see him demeaned now.

The court then instructed: *zilu shamu-hu ke-avda* — go assess the child's value as a slave, before and after the injury, to determine the *nezek* payment. The child's father objected: *lo ba'ina de-zilei be-milta* — he does not want this, because it is deeply degrading for his child to be assessed as a slave on the market. The court pressed him: *ve-ha ka-michayevat lei le-yinoka* — but you are still obligated to pay the child's damages. Rava's underlying concern, articulated more fully elsewhere, is that we do not have the proper *semicha* in Bavel to adjudicate cases that require assessing human beings like slaves — *kol ha-nishamin ke-eved ein govin oso be-Vavel* — and therefore the entire *nizak* assessment cannot be carried out in Bavel in the first place.

### Deferred Assessment

The resolution: *le-chi gadil me-fassina lei mi-didei* — when the child grows up, the father (or the now-grown child) can reach a settlement. The debt hangs over the *mazik* until the child reaches an age at which the assessment can properly be made and a resolution reached between the parties.

### Ox Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא תּוֹרָא דַּאֲלַס יְדֵיהּ דְּיָנוֹקָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ שַׁיְימוּהּו כְּעַבְדָּא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר: כׇּל הַנִּישּׁוֹם כְּעֶבֶד – אֵין גּוֹבִין אוֹתוֹ בְּבָבֶל! אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי תְּפַס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain ox that chewed [*da'alas*] the hand of a child,** injuring him. The case **came before Rava. He said to** the officers of the court: **Go appraise him as a slave. They said to** Rava: **But wasn't it** you, **Master, who said:** With regard to **anyone who is appraised as a slave** in order to determine the amount of a monetary penalty, that penalty **is not collected** by courts **in Babylonia?** Rava **said to them:** It **is not necessary** to appraise his value in order to force the guilty party to pay restitution, but it is nevertheless necessary to determine his value. This is **because if** the injured party **seizes** property from the one who caused him injury, and that property is equal in value to what the payment should be, the court will not compel him to return it.

*Hahu tora de-akhal yadei de-yinoka* — a parallel case: an ox bit off a child's hand. This came before Rava, who likewise instructed: *zilu shamu-hu ke-avda* — go assess the child's value as a slave. The child's father raised the same objection: *ve-ha mar hu de-amar kol ha-nishamin ke-eved ein govin oto be-Vavel* — did our master not himself teach that we do not collect such assessments in Bavel? Rava replied: *lo tzricha de-itfas* — we are dealing with a case where the party has already seized (*tafas*) a sum from the other side, and now the court can assess the proper amount to determine whether the seized amount was sufficient or whether more is owed. But initiating the case from scratch is something the Babylonian courts lack the authority to do in the first place.

## Authority in Bavel

### Rava Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: נִזְקֵי שׁוֹר בְּשׁוֹר, וְנִזְקֵי שׁוֹר בְּאָדָם – גּוֹבִין אוֹתוֹ בְּבָבֶל. נִזְקֵי אָדָם בְּאָדָם, וְנִזְקֵי אָדָם בְּשׁוֹר – אֵין גּוֹבִין אוֹתוֹ בְּבָבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Rava** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rava says:** Compensation for **damage** to **an ox** caused **by an ox and** for **damage** to **an ox** caused **by a person is collected** by courts **in Babylonia,** but compensation for **damage** to **a person** caused **by a person and** for **damage** to **a person** caused **by an ox is not collected** by courts **in Babylonia.**

This brings us to the formal articulation of Rava's rule. *De-amar Rava: nizkei shor be-shor, ve-nizkei shor be-adam — govin oto be-Vavel*. Cases in which one animal injured another animal, or an animal injured a person — those we do collect in Bavel. *Nizkei adam be-adam, ve-nizkei adam be-shor — ein govin oto be-Vavel*. But if a person injures another person, or a person causes damage to an animal, those we do not collect in Bavel.

### Elohim Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא נִזְקֵי אָדָם בְּאָדָם וְנִזְקֵי אָדָם בְּשׁוֹר, דְּלָא – ״אֱלֹהִים״ בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is different** about compensation for **damage** to **a person** caused **by a person and** for **damage** to **a person** caused **by an ox,** that it is **not** collected in Babylonia? If you say that **we require ordained judges** to collect damages, **and there are not** any ordained judges in Babylonia, then **so too,** in a case of **damage** caused by **an ox to an ox, and** damage caused by **a person to an ox,**

The *gemara* asks: *mai shna nizkei adam be-adam ve-nizkei adam be-shor? De-lo elohim ba'inan* — in those cases we require that the judges be called *elohim*, the term used in *Parshat Mishpatim* to refer to judges. These judges stand in place of God, and that designation requires the full *semicha* going back to Moshe Rabbeinu — *semicha* that can only be conferred in *Eretz Yisrael* and that was discontinued once the Sanhedrin ceased to function there. In Bavel, that *semicha* no longer existed, and therefore the courts lacked the authority to adjudicate such cases. *Ve-leika* — and that authority is simply not present.

### Shlichusaihu Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי שְׁנָא שׁוֹר בְּשׁוֹר וְשׁוֹר בְּאָדָם? דִּשְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ קָא עָבְדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is different** about damage to **an ox** caused **by an ox and** damage to **an ox** caused **by a man,** so that Babylonian judges can rule in these cases even though they are not ordained? The difference is **that we,** the judges of Babylonia, **perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael,

The *gemara* then asks why the animal-damage cases are different: *nami elohim ba'inan ve-leika* — surely animal cases also require *elohim*-level judges, and that is equally lacking? *Ella mai shna shor be-shor ve-shor be-adam de-shlichutaihu ka'avdinan* — the answer is that for those cases we serve as *shlichutaihu*, as the messengers and representatives of the original Sanhedrin. The rabbis of Bavel, and by extension *dayanim* today, serve as representatives of the last properly constituted Sanhedrin in *Eretz Yisrael*. This principle — *shlichusaihu ka'avdinan*, we do their mission in their name — is what allows courts to function in the post-*semicha* era at all. The authority is not original but derivative, and the derivativeness itself carries significant limitations.

### Shachiach Chesron Kis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: כִּי קָא עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ – בְּמִידֵּי דְּקִים לַן בְּגַוֵּיהּ; בְּמִידֵּי דְּלָא קִים לַן בְּגַוֵּיהּ – לָא עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ. אָמְרִי: שׁוֹר בְּשׁוֹר וְשׁוֹר בְּאָדָם נָמֵי לָא קִים לַן בְּגַוֵּיהּ – אֶלָּא פּוֹק חֲזִי (הֵיכָא) [כַּמָּה] מִזְדַּבְּנִי תּוֹרֵי בְּשׁוּקָא; אָדָם בְּאָדָם וְאָדָם בְּשׁוֹר נָמֵי, פּוֹק חֲזִי (הֵיכָא) [כַּמָּה] מִזְדַּבְּנִי עַבְדֵי בְּשׁוּקָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **state,** with regard to the distinction: **When we,** the judges of Babylonia, **perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael, we do so **in matters where** the ruling **is clear to us;** but **in matters where** the ruling **is not clear to us,** such as in the case of an injury to a person, which requires an appraisal, **we do not perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael. The Sages **state** an objection: In cases of damage caused to **an ox by an ox, and** damage caused to **an ox by a man,** cases that we do judge in Babylonia, the ruling **is also not clear to us,** as the damage must be appraised. **Rather,** how is the judgment assessed in those cases? We say: **Go out** and **see how** much **an ox is sold** for **in the marketplace,** and thereby assess the value of the damages, without a detailed appraisal. If so, in the case of damage caused to **a person by a person, and** damage caused to **a person by an ox,** say **too: Go out** and **see how a slave is sold in the** slave **market.** Why, then, are these latter cases not judged in Babylonia?

The *gemara* then defines the precise conditions under which *shlichusaihu ka'avdinan* can operate. The analogy is to *hoda'ot* and *halva'ot* — monetary admissions and loans. We serve as their representatives *be-midi d'kim lei be-gavei* — in matters that have established, clear-cut financial parameters. *Be-midi d'lo kim lei be-gavei — lo avdinan shlichutaihu* — in matters that are not clearly assessable in that fashion, we cannot serve as their representatives. The further requirements crystallize: *ki ka'avdinan shlichutaihu be-milta de-shachicha ve-it beih chesron kis* — we serve as their representatives only in cases that are (1) *shachicha*, commonly occurring, and (2) *chesron kis*, involving a direct financial loss that is being recovered. The rationale: just as the concern about *ne'ilat delet bifnei lav'in* — the risk of closing off access to loans if monetary disputes cannot be adjudicated — justifies representing the Sanhedrin in loan cases, so too in any case meeting these two criteria. Tosfos in *Yevamos* 47a applies this same logic to the acceptance of converts: *chashu nami le-ne'ilat delet bifnei gerim*, the Rabbis were concerned that overly strict requirements for conversion would close off access for sincere prospective converts, and therefore *shlichusaihu ka'avdinan* applies to conversion as well, even though conversion does not produce a direct *chesron kis* — the analogy to the spirit of the principle carries sufficient weight.

### Not Common Enough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ – בְּמִילְּתָא דִשְׁכִיחָא, וְאִית בֵּיהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס; אֲבָל מִילְּתָא דִשְׁכִיחָא וְלֵית בֵּיהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, אִי נָמֵי מִילְּתָא דְלָא שְׁכִיחָא וְאִית בֵּיהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס – לָא עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ. הִלְכָּךְ, אָדָם בְּאָדָם – אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית בֵּיהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא – לָא עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** explain the distinction this way: **When we perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael, we do so only **in a matter that is common and involves,** i.e., the payment is for, **a monetary loss. But in a matter that is common but does not involve a monetary loss, or in an uncommon matter which does involve a monetary loss, we do not perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael. **Therefore,** in a case of an injury caused to **a person by a person, even though it involves a monetary loss, since it is uncommon, we do not perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael;

*Hilchach adam be-adam — af al gav de-it beih chesron kis, keivan de-lo shachicha — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. One person injuring another person, even though it involves real financial loss, is not considered common enough (*shachicha*) for *shlichutaihu* to operate. The *halevi* that such cases do not occur more often — this is at least the *gemara*'s position, though one might note that what counts as *shachicha* is not always self-evident across different eras and contexts.

### Boshes Not Chesron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹשֶׁת – אַף עַל גַּב דִשְׁכִיחָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס – לָא עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case of **humiliation, even though it is common, since it does not involve a monetary loss, we do not perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael.

*Boshet* — embarrassment — presents the mirror-image problem. *Af al gav de-shachicha — keivan de-leit beih chesron kis — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. Embarrassment, while undeniably common, does not constitute *chesron kis* in the technical sense: the payment for *boshet* is not recovering money that was taken from you, but compensating you for an indignity inflicted upon you. There is no direct financial loss to restore. Rav Papa had in fact collected four hundred *zuz* for an embarrassment case, and Rav Chisda wrote to Rav Nachman sharply: *Chisda, Chisda — are you collecting fines in Bavel?* The principle is clear: both conditions — *shachiach* and *chesron kis* — must be present. *Be-milta de-shachicha ve-leit beih chesron kis — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. *I nami milta de-lo shachicha ve-it beih chesron kis — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. Either condition alone is insufficient; both must be met simultaneously.

### Shor Tam Muad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹר בְּשׁוֹר גּוֹבִין בְּבָבֶל? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: שׁוֹר שֶׁהִזִּיק – אֵין גּוֹבִין אוֹתוֹ בְּבָבֶל. דְּאַזֵּיק מַאן? אִילֵימָא דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם, מַאי אִירְיָא שׁוֹר דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם? אֲפִילּוּ אָדָם דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם נָמֵי אֵין גּוֹבִין אוֹתוֹ בְּבָבֶל! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא – דְּאַזֵּיק שׁוֹר, וְקָתָנֵי: אֵין גּוֹבִין אוֹתוֹ בְּבָבֶל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the *halakha* itself: **But** is it in fact the case that the courts **in Babylonia collect** restitution for damage caused to **an ox by an ox? But doesn't Rava say:** If **an ox caused damage,** the courts **in Babylonia do not collect** the compensation. The Gemara clarifies: To **whom** did the ox cause **damage? If we say that** it **damaged a person, why** did Rava **specifically** state his *halakha* in a case where there is **an ox that damaged a person? Even** in a case of **a person who damaged a person,** which is more common, the court **does not collect** the compensation **in Babylonia. Rather,** it **is obvious** that Rava meant that the ox **damaged an ox. And** Rava **teaches:** The court **does not collect** the compensation **in Babylonia.**

This leads to a closer analysis of the animal-damage cases. Rava's statement that *shor she-hizik ein govin oto be-Vavel* — damages caused by an ox cannot be collected in Bavel — must be carefully parsed. It cannot mean all ox damages, since we just established that *shor be-shor* and *shor be-adam* can be adjudicated in Bavel. *De-hizik man? I leima de-hizik adam* — if what he meant is that an ox injured a person, then that falls into the same category as *adam be-adam*, requiring the full authority of *elohim*-judges. But then why single out the *shor* — even a person injuring a person cannot be adjudicated in Bavel, so the rule would apply equally. *Ella peshita de-hizik shor* — it must be that Rava's prohibition refers to an ox injuring another ox, and specifically to *shor tam*, where *chatzi nezek* is, at least according to some opinions, classified as a *kenas* rather than *mamon*. A *kenas* requires even higher authority and cannot be adjudicated under *shlichusaihu* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָתָם בְּתָם, הָכָא בְּמוּעָד. וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: אֵין מוּעָד בְּבָבֶל! אָמְרִי: דְּאִיַּיעַד הָתָם וְאַיְּיתוּהּ לְהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **There,** Rava stated his ruling **with regard to an innocuous** ox, i.e., an ox that was not forewarned, whose owner pays for the damage as a penalty; and penalties are not collected in Babylonia. **Here,** Rava states his ruling **with regard to a forewarned** ox, and payment for damage it causes is not a penalty, but rather serves as compensation. The Gemara challenges: **But doesn't Rava say: There are no forewarned** oxen **in Babylonia,** as an ox can be forewarned only by ordained judges? The Sages **say** in response: This is a case **where** an ox **was forewarned there,** in Eretz Yisrael, **and** then its owners **brought it here,** to Babylonia.

*Amri: hatam be-tam* — indeed, we are speaking of *shor tam*. *Hacha be-muad* — whereas a *shor muad*, which pays full *nezek* as *mamon*, can be collected in Bavel.

### Shen VeRegel Muad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִילְּתָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא הִיא, וּמִילְּתָא דְלָא שְׁכִיחָא הָא אָמְרַתְּ דְּלָא עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ! דַּאֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן דְּהָתָם, וְיַיעֲדוּהּ הָכָא. סוֹף סוֹף, מִילְּתָא דְלָא שְׁכִיחָא הִיא, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מִילְּתָא דְלָא שְׁכִיחָא לָא (קָא) עָבְדִינַן שְׁלִיחוּתַיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But this is an uncommon matter,** as forewarned oxen are not frequently transported from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia. **And** with regard to **an uncommon matter, did you not say that we do not perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael? The Gemara answers: This is a case **where the Rabbis from there,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, who are ordained, **came and declared** this ox **forewarned, here,** i.e., in Babylonia. The Gemara challenges this interpretation: **Ultimately, this** is **an uncommon matter, and you say** that with regard to **an uncommon matter** that **we do not perform the agency of** the judges of Eretz Yisrael.

The *gemara* then raises the complication: if *shor muad* can be adjudicated in Bavel but *shor tam* cannot, how does a *shor* ever become *muad* in Bavel, given that establishing *muad* status requires three prior adjudicated incidents of *shor tam*? Two possibilities are proposed: the ox became *muad* in *Eretz Yisrael* and was then brought to Bavel; or Rabbis from *Eretz Yisrael* who possessed genuine *semicha* came to Bavel and established *muad* status there. Both are rejected on the same grounds: *sof sof milta de-lo shachicha hi* — in either case it is not a commonly occurring situation, and *lo avdinan shlichutaihu* for things that are not *shachiach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי אָמַר רָבָא – בְּשֵׁן וָרֶגֶל, דְּמוּעָדִין מִתְּחִילָּתָן נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** explain that **when Rava says** that courts in Babylonia collect restitution for damage caused by an ox, he means **with regard to** damage in the categories of **Eating and Trampling, which are forewarned from the outset.** All agree that since these are common forms of damage and involve monetary loss, the judges of Babylonia perform the agency of the judges of Eretz Yisrael and collect compensation for the damage.

*Ella ki amar Rava be-shen va-regel de-muadim mi-tchillatan ninhu* — the resolution is that Rava's statement refers to *shen va-regel*, tooth and foot damages, which are *muadim mi-tchillatan*, forewarned from the outset without the need for three prior incidents. They therefore could easily arise in Bavel and would seem to be candidates for adjudication there. Yet Rava rules that even *shen va-regel* cannot be adjudicated in Bavel — and that is where the *sugya* concludes for today.
