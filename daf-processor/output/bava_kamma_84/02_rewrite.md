# Bava Kamma 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ayin Tachat Ayin Intro

### Rambam Classification

Before turning to the *gemara* itself, it is worth pausing on a striking passage from the Rambam at the opening of *Hilchot Chovel U'Mazik* in *Sefer Nezikin*. The Rambam acknowledges that the topic of *ayin tachat ayin* — how we assess the valuation of damages when one person strikes another — is deeply polemical. From the plain sense of the *pesukim*, one might conclude that the Torah actually requires inflicting an equivalent injury upon the wrongdoer. Yet the Rambam insists: *af al pi she-devarim elu nir'im me-inyan Torah she-bichtav*, even though the way the *halacha* comes out — requiring monetary restitution — is supported by *drashot* and makes a certain amount of sense based on the *pesukim*, *kulan mephorashin hen mipi Moshe mei-Har Sinai*, all of these laws are in the category of *devarim hamekubalim mipi Moshe Rabbeinu*, things transmitted orally to Moshe and passed down through every generation. The Rambam continues: *elu avoteinu danim be-veit dino shel Yehoshua, beit dino shel Shmuel HaRamati ve-chol beit din u-veit din she-amdu mi-mot Moshe ve-ad achshav* — every *beit din* in Jewish history, from the time of Yehoshua through Shmuel HaRamati and down to the present, has always understood *ayin tachat ayin* to mean monetary compensation, because that is the tradition going back to Sinai. The *drashot* exist to justify what is, at bottom, an oral tradition — one that might have a certain hint in the Torah text or make a certain amount of logical sense, but whose ultimate authority rests on transmission rather than derivation.

### Five Categories

The Rambam's use of the term *devarim hamekubalim mipi Moshe Rabbeinu* is his own formulation; the *gemara* does not use that precise language. According to the Rambam, there are five categories of *Torah She-Ba'al Peh*. The first is *devarim hamekubalim mipi Moshe Rabbeinu*: received tradition that has a hint in the text and about which there is no *machloket*. The second is *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*: tradition that has no connection to the text whatsoever, and also has no *machloket*. The difference between these two categories is significant: the first is where *drashot* appear, while the second is purely tradition with no textual anchor at all. The third category — which generates virtually all *machlokot* — consists of laws derived from *drashot*, where a certain discretion is involved in the interpretive act and disagreement can therefore arise. The remaining two categories are *gezeirot* and *takanot*, the distinction between which is a topic for another occasion.

### Perek HaChovel Setup

We pick up from the fourth line from the bottom of 83b in *Bava Kamma*, where we left off yesterday. *Perek HaChovel* opened with a *mishnah* listing five types of compensation that a tortfeasor must pay — *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, *shevet*, and *boshet* — after which the *gemara* launched into the question of how we know that monetary compensation is required at all. Doesn't *ayin tachat ayin* sound as though one who blinds another's eye should have his own eye put out? Yesterday we saw the first major attempt: a *gezeirah shavah* from *makeh makeh*, deriving from the case of injuring an animal — *makeh beheima yeshalmena* — that injury requires monetary payment. Today's *daf* continues with many further attempted proofs, along with their objections and in some cases their rejections. The broader dynamic here reflects what the Rambam was pointing to: in defending a fundamentally oral tradition against critics — including, in the medieval period, the Karaites, against whom Sa'adia Gaon polemicized extensively — it became important for *Chazal* to justify the tradition exegetically, even when what is ultimately being demonstrated is the textual support for a practice already known through received law.

## Dostai: Unequal Eyes

### Large vs Small Eye

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda omer: ayin tachat ayin — mamon ata omer, o eino ela ayin mamash?* Is it money, or does it mean a literal eye? His argument proceeds as follows: *harei she-hayah eino shel zeh gedolah ve-eino shel zeh ketanah* — what if the *mazik*, the person who caused the injury, had a large eye, and the *nizak*, the person who was injured, had a small eye? The question of what precisely "large" and "small" means here is itself worth considering. Some *mefarshim* might read it as referring to the quality of vision rather than the physical dimensions of the eye, though the text literally says size. In any event, the thrust of the argument is that full reciprocal punishment becomes nonsensical once we acknowledge that there is virtually no case in which the equivalence will be perfectly equal.

### Equal Law Principle

*Heichi ani kore ba ayin tachat ayin?* How can I read this as meaning one eye in place of the other when they are not equal? *Ve-chi teima kol ki hai shakil minei mamona* — so perhaps then in such a case you take money from him — *ha-Torah amra: mishpat echad yihyeh lachem*, the Torah requires that the law be equally applicable. *Mishpat ha-shaveh le-chulchem* — the standard of justice must be consistent across all cases. If one person has a large eye and another has a small eye, then the law will produce an asymmetric result: blinding the *mazik*'s very large eye would be a greater loss than the injury he originally caused, or vice versa.

### Vision Not Eye

The *gemara* objects to Rabbi Dostai's proof: *amrei: mai kushya? Dilma nehora shakil minei nehora amar Rachmana nishkol minei* — perhaps it is only the vision that the Torah speaks of, not the physical eye itself. If so, the size of the eye is irrelevant; what matters is that you take away his vision, just as he took away the other person's vision, and the two are considered equivalent. *De-ilu* — in that case it would indeed be permissible to blind him, since it would be fully equivalent.

### Capital Case Analogy

This response to Rabbi Dostai leads the *gemara* to press further: *lo teima hachi — katan she-harag et ha-gadol, be-gadol she-harag et ha-katan, heichi katlinan lei?* What about a short person who killed a tall person, or a large person who killed a small person — *ha-Torah amra mishpat echad yihyeh lachem, mishpat ha-shaveh le-chulchem*? In capital cases, the life of the smaller person and the life of the larger person are considered equivalent in law. The court does not say: the life about to be taken by execution is worth more or less than the life that was taken in the crime. *Ela neshama shakil minei neshama amar Rachmana nishkol minei* — one life is equivalent to another life, because the Torah commanded that you take a life for a life. *Hacha nami nehora shakil minei nehora amar Rachmana nishkol minei* — so too here, one person's vision is equivalent to another's vision, and that is what you take. This constitutes a rejection of Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda's proof: the analogy to capital cases shows that courts routinely treat unequal things as legally equivalent, so the problem of unequal eye size does not force us to monetary compensation. However, the underlying question — whether *ayin tachat ayin* means money or actual physical injury — remains very much in dispute, and the *gemara* will press ahead with many further attempts.

## Shimon: Impossible Cases

### Blind Blinding Blind

*Tani idach*: a second suggestion, from a different *tanna*. *Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai omer: ayin tachat ayin — mamon ata omer, o eino ela ayin mamash?* His argument: *she-harei she-summa ve-simmei* — what if a blind person went and blinded someone else? There is no way to reciprocate, since there is no sight to take. Or *kiteia ve-kiteia* — a person missing a limb who went and amputated someone else's limb. *Chigger be-chigger* — a lame person who lamed another. *D'heicha ani mekayem be-zeh ayin tachat ayin?* In these cases, since the perpetrator already has the very injury he has now inflicted on another, reciprocity is physically impossible.

### Equal Law Again

*Ve-ha-Torah amra: mishpat echad yihyeh lachem, mishpat ha-shaveh le-chulchem.* The same principle of equal law must apply universally. You cannot have a system in which a sighted *mazik* has his eye put out while a blind *mazik* pays money — that is an inconsistent application of the law. That inconsistency itself demonstrates that the Torah cannot have intended literal physical reciprocity in the first place.

### Conditional Reciprocity

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: *amri: u-mai kushya? Dilma heicha d'efshar — efshar, heicha d'lo efshar — lo efshar u-fatrinan lei* — perhaps the system simply says: where it is possible to inflict equivalent punishment, do so; where it is impossible, either exempt him or collect money instead. This would be a nuanced rule rather than a blanket one.

### Treifa Objection

The *gemara* rejects this suggestion by means of a powerful analogy. *D'ilu teima hachi — treifa she-harag et ha-shalem, mai avdinan lei?* What if a person who is *treifa* — that is, mortally wounded and therefore halachically considered already as though dead, *gavra katila* — killed a healthy person? *Ha gavra katila hu*, Rashi notes: such a person is presumed to have a limited time to live, and executing him would therefore not be equivalent to the capital crime of taking a healthy person's full life. This creates the further problem of *edut she-i ata yakhol le-hazimah*: testimony that cannot be subjected to the full protection of *edim zomemim*. If witnesses testify against the *treifa* murderer and are later found to be conspiring witnesses, they themselves would be exempt from execution, since all they were trying to do was execute a *gavra katila* — someone already considered as dead. *D'hai mishtakchei edim zomemim peturim d'gavra katila ba'ei l'miktal*: the system of *edim zomemim* requires that whatever punishment the witnesses sought to impose upon their victim could in principle be turned back upon them. If it could not be — as here — the testimony is inadmissible from the outset. This is the disqualification of *edut she-i ata yakhol le-hazimah*, discussed at length in the first chapter of *Masechet Arachin* in the context of *hayotze le-haryeg*, a person already condemned and walking to execution: *lo nerach ve-lo ma'arichin oto*, he is assessed neither by others nor by himself, since he is treated as already dead. The point is that capital law itself produces analogous situations of structural impossibility, yet we do not abandon the rule of one punishment for all. The same logic therefore does not compel us to adopt a two-track system for *ayin tachat ayin*; and the suggestion of conditional reciprocity is rejected.

## Redundancy Proofs

### Yishmael Extra Word

*D'bei Rabbi Yishmael tana amar kra: ki yinaten bo* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives the monetary principle from a textual redundancy. The verse *ka'asher yitein mum ba-adam ken yinaten bo* — whatever one does to another shall be done to him — already states the principle of reciprocity. The word *ki yinaten bo*, appearing as a separate phrase, is therefore extra. *Ein netinah ela mamon*: the word *netinah*, giving, in its stand-alone usage refers to the giving of money, not the giving of an injury. Rabbi Yishmael's school, which is characteristically more restrictive in the types of *drashot* it is willing to make, derives from a superfluous word rather than claiming that *netinah* always means financial compensation.

### Yishmael Rejection

The *gemara* questions this: *ella ma'ata ka'asher yitein mum ba-adam* — the earlier clause also uses the root *natan* in describing the infliction of a physical injury (*mum*). Does that mean the injury itself is also a monetary matter? *Hach nami de-mamon hu?* Clearly not, since *mum* explicitly denotes a physical wound. The response is that *d'amri d'bei Rabbi Yishmael kra yateira darshi* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael makes *drashot* only from genuinely superfluous words. *Michdi ve-ish ki yitein mum ba-amito ka'asher asa ken ye'aseh lo* — one verse already covers the reciprocity principle fully. *Ki yinaten bo lama li? Shema mina mamon* — since this additional phrase is redundant, it must be teaching that the compensation takes the form of money. The parallel phrase *ka'asher yitein mum ba-adam* in turn is not itself superfluous but is needed to anchor the monetary compensation to the original act of physical injury, establishing that the financial payment is the legal equivalent of what was done.

### Chiya Hand-Hand

*D'bei Rabbi Chiya tana amar kra: yad be-yad* — the school of Rabbi Chiya derives the point differently. The phrase *yad be-yad*, a hand for a hand, does not mean that a hand is physically exchanged for a hand. Rather: *davar ha-nitan mi-yad le-yad* — it refers to something given from hand to hand, which is money. *U-mai nihu? Mamon.* Monetary compensation is what passes from one person's hand to the other.

### Chiya Foot Objection

This immediately raises a question: *ella me'ata regel be-regel* — if *yad be-yad* already teaches that compensation is monetary, why does the verse also need to say *regel be-regel*, a foot for a foot?

### Chiya Resolution

*Nami hachi hu. Amri d'bei Rabbi Chiya kra yateira darshi* — just as with the school of Rabbi Yishmael, the school of Rabbi Chiya is also deriving from redundancy. Since the verse has already stated *yad be-yad*, the further phrase *regel be-regel* is superfluous, and the redundancy as a whole teaches that monetary compensation — *mamon* — is the mandated form of payment. One might ask whether this creates a circular argument, particularly in connection with *edim zomemim*: the verse says *ve-asitem lo ka'asher zamam la'asot le'achiv*, we do to the conspiring witnesses what they sought to do to their victim. If we assume monetary damages, then *yad be-yad* is redundant and teaches monetary compensation; but the original question is precisely whether damages are monetary. The answer is that by the time *edim zomemim* is invoked, the form of the penalty is already known: if they sought a capital conviction, they are executed; if they sought a monetary obligation, they pay. Since we know that in the damages context *edim zomemim* pay money, this provides an independent baseline from which the redundancy of *yad be-yad* can be observed and the monetary principle extracted.

## Amoraic Proofs

### Abaye Death Risk

The *gemara* now moves from *tannaitic* sources to *amoraic* ones. Abaye derives from a *tanna* of the school of Chizkiyah: *ayin tachat ayin, nefesh tachat nefesh* — one pays an eye for an eye and a life for a life. The key clause is *ve-lo nefesh va-ayin tachat ayin* — you cannot take a person's life along with their eye as payment for taking out an eye. *Di salka daitach mamash* — if you thought it literally meant poking out his eye: *zimnin di-mashkachat lah ayin ve-nefesh tachat ayin, de-vahadi de-avir leh nafka leh nishmateh* — there are situations in which the attempt to take away someone's vision would end up killing them, whether from the pain, blood loss, or infection. In such a case, the literal punishment would inadvertently constitute a capital penalty for what was a non-capital offense, with no textual warrant.

### Assessment Before Injury

The *gemara* objects: *u-mai kushya? Dilma omdana omdinan leh* — perhaps we simply assess the person in advance. *Ei matzi le-kabulei, avdinan lei* — if the person can tolerate the procedure, we proceed; *ei lo matzi le-kabulei, lo avdinan lei* — if not, we refrain. Prior medical evaluation could eliminate the risk and preserve the literal punishment.

### Lashing Analogy

The *gemara* responds by invoking the parallel case of lashing: *tenan gabi malkus, amduhu va-met tachat yado, patur* — the *mishnah* teaches that if the court assessed how many lashes a person could tolerate and then the person died in the course of receiving them, the court representative administering the lashes is exempt. The court assesses as best it can, and if death occurs despite proper evaluation, there is no liability. The implication is that performing an assessment beforehand and then proceeding — even with the risk of death — is the accepted framework in physical punishment. This does not, however, prove that literal punishment cannot be the intended mode; it merely shows that the death risk would not automatically invalidate the system.

### Rav Zvid Petza

*Rav Zvid mishmeih de-Rava amar: amar kra petza tachat patza* — a burn for a burn. The phrase refers to a painful wound and is cited to establish that *tza'ar*, pain and suffering, is paid in the place of *nezek*, monetary damage. That is: *liten tza'ar bi-makom nezek* — the category of *tza'ar* is paid in a manner equivalent to how *nezek* is paid. *I salka daitach mamash* — if you thought the verse literally meant inflicting the equivalent injury: *ki heichi de-lehavei lei tza'ara* — why would the verse need to introduce *tza'ar* as a separate compensable category? If both parties undergo equivalent injuries, they will each have experienced equivalent pain; there would be no need for a separate financial payment for pain and suffering. *Ve-le-hai nami is lei tza'ara* — the *mazik* would also have the pain and suffering from the reciprocal injury. The very existence of *tza'ar* as a distinct category to be paid implies that the underlying mode of compensation is financial rather than physical.

### Sensitivity Objection

The *gemara* challenges this: *u-mai kushya? Dilma ika inish de-mefanak is lei tza'ara tefai, ve-ika inish de-lo mefanak leis lei tza'ara* — perhaps the category of *tza'ar* exists precisely because people feel pain differently. One person may be more sensitive and experience greater suffering from the same injury; another person may be more hardy and barely register the pain. Physical reciprocity alone cannot equalize these differences, and *tza'ar* as a financial payment serves to bridge that gap without requiring that the mode of primary compensation be monetary. *De-lo nafka minah le-meitav lei ha-hi de-veini beini* — you find the midpoint that equalizes the two extremes, paying the financial difference between the two parties' actual experiences of pain without abandoning the physical punishment itself.

### Tzaar-Nezek Link

The resolution, however, carries its own force. Once one concedes that *tza'ar* must be assessed and paid financially — because individual differences in pain sensitivity cannot be equalized by physical means alone — one has already accepted that there is a financial component to these injuries. And once *tza'ar* is established as *bi-makom nezek*, in the same legal register as *nezek*, it follows that *nezek* itself is also to be paid financially. The argument runs: if you are willing to say *tza'ar* can be entirely financial and need not take the form of causing the other person equivalent suffering, then since *tza'ar* stands in the place of *nezek*, *nezek* too is financial. At that point, the entire system of five payments is rendered financial, and the case for literal physical reciprocity collapses.

### Rav Pappa Healing

*Rav Pappa mishmeih de-Rava amar: amar kra ve-rapo yerape* — the Torah's provision of medical expenses is itself a proof. *Liten refuah bi-makom nezek* — medical expenses are paid as a form of compensation equivalent to *nezek*. If you thought the verse meant literal physical reciprocity — *de-ei salka daitach mamash* — then since both parties undergo equivalent injuries, both would require medical care: *ve-heichi de-hai ba'ei asya, hai nami ba'ei asya*. Why would the verse single out medical expenses as something the *mazik* pays to the *nizak*, if the *mazik* himself will have suffered equivalent damage requiring equivalent care?

### Medical Care Objection

The *gemara* counters: *salka daitach de-hai salka basar ve-hai lo salka basar* — perhaps one of the two injuries heals better than the other. Their physical conditions may differ, and the *mazik* may recover more quickly than the *nizak*, creating an asymmetry. *Heichi mehina takanta?* So there is still a need for some financial equalization between them.

### Medical Counter

The *gemara* acknowledges: *le-meitav lei hai chatzyo beini beini* — you find the middle ground and compensate the difference. This does not constitute a knockout proof that literal punishment is not the primary mode; as with the *tza'ar* case, you can accommodate the asymmetry with supplementary monetary payments without abandoning physical reciprocity as the principal rule. The argument therefore does not decisively close the question, and the *gemara* proceeds to Rav Ashi.

## Rav Ashi Shor

### Tachat Gezeira Shava

*Rav Ashi amar: atya tachat tachat mi-shor* — Rav Ashi proposes a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *tachat*. *Kesiv hacha: ayin tachat ayin* — here, in the laws of human injury, the word *tachat* appears. *U-chesiv hatam: shaleim yeshaleim shor tachat ha-shor* — there, in the laws of ox damages, the same word *tachat* appears. Just as ox damages are paid in money — a point established across the first six chapters of *Bava Kamma* — so too human bodily injuries are paid in money. *Ma halan mamon, af kan mamon.*

### Why Not Capital

The *gemara* objects: *mai chazit de-yalfat tachat tachat mi-shor? Neilaf tachat tachat me-adam* — why learn the *gezeirah shavah* from the ox case? Perhaps we should learn from the human case, specifically *de-ichtiv: ve-natata nefesh tachat nefesh*, where *tachat* appears in the context of capital crimes. *Ma halan mamash, af kan mamash* — just as capital punishment is literally carried out, so too physical injury should be literally repaid.

### Damages from Damages

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking a structural principle: *amri: dayninan nezikin mi-nezikin, ve-ein dayninan nezikin mi-mitah* — we learn damages from damages, not from capital crimes. The objection is then raised in the reverse direction: *aderaba, dayninan adam me-adam ve-ein dayninan adam mi-beheimah* — perhaps we should learn human-to-human cases from other human-to-human cases, not from animal cases. If so, the analogy to the ox is inappropriate, and we should learn from *nefesh tachat nefesh* instead. This mutual objection leaves the *gezeirah shavah* from ox damages in tension with the possible *gezeirah shavah* from capital crimes.

### Rav Ashi Rape

Rav Ashi therefore offers an alternative proof: *mi-tachat asher inah* — in the laws of rape, the Torah uses the word *tachat* in a context that is unambiguously monetary and involves human beings. *Yalpat lei adam me-adam* — we learn *tachat* for monetary compensation from a human-to-human case. *Ve-nezikin mi-nezikin* — and it is damages from damages, not a capital case. This satisfies both criteria simultaneously: it is a case of human beings dealing with human beings, and it is a case of monetary restitution for injury, not a capital analogue. The *gezeirah shavah* thus establishes on solid grounds that *ayin tachat ayin* refers to financial compensation.

## Rabbi Eliezer

### Eliezer Literal

*Tanya: Rabbi Eliezer omer ayin tachat ayin mamash* — a *baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Eliezer says that *ayin tachat ayin* means literally, actually, physically blinding the person's eye. This is a striking *gemara*: Rabbi Eliezer appears to stand alone against the consensus of all the *tannaim* surveyed above. The *gemara* asks with evident unease: *mamash salka datach? Rabbi Eliezer leit lei kulei hanei tanna'ei?* Does Rabbi Eliezer really disagree with all of these *tannaim*? That would undermine not only this entire *sugya* but also the Rambam's claim that *ayin tachat ayin* as monetary compensation is a *davar hamekubal mi-pi Moshe Rabbeinu* — an undisputed transmitted tradition.

### Rabbah Interpretation

*Amar Rabbah: lomar she-ein shamin oto ke-eved* — Rabbah resolves this by offering a different reading of Rabbi Eliezer's position. Rabbi Eliezer is not truly advocating for poking out people's eyes. Rather, his point is about the method of assessment. The standard *mishnah* formula assesses the *nizak* as a slave — how much would this person sell for before and after the injury — and the difference is the *nezek* payment. Rabbi Eliezer says: we do not use the slave-valuation method. Instead we assess the actual eye itself.

### Abaye Free Person

Abaye objects: *ela ke-man? Ke-ven chorin?* If not assessed like a slave, then you would assess him like a free person — *ben chorin mi is lei dmei?* But a free person has no market monetary value. There is no mechanism by which one assesses the worth of a free person's bodily integrity outside the slave-market framework.

### Rav Ashi Mazik

*Ela amar Rav Ashi: lomar she-ein shamin oto ba-nizak ela ba-mazik* — Rav Ashi's resolution is elegant. Rabbi Eliezer means that we do not assess how much the *nizak*'s eye was worth — that is, we do not calculate the damage suffered by the injured party in the standard *nezikin* framework. Instead, we assess how much the *mazik*'s eye is worth. The *mazik* pays the value of his own eye, not the *nizak*'s loss. This makes it structurally more like *kofer* — payment based on the value of the injurer — than like *nezek*, which is payment calibrated to the damage sustained by the victim. The distinction between *dmei nizak* and *dmei mazik* was already discussed in connection with *kofer* earlier in the tractate.

## Assessment Without Marke…

### Donkey Case

*Hahu chamra de-kata yadei de-yinoka* — there was a donkey that bit off a child's hand. The case came before *Rav Papa bar Shmuel*, who instructed: *zilu shimu lei arba'a devarim* — go collect the four categories of compensation. Rava objected: *ve-ha anan chamsha tenan* — don't we teach five categories? *Amar lei: le-var mi-nezek* — Rav Papa bar Shmuel meant four categories in addition to, or exclusive of, *nezek*. The underlying issue is that the case involves a donkey: an animal that causes injury is governed by the *shor* framework, not by *adam ha-mazik*, the laws of human bodily harm. A donkey pays only *nezek* and not the other four categories. Abaye then makes this explicit: *ve-ha chamor, chamor hu? Ve-chamor eino meshalem ella nezek* — it is a donkey, and a donkey only pays the damage, not pain, medical expenses, lost work, or embarrassment. The confusion arose because in Aramaic, *chamor* (donkey) and *chammar* (donkey driver) are easily conflated, and Rav Papa bar Shmuel had apparently thought it was a human being who caused the injury.

### No Authority Bavel

The court then instructed: *zilu shamu-hu ke-avda* — go assess the child's value as a slave, before and after the injury, to determine the *nezek* payment. The child's father objected: *lo ba'ina de-zilei be-milta* — he does not want this, because it is deeply degrading for his child to be assessed as a slave on the market. The court pressed him: *ve-ha ka-michayevat lei le-yinoka* — but you are still obligated to pay the child's damages. Rava's underlying concern, articulated more fully elsewhere, is that we do not have the proper *semicha* in Bavel to adjudicate cases that require assessing human beings like slaves — *kol ha-nishamin ke-eved ein govin oso be-Vavel* — and therefore the entire *nizak* assessment cannot be carried out in Bavel in the first place.

### Deferred Assessment

The resolution: *le-chi gadil me-fassina lei mi-didei* — when the child grows up, the father (or the now-grown child) can reach a settlement. The debt hangs over the *mazik* until the child reaches an age at which the assessment can properly be made and a resolution reached between the parties.

### Ox Case

*Hahu tora de-akhal yadei de-yinoka* — a parallel case: an ox bit off a child's hand. This came before Rava, who likewise instructed: *zilu shamu-hu ke-avda* — go assess the child's value as a slave. The child's father raised the same objection: *ve-ha mar hu de-amar kol ha-nishamin ke-eved ein govin oto be-Vavel* — did our master not himself teach that we do not collect such assessments in Bavel? Rava replied: *lo tzricha de-itfas* — we are dealing with a case where the party has already seized (*tafas*) a sum from the other side, and now the court can assess the proper amount to determine whether the seized amount was sufficient or whether more is owed. But initiating the case from scratch is something the Babylonian courts lack the authority to do in the first place.

## Authority in Bavel

### Rava Categories

This brings us to the formal articulation of Rava's rule. *De-amar Rava: nizkei shor be-shor, ve-nizkei shor be-adam — govin oto be-Vavel*. Cases in which one animal injured another animal, or an animal injured a person — those we do collect in Bavel. *Nizkei adam be-adam, ve-nizkei adam be-shor — ein govin oto be-Vavel*. But if a person injures another person, or a person causes damage to an animal, those we do not collect in Bavel.

### Elohim Requirement

The *gemara* asks: *mai shna nizkei adam be-adam ve-nizkei adam be-shor? De-lo elohim ba'inan* — in those cases we require that the judges be called *elohim*, the term used in *Parshat Mishpatim* to refer to judges. These judges stand in place of God, and that designation requires the full *semicha* going back to Moshe Rabbeinu — *semicha* that can only be conferred in *Eretz Yisrael* and that was discontinued once the Sanhedrin ceased to function there. In Bavel, that *semicha* no longer existed, and therefore the courts lacked the authority to adjudicate such cases. *Ve-leika* — and that authority is simply not present.

### Shlichusaihu Animals

The *gemara* then asks why the animal-damage cases are different: *nami elohim ba'inan ve-leika* — surely animal cases also require *elohim*-level judges, and that is equally lacking? *Ella mai shna shor be-shor ve-shor be-adam de-shlichutaihu ka'avdinan* — the answer is that for those cases we serve as *shlichutaihu*, as the messengers and representatives of the original Sanhedrin. The rabbis of Bavel, and by extension *dayanim* today, serve as representatives of the last properly constituted Sanhedrin in *Eretz Yisrael*. This principle — *shlichusaihu ka'avdinan*, we do their mission in their name — is what allows courts to function in the post-*semicha* era at all. The authority is not original but derivative, and the derivativeness itself carries significant limitations.

### Shachiach Chesron Kis

The *gemara* then defines the precise conditions under which *shlichusaihu ka'avdinan* can operate. The analogy is to *hoda'ot* and *halva'ot* — monetary admissions and loans. We serve as their representatives *be-midi d'kim lei be-gavei* — in matters that have established, clear-cut financial parameters. *Be-midi d'lo kim lei be-gavei — lo avdinan shlichutaihu* — in matters that are not clearly assessable in that fashion, we cannot serve as their representatives. The further requirements crystallize: *ki ka'avdinan shlichutaihu be-milta de-shachicha ve-it beih chesron kis* — we serve as their representatives only in cases that are (1) *shachiach*, commonly occurring, and (2) *chesron kis*, involving a direct financial loss that is being recovered. The rationale: just as the concern about *ne'ilat delet bifnei lav'in* — the risk of closing off access to loans if monetary disputes cannot be adjudicated — justifies representing the Sanhedrin in loan cases, so too in any case meeting these two criteria. Tosfos in *Yevamos* 47a applies this same logic to the acceptance of converts: *chashu nami le-ne'ilat delet bifnei gerim*, the Rabbis were concerned that overly strict requirements for conversion would close off access for sincere prospective converts, and therefore *shlichusaihu ka'avdinan* applies to conversion as well, even though conversion does not produce a direct *chesron kis* — the analogy to the spirit of the principle carries sufficient weight.

### Not Common Enough

*Hilchach adam be-adam — af al gav de-it beih chesron kis, keivan de-lo shachicha — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. One person injuring another person, even though it involves real financial loss, is not considered common enough (*shachiach*) for *shlichusaihu* to operate. The *halevi* that such cases do not occur more often — this is at least the *gemara*'s position, though one might note that what counts as *shachiach* is not always self-evident across different eras and contexts.

### Boshes Not Chesron

*Boshet* — embarrassment — presents the mirror-image problem. *Af al gav de-shachicha — keivan de-leit beih chesron kis — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. Embarrassment, while undeniably common, does not constitute *chesron kis* in the technical sense: the payment for *boshet* is not recovering money that was taken from you, but compensating you for an indignity inflicted upon you. There is no direct financial loss to restore. Rav Papa had in fact collected four hundred *zuz* for an embarrassment case, and Rav Chisda wrote to Rav Nachman sharply: *Chisda, Chisda — are you collecting fines in Bavel?* The principle is clear: both conditions — *shachiach* and *chesron kis* — must be present. *Be-milta de-shachicha ve-leit beih chesron kis — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. *I nami milta de-lo shachicha ve-it beih chesron kis — lo avdinan shlichutaihu*. Either condition alone is insufficient; both must be met simultaneously.

### Shor Tam Muad

This leads to a closer analysis of the animal-damage cases. Rava's statement that *shor she-hizik ein govin oto be-Vavel* — damages caused by an ox cannot be collected in Bavel — must be carefully parsed. It cannot mean all ox damages, since we just established that *shor be-shor* and *shor be-adam* can be adjudicated in Bavel. *De-hizik man? I leima de-hizik adam* — if what he meant is that an ox injured a person, then that falls into the same category as *adam be-adam*, requiring the full authority of *elohim*-judges. But then why single out the *shor* — even a person injuring a person cannot be adjudicated in Bavel, so the rule would apply equally. *Ella peshita de-hizik shor* — it must be that Rava's prohibition refers to an ox injuring another ox, and specifically to *shor tam*, where *chatzi nezek* is, at least according to some opinions, classified as a *kenas* rather than *mamon*. A *kenas* requires even higher authority and cannot be adjudicated under *shlichusaihu* at all. *Amri: hatam be-tam* — indeed, we are speaking of *shor tam*. *Hacha be-muad* — whereas a *shor muad*, which pays full *nezek* as *mamon*, can be collected in Bavel.

### Shen VeRegel Muad

The *gemara* then raises the complication: if *shor muad* can be adjudicated in Bavel but *shor tam* cannot, how does a *shor* ever become *muad* in Bavel, given that establishing *muad* status requires three prior adjudicated incidents of *shor tam*? Two possibilities are proposed: the ox became *muad* in *Eretz Yisrael* and was then brought to Bavel; or Rabbis from *Eretz Yisrael* who possessed genuine *semicha* came to Bavel and established *muad* status there. Both are rejected on the same grounds: *sof sof milta de-lo shachicha hi* — in either case it is not a commonly occurring situation, and *lo avdinan shlichutaihu* for things that are not *shachiach*. *Ella ki amar Rava be-shen va-regel de-muadim mi-tchillatan ninhu* — the resolution is that Rava's statement refers to *shen va-regel*, tooth and foot damages, which are *muadim mi-tchillatan*, forewarned from the outset without the need for three prior incidents. They therefore could easily arise in Bavel and would seem to be candidates for adjudication there. Yet Rava rules that even *shen va-regel* cannot be adjudicated in Bavel — and that is where the *sugya* concludes for today.
