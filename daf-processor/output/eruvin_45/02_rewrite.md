# Eruvin 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overlap & Techum

### Intro: Chutz L'Techum

Today's *daf* is 45, and we pick up at the bottom of 44b dealing with the issue of somebody who goes *chutz l'techum*. The first *mishna* of the chapter addressed going *chutz l'techum* because of an *ones* — one was forced outside the *techum*. This *mishna*, by contrast, speaks about going out for a *mitzvah* purpose, and also mentions going out *b'reshut* — by permission or choice — without yet specifying what that entails. The *halacha* established there is that one receives 2,000 *amot* from where one currently stands.

### Rabba vs. Rav Shimi

The question now debated in the *Gemara* between Rabba and Rav Shimi bar Chiya concerns what happens when one went out, found that one's services were no longer needed, and has 2,000 *amot* from one's current location. How does that relate to the original *techum* from home? If one has stepped beyond one's original 2,000 *amot* from home — if the new position lies outside the circle one began with — does the new 2,000 *amot* get one back to one's house, or does it simply define a new, independent *techum*?

The first position holds that if one is still within 2,000 *amot* of one's original *techum*, the overlap allows one to revert to one's old *techum*. But if one has left those 2,000 *amot*, one is confined to the new *techum* alone. The second position holds that even if one has left the original 2,000 *amot*, because the two *techumin* overlap, that overlap is sufficient to allow one to return home. And according to one reading in Rashi, the overlap does not merely restore the original *techum* — it grants both simultaneously. One could traverse all the way back through one's original *techum* and all the way forward through the new one, effectively having the combination of both.

### Rabbi Eliezer & Overlap

The *Gemara* picks up at the last line of 44b and raises a challenge. If without the special circumstance of a walled enclosure one would not invoke the principle of *havlaat techumin* — overlap of *techumin* — then how do we account for what we learned in the *mishna* at the top of 45a? Rabbi Eliezer says: *shtayim yikanes, shalosh lo yikanes* — if one steps over the 2,000-*amah* line by up to two *amot*, one may return; if by three *amot*, one may not. The reasoning is that Rabbi Eliezer holds one stands in the middle of one's four *amot*, meaning two *amot* extend in each direction. If one stepped over the 2,000-*amah* line by two *amot*, one's two *amot* in the backward direction will still overlap with the 2,000 *amot*, permitting re-entry. At three *amot*, one is completely outside — even one's two *amot* no longer create any overlap — and so one cannot return.

The *Gemara* asks: is not the explanation here that *Rabbi Eliezer l'teimei* — Rabbi Eliezer follows his own reasoning — since he holds one stands at the center of one's four *amot*, and the four *amot* granted by the Rabbis are *k'man d'mivlu damu*, as if absorbed into or overlapping the 2,000 *amot*, allowing one to re-enter one's original *techum* and travel all the way back home? This demonstrates that *havlaat techumin* — overlap — is indeed a significant and operative principle, permitting one to restore, at minimum, the original *techum*.

### Rabba bar bar Chana

Rabba bar bar Chana challenged this proof, saying to Abaye: Are you citing Rabbi Eliezer against the master — that is, against Rabba? Perhaps the Rabbis disagree precisely because they hold that *havlaat techumin* is not operative; that is the very point of their dispute with Rabbi Eliezer, and we may well rule like the *chachamim*.

The response was: yes, I am bringing my proof from Rabbi Eliezer, because I heard from the same master, Rabba himself, the following tradition: *ad kan lo pligi Rabbanan alei d'Rabbi Eliezer ela l'dvar reshut* — the Rabbis only disagree with Rabbi Eliezer in a case of *devar reshut*, where one overstepped the *techum* without any permissible cause. In such a case, since one should not have been *chutz l'techum* to begin with, the Rabbis refuse to allow the overlap to restore one's original *techum*. But *l'dvar mitzvah* — when one went *chutz l'techum* for a *mitzvah* purpose, as in our *mishna*, where one receives a full 2,000 *amot* rather than the usual four — in that case, even the Rabbis would agree that the overlap permits one to return home, and according to one version of Rashi, potentially to have both *techumin*.

### Practical Halacha

Before moving on, several practical *halachic* implications of the preceding discussion deserve attention. First, regarding Rabban Gamliel's ruling on *dir u'sohar* — a walled enclosure — the *Gemara* on yesterday's *daf* took for granted that we rule like Rav, who holds that we rule like Rabban Gamliel, meaning that even if one did not begin *Shabbat* inside the walled enclosure, one is given the entire enclosure upon entering it after going *chutz l'techum*. This is brought by the *Rishonim* as proof that the rule of Rabban Gamliel is accepted *halacha*.

A highly relevant modern application concerns someone whose flight is delayed on *Shabbat*: upon landing and stepping into the jetway or terminal, one finds oneself in a walled enclosure — and on this basis one would be granted the full space of that enclosure.

A second and equally important issue concerns the *mishna*'s listing of cases where one was placed *chutz l'techum* — including being forcibly transported to another city, *holichuhu l'ir acheres*, alongside the case of *dir u'sohar*. What constitutes "another city" here? The *Shulchan Aruch* and a number of *Rishonim* hold this refers specifically to a walled city, making it simply another instance of a walled enclosure. But the Rambam and other *Rishonim* — and some permit relying on this *b'shas hadechak* — understand it to mean even an unwalled city. Just as one who begins *Shabbat* within a city is given the entire city as a single unit without it counting against one's 2,000 *amot*, so too, if one is brought *chutz l'techum* and deposited in an unwalled city, one receives the full city. The practical implication is significant: if one's plane was delayed and one had no control over the situation, one may be given the full city in which the airport is located, entirely aside from any question about walled enclosures.

A further question raised concerns the case where one willfully chose to fly over *Shabbat* and land. There it is a *machlokes Rishonim* whether one who voluntarily left one's *techum* and finds oneself in a walled enclosure may walk the full space — the debate of the *mishna*'s emphasis on *ones* cuts in different directions among the *Rishonim*, and the practical question about delayed flights turns on exactly this distinction.

## Saving & Weapons

### Contradiction

The *mishna* concludes: *v'chol hayotzim l'hatzil chozrin limkoman* — and all who go out to save may return to their original place. The *Gemara* is troubled: the earlier part of the *mishna* established that one going out for a *mitzvah* has 2,000 *amot* from the new position — yet the conclusion seems to offer a blanket permission to go home regardless of distance. If one entered a new city in the course of the mission, one has the whole city plus 2,000 *amot* from its edge. But one is still defined by the new position. So which is it: 2,000 *amot*, or an unlimited return home?

### Rav Yehuda Answer

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav answers: *shechozrin bichlei zayin limkoman* — the *mishna*'s statement is not primarily about the distance one may travel. It comes to teach an additional and distinct leniency: that those who went out to save may return *carrying their weapons*. The permission to carry arms on *Shabbat* is the novel ruling — not necessarily an extension of travel beyond 2,000 *amot*.

Why is carrying weapons permitted? This is not a case of *heter sofo mishum techilaso* — the end permitted on account of the beginning. Rather, as will be seen, the reason is that even once the battle appears to have concluded, depositing one's weapons immediately exposes one to renewed attack. Therefore one may remain armed until safely home, as a matter of *pikuach nefesh*.

One might ask: why does the *mishna* say *limkoman* — back to their place — implying travel all the way home? Practically speaking, the *halacha* as ruled is that when one goes out to fight the enemy, one may go all the way home, not merely 2,000 *amot*, because the entire situation is one of *pikuach nefesh*. The 2,000-*amot* limit applies to those who went out for other permissible purposes. The *Gemara* at this stage does not emphasize this point, but *l'halacha* that is how it is understood.

### Rosh Hashanah Mishna

The *Gemara* then reconsiders: was there even a question to begin with? Perhaps *l'hatzil shani* — saving from attack is obviously different from other permitted departures, and so the blanket return makes self-evident sense without any contradiction. The *Gemara* replies: *ela ha kashya ha kashya* — it is a different difficulty that drove the question and its answer, namely the *mishna* in *Rosh Hashanah*.

That *mishna* recounts that originally, when witnesses came on *Shabbat* to testify about the new moon and went *chutz l'techum* to do so, they were confined to the courtyard where they had arrived — no special leniency yet existed for those who departed for a *mitzvah*. Rabban Gamliel the elder then enacted a *takana* that these witnesses receive 2,000 *amot* in every direction. The *mishna* then broadens this: *v'lo elu bilvad amru* — and they did not say this only for the witnesses, but also for a midwife coming to assist a birth, and for *ha'ba l'hatzil min hagayis umin hanahar umin hamapolet umin hadeika* — one who comes to save people from an attacking army, from a flooding river, from a collapsing building, or from a fire. All such individuals become like the inhabitants of the city they entered and receive 2,000 *amot* in every direction from its edge.

Now the difficulty becomes sharp: the *Rosh Hashanah mishna* explicitly includes saving from an army among the cases granted 2,000 *amot* — yet our *mishna* says those who go out to save may return home without any stated limit. So there are now two cases of *l'hatzil* — one capped at 2,000 *amot* and one apparently uncapped — and one cannot simply say *l'hatzil* is categorically different. This is the contradiction that produced the answer of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: the return-home clause speaks not to distance of travel but to carrying weapons.

### Weapons Braita

The *Gemara* elaborates by citing a *braita*: *barishonah hayu manichin klei zaynam b'veit hasamuch lachomah* — originally they used to leave their weapons in a house near the city wall after repelling an attack. On one occasion, *hikiru vahen oyvim* — the enemy recognized that they had disarmed, chased them, and the soldiers rushed to retrieve their weapons from the armory. In the ensuing crush, *hargu zeh et zeh yoter mimah shehargu oyvim* — they killed one another more than the enemy had killed them. This episode has the quality of moral instruction — a recurring motif in rabbinic literature — that internal disorder can cause us more damage than the enemy.

*B'osa shaah hitkinu* — at that moment they enacted that soldiers may keep their weapons on them until they have returned safely to their homes. This is the *takana* referred to in the *mishna*'s ruling. According to the approach of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, one's permitted distance of travel remains 2,000 *amot*, but the carrying of weapons is sanctioned for the full journey home as a *pikuach nefesh* measure.

One additional observation about calling this a *takana*: there is merit to the point that framing the law as a public enactment serves the purpose of broadcasting the rule — which is itself important for the safety of those returning, as the enemy would know not to expect the soldiers to disarm prematurely.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an independent resolution to the question of distance — setting aside the weapons issue entirely. He holds: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *Kan shenitzchu Yisrael et umot haolam* — where the Jews won the battle, they have only 2,000 *amot*, since they are presumably in a position of strength, especially if permitted to remain armed. *Kan shenitzchu umot haolam* — when the non-Jews prevailed, the soldiers may travel all the way home, since remaining at a fixed point 2,000 *amot* from the city would leave them dangerously exposed. The need to return home in that case is itself a *pikuach nefesh* concern, and no distance limit applies.

## Siege & Pikuach Nefesh

### Siege Categories

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav teaches: *nochrim shetzaru al ayarot Yisrael* — if non-Jews lay siege to Jewish towns, *ein yotzim aleihem bichlei zaynam v'ein mechalelin aleihem et haShabbat* — one may not go out to fight them with weapons or violate *Shabbat* on their account. This is a striking ruling, since one might assume any siege implicates *pikuach nefesh*.

A *braita* explains: *bameh devarim amurim* — when does this apply? *Sheba'u al iskei mamon* — when the siege concerns monetary matters, such as a dispute over tribute. In that case, one does not violate *Shabbat*; the remedy is to pay. But *ba'u al iskei nefashot* — if they have come for matters of life, where actual killing is intended, *yotzim aleihem bichlei zayin umechalelin aleihem et haShabbat* — one must fight and may violate *Shabbat*.

It is worth noting that the earliest documented instance of *Torah Sheba'al Peh* — of applying reasoned legal analysis to yield a novel practical ruling — appears to be the episode in *Sefer Chashmonaim* where soldiers, after being slaughtered because they refused to fight on *Shabbat*, concluded that God intends for them to fight back in self-defense. The principle that *pikuach nefesh docheh Shabbat* and that one may fight the enemy on *Shabbat* traces its earliest narrative expression there.

### Border Cities

There is a further and more expansive rule: *uve'ir hasmucha lasefar* — in a city near the border, *afilu lo ba'u al iskei nefashot* — even if the attackers came only over monetary matters, such as straw and stubble, *yotzim aleihem bichlei zayin umechalelin aleihem et haShabbat* — one may go out armed and violate *Shabbat*. A border city under siege is treated as a situation of *pikuach nefesh* in its own right, because allowing a border city to be overrun — even over a property dispute — poses an existential threat.

Rav Yosef bar Minyomi in the name of Rav Nachman states: *uveBavel ke'ir hasmucha lasefar damya* — Babylonia as a whole is treated as a city near the border. The *Gemara* clarifies that what is meant is *Nehardea* specifically, which was on the border of the Persian Empire and was in fact overrun by the Romans on multiple occasions. The mention of Nehardea thus serves as a concrete illustration of a border city in the relevant geopolitical context — the frontier between Rome and Persia.

This raises a genuinely interesting question: is the *Gemara* presenting this as a theoretical illustration, or is it ruling practically that Jews living in Nehardea would be obligated to defend the Persian frontier against Rome? There are documented cases of Jews serving in foreign armies, sometimes by conscription and sometimes by choice. If a Jew were a citizen of Nehardea during an attack, it is a real question whether the *Gemara* intends to say he could take up arms in defense of the Persian Empire on *Shabbat* — not merely as a matter of protecting his own life, but as a participant in the broader defense of the city.

### David & Keilah

Rav Dostai from Biri expounds the verse in Shmuel 1, chapter 23: *Vayagidu l'David lemor, Hinei Plishtim nilchamim b'Keilah v'heima shosim et hagranot* — they told David that the Philistines were fighting in Keilah and attacking the granaries. A *braita* teaches that Keilah was a city near the border — *ir hasmucha lasefar* — and the Philistines came only over monetary matters, *iskei teven v'kash*, straw and stubble. This aligns precisely with the rule about border cities, where even a monetary dispute triggers the obligation to fight on *Shabbat*.

David then asks God — through the *Urim v'Tumim* — whether to go and strike the Philistines. The *Gemara* pauses: what exactly was David asking? If the question was whether it was permitted or forbidden — a *halachic* question about *Shabbat* — he had no need to consult the *Urim v'Tumim*, for *Beit dino shel Shmuel ha'Ramati kayam* — the court of Shmuel the Ramathite was active. One does not ask *halachic* questions of the *Urim v'Tumim*. Rather, *i mazlach i lo mazlach* — he was asking whether he would succeed. This is confirmed by the divine response: *v'hoshat et Keilah* — you will save Keilah — which speaks to outcome, not to legal permissibility. The *Gemara* notes that this interpretation means the Keilah episode does not directly prove the rule about border cities on *Shabbat*, though the *braita* itself establishes that rule independently.

## Sleeping Traveler

### Mishna Setup

The next *mishna* introduces a distinct category: *mi she'yashan baderech* — one who was traveling and fell asleep before *Shabbat* began, encamping wherever one happened to be. Upon waking — presumably the next morning — one sees that one is near a city. *Harei hu samuch l'ir* — the city was within 2,000 *amot* of where one slept. *Ho'il v'lo haita kavanato l'kach* — since when *Shabbat* began, one was not intending to join that city, indeed was entirely unaware of its proximity, *lo yikanes* — one is not treated as an inhabitant of that city. One may travel toward the city but only up to one's 2,000-*amah* limit. If one camped 1,999 *amot* from the city's edge, one can enter one *amah* into it and no further.

This ruling requires appreciation of the underlying leniency that is being withheld. Ordinarily, if one begins *Shabbat* inside a city, the entire city is treated as a single unit and does not count against one's 2,000 *amot*. A further leniency allows that even if one is outside the city but within 2,000 *amot* of it, one may be treated as an inhabitant of the city — one's notional starting point is extended to the city itself. This is an unusual concession, since ordinarily one's *techum* begins from where one physically stands, not from a separate location one did not occupy. The *mishna* is debating whether this secondary leniency requires at minimum that one was aware of and intending toward the city when *Shabbat* began.

### Rabbi Meir & Yehuda

Rabbi Meir holds that without the requisite *kavanah* — intention or awareness — one is not considered to have begun *Shabbat* in the city, and one has only one's 2,000 *amot* from the point of encampment. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and holds that one may enter the city as if one had begun *Shabbat* there.

### Rabbi Tarfon

Rabbi Yehuda supports his position with a *ma'aseh*: *ma'aseh b'Rabbi Tarfon shehayah mahalech baderech v'chashchah lo* — it once happened that Rabbi Tarfon was traveling and darkness overtook him on Friday. He slept outside the city, unaware of its proximity. In the morning, some cowherds found him and told him: *Harei ha'ir l'fanecha* — the city is right before you. He rose, entered, went to the *beit medrash*, and taught there the entire day — treating himself as an inhabitant of the city he had not knowingly camped near.

Rabbi Meir dismisses this proof on two grounds. First: *shema b'libo hayta* — perhaps Rabbi Tarfon was not truly unaware; just because the cowherds told him does not mean he had no prior knowledge of the city's location. Second: *beit hamedrash muvla b'toch tchomav hayah* — perhaps the *beit medrash* itself was within his personal 2,000 *amot*, and he simply traveled there without invoking the city-inhabitant status at all. So the story proves nothing about the broader principle.

### Sleep Onset

A related but distinct case concerns one who sat down on the road and *lo yada shechashchah* — did not know that *Shabbat* was beginning, falling asleep before dark and remaining unconscious when *Shabbat* commenced. This goes beyond the prior question. The earlier debate concerned whether awareness of the city matters; this debate concerns whether consciousness at the moment *Shabbat* begins is necessary at all to establish one's *techum*.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds: *yesh lo alpayim amah l'chol ruach* — one has 2,000 *amot* in every direction from where one slept. Even without consciousness at the moment *Shabbat* began, one's physical presence at a location is sufficient to establish it as one's *makom* — one's place — for *techum* purposes.

The *chachamim* disagree: *ein lo ela arba amot* — one has only four *amot*. The *chachamim* apparently hold that some measure of conscious establishment of place is required, and an unconscious person has not done so.

The sub-disputes of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehuda about *how* to measure the four *amot* apply here as they do elsewhere. Rabbi Eliezer holds one stands at the center, so two *amot* extend in each direction. Rabbi Yehuda holds one may choose in which direction to deploy the four *amot*, though once one has committed to a direction one cannot retract.

## Multiple People

### Two People

A further case: if two people found themselves *chutz l'techum*, each with only four *amot*, and the four *amot* of one overlapped with the four *amot* of the other — *miktzat amotav shel zeh b'toch amotav shel zeh* — they may bring food and eat together in the shared middle space. However, *uvlevad shelo yotzi zeh mishelo l'toch shel chaveiro* — neither may bring objects from the portion of space that is exclusively one's own into the other's space. The reason is that one's vessels and possessions are also limited by one's *techum*, and carrying them from one's exclusive zone into the other's exclusive zone would take them outside their *techum*. The shared, overlapping zone is available to both; one's exclusive zone is not.

### Three People

When there are three such people, with the middle person overlapping with both neighbors but the two outer persons not overlapping with each other: *ha-emtzai muvla benehem* — the middle one is swallowed between them. He may eat with either of the two outer persons; they may eat with him. But *shnayim hachitzonim asurim ze im ze* — the two outer persons may not share with each other, as they have no overlapping space.

### Three Courtyards

Rabbi Shimon illustrates this with an analogy to three courtyards, each opening both into the others and into the *reshut harabbim*. If two of the courtyards each made a joint *eruv* with the middle courtyard — but not with each other — the middle courtyard may carry with either neighbor, and each neighbor may carry with the middle, but the two outer courtyards may not carry between themselves, as they made no *eruv* with each other.

The reason it matters that each courtyard has an opening to the *reshut harabbim* is significant: if the only access to an inner courtyard were through an outer one, it would compel all three to make a single combined *eruv*, since independence would be impossible. Where each has independent street access, they can each act independently in their *eruv* arrangements, producing this scenario of partial mutual permission.

## Rava's Question

### Cheftzei Hefker

Rava poses the foundational question about Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position: *mai kasavar Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri?* What is the conceptual basis for holding that a sleeping person establishes a *techum* where he lies? There are two possible ways to understand him.

The first possibility: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds *cheftzei hefker konim shevita* — that even ownerless, inanimate objects establish their *shevita* — their resting place for *Shabbat* — wherever they happen to be when *Shabbat* begins. No consciousness is required; physical presence at a location suffices. Under this view, the *Gemara* notes, *uvedin hu deliflu bekelim* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri could just as well have stated his position by the case of inanimate vessels. He chose to debate it by a sleeping person in order to *lehodia'acha kochan derabbanan* — to reveal the far-reaching position of the Rabbis: that even a sleeping person — who after all *could* have been awake and consciously established his place — does not, in the Rabbis' view, establish his *techum* where he sleeps. Conversely, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds it works even for ownerless objects.

It should be noted that by inanimate objects the question of *koneh shevita* is a *chumra*: if an ownerless object establishes its own *techum*, one is limited in how far one can carry it. If it does not, one's possession of it on *Shabbat* causes it to follow one's own *techum* — which is how one wants it defined, since one can take it wherever one goes.

The second possibility: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds *cheftzei hefker ein konim shevita* — ownerless objects do not establish their own *techum*. Consciousness is in principle required. Nevertheless, a sleeping person is different: *ho'il venir'or kanei, yashen nami kanei* — since had he been awake he would clearly have established this as his place, even while asleep it is considered established. Under this reading, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri requires at least potential intention — the capacity for consciousness — and a sleeping person satisfies that condition in a way an inanimate object does not.

The *Gemara* thus leaves open which of these two rationales underlies Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position.

### Rain Case

Rav Yosef attempts to resolve the question. He cites the case of rain: *geshamim sheyardu me'erev Yom Tov* — rain that fell on *erev Yom Tov*, before *Yom Tov* commenced. The ruling is *yesh lahem alpayim amah l'chol ruach* — the rain water has 2,000 *amot* from wherever it fell. Since the water was physically present at that location when *Yom Tov* began, it is defined by that location. By contrast, rain that fell *b'Yom Tov* itself — meaning it did not exist at any location when *Yom Tov* commenced — *harei hen k'raglei kol adam*, it follows the person who takes possession of it.

This appears to be direct evidence that an inanimate, ownerless substance — rain water — is *koneh shevita* wherever it lies when *Yom Tov* begins, supporting the first reading of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri.

Rav Safra challenges: *dilma begeshamim hasmuchim l'ir askinan* — perhaps the case concerns rain falling near a city whose inhabitants knew it was about to rain and had it in mind. Under that reading, the rain is not truly ownerless *hefker* — the people's anticipatory awareness effectively constitutes a quasi-intentional designation of where it fell. The rain therefore goes by where they expected it to fall, not because inanimate *hefker* establishes its own *techum*. One cannot extract a principle about *cheftzei hefker* from a case where human intention was implicitly present.

### Wells Case

The *Gemara* dismisses this objection and adduces clearer evidence from a *braita* about wells. A privately owned well — *bor shel yachid* — goes by the *techum* of its owner. A well owned by an entire city — *shel osa ha'ir* — goes by the *techum* of the city as a whole, even if individual inhabitants made their own private *eiruvi techumin* extending in different directions. So far there is no novelty.

The instructive case is *shel olei Bavel* — a well belonging to the travelers, meaning an ownerless public well on the highway used by anyone passing. The ruling is *k'raglei hamemalei* — it goes by the person who draws from it. This indicates that an ownerless object does *not* establish its own *techum*; it follows the one who takes possession.

But a second *braita* presents the case of *bor shel Shvatim* — a well owned by the *Shvatim*, meaning by all of *Klal Yisrael*. Although *Klal Yisrael* does own this well, the ownership is so diffuse — all of Israel has no single *techum* — that it is effectively equivalent to *hefker*. Yet the ruling here is *yesh lahem alpayim amah l'chol ruach* — it has 2,000 *amot* from the well itself. This well, despite its effectively ownerless status, *does* establish its own *techum*.

### Mapping Debate

These two *braitot* contradict each other directly on the question of ownerless objects. One holds *cheftzei hefker ein konim shevita* — they do not establish their own *techum*, and the *olei Bavel* well follows whoever draws from it. The other holds *cheftzei hefker konim shevita* — the *Shvatim* well establishes its own 2,000-*amah* radius. The *Gemara* concludes: *ela shma mina tannaim hi* — this is necessarily a dispute among *Tannaim*. *Ha Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, ha Rabbanan* — one *braita* follows Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position, and the other follows the *chachamim*. The debate about the wells maps precisely onto the debate of our *mishna* concerning the sleeping traveler.

### Rav Yosef

Abaye reported this entire exchange to Rav Yosef — including Rav Safra's attempt to dismiss the rain case by invoking the city-dwellers' anticipatory awareness, and Abaye's counter that the wells *braita* provides independent Tannaitic evidence of the dispute. Rav Yosef then points out that one could have refuted Rav Safra's objection from the rain case itself, without needing to invoke the wells at all. If the reason the rain establishes its *techum* from where it fell were only because the city's inhabitants had it in mind, then the *braita* should have stated *k'raglei anshei osa ha'ir* — it goes by the *techum* of the people of the city, since it would effectively be as if they placed it there. Instead, the *braita* says it goes 2,000 *amot* from where the rain fell — a point that would only make sense if the rain itself is *koneh shevita* at the location of its fall, independent of any human intention. Rav Safra's explanation thus collapses from within the rain case before one even reaches the wells.

The upshot of the entire discussion is that there is Tannaitic evidence for both positions. There are *Tannaim* who hold *cheftzei hefker konim shevita* — as expressed in the *Shvatim* well and arguably in the rain case — and *Tannaim* who hold *cheftzei hefker ein konim shevita* — as expressed in the *olei Bavel* well. The *Gemara* maps this dispute onto the debate between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the *chachamim* about whether a sleeping person establishes a *techum*, suggesting that the deeper question is not specific to human consciousness but concerns the general principle of whether physical presence at a location — without active awareness or intention — suffices to establish *shevita* there.
