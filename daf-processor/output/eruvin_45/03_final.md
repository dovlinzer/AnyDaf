# Eruvin 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overlap & Techum

### Intro: Chutz L'Techum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁיָּצָא בִּרְשׁוּת, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״כְּבָר נַעֲשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה״ — יֵשׁ לוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. אִם הָיָה בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם — כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who was permitted to leave** his Shabbat limit, i.e., he went out to testify that he had seen the new moon or for some life-saving purpose, **and they said to him** along the way: **The action has already been performed,** and there is no need for you to travel for that purpose, **he has two thousand cubits in each direction** from the location where he was standing when this was told to him. **If he was within** his original **limit,** it is considered **as if he had not left** his limit, and he may return to his original location.

Today's *daf* is 45, and we pick up at the bottom of 44b dealing with the issue of somebody who goes *chutz l'techum*. The first *mishna* of the chapter addressed going *chutz l'techum* because of an *ones* — one was forced outside the *techum*. This *mishna*, by contrast, speaks about going out for a *mitzvah* purpose, and also mentions going out *b'reshut* — by permission or choice — without yet specifying what that entails. The *halacha* established there is that one receives 2,000 *amot* from where one currently stands.

### Rabba vs. Rav Shimi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם הָיָה בְּתוֹךְ תְּחוּם שֶׁלּוֹ — כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ דָּמֵי. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְעָקַר — עָקַר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם הָיוּ תְּחוּמִין שֶׁנָּתְנוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים מוּבְלָעִין בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם שֶׁלּוֹ, כְּאִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא מִתְּחוּמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** The mishna **is saying as follows: If he was within his** original **limit, it is** considered **as if he had never left his house.** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that if he remained within his limit, he is considered as if he were in his house. Why is this statement necessary? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that, **since he moved** from his place with intention to leave his limit and go elsewhere, **he moved** and nullified his original place of residence. If so, his original place of residence would no longer determine his Shabbat limit, and instead he would have two thousand cubits in each direction from the location where he was standing when he was told that he need not travel. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that it is nonetheless considered as if he had never left his house. **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya said** that the mishna is **saying as follows: If** he left his original Shabbat limit, but **the** new **limit** of two thousand cubits in each direction **that the Sages granted him is subsumed within his** original **limit,** so that if he walks those two thousand cubits, he can return to within his original limit, then **it is as if he had never left his** original **limit,** and he may return to his house.

The question now debated in the *Gemara* between Rabba and Rav Shimi bar Chiya concerns what happens when one went out, found that one's services were no longer needed, and has 2,000 *amot* from one's current location. How does that relate to the original *techum* from home? If one has stepped beyond one's original 2,000 *amot* from home — if the new position lies outside the circle one began with — does the new 2,000 *amot* get one back to one's house, or does it simply define a new, independent *techum*?

The first position holds that if one is still within 2,000 *amot* of one's original *techum*, the overlap allows one to revert to one's old *techum*. But if one has left those 2,000 *amot*, one is confined to the new *techum* alone. The second position holds that even if one has left the original 2,000 *amot*, because the two *techumin* overlap, that overlap is sufficient to allow one to return home. And according to one reading in Rashi, the overlap does not merely restore the original *techum* — it grants both simultaneously. One could traverse all the way back through one's original *techum* and all the way forward through the new one, effectively having the combination of both.

### Rabbi Eliezer & Overlap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר הַבְלָעַת תְּחוּמִין מִילְּתָא הִיא, וּמָר סָבַר לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא. וְהָתְנַן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שְׁתַּיִם — יִכָּנֵס, שְׁלֹשָׁה — לֹא יִכָּנֵס. מַאי לָאו, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּאָמַר וְהוּא בְּאֶמְצָעָן — וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דִּיהַבוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן כְּמַאן דְּמִיבַּלְעָן דָּמוּ, וְקָאָמַר ״יִכָּנֵס״ — אַלְמָא הַבְלָעַת תְּחוּמִין מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rabba and Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya **disagree?** One **Sage,** Rav Shimi, **holds** that **the subsuming of** Shabbat **limits,** i.e., if one's original limit is subsumed within the new limit, one may pass from one to the other, **is something** significant and may be relied upon, whereas this **Sage,** Rabba, **holds** that **it is nothing** significant and cannot be relied upon. **Didn't we learn** in a mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If a person left his Shabbat limit by walking **two** cubits beyond it, **he may reenter** his original limit; but if he left his Shabbat limit by walking **three** cubits beyond it, **he may not reenter. What,** is it **not that Rabbi Eliezer follows his** standard line of **reasoning, in** that he **said** with regard to the four cubits a person is allotted wherever he is, he is set **in the middle of them,** i.e., he may walk two cubits in each direction? The Gemara explains that **the four cubits that the Sages gave** a person **are regarded** here **as being subsumed** within his original limit, **and** it is for this reason that **he said: He may reenter** his original limit. **Apparently** he is of the opinion that **the subsuming of** one Shabbat limit within another **is something** significant.

The *Gemara* picks up at the last line of 44b and raises a challenge. If without the special circumstance of a walled enclosure one would not invoke the principle of *havlaat techumin* — overlap of *techumin* — then how do we account for what we learned in the *mishna* at the top of 45a? Rabbi Eliezer says: *shtayim yikanes, shalosh lo yikanes* — if one steps over the 2,000-*amah* line by up to two *amot*, one may return; if by three *amot*, one may not. The reasoning is that Rabbi Eliezer holds one stands in the middle of one's four *amot*, meaning two *amot* extend in each direction. If one stepped over the 2,000-*amah* line by two *amot*, one's two *amot* in the backward direction will still overlap with the 2,000 *amot*, permitting re-entry. At three *amot*, one is completely outside — even one's two *amot* no longer create any overlap — and so one cannot return.

The *Gemara* asks: is not the explanation here that *Rabbi Eliezer l'teimei* — Rabbi Eliezer follows his own reasoning — since he holds one stands at the center of one's four *amot*, and the four *amot* granted by the Rabbis are *k'man d'mivlu damu*, as if absorbed into or overlapping the 2,000 *amot*, allowing one to re-enter one's original *techum* and travel all the way back home? This demonstrates that *havlaat techumin* — overlap — is indeed a significant and operative principle, permitting one to restore, at minimum, the original *techum*.

### Rabba bar bar Chana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה לְאַבָּיֵי: וּמִדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר קָמוֹתְבַּתְּ לֵיהּ לְמָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, דִּשְׁמִיעַ לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר: עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֶלָּא לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת, אֲבָל לִדְבַר מִצְוָה מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said to Abaye: Do you raise an objection against our Master,** Rabba, **from** the statement of **Rabbi Eliezer?** But isn't the *halakha* in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer? Abaye **said to him: Yes, as I heard from our Master** himself that **the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Eliezer only** with regard to one who went beyond his limit **for a voluntary matter, but** with regard to one who went out **for a mitzva matter, they agree with him** about the subsuming of limits, i.e., that if one limit is subsumed in another, it is permitted to pass between them. This demonstrates that the *halakha* recognizes the principle of the subsuming of limits.

Rabba bar bar Chana challenged this proof, saying to Abaye: Are you citing Rabbi Eliezer against the master — that is, against Rabba? Perhaps the Rabbis disagree precisely because they hold that *havlaat techumin* is not operative; that is the very point of their dispute with Rabbi Eliezer, and we may well rule like the *chachamim*.

The response was: yes, I am bringing my proof from Rabbi Eliezer, because I heard from the same master, Rabba himself, the following tradition: *ad kan lo pligi Rabbanan alei d'Rabbi Eliezer ela l'dvar reshut* — the Rabbis only disagree with Rabbi Eliezer in a case of *devar reshut*, where one overstepped the *techum* without any permissible cause. In such a case, since one should not have been *chutz l'techum* to begin with, the Rabbis refuse to allow the overlap to restore one's original *techum*. But *l'dvar mitzvah* — when one went *chutz l'techum* for a *mitzvah* purpose, as in our *mishna*, where one receives a full 2,000 *amot* rather than the usual four — in that case, even the Rabbis would agree that the overlap permits one to return home, and according to one version of Rashi, potentially to have both *techumin*.

### Practical Halacha

Before moving on, several practical *halachic* implications of the preceding discussion deserve attention. First, regarding Rabban Gamliel's ruling on *dir u'sohar* — a walled enclosure — the *Gemara* on yesterday's *daf* took for granted that we rule like Rav, who holds that we rule like Rabban Gamliel, meaning that even if one did not begin *Shabbat* inside the walled enclosure, one is given the entire enclosure upon entering it after going *chutz l'techum*. This is brought by the *Rishonim* as proof that the rule of Rabban Gamliel is accepted *halacha*.

A highly relevant modern application concerns someone whose flight is delayed on *Shabbat*: upon landing and stepping into the jetway or terminal, one finds oneself in a walled enclosure — and on this basis one would be granted the full space of that enclosure.

A second and equally important issue concerns the *mishna*'s listing of cases where one was placed *chutz l'techum* — including being forcibly transported to another city, *holichuhu l'ir acheres*, alongside the case of *dir u'sohar*. What constitutes "another city" here? The *Shulchan Aruch* and a number of *Rishonim* hold this refers specifically to a walled city, making it simply another instance of a walled enclosure. But the Rambam and other *Rishonim* — and some permit relying on this *b'shas hadechak* — understand it to mean even an unwalled city. Just as one who begins *Shabbat* within a city is given the entire city as a single unit without it counting against one's 2,000 *amot*, so too, if one is brought *chutz l'techum* and deposited in an unwalled city, one receives the full city. The practical implication is significant: if one's plane was delayed and one had no control over the situation, one may be given the full city in which the airport is located, entirely aside from any question about walled enclosures.

A further question raised concerns the case where one willfully chose to fly over *Shabbat* and land. There it is a *machlokes Rishonim* whether one who voluntarily left one's *techum* and finds oneself in a walled enclosure may walk the full space — the debate of the *mishna*'s emphasis on *ones* cuts in different directions among the *Rishonim*, and the practical question about delayed flights turns on exactly this distinction.

## Saving & Weapons

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַיּוֹצְאִים לְהַצִּיל חוֹזְרִין לִמְקוֹמָן. וַאֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא: ״אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה״, וְתוּ לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **All who go out to save** lives **may return to their** original **locations** on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: Does this mean that he may return to his original place **even** if he went out **more** than two thousand cubits beyond his limit? **Didn't the first clause say** that a person who was permitted to travel beyond his Shabbat limit is allotted **two thousand cubits, and no more?**

The *mishna* concludes: *v'chol hayotzim l'hatzil chozrin limkoman* — and all who go out to save may return to their original place. The *Gemara* is troubled: the earlier part of the *mishna* established that one going out for a *mitzvah* has 2,000 *amot* from the new position — yet the conclusion seems to offer a blanket permission to go home regardless of distance. If one entered a new city in the course of the mission, one has the whole city plus 2,000 *amot* from its edge. But one is still defined by the new position. So which is it: 2,000 *amot*, or an unlimited return home?

### Rav Yehuda Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁחוֹזְרִין בִּכְלֵי זַיִין לִמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** What this means is **that they may return with** their **weapons to their** original **locations,** provided they are within two thousand cubits.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav answers: *shechozrin bichlei zayin limkoman* — the *mishna*'s statement is not primarily about the distance one may travel. It comes to teach an additional and distinct leniency: that those who went out to save may return *carrying their weapons*. The permission to carry arms on *Shabbat* is the novel ruling — not necessarily an extension of travel beyond 2,000 *amot*.

Why is carrying weapons permitted? This is not a case of *heter sofo mishum techilaso* — the end permitted on account of the beginning. Rather, as will be seen, the reason is that even once the battle appears to have concluded, depositing one's weapons immediately exposes one to renewed attack. Therefore one may remain armed until safely home, as a matter of *pikuach nefesh*.

One might ask: why does the *mishna* say *limkoman* — back to their place — implying travel all the way home? Practically speaking, the *halacha* as ruled is that when one goes out to fight the enemy, one may go all the way home, not merely 2,000 *amot*, because the entire situation is one of *pikuach nefesh*. The 2,000-*amat* limit applies to those who went out for other permissible purposes. The *Gemara* at this stage does not emphasize this point, but *l'halacha* that is how it is understood.

### Rosh Hashanah Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּילְמָא לְהַצִּיל שָׁאנֵי! אֶלָּא אִי קַשְׁיָא, הָא קַשְׁיָא: דִּתְנַן בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה לֹא הָיוּ זָזִין מִשָּׁם כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ. הִתְקִין רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. וְלֹא אֵלּוּ בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חֲכָמָה הַבָּאָה לְיַלֵּד, וְהַבָּא לְהַצִּיל מִן הַגַּיִיס וּמִן הַנָּהָר וּמִן הַמַּפּוֹלֶת וּמִן הַדְּלֵיקָה — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר, וְיֵשׁ לָהֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. וְתוּ לָא? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: כׇּל הַיּוֹצְאִים לְהַצִּיל חוֹזְרִין לִמְקוֹמָן — אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the difficulty** with returning home in this situation? **Perhaps** in the case where people went out **to** fight and **save** lives the law **is different,** and they are allowed to go home even if they went more than two thousand cubits beyond the limit. **Rather, if there is a difficulty, this is the difficulty: As we learned** in a mishna in tractate *Rosh HaShana*, **at first** they would take the witnesses who had come to Jerusalem from a distant place on Shabbat to testify that they had seen the new moon, and bring them into a special courtyard, **and they would not move from there the entire day.** This was in accordance with the law governing one who was permitted to go out beyond his limit, as once he fulfilled his mission, he was no longer permitted to move beyond four cubits. However, **Rabban Gamliel the Elder instituted that they** should **have two thousand cubits in each direction,** so that witnesses not refrain from coming to testify. **And** it is **not only these** whom the Sages **said** are given two thousand cubits in the place that they have reached, **but even a midwife who comes to deliver** a child, **and one who comes to rescue** Jews **from** an invasion of gentile **troops or from a river or a collapsed building or a fire; they are like the inhabitants of the town** at which they arrive, **and they have two thousand cubits in each direction.** The question may be raised: Are they given **no more** than two thousand cubits? **Didn't it say** in the mishna: **All who go out to save** lives **may return to their** original **locations** on Shabbat, which indicates that they may walk **even more** than two thousand cubits?

The *Gemara* then reconsiders: was there even a question to begin with? Perhaps *l'hatzil shani* — saving from attack is obviously different from other permitted departures, and so the blanket return makes self-evident sense without any contradiction. The *Gemara* replies: *ela ha kashya ha kashya* — it is a different difficulty that drove the question and its answer, namely the *mishna* in *Rosh Hashanah*.

That *mishna* recounts that originally, when witnesses came on *Shabbat* to testify about the new moon and went *chutz l'techum* to do so, they were confined to the courtyard where they had arrived — no special leniency yet existed for those who departed for a *mitzvah*. Rabban Gamliel the elder then enacted a *takana* that these witnesses receive 2,000 *amot* in every direction. The *mishna* then broadens this: *v'lo elu bilvad amru* — and they did not say this only for the witnesses, but also for a midwife coming to assist a birth, and for *ha'ba l'hatzil min hagayis umin hanahar umin hamapolet umin hadeika* — one who comes to save people from an attacking army, from a flooding river, from a collapsing building, or from a fire. All such individuals become like the inhabitants of the city they entered and receive 2,000 *amot* in every direction from its edge.

Now the difficulty becomes sharp: the *Rosh Hashanah mishna* explicitly includes saving from an army among the cases granted 2,000 *amot* — yet our *mishna* says those who go out to save may return home without any stated limit. So there are now two cases of *l'hatzil* — one capped at 2,000 *amot* and one apparently uncapped — and one cannot simply say *l'hatzil* is categorically different. This is the contradiction that produced the answer of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: the return-home clause speaks not to distance of travel but to carrying weapons.

### Weapons Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁחוֹזְרִין בִּכְלֵי זַיִין לִמְקוֹמָן, כִּדְתַנְיָא: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מַנִּיחִין כְּלֵי זֵיינָן בַּבַּיִת הַסָּמוּךְ לַחוֹמָה. פַּעַם אַחַת הִכִּירוּ בָּהֶן אוֹיְבִים וְרָדְפוּ אַחֲרֵיהֶם, וְנִכְנְסוּ לִיטּוֹל כְּלֵי זֵיינָן, וְנִכְנְסוּ אוֹיְבִים אַחֲרֵיהֶן. דָּחֲקוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה, וְהָרְגוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁהָרְגוּ אוֹיְבִים. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ חוֹזְרִין לִמְקוֹמָן בִּכְלֵי זֵיינָן.
>
> **Translation:** In response, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** We must not infer from the mishna that they may go home even if they went out more than two thousand cubits from their limit, but rather **that they may return with** their **weapons to their** original **locations,** provided that they are within two thousand cubits. **As it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **At first** those returning from a rescue mission **would place their weapons in** the first house that they encountered upon their return, i.e., **the house nearest the wall,** to avoid carrying on Shabbat any more than necessary. **Once,** their **enemies noticed that they** were no longer carrying their weapons, **and** they **chased after them; and** the defenders **entered** the house **to take up their weapons** and fight, **and** their **enemies entered after them,** causing great confusion. In the chaos, the defenders began to **push one another, and** they **killed more of each other than** their **enemies killed** of them. **At that time** the Sages **instituted that they should return to their locations,** i.e., their destinations, **with their weapons.**

The *Gemara* elaborates by citing a *braita*: *barishonah hayu manichin klei zaynam b'veit hasamuch lachomah* — originally they used to leave their weapons in a house near the city wall after repelling an attack. On one occasion, *hikiru vahen oyvim* — the enemy recognized that they had disarmed, chased them, and the soldiers rushed to retrieve their weapons from the armory. In the ensuing crush, *hargu zeh et zeh yoter mimah shehargu oyvim* — they killed one another more than the enemy had killed them. This episode has the quality of moral instruction — a recurring motif in rabbinic literature — that internal disorder can cause us more damage than the enemy.

*B'osa shaah hitkinu* — at that moment they enacted that soldiers may keep their weapons on them until they have returned safely to their homes. This is the *takana* referred to in the *mishna*'s ruling. According to the approach of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, one's permitted distance of travel remains 2,000 *amot*, but the carrying of weapons is sanctioned for the full journey home as a *pikuach nefesh* measure.

One additional observation about calling this a *takana*: there is merit to the point that framing the law as a public enactment serves the purpose of broadcasting the rule — which is itself important for the safety of those returning, as the enemy would know not to expect the soldiers to disarm prematurely.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא. כָּאן — שֶׁנִּצְּחוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, כָּאן — שֶׁנִּצְּחוּ אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם אֶת עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an alternate resolution that **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** in the mishna in *Rosh HaShana* where they only permitted two thousand cubits, it is referring to a situation **where the Jews defeated the nations of the world,** i.e., the gentiles, in battle; in such a case there is no concern and they need not return to their original locations. Whereas **here,** in the mishna which indicates that the Sages permitted even more than two thousand cubits, it is referring to a situation **where the nations of the world defeated themselves,** i.e., the Jews, whom the Gemara refers to euphemistically as themselves; in such a case the Sages allowed the defeated soldiers to return to their original locations.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an independent resolution to the question of distance — setting aside the weapons issue entirely. He holds: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *Kan shenitzchu Yisrael et umot haolam* — where the Jews won the battle, they have only 2,000 *amot*, since they are presumably in a position of strength, especially if permitted to remain armed. *Kan shenitzchu umot haolam* — when the non-Jews prevailed, the soldiers may travel all the way home, since remaining at a fixed point 2,000 *amot* from the city would leave them dangerously exposed. The need to return home in that case is itself a *pikuach nefesh* concern, and no distance limit applies.

## Siege & Pikuach Nefesh

### Siege Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: נׇכְרִים שֶׁצָּרוּ עַל עַיְירוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵין יוֹצְאִין עֲלֵיהֶם בִּכְלֵי זֵיינָן, וְאֵין מְחַלְּלִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי נׇכְרִים שֶׁצָּרוּ וְכוּ׳. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים, כְּשֶׁבָּאוּ עַל עִסְקֵי מָמוֹן, אֲבָל בָּאוּ עַל עִסְקֵי נְפָשׁוֹת — יוֹצְאִין עֲלֵיהֶן בִּכְלֵי זֵיינָן, וּמְחַלְּלִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara discussed war on Shabbat, the Gemara cites **Rav Yehuda,** who **said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **gentiles who besieged Jewish towns, they may not go out** to fight **against them with their weapons, nor may they desecrate Shabbat** in any other way **due to them,** but rather they must wait until after Shabbat. **That was also taught** in a *baraita*, with a caveat: With regard to **gentiles who besieged, etc. In what** case **is this said?** It is said in a case **where** the gentiles **came** and besieged the town **with regard to monetary matters,** i.e., banditry. **However, if they came with regard to lives,** i.e., there is concern that the gentiles will attack, **they may go out against them with their weapons, and they may desecrate Shabbat due to them.**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav teaches: *nochrim shetzaru al ayarot Yisrael* — if non-Jews lay siege to Jewish towns, *ein yotzim aleihem bichlei zaynam v'ein mechalelin aleihem et haShabbat* — one may not go out to fight them with weapons or violate *Shabbat* on their account. This is a striking ruling, since one might assume any siege implicates *pikuach nefesh*.

A *braita* explains: *bameh devarim amurim* — when does this apply? *Sheba'u al iskei mamon* — when the siege concerns monetary matters, such as a dispute over tribute. In that case, one does not violate *Shabbat*; the remedy is to pay. But *ba'u al iskei nefashot* — if they have come for matters of life, where actual killing is intended, *yotzim aleihem bichlei zayin umechalelin aleihem et haShabbat* — one must fight and may violate *Shabbat*.

It is worth noting that the earliest documented instance of *Torah Sheba'al Peh* — of applying reasoned legal analysis to yield a novel practical ruling — appears to be the episode in *Sefer Chashmonaim* where soldiers, after being slaughtered because they refused to fight on *Shabbat*, concluded that God intends for them to fight back in self-defense. The principle that *pikuach nefesh docheh Shabbat* and that one may fight the enemy on *Shabbat* traces its earliest narrative expression there.

### Border Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָעִיר הַסְּמוּכָה לַסְּפָר, אֲפִילּוּ לֹא בָּאוּ עַל עִסְקֵי נְפָשׁוֹת אֶלָּא עַל עִסְקֵי תֶּבֶן וָקַשׁ — יוֹצְאִין עֲלֵיהֶן בִּכְלֵי זֵיינָן, וּמְחַלְּלִין עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וּבָבֶל, כָּעִיר הַסְּמוּכָה לַסְּפָר דָּמְיָא. וְתַרְגּוּמָא: נְהַרְדְּעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to a town that is** located **near the border, even if** the gentiles **did not come with regard to lives, but** rather **with regard to matters of hay and straw,** i.e., to raid and spoil the town, **they may go out against them with their weapons, and they may desecrate Shabbat due to them,** as the border must be carefully guarded, in order to prevent enemies from gaining a foothold there. **Rav Yosef bar Manyumi said** that **Rav Naḥman said: And Babylonia is considered like a town** located **near the border,** and war may be waged there on Shabbat even if the gentiles came for financial gain. **And this means** the city of **Neharde'a,** which was located near the border.

There is a further and more expansive rule: *uve'ir hasmucha lasefar* — in a city near the border, *afilu lo ba'u al iskei nefashot* — even if the attackers came only over monetary matters, such as straw and stubble, *yotzim aleihem bichlei zayin umechalelin aleihem et haShabbat* — one may go out armed and violate *Shabbat*. A border city under siege is treated as a situation of *pikuach nefesh* in its own right, because allowing a border city to be overrun — even over a property dispute — poses an existential threat.

Rav Yosef bar Minyomi in the name of Rav Nachman states: *uveBavel ke'ir hasmucha lasefar damya* — Babylonia as a whole is treated as a city near the border. The *Gemara* clarifies that what is meant is *Nehardea* specifically, which was on the border of the Persian Empire and was in fact overrun by the Romans on multiple occasions. The mention of Nehardea thus serves as a concrete illustration of a border city in the relevant geopolitical context — the frontier between Rome and Persia.

This raises a genuinely interesting question: is the *Gemara* presenting this as a theoretical illustration, or is it ruling practically that Jews living in Nehardea would be obligated to defend the Persian frontier against Rome? There are documented cases of Jews serving in foreign armies, sometimes by conscription and sometimes by choice. If a Jew were a citizen of Nehardea during an attack, it is a real question whether the *Gemara* intends to say he could take up arms in defense of the Persian Empire on *Shabbat* — not merely as a matter of protecting his own life, but as a participant in the broader defense of the city.

### David & Keilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי דְּמִן בֵּירֵי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּגִּידוּ לְדָוִד לֵאמֹר הִנֵּה פְלִשְׁתִּים נִלְחָמִים בִּקְעִילָה וְהֵמָּה שׁוֹסִים אֶת הַגֳּרָנוֹת״. תָּנָא: קְעִילָה עִיר הַסְּמוּכָה לַסְּפָר הָיְתָה, וְהֵם לֹא בָּאוּ אֶלָּא עַל עִסְקֵי תֶּבֶן וָקַשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֵמָּה שׁוֹסִים אֶת הַגֳּרָנוֹת״. וּכְתִיב ״וַיִּשְׁאַל דָּוִד בַּה׳ לֵאמֹר הַאֵלֵךְ וְהִכֵּיתִי בַּפְּלִשְׁתִּים הָאֵלֶּה וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל דָּוִד לֵךְ וְהִכִּיתָ בַפְּלִשְׁתִּים וְהוֹשַׁעְתָּ אֶת קְעִילָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Dostai** of the town **of Biri expounded: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "And they told David, saying: Behold, the Philistines are fighting against Ke'ila, and they rob the threshing floors"** (i Samuel 23:1), after which David asked God how he should respond. **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Ke'ila was a town** located **near the border,** and the Philistines **came only with regard to matters of hay and straw, as it is written: "And they rob the threshing floors." And** in the next verse **it is written: "Therefore David inquired of the Lord, saying: Shall I go and smite these Philistines? And the Lord said to David: Go and smite the Philistines, and save Ke'ila"** (i Samuel 23:2), which indicates that war may be waged in a border town on Shabbat, even with regard to monetary matters.

Rav Dostai from Biri expounds the verse in Shmuel 1, chapter 23: *Vayagidu l'David lemor, Hinei Plishtim nilchamim b'Keilah v'heima shosim et hagranot* — they told David that the Philistines were fighting in Keilah and attacking the granaries. A *braita* teaches that Keilah was a city near the border — *ir hasmucha lasefar* — and the Philistines came only over monetary matters, *iskei teven v'kash*, straw and stubble. This aligns precisely with the rule about border cities, where even a monetary dispute triggers the obligation to fight on *Shabbat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָמִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אִילֵּימָא אִי שְׁרֵי אִי אָסוּר — הֲרֵי בֵּית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שְׁמוּאֵל הָרָמָתִי קַיָּים. אֶלָּא: אִי מַצְלַח אִי לָא מַצְלַח. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֵךְ וְהִכִּיתָ בַפְּלִשְׁתִּים וְהוֹשַׁעְתָּ אֶת קְעִילָה״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof by asking: **What is** David's **dilemma? If you say** that he had a halakhic question and was in doubt **whether it was permitted or prohibited** to fight the Philistines on Shabbat, it is possible to respond: **But the court of Samuel from Rama was** then **in existence,** and rather than inquire by way of the *Urim VeTummim* he should have inquired of the Great Sanhedrin. **Rather,** he asked: **Will he succeed or will he not succeed** in his war? The Gemara comments: **This is also precise** in the language of the verse, **as it is written** in the response to David's query: **"Go and smite the Philistines, and save Ke'ila." Learn from this,** from the assurance that God gave David of his victory, that this was the subject of his inquiry.

David then asks God — through the *Urim v'Tumim* — whether to go and strike the Philistines. The *Gemara* pauses: what exactly was David asking? If the question was whether it was permitted or forbidden — a *halachic* question about *Shabbat* — he had no need to consult the *Urim v'Tumim*, for *Beit dino shel Shmuel ha'Ramati kayam* — the court of Shmuel the Ramathite was active. One does not ask *halachic* questions of the *Urim v'Tumim*. Rather, *i mazlach i lo mazlach* — he was asking whether he would succeed. This is confirmed by the divine response: *v'hoshat et Keilah* — you will save Keilah — which speaks to outcome, not to legal permissibility. The *Gemara* notes that this interpretation means the Keilah episode does not directly prove the rule about border cities on *Shabbat*, though the *braita* itself establishes that rule independently.

## Sleeping Traveler

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁיָּשַׁב בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְעָמַד וְרָאָה הֲרֵי (זֶה) הוּא סָמוּךְ לָעִיר, [הוֹאִיל] וְלֹא הָיְתָה כַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לְכָךְ — לֹא יִכָּנֵס. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יִכָּנֵס. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה הָיָה וְנִכְנַס רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן בְּלֹא מִתְכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a person who was sitting along the road** on Shabbat eve toward nightfall, unaware that he was within the city's Shabbat limit, **and** when **he stood up** after Shabbat had already commenced, **he saw that he was near the town,** i.e., within its limit, **since he had not intended** to acquire his place of residence in the town, **he may not enter** it, but rather he measures two thousand cubits from his place; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** **Rabbi Yehuda says: He may enter** the town. **Rabbi Yehuda said: It once happened that Rabbi Tarfon entered** a town on Shabbat **without intention** from the beginning of Shabbat to establish residence in the city.

The next *mishna* introduces a distinct category: *mi she'yashan baderech* — one who was traveling and fell asleep before *Shabbat* began, encamping wherever one happened to be. Upon waking — presumably the next morning — one sees that one is near a city. *Harei hu samuch l'ir* — the city was within 2,000 *amot* of where one slept. *Ho'il v'lo haita kavanato l'kach* — since when *Shabbat* began, one was not intending to join that city, indeed was entirely unaware of its proximity, *lo yikanes* — one is not treated as an inhabitant of that city. One may travel toward the city but only up to one's 2,000-*amah* limit. If one camped 1,999 *amot* from the city's edge, one can enter one *amah* into it and no further.

This ruling requires appreciation of the underlying leniency that is being withheld. Ordinarily, if one begins *Shabbat* inside a city, the entire city is treated as a single unit and does not count against one's 2,000 *amot*. A further leniency allows that even if one is outside the city but within 2,000 *amot* of it, one may be treated as an inhabitant of the city — one's notional starting point is extended to the city itself. This is an unusual concession, since ordinarily one's *techum* begins from where one physically stands, not from a separate location one did not occupy. The *mishna* is debating whether this secondary leniency requires at minimum that one was aware of and intending toward the city when *Shabbat* began.

### Rabbi Meir & Yehuda

Rabbi Meir holds that without the requisite *kavanah* — intention or awareness — one is not considered to have begun *Shabbat* in the city, and one has only one's 2,000 *amot* from the point of encampment. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and holds that one may enter the city as if one had begun *Shabbat* there.

### Rabbi Tarfon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן שֶׁהָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְחָשְׁכָה לוֹ, וְלָן חוּץ לָעִיר. לְשַׁחֲרִית מְצָאוּהוּ רוֹעֵי בָקָר, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: רַבִּי, הֲרֵי הָעִיר לְפָנֶיךָ, הִכָּנֵס! נִכְנַס וְיָשַׁב בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, וְדָרַשׁ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ. (אָמְרוּ לוֹ): מִשָּׁם רְאָיָיה? שֶׁמָּא בְּלִבּוֹ הָיְתָה, אוֹ בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ מוּבְלָע בְּתוֹךְ תְּחוּמוֹ הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: It once happened that Rabbi Tarfon was walking along the way** on Shabbat eve, **and night fell upon him, and he spent the night outside the town. In the morning, cowherds** who came to graze their cattle outside the town **found him** and **said to him: Master, the town is before you; enter. He entered and sat in the study hall and taught the entire day.** This indicates that one is permitted to enter. The other Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yehuda: Do you bring **proof from there? Perhaps he had it in mind** the day before to acquire residence in the city, **or** perhaps **the study hall was subsumed within his** Shabbat **limit.** If the study hall was within two thousand cubits of the spot where he established residence, all agree that he may enter there.

Rabbi Meir dismisses this proof on two grounds. First: *shema b'libo hayta* — perhaps Rabbi Tarfon was not truly unaware; just because the cowherds told him does not mean he had no prior knowledge of the city's location. Second: *beit hamedrash muvla b'toch tchomav hayah* — perhaps the *beit medrash* itself was within his personal 2,000 *amot*, and he simply traveled there without invoking the city-inhabitant status at all. So the story proves nothing about the broader principle.

### Sleep Onset

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁיָּשַׁן בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְלָא יָדַע שֶׁחָשֵׁיכָה — יֵשׁ לוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: וְהוּא בְּאֶמְצָעָן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לְאֵיזֶה רוּחַ שֶׁיִּרְצֶה יֵלֵךְ. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁאִם בֵּירַר לוֹ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was sleeping along the road** on Shabbat eve **and did not know that night had fallen, he has two thousand cubits in each direction;** this is the statement of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** who maintains that knowledge and awareness are not necessary for one to acquire residence, but rather, a person's presence in a given location establishes residence there. **But the Rabbis say: He has only four cubits,** as since he did not knowingly acquire residence, he did not establish a Shabbat limit. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** He has only four cubits total **and he is in the middle of them,** i.e., he has two cubits in each direction. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He may walk** four cubits **in any direction he wishes. But Rabbi Yehuda agrees that if he selected for himself** the direction in which he wants to walk those four cubits, **he cannot retract** and walk four cubits in a different direction.

A related but distinct case concerns one who sat down on the road and *lo yada shechashchah* — did not know that *Shabbat* was beginning, falling asleep before dark and remaining unconscious when *Shabbat* commenced. This goes beyond the prior question. The earlier debate concerned whether awareness of the city matters; this debate concerns whether consciousness at the moment *Shabbat* begins is necessary at all to establish one's *techum*.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds: *yesh lo alpayim amah l'chol ruach* — one has 2,000 *amot* in every direction from where one slept. Even without consciousness at the moment *Shabbat* began, one's physical presence at a location is sufficient to establish it as one's *makom* — one's place — for *techum* purposes.

The *chachamim* disagree: *ein lo ela arba amot* — one has only four *amot*. The *chachamim* apparently hold that some measure of conscious establishment of place is required, and an unconscious person has not done so.

The sub-disputes of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehuda about *how* to measure the four *amot* apply here as they do elsewhere. Rabbi Eliezer holds one stands at the center, so two *amot* extend in each direction. Rabbi Yehuda holds one may choose in which direction to deploy the four *amot*, though once one has committed to a direction one cannot retract.

## Multiple People

### Two People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם, מִקְצָת אַמּוֹתָיו שֶׁל זֶה בְּתוֹךְ אַמּוֹתָיו שֶׁל זֶה — מְבִיאִין וְאוֹכְלִין בָּאֶמְצַע, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יוֹצִיא זֶה מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ לְתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where **there were two** people in this situation, positioned in such a way that **part** of **the** four **cubits of one were subsumed within the** four **cubits of the other, they may** each **bring** food **and eat** together in the shared area **in the middle,** **provided that the one does not carry** anything **from his** four-cubit limit **into that of his fellow.**

A further case: if two people found themselves *chutz l'techum*, each with only four *amot*, and the four *amot* of one overlapped with the four *amot* of the other — *miktzat amotav shel zeh b'toch amotav shel zeh* — they may bring food and eat together in the shared middle space. However, *uvlevad shelo yotzi zeh mishelo l'toch shel chaveiro* — neither may bring objects from the portion of space that is exclusively one's own into the other's space. The reason is that one's vessels and possessions are also limited by one's *techum*, and carrying them from one's exclusive zone into the other's exclusive zone would take them outside their *techum*. The shared, overlapping zone is available to both; one's exclusive zone is not.

### Three People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה וְהָאֶמְצָעִי מוּבְלָע בֵּינֵיהֶן — הוּא מוּתָּר עִמָּהֶן, וְהֵן מוּתָּרִין עִמּוֹ, וּשְׁנַיִם הַחִיצוֹנִים אֲסוּרִין זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where there **were three** people in this situation, **and** certain parts of the four cubits of **the middle one were subsumed within** the respective limits of each of **the others,** so that he shared a certain area with each of them, **he is permitted** to eat **with** either of **them, and they are** both **permitted** to eat **with him; but the two outer ones are forbidden** to eat **with each other,** since they share no common area.

When there are three such people, with the middle person overlapping with both neighbors but the two outer persons not overlapping with each other: *ha-emtzai muvla benehem* — the middle one is swallowed between them. He may eat with either of the two outer persons; they may eat with him. But *shnayim hachitzonim asurim ze im ze* — the two outer persons may not share with each other, as they have no overlapping space.

### Three Courtyards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — לְשָׁלֹשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ, וּפְתוּחוֹת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. עֵירְבוּ שְׁתַּיִם עִם הָאֶמְצָעִית — הִיא מוּתֶּרֶת עִמָּהֶן וְהֵם מוּתָּרוֹת עִמָּהּ, וּשְׁתַּיִם הַחִיצוֹנוֹת אֲסוּרוֹת זוֹ עִם זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: To what is this comparable?** It is like **three courtyards that open into one another, and** also **open into a public domain.** If **the two outer** courtyards **established an *eiruv* with the middle one,** the middle one **is permitted to** carry to the two outer ones, **and they are permitted to** carry to **it, but the two outer** courtyards **are prohibited** to carry from **one to** the **other,** as they did not establish an *eiruv* with one another.

Rabbi Shimon illustrates this with an analogy to three courtyards, each opening both into the others and into the *reshut harabbim*. If two of the courtyards each made a joint *eruv* with the middle courtyard — but not with each other — the middle courtyard may carry with either neighbor, and each neighbor may carry with the middle, but the two outer courtyards may not carry between themselves, as they made no *eruv* with each other.

The reason it matters that each courtyard has an opening to the *reshut harabbim* is significant: if the only access to an inner courtyard were through an outer one, it would compel all three to make a single combined *eruv*, since independence would be impossible. Where each has independent street access, they can each act independently in their *eruv* arrangements, producing this scenario of partial mutual permission.

## Rava's Question

### Cheftzei Hefker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: מַאי קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי? מִסְבָּר קָא סָבַר: חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה. וּבְדִין הוּא דְּלִיפְלוֹג בְּכֵלִים. וְהָא דְּקָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּאָדָם — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כּוֹחָן דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: הוֹאִיל וְנֵיעוֹר קָנָה, יָשֵׁן נָמֵי קָנָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא, קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי בְּעָלְמָא: חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר אֵין קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: הוֹאִיל וְנֵיעוֹר קָנָה, יָשֵׁן נָמֵי קָנָה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava raised a dilemma: What does Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri hold?** Does **he hold** that **ownerless objects acquire residence** for Shabbat, i.e., even an article that does not belong to anyone acquires residence at the onset of Shabbat and can therefore be carried two thousand cubits in each direction? **And** according to this understanding, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri **should by right have disagreed** with the Rabbis even **about utensils** that were left in the field, i.e., that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, ownerless utensils can be moved two thousand cubits in each direction. **And the reason that they disagreed about a person** is **to convey the far-reaching** nature of the stringent ruling **of the Rabbis, that although there is** room **to say: Since** a person who is **awake acquires** for himself two thousand cubits, **he also acquires** them if he is **sleeping,** the mishna nonetheless **teaches us that** the Rabbis did **not** accept this argument, and this is why the dispute is taught specifically with respect to a person. **Or perhaps** we should understand his position differently, that **in general Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **ownerless objects do not acquire residence** of their own. **But here,** with regard to a person, **the reason is as follows: Since** a person who is **awake acquires** for himself two thousand cubits, **he also acquires** them if he is **sleeping.**

Rava poses the foundational question about Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position: *mai kasavar Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri?* What is the conceptual basis for holding that a sleeping person establishes a *techum* where he lies? There are two possible ways to understand him.

The first possibility: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds *cheftzei hefker konim shevita* — that even ownerless, inanimate objects establish their *shevita* — their resting place for *Shabbat* — wherever they happen to be when *Shabbat* begins. No consciousness is required; physical presence at a location suffices. Under this view, the *Gemara* notes, *uvedin hu deliflu bekelim* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri could just as well have stated his position by the case of inanimate vessels. He chose to debate it by a sleeping person in order to *lehodia'acha kochan derabbanan* — to reveal the far-reaching position of the Rabbis: that even a sleeping person — who after all *could* have been awake and consciously established his place — does not, in the Rabbis' view, establish his *techum* where he sleeps. Conversely, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds it works even for ownerless objects.

It should be noted that by inanimate objects the question of *koneh shevita* is a *chumra*: if an ownerless object establishes its own *techum*, one is limited in how far one can carry it. If it does not, one's possession of it on *Shabbat* causes it to follow one's own *techum* — which is how one wants it defined, since one can take it wherever one goes.

The second possibility: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds *cheftzei hefker ein konim shevita* — ownerless objects do not establish their own *techum*. Consciousness is in principle required. Nevertheless, a sleeping person is different: *ho'il venir'or kanei, yashen nami kanei* — since had he been awake he would clearly have established this as his place, even while asleep it is considered established. Under this reading, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri requires at least potential intention — the capacity for consciousness — and a sleeping person satisfies that condition in a way an inanimate object does not.

The *Gemara* thus leaves open which of these two rationales underlies Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position.

### Rain Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: גְּשָׁמִים שֶׁיָּרְדוּ מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב — יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. בְּיוֹם טוֹב — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּרַגְלֵי כׇּל אָדָם. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: ״חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה״, הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הִיא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: ״חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר אֵין קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה״, הָא מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְלֹא רַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** a solution to this dilemma from the following *baraita*: **Rain that fell on the eve of a Festival has two thousand cubits in each direction,** meaning that one is permitted to carry the rainwater within a radius of two thousand cubits. But if the rain fell **on the Festival** itself, **it is like the feet of all people,** as it did not acquire residence, and consequently one is permitted to carry this water wherever he is permitted to walk. **Granted,** it is understandable **if you say that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **ownerless objects acquire residence;** in accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** *baraita*? **It is** that of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** ben Nuri, and consequently the rain that fell on the eve of the Festival acquired residence in the spot where it fell. **However, if you say** that he maintains that **ownerless objects do not acquire residence,** in accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** *baraita*? **Neither** that of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** ben Nuri, **nor** that of **the Rabbis,** as it clearly indicates that rain acquires a place of residence even though it has no owner. Rather, we must say that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri is of the opinion that ownerless objects acquire residence, and this *baraita* is in accordance with his opinion.

Rav Yosef attempts to resolve the question. He cites the case of rain: *geshamim sheyardu me'erev Yom Tov* — rain that fell on *erev Yom Tov*, before *Yom Tov* commenced. The ruling is *yesh lahem alpayim amah l'chol ruach* — the rain water has 2,000 *amot* from wherever it fell. Since the water was physically present at that location when *Yom Tov* began, it is defined by that location. By contrast, rain that fell *b'Yom Tov* itself — meaning it did not exist at any location when *Yom Tov* commenced — *harei hen k'raglei kol adam*, it follows the person who takes possession of it.

This appears to be direct evidence that an inanimate, ownerless substance — rain water — is *koneh shevita* wherever it lies when *Yom Tov* begins, supporting the first reading of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב אַבָּיֵי וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סָפְרָא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְדִילְמָא בִּגְשָׁמִים הַסְּמוּכִין לָעִיר עָסְקִינַן, וְאַנְשֵׁי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר דַּעְתָּם עִילַּיְיהוּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּתְנַן: בּוֹר שֶׁל יָחִיד — כְּרַגְלֵי יָחִיד, וְשֶׁל אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר — כְּרַגְלֵי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר, וְשֶׁל עוֹלֵי בָּבֶל — כְּרַגְלֵי הַמְמַלֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye sat and recited this tradition. Rav Safra said to Abaye: Perhaps we are dealing with rain** that fell **near a city, and the inhabitants of that city had it in mind,** and that is why it acquires two thousand cubits in each direction. Abaye **said to him: It should not enter your mind** that such an understanding is correct, **as we learned** in a mishna: **A cistern that belongs to an individual,** its water is **like the feet of that individual,** the owner of the cistern, in that it may be carried wherever he is permitted to walk. **And** a cistern **that belongs to a particular city,** its water is **like the feet of** the people of **that city,** in that it may be carried wherever the inhabitants of that city may walk, i.e., two thousand cubits in each direction from the city. **And** a cistern **that belongs to pilgrims from Babylonia** on the way to Eretz Yisrael, meaning that it belongs to all Jews and has no particular owner, its water is **like the feet of the one who draws** the water, in that it may be carried wherever he is permitted to walk.

Rav Safra challenges: *dilma begeshamim hasmuchim l'ir askinan* — perhaps the case concerns rain falling near a city whose inhabitants knew it was about to rain and had it in mind. Under that reading, the rain is not truly ownerless *hefker* — the people's anticipatory awareness effectively constitutes a quasi-intentional designation of where it fell. The rain therefore goes by where they expected it to fall, not because inanimate *hefker* establishes its own *techum*. One cannot extract a principle about *cheftzei hefker* from a case where human intention was implicitly present.

### Wells Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: בּוֹר שֶׁל שְׁבָטִים — יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הָא — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, הָא — רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught** in a *baraita*: **A cistern that belongs to** one of **the tribes** and has no particular owner, its water **has two thousand cubits in each direction.** If so, these two sources **contradict each other,** as the mishna teaches that water that belongs to the entire community does not establish residence, whereas the *tanna* of the *baraita* holds that it may be carried two thousand cubits from its place. **Rather,** in order to resolve the contradiction, **learn from here: This** source, which states that the water may be carried two thousand cubits, was taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** who says that even ownerless objects acquire residence; **that** source, which states that water that does not belong to any particular person is like the feet of the one who draws it, was taught in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who say that ownerless objects do not acquire residence.

The *Gemara* dismisses this objection and adduces clearer evidence from a *braita* about wells. A privately owned well — *bor shel yachid* — goes by the *techum* of its owner. A well owned by an entire city — *shel osa ha'ir* — goes by the *techum* of the city as a whole, even if individual inhabitants made their own private *eiruvi techumin* extending in different directions. So far there is no novelty.

The instructive case is *shel olei Bavel* — a well belonging to the travelers, meaning an ownerless public well on the highway used by anyone passing. The ruling is *k'raglei hamemalei* — it goes by the person who draws from it. This indicates that an ownerless object does *not* establish its own *techum*; it follows the one who takes possession.

But a second *braita* presents the case of *bor shel Shvatim* — a well owned by the *Shvatim*, meaning by all of *Klal Yisrael*. Although *Klal Yisrael* does own this well, the ownership is so diffuse — all of Israel has no single *techum* — that it is effectively equivalent to *hefker*. Yet the ruling here is *yesh lahem alpayim amah l'chol ruach* — it has 2,000 *amot* from the well itself. This well, despite its effectively ownerless status, *does* establish its own *techum*.

### Mapping Debate

These two *braitot* contradict each other directly on the question of ownerless objects. One holds *cheftzei hefker ein konim shevita* — they do not establish their own *techum*, and the *olei Bavel* well follows whoever draws from it. The other holds *cheftzei hefker konim shevita* — the *Shvatim* well establishes its own 2,000-*amah* radius. The *Gemara* concludes: *ela shma mina tannaim hi* — this is necessarily a dispute among *Tannaim*. *Ha Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, ha Rabbanan* — one *braita* follows Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position, and the other follows the *chachamim*. The debate about the wells maps precisely onto the debate of our *mishna* concerning the sleeping traveler.

### Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי קָאָמַר רַב סָפְרָא, וְהָכִי אַהְדַּרִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאַמַּאי לָא תֵּימָא לֵיהּ מִגּוּפַהּ? אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ גְּשָׁמִים הַסְּמוּכִין לָעִיר עָסְקִינַן — הַאי ״יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ״?! הָא ״כְּרַגְלֵי אַנְשֵׁי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר״ מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **when** Abaye **came before Rav Yosef, he said to him: This is what Rav Safra said, and this is what I answered him.** Rav Yosef **said to him: And why did you not answer him from the** *baraita* **itself? If it should enter your mind that we are dealing with rain** that fell **near a city,** how can you understand the statement **that** the rainwater **has two thousand cubits in each direction?** According to your understanding, that the rainwater may be carried two thousand cubits because the inhabitants of the town had it in mind, the *baraita* **should have** said: The rainwater is **like the feet of the inhabitants of that city.** Rather, you must say that the inhabitants of the city did not acquire the water and that it may be carried within a radius of two thousand cubits, because the *baraita* was taught in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, that ownerless objects acquire residence.

Abaye reported this entire exchange to Rav Yosef — including Rav Safra's attempt to dismiss the rain case by invoking the city-dwellers' anticipatory awareness, and Abaye's counter that the wells *braita* provides independent Tannaitic evidence of the dispute. Rav Yosef then points out that one could have refuted Rav Safra's objection from the rain case itself, without needing to invoke the wells at all. If the reason the rain establishes its *techum* from where it fell were only because the city's inhabitants had it in mind, then the *braita* should have stated *k'raglei anshei osa ha'ir* — it goes by the *techum* of the people of the city, since it would effectively be as if they placed it there. Instead, the *braita* says it goes 2,000 *amot* from where the rain fell — a point that would only make sense if the rain itself is *koneh shevita* at the location of its fall, independent of any human intention. Rav Safra's explanation thus collapses from within the rain case before one even reaches the wells.

The upshot of the entire discussion is that there is Tannaitic evidence for both positions. There are *Tannaim* who hold *cheftzei hefker konim shevita* — as expressed in the *Shvatim* well and arguably in the rain case — and *Tannaim* who hold *cheftzei hefker ein konim shevita* — as expressed in the *olei Bavel* well. The *Gemara* maps this dispute onto the debate between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the *chachamim* about whether a sleeping person establishes a *techum*, suggesting that the deeper question is not specific to human consciousness but concerns the general principle of whether physical presence at a location — without active awareness or intention — suffices to establish *shevita* there. The discussion will continue on the following *daf*.
