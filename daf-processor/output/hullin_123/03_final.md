# Hullin 123 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Processing Skins

### Skins Distinct from Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁעִיבְּדָן [וְכוּ׳]. הִילֵּךְ אִין, לֹא הִילֵּךְ לָא? וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אוֹזֶן חֲמוֹר שֶׁטְּלָאָהּ לְקוּפָּתוֹ טְהוֹרָה, טְלָאָהּ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הִילֵּךְ! לֹא: טְלָאָהּ, הִילֵּךְ – אִין, לֹא הִילֵּךְ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** And all of these skins, in a case where one tanned them or spread them on the ground and trod upon them, are no longer classified as flesh and are ritually pure. The Gemara objects: The mishna indicates that if one trod upon them they are no longer classified as flesh, but if one did not tread upon them they do not cease being classified as flesh. But doesn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach: The ear of a donkey that one sewed into his basket is pure and is no longer classified as flesh. Just as the ear is no longer classified as flesh once it is sewed into a basket, so too skin that is spread on the ground, even if one did not tread upon it, should no longer be classified as flesh. The Gemara explains: No, this is not difficult. Sewing the ear is an action that nullifies the ear's classification as flesh. But spreading skin on the ground is not an action that nullifies the skin's classification as flesh unless one trod upon the skin. Therefore, if one trod upon the skin it is no longer classified as flesh, but if one did not tread upon it, it does not cease being classified as flesh.

The daf opens at the very bottom of 122b, with the *mishna*'s discussion of skins that are considered functionally distinct from meat. Certain animal skins — such as those of lizards and similar creatures, or chicken skin — are ordinarily treated as meat-like, and therefore carry the status of food with respect to the laws of *neveilah* and *tumas ochlin*. The *mishna* establishes that once such skins are processed — tanned, worked into leather or parchment — they lose that meat-like status entirely.

### Walking on Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה כְּדֵי עִבּוּד? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אַרְבַּעַת מִילִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that the skin must be trodden upon for the period required for tanning. The Gemara clarifies: How long is the period required for tanning? Rav Huna says that Rabbi Yannai says: The time which it takes one to walk four *mil*.

The *gemara* immediately focuses on one specific processing method: walking on the skin. It states *hilach in, lo hilach lo* — only if you walked on it does it lose its meat-like status; if you did not walk on it, the change in status has not yet occurred. The *gemara* then raises a challenge from a teaching of Rabbi Chiyya: if one takes the ear of a donkey and uses it as a cover or mat for one's box, that leather becomes *tahor* — it is no longer considered meat-like and can no longer receive *tumah*. This seems to suggest that actual use, rather than processing by walking, is what matters.

### Leather as Mat

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing two independent pathways. If one actually uses the leather in a particular capacity — for instance, spreading it over a box as a cover — then even without having walked on it, it exits the status of meat. Conversely, if one has not yet used it in such a way but has processed it by walking on it, that too suffices. Either processing or use, independently, is sufficient to transform the leather's status. It should be noted that while leather can in principle become *tamei* if fashioned into a receptacle, in these cases it would not.

## Four Mil Discussion

### Amount of Walking

How much walking is required to constitute sufficient processing? Rav Huna in the name of Rav Yannai states: *arba milin* — four *mil*. One *mil* takes approximately eighteen minutes to walk; four *mil* therefore corresponds to roughly seventy-two minutes of walking. This does not mean the piece of leather must be four *mil* long, but rather that seventy-two minutes' worth of walking upon it would constitute adequate processing, such that it is no longer considered meat but leather.

### Kneading Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ מִשּׁוּם דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לְגַבָּל. וְלִתְפִלָּה וְלִנְטִילַת יָדַיִם – אַרְבַּעַת מִילִין.
>
> **Translation:** Since the period of time it takes to walk four *mil* was mentioned, the Gemara lists *halakhot* that employ this period of time. Rabbi Abbahu says in the name of Reish Lakish: With regard to a professional kneader who is careful to maintain the ritual purity of the dough that he kneads for others, he must walk up to four *mil* in order to purify the vessel he is using by immersing it in a ritual bath. He is not required to walk farther than this unless the person hiring him pays for him to do so. And similarly, with regard to prayer, one who is traveling may not pray where he is if there is a synagogue within four *mil* ahead of him, but rather must continue traveling in order to pray in the synagogue. And similarly, with regard to washing one's hands before eating, one who is traveling may not eat without washing his hands if there is water within four *mil* ahead of him.

This leads to a broader digression on other *halachic* contexts in which the measure of four *mil* appears. Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Reish Lakish states that four *mil* is the operative distance for three situations: *legabel* (kneading dough), prayer (*litfillah*), and washing hands (*linetilat yadayim*). Rashi explains the first case as follows: a laborer has been hired to knead dough *betahara*, and in order to do so properly he must immerse the vessels; if obtaining water for that purpose requires traveling more than four *mil*, he is not obligated to do so. Tosafos objects to this reading, noting that the other examples in the *gemara* concern a traveler, and it seems incongruous to allow the food to be prepared in a state of *tumah* merely because the distance is a burden, even in the case of a hired worker.

Tosafos therefore offers an alternative: one is traveling and normally eats one's bread *betahara* — that is, not merely with washed hands, but observing the full practice of *chullin betahara* — and the opportunity to obtain suitable *tahor* bread can be reached by continuing the journey. If that destination is within four *mil*, one should wait. If it is more than four *mil* away, one need not maintain the *betahara* practice for this meal.

### Prayer & Minyan

Regarding prayer, Rashi explains that the question is whether to wait and daven in a *shul*. Tosafos refines this: the issue is specifically about davening with a *minyan*. If a *minyan* is reachable within four *mil*, one should wait rather than daven alone. If it is more than four *mil* away, one may proceed to daven without one. This is better understood as a question of what is preferable and ideal, rather than a strict personal obligation — *minyan* is primarily a communal obligation rather than an individual one — but where it can be reached within that distance, it is the recommended course.

A more commonly applicable reading of the *tfilah* case ties it to the question of available water, which runs as a unifying theme through all three situations. If one needs to wash one's hands before davening and water is unavailable, and water can be reached within four *mil*, one should wait for it. If not, one may clean one's hands by other means — on wood, stone, or the like — and daven without water.

### Netilat Yadayim

The case of *netilat yadayim* is perhaps the most practically relevant. One is traveling, has packed food, and is hungry but has no water. Must one wait to wash hands before eating bread? The rule states: if water is reachable within four *mil*, one waits. If not, one need not wait, and may eat the bread wrapped so as not to touch it directly, thereby forgoing *netilat yadayim* altogether. This is noteworthy because when we studied *netilat yadayim* we learned that wrapping alone is not ordinarily a solution — even wrapped bread normally requires *netilat yadayim*. Here, however, where water is not readily available and waiting would require more than four *mil*, the *halacha* permits this alternative. This is a highly practical ruling applicable whenever one is traveling without access to water.

### Time vs. Distance

This raises an important question: is four *mil* measured as distance or as time? Four *mil* on foot corresponds to seventy-two minutes. On a donkey, the same distance takes less time. In a car, it might take only a few minutes. If the measure is purely distance, then even a rest stop thirty-two miles away would theoretically require waiting — which could be only minutes by car. It would seem more straightforward to measure by time, since the question is fundamentally about how long one must wait, not how far one must travel. According to this understanding, if water can be reached within approximately seventy-two minutes, one should wait; if not, one may employ the permitted alternatives. This question — time versus distance — is not resolved here, and while it is surely discussed in the *poskim*, it remains an open and practically pressing *sheilah*.

### Not Backtracking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְפָנָיו, אֲבָל לְאַחֲרָיו – אֲפִילּוּ מִיל אֶחָד אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר, וּמִינַּהּ: מִיל הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר, הָא פָּחוֹת מִמִּיל – חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: With regard to prayer and washing one's hands, the Sages taught that one must travel four *mil* to find a synagogue or water only if the synagogue or the water is ahead of him, in the direction that he is traveling. But if it is behind him, he need not return even one *mil*. Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov said: From this statement one may infer that it is specifically a *mil* that one need not return; but one must return for a synagogue or water that is at a distance of less than one *mil*.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds that the teaching was not attributed to Rabbi Abbahu but to *Evo*, and that he enumerated four things, not three — the fourth being the very case of our *mishna*, that processing leather by walking requires four *mil*. This rounds out the list.

Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina introduces an important qualification: the rule of waiting up to four *mil* applies only when the water or the relevant resource lies *ahead* in one's journey. If one must retrace one's steps — traveling *backward* — even one *mil* is too much; one need not go back at all. The logic is clear: waiting while still advancing toward one's destination is merely a delay; backtracking means losing time and progress entirely. Rav Acha bar Yaakov infers from this that if it is less than one *mil* behind, one would in fact be required to turn back. So, practically: if one has just passed a rest stop and the next one is very far off, if the previous rest stop is within roughly eighteen minutes behind — and one can in fact turn back — one might be obligated to do so before eating without *netilat yadayim*.

## Army Tumah

### Troops & Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לִיגְיוֹן הָעוֹבֵר מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, וְנִכְנַס לְבַיִת – הַבַּיִת טָמֵא, שֶׁאֵין לָךְ כׇּל לִיגְיוֹן וְלִיגְיוֹן שֶׁאֵין לוֹ כַּמָּה קַרְקִפְלִין. וְאַל תִּתְמַהּ, שֶׁהֲרֵי קַרְקִפְלוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מוּנָּח בְּרֹאשׁ מְלָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in the *Tosefta*: In the case of a legion traveling for warfare from place to place, and one member of the legion enters a house, the house is impure, as there is no legion that does not have several scalps that its soldiers carry around for witchcraft. And you should not be surprised that they do so, as the scalp [*karkefal*] of Rabbi Yishmael was placed on the heads of kings.

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa*: if an army troop is passing through various cities and enters a house, one must assume everything in that house has become *tamei*. The reason: there is no army troop that does not have skulls among its members — apparently referring to the Roman practice of carrying the severed heads of defeated enemies.

### Rabbi Ishmael

The *baraisa* adds: *v'al titma* — do not be astonished by this — for the skull of Rabbi Yishmael rests in the palace of kings. This evokes the *Asara Harugei Malchus*, the Ten Martyrs commemorated in the *Yom Kippur* liturgy, and specifically the tradition about the flaying of Rabbi Yishmael's face. The connection to our *sugya* is not merely the general topic of *tumah* and *tahara*, but more specifically the theme of skin being removed from a body — a theme present in the *mishna* itself, which had mentioned the skin of humans and its use in making various vessels.

## Flaying Skin

### Flaying Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְשִׁיט בַּבְּהֵמָה וּבַחַיָּה, בַּטְּהוֹרָה וּבַטְּמֵאָה, בַּדַּקָּה וּבַגַּסָּה – לְשָׁטִיחַ, כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה. וּלְחֵמֶת – עַד שֶׁיַּפְשִׁיט אֶת הֶחָזֶה. הַמַּרְגִּיל – כּוּלּוֹ חִבּוּר לַטּוּמְאָה, לִיטַּמֵּא וּלְטַמֵּא. עוֹר שֶׁעַל הַצַּוָּאר – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חִבּוּר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חִבּוּר עַד שֶׁיַּפְשִׁיט אֶת כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The halakhic status of the hide of an animal after it was flayed is no longer like its flesh in terms of becoming impure and imparting impurity. Nevertheless, in the case of one who flays either a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal; a ritually pure animal that was slaughtered properly and afterward came in contact with impurity, e.g., the one flaying it is impure, or a ritually impure unslaughtered carcass; a small animal, e.g., sheep, or a large animal, e.g., cattle; and even after flaying the animal's hide is still partially attached to the flesh, the hide's halakhic status remains that of flesh in some circumstances. These circumstances are: If he is flaying the animal for the purpose of using the hide as a carpet, a tablecloth, or to drape over a couch, in which case he would cut the hide along the length of the animal from head to tail and then remove the hide from both sides, its halakhic status remains that of flesh until he has flayed the measure of grasping the hide, i.e., two handbreadths. And if he is flaying the animal for the purpose of crafting a leather jug, in which case he cuts a circle near the animal's neck and removes the hide in a downward movement, its halakhic status remains that of flesh until he flays the animal's entire breast. In the case of one who seeks to fashion a jug and begins flaying from the legs, until he removes the animal's hide in its entirety, the entire hide is considered as having a connection with the flesh and its halakhic status remains that of flesh with regard to impurity, i.e., with regard to becoming impure and with regard to imparting impurity. If one removed the entire hide except for the hide over the neck, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says: It is not considered to have a connection to the flesh, and the Rabbis say: It is considered to have a connection to the flesh until he removes the animal's hide in its entirety, including the neck.

The *gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which concerns the flaying (*hafshata*) of animals and the question of when skin that is in the process of being removed is still considered *halachically* connected to the meat. The *mishna* opens: one who flays (*hamafshit*) a *behemah* or *chayah*, whether *tahorah* or *teme'ah* — meaning whether the animal is kosher or non-kosher — and whether it is a small animal like a sheep or a large one like a cow (*badakah uvigasah*) — the rules that follow apply equally to all.

The practical import: if the skin is still considered connected to the meat, touching the skin is *halachically* equivalent to touching the meat itself. If the animal is a *neveilah* (or is a non-kosher species, which is by definition a *neveilah*), a *tahor* person who touches the connected skin becomes *tamei*. Conversely, if something *tamei* touches the skin while it is still connected, the meat becomes *tamei* as well.

### Mat Usage

The first case: if one is flaying the skin with the intention of using it as a mat (*lishatiach*) — the kind of animal-hide floor covering one associates with traditional and indigenous cultures — then the skin is considered connected to the meat only for the measure of *kedei achizah*, the amount one can grasp in one's hand. Once the flayed portion exceeds that amount, the part that has been peeled away is no longer considered connected to the meat.

What exactly is *kedei achizah*? Intuitively one might say a *tefach*, the breadth of a hand, but the *gemara* will clarify that it is actually two *tefachim*. The practical image: the leather is being slowly peeled away from the meat. The portion still flush against the meat is obviously connected. The flap that has been peeled away and is hanging free — up to *kedei achizah* worth — is still considered connected and can communicate *tumah* in either direction. Once the flap exceeds that measure, touching that portion does not communicate *tumah* to or from the meat. The portion still attached to the meat remains connected; only the status of the peeled flap is in question.

### Water Skin

The second case: if the intention is to use the skin as a water skin — a leather flask or canteen, sewn without seams for maximum capacity — then the skin remains connected to the meat until one removes the chest area. This is because the preferred method for making a water skin, in order to produce a large, seamless container, is to peel the skin from the legs upward toward the head. This is considerably more difficult than the alternative method, and the chest area is the hardest portion to separate. Until one has managed to pull the skin away through the chest, the entire skin in progress is still considered connected and touching any part of it communicates *tumah*.

If one flays from the head toward the feet, the rule is the same: it is all considered connected until one passes the chest. The difference is practical: working from head to feet means one reaches the critical threshold of the chest approximately midway through the process, while working from feet to head means one must complete almost the entire flaying before reaching that threshold. In either case, the chest is the pivot point.

### Neck Skin Debate

What about the neck skin that remains above the chest, once the chest has been passed? Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds that once you have gotten past the chest, the neck skin is no longer considered connected — *eino chibur*. The Sages disagree: it remains connected until the skin has been completely removed — *chibur ad sheyafsheet kulo*. Even though the neck skin will come off with minimal effort once the chest has been cleared — essentially in a moment — the Sages maintain it is still considered connected to the meat. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds that such easy removability means it is effectively already detached.

### Shomer Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מַאי? אָמַר רַב: טָהוֹר הַמּוּפְשָׁט, רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: טֶפַח הַסָּמוּךְ לַבָּשָׂר טָמֵא. מֵיתִיבִי: הַמַּפְשִׁיט כְּשִׁיעוּר הַזֶּה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, הַנּוֹגֵעַ בַּמּוּפְשָׁט טָהוֹר. מַאי לָאו אֲפִילּוּ בְּטֶפַח הַסָּמוּךְ לַבָּשָׂר? לָא, לְבַר מִטֶּפַח הַסָּמוּךְ לַבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: If one flays an animal for the purpose of making a carpet out of it, the halakhic status of the hide remains that of flesh until he has flayed the measure of grasping the hide. The Gemara asks: From that point forward, i.e., when one has flayed more than the measure of grasping of the hide, what is the status of the hide? Rav says: The entire section of the hide that has been flayed is pure because it no longer serves the flesh as a handle. But the hide that is still attached to the flesh serves the flesh as protection, and therefore it transmits impurity to the flesh and from the flesh, and joins together with the flesh to constitute the requisite measure to impart the impurity of food. But Rabbi Asi says: One handbreadth of the flayed hide that is next to the flesh is susceptible to impurity. Since the one who flays the hide holds this handbreadth of the hide while flaying, this section of the hide serves the flesh as a handle. The Gemara raises an objection to the statement of Rabbi Asi from a *baraita*: In the case of one who flays an unslaughtered animal carcass, once he has cut the hide along the length of the animal from head to tail and then removed this measure of the hide from both sides, then from that point forward, a person who touches the flayed hide is pure, because the flayed hide does not impart impurity of a carcass. What, is it not discussing even the handbreadth of hide next to the flesh, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Asi? The Gemara answers: No, there is no proof from this *baraita*, as it may be discussing the flayed hide except for the handbreadth next to the flesh.

This entire *mishna* raises a fundamental question: on what *halachic* principle does the connectedness of the skin operate? The two relevant categories are *shomer* (a protector of food, which communicates *tumah* when directly covering it) and *yad* (a handle, which communicates *tumah* through manipulation at a distance). Rashi reads these cases as involving *yad*, but this is difficult: the *yad* principle concerns the ability to manipulate the food by means of the appendage, and it is hard to see why the difficulty or ease of removing skin should bear on that question.

Tosafos offers a more compelling reading. This *mishna* is about *shomer*. The *shomer* principle requires that the protector be directly covering the food. The question in our *mishna* is whether a flap of skin that is partially peeled away — no longer flush against the meat — retains sufficient connection to the meat still beneath it to be treated as though it remains one unit. According to Tosafos, the degree of physical connection and the ease or difficulty of removal are precisely the right criteria for this question. When the skin is still tightly bound and hard to remove, even the peeled flap is considered one with the meat beneath, and functions as a *shomer*. When the skin will fall away effortlessly, it is as though it has already been removed.

This reading makes excellent sense of the *mishna*'s structure. At the beginning of the *mishna*, even a portion that has been peeled is considered connected because it remains tightly bound and the rest of the skin is still firmly in place. At the end of the *mishna*, the neck skin that is effectively about to fall off on its own is treated by Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri as already disconnected. The degree of adhesion governs the *halacha*.

This analysis is well illustrated by practical observation. In the flaying of animals, some areas of skin are barely adhered to the flesh at all and will peel away, or even flop off, with no effort. Other areas — particularly around the pelvis and the chest — are firmly bonded, often with a thick fat layer beneath, and require not merely pulling but active use of a knife to separate. It is precisely these firmly bonded areas that the *mishna* identifies as the thresholds of connection.

## Connected Skin Analysis

### Beyond Kdei Achizah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּעוֹר שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד הַבָּשָׂר – טָמֵא. עוֹר שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד הַבָּשָׂר – טָמֵא, הָא בְּטֶפַח הַסָּמוּךְ לַבָּשָׂר – טָהוֹר. תָּנָא: כׇּל טֶפַח הַסָּמוּךְ לַבָּשָׂר, עוֹר שֶׁכְּנֶגֶד הַבָּשָׂר קָרֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Come and hear a refutation of the opinion of Rabbi Asi from a *baraita*: Once an animal carcass has been flayed more than the measure of grasping the hide, if one touches the hide opposite the flesh before that hide was flayed he is impure. This statement indicates that the attached hide opposite the flesh is impure, but one handbreadth of the flayed hide that is next to the flesh is pure, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Asi. The Gemara answers: There is no proof from this *baraita*. The *tanna* who taught this *baraita* was referring to the entire handbreadth of flayed hide next to the flesh by calling it the hide opposite the flesh.

The *gemara* now asks directly: *mikan va'eilach mai* — what is the status of the skin beyond the amount of *kedei achizah*? The specific question is about the *tefach* of skin that has been peeled away but remains closest to the meat. Even once you have pulled back more than *kedei achizah*, that first *tefach* adjacent to the meat is still very tightly bound — requiring active use of a knife — and might still be considered connected.

### Rav vs. Rabbi Assi

Rav holds: *tahor hamufeshat* — everything that has been peeled away is *tahor*, even the skin right next to the meat, once sufficient progress has been made in the flaying. Rabbi Assi disagrees: *tefach hasamuch labasar tamei* — the *tefach* immediately adjacent to the meat remains *tamei* and is still considered connected. Only the skin beyond that *tefach* is regarded as disconnected.

### Baraita Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמַּפְשִׁיט בַּבְּהֵמָה וּבַחַיָּה, בַּטְּהוֹרָה וּבַטְּמֵאָה, בַּדַּקָּה וּבַגַּסָּה, לְשָׁטִיחַ – כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה, וְטֶפַח הַסָּמוּךְ לַבָּשָׂר – טָהוֹר. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּטֶפַח רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Come and hear a refutation of the opinion of Rabbi Asi from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:18): In the case of one who flays a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal, a ritually pure animal or a ritually impure unslaughtered carcass, a small animal or a large animal, if he is flaying the animal for the purpose of using the hide as a carpet, the halakhic status of the hide remains that of flesh until he has flayed the measure of grasping it, and one who touches one handbreadth of the flayed hide next to the flesh remains pure, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Asi. The Gemara answers: Here we are dealing with the first handbreadth of hide that is flayed in addition to the measure of grasping. In a case where only one handbreadth of hide has been flayed in addition to the measure of grasping, it is more comfortable for the one flaying the hide to grasp the edge of the hide rather than the handbreadth of hide that is nearest to the flesh. Furthermore, the most comfortable way to continue flaying is to do so with a knife without grasping the flayed hide at all. Therefore, Rabbi Asi concedes that the hide in this case does not serve the flesh as a handle and consequently does not impart impurity. But afterward, when one has already flayed more than three handbreadths of hide, one holds the handbreadth of hide next to the flesh while continuing to flay the animal, and since it serves the flesh as a handle it imparts impurity.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this dispute. A *baraisa* states: once one has flayed beyond the amount of *kedei achizah*, whoever touches any part of the flayed skin (*hanogea bamufshat*) is *tahor* — it does not communicate *tumah* in either direction. Does this not mean even the *tefach* closest to the meat? The *gemara* responds: no, the *baraisa* refers to the extended flap beyond that first *tefach*; the *tefach* adjacent to the meat would still be a *shomer* and would remain *tamei*.

A second *baraisa* is cited: touching the skin *shekeneged habasar* — opposite the meat — communicates *tumah*; conversely, the implication is that skin not directly opposite the meat would be *tahor*, which would seem to include the *tefach* closest to the meat. The *gemara* responds: the author of this *baraisa* treats the entire *tefach* closest to the meat as if it is equivalent to skin directly opposite the meat — calling it *or shekeneged habasar* — because it is so closely adhered that it is functionally part of the meat.

The *gemara* then cites a *Tosefta* that opens identically to our *mishna* and explicitly states: *vetefach hasamuch labasar tahor* — the *tefach* adjacent to the meat is *tahor*. This appears to support Rav directly. The *gemara* responds: the *Tosefta* is speaking about *tefach rishon* — the first *tefach*. What this means, according to Rashi, is that the first *tefach* after *kedei achizah* is still very difficult to remove, but once you get beyond that first additional *tefach*, everything comes off easily and is *tahor*. This explains the *Tosefta* as consistent with Rabbi Assi: it is saying that the *tefach* that is *tahor* is the one that has already been worked past, not the one immediately adjacent to the meat.

However, Tosafos notes that this reading is strained — if that first *tefach* is still hard to remove, that would be a greater reason for it to be *tamei*, not *tahor*. Tosafos cites a *girsa* of Rabbeinu Chananel that reads *tefach hasamuch labasar tamei*, directly supporting Rabbi Assi, with Rav's response being that he concedes the *tefach* immediately after *kedei achizah* is still connected — because it is still very tightly bound — but once one gets past that first additional *tefach*, everything thereafter, even the skin right up against the meat, becomes *tahor* because it will all fall away easily.

### Kdei Achizah Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: כַּמָּה כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה? טֶפַח. וְהָא תַּנְיָא: טִפְחַיִים! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: טֶפַח כָּפוּל. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כַּמָּה כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה? טֶפַח כָּפוּל.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in a *baraita*: How much is the measure of grasping? It is one handbreadth. The Gemara asks: But isn't it taught in another *baraita*: The measure of grasping is two handbreadths? Abaye says: The first *baraita* is referring to one who grasps a double handbreadth by folding the first two handbreadths together and grasping them. This explanation of Abaye is also taught in the *Tosefta* (8:18): How much is the measure of grasping? It is a double handbreadth.

How much is *kedei achizah*? One *baraisa* says a *tefach*; another says two *tefachim*. Abaye resolves this: when we say a *tefach*, we mean a doubled *tefach* — i.e., two *tefachim*. This is also consistent with Rav's position: *kedei achizah* (the initial grasping amount) corresponds to one *tefach*, and the next *tefach* beyond it remains considered connected because it is still tightly bound. Only beyond both of these — once more than two *tefachim* have been successfully peeled — does even the skin adjacent to the meat become *tahor*. The two *baraitot* thus refer to different thresholds: one to the *kedei achizah* proper, and one to the total amount including the adjacent *tefach*.

## Tallit Tearing

### Tallit Identity Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: טַלִּית שֶׁהִתְחִיל בָּהּ לְקוֹרְעָהּ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּקְרַע רוּבָּהּ – שׁוּב אֵינוֹ חִבּוּר, וּטְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in a mishna elsewhere (*Kelim* 28:8): A ritually impure garment that tears is rendered pure because it is no longer considered a useable garment. In the case of a ritually impure garment that one began to tear, once the majority of the garment is torn, the two sections are no longer considered as having a connection, and since it is no longer considered a useable garment, it is pure.

The *gemara* now introduces a digression that is, as we will see, directly connected to the question of when something is considered sufficiently separated or destroyed to lose its prior *halachic* identity. The *mishna* elsewhere states: a *talit* — here meaning an outer garment, not specifically a *tzitzit* garment — that has begun to be torn: once the majority of it has been torn, it is no longer considered a single unit (*eino chibur*), and it is *tehorah*. The mechanism here is the same as by vessels: destroying an object causes it to lose its identity, and with that loss of identity, its *tumah* falls away. One need not immerse it in a *mikvah*; tearing it past the majority achieves the same result. Less than that — even if the garment would not be worn in a torn state — there remains the possibility that it will be sewn up, and it is therefore still considered one piece and retains its *tumah*.

### Majority Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּטַלִּית טְבוּלַת יוֹם. דְּמִיגּוֹ דְּלָא חַס עֲלַהּ וְאַטְבְּלַהּ, לָא חָיֵיס עֲלַהּ וְקָרַע לַהּ רוּבַּהּ. אֲבָל טַלִּית שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְבוּלַת יוֹם – לָא, גְּזֵרָה דִּלְמָא לָא אָתֵי לְמִיקְרְעַהּ רוּבַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman says that Rabba bar Avuh says: The Sages taught this *halakha* only with regard to an impure garment that was already immersed in a ritual bath that day, but one must wait until nightfall for the purification process to be completed in order to wear the garment while eating ritually pure food. In such a case, tearing the majority of the garment renders it pure, and the Sages did not apply the requirement that the entire garment be torn. This is because the reason for this rabbinic decree is lest one tear only half of it out of concern for the damage to the garment. And since in this case, the owner of the garment showed that he was not concerned about damage to the garment by immersing it in a ritual bath, an act which damages the garment, so too he is presumably not concerned about the garment with regard to tearing it, and he will certainly tear the majority of it. But a garment that was not immersed that day is not rendered pure by tearing the majority of the garment due to a rabbinic decree lest one come to not tear the majority of the garment but only half of it.

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha qualifies: this applies only to a *talit tevulat yom* — a garment that was already immersed in the *mikvah* that same day and is awaiting nightfall to complete its purification. Why would someone tear such a garment rather than simply wait until nightfall? The *gemara* explains: since this person had no concern for the garment's integrity — evidenced by the fact that they immersed it in the *mikvah* in the first place, an act that damages cloth — we can trust them to tear past the majority. *Lo chayeis lah ve-kara lah rubah*: since they did not care for the garment, they will not hesitate to tear it fully past the majority.

But by a *talit* that has *not* been immersed in the *mikvah*, tearing the majority does not suffice to render it *tahor*. Why? This is a rabbinic *gezeirah*: we are concerned that, since the person is protective of the garment and preferred tearing it (a repairable act) to immersing it (which damages it more permanently), they will not bring themselves to tear a full majority. They may tear only forty or forty-nine percent and convince themselves they have done enough. In that case we would mistakenly consider it *tahor* when it is not. Therefore, the rabbis require that the *entire* garment be torn before we permit relying on this method.

### Rava's Objections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: שְׁתֵּי תְּשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר: חֲדָא – שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ טְבִילָה בַּת יוֹמָא עוֹלָה, וְעוֹד – עוֹלַת הָעוֹף לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לִיגְזַר, דִּילְמָא לָא אָתֵי לְמֶעְבַּד רוֹב שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba said: There are two refutations of this statement. One is that the decree should apply even to a garment that was immersed that day, lest people who see one eating ritually pure food while wearing such a garment say that immersion of a garment on the same day is sufficient for the garment to be worn while eating ritually pure food, and there is no need to wait until nightfall. And furthermore, if a garment that was not immersed that day is rendered pure only by tearing the entire garment and not by tearing merely a majority of the garment, due to a decree lest one tear less than a majority of the garment, so too, with regard to a bird burnt offering according to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that one is required to pinch merely a majority of two *simanim* in the nape of the neck in order to prepare it for sacrifice, let him decree against pinching the majority of two *simanim* lest one come to not perform the pinching on the majority of two *simanim*, but rather on merely half of them.

Rava objects with two responses (*shtei teshuvot badavar*). First: you are making a *gezeirah* that perhaps he will not fully tear the majority. I counter with my own *gezeirah*: if a person immersed a garment in the *mikvah* and is then seen using the cloth on that same day, people will assume it is because the *mikvah* immersion alone rendered it *tahor* — they will not realize he also tore it. They will therefore conclude that *tevulat yom* (immersion without waiting for nightfall) is sufficient to render something *tahor*. In the case you permit, therefore, you create an equal or greater concern. It would be better not to use that method at all — and then people will understand that the cloth is being used *because* it was torn, which is the actual reason for its *taharah*.

Second: you claim we must be concerned that a person trying to do a majority will stop short and only do slightly less than a majority. But consider the case of *olas ha'of* according to Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon: when performing *shechita* of a bird *olah*, he requires cutting the majority of both *simanim* without severing the head entirely. If we were genuinely concerned that someone aiming for the majority would come to do too little, we should be equally concerned there that the *kohen*, in his caution not to sever the head, will fail to achieve the full majority. Yet we do not make such a *gezeirah* — evidently we trust people to achieve the required *shiur* precisely. Tosafos adds that Rava's argument may not require demanding the *entirety* be done, but at minimum one should require a *rov hanikar* — an obvious, clearly perceptible majority, say seventy percent — rather than 51%, so that there is no room for confusion between the threshold amount and slightly below it. If even that is not required, it clearly shows we trust people to execute a *shiur* accurately.

### Trust in Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: דְּקָא אָמְרַתְּ גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ טְבִילָה בַּת יוֹמָא עוֹלָה – קִרְעָהּ מוֹכִיחַ עָלֶיהָ. וּדְקָא אָמְרַתְּ, עוֹלַת הָעוֹף לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לִיגְזַר – כֹּהֲנִים זְרִיזִים הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef responded to Rabba's two refutations and said to him: With regard to that which you said, that a rabbinic decree is necessary even with regard to a garment that was immersed that day lest onlookers say that immersion of a garment on that same day is sufficient, there is no reason for such a concern. This is because its tear proves that the garment is pure due to the tear, and not because one is allowed to wear an immersed garment while eating pure food before sunset. And with regard to that which you said, that with regard to a bird burnt offering according to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, let him decree against pinching the majority of the *simanim* lest one fail to pinch the majority, in fact there is no reason for such a concern. Rav Yosef explains: The reason there is no concern is that pinching is performed by the priests, who are vigilant with regard to mitzvot.

The *gemara* responds on behalf of the original position. To Rava's first objection — that people will confuse *tevulat yom* with full *taharah* — the answer is: *kra mochiach aleha*, the tear itself is visible. Although people saw the garment go to the *mikvah*, they will also see that it is torn, and they will understand that it is the tearing, not the immersion alone, that accounts for its current *tahor* status. This response is somewhat difficult, but that is the *gemara*'s answer.

To Rava's second objection from *olas ha'of*: *Kohanim zrizim heim* — *kohanim* are meticulous and punctilious in the performance of their service. We can trust them to achieve precisely the right amount — the full majority of both *simanim* — while simultaneously being careful not to sever the head entirely. That precision cannot be assumed of ordinary people handling garments. Rav Yosef adds that practically speaking, when performing *shechita* the practice is already to cut a *rov hanikar* — a clearly perceptible majority well beyond the technical 51% — so the concern about people confusing the threshold is already addressed in practice.

### No Gezeirah Here

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמַּפְשִׁיט בַּבְּהֵמָה וּבַחַיָּה, בַּטְּמֵאָה וּבִטְהוֹרָה, בַּדַּקָּה וּבַגַּסָּה, לְשָׁטִיחַ – כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה. הָא יָתֵר מִכְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה – טָהוֹר, אַמַּאי? לִיגְזַר, דִּילְמָא לָא אָתֵי לְמֶעְבַּד אֶלָּא כְּדֵי אֲחִיזָה, וְקָא נְגַע בְּטוּמְאָה, וְקָא מְטַהֲרִינַן לֵיהּ! אִי בְּטוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – הָכִי נָמֵי, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּטוּמְאָה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Come and hear a refutation to Rabba bar Avuh's explanation of the mishna in tractate *Kelim* from the mishna here: In the case of one who flays a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal, a ritually impure animal or a ritually pure animal, a small animal or a large animal, if he is flaying the animal for the purpose of using the hide as a carpet, the halakhic status of the hide remains that of flesh until he has flayed the measure of grasping the hide. The Gemara infers: But after one has flayed more than a measure of grasping the hide, the hide is pure. Why is this so? Let the Sages decree that even a hide that was flayed more than the measure of grasping is susceptible to impurity lest one who intends to flay more than a measure of grasping come to complete only a measure of grasping, and he is thereby touching a source of impurity, and we might mistakenly deem him pure. The Gemara answers: If the mishna were discussing impurity by Torah law, indeed the Sages would issue such a decree. But here we are dealing with impurity by rabbinic law, and therefore the Sages did not issue such a decree.

The *gemara* now attempts to use our original *mishna* about flaying to resolve the broader question. The *mishna* states: once one has flayed beyond *kedei achizah*, touching the peeled skin does not communicate *tumah*. This raises the same structural question: why do we not impose a *gezeirah* requiring more than just *kedei achizah*? If we are generally worried that people will stop short of the required *shiur*, we should demand they peel away more than the minimum before declaring the flap disconnected. The *gemara* asks: *amai*, why allow it with exactly *kedei achizah*? Should we not be concerned that someone will believe they have peeled away enough when they have not, will then touch the skin, and will declare themselves *tahor* when they are not?

The *gemara* answers: if this were a case of *tumah deoraita* — biblical *tumah* — you would be correct, and we would make such a *gezeirah*. But what case are we actually discussing? A case of *tumah derabbanan* — rabbinic *tumah*. Something rabbinically *tamei* touches the skin, and the question is whether this communicates *tumah* to the meat beneath. For rabbinic *tumah*, we do not apply the same stringencies and we do not impose additional safeguards.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח טָמֵא בַּטְּהוֹרָה, טָהוֹר בִּטְמֵאָה – טוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא! בִּטְרֵפָה. טְרֵפָה בַּת טַמּוֹיֵי הִיא? אִין, כְּדַאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: טְרֵפָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ מְטַמְּאָה בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: According to this answer, the case in the mishna of an impure person who flays a pure animal works out well. That could be referring to a case of impurity by rabbinic law. But in every case of a pure person who flays an impure animal, i.e., an unslaughtered animal carcass, the impurity of the animal is by Torah law. Therefore, in such a case the Sages should have issued a decree. The Gemara explains: The case taught in the mishna of a person who flays an impure animal is referring to a pure animal that was properly slaughtered, but after the slaughter the animal was found to have a wound that would have caused it to die within twelve months, thereby rendering it a *tereifa*. Such an animal imparts impurity by rabbinic law. The Gemara asks: Is a pure animal that is found to be a *tereifa* capable of imparting impurity? The Gemara answers: Yes, in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel's father, as Shmuel's father said: A *tereifa* that one slaughtered imparts impurity by rabbinic law if it is a sacrificial animal. Accordingly, the mishna is referring to the case of a sacrificial animal that was found to be a *tereifa*.

But one might object: the case in which a *tahor* person touches the skin of an animal that is a *neveilah* is certainly a *deoraita* concern. The *gemara* responds: *teinach tamei betahor* — it works when something rabbinically *tamei* touched *tahor* meat. But *tahor betamei* — when a *tahor* person touches the skin of a *tamei* animal — that is *tumah deoraita*. So why does the *mishna* not impose a *gezeirah* in that direction?

The answer: *bitreifa*. The *mishna*'s reference to a *teme'ah* animal does not mean a non-kosher species or a *neveilah*. It means a *treifa*. And a *treifa* that has been properly slaughtered is, biblically, *tahor*. There is, however, a rabbinic *tumah* for *treifa*, and therefore the entire *mishna* operates within the realm of rabbinic *tumah*, where no additional *gezeirah* is required.

### Treifa Tumah

The *gemara* then questions whether *treifa* carries any *tumah* at all, even rabbinic. The answer: yes — as taught by the father of Shmuel: *treifa sheshachta metamei bemukdashin* — a *treifa* that has been slaughtered, although it is *tahor* both biblically and rabbinically in ordinary contexts, is treated as *tamei* in the context of *kodshim*. If it comes into contact with *kodshim*, or if someone who has handled it wishes to eat *kodshim*, the *treifa* is considered *tamei* for those purposes. Thus, within the narrow world of *kodshim*-related concerns, the *treifa* carries a rabbinic *tumah*, and it is within that limited context that the *mishna* operates — allowing the *halacha* of *kedei achizah* to function without the imposition of a further *gezeirah*.

## Small Animals

### Shratzim Flaying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בֶּן יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַמַּפְשִׁיט בִּשְׁרָצִים – חִבּוּר, עַד שֶׁיַּפְשִׁיט אֶת כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Come and hear a refutation of Rabba bar Avuh's explanation of the mishna in tractate *Kelim* from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:19): Rabbi Dostai ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: With regard to one who flays creeping animals, its hide is considered to have a connection with the flesh, and it imparts impurity of a creeping animal until he flays the animal in its entirety.

The *gemara* now introduces a further case. Rav Dustai ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon states: *hamafshit beshratzim* — when one flays one of the eight *sheratzim*, the skin is considered connected to the *sheretz* until the skin is completely removed (*chibbur ad sheyafsheet es kulo*). Whether because the skin is very closely adhered or because there is insufficient area for *kedei achizah* to begin with, the rule is that complete removal is required before disconnection can be said to have occurred.

### Camel Skin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בְּגָמָל – אֵינוֹ חִבּוּר. לָא תֵּימָא הָא בְּגָמָל אֵינוֹ חִבּוּר, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: בְּעוֹר שֶׁעַל הַצַּוָּאר אֵינוֹ חִבּוּר, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: This *halakha* applies only with regard to creeping animals, but with regard to a camel or other non-kosher animals, when the hide is flayed more than a measure of grasping it is not considered to have a connection with the flesh and is pure, even though when the measure of grasping is flayed it is impure. Apparently, there is no rabbinic decree that even a hide that was flayed more than the measure of grasping imparts impurity lest one who intends to flay more than a measure of grasping come to flay only a measure of grasping, contrary to the opinion of Rabba bar Avuh. The Gemara rejects this proof: Do not say that this statement indicates that with regard to a camel, when the hide is flayed more than a measure of grasping it is not considered to have a connection with the flesh. Rather, say that this statement indicates that only with regard to a creeping animal is the hide considered to have a connection with the flesh until one flays the animal in its entirety. But with regard to a camel carcass and other non-kosher animals, in a case where one seeks to fashion a jug and begins flaying from the legs, if he removed the entire hide except for the hide over the neck, it is not considered to have a connection to the flesh, and this statement is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri stated in the mishna.

If by *sheratzim* complete removal is necessary, the implication (*al kiddush hashem*) is that by other animals — for instance, a camel — less than total removal would suffice. A camel, being a non-kosher species, is a *neveilah*, and thus carries *tumah deoraita*. This would seem to undercut the *gemara*'s earlier answer that our *mishna* deals only with rabbinic *tumah*: if the analogous case of a camel — involving biblical *tumah* — also requires only less than full removal, then our *mishna* is not merely a rabbinic concern, and yet we still do not impose a *gezeirah* demanding more than *kedei achizah*.

### Neck Skin Resolution

The *gemara* resists this inference. Do not say that by a camel less than full removal suffices — *lo teima ha begamal eino chibur*. That is not accurate. By a camel, just as by a *sheretz*, one would indeed require complete removal. Rather, the implication of the contrast is more limited: by *sheratzim*, even the neck skin remains connected and must be fully removed; by other animals, the neck skin alone does not need to be fully removed — but everything else does.

This reading — that "less than all" means "all except perhaps the neck" — preserves the *gemara*'s position that in virtually every case of biblical *tumah* we would demand complete removal and would not permit relying on the lesser standard of *kedei achizah*. The cases in our *mishna* that permit less than complete removal are therefore consistently attributed to rabbinic *tumah* contexts. The *gemara* thus continues to resist the plain reading of the *mishna* that *kedei achizah* is a general and broadly applicable standard even for biblical *tumah*.

## Tallit Qualifications

### Bottom Cloth Remaining

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא שִׁיֵּיר בָּהּ כְּדֵי מַעְפּוֹרֶת, אֲבָל שִׁיֵּיר בָּהּ כְּדֵי מַעְפּוֹרֶת – חִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna says in the name of Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yosei: The mishna taught that an impure garment, most of which has been torn, is no longer impure only when one did not leave untorn a part of the garment that is the measure of a scarf. But if he left an untorn piece that is the measure of a scarf, it is considered to have a connection, and the garment remains ritually impure.

Rav Huna in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yossi adds a further qualification: *lo shanu* — when do we say that tearing the majority renders the *talit tahor*? Only when one did not leave at the bottom of the garment sufficient fabric to serve as a *ma'aforet* — a wrap or kerchief-like cloth. But if one *did* leave enough at the bottom to function as a wrap — *shiyer bah kedei ma'aforet* — then even the torn piece remains considered connected (*chibur*) and it is not yet *tahor*.

This too runs against the simple sense of the *mishna*, which permits majority without qualification. It is noted that Tosafos cites a *gemara* indicating this additional restriction is rabbinic in nature, which makes sense: biblically, once the majority is torn, the *talit* has lost its identity and is *tahor*, but rabbinically, if a usable remnant remains at the bottom, the garment is treated as still extant and still *tamei*.

### Leather vs. Cloth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא טַלִּית, אֲבָל עוֹר – חֲלִים. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ עוֹר נָמֵי לָא חֲלִים.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish said: The mishna taught only with regard to an impure garment, most of which has been torn, that it is no longer impure, but an impure hide that was torn in such a manner remains impure because it is repairable. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even a hide that was torn in such a manner is no longer impure because it is not repairable.

Reish Lakish introduces a different qualification: *lo shanu ela talit* — the rule that tearing the majority renders the object *tahor* applies only to a cloth garment. But *aval or* — a leather garment — *cholim*, is strong and resilient. Because leather holds together better, tearing the majority of a leather garment does not sever its identity as a single unit in the same way, and therefore it does not become *tahor* by majority tearing. The reason appears to be one of the following: either that leather, even after the majority is torn, will not continue to tear naturally and still effectively retains its structural integrity; or, as Rashi explains, that when one sews up a torn leather garment it retains its integrity better than cloth.

### Leather Strength Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עוֹר טָמֵא מִדְרָס, חִשֵּׁב עָלָיו לִרְצוּעוֹת וְסַנְדָּלִים – כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן בּוֹ אִיזְמֵל טָהוֹר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיַּמְעִיטֶנּוּ מֵחֲמִשָּׁה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to the opinion of Reish Lakish from a mishna (*Kelim* 26:9): In the case of a hide that is impure with impurity imparted by treading, if the owner intended with regard to the hide to make it into straps and sandals, when he applies a scalpel [*izmel*] to the hide, the hide becomes pure; this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say: The hide does not become pure until he reduces the size of the hide to a measure of less than five handbreadths. In any event, when he reduces the hide to a measure of less than five handbreadths, everyone agrees that it becomes pure. Why is this so? Let us say according the opinion of Reish Lakish that the hide should not become pure, as it is repairable.

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish: *afilu or nami lo cholim* — even leather is not particularly strong, and therefore the same rule as cloth should apply. He then cites a case that implicitly challenges the proposition that we never rely on less-than-full destruction for leather: a leather item that has become *tamei midos* — because a *zav* sat upon it — where the owner decides to cut it into strips for sandals or a belt. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the moment one places the awl to the leather — even before actually cutting it, merely beginning the transformation — it becomes *tahor*. The Sages say: not until the leather has been reduced to less than five *tefachim*. Tosafos notes that less than five *tefachim* of leather corresponds to less than the majority for cloth.

The point is that even by leather, there is a threshold below which the object is destroyed and becomes *tahor* — we do not say leather is so strong that it can never lose its identity through cutting. Rabbi Yochanan's challenge to Reish Lakish is precisely this.

## Leather Transformation

### Awl Transformation

The *baraisa* at issue states: a leather item *tamei midos* — rendered *tamei* because a *zav* sat upon it, giving it the status of a seat or bed — where one intends to cut it into strips for sandals or leather bands. Rabbi Yehuda holds that as soon as one places the awl to the leather, the transformation has begun and it is *tahor*. This is a strikingly radical position — the mere initial act of beginning the cutting process suffices to change the object's *halachic* identity, even before any material change has occurred.

### Five Tefachim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְמַעֵט לַהּ (מארבע) [מֵאַרְבָּעָה] מִיהָא טָהוֹר, אַמַּאי? לֵימָא: הָא חֲלִים וְקָאֵי!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir holds that although breaking off a minority of the structure of the oven is insufficient, in any event when one reduces the size of the oven to less than four handbreadths in height the oven is rendered pure. Why is this so, according to the opinion of Reish Lakish? Let us say that this oven exists in a repairable state, as the plaster holds it together, and according to Reish Lakish it should therefore be considered connected and remain impure.

The Sages disagree: it becomes *tahor* only when it has been reduced to less than five *tefachim*. The reason is that five *tefachim* is the minimum size at which leather can serve as a usable garment or seat — below that threshold, the object can no longer function in the capacity that made it *tamei*, and so it is *tahor*. Tosafos draws the parallel: just as cloth becomes *tahor* when less than the majority remains, leather becomes *tahor* when less than five *tefachim* remain. The underlying logic is identical — it is no longer a functional object.

### Cut Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמְרִי דַּחֲלִים, הֵיכָא דְּקָא צָרֵי לֵיהּ לְהֶדְיָא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּמְקַצֵּעַ וּבָא לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ סְבִיבוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish responded to Rabbi Yoḥanan: When I said that a torn hide is repairable, I was referring to a case where one cut the hide in a straight line. By contrast, here we are dealing with a case where one cut the hide in a circular manner. In such a case, the hide is not repairable.

Finally, the *gemara* resolves the apparent tension with Reish Lakish's position by distinguishing between types of cuts. When does Reish Lakish say leather is strong and does not lose its identity so easily? *Heicha dekatzrei lei lehedya* — when one makes a straight linear cut, which can be sewn up and which allows the leather to retain its integrity. But the case in the *baraisa* — *bemekatz'o uvah derech sevivotav* — involves cutting all around the perimeter, creating strips in multiple directions, which leaves no possibility of restoring the object to its original form. For such cuts, even Reish Lakish would agree that the leather's identity is destroyed. The distinction therefore is between a straight cut (which leather can survive as a functional object) and circumferential or multiple-directional cutting (which irrevocably transforms it).

The *gemara* ends here, with the relationship between these cases — when a torn or peeled or cut object retains its identity versus when it loses it — forming the bridge that the *gemara* will continue to develop in the next daf.
