# Moed Katan 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shaving & Laundry Rules

### Intro & Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ מְגַלְּחִין בַּמּוֹעֵד: הַבָּא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וּמִבֵּית הַשִּׁבְיָה, וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְהַמְנוּדֶּה שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים, וְכֵן מִי שֶׁנִּשְׁאַל לְחָכָם וְהוּתַּר, וְהַנָּזִיר וְהַמְּצוֹרָע מִטּוּמְאָתוֹ לְטׇהֳרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these may shave** and cut their hair **on** the intermediate days of **a Festival: One who comes from a country overseas; and** one who is released **from a house of captivity; and one who comes out of prison** on the intermediate days of a Festival; **and one who had been ostracized** and therefore prohibited from cutting his hair, **and the Sages released him** from his decree of ostracism on the intermediate days of the Festival; **and similarly, one who** had vowed not to cut his hair and then **requested of a Sage** to dissolve his vow **and was released** from it on the intermediate days of the Festival; **and the nazirite** whose term of naziriteship ended on the intermediate days of a Festival; **and the leper** who needs to purify himself on the intermediate days and must shave his entire body in order to leave **his** state of ritual **impurity** and regain **his** ritual **purity.** Since these people were not able to cut their hair on the eve of the Festival, they are permitted to do so on the intermediate days of the Festival.

The fourteenth daf of Moed Katan opens a new *perek*, beginning at the bottom of 13b. While the tractate continues its treatment of the laws of *chol hamoed*, this *perek* will gradually transition into an extended discussion of *aveilut* and related areas of *halacha*. The opening subject, however, is a *mishnah* that identifies which individuals are permitted to shave and launder on *chol hamoed* — and, by implication, which are not.

### Who Can Shave

The *mishnah* opens: *ve'elu megalchin bamoed* — the following people are permitted to shave on *chol hamoed*. The list proceeds as follows: one who returns from *medinat hayam*, an overseas journey; one who returns from captivity (*mibeit hasheviyah*); and one who has been freed from prison (*hayotzei mibeit ha'asurin*). These are all individuals who plainly had no opportunity to shave beforehand and are only now in a position to do so. The *mishnah* also includes *hamenudeh sheheitiru lo chachamim* — someone who was placed in *cherem* and has now been released by the *chachamim*. The prohibition against shaving is itself part of the laws of *cherem*, so this person was similarly unable to do so while the *nidui* was in force. And *mi shenishal lechacham vehutar* — one who had made a vow and has now been released from it by a *chacham*.

These last two cases are notable. One could argue that both the *menudeh* and the one who took a vow brought their situation upon themselves — a case of *techilato bifshia vesofo be'ones*, where the initial conduct was negligent even if the eventual inability was involuntary. Yet the *mishnah* still permits them to shave on *chol hamoed*, since at the operative moment they lacked the option.

Finally, the *mishnah* includes *hanazir vehametzora ha'oleh mitumato letaharato* — a *nazir* and a *metzora* who are transitioning from a state of *tumah* to *tahara*. A *nazir* is ordinarily prohibited from cutting his hair; however, if he becomes *tamei*, the process of restarting his *nezirut* after achieving *tahara* involves cutting his hair. Similarly, the *tahara* process of a *metzora* requires cutting the hair. These individuals are therefore not merely permitted but are actively required to cut their hair as part of the *tahara* procedure. In a sense, this case is the least surprising of the list, since the haircut is itself a component of a *mitzvah* being performed at precisely this moment.

### Who Can Launder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ מְכַבְּסִין בַּמּוֹעֵד: הַבָּא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וּמִבֵּית הַשִּׁבְיָה, וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, וּמְנוּדֶּה שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים, וְכֵן מִי שֶׁנִּשְׁאַל לְחָכָם וְהוּתַּר. מִטְפְּחוֹת הַיָּדַיִם וּמִטְפְּחוֹת הַסְּפָרִים וּמִטְפְּחוֹת הַסְּפָג, הַזָּבִין וְהַזָּבוֹת וְהַנִּדּוֹת וְהַיּוֹלְדוֹת, וְכׇל הָעוֹלִין מִטּוּמְאָה לְטׇהֳרָה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּתָּרִין. וּשְׁאָר כׇּל אָדָם אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And these may launder** their clothes **on** the intermediate days of **a Festival: One who comes from a country overseas; and one who** is released **from a house of captivity; and one who comes out of prison** on the intermediate days of a Festival; **and one who had been ostracized and the Sages released** him from his decree of ostracism; **and similarly, one who** had vowed not to launder his clothes and he **requested from a Sage** to dissolve his vow **and was released** from it on the intermediate days of a Festival. **Hand towels; and barbers' towels,** which are used to cover a person having a haircut; **and body-drying towels,** all of which get quickly soiled, may be laundered on the intermediate days of a Festival. ***Zavim*,** men suffering from an impure venereal emission; ***zavot*,** women who experience a flow of menstrual-type blood on three consecutive days during a time of the month when they do not expect to experience menstrual bleeding; **menstruating women; women who have** just **given birth; and all others who leave** a state of **ritual impurity for** a state of **ritual purity** on the intermediate days of the Festival, **these** people **are** all **permitted** to launder their clothes in order to purify themselves. **But all other people are prohibited** from laundering during the intermediate days of the Festival.

The *mishnah* continues with *ve'elu mechabsin bamoed* — the following may launder on *chol hamoed*: one returning from overseas, from captivity, and from *beit ha'asurin*. In connection with the last category, the *mishnah* evokes the well-known episode of Yosef's release from prison, where the text records *vayegalach vayechaleif simlotav* — he shaved and changed his garments — thus providing a biblical parallel to both *megalchin* and laundering.

The list continues with the *menudeh* released by the *chachamim* and one released from a vow specifically not to wear laundered clothing. One who had vowed not to wear laundered garments and is now released may therefore launder. The *nazir* and *metzora* are not included in the laundering list, since there is no *halacha* requiring them to refrain from laundering their clothing.

The *mishnah* adds several categories of items that may be laundered on *chol hamoed* regardless of who owns them: *mitpachot hayadayim* — hand towels; *mitpachot hasaparim* — the towels used by barbers; and *mitpachot hasafag* — bath towels used for absorbing and drying. These items become soiled with regular use and must therefore be laundered repeatedly, even if they had been laundered before the *regel*. The underlying principle of the *mishnah* is that items or persons who had no prior opportunity to launder are permitted to do so on *chol hamoed*. The special categories of towels extend this logic: even if laundered beforehand, their functional necessity makes laundering on *chol hamoed* permissible.

It is worth noting, in passing, the somewhat curious inclusion of barbers' towels — which raises the implicit question of why a barbershop would be operating on *chol hamoed* at all. The most natural answer is that the barber is there to serve those very individuals — newly freed from prison and the like — who are themselves permitted to receive haircuts during this period.

### General Prohibition

The *mishnah* concludes with *ve-shaar kol adam assurin* — all other people are forbidden. In characteristic *mishnaic* fashion, the rule is stated as a summary after the exceptions have been enumerated. The general prohibition against shaving, getting a haircut, and laundering on *chol hamoed* is thus the default, and the preceding cases represent carefully defined departures from that default.

## Reason: Kevod Haregel

### Why Forbidden?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁאָר כׇּל אָדָם מַאי טַעְמָא אֲסוּרִין? כְּדִתְנַן: אַנְשֵׁי מִשְׁמָר וְאַנְשֵׁי מַעֲמָד אֲסוּרִין לְסַפֵּר וּלְכַבֵּס, וּבַחֲמִישִׁי מוּתָּרִין מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד הַשַּׁבָּת. וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי טַעְמָא — כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּנְסוּ לְמִשְׁמַרְתָּן כְּשֶׁהֵן מְנֻוּוֹלִין, הָכָא נָמֵי: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּנְסוּ לָרֶגֶל כְּשֶׁהֵן מְנֻוּוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that **all other people are prohibited** from performing these actions during the intermediate days? Why are these actions not included in the category of actions that are permitted in deference to the Festival? The Gemara answers: **As we learned** in a mishna (*Taanit* 15b): Both **the members of the priestly watch** whose week it is to serve in the Temple **and the members of the non-priestly watch** who accompanied the members of the watch to Jerusalem **are prohibited from cutting** their **hair or laundering** their clothes during that week. **But on the Thursday** of that week **they are permitted** to do so **in deference to Shabbat. And Rabba bar bar Ḥana said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: What is the reason** that the members of the priestly and non-priestly watch are prohibited from cutting their hair and laundering their clothes? It is **in order that they not enter their watch when they are untidy.** If it were permitted for them to cut their hair and launder their clothes during the week of their watch, they would leave their haircuts and laundry until the middle of that week and begin their service in a disorderly state. **Here, too,** ordinary people are prohibited from cutting their hair or laundering their clothes on the intermediate days of a Festival, **in order that they** complete all necessary preparations beforehand and **not enter the Festival when they are untidy.**

The *Gemara* opens with the question *ve-shaar kol adam mai taama assurin* — why are all other people forbidden? This is a striking question. One might have expected the *Gemara* simply to treat this as self-evident — these are prohibited activities on *chol hamoed* — and yet the *Gemara* finds it necessary to seek a principled rationale. To frame its answer, the *Gemara* draws a comparison from a different *mishnah*: *anshei mishmar ve-anshei maamad assurin lesaper u-lechabbes* — the *kohanim* serving their weekly rotation in the *Beit Hamikdash* and the representatives of *Klal Yisrael* standing in their corresponding service in the cities are forbidden to get haircuts or launder during their week of service, but are permitted to do so on Thursday in honor of *Shabbat*.

This parallel is itself surprising. These individuals are engaged in the service of the *Beit Hamikdash*, a time of *simcha* and honor — and yet they too are forbidden from shaving and laundering during that week. The *mishnah* implicitly assumes that others who are not in these special categories are also forbidden during ordinary times — and the categories of persons who may not do these things normally include the *menudeh* and the *metzora*, for whom the prohibition is a mark of social exclusion or ritual impurity.

### Prevent Menuvalim

The *Gemara* quotes Rabbah bar bar Chanah in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *mai taama? kedei she-lo yiknesu le-mishmartam keshen menuvalin* — the reason the *anshei mishmar* may not shave during their rotation is so that they should not enter their Temple service appearing disheveled and unpresentable. *Hacha nami kedei she-lo yiknesu la-regel keshen menuvalin* — and so too on *chol hamoed*: the prohibition exists so that people should not enter the *regel* in a *menuval* state.

The logic is as follows. The *regel* is a time when everyone wishes to appear presentable and festive. If shaving and laundering were permitted freely on *chol hamoed*, people would be inclined to procrastinate and put it off, knowing they could take care of it during the holiday when they had more leisure time. By prohibiting it on *chol hamoed*, the Rabbis ensure that people attend to their appearance before the *regel* begins. The irony, of course, is that once someone has genuinely been unable to prepare — through no fault of their own — the prohibition itself would leave them appearing *menuval* throughout the *regel*. This is precisely why the *mishnah* creates its exceptions: when there was truly no prior opportunity, the person is permitted to shave or launder so as to appear presentable on the *regel*. But for everyone who did have an opportunity and simply failed to use it, the Rabbis enforce the prohibition and require them to endure the social discomfort of appearing unkempt — a cost significant enough to deter future procrastination.

### Melachot vs. Tircha

This analysis raises a more fundamental question, which Tosafot addresses directly. On 14a, the second Tosafot, *Tosafot* asks: *af al pi shehu melacha haya lanu lehatiro l'tzorech hamoed* — even granting that shaving and laundering are *melachot*, we would normally permit *melachot* performed for the needs of the *moed*. Why the need for a special rationale? The question posed by the *Gemara* — why are these things forbidden — does not arise simply because they are *melachot*, since we have seen throughout this tractate that many *melachot* may be performed *l'tzorech hamoed*. The *Gemara*'s question is: why was no such exception created here? Why, specifically for shaving and laundering, was the rule made absolute, with only these narrow enumerated categories exempted?

The answer is precisely the concern of *kevod haregel*: if we created an open-ended exception *l'tzorech hamoed*, the result would be universal sloppiness on the *regel*, since everyone would claim the exception. *Tosafot* adds a further refinement: *af sheyeish melacha shemuteres b'moed she'ein ba tircha vegam prachmatia, mikol makom ze shehu l'yipui raui lehatir lichvod moed* — even granting that some *melachot* are permitted because they involve no great effort, and setting aside the issue of *prachmatia* (commerce), something done for personal beautification would also seem appropriate to permit for the honor of *moed*. Yet we do not permit it — because of the concern of *menuvalin*.

This discussion opens a broader question that runs through the entire tractate: what is the conceptual basis for the prohibitions of *chol hamoed*? The *Gemara* never once invokes the framework of the thirty-nine *melachot* in this context. There is no discussion of *ma'amer*, *dash*, or *kotzer*. The organizing principles seem to be something other than the classic Shabbat-derived categories. *Tosafot* on 12b, discussing gathering in one's produce to protect it from thieves, cites a view that attempts to fit the prohibition into one of the thirty-nine *melachot* — connecting it to the *melacha* of *asifa* associated with *chag ha'asif* — but Tosafot expresses uncertainty about how that fits, and then raises the alternative: *v'tzorech iyun iy torach belo melacha assur, ke'heichei de'asri b'prachmatia* — perhaps the operative category is *tircha* (burdensome effort) rather than *melacha* per se, as the *Gemara* itself suggests elsewhere.

*Tosafot* is thus navigating between two frameworks — the thirty-nine *melachot* on the one hand, and the looser category of *tircha* on the other — neither of which fully accounts for all the cases in the tractate. The prohibition on business (*prachmatia*) is a prime example: commerce is not one of the thirty-nine *melachot*, yet it is clearly prohibited on *chol hamoed*. And *tircha* also fails as a universal criterion: getting a haircut is not particularly burdensome, and neither are many of the other prohibited activities. A more plausible organizing principle may be the concept of *uvda d'chol* — activities that carry the character of a weekday — particularly professional and commercial labor that marks the rhythm of an ordinary working life. Agricultural field work is the paradigm case in an agrarian society; skilled craftsman labor (*melachet uman*) and commerce extend the same principle. Shaving and laundering may not fit neatly into any of these categories, which is precisely why the *Gemara* felt the need to supply a distinct rationale: the concern of *menuvalin* operating as an independent rabbinic policy.

This also sharpens a question about the nature of the violation itself. If someone goes ahead and shaves or launders on *chol hamoed*, have they violated a *melacha* of *chol hamoed* — however defined — or have they violated a distinct rabbinic enactment designed to compel prior preparation and preserve *kevod haregel*? The answer may affect how the violation is conceptualized and what consequences follow from it.

## Lo Plug Principle

### Lost Object Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: אָבְדָה לוֹ אֲבֵידָה עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל, כֵּיוָן דַּאֲנִיס — מוּתָּר, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא — לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira asks: If one lost an item on the eve of a Festival** and he was busy searching for it and had no time to cut his hair or launder his clothes before the Festival commenced, can one say that **since** he was **a victim of circumstances beyond his control,** which prevented from taking care of these matters prior to the Festival, **he is permitted** to cut his hair and launder his clothes on the intermediate days of the Festival? **Or perhaps since it is not** clearly **evident** to others that he failed to cut his hair or launder his clothes due to unavoidable circumstances, he is **not** permitted to perform these actions during the Festival week.

The *Gemara* asks: *ba'ei Rav Zera* — Rav Zera posed a question. If someone lost a lost object on *erev haregel* — that is, they spent the day before the *regel* running around searching for a lost item — and consequently had no opportunity to shave or launder, what is the *din*? On one hand, *keivan de-anis mutar* — since this person was genuinely *anus*, unable to attend to grooming through no real fault of their own, perhaps they should be permitted to shave on *chol hamoed*, analogous to the enumerated cases in the *mishnah*. On the other hand, *keivan delo michezya milta* — since the circumstances are not evident to others, the person does not fall into a recognized, visible category, and allowing the exception in such an open-ended case risks undermining the rule entirely.

### Lo Plug Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, יֹאמְרוּ: כׇּל הַסְּרִיקִין אֲסוּרִין, סְרִיקֵי בַּיְיתּוֹס מוּתָּרִין?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said,** in answer to this question, that there is a principle of the Sages that applies here. They prohibited the baking of decorated Syrian cakes for Passover, lest people tarry in their preparation of these elaborate cakes and the cakes become leavened. When Baitos ben Zunen wished to prepare the cakes in a way that would not lead to a violation of any prohibition, the Sages nevertheless prohibited it. They explained that were they to permit him to do so, others **would say: All the** decorated **Syrian cakes are forbidden,** but **the Syrian cakes of Baitos are permitted?** This teaches that the Sages do not permit exceptions when the reason for leniency is not clearly evident, like in the case of one who was busy looking for a lost item.

Abaye responds with a principle that captures the *Gemara*'s concern: *yomru kol hasrikin ha-asurin vesrikin d'Baitos mutarin* — people will say that all cut figures in dough are forbidden, but those of Baitos are permissible. This is the principle of *lo plug*, the refusal to make distinctions where the underlying circumstances are not self-evident to observers. The reference is to the *halacha* of *matzah*: one may not cut decorative designs into *matzah* dough before baking because the time required for such cutting risks allowing the dough to become leavened. Baitos, a baker, invented a device analogous to cookie cutters — a mechanism that performed the cutting instantaneously, eliminating any concern of leavening. One might argue that his *srikin* should be permitted since the underlying reason for the prohibition does not apply. But the *Gemara* forbids it: if we permit one person's designs while forbidding another's, observers will not understand the distinction, the rule will be effectively nullified, and every baker will claim a special justification.

The same logic applies here. If we permit shaving and laundering for anyone who presents sufficiently special circumstances — even where those circumstances are genuine — we create an amorphous, unverifiable category that could swallow the rule. The *Gemara*'s concern is not merely one of *marat ayin* but something more fundamental: when circumstances are not self-evident, there is no principled basis on which others can evaluate the legitimacy of a given exception, and the distinction between the permitted and the forbidden collapses entirely.

### One Shirt Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא חָלוּק אֶחָד מוּתָּר לְכַבְּסוֹ בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד, הָתָם נָמֵי, יֹאמְרוּ: כׇּל הַסְּרִיקִין אֲסוּרִין, סְרִיקֵי בַּיְיתּוֹס מוּתָּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira retorted: **And according to your reasoning, that which Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Anyone who has only one shirt is permitted to launder it on the intermediate days of a Festival, there too,** one should apply the principle that others **will say: All the** decorated **Syrian cakes are forbidden** but **the Syrian cakes of Baitos are permitted.** There, too, it is not clearly evident that the reason for leniency is that one has only one shirt.

The *Gemara* then challenges Abaye's reasoning by citing a ruling of Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol mi she'ein lo ela chaluk echad mutar lechabeso be'chol hamoed* — anyone who has only a single shirt is permitted to launder it on *chol hamoed*. This case bears a strong resemblance to the lost object scenario. If someone has only one shirt, who would know from observing them that they are in this special circumstance? There is nothing visible that marks them as belonging to a distinct, recognizable category — yet we nonetheless permit the exception. This seems to contradict Abaye's *lo plug* doctrine.

### Izuro Mochiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֵיזוֹרוֹ מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **Wasn't it** already **said about this** that **Mar, son of Rav Ashi, said: His belt is proof for him,** as when he launders his only shirt he will have to wear his outer garment tied with his belt so that his body is not exposed, and everyone will understand that he has no other shirt to wear.

The *Gemara* resolves this challenge by citing Mar bar Rav Ashi: *izuro mochiach alav* — his belt testifies on his behalf. The most appealing interpretation of this phrase is that the person laundering his only shirt is doing so bare-backed, wearing nothing but his belt. The sight of a person laundering his garment while wearing only a belt makes the circumstance visually self-evident: it is obvious to any observer why he is laundering on *chol hamoed* — he has no other garment to wear. The exception is therefore not invisible; it is rendered evident by the circumstances themselves.

The principle that emerges is nuanced. The *lo plug* doctrine does not require that every permissible case fall into a formally named, pre-defined category like *ba mimedinat hayam* or *yotzei mibeit ha'asurin*. Rather, the requirement is that the exception be *mochiah* — evident to observers in some concrete, perceptible way. Where circumstances visibly testify to the person's situation, an exception may be recognized even if it falls outside the explicit *mishnaic* categories. Where circumstances are not visible, the exception collapses back into the general prohibition.

The *Gemara* then records a version of Rav Zera's question attributed to Rav Ashi: what if a craftsman (*uman*) — presumably a barber — lost a lost object on *erev haregel*? Perhaps in that case it is evident, since observers would have seen his shop closed and would understand why clients went unserved. Or perhaps the closure of a shop is not sufficiently *mochiah* as a personal circumstance, since the barber is simply one person among many in a community where no one was getting haircuts. The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי מַתְנֵי, בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: אוּמָּן שֶׁאָבְדָה לוֹ אֲבֵידָה עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּאוּמָּן הוּא — מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא כִּי הָנָךְ — לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi taught** a different version of this passage, as follows: **Rabbi Zeira asks:** With regard to **a craftsman who lost an item on the eve of a Festival** and was busy searching for it, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted for him to launder his clothes and cut his hair on the intermediate days of the Festival because he had been unable to do so before the Festival? The Gemara explains the two sides of the question: Can one say that **since he is a craftsman, it is** clearly **evident** why he did not launder his clothes and cut his hair before the Festival, as many people would have come to his place of work and seen that he was busy looking for his lost article? **Or perhaps since** the special circumstances **are not** as clearly **evident as in those** cases in the mishna, it is **not** permitted for him to cut his hair or launder his clothes during the intermediate days of the Festival. The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

This discussion has a direct practical application for later generations. Some *poskim* have argued that a clean-shaven man who shaved on *erev yom tov* and by *chol hamoed* has visible stubble is in a category where his circumstances are *mochiah* — it is evident to observers that he began the *yom tov* looking presentable and is now looking unkempt through no choice of his own. The *Rogatchover* addresses this and raises the parallel to the lost object case, ultimately concluding that the evidence is still insufficient — since not everyone saw the man when *yom tov* began and not everyone can assess how long the growth has accumulated. However, a number of later *poskim* have taken a more permissive view, particularly in modern times when clean-shaving is the norm and a single day's growth is visibly and unambiguously distinguishable from a beard cultivated over weeks. The practical *halacha* on this point remains a matter of discussion.

## Overseas Travel Debate

### RY Forbids; Sages Allow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַבָּא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם — לֹא יְגַלֵּחַ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּצָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: One who comes **from a country overseas** on the intermediate days of a Festival is permitted to cut his hair. The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: One who comes from a country overseas** on the intermediate days of a Festival **must not shave** or cut his hair, **due to** the fact that **he left** his home **without permission.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a *tanna'ic* dispute about the case of someone returning from overseas. *Masnisin de-lo ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — the *mishnah* that permits such a person to shave is not the position of Rabbi Yehuda. The *baraita* records that Rabbi Yehuda forbids shaving for one who returns from *medinat hayam*, on the grounds that *she-yatza she-lo birshus* — he departed without permission, meaning there was nothing compelling him to make the journey. The implication is that he should have planned better and prepared before leaving.

Notably, this rationale sits in some tension with the *mishnah*'s allowances for the *menudeh* and the one who took a vow — individuals who more clearly brought their situations upon themselves — yet who are nonetheless permitted to shave. The *mishnah*'s treatment of the overseas traveler is therefore more generous than Rabbi Yehuda's view.

### Travel Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָשׁוּט — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר, לִמְזוֹנוֹת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר, לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא לְהֶרְוֵיחָא. מָר מְדַמֵּי לֵיהּ כְּלָשׁוּט, וּמָר מְדַמֵּי לֵיהּ כְּלִמְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to explain the definition and parameters of leaving without permission. **Rava said:** If one left Eretz Yisrael **to travel, all agree** that **it is prohibited** for him to shave during the intermediate days of the Festival because he left on his own initiative, and this is not considered an unavoidable circumstance. If he left **for** basic **sustenance,** i.e., due to financial difficulty, **all agree** that **it is permitted** for him to shave upon his return because he is regarded as having left due to circumstances beyond his control. **They disagree only** with regard to the case where he left **to** earn greater **profit,** i.e., he had enough to live on in Eretz Yisrael, but he left in order to increase his income. **One Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **compares him** to one who left **to travel, and the other Sage,** the Rabbis of the mishna, **compare him** to one who left **for** basic **sustenance.**

Rava offers a tripartite clarification of the debate. If one traveled *lashut* — for pleasure, on what amounts to a leisure voyage — *divrei hakol assur*: everyone agrees such a person may not shave upon returning, for he had no compelling reason to travel and should have planned accordingly. If one traveled *le-mezonos* — because basic livelihood required it, putting food on the table — *divrei hakol mutar*: everyone agrees he is permitted to shave, since the journey was effectively compelled by necessity. The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim* applies only *le-harvacha* — when the purpose of the journey was profit, going beyond basic sustenance to pursue a business opportunity. One position (*mar medamei lei le-lashut*) likens profit-seeking travel to a pleasure cruise — one could have foregone the opportunity and stayed home in time to prepare for *yom tov*. The other position (*umar medamei lei le-mezonos*) treats it as equivalent to providing for one's family — even making a profit is a form of provision, and the choice to travel was not truly discretionary.

### Profit-Travel Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה כְּשֶׁיָּצָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים כְּשֶׁיָּצָא בִּרְשׁוּת. מַאי שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת? אִילֵּימָא לָשׁוּט — וְהָאָמְרַתְּ דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר! וְאֶלָּא לִמְזוֹנוֹת — וְהָאָמְרַתְּ דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא, לְהֶרְוֵיחָא. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: נִרְאִין דְּבָרַי חֲכָמִים כְּשֶׁיָּצָא בִּרְשׁוּת, מַאי בִּרְשׁוּת? אִילֵּימָא לִמְזוֹנוֹת — הָא אָמְרַתְּ דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר! וְאֶלָּא לְהֶרְוֵיחָא — וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּהָא! הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְרַבָּנַן כְּשֶׁיָּצָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — לָשׁוּט, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ חֲכָמִים לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ עָלָיו אֶלָּא לְהֶרְוֵיחָא, אֲבָל לָשׁוּט — מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ. וְנִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה כְּשֶׁיָּצָא בִּרְשׁוּת, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — לִמְזוֹנוֹת, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לֹא נֶחְלַק עֲלֵיהֶם אֶלָּא לְהֶרְוֵיחָא, אֲבָל לִמְזוֹנוֹת — מוֹדֶה לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said:** The **statement of Rabbi Yehuda seems** more reasonable in a case **when he left without permission, and the statement of the Rabbis** seems more reasonable **when one left with permission.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** meant by **without permission? If we say** that it means that he left **to travel, didn't you say** that in this case **all agree** that **it is prohibited** for him to cut his hair upon his return? **Rather,** say that it means that he left **for** basic **sustenance.** But **didn't you say** that **all agree** that **it is permitted** for him to cut his hair in such a case? **Rather,** it is **obvious** that it means that he left in search of greater **profit. Say the latter clause** of the *baraita* as follows: **The statement of the Rabbis seems** more convincing **to Rabbi Yehuda** in the case **where one left with permission.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** meant by **with permission? If we say** that he left **for** basic **sustenance, didn't you say** that **all agree** that **it is permitted** for him to cut his hair upon his return? **Rather,** say that it means that he left **for** greater **profit.** But **didn't you say** in the first clause that **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda seems** more reasonable **in this** case? Therefore, according to Rava's distinctions, there is an internal contradiction within the *baraita*. The Gemara resolves the difficulty: **This is what** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **is saying: The statement of Rabbi Yehuda seems** more reasonable **to the Rabbis** in the case **where one left without permission. And what is** meant by without permission? It means a case where one left **to travel, as even the Rabbis disagree with** Rabbi Yehuda **only** when one left **for** greater **profit, but** when one left **to travel they agree with** Rabbi Yehuda that it is prohibited for one to shave and cut his hair. **And the statement of the Rabbis seems** more convincing **to Rabbi Yehuda** in the case **where one left with permission. And what is** meant by with permission? It is a case where one left **for** basic **sustenance, as even Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with** the Rabbis **only** when one left **for** greater **profit, but** when he left **for** basic **sustenance** Rabbi Yehuda **concedes to them** that it is permitted for him to shave and cut his hair. Therefore, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's intent was not to decide between the opinions of the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda but to clarify their dispute.

The *Gemara* then examines a statement of Rebbi (*Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi*) who appears to split his vote: he endorses Rabbi Yehuda's position *keshe-yatza she-lo birshus* and the *chachamim*'s position *keshe-yatza birshus*, which initially seems to confirm that they debate both cases. But the *Gemara* challenges this reading. If *she-lo birshus* refers to travel for pure leisure (*lashut*), everyone already agrees it is forbidden — there would be no need to side with Rabbi Yehuda. And if *birshus* refers to travel for basic livelihood (*le-mezonos*), everyone already agrees it is permitted — there would be no need to side with the *chachamim*.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *hachi kama* — Rebbi is not dividing his vote at all. He is making an analytical observation about where each position is compelling. When he says Rabbi Yehuda's view is correct *she-lo birshus*, he means it is correct even according to the *rabbanan* — because the case at hand (*lashut*) is one where everyone agrees. And when he says the *rabbanan*'s view is correct *birshus*, he means it is correct even according to Rabbi Yehuda — because the case at hand (*le-mezonos*) is one where everyone agrees. Rebbi is not adjudicating the disputed middle case; he is clarifying that the extremes are not actually in dispute, and the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim* applies only to the case of *le-harvacha* — travel for profit.

## Infants & Minors

### Minor Born in Moed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: קָטָן הַנּוֹלָד בַּמּוֹעֵד — מוּתָּר לְגַלֵּחַ בַּמּוֹעֵד, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בֵּית הָאֲסוּרִים גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה. בַּמּוֹעֵד — אִין, מֵעִיקָּרָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel said:** If **a baby was born on a Festival** and he has long hair that is bothering him, **it is permitted to cut** his hair **on the** intermediate days of the **Festival, as there is no greater prison than this,** the womb of his mother, due to which his hair could not have been cut prior to the Festival. A precise reading of Shmuel's statement indicates that if the child was born **on the Festival, yes,** his hair may be cut. However, if he was born **before** the Festival, but for some reason his hair was not cut, **no,** it is not permitted to cut it on the intermediate days of the Festival because there was time to do so before the Festival.

The *Gemara* records a ruling of Shmuel: *katan hanolad b'moed mutar legale'ach b'moed* — a minor born during *chol hamoed* is permitted to have his hair cut during *chol hamoed*. The reasoning is that the infant was, in a sense, confined in his mother's womb and had no prior opportunity to receive a haircut. He falls into the category of one who was genuinely *anus* and is now free to do what was previously impossible.

### Born Before Moed?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב פִּנְחָס: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ מוּתָּר לְגַלֵּחַ בַּמּוֹעֵד — מוּתָּר לְגַלֵּחַ בִּימֵי אֶבְלוֹ. הָא אָסוּר לְגַלֵּחַ בַּמּוֹעֵד — אָסוּר לְגַלֵּחַ בִּימֵי אֶבְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pineḥas raised an objection** from that which is taught in a *baraita*: **All of those** about **whom** the Sages **said: It is permitted to shave** and cut his hair **on** the intermediate days of the **Festival, it is** also **permitted to shave** and cut his hair **during the days of his mourning. But** from this it follows that **one who is prohibited from shaving** and cutting his hair **on** the intermediate days of **a Festival is** also **prohibited from shaving** and cutting his hair **during the days of his mourning.**

The *Gemara* immediately raises the implication of this ruling. If we permit the haircut only because the infant was born during *chol hamoed*, then an infant born before *chol hamoed* — even the day before — would apparently not be permitted to receive a haircut during *chol hamoed*. This is a striking result, since the infant is obviously not personally obligated in any *halacha* of grooming. It would seem that the prohibition of giving a haircut on *chol hamoed* applies even to a passive recipient who bears no personal obligation.

### Minors & Aveilus

Rav Pinchas draws an inference from the *mishnah*'s categories: *kol elu she-amru mutar legale'ach bemoed, mutar legale'ach bimei avlo* — all those whom the *Rabbis* permitted to shave on *chol hamoed* are similarly permitted to shave during their period of *aveilut*. The converse inference would then be: those who are forbidden to shave on *chol hamoed* are forbidden to shave as an *avel*.

This inference creates a problem. If the first version of the ruling — that only an infant born during *chol hamoed* may receive a haircut, while one born beforehand may not — is correct, then an infant born before *chol hamoed* falls among those forbidden to receive a haircut on *chol hamoed*. By the inference above, this would imply that such an infant is similarly forbidden to receive a haircut during a period of *aveilut* — in other words, that the laws of *aveilut* apply to a *katan*. But this cannot be correct, as a *baraita* explicitly teaches: *kor'in le-katan mipnei agmat nefesh* — one performs *kriah* for a *katan* (when the *katan* is the *avel*) solely to arouse grief among the bystanders, but the *katan* himself is not personally obligated in the laws of *aveilut*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ קָטָן אִית בֵּיהּ פְּלוּגְתָּא — נִמְצֵאת אֲבֵילוּת נוֹהֶגֶת בְּקָטָן. וְהָתַנְיָא: מְקָרְעִין לַקָּטָן, מִפְּנֵי עׇגְמַת נֶפֶשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that with regard to **a baby there is a distinction** between being born before the Festival and being born on it, and in some cases it is prohibited to cut the hair of a baby, then **you find that mourning** is **practiced** even **with a minor. Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One rends** the clothes of **a minor** whose relative has passed away, **due to** the desire to bring about feelings of **grief** among those who see him? However, there is no inherent requirement for the minor to observe any of the *halakhot* of mourning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִי קָתָנֵי הָא אֲסוּרִין! דִּלְמָא יֵשׁ מֵהֶן אָסוּר וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Is it taught** explicitly in the first *baraita* cited by Rav Pineḥas that if it is prohibited for one to have a haircut during the Festival, **it is** likewise **prohibited** to have a haircut during the week of mourning? That was merely an inference. **Perhaps there are** those **among them,** i.e., those listed in the mishna, for whom it is **prohibited,** while **there are** others **among them** for whom it is **permitted,** and the *halakhot* of mourning do not apply to a baby.

Rav Ashi resolves the difficulty by rejecting the converse inference altogether. The *mishnah*'s principle, properly understood, is one-directional: *mi katani ha-asurin?* — the *mishnah* does not say that those forbidden on *chol hamoed* are forbidden as *avelim*. The rule is: anyone permitted on *chol hamoed* is permitted as an *avel*. But among those forbidden on *chol hamoed*, the category is not monolithic — *yesh mehen asur ve-yesh mehen mutar* — some are forbidden as *avelim* and some are permitted. A *katan* who cannot receive a haircut on *chol hamoed* (under the first version of the ruling) may still be exempt from the haircut prohibition during *aveilut*, since he is simply not subject to the laws of *aveilut* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמֵימָר, וְאִי תֵּימָא רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי, מַתְנֵי הָכִי. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: קָטָן מוּתָּר לְגַלְּחוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד, לָא שְׁנָא נוֹלַד בַּמּוֹעֵד וְלָא שְׁנָא נוֹלַד מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar, and some say** it was **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, taught** that statement **in this** manner. **Shmuel said:** With regard to **a baby, it is permitted to cut his** hair **during the Festival.** It **is no different** whether **he was born during the Festival, and it is no different** whether **he was born beforehand.**

The discussion brings into sharp relief an underlying structural parallel between *chol hamoed* and *aveilut*: both are conditions in which shaving and laundering are prohibited. The *menudeh*, the *avel*, and the person who cannot shave on *chol hamoed* all share this common restriction. Yet these categories are not identical, and the relationship between them — one of analogy rather than equation — requires careful analysis.

## Haregel vs. Aveilus

### Aveilus Suspended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבֵל אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֲבֵילוּתוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׂמַחְתָּ בְּחַגֶּךָ״. אִי אֲבֵילוּת דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הוּא — אָתֵי עֲשֵׂה דְרַבִּים וְדָחֵי עֲשֵׂה דְיָחִיד. וְאִי אֲבֵילוּת דְּהַשְׁתָּא הוּא — לָא אָתֵי עֲשֵׂה דְיָחִיד וְדָחֵי עֲשֵׂה דְרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** § **A mourner does not practice** the *halakhot* of **his mourning on a Festival, as it is stated: "And you shall rejoice in your Festival"** (Deuteronomy 16:14). The Gemara explains: **If it is a mourning** period that had already begun **at the outset** of the Festival, **the positive mitzva of** rejoicing on the Festival, which is incumbent upon **the community, comes and overrides the positive mitzva of** the **individual,** i.e., the mourning. **And if the mourning** period began only **now,** i.e., the deceased died during the Festival, **the positive mitzva of the individual does not come and override the positive mitzva of the community.**

The *Gemara* articulates a fundamental principle: *avel eino noheg aveilut baregel* — an *avel* does not practice *aveilut* on *yom tov*. The source is the verse *vesamachta be-chagecha* — you shall rejoice in your festival. Two scenarios are then distinguished. If a person was already in *aveilut* before *yom tov* began: *ati asei de-rabbim ve-dachi asei de-yachid* — the communal positive commandment of *simchat yom tov* comes and overrides the individual positive commandment of *aveilut*. If a person becomes an *avel* during *yom tov* itself: *lo ati asei de-yachid ve-dachi asei de-rabbim* — the individual obligation of *aveilut* has even less power to displace the communal obligation of *simcha*, since the status quo is already the *asei* of the many.

The language of *asei de-rabbim* overriding *asei de-yachid* carries a significant implication: it treats *aveilut* as a positive commandment in its own right, which might suggest it is *de-oraita*.

### Aveilus Status

Tosafot addresses this directly. The phrase *asei de-yachid* implies that *aveilut* carries the weight of a biblical positive commandment. But, Tosafot notes, this reading would make the comparison one of communal *de-oraita* versus individual *de-oraita*, rather than *de-oraita* versus *de-rabbanan*. Tosafot then advances an alternative: perhaps *simchat haregel* as we experience it is itself only rabbinic. The biblical verse refers to the *korbanot* of *shalmei simcha*, not to the broader observance of *simchat yom tov* as a daily practice. If both *aveilut* and *simchat haregel* are rabbinic, the distinction between communal and individual obligation would explain why the former yields to the latter without requiring either to be of biblical origin.

This is a striking position — the *Pnei Yehoshua*, for example, never entertained the possibility that *simchat haregel* might be only rabbinic — but it is consistent with Tosafot's analysis. The broader question of whether *aveilut* is *de-oraita* is a major dispute among the *rishonim*. The predominant view limits any biblical dimension of *aveilut* to the first day; no *rishon* maintains that all seven days carry *de-oraita* force.

### Nidui on Haregel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדָּה, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּנְהוֹג נִידּוּיוֹ בָּרֶגֶל? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: דָּנִין דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת וְדִינֵי מַכּוֹת וְדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, וְאִי לָא צָיֵית דִּינָא — מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **one who was ostracized, what is** the *halakha*? Must he **observe the practices of ostracism,** or are they overridden by the mitzva to rejoice **on the Festival? Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** an answer from that which is taught: During the Festival **the court judges** cases of **capital law,** cases of **lashes,** and cases of **monetary law.** It is known that **if one does not listen to** and follow **the judgment,** among the possibilities of enforcement is that **we ostracize him** until he accepts the verdict.

The *Gemara* then pivots: *menudeh mahu she-yinhog niduyo be-regel?* — does a person in *nidui* continue to practice his status of *cherem* during *yom tov*? The question is sharpened by the structural parallel: *aveilut* and *nidui* share the same outward practices — no shaving, no laundering — and both are conditions of personal distress that run counter to the spirit of *simchat yom tov*. If *aveilut* is suspended by the *regel*, one might expect *nidui* to be suspended as well.

### Proof from Courts

Rav Yosef offers a proof that *nidui* does apply during the *regel*, drawn from the *halacha* that court cases — including capital cases, cases of lashing, and monetary cases — may proceed on *chol hamoed*. If a litigant refuses to comply with a court ruling (*ve-i lo tzayit dina*), the standard enforcement mechanism is *nidui*: placing the recalcitrant party in *cherem*. The very possibility of holding court on *chol hamoed*, therefore, presupposes the availability of *nidui* as an enforcement tool. If *nidui* did not apply during the *regel*, there would be no coercive mechanism available, and the court's authority would be effectively suspended. Since we know that courts do function on *chol hamoed*, *nidui* must be operative.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְדִלְמָא לְעַיּוֹנֵי בְּדִינֵיהּ? דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת דְּקָתָנֵי, הָכָא נָמֵי דְּקָטְלִין לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** There is no proof from here, **as perhaps** when the *baraita* states that the court judges cases, what it meant is that it **deliberates in judgment** on the Festival, but it does not actually reach a verdict. Therefore, the court never ostracizes someone during the Festival. Accordingly, there is no *a fortiori* argument proving that one observes the practices of ostracism during the Festival. **Because if you do not say so,** that the *baraita* is referring to deliberation, then in the cases of **capital law that are taught, here too, would we put someone to death** on the Festival?

Abaye challenges this proof. Perhaps the court merely investigates cases on *chol hamoed* without issuing a final ruling — *le-iyunei be-dinei* — in which case the need for *nidui* as an enforcement mechanism does not arise. To support this, Abaye notes the difficulty with capital cases specifically. If courts were issuing final verdicts on *chol hamoed*, they would be executing people on *yom tov*, which would disrupt *simchat yom tov* — not only for the condemned, but for the community at large. It is therefore more plausible that courts only conduct preliminary hearings on *chol hamoed*.

### Death Penalty Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא מִימַּנְעִי מִשִּׂמְחַת יוֹם טוֹב — דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְסַנְהֶדְרִין (שֶׁרָאוּ בְּאֶחָד) שֶׁהָרְגוּ אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ שֶׁאֵין טוֹעֲמִין כׇּל אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ עַל הַדָּם״. אֶלָּא: לְעַיּוֹנֵי בְּדִינֵיהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי לְעַיּוֹנֵי בְּדִינֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, נִמְצֵאתָ מְעַנֶּה אֶת דִּינוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **But** if so, the judges **would be prevented from rejoicing on the Festival, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says: From where** is it derived with regard **to the Sanhedrin who put someone to death that they may not taste** any food or drink **for the entire** rest **of the day? The verse states: "You shall not eat with the blood"** (Leviticus 19:26). It is not appropriate to eat during the same day that they caused bloodshed. **Rather,** the statement of the *baraita*, that the court judges cases of capital law, must be referring **to deliberating in judgment** and not actually reaching a verdict. **Here too,** in cases of monetary law and of lashes, it is referring **to deliberating in judgment,** without the possibility of ostracizing anyone during the Festival. Rav Yosef said **to him: If so,** if only deliberations are conducted during the Festival, **you find a perversion of justice** by delaying **his** verdict.

The *Gemara* responds that the scenario of preliminary investigation without final verdict is untenable in capital cases, due to the concern of *innuy hadin* — the prolongation and suspension of judgment constitutes a form of oppression of the accused. One may not rule a person liable to death without carrying out the sentence promptly; to investigate a capital matter and then defer the verdict is itself a violation.

How, then, could a court on *chol hamoed* handle such cases? The *Gemara* describes a procedure: the court would convene in the morning (*atu mitzafra*), conduct its deliberations (*u-me'aynei bedinei*), then adjourn for the day's festive meal (*ve-azlei ve-akhlei ve-shatei*), and reconvene at sunset (*vehadar atu be-shkiat ha-chammah*) to deliver the final verdict (*ve-gamrinan le-dineih*) and carry out the execution — whereupon nightfall would begin a new day, allowing the judges to eat once more. The procedure threads the needle between the prohibition of *lo tochlu al ha-dam* (the judges may not eat on the day they execute) and the prohibition of *innuy hadin* (the verdict may not be indefinitely suspended.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָתוּ מִצַּפְרָא וּמְעַיְּינִי בְּדִינֵיהּ, וְעָיְלִי, וְאָכְלִי וְשָׁתוּ כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא, וַהֲדַר אָתוּ בִּשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה וְגָמְרִי לְדִינֵיהּ וְקָטְלוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, the judges **come in the morning** as usual **and deliberate in judgment.** They then **enter** their homes **and eat and drink all day,** i.e., as much as they desire, in order to rejoice fully on the Festival. **Then they come** back to the courthouse close to **sunset and complete his judgment,** i.e., they dispense the verdict, and if necessary, **they kill him.** In this manner, they are not prevented from enjoying the Festival properly.

### Nidui Applies

Even granting that this procedural arrangement is technically feasible, the *Gemara*'s discussion leaves the primary inference intact: if courts actually proceed to final verdicts on *chol hamoed* — even through this elaborate scheduling — then *nidui* as an enforcement tool must be operative during the *regel*. The evidence from the court cases thus points toward the conclusion that *nidui*, unlike *aveilut*, is not suspended by *yom tov*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: וּמְנוּדֶּה שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** another support from a *baraita*: **And** it is the case with regard to **one who is ostracized, that the Sages permitted him** to get a haircut during the intermediate days of the Festival. This indicates that one who is ostracized need not observe the practices of ostracism during the Festival.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִי קָתָנֵי שֶׁהִתִּירוּהוּ חֲכָמִים? שֶׁהִתִּירוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים קָתָנֵי, דַּאֲזַל וּפַיְּיסֵיהּ לְבַעַל דִּינֵיהּ, וַאֲתָא קַמֵּי דְרַבָּנַן וּשְׁרוֹ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Is it taught** in the *baraita*: **That they permitted it,** which would indicate that **the Sages** permitted all those who were ostracized to cut their hair on the intermediate days of the Festival? Rather, **it is taught: They permitted him.** This indicates that it is referring to an individual case, **where one went and appeased his** opposing **litigant and the Sages came and released him** from his decree of ostracism.

This creates a challenge that the *Gemara* will continue to address: what distinguishes *nidui* from *aveilut* such that the communal *simcha* of *yom tov* overrides the latter but not the former? The *regel* cancels *aveilut*, yet apparently leaves *nidui* intact — a distinction that demands principled explanation.
