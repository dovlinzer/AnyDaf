# Bava Batra 169 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lost Documents Rule

### Braita Intro

The *braita* at the close of 168b is the subject around which the entirety of today's *daf* revolves. The *braita* opens with the following case: *harei sheba ve'amar avad shtar chovi* — someone comes forward and declares that he has lost the document recording a debt owed to him. Even if the original witnesses to that document come forward and testify — *af al pi she'amru eidim anu chasamnu venasanu bo* — that they were indeed the signatories and can vouch for the underlying transaction, *ein kosvin oso shtar*: we do not write him a replacement *shtar*.

### Why No Loan Rewrite

The reason is straightforward. Even granting that the claimant is entirely truthful about having lost the document, the risk of issuing a replacement is too great. He may yet find the original, leaving him with two *shtarot* each attesting that the same borrower owes him the same sum — and the temptation or opportunity to collect twice becomes real. Accordingly, no replacement *shtar* for a loan may be issued.

### Sale Docs Allowed

The *braita* continues: *bamei devarim amurim b'shtarei halvaa* — this rule applies specifically to loan documents. But *aval b'shtarei mekach u'mimkar kosvin chutz min achrayus shebo*: when the lost document was a bill of sale rather than a loan note, a replacement may be written — with one critical omission: the *achrayus* clause must be left out.

To appreciate why this distinction matters, consider the mechanics. If someone purchased a field from a seller and has lost his *shtar*, the core concern — that he owns the field — is not in doubt. Provided the witnesses testify to the original transaction and the replacement *shtar* bears the original date (not today's date, which would invite a separate abuse), what further danger remains? The answer lies in *achrayus*. Every bill of sale carries, explicitly or implicitly, a warranty: if the purchased field is subsequently seized by a creditor of the seller in satisfaction of a prior debt, the buyer is entitled to be reimbursed. Moreover, because the *achrayus* provision creates a lien dating back to the moment of the original sale, the buyer may collect that reimbursement not only from the seller directly but from anyone who subsequently purchased property from the seller — since those later buyers took their property already encumbered by this earlier lien.

The scheme that *achrayus* enables with a duplicate *shtar* runs as follows. Suppose the buyer produces one *shtar* — the replacement — and uses it to collect reimbursement from one of the seller's later buyers, claiming the field was seized from him in payment of the seller's debt. He then produces the original (which he "lost" but in fact retained) and uses it to collect again from a second buyer of the seller's property. The result is double collection from two different parties, each of whom thought he was buying free-and-clear property. To prevent this, the replacement *shtar* is issued without *achrayus*. A *shtar* lacking *achrayus* establishes the buyer's ownership of the field but confers no right of reimbursement whatsoever — *lo mimshabdei v'lo minichsei*, not from encumbered property and not from the seller's own assets. It is purely a title document. As a result, if the buyer were to produce both the original and the replacement, the replacement without *achrayus* would be useless for collecting from anyone, and the fraud is forestalled.

## Achrayus & Liens

### Liens on Sales

To understand how the *achrayus* mechanism actually operates, it is worth tracing the full chain of liability. A seller conveys a field to a buyer with *achrayus*. That *achrayus* places a lien on all property the seller subsequently sells: if the purchased field is one day seized to satisfy a debt the seller owed to some creditor, the buyer may demand reimbursement — and if the seller lacks personal assets, the buyer may seize property from those who bought from the seller after the original transaction, since their property was encumbered from the moment the earlier sale occurred. In practice, this means that the date of the original sale is determinative: any property the seller disposed of after that date is potentially subject to the buyer's lien.

### Double Collect

The double-collection scheme can now be reconstructed in detail. Let us call the original seller Reuven, the original buyer Dov, and the creditor Shimon. Reuven owes Shimon money. Shimon seizes the field Reuven had sold to Dov, in satisfaction of the debt. Dov, armed with his *shtar* containing *achrayus*, now goes to the people who purchased property from Reuven after Dov's original purchase — call them buyer number one and buyer number two — and seeks reimbursement. To do so, Dov must demonstrate to each of these buyers that the field was genuinely seized from him in payment of Reuven's debt; this typically requires Shimon to produce his court-issued authorization and confirm that he did indeed seize the field in satisfaction of a legitimate debt.

If Dov genuinely lost his *shtar* and was issued a replacement also bearing *achrayus*, he is now holding two *shtarot* both bearing the original date and both carrying the insurance clause. He uses the first to collect from buyer number one — a legitimate collection, since the field genuinely was seized. He then approaches Shimon: "Leave the field with me a while longer and we will go through the process again in a few years, and I will collect from buyer number two." After a suitable interval — long enough that courts and parties will have forgotten the earlier proceedings — Shimon again formally seizes the field, and Dov presents his second *shtar* to collect from buyer number two. The fraud succeeds because the two seizures are separated by enough time that nobody connects them.

## Lost Documents Rule…

### RSG Disagrees

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disputes even the leniency permitted by the Sages. *Ve-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer af shtar mecher shemachru ein kosvin* — not even a replacement bill of sale may be issued, even without the *achrayus* clause. His reasoning draws on a further ruling he articulates: *ha-noten matana l'chaveiro ve-hechezir lo es ha-shtar chazra matanaso* — one who gave a gift to another and subsequently returned the original *shtar* to the original owner has effectively reversed the gift. *Aval Chachamim omrim matanaso kayemes* — but the Sages disagree, holding that returning the *shtar* does not reverse the transfer.

### RSG Concern

The logic of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position is as follows. Just as a field may be acquired through the delivery of a *shtar*, so the return of that same *shtar* to the original owner functions as a reversal of the transfer, restoring ownership to him. If this is true, then when a buyer claims he has "lost" his *shtar*, the court cannot dismiss out of hand the possibility that the reason he no longer holds it is that he voluntarily returned it to the seller — thereby reversing the sale and restoring the field to the seller. If we write the buyer a replacement *shtar*, we may be enabling him to fabricate a claim of ownership in a field that is now legitimately the seller's again.

### Tosafos Solution

Tosafos raises a compelling difficulty with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position — one that he himself acknowledges is ripe for abuse. The *halacha* permits a seller to have a *shtar* drawn up by a scribe in anticipation of a sale, and this can be done without the buyer present. It is therefore possible for a seller, before delivering a *shtar* to the buyer, to have gone to one scribe and had one *shtar* prepared, and then gone to a second scribe and had an identical second *shtar* prepared. He delivers the first to the buyer. If the buyer later claims to have lost his *shtar*, the seller — according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — can simply produce the duplicate he had prepared from the second scribe and assert: "The reason the buyer no longer has his *shtar* is that he returned it to me, and I accepted it; that reversed the sale." The buyer cannot disprove this, because the seller is genuinely holding a copy.

The practical upshot, according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, is that the buyer must guard his *shtar* carefully. The Sages, by contrast, hold that returning a *shtar* does not reverse a sale — and therefore even if the seller produces a duplicate, it does not prove that the buyer voluntarily surrendered his ownership.

## Achrayus & Liens…

### Rav Safra Logic

The *Gemara* now turns to unpack the Sages' ruling. *Amar Mar: chutz me'achrayus shebo — mai ta'ama?* What is the precise rationale for disqualifying the *achrayus* clause from a replacement *shtar*? Rav Safra explains: *lefi she'ein kosvin shnei shtarot al sadei achat* — one simply does not write two *shtarot* for the same field both bearing *achrayus*. The concern is the double-collection scheme described above.

### Creditor Role

The mechanics of that scheme require some elaboration. Dov seeks reimbursement from buyer number one by claiming that the field was seized from him in satisfaction of Reuven's debt to Shimon. But buyer number one will not simply take Dov's word for it. He will want confirmation that the field was genuinely seized, that Shimon had legitimate court authorization to seize it, and that Dov is therefore truly entitled to reimbursement. This means Shimon must play a role in the process: he must produce the court documentation establishing his right to seize — the *beis din* authorization — confirming that Reuven failed to pay and that Shimon was entitled to collect from encumbered property. Only once buyer number one is satisfied on all these points will he accept Dov's claim and hand over reimbursement.

Now Dov approaches buyer number two with the second *shtar*. The same evidentiary demand will be made: prove that the field was seized. Dov again needs Shimon to cooperate — to appear or produce documentation confirming a second seizure. This is the scheme's critical vulnerability: Shimon must effectively seize the same field twice.

### Destruction Issue

This is where the scheme faces its most serious structural obstacle. From the moment Shimon moves to seize the field, Reuven — whose debt is being collected — will demand that the original *shtar chov*, the note establishing the debt, be torn up. It makes no sense for Reuven to allow Shimon to seize his property without receiving back the *shtar* that purports to obligate him, lest Shimon collect again later on a document that no longer reflects any outstanding obligation. The *Gemara* therefore asks: once the debt note has been torn up, how can Shimon go through the seizure process a second time? He no longer holds any documentation establishing that Reuven owes him anything.

## Seizure Process

### Tear Up Note

Rav Nachman resolves this by laying down a formal rule about how the seizure process operates: *kol tirfa delo kasiv beh kar'ina leshtara demilve lav tirfa hu* — any seizure document that does not itself state "we have torn up the original debt note" is not a valid seizure document at all. In other words, the court will not issue authorization for Shimon to seize property unless the original *shtar chov* is physically torn up at the moment the authorization is issued. This is a built-in safeguard: the very validity of the seizure documentation is conditioned on the destruction of the underlying debt note, ensuring that the same debt cannot be used to seize twice.

### Three Stages

The process of collecting on a debt through property seizure actually involves three sequential documents, each of which must explicitly reference and supersede the one before it.

The first is the *tirfa*. The *tirfa* is issued by *beis din* in response to the creditor's claim that the debtor has not paid. It authorizes the creditor to go out and identify property — whether still in the debtor's hands or in the hands of those who purchased encumbered property from him — that can satisfy the debt. At the moment the *tirfa* is issued, the original *shtar chov* is torn up; *kol tirfa delo kasiv beh kar'ina leshtara demilve lav tirfa hu*. Any *tirfa* that does not record the destruction of the original debt note is invalid.

The second document is the *adrachta*. Once the creditor has identified a specific piece of property that he wishes to claim, he returns to *beis din* and the *tirfa* is superseded by the *adrachta*, which specifies the particular field in question and formally authorizes seizure of that field. The *adrachta* must itself record that the *tirfa* has been torn up: *kol adrachta delo kasiv beh kar'ina letirfa lav adrachta hi*.

The third document is the *shuma*, the assessment. Even once the creditor holds an *adrachta* authorizing him to take a specific field, it remains to be determined how much of the outstanding debt that field actually satisfies — that is, what the field is worth relative to the total sum owed. The *shuma* is the formal valuation, and it likewise must reference the cancellation of the *adrachta*: *kol shuma delo kasiv beh kar'ina le'adrachta lav shuma hi*. Each stage in the chain therefore renders the prior stage null, ensuring that no document in the sequence can be reused to seize additional property.

### Original Ownership

Given this comprehensive system of document destruction, the *Gemara* asks: how is the double-collection scheme even theoretically possible? Once Shimon has gone through the entire process of seizing the field the first time, every document he held that gave him the right to do so has been torn up. He holds no *shtar chov*, no *tirfa*, no *adrachta*. On what basis could he claim the field a second time?

The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzricha d'ka'asi mi'koach d'avasai* — the concern arises not from a re-seizure on the basis of debt but from a claim of original ownership. Shimon (in our scheme, or whoever is playing his role) does not return as a creditor claiming Reuven owes him money. Instead he returns as a claimant who asserts that this field was always his — that it belonged to his fathers and was wrongfully taken from him, that Reuven essentially stole it. A claim of original ownership does not arise from the debt documentation at all; it is a separate cause of action that requires no *shtar chov* and is not affected by the destruction of the debt-collection documents. If such a claim is established, the field is removed from Dov's possession — and Dov, holding his second *shtar* with *achrayus*, is again entitled to seek reimbursement from buyer number two.

### Same Day Question

Rav Acha of Difti poses a question to Ravina: why bother with the elaborate scheme of waiting years before making the second claim? *V'lama lei l'meimar lei l'ba'al chov shofli v'behai ara v'eikum bah* — why does Dov need to say to Shimon "leave the field with me a while; hold off and then come claim it again in a few years"? Why not simply seize from buyer number one and buyer number two in rapid succession, effectively the same day? *Tipok lei d'keivan d'nakit teri shtarei taraf v'hadar taraf* — if he is holding two *shtarot* he could simply collect twice in immediate succession.

The *Gemara* answers: *im hachi nefishi lei ba'alei dinin* — if both collections happen at the same time or in very close succession, there will be a proliferation of litigants and word will spread quickly. People will discover that the same claimant is simultaneously asserting the field was seized in two different proceedings, and the fraud will be exposed. The scheme therefore depends on temporal separation: collect once, let the matter go quiet, and return years later before a different court that has no knowledge of the earlier proceedings.

## Receipt Solution

### Shovar Proposal

The *Gemara* now turns to whether there is any way to issue a replacement *shtar* with *achrayus* while still protecting the parties at risk. The proposal: *v'lichtov l'hai shtar ma'alya v'lichtov tavra l'mocher* — write the buyer a proper *shtar* with *achrayus*, and simultaneously write the seller a receipt — a *tavra*, or *shovar* — that explicitly invalidates any prior *shtarot*. The text of that receipt would read: *kol shtarei d'yaifku al ar'ah da psulin l'var min d'yaifuk b'zimna da* — any documents purporting to establish ownership of this field are invalid, except for the one issued at this particular time, i.e., the replacement *shtar*. The scheme is then foiled: the original *shtar*, should Dov try to produce it, is explicitly declared null by the seller's receipt. Only the designated replacement is valid.

The proposal also requires thinking through how the earlier scheme involved leapfrogging the seller. The earlier analysis assumed that Dov could proceed directly against buyer number one and buyer number two without involving Reuven at all — Reuven was not party to the scheme, and somehow could be bypassed. This new proposal imagines that Reuven will in fact always be drawn into any proceeding in which Dov attempts to collect, and once Reuven is involved he can produce the receipt that invalidates the original *shtar*. Rather than relying merely on Reuven verbally protesting — where it would be his word against Dov's — the receipt gives him documentation.

### Leapfrog Problem

This proposal was raised before Rav Pappa and before Rav Ashi. The *Gemara* rejects it, and the rejection turns precisely on the leapfrog problem. *D'dilma azil ba'al chov v'taref minei d'lokeach* — what happens if Dov simply goes directly to buyer number one and seizes from him without involving Reuven at all? Buyer number one, if he purchased his field with *achrayus*, will then want to seek reimbursement from Reuven, and Reuven will produce the receipt — but in the interim Dov has already seized the property. Moreover — and this is the *Gemara*'s more pointed objection — *v'shover gabi lekuchos leika*: the receipt is in the seller's possession, not in the possession of the buyers. The buyers do not have the receipt. If Dov can get to buyer number one or buyer number two before Reuven is pulled into the picture, the receipt does them no good.

The *Gemara* then anticipates an objection: surely the buyers will eventually bring the seller in, and once he is in the proceedings he will produce the receipt, the improper collection will be reversed, and Dov will be forced to return whatever he took. The *Gemara* dismisses this with a practical observation: *ad'hachi v'hachi shamit v'achil peiri* — in the meantime, while all the litigation is grinding forward, Dov has been living on that land or consuming its produce for years. By the time the matter is sorted out and a reversal ordered, those benefits have already been consumed and may be practically unrecoverable. The receipt mechanism is therefore not a satisfactory solution.

### Loan vs Land

One might press the point further: can the buyer simply leapfrog the seller in the first place? If someone purchased fields from the seller and has *achrayus*, are they not in a position to say — before handing over anything — "prove to us that you first exhausted your claim against the seller directly"? And if they insist on that, the seller would be involved from the outset, and the receipt would become effective. This question is not left unasked. The *Gemara* addresses it by distinguishing between loan cases and land cases.

In the loan case — where a creditor holds a *shtar chov* against a borrower, collects from the borrower, and then the borrower seeks to give him a receipt rather than getting the *shtar* back — the *Gemara* explains that the buyers of the borrower's property will naturally insist on involving the borrower before they yield their land. They will say: you are owed money; the primary obligor is the borrower; we are only a backup. Show us the borrower actually cannot pay before you come after our land. This forces the borrower into the proceedings, he produces the receipt, and the system functions.

### Land Not Cash

Here, however, the situation differs because what Dov is owed is not money but land. *Mi yad'ei de-man de-massik ara bezuzi lo m'fayis* — it is understood that a person whose entitlement is to land will not be satisfied with a monetary payment in lieu. The buyers therefore may recognize that they cannot simply point Dov to the seller and say "have him pay you in cash." Dov is owed land specifically, and that is why he may proceed directly against them. This is what enables the leapfrog, at least in the *Gemara*'s framing — though Tosafos himself raises the question sharply: if the seller does in fact have money, why should the buyers not insist that the seller pay first and thereby keep themselves out of the proceeding? If the seller can pay, why would the buyers allow their fields to be taken without first forcing the seller to be involved? Tosafos acknowledges the difficulty without resolving it fully, noting that the *Gemara*'s position seems to be that a person owed land may simply proceed against the encumbered property without first going to the seller, even if the seller might have assets. The matter remains somewhat difficult.

In any event, the conclusion stands: the *shovar* mechanism does not solve the problem, and therefore there is no way to issue a replacement *shtar* with *achrayus*. If the buyer loses his *shtar*, he may receive a replacement — but it will carry no *achrayus*. That is the price of carelessness with one's documents.

## Shtar Lo B'Achrayus

### Rava Default

The *Gemara* now turns to the broader question of what it means, in ordinary transactions, for a *shtar* to be written without *achrayus*. The question pivots on whether the absence of an explicit *achrayus* clause is itself meaningful or whether *achrayus* is always presumed as the default.

### Rav Ashi View

Rava and Rav Ashi dispute this directly. Rava holds: *achrayus ta'os sofer hu* — the omission of *achrayus* from a *shtar* is treated as a scribal error. *Achrayus* is always assumed to be intended; if it does not appear in the document it is because it was inadvertently left out, not because the parties wished to exclude it. Therefore, even a *shtar* that lacks an explicit *achrayus* clause is treated as if it contains one. Rav Ashi disagrees: *achrayus lav ta'os sofer hu* — the absence of *achrayus* is not a scribal error. If it was not written, it was not intended, and the *shtar* genuinely lacks warranty.

### Woman Story

An instructive case is recorded. A woman gave money to an agent to go purchase land on her behalf. The agent went and bought the land — but the *shtar* he obtained was written *shelo be'achrayus*, explicitly excluding the warranty. The woman came before Rav Nachman.

### Explicit Exclusion

Rav Nachman's position — consistent with his ruling that the *braita*'s replacement *shtar* must explicitly say there is no warranty, which itself proves that warranty is the default — is that the agent acted improperly. He was dispatched to act in the woman's best interest, and purchasing land without *achrayus* is simply not in anyone's best interest; it is never an acceptable outcome even if the agent secured a lower price, because the insurance on the land is a fundamental part of what the buyer needs.

### Nachman Solution

Rav Nachman does not, however, simply declare the original sale void and direct the parties to renegotiate from scratch. His remedy is more targeted: *zil zavna minei shelo be'achrayus vehadar zavnah nihala be'achrayus* — go buy the land from the original seller in your own name without *achrayus* (at the lower price the seller is willing to accept), and then turn around and sell it to the woman with *achrayus*. In other words, the agent must now personally absorb the risk: he buys the land without warranty and then resells it to the woman with full warranty, effectively providing the insurance himself out of his own obligation. This is a striking outcome — in effect, the agent is being penalized by being forced to stand behind the warranty personally — but it follows from the principle that an agent who acts outside the scope of his authorization cannot impose the consequences of that unauthorized act on his principal. This raises a broader point that recurs throughout the *Gemara*: the doctrine that a *shaliach* who acts contrary to the *meshale'ach*'s interests renders the *shlichus* void is in tension with the practical expectation that transactions conducted through agents will be stable. But that tension is not resolved here.

## Shtar Return Issue

### RSG Reversal

The *Gemara* returns to the dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Sages regarding the effect of returning a *shtar*. *Ve-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer ha-nosen matana l'chaveiro ve-hechezir lo es ha-shtar chazra matanaso* — if one gives a gift to another and the recipient returns the *shtar* to the giver, the gift is reversed.

### Chachamim No Reversal

*Aval Chachamim omrim matanaso kayemes* — the Sages hold that the return of the *shtar* does not reverse the gift or sale. Ownership, once transferred, is not undone by the mere physical return of the document.

### Conditional Theory

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama de-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — what is the reasoning behind his position? Rav Assi suggests: *na'aseh ke-omer lo sade zu netuna lecha kol zman she-ha-shtar beyadecha* — the sale or gift is implicitly conditioned on the recipient holding the *shtar*. The transfer is, in effect, valid only so long as the recipient retains the document; returning it dissolves the condition and undoes the transfer.

### Rava Objection

Rava objects sharply: *i hachi nignav o avad nami* — if the ownership is conditioned on holding the *shtar*, then if the *shtar* is stolen or lost, the sale would equally be reversed. That is clearly an unacceptable result. Nobody would agree that losing a document voids a completed property transfer. Rava therefore rejects Rav Assi's explanation and offers his own.

## Kinyan by Mesira

### Mesira Transfer

Rava's alternative explanation reframes the entire dispute in terms of a fundamental *machloket* in the law of acquisition: *be-osiyos niknos be-mesira kamiflagi* — the underlying disagreement is whether the content of a *shtar* — the *osiyos*, the letters — can be transferred through *mesira*, the act of physically handing over the document. This principle is familiar in the context of transferring a debt: if Reuven holds a *shtar* establishing that Shimon owes him money, can Reuven sell that debt to a third party simply by handing over the *shtar*? The question is whether *osiyos niknos be-mesira* — whether the letters that constitute the legal content of the document are acquired by the very act of having the document handed to you.

### Rava Position

Rava holds that *osiyos niknos be-mesira*: yes, handing over the *shtar* does transfer what the *shtar* represents. This principle applies in the context of debt transfer, where the question is whether simply delivering the *shtar chov* to a new holder suffices to transfer the claim to that holder, or whether a new *shtar* must be written in the new holder's name.

### Return Property

The extension to *karka* is direct. If a seller once used a *shtar* as the instrument of *kinyan* to transfer a field to a buyer — delivery of the *shtar* being the very act that completed the buyer's acquisition — then the return of that same *shtar* to the seller operates as another *mesira*, another delivery. Just as the original delivery of the *shtar* from seller to buyer transferred the field to the buyer, the return of that *shtar* from buyer to seller transfers it back. The buyer, by handing the document back, is in effect re-executing the *kinyan* in the seller's favor. This is the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel according to Rava's analysis.

### Chachamim Pos

The Sages, by contrast, hold *ein osiyos niknos be-mesira* — the letters are not acquired through the mere act of handing over the document. A *shtar* for *karka* is the instrument of *kinyan* when executed as such, but the subsequent physical transfer of that document back and forth does not operate as a new *kinyan* with each exchange. Ownership, once vested, does not shift based on who is physically holding the *shtar* at any given moment. Accordingly, returning the *shtar* to the seller does not reverse the sale, and the Sages rule that the gift or sale remains in force. The discussion of this foundational *machloket* — and its many ramifications for the law of document transfer — will continue on the following *daf*.
