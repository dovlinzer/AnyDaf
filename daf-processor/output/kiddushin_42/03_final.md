# Kiddushin 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sources of Shlichut

### Three Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּיחֵת עֲשָׂרָה אוֹ הוֹסִיף עֲשָׂרָה – תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה. מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּיָלֵיף מִגֵּירוּשִׁין – מָה לְגֵירוּשִׁין, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן חוֹל! אָמַר קְרָא: ״אַתֶּם״, ״גַּם אַתֶּם״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשָּׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the agent **subtracted ten** from the denominator and separated one-fortieth **or added ten** to the denominator and separated one-sixtieth, thereby giving more or less than the owner intended, **his *teruma* is *teruma*. From where do we** derive that one can appoint an agent to separate *teruma*? **And if you would say that** it **is derived from divorce,** one could argue: **What** is an aspect unique **to divorce is that it is** considered **a non-sacred** matter in relation to *teruma* and will have different *halakhot*, so one cannot learn from divorce that one can appoint an agent to separate *teruma*. The Gemara answers: The verse states with regard to *teruma*: "So you also shall set apart a gift unto the Lord of all your tithes" (Numbers 18:28). Once the **verse states "you,"** the addition of the word "also" in the term **"you also"** serves **to include an agent.**

The present daf deals with the institution of *shlichut* — agency — and three distinct biblical sources from which it is derived. The first is the law of *terumah*, where the verse reads *gam atem*, "even you," which is understood to include a *shaliach*. The second is *gerushin* (divorce), where the verse *veshilach veshilchah mibeito* — literally, "he shall send her out of his house" — is read as authorizing the appointment of an agent. The third is *korban Pesach*, where the verse states *veshachatu oto kol kehal adat Yisrael bein ha'arbayim*, "and the entire congregation of Israel shall slaughter it at dusk." Since every individual is obligated and yet every individual plainly did not perform the *shechita*, the obvious inference is that one person slaughtered on behalf of each household — a paradigmatic act of agency.

The *gemara* had already observed, on the previous daf, that two of these three sources — *korban Pesach* and *gerushin*/*kiddushin* — together suffice to establish *shlichut* across the full range of *halacha*. From *kodshim* alone one could not learn *gerushin*, since *gerushin* operates through thought (intention), whereas *kodshim* requires a physical act. And from *gerushin* alone one could not learn *kodshim*, for the reverse reason. By anchoring the principle in both extremes, everything between them is included. The question that follows, then, is why the Torah also needs the verse in *terumah*.

### Why Terumah Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּתְרוּמָה, וְנֵיתוֹ הָנָךְ וְנִגְמְרוּ מִינֵּיהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִפְרַךְ: שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָהּ בְּמַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let the Merciful One write in** the passage concerning ***teruma*** that one can appoint an agent, **and** the *halakha* that one can appoint an agent to act on his behalf in **these** other matters, i.e., divorce and betrothal, **can come and be derived from it.** The Gemara answers: Agency in these matters cannot be derived in this manner **because** the derivation **can be refuted:** Separating *teruma* is different, in **that it is** able to be separated **by** mere **thought.** It is enough for one to decide that a certain portion of his produce shall be *teruma* to have that status take effect. Just as there is this leniency unique to separating *teruma*, perhaps the leniency that enables one to appoint an agent is also unique to separating *teruma*.

The *gemara* picks up on 41b with the question: *ve'ella atem gam atem lama li* — why is the phrase *atem gam atem* in the *terumah* context needed at all, if *shlichut* can already be derived from *korban Pesach* and *gerushin*?

### Gam Atem - Like You

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא ״אַתֶּם״ ״גַּם אַתֶּם״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יַנַּאי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי ״גַּם אַתֶּם״ – מָה אַתֶּם בְּנֵי בְּרִית, אַף שְׁלוּחֲכֶם בְּנֵי בְּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** then **why do I** need the derivation that once the verse states **"you,"** the addition of the word "also" in the term **"you also"** serves to include an agent? The Gemara answers: It **is necessary to** function as the source of **the** statement **of Rabbi Yannai, as Rabbi Yannai says** that from the term **"you also,"** the following is derived: **Just as you,** those who appoint agents, **are members of the covenant,** i.e., Jews, **so too, your agents** must be **members of the covenant.** A gentile cannot separate *teruma* even if appointed as an agent by a Jew.

The *gemara* answers by invoking the teaching of Rabbi Yannai: *gam atem* does not come merely to affirm that a *shaliach* can separate *terumah*. That much might be derived elsewhere. Rather, it comes to define the parameters of a valid *shaliach* — specifically, to require that a *shaliach* be *like you*. As the *gemara* formulates it: *mah atem bnei berit af shluchaichon bnei berit* — just as you are members of the covenant, so too must your agent be a member of the covenant. A non-Jew cannot serve as one's *shaliach*. The verse teaches not the existence of *shlichut* but its qualifications.

### Learning From Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לְמָה לִי קְרָא? מִדְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן נָפְקָא! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הָעֶבֶד נַעֲשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל גֵּט מִיַּד בַּעְלָהּ שֶׁל אִשָּׁה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּתוֹרַת גִּיטִּין וְקִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **a verse** to teach **this** *halakha*? **It** is **derived from that** which **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A slave cannot become an agent to receive a bill of divorce from the hand of a woman's husband** on her behalf **because he is not** included **in the laws of bills of divorce and betrothal.** This indicates that there is a principle that if certain matters do not apply to a person, he cannot act as an agent with regard to those matters. Therefore, since gentiles are not commanded to separate *teruma*, they cannot be agents for its separation.

The *gemara* then pushes further: why is a verse even necessary to exclude a non-Jew? Cannot this be derived through logic, from the principle stated by Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan? Rabbi Yochanan taught that a slave cannot serve as a *shaliach* to receive a *get* on behalf of a woman from her husband, *lefi she'eino betorat gittin vekiddushin* — because the laws of marriage and divorce simply do not apply to him. The principle is that one cannot serve as an agent in a legal domain that is categorically inapplicable to oneself.

This principle readily explains the exclusion of a slave from *gittin* and *kiddushin*, since those laws do not govern a slave at all. And yet a slave is nonetheless *bnei berit* — he is within the covenant, partially obligated in *mitzvot*, and could in principle serve as an agent in other contexts. He can, for instance, separate *terumot u'maasrot* on behalf of someone or conduct a commercial transaction as a *shaliach*. The category-exclusion rule of Rabbi Yochanan thus accounts for why a slave cannot represent a woman in the receipt of a *get*, without requiring a special verse.

### Non-Jew Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: עֶבֶד, דְּלָאו בַּר הֶיתֵּירָא הוּא כְּלָל. אֲבָל נׇכְרִי, הוֹאִיל וְאִיתֵיהּ בִּתְרוּמָה דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ, דִּתְנַן: הַנׇּכְרִי וְהַכּוּתִי שֶׁתָּרְמוּ – תְּרוּמָתָן תְּרוּמָה, אֵימָא שָׁלִיחַ נָמֵי עָבֵיד, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was** nevertheless **necessary** to derive this *halakha* for the case of *teruma*, because it cannot be derived from the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan. This is as it might **enter your mind to say:** It is **a slave** who cannot serve as an agent to receive a bill of divorce, **since he cannot release** a woman by divorce **at all; but a gentile, since he is included in his own *teruma*, as we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 3:9): **A gentile or a Samaritan who separated *teruma*** from their own produce, **their *teruma* is considered *teruma*,** despite the fact that they are not obligated to do so, **I will say** that he can **also be** appointed as an **agent.** To counter this argument, the term "you also" **teaches us** that only members of the covenant can act as agents for separating *teruma*; gentiles cannot.

Given this logical principle, why would a verse be required to exclude a non-Jew from serving as a *shaliach* for *terumah*? A non-Jew is not subject to the laws of *terumah*, so surely the same logic applies: since *terumah* does not apply to him, he cannot be an agent for it. The *gemara* responds that the situation is actually more nuanced. Unlike the slave by *gittin*, a non-Jew is not categorically outside the orbit of *terumah*. The *mishnah* teaches: *ha'oved kochavim veha'kuti she'tarmu terumatan terumah* — if a non-Jew or a Samaritan separated *terumah*, that separation is halachically valid. Rashi notes that this assumes the non-Jew's ownership and processing of the grain do not exempt it from the obligation; but granted those assumptions, a non-Jew can in fact validly separate *terumah*. Since a non-Jew is thus "in the category" of *terumah* in some sense, one might conclude that he can also serve as a *shaliach* for *terumah* — *eima shaliach nami avid*. The verse therefore comes to exclude him.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּפָטַר, דִּתְנַן: תְּרוּמַת נׇכְרִי מְדַמַּעַת וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ חוֹמֶשׁ וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, ״אַתֶּם״ ״גַּם אַתֶּם״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to Rabbi Shimon, who exempts** one from paying an additional penalty for the *teruma* of a gentile, this explanation cannot be stated. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 3:9): If **the *teruma* of a gentile** gets mixed with regular produce, it is considered to be **a mixture of** *teruma* **and non-sacred** produce, **and** if a non-priest eats this mixture unwittingly **he is liable** to pay **for it** an additional **one-fifth** beyond its value, just as is the *halakha* with regard to one who unwittingly eats *teruma*. **But Rabbi Shimon exempts** him from paying the additional one-fifth, as he holds that the *teruma* of a gentile possesses no sanctity and is considered a mere gift to a priest. If so, if there is no need to learn the additional *halakha* that a gentile cannot act as an agent with regard to the separating of *teruma*, **why do I** need the derivation from **"you"** and **"you also"?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר מָר: ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא אֲרִיסִין, ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא שׁוּתָּפִין, ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא הַתּוֹרֵם אֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, אֵימָא: ״אַתֶּם״ – וְלֹא שְׁלוּחֲכֶם נָמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** to state this additional term, as it might **enter your mind to say: Since the Master says** that the word "you" is interpreted as a limitation, it means that **"you," but not sharecroppers,** can separate *teruma*, as a sharecropper cannot separate *teruma* from the produce of the owner of the field; **"you"** can separate *teruma* **but not partners** from shared property without the consent of the other; **"you"** can separate *teruma* **but not a steward [*apotropos*]** from the estate that he is administering; and **"you"** can separate *teruma* **but not one who separates** *teruma* **from** produce **that is not his,** you might **also say: "You"** can separate *teruma* **and not your agents.** The verse therefore **teaches us** that "you also" indicates that one can appoint an agent to separate *teruma*.

The *gemara*'s resolution points toward a dual requirement. There are in fact two distinct conditions for valid *shlichut*: first, the *shaliach* must be Jewish — he must be *bnei berit* — even if the relevant domain of law applies to him in some form (as with the non-Jew and *terumah*, or with commercial transactions generally). Second, even if the *shaliach* is *bnei berit* and fully within the covenant, he cannot serve as agent in a legal domain categorically inapplicable to him — as with the slave and *gittin*. The verse *gam atem* establishes the first condition; the logical principle of Rabbi Yochanan establishes the second.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, דְּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לְהַאי קְרָא לִדְרָשָׁא אַחֲרִינָא, מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁכׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל כּוּלָּן יוֹצְאִים
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question with regard to the basic derivation of the *halakha* of agency: **This works out well** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa,** who explains that the verse: "And the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall slaughter it in the afternoon" (Exodus 12:6), teaches the *halakha* of agency with regard to offerings. **But** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan, who derives a different exposition from this verse, from where do we** derive the *halakha* of agency with regard to offerings? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yonatan says: From where do we** derive that **all of the Jews can fulfill** their obligations

The *gemara* notes that according to Rabbi Shimon, this entire construction collapses. Rabbi Shimon holds that when a non-Jew separates *terumah*, the separation has no legal status — it is not *terumah*. The *mishnah* records the dispute: the *tanna kamma* holds that the *terumah* of a non-Jew causes *dima'a* (if mixed into other produce, it confers upon it the restricted status of *terumah*) and one who eats it inadvertently must pay *chomesh*; Rabbi Shimon exempts. According to Rabbi Shimon, a non-Jew is simply outside the category of *terumah*, and there would be no basis for thinking he could serve as a *shaliach* for it. So why is *gam atem* needed?

The *gemara* explains that even according to Rabbi Shimon, the verse serves a necessary function — albeit a different one — which leads into the next discussion.

## Word 'Atem' Exclusions

### Atem Excludes

The *gemara* picks up on the word *atem* in the phrase *gam atem*. Before the word *gam* appears — which the *gemara* uses to include a *shaliach* — there is the word *atem*, "you," which functions as a limiting term. The *gemara* records: *atem ve'lo arisim, atem ve'lo shutafin, atem ve'lo apitropus* — "you" and not sharecroppers, "you" and not partners, "you" and not an overseer. None of these individuals can separate *terumah* on behalf of the owner. A sharecropper can separate on his own portion, not on the owner's. A partner can separate on his own share, not his partner's. An *apitropus* — a guardian or overseer, including one appointed over orphans — lacks the authority to separate *terumah* on behalf of the principal. The word *atem* similarly excludes *hatoreim terumah chaveiro* — someone who simply separates another person's *terumah* without authorization.

All of these are individuals who have some relationship or connection to the owner or the property, yet are not the owner himself and not his appointed agent.

### Question on Shaliach

This raises a natural difficulty. If the word *atem* is so restrictive — excluding sharecroppers, partners, overseers, and others who have genuine connections to the property — one might conclude that a *shaliach* too is excluded. After all, a *shaliach* is not the owner himself; *atem* would seem to indicate that only the owner may act.

### Gam Includes Shaliach

The word *gam* — "even" — resolves this. *Gam atem*, "even you," extends the category to include a *shaliach*. The verse does two things simultaneously: through *atem* it restricts to the principal and excludes various connected but unauthorized parties; through *gam* it expands to include the one person who is authorized — the *shaliach*. This is the second explanation of why *gam atem* is needed even given the other biblical sources for *shlichut*: without it, one might have excluded the *shaliach* from *terumah* precisely because the word *atem* emphasizes the personal nature of the act.

## Korban Pesach Shlichut

### Rabbi Yonatan's Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפֶסַח אֶחָד? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁחֲטוּ אֹתוֹ כֹּל קְהַל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״, וְכִי כׇּל הַקָּהָל כּוּלָּם שׁוֹחֲטִים? וַהֲלֹא אֵינוֹ שׁוֹחֵט אֶלָּא אֶחָד! אֶלָּא מִכָּאן שֶׁכׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹצְאִים בְּפֶסַח אֶחָד – שָׁלִיחַ בְּקָדָשִׁים מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **with one Paschal offering?** Although it is impossible for all of the Jewish people to each eat an olive-bulk from one offering, they nevertheless fulfill their obligation to sacrifice the Paschal offering by sacrificing one animal, **as it is stated** with regard to the Paschal offering: **"And the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall slaughter it in the afternoon"** (Exodus 12:6). **Is it so that the whole assembly slaughters** it? **But only one** person from each group **slaughters. Rather,** it can be derived **from here that all of the Jews can fulfill** their obligations **with one Paschal** offering, despite the fact that not everyone will be able to eat from it. Since Rabbi Yonatan derives from this verse that one offering suffices, **from where does he** derive the *halakha* of **agency with regard to offerings?**

The *gemara* now takes up a difficulty that arises from a different direction. It was assumed above that the *korban Pesach* verse — *veshachatu oto kol kehal adat Yisrael* — teaches the principle of *shlichut* in the domain of *kodshim* (sacred things). But this works only according to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who reads that verse as establishing agency for the *shechita*. According to Rabbi Yonatan, that same verse is expended on an entirely different law, and cannot simultaneously teach *shlichut*.

The *baraisa* records: Rabbi Yonatan asks, how do we know that in principle all of Israel can fulfill their obligation through a single *korban Pesach*? He answers from the same verse — *veshachatu oto kol kehal adat Yisrael* — noting that the verse speaks of all of Israel slaughtering "it" (singular), implying that one sacrifice could serve the entire nation. The implication is that not every individual needs to eat a *kezayis* in order to be *yotzei*, since if all of Israel shared one small lamb, most would receive far less than a *kezayis*.

This is not the standard view, which holds that the entire purpose of *korban Pesach* — unlike other sacrifices, whose primary end is the *mizbeach* — is the eating. Accordingly, not eating a *kezayis* would normally render one not *yotzei*. (Indeed, the unique status of *korban Pesach* as an eating-focused sacrifice is evident in the law that if it is offered in a state of *tumah*, it is also eaten in *tumah*, an exception made precisely because eating is its essential purpose.) Rabbi Yonatan nonetheless derives from the verse that even a *kezayis* is not strictly required. The *gemara* opens 42a with his formulation: *minalan shekol Yisrael kulan yotzin b'Pesach echad*, "how do we know that all of Israel can fulfill their obligation through one *Pesach*?" — and answers from this verse.

### No Shlichut From Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** – מִינֵּיהּ. וְדִילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִית לֵיהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: He derives it **from that** very same verse, as it can be seen that one person slaughters the animal on behalf of the rest of the assembly. The Gemara rejects this: **But perhaps it is different there, since** the one slaughtering the animal **has partnership with them** in the offering. This does not prove that there is agency when the agent has no share in the offering.

If that is what the verse teaches — that all of Israel can share one *korban Pesach* and one need not eat a *kezayis* — then the verse has been "used" by Rabbi Yonatan for that *derashah*, and cannot simultaneously serve as the basis for *shlichut* by *kodshim*. The *gemara* therefore asks: *shaliach dikdash minalei* — from where, according to Rabbi Yonatan, do we derive that *shlichut* applies to sacred matters? We know it from *gerushin*, we know it from *terumah*, but those do not establish the principle for *kodshim*. *Kodshim* is the most stringent domain; if we lack a source there, the principle of agency may not apply to it at all.

### Scene Shows Shlichut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״וְיִקְחוּ לָהֶם אִישׁ שֶׂה לְבֵית אָבֹת שֶׂה לַבָּיִת״. וְדִילְמָא הָתָם נָמֵי, דְּאִית לֵיהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ? אִם כֵּן תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי לְמָה לִי? אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְהֵיכָא דְּשָׁיֵיךְ תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לְהֵיכָא דְּלָא שָׁיֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** he derives agency with regard to offerings **from here: "And they shall take to them every man a lamb, according to their fathers' houses, a lamb for a household"** (Exodus 12:3). This demonstrates that one person takes a lamb and slaughters it on behalf of the entire family. The Gemara asks: **But perhaps there too** there is agency **since** the one slaughtering the animal **has partnership with them?** The Gemara rejects this: **If so, why do I** need **two verses** to teach the same *halakha*? **If** the *halakha* stated in this verse **is not applicable for where it belongs,** i.e., with regard to an agent who is a partner in the offering, **apply it to where it does not belong,** so that even one who does not have a share in the offering can act as an agent.

The *gemara* answers: *minei* — you learn it from the case itself. Even if the stated *point* of the verse is to teach that one need not eat a *kezayis*, the verse nonetheless *describes* a scenario in which all of Israel own a single lamb and one person performs the *shechita* on behalf of everyone. That is a real instance of *shlichut* depicted in the text, even if it is not the *derasha*'s primary teaching. The *scenario* itself demonstrates the concept of agency in *kodshim*.

## Korban Pesach Shlichut…

### Co-Ownership Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״אִישׁ״ זוֹכֶה, וְלֹא הַקָּטָן זוֹכֶה. הָהוּא מֵ״אִישׁ לְפִי אׇכְלוֹ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **This** verse **is required for him to** teach another *halakha*, **that of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** This verse is the source for the *halakha* that **a man,** i.e., an adult, **can acquire** an item on behalf of others, **but** that **a minor cannot acquire** an item on behalf of others. The Gemara answers: **That** *halakha*, that only an adult can acquire an item on behalf of others, **is derived from** the verse: **"According to every man's eating** you shall make your count for the lamb" (Exodus 12:4). By employing the term "man," which indicates an adult, the verse teaches that only an adult can acquire an item on behalf of others.

The *gemara* rejects this solution: *vidilma shani hatam de'isai shutfus begavaihu* — perhaps there it is different because everyone has a partnership, a shared ownership, in the *korban Pesach*. When all members of a household jointly own the same lamb, the fact that one person performs the *shechita* on behalf of all the others does not prove anything about general *shlichut*. The co-owners are already united in a single enterprise; one of them representing the group follows naturally from that shared ownership. This is fundamentally different from a situation in which a wholly unrelated individual is appointed as one's agent — where there is no pre-existing connection or joint stake — and then acts on the principal's behalf.

*Tosafot* raises an obvious question: why did the *gemara* not ask this objection earlier, on the previous daf, when it used the *korban Pesach* as a source for *shlichut* without qualification? *Tosafot* answers that the discussion on the previous daf was not positing that all of Israel owned one *korban*. Rather, it was describing the standard practice: each family or group brings their own *korban*, and a single designated *shochet* performs the *shechita* for every group — you bring me your *korban* and I will slaughter it, and you bring me yours and I will slaughter it. In that scenario, the *shochet* does not share ownership with any of the groups for whom he slaughters. That is genuine *shlichut* even without a co-ownership connection. It is only here, where the *gemara* is speaking about all of Israel sharing one lamb, that the objection of *shutfus* — joint ownership as a potentially sufficient explanation — becomes relevant.

This discussion touches on a deeper conceptual distinction about the nature of agency. The kind of representation that flows from co-ownership or joint enterprise creates what might be called a corporate identity: the group is a single unit, and one member acts as the mouthpiece or representative of that unit. This is categorically different from the classical model of *shlichut*, in which an individual appoints an unrelated third party as his personal agent and the agent's act is legally attributed to the principal.

### Two Psukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִידָּךְ: הֲרֵי לְךָ כְּחֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן, מָה חֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן – אֵין אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו, אַף אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי – אִי אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If both verses (the one about *shechita* and the one about purchase) describe only cases of pre-existing connection and shared enterprise, why are two verses needed? If neither teaches anything beyond what the other already teaches by virtue of the *shutfus* rationale, one would be superfluous. The extra verse must therefore be doing additional work — teaching that *shlichut* applies even in the absence of any special connection or co-ownership.

The *gemara* then considers whether a different verse from the *korban Pesach* narrative can provide the required source. The purchase of the *korban Pesach* is described as well: *veyikchu lahem ish seh l'veit avot* — one person takes a lamb on behalf of the household. Here, before the purchase is made, no one yet has joint ownership; the joint ownership only comes into being *through* the act of acquisition. So this person is acquiring on behalf of everyone before anyone has become a co-owner, which looks more like genuine *shlichut*.

But the *gemara* presses the same objection: *vedilma hatam nami de'islei shutfei begavaihu* — perhaps even there, since these are all members of the same household and are all ultimately going to become co-owners together in this enterprise, their shared connection is sufficient to explain the representation without requiring a general principle of *shlichut*.

The *gemara* responds with a different argument: *im ken trei kraei lama li* — if both verses (the one about *shechita* and the one about purchase) describe only cases of pre-existing connection and shared enterprise, why are two verses needed? If neither teaches anything beyond what the other already teaches by virtue of the *shutfus* rationale, one would be superfluous. The extra verse must therefore be doing additional work — teaching that *shlichut* applies even in the absence of any special connection or co-ownership. However, the *gemara* immediately deflects this argument as well: *ki dehachi*, the purchase verse is not superfluous, because it is needed for the teaching of Rabbi Yitzchak.

## Shlichut Models

### Co-Owner Model

The co-ownership problem discussed above reflects a broader conceptual distinction between two models of representation. When a group of people share ownership of an object and one member of the group acts on behalf of all — performing the *shechita*, conducting the negotiation, making the decision — that individual is not necessarily functioning as a personal agent of each member individually. Rather, the group constitutes a single unit, and the person is the operative arm of that unit. The force of the act derives from the group's collective ownership and shared stake. This explains why one cannot derive general *shlichut* from the *korban Pesach* scenario: the case may merely illustrate how a joint enterprise operates, not how an individual appoints a stranger to act on his behalf.

### Shliach Tzibbur Model

This same conceptual distinction arises in a striking context discussed here. The only place in all of the *mishnah* where the principle *shlucho shel adam k'moso* — a person's agent is like himself — is stated explicitly is the *mishnah* in *Brachot* concerning the *shliach tzibbur*. And yet the *shliach tzibbur* presents an apparent paradox: the *gemara* elsewhere holds that one must pray for oneself and cannot discharge one's obligation through another's prayer. If a person simply stands and listens while the *shliach tzibbur* recites *Shemoneh Esrei*, intending to be *yotzei* through him as one would be *yotzei* through a personal agent, many authorities hold that this does not work. How, then, did the institution of *shliach tzibbur* function historically — particularly for those who were illiterate and could not pray independently?

The resolution is that the *shliach tzibbur* does not function as a personal agent of each individual congregant. He is not discharging each person's individual obligation as though he were that person's *shaliach*. Rather, the congregation as a whole constitutes a single corporate entity, and the *shliach tzibbur* is the voice of that entity — the mouthpiece through which the community as a single unit addresses God. He represents not each individual but the *tzibbur* itself.

### Silent vs. Repeated

This distinction corresponds to the difference between the silent *Shemoneh Esrei* and its repetition. The silent *Shemoneh Esrei* is *tefilla b'tzibbur* — an individual praying in the presence of a congregation. Each person is fulfilling a personal obligation as an individual, albeit within a communal setting. The repetition of *Shemoneh Esrei*, by contrast, is *tefilat ha'tzibbur* — the prayer *of* the congregation. The individual is no longer acting as an individual at that point; the community as a collective unit is praying through its representative. This is precisely the model that illuminates why the *korban Pesach* scenario may not prove general *shlichut*: *isley shutfei begavaihu*, they all share ownership of the sheep, and the one who performs the *shechita* represents the entire household as a single unit. *Sha l'veit avot* — a lamb for a household — means everyone has joint ownership, and one person is the operative arm of that household as a unit. This says nothing about how one individual unconnected to another may appoint that other as his personal agent.

## Iyakchu Lahem Ish

### Purchase on Behalf

Having exhausted the *shechita* verse, the *gemara* turns to the purchase verse: *veyikchu lahem ish seh l'veit avot* — one person takes a lamb on behalf of the household. The argument is that before the purchase, there is no joint ownership; the person is acquiring on behalf of others, which looks like genuine *shlichut*.

### Connection Objection

The *gemara* counters, as noted above, that even this case may be explainable through the lens of shared enterprise and pre-existing connection: perhaps they all gave him the money, perhaps they are already bound together as a household unit, and the representation flows from that shared context rather than from a general principle of *shlichut*.

### Why Two Psukim

If both verses — the *shechita* verse and the purchase verse — are limited to cases with pre-existing connection, neither would prove general *shlichut*. And yet two verses have been written. One of them, on this reading, would be entirely superfluous. That superfluity should be redirected to teach the general principle of *shlichut* even without connection. But the *gemara* closes off this move.

### Superfluous Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִידָּךְ: הֲרֵי לְךָ כְּחֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן, מָה חֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן – אֵין אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו, אַף אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי – אִי אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: *hahu me'ish lefi ochlo nafka* — the exclusion of minors from representing others in the acquisition of the *korban Pesach* is derived from the phrase *ish lefi ochlo* ("each man according to his eating"), where *ish* — man — already implies that a *katan* cannot take possession for others. If the exclusion of minors is derived from that *ish*, then the *ish* in *veyikchu lahem ish seh l'veit avot* is not needed for that purpose and is superfluous — and that superfluity is redirected to teach general *shlichut*. Without a second source for the *katan* exclusion, however, there would be no superfluity in the purchase verse, and no basis for deriving *shlichut* from it.

### Rabbi Yitzchak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִידָּךְ: הֲרֵי לְךָ כְּחֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן, מָה חֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן – אֵין אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו, אַף אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי – אִי אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו. מַאי טַעְמָא – מוֹרֵד בַּמַּלְכוּת הֲוָה, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״וַאדֹנִי יוֹאָב וְעַבְדֵי אֲדֹנִי עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה חֹנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzchak had stated the principle: *ish zocheh ve'lo katan zocheh* — a man can acquire possession, but a minor cannot. The *gemara* invokes the teaching of Rabbi Yitzchak in connection with the purchase verse. *Tosafot* raises the question: why does one need a verse specifically here, by *korban Pesach*, to teach that a minor cannot acquire? This is a general principle — *ein yad le'katan*, a minor has no legal capacity for *kinyan*. *Tosafot* answers: by *korban Pesach* one might have thought it was different. A minor can eat from the *korban Pesach* — *salka da'atcha ho'il de'shayach ba'achilat pesachim* — and can register as part of a group (*leihimanot alav*), participating in the offering even without owning it himself. One might therefore have thought that since a minor has a genuine connection to the *korban Pesach* through eating, perhaps he can also acquire it for others. The verse therefore specifies *ish* to teach that only an adult can effect an acquisition on behalf of others; a minor, though he can be part of the group through enrollment, cannot serve as the acquiring agent for others.

This interplay is of particular interest on the eve of Pesach. Minors — *ketanim* — stand at the center of the *Seder* experience, and indeed they can genuinely participate in the *korban Pesach* by joining a group. Yet the legal capacity to acquire the sacrifice on behalf of others belongs only to an adult.

Given that *ish lefi ochlo* already teaches that a minor cannot acquire for others, the word *ish* in *veyikchu lahem ish seh l'veit avot* is superfluous for that purpose. That superfluous *ish* is then the source for general *shlichut* in *kodshim* — teaching that even where there is no pre-existing co-ownership or connection, one person may act as the agent of another.

## Other Shlichut Contexts

### Nasi Land Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁשְּׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָשִׂיא אֶחָד נָשִׂיא אֶחָד מִמַּטֶּה״. תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ שְׁלִיחוּת מֵהָכָא! וְתִיסְבְּרָא דְּהָא שְׁלִיחוּת הוּא? וְהָא קְטַנִּים לָאו בְּנֵי שְׁלִיחוּת נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question from a different source: **But** consider **that** which **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived **that** the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself?** It is **as** it **is stated** with regard to the division of Eretz Yisrael among the Jewish people: **"And you shall take one prince of every tribe,** to take possession of the land" (Numbers 34:18). This indicates that the prince of each tribe acted as the agent to claim the land for each member of his tribe. **Let** the *halakha* of **agency be derived from here;** why is there a need to have the sources quoted above? The Gemara answers: **And** how can **you understand that this** process of the princes claiming the land **was** due to **agency? But minors cannot be** involved in **agency,** and the princes claimed the land for all members of their tribe, adults and minors alike.

The *gemara* now introduces a teaching of Rav Gidel in the name of Rav: *minayin she'shlucho shel adam k'moso* — from where do we derive that a person's agent is like himself? The verse cited is *v'nasi echad nasi echad*, from the account of the division of the Land of Israel, where one prince from each tribe participated in the allocation on behalf of his entire tribe. To the degree that any representation of individual tribal members was required, it was performed by the *nasi*.

The *gemara* then asks why this verse should be considered the source for *shlichut* at all, given the other sources already discussed. But more fundamentally, it questions the premise.

### Minors Represented

**Ve'tisabra** — does this actually make sense? Is the division of the land through tribal princes really a case of *shlichut*? *V'ha ketanim lav bnei shlichus ninhu* — minors cannot appoint a *shaliach*. Yet minors were certainly included in the tribal allocation, receiving their portions through the *nasi*. If this were *shlichut*, it could not have applied to them — a minor lacks the legal capacity to appoint an agent.

### Zchiyah Not Shlichut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא, כִּי הָא דְּרָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁזָּכִין לְאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָשִׂיא אֶחָד נָשִׂיא אֶחָד״. וְתִיסְבְּרַאּ זְכוּת הִיא? הָא חוֹבָה נָמֵי אִיכָּא, דְּאִיכָּא דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּהַר, וְלָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּבִקְעָה, וְאִיכָּא דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּבִקְעָה וְלָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּהַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the distribution of the land by the princes follows a different principle, **like that** statement **of Rava bar Rav Huna, as Rava bar Rav Huna says** that **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived **that one can act in a person's interest in his absence?** It is **as** it **is stated: "And** you shall take **one prince."** The princes were not appointed as agents and could act for the benefit of the minors. The Gemara asks: **And** how can **you understand** that **it is a benefit? But it was also** to their **disadvantage, as there is** one person **for whom it is preferable to him** to receive a portion **on a hill and it is not preferable to him** to receive a portion **in a valley, and there is** one person **for whom it is preferable to him** to receive a portion **in a valley and it is not preferable to him** to receive a portion **on a hill.** The prince might claim land for members of his tribe that they do not want, and one cannot act to another's disadvantage in his absence.

The *gemara* therefore reframes the teaching. What the verse by the division of the land teaches is not *shlichut* but rather *zechiyah* — the principle that one may take possession of something on behalf of another, even not in the other's presence, and even when the other is a minor. The teaching is reformulated: *minayin she'zachin le'adam she'lo bifanav* — from where do we know that one may acquire on behalf of another not in his presence?

The distinction between *zechiyah* and *shlichut* is conceptually significant. *Shlichut* requires appointment by the principal and depends upon the principal's legal capacity to appoint. *Zechiyah* operates differently: when an act is unambiguously to someone's benefit, a third party may act on that person's behalf without formal appointment, and without the other's capacity to appoint being a prerequisite. In this sense *zechiyah* is simultaneously more limited — it applies only when the benefit is clear and unambiguous — and more expansive — it does not require the formalities of *shlichut* and can operate even for a minor.

The *gemara* then presses this framing as well. *Zechus hu lo* — is it really an unambiguous benefit? *Hachova nami ika* — there is also a potential detriment. The *nasi* did not merely hand each person a gift; he determined which portion of the land each person received. *De'ika de'nicha lei behar ve'lo nicha lei bevika* — one person might have preferred the mountain, another might have preferred the valley. Determining which specific portion someone receives is not a pure benefit; it involves judgment calls and tradeoffs. This therefore cannot be the paradigmatic case of *zechiyah* either.

## Orphans & Trustees

### Apotropus Appointed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאֶלָּא כִּדְרָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן לִיתוֹמִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי אֲבִיהֶן שֶׁבֵּית דִּין מַעֲמִידִין לָהֶם אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס לָחוּב וְלִזְכּוֹת? ״לָחוּב״ אַמַּאי? אֶלָּא: ״לָחוּב עַל מְנָת לִזְכּוֹת״ – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְנָשִׂיא אֶחָד נָשִׂיא אֶחָד מִמַּטֶּה תִּקְחוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse is required to teach a different *halakha*, **like that** statement **of Rava bar Rav Huna, as Rava bar Rav Huna says** that **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: From where** is it derived **that** if **orphans came to divide their father's property, that the court appoints a steward for them,** both **to** their **disadvantage and to** their **benefit?** Before the Gemara completes the quote, it analyzes the statement: **Why** would the court appoint a steward **to** their **disadvantage? Rather,** it means **to** their **disadvantage in order to** achieve their eventual **benefit.** Once a steward has been appointed to control the orphans' estate, he has the authority to act to their temporary disadvantage if they are ultimately likely to benefit from the action. And the source of this *halakha* is as **the verse states: "And you shall take one prince of every tribe"** (Numbers 34:18).

The *gemara* finally settles on a more precise characterization of what the verse teaches. It is not general *shlichut* and not general *zechiyah*; rather it establishes a specific principle: *minayin le'yisomim she'bau lachlok b'nichsei avihen she'bezdin mamidin lahem apitropus l'chov u'lizkos* — from where do we know that when orphans (minors) come to divide their father's estate, the *beit din* can appoint a guardian (*apitropus*) to act on their behalf, with the authority to make tradeoffs that may involve both benefit and detriment? This authority is derived from the *nasi*'s role in dividing the land of Israel.

The parallel is instructive. The division of *Eretz Yisrael* was not purely beneficial for every individual in every respect; it involved allocations that some would have preferred differently. Yet it was conducted by the *nasi* on behalf of the entire tribe, including those who could not represent themselves. The authority to make such consequential, judgment-laden decisions on behalf of others — including those who lack legal capacity — derives from the fact that the representative was appointed by a higher authority. In the case of *Eretz Yisrael*, that authority was divine. In the case of orphans dividing an estate, the parallel authority is *beit din*.

### No Protest After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְתוֹמִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחְלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי אֲבִיהֶם – בֵּית דִּין מַעֲמִידִים לָהֶם אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, וּבוֹרְרִים לָהֶם חֵלֶק יָפֶה, וְאִם הִגְדִּילוּ – יְכוֹלִים לְמַחוֹת. וְרַב נַחְמָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: אִם הִגְדִּילוּ אֵינָם יְכוֹלִים לְמַחוֹת, דְּאִם כֵּן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the *halakhot* of stewardship, **Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** If **orphans came to divide their father's property, the court appoints** a steward **for them, and they select for them,** i.e., for each of the orphans, **a fine portion. And when they have grown up,** the orphans **can protest** the division and demand redistribution of the property. **And Rav Naḥman said his own** statement: **When they have grown up, they cannot protest, as if so, what advantage** is there to **the power of the court** over an ordinary person? In other words, to strengthen the authority of the court, it is required that its decisions not be questioned later on.

Rav Nachman bar Shmuel states the practical rule: when minors come to divide their father's estate, *beit din* appoints an *apitropus* and chooses a good portion for them — meaning the trustee attempts to achieve a fair and beneficial distribution for everyone under his charge.

Once the orphans reach majority, Rav Nachman rules in his own name: *im higdilu einam yecholim limchos* — they cannot protest the division that was made on their behalf. The principle invoked is *mah koach beit din yafeh*: if the court-appointed division could be undone the moment the orphans grew up and decided they were unhappy, the authority of the court would be rendered meaningless. A division backed by *beit din* is binding even against the future objection of those who were represented.

### Beis Din Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן ״אִם כֵּן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה״? וְהָתְנַן: שׁוּם הַדַּיָּינִים שֶׁפִּיחֲתוּ שְׁתוּת אוֹ הוֹתִירוּ שְׁתוּת – מִכְרָן בָּטֵל. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מִכְרָן קַיָּים. (וְאָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל): אִם כֵּן – מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה! וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִינָּנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is Rav Naḥman of** the opinion that there is a consideration of: **If so, what advantage** does **the court have** over an ordinary person? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 99b): The *halakha* with regard to **the appraisal by the judges** of the value of a piece of property in order to sell it is as follows: **Where they decreased** the price by **one-sixth** of its market value **or added one-sixth** to its market value, **their sale is void. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Their sale is valid. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: If so,** if the sale is void, then **what advantage** is there to **the power of the court** over an ordinary person? **And Rav Huna bar Ḥinnana says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis.** This indicates that Rav Naḥman does not accept the consideration of: What advantage is there to the power of the court over an ordinary person?

This contrasts with the ordinary principle applicable to *zechiyah she'lo bifanav*: when someone takes possession of an object for an absent party and that party is subsequently informed, the party can refuse the acquisition — *macha'ah* — if they did not want it. The presumption that the acquisition was beneficial can be overridden once the party expresses otherwise. But a court-appointed trustee's decision operates differently; it carries the authority of *beit din*, and the orphans' later preference cannot undo what was done under that authority.

### Error Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא – דִּטְעוֹ, הָא – דְּלָא טְעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. In this case,** where Rav Naḥman ruled that their transaction is void, in accordance with the Rabbis, the court **erred** by one-sixth. But in **that** case, where Rav Naḥman ruled that the orphans cannot protest when they grow up, **they did not err** by one-sixth.

A difficulty arises from a *mishnah* elsewhere. The *mishnah* teaches that when judges assess property values in order to authorize a creditor's seizure, and their assessment was off by a sixth or more — *shepichasu shtus o hosiru shtus* — the resulting transaction is void. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: the transaction stands, because voiding a court's assessment would undermine the authority of *beit din* — *mah koach beit din yafeh*. Rav Huna bar Chinena reported that Rav Nachman himself ruled in accordance with the *chachamim* (i.e., that the transaction is void). But Rav Nachman also said, in the context of the orphans, that one cannot protest a court-appointed division — on the same grounds of *mah koach beit din yafeh*. The *gemara* asks: does Rav Nachman really hold consistently by the principle of *mah koach beit din yafeh*, or does he apply it selectively?

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya — ha detau, ha delo tau* — it is not difficult. Where the court made a significant error in assessment — where the valuation was objectively wrong by a substantial margin — the transaction is void. The principle of *mah koach beit din yafeh* does not immunize genuine mistakes. Where there was no error, however — where the court exercised sound judgment and made reasonable tradeoffs — the result stands even against later objection.

### Preference vs. Error

The *gemara* then refines further. If the orphan is simply unhappy with the portion he received — not because the court made a mistake, but because he had a different preference — he cannot protest. This is the case to which *mah koach beit din yafeh* directly applies. The orphan might say: I would have preferred the north field rather than the east field, and here are my reasons. That is a preference, not a correction of an error. The court was authorized to exercise judgment and make tradeoffs. Its decision on matters of preference is final.

The three-tier structure thus emerges: (1) no error at all — the division stands absolutely; (2) mere preference, no objective error — the division still stands, under *mah koach beit din yafeh*; (3) a genuine and significant error in assessment — the division may be voided.

## Estate Division Rules

### Brothers Like Sales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ הֲרֵי הֵן כְּלָקוֹחוֹת. פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁתוּת – נִקְנֶה מִקָּח. יָתֵר עַל שְׁתוּת – בָּטֵל מִקָּח. שְׁתוּת – קָנָה, וּמַחְזִיר אוֹנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman says:** With regard to **brothers who divided** property received as an inheritance, **they are** considered **like** they are **purchasers** from each other, and the *halakhot* of fraud are like those for regular transactions: If there was an error of **less than one-sixth** in the distribution, the **transaction is acquired,** i.e., valid. If it was **more than one-sixth,** the **transaction is void.** If the error was precisely **one-sixth,** it is **acquired, and** the one who received more than his fair share **must return** the amount of the **fraud.**

Rav Nachman now shifts to a case without court involvement. When brothers divide their father's estate among themselves, *harei hein kelakuchos* — they are treated like buyers and sellers. The ordinary laws of commercial transactions govern the division.

### Ona'ah Thresholds

Those laws produce three outcomes based on the discrepancy between what each person received: if the disparity was less than a sixth (*pachos mishtus*), the division is valid and stands as-is — *nikna mekach*, the transaction holds. If the disparity exceeded a sixth (*yeser al shtus*), the division is void — *batel mekach* — and the brothers must divide again. If the disparity was exactly a sixth (*shtus*), the division is valid, but the one who received more must return the difference — *kana u'machzir ona'ah*.

These three outcomes mirror precisely the *halacha* of *ona'ah* in standard purchase-and-sale transactions, applied now to the estate division context.

### Shaliach Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רָבָא: הָא דַּאֲמַרַן ״פָּחוֹס מִשְּׁתוּת – נִקְנֶה מִקָּח״ – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא שַׁוְּיֵהּ שָׁלִיחַ, אֲבָל שַׁוְּיֵהּ שָׁלִיחַ, אָמַר: לְתַקּוֹנֵי שַׁדַּרְתָּיךָ וְלָא לְעַוּוֹתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: That which we said,** that with regard to **less than one-sixth** the **transaction** is valid and the item **is acquired, we said only** in a case **where** the brother receiving a smaller share **did not appoint an agent** to deal with the distribution on his behalf. **But if** the brother receiving a smaller share **appointed an agent,** this *halakha* does not apply, as the one who appointed the agent can **say: I sent you to** act for **my benefit and not to my detriment.** The agent's right to act in this capacity did not extend to a case where it was to the detriment of the one who appointed him.

Rava introduces an important qualification: the above rules — particularly the one holding that a discrepancy of less than a sixth does not affect the validity of the division — apply only where the brothers negotiated the division themselves. If one brother appointed a *shaliach* to represent him in the division, and the *shaliach* obtained a portion that was worth even slightly less than the other portions, the outcome is different. The principal can say: *letikuni shedartich ve'lo le'avusi* — I sent you to act for my benefit, not to my detriment. Since the agent's mandate is defined by the principal's interest, any outcome unfavorable to the principal — even below the *ona'ah* threshold — may be contestable. The practical concern this raises is obvious: anyone entering an estate division could simply appoint an agent and then, after the fact, use that agency relationship to void any outcome they disliked.

### If They Agree to B.D.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָא דַּאֲמַרַן ״יָתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת – בָּטֵל מִקָּח״ – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא אָמַר (נִיפְלִיגַן) [נִפְלוֹג] בְּשׁוּמָא דְּבֵי דִינָא, אֲבָל אָמַר נִפְלוֹג בְּשׁוּמָא דְּבֵי דִינָא – מִכְרָן קַיָּים, דִּתְנַן: שׁוּם הַדַּיָּינִים שֶׁפִּיחֲתוּ שְׁתוּת אוֹ הוֹתִירוּ שְׁתוּת – מִכְרָן בָּטֵל. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מִכְרָן קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **And that which we said,** that if the brothers erred by **more than one-sixth** the **transaction is void, we said only when** the brother receiving a smaller share **did not say: Let us divide** the estate **by an appraisal of the court. But** if **he said: Let us divide** it **by an appraisal of the court, the transaction is valid, as we learned** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 99b): This is the *halakha* with regard to **the appraisal** of an article's value in order to sell it, as done by **the judges:** In a case **where they decreased** the price by **one-sixth** of its market value **or added one-sixth** to its market value, **their sale is void. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Their sale is valid.**

A further qualification applies to the *shtus* case specifically. The rule that a division with exactly a one-sixth discrepancy is valid but requires repayment of the difference holds only where the brothers made no special agreement about the basis of the division. If, however, they declared that the division should be conducted on the model of a *beit din* assessment — *niflog b'shuma de'veit din* — then even a one-sixth discrepancy requires no repayment. The reasoning follows from the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: a court assessment stands even when off by a sixth, because *mah koach beit din yafeh*. If the parties voluntarily adopted the *beit din* model, the same robustness applies to their private division.

### No Ona'ah on Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָא דַּאֲמַרַן ״שְׁתוּת – קָנָה, וּמַחְזִיר אוֹנָאָה״ – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּמִטַּלְטְלֵי, אֲבָל בִּמְקַרְקְעֵי – אֵין אוֹנָאָה לְקַרְקָעוֹת. וּבִמְקַרְקְעֵי לָא אֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא דִּפְלוּג בְּעִילּוּיָא, אֲבָל פְּלוּג בְּמִשְׁחֲתָא – לָא, כִּדְרַבָּה. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה וְשֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן, אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹס מִכְּדֵי אוֹנָאָה – נָמֵי חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **And that which we said,** that if the brothers erred by **one-sixth** the one receiving a larger share **acquired** it **and he must return** the amount of the **fraud, we said only with regard to movable property. But with regard to land,** the *halakha* is that **there is no fraud with regard to land. And with regard to land, we said** that the *halakha* of fraud does not apply **only when they divided** it **according to the value** of the land. **But** if **they divided** it **by measure** and erred in the measurement, we do **not** say that there is no fraud. This is in accordance **with** the statement **of Rabba, as Rabba said: Any matter that is according to measure, or according to weight, or according to number,** if it turned out to be in error, **even** if the error was **less than the amount** that constitutes **fraud,** it **is also returned.**

Another qualification applies to the medium of the division. The rule that a one-sixth discrepancy requires repayment — *kana u'machzir ona'ah* — applies only to *metalteli*, movable property. For *karka* — land — there is no *ona'ah*: *ein ona'ah le'karka'ot*. An overcharge or undercharge in a land transaction, even by more than a sixth in terms of assessed value, does not affect the validity of the transaction. Since most estate divisions involve land, this significantly limits the practical scope of the *ona'ah* rules in this context.

*Tosafot* raises the question of how far the *karka* exemption extends. If the *mishnah*'s statement that *ein ona'ah le'karka'ot* means that land transactions are simply exempt from *ona'ah* in all cases — including where the discrepancy exceeds a sixth — then the entire framework of *shitus batel mekach* may not apply to land at all. Rashi's reading, however, appears to locate the *karka* rule specifically in the one-sixth case; whether a discrepancy greater than a sixth might void even a land transaction is a question Rashi's formulation leaves open.

### Measurement Errors

A final distinction must be drawn between two types of errors in a land division. The principle *ein ona'ah le'karka'ot* applies where the error was in the price assessment — that is, where the parties agreed on a certain acreage but differed in their valuation of that acreage, and the assessment of value turned out to be off. In such a case, the *karka* exemption applies and the transaction stands.

But where the error was in the measurement itself — the parties agreed that each would receive thirty acres, and a mistake in drawing the boundary lines left one person with twenty-eight and the other with thirty-two — that is a different category of error entirely. This is not a case of *ona'ah* (overcharge) but of *mekach ta'ut* (erroneous transaction): one party simply did not receive what was agreed upon. Rava rules accordingly: *kol davar she'bemiddah u'veshikul u'veminyan* — anything sold or divided by measure, weight, or count — if the quantity is wrong, the party who received less may demand correction *even if the discrepancy is less than the ona'ah threshold*. A numerical or measurement shortfall is not absorbed by the tolerance built into *ona'ah* law; it is a straightforward error that must be rectified regardless of its magnitude.
