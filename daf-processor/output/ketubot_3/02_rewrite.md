# Ketubot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ones BeGittin

### Ones Concept Intro

The *gemara* on the bottom of 3b takes up the question of *yesh ones begitin* — whether the claim of *ones*, an act of God or circumstance beyond one's control, is a relevant factor when a *get* has been given conditionally. If someone stipulates that a *get* should take effect under certain conditions, and those conditions are met only because of something entirely outside the husband's control — he became ill, was detained, or died — do we look at the situation as though the condition was not truly met in the sense he intended? The *gemara*'s first position is *ein ones begitin*: we do not factor that in. As was already noted, if we were to factor it in, things would become very complicated, since one could never know with certainty whether the conditions had truly been met.

### Get After Death

The *gemara* attempts to prove this from a *mishna* dealing with a case where a man says "This is your *get* if I die." This would seem to be an example of *ones*, and the question is whether we can infer something about what happens if he said "This will be your *get* if I don't return," and he then died — the condition being met only because of *ones*. However, the *gemara* concludes that the real point of that *mishna* is not to address the question of *ones* at all, but rather to establish the principle of *ein get achar misa* — that a *get* cannot take effect after a person has already died.

The *mishna* itself states: *harei zeh gitich im mati* — "This is your *get* if I die"; *harei zeh gitich mecholi zeh* — "This is your *get* if I don't recover from this illness"; *harei zeh gitich achar misa* — "This is your *get* after I die" — in all such cases, *lo amar klum*, it amounts to nothing. In each of these formulations, the husband is not merely imposing a stipulation; he is specifying the moment the *get* takes effect — when he dies, when he never rises from his sickbed. Since a *get* cannot take effect once the husband is dead, all these formulations fail. We thus already know the principle of *ein get achar misa* from the opening of that *mishna*.

### Dating Documents

Since the principle of *ein get achar misa* is already established by the beginning of the *mishna*, why does the *gemara* need to teach it again through the case of one who says "If I don't return in twelve months"? The *gemara* answers: *dilma l'apokei merabboseinu* — perhaps the second teaching is necessary to exclude the position of *Rabboseinu*. As a *braita* teaches, *Rabboseinu hitiru lenasei* — those authorities permitted the woman to remarry even when the husband said "This is your *get* after I die." How could they permit this given *ein get achar misa*?

The *gemara* asks: who is this *Rabboseinu*? The answer is *Bei Dina Desharu Mishcha* — the *beis din* that permitted non-Jewish oil. This is the *beis din* of *Rabbi Yossi*, or more precisely, a *beis din* that held like *Rabbi Yossi*, and they have become identified by their most famous leniency. Their position follows from the principle *zmano shel shetar mochiach alav* — that the date written in a legal document demonstrates something. If a *get* is dated from the day it was written, even when it contains a condition that it take effect upon death, the date in the document signals that it retroactively takes effect from the time of writing. According to *Rabbi Yossi*, then, there could in principle be a *get achar misa* as long as the document is dated.

This is precisely why the *mishna* must teach the principle of *ein get achar misa* twice: once as a general rule, and once to make clear that this holds even when the document bears a date — to reject *Rabbi Yossi*'s view. The case in the *mishna* of one who says "If I don't return in twelve months" and then dies is therefore not relevant to the question of *ones* at all. Its purpose is solely to reiterate *ein get achar misa* and to specify that a date in the document does not change this.

### Conditional Get Case

Having established that the earlier sources do not speak to the question of *ones*, the *gemara* turns to the end of that *mishna*. The case is: *me'achshav im lo basi mikan v'ad shenem asar chodesh* — if a man says explicitly that the *get* should take effect as of now if he does not return within twelve months. One can readily understand why a husband would phrase it this way — so that his wife would not be left as an *agunah* if he fails to return and she has no way of knowing what happened to him. *Umes b'soch shenem asar chodesh* — and he dies within the twelve months. The condition has been met: he did not return within the year. It is not a case of *get achar misa* because he explicitly said "from now." The *gemara* states: *harei zeh get* — it is a valid *get*. The fact that the condition was met only because of *ones*, because he died and could not control his return, is immaterial. *Ones* is not a factor.

### Dying vs. Sickness

The *gemara* immediately raises a qualification. *Mes v'hu hadin l'chola* — one might assume that the same would apply whether he died or merely became ill. But the *gemara* resists this inference. *Dilma mes davka* — perhaps dying is categorically different. *D'lo nicha lei d'tipal kamei yavam* — one of the main reasons a man might give his wife a conditional *get* in the first place, especially if he fears he might die, is so that she will not be subject to *yibum*, which was the entire subject of the previous tractate. If a man anticipates the possibility of death, that is perhaps precisely the kind of *ones* he had in mind when he formulated the condition. Death may well have been the very scenario he was thinking about. Getting sick, by contrast, is something he may never have anticipated — it represents a situation he would have wished he could have negated by returning, and therefore perhaps it should not be held against him.

This is the real conceptual heart of the issue. The question of *ones* in *gittin* is not primarily about blame or legal culpability in the usual sense. The governing principle of *ones rachmana patrei* — that the Torah does not hold a person liable for what is beyond their control — does not translate neatly here, since the issue is not one of fault but of whether the conditions stipulated were truly met. Nor does that principle distinguish between dying and becoming ill, since both are objectively outside one's control. The deeper question, as the *gemara* frames it, is really one of *da'as*: under what circumstances did the husband actually intend the *get* to take effect? The premise is that the husband's intention governs, and *ones* matters — or doesn't — depending on whether the particular event was one he would have included in his intent.

Of course, the difficulty is that one cannot run a legal system trying to reconstruct what was going on inside someone's mind. The general principle is *devarim shebalev einam devarim* — what is in one's heart is not legally operative. There is, however, an important nuance: the *Rambam* and the *Tosafos Rid* explain that *devarim shebalev einam devarim* applies with full force primarily in bilateral transactions — a sale, for instance, where money and goods change hands and only what is accessible to both parties can be legally relevant. But in a unilateral transaction — a gift, or a *get*, where the other party has no real say — what is in the giver's mind may carry more legal weight. So the question the *gemara* is working through is not a categorical principle about *ones*, but a question about intent: did I intend the *get* to take effect in these circumstances?

## River Case Analysis

### River Blockage

Having established the conceptual framework, the *gemara* attempts to derive a clear ruling from a concrete case. *De-hu de-amar le-hu* — a man said to witnesses: *i lo asina mi-kan ve-ad tlasin yomin, le-havei gitta* — if I don't return within thirty days, let it be a *get*. *Asa be-sof tlasin yomin* — he came at the end of thirty days, *u-paska mabra* — but a river blocked his path. He could not cross in time. *Amar le-hu chazu de-asai chazu de-asai* — he called out across the river: "Look, I am coming, I am coming!" His *ratzon* was evident: he wanted to negate the condition of the *get*. But he could not cross in time. *Amar Shmuel: lav shemei masia* — it is very well that he announced his arrival, says *Shmuel*, but he did not come. And therefore the *get* takes effect. *Ones* is irrelevant.

### Anticipated Ones

The *gemara* does not accept this as a conclusive proof, however. *Ve-dilma unsa de-shiyach shani* — perhaps a common type of *ones* is categorically different. Perhaps when the *ones* is something foreseeable, it simply cannot operate to negate the condition.

### Foreseeability

*De-keivan de-ibayei lei le-asnu-ei ve-lo asni* — since he could have stipulated otherwise and did not, he is responsible for the gap. He should have said: this will be a *get* if I do not return, unless I am held up by circumstances beyond my control. A river blocking one's path is a perfectly ordinary and foreseeable occurrence. Since he did not exclude it, we assume he intended the *get* to take effect regardless. *Ihu de-afsidei nafshiyei* — he has brought this upon himself.

This is again consistent with the *da'as*-centered reading of the issue. Something foreseeable is assumed to have been included in the husband's intent even if unstated, because any reasonable person would have thought of it. The remaining question is what happens with a truly extraordinary *ones* — something no normal person would ever anticipate, like being struck by lightning. Is that also assumed to be within the husband's intent, or do we say it was so far outside normal expectation that it genuinely was never part of what he envisioned? The *gemara* cannot resolve this from the river case alone, and concedes there is no clear proof.

## Raba's Position

### Raba's Reasoning

Rather than simply articulating *ein ones begitin* as a legal principle — which one might derive straightforwardly from *devarim shebalev einam devarim* — *Rava* formulates his position as a policy ruling, based on *sevara d'nafshei*, his own independent reasoning. He has no definitive source to cite. But the *gemara* then makes a critical move: it argues that the policy itself demands this position.

### Policy Problems

The *gemara* explains: *mishum tznuos u-mishum prutzos* — because of women who are overly cautious and because of women who are overly ready. If we were to say that *ones* matters and can negate the *get*, we would create an impossible social situation. On the one hand, *mishum tznuos* — there will be times when there is no *ones* at all, the condition was met straightforwardly, and yet the woman will worry: perhaps there was some act of God I have no knowledge of, perhaps he was struck by lightning, perhaps something beyond his control occurred. *U-me'aguna v'yasva* — she will sit and remain an *agunah* when she has every right to remarry.

On the other hand, *mishum prutzos* — there will be women too eager to move on. Even when there is genuine reason to believe there was an *ones*, *v'azla u-minsva* — she will say it was not an *ones* and proceed to remarry, *v'nimtza get batel u-vaneha mamzerim* — and it will turn out the *get* was never valid and her children will be *mamzerim*. You simply cannot run a society in which the validity of every *get* depends on an invisible and unknowable question of whether some unforeseeable act of God occurred in a distant place.

### Hafka'as Kiddushin

The *gemara* presses back sharply: are you saying that merely for policy reasons — *mishum tznuos u-mishum prutzos* — we would declare a married woman permitted to the world? *Sharinan eshes ish l'alma?* We would say it is always a valid *get*, ignoring *ones*, even when legally *ones* should have negated it?

The *gemara* answers: *in, kol d'mekadesh ada'ata d'rabanan mekadesh* — yes, because anyone who marries does so on the terms set by the rabbis. We do not live in a world of pure biblical Judaism alone; every legal act is performed within the framework that the rabbis have constructed and defined. *V'afkinhu rabanan l'kidushin minei* — and the rabbis can retroactively uproot the *kiddushin*. Under these circumstances, even though the *get* is technically defective because *ones* should have negated the condition, the rabbis retroactively declare the *kiddushin* void, so that the *get* — whatever its legal infirmity — is treated as effective.

This is one of the classic *sugyos* of *hafka'as kiddushin*, the retroactive uprooting of marriage, and it has obvious resonance in contemporary discussions about the *agunah* problem. Two important points about how it operates here deserve emphasis. First, this is not a case-by-case intervention. It is a general systemic rule, operative from the very moment of marriage. The *kiddushin* was contracted with this rule already in place: *ada'ata d'rabanan mekadesh* — the marriage was entered into on the rabbis' terms, which include the possibility of retroactive uprooting in defined circumstances. Second, even within this mechanism, a *get* is still required — even if defective. *Rashi* consistently emphasizes this point throughout the *sugya*: the rabbis uproot the *kiddushin* specifically *al yedei gittin she-hikshiru chachamim* — through *gittin* that the rabbis have validated in this manner. The *Rashba* elaborates on this principle, explaining that *hafka'as kiddushin* is not a bold direct exercise of rabbinic power. It operates as a hidden mechanism behind the scenes. The *Rashba* calls what is required a *srach get* — a semblance of a *get*, what one might call a fig leaf. The *get* may not function on its own terms, but it provides the formal occasion for the hidden mechanism of *hafka'as kiddushin* to operate. We do not walk in and announce that we are nullifying the *kiddushin* directly; rather, the *get* stands upfront, and the *hafka'as kiddushin* works silently behind it.

This same logic, the *Rashba* notes, operates in the *sugyos* of *Yevamos* involving *ed echad* — a single witness to the husband's death. There the "fig leaf" is not a *get* but the reliance on the single witness's testimony, however legally insufficient on its own. Behind that reliance, the *hafka'as kiddushin* provides the ultimate mechanism.

A small but illuminating point from *Tosafot*: the practice of reciting *kedas Moshe ve-Yisrael* at the moment of *kiddushin*, though nowhere explicitly mandated in the *gemara*, encodes this principle. *Moshe* represents *Torah de-oraisa*; *Yisrael*, the Jewish people as represented by the rabbis, signals *ada'ata d'rabanan mekadesh* — that the marriage is entered into on the rabbis' terms.

## Hafka'as Kiddushin

### Fig Leaf Get

The question then arises of exactly how the mechanism of *hafka'as kiddushin* operates in this case. The *get* here is technically defective — the condition was ostensibly not met in a legally valid sense, since *ones* arguably should have negated it. Yet the *get* stands as the fig leaf behind which *hafka'as kiddushin* operates. *Rashi*'s language throughout this *sugya* is precise: *afkinu rabbanan le-kiddushin kshe-yavo get kazeh achareihen* — the rabbis uproot the *kiddushin* when a *get* like this follows. The *get* need not be fully valid on its own; what it provides is the formal occasion for the rabbinic mechanism to operate. The *Rashba* reinforces this: direct, unadorned exercise of *hafka'as kiddushin* without any *get* would undermine the institutions of Jewish marriage altogether. The power exists, but it must operate through proper legal form, however attenuated.

### Money vs. Sex

*Ravina* poses a pointed question to *Rav Ashi*: *teynach de-kadesh be-chaspa* — this works well enough if the *kiddushin* was performed with money. In that case, the mechanism of *hefker beis din hefker* — the court's power to declare property ownerless — explains how the rabbis can retroactively undo the monetary *kiddushin*. They declare the money that effected the *kiddushin* to have been ownerless from the start, thus voiding the transaction. But *kadesh be-via mai ika le-meimar* — what happens if the *kiddushin* was *kiddushei biah*, effected through marital relations? The physical reality of the sexual act has occurred. How can the rabbis change that? How can they retroactively declare it not to have been an act of *kiddushin*?

### Redefining Acts

The *gemara* answers: *shavyuha rabbanan le-veilaso veilas zenus* — the rabbis can redefine the context. Not every act of sex is *kiddushin*. Most sexual relations between people have no legal status of *kiddushin* at all; they are *zenus*, non-marital relations. What makes a sexual act into *kiddushin* is the legal and social context in which it occurs — and the rabbis are the ones who determine what that context means. Retroactively, they declare that this particular act, which appeared to be marital, is to be regarded as *zenus* — legally unrecognized as a marriage-effecting act.

This is admittedly a striking *chiddush*. We might be dealing with a couple who have lived together for years, raised children, participated in communal life as a married couple — and retroactively we say no, that was all *beilas zenus*. One might ask: if witnesses were present who explicitly heard the man declare his intent to effect *kiddushei biah*, does the rabbinic recharacterization override even their testimony? The answer would have to be yes — the rabbis' framework for what constitutes *kiddushin* supersedes even how the witnesses understood the act they observed.

### Alternate View

There is a famous alternative interpretation of this passage, cited by *Rashi* though firmly rejected by him. According to this reading — which *Rashi* notes is reflected in some *rishonim* — the reason *kiddushei kesef* is easier to retroactively undo is not *hefker beis din hefker*, but rather because *kiddushei kesef* is itself only *derabbanan*. On this view, the only form of *kiddushin* that is *de-oraisa* is *biah*. *Kiddushei kesef* and *kiddushei shtar* are rabbinic constructs, and the rabbis can therefore undo what they created. As for *kiddushei biah*, which would be *de-oraisa*, this reading says the rabbis never recognized it as valid *kiddushin* in the first place — it was always treated as *beilas zenus* from the outset, given that the *gemara* elsewhere records that lashes were administered to one who effected *kiddushin* through *biah* because it was *pritzus*.

The implication of this view would be that the only form of *kiddushin* truly beyond rabbinic power to undo is *kiddushei shtar*. *Kiddushei kesef* is *derabbanan* and can be controlled; *kiddushei biah* is never recognized. *Rashi*, however, is sharply critical of this reading. He calls it *ta'us gomur* — a complete error — and points out that accepting it would mean that a woman who underwent *kiddushei biah* could simply walk away without a *get* on the basis of this reasoning alone, and *lo matzinu zos batalmud* — we find no such ruling anywhere in the Talmud. *Rashi* firmly rejects the entire construct.

## Alternative Position

### Ones Matters

The *gemara* now presents a second version of *Rava*'s position: *ikha de-amri, amar Rava ve-chein l'inyan gittin* — there are those who say that *Rava* actually ruled that *ones does* matter in practice, not merely in theory. On this reading, *Rava* held *yesh ones begitin* — we genuinely factor in *ones*, not just in principle but as a practical legal ruling.

### Mishna Challenge

The *gemara* immediately challenges this position from the *mishna*. The case of *me'achshav im lo basi mikan v'ad shenem asar chodesh*, where the husband said "from now if I do not return in twelve months" and he died within the twelve months — the *mishna* rules *harei zeh get*, it is a valid *get*. If *ones* mattered and could negate the *get*, then the fact that he died — surely an *ones* — should have invalidated it.

### Death Anticipated

The *gemara* responds: *lo, meis davka* — only dying is different. *D'lo nicha lei d'tipal kamei yavam* — death is not treated as a disqualifying *ones* because that may well have been precisely the contingency the husband had in mind when giving the *get*. He wanted the *get* to protect her from *yibum* in the event of his death; he intended the *get* to take effect in that scenario. Getting sick, however, is different — that may have been something he never anticipated and would never have intended to include within his condition. So on this version of *Rava*'s position, *ones* does matter, but dying is not classified as an *ones* in this context because the husband can be presumed to have intended that very outcome.

The *gemara* revisits the river case as well — *Shmuel*'s ruling that the man who was blocked by the river and called out "I am coming" has not fulfilled the condition — but notes that this too can be explained as a case of *unsa de-shchiach*, a foreseeable *ones* the husband should have anticipated and excluded explicitly.

### Final Conclusion

*Tosafot* works through the competing positions at length and concludes that we must adopt *ain ones begitin* — the ruling that *ones* does not negate a conditional *get* — as the operative position. The societal stakes are simply too high. With *yesh ones begitin*, the *tznuos* will become unnecessarily chained and the *prutzos* will remarry in situations they should not, producing *mamzerim*. The conclusion is thus driven not by pure legal logic but by the existential requirements of communal stability.

*Tosafot* does note, however, that even at the end of the matter, a residual question remains: there may be extreme cases of *ones* — something so extraordinary that no one could ever have anticipated it — where even those who rule *ain ones begitin* as a matter of policy might concede that the *ones* is operative. The *gemara* does not arrive at a fully clean resolution, and that ambiguity in the extreme case persists.

## Wednesday Wedding

### Three-Day Prep

Having concluded the discussion of *ones*, the *gemara* returns to the basic premise of the *mishna* that opens the tractate: virgins marry on Wednesday, so that if the husband has a *ta'anas besulim* — a complaint that she was not a virgin — he can go to court on Thursday morning. The choice of Wednesday rather than, say, Sunday was deliberate: the groom needed three days to prepare the wedding feast, so Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday were preparation days, with Wednesday as the wedding.

### Court Schedule

*Amar Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak*: *lo shanu ela mi-takanas Ezra v'eilach* — this rule applies only from the *takana* of *Ezra* onward. *Ezra* instituted regular courts that convened specifically on Mondays and Thursdays. Since Thursday was the fixed court day, Wednesday was the optimal wedding night — the husband could appear in court the very next morning. But before *Ezra*'s *takana*, when courts sat every day, *ishah nisas b'chol yom* — a woman could marry on any day, since no single day had any special relationship to the court schedule.

*Tosafot* raises a difficulty here: did *Ezra*'s *takana* actually *reduce* the frequency of court sittings? Before *Ezra*, courts apparently sat daily; afterward, only on Monday and Thursday. *Tosafot* offers two resolutions. The first: before *Ezra*, courts sat daily but only in the cities; *Ezra* decreed that courts should sit less frequently but extend their reach to the villages as well. The second: before *Ezra*, courts were convened on an ad hoc basis whenever the need arose; *Ezra* instituted fixed, regular courts with set hours and set days, replacing the ad hoc system with a structured institution.

### Variable Courts

The *gemara* makes clear that the historical question about pre-*Ezra* practice is not the point. *Hachi ka'amar* — what the *gemara* is actually saying is: *ei ika batei dinin di-kvu-in idna* — if today you had regular courts sitting every day, or conversely if today there were no fixed court schedule at all, then *k'kodem takanas Ezra* — the situation would resemble the pre-*Ezra* era, and *ishah nisas b'chol yom* — a woman could marry on any day. The connection between Wednesday and Thursday courts is entirely instrumental: it was never about the day itself but about ensuring the husband had recourse to the court immediately after the wedding night.

*Tosafot* notes an apparent problem with this reasoning: what about Friday? Even if courts are in session on every other day, a *beis din* cannot convene on *Shabbat*. How could marrying on Friday ever be permitted? *Tosafot* responds with two points. First, in practice the formal procedure that would be required — producing witnesses, conducting a full trial — is not what is needed today. All that is required is registering a complaint, which can be done informally before three people even on *Shabbat*. Second, *Rashi*'s understanding of the court's role in the original *takana* was about gathering witnesses for a potential capital prosecution, which is not at all the legal landscape of later generations. The concerns we now face regarding *ta'anas besulim* can be addressed without the same formal *beis din* structure.

### Core Requirement

The *gemara* then asks: even if there is no fixed Thursday court, does the three-day preparation requirement not still apply? *Ha ba'inan shakdu* — don't we still need the rabbis' conscientious provision that the groom spend three days preparing? The *gemara* answers: *tarach lei* — he can get married on any day, provided he has spent three days preparing. The *takana* imposes a minimum investment in wedding preparation, not a fixed calendar day. The day is entirely instrumental; the preparation is the substance.

## Takana Source

### Shakdu Source

The *gemara* asks: *mai shakdu* — where does this concept of *shakdu* — the rabbis' solicitude — come from? A *braita* provides the answer. *Mipnei ma amru besula nises l'yom ha-revi'i?* — Why did the rabbis say that a virgin marries on the fourth day of the week? *She'im haya lo ta'anas besulim, haya mashkim l'beis din* — so that if the husband has a complaint about her virginity, he can go to court first thing the next morning. And why not Sunday? *Shakdu chachamim al takanas bnos Yisrael* — the rabbis were conscientious, they took special care for the welfare of Jewish women, ensuring that the groom would invest in the wedding: *she-yehei torech b'seuda shlosha yamim* — that he should busy himself with preparing the feast for three days.

### Three-Day Pattern

The three-day pattern that results is: *echad b'shabbos, sheni b'shabbos, shlishi b'shabbos* — Sunday, Monday, Tuesday are the days of preparation; *u-v'revi'i konesah* — and on Wednesday he marries her. This is the full structure of the *takana*.

### Wednesday to Tuesday

The *baraita* continues: *u-mei-hasakana v'eilach, nahagu lichnos b'shlishi* — once a certain danger emerged, the practice shifted to marrying on Tuesday. *Ve-lo miku beyadam chachamim* — and the rabbis did not object to this departure from the original *takana*.

### Monday Exception

As for Monday: *u-v'sheni lo yichanes* — as a general rule one should not marry on Monday. There is a dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* about how to read this — whether it refers to the danger scenario or to ordinary circumstances — but *ve'i machmat ha'ones*, if there were special extenuating circumstances, getting married on Monday is nonetheless permitted. So the *takana* maintained its formal structure even as practical circumstances drove the actual custom toward Tuesday, with Monday available as a further fallback in cases of pressing need.

## Sakana Definition

### Ruler's Right

What was this *sakana* that caused the shift from Wednesday to Tuesday? The *gemara* considers and rejects the most direct possibility — that the non-Jewish authorities were persecuting the practice of Wednesday weddings as a religious observance, threatening death to those who continued it. If that were the case, the *gemara* notes, why not uproot the *takana* entirely rather than simply shift to Tuesday? A persecuting government that targeted Wednesday weddings would simply target Tuesday weddings next.

*Rava* therefore offers a different account of the danger: *betulah niseit beyom revi'i tiba'el le-hegmon techilah* — when a virgin married on Wednesday, the local ruler or governor would claim the right to sexual relations with the bride before the groom. This was the *droit de seigneur*, a real practice in medieval contexts where local nobles claimed first access to brides on their wedding night. The danger was thus not religious persecution of the *takana* as such, but a specific and predictable abuse that would reliably occur whenever a wedding took place on a fixed, known day. By shifting to Tuesday — a day the governor would not anticipate — the community could avoid the problem, since the ruler simply would not know when to show up.

### Tznuot Resistance

The *gemara* asks: *hai sakana* — why do you call this a *sakana*, a danger to life? *Ones hu* — it is an act of forced sex, an *ones*, but not a life-threatening situation. The woman is not being killed; she is being raped. Why frame it as a *sakana*?

The answer is: *mishum de'ika tznuos de-masran nafshaihu le-katla* — because there are women, the *tznuos*, so deeply modest and principled, that rather than submit they would actually resist unto death. For these women, the situation would genuinely become a *sakana*. *Ve-asyan lidei sakana* — they would come to risk their lives. The *tznuos* here are clearly acting *lifnim mishurat ha-din*, beyond the letter of the law; their resistance, though admirable, is not legally required.

### Submission Permitted

This leads the *gemara* to an important halakhic ruling: *ve-lidrosh lehu de-ones shari* — why not simply teach these women that submission is permitted? One might expect that since the woman is married — already having undergone *kiddushin* — this would fall under *giluy arayos*, which is one of the three cardinal sins subject to the rule of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor*, dying rather than transgressing. Why does the *gemara* assume she may simply submit?

This is a critical *gemara* for the discussion of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* as it applies to *giluy arayos*. *Tosafot* addresses the question with two answers. The first, advanced by *Rabbeinu Tam*, is that sex with a non-Jew belongs to a categorically different legal domain and does not constitute *giluy arayos* for purposes of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor*. *Tosafot* ultimately rejects this approach, though *Rabbeinu Tam* appears to have advanced it to deal with a specific practical case — a Jewish woman who had lived with a non-Jewish man and then sought to return to a Jewish husband after that man converted; *Rabbeinu Tam* argued that her relationship with the non-Jew did not trigger *asura le-bo'el* or the *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* prohibition.

The second answer, which is generally taken as the operative explanation, draws on the principle found in *Sanhedrin*: *isha karka olam* — a woman in the act of rape is categorically passive, and the rule of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* applies only to acts one actively performs. If someone threatens to use a person as an instrument of killing — throwing them onto an infant — the person need not give up their life if they are entirely passive. The question is whether we genuinely conceive of the woman as performing no act in the context of rape.

The *Ramban* offers what is perhaps the most persuasive resolution. He argues that the very nature of rape makes the choice of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* a false one. Even if a woman were to say "kill me rather than rape me," the rapist could choose to rape her anyway. The anatomy and physics of the situation mean that she cannot truly choose martyrdom over the act in the way that a man could, for instance, refuse to perform an idolatrous ritual. It is therefore not genuinely a situation where the rule of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* can be applied, since the act can be imposed on her regardless of her choice. This explains why a married woman may legally submit rather than give up her life.

### Education Risk

The *gemara* asks: if submission is permitted, why not simply educate the women? Why allow the situation to remain one of *sakana* when the *tznuos* could be told that halakha does not require them to die? The *gemara* answers: *ika prutzos ve-ika nami kohanos* — because of two distinct concerns.

The first is the problem of the *prutzos*: a little knowledge in the wrong hands is dangerous. If women are broadly taught that in such circumstances submission is permitted, some will use this information to rationalize submission in cases where it is *not* truly an *ones* — where there is no real compulsion at all. They will claim *ones* retroactively to justify conduct that cannot be justified.

The second is the particular problem of *kohanos* — women married to *kohanim*. Even though submission is legally permitted in a genuine *ones* scenario, the halakhic consequence for a *kohenes* is irreversible: she becomes forbidden to her *kohen* husband. For such women, the calculus is entirely different. They may rationally conclude that the irreversible halakhic consequence of submission — the permanent destruction of their marriage — is worse than the alternative. For them, the situation genuinely remains a *sakana*, and teaching them broadly that submission is permitted would not help them; it might actually harm them by obscuring the specific and severe consequence they face.

The *gemara* therefore maintains the framing of *sakana* rather than *ones*, and explains why public education was not the solution. One might find it deeply troubling that the *tznuos* were left exposed to lethal risk rather than being taught a ruling that would save their lives — all because of concern about the *prutzot* misusing the information. The better reading of the *gemara*, and the one that avoids the alternative implication that the rabbis were prepared to tolerate actual deaths in order to preserve the *takana* of Wednesday weddings, is that the *gemara*'s question is not about whether to move away from Wednesday. Of course the community moved to Tuesday. The *gemara*'s question is only about the framing: why describe the situation as *sakana* rather than *ones*? And the answer is that even with all the education in the world, there would still be *kohanos* for whom it remained a genuine life-threatening situation, and there would still be *prutzos* who would misuse the ruling.

## Takana Preservation

### Gezeira vs. Takana

The question remains: given all of this difficulty, why did the rabbis not simply uproot the *takana* of Wednesday weddings altogether? If the fixed Wednesday date was the source of the danger, why not abolish the fixed day entirely and permit marriage on any day? The *gemara* answers with a foundational principle: *gezeira avidah de-batlah* — a governmental edict will eventually pass away. But *takkanata de-rabbanan mi-kamei gezeira lo akrinan* — we do not uproot a rabbinic ordinance in the face of a governmental edict.

This reflects a deep institutional orientation. The rabbis took a long view. Legal systems and religious frameworks must be built to outlast the particular political pressures of any given era. To dismantle a *takana* every time history presents a threat would be to have no enduring legal structure at all. The *takana* of Wednesday weddings was not abolished; the community adapted around it, found a workaround, and waited for the edict to pass.

### Shift to Tuesday

The adaptation was the shift to Tuesday. The *takana* remained formally in place — Wednesday was still the halakhically ideal day for a virgin to marry — but the custom shifted to Tuesday without the rabbis objecting. The *takana* was not repealed; it was preserved in its theoretical integrity while practical necessity drove behavior in a different direction.

### Tuesday Strategy

The *gemara* asks the obvious question: *ee hachi, beshlishi nami atei u-va'eil* — if they start marrying on Tuesday, won't the governor simply show up on Tuesdays instead? The *gemara* answers: *misfeka lo akar nafshei* — out of uncertainty, he will not uproot himself and come. Once there is no fixed and predictable day — once weddings occur on whatever Tuesday or other day they happen to fall on, with no reliable pattern — the governor cannot know in advance when to appear. He would have to be present every day in every village, which is simply not practical. The uncertainty itself becomes the protective mechanism. The number of successful interceptions drops dramatically. Whereas a fixed Wednesday schedule turned every wedding into a predictable target, the shift to a variable day — theoretically any day, in practice often Tuesday — introduced enough unpredictability to make the abuse far less systematic.

This is how the *takana* was preserved: not by upending it, not by surrendering to the pressure, but by a careful and principled adaptation that maintained the legal framework while rendering the specific vulnerability much harder to exploit.
