# Avodah Zarah 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ad Hominem & Witness

There is a meaningful distinction between an *ad hominem* attack and the legitimate legal act of discrediting a witness. An *ad hominem* argument is a logical fallacy: rather than engaging with the substance of what someone has said, one attacks the character or conduct of the speaker as a way of dismissing the argument altogether. This is fallacious precisely because a person's character and the validity of their reasoning are, in principle, separable matters. Discrediting a witness, by contrast, is an entirely different operation — it removes the witness from the realm of credibility with respect to testimony, which is a legitimate and necessary halachic procedure. The two should not be conflated.

### Daily Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: צַעַר גָּדוֹל הָיָה לִי אֵצֶל רַבִּי אַסִּי, וְרַבִּי אַסִּי אֵצֶל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֵצֶל רַבִּי יַנַּאי, וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי אֵצֶל רַבִּי נָתָן בֶּן עַמְרָם, וְרַבִּי נָתָן בֶּן עַמְרָם אֵצֶל רַבִּי: תִּינוֹק גּוֹי מֵאֵימָתַי מְטַמֵּא בְּזִיבָה? וְאָמַר לִי: בֶּן יוֹמוֹ, וּכְשֶׁבָּאתִי אֵצֶל רַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר לִי: בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Zeira says: I had great trouble with Rabbi Asi** when I asked him the following question, **and** likewise **Rabbi Asi** experienced trouble **with Rabbi Yoḥanan** when he posed it to him. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** had trouble **with Rabbi Yannai, and Rabbi Yannai** had trouble **with Rabbi Natan ben Amram, and Rabbi Natan ben Amram** had trouble **with Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The inquiry was as follows: With regard to a male **gentile child, from when,** i.e., from what age, does he **impart ritual impurity as** one who experiences ***ziva*? And** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to me:** From when he is **one day old. And when I came to Rabbi Ḥiyya, he said to me:** From when he is **nine years and one day old.**

The shiur picks up on Avodah Zarah 36b, toward the bottom of the page, in the midst of the extended back-and-forth regarding what precisely the students of *Shammai* and *Hillel* enacted with respect to *binoteihem* — the relationships between Jewish men and non-Jewish women. Several aspects of the relevant laws had already been traced to earlier origins, including the *beit din* of *David*, the *beit din* of *Shem* the son of *Noach*, and the *Bnei Chashmonaim* during the Hasmonean period. The discussion now continues from where it left off.

## Yichud Laws

### Shammai & Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִיחוּד נָמֵי בֵּית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל דָּוִד גָּזְרוּ! דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה גָּזְרוּ עַל יִיחוּד. אָמְרִי: הָתָם יִיחוּד דְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל יִיחוּד דְּגוֹיָה — לָא, וַאֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ גְּזַרוּ אֲפִילּוּ אַיִּיחוּד דְּגוֹיָה. יִיחוּד דְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: רֶמֶז לְיִיחוּד מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יְסִיתְךָ אָחִיךָ בֶן אִמֶּךָ״, וְכִי בֶּן אֵם מֵסִית, בֶּן אָב אֵינוֹ מֵסִית? אֶלָּא, בֵּן מִתְיַיחֵד עִם אִמּוֹ, וְאֵין אַחֵר מִתְיַיחֵד עִם כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Seclusion** was **also** prohibited earlier, as **the court of** King **David decreed** that with regard to this matter. **As Rav Yehuda says: At that time,** after the incident involving Amnon and Tamar (see II Samuel 13:1–19), **they decreed with regard to seclusion.** The Sages **said** in response to the objection: **There,** in David's court, **seclusion with a Jewish woman** was prohibited, **but seclusion with a gentile woman** was **not** prohibited. **And** the students of Shammai and Hillel **came** and **decreed** a prohibition **even with regard to seclusion with a gentile woman.** The Gemara raises yet another difficulty: **Seclusion with a Jewish woman is** prohibited **by Torah law, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: Where is there an allusion in the Torah to** the prohibition against **seclusion? As it is stated: "If your brother, the son of your mother, entices you"** (Deuteronomy 13:7). **And does only** a half brother who is **the son of a mother entice** one to sin, whereas **the son of a father does not entice?** **Rather,** there is a greater concern that a maternal half brother might entice one to sin, as **a son secludes himself with his mother, and no other may seclude himself with any** of **those with whom relations are forbidden by the Torah.**

The question before the *Gemara* is what specifically was established by the students of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*. Certain dimensions of the prohibition against intermarriage appear to have biblical origins, while others derive from *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, and the precise scope of what the students of *Shammai* and *Hillel* added remained somewhat unclear.

### Brother's Seduction

The *Gemara* cites a teaching regarding *yichud*, the rule that a man and woman may not be together in private even absent any actual sexual contact. The verse cited is *ki yesitcha achicha ben imecha* — that your brother, the son of your mother, will seduce you to worship *avodah zarah*. The *Gemara* asks: does only a maternal brother seduce, but a paternal brother does not? Rather, says the *Gemara*: *ben mityached im imo* — the verse alludes to the fact that a son may be alone with his mother. This establishes that parent-child *yichud* is permitted, while *v'ein acher mityached im kol ha'arayot shebaTorah* — no one else may have *yichud* with any of those forbidden to him under *arayot* in the *Torah*.

### Yichud Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִיחוּד דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא דְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וַאֲתָא דָּוִד וּגְזַר אֲפִילּוּ אַיִּיחוּד דִּפְנוּיָה, וַאֲתוֹ תַּלְמִידֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל גְּזוּר אֲפִילּוּ אַיִּיחוּד דְּגוֹיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The prohibition against **seclusion** prescribed **by Torah law** applies specifically to **a married woman, and David came and decreed** a prohibition **even with regard to seclusion with an unmarried woman. And** later **the students of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel came** and **decreed even with regard to seclusion with a gentile woman.**

The *Gemara* then layers the prohibition: the *de'oraita* dimension of *yichud* applies specifically to *eshet ish*, a married woman. For the other *arayot* — the other categories of forbidden sexual relationships enumerated in the *Torah* — the prohibition of *yichud* is *d'rabbanan*, though it was established at an early period. Subsequently, *va-ata b'David v'gazar afilu al yichud d'penuyah* — the *beit din* of *David* extended the prohibition even to *yichud* between an unmarried man and an unmarried woman, or between a married man and an unmarried woman, even though no explicit prohibition is violated in such a case. One might ask why such *yichud* would be problematic at all, given that an unmarried woman could in principle marry the man in question, and indeed the marriage ceremony itself incorporates a moment of *yichud*. The *beit din* of *David*, however, was concerned with a broader fence against sexual encounters that were deemed socially problematic, even if not technically prohibited.

Finally, *v'atu talmidei Shammai v'Beit Hillel gazaru afilu al yichud d'avodat kochavim* — the students of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* extended the prohibition further still, to *yichud* between a Jewish man and a non-Jewish woman. The underlying assumption that originally might have rendered such *yichud* unproblematic — namely, that a Jewish man would have no business being involved with a non-Jewish woman — was set aside in favor of a more protective fence. The *Gemara*'s framing of *yichud* is generally from the perspective of the man's responsibility. The question of a Jewish woman secluded with a non-Jewish man is addressed differently, with concerns arising primarily in contexts involving coercion.

## Gezeirot Clarified

### Euphemism Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי עַל דָּבָר אַחֵר מִשּׁוּם דָּבָר אַחֵר? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: גָּזְרוּ עַל תִּינוֹק גּוֹי שֶׁיְּטַמֵּא בְּזִיבָה, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא תִּינוֹק יִשְׂרָאֵל רָגִיל אֶצְלוֹ בְּמִשְׁכַּב זְכוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated above that they issued a decree prohibiting the daughters of gentiles due to something else, idolatry. And they further issued a decree on something else due to something else. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: And they further issued a decree **on something else due to something else? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: They decreed upon** a male **gentile child that he imparts ritual impurity as** though he were a Jew who experienced a gonorrhea-like **discharge [*ziva*],** so **that a Jewish child will not become familiar with him,** leading **to homosexual intercourse.** The Sages employed a euphemism when referring to this decree.

The list of *gezeirot* enumerated earlier had stated: *pitanu shemanan mishum yeinam* — they prohibited bread and oil because of wine — *v'yeinam mishum b'noteihem* — and wine because of their daughters, meaning non-Jewish women — and *v'davar acher mishum davar acher* — and something else because of something else. The *Gemara* has now reached the conclusion that the *gezeirah* attributed to *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* regarding *b'noteihem* was the prohibition of *yichud* between a Jewish man and a non-Jewish woman, since the prohibition against intermarriage itself had already been established earlier. The question then becomes: what is the euphemism *davar acher mishum davar acher* pointing to?

### Non-Jewish Boys

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: gazaru al tinok oved kochavim she'itamei b'zivah* — they decreed that a non-Jewish male child conveys impurity with the status of a *zav*, such that sitting on a chair or surface he has rested upon conveys *tumah* — *she'lo yeihei tinok Yisrael ragil etzlo b'mishkav zachor* — in order to prevent close association between Jewish and non-Jewish boys that could lead to homosexual relations or pederasty. From the *Beit Sheni* period there are many sources — both internal to the tradition and external — that reflect the rabbinic class's intense preoccupation with *tumah v'taharah*, sometimes to a troubling degree. The *Tosefta* in *Yoma* records the disturbing account of one *kohen* stabbing another in the *Beit HaMikdash*, whereupon the victim's father rushed to remove the knife before the young man died — not out of concern for his son's life, but lest the knife become *tamei*. That story is meant to sharply reorient our sense of priorities. The term *tinok* in the present context does not mean an infant but a child — and as will become clear, the relevant age is nine years old.

## Age & Tumah

### Age Tradition Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁבָּאתִי וְהִרְצֵיתִי דְּבָרַי לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, אָמַר לִי: הַנַּח דְּבָרַי וֶאֱחוֹז דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא. דְּאָמַר: תִּינוֹק גּוֹי אֵימָתַי מְטַמֵּא בְּזִיבָה — בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד,
>
> **Translation:** **And when I came** back **and relayed** Rabbi Ḥiyya's **statement before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **he said to me: Discard my statement, and grasp the statement of Rabbi Ḥiyya, who says:** From **when** does **a gentile child impart ritual impurity as** one who experiences ***ziva*?** From when he is **nine years and one day old.**

*D'amar Rav Zeira: tza'ar gadol haya etzel Rabbi Assi* — Rav Zeira reports that he experienced great difficulty with Rabbi Assi regarding this question, and Rabbi Assi had had the same difficulty with Rabbi Yochanan, who had it with Rabbi Yannai, who had it with Rabbi Natan ben Amram, who had it with *Rebbi* — that is, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. This chain of student-to-teacher transmission traces the question back to its source. The question itself was: *tinok oveid kochavim me'ematai mitamei be'zivah?* — from what age does a non-Jewish child convey *tumah* with the status of a *zav*? *V'amar li ben yomo* — he was told it was from the very day of birth.

### Rabbi Chiya's Age

*U'keshebati etzel Rabbi Chiya, amar li ben tesha shanim v'yom echad* — but when Rav Zeira came to Rabbi Chiya, Rabbi Chiya said it was from nine years old and one day. This is characteristic of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's noted humility — he is the sage who consistently employs the phrase *omer ani*, placing his own opinion in a subordinate register, and here too he defers to Rabbi Chiya, his somewhat junior colleague and the compiler of the *Tosefta*, when he considers that Rabbi Chiya has the more reliable tradition or the more defensible position.

The *tza'ar gadol* that Rav Zeira experienced was the uncertainty of which opinion to follow — birth or nine years old. Nine years old represents a general halachic threshold for sexual significance with respect to certain questions, distinct from the age of legal majority which remains thirteen. It is worth noting that this threshold almost never corresponds to actual puberty, which is presumed at thirteen. The age of nine seems rather to reflect a recognition that from that age, a sexual act has certain legal consequences — which, in the context of widespread pederasty in the Greco-Roman world, represents a meaningful, if not fully developed, form of legal protection.

### Yichud Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְבִיאָה, מְטַמֵּא נָמֵי בְּזִיבָה. אָמַר רָבִינָא: הִלְכָּךְ, הָא תִּינוֹקֶת גּוֹיָה בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד, הוֹאִיל וּרְאוּיָה לְבִיאָה, מְטַמְּאָה נָמֵי בְּזִיבָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הַאי יָדַע לְאַרְגּוֹלֵי, וְהָא לָא יָדְעָה לְאַרְגּוֹלֵי? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the reason for this opinion: **Since** a nine-year-old boy **is fit to** engage in **intercourse, he also imparts ritual impurity as** one who experienced ***ziva*. Ravina said: Therefore,** with regard to **a female gentile child** who **is three years and one day old, since she is fit to** engage in **intercourse** at that age, **she also imparts impurity as** one who experienced ***ziva*.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara explains: It was necessary to state this ruling, **lest you say** that the *halakha* that a gentile who is suited for intercourse imparts impurity does not apply to a female. The possible difference between a male and female child is based on the fact that whereas **that** child, a nine-year-old male gentile, **knows** how **to accustom** others to sin by employing persuasion, **this** child, a three-year-old female gentile, **does not know** how **to accustom** others to sin until she matures. Therefore, Ravina **teaches us** that the *halakha* nevertheless applies to both male and female children.

The *Gemara* clarifies Rabbi Chiya's reasoning: *ho'il v'ra'ui l'bi'ah mitamei nami be'zivah* — since from age nine and one day a boy's participation in a sexual act is legally significant, an analogous legal significance is created for the laws of *tumah v'taharah*, specifically the status of *zivah*. There may also be a connection to the medical phenomenon that *zivah* is sometimes associated with a condition resembling gonorrhea; in any case, the *Gemara* is creating a kind of constructed *zav* status for non-Jewish boys from age nine onward, linked to the threshold of sexual significance.

### Ravina Girls

*Amar Ravina hilkach, ha-tinoket ovedet kochavim bat shalosh shanim v'yom echad* — Ravina extends the same logic to non-Jewish girls, concluding that they convey *tumah* with the status of a *zavah* from age three years and one day, since that is the corresponding halachic threshold of sexual significance for girls. One might ask whether this ruling is not obvious — if we established the same analogy for boys, should it not follow automatically for girls? The *Gemara* addresses this: *mahu detema hai yada le-argulei* — one might have thought that a nine-year-old boy can actively entice or seduce, whereas a three-year-old girl has no such capacity or inclination. *Ka mashma lan* — Ravina's point is that the legal construction is applied across the board regardless of this asymmetry, even where it is considerably less intuitive. It should be noted that this decree regarding non-Jewish girls applies specifically to them; for a Jewish girl or woman, the laws of *niddah* and *zivah* apply from the time she has the relevant physical discharge, regardless of age. Medical literature attests to the fact that even newborn girls can exhibit vaginal discharge of a sort that *Chazal* engaged with halachically, and this will be developed more fully when *Masechet Niddah* is reached.

## Oil Court

### Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיסְתְּמִיךְ וְאָזֵיל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה אַכַּתְפֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי שַׁמָּעֵיהּ, אָמַר לוֹ: שִׂמְלַאי, לֹא הָיִיתָ אֶמֶשׁ בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ כְּשֶׁהִתַּרְנוּ אֶת הַשֶּׁמֶן. אָמַר לוֹ: בְּיָמֵינוּ תַּתִּיר אַף אֶת הַפַּת! אָמַר לוֹ: אִם כֵּן קָרוּ לַן ״בֵּית דִּינָא שָׁרְיָא״! דִּתְנַן: הֵעִיד רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה עַל אַיַּיל קַמְצָא דְּכַן, וְעַל מַשְׁקֵה בֵּית מַטְבְּחַיָּא דְּכַן, וְעַל דְּיִקְרַב לְמִיתָא מְסָאַב, וְקָרוּ לֵיהּ ״יוֹסֵף שָׁרְיָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a relevant incident: **Rabbi Yehuda Nesia was traveling** while **leaning upon the shoulder of Rabbi Simlai, his attendant.** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to him: Simlai, you were not in the study hall last night when we permitted the oil** of gentiles. Rabbi Simlai **said to him: In our days, you will permit bread** of gentiles **as well.** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to him: If so,** people will **call us a permissive court. As we learned** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 8:4): **Rabbi Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida testified with regard to** the ***eil kamtza*,** a type of locust, that it is **kosher, and with regard to the liquids of the slaughterhouse** in the Temple that they are **ritually pure, and with regard to one who touches a corpse** that he is **impure,** as soon explained by the Gemara. **And** as a result, **they called him: Yosef the Permissive.**

The *Mishna* had stated that oil was prohibited by *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, and then permitted by *Rebbi*, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi the author of the *Mishna*. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia is the grandson of *Rebbi*, and the story as told here raises some difficulty: it seems to place the grandson in a first-person relationship to a court decision that one might have expected his grandfather to have made. It is possible that Rabbi Yehuda Nesia was a precocious young member of his grandfather's *beit din*, and thus present and involved when the oil was permitted — which would at least explain the personal terms of the narrative, even if some details remain puzzling.

*Amar lei Simlai: lav hayita emesh be-veit hamidrash keshe-hitarnu et ha-shemen* — so Rabbi Yehuda Nesia said to Rabbi Simlai: were you not in the *beit midrash* when we permitted the oil? *Amar lo beyameinu tatir af et ha-pat* — Rabbi Simlai replied: in your time you will probably permit even bread. He was alluding to the fact that *Rebbi* had already created some opening with respect to the conditions under which *pat akum* is prohibited, and now the grandson had permitted oil — it seemed a pattern was forming. *Amar lo im ken karu lan bei dina sharya* — Rabbi Yehuda Nesia replied: if so, they will call my court a permissive *beit din*, which is not a desirable reputation.

The basis of his concern is drawn from the precedent of *Masechet Eduyot*: *he'id Rabbi Yose ben Yoezer ish Tzeredah* — Yose ben Yoezer of Tzeredah testified three lenient rulings, and on account of them was given the epithet *Yosef Sharya* — the permissive Yosef.

### Yosef Sharya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם שְׁרָא תְּלָת, וּמַר שְׁרָא חֲדָא, וְאִי שָׁרֵי מָר חֲדָא אַחֲרִיתִי, אַכַּתִּי תַּרְתֵּין הוּא דְּהָוְיָין! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא [נָמֵי] שְׁרַאי אַחֲרִיתִי. מַאי הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Simlai **said to him: There,** Yosei ben Yo'ezer **permitted three** matters, **but the Master** has **permitted** only **one, and** even **if the Master permits one other** matter, these **will still constitute** only **two** permissive rulings. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to him: I** have already **permitted another** matter. The Gemara asks: **What is** the other matter that he permitted?

Rabbi Yehuda Nesia explains the arithmetic of his reluctance. Yose ben Yoezer had permitted three things and earned the reputation of a permissive authority. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia had already permitted one thing — the oil — and had a second permissive ruling on another matter, which he reveals momentarily. Were he to permit bread as well, he would have three, and thus earn the same epithet. His interlocutor points out that even permitting bread would give him only two, to which he responds that he already has a second leniency that he had not yet mentioned.

## Get After Death

### Conditional Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ אִם לֹא בָּאתִי מִכָּאן עַד שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ, וּמֵת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ — אֵינוֹ גֵּט, וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: וְרַבּוֹתֵינוּ הִתִּירוּהָ לִינָּשֵׂא, וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאן ״רַבּוֹתֵינוּ״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בֵּית דִּינָא דִּשְׁרוֹ מִשְׁחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that this is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 76b) that if one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce if I do not arrive from now until twelve months'** time, **and he died within twelve months,** then **it is not** a valid **bill of divorce** because it would not take effect until after the husband's death. **And it is taught with regard to** that mishna that **our Rabbis** nevertheless **permitted her to marry.** The Gemara continues: **And we say: Who** is the mishna referring to when it mentions **our Rabbis? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** This is referring to **the court that permitted the oil** of gentiles.

The answer is found in a *Mishna*: *ze gittech im lo bati mikan ve'ad shneim asar chodesh* — this is your *get*, if I do not return from here within twelve months. That is, a man departing on a long journey gives his wife a *get* now, contingent on his not returning within twelve months. *U'meit betoch shneim asar chodesh, eino get* — if he dies during the twelve months, it is not a valid *get*, because of the principle *ein get le'achar mita* — one cannot give a *get* retroactively after death. A deceased person has no legal standing to transfer a document, and moreover such a mechanism could be used to undermine the institution of *yibbum*, since the woman would not be subject to *yibbum* if she were already divorced.

### HaNasi Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: זְמַנּוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא הוֹרָה, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ כׇּל שְׁעָתוֹ, וְאָמְרִי לָהּ: כׇּל סִיעָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Tangentially, the Gemara examines the reason for the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia's court concerning a bill of divorce. **They hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says: The date** written **in a document proves** when **it** takes effect. In other words, the bill of divorce takes effect at the time written on it. Therefore, the divorce actually goes into effect before the husband's death, because it is retroactively initiated on the day that the bill was issued. The Gemara adds: **And Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, says:** In an earlier period, **Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi** also **ruled** that the bill of divorce should be valid, **but** the other Sages **did not concede to** his opinion during **his entire lifetime [*sha'ato*]. And some say** that **all of his colleagues [*si'ato*]** did not concede to his opinion.

*Ve'amar Rabbi Abba breih de'Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba: de'Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi horah velo hodu lo* — Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba, reported that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — grandfather of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia — had attempted to institute a more permissive ruling on this question, but the other rabbis did not agree with him. *Kol sha'ato* — throughout his entire lifetime they never accepted his position. An alternative version reads *kol sayato* — not during his whole life, but during his tenure: none of his supporters accepted it.

### Nesi'ah Permits

*Ve'amrinan: man Rabboseinu? Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: be dina desharu mshacha* — when the text says *rabboseinu* permitted the woman to remarry, the *Gemara* asks whose court this was. Rav Yehuda, quoting Shmuel in *Bavel*, says it was the same *beit din* that permitted the oil. That is, it was the *beit din* of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia — which is again slightly puzzling, since the grandfather had tried to enact the same ruling and been overruled. The *Gemara* explains that this position rested on the view of Rabbi Yosi.

*Savri lah ke'Rabbi Yosei, de'amar zmano shel shtar mochiach alav* — the court followed Rabbi Yosi's principle that the date written in the *shtar* is indicative of when the document is intended to take effect. Applied to the *get*: if the man gives the *get* today with a condition about the future, the date on the *get* demonstrates his intent that it should take effect from the moment of delivery. Therefore, if he dies before the twelve months are up, the *get* is not a *get le'achar mita* — it retroactively took effect at the time it was given, the condition having subsequently been fulfilled. The woman is thus free to remarry.

The question was raised regarding Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's situation: does his opinion retain any validity if his court overruled him? The answer is clearly no — he is first among equals, a voting member of the *sanhedrin* with one vote. The *Nasi* holds great authority and seniority, and by convention speaks last, but he does not override a majority ruling. When his grandson later took the same position and convened his own court, that court ruled in accordance with Rabbi Yosi, and the ruling stood.

### M'nat vs Ki

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים ״לִכְשֶׁתֵּצֵא חַמָּה מִנַּרְתִּיקָהּ״ — לְכִי נָפְקָא קָאָמַר לַהּ, וְכִי מָיֵית בְּלֵילְיָא — גֵּט לְאַחַר מִיתָה הוּא. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתֵּצֵא חַמָּה מִנַּרְתִּיקָהּ״ — מֵעַכְשָׁיו קָאָמַר לָהּ, וְכִי מָיֵית בְּלֵילְיָא — הָא וַדַּאי תְּנָאָה הָוֵי, וְגֵט מֵחַיִּים הוּא, כִּדְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּ״אִם תֵּצֵא״, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: זְמַנּוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּ״מֵהַיּוֹם אִם מַתִּי״, כְּ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו אִם מַתִּי״, וְרַבָּנַן לֵית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּ״זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ אִם מַתִּי״ גְּרֵידָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: All concur** that one who says that a bill of divorce will take effect **once the sun emerges from its sheath is saying to** his wife that it will be valid **once** the sun **comes out** in the morning. **And** therefore, if the husband **dies during the night,** before sunrise, **it is a posthumous bill of divorce,** which is invalid. Furthermore, if he said to her: **On the condition that the sun will come out of its sheath,** then **he is saying to** his wife that the bill of divorce will take effect retroactively **from now,** on the condition that the sun emerges. **And** accordingly, **if he dies during the night, this is certainly** a fulfilled **condition, and it is a bill of divorce** which takes effect retroactively, **while** he is **alive; in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Huna. As Rav Huna says:** With regard to **anyone who states** a provision employing the language: **On the condition,** it **is tantamount to his stating** in the provision that the document takes effect retroactively **from now.** **They disagreed only in** the case of one who said to his wife: This will be your bill of divorce **if** the sun **emerges** from its sheath, and the husband died during the night. **Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who says** that **the date** written **in a document proves** when **it** takes effect, **and it is** therefore **considered as though** the husband said: **From today if I die,** or **as though** he said: **From now if I die. And the Sages do not** accept the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei, and** consequently they maintain that **it is considered as though** the husband said **only: This is your bill of divorce if I die,** in which case the bill of divorce is not valid, as it would take effect only after the husband's death.

*Amar Abaye: hakol modim l'chsheteitzei chamah minartikei* — Abaye introduces a related case. If a man gives his wife a *get* and says it should take effect *l'chi nafka* — when the sun comes out of its pouch — meaning at sunrise the next morning, and the man dies during the night before sunrise: *ki meit beleilaya get l'achar mita hu* — that is a *get le'achar mita*, because he used the language *l'chi nafka*, making the effectiveness of the *get* depend on the future moment of the condition's fulfillment, not on the present moment of its delivery.

But if he said *al m'nat sheteitzei chamah minartikei* — on the condition that the sun comes out of its pouch — then *me'achshav ka'amar lah*: using the formulation *al m'nat* is equivalent to saying the *get* should take effect now, contingent on the later condition. *Hu kidei Rav Huna, de'amar Rav Huna: kol ha-omer al m'nat ke-omer me'achshav dami* — this is consistent with Rav Huna's general rule that whoever says *al m'nat* is treated as though he said *me'achshav*, from right now. The *get* is thus a *get m'chayim* — a *get* given while the husband was alive — and the condition's subsequent fulfillment does not create the problem of *get le'achar mita*.

*Lo nechelku ela be-im* — the dispute between the authorities concerns the intermediate case where the man uses the word *im*, if: *im tetzei chamah minartikei*, if the sun comes out of its pouch. Is *im* more like *ki* — in which case the *get* only takes effect when the condition is fulfilled — or more like *al m'nat* — in which case the *get* takes effect now and the condition retroactively validates it? On this question the *poskim* divide.

It is worth noting in this context the distinction between *al m'nat* as a retroactive condition and *l'chi nafka* as a delayed effect. These two formulations have very different consequences precisely for the question of *get le'achar mita*. If the *get* only takes effect when the condition is fulfilled, and the husband has died before that point, then the *get* is *le'achar mita* and invalid. If the *get* takes effect now, with the later condition retroactively confirming its validity, then the husband's death in the interim is not an obstacle, provided the condition is ultimately satisfied.

### Shtar Date

Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi savar la ke'Rabbi Yossi, de'amar zmano shel shtar mochiach alav, ve'hava lei k'me'hayom* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi held, following Rabbi Yosi, that the date written in the *shtar* demonstrates that the parties intended the document to take effect from that date, making it equivalent to saying *me'hayom*, from today, and thus *me'achshav im meti* — from now if I die. *Ve'rabbanan leit lehu de'Rabbi Yossi, ve'hava lei k'zeh giteich im meti gerada* — the other rabbis rejected Rabbi Yosi's principle and therefore read the *get* as saying: this is your *get* if I die, without any retroactive element, which then fails the *get le'achar mita* problem.

Why did Rabbi Yehuda Nesia not want to be called *beit din sharya*? A question along these lines was posed to Rabbi Yosef Chaim Sonnenfeld, one of the leading *Charedi* authorities of early 20th-century Jerusalem. Someone pointed out: is it not a virtue to be lenient? Do we not say *koach de'hetera adifa lei* — that demonstrating the ability to permit reflects greater confidence and mastery of halacha than simply prohibiting? Rabbi Sonnenfeld replied: yes, *koach de'hetera adifa lei* applies, but there are limits that one does not cross. One should not develop a reputation of universal leniency, because that reputation erodes the credibility of one's rulings when stringency is genuinely required, and it can tempt people to treat uncertain cases as though they are clear. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia's hesitation reflects precisely this concern — not a rejection of the value of leniency, but an awareness of when that value has reached its threshold.

## Yoezer Leniencies

### Ayal Kamtza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: הֵעִיד יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר אִישׁ צְרֵידָה עַל אַיַּיל קַמְצָא דְּכַן, וְעַל מַשְׁקֵה בֵּי מַטְבְּחַיָּא דְּכַן, וְעַל דְּיִקְרַב לְמִיתָא מְסָאַב, וְקָרוּ לֵיהּ ״יוֹסֵף שָׁרְיָא״. מַאי אַיַּיל קַמְצָא? רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: שׁוֹשִׁיבָא, וְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַמֵּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא אָמַר: סוּסְבִּיל. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: שׁוֹשִׁיבָא, וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּרֹאשׁוֹ אָרוֹךְ. מָר סָבַר: רֹאשׁוֹ אָרוֹךְ אָסוּר, וּמָר סָבַר: רֹאשׁוֹ אָרוֹךְ מוּתָּר. רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַמֵּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא אָמַר: סוּסְבִּיל, בְּרֹאשׁוֹ אָרוֹךְ — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאָסוּר, וְהָכָא בִּכְנָפָיו חוֹפִין אֶת רוּבּוֹ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר רוּבָּא כֹּל דְּהוּ בָּעֵינַן, וּמָר סָבַר רוּבָּא דְּמִנְּכַר בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself: Yosei ben Yo'ezer of Tzereida testified with regard to** the ***eil kamtza*** that it is **kosher, and with regard to the liquids of the slaughterhouse** in the Temple that they are **ritually pure, and with regard to one who touches a corpse** that he is **impure. And** as a result, **they called him: Yosef the Permissive.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the ***eil kamtza*? Rav Pappa says:** It is a long-headed locust called ***shoshiva*, and Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami says in the name of Ulla:** It is a locust called ***susbil*.** The Gemara explains: **Rav Pappa says** it is **a *shoshiva*, and** accordingly Yosei ben Yo'ezer and the other Rabbis **disagree with regard to a long-headed** locust: One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **a long-headed** locust is **prohibited, and** one **Sage,** Yosei ben Yo'ezer, **holds** that **a long-headed** locust is **permitted. Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami says in the name of Ulla** that it is **a *susbil*, and** accordingly, **with regard to a long-headed** locust, **everyone agrees that** it is **prohibited. And here they disagree with regard** to a locust **whose wings barely cover most of its** body: One **Sage,** Yosei ben Yo'ezer, **holds** that **we require** only **a minimal majority** of the locust's body to be covered by its wings, **and** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **we require a noticeable majority** of the body to be covered.

*Gufa* — the *Gemara* now returns to the three rulings of Yose ben Yoezer themselves. *He'id Yossi ben Yoezer ish Tzreida: al ayal kamtza dachan* — he testified that the *ayal kamtza* locust is *tahor*, that is, kosher. *Ve'al mashke beit mitbachaya dachan* — that the liquids in the slaughterhouse of the Temple are *tahor*. *Ve'al di-krav le-meita mesta'av* — and that one who comes in contact with a corpse becomes *tamei*. *Vekaru lei Yosef Sharya* — and they called him the permissive Yosef.

*Mai ayal kamtza?* — What is the *ayal kamtza*? Rav Pappa said it is the *shushiva*, and Rav Chiyya bar Ami in the name of Ulla said it is the *susbil*. These are evidently two different species or descriptions of locusts, and we no longer have reliable knowledge of the precise taxonomy. Some communities still claim a *mesora* regarding which locusts are kosher; many have lost that *mesora*, which is a significant loss. *Rashi* gives the description in four words: *min chagav verosho aruch* — a type of locust with a long head.

*Rav Pappa amar Shushiva vekamipalgi berosho aruch* — the debate between Rav Pappa and Rav Chiyya bar Ami concerns the long head. One holds that a locust with a long head is prohibited; the other holds that one with a long head is in fact permitted. *Rav Chiyya bar Ami mishmei de'Ulla amar Susbil*, and *Rashi* explains regarding the *susbil* that it does not have a long head, *aval ein knafav chofin et rubo*, but its wings do not cover the majority of its body in a clearly visible way — only barely, *betzimtzum*. The question then is what kind of *rov* the halacha requires for the wings to "cover the majority of the body," one of the conditions for a kosher locust: does it suffice that the wings cover a bare majority of 50.0001%, or must the majority be visibly discernible? *Mar savar rova kol dehu* — one holds that any majority suffices; *umar savar rova deminchar ba'ina* — the other holds that a visible majority is required.

As for *kulei alma lo pligi de'assur* — all agree that a locust with a long head is prohibited. The debate concerns only the *susbil*, whose wings barely cover the majority of the body: *vehacha bichnafav chofin et rubo al yedei hadachak kamipalgi* — and this case is the subject of the dispute.

### Temple Liquids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל מַשְׁקֵה בֵּי מַטְבְּחַיָּא דְּכַן. מַאי ״דְּכַן״? רַב אָמַר: דְּכַן מַמָּשׁ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: דְּכַן מִלְּטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים, אֲבָל טוּמְאַת עַצְמָן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן. רַב אָמַר: ״דְּכַן מַמָּשׁ״, קָסָבַר: טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין דְּרַבָּנַן, וְכִי גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן טוּמְאָה בְּמַשְׁקִין דְּעָלְמָא, אֲבָל בְּמַשְׁקֵה בֵּי מַטְבְּחַיָּא לָא גְּזַרוּ רַבָּנַן. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: דְּכַן מִלְּטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים, אֲבָל טוּמְאַת עַצְמָן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן, קָסָבַר: טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא לְטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים דְּרַבָּנַן, וְכִי גְּזַרוּ רַבָּנַן בְּמַשְׁקִין דְּעָלְמָא, בְּמַשְׁקִין בֵּי מַטְבְּחַיָּא לָא גְּזַרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated above: **And** Yosei ben Yo'ezer testified **with regard to the liquids of the slaughterhouse** in the Temple that they are **ritually pure.** The Gemara asks: **What** did Yosei ben Yo'ezer mean when he said they are **pure? Rav says:** He meant that they are **actually ritually pure. And Shmuel says: They are pure** in the sense that they do not **impart ritual impurity to other** substances; **but they themselves** can contract **impurity.** The Gemara explains the reasons for these opinions. **Rav says** that these liquids are **actually pure,** as **he maintains** that **the ritual impurity of liquids** applies **by rabbinic law, and when the Sages decreed impurity** upon liquids, they did so only **with regard to ordinary liquids. But the Sages did not issue** their **decree with regard to the liquids of the slaughterhouse** in the Temple. **And Shmuel says:** The liquids are **ritually pure** in the sense that they do not **impart impurity to other** substances; **but they themselves** can contract **impurity,** as Shmuel **maintains** that the **ritual impurity of liquids** themselves is **by Torah law,** whereas their capacity **to impart impurity to other** substances is **by rabbinic law. And when the Sages issued** this **decree,** they did so only with regard **to ordinary liquids.** But **they did not issue** their **decree** with regard **to the liquids of the slaughterhouse** in the Temple.

*Al mashke bei mitbachaya dachyan* — regarding the liquids from the Temple slaughterhouse being *tahor*: this topic receives extensive treatment in the second chapter of *Pesachim*, and the *sugya* there should be consulted. It is worth noting the use of the Aramaic *dachya* for *tahor*, which is somewhat unusual in the *Talmud* in this form.

*Rav amar dachyan mamash* — Rav says they are actually and fully *tahor*. *Kasavar tumat mashkin derabbanan* — Rav holds that the entire institution of *tumat mashkin*, whereby the seven liquids can become *tamei*, is only a rabbinic law. *Vechi gazru rabbanan tumah bemashkim de'alma, aval bemashke bei mitbachaya lo gazru rabbanan* — and when the rabbis instituted *tumah* for liquids, they applied it to ordinary liquids in the world but did not apply it to the liquids of the Temple slaughterhouse.

*Ushmuel amar dachyan miletamei acherim, aval tumat atzman yesh bahem* — Shmuel says they are *tahor* only in the sense that they cannot make other things *tamei*, but they can themselves become *tamei*. *Kasavar tumat mashkin de'oraita, letamei acherim derabbanan* — Shmuel holds that liquids becoming *tamei* is a *de'oraita* rule, but their capacity to convey that *tumah* to other objects or persons is only *derabbanan*. Crucially, both Rav and Shmuel agree that whatever rabbinic law was instituted regarding liquids applies only outside the Temple, not within it; their dispute concerns what is *de'oraita* and what is *derabbanan*. According to Rav, nothing in *tumat mashkin* is *de'oraita*; according to Shmuel, the capacity to become *tamei* is *de'oraita*, but the capacity to convey *tumah* is *derabbanan*.

One might note that the *Torah* does speak of liquids conveying *tumah* — for instance, *vechi yarak hazav al hatahor* — but that verse concerns *ruko shel zav*, the bodily emissions of a person who is a *zav*, which operate not through the law of *mashkin* but as an extension of the *zav*'s own body. *Tumat mashkin* refers specifically to the seven liquids: water, dew, blood, honey, milk, wine, and olive oil. These are the liquids relevant both to the laws of *machshirin* — if they fall on a food, that food becomes susceptible to *tumah* — and to *tumat mashkin* proper, which concerns whether those liquids themselves can become *tamei*. The two principles are distinct.

Either way, Yose ben Yoezer's ruling was a leniency: whether he meant the liquids cannot become *tamei* at all, or that they cannot convey *tumah* to other things, both readings represent a more lenient position than what preceded him.

### Corpse Impurity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל דְּיִקְרַב לְמִיתָא מְסָאַב, וְקָרוּ לֵיהּ ״יוֹסֵף שָׁרְיָא״. ״יוֹסֵף אָסְרָא״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! וְעוֹד, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה בַּחֲלַל חֶרֶב אוֹ בְמֵת וְגוֹ׳״! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּיִקְרַב — טָמֵא, דְּיִקְרַב בִּדְיִקְרַב — טָהוֹר, וַאֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ וּגְזוּר אֲפִילּוּ דְּיִקְרַב בִּדְיִקְרַב, וַאֲתָא אִיהוּ וְאוֹקְמַהּ אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated: **And** Yosei ben Yo'ezer testified **with regard to one who touches a corpse** that he is **impure, and** as a result **they called him: Yosef the Permissive.** The Gemara questions this: Since he issued a stringent ruling, **they should have** called him: **Yosef the Prohibiting. And furthermore,** this *halakha* **is** explicitly written **in the Torah, as it is written: "And whosoever in the open field touches one that is slain with a sword, or one that is dead,** or a bone of a man, or a grave, shall be impure seven days" (Numbers 19:16). The Gemara explains: **By Torah law one who touches** a corpse is **ritually impure,** but **one who touches** another **who** has **touched** a corpse is **pure. And** the Sages **came and decreed** that **even one who touches** another **who** has **touched** a corpse is also impure. **And** Yosei ben Yo'ezer **came and established** the *halakha* **in accordance with** the original, more lenient **Torah law.**

*Ve'al di-krav le-meita mesta'av* — and the third ruling, that one who comes into contact with a corpse becomes *tamei*. *Vekaro lei Yosef Sharya* — and yet they called him the permissive Yosef. The difficulty is immediate: *Yosef assara mibayei lei* — this ruling sounds like a stringency, not a leniency. Furthermore, *de'oraita hi* — touching a corpse renders one *tamei* by explicit *Torah* law, as stated in the verse *vechol asher yiga al penei hasadeh bechalal cherev o vemet*. What leniency is involved?

The answer requires understanding an important distinction in the *de'oraita* law. *De'oraita dikrav tamei, dikrav bedikrav tahor* — the *Torah* law itself imposes *tumah* only on one who directly touches the corpse. If you touch something that was touching the corpse — one step removed — you are *tahor* under *Torah* law. *Va'atu inhu ve'gazru afilu dikrav bedikrav* — the rabbis subsequently decreed that even contact with something that touched the corpse conveys *tumah*.

*V'ata ihu ve'ukma ade'oraita* — and Yose ben Yoezer came and said: return to the *de'oraita* rule. *Dikrav bedikrav nami* — he rescinded the rabbinic stringency that applied *tumah* to the second degree of contact and restored the original *de'oraita* standard, which holds the one-step-removed contact to be *tahor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּיִקְרַב בִּדְיִקְרַב נָמֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בּוֹ הַטָּמֵא יִטְמָא״! אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּמָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא דְּיִקְרַב בִּדְיִקְרַב, בְּחִיבּוּרִין — טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה, שֶׁלֹּא בְּחִיבּוּרִין — טוּמְאַת עֶרֶב, וַאֲתוֹ אִינְהוּ וּגְזוּר אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּחִיבּוּרִין טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה, וַאֲתָא אִיהוּ וְאוֹקְמַהּ אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא מַאי הִיא? דִּכְתִיב: ״הַנֹּגֵעַ בְּמֵת לְכׇל נֶפֶשׁ אָדָם וְטָמֵא שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בּוֹ הַטָּמֵא יִטְמָא״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ הַנֹּגַעַת תִּטְמָא עַד הָעָרֶב״, הָא כֵּיצַד? כָּאן בְּחִיבּוּרִין, כָּאן שֶׁלֹּא בְּחִיבּוּרִין. אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא: לָאו אָמֵינָא לְכוּ לָא תִּתְלוֹ בֵּיהּ בּוּקֵי סְרִיקֵי בְּרַב נַחְמָן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **One who touches** another **who** has **touched** a corpse **is also** rendered impure **by Torah law, as it is written: "And whatsoever the impure person touches shall be impure"** (Numbers 19:22). **The Sages stated this** difficulty **before Rava in the name of Mar Zutra, the son of Rav Naḥman, who said** a response **in the name of Rav Naḥman: By Torah law,** one **who touches** another **who touches** a corpse **while** the second individual is **in concurrent contact** with the corpse is impure with **seven-**day **impurity.** If this occurs **while** the second individual is **not in concurrent contact** with the corpse, he contracts **impurity** until the **evening. And** the Sages **came and decreed that even** where there is **no concurrent contact,** one still contracts **seven-**day **impurity** when he touches someone who touched a corpse. **And** subsequently Yosei ben Yo'ezer **came and established** the *halakha* **in accordance with** the original **Torah law.** **What is** the source of this *halakha*, prescribed **by Torah law? As it is written: "He that touches the dead, even any man's dead body, shall be impure seven days"** (Numbers 19:11), **and it is written: "And whatsoever the impure person touches shall be impure"** (Numbers 19:22). These two verses indicate that one contracts ritual impurity for seven days. **And** yet **it is** also **written: "And the soul that touches him shall be impure until evening"** (Numbers 19:22). **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? **Here,** in the first two verses, the Torah is discussing **concurrent contact,** which results in impurity of seven days; **there,** in the last verse, it is discussing a case **where** there is **no concurrent contact,** and therefore the individual in question is impure only until the evening. **Rava said to** the Sages who suggested that explanation citing Rav Naḥman: **Didn't I tell you not to hang empty pitchers [*bukei*] upon Rav Naḥman,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: סְפֵק טוּמְאָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים הִתִּיר לָהֶן. וְהָא הִלְכְתָא מִסּוֹטָה גָּמְרִינַן לַהּ, מָה סוֹטָה רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, אַף טוּמְאָה רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד! הָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה וְאֵין מוֹרִין כֵּן, וַאֲתָא אִיהוּ וְאוֹרִי לֵיהּ אוֹרוֹיֵי. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, קוֹרוֹת נָעַץ לָהֶם, וְאָמַר: עַד כָּאן רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, עַד כָּאן רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד. כִּי אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָא מַיָּא בְּשִׁיקַעְתָּא דִּבְנַהֲרָא, זִילוּ טְבוּלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **this is** what **Rav Naḥman said:** Yosei ben Yo'ezer **permitted for them** a case of **uncertain impurity** contracted **in a public domain.** In other words, Yosei ben Yo'ezer ruled leniently that one who is unsure whether or not he came in contact with a corpse in the public domain is ritually pure. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't we learn** this ***halakha* from** the case of **a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful [*sota*]: Just as a *sota*** can be made to drink the bitter waters only when she is suspected of engaging in adultery in **a private domain, so too,** uncertain **ritual impurity** is considered impure only when one suspects that he came into contact with it in **a private domain?** This shows that even by Torah law one who is unsure whether or not he touched a corpse in the public domain remains pure. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** in explanation: This is the ***halakha*, but** a public **ruling is not issued to that effect.** Consequently, the masses treated this matter with stringency. **And** Yosei ben Yo'ezer **came and instructed** the masses to follow the original **instruction** of the Torah. Therefore, his ruling was in fact a leniency. The Gemara provides support for Rabbi Yoḥanan's explanation. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that Yosei ben Yo'ezer **drove stakes** into the ground **for** the people **and said: Until here** is **the public domain,** and **until there** is **the private domain,** so that they would know the *halakha* if they suspected that they had touched a corpse. The Gemara relates that **when** people **came before Rabbi Yannai** because they suspected that they might have come into contact with a source of impurity in the public domain, **he said to them:** Why involve yourselves in matters of uncertainty? **There is deep water in the river; go immerse** yourselves in it, and resolve the problem in this manner.

*Amaru rabanan kamei d'Rava mishmei d'Mar Zutra brei d'Rav Nachman, d'amar mishmei d'Rav Nachman: d'Oraita d'krav b'd'krav b'chiburin tumat shiva* — there is, however, a further refinement. The rabbis reported to Rava in the name of Mar Zutra son of Rav Nachman, quoting Rav Nachman: the *de'oraita* rule is that if you touch something which is *currently attached* to the corpse — *b'chiburin* — then even that one-step-removed contact carries *tumat shiva*, seven days of *tumah*, because the attached object is considered an extension of the body itself. *Shelo b'chiburin, tumat erev* — but if the object is no longer attached, then contact with it produces only one day of *tumah*, resolved by immersion in a *mikveh*.

*V'atu inhu vegazru afilu shelo b'chiburin tumat shiva* — the rabbis then extended the seven-day *tumah* even to objects no longer attached to the body. *V'ata ihu ve'ukma ade'oraita* — and Yose ben Yoezer came and said: let us return to the *de'oraita* rule, under which contact with something detached from a corpse carries only one day of *tumah*.

The biblical basis: *hanogea b'met l'chol nefesh adam v'tamei shivat yamim* — one who touches a corpse is *tamei* for seven days. *V'chol asher yiga bo hatamei yitma* — and whatever the *tamei* person touches also becomes *tamei*. *Hanefesh hanogaat titema ad haerev* — but the one who touches a *tamei* person or object is *tamei* only until evening. *Hekeitzad?* — How are these verses reconciled? *Kan b'chiburin kan shelo b'chiburin* — the first case is where the object was attached to the corpse, which conveys seven-day *tumah*; the second is where the object was separate, which conveys only one-day *tumah*.

*Amar lehu Rava: lav amina l'chu lo titlu bi buki sariki* — Rava said to those who had reported Rav Nachman's statement: did I not tell you not to attribute to me empty vessels? *Rashi* glosses this as *kadim reikim* — empty jars, meaning they had gotten the teaching wrong. *Ela Rav Nachman hachi amar Rav Nachman* — and Rava clarified: this is what Rav Nachman actually said.

## Safek Tumah Public

### Reshut Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְפֵק טוּמְאָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים הִתִּיר לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Yosei ben Yo'ezer **permitted for them** a case of **uncertain impurity** contracted **in a public domain.**

*Safek tumah birshut harabim hitir lahem* — Yose ben Yoezer instituted the ruling that in a doubtful case of *tumah* occurring in a public domain, one is *tahor*. Until this point, the principle had been that every *safek de'oraita* is treated *l'chumra* — stringently, as *tamei*. Yose ben Yoezer said: in *reshut hayachid*, a private domain, a *safek* of *tumah* is treated stringently; but *safek tumah birshut harabim* is treated leniently, and the person is considered *tahor*. *V'ha hilcheta miSotah gamrinan lah* — this law is derived by analogy from the laws of *Sotah*: just as the seclusion of *Sotah* is necessarily *birshut hayachid* — in private — and creates a doubtful case, so too the law of *safek tumah birshut hayachid* is *tamei*, while *birshut harabim* is *tahor*.

### Waters Shik'ata

The *tza'ar gadol* that Rav Zeira experienced was the uncertainty of which opinion to follow, recorded earlier in the chain. The *Gemara* provides support for Rabbi Yoḥanan's explanation that Yose ben Yoezer's ruling about *safek tumah birshut harabim* was a leniency: *Ha amar Rabbi Yochanan halacha v'ein morin kein* — Rabbi Yochanan had said that this is the *halacha* but we do not teach it publicly, out of concern that people will extend the leniency beyond its legitimate parameters and treat cases that are not truly doubtful as though they were. *V'ata ihu v'ori lah* — and Yose ben Yoezer came and publicly proclaimed this principle, because it had been transmitted as an ancient tradition, perhaps *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, and it seemed inappropriate to continue keeping it concealed from the public.

### Rivers Tevilah

This ruling is notable because it is not strictly about *safek tumah birshut harabim*, but about whether a *safek mikveh* in a public place can be relied upon to remove an existing status of *tumah* — a more complex question. One who already has a *chezkat tumah*, an established status of *tumah*, may not be able to rely on a doubtful *mikveh* to remove it, since the principle of *safek tumah birshut harabim* addresses newly arising doubt, not the removal of established *tumah*. This question is extensively treated at the beginning of *Masechet Niddah*, in the *sugya* about a *mikveh* whose water may have dropped below 40 *se'ah* at an indeterminate point.

Regarding *tevilah* in natural bodies of water more generally: the *Rishonim* address this at length, including in a famous extended *Ran* in *Nedarim* near daf 40, which runs to approximately three pages. The *sugya* identifies several distinct problems with immersion in a river. Rainwater is only *metaher* when stationary, not flowing; a river therefore presents a potential problem. Additionally, immersing in a public place creates fear of being observed, which causes the person to rush and may invalidate the immersion. There is also the problem of mud at the riverbed. The *Rishonim* address these issues in various ways; generally, immersion in a lake or the ocean is preferable, as it addresses some of these difficulties more satisfactorily than a flowing river does. The question of tidal estuaries — bodies of water that are technically rivers but flow in both directions with the tides — is an interesting extension of this discussion. The Hudson River, for instance, is a fjord and not a true river in the hydrological sense: it has tides and flows bidirectionally all the way up to Troy, New York. Whether this changes the halachic analysis relative to a true river is a question that would need to be addressed on its own terms.

## Shlukos Intro

### Shlukos Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁלָקוֹת. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָמַר קְרָא ״אֹכֶל בַּכֶּסֶף תַּשְׁבִּרֵנִי וְאָכַלְתִּי וּמַיִם בַּכֶּסֶף תִּתֶּן לִי וְשָׁתִיתִי״, כַּמָּיִם — מָה מַיִם שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּנּוּ, אַף אוֹכֶל שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And boiled** vegetables prepared by gentiles are prohibited. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The verse states** that when Moses asked Sihon, King of the Amorites, for passage through his land, he said: **"You shall sell me food for money, that I may eat; and give me water for money, that I may drink"** (Deuteronomy 2:28). By juxtaposing food and water, the verse teaches that food is **like water: Just as** Moses wished to purchase **water that was unchanged, so too,** he wished to purchase **food that was unchanged,** i.e., uncooked. Evidently, this is because foods cooked by gentiles are prohibited.

*Ve'hashlukos* — the *Mishna* had listed *shlukos* among the prohibited foods of non-Jews. What is *shlukos*? The *Gemara* in *Berachot* discusses the *bracha* to be recited on *shlukos* — the *halacha* is *ha'adama*, though there is debate in that *sugya* about whether it should be *shehakol* or *ha'adama*, and *Yerushalmi* takes a different position. In any case, *shlukos* is a vegetable soup made by boiling vegetables in water and then removing the vegetables, leaving the liquid that has absorbed their flavor.

### Bishul Akum

This halacha is what we know today as *bishul akum* — food cooked by a non-Jew. The most intuitive reason to prohibit such food would be *kashrut* concerns: perhaps the non-Jewish cook added a non-kosher ingredient without the Jewish consumer's knowledge. That concern is real and addresses a *de'oraita* dimension. But there is also a separate rabbinic prohibition against food cooked by non-Jews even when all the ingredients are demonstrably kosher. The tension between these two concerns — the *de'oraita* worry about non-kosher ingredients and the *derabbanan* rule against *bishul akum* as an independent category — is what the *Gemara* will now turn to address. The *Mishna*'s specific use of *shlukos* as its example is itself worthy of attention, and the precise rationale for the rabbinic prohibition — likely connected, at least in part, to concerns about social intimacy leading to intermarriage — will be developed in the continuation.
