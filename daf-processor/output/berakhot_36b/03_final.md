# Berakhot 36b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzlaf & Fruit Definition

### Tzlaf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: צָלָף שֶׁל עׇרְלָה בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ זוֹרֵק אֶת הָאֲבִיּוֹנוֹת וְאוֹכֵל אֶת הַקַּפְרֵיסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: A caper-bush during the first three years of its growth [*orla*] outside of Eretz** Yisrael, when its fruits are prohibited by rabbinic and not Torah law, **one throws out the berries,** the primary fruit, **but eats the buds.**

The sugya opens with a ruling of *Rav Yehuda amar Rav* concerning the *tzlaf*, the caper bush, which produces several distinct products. As established earlier in the discussion, the products for which the bush is primarily cultivated, as well as those which qualify objectively as its fruit, receive the *bracha* of *borei pri ha'etz*. Products that are either secondary in purpose or do not meet the definition of fruit receive *borei pri ha'adama*. This distinction immediately raises a deeper conceptual question: what exactly defines a fruit? Is it purely functional — determined by what the tree is primarily cultivated for — or is there an objective botanical or halachic reality to the category of fruit that transcends the particular use to which it is put?

To explore this, the *Gemara* turns to other areas of *halacha* in which the definition of fruit is equally consequential. What follows is a sustained comparative analysis across multiple halachic domains — *brachos*, *orlah*, *ma'aser*, *kilayim*, and eventually *tumah* and *shemita* — built on the striking assumption that a single unified definition of fruit operates consistently across all of them.

### Orlah & Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דַּאֲבִיּוֹנוֹת פֵּירֵי וְקַפְרֵיסִין לָאו פֵּירֵי? וּרְמִינְהוּ: עַל מִינֵי נִצְפָּה, עַל הֶעָלִים וְעַל הַתְּמָרוֹת אוֹמֵר "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה", וְעַל הָאֲבִיּוֹנוֹת וְעַל הַקַּפְרֵיסִין אוֹמֵר "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָעֵץ"!
>
> **Translation:** Is that to say that the berries are fruit of the caper, **and the bud is not fruit? The Gemara raises a contradiction from what we learned in the mishna cited above: With regard to the parts of the caper-bush [*nitzpa*], over the leaves and young fronds, one recites: Who creates fruit of the ground, and over the berries, and buds he recites: Who creates fruit of the tree.** Obviously, the buds are also considered the fruit of the caper-bush.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: צָלָף מִתְעַשֵּׂר תְּמָרוֹת וַאֲבִיּוֹנוֹת וְקַפְרֵיסִין. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין מִתְעַשֵּׂר אֶלָּא אֲבִיּוֹנוֹת בִּלְבַד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא פֶּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rav's **statement is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer says: A caper-bush is tithed from** its component parts, **its young fronds, berries and buds,** as all these are considered its fruit. **And Rabbi Akiva says: Only the berries alone are tithed, because it** alone **is** considered **fruit.**

The *Gemara* immediately presses: does this mean the *avyonos* are a fruit and the *kafrisin* are not? If so, how do we reconcile this with the *braisa* cited earlier in the context of *brachos*, which ruled that one says *borei pri ha'etz* on both the *avyonos* and the *kafrisin*? This is a significant question, and it illustrates one of the *Gemara*'s foundational assumptions: that halachic categories are consistent across domains. In secular legal systems one would never presume that the definition of "fruit" in one area of law necessarily matches the definition in an entirely different area of law. In *halacha*, however, the *Gemara* proceeds from the working assumption that these categories are universal — a methodological premise worth pausing to appreciate.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by noting that the ruling of *Rav Yehuda amar Rav* follows the position of *Rabbi Akiva*, as stated in a *Mishna* in *ma'asros*. There, *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that the caper bush is subject to *ma'aser* on its *timros*, *avyonos*, and *kafrisin* — all three products are considered fruit for tithing purposes. *Rabbi Akiva*, by contrast, rules *ein misa'er ela avyonos bilvad* — only the *avyonos* are subject to *ma'aser*, because only they qualify as *pira*, as fruit.

### Ma'aser Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא: "הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא"! אִי אָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ בָּאָרֶץ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: כׇּל הַמֵּיקֵל בָּאָרֶץ — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, אֲבָל בָּאָרֶץ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** If this is the case, why did Rav issue what seemed to be an independent ruling regarding *orla*? **He should have** simply **said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** from which we could have drawn a practical halakhic conclusion regarding *orla* as well. The Gemara responds: **Had** Rav **said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, I would have said** that the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion **even in Eretz** Yisrael. Therefore, **he teaches us** by stating the entire *halakha*, that there is a principle: **Anyone who is lenient** in a dispute with regard to the *halakhot* of *orla* **in Eretz** Yisrael, **the *halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **but not in Eretz** Yisrael.

The *Gemara* then asks: why not simply state that we rule like *Rabbi Akiva* altogether, in every domain? The answer establishes an important principle. The leniency toward *Rabbi Akiva*'s position is not a blanket adoption of his view across all categories. Rather, it is a product of a specific principle governing *orlah*: *kol ha-meikel b'aretz, halacha k'moso b'chutz la'aretz* — if one takes a lenient position regarding *orlah* in *Eretz Yisrael*, we rule in accordance with that lenient position outside the Land. Since *orlah* outside Israel has the status of *halacha l'Moshe m'Sinai* with an associated tradition of leniency, we are permitted to rely on minority opinions in that domain.

But this does not mean that the underlying conceptual category of fruit is different in different areas of law. The *Gemara* is clear that in *brachos*, *orlah*, and *ma'aser*, the definition of fruit is, in principle, the same. The difference is not definitional but legal: the permissibility of relying on a lenient opinion varies by domain.

### Orlah Leniencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא: "הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ", דְּכׇל הַמֵּיקֵל בָּאָרֶץ — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. אִי אָמַר הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי גַּבֵּי מַעְשַׂר אִילָן, דְּבָאָרֶץ גּוּפָא מִדְּרַבָּנַן, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי עׇרְלָה, דְּבָאָרֶץ מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֵימָא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ נָמֵי נִגְזוֹר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: If so, then **let** Rav **say: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva outside of Eretz** Yisrael **as anyone who is lenient** in a dispute with regard to the *halakhot* of *orla* **in Eretz** Yisrael, **the *halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion **outside of Eretz** Yisrael. The Gemara answers: **Had he said that, I would have said: That only applies with regard to the tithing of trees,** which even **in Eretz** Yisrael **itself is an obligation by rabbinic law; but with regard to *orla*,** which **is** prohibited **in Eretz** Yisrael **by Torah law, say that we should issue a decree** prohibiting *orla* **even outside of Eretz** Yisrael. Therefore, **he teaches us** that even with regard to *orla*, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva.

To explain why we could not simply declare that we rule like *Rabbi Akiva* for all *orlah* purposes outside Israel, the *Gemara* offers a further consideration. One might have assumed that *Rabbi Akiva*'s leniency could only be applied to *ma'aser* of fruit trees, since *ma'aser* of fruit is itself rabbinic even within the Land. Outside the Land it would be doubly rabbinic — a *drabbanan* of a *drabbanan* — and therefore a natural candidate for leniency. But one might have thought that *orlah*, which is *de-Oraisa* in the Land, cannot be treated so leniently even outside Israel. The ruling teaches that nevertheless, for *orlah* outside Israel, we do adopt the lenient position and restrict the prohibition to the fruit itself.

The depth of leniency permitted by *orlah* in *chutz la'aretz* is illustrated by the *Mishna*: *safek orlah* — doubtful *orlah* — is forbidden in *Eretz Yisrael* and permitted in *Suria* (territories conquered by David and the Hasmoneans). Outside of that entirely, the leniency goes further still: one may purchase fruit directly from a vendor's orchard even knowing that some of the trees are *orlah*, as long as one does not witness the vendor picking directly from those trees. The license to create the doubt, not merely to be lenient when the doubt arises on its own, reflects how far the leniency extends.

This raises a further question: given such sweeping leniency for *orlah* in *chutz la'aretz*, why not rely on *Beit Shammai*, who would classify the caper bush as a vegetable and thereby exempt even the *avyonos* from *orlah* restrictions? To this the *Gemara* responds: *Rabbi Akiva bimkom Rabbi Eliezer avdinan kevateih* — we are prepared to rely on *Rabbi Akiva* even though *Rabbi Eliezer* disagrees, because *Rabbi Akiva* represents a recognized minority position. But *Beit Shammai bimkom Beit Hillel eino mishnah* — once *Beit Shammai* has been definitively overruled by *Beit Hillel*, their ruling is no longer even a minority opinion upon which we may rely. This point carries broader significance for the general principle of *da'at yachid bish'at ha-dechak* — reliance on a minority opinion in times of pressing need. Not every position ever articulated constitutes a legitimate minority opinion; a view that has been wholly rejected loses that status.

### Kilayim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: צָלָף, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כִּלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם. וּבֵית הַלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵין כִּלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם. אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ מוֹדִים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּעׇרְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** As we learned in a mishna with regard to the laws of forbidden mixtures of diverse kinds that **Beit Shammai say: A caper-bush is** considered **a diverse kind in the vineyard,** as it is included in the prohibition against planting vegetables in a vineyard. **Beit Hillel say:** A caper-bush is **not** considered **a diverse kind in a vineyard.** Nevertheless, **these and those,** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, **agree** that the caper-bush is **obligated in** the prohibition of ***orla*.**

A new halachic category enters the discussion: *kilayim*. While one may plant trees alongside a vineyard, one may not plant grain or vegetables there. The question is how to classify the caper bush. *Beit Shammai* rules that it is *kilayim b'kerem* — it may not be planted in a vineyard, implying that it is not a tree. *Beit Hillel* rules *ein kilayim b'kerem* — it is not subject to that prohibition, treating it as a tree. Yet remarkably, both schools agree — *elu v'elu modim she-chayavin b'orlah* — that the caper bush is subject to *orlah*, which is categorically a law applying to trees. Here, then, is an explicit instance within the *Gemara* itself where the category of "tree" diverges between two areas of *halacha*. What is a tree for *orlah* may not be a tree for *kilayim*.

### Beit Shammai Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ צָלָף בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים כִּלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם, אַלְמָא מִין יָרָק הוּא, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ מוֹדִים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּעׇרְלָה, אַלְמָא מִין אִילָן הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** Before dealing with the problem posed by Ravina to Mar bar Rav Ashi, the Gemara notes an internal contradiction in this mishna. **This** mishna **itself is problematic: You said that Beit Shammai say: A caper-bush is** considered **a diverse kind in a vineyard; apparently,** they hold that **it is a type of vegetable** bush, **and then you taught: These and those,** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, **agree** that the caper-bush is **obligated in** the prohibition of ***orla*; apparently, it is a type of tree.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לְהוּ, וְעָבְדִי הָכָא לְחוּמְרָא וְהָכָא לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult; Beit Shammai are uncertain** whether the caper-bush is a vegetable bush or a tree, **and here,** regarding diverse kinds, **they act stringently and here,** regarding *orla*, **they act stringently.**

The *Gemara* recognizes that *Beit Shammai*'s position appears internally contradictory: they call it *kilayim* in a vineyard — implying it is a vegetable — yet agree it is subject to *orlah* — implying it is a tree. The *Gemara* resolves this: *Beit Shammai sepuki mesapka lehu* — they are simply uncertain how to classify the caper bush. Faced with this uncertainty, they apply stringency in both directions: they treat it as a tree for *orlah* purposes, where being stringent means applying the prohibition, and they treat it as a vegetable for *kilayim* purposes, where being stringent means imposing the restriction. The position is coherent as an expression of consistent stringency under conditions of doubt, even if the legal treatment differs across categories. What is striking is that the *Gemara* does not invoke this as a license to treat definitions differently across categories in general. Every prior opportunity to distinguish the categories was declined; the assumption of cross-category consistency holds firm. *Beit Shammai*'s divergent treatment is explained not as a principled distinction between domains but as an application of *chumra* in a case of genuine uncertainty.

### Safek Orlah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי הָוֵה לֵיהּ סָפֵק עׇרְלָה, וּתְנַן: סָפֵק עׇרְלָה — בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל אָסוּר, וּבְסוּרְיָא מוּתָּר. וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ יוֹרֵד וְלוֹקֵחַ — וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִרְאֶנּוּ לוֹקֵט.
>
> **Translation:** In any case, according to Beit Shammai** the caper-bush has the status of **uncertain *orla*,** and **we learned** the consensus *halakha* in a mishna: **Uncertain *orla* in Eretz Yisrael is forbidden to eat, and in Syria it is permitted,** and we are not concerned about its uncertain status. **Outside of Eretz** Yisrael, the gentile owner of a field **may go down** into his field **and take** from the *orla* fruit, and **as long as** the Jew **does not see him gather** it, he may purchase the fruit from the gentile.

The rules of *safek orlah* — doubtful *orlah* — further illuminate the graduated structure of leniency across territories. In *Eretz Yisrael*, doubtful *orlah* is forbidden. In *Suria*, doubt is resolved leniently and the fruit is permitted. In *chutz la'aretz*, the leniency extends even to cases where one has proactively placed oneself into a position of doubt — one may purchase from a vendor's orchard knowing that some trees are *orlah*, provided one does not witness the picking from those specific trees. This graduated framework shapes the entire discussion of which opinions we are prepared to rely upon by domain and by territory.

## Kafrisin Status

### Shomer Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּנַעֲשָׂה שׁוֹמֵר לַפְּרִי, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: "וַעֲרַלְתֶּם עׇרְלָתוֹ אֶת פִּרְיוֹ", אֶת הַטָּפֵל לְפִרְיוֹ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — שׁוֹמֵר לַפְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara approaches this matter from a different perspective: **Let us derive** the *halakha* that buds are included in the prohibition of *orla* from the fact that the bud **serves as protection for the fruit, and the Torah says:** "When you enter the land and plant any tree for food **you shall regard its fruit [*et piryo*] as *orla*"** (Leviticus 19:23), and ***et piryo*** is interpreted to mean **that which is secondary to the fruit. What is that? That** section of the plant **which is protection for the fruit.** The buds should be prohibited as *orla*, since they protect the fruit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֵיכָא אָמְרִינַן דְּנַעֲשָׂה שׁוֹמֵר לַפְּרִי — הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ בֵּין בְּתָלוּשׁ בֵּין בִּמְחוּבָּר, הָכָא בִּמְחוּבָּר — אִיתֵיהּ, בְּתָלוּשׁ — לֵיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Where do we say** that a section of the plant **becomes protection for the fruit?** That is specifically **when it exists both when** the fruit **is detached** from the tree **and when it is** still **connected** to the tree. However, **here, it exists when** the fruit **is connected** to the tree, **but when it is detached it does not,** and since the protection falls off of the fruit when it is picked, it is no longer considered protection.

Even granting that the *kafrisin* are not themselves defined as fruit — whether one accepts *Rashi*'s identification of them as a kind of shell or *Steinsaltz*'s identification as floral buds — the *Gemara* presses a further question. Why should the *kafrisin* not be forbidden under *orlah* for a different reason entirely: not as fruit, but as a *shomer* — a protective covering of the fruit? The *Torah* states *va-araltem et piryo*, and the word *et* is taken to extend the prohibition: *et ha-tafel le-piryo* — not merely the fruit itself but what accompanies it. The *shomer*, the protective covering of the fruit, is included in the *orlah* prohibition even if it does not itself qualify as fruit.

*Rava* formulates the initial criterion: *heicha d'ita bein bi-tlush bein bi-mechubar* — something qualifies as a *shomer* only when it remains attached to the fruit even after the fruit has been detached from the tree. A walnut shell, for example, continues to enclose the fruit after plucking. Here, however, the *kafrisin* — whether shell or bud — is no longer present once the fruit is detached. According to *Rashi*, the shell falls away after plucking; according to *Steinsaltz*, it does not come off with the fruit at all. In either reading, it fails the criterion.

### Shomer Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: הָנֵי מְתַחֲלֵי דְעׇרְלָה אֲסִירִי, הוֹאִיל וְנַעֲשׂוּ שׁוֹמֵר לְפֵירֵי. וְשׁוֹמֵר לְפֵירֵי אִימַּת הָוֵי — בְּכוּפְרָא, וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ "שׁוֹמֵר לְפֵירֵי".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation as well: **Is that so? Didn't Rav Naḥman say** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: Those *orla* date coverings are prohibited, because they became protection for the fruit. And when do** these coverings serve as **protection for the fruit? When the fruit is still young; and he,** nevertheless, **calls them protection for the fruit.** This indicates that in order to be considered protection for the fruit it need not remain until the fruit is fully ripened.

*Abaye* challenges *Rava*'s formulation with a contradiction from the laws of *tumah*. The *pitma* — the hard crown-like protrusion at the base of a pomegranate — is considered a unit with the pomegranate for purposes of combining toward the minimum measure that makes something susceptible to *tumah*. Yet the *netz* — the fine fibrous material inside that crown — does not combine for that measure, indicating it is not considered food. And yet a *braisa* on *orlah* teaches that the *netz* of a pomegranate is *chayav be-orlah*, included in the prohibition as a *shomer* of the fruit. If the *netz* falls away and deteriorates after plucking — making it unavailable in the detached state — and it is still considered a *shomer*, then *Rava*'s criterion that the *shomer* must persist after plucking cannot be correct.

### Pomegranate & Dates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הֵיכָא אָמְרִינַן דְּנַעֲשָׂה לְהוּ שׁוֹמֵר לְפֵירֵי — הֵיכָא דְּבִשְׁעַת גְּמַר פֵּירָא, הַאי קַפְרֵס לֵיתֵיהּ בִּשְׁעַת גְּמַר פֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** another explanation: **Where do we say** that a section of the plant **becomes protection for the fruit? Where it exists when the fruit is ripe. This bud does not exist when the fruit is ripe,** because it falls off beforehand.

*Rava* revises his position: *ela amar Rava, heicha d'iteih bish'at gmar peira* — a *shomer* is defined as something present when the fruit reaches its state of completion, when it fully ripens. The *kafrisin* is not present at that stage — it has already fallen away before the fruit matures — and therefore it is not a *shomer*.

A second challenge arises from *Rav Nachman* citing *Rabbah bar Avuha*: certain coverings associated with dates under *orlah* — referred to as *mitchali* — are forbidden, and they are described as *hovei vena'asu shomer la-pri*, protectors of the fruit. Yet *Rav Nachman* specifies that these *mitchali* protect the date *be-kuphra* — when the date is still fully unripe. If the *mitchali* is present only in the unripe stage and not when the date has finished ripening, then *Rava*'s revised criterion — presence at the moment of full ripening — would equally exclude them. Yet they are still deemed *shomrim*. The *Gemara* therefore cannot sustain *Rava*'s revised rule as stated.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דִּתְנַן רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: סְמָדַר אָסוּר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא פֶּרִי. וּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rav Naḥman held** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Yosei says: The grape-bud,** i.e., a cluster of grapes in its earliest stage, immediately after the flowers dropped from the vine, **is prohibited** due to *orla*, **because it is** already considered **a fruit.** According to this opinion, even the date coverings that exist in the earliest stage of the ripening process are nevertheless considered protection for the fruit and prohibited due to *orla*. **The Rabbis disagree with him,** explaining that fruit at that stage is not considered fruit; and, therefore, the date coverings and caper-bush buds are not considered protection for the fruit and are not prohibited due to *orla*.

The resolution comes through a fundamental dispute about when a fruit is considered a fruit at all. The *Gemara* invokes the opinion of *Rabbi Yosi*: *samadar assur mipnei shehu peri* — unripe grapes at the *samadar* stage are already considered fruit and therefore already subject to the prohibition. The *Rabbanan* disagree with *Rabbi Yosi* and hold that the grape is not yet a fruit at the *samadar* stage; it attains the status of fruit only at the later *boser* stage.

*Rav Nachman* holds like *Rabbi Yosi*. According to that view, even the earliest stages of fruit development constitute *peri*, and therefore something protecting the incipient fruit at that early stage is already a *shomer la-pri* — it is protecting something that is already halachically a fruit. The *mitchali* of dates are forbidden because, under *Rabbi Yosi*'s expansive definition, even the earliest unripe date is already a fruit, and its covering is therefore already a *shomer*. According to the *Rabbanan*'s more restrictive view, fruit status is achieved only later, and thus a covering present only in the very earliest stages would not yet qualify as a *shomer la-pri*.

### Shemita Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב שִׁימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: וּבִשְׁאָר אִילָנֵי מִי פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ? וְהָתְנַן: מֵאֵימָתַי אֵין קוֹצְצִין אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת בַּשְּׁבִיעִית — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הָאִילָנוֹת מִשֶּׁיּוֹצִיאוּ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: הֶחָרוּבִין מִשֶּׁיְּשַׁרְשְׁרוּ, וְהַגְּפָנִים מִשֶּׁיְּגָרְעוּ, וְהַזֵּיתִים מִשֶּׁיָּנֵיצוּ, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הָאִילָנוֹת מִשֶּׁיּוֹצִיאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi of Neharde'a strongly objects to this** *halakha*: **Do the Rabbis disagree with** the opinion of Rabbi Yosei **with regard to** the fruits **of all other trees** besides grapes, that even in the very first stage of ripening, they are considered fruit? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to fruit, which grows during the Sabbatical year, the Torah says: "And the Shabbat of the land shall be to you for eating" (Leviticus 25:6). The Sages inferred, for eating, and not for loss. Because one is prohibited from discarding fruit grown during the Sabbatical year, the question is raised: **From when may one no** longer **cut trees during the Sabbatical year** as he thereby damages the fruit? **Beit Shammai say: All trees, from when** the blossoms fall off and **fruit** begins to **emerge** in its earliest stage. **And Beit Hillel say:** There is a distinction between different types of trees. **Carob trees** may not be cut **from when they** form **chains** of carobs, **vines** may not be cut ***misheyegaru*,** explained below, **olives from when they blossom, and all other trees from when** fruit **emerges.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: הוּא בּוֹסֶר, הוּא גֵּרוּעַ, הוּא פּוֹל הַלָּבָן. פּוֹל הַלָּבָן סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שִׁיעוּרוֹ כְּפוֹל הַלָּבָן. מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר בּוֹסֶר אִין, סְמָדַר לָא — רַבָּנַן, וְקָתָנֵי שְׁאָר כׇּל הָאִילָנוֹת — מִשֶּׁיּוֹצִיאוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Asi said:** *Sheyegaru* in our mishna is to be understood: **It is an unripe grape, it is a grape kernel, it is a white bean.** Before this is even explained, the Gemara expresses its astonishment: **Does it enter your mind** that the grape is, at any stage, **a white bean? Rather, say: Its size,** that of an unripe grape, is equivalent to the size **of a white bean.** In any case, **whom did you hear that said: An unripe grape, yes,** is considered fruit, while **a grape-bud, no,** is not considered fruit? Wasn't it **the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Yosei, **and it is taught** that, according to these Sages, one is forbidden to cut **all other trees from when** fruit **emerges.** This indicates that even they agree that from the very beginning of the ripening process, the fruit is forbidden due to *orla*.

The question of when something first attains the status of fruit extends naturally into the domain of *shemita*. The *Mishna* in *shevi'it* addresses when cutting down a tree constitutes harvesting fruit rather than felling timber — a question that turns on whether the tree already bears what counts as fruit. *Beit Shammai* rules that for all trees, the threshold is *misheyotziu* — once the fruit has emerged in any form. *Beit Hillel* distinguishes by species: carobs become fruit when they form a chain-like cluster (*misheyisharsheru*); grapevines when the berries reach the size of a white bean (*misheyigreu*, which *Rav Assi* clarifies means *ke-shiur pul halavan*); olives when they blossom (*misheyanetzu*); and all other trees also from *misheyotziu*, when the fruit first emerges.

The *Gemara* draws the inference: *meimatai shema'ta lehu* — from whom did we learn that grape fruit status is withheld until the *boser* stage and not granted at the earlier *samadar* stage? From the *Rabbanan*, who dispute *Rabbi Yosi*. And yet by *shemita*, even the *Rabbanan* appear to hold that fruit status is conferred once the fruit emerges from the tree — a stage that seems earlier than the *boser* threshold they apply by *orlah*. This evidence suggests that even those who dispute *Rabbi Yosi* may concede that a fruit is legally a fruit at a quite early stage of development, which would in turn support considering the *mitchali* a genuine *shomer* even in an early stage.

Throughout this discussion, a methodological observation bears noting: the *Gemara* is entirely comfortable distinguishing between different species of trees when it comes to the empirical facts of botanical development — the vine reaches a meaningful threshold at a different moment than the carob or the olive, and the *Rabbanan* track those differences carefully. Yet the abstract legal concept — at what stage of development does something become a *peri*? — is assumed to be uniform across categories. Evidence drawn from *shemita* is introduced to illuminate *orlah*, evidence from *tumah* is brought to bear on *brachos*, and throughout, the *Gemara* never questions whether these domains might apply different definitions. The universality of the conceptual category is the constant; only the facts differ from species to species.

### Kafrisin Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הֵיכָא אָמְרִינַן דְּהָוֵי שׁוֹמֵר לְפֵרֵי — הֵיכָא דְּכִי שָׁקְלַתְּ לֵיהּ לְשׁוֹמֵר מָיֵית פֵּירָא. הָכָא כִּי שָׁקְלַתְּ לֵיהּ — לָא מָיֵית פֵּירָא. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וְשַׁקְלוּהּ לְנֵץ דְּרִמּוֹנָא, וִיבַשׁ רִמּוֹנָא, וְשַׁקְלוּהּ לְפִרְחָא דְבִיטִיתָא — וְאִיקַּיַּים בִּיטִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** a different explanation: **Where do we say** that a section of the plant **becomes protection for the fruit? Where if you remove the protection, the fruit dies. Here,** in the case of the caper-bush, **when you remove** the bud, **the fruit does not die.** In fact, **there was an incident and they removed the flower of a pomegranate, and the pomegranate withered. And they removed the flower of the fruit of a caper-bush and the fruit of the caper-bush survived.** Therefore, buds are not considered protection for the fruit.

*Rava* ultimately reformulates the criterion for *shomer* in its final form: *heicha de-ki shaklas lei la-shomer mayis pira* — something is a *shomer* of the fruit when removing it would cause the fruit to die. *Heicha de-ki shaklas lei lo mayis pira* — if removing it does not harm the fruit, it is not a *shomer*. And remarkably, the *Gemara* reports that this was tested empirically. *Hava uvda ve-shakluha le-netz de-rimona ve-yavesh rimona* — they removed the *netz* of a pomegranate, and the pomegranate died. *Ve-shakluha le-pircha de-vitisa ve-chaya vitisa* — they removed the corresponding covering of the caper bush, and it survived. The empirical result confirmed the theoretical distinction: the pomegranate crown is a genuine *shomer*, the *kafrisin* of the caper bush is not.

### Kafrisin Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (וְהִלְכְתָא כְּמָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי דְּזָרֵיק אֶת הָאֲבִיּוֹנוֹת וְאָכֵיל אֶת הַקַּפְרֵיסִין. וּמִדִּלְגַבֵּי עׇרְלָה לָאו פֵּירָא נִינְהוּ, לְגַבֵּי בְּרָכוֹת נָמֵי לָאו פֵּירָא נִינְהוּ, וְלָא מְבָרְכִינַן עֲלַהּ "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָעֵץ", אֶלָּא "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה".)
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Mar bar Rav Ashi, who discarded the berries and ate the buds. And since they are not** considered **fruit with regard to *orla*, they are also not** considered **fruit with regard to blessings, and one does not recite over them: Who creates fruit of the tree, but rather: Who creates fruit of the ground.**

The parenthetical *vehilchasa* that concludes the passage — ruling like *Mar bar Rav Ashi* who discarded the *avyonos* and ate the *kafrisin* — and then drawing the inference *umidal-gabei orlah la peira ninhu, legabei brachos nami la peira ninhu*, such that one says *borei pri ha'adama* rather than *borei pri ha'etz* on the *kafrisin*, is noted by *Tosafot* as likely a *Gaonic* insertion. *Rashi* explicitly states in related passages *lo garsinan*, that he does not accept this text as original, attributing it to the *Hilchos Gedolos* of *Rav Yehudai Gaon* — a major early *Gaonic* work whose rulings were appended to *Gemara* quotations in Aramaic and consequently absorbed into some manuscript traditions of the *Gemara* itself.

*Tosafot* goes further and rejects the ruling substantively. Since the leniency regarding *kafrisin* by *orlah* in *chutz la'aretz* is precisely that — a leniency particular to that context — it cannot be used to strip the *kafrisin* of their *bracha* status in *brachos*. The *kafrisin* genuinely qualify as fruit, and one accordingly recites *borei pri ha'etz* on them, regardless of how we rule under *orlah* outside Israel.

## Peppers & Spices

### Pilpeli Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּלְפְּלֵי, רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: "שֶׁהַכֹּל", רָבָא אָמַר: לָא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The question arose with regard to the blessing over **peppers. Rav Sheshet said:** One who eats peppers must recite: **By Whose word all things came to be. Rava said: One** need **not recite** a blessing **at all.**

The *Gemara* turns to *pilpeli* — peppercorns, specifically the dried spice we know as black pepper, entirely unrelated to the sweet peppers of the New World. The question is what *bracha*, if any, one recites when eating them.

### Rav Sheshet/Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדָא רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כַּס פִּלְפְּלֵי בְּיוֹמָא דְכִפּוּרֵי — פָּטוּר, כַּס זַנְגְּבִילָא בְּיוֹמָא דְכִפּוּרֵי — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **This is consistent with Rava's opinion** that eating peppers is not considered eating, **as Rava said: One who chews on peppers on Yom Kippur is exempt, one who chews on ginger on Yom Kippur is exempt.** Eating sharp spices is an uncommon practice, and is therefore not considered to be eating, which is prohibited by Torah law on Yom Kippur.

*Rav Sheshet* rules *shehakol* — one recites *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*. *Rava* rules *lo klum* — one recites no *bracha* at all. *Rava le-ta'ameih*, *Rava* follows his own established principle: *kas pilpeli be-Yom Hakippurim patur* — one who chews pepper on *Yom Kippur* is exempt from punishment, because it is not considered eating food. Similarly, *kas zangvila be-Yom Hakippurim patur* — chewing ginger on *Yom Kippur* incurs no penalty. They are spices, not food, and therefore one who consumes them in this form has not eaten in any legally meaningful sense. *Rava* does add: *aval assur* — it is nonetheless prohibited, as there is still a concern even without the full *de-Oraisa* violation.

### Yom Kippur Test

The invocation of *Yom Kippur* introduces yet another halachic domain into the cross-categorical analysis. The question of what constitutes eating, which governs the *Yom Kippur* fast, is being used here to illuminate what constitutes food for purposes of *brachos*. Dried spices consumed directly, argues *Rava*, are not food in any meaningful sense in either domain.

### Orlah & Peppers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר "וַעֲרַלְתֶּם עׇרְלָתוֹ אֶת פִּרְיוֹ" אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁעֵץ מַאֲכָל הוּא? אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "עֵץ מַאֲכָל" — לְהָבִיא עֵץ שֶׁטַּעַם עֵצוֹ וּפִרְיוֹ שָׁוֶה, וְאֵיזֶהוּ? — זֶה הַפִּלְפְּלִין. לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁהַפִּלְפְּלִין חַיָּיבִין בְּעׇרְלָה, וּלְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁאֵין אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל חֲסֵרָה כְּלוּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אֶרֶץ אֲשֶׁר לֹא בְמִסְכֵּנֻת תֹּאכַל בָּהּ לֶחֶם לֹא תֶחְסַר כֹּל בָּהּ".
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raised an objection** to this based on what was taught in a *baraita*, that regarding the verse: "When you enter the land and plant any tree for food you shall regard its fruit as *orla*" (Leviticus 19:23), **Rabbi Meir would say: By inference from that which is stated: "You shall regard its fruit as *orla*," don't I know that it is** referring to **a tree** that produces **food? Rather,** for **what** purpose **does the verse state: "Any tree for food"? To include a tree whose wood and fruit have the same taste. And which** tree **is this? This is the pepper** tree. **And** this comes **to teach you that the peppers,** and even the wood portions, are edible and are therefore **obligated in** the prohibition of ***orla*, and to teach that Eretz Yisrael lacks nothing, as it is stated: "A land where you shall eat bread without scarceness, you shall lack nothing"** (Deuteronomy 8:9). **Obviously,** peppers are fit for consumption.

A *braisa* is introduced as a challenge. *Rabbi Meir* derives from the *pasuk* — *va-araltem orlato et piryo*, the command to treat as *orlah* the fruit of one's tree, with the phrase *etz ma'achal*, a fruit-bearing tree — that the prohibition extends to a tree whose wood tastes like its fruit. And which tree is that? *Ve-eizehu zeh, ha-pilpilin* — that is the pepper vine, whose bark reportedly tastes similar to the peppercorn. The *braisa* therefore establishes that peppers are *chayav be-orlah* and *ein Eretz Yisrael chasera klum* — Israel lacks nothing, including the pepper vine. The *Gemara* notes that the verse *lo techsar kol bah* — "you will lack nothing in it" — attests to the completeness of the Land's produce.

If peppers are subject to *orlah*, they must be classified as fruit. And if they are fruit, one might expect at minimum a *bracha* when eating them — perhaps even *borei pri ha'etz*. The *Gemara*'s resolution is critical.

### Moist/Dried States

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּרַטִּיבְתָּא, הָא בְּיַבִּשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult,** as there is a distinction between two different cases. **This,** where Rabbi Meir spoke of peppers fit for consumption, **is** referring to a case **when it is damp** and fresh; **and this,** where chewing on pepper is not considered eating on Yom Kippur and does not require a blessing, **is** referring to a case **when it is dry.**

*Lo kashya, ha bir-tivta ve-ha bir-yaveshta* — there is no contradiction, because the two cases concern different states of the pepper: moist (*brit'vuta*) versus dried (*yaveshta*). When pepper is moist it is food, and one would make an appropriate *bracha*. When it has dried into the form of a spice, it is no longer in a state in which it functions as food that one would normally eat directly, and according to *Rava* one makes no *bracha* at all in that state. The same resolution applies to the ginger discussion: *Ravina* (or in some versions *Rava* himself) rules that ginger from India is *sharya* and one recites *borei pri ha'adama* on it, and the *Gemara* reconciles the apparent conflict by distinguishing between moist ginger, on which a *bracha* is recited, and dried ginger, on which no *bracha* is recited.

### Tosafot Spices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְמָרִימָר: כַּס זַנְגְּבִילָא בְּיוֹמָא דְכִפּוּרֵי פָּטוּר? וְהָא אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי הִמְלְתָא דְּאָתְיָא מִבֵּי הִנְדּוּאֵי — שַׁרְיָא, וּמְבָרְכִינַן עֲלַהּ "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה". לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּרַטִּיבְתָּא, הָא בְּיַבִּשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this discussion of chewing pepper on Yom Kippur, the Gemara cites what **the Sages said to Mareimar:** Why **is one who chews ginger on Yom Kippur exempt? Didn't Rava say: One is permitted** to eat **the ginger that comes from India, and over it, one recites: Who creates fruit of the ground.** With regard to blessing, it is considered edible; therefore, with regard to chewing on Yom Kippur, it should be considered edible as well. The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult. This,** where a blessing is recited, **is** referring to a case **when it is damp** and fresh; **this,** where no blessing is recited, **is** referring to a case **when it is dry.**

*Tosafot* takes up the practical implications. For moist spices that people eat directly, one recites *borei pri ha'adama* (and, he implies, not *borei pri ha'etz* even for the moist peppercorn from its vine). For dried forms of things that are normally consumed only in moist form, no *bracha* at all. The operative principle is *orchei* — normal usage. For something like cinnamon sticks (*eitz she-pinamon*, which *Tosafot* calls *kanila*), people do in fact eat them dried, and one accordingly recites *borei pri ha'adama* even in the dried state. Similarly, *Tosafot* rules that sugarcane receives *borei pri ha'etz* because it is a tree product that people eat in its natural state.

### Ginger Modern Use

The category of "what people normally eat" is inherently dynamic, and *Tosafot*'s discussion makes clear that it is not a fixed historical datum. *Tosafot* is describing the practice of his own time, and what was "normal" to eat versus use only as a flavoring could shift. This has direct practical relevance: dried ginger is today sold as a candied snack, eaten directly for pleasure, and one who consumes it in that fashion is eating it in a manner that now falls squarely within the category of *orchei le-ochlo* — the normal way to consume it. A *bracha* would therefore be appropriate in contemporary circumstances, though the specific category — *borei pri ha'adama* — would follow from *Tosafot*'s framework. The general principle is that changes in how food is actually consumed can and do affect the applicable *bracha* category.

## Mezonos Category

### Chavitz/Daysa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲבִיץ קְדֵרָה וְכֵן דַּיְיסָא, רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: "שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ". רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: "בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar dispute with regard to the blessing be recited over ***ḥavitz*,** a dish consisting of flour, oil, and honey cooked in a **pot as well as pounded grain. Rav Yehuda said** that one recites: **By Whose word all things came to be. Rav Kahana said** that one recites: **Who creates the various kinds of nourishment.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a new food type: *chavitz kdera*, a mixture of oil, honey, and flour cooked together — something resembling a sweet grain porridge or thick oatmeal. The closely related *daysa* is apparently a cooked or partially processed crushed grain dish, perhaps closer to granola in texture. The question is what *bracha* to recite.

*Rav Yehuda* rules *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro* — one makes *shehakol*, because food in this processed state is not what people would normally eat. *Rav Kahana* rules *borei minei mezonos*. The disagreement seems to turn on whether the *bracha* follows the grain component or the honey component of the mixture.

### Ikar vs. Tafel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדַיְיסָא גְּרֵידָא כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּ"בוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת". כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּדַיְיסָא כְּעֵין חֲבִיץ קְדֵרָה, רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר "שֶׁהַכֹּל", סָבַר דּוּבְשָׁא עִיקָּר. רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר "בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת", סָבַר סְמִידָא עִיקָּר. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מֵחֲמֵשֶׁת הַמִּינִין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו "בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With regard to pounded grain alone, everyone agrees that one recites: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment. When they argue, it is with regard to pounded grain** mixed with honey, **in the manner of a *ḥavitz*** cooked in a **pot. Rav Yehuda said** that one recites: By Whose word **all things** came to be, as he **held that the honey is primary,** and on honey one recites: By Whose word all things came to be. **Rav Kahana said** that one recites: **Who creates the various kinds of nourishment,** as **he held that the flour,** as is the case with all products produced from grain, **is primary,** and therefore one recites: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment. **Rav Yosef said: It is reasonable** to say **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Kahana, as Rav and Shmuel both said: Anything that has of the five species** of grain **in it, one recites over it: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment,** even if it is mixed with other ingredients.

The *Gemara* clarifies when the dispute actually applies. *Daysa greida* — plain crushed grain porridge on its own — is uncontested: *kulei alma lo pligi de-borei minei mezonos*, everyone agrees one makes *borei minei mezonos* on straightforward oatmeal. The dispute applies only to *daysa ke-ein chavitz kedeira* — a grain porridge prepared in the manner of *chavitz kedeira*, meaning it has been substantially sweetened with honey or another dominant additive.

*Rav Yehuda* rules *shehakol* because *savar dusha ikar* — he considers the honey to be the *ikar*, the primary ingredient: the grain is merely the vehicle. *Rav Kahana* rules *borei minei mezonos* because *savar smida ikar* — the flour is the *ikar*, regardless of how sweet the dish has become.

*Rav Yosef* states that *Rav Kahana*'s position is the more logical one, and grounds this in the ruling of *Rav* and *Shmuel*.

### Five Grains Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מֵחֲמֵשֶׁת הַמִּינִין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו "בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת". וְאִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מֵחֲמֵשֶׁת הַמִּינִין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakha* of the blessing recited over the five species of grain, the Gemara clarifies **the matter itself. Rav and Shmuel both said: Anything that has of the five species** of grain **in it, one recites over it: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment.** Elsewhere, **it was stated** that **Rav and Shmuel both said: Anything that is from the five species** of grain, **one recites over it: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment.** This is problematic, as these statements appear redundant.

*Rav u-Shmuel d'amri tarvaihu* — *Rav* and *Shmuel* both rule: *kol she-yesh bo mi-chameshet ha-minim mevarchin alav borei minei mezonos* — anything that contains one of the five grains — wheat, barley, spelt, oats, and rye — receives the *bracha* of *borei minei mezonos*. This ruling functions as an objective halachic definition that supersedes the subjective question of which ingredient the eater prefers or considers primary. The five grains, when present in a food item in a substantive way, always constitute the *ikar*.

This point deserves some reflection, because the *Mishna* later in the tractate appears to treat *ikar* and *tafel* in a more subjective manner — as when the *Mishna* rules that one who eats herring with bread to absorb the salt does not make a *bracha* on the bread because the herring is the *ikar*. The *Gemara*'s approach here is notably more categorical: whenever the five grains are present in a manner that goes beyond a purely adhesive function — *lid'vukei b'alma*, merely to bind ingredients together — they are objectively the *ikar*, and *borei minei mezonos* is the required *bracha*. The exceptional case recognized by the *Gemara* is where the grain is present solely *lid'vukei b'alma*, to hold the food together structurally, with no purpose of sustenance — a category that requires careful judgment in practice. The breadcrumb coating of *schnitzel*, for instance, is clearly not present to fill a person, and though it is arguably not *lid'vukei* either, the overwhelming practice is not to recite *borei minei mezonos* on such a dish.

### Five Grains Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן "כֹּל שֶׁהוּא" הֲוָה אָמֵינָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ, אֲבָל עַל יְדֵי תַּעֲרוֹבוֹת — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Both statements are **necessary,** as **had he taught us** only: **Anything that is** from the five species of grain, one recites over it: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment, **I would have said** that is **because** the grain **is in its pure,** unadulterated form, **but** if one eats it **in** the context of **a mixture, no,** one does not recite: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment.

The *Gemara* then records a second, parallel statement by *Rav* and *Shmuel*: *kol she-hu mi-chameshet ha-minim mevarchin borei minei mezonos* — not only mixtures containing the five grains but any product made from the five grains by itself receives *borei minei mezonos*. Both formulations are necessary, and the *Gemara* explains why.

If only the first statement had been issued — that a mixture containing the five grains gets *borei minei mezonos* — one might have thought that the *bracha* applies only when the grain appears in a mixture because its prominence there depends on its being the recognized staple ingredient, but that grain eaten on its own, in isolation, might not receive *borei minei mezonos*. The second statement teaches that even a pure grain preparation receives *borei minei mezonos*.

Conversely, if only the second statement had been given — that things made from the five grains receive *borei minei mezonos* — one might have extended this logic to rice and millet, arguing that when they appear in a pure preparation *be-einei*, as the only ingredient, they too deserve *borei minei mezonos*. The first statement teaches that even when they appear in a mixture, only the five grains trigger *borei minei mezonos* — rice and millet, regardless of context, never do. Both statements are therefore necessary to establish the complete picture: the five grains always confer *mezonos* status; rice, millet, and all other grains never do.

### Rice Bread Baraisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹרֶז וְדוֹחַן לָא מְבָרְכִינַן "בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת"? וְהָתַנְיָא: הֵבִיאוּ לְפָנָיו פַּת אוֹרֶז וּפַת דּוֹחַן — מְבָרֵךְ עָלָיו תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף כְּמַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the previous conclusion, the Gemara asks: **And** over **rice and millet we do not recite: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment? But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **If they brought before him rice bread or millet bread, he recites the blessing over it** both **before and after, as** he would recite the blessing over **a cooked dish** containing dough from the five species of the grain.

This ruling generates a striking question. A *braisa* is cited: *hayu lefanav pas orez u-pas dochan*, if someone had before them rice bread or millet bread, *mevarech alav techila ve-sof ke-mayseh kederah* — one recites a *bracha* at the beginning and at the end, in the manner of *mayseh kederah* (cooked flour products of the five grains). And by *mayseh kederah*, the beginning *bracha* is *borei minei mezonos* and the concluding *bracha* is *bracha achas me'ein shalosh*, the abbreviated form of *Birkat Hamazon* known as *al ha-michya*. This *braisa* seems to treat rice bread as fully equivalent to a *mezonos* product of the five grains.

### Rice Bracha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה וְלָא כְּמַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה: כְּמַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה דִּמְבָרְכִין עָלָיו תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף, וְלָא כְּמַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה דְּאִילּוּ בְּמַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה — בַּתְּחִלָּה "בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי מְזוֹנוֹת", וּלְבַסּוֹף בְּרָכָה מֵעֵין שָׁלֹשׁ, וְאִילּוּ הָכָא — בַּתְּחִלָּה מְבָרֵךְ עָלָיו "שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ", וּלְבַסּוֹף "בּוֹרֵא נְפָשׁוֹת רַבּוֹת וְחֶסְרוֹנָן".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: Indeed, rice or millet is **like a cooked dish, and is not like a cooked dish** in every sense. The Gemara elaborates: It is considered **like a cooked dish** in that **one recites a blessing over it** both at the **beginning and** the **end. And it is unlike a cooked dish** in that **over a cooked dish, at the start, one recites: Who creates the various kinds of nourishment, and at the end,** one recites **one blessing abridged from** the **three** blessings of Grace after Meals; **whereas here, over** rice, **at the start, one recites: By Whose word all things came to be, and at the end,** one recites: **Who creates the many forms of life and their needs for all that You have created.**

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by reinterpreting the comparison: *kemayseh kederah velo kemayseh kederah* — the *braisa* means the rice bread is like *mayseh kederah* in that one recites a *bracha* at both the beginning and the end, but it is unlike *mayseh kederah* in the specific content of those *brachos*. For *mayseh kederah* made from the five grains, the *brachos* are *borei minei mezonos* and *al ha-michya*. For rice bread, the *Gemara* concludes, the beginning *bracha* is *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro* and the concluding *bracha* is *borei nefashos rabos ve-chesronan*.

This pairing — *shehakol* before and *borei nefashos* after — for rice is, as the *Gemara* itself acknowledges through the structure of the passage, a somewhat unexpected combination. It reflects the view of *Rav* and *Shmuel* that rice simply never attains *mezonos* status. The position that one makes *borei minei mezonos* on rice, which is indeed the accepted practice today, represents a different strand of the discussion. It will emerge as the conclusion of the *Gemara*'s continued analysis, yielding the contemporary ruling that rice receives *borei minei mezonos* before and *borei nefashos* after — a distinctive marriage of the two categories that results from treating rice as important enough to deserve a meaningful opening *bracha*, while still declining to grant it the concluding *bracha* of *al ha-michya* that is reserved for the five grains.

### Borei Nefashos

The tractate introduces *borei nefashos rabos ve-chesronan* for what appears to be the first time in this sugya. *Tosafot* explains the full *nusach*: *borei nefashos rabos ve-chesronan al kol mah she-barata le-hachayos bahem nefesh kol chai* — You create for living beings many necessities and their lacks, for all that You have created to sustain the life of every creature. The *bracha* encompasses two categories: *chesronan* — the necessities of life, the things without which existence is impossible, such as salt and water — and the full range of foods that God has provided not merely for survival but for pleasure and quality of life, including eggs, meat, milk, and the like. *Tosafot* emphasizes that the *nusach* deliberately includes both dimensions: what sustains us and what enriches us. This makes *borei nefashos* the most broadly inclusive of the concluding *brachos*, appropriate for any food that provides sustenance or pleasure but does not merit the more specific concluding *brachos* reserved for bread, wine, the five grains, or the seven species of the Land of Israel.
