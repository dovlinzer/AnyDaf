# Nazir 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumat Met Framework

### Ohel vs. Touch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר מָר: טוּמְאָה בּוֹקַעַת וְעוֹלָה, בּוֹקַעַת וְיוֹרֶדֶת. וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי נְגִיעָה, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, תַּנְיָא: ״(וְכׇל) הַנּוֹגֵעַ בַּעֶצֶם אוֹ בֶחָלָל״, ״בַּעֶצֶם״ — זֶה עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, ״אוֹ בֶחָלָל״ — זֶה אֵבֶר הַנֶחְלָל מִן הַחַי וְאֵין בּוֹ לְהַעֲלוֹת אֲרוּכָה,
>
> **Translation:** **As the Master said: Ritual impurity** in a sealed grave **breaks through** and **ascends and breaks through and descends,** so that the grave imparts impurity like the corpse itself. **While with regard to contact** with these sources of impurity, **Rav Yehuda said** that **it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse **"and** upon **him that touched a bone, or the slain,** or the dead, or the grave" (Numbers 19:18) is expounded as follows: **"A bone"; this is a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk. "Or the slain"; this is a limb slain,** i.e., severed, **from the living, which does not contain** enough flesh **for it to heal.**

The daf opens by returning to a foundational question in the laws of *tumat met*: which objects generate *tumah* through *ohel* — that is, by being under the same roof as a *met* — and which generate *tumah* only through direct contact? The two *pesukim* that govern *tumat met* both appear, at the level of *peshat*, to be discussing touch: *vechol asher yigah al p'nei hasadeh* — "and anyone who touches on the face of the field" — refers specifically to the open field, meaning not under a roof, so it is not *tumat ohel*. The second *pasuk*, *ve'al hanogea*, similarly seems to address touch. Yet the *midreshei halakha* read *al p'nei hasadeh* as describing a situation where a person is *over* the face of the *met* — leaning above it without making direct contact — and thereby interpret this *pasuk* as speaking about *ohel*. From this reading, the first *pasuk* yields the items that convey *tumah* through *ohel*, and the second yields the items that convey *tumah* through touch.

### Pesukic Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אוֹ בְּמֵת״ — זֶה אֵבֶר הַנֶחְלָל מִן הַמֵּת, ״אוֹ בְקָבֶר״ — אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: זֶה קֶבֶר שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הַדִּיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues its interpretation of the verse. **"Or the dead"; this is a limb slain from a corpse,** which is not covered by enough flesh for it to heal were he alive. **"Or the grave"; Reish Lakish said: This is a grave** that contained a corpse from **before** the time **the command** of these *halakhot* was given to the Jewish people. These graves render people and items ritually impure by contact, but do not carry the *halakhot* of impurity imparted in a tent.

From the first *pasuk*, the *midreshei halakha* derive that *chalal*, *cherev*, *met*, *etzem adam*, and *kever* all cause *tumah* through *ohel*. These five categories are not identical in their natures, and unpacking what each term signifies is essential to understanding how the system operates.

### Cherev Doctrine

*Chalal* in this context is understood to mean something that was once an *eiver min hachai* — a limb severed from a living person — that is now dead in isolation, even without a complete corpse. *Cherev* introduces what is perhaps the most striking principle: *cherev harei hu kechalal* — the sword is like the corpse itself. The significance of this principle is considerable. A *met* is an *avi avot hatumah*, meaning that whoever touches it is rendered an *av hatumah* and is *tamei* for seven days. The rule of *cherev harei hu kechalal* extends this status to the sword — or more broadly, as we will see, to certain metal vessels — such that whoever touches it also becomes an *av*, not merely a *rishon*, and is *tamei* for seven days. The sword does not go down a level of *tumah* by virtue of having been in contact with the *met*; it retains the full status of the *met* itself.

### Mishkav Analogy

This is conceptually parallel to another domain of *halakha*: the *mishkav u'moshav* of a *zav*. A *zav* is an *av hatumah*. When a *zav* sits or lies upon something, that object — the *mishkav* or *moshav* — does not become a *rishon* as an ordinary vessel would through contact. It becomes an *av hatumah* by virtue of its identity as the seat of the *zav*. Famously, this applies even if the *zav* is separated from his seat by many cushions — the image invoked is of a princess and the pea — because what defines the *mishkav* is not physical contact but a status relationship. In a similar conceptual register, *cherev* acquires the full status of the *met* not by simple contact but by something more like identity-level association. Although we will arrive later in today's daf at a broader definition of what counts as *cherev* — potentially extending to all metal vessels that shared an *ohel* with a *met* or touched it — the basic principle is that these objects are conceived as *avi avot hatumah*, just as the *met* itself.

### Tumat Ohel Items

The five items that cause *tumat ohel* are thus: *chalal*, *cherev*, *met*, *etzem adam*, and *kever satum*. The last of these, *kever satum* — a sealed coffin — requires explanation. One might have thought that a coffin would function as an *ohel* and thereby *contain* the *tumah*, preventing it from spreading upward. But the *halakha* of *kever satum* holds that if the coffin is sealed on all sides — walls and top — then the entire coffin is treated as the *met* itself, and the *tumah* is *metamei* not only in line with the *met* but in line with the entire coffin. The coffin does not serve as an *ohel*; it is, in legal terms, a *met*. This is a critical principle underlying the *halakha* of graveyards: one cannot simply argue that the coffin contains the *tumah*.

### Bone Amount Distinctions

The category of *etzem adam* introduces the most significant difference between *tumat ohel* and *tumat maga*. For *tumat ohel*, the bone must be of sufficient quantity to represent the person — *rov minyan*, *rov binyan*, a *shidrah*, a *gulgolet*, or a corresponding quantity of bones. This is *etzem adam*. For *tumat maga*, however, even a bone the size of a barley grain — *etzem kese'orah* — causes *tumah* through touch. This distinction between *etzem adam* and *etzem* is the principal point of divergence between the two *pesukim*, and it will become the pivot of the Talmudic debate immediately to follow.

## Braita Analysis

### Reish Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי אֵבֶר מִן הַמֵּת, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאִית בֵּיהּ עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה הַיְינוּ הַנּוֹגֵעַ בַּעֶצֶם! אֶלָּא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי רַחֲמָנָא רַבְּיֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **this limb from a corpse,** which is listed above as imparting ritual impurity by contact, **what are the circumstances** of the case? **If** the limb **contains a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk, this is** included in the *halakha* of **one who touches a bone,** and it was already taught that a bone imparts impurity by contact. **Rather,** it must be **that it does not contain a bone that is a barley-grain**-**bulk, and even so the Merciful One includes it** in the *halakhot* of impurity, despite the fact that it does not contain enough flesh. This indicates that a nazirite shaves for a limb of this kind, in accordance with the opinion of Reish Lakish.

The discussion of the second *pasuk*, which the *midreshei halakha* read as governing *tumat maga*, provides the occasion for a major dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan. Reish Lakish contends that there exists a category of *eiver min hachai* and *eiver min hameis* — severed limbs — that lacks sufficient flesh to survive if reattached, yet still causes *tumah* through touch. His proof comes from a *braita*.

### Braita Text

Rav Yehuda introduces the relevant *braita* with the word *tanya*. The *braita* expounds the *pasuk* *ve'al hanogea be'etzem o vechalal*: "touching a bone" refers to *etzem kese'orah*; "or a *chalal*" refers to an *eiver* that was severed from a living person and does not have sufficient flesh to heal — *ein bo lehaalos arucha*; "or a *meis*" refers to *eiver min hameis*; "or a *kever*" — and here Reish Lakish interjects that this *kever* is *kever shelifnei hadibbur*, the grave before the divine speech, a phrase we will return to shortly.

### Eiver Definitions

Within the *braita*, the distinction between the two types of severed limbs is as follows. An *eiver min hachai* or *eiver min hameis* that has sufficient flesh — enough that the limb could survive if reattached — is the standard case that causes *tumah* through *ohel*. An *eiver min hachai* or *eiver min hameis* that does not have sufficient flesh is the more unusual case, and it is this case that Reish Lakish says the *braita* addresses in the context of *tumat maga*.

### Limb Analysis

The *gemara* then presses: what exactly is the case of *eiver min hameis* in this *braita*? If the limb contains an *etzem kese'orah*, then it already causes *tumah* as a bone — *hainu hanogea be'etzem*. So the *braita* must be speaking of a case where the bone within the limb is insufficient to qualify independently. There is, then, a limb with insufficient bone to cause *tumah* as a bone, insufficient flesh to heal, and yet it causes *tumah* through contact. This, says Reish Lakish, demonstrates that a limb without sufficient flesh retains independent *tumah*-causing status by virtue of being a limb, and the *Torah* includes it explicitly — *rachmana rabyei*. The relevance to our *masechta* is that Reish Lakish infers that a *nazir* should also be *megaleach* upon touching such a limb, even though the *mishnah* listing the causes of *nazir megaleach* mentioned only limbs with sufficient flesh. The *mishnah* may have omitted it, but the *midrash halacha* makes clear that the *tumah* exists.

### Rav Yochanan Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית בֵּיהּ. וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְמַגָּעוֹ — תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְמַשָּׂאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: In answer to this proof of Reish Lakish, **Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said to you: Actually,** you should explain **that it does contain** a bone the volume of a barley-grain-bulk, **and if it does not** need to refer to **the matter of its contact,** as the verse explicitly states: "Anyone who touches a bone," **have it refer to the matter of its carrying,** i.e., that one who carries it is also rendered ritually impure.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *le'olam de'is beih* — the limb in the *braita* does in fact contain an *etzem kese'orah*. The apparent redundancy of the *braita* mentioning this limb separately from "bone" is not because it is teaching a new category; it is teaching a different mode of *tumah*-transmission. *V'ei eino inyan lemaga, tanehu inyan lemassa* — if the text is not needed to teach about *touching*, apply it to teach about *carrying*. This is the standard *talmudic* principle of *im eino inyan l'zeh, tnehu inyan l'zeh*.

### Masa Derivation

The structure of the two *pesukim* establishes two modes of *tumah*: *ohel* and *maga*. But there is a third mode — *massa*, carrying — and it needs a Scriptural source. Rabbi Yochanan's position is that there is no independent category of *eiver* without sufficient flesh causing *tumah* through touch; there is simply a second application of *etzem kese'orah*, and the redundancy derives *tumat massa*. The *halakhic* rule that carrying is equivalent to touching — as stated in the *mishnah* — still needs a textual basis, and that is what the apparent duplication in the *braita* provides. This is the substance of the Reish Lakish–Rabbi Yochanan dispute as it stands on this *amud*.

## Kever Lifnei HaDibbur

### Tosafot Interpretations

We now return to the striking phrase that Reish Lakish appended to his reading of the *braita*: the *kever* mentioned in the *pasuk* governing *tumat maga* is *kever shelifnei hadibbur* — the grave before the divine speech. *Tosafot* identifies two possible interpretations of this phrase.

### Non-Jew Status

The first interpretation understands *lifnei hadibbur* as referring to the period before *Matan Torah*. The underlying question is: what was the status of graves and the *tumah* they generate before the Torah was given? *Tosafot* links this to the broader question of when the people of Israel shifted from the status of *bnei Noach* to the status of *Yisrael*. Was it from the moment Avraham received *brit milah*? From *ma'amad Har Sinai*? There is an entire work — attributed to the *Mishneh Lamelech*, entitled *Parshat Drachim* — that works through the *midrashim* through this lens. One famous illustration: the debate between Yosef and his brothers, in which he accused them of eating *eiver min hachai*, turned precisely on this question. Yosef believed they already had the status of *bnei Yisrael* and were subject to more stringent obligations; they believed they retained the status of *bnei Noach*.

*Tosafot* notes, however, a difficulty with this first interpretation. If one accepts the view of *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon that non-Jews are *metamei* through *ohel* — that is, if the rule *atem atem keruyim adam* limiting *tumat ohel* to Jews is not accepted — then there would be no need for a special *pasuk* to teach that a grave from before *Matan Torah* conveys *tumah* through *maga*. Everyone already concedes that non-Jews convey *tumah* through touch; that is uncontested even by Rabbi Shimon. So what novel teaching is *kever shelifnei hadibbur* adding? If the point were about the status of the person — Jewish versus non-Jewish — and if touching a non-Jew's grave already conveys *tumah*, why do we need a *drasha* about the period before the Torah was given?

### Pre-Legislation Theory

This leads *Tosafot* to offer a second interpretation, which is, one might argue, the more natural reading. The *dibbur* in question is not the moment of *Matan Torah* as a whole, but the specific moment when the particular *parasha* of *tumat met* was legislated. The *parasha* of *adam ki yamut b'ohel* appears in *Chukat*, far into the forty years in the wilderness — not at Sinai. It is entirely possible that between *Matan Torah* and the giving of that specific *parasha*, people came into contact with the dead and entered a state of *tumah* before those laws existed. What is the status of such a person once the *parasha* is given?

### Ritva Solution

The *Ritzba* — cited within *Tosafot* — addresses this question by distinguishing between the metaphysical reality of *tumah* and the legal legislation that addresses it. The *Ritzba* holds that Jews possess *tumah* status even before the law is given; what *kever shelifnei hadibbur* teaches is that the laws, once legislated, apply retroactively to those who entered that status beforehand. This is not a punitive or retroactive legislation in the ordinary legal sense; rather, it reflects a presumption that *tumah* is a metaphysical condition that exists independently of whether *halakha* has yet defined its consequences. Once the *halakha* is given, it addresses the person's actual state, whatever the origin of that state.

### Parallel Cases

*Tosafot* draws a parallel to other areas of *tumah*. A *pasuk* in *Perek Haba al Ha'arayot* similarly derives the status of a *zav shelifnei hadibbur* — a man who experienced a *zov* before *Parshat Zavim* was given. The same question applies to a woman who had her period before *Parshat Niddah* was legislated: is she a *niddah* from the moment that *parasha* takes effect? The *drasha* answers that yes, even if the condition arose before the legislation, these laws apply. What this implies is that these *tumot* are not created by the law; the condition exists, and the law governs it once it is given.

### Metaphysics & Law

This raises a genuinely profound question: to what degree is *tumah v'tahara* a metaphysical reality, and to what degree is it purely a legal construct? If *tumah* is only a legal category, then it cannot exist before the law is enacted, and any attempt to apply it retroactively would be incoherent. But if *tumah* has an independent metaphysical reality — if it is a condition that attaches to a person regardless of legislation — then the law, when given, simply addresses a pre-existing state. One practical formulation: it is not that the person is held liable for having entered the *Mikdash* or otherwise violated a prohibition before the law was given — they violated nothing, since no prohibition existed. But from the moment the *parasha* is given, the person's current state is what it is: they are *tamei*, they must wait seven days, they must undergo the sprinkling of *parah adumah*, and all the attendant *halakhot* now apply to their actual condition. The law did not create the condition; it chose to address it.

## Nazir Non-Megaleach List

### Sprinkling Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַזֶּה בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי וּבַשְּׁבִיעִי וְסוֹתֵר וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught with regard to any nazirite who became impure from one of the listed sources of impurity: **And** a priest **sprinkles** the ashes of the red heifer on him **on the third and on the seventh** days, **and he negates** all the previous days he counted toward his naziriteship, and he does not begin counting a fresh term of naziriteship until after he becomes pure and brings his offerings.

The *mishnah* concludes with the phrase *umaza bashlishi uvisvi'i vesoteis* — he sprinkles on day three and day seven, and it destroys the earlier days. A *nazir* who contracts one of the qualifying forms of *tumat met* must undergo the full *tahara* process, including *hazayah* on days three and seven, and the days of *nezirut* already completed are invalidated. The *nazir* may not begin counting anew until he becomes *tahor*, and he brings his *korbanos* as required.

### Counting Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״עַד שֶׁיִּטְהַר״, בַּשְּׁבִיעִי קָאֵי — עַד דְּעָבֵיד הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ, וּמַנִּי — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא. אוֹ דִילְמָא בַּשְּׁמִינִי קָאֵי, וּמַאי ״עַד שֶׁיִּטְהַר״ — עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו. וּמַנִּי — רַבָּנַן הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: What is the meaning of **that which is taught** that a nazirite does not start counting **until he is purified?** Does the mishna refer to one who is **standing on the seventh** day of his purification and mean: **Until** he **performs** the requisite waiting for **sunset? And whose** opinion is it? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who rules that a nazirite starts counting from the eighth day, even before he brings his offerings. **Or perhaps** it is referring to one who is **standing on the eighth** day. **And** then, **what** is the meaning of: **Until he is purified?** It means: **Until he brings his offerings. And who is** the author of this opinion? **It is the Rabbis,** who maintain that he begins his fresh period of naziriteship only after he has sacrificed his offering of impurity (see 18b).

The *gemara* raises a question about the precise reading of the *mishnah's* phrase *ad she-yit-har* — until he becomes *tahor*. Does this mean he begins his new count on day seven, once *tahara* is technically achieved, or only on day eight, after the *korbanos* are brought? *Tosafot* notes that the version of the *mishnah* available to the *gemara* did not include the phrase *umeivi es korbanosav* — "and he brings his *korbanos*" — so the question is genuinely open from the text alone.

### R. Eliezer vs. Rabbanan

The *gemara* frames the dispute as follows. If *ad she-yit-har* means until the end of day seven — at which point *ha'erev shemesh* has passed and *tahara* is complete — then the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the *nazir* begins counting his *nezirut taharah* on day seven, since he is fully *tahor* by nightfall. If *ad she-yit-har* means until after the *korbanos* are brought, which occurs on day eight, then the *mishnah* follows the Rabbanan, who hold that *nezirut taharah* does not take effect until day eight.

### Braita Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: ״מַתְחִיל וּמוֹנֶה מִיָּד״, הָא רֵישָׁא מַאי ״עַד שֶׁיִּטְהַר״ — עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו, וּמַנִּי — רַבָּנַן הִיא דְּאָמְרִי: נְזִירוּת דְּטׇהֳרָה עַד שְׁמִינִי לָא חָיְילָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of this dilemma **from** the fact **that it teaches in the latter clause** of the next mishna that a nazirite who does not have to bring an offering **starts counting immediately** after the conclusion of his seven days of purification. The Gemara infers from this mishna: If so, **what is** the meaning of the different term used in **the first clause: Until he is purified?** It means **until he brings his offerings, and whose** opinion is it? **It is** that of **the Rabbis, who say** that the **naziriteship of purity does not commence until the eighth** day.

The *gemara* resolves the question by cross-referencing the *mishnah* immediately to follow — the list of things for which a *nazir* is *not* *megaleach*. That *mishnah* states: *umaza bashlishi uvisvi'i umatchil umoneh miyad* — he sprinkles on day three and seven and begins counting immediately. In this case, since the *nazir* is not *megaleach*, he does not bring *korbanos*, and *miyad* naturally refers to the moment he becomes *tahor* on day seven after the sprinkling. If "immediately" in the non-*megaleach* case means right after day seven, then "until he becomes *tahor*" in the *megaleach* case must refer to something later — namely, day eight after the *korbanos*. The contrast between the two formulations reveals that *miyad* is the earlier start point and *ad she-yit-har* is the later one.

### Day Eight Start

The *gemara* concludes: *manu Rabbanan hi d'amri nezirut tahara ad shemini lo chayila* — this accords with the Rabbanan, who hold that *nezirut tahara* does not take effect until day eight. The *nazir* who must bring *korbanos* cannot begin counting toward his new *nezirut* until after those *korbanos* are offered. The comparison with the non-*megaleach* case, where counting resumes immediately after day seven, establishes that the requirement to bring *korbanos* is what delays the start in the standard *megaleach* case, and that delay continues until day eight.

## Tumat But Not Megaleach

### Beit HaPras

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֲבָל הַסְּכָכוֹת, וְהַפְּרָעוֹת, וּבֵית הַפְּרָס,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna listed the sources of ritual impurity for which a nazirite must shave. This mishna adds: **However,** the nazirite does not shave for these: **The hanging** branches over a corpse, i.e., a tree overhanging a body that a nazirite passes, but it is uncertain which branches are over a corpse; **and the projecting** stones from fences when the place of the impurity is unknown; **and a *beit haperas*,** a place that contained a grave and was plowed. In the latter case, the entire area around the grave is impure from a corpse due to an uncertainty, as it might contain human bones.

The *mishnah* now presents a list of things for which a *nazir* is *not megaleach*. The first items are *beit hapras* and *eretz amim*. A *beit hapras* is a field over which a grave was plowed. The rabbis decreed that one who passes through such a field is *tamei*, out of concern that tiny fragments of bone may have been scattered across a wide area. Biblically, however, this decree does not apply: the area is too large, the fragments too small, and the concern too attenuated to rise to the level of *de'oraita tumah*. Since a *nazir* is only *megaleach* for qualified *tumat met de'oraita*, *beit hapras* does not trigger *megaleach*.

### Eretz Amim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים, וְהַגּוֹלֵל, וְהַדּוֹפֵק, וּרְבִיעִית דָּם, וְאֹהֶל, וְרוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת, וְכֵלִים הַנּוֹגְעִים בְּמֵת, וּבִימֵי סְפָרוֹ, וּבִימֵי גְמָרוֹ — עַל אֵלּוּ אֵין הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues its list: **And the land of the nations,** i.e., a nazirite left Eretz Yisrael for another land. The Sages decreed that all land outside of Eretz Yisrael is ritually impure. **And the grave cover; and the grave walls** upon which the cover rests; **and a quarter-***log* **of blood** from a corpse; **and a tent; and a quarter-***kav* **of bones** of a corpse; **and vessels that are touching a corpse. And** if the nazirite is **in the days of his counting,** i.e., the seven days a leper must count after purification from his leprosy; **or in his days of full** leprosy, when he is a full-fledged leper, **for these the nazirite does not shave.** This is the case even if he is rendered impure by one of the sources listed in the previous mishna.

*Eretz amim* — the land outside *Eretz Yisrael* — was declared *tamei* by rabbinic decree. The reasons given are multiple. One is a concern that Jewish bodies are buried throughout the diaspora, though the practical force of this reasoning is complicated. A more compelling explanation, which *Tosafot* states explicitly and which we will revisit, is that this decree was enacted as a social and national measure to encourage Jews to remain in *Eretz Yisrael*. There may also be a theological dimension — *Eretz Yisrael* as the uniquely sanctified land, and the outside as *eretz hatzmei'ah*. In any case, the *tumah* of *eretz amim* is rabbinic, and a *nazir* is not *megaleach* for it.

### Golel ve'Dofek

*Golel ve'dofek* is a category subject to a dispute between Rashi and *Tosafot* regarding its physical referent. Rashi, in *Perek Kama* of *Ketubot*, explains that the *golel* is the cover of the coffin and the *dofek* refers to the side panels. *Tosafot* disagrees, citing the *gemara* in *Perek Bameh Madlikin* which derives *golel ve'dofek* from the phrase *al p'nei hasadeh*, which implies objects that are exposed above ground, not buried. Rabbeinu Tam therefore explains that *golel* is the tombstone — the *matzeiva* — and *dofek* refers to the stones upon which the tombstone leans for support. A third possibility, raised in discussion, is that these terms refer to the stone sealing a catacomb, which accords with archaeological evidence that both above-ground and below-ground burial practices coexisted.

### Golel Status

What is significant about *golel ve'dofek* is that it is *de'oraita tumah* — specifically *tumat meis de'oraita* — and yet a *nazir* is not *megaleach* for it. The *Torah* derives from *al p'nei hasadeh* that the tombstone and its supporting stones carry biblical *tumah*, yet they are not included in the list of *nazir megaleach*. This is a striking principle: *de'oraita* status does not automatically entail *nazir megaleach*. The previous *mishnah* establishing when a *nazir* is *megaleach* never mentioned touching a *kever satum* on a part not directly opposite the *met*; the present *mishnah* clarifies that *golel ve'dofek*, though biblically *tamei*, is excluded from the *megaleach* requirement.

The practical corollary emerges from *Masechet Smachot*, which states: *kol meis she'ein hanazir megaleach alav, ein kohen muzhar alav* — any *tumah* for which a *nazir* is not *megaleach* is also one for which a *kohen* is not prohibited. This principle extends the significance of the *megaleach* list well beyond *nezirut* into *kohen* law.

### Revi'is Dam

*Revi'is dam* — a quarter-*log* of blood from a corpse — causes *tumah de'oraita*, whether through touch or *ohel*. Yet the *nazir* is not *megaleach* for it, because the qualifying quantity for *nazir megaleach* by blood is *chatzi log*, not *revi'is*.

### Ohel Rova Atzmos

Similarly, *ohel rova atzmos* — being under the same roof as a quarter-*kav* of bones — constitutes *tumat meis de'oraita* through *ohel*, yet it does not trigger *megaleach*. The threshold for *nazir megaleach* by bones in an *ohel* context is *chatzi kav*, not *rova kav*. These cases represent the other face of the distinctions that dominated the previous days of the *daf*: smaller quantities of blood and bone are *de'oraita tamei*, but they fall below the threshold at which *nazir megaleach* applies.

### Vessels Touching Corpse

The next category is *keilim hanogaim b'meis* — vessels that touched the corpse. The more interesting reading of this case, and the one with the most *halakhic* consequence, is that these are vessels falling into the category of *cherev harei hu kechalal*. Such vessels do not merely become an *av* through contact; they acquire the status of *avi avot hatumah*, like the *met* itself. A *nazir* who touches such a vessel would become an *av* and be *tamei* for seven days — a full *tumat meis* by all accounts. Yet the *mishnah* teaches that a *nazir* is not *megaleach* for this. These vessels are *de'oraita tumat meis* at the level of *avi avot*, and still not *megaleach*.

### Rav Goren Innovation

This ruling has a remarkable practical application in the *halakhot* of *kohanim*. We noted earlier the principle from *Masechet Smachot*: *kol meis she'ein hanazir megaleach alav, ein kohen muzhar alav*. If a vessel that has acquired the status of *cherev harei hu kechalal* is one for which a *nazir* is not *megaleach*, it follows that a *kohen* is not prohibited from touching such a vessel.

Rav Goren — not Rav Herzog, as initially misstated — proposed an innovative *halakhic* solution to the question of whether a *kohen* may study medicine and thereby come into repeated contact with corpses. The background is as follows: even granting the position that a *kohen* who is already *tamei meis* cannot become *re-tamei* simply by touching the *met* again, there remains the concern that active contact with the *met* raises the level of the *kohen's tumah*. Rav Goren's solution: bring metal rings into the room where the *met* is located. These rings thereby acquire the status of *cherev harei hu kechalal* — they become *avi avot hatumah*, as *tamei* as the *met* itself. Since a *kohen* is permitted to touch these rings (a *nazir* would not be *megaleach* for them, and accordingly a *kohen* is not *muzhar* on them), the *kohen* can then wear these rings while handling the *met*. By wearing an object that is already at the *avi avot* level, the *kohen* is not raising his level of *tumah* through direct contact with the *met* — he is already in contact with something of equivalent status. The logical and *halakhic* elegance of this approach generated significant controversy; Rav Moshe Feinstein reportedly had a strong reaction against it.

### Metzora Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַזֶּה בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי וּבַשְּׁבִיעִי,
>
> **Translation:** **And** in those cases listed that involve ritual impurity from a corpse, **one sprinkles** the purification water upon him **on the third and on the seventh** days of his purification,

The *mishnah* then turns to entirely different categories of *tumah* — those that are not *tumat meis* at all. A *metzora* in the stage of *yimei gmaro* — the second week of quarantine, when the spot has already spread and the *kohen* has sealed a full determination — or in *yimei seforo* — the subsequent seven-day counting period during which the *metzora* undergoes *tahara* before bringing *korbanos* on day eight — is a full *metzora* in the *halachic* sense. Tzaraas *tumah* is its own *de'oraita* category. Yet since it is not *tumat meis*, there is no *megaleach*. For these cases, the *mishnah* states: he is not *megaleach*, he sprinkles on days three and seven because there is *tumat meis* as well (since whatever *tumat meis* he may have contracted still requires *hazayah*), earlier days are not destroyed, and he begins counting *miyad* — immediately — once he becomes *tahor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר אֶת הַקּוֹדְמִים, וּמַתְחִיל וּמוֹנֶה מִיָּד, וְקׇרְבָּן אֵין לוֹ. בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ: יְמֵי הַזָּב וְהַזָּבָה וִימֵי הֶסְגֵּרוֹ שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ עוֹלִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **and he does not negate the earlier** days of his naziriteship, but they are considered part of his naziriteship term. **And he starts counting** the rest of his naziriteship to complete his term **immediately** after his purification, **and he has no** obligation to sacrifice **an offering** of impurity for these sources of ritual impurity. **Actually they said** an ancient tradition that these **days of** the impurity of **the *zav*** (Leviticus 15:1–15) **and the *zava*** (Leviticus 15:25–30) **and the days of the confinement of a leper** before he is confirmed as a full-fledged leper (Leviticus 13:4–5) **count for him** toward the period of his naziriteship.

The *mishnah* also clarifies that he does not bring the *korban* associated with *nazir megaleach*, since no *megaleach* has occurred.

### Light Tumot Counting

*Umbe'emes amru* — in truth they said — the *mishnah* adds a further distinction. *Yimei hazav v'hazavah* — the days in which a man is a *zav* or a woman is a *zavah* — and *yimei hesgeiro shel metzora* — the days of the first week of a *metzora's* quarantine, before a definitive diagnosis — not only do not trigger *megaleach*, they can actually count positively toward the *nazir's* total count of days. Since none of these constitute *tumat meis*, they do not prevent *nezirut* days from accumulating.

One might think this is obvious — what *tumah* other than *tumat meis* would interrupt *nezirut*? The real *chiddush* is what this implies by contrast with the first case. The *mishnah* contrasting these lighter cases with the case of the *metzora* in *yimei gmaro* or *yimei seforo* reveals something important: even for the heavier *metzora*, it is not *tumat meis* that prevents counting — it is the *tzaraas* state itself. The *nazir* in that state cannot count those days toward his *nezirut*, and must wait until he exits the *metzora* status entirely before resuming. The reason is not yet fully explained by the *gemara* and will require further examination.

### Sakkot & Peraot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאֵלּוּ הֵן הַסְּכָכוֹת — אִילָן הַמֵּיסֵךְ עַל הָאָרֶץ. וּפְרָעוֹת — הַיּוֹצְאוֹת מִן הַגָּדֵר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains the terms hangings and projections by citing a mishna (*Oholot* 8:2). **And these are the hangings: A tree that overhangs the earth. And** the **projections** are stones **that protrude from a fence.**

The *gemara* now defines the terms *sakkot* and *peraot* from the *mishnah*. *Sakkot* are trees that overhang the ground — *ilan hamaysach al ha'aretz*. *Peravot* are stones protruding from a wall — *avanim hayotzos min hageder* — adjacent to a cemetery. Walking under either constitutes only a rabbinic *tumah*, and therefore a *nazir* is not *megaleach* for either.

### Sakkot Non-Ohel

*Tosafot* explains, citing *Perek Kama d'Niddah*, that a tree is not a valid *ohel* from a *de'oraita* perspective because the branches are spread apart and do not constitute a solid *tefach* of coverage. The leaves and branches vary in density, and there is no reliable *tefach-by-tefach* roof. Even if there is certainly a *kezayit min hamet* buried beneath one of these trees, one who walks under it is *tahor mi-de'oraita*. Even walking under all of them would not create *de'oraita* liability, because one was never under a proper *ohel*. The rabbinic decree imposes *tumah* on one who passes beneath such trees, but since it is rabbinic, *nazir* is not *megaleach*.

### Peraot Rabbinic

The case of *peraot* is different in its rationale. A stone overhang can in principle constitute a proper *ohel* — that is not the issue. The issue is uncertainty about whether there is a *meis* beneath any given stone. *Tosafot* explains: this is a wall adjacent to a cemetery, and there may be a *nefel* — an infant who did not survive — buried beneath one of the protruding stones. The scenario of a protruding stone above a cemetery wall raises the question of how the *tumah* passes around or through the wall to the other side, which is why the case is framed in terms of a burial near or beneath the stone itself rather than across from a grave. In any case, one does not know with certainty whether there is a *meis* beneath the specific stone under which one walks. This is *sefeik tumah b'reshut harabbim* — a doubtful *tumah* in a public domain — which the rule of the *Torah* renders *tahor*. It is only by rabbinic decree that one is considered *tamei* for passing beneath such a stone. Since the *tumah* is rabbinic, *nazir* is not *megaleach*.

The distinction between the two cases is thus: by *sakkot*, the *ohel* itself is not a valid *de'oraita ohel* — the branches are insufficiently solid. By *peraot*, the *ohel* is potentially valid, but there is *sefeik* regarding the existence of a *meis* beneath it. In both cases the result is the same — only rabbinic *tumah*, and therefore no *nazir megaleach*.
