# Sanhedrin 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Textual Layout & Structu…

### Printer's Division

We open the Babylonian Talmud tractate of *Sanhedrin* on daf 2. The first striking feature of this daf is that the entire first *amud* is dominated by the *mishnah* — the *gemara* proper does not begin until later. This is worth pausing over, because it reflects a deliberate editorial choice by the publishers, not some intrinsic structure of the text itself.

The *mishnah* as it appears in printed editions of the Bavli was part of the manuscript tradition of the *gemara* — the two were not physically separate documents in the manuscripts. It was the printers, when moving from manuscript to the printed *Shas*, who decided how to divide the *mishnah* from the *gemara* and how large each presented unit of *mishnah* would be before its corresponding *gemara* followed. A printer could have divided the *mishnah* into many small chunks, each followed immediately by its relevant *gemara*, or into larger thematic blocks with correspondingly large sections of *gemara*. This was entirely a publisher's decision. Here, the printer chose to present the entire first chapter's *mishnah* as one continuous unit — most likely because it is unified by a clear thematic thread — and then to follow it with the *gemara* in full. It could just as easily have been divided into three-line segments with *gemara* interspersed.

### Transition & Ordering

We arrive at *Sanhedrin* coming directly from *Bava Batra*, making a natural thematic transition: from the substantive laws of property and commerce addressed in the *Bava*s to the laws of adjudication, courts, and judicial procedure in *Sanhedrin*. The general ordering of tractates within a *seder* in *Shas*, while the *gemara* sometimes offers thematic justifications, is primarily based on the number of *perakim* and overall size. The three *Bava*s together constitute *Nezikin* — thirty *perakim* as one unified tractate.

### Sanhedrin & Makkos

*Sanhedrin* and *Makkos* were originally a single tractate, just as the three *Bava*s were originally one. *Makkos* is a short tractate of only three *perakim*, while *Sanhedrin* runs eleven *perakim* in our editions — but properly speaking it is fourteen chapters, the remaining three being *Makkos*. They were separated because fourteen *perakim* was simply too large to remain as one tractate. The thematic coherence is evident: *Sanhedrin* deals with the court system and the administration of punishments, including capital punishment, while *Makkos* addresses the administration of lashes. The ordering of *Makkos* before *Shevuos* makes sense precisely because *Makkos* is organically part of *Sanhedrin*.

## Courts of Three

### Basic Categories

The *mishnah* opens: *dinei mamonos bishloshah* — monetary matters are adjudicated by a court of three. *Gezeilos vachavalos bishloshah* — theft and personal injury, likewise three. *Nezek vechatzi nezek, tashlumei kefel, tashlumei arba'ah vechamishah bishloshah* — full damages or half-damages (as in the case of a *tam* ox that gores), double payment in cases of burglary, and fourfold or fivefold payment when a stolen ox or sheep is subsequently slaughtered or sold — all of these, even though they move from compensation into the realm of monetary fines, are also adjudicated by a *beis din* of three.

Each ruling here carries its own element of surprise. One might have assumed that imposing monetary fines, which go beyond mere compensation, would require a larger court. The *mishnah* establishes that three suffice for the full range of monetary liability.

### Special Monetary Cases

The *mishnah* continues: *ha-onus veha-mefateh veha-motzi shem ra bishlosha* — the case of one who rapes an unmarried woman, one who seduces her, or a husband who falsely accuses his wife of not having been a virgin at the time of their marriage — these too are adjudicated by three judges. These cases fall within the category of fines: if the accuser is lying, he pays a monetary penalty, which is why they are grouped with the other fine-based cases.

### Slander Dispute

However, the *mishnah* immediately records a dissent: *divrei Rabbi Meir* — that is Rabbi Meir's position. *Vachachamim omrim ha-motzi shem ra b'esrim v'shlosha* — the Sages say the case of *motzi shem ra*, the false accusation of non-virginity, requires twenty-three judges, *mipnei she-yesh bo dinei nefashos* — because it contains within it the potential for a capital outcome. If witnesses emerge confirming that the wife did in fact commit adultery, the case becomes one of *dinei nefashos* and the woman's life is at stake. The Sages therefore require the full capital court.

### Lashes & Size

*Makos bishlosha* — lashes are administered on the basis of a *beis din* of three. This again is presented as a *chiddush*. If monetary fines required only three, one might well have assumed that corporal punishment — actual physical infliction of pain — demands a more substantial court. The *mishnah* says no, three suffice. *Mishum Rabbi Yishmael amru b'esrim v'shlosha* — Rabbi Yishmael, however, disagrees, holding that lashes are sufficiently analogous to a capital execution that they require a *beis din* of twenty-three. Bodily punishment, in his view, cannot be imposed by a mere three-judge court.

## Ritualistic Functions

### New Month Sanctification

The *mishnah* now shifts registers. We move from courts adjudicating disputes or imposing punishments to courts performing representational and ritualistic functions on behalf of the Jewish people. *Ibbur hachodesh bishlosha* — sanctifying the new month requires three. The *mishnah* uses the term *ibbur hachodesh* rather than *kiddush hachodesh* because the act in question is specifically the declaration that the outgoing month has concluded — that it was a twenty-nine-day month — thereby establishing when the new month begins. An important distinction must be drawn here: requiring only three judges does not mean any ad hoc *beis din* may perform this function. The size of the court and the question of who is authorized to represent the entire Jewish people in making this declaration are separate matters. *Kiddush hachodesh* must be performed by the designated court empowered to do so — it is simply that the minimum number of judges is three.

### Leap Year Declaration

*Ibbur hashanah bishlosha divrei Rabbi Meir* — declaring a leap year, that is, adding a thirteenth month to the calendar, requires three according to Rabbi Meir. *Mishum Gamliel amru bishlosha matchilin* — in the name of Rabban Gamliel, one may begin with three, *uv'chamisha nosin venosin* — debate the matter with five, given the many factors and considerations involved, *vegomrin beshivah* — and the final decision must be concluded with seven. *Ve-im nigmaru bishlosha me'ubaros* — but if in practice the final ruling was made by only three, it is nevertheless valid after the fact. This formulation raises an interesting question beyond the simple *lechatchilah u'bediavad* distinction: if seven are ultimately required and three suffice only *bediavad*, why not simply convene seven from the outset? And why the progressive escalation in the number of participants?

### Sin Offering Ritual

*Semichas zekenim* — this does not refer to the ordination of judges, as the name might suggest, but rather to the laying of hands by the elders upon the *par helam davar shel tzibbur*. When the *Beis Din HaGadol* issues an erroneous ruling and the majority of *Klal Yisrael* follows it and commits an inadvertent sin for which an individual would bring a *korban chatas*, the court upon recognizing its error brings a special communal *korban*. The laying of hands upon that *korban* requires only three. This is presented as a striking *chiddush*. This *korban* is the consequence of an error by the highest court of seventy-one. One would naturally expect that even if not all seventy-one members are needed, at least a more substantial representation of that court would be required. The fact that three suffice is genuinely surprising.

### Heifer Neck Breaking

*Va'arufas eglah bishlosha* — the ritual of *arifas eglah*, performed when an unidentified murder victim is found outside a city, requires three. In that ritual, the elders of the nearest city break the neck of a heifer, lay their hands upon it, and declare that their hands did not shed this blood. Here the *beis din* is not representing *Klal Yisrael* as a whole but rather the city. Still, one might have assumed that the full city court — ordinarily a *beis din* of twenty-three — would be required. The *mishnah* establishes that three representatives of that court suffice to stand in for the entire body. This, too, is a significant *chiddush*: three members of a court can represent the full court in performing a solemn communal ritual.

### Heifer Ritual Dispute

*Divrei Rabbi Shimon* — that is Rabbi Shimon's ruling that three suffice for *arifas eglah*. *Rabbi Yehuda omer b'chamisha* — Rabbi Yehudah disagrees, requiring five for this ritual.

## Status & Sanctification

### Chalitza & Refusal

Having surveyed courts that represent the people in ceremonial or representational capacities, the *mishnah* now turns to a third category: courts whose presence is required not to adjudicate a dispute, not to administer punishment, and not to represent the community at large, but rather to lend formal validity to a personal legal process. The closest analogy — though not cited in the *mishnah* because it was formulated later — is the requirement of a *beis din* for conversion. Here, the *beis din*'s role is to ritualize and validate the proceedings.

*Hachalitza v'ha-miunin bishlosha* — both *chalitza* and *miunin* require three. *Chalitza* is the process by which a *yavam* who declines to perform *yibum* releases his deceased brother's wife from the bond of *yibbum*: she removes his sandal, spits before him, and recites the prescribed declaration. The Torah itself specifies that this be done *le'einei hazekenim* — in the presence of elders. There is also arguably a representational dimension: the presence of the elders may serve to witness on behalf of the community that this woman is now released. The *beis din* of three is the minimum that satisfies this requirement.

*Miunin* — the refusal of marriage by a minor — applies when a girl below *bas mitzvah* was married off by her mother and brothers, a rabbinic institution created for cases where the father is absent and there is a clear societal and economic need to enable such a marriage. The Torah's marriage law vests this authority only in the father; the Rabbis extended a limited form of it to the mother and brothers specifically to address such situations. This rabbinic marriage carries with it the girl's right to refuse her husband at any point before she reaches *bas mitzvah* — once she attains *bas mitzvah* while living with her husband, it is too late. That act of refusal, like *chalitza*, must take place in the presence of a *beis din* of three.

### Value Assessment

*Neta revai u'maaser sheini she'ein damav yedua bishlosha* — when a person wishes to redeem his fourth-year fruit or his *maaser sheini* onto money and bring that money up to Jerusalem, and the precise value of the produce is not obvious or agreed upon, a *beis din* of three must assess the proper value. This differs from the case of items sanctified to the *Beis HaMikdash*, where the Temple treasurer is involved in the valuation. Here, the person is simply converting his own produce into money for his personal obligation to bring it to Jerusalem — but where the value is uncertain, a formal assessment by three judges is required.

### Sanctified Item Redempti…

*Ha-hekdeshos bishlosha* — when sanctified property is being redeemed, a *beis din* of three is required. *Ha-erachin ha-metaltelin bishlosha* — when one undertakes an *erech* in the sense of the valuation of movable property, this too requires three, though the *gemara* will investigate precisely what category of *erachin* the *mishnah* has in mind, since the primary usage of *erachin* in the Torah concerns the fixed valuations of human beings. *Rabbi Yehuda omer echad mehen kohen* — Rabbi Yehudah adds that in these cases involving *hekdesh*, since the proceedings have a ritual character connected to sacred property, one of the three judges must be a *kohen*.

### Land & Human Sanctificat…

*Ha-karkaos tisha v'kohen* — for the redemption of sanctified land, the court must consist of ten, with one of them being a *kohen*, for a total of ten. This number — ten plus one — no longer fits the standard *beis din* model, and the *gemara* will clarify its basis. *V'ha-adam k'yotzei bahen* — and similarly for the sanctification and redemption of persons; the *gemara* will note the surprising premise that a human being can be sanctified at all.

## Capital Crimes (23)

### Capital Crime Baseline

The *mishnah* now returns to the classic judicial function: adjudicating criminal cases. Having worked through the range of courts of three and then the courts of five and ten for ritual purposes, we arrive at the next major threshold. *Dinei nefashos b'esrim u'shlosha* — capital crimes require a *beis din* of twenty-three.

### Animal Capital Crimes

*Ha-roveia v'ha-nirva b'esrim u'shlosha* — even when the capital proceeding concerns an animal rather than a human being, the same court of twenty-three is required. An animal used in an act of bestiality — whether the *roveia*, the penetrating animal, or the *nirva*, the one penetrated — must be formally judged by a *beis din* of twenty-three before it is executed. The *mishnah* provides its biblical basis: *she-ne'emar v'haragta es ha-isha v'es ha-beheima* — in a case involving a woman, the verse states "you shall kill the woman and the animal," and similarly, *v'omer v'es ha-beheima taharogu* — in the case involving a man, "the animal you shall kill." The execution of the animal is equated by the Torah with the execution of the human participant, and therefore the same procedural requirements apply.

It is noteworthy that this is the first point in the entire *mishnah* where a biblical verse is cited — and it is cited precisely to justify the formal judicial process for an animal. The intuitive response would be: it is an animal, simply kill it. The *mishnah* insists that a full *beis din* of twenty-three must adjudicate the matter.

### Goring Ox Dispute

*Shor haniskal b'esrim v'shlosha* — a goring ox that has killed a person and is to be stoned also requires a *beis din* of twenty-three. The *mishnah* again cites a verse: *v'hashor yisakeil v'gam be'alav yumas* — "the ox shall be stoned, and also its owner shall be put to death." The phrase about the owner's death is not literal — it indicates the degree of culpability — but the *mishnah* reads from it the principle of *k'mitat ba'al kach mitat hashor*: just as executing the owner would require a *beis din* of twenty-three, so too does executing the ox.

The *mishnah* then lists dangerous wild animals — the wolf (*ze'ev*), the lion (*ari*), the bear (*dov*), the leopard (*namer*), and the *bardelas* (hyena or polecat), as well as the snake — stating that *misasan b'esrim v'shlosha*, their execution also requires twenty-three, since they are functionally equivalent to a *shor haniskal*, even though they are not literally oxen.

Why mention these animals at all? Because it sets up the disagreement that follows. *Rabbi Eliezer omer* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that these animals, unlike a domesticated ox that momentarily lost control, are constant and ongoing threats to human life. Therefore, *kol ha-kodem l'horgan zachah* — whoever kills them first has done a meritorious act; one need not wait for a *beis din*. *Rabbi Akiva omer misasan b'esrim v'shlosha* — Rabbi Akiva disagrees: even these dangerous animals must go through the same formal procedure as a *shor haniskal*, requiring a *beis din* of twenty-three.

## High Court (71)

### Tribe, Prophet, Kohen Ga…

*Ein danin lo es ha-shevet v'lo es nevi ha-sheker v'lo es kohen gadol ela al pi beis din shel shivim v'echad* — three categories of defendants can be tried only before the *beis din* of seventy-one: an entire tribe accused of *avodah zarah*, a false prophet, and a *kohen gadol*. The rationale in each case relates to the scope of the threat or the stature of the defendant. A false prophet poses an existential spiritual danger to the entire people, capable of leading all of Israel astray by misrepresenting the word of God. A tribe in collective rebellion against God is a communal catastrophe. The *kohen gadol* stands at the apex of the religious leadership of the nation. These are individuals and entities whose judgment must be rendered by the court that represents all of Israel.

### Discretionary Warfare

*V'ein motzin l'milchemet ha-reshut ela al pi beis din shel shivim v'echad* — one may not go out to wage a *milchemet reshut*, a discretionary war, without the authorization of the *beis din* of seventy-one. A *milchemet reshut* is distinguished from the obligatory wars of conquering the Land of Israel and — as the *Rambam* adds — defending Israel against enemies. A war of expansion or strategic choice, by contrast, requires the formal authorization of the *beis din* of seventy-one. This represents yet another role of the high court: not adjudicating a case, but authorizing a major national decision on behalf of the people, representing both the authority of the community and, through appropriate channels, the authority of God.

### Jerusalem Expansion

*V'ein mosifin al ha-ir v'al ha-azarot ela al pi beis din shel shivim v'echad* — one may not extend the halakhic borders of Jerusalem or expand the courtyard of the *Beis HaMikdash* without the authority of the *beis din* of seventy-one. The existing borders of Jerusalem and the *azaros* carry halakhic sanctity, and their formal expansion is a decision that affects the entire Jewish people and must be authorized by its highest representative body.

### Appointing Lower Courts

*Ein osin sanhedriyot l'shvatim ela al pi beis din shel shivim v'echad* — the appointment of *Sanhedrin* courts for the individual tribes, each of which consisted of twenty-three judges, requires the authorization of the *beis din* of seventy-one. The *Sanhedrin Gedolah* is the source of judicial authority in Israel, and the establishment of subordinate *sanhedriyot* flows from it.

### Idolatrous City

*Ein osin Ir Nidachas ela al pi beis din shel shivim v'echad* — the condemnation of an *ir nidachas*, an entire city that has been led into *avodah zarah* and is subject to total destruction, can only be decreed by the high court of seventy-one. The enormity of the act — wiping out an entire city — demands the highest level of judicial authority. The *mishnah* adds a brief but striking digression on the parameters of this law: one does not make an *ir nidachas* of a border city (*ein osin ir nidachas basfer*), since destroying a city on the frontier would create a dangerous opening for enemy attack. And one does not condemn three cities simultaneously as *ir nidachas*, as this would devastate the country. One or two cities, however, may be condemned.

## Biblical Derivations

### Seventy Elders

The *mishnah* steps back and restates the framework in summary: *Sanhedrin Gedolah haysa shivim v'echad* — the high court consisted of seventy-one members. *U'ktanah shel esrim u'shlosha* — the smaller courts, including the tribal *sanhedriyot* capable of adjudicating capital cases, consisted of twenty-three. Now the *mishnah* turns to biblical derivations.

*Minayin l'gedolah she'hi shivim v'echad* — where do we derive that the high court is seventy-one? *She-ne'emar esfa li shivim ish miziknei Yisrael* — "gather for me seventy men from the elders of Israel," those who were to assist Moshe in leading the people. That body of seventy is understood as a deliberative court, not as seventy individuals each independently governing separate units of the population, but as a single representative body for all of Israel. *U'Moshe aleihem* — and Moshe was over them, bringing the total to seventy-one. This is a striking rereading of that narrative. *Rabbi Yehuda omer shivim* — Rabbi Yehudah holds the *Sanhedrin* consisted of only seventy, which immediately raises the objection that an even-numbered *beis din* is procedurally invalid. That issue will be addressed.

### Twenty-Three Derivation

*U'minayin l'ktanah she'hi esrim u'shlosha* — how do we know the smaller court is twenty-three? *She-ne'emar v'shaftu ha'eidah v'hitzilu ha'eidah* — "the community shall judge and the community shall save" — a verse applied to capital cases. The *derashah* reads the two occurrences of *eidah* as referring to two distinct groups within the *beis din*: *eidah shofetes*, the group that judges toward guilt — and *eidah matzeles*, the group that works toward acquittal. Each *eidah* consists of ten, giving us twenty.

How do we know an *eidah* is ten? *She-ne'emar ad masai la'eidah hara'ah hazos* — "until when shall I bear this evil community" — a verse referring to the spies. Yehoshua and Kalev were righteous; the remaining ten spies constituted the *eidah ra'ah*, the evil community. Thus an *eidah* is ten.

So we have ten arguing for guilt and ten arguing for innocence — twenty in total. This is specifically a capital case, which is why this verse applies to the derivation of the court size for *dinei nefashos*.

### Majority Rules

But we are still only at twenty, and a *beis din* for capital cases is twenty-three. *U'minayin l'havi od shlosha* — how do we get three more?

From the verse: *lo tihyeh acharei rabim l'ra'os* — "do not follow the majority to evil." This is read as a halakhic principle: a simple majority is insufficient to convict. The verse cautions against mechanically following the majority when the outcome is to find someone guilty. *Shome'a ani she-ye-hei imahem l'tovah* — from this I can infer that a simple majority is sufficient to acquit.

Then the *mishnah* asks: *im ken lamah ne'emar acharei rabim le-hatot* — if a simple majority suffices for acquittal, what need is there for the verse to state "follow the majority"? *Lo k'hatayascha l'tovah hatayascha l'ra'ah* — because the majority required to convict is not the same as the majority required to acquit. A simple majority of one is sufficient for acquittal. Conviction, however, requires a majority of two: *hatayascha l'ra'ah al pi shnayim*.

### Even Number Problem

Now the arithmetic follows. We have ten arguing for innocence. For conviction, the side arguing for guilt must exceed the innocence side by at least two. That means twelve must argue for guilt against ten for innocence, bringing the total to twenty-two. *V'ein beis din shakul* — but you cannot have an even-numbered *beis din*. *Mosivin aleihem od echad harei kan esrim u'shlosha* — therefore one more is added, giving us twenty-three.

The observant reader will immediately note a tension: if the *beis din* has twenty-three members, a majority of two requires thirteen votes for guilt against eleven for innocence — not twelve against ten. How this is resolved with the requirement of a two-vote majority will be addressed in the *gemara*.

### City Population

*V'kamah ye-hei b'ir v'teihei re'uyah l'Sanhedrin* — the *mishnah* now asks how large a city must be to merit its own *Sanhedrin* of twenty-three. *Me'ah v'esrim* — 120. This reflects the notion that the members of the *Sanhedrin* must be drawn from the general population, and the city must be large enough to support such a court while maintaining a viable population base.

### Rabbi Nechemiah's View

*Rabbi Nechemiah omer masayim u'shloshim k'neged sarei asaros* — Rabbi Nechemiah requires 230, reasoning from the institution of *sarei asaros*, the leaders of tens appointed on the advice of Yisro. The minimum rung of communal authority is authority over ten people. For each of the twenty-three members of the *Sanhedrin* to hold at least that minimum level of authority, the city must contain 230 adult males — ten per judge. Whether the judges themselves are counted within or apart from those ten is a question left open.

This concludes the entire sweep of *mishnayos* that make up the first *perek* of *Sanhedrin*. We now turn to the *gemara*.

## Theft vs. Civil Cases

### Enumeration Question

The *gemara* opens with a challenge to the structure of the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* states *dinei mamonos bishlosha* and then immediately adds *gezeilos vachavalos bishlosha* — monetary matters require three, then theft and personal injury require three. *Atu gezeilos vachavalos lav dinei mamonos ninhu* — but are theft and personal injury not themselves monetary matters? Why enumerate them separately? They would seem to be an obvious subset of the opening category.

### Rabbi Avahu's Interpreta…

*Amar Rabbi Avahu mah hen kasani* — Rabbi Avahu resolves this by reinterpreting the structure of the *mishnah*. The opening clause *dinei mamonos bishloshah* is not a general umbrella statement to which everything below is a specification. Rather, it is itself a definition. The *mishnah* is telling you what it means by *dinei mamonos*: *mah hen dinei mamonos — gezeilos vachavalos* — what is meant by "monetary matters"? Theft and personal injury. Nothing broader.

This reading has a striking implication. The most obvious cases of *dinei mamonos* — ordinary civil disputes, debts, loans, contract claims — are deliberately excluded from the *mishnah*'s category. *Aval hoda'os vehalva'os* — but admissions and loans, standard civil cases not involving torts, are not being included.

The *gemara* presents the double necessity for stating it this way. If the *mishnah* had said only *dinei mamonos bishlosha* without specifying *gezeilos vachavalos*, one might have understood it to include the full range of monetary disputes, certainly encompassing standard loans and civil claims. By explicitly saying *gezeilos vachavalos*, the *mishnah* signals that the category is restricted. Conversely, if the *mishnah* had listed only *gezeilos vachavalos* without the umbrella term *dinei mamonos*, one might have read it as illustrative — meaning *gezeilos vachavalos* are mentioned because that is where the derivation of "three" originates, but in practice everything, including civil cases, is included. The double formulation — umbrella statement plus specification — is therefore necessary to achieve precision: *dinei mamonos* means and is limited to *gezeilos vachavalos*, excluding ordinary loans and civil disputes.

It is worth noting that this reading is not the natural one. A straightforward reader of the *mishnah* would likely understand *dinei mamonos* as covering all civil matters, with *gezeilos vachavalos* then introduced as cases that might have been thought to belong to a different category. Rabbi Avahu is saying the opposite: the *mishnah* is restricting *dinei mamonos* to tort-like cases and deliberately leaving out ordinary civil litigation.

### Biblical Derivation

*Dechtiv* — and now the *gemara* explains where the requirement of three originates. The verse states *venikrav ba'al habayit el ha'elohim* — "the owner of the house shall be brought to the judges." This concerns the case of a *shomer*, a bailee, who denies that an object was ever entrusted to him, or who falsely claims it was damaged or lost, and then swears falsely to that effect. When witnesses expose the lie before a *beis din*, the *shomer* is liable. This is a case of *gezeilah*: the *shomer* actively possesses someone else's property and is keeping it from its owner.

*Rashi* draws an important distinction here. If someone fails to repay a loan and simply denies owing the money, he is certainly stealing in a moral sense — he is withholding money that belongs to another. But that is not *gezeil* in the technical sense, because there is no discrete object in his possession that belongs to the claimant. *Gezeil* requires that an identifiable object belonging to someone else is being actively withheld. The *shomer* who has the object in hand and lies about it is the paradigmatic case of *gezeil*, and it is from that case — *el ha'elohim*, to the judges — that we derive the requirement of three.

*Gezeilos* and *chavalos* are then treated as functionally analogous: *mah li chaval begufo mah li chaval bemamono* — whether you injured a person's body or injured his property, the underlying category is the same. Consequently, *gezeilos* is the classic source for the three-judge requirement. Since that is the derivation, one might have argued that the *mishnah* mentions *gezeilos* not to exclude civil cases but simply because *gezeilos* is the textual basis — yet in practice, everything, including loans and civil disputes, would require the same three. The *gemara* rejects this: *tani mah hen dinei mamonos gezeilos vachavalos* — the *mishnah* is specifically defining *dinei mamonos* as limited to *gezeilos vachavalos*, thereby excluding standard civil cases from the same halakhic framework.

### Loans Don't Need Status

*Aval hoda'os vehalva'os lo* — so civil disputes and loan claims are excluded. But excluded from what, exactly? *Ul'mai* — for what specific halakhic requirement? *Eilima d'lo ba'inan shlosha* — one might think the exclusion means they do not need three at all. But that cannot be right. *Veha amar Rabbi Avahu* — Rabbi Avahu himself ruled that *shnayim she'danu dinei mamonos, divrei hakol ein dineihem din* — a panel of two that adjudicated a monetary case has not rendered a valid judgment, according to all opinions. So even ordinary loans and civil disputes require three. Rabbi Avahu who formulated the exclusion of civil cases from the *mishnah*'s category is the same Rabbi Avahu who insists that anything fewer than three is invalid for monetary cases.

### Mumchin Concept

*Ella d'lo ba'inan mumchin* — the exclusion, therefore, is not from the requirement of three judges, but from the requirement that those three judges be *mumchin*. For *gezeilos vachavalos* — the cases enumerated by the *mishnah* — the three judges must be *mumchin*. For ordinary loans and civil disputes, while three judges are still required, they do not need to be *mumchin*.

What is a *mumche*? *Rashi* explains: *smuchim*, those who have received *semicha* — the formal chain of rabbinic ordination tracing back to Moshe Rabbeinu — and moreover, *venatlu reshut miNasi ladun*, those who have received political authorization from the *Nasi* to adjudicate. The full apparatus of formal judicial appointment is required for *gezeilos* and *chavalos*. For ordinary civil disputes, ordinary qualified judges suffice — three, but not necessarily *mumchin*. This distinction, and its far-reaching implications, will be developed in the *gemara* as we continue.
