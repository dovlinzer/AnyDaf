# Zevachim 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul Sources

### Opening & Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב – לֹא יְעָרֵב בּוֹ מַחְשָׁבוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"neither shall it be credited [*yeḥashev*]"** indicates that to render an offering *piggul*, the priest **does not mix other** improper **intentions [*maḥashavot*] with** intent of *piggul*. If he has more than one type of improper intent, the offering is disqualified, but it is not rendered *piggul*.

The daf begins at the bottom of 28b, eleven lines from the end, at the words *vekamei mai*, in the midst of a sustained effort to identify the *pesukim* from which the laws of *piggul* are derived. The central questions are where we learn that a *machshava* of *chutz lizmano* — an intent to consume a sacrifice outside its permitted time — renders the offering *piggul* and incurs *karet* for one who eats it, and, correspondingly, where we learn that a thought of *chutz limkomo* — intent to consume it in the wrong place — constitutes only a *lo ta'aseh*, a negative prohibition, without the penalty of *karet*.

### Long & Short Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״פִּגּוּל״ – זֶה חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ. ״יִהְיֶה״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמִּצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** When the verse states: **"*Piggul*," this is** referring to an offering that one had intent to consume **outside its** designated **area.** And since the verse states: **"It shall be,"** and not: They shall be, this **teaches that** intent to consume half an olive-bulk beyond its designated time and intent to consume half an olive-bulk outside its designated area **join together with one another** to constitute the minimum measure required to disqualify the offering.

The *gemara* works with two *pesukim*. The first, the longer one, appears in *Tzav* (Vayikra 7:18): *ve'im he'achol ye'achel mibsar zevach shelamav bayom hashlishi lo yeratzeh hamakriv oto lo yechashev lo piggul yihyeh venefesh ha'ochelet mimenu avonah tisa*. If one eats from the flesh of his *shelamim* on the third day, it will not be accepted; the one who offers it shall have it not reckoned to him; it shall be *piggul*, an abomination; and whoever eats of it shall bear his iniquity. The second, shorter *pasuk* appears at the opening of *Kedoshim* (Vayikra 19:7): *ve'im he'achol ye'achel bayom hashlishi piggul hu lo yeratzeh* — and it shall be *piggul*, it will not be accepted — followed by an explicit statement of *karet*: *venichreta hanefesh hahi me'ameha*.

Although the plain sense of both *pesukim* concerns *notar* — leftover sacrificial meat eaten after its permitted time — the *gemara* reads them as speaking of *machshava*, of intent, since a *korban* cannot logically be retroactively invalidated by what someone does with its meat two days after the fact.

## Rava's Derivation

### All from Tzav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כּוּלְּהוּ מִקְּרָא אֲרִיכָא אָתַיִין; דִּכְתִיב: ״הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל״ – בִּשְׁתֵּי אֲכִילוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אֶחָד אֲכִילַת אָדָם וְאֶחָד אֲכִילַת מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says:** One must accept the original interpretation of Shmuel's statement, that **all of** these *halakhot* with regard to intent to consume the offering beyond its designated time or outside its designated area **are derived from the long verse. As it is written:** "If any of the flesh of his peace offerings be at all eaten on the third day, it shall not be accepted, neither shall it be credited to he who offers it; it shall be *piggul*, and the soul that eats of it shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 7:18). When the verse states: **"Be at all eaten [*he'akhol ye'akhel*],"** using a doubled verb, **the verse speaks of** intent with regard to **two consumptions, one consumption by a person, and one consumption by** the **altar,** i.e., burning the offering. Improper intent with regard to either of these acts renders the offering *piggul*.

The *gemara* had struggled to resolve precisely which *pasuk* addresses *chutz lizmano* and which *chutz limkomo*, and which carries *karet*. It had reached an impasse. Now, in a fresh approach: *ela amar Rava* — rather, Rava says — *kulu mikra arichta ati*, all of the fundamental laws of *piggul* are derivable from the single long *pasuk* in *Tzav*.

### Double Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח שְׁלָמָיו״ – מָה שְׁלָמִים מְפַגְּלִין וּמִתְפַּגְּלִין, אַף כֹּל מְפַגְּלִין וּמִתְפַּגְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"any of the flesh of his peace offerings"** indicates that **just as a peace offering** possesses that which **renders *piggul* and** that which **is rendered *piggul*,** i.e., the blood, which renders the offering *piggul* through one's intent with regard to its rites, and the meat, which is rendered *piggul* through such intent, **so too** the verse is referring to **all** offerings that possess that which **renders** an offering ***piggul* and** that which **is rendered *piggul*.**

The *pasuk* opens *he'achol ye'achel*, a doubled verb. The *gemara* interprets this as speaking of two distinct types of eating: *achilat adam*, human eating, and *achilat mizbe'ach*, the consumption by the altar. Accordingly, a *piggul* thought may concern either the intent that a human will eat the meat of the *korban* at the wrong time or the intent that the *emurim* — the entrails — will be burned on the altar at the wrong time. The act of *zorek hadam*, throwing the blood, is both the act during which the *piggul* thought is formed and the subject around which the entire *avoda* turns. The focus of the double language, however, is on these two forms of consumption: the *basar* and the *emurim*.

## Rava's Derivation…

### Shelamim Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ – זֶה חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ. ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״ – כְּהַרְצָאַת כָּשֵׁר, כֵּן הַרְצָאַת פָּסוּל; וּמָה הַרְצָאַת כָּשֵׁר – עַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיבוּ כׇּל מַתִּירָיו, אַף הַרְצָאַת פָּסוּל – עַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיבוּ כׇּל מַתִּירָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The term "on the **third** day" indicates that the verse is referring to intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time,** as peace offerings may be eaten for only two days. The phrase **"it shall not be accepted"** indicates that an offering is deemed *piggul* following the stage of the service at which it would have otherwise effected atonement. Therefore, **like the acceptance of a fit** offering, **so** is **the acceptance of a disqualified** offering, **and just as the acceptance of a fit** offering does not occur **until one sacrifices all its permitting factors,** i.e., one sprinkles the blood properly, **so too the acceptance of a disqualified** offering does not occur **until one sacrifices all its permitting factors.**

The *pasuk* specifies *mibsar zevach shelamav* — from the flesh of his *shelamim* sacrifice. The *gemara* asks: why are *shelamim* the paradigm for *piggul*? Because *shelamim maphalgin umitpalgin* — *shelamim* divide and are divisible. That is, in a *shelamim* there is both the act through which the *piggul* operates (the blood service) and the result that becomes permitted through that act (the meat). The *shelamim* thus has a clear *matir* on one side and a *muttar* on the other. This principle, *maphalgin umitpalgin*, is the basis for the rule that *piggul* applies to any *korban* that has both these features. What is thereby excluded? The *mincha* of the *Kohen Gadol*, which is burned entirely on the altar — there is no portion that becomes permitted to a human through the act of offering, and thus the structure of a *matir* and a *muttar* does not apply.

### Shlishi & Hartzaah

The *pasuk* then says *bayom hashlishi* — on the third day. This is the concept of *chutz lizmano*: the thought to consume the offering beyond its permitted time. The *pasuk* continues *lo yeratzeh* — it will not be accepted. From here the *gemara* derives: *k'hartzat kasher ken hartzat pasul* — just as a *kasher* sacrifice must go through the full process of *hartza'ah* to be accepted, so too even in the case of *piggul* the offering achieves the status of *piggul* only if the entire process is carried out properly. This is one of the genuine ironies of the laws of *piggul*: it is only when everything is done correctly that the *piggul* thought takes full effect.

### Matirim & Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַמַּקְרִיב״ – בְּהַקְרָבָה הוּא נִפְסָל, וְאֵינוֹ נִפְסָל בִּשְׁלִישִׁי. ״אוֹתוֹ״ – בַּזֶּבַח הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר בַּכֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The term **"he who offers"** indicates that *piggul* **is disqualified at** the time of **offering,** due to improper intent, **and it is not disqualified on the third** day literally. And when the verse states **"it,"** this indicates that **the verse is speaking of** a disqualification of the **offering** only, **but it is not speaking of** the **priest,** who is not disqualified from the priesthood through such intent.

The principle is stated explicitly: *mah hartzat kasher ad she'yakrivu kol matirav* — just as an offering becomes accepted only after all of its *matirim* are brought, so too *hartzat pasul ad she'yakrivu kol matirav* — even by a *pasul* offering, specifically a *piggul*, one achieves the status of *piggul* only after all the *matirim* are properly offered. If the blood is spilled rather than properly thrown, there is no *piggul*. If the *zrikah* is performed *shelo lishmo*, the *piggul* thought is neutralized. Everything must be done in perfect conformity with *halacha* — the sole imperfection permitted, as it were, is the *piggul* thought itself. *Ad she'yakrivu kol matirav.*

### Time of Piggul

The *pasuk* says *hamakriv oto* — the one who offers it. From this the *gemara* derives: *b'hakrava hu nifsal v'eino nifsal bashlishi* — the *korban* becomes invalid at the time of its being offered, not on the third day itself. Contrary to the plain sense of the *pasuk*, the invalidation does not occur when the *notar* is actually eaten on the third day. It is the *machshava* at the moment of *hakrava* that renders the *korban piggul*.

### Lo Yechashev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ הָאֹכֶלֶת מִמֶּנּוּ״ – אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם; וְאֵיזֶה? זֶה חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – דְּגָמַר ״עָוֹן״–״עָוֹן״ מִנּוֹתָר, דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ בְּז״ב.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, when the verse states: **"And the soul that eats of it** shall bear his iniquity," the term "of it" indicates that only **one** of the above disqualifications carries the penalty of *karet*, **but not two** of them. **And which is this?** It is intent to consume the offering **beyond its** designated **time, as** one **derives** by way of verbal analogy between **"iniquity"** and **"iniquity" from *notar*** (Leviticus 19:8), that "bear his iniquity" means that one is liable to receive *karet*. The comparison is apt **since** *notar* **is similar to** intent to consume the offering beyond its designated time **in** that both prohibitions share the features mentioned above that form the acronym: ***Zayin*, *beit*.**

The phrase *lo yechashev lo* — it shall not be reckoned to him — teaches: *lo ye'arev bo machshavot acherot*, other invalidating thoughts may not be mixed in. If, in addition to a *chutz lizmano* thought, there is also a thought of *chutz limkomo*, that additional thought removes the *korban* from the category of *piggul*. The *korban* remains *pasul*, but it is no longer *piggul*, and therefore one who eats from it does not incur *karet*. This flows directly from the same principle that requires *kol matirav* to be offered properly: a *piggul* can exist only when the sole flaw is the *piggul* thought. Any other invalidating thought compromises that exclusivity.

### Yihyeh & Combining

The word *piggul yihyeh* — it shall be *piggul* — teaches, by the word *yihyeh*, that *mitztarfin zeh im zeh*: different categories of improper intent combine with one another. If one had a thought to eat half a *kezayit* in the wrong place and half a *kezayit* at the wrong time, even though there is no full *kezayit* of a single type of *piggul* thought, and even though the presence of a *chutz limkomo* thought means it cannot be *piggul*, nonetheless both thoughts together constitute a *kezayit* of improper intent sufficient to render the *korban pasul*. Not *piggul*, but *pasul*.

### Mimenu Singular

The *pasuk* then reads *venefesh ha'ochelet mimenu* — the soul that eats *mimenu*, from it, singular. *Echad velo shnayim* — only one of the two types of improper thought carries the punishment of *karet*. Having established that *chutz limkomo* and *chutz lizmano* combine for *psul* but represent two different categories, the singular *mimenu* now tells us that only one of them — not both — incurs *karet*.

### Chutz Lizmano Karet

Which one is it? *Zeh chutz lizmano* — it is *chutz lizmano* that carries *karet*. The reasoning: *degmar avon avon minosar* — we derive it through a *gezeirah shavah* of the word *avon* appearing here (*avonah tisa*) and the same word appearing in the *pasuk* in *Kedoshim* (*avono yisa*), which speaks explicitly of *karet* and which, in its plain sense, concerns *notar*. Both *notar* and *chutz lizmano* are fundamentally about *zman*, about time — and the close conceptual and textual connection between them, *dedami lei bizman*, is what establishes that the *karet* flows to *chutz lizmano* and not *chutz limkomo*. Furthermore, *chutz limkomo* has no application by a *bamah*, a private altar — there is no fixed *makom* in such a context — whereas *chutz lizmano* applies there as well, reinforcing the alignment of *chutz lizmano* with the punishment derived from *notar*.

## Piggul Sources…

### Shelamim Paradigm

With all of the major laws of *piggul* now derived from the single long *pasuk* in *Tzav* through Rava's comprehensive *derasha*, the question naturally arises about the role of the shorter *pasuk* in *Kedoshim*. Before proceeding to Rav Pappa's challenge, it is worth noting again the underlying logic of the *shelamim* paradigm. The principle of *maphalgin umitpalgin* — that the *shelamim* divides and is divided — means that there is a clear structure in which an act of blood service on one side permits both the eating of the meat by the *ba'alim* and the burning of the *emurim* on the altar. Any *korban* that shares this structure, in which one set of acts (*matirim*) permits another set of resulting consumptions, is subject to the laws of *piggul*. What is excluded is precisely that which lacks this bilateral structure.

## Rav Pappa & Kedoshim

### Rav Pappa's Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: לְדִידָךְ, ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ דְּפָרָשַׁת ״קְדֹשִׁים תִּהְיוּ״ – מַאי דָּרְשַׁתְּ בֵּיהּ? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמָקוֹם שֶׁיְּהֵא מְשׁוּלָּשׁ בְּדָם, בְּבָשָׂר וּבְאֵימוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava: According to your** opinion, in **what** way do **you interpret** the reference to the **third** day (Leviticus 19:7) **in the** Torah **portion** that begins: **"You shall be holy"?** Rava responded: **That** verse is **necessary to** teach **that** a given **location** is considered to be outside the offering's designated area only if it **is** so **in three ways,** i.e., outside the permitted limits **of** the **blood, of** the **meat, and of** the **sacrificial portions.**

Rav Pappa challenged Rava: having derived all the laws of *piggul* from the long *pasuk* in *Tzav*, what does the *pasuk* in *Kedoshim* — *ve'im he'achol ye'achel bayom hashlishi* — come to teach that is not already known from *Tzav*?

### Makom Meshulash

Rava responds: the *pasuk* in *Kedoshim* is needed to teach the requirement of *makom meshulash* — a tripartite place. *Hu mibaei lei lemakom shemeshulash bedam bebasar uve'imurim*: the laws of *piggul* apply only where the relevant location is equally valid — or equally invalid — with respect to all three: the *dam*, the *basar*, and the *emurim*. This is a distinct and additional *halacha* that could not simply be derived from the *pasuk* in *Tzav*.

### Rashi Heichal

Rashi understands the requirement of *makom meshulash* as follows: a thought of *chutz limkomo* constitutes a valid *piggul* thought only if the place contemplated is invalid for all three — the blood, the meat, and the *emurim*. The *heichal* presents the relevant test case. The *heichal* is not a place where one eats the *basar* of *kodshei kodashim*, nor where the *emurim* are burned; however, the blood of certain inner *chataot* is applied there on *Yom Kippur*. Since the *heichal* is not uniformly invalid for all three, a thought to eat the *korban* inside the *heichal* does not qualify as a *chutz limkomo* thought that creates *piggul*. Rashi's reading thus focuses on whether the contemplated location is invalid in a uniform and tripartite manner.

### Tosafot Views

Tosafot offer alternative interpretations. One explanation holds that *makom meshulash* refers to the type of *korban* being brought: *piggul* applies only to offerings brought on the outer *mizbeach*, since the outer *mizbeach* is the place for the meat of the *olah*, for the blood, and for the *emurim*. By contrast, a *korban* whose blood is applied on the inner *mizbeach* — such as certain inner *chataot* — would not generate *piggul* since the inner *mizbeach* is not a place for all three elements. Tosafot itself notes the difficulty with this position, since evidence from elsewhere in the *gemara* indicates that *piggul* does apply to inner *chataot*. A second explanation offered by Tosafot shifts the focus entirely to the person performing the *avoda*: the *kohen* must be standing in a place that is *meshulash*, meaning in the *azara*, a location that is equally valid for blood, meat, and the burning of *emurim*. If the *kohen* is performing the *shechita* while standing outside the *azara* — which is in itself *kasher*, since a long knife can extend across the threshold — but has a *piggul* thought at that moment, the thought is not operative, since he is not standing in a place that qualifies as *makom meshulash*.

### Need for Kedoshim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיפּוֹק לִי מִקְרָא קַמָּא: ״אִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל״ – מִדְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן ״שְׁלִישִׁי״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why not **let me derive** that *halakha* **from the first verse:** "And **if** any of the flesh of his peace offerings **be at all eaten** on the third day" (Leviticus 7:18), **since the Merciful One expresses** that verse as well **with the term "third"?** Since Rava derives the disqualification of intent to consume an offering outside its designated area from this verse, why not let him derive this *halakha* from the verse as well?

One might ask why the *pasuk* in *Tzav* alone could not teach this requirement. The *gemara* explains: in the *pasuk* of *Tzav*, the word *shelishi* — third — appears, followed immediately by the word *piggul*. Had we tried to derive *makom meshulash* from *shelishi* in *Tzav*, we would have read *shelishi* as a *prat* narrowing the scope of *piggul* to a tripartite context, and then *piggul* immediately afterward would function as a *klal*, broadening it back out — resulting in a *klal mosif al haprat* that would expand *piggul* to apply even to non-tripartite locations. As Rav Ashi related in the name of Rav Masna: *im mehatam hava amina shelishi prat piggul klal venasa klal mosif al haprat veyisrebu shear mekomot* — I would have concluded that other locations are included, and the tripartite limitation would be lost. Therefore the separate *pasuk* in *Kedoshim* was necessary to anchor the requirement of *makom meshulash* without being subjected to such an expansive reading. That, and no more, is the function of the *pasuk* in *Kedoshim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב מַתְנָה; אָמַר לִי: אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ – פְּרָט, ״פִּגּוּל״ – כְּלָל; וְנַעֲשֶׂה כְּלָל מוּסָף עַל הַפְּרָט, וְאִיתְרַבּוֹ שְׁאָר מְקוֹמוֹת; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I stated** this ***halakha* before Rav Mattana,** and **he said to me: If** one were to derive this *halakha* only **from there** (Leviticus 7:18), **I would say:** The term **"third"** is **a detail,** and the term **"*piggul*"** is **a generalization, and** whenever a detail is followed by a generalization, **the generalization becomes an addition to the detail, and** consequently, all **other areas are included** as well. The additional verse **teaches us** that the *halakha* includes only those areas that are outside in three ways.

## Baraitot Disputes

### R. Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח שְׁלָמָיו״ – אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כּוֹף אׇזְנְךָ לִשְׁמוֹעַ – בִּמְחַשֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל מִזִּבְחוֹ בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאוֹכֵל מִזִּבְחוֹ לְיוֹם שְׁלִישִׁי? אָמַרְתָּ: אַחַר שֶׁהוּא כָּשֵׁר, יַחְזוֹר וְיִפָּסֵל?!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: **"And if any of the flesh of his peace offerings be at all eaten** on the third day, it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 7:18): **Rabbi Eliezer said: Bend your ear to hear** the true meaning of the verse: **The verse is speaking of one who intends to partake of his offering on the third day. Or** perhaps it is referring **only to** one **who** actually **partakes of his offering on the third day,** per the straightforward meaning of the verse? But that cannot be; would **you say** that **after** the offering **is fit** for the first two days, **it should then be disqualified** retroactively by being eaten on the third day?

With the derivations from the *pesukim* now firmly established, the *gemara* turns to a *baraita* that examines the same *pasuk* in *Tzav* from a different angle. *Tanu rabbanan*: our Rabbis taught. Rabbi Eliezer opens: *kof oznecha lishmoa* — bend your ear and listen carefully, for this *pasuk* is being read against its plain sense. *Bemachshev le'echol mizivcho bayom hashlishi hakatuv medaber* — the *pasuk* is speaking of one who intends to eat from the sacrifice on the third day. He is not speaking of the case where someone actually ate the *notar*. The alternative — that the *pasuk* describes someone who literally consumed the meat on the third day, and that the *korban* is thereby retroactively invalidated — is untenable. *Achar shehuchshar yachzor viyepasel?* Can something that was already accepted retroactively become invalid? No: *lo yeratzeh* tells us the *korban* will not be accepted, and this cannot logically refer to a retroactive undoing of an offering that was already valid at the time of the blood service. The *pasuk* must therefore be read as concerning the *machshava* at the time of *hakrava*.

### R. Akiva Tzad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הֵן! מָצִינוּ בְּזָב וְזָבָה וְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם – שֶׁהֵן בְּחֶזְקַת טׇהֳרָה, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁרָאוּ סָתְרוּ; אַף אַתָּה אַל תִּתְמַהּ עַל זֶה – שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּכְשַׁר, שֶׁיַּחְזוֹר וְיִפָּסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: Yes,** it could be disqualified retroactively. After all, **we find with regard to a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*], or a woman who experiences a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period [*zava*], or a woman who observes** a clean **day for a day** when she experiences a discharge, **that they** all **have the presumptive status of purity** after immersing. **But once they have seen** another discharge the same day, **they have negated** this status retroactively, and everything they had touched after immersing becomes impure. Therefore, **you as well should not be surprised about this** offering consumed beyond its designated time, **that even though it was rendered fit, it may then be disqualified** retroactively.

Rabbi Akiva pushes back: *hen* — yes, retroactive invalidation is not impossible. We find it in the case of a *zav* and a *zavah* and a *shomeret yom kineged yom*. A *zav* who has experienced three emissions and a *zavah* who has seen blood on three consecutive days outside her menstrual cycle must each count seven clean days before immersing. By the principle of *miktzat hayom k'kulo*, part of the seventh day counts as the whole, and they may immerse in the *mikvah* on the morning of that day. But if later on the seventh day a further emission or bleeding occurs, their entire count is retroactively annulled. Similarly, a *shomeret yom kineged yom* — a woman who saw blood one or two days outside her regular cycle and must observe one clean day — may immerse in the *mikvah* once the clean day has begun, but if she subsequently sees blood, her *mikvah* immersion is retroactively ruined and the count must begin again. Rabbi Akiva argues: we thus do have a recognized category of retroactive invalidation. *She'af al pi shehuchshar yachzor viyepasel* — even if something became *kasher*, it is possible for it to subsequently become *pasul* retroactively.

### R. Eliezer Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״הַמַּקְרִיב״ – בִּשְׁעַת הַקְרָבָה הוּא נִפְסָל, וְאֵינוֹ נִפְסָל בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר ״הַמַּקְרִיב״ אֶלָּא זֶה כֹּהֵן הַמַּקְרִיב? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֹתוֹ״ – בַּזֶּבַח הוּא מְדַבֵּר, וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר בַּכֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: But** the same verse **says: "He who offers** it," indicating that the offering **is disqualified at the time of** its **offering, and it is not disqualified on the third** day. **Or** perhaps when the verse **says: "He who offers** it," **this** is referring **only** to **the priest who sacrifices** the offering, indicating that he is disqualified. But if so, it would have been enough for the verse to state: He who offers shall not be credited. Therefore, **when it states** instead: "Neither shall it be credited to he who offers **it,"** this indicates that the verse **is speaking of the offering, and it is not speaking of the priest.**

Rabbi Eliezer replies that the cases of *zav*, *zavah*, and *shomeret yom kineged yom* are based on the specific principle of *miktzat hayom k'kulo* — we permit the person to immerse in the morning on the anticipation that the remainder of the day will proceed as it began. That is categorically different from the situation of a *korban* which is accepted at the time of the blood service, and then, two days later, someone eats from the *notar* and allegedly undoes the original *hakrava*. The two cases are simply not comparable. Rather, Rabbi Eliezer turns to the *pasuk* itself: *hamakriv oto* — the one who offers it — focuses us on the moment of *hakrava*. *B'sha'at hakravah hu nifsal v'eino nifsal bashlishi*: the invalidation occurs at the time of offering, not on the third day. This is derived not from logic alone but from the textual emphasis of *hamakriv* — pointing to the act of offering as the locus of invalidation.

### Ben Azzai Oto

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: ״אֹתוֹ״ מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תְאַחֵר לְשַׁלְּמוֹ״ – יָכוֹל אַף מְאַחֵר נִדְרוֹ בְּלֹא יֵרָצֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֹתוֹ״ – אוֹתוֹ בְּלֹא יֵרָצֶה, וְאֵין הַמְאַחֵר נִדְרוֹ בְּלֹא יֵרָצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Azzai says: Why** must **the verse state: "It"?** It is necessary **because** elsewhere **it is stated:** "When you shall vow a vow to the Lord your God, **you shall not delay paying it;** for the Lord your God will require it from you, and it will be sin in you" (Deuteronomy 23:22). One **might** have thought that **even** one who **is late** in fulfilling **his vow** is included **in: "It shall not be accepted"** (Leviticus 7:18). Therefore, **the verse states: "It,"** to indicate that **it,** an offering rendered *piggul*, **is** included **in** the *halakha* of: **"It shall not be accepted," but** the animal of **one who is late** in fulfilling **his vow is not** included **in** the *halakha* of: **"It shall not be accepted."**

Ben Azzai then introduces an entirely different reading of *oto*. *Oto mah talmud lomar?* What does the word *oto* — it, this — come to teach? Not the point that it is the *korban* and not the *kohen* that becomes invalid. Rather, Ben Azzai reads *oto* as limiting the scope of *lo yeratzeh*: only this *korban*, the one with a *piggul* thought, is subject to *lo yeratzeh*. *Oto belo yeratzeh ve'ein me'acher nidro belo yeratzeh* — it is this case that incurs *lo yeratzeh*, but one who is delinquent in fulfilling his vow through *bal te'acher* does not thereby render his *korban* invalid. Perhaps one might have thought that violating *bal te'acher* — the prohibition against delaying the fulfillment of a vow beyond the prescribed time — causes the *korban* itself to become unacceptable. *Oto* teaches otherwise.

### Acherim View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: ״לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב״ – בְּמַחְשָׁבָה הוּא נִפְסָל, וְאֵינוֹ נִפְסָל בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** ***Aḥerim* say:** One can conclude that the verse (Leviticus 7:18) is referring to improper intention rather than improper consumption from the phrase **"neither shall it be credited [*yeḥashev*],"** which indicates that the offering **is disqualified by** improper **intention [*maḥashava*], and it is not disqualified** by consumption **on the third** day.

Acherim — others — take a different approach: *lo yechashev bemachshavah hu nifsal v'eino nifsal bashlishi*. Rather than deriving the primacy of *machshava* from *hamakriv*, Acherim ground it in the phrase *lo yechashev* — it shall not be reckoned. This is in truth the most natural textual anchor for the entire principle, since the very language of *lo yechashev* centers on thought and reckoning. The *korban* is invalidated through *machshava*, not through what occurs on the third day.

### Korban Not Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן עַזַּאי – דִּבְזֶבַח הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ בְּכֹהֵן, מְנָא לֵיהּ? אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדַּאֲחֵרִים, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִדִּכְתִיב ״לֹא יֵרָצוּ״ וְ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״ – זִיבְחָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And ben Azzai,** who interprets the term "it" as excluding vows that were fulfilled late, **from where does he** derive **that the verse is speaking of** the **offering and not of** the **priest?** The Gemara responds: **If you wish, say** that **he derives it from** the statement **of *Aḥerim*** that the verse rules out disqualification on the third day itself. This would be necessary only if the verse is referring to the offering and not the priest. **Or, if you wish, say** instead: **From** the fact **that it is written: "They shall not be accepted"** (Leviticus 22:25), **and: "It shall not be accepted"** (Leviticus 7:18), it is evident that the verse **is** referring to **an offering,** which may be accepted or may not be accepted.

The *gemara* then asks: Ben Azzai uses *oto* to exclude *bal te'acher* — but where does he learn that it is the *korban* that becomes invalid and not the *kohen* himself? Two answers are offered. First, from Acherim: the phrase *lo yechashev* tells us it is invalidated through *machshava* and not on the third day, which only makes sense if we are speaking of the *korban*, not the *kohen*. There is no imaginable scenario in which a *kohen* becomes personally invalid on the third day due to *notar*. Second, from the phrase *lo yeratzeh* itself: *ritzui* and *hartzaah* are terms that throughout the *korbanot* literature refer to the sacrifice being accepted, not to the standing of the *kohen*. Accordingly, Ben Azzai does not need *oto* to establish that it is the *korban* and not the *kohen* — *lo yeratzeh* already makes this plain — and *oto* is therefore free to teach the *halacha* of *bal te'acher*.

### Ben Azzai Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן עַזַּאי – אוֹתוֹ בְּ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״ וְאֵין מְאַחֵר נִדְרוֹ בְּ״לֹא יֵרָצֶה״, מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא?! מִדַּאֲחֵרִים נָפְקָא! דְּתַנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: יָכוֹל יְהֵא בְּכוֹר שֶׁעִיבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ – כִּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, וְיִפָּסֵל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to the statement of **ben Azzai** that one derives from the word "it" that **it,** i.e., *piggul*, is included **in** the *halakha* of: **"It shall not be accepted," but** the animal of one who **is late** in fulfilling **his vow is not** included **in** the *halakha* of: **"It shall not be accepted,"** is this *halakha* **derived from here? It is derived from** the statement **of *Aḥerim*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: ***Aḥerim* say:** One **might** have thought that **a firstborn** animal **whose** first **year passed,** during which time it ought to have been sacrificed, **should be** **like disqualified consecrated** animals, **and it should be disqualified** from being sacrificed, as though it had developed a blemish.

The *gemara* presses further: does Ben Azzai actually derive the *bal te'acher* exemption from *oto* in our *pasuk*? Perhaps he derives it from elsewhere — from a different *pasuk* altogether. *Me'acharina nafka* — he learns it from another source. A *baraita* is cited: Acherim say that one might have thought a *bechor* that passed its first year — like other *kodashim* that are invalid if brought at the wrong age — is similarly rendered *pasul*. The *Torah* teaches otherwise by linking *bechor* to *ma'aser* in the *pasuk* of *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha ma'asar deganecha* etc. *Makish bechor lema'aser*: just as *ma'aser* is not invalidated by being consumed in a different year — since the whole point is that every three years one clears out the *ma'aser* — so too a *bechor* is not invalidated by the passage of years. *Af bechor eino nifsal mishanah lachaverta*. We thus have a source for the *bechor* case from the *hekesh*. Why then does Ben Azzai need *oto* from our *pasuk*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ וְגוֹ׳״ – מַקִּישׁ בְּכוֹר לְמַעֲשֵׂר; מָה מַעֲשֵׂר – אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, אַף בְּכוֹר – אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל מִשָּׁנָה לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God,** in the place which He shall choose to place His name there, **the tithe of your grain,** of your wine, and of your oil, and the firstborns of your herds, and of your flocks" (Deuteronomy 14:23). The verse **juxtaposes a firstborn** animal **to** the **tithe** of grain to indicate that **just as** the **tithe is not disqualified** by being kept over **from** one **year to the next** (see Deuteronomy 14:28), **so too a firstborn** animal **is not disqualified** by being kept over **from** one **year to the next.** If so, one already derives from this verse that an offering is not disqualified even if it is brought late.

### Rav Ashi Why Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּכוֹר, דְּלָאו בַּר הַרְצָאָה הוּא; אֲבָל קָדָשִׁים, דִּבְנֵי הַרְצָאָה נִינְהוּ – אֵימָא לָא לִירַצּוֹ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Nevertheless, the verse cited by ben Azzai **was necessary,** as without it, it might **enter your mind to say** that **this matter** applies only to **a firstborn, which is not for acceptance,** i.e., it does not serve to atone for any sin and is merely a gift for the priest, **but** with regard to other **sacrificial** animals **that are for acceptance, say** that **they shall not effect acceptance** when brought late. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that even these others are not disqualified when brought late.

Rav Ashi explains: *itztarech* — both sources are needed. Without *oto*, one might have said: granted, a *bechor* is not invalidated by the passage of time — *d'lav bar hartza'ah hu* — because a *bechor* does not come to atone or to establish a relationship of *hartza'ah* between Israel and God. It is a recognition of divine ownership of the firstborn, not a volitional act of drawing close to God. *Aval kodashim d'vnei hartza'ah ninhu* — but other *kodashim*, which genuinely function as acts of *hartza'ah*, acts of seeking divine acceptance — an *olah* that atones for the violation of a positive commandment, *shelamim* that cultivate the bond between Israel and their Father in heaven — one might have thought that if the person violated *bal te'acher* with respect to these, the *korban* itself loses its validity. *Ka mashma lan* — therefore *oto* is needed to teach that even these *kodashim*, when offered after *bal te'acher*, remain *kasher*.

### Kodashim Hartza'ah

The broader principle now emerges clearly: Ben Azzai requires *oto* specifically because the *bechor* precedent, derived from the *hekesh* to *ma'aser*, would not have been sufficient to establish the rule for standard *kodashim* that are *bnei hartza'ah*. The conceptual distinction between a *bechor* — which lacks the quality of *hartza'ah* — and voluntarily offered *korbanot* is significant enough that without *oto*, we would not have been able to generalize from one to the other. *Oto* bridges that gap.

### Why Oto Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאַכַּתִּי מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא?! ״וְהָיָה בְךָ חֵטְא״ – וְלֹא בְּקׇרְבָּנְךָ חֵטְא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **But still,** isn't this *halakha* **derived from** a *baraita* **elsewhere:** The verse states: "When you shall vow a vow to the Lord your God, you shall not delay paying it; for the Lord your God will require it from you; **and it will be sin in you"** (Deuteronomy 23:22), indicating that there is sin in you, **but** there is **no sin in your offering,** and therefore it is not disqualified due to the delay?

A further question presents itself: why does Ben Azzai need *oto* at all, given that the *pasuk* of *bal te'acher* itself states *lo te'acher leshalmo*, and the sequel reads *ve'haya becha cheit* — the sin shall be *in you*? The *drasha* seems obvious: *becha cheit v'lo b'korbanacha cheit* — the sin is in you, not in your *korban*; the *korban* is not invalidated. If so, *oto* is superfluous for this purpose.

### Wife Death Neder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן עַזַּאי – הָהוּא מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: ״וְהָיָה בְךָ חֵטְא״ – וְלֹא בְּאִשְׁתְּךָ חֵטְא. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מֵתָה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מְבַקְּשִׁין מִמֶּנּוּ מָמוֹן וְאֵין לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם אֵין לְךָ לְשַׁלֵּם לָמָּה יִקַּח מִשְׁכָּבְךָ מִתַּחְתֶּיךָ״ – בְּהַאי עָוֹן דְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״ נָמֵי מֵתָה; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Ben Azzai requires that** verse to teach a different *halakha*: The phrase **"and it will be sin in you"** indicates that there will be sin in you, **but** there will **not** be **sin in your wife.** It might **enter your mind to say: Since Rabbi Elazar said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan** who said: **A man's wife does not die unless** his creditors **ask him for money** that he owes **and he does not have** it, **as it is stated: "If you do not have with what to pay, why should he take away your bed from under you?"** (Proverbs 22:27); therefore, one's wife **also dies for this transgression of** the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** since one has failed to fulfill his commitment. Consequently, the verse **teaches us** that the sin is imputed to him alone.

The *gemara* explains that Ben Azzai requires *ve'haya becha cheit* for an entirely different *derasha*: *v'lo b'ishtecha cheit* — the sin falls upon you, not upon your wife. The point of departure is a teaching cited in the name of Rabbi Eliezer (and some say Rabbi Yochanan): *ein ishto shel adam meta ela im kein mevakshim mimeno mamon v'ein lo* — a man's wife dies only when Heaven demands payment from him and he has nothing with which to pay. Tosafot understands this specifically to refer to the case of *bal te'acher* — when a man has made a vow to bring a *korban* to the *Beit Hamikdash* and has delayed beyond the prescribed time. One might have thought that in such a case, the principle of collective familial consequence would apply, and his wife might suffer as a result. Ben Azzai therefore derives from *ve'haya becha cheit* that by *bal te'acher* specifically, the sin rests with him alone: *bechetecha v'lo be'ishtecha chetecha*. The *gemara* does not deny the general principle of familial consequence — it is referenced with respect to cases of *gezel* or other unpaid obligations — but it limits it so that *bal te'acher* is not among the causes that trigger such consequences.

### Malkaot Taught

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּמַחְשָׁבָה הוּא נִפְסָל, וְאֵינוֹ נִפְסָל בִּשְׁלִישִׁי. וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר – ״לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* stated that ***Aḥerim* say:** The phrase "neither shall it be credited" indicates that the offering **is disqualified by** improper **intention, and it is not disqualified** by consumption beyond its designated time **on the third** day. The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Eliezer,** who derives this *halakha* from a different source, **what does he do with** the phrase **"neither shall it be credited"?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יַנַּאי – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: מִנַּיִן לְמַחְשָׁבוֹת שֶׁמּוֹצִיאוֹת זוֹ מִזּוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב״ – לֹא יְעָרֵב בּוֹ מַחְשָׁבוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **He requires it for** that **which Rabbi Yannai** says, **as Rabbi Yannai says: From where** is it derived **with regard to intentions that they displace one another,** i.e., that an additional improper intention prevents the offering from being rendered *piggul*? **As it is stated: "Neither shall it be credited [*yeḥashev*],"** indicating that an offering is rendered piggul only if the priest does **not mix other** improper **intentions [*maḥashavot*] with** intent of *piggul*.

The *gemara* then turns to an additional teaching cited by Rav Mari in the name of Rabbi Yannai. One reading of *lo yechashev* goes beyond its use in limiting the role of competing thoughts: *minayin lemachshavot bekodashim shehu lokeh* — how do we know that one who entertains a *piggul* thought receives *malkaot*, lashes? *Talmud lomar lo yechashev* — the *pasuk* teaches that one may not have this thought; it is a negative prohibition, and the violator receives *malkaot*.

### Lav Lo Ma'aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב מָרִי מַתְנֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: מִנַּיִן לִמְחַשֵּׁב בְּקָדָשִׁים שֶׁהוּא לוֹקֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יֵחָשֵׁב״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Mari teaches** that **Rabbi Yannai says: From where** is it derived **that one who has** improper **intentions with regard to sacrificial** animals **is flogged** like one who transgressed a prohibition? **The verse states: "Neither shall it be credited [*lo yeḥashev*],"** i.e., one reads the verse as if it states: One shall not have improper intention [*lo y'ḥashev*].

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב מָרִי: לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא, וְכׇל לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה – אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה – לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rav Mari:** But **it is a prohibition that does not involve an action, and** the principle is that **for** transgressing **any prohibition that does not involve an action one is not flogged.** Rav Mari **said to him: It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: For** transgressing **a prohibition that does not involve an action one is flogged.**

Rav Ashi challenges Rav Mari: *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh hu* — this is a negative prohibition without a physical act. And the general rule is *kol lav she'ein bo ma'aseh ein lokin alav* — one does not receive *malkaot* for a prohibition that involves no physical deed. A *piggul* thought, being purely mental (or at most verbal), does not constitute a *ma'aseh*, and therefore *malkaot* should not apply.

### R. Yehudah Malkaot

Rav Mari responds: *Rabi Yehuda hi* — this follows the position of Rabbi Yehudah, who holds that *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh lokin alav*, that even a prohibition with no accompanying physical act can incur *malkaot*. This is a known position of Rabbi Yehudah applied here to the case of the *piggul* thought.

## Mishnah Four Avodot

### Four Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹחֵט, וְהַמְקַבֵּל, וְהַמְהַלֵּךְ, וְהַזּוֹרֵק; לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל, וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר; כְּזַיִת; חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת; וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **This is the principle:** With regard to **anyone who slaughters** the animal, **or who collects** the blood, **or who conveys** the blood, **or who sprinkles** the blood, with intent **to partake of an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **partakes** of it, e.g., the meat of the offering, **or to burn an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **burns** it on the altar, if his intent was to partake of or to burn the measure of **an olive-bulk outside its** designated **area,** the offering is **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.** If his intent was to do so **beyond its** designated **time,** the offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it, provided that the permitting factor,** the blood, **is sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva.**

The *mishnah* now presents a comprehensive *klal* — a general rule — governing the laws of *piggul*. There are four acts of the blood service during which a *piggul* thought is operative: *shochet*, *makabel*, *mehalech*, *zorek* — slaughtering, receiving the blood, walking it to the *mizbeach*, and throwing it. A *piggul* thought formed during any one of these four acts can render the *korban piggul*.

### Kavana Requirements

Several conditions govern the efficacy of the *piggul* thought. The thought must concern *davar shedarcho le'echol* — something normally eaten, meaning the *basar* of the *korban* — or *davar shedarcho lehaktir* — something normally burned, meaning the *emurim*. A thought to eat the *emurim* or to burn the *basar* does not qualify. Furthermore, the thought must concern at least a *kezayit* — the minimum halachic measure of eating — since anything less is too insignificant to trigger *piggul*.

### Chutz Limkomo

If the improper thought is *chutz limkomo* — intending to eat or offer the *korban* in the wrong place — the *korban* becomes *pasul* and there is no *karet* for one who subsequently eats from it. *Pasul ve'ein bo karet*.

### Chutz Lizmano

If the improper thought is *chutz lizmano* — intending to eat or offer the *korban* at the wrong time — the *korban* becomes *piggul*, and one who eats from it is liable for *karet*. *Piggul vechayavin alav karet*.

### Matir Kemitzvato

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד קָרַב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ? שָׁחַט בִּשְׁתִיקָה, קִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק בִּשְׁתִיקָה; אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – זֶהוּ שֶׁמַּקְרִיב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **How** is **the permitting factor sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva?** If one **slaughtered** the animal **in silence** with no specific intent, and **he collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood with intent to burn or partake of the offering **beyond its** designated **time; or** if one **slaughtered** it with intent to burn or partake of the offering **beyond its** designated **time, and he collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood **in silence; or** if one **slaughtered** the animal **and collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood with intent to burn or partake of the offering **beyond its** designated **time, that is** the case **where** one **sacrifices the permitting factor in accordance with its mitzva.**

A critical condition applies to both: *uvilvad sheyakriv hamatir kemitzvato* — provided that the *matir*, that which permits the *korban*, is brought in the proper manner. The *matir* refers to the act that makes the *korban* and its parts permissible — the blood service, or in the case of a *mincha* the *kometz* and the *levona*. *Kemitzvato* is the key word: not only must the physical acts be completed, but they must be performed without any additional invalidating thought.

### Kemitzvato Example

How is *kemitzvato* illustrated? If the *shechita* was performed silently and without any improper thought, and then the *kibbul*, the *halacha*, and the *zrikah* were all performed with a *chutz lizmano* thought, that is a case of *hikriv hamatir kemitzvato* — the *matir* was brought properly. Even if three of the four acts were performed with the *piggul* thought, as long as everything was otherwise valid and no other invalidating thought intruded, the *korban* is *piggul*. The presence of additional *chutz lizmano* thoughts in subsequent acts does not take it out of *piggul*.

### Lo Karav Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד לֹא קָרַב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ? שָׁחַט חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, קִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, קִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ (וְלִזְמַנּוֹ).
>
> **Translation:** **How** is **the permitting factor not sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva?** If one **slaughtered** the animal with intent to burn it or partake of it **outside its** designated **area,** and **he collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood with intent to burn it or partake of it **beyond its** designated **time; or** if one **slaughtered** the animal with intent to burn it or partake of it **beyond its** designated **time,** and **collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood with intent to burn it or partake of it **outside its** designated **area; or** if one **slaughtered** the animal **and collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood with intent to burn it or partake of it **outside its** designated **area,** these are cases in which the permitting factor is not sacrificed in accordance with the mitzva.

What are examples of *lo karav hamatir kemitzvato* — cases where the *matir* was not brought properly? If the *shechita* was performed with a *chutz limkomo* thought and then the remaining acts were performed with a *chutz lizmano* thought: since the first act introduced a different type of invalidating thought, the subsequent *piggul* thought is ineffective. Alternatively, even if the *shechita* was performed with a *chutz lizmano* thought — establishing a *piggul* intent from the very first act — if a later act, such as the *zrikah*, is performed with a *chutz limkomo* thought, the *piggul* is annulled. A *piggul* requires that all the *matirim* be brought without any invalidating thought other than the *piggul* thought itself. The same applies to *korbanot* for which *shelo lishmo* is disqualifying: a *pesach* or *chatat* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* renders the *korban pasul*, and if *shelo lishmo* appears in any subsequent act alongside or following the *piggul* thought, the *lo karav kemitzvato* condition is not met.

### Half Kezayit Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפֶּסַח וְהַחַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, קִיבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן; אוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט וְקִבֵּל וְהִלֵּךְ וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – זֶהוּ שֶׁלֹּא קָרַב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, in the case of **the Paschal offering and the sin offering that one slaughtered not for their sake, and** one **collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood with intent to burn it or partake of it **beyond its** designated **time; or** if one **slaughtered** the Paschal offering or sin offering with intent to burn it or partake of it **beyond its** designated **time,** and **he collected and conveyed and sprinkled** the blood **not for their sake; or that one slaughtered** the Paschal offering or sin offering **and collected and conveyed and sprinkled their blood not for their sake, that is** the case of an offering **whose permitting factor is not sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת בַּחוּץ כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר; כְּזַיִת לְמָחָר כְּזַיִת בַּחוּץ; כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בַּחוּץ כַּחֲצִי זַיִת לְמָחָר; כַּחֲצִי זַיִת לְמָחָר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בַּחוּץ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** If one performed one of the sacrificial rites with the intent **to eat an olive-bulk outside** its designated area and **an olive-bulk the next day,** or **an olive-bulk the next day** and **an olive-bulk outside** its designated area, or **half an olive-bulk outside** its designated area and **half an olive-bulk the next day,** or **half an olive-bulk the next day** and **half an olive-bulk outside** its designated area, the offering is **disqualified and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.**

If one had a thought to eat a half *kezayit* in the wrong place (*chutz limkomo*) and a half *kezayit* at the wrong time (*chutz lizmano*), the *mishnah* teaches: *pasul ve'ein bo karet* — invalid but no *karet*. The *halacha* here combines two principles already derived. First, from *yihyeh*, we learned that *mitztarfin zeh im zeh*: thoughts of *chutz limkomo* and *chutz lizmano* combine to constitute a *kezayit* of improper intent. Second, since a full *kezayit* of *piggul* thought was never formed — and since the *chutz limkomo* thought is present — the *korban* cannot be *piggul*. The combination is sufficient for *psul* but not for *piggul* and *karet*.

### R. Yehudah First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁמַּחְשֶׁבֶת הַזְּמַן קָדְמָה לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הַמָּקוֹם – פִּגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת. וְאִם מַחְשֶׁבֶת הַמָּקוֹם קָדְמָה לְמַחְשֶׁבֶת הַזְּמַן – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וָזֶה – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. לֶאֱכוֹל כַּחֲצִי זַיִת לְהַקְטִיר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת – כָּשֵׁר; שֶׁאֵין אֲכִילָה וְהַקְטָרָה מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **this is the principle:** In **any** case **where the intent** with regard to **the time preceded the intent** with regard to **the area,** the offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it. And if the intent** with regard to **the area preceded the intent** with regard to **the time,** the offering is **disqualified and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it. And the Rabbis say:** In both **this** case **and that** case, the offering is **disqualified and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.** If his intent was **to eat half an olive-bulk** and **to burn half an olive-bulk** not at the appropriate time or in the appropriate area, the offering is **fit because eating and burning do not join together.**

Rabbi Yehudah disagrees. His principle: *kol she'machshavat zman kadma l'machshavat hamakom piggul vechayavin alav karet* — whenever the thought of time (*chutz lizmano*) precedes the thought of place (*chutz limkomo*), the *korban* is *piggul* and one who eats it is liable for *karet*. Rabbi Yehudah holds that once a *piggul* thought has been formed, a subsequent thought of *chutz limkomo* — whether during the same act or a later one — cannot undo what has already been established. Once the *korban* has been made *piggul*, it cannot be un-*piggul*'d.

### R. Yehudah Order

The order matters to Rabbi Yehudah in both directions. *Im machshavat hamakom kadma l'machshavat hazman* — if the thought of place preceded the thought of time, the *korban* was already rendered invalid before the *piggul* thought arrived, and it cannot subsequently become *piggul*. *Pasul ve'ein bo karet*. The asymmetry in Rabbi Yehudah's position is clear: *chutz lizmano* first yields *piggul* and *karet*, which are not dislodged by a later *chutz limkomo* thought; *chutz limkomo* first yields *psul* without *karet*, which cannot be converted into *piggul* by a later *chutz lizmano* thought.

### Chachamim View

The *chachamim* reject this asymmetry entirely: *zeh v'zeh pasul ve'ein bo karet* — in either order, the result is *psul* without *karet*. Whenever thoughts of *chutz lizmano* and *chutz limkomo* are intermixed, regardless of which comes first, there is no *piggul* and no *karet*.

### No Achila Haktara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר אִילְפָא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת; אֲבָל בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל עֵירוּב מַחְשָׁבוֹת הָוֵי. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the disagreement between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis concerning a case where one has intent with regard to both time and area, **Ilfa says:** There is **a disagreement** only **with regard to two** sacrificial **rites,** i.e., where each intent was held during a different rite, **but** if both were held **in** the course of **one rite, all agree** that **it constitutes a combination of intentions,** and the offering is not rendered *piggul*. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** if the intentions were both held **in** the course of **one rite,** there is still **a disagreement.**

A final distinction closes the *mishnah*: *le'echol kechatzi zayit lehaktir kechatzi zayit* — if one had a thought to eat half a *kezayit* of *basar* at the wrong time and to burn half a *kezayit* of *emurim* at the wrong time. Even though both thoughts are *chutz lizmano* thoughts, one pertaining to *achilat adam* and one to *achilat mizbeach*, they do not combine. *Klal she'ein achila vehaktara mitztarvin* — eating and burning are categorically distinct modes of consumption and do not join to form a *kezayit*. For the thoughts to combine and create *psul*, they must be of the same type. A half *kezayit* of human eating and a half *kezayit* of altar burning, even when both are directed to the wrong time, remain separate and do not constitute a *kezayit* of a unified *piggul* intent.
