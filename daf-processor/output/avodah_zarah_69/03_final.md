# Avodah Zarah 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Supervision & Trust

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נׇכְרִי שֶׁהָיָה מַעֲבִיר עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל כַּדֵּי יַיִן מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, אִם הָיָה בְּחֶזְקַת הַמִּשְׁתַּמֵּר — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a gentile who was transporting barrels of wine from** one **place to** another **place** together **with a Jew, if** the wine **was under the presumption of being supervised,** it is **permitted.**

Daf 69 continues the tractate's broader sweep through various areas of *kashrus* and halachic concern. Having dealt with questions of earning wages from non-Jews and the laws of forbidden mixtures, the Gemara now shifts to a distinct but related area: the question of whether one can trust that wine left in the presence or custody of a non-Jew has not been touched or tampered with. The animating concern is not merely theft or adulteration but the possibility that the non-Jew, finding himself unobserved, will either pour the wine as a libation — *yayin nesech* — or handle it in a way that renders it forbidden.

### Topic Shift

The governing principle that structures this entire discussion is *chezkas ha-mishtamer* — the presumption that the wine is being guarded — and the conditions under which that presumption holds or dissolves. The Mishna presents a series of cases that progressively test the edges of this principle, and the Gemara then probes each one for its logical underpinnings.

## Transport & Presumption

### Transport Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הוֹדִיעוֹ שֶׁהוּא מַפְלִיג כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּשְׁתּוֹם וְיִסְתּוֹם וְיִגּוֹב. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפְתַּח אֶת הֶחָבִית וְיִגּוֹף וְתִיגּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** But **if** the Jew **notified him that he was going far away,** the wine is forbidden if the Jew left for **a sufficient** amount of time **for** the gentile **to bore a hole [*sheyishtom*]** in the barrel, **seal** it again with plaster, **and** for the plaster to **dry. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** The wine is forbidden only if it was **sufficient** time **for** the gentile **to open the barrel** by removing the stopper altogether, **stop** it again by making a new stopper, **and** for the new stopper to **dry.**

The Mishna opens with the following scenario: a non-Jew was transporting barrels of wine belonging to a Jew from one location to another — *oved kokhavim she-hayah ma'avir im Yisrael kadei yayin mimakom lemakom*. The question is whether the Jew can trust that the wine has not been touched or replaced.

### Chezkas Mishtamer

The Mishna rules: *im hayah be-chezkas ha-mishtamer* — if the wine is in a state of *chezkas ha-mishtamer*, it is permitted. The simple reading of this phrase is that the Jew has not informed the non-Jew of any extended absence; the non-Jew therefore cannot be confident that the Jew will not appear at any moment. The contrast case is *im hodio she-hu maflig* — if the Jew explicitly told the non-Jew that he would be away for a period of time, giving the non-Jew a window in which he knows he will not be interrupted. In that case, the wine may become problematic depending on how long that window is.

The phrase *chezkas ha-mishtamer* thus captures something more than mere physical presence. It describes a state in which the possibility of surprise inspection is alive enough in the non-Jew's mind that he will not risk acting improperly. This will be clarified further by the Gemara's analysis of the *beraita* on workers.

### Time Thresholds

When the Jew has informed the non-Jew of his departure, what period of time triggers concern? The *Tanna Kamma* says: *kedei she-yistom ve-yistom* — enough time to pierce a hole in the sealed top of the barrel, siphon off some wine (or substitute inferior wine), and then replaster and reseal the hole, waiting for the plaster to dry. The concern is that a hole of this kind might not be detectable once properly filled. If the non-Jew has been given enough uninterrupted time to accomplish this, the wine is forbidden.

### Tanna/Rabban Debate

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees. He is not concerned about hole-piercing at all — as the Gemara will later explain, such tampering is always detectable. The only tampering he fears is something more drastic: *kedei she-yiftach es ha-chavis ve-yigov ve-yisnagov* — enough time to completely remove the barrel's cover, draw off wine, fashion a replacement cover, reseal it entirely, and allow the new seal to dry. This requires considerably more time than making a small hole, and it is only this lengthier process that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* treats as grounds for concern.

## Wagon & Ship

### Wagon/Ship Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּנִּיחַ יֵינוֹ בְּקָרוֹן אוֹ בִּסְפִינָה, וְהָלַךְ לוֹ בְּקָפֶנְדַּרְיָא, נִכְנַס לִמְדִינָה וְרָחַץ — מוּתָּר. אִם הוֹדִיעוֹ שֶׁהוּא מַפְלִיג — כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּשְׁתּוֹם, וְיִסְתּוֹם, וְיִגּוֹב. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפְתַּח אֶת הֶחָבִית, וְיִגּוֹף, וְתִיגּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who placed his wine in a wagon or on a ship** with a gentile, **and went on his way by a shortcut [*bekappendarya*],** such that the gentile does not know when the Jew will encounter him, even if the Jew **entered the city and bathed,** the wine is **permitted,** because the gentile would not use the wine for a libation, for fear the owner might catch him at it. **If** the Jew **informed** the gentile **that he was going away** for a long period of time, the wine is forbidden if it was **sufficient** time **for** the gentile **to bore a hole** in the barrel, **seal** it again with plaster, **and** for the plaster to **dry. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** It is forbidden only if it was **sufficient** time **for him to open the barrel** by removing the stopper altogether, **stop** it again, **and** for the new stopper to **dry.**

The Mishna then presents a second scenario: *ha-meniach yeino be-karon o be-sefinah* — a Jew who left his wine in a wagon or on a ship and then departed. Specifically, he took a shortcut — *ve-halach lo be-kapandarya* — and entered a town, perhaps to bathe. He was away from the wagon or ship for some period, having left the non-Jew alone with the wine.

### Absence Indication

The Mishna rules this *mutar* so long as the Jew did not declare how long he would be away. Even though he was visibly engaged in some activity at a distance, since he gave no explicit statement of duration, the non-Jew cannot rule out a sudden return. However, if he did say *im hodio she-hu maflig* — that he would be absent for a defined stretch — then the same time thresholds apply: according to the *Tanna Kamma*, enough time to pierce and reseal; according to *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, enough time to fully remove and replace the cover.

## Store Case

### Store Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּנִּיחַ נׇכְרִי בַּחֲנוּת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֹּצֵא וְנִכְנָס — מוּתָּר, וְאִם הוֹדִיעוֹ שֶׁהוּא מַפְלִיג — כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּשְׁתּוֹם וְיִסְתּוֹם וְיִגּוֹב. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפְתַּח אֶת הֶחָבִית וְיִגּוֹף וְתִיגּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who left a gentile in** his **shop, even if** the Jew **went out and came in** and was not there all the time, the wine is **permitted. But if** the Jew **informed** the gentile **that he was going away** for a long period of time, the wine is forbidden if it was **sufficient** time **for** the gentile **to bore a hole** in the barrel, **seal** it again with plaster, **and** for the plaster to **dry. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** The wine is forbidden only if it was **sufficient** time **for** him **to open the barrel, stop** it again, **and** for the new stopper to **dry.**

The third case: *ha-maniach oved kochavim be-chanuto* — a Jew who left a non-Jew unattended in his store. Whether this is a hired storekeeper or simply a customer left alone is not entirely clear from the text.

### Coming & Going

Here too, the Mishna rules *mutar* so long as the Jew did not specify the length of his absence, even if he was *yotzei ve-nichnas* — going in and out repeatedly. The phrase *yotzei ve-nichnas* is the classical formulation for a type of supervision that falls short of constant presence, yet is sufficient because the person being supervised never knows when the supervisor will next appear. It is worth noting that the Mishna's formulation is in fact *yotzei ve-nichnas* — one who is fundamentally present but occasionally steps out — rather than the inverse pattern, which might describe someone who merely drops in at intervals. The whole point of the leniency is that the non-Jew perceives the possibility of surprise arrival at any moment.

If, however, the Jew did announce his departure and its duration, the same thresholds apply: *kedei she-yistom ve-yistom ve-yigov* according to the *Tanna Kamma*, and *kedei she-yiftach es ha-chavis ve-yigafeiha ve-yachzor ve-tigof* according to *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*.

## Table & Vessels

### Table Drinking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה אוֹכֵל עִמּוֹ עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן, וְהִנִּיחַ לְגִינִין עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן וְלָגִין עַל הַדּוּלְבְּקִי, וְהִנִּיחוֹ וְיָצָא — מַה שֶּׁעַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן אָסוּר, שֶׁעַל הַדּוּלְבְּקִי מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** If a Jew **was eating with** a gentile **at the table, and left jugs [*laginin*]** of wine **on the table and a jug on the side table [*hadulebaki*], and he left it and went out, what is on the table** is **forbidden,** as it is likely that the gentile handled it, whereas **what is on the side table** is **permitted.**

A fourth scenario: *haya ochel imo al ha-shulchan* — the Jew was eating and drinking at the same table as a non-Jew, serving non-*mevushal* wine. It is remarkable that the Mishna describes this situation at all. The Jew was presumably pouring and the non-Jew was not being given license to handle the wine during the shared meal.

### Open vs. Sealed

The complication arises when the Jew steps away: *ve-hiniach laginim al ha-shulchan ve-lagin al ha-dulbeki ve-hiniach ve-yatza* — he left pitchers (*laginim*) on the table and also a pitcher on the *dulbeki*, a kind of wine stand or counter (translated by some as "cupboard"), and then departed. The Mishna rules: *ma she-al ha-shulchan assur* — whatever is on the table is immediately forbidden.

This is striking because in all the earlier cases, the wine was permissible as long as no specific departure time was announced. Here, the wine on the table is forbidden even without such an announcement and even without giving the non-Jew any extended period of unsupervised time. Two factors converge to produce this stringency: first, the non-Jew has been drinking at the table and already feels a degree of freedom with respect to the wine; second, as Tosafot notes, the pitchers on the table are already open, so no special effort — no piercing a seal or removing a cover — is required to touch the wine. Even the fear of a surprise return is insufficient to protect open wine in that kind of setting.

### Table vs. Cupboard

By contrast, *ma she-al ha-dulbeki mutar* — what is on the cupboard or counter is permitted. The non-Jew does not feel the same degree of freedom with vessels that are not right in front of him at the table.

### Permissive Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: הֱוֵי מוֹזֵג וְשׁוֹתֶה — אַף שֶׁעַל הַדּוּלְבְּקִי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** But if** the Jew **said to** the gentile: **Mix** water with the wine **and drink, even** the jug **that is on the side table** is **forbidden.**

However, if the Jew said to the non-Jew at some point during the meal — *hevei mazeg ve-shoteh* — "help yourself, pour and drink" — then even the vessels on the *dulbeki* become forbidden. Once such an encouraging and permissive statement has been made, the non-Jew feels entirely at liberty to take from wherever the wine is stored, and *af shel dulbeki assur* — even what is on the counter is forbidden.

### Sealed Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָבִיּוֹת פְּתוּחוֹת אֲסוּרוֹת, סְתוּמוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפְתַּח וְיִגּוֹף וְתִיגּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **open barrels are forbidden,** but **sealed** barrels **are permitted** unless the Jew was out of the room for **sufficient time for** the gentile **to open** the barrel by removing the stopper, **and stop** it again by making a new stopper, **and** for the new stopper to **dry.**

The exception concerns sealed barrels. Even after a permissive statement like "make yourself at home," *chaviyot stumot mutarot* — sealed barrels remain permitted, because the non-Jew does not feel sufficiently at ease with respect to vessels that require significant effort to open. The sealed barrels revert to the general rule: they only become problematic once enough uninterrupted time has passed — namely, *kedei she-yiftach ve-yigof ve-tigov*, the time to remove and replace the entire cover, in accordance with *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s position, whose ruling frames the end of the Mishna.

## Workers Beraita

### Beraita Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי דָמֵי ״בְּחֶזְקַת הַמִּשְׁתַּמֵּר״? כִּדְתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ חֲמָרָיו וּפוֹעֲלָיו טְעוּנִין טְהָרוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ הִפְלִיג מֵהֶן יוֹתֵר מִמִּיל — טׇהֳרוֹתָיו טְהוֹרוֹת, וְאִם אָמַר לָהֶן: ״לְכוּ וַאֲנִי בָּא אַחֲרֵיכֶם״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּתְעַלְּמָה עֵינוֹ מֵהֶם — טׇהֳרוֹתָיו טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What are the circumstances** described by the phrase: **Under the presumption of being supervised?** The Gemara explains: It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one's **donkey drivers and laborers were** unreliable with regard to ritual impurity [*amei ha'aretz*], and they were **laden with** wine or produce that was **ritually pure,** and he had instructed them not to tamper with it but he does not know whether or not they heeded him, **even** if **he went away from them** to a distance of **more than a *mil*, his pure items are** still **pure,** as it may be presumed that they heeded his instructions. **But if he said to them: Go and I will come after you,** so that they knew he would not be going with them, then **once they are out of his sight his pure items are impure.**

The Gemara now probes the precise meaning of *chezkas ha-mishtamer*. The Mishna contrasted it with *hodio she-hu maflig* — telling the non-Jew of a departure — but the phrase itself implies perhaps a more active form of oversight. To clarify, the Gemara cites a *beraita*: *harei she-hayu chamrav u-fo'alav te'unim taharot* — a case where one's donkey-drivers and workers were carrying produce in a state of *taharah* (purity), and the owner stepped away. The concern here is not *yayin nesech* but rather *tumah*: the workers are *amei ha-aretz* who may be careless about handling *tahor* items. If the owner left without announcing his absence, the produce remains *tahor*, even if he traveled more than a *mil* away — because the workers remain afraid he may reappear without warning.

### No Limit vs. Stated

The key distinction is between leaving without specifying an absence and saying *lechu va-ani ba achareichem* — "go on ahead, I'll catch up." Once he says that, the workers understand they have a guaranteed window of unsupervised time, and the moment they pass out of his sight — *keivan she-nitalma eino mehem* — the *taharot* are deemed *tamei*.

### Friend Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק: רֵישָׁא בִּמְטַהֵר חֲמָרָיו וּפוֹעֲלָיו לְכָךְ. אִי הָכִי, סֵיפָא נָמֵי? אֵין עַם הָאָרֶץ מַקְפִּיד עַל מַגַּע חֲבֵירוֹ. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ רֵישָׁא נָמֵי נֵימָא הָכִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in **the first clause,** where the produce is pure, **and what is different** in **the latter clause,** where it is impure? **Rav Yitzḥak said:** The ruling of **the first clause** is stated **with regard to** a case where **he purified his donkey drivers and laborers for this** assignment by having them immerse so they would not transfer impurity to the produce. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If that is so,** in **the latter clause** this would **also** apply. The Gemara answers: **An *am ha'aretz* is not punctilious about contact with another** person. Although they themselves were purified, they may have met another *am ha'aretz* on the way, and the produce would be rendered impure by him. The Gemara further objects: **If so,** then **let us say so even** in **the first clause;** the produce should be impure in that case as well.

The Gemara raises an internal difficulty, however. If this *beraita* is dealing with open barrels of wine (or open containers of *tahor* produce), the concern about *tumah* arises not only from the workers themselves — who have been made *tahor* beforehand — but from any passing friend of theirs who might touch the items. An *am ha-aretz* does not mind if his friend touches his produce: *ein am ha-aretz makpid al maga chavero*. So even in the first case, once the owner is out of sight, even if no explicit announcement was made, surely one must worry that a *tamei* friend will come by?

### Shortcut Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּבָא לָהֶם דֶּרֶךְ עֲקַלָּתוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is a case **where he came to them in a roundabout way,** such that they would not know from where he might appear, and they would be afraid to tamper with the goods because he might catch them in the act.

Rava resolves this: *de-ba lehu derech al-achlasson* — the workers remain fearful even of an apparently absent owner because he might take a shortcut and reappear suddenly. The owner's ability to cut across an alternate path and arrive unexpectedly is what keeps the workers on guard even when he has gone some distance. This is exactly what *chezkas ha-mishtamer* means in our Mishna as well: it is not merely the absence of an explicit announcement, but the active possibility — appreciated by the non-Jew — that the owner could appear at any moment by an unexpected route. Once the owner says "I'll catch up," that element of surprise is removed — *samcha da'ataihu* — they relax, knowing he is definitively behind them, and the protection of *chezkas ha-mishtamer* dissolves.

## Three Cases Need

### Case Redundancy Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּנִּיחַ נׇכְרִי בַּחֲנוּתוֹ כּוּ׳. הַמַּנִּיחַ יֵינוֹ בְּקָרוֹן אוֹ בִּסְפִינָה כּוּ׳. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי תְּנָא נׇכְרִי — דְּסָבַר: דִּלְמָא אָתֵי וְחָזֵי לֵיהּ, אֲבָל בְּקָרוֹן אוֹ בִּסְפִינָה — אֵימָא דְּמַפְלֵיג לַהּ לִסְפִינְתֵּיהּ וְעָבֵיד מַאי דְּבָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches similar cases, including the case of **one who left a gentile in his shop,** and the case of **one who placed his wine in a wagon or on a ship.** The Gemara explains: **And it is necessary** for the mishna to cite all these cases, even though they appear similar, **as, had** the mishna **taught** only the case of the **gentile** transporting a Jew's barrels, one might assume that the reason there is no concern that the gentile used the wine in that case is **because he thinks** that **perhaps** the owner **will come and see him. But in a wagon or on a ship,** one might **say that he can take his ship far off and do what he desires** in such a way that the owner cannot see him.

The Gemara now asks the obvious question: given that all three cases in the Mishna — the porter, the wagon or ship, and the store — involve the same basic principle (non-Jew alone with wine, departure announced or not), why enumerate them separately? If the rule is identical, one case would suffice.

### Scenario Risks

The Gemara answers that each case presents a counterintuitive consideration that makes it seem as though the general rule should not apply there. The Mishna therefore teaches explicitly that the same principle governs in each situation, despite the unique features that might suggest otherwise.

### Boat Opportunity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא בְּקָרוֹן אוֹ בִּסְפִינָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר: דִּלְמָא אָתֵי בְּאוֹרְחָא אַחֲרִיתִי וְקָאֵי אַגּוּדָּא וְחָזֵי לִי, אֲבָל נׇכְרִי בַּחֲנוּתוֹ אֵימָא: אָחֵיד לֵהּ לְבָבָא וְעָבֵיד כֹּל דְּבָעֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the mishna **taught** only the case where the Jew placed his wine **in a wagon or on a ship,** one might assume that the concern there is **because** the gentile **thinks: Perhaps he will come on a different road or stand on the bank** of the river **and see me. But** in the case of **a gentile in his shop,** one might **say** that **he can hold the door** closed **and do whatever he desires** with no concern of being seen. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that in all of these cases the same ruling applies, and there is no concern unless the Jew informed the gentile that he is going a great distance away.

In the wagon or boat case, one might have thought that a non-Jew who sees the Jew depart from the boat into a town — engaging in what will clearly be a time-consuming activity — will feel entirely confident that he has a free hand. Even if no explicit time was stated, leaving a boat and going into a town seems to communicate an extended absence more clearly than simply getting a bit ahead on a road. The *chiddush* is that even here, the non-Jew cannot be certain: perhaps the owner will stand at the dock and observe him; perhaps he will take an alternate route back. The Gemara phrases this as *dilma atei be-orcha achrisi ve-kai aguda ve-chazei lei* — perhaps he will come by a different road and stand at the shore and see him. The fear of discovery through such means is sufficient to maintain *chezkas ha-mishtamer*.

### Store Discovery

Similarly, in the store case, one might reason that the non-Jew left alone in a locked store will simply lock the door and proceed to do whatever he wishes without fear of interruption. The Mishna teaches that this reasoning does not apply.

### Discovery Deterrent

The *chiddush* of the store case is the sufficiency of the fear of being caught having locked the door. If the owner returns and finds the door bolted from the inside, he will demand an explanation. That fear alone — even without any specification of time — is enough to keep the non-Jew in check. The principle at work here is that it is the psychological pressure of potential surprise discovery, not the formal announcement of a timed absence, that determines whether *chezkas ha-mishtamer* applies.

## Plaster vs. Tar

### Seal Material

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁל סִיד, אֲבָל בְּשֶׁל טִיט — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפְתַּח וְיִגּוֹף וְיִגּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** § There is a dispute in the mishna with regard to the length of time that causes the wine to be rendered forbidden if the owner notified the gentile that he is going off some distance. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **dispute** is only **with regard to** the case of a stopper made **of lime plaster,** in which a hole can be sealed without being detected; **but with regard to** the case of a stopper made **of clay,** in which a patch would be detected, **everyone agrees** that the wine is forbidden only if there was **enough time for** the gentile **to open** the barrel by removing the stopper entirely, **stop** it again with a new stopper, **and** for the new stopper to **dry.**

Having established when *chezkas ha-mishtamer* fails and what window of time then becomes legally significant, the Gemara turns to the debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — specifically, the question of whether one must fear that the non-Jew made a small hole and resealed it, or only that he removed the entire cover and replaced it. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *machlokes be-shel sid* — their dispute applies only where the seal of the barrel is made of plaster. When the seal is white plaster, a repaired hole on the surface may look identical to the surrounding material. That is the context in which the *Tanna Kamma* worries about undetectable tampering.

By contrast, if the seal is made of *tit* — a dark tar-like substance — *divrei ha-kol kedei she-yiftach ve-yigof ve-yigov*: everyone agrees we only fear the removal and replacement of the entire cover, because a hole patched with lighter material in a dark tar seal would be immediately obvious. There is no debate in the tar case.

### Plaster Detection

The logic of the plaster debate requires some elaboration. If the cover is plaster and a small hole is made from above and then refilled, the question is whether the repair is detectable. *Milma'alah* — from the top surface — it may look seamless, especially since plaster can be smoothed over to match the surrounding color. *Milmatah* — from underneath the cover — the repair may be more evident: the fill will not have been smoothed from below, may leave a visible gap, and the newer material may be a different shade from the older plaster that has been in contact with wine.

### RSBG Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לַחֲכָמִים: וַהֲלֹא סִתּוּמוֹ נִיכָּר בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָה וּבֵין מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this explanation of the dispute from a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said to the Rabbis: But isn't the sealing of** the hole **noticeable both from above and from below,** and therefore the gentile will be wary of doing so?

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* presents a challenge to the *Chachamim*: *ve-halo situmo nikar bein milma'alah bein milmatah* — is not the patching visible both above and below? The Gemara explains that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* was not certain which material the *Chachamim* were discussing. His challenge was structured as a dilemma: if you are speaking of a tar seal, the patch is obvious both above and below, so there is no reason to fear hole-piercing at all — both sides should agree. If you are speaking of plaster, I still challenge you: even if the top looks fine, the underside will reveal the tampering.

### Thief Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הוּא דְּלָא יָדַע מַאי קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לְהוּ: אִי בְּשֶׁל טִיט קָאָמְרִיתוּ — סִתּוּמוֹ נִיכָּר בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָה וּבֵין מִלְּמַטָּה, וְאִי בְּשֶׁל סִיד קָאָמְרִיתוּ — נְהִי דִּלְמַעְלָה לָא יְדִיעַ, לְמַטָּה מִיהָא יְדִיעַ. וְרַבָּנַן, כֵּיוָן דְּמִלְּמַעְלָה לָא יְדִיעַ, לָא מַסִּיק אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּאָפֵיךְ וַחֲזֵי לֵיהּ. אִי נָמֵי, זִימְנִין דְּחָלֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It was Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel who did not know what the Rabbis were saying, and this** is what **he is saying to them: If you are speaking of** a stopper made **of clay, its sealing is noticeable both from above and from below. And if you are speaking of** a stopper made **of lime plaster, granted, it is not noticeable from above,** but **it is noticeable, in any event, from below. And** how would **the Rabbis** respond to this claim? They maintain that **since it is not noticeable from above** that there is a patch, **it does not occur to** the gentile **that** the owner **will turn** the stopper **over and see** the patch. **Alternatively,** the Rabbis could answer that **sometimes it seals firmly** and is not detectable.

The *Chachamim* respond: *keivan de-lemala lo yedia, lo massik adaateih de-appik ve-chazei lei* — since from the surface nothing appears amiss, the would-be thief simply does not think ahead to the possibility that someone will remove the cover and inspect the underside. He convinces himself that the top looks normal, that he will get away with it, and does not mentally project far enough to realize that the bottom will reveal him. Furthermore, *zimnin de-chalim* — sometimes the fill does eventually blend in below as well, and the thief may count on this. This explains why the *Tanna Kamma* takes the hole-piercing scenario seriously in the plaster case: the thief believes he can execute it without detection.

## Halachic Ruling

### Halacha Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, הוֹאִיל וּתְנַן סְתָמָא כְּוָותֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, since we learned an unattributed** mishna **in accordance with his** opinion.

Rava rules: *halacha ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the law follows *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. We do not fear the small-hole scenario; the only concern is the wholesale removal and replacement of the cover.

### Stam Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הָיָה אוֹכֵל עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן עִמּוֹ, וְהִנִּיחַ לָגִין עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן, לָגִין עַל הַדּוּלְבְּקִי וְהִנִּיחַ וְיָצָא — מָה שֶׁעַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן אָסוּר, מָה שֶׁעַל הַדּוּלְבְּקִי מוּתָּר, וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: הֱוֵי מוֹזֵג וְשׁוֹתֶה — אַף שֶׁעַל הַדּוּלְבְּקִי אָסוּר. חָבִיּוֹת פְּתוּחוֹת אֲסוּרוֹת, סְתוּמוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפְתַּח וְיִגּוֹף וְתִיגּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as we learned** in the last clause of the mishna: If a Jew **was eating with** a gentile **at the table, and left jugs** of wine **on the table and a jug on the side table, and he left it and went out, what is on the table** is **forbidden,** as it is likely that the gentile handled it, whereas **what is on the side table** is **permitted. But if** the Jew **said to** the gentile: **Mix** water with the wine **and drink, even** the jug **that is on the side table** is **forbidden.** Similarly, **open barrels are forbidden,** but **sealed** barrels **are permitted** unless the Jew was out of the room for **sufficient time for** the gentile **to open** the barrel by removing the stopper, **stop** it again by making a new stopper, **and** for the new stopper to **dry.**

The evidentiary basis for this ruling is the structure of the Mishna itself. The Mishna's final case — the case of eating at the table with a non-Jew — concludes with the ruling about sealed barrels: *chaviyot stumot mutarot, kedei she-yiftach ve-yigof ve-tigov*. This formulation quotes only the position of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* and does so without attribution — it appears as a *stam mishna*, an anonymous ruling. An anonymous ruling at the end of a Mishna indicates the normative halachic position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כּוּלָּהּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל קָתָנֵי לַהּ? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious?** This ruling is stated explicitly in the mishna. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel teaches the entire** clause, and this is the continuation of his previous statement and not an unattributed statement of the mishna, Rava **teaches us** that this is not so.

The Gemara briefly asks whether this is even a novel point — surely it is obvious from the *stam mishna* that we rule accordingly. The response is that one might have read the final passage of the Mishna not as a *stam* ruling but as a continuation of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s quoted position earlier in that same case. The Gemara therefore establishes explicitly: the closing lines of the Mishna function as an independent, anonymous normative statement, not merely as the tail of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s attributed opinion.

## Sealed Wine Problem

### Logic Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּקַיְימָא לַן כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּלָא חָיֵישׁ לְשִׁתּוּמָא, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּלָא חָיֵישׁ לְזִיּוּפָא, הָאִידָּנָא מַאי טַעְמָא לָא מוֹתְבִינַן חַמְרָא בְּיַד גּוֹיִם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And since we maintain** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who is not concerned about a bored hole** in the barrel, **and** even though there is a concern that the gentile may have opened and replaced the stopper **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who is not concerned with forgery** of the seal, because excessive effort is required to forge a seal (see 31a), therefore, **nowadays, what is the reason we do not place wine** in sealed barrels **in the possession of gentiles?**

With the halachic ruling in place, the Gemara raises a searching and practically significant question. We have now established two things: first, that we rule like *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* and are not concerned about hole-piercing — only the wholesale removal of the seal; second, that we rule like Rabbi Eliezer (from an earlier chapter) that a single seal suffices to protect wine in transit. These two rulings together seem to eliminate nearly every ground for concern when leaving sealed wine in the custody of a non-Jew.

### Single Seal

The relevance of the ruling like Rabbi Eliezer is this: the earlier dispute about *chotam* and *chotam betoch chotam* — single versus double seal — was resolved in favor of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that one seal is sufficient. The practical meaning is that a sealed barrel with one intact seal can be trusted; we are not worried that the seal was broken and forged.

### Permission Challenge

The Gemara therefore asks: *ve-chi me-achar de-kaima lan ke-vatteih de-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel de-lo chaishinan le-situmi, ve-hilchesa ke-vatteih de-Rabbi Eliezer de-lo chaishinan le-ziyufa* — now that we are not afraid of a small pierced hole, and we are not afraid of seal-forging, what exactly are we afraid of when we restrict leaving sealed wine with non-Jews at all? Why not simply leave a sealed barrel of wine with a non-Jew indefinitely?

## Shaika Air Hole

### Siphon Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם שַׁיְיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** It is **because of the bunghole,** the hole in a barrel through which one smells the wine, which the gentile might widen a bit to drink from it.

The Gemara answers: *mishum sheika* — because of the *sheika*. The *sheika* is a tiny air hole that was made in the stopper of the barrel to allow the wine's aroma to escape — a standard feature of how barrels were constructed. Through this tiny hole, a non-Jew could insert a thin straw or tube and siphon off wine without breaking the seal at all. This does not require piercing a new hole (which would be detectable), forging a seal (which is prevented by the single seal's integrity), or removing the cover (which is the threshold *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* requires). It exploits a pre-existing aperture.

### Hole Authorities

Rashi explains *sheika* as *nekev dak me'od she-be-migufas chavit le-hotzi rei'ach ha-yayin* — a very fine hole in the barrel's stopper to allow the wine's scent to exit — and notes that the concern is that the non-Jew will *yashuf sham, yekadei'ach sham be-miktzat ve-yitom min ha-yayin* — probe that hole with a straw and taste or draw off wine. Tosafot offers a similar reading, differing slightly in the precise terminology but arriving at the same functional concern.

### Wood Plank Risk

For barrels made of wood rather than pottery, the concern is structurally analogous, as Tosafot explains. Wooden barrels are constructed from planks (*nissarim*) fitted together, and a thin knife blade inserted between the planks can create a gap through which a straw could be inserted to siphon wine — *she-yitkevuhu ovdei kochavim be-chudo shel sakkin bein ha-nissarim* — and this would not constitute the detectable tampering that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* requires. Thus, even for the wooden barrels of medieval Europe, the *sheika* concern applied in a functionally equivalent form, and the question of what protections were required for transporting wine remained practically urgent.

## Tosafot Sealing

### Owner Inspection

Tosafot undertakes an extended analysis of how the rulings on *chotam* and *ziyuf* translate into practical guidance for transporting wine. One approach, consistent with ruling like Rabbi Eliezer, is that a single seal is sufficient when the owner himself will later inspect the barrel. If the owner knows what his own seal looked like and will examine it upon return, a single intact seal gives him enough information to determine whether it has been tampered with.

### Seal vs. Delivery

This basis for the leniency breaks down, however, in a delivery scenario. If the owner sends the sealed barrel with a non-Jew to a recipient — Chanan, for example — the recipient has never seen the original seal and cannot tell whether it has been broken and reforged. In such a case, more protection may be required.

### Minhag Olam

Tosafot acknowledges that the prevalent practice — *minhag ha-olam* — had already been to send wine with a single sealed barrel even without the recipient recognizing the seal. Tosafot is prepared to validate this practice *be-di'avad* at minimum, noting that the *stam* Gemara's ruling like Rabbi Eliezer supports it. The text reads: *vegam minhag ha-olam haya she-kevar lishloach yayin be-chotam echad belo chozer u-makiro* — the practice was already to send wine with one seal without the sender going back to inspect it personally.

### Wood vs. Pottery

Rabbeinu Yitzchak ben Yehuda raised the issue with Rashi that the barrels of the Talmudic period, being pottery, are not equivalent to the wooden barrels of medieval France and Germany. For pottery barrels, a single seal on the mouth of the barrel was sufficient. But for wooden barrels made of fitted planks, even with a seal on the top, the planks themselves are a vulnerability — *be-chudo shel sakkin bein ha-nissarim* — and wine can be extracted without disturbing the seal at all. Rabbeinu Yitzchak therefore held that wooden barrels require additional protection beyond what sufficed for pottery.

### Vessel Recognition

Even setting aside the question of how the barrel itself might be breached, Tosafot identifies a further concern: complete substitution of the barrel. The non-Jew need not break the seal at all; he can simply replace the entire barrel with a different one bearing an intact seal of his own. Against this concern, Tosafot argues that *hakarat ha-kli* — recognition of the vessel — is necessary: the recipient must be familiar enough with the specific barrel to know that it is the same one that was sent. If he cannot recognize it, he has no assurance that what he received is what was dispatched, regardless of the seal's condition.

### Multi-Layer Seal

Tosafot proceeds to enumerate additional protective measures that address these vulnerabilities. Wrapping the barrel in leather (*or*) affixed with nails prevents access between the planks. This leather covering functions as an additional layer of protection over and above the sealed top, ensuring that a knife cannot be inserted into the joins.

### Signature Seal

The most sophisticated protection Tosafot describes is the *signature-across-seam* method, analogous to signing one's name across the flap of an envelope. One writes Hebrew letters (*osiyot*) with half the letter on the leather covering and half on the body of the barrel itself — *chatzyan al ha-or ve-chatzyan al dofnei karshei ha-chavis*. If the non-Jew were to lift the leather, he could not realign it to restore the appearance of the original inscription. Moreover, since the letters are in Hebrew, the non-Jew cannot replicate them. Metal bands (*chashuk*) are then placed around the barrel to secure the leather in position.

The practical conclusion that emerges from Tosafot's discussion spans a wide spectrum. At the most lenient end, *be-di'avad* any single-sealed barrel may be trusted. At the other extreme, full protection requires leather wrapping, Hebrew lettering across the seam, metal bands, and recipient recognition of the vessel. The *lechatchila* standard sits somewhere in the middle: at a minimum, the air hole must be sealed — the *sheika* must be blocked — and ideally Hebrew lettering should be present so that any tampering with the seal would destroy the letters in a way the non-Jew cannot repair.

## Prostitute Cases

### Goy Prostitute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: זוֹנָה גּוֹיָה, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְסוּבִּין אֶצְלָהּ — חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי, נְהִי דְּתָקֵיף לְהוּ יִצְרָא דַעֲבֵירָה, יִצְרָא דְּיֵין נֶסֶךְ לָא תָּקֵיף לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** In the case of **a gentile prostitute,** where **Jews** are **dining at her** table, **the wine** at the table is **permitted. Granted, their passion for the sin** of harlotry **overwhelms their** judgment, but **the passion for wine** used for **a libation does not overwhelm their** judgment, and they will not allow her to use it for a libation.

The Gemara now presents what is, by any measure, a striking scenario. Rava states: *zona ovedes kochavim ve-Yisrael mesubin etzlah* — a non-Jewish prostitute, with a Jewish man as her patron, eating and drinking wine at her table.

### Control Determinant

The ruling is: *chamar shari* — the wine is permitted. We are not concerned that the wine was touched improperly or used for libation.

### Separate Sins

The reasoning is carefully drawn: *nehi de-takif lei yitzra de-aveira* — granted that he has a powerful *yeitzer hara* for this particular transgression, specifically the sexual sin he is clearly committing. *Yitzra de-yayin nesech lo takif lei* — his inclination toward *yayin nesech*, toward using wine as a libation or permitting it to be handled improperly, is not similarly activated. The fact that a person transgresses in one domain does not mean he is reckless across all domains. One who will violate the laws of sexual morality does not necessarily give his companion license to touch his wine, and his own care for *kashrus* does not dissolve merely because he is sinning in another regard. The wine, therefore, remains his to control, and we assume he has not permitted it to be touched.

### Jewish Prostitute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹנָה יִשְְׂרְאֵלִית וְגוֹיִם מְסוּבִּין — חַמְרָא אָסוּר, מַאי טַעְמָא? הוֹאִיל וְזִילָה עֲלַיְיהוּ, בָּתְרַיְיהוּ גְּרִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a Jewish prostitute and gentiles dining** with her, **the wine** is **forbidden. What is the reason?** It is that **since she is contemptible in their** eyes, **she is subjugated to them,** and they use the wine for a libation without consideration for her.

The reverse scenario is then presented: *zonah yisre'elit ve-ovdei kochavim mesubin* — a Jewish prostitute with a non-Jewish patron.

### Patron Control Logic

Here the ruling is reversed: *chamra asir* — the wine is forbidden.

The Gemara explains: *ho'il ve-zila aleihu, batraihu grira* — since she has made herself lowly before him, she follows after him. The logic is that in the relationship between a prostitute and her patron, it is the patron who holds the position of control and the prostitute who defers. When the patron is Jewish, he determines what happens to the wine — he controls the situation, maintains his own standards, and does not give her license to handle it improperly. When the patron is non-Jewish, the dynamic is inverted: she defers to him, and either she will permit him to handle the wine freely or she herself will act in accordance with his will, which may include offering the wine as a libation. In either case, the wine comes under his effective control, and since he is a non-Jew whose handling renders wine forbidden, the wine is prohibited.
