# Ketubot 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Amud ID

### Amud Corrections

Today's *daf* is 104. We pick up on 103b — not 104b or 105a as one might initially suppose — in the middle of a series of narratives concerning the final days of Rebbe and the transition of leadership that followed his death.

### Rebbe's Final Days

The most recent episode in the *gemara* had described how Rebbe appointed Chanina bar Chama as *rosh yeshiva*. The *gemara* had further noted how Chanina bar Chama deferred to Rabbi Afas, who was older, allowing Rabbi Afas to occupy the position for several years before him.

---

## Why Not R. Chiya?

### Chiya Death Chronology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הֲוָה רַבִּי חִיָּיא! נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי קִבְרוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי, וְהוֹרַדְתִּי עָלָיו דְּמָעוֹת! אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't Rabbi Ḥiyya** there? Why didn't Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi appoint him as head of the yeshiva? The Gemara answers: **He died** before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya say: I saw the grave site of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and I shed tears over it?** The Gemara answers: **Reverse** the names. It was Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi who said that he saw the grave site of Rabbi Ḥiyya.

With this background established, the *gemara* raises a striking difficulty. The passage begins approximately ten lines before the wider-set lines, just below the citation from Iyov 22. The *gemara* asks: *veha havi Rabbi Chiya* — but Rabbi Chiya was present. Why was Rabbi Chanina appointed *rosh yeshiva* rather than Rabbi Chiya, who was by all accounts Rebbe's preeminent *talmid* and the editor of the *Tosefta*? The *gemara* answers: *nach nafshe* — Rabbi Chiya had already passed away before Rebbe died.

But this answer immediately invites a challenge. Does the *gemara* not record a statement by Rabbi Chiya himself — *ani ra'isi kivro shel Rebbe* — that he had seen Rebbe's grave and wept over it? This implies Rabbi Chiya survived Rebbe, not the reverse. The *gemara* responds: *ipocha* — reverse the attribution. It was Rebbe who saw Rabbi Chiya's grave.

### Source Contradictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁמֵּת רַבִּי בָּטְלָה קְדוּשָּׁה! אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya say:** On **that day that Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **died, sanctity ceased?** The Gemara answers: **Reverse** the names. It was Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi who made this statement about Rabbi Ḥiyya.

The *gemara* then raises a second difficulty: a *braisa* records Rabbi Chiya's statement that *oso yom shemeis Rebbe batla kedusha* — on the day Rebbe died, holiness ceased. This again implies Rabbi Chiya was alive to witness Rebbe's death. Again the *gemara* proposes: *ipocha* — reverse it, and understand it as Rebbe saying this about Rabbi Chiya's death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁחָלָה רַבִּי, נִכְנַס רַבִּי חִיָּיא אֶצְלוֹ וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁהוּא בּוֹכֶה, אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתָּה בּוֹכֶה? וְהָתַנְיָא: מֵת מִתּוֹךְ הַשְּׂחוֹק — סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ, מִתּוֹךְ הַבֶּכִי — סִימָן רַע לוֹ. פָּנָיו לְמַעְלָה — סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ, פָּנָיו לְמַטָּה — סִימָן רַע לוֹ. פָּנָיו כְּלַפֵּי הָעָם — סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ, כְּלַפֵּי הַכּוֹתֶל — סִימָן רַע לוֹ. פָּנָיו יְרוּקִּין — סִימָן רַע לוֹ, פָּנָיו צְהוּבִּין וַאֲדוּמִּים — סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ. מֵת בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ. בְּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת — סִימָן רַע לוֹ. מֵת בְּעֶרֶב יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — סִימָן רַע לוֹ, בְּמוֹצָאֵי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ. מֵת מֵחוֹלִי מֵעַיִים — סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרוּבָּם שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים מִיתָתָן בְּחוֹלִי מֵעַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **When Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **fell ill, Rabbi Ḥiyya entered** to be **with him and found him crying. He said to him: My teacher, for what** reason **are you crying? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*:  If one **dies while laughing, it is a good sign for him; while crying, it is a bad sign for him.**  If one dies with **his face upward, it is a good sign for him;** with **his face downward, it is a bad sign for him.**  If one dies with **his face facing the people** standing around him, **it is a good sign for him;** with **his face facing the wall, it is a bad sign for him.**  If one's **face** is **sallow, it is a bad sign for him;** if **his face** is **yellow or ruddy, it is a good sign for him.**  If one **dies on the Shabbat eve it is a good sign for him,** because he is heading straight into the Shabbat rest; if one dies **at the conclusion of Shabbat it is a bad sign for him.**  If one **dies on the eve of Yom Kippur, it is a bad sign for him,** as his sins have not yet been forgiven; if one dies at **the conclusion of Yom Kippur it is a good sign for him,** because he died after his sins have been forgiven.  If one **dies due to an intestinal disease, it is a good sign for him, because most of the righteous die due to intestinal disease.**

A third source complicates matters further. Another *braisa* records that when Rebbe fell ill, Rabbi Chiya came to visit him and found him weeping. Rabbi Chiya asked: *mipnei ma ata boche* — why are you crying? The *braisa* had just stated that dying while laughing is a good sign (*siman yafe lo*) while dying in tears is a bad sign (*siman ra lo*), and the list of omens continues: dying face-up is a good sign, face-down a bad sign; facing the people, a good sign; facing the wall, a bad sign; a sallow, greenish complexion is a bad sign; a bright and ruddy face is a good sign. Dying on *erev shabbos* is a good sign — one enters *olam haba* as *shabbos* begins; dying at *motzaei shabbos* is a bad sign. Dying on *erev Yom Kippur* is a bad sign, for one dies before one's sins are atoned; dying at *motzaei Yom Kippur* is a good sign. And dying from an illness of the digestive tract — *cholei me'ayim* — is a good sign, *mipnei sheruban shel tzaddikim meisim becholi me'ayim*, because we know that most righteous people die from such illness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא אַתּוֹרָה וּמִצְוֹת קָא בָכֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: I am crying for the Torah and the mitzvot** that I will be unable to fulfill after I die.

Rebbe's answer to Rabbi Chiya's question was: *ana al Torah umitzvos kavachina* — I am weeping on account of the Torah and *mitzvos* I am about to lose. This is not quite an answer to the concern about the omen of dying in tears, but the *braisa* clearly depicts Rabbi Chiya present at Rebbe's bedside while Rebbe was still alive. The *gemara* again proposes *ipocha*, reversing the attribution — but by now, the evidence is piling up that it was in fact Rebbe who predeceased Rabbi Chiya, and the reversals are growing strained.

### Chiya's Mitzvot Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, רַבִּי חִיָּיא עָסוּק בְּמִצְוֹת הֲוָה וְרַבִּי סָבַר לָא אֲפַגְּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** instead that **actually** we **do not** need to **reverse** the names in all of the above statements, but rather explain that **Rabbi Ḥiyya was occupied with** the performance of **mitzvot and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **thought: I will not hold him back** from his performance of mitzvot by appointing him head of the yeshiva.

The *gemara* therefore offers a decisive alternative: *le'olam lo siphach* — do not reverse any of these sources. Rabbi Chiya did indeed outlive Rebbe. The reason he was not appointed *rosh yeshiva* is that *Rabbi Chiya asak bemitzvos hava* — Rabbi Chiya was deeply engaged in going out into the world and doing *mitzvos*, not remaining cloistered in the *beis medrash*. The *gemara* adds: *Rebbe savar lo afagnei* — Rebbe did not wish to injure him, meaning he did not want to deprive Rabbi Chiya of the opportunity to continue that work by tethering him to the *rosh yeshiva* appointment. Crucially, this does not imply Rabbi Chiya was intellectually inferior to Chanina bar Chama — if anything, the framing suggests the opposite. Rebbe simply refused to strip him of the freedom to pursue his broader mission.

## Chanina vs. Chiya

### Chanina's Intellect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּכִי הֲווֹ מִינְּצוּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא: בַּהֲדֵי דִידִי מִינְּצֵת? דְּאִם חַס וְשָׁלוֹם נִשְׁתַּכְּחָה תּוֹרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, מַהְדַּרְנָא לֵיהּ מִפִּלְפּוּלַי.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** the background to an exchange that took place **when Rabbi Ḥanina and Rabbi Ḥiyya argued. Rabbi Ḥanina said to Rabbi Ḥiyya: You are arguing with me? If, Heaven forfend, the Torah would be forgotten from the Jewish people, I would restore it through my analyses,** i.e., using my abilities of analysis I would be able to rediscover all that had been lost.

This brings the *gemara* to a celebrated passage. Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Chiya were once engaged in vigorous debate — the *gemara* uses the word *mintzi*, they were fighting, not with fists, but fiercely with words. Rabbi Chanina said to Rabbi Chiya: *bahadi didi minatzit?* — do you think you can contend with me? *De'im chas veshalom mishtakcha Torah miYisrael* — for if, God forbid, Torah were forgotten from the Jewish people, *mehadarna lah mipilpuli* — I could restore it all through my intellectual analytical powers alone.

### Chiya's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֲנָא עֲבַדִי דְּלָא מִשְׁתַּכְחָה תּוֹרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל. דְּאַיְיתִינָא כִּיתָּנָא וְשָׁדֵיינָא, וּמְגַדֵּלְנָא נִישְׁבֵּי, וְצָיֵידְנָא טְבֵי(א), וּמַאֲכֵילְנָא בִּישְׂרָא לְיַתְמֵי, וְאָרֵיכְנָא מְגִילְּתָא מִמַּשְׁכֵי דִטְבֵי, וְסָלֵיקְנָא לְמָתָא דְּלֵית בַּהּ מַקְרֵי דַרְדְּקֵי, וְכָתֵיבְנָא חַמְשָׁא חוּמְשֵׁי לְחַמְשָׁא יָנוֹקֵי, וּמַתְנֵינָא שִׁיתָּא סִידְרֵי לְשִׁיתָּא יָנוֹקֵי, לְכֹל חַד וְחַד אֲמַרִי לֵיהּ אַתְנִי סִידְרָךְ לְחַבְרָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya said** to Rabbi Ḥanina: **I am working** to ensure **that the Torah will not be forgotten from the Jewish people. For I bring flax and I plant it, and I** then **weave nets** from the flax fibers. **I** then go out and **trap deer, and I feed** the **meat to orphans, and I form scrolls from the skins of the deer. And I go to a town that has no teachers of children in it and I write the five books** of the Torah **for five children. And I teach the six orders** of the Mishna **to six children. To each and every one** of these children **I say: Teach your order to your friends.** In this way all of the children will learn the whole of the Torah and the Mishna.

Rabbi Chiya replied: *ana avdi dela mishtakcha Torah miYisrael* — I am the one who ensures that Torah will not be forgotten from Israel in the first place. You focus on how brilliant you are and how you could reconstruct everything after the fact; I will go out and prevent the loss from occurring at all.

### Chiya's Method

He then described his method in striking detail. *De'aisina kitna* — I take flax and plant it. From what grows I weave a net. *Vetzaidna tavya* — I trap deer. *Umachilna bisra leyasmi* — I feed the meat to orphans. *Va'arichna megilta mimishchei detavya* — I prepare Torah scrolls from the hides of the deer. *Vesalikna lemata delais bah makrei dardakei* — I go to a city that has no teacher for children, no *cheder* teacher. *Vuchisivna chamisha chumshei lechamisha yanukei* — I write out the five books of the Torah for five different children. *Umasnina shita sidrei leshita yanukei* — and I teach the six orders of *Mishnah* to six different children, one order per child.

### Distribution Model

Then comes the key instruction: *ulechol chad vechad amrei lei, atni sidrach lechavrach* — to each child I say: teach what you know to your friend. The result is a network in which everyone is simultaneously student and teacher, the responsibility is distributed broadly, and no single person bears the entire weight. One need not be the most brilliant mind in the room to take part in the sacred work of sustaining Torah. This is also, implicitly, a pointed critique of Rabbi Chanina's posture: the idea that Torah rests entirely on one person's extraordinary intellect — that it is all about *me* and *my* Torah — is precisely the kind of thinking Rabbi Chiya's model challenges. And beyond the intellectual dimension, there is a further point: a great *talmid chacham* need not confine himself to the *beis medrash*. He can go out and plant flax, catch deer, feed orphans. His responsibilities extend into the real world, not only into the intellectual sphere.

### Rebbe Approves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: כַּמָּה גְּדוֹלִים מַעֲשֵׂה חִיָּיא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי: אֲפִילּוּ מִמְּךָ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֲפִילּוּ מֵאַבָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם, לֹא תְּהֵא כָּזֹאת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** what **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi referred to when he **said: How great are the actions of Ḥiyya. Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said to** his father: **Even** greater **than your** works? **He said to him: Yes. Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **Even** greater **than** the work of Rabbi Yosei, my **father?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Heaven forfend. Such** comments **should not be** made **among the Jewish people.**

*Vahaynu de'amar Rebbe* — and this is the meaning of Rebbe's famous statement: *kama gedolim ma'asei Chiya* — how great are the deeds of Chiya. The contrast the *gemara* draws is pointed: the *rosh yeshiva* kept in his intellectual tower, and the person who goes out into the world. And again, the *gemara* nowhere suggests Rabbi Chiya was incapable of being *rosh yeshiva* — only that Rebbe refused to confine him to it.

## Clarifications

### Shimon's Challenge

Rabbi Shimon bar Rebbe then pressed Rebbe: *afilu mimcha?* — is Rabbi Chiya even greater than you? Rebbe answered: *in* — yes, his deeds certainly are.

### Yehoshaphat Parallel

Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yossi then asked: *afilu me-abba* — even greater than my father? Rebbe answered: *chas veshalom, lo tehei kazot be-Yisrael* — God forbid, may no such thing be said in Israel. The precise point of the Rabbi Yossi comparison is left somewhat opaque by the *gemara*, though it is worth noting that Rabbi Yossi, unlike a pure *rosh yeshiva*, bore political responsibilities as *nasi*. In any case, the *gemara* moves on.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְאֶת יִרְאֵי ה׳ יְכַבֵּד״, וְאָמַר מָר: זֶה יְהוֹשָׁפָט מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה, כְּשֶׁהָיָה רוֹאֶה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הָיָה עוֹמֵד מִכִּסְּאוֹ, וּמְחַבְּקוֹ וּמְנַשְּׁקוֹ, וְקוֹרֵא לוֹ: רַבִּי רַבִּי, מָרִי מָרִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so** that it is correct to behave in such a manner? **But isn't it written: "But he honors those that fear the Lord"** (Psalms 15:4), **and the Master said: This** is referring to **Jehoshaphat, king of Judea. When he would see a Torah scholar he would rise from his throne and hug him and kiss him and call to him: My teacher, my teacher, my master, my master.** This demonstrates that it is appropriate even for a king to behave with affection toward Torah scholars.

The discussion of the challenge regarding whether Rebbe's instruction to Rabban Gamliel — to hold his leadership *beramim*, in heights, and to inspire awe in students — contradicts the verse *ve'et yirei Hashem yechabed*, that one should honor those who fear God, is addressed directly. The *gemara* cites the tradition that this verse refers to Yehoshaphat king of Yehuda, who would rise from his throne, embrace, and kiss a Torah sage, calling out *Rabbi, Rabbi, Mari, Mari* — my teacher, my teacher, my master, my master. Surely even a king is obligated to show honor to Torah sages. The *gemara* resolves the tension: *lo kashya ha betzina ha befarhesya* — it is not difficult. In private, one must cultivate *yirat cheit* and genuine humility; in public, one must embody the dignity of the office. It is a difficult balance to maintain — to hold genuine humility in one's inner life while not allowing that humility to undercut the authority the position demands.

## Deathbed Instructions

### Youngest Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: לִבְנִי קָטָן אֲנִי צָרִיךְ. נִכְנַס רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֶצְלוֹ, מָסַר לוֹ סִדְרֵי חׇכְמָה,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the narrative of the impending death of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **He said to them: I need my younger son. Rabbi Shimon entered his** presence. **He transmitted to him the orders of wisdom,** including how he should conduct himself and the essential principles of the Torah.

*Amar lehu livni katana ani tzarich* — Rebbe said, I need my youngest son. Rabbi Shimon entered. *Umasar lo sidrei chochmah* — Rebbe transmitted to him the *sidrei chochmah*, the tools or structures of wisdom. The precise content of this transmission is left unspecified — commentators suggest it concerned how a sage should conduct himself, how to present himself to the larger community, perhaps certain approaches to learning. The narrative suggests something secret or esoteric, because one does not transmit the full practical skills of a vocation in the final moments of life; what one transmits at such a moment is something of a different order.

### Eldest Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: לִבְנִי גָּדוֹל אֲנִי צָרִיךְ. נִכְנַס רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אֶצְלוֹ, וּמָסַר לוֹ סִדְרֵי נְשִׂיאוּת. אָמַר לוֹ: ״בְּנִי, נְהוֹג נְשִׂיאוּתָךְ בְּרָמִים, זְרוֹק מָרָה בַּתַּלְמִידִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: I need my older son. Rabban Gamliel entered his** presence, **and** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **transmitted to him the procedures** of the office **of the *Nasi*.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Rabban Gamliel: **My son, conduct your** term as ***Nasi* with assertiveness** and **cast fear upon** your **students,** i.e., treat them in a firm manner so that they will fear you.

*Amar lehu livni gadol ani tzarich* — Rebbe then said, I need my eldest son. Rabban Gamliel — who was to become the *nasi* — entered. *Umasar lo sidrei nesiyut* — Rebbe transmitted to him the structures of leadership. Again, the language of transmission at the deathbed suggests something beyond ordinary professional instruction.

### Leadership Balance

Rebbe's explicit charge to Rabban Gamliel was: *beni nehog nesiyutcha beramim* — conduct your leadership in heights, assume positions of honor. Rashi explains *beramim* as meaning *she-tehei yoshev bein gedolim* — that one must sit among the great, assume the appropriate posture of position, even if not by personal inclination. *U-zerok mara be-talmidim* — and cast fear into the students. Even if that is not one's natural disposition, one must act in a way befitting the office. The *gemara* immediately asks whether this conflicts with the obligation to honor those who fear God, and resolves it as noted above: privately one cultivates humility, publicly one must own the role.

## Clarifications…

### Private vs. Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּצִינְעָא, הָא בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** display of affection should be applied **in private,** when only the teacher and student are present, and **that** stern demeanor should be applied **in public,** in order to ensure the teacher's authority.

The resolution — *lo kashya ha betzina ha befarhesya* — draws the distinction between private conduct and public conduct. In private, genuine humility and *yirat cheit* are the measure. In public, the dignity and authority of the office must be upheld. The challenge, as the *gemara* implicitly acknowledges, is maintaining both simultaneously: that the *yirat Shamayim* not collapse into a performance, and that the public role not harden into arrogance. This tension runs through the entire sequence of deathbed instructions.

## Rebbe's Relocation

### Air Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי מוּטָל בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, וּמָקוֹם מוּכָן לוֹ בְּבֵית שְׁעָרִים. וְהָתַנְיָא: ״צֶדֶק צֶדֶק תִּרְדֹּף״ — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רַבִּי לְבֵית שְׁעָרִים!
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi was **lying** ill in **Tzippori and a** burial **site was ready for him in Beit She'arim.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **"Justice, justice shall you follow"** (Deuteronomy 16:20); **follow Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **to Beit She'arim,** i.e., one should seek to have his case adjudicated by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's court in Beit She'arim. This indicates that Beit She'arim, not Tzippori, was Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's place of residence, and therefore he must have been lying ill in Beit She'arim.

A *braisa* then records: *Rabbi mutal be-Tzippori* — Rebbe was sick in Tzippori, yet *umakom muchan lo be-Beit She'arim* — the place designated for his burial was Beit She'arim. Rebbe had relocated to Tzippori in the final years of his life, having previously maintained his seat of *nesiyut* in Beit She'arim for many years — the famous *kuchin*, catacombs, remain there. The *gemara* notes that a *braisa* had stated *tzedek tzedek tirdof, halach achar Rabbi le-Beit She'arim*, suggesting Rebbe was based in Beit She'arim. The *gemara* resolves this: *Rabbi be-Beit She'arim havah, ella keivan de-chalash, amtivuhu le-Tzippori* — he had been in Beit She'arim, but once his health declined they brought him to Tzippori, *demadalya de-ayara u-vasim avira* — because it is elevated and the air is sweeter, the air quality being better for his condition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי בְּבֵית שְׁעָרִים הֲוָה, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דַּחֲלַשׁ אַמְטְיוּהִי לְצִיפּוֹרִי, דְּמִדַּלְיָא וּבְסִים אַוֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **was in Beit She'arim, but when he became ill they transferred him to Tzippori, which is** situated at a **high** altitude **and** whose **air is scented.**

### Fast Day Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יוֹמָא דְּנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי, גְּזַרוּ רַבָּנַן תַּעֲנִיתָא, וּבְעוֹ רַחֲמֵי, וְאָמְרִי: כֹּל מַאן דְּאָמַר ״נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי״ — יִדָּקֵר בַּחֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** § It is related that **on the day that Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **died, the Sages decreed a fast, and begged for** divine **mercy** so that he would not die. **And they said: Anyone who says that Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **has died will be stabbed with a sword.**

*Hahu yoma de-nach nafshei de-Rebbi* — on the day that Rebbe was to die, it was not yet the moment of death but it was clear that the end was imminent. *Gazru rabbanan taanita* — the Rabbis decreed a fast day and prayed intensely. *Ve-amri: kol man de-amar nach nafshei de-Rebbi, yidaker be-charev* — they declared that anyone who announced Rebbe had died would be run through with a sword. This reflects both the raw denial of the moment and their wish to sustain their prayers without any disruption.

## Maidservant Prayer

### Prayer for Life

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְלִיקָא אַמְּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי לְאִיגָּרָא, אָמְרָה: עֶלְיוֹנִים מְבַקְּשִׁין אֶת רַבִּי, וְהַתַּחְתּוֹנִים מְבַקְּשִׁין אֶת רַבִּי. יְהִי רָצוֹן שֶׁיָּכוֹפוּ תַּחְתּוֹנִים אֶת הָעֶלְיוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The maidservant of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **ascended to the roof** and **said: The upper** realms **are requesting** the presence of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and the lower** realms **are requesting** the presence of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **May it be** the **will** of God **that the lower** worlds **should impose** their will **upon the upper** worlds.

*Selika amteih de-Rebbi* — Rebbe's maidservant ascended to the roof. She declared: *elyonim mevakshim et Rebbi, ve-tachtonim mevakshim et Rebbi* — the supernal realms desire Rebbe's soul, and those below wish to keep him here. *Yehi ratzon she-yakufu tachtonim et elyonim* — may it be God's will that the lower realms prevail over the upper, and that the prayers to keep Rebbe alive be accepted.

### Observing Suffering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזַאי כַּמָּה זִימְנֵי דְּעָיֵיל לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא וְחָלַץ תְּפִילִּין וּמַנַּח לְהוּ וְקָמִצְטַעַר, אֲמַרָה: יְהִי רָצוֹן שֶׁיָּכוֹפוּ עֶלְיוֹנִים אֶת הַתַּחְתּוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** However, **when she saw how many times he would enter the bathroom and remove** his **phylacteries,** and then exit **and put them** back **on, and** how **he was suffering** with his intestinal disease, **she said: May it be** the **will** of God **that the upper** worlds **should impose** their will **upon the lower** worlds.

Then she observed Rebbe's condition. *Keivan de-chazai kama zimnei de-ayeil le-veit ha-kissei ve-chalatz tefillin u-manach le-hu ve-ka mitzta'er* — she saw how many times he had to go to the bathroom, removing his *tefillin* each time and replacing them, and how much anguish he was suffering. It is worth noting — as a participant in the *shiur* pointed out — that this suffering is precisely the *cholei me'ayim*, the digestive illness, that the *braisa* just described as a good sign for a righteous person; the maidservant, however, saw only the unbearable pain. She reversed her prayer: *yehi ratzon she-yakufu elyonim et ha-tachtonim* — may the upper realms prevail, may Rebbe be released.

### Moment of Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הֲווֹ שָׁתְקִי רַבָּנַן מִלְּמִיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי. שָׁקְלָה כּוּזָא, שָׁדְיָיא מֵאִיגָּרָא [לְאַרְעָא], אִישְׁתִּיקוּ מֵרַחֲמֵי, וְנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Sages,** meanwhile, **would not be silent,** i.e., they would not refrain, **from begging for mercy** so that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would not die. So **she took a jug [*kuza*]** and **threw it from the roof to the ground.** Due to the sudden noise, the Sages **were** momentarily **silent** and refrained **from** begging for **mercy, and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **died.**

*Ve-la havu shatkei rabbanan milimeiv'ei rachamei* — but the Rabbis would not stop praying, and their prayers were effectively keeping Rebbe's soul tethered to this world. *Shakla kuza ve-shadya mei-igra le-ar'a* — the maidservant took a jug and threw it from the roof to the ground. *Ishtiku me-rachamei* — the Rabbis were startled into momentary silence. *Ve-nach nafshei de-Rebbi* — in that moment, Rebbe died.

### Medical Ethics

This *gemara* is invoked in discussions of medical ethics regarding end-of-life care. The question it raises is not one of actively hastening death, but whether there exists an obligation to employ every possible measure to sustain life down to its last second, or whether it can sometimes be an act of *rachamim* to allow the natural process of death to proceed without further intervention.

### David Parallel

A parallel is worth noting, raised in the *shiur*: the story of David HaMelech, who similarly would not stop learning, so that *malach ha-mavet* had to resort to a distraction to accomplish its task. It is likely an intentional resonance, given that Rebbe traced his lineage back to David HaMelech. David had *malach ha-mavet* on one side, Rebbe had the Rabbis' prayers on the other — in both cases, continuous engagement prevented the moment of death, and in both cases an external distraction broke the continuity. The parallel is further enriched by the observation that in *Eretz Yisrael*, unlike in *Bavel*, political and Torah leadership were not neatly separated. Rebbe, as *nasi*, was himself appointing the *rosh yeshiva* — something striking in its own right. And just as Yehoshaphat had been mentioned moments earlier, the implicit threads connecting Rebbe to the Davidic line run through the entire passage.

## Bar Kappara Eulogy

### Garment Torn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְבַר קַפָּרָא: זִיל עַיֵּין, אֲזַל אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ. קַרְעֵיהּ לִלְבוּשֵׁיהּ וְאַהְדְּרֵיהּ לְקִרְעֵיהּ לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ, פְּתַח וַאֲמַר: אֶרְאֶלִּים וּמְצוּקִים אָחֲזוּ בַּאֲרוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ, נִצְּחוּ אֶרְאֶלִּים אֶת הַמְּצוּקִים, וְנִשְׁבָּה אֲרוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַתּוּן קָאָמְרִיתוּ וַאֲנָא לָא קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to bar Kappara: Go and ascertain** the condition of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. **He went** and **found that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **had died. He tore his clothing and reversed** them so that **the tear** would be **behind him** and not be noticed. When he returned to the Sages **he opened** his remarks **and said:** The **angels [*erelim*] and righteous** mortals [*metzukim*] both **clutched the sacred ark. The angels triumphed** over **the righteous, and the sacred ark was captured. They said to him:** Has **he died? He said to them: You have said it and I did not say it,** as it had been decided that no one should say that he died.

*Amru lei rabbanan le-Bar Kappara: zil ayein* — the Rabbis sent Bar Kappara to see what was happening. Perhaps this was prompted by the sound of the thrown jug, perhaps by some other sign that something had changed. *Azal ashkacheih de-nach nafshei* — he went in and found Rebbe had died. *Kareih le-levushei ve-ahadreih le-kareih le-achorei* — he rent his garment and turned the tear to the back, presumably so as not to immediately shock the assembled sages and students outside.

### Poetic Eulogy

*Patach ve-amar* — he came out and declared: *erelim u-metzukim achazu ba-aron ha-kodesh* — the angels and the righteous ones grasped the holy ark. *Nitzchu Areilei et ha-Metzukim* — the angels prevailed over the righteous ones. *Ve-nishbah aron ha-kodesh* — and the holy ark was taken captive. The *aron ha-kodesh* refers to Rebbe himself; the *metzukim* are the righteous ones — from *metzukei eretz*, those who are the foundations of the earth. The heavenly forces won the struggle over the earthly ones.

### Evasion Technique

*Amru lei: nach nafshei?* — the Rabbis asked him: is he saying Rebbe has died? *Amar lehu: atun kamritu, va'ana lo kamina* — you said it, not I. Bar Kappara was not about to risk being run through with the sword the Rabbis had sworn to apply to anyone who made the announcement. The maneuver recalls the principle that one should not be *motzi dibah*, not voice something ruinous — by constructing his statement in poetic, deniable form, Bar Kappara communicated the news while maintaining that he himself had never said it outright.

## Final Prayer

### Ten Fingers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁעַת פְּטִירָתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי זָקַף עֶשֶׂר אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה, אֲמַר: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם! גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לְפָנֶיךָ שֶׁיָּגַעְתִּי בְּעֶשֶׂר אֶצְבְּעוֹתַי בַּתּוֹרָה, וְלֹא נֶהֱנֵיתִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶצְבַּע קְטַנָּה. יְהִי רָצוֹן מִלְּפָנֶיךָ שֶׁיְּהֵא שָׁלוֹם בִּמְנוּחָתִי.
>
> **Translation:** It is further related: **At the time of the death of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **he raised his ten fingers toward Heaven** and **said** in prayer: **Master of the Universe, it is revealed and known before You that I toiled with my ten fingers in the Torah, and I have not derived any benefit** from the world **even with** my **small finger. May it be Your will that there be peace in my repose.**

*Bish'at ptirato she'Rebbe* — at the moment of Rebbe's death, *zakaf eser etzba'otav klapei ma'alah* — he raised his ten fingers toward heaven and declared: *Ribono shel olam, galui veyadu'ah lefanecha she'yagati be'eser etzba'otai batorah* — Master of the Universe, it is known and revealed before You that I have labored with all ten fingers in Torah, *v'lo neheniti afilu be'etzba ktanah* — and I did not derive benefit from this world even so much as a little finger's worth. *Yehi ratzon milfanecha she'yehei shalom bimnuchati* — may it be Your will that there be peace in my rest.

The claim that he derived no benefit from the world is striking in light of other *gemaros* that describe Rebbe and Antoninus sharing fine delicacies at their table. One resolution, noted in the *shiur*, lies in the earlier distinction between private and public conduct: Rebbe may have participated in such meals as a matter of necessity for his public role as *nasi*, without genuinely desiring those pleasures for himself. Another reading, suggested during the discussion, is that *lo neheneti* refers specifically to Torah work: he never took *honoraria* for giving a *shiur* or used his Torah scholarship as a means of personal gain. *Tosafot*, however, reads the phrase literally, citing a *midrash* that while one prays for Torah to enter one's body, one should also pray that delicacies not enter one's body — the statement being about not investing one's life in physical pleasure rather than spiritual endeavor.

### Heavenly Voice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״יָבֹא שָׁלוֹם יָנוּחוּ עַל מִשְׁכְּבוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** A Divine Voice emerged and said: "He enters in peace, let them rest on their resting places."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מִשְׁכָּבְךָ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! מְסַיֵּיעַ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר גַּמָּדָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר גַּמָּדָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַצַּדִּיק נִפְטָר מִן הָעוֹלָם, אוֹמְרִים מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, צַדִּיק פְּלוֹנִי בָּא. אוֹמֵר לָהֶם: יָבוֹאוּ צַדִּיקִים וְיֵצְאוּ לִקְרָאתוֹ, וְאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: ״יָבֹא בְּשָׁלוֹם״, יָנוּחוּ עַל מִשְׁכְּבוֹתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why does it say: "They rest in their beds," in the plural? **It should have** said: **In your bed,** in the singular, as the beginning of the verse is phrased in the singular. The Gemara notes: This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Gamda. As Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Gamda said** that **Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul said: At the time when a righteous individual departs from the world, the ministering angels say before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, the righteous** individual **so-and-so is coming.** The Holy One, Blessed be He, then **says to them: The righteous should come** forth **and they should go out toward him. And** the righteous **say to** the newly deceased individual: **He enters in peace,** and subsequently, the righteous **rest in their beds.**

*Yatzta bat kol ve-amrah* — a heavenly voice responded with the verse: *yavo shalom, yanuchu al mishkevotam* — they come in peace, let them rest on their resting places. The *gemara* notes a slight grammatical puzzlement: since the *bat kol* was responding to Rebbe's prayer for peace in his rest, one might have expected the singular *mishkevecha* — your resting place — rather than the plural *mishkevotam* — their resting places. This question opens the next teaching.

### Righteous Greeters

*Mesiyi'a lei* — this supports the teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda (a different Rabbi Chiya than the one discussed earlier) citing Rabbi Yose bar Shaul: *bish'a she'hatzaddik niftar min ha'olam* — when a righteous person departs from the world, the ministering angels say before God: *Ribono shel olam, tzaddik ploni ba* — Master of the Universe, the righteous person so-and-so is arriving. God responds: *yavo'u tzaddikim ve-yeitzu likrato ve-omeru lo yavo veshalom* — let all the righteous people of the heavenly realm come out to greet him and say to him, come in peace. Then, once they have greeted him, *yanuchu al mishkevotam* — all the righteous ones return to their resting places. The plural refers to the righteous ones of the heavenly realm who came out to welcome the newly arrived.

### Three Angel Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַצַּדִּיק נִפְטָר מִן הָעוֹלָם, שָׁלֹשׁ כִּיתּוֹת שֶׁל מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת יוֹצְאוֹת לִקְרָאתוֹ, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ: ״בֹּא בְּשָׁלוֹם״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: ״הוֹלֵךְ נִכְחוֹ״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ: ״יָבֹא שָׁלוֹם יָנוּחוּ עַל מִשְׁכְּבוֹתָם״. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהָרָשָׁע נֶאֱבָד מִן הָעוֹלָם, שָׁלֹשׁ כִּיתּוֹת שֶׁל מַלְאֲכֵי חַבָּלָה יוֹצְאוֹת לִקְרָאתוֹ, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: ״אֵין שָׁלוֹם אָמַר ה׳ לָרְשָׁעִים״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ: ״לְמַעֲצֵבָה יִשְׁכַּב״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ: ״רְדָה וְהׇשְׁכְּבָה אֶת עֲרֵלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: At the time when a righteous individual departs from the world, three contingents of ministering angels go out toward him. One says to him: Enter in peace; and one says** to him: Each one **that walks in his uprightness; and one says to him: He enters in peace, they rest in their beds. At the time when a wicked person perishes from the world, three contingents of angels of destruction go out toward him. One says** to him: **"There is no peace, says the Lord concerning the wicked"** (Isaiah 48:22); **and one says to him: "You shall lie down in sorrow"** (Isaiah 50:11); **and one says to him: "Go down, and be laid with the uncircumcised"** (Ezekiel 32:19).

Rabbi Elazar adds further detail: *bish'a she'tzaddik niftar min ha'olam, shalosh kitot shel mal'achei hasharet yotzot likrato* — three groups of ministering angels come out to greet the righteous person. One group says: *yavo veshalom* — come in peace. The second says: *holech nechucho* — go in your righteousness. The third says: *yavo shalom yanuchu al mishkevotam* — come in peace, and let all rest on their resting places.

Conversely, *bish'a she'rasha ne'evad min ha'olam* — when the wicked person is destroyed from the world — three groups of destructive angels come out to meet him. The first declares: *ein shalom amar Hashem la-resha'im* — there is no peace, says the Lord, for the wicked. The second: *l'ma'atzevah yishkav* — in suffering he shall lie down. The third: *redei vehashkevah et areilim* — go down and lie with the uncircumcised, descending to the depths.

### No Worldly Benefit

Returning to Rebbe's declaration that he never derived benefit from even a little finger's worth of this world: as noted, this stands in some tension with other sources depicting Rebbe's considerable material circumstances. The resolution that his public role as *nasi* required certain outward trappings of position, without his inwardly desiring them, is plausible. The alternative reading — that *lo neheneti* means he never used his Torah learning as a means of personal material gain, never took payment for teaching — offers a sharper and more textually integrated *pshat*, though *Tosafot* reads the phrase in its more straightforward sense of physical asceticism.

## Mourning Period

### Holiness Died

The *gemara* returns to the statement *oso yom shemeis Rebbe batla kedusha* — on the day Rebbe died, holiness ceased. This was one of the statements the *gemara* earlier proposed reversing, attributing it to Rebbe about Rabbi Chiya rather than vice versa. A related *braisa* at the end of Sotah records: *mishemeis Rebbe batla anavah ve-yirah* — when Rebbe died, humility and fear ceased as well. The *gemara* notes that *kedusha* encompasses all of these qualities.

### Priestly Purity

*Tosafot* then cites a remarkable tradition. *Rav Rabbi Chaim Kohen haya omer* — and one notes that he was a *Kohen* — *ilmalei hayisi kesheniftar Rabbeinu Tam, hayisi mitamei lo* — had I been present when Rabbeinu Tam died, I would have made myself *tamei* for him. His reasoning: *de-batla kedusha de-ka'amar hacha hinu kedushas kehuna* — the cessation of holiness spoken of in the *gemara* refers to the holiness of *kehuna*; on that day it was as though there were no operative laws of *kehuna*, as when an immediate relative dies and even *Kohanim* would properly become *tamei*. The Talmudic phrase, he argues, applies only to *oso yom* — that specific day — and not beyond it, as indicated by the precise language *oso yom davka*.

### Raavad Instance

*Tosafot* further notes that the Yerushalmi in *Berachot*, in the chapter of *mi shemeiso*, states explicitly: *de-keshemeis Rebbe amru ein kehuna hayom* — when Rebbe died, they said there is no *kehuna* today. *Tosafot* adds a note of caution, suggesting the Yerushalmi may be speaking specifically of *tumas beis haperas*, which is *de-Rabbanan*, and perhaps not of full *Torah*-level *tum'ah*. Nevertheless, the historical precedent is noted: when the *Raavad* of Posquières died, the *Kohanim* of that community in fact became *tamei* in order to participate in his burial — an event recorded in Twersky's study of the *Raavad*. This reflects the concept that upon the death of the *gadol ha-dor*, the laws of priestly purity are, for that singular day, treated as suspended for all of *Klal Yisrael*.

## Ketubah Duration

### Mishnah Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהִיא בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ, גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לְעוֹלָם. כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהִיא בְּבֵית בַּעְלָהּ, גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים. שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים שֶׁתַּעֲשֶׂה טוֹבָה כְּנֶגֶד כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **As long as** a widow **is** living **in the house of her father** and is being supported by her husband's heirs, **she may always collect** payment of **her marriage contract,** even after many years. **As long as she is** living **in the house of her husband, she may collect** payment of **her marriage contract until twenty-five years** later, at which point she may no longer collect the payment. This is **because there is** enough time **in twenty-five years for her to do favors** and give to others, thereby spending the resources of the orphans, until what she has spent **equals** the value of **her marriage contract.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir, who said** it **in the name of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

The *gemara* now turns to a new *Mishnah* on 104a. *Kol zman she'hi be-veis aviha, gova kesuvasa le'olam* — as long as the widow resides in her father's house, she may collect her *ketubah* forever. Since it is a debt owed to her, the mere passage of time does not constitute a waiver. *Kol zman she'hi be-veis ba'ala* — but if she is living in her deceased husband's household and being supported by the estate, *gova kesuvasa ad esrim ve-chamesh shanim* — she may only collect her *ketubah* for 25 years, according to Rabbi Meir.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהִיא בְּבֵית בַּעְלָהּ — גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לְעוֹלָם, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהִיא בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ — גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say** the opposite: **As long as she is** residing **in the house of her husband she may always collect** payment of **her marriage contract,** since during this time the heirs are caring for her and she is therefore embarrassed to sue them for payment of her marriage contract. However, **as long as she is in the house of her father she may collect** payment of **her marriage contract until twenty-five years** later, and if by then she has not sued for it, it is assumed that she has waived her rights to it.

The Sages, however, hold the inverse: *kol zman she'hi be-veis ba'ala, gova kesuvasa le'olam* — when she is in her husband's household, she may always collect it. *Kol zman she'hi be-veis aviha, gova kesuvasa ad esrim ve-chamesh shanim* — when she is in her father's house, she may only collect it within 25 years, after which her silence is construed as *mechila*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתָה — יוֹרְשֶׁיהָ מַזְכִּירִין כְּתוּבָּתָהּ עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **she died, her heirs mention her marriage contract** up **until twenty-five years** later.

### 25-Year Theory

Rabbi Meir's position rests on an accounting theory: *she-yeish be-esrim ve-chamesh shanim she-ta'aseh tovah keneged kesuvasa* — over the course of 25 years, the small incremental benefits she has received from the estate — gifts here, use of funds there, various informal advantages — will have cumulatively added up to the total value of her *ketubah*, even if none of it was formally designated as payment.

### Embarrassment Factor

The Sages' inverse ruling — that a widow in her husband's house never loses her claim through silence — rests on a different logic entirely. When she is living among the heirs, being housed and fed by them, and she has not demanded payment, it is not because she has forgiven the debt. Rather, she is simply embarrassed to ask. She cannot easily turn to the heirs who are actively supporting her and demand: now pay me my *ketubah*. Her silence is therefore not *mechila* but *kissufa* — embarrassment. When she is in her father's house and entirely separate from the heirs, however, 25 years of silence is interpreted as genuine waiver.

### Heirs Continue

*Meisa* — if the widow dies within the 25-year period, *yoresheha mazkirim kesuvasa ad esrim ve-chamesh shanim* — her heirs continue the count. If she died after 10 years, her heirs have the remaining 15 years to pursue the claim. After those 15 years, the silence of her heirs is similarly treated as *mechila*.

## Abaye Questions

### Poor Woman Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: עֲנִיָּיה שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים, וּמָרְתָּא בַּת בַּיְיתּוֹס עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna stated that according to Rabbi Meir, over a period of twenty-five years a woman will spend a sum equal to her marriage contract from the resources of the orphans. **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** Is it true that **the poorest** woman **among the Jewish people,** whose marriage contract is of minimal value, will not spend this amount **until twenty-five years** have passed, **and Marta bat Baitos,** who was very wealthy and whose marriage contract was worth a huge sum, will also spend a sum equal to her marriage contract **within twenty-five years?**

The *gemara* now examines Rabbi Meir's theory. *Amar lei Abaye le-Rav Yosef*: consider *aniya she-beYisrael* — the poorest woman in Israel, whose *ketubah* is the bare minimum. Rabbi Meir says that over 25 years she will have received little benefits adding up to the *ketubah* value. That is at least conceivable with a small amount. But what of the 25-year rule on its own terms for such a woman?

### Wealthy Woman Case

And on the other end: *Marta bat Baisos* — the enormously wealthy woman known from elsewhere in the tractate — whose *ketubah* must have been worth an enormous sum. How can 25 years of small informal benefits possibly add up to the total of her *ketubah*?

### Camel Burden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפוּם גַּמְלָא שִׁיחְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: According to the camel is the load,** i.e., a wealthy woman, whose marriage contract is of greater value, will spend more money over a particular period of time than a poor woman, whose marriage contract is of lesser value.

*Amar lei: lifum gamla shichna* — the burden is proportional to the camel. The benefits a wealthy woman receives living in her late husband's household are not a few pennies here and there. She might buy herself expensive jewelry, attend costly entertainments, make use of resources on a scale commensurate with her station. The informal benefits scale with the wealth of the estate, and therefore they add up to the *ketubah* amount within 25 years even for a woman of great wealth.

### Pro-Rata Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מַהוּ שֶׁתְּשַׁלֵּשׁ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **According to Rabbi Meir,** the amount of benefit she gains is determined by the years that have passed. If so, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **she divides** the value of her marriage contract in accordance with the number of years that have gone by, such that if some of the twenty-five years passed, she forfeits the proportionate value of her marriage contract? No answer was found for this dilemma, and the Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

*Iba'u lehu* — a question is then raised: *le-Rabbi Meir, mahu she-titshales?* — does Rabbi Meir's theory allow for pro-rating the *ketubah*? If the *ketubah* is paid off gradually through small benefits accrued over time, ought one not assume that after, say, 12 and a half years, half the *ketubah* has already been effectively paid off — such that a claim brought at that point would only be for the remaining half? The question is left unresolved.

## Sages' Theory

### Waiver Model

The Sages' position, by contrast, has nothing to do with gradual payment. Their theory is not that small benefits are credited against the *ketubah*, but rather that 25 years of complete silence constitutes *mechila* — a waiver. If she has not demanded her *ketubah* in 25 years, she has implicitly relinquished it.

### Sunset Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל זְמַן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: אֲתַאי קוֹדֶם שְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה — גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, לְאַחַר שְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה לָא גָּבְיָא? בְּהַהִיא פּוּרְתָּא אַחֵילְתַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § We learned in the mishna: **And the Rabbis say: As long as** she is in her husband's house she may collect payment of her marriage contract at any time, but while she is in her father's house she may collect it only within twenty-five years. **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** If **she came before the setting of the sun** at the end of the twenty-five-year period, **she collects** payment of **her marriage contract,** but if she came **after the setting of the sun she may not collect** it? **In that slight** period of time **did she waive** her rights to the payment of her marriage contract?

*Amar lei Abaye le-Rav Yosef*: if the widow comes to collect her *ketubah* on the last day of the 25-year period — *atai kodem shekias hachama*, she comes before sunset — she collects her full *ketubah*. *Ve-achar shekias hachama*, one minute after sunset — *lo gavya*, she receives nothing? Does that one brief moment of additional silence transform her waiver?

### Defining Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, כׇּל מִדַּת חֲכָמִים, כֵּן הִיא. בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה — טוֹבֵל, בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה חָסֵר קוּרְטוֹב — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִטְבּוֹל בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: Yes. All the measures of the Sages** that prescribe specific parameters or sizes **are such** that if one oversteps the fixed limits, he has not accomplished anything as far as the *halakha* is considered. Consequently, **in** a ritual bath containing **forty *se'a*** of water, **one may immerse** and become ritually pure. However, **in** a ritual bath containing **forty *se'a* less** one ***kortov*,** a small amount, **he is unable to immerse therein** and become ritually pure.

*Amar lei: in. Kol middos chachamim kein hu* — yes, this is the nature of the Sages' defined measures. One must draw a line somewhere. *B'arba'im se'ah tovel* — with 40 *se'ah* of water one may immerse in the *mikvah*. *B'arba'im se'ah chasar kurtov, eino yachol litbol bahen* — if even a minuscule amount is missing, the immersion is invalid. Defined thresholds require defined boundaries; that is the nature of rabbinic legislation.

## Document Status

### Document Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הֵעִיד רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדֶיהָ, אֲבָל שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדֶיהָ — גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לְעוֹלָם. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדֶיהָ — אֵינָהּ גּוֹבָה אֶלָּא עַד עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, testified before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi and **said in the name of his father,** Rabbi Yosei: **They taught** all of the above **only** in a case where **she does not have a marriage contract in her possession,** such as in a locale where the custom is not to write a marriage contract, **but** in a situation **where she does have a marriage contract in her possession, she may collect** payment of **her marriage contract forever. And Rabbi Elazar said: Even if she has a marriage contract in her possession, she** still **collects** payment of her marriage contract **only within twenty-five years** after the death of her husband.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi testified before Rebbe, citing his father's ruling — *lo shanu ela she-ein shtar ketubah yotzei mitachas yadah* — when is *mechila* assumed after 25 years? Only when she is not holding onto the *ketubah* document. If she has no written document, one might explain her silence as passive neglect of a verbal obligation. But if she holds the *shtar*, she has no need to be pressing for collection — the document is there whenever she wishes to use it. One cannot construe her silence as *mechila* in such circumstances. *Aval shtar ketubah yotzei mitachat yadah, gova kesubatah l'olam* — if she is holding the *shtar*, she may always collect.

### Document Irrelevant

Rabbi Elazar disagrees: *afilu shtar ketubah yotzei mitachas yadah, eino govei ela ad esrim ve-chamesh shanim* — even if she holds the *ketubah* document, she still only has 25 years. The document does not insulate her from the waiver rule.

### Creditor Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַזְכָּרָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי, אִי דְּלָא נָקֵט שְׁטָרָא — בְּמַאי גָּבֵי? אֶלָּא דְּנָקֵיט שְׁטָרָא: וּבַעַל חוֹב הוּא דְּלָאו בַּר אַחוֹלֵי הוּא, הָא אַלְמָנָה אַחֵילְתַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet raised an objection** against the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, based upon the *Tosefta* (*Ketubot* 12:3): **A creditor may collect** the money he is owed even after a long time has passed **without** his having **mentioned** the debt. The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If he does not hold the document** that records the debt, **with what is he collecting** the debt? **Rather,** it must be that **he does hold the document.** It can be inferred that even so, it is specifically **a creditor, who** it could be assumed **is not one** to have **forgiven** his debt, who may continue to collect the debt after a long period of time. **But a widow** is presumed to have **waived** her rights to the payment of her marriage contract even if she has the marriage contract in her possession. This conclusion contradicts the statement of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei.

*Masiv Rav Sheshes*: the *gemara* objects from a principle concerning ordinary creditors. *Ba'al chov gova she-lo be-hazkarah* — a creditor can collect at any time without ever needing to announce his intent to collect. We never assume a regular creditor has been *mochel* his debt through silence. If so, why would we treat a widow differently? *Heichi dami?* — what is the case of the *ba'al chov*? If he holds no *shtar*, how does he collect? It must be that he holds a *shtar*. And we say the *ba'al chov* with a *shtar* never waives — yet for the widow, even holding a *shtar*, we say she waives after 25 years? That seems inconsistent.

### Admission Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ: לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא נָקֵיט שְׁטָרָא, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּשֶׁחַיָּיב מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states that Rav Sheshet **raised the objection and he resolved it: Actually,** the case in the *Tosefta* is where the creditor **does not hold a document** that records the debt, **and** the reason he may collect the debt is because **here we are dealing with** a case **where the debtor admits** that he owes the creditor money. Consequently, it cannot be proven from this case that a widow who has a marriage contract in her possession is unable to collect its payment.

The *gemara* answers: *hu masiv lah ve-hu m'farek lah* — he who raised the difficulty resolves it. The comparison breaks down because the cases are not identical. The widow case can be constructed without a *shtar*, because a widow does not need a document to prove her debt — *tenai beis din*, the condition is established by the marriage itself; she need only prove the death of her husband. A regular creditor without a *shtar* generally cannot prove his claim. The comparison must therefore be on equal terms: a *ba'al chov* in a case where the debtor admits the debt — *d'chayiv modeh* — would similarly not require a *shtar*, and in such a case we may well be comparing two parallel scenarios.

### Debate Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא, שׁוֹנִין: גְּרוּשָׁה הֲרֵי הִיא כְּבַעַל חוֹב. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא נְקִיטָא כְּתוּבָּה, בְּמַאי גָּבְיָא? אֶלָּא לָאו דִּנְקִיטָא כְּתוּבָּה, וּגְרוּשָׁה הִיא דְּלָאו בַּת אַחוֹלֵי הִיא, הָא אַלְמָנָה אַחֵילְתַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rabbi Ela say:** The Sages **teach** in a *baraita*: **A divorcée is like a creditor** and may collect her marriage contract after a long period of time even if she has not made mention of it during the course of that time? The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If she does not hold a marriage contract** in her possession, **with what is she collecting** payment? **Rather, is it not** that **she holds a marriage contract** in her possession, **and it is a divorcée** who may collect under these circumstances, **as she is not one** who could be assumed to have **waived** the rights to the payment owed to her, as she does not maintain a relationship with the family that would prompt her to waive the rights to her claims? **But a widow** is likely to **waive** the rights to her claim, even though she is in possession of a contract.

*Ve-amar Rabbi Ila shonin gerusha harei hi ke-ba'al chov* — Rabbi Ila teaches that a divorced woman is treated like a *ba'al chov*, because a *gerusha* would never be *mocheles* her *ketubah*; the relationship was by definition adversarial, and forgiveness of the debt on her part would be most unlikely. The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami?* — if she holds no *ketubah*, how is she collecting? She must be holding one. And we say about the *gerusha* holding her *ketubah* that she is never *mocheles*. Does it follow that a widow holding her *shtar* is, by contrast, *mocheles*? The *gemara* again insists: *hacha nami keshechayav modeh* — we are speaking of a case where the other party admits the debt; one can construct a case without a *shtar* for a divorced woman just as for any creditor. The conclusion toward which the *gemara* is moving is that *mechila* by an *almanah* applies only in the absence of a *shtar*, and that possessing the document insulates her from any waiver claim.

## Later Resolution

### Reset on Demand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: תָּנֵי רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר קָזָא בְּמַתְנִיתָא דְּבֵי בַּר קָזָא: תָּבְעָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ — הֲרֵי הִיא כְּבַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Rav Yehuda bar Kaza teaches in a *baraita* of the school** of **bar Kaza:** If the widow **demanded** payment of **her marriage contract,** it is **as though she is at the beginning** of her period of widowhood, and she has another twenty-five years from that point during which she may demand payment of her marriage contract.

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak tani Rav Yehuda bar Kaza be-matnisa d'bei bar Kaza* — Rabbi Yehuda bar Kaza teaches in the *baraisos* of *Bei bar Kaza*: *tav'ah kesuvasa, harei hi ke-vatchila* — if she has formally demanded her *ketubah*, the clock begins again. Once she comes before a court and registers her intent to collect, she has another 25 years before any further silence would be construed as *mechila*. She need not actually collect the *ketubah* within those 25 years — she only needs to have made her intention known, and then the 25-year period recommences from that demand.

### Document Protects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיָה שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדֶיהָ — גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And if she has a marriage contract in her possession, she may collect** payment of **her marriage contract forever.**

*Ve-im hayta ketubah yotzeis mitachat yadah, gova kesuvasa le'olam* — and if she is holding the *shtar ketubah*, she may collect forever, without any 25-year limitation.

### No Document Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יַעֲקֹב: יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, כְּשֶׁשְּׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדָהּ מַחְלוֹקֶת, אוֹ כְּשֶׁאֵין שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדָהּ? וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי מִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Naḥman, son of Rav Ḥisda, sent** the following message **to Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov: Our teacher, instruct us.** Does the **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis apply in a case **where she has a marriage contract in her possession, or** does it apply only in a case **where she does not have a marriage contract in her possession? And in accordance with the statement of whom is the *halakha*** decided?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: בְּשֶׁאֵין שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדָהּ מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדָהּ — גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לְעוֹלָם. וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov **sent** back this answer **to him: When she does not have a marriage contract in her possession,** there is **a dispute,** but in a case **where she has a marriage contract in her possession,** all agree that **she may collect** payment of **her marriage contract forever.** And in a case where there is a dispute, the ***halakha* is in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis.**

A question sent from Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda to Rav Nachman bar Yaakov clarifies the structure of the debates: *keshtar ketubah yotzei mitachat yadah machloket, o ke-she-ein shtar ketubah yotzei mitachat yadah?* — is the debate between Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Elazar taking place when she is holding the *shtar*, or when she is not? *Shelach lei*: the response was *be-she-ein shtar ketubah yotzei mitachat yadah machloket* — the debate concerns the case where she is not holding a *shtar*. When she holds the *shtar*, everyone agrees she may collect forever. *Ve-halacha ke-divrei chachamim* — and the *halacha* follows the Sages, meaning that when she is in her father's house without a *shtar* and has been silent for 25 years, *mechila* is assumed, but when she is in her husband's household, her silence is never interpreted as *mechila* — the *kissufa* factor prevails and she retains her right to collect regardless.

## Base vs. Addition

### Base Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מָנֶה מָאתַיִם, אֲבָל תּוֹסֶפֶת — יֵשׁ לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he cited a dispute: **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said in the name of bar Kappara: They taught** that a widow is presumed to have waived her rights to payment of her marriage contract after twenty-five years **only** with regard to **one hundred dinars** or **two hundred dinars,** which constitute the principal payment of the marriage contract. **However, she** still **has** the right to demand payment of **the additional** sum recorded in the marriage contract.

*Ki ata Rav Dimi*: when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he transmitted a ruling in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, in the name of Bar Kappara: *lo shanu ela maneh matayim* — the entire framework of *mechila* after 25 years applies only to the base *ketubah* amounts of 100 and 200 *dinars*. *Aval tosefet yesh lah* — but the *tosefet*, the additional amounts voluntarily added by the husband, she retains in any event. The reasoning is that the *tosefet* is in the nature of a gift, and one does not assume *mechila* in the context of a gift the way one might in the context of a standard marital obligation.

### Tosafet Waiver

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ תּוֹסֶפֶת אֵין לָהּ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַיְיבוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: תְּנַאי כְּתוּבָּה כִּכְתוּבָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said: **She does not have** the right to demand payment **even of the additional** sum recorded in the marriage contract, **as Rabbi Aivu said** that **Rabbi Yannai said: The stipulation** of an additional sum **in the marriage contract is like** the principal sum of **the marriage contract.** Consequently, if she waived her rights to the principal sum of the marriage contract, she has waived her rights to the additional sum as well.

Rabbi Abbahu, however, citing Rabbi Yochanan, rules otherwise: *afilu tosefet ein lah* — even the *tosefet* is forfeited after 25 years. The support for this position comes from Rabbi Aibu citing Rabbi Yannai: *tnai ketubah ke-ketubah dami* — the conditions and additions of the *ketubah* are treated as equivalent to the base *ketubah* itself; the 25-year waiver rule applies to the whole.

### Abbahu View

The dispute between these positions thus turns on whether the *tosefet* retains the character of a gift — and is therefore never subject to *mechila* — or whether, once incorporated into the *ketubah* structure, it is treated identically to the base amount for all purposes including the 25-year waiver rule.

### Tosafet Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מָנֶה מָאתַיִם, אֲבָל תּוֹסֶפֶת יֵשׁ לָהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב הוּנָא: אֲמַר רַב הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתִּיק(ן) קָאָמְרַתְּ, אוֹ אַשְׁקְיַין קָאָמְרַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתִּיק(ן) קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** It **was also stated** that other *amora'im* debated this matter: **Rabbi Abba said** that **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: They taught** that she is considered as having waived her rights to payment of her marriage contract **only** with regard to the principal payment of **one hundred** or **two hundred dinars, but she** still **has** the right to demand payment of **the additional** sum recorded in the marriage contract. **Rabbi Abba said to Rav Huna:** Did **Rav** really **say so?** Rav Huna **said to** Rabbi Abba: **Did you say** that in order to **silence me,** because you disagree with this ruling? **Or did you say** that because you are so satisfied with this ruling that you would like to **give me** wine to **drink?** Rabbi Abba **said to him: I said** that in order to **silence** you. This indicates that Rabbi Abba disagrees with Rav and accepts the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan on this issue.

*Itmar nami*: Rabbi Abba citing Rav Huna citing Rav rules that only the base 100 and 200 are subject to the waiver — the *tosefet* she retains. Rabbi Abba then challenged Rav Huna: *amar Rav hachi?* — did Rav really say this? Rav Huna replied: *ashtukin kaamart o ashkayin kaamart?* — when you challenge whether Rav said this, are you trying to silence me (*ashtukin*), meaning you disagree, or would you like to raise a drink to this ruling (*ashkayin*), meaning you approve and want to be sure of its attribution? *Amar lei: ashtukin kamina* — I want to silence you, Rav Huna replied; he did not accept the ruling as stated.

## Case Study

### Chiya Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמָתֵיהּ דְּרַב חִיָּיא אֲרִיכָא אִינְתַּת אֲחוּהּ הֲוַאי, וְאַלְמָנָה בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ הֲוַאי, וְזָנַהּ עֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמֵשׁ שְׁנִין בְּבֵי נָשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates that **the mother-in-law of Rav Ḥiyya Arikha,** so named because of his height, as the word *arikha* literally means long, **was** also **the wife of his brother, and she was a widow** who resided **in the house of her father, and** Rav Ḥiyya **sustained her** for **twenty-five years in the house of her father,** from his brother's estate.

The *gemara* now presents a concrete case illustrating these principles. The mother-in-law of Rabbi Chiya Aricha — a different Rabbi Chiya — was the widow of his brother, who had died leaving Rabbi Chiya Aricha as the surviving heir and thus responsible for the estate. She had been living in her father's house — *be-vei nasha* — rather than in her deceased husband's household. *Ve-zana esrim ve-chamisha shanim* — Rabbi Chiya Aricha supported her for 25 years. He brought her food on his own shoulder, by his own account, taking great personal care of her provision.

### Heir Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: הַב לִי מְזוֹנֵי. אֲמַר לַהּ: לֵית לִיךְ מְזוֹנֵי. הַב לִי כְּתוּבָּה. אֲמַר לַהּ: לָא מְזוֹנֵי אִית לִיךְ, וְלָא כְּתוּבָּה אִית.
>
> **Translation:** **At the end** of the twenty-five years, **she said to him: Give me** my **sustenance. He said to her: You do not have** the right to continue to demand **sustenance.** She said to him: In that case, **give me** the payment of my **marriage contract. He said to her:** After twenty-five years, **you have no** right to demand **sustenance and you have no** right to demand payment of your **marriage contract.**

After 25 years she said to him: *hav li mezonei* — continue to pay my food bill. He replied: *leis lach mezonei* — you have no more right to *mezonot*, the 25-year period has elapsed. She then said: *hav li ktuvah* — give me my *ketubah*. He replied: *lo mezonei is lach ve-lo ktuvah is lach* — you have neither *mezonot* nor *ketubah*; you have waived everything.

### Judge Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּבַעְתֵּיהּ לְדִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לִי אִיזִי גּוּפָא דְעוֹבָדָא הֵיכִי הֲוָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זָנִיתַהּ עֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמֵשׁ שָׁנִים בְּבֵי נָשָׁא, בְּחַיֵּי דְמָר, דִּבְכַתְפַאי אַמְטַאי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **She summoned him for judgment before Rabba bar Sheila. He said to** Rav Ḥiyya Arikha: **Tell me, then, what was the essence of the case?** Rav Ḥiyya Arikha **said to him: I sustained her** for **twenty-five years in the house of her father,** and I swear **by the life of the Master,** i.e., by your life, **that I delivered** her sustenance **to her** regularly **on my** own **shoulders.**

*Taviesay le-dina kamai d'Rabba bar Shila* — she brought him to judgment before Rabba bar Shila. The judge asked him to describe exactly what had occurred. Rabbi Chiya Aricha recounted that he had supported her for 25 years while she lived in her father's house, personally delivering the food with great care and dedication. Rabba bar Shila said: and this is precisely the point. *D'amrinan mishum kissufa hu d'lo tavah* — the reason she did not demand payment in 25 years is that she was *embarrassed* to ask, because you took such excellent care of her. The Sages' rule that living in the husband's household suspends *mechila* due to *kissufa* applies here as well: when a creditor is so attentive and generous that the widow feels she cannot press a demand, her silence is not waiver but *kissufa*.

### Payment Ordered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא מַאי אֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהִיא בְּבֵית בַּעְלָהּ גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ לְעוֹלָם, דְּאָמְרִינַן: מִשּׁוּם כִּיסּוּפָא הוּא דְּלָא תָּבְעָה. הָכָה נָמֵי: מִשּׁוּם כִּיסּוּפָא הוּא דְּלָא תָּבְעָה, זִיל הַב לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Sheila **said to him: What is the reason** that **the Sages said** that **as long as** the widow **is in the house of her husband, she may always collect** payment of **her marriage contract?** It is **because we say** that **it is due to embarrassment that she did not demand** payment of her marriage contract, because she is in her husband's house and his heirs are treating her well. **Here too,** in this case, **it is due to embarrassment that she did not demand** payment of her marriage contract, as you treated her with great respect despite the fact that she was living in her father's house. Therefore, **go** and **give her** the payment of her marriage contract.

*Zil havlah* — go pay her. The judge ruled that the silence was not *mechila* and ordered continued payment.

### Escalation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא אַשְׁגַּח. כְּתַב לַהּ אַדְרָכְתָּא אַנִּיכְסֵיה. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי מָר הֵיכִי דָּנַן! אָמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר דָּנָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥiyya Arikha **did not heed** the ruling of Rabba bar Sheila and did not give her the payment of her marriage contract. Rabba bar Sheila **wrote an authorization for her to** seize **his property** in payment of the debt. Rav Ḥiyya Arikha **came before Rava,** and **he said to him: Let the Master see how** Rabba bar Sheila has **judged me.** Rava **said to him: He has judged you well.**

*Ve-la ashgach* — he did not comply. *Katav lah adrachta anichsei* — the court then issued a written seizure order against his property, empowering her to collect from it. The case then came before Rava.

## Seizure Dispute

### Produce Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי, לֵיזִיל לַהְדַּר לִי פֵּירֵי דְּמִן הָהוּא יוֹמָא עַד הָאִידָּנָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: אַחְוִי לִי אַדְרָכְתִּיךְ. חַזְיַיהּ דְּלָא הֲוָה כְּתִוב בָּהּ ״וְאִישְׁתְּמוֹדַעְנָא דִּנְכָסִים אֵלּוּ דְּמִיתָנָא אִינּוּן״. אֲמַר לַהּ: אַדְרָכְתָּא לָאו שַׁפִּיר כְּתִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The woman **said to** Rava: **If so, he should go** and **return to me** the **produce** that has grown on the property that I have a right to receive as payment, **from that day** that I received authorization to seize his property **until today. He said to her: Show me your** document of **authorization. He saw that it was not written in it: And it is known to us that these properties are** from the estate **of the deceased. Rava said to her: The authorization is not written well.** Consequently, the property is not considered as though it were yours from the time that the authorization was written, and you do not have a right to the produce.

When the case came before Rava, Rabbi Chiya Aricha complained: *chazi mar heichi danyin* — do you see how they judged me? They issued a seizure of my property. Rava replied: *shappir duncha* — that was a sound judgment; you must continue to pay her.

The widow then pressed further. Since she now held the seizure document, she claimed: *neizil ve-neihadar lei peira mi-hahu yoma ad idna* — let him return to me the produce of that property from the time the seizure document was written until now. She had been entitled to that property, she argued, and he had withheld it; she should be compensated for the produce generated from it during that period.

*Amar lah: achvi li adrachtich* — Rava said, show me the document. *Ve-lo hava katav bah: ve-ishtemodana de-nichsei de-misna inun* — and he found that the document did not contain the specification: and it is known to us that these properties belong to the deceased. A proper seizure order in this context must identify the specific property the widow has a right to seize — namely, the property that had belonged to her dead husband. The document lacked this specification.

### Document Defect

*Adrachta la shappir ktiva* — the seizure document was not properly written, Rava ruled, because without proper identification, the widow might go and seize the wrong property entirely. If she were to seize the heir's own property rather than her deceased husband's estate, she might improve it, and meanwhile her deceased husband's property — the one she actually has rights to — might depreciate. Later, the heir would come to court and say: take your property and give me back mine. This creates potential for significant confusion and loss.

### Wrong Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: תֵּיזִיל אַדְרַכְתָּא, אֶישְׁקוֹל מִיּוֹמָא דִּשְׁלִימִי יוֹמֵי אַכְרָזְתָּא עַד הַשְׁתָּא. אֲמַר לַהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא כְּתִיב טָעוּתָא בְּאַדְרָכְתָּא, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דִּכְתִיב טָעוּתָא בְּאַדְרָכְתָּא — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **She said to him: Let the authorization go,** i.e., even if I have no right to the produce that grew from the time the authorization was written, **I should** have the right to **take** the produce that grew **from the time when the days of announcement were completed,** after the court assessed the value of the property, **until now. He said to her: This applies** only in a case **where there was no error written into the authorization, but where there was an error written into the authorization, we have no** right to collect the debt **with it.** You therefore have no rights to any of the produce.

Rava's ruling was therefore precise: she is entitled to seize and collect from her deceased husband's property for her support, not from the heir's own property. The defect in the seizure document, which failed to identify which property was subject to seizure, rendered the document invalid and prevented her from claiming produce from the time of its writing.

### Final Property Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר אַחְרָיוּת טָעוּת סוֹפֵר הוּא. אֲמַר לַהּ רָבָא: בְּהָא לֵיכָּא לְמֵימַר טָעוּת סוֹפֵר הוּא, דִּבְהָא אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא טָעֵי. מֵעִיקָּרָא הוּא סְבַר הָנֵי וְהָנֵי דִּידֵיהּ, מָה לִי מֵהָנֵי, מָה לִי מֵהָנֵי, וְלָא הִיא. זִימְנִין דְּאָזְלָה וּמַשְׁבְּחָה לְהוּ וּדְבַעְלָהּ מַכְסְפִי, וְאָמַר לַהּ: שְׁקוּל דִּידָךְ וְהַב לִי דִּידִי, וְאָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי לַעַז עַל בֵּי דִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The widow then attempted a fallback position: even if not from the time of the document, she argued she should receive produce from the time the auction process had concluded and property had been assigned, prior to actual written seizure. **She said to him: But wasn't it** you, **Master, who said** that omission of the **guarantee** of the sale from the document **is a scribal error,** and it is considered as though the guarantee were written in the document? Here too, say that the omission of the above clause is regarded as a scribal error and is considered as though it were written in the authorization.
>
> **Rava said to her: In this** case, **it cannot be said** that the clause was meant to be included in the document and **it** was left out due to a **scribal error, because in this** case **even Rabba bar Sheila erred** and thought that the clause should not be included. **Initially,** Rabba bar Sheila **thought** as follows: Since **these** properties that always belonged to Rav Ḥiyya Arikha **and those** properties that had belonged to his deceased brother **are** all **his,** i.e., Rav Ḥiyya Arikha's, as he inherited his brother's property, **what** difference does it make **to me** if she collects **from these** properties, and **what** difference does it make **to me** if she collects **from those** properties? Although only the property of her deceased husband is liened for the payment of her marriage contract, it should not really matter whether she collects from this property or from other property belonging to the heir. **But that is not so. Sometimes** the widow **will go and improve** the property of the heir, thinking that she will receive her payment from it, **and** the property **of her** deceased **husband** will **depreciate** due to neglect on the part of the heir, who knows that it is this property that is liened to ensure payment of the widow's marriage contract. **And** eventually, the heir will **say to her: Take your** property, i.e., the property that belonged to your husband, **and give me my** property. **And** people **will come to cast aspersion on the court** as not being concerned for the welfare of the woman, who will be left with the depreciated property. Consequently, the document of authorization to seize property must specify exactly which property belonged to the widow's deceased husband, which she is entitled to collect. Since Rabba bar Sheila erred and did not write this specification, the document of authorization he wrote was useless.

The widow then attempted a fallback position: even if not from the time of the document, she argued she should receive produce from the time the auction process had concluded and property had been assigned, prior to actual written seizure. *Amar lah: hani milei heicha de-lo ktiv tauta be-adrachta* — Rava ruled that this argument would hold only where there was no error in the seizure document itself. *Aval heicha de-ktiv tauta be-adrachta, leis lan bah* — where there is an error in the document, the entire process is voided, and actual possession is required before any rights in the produce can be claimed.

The heir argued that Rava himself held that *achrayut ta'us sofer hu* — an omission in a document is treated as a mere scribal error and does not invalidate the fundamental transaction. Rava responded: *beha lo matzi lemeimar ta'us sofer hu* — in this case, that principle cannot apply, *deha chazina de-veshivna ta'ei* — because this was a genuine error in judgment, not a scribal oversight. *Me'ikara hu savar ha ve-ha didei hu* — the court officer who drafted the document actually believed that all the property belonged to the heir, making it immaterial from which specific portion the widow collected. *Mai mehai u-mai mehai?* That was an error: she is not entitled to any of the heir's own property. *Zimnin de-azil u-meshabbach lehu ve-havu lehu nichsei* — if she were to take the heir's property, she might improve it, while her deceased husband's property simultaneously depreciates, and then the heir would come and demand: take yours and return mine — a result that would cast disrepute on the *beis din*. She is therefore limited to the property of her deceased husband for purposes of support collection, and the technical defects in the seizure document preclude any claim to produce from an earlier point.

Notwithstanding all these technical complexities, however, Rava's fundamental ruling that the heir must continue paying her support stands — the defects in the seizure document speak to which property may be seized, not to whether the underlying obligation to pay exists.

*Chazak u-varuch.*
