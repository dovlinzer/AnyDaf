# Hullin 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ta'am vs. Shishim

### Gid Hanashe & Ta'am

To situate today's daf properly, it is worth summarizing the central conceptual terrain covered on the previous page, since it underlies everything that follows. The *mishna* opened with a discussion of the prohibition of *gid hanashe* in the context of cooking, and the principle that emerged from it was *ta'am ke'ikar* — taste is legally equivalent to the thing itself. If a forbidden substance imparts its taste to a permissible mixture, the mixture is prohibited. This principle is broadly accepted as biblical in force, such that the forbidden taste carries the same legal weight as the forbidden substance from which it derived.

### Methods of Transfer

The *Gemara* then explored the mechanisms by which taste is transferred. The *mishna* had mentioned cooking, and Shmuel added three further modes: roasting, salting, and pickling. Roasting, in Shmuel's view, does not transfer taste throughout the entire dish; Tosafot and other *rishonim* clarify that it does transfer taste to a thin surface layer immediately adjacent to the roasted item — though it does not spread further unless the item is particularly fatty, which raises its own practical questions. Salting, Shmuel says, operates like roasting — it transfers taste, but only to a limited degree, akin to direct heat without liquid. Pickling, by contrast, involves liquid and therefore functions like cooking, transferring taste throughout.

### Two Models

The deeper question the *Gemara* addressed is how one determines whether taste has been imparted when two substances are cooked together. The *mishna* itself mentioned taste as the criterion, and offered a conceptual model: imagine the forbidden item were meat and the permissible mixture were turnips — would the taste spread? Rabbi Yochanan introduced a further model, using onions as a stand-in for the forbidden substance on the grounds that onions have a particularly penetrating flavor, making this approach inherently stringent. Alongside this experiential, taste-based approach stands a second model: the quantitative rule of *shishim* — sixty. If the permissible portion is sixty times the volume of the forbidden substance, no concern of taste impartation remains. This serves as a universal, calculable substitute for the otherwise contextual and subjective determination of taste.

### Non-Jewish Taster

Rabbi Yochanan introduced a third approach: when the forbidden substance cannot be tasted directly — either because it is non-kosher or because one cannot identify its flavor — a non-Jewish taster may be relied upon. Later *poskim* debated the precise conditions under which such reliance is valid, including whether the taster's professional reputation must be at stake or whether it matters if they are aware their judgment is being used halachically. Rava then demonstrated how all three approaches may be integrated. If the admixture involves a kosher substance, a Jew may taste it directly. If it involves a non-kosher substance, a non-Jewish taster may be employed. And when neither option is available — or when the nature of the mixture makes taste determination impossible — one falls back on the measure of *shishim*.

### Sephardic vs. Ashkenazi

This integration of approaches reflects the Sephardic ruling, which follows the *Gemara's* graduated logic: the taste of a Jew for *heter*, the taste of a non-Jew for *issur*, and *shishim* as the fallback when tasting is unavailable. Ashkenazic practice, by contrast, largely dispenses with contextual taste determinations and applies *shishim* as the standard rule in virtually all cases, though in principle a Jew's tasting of a permissible substance remains valid even according to Ashkenazim. The practical distinction between these approaches has less to do with *how* one measures and more to do with the question of whether a given prohibition is *de'oraita* or *derabanan* in character — a point that will become significant below.

## Eggs & Taste

### Eggs & 60

The *Gemara* now turns to the case of non-kosher eggs mixed with kosher eggs. The principle stated is: *beitza beshishim* — an egg requires sixty times its volume to be nullified — and the egg itself does not count toward that sixty. This latter point is simply the standard rule: one always requires sixty against the forbidden item, and the item itself is not part of the count.

### Hard Shells Argument

Rav Idi bar Avin poses a challenge to Abaye: the assumption that an egg imparts taste when cooked with other eggs seems questionable on its face. Eggs have hard shells that would seem to block the transfer of flavor, and the *Gemara* invokes a common expression — *k'maya d'bei'ei b'alma*, "like mere egg water" — used precisely to describe something utterly flavorless. The expression itself is evidence that the liquid produced by cooking eggs was understood to convey almost no taste whatsoever. So why should a non-kosher egg cooked with kosher eggs be a concern?

### Chick Exception

The *Gemara* resolves this by specifying the case: *bebeitat efroach* — we are dealing with an egg that contains a developing chick. Such an egg is prohibited not merely because it derives from a non-kosher species but because it contains what is effectively an unhatched living creature, with a status akin to *neveilah*. That kind of egg does give off taste. By contrast, an ordinary egg from a non-kosher bird — without any developing chick — does not give off taste and would not be a concern. The *Gemara* accordingly transitions to discussing a *baraita* that describes mixing kosher and non-kosher eggs.

### Baraita Clarification

The *baraita* states that if one boiled kosher eggs together with non-kosher eggs, and the non-kosher eggs impart taste, the entire mixture is forbidden. A challenge arises: if this first clause refers to eggs from non-kosher species rather than eggs containing chicks, and such eggs can apparently impart taste, then the second clause of the *baraita* — which introduces the case of eggs found to contain a chick — would be entirely redundant.

The *Gemara* first attempts to resolve this by saying the *baraita* is not presenting two distinct cases but one case explained in two parts: the second clause clarifies what "non-kosher egg" means in the first — namely, an egg with a chick inside. The first clause describes the prohibition; the second tells you what category of egg produces it.

However, a counter-reading is raised: the more natural reading of the *baraita* is that it presents two separate cases. The first case refers to an egg from a non-kosher bird (without a chick), and the second introduces the case of a chick specifically, so that you know the first case is distinct from it — and yet even the first case, the egg from a non-kosher bird, could in theory impart taste and be a concern. The *Gemara* ultimately leans on real-world knowledge to resist this reading. Given that normal eggs, even from non-kosher species, do not meaningfully impart taste, the *Gemara* is willing to read the *baraita* as presenting a single case: only an egg containing a chick is a genuine concern. An egg from a non-kosher bird, absent a chick, does not give off taste and would not render the mixture forbidden.

### Absorption Question

A further question — raised in the course of this discussion — is whether the hard shell of an egg also prevents it from *absorbing* taste from a forbidden medium, not merely from imparting it. The *Gemara's* discussion here addresses the "giving off" side, not the "absorbing" side. Yet the *Gemara* does appear to assume that eggs are capable of absorbing taste — that the shell does not fully seal the interior against external flavors. Whether eggs absorb through the shell when cooked in a non-kosher vessel remains a live practical question, and one that the *Gemara* here does not definitively resolve. What is clear from this passage is that the leniency regarding eggs is specifically about their not imparting taste outward, not about their being impervious to absorbing taste inward.

## Fat in Meat

### Pot Absorption

The *Gemara* introduces a new scenario: a *kezayit* of forbidden fat (*chelev*) fell into a pot of meat. Rav Ashi considered being lenient by measuring not the current contents of the pot against the fat, but rather including what the pot had absorbed over the time since the fat fell in. His reasoning: by the time the question was brought before him — perhaps an hour after the fact — some of the meat had been absorbed into the walls of the pot, and if one could credit that absorbed meat toward the sixty, the ratio might become sufficient to nullify the fat.

### Proportional Absorption

The rabbis rejected this reasoning. Their objection is precise: just as the pot absorbed some of the permissible meat over that hour, it likewise absorbed some of the forbidden fat proportionally. The ratio of permissible to forbidden in the pot's walls mirrors the ratio in the pot itself at any given moment. Therefore, one cannot artificially increase the count of the permissible by adding what the pot absorbed, because the pot absorbed from both sides equally. The proper approach is to assess the current proportions in the pot, which reflect the original proportions when the fat first fell in. There is no basis for a leniency here.

## Half K'Zayit

### Thirty Halves Proposal

A related but distinct scenario: half a *kezayit* of forbidden fat fell into a pot of meat. Mar bar Rav Ashi proposed that in this case, thirty half-*zayit* portions of permissible meat would suffice to nullify it — that is, thirty against a half, rather than the standard sixty. His reasoning appears to have been that since a half-*kezayit* does not carry the full biblical penalty of lashes — it is below the minimum *shiur* for punishment — perhaps it rises only to the level of a rabbinic prohibition, and rabbinic prohibitions may be handled with a more lenient measure.

### Rav Ashi Rebuke

His father Rav Ashi rebuked him sharply: do not be lenient with rabbinic *shiurim*. The phrase *d'rabbanan* in this context carries ambiguity — it could modify the prohibition (which may be only rabbinically prohibited because it involves a sub-*shiur* quantity) or it could modify the measure of sixty itself (which many understand to be a rabbinic standard, a worst-case-scenario stand-in for taste). Rav Ashi's rebuke addresses both possibilities simultaneously. Furthermore, Rav Ashi adds: taste is not always negated at sixty — sometimes things are tasted even below that proportion. Sixty is a conservative outer bound, a *worst-case scenario*, precisely so that one need not investigate taste case by case. To start reducing it to thirty in certain situations undermines the entire function of the standard.

### Chatzi Shiur Asur

Moreover, Rav Ashi invokes Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: *chatzi shiur asur min haTorah* — even a half-measure of a forbidden substance is biblically prohibited. One does not incur lashes for less than the full *shiur*, and one certainly does not face the penalty of *karet*, but the act of consumption is itself a biblical prohibition. This means Mar bar Rav Ashi's premise — that a half-*kezayit* of fat is only rabbinically forbidden — was mistaken from the outset. The *Gemara* notes parenthetically that whether a half-*shiur* of an *issur karet* is more severe than a half-*shiur* of a mere *issur lav* is a question the Ramban raises elsewhere, but that discussion belongs to a different *sugya*.

## Sixty vs. 61 Eggs

### 60/61 Tradition

A tradition is cited from the school of the *nasi* in the name of Rabbi Yaakov, transmitted through a long chain of authorities ultimately going back to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi via Bar Kappara: *beitzah beshishim asurah, beshishim ve'achat muteret* — if there are sixty eggs (against the forbidden one), it is forbidden; if there are sixty-one, it is permitted. On its face this seems to simply restate the standard rule of sixty, but the phrasing is subtly ambiguous and gives rise to a difficulty.

### Counting Ambiguity

Rav Zeira presses Rav Shimon bar Abba on this tradition. The problem is that the formulation draws a line between permitted and forbidden, and whenever a tradition establishes such a boundary it must be precise. In this case, it was not. Two great authorities of the generation — Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi and Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani — both transmitted this ruling in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, yet they themselves were uncertain as to its precise meaning. The question they could not resolve: does "sixty-one permits" mean sixty-one total eggs including the forbidden one — which is the standard rule, since one need sixty *against* it plus the item itself equals sixty-one — or does it mean sixty-one *in addition to* the forbidden egg, for a total of sixty-two? The latter reading would represent an unusual stringency by eggs, requiring not sixty against the forbidden item but sixty-one. No explanation is given in the *Gemara* for why eggs would carry this extra stringency, and the *rishonim* offer various suggestions. What is notable is that the *Gemara* entertains this possibility despite having already established that eggs are if anything *less* of a taste concern than other substances — one might have expected leniency with eggs, not stringency.

### Yitzchak's Clarity

Rav Yitzchak the son of Rav Yehuda then provides a definitive statement: *beitzah beshishim u-mah hi asurah, beshishim ve'achat ve-hi muteret* — an egg with sixty others is forbidden, sixty-one including the egg itself and it is permitted. According to Rashi's reading, this formulation actually means sixty-one against it plus the egg for a total of sixty-two — confirming the stringency. By this reading, an egg requires one more against it than the standard rule would demand. The number sixty was clearly emerging as the dominant standard in the Amoraic period, but as these discussions reveal, its precise application — including or excluding the forbidden item, and whether certain categories warrant deviation from the norm — was still being worked out.

## Evolution to 60

### Forty-Seven/45

The *Gemara* now traces how the number sixty crystallized historically. Someone came before Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi with a case involving a certain proportion of forbidden to permissible substance. Rabban Gamliel responded: my father would not accept forty-seven as sufficient — and you want me to accept forty-five? The force of the statement is clear: if the previous authority held firm even when the proportion was more favorable, there is no basis to be lenient now with a less favorable one.

### 45/43

A parallel case: someone came before Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi. He replied: my father would not accept forty-five as sufficient — and you want me to accept forty-three? The same structure, the same reasoning, one step further along.

### Lenient Reading

Rashi, however, reads these same passages in precisely the opposite direction. According to Rashi, the language *abba lo shi'er* — "my father did not measure it" — means not that the father forbade the case with the larger proportion, but that the father *permitted* it with that proportion without even requiring more. Thus: my father permitted it with forty-seven, therefore I can permit it with forty-five; my father permitted it with forty-five, therefore I can permit it with forty-three. On this reading, these authorities were actually ruling *more leniently* than sixty, not working toward it. Either way — whether the tradition records stringencies or leniencies — the key observation is the same: sixty was not yet the fixed number. The precise proportion had not yet crystallized.

### From Taste to Numbers

This historical picture is exactly what one would expect from the trajectory the *Gemara* has been describing. The process began with the biblical concept of taste — does the forbidden substance impart its flavor? — and then developed practical tools for measuring that taste: stand-in models such as onions, the use of a non-Jewish taster, and eventually quantitative thresholds. The move toward numbers is a move toward universality and predictability. A universal number like sixty does not require one to know what the forbidden item was, what the permissible mixture was, or to find a taster. One simply counts. But it takes time for any such universal standard to crystallize, and these traditions from the children and students of Rabbi reflect that transitional moment.

### Thirty Question

A final vignette from this period: someone came before Rabbi Chiya with a case. Rabbi Chiya responded: *klum yesh shloshim?* — is there even thirty? The remark was not a ruling that thirty would suffice. As Rabbi Chanina clarifies, it was an exaggeration — a rhetorical dismissal. The proportion was so evidently inadequate that Rabbi Chiya did not even engage with the specific number. He simply noted that there was not even thirty, meaning the case was obviously problematic, and there was nothing to discuss.

## Sixty/100 Drash

### All by 60

The *Gemara* now moves from the historical development to scriptural grounding. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, in the name of Bar Kappara: *kol issurin she-ba-Torah beshishim* — all Torah prohibitions are measured by sixty. This statement distills the entire preceding discussion into a single universal principle: regardless of what the forbidden item is or what the permissible mixture is, sixty is the measure.

### All by 100

A second tradition, also in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi via Bar Kappara — but transmitted through a different chain — states the opposite: *kol issurin she-ba-Torah beme'ah* — all Torah prohibitions are measured by one hundred. One hundred does appear in *mishna* contexts, specifically in tractates of *Zeraim* concerning agricultural prohibitions such as *terumah* and *orlah*, where a higher threshold applies. But as a universal standard for general taste-based prohibitions, it is a striking claim. The *Gemara* identifies a single source from which both sixty and one hundred are derived — the case of the ram's foreleg offered in connection with the *nazir's* sacrifice — and explains how the two traditions emerge from different ways of analyzing that case.

## Ram's Foreleg

### Nazir's Ram

The case is as follows: a *nazir* who completes his period of abstinence brings a ram as a *shelamim* offering. The foreleg of that ram — the *zeroa* — is designated for the *kohen* and may not be eaten by non-*kohanim*. The foreleg is cooked together with the rest of the ram. The question is: how can a non-*kohen* eat the rest of the ram, given that the foreleg — which is forbidden to non-*kohanim* and functions in status like *kodshei kodashim* — was cooked in the same pot? Should not its taste transfer to the rest of the meat and render it forbidden? The answer offered by the *Gemara* is that the proportions of the foreleg to the rest of the ram are such that taste is nullified — and this proportional relationship becomes the model for the universal standards of sixty and one hundred.

### Cooking Methods

A *baraita* is cited on the question of how the foreleg is cooked. One view holds that *bisheila* means the foreleg is cooked whole and intact with the ram. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai reads *bisheila* to mean specifically that it is cooked together with the ram in the same pot. The precise tannaitic dispute concerns the procedure: one opinion holds that the foreleg is severed first and then cooked together with the rest of the ram in the same vessel; another holds that it is all cooked together first and then the foreleg is separated; a third possibility is that the foreleg is cooked in a separate pot entirely. The *Gemara* notes that according to the first approach, the *Gemara's* derivation applies according to all opinions, since in every case the foreleg ends up cooked together with the rest of the ram. According to the second approach, the derivation applies only according to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, since the view that the foreleg is cooked separately would eliminate the very scenario from which we are learning.

### 60 vs. 100

The dispute between sixty and one hundred now resolves itself as a disagreement about which proportions to measure. The opinion that sixty is the standard holds: *basar va'atzamot bahadei basar va'atzamot misha'arinan* — we measure meat and bones against meat and bones. When you compare the total mass of the foreleg (meat and bone together) to the total mass of the rest of the ram (meat and bone together), the ratio is approximately one to sixty. The opinion that one hundred is the standard holds: *basar bahadei basar misha'arinan* — we measure meat against meat alone. The foreleg, like a chicken's drumstick, has a relatively high ratio of bone to meat compared with the rest of the animal. If you strip out the bones and compare only the meat of the foreleg to the meat of the rest of the ram, the proportional meat in the foreleg is smaller — and the ratio of the rest of the ram's meat to the foreleg's meat comes out to approximately one hundred.

### Bones Question

Rashi raises the underlying question that drives this dispute: do bones absorb taste, and do bones give off taste? There is marrow in bones; they may well contribute to and absorb flavor to some degree. If one includes bones as taste-active, one measures the full mass of bone and meat against bone and meat, yielding sixty. If one treats only meat as taste-active — whether because bones neither give nor absorb significantly, or because they do so at a different rate — then one measures meat against meat alone, yielding one hundred. Ordinarily, the *Gemara* assumes a rather inclusive approach to what counts toward the sixty: everything in the pot, including liquids, solids, and even the walls of the vessel, in principle contributes. But the question of bones specifically — and whether the bones of the forbidden substance itself are counted, and whether the marrow within them is treated as taste-active — is a live issue that this *Gemara* directly engages.

## Exception or Rule

### Exceptional Status

Having established the foreleg as the model for sixty and one hundred, the *Gemara* raises a fundamental objection: the foreleg case is described in a *baraita* as *heter ha-ba mi-kol issur* — something permissible that derives from what would otherwise be an exceptional category of prohibition. In other words, the *baraita* itself signals that this case is unusual. If it is unusual, how can we learn universal rules of sixty and one hundred from it? The concern runs in both directions: if it is an exception, perhaps the Torah was more lenient here than the general rule, in which case sixty or one hundred may not actually be sufficient in ordinary cases.

### Rabbi Yehuda Exception

Abaye offers the first answer. The *baraita's* characterization of the foreleg case as exceptional applies specifically to the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *min bemino* — a substance mixed with its own kind — is never subject to *bitul* regardless of proportions. Blood mixed with blood, meat mixed with meat: for Rabbi Yehuda, the identical nature of the two substances means the forbidden component can never be considered nullified, no matter how small it is. The foreleg is meat cooked with meat; under Rabbi Yehuda's principle, it should never be *batel*. The Torah nonetheless declares it *batel* at sixty or one hundred — and that is the *chidush*, the innovation. This case represents the Torah making an exception to Rabbi Yehuda's principle.

### Yom Kippur Parallel

The *Gemara* establishes the basis for Rabbi Yehuda's general principle: the verse concerning the Yom Kippur service says to take from the blood of the ox *and* from the blood of the goat, mix them, and then sprinkle — which implies that even after mixing, both bloods retain their distinct identities and neither is ever nullified by the other, despite the obvious quantitative disproportion (an ox produces far more blood than a goat). This establishes Rabbi Yehuda's *din*: same-kind mixtures are never subject to *bitul*. The foreleg case thus represents a specific scriptural exception to that principle.

### Learning Stringencies

This then resolves the *Gemara's* concern about deriving a general rule from an exceptional case. There is a principle that one does not learn leniencies from exceptional cases — *chidush lo gamrinan*. But sixty and one hundred are not leniencies learned from the foreleg. They are *stringencies*. Biblically, a minority is ordinarily nullified by a majority — *de'oraita beruba batel*. According to Rashi, this is because taste itself is not a biblical concern, and without a concern of taste, a simple majority suffices. According to Tosafot, the reason is different: in a case of *min bemino*, where everything tastes the same, there is no taste-impartation concern at all, and therefore a simple majority would be sufficient even if one accepts taste as a biblical category. Either way, requiring sixty or one hundred rather than a simple majority is a *stringency* relative to the biblical baseline. And stringencies may be learned from exceptional cases. The foreleg teaches us to be more demanding than the raw biblical minimum — and that teaching applies universally.

## Ta'am by Kodashim

### Ta'am Exception

Rava offers an alternative framing of what makes the foreleg case exceptional. The *baraita* describes it as an exception, Rava says, because the Torah here sets aside the concern of *ta'am ke'ikar* — the principle that taste is legally equivalent to the substance itself. In all other contexts of *kodashim*, *ta'am ke'ikar* applies and creates a prohibition. Here, the Torah permits despite the potential transfer of taste. The exception is not about *min bemino* per se, as Abaye suggested, but about the suspension of *ta'am ke'ikar* in this specific context.

### Rashi Position

Rashi draws from Rava's formulation a significant implication: if *ta'am ke'ikar* is described as a problem specifically "by *kodashim*," this implies that it is not a biblical problem in ordinary, non-*kodashim* contexts. In other words, Rashi reads this *Gemara* as grounding a position that taste-based prohibitions are rabbinic in origin, not biblical, when it comes to ordinary food mixtures. The biblical mechanism of *bitul* is then fundamentally quantitative — about proportional insignificance — and not primarily about whether flavor has been imparted. This is why sixty and one hundred can be derived as independently meaningful thresholds, not merely as practical proxies for taste.

### Tosafot Position

Tosafot vigorously rejects this reading. In Tosafot's view, *ta'am ke'ikar* is unambiguously a biblical prohibition in all contexts, as demonstrated by numerous *sugyot* elsewhere. The foreleg case does not prove that taste is only a rabbinic concern in ordinary cases; it simply presents a situation where *ta'am ke'ikar* in its ordinary sense is not triggered, because all the material in the pot is of the same kind and tastes the same. The entire *sugya* is therefore not overturning the biblical status of taste, but rather addressing what happens when the taste concern is neutralized by identity of species.

### Residual Taste

Tosafot offers a more precise reading of what Rava means by describing the foreleg case as an exception regarding *ta'am ke'ikar*. What the foreleg case sets aside is not full *ta'am ke'ikar* — which would require actual discernible flavor transfer — but something more subtle: *ta'am she'ein mishtaneh*, residual taste that lingers even when nothing qualitatively distinct can be detected. In *kodashim*, even such imperceptible residual transfer of taste-properties is normally a concern and creates *kodashim*-level restrictions on whatever absorbed it. The foreleg case is lenient specifically regarding this category of residual taste, not regarding the mainstream prohibition of *ta'am ke'ikar*. And since this is a leniency, it cannot be extended beyond the specific case.

### Chatat Absorption

The *Gemara* grounds the normal *kodashim* stringency regarding residual taste in a verse: *kol asher yigga biv'sarah yikdash* — anything that touches the flesh of a *chatat* becomes holy. This is interpreted to mean that if a *korban* absorbs the juices of a *chatat*, it takes on the stringencies of the *chatat*: if the *chatat* was invalid, the absorbing *korban* is treated as invalid; if the *chatat* was valid but subject to a one-day eating restriction, a *korban* that would normally be eaten over two days and absorbed from the *chatat* may only be eaten for one day. This applies even when the two *korbanot* are the same kind — *min bemino* — and even when the quantity transferred is minimal and qualitatively indistinguishable. It demonstrates that in *kodashim*, residual taste transfer is a real and operative concern, which is precisely what the foreleg case sets aside as an exception.

### Conclusion

The *Gemara* completes its argument symmetrically. One might ask: if the foreleg case is an exception that sets aside residual-taste concerns, why not learn from it a general rule that residual taste is always inconsequential? The answer is the same as before: we do not learn leniencies from exceptional cases. And one might further ask: if the case is entirely exceptional, how can we derive from it the standards of sixty and one hundred? Again: because sixty and one hundred are stringencies relative to the biblical baseline. Without the foreleg, one would say that a simple majority suffices — either because taste is not biblically operative (Rashi) or because in a same-kind mixture there is nothing to taste (Tosafot). The foreleg demands more than a majority, and that greater demand is a stringency we extend universally. The extraordinary nature of the case, then, does not prevent us from learning stringencies from it — and it is precisely from the proportions of that cooked arm to the rest of the ram that the measures of sixty and one hundred are derived and applied to all forbidden admixtures throughout the Torah.
