# Nazir 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hekdesh Ta'us Intro

### Shammai/Hillel Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶקְדֵּשׁ טָעוּת — הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: Consecration** that one performs in **error** nevertheless renders property **consecrated, and Beit Hillel say** it is **not consecrated.**

Perek Hamishi opens with a dispute that, at first glance, seems to belong more naturally to the laws of *hekdesh* than to *nezirut*. The *mishnah* states: *Beis Shammai omrim hekdesh ta'us hekdesh* — Beis Shammai holds that a mistaken consecration is nonetheless a valid consecration. Beis Hillel disagrees: *ein hekdesh* — the consecration does not take effect. Taking the *mishnah* at face value, the question is whether an act of *hekdesh* grounded in a mistaken premise has any legal force.

### Link to Perek Sheini

This dispute does not arise in isolation. All the way back in Perek Sheini (daf 9), the *mishnah* records a parallel controversy: if someone declares *hareini nazir min ha-gerogerot u'min ha-devilah* — "I am a *nazir* from figs and pressed dates" — Beis Shammai says he is a *nazir* while Beis Hillel says he is not. The Gemara there explained: *Beis Shammai leta'myahu d'amri ein she'elah b'hekdesh, u'cheivan d'ein she'elah b'hekdesh ein she'elah b'nezirut* — Beis Shammai reasons that just as one cannot obtain release from *hekdesh*, so too one cannot obtain release from *nezirut*, since the Torah applies the word *kodesh* to the *nazir*, as in the *pasuk*: *kadosh yihyeh gadal peras se'ar rosho*. The stickiness of *hekdesh* thus anchors Beis Shammai's broader position on the irrevocability of *nezirut*.

### Shammai's Logic

Beis Shammai's reasoning, then, is that *hekdesh* is an unusually durable legal category — once something is consecrated, it cannot simply be undone. That same quality of durability is what Beis Shammai invokes here: if *hekdesh* cannot be released, it follows that even a mistaken *hekdesh* should take hold, since the underlying legal mechanism admits no exceptions.

### Three Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר ״שׁוֹר שָׁחוֹר שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִבֵּיתִי רִאשׁוֹן הֲרֵי הוּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, וְיָצָא לָבָן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ הֶקְדֵּשׁ. ״דִּינַר זָהָב שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה בְּיָדִי רִאשׁוֹן הֲרֵי הוּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, וְעָלָה שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ הֶקְדֵּשׁ. ״חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן שֶׁתַּעֲלֶה בְּיָדִי רִאשׁוֹנָה הֲרֵי הִיא הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, וְעָלְתָה שֶׁל שֶׁמֶן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **How so; what is considered an act of erroneous consecration? If one said: A black bull that will emerge from my house first is consecrated, and a white bull emerged first, Beit Shammai say** it is **consecrated and Beit Hillel say** it is **not consecrated. Similarly, if one said: A gold dinar that will come up first in my hand is consecrated, and** when he reached into his pocket a dinar **of silver came up, Beit Shammai say** it is **consecrated and Beit Hillel say** it is **not consecrated. Likewise, if one said: A barrel of wine that will come up first in my hand** when I enter the cellar **is consecrated, and** a barrel **of oil came up** in his hand instead, **Beit Shammai say** it is **consecrated and Beit Hillel say** it is **not consecrated.**

The *mishnah* provides three concrete illustrations. First: one declares that the first black ox to emerge from his house shall be *hekdesh*, but a white ox emerges instead — Beis Shammai says it is *hekdesh*, Beis Hillel says it is not. Second: one declares that the first gold coin to come up from his pocket shall be *hekdesh*, but a silver coin comes up — again Beis Shammai says *hekdesh*, Beis Hillel disagrees. Third: one declares that the first barrel of wine that comes to hand shall be *hekdesh*, but it turns out to be oil — same dispute. Three cases, seemingly making the same point. The reason the *mishnah* requires all three will become clear as the Gemara proceeds.

## Temurah Explanation

### Beginning/End Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים הֶקְדֵּשׁ כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי — דְּיָלְפִינַן תְּחִלַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ מִסּוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that **Beit Shammai say** that **consecration** performed in error renders property consecrated, and Beit Hillel say it is not consecrated. The Gemara analyzes their dispute: **What is the reason of Beit Shammai?** They maintain **that we derive** the *halakha* of **the initial** stage **of consecration from the final** stage **of consecration.**

The Gemara opens its analysis by asking: what is Beis Shammai's rationale? The first answer offered is *yalfinan techilat hekdesh mi-sof hekdesh* — we derive the rule governing the inception of *hekdesh* from the rule governing a later stage of *hekdesh*.

### Temurah Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה תְּמוּרָה אֲפִילּוּ בְּטָעוּת — אַף הֶקְדֵּשׁ אֲפִילּוּ בְּטָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** an act of **substitution** takes effect **even in error,** i.e., if one meant to say that his black bull should be a substitute for his consecrated animal and he actually said: This white bull, the white bull is rendered consecrated, **so too,** the initial stage of **consecration** takes effect **even** when done **in error.**

The specific comparison is to *temurah* — the act of attempting to substitute one animal for a consecrated one. Once an animal is *hekdesh*, it cannot be redeemed simply by designating a replacement; instead, both animals become consecrated. Crucially, *temurah* operates even when the substitution is mistaken: if one said "this black ox in exchange for my *korban*" and it turned out to be white, the *temurah* is still valid. The argument is therefore: *ma temurah afilu b'ta'ut af hekdesh afilu b'ta'ut* — just as *temurah* works even with a mistaken identification, so too the initial act of *hekdesh* should work even when mistaken.

### Hillel's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: הָנֵי מִילֵּי תְּמוּרָה. אֲבָל אַחוֹתֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּטָעוּת — לָא מַחֲתִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: This matter,** i.e., the *halakha* that consecration takes effect even when done in error, applies only to **substitution,** where there is an animal that is already fully consecrated. **However, we do not have** the initial status of **consecration descend** upon an item **in error.**

Beis Hillel rejects this comparison on two grounds, both noted by Rashi. First, *temurah* is a *gezerat hakatuv* — a specific biblical decree — and one cannot extrapolate from a unique legal rule to a general principle. Second, and more substantively, there is a conceptual difference between making a mistake *within the realm of hekdesh* — as in *temurah*, where *kedushah* is already present — and mistakenly *bringing something into* the realm of *hekdesh* in the first place. Beis Hillel's formulation is: *hani milei temurah, u'le-kho de-hekdesh b'ta'ut lo machtinan* — that rule applies to *temurah*, but I will not allow an item to enter *hekdesh* through a mistake at the outset.

### Timing Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבֵית שַׁמַּאי, מָה אִילּוּ אָמַר ״הָרֵי זֶה תַּחַת זֶה לַחֲצִי הַיּוֹם״ מִי הָוְיָא תְּמוּרָה מֵהַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא? אֶלָּא עַד דְּמָטֵי חֲצִי הַיּוֹם הוּא דְּהָוְיָא תְּמוּרָה. הָכִי נָמֵי לְכִי מִיגַּלְּיָא מִילְּתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** who derive the *halakha* of the initial stage of consecration from substitution, **just as if one said, at the start of the day: This animal is a substitute in exchange for this animal in the middle of the day, would it become a substitute from that time** when he issued the statement, in opposition to his explicit statement? It would not. **Rather,** Beit Shammai certainly concede that the animal does not become consecrated as a substitute **until the middle of the day arrives,** at which point **it becomes a substitution. So too,** in the case of the mishna, the consecration should take effect **when the situation is revealed** to be in accordance with his statement, i.e., only if a black bull emerges first. Only then should the animal be rendered consecrated, but not if a white bull emerges first. Why do Beit Shammai hold that in the case of the mishna the consecration takes effect in opposition to his explicit statement?

The Gemara presses Beis Shammai further. Consider a case where someone says, "Let this animal be a *temurah* as of noon today" — the *temurah* would not take effect at the moment of speech, but only when noon arrives. The condition must be fulfilled before the legal change occurs. By the same logic, in our *mishnah*, the person said the *black* ox — so why should anything happen before a black ox actually emerges? The white ox that came out is not what was specified, and the *temurah* analogy would seem to support waiting until the specified condition is met rather than allowing the wrong item to be consecrated.

## Rav Pappa Reinterpret

### First Black Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״רִאשׁוֹן״ לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Beit Shammai concede that consecration does not take effect in opposition to one's explicit statement. Rather, they maintain that it is **for this reason that the man states:** The black bull that will emerge from my house **first,** as he means the following: **When** the **first** black bull of all the black bulls I possess **will emerge** from my house, it will be consecrated. When Beit Shammai ruled that the bull is consecrated, they were referring to the first black bull that emerged, even if it was not the first bull that emerged, as a white bull preceded it.

At this point, Rav Pappa offers a different approach entirely. Rather than defending the *temurah* comparison, he reframes the *mishnah*'s case: *lekach ne'emar rishon le-khesheyetze rishon, ve-hashor shachor ka'amar* — what the person actually meant was "the first black ox to come out." The *mishnah* is not describing a scenario with only two oxen — one black, one white — where the wrong one emerged. Rather, the person owned multiple oxen, including several black ones, and designated "the first of the black oxen to come out of my house." When the white ox walked out, that was simply irrelevant to his declaration. The first black ox to subsequently emerge is then consecrated.

### Not Actually Mistake

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״שׁוֹר שָׁחוֹר״ קָאָמַר, מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ אֶלָּא הַאי? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִית לֵיהּ תְּרֵין תְּלָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But he said: Black bull,** and **are we not dealing** even with a case **where he only has this** one black bull? If so, the Gemara's initial interpretation of his statement is correct: The black bull is consecrated only if it is the first to emerge, but not if a white bull precedes it. The Gemara answers: **No;** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* with regard to a case **where he has two** or **three** black bulls.

This reinterpretation fundamentally changes the character of the case: it is no longer, strictly speaking, a *ta'us* at all. The speaker was deliberate and specific; there was no error in the premise of his declaration. So why does the *mishnah* frame this as a case of *hekdesh ta'us*?

### Ta'us Redefined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אִם כֵּן, ״שֶׁיֵּצֵא בָּרִאשׁוֹן״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: If so,** i.e., if he intended to consecrate the first of his black bulls to emerge from the house, **he should have** said: The **first** black bull **that will emerge** from my house. Since he did not say this, he must have meant that the black bull should be consecrated only if it is the first bull of any kind to emerge.

The Gemara explains that *ta'us* here does not mean an outright mistake but rather an ambiguous or non-definitive statement — as distinct from a clear, unambiguous act of *hekdesh*. The declaration "the first one to come out of my house shall be *hekdesh*" has an element of imprecision that invites reinterpretation, and it is this quality that the *mishnah* calls *ta'us*.

### Gemara Objects

The Gemara challenges Rav Pappa: the *mishnah* says "the first to come out of my house," not "the first black one." If the person truly meant "the first of the black ones," the phrasing of the *mishnah* seems to point in a different direction. One could answer that "from my house" is simply specifying the location as opposed to another outbuilding, and that the primary criterion was the color — but the textual difficulty remains.

### Pappa Responds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא מִבַּרְנִישׁ לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הַאי הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּטָעוּת הוּא? הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּכַוּוֹנָה הוּא! מִשּׁוּם דְּאַטְעֲיֵיהּ לְדִיבּוּרֵיהּ קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava from Barnish said to Rav Ashi,** with regard to the explanation of Rav Pappa: Is **this** case he mentioned one of **erroneous consecration? It is intentional consecration.** According to the interpretation of Rav Pappa, there is no error. He intended to consecrate the first black bull that emerged, and that is what was consecrated. The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, it is called an erroneous consecration **because he erred in his first statement.** His statement of consecration gave the mistaken impression that he desired to consecrate the first bull that emerges, even if it is white. In any case, Rav Pappa indicates that even Beit Shammai hold that an erroneous act of consecration does not take effect.

Rav Pappa of Barnish responds to Rav Ashi: *mishum de-ati le-diburei kama* — because the declaration as stated comes across as somewhat misleading or ambiguous relative to what was actually intended. The face value of the words suggests one thing, while the speaker's real intent was another. It is precisely this gap between surface meaning and actual intent that requires reinterpretation and that justifies applying the label of *ta'us* to what is, in reality, a deliberate but loosely worded declaration.

## Shammai True Ta'us

### Question Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּטָעוּת לָא הָוֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ? וְהָתְנַן: מִי שֶׁנָּדַר בְּנָזִיר וְנִשְׁאַל לַחֲכָמִים וְהִתִּירוּ, וְהָיְתָה לוֹ בְּהֵמָה מוּפְרֶשֶׁת — תֵּצֵא וְתִרְעֶה בָּעֵדֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this assumption: **And** do **Beit Shammai hold** that an indisputably **erroneous** act of **consecration is not** considered an act of **consecration? But didn't we learn** in the mishna (31b): With regard to **one who took a vow of naziriteship and** later **made a request to the halakhic authorities** to dissolve his vow, **and they dissolved** his vow, **and he had** already **separated an animal** for one of his nazirite offerings beforehand, **it shall go out and graze among the flock,** like any other non-sacred animal.

Having established Rav Pappa's model — in which the *mishnah*'s case is not a genuine mistake but a deliberately ambiguous formulation — a natural question emerges: does Beis Shammai actually hold that a true *hekdesh ta'us*, a real mistake in the underlying premise, would be valid? The reinterpretation implies that a genuine *ta'us* might in fact not work even according to Beis Shammai, since the whole effort was to show that the *mishnah*'s case is not a *ta'us* at all.

### Neder/Chacham Case

The Gemara raises a *mishnah* that bears directly on this question: *mi she-nadar be-nazir ve-nishal le-chakham ve-hittiro, ve-hayetah lo behemah meforeshet, tetzei ve-tir'eh ba-eder* — if someone vowed to be a *nazir*, consulted a *chakham* who released him, and had already set aside a specific animal for his *korban*, that animal goes out to graze freely among the flock. The reason is that the *chakham* is *oker ha-neder me-ikaro* — he uproots the vow retroactively, as if it never existed. Since the *nezirut* never legally occurred, the animal designated for its *chatat* was never truly consecrated.

### Hillel Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: אִי אַתֶּם מוֹדִים שֶׁהֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּטָעוּת הוּא, וְתֵצֵא וְתִרְעֶה בָּעֵדֶר?!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: Don't you concede that** the reason for this ruling is because **it is an erroneous** act of **consecration, and** that a consecration of this kind does not take effect, and that is the reason **it shall go out and graze among** the **flock?**

Beis Hillel invokes this case against Beis Shammai: *i attem modim be-she-hekdesh b'ta'ut ve-tetzei ve-tir'eh ba-eder?* — do you not concede that in this case, which involves a *hekdesh ta'us*, the animal is not consecrated and simply grazes freely? If Beis Shammai truly holds that *hekdesh ta'us* is valid *hekdesh*, the animal should remain consecrated even after the *chakham* releases the vow.

### Status Change Analysis

The answer — which both Rav Pappa's framework and, as Tosafot notes, straightforward analysis would supply — is that this is not a *hekdesh ta'us* case at all. The person fully intended to be a *nazir* and deliberately designated the animal; there was no mistake in the premise. What subsequently changed was not the discovery of an error but a conscious legal act: the *chakham*'s retroactive release of the vow. There is a fundamental difference between a *ta'us* — where the very premise of the consecration was mistaken from the outset — and a deliberate act that is later undone through a recognized legal mechanism. Beis Shammai can therefore concede this case entirely without any concession regarding genuine *hekdesh ta'us*. According to Rav Pappa, moreover, our *mishnah*'s case is itself not a genuine *ta'us*, so the entire exchange is operating on a separate track from the *mishnah*'s core ruling.

## Abaye Past Tense

### Vocalization Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לָא קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ דְּקָאֵים בְּצַפְרָא, אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּקָאֵים בְּטִיהֲרָא, וְאָמַר: שׁוֹר שָׁחוֹר שֶׁיָּצָא מִבֵּיתִי רִאשׁוֹן לֶיהֱוֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ. וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לָבָן נְפַק. וַאֲמַר לְהוֹן: אִי הֲוָה יָדַעְנָא דְּלָבָן נְפַק לָא אֲמַרִי שָׁחוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** a different explanation of the mishna: It should **not enter your mind** that the mishna is dealing with one **who was standing in the morning** and referred to a future event, i.e., that an animal will emerge from the house. **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** With one **who is standing at noon,** after the bulls had already left the house, **and said:** The **black bull that emerged first from my house first shall be consecrated. And** people **said to him: A white** bull **emerged** first. **And he said to them: Had I known** that **a white** bull **emerged, I would not** have **said black.** Therefore, the consecration was erroneous.

Abaye offers a third explanation of the *mishnah*, one that depends on a subtle difference in vocalization. The letters *shin-yud-tzadi-alef* can be read either as *she-yeitzei* — "that will come out," future tense — or as *she-yatza* — "that came out," past tense. The *mishnah*, as typically read, seems to use the future: one declares in advance that whatever black ox will emerge from his house shall be *hekdesh*. But the letters themselves are equally consistent with a past-tense reading, and Abaye proposes that this is precisely how the *mishnah* should be understood.

### Past Tense Reading

On Abaye's reading, the *mishnah* describes not a prospective declaration but a retrospective one: the speaker is standing in the afternoon or evening, after an animal has already come out of his house. He declares, "The black ox that came out of my house — let it be *hekdesh*."

### First Animal Intent

The case then proceeds as follows: it becomes known — or is pointed out — that the animal which actually emerged was white, not black. The speaker responds: had I known it was a white ox that came out, I would have said "the white ox." My intention was simply to consecrate the first animal, whatever it was. The color was incidental to the declaration; what mattered was that it was the first to come out. Beis Shammai holds this consecration valid.

### Extension Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ דְּקָאֵים בְּטִיהֲרָא עָסֵיק? וְהָקָתָנֵי: ״דִּינָר שֶׁל זָהָב שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה״! תְּנִי ״שֶׁעָלָה״. ״חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן שֶׁתַּעֲלֶה״! תְּנִי ״שֶׁעָלְתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How can you say** that the mishna **deals** with one **who is standing at noon** and is speaking of a past event? **But** in a subsequent example the mishna **teaches: A gold dinar that will come up** in my hand first shall be consecrated, which is clearly referring to a future event. The Gemara answers: You should emend the mishna and **teach:** A gold dinar **that came up,** in the past tense. The Gemara continues to ask: Didn't the mishna state: **A barrel of wine that will come up** in my hand first shall be consecrated, which is also referring to the future tense. The Gemara similarly answers that one should **teach** in the mishna: A barrel **that** already **came up.**

What makes Abaye's explanation elegant for the first case creates a difficulty for the other two. The *mishnah*'s second case uses *she-ya'aleh* — "that will come up" — in connection with the gold coin, and the third uses *she-ta'aleh* in connection with the barrel of wine. These forms are clearly future tense and do not naturally admit the same vocalic shift that worked for *she-yatza*.

### Vocalization Solutions

The Gemara resolves this by adjusting the vocalizations of the other two cases as well. For the coin, read *she-ala* instead of *she-ya'aleh*; for the barrel, read *she-altah* instead of *she-ta'aleh* — both past-tense forms. On this reading, all three cases follow the same model: the speaker designated something after the fact, mistakenly identifying it by a secondary attribute (color, material, contents), when his actual intent was simply to consecrate whatever had come to hand first.

## Eye Framework Analysis

### Rav Chisda Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אוּכָּמָא בְּחִיוָּרָא — לַקְיָא, חִיוָּרָא בְּאוּכָּמָא — לַקְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: A black** bull **among white ones** is **deficient,** as white bulls are superior in quality, and **a white** patch **on a black** bull is **a deficiency.**

With the three explanations of the *mishnah* laid out, the Gemara shifts to a related analysis introduced by Rav Chisda. He states: *ukma b'chivrei lakya, chivrei b'ukmei lakya* — a black ox among white ones is inferior, and a white ox among black ones is also inferior. In a white herd, a black ox stands out unfavorably; in a black herd, a white ox is at a disadvantage. This principle of relative quality among livestock now becomes the lens through which the three *mishnah* cases are examined.

### First Case Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״שָׁחוֹר שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִבֵּיתִי רִאשׁוֹן הֶקְדֵּשׁ״. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין: כִּי מַקְדִּישׁ — בְּעַיִן רָעָה מַקְדִּישׁ, וְאָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הָוֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** Having stated these assessments, the Gemara returns to discuss the mishna. **We learned** in the mishna that if one said: The **black** bull **that will emerge from my house first is consecrated,** and a white one emerged. **It entered our minds** to assume that **when** one **consecrates** property to the Temple treasury **he consecrates sparingly,** i.e., he does not give his property that is superior in quality or value, unless he expressly says so. **And** yet **Beit Shammai say** that the white bull in this case **is consecrated,** which indicates that the white one is inferior in quality, which contradicts the statement of Rav Ḥisda.

The first case — the black ox — thus involves an item of lesser quality, given that the herd is presumed to be white. If Beis Shammai holds that this consecration is valid, one might infer that a person can consecrate *b'ayin ra'ah* — in a "bad eye," with a less generous disposition, giving *hekdesh* an inferior item while keeping the better one. The difficulty is determining whether Beis Shammai's ruling reflects that standard or a more generous one.

### Second Case Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי, בְּעַיִן יָפָה מַקְדִּישׁ? ״דִּינַר שֶׁל זָהָב שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה בְּיָדִי רִאשׁוֹן״ וְעָלָה כֶּסֶף, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines this assumption: **Rather, what then?** Will you say that according to the opinion of Beit Shammai one typically **consecrates generously** and donates his property that is superior? However, the continuation of the mishna states that if one said: The **gold dinar that will come up in my hand first, and a silver one came up, Beit Shammai say it is consecrated.** If Beit Shammai hold that one would have in mind to consecrate only the superior property, why would the inferior silver coin be consecrated?

The second case complicates the picture. The person said *zahav* — gold — but a silver coin emerged. Gold is more valuable than silver, so the person had intended to give the better item and ended up giving the lesser one. This would suggest he was consecrating *b'ayin yafah* — with a "good eye," intending generosity toward *hekdesh* — yet Beis Shammai says even the lesser silver coin is consecrated.

### Third Case Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי — בְּעַיִן רָעָה מַקְדִּישׁ? חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן שֶׁתַּעֲלֶה בְּיָדִי רִאשׁוֹן, וְעָלָה שֶׁל שֶׁמֶן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הֶקְדֵּשׁ. וְהָא שֶׁמֶן עָדִיף מִיַּיִן! אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: בְּגָלִילָא שָׁנוּ, דְּחַמְרָא עָדִיף מִמִּשְׁחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **Rather, what then?** Does a person **consecrate sparingly?** Yet the subsequent example of the mishna states that if one said: **A barrel of wine that will come up in my hand first, and one of oil came up, Beit Shammai say** it is **consecrated. But oil is preferable to wine,** so why is the oil consecrated? The Gemara answers: **If** the problem is **due to that, this** is **not difficult,** as this mishna **was taught in the Galilee, where wine is preferable to oil.** Olive trees are plentiful in the Galilee, and therefore oil is cheaper than wine. Therefore, the entire mishna can be explained in accordance with the opinion that people consecrate sparingly.

The third case adds further complexity. Wine was declared, oil emerged. Whether wine or oil is the superior commodity depends on region: in Galilee, wine is more valuable, making this a case of giving the lesser item; in other places, oil is more valuable, reversing the quality calculus entirely. The three cases thus seem to move back and forth between *ayin yafah* and *ayin ra'ah* without a consistent pattern.

### Resolution by Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אוּכָּמָא — לְמַשְׁכֵּיהּ, סוּמָּקָא — לְבִשְׂרֵיהּ, חִיוָּרָא — לְרִדְיָא. וְהָאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אוּכָּמָא בְּחִיוָּרָא לַקְיָא! כִּי אֲמַרִי, בְּתוֹרָא דְקַרְמוֹנָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes another statement with regard to bulls: **And Rav Ḥisda said** with regard to bulls: **A black** bull is good **for its hide; a red one** is good **for its meat;** while **a white** bull is good **for plowing.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav Ḥisda say: A black** bull **among white ones** is **deficient,** which indicates that a black one is inferior in all regards? The Gemara again answers that Rav Ḥisda could reply: **When I said** that, I was referring only **to a Karmanian bull,** but not to other bulls.

Rav Chisda resolves the difficulty by introducing a use-based framework: *ukma le-mashcha, sumka le-visra, chivra le-radya* — a black ox is best for leather (the hide), a red ox is best for eating, and a white ox is best for plowing. The quality of an animal is not absolute but relative to its intended purpose. With this principle, each of the three cases can be assigned a consistent value — whether the consecrated item represents a more or a less desirable choice depends on what the item is being used for. As for the first case, Rav Chisda adds: when I said that a black ox in a white herd is inferior, I was speaking of a particular type of ox — *turei d'Karmonaye*, oxen of that specific province — not of oxen in general. In ordinary circumstances, the black ox can in fact be the more valuable one, depending on its intended use. This allows all three cases to be read consistently.

## Maaser Proof

### Miscount Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: אִי אַתֶּם מוֹדִים בְּמִי שֶׁטָּעָה וְקָרָא לַתְּשִׁיעִי ״עֲשִׂירִי״, וְלָעֲשִׂירִי ״תְּשִׁיעִי״, וְלָאַחַד עָשָׂר ״עֲשִׂירִי״, שֶׁהוּא מְקוּדָּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai said to** Beit Hillel: **Don't you concede with regard to one** who was separating the animal tithe from his herd, i.e., passing his animals before him single file and consecrating every tenth one as a tithe, **that** if he **erred and called the ninth** animal: **Tenth; and the tenth: Ninth; and the eleventh: Tenth, that** each of them **is consecrated?** This proves that an erroneous act of consecration does take effect.

Beis Shammai now presses its case for the validity of *hekdesh ta'us* by mounting a counter-proof from the laws of *maaser behemah*. The argument runs as follows. When counting one's animals to designate the tithe, the tenth animal to pass under the rod becomes *maaser*. But what happens if the count goes wrong? The *mishnah* addresses a case where someone miscounted and called the ninth animal the tenth, the tenth animal the ninth, and the eleventh animal the tenth — thereby producing three animals, each called "tenth" at some point in the counting.

### All Three Sanctified

Despite the error, all three animals acquire the sanctity of *maaser*. The biblical verse states: *kol asher ya'avor tachat ha-shevet ha-asiri yihyeh kodesh l-Hashem* — whatever passes under the rod as the tenth shall be holy. The law sanctifies the tenth animal through the act of the rod passing over it, and the miscounting does not undo the consecration that attached to each animal at the moment it was designated.

### Shammai's Proof

This, says Beis Shammai, is a clear demonstration that *hekdesh ta'us* is valid: the person made an obvious mistake — one cannot have three different animals each genuinely be the tenth — and yet all three become *maaser*. If the Torah permits *hekdesh* through *ta'us* in the case of *maaser*, the same should apply to *hekdesh* generally.

### Hillel Rejects Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל: לֹא הַשֵּׁבֶט קִידְּשׁוֹ. וּמָה אִילּוּ טָעָה וְהִנִּיחַ אֶת הַשֵּׁבֶט עַל שְׁמִינִי וְעַל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר, שֶׁמָּא עָשָׂה כְּלוּם? אֶלָּא, כָּתוּב שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ הָעֲשִׂירִי — הוּא קִידֵּשׁ הַתְּשִׁיעִי
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Hillel said to them:** It is **not the rod** that **consecrates it.** The touch of the rod does not consecrate the animal, nor does the fact that he said: Tenth, by mistake. Not all errors cause the tithe to be consecrated, and the proof is as follows: **And what** would be the *halakha* **if he had erred and placed the rod on the eighth or on the twelfth,** and labeled them: Tenth? Can it be suggested that **perhaps he performed anything** of consequence? The *halakha* is that the eighth or twelfth animal cannot be consecrated as tithe. **Rather,** why is the ninth or eleventh animal consecrated? There is a specific reason for this *halakha*, as the same **verse that consecrated the tenth** also **consecrated the ninth**

Beis Hillel rejects the proof: *lo ha-shevet kidsho* — it is not the person's speech that consecrates the animal; it is the rod. The *kedushah* of *maaser behemah* is a *gezerat hakatuv*, a specific biblical decree, and the mechanism of consecration is the physical act of the rod touching the animal, not the verbal designation. Since the source of the *kedushah* is the rod rather than the spoken word, one cannot draw conclusions about the validity of verbal *hekdesh b'ta'us* from this case.

### Hillel Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶת אַחַד עָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **and the eleventh.** It is a Torah edict that the consecration takes effect with regard to those two animals. Therefore, one cannot infer from this case that an erroneous act of consecration takes effect.

Beis Hillel reinforces the point with a reductio: *u-mah ilu ta'ah be-hanahat ha-shevet al shemini ve-al shenim asar shema asah klum* — if someone mistakenly placed the rod on the eighth animal or the twelfth, nothing would happen; no *kedushah* would result. This proves that it is not the person's declaration or intent that drives the consecration in the *maaser* case. Rather, as Beis Hillel concludes: *ela ha-katuv she-kiddesh asiri* — it is the scriptural verse itself that consecrates the tenth, making this an entirely sui generis mechanism. The *maaser* case therefore provides no basis for the broader principle Beis Shammai seeks to establish.

## Nazir/Status Distinction

### Animal Status Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנָּדַר בְּנָזִיר, וְנִשְׁאַל לְחָכָם וַאֲסָרוֹ — מוֹנֶה מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁנָּדַר. נִשְׁאַל לְחָכָם וְהִתִּירוֹ, הָיְתָה לוֹ בְּהֵמָה מוּפְרֶשֶׁת — תֵּצֵא וְתִרְעֶה בָּעֵדֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who took a vow of naziriteship,** who then regretted his vow and stopped observing the prohibition against drinking wine, **and** later **requested of a halakhic authority** to dissolve his vow, **and** the authority **ruled** that **he is bound** by his vow, finding no reason to dissolve it, he **counts** the term of naziriteship **from the time that he vowed,** including the days when he acted as though the vow were dissolved. In a case where **he requested of a halakhic authority** to dissolve his vow **and** the authority **dissolved it,** if **he had an animal separated** as a nazirite offering **it shall go out and graze among** the **flock.**

Having worked through Beis Shammai's proof from *maaser* and Beis Hillel's rebuttal, the Gemara returns to the case introduced earlier: why is the animal designated for the *chatat* of a *nazir* not consecrated once the *chakham* releases the vow? If Beis Shammai holds that *hekdesh ta'us* is valid, and the designation of the animal was made under the assumption of a *nezirut* that turned out never to have legally existed, should that not be a *hekdesh ta'us* that Beis Shammai would validate?

### Conscious Act Distinction

The answer is that this is simply not a case of *hekdesh ta'us*. The person who designated the animal did so consciously, deliberately, and with full intent. There was no mistake in the premise at the moment of the act. What happened subsequently — the *chakham*'s retroactive release of the vow — is an entirely different legal phenomenon. An intentional act that is later reversed by a recognized legal mechanism is categorically distinct from an act that was mistaken in its very premise from the outset.

### Summary Distinction

The distinction that emerges from the entire sugya is therefore this: *hekdesh ta'us*, as the term is used in the *mishnah* and debated by Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel, refers to a situation where the premise underlying the consecration was erroneous at the moment of the declaration. It does not encompass a case where a person consciously and correctly performed an act of *hekdesh* and subsequently undid it through legitimate means. Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel agree on the latter — the animal goes out to graze — and their dispute is confined to the former.
