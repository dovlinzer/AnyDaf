# Eruvin 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Three Positions

### Opening remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כְּשֶׁתִּמְצָא לוֹמַר, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר גַּגִּין רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמָן, חֲצֵירוֹת רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמָן, קַרְפֵּיפוֹת רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמָן. לְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים: גַּגִּין וַחֲצֵירוֹת רְשׁוּת אַחַת, קַרְפֵּיפוֹת רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כּוּלָּן רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** After careful analysis, **you will find** that you can **say** that **according to the statement of Rabbi Meir, roofs are a domain in and of themselves,** and one may carry from one roof to another; likewise **courtyards are** considered **a domain in and of themselves,** and one may likewise carry from one courtyard to another; and similarly, **enclosures are a domain in and of themselves.** However, one may not carry from a roof to a courtyard or from a courtyard to an enclosure. **According to the statement of the Rabbis, roofs and courtyards** constitute **one domain,** and therefore, one may carry even from a roof to a courtyard; **however, enclosures are one** discrete **domain.** One may not carry from a courtyard to an enclosure or from an enclosure to a courtyard. **According to the statement of Rabbi Shimon, all of them,** roofs, courtyards, and enclosures, **are one domain,** and therefore it is permitted to carry between any of them.

We begin at the very bottom of 91b, continuing the discussion that has occupied this *perek*: which domains, in the absence of an *eiruv*, are considered sufficiently unified that a vessel originating in one may be carried freely throughout them all? The underlying assumption throughout is that all the spaces in question — rooftops, courtyards, enclosed yards, balconies, alleys — are each individually a *reshut hayachid*. The question is not their basic legal status, but rather how to define the groupings that govern inter-domain movement on Shabbos when no *eiruv* has been made.

### Three positions explained

*Rav Yehuda* opens with the phrase *keshetimtzei lomar* — "when you find yourself to say," a locution meaning something like "upon reflection you will realize." Reflecting carefully on the debate, he articulates three distinct positions.

*Ledivrei Rabbi Meir*: rooftops (*gagin*) constitute their own domain, courtyards (*chatzerot*) constitute their own domain, and enclosed yards (*karpifot*) constitute their own domain. Each genre is internally unified but externally separate. You may carry from one rooftop to another rooftop, from one courtyard to another courtyard, from one *karpaf* to another *karpaf* — but you may not carry from a *gag* to a *chatzer* or from a *chatzer* to a *karpaf*.

*Ledivrei Rabbi Shimon*: all three combined — *gag*, *chatzer*, and *karpaf* — form a single unified domain. One might initially read Rabbi Shimon's language in the *Mishnah* — *echad gagot ve'echad chatzerot ve'echad karpifot reshut achat* — as meaning that each category is its own respective unit. The *Gemara* clarifies, and will shortly confirm from a *baraita*, that Rabbi Shimon means all three together form one unified *reshut*. Vessels that originate in any of these spaces may be carried freely into any of the others.

The *Chachamim* represent a position that the *Gemara* will develop in greater detail. Their disagreement with Rabbi Meir in the *Mishnah* concerned rooftops of different ownership: Rabbi Meir holds that all rooftops form one collective domain regardless of ownership, while the *Chachamim* hold that different ownership creates different domains — *kol echad ve'echad reshut bifnei atzmo*. By extension, the *Gemara* understands that the *Chachamim* occupy a middle position between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon, one that will be elaborated as the *sugya* progresses.

### Practical implications

The practical stakes of this debate concern vessels: which objects may be carried between which spaces, and under what conditions? The working assumption is that we are speaking of vessels that began Shabbos — *shavtu* — within one of the outdoor domains, not inside a house. House vessels present an entirely different problem, one tied directly to the *eiruv* question. The three-way debate governs only which outdoor *reshuyot* are considered sufficiently unified to permit free movement of vessels originating within them.

### Yards A & B example

To make this concrete: consider two courtyards, A and B, which did not make a joint *eiruv* with each other. If the residents of A *did* make an *eiruv* among themselves, house vessels may be brought out into A's courtyard. But the rule is that from courtyard A to courtyard B, one may only carry *chatzer* vessels — items that began Shabbos in the courtyard itself, not in the house. This raises an immediate difficulty: if A made an *eiruv*, house vessels can legitimately appear in A's yard. Now there are two kinds of vessels sitting side by side in the same courtyard — one that began in the house and one that began in the yard — and one is permitted to be carried to B while the other is not. How is one supposed to remember which is which?

This is precisely the concern the *Gemara* will now turn to examine. The underlying question is: does the permissibility of carrying yard vessels from A to B depend on whether A has made an internal *eiruv* or not?

## Baraita: Rav & R. Meir

### Baraita: roofs vs. courts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב, תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: כׇּל גַּגּוֹת הָעִיר רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן, וְאָסוּר לְהַעֲלוֹת וּלְהוֹרִיד מִן הַגַּגִּין לֶחָצֵר וּמִן הֶחָצֵר לְגַגִּין. וְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בֶּחָצֵר — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָן בֶּחָצֵר, בַּגַּגִּין — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָן בַּגַּגִּין, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא גַּג גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה אוֹ נָמוּךְ עֲשָׂרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד רְשׁוּת לְעַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* **was taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav,** and a *baraita* **was taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda.** The Gemara elaborates. A *baraita* **was taught in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav,** that according to the Rabbis one may carry only four cubits on each roof: **All the roofs of a city are one domain, and it is prohibited to carry** objects **up or carry** them **down, from the roofs to the courtyard or from the courtyard to the roofs. And** with regard to **vessels that were inside a courtyard** when **Shabbat** began, **it is permitted to carry them in the courtyard** even if an *eiruv* was not established, and it is likewise permitted to carry them from that courtyard to other courtyards. With regard to vessels that were **on the roofs** when **Shabbat** began, **it is permitted to carry them on the roofs, provided that one roof is neither ten** handbreadths **higher nor ten** handbreadths **lower** than the other. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Each and every one of** the roofs **is a domain in and of itself, and one may move** objects **on** each roof **only within four** cubits.

The *Gemara* announces *tanya k'vasei d'Rav* and simultaneously *tanya k'vasei d'Rabbi Yehuda* — a *baraita* has been taught that supports both Rav's ruling and Rabbi Yehuda's interpretation of the *Mishnah*'s positions.

The *baraita* states: *kol gagot ha'ir reshut achat hen* — all the rooftops of the city are one domain. This is the position of Rabbi Meir: the rooftops collectively form a single unified *reshut*. But the *baraita* immediately qualifies: *v'assur l'ha'alos u'lehorid min ha'gag la'chatzer* — it is forbidden to carry up or down between the rooftop and the courtyard, and correspondingly, from the courtyard to the rooftop. Vessels that began Shabbos in the *chatzer* may be carried within the *chatzer*; vessels that began on the roof may be carried across the rooftops. But the two genres do not communicate with each other. This elegantly confirms Rabbi Yehuda's formulation: all rooftops are one, all courtyards are one, and the two categories are mutually distinct.

The *baraita* adds: *uvilvad shelo yehei gag gavoah asarah o namuch me'chatzer* — provided that no rooftop is ten *tefachim* higher or lower than the adjacent one, which replicates the *Mishnah*'s own qualification and grounds the ruling in the earlier discussion.

### Chachamim: 4 amot limit

*Vachachamim omrim*: the *Chachamim* say *kol echad ve'echad reshut le'atzmo* — each individual rooftop is its own domain. One may not carry from rooftop A to rooftop B. And crucially: *ve'ein metaltelin bo ella be'arba* — even within a single rooftop, one may not carry more than four *amot*. This is exactly the position Rav articulated earlier in explaining the *Chachamim*: once a rooftop is fully exposed to an adjacent rooftop to which carrying is forbidden, the space is treated as compromised, and even movement within it is limited to four *amot*.

This *baraita* thus provides clean textual confirmation of both Rav's interpretation of the *Chachamim* and Rabbi Yehuda's account of Rabbi Meir.

## Oil & Towels Story

### Tekoa bathing story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: אָמַר רַבִּי, כְּשֶׁהָיִינוּ לוֹמְדִים תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּתְקוֹעַ, הָיִינוּ מַעֲלִין שֶׁמֶן וַאֲלוּנְטִית מִגַּג לְגַג, וּמִגַּג לְחָצֵר, וּמֵחָצֵר לְחָצֵר, וּמֵחָצֵר לְקַרְפֵּף, וּמִקַּרְפֵּף לְקַרְפֵּף אַחֵר, עַד שֶׁהָיִינוּ מַגִּיעִין אֵצֶל הַמַּעְיָין שֶׁהָיִינוּ רוֹחֲצִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, a *baraita* **was taught in accordance with Rav Yehuda's** interpretation of the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: When we were studying Torah with Rabbi Shimon in Tekoa, we would carry oil** for smearing **and a towel** for drying **from roof to roof, and from roof to courtyard, and from courtyard to courtyard, and from courtyard to enclosure, and from enclosure to enclosure,** to refrain from carrying in a prohibited place, **until we reached the spring in which we would bathe.**

The *Gemara* announces *tanya k'vasei d'Rabbi Yehuda* — a *baraita* supports Rabbi Yehuda's reading of Rabbi Shimon's position specifically, demonstrating that Rabbi Shimon means not that each genre is internally unified but that all genres together are one.

Rabbi states: *keshehayinu lomdim Torah etzel Rabbi Shimon biTkoa* — when we were studying Torah with Rabbi Shimon in Tekoa. On Shabbos they would bathe — with cold water or natural hot springs, which is permitted, since the prohibition on bathing in hot water stems from concern that one will come to heat the water on Shabbos. *Hayinu ma'alin shemen ve'aluntit* — they would carry oil and towels. And they would carry them *migag legag, umigag lechatzer, umichatzer lechatzer, umichatzer lekarpef, umikarpef lekarpef acher* — from roof to roof, from roof to courtyard, from courtyard to courtyard, from courtyard to *karpaf*, from one *karpaf* to another — *ad shehayinu magi'im etzel hama'ayan shehayinu rochtzin bo* — until they reached the spring where they would bathe.

This account demonstrates unmistakably that in Rabbi Shimon's academy and by his ruling, *gag*, *chatzer*, and *karpaf* were treated as a single unified domain. One may move freely between all of them. Notably, the account begins *migag* — from the rooftop — because the items must begin Shabbos within one of the outdoor domains. One cannot take something from inside the house; the oil and towels were presumably stored on the rooftop before Shabbos began.

### Torah scroll danger story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בִּשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה וְהָיִינוּ מַעֲלִין תּוֹרָה מֵחָצֵר לְגַג, וּמִגַּג לְחָצֵר, וּמֵחָצֵר לְקַרְפֵּף לִקְרוֹת בּוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין שְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה רְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** And similarly, **Rabbi Yehuda said:** There was **an incident during a time of danger,** when decrees were issued that banned religious observance, **and we would carry a Torah scroll from courtyard to roof, and from roof to courtyard, and from courtyard to enclosure, to read from it.** The Sages **said to him:** The *halakha* cannot be determined from that incident, as an incident occurring during **a time of danger is no proof.** At a time of danger it is permitted to carry even in places where carrying is ordinarily prohibited by rabbinic law.

Rabbi Yehuda then offers an additional story: *ma'aseh bish'at hasakanah* — there was an incident in a time of danger in which a *Sefer Torah* was carried *michatzer legag, umigag lechatzer, umichatzer lekarpef* — from courtyard to rooftop to courtyard to *karpaf* — in order to read from it. The *Sefer Torah* presumably could not be left in the synagogue lest it be seized, and so it was moved on Shabbos itself from one outdoor domain to another to reach the place where it was needed.

The response is immediate: *ein sha'at hasakanah re'ayah* — a time of danger cannot serve as a legal proof. When lives or sacred objects are at stake, authorities may permit what they would otherwise forbid, and one cannot derive ordinary *halacha* from emergency practice.

A notable detail: in the bathing story, the oil and towels begin *migag* — from the rooftop — underlining that the items must originate in one of the permitted outdoor domains. The *Sefer Torah* story is somewhat less clear on this point, which may be precisely why it is dismissed: one cannot be certain the movement was technically licit rather than an emergency accommodation.

The broader discussion in the *baraita* at this point touches on the question of oil and rubbing on Shabbos — *sicha* (anointing) is prohibited on Yom Kippur but not on Shabbos, and oil with the viscosity of typical oil may be rubbed on the body freely. Similarly, the question of towels and squeezing (*sechitah*) had been addressed earlier in the *masechet*.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav's ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְהוּא שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ. אֲבָל עֵירְבוּ — לָא, דְּגָזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי מָאנֵי דְבָתִּים לֶחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, provided that** the residents of each courtyard **did not establish a** separate ***eiruv*** for themselves, as in that case they may not move objects from their houses into the courtyard. **However, if they established** a separate ***eiruv*** for each courtyard, without establishing an *eiruv* between the various courtyards, **no,** that is not the *halakha*, **as we issue a decree lest one come to take out vessels** from one **of** the **houses to the courtyard,** an action that is fundamentally permitted, and subsequently proceed to carry them out to a different courtyard with which an *eiruv* had not been established, which everyone agrees is prohibited.

*Amar Rav, halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Shimon. The most expansive position prevails: *gag*, *chatzer*, and *karpaf* are all one domain, and vessels originating in any of them may be carried freely throughout all of them.

### Rav's confusion concern

But Rav immediately qualifies: *vehu shelo eirvu* — and this applies only if they did not make an *eiruv*. Not merely that A and B did not make a joint *eiruv* with each other — that would render the entire discussion moot — but that A did not even make an internal *eiruv* among its own residents.

Why does this matter? *Degazrinan dilma ati l'apokei manei debatim lechatzer* — because we are concerned that if A made an internal *eiruv*, house vessels can legitimately be brought out into A's courtyard. Now the courtyard of A contains both yard vessels (which may be carried to B) and house vessels (which may not). Rav fears that one will lose track of which vessel started where and will carry a house vessel to B in the mistaken belief that it is a yard vessel.

There is a genuine tradeoff here: if you make an *eiruv* in A, you gain the ability to bring house vessels out into A's yard, but you forfeit the ability to carry yard vessels from A to B, because the risk of confusion is too great. If you forgo the *eiruv* in A, you cannot bring house vessels out at all, but yard vessels remain mobile across all the unified domains. It is not that one option is simply better than the other — they are genuinely different modes of carrying permission, and one must choose.

### Shmuel: no confusion fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בֵּין עֵירְבוּ בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִי לְחָשְׁךָ, בֵּין עֵירְבוּ וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** The *halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Shimon, **whether they established an *eiruv* or whether they did not establish an *eiruv*. And similarly, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Who whispered to you,** who told you that there is a difference **whether they established an *eiruv* or whether they did not establish an *eiruv*?**

*U'Shmuel amar: bein eirvu bein shelo eirvu* — Shmuel disagrees. Whether A made an internal *eiruv* or not, yard vessels may still be carried from A to B. Shmuel is not afraid that people will confuse house vessels with yard vessels.

*Vechein amar Rabbi Yochanan* — and Rabbi Yochanan concurs with Shmuel. Tosafot notes the phrase used here — *mi lachshacha*, roughly "who whispered this distinction to you?" — is not common in the Talmud but appears a number of times in the *Torat Kohanim* (the *Sifra* on *Vayikra*). In context it conveys something like: on what basis do you draw this distinction between made-an-*eiruv* and did-not-make-an-*eiruv*? The allowance to carry yard vessels between courtyards is categorical, regardless of whether A has made an internal *eiruv*.

### Rav Chisda's challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: לִשְׁמוּאֵל וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, יֹאמְרוּ: שְׁנֵי כֵלִים בְּחָצֵר אַחַת, זֶה מוּתָּר וְזֶה אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda strongly objects to this** ruling. **According to Shmuel and according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** people **will say** with regard to **two vessels** located **in the same courtyard,** one of which was in the courtyard when Shabbat began while the other was in the house, that moving **this** one, which was in the courtyard at the start of Shabbat, to another courtyard is **permitted, while** moving **that one,** which was in the house at the start of Shabbat, to another courtyard, is **prohibited.**

*Metiv lei Rav Chisda le'Shmuel* — Rav Chisda raises an objection to Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan. *Yehyu shnei keilim be'chatzer achat* — you will have two vessels sitting in the same courtyard, *ze mutar ve'ze assur* — one permitted to be carried to the adjacent yard and one not. The practical impossibility of enforcing this distinction makes the ruling unworkable. If A has made an *eiruv* and both a house vessel and a yard vessel are now sitting together in A's courtyard, who can realistically remember which is which?

## R. Shimon's logic

### No gezeirah principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּלָא גָּזַר. דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה, לְשָׁלֹשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ וּפְתוּחוֹת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְעֵירְבוּ שְׁתֵּי הַחִיצוֹנוֹת עִם הָאֶמְצָעִית — הִיא מוּתֶּרֶת עִמָּהֶן, וְהֵן מוּתָּרוֹת עִמָּהּ, וּשְׁתֵּי הַחִיצוֹנוֹת אֲסוּרִין זוֹ עִם זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In this regard, **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard **reasoning, as he did not issue a decree** due to these concerns. **As we learned** in a mishna, **Rabbi Shimon said: To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to three courtyards that are open into each other, and that are** also **open into a public domain.** If **the two outer** courtyards each **established an *eiruv* with the middle one,** it is **permitted** for the residents of the middle one to carry **into** the two outer ones, **and they,** the residents of the two outer ones, **are permitted** to carry **into it, but** for the residents of **the two outer** courtyards it is **prohibited** to carry **into each other,** as they did not establish an *eiruv* together.

The *Gemara* responds: *Rabbi Shimon le'taameih* — Rabbi Shimon acts consistently with his own broader reasoning. Rabbi Shimon does not issue a *gezeirah* out of concern for eventual confusion. This is not merely an assertion; it is demonstrated from a *Mishnah* we have seen earlier in the *masechet*.

### Three courtyards case

*Amar Rabbi Shimon: lamah hadavar domeh? Leshalosh chatzerot hapetuchot zo l'zo* — Rabbi Shimon compares his position to the case of three courtyards open to one another and each also opening into the *reshut harabim*. In this scenario, A made an *eiruv* with B, and C made an *eiruv* with B, but A and C did not make an *eiruv* with each other. The result is: B may carry freely with A, and B may carry freely with C, but A and C are forbidden to each other.

Now consider what this looks like in practice: vessels from A may enter B, and vessels from C may also enter B. Courtyard B now contains three types of vessels — its own, A's, and C's — with different rules applying to each. A's vessels may return to A or stay in B but not go to C. C's vessels may return to C or stay in B but not go to A. B's own vessels may go anywhere. Yet Rabbi Shimon, in that *Mishnah*, was not concerned that this complexity would lead to confusion and error.

By precise analogy: if Rabbi Shimon is unconcerned that people will confuse A-vessels, B-vessels, and C-vessels in the multi-courtyard case, he will similarly be unconcerned that people will confuse house vessels with yard vessels in the A-and-B case. The *gezeirah* Rav invokes — the fear of confusion — is one Rabbi Shimon simply does not invoke.

### Conclusion: R. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא גָּזַר דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי מָאנֵי דְּהָא חָצֵר לְהָא חָצֵר, הָכִי נָמֵי לָא גָּזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי מָאנֵי דְבָתִּים לְחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in that case Rabbi Shimon **did not issue a decree** prohibiting one to carry objects from the middle courtyard to one of the outer ones **lest one come to take out vessels from this** outer **courtyard to that** outer **courtyard,** despite the fact that both sets of vessels are located in the middle courtyard. **Here too, we do not issue a decree lest one come to take out utensils** from one **of the houses to the courtyard,** and carry them to a different courtyard.

The upshot is that according to Rabbi Shimon himself, it would make no difference whether A made an internal *eiruv* or not. Even if A's yard contains both house vessels (carried out via the *eiruv*) and original yard vessels, Rabbi Shimon trusts that people will keep track. He permits yard vessels to move between the unified domains — *gag*, *chatzer*, *karpaf* — regardless. The *gezeirah* against confusion is not part of his legal reasoning.

## Balcony/Courtyard Ba (II)

### Height ownership test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר וְאַנְשֵׁי מִרְפֶּסֶת שֶׁשָּׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ, כׇּל שֶׁגָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — לַמִּרְפֶּסֶת. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — לֶחָצֵר. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁהָיוּ אֵלּוּ שֶׁל רַבִּים וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁל רַבִּים, וְעֵירְבוּ אֵלּוּ לְעַצְמָן וְאֵלּוּ לְעַצְמָן. אוֹ שֶׁל יְחִידִים שֶׁאֵין צְרִיכִין לְעָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita*: With regard to **the residents of** houses that open directly into **a courtyard and the residents of** upper stories that open onto **a balcony** from which stairs lead down to that courtyard, **who forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*** between them, **anything ten handbreadths high** in the courtyard, e.g., a mound or a pillar, is attributed **to the balcony** in terms of its use on Shabbat. The residents of the balcony may move objects between the mound or pillar and their apartments. Anything **lower than that** is attributed **to the courtyard. In what** case **is this statement,** that it is prohibited for the residents of the courtyard to carry to the balcony and vice versa, **stated?** It is in a case **where** the residents of the courtyard **were many, and** the residents of the balcony were **many, and these established an *eiruv* for themselves, and those established an *eiruv* for themselves; or** if the courtyard and the balcony were occupied by **individuals who need not establish an *eiruv*** for themselves.

The *Gemara* brings a new *baraita* concerning a *mirpeset* (balcony) and a *chatzer* (courtyard) that did not make an *eiruv* with one another. There is a structure — a mound or post — in the shared space, and the question is whether it "belongs" to the balcony level or the courtyard level for purposes of who may use it. The rule: *kol shegavoah asarah tefachim lemirpeset, pachot mikan lachatzer* — anything higher than ten *tefachim* belongs to the *mirpeset*; anything lower belongs to the *chatzer*. The *Gemara* earlier in the *masechet* had discussed the various ways to read this rule, but the principle itself is what matters here.

### Many people, no eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָיוּ שֶׁל רַבִּים, וְשָׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ — גַּג וְחָצֵר וְאַכְסַדְרָה וּמִרְפֶּסֶת כּוּלָּן רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** if the residents of the courtyard and the balcony **were many, and they forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*** for their courtyard or balcony, in that case **roof, and courtyard, and portico, and balcony are all one domain,** and it is permitted to carry from one to another any vessels that were located in any one of them when Shabbat began.

When does this height-based ownership rule apply? When there are many people living on each level and they each made their own internal *eiruv* — A's people made an *eiruv* among themselves, B's people made an *eiruv* among themselves — but they did not make a joint *eiruv* between the two levels. In that scenario the distinction between their spaces is meaningful, and the ownership of the mound matters.

But the *baraita* then states: if there were many people on each level and nobody made any *eiruv* at all — not even an internal one — then *gag, chatzer, achsadra, umirpeset kulan reshut achat hen* — rooftop, courtyard, *achsadra* (portico), and balcony are all one unified domain. The ownership question dissolves, because anything originating in any of these spaces may move freely among all of them.

### Listed vs. omitted spaces

Strikingly, the *baraita*'s list — *gag, chatzer, achsadra, umirpeset* — does not include *karpaf* or *mavoi*. This is not an accident. All four listed spaces share a characteristic: they are areas of regular or at least routine use. The *chatzer* is the yard directly in front of the house, used daily; the rooftop is accessible and functional; the *achsadra* is a portico or hallway through which people regularly pass; the *mirpeset* is a balcony in regular use. The *karpaf*, by contrast, is a more remote enclosed space, less frequently used, more like a vacant lot. The *Gemara* understands this omission as principled: the *karpaf* belongs to a different legal-functional category.

## Balcony/Courtyard Ba (II)

### Baraita is Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּלֹא עֵירְבוּ, הָא עֵירְבוּ לָא! הָא מַנִּי — רַבָּנַן הִיא. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּלָא קָתָנֵי קַרְפֵּף וּמָבוֹי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The reason** that carrying between them is permitted is **that they did not** each **establish** their own ***eiruv*;** however, if **they** each **established** their own ***eiruv*, no,** it is prohibited to carry between them. This inference supports Rav and poses a difficulty for Shmuel. The Gemara answers: **Whose** opinion is represented by **this** *baraita*? **It is** that of **the Rabbis,** who maintain that roofs and courtyards form a single domain, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. The Rabbis indeed issued a decree that carrying is prohibited if each group established a separate *eiruv*. The Gemara comments: **The language of the *baraita* is also precise, as it is not teaching** the cases of **an enclosure and an alleyway,** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, but only the cases of a roof and a courtyard, in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from here** that this is the correct understanding.

The *Gemara* draws the inference: this *baraita* cannot represent Rabbi Shimon's position, because Rabbi Shimon — as we know from the bathing story and from the three-courtyards *Mishnah* — includes *karpaf* along with *gag* and *chatzer* in the unified domain, and does not draw a line that excludes less-frequented spaces. The *baraita*, by omitting *karpaf*, must represent the *Chachamim*. This confirms that the *Chachamim* hold a genuine middle position: they unify the regularly-used outdoor spaces (*gag, chatzer, achsadra, mirpeset*) but treat *karpaf* and *mavoi* as distinct genres.

## Five courts & alley

### Setup: no eruv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ וּפְתוּחוֹת לְמָבוֹי, וְשָׁכְחוּ כּוּלָּם וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ — אָסוּר לְהַכְנִיס וּלְהוֹצִיא מֵחָצֵר לַמָּבוֹי וּמִן הַמָּבוֹי לֶחָצֵר. וְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בֶּחָצֵר — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָן בֶּחָצֵר, וּבַמָּבוֹי אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** from another *baraita*: With regard to **five courtyards that are open to each other and are** also **open to an alleyway, and** the residents of **all** the courtyards **forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*, it is prohibited to carry in or carry out, from a courtyard to the alleyway or from the alleyway to a courtyard. And** with regard to **vessels that were in the courtyard** when **Shabbat** began, **it is permitted to carry them in the courtyard, but in the alleyway it is prohibited** to carry them.

The *Gemara* now introduces a case involving five courtyards open to one another and all opening into a *mavoi* (alley): *chamesh chatzerot hapetuchot zo l'zo ufetuchot lemavoi, veshachachu kulam velo eirvu* — all of them forgot and did not make an *eiruv*, not even an internal one among themselves.

### Chachamim: distinct domains

The ruling that follows: *assur lehachnis ulehotzi mechatzer lemavoi umin hamavoi lechatzer* — it is forbidden to carry from courtyard to *mavoi* or from *mavoi* to courtyard. This is assumed to represent the position of the *Chachamim*, who, as we have now established, treat *mavoi* (like *karpaf*) as a domain categorically distinct from the regularly-used outdoor spaces. Even though nobody made any *eiruv* — so there is no concern of confusing house vessels with yard vessels — the *Chachamim* still prohibit movement between *chatzer* and *mavoi* because these are different *genres* of domain, not merely different individual spaces.

### Within each domain

*Aval kelim sheshavtu bechatzer mutar letaltelam bechatzer* — but vessels that began Shabbos in the courtyard may be carried within the courtyard freely. The *chatzer* is internally unified. However — and here is something striking — *uvemavoi assur*: vessels in the *mavoi* may not be freely carried even within the *mavoi* itself. Why the *mavoi* should be worse in this respect than the *chatzer* is a question the *Gemara* will take up shortly.

### R. Shimon adds karpef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל רַבִּים, וְשָׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ, גַּג וְחָצֵר וְאַכְסַדְרָה וּמִרְפֶּסֶת וְקַרְפֵּף וּמָבוֹי — כּוּלָּן רְשׁוּת אַחַת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon permits** doing so, **as Rabbi Shimon would say: Whenever** the courtyards **are** in the possession **of many** people, **and they forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*,** the **roof, and courtyard, and portico, and balcony, and enclosure, and alleyway are all one domain.** These areas are all classified as private domains, and therefore, it is permitted to carry from one to the other.

*Rabbi Shimon omer* — Rabbi Shimon allows carrying from *chatzer* to *mavoi*. His formulation: *kol zman shehen shel rabbim veshachachu velo eirvu* — as long as many people live there and none of them made an *eiruv* — then *gag, chatzer, achsadra, mirpeset, ve'karpaf umavoi* — all of these, including *karpaf* and *mavoi*, *kulan reshut achat hen*, are one domain. This confirms the full reach of Rabbi Shimon's position: nothing is excluded. The *Chachamim*'s categorical distinction between the regularly-used spaces and the less-used *karpaf* and *mavoi* does not feature in Rabbi Shimon's reasoning at all.

## R. Shimon's concession

### Why 'no eruv'?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּלֹא עֵירְבוּ, הָא עֵירְבוּ — לָא. מַאי ״לֹא עֵירְבוּ״: לֹא עֵירְבוּ חֲצֵירוֹת בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, הָא חָצֵר וּבָתִּים — עֵירְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The reason** that carrying between them is permitted is **that they did not** each **establish** their own ***eiruv*;** however, if **they** each **established** their own ***eiruv*, no,** it is prohibited to carry between them. This inference supports Rav and poses a difficulty for Shmuel. The Gemara answers: **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **They did not establish an *eiruv*?** It means that the residents of the **courtyards did not establish an *eiruv* with each other; however, the** residents of each **courtyard established an *eiruv*** with the residents of **the houses** inside it, and it is nevertheless permitted to carry between them, in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel.

The *Gemara* immediately questions Rabbi Shimon's formulation. He specifies *velo eirvu* — and they did not make an *eiruv*. Does this mean that his leniency applies *only* if no *eiruv* was made? The implication would be that if A did make an internal *eiruv*, Rabbi Shimon would be more restrictive — which seems inconsistent with the position we have established for him.

### Meaning of 'no eruv'

The *Gemara* offers a first resolution: *mai lo eirvu? Lo eirvu chatzerot bahadi hadadi* — what does "they did not make an *eiruv*" mean? It means they did not make the grand collective *eiruv* — the *shituf* — that would have unified all the courtyards and the *mavoi* into a single shared space. It does *not* mean that no individual *eiruv* was made within courtyard A. In other words, Rabbi Shimon is saying: even in the scenario where the big collective *eiruv* was not made — a scenario that requires a ruling about what may be done — I will tell you that yard vessels may still move freely between all these spaces.

### Concession to opponents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״לֹא עֵירְבוּ״ קָתָנֵי! מַאי ״לֹא עֵירְבוּ״: לֹא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְדִבְרֵיהֶם דְּרַבָּנַן קָאֲמַר לְהוּ: לְדִידִי לָא שְׁנָא עֵירְבוּ, וְלָא שְׁנָא לָא עֵירְבוּ. אֶלָּא לְדִידְכוּ, אוֹדוֹ לִי מִיהַת דְּהֵיכָא דְּלֹא עֵירְבוּ רְשׁוּת אַחַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty. **But doesn't** the *baraita* **state: They did not establish an *eiruv*,** indicating that they did not establish any *eiruv* at all, either with the residents of the other courtyard or within each courtyard? The Gemara rejects this argument. **What** is the meaning of: **They did not establish an *eiruv*?** It means that **they did not merge** the courtyards facing the alleyway. **And if you wish, say** instead: **Rabbi Shimon is speaking to the Rabbis in accordance with their** own **opinion,** not enumerating the leniencies inherent in his own ruling. His statement should therefore be understood as follows: **According to my own** opinion, **there is no difference** if **they established an *eiruv* and there is no difference** if **they did not establish an *eiruv*. However, according to your** opinion, **agree with me at least that** in a case **where they did not establish an *eiruv* it is** all considered **one domain.**

The *Gemara* offers an alternative interpretation: *Rabbi Shimon ledivreihemdeRabbanan ka'amar* — Rabbi Shimon is formulating his ruling in terms that concede a premise to those who argue with him. From Rabbi Shimon's own perspective, *lo shena eirvu velo shena lo eirvu* — it makes no difference whether an *eiruv* was made in A or not; he is not afraid of vessel confusion regardless. But he addresses his opponents on their own terms: even if you, the *Rabbanan*, would be concerned about confusion when an *eiruv* exists in A and house vessels mix with yard vessels in the courtyard — *aval odu li mihat d'heicha d'lo eirvu* — at least concede to me that where no *eiruv* was made, where there is simply no possibility of a house vessel having entered the courtyard, you should agree that yard vessels may move freely from *chatzer* to *mavoi*.

### Chachamim: genre difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: לָא — שְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis said to him: No,** although we agree with you in the cases of a roof, courtyard, portico, and balcony, in the cases of an enclosure and an alleyway we disagree, as **they are two domains** and therefore it is prohibited to carry from one to the other.

*Ve'amru lei Rabbanan*: the *Rabbanan* respond — *lo*. Even that concession we will not make. We are not restricting movement from *chatzer* to *mavoi* out of fear of vessel confusion. We restrict it because *shear reshuyot bamavoi asurin* — the *mavoi* is a categorically different type of domain from the courtyard. The prohibition is not remedied by the absence of an *eiruv*; it is structural. The *Chachamim*'s middle position does not rest on confusion concerns at all — it rests on a principled taxonomy of domain types.

## Mavoi vessel status

### Mavoi-origin vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וּבַמָּבוֹי אָסוּר. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. אֵימָא: וּלְמָבוֹי — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above in the *baraita*: Vessels that were in a courtyard at the start of Shabbat may be carried within the courtyard, **but in the alleyway it is prohibited.** The Gemara asks: **Let us say** that this **supports that** which **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav said, as Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav said:** In **an alleyway in which they did not merge** the courtyards facing it, **one may carry only within four cubits.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion. **Say** that the *baraita* means: **But to an alleyway it is prohibited,** i.e., it is prohibited to carry from the courtyard to the alleyway; however, within the alleyway itself it is permitted to carry.

The *Gemara* now focuses on an unexplained element of the *baraita*: *uvemavoi assur* — vessels originating in the *mavoi* may not be freely carried even within the *mavoi* itself. This is asymmetric with the *chatzer*: courtyard vessels may be carried freely throughout the courtyard even without an *eiruv*, but *mavoi* vessels apparently cannot be carried freely within the *mavoi*. The *Gemara* asks whether this supports the position of Rabbi Zeira.

### Rabbi Zeira: 4 amot limit

*De'amar Rabbi Zeira amar Rav*: Rabbi Zeira stated in the name of Rav — *mavoi shelo nishtatfu bo, ein metaltelin ella be'arba amot* — a *mavoi* in which no *shituf* (the alley-level communal contribution) was made allows carrying only four *amot*. This parallels the ruling about the rooftop according to the *Chachamim*: just as the *Chachamim* limit carrying on a single rooftop to four *amot* because the roof is exposed to adjacent forbidden space, so too the *mavoi* without a *shituf* is restricted to four *amot*.

The *Gemara* then reinterprets the *baraita*'s phrase *uvemavoi assur*: it does not mean that vessels originating in the *mavoi* may not be carried within the *mavoi*. Rather, *u-lemavoi assur* — one may not carry *to* the *mavoi* from the *chatzer*. The *baraita* is restating, for emphasis, the point already established: that the *Chachamim* categorically forbid movement between *chatzer* and *mavoi* regardless of whether an *eiruv* existed.

### Why mavoi is stricter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא! מִשְׁנָה יַתִּירָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: כִּי פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָנֵי מִילֵּה הֵיכָא דְּעֵירְבוּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא עֵירְבוּ — מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty. If so, **that is** identical to **the first clause** of the *baraita*. The *tanna* would not have taught the very same thing twice. The Gemara answers: **The** apparently **superfluous teaching was necessary, lest you say: When the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Shimon, it is only** in a case **where they established an *eiruv*, but** in a case **where they did not establish an *eiruv*,** the Rabbis **concede to** Rabbi Meir that it is all considered one domain and carrying is permitted. The *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Shimon in both cases, as they prohibit carrying in the alleyway even if the residents did not establish an *eiruv*.

Why should the *mavoi* without a *shituf* be more restricted than the *chatzer* without an *eiruv*? The *Gemara* suggests one possible explanation: a *mavoi*, as a street-like passage, has some of the qualities of a *carmelit* — the semi-public intermediate domain — and therefore receives correspondingly stricter treatment. Other *sugyot* in the *Gemara* ground this restriction differently, invoking concerns about confusion of vessel types. The *Gemara* here does not fully resolve the question, but the *carmelit* analogy stands as a reasonable account.

The *Gemara* then adds a further note: the *Rabbanan* genuinely mean their categorical position. One might think that when they prohibit movement from *chatzer* to *mavoi*, they do so only because an *eiruv* was made in the courtyard and they fear confusion about house vessels. *Kama'shma lan* — the *baraita*'s reiteration comes to teach us that is not so. Even where no *eiruv* was made at all and there is no conceivable confusion, the *Chachamim* still hold that *chatzer* and *mavoi* are distinct domains, and one may not carry between them.

### Three-position summary

The full three-position framework can now be stated clearly. Rabbi Meir: each domain type — *gag*, *chatzer*, *karpaf* — is internally unified but externally separate from the others. The *Chachamim*: the regularly-used outdoor spaces (*gag*, *chatzer*, *achsadra*, *mirpeset*) are unified with one another, but *karpaf* and *mavoi* are categorically separate. Rabbi Shimon: all outdoor spaces — *gag*, *chatzer*, *achsadra*, *mirpeset*, *karpaf*, and *mavoi* — are one unified domain, and vessels originating in any of them may move freely throughout all of them. The further debate between Rav and Shmuel — whether Rabbi Shimon's leniency applies even when an internal *eiruv* has been made in one of the courtyards — turns on whether Rabbi Shimon is concerned about confusion between house vessels and yard vessels. The answer is that he is not: consistent with his position in the three-courtyards case, Rabbi Shimon makes no *gezeirah* against such confusion.
