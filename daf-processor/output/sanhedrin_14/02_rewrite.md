# Sanhedrin 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Semikhat Zekeinim: 3 vs 1

### Can One Person Give Semi…

The *mishnah* established that *semikhat zekeinim* — the classical *semikhah* tracing its lineage all the way back to Moshe Rabbeinu, conferring the authority to represent God, to be called a *beit din* of *Elohim*, and to adjudicate *dinei kenasos*, administer *malkos*, and carry out *misos beit din* — requires three participants. The *gemara*, picking up four lines from the bottom of 13b, immediately challenges this: *v'chad lo samikh?* Is it truly impossible for a single individual to confer *semikhah*?

The challenge is rooted in a tradition cited by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *baram zakhor oso ish letov, v'Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava shemo* — this man should be remembered for good, and his name is Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava — *she-ilmalei hu nishtakhu dinei kenasos mi-Yisrael*, for were it not for what he did, the laws of *kenas* would have been forgotten among the Jewish people.

The *gemara* immediately presses the language: what does it mean they would have been "forgotten"? Surely the laws themselves could simply be relearned. The answer: the word *nishtakhu* must be understood not as a forgetting of legal content but as a complete cessation of authority — *batlu dinei kenasos mi-Yisrael*. The entire chain of *semikhah*, and with it the power to actually adjudicate *kenasos*, administer *malkos*, and carry out *misos beit din*, would have been irrevocably severed.

### Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava's…

The historical context is the Hadrianic persecutions, the period of Rabbi Akiva and his students. The Roman government had issued a series of sweeping decrees: anyone who confers *semikhah* will be killed; anyone who receives *semikhah* will be killed; any city in which *semikhah* takes place will be laid waste; and anything within the city limits of those cities will be uprooted.

Confronted with this existential threat to the chain of *semikhah*, Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava acted with extraordinary ingenuity and courage. He positioned himself *bein shnei harim gedolim u-vein shtei ayaros gedolos u-vein shnei techumei Shabbos* — between two large mountains, between two large cities, and between the *techumim* of two population centers: *bein Usha l'Shefaram*. Usha, of course, was itself a major seat of the *beit din*. By situating himself in open territory between cities, no single city could be destroyed, and the sheer impracticality of simultaneously destroying two major cities offered a measure of protection to the cities themselves, if not to the participants directly.

There he conferred *semikhah* upon five elders: Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Shimon, Rabbi Yosi, and Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua — the classic generation of *talmidei Rabbi Akiva*, as we shall see momentarily. Rav Abba adds that Rabbi Nechemiah was also among the recipients.

When the enemies discovered what was happening — and one wonders about the historical realities that prevented this from being conducted in secrecy — Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava urged those who had received *semikhah* to flee: *bnai rutzu*. When they asked what would become of him, he answered, *hareini mutal lifneihem k'even she-ein lah hofkhin* — I will stand before them as an immovable stone. The story ends with a stark image: *v'asu oto k'khivra* — they made him like a sieve, pierced by three hundred iron lances. The precise mechanics of how he held off the soldiers long enough for the others to escape, and why the *semikhah* could not be conducted covertly, are not elaborated upon by the *gemara*, though one can speculate about the historical realities of that moment.

### Three Were Present

This powerful story seems to present a difficulty: it sounds as though Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava acted alone, yet the *mishnah* requires three for *semikhah*. The *gemara* resolves this cleanly: *achrini hava bahadei* — there were two others with him. *V'hai d'lo khashiv lehu mishum kevodo d'Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava* — the reason they are not mentioned is out of deference to his honor. He was the one who mattered; the story wanted to be told about him. The other two, who must themselves have possessed *semikhah*, accompanied him and participated, but they were secondary figures in what was fundamentally his act of heroism. One wonders, of course, what became of those two as well.

### Rabbi Meir's Semikha Cha…

The *gemara* now turns to a detail within the story: was it truly Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava who conferred *semikhah* upon Rabbi Meir? Rabba bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol ha'omer Rabbi Meir lo samcho Rabbi Akiva eino ela to'eh* — whoever says that Rabbi Akiva did not give *semikhah* to Rabbi Meir is simply mistaken. This is a striking formulation, clearly directed against some tradition or claim to the contrary, and it underscores that these figures were all, at their core, *talmidei Rabbi Akiva*.

The resolution: Rabbi Akiva did indeed confer *semikhah* upon Rabbi Meir, but *lo kibluhu* — the community did not accept him. Rashi explains, three lines from the top of 14a: *l'fi she-adayin haya bachur* — perhaps Rabbi Meir was still too young, or had the learning but not yet the gravitas, and the community was not prepared to recognize his authority. Subsequently, when Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava conferred *semikhah* upon him — *samchei Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava v'kibluhu* — the community accepted it. Perhaps Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava's standing was of a different order, or perhaps Rabbi Meir had by then matured to the point where the community was ready to recognize him. This also may shed light on how the ceremony became known to the Romans: once someone is elevated to a position of authority and publicly recognized as such by a community, it is difficult to keep that quiet.

## Semikha Outside Eretz Yi…

### No Semikha in Chutz LaAr…

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi now asserts a geographic restriction: *ein semikhah b'chutz la'aretz* — *semikhah* cannot be conferred outside of the Land of Israel. This is somewhat analogous to the principle that *nevuah* is fundamentally rooted in *Eretz Yisrael*: the authority being transmitted traces back to Moshe and ultimately represents a kind of divine presence, and the source of that presence is anchored in the Land.

But what precisely does *ein semikhah b'chutz la'aretz* mean? One might have thought it means that even *semikhah* conferred in *Eretz Yisrael* does not grant its holder the authority to adjudicate once he is in *chutz la'aretz*. That reading is immediately rejected: *v'ha tnan* — the *mishnah* in Makkos states explicitly that *Sanhedrin noheget bein ba'aretz bein b'chutz la'aretz* — the *Sanhedrin* can function in *chutz la'aretz*. Indeed, the concept of *shlikhuta'ihu kavidnan* — even where full *semikhah* no longer exists, we act as agents of a prior *beit din* that did possess it — and certainly a holder of actual *semikhah* retains his authority wherever he goes.

The correct reading of the restriction, therefore, is simply this: *d'lo samkhinan b'chutz la'aretz* — the act of conferring *semikhah* itself must take place in *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Proximity Requirement

But is the rule really about *Eretz Yisrael* as such, or is it about something else? The *gemara* presses further. One obvious case is already clear: if those conferring *semikhah* are in *chutz la'aretz* and those receiving it are in *Eretz Yisrael*, the absence of the *mesmakhim* from the Land plainly disqualifies the act. That is *peshita* — obvious, since the authority must flow from within *Eretz Yisrael*.

The more interesting question is the reverse: *somkhin ba'aretz v'nismakhin b'chutz la'aretz* — the conferring authorities are in *Eretz Yisrael*, and the recipients are in *chutz la'aretz*. Given that the *gemara* elsewhere noted that *semikhah* does not require physical hand-laying — *samchinan lei bishmei*, one simply declares the person a *rebbe* — one might envision a scenario where the grantors in *Eretz Yisrael* confer *semikhah* by declaration upon someone across the border. Would this work, since the source of authority is firmly in the Land?

The *gemara* poses this question, and the evidence suggests that the real issue is not the geographic category of *chutz la'aretz* per se, but rather the requirement of physical proximity between the grantor and the recipient.

### Physical Presence Exampl…

Several narratives converge to establish this point. First: *Rabbi Yochanan hava mitzta'er alei de-Rav Shemen bar Abba d'lo hava gabei d'lismachiei* — Rabbi Yochanan was deeply anguished that Rav Shemen bar Abba was never in the same location as him and that he was therefore never able to confer *semikhah* upon him. The very fact that Rabbi Yochanan's anguish was precisely that they were never together — and not that he simply sent a letter of appointment — demonstrates that physical co-presence is a prerequisite.

A second set of narratives follows. Rabbi Shimon ben Zirod and Rabbi Yonatan ben Achmai — with some versions of the tradition reversing which name belongs to which role — both sought *semikhah*. The one who was present at the time received it; the one who was not present did not. Here too the *gemara* notes that the verb *samchuhu* is in the plural, indicating that the story is not specifically about Rabbi Yochanan but about whoever the *mesmakhim* were in that setting — and in both cases, presence determined the outcome.

A third narrative: Rabbi Yochanan was working to confer *semikhah* upon Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Oshaya — *havu kamishtadlei Rabbi Yochanan lemismachinhu* — yet *lo hava mistaya milta*, it simply never came together. Rabbi Yochanan was anguished. At this point, Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Oshaya themselves told him not to be distressed, because they were descendants of the house of Eli: *d'anan m'devei Eli katinan*. And Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan that we know from the verse *velo yihyeh zaken b'veitcha kol hayamim* — there will not be an elder in your house all the days — that no descendant of Eli's house would ever attain *semikhah*.

The *gemara* asks what *zaken* means here. If it means literally growing old in years — well, the verse already says elsewhere that the men of his house would die while still young men. Rather, the curse refers specifically to the status of *zaken* as elder and authority: *ela semikhah* — no one of the house of Eli would ever receive *semikhah*. So Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Oshaya were telling Rabbi Yochanan: the obstacle was not logistical, it was metaphysical — it was simply not meant to be.

## Rabbi Zeira & Authority

### Zeira's Reluctance

*Rabbi Zeira hava mitmar lemismichei* — Rabbi Zeira was hiding so as not to receive *semikhah*. His reluctance was grounded in the teaching of Rabbi Elazar: *le'olam hevei kabil v'kayam* — always be inconspicuous, dark, and you will survive. The implicit logic: positions of authority bring exposure, responsibility, and *tzaros*. With *semikhah* comes not only the power to adjudicate *dinei kenasos*, but the full weight of public rabbinic authority, of representing the tradition with all that entails. Rabbi Zeira preferred to remain in the shadows.

### Authority & Atonement

But when Rabbi Zeira then encountered a different teaching attributed to Rabbi Elazar — *ein adam oleh ligdulah ela im ken mochlin lo al kol avonotav* — that no one ascends to a position of authority unless Heaven has forgiven all his sins — the calculus changed. If accepting authority is itself a vehicle for *kaparah*, then the willingness to shoulder that burden is not merely sacrifice; it carries a profound spiritual benefit. Upon hearing this, *amtzei lei anafshei* — Rabbi Zeira made himself available to receive *semikhah*.

### Song for Rabbi Zeira

At the ceremony of *semikhah*, songs were composed in honor of the recipient. For Rabbi Zeira — the one who had been reluctant, the one who did not seek the honor — they sang: *lo kachal v'lo srak v'lo pirchus v'ya'alat chen* — without eye shadow, without rouge, without hair adornment, and yet the *kallah* is beautiful. The natural beauty shines through. Rabbi Zeira, unpretentious and genuinely a *talmid chacham*, needed no adornment; his inner qualities were self-evident.

### Community Acceptance

The songs varied depending on the recipient. For Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi, the community sang: *kol min dein kol min dein somchu lana* — anyone like this, anyone like this, give *semikhah* for our sake. The community was celebrating not merely the individual but the quality he represented, expressing the wish that more such figures would receive *semikhah*. The flip side was also expressed: *lo tismchu lana lo misarmitin v'lo misarmisin* — do not give *semikhah* for our sake to the unworthy. Rashi explains these terms as referring to people who are puffed up but empty of *Torah*, or who pervert its teachings. Another version of the formula: *lo machmisin v'lo matremisin* — not those who withhold *Torah*, not those who compromise their integrity, with the last term left unclear even by Rashi. The community is declaring its investment in the quality of those who receive the mantle of authority.

Finally, a vivid vignette: when Rabbi Abbahu would come from the *yeshiva* to the Caesar's house — we have seen in other contexts that certain rabbis had dealings with the Roman government — the matrons of the imperial household would come out and sing in his honor: *rabba d'amei, midbarna d'umsei, botzina d'nehura, brich maseich l'shalom* — the great one of his people, the leader of his nation, the candle of light, blessed is your coming in peace. This language, notably, is the Aramaic that will later be used extensively in the *Zohar*. One imagines the women sang in Greek or Latin rather than Aramaic, but the content has been preserved in these terms.

With that, the *gemara*'s treatment of *semikhat zekeinim* concludes.

## Arifat Egla: 3 vs 5

### Basic Dispute

The *mishnah* stated: *semikhat zekeinim va'arifat egla bishlosha divrei Rabbi Shimon; Rabbi Yehuda omer b'chamisha* — *arifat egla* requires three according to Rabbi Shimon, and five according to Rabbi Yehuda. A *beraita* now unpacks the derivations on both sides.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

The verse reads: *v'yatzu zekeinecha v'shofte-cha* — your elders and your judges will go out. Rabbi Yehuda reads: *zekeinecha* — two; *v'shofte-cha* — two more; and since a *beit din* cannot be evenly numbered, one more is added — *harei kan chamisha*, a total of five.

### Rabbi Shimon's Position

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, reads only *zekeinecha* — two — and since a *beit din* cannot be even, adds one more — *harei kan shlosha*, a total of three. He does not derive an additional two from the word *shofte-cha*.

The *gemara* immediately asks: what does Rabbi Shimon do with the word *shofte-cha*? He requires it to teach something else: *miyuchadim she-b'shofte-cha* — it is the select ones among your judges, meaning this must come from the *beit din hagadol*, not from an ordinary court.

And what does Rabbi Yehuda do to derive *beit din hagadol* without relying on *shofte-cha* for that purpose? He derives it from *zekeinecha* itself: since the verse could have written the simpler *ziknei* and instead wrote the possessive *zekeinecha*, that indicates the special, select elders — the *beit din hagadol*.

Rabbi Shimon responds: he needed *zekeinecha* precisely because without it he would have read *ziknei* to mean merely elderly people from the marketplace — *ziknei hashuk* — not judges at all. So *zekeinecha* establishes that these are genuine judicial authorities, but not necessarily the *beit din hagadol* — it would have implied only the smaller *sanhedrin* of twenty-three. It is the word *shofte-cha* that elevates the requirement to the *beit din hagadol* through the phrase *miyuchadim she-b'shofte-cha*. Rabbi Shimon therefore cannot use *shofte-cha* to add two more judges; it is fully occupied with its other function.

Rabbi Yehuda, for his part, derives *beit din hagadol* through a *gzera shava*: *gamir ziknei ziknei* — there is a verbal analogy between *ziknei* here and *v'samchu ziknei ha'eida es yadeihem* by the *par helam davar shel tzibur*, the case where the *beit din hagadol* errs in its *hora'ah*. That context unambiguously involves the *beit din hagadol*, and the shared term *ziknei ha'eida* — the ה' indicating "the special ones of the congregation" — establishes that the *zekenim* of our verse are likewise the *beit din hagadol*. Having derived *beit din hagadol* from the *gzera shava*, Rabbi Yehuda can then use *shofte-cha* for its numerical function: the *vav* of *v'shofte-cha* adds two more judges to the count.

The *gemara* raises a further question: if Rabbi Yehuda is making a *gzera shava* to *par helam davar*, why not derive the number itself from there as well, making *shofte-cha* unnecessary? The answer: it is specifically the *vav* of *v'shofte-cha* — the conjunctive prefix — that Rabbi Yehuda reads as a numerical signal, adding two to the count. Rabbi Shimon does not interpret the *vav* this way — *Rabbi Shimon vav lo darish* — and therefore draws no numerical inference from it.

The *gemara* then raises an arithmetical challenge to both positions: the verse also uses the verbs *v'yatzu* — they went out — and *u'madedu* — they measured — each of which could similarly be read as implying two additional participants, driving the totals to nine and seven respectively. The *gemara* responds that these verbs are required for a different teaching entirely: *v'yatzu hein v'lo shluchan* — they themselves must go out and perform the measurement; they cannot send representatives. And *u'madedu* teaches that even when the proximity of the body to a particular city is obvious — *she'afilu nimtza be'alil l'ir* — the act of measuring is still obligatory as a *mitzvah*. This discussion carries into the top of 14b.

## Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov

### Eliezer ben Yaakov's View

Having established the three-versus-five dispute between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda, the *gemara* notes that the *mishnah* does not accord with a third *tanna* altogether: *matnitin d'lo ki hai tanna*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds an entirely different position. Reading the same verse — *v'yatzu zekeinecha v'shofte-cha* — he interprets *zekeinecha* not as a small delegation but as the full *Sanhedrin*: *zekeinecha zu Sanhedrin*. And *shofte-cha* — your judges — refers not to additional judicial members but to the political and religious leadership of the nation: *zeh melech v'kohen gadol* — a king and a *kohen gadol*.

The king is derived from the verse *melech b'mishpat ya'amid aretz* — the king through justice establishes the land — associating royal authority with the judicial process. The *kohen gadol* is derived from *u'vata el ha-kohanim ha-leviyim v'el ha-shofet* — which is admittedly a somewhat unusual reading, since that verse distinguishes the *kohanim* from the *shofet*, but the association is drawn nonetheless. For Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, the gravity of *egla arufa* — a case of unresolved death with no perpetrator found, and the communal responsibility to declare that one's hands did not shed this blood — demands the presence of the entire national leadership: the *Sanhedrin*, the king, and the *kohen gadol*.

### Whole Sanhedrin Needed?

A question is then raised: does Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov dispute only on one point or on two? *Bachada pligi o bitartei pligi?* Does he merely add the king and the *kohen gadol* while otherwise agreeing with either Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Shimon regarding the number of judges — accepting three or five as the representative *beit din* — or does he additionally require that the entire *Sanhedrin* be present? *O dilma b'Sanhedrei nami pligi d'amar kula Sanhedrei ba'inan?* Given his reading of *zekeinecha zu Sanhedrin*, it would seem to imply the latter.

### Zaken Mamre Proof

Rav Yosef proposes a proof from the laws of *zaken mamre*. The *mishnah* teaches: if a rebellious elder — one who defies the ruling of the court and continues his own teaching — encounters the *Sanhedrin* outside of the *Beit Hamikdash*, at *Beit Pagi*, which is within Jerusalem but clearly not on the *Har Habayit*, and he rebels against them there — one might think his defiance still constitutes the offense of *zaken mamre*. But *talmud lomar: v'kamta v'alita el ha-makom* — you shall arise and go up to the place — *melamed she-hamakom gorem*: it is the location that determines the status. The rebellious elder acquires the designation of *zaken mamre* only if he defies the *Sanhedrin* while they are seated in their place of authority in the *Lishkat Hagazit*.

This raises the question: *d'nafak man?* — who exactly is not in the *Lishkat Hagazit*? If only some members have left, then *dilma hanach d'ika gava'i kaimi kavatei* — perhaps those remaining inside would have sided with him, and one cannot characterize his position as a defiance of established authority when the full court has not rendered a unanimous position. For the designation of *zaken mamre* to be meaningful, it must be the case that the entire *Sanhedrin* has departed — and therefore the scenario of *Beit Pagi* presupposes a situation where all of them are outside together.

### Why Sanhedrin Left

But why would the entire *Sanhedrin* be outside together? If it is for a personal, optional matter — *lidvar reshut* — the *gemara* objects: they cannot simply leave, because of the verse *sharreich agan ha-sohar al yichsar ha-mazeg* — your navel is like a round goblet, the mixed wine shall not be lacking. This phrase from *Shir Hashirim* is read as establishing that the *Lishkat Hagazit* must always maintain a minimum quorum. Rashi explains: if one member needs to leave, there must remain at least twenty-three — the equivalent of a *sanhedrin ketana*. Tosafos adds a *gematriya*: *mazeg* (מזג) equals fifty; *al yichsar ha-mazeg* means one may not be lacking fifty, leaving a minimum of twenty-two, which rounds up to twenty-three since *ein beit din shakul*. Either way, the principle holds that the *Lishkat Hagazit* cannot be vacated for a discretionary purpose.

It follows that if the entire *Sanhedrin* is absent from the *Lishkat Hagazit*, it must be for a *dvar mitzvah* — a mandatory communal purpose. And what *mitzvah* would require the entire *Sanhedrin* to convene outside? *Heichi dami lav l'medidas egla* — it must be for the measuring and the *egla arufa* procedure. This proves that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov requires the full *Sanhedrin* for *arifat egla* — not merely three or five representatives.

Abaye, however, deflects this proof: the *Sanhedrin* might have departed together for a different *mitzvah* entirely — *sheyatzu lehosif al ha'ir v'al ha'azarot* — to expand the boundaries of Jerusalem or of the *azaros* of the *Beit Hamikdash*, which, as the *mishnah* elsewhere states, requires a *beit din* of seventy-one. A second *beraita* then affirms Rav Yosef's position explicitly, spelling out that the scenario where the *zaken mamre* finds the court at *Beit Pagi* encompasses two cases: the court had left either for the measuring of the *egla* or to expand the city and *azaros* boundaries.

## Minimum Sanhedrin Present

### Cannot Lack Minimum

With the conclusion that the entire *Sanhedrin* must not be absent from the *Lishkat Hagazit* for a discretionary purpose, the *gemara* articulates the underlying principle: one cannot simply walk out and reduce the assembly below a certain threshold.

### Mixed Wine Metaphor

The source is the verse from *Shir Hashirim*: *sharreich agan ha-sohar al yichsar ha-mazeg* — your navel is like a round goblet; the mixed wine shall not be lacking. Rashi explains the application directly: if one of the *Sanhedrin* must leave, it is permitted only if there remain at least twenty-three, corresponding to the *sanhedrin ketana*; if not, he may not leave.

Tosafos develops the image of mixed wine further. *De'al chad terei* — mixed wine is one part wine to two parts water. Just as the wine remains the essential component — the minimum that must be present to call the mixture wine at all — so too the minimum essential core, the *sanhedrin ketana* of twenty-three, must remain in the *Lishkat Hagazit*. The *gematriya* of *mazeg* adds a complementary derivation: fifty, the number one may not be "lacking," meaning one may not remove fifty members, leaving at least twenty-two — which, given the rule of *ein beit din shakul*, becomes twenty-three.

The practical upshot is that the *Sanhedrin* as a body can leave the *Lishkat Hagazit* only for a genuine *dvar mitzvah*, such as the *egla* measurement or the expansion of the city's sacred boundaries, and even then the *Lishkat Hagazit* must retain a *sanhedrin ketana*.

## Neta Revai & Maaser Sheni

### Uncertain Value Produce

The *mishnah* states that *neta revai* and *maaser sheni* of uncertain value require three parties for redemption. To clarify: *maaser sheni* — the second tithe taken in years one, two, four, and five of the *shemitah* cycle — was ideally brought to Jerusalem. If the owner did not wish to transport the produce, he could redeem it onto money and bring the money to Jerusalem to spend there. Similarly, *neta revai* — the fruit of the fourth year of a tree's growth, following the three years of *orlah* — could be redeemed. The *gemara* now explains what the *mishnah* means by "uncertain value."

The *beraita* specifies: *eizo hu maaser sheni she'ein damav yedu'in?* What constitutes *maaser sheni* of uncertain value? Three cases: produce that has rotted — *peiros she-hirkivu* — and can now be sold only as animal fodder; wine that has developed a film — *yayin she-hikrim* — making it substandard; or coins that have become rusted — *ma'os she-hechlidu*. In each case, there is no standard market price, and judgment is required to assess value. The fear is that without external oversight, the owner may not be fully objective in his assessment.

### Three Assessors, Not Jud…

The *beraita* then clarifies who these three must be: *maaser sheni she'ein damav yedu'in, podno oto b'shlosha lakuchos* — one redeems it with three buyers, meaning three commercial experts who are practiced in assessment. Rashi defines *lakuchos* as *socharim b'ki'in b'shuma* — merchants who are skilled in appraisal.

This is striking, because the entire context of the *mishnah* is the authority of *beit din*, yet here the "three" has nothing to do with a *beit din* at all — it is simply a panel of independent assessors. Two proofs confirm this: first, the *beraita* states *aval lo b'shlosha she'einan lakuchos* — three non-experts will not suffice, regardless of their judicial standing; second, and definitively, *v'afilu nochri echad meihem* — a non-Jew can serve as one of the three. Since a non-Jew cannot be part of a *beit din*, this conclusively establishes that what we are dealing with is not a judicial panel but an objective commercial appraisal. The inclusion of this *halakhah* under the heading of the *mishnah*'s discussion of *beit din* numbers is therefore noteworthy. Finally, the *beraita* permits even one of the three to be the owner himself — *v'afilu echad meihem ba'al ha-bayit* — since he presumably has expertise in his own produce; but he alone cannot assess, as his objectivity cannot be assumed, and he therefore requires two independent parties alongside him.

### Shared Interests Question

Rabbi Yirmiyah raises a question: *shlosha u-matilin l'toch kis echad mahu* — what if the three assessors all pool their income into a single fund, meaning they are business partners? Does that still constitute three independent assessments?

The *gemara* brings a potential answer: *ish u-shtei nashav podin maaser sheni* — a man and his two wives can redeem *maaser sheni* together. Here is a case of three people who are functionally one household, and yet it is permitted. However, the *gemara* resists treating this as a complete proof. *Dilma k'gon Rav Papa u-vas Abba Sura* — perhaps it refers to a case like Rav Papa and the daughter of Abba Sura: Rashi, citing the tractate Ketubot, notes that she earned her own income independently and did not rely on her husband's resources. In such a case, the wife is economically independent and constitutes a genuinely separate party. The question of whether three assessors who are fully intertwined economically — who truly pool everything — can constitute an acceptable panel thus remains unresolved.

Tosafos adds that some hold the mechanism here to be auction — going by the highest bid — but notes that the discussions about wives and shared funds do not necessarily support that interpretation.

## Hekdesh Redemption

### Ten for Any Hekdesh

Having treated *neta revai* and *maaser sheni*, the *mishnah* moves to *hekdesh* — dedicated property. Here the dynamic is different: whereas *maaser sheni* is the owner's property being transferred onto money, *hekdesh* belongs to the *Beit Hamikdash*, and the transaction removes it from the domain of *hekdesh* by assessing and paying its value. The *mishnah* states that redemption of *hekdesh* requires three. But the *gemara* immediately notes: *matnitin d'lo ki hai tanna* — the *mishnah* does not accord with a competing *tanna*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — again this figure — holds that even to redeem something as minor as a small utensil of *hekdesh*, a fork perhaps, *tzricha asara bnei adam lifdosa* — ten people are required.

### Counting Kohen in Parsha

Rav Papa said to Abaye: *bishlama l'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov d'amar k'Shmuel* — I understand where Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov gets the number ten. He holds like Shmuel, *d'amar Shmuel asara kohanim kesuvin b'parsha* — Shmuel taught that the word *kohen* is written ten times in the relevant *parasha*.

This *parasha* is at the end of *Bechukotai* in *Vayikra*, chapter 27, which discusses the redemption of things dedicated to *hekdesh*. The word *kohen* appears in three different subsections. In the subsection dealing with the poor person who cannot pay his *erekh* — the fixed Torah valuation — the verse (27:8) repeats *kohen* three times: *v'he'emido lifnei ha-kohen, v'he'erich oso ha-kohen, ya'aricenu ha-kohen*. In the subsection dealing with a non-sacrificial animal dedicated to *hekdesh* (27:11), *kohen* appears three more times. And in the subsection dealing with land dedicated to *hekdesh*, the word *kohen* appears four times. Three plus three plus four equals ten. While this does not require ten *kohanim* — as will be clarified on the next *daf*, where we will see that only one of the ten must be a *kohen* — the ten appearances of the word establish the number ten as the required quorum for the redemption assessment.

### Rabbis Say Three

Having understood Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position, the obvious question is: *ela rabanan shlosha mna lehu* — from where do the Rabbis derive their number of three? The natural answer would seem to be that since the relevant subsection for *hekdesh* (the non-sacrificial animal) contains three mentions of *kohen*, three is the applicable number for that category.

### Land vs Hekdesh Numbers

But this creates an immediate difficulty: *vechi teima dechsivi behu shlosha shlosha, karka'os dechsivi behu arba'ah tisgei be'arba'ah* — if the subsection for *hekdesh* says *kohen* three times and therefore three suffices, then for land, where *kohen* appears four times, four should suffice. Yet everyone agrees that the redemption of dedicated land requires ten judges. The *gemara* responds that for land, one does not count only the mentions within the land subsection but accumulates all previous mentions as well, reaching a running total of ten by that point.

The *gemara* then presses: if we adopt that cumulative counting methodology, then by the time we reach the *hekdesh* subsection, the running total of *kohen* appearances is already six — *hekdesh de-ishtalmu bo shita libei shita* — and therefore *hekdesh* should require six, not three. The Rabbis' derivation of three remains unexplained. The *gemara* concludes: *kashya* — this is a difficulty, and it is left unresolved.
