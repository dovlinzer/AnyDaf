# Menachot 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Previous Review

### Omer & Shtei HaLechem


Our daf opens with a brief review of issues raised in the previous shiur. Upon reflection, it becomes clear that no authority — not even a hint — suggests that the oil for the Omer must come specifically from Eretz Yisrael. The assumption throughout is that the geographic restriction applies to the grain of the Omer, which makes contextual sense. The Rambam, like Tosafot, understands that all the discussions about the source and grade of flour and oil are not limited to the Omer and Shtei HaLechem but apply to all menachot generally. This framing is important to keep in mind as we move through the sugyot ahead.

### Bikkurim Sourcing


There is, however, a significant Sifrei regarding bikkurim that merits mention. The Sifrei explicitly states that bikkurim must come from Eretz Yisrael proper and not from Ever HaYarden. Two distinct drashot underlie this ruling. The first derives from the phrase *eretz zavat chalav u'devash*, with the Sifrei arguing that Ever HaYarden does not meet this description. The second drasha is even more striking: it is based on the phrase *ha'adama asher natata li* — the land that You gave me — and the Sifrei reads into this *velo shenatalta me'atzmechem*, meaning land that you did not simply take for yourselves. This is an interesting theological point embedded in the halakhic derivation. Notably, there is no parallel discussion regarding the Omer or Shtei HaLechem on this question, though the Acharonim debate it extensively. No earlier authority addresses it in that context.

## Oil Sourcing

### Anpaktan Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין לֹא מִבֵּית הַזְּבָלִים, וְלֹא מִבֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִים, וְלֹא מִן מַה שֶּׁנִּזְרַע בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר. אֵין מְבִיאִין אַנְפַּקְטָן, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר. אֵין מְבִיאִין מִן הַגַּרְגְּרִין שֶׁנִּשְׁרוּ בְּמַיִם, וְלֹא מִן הַכְּבָשִׁים, וְלֹא מִן הַשְּׁלוּקִים, וְאִם הֵבִיא – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not bring** a meal offering containing oil from olives taken **from a fertilized** olive **grove, nor** from olives taken **from an irrigated** olive **grove, nor** from olives taken **from** an olive grove where grain **was sown between** the trees. **But if one did bring** a meal offering containing oil from such groves, it is **valid. One may not bring** a meal offering containing oil from **unripe olives [*anpiktan*], but if one did bring** it, it is **valid. One may not bring** a meal offering containing oil **from olives that were soaked in water, nor from pickled** olives, **nor from boiled** olives, **and** even if **one did bring** it, it is **not valid.**

The Gemara opens the main discussion of our daf at the very last line of 85b, establishing that just as grain from a manured field or a field requiring heavy fertilization is invalid for offerings, so too is oil derived from olives grown under those conditions. The Mishnah then states that one may not bring *anpaktan* — or *anpaknon* in some readings — which, as will become clear, refers to oil pressed from unripe olives. However, the Mishnah continues, *v'im hevi kasher* — if one did bring it, it is valid after the fact.

Here a textual difficulty arises immediately. Rashi's girsa of the Mishnah reads *v'im hevi pasul* — that even b'di'avad it is invalid — rather than kasher. Meanwhile, a Braita explicitly states: do not bring anpaktan, and if you did bring it, it is pasul, adding the reason *mipnei shehu seraf* — because it is not really oil at all but rather a kind of sap or raw juice. According to Rashi's reading, both the Mishnah and the Braita agree that it is invalid b'di'avad, and the question between them is whether it constitutes invalid oil or no oil at all. According to the girsa of Tosafot and most other Rishonim, the Mishnah says it is kasher b'di'avad while the Braita says it is pasul, and the question is simply whether this substance qualifies as oil for the purposes of menachot.

### Rabbi Chiya vs Rabbi Shi…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הֵבִיא – פָּסוּל (וְהָתַנְיָא: וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר) מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא שְׂרָף. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא, הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי, דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא זָרֵיק לֵיהּ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי מְטַבֵּל [בֵּיהּ] (לֵיהּ), וְסִימָנָיךְ: עֲשִׁירִים מְקַמְּצִין.
>
> **Translation:** **but if one did bring** a meal offering made of such oil, **it is not valid. But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one did bring it, it is valid, **because it is** regarded merely as **sap** and not as oil? This contradicts this mishna, which assumes that it is regarded as oil. **Rav Yosef said:** It is **not difficult** to resolve this contradiction. **This** *baraita* expresses the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya,** and **that** mishna expresses the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As Rabbi Ḥiyya would toss** such oil **away,** as he did not consider it to be oil, **and Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **would dip** his food into it, as he considered it to be oil. The Gemara concludes: **And your mnemonic** by which to remember their respective opinions is: The **wealthy are parsimonious,** i.e., Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, was wealthy, and he did not toss the oil away.

Rav Yosef resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya* — it is not difficult. The Mishnah reflects the view of Rabbi Chiya, and the Braita reflects the view of Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi. Both were contemporaries in the household of Rebbi: Rabbi Chiya was Rebbi's nephew, and Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi was Rebbi's son. Their disagreement concerns whether unripe olive oil is treated as something functional — edible oil of a lower grade — or essentially discarded as unfit substance.

The Gemara provides a mnemonic: *ashirin mekamtzin* — the wealthy are stingy. Rabbi Shimon, son of the Nasi Rebbi, who came from a wealthy household, would not waste the oil pressed from unripe olives and would actually use it — *matpil bah*, he would dip his food into it. Rabbi Chiya, by contrast, would throw it away — *zarik lah* — regarding it as not genuinely usable. The point of the mnemonic is that the wealthy, paradoxically, do not like to waste even lower-quality materials. The disagreement thus determines whether such oil constitutes a legitimate, if inferior, oil that would be kasher b'di'avad for menachot, or whether it is so fundamentally not-oil that it fails even b'di'avad.

### Shemen Hamor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר״ – מַאי ״שֶׁמֶן הַמּוֹר״? רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר: סְטָכְתָּא. רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: שֶׁמֶן זַיִת שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁלִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara digresses to discuss oil of myrrh: In describing the treatments provided to the women in preparation for their meeting with King Ahasuerus, the verse states: "For so were the days of their anointing accomplished: **Six months with oil of myrrh,** and six months with sweet odors and with other ointments of the women" (Esther 2:12). The Gemara asks: **What is "oil of myrrh"? Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya says:** It is the aromatic oil called ***setakhta*. Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says:** It is **an oil** derived **from olives that have not** yet **reached one-third** of their growth; the acidic oil is effective as a depilatory.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַנְפִּקִנוֹן – שֶׁמֶן זַיִת שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא שְׁלִישׁ, לָמָּה סָכִין אוֹתוֹ? שֶׁמַּשִּׁיר אֶת הַשֵּׂיעָר וּמְעַדֵּן אֶת הַבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This** explanation of Rav Yirmeya bar Abba **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The term ***anpikanon*** is referring to **olive oil** produced from olives **that have not** yet **reached one-third** of their growth. And **why do** women **spread it** on their bodies? They do so **because it removes the hair and pampers the skin.**

Since the discussion concerns oil from unripe olives, the Gemara takes an aggadic detour in connection with the verse in Esther describing the six months of purification using *shemen hamor*. The Gemara asks: *mai shemen hamor* — what exactly is this myrrh oil? Rav Huna bar Chiya identifies it with *satkesta*. Rav Yosef suggests it might be cinnamon. Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba offers the identification that connects this passage to our sugya: *shemen zayit shelo hevi shlish* — oil from olives that have not reached one-third of their ripening. In other words, shemen hamor is understood by Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba to be none other than what our Mishnah calls anpaktan.

A Braita confirms this identification. Rabbi Yehuda states explicitly: *anpaknon* — oil from olives that have not yet reached shlish — *lama sakhin oso?* Why would one anoint oneself with it? Because *meshir et hase'ar* — it acts as a depilatory, causing body hair to fall away — *um'aden et habasar* — and it beautifies and moisturizes the skin. This explains why it was used in the purification regimen for women. It also illuminates why one might not be able to use it for a mincha: it is not primarily a food substance but rather a cosmetic agent, and its status as genuine oil for ritual consumption is accordingly uncertain.

### Bad Oils & Ba'al Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין מִן הַגַּרְגְּרִין שֶׁנִּשְׁרוּ בְּמַיִם. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שֶׁמֶן זַיִת כָּבוּשׁ, שָׁלוּק, שָׁרוּי, וְשֶׁל שְׁמָרִים, וְשֶׁל רֵיחַ רָע – לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One may not bring** meal offerings containing oil **from olives that were soaked in water,** nor from pickled olives, nor from boiled olives, and even if one did bring it, it is not valid. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Olive oil** produced from **pickled** olives, **boiled** olives, or olives **soaked** in water, **and** oil made **from** olive **sediment, and** oil **with a foul odor may not be brought** as part of a meal offering, **and** even **if one did bring** it, it is **not valid.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבָּה: הִקְדִּישׁוֹ, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּלְקֶה עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם בַּעַל מוּם? כֵּיוָן דְּפָסוּל – כְּבַעַל מוּם דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין בַּעַל מוּם אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asks:** If one **consecrated** one of these unfit oils for use in meal offerings, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he should be flogged for** consecrating **it due to** the prohibition against consecrating **a flawed** item as an offering? Does one say that **since it** is **unfit,** it is **comparable to a blemished** animal? **Or perhaps** the prohibition against consecrating **a flawed** item **applies only to an animal.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The end of the Mishnah lists additional prohibited sources, and the Gemara cites a Braita expanding on them: *shemen zayit kavush, shaluk, sharui, veshel shemarim, veshel reach ra* — olive oil derived from pickled olives, boiled olives, olives soaked in water, oil that came from the lees of olives, or oil with a bad smell. Regarding all of these, *lo yavi — v'im hevi pasul* — one should not bring them, and if one did, the offering is invalid. The Gemara notes the remarkable absence of any explicit pesukim here; this is entirely a determination of Chazal that these substances either do not constitute oil at all or certainly do not constitute the kind of fit oil required for the mikdash. The Gemara does indicate that by the Menorah there is at least a pasuk — *shemen zayit zach* — that provides some textual criterion, but no such pasuk exists for menachot.

Rava then poses a question that parallels exactly what he asked earlier regarding wormy wood and wormy wheat: *hikdisho mahu sheyilkeh alav mishum *ba'al mum*?* If someone consecrated such oil — oil from soaked, boiled, or pickled olives — do we consider it formally analogous to a *ba'al mum*, such that one would transgress by consecrating something with a mum? The question is: *keivan depasul k'ba'al mum dami* — since it is invalid, should we treat it literally as a *ba'al mum* to the point of incurring a transgression for consecrating it? Or does *ein *ba'al mum* ela b'behema* — the concept of *ba'al mum* applies only to animals, and while this oil is invalid and we may draw analogies from *ba'al mum*, we would not impose the actual transgression? The Gemara leaves this *teiku* — unresolved. Tosafot notes, interestingly, that this exact question with the exact same language is asked in three places — by flour, by wood, and now by oil. It is possible that Rava asked it only once and the Gemara applies it to all three contexts.

## Harvests & Pressings

### First Harvest Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה זֵיתִים הֵן, וּבָהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה שְׁלֹשָׁה שְׁמָנִים. הַזַּיִת הָרִאשׁוֹן מְגַרְגְּרוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַזַּיִת, וְכוֹתֵשׁ וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַסַּל, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סְבִיבוֹת הַסַּל – זֶה רִאשׁוֹן. חָזַר וְטוֹעֵן בְּקוֹרָה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּאֲבָנִים – זֶה שֵׁנִי. חָזַר וְטָחַן, וְטָעַן – זֶה שְׁלִישִׁי. הָרִאשׁוֹן – לַמְּנוֹרָה, וְהַשְּׁאָר – לִמְנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There are three** harvests of **olives** each year, **and in each** of **them, three** different grades of **oils** are produced. How is **the first olive** harvest processed? **One picks** the ripe olives **at the top of the olive** tree, as those are the first to ripen, **and crushes** them in a mortar **and places** them **inside** the bottom of a wicker **basket** that has many small holes in it. The oil will then drip from the olives through those holes into a vessel placed underneath the basket. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One positions the olives on the walls, **surrounding the basket.** This produces more refined oil, as the dregs stick to the walls of the basket. **This is the first** grade of oil produced from the first harvest. After the oil ceases to seep from the crushed olives, **one then presses down with** a wooden **beam** upon them, causing additional oil to flow from the basket into the vessel. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The excessive pressure produced by pressing down with a beam would cause some of the flesh of the olives to get mixed in with the oil, compromising its quality. Rather, one applies pressure **by** placing **stones** upon the olives. **This is the second** grade of oil. **One then ground** the olives with a millstone **and pressed down** with a beam on those olives to extract any remaining oil; **this is the third** grade of oil. **The first** grade is fit **for** kindling **the Candelabrum,** which requires: "Refined olive oil" (Leviticus 24:2), **and the rest** are fit **for** use in **meal offerings.**

The Mishnah now teaches: *shlosha zeisim hem* — there are three olive harvests — *uvahen shlosha shlosha shemanim* — and within each harvest, three grades of oil. The first harvest involves picking the individual ripe olives — *gargara* referring to the individual olive — from the top of the tree, where they receive the most sunlight and ripen first. After collecting these olives, one performs *kotesh* — grinds them — and places them *letoch hasal*, into a wicker basket. The basket functions as a sieve: the actual olive paste remains above while the oil flows through. This yields the first extraction. Then *chazar veta'an bekora* — one places the heavy beam upon it — pressing out additional oil. This is the second extraction. One then grinds again and presses again under the beam, yielding the third extraction.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Method


Rabbi Yehuda offers a series of refinements throughout, consistently oriented toward maximizing the purity and clarity of the oil. Rather than grinding with a *reichayim* — a millstone — Rabbi Yehuda says one should use a *machteshet*, a mortar and pestle, which pounds the olives without grinding them as intensively and therefore keeps the oil cleaner. Instead of pressing with the beam, Rabbi Yehuda says *ba'avanim* — one should use stones. Rashi explains that stones, depending on their size relative to the beam, would apply pressure more slowly, requiring more patience but ultimately pressing out pure oil without the same degree of forcing impurities through. And rather than placing the ground olive paste at the base of the basket, Rabbi Yehuda says *svivot hasal* — one should place it at the inner upper lip of the basket, so that the olive paste adheres to the top and the oil runs cleanly down and is then filtered through the sieve, rather than having the paste potentially pass through the sieve from the bottom.

One might ask whether these debates reflect genuine uncertainty about actual practice or whether they concern what should have been done in the *beit haMikdash* specifically. Since the Tannaim lived in Eretz Yisrael and were still producing olive oil themselves, all of these phenomena were in use. Some practitioners used the millstone; others used the mortar and pestle. Some placed the paste at the base of the basket; others at the lip. The Tannaim are debating which practice should be considered standard and paradigmatic. Rabbi Yehuda's position is consistently that one should take whatever steps produce the clearest, most refined oil for the mikdash.

The Gemara then raises an internal tension in our Mishnah. The Mishnah says *kotesh* — it uses mortar-and-pestle grinding — which the Braita identifies as the position of Rabbi Yehuda. Yet the Mishnah then says the olive paste goes into the *sal*, the basket, at the base — which the Braita identifies as the position of the Chachamim. Our Mishnah thus appears to be a hybrid, agreeing with Rabbi Yehuda on one point and with the Chachamim on another. The Gemara confirms this: *ha tanna savar lah k'vateih b'chada u'palig aleih b'chada* — the tanna of our Mishnah follows Rabbi Yehuda in one respect and disagrees in another.

### Later Harvests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשֵּׁנִי – מְגַרְגְּרוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַגָּג, וְכוֹתֵשׁ, וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַסַּל, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סְבִיבוֹת הַסַּל – זֶה רִאשׁוֹן. טוֹעֵן בְּקוֹרָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּאֲבָנִים – זֶה שֵׁנִי. חָזַר וְטָחַן, וְטָעַן – זֶה שְׁלִישִׁי. הָרִאשׁוֹן – לַמְּנוֹרָה, וְהַשְּׁאָר – לִמְנָחוֹת. שְׁלִישִׁי: עוֹטְנוֹ בְּבֵית הַבַּד, עַד שֶׁיִּלְקֶה, וּמַעֲלֵהוּ וּמְנַגְּבוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַגָּג, כּוֹתֵשׁ וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַסַּל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סְבִיבוֹת הַסַּל – זֶה רִאשׁוֹן. טוֹעֵן בְּקוֹרָה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּאֲבָנִים – זֶה שֵׁנִי. חָזַר וְטָחַן וְטָעַן – זֶה שְׁלִישִׁי. הָרִאשׁוֹן – לַמְּנוֹרָה, וְהַשְּׁאָר – לִמְנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** How is **the second** olive harvest processed? **One picks** the crop of olives that is accessible while one is standing **on the rooftop.** This was the second lot of olives to ripen. **And one crushes** it in a mortar **and places** those olives **into** a wicker **basket,** allowing the oil to drip through the holes into a vessel underneath. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One positions the olives on the walls, **surrounding the basket. This** is the **first** grade of oil from the second harvest. **One** then **presses down** upon those olives **with** a wooden **beam,** thereby producing more oil. **And Rabbi Yehuda says:** One applies pressure only **by** placing **stones** upon them. **This** is the **second** grade of oil. **One then ground** the olives with a millstone **and pressed down** upon them with a beam, thereby extracting any remaining oil; **this** is the **third** grade of oil. **The first** grade is fit **for** kindling **the Candelabrum, and the rest** are fit **for** use in **meal offerings.** How is the **third** olive harvest processed? This harvest consists of all the olives that still remain on trees. **One packs it into a vat [*oteno*] in** the building that houses **the olive press [*beit habbad*]** where it remains **until it softens, and** then **one raises** it **up** to the roof **and dries it on the rooftop** to remove the foul-smelling liquid secreted from the olives while in the vat. Then, **one crushes** the olives in a mortar **and places** them **into** a wicker **basket,** allowing the oil to drip through the holes into a vessel underneath. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** One positions them on the walls, **surrounding the basket. This** is **the first** grade of oil. **One** then **presses down** on those olives **with** a wooden **beam,** thereby producing more oil. **And Rabbi Yehuda says:** One applies pressure only **by** placing **stones** upon them. **This** is the **second** grade of oil. **One would then grind** the olives with a millstone **and press down** upon them with a beam, thereby extracting any remaining oil; **this** is the **third** grade of oil. **The first** grade is fit **for** kindling **the Candelabrum, and the rest** are fit **for** use in **meal offerings.**

For the second harvest, one collects the individual olives not from the very top of the tree but from a mid-level — described as the rooftop level, since one would climb to the rooftop or ladder to reach that height — and then proceeds through the same three-stage process of initial extraction, beam pressing, re-grinding, and pressing again. *harishon limnora* — the first extraction may be used for the Menorah — *vehashar limnachot* — the remainder may be used for menachot.

The third harvest presents a different scenario. These are olives that never fully ripened on the tree due to lack of sunlight, so one brings them to the *beit habad* — the olive pressing house — and waits *ad sheyilkeh* — until they soften, until they become ripe after picking. One then brings them up to the rooftop and spreads them out — *ke'min tamara*, Rashi says, in a pyramid shape — *ad sheyazuvu meimav*, until the water content flows off. This is significant: freshly pressed olives have a high water content, and the water must be separated from the oil. After the water drains off, one brings the olives to the pressing house, grinds them, places them in the basket, and proceeds through the same three-stage extraction process. Surprisingly, the ruling is the same: *harishon limnora* — even the first pressing of the third harvest is fit for the Menorah — *vehashar limenachot* — the rest for menachot.

The principle that emerges is that for the Menorah, it does not matter which harvest the oil comes from; what matters is that it is the first pressing, which produces clear, pure oil regardless of the harvest's overall quality. For menachot, any pressing from any harvest is acceptable as long as it constitutes genuine oil.

### Oil Grading System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁבָּרִאשׁוֹן – אֵין לְמַעְלָה הֵימֶנּוּ. הַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁבָּרִאשׁוֹן וְהָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁבַּשֵּׁנִי – שָׁוִין. הַשְּׁלִישִׁי שֶׁבָּרִאשׁוֹן וְהַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁבַּשֵּׁנִי וְהָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁבַּשְּׁלִישִׁי – שָׁוִין. הַשְּׁלִישִׁי שֶׁבַּשֵּׁנִי וְהַשֵּׁנִי שֶׁבַּשְּׁלִישִׁי – שָׁוִין. הַשְּׁלִישִׁי שֶׁבַּשְּׁלִישִׁי – אֵין לְמַטָּה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Having enumerated the nine grades of oils in the previous mishna, this mishna proceeds to rank them by their quality: As for **the first** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the first** harvest, **there is none superior to it. The second** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the first** harvest **and the first** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the second** harvest are of **equal** quality; there is no reason to choose one over the other. **The third** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the first** harvest **and the second** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the second** harvest **and the first** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the third** harvest are of **equal** quality. **The third** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the second** harvest **and the second** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the third** harvest are of **equal** quality. As for **the third** grade of oil **that is** produced **from the third** harvest, **there is none inferior to it.**

The next Mishnah introduces a grading system for oil quality. The scheme is elegant: add the harvest number to the pressing number, and the lower the sum, the higher the grade. *haRishon shebarishon* — the first pressing of the first harvest (1+1=2) — *ein lema'ala himenu* — there is nothing superior to it. *haSheni shebarishon* and *harishon shebasheini* — the second pressing of the first harvest and the first pressing of the second harvest (both summing to 3) — *shavin*, are of equal grade. *haShlishi shebarishon*, *hasheni shebasheini*, and *harishon shebashlishi* (all summing to 4) are equal grades. And so on up through *hashlishi shebashlishi* (3+3=6) — *ein lemata himenu* — the lowest grade of all.

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty: when the Mishnah says these pairs are *shavin* — equal — is that true? Surely the first pressing of the third harvest differs from the third pressing of the first harvest, because the first pressing of any harvest is fit for the Menorah, while the third pressing is not! Rav Pappa bar Yitzchak resolves this: *mai shavin — shavin limenachot* — the Mishnah means they are equal specifically for the purpose of menachot. When ranking oil for menachot, where the criterion is taste and quality, these pairs are indeed equivalent in grade. But when the question is Menorah eligibility, the first pressing of any harvest is in a separate category precisely because what matters for the Menorah is not taste but clarity and clean burning.

## Menorah vs Menachot Oil

### Kal VaChomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת הָיוּ בַּדִּין שֶׁיִּטְעֲנוּ שֶׁמֶן זַיִת זָךְ, מָה מְנוֹרָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ לַאֲכִילָה טְעוּנָה שֶׁמֶן זַיִת זָךְ, מְנָחוֹת שֶׁהֵן לַאֲכִילָה אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּטְעֲנוּ שֶׁמֶן זַיִת זָךְ! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״זָךְ כָּתִית לְמָאוֹר״, וְאֵין זָךְ כָּתִית לִמְנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Also,** with regard to **all the meal offerings, it was logical that they should require** the highest quality of **refined olive oil,** just like the Candelabrum. Because **if** the **Candelabrum, whose** oil **is not to be consumed** on the altar, **requires refined olive oil,** then **meal offerings, which** are **to be consumed** on the altar, **is it not logical that they should require refined olive oil?** To dispel this notion, **the verse states: "Refined pounded** olive oil **for illumination"** (Leviticus 24:2), which indicates that the high-quality, refined, pounded oil is required for the Candelabrum, **but there is no** need for **refined pounded** olive oil **for meal offerings.**

The Mishnah reflects explicitly and in a notably self-aware way on the derivation of the purity standard. It opens: *af kol hamenachos hayu vadin* — in principle, all menachot should be required to use pure olive oil. The *kal vachomer* would run as follows: *mah menorah she'einah le'achila te'una *shemen zayit* zach* — the Menorah, which is not for eating, requires pure beaten olive oil — *menachos shehen le'achila, eino din sheyit'anu *shemen zayit zach*?* — menachot, which are eaten by the Kohanim, should certainly require pure beaten oil all the more so. This is a striking formulation in the Mishnah itself, offering not merely a ruling but a reflection on why the *kal vachomer* might have applied.

### Zach Katit LaMe'or

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שָׁוִין? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רִאשׁוֹן לַמְּנוֹרָה וְהַשְּׁאָר לִמְנָחוֹת! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי שָׁוִין – שָׁוִין לִמְנָחוֹת. אַף [כׇּל] הַמְּנָחוֹת הָיוּ בַּדִּין [וְכוּ׳]. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״זָךְ״ – אֵין ״זָךְ״ אֶלָּא נָקִי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״כָּתִית״ – אֵין ״כָּתִית״ אֶלָּא כָּתוּשׁ. יָכוֹל יְהֵא זָךְ כָּתִית פָּסוּל לִמְנָחוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְעִשָּׂרוֹן סֹלֶת בָּלוּל בְּשֶׁמֶן כָּתִית״, אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לַמָּאוֹר״? אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי הַחִיסָּכוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: How can the mishna state that the second grade of the first harvest and the first grade of the second harvest are of **equal** quality? **But didn't you say** that the **first** grade of each harvest is fit **for** kindling **the Candelabrum and the rest** are fit only **for** use in **meal offerings?** It would appear then that the first grade in any harvest is actually superior to the second grade of other harvests. To resolve this, **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: What** does the mishna mean when it states that **they are** of **equal** quality? It means that **they are equal with regard to meal offerings,** and there is no reason to choose one over the other. § The mishna teaches: **Also** with regard to **all the meal offerings, it was logical** that they should require refined olive oil. To dispel this notion, the verse states: "Refined pounded olive oil for illumination" (Leviticus 24:2). **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse cited in the mishna: The word **"refined"** means **nothing other than clean** oil, which flows by itself from the olives without applying any pressure. **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the word **"pounded"** means **nothing other than** olives **crushed** with a mortar, but not with a millstone. One **might** have thought that **refined, pounded** oil **is unfit for meal offerings,** since the verse specifies that this oil is to be used for illumination. To dispel this notion, **the verse states** with regard to the meal offering brought with the daily offering: **"And a tenth of fine flour, thoroughly mixed with** a quarter of a *hin* of **pounded oil"** (Exodus 29:40). This indicates that pounded oil is fit to be used in meal offerings. **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states** that the refined pounded oil is **"for illumination"? Rather,** the Torah requires the use of refined pounded oil only for the Candelabrum, **due to the sparing [*haḥisakhon*]** of money, as the highest-quality oil is very expensive.

Yet the Mishnah immediately states: *talmud lomar zach katit lame'or* — the Torah says pure, beaten oil *for the light* — *ve'ein zach katit limenachos* — and not for menachot. The verse limits the requirement to the Menorah. A Braita elaborates on the meaning of the terms. *ein zach ela naki* — zach means free of impurities, clean. Rabbi Yehuda says *ein katit ela katush* — katit means specifically pounded with a mortar and pestle rather than ground with a millstone. Together, *zach katit* describes oil that is both uncontaminated and produced through the more careful pounding method.

The Braita then entertains the possibility that this high-grade oil — *zach katit* — might actually be forbidden for menachot, since the verse assigns it specifically to the Menorah. This possibility is resolved by a *gezera shava*: the Torah uses the word *katit* both by the Menorah (*shemen zayit zach katit lame'or*) and by a mincha (*belula beshemen katit revit hahin*). The shared term teaches that the same quality of oil used for the Menorah is also permissible for menachot. The direction of the inference thus runs the other way from the initial concern: we use the gezera shava not to exclude the oil from menachot but to confirm it may be used for both.

### Chisachon Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״חִיסָּכוֹן״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason for being **sparing? Rabbi Elazar says:** The intention is that **the Torah spared the money of the Jewish people** and did not require that the highest-quality oil be used for the meal offerings.

If the same oil is permissible for both, *im kein ma talmud lomar lame'or?* — why does the Torah specify that it is *for the light*? The answer: *mipnei hachisachon* — because of fiscal burden. Rabbi Elazar explains: *haTorah chasa al mamonam shel Yisrael* — the Torah is solicitous of the financial resources of Israel. This principle appeared earlier at 76b and is applied here in a specific way. The first pressing is the most expensive oil to produce. For the Menorah, which was funded from communal resources and required a relatively fixed, limited quantity of oil, the Torah mandates the highest standard. But for menachot, which were brought far more frequently and primarily by private individuals — and notably, it was the poor who most commonly brought menachot — the Torah allows the full range of acceptable oils rather than demanding the most expensive first-pressing oil exclusively.

## Menorah Theology

### Purpose of Menorah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צַו אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיִקְחוּ אֵלֶיךָ שֶׁמֶן זַיִת זָךְ״, אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: ״אֵלֶיךָ״ – וְלֹא לִי, לֹא לְאוֹרָה אֲנִי צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the Candelabrum and other aspects of the Temple. The verse states: **"Command the children of Israel, and they shall take for yourself refined pounded olive oil** for illumination, to kindle the lamps continually" (Leviticus 24:2). **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** God tells the Jewish people that the oil should be taken **"for yourself,"** to indicate that it is for their benefit **and not for My** benefit, as **I do not need its light.**

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani focuses on the word *eilecha* — for you — and reads it as addressed to Israel: the Menorah is for your sake, not for Mine. *veliora ani tzarich?* — does God need the light? Of course not. Tosafot notes that this should not be read as God speaking about His own needs in the abstract but, in light of another Gemara, as referring even to the Kohen who entered: the Kohen who worked in the *beit haMikdash* also did not need the Menorah's light in any ordinary functional sense. The point of the Menorah is symbolic — it represents the presence of God — not to illuminate a room for practical purposes.

### Arrangement Symbolism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֻׁלְחָן בַּצָּפוֹן, וּמְנוֹרָה בַּדָּרוֹם – אָמַר רַבִּי זְרִיקָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא לַאֲכִילָה אֲנִי צָרִיךְ, וְלֹא לְאוֹרָה אֲנִי צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to the **Table** of the shewbread, located **in the north** of the Sanctuary, **and** the **Candelabrum,** located **in the south** of the Sanctuary, **Rabbi Zerika says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** God said to the Jewish people: **I do not require** the Table **for eating, nor do I require** the Candelabrum **for its illumination.** In evidence of this, the Candelabrum was not positioned close to the Table, as is done by one who sets a table with food in order to eat there.

Rav Zutra in the name of Rabbi Elazar expands the theme: *lo le'achila ani tzarich ve'lo le'ora ani tzarich* — I do not need food and I do not need light. The Shulchan was in the north and the Menorah in the south — placed on opposite sides of the sanctuary. Rashi and Tosafot observe that in a normal domestic setting, one would place the lamp near the table. The fact that they are on opposite sides signals that neither is for God's benefit. God provides food and light to Israel; Israel is not providing these things to God. The table and the lamp are there for Israel, as symbols of divine provision, not divine need.

### Windows & Divine Light

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיַּעַשׂ לַבָּיִת חַלּוֹנֵי שְׁקֻפִים אֲטֻמִים״ – תָּנָא: שְׁקוּפִין מִבִּפְנִים, וַאֲטוּמִים מִבַּחוּץ, לֹא לְאוֹרָה אֲנִי צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard the Temple built by King Solomon, the verse states: **"And he made for the House, windows narrow and broad"** (I Kings 6:4). The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: Typically, windows are constructed to widen toward the inside in order that the light from the outside would be dispersed throughout the room. For the Temple, God said: Make the windows **narrow within and broad without,** as **I do not require its illumination.** On the contrary, the light of the Temple is to be radiated outward.

The verse in Kings describing the windows of Shlomo's *beit haMikdash* states they were *shakufim atumim* — transparent and sealed, seemingly contradictory terms. A Tanna resolves: *shakufim mibifnim ve'atumim mibachutz* — wide on the inside, narrowed to a point on the outside. Tosafot reads this as the windows being the exact reverse of ordinary windows. Normal windows are wider on the outside to channel sunlight in, narrowing as they approach the interior. The windows of the *beit haMikdash* were the opposite: narrow on the outside, widening on the inside. The purpose was not to bring external light into the sanctuary but to channel the inner light of the *beit haMikdash* — the light of the Shechinah — outward to the world. This is entirely consistent with *lo le'ora ani tzarich*: God does not receive light from outside, God sends light outward.

### Ner Ma'aravi Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִחוּץ לְפָרֹכֶת הָעֵדוּת בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – עֵדוּת הוּא לְכׇל בָּאֵי עוֹלָם שֶׁהַשְּׁכִינָה שׁוֹרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: לְאוֹרָה אֲנִי צָרִיךְ? וַהֲלֹא כׇּל אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה שֶׁהָלְכוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמִדְבָּר לֹא הָלְכוּ אֶלָּא לְאוֹרוֹ! אֶלָּא עֵדוּת הוּא לְכׇל בָּאֵי עוֹלָם שֶׁהַשְּׁכִינָה שׁוֹרָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. מַאי עֵדוּתָהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: זֶה נֵר מַעֲרָבִי, שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין בָּהּ שֶׁמֶן כְּנֶגֶד חַבְרוֹתֶיהָ, וּמִמֶּנָּה הָיָה מַדְלִיק, וּבָהּ הָיָה מְסַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** God instructed Aaron to kindle the Candelabrum: **"Outside the Curtain of the testimony in the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 24:3). The dividing curtain is referred to here as: The Curtain of the testimony, to indicate that the illumination of the Candelabrum **is testimony to all of humanity that the Divine Presence rests among the Jewish people. And if you** question this and **say:** How is this testimony; perhaps the Candelabrum is lit for illumination? To this God would respond: Do **I need its light? But isn't** it so that for **all forty years that** the **Jewish people walked in the wilderness** of Sinai until they entered Eretz Yisrael, **they walked exclusively by His light,** i.e., from the pillar of fire that guided them at night. If God provides light for others, he certainly does not need it Himself. **Rather,** evidently, the illumination of the Candelabrum **is testimony to all of humanity that the Divine Presence rests among the Jewish people. What** provides **its testimony? Rava says:** The testimony **is** provided by **the westernmost lamp** of the Candelabrum, **in which they place** a quantity of **oil equivalent** to that placed in **the other** lamps, **and** nevertheless it continues to burn longer than any of the other lamps. It burns so long that every evening, **from it** the priest **would kindle** the Candelabrum, i.e., he lit that westernmost lamp first, **and** the following morning, **with it he would conclude** the preparation of the lamps for the following evening's lighting, because it remained alight longer than any of the other lamps. This perpetual miracle was testimony to God's continuous presence among His people.

The Gemara cites the verse: *michutz laparochet ha'edut be'ohel moed* — this is where the Menorah is to be lit — *edut hi lechol ba'ei olam shehashechina shora b'Yisrael* — it is a testimony to all people of the world that the Divine Presence dwells among Israel. The Menorah is not merely a lamp but a proclamation. *ve'im tomar le'ora ani tzarich* — and lest one argue that we light it for God's benefit — *vahalo kol arbaim shana shehalchu Yisrael bamidbar lo halchu ela le'oro* — for forty years in the desert, Israel walked by God's light, the pillar of fire. God is the source of light, not the recipient.

Rava identifies the specific proof of this testimony: *zeh ner ma'aravi* — it is the western light of the Menorah. One lights all the lamps of the Menorah with equal amounts of oil at nightfall, giving each enough to burn through the night. Yet the ner ma'aravi would remain burning in the morning when all the others had gone out, and — miraculously — it would continue burning through the entire following day even though it had been given only enough oil for the night. The Kohen would clean the other lamps first thing in the morning but would not extinguish and clean the ner ma'aravi until the following nightfall, because it was still burning. One then lit the other lamps from the ner ma'aravi before extinguishing it, putting in new oil and new wicks and relighting it — so that there was essentially an unbroken continuity of fire, never a moment when all lights were fully extinguished and a new fire started from scratch. This practice subsequently becomes relevant in the Gemara in Shabbat in discussions of *madlikin miner lener* — lighting one lamp from another.

There are other Gemarot that indicate this miracle operated specifically *be'itim she'hayu Yisrael osim ratzono shel Makom* — in periods when Israel was faithful, as in the time of *shimon hatzaddik*. During periods when the leadership of the *beit haMikdash* was less ideal, the ner ma'aravi would also extinguish in the morning. In order to sustain the visible continuity of the practice, oil would be added in the morning to ensure the ner ma'aravi burned until nightfall, even though it was no longer miraculous. The concept of *ner tamid* is understood in this context — and may be related as well to *eish tamid*, which is stated explicitly by the mizbeiach. This testimony — the continuous western light — was thus the ongoing visible sign that the Shechinah dwelt among Israel.

## Wine Sourcing

### Wine Regions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵאַיִן הָיוּ מְבִיאִין אֶת הַיַּיִן? קְדוֹחַיִם וַעֲטוּלַיִן – אַלְפָּא לַיַּיִן, שְׁנִיָּה לָהֶן – בֵּית רִימָּה וּבֵית לָבָן בָּהָר, וּכְפַר סִיגְנָא בַּבִּקְעָה. כׇּל אֲרָצוֹת הָיוּ כְּשֵׁרוֹת, אֶלָּא מִיכָּן הָיוּ מְבִיאִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **From where would they bring the wine** for libations? **Keduḥim and Attulin** are the **primary** sources **for wine. Secondary to them** is **Beit Rima and Beit Lavan,** located **in the mountain, and** the **village of Signa,** located **in the valley. All the regions were valid** sources for wine; **but** it was **from here,** i.e., the aforementioned locations, that **they would bring** the wine.

The Mishnah now turns to wine. It is worth noting at this point — and it becomes obvious here — that the earlier Mishnayot about flour and oil were not limited to the Omer: the discussion has become entirely general, covering all offerings, because wine was not a mincha at all, yet it is discussed in the same sequence and with the same structure.

*Me'ayin hayu mevi'in et hayayin?* — from where was the wine brought? Keduchim and Atulin are identified as *alfa leyayin* — the alpha, the finest wine regions. Second to them are Beit Rima and Beit Lavan in the mountains, and Kfar Signa in the valley. Nevertheless, *kol ha'aratzot hayu kesherot* — all lands were fit, even outside of Eretz Yisrael — but the ideal was to bring from these superior regions.

### Bad Wine Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין לֹא מִבֵּית הַזְּבָלִים, וְלֹא מִבֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִים, וְלֹא מִמַּה שֶּׁנִּזְרַע בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר. אֵין מְבִיאִין הַלִּיסְטְיוֹן, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר. אֵין מְבִיאִין יָשָׁן – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי, וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין. אֵין מְבִיאִין לֹא מָתוֹק, וְלֹא מְעוּשָּׁן, וְלֹא מְבוּשָּׁל, וְאִם הֵבִיא – פָּסוּל. וְאֵין מְבִיאִין מִן הַדָּלִיּוֹת, אֶלָּא מִן הָרַגְלִיּוֹת, וּמִן הַכְּרָמִים הָעֲבוּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not bring** libations of wine that come **from a fertilized vineyard, or from an irrigated vineyard, or from** a vineyard in **which** grain **was sown between** the vines. **But if one did bring** a libation from such wine, it is **valid. One may not bring** libations from **sweet wine made from sun-dried grapes [*hilyasteyon*], but if one did bring** a libation from such wine, it is **valid. One may not bring** wine **aged** for one year; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **but the Rabbis deem it valid. One may not bring** libations from **sweet** wine, **nor** from wine produced from **smoked** grapes, **nor** libations **from boiled** wine, **and if one did bring** a libation from such wine, it is **not valid. And one may not bring** wine produced from **grapes suspended** on stakes or trees; **rather,** one brings it **from grapes at foot** height, i.e., that rest on the ground, which are superior-quality grapes, and from **vineyards that are cultivated,** i.e., where one hoes beneath the vines twice a year.

The Mishnah then lists disqualifying conditions that mirror those for grain and oil. *ein mevi'in lo mibeit hazivalim* — one does not bring wine from vineyards grown in manured fields — *velo mibeit hashlachim* — nor from irrigated fields — *velo mima shenizra beineihem* — nor from vines between which other crops were planted, which produces inferior-quality grapes regardless of any kilayim concerns. In all these cases, *ve'im hevi kasher* — if one brought it after the fact, it is valid. One also may not bring *helistion* — grapes that became overly ripe, producing an excessively sweet wine — though if one did, it is kasher b'di'avad. Similarly, one may not bring wine that is excessively sweet, *matok*, which appears to refer to wine that is sweetened differently from helistion — perhaps artificially sweetened or sweetened after extraction.

### Old Wine Debate


The Mishnah states: *ein mevi'in yashan* — one may not bring old wine. This is a surprising ruling. One would expect that aged wine is superior, and in fact elsewhere old flour was permitted. Yet *divrei Rabbi* — this is Rebbi's position — and *chachamim machshirin* — the Sages validate old wine. The Gemara will address why Rebbi has a concern with old wine, and if the Sages consider it superior, one might expect them to go further and say *mutav sheyehei* — it is preferable. These questions will be taken up in the Gemara.

### Cooked Wine


The Mishnah then states that one may not bring *me'ushan* — smoked wine — nor *mevushal* — cooked wine — *ve'im hevi pasul* — and if one brought either of these, it is invalid even b'di'avad. This is a significantly stricter standard than the categories above. The halakhic significance of yayin mevushal extends into contemporary practice: the Rambam holds that wine used for Kiddush must be wine that could have been brought al gabei hamizbe'ach. Since our Mishnah rules yayin mevushal as pasul even b'di'avad for the altar, Sefardim accordingly do not use yayin mevushal for Kiddush.

### Vine Growing Method


*Ve'ein mevi'in min hadaliyyot* — one may not bring wine from vines that grew on trellises — *ella min haregaliyyot* — but only from vines that grow along the ground — *umin hekeramim ha'avudim* — and from worked, cultivated vineyards. The Gemara will explain what *keramim ha'avudim* means precisely. The reasoning appears to be that trellis-grown vines do not produce the same quality grapes as ground-growing vines.

### Wine Processing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ בַּחֲצָבִים גְּדוֹלִים, אֶלָּא בְּחָבִיּוֹת קְטַנּוֹת, וְאֵינוֹ מְמַלֵּא אֶת הֶחָבִית עַד פִּיהָ, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא רֵיחוֹ נוֹדֵף. אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא מִפִּיהָ – מִפְּנֵי הַקְּמָחִין, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּלֶיהָ – מִפְּנֵי הַשְּׁמָרִים, אֶלָּא מֵבִיא מִשְּׁלִישָׁהּ מֵאֶמְצָעָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** when producing wine for libations, **one should not collect** the wine **into large barrels,** as it causes the wine to spoil; **rather,** it should be placed **in small casks. And one does not fill up** the cask **until its mouth;** rather, one leaves some empty space **so that its fragrance will** collect there and **diffuse** when the lid is opened. **One should not bring** libations **from** wine that rests at the **mouth of** the cask **due to the flour-**like white scum that floats on the surface, **nor from** the wine at **bottom of** the cask **due to the sediment** that collects there. **Rather, one brings from** the wine in **its middle third.**

The Mishnah specifies that one should not gather the grapes in *chatzavim gedolim* — large vats — but rather in *chaviyot ketanot* — smaller barrels. One should not fill the barrel all the way to its lip; leaving a gap preserves the aroma, since *ke'dei shereiccho nodef* — so that the fragrance is captured within rather than escaping. When extracting the wine, one does not take it from the very top of the barrel, *mipnei hakemachim* — because of the impurities that float to the surface — nor from the very bottom, *mipnei hashemarim* — because of the lees. Rather, *mevi mi-shlisha*, *me'emtza'ah* — one draws from a point a third of the way up from the bottom, or from the middle — installing a faucet in the middle of the barrel and drawing from that point.

### Quality Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הוּא בּוֹדֵק? הַגִּזְבָּר יוֹשֵׁב, וְהַקָּנֶה בְּיָדוֹ, זָרַק הַגִּיר, הִקִּישׁ בַּקָּנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **How does** the Temple treasurer **inspect** the wine to determine that it is from the middle of the cask? The **treasurer sits** alongside the cask **and** has **the** measuring **reed in his hand.** The spigot is opened and the wine begins to flow. If he sees that the wine emerging **draws** with it **chalk**-like scum, **he** immediately **knocks with the reed** to indicate that the spigot should be closed.

The Mishnah describes how the gizbar — the Temple treasurer — would conduct quality inspection. He would sit with a stick in hand and open the faucet. First he would discard *hage'ir* — the initial cloudy discharge that flows out when a faucet is first opened, much as one lets a tap run to clear the cloudy initial flow. After discarding that, he would bang the stick — *hikish bakane* — to signal silence. The Gemara indicates there was a belief that human speech adversely affects the quality of wine as it is drawn, so the bang of the stick was not merely procedural but was a call for everyone to be quiet as the wine was extracted.

### Impurity & Ba'al Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ קְמָחִין – פָּסוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּמִימִים יִהְיוּ לָכֶם״, ״וּמִנְחָתָם״, ״תְּמִימִים יִהְיוּ לָכֶם וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** Wine **in which there is flour-**like white scum is **unfit** for libations, **as it is stated** with regard to animal offerings: **"Unblemished they shall be for you…and their meal offering** shall be fine flour mixed with **oil…unblemished they shall be for you, and their libations"** (Numbers 28:19–20, 31). This indicates that animal offerings, meal offerings, and libations must all be brought from flawless products. Therefore, the presence of flour-like white scum in wine renders it unfit.

Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Yehuda takes a stricter position: *she-yesh bo kemachim pasul* — wine that has any of these floating impurities is entirely invalid, not merely requiring that the impurities be removed. He derives this from the verse *temimim yiheyu lachem u-minchatam... temimim yiheyu lachem ve-niskeichem* — the offerings must be *temimim*, perfect. Although the term *ba'al mum* is not used explicitly, the requirement of *tamim* implies that any imperfection — including floating impurities in the wine — renders the entire offering invalid.

## Ba'al Mum Analysis

### Abaye: Choleh Case


Abaye challenges this reasoning. Even granting that inferior quality can invalidate an offering without a formal *ba'al mum*, this should cut both ways for flour and oil as well. He adduces the case of *choleh* — a sick animal — which the Mishnah rules is pasul even though it has no technical physical mum. The source is the verse *vechi tagishu pise'ach vecholeh* — and when you bring the lame and the sick, it is invalid. This shows that inferior physical condition is sufficient to render an offering pasul, independent of any formal enumerated blemish. By analogy, inferior-quality flour or oil should also be pasul by virtue of their deficiency alone, without needing to characterize them as *ba'al mum*.

### Rava: Gezeirat HaKatuv


Rava responds: the case of choleh is different. There is a *gezeirat hakatuv* — an explicit scriptural decree — that addresses the sick animal and declares it pasul. That pasuk provides the basis for invalidating the choleh despite the absence of a formal mum. But we have no comparable pasuk addressing the invalidation of inferior-quality flour or oil on their own terms. Without such a textual source, one cannot automatically transfer the logic from choleh to oil and flour.
