# Berakhot 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Statutory vs Personal

### Recap: Private Prayer

Today's daf is 28. By way of a brief recap: it is worth noting that in the middle of all the discussions about the regular *shemoneh esrei* we encountered a *mishnah* about a private *tefillah* that Rav Nechunya ben HaKana would say when he would enter the *beit midrash*. Some communities still distribute stickers to place inside their *gemaras* so as to have that *tefillah* readily available. But the placement of that *mishnah* is striking — right in the middle of a discussion of the hours and times of *davening*, and flanked by another *mishnah* concerning the eighteen *brachot*, we encounter this private, personal prayer. This juxtaposition raises for us the fundamental question of how statutory prayer and personal prayer must find a way to coexist. That *mishnah* was seemingly out of place, and that very dislocation is itself meaningful.

### Why 18 Brachot?

One is left to wonder why the *gemara* needs to find a symbolism for the number eighteen at all. Perhaps it is simply that eighteen is what we have. Yet somehow, when a number is chosen, there tends to be symbolism embedded in it. The *gemara* offers several answers: one derives the number from the phrase *havu l'Hashem benei elim*, a *perek* in *Tehillim* concerned with praising God; another from the names of God in the *shema*; and a third from the vertebrae of the spine — which, as was noted, does not appear to be anatomically accurate. What is interesting is that these various derivations seem to suggest different models of what *tefillah* is fundamentally about. Is it primarily about praising God — *havu l'Hashem kavod v'oz*? Is it more akin to the *shema*, which is an attestation of faith and a reaffirmation of our relationship with God — less about giving praise and more about underscoring our connection? Or is it about the bending and submission of supplication, which is perhaps how we most naturally understand the *shemoneh esrei*? The different symbolic answers thus point toward different underlying conceptions of what *tefillah* is and what it is meant to accomplish.

## Shimon HaPakuli

### Organization at Yavne

We now continue at the bottom of 28b, two lines from the bottom. The *gemara* states: *tanu rabbanan* — Shimon HaPakuli organized (*hisdir*) the eighteen *brachot* before Rabban Gamliel in their proper order (*al haseder*) in Yavne. This is a famous *gemara* that scholars discuss extensively in trying to understand the precise history of when the *shemoneh esrei* was composed and organized. The key word is *hisdir* — he organized, not composed. These *brachot* presumably already existed in some form. Indeed, there is evidence from the period of the *tannaim* that the texts were not yet fully fixed; there was a certain latitude in the precise wording, with a clear structure and defined concluding formula (*chatimah*) for each *bracha*, but room for variation. In some places there may even have been latitude regarding the structure itself, and perhaps certain *brachot* could be combined. What Shimon HaPakuli did, then, was to establish the canonical sequence of the eighteen *brachot* before Rabban Gamliel in Yavne.

## Birkat HaMinim

### Need for Establishment

Rabban Gamliel then addressed the Sages: is there anyone who knows how to establish the *birkat ha-Tzadukim* — the *bracha* against the Sadducees? Our text reads *Tzadukim*, but almost certainly the original reading is *minim*. *Birkat HaMinim* — the blessing against the heretics — is what this *bracha* is. As has been noted on many occasions, *minim* in the *gemara* almost always refers to the Jewish Christians, not to apostates generally. They represented the most pernicious form of internal threat, people who were of us yet who had adopted heretical beliefs, and who, as we know, eventually broke away entirely and became a separate religion. So the question is: who can compose and establish this particular *bracha*?

### Shmuel HaKatan

Shmuel HaKatan stood up and it was he who established it — who wrote it, presumably. Then the following year (*l'shanah acheret*) he forgot it, apparently in the midst of *davening shemoneh esrei* while serving as *shliach tzibur*. He looked into it, trying to remember, for two or three hours (*hishkif bah shtayim v'shalosh sha'ot*), and yet despite his inability to recall it, they did not remove him from his post.

### Removal Criteria

The *gemara* asks: why not? For Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: if a *shliach tzibur* makes an error in any of the other *brachot*, one does not remove him; but if he errs in *birkat ha-minim*, one does remove him — *chaishinan shema min hu* — we suspect that perhaps he himself is a *min*. This reflects the acute anxiety surrounding the presence of *minim* within the community: they were among us, they looked like us, but they harbored heretical positions, and their pernicious influence had to be guarded against. A *shliach tzibur* stumbling over precisely this *bracha* aroused suspicion that the reason for his difficulty was his own secret sympathy for the *minim*.

It is worth pausing to note the verb used: *ma'alin oto* — literally, they raise him up. This is because, as we learned earlier, one *davens* from a low place, and one is raised (*ma'alim*) before the *teivah* when appointed *shliach tzibur*, meaning one descends. So when one is removed, one is raised back up — *ma'alim* is thus the language of removal from that position. And critically, this removal apparently occurs even in the middle of *davening*, right then and there. Someone else is put in, and the suspected *min* is brought back up. This reflects the theme that will continue later — *hato'eh b'shma shma machzirin oso* — the concern about who is actually leading the prayer, and the vigilance exercised regarding the *shliach tzibur*'s identity and sincerity.

What this tells us more broadly is that what a person says in prayer is understood as a profound declaration of religious identity. The assumption was that someone who was a closet *min* would rather stumble over the *bracha* and reveal himself than say something he did not believe. Prayer, in this view, is not merely ritual — it is testimony.

The distinction between stumbling at the beginning of a *bracha* versus stumbling in the middle is also significant. The *gemara* cites Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: the rule of removal applies only if one did not begin the *bracha* at all (*shelo hitchil bah*), but if one began it (*hitchil bah*), one finishes it (*gomrah*). Once someone has begun saying the *bracha*, we are no longer suspicious. Of course, once the rule is known, it is possible to game it; but the underlying principle is that beginning the *bracha* demonstrates a willingness to say it, and that allays the concern.

### Righteous Reverting

Now the *gemara* turns to the case of Shmuel HaKatan himself, and asks: why were they not concerned about him? The answer given is: *shani Shmuel HaKatan d'ihu tiknah* — Shmuel HaKatan is different because he himself established it. We would hardly suspect the author of the *bracha* of being the very person it was directed against — though it is noted with some irony that this is sometimes precisely how things work out.

But the *gemara* presses further: *venechosh shema hadar bei* — perhaps he had a change of heart? Perhaps he began as a fierce opponent of the *minim* and was later drawn toward them?

Abaye responds: *gmiri d'tava lo havi bisha* — we have a received tradition that a good person does not become bad. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: but does not the *pasuk* state, *u'vshuv tzadik mitzidkaso v'asah avel* — when a righteous person turns from his righteousness and does iniquity? The *gemara* answers: that refers to someone who was originally wicked (*rasha mei'ikara*), became righteous, and then reverted. But someone who was righteous from the outset (*tzadik mei'ikara*) will not turn back. And again the *gemara* challenges: but is that really so? Does not the *mishnah* itself say, *al ta'amin b'atzmecha ad yom mosecha* — do not trust yourself until the day of your death? And it proves the point:

*sheharei Yochanan Kohen Gadol shimash b'khuna gedola shmonim shana u'levasof na'aseh Tzaduki* — Yochanan the *kohen gadol* served in the high priesthood for eighty years and at the end became a *Tzaduki*.

### Yochanan Kohen Gadol

This example is significant in a number of ways. The *Tzadukim* were likely *Tzadukim* here rather than *minim* because the *kohanim* were the primary constituency of the Sadducean movement. We know from Josephus and other sources that the *Tzadukim* were dominant among the priestly class. The *Tzadukim* rejected the *Torah she'be'al peh* — the rabbinic oral tradition — and accepted only the written Torah (*Torah she'biktav*), developing their own readings of the biblical text. They were Jewish, as indeed were the *minim* — the Jewish Christians. The concern of *chazal* regarding the *kohen gadol* following Sadducean rulings, particularly on *Yom Kippur*, is well attested in the *gemara* elsewhere.

The observation arises that while the rabbis had some ability to determine who would serve as *shliach tzibur* in the synagogue, they had no comparable control over who served as *kohen gadol*, given that the Roman authorities had their own investment in that role. Moreover, even control over the synagogue was not straightforward: the archaeological evidence suggests that the leadership of the synagogue was not rabbinic — there was an independent, largely lay leadership. Rabbis appear more often in the evidence as visitors to synagogues, or as figures who prayed in their own *beit midrash*. So the rabbis had more sway in the synagogue than in the *beit hamikdash*, but even there, not unchallenged authority.

Returning to Yochanan Kohen Gadol: Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction between his example and the principle by saying *hu Yannai hu Yochanan* — this Yochanan is the same as Yannai. That is, Yannai was originally wicked — we know all the terrible stories about Yannai killing off the sages — and Yochanan is simply another name for that same figure, who was a *rasha* from the start and therefore capable of backsliding.

Rava, however, disagrees: *Yannai lachud v'Yochanan lachud* — they were separate individuals. Yannai was wicked from the outset; Yochanan was righteous from the outset. And Yochanan, who served as *kohen gadol* for eighty years as a righteous man, nonetheless became a *rasha* at the end. This is Rava's position: even a *tzadik mei'ikara* can backslide.

The *gemara* then notes: *hani'cha l'Abaye* — according to Abaye everything is consistent; but *l'Rava kashya* — according to Rava there is a difficulty. If Rava holds that even a lifelong *tzadik* can revert to evil, why were they not suspicious of Shmuel HaKatan?

### Author Exception

Rava's answer: *shani Shmuel HaKatan d'atchil beh* — Shmuel HaKatan is different because he had begun saying the *bracha* correctly before he started stumbling. As stated by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: the rule of suspicion applies only when one has not begun the *bracha*, but once one has begun it, we allow one to finish. Since Shmuel HaKatan began saying *birkat ha-minim* properly, his subsequent difficulty is not grounds for suspicion.

It is also worth noting who Shmuel HaKatan is beyond this episode. His statement in *Pirkei Avos* — which is in fact a *pasuk* rather than his own formulation — is *binpol oyvecha al tismach*: do not rejoice when your enemy falls. Some have suggested a meaningful connection: the same Shmuel HaKatan who was chosen to compose the *bracha* against the *minim* is precisely the one who teaches that one should not take pleasure in the downfall of one's enemies. The implication is that *chazal* felt a need for someone to compose this *bracha* in a spirit that was not vindictive or triumphalist but measured and soberly vigilant.

One further note on *birkat ha-minim* itself: the question of what *hitkein* actually means is not entirely settled — does it mean the *bracha* was entirely absent beforehand, or that it existed in some form and was now formalized? There are also notably divergent manuscript traditions for the text of *birkat ha-minim*, going beyond what can be explained by censorship or self-censorship alone. There appear to be genuinely different textual traditions regarding its length and content. And there is the related question of the number nineteen: we do not actually have eighteen *brachot* in *shemoneh esrei* today, we have nineteen, and the general assumption is that *birkat ha-minim* — added at Yavne — was the nineteenth. However, this is not universally accepted. Some hold that the nineteenth *bracha* arose from the splitting of *boneh Yerushalayim* and *matzmiach keren yeshua*, which are very similar and appear as a single *bracha* in some texts. The precise question of how the final canonical form coalesced and which became the nineteenth *bracha* remains a matter of scholarly discussion.

## Prayer Numerology

### Seven of Shabbat

With that overview of the eighteen — or nineteen — *brachot* in view, we now turn to the other numbers associated with *shemoneh esrei*, on the assumption that these numbers are not arbitrary but symbolically chosen. The *gemara* on daf 29 asks: *hani sheva de-Shabata kineged mi* — the seven *brachot* of the *shabbat shemoneh esrei*, what do they correspond to?

This seems like it should have an easy answer. Yet interestingly the *gemara* does not offer the obvious one. Rav Chalafta ben Shaul says: they correspond to the seven times the word *kol* appears in the phrase *kol Hashem al ha-mayim* in the *perek* of *Tehillim* that we recite during *kabbalat shabbat*. The answer is frankly puzzling. *Kabbalat shabbat* was composed in the sixteenth century — why should we assume that particular *perek* in *Tehillim* was associated with *shabbat* at the time of the *gemara*? And why not simply say the obvious: seven *brachot* for the seventh day of the week? Furthermore, from a structural standpoint, the *shabbat amidah* is really three plus three plus one, meaning there is essentially only one distinctly *shabbat bracha*. So the symbolism is roundabout at best. One possibility is that the connection runs through *mattan Torah*, which according to one tradition occurred on *shabbat*, and that the seven *kolot* at Sinai provide the underlying link — but this *perek* in *Tehillim* is not about *mattan Torah* either, making the derivation quite indirect.

### Nine of Rosh Hashanah

The *gemara* then asks: *hani tesha de-Rosh Hashanah kineged mi* — what do the nine *brachot* of *Rosh Hashanah* correspond to? Here too one might offer a structural answer: the three opening *brachot*, the three closing *brachot*, and the three middle *brachot* of *malchuyot*, *zichronot*, and *shofarot*, which *chazal* derive from elsewhere. The difficulty is that this yields ten, not nine, because there is also *kedushat ha-yom* — *mekadesh Yisrael v'yom ha-zikaron*. In practice, this *bracha* is combined with *malchuyot* — *melech al kol ha-aretz mekadesh Yisrael v'yom ha-zikaron* — and there is in fact a debate among the *tannaim* about exactly how to compress what would logically be ten *brachot* into nine, with various proposals for where the conflation occurs. Everyone agrees the target number is nine, even though the structure is somewhat ambiguous. This is also the reason why, according to some, the *amidah* of *Rosh Hashanah* opens with *hamelech ha-kadosh* rather than *ha-kel ha-kadosh* — because for some authorities that first *bracha* itself already incorporates an element of *malchuyot*.

Rabbi Yosef answers the *gemara*'s question: the nine *brachot* correspond to the nine times that God's name is mentioned in Chana's prayer. The connection is established: *b'Rosh Hashanah nifkadu Sarah Rachel v'Chana* — Sarah, Rachel, and Chana were all remembered by God on *Rosh Hashanah*, which is why Chana's prayer features as the *haftarah*. And therefore, the nine *brachot* mirror the nine appearances of God's name in her prayer. The answer is not without a certain weakness — Chana's *tefillah* is not obviously a central thematic pillar of *Rosh Hashanah* — but the connection through the *haftarah* and through the tradition of *pekidah* on *Rosh Hashanah* gives it some purchase. When the *pasuk* says *v'Hashem pakad et Sarah*, the tradition understands this to mean she conceived on *Rosh Hashanah*.

### Twenty-Four of Taanit

The *gemara* then asks about the twenty-four *brachot* of *taanit*. For a public fast (*taanit tzibur*) declared in times of drought — not the personal fasts like *Tisha b'Av* — six additional *brachot* were appended to the standard eighteen, yielding twenty-four. Rabbi Chelbo answers: these correspond to the twenty-four expressions of *renanah* — that is, of song, prayer, and supplication — that Shlomo used when he brought the *aron* into the *kodesh kadashim*. The word *renana*, as Rashi explains, serves as an umbrella term encompassing *rina*, *tefila*, and *techina* — various registers of prayer and rejoicing — and counting all the different words Shlomo employed that fall into this category yields twenty-four.

The *gemara* immediately asks: if so, why not say twenty-four *brachot* every day? Shlomo's prayer is, after all, a general prayer about the efficacy of prayer directed toward the *beit hamikdash* — it is not specific to times of national distress.

The *gemara* answers: *eima de'amrinhu Shlomo b'yoma de'rachmei* — Shlomo said these things on a day of divine compassion, when the *aron* was being brought into the *beit hamikdash* and the heavens were open. *Anan nami b'yoma de'rachmei amrinan lehu* — we likewise say them on a day of compassion. The parallel is that a *taanit tzibur* is also a time when we are reaching for divine compassion.

Rashi, however, notes a difficulty: Shlomo was speaking at a moment when God was already actively showing compassion, whereas we say twenty-four *brachot* on a *taanit* precisely because we feel that divine compassion has been withheld — we are praying for rain that has not come. To bridge this gap, Rashi cites a *midrash* that relates how when Shlomo tried to bring the *aron* in, the gates of the *heichal* locked shut and would not open. Shlomo then had to make a special plea to God, invoking the merit of David his father, after which the gates finally opened — publicly demonstrating that even though David himself could not build the *beit hamikdash*, the gates opened in his merit. According to Rashi's reading, then, Shlomo's prayer was itself a moment of supplication and petition, not merely one of gratitude and triumph, and in that respect it parallels our own situation on a *taanit*.

## Abbreviated Prayer

### Me'ein Opinion

Having concluded the discussion of symbolic numbers, the *gemara* now turns to the question of abbreviated prayer. The *mishnah* records a dispute: Rabbi Yehoshua says one may say *me'ein shemoneh esrei* — a condensed version. Rabbi Akiva says: if one is fluent, one says the full *shemoneh esrei*; if not, one says the condensed version. The context is important — in a period without *siddurim*, and with widespread inability to read or memorize the full text, it could not be assumed that everyone knew how to recite the complete *shemoneh esrei*.

### Rav's Version

The *gemara* asks: what exactly is *me'ein shemoneh esrei*? Rav says: a short version of each individual *bracha* (*me'ein kol bracha u'bracha*). One still recites eighteen *brachot*, each with its proper opening and concluding formula (*chatimah*), but with an abbreviated body — perhaps a single phrase before the closing *bracha*. The structure of eighteen *brachot* is preserved; only the content of each is compressed.

### Havineinu Text

Shmuel, by contrast, offers a single unified text called *havineinu* — from the opening words *havineinu Hashem Elokeinu*. This formulation does not give each middle *bracha* its own separate blessing; rather, it covers all twelve (or thirteen) middle *brachot* in a single passage with roughly two words devoted to each theme. The full text runs as follows: *havineinu Hashem Elokeinu ladaat derachecha* — let us know your ways, corresponding to *chonen ha-daat*; *umol l'veinu yiratecha* — circumcise our hearts to fear you, corresponding to *hashiveinu*; *vatislach lanu* — forgive us, corresponding to the *bracha* of *selichah*; *lihyot geulim* — corresponding to *goel Yisrael*; *viharchekeinu mimachoveinu* — distance us from our suffering, corresponding to *rofeh cholei amo Yisrael*; *vidashneinu binot artzecha* — make us flourish in the dwelling of your land, corresponding to *mivarech ha-shanim*; *unfutzoiseinu mearba tikabetz* — gather our dispersed from the four corners, corresponding to *mekabetz nidchei amo Yisrael*; *vehatoim al daat-cha yishpotu* — those who stray from your will shall be judged, corresponding to *melech ohev tzedakah umishpat* (Rashi offers two readings here: either those who transgress your word shall be judged, or those who err in judgment shall judge correctly — and Rashi presents both); *veal rishaim tanif yadecha* — raise your hand against the wicked, corresponding to *shover oyvim umachnia zeidim*; *viyismechu tzadikim b'vinyan irecha ubitikun heichalecha* — the righteous shall rejoice in the building of your city and the restoration of your sanctuary; *ubitzmichat keren l'David avdecha* — and in the flourishing of the Davidic dynasty, incorporating both *boneh Yerushalayim* and *matzmiach keren yeshuah*; *uva'arichat ner l'ven Yishai meshichecha* — and in the light of Jesse's son, your anointed; *terem nikra Atah taaneh* — before we call out, you have already answered us. Then one concludes with *baruch Atah Hashem shomea tefilla* — corresponding to *shomea tefillah*. With approximately twenty words, one has covered all the middle *brachot*, and then one recites the standard final three.

### Abaye's Objection

Abaye used to curse (*layeit alah*) anyone who would recite *havineinu*. While it is technically valid — one is *yotzei* according to some opinions in the *mishnah* — Abaye's view is that it is not the proper way to *daven*. If one is serious about prayer, one does not settle for *havineinu*.

### Havineinu Timing

Against Abaye, Rav Nachman says in the name of Shmuel: *kol hashanah kulo mitpallel adam havineinu* — in fact, a person may recite *havineinu* throughout the entire year, *chutz mimotzaei Shabbat umotzaei Yom Tov* — except on *motzaei Shabbat* and *motzaei Yom Tov*. Why the exception? *Mipnei shetzarich lomar havdalah b'Chonen HaDaat* — because on those occasions one is required to insert *havdalah* into *chonen ha-daat*, and since *havineinu* does not accommodate this insertion, one must revert to the full *shemoneh esrei*.

### Insertion Problem

This naturally raises the question: why not simply include *havdalah* within the *havineinu* text? Rava bar Shmuel asks: why not say a fourth *bracha* independently — recite *chonen ha-daat* separately with *havdalah*, and then proceed with *havineinu*? The *gemara* objects: Rabbi Akiva's opinion that *havdalah* constitutes its own fourth *bracha* is not the accepted ruling — we do not add *brachot*. The established number for *shemoneh esrei* is eighteen, not nineteen; similarly, the *havineinu* version is established as seven *brachot* (three plus *havineinu* plus three), and we do not expand it to eight.

Mar Zutra then asks: why not incorporate *havdalah* directly into the *havineinu* text — simply inserting the phrase *hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol* into the opening line? The *gemara* leaves this as *kashya*, a genuine difficulty. What seems to emerge is that while the text of *havineinu* is a fixed digest, insertions into it are not permitted. Whenever an insertion is required — such as *havdalah* — and it cannot be accommodated, one must return to the standard full *shemoneh esrei*.

### Mistake vs Intention

The tension continues: Ravina bar Shmuel also asks, concerning the requirement to recite *havineinu* only through the winter half of the year when *tal u'matar* must be inserted into *birchat ha-shanim* — why not incorporate *v'sen tal u'matar livracha* directly into the phrase *ve-dashnenu binot artzecha*? The *gemara* answers: *amri ati le-itroudi* — that will cause confusion and distraction. The *gemara* then asks: why did that answer not apply equally to the proposal of inserting *havdalah* at the beginning of *havineinu*? The answer: there, since the insertion comes at the very beginning of the text, before one has gotten deep into the passage, it does not cause confusion — *lo matrid*. But here, the insertion would occur in the middle of the text, and that is liable to lead one astray — *matrid*.

Rabbah then raises another proposal: why not insert *tal u'matar* into *shomea tefillah*, the *bracha* that serves as a catch-all for personal petitions? At this point the *gemara* cites a ruling of Rav Tanchum in the name of Rav Assi: if one forgot to mention *gevurot geshamim* (*mashiv ha-ruach umorid ha-geshem*) in *techiyas ha-meitim*, one must go back and repeat; but if one forgot to say *tal u'matar* in *birchat ha-shanim*, one does not have to go back, because *mipnei she'yachol lomrah b'shomea tefillah* — one can insert it into *shomea tefillah*. And similarly, if one forgot *havdalah* in *chonen ha-daat*, one does not have to repeat, *mipnei she'yachol lomrah al ha-kos* — because one can fulfill *havdalah* on a cup of wine.

So the *gemara* asks: since *tal u'matar* can be inserted into *shomea tefillah* even when one has made a mistake, why not permit its insertion into *shomea tefillah* within the *havineinu* text proactively? The *gemara* answers: *ta'ah shani* — the case of error is different. When one has made a mistake, we allow a retroactive remedy through *shomea tefillah*; but we do not permit one to construct one's prayer from the outset in a manner that relies on *shomea tefillah* to carry an element that belongs elsewhere. Since there is no proper place within *havineinu* to say *tal u'matar* during the rainy season, one may not say *havineinu* at all during that period — and since that amounts to approximately half the year, the practical reach of *havineinu* is significantly curtailed.

One might note a parallel to later *halachic* discussions — for instance, the Sephardic practice at *musaf* for *Yom Tov* not to specify the *korbanot* of each particular holiday, a practice the *Shulchan Aruch* relates to the concern that the many variations will cause errors. The general principle of not introducing complexity that risks undermining the fixed structure of prayer runs throughout these discussions.

## Missing Elements

### Mishiv HaRuach

We now proceed to the *gemara*'s more systematic treatment of what happens when elements of *shemoneh esrei* are missing. The ruling of Rav Tanchum citing Rav Assi is restated formally: if one forgot to mention *gevurot geshamim* — the phrase *mashiv ha-ruach umorid ha-geshem* — in the second *bracha* (*techiyas ha-meitim*), one is made to go back (*machzirin oto*).

### Tal U'Matar Exception

By contrast, if one forgot to say *she'eilah* — *v'sen tal u'matar livracha* — in *birchat ha-shanim*, one is not made to go back, because one can say it in *shomea tefillah*.

### Havdala Exception

And if one forgot *havdalah* in *chonen ha-daat*, one is not made to go back, because one can say it *al ha-kos* — when making *havdalah* on a cup of wine.

A *baraita* is then cited that seems to contradict this ruling: it states that if one forgot *she'eilah* in *birchat ha-shanim*, one is made to go back — seemingly at odds with the ruling just given. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ha b'yachid ha b'tzibbur* — one ruling applies to an individual praying alone, the other to someone praying in a congregation. In a congregation (*b'tzibbur*), one is not made to go back because one can hear the *shemoneh esrei* from the *shliach tzibur* and be *yotzei* through him. This is the first place the *gemara* makes explicit that one can discharge one's *shemoneh esrei* obligation by listening to the *shliach tzibur*. This was, in fact, the original purpose for which the *shliach tzibur* was instituted — for those who did not know how to *daven* — but here we see it applied even to someone who does know how to *daven* but made a mistake.

### Private vs Communal

However, the *gemara* immediately questions this reasoning: if the basis for not requiring one to go back is that one can hear the *shemoneh esrei* from the *shliach tzibur*, then the ruling should say that — rather than saying the reason is that one can insert it into *shomea tefillah*. The *gemara* therefore retracts: *ella idi v'idi b'yachid* — both rulings actually refer to an individual praying alone, and the distinction is different. The question is simply: at what point did one remember the omission? *Ha d'adkar kodem shomea tefillah ha d'adkar basar shomea tefillah* — if one remembered before reaching *shomea tefillah*, one inserts the missing element there; if one only realized after having already said *shomea tefillah*, one can no longer insert it there and must go back to repeat the entire *shemoneh esrei*. This also explains the *havdalah* case: there too, if one finished *shemoneh esrei* and realized one forgot *havdalah* entirely, one does not repeat — not because one can insert it in *shomea tefillah*, but because one can say *havdalah al ha-kos*.

A practical *halachic* corollary: if one has already made *havdalah al ha-kos* before *davening maariv*, does one still say *havdalah* in *shemoneh esrei* when one does daven? The answer is that one does — because *havdalah* in *shemoneh esrei* is not merely a vehicle for fulfilling the obligation of *havdalah*. It reflects the *gemara*'s principle that *shemoneh esrei* should always mirror the character of the moment (*b'chol yom ten lo mei'ein birchosav*). *Havdalah* in *chonen ha-daat* on *motzaei shabbat* expresses something about the nature of this liminal time — the distinction between *kodesh* and *chol* — which ought to be reflected in one's *amidah* regardless of whether one has already discharged the formal *havdalah* obligation elsewhere.

## Yaaleh V'Yavo

### Return to Refeit

The *gemara* on daf 29b states in the name of Rav Tanchum citing Rav Assi citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: if one forgot to mention *ya'aleh v'yavo* on *Rosh Chodesh* in *retzei* (the *avodah bracha*), one goes back to *retzei*. If one remembered while already at *modim*, one still goes back to *retzei*. If one remembered at *sim shalom*, one goes back to *retzei*. And if one finished the entire *shemoneh esrei* (*v'im siyeim*), one goes back to the beginning (*chozer l'rosh*).

This is worth pausing over, because it represents a *chiddush*. We had just established that if one forgot *v'sen tal u'matar* in *birchat ha-shanim*, one can remedy this by inserting it into *shomea tefillah*. By that logic, one might have expected that missing *ya'aleh v'yavo* in *retzei* would simply require going back to the beginning of *shemoneh esrei*. Instead, the *gemara* permits one to return specifically to the *bracha* that was deficient — *retzei* — and continue from there, rather than repeating the entire *amidah* from the start. That is the *chiddush*.

### Akar Raglav

Rav Papa the son of Rav Acha bar Adda qualifies the ruling: when we said that finishing *shemoneh esrei* means going back to the beginning, that applies only once one has uprooted one's feet (*akar raglav*) — that is, taken the three steps back. But if one has not yet stepped back (*lo akar raglav*), one can return to *retzei* rather than having to repeat from the beginning.

The concept of *akar raglav* is significant. It reflects the view that until one has physically stepped back, one remains *omed lifnei hamakom* — standing in God's presence — even if the text of *shemoneh esrei* is technically complete. This principle has direct *halachic* relevance for the practice of reciting *vidui* at the end of *shemoneh esrei* on *Yom Kippur*: it should be recited while one is still in that state of *omed lifnei hamakom*, before stepping back. There is a question in the *poskim* about whether this requires literal stepping, or whether saying *yihyu l'ratzon imrei fi v'hegyon libi lefanecha Hashem tzuri v'goali* — which effectively says "I am done, please accept my prayer" — constitutes a conceptual *akiras raglav*. The personal opinion expressed here is that one should say *vidui* before *yihyu l'ratzon*, so that the *vidui* remains attached to the *shemoneh esrei* while one is still *omed lifnei hamakom*, in accordance with what is clearly the *gemara*'s intent that *vidui* be understood as integrally connected to the *amidah* without being formally inserted into its text.

There is also a structural point: saying that one has finished *shemoneh esrei* but is still *omed lifnei hamakom* suggests that in some sense the *shemoneh esrei* is not fully over. It is somewhat like *toch k'dei dibbur* — within the moment of the act — in that one has not yet stepped out of the mode, and so going back to correct remains possible. This is the remarkable practical *chiddush* of this *gemara*.

### Tachanun Exception

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak further qualifies: when we said that once one has stepped back one must go back to the beginning if one missed *ya'aleh v'yavo*, that applies only when one does not normally add *tachanunim* — personal supplications — after *tefillah*. But if one does normally say personal supplications after *tefillah* — Rashi explains this as referring to *elokai netzor leshoni meira* and similar private prayers said while still in the *amidah* position — then even after physically stepping back, one may still return to *retzei*.

There is also a second version of this ruling reported in the name of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak that operates in the more stringent direction: we say that not having stepped back means one can go back to *retzei* — but only if one regularly says *tachanunim* after prayer. It is not sufficient merely not to have moved; one must also have something remaining to say, which keeps one in the mode of *omed lifnei hamakom*. If one is not in the habit of adding private prayers, then even without stepping back, once the fixed text is done one is done, and must go back to the beginning.

The conclusion that emerges from all of this is: two things can terminate the extended state of *omed bitfillah* — physically moving out of the zone by stepping back, or having nothing left to say because one does not normally add personal prayers. If one has not moved and normally would still be saying personal prayers at this point, one remains in the mode of prayer even though the fixed text is complete, and can still go back and correct. This is both *halachically* significant and conceptually meaningful: the personal supplications, when recited regularly, become incorporated into the act of prayer in such a way as to extend what it means to be *omed bitfillah*. The private prayer does not merely follow the fixed prayer — it becomes part of it, and sustains it.

A related question arises: if there are no personal supplications after the final *bracha*, is not *yihyu l'ratzon* still always said? Yes — and this is why *yihyu l'ratzon* itself may function as a kind of *akiras raglav* for some *poskim*. Similarly, the practice of saying *oseh shalom bimromav* and *yehi ratzon she-yibaneh beit ha-mikdash* after the final *yihyu l'ratzon* raises the question of whether that too still counts as *omed bitfillah*, or whether the first *yihyu l'ratzon* has already constituted a conceptual departure. Different *poskim* differ on this, and the matter connects directly to the timing of *vidui* on *Yom Kippur*.

## Fixed vs Supplication

### Rabbi Eliezer

The *mishnah* records: *Rabbi Eliezer omer ha'oseh tfilatoh keva ein tfilatoh tachanunim* — one who makes one's prayer fixed, it is not considered supplication. Some scholars have understood this as Rabbi Eliezer rejecting the entire institution of statutory prayer (*shemoneh esrei*) — that one should simply say one's own words rather than recite a fixed text. It seems unlikely, however, that so radical a *halachic* position would be expressed in so oblique and aphoristic a form, in a context where every other voice assumes statutory prayer as the baseline. Let us see how the *gemara* understands it.

### Burden vs Opportunity

The *gemara* asks: *mai keva* — what does it mean to make one's prayer *keva*, fixed? Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi says in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: it means that one's prayer is like a burden (*masa*) — an obligation one fulfills grudgingly. The ideal is to approach prayer as an opportunity, not a chore.

Rav Soloveitchik developed this insight with particular clarity. He noted that while there is generally a debate about whether *mitzvot tzrichot kavanah* — whether one must have conscious intent before performing a *mitzvah* — the relevant *kavanah* for *shemoneh esrei* is knowing one stands before God and understanding the meaning of the words. But what of the *kavanah* of being *yotzei* one's obligation of *tefillah*, which according to the Rambam is a biblical obligation? Rav Soloveitchik argued: not only is such a *kavanah* unnecessary, but having it actually violates Rabbi Eliezer's ruling — one is making one's *tefillah keva*, approaching it as an obligation to discharge rather than an opportunity to seize. This reading is already latent in Rashi, who explains *keva* as saying to oneself: *chok kavua hu alai l'falel v'tzarich ani latzeit yedei chovasi* — it is a fixed statute upon me to pray, and I must discharge my obligation.

Of course, the practical counterargument arises: sometimes one simply does not feel like *davening*, or one feels the inadequacy of *kavanah* acutely, and the sense of obligation is precisely what carries one through. The response is not that this is wrong — it is clearly better to *daven* without full *kavanah* than not to *daven* at all — but that the ideal is different, and the sense of obligation should not be what motivates one's prayer.

### Personal Insertion

The *rabbanan* add a second dimension: *kol she-eino omer balashon tachanunim* — one who does not say it in the language of supplication. This shifts the question from one's interior disposition to the actual form of one's speech: is one reciting the words in a way that is rote and flat, or is one genuinely expressing feeling through the words?

Rabbah and Rav Yosef push the definition further: *kol she-eino yachol lechadesh bo davar* — one who cannot add something new to it. According to this view, it is not sufficient to have the right feelings or the right tone; one must actually insert a personal element into the fixed text. A person who recites *shemoneh esrei* without being able to add any novel personal words is making it *keva* rather than *tachanunim* — not thereby failing to be *yotzei*, but certainly not fulfilling the ideal.

### Rav Zeira's Concern

Rav Zeira immediately registers the practical difficulty: *ana yachilna lechidushei bamilsa* — I am capable of adding something new to my *davening* — *umistafina dilma mitaraivna* — but I am afraid that if I do, I will get confused and lose my place in the fixed text. This directly echoes the concern raised earlier in the discussion of *havineinu*: the latitude to insert personal material is inherently in tension with the stability of the fixed text, and too much freedom in inserting may undermine the ritual correctness of the whole.

### Davening with Sun

Abaye bar Avin and Rav Chanina bar Avin offer a different answer: *kol she-eino mitpallel im dimumei chama* — one who does not pray with the reddening (*dimumei*) of the sun. The suggestion here is that the way to avoid *keva* may not be a matter of thought, feeling, or text at all, but of environment and circumstance. *Davening shachrit* at the moment of sunrise and *mincha* at the time of sunset — connecting prayer to the rhythm of nature — generates a sense of *tachanunim* that no rote recitation in a vacuum can replicate. This is the *mitzva* of the *vatikin*, as cited by Rabbi Chiya bar Abin in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mitzva lehitpallel im dimumei chama*, and the *pasuk* is *yira'ucha im shamesh velifnei yareiach dor dorim* — they shall fear You with the sun, and before the moon, generation after generation.

### Sunrise Danger

Yet the *gemara* closes with a cautionary note: *leti'at Abaye bema'arava* — in *Eretz Yisrael* they would curse or mock one who tried to *daven* precisely at sunrise. Why? *Dilma mitarfi lei sha'ata* — because if one is overly focused on timing one's prayer to coincide exactly with the moment of sunrise, one risks becoming so distracted by the timing that one actually misses the proper time for *shemoneh esrei* altogether. The same concern that animated the discussion of *havineinu* — the risk that trying to do something better leads to doing something worse — applies here as well.

These final lines of the *gemara* are a remarkable distillation of the central tension of the entire daf. The *gemara* does not ask whether one should say the fixed text — that is assumed. The question is how one brings personal prayer into that fixed text: through thoughts and feeling, through actual changes to the wording, through environmental attunement. But at every turn, the danger lurks that too much latitude will cause confusion and undermine the fixed structure. How much can the balance between fixed and personal prayer be permitted to play out? There is no single answer; the *gemara* holds the tension. And it is worth noting that this question frames the entire daf — beginning with the seemingly misplaced private prayer of Rav Nechunya ben HaKana inserted into the discussion of statutory *shemoneh esrei*, and ending here, with Rabbi Eliezer's insistence that fixed prayer without personal dimension is not truly supplication at all.
