# Nedarim 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Charata vs. Pesach

### Charata and Pesach

The daf opens in the midst of a discussion about the practical process of *hataras nedarim* — releasing individuals from vows. Two foundational concepts govern this process. The first is *charata*: simple retrospective regret, the sense that "I wish I had never done this." The second is *pesach*: a retroactive disavowal grounded in an element of error, the sense that "had I been thinking clearly at the time, I never would have made this vow in the first place." This latter concept resembles the *arba nedarim* of our *Mishna*, where it is clear from the outset that the vow-taker did not fully mean what he said. The *pesach*, by contrast, involves demonstrating in hindsight that had the person been fully deliberate, he would not have intended the vow — making it a subtler but related form of the same underlying principle.

### Opening with Charata

Since *pesach* is the more substantive and concrete basis for release — it demonstrates with greater precision why the vow was never truly binding — one might assume it is always required. The question the *Gemara* is working through, then, is whether *potchin b'charata*: whether one may open the process of *hataras nedarim* with simple regret, or whether a specific *pesach* is always necessary.

## Rabbi Yannai Case

### Heaven Opens Ledger

The *Gemara* presents a series of cases in which different *Amoraim* released vows, some on the basis of *charata* and others on the basis of *pesach*. We now pick up three lines from the top of 22a. The grandson of Rabbi Yannai came before his grandfather seeking release from a vow. Rabbi Yannai opened with the following: "Had you known that when one takes a vow, the heavenly ledger is opened and your deeds are scrutinized — that the act of vowing triggers a divine audit of your entire conduct — would you have made the vow?" The grandson replied that he would not, and Rabbi Yannai thereupon released him.

### Verse Proof

Rabbi Abba identifies the scriptural basis for this principle: *ve-acharei nedarim levaker* — "after vows you will be inspected." The logic is that the vow-taker implicitly considers himself righteous enough to go beyond what the *Torah* has forbidden. The *Torah*, accordingly, responds by scrutinizing whether the person truly merits that self-assessment.

### Too Generic

This case is interesting on several levels simultaneously. At one level it concerns the formal *halakha* of how *hataras nedarim* operates. At another level it reflects the idea that the process depends on *pifo v'libo shavin* — the mouth and heart must be in agreement — so that a *chacham* can inquire into whether the vow-taker truly and fully intended the vow. And at yet another level, the case serves as an occasion to explore the religious status of vowing in general.

Yet whatever its virtues, Rabbi Yannai's opening is entirely generic. There is nothing case-specific about it: any vow-taker, in any situation, could be told that taking a vow triggers heavenly scrutiny. A proper *pesach* is meant to be concrete and particular — "had you been thinking about *this* specific consideration, you would not have made *this* vow." Rabbi Yannai's formulation, whatever its rhetorical power, does not achieve that.

## Rejecting Scare Tactics

### No Heavenly Ledger

The *Gemara* rules explicitly: even though Rabbi Yannai opened with the heavenly ledger, we do not open this way. The objection is twofold. First, as noted, it is too generic to function as a genuine *pesach*. Second, and crucially, the Ran explains that such an opening is a scare tactic: by confronting the petitioner with the specter of divine punishment, you may frighten him into saying he regrets the vow even if he does not genuinely feel that way. The danger is that you will elicit an answer that reflects fear rather than authentic reconsideration.

### Bamah Statement Rejected

The *Gemara* extends this ruling to another formulation. We also do not open with what was taught in a *braisa* in the name of Rabbi Natan: *ha-noder ke'ilu bana bamah, ve-ha-mekaymo ke'ilu makriv alav korban* — one who makes a vow is as if he built an unauthorized altar (*bamah*), and one who goes through with fulfilling it is as if he offered a sacrifice upon it.

### Bamah Philosophy

This is a striking and philosophically rich statement about the nature of vowing. The *bamah* metaphor deserves careful attention. The person who makes a vow is attempting to do more than what the *Torah* demands — to adopt additional prohibitions beyond those the *Torah* has already established. The implicit assumption is that self-denial is the paradigmatic religious act, so that doing more of it constitutes a higher level of piety. But this reasoning reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of what religious practice is.

The *bamah* comparison makes precisely this point. During a period when all sacrifice must be offered at the central *Beit HaMikdash*, building a private altar is not an act of greater piety — it is a transgression. Religion, in this view, is not a private, self-initiated enterprise but a communal, God-defined one. One does not earn credit by going off on one's own and constructing a personal religious supplement. And of course, the sin compounds itself: building the *bamah* is one violation, but actually offering upon it is a further and graver one. By analogy, making the vow is already problematic, but fulfilling it only deepens the problem — which is precisely why seeking *hataras nedarim* is so important.

It bears noting that there were historical periods when *bamot* were permissible — when there was no central *Beit HaMikdash*, private altars served a legitimate religious function. One can understand why a person might feel that the institutionalized framework of religion does not fully answer his spiritual needs, and that some personal religious initiative is called for. The *Gemara* is not denying that impulse entirely. But the specific context here — making things forbidden upon oneself beyond what the *Torah* requires — is the target of the critique.

### Ran's Analysis

The Ran addresses this directly. He writes that the reason we compare the vow-taker to one who builds a *bamah* is precisely because the vow-taker believes he is performing a *mitzvah*. His reasoning runs as follows: the *Torah* prohibits many things, and since the prohibition of things is itself a religious value, he can make himself even more religious by prohibiting yet more things upon himself. The *Gemara* — and the Ran in expounding it — declares this a fundamental error.

The Ran draws on a passage in the Yerushalmi in the perek *potchin lo*: "Is it not enough what the *Torah* has forbidden, that you must go and make more things forbidden upon yourself?" This is a trenchant formulation. The Rambam echoes this sentiment extensively in his discussions of the golden mean and the dangers of excessive self-denial: more restriction is not always a religious good, and may in fact be a religious failure.

The Ran adds a further dimension specific to the *neder* as a legal form: a *neder* draws its force from the *korban* model, since the prohibiting formula *konam* renders an object like a *korban*. If that quasi-*korban* is not desired by God — because the vow was not a legitimate religious act — then it is as though one has offered a sacrifice outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. Moreover, the Ran notes, the end of the *braisa* — "one who fulfills the vow is as if he offered a sacrifice upon the *bamah*" — can function as a *pesach* in the positive sense: to get out of the vow is to stop the process of bringing this unwanted quasi-sacrifice. Not seeking *hataras nedarim* means continuing to "offer" on an unauthorized altar.

### Abaye-Rava Debate

Despite all this, a debate arises as to whether we may at least open with the *beginning* of the statement — that making the vow was like building a *bamah* — even if we do not use the *end* of the statement about offering a sacrifice upon it. Building a *bamah* does not involve the same severity as actually bringing a sacrifice upon it; perhaps we can use the milder comparison without the scare-tactic concern.

Abaye says we open with the beginning of the statement; Rava says we do not. There is also a version of this dispute attributed differently to Rav Tavyumi, in which everybody agrees we do not open with the end — that it would be like bringing a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — but Abaye and Rava dispute the beginning.

The final *halakha* is: *lo patchinin lo bereisha velo besayfa* — we open with neither the beginning nor the end. We do not say it is like building a *bamah*, and certainly do not say it is like bringing a *korban* upon it. The upshot is that we do not open with any of these scare-tactic formulations, however instructive they may be as theological statements about the nature of vowing.

## Anti-Scare Tactics

### Shmuel: Vow-Keepers Wick…

The *Gemara* continues: we also do not open with the statement of Shmuel. Shmuel taught that even one who fulfills a vow is called a *rasha* — a wicked person. This too would function as an extreme scare tactic: even going through with your obligation is sinful.

### Verse on Ceasing Vows

Rabbi Abbahu identifies the verse: *ve-chi techdal lindor lo yihyeh becha cheit* — "if you cease to make vows, there will be no sin in you." He then applies a *gezeira shava*, linking the word *chadala* (ceasing) in this verse to the same word appearing elsewhere: *sham reshaim chadlu rogez* — "there the wicked have ceased their anger and discord." The linguistic connection associates the cessation of vowing with the domain of the *resha'im*, confirming that one who makes vows and fulfills them is in some sense counted among the wicked.

### Sages' Teaching

Rav Yosef notes that the same idea appears in material we ourselves have studied: *kenidrei kesherim lo amar klum* — if someone says "this should be binding upon me like the vows of upright people," he has said nothing, because upright people do not make vows. But *kenidrei resha'im* — like the vows of wicked people — that is a binding formula, invoking *nazir*, *korban*, or *shvua*. Thus even the *Mishnayos* themselves encode the assumption that vow-taking is the province of the wicked, while those who are truly righteous do not vow at all.

The broader conclusion of this entire section is that the *Gemara* in *Nedarim* consistently presents vowing in a negative light — not as the only view in *Chazal*, but certainly as the dominant thrust of this *masechet*. The statements cited as potential *pesakim* are rejected not because they are wrong, but because they would function as scare tactics, potentially inducing people to claim regret they do not genuinely feel.

## Anger Digression

### Gehinnom & Anger

The *Gemara* now pivots into what it frames as a digression — though the connections to the surrounding material are not entirely incidental. Since the previous discussion mentioned *resha'im* and their anger, the *Gemara* transitions into a series of statements about the consequences of anger. Rabbi Yonatan — or Rabbi Yochanan — teaches: *kol ha-ko'es kol minei gehenom sholtim bo* — anyone who gets angry, all forms of *gehenom* take hold of him.

### Verse on Anger

The scriptural basis comes from *Kohelet*: *haseir kaas milibecha veha'aveir ra'ah mibsarecha* — "remove anger from your heart and remove evil from your flesh." The *Gemara* identifies *ra'ah* with *gehenom*, citing: *vegam rasha leyom ra'ah* — "and also the wicked person is destined for a day of evil" — where the "day of evil" denotes a concrete punishment awaiting him.

It is worth pausing over what the *Gemara* means here. The text of *Kohelet* is saying something relatively straightforward: anger eats away at you internally, affects how you think and act, and corrodes your life from within. The *Gemara*'s more graphic formulation — that all forms of *gehenom* control the angry person — may well be understood not as a description of otherworldly punishment but as a vivid depiction of the natural consequences of rage. Anger makes a person irrational; it leads him to transgress things he would never otherwise violate; it burns him from within and destroys his relationships and his better judgment. That is the *peshat* of the *pasuk* in *Kohelet*, rendered in the *Gemara*'s characteristic style.

### Physical Effects

The *Gemara* adds a further consequence: *ha-tachtoniyot sholtot bo* — hemorrhoids take hold of him as well. The *pasuk* is brought from *Devarim*: *venatan Hashem lecha sham lev ragaz* — God will give you there an angry heart — along with *vechilyon einayim* (dimming of the eyes) and *vedaavon nefesh* (languishing of the soul). The *Gemara* asks: what is the ailment that simultaneously afflicts the eyes and pains the soul? The answer: hemorrhoids. The connection to *kaas* and internal burning is the underlying physiological intuition — that extreme emotional agitation produces physical inflammation and internal distress. Indeed, the entire phenomenon here reflects a sophisticated understanding that emotional states have concrete bodily consequences.

### Chuza Murder Story

The *Gemara* presents a striking narrative illustrating where unchecked anger leads. When Ulla traveled up to *Eretz Yisrael*, two men from Chuza accompanied him on the journey. During the trip, one of them fell into a violent rage and killed the other.

### Ulla's Fear Response

The murderer then turned to Ulla and asked: *ya'u avdi?* — "Did I do well? Was it all right what I did?" Ulla, fearing for his own life, replied yes, and — depending on the textual variant — either himself exposed the neck of the murder victim or told the murderer to do so. When Ulla later came before Rabbi Yochanan, he expressed concern: *dilma chas veshalom achzeiki* — "perhaps, God forbid, I strengthened the hands of a sinner by affirming what he did?" Rabbi Yochanan reassured him: *nafshecha hatzalta* — you saved yourself. Under normal circumstances such a response would be impermissible, but the imperative of self-preservation overrides it here.

One might also raise a related question about the instruction to expose the murder victim's neck — whether hastening the death of someone already dying might touch on the *halachic* question of *chayei sha'ah*, and whether the imperative of *chiyuv hatzala* applies differently to someone whose life is terminal. These are complex questions that belong to the *poskim*, but the narrative at least provides a concrete case around which to think through the issues.

### Exile Requirement

What astonished Rabbi Yochanan was not Ulla's conduct but the fact that such murderous anger could have erupted at all. The verse says *venatan Hashem lecha sham lev ragaz* — God will give you *there*, in exile, an angry heart. The context is *Bavel*, the place of exile. How, then, could such anger manifest before crossing the Jordan into *Eretz Yisrael*? Ulla replied: *hahu sha'ata lo avrinan Yardena* — at that moment we had not yet crossed the Jordan; we were still in *chutz la'aretz*, and so the condition of exile-driven anger still applied. This is somewhat ironic given what is known historically of the intense sectarian passions that characterized life in *Eretz Yisrael*, but the *Gemara* reads the verse as limiting this particular form of rage to the exilic condition.

### Anger Consequences

The *Gemara* continues with several additional statements about the consequences of anger. Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *afilu Shechina eina chashuva k'negdo* — when a person is angry, he does not even care about God's presence. The *pasuk* cited: *rasha k'govah apo bal yidrosh ein Elokim kol mizmosav* — the wicked person, in the height of his anger, does not seek God; God is absent from all his thoughts. This rings psychologically true: rage is totalizing. Nothing matters to the person in its grip — not prior commitments, not relationships, not religious obligations.

Rav Yirmiyah of Difti adds: anger causes a person to forget his Torah learning and to become increasingly foolish. The verse: *ki cha'as b'cheik k'silim yanu'ach* — anger rests in the bosom of fools. And Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds a diagnostic dimension: if a person gets angry, it is a known indicator that his sins outweigh his merits. The verse: *u-va'al cheima rav pasha* — the person given to anger has abundant transgression.

### Sin and Scripture

Rav Ada, son of Rav Chanina, offers a remarkable observation. Had Israel not sinned in the *cheit ha-miraglim* — the sin of the spies — they would have received nothing more than the five books of the *Torah* and the book of *Yehoshua* alone. Why *Yehoshua*? Because *Yehoshua* addresses the value and the division of *Eretz Yisrael*, providing narrative closure to the *Torah*'s foundational story of the people entering the land. But the rest of *Tanach* — the *Nevi'im Acharonim* in particular — exists primarily to deliver *mussar* to *Bnei Yisrael* for their repeated failures. Had they not sinned, all the religious wisdom they needed would have been contained in the five books of Moshe.

The verse cited: *ki berov chochma rov kaas* — for in the abundance of wisdom there is an abundance of anger. The accumulation of wisdom through the prophetic books was itself a product of the accumulation of sin, which required a divine response. There is something simultaneously humbling and moving in this formulation: the magnificent corpus of *Nevi'im* that contains some of the most eloquent articulations of Torah values exists precisely because the people needed to be called back from their failures. The wisdom was real, but its occasion was loss.

## Elohei Yisrael Vows

### Rav Assi's Rule

The *Gemara* returns now to the main discussion. Rav Assi teaches: *ein nizkakim l'Elohei Yisrael* — we do not attend to vows made in the name of the God of Israel, with one exception: *shalom bayis*.

### Wife's Vow Case

The illustrative case: *konam ishti nehenet li* — "may my wife derive no benefit from me" — taken because she stole his wallet or struck his son. If it then becomes clear that she did neither of those things, the question of how to release the vow arises. One might note that if he explicitly stated these false premises as the basis of the vow, it could well be a case of *nidrei shegagot* — vows taken on false premises — which are inherently non-binding and would not require formal *hataras nedarim* at all. The Ran discusses this point and some commentators accordingly bracket that portion of the case.

### Neder or Shvua

There is a significant interpretive dispute as to what precisely is meant when someone vows "in the name of *Elokei Yisrael*." The Ran, along with *Tosfot* and the Rambam, understands this as referring to a *shvua* — an oath. Taking an oath in God's name is a distinct and weightier act than an ordinary *neder*, which derives its force from the *korban* framework rather than from invoking God directly. On this reading, the passage is discussing a situation that arose in the context of *hataras nedarim* but actually involves the question of whether a *chacham* can release a *shvua* taken in God's name.

This connects to the broader dispute between *Beit Shamai* and *Beit Hillel*: *Beit Shamai* holds that one never performs *hatara* for a *shvua*, since a *shvua* engages God's name and is about one's personal integrity and honesty in a way that a *neder* is not; *Beit Hillel* holds that *hatara* is available for a *shvua* as well. According to Rav Assi, even *Beit Hillel* would agree that a *shvua* taken specifically in the name of *Elokei Yisrael* is too weighty to release — unless it stands in the way of *shalom bayis*. The Ran draws a beautiful parallel: just as the *midrash* teaches that God allows His name written in holiness to be erased in water in order to make peace between husband and wife (in the *sotah* procedure), so too a *shvua* taken in His name can be undone for the sake of marital peace.

Rashi, however, reads the case differently: the formula *konam ishti nehenet li* is explicitly *lashon neder*, the language of a vow, and so we are speaking of an actual *neder* — but one made with God's name invoked alongside it, creating a hybrid between the *neder* and *shvua* forms. Even without the full weight of a *shvua*, using God's name in the context of a *neder* adds sufficient gravity that we are reluctant to release it absent a compelling reason like *shalom bayis*.

### Rav Assi to Woman

When the woman comes before Rav Assi to seek release, he says to her: *i nadart b'mohi, mizdakkekna lach* — had you used the term *mohi*, which functions as a *kinuy* (euphemism) for an oath, I would have attended to you and found a way to release you. *Hashta d'lo nadart b'mohi ela b'Elokei Yisrael, lo mizdakkekna lach* — but since you used not a *kinuy* but the name *Elokei Yisrael* itself, I cannot help you. The terminological distinction carries decisive *halachic* weight: a *kinuy* or euphemism for a *shvua* is releasable; a vow or oath taken explicitly in the divine name is not — except for *shalom bayis*.

The confusion in terminology throughout this passage — the word *nadart* used to describe what appears to be a *shvua* — is itself revealing. The *Gemara* is not always precise in distinguishing between *nedarim* and *shvuot*, a point the Ran explicitly acknowledges.

### Kahana-Yosef Story

The *Gemara* presents a telling anecdote. Rav Kahana came to the home of Rav Yosef, who offered him something to eat. Rav Kahana declined: *mari kula — lo ta'imna* — in the name of the Master of all, I will not eat anything. Rav Yosef replied in kind: *lo mari kula lo ta'imna lei* — in the name of the Master of all, I will not feed you anything. The *Gemara* notes that this makes sense from Rav Kahana's side — he took a *shvua* not to eat. But why did Rav Yosef need to invoke God's name at all? The *Gemara* concludes that Rav Yosef was not taking a new *shvua* of his own, but was rather communicating: since you have invoked God's name in your *shvua* not to eat, I am constrained — I cannot help you out of it. I am therefore unable to give you anything to eat. The story is instructive in demonstrating how casually and consequentially God's name was being invoked in everyday situations — something the *Gemara* clearly views as problematic.

## Halakha & Geonic Views

### Rav Nachman Ruling

The *Gemara* now delivers the bottom-line *halakha* through Rav Nachman: *hilchata potchin b'charata venizkakim l'Elohei Yisrael* — the law is that we open with *charata* (simple regret), and we do attend to vows taken in the name of *Elohei Yisrael*. Both rulings cut in the direction of accessibility and leniency: we do not require a specific *pesach*, and we do not treat the invocation of God's name as an absolute bar to release.

### Ran's Clarification

The Ran clarifies the scope of Rav Nachman's ruling: *potchin b'charata bein b'neder bein b'shvua* — we open with *charata* for both a *neder* and a *shvua*; and *nizkakim nami l'chatchila l'Elohei Yisrael* — we attend *l'chatchila* even to vows taken in God's name. The Ran further adds that this applies even if the person was holding a *Sefer Torah* while taking the oath. Being in physical contact with a *Sefer Torah* during an oath is a psychological intensifier — analogous to placing one's hand on a Bible — but it does not alter the fundamental *halachic* analysis. The *Sefer Torah* is relevant in the context of *shvuat ha-dayanim*, a court-administered oath, where such tangible contact is used to heighten solemnity; it does not render the *shvua* irrevocable for purposes of *hatara*.

### Gaon Disagreement

The Ran records that an unnamed *Gaon* ruled that one who swears in the name of the *Sefer Torah* or the *Aseret Ha-Dibrot* has no release ever — *ein lo hafara olamit*. The Ran dismisses this position unequivocally: *leita le-milta klal* — it has no standing whatsoever. The *din* is the *din* of the *Gemara*, which releases even *shvuot* through *charata*, and *kol she-ken* for *nedarim*.

### Rav Hai Gaon

Rabbeinu Hai Gaon takes a more measured but still significantly stricter position. Against the plain ruling of the *Gemara*, Rav Hai holds that for *nedarim* one must open with a *pesach* — not *charata*. His reasoning is that we should practice as Rav Ashi described, releasing vows only in a manner analogous to the *arba nedarim*: there must be a genuine ground for retroactive disavowal. *Charata* alone is too vague. Furthermore, Rav Hai holds that for *shvuot*, even a *pesach* is insufficient — we never release a *shvua* except in a case of *davar mitzvah* or *shalom bayis*.

### Mar Yehudai Gaon

Mar Yehudai Gaon goes further still. In his view, *nedarim* are to be treated like *shvuot*: we do not release either except in cases of *mitzvah* or *shalom bayis*, and even then only through a genuine *pesach*, not *charata*. He adds that the *pesach* in question must be one established by a *chacham* — *petach d'chacham* — not the husband's unilateral *petach* (*petach d'ba'al*), which is a different mechanism belonging to the realm of *hafara* rather than *hatara*.

### Final Ruling

Both the Rambam and the *Shulchan Aruch* rule in accordance with the *Gemara*, not the *Geonim*. Their position is that we are always available to release someone from either a *neder* or a *shvua*, whether taken in God's name or not, whether through *pesach* or through *charata*, and that no additional barriers beyond what the *Gemara* itself establishes should be placed in the path of one seeking release. The *Geonim* may have had policy reasons for their stringency — making vows feel weightier so that people would not take them lightly, knowing release would always be available — but as a matter of *ikar hadin*, their positions were not accepted.

There is a real tension embedded in this entire question. The easier it is to obtain release from a vow, the more it may encourage people to make vows casually. Yet the harder it is to obtain release, the more trapped people become in obligations that may be causing them real harm. The *Gemara* resolved this tension decisively in favor of accessibility — and the *Rambam* and *Shulchan Aruch* affirmed that resolution.
