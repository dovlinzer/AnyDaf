# Hullin 135 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reishit HaGez Intro

### Opening

With the penultimate chapter of Masechet Chullin, the Mishnah introduces *reishit hagez* — the first of the shearings — which represents the inaugural priestly gift derived from non-korbanot animals. Up to this point, the tractate has been occupied with *zroa lechayayim veKeivah*, the meat gifts taken from slaughtered animals. Now the subject shifts to wool, a gift that is structurally different and limited in scope.

### Mishnah Applicability

The Mishnah opens with a sweeping statement of applicability: *reishit hagez* applies both in *Eretz Yisrael* and outside of it, both when the *Beit HaMikdash* stands and when it does not. This broad applicability is unsurprising, since the obligation has no inherent connection to the Temple or to the Land. The Mishnah further specifies that the obligation applies only to non-sanctified animals (*chulin*), not to sanctified ones (*mukdashin*) — a ruling parallel to what we encountered with *zroa lechayayim veKeivah*.

### Zroa vs. Gez

Notably, the Mishnah does not here digress into the question of whether the animal was sanctified before or after it developed a blemish — a digression that did appear in the context of *zroa lechayayim veKeivah*. That discussion is deferred to Masechet Bechorot.

The Mishnah then enumerates the stringencies of *zroa lechayayim veKeivah* relative to *reishit hagez*. The meat gifts — the foreleg, jaw, and stomach — apply to both cattle and sheep (*bakar u-tzon*), and they apply even if one slaughters only a single animal, whether much or little (*be-merubeh u-ve-mu'at*). *Reishit hagez*, by contrast, applies exclusively to sheep (*rachelot*). Goat hair and cattle wool, even though they have their uses — and even in the *Mikdash* context — are not subsumed under the word *gez*, shearing. Shearing, as a halachic category, is reserved for sheep alone. Furthermore, *reishit hagez* requires a plurality of animals, which brings us to the next significant debate.

---

## Merubeh Debate

### Tzon Etymology

The Torah's formulation is *reishit gez tzoncha* — the first of the shearings of your flock. The word *tzon*, flock, is not the word for a single sheep. Just as "cattle" in English implies a group, *tzon* connotes a collective, a flock — not one lone animal. One would never refer to a single sheep as a *tzon*. Therefore, the obligation cannot apply to one sheep alone; by the very semantics of the verse, a plurality is required.

### Merubeh Requirement

The question then becomes: what is the minimum that qualifies as a *merubeh*, a plurality? The term "flock" is evocative but imprecise. How many sheep does one need to be obligated?

### Two vs. Five

*Beit Shammai* says two sheep suffice, as evidenced by the verse (Isaiah 7:21): *yichyeh ish egla bakar u-shtei tzon* — "a man shall raise a calf and two of the flock." The word *tzon* is applied there to just two, demonstrating that two animals can constitute a flock. In this instance, it is *Beit Shammai* who is more stringent, imposing the obligation even on a minimal flock of two.

*Beit Hillel* requires five, citing the verse (I Samuel 25:18): *chamesh tzon asuyot* — "five sheep ready," where the context of *tzon* appears alongside a quantity of five, laden with their wool. *Beit Hillel* finds the context more apt: not only does this verse invoke *tzon*, but the notion of animals laden with wool makes it specifically relevant to shearing.

### Rabbi Dosa View

*Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas* introduces an additional threshold. He agrees that five sheep are required, but he further demands that each individual sheep produce a minimum quantity of wool — specifically, one and a half *maneh* (a *peras* is a half *maneh*) — before the obligation is triggered. If a given sheep yields only a negligible amount of wool, it does not count toward the requirement in his view. The obligation arises only when the shearing is substantive: five sheep, each producing at least this minimum.

### Chachamim View

The Sages (*Chachamim*) disagree and hold that five sheep are sufficient regardless of the quantity each produces — *kol shehu*, any amount. Even the tiniest yield from each of five sheep creates the obligation. The Gemara will subsequently probe what exactly *kol shehu* means in practice.

---

## Amount & Wool Status

### Five vs. Ten Sela'im

Once the threshold of obligation is established, the Mishnah turns to the amount that must actually be given to the *kohen*. The minimum gift is defined by weight: the equivalent of five *sela'im* as measured in Judea, which equals ten *sela'im* in Galilee. The Judean *sela* was twice the weight of the Galilean one, so the underlying physical quantity is the same — roughly a modest but meaningful portion of wool, approximating what we will later see is enough to produce a small garment. When the Gemara reaches the detailed discussion of this measure, the proportional dimensions can be translated into more concrete terms; for now, it is sufficient to note that there is both a fixed minimum and, as the Gemara will explore, a proportional component that scales with larger shearings.

### Laundering Required

The Mishnah adds that the wool given to the *kohen* must be *meluban* — clean and laundered — and not in its raw, dirty state. Sheep wool comes off the animal in an unprocessed condition, having been exposed to the elements, mud, and general pastoral realities. One is not permitted to hand the *kohen* wool in that state; it must be cleaned first.

### Wool Cleaning

This raises a practical question about the cleaning process. Wool contains *lanolin*, a natural oil embedded in the fibers, which means simple washing may be insufficient. The cleaning of raw wool involves both washing and combing to produce usable material. Whatever the precise process, the halachic point is clear: the wool must be presented in a clean, processed form.

### Small Garment

The Mishnah anchors the minimum amount through a functional criterion: the gift to the *kohen* must be enough to make a small garment. As the verse states, *titen lo* — you shall give to him — implying a gift of real usability and significance. The *kohen* must be able to do something with what he receives; the minimum is therefore the smallest meaningful garment, something like a small belt.

### Dyed Before Gift

An important case: if the owner did not manage to give the wool to a *kohen* before proceeding to dye it — completing the cleaning process and then applying dye — he is *patur*, exempt. This ruling requires explanation.

### Dyeing Transforms

The exemption for wool that was dyed before the gift was given reflects the principle that dyeing constitutes a transformative act (*shinui*), a significant enough change to the object that it is treated halachically as if the original item no longer exists in its prior form. This principle appears in other contexts in the Mishnah: if one receives another's wool to clean and accidentally dyes it, the act of dyeing is considered significant enough to constitute a kind of conversion of the object — the original owner's claim is displaced, and the person who dyed it must compensate monetarily, but the wool in its changed form is now considered his.

In the context of *matnot kehunah*, the question of "stealing" a priestly gift is conceptually complex. Earlier in the tractate, there was discussion of whether the priestly gifts can be stolen at all, given that no specific *kohen* has yet acquired them. A butcher who gives the meat to one *kohen* rather than another is not considered a thief — he has simply moved the gift. But transformation is different. When the wool is dyed, it is no longer the same object. The Mishnah therefore concludes that dyeing the wool effectively removes it from the category of *matnot kehunah*: the original obligation is discharged — wrongly, but factually — and the obligation to give does not extend to the dyed product. One has undoubtedly done something wrong, but the dyed wool itself is no longer subject to the gift requirement.

### Clean Without Dye

By contrast, if the wool was laundered but not dyed, the owner is obligated to give it to the *kohen*. This is self-evident from the earlier ruling that the wool must be given in a *meluban* state. Laundering is not a transformation; it is merely the required preparation. The obligation therefore remains fully intact.

## Ownership Rules

### Non-Jew Owner

If one purchases the shearings of a non-Jew's sheep, one is exempt from *reishit hagez*. The Gemara will root this in the word *tzoncha* — your sheep — which implies that the sheep must be owned by a Jew. If the animals belong to a non-Jew, the obligation never attaches, regardless of who subsequently acquires the wool.

### Jewish Seller

If one purchases the shearings from a fellow Jew — a situation analogous to buying meat from a butcher after slaughter — the question is who bears the obligation. The rule is: if the seller retained some wool, the obligation falls on the seller. Just as the wool does not have a fixed recipient among the *kohanim* until designated, what matters is who holds the unseparated wool. If the seller kept even a small portion, that remaining wool is what he must designate for the *kohen*.

### No Remainder

If the seller sold all of his wool and retained nothing, he has nothing from which to fulfill the obligation. In such a case, the buyer becomes obligated and must give from his portion. This parallels the earlier principle that *matnot kehunah* cannot be discharged with monetary compensation — the physical item in whose possession the obligation resides is what matters.

### Wool Categories

The Mishnah introduces a further nuance. What if the seller had two distinct types of wool — for example, wool from black sheep and wool from white sheep, or wool from males and wool from females? These different categories apparently produce wool of different qualities or characters.

### Separate Categories

If the seller sold one category entirely — say, all the black wool — but retained the other category — say, all the white wool — one might think: the seller still has wool in his possession, so the entire obligation rests on him. The Mishnah rejects this. Because the two types of wool are considered distinct shearings, each category is treated independently. The buyer, who holds all the black wool, must give from his category; the seller, who holds all the white wool, gives from his. As the Mishnah formulates it: *zeh noten le-atzmo ve-zeh noten le-atzmo* — each one gives from his own.

One might ask whether a *shutafut*, a partnership in a single flock, creates any comparable complexity — specifically, does the word *tzoncha*, "your sheep," in the singular, require sole ownership and thereby exempt partners? This question is explicitly raised and will be taken up by the Gemara.

## Sanctified Exclusion

### Qedoshim Question

The Gemara opens its analysis by asking the obvious question: why does the obligation of *reishit hagez* not apply to *kodashim*, sanctified animals?

### Tzoncha Exclusion

The answer is derivational: the verse reads *tzoncha* — your sheep — which means sheep owned by the *baalim*, the lay owner, and thereby excludes *tzon hekdesh*, sheep owned by *hekdesh*, the Temple. The verse's singular possessive is interpreted to demand lay ownership.

### Why Verse Needed

The Gemara then asks why such a verse was necessary in the first place. Is it not obvious that one cannot shear sanctified animals? After all, the Torah explicitly states *lo tagoz bechor tzoncha* — you may not shear the firstborn of your sheep — and what applies to the *bechor* applies by extension to all sacrificial animals. The prohibition on shearing *kodshei mizbe'ach* is biblical. So why would anyone think the obligation of *reishit hagez* could apply?

### Kodshim Types

The answer introduces a critical distinction: there are two categories of sanctified animals. *Kodshei mizbe'ach* — animals sanctified as sacrifices — are indeed forbidden to be shorn. But *kodshei bedek habayit* — animals sanctified for the Temple treasury, whose value is donated rather than their bodies being offered on the altar — occupy a different legal space. Perhaps the Temple itself could shear such animals as a means of generating income, and the question would arise whether it must then give *reishit hagez* to a *kohen*.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

On this point, *Rabbi Eliezer* rules that even *kodshei bedek habayit* are forbidden to be shorn or put to work. However, the Gemara clarifies that *Rabbi Eliezer*'s prohibition is only rabbinic (*miderabbanan*), not biblical. The reason is likely concern about confusion with *kodshei mizbe'ach*. At the biblical level, shearing *kodshei bedek habayit* is permitted. Accordingly, if someone violated the rabbinic restriction and shorn such an animal, the question of whether *reishit hagez* must be given is a genuine one — and it is for that scenario that the verse is required.

---

## Sanctified Scenarios

### Redemption Solution

The Gemara now works through the precise scenario in which one might have thought the obligation of *reishit hagez* applies to sanctified animals. If someone violated the rabbinic restriction and shorn *kodshei bedek habayit*, perhaps the solution is: redeem the wool (by compensating the Temple treasury for its value), and then give a portion of the wool to the *kohen*. This would satisfy both the obligation to the Temple and the obligation of *reishit hagez*. Hence a verse is needed to tell us this is not required.

### Redemption Problem

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty: how could one even redeem the wool? The Torah requires that when redeeming a sanctified animal, *ve-he'emido ha-kohen ve-he'erich oto lifnei Hashem* — the priest shall stand it up and assess it before God. The rabbinic understanding of this verse is that sanctified animals can only be redeemed when they are alive and able to be "stood up." Shorn wool, having been separated from the living animal, cannot be stood up, and therefore arguably cannot be redeemed. This difficulty arises specifically according to the opinion that the requirement of *ha'amada ve-ha'aracha* — standing up and assessment — applies even to *kodshei bedek habayit*, not only to sacrificial animals. According to the opinion that *kodshei bedek habayit* do not require *ha'amada ve-ha'aracha*, the redemption path remains open; but according to the other opinion, even that path is foreclosed. Either way, the redemption route seems unavailable.

### Rav Mani Solution

The Gemara resolves this impasse through a case constructed by *Rav Mani bar Patish* in the name of *Rabbi Yannai*. The operative scenario is one where the owner sanctified his animal to *bedek habayit* but explicitly excluded the wool from the sanctification: *makdish behemto le-bedek ha-bayit chutz mi-gizoteha* — "I am sanctifying my animal to the Temple treasury except for its shearings." By retaining ownership of the wool, the owner never transferred it to *hekdesh*. He then shears the wool — which he owns — and the question is whether he must give *reishit hagez* from that wool to a *kohen*.

### Own Wool Exempt

This is the scenario the verse addresses. Even though the owner retained possession of the wool, the sheep itself belongs to *hekdesh*. The verse *tzoncha* — your sheep — requires not only that the wool be yours but that the sheep be yours as well. Since the sheep is owned by the Temple treasury, the obligation of *reishit hagez* does not apply, even to the wool the owner legitimately retained. Hence the verse was needed.

## Kodshei Mizbe'ach

### Sanctity Spreads

Having established why the verse is needed in the case of *kodshei bedek habayit*, the Gemara asks: why not apply the same reasoning — sanctifying the animal but retaining ownership of the wool — even to *kodshei mizbe'ach*, sacrificial animals? If that were possible, the same question would arise and the same verse would be needed for the same reason, without having to invoke *bedek habayit* at all. The Gemara answers: *kachshi* — it weakens the animal. Shearing is a physically diminishing act; it causes some degree of debilitation to the sheep. If the animal is a sacrifice, one cannot perform an act that impairs it, as that would impinge on the sanctity of the sacrifice and potentially constitute *me'ilah*. Therefore, the scenario of retaining ownership of the wool while sanctifying the animal cannot be constructed for *kodshei mizbe'ach*.

### Shearing Weakens

One might then ask: if the owner retains ownership not only of the wool but also of the animal's diminishment — *chutz mi-giza ve-kechisha*, "except for the shearing and the weakening" — couldn't one construct the scenario even for a sacrifice? The Gemara addresses this directly: no. With a sacrifice, one cannot play these games of subdivided ownership. The sanctity of a *korban* is not merely a matter of who legally owns which portion; it defines the very identity and nature of the animal. Once an animal is consecrated as a sacrifice, its entire being is defined as a *korban*, and one cannot carve out a retained interest in its diminishment as if that were a separable portion.

### Temurah Proof

The Gemara grounds this principle in a debate in Masechet Temurah. *Rabbi Yose* rules: *ha-omer raglah shel zo olah kulah olah* — if one declares "the leg of this animal is an *olah*," the entire animal becomes an *olah*. The sanctity of a sacrifice, once applied to any part, spreads to the whole. Even *Rabbi Meir*, who disagrees in the specific case of the leg — arguing that the leg, since the animal can survive without it, does not carry the soul of the animal and therefore sanctifying it does not sanctify the whole — would agree that if one sanctifies a part upon which the animal's life depends (*davar she-ha-neshamah teluyah bah*), then the entire animal becomes sanctified. The upshot is clear: by *kodshei mizbe'ach*, the identity of the animal as a *korban* is total and indivisible. Ownership subdivisions that are permissible for *bedek habayit* simply do not function for sacrificial animals. This is why the Gemara is forced to develop the scenario through *bedek habayit*, and why the verse *tzoncha* was needed.

## Plural Override

### Challah Reishit

The Gemara now pivots to a broader inquiry. If *Rabbi Elai*'s principle is that a singular possessive in the Torah (*tzoncha* — your sheep) demands sole ownership and excludes partnership, does this principle apply uniformly across other agricultural and priestly obligations? The Gemara works through *challah*, *peah*, and *terumah*.

For *challah*, there is a potential analogy: the Torah uses the word *reishit* — "first" — both for *challah* and for *reishit hagez*. One might propose: just as *reishit hagez* excludes partnership (according to *Rabbi Elai*), so too should *challah* exclude partnership.

### Plural Challah

The response is that the Torah writes *arisoteichem* — your kneading troughs, in the plural — which overrides any exclusion of partnership. The plural possessive signals that even when multiple people are involved, the obligation applies.

### Trough Amount

The Gemara then asks: if the plural *arisoteichem* resolves the partnership question, what independent work does the word do? The answer is that *arisoteichem* comes to teach the minimum quantity that triggers the *challah* obligation — specifically, the amount equivalent to the *omer* of *man* that each person received in the wilderness (*omer le-gulgoles*). The Torah thus uses this word to anchor the size of the dough that obligates in *challah*.

### Peah Plural

For *peah*, the Torah says *sadcha* — your field — in the singular, which might suggest sole ownership is required and partnership is excluded. But elsewhere the verse reads *u-ve-kutzrechem et ketzir artzechem* — "when you harvest the harvest of your land" — in the plural, which overrides the singular and includes partnerships.

### Peah Non-Jew

Nevertheless, *sadcha* in the singular still does meaningful work: it excludes partnership with a non-Jew. If a non-Jew is a partial owner of the field, the Jewish partner is exempt from the obligation of *peah* for the non-Jew's portion.

---

## Kodshei Mizbe'ach (II)

### Part vs. Whole

The Gemara returns to sharpen the principle from Temurah. The debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yose* is more precisely framed: *Rabbi Meir*'s leniency applies only when the sanctified part is one from which the animal could be separated and still survive — such as a leg. That part lacks the *neshamah*, the life-force of the animal, and therefore sanctifying it alone does not define the whole.

### Korban Identity

But where the sanctified part is one upon which the animal's life depends — its heart, chest, or head — even *Rabbi Meir* agrees that the sanctity spreads to the entire animal. This is not merely a legal technicality of ownership spreading; it is a statement about the identity of a *korban*. A sacrifice is defined as a unified whole, an offering of the entire being. Once any life-sustaining part is consecrated, the animal's identity as a *korban* is total. This is why one cannot construct retained partial ownership in the context of *kodshei mizbe'ach* the way one can for *bedek habayit*.

---

## Plural Override (II)

### Terumah Concession

The Gemara introduces *Rava*'s formulation: *Rabbi Elai* concedes that *terumah* does not require sole ownership — partnership in *terumah* still obligates.

### Plural Terumah

Although the Torah writes *degancha* — your grain — in the singular, which by *Rabbi Elai*'s logic would exclude partnership, the Torah also writes *trumoteichem* — your plural *terumah* — which overrides the singular and includes partnership. The plural possessive functions as an explicit signal that the obligation extends to shared ownership.

### Non-Jew Excluded

The singular *degancha* is not therefore without purpose: it excludes partnership with a non-Jew. When a non-Jew is a part owner of the field, the Jewish partner's portion remains obligated in *terumah*, but the singular possessive excludes the non-Jew's portion from the obligation. This is the minimum work the singular can do once the plural has overridden its exclusion of Jewish partnerships.

## Rava's Wool Case

### Wool Sanctified

*Rava* introduces a distinct and simpler scenario: rather than sanctifying the animal to the Temple treasury while retaining the wool, what if a person sanctified only the wool itself — not the sheep at all — to *hekdesh*?

### Redeem Question

The Gemara asks: once the person wishes to shear the sheep, is he obligated to redeem the sanctified wool and then give a portion of that redeemed wool to the *kohen* as *reishit hagez*?

### Titen Excludes

The answer derives from the word *titen* in the verse *titen lo* — you shall give to him. The Gemara reads this as establishing: *mi she-ein mechusar ela giza u-netina* — only in a case where nothing stands between you and giving it to the *kohen* except shearing and giving is one obligated. This excludes *ze she-mechusar giza pidya u-netina* — a case where, in addition to shearing and giving, one must also first perform an act of redemption. Where an intermediate step of redemption is required before the gift can be transferred to the *kohen*, the obligation does not apply.

---

## Partnership Debate

### Partnership Q

The Gemara now addresses the central question raised by the Mishnah's wording: what does *tzoncha* — your sheep — exclude? Does it exclude partnership with a non-Jew, partnership with a fellow Jew, or both?

### Rabbi Elai View

*Rabbi Elai* holds that *tzoncha*, in the singular possessive, requires sole ownership. The singular implies: exclusively yours — not *tzonechem*, not shared. Therefore even partnership with a fellow Jew exempts one from *reishit hagez*. If two Jews jointly own a flock, neither is obligated to give *reishit hagez* from the shared shearings.

### Chachamim View

The *Chachamim* disagree. They read *tzoncha* more narrowly: the singular possessive excludes only partnership with a non-Jew, whose partial ownership renders the entire animal's status ambiguous in a way that discharges the obligation. Partnership with a fellow Jew, however, does not disrupt the obligation; both Jewish partners remain obligated, each giving from their respective portion.

### Reishit Separation

The Sages support their reading from the structure of the verse itself. The Torah uses the word *reishit* twice — once in the context of *terumah* (*reishit degancha*) and once here (*reishit gez tzoncha*). Had the two been meant to be read as a single continuous obligation, the Torah could have written simply *reishit degancha ve-gez tzoncha titen lo*. By repeating *reishit*, the Torah separates the two categories into independent subjects. Therefore, one cannot transfer the exclusions derived from *terumah* to *reishit hagez*; each stands independently, and *tzoncha* by the shearings does no more than exclude the non-Jew.

### Elai Counter

*Rabbi Elai* responds by invoking the *vav* that connects the two clauses: *ve-reishit gez tzoncha titen lo*. The conjunctive *vav* ties the law of *reishit hagez* back to the preceding discussion of *terumah*, allowing the exclusions already derived there — namely, that partnership with a non-Jew is excluded by *degancha* — to carry over to the case of shearings. Having already established the exclusion of non-Jewish partnership from *terumah*, when we reach *tzoncha* in the context of the shearings, that word is free to do additional work: it now excludes even Jewish partnership.

---

## Breira Debate

### Field Partnership

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* that examines a case of a Jew and non-Jew who jointly own a field and together harvest it. The central question is what happens to the *terumah* and *maaser* obligations of the grain they divide at harvest.

### Yesh Breira

According to *Rabbi* (here understood to represent the position of *yesh breira* — retroactive assignment), when the two partners divide the harvest and each takes his portion, we say retroactively that each partner's share was always "meant" to come from his portion of the field. The Jew's 50 bushels grew from the Jewish half of the field; the non-Jew's 50 bushels grew from the non-Jew's half. The result is that the grain is intermingled in terms of obligation: the Jew's portion consists of grain that is partly *tevel* (obligated in *terumot u-ma'asrot*) and partly fully exempt, because his portion grew partly from the Jewish-owned half and partly — by the logic of this view — the non-Jew's portion is carved out separately.

### Ein Breira

*Rabban Gamliel* holds *ein breira*: there is no retroactive assignment. The division at harvest is what determines ownership, and it is a clean division. Whatever the Jew took is fully in his possession, and since his ownership is total, his entire portion is obligated in *terumah* and *maaser*. The non-Jew's portion is entirely exempt. The two positions thus produce opposite practical outcomes: under *yesh breira*, the Jew's grain is a mixture of obligated and exempt; under *ein breira*, it is entirely obligated.

### Agreed Exempt

The Gemara notes that on one point all opinions agree: the non-Jew's portion is exempt from *terumah* and *maaser*, and the Jew's portion — to whatever extent it is his — is obligated. The dispute is only about whether we retroactively assign clean ownership boundaries (yesh *breira*) or whether the mixed-field reality means the Jew holds a grain supply that was always compositionally mixed (*ein breira*).

### Rashi Solution

There is a lengthy analysis here from *Rashi*, who works out how one actually fulfills the obligation under the position of *Rabbi* (*ein breira*). If the Jew holds 100 bushels that are conceptually 50% obligated and 50% not, he cannot simply give five bushels as *maaser* (a tenth of the obligated 50), because those five bushels are themselves 50% obligated and 50% not. Instead, *Rashi* explains, he should take grain from a different, entirely Jewish-owned field — grain that is 100% obligated — and use five bushels of that to serve as the *maaser* for his 50 obligated bushels mixed into these 100. This way, he gives only five rather than ten, since only 50 of his 100 are obligated. Under *Rabban Gamliel*'s view (*yesh breira*), all 100 are obligated, and he would give ten.

### Divisible vs. Unit

This discussion illuminates an important distinction that *Rashi* draws between grain and animals. For grain, the principle is that grain is inherently divisible: every kernel is its own unit, and the non-Jew's fractional ownership does not "contaminate" or define the Jew's portion as a whole — it merely means some of the grain is his and some is not, and these can, at least conceptually, be sorted out. Animals, by contrast, are indivisible units. A sheep or cow is one animal. If a non-Jew holds even a partial ownership in that animal, the non-Jew's ownership is not confined to a separable portion — it colors the whole animal. This is why, when a non-Jew is a partner in a sheep, the Jew is fully exempt from *zroa lechayayim veKeivah* and *reishit hagez*, whereas when a non-Jew is a partner in a grain field, the Jew's portion of the grain remains fully obligated. The conceptual divisibility of grain versus the indivisibility of an animal as a single halachic unit is the structural distinction *Rashi* identifies to reconcile these seemingly disparate rulings.
