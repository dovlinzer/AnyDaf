# Bekhorot 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hachashud Overview

### Opening

Today's *daf* is 30. We pick up at the very bottom of 29b.

### Previous Context

The preceding discussion addressed the prohibition against purchasing from someone who is suspected of violating certain religious obligations. After a rich analysis of the prohibition against receiving payment for *mitzvos* and its possible exceptions, the *Gemara* turned to cases where someone may be *shochet* a *bechor*, or selling *shvi'is* produce in violation of the law, and ruled that one may not purchase from such a person. Two distinct rationales were proposed: first, that the object itself may be *asur b'hana'ah*, and one wishes to avoid inadvertently benefiting from forbidden goods; second, that the purchaser should not indirectly support the seller's prohibited activity.

An interesting *nafka mina* between these two rationales arises when, for instance, someone purchases from a third party who had already bought from the suspected seller, or when someone wishes to use items that were purchased from the suspected seller and subsequently discarded. May one pick up and use such items? The plain reading seems to focus on the object itself, but in other contexts the *Gemara* is concerned about the purchaser indirectly supporting illegal activity — particularly in discussions about buying from a *gannav*. The question of which rationale is primary thus carries significant practical implications.

### Teruma Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֶחָשׁוּד לִהְיוֹת מוֹכֵר תְּרוּמָה לְשׁוּם חוּלִּין — אֵין לוֹקְחִים מִמֶּנּוּ אֲפִילּוּ מַיִם וָמֶלַח, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ זִיקַּת תְּרוּמָה וּמַעַשְׂרוֹת אֵין לוֹקְחִים מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who is suspect with regard to selling *teruma* under the guise of non-sacred** produce, **one** may **not purchase even water and salt from him;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says: One** may **not purchase from him any** item **that has relevance to *teruma* and tithes.**

The *mishna* at the bottom of 29b introduces the following case: *hachashud l'hiyos mocher truma l'shum chulin* — a person suspected of selling *truma* under the guise of *chulin*. If a non-*kohen* were to eat such produce, he would be *chayav misa bidei shamayim*. Accordingly, *ein lokchim mimenu* — one may not purchase from him. The *mishna* proceeds to extend this prohibition, as we shall see, well beyond the object of suspicion itself.

## Rabbi Yehuda vs. Shimon

### R. Yehuda's Fine

Rabbi Yehuda rules: *afilu mayim u'melach* — one may not purchase from this person even water and salt. This is clearly a broader societal measure, going beyond the narrow concern of inadvertently benefiting from a forbidden object. It is unmistakably a fine (*knas*), designed to create a disincentive for such behavior and protect the integrity of communal commerce. Water and salt, of course, carry no *truma u'ma'asros* obligation — they do not grow from the ground — yet Rabbi Yehuda extends the prohibition to them nonetheless.

### R. Shimon's View

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *kol she'yesh bo zikas truma u'ma'asros, ein lokchim mimenu* — anything that has a potential connection to *trumos u'ma'asros*, one may not purchase. But items entirely outside the orbit of *trumos u'ma'asros*, such as water and salt, may freely be bought from this person. Rabbi Shimon, then, does not extend the prohibition to a general *knas*, but confines it to items within the relevant category.

### Zikas Teruma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי קִרְבֵי דָגִים, דִּמְעָרְבִי בְּהוּ שֶׁמֶן זַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is added** by Rabbi Shimon's statement that one may not purchase any item that has relevance to *teruma* and tithes from one who is suspect with regard to selling *teruma*? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon's statement serves **to add fish innards** to this prohibition, **as** people **mix olive oil with them,** and it might be oil of *teruma*.

The *Gemara* asks: *l'asuyei mai* — what does Rabbi Shimon's expansive language ("anything connected to *trumos u'ma'asros*") actually include? The answer given is *l'asuyei kirvei dagin* — fish guts. Why should fish guts be subject to suspicion regarding *trumos u'ma'asros*? Because *d'm'arvei bo shemen zayis* — it is common practice to mix olive oil into them, and olive oil is certainly subject to the obligations of *truma* and *ma'aser*. Accordingly, even though the direct connection is indirect and perhaps the oil content is small, one may not purchase fish guts from a suspected seller — but water and salt, which have no conceivable connection, remain permissible.

## Butcher & Nuts

### Butcher Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא טַבָּחָא דַּהֲוָה חָשׁוּד לְזַבּוֹנֵי תַּרְבָּא דְּאַטְמָא בְּמַר דְּכַנְתָּא, קַנְסֵיהּ רָבָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי אֲפִילּוּ אַמְגּוֹזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain butcher who was suspected of selling** **fat of the** kidney adjacent to the **thigh,** which is forbidden, in place **of fat of the intestines,** which is permitted. **Rava penalized him** and prohibited him **even from selling nuts.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a case more immediately recognizable from the world of *kashrus*. There was a *tabcha* — a butcher — who was suspected of selling the fat of the kidneys (*tarba d'atuma*) under the guise of *d'kanta*, a particular type of fat connected to the intestines that is in fact permitted. In other words, he was selling *cheilev* while claiming it was the permissible variety, and there were grounds to suspect that this was not the case.

The butcher's scheme was elaborate. Children are naturally drawn to nuts — the *Gemara* in Bava Metzia notes that merchants would distribute nuts to attract children and, through them, their parents to the shop. This butcher exploited that tendency: he would give nuts to the children of the *shochtim*, seduce them with small gifts, and thereby induce them to bring him their fathers' discarded *cheilev*, which he would then sell as permitted fat. The nuts were thus a direct instrument in the commission of the offense, a tool of corruption embedded in the very mechanism of his crime. The act may also have implicated *gezel*, beyond the *kashrus* concerns.

### Rava's Fine

*Kansyei Rava l'mizvnei afilu amguzei* — Rava fined him, ruling that no one should purchase from him, not even nuts.

### Rav Papa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֲפִילּוּ מַיִם וָמֶלַח נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava: In accordance with** which opinion in the mishna is your ruling? It must be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. If it is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** then you should prohibit this butcher from selling **even water and salt.**

Rav Papa challenged Rava: *k'man* — like whom is this ruling? If it follows Rabbi Yehuda, who imposes a broad *knas* extending even to water and salt, then the fine should extend far beyond nuts. And if it follows Rabbi Shimon, who limits the prohibition to items connected to the offense, why are nuts — which seem unrelated to the *kashrus* of *cheilev* — included?

### Nuts Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וּבְאִיסּוּרָא דְּגוּפֵיהּ קָנְסִינַן לֵיהּ. סְתָם דַּרְדְּקֵי גְּרוּ בְּאַמְגּוֹזֵי, וְאָזֵיל וּמְשַׁבֵּשׁ לִבְנֵי טַבָּחֵי, וְגָרֵי לְהוּ בְּאַמְגּוֹזֵי, וּמַיְיתוּ לֵיהּ תַּרְבָּא דְּאַטְמָא, וּמְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ בְּמַר דְּכַנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava replied: **Actually,** my ruling is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that one is suspected only about those items that are related to the prohibition in question. **And we are** in fact **penalizing him with regard to** items related to **the prohibition itself.** How so? **Ordinary children are enticed by nuts, and** that individual **went and misled the children of butchers and enticed them with nuts, and they brought him fat of the** kidney that is adjacent to the **thigh, and he sold it as** though it were **fat of the intestines.**

The *Gemara* resolves this: *u'l'olam Rabbi Shimon*. Rava is in fact following Rabbi Shimon, but a more nuanced reading of Rabbi Shimon than one might initially assume. Rabbi Shimon does not categorically oppose *kinsei* (fines); rather, he insists that any fine must bear a meaningful connection to the transgression itself. The *Gemara* reframes his position: *b'issura u'b'issura d'gufei kansinan lei* — we fine the transgressor in matters that were directly implicated in the act of transgression. Nuts were not incidental; they were the very mechanism by which the butcher recruited the children of *shochtim* to procure the forbidden fat. The connection is thus real and organic, not formal. Water and salt, by contrast, are entirely unrelated to the crime.

This *Gemara* speaks directly to a contemporary concern. When a butcher is discovered to have been systematically selling *treifa* as *kosher* — cases of this kind are not unknown — the intuition that the entire business must be shut down makes sense at a societal level. Without meaningful disincentive, the only cost of the wrongdoing is that one ceases to do it. The broader communal dimensions of *shochtim* suspected of improper practice were discussed in Chullin as well, where various levels of fines and removal from their positions were addressed. There, too, the question of *chashud* versus definitively known malpractice arises, and whether our responses to these different states should be consistent.

## Chashud Context

### Perek Relevance

This *perek* of Bekhorot has ranged across a remarkable span of contemporary relevance. We began with the question of receiving payment for *mitzvos* and worked through the halachic landscape of medical and legal practice, *garmi*, and the nature of *din*. Now we arrive at a central discussion of the institution of *chashud* — suspicion of violating religious law — which has profound implications for everyday communal life.

We begin with the narrow case of *chashud* in commercial dealings: may one buy from someone suspected of violating *shvi'is* or *bechor* prohibitions? But the discussion quickly broadens. The question that now drives the *Gemara* is whether suspicion established in one domain necessarily implies suspicion in another. This question has very direct contemporary relevance — not only in theory, but in how we navigate trust and distrust in actual relationships and institutions.

The same analysis that governs whether to trust a suspected *shvi'is* violator about *ma'aser* also governs — at least in principle — whether to trust someone known to cheat in business about *kashrus*, or whether violations in one area of religious life imply laxity in another. The *Gemara*'s approach here, as we shall see, is grounded not in formal halachic equivalence but in a realistic, and somewhat sobering, reading of human psychology.

There is also a systemic concern that runs through this discussion. The authority to declare someone *chashud* and thereby restrict their commercial standing is real rabbinic power. Used responsibly, it serves society. But the history of *kashrut* certification demonstrates that such authority can also be wielded irresponsibly — leveraged for policy goals entirely unrelated to the actual substance of *kashrus*. A *hashgacha* withheld because of mixed-gender seating, or because a venue stays open on *motzei Shabbos*, reflects a very different use of this authority than the *Gemara* envisions. Conversely, it is genuinely puzzling that *hashgachos* are routinely granted to restaurants on *Sukkot* without any verification that the *sukka* on the premises is *kosher*. A *sukka* with inadequate *schach* actively misleads patrons who assume the establishment's *hechsher* implies a *kosher sukka*; that is a direct instance of *machshil* — causing others to sin — that falls squarely within the *Gemara*'s own framework of communal responsibility.

### Core Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית — אֵינוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת, הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַמַּעַשְׂרוֹת — אֵינוֹ חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית. הֶחָשׁוּד עַל זֶה וְעַל זֶה — חָשׁוּד עַל הַטְּהָרוֹת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁהוּא חָשׁוּד עַל הַטְּהָרוֹת — וְאֵינוֹ חָשׁוּד לֹא עַל זֶה וְלֹא עַל זֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: הֶחָשׁוּד עַל דָּבָר — לֹא דָּנוֹ וְלֹא מְעִידוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who is suspect with regard to the Sabbatical** Year **is not suspect with regard to tithes;** and likewise, **one who is suspect with regard to tithes is not suspect with regard to the Sabbatical** Year. **One who is suspect with regard to this,** the Sabbatical Year, **or with regard to that,** tithes, is **suspect with regard to** selling ritually impure foods as though they were **ritually pure** items. **But there are those who are suspect with regard to ritually pure** items **who are not suspect with regard to this,** the Sabbatical Year, **nor with regard to that,** tithes. **This is the principle** with regard to these matters: Anyone **who is suspect with regard to** a specific **matter** may **neither adjudicate** cases **nor testify** in cases involving that matter.

The governing principle stated in the *mishna* is this: *hachashud al davar echad eino chashud al davar acher* — being suspect for one thing does not make one suspect for everything. The *mishna* also includes the important procedural rule *hachashud al davar lo dano velo meido* — a person who is suspected of a matter may not serve as a judge or witness regarding that matter, tying back to earlier discussions of judicial qualification. But the essential *zeh haklal* is the principle that suspicion is categorical, not universal.

## Transfer of Suspicion

### Shvi'is/Maasrot

The *mishna* states: *hachashud al hashvi'is eino chashud al hama'asros*. Someone suspected of dealing improperly with *shvi'is* produce — trading in it in violation of sabbatical year law — is not thereby suspected of misrepresenting *ma'aser*. The *ma'aser* in question, as *Tosafot* notes and as the *Gemara* confirms by context, is *ma'aser sheni*, which must be consumed within the walls of Jerusalem. Selling it as ordinary *chulin* would lead a purchaser to violate its restrictions.

### Reverse Rule

The reverse equally holds: *hachashud al hama'aser eino chashud al hashvi'is*. Suspicion for misrepresenting *ma'aser sheni* does not translate into suspicion for *shvi'is* violations. The two categories, despite their shared agricultural character, are treated as independently assessed.

### Both Suspect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחָשׁוּד עַל זֶה וְעַל זֶה — חָשׁוּד עַל הַטְּהָרוֹת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁהוּא חָשׁוּד עַל הַטְּהָרוֹת — וְאֵינוֹ חָשׁוּד לֹא עַל זֶה וְלֹא עַל זֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: הֶחָשׁוּד עַל דָּבָר — לֹא דָּנוֹ וְלֹא מְעִידוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who is suspect with regard to this,** the Sabbatical Year, **or with regard to that,** tithes, is **suspect with regard to** selling ritually impure foods as though they were **ritually pure** items. **But there are those who are suspect with regard to ritually pure** items **who are not suspect with regard to this,** the Sabbatical Year, **nor with regard to that,** tithes.

*Hachashud al zeh v'al zeh harei zeh chashud al hataharos*: if someone is suspect for either of these — that is, for *shvi'is* or for *ma'aser* — then he is suspect on *taharos* as well. *Taharos* here refers to the standard of eating *chulin b'taharah*, the scrupulous purity observance associated with the institution of *chaveirus*. This is a considerably higher level of practice. The logic is: *keivan d'chashid ad'oraisa kol shekein ad'rabbanan* — if someone is suspect for a *d'oraisa* violation, certainly he is suspect for a *d'rabbanan* one. These categories are treated as psychologically continuous, and violation of the heavier concern implies likely violation of the lighter.

### Taharot Exception

Yet the *mishna* adds: *v'yeish shehu chashud al hataharos v'eino chashud al zeh v'al zeh* — there are cases in which someone is suspect regarding *taharos* but not regarding *shvi'is* and *ma'aser*. The reasoning is the reverse: *nehi d'adrabbanan chashid ad'oraisa lo chashid* — one may be lax about a *d'rabbanan* obligation without extending that laxity to a *d'oraisa*. Suspicion moves more readily from stricter to more lenient than in the other direction.

## Psychological Basis

### Gemara Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? שְׁבִיעִית לָא בָּעֲיָא חוֹמָה, מַעֲשֵׂר — כֵּיוָן דְּבָעֵי חוֹמָה, חֲמִיר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one who is suspect with regard to the Sabbatical Year is not suspect with regard to tithes. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** One is **not required** to eat produce of the **Sabbatical** Year within the **wall** of Jerusalem, and therefore he treats it lightly. As for second **tithe, since** one is **required** to eat it only within the **wall** of Jerusalem, it is possible that it seems more **stringent for him,** and therefore he is not suspect, despite the suspicions with regard to produce of the **Sabbatical** Year.

The *Gemara* asks *mai ta'ama* — what is the underlying rationale for these distinctions? Why does suspicion for *shvi'is* not translate directly into suspicion for *ma'aser* and vice versa? After all, they are both agricultural *mitzvos*; why would someone careful about one necessarily be careful about the other, or careless about one and careful about the other?

### Pidyon Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. מַאי טַעְמָא? מַעֲשֵׂר אִית לֵיהּ פִּדְיוֹן, שְׁבִיעִית — כֵּיוָן דְּקָא מִיתַּסְרָא לֵיהּ וְלֵית לֵיהּ פִּדְיוֹן — חֲמִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **One who is suspect with regard to tithe** is not suspect with regard to the Sabbatical Year. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** The Gemara answers: Since **there is redemption** for **tithes,** one might treat them lightly. As for produce of the **Sabbatical** Year, **since it is** always **prohibited to him and there is no redemption** of it, it is possible that it seems more **stringent for him,** and therefore he is not suspect.

The *Gemara*'s answer is revealing. *Shvi'is lo baei chomah* — *shvi'is* produce has no mechanism of redemption; once it has taken on sabbatical holiness it cannot be redeemed, and it is forbidden in certain uses permanently. *Ma'aser sheni*, however, *keivan dbaei chomah* — since it must be brought within the walls of Jerusalem — is treated in the mind of the owner with greater sense of gravity and sanctity. But equally, because *ma'aser sheni* has *pidyon* — it can be redeemed with money, and the redeemed coin is then brought to Jerusalem in its place — a person can rationalize that the prohibition is less absolute. *Shvi'is*, without *pidyon*, seems in that person's mind to admit no such escape route, and is thus treated with greater severity.

### Compartmentalization

The *Gemara* is not engaging in objective halachic ranking here. It is making a psychological observation about how people actually think about religious obligation, and it is that psychological reality — not formal halachic equivalence — that drives the rules of *chashud*. People compartmentalize their religious lives, assigning different levels of subjective gravity to different prohibitions in ways that may not track their actual halachic weight. The *Gemara* acknowledges this reality without endorsing it.

### Modern Examples

This has very direct practical implications. The sad but familiar phenomenon of a person who is scrupulous about the ritual dimensions of *kashrus* but entirely lax about *bein adam l'chaveiro* — financial dishonesty, unfair business practice — is precisely what the *Gemara* is describing. Such a person has rationalized that what is "merely ethical" is not really religious, even though violations of *bein adam l'chaveiro* are full *mitzvos*. And in the other direction: if someone is known to have been unfaithful to a spouse, or to have engaged in financial fraud, does that mean one cannot trust their *kashrus*? The *Gemara*'s answer, counterintuitive as it may feel, is that in general, one category does not transfer to another. People compartmentalize, and the compartmentalization is real enough to be halachically significant.

The question becomes more complex when the same halachic category is divided along other lines — for instance, someone who eats non-*kosher* outside the home but claims to maintain *kashrus* inside. This is a genuine puzzle, since the categories as the *Gemara* defines them are predominantly psychological units, not strictly formal ones. The halachic answer would likely follow the psychological reality: if there is a well-defined and stable phenomenon in which people are genuinely more stringent at home regardless of their outside practice, and that phenomenon does not bleed from one context into the other, then *chashud* established for outside practice would not necessarily extend to the home. But if observation suggests the full range of behavior in the home correlates with outside practice, then the concern would carry over. The *Gemara*'s framework is not a fixed formal grid; it tracks what people actually do.

Alongside this is the question of individual knowledge. These rules establish the presumptions that govern how we treat people in the absence of specific information. But if one has direct personal knowledge of a particular individual — if one has observed how carefully they behave in private — that specific evidence can override the general presumption. This is a meaningful distinction: the rules of *chashud* tell us what to assume about a stranger in a given category; they do not prevent individual evidence from displacing that assumption when such evidence is available.

Two further principles operate within this framework. First, *chashud al davar kal eino chashud al davar chamur* — suspicion regarding a lenient matter does not establish suspicion regarding a stringent one. Second, even within a single category, the question of how psychologically unified that category is in the person's own mind remains relevant. Whether violating second-day *yom tov* makes one suspect for first-day *yom tov*, or whether *yom tov* violations imply *Shabbos* violations, depends on whether people in fact treat these as one unit in practice. The *kal v'chomer* logic that might formally suggest suspicion transfers from lenient to stringent does not automatically apply — sometimes it does, and sometimes the psychological distance between them is large enough to defeat it.

The *Gemara*'s acknowledgment of this reality is not without tension. One might object that the framework seems to condone compartmentalized thinking. But if we were to take the opposite approach — treating every known violation as generating universal suspicion — we would, at a practical level, be unable to eat in a very large proportion of homes. Halacha must contend with the world as it is, and *Chazal*'s framework for *chashud* represents a principled effort to do exactly that.

## Individual Evidence

### Baraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ חָשׁוּד עַל הַטְּהָרוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? נְהִי אַדְּרַבָּנַן חֲשִׁיד, אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא חֲשִׁיד. וּרְמִינְהִי: נֶאֱמָן עַל הַטְּהָרוֹת — נֶאֱמָן עַל שְׁבִיעִית, הָא חֲשִׁיד — חֲשִׁיד!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **But there are those who are suspect with regard to ritually pure** items who are not suspect with regard to this, the Sabbatical Year, or with regard to that, tithes. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? Granted,** he is **suspect** with regard to prohibitions that are **by rabbinic law,** but **he is not suspect** with regard to prohibitions that are **by Torah law. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: If one is **trusted with regard to ritually pure** items he is **trusted with regard to** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year. It can be inferred from here that if he is **suspect** with regard to ritually pure items he is likewise **suspect** with regard to produce of the Sabbatical Year.

A *baraita* appears to complicate the picture. *Ne'eman al hataharos ne'eman al hashvi'is; hachashud chashud* — if one is trusted on *taharos*, one is trusted on *shvi'is*; and if one is suspect, one is suspect. This seems to contradict the *mishna*'s position that suspicion on *taharos* (a *d'rabbanan*) does not translate into suspicion on *shvi'is* (a *d'oraisa*).

### Home Observation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעַי: מַתְנִיתִין כְּשֶׁרְאִינוּהוּ שֶׁנּוֹהֵג בְּצִינְעָא בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ilai says: The mishna** is referring to a case **where they saw him practicing** stringencies with regard to produce of the Sabbatical Year and tithes **privately, within his home.**

Rabbi Ila'i resolves the contradiction. The *mishna*'s phrasing is carefully chosen: *v'yeish shehu chashud* — "there are cases" of someone suspect on *taharos* who is not suspect on *shvi'is*. This is not a blanket rule but a contingent one. Generally, to be sure, suspicion on *taharos* and suspicion on *shvi'is* do go together in the *baraita*'s formulation. But there are specific cases where they can be distinguished — and this is possible precisely when individual evidence overrides the general presumption. If one has looked into this person's home and observed that he is careful about *shvi'is* and *ma'aser* in private — not for appearances, since we assume he does not suspect he is being watched — then even if he is suspect on *taharos*, we do not extend that suspicion to *shvi'is* and *ma'aser*.

### Rabbi Ila'i Answer

This is a nuanced middle category. The general rule is that suspicion in one area implies suspicion in a related area. But because it is *theoretically possible* that some people distinguish in their minds between *taharos* on one side and *shvi'is/ma'aser* on the other, the presumption is not absolute. When concrete private evidence demonstrates that this particular individual genuinely does maintain that distinction, we honor that evidence and do not translate suspicion from one category to the other. This is not simply about one instance of observed care overriding a known pattern of violation; it is about the fact that the very structure of the rule — grounded in psychology, not formal law — admits of exceptions when the psychology of the individual in question can actually be verified.

## Stringency Debate

### R. Yannai View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַנַּאי בְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דַּהֲוָה חֲשִׁיד לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ, וַאֲתָא קַמֵּי רַבָּנַן וְקַבֵּיל אַתַּרְוַיְיהוּ, וַהֲדַר אִיחֲשַׁד אַחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ, דְּאָמְרִינַן: מִיגּוֹ דַּחֲשִׁיד אַהָא — חֲשִׁיד נָמֵי אַאִידַּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai, son of Rabbi Yishmael, said** that there is an alternative answer: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where one was suspect with regard to both** ritually pure items and produce of the Sabbatical Year, **and he came before the Sages and accepted** upon himself **that** he would observe **both of them, and afterward he was suspected with regard to one of them.** The reason for the *halakha* here is **that we say: Since he is suspect with regard to this** one of them, **he is also suspect with regard to the other** one.

Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Ishmael offer an alternative resolution to the same *baraita*. They maintain that the *mishna*'s straightforward reading is correct: if one is suspect on *taharos* (*d'rabbanan*), one is simply not suspect on *shvi'is* (*d'oraisa*). The *kal v'chomer* argument — that if you will violate a minor prohibition you will certainly violate a more severe one — does not apply here, because the categories remain psychologically distinct for most people. The *baraita* that treats them as continuous is therefore understood as referring to a specific situation: *k'gon de-havat chashud de-taryehu* — a case in which the person had been suspect for *both* categories. He then came before the rabbis, expressed *teshuva*, and accepted upon himself to be rigorous about both *shvi'is* and *taharos*. Subsequently, we observe backsliding in one of them. In that case, *migo de-ichshad ahai chashinan nami adach* — since there has been relapse on one matter that he had accepted as a package, we are concerned about relapse on the other as well.

### Gemara Response

This is admittedly a narrow *okimta* of the *baraita* — it does not read as the plain sense of the text. Nevertheless, it preserves the *mishna*'s general rule intact: as a matter of ordinary presumption, *taharos* and *shvi'is/ma'aser* are separate categories, and suspicion does not flow from one to the other. The *baraita*'s language of mutual implication applies only in this confined case of accepted-then-violated *teshuva*, where both categories had already been treated as one unit by the person himself through his declared acceptance, and backsliding in one therefore reopens suspicion across both.

## Stam Mishna

### R. Akiva Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סְתִימְתָּאָה, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית — חָשׁוּד עַל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: One who is suspect with regard to the Sabbatical Year is not suspect with regard to tithes. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the statement of Rabbi Akiva, the unattributed.** Most unattributed statements of *tanna'im* were formulated by Rabbi Akiva's students and reflect his opinions. **But the Rabbis say:** One who is **suspect with regard to** produce of **the Sabbatical** Year **is suspect with regard to tithe.**

Rabbi Abba bar Bar Hana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *zu divrei Rabbi Akiva stima'ah* — this *mishna* is a *stam mishna* authored by Rabbi Akiva. Rashi explains this simply on the grounds that a great many *stam mishnayos* follow Rabbi Akiva, mediated through Rabbi Meir who edited them *aliba d'Rabbi Akiva*.

### Sages' View

However, *aval chachamim omrim* — the Sages disagree: *chashud al hashvi'is chashud al hama'aser*. The suspicion does transfer from *shvi'is* to *ma'aser*. The question is whether this represents a dispute only about one particular application of the general principle, or a deeper disagreement about the principle itself.

### Rabbi Yehuda ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן חֲכָמִים? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּבְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שְׁבִיעִית חֲמִירָא לְהוּ, דְּהָהוּא דַּהֲוָה קָרֵי לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ ״דַּיָּירָא בַּר דַּיָּירְתָּא״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תֵּיתֵי לִי דְּלָא אֲכַלִי פֵּירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית כְּוָותָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** are the Sages referred to here as **the Rabbis?** It is **Rabbi Yehuda, as in Rabbi Yehuda's locale they** considered produce of the **Sabbatical** Year an especially **stringent** prohibition. Therefore, if a resident of that place was suspect with regard to the Sabbatical Year, he was certainly not trusted with regard to tithes. **As** in that locale, if **a certain** person **would call another: A convert, son of** a female **convert,** wishing to insult him, that person would **say to him** in reply: **I have** a blessing **coming to me, as I do not eat produce of the Sabbatical** Year **like you** do. This indicates that the accusation of eating produce of the Sabbatical Year was a great insult, since they treated the Sabbatical Year stringently.

*Man chachamim* — who are these "Sages"? The *Gemara* identifies them as Rabbi Yehuda. The rationale: *d'v'atria d'Rabbi Yehuda shvi'is chamira le-hu* — in the locale of Rabbi Yehuda, *shvi'is* was treated with extraordinary severity. In that community's consciousness, violation of *shvi'is* was considered so grave that it was reasonable to conclude that anyone willing to transgress it would certainly be suspect for other agricultural *mitzvos* as well. This is thus not a general dispute about the principle that categories remain separate; it is a dispute about one particular application driven by local social reality.

### Insult Proof

The *Gemara* cites evidence that *shvi'is* was indeed considered especially weighty in Rabbi Yehuda's place: *de-hahu de-havat kari lei le-chavrei dayara bar dayara* — there was a case in which someone called his adversary "a convert the son of a convert" as an insult, which raises its own serious questions given the *mitzvah* of *v'ahavtem et hager*. The adversary responded: *taysi li d'lo achli de'shviis kevaseich* — "at least it is to my credit that I do not eat *shemittah* produce the way you do." The fact that eating *shemittah* improperly was used as an insult on par with calling someone a convert illustrates how extraordinarily grave the sin was considered in that community. Given that weight, one who would transgress *shvi'is* in Rabbi Yehuda's locale would indeed be considered suspect across related categories.

## Rabbi Meir Extreme

### R. Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סְתִימְתָּאָה, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חָשׁוּד עַל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר חָשׁוּד עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית. וּמַאן חֲכָמִים? רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: חָשׁוּד לְדָבָר אֶחָד הָוֵי חָשׁוּד לְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the statement of Rabbi Akiva, the unattributed. But the Rabbis say:** One who is **suspect with regard to tithe is suspect with regard to** produce of the **Sabbatical** Year. **And who** are the Sages referred to here as **the Rabbis? It is Rabbi Meir, who says:** One who is **suspect with regard to one matter is suspect with regard to the entire Torah.**

There is an alternative tradition: *ika d'amri* — others say that the *Sages* who dispute the *stam mishna* of Rabbi Akiva are actually Rabbi Meir, not Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir is identified as the authority who holds: *chashud l'davar echad havi chashud l'chol hatorah kullah* — if someone is suspect for one matter, he is suspect for the entirety of *Torah*. This is not merely a dispute about which agricultural categories imply one another; it is a fundamental position about the nature of *chashud*.

A *baraita* records this explicitly: *am ha'aretz shekibel alav divrei chaveirus* — if an *am ha'aretz* accepted upon himself the practices of *chaveirus* — the fellowship of scrupulous purity observance — *v'nechshad l'davar echad, nechshad l'chol hatorah kullah, divrei Rabbi Meir* — and was then suspected for one thing, he is suspect for the entirety of *Torah*. This is Rabbi Meir's view. *V'chachamim omrim eino nechshad ella l'oso davar bilvad* — the Sages say he is only suspect for that particular matter.

### All-or-Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: עַם הָאָרֶץ שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת, וְנֶחְשַׁד לְדָבָר אֶחָד — נֶחְשַׁד לְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נֶחְשָׁד אֶלָּא לְאוֹתוֹ דָּבָר בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Demai* 2:4): With regard to **an *am ha'aretz*,** i.e., one who is unreliable with regard to ritual impurity and tithes, **who accepts upon himself** the commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status,** i.e., that he will be stringent in all matters observed by *ḥaverim*, including *teruma*, tithes, and *ḥalla*, and also undertake to eat only food that is ritually pure, and the Sages accepted him as trustworthy **but** subsequently he **was suspected with regard to one matter** in which others saw him act improperly, **he is suspected with regard to the entire Torah.** This is the **statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: He is suspected only with regard to that particular matter.**

The implications of Rabbi Meir's position are striking. If anyone willing to violate one *d'oraisa* prohibition has thereby demonstrated that they do not fully accept the system, and must therefore be suspected of violating anything, the societal consequences would be severe. It is precisely the Sages' countervailing position — that suspicion is specific, not universal — that allows communal life to function by acknowledging the psychological reality of compartmentalized observance. The *Gemara*'s overall framework follows the Sages, not Rabbi Meir.

## Chaveirim Fellowship

### What is Chaveirim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגֵּר שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֲפִילּוּ נֶחְשַׁד לְדָבָר אֶחָד — הָוֵי חָשׁוּד לְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, וַהֲרֵי הוּא כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד. נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ, דְּאִי קַדֵּישׁ — קִידּוּשָׁיו קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** It is also taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Demai* 2:4): With regard to **a convert who accepted upon himself** upon his conversion **matters of Torah,** i.e., all of the mitzvot, **even if he is suspect with regard to one matter** alone, **he is suspect with regard to the entire Torah, and he is** considered **like a Jewish transgressor [*meshummad*],** who habitually transgresses the mitzvot. The Gemara explains that the practical **difference** resulting from the fact that he is considered like a Jewish transgressor is **that if he betroths** a woman, **his betrothal is** a valid **betrothal,** and they are married. Although he is suspect with regard to the entire Torah, he does not return to his prior gentile status.

The institution of *chaveirus* — often translated as "fellowship" — refers to a defined community of people scrupulous in matters of *teruma u'ma'aser* and *taharos*, whose defining characteristic was eating their *chulin b'taharah*. This is not simply a synonym for the class of *talmidei chachamim*, though the two communities substantially overlapped. Conceptually, *chaveirus* is a purity-centered eating fellowship, and the emphasis on who eats with whom, and under what conditions of *taharah*, was treated with great seriousness in the period of the Second Temple and continued afterward. The question of whom one can trust regarding the handling of food — whether it has been kept *b'taharah*, whether *teruma u'ma'asros* have been properly separated — defines the social and religious boundaries of this community.

### Chaveirim Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת, חוּץ מִדָּבָר אֶחָד — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ. גּוֹי שֶׁבָּא לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, חוּץ מִדָּבָר אֶחָד — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ דִּקְדּוּק אֶחָד מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who comes to accept upon himself** the commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status except for one matter,** which he does not wish to observe, **he is not accepted,** and he is not trustworthy even with regard to those matters that he does wish to accept upon himself. Likewise, in the case of **a gentile who comes to** convert and takes upon himself to **accept the words of Torah except for one matter, he is not accepted** as a convert. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: Even** if he refuses to accept **one detail of rabbinic law,** he is not accepted.

The *baraita* now addresses the process by which an *am ha'aretz* may be accepted into this fellowship. The rule is clear: if he accepted upon himself the obligations of *chaveirus* but said *chutz midavar echad* — there is one thing I am unwilling to accept — *ein mekablin oso* — he is not accepted. Membership in the fellowship is all-or-nothing. One cannot be a *chaver* on the condition that certain obligations are waived.

Several additional nuances follow from later material in this *sugya*. One concerns the *Levites* and *kohanim*. Just as the *am ha'aretz* must accept all conditions to be received into the fellowship, so too a *levi* wishing to be accepted as a practicing *levi* for Temple service, or a *kohen* wishing to take his place among the functioning *kohanim* in the *Beis HaMikdash*, cannot refuse even one element. *Ein mekablin oto* — he is not accepted. The source given: *hamakriv es dam hashalmim lo yiheyeh chok olam l'maneh* — the one who offers the blood of the *shelamim*, it is his eternal statute. *Avodah mesura livnei Aharon* — Temple service is entrusted to the sons of Aaron. *Kol kohen she'eino modeh bah ein lo chelek bakehuna* — any *kohen* who does not acknowledge and accept this in its entirety has no *chelek* in the *kehuna*. The term *chelek* here specifically refers to his entitlement to a portion of the *korbanos* and *matnos kehuna* when these are divided; the question of whether he is thereby excluded from the service itself, or only from the apportionment of benefits, is a separate matter.

### Ger Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בֶּן לֵוִי שֶׁבָּא לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי לְוִיָּה, וְכֹהֵן שֶׁבָּא לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי כְהוּנָּה, חוּץ מִדָּבָר אֶחָד — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַמַּקְרִיב אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים וְגוֹ׳״, הָעֲבוֹדָה הַמְּסוּרָה לִבְנֵי אַהֲרֹן, כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹדֶה בָּהּ — אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בַּכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And similarly,** in the case of **a Levite who comes to accept the matters of a Levite, or a priest who comes to accept the matters of priesthood, except for one matter, he is not accepted. As it is stated:** "He among the sons of Aaron, **that sacrifices the blood of the peace offerings,** and the fat, shall have the right thigh for a portion" (Leviticus 7:33). This means that with regard to **the** Temple **service, which is handed** over **to the sons of Aaron, any priest who does not admit to it** in its entirety **has no share in the priesthood.**

The same logic is now applied to the prospective convert. *O oved kochavim sheba lekabbel divrei Torah chutz midavar echad, ein mekablin oso* — a non-Jew who comes to accept the words of *Torah* but says there is one thing he will not accept, is not accepted. Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda extends this even further: *afilu dikduk echad midivrei sofrim* — even refusing one refinement of rabbinic law is sufficient to preclude acceptance.

## Kabbalat Mitzvot

### Conversion Validity

This *baraita* immediately raises the question of the scope of *kabbalas mitzvos* in conversion. One could read it as establishing that unless a prospective convert is prepared to observe every *mitzvah* in full, the conversion is simply invalid — not merely inadvisable. However, the more careful reading, endorsed by most *Rishonim*, is that this *baraita* addresses the *lechatchila* standard — the question of whether the *beis din* should proceed with the conversion — rather than defining the criteria for a *bedieved* valid act of *geirus*. The *sugya* governing what actually constitutes a valid act of *kabbalas mitzvos* is in Yevamos, and these two discussions must not be conflated.

### Rav Moshe View

Rav Moshe Feinstein addresses this in a *teshuvah*. While he frames it as a possibility rather than a definitive ruling, the weight of the *Rishonim* strongly supports reading *mekablin oso* as a *lechatchila* judgment about whether to proceed, not as a definition of what makes the act of conversion *chal*. It is the question of whether it is appropriate for the *beis din* to accept this person, not whether, if they did, the conversion would be halachically binding.

### Avnei Nezer

The Avnei Nezer draws an important distinction that illuminates the issue: there is a difference between *ol mitzvos* — accepting the yoke of the commandments, acknowledging that one is obligated — and *kiyom mitzvos* — actually performing them. One can genuinely accept *ol mitzvos* while not yet being at the stage of full *kiyum*. No one can reasonably learn everything before conversion; the *Gemara* itself describes the pre-conversion instruction as teaching *miktzas mitzvos kalos u'miktzas mitzvos chamoros* — a sampling of lighter and more serious commandments — which already implies that the convert will not have mastered the full corpus. The meaningful question, then, is whether the convert genuinely accepts the obligation, even if full practice has not yet been achieved — as opposed to someone who says explicitly, "I understand what I am supposed to do, but I am not yet ready to do it." Whether the latter can constitute *kabbalas mitzvos* is itself a significant question.

### Modern Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגֵּר שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֲפִילּוּ נֶחְשַׁד לְדָבָר אֶחָד — הָוֵי חָשׁוּד לְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, וַהֲרֵי הוּא כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד. נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ, דְּאִי קַדֵּישׁ — קִידּוּשָׁיו קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** It is also taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Demai* 2:4): With regard to **a convert who accepted upon himself** upon his conversion **matters of Torah,** i.e., all of the mitzvot, **even if he is suspect with regard to one matter** alone, **he is suspect with regard to the entire Torah, and he is** considered **like a Jewish transgressor [*meshummad*],** who habitually transgresses the mitzvot. The Gemara explains that the practical **difference** resulting from the fact that he is considered like a Jewish transgressor is **that if he betroths** a woman, **his betrothal is** a valid **betrothal,** and they are married. Although he is suspect with regard to the entire Torah, he does not return to his prior gentile status.

These issues are not merely theoretical. The controversy in Israel over the retroactive invalidation of conversions — where a *beis din* went back and examined a woman's post-conversion practice, found it wanting, and concluded she had never been halachically Jewish — is directly addressed by this *Gemara*. Those who advocated for such invalidation were not only establishing an extremely high *kabbalas mitzvos* standard; they were doing something still more troubling: using behavior *after* conversion as evidence that the mental state *at the moment of conversion* had been deficient.

### Ger Status

The *Gemara* is unequivocal on this point. A *ger* could emerge from the *mikvah* and immediately worship *avodah zarah* — and he is still a Jew. Backsliding after conversion does not retroactively invalidate the act. The question of *kabbalas mitzvos* pertains solely to the moment of conversion itself. To go back years later, examine subsequent observance, and use it to unwind the original act is precisely what this *Gemara* forecloses. The *Gemara* in Bekhorot in fact extends this principle in an interesting direction: it considers the case of a *ger shekibel alav divrei Torah v'nechshad l'davar echad* — a convert who accepted *Torah* and was subsequently suspected for some violation. One reading of the text is that he is then suspect for everything; but the *Shittah Mekubetzet* presents an alternate *girsa* that changes the meaning entirely: *u'v'ger shekibel alav divrei Torah v'nechshad l'davar echad va'afilu chashud l'chol hatorah kullah harei hu k'Yisrael l'chol devarav* — whether a convert is suspect for one thing or for everything, even if he violates every single *mitzvah* in the *Torah*, he remains a Jew in every respect. *Nafka mina*: *dekiddesh kiddushav kiddushin* — if he were to contract *kiddushin*, the act is fully valid. He may not be trusted about *kashrus*, *Shabbos*, and so on — but his Jewish status is not in question.

The alternate *girsa* is strongly preferable, both because it fits the logical structure better and because the reading that a convert is treated with *greater* suspicion than a born Jew — that a single violation puts all of his religious trustworthiness at stake in a way it would not for others — raises serious difficulties given the *mitzvah* of *v'ahavtem et hager*. There is a strand in *Chazal* that is suspicious of converts backsliding, and that may indeed be reflected in the plain reading of some versions of this text; but the *Shittah Mekubetzet*'s reading, which makes the point about irrevocability of Jewish status, is the more coherent and more compelling one.

### Hillel Story

The *Gemara* about Hillel and the prospective convert who came demanding to be taught *Torah shebichtav* but not *Torah sheba'al peh* — or who said, teach me the entire *Torah* while I stand on one foot, and then make me *Kohen Gadol* — is a direct counterpoint to this *baraita*. Shamai turned him away, which seems consistent with the rule of *chutz midavar echad*. Hillel accepted him, he became extraordinarily *frum*, and the *Gemara* celebrates the outcome. How do we reconcile this with the apparent rule that one who refuses even one element cannot be accepted?

*Tosafot* explains: Hillel was *somech al chochmoso* — he relied on his own judgment that despite the person's stated position, he could be brought to full observance. This is not a contradiction of the rule; it is the exercise of judicial discretion. The rule of *chutz midavar echad* is not an absolute prohibition on accepting such a person — it is the default standard. The *beis din* retains the authority to assess whether, in a particular case, acceptance is nevertheless the right decision, as Hillel concluded it was. This reading places the rule in its proper register: it governs ordinary cases and establishes the presumption; it does not eliminate the court's discretion in exceptional circumstances. The *baraita* and the Hillel story are complementary, not contradictory.

## Gradual Acceptance

### Home Observation Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא לְקַבֵּל דִּבְרֵי חֲבֵירוּת, אִם רְאִינוּהוּ שֶׁנּוֹהֵג בְּצִינְעָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ — מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְלַמְּדִין אוֹתוֹ. וְאִם לָאו — מְלַמְּדִין אוֹתוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ, וְהוּא לָמֵד כְּדַרְכּוֹ וְהוֹלֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who comes to accept upon himself** a commitment to observe **the matters** associated with ***ḥaver* status, if we have seen that he practices** such matters **in private, within his home, he is accepted, and afterward he is taught** the precise details of being a *ḥaver*. **But if** we have **not** seen him act as a *ḥaver* in his home, **he is taught** first **and afterward accepted. Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: Whether** in **this** case **or that** case, **he is** first **accepted, and he** then **continues to learn in** the **usual manner,** i.e., as a *ḥaver* he learns from others how to behave.

The *baraita* describes the process of accepting a prospective *chaver* with particular sensitivity to what can actually be known about the person. *She'reinuhu she'noheg b'tzina betoch beito* — if we have observed him conducting himself carefully in private, within his own home, then *mekablin oto v'achar kach melamdin oto* — you accept him immediately and then teach him going forward. The reasoning is clear: behavior observed in private carries a high degree of reliability. A person scrupulous about *purity* practice at home when no one is watching is unlikely to be performing for appearances; the behavior reflects his genuine values. In such a case, one can accept him as a *chaver* immediately, trusting that instruction will bring him to full compliance with the fellowship's standards.

### Teach First

If, however, no such private observation has been made, the process is reversed: *melamdin oto v'achar kach mekablin oto* — first you teach, then after demonstrated adherence you accept. The person must first show that he has internalized the obligations and can live by them before being granted the trust that membership in the fellowship implies.

### R. Shimon View

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai takes the most generous position: *bein kach u'vein kach mekablin oto, v'holech k'darko v'holech* — in either case, accept the person, and he will proceed and grow in his own way. This is a notably expansive and gracious view, especially striking from the same Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai who was famously intolerant while in the cave. Perhaps this teaching reflects the perspective he reached after emerging from it.

## Ladder Observance

### Hand-washing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְקַבְּלִין לִכְנָפַיִם, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְקַבְּלִין לִטְהָרוֹת, וְאִם אָמַר: ״אֵינִי מְקַבֵּל אֶלָּא לִכְנָפַיִם״ — מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ. קִיבֵּל לַטְּהָרוֹת וְלֹא קִיבֵּל לַכְּנָפַיִם — אַף לַטְּהָרוֹת לֹא קִיבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: An *am ha'aretz* who wishes to become a *ḥaver* **is accepted** first **with regard to hands,** i.e., he is presumed to be stringent concerning the ritual purity of his hands by making sure to wash his hands before handling pure items, **and afterward he is accepted** as trustworthy **for purity** in general. **And if he says: I** wish to **accept** purity **only with regard to hands, he is accepted** for this. If he wishes to **accept** upon himself the stringencies of a *ḥaver* **with regard to ritual purity but he does not accept** upon himself the stringencies **with regard to hands,** i.e., to wash his hands, which is a simple act, **he is not accepted even for purity** in general.

*Mekablin lichnafayim* — one can be accepted into a partial form of the fellowship specifically for the practice of hand-washing before eating. The term *lichnafayim* is unusual — it may reflect a connection to *kapayim* (hands) — and refers to the scrupulous washing of hands before eating *chulin*. This is a lower rung of *taharah* practice, rabbinic in character.

### Progression Path

Once accepted at this level, one can subsequently progress and be accepted at the full level of *taharos* observance — eating *chulin b'taharah* in the complete sense. The ladder moves upward: first hand-washing, then full *taharos*.

### Ladder Logic

The one-way structure of this ladder is important. If someone wishes to be accepted only at the hand-washing level and explicitly says *eini mekabel ela lichnafayim* — I accept only that and not the full *taharos* obligations — *mekablin oso*: you accept him at that level, and that partial membership is genuine and coherent. The categories are separable; being trusted on hand-washing is not meaningless simply because one has not accepted full *taharos* obligations.

But the reverse does not work. *Kibel l'taharos velo kibel lichnafayim* — if someone says he will observe the full *taharos* obligations but not the hand-washing level, *af l'taharos lo kibel* — his claimed acceptance of *taharos* is not credited. The lower rung is constitutive of the higher; one cannot meaningfully claim the upper level while refusing the lower. You can stop at any step going up, but you cannot reach the higher step without having secured the lower one.

## Probation Periods

### Duration Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עַד כַּמָּה מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתוֹ? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לְמַשְׁקִין — שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, לִכְסוּת — שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Until when is he accepted,** i.e., how much time must elapse before he is considered trustworthy as a *ḥaver*? **Beit Shammai say: With regard to liquids, thirty days. With regard to** impurity of **clothing,** about which *ḥaverim* would be careful as well, **twelve months. And Beit Hillel say: Both** with regard to **this,** liquids, **and that,** clothing, he must maintain the practice **for twelve months** before he is fully accepted as a *ḥaver*.

Having established the framework for acceptance, the *baraita* raises a further question: *ad matai mekablin oto* — how long must someone be observed before they are considered fully accepted and trusted, moving out of any probationary status? Implicit in this question is that acceptance into the fellowship involves a period during which the new member is under observation, and only after demonstrating sustained adherence do they receive the full trust that membership implies with respect to *terumot u'ma'asrot* and *taharos*.

### Beit Shammai

*Beit Shammai* rule: *lemashkin shloshim yom* — for liquids, a thirty-day probationary period. Liquids are particularly susceptible to *tumah* in rabbinic law, and careful handling of them is an important marker of *taharos* observance. For garments — whose *tumah*, as the garments of *amei ha'aretz* resembling those of a *zav*, a *zavah*, or a *niddah*, is less severe — *Beit Shammai* require twelve months of observation before full trust is extended.

### Beit Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, הָוֵה לֵיהּ מִקּוּלֵּי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּמֵחוּמְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל, אֶלָּא בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה לִשְׁלשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, this is** one **of** the rare cases of **the leniencies of Beit Shammai and of the stringencies of Beit Hillel,** and yet it is not included in tractate *Eduyyot*, which lists all of the cases where Beit Shammai are more lenient than Beit Hillel. **Rather,** the text of the *baraita* must be emended so that it reads: **Beit Hillel say: Both** with regard to **this,** liquids **and that,** clothing, he must maintain the practice **for thirty** days before he is fully accepted as a *ḥaver*.

*Beit Hillel* rule: *echad bezeh v'echad bezeh shneim asar chodesh* — both liquids and garments require twelve months. The *Gemara* notes that this is one of the rare cases where *Beit Hillel* appears more stringent than *Beit Shammai*, and asks why it is not listed among the known stringencies of *Beit Hillel*. The answer revises the reading: *Beit Hillel* actually holds *echad bezeh v'echad bezeh shloshim yom* — thirty days for both. We will continue with the discussion of these probationary standards in tomorrow's *daf*.
