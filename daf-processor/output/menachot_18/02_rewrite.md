# Menachot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Levona & Kometz Order

### Levona Placement

We begin at the bottom of 17b, but before turning to the text, it is worth resolving a disagreement that had emerged in the course of the previous sessions. The question concerned the mechanics of how the *levona* was brought in the preparation of the *mincha*. One understanding — based on a book that had been consulted — was that the *levona* was placed together with the *kometz* into the *kli* and then everything was brought to the *mizbeach* together. The other understanding, consistent with the way the *gemara* has been reading throughout the preceding dapim, is that the *kometz* is first taken into the *kli*, and only afterwards is the *levona* gathered — that the two are performed as separate acts. This is borne out by the *gemara*'s extended discussion of *pigul*, which throughout assumes that the *kometz* and *levona* are handled separately, and even considers cases where one might be done before the other, or where the order is reversed.

### Rambam vs Rashi

Upon investigation, it becomes clear that the first understanding reflects the position of the Rambam. The Rambam does hold that the *levona* and *kometz* are placed together in the *kli*, and he bases this on a line found in the *gemara* in Sotah. However, the *Griz*, the *Keren Orah*, and other *Acharonim* point out that this stands directly against Rashi, who throughout our *gemara* has consistently maintained the other position — namely, that they are separate. These authorities argue that even the Rambam would have to concede that *b'dieved*, if the *levona* was placed in a separate *kli* and burned separately, the *korban* would be valid, for otherwise the many *sugyot* we have been learning cannot be reconciled with his view. His position, they conclude, is a reading of a single passage in Sotah that is in tension with the dominant thrust of our *sugya*.

There is a further distinction between the two positions. According to Rashi — and according to the simple reading of all these *gemarot* — there is no *avoda* associated with the *levona* until the moment it is burned. There is no *nesinas kli* for the *levona*, no *hilluch*; the *avoda* is the burning itself. This explains why the discussion of *mafgil b'chatzi matir* only arises at the point of the actual *haktara* — until then, the *levona* is not performing any halachic function. The Rambam, by contrast, who holds that the *levona* has a *nesinas kli* and a *hilluch* of its own, accordingly extends the concept of *chatzi matir* to those earlier stages as well — an approach that is very much against the *gemara* as we have learned it, which at most considered it at the point of *hilluch*.

### Separate vs Together

This also illuminates a practical question: according to Rashi, how does one physically manage the two separate acts? The *kometz* is taken in hand, then one walks to the *mizbeach* and burns it — but one is already holding it, so the two must necessarily be done sequentially. One could, of course, bring the *mincha* to the top of the *mizbeach* and perform the acts one after the other there. The Rambam's reading simplifies the physical process considerably, but it does so on the basis of that single line in Sotah rather than on the *sugyot* we have before us. A further point: according to Rashi, since the acts must be done separately, either two *kohanim* are involved or it requires a degree of juggling — a practical consideration that underscores the different implications of the two approaches.

## Lehanniach Status

### Tanna Kamma

The *gemara* at the bottom of 17b now sets out a three-way debate regarding the status of *lehanniach* — the case where one performs the *avoda* of the *mincha* while intending to leave over some of it. The debate that has been discussed until now concerned the cases of *achilas adam* and *achilas mizbeach* — that is, intending to eat the *shirayim* or to burn the *kometz* or *levona* outside its proper time. The *braisa* did not specify what the *halacha* would be if one intended merely to leave something over. The *gemara* now clarifies that there are three distinct positions on precisely this question.

The *tanna kamma* holds that the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* applies only to *achilas adam* and *achilas mizbeach*. As for *lehanniach* — the thought of leaving it over — this is *divrei hakol kasher*, everyone would agree that the *korban* is valid. Even Rabbi Eliezer, who rules *pasul* in those other cases on a *derabanan* level, would not be troubled by *lehanniach*, because *lehanniach* falls entirely outside the category of *pigul*. The category of *pigul* is narrowly defined: it concerns thoughts of eating or burning or *zerikat hadam*. The thought of leaving something over is a qualitatively different kind of thought, and therefore, even according to the *derabanan* position of Rabbi Eliezer, it would not generate a problem.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda takes the opposite position. He holds that the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* applies to *achilas adam* and *achilas mizbeach*, but that *lehanniach* is *divrei hakol pasul* — everyone would agree it is *pasul*, even the *rabbanan* who otherwise are more lenient. The *gemara* immediately explains the reasoning.

### Kol Damo Gezeira

The *gemara* offers a *gezeira*: *maktzas damo atu kol damo* — we apply a *gezeira* from the case of leaving over part of the blood to the case of leaving over all of the blood. The *gemara* asserts that even those *rabbanan* who maintain that a *machshava* to leave over the blood does not constitute a *d'oraisa* problem would agree that if one intended to leave over *all* of the blood, it would be *pasul d'oraisa*. The reason is that were one to actually do so — to leave over all the blood rather than performing *zerikat hadam* — the *korban* would never have had its blood duly sprinkled, and it would consequently be *pasul*.

This connects to a *mishna* in Zevachim — recorded at 36 — which discusses the case of one who slaughters a *zevach* intending to leave over its blood or *eimurim* until the next day, or to take them outside their proper boundary. There, Rabbi Yehuda rules *pasul* and the *chachamim* rule *kasher*. The logic of the *gezeira* here is that since *lehanniach kulo* — leaving over all the blood — would be *pasul d'oraisa* if actually done, the *rabbanan* who are lenient about the *machshava* in the ordinary cases would still acknowledge that a thought to leave over even *part* of the blood constitutes a *psul derabanan*, by *gezeira* from the more severe case. It is not a fully airtight proof, but this is the structure of Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning: *lehanniach*, precisely because of its potential to shade into the gravely disqualifying *lehanniach kulo*, is treated more severely than *achilas adam* or *achilas mizbeach*.

One might ask: what exactly is *lehanniach miktzas*? If one performs *zerika* in the ordinary way, there is always some blood left over that gets poured out — so what meaningful thought of *lehanniach miktzas* can one have? The answer is that *lehanniach miktzas* here means something like: one catches the blood in the *kli* and intends not to do anything with it — not to perform the *zerika* at all, or to leave some portion that should have been *nizrak* sitting untouched. Once this is clarified, it becomes clear why Rabbi Yehuda's position is internally coherent: the thought that one would not properly perform *zerika* on part of the blood is a thought that strikes at the validity of the *korban* in a way that thoughts about *achilas adam* do not.

### Rav Elazar

The third position belongs to Rav Elazar. He says that *lehanniach* falls within the same debate as all the other cases: Rabbi Eliezer rules it *pasul* and the *chachamim* rule it *kasher*. According to Rav Elazar, there are two separate questions: first, whether the debate of *dam mizbeach* — intending to sprinkle blood at the wrong altar — applies, and there the *gemara* had offered a fairly convincing argument that it could constitute real *pigul*, but since Rabbi Eliezer says only *pasul*, it must be purely *derabanan*; and second, what is the status of *lehanniach*? The *tanna kamma* says it is better — entirely outside the *derabanan* category. Rabbi Yehuda says it is worse — everyone agrees it is *pasul*. Rav Elazar places it in the same category as all the contested cases: Rabbi Eliezer renders it *pasul derabanan*, and the *chachamim* say it is *kasher*.

## Yosef HaBavli Story

### Repeated Kasher Answers

The *gemara* now introduces a *braisa* that serves a halachic purpose, but does so through a remarkably moving narrative. The *braisa* opens: *keshenachti limtzos midosav* — when I came to clarify my learning, or, in the alternate reading, to distill the essence of the teaching of Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua. Rashi explains *limtzos midosav* as *leida mitzui talmudo* — to know the distillation or essence of what he had learned, to get things clear. So the speaker — identified shortly as Rabbi, Rebbi — went to Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua for precisely this purpose.

There, he found Yosef HaBavli seated before Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, described as exceedingly dear to him — *chaviv lo beyoter ad leachat*. Rashi notes two possible readings: either Yosef was an exceptionally prized student of Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, or Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua was himself exceedingly dear to Yosef. The word *leachat* may simply mean "to an extreme," and indeed the *Megillat Setarim* of Rabbeinu Nissim understands *ad leachat* as *ad meod* — to an extraordinary degree. Rashi also offers that it could mean that their relationship held together *until* the following particular *halacha* — which is about to be described.

Yosef HaBavli asked Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua: if one slaughters a *zevach* and intends to leave over its blood until the next day, what is the *halacha*? Note that this is not a *menachot* case but the *mishna* from Zevachim — though it is being cited here by way of reference in the Tosefta of Zevachim, chapter 2, as noted in the margin of the page. Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua answered: *kasher*. That evening, Yosef asked again, and was told *kasher*. The following morning, *kasher*. At midday, *kasher*. The impression is either that they were reviewing the teaching in the manner of an oral culture, or that Yosef kept pressing — kept returning to the question.

### Rabbi Eliezer Dispute

Finally, by *mincha* time, Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua added a qualification: the ruling is *kasher*, but *ella sheRabbi Eliezer posel* — it is true that there is an opinion of Rabbi Eliezer that this is *pasul*. That caveat had been withheld through an entire day of questioning, and only now, by the afternoon, did it emerge.

At that moment, *tzahavu panav shel Yosef HaBavli* — Yosef HaBavli's face lit up. Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, seeing this, said to him: Yosef, *kimdumani shelo kivanu shemuateinu ad ata* — it seems to me that our learning has not been aligned until now. The implication is pointed: here he had been teaching this student the *halacha*, and the student was not satisfied with the ruling he gave — instead, the student lit up at the mention of the dissenting opinion. For a rabbi trying to raise his prize student to *pasken halacha* according to the accepted view, this was troubling.

### Yosef's Explanation

Yosef responded: Rabbi, I accept entirely what you rule as *halacha*. I am not trying to *pasken* otherwise. But *Rabbi Yehuda pasul shana li* — when I learned from Rabbi Yehuda, he taught me that it was *pasul*. I do not follow that ruling over yours, but I was seeking the source for Rabbi Yehuda's position. *Dechazarti al kol talmidav uvikashti mechaveirai velo matzati* — I consulted all of Rabbi Yehuda's students, I sought corroboration from my colleagues, and no one could confirm it. Rashi explains that Yosef was therefore worried that he had misremembered — that perhaps what he recalled in Rabbi Yehuda's name was inaccurate. *Achshav sheshanita li pasul, hechzarta li avedati* — now that you have told me that Rabbi Eliezer says it is *pasul*, you have returned to me my lost object. Now I know that what I remembered from Rabbi Yehuda is in fact authentic — there is indeed an opinion out there that supports what I heard.

It bears noting that Yosef HaBavli is explicitly identified in the Tannaitic literature as coming from Bavel, just as Hillel before him was called *habavli* — because the dominant *tannaim* were in Eretz Yisrael, and those who came from Bavel were labeled accordingly.

### Emotional Response

What followed was extraordinary. *Zalgu einav dimaot shel Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua* — Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua's eyes poured with tears. He said: *ashreichem talmidei chachamim* — praised are the students of the *chachamim*. Here *talmidei chachamim* is used in its narrowest sense: Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua is the *chacham*, and Yosef was his *talmid*. He said: *divrei Torah chavivin aleichem beyoter* — the words of Torah are excessively dear to you. He then recited the verse *mah ahavti Toratecha kol hayom hi sichati* — how much I love Your Torah, all day long it is my speech. The tears came because he saw that this student was not merely after the *halacha* for practical purposes; he cared about every opinion, every tradition, and was so distressed at the thought of having misremembered even a minority position that he spent tremendous effort seeking corroboration. That, Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua said with tears, is what it means to love Torah.

### Transmission Resolved

The *gemara* then resolves the chain of transmission. Rabbi Yehuda was the son of Rabbi Ilai. Rabbi Ilai was a student of Rabbi Eliezer. Therefore, Rabbi Eliezer's teaching that *lehanniach* is *pasul* was transmitted through Rabbi Ilai to his son Rabbi Yehuda, and that is precisely what Yosef had heard from Rabbi Yehuda. *Lefichach shanah lecha mishnat Rabbi Eliezer* — that is why Rabbi Yehuda transmitted to you the position of Rabbi Eliezer. This does not mean it is the ruling *halacha*, but it does confirm that what Yosef had remembered was a legitimate and traceable opinion. The question of why none of Rabbi Yehuda's other students could corroborate it remains intriguing, but the *gemara* leaves it unaddressed.

The *gemara* brought this entire story to resolve a precise halachic point — and it is remarkable how it uses such a deeply human and emotionally resonant narrative to resolve what is ultimately a narrow question of whose opinion Rabbi Yehuda was transmitting.

## Divrei HaKol Analysis

### Lost Object Problem

The *gemara* now turns to analyze Rabbi Yehuda's claim that *lehanniach* is *divrei hakol pasul*. The *gemara* challenges: if Rabbi Yehuda was indeed claiming that everyone agrees it is *pasul* — both Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* — then when Yosef HaBavli said *hechzarta li avedati*, you have returned to me my lost object, what sense does that make? What was returned to him?

The problem is this: Yosef had heard from Rabbi Yehuda that *lehanniach* is *pasul*, and he was concerned he had misremembered. He came to Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, who told him that the *halacha* is *kasher* but that Rabbi Eliezer says *pasul*. Now, if Rabbi Yehuda was claiming *divrei hakol pasul*, then Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua's statement actually tells the opposite story — Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua is saying it is a *machlokes* between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim*, not that everyone agrees it is *pasul*. In that case, Yosef should not have felt his lost object was returned; the teaching he received from Rabbi Yehuda — that it is *divrei hakol pasul* — has not been corroborated at all. What has been corroborated at most is that there exists some opinion of *pasul*, but Rabbi Yehuda's stronger claim — that it is *divrei hakol* — has actually been *contradicted*.

The *gemara* sharpens this: *ihu plugta ka'amar lei* — perhaps Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua was presenting a *machlokes*, telling Yosef that it is debated. If so, what Yosef heard from Rabbi Yehuda and what Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua said would be two different things: Rabbi Yehuda apparently transmitted it as though everyone says *pasul*, while Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua says it is a dispute. The lost object, such as it is, would only be the bare fact that there exists a *pasul* opinion — but Rabbi Yehuda's specific claim of *divrei hakol* has not been vindicated.

Furthermore, the *gemara* notes, when the narrative explains why Rabbi Yehuda taught this — *ha mipnei sheRabbi Yehuda beno shel Rabbi Ilai, Rabbi Ilai talmido shel Rabbi Eliezer* — that framing sounds like Rabbi Yehuda was presenting the view of Rabbi Eliezer and his tradition, not claiming universal agreement. The very explanation given for why Rabbi Yehuda said *pasul* — that he was following his father's *rebbe* — implies that he was presenting one side of a dispute, not asserting consensus.

### Explicit Divrei HaKol

The *gemara* pushes toward the conclusion that Rabbi Yehuda explicitly claimed *divrei hakol pasul* — that in his formulation, it was not presented as a *machlokes* at all. The problem this creates for the story is then answered: *dehadar leh mishum paslus d'alma* — what was returned to Yosef was, at minimum, the corroboration that there is an opinion of *pasul* out there. Even if what Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua told him does not fully vindicate the specific framing of *divrei hakol*, it at least establishes that the underlying tradition of *pasul* is real — that Yosef had not confabulated it. The *avedah* that was returned was not the precise wording of Rabbi Yehuda's claim, but the kernel of truth within it.

A cleaner way to read the story, it might seem, would be to say that Rabbi Yehuda was not being precise about whether his position was *divrei hakol* or merely his own ruling; he simply stated *pasul* because he was following the tradition of Rabbi Eliezer, without explicitly asserting it was unanimous. But the *gemara* concludes that from the way Rabbi Yehuda stated it — simply *pasul*, without qualification — it does sound as though he was claiming consensus. The *gemara* thus lands on the position that Rabbi Yehuda did claim *divrei hakol*, and the story works because the return of the *avedah* is understood in the more modest sense of confirming that the *psul* tradition is authentic, even if not that it is universally held.

## Mincha Defects List

### Lo Yatzak

The *gemara* now turns from the question of invalid intentions to the actual process of preparing the *mincha* — though characteristically, it approaches the process through the lens of which defects are or are not *me'akev*. The *mishna* presents a list of procedural omissions or errors that do not invalidate the *mincha*. The first is *lo yatzak* — the pouring of oil was not performed. By way of background: the basic preparation of a *mincha belula b'shemen* involves placing oil in the vessel, then the flour, mixing the two, and finally performing *yetzika* — an additional pouring of oil on top. The full *log* of oil must be used, and that is *me'akev*; but many of the ways in which the oil is applied and mixed are not. *Lo yatzak* refers to the case where the *yetzika* was not done — though as the *gemara* will immediately clarify, the precise meaning of this phrase requires examination.

### Lo Balal

*Lo balal* — the flour and oil were not mixed together; the flour was left sitting atop the oil without being kneaded into dough. The *mishna*'s inclusion of this case implicitly teaches that *belila* is not *me'akev*, at least not as an absolute requirement.

### Other Defects

The *mishna* continues with a series of additional defects, each of which nevertheless leaves the *mincha kasher*. *Lo pitet* — the priest did not break the baked *mincha* into pieces. Certain *minchot* are baked, and the Torah states *patot otah pitim* — one must break them after baking, folding and breaking into pieces so that the *kometz* can be taken. Rashi clarifies that one had to break a small part to enable the taking of the *kometz*, but the requirement to break the whole thing into pieces was not fulfilled. *Lo malach* — salt was not applied, despite the Torah's requirement *al kol korbancha takriv melach*. One has violated a *lav*, but the *korban* as a *cheftza* remains *kasher*. *Lo henif* — the waving was not performed; only certain *minchot* require *hanafa*, namely *minchat chotei*, *minchat kanaot*, and the *omer*, and even for those, failure to wave does not invalidate. *Lo higish* — the *mincha* was not brought to touch the corner of the *mizbeach*, as the Torah requires before the *kometz* is taken. *O shepitetan pitim merubot* — or one performed *ptita* but produced large chunks rather than the properly sized pieces. *Lo mashchan* — the *meshicha*, the anointing-style application of oil to certain *minchot* such as the *rkikei matzot*, was not performed. In all of these cases: *kasheira* — the *mincha* is valid.

## Yetzika Me'akev

### Yetzika Required

The *gemara* immediately interrogates the opening case. *Mai lo yatzak* — what does *lo yatzak* mean? *Ilima lo yatzak klal* — if it means that no *yetzika* was performed at all, there is an immediate difficulty: the Torah indicates that *yetzika* is *me'akev*. Rashi explains the source: the verse *veyatzakta aleha shemen, mincha hi* — the word *hi* signals that it is essential, that without the pouring it is not a *mincha*. The *gemara* takes this as given and does not even bother to spell out the derivation at length; it simply assumes that *yetzika* is *me'akev* and asks how the *mishna* could say *lo yatzak* is *kasher*.

### Non-Kohen Yetzika

The *gemara* therefore answers: *lo yatzak kohen ela zar* — *lo yatzak* does not mean that no *yetzika* was performed, but that a non-*kohen* performed it. The principle governing the *mincha* is *mikmitza va'eilach mitzvas kehuna* — from the taking of the *kometz* onward is the domain of *kehuna*. Everything prior to the *kometz* — including the *yetzika* and the *belila* — can be done by a non-*kohen*, a *zar*. The *mishna* is therefore teaching that even when a *zar* performed the *yetzika*, the *mincha* is *kasher b'dieved*.

One might ask: is the *mishna* teaching what is *b'dieved* permissible, or merely stating what is the case *lechatchila*? The question is apt, as reading *lo yatzak* to refer to a *zar* is clearly departing from the plain meaning — the *pshat* would suggest it means the *yetzika* was not done at all.

### Tosefta Support

The *gemara* then asks: by the same logic, should not *lo balal* also be read as referring to a *zar*? And if so, the implication would be that if *belila* was not done at all, it would be *pasul* — only the case of a *zar* performing it would be *kasher*. But this is demonstrably not so: the *mishna* elsewhere teaches *shishim nivlalim, shishim ve'echad ein nivlalim* — sixty *isronot* of flour can be mixed together with their *log* of oil, but sixty-one cannot, because at that quantity a proper *belila* is physically impossible. And the *gemara* there had asked: *ki ein nivlalim mah havi* — if they cannot be mixed, why does it matter? Does not our *mishna* say *lo balal kasheira* — even if you didn't mix it at all, it is *kasher*? That question, and the answer of Rav Zeira to be discussed shortly, treats *lo balal* entirely literally — as meaning it was not mixed at all. The *gemara* therefore accepts that *lo balal* is taken at its plain meaning, and resolves the tension between *lo yatzak* and *lo balal* simply: *ha ke-isa ve-ha ke-isa* — each phrase means what fits its context. *Lo yatzak* means a *zar* performed it; *lo balal* means it was not done at all. This is not an entirely satisfying answer given the symmetry of the two phrases, but the *gemara* is compelled to it by the established principle that *yetzika* is *me'akev* while *belila* is not.

## Mincha Defects List…

### Fitness for Mixing

The *gemara* now returns to the principle articulated by Rav Zeira in the context of the sixty-*isronot* discussion: *kol haraui l'blila ein blila me'akevet bo, v'chol she'eino raui l'blila blila me'akevet bo* — anything fit for mixing is not invalidated by the absence of actual mixing, but anything that is not fit for mixing is invalidated. The principle sounds paradoxical: if something cannot be mixed, it is *pasul*, but if it can be mixed and simply was not, it is *kasher*. The point is that *b'dieved* one does not have to perform the *belila*, but the *mincha* must have been capable of being mixed. Hence, sixty *isronot* in one *kli* — which could, with considerable effort, be mixed — are *kasher* even without actual mixing. Sixty-one *isronot* — which physically cannot be properly mixed — are *pasul* and must be brought in two separate *keilim*.

This principle, *kol haraui l'blila*, has a reach that extends far beyond the laws of *menachot*. Anyone who has learned any significant portion of *Shas* will have encountered Rav Zeira's principle deployed across a wide range of contexts, where the *gemara* invokes the idea that something does not need to be actually done, only to be capable of being done. Tosafot addresses the question of when this principle applies and when it does not, and offers an answer that goes to the heart of the matter: it depends on whether the Torah is describing a quality or character of the object, or mandating an action. When the Torah says *mincha belula ba'shemen* — a *mincha* that is mixed with oil — the word *belula* is an adjective describing the character of the *mincha*, not a verb commanding an act of mixing. One can therefore say: is this a *mincha belula*? It has the potential to be *belula* — that is sufficient to regard it as having that quality. But when the Torah mandates that one must perform an act, the mere capacity to have performed it does not substitute for the act itself.

This same logic underlies similar principles elsewhere in *halacha*. *Kol ha'omed liktzira kekatzur dami*, *kol ha'omed ligiza kigzuz dami* — produce ready for harvest is treated as already harvested, wool ready for shearing is treated as already shorn. In all of these, what is at stake is the characterization of an object at a certain stage of readiness, not the completion of an act. The principle does not apply where an action is itself the requirement, because if the act has not been done, it has simply not been done. This is also why attempts to extend the principle humorously — as if potential for prayer counted as prayer — are not taken seriously; acts are acts, and fitness to perform them is not their performance.

An interesting related application occurs in the laws of *tevila*: one need not have water actually enter the mouth or other orifices, but there must be no *chatzitza* — the orifice must be *ra'ui l'viat mayim*, capable of water entering, even if water does not actually enter. Whether this is a genuine application of the same principle is a more complex question, given that the *tevila* itself is an act — but at minimum, the *gemara* invokes the underlying logic of fitness versus actuality in that context as well.

### Ptitim Merubot

The *gemara* now jumps to the last case in the *mishna*: *o she'pitetan pitim merubot, kasher* — or one performed the *ptita* but produced *ptitim merubot*, and it is *kasher*. The *gemara* initially asks: if *lo patat klal* — not performing *ptita* at all — is *kasher*, is there any question that making large pieces would be *kasher*? Surely if doing nothing is fine, doing something imperfectly is certainly fine. Rashi clarifies that in the case of *lo patat klal*, one had done a minimum breaking in order to enable the taking of the *kometz*, but had not broken the rest.

The *gemara* therefore resolves that *ptitim merubot* does not mean large pieces; it means the opposite — that one over-broke the *mincha*, producing too many small pieces. The word *merubot* here means numerous, not large. The question then becomes: why would I think that breaking it into too many pieces might be *pasul*? The reasoning would be: perhaps, having gone past the point of no return — having reduced the *mincha* to pieces too small — one has entered a qualitatively different and invalidating state. From *lo patat klal* I might invoke something like *kol haraui*, since the *mincha* could still be broken into proper pieces; but once it has been excessively broken and cannot be reconstituted, perhaps that crosses a line.

The *gemara* then offers an alternate answer: perhaps *ptitim merubot* does mean large pieces, and the *chiddush* is the following — *hava amina* that one might invalidate large pieces because they occupy an intermediate state: they are no longer *chalot*, whole loaves (*torat chalot*), and they are not *ptitim*, properly sized small pieces (*torat ptitim*). The Torah associates the *mincha* with recognized forms — *chalot matzot belulot ba'shemen*. If one has broken them but not into the appropriate small pieces, perhaps this middle state, recognizable as neither one form nor the other, has no halachic standing. *Kama'shmalan* — the *mishna* teaches that even this is not *me'akev*; the *mincha* is *kasher b'dieved* regardless.

## Rabbi Shimon Position

### Rabbi Shimon's View

The *gemara* now observes: *leima matnitin d'lo k'Rabbi Shimon* — let us say that our *mishna* does not accord with the view of Rabbi Shimon. This brings us back to the earlier point about *lo yatzak* being performed by a *zar*, which the *mishna* treats as *kasher*. The *gemara* cites a *braisa*: Rabbi Shimon says, *kol kohen she'eino modeh ba'avoda ein lo chelek bakehuna* — any *kohen* who does not agree to the *avoda* has no portion in *kehuna*. The meaning is that a *kohen* must be prepared to perform all of the *avodot* and must affirm their value. There are *gemarot* elsewhere that discuss *kohanim* who disparage certain parts of the *avoda*, and this raises questions about their legitimacy. Rabbi Shimon appears to go further: one who does not affirm the *avoda* — whether by refusing to perform it or by failing to accept it as meaningful — has no portion in *kehuna* and may not perform the *avoda* at all, even those parts he would prefer to do. The verse cited is *hamakriv et dam hashlamim v'et hachelev mibnei Aharon, lo tihiye shok hayamin lemana* — the one who offers the blood and the *chelev* from among the sons of Aharon, to him shall be the right thigh as a portion. The *gemara* reads this as: only *hamakriv* — one who participates fully — receives a portion.

### Fifteen Avodot

From this verse, one might have thought the principle applies only to the *shlamin*. The *braisa* therefore asks: *minayin l'rabot tet-vav avodot* — how do we know to extend it to all fifteen *avodot* of the *kohen*? The list that follows is striking for what it includes. *Hayetzikot* — the pouring of oil — appears at the head, indicating that Rabbi Shimon regards *yetzika* as an act requiring a *kohen*. *V'hablilot* — the mixing. *V'haptitot* — the breaking into pieces. These three are precisely the acts that our *mishna* treats as performable by a *zar*, and Rabbi Shimon places them in the category of *avodot* requiring a *kohen*. The list continues: *v'hamilchot* (salting), *v'hatnufot* (waving), *v'hahagashot* (the bringing to the *mizbeach*), *v'hakmizot* (the taking of the *kometz*), *v'hahaktarot* (the burning), *v'hamlikot* (the *mlika* of bird offerings), *v'hakabalot* (receiving the blood), *v'haza'ot* (sprinkling the blood). Notably absent is *holacha*, the carrying of the blood, which, as Tosafot notes, Rav Shimi holds may be done by a *zar*. The list then extends beyond the *Mikdash* entirely: *v'hashkat sota* (making the *sota* drink), *v'arifat egla arufa* (breaking the neck of the *egla arufa*, which is not performed in the *Mikdash* at all), and *taharat metzora* (the purification of the *metzora*, which includes *marot nega'im* as well — a *kohen*'s function but not limited to the *Mikdash*). Finally, *u'nesiat kapayim bein mibifnim bein mibachutz* — the *birkat kohanim*, whether performed inside the *Mikdash* or outside. The inclusion of these last items is fascinating: Rabbi Shimon's principle of *modeh ba'avoda* extends to any act that is defined as an *avoda* of the *kohen*, regardless of whether it is specific to the *Mikdash*.

The source for extending beyond the *shlamin* to all fifteen *avodot*: *talmud lomar mibnei Aharon* — any *avoda hamesura livnei Aharon*, any act entrusted to the sons of Aharon — a *kohen* who does not affirm it has no portion in *kehuna*.

### Rav Nachman's Resolution

Rav Nachman says it is not difficult: *kan b'minchat Kohanim, kan b'minchat Yisrael* — the distinction is between a *minchat Yisrael* and a *minchat Kohanim*. A *minchat Yisrael* is one that has a *kmitza* — only the *kometz* is burned, and the *shirayim* are consumed by the *kohanim*. For such a *mincha*, the principle *mikmitza va'eilach mitzvas kehuna* applies: everything prior to the *kometz*, including *yetzika* and *blila*, can be performed by a *zar*. The verse *limed al yetzika u'blila sheksheira b'zar* — teaches that a non-*kohen* may perform these preparatory acts. Rabbi Shimon, Rav Nachman suggests, would agree with this. Where Rabbi Shimon says the full process requires a *kohen* is in the case of *minchat Kohanim*, where there is no *kmitza* — the entire *mincha* is burned. Since there is no act of *kmitza* to mark the transition to *kehuna* work, the requirement of *kehuna* applies *me'ikara*, from the very outset.

### Rava's Objection

Rava challenges this resolution. *Michdi minchat Kohanim meheicha itrabbi l'yetzika?* — from where does the *Torah* derive that *yetzika* is required in a *minchat Kohanim* at all? It is derived *miminchat Yisrael* — from the *mincha* of a *Yisrael*. If so, *mah hatam kesheira b'zar, af hacha nami kesheira b'zar* — just as a *zar* is *kasher* in that case, so too a *zar* should be *kasher* in the case of *minchat Kohanim*. The same argument is then repeated in the parallel version of the discussion: *minayin she'ein nekmatzot* — how do we know that the acts for *minchot* without a *kmitza* are required? We learn from *minchot* that do have a *kmitza*. Therefore they should follow the same rule: if a *zar* is valid there, a *zar* should be valid here too. Rava concludes: *ela michvarta, matnitin d'lo k'Rabbi Shimon* — it is better to simply say that our *mishna* does not accord with Rabbi Shimon's view. Rabbi Shimon holds that the entire process of a *mincha* requires a *kohen* from the beginning, while the *tanna* of our *mishna* holds that only from the *kmitza* onward is it *mitzvas kehuna*.

### Kedusha Analysis

A beautiful observation of Rav Soloveitchik on Rabbi Shimon's principle of *kol kohen she'eino modeh ba'avoda*: Rav Soloveitchik notes a fundamental difference between the structure of *kehuna* and the structure of *leviya*. *Leviim* are defined by their *tafkid* — their specific job. A *levi* is a gatekeeper or a singer; his role is defined by the particular function he performs. *Kohanim*, by contrast, are not primarily defined by any specific task they perform. Rather, as we have just seen, a *kohen* who is not prepared to perform all the *avodot* — who picks and chooses — has no portion in *kehuna*. This is because *kohanim* are defined not by their function but by an intrinsic *kedusha*. *V'kidashto ki lechem Elokecha* — you shall sanctify him, because he brings the bread of God. The *kedusha* of a *kohen* is not derived from the acts he performs; rather, it is an intrinsic sanctity that demands total dedication and readiness for any act of *avoda*. A *kohen* who would claim a portion in *kehuna* while refusing certain *avodot* has fundamentally misunderstood the nature of *kehuna*: the *kedusha* does not come from the specific role, but from the total commitment to service in the *Mikdash* — and that commitment must be wholehearted and without reservation.
