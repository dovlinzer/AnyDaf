# Nedarim 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Marital Relations

### Completing Daf 20

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלֹא תָתוּרוּ אַחֲרֵי לְבַבְכֶם״, מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי: אַל יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם בְּכוֹס זֶה וְיִתֵּן עֵינָיו בְּכוֹס אַחֵר. אָמַר רָבִינָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא, אֶלָּא דַּאֲפִילּוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The verse states: **"And that you not go about after your own heart"** (Numbers 15:39). **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said** that it is derived **from here** that **a man should not drink from this cup while setting his eyes on another cup,** i.e., one should not engage in sexual intercourse with one woman while thinking about another woman. **Ravina said:** This statement is not necessary with regard to an unrelated woman. Rather, it **is necessary only** to state **that even** with regard to **his** own **two wives,** he should not engage in sexual intercourse with one while thinking about the other.

We begin today by completing Daf 20, picking up at the bottom of 20b with the verse *velo tasuru acharei levavchem*. The preceding *sugyah* on Daf 20 constituted one of the most remarkable discussions in all of *Shas*, dealing at length with the question of which physical acts are permissible between husband and wife during marital relations. We encountered two poles: the position associated with *Imma Shalom* and Yochanan ben Dahavai — closely aligned with the stringent posture of Rabbi Eliezer, who would rush through the marital act lest he entertain an improper thought — and the opposing voice of Rabbi Yochanan, who rules definitively: *ein halakhah k'Yochanan ben Dahavai, ella kol mah she-adam rotzeh la'asot b'ishto oseh*. Whatever a man wishes to do with his wife, he may do. The language, it should be noted, reflects the male perspective of the *Gemara*, though it is typically paraphrased today as the couple being permitted to do whatever they wish together — and the underlying position is that this is entirely unproblematic, not merely technically permissible.

One striking feature of the *Gemara*'s rhetorical strategy here is worth noting. The discussion began with anxieties about how improper conduct would affect the children — a claim that, had it remained in the *halakhic* domain, would have had to contend with Rabbi Yochanan on *halakhic* terms. But Rabbi Yochanan's framing as *ein halakhah* essentially relocates Yochanan ben Dahavai's concerns outside the *halakhic* conversation altogether. Whatever metaphysical ideas one might hold about proper and improper conduct, the *Gemara* is insisting that those anxieties belong to a non-*halakhic* register. *Halakhically*, there is nothing problematic here.

### Intent vs. Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבָרוֹתִי מִכֶּם הַמֹּרְדִים וְהַפּוֹשְׁעִים בִּי״, אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי תֵּשַׁע מִדּוֹת, בְּנֵי אׇסְנַ״‎ת משגע״‎ח.
> בְּנֵי אֵימָה, בְּנֵי אֲנוּסָה, בְּנֵי שְׂנוּאָה, בְּנֵי נִידּוּי, בְּנֵי תְמוּרָה, בְּנֵי מְרִיבָה, בְּנֵי שִׁכְרוּת, בְּנֵי גְּרוּשַׁת הַלֵּב, בְּנֵי עִרְבּוּבְיָא, בְּנֵי חֲצוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And I will purge out from among you the rebels, and those that transgress against Me"** (Ezekiel 20:38). **Rabbi Levi said: These are children of** those who have **nine traits,** who are defective from their conception and from whom rebels and transgressors emerge. The mnemonic for these nine traits is **children of** the acronym ***aleph*, *samekh*, *nun*, *tav*, *mem*, *shin*, *gimmel*, *ayin*, *ḥet*. The children of nine traits are as follows: **Children of fear [*eima*],** i.e., where the wife was afraid of her husband and engaged in sexual intercourse with him out of fear; **children of a woman who was raped [*anusa*]; children of a hated woman [*senua*],** i.e., a woman who was hated by her husband; **children of ostracism [*niddui*],** i.e., one of the parents was ostracized by the court; **children of substitution [*temura*],** i.e., while engaging in intercourse with the woman, the man thought that she was another woman; **children of strife [*meriva*],** i.e., the parents engaged in intercourse while they were quarreling; **children of drunkenness [*shikhrut*],** i.e., the parents engaged in intercourse while they were drunk; **children of a woman who was divorced in the heart [*gerushat halev*],** i.e., the husband had already decided to divorce her when they engaged in intercourse; **children of mixture [*irbuveya*],** i.e., the man did not know with which woman he was engaging in intercourse; **children of a shameless woman [*ḥatzufa*]** who demands of her husband that he engage in intercourse with her.

Now, however, the *Gemara* makes an important shift. It introduces the verse *velo tasuru acharei levavchem* — you shall not stray after your hearts — and cites Rebbe: *al yishteh adam b'kos zeh v'yiten einav b'kos acher*. A person should not drink from one cup while setting his eyes on another — a metaphor for having marital relations with one's wife while thinking of another woman. Ravina sharpens this: *lo nitzrecha ella d'afilu b'shnei nashav* — this applies even between a man's two wives; he should not be intimate with one while his thoughts are directed toward the other.

This passage, notably, focuses not on *acharei eineichem* but on *acharei levavchem* — it is not about what the eyes perceive but about what the heart strays toward. The man sees the woman before him but is mentally elsewhere, straying after his own inner fantasy. The problem the *Gemara* is now identifying is not the nature of the physical act at all, but whether the act of intimacy is an act of genuine *relating* to the person in front of you, or merely *using* that person as a stand-in for someone else you would rather be with. That is the distinction — not the particular activity, but whether the person before you is truly present to you.

There is an important question lurking here about the role of fantasy in marital life generally, and some *poskim* do address it. However, the *Gemara* appears to be speaking specifically about a case where one imagines oneself with another person entirely and treats one's actual spouse as merely playing that role — a functional displacement rather than ordinary arousal. That is what is prohibited.

### Nine Traits

The *Gemara* continues with the verse *uvaroti mikem hamordim vehaposheim bi* — I will sift out from you those who rebel and sin against Me. Rabbi Levi reads this as a reference to *bnei tisha middot* — children conceived through nine types of deficient conduct. Where yesterday the *Gemara* was asking whether particular physical acts would produce physically flawed children — a claim resoundingly rejected — now it asks whether a different kind of deficiency, one of character and relational disposition, will produce children of poor character. The problem has shifted from the physical to the moral, from what kind of act was performed to what kind of relating was occurring between the spouses. The acronym *Asnat Mashgaach* encodes the nine types.

### Fear & Hatred

The first is *bnei eima* — children conceived when the wife is acting out of fear of her husband. The *Ran* explains: *lashon yira shehetil eima al ishto lehizakek lo shelo kirtzonah* — the husband has instilled fear in her such that she submits against her genuine will. Then *bnei anusa* — children of a woman who is literally coerced. Then *bnei senuah* — when he hates her; he feels no love, no care, but treats her as an available means of physical gratification. The *Ra'avad*, in his discussion of this passage, pointedly symmetrizes the case: it is the same whether he hates her or she hates him. Either way, one party is using the other purely for self-gratification rather than relating to them out of love or care.

*Bnei nidui* — children conceived while the husband is under a ban — is the one case in this list that stands apart, as it is simply a *halakhic* violation unrelated to the relational theme. *Bnei tmurah* — exchange — refers to a case of confusion, where one believes he is with one wife but is in fact with another. *Bnei meriva* — children of strife — means they are in the middle of a fight, not as makeup sex after reconciliation, but mid-conflict, where one thinks: we may be fighting, but we might as well have relations. *Bnei shichrut* — intoxication — means one or both are drunk, according to the *Ra'avad*, and not mentally fully present. *Bnei grushat halev* — divorced in the heart — means the husband has already decided, internally and unilaterally, to divorce his wife but has not yet told her; he proceeds with intimacy as if the marriage is still intact in spirit, which it is not.

*Bnei irvuvya* — children of confusion — is somewhat unclear: the *Ran* understands it as referring to relations with multiple partners, which would make it a case of adultery; Rashi suggests a case where a man cannot distinguish which of his wives he is with, similar to *tmurah*. Finally, *bnei chatzufa* — the audacious woman — we will return to shortly.

With the exception of *tmurah* and *chatzufa*, all of these cases share a single common feature: the spouse is being used as an object of self-gratification rather than genuinely encountered and related to as a person. This is what the *Gemara* identifies as the source of harm — not some physical impropriety, but the failure of genuine relational presence. Yesterday's discussion was about which physical acts are permitted; today's conclusion is that the real *halakhic* demand is not about the type of act but about whether intimacy is an act of connection or an act of use.

### Female Initiation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁאִשְׁתּוֹ תּוֹבַעְתּוֹ — הָוְיִין לוֹ בָּנִים שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ בְּדוֹרוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא הָיוּ כְּמוֹתָם. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָבוּ לָכֶם אֲנָשִׁים חֲכָמִים וּנְבֹנִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וָאֶקַּח אֶת רָאשֵׁי שִׁבְטֵיכֶם״, וְלָא כְּתִיב ״נְבוֹנִים״. וּכְתִיב ״יִשָּׂשכָר חֲמֹר גָּרֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״מִבְּנֵי יִשָּׂשכָר יוֹדְעֵי בִינָה לַעִתִּים״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani say** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: Any man whose wife demands of him** that he engage in sexual intercourse with her **will have children the likes of whom did not exist even in the generation of Moses our teacher? As it is stated: "Get you wise men, and understanding,** and well known from each one of your tribes, and I will make them head over you" (Deuteronomy 1:13); **and it is written** subsequently: **"So I took the heads of your tribes,** wise men, and well known" (Deuteronomy 1:15). **And it does not say** that they were **understanding.** Evidently, even Moses could not find understanding men in his generation. **And** by contrast, **it is written: "Issachar is a large-boned donkey"** (Genesis 49:14). The Sages transmitted a tradition that this is an allusion to the incident when Jacob came in from the field riding on a donkey, and Leah went out to greet him, saying: "You must come in to me; for I have hired you with my son's mandrakes" (Genesis 30:16). Issachar was conceived from their subsequent sexual intercourse. **And it is written: "And of the children of Issachar, men that had understanding of the times"** (I Chronicles 12:33). The descendants of Issachar were understanding men. It is derived from here that a woman who demands from her husband that he engage in sexual intercourse with her has a positive effect on their children.

The *Gemara* now turns to *bnei chatzufa* — the audacious child — which the *Gemara* understands to mean that the wife demanded relations with her husband. One might have thought this was entirely praiseworthy. Indeed, the *Gemara* itself immediately raises the objection: *ayni?* Is that really true? Does it not say — *de'amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yochanan* — that any man whose wife is *tova'ato*, who actively solicits him, will have children of extraordinary quality, surpassing even those of the generation of Moshe Rabbeinu? The proof: when Moshe called for men who are *chachamim u'nevonim*, he could not find people of *tevunah*. But the tribe of Yissachar, who are *yodei binah l'ittim*, did possess that quality — and Yissachar was conceived when Leah was *tova'at* Yaakov. The plain implication is that a wife actively soliciting her husband produces exceptional children.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהִיא דְּמַרְצְיָא אַרְצוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** *baraita* is not referring to a woman who demands intercourse explicitly, but rather to one **who entices** her husband, so that he understands that she wants to engage in sexual intercourse with him. They consequently have excellent children.

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *hahi d'martza artzuyei* — that was a case of seduction and persuasion, not outright demand. Leah seduced; she did not command. The permissibility, even the virtue, lies in *martza artzuyei* — hinting, enticing, drawing close — not in *tova'at b'peh*, an explicit verbal demand.

This distinction, however, naturally raises the question of where exactly the line lies between the two. The *teshuvah* of the *Yehuda Ya'aleh* reasons that since the *Gemara* validates what Leah did — and Leah said *elai tavo*, "come to me," which is already quite explicit — any expression short of an absolute demand ("you have no choice in the matter") would fall within the permitted category of *martza artzuyei*. What the wife cannot do is strip the husband of any say in the matter.

### Connection & Care

Even so, the very fact that the *Gemara* takes Leah's *tova'at* as a model that produces children greater than those of Moshe's generation is a striking recalibration of what might have been assumed to be a patriarchal assumption — that the husband always initiates, that the wife has no voice. The *Gemara*, far from affirming that assumption, holds it up and says: not only is female initiative acceptable, it is the paradigm of the ideal.

And with that, we complete the *perek* with *hadran alach ve'elu mutarin*. The conclusion of this entire extended discussion about marital intimacy is that *halakhah* is not primarily concerned with which physical acts are performed. What *halakhah* demands is that intimacy be an act of genuine connection — that each partner be present to and for the other, not using the other as a means to some private end. That is the core of what the *Gemara* is teaching about marital relations.

## Four Vows

### Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הִתִּירוּ חֲכָמִים: נִדְרֵי זֵרוּזִין, וְנִדְרֵי הֲבַאי, וְנִדְרֵי שְׁגָגוֹת, וְנִדְרֵי אוֹנָסִין. נִדְרֵי זֵרוּזִין כֵּיצַד? הָיָה מוֹכֵר חֵפֶץ, וְאָמַר: ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֵינִי פּוֹחֵת לְךָ מִן הַסֶּלַע״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֵינִי מוֹסִיף לָךְ עַל הַשֶּׁקֶל״, שְׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דִּינָרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The Sages dissolved four** types of **vows** without the requirement of a request to a halakhic authority: **Vows of exhortation, vows of exaggeration, vows that are unintentional, and vows** whose fulfillment is impeded by **circumstances beyond** one's **control.** The mishna explains: **Vows of exhortation** are those by which one encourages another using vow terminology that is exaggerated. **How so? One was selling an item and said: I will not lower** the price **for you** to less **than a *sela*,** as **that is *konam*,** forbidden **as if** it were **an offering,** for me. **And the other one,** the buyer, **says: I will not raise** my payment **to you** to **more than a shekel,** as **that is *konam*** for me. In this case, one may assume that **both want** to complete the deal **at three dinars,** and they did not intend to vow but only exaggerated for purposes of bargaining.

We now turn to a new area of the laws of *nedarim*. So far the *masechta* has been dealing largely with questions of interpretation: what words constitute a *neder*, how we read ambiguous formulations, to what extent a person's own interpretation of their language is credited, and so forth. One question that has not yet been addressed directly is that of *piv v'libo shavin* — the alignment of one's words and one's heart. What happens when someone utters the unambiguous language of a *neder* but was not truly intending to bind themselves? Not a case of misused words, where one meant *cherem* in the sense of a fishing net — but a case where the language was clearly *neder*-language, yet the speaker's frame of mind was simply not in sync with a genuine intention to be bound. That is what the *Mishnah* now addresses.

*Arba'ah nedarim hitiru chachamim* — the sages permitted four categories of vows, meaning these vows are not halakhically binding. The four are: *nidrei zeruzin* — spurring vows; *nidrei havai* — vows of nothingness or hyperbole; *nidrei shegagot* — vows made in error; and *nidrei onsin* — vows made under compulsion. In each case, the speaker used what sounds like *neder*-language and was aware that those were *neder*-words — yet because of their particular frame of mind at the time, the vow does not take effect.

### Spurring Vows

The example of *nidrei zeruzin*: a seller says *konam she-ani pocheis lach min ha-sela* — this object should be forbidden to you if I sell it for less than a *sela* (four *zuz*). The buyer counters: *konam she-ani mosif lach al ha-shekel* — it should be forbidden to me if I pay more than a *shekel* (two *zuz*). *Shneihem rotzim b'shloshah dinarim* — both of them are in fact aiming for three *dinarim*. Neither side actually intends to forbid anything; the vow-language is deployed as a haggling tool, a way of pressing the other side. Since neither party is genuinely serious about binding themselves, the vows do not take hold.

### Conditional Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הִתִּירוּ חֲכָמִים כּוּ׳. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֲמַרְתְּ לַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה: מַאן תְּנָא אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: לְעוֹלָם אֵין אֶחָד מֵהֶן נָזִיר, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה נְזִירוּת אֶלָּא לְהַפְלָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states: **The Sages dissolved four** types of **vows. Rabbi Abba bar Memel said to Rabbi Ami: You said to us in the name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this mishna of **four vows? It** is the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said in the name of Rabbi Tarfon:** With regard to two people who entered into a wager, and each declared that he would become a nazirite if the other was right, then **actually neither of them** becomes **a nazirite, because naziriteship is determined only by explicitness** of intent. One cannot become a nazirite unless he vows clearly and with certitude. Here too, since the intent of the wagering parties was not actually to vow, the *halakha* is that the vow is invalid.

The *Gemara* raises a question about the *tanna* behind this *mishnah*. Rav Abba bar Mamal reports in the name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah (the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) that the author is Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai, who said in the name of Rabbi Tarfon: *l'olam eino nazir lefi shelo nitnah nezirut ela l'hafla'ah* — a conditional vow of *nezirut* never takes effect because *nezirut* requires *hafla'ah*, precision and absoluteness at the moment of utterance. The relevance: the *zeruzin* case involves a conditional structure ("if I go lower than..."), and Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai may therefore be reading the permissibility as stemming from the conditionality itself, not from lack of seriousness. Even a completely serious conditional *neder* might not be binding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, מִי קָתָנֵי ״שְׁנֵיהֶן רָצוּ״? ״שְׁנֵיהֶן רוֹצִין״ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda. **Does** the mishna **teach: Both wanted** the price to be three dinars? **It teaches: Both want,** in the present tense, demonstrating that they would have been satisfied with that price from the outset and never intended to vow, so the vow is not binding.

Rava objects: *afilu teima rabanan* — one can explain this even according to the *rabbanan* who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai on conditional *nezirut*. The *mishnah* does not say *shneihem ratzu*, "both wanted the same middle outcome and the conditions were therefore never really triggered." It says *shneihem rotzim*, they *want* the same thing — meaning from the outset, they never intended the vow to be binding at all. That is the reason it is not a *neder*: not some technical feature of conditionality, but the straightforward absence of genuine intent.

### Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אָמַר לוֹ טְפֵי מִסֶּלַע, וְהַלָּה אֹמֵר בְּצִיר מִשֶּׁקֶל, נִדְרָא הָוֵי, אוֹ זֵרוּזִין הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** If the seller **said to** the buyer in the form of a vow that the price must remain **more than a *sela*, and the other** declared in the form of a vow that the price must remain **less than a shekel,** what is the *halakha*? **Is this a vow,** where each stands firmly in his position? **Or** perhaps this **is** also merely for **encouragement?**

Ravina then poses a sharper case to Rav Ashi: what if the seller said he will not go lower than *more than a sela* — say, four *zuz* and a fraction — and the buyer said he will not go higher than *less than a shekel*, say one *zuz* and a fraction? The parties are now being quite precise; they are not using round numbers. Precision in a financial negotiation might suggest genuine seriousness — the more exact your formulation, the more it looks like a real commitment rather than theatrical haggling. Perhaps in that case the vow *would* be binding?

### Exaggeration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: הָיָה מְסָרֵב בַּחֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁיֹּאכַל אֶצְלוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ ״קֻוֽנָּם בֵּיתְךָ שֶׁאֲנִי נִכְנָס״, ״טִיפַּת צוֹנֵן שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם״ — מוּתָּר לִיכָּנֵס לְבֵיתוֹ וְלִשְׁתּוֹת הֵימֶנּוּ צוֹנֵן, שֶׁלֹּא נִתְכַּוֵּון זֶה אֶלָּא לְשׁוּם אֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: We** already **learned** about a similar case: If one **was importuning another that he should eat with him** and the other refused **and said to him: Entering your house is *konam* for me,** or: **A drop of cold** water is *konam* for me, and for **that** reason **I** will not **taste** it with you, **it is permitted** for him **to enter his house and drink cold** water **since he intended it** as a vow **only for the purpose of eating and drinking** a large amount but did not mean literally that he would not drink anything.

Rava responds by pointing to a *baraita*: if someone was being persistently pressed by his friend to come eat at his house and finally said *konam beitecha she-ani nichnas*, "your house should be forbidden to me — I won't enter it," or even *tipat tzonen she-ani to'eim*, "I won't drink even a drop of cold water by you" — in such a case the person *is* permitted to enter the house and drink cold water. *Shelo nitkavein zeh ela l'shum achilah u-shtiyah* — the speaker's genuine intention went no further than refusing to participate in the meal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? וְהָא ״טִיפַּת צוֹנֵן״ קָאָמַר! אֶלָּא מִשְׁתַּעֵי אִינִישׁ הָכִי. הָכָא נָמֵי מִשְׁתַּעֵי אִינִישׁ הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why** is this permitted? **But he said: A drop of cold** water, so how can you say that the vow is only with regard to drinking a large amount? **Rather,** it must be that **a person speaks this way** in exaggerated terms but does not mean literally what he says. **Here also,** in the case of the buyer and seller, **a person speaks this way.** He exaggerates and does not intend the literal meaning of his words, even though he was quite precise in his wording.

The *Ran* explains: *af al gav d'nachis l'diyuka* — even though the person went down to minute precision, specifying "a drop of cold water," and thereby distanced himself far from what his friend wanted, nevertheless he did not literally mean it. The language of a single drop is recognizable as hyperbole, not as a real commitment to avoiding the house altogether.

This case raises a significant point: do we say that the exaggerated extensions simply fall away while the core intention remains binding, or that since the entire formulation was hyperbolic nothing is binding at all? The *baraita* implies the former: the person is permitted to enter the house and to drink water, but is presumably still bound by the core refusal to sit down for a meal. The exaggerated shell dissolves, but what the speaker genuinely intended — declining the invitation to eat — remains. That is a notable *chiddush*: *neder*-annulment is not always all-or-nothing; the exaggerated perimeter can be stripped away to reveal and enforce the actual intent at the core.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי דָּמֵי? גַּבֵּי צוֹנֵן, צַדִּיקִים אוֹמְרִים מְעַט וְעוֹשִׂין הַרְבֵּה —
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him:** **Are** these cases **comparable? Concerning** the case of **cold** water, the **righteous say little and do much.** Therefore, when the host says to the other: Enter my house and drink a drop of cold water, he intended to offer him an entire meal. So too, the one who vows is referring to an entire meal and not literally to a drop of water. Therefore, he may drink a bit of cold water in the host's house.

Returning to the question of precision: the *Gemara* distinguishes. *Tzaddikim omrim me'at v'osim harbeh* — righteous people promise little and deliver much. A person who says "I will give you just a drop of water" and then produces a feast is not being imprecise; he is using the conventional idiom of understatement. Inversely, someone who says he won't even accept a drop is using the conventional idiom of overstatement. In both cases the speaker's actual intention diverges from the literal words, and this is recognized social usage. The precise number in the haggling scenario, however, may be different — there it might genuinely signal that the speaker means exactly what he said. The *Gemara* concludes: *teiku*. The question of whether extreme numerical precision in a *zeruzin*-type context creates a real *neder* remains unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא סְפֵיקָא הוּא, דִּלְמָא פָּחוֹת מִסֶּלַע וְיוֹתֵר עַל שֶׁקֶל קָאָמַר, וְזֵירוּזִין הָוֵי, אוֹ דִּלְמָא דַּוְקָא קָאָמַר, וְנִידְרָא הָוֵי. תִּבְּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** But **here it is uncertain. Perhaps** when the seller **says** he will only accept more than a *sela*, he really intends to accept **less than a *sela*, and** when the buyer says he will not pay more than a shekel, he really intends to pay **more than a shekel; and** this **is** also **an exhortation** vow. **Or, perhaps he meant specifically** what he said, **and it is a vow,** because he did not intend to compromise on the price. The Gemara concludes: **The dilemma remains** unresolved.

### Halakhic Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הַלָּלוּ צְרִיכִין שְׁאֵלָה לְחָכָם. כִּי אַמְרִיתָא קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר: תַּנָּא תָּנֵי ״אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הִתִּירוּ חֲכָמִים״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ צְרִיכִין שְׁאֵלָה לְחָכָם?!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav Asi said: These four vows** that are taught in the mishna still **require a request** made **to a halakhic authority** to dissolve them. Rav Yehuda continues: **When I said** this *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said: The *tanna* teaches** that **the Sages dissolved four vows, and you say they require a request** made **to a halakhic authority?**

Rav Yehuda then reports in the name of Rav Assi: *arba'ah nedarim hallalu tzerichin she'eilah lehacham* — these four categories of vows still require formal release by a *hacham*. Even though the *chachamim* declared them permissible, one should still go through the process of *hatarat nedarim* to avoid treating vow-language lightly. When Rav Assi reported this ruling before Shmuel, Shmuel challenged it: the *tanna* explicitly teaches that the *chachamim permitted* these vows — which implies they are automatically non-binding and require no formal release. How can you say they require a *hacham*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף מַתְנִי לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא בְּהַאי לִישָּׁנָא: אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֵין חָכָם רַשַּׁאי לְהַתִּיר אֶלָּא כְּעֵין אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הַלָּלוּ. קָסָבַר אֵין פּוֹתְחִין בַּחֲרָטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef taught this *halakha* in this manner: Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav Asi said: A halakhic authority is able to dissolve only** a vow that is **similar to these four vows** in that it was not intended to be a vow at all but was simply expressed in the language of a vow. In addition, he can dissolve a vow taken by mistake. The Gemara comments: It can be derived from here that **he holds that** a halakhic authority **does not broach** dissolution based **on regret.** A halakhic authority must search for a factor that, had the one making the vow been aware of it at the time of the vow, he would not have vowed. Simply expressing regret about the vow is an insufficient basis on which to dissolve it.

In light of this challenge, Rav Yosef teaches the tradition in a different formulation that does not carry the same vulnerability: *ein chacham rashai lehattir ella ke'ein arba'ah nedarim hallalu* — a *hacham* is not permitted to release a *neder* unless it approximates one of these four categories. This version of the statement shifts the function of the four categories: rather than being cases that themselves require release, they become the paradigm by which all *hatarat nedarim* is measured. A *hacham* releasing any *neder* must be able to bring the case into alignment with one of these four models.

## Vow Annulment

### Model of Annulment

With that formulation — *ein chacham rashai lehattir ella ke'ein arba'ah nedarim hallalu* — we arrive at the heart of the *halachah* of *hatarat nedarim*. The *Gemara* now uses these four categories as the template for understanding what *hatarat nedarim* is doing and how it works.

Consider the case: a person comes to a *hacham* to be released from a *neder* they clearly meant at the time. The question is, on what basis can the *hacham* permit it? The underlying logic of the four categories is that the person, in each case, was never truly and fully intending for the vow to be binding. In the haggling case this is obvious to everyone — no one seriously meant to forbid anything. But that same underlying structure — "at the time, I did not fully mean it to be binding" — is what the *hacham* must be able to reconstruct in any case where he releases a *neder*. The case before him will of course have appeared, from the outside, to be a genuine *neder*. That is why it needs a *hacham*. But the *hacham*'s work is to find the point at which the person's actual frame of mind at the time was not fully in sync with the binding force of what he said.

### Petach & Charata

This brings us to the central distinction in *hatarat nedarim* between two modes of release: *petach* and *charata*. A *petach* — an "opening" — is the identification of some consideration that the person had not thought through at the time of the vow; had they considered it, they would not have made the vow. A *charata* — "regret" — is the person's retrospective wish that they had never made the vow at all, independent of any specific unconsidered circumstance.

The relationship between *petach* and the *arba'ah nedarim* model is clear: the *petach* says, "you never fully meant it, because had you thought it through, you wouldn't have done it." That is structurally analogous to the haggling case — in both situations, the person's genuine will, properly understood, was not behind the vow. *Charata*, by contrast, concedes that at the time the person fully intended and meant the vow — but now, in hindsight, wishes they had not. That is a different kind of claim.

### Starting w/ Petach

Hence the principle — which follows directly from *ein chacham rashai lehattir ella ke'ein arba'ah nedarim hallalu* — of *ein potchin b'charata*: one does not open a *hatarat nedarim* process by asking the person whether they regret making the vow. You start with *petach*. You try to find some circumstance the person had not fully considered, such that you can say: had you thought this through, you would not have made the vow.

### Types of Charata

The *Ran* draws an important further distinction within *charata* itself. There is *charata me'ikara* — regret from the root — which means the person wishes they had never made the vow at all. And there is *charata dehashtah* — regret of now — which simply means that the person no longer wants to be bound going forward. The *Ran* notes that the second is not *charata* in any meaningful sense at all: every person who shows up seeking release from a *neder* no longer wants to be bound. That is why they came. True *charata* requires that the person genuinely wish that the vow had never been made in the first place — a retroactive reframing of the original moment.

### Rav Huna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, אָמַר לֵיהּ: לִבָּךְ עֲלָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, וְשַׁרְיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an incident that illustrates a different opinion concerning broaching dissolution based on regret. There was **a certain** person **who came before Rav Huna** to request dissolution of a vow. Rav Huna **said to him: Is your heart upon you?** Do you still have the same desire that you had when you made the vow? **He said to him: No. And** Rav Huna **dissolved** the vow **for him.** Since Rav Huna dissolved the vow based on regret alone, he evidently holds that one may broach dissolution based on regret.

The *Gemara* now presents a series of cases. Someone came before Rav Huna, who asked him: *libecha alecha?* — is your heart upon you? The person said: no. And Rav Huna released him.

The *Ran* explains this question as probing the person's frame of mind. The *Ran* paraphrases: *haya libecha meyushav alecha b'sha'ah shenadarta* — were you of settled mind when you made the vow? *V'gam achshav eincha toheh b'nidrekha ad hayom* — and have you also had no regret about it until now? *Ela shemehayom ulehalan ata rotzeh lachzor bach* — or is it only from today onward that you want to withdraw? That would be *charata dehashtah*, not *charata me'ikara*, and would not suffice. But if the person's answer of "no" means: no, I am *not* only changing my mind now — I actually regret ever having made it — then that is *charata me'ikara*, and Rav Huna releases him. This is therefore a case where *charata* is used as the basis for release, not *petach*.

One might ask whether this stands in tension with the principle of *ein potchin b'charata*. The answer appears to be that *ein potchin b'charata* means you do not *start* with *charata* — you prefer to find a *petach* — but *charata* remains a valid basis for release when it is genuine *charata me'ikara*.

### Middle Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ הָיוּ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁיְּפַיְּיסוּךְ בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה מִי נָדַרְתָּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא, וְהִתִּירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, there was **a certain** person **who came before Rabba bar Rav Huna** to dissolve his vow. Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to him: Had there been ten people who** could have **appeased you at the time** you vowed, **would you have made the vow? He said to him: No. And he dissolved** the vow **for him.**

Rabba bar bar Chana took a somewhat different approach in his case. He asked the person: *ilu hayu asara bnei adam sheyefaysucha b'ota sha'a, mi nadarta?* — if ten people had been there to calm you down and persuade you at the time, would you still have made the vow? The person said no, and Rabba bar bar Chana released him.

The *Ran* identifies this as occupying a middle ground between *petach* and *charata*. It is more than pure *charata* — rather than simply asking whether you regret it, it asks whether you could have been persuaded otherwise at the very moment of the vow. That slightly retrojects the counterfactual into the original moment, getting closer to the idea that the vow was not fully intended. But it falls short of a standard *petach*, which identifies a concrete circumstance the person had failed to consider — an internal lapse of reasoning rather than an external persuasion that might have intervened.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אוֹמְרִים לוֹ לָאָדָם: לֵב זֶה עָלֶיךָ? אִם אָמַר ״לָאוּ״ — מַתִּירִין אוֹתוֹ. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: אוֹמְרִים לוֹ לָאָדָם: אִילּוּ הָיוּ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁיְּפַיְּיסוּךְ בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה מִי נָדַרְתָּ? אִם אָמַר ״לָאוּ״ — מַתִּירִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The halakhic authorities who dissolve the vow **say to the person** who vowed: **Is this heart,** i.e., this desire, still **upon you? If he says no, they dissolve it. Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in the name of his father: They say to the person** who vowed: **Had there been ten people who** could have **appeased you at the time, would you have made the vow? If he says no, they dissolve it.**

A *baraita* records Rabbi Yehuda's formulation: *omrim lo l'adam, lev zeh alecha* — you say to a person: is this same heart upon you? If he says no, you permit him. The *Ran* explains this similarly to Rav Huna: you are asking whether the person is simply changing their mind now, or whether they genuinely feel, in retrospect, that the vow should never have been made. "No, this is not the same heart I have now" — meaning: I am not the same person in the same state of mind who made that vow; I deeply wish it had never happened — is *charata me'ikara*, and suffices.

This leads to a practical point. The principle *ein potchin b'charata* means that a *hacham* should not begin with the *charata* question — not because *charata* never works, but because we take the language of vows seriously, and the preferred approach is to locate a *petach* that shows the person's genuine will was never fully behind the vow. *Charata* is harder — it concedes the full intentionality of the original moment and asks for release on the basis of subsequent regret. It works, but it is not where one begins.

### Rav Assi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּדוּ תָּהֵית? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, וְשַׁרְיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there was **a certain** person **who came before Rabbi Asi** to request dissolution of a vow. Rabbi Asi **said to him: Do you have regret? He said to him** rhetorically: **No,** do I not have regret? In other words, certainly I have regret. **And he dissolved** the vow **for him.**

Rav Assi asked a person who came before him: *kiduta shita* — are you astounded at what you originally did? The person said no — meaning, effectively, of course, how could I not be regretful of it? — and Rav Assi released him. The *Ran* notes the somewhat puzzling idiom: the person answers in the negative to what seems to be a question whose affirmative answer would grant release. The *Ran* suggests it was a rhetorical formulation — "am I *not* astounded?" — but in any event the substance is clear: this is another formulation aimed at eliciting *charata me'ikara*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בָּעֵית נָדוּר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילּוּ לָא מַרְגְּזִין לִי לָא בָּעֵינַן כְּלוּם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּהֵא כְּבָעֵית.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, there was **a certain** person **who came before Rabbi Elazar,** and Rabbi Elazar **said to him: Did you want to vow?** Was this really your desire? **He said to** Rabbi Elazar: **If they had not angered me, I would not have wanted anything. He said to him: Let it be like you want,** and the vow is dissolved.

Someone came before Rabbi Eliezer, who asked: *be'ita nadur?* — do you want your vow to remain? The person replied: *ilu lo margizin li, lo be'inan klum* — had they not angered me at that time, I would never have wanted any of this. Rabbi Eliezer said: *tehei k'va'is* — let it be as you wished. The vow is released, because you only made it in anger and genuinely wish those circumstances had never arisen.

### Woman & Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּאַדַּרְתַּהּ לִבְרַתַּהּ, אֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אָמַר לַהּ: אִילּוּ הֲוָה יָדְעַתְּ דְּאָמְרָן מְגֵירָתִיךְ עֲלַהּ דִּבְרַתִּךְ:
>
> **Translation:** In another instance, there was **a certain woman who took a vow with regard to her daughter** that the daughter may not benefit from her, and she **came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** to dissolve the vow. **He said to her: Had you known that your neighbors would say about your daughter:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילּוּ לָא חֲמָאת בָּהּ אִימַּהּ מִילִּין דַּעֲזִיבָה — בִּכְדִי לָא אַדַּרְתַּהּ! מִי אַדַּרְתַּהּ? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לָא, וְשַׁרְיַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Had her mother not seen inappropriate [*aziva*] matters** or behavior **in her** that should be stopped, **she would not have taken a vow with regard to her for nothing;** had you known that the neighbors would say that, **would you have taken a vow with regard to her? She said to him: No, and he dissolved** the vow **for her.**

A final case: a woman had made her daughter forbidden to benefit from her — a severe vow of estrangement. She came before Rabbi Yochanan to be released. Rabbi Yochanan asked her: *ilu avita yadat d'amrin megirataikh, alu d'bartakh, ilu lo chama bat imma milin d'aziva, bechdi lo adarta* — had you known that your neighbors would say, "Look at her daughter — if her own mother made such a vow against her, the daughter must have done terrible things worthy of abandonment," would you have made the vow for no reason? The woman said no, and Rabbi Yochanan released her.

This is a clear case of *petach*. Had the woman stopped to think through the social implications of her vow — what it would signal to the neighbors about her daughter's character — she would never have made it. She was acting on anger without fully thinking through all the consequences. The *petach* here is not an internal circumstance she failed to consider but an external social reality: the vow itself, once made, would broadcast a devastating statement about her daughter to the world. That unconsidered implication is the *petach* through which Rabbi Yochanan opens the release. And it is a perfect illustration of the paradigm: you did not fully mean it, because had you fully thought it through, you would not have done it.
