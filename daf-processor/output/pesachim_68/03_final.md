# Pesachim 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ba'al Keri Status

### Three Levels Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַפֶּסַח בְּשַׁבָּת — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מַחְיִּיב, וַחֲכָמִים מְפַטְּרִים. הַדָּלִק אֶת הַנֵּר, הַטּוֹחֵן, הַטִּיגוּן וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA. If one **slaughters the Paschal lamb on Shabbat**, **Rabbi Eliezer obligates** a sin offering for the desecration of Shabbat; **and the Sages exempt** him, as the obligation to slaughter the Paschal lamb overrides Shabbat restrictions. The mishna continues with other examples of creative acts that Rabbi Eliezer permits on Shabbat for the sake of the Paschal lamb: **kindling a fire, grinding** grain, **roasting** the lamb, etc.

Today's *daf* is 68, and we begin with literally the last word on 67b. The discussion concerns the *ba'al keri*, and we arrive at it through a chain of digressions: the *korban Pesach* brought on *Shabbat* led us to the question of the *Pesach* brought *bi-tumah*, which in turn brought us to the *pasuk* of *ish ish ki yiheyeh tamei la-nefesh* — that an individual who is *tamei* from corpse-*tumah* brings *Pesach Sheni*, while a *tzibur* in that state brings the *Pesach Rishon*. That discussion then opened into an analysis of the different levels of *tumah* and their corresponding camp restrictions.

The fundamental structure is a three-way hierarchy. *Tamei meis* is the most familiar form of *tumah*, yet paradoxically it is the least severe for purposes of camp exclusion. More severe are the *zav* and the *metzora*, whose *tumah* emanates from their own bodies — *tumah yotzei mi-gufo* — and it is this quality, not mere severity, that governs how far they must be distanced from the *Beis HaMikdash*. The resulting division: a *tamei meis* must leave the *Mikdash* precincts but may remain on *Har HaBayit*, which corresponds to *Machaneh Leviyah*; a *zav* must leave *Machaneh Leviyah* entirely but may remain within *Yerushalayim*, which is *Machaneh Yisrael*; and a *metzora* must be expelled even from there.

### Ba'al Keri Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חוּמְרֵיהּ דִּמְצוֹרָע מִזָּב? שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן פְּרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה, וְאָסוּר בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה. אַדְּרַבָּה זָב חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וּמְטַמֵּא כְּלִי חֶרֶס בְּהֶיסֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the stringency of a leper over a *zav*? That** a confirmed leper **is required to let his hair grow and rend his garments and he is prohibited from marital relations,** none of which applies to a *zav*. But **on the contrary,** there is room to say that the legal status of **a *zav* is** more **severe, for** a *zav* **imparts ritual impurity to that upon which he lies or sits** even if he does not come into direct contact with it, **and he imparts ritual impurity to an earthenware vessel through movement.**

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* asks: where does the *ba'al keri* fit? On one hand, his *tumah* issues from his own body, making him qualitatively similar to the *zav*. On the other hand, his *tumah* lasts only one day, making him far more lenient. The question is whether he belongs in the *zav* category — and is therefore excluded from *Har HaBayit* — or whether he is treated like a *tamei meis* and permitted on *Har HaBayit*. *Rabbi Yochanan* had stated that a *ba'al keri* is like a *zav* for this purpose, but this ruling is now subjected to challenge.

### Boel Niddah Equation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹעֵל נִדָּה כִּטְמֵא מֵת. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְטוּמְאָתָם, הַאי טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ, וְהַאי טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ. אֶלָּא לָאו, לְמַחֲנוֹתָם.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the continuation of the previously cited mishna that the law governing **one who had relations with a menstruating woman is like** the law governing **one who is ritually impure due to a corpse. With regard to what** did they formulate this comparison? **If we say** they made the comparison with regard to **their impurity,** that they are both impure for seven days, **impurity for seven days is** explicitly **written about this one,** someone who had relations with a menstruating woman, **and impurity for seven days is** explicitly **written about that one,** someone who is impure due to a corpse, and so there would be no need for the mishna to inform us of these laws. **Rather, is it not** that they are compared with regard to **their camps,** to teach that one who had relations with a menstruating woman is sent out from the same camp as someone ritually impure due to a corpse?

The *Gemara* raises a *meitivei* from the conclusion of a *braita*: *boel niddah ke-tamei meis* — a man who has relations with a *niddah* is equated to one who has contracted corpse-*tumah*. The *Gemara* asks: for what *halacha* does this equation apply? If the intent is merely to equate their degree of *tumah*, that point is superfluous, since both already carry a seven-day *tumah* by explicit Torah law. The equation must therefore be teaching something about their camp restrictions: a *boel niddah* shares the same camp status as a *tamei meis*. Since his *tumah* does not originate within his own body but is contracted through external contact, he belongs in the least restrictive category and may even enter *Machaneh Leviyah* — he is only excluded from the *Mikdash* itself.

From this conclusion about the *seifa* of the *braita*, the *Gemara* reasons: since the end of the *braita* speaks about camp restrictions, the beginning of that same *braita* — which equated the *ba'al keri* to a *zav* — must also be speaking about camps, meaning the *ba'al keri* must leave *Machaneh Leviyah* and is forbidden on *Har HaBayit*. A sharp question immediately arises: if a *boel niddah* is in the least-restrictive category, but one who is *boel niddah* has presumably also become a *ba'al keri*, would that not place him in the more restrictive *zav* category as well? The answer is that it is possible in principle to be a *boel niddah* without becoming a *ba'al keri*, and the *braita* presumably addresses that case.

### Boel Niddah Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּסֵיפָא לְמַחֲנוֹתָם הָוֵי — רֵישָׁא נָמֵי לְמַחֲנוֹתָם! מִידֵּי אִירְיָא?! הָא כִּדְאִיתָא וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And from the** fact that the comparison in **the latter clause** of the mishna relates to **their camps,** it stands to reason that the comparison in **the earlier clause** of that same mishna between one who experienced a seminal emission and one who became ritually impure through contact with a creeping animal **also** relates to **their camps,** and not as we explained it earlier. The Gemara rejects this argument: **Are** the cases **comparable? This** case is **as it is, and that** case is **as it is;** each part of the mishna formulates its own comparison.

The *Gemara* rejects this line of reasoning with *midi irya*: the two halves of the *braita* need not serve the same legal function. Just because the *seifa* addresses camp restrictions does not compel us to read the *reisha* as doing the same. Each clause stands on its own.

### Ba'al Keri Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מְצוֹרָע חָמוּר מִזָּב, וְזָב חָמוּר מִטְּמֵא מֵת, יָצָא בַּעַל קֶרִי שֶׁטְּמֵא מֵת חָמוּר מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from that which was taught: The legal status of **a leper is more severe than a *zav* and** the legal status of **a *zav* is more severe than one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse,** with regard to which camps they are prohibited to enter; **to the exclusion of a person who experienced a seminal emission, for** the legal status of **one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse is more severe than his** status.

The *Gemara* presses further. A second *braita* states: *metzora chamur mi-zav*, *v'zav chamur mi-tamei meis* — the *metzora* is stricter than the *zav*, and the *zav* is stricter than the *tamei meis* — clearly articulating the camp hierarchy. The *braita* then adds: *yatzah ba'al keri*, the *ba'al keri* is excluded, because *tamei meis chamur mimenu* — a *tamei meis* is even stricter than he is.

### Qualitative Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי יָצָא? לָאו: יָצָא מִכְּלַל זָב וּבָא לִכְּלַל טְמֵא מֵת, דְּהָא טְמֵא מֵת חָמוּר מִמֶּנּוּ — וּמוּתָּר בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה! לֹא, יָצָא מִמַּחֲנֵה טְמֵא מֵת וְנִכְנַס לְמַחֲנֵה זָב, וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּטְמֵא מֵת חָמוּר מִמֶּנּוּ (דְּמוּתָּר) בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה — לְמַאי דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wishes to clarify this enigmatic statement: **What** is the meaning of the words, **to the exclusion** of a person who experienced a seminal emission? **Is** the intent **not** that **he is excluded from the category of a *zav* and enters the category of one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse, for** the legal status of **one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted **by a corpse is more severe than his** status, as his impurity lasts for seven days, **and** nonetheless **he is permitted in the Levite camp.** We should therefore learn from here that someone who experienced a seminal emission is also permitted in the Levite camp. The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** the intent is that one who experienced a seminal emission is **excluded from the camp of one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse, and he enters the camp of a *zav*,** meaning that he is excluded from the Levite camp, just like a *zav*. **And although** the legal status of **one who is ritually impure** with impurity imparted by **a corpse is more severe than his** status **and** he **is permitted in the Levite camp,** and so it would seem that one who experienced a seminal emission should similarly be permitted, nonetheless, **we compare him to that to which he is similar.** The ritual impurity of one who experienced a seminal emission is fundamentally similar to that of a *zav*, and different from that of one who is ritually impure due to a corpse.

The *Gemara* rejects this reading and proposes an alternative. The word *yatzah* need not mean he is excluded from the *zav* category into a more lenient one; it can mean he is excluded from the *tamei meis* category despite being even more lenient than a *tamei meis*. The exception runs in the other direction: your instinct would have been to place him with *tamei meis* since he is so mild, but the *braita* tells you no — *bnichnas lemachaneh zav*, he enters the *zav* category. Why? *Ve'af al gav detamei meis chamur mimenu* — even though a *tamei meis* is more severe in terms of duration, the *ba'al keri* is nonetheless an exception to the severity-based hierarchy, because the operative principle is *le-mai de-dami lei midaminan lei* — we categorize him according to what he is most similar to qualitatively, not according to where he falls in the severity ranking. Since his *tumah*, like that of the *zav*, issues from his own body, he is classified with the *zav* — and is therefore forbidden on *Har HaBayit* — regardless of the fact that in terms of duration his *tumah* is far milder.

This leaves the *Gemara* without a clean resolution, since the argument about classification based on qualitative similarity rather than severity is offered as a possible reading but not unanimously established.

### Rav Yitzchak Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: ״וְיָצָא אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ — זוֹ מַחֲנֵה שְׁכִינָה. ״לֹא יָבֹא אֶל תּוֹךְ הַמַּחֲנֶה״ — זוֹ מַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה. מִכָּאן לְבַעַל קֶרִי שֶׁיֵּצֵא חוּץ לִשְׁתֵּי מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **A *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi:** "If there be among you a man that is impure by reason of a nocturnal occurrence, he shall go outside the camp, he shall not come within the camp" (Deuteronomy 23:11). This verse may be expounded as follows: **"He shall go outside the camp," this is the camp of the Divine Presence. "He shall not come within the camp," this is the Levite camp. From here** we derive that **a person who experienced a seminal emission must go out from two camps.**

A *tana* taught before *Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi*, still addressing the question of which camps the *ba'al keri* must leave. The relevant *pasuk*, *ish asher lo yiheyeh tahor mikreh laylah*, explicitly concerns a *ba'al keri*. The verse continues: *veyatza michutz lamachaneh* — he goes out of the camp — *lo yavo el toch hamachaneh* — he shall not enter inside the camp. The *braita* reads these two clauses as referring to two different camps: *veyatza michutz lamachaneh, zo machaneh Shechinah* — he must leave the camp of the *Shechinah*, meaning the *Mikdash* precinct; *lo yavo el toch hamachaneh, zo machaneh Leviyah* — and he must not enter *Machaneh Leviyah* either. The conclusion: *mikan le-va'al keri she-yatza chutz li-shtei machanot* — the *ba'al keri* must be outside both camps, supporting *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position.

### Ejection Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַכַּתִּי לָא עַיֵּילְתֵּיהּ, אַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yitzchak **said to** the *tanna*: Something is amiss in the wording of this *baraita*: **You have not yet brought him in** and **you** already **send him out?**

When the *tana* presented this *braita*, *Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi* challenged him: *akti lo ayiltei apiktei* — you haven't even brought him in yet, and you're taking him out? The point of the challenge is this: if the first clause says he must leave *Machaneh Shechinah*, then the second clause — "do not enter the camp" — cannot logically refer to *Machaneh Leviyah*, because he has only been ejected from *Machaneh Shechinah*. He has not yet been expelled from *Machaneh Leviyah*, so there is nothing to prohibit him from re-entering. For a prohibition of entry to make sense, he must first have been ejected from that very camp. The *braita* as originally read is therefore internally incoherent.

### Two Camps Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵימָא: ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ — זוֹ מַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה. ״לָא יָבֹא אֶל תּוֹךְ הַמַּחֲנֶה״ — זוֹ מַחֲנֵה שְׁכִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** emend the *baraita* and **say** that it reads as follows: **"Outside the camp," this is the Levite camp. "He shall not come within the camp," this is the camp of the Divine Presence.**

The resolution proposed is to reverse the order in which the clauses are matched to the camps. *Michutz lamachaneh, zo machaneh Leviyah* — the first clause, the positive obligation to leave, applies to *Machaneh Leviyah*; *lo yavo el toch hamachaneh, zo machaneh Shechinah* — the prohibition of entry applies to *Machaneh Shechinah*. This reading is both logically coherent and halachically significant. Once he has left *Machaneh Leviyah*, the prohibition of not entering applies specifically to *Machaneh Shechinah*, the more interior precinct. The practical consequence: remaining in *Machaneh Leviyah* transgresses the positive commandment of *veyatza*, while actually entering *Machaneh Shechinah* additionally violates the negative prohibition of *lo yavo*. Thus the *ba'al keri* is subject to two distinct prohibitions corresponding to two different camps.

### Ravina Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: אֵימָא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לְמַחֲנֵה שְׁכִינָה, וְלַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: ״וְיָצָא אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה וְלֹא יָבֹא אֶל תּוֹךְ״, ״הַמַּחֲנֶה״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina strongly objects to** this exposition of the verse: **Say** that both **this and that** refer **to the camp of the Divine Presence,** that he must leave the camp of the Divine Presence and may not enter it; and the repetition comes to teach **that he violates a positive** command to leave the camp **and a negative** command barring entry into the camp. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse say** only: **"He shall go outside the camp, he shall not come within,"** or "within it." **Why do I** need the repetition of the word **"the camp"?**

*Ravina* objected to this entire framework. In his view, both clauses of the *pasuk* refer to the same camp — *Machaneh Shechinah* — and the reason the Torah states the matter twice is not to identify two different camps but to establish both a positive and a negative commandment applying to the single prohibition of entering *Machaneh Shechinah*. This minimalist reading is consistent with the principle of *tafasta merubah lo tafasta*: when in doubt, read the *pasuk* as narrowly as possible.

### Repetition Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Conclude from this** that it is **to give him a different camp;** it refers not to the same camp that he left but rather to a different one.

The *Gemara* responds that the word *hamachaneh* appears twice in the verse. If the intent were only to establish a positive and negative commandment regarding one camp, the Torah could have written *veyeitzei el michutz lamachaneh velo yavo el tokhah* — using a single instance of the word. The repetition of *hamachaneh* as a full noun implies that a second, distinct camp is being invoked. The conclusion: the *ba'al keri* is at minimum forbidden in *Machaneh Leviyah* by virtue of the positive commandment of *veyatza*, with the additional negative prohibition applying to *Machaneh Shechinah*.

### Har HaBayit Practice

It should be noted that the only *chiyuv karet* applies when any *tamei* individual — regardless of which category of *tumah* — actually enters *Machaneh Shechinah*, meaning the *azarah* precinct of the *Mikdash*. All of the other restrictions are of lesser severity. A *metzora* entering *Yerushalayim* violates at most a *lo ta'aseh*, according to some opinions only an *asseh*. A *zav* ascending *Har HaBayit* violates a *lo ta'aseh* without *karet*. And the *ba'al keri*'s status on *Har HaBayit* is itself debated: whether he is prohibited at all, as *Rabbi Yochanan* holds, and if so, whether the prohibition is a *lo ta'aseh* or merely an *asseh*, remains unresolved by the *Gemara*.

These questions carry direct practical relevance for those who ascend *Har HaBayit* today. Even those who believe they can navigate around the approximate location of the *makom hamikdash* — and are therefore unconcerned about the *chiyuv karet* that applies to a *tamei* entering the *azarah* — must still contend with these questions. A woman who is a *niddah* is certainly in the more stringent category and is forbidden. For a man who is a *ba'al keri*, the *Gemara*'s unresolved debate means that tour organizers would need to ensure participants either refrained or immersed in a *mikvah* beforehand. But even immersion creates a further complication: the status of a *tvul yom* of a *ba'al keri* — one who has immersed but whose *tumah* does not end until nightfall — may not be fully equivalent to taharah, and thus the prohibition may persist even after immersion. Beyond that, there is a further question of *bi'ah bemiktzat* — whether partial entry, such as extending a hand into the wall, constitutes a prohibited act — and disagreement about precisely where *Har HaBayit* begins, whether at the inner or outer face of the surrounding wall. These compounding uncertainties make the practical *halacha* of ascending *Har HaBayit* considerably more complex than is sometimes appreciated.

One broader question that the *Gemara* does not address is worth noting. If a *ba'al keri* is forbidden in *Machaneh Leviyah*, it is difficult to understand how the *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* in the *midbar*, who resided within *Machaneh Leviyah*, could have maintained conjugal relations with their wives. Any seminal emission — whether through relations or otherwise — would have immediately required them to exit the camp until they could immerse in a *mikvah* and wait out the day. Given the further question of whether a *tvul yom* of a *ba'al keri* may re-enter, it is not obvious how they could have returned home even after immersing.

## Shabbat Acts Pesach

### Michui Kreivim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִיחוּי קְרָבָיו וְכוּ׳. מַאי מִיחוּי קְרָבָיו? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: שֶׁמְּנַקְּבָן בְּסַכִּין. (רַב) חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: שִׁירְקָא דִמְעִיָּיא דְּנָפְקָא אַגַּב דּוּחְקָא דְסַכִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **cleaning the intestines** of the Paschal lamb overrides Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **What is** meant by **cleaning the intestines? Rav Huna said:** It means **that he punctures them with a knife** allowing the excrement to exit. **Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav said:** It refers to the removal of **the secretions of the intestine, which come out through the pressure of the knife** and would ruin the entire sacrifice and cause it to become putrid were they allowed to remain in the intestines.

Returning now to the *Mishnah* concerning what may be done on *Shabbat* for the sake of the *korban Pesach*, one of the permitted acts is the cleansing of the intestines — *michui kreivim* — the removal of fecal matter from the intestinal lining. This is required because the *eimurim*, the sacrificial portions, cannot be offered in a state of contamination from their contents. The *Gemara* asks: what exactly does this *michui kreivim* involve, and where lies the boundary between the permitted cleansing and the prohibited rinsing in water?

### Rav Huna vs Chiyya

*Rav Huna* says the method involves making a hole in the intestine with a knife — *shemenakvin be-sakin* — and through that incision squeezing out the digested contents. *Chiyya bar Rav* describes it differently: *shirka damei aya d'nafka agav duchka d'sakina* — the liquids and matter of the intestines that come out through the pressure exerted by the knife, without necessarily puncturing through. The practical difference is subtle but significant. According to *Rav Huna*, the knife is used as a cutting instrument to create an opening; according to *Chiyya bar Rav*, it is used as a pressing implement, with the knife's edge or flat providing the force needed to expel the contents. The reason rinsing in water remains forbidden while this mechanical method is permitted is presumably that the cleansing by knife pressure is the minimum necessary act of *tzorech hakravah* — preparation required for the *korban* itself — whereas rinsing would constitute an additional, separable *melacha* that could have been performed before *Shabbat*.

### Isaiah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר): מַאי טַעְמָא דְּחִיָּיא בַּר רַב? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְחׇרְבוֹת מֵחִים גָּרִים יֹאכֵלוּ״, מַאי מַשְׁמַע? כְּדִמְתַרְגֵּם רַב יוֹסֵף ״וְנִכְסֵיהֹן דְּרַשִּׁיעַיָּא צַדִּיקַיָּא יַחְסְנוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer said: What is Ḥiyya bar Rav's reason** for explaining the term in this manner? **As it is written:** "Then shall the lambs feed as in their pasture, **and the ruins of the fat ones [*meḥim*] shall wanderers eat"** (Isaiah 5:17). **From where may it be inferred** that this verse is in any way connected to our discussion? **As Rav Yosef translates** this verse: **"And the righteous shall inherit the possessions of the wicked."**

*Rabbi Elazar* asks: what is the source that lends support to the view of *Chiyya bar Rav*, that it is specifically the pressure of a knife that forces out the contents? He cites the *pasuk* in *Yeshayahu*: *v'charvot mechim garim yocheilu*, and explains it using the *targum* of *Rav Yosef*: *u-nichsei d'rasha'aya tzadika ya-achasnun* — the property of the wicked the righteous will inherit. The word *garim* is thus read as referring to the righteous, and *charvot mechim* — the sword of the *mechim* — identifies the disgusting material driven out by the blade. *Rashi* explains that *garim* evokes *Klal Yisrael* as sojourners in this world, somehow identifying them with the righteous who will inherit the substance of the wicked.

## Techiyat HaMetim

### Righteous Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְרָעוּ כְבָשִׂים כְּדׇבְרָם״, אָמַר מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַב: כַּמְדוּבָּר בָּם. מַאי כַּמְדוּבָּר בָּם? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״וְחׇרְבוֹת מֵחִים גָּרִים יֹאכֵלוּ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא אִי כְּתִיב ״חׇרְבוֹת״ — כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״וְחׇרְבוֹת״, מִילְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** Having explained the latter part of the verse in Isaiah, the Gemara turns to the beginning of that same verse. **"Then shall the lambs feed as in their pasture** [***kedavram*]." Menashya bar Yirmeya said that Rav said: As was said about them** [***kamedubar bam*],** i.e., as the prophet promised. To **what** prophecy does the verse refer with the expression **"as was said about them"? Abaye said:** It is referring to the continuation of the verse: **"And the ruins of the fat ones shall wanderers eat." Rava said to him** that this cannot be: **Granted, were it written** only **"the ruins** of the fat ones," it would be possible to explain **as you said. Now that it is written "and the ruins,"** with the addition of the word "and," this indicates that **it states something else,**

The discussion of that verse in *Yeshayahu* draws the *Gemara* into a substantial *excursus* on *techiyat ha-metim* and the power that will be granted to the righteous in the future. The *pasuk* continues: *v'ra'u kvassim k'dabram* — the flock will graze as has been spoken of them. *Rav Nachman bar Yirmeya* in the name of *Rav* interprets *k'medubar bam* — "as has been spoken about them" — as a reference to the second half of that very verse. *Abaye* elaborates: the flock, which is *Yisrael*, will receive what has been spoken to them, namely the inheritance of the wealth of the wicked described in the *seifa*.

*Rava* objects. If the word had been written *charvot* without a *vav*, one could read the second half of the *pasuk* as completing the thought of the first half. But since it is written *v'charvot* — with a *vav* — it introduces an independent new statement: *milta chadta hi deka'amar*, the second clause is a separate point entirely. The first clause must therefore be interpreted independently.

### Rav Hananel Resurrection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כִּדְרַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב. דְּאָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב: עֲתִידִין צַדִּיקִים שֶׁיְּחַיּוּ אֶת הַמֵּתִים. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְרָעוּ כְבָשִׂים כְּדׇבְרָם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״יִרְעוּ בָשָׁן וְגִלְעָד כִּימֵי עוֹלָם״. בָּשָׁן — זֶה אֱלִישָׁע הַבָּא מִן הַבָּשָׁן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיַעְנַי וְשָׁפָט בַּבָּשָׁן״, וּכְתִיב: ״פֹּה אֱלִישָׁע בֶּן שָׁפָט אֲשֶׁר יָצַק מַיִם עַל יְדֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ״. גִּלְעָד — זֶה אֵלִיָּהוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלִיָּהוּ הַתִּשְׁבִּי מִתּוֹשָׁבֵי גִלְעָד וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** This verse should be understood **in accordance with** what **Rav Ḥananel said that Rav said. For Rav Ḥananel said that Rav said: In the future, the righteous will resurrect the dead. It is written here: "Then shall the lambs feed [*vera'u*] as in their pasture,"** the lambs serving as an allusion to the righteous, **and it is written there:** "Tend your people with your staff, the flock of your heritage, who dwell alone in the wood, in the midst of Carmel; **let them feed [*yiru*] in Bashan and Gilad as in the days of old"** (Micah 7:14). **"Bashan" is** an allusion to the prophet **Elisha, who came from the Bashan.** How do we know that Elisha came from Bashan? **As it is stated:** "Joel the chief, and Shafam the next, **and Yanai and Shafat in the Bashan"** (I Chronicles 5:12), **and it is written: "Here is Elisha ben Shafat who poured water on the hands of Elijah"** (II Kings 3:11). **"Gilad" is** an allusion to **Elijah, as it is stated: "And Elijah the Tishbite, who was of the inhabitants of Gilad, said"** (I Kings 17:1).

*Rava* therefore invokes the tradition of *Rav Chananel* in the name of *Rav*: *atidin tzadikim sheyichyu et hametim* — in the future, the righteous will have the power to resurrect the dead. The verse *v'ra'u kvassim k'dabram* is read by means of a *gezeira shava* connecting *vera'u* here to *yir'u Bashan vegilad kimei olam* in the prophet *Micah* — that *Bashan* and *Gilad* will shepherd as in the days of old. *Bashan* is identified as *Elisha*, *haba min ha-Bashan*, who came from the locale of *Bashan*, as established by the connection between the *pasuk* in *Melachim* placing *Shafat* in *Bashan* and the identification of *Elisha ben Shafat* as the attendant who poured water over the hands of *Eliyahu*. *Gilad* is *Eliyahu*, as the verse says *Eliyahu ha-Tishbi mi-toshavei Gilad*. The verse in *Micah* therefore promises that as *Eliyahu* and *Elisha* shepherded — meaning as they brought back the dead in the days of old — so will this power be restored.

### Resurrection Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: עֲתִידִים צַדִּיקִים שֶׁיְּחַיּוּ מֵתִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עוֹד יֵשְׁבוּ זְקֵנִים וּזְקֵנוֹת בִּרְחֹבוֹת יְרוּשָׁלִָים וְאִישׁ מִשְׁעַנְתּוֹ בְּיָדוֹ מֵרֹב יָמִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְשַׂמְתָּ מִשְׁעַנְתִּי עַל פְּנֵי הַנָּעַר״.
>
> **Translation:** This idea is derived from a different source as well. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said that Rabbi Yonatan said: In the future the righteous will resurrect the dead, as it is stated: "Old men and old women shall yet again dwell in the streets of Jerusalem, and every man with his staff in his hand for very age"** (Zechariah 8:4). **And** the staff will then be used as it was used by Gehazi when Elisha sent him to bring the son of the Shunamite woman back to life, as **it is written: "And you shall lay my staff on the face of the child"** (II Kings 4:29).

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* in the name of *Rabbi Yonatan* extends this further: *atidin tzadikim sheyichyu metim* — not only *Eliyahu* and *Elisha*, but the *tzadikim* in general will in the future have the power to resurrect the dead. The source is the *pasuk* in *Zechariah*: *od yeshvu zkenim u-zkenot birchovot Yerushalayim, v'ish mishanto beyado merov yamim* — elder men and women will sit in the streets of *Yerushalayim*, each one with a staff in hand from great old age. The connection: we know from the episode of *Elisha* — *v'shamta mishanti al pnei ha-na'ar* — that a staff can serve as the instrument of *techiyat ha-metim*. Since the *pasuk* in *Zechariah* says every *zaken* and *zkenah* will hold such a staff, the implication is that every *tzadik*, or at least every elder, will possess this power to resurrect the dead.

### Death Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״בִּלַּע הַמָּוֶת לָנֶצַח״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הַנַּעַר בֶּן מֵאָה שָׁנָה יָמוּת״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, כָּאן בַּגּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** As we have been discussing the world of the future and the resurrection of the dead, the Gemara cites additional statements on these topics. **Ulla raised a contradiction** between two verses: In one verse **it is written: "He will destroy death forever,** and the Lord God will wipe away tears from off all faces, and the insult of His people shall He take away from off all the earth; for the Lord has spoken" (Isaiah 25:8). **And** in another verse **it is written:** "There shall be no more there an infant who lives a few days, nor an old man who has not filled his days; **for the youngest shall die a hundred years old"** (Isaiah 65:20), implying that people will live long lives, but death will not be totally eradicated. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: Here,** in the first verse, it is referring to **Jews,** who will not die at all, while **there,** in the second verse, it is referring to **gentiles,** who will live exceedingly long lives but eventually die.

*Ulla* raised a contradiction between two verses. One verse states *bila hamavet la-netzach* — death will cease forever in the future time. A second verse states *ki ha-na'ar ben me'ah shanah yamut* — that even a young death will occur at a hundred years, implying that death continues to exist, merely postponed to very old age.

### Israel vs Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגוֹיִם מַאי בָּעוּ הָתָם? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעָמְדוּ זָרִים וְרָעוּ צֹאנְכֶם וּבְנֵי נֵכָר אִכָּרֵיכֶם וְכֹרְמֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are gentiles doing there** in the future world? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And strangers shall stand and feed your flocks, and the sons of the alien shall be your plowmen and your vineyard workers"** (Isaiah 61:5).

The *Gemara* then asks: what are non-Jews doing in the *Olam HaBa* at all? The answer is drawn from the *pasuk* in *Yeshayahu*: *v'amdu zarim v'ra'u tzonechem, u-vnei nechar ikareichem v'charmeichem* — the foreigners will shepherd the flocks of *Yisrael* and tend their fields and vineyards. The non-Jews will be present in a functional, servile capacity in that future world, and while they will live extremely long lives, it is *Yisrael* that will live forever.

This is a striking *Gemara*, and it is worth pausing on its broader implication. When the *Gemara* says that the *tzadikim* will perform *techiyat ha-metim*, it is making a remarkable claim about agency. Whether this means that the event we call *techiyat ha-metim* — the general resurrection — will be executed by the *tzadikim*, or whether it means that after the initial resurrection, the ongoing power over life and death will reside with the righteous, is not entirely clear. It may mean that following the resurrection, death will no longer be absolute — if someone dies, a *tzadik* can restore them. The *Gemara*'s claim in either case is arresting: the ultimate mastery over life and death will pass into human hands, or at least into the hands of the righteous. In one reading, this is a statement about the fullness of reward that the *tzadikim* will receive; in another, it carries the more radical implication that even *techiyat ha-metim*, which we associate entirely with divine power, will in some sense come from within. The connection to the earlier discussion is beautiful: where the earlier portion of the *daf* dealt with bodily *tumah* creating distance from the *Mikdash* and from the divine presence — where access to God is constrained by circumstances beyond one's control — here we arrive at a vision in which even life and death become subject to human agency.

### Luminaries Brightness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וְחָפְרָה הַלְּבָנָה וּבוֹשָׁה הַחַמָּה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה אוֹר הַלְּבָנָה כְּאוֹר הַחַמָּה וְאוֹר הַחַמָּה יִהְיֶה שִׁבְעָתַיִם כְּאוֹר שִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda raised a contradiction** between two verses: In one verse **it is written: "Then the moon shall be confounded and the sun ashamed,** when the Lord of Hosts shall reign in Mount Zion and in Jerusalem, and before His elders will be His glory" (Isaiah 24:23), which indicates that in the future there will be no light at all from the sun or the moon. **And** elsewhere **it is written: "Moreover the light of the moon shall be as the light of the sun, and the light of the sun shall be sevenfold, as the light of seven days,** on the day that the Lord binds up the breach of His people and heals the stroke of their wound blow" (Isaiah 30:26), which indicates that the light of the sun and moon will be even brighter than before.

*Rav Chisda* raised a further contradiction. One *pasuk* states *v'chafra ha-levana u-vosha ha-chama* — the moon will be embarrassed and the sun ashamed, their light rendered meaningless before the radiance of God in the future time. A second *pasuk* states *v'haya or ha-levana k'or ha-chama, v'or ha-chama yiheyeh shivtayim k'or shivat ha-yamim* — the moon will shine with the strength of the sun, and the sun will be seven times as powerful as the light of the seven days of creation. *Rashi* understands this as forty-nine times the power of each individual day, yielding light many hundreds of times more intense than now. These two visions are in direct conflict: one depicts luminaries outshone into irrelevance, the other depicts them blazing with unprecedented brilliance.

### Mashiach vs Olam HaBa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, כָּאן לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: Here,** where it says that the sun and the moon will be ashamed before the glow of the Divine Presence, it is referring **to the World**-**to**-**Come,** which is an entirely different world; while **there,** where it says that their light will increase, it is referring **to the days of the Messiah.**

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *kan l'Olam HaBa, kan le-Yemot HaMashiach* — the verse depicting the embarrassed luminaries refers to *Olam HaBa*, while the verse depicting their extraordinary brightness refers to *Yemot HaMashiach*. The distinction is fundamental. Messianic times will be miraculous but will still involve a form of normal earthly life — people will eat, there may still be death of some kind, nature will be transformed but not transcended. The *Olam HaBa* that follows Messianic times is the complete utopia in which only the divine light obtains and the luminaries are altogether eclipsed.

This distinction is significant because for a contemporary audience the term *Olam HaBa* typically evokes the world of souls after death. Much of that reorientation was due to the *Rambam*, who emphasized the eternal life of the soul and deliberately de-emphasized miraculous earthly transformation as the content of the eschatological promise. But in the plain meaning of this *Gemara*, *Olam HaBa* is a future state of existence on this earth — the sequence runs from this world, to Messianic times, to *Olam HaBa*.

### Shmuel Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר: אֵין בֵּין הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ אֶלָּא שִׁיעְבּוּד מַלְכִיּוֹת בִּלְבַד, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּמַחֲנֵה שְׁכִינָה, כָּאן — בְּמַחֲנֵה צַדִּיקִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Shmuel, who said** that **there is no difference between this world and the days of the Messiah except for subjugation** to foreign **kingdoms,** but in all other ways, including the illumination of the celestial bodies, the world order will remain unchanged, **what is there to say** to reconcile these verses? The Gemara answers: **This and that** refer **to the World**-**to**-**Come, and** it is **not difficult: Here,** where it says that the sun and the moon will be totally ashamed, it refers to **the camp of the Divine Presence;** while **there,** where it says that the light of the sun and moon will be greatly magnified, it refers to **the camp of the righteous.**

For *Shmuel*, who held that the only difference between this world and *Yemot HaMashiach* is *shibbud malkhuyot* — the end of subjugation to foreign powers — with no miraculous transformation of nature, the resolution cannot appeal to Messianic times as the referent of the verse about brilliant luminaries. The *Gemara* therefore says: for *Shmuel*, both verses refer to *Olam HaBa*, and the contradiction is resolved differently.

### Rambam Approach

In this connection it is worth noting the *Rambam*'s famous treatment of these questions, which he discusses in his *hakdamah le-Peirush ha-Mishnayot*. The *Rambam* was deeply troubled by the amount of religious energy people invested in speculating about the details of *Yemot HaMashiach* — who one's spouse would be after resurrection, whether one would be resurrected at the age and with the infirmities one had at death, and similar questions. His view was that such speculation is religiously unproductive: we simply do not know, nobody has been there, and our obligations lie in this world.

At the same time, in *Hilchos Melachim* the *Rambam* writes — attributing it to the consensus of *Chazal* — *ein bein olam hazeh li-Yemot HaMashiach ela shibbud malkhuyot*, that the only difference between this world and Messianic times is the end of foreign domination. This is in fact the minority opinion of *Shmuel* in the *Gemara*, yet the *Rambam* presents it as the authoritative consensus, precisely because he wished to discourage miraculous expectations of Messianic times and redirect attention to present obligations.

The irony, as *Dr. David Berger* observed in an article written approximately twenty years ago, is that this attempt to demystify Messianic times had the exact opposite of its intended effect. By insisting that the *Mashiach* need perform no miracles and that the only distinguishing criterion is the end of political subjugation, the *Rambam*'s formulation made it far easier for subsequent messianic movements to advance their claims. When challenged — "the world looks exactly the same, how can this be the true redemption?" — a follower could simply reply: according to the *Rambam*, nothing miraculous is required. *Dr. Berger* identifies three major movements that exploited this dynamic: the followers of *Shabbetai Zevi*, certain elements within *Chabad*, and the religious Zionist concept of *aschalta di-ge'ulah* — the idea that the establishment of the State of Israel represents the beginning of the redemption, with *shibbud malkhuyot* progressively diminishing.

### Resurrection Healing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״אֲנִי אָמִית וַאֲחַיֶּה״, וּכְתִיב: ״מָחַצְתִּי וַאֲנִי אֶרְפָּא״. הַשְׁתָּא אַחוֹיֵי מַחֲיֵי, מְרַפֵּא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן! אֶלָּא, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מָה שֶׁאֲנִי מֵמִית — אֲנִי מְחַיֶּה, כְּמוֹ שֶׁמָּחַצְתִּי — וַאֲנִי אֶרְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a contradiction** between two parts of a verse. **It is written: "I put to death and I make live"** (Deuteronomy 32:39) **and** in that same verse **it is written: "I wound and I heal." Now** once it says that **He gives life** to the dead, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that **He** can **heal** those who are still alive? What then does the second clause add to the first? **Rather,** the second clause clarifies the first one: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Those** same people **whom I put to death I will bring to life, just as** those people **whom I wounded I will heal.**

The *Gemara* then turns to another verse. *Rava* raises a contradiction. One verse says *ani amis va-achayeh* — I will bring death and I will bring life. A second says *machatzti va-ani erpa* — I have struck down and I will heal. The challenge: if God already told us He will bring life, is it not self-evident that He can heal? Why add the anti-climactic assurance of healing after promising resurrection?

The *Gemara* answers: God is saying *mah she-ani memis ani mechayeh* — the very person whom I cause to die, I will resurrect. It is not a general statement about death and life as separate events but a promise that the identical individual who died will be restored. The parallel with the end of the verse clinches this: when the verse says "I struck down and I will heal," *healing* obviously refers to healing the same person who was struck — so too, "I will bring life" refers to restoring life to the very person who died. This is an affirmative scriptural basis for the doctrine of *techiyat ha-metim*.

### Sequential Resurrection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲנִי אָמִית וַאֲחַיֶּה״ יָכוֹל מִיתָה בְּאֶחָד וְחַיִּים בְּאֶחָד, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהָעוֹלָם נוֹהֵג, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מָחַצְתִּי וַאֲנִי אֶרְפָּא״, מָה מַכָּה וּרְפוּאָה בְּאֶחָד, אַף מִיתָה וְחַיִּים בְּאֶחָד. מִכָּאן תְּשׁוּבָה לָאוֹמְרִים אֵין תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה. דָּבָר אַחֵר: בַּתְּחִלָּה מַה שֶּׁאֲנִי מֵמִית אֲנִי מְחַיֶּה, וַהֲדַר מַה שֶּׁמָּחַצְתִּי וַאֲנִי אֶרְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **"I put to death and I make live";** one **might have thought** that this refers to **death for one** person **and life,** i.e., birth, **for another** person, **in the customary manner of the world.** Therefore, **the verse states: "I wound and I heal"; just as the wounding and the healing** mentioned here clearly refer to the **same** person, **so too death and life** refer to the **same** person. **From this** verse, there is **a refutation to those who say that there is no Torah source for the resurrection of the dead,** for it is explicitly mentioned in this verse. **Alternatively,** the verse can be explained as follows: **At first, those whom I put to death I will bring to life,** but they will be revived with the same injuries that they had when they died; **and subsequently, those whom I wounded I will heal,** meaning that their injuries will be healed after they are resurrected.

A *braita* elaborates on this point. One might have thought *ani amis va-achayeh* means I will cause one person to die and bring new life to a different person — the ordinary workings of the natural world, where some die and others are born. Therefore the verse adds *machatzti va-ani erpa*: just as wounding and healing refer to the same person, so do death and life. *Mikan teshuvah la-omrim ein techiyas ha-meitim min ha-Torah* — this is the Torah-level refutation of those who deny that resurrection of the dead is grounded in the Written Torah.

An alternative reading of the verse is then offered: *betechilah mah she-ani memis ani mechayeh* — first God will resurrect the dead; *ve-hadar mah shemachatzti va-ani erpa* — and subsequently, He will heal all the wounds and physical afflictions with which they were resurrected. The sequence is first resurrection, then healing — those who return to life bearing old wounds or missing limbs will afterward be made whole. According to this reading, the two halves of the verse describe two sequential stages of the eschatological process, both occurring in Messianic times.

## Shabbat Acts Pesach (continued)

### Blemish Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִיחוּי קְרָבָיו וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה חֲבִיבָה מִצְוָה בִּשְׁעָתָהּ, שֶׁהֲרֵי הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים וְאֵבָרִים וּפְדָרִים כְּשֵׁרִים כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה, וְאֵין מַמְתִּינִים לָהֶם עַד שֶׁתֶּחְשַׁךְ. הַרְכָּבָתוֹ וַהֲבָאָתוֹ וְכוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: חוֹתְכִין יַבֶּלֶת בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא בַּמְּדִינָה. וְאִם בִּכְלִי — כָּאן וְכָאן אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that when the eve of Passover occurs on Shabbat, **burning the fats** of the Paschal lamb overrides Shabbat. The Gemara notes that **it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **Rabbi Shimon said: Come and see how dear is a mitzva** performed **in its** proper **time. For burning the fats and limbs and inner fats is valid all night** and it would have been possible to wait until the conclusion of Shabbat and burn them at night, but nonetheless **we do not wait with them until nightfall;** rather, we burn them immediately, even on Shabbat. The mishna also taught that **carrying** the Paschal lamb through a public domain, **bringing it** from outside the Shabbat limit and cutting off its wart do not override Shabbat. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another mishna in tractate *Eiruvin*, which teaches: **One may cut off a wart** by hand on Shabbat **in the Temple but not in** the rest of **the country** outside the Temple. **And if** the wart is to be removed **with an instrument, it is forbidden** both **here,** in the Temple, **and there,** outside the Temple.

Returning to the *Mishnah* on what may be done on *Shabbat* for the *korban Pesach*: a *braita* in *Eruvin* teaches that for any *korban* brought to the *Mikdash* on *Shabbat*, one may cut off a blemish — a *mum* such as a wart or hanging piece of skin — on *Shabbat*, because the principle *ein shevut ba-Mikdash* applies: rabbinic prohibitions are not observed when they conflict with the needs of the *Mikdash* service. The same *braita* continues: *aval lo ba-medinah* — but outside the *Mikdash*, this is not permitted. And if done with a vessel — a knife or similar implement — it is forbidden in both locations.

The *Mishnah* under discussion prohibits cutting off such a blemish in terms that appear to be absolute. This creates an apparent contradiction with the *braita* that allows it within the *Mikdash* by hand.

### Hand vs Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא, חַד אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בַּיָּד; הָא בְּלַחָה, הָא בִּיבֵשָׁה. וְחַד אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּלַחָה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בַּיָּד, הָא בִּכְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** Two *amora'im*, **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina,** disagreed about how to resolve this contradiction. **One** of them **said:** Both **this** mishna in *Pesaḥim* **and that** mishna in *Eiruvin* speak of cutting off the wart **by hand. This** mishna that forbids cutting it off refers to **a moist** wart, which is considered like the flesh of the animal. It is therefore prohibited by rabbinic decree to cut off the wart; and since it could have been removed before Shabbat, the decree applies even in the Temple, where rabbinic decrees are generally not applicable. **That** mishna that permits cutting it off refers to **a dry** wart, which breaks apart by itself, and so there is no prohibition even by rabbinic decree to cut it off. **And** the other **one said:** Both **this** mishna **and that** mishna speak of cutting off **a moist** wart, **and** it is **not difficult. This** mishna that says it is permitted talks about removing the wart **by hand,** which is prohibited only by a rabbinic decree that was not applied to the Temple; whereas **that** mishna that says it is prohibited talks about removing the wart **with an instrument,** which is prohibited by Torah law and forbidden everywhere.

Two *Amoraim*, *Rabbi Elazar* and *Rabbi Yose bar Chanina*, each offer a resolution, and they disagree with one another.

The first opinion holds: *idi ve-idi beyad* — both the *Mishnah* and the *braita* are speaking about doing the act by hand, without a vessel. The difference is *ha belacha ha beyavesha* — the *braita* permits it when the blemish is dry, no longer physiologically integrated into the body, while the *Mishnah* prohibits it when the blemish is still moist and genuinely part of the body. According to this view, two criteria must be met to permit the act even within the *Mikdash*: using one's hand rather than a vessel, and the blemish being dried out.

The second opinion holds: *idi ve-idi belacha* — both are speaking about a moist blemish. The difference is *ha beyad ha be-kli* — the *braita* permits it when done by hand within the *Mikdash*, while the *Mishnah* prohibits it when done with a vessel, inside or outside the *Mikdash*. According to this view, only one criterion governs: if done by hand, it is permitted even when moist; if done with a vessel, it is forbidden everywhere.

### Difficulty with First Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר: הָא בַּיָּד, הָא בִּכְלִי — מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בַּיָּד, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּלַחָה הָא בִּיבֵשָׁה? אָמַר לָךְ: יְבֵשָׁה — מִפְרָךְ פְּרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that **this** mishna speaks about cutting off the wart **by hand and that** mishna speaks about cutting it off **with an instrument, what is the reason that he did not state** like the other *amora* that **this and that** talk about cutting off the wart **by hand, and** it is **not difficult; this** mishna speaks of **a moist** wart, while **that** mishna speaks of **a dry** wart? The Gemara answers that **he** could have **said to you: A dry** wart **breaks apart** by itself, and so there would be no need to teach us that it may be removed.

The *Gemara* then challenges the first opinion. If the blemish is dried out — *mifrach parich*, it is merely crumbling — why would there be any prohibition even with a vessel, and why would there be any prohibition outside the *Mikdash*? If it is no longer genuinely part of the body, removing it should present no halachic difficulty under any circumstance. The second opinion escapes this difficulty: according to it, the dried-out case is indeed entirely unproblematic, and both the *Mishnah* and the *braita* deal only with the moist case.

### Mishnah Repetition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּלַחָה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בַּיָּד, הָא בִּכְלִי — מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּלַחָה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בַּיָּד, הָא בִּכְלִי? אָמַר לָךְ: כְּלִי — הָא קָתָנֵי הָתָם: אִם בִּכְלִי, כָּאן וְכָאן אָסוּר. וְאִידַּךְ? הָא דְּקָתָנֵי כְּלִי הָכָא, פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns to a further question: **according to** the second opinion — **that the distinction is between hand and vessel, and that the *braita* already explicitly states *im be-kli, kan ve-kan assur* — if done with a vessel, it is forbidden in both locations — why does the *Mishnah* need to repeat this prohibition?** The *Gemara* answers: **he** could have **said to you:** The case of cutting off the wart with **an instrument is taught there** in *Eiruvin* in that very same mishna: **If** the wart is to be removed **with an instrument, it is forbidden** both **here,** in the Temple, **and there,** outside the Temple. Therefore, there would be no reason to repeat the same *halakha* here in this mishna, as it is stated explicitly in the other mishna. The Gemara asks: **And the other** *amora*, how does he account for the repetition? The mishna **here teaches** the law with regard to **an instrument** because **it comes to teach us the dispute** between **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua;**

The *Gemara* turns to a further question: according to the second opinion — that the distinction is between hand and vessel, and that the *braita* already explicitly states *im be-kli, kan ve-kan assur* — if done with a vessel, it is forbidden in both locations — why does the *Mishnah* need to repeat this prohibition? It is already stated explicitly in the *braita*.

The *Gemara* answers: *pulgeta de-Rabbi Eliezer ve-Rabbi Yehoshua ata leashmienan* — the *Mishnah* repeats the prohibition in the specific context of the *korban Pesach* in order to address the position of *Rabbi Eliezer*, who holds that preparatory acts which could have been done before *Shabbat* may nonetheless be performed on *Shabbat* for the sake of the *korban Pesach*. Since *Rabbi Eliezer* is lenient even about certain *melacha* for *hechsher mitzvah*, one might think he would certainly permit rabbinic prohibitions in this context. The *Mishnah* therefore needs to state explicitly, even in the context of this great obligation, that cutting a *mum* with a vessel remains forbidden — even *Rabbi Eliezer* does not extend his leniency to permit that.

## R. Eliezer vs R. Yehoshua

### Eliezer Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וּמָה אִם שְׁחִיטָה דִּתְרוּמַת דַּרְבָּנַן הִיא, דָּחֲיָא שַׁבָּת, מַכְשִׁירֵי מִצְוָה לָא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer said** that **if slaughter, which is ordinarily forbidden on Shabbat as a biblically prohibited labor, nevertheless overrides Shabbat when performed for the sake of the Paschal lamb, then activities that are prohibited by rabbinic decree should certainly override Shabbat when performed for that purpose.**

The dispute between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* now comes into focus. *Rabbi Eliezer* argues: *umah im shechita* — if the *shechita* of the *korban Pesach* overrides *Shabbat*, it is certainly the case that preparatory acts of a rabbinic character should also be permitted. If the Torah overrides *Shabbat* for the core act, surely rabbinic restrictions that merely facilitate the performance of that core act should be set aside as well.

### Yehoshua Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּאָמַר שִׂמְחַת יוֹם טוֹב נָמֵי מִצְוָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua** responds by invoking the obligation of **simchat Yom Tov**. **Rabbi Yehoshua** argues that the law governing an optional activity, such as preparing food on a Festival, cannot be brought as proof with regard to the mitzva of offering the Paschal lamb. Rabbi Eliezer counters that the law governing a Festival proves otherwise. Rabbi Eliezer countered that the law governing an optional activity, such as preparing food on a Festival, cannot be brought as proof with regard to the mitzva of offering the Paschal lamb.

*Rabbi Yehoshua* counters with an analogy from *Yom Tov*. On *Yom Tov*, cooking is permitted — yet even so, one may not chop wood on *Yom Tov* that could have been chopped the day before, even though the wood is needed for the cooking. This demonstrates that the permission to perform the core act does not automatically license all preparatory acts that could have been completed earlier, including those that are only rabbinically prohibited.

### Reshut vs Mitzvah

*Rabbi Eliezer* responds: the analogy from *Yom Tov* fails because cooking on *Yom Tov* is a *reshut* — a permitted but optional act — not a true obligatory *mitzvah*. The license to override *Shabbat* or to set aside other restrictions flows from the nature and gravity of the underlying obligation. A *reshut* provides very limited license; a genuine *mitzvah* like *korban Pesach* provides far more. Therefore, what we see on *Yom Tov* — that a *reshut* does not license preparatory acts done before — tells us nothing about what a true *mitzvah* can license.

### Simchat Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין לוֹ לְאָדָם בְּיוֹם טוֹב אֶלָּא, אוֹ אוֹכֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה, אוֹ יוֹשֵׁב וְשׁוֹנֶה. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: חַלְּקֵהוּ, חֶצְיוֹ לַאֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה, וְחֶצְיוֹ לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **For it was taught** in a *baraita* that these two *tanna'im* disagreed about this matter: **Rabbi Eliezer says: A person has nothing but** to choose **on a Festival; he either eats and drinks or sits and learns** the entire day, but there is no specific mitzva to eat on the Festival. **Rabbi Yehoshua,** on the other hand, **says: Divide** the day, **half of it for eating and drinking and half of it for the study hall,** for he holds that eating and drinking are obligatory on the Festival.

*Rabbi Yehoshua* responds by invoking the obligation of *simchat Yom Tov*. The *Gemara* introduces a *braita* that reflects this same debate in a different context: *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* dispute the nature of *Yom Tov* itself. *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that *Yom Tov* presents a binary: one may spend it entirely in divine service — *yoshev ve-shoneh* — or entirely in physical pleasure — *ochel ve-shote* — but the two are not mixed. *Rabbi Yehoshua* holds one must split the day: *chetzyo le-achilah, chetzyo le-Beis HaMidrash* — half for eating and physical enjoyment, half for Torah study.

The significance for the dispute is this: *Rabbi Yehoshua* maintains that *simchat Yom Tov* — specifically the physical enjoyment of eating and drinking — is itself a genuine *mitzvah*, not a mere *reshut*. Since even for this genuine *mitzvah* we do not permit preparatory acts on *Shabbat* that could have been done before, the same should apply to the *korban Pesach*. *Rabbi Yehoshua le-ta'amei* — this is entirely consistent with his position that physical *simchat Yom Tov* is an obligatory *mitzvah*, and therefore his analogy holds: since even a *mitzvah* like *simchat Yom Tov* does not license the performance on *Shabbat* of acts that could have been done before, the *korban Pesach* provides no greater license.

### Atzeres Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״עֲצֶרֶת לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״עֲצֶרֶת תִּהְיֶה לָכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And both of them derived** their opinions from **one verse,** i.e., the two of them addressed the same textual difficulty, resolving it in different ways. For **one verse says: "It shall be an assembly for the Lord your God;** you shall do no labor" (Deuteronomy 16:8), which indicates that the day is set aside for Divine service, **and another verse says: "It shall be an assembly for you;** you shall do no servile labor" (Numbers 29:35), which indicates a celebratory assembly for the Jewish people.

*Rabbi Yochanan* observes that both *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* derive their positions from the same verse. The Torah uses the word *atzeres* — a holding back, an assembly — twice in adjacent contexts: once *Atzeres l'Hashem Elokecha* — the *atzeres* is for God — and once *Atzeres yiheyeh lachem* — the *atzeres* is for you.

### All or Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: אוֹ כּוּלּוֹ לַה׳, אוֹ כּוּלּוֹ לָכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer holds** that the two verses should be understood as alternatives. Either the day is entirely dedicated to God — **or** it is **entirely for you.** There is no mandated mixture.

*Rabbi Eliezer* reads these two formulations as alternatives. Either the day is entirely dedicated to God — *kulo l'Hashem* — or it is entirely for oneself — *kulo lachem*. There is no mandated mixture. One may choose a path of total spiritual engagement or total physical enjoyment, and either constitutes a valid fulfillment of *Yom Tov*. On this reading, the physical enjoyment of *Yom Tov* is not obligatory — it is merely one option — and therefore constitutes a *reshut* rather than a *mitzvah*.

### Half and Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: חַלְּקֵהוּ, חֶצְיוֹ לַה׳ וְחֶצְיוֹ לָכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that it is possible to fulfill both verses: **Split** the day into two, **half of it for God and half of it for you.**

*Rabbi Yehoshua* reads the two formulations not as alternatives but as instructions to divide: *chalkeihu*, split it — *chetzyo l'Hashem, chetzyo lachem* — half the day for God, half for oneself. On this reading, the physical dimension of *Yom Tov* is not optional but mandatory, and thus constitutes a genuine *mitzvah*. Both Sages derive their entire halachic framework from the tension within this single verse, reading it in opposite directions.
