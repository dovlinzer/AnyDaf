# Gittin 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Holech k'Zachi

### Rejecting Shaliach Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ, אִם אָמַר לוֹ הַבַּעַל: "אִי אֶיפְשִׁי שֶׁתְּקַבֵּל לָהּ, אֶלָּא הוֹלֵךְ וְתֵן לָהּ", אִם רָצָה לַחְזוֹר – יַחְזוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** if the husband **said to** the agent whom the woman designated to receive the bill of divorce: **I do not want [*ee ifshi*] for you to receive** the bill of divorce **for her; rather, deliver** it **and give** it **to her,** then **if** the husband **seeks to retract** his designation and cancel the agency, **he can retract** it until it reaches his wife's possession. Since the husband does not agree to have the divorce take effect upon receipt by his wife's agent, he changes the designation of the agent and designates him as his own agent for delivery. Therefore, the divorce takes effect only when the bill of divorce reaches his wife's possession.

The sugya opens in the midst of a discussion central to the sixth *perek*, *Haomer* (also known as *Hitkabel*), concerning whether the word *holech* — used by a husband when addressing the wife's agent — functions as *k'zachi*, meaning that the *get* takes effect immediately upon being received by that agent. The question turns on the fact that the husband is speaking to someone who already stands in the position of a *shaliach l'kabala*, that is, an agent appointed by the wife to receive the *get* on her behalf. Given that predisposition, does *holech* — "go, bring this *get*" — transform into an act of immediate transfer?

The *Gemara* introduces a *ta shma* from the *Mishnah* (Gittin 66b): *lefichach im amar lo habaal ee efshi shetikabel lah ela holech v'ten lah* — if the husband says to the wife's appointed *shaliach l'kabala*, "I do not want you to accept the *get* on her behalf; rather, go and deliver it to her," then *im ratza lachzor yachzor* — he can retract, because the agent has now been repositioned as a mere *shaliach l'holacha*, and the *get* has not yet been given. The *Gemara* draws a pointed inference: *ta'ama d'amar ee efshi* — the reason he can retract is specifically because he explicitly rejected the *shaliach l'kabala* role. But *ha lo amar ee efshi*, had he simply said *holech* without the rejection, *ratza lachzor lo yachzor* — he could not retract. This implies that *holech*, addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*, functions as *zachi* — immediate acquisition on the wife's behalf.

One might ask: how can the husband unilaterally override the agency that the wife established? The answer is that the husband is not obligated to respect her appointment. He can refuse to give the *get* in the capacity she designated. The *chidush* of the *Mishnah* is precisely that the husband retains control — he alone decides in what capacity the *get* passes hands.

### Holech vs. Heilech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא בְּ"הֵילָךְ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that suggestion. **Perhaps** the mishna is not referring to a case where the husband said: Deliver [*holekh*]; rather, the mishna is referring to a case **where** the husband said: **Here you are [*heilakh*].** The husband is thereby saying: Here you are and it is yours, which is certainly an expression of acquisition.

The *Gemara* pushes back on the proof: *dilma be'heilech* — perhaps the *Mishnah*'s operative word is not *holech* but *heilech*. To modern ears the two sound nearly identical, but they are semantically distinct words. *Holech* connotes *holacha*, delivery. *Heilech* — *hei lach* — means "here it is for you," a direct act of transfer. If the *Mishnah* is speaking of *heilech*, then the case where the husband must explicitly say *ee efshi* to prevent immediate effect is a case of *heilech*, not *holech*, and we have learned nothing about whether *holech* by itself connotes *zachi*. Whether the *Gemara* is proposing an alternative *girsa* in the *Mishnah* or simply raising an interpretive possibility is left somewhat unclear, but the consequence is plain: the question of *holech k'zachi dami* remains open, to be revisited.

### Zachi Definition

The unresolved question — whether *holech*, when addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*, functions as *zachi* — will continue to drive the sugya throughout the daf. As will become evident, this is not merely a question of linguistics but of fundamental agency law: does the contextual framing supplied by the *shaliach* and the wife's prior appointment color the husband's words, or does the plain meaning of *holech* govern regardless of context?

## Gender & Shaliach

### Default Roles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא – אִישׁ הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ לְהוֹלָכָה, שֶׁכֵּן בַּעַל מוֹלִיךְ גֵּט אִשְׁתּוֹ; וְאִשָּׁה הָוְיָא שָׁלִיחַ לְִקַבָּלָה, שֶׁכֵּן אִשָּׁה מְקַבֶּלֶת גִּיטָּהּ מִיַּד בַּעְלָהּ. אִישׁ – לְקַבָּלָה, וְהָאִשָּׁה – לְהוֹלָכָה, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to discuss the fundamental *halakha* of agency with regard to a bill of divorce. It is **obvious** that a **man can be** designated **an agent for delivery, as a husband delivers his wife's bill of divorce. And** similarly, it is obvious that **a woman can be** designated **an agent for receipt, as a woman receives her bill of divorce from the hand of her husband.** However, with regard to designating **a man** as an agent **for receipt and** designating **a woman** as an agent **for delivery, what** is the *halakha*?

The *Gemara* pivots to a related structural question. *Pshita* — it is obvious — that a man (*ish*) can serve as a *shaliach* to deliver a *get* to the wife (*shaliach l'holacha*), since men are the ones who give *gittin* to their wives. Equally obvious is that a woman can represent the wife to receive the *get* (*shaliach l'kabala*), since women are the ones who receive *gittin*.

### Reverse Roles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, הָאוֹמֵר: "הִתְקַבֵּל גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי", אוֹ "הוֹלֵךְ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי", אִם רָצָה לַחְזוֹר – יַחְזוֹר. הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה: "הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", אִם רָצָה לַחְזוֹר – לֹא יַחְזוֹר. מַאי, לָאו בְּחַד שָׁלִיחַ – וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כָּשֵׁר לְקַבָּלָה כָּשֵׁר לְהוֹלָכָה? לָא, בִּשְׁנֵי שְׁלוּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna. With regard to **one who says** to another: **Receive this bill of divorce for my wife, or: Deliver this bill of divorce to my wife** as my agent, **if** the husband **seeks to retract** his designation, **he can retract** it. However, in the case of **a woman who said** to an agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me, if** the husband gives that agent the bill of divorce and then **seeks to retract** his decision **he cannot retract** it. **What, is it not** referring to **one** and the same **agent** in both cases, **and conclude from it** that the agent who is **valid for receipt is valid for delivery** as well? The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it is possible to explain that the reference in the mishna is **to two** different **agents,** an agent for delivery who is a man and an agent for receipt who is a woman.

But what of the reverse? Can a man serve as a *shaliach l'kabala* — appointed by the wife to receive the *get* — and can a woman serve as a *shaliach l'holacha*, appointed by the husband to deliver it? The *Gemara* raises this as a genuine question. The underlying *hava amina* is drawn from the principle of *shlichus*: an agent must be "in the *parsha*" of the transaction they are performing. Just as a non-Jew cannot serve as an agent for something from which he is excluded by the Torah, perhaps the role of *shaliach l'kabala* is limited to those whose natural position in the *parsha* of *gittin* is that of receiving — i.e., women — and the role of *shaliach l'holacha* is limited to those who naturally give — i.e., men. The *Gemara* considers whether we require not just general inclusion in the subject matter of *gittin* but specific inclusion in the relevant transactional role.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *Mishnah* (*Haomer hitkabel get zeh l'ishti o holech get zeh l'ishti*): a husband appoints an agent to receive or convey the *get*, and then a woman appoints someone (*Hitkabel li giti*) to receive it. The *Gemara* wonders whether the *Mishnah* speaks of the same individual serving both roles — in which case we would have proof that whoever is valid for one role is valid for the other. But the *Gemara* dismisses this: *lo, bishnei sheluchim* — perhaps the *Mishnah* refers to two different agents, a man appointed by the husband and a woman appointed by the wife, respectively.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לְפִיכָךְ, אִם אָמַר לוֹ הַבַּעַל: "אִי אֶיפְשִׁי שֶׁתְּקַבֵּל לָהּ, אֶלָּא הוֹלֵךְ וְתֵן לָהּ", אִם רָצָה לַחְזוֹר – יַחְזוֹר. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּחַד שָׁלִיחַ הוּא, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כָּשֵׁר לְקַבָּלָה כָּשֵׁר לְהוֹלָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **Come** and **hear** an additional proof from the mishna: **Therefore,** if the husband **said to** the woman's agent of receipt: **I do not want you to receive** the bill of divorce **on her** behalf; **rather, deliver** it **and give** it **to her, if** the husband **seeks to retract** his designation, **he can retract** it. **And isn't** the case **here** one **where it is one agent** whose designation the husband changes from an agent of receipt to an agent of delivery, **and conclude from** the mishna that an agent who is **valid for receipt is valid for delivery** as well?

The *Gemara* then turns to the case in the *Mishnah* where the husband explicitly switches the agent from *shaliach l'kabala* to *shaliach l'holacha* (*ee efshi shetikabel lah ela holech v'ten lah*). Here it is clear we are dealing with one and the same person operating in both capacities. From this it follows: *kashar l'kabala kashar l'holacha* — whoever is valid to receive is valid to deliver. But the *Gemara* again limits the proof: *pshot minah* — we may conclude that one person can serve in both capacities, but perhaps only a man, given the precedent that a father receives a *get* on behalf of his minor daughter (*av mekabel get l'vito ktanah*). A man is therefore "in the *parsha*" of receiving a *get*, but the question of whether a woman can deliver remains unresolved.

### Women as Deliverers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשׁוֹט מִינַּהּ אִישׁ הָוֵי שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבָּלָה – שֶׁכֵּן אָב מְקַבֵּל גֵּט לְבִתּוֹ קְטַנָּה; אִשָּׁה לְהוֹלָכָה – תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, מַאי? אָמַר רַב מָרִי, תָּא שְׁמַע: אַף הַנָּשִׁים שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱמָנוֹת לוֹמַר מֵת בַּעְלָהּ, נֶאֱמָנוֹת לְהָבִיא אֶת גִּיטָּהּ – וְהָתָם הוֹלָכָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concedes that this proof is partially effective: **Resolve from** the mishna that **a man can be an agent for receipt,** and that is reasonable, **as a father receives a bill of divorce on** behalf **of his daughter** who is **a minor** because she lacks the halakhic competence to receive it herself. However, with regard to whether **a woman** can be an agent **for delivery, raise the dilemma: What** is the *halakha*? **Rav Mari said: Come** and **hear** a resolution based on the mishna (23b): **Even the women who are not deemed credible to say** that a woman's **husband died,** because they are suspected of seeking to harm her, **are deemed credible to bring her bill of divorce** to her. **And there,** in the case in that mishna, the woman **is** an agent for **delivery.**

Rav Mari resolves this by citing an earlier *Mishnah*: *af nashim she'einam ne'emanot lomar met baalah ne'emanot lehaviv et gittah* — women who are not believed to testify that a husband has died (such as the mother-in-law and sister-in-law) are nonetheless believed to deliver a *get* and to declare *bifnai nichtav uvifnai nechtam*. Here women are explicitly functioning as agents of delivery. Rav Ashi adds that the *seifa* of that *Mishnah* is even more striking: *ha'ishah atzmah mevia et gitah* — the woman herself can deliver her own *get*, and we established that this refers to a case of *holacha*. Even if it is conceptually unusual to speak of a woman serving as her own *shaliach l'holacha*, the legal point is clear: women can perform the act of delivery. With this, the *Gemara* establishes black and white that both men and women may serve in either capacity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִסֵּיפָא נָמֵי שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ – דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: הָאִשָּׁה עַצְמָהּ מְבִיאָה אֶת גִּיטָּהּ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁהִיא צְרִיכָה לוֹמַר: "בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם"; וְאוֹקֵימְנָא בְּהוֹלָכָה; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Learn** a resolution to that dilemma **from the latter clause** of that mishna **as well, as the latter clause** of that mishna **teaches: The woman herself may bring her** own **bill of divorce, provided that she is required** by the court **to state** in its presence: **It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, and we established** that the woman acts as an agent **for delivery.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** the latter clause of that mishna that a woman can be designated as an agent for delivery.

## Misrepresentation

### Agent's Lie

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: "הָבֵא לִי גִּיטִּי", וְ"אִשְׁתְּךָ אָמְרָה הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", וְהוּא אָמַר: "הֵילָךְ כְּמָה שֶׁאָמְרָה";
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that if a woman says to an agent: **Bring my bill of divorce to me, and** the agent then says to her husband: **Your wife said receive my bill of divorce for me, and** the husband hands him the bill of divorce and **says: Here you are, as she said;** that the *amora'im* engage in a dispute as to the *halakha*. Is the *halakha* determined by what his wife said, in which case the divorce takes effect only when the bill of divorce reaches the woman's possession, or is it determined by what the agent said, in which case the divorce takes effect when the bill of divorce is handed to the agent?

The *Gemara* now introduces a series of cases in which the *shaliach* misrepresents his role to the husband. The woman tells her agent *havei li giti* — go bring me my *get*, which is understood as appointing him as a *shaliach l'holacha* on behalf of the husband, or at minimum as someone who will facilitate delivery. The agent then goes to the husband and reports: *ishtecha amra hitkabel li giti* — your wife wants me to receive her *get* for her, i.e., to serve as *shaliach l'kabala*. This is a misrepresentation, perhaps not intentional, but a misrepresentation nonetheless.

The husband then responds: *heilech kemah she'amrah* — "here it is, as she said." The question is what the husband means by "as she said." Does he mean as you are representing what she said — following the agent's report — or what she actually said?

### Relying on Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ הִגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ – אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַדִּיבּוּרָא דִידֵיהּ קָא סָמֵיךְ – דְּאִי אַדִּיבּוּרָא דִידַהּ קָא סָמֵיךְ, מִכִּי מָטֵי גִּיטָּא לִידַהּ מִיהָא תִּיגָּרַשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says** that **Rav says:** In that case, **even** if **the bill of divorce reached her possession, she is not divorced.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** this statement that the husband **relies on** the agent's **statement** as to what his status is, and hands him the bill of divorce as an agent for receipt. However, since the woman did not designate him as an agent for receipt and the husband lacks the authority to designate him as an agent of receipt, there is no agency and the divorce does not take effect. **As, if** the contrary were the case, that when he hands the bill of divorce to the agent the husband **relies on** his wife's **statement** as to what the agent's status is, then **at least when the bill of divorce reaches her possession let her be divorced,** as she designated the agent as an agent of delivery for her husband.

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha citing Rav, rules: *afilu higia get l'yadah einah megureshet* — even if the *get* ultimately reaches the wife's hand, she is not divorced. The reasoning: the husband said *heilech kemah she'amrah*, meaning he is relying on the agent's representation. He believes the agent is a *shaliach l'kabala* and intends the *get* to take effect immediately upon the agent receiving it. But in reality the wife never appointed this man as her *shaliach l'kabala*, so no valid *kabala* occurs. And since the husband never designated him as his *shaliach l'holacha* either, the subsequent delivery to the wife is also legally empty. *Shma mina adibura didei kasamich* — the husband's words are pegged to what the agent says, not to what the wife actually instructed.

The reverse inference follows: *d'i adibura didah kasamich* — if instead the husband's intent tracked what the wife actually said, then since she truly wanted *holacha*, he would be implicitly appointing the agent as his *shaliach l'holacha*, and the *get* should work when delivered. The fact that Rav holds it does not work proves that the husband operates on the basis of the agent's report, not the wife's actual words.

### Rav Ashi Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: How can** these cases **be compared?**

Rav Ashi raises an objection: *hachi hashta* — what kind of comparison is this? He notes that the case under discussion, in which the wife said *kabala* but the agent reported *holacha*, cannot serve as the basis for the broader rule about *adibura didei kasamich*, because the consequences in this case happen to be symmetrical in a way that obscures which principle is operative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא אִי אִיתְּמַר אִיפְּכָא: "הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", וְ"אִשְׁתְּךָ אָמְרָה הָבֵא לִי גִּיטִּי", וְהוּא אֹמֵר: "הֵילָךְ כְּמָה שֶׁאָמְרָה"; וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת – אַלְמָא אַדִּיבּוּרָא דִידַהּ הוּא דְּקָא סָמֵיךְ;
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if the opposite was stated,** i.e., a case where the woman said: **Receive my bill of divorce for me,** and the agent said to the husband: **Your wife said: Bring me my bill of divorce, and** the husband **said: Here you are, as she said, and Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says** that **Rav says: Once the bill of divorce reaches** the agent's **possession, she is divorced,** it would be understandable. **Apparently,** the husband **relies upon her statement** that the agent is an agent of receipt, and he intended to give the agent the status the wife assigned, not the one the agent says she assigned.

Rav Ashi constructs the mirror-image scenario: suppose the wife had said *hitkabel li giti* — appoint yourself as my *shaliach l'kabala* — and the agent instead reports to the husband *havei li giti*, "she wants me to deliver the *get*." The husband says *heilech kemah she'amrah*. Now: if the husband is going by the agent's words, he is genuinely appointing the agent as his *shaliach l'holacha*, and the *get* should work when delivered. If instead he is going by what she actually said, he is in effect ratifying the *kabala* appointment, and she would be divorced the moment the agent receives the *get*. According to Rav Nachman, who rules that it works right away in this scenario (*kivan she'higia l'yado megureshet*), we would conclude *dibura didah hu d'kasamich* — the husband tracks her actual words, not the agent's representation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי "לְיָדָהּ" – אַדִּיבּוּרָא דִידֵיהּ קָא סָמֵיךְ. אֶלָּא הָכָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּעַקְרַיהּ שָׁלִיחַ לִשְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ לִגְמָרֵי הוּא; דְּאָמַר: שָׁלִיחַ לְִקַבָּלָה הָוֵינָא, לְהוֹלָכָה לָא הָוֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** if Rav Naḥman ruled: Once the bill of divorce reaches **her possession,** she is divorced, one could conclude that the husband **relies on** the agent's **statement,** and based on that statement, the agent is designated as an agent for delivery. **However, here,** in the case cited, where Rav Naḥman rules that she is not divorced, it is not because the husband relies on one statement or the other. Rather, it is **due to** the fact **that** by means of his statement **the agent negates his agency entirely, as he said: I am an agent for receipt,** meaning: **I am not to be an agent for delivery.** He is essentially saying that he is not prepared to go to the trouble of delivering the bill of divorce to her. Therefore, even if he does ultimately deliver the bill of divorce to her, he is an agent neither for the woman nor for her husband. No conclusion can be drawn with regard to the question of which statement the husband relies upon.

But Rav Ashi argues that these two scenarios are not equivalent. In the original case — where the agent reports *kabala* but was actually appointed for *holacha* — the reason the *get* fails is not because the husband tracks the agent's words. The reason is that the agent, by declaring himself a *shaliach l'kabala*, is effectively *rejecting* the role he was assigned. He was sent to deliver the *get* back to the wife; by announcing himself as a *shaliach l'kabala*, he is declining that mission. The *Gemara* articulates this: *de'amar shaliach l'kabala havina l'holacha lo havina* — he has repudiated the *holacha* role, and since he is not validly a *shaliach l'kabala* either, nothing works. This has nothing to do with whether the husband follows the agent's words or the wife's; the failure is internal to the agent's own act of rejection.

### Rejecting Delivery Role

The *Gemara* thus establishes a principle: when a *shaliach* makes his mission easier by claiming a different role than the one he was assigned — specifically, by claiming *kabala* when he was designated for *holacha*, thereby ending his obligation once he receives the *get* rather than carrying it back — we interpret this claim as a rejection of the original role. Since he cannot validly occupy either position (not the wife's *shaliach l'kabala* because she never appointed him thus, not the husband's *shaliach l'holacha* because he has declined it), the *get* has no legal effect.

The consequence is that from Rav Nachman's ruling we cannot directly derive the broader principle about *adibura didei kasamich*. The *Gemara* leaves this as an open question, pursued through the cases that follow.

## Contextual Interpretation

### Reinterpretation Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא: תָּא שְׁמַע, הָאוֹמֵר: "הִתְקַבֵּל גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי" אוֹ "הוֹלֵךְ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי", רָצָה לַחְזוֹר – יַחְזוֹר. טַעְמָא דְּרָצָה, הָא לֹא רָצָה – הָוֵי גֵּט;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya says: Come** and **hear** an objection to the statement of Rav Naḥman from the mishna: With regard to **one who says** to another: **Receive this bill of divorce for my wife, or: Deliver this bill of divorce to my wife** as my agent, **if** the husband **seeks to retract** his designation **he may retract** it. Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya infers: **The reason** she is not divorced is that **he seeks** to retract his designation. **However,** if he **did not seek** to retract it, **it is** a valid **bill of divorce.**

Rav Huna bar Chiya introduces a new *ta shma* from the *Mishnah*: *ha'omer hitkabel get zeh l'ishti o holech get zeh l'ishti ratza lachzor yachzor* — if the husband says "receive this *get* for my wife" or "convey it to her," he can retract. This is the *Mishnah*'s basic case. The *Gemara* asks an arresting question: *ve'amai habaal lav bar shavei shaliach l'kabala hu?* — why does the *Mishnah* treat this as a valid appointment at all? The husband says *hitkabel*, "receive it," which literally implies a *shaliach l'kabala* appointment — but the husband cannot appoint a *shaliach l'kabala* for his wife; only she can do that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? הָא בַּעַל לָאו בַּר שַׁוּוֹיֵי שָׁלִיחַ לְִקַבָּלָה הוּא! אֶלָּא אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו לְגָרְשָׁהּ, מֵימָר אָמַר: תִּיגָּרַשׁ כֹּל הֵיכִי דְּמִגָּרְשָׁה; הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו לְגָרְשָׁהּ, מֵימָר אָמַר: תִּיגָּרַשׁ כֹּל הֵיכִי דְּמִגָּרְשָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** He asks: **But why** is she divorced when the husband says: Receive this bill of divorce for my wife? **Isn't the husband ineligible to designate an agent for receipt? Rather, we say: Once he decided to divorce her, he said** to himself: **Let her be divorced any way that she is divorced.** His statement: Receive this bill of divorce for my wife, does not prevent the divorce from taking effect. **Here too,** in the case mentioned by Rav Naḥman, **once he decided to divorce her, he says: Let her be divorced any way that she is divorced.**

The answer is that we do not read his words with rigid literalism. *Keivan shenatan einav l'garsha* — since it is clear he intends to divorce her, *meimar amar tigaraish b'chol heichi demigaraisha* — we interpret his words in whatever way will make a valid *get* possible. When he says "receive it for my wife," the only legally operative reading is "deliver it to my wife," and so we interpret it accordingly. The husband is presumed to want the *get* to work, and we bend his words toward the only legally valid form they can take.

The *Gemara* then asks: *hacha nami* — why not apply the same principle in our case of misrepresentation? Even if the agent is claiming *kabala* when he was designated for *holacha*, should we not interpret the husband's words (*heilech kemah she'amrah*) in whatever way will make the *get* valid?

### Law vs. Fact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין שְׁלִיחוּת לְקַבָּלָה, וְגָמַר וְנָתַן לְשֵׁם הוֹלָכָה; הָכָא – טָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case where one says: Receive this bill of divorce for my wife, the principle that **a person knows that agency for receipt cannot** be designated by the husband is in effect, **and he decided to give** the bill of divorce to the agent **for the sake of delivery.** When he told the agent to receive the document, his intent was that the agent should receive the document in order to deliver it to his wife, not that the act of divorce should take effect when the agent receives it. However **here,** in the case where the agent misrepresented what the woman said, the husband **errs** and relies upon the statement of the agent, who said that he is the woman's agent for receipt.

The *Gemara* draws a sharp distinction: *hatam ihu yada de'ein shlichut l'kabala gabeih venachti l'shem holacha* — in the *Mishnah*'s basic case, any person who knew the *halacha* would have understood that the husband cannot appoint a *shaliach l'kabala*, and so we may say that deep down he intended the only legally valid interpretation: *holacha*. His error, if any, was one of legal expression, not substantive intent. *Gamar ve-natan l'shem holacha* — at the moment he acted, he implicitly conformed to the legal requirement that would make his transaction work.

But *hacha ta'i* — in the case of misrepresentation, the husband's error is not about law but about fact. He genuinely believes the agent is the wife's *shaliach l'kabala*. Given those (mistaken) facts, his stated intention would have been perfectly valid. We therefore cannot say he "really meant" something else; he meant exactly what he said, under a mistaken assumption about the facts. Since his intent — as he understood it — was coherent and legal under his own perception of reality, we have no basis to reinterpret his words. The distinction, then, is: mistake of law permits reinterpretation toward the legal form, because we assume a person wishes to act in accordance with law; mistake of fact does not, because the person's stated intent was already internally consistent.

### Minor Wife Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, קְטַנָּה שֶׁאָמְרָה: "הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי" – אֵינוֹ גֵּט עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ. כִּי מָטֵי גִּיטָּא לִידַהּ – מִיהָא מִגָּרְשָׁה, וְאַמַּאי? וְהָא לָאו שָׁלִיחַ לְהוֹלָכָה שַׁוְּיֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו לְגָרְשָׁהּ, מֵימָר אָמַר: תִּיגָּרַשׁ כֹּל הֵיכִי דְּמִגָּרְשָׁה; הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו לְגָרְשָׁהּ, מֵימָר אָמַר: תִּיגָּרַשׁ כֹּל הֵיכִי דְּמִגָּרְשָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** an objection to the statement of Rav Naḥman from a mishna (65a). In the case of **a minor girl who said** to an agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me, it is not a bill of divorce** that takes effect **until it reaches her possession,** as a minor is incapable of designating an agent. Rava infers: **In any event, once the bill of divorce reaches her possession she is divorced.** He asks: **But why** is that the case? The husband **did not designate him an agent for delivery. Rather, we say: Once he decided to divorce her, he said** to himself: **Let her be divorced any way that she is divorced,** and the agent is designated as his agent for delivery. **Here too,** in the case mentioned by Rav Naḥman, **once he decided to divorce her, he says: Let her be divorced any way that she is divorced.**

Rava introduces a further *ta shma*: *ketana she'amra hitkabel li giti eino get ad she'yagi'a get l'yadah* — a minor girl cannot appoint a *shaliach*, so if she tells someone to receive her *get*, it is not a *get* until the *get* reaches her hand. The *Gemara* presses: but when she gets it, is she even then divorced? After all, if the husband gave the *get* to this person with the intent that he receive it for her as a *shaliach l'kabala*, and that appointment was void, the husband never designated him as his own *shaliach l'holacha* — so how does delivery to her work at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין שְׁלִיחוּת לְקָטָן, וְגָמַר וְנָתַן לָהּ לְשׁוּם הוֹלָכָה דִּידֵיהּ; הָכָא – טָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case where the minor girl designated the agent, the principle that **a person knows that there is no agency for a minor** applies, **and he decided to give** the bill of divorce to the agent as an agent **for delivery.** However, **here,** in the case where the agent misrepresented what the woman said, the husband **errs** and relies upon the statement of the agent, who said that he is the woman's agent for receipt.

Abaye answers: *kevan de'natan me'amen l'gareshah meimar megaresh k'heichi demigarsha* — since it is evident the husband intends to divorce her, we interpret the very act of handing the *get* to this person as a *shaliach l'holacha* appointment, even though he never said so explicitly. We reinterpret his act to align with the only legally operative mode by which divorce could occur.

The *Gemara* immediately limits this ruling: *hatam ihu yada de'ein shlichus l'katan* — there, the husband should know that a minor cannot appoint a *shaliach*, and therefore *gamar v'nasa l'shum halakha l'halakhta didei* — his act of giving the *get* is understood as an implicit *shaliach l'holacha* designation, because that is the only legally valid framework for what he was trying to accomplish. *Hacha ta'i* — but in the misrepresentation case, the error is factual, and the same logic does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: "הָבֵא לִי גִּיטִּי", וְ"אִשְׁתְּךָ אָמְרָה הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי"; "הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", וְ"אִשְׁתְּךָ אָמְרָה הָבֵא לִי גִּיטִּי", וְהוּא אֹמֵר: "הוֹלֵךְ וְתֵן לָהּ", "זְכֵי לָהּ", וְ"הִתְקַבֵּל לָהּ" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, יַחְזוֹר; מִשֶּׁהִגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ – מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **Come** and **hear** an objection to the statement of Rav Naḥman from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 6:2). With regard to a woman who said to an agent: **Bring me my bill of divorce, and** the agent said to her husband: **Your wife said: Receive my bill of divorce for me;** or a woman who said: **Receive my bill of divorce for me, and** the agent said to her husband: **Your wife said: Bring me my bill of divorce, and** the husband **said** to the agent: **Deliver and give** it **to her,** or: **Acquire** it **for her, or: Receive** it **for her,** once the husband hands the bill of divorce to the agent for receipt, if he **seeks to retract** his designation **he may retract** it. However, **once the bill of divorce reaches her possession,** she is **divorced.**

The *Gemara* then continues with a *tanu rabbanan* that presents multiple permutations — a *shaliach* reporting *kabala* when appointed for *holacha*, and vice versa, combined with various responses from the husband (*holech v'ten lah*, *zechi lah v'hitkablah*) — all converging on the same ruling: she is divorced only *mishe'yagi'a get l'yadah*, upon actual receipt. The *Gemara* considers whether this *baraita* proves that *dibura didei kasamich* (the husband tracks the agent's report) or *dibura didah* (the husband tracks her actual words), but in each permutation the *Gemara* finds a way to read the ruling as consistent with either interpretation. The back-and-forth concludes without directly disproving Rav Nachman, though the weight of the analysis leans against his categorical formulation.

## Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Natan

### Tanna Kamma Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", וְ"אִשְׁתְּךָ אָמְרָה הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: "הוֹלֵךְ", וְ"תֵן לָהּ", "זְכֵי לָהּ", וְ"הִתְקַבֵּל לָהּ" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, לֹא יַחְזוֹר. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: "הוֹלֵךְ וְתֵן לָהּ" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, יַחְזוֹר; "זְכֵי לָהּ" וְ"הִתְקַבֵּל לָהּ" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, לֹא יַחְזוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 6:1) that if the woman says to an agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me, and** the agent says to her husband: **Your wife said: Receive my bill of divorce for me, and** the husband **says: Deliver and give** it **to her,** or: **Acquire** it **for her, or: Receive** it **for her,** once the husband hands the bill of divorce to the agent for receipt, if he **seeks to retract** his decision **he cannot retract** it, because the divorce has already taken effect. **Rabbi Natan says:** If the husband said: **Deliver and give** it **to her,** and **he seeks to retract** his designation, then as long as it has not yet reached the woman's possession **he can retract** it, because the husband designated him an agent for delivery. However, if the husband said: **Acquire** it **for her, or: Receive** it **for her,** and he **seeks to retract** his decision **he cannot retract** it.

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* that engages the core *machlokes* directly. The case: the agent presents himself as the wife's *shaliach l'kabala*, and the husband says *holech v'ten lah* — "go and give it to her." The *tanna kamma* rules: *ratza lachzor lo yachzir* — he may not retract. The *tanna kamma* further holds the same for *zechi lah v'hitkablah* — "acquire it for her and receive it." In both formulations, the act is immediately binding. This is the *tanna kamma*'s position that *holech k'zachi* — that *holech*, addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*, conveys immediate legal effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּכוּלָּן – אִם רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, לֹא יַחְזוֹר. אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: "אִי אֶיפְשִׁי שֶׁתְּקַבֵּל לָהּ, אֶלָּא הוֹלֵךְ וְתֵן לָהּ", רָצָה לַחְזוֹר – יַחְזוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: In all of** those cases, **if he seeks to retract** his decision **he cannot retract** it, because when he hands the document to the agent, the bill of divorce takes effect immediately. **However, if he said to** the agent explicitly: **I do not want you to receive** the bill of divorce **for her; rather, deliver and give** it **to her,** then if **he seeks to retract** his decision **he can retract** it.

Rabbi, whose position is then stated, agrees with the *tanna kamma* on this fundamental point: *be-chulan im ratza lachzor lo yachzir* — in all these formulations he cannot retract. However, Rabbi adds an important qualification: *aval im amar lo ee efshi she'tikabel lah ela holech v'ten lah* — if the husband preceded *holech* with the explicit rejection *ee efshi*, then *ratza lachzor yachzir* — he can retract. This is precisely the case from the opening of our sugya: when *holech* is framed by an explicit *ee efshi*, it reverts to its plain meaning of delivery, not immediate *zachi*. The *Gemara* notes: *Rabbi hainu tanna kamma* — Rabbi's position is essentially the same as the *tanna kamma*'s. The *Gemara* explains this by saying that Rabbi is either clarifying the force of the *ee efshi* formulation, or identifying who the *tanna kamma* is.

### Rabbi Natan Position

The *tanna kamma* (and Rabbi) hold that *holech k'zachi* when addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*; Rabbi Natan holds that even in that context *holech* means only delivery. However, *zechi lah v'hitkablah ratza lachzor lo yachzor* — when the husband uses an explicit *zechi* or *hitkabel* formulation, it is immediately binding.

This makes the debate precise: the *tanna kamma* (and Rabbi) hold that *holech k'zachi* when addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*; Rabbi Natan holds that even in that context *holech* means only delivery. The *Gemara* observes that this debate maps directly onto the question raised at the beginning of the *perek*: is *holech k'zachi dami*?

The *Gemara* then raises a question about Rabbi Natan: does he agree that *heilech* is *k'zachi*? Logically it seems obvious — *heilech* is *hei lach*, "here it is for you," an unmistakable act of transfer, entirely different from *holech*. The only reason one might hesitate is because the words sound superficially similar. But substantively they are entirely distinct.

### Explicit Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: "הוֹלֵךְ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, יַחְזוֹר. "הֵילָךְ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, לֹא יַחְזוֹר. מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר בְּ"הוֹלֵךְ" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, יַחְזוֹר – רַבִּי נָתָן; וְקָאָמַר בְּ"הֵילָךְ" – רָצָה לַחְזוֹר, לֹא יַחְזוֹר; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ – "הֵילָךְ" כִּ"זְכֵי" דָּמֵי; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **Come** and **hear** proof with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Natan from a *baraita*. If the husband said: **Deliver this bill of divorce to my wife,** if he **seeks to retract** his decision **he can retract** it. If he said: **Here you are; this bill of divorce** is **for my wife,** if the husband **seeks to retract** his decision **he cannot retract** it. **Whom did you hear who said with regard to** a case where the husband said: **Deliver,** that if the husband **seeks to retract** his decision **he can retract** it? It is **Rabbi Natan; and he says in** the case of a husband who says: **Here you are,** that if the husband **seeks to retract** his decision **he cannot retract** it. **Learn from** the *baraita* that according to the opinion of Rabbi Natan, if the husband says: **Here you are,** it is **comparable** to a case **where** the husband said: **Acquire.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** the *baraita* that it is so.

The *Gemara* introduces a *baraita* that makes the distinction explicit: *holech get zeh l'ishti ratza lachzor yachzor* — if a husband says *holech*, he can retract; *heilech get zeh l'ishti ratza lachzor lo yachzor* — if he says *heilech*, he cannot. The *Gemara* asks: *man sham'at lei d'amar b'holech ratza lachzor yachzor* — who holds that *holech* allows retraction even when the agent is a *shaliach l'kabala*? Rabbi Natan. And this same *baraita* says that *heilech* does not allow retraction. *Shma mina heilech k'zachi dami shma mina* — we therefore conclude that even according to Rabbi Natan, *heilech* is *k'zachi*, which is sensible, since the words are semantically unrelated despite their acoustic similarity.

The final resolution: the question of *holech k'zachi* is a *machlokes tannaim* between the *tanna kamma* (and Rabbi) on one side, and Rabbi Natan on the other. The *tanna kamma* holds *holech k'zachi* in the context of a *shaliach l'kabala*; Rabbi Natan holds it is not, but *heilech k'zachi* according to all opinions.

## Holech Zachi Doubt

### Dual Agency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: "הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", וְ"אִשְׁתְּךָ אָמְרָה הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי", וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: "הוֹלֵךְ וְתֵן לָהּ" – אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: נַעֲשָׂה שְׁלוּחוֹ וּשְׁלוּחָהּ – וְחוֹלֶצֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** with regard to a woman who says to her agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me, and** the agent says to her husband: **Your wife said: Receive my bill of divorce for me, and** the husband **says** to the agent: **Deliver and give** it to **her,** that **Rabbi Abba says** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** The agent **becomes** both the husband's **agent** for delivery of the bill of divorce **and** the wife's **agent** for receipt. Therefore, if the husband dies childless after handing the bill of divorce to the agent **but** before it reaches his wife's possession, **she performs *ḥalitza*** with the husband's brother due to the possibility that the agent was an agent of delivery and she was therefore not yet divorced. However, her husband's brother may not enter into levirate marriage with her, due to the possibility that the agent was an agent of receipt, in which case she was divorced and is forbidden to the brother.

Having established the *machlokes*, the *Gemara* returns to a case that generates genuine uncertainty even in practice. The wife's agent presents himself to the husband as a *shaliach l'kabala*. The husband responds: *holech v'ten lah*. This is exactly the disputed case — does *holech* here mean *zachi*, following the *tanna kamma*, or mere delivery, following Rabbi Natan?

### Both Agents

Rav Abba, citing Rav Huna citing Rav, rules: *na'asch shlucho u'shlucha* — the agent becomes simultaneously the husband's agent and the wife's agent. This is, as the *Gemara* effectively acknowledges, a way of expressing genuine uncertainty. The wife appointed him as her *shaliach l'kabala*; the husband's *holech* may or may not have ratified that role or instead designated him as a *shaliach l'holacha*. We cannot determine which.

### Safek Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּמְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ לְרַב אִי "הוֹלֵךְ" כִּ"זְכֵי" דָּמֵי, אִי לָאו כִּ"זְכֵי" דָּמֵי?! וְהָא אִתְּמַר: "הוֹלֵךְ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי שֶׁאֲנִי חַיָּיב לוֹ", אָמַר רַב: חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, וְאִם בָּא לַחְזוֹר – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that Rav was uncertain whether** a case where the husband said: **Deliver,** is **comparable** to a case where the husband said: **Acquire,** or **whether** it is **not comparable** to a case **where** he said: **Acquire? But wasn't it stated** that in a case where one said to his agent: **Deliver one hundred dinars to so-and-so, as I owe him** that sum, **Rav says:** The person who designated the agent **bears** financial **responsibility** for this money, and if it is lost he is required to pay the debt to his creditor. Nevertheless, **if** the person who designated the agent **seeks to retract** his designation and take the money back from the agent, then **he cannot retract** it, because the creditor acquires the money from the moment that the debtor handed it to his agent of delivery. Apparently, according to Rav, saying: Deliver, is like saying: Acquire.

The practical consequence flows from this ambiguity: *vechol sheken* — and certainly, once she receives the *get*, she is divorced *mima nafshach*, regardless. The uncertainty pertains to the moment before she receives it. If the husband or the wife dies in the interim, the critical question becomes: is she an *almanah* (widow, who may perform *yibbum*) or a *grushah* (divorcee, who may not)? Rav's answer is that she must perform *chalitzah* and cannot perform *yibbum* — a practical expression of *safek*, because if she were certainly an *almanah* she could perform *yibbum*, and if she were certainly a *grushah* she would need neither. The requirement of *chalitzah* reflects genuine doubt as to her status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם – סְפֵק מָמוֹנָא לְקוּלָּא, הָכָא – סְפֵק אִיסּוּרָא לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof. There is a distinction between the agent for delivery of a bill of divorce and the agent for delivery of the repayment of a loan. **There,** in the case of repayment of a loan, because there is **uncertainty** with regard to **monetary** law, the ruling is **lenient,** as one does not extract money from another in cases of uncertainty with regard to monetary law. However, **here,** in the case of divorce there is **uncertainty** with regard to **ritual** law and the ruling is **stringent.**

The conclusion of this entire discussion, then, is that the question of *holech k'zachi* is an unresolved *machlokes tannaim*, and even at the level of *amoraim* Rav leaves it as a *safek*, applying domain-appropriate rules to navigate the uncertainty.

## Two Agents Question

### Rav's Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה עוֹשָׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל לָהּ גִּיטָּהּ מִיַּד שְׁלִיחַ בַּעְלָהּ. וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: אִשָּׁה עוֹשָׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל לָהּ גִּיטָּהּ מִיַּד שְׁלִיחַ בַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says: A woman cannot appoint an agent to receive her bill of divorce from the hand of her husband's agent, and Rabbi Ḥanina says: A woman can appoint an agent to receive her bill of divorce from the hand of her husband's agent.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a new ruling from Rav: *ein ha'isha osa shaliach l'kabel gitah miyad shaliach ba'alah* — a woman may not appoint an agent to receive her *get* from the husband's agent. Rabbi Chanina disagrees: *isha osa shaliach l'kabel gitah miyad shaliach ba'alah* — she may.

The scenario is one in which neither the husband nor the wife wishes to be present at the moment of delivery. The husband appoints an agent to deliver the *get*; the wife wishes to appoint her own agent to receive it from that *shaliach*. In principle, if each party may have a *shaliach*, why should two agents interposing between the parties be invalid? The question is what concern underlies Rav's restriction.

### Honor Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם בִּזָּיוֹן דְּבַעַל;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rav,** who said that she cannot appoint an agent to receive a bill of divorce from an agent? The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say:** It is **due to** the fact that her unwillingness to receive the document directly from his agent could be construed as a display of **contempt for** her **husband.**

The *Gemara* offers one possible reason: *mishum bizayon d'baal* — out of concern for the honor of the husband. The husband, who holds all the legal power in this transaction, may feel affronted if his wife does not personally receive the *get* or attend to the matter herself. Even though the husband has appointed his own *shaliach* and will not be personally present either, there may be an implicit expectation that the wife — the recipient — will receive the *get* herself. If the husband learned after the fact that she had sent a *shaliach*, he could claim: had I known she intended to send an agent, I never would have authorized my *shaliach* to give it to someone other than her personally. In the case where the husband himself hands the *get* to the wife's *shaliach*, no such complaint is possible — he saw the situation and consented. But when it is his *shaliach* acting, and he is not present, the concern of *bizayon d'baal* becomes operative.

### Indirect Acquisition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם חֲצֵרָהּ הַבָּא לְאַחַר מִיכָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that it is a decree issued **due to her courtyard that comes** into her possession **thereafter.** If a husband places a bill of divorce in a courtyard that does not belong to his wife, and his wife then purchases the courtyard, the divorce does not take effect, because the husband neither gave the bill of the divorce directly to his wife nor did he place it in her property. Her subsequent purchase of the courtyard is tantamount to her finding and taking the document. In this case too, the woman designated her agent for receipt, who in this sense is comparable to her property or her courtyard, subsequent to her husband's handing the bill of divorce to his agent of delivery. Were the divorce valid in that case, one might mistakenly conclude that divorce is likewise valid in a case where he placed the document in a courtyard that she subsequently acquired.

The second possible reason: *mishum chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* — a concern rooted in the laws of *chatzer*, an enclosed domain that can effect acquisition. The rule is that if I place a *get* in a field that at that moment belongs to the wife, she acquires it and is divorced. But if the field was not yet hers at the moment of placement — if she subsequently purchased the field with the *get* already inside — she is not divorced, because acquisition via *chatzer* must be contemporaneous with ownership.

The concern here is analogous. The husband's *shaliach* holds the *get* in his hands in the capacity of the husband's agent. If the wife's *shaliach* then "receives" the *get* from him without any active transfer occurring — especially if her *shaliach* was appointed after the husband's *shaliach* was already in possession of the *get* — this resembles the case of a field acquired after the *get* was placed in it. There was no genuine moment of delivery; the *get* simply transitioned from one holding to another without a clear act of *netina*. The *Gemara* distinguishes: if the wife had appointed her *shaliach* before the husband appointed his, the concern of *chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* is less applicable, since her *shaliach*'s status pre-dates the husband's *shaliach*'s possession.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דִּקְדַמָה אִיהִי, וְשַׁוִּיָּה שָׁלִיחַ מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** these two reasons? The Gemara answers: The difference **between them** is in a case **where she first appointed an agent from the outset,** before the husband designated his agent for delivery. That case is not at all comparable to the case of a courtyard that a woman purchases after the document is placed there. However, the concern remains that it could be construed as a display of contempt for her husband.

*Tosafot* suggests a more straightforward *nafka mina* between the two reasons: if the husband explicitly states that he is content for his *shaliach* to deliver to the wife's *shaliach*, the concern of *bizayon d'baal* is obviously inapplicable — he has waived any such objection. Whether the divorce is valid in that case depends entirely on whether the *chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* concern survives the husband's explicit consent, which it might, since that concern is rabbinic in character and not dependent on the husband's subjective preference. An additional *nafka mina* concerns *b'diavad*: if the restriction is grounded in honor, perhaps *b'diavad* the *get* is valid; if it is grounded in the acquisition concern, *b'diavad* may be more stringent.

### Rabbi Chanina Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּשַׁדַּר לַהּ גִּיטָּא לִדְבֵיתְהוּ, אֲזַל שְׁלִיחָא אַשְׁכְּחַהּ כִּי יָתְבָה וְקָא לָיְשָׁא, אֲמַר לַהּ: "הֵילָךְ גִּיטָּךְ", אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: "לֶיהֱוֵי בִּידָךְ". אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אִם אִיתָא לִדְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, עֲבַדִי בַּהּ עוֹבָדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who sent a bill of divorce to his wife. The agent went and found her while she was sitting and kneading. He said to her: Here you are,** take **your bill of divorce. She said to him:** My hands are covered with dough and therefore **let** the bill of divorce **be in your hand,** i.e., serve as my agent for receipt. **Rav Naḥman said: If it is so that** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥanina** and she can designate an agent to receive a bill of divorce from her husband's agent, then **I would perform an action with regard to** this woman and rule that the divorce takes effect.

Rabbi Chanina holds that the wife may appoint her own agent to receive from the husband's agent. The *Gemara* reports that Rav Nachman said: *im ita l'deRabbi Chanina avdinan uvda* — had Rabbi Chanina's ruling been before me in the relevant case, I would have ruled accordingly. The case in question: a man sent a *get* to his wife; the *shaliach* found her kneading dough and therefore her hands were occupied. He said to her *heilech giteich* — "here is your *get*." She replied: *leihavei b'yadech* — "you hold it for me," thereby attempting to turn the husband's *shaliach l'holacha* into her *shaliach l'kabala*.

### One vs. Two Agents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְאִם אִיתָא לִדְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, עֲבַדְתְּ בַּהּ עוֹבָדָא?! הָא לֹא חָזְרָה שְׁלִיחוּת אֵצֶל הַבַּעַל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: And if it is so that** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥanina, would you perform an action with regard to** this woman? In this case, as the agent did not actually hand her the bill of divorce, the **agency** was not completed and consequently the agent **did not return to the husband** and report that he performed the task for which he was designated. By designating him as her agent before he completed the agency on behalf of the husband, the wife nullified the agency of the husband. Therefore, the divorce does not take effect.

Rava immediately objects to Rav Nachman: *im ita d'Rabbi Chanina, avdas bei uvda? Halo hadra shlichus eitzel habaal* — even according to Rabbi Chanina, would you really have ruled that way? What Rabbi Chanina permits is the scenario of two genuine agents: the husband's *shaliach* gives the *get* to the wife's *shaliach*, with both parties maintaining their distinct roles. What the woman in this case attempted was fundamentally different: she tried to convert the husband's *shaliach* himself into her *shaliach*, not by receiving from him but by having him change sides.

This is the key distinction. When there are two separate agents, there is a genuine act of delivery: the husband's *shaliach* gives, and the wife's *shaliach* receives. Both identities persist through and after the act. But when the wife attempts to transform the husband's *shaliach* into her own — as in this case, where she says "you hold it for me" — there is no moment of delivery at all. The agent's identity would have to shift from representing the husband to representing the wife without any intervening act of *netina*. The *Gemara* phrases this as *hadra shlichus eitzel habaal* — the *shlichus* would have to revert, or more precisely, there would be no point at which the *shaliach* could return to the husband and report: "I have completed the delivery," because his identity changed before the act was accomplished.

This case, then, is not what Rabbi Chanina had in mind. Rabbi Chanina's leniency applies to two distinct agents occupying their respective roles simultaneously. Transforming a single agent's identity mid-transaction is a categorically different problem — one that closely resembles the very concern of *chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* that underlies Rav's restriction, inasmuch as there is never a genuine, identifiable moment when the *get* passes from one party's legal domain to another's. This distinction will be taken up further in the continuation of the sugya.
