# Gittin 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Holech k'Zachi

### Rejecting Shaliach Role

The sugya opens in the midst of a discussion central to the sixth *perek*, *Haomer* (also known as *Hitkabel*), concerning whether the word *holech* — used by a husband when addressing the wife's agent — functions as *k'zachi*, meaning that the *get* takes effect immediately upon being received by that agent. The question turns on the fact that the husband is speaking to someone who already stands in the position of a *shaliach l'kabala*, that is, an agent appointed by the wife to receive the *get* on her behalf. Given that predisposition, does *holech* — "go, bring this *get*" — transform into an act of immediate transfer?

The *Gemara* introduces a *ta shma* from the *Mishnah* (Gittin 66b): *lefichach im amar lo habaal ee efshi shetikabel lah ela holech v'ten lah* — if the husband says to the wife's appointed *shaliach l'kabala*, "I do not want you to accept the *get* on her behalf; rather, go and deliver it to her," then *im ratza lachzor yachzor* — he can retract, because the agent has now been repositioned as a mere *shaliach l'holacha*, and the *get* has not yet been given. The *Gemara* draws a pointed inference: *ta'ama d'amar ee efshi* — the reason he can retract is specifically because he explicitly rejected the *shaliach l'kabala* role. But *ha lo amar ee efshi*, had he simply said *holech* without the rejection, *ratza lachzor lo yachzor* — he could not retract. This implies that *holech*, addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*, functions as *zachi* — immediate acquisition on the wife's behalf.

One might ask: how can the husband unilaterally override the agency that the wife established? The answer is that the husband is not obligated to respect her appointment. He can refuse to give the *get* in the capacity she designated. The *chidush* of the *Mishnah* is precisely that the husband retains control — he alone decides in what capacity the *get* passes hands.

### Holech vs. Heilech

The *Gemara* pushes back on the proof: *dilma be'heilech* — perhaps the *Mishnah*'s operative word is not *holech* but *heilech*. To modern ears the two sound nearly identical, but they are semantically distinct words. *Holech* connotes *holacha*, delivery. *Heilech* — *hei lach* — means "here it is for you," a direct act of transfer. If the *Mishnah* is speaking of *heilech*, then the case where the husband must explicitly say *ee efshi* to prevent immediate effect is a case of *heilech*, not *holech*, and we have learned nothing about whether *holech* by itself connotes *zachi*. Whether the *Gemara* is proposing an alternative *girsa* in the *Mishnah* or simply raising an interpretive possibility is left somewhat unclear, but the consequence is plain: the question of *holech k'zachi dami* remains open, to be revisited.

### Zachi Definition

The unresolved question — whether *holech*, when addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*, functions as *zachi* — will continue to drive the sugya throughout the daf. As will become evident, this is not merely a question of linguistics but of fundamental agency law: does the contextual framing supplied by the *shaliach* and the wife's prior appointment color the husband's words, or does the plain meaning of *holech* govern regardless of context?

## Gender & Shaliach

### Default Roles

The *Gemara* pivots to a related structural question. *Pshita* — it is obvious — that a man (*ish*) can serve as a *shaliach* to deliver a *get* to the wife (*shaliach l'holacha*), since men are the ones who give *gittin* to their wives. Equally obvious is that a woman can represent the wife to receive the *get* (*shaliach l'kabala*), since women are the ones who receive *gittin*.

### Reverse Roles

But what of the reverse? Can a man serve as a *shaliach l'kabala* — appointed by the wife to receive the *get* — and can a woman serve as a *shaliach l'holacha*, appointed by the husband to deliver it? The *Gemara* raises this as a genuine question. The underlying *hava amina* is drawn from the principle of *shlichus*: an agent must be "in the *parsha*" of the transaction they are performing. Just as a non-Jew cannot serve as an agent for something from which he is excluded by the Torah, perhaps the role of *shaliach l'kabala* is limited to those whose natural position in the *parsha* of *gittin* is that of receiving — i.e., women — and the role of *shaliach l'holacha* is limited to those who naturally give — i.e., men. The *Gemara* considers whether we require not just general inclusion in the subject matter of *gittin* but specific inclusion in the relevant transactional role.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *Mishnah* (*Haomer hitkabel get zeh l'ishti o holech get zeh l'ishti*): a husband appoints an agent to receive or convey the *get*, and then a woman appoints someone (*Hitkabel li giti*) to receive it. The *Gemara* wonders whether the *Mishnah* speaks of the same individual serving both roles — in which case we would have proof that whoever is valid for one role is valid for the other. But the *Gemara* dismisses this: *lo, bishnei sheluchim* — perhaps the *Mishnah* refers to two different agents, a man appointed by the husband and a woman appointed by the wife, respectively.

The *Gemara* then turns to the case in the *Mishnah* where the husband explicitly switches the agent from *shaliach l'kabala* to *shaliach l'holacha* (*ee efshi shetikabel lah ela holech v'ten lah*). Here it is clear we are dealing with one and the same person operating in both capacities. From this it follows: *kashar l'kabala kashar l'holacha* — whoever is valid to receive is valid to deliver. But the *Gemara* again limits the proof: *pshot minah* — we may conclude that one person can serve in both capacities, but perhaps only a man, given the precedent that a father receives a *get* on behalf of his minor daughter (*av mekabel get l'vito ktanah*). A man is therefore "in the *parsha*" of receiving a *get*, but the question of whether a woman can deliver remains unresolved.

### Women as Deliverers

Rav Mari resolves this by citing an earlier *Mishnah*: *af nashim she'einam ne'emanot lomar met baalah ne'emanot lehaviv et gittah* — women who are not believed to testify that a husband has died (such as the mother-in-law and sister-in-law) are nonetheless believed to deliver a *get* and to declare *bifnai nichtav uvifnai nechtam*. Here women are explicitly functioning as agents of delivery. Rav Ashi adds that the *seifa* of that *Mishnah* is even more striking: *ha'ishah atzmah mevia et gitah* — the woman herself can deliver her own *get*, and we established that this refers to a case of *holacha*. Even if it is conceptually unusual to speak of a woman serving as her own *shaliach l'holacha*, the legal point is clear: women can perform the act of delivery. With this, the *Gemara* establishes black and white that both men and women may serve in either capacity.

## Misrepresentation

### Agent's Lie

The *Gemara* now introduces a series of cases in which the *shaliach* misrepresents his role to the husband. The woman tells her agent *havei li giti* — go bring me my *get*, which is understood as appointing him as a *shaliach l'holacha* on behalf of the husband, or at minimum as someone who will facilitate delivery. The agent then goes to the husband and reports: *ishtecha amra hitkabel li giti* — your wife wants me to receive her *get* for her, i.e., to serve as *shaliach l'kabala*. This is a misrepresentation, perhaps not intentional, but a misrepresentation nonetheless.

The husband then responds: *heilech kemah she'amrah* — "here it is, as she said." The question is what the husband means by "as she said." Does he mean as you are representing what she said — following the agent's report — or what she actually said?

### Relying on Agent

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha citing Rav, rules: *afilu higia get l'yadah einah megureshet* — even if the *get* ultimately reaches the wife's hand, she is not divorced. The reasoning: the husband said *heilech kemah she'amrah*, meaning he is relying on the agent's representation. He believes the agent is a *shaliach l'kabala* and intends the *get* to take effect immediately upon the agent receiving it. But in reality the wife never appointed this man as her *shaliach l'kabala*, so no valid *kabala* occurs. And since the husband never designated him as his *shaliach l'holacha* either, the subsequent delivery to the wife is also legally empty. *Shma mina adibura didei kasamich* — the husband's words are pegged to what the agent says, not to what the wife actually instructed.

The reverse inference follows: *d'i adibura didah kasamich* — if instead the husband's intent tracked what the wife actually said, then since she truly wanted *holacha*, he would be implicitly appointing the agent as his *shaliach l'holacha*, and the *get* should work when delivered. The fact that Rav holds it does not work proves that the husband operates on the basis of the agent's report, not the wife's actual words.

### Rav Ashi Objects

Rav Ashi raises an objection: *hachi hashta* — what kind of comparison is this? He notes that the case under discussion, in which the wife said *kabala* but the agent reported *holacha*, cannot serve as the basis for the broader rule about *adibura didei kasamich*, because the consequences in this case happen to be symmetrical in a way that obscures which principle is operative.

Rav Ashi constructs the mirror-image scenario: suppose the wife had said *hitkabel li giti* — appoint yourself as my *shaliach l'kabala* — and the agent instead reports to the husband *havei li giti*, "she wants me to deliver the *get*." The husband says *heilech kemah she'amrah*. Now: if the husband is going by the agent's words, he is genuinely appointing the agent as his *shaliach l'holacha*, and the *get* should work when delivered. If instead he is going by what she actually said, he is in effect ratifying the *kabala* appointment, and she would be divorced the moment the agent receives the *get*. According to Rav Nachman, who rules that it works right away in this scenario (*kivan she'higia l'yado megureshet*), we would conclude *dibura didah hu d'kasamich* — the husband tracks her actual words, not the agent's representation.

But Rav Ashi argues that these two scenarios are not equivalent. In the original case — where the agent reports *kabala* but was actually appointed for *holacha* — the reason the *get* fails is not because the husband tracks the agent's words. The reason is that the agent, by declaring himself a *shaliach l'kabala*, is effectively *rejecting* the role he was assigned. He was sent to deliver the *get* back to the wife; by announcing himself as a *shaliach l'kabala*, he is declining that mission. The *Gemara* articulates this: *de'amar shaliach l'kabala havina l'holacha lo havina* — he has repudiated the *holacha* role, and since he is not validly a *shaliach l'kabala* either, nothing works. This has nothing to do with whether the husband follows the agent's words or the wife's; the failure is internal to the agent's own act of rejection.

### Rejecting Delivery Role

The *Gemara* thus establishes a principle: when a *shaliach* makes his mission easier by claiming a different role than the one he was assigned — specifically, by claiming *kabala* when he was designated for *holacha*, thereby ending his obligation once he receives the *get* rather than carrying it back — we interpret this claim as a rejection of the original role. Since he cannot validly occupy either position (not the wife's *shaliach l'kabala* because she never appointed him thus, not the husband's *shaliach l'holacha* because he has declined it), the *get* has no legal effect.

The consequence is that from Rav Nachman's ruling we cannot directly derive the broader principle about *adibura didei kasamich*. The *Gemara* leaves this as an open question, pursued through the cases that follow.

## Contextual Interpretation

### Reinterpretation Rules

Rav Huna bar Chiya introduces a new *ta shma* from the *Mishnah*: *ha'omer hitkabel get zeh l'ishti o holech get zeh l'ishti ratza lachzor yachzor* — if the husband says "receive this *get* for my wife" or "convey it to her," he can retract. This is the *Mishnah*'s basic case. The *Gemara* asks an arresting question: *ve'amai habaal lav bar shavei shaliach l'kabala hu?* — why does the *Mishnah* treat this as a valid appointment at all? The husband says *hitkabel*, "receive it," which literally implies a *shaliach l'kabala* appointment — but the husband cannot appoint a *shaliach l'kabala* for his wife; only she can do that.

The answer is that we do not read his words with rigid literalism. *Keivan shenatan einav l'garsha* — since it is clear he intends to divorce her, *meimar amar tigaraish b'chol heichi demigaraisha* — we interpret his words in whatever way will make a valid *get* possible. When he says "receive it for my wife," the only legally operative reading is "deliver it to my wife," and so we interpret it accordingly. The husband is presumed to want the *get* to work, and we bend his words toward the only legally valid form they can take.

The *Gemara* then asks: *hacha nami* — why not apply the same principle in our case of misrepresentation? Even if the agent is claiming *kabala* when he was designated for *holacha*, should we not interpret the husband's words (*heilech kemah she'amrah*) in whatever way will make the *get* valid?

### Law vs. Fact

The *Gemara* draws a sharp distinction: *hatam ihu yada de'ein shlichut l'kabala gabeih venachti l'shem holacha* — in the *Mishnah*'s basic case, any person who knew the *halacha* would have understood that the husband cannot appoint a *shaliach l'kabala*, and so we may say that deep down he intended the only legally valid interpretation: *holacha*. His error, if any, was one of legal expression, not substantive intent. *Gamar ve-natan l'shem holacha* — at the moment he acted, he implicitly conformed to the legal requirement that would make his transaction work.

But *hacha ta'i* — in the case of misrepresentation, the husband's error is not about law but about fact. He genuinely believes the agent is the wife's *shaliach l'kabala*. Given those (mistaken) facts, his stated intention would have been perfectly valid. We therefore cannot say he "really meant" something else; he meant exactly what he said, under a mistaken assumption about the facts. Since his intent — as he understood it — was coherent and legal under his own perception of reality, we have no basis to reinterpret his words. The distinction, then, is: mistake of law permits reinterpretation toward the legal form, because we assume a person wishes to act in accordance with law; mistake of fact does not, because the person's stated intent was already internally consistent.

### Minor Wife Case

Rava introduces a further *ta shma*: *ketana she'amra hitkabel li giti eino get ad she'yagi'a get l'yadah* — a minor girl cannot appoint a *shaliach*, so if she tells someone to receive her *get*, it is not a *get* until the *get* reaches her hand. The *Gemara* presses: but when she gets it, is she even then divorced? After all, if the husband gave the *get* to this person with the intent that he receive it for her as a *shaliach l'kabala*, and that appointment was void, the husband never designated him as his own *shaliach l'holacha* — so how does delivery to her work at all?

Abaye answers: *kevan de'natan me'amen l'gareshah meimar megaresh k'heichi demigarsha* — since it is evident the husband intends to divorce her, we interpret the very act of handing the *get* to this person as a *shaliach l'holacha* appointment, even though he never said so explicitly. We reinterpret his act to align with the only legally operative mode by which divorce could occur.

The *Gemara* immediately limits this ruling: *hatam ihu yada de'ein shlichus l'katan* — there, the husband should know that a minor cannot appoint a *shaliach*, and therefore *gamar v'nasa l'shum halakha l'halakhta didei* — his act of giving the *get* is understood as an implicit *shaliach l'holacha* designation, because that is the only legally valid framework for what he was trying to accomplish. *Hacha ta'i* — but in the misrepresentation case, the error is factual, and the same logic does not apply.

The *Gemara* then continues with a *tanu rabbanan* that presents multiple permutations — a *shaliach* reporting *kabala* when appointed for *holacha*, and vice versa, combined with various responses from the husband (*holech v'ten lah*, *zechi lah v'hitkablah*) — all converging on the same ruling: she is divorced only *mishe'yagi'a get l'yadah*, upon actual receipt. The *Gemara* considers whether this *baraita* proves that *dibura didei kasamich* (the husband tracks the agent's report) or *dibura didah* (the husband tracks her actual words), but in each permutation the *Gemara* finds a way to read the ruling as consistent with either interpretation. The back-and-forth concludes without directly disproving Rav Nachman, though the weight of the analysis leans against his categorical formulation.

## Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Na…

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *Gemara* now presents a *baraita* that engages the core *machlokes* directly. The case: the agent presents himself as the wife's *shaliach l'kabala*, and the husband says *holech v'ten lah* — "go and give it to her." The *tanna kamma* rules: *ratza lachzor lo yachzir* — he may not retract. The *tanna kamma* further holds the same for *zechi lah v'hitkablah* — "acquire it for her and receive it." In both formulations, the act is immediately binding. This is the *tanna kamma*'s position that *holech k'zachi* — that *holech*, addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*, conveys immediate legal effect.

Rabbi, whose position is then stated, agrees with the *tanna kamma* on this fundamental point: *be-chulan im ratza lachzor lo yachzir* — in all these formulations he cannot retract. However, Rabbi adds an important qualification: *aval im amar lo ee efshi she'tikabel lah ela holech v'ten lah* — if the husband preceded *holech* with the explicit rejection *ee efshi*, then *ratza lachzor yachzir* — he can retract. This is precisely the case from the opening of our sugya: when *holech* is framed by an explicit *ee efshi*, it reverts to its plain meaning of delivery, not immediate *zachi*. The *Gemara* notes: *Rabbi hainu tanna kamma* — Rabbi's position is essentially the same as the *tanna kamma*'s. The *Gemara* explains this by saying that Rabbi is either clarifying the force of the *ee efshi* formulation, or identifying who the *tanna kamma* is.

### Rabbi Natan Position

Rabbi Natan takes a fundamentally different view: *holech v'ten lah ratza lachzor yachzor* — even when the husband says *holech* in the context of a *shaliach l'kabala*, he may retract. *Holech*, for Rabbi Natan, never means *zachi*, regardless of context. However, *zechi lah v'hitkablah ratza lachzor lo yachzor* — when the husband uses an explicit *zechi* or *hitkabel* formulation, it is immediately binding.

This makes the debate precise: the *tanna kamma* (and Rabbi) hold that *holech k'zachi* when addressed to a *shaliach l'kabala*; Rabbi Natan holds that even in that context *holech* means only delivery. The *Gemara* observes that this debate maps directly onto the question raised at the beginning of the *perek*: is *holech k'zachi dami*?

The *Gemara* then raises a question about Rabbi Natan: does he agree that *heilech* is *k'zachi*? Logically it seems obvious — *heilech* is *hei lach*, "here it is for you," an unmistakable act of transfer, entirely different from *holech*. The only reason one might hesitate is because the words sound superficially similar. But substantively they are entirely distinct.

### Explicit Distinction

The *Gemara* introduces a *baraita* that makes the distinction explicit: *holech get zeh l'ishti ratza lachzor yachzor* — if a husband says *holech*, he can retract; *heilech get zeh l'ishti ratza lachzor lo yachzor* — if he says *heilech*, he cannot. The *Gemara* asks: *man sham'at lei d'amar b'holech ratza lachzor yachzor* — who holds that *holech* allows retraction even when the agent is a *shaliach l'kabala*? Rabbi Natan. And this same *baraita* says that *heilech* does not allow retraction. *Shma mina heilech k'zachi dami shma mina* — we therefore conclude that even according to Rabbi Natan, *heilech* is *k'zachi*, which is sensible, since the words are semantically unrelated despite their acoustic similarity.

The final resolution: the question of *holech k'zachi* is a *machlokes tannaim* between the *tanna kamma* (and Rabbi) on one side, and Rabbi Natan on the other. The *tanna kamma* holds *holech k'zachi* in the context of a *shaliach l'kabala*; Rabbi Natan holds it is not, but *heilech k'zachi* according to all opinions.

## Holech Zachi Doubt

### Dual Agency

Having established the *machlokes*, the *Gemara* returns to a case that generates genuine uncertainty even in practice. The wife's agent presents himself to the husband as a *shaliach l'kabala*. The husband responds: *holech v'ten lah*. This is exactly the disputed case — does *holech* here mean *zachi*, following the *tanna kamma*, or mere delivery, following Rabbi Natan?

The *Gemara* asks: *l'meimra d'misfaka lei l'Rav ee holech k'zachi dami o lav k'zachi dami* — does this mean that Rav himself is uncertain about the *halacha* in this dispute?

### Both Agents

Rav Abba, citing Rav Huna citing Rav, rules: *na'aseh shlucho u'shlucha* — the agent becomes simultaneously the husband's agent and the wife's agent. This is, as the *Gemara* effectively acknowledges, a way of expressing genuine uncertainty. The wife appointed him as her *shaliach l'kabala*; the husband's *holech* may or may not have ratified that role or instead designated him as a *shaliach l'holacha*. We cannot determine which.

### Safek Status

The practical consequence flows from this ambiguity: *vechol sheken* — and certainly, once she receives the *get*, she is divorced *mima nafshach*, regardless. The uncertainty pertains to the moment before she receives it. If the husband or the wife dies in the interim, the critical question becomes: is she an *almanah* (widow, who may perform *yibbum*) or a *grushah* (divorcee, who may not)? Rav's answer is that she must perform *chalitzah* and cannot perform *yibbum* — a practical expression of *safek*, because if she were certainly an *almanah* she could perform *yibbum*, and if she were certainly a *grushah* she would need neither. The requirement of *chalitzah* reflects genuine doubt as to her status.

### Money Analogy

The *Gemara* then observes that Rav himself seems to have ruled in an analogous monetary case: *holech maneh l'ploni she'ani chayav lo* — "go bring a hundred dinars to so-and-so whom I owe" — Rav rules *chayav b'achrayuto*, the sender remains responsible for the debt (since the recipient never appointed this person to collect on his behalf), but *v'im ba lachazor eino chozer* — the sender cannot demand the money back, because the *shaliach*'s possession functions to some degree as acquisition for the recipient. This seems to imply that *holech* means *zachi* even in a non-*get* context. The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *hatam safek mamona l'kula hacha safek issura l'chumra* — there, in a monetary case, a *safek* defaults toward leniency (the money remains in the *shaliach*'s hands), but here, in a matter of marital status, a *safek* requires stringency. Both are cases of *safek*; the difference lies only in how *safek* is resolved in each domain.

The conclusion of this entire discussion, then, is that the question of *holech k'zachi* is an unresolved *machlokes tannaim*, and even at the level of *amoraim* Rav leaves it as a *safek*, applying domain-appropriate rules to navigate the uncertainty.

## Two Agents Question

### Rav's Restriction

The *Gemara* now introduces a new ruling from Rav: *ein ha'isha osa shaliach l'kabel gitah miyad shaliach ba'alah* — a woman may not appoint an agent to receive her *get* from the husband's agent. Rabbi Chanina disagrees: *isha osa shaliach l'kabel gitah miyad shaliach ba'alah* — she may.

The scenario is one in which neither the husband nor the wife wishes to be present at the moment of delivery. The husband appoints an agent to deliver the *get*; the wife wishes to appoint her own agent to receive it from that *shaliach*. In principle, if each party may have a *shaliach*, why should two agents interposing between the parties be invalid? The question is what concern underlies Rav's restriction.

### Honor Concern

The *Gemara* offers one possible reason: *mishum bizayon d'baal* — out of concern for the honor of the husband. The husband, who holds all the legal power in this transaction, may feel affronted if his wife does not personally receive the *get* or attend to the matter herself. Even though the husband has appointed his own *shaliach* and will not be personally present either, there may be an implicit expectation that the wife — the recipient — will receive the *get* herself. If the husband learned after the fact that she had sent a *shaliach*, he could claim: had I known she intended to send an agent, I never would have authorized my *shaliach* to give it to someone other than her personally. In the case where the husband himself hands the *get* to the wife's *shaliach*, no such complaint is possible — he saw the situation and consented. But when it is his *shaliach* acting, and he is not present, the concern of *bizayon d'baal* becomes operative.

### Indirect Acquisition

The second possible reason: *mishum chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* — a concern rooted in the laws of *chatzer*, an enclosed domain that can effect acquisition. The rule is that if I place a *get* in a field that at that moment belongs to the wife, she acquires it and is divorced. But if the field was not yet hers at the moment of placement — if she subsequently purchased the field with the *get* already inside — she is not divorced, because acquisition via *chatzer* must be contemporaneous with ownership.

The concern here is analogous. The husband's *shaliach* holds the *get* in his hands in the capacity of the husband's agent. If the wife's *shaliach* then "receives" the *get* from him without any active transfer occurring — especially if her *shaliach* was appointed after the husband's *shaliach* was already in possession of the *get* — this resembles the case of a field acquired after the *get* was placed in it. There was no genuine moment of delivery; the *get* simply transitioned from one holding to another without a clear act of *netina*. The *Gemara* distinguishes: if the wife had appointed her *shaliach* before the husband appointed his, the concern of *chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* is less applicable, since her *shaliach*'s status pre-dates the husband's *shaliach*'s possession.

*Tosafot* suggests a more straightforward *nafka mina* between the two reasons: if the husband explicitly states that he is content for his *shaliach* to deliver to the wife's *shaliach*, the concern of *bizayon d'baal* is obviously inapplicable — he has waived any such objection. Whether the divorce is valid in that case depends entirely on whether the *chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* concern survives the husband's explicit consent, which it might, since that concern is rabbinic in character and not dependent on the husband's subjective preference. An additional *nafka mina* concerns *b'diavad*: if the restriction is grounded in honor, perhaps *b'diavad* the *get* is valid; if it is grounded in the acquisition concern, *b'diavad* may be more stringent.

### Rabbi Chanina Permits

Rabbi Chanina holds that the wife may appoint her own agent to receive from the husband's agent. The *Gemara* reports that Rav Nachman said: *im ita l'deRabbi Chanina avdinan uvda* — had Rabbi Chanina's ruling been before me in the relevant case, I would have ruled accordingly. The case in question: a man sent a *get* to his wife; the *shaliach* found her kneading dough and therefore her hands were occupied. He said to her *heilech giteich* — "here is your *get*." She replied: *leihavei b'yadech* — "you hold it for me," thereby attempting to turn the husband's *shaliach l'holacha* into her *shaliach l'kabala*.

### One vs. Two Agents

Rava immediately objects to Rav Nachman: *im ita d'Rabbi Chanina, avdas bei uvda? Halo hadra shlichus eitzel habaal* — even according to Rabbi Chanina, would you really have ruled that way? What Rabbi Chanina permits is the scenario of two genuine agents: the husband's *shaliach* gives the *get* to the wife's *shaliach*, with both parties maintaining their distinct roles. What the woman in this case attempted was fundamentally different: she tried to convert the husband's *shaliach* himself into her *shaliach*, not by receiving from him but by having him change sides.

This is the key distinction. When there are two separate agents, there is a genuine act of delivery: the husband's *shaliach* gives, and the wife's *shaliach* receives. Both identities persist through and after the act. But when the wife attempts to transform the husband's *shaliach* into her own — as in this case, where she says "you hold it for me" — there is no moment of delivery at all. The agent's identity would have to shift from representing the husband to representing the wife without any intervening act of *netina*. The *Gemara* phrases this as *hadra shlichus eitzel habaal* — the *shlichus* would have to revert, or more precisely, there would be no point at which the *shliach* could return to the husband and report: "I have completed the delivery," because his identity changed before the act was accomplished.

This case, then, is not what Rabbi Chanina had in mind. Rabbi Chanina's leniency applies to two distinct agents occupying their respective roles simultaneously. Transforming a single agent's identity mid-transaction is a categorically different problem — one that closely resembles the very concern of *chatzer haba'ah me'achareha* that underlies Rav's restriction, inasmuch as there is never a genuine, identifiable moment when the *get* passes from one party's legal domain to another's. This distinction will be taken up further in the continuation of the sugya.
