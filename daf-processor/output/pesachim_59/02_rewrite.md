# Pesachim 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamid & Pesach Order

### Tamid Timing

The fifth *perek* of *Pesachim*, *Tamid Nishchat*, opens a vast central section of the tractate — roughly fifty *daf* in length, equal in size to all the rest of *Pesachim* combined — that deals almost exclusively with *korbanos* and the bringing of the *korban pesach*. One could easily imagine this material having been placed in *Seder Kodashim*.

The *gemara* picks up from where the previous *daf* concluded, at the bottom of 58b, two lines from the end. The *korban tamid* — the daily afternoon sacrifice — is ordinarily slaughtered at 8.5 halachic hours (2:30 PM) and offered on the *mizbe'ach* at 9.5 hours (3:30 PM). On *erev Pesach*, however, it was brought earlier to allow sufficient time for the *korban pesach*, and when *erev Pesach* fell on *erev Shabbat*, it was moved even earlier still — as early as 6.5 hours (12:30 PM), the earliest permissible time for *mincha* and therefore the earliest the afternoon *tamid* can be brought. The underlying assumption is that the *korban pesach* comes after the afternoon *tamid*, even though the general rule is that no *korban* follows the afternoon *tamid* — it is the last of the day's *korbanos*.

### Erev & Bein Arbayim

The *baraisa* cited at the top of 59a articulates the hierarchy: *tamid kodem l'Pesach* — the *tamid* comes before the *korban pesach*; *Pesach kodem l'ketores* — the *pesach* precedes the afternoon *ketores*; and *haketores kodemes l'neiros* — the *ketores* precedes the lighting of the *menorah*. These two final services — burning the incense and lighting the *neiros* — were performed not in the outer *azarah* but in the inner *heichal*, the roofed interior of the *Beit Hamikdash*. The question is how we know any of this ordering, and the *gemara* proceeds to derive it.

*Ye'achar davar shene'emar bo ba'erev u'vein ha'arbayim* — something is placed later when the *Torah* describes it using both the term *erev* and the term *bein ha'arbayim*. By the *korban pesach*, the *Torah* uses both: *tishchat es haPesach ba'erev* (Deuteronomy 16:6) and *v'shachatu oso kol kehal adas Yisrael bein ha'arbayim* (Exodus 12:6). This double language of the afternoon period signals that the *pesach* comes as late as possible in the day. By contrast, the *tamid* uses only the single term *bein ha'arbayim*: *es hakeves hasheni ta'ase bein ha'arbayim* (Numbers 28:4). Therefore the *korban pesach*, marked by the double afternoon language, comes later than the *tamid*, which carries only the single term.

### Erev Definition

The terms deserve clarification. *Erev* does not mean night — it means the darkening, the waning of the day, the late afternoon. The word shares a root with *ma'arav*, the west, because the sun begins to set toward the west as the afternoon progresses. *Bein ha'arbayim* — literally "between the two *erevs*" — spans from the moment the sun passes its midday zenith and begins its descent to the moment it actually dips below the western horizon. The entire afternoon period thus constitutes *bein ha'arbayim*, while *layla* proper begins only with nightfall.

This is also the occasion to note the important *Rashbam* at the very beginning of the *Torah* on *vayehi erev vayehi voker yom echad*. The *Rashbam* argues that the *pshat* is not that the day begins with the night. Rather, *vayehi erev* means the sun set at the end of the day of creation, and *vayehi voker* means it rose the following morning, completing the previous day. In this reading, *erev* and *boker* demarcate the natural day — from sunrise to daybreak — rather than the halachic day that begins at nightfall.

### Pesach Double Language

Returning to the *gemara*'s argument: the double language of *erev* and *bein ha'arbayim* by the *korban pesach* establishes that it comes later than something that carries only the single term *bein ha'arbayim* — which is the *tamid*. This derives the first element of the ordering.

The *gemara* then raises an immediate question: if that is the logic, why shouldn't the *ketores* and *neiros* also precede the *korban pesach*? Those services likewise use only the single term *bein ha'arbayim*, not the double language. By the same reasoning, the *pesach* — with its double language — should come even after the *ketores* and *neiros*. This is precisely the question the *gemara* takes up.

### Menora & Incense

The *gemara* answers: *shani hasam dimi'at Rachmana oso* — there it is different, because the *Torah* excludes by the word *oso*. A *baraisa* is cited: the verse says *yaaroch oso Aharon u'vanav me'erev ad boker* — Aaron and his sons shall arrange *it*, the *menorah*, from evening until morning (Exodus 27:21). From *me'erev ad boker* we derive that one must provide enough oil for the *menorah* to burn through even the longest winter night. But a second teaching is derived as well: *ein lecha avoda shekesheira me'erev ad boker ela zo bilvad* — this is the only *avoda* in the *Beit Hamikdash* that runs from evening until morning. Why? Because the verse says *yaaroch oso* — *it*, and only *it*, spans the entire night. Nothing else runs from evening through morning.

### Menora Night Service

The force of the word *oso* is understood on two levels. It limits the nighttime duration to the *neiros* alone, but it also implies something about when the *hadlakas neiros* begins: precisely because the *menorah* is meant to burn throughout the entire night, starting from *me'erev*, it must be lit as late as possible in the afternoon, right at the threshold of nightfall. *Oso* therefore teaches that only the *neiros* begin at the very end of the afternoon and extend through the night — nothing else shares that property. This is how the *gemara* establishes that the *neiros* occupy the absolute latest slot in the daily sequence of *avodot*.

A student raises the intuitive point that one lights candles when it begins to grow dark precisely so they can be seen and tended before full darkness sets in — especially since the *avodah* in the *Beit Hamikdash* was fundamentally a daytime service. Nighttime activity in the *mikdash* was generally limited to completion work, such as burning leftover fats; active *avodah* was a daytime enterprise. Lighting the *neiros* at the very last moment of the day, while one can still see, makes practical sense on both grounds.

## Ketores & Neiros

### Ketores Linked

The *gemara* adds: *v'ikish ketores l'neiros* — the *ketores* is linked to the *neiros* through a shared verse. The verse in Exodus 30:8 reads: *u've'haalos Aharon es ha-neros bein ha'arbayim yaktirenah* — and when Aaron raises the flames of the *menorah* in the afternoon, he shall burn the incense. Because the two services appear together in a single verse, they are understood to occur at or near the same time, both toward the end of the afternoon.

### Pesach Before

The first opinion — which the *gemara* ultimately endorses — holds that the *korban pesach* comes before the *ketores* and *neiros*. The sequence is: *tamid*, then *pesach*, then *ketores*, then *neiros*.

### Neiros Last

The *gemara* then introduces a *baraisa* that actually sides with the position the questioner raised: *tamid kodem l'ketores, ketores kodemes l'neiros, v'neiros kodmin l'Pesach* — the *tamid* comes first, then the *ketores*, then the *neiros*, and only then the *korban pesach*. The *baraisa*'s reasoning is precisely the double-language argument: since *pesach* says both *erev* and *bein ha'arbayim*, it should come last of everything.

### Oso Exclusion

The *gemara* challenges this *baraisa*: but the verse said *oso* — only the *neiros* extend from evening to morning, and from that exclusion we derived that the *neiros* come at the very latest. How can the *pesach* then come even after the *neiros*?

The answer is that the word *oso* is needed for a different limitation: *le'mi'utei avoda she'bifnim* — to tell us that the *neiros* come last only among the services performed *bifnim*, inside the *heichal*. The *heichal* housed the *menorah*, the *shulchan*, and the golden *mizbe'ach* — all under a roof, as distinct from the outdoor *azarah*. The word *oso* therefore says: of the interior services, the *neiros* come absolutely last. Between the two interior services — *ketores* and *neiros* — the *neiros* come after.

### Ketores First

But why would one have thought the *ketores* should come after the *neiros*? Because the verse reads *u've'haalos Aharon es ha-neros bein ha'arbayim yaktirenah* — when Aaron raises the *menorah*, he shall burn the incense. Taken at face value, this sounds as though you first light the *menorah* and then burn the *ketores*. The *gemara* therefore needed *oso* to teach otherwise: *yaktirenah* — he shall be burning the incense, meaning the *ketores* is already burning at the time he lights the *menorah*. The *ketores* precedes the *neiros*. The preposition *be'haalos* functions as a temporal marker — while he is lighting, the *ketores* should already be underway — not as a sequential indicator.

Thus the *gemara* has accounted for both pieces: the *neiros* come last of the interior services because of *oso*; the *ketores* precedes the *neiros* because of the verse *u've'haalos*; and the *korban pesach* comes before both *ketores* and *neiros* according to the primary opinion, while coming after the *tamid* because of its double afternoon language.

## Morning Ketores

### Ketores Earliest

A new *baraisa* is introduced: *ein lecha davar she'kodem l'Tamid shel shachar ela ketores bilvad* — nothing precedes the morning *tamid* except the *ketores* alone. The morning *ketores* and the afternoon *ketores* thus bracket the entire day's service from both ends, just as the morning and afternoon *tamid* bracket the *korbanos*. The *ketores* frames the day even more broadly.

The derivation: by the *ketores* the verse says *baboker baboker* — morning, morning — a double language of morning (Exodus 30:7). This parallels the logic of *erev* and *bein ha'arbayim* for the afternoon: double language means that service comes at the most extreme point of the time period. The morning *tamid* says only *baboker* once (*es hakeves echad ta'ase vaboker*, Numbers 28:4), so the *ketores* — with its double *baboker* — precedes even the morning *tamid*.

### Complete Order

The *baraisa* concludes: *v'ein lecha davar she'mit'akev achar Tamid shel bein ha'arbayim ela ketores v'neiros — u'Pesach* — nothing is held back, nothing is postponed until after the afternoon *tamid* except the *ketores*, the *neiros*, and the *pesach*. The complete daily order is therefore: *ketores* (morning), morning *tamid*, [other *korbanos* throughout the day], afternoon *tamid*, *korban pesach*, *ketores* (afternoon), *neiros*.

The word *mit'akev* is notable. In halachic terminology *me'akev* denotes something that is indispensable — without it the requirement is not fulfilled. Here it carries the meaning of being held back or postponed, indicating that these three services alone are performed after the afternoon *tamid*.

## Mechusar Kippurim

### Tahara Scenario

The *baraisa* now introduces a case not yet discussed: *mechusar kippurim b'erev haPesach* — someone who has not completed the purification process on *erev Pesach*. A *zav* or *metzora*, for instance, cannot eat *korbanos* until the final stage of *tahara* is complete, which requires bringing a *korban*. On the fourteenth of Nisan, such a person would want to bring that final *korban*, achieve *tahara*, and be able to eat the *korban pesach* that night.

The scenario that arises: the person has failed to bring the *korban* before the afternoon *tamid*. Normally, it is too late. But not bringing it means being unable to eat the *korban pesach* that evening. Should an exception be made?

### Tanna Kamma Allow

The *tanna kamma* says yes: *she'tovel v'shoneh v'ochel pesach l'erev* — on *erev Pesach*, a *mechusar kippurim* who has missed the window may still bring the *korban* after the afternoon *tamid*, immerse once more, and eat the *korban pesach* that night. The weight of the *korban pesach* obligation justifies the exception.

### Rabbi Yishmael Expand

*Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka* goes further: *af mechusar kippurim b'she'ar yemot hashanah she'tovel v'ochel b'kodashim l'erev* — even on an ordinary day, if a *mechusar kippurim* has already brought some other *korban* that day and now realizes too late that he cannot eat from it without completing his *tahara* process, he may bring the completing *korban* after the afternoon *tamid* in order to eat from the *korban* brought earlier that day. The concern here is the obligation to eat *korbanos* — there is a *mitzvah* of eating them.

### Hashlama Question

The *gemara* asks: *bishlema l'tanna kamma* — the *tanna kamma* is understandable. The positive *mitzvah* of *korban pesach*, which carries *kares* for its violation, can override the positive *mitzvah* of *hashlama* — the obligation to complete all *korbanos* before the afternoon *tamid*, derived from the verse *v'hiktir aleiha chelvei ha'shlamim* at the beginning of *Tzav*. The word *hashlamim* is read as *hashlama* — completion — teaching that all *korbanos* should be completed around the morning *tamid*, and certainly before the afternoon *tamid*.

This analysis involves a striking move: what the *Torah* presents as an indication of the *seder hayom* — the ordered sequence of Temple services — is treated by the *gemara* as a genuine *mitzvat asei*. One might have thought the *Torah* was merely describing procedure, leaving open questions about the *kashrus* of things done out of order. But the *gemara* frames the orderly completion of *korbanos* as a positive commandment, such that the weightier *mitzvat asei* of *korban pesach* — reinforced by *kares* — can displace it.

But according to *Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*, who allows this even for ordinary *korbanos*: what makes the *mitzvat asei* of eating a *korban* stronger than the *mitzvat asei* of *hashlama*? Why should we set aside the rule governing the order of Temple services merely so someone can eat their *shelamim*?

### Bird Chatas Only

*Ravina* answers in the name of *Rav Chisda*: *hacha be'chatat ha'of tanan* — we are speaking specifically of a bird *chatat*. By a bird *chatat*, there is no burning on the altar; only blood is applied. And the entire prohibition against performing *avodah* after the afternoon *tamid* is derived from a verse about burning on the altar — specifically the passage at the beginning of *Tzav* (*zot torat ha'olah... v'hiktir aleiha chelvei ha'shlamim*), which speaks throughout about what is burned on the *mizbe'ach*. If the only act performed is sprinkling blood — with nothing burned on the altar — the prohibition has not technically been violated. *Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka*, on this reading, permitted only the narrow case where all that remained was a bird *chatat*, requiring no burning.

### Tomorrow Burning

*Rav Pappa* disagrees and offers a different resolution: *afilu teima be'chatat behemah* — even a regular animal *chatat* can be accommodated. His solution: *ma'aleinan b'rosh ha'mizbe'ach* — after sprinkling the blood, you do not burn the *emurim* that night. Instead, you lift the fat and innards up and place them on top of the *mizbe'ach* — not on the fire, but on the surrounding ledge. The rule of *lina* — invalidation due to being left overnight — does not apply to offerings already resting on the *mizbe'ach* itself. Then the following morning, after the morning *tamid*, you burn the fat. The critical act of the *korban* — throwing the blood on the altar — has been performed; the owner's obligation is essentially effected thereby. The burning of the *emurim*, while required, is deferred without invalidating the *korban*.

### Other Korbanos

The *gemara* then asks: *v'ha'ika asham* — what about the *asham*? A *metzora*, for instance, must bring not only a *chatat* but also an *asham* and an *olah*. *Rav Pappa*'s solution extends naturally to all these: in each case, sprinkle the blood and defer the burning until the next morning via placement on top of the *mizbe'ach*. But for *Ravina*, who limited the allowance to the bird *chatat* — which has no burning component — the *asham* poses a serious problem, since an *asham* cannot be brought as a bird.

*Ravina* would have to say that we are speaking of a scenario where the *asham* and *olah* had already been brought earlier in the day, leaving only the bird *chatat* outstanding. But the *gemara* challenges this: could you really say the *olah* was brought before the *chatat*? A *baraisa* teaches explicitly from the verse *v'hikriv es asher la'chatat rishona* (Leviticus 5:8) — and it shall bring the *chatat* first — that this is a *binyan av* applying to all cases: whenever a *chatat* and an *olah* come together, the *chatat* precedes. The *chatat* cleanses — whether from sin or from *tumah* — and only once cleansed can the person draw close to God through the *olah*. The *olah* cannot precede the *chatat*.

### Chatas Before Olah

The principle is emphatic: *le'chol chatos she'kodemes le'chol olos haba'os imahen* — every *chatat* precedes every *olah* that comes together with it. This is stated as a universal rule derived from the *binyan av*. Moreover, *kayma lan* — it is established — that *afilu chatat ha'of kodemes l'olat behemah* — even a bird *chatat*, inexpensive as it is, precedes an animal *olah*. So there is no way to describe a scenario where the *olah* was already brought before the bird *chatat*.

### Olat Metzora

*Rava* resolves this by noting a special leniency for the *olat metzora* specifically. The verse in Leviticus 14 reads *v'he'elah ha'Kohen et ha'olah* — using the *hif'il* perfect form rather than the future imperfect *ya'aleh*. The *drasha*: *she'he'elah kevar* — even if the *olah* had already been brought up, that is acceptable. For a *metzora*, uniquely, the *olah* may precede the *chatat*, because the *Torah*'s formulation implies that even a situation in which the *olah* already happened is accommodated. This resolves the *gemara*'s challenge to *Ravina*: one can construct a valid scenario where the *olah* was brought before the *chatat* — but only in the case of a *metzora*, owing to this special verse.

## Emurim Overnight Risk

### Kohanim Risk

The discussion now turns to a practical challenge against *Rav Pappa*'s solution. *Rav Shemen bar Abba* asks *Rav Pappa*: *le'didach*, according to your approach — that you lift the fats onto the top of the *mizbe'ach* (*ma'alu u'melina be'rosho shel mizbe'ach*) and leave them there overnight to be burned the next morning — are we going to arrange a situation that leads the *kohanim* to stumble? A *kohen* ascending the *mizbe'ach* at night to tend the fire will see meat and fat resting there. He will naturally assume it is leftover material from *korbanos* brought earlier in the day — which is precisely what is burned during the overnight hours. He will not realize this was placed there intentionally to be left until morning, and he will burn it, thereby causing a violation of the principle that nothing brought after the afternoon *tamid* may be burned on the altar.

The *gemara* treats this potential violation as a genuine *takalah* — a stumbling, a transgression — reflecting how seriously it takes the rule of *hashlama* and the prohibition against burning something on the altar that derives from a *korban* offered after the afternoon *tamid*.

### Kohanim Careful

*Rav Pappa* responds: *kohanim zerizim hein* — *kohanim* are scrupulous and careful. They will inform one another: the material in that corner is not to be touched; it waits until morning. Their diligence and mutual oversight ensure no inadvertent violation.

### Meat Eating Rule

*Rav Huna bar Rav Nasan* then raises a different and deeper challenge. As long as the *emurim* have not been burned — *kol zman d'lo niskatru emurim* — the *kohanim* cannot eat the meat: *kohanim lo matzi achli basar*. The source: a *baraisa* asks whether *kohanim* might eat the breast and thigh of a *shelamim* before the fats have been burned. The verse teaches: *v'hiktir ha'Kohen es hachelev ha'mizbeicha* — only after the *kohen* has burned the fat on the altar does the *Torah* say *v'hayah hachazeh l'Aharon u'l'vanav* — the breast belongs to Aaron and his sons. The sequence is fixed: burn the *emurim* first, then the *kohanim* may eat.

This generates a cascade: if the *kohanim* cannot eat the meat, the owners are not atoned. The source for this: *v'achlu osam asher kuppar bahem* — they shall eat them, those through whom atonement is made. This is the basis for the concept of *mishulchan gavo'ah kacha'u* — the *kohanim* eat from God's table, as it were; their consumption in the sacred precincts is an extension of the altar's consumption. First God's portion — the *emurim* — is offered; then the *kohanim* eat their portion; and through that entire process the owners' atonement is complete.

The difficulty is now acute: the whole purpose of *Rav Pappa*'s scheme was to enable the person to achieve *tahara* and eat the *korban pesach* that night. But if the *emurim* cannot be burned until morning, the *kohanim* cannot eat the meat that night, and therefore the owner's atonement — which was the prerequisite for *tahara* — is not complete.

### Lost Meat Rule

The *gemara* resolves this with a principle of *de facto* impossibility. *Kivan d'lo efshar* — since it is not possible to burn the fat tonight — the Rabbis treat it as if the meat were lost. A *baraisa* supports this: *yachol nitmu imurin o she'avdu lo yiheyu kohanim zochin ba'chazeh v'shok* — one might think that if the fats became *tamei* or were lost, the *kohanim* cannot eat their portion. The verse teaches otherwise: *v'hayah hachazeh l'Aharon u'l'vanav* — the breast belongs to Aaron and his sons regardless. *Mikol makom* — in any case, even without the burning of the *emurim*, the *kohanim* eat and the owners are atoned.

The principle that emerges is that when burning is impossible, the Rabbis declare the situation equivalent to lost *imurim*, and in that case the *kohanim* may eat immediately — their eating effects the owner's atonement even without the burning having taken place. At the deepest level, the *gemara* acknowledges that *b'diavad*, it is all about the blood. If the meat and fat became *tamei* or lost entirely, no one says the owner must bring another *korban*. What the *gemara* explores here is the *lechatchila* process by which atonement is fully realized — and it concludes that when circumstances prevent the full process, the blood alone suffices.

## Night Burning

### Yolin Question

*Rav Kahana* raises a new tension from a different set of verses. One verse states *lo yalin chelev chagagi ad boker* — the fat of my festival sacrifice shall not remain overnight until morning. The implication of the verse: *ad boker hu d'lo yalin* — only until morning is it prohibited to leave it; but *kol halailah kulo yalin* — throughout the entire night it may remain, i.e., the fat can and should be burned throughout the night. Yet another verse — *v'hiktir aleha chelvei ha'shlamim* — is the very verse from which we derived *hashlama*, that all burnings must be completed in connection with the morning *tamid*, before the afternoon *tamid*. How can these be reconciled? How can the fat be burned through the night if everything must be completed before the afternoon *tamid*?

### Pesach Yolin

*Rav Kahana* himself raises the question and provides the answer. The resolution is that the burning of fat through the night refers to *korbanos* whose blood was sprinkled before the afternoon *tamid*. For those, the nighttime burning is simply the completion of a *korban* that was already properly brought — it is wrap-up, not a new initiation. What is forbidden is burning on the *mizbe'ach* something that belongs to a *korban* whose blood was sprinkled after the afternoon *tamid*. The restriction is about the initiation of a *korban* after the *tamid*, not about completing one that was validly begun earlier.

### Weekday on Yom Tov

A further challenge is posed by *Rav Safra* to *Rav* — some say *Rava*. One verse states *lo yalin laboker zevach chag haPesach* — do not leave the sacrifice of *Pesach* until morning. On the simple reading this refers to the meat, which must be eaten before morning. But on the reading being discussed here, it implies that the fat of the *korban pesach* may be burned throughout the night. The difficulty: that night is the fifteenth of Nisan — *Yom Tov*. The *korban pesach* was brought on the fourteenth. Another verse establishes *olat Shabbat b'Shabbato* — the *Shabbat olah* in its *Shabbat* — and from this is derived *v'lo olat chol b'Shabbat* and *v'lo olat chol b'Yom Tov* — you may not burn the fat of a weekday *korban* on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. How then can the fat of the *korban pesach*, which was brought on the fourteenth, be burned on the fifteenth?

### Shabbat Solution

*Rav Abba bar Chiyya* had already posed this question to *Rabbi Abbahu*, and *Rabbi Abbahu* answered: *hacha b'arba'asar she'chal liyos b'Shabbat askinan* — here we are dealing with the special case where the fourteenth of Nisan falls on *Shabbat*. When *erev Pesach* is *Shabbat*, the *korban pesach* brought that *Shabbat* is considered *chelvei Shabbat* — fat of a *Shabbat korban*. And the rule that permits burning yesterday's fat on *Yom Tov* applies when yesterday was *Shabbat*, since the verse permits *olat Shabbat* to be offered on the following day. Thus the fat of the *pesach* — a *Shabbat korban* — may be burned on the night of the fifteenth.

### Pasuk Self-Limit

The questioner presses: is it really acceptable to constrain an entire verse to such a narrow case — when *erev Pesach* coincides with *Shabbat* — simply to resolve this technical conflict? Why not simply take the verse at its plain meaning and allow the burning?

The response: *shavkineihu l'kra d'hu dachik u'moki anafshei* — leave the verse alone; it is the verse itself that is under pressure and forces itself into this narrow interpretation. The Artscroll renders this: it forces itself to be interpreted this way. The Soncino: it is compelled to establish its own particular case.

This is a conceptually significant moment. The plain sense of the verse seems to permit burning the *pesach* fat throughout the night of the fifteenth. But because that plain sense conflicts irreconcilably with the established rule — derived from another verse — that one cannot burn a weekday *korban*'s fat on *Yom Tov*, the *gemara* concludes that the verse must be confining itself to the extraordinary case where the fourteenth fell on *Shabbat*. The verse does not establish a new leniency overriding existing rules; rather, the internal pressure of the halakhic system forces the verse into its only coherent application.

This illustrates something fundamental about *Torah she'ba'al peh* and its relationship to the *psukim*. When the plain meaning of a verse conflicts with our understanding of the broader halakhic framework as derived from other verses and principles, the Rabbinic reading — grounded in consistency and systemic coherence — prevails. The verse does not override the system; the system compels the verse into its narrow, self-defined meaning. *Moki anafshei* — it establishes itself, within the constraints the system imposes on it.
