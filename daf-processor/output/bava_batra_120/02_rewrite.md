# Bava Batra 120 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bnot Tzelofchad Aggada

### Yocheved Birth Story

The daf opens in the middle of an *aggadic* section discussing the manner in which *Bnot Tzelofchad* approached Moshe and Aharon, and it includes a digression touching on the famous *aggadata* concerning Yocheved. The tradition records that Yocheved was born at the precise moment that the sons of Yaakov entered Mitzrayim, and that she was 130 years old when she gave birth to Moshe. The Gemara recalls the well-known episode of Amram separating from her and then, through the prophecy of Miriam, remarrying her — Miriam's prophetic insight leading ultimately to Moshe's birth.

### Women's Wisdom

The reason the Gemara introduced this *aggadata* in the first place was a discussion of the age of *Bnot Tzelofchad* and questions of childbearing. But there is an additional thematic layer worth noting: once again, a woman — Miriam — through her prophetic wisdom, knows more than her father Amram about the correct course of action. The parallel with *Bnot Tzelofchad* is striking: just as Miriam knew better than Amram, so too did *Bnot Tzelofchad* know better than Moshe in the sense of pressing their claim. The interplay of female wisdom and prophetic agency runs through both stories.

---

## Wisdom vs. Age

### Different Orders

The Gemara now turns to a textual question: *Bnot Tzelofchad* are listed in different orders in different contexts in the Torah. Later in Bemidbar, in the epilogue to the story where the uncles come to complain and the daughters ultimately marry their cousins, the names are given in the order of their birth — *derech gedulatan*, in accordance with their age. By contrast, when they approached Moshe with their legal claim, the names appear in a different sequence. The Gemara resolves this by explaining that the second context follows *derech chachmatan* — the order of their wisdom. Since the Gemara earlier declared that *chachmaniyot hen*, that they were women of great wisdom, it stands to reason that when they are coming forward with an intellectual claim, the wisest among them is listed first. When the epilogue records their marriages, however — where older daughters would naturally wed before younger ones — the order reverts to age.

### Yeshiva/Mesiba Rule

This distinction is then taken as support for a ruling of Rabbi Ammi: *mesayei lei l'Rabbi Ammi*, it lends support to what Rabbi Ammi said. Rabbi Ammi taught that *b'yeshiva halach achar chachma* — when people are gathered for an intellectual discourse or deliberation (here *yeshiva* does not mean a study hall in the modern sense, but rather a formal sitting for discussion), one gives precedence and a place of honor to the wisest participant. But *b'mesiba halach achar zikna* — at a meal, where the gathering is more social in character, one follows age. This is also consonant with the episode in Bereishit where Yosef seats his brothers at the meal in precise birth order: *habechor kevichorato vehatzair ketzeirato*.

### Significant Gap Req.

The Gemara then adds an important qualification from Rav Ashi: *vehu d'maflig b'chachma vehu d'maflig b'zikna* — this rule of giving precedence to the wiser or older person applies only where there is a *significant* gap. If two siblings are both highly capable and one is merely somewhat sharper than the other, that marginal difference does not justify honoring the younger over the older. One might ask: if the context is fundamentally intellectual, why should the size of the gap matter? The answer is that wisdom is not as objectively measurable as age. Unless the difference is obvious and dramatic — unless one person is unmistakably on a different level entirely — it is not an appropriate basis on which to set aside the clearer, more quantifiable standard of age.

There is a further reason as well. Being told that one's younger sibling is being honored because he is smarter carries a sting that is fundamentally different from the convention of respecting an older sibling. Age is something neither party controls; it reflects no personal judgment. To say, however, that we are honoring your younger brother because he is more intelligent reads as a direct critique. Consequently, only a truly overwhelming difference in wisdom justifies overriding the ordinary convention.

### Rashbam Logic

The *Rashbam* makes this point explicit and also addresses the reverse scenario — why is there a *significant gap* requirement even when honoring the older person at a meal? His analysis proceeds as follows. If the younger sibling is so dramatically more gifted — a genius of the highest order — then even at a meal, where the default is to follow age, if the age gap is minimal, one might honor the younger prodigy. It is only if the elder is *significantly* older that the convention of honoring age holds firmly even in the presence of outstanding intellectual distinction.

The *Rashbam* then addresses the case where neither party has a significant advantage in wisdom and neither is significantly older. He writes: *nir'eh b'einai de-v'chol makom holech achar ziknah* — it appears to him that by default one always follows age. The reasoning, as he articulates it, is that honoring someone who is only somewhat smarter causes embarrassment: *ein lanu l'vayesh et ha-zaken*. We should not embarrass the elder by suggesting, even implicitly, that he is less intelligent. By contrast, the person of somewhat greater wisdom is not embarrassed when the elder receives precedence: *v'he-chacham eino bosh b'kach*, because everyone understands that the societal convention is to honor the older. It is only when the age gap or the wisdom gap becomes large enough to be unmistakable that the relevant criterion shifts from its default.

One interesting question raised during the discussion was what precisely *chochmah* means in this context — whether it refers to raw intelligence, erudition in learning, or something closer to wisdom in the broader sense. The observation was made that *Bnot Tzelofchad* were praised as *chachmaniyot* not because of book knowledge but because of judgment and insight. The conclusion reached was that *chochmah* here is probably closer to wisdom than to mere intelligence or accumulated information.

## Tzelofchad Equal/Tribe

### Shkulot Hayu

The Gemara now introduces a teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *Bnot Tzelofchad shkulot hayu* — the daughters of Tzelofchad were all of equal worth. The different orderings of their names in different contexts are not actually indicating hierarchy; rather, they are employed precisely to demonstrate that no one daughter was superior to the others. The Gemara invokes the same interpretive move used for Moshe and Aharon, where the Torah alternates between *Moshe v'Aharon* and *Aharon u'Moshe* to convey that neither was greater than the other. The verse *vatihyenah bnot Tzelofchad* lists them all together, and the Gemara derives: *havayah achat l'chulan* — they shared a single, equal standing.

### Marriage Permission

Rav Yehuda then teaches in the name of Shmuel: *Bnot Tzelofchad huteru linasei l'chol hashvatim* — the daughters of Tzelofchad were permitted to marry from any tribe. This is a striking statement, since throughout this chapter the impression has been that the *p'sukim* in Bemidbar explicitly require daughters who inherit to marry within their tribe so that the *nachalah* does not pass to another tribe. Rav Yehuda appears to be saying the direct opposite of the straightforward reading.

### Advice vs. Restriction

The source Rav Yehuda cites is *latov b'eineihen tihyenah l'nashim* — let them marry whomever seems good to them. He reads this as granting permission to marry freely, setting aside the subsequent phrase *ach l'mishpachat mateh avihem* — only within the family of their father's tribe. That trailing clause, the Gemara explains, is not a legal restriction but *eitza tovah* — good counsel. The Torah is advising them that it is in their best interest to marry within the tribe, not commanding it. The formulation *ha-hagun lahem* — that which is fitting for them — frames tribal endogamy as prudent and beneficial rather than obligatory. Whether *hagun* here refers to the preservation of the *nachalah*, or more broadly to the likelihood of finding a compatible partner within one's own family and background, is a question the discussion leaves suggestively open. There is also a resonance with the observation that they were praised as *tzidkaniyot* — righteous women — and that by following this divine counsel they fulfilled exactly what was expected of women of such character.

## Rava's Challenge

### Dor Sinai/Future

Rava raises a challenge from a *baraita*. The *baraita* exposits the verse *emor aleihem ledoroteichem kol ish asher yikrav...* in the context of *tumah* and *kodashim*. It derives that *emor aleihem* refers to the people standing at Har Sinai in the moment of the giving of the Torah, while *ledoroteichem* — for your generations — extends the law to all future generations. The *baraita* then asks: if you state it for the fathers, why state it for the children, and vice versa? The answer is *mipnei sheyesh ba'avot ma she'ein babanim v'yesh babanim ma she'ein ba'avot* — because there are *mitzvot* that applied to the generation of the wilderness but not to future generations, and there are *mitzvot* that apply to future generations but not to that original generation. Since each category exists, one cannot simply infer one from the other; the paradigmatic verse must therefore state both explicitly.

The *baraita* then illustrates a *mitzvah* that applied to the fathers but not the children: *v'chol bat yoreshet nachala mimishpachat mateh aviha tihyeh l'isha* — every daughter who inherits must marry within her father's tribe. The Gemara reads this as applying only to that generation of *dor Sinai*, not to future generations. This is precisely what has been maintained throughout the chapter — that the restriction of marrying within the tribe was a *mitzvah* limited in its scope to the generation of the wilderness. The *Rashbam* likewise notes that *mitzvot* tied to the land of Israel, such as *bikurim* and *terumot uma'asrot*, would represent the opposite case: obligations that apply to later generations but not to the generation of the *midbar*.

### One Gen. Restriction

With this *baraita* in hand, Rava's challenge to Shmuel's statement becomes clear. The *baraita* states explicitly that *v'chol bat yoreshet nachala* was a real legal obligation — not merely advice — for the generation of the *midbar*. This seems to contradict Shmuel's position that *Bnot Tzelofchad* were permitted to marry freely. The Gemara resolves this — *hu motiv lah v'hu mefarek lah*, he raised the difficulty and he answered it — by drawing a careful distinction: *levar miVnot Tzelofchad*, the restriction applied to *other* women of that generation who were in similar circumstances, that is, daughters who inherited on account of the initiative of *Bnot Tzelofchad* and found themselves holding a *nachalah*. Those women were obligated to marry within their tribe. But *Bnot Tzelofchad* themselves, by virtue of their being *tzidkaniyot v'chachmaniyot*, were accorded a kind of exceptional permission. Future generations entirely are free to marry whomever they wish, *Bnot Tzelofchad* are free to marry whomever they wish, and for all of them the counsel is to marry within the tribe. The only ones legally bound are the other women of that specific generation who piggybacked on *Bnot Tzelofchad*'s legal claim. This represents a strikingly narrow reading of what seemed to be a broad prohibition in the *p'sukim*.

## Gen. Limitation Rule

### Shchutei Chutz

The Gemara now investigates from where exactly the Gemara derives that the obligation to marry within the tribe was limited to the generation of the *midbar*. Rava states: *amar kra, zeh hadavar asher tziva Hashem* — the verse says "this is the matter which Hashem commanded," and *davar zeh* carries a limiting force: *lo yehei noheg ela b'dor zeh* — this applies only to this generation. But a challenge immediately arises: the passage dealing with *shchutei chutz* — one who slaughters a consecrated animal outside the *Mikdash* — also opens with the formula *zeh hadavar asher tziva Hashem*. Does that mean the prohibition of *shchutei chutz* also applied only to the generation of the wilderness? Clearly not. The Gemara answers: *shani hatam d'chtiv ledorotam* — that case is different, because the verse there explicitly states *ledorotam*, for their generations, establishing unambiguously that the law extends to all time.

The same question is then raised regarding the *parasha* of *nedarim*: *zeh hadavar asher tziva Hashem ish ki yidor neder...* — does the *zeh hadavar* there mean the entire *parasha* of vows and *hatarat nedarim* was only for the generation of the *midbar*? *Hachi nami delo yehei noheg ela b'dor zeh?* The Gemara answers that the *zeh hadavar* in the *nedarim* passage is linked by *gezeira shava* to the *zeh hadavar* in *shchutei chutz*, and since the *shchutei chutz* passage contains *ledorotam*, this establishes that the *nedarim* passage too applies to all generations.

### No Gezeira Shava

One might then try the same move for *Bnot Tzelofchad*: since both the *nedarim* passage and the passage regarding daughters who inherit contain the phrase *zeh hadavar*, why not link them by *gezeira shava* and say that daughters who inherit are also not limited to one generation? The Gemara rejects this: *bishlama hatam itrich gezeira shava* — there, a *gezeira shava* was genuinely needed, because the word *zeh* would otherwise have been superfluous. Its presence is not meant to limit the law to one generation; rather, it serves as the vehicle for the *gezeira shava* itself, creating the link to *shchutei chutz* and its explicit *ledorotam*. Here, by contrast, if the Torah had wanted to indicate that the restriction applies to all generations, it would simply have said nothing — silence would have sufficed and the default rule would apply. *Lishtok kra minei v'ana yadana d'ledoros hu.* The only reason to write *zeh* here, where there is no *gezeira shava* to be made and no surplus purpose for the word, is precisely to limit the law to that moment. This is the textual basis for the ruling that the restriction on daughters who inherit marrying within the tribe was confined to the generation of the *midbar*.

## Gezeira Shava

### Zeh Hadavar

The Gemara now turns to elaborating the *gezeira shava* itself between *shchutei chutz* and *nedarim*. The *baraita* teaches: *ne'emar kan zeh hadavar* — it says *zeh hadavar* in the *nedarim* passage — *v'ne'emar l'halan zeh hadavar* — and it says *zeh hadavar* in the *shchutei chutz* passage. The *parasha* of *shchutei chutz* is addressed to Aharon, his sons, and all of Israel: *dabeir el Aharon v'el banav v'el kol bnei Yisrael*. The *parasha* of *nedarim* is addressed to *rashei hamatos livnei Yisrael* — the heads of the tribes. The *gezeira shava* teaches that each passage imports something from the other: just as *shchutei chutz* was directed to Aharon, his sons, and all Israel, so too does the law of *nedarim* apply in a context that includes all Israel; and just as *nedarim* was given to *rashei hamatos*, so too is the concept of *rashei hamatos* relevant to *shchutei chutz*.

### Three Hedyotot

What is the practical *halachic* import of bringing *Aharon uvanav v'chol Yisrael* into the context of *nedarim*? Rav Acha bar Yaakov explains: *lomar she'hafarat nedarim b'shloshah hedyotos* — to teach that *hatarat nedarim* may be performed by three ordinary laypeople who are not *talmidei chachamim*. The background is as follows. The *parasha* of *nedarim* is addressed to *rashei hamatos* — the heads of the tribes — and from the phrase *lo yachel devaro* the Gemara derives that while the one who made the vow cannot nullify his own words, *aval acheirim matirin umeichalin lo* — others can release him. Those "others" are the *rashei hamatos*, which implies a sage or authority figure. The *gezeira shava* therefore comes to expand this: since the *shchutei chutz* passage involves all of Israel — not only the distinguished leadership — the capacity for *hatarat nedarim* is not confined to established authorities. Three ordinary individuals, *shloshah hedyotos*, may together constitute a *beis din* for this purpose. As the Gemara notes, following Tosafos, once we know the number must be greater than one, the halachically significant grouping is three. The role of *rashei hamatos* in the *nedarim* passage is, in turn, read through the teaching of Rav Chisda in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *b'yachid mumche* — a single authoritative expert may perform *hatarat nedarim* alone. The *rashei hamatos* thus teaches the sufficiency of a single *mumche*, while *kol Yisrael* teaches the sufficiency of three *hedyotos*.

## Hatara for Hekdesh

### Rav Sheshet

Returning to the *gezeira shava*, the Gemara asks: what does importing *rashei hamatos* into the context of *shchutei chutz* teach? Rav Sheshet answers: *lomar she'yesh she'eilah b'hekdesh* — to teach that one may perform *she'eilah*, the act of retroactively releasing a vow before a sage, with respect to *hekdesh* — items consecrated to the *Mikdash*. The underlying question is whether *hatarat nedarim* extends beyond its most obvious domain of personal prohibitions to encompass acts of *hafla'ah* more broadly. *Hafla'ah* is the category of verbal acts that change the status of a person or object — *ish ki yafli lindor neder nazir*, and similarly the verbal sanctification of *hekdesh*. Since consecrating an animal to the *Mikdash* is structurally analogous to making a *neder*, one might have thought that the same mechanism of *she'eilah* before a *chacham* could undo it.

### Hafla'ah Concept

This brings into focus the broader category of *hafla'ah*. When one makes a *neder*, the speech act transforms the status of an object: these fruits become forbidden to me as if they were *hekdesh*. The term *konam*, commonly used in *nedarim*, is indeed a linguistic surrogate for *korban*: just as one's words can sanctify an animal to the *Mikdash*, they can impose an analogous forbidden status on ordinary objects. The same logic applies to *nezirut*: declaring oneself a *nazir* is an act of *hafla'ah* that transforms one's personal status through speech. The question then becomes: does *hatarat nedarim* extend across all instances of *hafla'ah*, including *hekdesh* proper?

The question has real stakes. If one consecrates an animal to *bedek ha-bayit* — for the maintenance of the *Mikdash* — and subsequently regrets it, can one go to a *chacham* and have the *hekdesh* undone? The act of consecration is weighty, and there is reason to think that once something truly belongs to the *Mikdash*, it is beyond the reach of retroactive dissolution. Furthermore, once *terumah*, for example, has actually been given to a *kohen*, it is out of one's hands in a way that is qualitatively different from a personal prohibition. The Gemara's interest is in whether the link established by the *gezeira shava* extends the reach of *she'eilah* into the domain of *hekdesh*.

One additional dimension is worth noting. Consecrating an animal and then slaughtering it outside the *Mikdash*, so as to be liable for *karet*, raises the theoretical possibility that one could retroactively undo the *hekdesh* through *she'eilah* — *had I known I would be liable for karet, I never would have sanctified that animal* — and thereby escape liability. This underscores why the question of whether *she'eilah* applies to *hekdesh* has significant practical and conceptual ramifications.

A related question arose regarding *kiddushin*: could the speech act of *harei at mekudeshes li* be understood as an act of *hafla'ah*? This seems partially correct. Rashi emphasizes that the language of *kiddushin* does more than express intent; it effects a status change, and it appears to be borrowed from the conceptual world of *hekdesh* and *hafla'ah*. The view that *kiddushin* involves a speech act that effects a *kinyan* of status — beyond merely transferring property — finds resonance in this world. By contrast, *divrei shchiv meira*, the bequest of one on his deathbed, which operates *kesuvim u'mesurim dami*, is distinct: it is fundamentally about transferring ownership, not about changing the status of an object, and so belongs to a different analytical category.

### Shammai/Hillel

The Gemara then reveals that the question of *she'eilah b'hekdesh* is itself the subject of a fundamental dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Beit Hillel holds that *she'eilah b'hekdesh* is valid — one can approach a *chacham* and have a mistaken or regretted consecration retroactively undone. Beit Shammai holds there is no such thing: *ein she'eilah b'hekdesh* — *hekdesh* cannot be dissolved through *hatarat nedarim*.

## Hekdesh Taus

### Piv v'Libo

The source for Beit Shammai's position is a *Mishnah*: *Beit Shammai omrim hekdesh taus hekdesh* — if one consecrates something in error, it is nevertheless *hekdesh*. Beit Hillel disagrees: *eino hekdesh*. The principle underlying Beit Hillel's position is *neder piv v'libo shavim* — what one says must be in alignment with what one intends. When one sanctifies an item while mistaken about what that item is, the speech act is not in sync with the speaker's actual intent, and the *hekdesh* therefore does not take effect. Beit Shammai, by contrast, holds that *hekdesh* is of such gravity that the speech act alone is binding, independent of whether it reflects one's true intention.

### Temura Example

Beit Shammai's position finds support in the laws of *temura*. By *temura*, one attempts to transfer the *kedushah* from one animal to another — designating animal B as a substitute for the originally consecrated animal A. The *halacha* is that animal B acquires *kedushah* but animal A does not lose it: both become *hekdesh*. Since the entire point of the *temura* was to remove the *kedushah* from animal A, the outcome — that animal A remains *hekdesh* — was entirely contrary to what the person intended. Had he known this would be the result, he never would have performed the exchange. And yet animal B still becomes *hekdesh*. This demonstrates, according to Beit Shammai, that *hekdesh b'taus hekdesh*: even when the act of consecration is demonstrably an error, even when the error is obvious at the moment of the act, the *hekdesh* takes effect. If a patent, contemporaneous error cannot undo *hekdesh*, then surely retroactive regret cannot dissolve it either. Beit Shammai thus concludes that there is no *she'eilah b'hekdesh*.

## Shammai Gezeira Shava

### No Hatara Hekdesh

For Beit Hillel, the *gezeira shava* of *zeh zeh* between *shchutei chutz* and *nedarim* teaches the possibility of *she'eilah b'hekdesh*. The question arises: for Beit Shammai, who rejects *she'eilah b'hekdesh*, what does the *zeh hadavar* of *shchutei chutz* come to teach? It cannot be used for the same *gezeira shava* that Beit Hillel employs.

### Shechita Not Meliga

For Beit Shammai, the *zeh hadavar* by *shchutei chutz* is not available for a *gezeira shava* and instead teaches something internal to the laws of *shchutei chutz* itself: *al hashochait hu chayav, v'eino chayav al hamalik* — one is liable only for *shechita* performed outside the *Mikdash*, not for *melikah* performed outside. *Melikah* is the form of slaughter performed on bird offerings — using the fingernail at the back of the neck — and it is performed exclusively by *kohanim* within the *Mikdash*. If a bird offering is slaughtered outside the *Mikdash* by *shechita*, one is liable for *shchutei chutz*; if *melikah* is performed outside, one is not liable. The word *zeh*, in Beit Shammai's reading, serves to define and limit the scope of the prohibition in precisely this way, rather than to create a *gezeira shava* with the *nedarim* passage.

Similarly, for Beit Shammai, *zeh hadavar* by *rashei hamatos* teaches the distinction between the *chacham*'s mode and the husband's mode of addressing vows — that the *chacham* releases (*matir*) while the husband annuls (*meifer*), each operating exclusively in his own domain. This is the *halacha* that Beit Shammai derives from the *zeh* of the *nedarim* passage in place of the *gezeira shava*.

## Hatara/Hafara

### Hafara Husband

The distinction between *hatara* and *hafara* — between the sage's release and the husband's annulment — is a fundamental one that deserves careful attention. The husband's *hafara* operates by virtue of the verse *ki hefer osam b'yom shamo*: upon hearing his wife's vow, the husband has until the end of that day to exercise his power of *hifara*. What he does is void the vow — not retroactively, but going forward. The vow was fully binding at the moment it was made. If the wife violated it before the husband voided it, she has transgressed and bears responsibility for that transgression. The husband's power is prospective: from the moment he exercises *hafara*, the vow no longer binds her.

### Hatara Sage

The *chacham*'s *hatara* is entirely different in nature. The sage does not merely override the vow; he undoes it retroactively. The process is one in which the person who made the vow articulates that had he foreseen a particular circumstance or consequence, he never would have made it. On that basis, the *chacham* releases the vow — *matir*, as one unties a knot — and the vow is treated as if it had never existed. There is no time limit analogous to that day's hearing; and if the person violated the vow, the retroactive dissolution means that no violation occurred.

### Language Matters

These two modes are strictly separate and the language used must correspond to the correct mode. If a *chacham* performing *hatarat nedarim* employs the language of *hafara* — saying *mufar lach* rather than *mutar lach* — the *hatara* is ineffective. Conversely, if a husband employs the language of *hatara* rather than *hafara*, his act has no effect. The modes are not interchangeable, and using the wrong terminology for the wrong actor voids the act entirely. In practice, the formulation *hakol mutarim lach* is the language appropriate for the *chacham*, while *heifer* language is reserved for the husband.

## Moados/Nedarim

### Shabbat Exclusion

The Gemara now turns to a *baraita* that addresses the final verse in *Parshat Emor*: *vayedaber Moshe et moadei Hashem el bnei Yisrael*. The *parasha* had opened with *eileh moadei Hashem asher tikre'u otam mikra'ei kodesh eileh heim moadai sheishes yamim te'aseh melacha*, with *Shabbat* listed at the beginning. But the concluding verse — *vayedaber Moshe et moadei Hashem* — seems to refer back to only part of what was taught. Rabbi Yossi HaGlili teaches: *moadei Hashem ne'emru, Shabbat Bereishit lo ne'emra* — the *moadim* were stated, but *Shabbat Bereishit* was not included in this final summary. Ben Azzai adds: *moadei Hashem ne'emru, hafarat nedarim lo ne'emra* — the *moadim* were stated but *hafarat nedarim* was not included.

A sage named Rabbi Yossi bar Nasan had received a tradition of this *baraita* but could not explain it. So troubled was he that he traveled first to Narda'ah and then to Mechuza in pursuit of Rav Sheshet to obtain the explanation.

### Mumchinim

Rav Sheshet explained both teachings. Regarding Rabbi Yossi HaGlili's exclusion of *Shabbat Bereishit*: the verse *asher tikre'u otam mikra'ei kodesh* — you shall declare them holy — implies that these occasions require *kiddush beis din* to take effect. One might have thought, since *Shabbat* is mentioned alongside the *moadim* and the same formula of declaration appears, that *Shabbat* too requires a formal declaration by a *beis din* each week. The final verse, *vayedaber Moshe et moadei Hashem*, clarifies that only the *moadim* are included in this requirement; *Shabbat Bereishit* does not require *kiddush beis din*. Its sanctity is inherent and does not depend on human proclamation.

Regarding Ben Azzai's exclusion of *hafarat nedarim*: Rav Sheshet explained that *moadei Hashem tzerichin mumchin* — the *moadim* require a *beis din* of *mumchin*, of expert authorities, for the purpose of *kiddush ha-chodesh* and the determination of the calendar. *Hafarat nedarim lo ne'emra mumchin* — *hatarat nedarim* does not require *mumchin*. This is the source that Beit Shammai uses to derive that *hatarat nedarim b'shloshah hedyotos* is valid — a point that Beit Hillel derived from the *gezeira shava*, but which Beit Shammai, rejecting that *gezeira shava*, must learn from elsewhere.

### Resolved

The role of *rashei hamatos* in the *nedarim* passage is then accounted for by the teaching of Rav Chisda in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *b'yachid mumche* — *rashei hamatos* teaches that a single individual who is a *mumche* may perform *hatarat nedarim* alone. The distinction is thus: *kiddush ha-chodesh* requires a *beis din* of expert *mumchin*; *hatarat nedarim* does not require *mumchin* at all and may be performed by three *hedyotos* — and if there is a single *mumche*, he suffices alone.

## Shabbat Sanctity

### Inherent Kedusha

The Gemara's teaching that *Shabbat Bereishit* does not require *kiddush beis din* points to a fundamental theological distinction. The *moadim* — Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot, and the other festival days — acquire their sanctity through human action: the *beis din* sanctifies the new month, which determines when the festivals fall, and in this sense *mekadesh Yisrael v'ha-zmanim* — Israel sanctifies the appointed times. Remove that human act, and there is no festival. *Shabbat*, by contrast, possesses an intrinsic *kedushah* rooted in creation itself. It is holy whether or not any human authority declares it so.

### Tosefet Shabbat

And yet the making of *kiddush* on *Shabbat* is not therefore meaningless. Rav Soloveitchik developed this point in several writings, arguing that the *kiddush* we make on Friday night — drawn from *zachor et yom ha-Shabbat le-kadsho* — adds a layer of human sanctity upon an already-sanctified day. *Shabbat* will be *Shabbat* regardless, but our *kiddush* brings our own consecration to it. This is reflected in the concept of *tosefet Shabbat*: by accepting *Shabbat* early, before nightfall, we extend its sanctity in a way that reflects human agency. The *moadim* exist only because of human sanctification; *Shabbat* exists independently of it. But just as human beings can add sanctity to the *moadim* through the declaration of *kiddush ha-chodesh*, so too can the individual add a personal dimension of sanctity to *Shabbat* through *kiddush* and *tosefet Shabbat* — not creating what was not there, but joining one's own act of sanctification to what God has already sanctified from the beginning of creation.
