# Gittin 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Freeing Slaves

### Rav Yehuda/Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָפוּ אֶת רַבָּהּ וַעֲשָׂאָהּ בַּת חוֹרִין, וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מִנְהַג הֶפְקֵר נָהֲגוּ בָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **and** the court **forced her master** to emancipate her, **and he made her a free woman. And Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** in explanation of why they forced him to do this: **They took liberties with her,** i.e., people engaged in sexual intercourse with her freely. This demonstrates that it is permitted to free a slave to prevent people from violating prohibitions.

The daf opens at the very bottom of 38a, in the midst of a discussion concerning slaves and *tikkun olam* — though not necessarily in the direction one might have expected. The Gemara presents the following case: there was a female slave in Pumbedita (*hahi amata de'hava be-Pumbedita*) who was being sexually exploited (*de'hava kam'avdi ba issura*). Abaye declared that were it not for the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — *kol hamesachrer avdo over be-asei*, anyone who frees his slave transgresses a positive commandment — he would have compelled the master to write her a *get shichrur*, a writ of manumission, so that she could achieve full free status and thereby be protected from abuse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַמְשַׁחְרֵר עַבְדּוֹ עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְעוֹלָם בָּהֶם תַּעֲבוֹדוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discussing **the** matter **itself** cited above. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Anyone who emancipates his slave violates a positive mitzva, as it is stated: "Of them may you take your bondmen forever"** (Leviticus 25:46). This is a positive mitzva requiring that one subjugate slaves their entire lives. Therefore, it is prohibited to emancipate them.

The positive commandment in question derives from the verse *le'olam bahem ta'avodu* — you shall enslave them forever. The irony of this *mitzvat asei* is not lost: given the centrality of *Yetziat Mitzrayim* to the Jewish moral imagination, a positive obligation to maintain the institution of slavery is striking. One might distinguish between the Torah tolerating the institution of slavery — which could be understood as a concession to the realities of the ancient world — and actively reinforcing it through a prohibitory commandment. The latter is harder to account for. Perhaps the concern was systemic: freeing an individual slave without addressing the underlying economic structure may have been seen as disruptive in ways that were not necessarily in the slave's best interest. Or perhaps the commandment was understood as applying only when there is no legitimate purpose — a reading that several *Rishonim* favor, as we will see shortly.

### Abaye's Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם לָא לְעֶבֶד חַזְיָא וְלָא לְבֶן חוֹרִין חַזְיָא, הָכָא אֶפְשָׁר דִּמְיַחֵד לַהּ לְעַבְדֵּיהּ, וּמְנַטַּר לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of a half-maidservant half-free woman, **she is not fit for** marrying **a slave and she is not fit for** marrying **a freeman.** This is why she is available to all, and the only way to solve this problem is to emancipate her. **Here,** in the case of the maidservant, **it is possible** for the master **to assign her to** marry **his slave, and** that slave **will guard her** from people who wish to be promiscuous with her. Therefore, it is not necessary to emancipate her.

Ravina qualifies the ruling: *ki ha modei Rav Yehuda* — Rav Yehuda himself would concede that in a case of ongoing transgression (*mishum milsa de'issura*), freeing the slave is permitted. Whether this means the prohibition is overridden or simply inapplicable when there is legitimate cause is a matter of debate among the *Rishonim*, and the Gemara returns to this question when it reaches the famous case of Rabbi Eliezer and the *minyan*.

### Half-Slave Woman

The Gemara now raises a challenge: there is a teaching (*ma'aseh b'ishah achas*) about a woman who was half-slave, half-free — owned jointly by two partners, one of whom freed her and the other did not. Her status was entirely ambiguous: she could not marry a Jewish man, nor could she marry a slave. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains that people were treating her with contempt, exploiting her as if she were ownerless (*minhag hefker nahagu bah*). In that case the master was compelled to free her.

Does this case not demonstrate that Abaye should have acted similarly with the slave in Pumbedita? The Gemara distinguishes the two: in the case of the half-free woman, there was no alternative — *lo l'eved chazya v'lo l'ven chorin chazya*, she could neither marry a slave nor a free man. In the Pumbedita case, however, it would have been possible to arrange for her a slave husband who could protect her (*efshar d'meyachad lah avdi v'minatrah*). Since an alternative existed that did not require overriding the *mitzvat asei*, Abaye felt constrained from compelling her manumission.

### Rabbi Eliezer Minyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁנִּכְנַס בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְלֹא מָצָא עֲשָׂרָה, וְשִׁחְרֵר עַבְדּוֹ וְהִשְׁלִימוֹ לַעֲשָׂרָה! מִצְוָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving Rabbi Eliezer, who entered a synagogue** to pray, **and he did not find** a quorum of **ten** men, **and he emancipated his slave and had him complete** a quorum of **ten.** This demonstrates that one is permitted to emancipate his slave. The Gemara answers: Freeing a slave to enable the performance of **a mitzva,** e.g., completing a quorum, **is different.** This does not demonstrate that in general one is permitted to emancipate his slave.

The Gemara returns to examine the core ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel. It raises a well-known challenge: *ma'aseh b'Rabbi Eliezer shenichnas l'veit ha'knesses v'lo matza asarah* — Rabbi Eliezer entered the synagogue and did not find ten men to form a *minyan*. He freed his slave (*shichrar avdo*) and thereby completed the quorum (*v'hishlimo l'asarah*).

The Gemara responds: *mitzvah shani* — for a *mitzvah*, the case is different. This formulation invites scrutiny. If the prohibition against freeing slaves is a *mitzvat asei d'oraita*, how does a rabbinic obligation to maintain a *minyan* override it? The Ramban addresses this directly, citing the Gemara's teaching that when God arrives at a synagogue and finds fewer than ten people assembled, divine anger is immediate. The theological weight of the *minyan* thus transcends the technical question of whether it is *d'oraita* or *d'rabbanan*. Other *Rishonim*, however, favor the alternative reading: the prohibition against freeing slaves was never intended to apply when there is a legitimate purpose. On this reading, Rabbi Eliezer did not override the commandment — he simply did not transgress it, because the commandment is limited to gratuitous manumission.

One might further ask: when the slave is freed, does he not undergo a kind of *geirus*? In fact, the acts associated with conversion — *milah* and *tevilah* — were already performed when he was made a slave. But those acts were, in a sense, held in suspension by the reality of his enslavement. Upon manumission, that earlier status is released and given full expression. The Gemara does not view the prospect of the slave accumulating additional *mitzvot* through freedom as sufficient grounds by itself to mandate manumission; the concern about actively overriding a *mitzvat asei* remains the operative consideration.

### Yishmael vs Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לְעוֹלָם בָּהֶם תַּעֲבוֹדוּ״ – רְשׁוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: חוֹבָה. וְדִילְמָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר לַהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר רְשׁוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection from a *baraita* to the proof citing the incident involving Rabbi Eliezer: **The Sages taught: "Of them may you take your bondmen forever,"** is **optional;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** It is **an obligation.** The Gemara now explains the Gemara's objection: **But perhaps Rabbi Eliezer holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that it is **optional.** Therefore, the incident involving Rabbi Eliezer cannot serve as a proof that even those who hold that it is prohibited to free a slave would hold that it is permitted to free a slave to enable the performance of a mitzva.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ; דְּתַנְיָא בְּהֶדְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **cannot enter your mind** to say that Rabbi Eliezer holds that enslaving them permanently is optional, **as it is taught explicitly** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** "Of them may you take your bondmen forever" is **an obligation.**

A *baraita* is then cited: *Tanu Rabbanan* — from the verse *le'olam bahem ta'avodu*, Rabbi Yishmael derives that maintaining a slave is merely permitted (*reshut*), not obligatory. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, rules *chova* — it is an obligation. The Gemara then considers whether Rabbi Eliezer, who freed his slave for the *minyan*, might have held like Rabbi Yishmael. This is rejected: a *baraita* states explicitly that *Rav Elazar omer chova* — Rav Elazar himself rules it is an obligation, the position of Rabbi Akiva. His freeing of the slave must therefore be explained on other grounds, as discussed above.

### Three Ways Lose Wealth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: בְּהָנֵי תְּלָת מִילֵּי, נָחֲתִי בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים מִנִּכְסֵיהוֹן: דְּמַפְּקִי עַבְדַיְיהוּ לְחֵירוּתָא, וּדְסָיְירִי נִכְסַיְיהוּ בְּשַׁבְּתָא, וּדְקָבְעִי סְעוּדְתַּיְיהוּ בְּשַׁבְּתָא בְּעִידָּן בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁתֵּי מִשְׁפָּחוֹת הָיוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, אַחַת קָבְעָה סְעוּדָּתָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת וְאַחַת קָבְעָה סְעוּדָּתָהּ בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, וּשְׁתֵּיהֶן נֶעְקְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In connection with this issue, **Rabba said: With these three matters homeowners become impoverished: That they emancipate their slaves; and that they inspect their property on Shabbat; and that they set their meals on Shabbat at the time of** the sermon in **the study hall,** so that they miss it, **as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There were two families in Jerusalem, one** that **set its meal on Shabbat and one** that **set its meal on the eve of Shabbat, and both of them were uprooted.** One family was uprooted because they caused the suspension of Torah study, and the other was uprooted because by eating their meal on Shabbat eve, they did not properly distinguish between Shabbat and Shabbat eve.

Rava offers a related observation: *behani telat mili nacheti ba'alei batim minichseihon* — there are three ways in which wealthy householders descend from their wealth. First, freeing their slaves. Second, inspecting their property on *Shabbos* (*desayrei nichseihon beshabta*). Third, establishing their main meal on *Shabbos* at the time when the public should be attending the rabbinic lecture in the *beit midrash* (*dekeva'ei seudasa beshabta be'idna debei midrasha*).

The convergence of these three cases suggests something beyond mere economic observation. There is clearly a *mida kineged mida* dimension: in each case, the person imagines they are acting generously or properly, but in fact they are misusing what has been entrusted to them — their slaves, their property, or their table — and they are punished accordingly. In the case of *Shabbos* morning, a lavish *kiddush* scheduled to coincide with the public lecture is not *oneg Shabbos* — it is a violation of the communal obligation to learn.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds a historical illustration: two prominent families in Jerusalem were destroyed (*ne'ekru*) because of misuse of their *Shabbos* meals — one made its major feast on *Shabbos* day at the wrong time, and one on *erev Shabbos*. Rashi offers two explanations for the problem with the *erev Shabbos* family. One is that they made their feast on Friday night; Rashi notes that the principle *kavod yom kodem le'kavod laila* — the honor of the day takes precedence over the night — means that by making Friday night more lavish than *Shabbos* day they inverted the proper hierarchy. When told not to feast during the *shiur* on *Shabbos* day, they shifted to Friday night — but introduced a new problem. Rashi's second explanation is that they feasted Friday afternoon, arriving at *Shabbos* already satiated and unable to honor *Shabbos* with the proper appetite.

## Methods of Freedom

### Sanctification Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַב: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ עַבְדּוֹ, יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? גּוּפֵיהּ – לָא קַדִּישׁ; לִדְמֵי – לָא קָאָמַר; דְּלֶיהְוֵי ״עַם קָדוֹשׁ״ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **one who consecrates his slave,** the slave **is emancipated.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? **He did not consecrate** the slave **himself,** as the slave cannot become consecrated to be an offering. If you say that it is only **with regard to his monetary** value that he is consecrated, i.e., the owner pledges to give the value of his slave to the Temple, his owner **did not say** this. Therefore, it must be **that he said** that this slave **should be** a member of the **sacred nation,** meaning that the slave should be emancipated and become a Jew.

The Gemara now transitions from the permissibility of freeing a slave to the mechanisms by which a slave may actually become free. Rava cites Rav: *hamakdish avdo yatza l'cheirus* — if a person sanctifies (*makdish*) his slave, the slave goes free. The mechanism is not obvious, and Rashi and Tosafot understand it differently.

The Gemara's first step is to note *gufeih lo kadesh* — you cannot actually make the slave's body holy in the direct sense. Rashi understands this to mean that you cannot sanctify him as you would a beam designated for the *Beis Hamikdash* — he cannot be treated as a physical object consecrated to Temple use. Tosafot reads the principle differently: the slave cannot become *assur behana'ah*, forbidden in benefit, as consecrated property typically becomes, because he remains a human being. It is impossible for a person to be forbidden in benefit — he cannot refrain from benefiting himself, nor can others be categorically prohibited from all contact with him. Tosafot therefore understands *gufeih lo kadesh* to mean that he cannot acquire the standard legal character of *hekdesh* property.

Rashi then raises the obvious alternative: perhaps when one says *makdish avdo*, one means that the slave is transferred to the ownership of the *Beis Hamikdash* to be sold for his monetary value — *lidmei*. Rashi's answer to this is striking. He invokes the principle of automatic upgrade of holiness (*kedushah*): just as an unblemished animal designated to be sold by *hekdesh* automatically acquires full sacrificial sanctity rather than the limited sanctity one attempted to impose, so too a slave who is made *makdish* cannot have his *kedushah* artificially limited. The maximum expression of *kedushah* that can apply to a human being is membership in the holy people — *am kadosh*. Therefore, Rashi says, when one sanctifies a slave, the *kedushah* manifests itself as freedom: the slave becomes a free Jew.

### Renouncing Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב: הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ, יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. מַאן דְּאָמַר מַקְדִּישׁ – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן מַפְקִיר; מַאן דְּאָמַר מַפְקִיר – אֲבָל מַקְדִּישׁ לָא, דִּלְמָא לִדְמֵי קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef** says that **Rav says:** With regard to one **who renounces ownership of his slave,** the slave **is emancipated.** The Gemara points out: According to **the one who says** that **one who consecrates** his slave emancipates him, this is **all the more so** with regard to **one who renounces ownership.** But according to **the one who says** that **one who renounces ownership** of his slave emancipates his slave holds that this is the *halakha* only if one renounces ownership of his slave; **but one who consecrates** his slave does **not** emancipate him, as **perhaps** when he consecrated his slave **he said** that his slave is consecrated **with regard to his monetary** value, he should be sold and the profit donated toward the Temple maintenance.

Rav Yosef then cites Rav's parallel teaching: *hamafkir avdo yatza l'cheirut* — if one renounces ownership (*mafkir*) of his slave, the slave goes free. This is in one sense more intuitive: if the slave is no longer owned by anyone, he is no longer enslaved as property. The Gemara notes the logical relationship between the two cases: whoever accepts the more counterintuitive proposition — that sanctification (*makdish*) results in freedom — will certainly accept the more straightforward case of renouncing ownership (*mafkir*). Conversely, one who begins only from the *mafkir* case need not accept the *makdish* case, since there the natural reading would be that the slave becomes owned by *hekdesh* for his monetary value.

### Status vs Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: צָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁיחְרוּר, אוֹ לֹא צָרִיךְ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה, יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In both of these cases, where the slave is emancipated after his owner renounces his ownership of him or consecrates him, does the slave **require a bill of manumission, or does he not require** a bill of manumission? The Gemara suggests a proof to resolve this dilemma: **Come** and **hear that** which **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Rav says:** In **both this** case, where one consecrates his slave, **and that** case, where one renounces ownership of his slave, the slave **is emancipated but** nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission.**

This generates the central question of the sugya: *tzarich get shichrur o lo?* When an owner verbally renounces property ownership of a slave — whether by *makdish* or *mafkir* — is that declaration alone sufficient to change the slave's personal status? Or does the slave's personal status, his identity as an *eved*, require a separate *get shichrur* even after property ownership has ended?

The Gemara here revisits the position of Shmuel, mentioned previously in the sugya: *eved ish* — a slave is a slave only as long as he is owned by someone. On Shmuel's view, personal status follows property ownership automatically: the moment ownership ends, the personal status of slavery ends with it, and no writ is required. But this position is now placed in tension with other opinions.

### Get Required

The Gemara resolves the question with the ruling of Rav Chiyya bar Avin in the name of Rav: *echad zeh v'echad zeh yatza l'cheirus v'tzarich get shichrur* — in both the case of *makdish* and the case of *mafkir*, the slave is no longer enslaved in the property sense but still requires a *get shichrur* to change his personal status. This is an explicit rejection of Shmuel's position: property ownership and personal status are treated as legally distinct, and the cessation of one does not automatically entail the cessation of the other.

### Treasurers Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: וּמוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין – הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן עֲבָדִים, אֵין הַגִּזְבָּרִין רַשָּׁאִין לְהוֹצִיאָן לְחֵירוּת; אֲבָל מוֹכְרִין אוֹתָן לַאֲחֵרִים, וַאֲחֵרִים מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן לְחֵירוּת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, אַף הוּא – נוֹתֵן דְּמֵי עַצְמוֹ וְיוֹצֵא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמוֹכְרוֹ לוֹ! מַתְנִיתָא קָא רָמֵית עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַב?! רַב תַּנָּא הוּא, וּפָלֵיג.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: And we raise an objection** from a *baraita* **to our *halakha*** that Rav said that one who consecrates his slave emancipates him: With regard to **one who consecrates** all **his possessions, and among them were slaves,** the Temple **treasurers are not allowed to emancipate them. However, they may sell** the slaves **to others, and** these **others** may **emancipate them. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say** that **even** the slave **himself can give his own monetary value and is emancipated, due to** the fact **that it is as if** the Temple treasurer **sold him to himself.** This demonstrates that the act of consecrating one's slave does not emancipate him. The Gemara rejects this argument: Do **you raise an objection to Rav** from **a *baraita*? Rav** himself **is a *tanna*, and,** as such, has the authority to **dispute** the determination in the *baraita*.

Rava then raises a challenge to Rav's position on *makdish*. A *baraita* teaches: if someone declares *hamakdish kol nechasai* — all my property is sanctified — and among that property are slaves, the *gizbarin* (the Temple treasurers, administrators of *hekdesh*) may not free those slaves. Rather, they may sell them to others, and those others may then free them. This *baraita* appears to assume that when a master sanctifies his property including slaves, the slaves become owned by *hekdesh* — they do not automatically become free. This directly contradicts Rav's position.

### Rashi Value Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״אַךְ כׇּל חֵרֶם וְגוֹ׳ מֵאָדָם״ – אֵלּוּ עֲבָדָיו וְשִׁפְחוֹתָיו הַכְּנַעֲנִים! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּאָמַר לִדְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection from a *baraita* to the opinion of Rav: **Come** and **hear: "Notwithstanding, no dedicated thing** that a man may dedicate to the Lord from all that he has, whether of man or beast, or of the field of his possession, shall be sold or redeemed" (Leviticus 27:28). The Sages interpret the verse as follows: **"Of man"; these are his Canaanite slaves and maidservants.** This demonstrates that one may consecrate his slaves and they are not emancipated as a result. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** This is referring to a case **where** the master **said** explicitly that he is consecrating them **with regard to their monetary** value.

The Gemara's first response is that Rav, who was of the transitional generation between *tanna'im* and *amora'im*, is permitted to disagree with a *baraita* (*Rav tanna u'falig*). The Gemara then tries to find a reading of a subsequent source — a verse involving *cherem* — that would be consistent with Rav's position, and asks whether one could simply say that in all the problematic cases the owner specified *ledmai*, that he was sanctifying only for monetary value. If so, Rav would agree that the slave is not automatically freed, since the owner limited the consecration explicitly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אִידַּךְ נָמֵי דְּאָמַר לִדְמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then why not say that **the other** *baraita* quoted above is **also** referring to a case **where one said** explicitly that the slave is consecrated **with regard to his monetary** value? Why is it necessary to answer that Rav disagrees with that *baraita*?

Rashi's answer to this question is distinctive. For Rashi, even an explicit *ledmai* — "I sanctify him for his monetary value" — does not avoid the automatic upgrade problem if the slave himself is being sanctified. The *kedushah* imposed on the slave himself will always express itself maximally as freedom. The only way to avoid the slave going free, on Rashi's reading, is if the owner never sanctified the slave at all — instead, the owner accepted a personal monetary obligation to pay the slave's value to the *Beis Hamikdash*. In that case, *ledmai* means not "the slave is consecrated for his value" but "I personally undertake to pay his value"; the slave himself is entirely unsanctified and remains enslaved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אוֹ בְּכֶסֶף אוֹ בִּשְׁטָר; וְהַאי – פְּקַע לֵיהּ כַּסְפֵּיהּ. וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵהַאי תַּנָּא –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This is what he is saying:** A slave can be emancipated **either via money or via a bill** of manumission. **And** with regard to **this** master, who despaired of recovering his slave, **his monetary** hold over this slave **is abrogated,** and he can be emancipated only via a bill of manumission. **And** this statement, that generally a slave can be emancipated either via a bill of manumission or by paying money, serves **to exclude** the opinion **of this *tanna*** in the following *baraita*.

This reading allows Rashi to explain why, in the case of the *gizbarin*, the *baraita* speaks of the treasurers selling the slave and others freeing him: clearly the slave himself has been consecrated and is owned by *hekdesh*, since the treasurers are transacting with him directly. That scenario is therefore one that Rav must reject — it represents a position incompatible with his own, and that is why the Gemara required the answer of *Rav tanna u'falig*.

### Rabbi Meir Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ עַבְדּוֹ – עוֹשֶׂה וְאוֹכֵל, שֶׁלֹּא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶלָּא דָּמָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection to Rav's opinion from a *baraita*: **Come** and **hear:** In a case of **one who consecrates his slave,** the slave **works and is sustained** as compensation for his labor, **as** the master **consecrated only his monetary** value and donates that sum to the Temple treasury. This demonstrates that the slave does not become consecrated, as he may still work for the master, and he is also not emancipated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם מוֹצִיא דְּבָרָיו לְבַטָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: A person does not make a statement** of consecration **for naught.** If one declared an item consecrated, even if he did not use the correct formulation, his statement is interpreted in a manner to render it meaningful. Therefore, although the master did not say that the slave is consecrated with regard to his monetary value, his statement is interpreted in this way. However, Rav holds in accordance with the dissenting opinion of the Rabbis. In their opinion the slave is not consecrated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְכֵן הוּא, שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁ עַצְמוֹ – עוֹשֶׂה וְאוֹכֵל, שֶׁלֹּא הִקְדִּישׁ אֶלָּא דָּמָיו. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא – שַׁפִּיר;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This too stands to reason, as it teaches in the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **And so too,** a freeman **who consecrated himself works and is sustained** from his labor, **as he consecrated only his monetary** value and he did not consecrate his body. **Granted, if you say** that this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** then the entire *baraita* works out **well.** According to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, even when he consecrated himself, his statement is interpreted so that it is referring to a type of consecration that is meaningful.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבָּנַן הִיא, בִּשְׁלָמָא עַבְדּוֹ – לִדְמֵי קָאֵי, אֶלָּא אִיהוּ – לִדְמֵי קָאֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if you say** that **it is** the opinion of **the Rabbis, granted,** the first clause of the *baraita* that deals with one who consecrates **his slave** is understood, as **his slave exists** primarily **for monetary** value. It is logical that when the owner consecrates him he intends to consecrate his monetary value. **However,** in the case of the latter clause of the *baraita*, which is referring to one who consecrates himself, does **he exist for monetary** value? Therefore, the *baraita* must be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

The Gemara then cites a further *baraita*: *hamakdish avdo oseh v'ochel shelo hikdish ela damav* — if one sanctifies his slave, the slave continues to work and consume as before, for the owner only sanctified his monetary value. Here the slave does not go free; the *baraita* seems to present a middle position in which the slave is not transferred to *hekdesh* ownership and is not freed, but the owner bears an obligation to pay his value.

The Gemara identifies this as the position of Rabbi Meir, based on his principle *ein adam motzi devarav l'vatala* — a person does not issue words for naught. The application here runs as follows: when an owner says *makdish avdo*, he cannot literally sanctify the slave's body (for the reasons discussed), so his words must be given some other valid legal meaning. Rabbi Meir's principle directs us to reinterpret the declaration as the owner accepting a personal financial obligation — to pay the slave's market value to the *Beis Hamikdash*. This is similar to Rabbi Meir's ruling elsewhere that if someone says *erko alai* — his assessed value is upon me — for a person whose *erech* under Torah law is zero (such as an infant under one month old), we do not conclude that nothing is owed; rather, we reinterpret the word *erech* to mean the person's market value.

Rashi explains that under this model, the slave himself is entirely unsanctified — *lo hikdish ela damav* means the owner assumed a personal debt, not that the slave acquired any sacred status. Accordingly, the slave continues to work, the master retains his labor, and no question of automatic upgrade arises. For Rav, there are thus two possible models when one is *makdish* a slave: either the sanctification applies to the slave himself and automatically results in freedom (the standard *makdish* case), or the owner's declaration is reinterpreted as a personal financial commitment and the slave is not sanctified at all (the Rabbi Meir model). The *baraita* of *hamakdish avdo oseh v'ochel* reflects the second model, and the Gemara confirms this by noting that a parallel clause — *v'chen hu she'hikdish atzmo*, if the slave sanctifies himself — makes sense only under Rabbi Meir's reading, since a slave has no external owner to transfer him to *hekdesh* ownership; the only coherent interpretation is that he undertook a personal monetary obligation.

## Holiness Debate (II)

### All Agree Holy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי – הַמַּקְדִּישׁ עַבְדּוֹ, אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין בִּשְׂעָרוֹ. מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: קָדוֹשׁ; וּמָר סָבַר: לָא קָדוֹשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this dispute with regard to one who consecrates his slave is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** A *baraita* taught: With regard to **one who consecrates his slave,** if one then makes use of the slave, there **is no misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One misuses** property consecrated to the Temple if one makes use **of his hair. What, is it not that they disagree about this** issue, **as** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that the slave is **consecrated,** and therefore the *halakhot* of misuse of consecrated property apply, **and** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the slave is **not consecrated?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא?! הַאי ״מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ״ וְ״אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ״?! הַאי ״קָדוֹשׁ״ וְ״אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how can **you understand** that to be their dispute? If that were the case, then **these** expressions of: **One misuses** property consecrated to the Temple, **and:** There **is no misuse** of property consecrated to the Temple, are not the correct expressions. **They should have** used **these** expressions, of: **Consecrated, and: Not consecrated,** if that were actually the subject of their dispute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קָדוֹשׁ, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: עַבְדָּא כִּמְקַרְקְעֵי דָּמֵי; וּמָר סָבַר: כְּמִטַּלְטְלִי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, everyone** agrees that the slave is **consecrated,** in opposition to the opinion of Rav. **And here they disagree about this, as** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **a slave** is **considered equivalent to land,** as slaves are compared to land in several areas of *halakha*. Just as the misuse of consecrated property does not apply in the case of land, so too, it does not apply to slaves. **And** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that they are **considered equivalent to movable property,** and therefore the *halakhot* of misuse of consecrated property do apply to slaves.

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* that shifts the framing entirely: *hamakdish avdo ein moalin bo* — if one sanctifies his slave, there is no transgression of *me'ilah* (misuse of sacred property) with respect to the slave. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds: *moalin b'sa'aro* — there is *me'ilah* with respect to his hair.

The Gemara asks whether this dispute tracks the fundamental question of whether the slave is sanctified at all. But this reading is rejected: *de'chulei alma kadosh* — everyone agrees the slave is sanctified. The debate is not about whether *kedushah* applies but about the legal category into which the slave falls for purposes of *me'ilah*. One opinion holds that *avda k'mankarkei dami* — a slave has the legal status of land — and the laws of *me'ilah* do not apply to land. The other holds *k'mitalteli dami* — a slave is like chattel — and *me'ilah* does apply. This entire discussion stands against Rav's position that the slave goes free upon *makdish*: according to this *baraita*, the slave is genuinely sanctified and remains enslaved.

### Hair Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַדְּמִיפַּלְגִי בִּשְׂעָרוֹ, לִיפַּלְגוּ בְּגוּפוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **If that is so,** and they disagree with regard to whether a slave is equivalent to land or movable property, **instead of disagreeing with regard to** whether making use of the slave's **hair** constitutes a misuse of consecrated property, **let them disagree with regard to** whether making use of the slave **himself** constitutes a misuse of consecrated property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עַבְדָּא כִּמְקַרְקְעֵי דָּמֵי, וְהָכָא בִּשְׂעָרוֹ הָעוֹמֵד לִיגָּזֵז קָמִיפַּלְגִי – מָר סָבַר: כְּגָזוּז דָּמֵי; וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו כְּגָזוּז דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that **everyone** agrees that **a slave** is **considered equivalent to land,** and making use of the slave himself does not constitute a misuse of consecrated property. **And here they disagree with regard to his hair that is ready to be cut,** whether it is still considered a part of the slave. One **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that it **is considered as** if it were already **cut** and is no longer part of the slave. Since it is viewed as detached, it is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse of consecrated property, like all movable property. **And** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that it **is not considered as** if it were already **cut.** Until it is cut, the hair is part of the slave, and, like all land, is not subject to the *halakhot* of misuse of consecrated property.

The question about hair — *se'ar ha'omed ligzoz* — arises because hair that is ready to be shorn occupies an ambiguous status. It is physically connected to the slave's body, but it is also effectively detached in the sense that it serves no further functional purpose while still attached. The debate in the *baraita* is whether hair that is ready to be shorn is considered as if it has already been cut — *k'gazuz dami* — and therefore treated as chattel (with *me'ilah* applying), or whether it is still considered part of the slave's body — *lav k'gazuz dami* — and therefore categorized like land (with no *me'ilah*).

## Holiness Debate (II)

### Grapes Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי – כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי? דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ דְּבָרִים שֶׁהֵם כַּקַּרְקַע וְאֵינָן כַּקַּרְקַע, וְאֵין חֲכָמִים מוֹדִים לוֹ. כֵּיצַד? ״עֶשֶׂר גְּפָנִים טְעוּנוֹת מָסַרְתִּי לָךְ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵינָן אֶלָּא חָמֵשׁ״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב; וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע הֲרֵי הוּא כַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the opinions of **these *tanna'im* are parallel** to the opinions of **those *tanna'im*, as we learned** in a mishna (*Shevuot* 42b) that **Rabbi Meir says: There are** certain **things that are like land** with regard to their form **but are not** treated **like land** from a halakhic perspective, **but the Rabbis do not admit to him** that this is so. **How so?** If one makes the claim: **I gave you ten grapevines laden** with fruit to guard, **and the other one says: They are only five** vines, then **Rabbi Meir obligates** the defendant to take an oath, for one who makes a partial admission to a claim concerning movable property is obligated to take an oath that he is stating the truth. **And the Rabbis say:** The halakhic status of **anything** that is **attached to the ground is like the ground** itself, and therefore one does not take an oath in this case, as one does not take an oath if one makes a partial admission to a claim concerning land.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: עֲנָבִים הָעוֹמְדוֹת לִיבָּצֵר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ; דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: כִּבְצוּרוֹת דָּמְיָין; וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָאו כִּבְצוּרוֹת דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the comparison: **And** with regard to this, **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** that the practical difference **between them** is not concerning all vines. It exists only in a case of **grapes that are ready to be harvested, as Rabbi Meir holds** that since they are ready to be harvested, **they are considered like** they are already **harvested,** and the defendant must take an oath, as he is denying a claim concerning movable property. **And the Rabbis hold** that **they are not considered like** they are already **harvested,** and they still have the status of land. This dispute seems to be identical to the dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר – עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר הָתָם, אֶלָּא בַּעֲנָבִים – דְּכַמָּה דְּקָיְימָן מִיכְחָשׁ כְּחִישִׁי; אֲבָל הָכָא – כַּמָּה דְּקָאֵי אַשְׁבּוֹחֵי מַשְׁבַּח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: The dispute in the *baraita* with regard to a slave's hair is not necessarily parallel to the dispute with regard to grapes. **Even** if **you say** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** that grapes that are about to be harvested are considered like they are already harvested, this does not dictate one's opinion with regard to a slave's hair that is ready to be cut. **Rabbi Meir states** his opinion **only there, with regard to grapes, since the longer they remain** on the vine after ripening the more they **become withered** and are ruined. Since they no longer benefit from their attachment to the ground, they are considered to be like movable property. **However, here,** with regard to hair, **the longer it remains** on the slave the more **it improves,** i.e., grows, and therefore it should not be considered as if it were already cut.

The Gemara finds a parallel for this question in a *baraita* and *mishnah* concerning Rabbi Meir's ruling that there are things which are like land yet not treated as land for purposes of certain oaths (*yesh devarim she'hein k'karka v'einan k'karka*). The specific case involves ten grapevines laden with grapes: one party claims to have transferred ten laden vines, the other concedes only five. Is the admission regarding the grapes a *modeh b'miktzat* requiring an oath? Rabbi Meir says yes; the Sages say no, since anything attached to the ground is treated as land (*ha-mechubar le-karka harei hu ke-karka*).

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina clarifies that the debate does not concern the vines themselves — all agree the vines are like land — but the grapes that are *omdot livtzor*, ripe and ready for harvest. Rabbi Meir holds *k'vtzurot damyan* — they are considered as if already harvested; the Sages hold *lav k'vtzurot damyan* — they are not. The parallel to the hair question is exact: in both cases, the question is whether readiness for detachment causes the item to be treated as if already detached.

The Gemara, however, declines to equate the two debates directly. Even if Rabbi Meir considers ripe grapes as effectively harvested, he might not apply the same logic to hair. The reason: grapes that remain on the vine past the optimal harvest time deteriorate (*kama de-kaimi machash kachishi*) — their connection to the ground is actively harmful, and there is strong motivation to sever it. Hair, by contrast, continues to grow and increase in value the longer it remains (*kama de-kaimi ashvuchi mashvchi*) — it remains intrinsically connected to the body and beneficial in that connection. Therefore, even hair that is ready to be shorn might not be treated as if already shorn, and the two *tanna'im* debating the hair may not track Rabbi Meir and the Sages on the grapes.

## Deceased Convert

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לְהַאי שְׁמַעְתָּא דְרַב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָמַר רַב הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** In connection with Rav's statement that one who renounces ownership of his slave emancipates the slave, the Gemara recounts: **When Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef ascended** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael, **he stated this *halakha* of Rav,** that one who renounces ownership of his slave emancipates the slave, **before Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him** in astonishment: Did **Rav** actually **say so?**

### Rabbi Yochanan Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: אָמַר רַב כְּווֹתִי? וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: לָא סַיְּימוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלָא אָמַר רַב צָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר?! וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ – דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yoḥanan was not objecting to Rav's opinion, but **this is what he said to him: Did Rav** actually **say in accordance with my** opinion? **And** alternatively, **there are** those **who say** that Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef **did not conclude** Rav's statement **before** Rabbi Yoḥanan by saying that nevertheless the slave requires a bill of manumission. Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** him: **And didn't Rav say that** the slave **requires a bill of manumission? And Rabbi Yoḥanan** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to one **who renounces ownership of his slave,** the slave **is emancipated but** nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁיחְרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself: Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to one **who renounces ownership of his slave,** the slave **is emancipated but** nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission** to be fully considered a freeman and to be able to marry a Jewish woman.

Rabbi Yochanan's reaction was: *amar Rav hachi?* — did Rav really say this? The Gemara clarifies the ambiguity: one version of the exchange records Rabbi Yochanan as genuinely surprised that Rav held this way, prompting the Gemara to note that Rabbi Yochanan himself said precisely the same thing (*amar Ulla amar Rabbi Yochanan ha-mafkir avdo yatza le-cherut ve-tzarich get shichrur*) — so his question was not a challenge but an expression of pleased agreement: "Rav says it just like I do?" An alternate version records that Rabbi Chiyya bar Yosef had not yet completed the teaching before Rabbi Yochanan interjected, resulting in a momentary misunderstanding that was quickly resolved. At the end of the day, both Rav and Rabbi Yochanan agree: renouncing property ownership of a slave does not automatically change his personal status; a *get shichrur* is still required.

### Slaves Take Themselves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְעוּלָּא: גֵּר שֶׁמֵּת וּבִזְבְּזוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל נְכָסָיו, וְהָיוּ בָּהֶן עֲבָדִים – בֵּין גְּדוֹלִים וּבֵין קְטַנִּים, קָנוּ עַצְמָן בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלִים – קָנוּ עַצְמָן בְּנֵי חוֹרִין; קְטַנִּים – כׇּל הַמַּחְזִיק בָּהֶן, זָכָה בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba raised an objection to Ulla** from a *baraita*: If **a convert died and Jews plundered his property,** as his possessions became ownerless property with his death because he had no heirs, **and among** his possessions **were slaves,** then, **whether** the slaves were **adults or minors, they acquire** ownership of **themselves** and become **freemen,** as they can acquire themselves from the ownerless property. **Abba Shaul says: Adult** slaves **acquire** ownership of **themselves** and become **freemen.** However, with regard to **minor** slaves, **anyone who takes possession of them acquires them.**

Rabbi Abba then challenges Ulla with a *baraita* about a *ger* — a convert — who dies leaving no heirs. Because conversion severs all halakhic familial relationships, a convert who has no children dies with no legal relatives. His property becomes ownerless, and anyone who seizes it acquires it. If among his property there are adult slaves, those slaves — at the moment of the owner's death — find themselves in a state equivalent to *hefker*: they are owned by no one. The *baraita* rules *kanu atzman b'nei chorin* — the adult slaves take possession of themselves and become free people.

Abba Shaul qualifies this: adult slaves can indeed take possession of themselves, but minor slaves cannot. A halakhic minor lacks the legal capacity to acquire property, including themselves. Therefore, minor slaves in the deceased convert's estate remain legally ownerless objects available for acquisition by whoever seizes them first.

### Writ Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מִי כָּתַב גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר לְאֵלּוּ?
>
> **Translation:** This appears difficult for Rabbi Yoḥanan, as one could ask: **But who wrote a bill of manumission for those** slaves who became free men? Their owner died without freeing them, and nevertheless they are emancipated. Therefore, the *baraita* demonstrates that when a slave becomes ownerless, he is emancipated entirely and does not require a bill of manumission to be considered a freeman.

This *baraita* appears to challenge Rabbi Yochanan's position directly: if *mafkir* requires a *get shichrur* for personal status change, and the convert who died left no heirs and no one alive to write such a writ, how can the adult slaves go free? The challenge is pointed: *mi katav get shichrur l'elu?* — who writes them their writ of manumission?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: דָּמֵי הַאי מִדְּרַבָּנַן כִּדְלָא גָּמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי שְׁמַעְתָּא. וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, קָסָבַר עוּלָּא: עַבְדּוֹ דְּגֵר כִּי אִשְׁתּוֹ – מָה אִשְׁתּוֹ מִשְׁתַּלַּחַת בְּלֹא גֵּט, אַף עֲבָדָיו מִשְׁתַּלְּחִים בְּלֹא גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said** in reply: **This** one **of the Rabbis,** Rabbi Abba, **is like a person who has not studied *halakha*.** He refused to address Rabbi Abba's objection, as he did not think it was worthy of a response. The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that these slaves do not require a bill of manumission? **Rav Naḥman said** that **Ulla holds** as follows: **A slave of a convert is comparable to his wife** in this case. **Just as his wife is freed** by his death **without a bill of divorce,** and she is no longer considered married with regard to any *halakhot*, **so too, his slaves are freed without a bill** of manumission.

Ulla's reply is reported as dismissive — he compared the questioner to someone who had never learned the relevant *sugya*. But the Gemara presses: *ta'ama mai?* Setting aside the tone, what is the actual answer?

### Master-Slave Relation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִתְנַחַלְתֶּם אוֹתָם לִבְנֵיכֶם אַחֲרֵיכֶם לָרֶשֶׁת אֲחוּזָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer: **If so,** if a slave is comparable to a wife, then **even** the slave of **a Jew** that dies should be freed entirely **as well,** just as his wife is. Why should this *halakha* apply only to the slave of a convert? The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to slaves: **"And you may make them an inheritance for your children after you, to hold for a possession"** (Leviticus 25:46). Therefore, slaves are not emancipated with the death of their owner, as the heirs have a right to the slaves. However, in the case of a convert who does not have heirs, the slaves are emancipated.

Rav Nachman explains Ulla's reasoning: *kasavar Ulla d'ger k'ishto* — Ulla holds that the master-slave relationship is analogous to the husband-wife relationship with respect to the *get*. Just as a wife who is freed by her husband's death does not require a *get* — death itself dissolves the personal bond — so too a slave whose master dies without heirs is freed without a writ. The *get shichrur* is necessary only when the master-slave relationship continues in some form — specifically, when there remains a living person who holds the legal identity of master, even if property ownership has already been renounced.

The key distinction is between two components of the master-slave relationship: the property dimension (ownership of the slave as chattel) and the personal dimension (the relational identity of master and slave). Even after property ownership ends — whether through *mafkir* or through *hekdesh* — the personal relationship persists. The former master retains the legal standing to issue a *get shichrur* precisely because, at the level of personal status, the identity of master has not yet dissolved. It is that ongoing personal relationship that makes the *get* necessary — and also makes the former master the uniquely qualified person to write it.

When the master dies and there are heirs, those heirs inherit not only the property dimension but also the relational identity of master. They become the ones in the position to write the writ. When the *ger* dies with no heirs, however, there is no one who inherits the identity of master. With no living master, the relational basis for the personal status of slavery dissolves entirely — just as a wife is freed without a *get* when her husband dies. No writ is needed because there is no surviving master to write one, and the absence of a master means the slave's personal identity as a slave has no anchor.

### Master Dies After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ וָמֵת – נָמֵי; אַלְּמָה אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַמַּפְקִיר עַבְדּוֹ וָמֵת – אוֹתוֹ הָעֶבֶד אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה? דְּאַמֵּימָר קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then in a case of **one who renounces ownership of his slave and dies,** the slaves should not require a bill of manumission **as well,** as they are not part of the inheritance of the children. **Why** then **did Ameimar say** that with regard to **one who renounces ownership of his slave and dies,** there is **no remedy for that slave** and he cannot marry a Jewish woman, as there is no one to emancipate him? The Gemara states: This statement **of Ameimar** poses **a difficulty** to Rav Naḥman's explanation of Ulla's statement.

The Gemara presses this logic with a new case: what if a master first renounces property ownership (*mafkir avdo*) — so the slave is no longer owned as property but still requires a *get shichrur* for personal status — and then the master dies? By the same reasoning applied to the deceased convert, one would expect the slave to go free automatically: the master is dead, no heir exists to assume the identity of master (since the slave was already *mafkir* and therefore is not an inherited asset), and therefore the relational basis for the personal status of slavery should dissolve. Should this case not parallel the *ger* who dies?

### Ameimar Stuck

The Gemara notes that Ameimar rules otherwise: *hamafkir avdo u'met oto eved ein lo takanah* — if one renounced ownership of his slave and then died, that slave has no remedy. He is stuck: he is not owned as property, so he works for himself and retains his own earnings, but his personal status as slave has not changed. He cannot marry a Jewish woman and is not fully obligated in *mitzvot*. He needs a *get shichrur* to change his personal status, but there is no living master with standing to write one. The Gemara concedes: *de'Ameimar kashya* — Ameimar's ruling is difficult to reconcile with the model derived from the *ger*. The two positions stand in tension, and the Gemara leaves Ameimar's ruling as a difficult point.

### Writ Problem

The broader theoretical point stands: Rav and Rabbi Yochanan hold that property ownership and personal status are legally distinct. Renouncing property ownership — whether through *mafkir* or *makdish* — does not automatically dissolve the personal status of slavery. Only a *get shichrur* issued by the person holding the legal identity of master can accomplish that. The cases of the deceased *ger* and the *mafkir* who subsequently dies represent edge cases where the legal machinery breaks down: the writ is required but the person uniquely qualified to issue it no longer exists.

### Abba Shaul Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul** with regard to a convert who died and left behind slaves.

Finally, Rav Yaakov bar Idi cites Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *halacha k'Abba Shaul* — the ruling follows Abba Shaul. In the case of a convert who dies with no heirs, adult slaves acquire themselves and go free, but minor slaves do not and may be seized by anyone. Rav Zeira asks Rav Yaakov bar Idi whether this ruling was received explicitly or inferred from something else, and the Gemara begins to explore what the inferential basis might have been — though this discussion extends beyond the present daf.
