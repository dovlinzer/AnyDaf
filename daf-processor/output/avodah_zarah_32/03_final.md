# Avodah Zarah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine Review & Assur BeHa…

### Wine Transport Review

Today's daf is 32. We pick up at the bottom of 31b, approximately five lines from the bottom of the amud.

### Non-Jewish Contact

The previous session's discussion centered on the transport of wine and the concerns surrounding tampering, seals, and supervision. Along the way it became explicit — though it had been implicit throughout — that even a non-Jew's mere touching of wine renders it problematic.

### Assur BeHana'ah Status

The earlier discussion also addressed the question of whether the wine in question is *assur behana'ah* or not. The basic ruling that emerged is that non-Jewish wine is *assur behana'ah*, though the Gemara did engage with a scenario in which uncertainty about whether a non-Jew actually touched the wine might leave it in an intermediate category — forbidden for drinking but potentially permissible for *hana'ah* — though that middle position was never fully explicated.

## Exposed Water Discussion

### Rav's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הַאי שִׁיכְרָא דַּאֲרַמָּאָה שְׁרֵי, וְחִיָּיא בְּרִי לָא נִישְׁתֵּי מִינֵּיהּ. מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי שְׁרֵי — לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁרֵי, אִי אֲסִיר — לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֲסִיר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: This Aramean beer is permitted, but my son Ḥiyya does not drink from it.** The Gemara asks: **Whichever** way **you** look at this matter, Rav's statement is difficult: **If** the beer is **permitted,** then it is **permitted to everyone,** and there is no reason for his son to refrain from drinking it. And **if** it is **prohibited,** it is **prohibited to everyone,** and why would Rav say it is permitted?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַב סָבַר מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּיָא, וְאָזֵיל מְרוֹרָא דִּכְשׁוּתָא וְקָלֵי לֵיהּ זִיהֲרֵיהּ, וְדִלְקֵי מַלְקֵי לֵיהּ טְפֵי, וְחִיָּיא בְּרִי הוֹאִיל וּלְקֵי לָא נִישְׁתֵּי מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather, Rav holds** that the prohibition is **due to exposure, but the bitterness of the hops** in the beer **goes and impairs the** snake's **venom,** so that it is safe for an average person to drink. **But** a person of **weak** constitution **is weakened further** by the impaired venom, **and** Rav was saying: In the case of **my son Ḥiyya, since he is weak, he does not drink from it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַשְּׁרָצִים יֵשׁ לָהֶן אֶרֶס, שֶׁל נָחָשׁ מֵמִית, שֶׁל שְׁרָצִים אֵינוֹ מֵמִית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב: בַּר אַרְיָא, תָּא וְאֵימָא לָךְ מִילְּתָא מְעַלְּיָיתָא דַּהֲוָה אָמַר רַב אֲבוּךְ, הָכִי אָמַר אֲבוּךְ: הָנֵי אַרַמָּאֵי זוּקָאנֵי — (דַּהֲווֹ שָׁתוּ) [מִשּׁוּם דְּשָׁתוּ] גִּילּוּיָא, (וְלָא מִתוּ) [וְהַאי דְּלָא מָיְיתִי] — אַיְּידֵי דְּאָכְלִי שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים חֲבִיל גּוּפַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: All creeping animals possess venom;** that **of a snake kills,** whereas the venom **of** other **creeping animals does not kill. Shmuel said to Ḥiyya bar Rav: Son of a lion! Come and I will say to you a superior matter that your father, Rav, said. This** is what **your father said: These Arameans** are **swollen [*zukanei*] because they drink exposed** liquids, **but they did not die** from doing so **since they eat repugnant creatures and creeping animals,** which **heat their bodies** and thereby render them less susceptible to the venom.

### Non-Jewish Constitution

The explanation given is: *adi de'achlei shekatzim uremasim chavilei gufaihei* — since they eat all manner of forbidden and perhaps even mildly poisonous creatures, this damages, or in some sense conditions, their bodies so that they are not harmed by what would otherwise be dangerous. Their constitution, shaped by a lifetime of consuming such things, renders them immune. Accordingly, their surviving *giluy* cannot serve as proof that the concern is unfounded for others.

## Beer Vinegar Discussion

### Beer Vinegar Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי חַלָּא דְּשִׁיכְרָא דַּאֲרַמָּאָה אָסוּר, דִּמְעָרְבִי בֵּיהּ דּוּרְדְּיָא דְּיֵין נֶסֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: This vinegar** made **of Aramean beer is prohibited, as they mix in it yeast of wine** used for **a libation.**

The Gemara then moves to a new teaching: *amar Rav Yosef*. This appears on the last line of 31b. *Hai chala deshichra de'arma assur* — the beer vinegar of non-Jews is forbidden. The reason: *dema'arvi bei durdaya de'yayin nesech* — because they mix into it the lees, the grape sediment, of what is called *yayin nesech*. It should be noted that the term *yayin nesech* here functions loosely; it refers more precisely to *stam yeinam*, the ordinary wine of non-Jews, which is treated as forbidden through an extension of the prohibition against wine poured as a libation. The point is that non-Jews sometimes add grape-derived additives — grape skins, sediment, lees — to their beer vinegar, and it is on account of these additives that the product becomes forbidden.

This is, notably, distinct from the concerns raised in earlier discussions about non-Jewish beer more generally. There, the issue was proximity and social entanglement — the concern of *benosei akum* — but stepping outside the establishment was sufficient to remove the concern. Beer per se was not treated as a forbidden food. Here, with beer vinegar, the concern is different and more direct: a possible wine-derived additive renders the product genuinely problematic.

### Warehoused Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וּמֵאוֹצָר שְׁרֵי, כֵּיוָן דִּמְעָרְבִי בֵּיהּ מִסְרָא סְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: But** vinegar **from a storeroom is permitted, since** if another substance is **mixed with it, it would spoil** over time.

*Amar Rav Ashi:* however, *ume'otzar shari* — if the beer vinegar is drawn from a warehouse or storehouse, it is permissible.

### Spoilage Logic

The reasoning is: *keivan dema'arvi bei mistari* — because when they add grape additives to a product intended for storage, those same additives will cause it to spoil more quickly. Merchants storing goods for later sale therefore do not add grape material to items in storage; they would only add such things at or after the point of sale, once the product has already been drawn from storage. Accordingly, when one obtains beer vinegar from a warehouse, one may assume with confidence that it contains no grape additive and is thus permissible.

These are the kinds of practical determinations that characterized *kashrus* practice across centuries and in many communities where certified supervision was unavailable. In the absence of a *hechsher* infrastructure, halakhic authorities made carefully reasoned assumptions about commercial practice, and those assumptions guided what people could and could not consume.

## Tosafot on Mead

### Mead Concern

Tosafot extends this discussion with practical application. Tosafot notes that just as the Gemara raises the concern of grape additives in beer vinegar, there is an analogous concern regarding mead — *shichra devash*, the drink of honey, referred to in the vernacular as *meet*. Tosafot writes: *tzrich lehizaher beshichra devash* — one must be careful with mead — *livdok im matilim bo shimrei yayin* — to check whether they add grape leaves or grape material to it, *le'hamid maher*, presumably to accelerate fermentation, much as a small amount of grape material can initiate or speed up fermentation in other contexts. *Ki yesh mekomos she'osim ken* — there are indeed places where this is done. *Ve'az vadai hu assur* — in such cases the mead is certainly forbidden.

The force of Tosafot's concern here is not derived directly from the Gemara's case; rather, Tosafot is drawing on independent knowledge of commercial practice to issue what amounts to a *kashrus* alert: in general you may purchase mead from a non-Jew, but in locales where it is known that grape material is added, one may not.

### Stored Beverages Rule

Tosafot then draws the broader conclusion from the principle established by Rav Ashi: *hilkach nira demutar liknos kol minei mashkeh min ovdei kochavim* — it appears permissible to purchase all varieties of drinks from a non-Jew, including apple juice (*yayin tapuchim*) and strawberry juice (*mei tutim*) and the like. The reasoning is the same as in the Gemara's discussion of stored beer vinegar: wine or grape additives, if introduced into a product held in storage, would cause the product to spoil. Tosafot generalizes this principle — assuming it holds true for all stored liquids — and concludes that one need not be concerned about possible wine admixture in any stored drink, unless one has specific reason to believe otherwise.

This permissibility, however, is conditioned on an important visibility requirement: *uvelvad sheyireh Yisrael moshech min hachavit* — specifically, the Jew must see the non-Jew drawing the liquid from the barrel. If the product is drawn from storage before one's eyes, one may rely on the principle that stored goods do not contain grape additives. Tosafot distinguishes between liquid held in storage — which is assumed safe — and freshly prepared liquid, where no such assumption applies.

### Back Room Caveat

A further caveat is added: *aval im lo ra'ahu moshech min hachavit* — if one does not see the non-Jew drawing from the barrel but instead the non-Jew goes to a back storage room and brings it out, the matter requires closer attention — *az hadavar mischalek* — the ruling then depends on further considerations.

### Price & Admixture Risk

Tosafot then refines the caveat based on economic logic. If the non-Jew retrieves the drink from the back room and the drink in question is more expensive than wine, then even though it is presumably stored goods, one must be concerned — *ye'aser shema eirev bo yayin* — that he may have mixed in some wine. Why? *Lehistaker o leharviach* — to make a greater profit, essentially watering down the product with something cheaper. If, on the other hand, wine is more expensive than the drink being sold, then *ein lachush shema eirev bo yayin lehachshilo* — there is no reason to fear that he deliberately mixed in wine merely to cause the Jew to transgress. No one adulterates a more expensive product into a cheaper one for profit; the concern only arises when wine is the cheaper of the two.

To summarize Tosafot's position: any stored drink may be purchased from a non-Jew without concern about grape admixture. The only additional concern arises when one does not witness the non-Jew drawing from the barrel, in which case the relative price of wine determines whether there is genuine grounds for suspicion.

## Hadrianic Pottery

### Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וָחֶרֶס הַדְרְיָינִי. מַאי הַדְרְיָינִי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חֶרֶס שֶׁל הַדְרְיָינוּס קֵיסָר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And Hadrianic earthenware** is prohibited. The Gemara asks: **What is Hadrianic** earthenware? **Rav Yehuda says that Shmuel says:** It is **earthenware of Emperor Hadrian.**

The Mishnah also lists *cheres hadiyani* — Hadrianic pottery — among the items that are *assur behana'ah*. The Gemara asks: *mai hadiyani?* What is Hadrianic pottery? *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel:* it is the pottery of Hadrian the Caesar.

### Rav Dimi's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: קַרְקַע בְּתוּלָה הָיְתָה שֶׁלֹּא עֲבָדָהּ אָדָם מֵעוֹלָם, עֲבָדָהּ וּנְטָעָהּ, וְרָמֵי לֵיהּ לְחַמְרָא בְּגוּלְפֵי חִיוָּרֵי, וּמָיְיצִי לְהוּ לְחַמְרַיְיהוּ, וּמְתַבְּרוּ לְהוּ בְּחַסְפֵי וְדָרוּ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּמָטוּ תָּרוּ לְהוּ וְשָׁתוּ. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: וְרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁלָּנוּ כִּשְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came, he said: There was** an expanse of **virgin soil that no man** had **ever tilled** before, and Hadrian **tilled it and planted** grapevines in **it,** which yielded wine of the highest quality. **And** they **placed** this **wine in white jugs, and** the jugs **absorbed the wine. And they** would **break** the jugs into **shards and carry** the shards **with them, and anywhere that they stopped, they soaked** these shards in water **and drank** the water. The Gemara notes that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: And our first-**rate wine **is like** the wine produced by the **third** usage of **their** Hadrianic earthenware.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he provided the following account of how this pottery was produced: *karka besula hayta shelo avda adam me'olam* — they began with virgin soil, land that had never been cultivated. Such land retains all of its nutrients and natural potency. They then plowed this land, planted it with grapevines, and produced wine from the resulting grapes. Because the soil had never been worked before, the wine produced from it was extraordinarily strong and concentrated. They then took this highly potent wine and placed it in *gufi chivri* — white pottery vessels — which were uniquely absorbent. The pottery absorbed the wine entirely into its walls.

### Wine Transport Method

The pottery was then broken into small shards. *Umitru lehu b'chafsabi* — the shards were collected, and *v'daru b'hadaihu* — the Roman army would carry these shards with them on campaign. Wherever they traveled, *taru lehu v'shatu* — they would immerse a shard in water, and the concentrated wine absorbed in the pottery would reconstitute, yielding wine. This was, in effect, a form of dehydrated wine — a portable concentrate for military use. The white pottery was the carrier medium, and the shards themselves were essentially solidified wine.

### Wine Quality

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi:* our finest quality wine — *rishon she'bahen* — is equivalent only to their third-grade product — *kishlishi she'lahen* — meaning the wine reconstituted from these pottery shards at its third remove is still superior to the best Jewish wine. This gives a sense of just how intensely concentrated the wine absorbed into this pottery actually was.

## Pottery Status Debate

### Central Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לִסְמוֹךְ בָּהֶן כַּרְעֵי הַמִּטָּה? רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר — שְׁרֵי אוֹ אָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* if one wishes to use such shards **to support the legs of a bed with them?** Is one who **desires** a prohibited item's continued **existence** in order **to** use it for **another matter,** i.e., not for the prohibited purpose, **permitted** to use it **or prohibited** from doing so? In this case, no benefit whatsoever is derived from the wine absorbed within the shards, but the shards themselves are being used to support the bed.

*Iba'ye lehu* — a question was posed: *mahu lismoch bahen karia d'mita* — what is the law if one wishes to use these pottery shards to support, for example, a wobbly bed leg? The shard is being used as pottery — as a structural prop — not as wine. Is that permissible, or do we treat the shard as though it were wine itself, making even such incidental use forbidden?

The stakes of this question are significant. Very few things are *assur behana'ah* entirely, and the question here is whether the Mishnah's prohibition on Hadrianic pottery extends to any benefit derived from it whatsoever, or whether it is limited to extracting the wine from it. Two considerations compound the question: first, the factual situation — these shards are so saturated with wine that the Romans used them to reconstitute actual wine, making them functionally indistinguishable from wine itself; second, the categorical consideration — this falls under *stam yeinam* and related wine prohibitions, which carry their own weight of stringency.

Additionally, the language of the Mishnah is itself suggestive: the Mishnah says the pottery is forbidden, without limiting the prohibition to situations where wine is extracted from it. This formulation invites the question of how broadly the prohibition extends.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חַד אָסַר וְחַד שָׁרֵי, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּמַאן דְּאָסַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma, **as Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yoḥanan** engaged in a dispute in this case: **One prohibited** using the shards in such a fashion, **and one permitted** this practice. The Gemara adds: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of the Sage **who prohibited** it.

*Rotzeh b'kiyumo yedei d'var acher sharya o assur* — if you want the pottery there and the benefit you derive is from the pottery itself, not from the wine in it, is that permitted or forbidden? The Gemara notes that Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yochanan disagree on this, one forbidding and one permitting — though this formulation, it should be noted, is the subject of textual debate, as many such rulings in the Bavli are suspected of being later Geonic glosses rather than original Talmudic text.

### Barrels & Flasks Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַדַּרְדּוּרִין וְהָרוּקְבָּאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, יַיִן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל כָּנוּס בָּהֶן — אָסוּר בִּשְׁתִיָּה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה. הֵעִיד שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גּוּדָּא לִפְנֵי בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל עַל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, שֶׁשָּׁתָה מִמֶּנּוּ בְּעַכּוֹ, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* to the Sage who deems it permitted: With regard to **the jugs [*dardurin*] and flagons [*rokva'ot*] of gentiles** that have **a Jew's wine contained in them,** one is **prohibited from drinking** the wine, **but** one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it. The Gemara notes that **Shimon ben Guda testified before the son of Rabban Gamliel with regard to Rabban Gamliel that he drank from it in Akko, but** the Sages **did not concede to** the ramifications of his testimony.

The Gemara raises a challenge. A Baraita discusses *hardardurin* and *rokva'os* — various types of barrels and leather flasks belonging to non-Jews — and addresses what happens if a Jew stores his own wine in them. If a Jew places his wine in a non-Jewish vessel, there is concern that wine previously absorbed into the walls of that vessel will leach back out into the Jewish wine. Under normal *kashrus* principles, if everything is cold, such absorption is generally not a concern — *noten ta'am* under cold conditions does not typically create a prohibition. Yet here, the Gemara seems to indicate heightened concern, perhaps because wine vessels absorb so thoroughly, or perhaps due to the particular stringency applied in the domain of non-Jewish wine. The result: *assur b'shtiya u'mutar behana'ah* — the wine becomes forbidden for drinking but remains permissible for benefit.

This is another instance of the intermediate category that appeared earlier: not full *yayin nesech* status, not outright permission, but a middle level reflecting uncertainty or trace-level absorption. Notably, this is not the exotic case of Hadrianic pottery; this is ordinary barrels and leather flasks. The concern is entirely about wine leaching back out from the absorbed material — the vessels themselves have not been used in any idolatrous act.

### Rabban Gamliel Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ. וּרְמִינְהִי: יַיִן הַבָּא בְּרוּקְבָּאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם אָסוּר בִּשְׁתִיָּה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, הֵעִיד שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גּוּדָּע לִפְנֵי בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל עַל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שֶׁשָּׁתָה מִמֶּנּוּ בְּעַכּוֹ, וְהוֹדוּ לוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated that Shimon ben Guda provided testimony, **but** the Sages **did not concede** to its ramifications. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction:** With regard to **wine that comes in the flagons of gentiles,** one is **prohibited from drinking** the wine, **but** one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it. **Shimon ben Guda testified before the son of Rabban Gamliel with regard to Rabban Gamliel that he drank from it in Akko, and they conceded to him.** This directly contradicts the episode cited above.

The Baraita continues: *heid Rabbi Shimon ben Guda lifnei bno shel Rabban Gamliel* — Rabbi Shimon ben Guda testified before Rabban Gamliel's son about his father, that Rabban Gamliel himself had drunk wine stored in such non-Jewish flasks in Akko, demonstrating that it is entirely permissible. *V'lo hodu lo* — and they did not agree with him. The sages around — whether or not Rabban Gamliel's son himself agreed is left unclear — rejected the testimony, effectively saying that Rabban Gamliel would never have done such a thing, or alternatively that even if he did, they do not accept that as the normative ruling.

## Leather Flask Ruling

### Saddle Cloth Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹדוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם — רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קַפּוֹסַאי: אָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת מֵהֶן שְׁטִיחִין לַחֲמוֹר, וְהָא הָכָא דְּרוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר, וְקָתָנֵי דְּאָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** Concerning **wineskins** that **belong to gentiles, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kefusai:** It is **prohibited to fashion from them** items such as **blankets to** cover **a donkey,** as one derives benefit from them. The Gemara explains the objection: **And here,** in the case of wineskins used as donkey covers, **he desires its** continued **existence for another matter, and** yet the *baraita* **teaches that** it is **prohibited** to use it for this purpose.

The Gemara then cites Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kaphusai: *assur la'asos mehem davar acher* — specifically, one may not use a non-Jewish leather flask as a saddle cloth for one's donkey. Here the benefit derived is purely from the leather — there is no intent to extract wine from it — yet it is forbidden. This appears to support the position that even using the vessel itself for a purpose entirely unrelated to wine is prohibited.

### Challenge to Buy Jugs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, קַנְקַנִּים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם לִיתַּסְרוּ לְמִיזְבַּן! מַאי שְׁנָא נוֹדוֹת וּמַאי שְׁנָא קַנְקַנִּים? אָמַר רָבָא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִבָּקַע נוֹדוֹ וְיִטְּלֶנּוּ וְיִתְפְּרֶנּוּ עַל גַּבֵּי נוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara retorts: **And according to your reasoning, it should be prohibited to sell jugs belonging to gentiles,** and yet Jews sell them frequently; **what is different** about **wineskins,** from which one may not derive indirect benefit, **and what is different** about **jugs,** which may be sold for indirect benefit? The Gemara answers that **Rava says:** There is a rabbinic **decree** that one may not sell wineskins of gentiles **lest his** own **wineskin break** open, **and** to repair it **he would take** the gentile's wineskin **and sew it onto his wineskin.** This would cause the wine absorbed in the gentile's wineskin to mix with the wine of the Jew and render it forbidden.

The Gemara presses this logic: if we are genuinely concerned about the absorbed wine in non-Jewish vessels, why do we permit purchasing pottery jugs from non-Jews for use in storing our own wine? Clearly those jugs were previously used for non-Jewish wine. *Mai shna nod u'mai shna kankanim* — what is the difference between the leather flask and pottery jugs?

### Rava's Answer

*Amar Rava:* the distinction is not about the vessel per se but about a specific rabbinic concern — *gezeirah shema yibaka nodo* — there is a concern that if one uses a leather flask as a saddle cloth while traveling, the flask might split or crack, and *veyatilnu veyitferenu al gabei nodo* — one might then be tempted to use it as a patch for a leaking wine flask. In that case, the absorbed non-Jewish wine in the leather patch would leach into one's own wine. This is the specific danger the rabbis addressed with their decree. It does not reflect a general principle that one cannot derive any benefit from a vessel that absorbed non-Jewish wine. With pottery jugs, that particular concern does not apply — one does not patch a cracked pottery jug with a shard of pottery — and therefore purchasing and using them is permitted.

The implication is that in ordinary cases, deriving benefit from the vessel itself is not prohibited; only the absorption of the wine back into one's own wine is the concern. The saddle cloth prohibition is a *gezeirah* against a specific scenario, not a general rule.

### Concentration Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר רוֹצֶה בְּקִיּוּמוֹ עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר אָסוּר, מַאי שְׁנָא קַנְקַנִּים דִּשְׁרוּ? אָמַר לָךְ: הָתָם לֵיתֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ, הָכָא אִיתֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that someone who **desires** the continued **existence of** a prohibited item **for another matter is prohibited** from using the item in this way, **what is different** about **jugs that** purchasing them is **permitted?** The Gemara explains that this Sage could have **said to you: There,** with regard to the jugs, **there is no substantive prohibited** entity, whereas **here,** in the case of Hadrianic earthenware, **there is a substantive prohibited** entity, as the wine is recognizable in the earthenware.

The Gemara then returns to the question of Hadrianic pottery and addresses it from the perspective of the opinion that holds using the pottery itself is forbidden: *hasam leseih le-issura be'einei hacha iseih le-issura be'einei* — in ordinary vessels the wine absorption is low-level and the wine does not exist as wine within the pottery in any meaningful sense. But in Hadrianic pottery, the wine is so fully absorbed and so intensely concentrated — the Romans reconstituted actual wine from it — that the pottery is treated as though it *is* the wine. It is not a vessel that once stored wine; it is effectively a solid form of wine itself. Accordingly, the prohibition extends not just to extracting the wine but to deriving any benefit from the shard.

This resolves the inner logic of the debate: for ordinary vessels, derived benefit is the prohibition only when wine leaches back out; for Hadrianic pottery, the pottery *is* the wine, and therefore even non-wine-related use is forbidden under the same principle as *stam yeinam* itself.

## Conflicting Testimonies

### First Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ״ דְּקָאָמַר הָתָם? כׇּל סִיעָתוֹ, אֲבָל בְּנוֹ מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: גּוּדָּא לְחוֹד, וְגוּדָּע לְחוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of the sentence: **But they did not concede to** the ramifications of his testimony, **which was stated there,** in the first account? The meaning is that the rest of **his entire company,** i.e., the Sages, did not concede, **but his son did concede to him. If you wish, say** instead that **Guda** with the letter *alef*, as stated in the first episode, is **discrete, and Guda** with an *ayin*, in the second account, is **discrete,** i.e., the two incidents are not referring to the same individual.

The Gemara now returns to the testimony of Rabbi Shimon ben Guda, which ended with *v'lo hodu lo* — the sages did not concede. But a separate Baraita appears to relate the same incident with a different conclusion: *v'hodu lo* — they did concede. How are these two contradictory endings to be reconciled? The first reconciliation offered by the Gemara: *hatam chavraya lo hodu lo aval bno modeh lo* — when it says they did not agree, it means the other sages present did not accept the testimony, but Rabban Gamliel's own son did accept it. According to this resolution, the son accepted his father's conduct as authoritative, while the other rabbis rejected it. This version would leave the normative ruling on the side of stringency.

### Second Version

The Gemara offers a second resolution: *iba'it eima, Guda l'chud u'Guda l'chud* — the two accounts actually refer to two different people, both named Guda — one spelled with an *aleph* and one with an *ayin*. The first Guda testified and the sages did not accept his account. Subsequently a second Guda testified the same thing, and *l'zeh hodu acharei khen* — to him they eventually agreed.

### Storage Implications

According to this second resolution, the final normative conclusion may be that storing wine in non-Jewish vessels is actually permissible — *mutar b'shtiya* — which would in turn explain the practice of purchasing pottery jugs from non-Jews for wine storage without apparent concern. With the exception of the extraordinary case of Hadrianic pottery, where the shards are treated as the wine itself, the Gemara may be endorsing a position that ordinary cold absorption from non-Jewish vessels does not create a prohibition on the stored wine. This remains somewhat ambiguous, and the Gemara acknowledges that the question does not fully resolve; how long wine is kept in such a vessel, the nature of the materials, and other circumstances may all play a role. The extended treatment of these cases at the end of the tractate will revisit them in greater detail.

## Leather Heart Cutout

### Definition & Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹרוֹת לְבוּבִין. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ עוֹר לָבוּב? כָּל שֶׁקָּרוּעַ כְּנֶגֶד הַלֵּב וְקָדוּר כְּמִין אֲרוּבָּה, יֵשׁ עָלָיו קוֹרֶט דָּם — אָסוּר,
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches: **And hides** with a tear opposite the **heart** are prohibited. **The Sages taught: What is** considered **a hide** with a tear opposite the **heart? Any** hide **that is torn opposite the heart and incised** in a shape **similar to an aperture,** and which **has a trace of** coagulated **blood on it,** is **prohibited.**

The Mishnah moves on to another category of items forbidden in benefit: *orot levuvin* — leather from which a piece has been cut out corresponding to the heart of the animal. The concern is that the leather was used in some idolatrous practice — apparently, cutting open the living animal over its heart in the course of a ritual act, reminiscent of the most extreme forms of pagan sacrifice. The leather with this characteristic excision is therefore suspect as having been used in idolatrous worship.

### Blood Indicator

The Rabbis taught: *eizehu or lavuv?* — what constitutes a *levuv* hide? *Kol she'karua k'neged halev* — any hide that is torn open opposite the heart area, in a rounded shape like a skylight. *Yesh alav koret dam assur* — if there is a trace of blood absorbed into the leather, it is forbidden, since the presence of blood indicates that the cut was made while the animal was still alive, i.e., as part of an act of worship. *Ein alav koret dam mutar* — if there is no blood, it is permitted, as the absence of blood suggests the cut was made post-mortem, during the flaying process, and thus had no idolatrous significance.

### Salted Leather Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין עָלָיו קוֹרֶט דָּם — מוּתָּר. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא מְלָחוֹ, אֲבָל מְלָחוֹ — אָסוּר. אֵימָא: מִלְחוֹ הֶעֱבִרַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If it does **not** have **a trace of blood upon it,** then it **is permitted. Rav Huna says: They taught** that a bloodless hide is permitted **only** in a case **where** the gentile **did not salt it, but** if he **salted it,** it is **prohibited,** as **I say: Its salting removed** the trace of blood.

*Amar Rav Huna:* this distinction applies only when the leather has not been salted. *Aval maluach assur* — if the leather has been treated with salt, it is forbidden even in the absence of visible blood. The reasoning: *eimar milcha avrateih* — one may say that the salt drew the blood out, eliminating any visible trace. Thus the absence of blood on salted leather cannot serve as exculpatory evidence.

### Shape Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַקֶּרַע שֶׁלּוֹ עָגוֹל — אָסוּר, מָשׁוּךְ — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** A hide is **prohibited** only **when the tear** around its heart is **circular,** but if it is **elongated,** it **is permitted.**

Rabban Gamliel adds a further criterion: the shape of the cut is determinative. If the cut is circular — *agol* — it is forbidden, as that shape corresponds to the characteristic form of the idolatrous practice. If it is elongated — *mashucha* — it is permitted, as that shape does not correspond to the ritual practice.

This might seem in tension with the earlier description of the cut as resembling a skylight — a shape one might associate with a square or rectangular opening with flaps — but Rabban Gamliel clarifies that the operative distinction is between circular and non-circular.

### Rav Yosef-Abaye Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הֲלָכָה — מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי נָפְקָא לָךְ מִינַּהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גְּמָרָא גְּמוֹר, זְמוֹרְתָּא תְּהֵא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Rav Yosef says** that **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: If one rules that the ***halakha*** is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, does that mean **by inference that** the Rabbis **disagree,** or perhaps there is no dispute and everyone accepts the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel? Rav Yosef **said to him: What difference is there to you** whether or not the Rabbis disagree? In either case the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Abaye **said to him,** invoking a folk expression with regard to one who learns without achieving understanding: Is it simply **learn the lesson;** let **it be** like **a song?** In other words, is it sufficient to simply parrot the halakhic ruling? No; it is necessary to examine an issue to understand it even if it does not yield a practical halakhic difference.

*Amar Rav Yosef amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: halacha k'Rabban Gamliel* — the ruling follows Rabban Gamliel. *Amar lei Abaye:* Abaye challenged Rav Yosef — when you say we rule like Rabban Gamliel, the implication is that he is *disagreeing* with the *Tanna Kamma*. But perhaps Rabban Gamliel is merely clarifying what the *Tanna Kamma* already meant, not introducing any novelty. If so, stating that we rule like him conveys nothing.

*Amar lei Rav Yosef:* what practical difference does it make whether he disagrees or clarifies, if the ruling is the same? *Amar lei Abaye: gemara gemorei zimurta tehei?* — are we to go around reciting our traditions like a meaningless little ditty? If Rabban Gamliel adds nothing beyond the *Tanna Kamma*, then declaring that we rule like him is a statement without content, and Abaye refuses to issue such a ruling. Halakhic precision requires that every ruling mean something; if Rabban Gamliel is only clarifying, then no ruling needs to be issued in his name. Even absent any practical *nafka mina*, the integrity of the tradition demands exactness in formulation.

## Meat & Worship

### Pre-Entry Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּשָׂר הַנִּכְנָס לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מוּתָּר. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, הָאָמַר: סְתָם מַחְשֶׁבֶת גּוֹי לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches: **Meat that enters the** house of **idol worship,** before it is sacrificed, is **permitted.** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as if** one were to posit that it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** this would be difficult: **Doesn't he say:** When slaughtering an animal, **a gentile's unspecified intention** is to use it **for idol worship?** Accordingly, Rabbi Elazar would disagree with the ruling of the mishna that meat entering a house of idol worship is permitted.

The Mishnah turns to yet another case: *basar hanichnas l'avodat kochavim mutar* — meat that is being brought into a house of idol worship is permissible. If one encounters a non-Jew carrying meat toward a temple of idolatry and purchases it before he enters, one may derive benefit from it — not for personal consumption since the non-Jew slaughtered it, but the meat may be resold or otherwise used. Despite knowing where the meat is headed, the act of bringing it to the temple has not yet occurred and no idolatrous act has yet taken place with it.

### Rabbinic Attribution

*Man tanna?* — who is the author of this ruling? *Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan:* this is not consistent with the position of Rabbi Elazar.

### Rabbi Elazar's View

For Rabbi Elazar holds: *stam machshevet avodat kochavim l'avodat kochavim* — we assume that the standard intention of a non-Jew who slaughters an animal is to offer it to his god. The act of slaughtering is a classic form of worship — one offers an animal to one's deity — and therefore, under Rabbi Elazar's view, any animal slaughtered by a non-Jew must be presumed to have been slaughtered with idolatrous intent, rendering the meat forbidden in benefit at the moment of slaughter. On that view, the meat was already problematic long before it arrived at the temple gates.

### Main Position

Our Mishnah, however, goes well beyond merely rejecting that position. It rules that even meat that is actively being carried into the temple at this moment is still permissible — we do not presume that the slaughter itself, performed this morning, was an act of idolatrous offering. The intent to use the meat in worship does not retroactively render the act of slaughter an act of worship. This is a striking ruling and represents a significant limitation on the scope of concern.

### Post-Exit Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיּוֹצֵא אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּזִבְחֵי מֵתִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלֵיכָּא תִּקְרוֹבֶת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And** meat **that exits** the house of idol worship **is prohibited because it is** considered **as offerings to the dead,** i.e., idols. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that it is classified as such? It is because it is **impossible that it is not an idolatrous offering.** The Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion does this reflect? It **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira.**

*V'hayotzei assur mipnei shehu k'zivchei meitim* — but once the meat exits the temple, it is forbidden, because at that point it is considered like *zivchei meitim*, sacrifices to the dead. Whatever occurred inside the temple — and the Gemara notes *ei efshar l'likut shel avodat kochavim* — it is impossible that during his time inside the temple he did not in some way offer it up — the meat is now treated as having been presented to an idol and is therefore forbidden in benefit.

## Tumah & Dead Bodies

### Ba'al Pe'or Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְתִקְרוֹבֶת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּצָּמְדוּ לְבַעַל פְּעוֹר וַיֹּאכְלוּ זִבְחֵי מֵתִים״, מָה מֵת מְטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל, אַף תִּקְרוֹבֶת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: From where** is it derived **that an idolatrous offering imparts ritual impurity by means of a tent** to an individual or item situated together with it under the same structure, even if they do not come into direct contact? **As it is stated: "They joined themselves also unto Baal of Peor, and ate the offerings to the dead"** (Psalms 106:28). **Just as a corpse imparts ritual impurity by means of a tent, so too idolatrous offerings impart ritual impurity by means of a tent.** Similarly, Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira would hold that just as it is prohibited to derive benefit from a corpse, so too it is prohibited to derive benefit from idolatrous offerings.

*Mani?* — whose position does this represent? *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira hi* — it is Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira. The Gemara then cites the underlying derivation: *minayin l'tikroves avodat kochavim she'metamei b'ohel?* — from where do we know that an offering made to an idol conveys *tumah* under a tent, like a corpse? *She'ne'emar: Vayitzmadu le'Ba'al Pe'or vayochlu zivchei meitim* — as it says, "They attached themselves to Ba'al Pe'or and ate the sacrifices of the dead." The verse equates idolatrous offerings with the dead; just as a corpse conveys *tumah* to anything under the same roof, so too does an offering to idolatry.

### Tosafot's Question

Tosafot raises a sharp question: why does the Gemara need to attribute this to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira at all? The prohibition against deriving benefit from *tikroves avodat kara* is universally agreed upon. No authority disputes that items offered to idolatry are *assur behana'ah*. So what is the novel contribution of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's derivation, and why does the Gemara feel it necessary to invoke his position?

### First Answer

Tosafot's first answer: the case in our Mishnah is not a classic *tikroves* in the recognized halakhic sense. A standard act of idolatrous offering involves an action one would recognize as a formal act of worship — slaughtering an animal on an altar, pouring a libation, burning incense, or the like. But what happened here? A non-Jew walked into a temple with some meat, did something unspecified, and walked out. Is that recognizable as a proper act of worship? One might have thought not — that without a formally recognized act of offering, the meat does not acquire the status of *tikroves*. It is to address this ambiguity that Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's stringent derivation is invoked: by equating idolatrous offerings with dead bodies and insisting on the highest level of stringency in this domain, even an act that does not conform to a classic mode of offering is subsumed within the prohibition. The analogy to the *sheitel* controversy of recent memory is apt: when hair is brought into a temple and offered in the context of ritual practice, the question of whether that constitutes recognizable *tikroves* — and whether Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira's reasoning would apply — is precisely the kind of question this Gemara engages.

### Second Answer

Tosafot's second answer: the Mishnah itself signals that something beyond mere *issur hana'ah* is at stake. Throughout the Mishnah's list of prohibited items — wine, pottery, leather — the Mishnah gives reasons for the prohibition in each case. Here, uniquely, the Mishnah specifies that the meat is forbidden because it is *zivchei meitim*, sacrifices to the dead. This is not merely a restatement of the *issur hana'ah*; the phrase *zivchei meitim* is drawn from the verse about Ba'al Pe'or and carries the additional implication of *tumah*. The Mishnah's choice of this precise language signals that it intends not only to prohibit benefit but also to convey that the offering carries the *tumah* status of a corpse. And that additional ruling — *tumah be'ohel* from an idolatrous offering — is precisely the position of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira. Hence the Gemara attributes the Mishnah to him: not because he alone prohibits benefit from *tikroves*, but because he alone extends the prohibition to include corpse-like *tumah*, and the Mishnah's phrasing indicates it is teaching that point as well.

Tosafot thus clarifies: no one disputes that *tikroves avodat kochavim* is *assur behana'ah*. The attribution to Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira addresses either the non-classic nature of the offering in this case, or the Mishnah's additional teaching about *tumah* — or both.

## Non-Jew Festival Travel

### To Festival Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוֹלְכִין לַתַּרְפּוּת — אֲסוּרִין לָשֵׂאת וְלָתֵת עִמָּהֶם. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גּוֹי הַהוֹלֵךְ לַתַּרְפּוּת, בַּהֲלִיכָה — אָסוּר, דְּאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֵי קַמֵּי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, בַּחֲזָרָה — מוּתָּר, מַאי דַהֲוָה הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **those going to a festival of idolatry,** it is **prohibited to engage with them in business.** The Gemara notes that **Shmuel says:** In the case of **a gentile who goes to a festival of idolatry,** if he is **on** his **way to** the festival **it is prohibited** to engage in business with him, **as he** subsequently **goes and** offers **thanks before** the object of **idol worship. Upon** his **return it is permitted,** as **what was, was,** i.e., he has already finished his worship, and refraining from engaging in business with the gentile at this stage will accomplish nothing.

The Mishnah introduces the case of *haholechin letarpot* — those going to the festival. *Tarpot* appears to be a contemptuous wordplay, perhaps on *tinofet* (filth) or *beit hartofet*, suggesting the pagan festival. A non-Jew traveling to such a festival: *assurin laset ulatet imahen* — one may not do business with him on his way there.

### Worship Benefit

The reason, as Shmuel explains: *bahalicha assur de'azlu modei kammai avodat kochavim* — because he is going to prostrate himself before and honor his idols. If you sell him goods or make a transaction with him, the money or merchandise will end up being used in, or contributing to, his act of idolatrous worship. This maps directly onto the concern from the first chapter of the tractate regarding business dealings in the days before a pagan festival: the worry is that the non-Jew's benefit or joy from the transaction will translate into enhanced participation in the idol worship.

### Return Permission

*Bachazara mutar* — on his way back, however, one may do business with him. The reasoning: *mai de'hava hava* — what was, was. He has already worshipped; no future transaction will contribute to or enhance the act that has already concluded. The business dealings on the return journey cannot be said to support idol worship that is already complete and past.

## Jew Festival Travel

### To Festival Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשְׂרָאֵל הַהוֹלֵךְ לַתַּרְפּוּת — בַּהֲלִיכָה מוּתָּר, דִּלְמָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ וְלָא אָזֵיל; בַּחֲזָרָה אָסוּר, כֵּיוָן דַּאֲבִיק בַּהּ, מִהְדָּר הָדַר אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, with regard to **a Jew who goes to a festival of idolatry,** if he is **on** his **way to** the festival **it is permitted** to engage in business with him, as **perhaps he will retract from** his plan **and will not go. Upon** his **return, it is prohibited, since he will cleave to it** and **go** to worship idolatry **once again.**

The rules are reversed for a Jew who is traveling toward a pagan festival. *Bahalicha mutar* — one may do business with him on his way there. The reason: *dilma hadar bei velo azal* — perhaps he will have second thoughts and turn back. We extend to the Jew the benefit of the doubt that he may yet have a change of heart, a *hirhur shel teshuvah*, and not participate in the idolatrous worship at all. Since there remains a real possibility that he will not actually engage in worship, his journey toward the festival does not yet render transactions with him problematic.

### Return Prohibition

*Bachazara assur* — on his return, however, one may not do business with him. *Keivan de'azik ba* — since he actually went, and presumably attended the festival, we now assume he worshipped. And unlike a non-Jew who practices idolatry as a matter of unremarkable routine, a Jew who has actively sought out pagan worship is now presumed to be genuinely devoted to it. One must fear that future transactions with him will in some way feed into continued idolatrous practice — *mehadur hadur azil* — he will return again to the festival.

### Habitual Worshiper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל הַהוֹלֵךְ לַתַּרְפּוּת, בֵּין בַּהֲלִיכָה בֵּין בַּחֲזָרָה — אָסוּר! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד, דְּוַדַּאי אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that with regard to **a Jew who goes to a festival of idolatry, whether** he is **on the way** there **or returning** from it, it is **prohibited** to engage in business with him? The Gemara answers that **Rav Ashi said: When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is **in** reference to **an apostate Jew, who** will **certainly go** to the festival, and will certainly not change his mind.

A Baraita challenges this, ruling that for a Jew, one may not do business either on the way there or on the return. *Amar Rav Ashi:* that Baraita refers to a *Yisrael mumar*, a Jew who is a confirmed and habitual idolater — *devai azil* — one who habitually goes to these festivals. For such a person, the going and the coming back are equally suspect: going will certainly culminate in worship, and returning signals that he will go again. The distinction between the going and the return applies only to a Jew who is not known to be habitual; for a confirmed idolater, all dealings are forbidden in both directions, just as with a non-Jew on the way to the festival.

## Market Day Fair Rules

### Non-Jew Fair Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גּוֹי הַהוֹלֵךְ לְיָרִיד, בֵּין בַּהֲלִיכָה בֵּין בַּחֲזָרָה — מוּתָּר. יִשְׂרָאֵל הַהוֹלֵךְ לְיָרִיד, בַּהֲלִיכָה — מוּתָּר, בַּחֲזָרָה — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **a gentile who goes to a fair,** where goods and idols are bought and sold, **whether** he is **on the way** there **or returning** from it, it is **permitted** to engage in business with him. With regard to **a Jew who goes to a fair,** when he is **on the way** there, it is **permitted** to engage in business with him, but if he is **returning** from it, it is **prohibited.**

The Gemara then teaches: *oved kochavim holech leyarid* — a non-Jew going to a market fair (*yarid*). *Bein behalicha bein bachazara mutar* — one may do business with him both going and returning. The market fair, unlike the festival, is not a site of idolatrous worship. There is no prayer, no prostration, no libation. Accordingly, transactions in connection with a market fair are free of the concerns that govern festival dealings.

### Jew Fair Rules

However, *Yisrael holech leyarid* — a Jew going to a market fair — the rules shift. *Behalicha mutar* — one may do business with him going, since the fair is not a place of worship and there is no reason yet to suspect any idolatrous activity. *Bechazara assur* — but on his return, one may not do business with him.

### Contaminated Money Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא יִשְׂרָאֵל דְּבַחֲזָרָה אָסוּר? דְּאָמְרִי: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה זַבֵּין, דְּמֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אִיכָּא בַּהֲדֵיהּ. גּוֹי נָמֵי נֵימָא: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה זַבֵּין, דְּמֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אִיכָּא בַּהֲדֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **a Jew that** it is **prohibited** to engage in business with him when he is **returning?** The Gemara answers that this is in accordance with that **which** the Sages **say: He sold** objects of **idol worship,** and therefore **money** gained from **idol worship is in his possession.** Since the money in his possession is assumed to have been gained through objects of idolatry, it is prohibited to engage in business with the Jew. The Gemara challenges: But with regard to **a gentile, also, let us say: He sold** objects of **idol worship,** and therefore **money** gained from **idol worship is in his possession.** Why is it prohibited to do business only with the Jew?

The reason: *de'amar avodas kochavim zavin* — we must suspect that he sold idols. In a pagan market fair, the trade in small idols and cult objects is a natural part of commerce. A Jew attending such a fair is suspected of having dealt in these items — perhaps buying from one non-Jew and reselling to another, perhaps acting as a middleman. Even if he did not manufacture them himself, any money in his possession after the fair may be *dmei avodat kochavim* — proceeds from the sale of idols — and one may not derive benefit from such money.

### Benefit of Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גּוֹי, אָמְרִינַן: גְּלִימָא זַבֵּין, חַמְרָא זַבֵּין; יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי נֵימָא: אֵימוֹר גְּלִימָא זַבֵּין, חַמְרָא זַבֵּין! אִי אִיתָא דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ הָכָא, הֲוָה מְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather,** with regard to **a gentile, we say: He sold clothing** or **he sold wine,** but not idols. The Gemara questions this: Then with regard to **a Jew, also, let us say:** One should **say** that **he sold clothing** or **he sold wine,** but not idols. The Gemara explains: **If he had** only wine or clothing to sell, **he would have sold** them **here** instead of going to the fair. The fact that he exerted himself to travel to a fair of gentiles indicates that he intended to sell idols.

But one may ask: a non-Jew returning from the same fair might equally have sold idols. Why do we not apply the same suspicion to him? *Oved kochavim amrinan glima zavin, chamra zavin* — for the non-Jew, we say he sold ordinary merchandise: a cloak, some wine. We extend him the presumption that he traded in legitimate goods.

Why is no such presumption extended to the Jew? *Ei is lei, dehacha hava mazvin lei* — if the Jew had ordinary merchandise to sell, he would have sold it within the Jewish community. The very fact that he made the effort to go to a non-Jewish market fair raises the suspicion that he was selling something he could not sell among Jews — and the most obvious candidate is cult objects, idols, and the like. Therefore, when a Jew returns from such a fair, we presume that some portion of his money may be proceeds from idol sales, and dealings with him are accordingly restricted.
