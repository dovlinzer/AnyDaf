# Ketubot 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 50 Shekel Fine Status

### Knas Identity After Court

The sugya before us concludes a sustained discussion that properly belongs to tractate *Shavuot*, yet bears directly on our *mishnah* in Ketubot. The underlying question is whether the fifty *shekel* fine paid in cases of rape retains its identity as *knas* even after *ha'amadah be-din* — that is, after court proceedings have been initiated — or whether *ha'amadah be-din* converts it into ordinary *mamon*. The practical stakes are considerable: if it remains *knas*, the defendant cannot be obligated through his own admission, it cannot be bequeathed to the father's sons if the father dies before collection, and there is no liability for a *korban shavuah* if one falsely denied it under oath.

### Rabbi Shimon Exception

*Rabbi Shimon*'s position, as it emerges both from our *mishnah* and from the parallel *mishnah* in *Shavuot*, is that the fifty *shekel* fine retains its identity as *knas* throughout — even after *ha'amadah be-din*. This is the source of his ruling in our *mishnah* that if the father dies before collecting the fine, it passes not to the brothers but to the daughter herself. Were it *mamon* once *ha'amadah be-din* occurred, it would pass to the sons like any other monetary debt.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava, however, maintains that *ha'amadah be-din* does in fact convert *knas* into *mamon* — with one notable exception. By the law of *korban shavuah*, even after the conversion, a special verse — *ve-kichesh* — teaches that if the obligation began its life as *knas*, no *korban* is ever incurred for a false denial, even once it has become *mamon*. So for *Rabbi Shimon*, the fifty *shekel* fine is categorically different: it remains *knas* across the board. For the *rabbanan* and for Rava's reading of *Rabbi Shimon*, only the *korban shavuah* exemption is categorically preserved, while the *mamon* conversion is otherwise standard.

The *gemara* arrives at this picture by parsing a detail: if *Rabbi Shimon* holds the fifty *shekel* is fundamentally *knas* in all respects, why does the *mishnah* in *Shavuot* need to cite the verse *ve-kichesh* to exempt it from *korban shavuah*? The verse *ve-natan* — which grounds the daughter's right to collect upon the father's death — should have sufficed to establish the *knas* identity of the fifty *shekel* for all purposes, including exemption from *korban*. The *gemara* answers that the verse *ve-kichesh* is needed for a specific and unusual scenario.

### Bogeret Exception

The scenario is: *kegon she-amdah be-din u-vagrah* — a case in which the girl first underwent *ha'amadah be-din* and then became a *bogeret*, an emancipated adult. Once she matures, the obligation is no longer directed to the father under the verse *ve-natan le-avi ha-na'arah* but shifts to her. This combination of *ha'amadah be-din* and her subsequent maturation together converts the fifty *shekel* into *mamon*, even according to *Rabbi Shimon*. The father may then inherit it back from the daughter — but he does so as *mamon*, not *knas*. In this scenario, then, even *Rabbi Shimon* concedes the conversion.

The *gemara* therefore explains: *de-hasam ki ka-yaris aviha mina didah ka-yaris* — when the father inherits it, he inherits it as her property. Why, then, is there still no *korban shavuah*? Here the verse *ve-kichesh* is essential: it teaches that even when the obligation has been fully converted into *mamon* in every other respect, if it originated as *knas*, the *korban shavuah* exemption persists permanently.

### Knas Origin Rule

The *gemara* presses further: if even *Rabbi Shimon* agrees this is now *mamon*, how can we call it *knas* and thereby exempt it from *korban shavuah*? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this with the principle: *yatzu eilu she-ikaran knas* — "excluded are those whose very origin is *knas*." It is not necessary that the obligation currently retain its *knas* character; it is sufficient that it began as *knas*. The *korban shavuah* exemption, in *Rabbi Shimon*'s view, is categorical and permanent, anchored in the origin of the obligation rather than in its present legal status.

To summarize the layered structure: *Rabbi Shimon* holds (a) that generally the fifty *shekel* remains *knas* even after *ha'amadah be-din*, unlike other fines; (b) that in the specific *bogeret* scenario it does become *mamon* even for *Rabbi Shimon*; and (c) that in all cases where the obligation originated as *knas*, *ve-kichesh* permanently exempts it from *korban shavuah*, whether or not it has since been converted into *mamon*.

## Father's Implicit Demand

### Implicit Demand Debate

The *gemara* now returns to the surface-level dispute between *Rabbi Shimon* and the *rabbanan*. A *beraita* cites *Rabbi Shimon* as exempting from *korban shavuah* in the case where the father demands payment for the rape of his daughter, apparently because the demand is for *knas*, and one who admits to *knas* is exempt — *modeh be-knas patur*. The *gemara* notes that this implies it is still genuinely *knas* rather than merely a case where the original *knas* survives by force of verse. If *ha'amadah be-din* had converted it to *mamon*, the exemption would need to be explained differently — not simply as "it is *knas*."

The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that *Rabbi Shimon* is speaking *le-divreichem de-rabbanan* — framing his argument in terms his opponents will accept. His reasoning runs as follows: according to you, *rabbanan*, who hold that once it becomes *mamon* one incurs *korban shavuah*, you should at least concede that where there has been no *ha'amadah be-din*, it is still purely *knas*, and *modeh be-knas patur* — so there should be no *korban shavuah* even on your own terms. As for Rav's own position, *ve-kichesh* tells him there is never a *korban shavuah* on anything that began as *knas*, regardless.

### Torah Exemption

The top of our *daf* — 43a — introduces the *rabbanan*'s response and the analytical underpinning of the dispute. The *gemara* asks: *be-mai kamiplagei* — what exactly is the interpretive disagreement? When a father says to someone "you raped my daughter, pay me," what is he implicitly demanding — the fixed fifty *shekel knas*, or the indeterminate *boshet* and *pagam*, the shame payment and the diminishment in her value? Rav Pappa frames the dispute in these terms.

### Fixed vs. Amorphous

*Rabbi Shimon* holds: *lo shavik inish midi de-kaitz ve-tava midi de-lo kaitz* — a person does not abandon a claim with a fixed, determinate amount in order to demand something amorphous and indeterminate. The fifty *shekel* is concrete; *boshet* and *pagam* are subject to court assessment and could be anything. Therefore, the father is presumed to be demanding the *knas*.

### Escape vs. No Escape

The *rabbanan* hold the opposite: *lo shavik inish midi de-chi modi bei lo miftar* — a person does not abandon a claim from which the defendant cannot escape by confessing. If the demand is for *mamon*, an admission by the defendant obligates him to pay immediately, with no exit. By contrast, if the demand is for *knas*, an admission paradoxically exempts the defendant — *modeh be-knas patur* — and the father gains nothing from forcing a confession. The *rabbanan* therefore reason that the father is implicitly demanding *boshet* and *pagam*, not the *knas*, because that is the demand that can be enforced through admission.

This is ultimately an argument about human psychology and litigation strategy rather than a principled legal distinction. Interestingly, the *gemara* seems to assume these are mutually exclusive demands — it does not consider why the father would not be presumed to be demanding both simultaneously.

What remains clear as the halachic bottom line for our *mishnah* is *Rabbi Shimon*'s position that the fifty *shekel* retains its *knas* identity even after *ha'amadah be-din*, and therefore if the father dies before it is collected, the daughter — not the brothers — is entitled to collect it. That is the *chidush* of *Rabbi Shimon* that is directly relevant to our discussion in Ketubot.

## Daughter's Earnings Righ…

### Earnings Question

With the foregoing *sugya* concluded, the *gemara* transitions to a new but related topic raised by Rabbi Avina to Rav Sheshet: *bat ha-nizonet min ha-achin* — a daughter who is being supported by the brothers from the estate after her father's death. What are the brothers' rights, if any, to her *maaseh yadayim*, her earnings?

Some background is necessary. When a man dies, his estate passes to his sons. The daughters do not inherit. To protect the daughters, a standard *tenai ketubah* — indeed, one that applies even if not explicitly written — requires the estate to support any daughters until they marry. This is the case of *bat ha-nizonet min ha-achin*: the brothers have no discretion; the conditions of the *ketubah* bind the estate to provide for her.

The question is whether the estate, in exchange for this support, acquires rights to the daughter's earnings. On the surface, this resembles the *ketubot* arrangement between husband and wife, where the husband's support obligation corresponds reciprocally to his right to his wife's earnings — a rabbinic exchange of *maaseh yadayim* for *mezonot*. One might extend the same logic here: since the estate is providing her sustenance, perhaps the estate should receive her earnings.

### Reciprocity Principle

The difficulty is that the husband-wife exchange is explicitly a rabbinic reciprocal institution, created precisely because neither spouse has an entirely unilateral legal claim over the other. The father's relationship to his daughter's earnings, by contrast, is unilateral and biblical. The *gemara* grounds this in the fact that a father may sell his daughter as an *amah ivriyah* — a Hebrew maidservant — which is essentially a transfer of the right to her labor and earnings. This right is not conditional on his supporting her; it is a status right rooted in his paternal authority.

This is reinforced by an important distinction: *hatam mididei mizna* — in the father's case, he feeds the daughter from his own pocket and at his own initiative, since the Torah does not actually obligate him to support her. The *rabbanan* instituted that he is entitled to her *maaseh yadayim* in part to avoid the resentment (*eivah*) that would arise if he were feeding her voluntarily while she pocketed everything she earned or found. By contrast, the brothers are not feeding her from their own earnings; they are disbursing funds from the inherited estate. Moreover, they have no choice — the estate is legally obligated to support her as a condition of the *ketubah*. The volitional, unilateral character of the father's support is entirely absent.

### Widow vs. Daughter

Rav Sheshet attempted to answer the question from a *mishnah* regarding the *almana* — the widow. The *mishnah* states that a widow supported by the estate's heirs (*nizonet min nichsei yetomim*) surrenders her earnings to the estate in exchange. By analogy, the daughter supported by the estate should similarly surrender her earnings.

The *gemara* rejects the comparison: *mi dami?* The situations are not parallel, because the deceased husband's concern for his widow and his concern for his daughter are not equivalent — at least not along every axis.

### Father's Priorities

The *gemara* distinguishes between two dimensions: *ziluta* (shame and degradation) and *harvacha* (profit and financial gain). The principle it advances is: *le-inyan ziluta*, when it comes to avoiding degradation, the father demonstrated greater concern for his widow than for his daughter — as evidenced by the rule in *Bava Batra* that in a small estate (*nichsim mu'atim*), if there is not enough to support both the widow and the daughters, the widow takes precedence and the daughters must go begging. Yet *le-inyan harvacha*, when it comes to retaining financial upside, the father is more concerned that his daughter pocket her earnings than that his wife do so.

One might initially find this distinction arbitrary — the *gemara* could seemingly have argued the reverse. But one compelling explanation offered in discussion is that the father has an interest in his daughter accumulating a dowry in order to marry her off advantageously. He would therefore want her to keep whatever she earns. His wife's financial accumulation beyond what is needed for her maintenance is less of a priority in his thinking.

The *gemara* challenges this distinction by citing Rabbi Abba quoting Rabbi Yosei: *asu almana etzel ha-bat ke-vat etzel ha-achin* — they treated the widow vis-à-vis the daughter as the daughter vis-à-vis the brothers. This means the widow takes precedence over the daughter in a small estate, just as the daughter takes precedence over the brothers. Doesn't this prove the husband was more concerned with his wife than with his daughter? The *gemara* responds that this holds true only for the *ziluta* dimension — it is more degrading for a widow to beg than for a daughter. In terms of *harvacha*, the opposite priorities apply, and the father's posthumous concern for his daughter's financial gain exceeds his concern for his wife's.

## Mishna Implication

### Earnings During Life

The *gemara* now turns to our *mishnah* itself to attempt a proof about the daughter's entitlement to her own earnings. The *mishnah* states: *maaseh yadeha u-metziatah* — her earnings and her found objects — *af al pi she-lo gavtah*, even if she has not yet collected them, once earned during the father's lifetime, pass to the brothers upon the father's death. The *gemara* infers from this framing: *ta'ama de-ve-chayei ha-av* — the reason it goes to the brothers is specifically that she earned it while the father was alive; but *ha le-achar mitat ha-av le-atzmah* — had she earned it after the father's death, she would keep it herself. The *mishnah* thus implies strongly that earnings after the father's death remain hers.

### All or Excess?

The *gemara* immediately tests this inference: *mai lav bi-mezonot* — is this not speaking even in a case where the estate is supporting her? The attempted response is to say *lo*, perhaps the *mishnah* refers only to a case where she is *not* being supported by the estate — and if she is unsupported, of course she keeps her earnings, so nothing has been proved about the case of *bat ha-nizonet min ha-achin*.

The *gemara* pushes back: if it were referring to the unsupported case, the *mishnah*'s implication would be obvious and thus no *chiddush* at all. Even according to the opinion that a master may say to a non-Jewish slave *aseh imi ve-eineni zoncha* — work for me without my feeding you — this does not apply to a Jewish slave, regarding whom the Torah writes *imcha*, "with you," implying an obligation of care. Certainly the daughter is no worse than a Jewish slave in this regard. So if the estate is not supporting her, it is trivially obvious she keeps her earnings. The *mishnah* must therefore be speaking about the supported case and still teaching she keeps her earnings — which would be the *chiddush*.

### Hadafah Theory

Rava bar Ula offers a way to preserve the *gemara*'s resistance. Even granting that the *mishnah* concerns a supported daughter, perhaps she does not keep *all* her earnings. Perhaps she only keeps the *hadafah* — the additional earnings beyond the cost of her upkeep. On this reading: if her weekly food and lodging costs one hundred units and she earns one hundred and fifty, she keeps only fifty, using the first hundred to repay the estate for her support. The *mishnah*'s implication that she keeps earnings after the father's death would then prove only entitlement to the *hadafah*, not to the full amount.

## Earnings & Found Objects

### Found Object Problem

Rav Yosef dismisses this proposal and argues that the *mishnah* itself contains a stronger proof — one that cannot be deflected by the *hadafah* reading. The *mishnah* packages earnings together with *metziatah* — found objects. When the *mishnah* says that earnings not yet collected (*af al pi she-lo gavtah*) become debts to the father that pass to the brothers upon his death, it makes the same statement about found objects not yet collected. But this raises an obvious difficulty: *metziatah me-man gavya?* How does one "collect" a found object? A found object, the moment it is acquired, is in the finder's possession. There is no prior stage at which it is "owed" to her and then collected later. The formulation makes no sense as applied to found objects in isolation.

### Earnings=Found Objects

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ella lav hachi ka'amar — maaseh yadeha ke-metziatah* — the *mishnah* is not making two parallel statements; it is equating earnings to found objects. Just as found objects: *mah metziatah be-chayei av le-av, le-achar mitat av le-atzmah* — found objects during the father's lifetime go to the father, but after the father's death they go to her — so too earnings: during the father's lifetime they go to him, but after his death they go to her. And crucially, when it comes to found objects, everyone agrees she keeps them entirely, not merely the *hadafah*. By packaging earnings together with found objects, the *mishnah* teaches that she keeps her earnings entirely as well, even when supported by the estate.

This is Rav Yosef's decisive argument: the *hadafah* theory cannot survive the equation with found objects, because no one ever suggested the daughter owes the estate for found objects found after the father's death. The full amount is hers.

### Volitional Support

Why is it taken for granted that she keeps found objects after the father's death? Rashi explains: *de-metziatah peshita lan de-le-atzmah* — even during the father's lifetime, the father's entitlement to his daughter's found objects was contingent on his actually supporting her — *semucha al shulchano*, eating at his table. The *gemara* in Bava Metzia establishes this. And why was it instituted that the father gets her found objects in the first place? Because of *eivah* — resentment. Since the father is not *legally* obligated to support his daughter yet is doing so volitionally, we worried that he would stop feeding her if she pocketed everything she found. So we gave him the found objects as an incentive.

### Mandatory Obligation

Once the father is dead, however, the entire rationale evaporates. The estate is *obligated* to support the daughter — it is a condition of the *ketubah*, not a voluntary act. There is no volitional benevolence to protect and no *eivah* to worry about. Therefore she unambiguously keeps her found objects. And since the *mishnah* equates earnings to found objects, the conclusion extends equally: she keeps all her earnings after the father's death, even the portion that would cover the cost of her upkeep.

## Brothers Don't Inherit

### Rav Yehuda's Ruling

The *gemara* reinforces this conclusion with a direct *amoraic* statement. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *bat ha-nizonet min ha-achin maaseh yadeha le-atzmah* — a daughter supported by the brothers keeps her earnings. The brothers do not inherit the father's entitlement to her *maaseh yadayim*.

### Scripture Proof

Rav Kahana asks: what is the reason? The *gemara* cites the verse in the context of non-Jewish slaves: *ve-hitnachaltem otam li-vneichem achareichem* — "you shall bequeath them to your sons after you." *Otam li-vneichem* — your slaves you bequeath to your sons; *ve-lo benoteichem li-vneichem* — but not your daughters to your sons. The verse teaches that the paternal rights a father held over his daughter's labor and earnings are not heritable. Just as one bequeaths non-Jewish slaves to one's children — because the master's rights over a slave are a transferable ownership — by contrast, the father's rights in his daughter are not of that nature and do not pass to the sons.

### Rabba Objects

Rabba objects: perhaps the verse is speaking only about extraordinary payments — *fitzuyei ha-bat, kinsatah ve-chavalatah* — the damages, fines, and injury payments the father would collect on his daughter's behalf. These are by their nature more intrinsically the daughter's, since they compensate for wrongs done to her person, and they are less common. Perhaps it is specifically these that do not pass to the sons, while ordinary *maaseh yadayim* — regular earnings — does pass, since the father's biblical entitlement to earnings was more like a property right that should be inheritable.

Rabbi Chanina indeed reads the verse this way: *be-fitzuyei ha-bat u-knasatah ve-chavalatah ha-katuv medaber* — the verse is specifically speaking about injury, seduction, and fine payments, not ordinary earnings.

### Injury Distinction

The *gemara* questions this reading by noting that *chavalos* — injuries — present their own complication. Even during the father's lifetime, injury payments (*tza'ara de-gufa*) are not simply pocketed by the father; they are held in a form of escrow for the daughter, since it is her body that was harmed. So the verse cannot be referring to injury payments as something the father would have claimed and now cannot bequeath, since he never had full claim to them in the first place.

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina resolves this: *she-patza'ah be-faneha* — the reference is to a blow to her face, a non-permanent injury. The distinction between permanent and non-permanent injury is what determines who collects. For a permanent injury — something that will diminish her for life, well beyond her period as a *ketanah* and *na'arah* — the payment is for the daughter, not the father, because it is fundamentally her loss. For a non-permanent injury, or one that affects only the period during which the father could have sold her or married her off, the father absorbs the loss and collects the payment.

Tosafot develops this logic further: an injury that heals before the daughter reaches adulthood — before she becomes a *bogeret* — is the father's loss, because it diminished the value of his asset during the period he controlled it. An injury that persists beyond that point is her loss, and she collects accordingly.

The distinction between *Rabbi Shimon* and the *rabbanan* as to what the verse excludes — whether regular earnings or only fines and injury payments — thus remains analytically live. But the *gemara* has already demonstrated from the *mishnah* and from the Rav Yehuda/Rav tradition that ordinary earnings also remain hers.

### Final Halacha

The *gemara* closes with competing traditions. Ami bar Maimra told Rav Ashi that in Nehardea the tradition was *hilchesa ke-vateih de-Rav Sheshet* — we rule like Rav Sheshet, meaning the daughter's earnings go to the estate. Rav Ashi himself ruled *hilchesa ke-vateih de-Rav* — we rule like Rav: the daughter keeps her earnings; the brothers do not inherit the father's rights over them. And this is the accepted *halacha*: *ve-hilchesa ke-vateih de-Rav*.

## Ketubah Rights (Betr (II)

### Eirusin Divorce

The *mishnah* now turns to the question of *ketubah* collection rights when a father betrothes his daughter and the marriage subsequently dissolves. The relevant legal background is that after *eirusin* but before *nissu'in*, the daughter remains under her father's authority. If the *eirusin*-stage marriage ends — whether through divorce or the death of the husband — she reverts entirely to paternal control.

The first case: *ham'ares et bito ve-gerashah* — the father betrothes his daughter and she is then divorced. The question is who collects the *ketubah*. Since she never reached *nissu'in* and therefore never left her father's control, the *ketubah* collection right belongs to the father. This ruling assumes, incidentally, that the *ketubah* obligation attaches already at *eirusin*, a position that is itself debated.

### Eirusin Widowed

The same result obtains in the parallel case: *iresah ve-nit'almanah* — the father betrothed her and the husband died. Since she was still an *arusah* and had not yet undergone *nissu'in*, she remained under the father's authority. Therefore the father again collects the *ketubah*.

### Nissu'in Collection

The scenario shifts dramatically once *nissu'in* takes place. *Hisi'ah ve-gerashah* — the marriage was consummated and she was then divorced; *hisi'ah ve-nit'almanah* — consummated and she became widowed. In both cases: *kesuvatah shelah* — she collects her own *ketubah*. Indeed she may have a *ketubah* from two marriages, and she collects both. Once *nissu'in* occurs, the father loses all rights over her person and her monetary entitlements. She is in her own domain.

---

## Ketubah Rights (Betr (II)

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

*Rabbi Yehuda* introduces a refinement: *harishonah shel av* — even after *nissu'in*, the *ketubah* from the first marriage, the one the father initiated through *kidushei ketanah*, belongs to the father. His reasoning appears to be that since the father originated the *eirusin*, the financial obligations that arose from that *eirusin* remain directed to him even after the *nissu'in* converts her status.

### Gemara Rejects Yehuda

The *rabbanan* reject *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position categorically: *mi-she-hisi'ah ein av reshut bah* — once *nissu'in* has taken place, the father has no authority over her whatsoever, and any claim to her *ketubah* that might have existed before is extinguished. *Nissu'in* functions as a total legal break, severing all paternal rights regardless of whether the father originally arranged the match.

## Deaths vs. Divorces

### Two Deaths Avoidance

Before proceeding to the *gemara*'s analysis of the *mishnah*'s rulings, the *gemara* pauses to note a structural choice in how the *mishnah* composed its cases. The *mishnah* gives one scenario of divorce (*gerushah*) and one of widowhood (*almanah*) to illustrate a progression of two marriages. One might ask: why not give both scenarios as deaths — why not say she was widowed twice? Why the asymmetry of one divorce and one death?

### Husband Deaths Debate

The *gemara* answers: *aval nit'almanah trei zimnei lo chazya le-inasba* — if two husbands had died on her, she would no longer be fit to remarry. The *gemara* notes that there is an unresolved debate about a woman whose husbands keep dying: at what point does a pattern of deaths create a *chazakah* — a legal presumption — that she is in some way the cause, such that a third husband should not take her? Is it after two deaths, or after three?

### Rabbi's Two-Time Rule

The *gemara* notes *agav orcha* — incidentally, in passing — *kasavar tana ke-Rabbi* — our *mishnah* is following the position of *Rabbi* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi). *Rabbi* holds *de-amar be-trei zimnei havei chazakah* — a presumption is established after two occurrences. Therefore, once two husbands have died on a woman, the presumption already applies and she may not remarry. By constructing its case as one divorce and one death rather than two deaths, the *mishnah* sidesteps this concern entirely, implicitly aligning itself with *Rabbi*'s two-occurrence rule.
