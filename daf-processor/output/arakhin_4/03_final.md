# Arakhin 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohanim in Megillah

### Hakol Overview

The fourth *daf* of *Arakhin* continues the *gemara*'s extended treatment of the word *hakol* — "everyone" — and its companion phrase *kohanim levi'im v'yisrael*. Before returning to the primary themes of the *mishnah*, the *gemara* surveys a series of parallel *mishnayot* and *beraitot* that employ the same formulation, examining in each case why it was necessary to specify that *kohanim*, *levi'im*, and *yisraelim* are all included.

### Megillah Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִים בְּמִקְרָא מְגִילָּה, כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא לִיבַטֵּיל עֲבוֹדָתָם, וְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּהֲנִים בַּעֲבוֹדָתָם, וּלְוִיִּם בְּדוּכָנָן, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַעֲמָדָן — מְבַטְּלִין עֲבוֹדָתָם וּבָאִין לִשְׁמוֹעַ מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Everyone is obligated in the reading of the Megilla** including **priests, Levites, and Israelites.** The Gemara asks as before: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary to** teach that they **cancel their service** in the Temple and come to hear the reading of the Megilla, **and** this is **in accordance** with **that** which **Rav Yehuda** says that **Shmuel says. As Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: The priests at their** Temple **service, and the Levites on their platform** in the Temple where they sang the daily psalm, **and the Israelites at their** non-priestly **watches** for the offerings of the community, all **cancel their service and come to hear the reading of the Megilla.**

Beginning two dots from the top of the *daf*, the *gemara* cites: *hakol chayavin bemikra megilla* — everyone is obligated in the reading of the *megilla* — *kohanim levi'im v'yisraelim*. The *gemara* immediately objects: *peshita* — is this not obvious? It answers: *lo, tzricha levatel avodatam* — no, it is necessary to teach that even the *kohanim* would interrupt their *avoda* in the *Beit HaMikdash* in order to hear the *megilla* reading. This is consistent with the ruling of *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: *kohanim b'avodatam v'levi'im b'duchanam v'yisrael b'ma'amadam* — even if the *kohanim* are in the midst of the Temple service, the *levi'im* are on their *duchan* singing, and the *yisrael* representatives are present at their *ma'amad* (the rotation of Israelites who attended the communal *korbanot* on behalf of the people) — nevertheless, *mevatlin avodatan u'va'in lishmoa mikra megilla*: they stop the *avoda* and come to hear the *megilla*.

### Pirsumei Nisa

This is a striking formulation, particularly given that *Rav Yehuda* spoke at a time when the *Beit HaMikdash* no longer stood, making the description entirely theoretical. Why paint so graphic a picture? Two considerations suggest themselves. First, to underscore the centrality of *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle — which is especially characteristic of *megilla* and *Chanukah*, as reflected in the *beracha* of *she'asa nissim l'avoteinu*. Unlike *Chanukah*, which is observed privately at night in one's home, *mikra megilla* is a markedly public *mitzvah*, performed in community and, in the primary sense recognized by the *gemara* and *mishnah*, during the day. The point, then, may be to emphasize that because *pirsumei nisa* demands collective public participation, everything else — even the *avoda* in the *Beit HaMikdash* — must yield.

A second consideration is historical framing. The miracle of the *megilla* occurred precisely during the period when the Second *Beit HaMikdash* was being rebuilt. One might thus have been tempted to frame the *nisa* of *megilla* specifically within the context of the Temple's restoration — reasoning that the Temple service, which the miracle enabled, should not be displaced by its commemoration. The *gemara* counters this framing dramatically: the very *avoda* in the *Beit HaMikdash* stands down for *mikra megilla*. Of course, the *nisa* of the *megilla* transcends any narrow connection to the Temple — it was a moment when the Jewish people as a whole, not merely their religion, faced annihilation — and therefore its commemoration cannot be circumscribed by Temple imagery alone.

### Zimun Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִים בְּזִימּוּן, כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָאָכְלִי קׇדָשִׁים. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אוֹתָם אֲשֶׁר כֻּפַּר בָּהֶם״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא כַּפָּרָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Everyone is obligated to form a *zimmun*** and recite Grace after Meals, including **priests, Levites, and Israelites.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary to** teach this *halakha* in a case **where** the priests **partake of sacrificial** meat. It might **enter your mind to say** that since **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And they shall eat those things with which atonement was made"** (Exodus 29:33), indicating that the owners of those offerings thereby achieve atonement, **and** therefore **this** consumption **is** an obligatory ritual act to bring about **atonement,** consequently, it is not considered a social meal requiring one to form a *zimmun*.

The *gemara* then turns to: *hakol chayavin b'zimmun* — everyone is obligated in *zimmun* — *kohanim levi'im v'yisraelim*. Again the *gemara* asks *peshita*, and again it answers: *lo, tzricha* — the novel case is where *kohanim* are eating *kodashim*, their portions of the sacrifices. Let us first consider the case of three *kohanim* eating *korbanot* together — with bread, as required for *zimmun* — before introducing the question of combining with *yisraelim*.

### Kohanim Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, ״וְאָכַלְתָּ וְשָׂבָעְתָּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא אִיתַנְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that since **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And you shall eat, and be satisfied,** and bless the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 8:10), the obligation to recite Grace after Meals is contingent upon eating and being satisfied. Since **there is** eating and satisfaction in the case of priests partaking of the sacrificial meat, they are obligated to form a *zimmun*, despite the fact that the purpose of the meal is to bring about atonement.

One might have thought that *kohanim* eating *korbanot* would not make a *zimmun*. The *gemara* states: *salka da'atakh amina* — I might have thought — *akhlu otam asher kuppar bahem amar Rachmana*: the Torah teaches that when the *kohanim* eat the sacrificial portions, this achieves atonement for the *bnei yisrael* who brought the offerings. From this one might reason that the *kohanim*'s eating is not primarily a personal act of physical sustenance — it is, as it were, a ritual act, a consumption analogous to the altar's consumption, a representation of divine or sacrificial acceptance. If so, perhaps it is not appropriate to frame their eating in the terms that ground *birkat hamazon* — gratitude for one's own physical nourishment. Perhaps the *kohanim* eating *korbanot* should not even make a *beracha* at all.

To this the *gemara* responds: *v'achalta v'savata amar Rachmana* — the Torah says "you shall eat and be satisfied," and this applies equally here. Even if the conceptual framing of the *kohanim*'s eating is that of a ritual act — the altar consuming, atonement being achieved — the physical reality is that they are nourishing themselves, filling their bellies, deriving genuine benefit. That reality obligates them in *birkat hamazon*.

This raises a genuine question: is the *gemara*'s conclusion reframing how we understand the act of the *kohanim*'s eating — telling us to think of it also as personal sustenance, a form of payment or gift for their service — or is it leaving the ritual framing intact while saying that the derived benefit itself, independent of how we frame the act, creates the obligation? Either way, the point is established: the *kohanim* must recite *birkat hamazon*. And there is a profound dimension to this ruling, as one participant noted: it keeps the *kohanim* anchored in their humanity, preventing them — and the people — from regarding them as purely divine representatives who transcend ordinary human gratitude.

A textual note is worth flagging: the *gemara* speaks of *zimmun* rather than *birkat hamazon* as such, and the *psukim* it invokes seem more naturally to address the personal *beracha* than the communal *zimmun*. *Rabbeinu Gershom*'s text actually reads *beracha* where our text has *zimmun*, suggesting a variant tradition. In any event, the *gemara* transitions immediately to *zimmun* as a *tziruf* — a grouping — in the next clause.

There is also a practical question lurking here: *birkat hamazon* requires bread, and the primary sacrificial portions are meat. The answer may be that the eating of *menachot* — flour offerings that are among the *kohanim*'s portions — is included, though the *gemara* does not spell this out explicitly.

### Tziruf Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מִצְטָרְפִין לְזִימּוּן, כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָאָכְלִי כֹּהֲנִים תְּרוּמָה אוֹ קׇדָשִׁים, וְזָר קָאָכֵיל חוּלִּין. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִי בָּעֵי זָר לְמֵיכַל בַּהֲדֵי כֹּהֵן לָא מָצֵי אָכֵיל, אֵימָא לָא לִיצְטָרֵף, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: נְהִי דְּזָר בַּהֲדֵי כֹּהֵן לָא מָצֵי אָכֵיל, כֹּהֵן בַּהֲדֵי זָר מָצֵי אָכֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Everyone joins a *zimmun*: Priests, Levites, and Israelites.** The Gemara asks again: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary to** teach this *halakha* in a case **where** the **priests are partaking of *teruma*,** the portion of produce designated for priests, **or sacrificial** meat, **and a non-priest is eating non-sacred** food. In such a case, it might **enter your mind to say** that **since if the non-priest wants to eat together with the priest he cannot eat** with him from the *teruma*, as it is forbidden to him, one could **say** that **he cannot join** with the priests for a *zimmun*. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that he can join them, as **although a non-priest cannot eat together with a priest** from his *teruma*, nevertheless, **a priest can eat together with a non-priest** from his food. Consequently, they can join together to form a *zimmun*.

The *gemara* then addresses: *hakol mitztarfin lezimmun* — everyone can combine for *zimmun* — *kohanim levi'im v'yisraelim*. Again, *peshita*. The novel case: *lo tzricha dekachli kohanim teruma*, where the *kohanim* are eating *teruma* or *kodashim* — food a *zar* cannot eat — while the *yisrael* is eating ordinary *chullin*. One might have thought they cannot form a *zimmun* group, since *salka da'atakh amina*: if the *zar* wanted to share the *kohen*'s food, *lo matzi achil* — he cannot eat it. Perhaps they cannot be considered a joint eating. The *gemara* responds: although the *zar* cannot eat with the *kohen*, the *kohen* can eat with the *zar* — there is theoretical mutuality in one direction, and that suffices to create the *tziruf*.

This ruling has immediate practical implications. *Tosafot* raises the case of *milchigs* and *fleishigs*: if one person is eating dairy and another meat, they can still form a *zimmun*, since the dairy eater could wash out his mouth and in principle eat with the meat eater (though some hold the *zimun* leader should be the dairy eater, who is the one with theoretical ability to join). More complex is the case of hard cheese, where one follows the custom of waiting six hours — or someone with halakhic dietary restrictions versus merely physical intolerances such as lactose intolerance or gluten sensitivity. The general principle emerging from our *gemara* is: as long as at least one person can in theory eat with the others, *tziruf* is possible. But where no one can halakhically eat with anyone else, *zimmun* cannot be made. Whether purely physiological barriers — health conditions rather than halakhic prohibitions — have the same effect is a matter discussed in later *halacha*, but it is a serious possibility that they would equally preclude *tziruf*.

## Machatzit Hashekel

### Return to Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מַעֲרִיכִין, כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְבֶן בּוּכְרִי, דִּתְנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הֵעִיד בֶּן בּוּכְרִי בְּיַבְנֶה: כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁשּׁוֹקֵל אֵינוֹ חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Everyone takes** vows of **valuation…priests, Levites, and Israelites.** Isn't that **obvious? Rava said:** This *halakha* **is necessary only according to** the opinion of **ben Bukhri,** who holds that priests are not obligated to contribute a yearly half-shekel to purchase the communal supplies. Therefore, one might have thought that they are not subject to the *halakha* of valuations, as the Gemara will explain. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 1:4) that **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **ben Bukhri testified** before the Sages **in Yavne: Any priest who contributes** the half-**shekel** for communal offerings **is not** considered **a sinner,** despite the fact that he is not obligated to contribute.

With these parallel cases completed, the *gemara* at last returns to our *mishnah*: *hakol maarichin* — everyone can make an *erech* vow — *kohen levi v'yisrael*. The structure has been almost chiastic: beginning with *hakol* in the *mishnah*, traversing all the parallel *hakol* formulations, and now returning to the *mishnah* itself. Why did the *mishnah* need to specify *kohen levi v'yisrael* in the context of *erchin*? *Peshita* — obviously anyone can be *maariach*! *Amar Rava lo nitzrecha ela l'Ben Buchri* — Rava says it is needed only to address the position of *Ben Buchri*.

### Ben Buchri Opinion

The *mishnah* in *Shekalim* (and elsewhere in *shas*) records: *amar Rabbi Yehuda he'id Ben Buchri b'Yavne she-kol Kohen she-shokel eino chotei* — Ben Buchri testified at Yavneh that any *kohen* who contributes the half-*shekel* has not sinned. The implication is that *kohanim* are not obligated to give; if they choose to contribute, they may do so without transgression, but it is optional. This testimony was given at Yavneh, the center of rabbinic reconstruction after the destruction of the Temple, where scholars gathered to record traditions of *Torah she-be'al peh*.

### Yochanan ben Zakkai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: לֹא כֵּן, אֶלָּא כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ שׁוֹקֵל חוֹטֵא, אֶלָּא שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים דּוֹרְשִׁין מִקְרָא זֶה לְעַצְמָן: ״וְכׇל מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה לֹא תֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda added that **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said to** ben Bukhri: **That is not** the case; **rather, any priest who does not contribute his** half-**shekel** is considered **a sinner,** as they are obligated in this mitzva like all other Jews. **But** with regard to **the priests** who do not contribute the half-shekel, in order to excuse themselves from the mitzva they **interpret this verse to their own** advantage: **"And every meal offering of the priest shall be wholly made to smoke; it shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 6:16).

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai responds sharply: *lo chen* — that is not the *halacha*. Rather: *kol Kohen she-eino shokel chotei* — any *kohen* who does *not* contribute is sinning. It is not merely permitted; it is required. Why do *kohanim* think otherwise? *She-ha-Kohanim dorshim mikra zeh l'atzman* — they interpret a verse in their own self-interest: *v'chol minchat Kohen kalil tihyeh lo te'achel* — every flour-offering of a *kohen* must be entirely burned and may not be eaten. From this they reason as follows: the obligation of the half-*shekel* is to become a partner in the communal sacrifices. Among those communal sacrifices are the *omer*, the *shtei ha-lechem* of *Shavuot*, and the *lechem ha-panim* — all *menachot* that are eaten. If *kohanim* were to contribute the half-*shekel* and become part-owners of these communal *menachot*, their *minchat kohen* ownership would require those *menachot* to be burned entirely rather than eaten. And so, they say, *shelanu hem* — they would become ours — and such consecrated items could no longer be eaten. Out of concern for the integrity of the communal *korbanot*, they refrain from contributing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְעוֹמֶר וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁלָּנוּ הֵם, הֵיאַךְ הֵם נֶאֱכָלִין?
>
> **Translation:** Those priests claim as follows: **Since** the ***omer*** offering, the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan, **and the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering of two loaves from the new wheat, brought on the festival of *Shavuot*, **and the shewbread** placed on the Table in the Sanctuary each Shabbat, which are all meal offerings, **are ours,** i.e., if we would contribute half-shekels we would have partial ownership of these communal offerings, as they are purchased with the half-shekels, **how** then **can they be eaten?** They would be regarded as priests' meal offerings, which must be wholly burnt, in accordance with the aforementioned verse.

This is a familiar human dynamic. As Rav Soloveitchik reportedly counseled: a rabbi must be the first to donate at an appeal and must always be present for *minyan* — because it is precisely the communal servant who is most tempted to reason that his service exempts him from communal obligation. The *kohanim* doring *mikra zeh l'atzman* is a paradigmatic example of self-interested exegesis that nonetheless has a surface plausibility.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבֶן בּוּכְרִי נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דִּלְכַתְּחִילָּה לָא מִיחַיְּיבִי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי, כִּי מַיְיתֵי נָמֵי — חוֹטֵא הוּא, דְּקָא מְעַיֵּיל חוּלִּין לָעֲזָרָה! דְּמַיְיתֵי לְהוּ וּמָסַר לַצִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **But according to** the opinion of **ben Bukhri, as well,** why is a priest who contributes a half-shekel not considered a sinner? **Since he is not obligated to bring** the half-shekel ***ab initio*, when he brings** the half-shekel **he is also a sinner, since he is** causing the **bringing** of **a non-sacred** item **into the** Temple **courtyard.** The Gemara answers **that** the priest **brings and transfers** the half-shekel as a consecrated gift **to the community,** so it is considered part of the communal funds.

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai dismisses this reasoning: *minchat kohen* applies only when a *kohen* is the complete owner, not when his ownership is a fractional share in a collective. And beyond that technicality lies a more fundamental principle.

### Tzibbur Concept

*Halacha* generally does not recognize a corporate entity in the modern legal sense — it knows partnerships, not corporations. Yet in a handful of contexts, it does operate with the concept of an abstract communal entity. One is *yorshim* who inherit an estate collectively. The other is the *tzibbur*: even if millions of individuals each contribute a half-*shekel*, the communal sacrifices are not owned by millions of fractional partners. They are owned by the *tzibbur* as a single abstract corporate entity. Therefore, even if *kohanim* contribute, their contribution dissolves into this corporate ownership — it does not create a personal partial ownership in the *kohen* that would redefine the *minchat kohen* status of the communal *menachot*. The *korban* belongs to the *tzibbur*, not to any individual, including *kohanim*.

### Excluding Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״וְכׇל עֶרְכְּךָ יִהְיֶה בְּשֶׁקֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ״, כׇּל דְּאִיתֵיהּ בִּשְׁקָלִים — אִיתֵיהּ בַּעֲרָכִין, וְהָנֵי כֹּהֲנִים, הוֹאִיל וְלֵיתַנְהוּ בִּשְׁקָלִים — לֵיתַנְהוּ בַּעֲרָכִין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the relevance of ben Bukhri's opinion to the question of the priests' status with respect to valuations. According to ben Bukhri, who exempts the priests from contributing the half-shekel, **it might enter your mind to say** that **since it is written: "And all your valuations shall be according to the shekel of the Sanctuary"** (Leviticus 27:25), perhaps this juxtaposition teaches that **anyone who is** included **in** the obligation to contribute half-**shekels is** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuations. But** with regard to **these priests, since they are not** included **in** the obligation to contribute half-**shekels, they are** also **not** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuations.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that even according to the opinion of ben Bukhri, priests are included in the *halakha* of valuations.

This brings us back to the question of *erchin*. *Salka da'atakh amina*: since the verse states *vechol erkecha yiheyeh beshekel hakodesh* — every valuation shall be in the *shekel* of the sanctuary — one might link *erchin* to *machatzit hashekel*. If *kol de'isa bishkalim isa be'erchin* — whoever is in the category of *shkalim* is in the category of *erchin* — and since *kohanim* (according to *Ben Buchri*) are not in the category of *shkalim*, one might conclude they are likewise excluded from *erchin*. Therefore the *mishnah* teaches: *kamashma lan* — even so, *kohanim* who undertake an *erech* vow are obligated to pay.

### Abaye Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַאי ״וְכׇל עֶרְכְּךָ״, לְכׇל עֲרָכִין שֶׁאַתָּה מַעֲרִיךְ לֹא יְהוּ פְּחוּתִין מִסֶּלַע הוּא דַּאֲתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: One could not have thought that **this** phrase: **"And all your valuations** shall be according to the shekel of the Sanctuary" (Leviticus 27:25), might be coming to exempt the priests, as **it comes to** teach something else, namely that **all valuations that you assess should not be less than** the value of **a *sela*** coin. In other words, a poor person, who gives according to his means rather than the amount prescribed in the Torah, does not fulfill his obligation unless he gives at least a shekel, which is a *sela* coin.

*Amar lei Abaye*: that verse — *vechol erkecha yiheyeh beshekel hakodesh* — is not intended to link *erchin* to *machatzit hashekel* at all. It comes for an entirely different *drasha*: *lechol erchin she'atah maariach lo yifchasu misela d'oraysa* — for all *erchin* valuations, the minimum unit of payment shall be one *sela* (shekel). Why is this needed? The Torah's smallest fixed *erech* is three *shekels* (for a female infant). So what does a minimum of one *shekel* contribute? The answer lies in *hasagat yad* — the sliding scale. The Torah provides that if the one who vowed cannot afford the assessed amount, the *kohen* shall reassess according to the vower's means. But this sliding scale has a floor: even in the most reduced circumstances, the minimum obligation is one *shekel*. That is what the verse teaches. It has no implications for who is or is not included in the *erchin* system.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״וּפְדוּיָו מִבֶּן חֹדֶשׁ תִּפְדֶּה בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״, כׇּל דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן — אִיתֵיהּ בַּעֲרָכִין, וְהָנֵי כֹּהֲנִים הוֹאִיל וְלֵיתַנְהוּ בְּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן — לֵיתַנְהוּ בַּעֲרָכִין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** It **was necessary** for the mishna to teach that priests are included in the *halakha* of valuation, as **it might enter your mind to say: Since it is written** with regard to a firstborn: **"And their redemption money, from a month old you shall redeem them, shall be, according to your valuation,** five shekels of silver" (Numbers 18:16), one might have said that **anyone** included **in** the mitzva of **redemption of the** firstborn **son is** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuations; but** with regard to **these priests, since they are not** included **in** the mitzva of **redemption of the** firstborn **son,** they **are** also **not** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuations.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that priests are also included in the *halakha* of valuations.

*Abaye* then offers his own *hava amina*: the verse *upduyav miben chodesh tifdeh beerchecha* — you shall redeem them from one month onward according to your *erech* — uses the word *erech* in the context of *pidyon haben*. Since a male infant's *erech* (five *shekels*) matches the five-*shekel* cost of *pidyon hebben*, there is a conceptual link. One might say: *kol de'ita bepidyon haben ita be'erchin* — whoever is in the *pidyon haben* category is in the *erchin* category. Since *kohanim* and *levi'im* do not redeem their own children (*pidyon haben* does not apply to them), they might also be excluded from *erchin*. Hence the *mishnah*'s ruling is necessary.

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, גַּבֵּי אֵיל אָשָׁם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת אֲשָׁמוֹ יָבִיא לַה׳ אַיִל תָּמִים מִן הַצֹּאן בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּכׇל דְּאִיתֵיהּ בַּעֲרָכִין אִיתֵיהּ בְּאֵיל הָאָשָׁם, טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ בַּעֲרָכִין לֵיתַנְהוּ בְּאֵיל הָאָשָׁם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: **If that is so,** that one can expound the term "according to your valuation" in this manner, one could expound similarly **with regard to** the **ram of** the **guilt offering** brought by one who robs and then takes a false oath. **As it is written** with regard to that offering: **"And he shall bring his guilt offering to the Lord, a ram without blemish out of the flock, according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 5:25). One can argue that **so too, anyone** who **is** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuations is** included **in the ram of the guilt offering.** Therefore, **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite, who are not** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuations,** as stated in the mishna, should also **not** be included **in the ram of the guilt offering.** But that is not so, as there is no opinion that exempts them from this offering.

*Amar lei Rava*: one cannot draw such far-reaching conclusions from the shared word *erech*, because this would lead to absurd results. The verse concerning the *ayil asham* — the guilt-offering ram — also uses the phrase *beerchecha*: *ve'et ashamo yavi l'Hashem ayil tamim min hatzon beerchecha*. If the shared word *erech* creates categorical linkage, one might say *kol de'ita be'erchin ita be'ayil asham* — whoever is in the category of *erchin* is required to bring the ram — and therefore *tumtum* and *androgynos*, who are excluded from *erchin* valuations, would also be exempt from bringing a guilt offering. That is clearly impossible: liability for sin cannot depend on indeterminate gender. Therefore the word *beerchecha* cannot bear so much categorical weight, and *Abaye*'s *hava amina* proves too much.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אָשֵׁי: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ לִפְנֵי הַכֹּהֵן״, וְלֹא כֹּהֵן לִפְנֵי כֹּהֵן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said, and some say** it was **Rav Ashi:** It **was necessary** to teach that priests are included in the *halakha* of valuation because **it might enter your mind to say: Since it is written** concerning valuations: "But if he is too poor for your valuation, **then he shall be set before the priest"** (Leviticus 27:8), this indicates that a poor man is placed before the priest for valuation, **but a priest is not** placed **before** another **priest** for valuation. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that priests are also included in the *halakha* of valuations.

*Rava* (some say *Rav Ashi*) offers the correct *hava amina*: the verse states *vehe'emido lifnei hakohen* — you shall stand him before the *kohen* — referring to the person whose means are assessed for the sliding scale. The *kohen* plays the role of assessor and collector; the rest of *bnei yisrael* are those who make the vows. One might have thought these are categorically distinct roles — *velo kohen lifnei kohen*, a *kohen* does not stand before a *kohen* — and that a *kohen* therefore cannot occupy the role of vow-maker. This parallels *Ben Buchri*'s reasoning about *machatzit hashekel*: those who administer the Temple and those who fund it are different classes. *Kamashma lan*: the *mishnah* teaches that although the *kohen* plays the assessor's role, he can equally stand in the role of the vow-maker.

## Scope of Arak'in

### Braita Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נֶעֱרָכִין״ — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מְנוָּּול וּמוּכֵּה שְׁחִין. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״ — לְהָבִיא עֵרֶךְ סָתוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara stated (2a) that the mishna's statement: And everyone **is valuated,** serves **to add** that **a repulsive** man **and one afflicted with boils** are valuated in accordance with the Torah's categories of age and sex despite their lack of market value. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? The Gemara answers that it is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "When a man shall clearly utter a vow of persons to the Lord, **according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:2). The term serves **to include an unspecified valuation,** as explained below.

Having resolved why the *mishnah* specifies *kohen levi v'yisrael*, the *gemara* turns to the next component of the *mishnah*: *hakol ne'erachim* — everyone can be the *object* of an *erech* valuation. What does this come to include? *Ne'erachim le'atuyei menuval u'mukat shechin* — it includes even a repulsive person covered with blemishes who would have no market value. This leads the *gemara* to a major *braita* that it quotes in full before analyzing clause by clause. *Detanu rabanan* — the sages taught in a *braita* — and the *braita* opens: *be'erkecha* — "according to the value" — *lehavi erech satum*, to include an unexplicated valuation.

### Erech Stum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״ — עֵרֶךְ כּוּלּוֹ הוּא נוֹתֵן, וְלֹא עֵרֶךְ אֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the verse teaches that **one gives the valuation of his entire** self **and not the valuation of** his **limbs.** If someone vows that he will give the valuation of a limb, he is not obligated to give anything.

The word *be'erkecha* teaches that even if a person said *erech* without specifying whose *erech* — simply "I will pay an *erech*" — the obligation is valid and binding. The vower has not rendered the pledge meaningless by failing to name the subject; we will see shortly what amount is owed.

### Complete Person

A second teaching from *be'erkecha*: *erech kulo hu notein velo erech evarim* — one pays the full *erech* of a person, not the *erech* of a limb. If someone says "I will pay the *erech* of that person's arm," he pays nothing. The word *erech* applies to a whole person and the Torah's fixed values correspond to whole persons. An *erech* of a limb has no legal force.

### Nefashot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מוֹצִיא אַף דָּבָר שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נְפָשׁוֹת״, נְפָשׁוֹת — וְלֹא הַמֵּת. אוֹצִיא אֶת הַמֵּת, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת הַגּוֹסֵס? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהֶעֱמִיד... וְהֶעֱרִיךְ״ — כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּהַעֲמָדָה, יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּהַעֲרָכָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּהַעֲמָדָה, אֵינוֹ בְּהַעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought **that I** should **exclude even** the valuation of **an item upon which the soul is dependent,** without which one would die, e.g., the liver. Therefore, **the verse states: "Persons [*nefashot*],"** which teaches that if one valuated a limb upon which the soul [*nefesh*] is dependent, he is obligated to give the valuation of his entire self. The Gemara further derives from the term **"persons"** that one is obligated to pay the valuation only of a live person, **and not** the valuation of **the dead.** If one valuates a deceased person, his statement is of no effect. Furthermore, one might have thought that **I should exclude** only **the dead** from valuation, **but I should not exclude a moribund** person, who is about to die but is still alive, and who is therefore included in the category of "persons." Therefore, **the verse states: "Then he shall be set** before the priest, **and** the priest **shall value** him" (Leviticus 27:8). This teaches that **anyone who is** included **in** the category of **setting,** i.e., he can stand, **is** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuation. And anyone not** included **in** the category of **setting,** who cannot stand, e.g., one who is on his deathbed, is **not** included **in** the *halakha* of **valuation.** Therefore, one who valuates a moribund individual is not obligated to pay anything.

From here the *braita* proceeds to a qualification. *Yachol she'ani motzi af davar she'haneshama tluyah bo* — one might think that even the *erech* of a vital organ, something life depends upon, is excluded. *Talmud lomar nefashot* — the Torah says "souls," indicating that anything upon which the soul depends counts. Therefore, if someone says "I will pay the *erech* of that person's heart," that is treated as if he pledged the entire *erech*, since the heart is a vital organ. The principle is asymmetric: ordinary limbs produce no obligation; vital organs produce the full obligation.

### Living Only

The *braita* continues: *nefashot velo hamet* — "souls," not a corpse. The *erech* of a dead person cannot be pledged — the obligation requires a living subject. *Hotzi hamet velo hotzi et hagoses* — perhaps one has excluded only a corpse but not a *goses* (a person in the final throes of death)? *Talmud lomar vehe'emid vehe'erich* — the *kohen* shall stand and evaluate. *Kol she'yeshno be'ha'amada yeshno be'ha'aracha* — whoever can be stood up is subject to valuation; *ve'chol she'eino be'ha'amada eino be'ha'aracha* — whoever cannot be stood up is not. A *goses*, halakhically, is not considered in the category of one who can be stood up, and is therefore excluded.

The *gemara* notes a slight redundancy: if we can already exclude a corpse from the principle that it cannot be stood up, why do we need *nefashot velo hamet* as a separate source? The *gemara* answers: we did not really need the verse to exclude the corpse — *kiri bei nefesh nefashot*, the plural form teaches other things, as the *gemara* proceeds to enumerate.

### Multiple Persons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״נְפָשֹׁת״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אֶחָד שֶׁהֶעֱרִיךְ אֶחָד, אֶחָד שֶׁהֶעֱרִיךְ מֵאָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נְפָשֹׁת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** since the verse states: **"Persons,"** in the plural, it is expounded as follows: Were the verse to have written: A person, **I** would **have** derived **only** that this applies to **one** person **who valuated one** person. **From where** is it derived that even if **one** person **valuated a hundred** people, it is also an effective evaluation? **The verse states: "Persons,"** in the plural. This teaches that in such a case he must pay the valuation of each and every one of the hundred.

*Davar acher nefashot*: *ein li ela echad she'he'erich echad* — I know only that if one person valued one other person, each individual pledge is binding. But what if someone said "I will pay the *erech* of *Reuven Shimon Levi Yehuda*," naming many people in a single utterance while using the word *erech* only once? One might think only a single *erech* payment is owed. *Talmud lomar nefashot* — the plural teaches that each named person creates a separate obligation, and all must be paid.

### Women Obligors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״נְפָשֹׁת״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ שֶׁהֶעֱרִיךְ בֵּין אִישׁ בֵּין אִשָּׁה, אִשָּׁה שֶׁהֶעֱרִיכָה אִישׁ, אִשָּׁה שֶׁהֶעֱרִיכָה אִשָּׁה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נְפָשֹׁת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** one can expound the plural form of **"persons"** as follows: Were the verse to have written only: A person, one might have said that **I can** derive **only** that this *halakha* applies to **a man who valuates** another, **whether a man or a woman,** as the section begins: "When a man clearly utters a vow" (Leviticus 27:2). **From where** is it derived that the *halakha* of valuations also applies to **a woman who valuated a man,** or **a woman who valuated a woman?** The same **verse states:** "Vow of **persons** to the Lord, according to your valuation" (Leviticus 27:2), to include women.

*Davar acher nefashot*: the Torah's formulation opens with *ish ki yafli be'erkecha* — "a man who articulates your *erech*" — establishing the paradigm of a man undertaking the vow. *Ein li ela ish she'he'erich bein ish bein isha* — I know only that a man can make an *erech* vow for a man or for a woman. But what of a woman who vows to pay the *erech* of a man, or of a woman? *Talmud lomar nefashot*: the word *nefashot* is read here as the subject — those who undertake the vow — as well as the object. Whether the vow-maker is male or female, the obligation is valid.

### Menuval Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״נְפָשֹׁת״ — לְרַבּוֹת מְנוָּּול וּמוּכֵּה שְׁחִין. שֶׁיָּכוֹל ״נֶדֶר בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״: כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּדָמִים — יֶשְׁנוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּדָמִים — אֵינוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נְפָשֹׁת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the word **"persons"** serves **to add a repulsive** man **and one afflicted with boils.** This derivation is necessary **as** one **might** have thought that since the verse states: **"A vow** of persons to the Lord, **according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:2), it is juxtaposing a person who is valuated and one who is the object of a vow of assessments. Therefore, **anyone who is** included **in** the category of **assessments,** i.e., one who is obligated to pay his assessment to the Temple if he takes such a vow, **is** also included **in** the category of **valuations.** If he vows to pay his valuation, he must pay. **But anyone who is not** included **in** the category of **assessments is not** included **in** the category of **valuations.** Having no market value, these people are not subject to assessment, and are consequently also not subject to valuation. **The verse,** therefore, **states: "Persons [*nefashot*],"** teaching that anyone who has any amount of life [*nefesh*] is subject to valuation.

*Davar acher nefashot*, a third application: *lerabot menuval u'mukat shechin* — to include even the repulsive person and the one afflicted with boils who would fetch no price on the slave market and therefore would have no *damim* (market value). One might have thought: since the Torah links *neder* and *erech* — vowing and valuation — *kol she'yeshno be'erchin yeshno bedamim, ve'chol she'eino be'erchin eino bedamim* — only someone on the *erech* chart would have an actionable market-valuation pledge, and since a person without market value seems outside the system, perhaps there is no *erech* obligation either. *Talmud lomar nefashot*: souls — the fixed *erech* values apply to all living persons regardless of market worth.

The *gemara* explains the economy of these multiple *drashot* from a single word. Each of the earlier derivations — multiple persons, female vow-makers — could arguably have been deduced without a verse. *Shakul hu v'yavo'u kulam*: when there is no compelling reason to prefer one application of a verse over another, we say the verse encompasses all equally plausible teachings. The one application that genuinely required the verse is the *menuval u'mukat shechin* case, because there one might truly have reasoned that a person without market value falls outside the *erchin* system entirely. That is the distinctive *chiddush* the word *nefashot* comes to teach.

## Minimum Value

### Erech Satum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״ — לְרַבּוֹת עֵרֶךְ סָתוּם. מַאי עֵרֶךְ סָתוּם? דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר ״עֵרֶךְ סָתוּם עָלַי״ נוֹתֵן כַּפָּחוֹת שֶׁבָּעֲרָכִין, וְכַמָּה פָּחוֹת שֶׁבָּעֲרָכִין? שְׁלֹשֶׁת שְׁקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** above in the beginning of the *baraita* that the verse: "When a man shall clearly utter a vow of persons to the Lord, **according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:2), serves **to include an unspecified valuation.** The Gemara asks: **What** is **an unspecified valuation?** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to donate **an unspecified valuation,** without specifying any particular person, **gives** the amount **of the smallest of the valuations. And how much is the smallest of the valuations?** It is **three shekels,** which is the valuation of a female who is younger than five years old (Leviticus 27:6).

The *gemara* now analyzes the *braita*'s first clause: *be'erkecha lehavi erech satum* — the word *be'erkecha* includes an unexplicated valuation. *Mai erech satum*? What does an unexplicated *erech* obligate? *Detanya ha'omer erech satum alai* — if someone says "an unspecified *erech* is upon me" — *noten kepachot shebe'arachin*: he pays the minimum of all the *erchin* values.

### Three Shekels Min

*Ve'kamma pachot shebe'arachin*? What is that minimum? *Sheloshah shkalim* — three *shekels*. The Torah's fixed schedule yields a minimum of three *shekels* for a female infant between one month and five years. That is the floor. Why not simply pay the maximum of fifty *shekels*?

### Grab Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא חֲמִשִּׁים! תָּפַשְׂתָּה מְרוּבֶּה — לֹא תָּפַשְׂתָּה, תָּפַשְׂתָּה מוּעָט — תָּפַשְׂתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **And** why not **say** that one who does not specify a person should give **fifty** shekels, which is the largest of the valuations, that of a male between the ages of twenty and sixty (Leviticus 27:3)? The *baraita* answers: This ruling is based on the principle that if **you grasped a lot you did not grasp** anything, but if **you grasped a little, you grasped** something. In other words, if one can derive two sums from the verses, one should choose the smaller number, as it is included within the larger number and is therefore considered certain.

*Ve'eima chamishim*? The *gemara* invokes the principle: *tafasta merubbah lo tafasta, tafasta mu'at tafasta* — if you grab too much you do not have it, if you grab a little you keep it. When the amount of an obligation is uncertain, we assume the minimum. Any amount higher than three *shekels* would require explicit justification; the bare minimum is what the unspecified pledge obligates.

But perhaps the minimum should be one *shekel*, since the verse *vechol erkecha yiheyeh beshekel hakodesh* establishes that the sliding scale descends no lower than one *shekel*? The *gemara* responds: *hahu behasagat yad hu dechtiv* — that verse addresses *hasagat yad*, the reduced payment for one who cannot afford the standard amount. The normal minimum — for one who can pay — is three *shekels*. The one-*shekel* floor is a lower boundary for impoverished vow-makers only.

### Hasagat Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא שֶׁקֶל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכׇל עֶרְכְּךָ יִהְיֶה בְּשֶׁקֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ״! הָהוּא בְּהֶשֵּׂג יָד הוּא דִּכְתִיב. וְאֶלָּא קָרָא לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִידּוֹן בְּהֶשֵּׂג יָד. מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּמְפָרֵשׁ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **And** why not **say** that smallest of the valuations is one **shekel, as it is written: "And all your valuations shall be according to the shekel of the Sanctuary"** (Leviticus 27:25)? The *baraita* answers: **That** verse **is written with regard to affordability.** If one vows to donate the valuation of a person to the Temple treasury but does not have sufficient funds to fulfill his vow, he must pay at least a shekel. The Gemara asks: **But** since the payment of three shekels can be derived from the principle of grasping the lesser amount, **why do I** need **the verse** "according to your valuation"? **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The verse is required **to teach that** unlike other valuations, where a person who cannot afford the set amount can fulfill his obligation with one shekel, in the case of an unspecified valuation even a poor person's obligation **is not determined by affordability,** and he must give at least three shekels. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara answers: It is because it is **considered as though** he **explicitly** vowed that he is obligated to pay three shekels. The principle of affordability applies only to the valuations fixed by the Torah.

This raises a further question: if the verse establishing the one-*shekel* minimum is already derivable from first principles (since we would conclude the minimum is three *shekels* regardless), what new information does the verse convey? *Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha*: the verse teaches that when someone makes an unexplicated *erech* pledge — *erech satum* — the obligation is *not* subject to the *hasagat yad* sliding scale. Why? *Kemeforash dami* — it is as though he stated the amount explicitly. The sliding scale operates because when a person names a specific subject — "the *erech* of *Reuven*" — there is someone to assess: if he cannot pay the full amount, the *kohen* can evaluate him according to his means, choosing among different "books of valuation," so to speak. But when no specific person is named, there is nothing to assess. The pledge translates directly to the fixed sum of three *shekels*. Saying "an *erech*" without a subject is tantamount to saying "three *shekels*" explicitly — and explicit pledges cannot be reduced by *hasagat yad*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: נִידּוֹן בְּהֶשֵּׂג יָד. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא כִּמְפָרֵשׁ דָּמֵי, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says** the opposite: The term "according to your valuation" actually teaches that even an unspecified valuation is **determined based on affordability,** and a poor person fulfills his obligation by giving one shekel. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** Why would one think that this case differs from any other valuation? The Gemara answers that the verse is necessary **lest you say** that one who obligates himself in an unspecified valuation is **considered as though** he **articulated** that he is obligated to pay three shekels, and may not pay less. Therefore, this verse **teaches us** that an unspecified valuation is also subject to affordability, like other valuations.

The practical consequence is significant: if such a person genuinely cannot afford three *shekels*, the debt to the Temple remains outstanding. There is no mechanism to reduce it below three.

The *gemara* then records an *ika d'amri* — an alternative version of *Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha*'s ruling — to the opposite effect: the *erech satum* is subject to *hasagat yad*. The *chiddush* here is precisely the reverse: one might have thought that because the unexplicated pledge is *kemeforash dami* — like an explicit amount — it is fixed and not reducible. The *kamashma lan* is that since the person did not in fact say three *shekels* explicitly but only said "*erech*," the *kohen* retains the latitude to assess the obligation downward according to the vower's means.

## Tumtum/Androgynos

### Gender Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָיָה עֶרְכְּךָ״ — לְרַבּוֹת טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס לְדָמִים, שֶׁיָּכוֹל: נֶדֶר ״בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״ — כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּדָמִים, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין אֵינוֹ בְּדָמִים, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהָיָה עֶרְכְּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* teaches that the apparently superfluous phrase: **"Then your valuation shall be"** (Leviticus 27:3), serves **to include a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite in** the *halakha* of **assessments,** despite the fact that they are not included in the *halakha* of valuations. If one assesses a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite, he is obligated to give that assessment. The *baraita* explains **that** one **might** have thought that since the verse states: **"A vow** of persons to the Lord, **according to your valuation," anyone who is** included **in** the category of **valuations is** also included **in** the category of **assessments, but anyone who is not** included **in** the category of **valuations is** also **not** included **in** the category of **assessments.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Then your valuation shall be,"** to include a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite in the *halakha* of assessment.

The *braita* also teaches that the verse *vehaya erkecha hazachar* — "and your valuation shall be for the male" — establishes that the *erech* system applies only to *zachar vadai* and *nekeva vadai*: definitively male and definitively female individuals. *Velo tumtum ve'androgynos* — a *tumtum* (one whose gender is indeterminate due to anatomical ambiguity) and an *androgynos* (one with characteristics of both sexes) cannot be the objects of a fixed *erech* valuation. One might further wonder: since the verse says *hazachar*, perhaps a *tumtum* who is not definitively male could nonetheless pay the female *erech* value — treating "not male" as equivalent to "female"? The verse answers this as well: *vehaya erkecha hazachar ve'im nekeva hi* — the Torah specifies both male and female explicitly, meaning *zachar vadai nekeva vadai*, definitively one or the other. There is no middle category in the *erchin* chart.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַזָּכָר״ — זָכָר, וְלֹא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס. יָכוֹל לֹא יְהוּ בְּעֵרֶךְ אִישׁ, אֲבָל יִהְיוּ בְּעֵרֶךְ אִשָּׁה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהָיָה עֶרְכְּךָ הַזָּכָר... וְאִם נְקֵבָה הִיא״ — זָכָר וַדַּאי, נְקֵבָה וַדָּאִית, וְלֹא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: This *halakha*, that a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite are not included in valuation, is derived from the term: **"The male"** (Leviticus 27:3). The definite article teaches that this *halakha* applies specifically to **a male, and not** to **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite.** One **might** have thought that a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite **should not be** valuated **by** the **valuation of a man, but** they **should be** valuated **by** the **valuation of a woman.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Then your valuation shall be for the male…and if it be a female"** (Leviticus 27:3–4). This teaches that valuation applies only to **a definite male** or **a definite female, but not** to **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite,** who are categorized as neither male nor female.

### Market Value OK

Nevertheless, *tumtum* and *androgynos* are not without halakhic valuation altogether. The *braita* teaches: *vehaya erkecha lerabot tumtum ve'androgynos ledamim* — the word *vehaya erkecha* comes to include *tumtum* and *androgynos* for purposes of *damim*, market valuation. Even though they cannot be the subject of a fixed *erech*, if someone says *damav alai* — I will pay this person's market value — the pledge is binding.

The *gemara* asks: why do we need a verse for this? Even a palm tree can be consecrated by declaring its value — *lo yehei ela d'mei dikla* — one can dedicate the value of any object to *hekdesh*. Why would we think that gender ambiguity could prevent a market valuation from taking effect?

### Nidon Bichvodo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לוֹמַר שֶׁנִּידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The verse serves **to say that** a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite are **assessed by** the **significance** of the body part that one specified. If one vowed to give a vital part of his body, he is not merely obligated to pay the value of that organ but must give the value of his entire body.

*Amar Rava*: the verse comes to teach that *nidon bichvodo* — they are judged according to their honor. What does this mean? *Rashi* explains it as follows: within the law of *erchin*, we learned from the word *nefashot* that a vital organ represents the whole person — if one says "the *erech* of this person's heart," one pays the full *erech*, not a fractional amount. The question is whether this principle — that a vital organ stands in for the whole — extends also to market valuations. If someone says "I will pay the market value (*damim*) of this person's heart to *hekdesh*," do we assess the entire market value of the person, treating the heart as representing the whole? This is what *nidon bichvodo* means: the vital organ is assessed at the honor of the whole person's value.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״נֶדֶר בְּעֶרְכְּךָ״ כְּתִיב, כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין — נִידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בַּעֲרָכִין — אֵינוֹ נִידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** As it might **enter your mind to say** that since **it is written** in the verse: **"A vow** of persons to the Lord, **according to your valuation,"** a person who is valuated is juxtaposed to one who is the object of a vow of assessments. Therefore, **anyone who is** included **in** the category of **valuations is** also included **in** the *halakha* of being **assessed by** the **significance** of the body part that he specifies. **But anyone who is not** included **in** the category of **valuations,** such as a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite, **is not** included **in** the *halakha* of being **assessed by** the **significance** of the body part that he specifies. Therefore, the verse teaches that although a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite are not included in valuations, they are nevertheless included in the *halakha* of being assessed by the significance of the body part in question.

The *hava amina* was: since we derive this principle from the laws of *erchin* — specifically from *nefashot* in that context — perhaps it applies only to persons who are in the *erchin* category. A *tumtum* or *androgynos*, excluded from fixed *erech* valuations, might not benefit from this principle, and if one said "the market value of this *tumtum*'s heart," one might have to assess only the heart in isolation rather than the whole person. The verse therefore teaches that *tumtum* and *androgynos* are *nidon bichvodo* for market valuations as well.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּדְלָא אִיתֵיהּ בַּעֲרָכִין, מִי נִידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״רֹאשׁ עֶבֶד זֶה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ הוּא וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שׁוּתָּפִין בּוֹ, ״רֹאשׁ עֶבֶד מָכוּר לְךָ״ מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם, ״רֹאשׁ חֲמוֹר זֶה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ הוּא וְהֶקְדֵּשׁ שׁוּתָּפִין בּוֹ, ״רֹאשׁ חֲמוֹר מָכוּר לָךְ״ מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **And is it true** that one who **is not** included **in valuations is** nevertheless **assessed by** the **significance** of the body part that he specifies? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one says: The **head of this slave** is **consecrated property, he and the Temple** treasury are **partners in** the entire slave. Similarly, if one says to another: The **head of** this **slave** is **sold to you,** they **appraise** the slave and split the value **between them.** Likewise, if one says: The **head of this donkey** is **consecrated property, he and the Temple** treasury are **partners in** the donkey. And if one says to another: The **head of** this **donkey** is **sold to you,** they **appraise** the donkey and split the value **between them.**

*Amar lei Abaye*: is it truly the case that anyone outside the *erchin* category is not *nidon bichvodo*? This seems demonstrably false. A *braita* teaches: *rosh eved zeh hekdesh* — if one says "the head of this slave is *hekdesh*" — *hu ve'hekdesh shutafin bo*: the person and *hekdesh* become joint owners of the slave. Similarly, *rosh eved machur lach* — "the head of this slave is sold to you" — creates a partnership.

### Body Part Values

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״רֹאשׁ פָּרָה מָכוּר לְךָ״ — לֹא מָכַר אֶלָּא רֹאשָׁהּ שֶׁל פָּרָה. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ: ״רֹאשׁ פָּרָה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ אֵין לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ אֶלָּא רֹאשָׁהּ. וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּהָא מִזְדַּבַּן רֵישָׁא דְתוֹרָא בְּבֵי טַבָּחָא.
>
> **Translation:** But if one says to another: The **head of** this **cow** is **sold to you,** he has **sold him only the head of the cow. Moreover, even** if he says: The **head of** this **cow** is **consecrated property, the Temple** treasury **has** ownership **only** of the cow's **head. And Rav Pappa said:** This difference between these cases is **due to** the fact **that** the **head of an ox is sold** independently **in the butcher shop.**

The same principle applies to animals: *rosh chamor zeh hekdesh* — "the head of this donkey is *hekdesh*" — creates a partnership between the owner and *hekdesh*. The same for *rosh chamor machur lach*. However, *rosh parah machur lach* — "the head of this cow is sold to you" — means only the head itself: *lo machar ela roshah*. Why the difference? *Amar Rav Papa*: because in the butcher shops, a cow's head is sold separately — *de-hamizdavin reisha de-tora be-veit tavcha* — and therefore "head of a cow" denotes the literal head, not a representative share of the whole animal.

What emerges from these cases is that saying a body part does not in general cause the whole to be treated as represented — rather, it typically creates co-ownership. The vital-organ principle of *nidon bichvodo* thus stands in need of further analysis: does saying a vital organ truly assign the full value to one party, or does it create some form of shared ownership? And does this principle apply even outside the formal *erchin* context? These questions remain open as the *daf* concludes, and the *gemara* will continue the discussion on the following day.
