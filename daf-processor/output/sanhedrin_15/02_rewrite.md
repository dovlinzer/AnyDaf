# Sanhedrin 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Erchin Metaltelin

### Definition & Grammar

The *mishnah* on 14b introduces the concept of *erchin ha-metaltelin* — movable *erchin*. To understand what this means, one must first recall the nature of *erech* itself: when a person declares "the *erech* of so-and-so is upon me," the *Torah* assigns fixed valuations based on the subject's age and sex, and those sums are owed to the *Beis Hamikdash*. The question is what it means for *erchin* to be "movable."

### Rav Gidal Vessels

Rav Gidal citing Rav explains: *ba-omer erech kli zeh alai* — someone who declares the *erech* of a vessel upon himself. Of course, the *Torah*'s system of *erech* does not apply to vessels at all; *erech* exists only for persons. What then does such a declaration mean? Rav Gidal teaches that since a person knows full well that *erech* has no technical application to a vessel, when he uses that language he must have meant its market value — *damim*. He concludes his vow and intends to pay the object's worth. Therefore *noten damav* — he pays its monetary value. And because we are now speaking of assessing the value of movable property, a *beis din* of three suffices.

This phenomenon — using a term loosely and having the law interpret it according to intent rather than strict terminology — appears in other halachic contexts as well. The Gemara in tractate *Nedarim* discusses *neder bi-leshon shvuah* and *shvuah bi-leshon neder*: people often employ technically incorrect vocabulary, and the halacha asks what they actually intended. Here the principle is the same: *adam yode'a she-ein erech le-kli* — a person knows a vessel has no *erech*, and so *vegamar ve-amar leshum damim* — he concluded and spoke for the purpose of monetary value.

### Metaltelin Designated

The *Gemara* then raises a grammatical objection: the phrase *erchin ha-metaltelin* seems to say that the *erchin* themselves are movable, whereas what is intended is *erchin shel metaltelin* — the *erech* of movable property. One resolution is simply to emend or reread the *mishnah* as *erchin shel metaltelin*. This is offered in the name of Rav Chisda citing Avimi, who explains the case as *be-matpis metaltelin le-erchin* — one who designates movable goods toward the satisfaction of an *erech* obligation. Having said "the *erech* of a person is upon me," one sets aside cattle or household items as the means of payment. The question of how to value those items requires a *beis din* of three.

### Abbahu & Yerushalmi

Rabbi Abbahu offers a third reading: *ba-omer erki alai be-karka ligvot mimena metaltelin* — one who declared an *erech* upon himself and is being collected from via movable property or land. This corresponds to the *Yerushalmi*'s approach: if one pays up with *metaltelin*, a *beis din* of three is needed to assess value; if one pays with *karka*, a *beis din* of ten is required. This better parallels the standard framework of redeeming sanctified property, though here the distinction turns on what one is paying *with*, not what has been sanctified.

### Rav Acha Three Din

Rav Acha of Difti challenges Ravina on this point. The three-versus-ten distinction makes intuitive sense when one is *redeeming* from *hekdesh* — taking something out — because *hekdesh*'s interests need representation, and a larger *beis din* protects against undervaluation of land. But what about when one is simply *giving to hekdesh* — *le-ayulei le-hekdesh*? Why would one need a *beis din* at all? There is no adversarial party whose interests require protection. *Le-ayulei le-hekdesh shlosha lama li?*

### Beis Din Purpose

The response is elegant: *svara hu* — it is self-evident. *Mah li le-ayulei mah li le-apukei* — what difference does it make whether you are giving to or taking from *hekdesh*? The reason a *beis din* is required in either case is not primarily ritualistic but practical: *di-oma* — you might make a mistake in estimating value. Whether you are redeeming *karka* from *hekdesh* or paying the value of *karka* to satisfy an obligation, land is difficult to appraise. You need a larger body — not because it ritualizes the redemption, but because more expert eyes ensure the valuation is correct. One might therefore ask whether this is truly a *beis din* in the juridical sense or more like an appraisal panel. That question will resurface as we examine who must constitute this group.

## Kohen Requirement

### Rabbi Yehudah Kohen

Rabbi Yehudah holds that a *kohen* must be one of the three who assesses *metaltelin* or *erchin*. This is his distinctive position: even for the smaller *beis din* of three, one member must be a *kohen*.

### Rav Pappa Question

Rav Pappa asks Abaye: *bishlama le-Rebbe Yehudah hayinu dikesiv Kohen* — granted, according to Rabbi Yehudah, the Torah's repeated mention of *kohen* in these passages is intelligible, since he requires a *kohen* even for the three. But *ela le-rabbanan Kohen lamah li?* — according to the *rabbanan*, who ultimately do not require a *kohen* even for the ten, why does the Torah mention *kohen* at all in these passages? This question is left standing: *kashya*.

### Ten Kohenim Count

The *Gemara* then asks how we know that for *karka*, one needs ten, and that at least one of those ten must be a *kohen*. Shmuel answers: *asarah Kohanim kesuvim ba-parsha* — the word *kohen* appears ten times in the relevant section of the Torah. One occurrence establishes the requirement of a *kohen* — *chad le-gufo*. The remaining nine repetitions create a sequence of *miyut achar miyut* — limitation after limitation — and the hermeneutical principle is *ein miyut achar miyut ela le-rabbos*: each successive limiting term actually expands the category. So the cascading repetitions of *kohen* come to teach that even a non-*kohen*, a *Yisrael*, may fulfill that role. The upshot is *va-afilu tish'ah Yisrael ve-echad Kohen* — nine Israelites and one *kohen* suffice, meaning the *kohen* is one of the ten, not an addition to them.

### Five Kohenim Five

Rav Huna son of Rav Nassan objects to this reading: why must the alternation work in only one direction? The first mention of *kohen* requires a *kohen*; the second, as a *miyut achar miyut*, opens the category to a *Yisrael*; the third reverts to requiring a *kohen*; the fourth again expands to *Yisrael* — and so on, yielding five *kohanim* and five Israelites.

### Kashya Consensus

The *Gemara* leaves this *kashya* unresolved. Nevertheless, the consensus position for *karka* cases is clear: ten are required, at least one of whom is a *kohen*.

## Adam Kadosh

### Abbahu Adam Value

The *mishnah* includes *adam* — a person — in the list of cases requiring a *beis din* of ten. But as the *Gemara* asks: *Adam mi kadosh?* Since when does a free person become sanctified? One cannot sanctify a free person. The *Yerushalmi* suggested this referred to a slave; the *Bavli* takes a different approach. Rabbi Abbahu explains: *ba-omer demay alai* — one who declares "my value is upon me," or the value of another person. Crucially, this person did not say *erech*, invoking the Torah's fixed valuation tables, but *damim* — the market value.

### Slave Market Price

How does one assess the market value of a free person? The *beraita* teaches: *ha-omer demay alai shamin oso ke-eved ha-nimkar ba-shuk* — one appraises how much that person would fetch if sold as a slave. And because *eved itkes le-karka'os* — slaves are halachically equated with land — this assessment is treated like an assessment of land, and therefore requires a *beis din* of ten. Notably, the person has not been sanctified; one has merely committed to paying a sum equivalent to the person's slave-market value. And yet, just as paying with or for land requires ten even when the land itself has not been formally sanctified, so too here.

### Tosafot Slaves

*Tosafot* raises the obvious question: if the case involves assessing someone like a slave, why does the *mishnah* use the word *adam* — a free person — rather than simply saying *avadim*? *Tosafot* answers that *adam* does not naturally connote a slave; had the *mishnah* meant a slave it would have said *avadim ke-yotzei ba-hem*. Additionally, there is the view of Rabbi (Rebbi) that if one sanctifies his slave, the act of sanctification liberates the slave — making the whole framework of "sanctified slave" conceptually fraught from the outset.

### Sanctified Valuations

The combined picture, then, is this: the *Yerushalmi* began by assuming *adam* referred to a slave, then retreated to a free person but did not fully explain the mechanism. The *Bavli* through Rabbi Abbahu and the *beraita* fills in the gap: you have committed to paying the equivalent of a person's value as assessed on the slave market, no actual sanctification of a person occurred, and you need a *beis din* of ten to conduct that assessment — parallel to the appraisal of land.

## Hair Cutting

### Hair Valuation

Rabbi Avin poses a question that grows naturally from the foregoing: *se'ar ha-omed ligzoz be-kama* — if someone declared the value of a person, and that person's hair is ready to be cut, do you include the hair's value in the assessment? And if the hair is included as a distinct item, what size *beis din* is needed to assess it? Is the hair considered like *gazuz* — already cut, hence *metaltelin* — requiring three? Or is it *ke-mechubar* — like something still attached to the body, which is equated with land — requiring ten?

### Meila Hair Laws

To resolve this question, the *Gemara* brings a parallel from the laws of *me'ilah*. When one sanctifies a slave, the slave himself is not subject to the full laws of *me'ilah* — misappropriation of *hekdesh* property — because slaves are equated with *karka*, and *me'ilah* does not apply to land. But what of the slave's hair?

### Meila Applies

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *mo'alin bi-se'aro* — the laws of *me'ilah* do apply to the hair of a sanctified slave. The underlying debate, the *Gemara* establishes, turns precisely on the status of hair that is ready to be cut: *de-bi-se'ar ha-omed ligzoz pligi* — that is the contested case. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, hair ready to be cut is not considered part of the slave's body for purposes of *me'ilah* — it is functionally detached, hence *metaltelin*, and *me'ilah* applies. The *Gemara* concludes that the very same dispute governs Rabbi Avin's question: the tannaim who debate *me'ilah* for the hair are debating whether it requires a *beis din* of three or ten. *Shema mina*.

It is worth noting that a similar question surfaces in the *poskim* regarding *chatzitzah* before *mikveh*: when hair or fingernails are ready to be cut, is the portion that would be exposed upon cutting already considered extraneous, creating a *chatzitzah* on what lies beneath it? The general ruling is that there can be no *chatzitzah* on a part of the body not yet exposed, but the conceptual parallel to our *Gemara* is evident.

### Grapes Harvest

The *Gemara* attempts to identify whether the *tannaim* debating the hair's status are the same as those in a separate dispute. The *mishnah* elsewhere records that Rabbi Meir holds: *yesh devarim she-hen ke-karka ve-einam ke-karka* — there are things that are like land but not fully like land. His illustrative case: one who deposits ten laden vines — *gafnim te'unot* — with another, and the bailee claims there were only five. Is this a *modeh be-miktzat* requiring an oath, or is the oath-exemption for *karka* disputes applicable? Rabbi Meir rules that an oath is required, while the *chachamim* hold *kol ha-mechubar la-karka harei hu ke-karka* — whatever is attached to the ground is treated as land, exempt from oath.

Rabbi Yossi son of Rabbi Chanina clarifies: *be-anavim ha-omdot li-vatzer askinan* — the case concerns grapes ready to be harvested. Rabbi Meir holds they are *ki-vtzurot* — as if already harvested, hence movable; the *chachamim* hold they remain *mechubar* and are treated as land.

### Hair Grapes Differ

The *Gemara* rejects the identification of these disputes. One might have supposed that Rabbi Meir, who considers harvest-ready grapes equivalent to harvested ones, would similarly consider cut-ready hair equivalent to cut hair. But this does not follow. *Ad kan lo ka-amar Rabbi Meir hatam ela kol kama de-shabak lehu mikachshi* — Rabbi Meir's reasoning in the grape case is that leaving the grapes on the vine past their time causes them to deteriorate; there is a practical imperative to harvest them immediately, and thus they are treated as already detached. *Aval hacha* — but with hair, *kol kama de-shabak lehu ashbuchei mashbechei* — the longer the hair remains on the head the more it grows and the better it becomes. There is no imperative to cut it now. Therefore, even Rabbi Meir might agree that uncut hair is considered *mechubar* to the person, not yet detached, and the entire body of tannaitic opinion might hold that such hair requires a *beis din* of ten rather than three.

## Bestiality Rova

### Rova Nirva

The *mishnah* lists cases of *rova ve-nirva* — a male animal that performed an act of bestiality and a female animal subjected to such an act — as requiring a *beis din* of twenty-three. The *Gemara* notes that the *mishnah* draws no distinction: *lo shena rova zachar* — it makes no difference whether the male animal penetrated a male, *ve-lo shena rova nekevah* — or a female.

### Male Penetrator

The female case is textually straightforward: the Torah says *ve-haragta et ha-ishah ve-et ha-behemah* — you shall kill the woman and the animal — making clear that both parties are executed, with the standard *beis din* of twenty-three governing the animal's death. But the case where the male animal was the penetrator — *rova zachar* — is not explicitly stated. The source is derived from the verse *kol shochev im behemah mot yumat* — anyone who lies with an animal shall be put to death. This refers to a man as penetrator. Yet there is a parallel verse in *Vayikra*: *ish asher yiten shichavto* — a man who gives forth his seed — describing the same scenario. Since both verses describe an identical case, one of them is superfluous for that case.

### Textual Parallelism

*Im eino inyan le-shochev* — since one verse is not needed to teach the case of the human as penetrator — *tanehu inyan le-nishkav* — apply it to the case where the human is the one penetrated, i.e., where the male animal is the *shochev* and the man is the *nishkav*. But why frame the reversed-role case in the language of *shochev*? *Va-afkeih Rachmana bi-leshon shochev le-akushei nishkav le-shochev* — the Torah expressed it in the language of the active role in order to create a textual equation between the two cases. *Mah shochev hu be-Beis Din shel esrim u-shloshah* — just as the case where the human is the penetrator requires a *beis din* of twenty-three for the animal, *af nishkav hu be-Beis Din shel esrim u-shloshah* — so too the reversed case. The repetition of the verse, framed in the language of the active role even when describing the passive scenario, links the two cases and establishes identical procedural requirements.

## Shor Haniskal

### Owner Ox Parallel

The *mishnah* rules that the *shor ha-niskal* — a goring ox sentenced to death — is judged by a *beis din* of twenty-three. The textual basis: *ha-shor yisakel ve-gam ba'alav yumat* — the ox shall be stoned and also its owner shall be put to death. *Ke-mitat ha-ba'alim kach mitat ha-shor* — the same way you would execute a person, with a *beis din* of twenty-three, so too you execute the ox.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye challenges Rava: how do we know that *ve-gam ba'alav yumat* is teaching that the ox requires a *beis din* of twenty-three? Perhaps the verse should be read literally — *yumat* means the owner himself is put to death.

### Lighter Penalty

Indeed, the *Gemara* entertains a sophisticated *peshat* reading of the verse: *ve-gam ba'alav yumat* could mean the owner faces a death penalty, but a lighter one than he would have received had he himself done the killing. If a person kills directly with his own hands, his execution is by the sword. Here, since it was his ox that killed, *yumat* — he is executed — but by *chenek* (strangulation), a lighter form of execution. This yields a coherent reading: the owner receives a diminished death penalty commensurate with his indirect responsibility.

### No Death Kofer

The *Gemara* begins to dismantle this reading. It first proposes that even without the word *yumat*, one would never have read *ha-shor yisakel ve-gam ba'alav* to mean *skilah* (stoning) for the owner, because *katil ihu be-sayif* — if he himself had done the killing his penalty would have been the sword — and *mammona be-skilah salka da'atach?* — would you impose a more severe penalty on someone for his ox's act than for his own? That is inconceivable, so *yumat* is not needed to prevent us from reading *skilah*. Yet the *Gemara* then presses further: perhaps *yumat* comes to tell us the owner receives *chenek* rather than the sword — a lenient death rather than no death. What rules this out?

The answer comes from the verse that explicitly authorizes a *kofer* payment: *im kofer yushat alav* — if a ransom is imposed upon him. The *Gemara* initially suggests that this availability of *kofer* proves the owner is not truly subject to execution. But then an audacious counterargument emerges: *adaraba mishum hi gufa* — on the contrary, the *kofer* clause makes perfect sense precisely *because* the owner deserves death. *Lo tikchu kofer le-nefesh rotzeach* — we do not accept a monetary ransom for a murderer who killed with his own hands. But *katal shoro* — when his ox killed, *li-prok nafshaya be-mammona* — then he may redeem himself with money. This is in fact a beautiful *peshat* of the two verses read together: *ve-gam ba'alav yumat* — the owner deserves death — but *im kofer yushat alav* — since he did not do it himself, we permit monetary redemption, which we would otherwise forbid for a killer.

### Chizkiyah Yomus

Chizkiyah resolves the impasse with a teaching from his school: *amar kra yumat ha-makeh rotzeach hu* — the Torah says "he who strikes shall be put to death, for he is a murderer." *Al retzichato atah horgo* — you execute him for *his own* murder. *Ve-i atah horgo al reziichat shoro* — but you do not execute him for his ox's killing. So *yumat* in the *shor ha-niskal* passage cannot be literal execution of the owner; it means something else — namely, that the same procedural standard applied to human capital cases (*beis din* of twenty-three) governs the ox's execution.

### Policy Reasoning

Beyond the textual derivation, it is worth pausing to consider why *chazal* were motivated to resist the *peshat* reading so strongly. The literal reading would mean that a wealthy owner could buy his way out of execution while a poor owner would be put to death — a troubling inequality before the law. Moreover, it would impose capital punishment for negligence rather than deliberate murder, eroding the principle that *al retzichato atah horgo* — only for one's own act is execution warranted. Some *rishonim* suggest that even according to *chazal*'s reading, *ve-gam ba'alav yumat* retains moral force: the owner is *deserving* of death, but the legal system will never impose it, imposing *kofer* instead — analogous to some explanations of *ayin tachat ayin* (monetary compensation rather than literal retaliation), where the verse expresses the moral claim while the practical implementation is monetary.

## Shor Sinai

### Sinai Beis Din

The *Gemara* closes with a question regarding *shor Sinai* — an ox (or person) that drew too close to Mount Sinai during the revelation. The Torah declares *im behemah im ish lo yichyeh* — whether animal or human, it shall not live. The question posed: *shor Sinai be-kama?* — would executing such an animal require a *beis din* of twenty-three?

### Peshat Killing

The *peshat* of the relevant verses is that transgressors at Sinai were to be killed immediately and at a distance — stones hurled or arrows shot — because no one was permitted to touch the mountain: *ki sakol yisakel o yaro yiyareh*. The Rabbis, however, read those same words as establishing the standard punishments of *skilah* (throwing from a height and then stoning) applicable in later generations, and the implication is that even at Sinai those who drew close would have been judged by a *beis din*. The question then is whether the animal would similarly require a *beis din* of twenty-three.

### Rami Yechezkel

Rami bar Yechezkel resolves the question with a *beraita*: *im behemah im ish lo yichyeh* — *mah ish be-khaf-gimel* (23), just as a person is never executed without a *beis din* of twenty-three, *af behemah be-khaf-gimel* — so too an animal. This *drashah* extends the rule from that singular moment at Sinai to all future generations: the procedural requirement of twenty-three for capital cases involving animals is derived from the parallel phrasing that equates the fate of animal and human at Sinai.
