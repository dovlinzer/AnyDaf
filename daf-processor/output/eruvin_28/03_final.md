# Eruvin 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah & Global Statement

### Mishnah Overview

Today's daf is 28, and we pick up from the bottom of 27b. The *mishnah* had established two parallel rules: one may use virtually anything for *eruv*, and virtually anything for *maaser sheni*, with the exception of *maaser sheni* money, water, and salt. The structure of today's discussion follows from those two broad permissions, and the bulk of the daf is devoted to qualifying and limiting them — adding, case by case, to the list of exceptions.

### Reb Yochanan's Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאן דִּמְתַרְגֵּם לִי ״בַּבָּקָר״ אַלִּיבָּא דְּבֶן בַּג בַּג, מוֹבֵילְנָא מָאנֵיהּ אַבָּתְרֵיהּ לְבֵי מַסּוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Whoever interprets** the words **"on oxen" for me in accordance with** the opinion of **ben Bag Bag, I will carry his clothes after him into the bathhouse,** i.e., I will honor him to such an extent that I will be prepared to treat him as a servant treats his master.

Rabbi Yochanan had already cautioned that one should not rely on global statements of this kind, even when they are accompanied by explicit exceptions. His point is that such formulations are inherently imprecise: the "except" clause does not exhaust the exclusions, and the day's learning will bear this out repeatedly. We begin with *maaser sheni* and then circle back to *eruv*.

## Maaser Sheni Fish Brine

### Fish Brine Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דָּגִים אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, הָנֵי דָּגִים — גִּידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע נִינְהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר וְלַד וַלְדוֹת הָאָרֶץ, דָּגִים — מִמַּיָּא אִיבְּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** these two *baraitot*? **Abaye said: There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to **fish. According to the one who said** that we apply the law to that which is **the produce of produce and grown from the ground, fish are** regarded as having **grown from the ground.** However, **according to the one who said** we apply it to that which is **the offspring of the offspring of the earth, fish were created from water.** Therefore, they are not of the offspring of the earth, and consequently they are not included among the items that may be purchased with second-tithe money.

The *Gemara* opens with a *tannaim* dispute concerning fish brine. Two *tannaim* both exclude plain salt water but debate whether fish brine is included within the scope of *maaser sheni* purchases. Both, however, agree that actual fish are permissible. A third view holds that fish are excluded entirely. The *Gemara* traces this disagreement to a fundamental difference in hermeneutical method applied to the *pasuk* in Devarim: *venatata hakesef bechol asher te'aveh nafshecha babakar uvatzon uvayayin uvashichar uv'chol asher tish'alcha nafshecha* — "you shall give the money for whatever your soul desires: cattle, sheep, wine, or strong drink, or whatever your soul asks." The structure of this verse is: a general statement, a specific list, and another general statement.

### Hermeneutical Schools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי דָּרְשִׁי כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַתָּה הַכֶּסֶף בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ״ — כָּלַל, ״בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן וּבַיַּיִן וּבַשֵּׁכָר״ — פָּרַט, ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׁאׇלְךָ נַפְשֶׁךָ״ — חָזַר וְכָלַל. כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט. וּמָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ: פְּרִי מִפְּרִי, וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, אַף כֹּל פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** other ***tanna'im* expound** the verse according to the principle of **generalizations and details, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: The phrase: **"And you shall bestow the money on all that your heart desires"** is **a generalization,** as no particular type of food is specified. The phrase: **"On cattle, on sheep, on wine, and on strong drink"** is **a detail,** as specific types of food are mentioned. When the verse concludes: **"On whatever your soul requests," it has generalized again,** as no specific type of food is mentioned. Since the verse is formulated as **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization, you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail. Just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as the produce of produce,** i.e., not only the produce itself but also things that come from it, such as the calf that comes from a cow or grapes from a seed, **and** they are also things **grown from the ground,** as all of these items grow from the ground or receive their main sustenance from it, **so** it includes **all** things that are **the produce of produce and are grown from the ground.**

The question is how to apply hermeneutical principles to this three-part structure. The school of Rebbi Yishmael employs the rule of *klal u'prat u'klal* — general, particular, general — which yields a category defined by similarity to the specific middle list. The school of Rebbi Akiva, by contrast, applies *ribuy u'mi'ut u'ribuy* — inclusion, exclusion, inclusion — which produces a considerably broader category. Under *ribuy u'mi'ut u'ribuy*, once there is a *ribuy* and a *mi'ut*, the second *ribuy* implies that virtually everything is included except what is most dissimilar from the list — which would mean only things like water and salt, substances that belong to neither the animal nor the plant world. Under *klal u'prat u'klal*, however, the derived category must be *ke'ein ha'prat*, similar to the specific items enumerated, and the analysis becomes considerably more restrictive.

### Pri Mipri & Mushrooms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ: וְלַד וַלְדוֹת הָאָרֶץ, אַף כֹּל וְלַד וַלְדוֹת הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught in another** *baraita*: **Just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as the offspring of the offspring of the earth,** i.e., things that come from items that came from the ground, **so** it includes **all** things that are **the offspring of the offspring of the earth.**

The *Gemara* now asks: what, precisely, is "similar" to the *prat* according to those who apply *klal u'prat u'klal*? The answer given is *mah ha'prat meforash pri mipri ve'gidulei karka* — just as the specific items in the list are a "fruit from a fruit" and grow from the ground, so too anything derived from such a category is included. The term *pri mipri* is not limited to literal fruit in its natural state; as Rashi in Bava Kamma explains, it encompasses things produced from fruit — wine, for instance, is the product of grapes and qualifies as *pri mipri*. The requirement of *gidulei karka* means the item must derive from something that grows from the ground.

This formulation excludes mushrooms on two grounds: first, a mushroom does not come from another mushroom in the ordinary reproductive sense; second, and more compellingly, mushrooms grow from decomposing matter and do not draw their nutrients from the ground the way ordinary plants do. They are not truly *gidulei karka*. Rashi in Bava Kamma states this explicitly, and notably, the *braita* that appears at the beginning of the *Sifra* — the *braita* of Rebbi Yishmael's thirteen hermeneutical principles — uses precisely this case, *maaser sheni* and the exclusion of mushrooms, as the paradigmatic illustration of *klal u'prat u'klal*.

### Vlad Vladot & Fish

A second *braita* formulates the same rule differently: *mah ha'prat meforash vlad vladot aretz* — just as the specific items are "descendants of the descendants of the land," so too the full category must satisfy that description. One might assume that *vlad vladot aretz* and *pri mipri* are simply two ways of expressing the same idea, but the *Gemara* asks whether there is a practical difference between them.

Abaye answers: *dagim*, fish, constitute the point of difference. According to the formulation *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka*, fish might be included, since one could argue that they live off of things that ultimately draw their nourishment from the ground. Tosafot notes in multiple places that *gidulei karka* can be understood in either a narrow sense — things that literally spring from the earth, like plants — or in a broader sense — things whose nutritional chain traces back to the ground. If the broader reading is intended, fish, which feed on vegetation in the water or on other fish that do so, might qualify. The *Gemara* will proceed to challenge this.

According to the formulation of *vlad vladot ha'aretz*, however, the critical question is not how the creature currently sustains itself but how it was created in *maaseh bereishit*. Land animals were fashioned from the ground; fish, *dagim mi'maya ivru*, were created from the water. Accordingly, fish are excluded under *vlad vladot ha'aretz* in a way they might not be under *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka*.

### Objection to Abaye's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר אַבָּיֵי דָּגִים גִּידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע נִינְהוּ? וְהָאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אָכַל פּוּטִיתָא — לוֹקֶה אַרְבַּע. נְמָלָה — לוֹקֶה חָמֵשׁ. צִירְעָה — לוֹקֶה שֵׁשׁ. וְאִם אִיתָא — פּוּטִיתָא נָמֵי לִילְקֵי מִשּׁוּם ״הַשֶּׁרֶץ הַשֹּׁרֵץ עַל הָאָרֶץ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Did Abaye say** that **fish are** regarded as having been **grown from the ground? Didn't Abaye say the following?** If **one ate a *putita*,** a certain water insect, **he is given four** sets of **lashes,** as he has transgressed four separate negative Torah commandments, two that relate to creeping animals in general and two that relate to water insects in particular. If he ate **an ant, he is given five** sets of **lashes** for violating the two general prohibitions and another three negative commandments stated with regard to insects that creep upon the earth. If he ate **a hornet, he is given six** sets of **lashes,** for in addition to the prohibitions applying to an ant, he has transgressed a prohibition stated with regard to flying insects. **And if it is** correct that something that lives in water is considered as growing from the ground, one who eats **a *putita* should also be given lashes for** violating the following prohibition: "And **every creeping thing that creeps upon the earth** is a detestable thing; it shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 11:41). Rather, fish must certainly not be considered as growing from the ground, and therefore this explanation is to be rejected.

The *Gemara* then questions Abaye's premise. Would Abaye truly classify fish as *gidulei karka*? Abaye himself has a famous statement at the top of 28a: one who eats a *putita* — a water insect — receives four lashes, corresponding to four distinct prohibitions. The count depends on the creature being classified as a water creature rather than a land creature. If fish and water insects were considered *gidulei karka*, a water insect would also fall under the prohibition of *sheretz ha'shoretz al ha'aretz* and the lash count would increase. Since Abaye does not include that prohibition in his count, he evidently does not treat water creatures as *gidulei karka*. Ravina therefore proposes a different answer.

### Birds & Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא: עוֹפוֹת אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, הָנֵי נָמֵי — גִּידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע נִינְהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר וְלַד וַלְדוֹת הָאָרֶץ, הָנֵי עוֹפוֹת — מִן הָרְקָק נִבְרְאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Ravina said: There is** a practical difference **between** the two *baraitot* with regard to **fowl. According to the one who said** that one may use second-tithe money only to purchase food which is **the produce of produce and grown from the ground, these** fowl **are also** regarded as having **grown from the ground.** However, **according to the one who said** that we apply it to that which is **the offspring of the offspring of the earth, these fowl were created from mud** and not from the ground, and consequently they are not included among the items that may be bought with second-tithe money.

Ravina suggests that *ofos*, birds, are the true point of difference between the two formulations. Under *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka*, birds are included: they currently live off of what grows from the ground and satisfy the criterion of *gidulei karka* in the broader sense. Under *vlad vladot ha'aretz*, however, the question is how they were created, and here the *Gemara* invokes a well-known textual difficulty. In the first chapter of Bereishit, birds appear to have been created from the water: *yishretzu hamayim sheretz nefesh chaya ve'of ye'ofef al ha'aretz*. Yet in the second chapter, the verse states: *vayitzer Hashem Elokim min ha'adama kol chayat ha'sadeh ve'et kol of ha'shamayim* — He formed from the ground all the beasts of the field and all the birds of the heavens. How can birds be said to have been formed from the ground if the earlier account created them from the water?

The *Gemara*'s resolution is that birds were created from *rakak* — mud, a mixture of water and earth. Mud is not simply ground, and therefore birds do not qualify as *vlad vladot ha'aretz* in the full sense. Accordingly, the practical difference between the two formulations is this: *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka* includes birds but excludes fish; *vlad vladot ha'aretz* excludes both. On fish, both approaches agree on exclusion.

### Abstraction Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דִּמְרַבֵּי עוֹפוֹת, קָסָבַר: כְּלָלָא בָּתְרָא דַּוְקָא. פְּרָט וּכְלָל — נַעֲשֶׂה כְּלָל מוּסָף עַל הַפְּרָט, וְאִיתְרַבּוּ לְהוּ כׇּל מִילֵּי. וְאַהְנִי כְּלָלָא קַמָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי כֹּל דְּלָא דָּמֵי לֵיהּ מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The one who includes fowl holds** that when there is a generalization, a detail, and another generalization, **the latter generalization is primary.** Therefore, the rule is similar to that governing **a detail** followed by **a generalization,** which maintains that **the generalization is considered an addition to the detail, and all** other **items are included.** However, **the first generalization is effective in excluding anything that is not similar to** it **in two respects,** as it is nonetheless a case of a generalization, a detail, and a generalization. Therefore, he excludes anything that does not grow from the ground and is not the produce of produce.

This leaves an important question. Both formulations belong to the *klal u'prat u'klal* school — they agree that the derived category must resemble the *prat*. Why then does one formulation yield a broader category (including birds) while the other yields a narrower one (excluding birds)? The *Gemara* asks: *man de'amar be'ofos mai ta'ama u'man de'miat ofos mai ta'ama* — what is the underlying reasoning of each position?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דִּמְמַעֵט עוֹפוֹת, קָסָבַר: כְּלָלָא קַמָּא דַּוְוקָא, כְּלָל וּפְרָט — וְאֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט: הָנֵי — אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא — לָא. וְאַהְנִי כְּלָלָא בָּתְרָא לְרַבּוֹיֵי כֹּל דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה צְדָדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And the one who excludes fowl holds** that **the first generalization is primary.** Therefore, a generalization, detail, and generalization is similar to a single **generalization** that is followed by **a detail,** with regard to which we maintain that **the generalization only includes that which is** spelled out **in the detail.** Therefore, with regard to **these** items mentioned in the verse, **yes,** one may purchase them with second-tithe money. With regard to **something else, no,** one may not. However, **the latter generalization is effective to include anything that is similar to it in three respects,** namely, it is the produce of produce, grows from the ground, and is offspring of the offspring of the earth, to the exclusion of fowl.

The *Gemara*'s answer is subtle. Even within the framework of *klal u'prat u'klal*, one can ask which building block is primary. One approach takes the first *klal u'prat* as the foundational unit: that unit, in isolation, yields only the *prat* — a very narrow list. The subsequent *klal* then broadens it, but only moderately, because one is expanding something small. The result is a list that requires resemblance in three characteristics — *pri mipri*, *gidulei karka*, and *vlad vladot ha'aretz*. This is the position of *klala kamma davka*: the first *klal* is the operative anchor.

The other approach takes the *prat u'klal* as the primary building block. That unit, in isolation, yields the *klal* — a very expansive list. The first *klal* then contracts it, but one is contracting something already very large, so the final list remains broad. The result requires resemblance in only two characteristics. This is *klala basra davka*: the final *klal* is the operative anchor. The former position excludes birds; the latter includes them.

The overall upshot of this entire hermeneutical analysis is that the *mishnah*'s sweeping statement — that one may use *maaser sheni* money for anything — is far more limited than it first appears. Even granting the most expansive reading within *klal u'prat u'klal*, mushrooms and fish are excluded. On the view of *klala kamma davka*, birds are excluded as well. The *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah* who said "anything" may well have been operating according to the Rebbi Akiva school of *ribuy u'mi'ut u'ribuy*, for whom only water and salt fall outside the category — which is why the exception he listed was so limited.

## Eruv Edibility Rules

### Eatable vs. Uneatable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: מְעָרְבִין בְּפַעְפּוּעִין וּבַחֲלַגְלוֹגוֹת וּבְגוּדְגְּדָנִיּוֹת, אֲבָל לֹא בַּחֲזִיז וְלֹא בְּכַפְנִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat,** who said **in the name** of **Rav: One may establish an *eiruv*** with cheap and unimportant produce such as **cress, purslane, and sweet clover, but** one may **not** establish an *eiruv* **with green grain or with unripe dates.**

The discussion now shifts to *eruv*. Rav Yehuda, in the name of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat, in the name of Rav, states: one may make an *eruv* with *papuin* (cress), with *chalgoogloit* (purslane), and with *gud gadaniyot* (sweet clover). One may not make an *eruv* with *chaziz* (green corn or green wheat, the immature stalk of grain cut before it has fully developed) or with *chafniyot* (unripe dates). The criterion implicit in this list is fitness for human consumption: things that are genuinely edible qualify, while things used primarily as animal fodder, or that are insufficiently ripe, do not.

### Clover Sterilization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְגוּדְגְּדָנִיּוֹת מִי מְעָרְבִין? וְהָתַנְיָא: גּוּדְגְּדָנִיּוֹת — מְרוּבֵּי בָנִים יֹאכֵלוּ, חֲשׂוּכֵי בָנִים לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ. וְאִם הוּקְשׁוּ לְזֶרַע — אַף מְרוּבֵּי בָּנִים לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But may one establish an *eiruv* with sweet clover? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to **sweet clover** that **those who have many children may eat** it, but **those without children may not eat** it, as it is harmful to one's reproductive capacity; **and if it was hardened into seed,** i.e., if it became very hard and already fit to be planted, **even those who have many children may not eat** it? Therefore, we see that it is prohibited to eat sweet clover. How can it possibly be used to establish an *eiruv*?

The *Gemara* immediately questions the inclusion of sweet clover. A *braita* teaches that sweet clover promotes sterility in men. The recommendation is that those who have many children may eat it — *gud gadaniyot marbe banim, merube banim yochlu* — while those who have no children should not — *chashuchei banim lo yochlu*. And if the plant has hardened into seed, even those with many children should avoid it for general health reasons. How then can sweet clover be classified as fit for human consumption and therefore usable for *eruv*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמָא: אַשֶּׁלֹא הוּקְשׁוּ לְזֶרַע, וּמְרוּבֵּי בָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Interpret** Rav's statement as referring to sweet clover **that was not** yet **hardened into seed, and** its use for establishing an *eiruv* is limited to **those who have many children** and are therefore permitted to eat it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם לַחֲשׂוּכֵי בָנִים, דְּהָא חֲזוּ לִמְרוּבֵּי בָנִים. מִי לָא תְּנַן: מְעָרְבִין לַנָּזִיר בְּיַיִן וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל בִּתְרוּמָה. אַלְמָא: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא חֲזֵי לְהַאי — חֲזֵי לְהַאי. הָכָא נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא חֲזֵי לְהַאי — חֲזֵי לְהַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish,** you can **say** instead that **actually,** sweet clover is fit for an *eiruv* even **for those without children because it is fit** to be eaten by **those who have many children.** The food used for an *eiruv* must be edible, but it does not need to be edible for the particular person using it as his *eiruv*. **Didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One may establish an *eiruv* for a nazirite with wine, and for an Israelite with *teruma*? Apparently,** these items may be used as an *eiruv* **even though** they **are not fit for this** person, because they are **fit for that** other person. **Here too, even though** the sweet clover **is not fit for this** person, it may be used because **it is fit for that** other person.

The *Gemara* offers three responses. First, the statement permitting its use for *eruv* refers specifically to before the plant has hardened into seed, and to individuals who are not concerned about its reproductive effects. Second — and this is the more conceptually significant answer — even a person who cannot eat something may use it for *eruv* if it is food for others. The *mishnah* itself provides the precedent: one may make an *eruv* for a *nazir* using wine, and for an Israelite using *terumah*, even though the *nazir* may not drink wine and a non-*kohen* may not eat *terumah*. The principle is *af al gav de'lo chazi le'hai chazi le'hai* — even if it is not fit for this one, it is fit for that one, and that suffices to define it as food. The same logic applies here: sweet clover is fit for those without fertility concerns. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי קָאָמַר רַב, בְּהַנְדְּקוֹקֵי מָדָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish,** you can **say** instead: **When Rav said** that sweet clover may be used for an *eiruv*, he was referring to **Median clover,** which is of superior quality and is not harmful.

Third, Rav's original statement permitting its use for *eruv* refers specifically to the Median variety of sweet clover, which poses no health concerns at all.

### Green Corn in Bavel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַחֲזִיז לָא? וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כְּשׁוּת וַחֲזִיז מְעָרְבִין בָּהֶן, וּמְבָרְכִין עֲלֵיהֶן ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers the continuation of Rav's statement: **And** may one **not** establish an *eiruv* **with green grain? Didn't Rav Yehuda say that Rav said:** In the case of **dodder and green grain, one may establish an *eiruv* with them; and** when eating **them one recites the blessing: Who creates the fruit of the ground?**

The *Gemara* then turns to *chaziz*, green corn, which was listed as impermissible for *eruv*. Yet Rav Yehuda himself, in the name of the same Rav, states elsewhere that *kshuta ve'chaziz* — hops and green corn — may be used for *eruv*, and one recites *borei pri ha'adama* over them, since they grow from the ground and are fit for eating. There is thus a direct contradiction within Rav's own rulings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — מִקַּמֵּי דַּאֲתָא רַב לְבָבֶל, הָא — לְבָתַר דַּאֲתָא רַב לְבָבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** first statement, according to which green grain may not be used for an *eiruv*, was made **before Rav came to Babylonia. That** second statement was made **after Rav came to Babylonia** and saw that people there ate green grain, at which point he ruled that it is fit to be used for an *eiruv*.

The *Gemara* resolves this: the statement prohibiting *chaziz* was issued before Rav came to Bavel, when he was living in Eretz Yisrael, where green corn was not consumed by humans and was considered animal fodder. Once he arrived in Bavel and observed that people there do eat it, he revised his position. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָבֶל הָוְיָא רוּבָּא דְּעָלְמָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַפּוֹל וְהַשְּׂעוֹרָה וְהַתִּילְתָּן שֶׁזְּרָעָן לְיָרָק — בָּטְלָה דַּעְתּוֹ אֵצֶל כׇּל אָדָם. לְפִיכָךְ: זַרְעָן חַיָּיב, וְיַרְקָן פָּטוּר. הַשַּׁחֲלַיִים וְהַגַּרְגִּיר שֶׁזְּרָעָן לְיָרָק — מִתְעַשְּׂרִין יָרָק וְזֶרַע, זְרָעָן לְזֶרַע — מִתְעַשְּׂרִין זֶרַע וְיָרָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is Babylonia the majority of the world?** Laws are established according to the custom prevalent in most of the world. **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **beans, barley, and fenugreek that** one **planted** in order to use **as an herb,** e.g., as animal fodder, **his opinion is rendered irrelevant by the opinions of all other people?** Since most people do not act this way, we do not consider this particular person's intention to be significant. **Therefore, one is obligated** to tithe **their seeds, and their herbs are exempt.** When one harvests these plants in their green state, before their seeds have matured, they are regarded as not having fully ripened. However, in the case of **cress and arugula,** which are commonly eaten both in their green state and as seeds, if **one planted them** in order to use them **as herbs, they are tithed** both **as herbs and as seeds;** if **one planted them for** their **seeds, they are tithed as seeds and as herbs,** no matter how they are eaten. In any case, the first part of the *baraita* teaches that the law is determined in accordance with the common custom of most of the world and not with the practice in one particular place.

The *Gemara* objects to this resolution: is Bavel the majority of the world? Why should local Babylonian practice override the general human norm?

### Intention Changes

The objection draws on a *braita* about *pul* (beans), *se'ora* (barley), and *tilson* (fenugreek). These plants are ordinarily cultivated for their seeds. If one planted them with the intention of harvesting them as vegetables — eating their leaves and stalks — *batla da'ato etzel kol adam*: the individual's idiosyncratic intention is voided by the universal norm. The legal consequence is that *ma'aser* obligation attaches to their seeds but not to their vegetable portion, regardless of what the planter intended. One cannot redefine what something is by deciding to use it differently than everybody else does.

By contrast, *shachalayim* (cress) and *gargir* (arugula) are plants that people commonly eat both as vegetables and for their seeds, so intention does matter for them. If one planted them as vegetables, the vegetable character is primary; if for seed, the seed character is primary. But for the grains that are universally understood as seed crops, the individual who plants them as vegetables is simply ignored.

The implication for *chaziz* is sharp: if the majority of the world considers it animal food, the fact that people in Bavel eat it should be dismissed the same way idiosyncratic individual intentions are dismissed. The *Gemara* does not fully resolve the tension here. The answer ultimately given is that when Rav permitted *chaziz* in Bavel, he was referring to a specific cultivated variety — *digunaisa*, the garden variety of *chaziz* — which is universally eaten and unambiguously considered human food. That resolves the contradiction: the prohibition applied to the wild variety; the permission applies to the garden variety.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר רַב, בִּדְגִנּוּנְיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** he was referring **to the garden variety,** which is commonly eaten.

The question of whether Bavel's widespread practice suffices to alter the legal category is left somewhat open. Tosafot notes the difficulty: Bavel is not a few individuals but an entire large region, and it is counterintuitive to dismiss an entire country's food culture as *batla da'ato*. The distinction the *Gemara* seems to draw is between something whose basic definition is animal food — where even a whole country of human consumers cannot redefine it — and something whose basic definition is human food, where secondary reasons that prevent certain individuals from eating it (allergies, religious restrictions, health concerns) are irrelevant because the item's fundamental character remains edible.

### Child's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא כִּי הֲוָה חֲלִישׁ מִגִּרְסֵיהּ, הֲוָה אָזֵיל וְיָתֵיב אַפִּיתְחָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה בַּר אַמֵּי, אָמַר: כִּי נָפְקִי וְעָיְלִי רַבָּנַן — אֵיקוּם מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ וַאֲקַבֵּל בְּהוּ אַגְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **when Rabbi Zeira was exhausted from his studies, he would go and sit at the entrance** to the academy **of Rav Yehuda bar Ami,** and **say: When the Sages go in and out, I shall stand up before them and receive reward for** honoring **them,** as it is a mitzva to honor Torah scholars. Too tired to engage in actual Torah study, he sought a way to rest while fulfilling a different mitzva at the same time.

While on the topic of hops and green corn, the *Gemara* relates a charming episode. Rabbi Zeira, when he needed a break from learning and wanted to accumulate *s'char* even during his rest, would sit by the doorway of Rav Yehuda bar Ami and rise in honor of the rabbinic students passing in and out of the *beis medrash*. On one such occasion, a schoolchild emerged. Rabbi Zeira asked what his teacher had taught him. The child said: for *kashus* (hops), one recites *borei pri ha'adama*; for *chaziz* (green corn), one recites *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*. This accords with what we have seen: *chaziz* is not fully fit for eating and therefore warrants only *shehakol*, while hops are fit and warrant *borei pri ha'adama*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַק אֲתָא יָנוֹקָא דְּבֵי רַב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אַגְמְרָךְ רַבָּךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּשׁוּת ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה״, חֲזִיז ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִיסְתַּבְּרָא, הַאי — מֵאַרְעָא קָא מִרַבֵּי, וְהַאי — מֵאַוֵּירָא קָא מִרַבֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** Once, a young **school child was leaving** the study hall. Rabbi Zeira **said to him: What did your teacher teach you** today? **He said to him:** The proper blessing for **dodder** is: **Who creates the fruit of the ground;** the proper blessing for **green grain** is: **By Whose word all things came to be.** Rabbi Zeira **said to him: On the contrary, the opposite is** more **reasonable,** as **this,** the green grain, **derives nourishment from the ground, whereas that,** the dodder, **derives nourishment from the air,** and it is fitting to recite a blessing over each item in accordance with its source of nourishment.

Rabbi Zeira challenged the ruling: *adaraba ipcha mistabra* — the opposite seems more logical. Green corn grows directly from the ground and should therefore merit *borei pri ha'adama*. Hops, by contrast, grow as a parasitic plant, drawing their nourishment from a host plant rather than directly from the soil, and should therefore receive only *shehakol*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּיָנוֹקָא דְּבֵי רַב. מַאי טַעְמָא: הַאי — גְּמַר פֵּירֵי, וְהַאי — לָאו גְּמַר פֵּירֵי. וּמַאי דְּקָאָמְרַתְּ: הַאי מֵאַרְעָא קָא רָבֵי, וְהַאי מֵאַוֵּירָא קָא רָבֵי — לָא הִיא. כְּשׁוּת נָמֵי מֵאַרְעָא קָא רָבֵי, דְּהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּקָטְלִינַן לַהּ לְהִיזְמְתָא וּמָיְיתָא כְּשׁוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **The *halakha* is in accordance with the** young **school child. What is the reason** for this? **This,** the dodder, is **fully ripened produce, and that,** green grain, is **not fully ripened produce.** If produce is not fully ripened one can only recite the blessing: By Whose word all things came to be. **And that which you said: This,** the green grain, **derives nourishment from the ground, whereas that,** the dodder, **derives nourishment from the air, this is not so. Dodder also derives nourishment from the ground, for we see that when the** prickly **shrub is cut off, the dodder** attached to it **dies.** This shows that dodder also derives its nourishment from the ground, albeit indirectly.

The *Gemara* rules: *ve'hilchata k'yanuka d'vei Rav* — the law follows the schoolchild. *Hai gmar peira* — hops are a completed, ripened fruit, and therefore receive *borei pri ha'adama*. *Lav gmar peira* — green corn at this stage is not a completed fruit; it is not only unripe but, as established earlier, not even considered human food at this stage, meriting only *shehakol*. As for Rabbi Zeira's objection that hops grow from the air rather than the ground: the *Gemara* rejects this. *Keshuta nami me'ara ka'rabei* — hops also grow from the ground, indirectly. We can see this empirically: if you cut down the host bush, the hops die. This demonstrates that the hops are drawing real nourishment from the host plant, which in turn draws it from the ground — and that chain is sufficient to call them *gidulei karka* and to warrant *borei pri ha'adama*. This is also what distinguishes hops from mushrooms, which can grow on decaying wood entirely disconnected from the ground.

### Unripe Dates Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכַפְנִיּוֹת אֵין מְעָרְבִין? וְהָתַנְיָא: קוֹר נִיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין. וְכַפְנִיּוֹת נִקָּחוֹת בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וּמִטַּמְּאוֹת טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now considers the next part of Rav's statement: **And** is it correct that **one may not establish an *eiruv* with unripe dates? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Heart of palm,** the soft, edible inner core of a palm tree, **may be bought with** second-**tithe money; but it does not contract the** ritual **impurity of foods,** as it is not actually a food, but rather a part of the tree itself. **And unripe dates may be bought with** second-**tithe money, and** they even **contract the** ritual **impurity of foods.**

The *Gemara* returns to *chafniyot*, unripe dates, which were listed as impermissible for *eruv*. A *braita* complicates this: *kore* (heart of palm) may be purchased with *maaser sheni* money, but does not contract the ritual impurity of foodstuffs (*tum'at ochlin*). Unripe dates, by contrast, may also be purchased with *maaser sheni* money and *do* contract *tum'at ochlin* — they are therefore considered food. If they are food, why can they not be used for *eruv*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבֵי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: קוֹר הֲרֵי הוּא כְּעֵץ לְכׇל דְּבָרָיו — אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וְכַפְנִיּוֹת הֲרֵי הֵן כִּפְרִי לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם — אֶלָּא שֶׁפְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says** this somewhat differently: **Heart of palm is like a tree in all its** legal **aspects, except that it may be bought with** second-**tithe money,** as it is edible. **And unripe dates are like fruit in all regards,** as they are actual fruit, **except** with respect to one characteristic, which is **that they are exempt from tithes** because they are not yet fully ripened.

Rabi Yehuda in the same *braita* adds his position: *kore* is like wood in all respects except that it may be purchased with *maaser sheni* money. As for unripe dates, they are fully a fruit in all respects except that they are exempt from *ma'aser* — because they have not yet reached the stage of full ripeness that triggers *ma'aser* obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, בִּדְנִיסְחָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** the *baraita* is referring to the **fruit** of palms that **never** fully **ripen**. They are therefore regarded as full-fledged fruit even in their unripe state. Rav, however, was referring to the fruit of palms, which eventually ripen. Their unripe state is merely a transitional stage in their development.

The *Gemara* initially suggests that the *braita*'s statements about unripe dates refer to the male palm variety, which never ripens and is therefore always in the same state. For that variety, even the unripe stage is the final and consumable form, and it is unambiguously food. The *Gemara* rejects this: if the male variety were meant, Rabbi Yehuda would never have exempted it from *ma'aser*, because for a variety that never develops further, whatever stage it has reached is its maximum — and Rabbi Yehuda himself explicitly rules elsewhere that the unripe dates of certain specific locations, such as the dates of Tuvina, are *chayav* in *ma'aser*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בְּהָא לֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא הוּזְכְּרוּ פַּגֵּי בֵיתְיוֹנֵי אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן מַעֲשֵׂר בִּלְבַד. פַּגֵּי בֵיתְיוֹנֵי וַאֲהִינֵי דְטוֹבִינָא חַיָּיבִין בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, would Rabbi Yehuda say with regard to this** that **they are exempt from tithes? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: The unripe figs of** the place called **Beityoni were only mentioned with regard to tithes,** as it was stated: In the case of **the unripe figs of Beityoni, and the unripe dates of** the place called **Tuvina, one is obligated to tithe them** even though they never ripen, since they are considered full-fledged fruit in all respects?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם לָאו בְּנִיסְחָנֵי, וּלְעִנְיַן טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין שָׁאנֵי, כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְמַתְּקָן עַל יְדֵי הָאוּר, הָכָא נָמֵי: הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְמַתְּקָן עַל יְדֵי הָאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say as follows: **Actually,** the *baraita* **is not referring to** the **fruit** of palms that **never** fully **ripen,** but rather to the fruit of palms that eventually ripen. However, the *halakha* **pertaining to the** ritual **impurity of foods is different,** and an item's status as a food with regard to the impurity of foods cannot be brought as proof of its status as a food with regard to an *eiruv*. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said** elsewhere: **Since they are fit to be sweetened through** cooking with **fire,** they are regarded as food for the purpose of tithes; **here too,** we can say: **Since they are fit to be sweetened through** cooking with **fire,** unripe dates are fit to contract the impurity of foods. However, with regard to an *eiruv*, we require food that is ready for consumption, and something that can be prepared to become food is not sufficient.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that both the *braita* and our original ruling refer to the ordinary female palm variety. The resolution rests on an important distinction introduced by Rabbi Yochanan: *ho'il ve'ra'uy l'matkan al yedei ha'ur* — since they can be sweetened by cooking them over fire, they qualify as food for purposes of *tum'at ochlin* and for redemption with *maaser sheni* money. But *eruv* imposes a stricter standard: the food must be fit for eating in exactly the state it is in right now, without requiring any preparation. Unripe dates that need to be cooked before they are palatable fail this test. Raw meat provides a parallel: one may certainly use *maaser sheni* money to purchase raw meat, but one may not use raw meat for *eruv*, because it is not currently fit for immediate consumption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא אִתְּמַר דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? אַהָא דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁקֵדִים הַמָּרִים — קְטַנִּים חַיָּיבִין, גְּדוֹלִים פְּטוּרִין. מְתוּקִים — גְּדוֹלִים חַיָּיבִין, קְטַנִּים פְּטוּרִין. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: זֶה וָזֶה, לִפְטוּר. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: זֶה וָזֶה לְחִיּוּב. אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: הוֹרָה רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בְּצִיפּוֹרִי כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר זֶה וְזֶה לִפְטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Where was** this comment of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** originally **stated?** The Gemara answers: It was stated **on this** ruling, **which was taught** in a *baraita*: One is **obligated** to tithe **bitter almonds** while they are still **small** and green, as they are fit to be eaten while still undeveloped. When they are **large,** however, one is **exempt** from tithing them, as they are no longer edible. One is **obligated to** tithe **sweet, large** almonds, whereas one is **exempt from** tithing **small ones,** as they have not yet fully ripened. **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yosei, said in the name of his father:** One is **exempt from** tithing **both this and that,** large and small bitter almonds. **And some say** that he said in the name of his father: One is **obligated to** tithe **both this and that. Rabbi Ila said: Rabbi Ḥanina ruled in Tzippori in accordance with the one who said:** One is **exempt from** tithing **both this and that.**

The context for Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is a *braita* about *shkedim ha'marim*, bitter almonds. When small and unripe, bitter almonds are still edible and are therefore obligated in *ma'aser*; when large and ripe, they are bitter and inedible and are therefore exempt. Sweet almonds follow the opposite pattern: large ones are obligated; small, unripe ones are exempt. Rabbi Shimon bar Yossi in the name of his father says both sizes of the bitter variety are exempt. Others say both are obligated. Rabbi Yochanan's statement responds to the latter position: how can the large, bitter almonds be obligated if they are inedible?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר זֶה וָזֶה לְחִיּוּב, לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְמַתְּקָן עַל יְדֵי הָאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to** the view of **the one who said** one is **obligated to** tithe **both this and that, for what are** large, bitter almonds **suitable? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Since** these almonds **are fit to be sweetened** and made edible **through** cooking with **fire,** they are regarded as food for the purpose of tithes.

*Ho'il ve'ra'uy l'matkan al yedei ha'ur* — since they can be made palatable by cooking, they count as food for *ma'aser* and *tum'at ochlin* purposes. But that leniency does not extend to *eruv*, where immediate edibility is required.

## Heart of Palm

### Kore as Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: קוֹר הֲרֵי הוּא כְּעֵץ לְכׇל דְּבָרָיו, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר. הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further examines the *baraita* cited earlier. **The Master said** that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Heart of palm is like a tree in all its** legal **aspects, except that it may be bought with** second-**tithe money.** The Gemara asks: Rabbi Yehuda's opinion **is** identical to that of **the first *tanna*.**

Having addressed unripe dates, the *Gemara* returns to the statement about *kore*, the heart of palm. Rabbi Yehuda's ruling was: *kore* is like wood in all respects, except that it may be purchased with *maaser sheni* money. This appears to say exactly what the first *tanna* said — namely, that it is purchasable with *maaser sheni* money but not considered food for *tum'at ochlin* purposes.

### Boiling & Frying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁלָקוֹ וְטִגְּנוֹ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: There is** a practical difference **between them** in a case where **one boiled or fried** the heart of palm. According to Rabbi Yehuda, it does not contract the ritual impurity of foods even if it was boiled or fried, whereas the first *tanna* holds that in that case it does contract impurity.

Abaye explains the difference between them: *shulka ve'tigna ika beinaihu* — boiling and frying. According to Rabbi Yehuda, who categorically declares *kore* to be wood, even boiling and frying it would not change its status. It would be, as it were, fried wood. According to the first *tanna*, who does not make such a categorical declaration, perhaps once the heart of palm has been sufficiently prepared and rendered genuinely edible, it would acquire the status of food for *tum'at ochlin* purposes.

### Rava on Bracha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: מִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁלָקוֹ וְטִגְּנוֹ לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: הָעוֹר וְהַשִּׁילְיָא אֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין. עוֹר שֶׁשְּׁלָקוֹ, וְשִׁילְיָא שֶׁחִישֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ — מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava strongly objects to this: Is there** really **anyone who said** that even if **one boiled or fried it,** it does **not** contract the ritual impurity of foods? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The hide and the placenta** of an animal, which people do not typically eat, **do not contract the** ritual **impurity of foods;** however, **a hide that one boiled** until it became edible **and a placenta that one intended** to eat **do contract the impurity of foods?** This indicates that even something not originally fit to be eaten contracts the impurity of foods once it has been boiled or fried, and the same should apply to heart of palm according to all opinions.

Rava challenges Abaye's distinction. Would anyone truly say that boiling and frying fails to confer food status? A *braita* explicitly addresses the case of animal hide and placenta: hide is not food and does not receive *tum'at ochlin*; but hide that has been boiled, or a placenta that one intended to eat, does receive *tum'at ochlin*. Even hide — something that no one thinks of as food in its ordinary state — becomes food once sufficiently prepared. It strains credulity to say that boiled and fried heart of palm would not be food for anyone.

### Rav Yehuda vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ בְּרָכָה. דְּאִתְּמַר: קוֹר — רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה״, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to **the blessing** that must be recited prior to eating, **for it was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed about the blessing recited over heart of palm: **Rav Yehuda said** the appropriate blessing is: **Who creates the fruit of the ground. And Shmuel said** the appropriate blessing is: **By Whose word all things came to be.**

Rava therefore proposes a different resolution: *ika beinaihu bracha* — the operative difference between the two opinions is the blessing one recites over *kore*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה״ — אוּכְלָא הוּא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ״ — כֵּיוָן שֶׁסּוֹפוֹ לְהַקְשׁוֹת, לָא מְבָרְכִינַן עִילָּוֵיהּ ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two opinions: **Rav Yehuda said** the appropriate blessing is: **Who creates the fruit of the ground, as it is food.** Since heart of palm is edible it is called a fruit, and we recite a blessing over it in the manner of all fruits. **And Shmuel said** the appropriate blessing is: **By Whose word all things came to be. Since it will eventually harden** and become like an inedible tree, **we do not recite over it the blessing: Who creates the fruit of the ground,** as it will eventually lose the status of a fruit.

The debate over the *bracha* on *kore* is attributed to Rav Yehuda and Shmuel. Rav Yehuda holds that one recites *borei pri ha'adama*, because *kore* is food. Shmuel holds that one recites *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*, because *kivan shesofo lehakshot* — since the heart of palm will eventually harden into the trunk of the tree, it is considered part of the tree and therefore not a fruit of the ground in the relevant sense. Shmuel himself conceded to Rav Yehuda's reasoning, saying *kevatech mistabra* — your opinion is more logical — pointing to the analogy of a radish, which will also eventually harden yet still receives *borei pri ha'adama*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, כְּווֹתָיךְ מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּהָא צְנוֹן שֶׁסּוֹפוֹ לְהַקְשׁוֹת, וּמְבָרְכִינַן עֲלֵיהּ ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: *Shinnana*, your opinion is reasonable, as a radish will eventually harden, and** yet **we recite over it the blessing: Who creates the fruit of the ground.**

### Final Ruling on Kore

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, צְנוֹן נָטְעִי אִינָשֵׁי אַדַּעְתָּא דְפוּגְלָא, דִּיקְלָא — לָא נָטְעִי אִינָשֵׁי אַדַּעְתָּא דְקוֹרָא. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקַלְּסֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה, הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But it is not so,** because **people plant radish with the intention** of eating it while it is **soft; but people do not plant palm trees with the intention** of eating **heart of palm.** Therefore, heart of palm is not considered the fruit of the palm, but rather food extracted from it, over which only the following blessing should be recited: By Whose word all things came to be. **And** the Gemara concludes: **Even though Shmuel praised Rav Yehuda, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel.**

The *Gemara* rejects the analogy. A radish is planted expressly to be eaten as a radish; it is inherently the "fruit" the farmer seeks. A palm tree, by contrast, is not planted for its heart of palm — *lo nati inash ada'ata d'kora*. The heart of palm is an incidental byproduct of a tree planted for its dates. Since the heart of palm was never the intended product, and since it will inevitably become part of the wooden trunk, it retains its identification with the tree and is not reclassified as food. Accordingly: *hilchata kevasei de'Shmuel* — the law follows Shmuel. Despite Shmuel's own expressed admiration for Rav Yehuda's position, the ruling is *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro* over *kore*.
