# Eruvin 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah & Global Stateme…

### Mishnah Overview

Today's daf is 28, and we pick up from the bottom of 27b. The *mishnah* had established two parallel rules: one may use virtually anything for *eruv*, and virtually anything for *maaser sheni*, with the exception of *maaser sheni* money, water, and salt. The structure of today's discussion follows from those two broad permissions, and the bulk of the daf is devoted to qualifying and limiting them — adding, case by case, to the list of exceptions.

### Reb Yochanan's Warning

Rabbi Yochanan had already cautioned that one should not rely on global statements of this kind, even when they are accompanied by explicit exceptions. His point is that such formulations are inherently imprecise: the "except" clause does not exhaust the exclusions, and the day's learning will bear this out repeatedly. We begin with *maaser sheni* and then circle back to *eruv*.

## Maaser Sheni Fish Brine

### Fish Brine Debate

The *Gemara* opens with a *tannaim* dispute concerning fish brine. Two *tannaim* both exclude plain salt water but debate whether fish brine is included within the scope of *maaser sheni* purchases. Both, however, agree that actual fish are permissible. A third view holds that fish are excluded entirely. The *Gemara* traces this disagreement to a fundamental difference in hermeneutical method applied to the *pasuk* in Devarim: *venatata hakesef bechol asher te'aveh nafshecha babakar uvatzon uvayayin uvashichar uv'chol asher tish'alcha nafshecha* — "you shall give the money for whatever your soul desires: cattle, sheep, wine, or strong drink, or whatever your soul asks." The structure of this verse is: a general statement, a specific list, and another general statement.

### Hermeneutical Schools

The question is how to apply hermeneutical principles to this three-part structure. The school of Rebbi Yishmael employs the rule of *klal u'prat u'klal* — general, particular, general — which yields a category defined by similarity to the specific middle list. The school of Rebbi Akiva, by contrast, applies *ribuy u'mi'ut u'ribuy* — inclusion, exclusion, inclusion — which produces a considerably broader category. Under *ribuy u'mi'ut u'ribuy*, once there is a *ribuy* and a *mi'ut*, the second *ribuy* implies that virtually everything is included except what is most dissimilar from the list — which would mean only things like water and salt, substances that belong to neither the animal nor the plant world. Under *klal u'prat u'klal*, however, the derived category must be *ke'ein ha'prat*, similar to the specific items enumerated, and the analysis becomes considerably more restrictive.

### Pri Mipri & Mushrooms

The *Gemara* now asks: what, precisely, is "similar" to the *prat* according to those who apply *klal u'prat u'klal*? The answer given is *mah ha'prat meforash pri mipri ve'gidulei karka* — just as the specific items in the list are a "fruit from a fruit" and grow from the ground, so too anything derived from such a category is included. The term *pri mipri* is not limited to literal fruit in its natural state; as Rashi in Bava Kamma explains, it encompasses things produced from fruit — wine, for instance, is the product of grapes and qualifies as *pri mipri*. The requirement of *gidulei karka* means the item must derive from something that grows from the ground.

This formulation excludes mushrooms on two grounds: first, a mushroom does not come from another mushroom in the ordinary reproductive sense; second, and more compellingly, mushrooms grow from decomposing matter and do not draw their nutrients from the ground the way ordinary plants do. They are not truly *gidulei karka*. Rashi in Bava Kamma states this explicitly, and notably, the *braita* that appears at the beginning of the *Sifra* — the *braita* of Rebbi Yishmael's thirteen hermeneutical principles — uses precisely this case, *maaser sheni* and the exclusion of mushrooms, as the paradigmatic illustration of *klal u'prat u'klal*.

### Vlad Vladot & Fish

A second *braita* formulates the same rule differently: *mah ha'prat meforash vlad vladot aretz* — just as the specific items are "descendants of the descendants of the land," so too the full category must satisfy that description. One might assume that *vlad vladot aretz* and *pri mipri* are simply two ways of expressing the same idea, but the *Gemara* asks whether there is a practical difference between them.

Abaye answers: *dagim*, fish, constitute the point of difference. According to the formulation *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka*, fish might be included, since one could argue that they live off of things that ultimately draw their nourishment from the ground. Tosafot notes in multiple places that *gidulei karka* can be understood in either a narrow sense — things that literally spring from the earth, like plants — or in a broader sense — things whose nutritional chain traces back to the ground. If the broader reading is intended, fish, which feed on vegetation in the water or on other fish that do so, might qualify. The *Gemara* will proceed to challenge this.

According to the formulation of *vlad vladot ha'aretz*, however, the critical question is not how the creature currently sustains itself but how it was created in *maaseh bereishit*. Land animals were fashioned from the ground; fish, *dagim mi'maya ivru*, were created from the water. Accordingly, fish are excluded under *vlad vladot ha'aretz* in a way they might not be under *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka*.

The *Gemara* then questions Abaye's premise. Would Abaye truly classify fish as *gidulei karka*? Abaye himself has a famous statement at the top of 28a: one who eats a *putita* — a water insect — receives four lashes, corresponding to four distinct prohibitions. The count depends on the creature being classified as a water creature rather than a land creature. If fish and water insects were considered *gidulei karka*, a water insect would also fall under the prohibition of *sheretz ha'shoretz al ha'aretz* and the lash count would increase. Since Abaye does not include that prohibition in his count, he evidently does not treat water creatures as *gidulei karka*. Ravina therefore proposes a different answer.

### Birds & Creation

Ravina suggests that *ofos*, birds, are the true point of difference between the two formulations. Under *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka*, birds are included: they currently live off of what grows from the ground and satisfy the criterion of *gidulei karka* in the broader sense. Under *vlad vladot ha'aretz*, however, the question is how they were created, and here the *Gemara* invokes a well-known textual difficulty. In the first chapter of Bereishit, birds appear to have been created from the water: *yishretzu hamayim sheretz nefesh chaya ve'of ye'ofef al ha'aretz*. Yet in the second chapter, the verse states: *vayitzer Hashem Elokim min ha'adama kol chayat ha'sadeh ve'et kol of ha'shamayim* — He formed from the ground all the beasts of the field and all the birds of the heavens. How can birds be said to have been formed from the ground if the earlier account created them from the water?

The *Gemara*'s resolution is that birds were created from *rakak* — mud, a mixture of water and earth. Mud is not simply ground, and therefore birds do not qualify as *vlad vladot ha'aretz* in the full sense. Accordingly, the practical difference between the two formulations is this: *pri mipri ve'gidulei karka* includes birds but excludes fish; *vlad vladot ha'aretz* excludes both. On fish, both approaches agree on exclusion.

### Abstraction Debate

This leaves an important question. Both formulations belong to the *klal u'prat u'klal* school — they agree that the derived category must resemble the *prat*. Why then does one formulation yield a broader category (including birds) while the other yields a narrower one (excluding birds)? The *Gemara* asks: *man de'amar be'ofos mai ta'ama u'man de'miat ofos mai ta'ama* — what is the underlying reasoning of each position?

The *Gemara*'s answer is subtle. Even within the framework of *klal u'prat u'klal*, one can ask which building block is primary. One approach takes the first *klal u'prat* as the foundational unit: that unit, in isolation, yields only the *prat* — a very narrow list. The subsequent *klal* then broadens it, but only moderately, because one is expanding something small. The result is a list that requires resemblance in three characteristics — *pri mipri*, *gidulei karka*, and *vlad vladot ha'aretz*. This is the position of *klala kamma davka*: the first *klal* is the operative anchor.

The other approach takes the *prat u'klal* as the primary building block. That unit, in isolation, yields the *klal* — a very expansive list. The first *klal* then contracts it, but one is contracting something already very large, so the final list remains broad. The result requires resemblance in only two characteristics. This is *klala basra davka*: the final *klal* is the operative anchor. The former position excludes birds; the latter includes them.

The overall upshot of this entire hermeneutical analysis is that the *mishnah*'s sweeping statement — that one may use *maaser sheni* money for anything — is far more limited than it first appears. Even granting the most expansive reading within *klal u'prat u'klal*, mushrooms and fish are excluded. On the view of *klala kamma davka*, birds are excluded as well. The *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah* who said "anything" may well have been operating according to the Rebbi Akiva school of *ribuy u'mi'ut u'ribuy*, for whom only water and salt fall outside the category — which is why the exception he listed was so limited.

## Eruv Edibility Rules

### Eatable vs. Uneatable

The discussion now shifts to *eruv*. Rav Yehuda, in the name of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat, in the name of Rav, states: one may make an *eruv* with *papuin* (cress), with *chalgoogloit* (purslane), and with *gud gadaniyot* (sweet clover). One may not make an *eruv* with *chaziz* (green corn or green wheat, the immature stalk of grain cut before it has fully developed) or with *chafniyot* (unripe dates). The criterion implicit in this list is fitness for human consumption: things that are genuinely edible qualify, while things used primarily as animal fodder, or that are insufficiently ripe, do not.

### Clover Sterilization

The *Gemara* immediately questions the inclusion of sweet clover. A *braita* teaches that sweet clover promotes sterility in men. The recommendation is that those who have many children may eat it — *gud gadaniyot marbe banim, merube banim yochlu* — while those who have no children should not — *chashuchei banim lo yochlu*. And if the plant has hardened into seed, even those with many children should avoid it for general health reasons. How then can sweet clover be classified as fit for human consumption and therefore usable for *eruv*?

The *Gemara* offers three responses. First, the statement permitting its use for *eruv* refers specifically to before the plant has hardened into seed, and to individuals who are not concerned about its reproductive effects. Second — and this is the more conceptually significant answer — even a person who cannot eat something may use it for *eruv* if it is food for others. The *mishnah* itself provides the precedent: one may make an *eruv* for a *nazir* using wine, and for an Israelite using *terumah*, even though the *nazir* may not drink wine and a non-*kohen* may not eat *terumah*. The principle is *af al gav de'lo chazi le'hai chazi le'hai* — even if it is not fit for this one, it is fit for that one, and that suffices to define it as food. The same logic applies here: sweet clover is fit for those without fertility concerns. Third, Rav's original statement permitting its use for *eruv* refers specifically to the Median variety of sweet clover, which poses no health concerns at all.

### Green Corn in Bavel

The *Gemara* then turns to *chaziz*, green corn, which was listed as impermissible for *eruv*. Yet Rav Yehuda himself, in the name of the same Rav, states elsewhere that *kshuta ve'chaziz* — hops and green corn — may be used for *eruv*, and one recites *borei pri ha'adama* over them, since they grow from the ground and are fit for eating. There is thus a direct contradiction within Rav's own rulings.

The *Gemara* resolves this: the statement prohibiting *chaziz* was issued before Rav came to Bavel, when he was living in Eretz Yisrael, where green corn was not consumed by humans and was considered animal fodder. Once he arrived in Bavel and observed that people there do eat it, he revised his position. The *Gemara* objects to this resolution: is Bavel the majority of the world? Why should local Babylonian practice override the general human norm?

### Intention Changes

The objection draws on a *braita* about *pul* (beans), *se'ora* (barley), and *tilson* (fenugreek). These plants are ordinarily cultivated for their seeds. If one planted them with the intention of harvesting them as vegetables — eating their leaves and stalks — *batla da'ato etzel kol adam*: the individual's idiosyncratic intention is voided by the universal norm. The legal consequence is that *ma'aser* obligation attaches to their seeds but not to their vegetable portion, regardless of what the planter intended. One cannot redefine what something is by deciding to use it differently than everybody else does.

By contrast, *shachalayim* (cress) and *gargir* (arugula) are plants that people commonly eat both as vegetables and for their seeds, so intention does matter for them. If one planted them as vegetables, the vegetable character is primary; if for seed, the seed character is primary. But for the grains that are universally understood as seed crops, the individual who plants them as vegetables is simply ignored.

The implication for *chaziz* is sharp: if the majority of the world considers it animal food, the fact that people in Bavel eat it should be dismissed the same way idiosyncratic individual intentions are dismissed. The *Gemara* does not fully resolve the tension here. The answer ultimately given is that when Rav permitted *chaziz* in Bavel, he was referring to a specific cultivated variety — *digunaisa*, the garden variety of *chaziz* — which is universally eaten and unambiguously considered human food. That resolves the contradiction: the prohibition applied to the wild variety; the permission applies to the garden variety.

The question of whether Bavel's widespread practice suffices to alter the legal category is left somewhat open. Tosafot notes the difficulty: Bavel is not a few individuals but an entire large region, and it is counterintuitive to dismiss an entire country's food culture as *batla da'ato*. The distinction the *Gemara* seems to draw is between something whose basic definition is animal food — where even a whole country of human consumers cannot redefine it — and something whose basic definition is human food, where secondary reasons that prevent certain individuals from eating it (allergies, religious restrictions, health concerns) are irrelevant because the item's fundamental character remains edible.

### Child's Teaching

While on the topic of hops and green corn, the *Gemara* relates a charming episode. Rabbi Zeira, when he needed a break from learning and wanted to accumulate *s'char* even during his rest, would sit by the doorway of Rav Yehuda bar Ami and rise in honor of the rabbinic students passing in and out of the *beis medrash*. On one such occasion, a schoolchild emerged. Rabbi Zeira asked what his teacher had taught him. The child said: for *kashus* (hops), one recites *borei pri ha'adama*; for *chaziz* (green corn), one recites *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*. This accords with what we have seen: *chaziz* is not fully fit for eating and therefore warrants only *shehakol*, while hops are fit and warrant *borei pri ha'adama*.

Rabbi Zeira challenged the ruling: *adaraba ipcha mistabra* — the opposite seems more logical. Green corn grows directly from the ground and should therefore merit *borei pri ha'adama*. Hops, by contrast, grow as a parasitic plant, drawing their nourishment from a host plant rather than directly from the soil, and should therefore receive only *shehakol*.

The *Gemara* rules: *ve'hilchata k'yanuka d'vei Rav* — the law follows the schoolchild. *Hai gmar peira* — hops are a completed, ripened fruit, and therefore receive *borei pri ha'adama*. *Lav gmar peira* — green corn at this stage is not a completed fruit; it is not only unripe but, as established earlier, not even considered human food at this stage, meriting only *shehakol*. As for Rabbi Zeira's objection that hops grow from the air rather than the ground: the *Gemara* rejects this. *Keshuta nami me'ara ka'rabei* — hops also grow from the ground, indirectly. We can see this empirically: if you cut down the host bush, the hops die. This demonstrates that the hops are drawing real nourishment from the host plant, which in turn draws it from the ground — and that chain is sufficient to call them *gidulei karka* and to warrant *borei pri ha'adama*. This is also what distinguishes hops from mushrooms, which can grow on decaying wood entirely disconnected from the ground.

### Unripe Dates Issue

The *Gemara* returns to *chafniyot*, unripe dates, which were listed as impermissible for *eruv*. A *braita* complicates this: *kore* (heart of palm) may be purchased with *maaser sheni* money, but does not contract the ritual impurity of foodstuffs (*tum'at ochlin*). Unripe dates, by contrast, may also be purchased with *maaser sheni* money and *do* contract *tum'at ochlin* — they are therefore considered food. If they are food, why can they not be used for *eruv*?

Rabi Yehuda in the same *braita* adds his position: *kore* is like wood in all respects except that it may be purchased with *maaser sheni* money. As for unripe dates, they are fully a fruit in all respects except that they are exempt from *ma'aser* — because they have not yet reached the stage of full ripeness that triggers *ma'aser* obligation.

The *Gemara* initially suggests that the *braita*'s statements about unripe dates refer to the male palm variety, which never ripens and is therefore always in the same state. For that variety, even the unripe stage is the final and consumable form, and it is unambiguously food. The *Gemara* rejects this: if the male variety were meant, Rabbi Yehuda would never have exempted it from *ma'aser*, because for a variety that never develops further, whatever stage it has reached is its maximum — and Rabbi Yehuda himself explicitly rules elsewhere that the unripe dates of certain specific locations, such as the dates of Tuvina, are *chayav* in *ma'aser*.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that both the *braita* and our original ruling refer to the ordinary female palm variety. The resolution rests on an important distinction introduced by Rabbi Yochanan: *ho'il ve'ra'uy l'matkan al yedei ha'ur* — since they can be sweetened by cooking them over fire, they qualify as food for purposes of *tum'at ochlin* and for redemption with *maaser sheni* money. But *eruv* imposes a stricter standard: the food must be fit for eating in exactly the state it is in right now, without requiring any preparation. Unripe dates that need to be cooked before they are palatable fail this test. Raw meat provides a parallel: one may certainly use *maaser sheni* money to purchase raw meat, but one may not use raw meat for *eruv*, because it is not currently fit for immediate consumption.

The context for Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is a *braita* about *shkedim ha'marim*, bitter almonds. When small and unripe, bitter almonds are still edible and are therefore obligated in *ma'aser*; when large and ripe, they are bitter and inedible and are therefore exempt. Sweet almonds follow the opposite pattern: large ones are obligated; small, unripe ones are exempt. Rabbi Shimon bar Yossi in the name of his father says both sizes of the bitter variety are exempt. Others say both are obligated. Rabbi Yochanan's statement responds to the latter position: how can the large, bitter almonds be obligated if they are inedible? *Ho'il ve'ra'uy l'matkan al yedei ha'ur* — since they can be made palatable by cooking, they count as food for *ma'aser* and *tum'at ochlin* purposes. But that leniency does not extend to *eruv*, where immediate edibility is required.

## Heart of Palm

### Kore as Wood

Having addressed unripe dates, the *Gemara* returns to the statement about *kore*, the heart of palm. Rabbi Yehuda's ruling was: *kore* is like wood in all respects, except that it may be purchased with *maaser sheni* money. This appears to say exactly what the first *tanna* said — namely, that it is purchasable with *maaser sheni* money but not considered food for *tum'at ochlin* purposes.

### Boiling & Frying

Abaye explains the difference between them: *shulka ve'tigna ika beinaihu* — boiling and frying. According to Rabbi Yehuda, who categorically declares *kore* to be wood, even boiling and frying it would not change its status. It would be, as it were, fried wood. According to the first *tanna*, who does not make such a categorical declaration, perhaps once the heart of palm has been sufficiently prepared and rendered genuinely edible, it would acquire the status of food for *tum'at ochlin* purposes.

### Rava on Bracha

Rava challenges Abaye's distinction. Would anyone truly say that boiling and frying fails to confer food status? A *braita* explicitly addresses the case of animal hide and placenta: hide is not food and does not receive *tum'at ochlin*; but hide that has been boiled, or a placenta that one intended to eat, does receive *tum'at ochlin*. Even hide — something that no one thinks of as food in its ordinary state — becomes food once sufficiently prepared. It strains credulity to say that boiled and fried heart of palm would not be food for anyone.

Rava therefore proposes a different resolution: *ika beinaihu bracha* — the operative difference between the two opinions is the blessing one recites over *kore*.

### Rav Yehuda vs. Shmuel

The debate over the *bracha* on *kore* is attributed to Rav Yehuda and Shmuel. Rav Yehuda holds that one recites *borei pri ha'adama*, because *kore* is food. Shmuel holds that one recites *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro*, because *kivan shesofo lehakshot* — since the heart of palm will eventually harden into the trunk of the tree, it is considered part of the tree and therefore not a fruit of the ground in the relevant sense. Shmuel himself conceded to Rav Yehuda's reasoning, saying *kevatech mistabra* — your opinion is more logical — pointing to the analogy of a radish, which will also eventually harden yet still receives *borei pri ha'adama*.

### Final Ruling on Kore

The *Gemara* rejects the analogy. A radish is planted expressly to be eaten as a radish; it is inherently the "fruit" the farmer seeks. A palm tree, by contrast, is not planted for its heart of palm — *lo nati inash ada'ata d'kora*. The heart of palm is an incidental byproduct of a tree planted for its dates. Since the heart of palm was never the intended product, and since it will inevitably become part of the wooden trunk, it retains its identification with the tree and is not reclassified as food. Accordingly: *hilchata kevasei de'Shmuel* — the law follows Shmuel. Despite Shmuel's own expressed admiration for Rav Yehuda's position, the ruling is *shehakol nihiyeh bidvaro* over *kore*.
