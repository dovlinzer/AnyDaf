# Bekhorot 45b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Invalidities

### Opening

Today's daf is 46, though we begin with the *mishnah* on 45b. The *mishnah* before us covers a remarkably diverse set of invalidities unique to human *kohanim* — some physical, some behavioral, and some rooted in what the *gemara* will classify not as a *mum* per se, but as a failure to meet the standard of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon*, meaning a characteristic that is not representative of the normative center of the priestly population.

### Racial Characteristics

The *mishnah* opens with *haKushi*, the Ethiopian, followed by *hagichor* and *halavkan*. These terms, whose precise meanings will be clarified by the *gemara*, refer to skin coloration — dark, reddish, and white complexions respectively. Even if such a complexion is entirely natural for a given individual, it may nonetheless fall outside the range of what is typical among *kohanim*, placing it in the category of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon*. Notably, this classification does not require that the characteristic be a formal *mum*; it is sufficient that it renders the *kohen* an outlier within the priestly class.

### Physical Deformities

The *mishnah* continues with *hakipeach* and *hanannas* — likely referring to a giant and a dwarf respectively, as the *gemara* will elaborate — as well as certain skin conditions. These physical traits, like the complexion variations above, are treated as disqualifying characteristics not because they are blemishes in the classical sense, but because they deviate from the norm.

### Mental & Behavioral

The *mishnah* then lists *hacherish*, the deaf-mute; *hashoteh*, which the *gemara* in *Chagigah* discusses at length and which appears to describe something closer to what we might today call a severe dissociative or psychotic condition; and *hashikor*, the intoxicated *kohen*. The inclusion of these non-physical characteristics is significant. Just as we saw in earlier *mishnayot* with conditions like epilepsy and asthma, the criterion here is not purely anatomical but extends to anything that is visibly manifest through the person's conduct or condition. The common denominator appears to be that the characteristic — whether physical or behavioral — is outwardly apparent.

The *gemara* will clarify that *shikor* belongs to a distinct legal category: drunkenness does not merely render the *kohen* atypical within his class, but actively *mechalel avodah*, desecrating the Temple service. This places it in a different conceptual register from the rest of the list, a point the *gemara* addresses explicitly.

### Leprosy & Principle

The *mishnah* adds *ba'alei nega'im tehorim* — a *kohen* who has a leprosy spot that is technically *tahor* and does not render him impure, but which is nonetheless a visible disfigurement. This too is a disqualifying condition for human *kohanim*.

The *mishnah* then states its governing principle: *psulim ba'adam, ukesherim babehema* — all of these are disqualifying for human *kohanim* but are not problematic for animals. This formulation serves as the bookend to the opening line of the *perek*. The opening *mishnah* established that humans face certain invalidities unknown to animals, and this line closes the list. The conceptual framework, as the *gemara* understood it, is not the standard category of *mum* — which is why these conditions do not invalidate an animal — but rather the uniquely priestly standard of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon*.

It is worth noting that while most of the cases in this *perek* are not formal *mumim*, there were some along the way — relating to the eyes, the arms, or conditions derived directly from biblical verses — that do constitute a *mum* in the traditional sense. Nevertheless, the majority, and certainly the defining character of this *perek*, involves conditions specific to human priestly service.

### Amoraim Views

*Rabbi Chanina* adds that a *shoteh* animal is also *einah min hamuvchar* — not of the finest quality for a *korban*. One might ask why mental stability would matter for an animal being brought as a sacrifice, given that the act of *shechita* elevates the animal's status. The answer may lie in the concern that presenting a *shoteh* animal signals that one is giving God the cast-offs — something one could not use and therefore decided to consecrate. The concern thus pertains to the quality and intent of the gift, not merely its legal status at the moment of *shechita*.

*Rabbi Eliezer* adds the category of *ba'alei hadildulin* — *kohanim* with hanging flaps of skin — as disqualifying for humans but not for animals. The precise novelty of his ruling is somewhat unclear, since presumably there is general agreement that such a condition is problematic for *kohanim*. It may be that he is explicitly stating its permissibility for animals, or that he is clarifying a case left ambiguous by the earlier debates concerning the *gibben* and similar conditions.

## Gemara Definitions

### Color Definitions

The *gemara* now proceeds to define the three color-based conditions listed in the *mishnah*. *Cushi* means *uchma*, black. *Gichor* and *lavkan* initially appear counterintuitive: one would expect *lavkan*, cognate with *lavan*, to mean white, but the *gemara* reverses the expected pairing. The *gemara* demonstrates through Aramaic vernacular: *gichor* corresponds to *sumka*, red, as evidenced by the expression *sumka gichaya* used in popular speech to describe red things. And *lavkan* is *chivra*, white, as supported by an incident involving a merchant who called out for *lukiani* — white sheep — and was indeed found to be selling particularly white animals. The *gemara* thus grounds its definitions in spoken Aramaic idiom rather than straightforward etymology.

### Race Context

This passage, though formally consistent with the broader framework of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon*, resonates very differently from other invalidities discussed in the *perek*. Characteristics like being left-handed or lacking an outie navel, for instance, affect perhaps ten percent of the population and are randomly distributed — they carry no historical weight. Racial characteristics, by contrast, are bound up with centuries of persecution, enslavement, and dehumanization in ways that cannot simply be bracketed out. Conceptually, the *gemara*'s framework treats these as parallel cases — outliers relative to the normative center of the kohen population — but the moral and theological resonance is of an entirely different order.

It is worth noting that the *Rambam*, while acknowledging this distinct category, describes it not as *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon* but as a *mum she'eino shaveh b'vhemah u'v'adam* — a blemish that does not apply equally to animals and humans. The *gemara*'s own tendency is to classify nearly every case in these *mishnayot* as *psul* rather than *mum*, but the *Rambam* effectively subsumes this into a broader understanding of *mum*.

### Geography & Marriage

A natural question arises: does geography affect the application of these laws? Jewish communities from Yemen look very different from those of Germany, whether due to climate, intermarriage over generations, or both. The *gemara* does not discuss regional variation. Its implied assumption is that one looks at the entirety of the *kohen* population globally, and that an individual who is an outlier within that entire class — even if he would be entirely typical within a regional subset — is still subject to this disqualification. Whether all *kohanim* from, say, a predominantly dark-skinned community would collectively be *pasul* relative to the global distribution, or whether the standard is determined regionally, is simply not addressed by the *gemara*.

A related question concerns intermarriage and conversion. A *kohen* may not marry a *giyoret*. And a *Cushi* who converts would not become a *kohen* in any case. Yet these questions point toward a broader tension: the categories here are treating complexion as a characteristic defined relative to a population standard, yet that standard was plainly shaped by the particular demographic reality the *amora'im* knew. The *gemara* has no awareness of large communities of Jews — such as the Ethiopian *Beta Israel* — who are dark-skinned as a normative population reality. *Rashi* uses the term *Kushi* to refer to a complexion rather than an ethnicity, and that reading is supported by the *gemara*'s own final discussion here about genetics, which treats these characteristics as continuous traits within a population rather than as fixed racial categories.

### Theology Problem

This brings us to a genuinely difficult theological question. Taken together, the *mishnah* presents an image of priestly perfection that is implicitly male, physically normative by a specific standard, and — as this passage makes clear — characterized by a particular complexion. Students who absorb this material from youth internalize these as standards of divine preference. And this is not abstract theology; it is operative *halacha* that shapes who may perform the Temple service and, by implication, who is seen as the ideal vehicle of divine worship.

The most that can be said in partial mitigation is that the *gemara* consistently works to reclassify these conditions as *psul* rather than *mum* — that is, not as defects in any inherent sense, but as departures from a statistical norm. This does not resolve the problem, but it does shift the framing somewhat: we are not calling these characteristics blemishes, only acknowledging that they fall outside the center of a distribution. That said, the *Rambam* does not even maintain this distinction cleanly, and in any case the social and psychological effect of the classification is not much altered by the technical category assigned to it.

One approach to contextualizing these standards is to situate them within the broader aesthetics of the *Beit HaMikdash* — a carefully constructed visual and ritual environment designed to meet people where they are, making concessions to human aesthetic psychology in order to draw them toward worship. But this explanation immediately raises a further question: if a concession is being made to human limitation, where is the corrective teaching that prevents the concession from calcifying into an internalized ideal? The *gemara* does not provide one, and that silence is itself significant. The attitudes that generated these rules are not external to the system — they permeate it, and the system at times reinforces rather than critiques them. That is a challenge halakhic Judaism must continue to grapple with seriously.

## Height Defects

### Tall Definition

The *gemara* now turns to the term *kipeach* from the *mishnah*, which *Rav Zevid* teaches refers to someone who is *gavo'ah* — extremely tall. This seems initially puzzling, given that *Moshe Rabbeinu* is said to have stood ten *amot* tall.

### Tall & Thin

The *gemara* raises this objection directly: does God not praise Himself through those of great stature? The verse states that the Amorites were as tall as cedars — and God wiped them out before Israel, implying that being tall was a mark of formidability and even grandeur. *Rav Papa* resolves the contradiction: when the *mishnah* disqualifies a *gavo'ah*, it refers specifically to someone who is tall and disproportionately thin — what one might call a beanpole, an extremely elongated and attenuated figure. Such a form is visually unusual and therefore falls within the category of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon*. A person who is tall but normally proportioned presents no such concern.

### Moses Example

This resolution accounts for *Moshe Rabbeinu*: he was ten *amot* tall, but he was normally proportioned, not disproportionately thin. Only the beanpole configuration — extreme height combined with extreme thinness — is disqualifying. A well-proportioned tall individual does not stand out as an outlier in a way that would trigger this category of *psul*.

### Genetic Advice

*Resh Lakish* offers a striking observation that reflects a pre-Mendelian awareness of hereditary patterns. He teaches: *gavo'ah lo yisa gavo'hit* — an extremely tall man should not marry an extremely tall woman, *shema yetzei meihen toren* — lest their child emerge like a ship's mast, a beanpole. The concern is a multiplier effect: combining two extreme traits in both parents intensifies the trait in the offspring. The same principle applies in every direction. *Nannas lo yisa nannaset* — a dwarf should not marry a female dwarf, *shema yetzei meihen etzba'i* — lest the child be the size of a finger, a Tom Thumb. *Lavan lo yisa levana* — a very fair-skinned person should not marry another, *shema yetzei meihen bohak* — lest an albino emerge. And *shachor lo yisa sh'chora* — a very dark-complexioned person should not marry another, *shema yetzei meihen tapu'ach* — lest the child be as dark as a blackened pot.

It bears noting that *Resh Lakish* here does not appear to be speaking of racial groups but of individuals within a population who fall at the extreme ends of a continuous distribution. The concern is that extreme outliers on either end of a spectrum should not compound their children's deviation by marrying someone at the same extreme.

### Interracial Logic

This passage also suggests that the *gemara*'s framework here is oriented toward preventing the multiplication of extremes rather than toward any hierarchy of racial superiority. The advice is symmetric: dark should not marry dark for the same reason that fair should not marry fair. This connects to the broader observation in the *gemara* about the importance of marrying someone who complements one's characteristics — a principle that extends, in other contexts, to intellectual lineage as well, such as the preference for a *talmid chacham* to marry a *bat talmid chacham* in order to preserve and strengthen that quality in the next generation.

## Drunk & Desecration

### Shikor Desecration

The *gemara* now addresses *hashikor* — the intoxicated *kohen*. The *gemara* notes that *shikor mechalel avodah*: drunkenness desecrates the Temple service. This is significant on two levels. First, the ground of disqualification is not a condition of the person but an act — drunkenness — and it actively invalidates the *avodah* performed, whereas everything else in our list, operating through the category of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon*, does not invalidate the *avodah* performed. Second, this means the drunk *kohen* belongs to a fundamentally different legal category from the rest of the *mishnah*'s list.

### Biblical Source

The biblical source is the verse: *yayin veshichar al tesht atah uvanecha itach bevochem el ohel moed velo tamutu ulehavdil bein hakodesh uvein hachol* — wine and *shechar* shall not be drunk by you or your sons when you enter the Tent of Meeting, lest you die, and to distinguish between the holy and the profane. From this verse, the *gemara* derives that intoxication causes one to lose the capacity to distinguish holy from profane, and performing the *avodah* in that state constitutes a desecration. The drunk *kohen*'s disqualification is thus not an expression of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon* — it does not categorize him as atypical among *kohanim* — but a direct biblical prohibition with its own independent mechanism.

### Shechar Definition

The question then becomes: what is included in the prohibition of *yayin veshichar*? *Rav* understands *shechar* as *yayin derech shikruto* — wine in its intoxicating mode, meaning a highly alcoholic wine. This reading is debated, and the *gemara* raises the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds that consuming certain foods — dates, milk, or honey (which *Rashi* understands as mead) — and entering the Temple in that condition also constitutes a transgression. The *gemara* rules against *Rabbi Yehuda* on this point. The upshot is that intoxicating substances that fall outside the biblical prohibition of *yayin veshichar* would not *mechalel* the *avodah* in the full biblical sense, but may still fall under the category of *shaveh bezaro shel Aharon* — making the *kohen* stand out behaviorally among his peers. Whether that secondary categorization is biblical or rabbinic in character is left somewhat open by the *gemara*.

## Animal Disqualifications

### Oto ve'et Beno

The *mishnah* now turns to its closing section: *elu kesherim ba'adam u'pesulim be'behemah* — a list of conditions that disqualify animals but are not problematic for human *kohanim*. The first is *oto ve'et beno*: the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its offspring on the same day. For animals brought as *korbanot*, this constitutes a disqualification. The corresponding human case would presumably be a *kohen* and his son serving in the Temple on the same day — which raises no issue whatsoever. The comparison is admittedly somewhat forced, and the *gemara* will interrogate it further.

### Treifa & Dofen

The *mishnah* continues with *treifah* — an animal with an internal injury that renders it halakhically invalid. This is not a *mum* in the visible sense, as the condition is entirely internal. It is worth recalling that the *gemara* earlier raised the question of whether an animal missing an internal organ — beyond a simple *treifah* hole — would violate the requirement of *tamim*; the *gemara* seemed to rule that it would. What the analogous situation would be for a human *kohen* is not entirely clear, but the question is not without substance. In any case, *treifah* as a category is not a concern for human *kohanim*.

Similarly, *yotzei dofen* — an animal born by caesarean section — is disqualified as a *korban*, derived from the verse *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled*, which specifies natural birth. This requirement does not extend to *kohanim*.

### Bestiality & Goring

The *mishnah* lists animals that were used in bestiality or that gored and killed a human — the *shor haniskal* — as invalid for use as *korbanot*, derived from biblical verses, but not as analogies for *kohanim*. Whatever sexual or violent sins might correspond in a human context, the *gemara* questions whether that corresponding line is even present in the text of the *mishnah*, and in any case such sins do not automatically disqualify a *kohen* from serving.

### Kohen Sins

That said, the *mishnah* does identify certain human sins that do create a priestly disqualification. One is *avodah zarah*: a *kohen* who has worshipped idolatry is disqualified even after *teshuvah*, for the obvious reason that he has functioned as a priest of another religion. The other is *she-mitat adam* — murder. The *gemara* teaches that a *kohen* who has committed murder may not perform the *duchening*, derived from the verse *yedeichem damim maleu* — your hands are filled with blood, how can you raise them to bless? The focus on the hands and the metaphor of bloodguilt is striking and poignant. Whether this applies to accidental killing, and whether *teshuvah* can reverse the disqualification, are questions the *gemara* raises but does not fully resolve. What is notable is that this disqualification is stated explicitly in the context of *duchening* but is not as clearly articulated in the context of the *avodah* itself.

## Kohen Violations

### Marriage Violation

The *mishnah* now addresses *hanosei nashim be'aveirah* — a *kohen* who has married a woman forbidden to him, such as a *gerushah* or a *chalutza*, and who continues to live with her in violation of the laws of *kehunah*. This ongoing state of sin renders him unfit to perform the *avodah*. However, the *mishnah* provides a mechanism by which he can continue to serve without immediately dissolving the marriage: *noder ve'oved* — he takes a vow and continues the *avodah*. The vow renders his wife sexually forbidden to him, which is a step toward resolving the violation even before the formal *geirushin* takes place. He then descends from the altar and divorces her.

In practical contemporary terms, this is a genuinely serious matter. A *kohen* married to a *gerushah* is not permitted to *duchen* in the synagogue. While poskim today do not generally compel divorce — the *Rama* rules that we no longer forcibly dissolve marriages in these cases — no halakhic authority sanctions the *kohen* continuing to function in his priestly role while the violation persists. The *gemara*'s ideal is clear: the violation should cease. The contemporary application is to refrain from condoning the prohibited role without compulsion.

### Neder vs Kabala

The *gemara* immediately asks: why is a *neder* sufficient? Surely he might go to a *chacham* and obtain a *heter* on his vow, thereby freeing himself from the vow's effect without having completed the divorce. This is a penetrating question, because one might assume no reputable *chacham* would annul a vow whose annulment enables the continuation of a sin. The *gemara* confirms this: the *chacham* must be informed of the specifics of the vow — *tzarich lifaret et haneder* — and no scholar of integrity will permit a vow when he knows that annulling it enables ongoing biblical transgression.

The *mishnah* then contrasts this with the case of *metamei lameitim* — a *kohen* who regularly becomes ritually impure through contact with the dead in violation of his priestly obligations. Here the mechanism for restoring his eligibility is not a *neder* but a *kabala* — a personal commitment to cease the behavior. The *gemara* asks: why the asymmetry? Why does the marriage violation require a *neder* while the *tumah* violation requires only a *kabala*?

The conceptual distinction between *neder* and *kabala* also maps onto a distinction between the two types of obligation. A *neder* is object-oriented: the *kohen* renders the woman forbidden to him as an object, without directly engaging his own subjective intention. This differs from a *shavuah*, which directly addresses personal obligation. The classic formulation of *mushba v'omed m'Har Sinai* — that a *shavuah* cannot take effect against a pre-existing biblical obligation — applies to *shavuot* but not to *nedarim*, because the *neder* operates by objectifying the forbidden item rather than directly counteracting his personal duty. Here, moreover, the *neder* is reinforcing rather than counteracting his biblical obligation, which makes it all the more straightforward.

### Annulling Vows

The *gemara* addresses the remaining problem: what about the opinion that holds one need not specify the particulars of a vow in order to obtain annulment? If that is the rule, the *kohen* could obtain a *heter* from a *chacham* without disclosing the underlying sin. The *gemara* resolves this: we ensure the vow is taken *berabim* — publicly. A public vow is objectified and is no longer merely a matter of the individual's internal intent; it becomes, in some sense, the property of the public. The *kohen* therefore cannot unilaterally obtain a *heter*, since the vow is no longer exclusively his to rescind.

But the *gemara* presses further: what about the opinion that even a vow made publicly can be annulled? The response is *de'adrinan lei al da'ata derabbim* — we ensure the vow is taken not merely in public, but *al da'at rabbim*, on the mindset of the masses, meaning the *kohen* explicitly declares that the vow is defined and interpreted according to what the general public would understand and intend by it, not by his own private understanding. *Meimar* rules as a matter of *halacha*: even according to the opinion that a *neder shehudar berabbim* can be annulled, a *neder al da'at rabbim* has no *hafara* — it cannot be undone.

## Heter Nedarim

### Public vs Intent

The conceptual distinction that emerges from the *gemara*'s discussion is a nuanced one. A *neder shehudar berabbim* — a vow declared in public — is disqualified from *heter* by virtue of the act of publicization itself. The vow has been externalized and objectified by having been made before an audience. A *neder al da'at rabbim* — a vow made explicitly on the basis of the masses' intent — operates differently: the vow is defined not merely by the fact of its public declaration, but by having been delegated, as it were, to the understanding of the community. It is the community's conception of the vow's terms that governs, not the individual's own.

*Tosafot* explains the underlying logic of why *al da'at rabbim* retains a *heter* for the sake of a *mitzvah*: since the vow is defined by what the masses would have intended, one can argue that most people would not have intended a vow to block the performance of a *mitzvah*. This implicit limitation — that the community's intent would not have extended to preventing a *mitzvah* — is what creates room for *hatara* in such circumstances. This is precisely the distinction between the two categories: *berabim* objectifies the vow completely, making any implicit limitation irrelevant; *al da'at rabbim* still operates through a framework of communal intent, which is susceptible to the mitzvah exception.

### Mitzvah Exception

The *gemara* rules that even a *neder al da'at rabbim* can be annulled when the annulment serves the purpose of a *mitzvah* — *aval lidvar mitzvah, yesh lo hafara*. The *mitzvah* consideration effectively overrides the otherwise ironclad quality of the communal vow.

### Rav Acha Story

The case the *gemara* adduces to illustrate this principle is deeply troubling. A *mikri dardeki* — a teacher of young children — named *Rav Acha* was apparently subjected to a vow preventing people from benefiting from his teaching, because he had mistreated the children in his charge. *Rashi* states that children died as a result of his conduct. The vow was apparently taken *al da'at rabbim*. Nevertheless, *Ravina* came and reversed the vow. The justification: *de'ishtachach de'havei dayka kevatei* — it was found that there was no other teacher as skilled as he was.

### Abuse Dismissal

This is a passage that deserves to be read with full moral seriousness rather than bracketed as merely a technical illustration. The dynamic described here — in which documented, severe abuse, attributed to a specific individual, is effectively set aside because of his professional excellence — is precisely the pattern that enables institutional abuse to persist. The person of acknowledged misconduct is retained, his harmful behavior minimized or explained away, because those in authority weigh his professional value against the harm he has caused and find the former to outweigh the latter.

The *gemara* appears to present this as a valid application of the *mitzvah* exception: since teaching children Torah is a *mitzvah*, and since no equally qualified replacement could be found, the *hatara* was justified. Whether this represents the *gemara*'s normative endorsement of that reasoning or simply its record of what occurred is a question worth pressing. What is not in doubt is the power of the precedent it establishes, and the danger of internalizing it as an acceptable trade-off. The logic of "but he is such a gifted teacher" has permitted enormous harm in communities across generations, and encountering it here — embedded in a *halachic* discussion of vow annulment — should give any careful reader serious pause.

The broader distinction *Tosafot* draws between *berabim* and *al da'at rabbim* clarifies why the *mitzvah* exception operates only in the latter case. When a vow is merely public, its objectification is total and admits no implicit conditions. When a vow is *al da'at rabbim*, it remains tethered to what the community would have intended — and the community, one may argue, would not have intended to block a genuine *mitzvah*. That is the formal logic. How it interacts with the moral calculus of protecting vulnerable children from harm is a question the *gemara* does not ask.

## Bechor Definitions

### Four Categories

The *mishnah* at the opening of the new *perek* presents four logical categories for the status of *bechor*: some who are *bechor lenachalah* but not *bechor lakohen*; some who are *bechor lakohen* but not *lenachalah*; some who are *bechor* for both purposes; and some who are *bechor* for neither. This *perek* is among the most widely studied in *Bekhorot*, because it directly addresses the laws of *pidyon haben* and the *bechor* inheritance entitlement — topics of immediate practical relevance.

### Peter vs Reishis

The *mishnah* identifies two distinct conceptual foundations for *bechor* status in these two domains. For *pidyon haben*, the operative concept is *peter rechem* — the breaching of the womb. This is a maternal definition: it refers to the first male child to emerge from the mother. For the inheritance of a double portion, the operative term is *reishit ono* — the first of his strength — a paternal definition rooted in the father's first viable offspring.

### Parent & Viability

Two major distinctions flow from this conceptual difference. First, the definition is *mother-based* for the *kohen* and *father-based* for inheritance. A man with multiple wives has a potential *bechor* from each wife for purposes of *pidyon haben*, while for inheritance, only one child can be the *bechor*. Second, the concept of *reishit ono* introduces a *viability requirement* for inheritance that is absent from *peter rechem*. A child must be the first *viable* offspring of the father to receive the *pi shenayim*. For *pidyon haben*, viability is not the criterion; what matters is whether a prior pregnancy had opened the womb, regardless of outcome.

## Bechor Inheritance

### After Miscarriage

The *mishnah* illustrates the first category — *bechor lenachalah* but not *bechor lakohen* — with a case where the firstborn viable child is preceded by a miscarriage or non-viable birth. Even if the first fetus emerged alive, if it was an eight-month premature infant and thus not viable — or if it was a full-term child that was born dead — it has already opened the womb. The subsequent child is therefore not *peter rechem* and does not require *pidyon haben*. But that subsequent child is the first *viable* offspring of the father and thus inherits the double portion as *bechor lenachalah*.

### Misshapen Fetus

A further case involves a miscarriage that does not have recognizable human form — it looks instead like an animal, a wild beast, or a bird. *Rabbi Meir* holds that even such a misshapen fetus counts as a prior birth for *peter rechem* purposes, so that the subsequent child is not *bechor lakohen*. *The Sages* disagree: *ad sheyehei bo mitzurat adam* — the prior birth must have a recognizably human form. If it does not, it has no legal effect on the subsequent child's *bechor* status for either purpose.

### Fetal Forms

The *mishnah* then enumerates specific fetal forms that, despite their unusual appearance, are treated as having human form and thus as legally significant births. These include a *sandal* — a fetus whose limbs are undifferentiated, forming one flat mass like a sandal sole; a *shilya*, the placenta or embryonic sac; a *shfiyr merukam*, a fetus that is formed to a degree of development (the detailed criteria being discussed in *Niddah*, but generally understood as forty days' development); and a fetus that had to be dismembered in utero to save the mother's life. All of these count as prior births for the purposes of *peter rechem*, so any child born after them will not be subject to *pidyon haben* — but will still be the *bechor lenachalah* if he is the first viable male born to his father.

## Bechor Kohen

### Childless Woman

The *mishnah* now presents the reverse category: *bechor lakohen* but not *bechor lenachalah*. The first case is that of a man who already has children and marries a woman who has not yet given birth. The first son she bears is her *peter rechem* — he is the first to breach her womb — and therefore requires *pidyon haben*. But he is not the man's *bechor lenachalah*, because the man already has older children who precede him for inheritance purposes.

### Conversion Status

The second case involves a woman who was a slave or a non-Jew and gave birth before her conversion or manumission. After becoming Jewish, she gives birth again. This new child is her first *Jewish* birth, and the *gemara* treats *peter rechem* as operative here for *pidyon haben*. But for inheritance, the child is not the man's *bechor lenachalah* if the father already has children. And if the question concerns the father's status, the principle of *katan shenolad dami* comes into play: when the father himself was a convert or freed slave, the children born before his change of status are not legally his children for inheritance purposes in Jewish law.

### R. Yose View

*Rabbi Yose HaGlili* takes a broader view: the first child born as a Jew counts as *peter rechem* even if the mother had biological children prior to her conversion, deriving this from the phrase *peter rechem beYisrael* — the first to breach the womb *in Israel*, meaning as a Jew. According to *Rabbi Yose HaGlili*, even if the child born after conversion is not the mother's first biological birth, it is her first Jewish birth and therefore obligates *pidyon haben*. This opinion does not carry the day as the majority ruling, but it reflects a coherent reading of the verse's qualifying phrase.

### Childless Man

The reverse situation — a woman who already has children marrying a man who does not — produces a *bechor lakohen* for the first child born of their union, since it is her first birth with this man and the child's first emergence from the womb, but not a *bechor lenachalah*, since the child is not the first viable son of this particular father.

### Pregnant Status

A pregnant convert or freed slave presents a related scenario. She gives birth after her conversion or manumission. The child is her first Jewish birth, making him *bechor lakohen* for *pidyon haben*. But the principle of *katan shenolad dami* — the child is treated as a new legal entity with no inherited status from the pre-conversion period — means the child has no paternal relationship in Jewish law that would establish him as a *bechor lenachalah* in the relevant sense.

## Paternity Doubt

### Mixed Infants

The *mishnah* concludes with cases of ambiguous paternity that affect *bechor* status. In the first scenario, two infants are mixed up at birth: one is subject to *pidyon haben* and one is not, because for the second mother it was not a first birth, or because she is a *bat kohen* or *bat levi* whose firstborn son is exempt. When the infants cannot be identified, the obligation of *pidyon haben* still applies — the father whose son requires redemption knows that somewhere among these two infants his son exists and is obligated, and he performs the *pidyon haben* for whichever one is his. But neither child can claim the inheritance of a *bechor* against the other brothers, because neither can prove his identity.

### Quick Remarriage

The second scenario involves a woman whose husband died and who remarried within three months — in violation of the requirement to wait, intended precisely to establish the paternity of a subsequent child. If she then gives birth, the father is uncertain: the child could be the son of the first husband or the second. For inheritance, this uncertainty is fatal to any claim as *bechor lenachalah*, since the child cannot prove which man is his father and therefore cannot establish entitlement against competing heirs. But for *pidyon haben*, paternity is irrelevant: the child is unambiguously this woman's first birth, and the status of *peter rechem* is established beyond doubt.

### Resolution

The resolution thus maps cleanly onto the underlying conceptual distinction established at the outset of the *perek*. *Bechor lakohen* is determined by the mother: as long as this child is known to be her first birth, he is *peter rechem* and *pidyon haben* applies, regardless of who his father is. *Bechor lenachalah* is determined by the father and requires proof: without certainty about paternity, no inheritance claim can be established. The child in these cases is in truth a *bechor lenachalah* — his biological reality has not changed — but he cannot practically claim that status, because civil monetary law requires proof, and here proof is unavailable.
