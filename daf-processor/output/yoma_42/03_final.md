# Yoma 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Service Overview & Red String

### Service Steps & Red String

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קָשַׁר לָשׁוֹן שֶׁל זְהוֹרִית בְּרֹאשׁ שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית שִׁילּוּחוֹ, וְלַנִּשְׁחָט — כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית שְׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The High Priest **tied a strip of crimson** wool **upon the head of the scapegoat and positioned** the goat **opposite the place from which it was dispatched,** i.e., near the gate through which it was taken; **and** the same was done to **the** goat that was to be **slaughtered, opposite** the **place of its slaughter.**

Before turning to the substance of today's *daf*, it is worth pausing to situate ourselves within the *avodat Yom Kippur* as we have been tracking it. The first step of the day is the *kohen gadol*'s *vidui* over his *par*, performed in the northern portion of the *azarah*. The second step is the *goralot*, the drawing of lots over the two goats. The third step — and the one that has been occupying the *Gemara*'s attention — is the tying of the red string. The fourth step is a second *vidui* over the *par*, this time on behalf of all the *kohanim*.

The question arises: who ties the red string in this third step? The relevant term is *kashar* — he ties — but there is no clear discussion requiring the *kohen gadol* specifically to perform it. More fundamentally, the red string tied onto the goat designated *la'azazel* is not mentioned anywhere in the Torah. The Torah does mention red strings in other contexts, and the *Gemara* has been drawing comparisons between them. According to the *Mishnah*, the red string is placed on the goat *la'azazel*, which is then stationed near the gate from which it will depart. The *Gemara* also records that a red string is placed around the neck of the goat designated *l'Hashem* — a detail that, as far as can be determined, is not echoed in the liturgical retellings of the *avodah*.

### Red String Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָנִין אָמַר רַב: עֵץ אֶרֶז וּשְׁנִי תּוֹלַעַת שֶׁקְּלָטָתַן שַׁלְהֶבֶת — כְּשֵׁרָה. מֵיתִיבִי: נִתְהַבְהֵב הַלָּשׁוֹן — מֵבִיא לָשׁוֹן אַחֵר וּמְקַדֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Torah requires that as part of the preparation of the ashes of the red heifer, cedar wood, hyssop, and a strip of crimson be cast "into the midst of the burning of the heifer" (Number 19:6). The Gemara discusses what happens if these items burn before actually reaching the burning mass of the heifer: **Rabbi Ḥanin said** that **Rav said:** If **the cedar wood and the strip of crimson were caught by the flame** of the burning heifer, and they burned in the air before coming into contact with the mass of the heifer itself, **it is valid.** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If the strip of crimson **was singed** before reaching the heart of fire, **he brings another strip and sanctifies it** by ensuring it burns together with the mass of the heifer.

The comparison that has been driving this discussion is between the red string of the *sa'ir* and the red string of the *parah adumah*. The red string used in the *parah adumah* ritual — together with the *eitz erez* and the *ezov* — is cast into the burning of the *parah*. This is where the issue of minimum weight arises: the verse uses the word *v'hishlich*, "and he cast it," and there is a question whether this implies that the object must fall immediately and directly when thrown, rather than floating down slowly. Does the red string need a minimum weight to ensure it descends with the directness that "casting" implies? That debate about *koved* is what the *Gemara* now resumes.

### Three Red Strings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּקוֹלַחַת, כָּאן בְּנִכְפֶּפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This is not difficult: Here,** the *baraita* is dealing **with** a case in which the flame **blazes high.** Since the strip was still a significant distance from the burning mass, its burning is invalid. **There,** Rabbi Ḥanin is dealing **with** a case in which the flame **blazes low,** in close proximity to the mass of the heifer. Therefore, it is considered to have been burned together with it and is valid.

There are in fact three red strings discussed in connection with these rituals: the string of the *parah adumah*, the string of the *sa'ir hamishtalach*, and the string of the *metzora* — the latter used in the purification process described in the Torah. All three will figure in the discussion of minimum weight requirements, each carrying a different amount for reasons that will become clear.

## Weight Minimums Debate

### Rava: Weight Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אוֹמֵר: כּוֹבֶד תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: לָמָּה כּוֹרְכָן — כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כּוּלָּן בַּאֲגוּדָּה אַחַת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהֶן כּוֹבֶד, וְיִפְּלוּ לְתוֹךְ שְׂרֵיפַת הַפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The *baraita* and Rabbi Ḥanin's ruling follow different opinions with regard to whether the strip of crimson needs to have weight. The *baraita* assumes the items must reach the heart of the fire; therefore, they must have weight. Rabbi Ḥanin assumes the items don't need to reach the heart of the fire; therefore, they do not need to have weight. The issue of whether the strip of crimson needs to have **weight is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Why** does **he wrap** the cedar wood and the hyssop together using the strip of crimson? **So that they will all be in a single bundle** and burn simultaneously, as implied by the fact the Torah mentions all three together; **this is the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: So that they will have weight and fall into the burning heifer.**

The *Gemara* on the bottom of 41b states: *amar lei Rava, koved tana'i hi* — Rava declares that the question of whether the red string of the *parah adumah* requires a minimum weight is in fact a dispute among *tanna'im*. The *Gemara* notes that, unlike the red string of the *sa'ir*, the string of the *parah adumah* does not need to be divided in two; the only question there is one of weight.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** eitiveih Abaye, keitzad hu oseh, korchan beshiyarei lashon. אֵימָא בִּזְנַב לָשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye raised an objection to this: *eitiveih Abaye, keitzad hu oseh*? A *Mishnah* concerning *parah adumah* states *korchan beshiyarei lashon* — one wraps the *erez* and *ezov* with the remnant of the red string. The word *shiyarei* seems to imply that the string was indeed cut, suggesting a minimum length — and therefore a minimum weight — is required for the *parah adumah* string as well. The *Gemara* responds: *eima biznav lashon* — this does not mean a literal remnant from having been cut, but rather a frayed end, like the fringe at the bottom of a comb. The *shiyarei* here refers to the appearance of fraying, not to an actual division of the string.

### Flame vs. Fire Debate

The *Gemara* presses further with another challenge: if *shechitah* is not an *avodah* on *Yom Kippur*, why is it *psulah be-zar* by the *parah adumah*? Is the *parah adumah* not even a *korban* — it is sanctified with the sanctity of *kadshei bedek ha-bayit*, like a beam of wood consecrated for Temple use, not with intrinsic sacrificial sanctity — so should it not be *a fortiori* permissible with a *zar*? The *Gemara* responds with an important principle.

### El Toch Requirement

If the fire has very high leaping flames, and the string is consumed while held aloft above it, one has not fulfilled the requirement, because the Torah says *el toch srefat haparah* — into the very midst of the burning of the *parah*. But if the flames are low, then being consumed at that low level is tantamount to being within the fire itself, because one is so close to the physical source of the combustion. The critical point is not that the string must physically strike the ground or the pile on the bottom, but that it must be close enough to the base of the flame to count as being *el toch* — within the midst. Being consumed by a high flame, far removed from the source, does not satisfy this criterion.

### Three Weight Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁלֹשׁ לְשׁוֹנוֹת שָׁמַעְתִּי, אַחַת שֶׁל פָּרָה, וְאַחַת שֶׁל שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ, וְאַחַת שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע. אַחַת מִשְׁקַל עֲשָׂרָה זוּז, וְאַחַת מִשְׁקַל שְׁנֵי סְלָעִים, וְאַחַת מִשְׁקַל שֶׁקֶל, וְאֵין לִי לְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said** in the name of **Rabbi Yoḥanan: I heard** a teaching that there is a distinction between **three** strips of crimson: **One of the** red **heifer, and one of the scapegoat, and one of the leper. One** of them must have the **weight of ten *zuz*; and one** of them must have the **weight of two *sela*,** which is eight *zuz*; **and one** of them must have the **weight of a shekel,** which is two *zuz*, **but I cannot explain** which is which.

Returning to the question of minimum weight: *ki ata Rav Dimi amar Rabbi Yochanan — shalosh leshonot shamati*. When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that he had heard a teaching about the three red strings — that of the *parah adumah*, that of the *sa'ir hamishtalach*, and that of the *metzora*. Each has a different weight requirement: one must weigh ten *zuz*, one two *sela'im*, and one one *shekel*. The proportions are easy to remember: ten, two, and one. However, Rabbi Yochanan could not explain which weight belongs to which context.

### Ravin Explains Weights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל פָּרָה — מִשְׁקַל עֲשָׂרָה זוּז, וְשֶׁל שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ — מִשְׁקַל שְׁנֵי סְלָעִים, וְשֶׁל מְצוֹרָע — מִשְׁקַל שֶׁקֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The strip of crimson **of the** red **heifer** has the **weight** of **ten *zuz*; and** the strip **of the scapegoat** has the **weight of two *sela*,** which is eight *zuz*; **and** the strip **of the leper** has the **weight of a shekel,** which is two *zuz*.

When Ravin subsequently came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he provided the explanation in the name of Rav Yonatan, at the top of 42a. The *parah adumah* string must weigh the most — ten *zuz* — because it must be cast into the fire and fall in directly. The *sa'ir hamishtalach* string requires two *sela'im*, the intermediate amount, because it must be long enough to be divided into two portions. It is admittedly somewhat indirect to use weight as a proxy for length — weight only serves as a reliable indicator of length if one assumes a standard thickness of string — but that is the logic employed. The *metzora* string requires only one *shekel*, since neither of the previous considerations applies: it does not need to be cast into fire and it does not need to be divided.

One might ask why the *metzora* string needs any minimum at all. The *Gemara* does not offer a clear explanation for this baseline requirement. Rav Yochanan adds:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן חֲלַפְתָּא וְרַבָּנַן בְּפָרָה, חַד אָמַר: מִשְׁקַל עֲשָׂרָה, וְחַד אָמַר: מִשְׁקַל שֶׁקֶל. וְסִימָנָיךְ: ״אֶחָד הַמַּרְבֶּה וְאֶחָד הַמַּמְעִיט״.
>
> **Translation:** And **Rabbi Yoḥanan** further said: **Rabbi Shimon ben Ḥalafta and the Rabbis disagree** with regard to the strip of crimson **of** the red **heifer. One said:** It has the **weight of ten** *zuz*. **And one said:** It has the **weight of one shekel. And your mnemonic** for remembering that while both assume that only one of the extreme values was required no one suggests the middle value of two *sela* is required, is the aphorism from a mishna: God equally values **both the one who gives much and the one who gives little** as long as his intention is to Heaven.

*pligi vah Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta v'rabanan* — there is a dispute between Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta and the Sages specifically about the *parah* string. One says it must weigh ten *zuz*, the other says merely one *shekel*. The memorable quality of this dispute is its symmetry: one goes to the highest extreme, the other to the lowest, and nobody assigns the *parah* the middle value of two *sela'im*. The mnemonic offered is the famous phrase *echad hamarbeh v'echad hamamit, uvilvad sheyachaven libo laShamayim* — whether one does more or less, what matters is that one's heart be directed toward Heaven.

### Debate Focus Corrected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: לָא בְּפָרָה פְּלִיגִי, אֶלָּא בְּשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ פְּלִיגִי. וְהָהוּא יוֹמָא נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבְיָא בַּר קִיסִי, וְאַנְּחוּ בַּהּ סִימָנָא: רַבְיָא [בַּר] קִיסִי מְכַפֵּר כְּשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti said to Ravina:** It is **not with regard to the** strip of the red **heifer** that **they disagree; rather,** it is **with regard to** the strip of **the scapegoat** that **they disagree. And on that** very **day** that they disputed this issue, **Ravya bar Kisi** died, **and they made a mnemonic out of it,** associating the *halakha* with his name: The death of **Ravya bar Kisi atones like the scapegoat,** since the death of the righteous person atones for his generation.

At this point, Rav Yirmiyah of Dipti corrects what he sees as a misidentification of the debate's subject: *lo b'parah pligi, ela bisi'arei hamishtalach pligi* — the dispute between the extreme of ten and the extreme of one is not about the *parah adumah* string at all, but about the *sa'ir hamishtalach*. Why one would insist on such a heavy string for the scapegoat, while another would permit the lightest possible string, is not fully explained — though one might speculate that preventing the string from blowing off or breaking during the goat's journey could motivate a heavier requirement.

### Death & Scapegoat Link

The *Gemara* then notes: *ve-hahu yoma nach nafshei de-Rav Ya bar Kisi* — on that very day when this dispute was being debated, Rav Ya bar Kisi passed away. *Ve-anchu vah siman* — they placed this as a mnemonic to remember that the debate concerned the *sa'ir hamishtalach* rather than the *parah adumah*: *Rav Ya bar Kisi mechaper ke-sa'ir hamishtalach* — Rav Ya bar Kisi atones like the scapegoat atones. On the surface this is presented as a simple memory device, but it opens onto one of the more theologically striking passages in this section of the *Gemara*.

The principle at work here is *mitat tzadikim mechaperet* — the death of righteous individuals brings atonement. This idea merits reflection precisely because it tends to be underemphasized in traditional discourse, perhaps because it was appropriated so centrally by Christian theology in connection with the death of Jesus. But here it appears straightforwardly in the *Gemara* as an accepted theological datum, analogized to the *sa'ir hamishtalach*. The parallel is evocative: the goat carries the sins of the community; the *tzadik*, one might say, carries its merit. Both are in some sense removed from the community — one cast away to the wilderness, the other taken by death — and yet both, by their departure, effect some form of metaphysical atonement.

This also invites reflection on why the *sa'ir la'azazel* itself brings atonement, which is theologically difficult in its own right. Unlike a *korban* offered on the altar — where one can understand the symbolism of a life given to God, perhaps even representing the offerer's own life as in the logic of the *akeidah* — the scapegoat is not offered to God at all. It is sent away, driven off a cliff, dispatched to *Azazel*, whatever that means. The mechanics of its atonement remain genuinely opaque. The *Gemara* connects these two opaque forms of atonement — the scapegoat and the death of the righteous — and allows that parallel to stand without resolving either.

Regarding terminology: the word *lashon* — literally "tongue" — is the term used in rabbinic Hebrew for the red string, as opposed to *chut*, which is the biblical term found in *chut ha-shani*. A *lashon* in this context refers specifically to a string or cord, and more precisely to the distinctive red string used in these rituals.

## Slaughterings Debate

### Which Needs Kohen?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: שְׁתֵּי שְׁחִיטוֹת שָׁמַעְתִּי, אַחַת שֶׁל פָּרָה, וְאַחַת שֶׁל פָּרוֹ. אַחַת כְּשֵׁרָה בְּזָר, וְאַחַת פְּסוּלָה בְּזָר, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: I heard** a teaching that there is a distinction between **two slaughters: One of the** red **heifer, and one of the bull** of the High Priest on Yom Kippur. The slaughter of **one** of them **is valid** even if done **by a non-priest, and** the slaughter of the other **one is invalid** if done **by a non-priest. But I do not know which of them** is which.

The *Gemara* now introduces a new parallel to the three-string discussion: *amar Rabbi Yitzchak, shtei shechitot shamati* — Rabbi Yitzchak says he heard a teaching about two *shechitot*: one of the *parah adumah* and one of the *par* of the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. Just as the three strings had different weight requirements that were debated and disputed, these two *shechitot* differ in their requirements regarding whether a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — may perform them: *achat kesheirah be-zar*, one is valid if performed by a non-*kohen*, *ve-achat psulah be-zar*, the other is not. And *ve-lo yadana hei mineihu* — he does not know which is which.

One might suppose the answer is obvious. But the question is genuinely complex. By *Yom Kippur*, there is a general principle that everything must be performed by the *kohen gadol*. Yet the *Gemara* has already established that certain actions on *Yom Kippur* are not considered *avodah* and therefore may not require the *kohen gadol* — the drawing of the lots being one example. *Shechitah* is normally *kesheirah be-zar* in all contexts. Does that remain true even on *Yom Kippur*? And conversely, why should the *parah adumah* — performed outside the *Mikdash* entirely — require a *kohen* for its *shechitah*?

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: שְׁחִיטַת פָּרָה וּפָרוֹ, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל. חַד אָמַר: פָּרָה פְּסוּלָה, פָּרוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. וְחַד אָמַר: פָּרוֹ פְּסוּלָה, פָּרָה כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** An amoraic dispute **was stated:** With regard to the **slaughter of the** red **heifer and the bull** of the High Priest on Yom Kippur, there is a dispute between **Rav and Shmuel: One said:** A red **heifer** slaughtered by a non-priest **is invalid,** whereas the **bull** of the High Priest slaughtered by a non-priest **is valid. And** the other **one said: His bull is invalid,** but the red **heifer is valid.**

*Itmar* — there is a recorded dispute: *Rav u-Shmuel*. One says the *shechitah* of the *parah* is *psulah be-zar* and the *shechitah* of the *kohen gadol*'s *par* on *Yom Kippur* is *kesheirah be-zar*; the other says the opposite. The *Gemara* then attempts to assign the positions: *tistayem de-Rav hu de-amar parah psulah* — it can be concluded that it is Rav who holds the *parah* is invalid when performed by a non-*kohen*.

### Evidence for Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַב הוּא דְּאָמַר פָּרָה פְּסוּלָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: שְׁחִיטַת פָּרָה בְּזָר פְּסוּלָה, וְאָמַר רַב עֲלַהּ: ״אֶלְעָזָר״ וְ״חוּקָּה״ שָׁנִינוּ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Conclude** that **it is Rav who said** that if **the heifer** is slaughtered by a non-priest it **is invalid, as Rabbi Zeira said: The slaughter of the heifer by a non-priest is invalid. And Rav said concerning this** statement of Rabbi Zeira: This is because **we learned in** the Torah's description of the slaughter of the red heifer both **"Elazar** the priest" (Numbers 19:3) and **"statute"** (Numbers 19:2). Because Elazar is mentioned, it indicates the slaughter should be performed by a priest, and the use of the term "statute" teaches that one may not deviate from any of the details of the service as delineated in the verses; if one does deviate, the service is invalid. Clearly then, Rav held that if the heifer is slaughtered by a non-priest it is invalid.

The evidence for this attribution comes from Rabbi Zeira, who says *shechitat parah be-zar psulah*, and then from Rava's comment on that ruling: *Elazar ve-chukah shaninu bah* — the reason is that the *parah adumah* pericope in *Parashat Chukat* repeatedly invokes *Elazar ha-kohen*, and the *parsha* opens with the phrase *zot chukat ha-Torah*. The word *chok* signals that something is absolutely binding and indispensable. The combination of the *chok* language and the repeated emphasis on *Elazar* yields the ruling that a *kohen* is required. Since Rav appears to be affirming and explaining this ruling, he is identified as its author.

### The Challenge from Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, מַאי שְׁנָא פָּרָה — דִּכְתִיב ״אֶלְעָזָר״ וְ״חוּקָּה״, פָּרוֹ נָמֵי — הָא כְּתִיב ״אַהֲרֹן״ וְ״חוּקָּה״! שְׁחִיטָה לָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes Rav's opinion: **And** according to **Rav, what is different** with regard to the red **heifer,** that if it is slaughtered by a non-priest it is invalid? As Rav explained, it is different in **that,** with regard to it, **it is written "Elazar" and "statute."** But with regard to **the bull** of the High Priest **it is also written "Aaron"** the High Priest (Leviticus 16:11) and **"statute"** (Leviticus 16:34). Based on Rav's logic, that indicates that the priest is indispensable. The symmetrical argument applies to *Yom Kippur*: the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is framed by *Bezot yavo Aharon* and concludes with *ve-hayeta zot lachem l'chukat olam*, again combining the personality of *Aharon* with the language of *chok*. By the same logic, everything on *Yom Kippur* should require a *kohen gadol*. But the *Gemara* responds: **Slaughtering is not** classified as a sacrificial **service;**

### Shechitah Not Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי פָּרָה נָמֵי! שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה, דְּקׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** therefore, it does not have to be done by a priest. The Gemara challenges this answer: **If so,** by the same logic, the red **heifer** should **also** be valid when slaughtered by a non-priest. The Gemara concludes: The logic that slaughtering is not classified as a sacrificial service does not apply in the case of the red heifer because the red **heifer is different.** It has the sanctity **of** items **consecrated for Temple maintenance,** and therefore the principles that apply to other offerings do not necessarily apply to it.

Why is *shechitah* not an *avodah*? Rashi's explanation points to the evidence from the opening *parsha* of *Vayikra*: the Torah describes the owner bringing the *korban* and performing *shechitah*, and only then introduces *bnei Aharon* to receive the blood. The *kohanim* enter the picture after the *shechitah*, which is evidence that *shechitah* does not require a *kohen* as a general rule. Since the criterion for being an *avodah* is that it requires a *kohen*, the general permissibility of *shechitah* by a non-*kohen* is evidence that it is not an *avodah*. Once it is not an *avodah*, even on *Yom Kippur* it is excluded from the *kohen gadol* requirement.

Tosafot offers a different explanation: *shechitah* is not an *avodah* because it is a procedure required even outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. *Melikah* — the nipping of a bird's neck in the *Mikdash* — is a uniquely Temple ritual and therefore an *avodah* requiring a *kohen*. But *shechitah* is performed universally: for ordinary meat, for *korbanot*, for the *eglah arufah* outside the *Mikdash*. This universality defines it as something that, even when it takes place in the *Mikdash*, retains a non-*avodah* character. The actions that are genuinely *avodah* — receiving the blood, moving the blood, throwing the blood on the altar — are all uniquely Temple acts.

### Rav Sheshet's Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו כָּל דְּכֵן הוּא? אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמַּרְאוֹת נְגָעִים, דְּלָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא וּבָעֲיָא כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** But **is it not** arguable, by **an *a fortiori* inference,** that the slaughter of the red heifer by a non-priest is valid? If the slaughter of the bull of the High Priest, which is an actual offering, is valid when performed by a non-priest, then all the more so the slaughter of the red heifer, which is not an offering, should be valid when similarly performed by a non-priest. **Rav Shisha, son of Rav Idi, said:** The slaughter of the red heifer by a non-priest is invalid. The *halakha* is **just as in** the case of **appearances of leprosy, which,** despite their **not** being a sacrificial **service,** still **require the priesthood.** Only a priest may declare the signs of leprosy to be pure or impure. It is apparent from this case that the logic of the *a fortiori* inference does not hold.

The *Gemara* presses: if *shechitah* is not an *avodah* on *Yom Kippur*, why is it *psulah be-zar* by the *parah adumah*? Is the *parah adumah* not even a *korban* — it is sanctified with the sanctity of *kadshei bedek ha-bayit*, like a beam of wood consecrated for Temple use, not with intrinsic sacrificial sanctity — so should it not be *a fortiori* permissible with a *zar*? The logical point here is important. Just because every *avodah* requires a *kohen* does not mean that every requirement of a *kohen* indicates an *avodah*. There are non-*avodah* functions that nonetheless require *kehunah*. The *parah adumah* process is entirely outside the *Mikdash* and is not about *avodah* at all — but if the Torah requires a *kohen* for some part of it, it requires a *kohen* for all of it, including *shechitah*. Inside the *Mikdash*, where the framework is *avodah*, one excludes *shechitah* from the *kohen gadol* requirement because it is not an *avodah*. Outside the *Mikdash*, that framework does not apply, and if the *kohen* requirement is there at all, it governs the entire process.

### Attention Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב: שֶׁלֹּא יַסִּיחַ דַּעְתּוֹ מִמֶּנָּה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל, שֶׁלֹּא יַסִּיחַ דַּעְתּוֹ מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ: מִ״וְּשָׂרַף אֶת הַפָּרָה לְעֵינָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav,** who assumes the slaughter is performed by the priest himself, how does he interpret the phrase: "Before him"? He understands it to mean **that he should not divert his attention from** the heifer from the moment of slaughtering until the completion of the process. As with other matters of purification, one is required to maintain his attention on the matter; any distraction can invalidate the process. **And from where does Shmuel,** who already derived something from this verse, derive **that he should not divert his attention** from it? **He derives it** from the verse: **"And he shall burn the heifer in his sight"** (Numbers 19:5).

The *Gemara* continues: *she-lo yasi'ach da'ato mimena* — Rav's understanding of the phrase *v'shachat osah le'fanav*, "and he shall slaughter it before him," is not that someone else performs the *shechitah* in *Elazar*'s presence, but that *Elazar* himself performs the *shechitah* while being fully psychologically present, not distracted. This is one of the remarkable features of the *parah adumah* ritual — the requirement of mental presence and attentiveness.

Shmuel, who holds the *parah adumah* does not require a *kohen* for *shechitah*, reads *v'shachat osah le'fanav* straightforwardly as indicating that someone else performs the *shechitah* while *Elazar* supervises. Where does Shmuel then derive the non-distraction requirement?

### Both Verses Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב: חַד בִּשְׁחִיטָה וְחַד בִּשְׂרֵיפָה. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בִּשְׁחִיטָה — מִשּׁוּם דִּתְחִילַּת עֲבוֹדָה הִיא, אֲבָל שְׂרֵיפָה — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בִּשְׂרֵיפָה — מִשּׁוּם דְּהַשְׁתָּא הוּא דְּמִתַּכְשְׁרָא פָּרָה, אֲבָל שְׁחִיטָה — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And what does** **Rav** learn from this verse? He holds that **one** verse is needed to teach the requirement to maintain one's attention **with regard to slaughtering, and one** verse is needed to teach the requirement **with regard to burning. And** it is **necessary** to have the requirement stated in both cases **since, if the Merciful One had written it** only **with regard to slaughtering,** I might have limited the requirement to that case **because it is the start of the** Temple **service; but** with regard to **burning, I could say** that **no,** there is no requirement. Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach the requirement also in the case of burning. **And if the Merciful One had written** the requirement only **with regard to burning,** I might have limited it to that case, **because it is now that** the **heifer is** actually **being made ready** to be used; **but** with regard to **slaughtering, I could say** that **no,** there is no requirement. Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach the requirement also in that case.

### What Is Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי אֲסִיפַת אֶפְרָהּ וּמִילּוּי מַיִם לְקִידּוּשׁ, ״לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת לְמֵי נִדָּה״ כְּתִיב. אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי הַשְׁלָכַת עֵץ אֶרֶז וְאֵזוֹב וּשְׁנִי תוֹלָעַת, דְּלָאו גּוּפַהּ דְּפָרָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** As the requirement is mentioned with regard to two stages of the rite of the red heifer, this suggests that it does not exist for every stage. Therefore, the Gemara asks: It is written this way in order **to exclude what** stage? **If we say, to exclude** from the requirement the stages of **gathering its ashes and filling the water for sanctification,** i.e., taking the water in order to pour it on the ashes and mix them together, this is mistaken because: **"And it** shall be kept** for the congregation of the children of Israel** for a water of sprinkling"** (Numbers 19:9) **was written,** and the use of the term "shall be kept" indicates that one must maintain attention even during these stages. **Rather,** it is **to exclude** from the requirement the stage of **casting the cedar wood, and the hyssop, and the strip of crimson** into the fire. The requirement does not apply to this stage, **as they do not** pertain to **the heifer itself.**

## Proof from Daytime

### Braita's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא הַזָּאַת מֵימֶיהָ שֶׁאֵין כְּשֵׁרִין בָּאִשָּׁה כְּבָאִישׁ, וְאֵין כְּשֵׁרִין אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם. מִנַּיִין לְרַבּוֹת שְׁחִיטָתָהּ וְקַבָּלַת דָּמָהּ וְהַזָּאַת דָּמָהּ וּשְׂרֵיפָתָהּ וְהַשְׁלָכַת עֵץ אֶרֶז וְאֵזוֹב וּשְׁנִי תוֹלָעַת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תּוֹרָה״. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אַף אֲסִיפַת אֶפְרָהּ וּמִילּוּי מַיִם וְקִידּוּשׁ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֹאת״.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** a derivation **only** with regard to **the sprinkling of** the red heifer's **waters, that they are not valid** if sprinkled **by a woman as** opposed to **when** sprinkled **by a man, and that** the sprinkling **is valid only during the day.** The verse states: "And the pure one shall sprinkle upon the impure on the third day" (Numbers 19:19). The use of the terms "pure one," in the masculine form, and "day," indicate that the sprinkling is valid only if performed by a man during the day. **From where** is it derived **to include** the following stages among those stages that must be performed by a man during the day in order to be valid: **Its slaughter; and** the **collection of its blood; and** the **sprinkling of its blood; and its burning; and** the **casting of** the **cedar wood, and** the **hyssop, and** the **strip of crimson** into the fire? **The verse states: "Law"** (Numbers 19:2), at the beginning of the section on the red heifer, to indicate that the same rules govern all stages of the rite. As such, I **might** have thought that **I should include even the collection of its ashes, and the filling of waters, and the sanctification.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "This is the statute of the law" (Numbers 19:2). The word **this** limits the scope of the requirement that the rite must be performed by a man and during the day from applying to these stages.

*Masiv Rav Yehoshua bar Abba l'siyu'ei le-Rav* — Rav Yehoshua bar Abba brings a challenge in support of Rav's position, which holds that the *shechitah* of the *parah adumah* is *psulah be-zar*. The source is a *braita* that addresses which parts of the *parah adumah* process must be performed during the day. The structure of the argument is as follows.

The *braita* begins: *ein li ella hazat meimeha she-ein kesheirah be-isha ke-ve-ish ve-ein kesheirah ella bayom* — we know that the sprinkling of the purification water cannot be done by a woman as it can by a man, and it can only be done during the day. From this foundation, the *braita* asks: how do we derive that the other parts of the process — *shechitah*, receiving the blood, sprinkling the blood, burning the *parah*, and throwing the cedar, hyssop, and red string into the fire — also must be done during the day? The answer: *Talmud lomar, zot chukat ha-Torah* — the word *Torah* here is inclusive, drawing everything under the same category. Since the sprinkling must be daytime, and everything is grouped together, all other parts of the process must also be daytime.

But perhaps the daytime requirement extends further still — to gathering the ashes, filling the water, and the *kiddush* of putting the ashes in the water? *Talmud lomar, zot* — the word *zot* is limiting; not everything falls within the category.

### Torah Language Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת אֵלּוּ, וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת אֵלּוּ? אַחַר שֶׁרִיבָּה הַכָּתוּב וּמִיעֵט, אָמְרַתְּ: הֲרֵי אָנוּ לְמֵדִין כּוּלָּן מֵהַזָּאַת מֵימֶיהָ, מָה הַזָּאַת מֵימֶיהָ אֵינָן כְּשֵׁרִין בָּאִשָּׁה כְּבָאִישׁ, וְאֵין כְּשֵׁרִין אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם — אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא שְׁחִיטָתָהּ וְקַבָּלַת דָּמָהּ, וְהַזָּאַת דָּמָהּ וּשְׂרֵיפָתָהּ, וְהַשְׁלָכַת עֵץ אֶרֶז וְאֵזוֹב וּשְׁנִי תוֹלָעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if the word "law" serves to include all stages in the *halakha* and the word "this" serves to exclude some stages, **what did you see to include these** stages **and to exclude those? After** noting **that the verse includes** some stages **and excludes** others, **you should say** the following logical argument: **Indeed, we learn all** the stages **from the sprinkling of its waters: Just as the sprinkling of its waters is not valid** if done **by a woman** as opposed to **when** done **by a man, and it is valid only during the day, so too I will include** in the same requirement **its slaughter; and the collection of its blood; and the sprinkling of its blood; and its burning; and** the **casting of** the **cedar wood, and** the **hyssop, and** the **strip of crimson.**

The obvious question is: what criterion determines what falls inside the category and what falls outside? The *braita* states: *achar she-riba ha-katuv u-mi'et, amarta, harei anu le-meidin kulam me-hazat meimeha* — after the Torah both included and excluded, one reasons that everything is learned from the sprinkling. The logic is: just as the sprinkling cannot be done by a woman (only by a man) and can only be done during the day, we will apply both of those criteria to the other parts of the process.

### Category Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְאֵין כְּשֵׁרִין בָּאִשָּׁה כְּבָאִישׁ — אֵין כְּשֵׁרִין אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם. וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֲסִיפַת אֶפְרָהּ וּמִילּוּי מַיִם וְקִידּוּשׁ, הוֹאִיל וּכְשֵׁרִין בָּאִשָּׁה כְּבָאִישׁ — כְּשֵׁרִין נָמֵי בַּיּוֹם וּבַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Since these** stages **are not valid** if done **by a woman** as opposed to **when** done **by a man,** as indicated by the verses that state that they are to be done by a serving priest, who by definition is a man, then it is logical to also include these cases in the *halakha* that **they are valid only during the day. And I exclude the collection of its ashes, and the filling of water, and the sanctification, since these** stages **are valid** when done **by a woman as** they are **when** done **by a man,** as the verses never suggest a requirement that they be done by a man. Therefore, it is logical that these stages **are valid both during the day and during the night.**

The category determination then proceeds: *ho'il ve-ein kesheirah be-isha ke-ve-ish ein kesheirah ella bayom* — since the other parts of the process share the criterion of requiring a man rather than a woman, we will also apply the criterion of daytime to them. *Af ani arbeh shechitata ve-kabbalat dama ve-hazat dama ve-sreifata ve-hashlachat eitz erez ve-ezov u-sheni tola'at* — I will include the *shechitah*, the receiving of the blood, the sprinkling of the blood, the burning, and the throwing of the wood and red string. All of these share the criterion of not being performable by a woman; therefore, by analogy to the sprinkling, they all require daytime.

*U-motziani asifat afara u-milui mayim ve-kiddush* — I will exclude the gathering of the ashes, the filling of the water, and the *kiddush*. Why? *Ho'il ve-ksheirim be-isha ke-ve-ish* — since those can be done by either a man or a woman, they do not share that restricting criterion, and therefore *ksheirim nami be-yom u-va-lailah* — they may be performed at night as well. The logic is essentially: those things that already share one of the two restrictive features of the sprinkling will be assumed to share the other; those that share neither restrictive feature share neither.

### Rashi on Judgment

A remarkable *Rashi* must be noted here. Rashi comments on the *braita*'s logic of inclusion and exclusion, observing that *ve-lo peiresh mah ribah u-mah mi'at* — the Torah does not specify what falls into which category. *U-mei'atah lo masran ela le-hitbonen bah et ha-emet ve-et ha-yashar* — and therefore the Torah handed it over to the Sages only to reason into it, to find out what is true and what is right. The word *yashar* — straight, upright — here suggests that the Torah deliberately left this categorization to rational and moral judgment. It is a striking formulation: not *da'at Torah* in the sense of an authoritative ruling, but an explicit invitation to the exercise of reasoned human judgment within the halakhic framework.

### Woman Exclusion Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי מַאי תְּיוּבְתָּא? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּפְסוּלִין בָּאִשָּׁה פְּסוּלִין נָמֵי בְּזָר, הַזָּאַת מֵימֶיהָ תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁפְּסוּלִין בָּאִשָּׁה וּכְשֵׁרִין בְּזָר!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua bar Abba originally cited the *baraita* to challenge Shmuel's opinion that the slaughter of the red heifer by a non-priest is valid. The Gemara asks: **And what is the challenge from this** *baraita*? **If we say that** since **that** these stages **are invalid** if done **by a woman** one should conclude that **they are also invalid** if done **by a non-priest,** then **let** the stage of **sprinkling the waters serve as a proof** that one should not necessarily connect these two *halakhot*, **because** the sprinkling is **invalid** if done **by a woman and** yet **valid** if done **by a non-priest.** If so, what was the basis for the challenge?

This leads to the central argument of the *braita* as a proof that *shechitah be-zar psulah*. The *Gemara* asks: *ve-ha mai tiyuvta* — in what way is this actually a challenge to the position that *shechitah* can be done by a non-*kohen*? The apparent argument is: *eilema mid-paslinan be-isha paslinan nami be-zar* — from the fact that we disqualify a woman, we disqualify a non-*kohen*; the exclusion of women is really about their not being *kohanim*, and therefore it demonstrates that *shechitah* requires a *kohen*.

### Sprinkling Exception

But the *Gemara* objects: *haza'ah tochi'ach* — the sprinkling itself disproves this inference. The sprinkling cannot be done by a woman but can be done by a non-*kohen*. So the exclusion of women is not necessarily about requiring a *kohen*. If the sprinkling — which was the paradigm case from which the whole argument was derived — requires a man but not a *kohen*, then perhaps *shechitah* also merely requires a man without requiring a *kohen*.

Abaye responds:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הַיְינוּ תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ: אִשָּׁה מַאי טַעְמָא? ״אֶלְעָזָר״ — וְלֹא אִשָּׁה, זָר נָמֵי: ״אֶלְעָזָר״ — וְלֹא זָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This is the challenge:** With regard to **a woman, what is the reason** she may not perform these stages? The verse states **"Elazar,"** indicating the requirement for a man **and not a woman.** The same logic should **also** apply to **a non-priest:** The verse states: **"Elazar,"** indicating the requirement for a priest **and not a non-priest.**

*hainu tiyuvteih* — this is precisely why it remains a challenge. *Isha mai ta'ama* — what is the basis for excluding a woman from *shechitah* and from burning and from receiving the blood? For the sprinkling, we know from explicit *pesukim* (*ish tahor*, *ish tahor*) that it must be a man, and separately that a non-*kohen* is valid. But for the *shechitah* and burning — where does the exclusion of a woman come from? The only basis in the text for excluding a woman from those parts of the process is *Elazar ve-lo isha* — the verse specifying *Elazar*, which excludes a woman. But if that is the basis, then *zar nami* — a non-*kohen* is likewise excluded, since the specification of *Elazar* implies *Elazar ve-lo zar*, not *Elazar* and not a non-*kohen*. The exclusion of woman in this part of the process is inseparable from the exclusion of the non-*kohen*; both flow from the same verse. Therefore, one cannot argue that *shechitah* requires a man but not a *kohen* — for those two conclusions are driven by separate textual grounds only in the case of the sprinkling, not for *shechitah* and the other parts of the process.

## Pesukim Reading

### Ulla's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כּוּלָּהּ, מַשְׁמָע מוֹצִיא מִיַּד מַשְׁמָע, וּמַשְׁמָע מִמֵּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers other aspects of the rite of the red heifer: **Ulla said:** Throughout **the entire passage** of the red heifer, certain conditions apply to some stages of the rite, but not to others. These conditions are derived from the phrasing of the verses. In some stages, the conditions **implied** by the phrasing of the verse **preclude** the application of conditions that are **implied** by a previous verse describing a previous stage. A verse describing one stage may imply the existence of a certain condition which applies to that stage but a verse describing a subsequent stage indicates that that condition does not apply to the subsequent stage. However, in other stages, the conditions **implied** by the phrasing of the verse stand **on their own** and continue to apply in subsequent stages, i.e., the verses in subsequent stages do not exclude the application of the condition derived from the verses in a previous stage.

*Amar Ulla kol ha-parsha kullah mashma motzi miyad mashma u-mashma mimela* — Ulla offers a general observation about the entire *parsha* of *parah adumah*: throughout the section, the verses oscillate between *mashma* — the simple, natural reading of the text — and *motzi miyad mashma* — exceptions that deviate from what the text plainly implies. Sometimes the rabbinic ruling follows naturally from the plain sense of the verse; sometimes the ruling deliberately departs from it.

### Mashma Definition

A note on terminology is in order here. The word *mashma* — "it sounds like," "it implies" — is closely related to the word *pshat*, but they are not identical. We commonly assume *pshat* means the plain, simple sense of a verse: what the text would have meant to its original audience. But in rabbinic usage, *pshat* more precisely refers to the standardized, canonically accepted rabbinic reading. This is evidenced by the *Gemara*'s locution *pshuto ke-mashmao* — the *pshat* of this verse is as it sounds. If *pshat* simply meant the plain sense, this phrase would be tautological. Rather, *mashma* is the genuinely plain sense of the text — what the words naturally imply — while *pshat* is the accepted rabbinic interpretation, which sometimes coincides with *mashma* and sometimes does not. What Ulla is saying, then, is that throughout the *parah adumah parsha*, the rabbis sometimes follow the natural sense and sometimes depart from it.

### Elazar Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּנְתַתֶּם אוֹתָהּ אֶל אֶלְעָזָר הַכֹּהֵן״ — אוֹתָהּ לְאֶלְעָזָר, וְלֹא לְדוֹרוֹת לְאֶלְעָזָר. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: לְדוֹרוֹת בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: לְדוֹרוֹת בְּכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to demonstrate Ulla's claim by expounding the verses throughout the passage of the red heifer: The verse states with regard to the red heifer: **"And you shall give it to Elazar the priest** and he shall take it out to the outside of the camp, and he shall slaughter it before him" (Numbers 19:3). The word "it" implies that only **that** red heifer, i.e., the first one, was to be given **to Elazar,** who was the deputy High Priest, to perform its rite, but that the red heifer in future **generations** does **not** need to be given **to Elazar,** i.e., to a priest of similar rank. If so, who does perform the rite of the red heifer? **Some say: In** future **generations,** it should be done **by** the **High Priest. And some say: In** future **generations,** it can be done even **by a common priest.**

The *Gemara* now reads specific verses. *Ve-nasattem otah el Elazar ha-kohen* — and you shall give it to *Elazar ha-kohen*. The rabbinic reading is: *otah Elazar ve-lo le-dorot Elazar* — for this generation, it must specifically go to *Elazar*; for future generations, the requirement of *Elazar* specifically does not apply. The question is: what does apply *le-dorot*? Here the *Gemara* records two opinions. *Ika de-amri: le-dorot be-kohen gadol* — some say that for future generations, the *parah adumah* requires a *kohen gadol*. *Ve-ika de-amri: le-dorot be-kohen hedyot* — others say it requires only an ordinary *kohen*.

The *mashma* of the text, if one were asked to extrapolate from the Torah's repeated emphasis on *Elazar*, might suggest someone elevated — perhaps a *segan*, a second-in-command, someone close to the level of *Elazar* without being the *kohen gadol* himself. But neither opinion follows that middle path. The question is whether the departure from *Elazar* goes up (to *kohen gadol*) or down (to ordinary *kohen*).

### Gezera Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְדוֹרוֹת בְּכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט — שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְדוֹרוֹת בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, מְנָא לֵיהּ? גָּמַר ״חוּקָּה״ ״חוּקָּה״ מִיּוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says** that **in** future **generations** it can be done even **by a common priest,** it works out **well,** since there is no indication to the contrary. **But according to the one who says** that **in** future **generations** it should be done **by the High Priest, from where** does he derive this requirement? **He derives** the *halakha* **from** the service of **Yom Kippur** through a verbal analogy between the word **"statute"** (Numbers 19:2) written with regard to the red heifer and the word **"statute"** (Leviticus 16:34) written with regard to the Yom Kippur service: Just as on Yom Kippur the entire service is performed by the High Priest, so too, in future generations the rite of the red heifer must be done entirely by the High Priest.

*Bishlama le-man de-amar le-dorot be-kohen hedyot* — if you hold that an ordinary *kohen* suffices for future generations, this is straightforward: the Torah's specific mention of *Elazar* applied only to that moment, and afterward any *kohen* suffices. But if you hold that future generations require a *kohen gadol* — *mena leh*? Where does that come from? *Gamar chukah chukah mi-Yom ha-Kippurim* — a *gezera shava*: the Torah uses the word *chok* in the context of *parah adumah* and uses the same word *chok* in the context of *Yom Kippur*. Just as *Yom Kippur* requires a *kohen gadol*, so too *parah adumah* requires a *kohen gadol*.

This conclusion is striking in its implications. Throughout this *sugya*, the working assumption has been that the *Yom Kippur* service requires a *kohen gadol* and the *parah adumah* requires an ordinary *kohen*. Those two rituals — which the Torah presents as the defining communal *avodot* of their respective domains, each with a specific personality attached (*Aharon* for *Yom Kippur*, *Elazar* for *parah adumah*) — translate into the same institutional requirement *le-dorot* according to this opinion: in both cases, only a *kohen gadol*. The prevailing assumption runs the other way — *Yom Kippur* becomes *kohen gadol*, *parah adumah* becomes ordinary *kohen* — but the *gezera shava* collapses that distinction entirely, binding these two great rituals together through the shared word *chok*, so that the personality of *Aharon* and the personality of *Elazar* become, for all future generations, the same office.
