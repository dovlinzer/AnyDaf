# Sanhedrin 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Psulei Edut

### Opening Remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ הֵן הַפְּסוּלִין: הַמְשַׂחֵק בְּקוּבְיָא, וְהַמַּלְוֶה בְּרִיבִּית, וּמַפְרִיחֵי יוֹנִים, וְסוֹחֲרֵי שְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these** on the following list **are the ones** who are **disqualified** by the Sages from bearing witness due to their unseemly behavior, as they are considered wicked individuals guilty of monetary transgressions: **One who plays with dice [*bekubbiyya*]** for money, **and one who lends** money **with interest, and those who fly pigeons, and merchants** who trade in the produce **of the Sabbatical** Year, which may be eaten but may not be sold as an object of commerce.

The Mishna on Sanhedrin 25a introduces a category of individuals who are *pasul* — disqualified — both for *edut* (bearing witness) and for *dayanut* (serving as judges). The Mishna appears as a continuation of the preceding discussion: having established in the previous Mishna that one may in certain contexts rely on a technically disqualified witness, the Mishna now proceeds to identify precisely who those disqualified individuals are.

### Rashi's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בַּתְּחִילָּה הָיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתָן אוֹסְפֵי שְׁבִיעִית. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָאַנָּסִין, חָזְרוּ לִקְרוֹתָן סוֹחֲרֵי שְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: Initially,** people **would call them: Gatherers** of the produce **of the Sabbatical** Year. **Once the tax collectors grew abundant they would then call them: Merchants** who trade in the produce **of the Sabbatical** Year, as the Gemara will explain.

Rashi, in his comment under the *dibur hamatchil* of *hamasachek be-kubiya*, offers a general introduction to the entire Mishna: *kulan me'ein gazlanim hen* — all of these individuals are, in some sense, akin to thieves. The Torah states *al tashet rasha ed* — do not place a wicked person as a witness — and Rashi derives by *kal va-chomer* that all the more so, a *rasha* may not serve as a *dayan*. This framework of quasi-theft governs the Mishna's logic throughout.

### Categories Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן אוּמָּנוּת אֶלָּא הוּא, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהֶן אוּמָּנוּת שֶׁלֹּא הוּא – כְּשֵׁרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: When** are the people listed above disqualified from bearing witness? It is **when they have no occupation but this one. But** if **they have an occupation other than this one,** although they also make money by these inappropriate means, **they are fit** to bear witness.

The Mishna enumerates the following disqualified individuals: *hamasachek be-kubiya* — one who plays dice or engages in this gambling game (in modern Hebrew, *kubiyot* are dice, though the Gemara will clarify the precise nature of this game); *hamalveh be-ribit* — one who lends money on interest; *mafrichei yonim* — those who fly pigeons or doves, the precise meaning of which the Gemara will explain; and *sochrei shevi'it* — those who trade commercially in produce of the *shemita* year, one of whose stipulations is that one may not profit from produce grown that year.

The Mishna adds a statement of Rabbi Shimon: at first these individuals were called *osvei shevi'it* — those who merely gathered *shevi'it* produce — but once tax collectors proliferated, the designation shifted to *sochrei shevi'it*, traders in *shevi'it* produce. The Gemara will explain what lies behind this shift in terminology.

### R. Yehuda's Condition

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies the preceding list: *eimatai* — when does the disqualification apply? Only *bizman she'ein lahem omanut ela hu* — when these activities are their sole occupation and they have no other trade. But if they do have another legitimate occupation, they are *kasherin*. The Gemara will address at length whether Rabbi Yehuda is here disagreeing with the *tanna kamma* of the Mishna or merely clarifying and elaborating upon it.

## Dice Player Debate

### Rami: Asmakhta Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשַׂחֵק בְּקוּבְיָא, מַאי קָא עָבֵיד? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה אַסְמַכְתָּא, וְאַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to **one who plays with dice** for money, **what is he doing** that disqualifies him from bearing witness? **Rami bar Ḥama says:** He is disqualified **because** gambling **is a transaction with inconclusive consent [*asmakhta*],** as one who gambles does not have conclusive consent to pay when he loses since he plays under the assumption that he will win. **And** as **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition,** the one who wins takes money that is not legally his, and is considered a robber.

The Gemara opens its analysis with a question: *hamasachek be-kubiya mai ka'avid* — what exactly has the dice player done wrong? Rami bar Chama answers: *mishum de-hava asmakhta ve-asmakhta lo kanya* — because it constitutes an *asmakhta*, and an *asmakhta* does not effect a legal acquisition. The principle of *asmakhta*, encountered in Bava Batra and elsewhere, holds that when a person makes a stipulation predicated on an assumption — typically that a certain outcome will obtain — the underlying transfer of ownership is not halachically valid, because the person never genuinely resolved to relinquish his money. The classic case is one who owes a debt and says: if I do not repay by a certain date, I will pay double — he assumes he will pay on time and never truly commits to the penalty. So too, says Rami bar Chama, each gambler assumes he will win, and therefore never truly relinquishes ownership of the money he stakes. When the other party collects, he is taking money whose ownership was never genuinely transferred — rendering him, in effect, a thief.

### Rav Sheshet: Yishuvo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: כֹּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא לָאו אַסְמַכְתָּא הִיא, אֶלָּא לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֲסוּקִין בְּיִישּׁוּבוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says: Any case like this is not an *asmakhta*.** In a game of dice both sides realize that they might lose, and consent at the outset to pay if that happens. **Rather,** those who play dice are disqualified **because they are not involved in settling the world,** i.e., in productive occupations that demand hard work. Since they do not realize the effort required of most people to earn a living they might not be scrupulous about other people's money, and their testimony cannot be relied upon.

Rav Sheshet disagrees: *kol ki hai gavna lav asmakhta hi* — this type of case is not an *asmakhta* at all. Rashi explains the distinction carefully: a true *asmakhta* is one where the person relies on some anticipated event and mistakenly believes it will not occur — *to'eh ve-savar lo yavo lidei kach*. But in the gambling context, the gambler does not know whether he will win or lose. Because the outcome is entirely uncertain, and he stakes his money regardless, we say that *mi-sfeika gamar ve-aknei* — he fully resolves, even out of uncertainty, to transfer ownership. There is therefore no *asmakhta* problem, and the money is legitimately acquired by the winner.

Instead, Rav Sheshet offers an entirely different rationale for the disqualification: *ela le-fi she-ein asukin be-yishuvo shel olam* — it is because gamblers are not engaged in productive activity that benefits the world. The disqualification is not a function of the mechanics of acquisition but of the character of the person's occupation.

### Practical Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דִּגְמַר אוּמָּנוּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the practical difference **between** the explanations of Rami bar Ḥama and Rav Sheshet? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them** is in a case **where** the dice player **learns another occupation,** and is involved in that occupation as well. According to Rav Sheshet, since he has a productive occupation he is fit to bear witness. According to Rami bar Ḥama, since he is considered a robber, as the money he gains from gambling is not legally his, he is disqualified.

The Gemara identifies the practical difference between the two opinions: the case where the gambler has also learned another legitimate trade. According to Rav Sheshet, whose disqualification rests on non-productive activity, such a person would be *kasher* for testimony and judgment — he is, after all, contributing productively to society through his other trade. According to Rami bar Chama, however, whose disqualification rests on the *asmakhta* doctrine and the quasi-theft involved, the existence of another trade is irrelevant — the gambler remains, in effect, a thief regardless of his other occupation.

One might ask whether a *batlan* — a person who is simply idle and does nothing — would similarly be disqualified under Rav Sheshet's reasoning. The answer is probably not, because *asukin be-yishuvo shel olam* implies not merely the absence of productive activity but the presence of something actively harmful alongside that absence.

### Mishna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן אוּמָּנוּת אֶלָּא הוּא; אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהֶן אוּמָּנוּת שֶׁלֹּא הוּא – הֲרֵי זֶה כְּשֵׁרִים. אַלְמָא, טַעְמָא דְמַתְנִיתִין מִשּׁוּם יִישּׁוּבוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם הוּא. קַשְׁיָא לְרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against the opinion of Rami bar Ḥama: **But we learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda said: When** are they disqualified from bearing witness? It is **when they have no occupation but this one. But** if **they have an occupation other than this one they are fit** to bear witness. **Apparently, the reason that the mishna** disqualifies these individuals from bearing witness **is that** they are not involved in **settling the world,** in accordance with the opinion of Rav Sheshet. This poses **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rami bar Ḥama.**

The Gemara raises a powerful challenge to Rami bar Chama from the Mishna itself: Rabbi Yehuda says *eimatai* — the disqualification applies only when the person has no other occupation, but if he does, he is *kasher*. This strongly implies that the rationale underlying the Mishna is indeed *yishuvo shel olam* — lack of productive engagement — which would support Rav Sheshet and contradict Rami bar Chama.

### Amatai = Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֵימָתַי״ וּ״בַמֶּה״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְפָרֵשׁ דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״אֵימָתַי״ – לְפָרֵשׁ, וּ״בַמֶּה״ – לַחְלוֹק. וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא ״אֵימָתַי״ לְפָרֵשׁ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **When** does this *halakha* apply, **or: In what** case is this statement said, he intends **only to explain the statement of the Rabbis,** not to disagree with them? Granted, **Rabbi Yoḥanan** disagrees with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and **says** that only the term: **When** does this *halakha* apply, indicates that Rabbi Yehuda intends **to explain** the previous statement of the Rabbis, **but** the term: **In what** case is this statement said, indicates that he intends **to disagree. But** according to **everyone,** the term **when** indicates that he intends **to explain** the previous statement. This is difficult according to Rami bar Ḥama.

The Gemara responds that one might think Rabbi Yehuda is arguing against the *tanna kamma*, but two Amoraic principles preclude this reading. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught: *kol makom she-amar Rabbi Yehuda eimatai u-vemah eino ela le-fareish divrei chachamim* — wherever Rabbi Yehuda says *eimatai* or *u-vemah*, he is coming to clarify and elaborate upon the position of the *chachamim*, not to dispute them. Rabbi Yochanan refines this: *eimatai* always signals clarification, while *u-vemah* signals disagreement. Since our Mishna uses *eimatai*, everyone agrees that Rabbi Yehuda is merely explicating the *tanna kamma* — and the Mishna therefore implies that the reason for disqualification is *yishuvo shel olam*, which is difficult for Rami bar Chama.

### Rami Bar Chama's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבְרָא אַגַּבְרָא קָא רָמֵית? מָר סָבַר: פְּלִיגִי, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Are you setting** the statement of one **man against** the statement of another **man?** One **Sage,** Rami bar Ḥama, **holds** that Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **disagree** even when Rabbi Yehuda employs the term: When does this *halakha* apply, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and similarly Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **they do not disagree.**

The Gemara resolves this by noting that Rami bar Chama may simply disagree with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and Rabbi Yochanan on the interpretive principle itself — *mar savar pligei u-mar savar lo pligei* — and maintains that Rabbi Yehuda's *eimatai* does signal genuine disagreement with the *tanna kamma*.

### Tarfon's Nezirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: בֵּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אוּמָּנוּת שֶׁלֹּא הוּא, בֵּין שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אוּמָּנוּת אֶלָּא הוּא – הֲרֵי זֶה פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do they not disagree? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Whether** a dice player **has an occupation other than this one,** or **whether he does not have an occupation other than this one, he is disqualified** from bearing witness? This *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Rami bar Ḥama.

The Gemara then introduces a *beraita* that initially seems to support Rami bar Chama's opponent but ultimately vindicates him. The *beraita* records Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon: two men are seated and a third passes by. One says, "I hereby become a *nazir* if that passerby is a *nazir*," and the other says, "I hereby become a *nazir* if he is not a *nazir*." After they discover the truth, one of them has evidently stated the correct condition — yet Rabbi Tarfon rules that *neither* of them becomes a *nazir*. The reason: *she-al ha-safek amar devarav techilla* — each spoke his declaration initially from a position of uncertainty. Since the verse requires *le-hafla* — an explicit, unequivocal declaration — and neither spoke with that certainty, neither achieves *nezirut*.

### Beraita Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, לְעוֹלָם אֵין אֶחָד מֵהֶן נָזִיר, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְּנָה נְזִירוּת אֶלָּא לְהַפְלָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** *baraita* is not the opinion of the Rabbis in the mishna, but rather **it is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Tarfon:** In the case where two people quarreled and each of them declared that if the other is right he will become a nazirite, **actually, neither of them** becomes **a nazirite, as naziriteship is determined only by explicitness.** A vow of naziriteship does not take effect if the individual does not vow clearly and with certitude. Here too, Rabbi Tarfon maintains that one who bets on games played with dice is considered a thief, as one can acquire the money of another legally only if the latter gives it to him with certain and conclusive intent. Since one who plays dice is not certain that he will have to pay the other player, as he considers it likely that he will win, the transaction is an *asmakhta* and is legally invalid.

The Gemara draws the implication: according to Rabbi Tarfon, even when a person makes a conditional declaration regarding something that is entirely beyond his control and about whose outcome he is wholly ignorant, it is still an *asmakhta* — *afilu hachi havya asmakhta*. And we do not say *mi-sfeika gamar ve-aknei* — that the uncertainty itself generates a full resolve to transfer ownership or commit to the condition. This *beraita*, which represents the position of Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon, is thus a third opinion distinct from the *tanna kamma* of our Mishna, and it directly supports Rami bar Chama's position that gambling transactions are *asmakhta* and that the *masachek be-kubiya* is disqualified on those grounds.

## Interest Lender

### Rava on Borrower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַלְוֶה בְּרִבִּית. אָמַר רָבָא: לָוָה בְּרִבִּית – פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת. וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: מַלְוֶה בְּרִבִּית? מִלְוָה הַבָּאָה בְּרִיבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one who lends** money **with interest** is disqualified from bearing witness. **Rava says: One who borrows** money **with interest is disqualified from bearing witness.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that specifically **one who lends** money **with interest** is disqualified? The Gemara answers: The reference in the mishna is to **a loan that comes with interest,** and is teaching that all those who participate in the loan are disqualified.

Rava adds: *loveh be-ribit pasul* — not only the lender but also the borrower who takes a loan on interest is *pasul* for testimony. The Gemara challenges this from our Mishna, which specifies only *malveh*, the lender. Rava responds that the Mishna should be read as *milveh be-ribit* — a loan that comes with interest — indicating that anyone involved in an interest-bearing loan is disqualified. This would include both creditor and debtor, and there is a *machloket Rishonim* as to whether Rava's ruling extends further to the guarantor and the witnesses to the loan document — all of whom, according to the Mishna in Bava Kamma, transgress a *lav* by their involvement.

### Bar Binyatus Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּר בִּינִיתּוֹס אַסְהִידוּ בֵּיהּ תְּרֵי סָהֲדֵי. חַד אָמַר: קַמֵּי דִּידִי אוֹזֵיף בְּרִיבִּיתָא, וְחַד אָמַר: לְדִידִי אוֹזְפַן בְּרִיבִּיתָא. פַּסְלֵיהּ רָבָא לְבַר בִּינִיתּוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts: **Two witnesses testified about bar Binittos. One said: He lent** money **with interest in my presence, and** the other **one said: He lent me** money **with interest. Rava rendered bar Binittos disqualified** from bearing witness and from serving as a judge.

The Gemara presents a case: two witnesses came and testified about a man named Bar Binyatus. The first said, "In my presence, Bar Binyatus lent money with interest." The second said, "He gave *me* a loan with interest." Rava disqualified Bar Binyatus on the strength of this testimony.

### Self-Disqualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רָבָא הוּא דְּאָמַר: לָוָה בְּרִיבִּית פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ רָשָׁע, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״אַל תָּשֶׁת רָשָׁע עֵד״. רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: אָדָם קָרוֹב אֵצֶל עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין אָדָם מֵשִׂים עַצְמוֹ רָשָׁע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't Rava the one who said** that **one who borrows** money **with interest is disqualified from bearing witness? And** accordingly the latter witness **is considered a wicked man,** as by his own account he borrowed money with interest from bar Binittos, **and the Torah states: Do not place a wicked man as a witness** (see Exodus 23:1). Consequently, the testimony of that witness cannot be accepted, and bar Binittos should not have been disqualified. The Gemara answers: **Rava** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rava says: A person is his own relative and a person cannot make himself wicked.** Consequently, the part of the testimony that relates to the witness's own status is not accepted, while the part that relates to bar Binittos is accepted.

The question arises: Rava himself ruled that a borrower who takes a loan with interest is *pasul* for testimony — meaning the second witness has himself admitted to a transgression that renders him a *rasha*. How, then, can his testimony be valid? The answer rests on Rava's own principle, stated elsewhere: *adam karov etzel atzmo ve-ein adam mesim atzmo rasha* — a person is considered his own *karov*, his own relative, and therefore cannot render himself a *rasha* through his own testimony. When the second witness says "he lent *me* money with interest," that portion of his statement cannot disqualify him personally — one cannot bear testimony against oneself in a manner that produces legal consequences for the speaker. However, the part of his testimony that pertains to Bar Binyatus — namely, that Bar Binyatus engaged in interest-lending — remains valid. Thus Rava accepts the testimony with respect to Bar Binyatus while disregarding its self-incriminating dimension.

## Shochet & Teshuva

### Shochet Caught

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא טַבָּחָא דְּאִישְׁתְּכַח דִּנְפַקָא טְרֵיפְתָּא מִתּוּתֵי יְדֵיהּ, פַּסְלֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן וְעַבְּרֵיהּ. אֲזַל רַבִּי מַזְיֵהּ וְטוּפְרֵיהּ. סְבַר רַב נַחְמָן לְאַכְשׁוֹרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain slaughterer about whom it was discovered that** a *tereifa,* **an animal with a wound that would have caused it to die within twelve months, emerged from his possession.** In other words, he sold *tereifa* meat without informing the customers of its status. **Rav Naḥman disqualified him** from bearing witness **and removed him** from his position as a slaughterer. The slaughterer subsequently **went** and **grew his fingernails and his hair** out of remorse over his actions. **Rav Naḥman thought to deem him fit** again for bearing witness, as he clearly repented, and once someone repents for his sin, his status as a valid witness is restored.

The Gemara relates an incident involving a *shochet* — a ritual slaughterer — who was found to have been selling *tereifot*, animals with pre-existing injuries that rendered them non-*kosher*, while passing them off as permissible meat. Rav Nachman disqualified him for testimony and removed him from his position as *shochet*. The *shochet* subsequently let his hair and fingernails grow as visible signs of contrition.

### True Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: דִּילְמָא אִיעָרוֹמֵי קָא מַעֲרֵים? אֶלָּא מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? כִּדְרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין, דְּאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַטְּרֵיפוֹת אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁיֵּלֵךְ לְמָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין אוֹתוֹ וְיַחְזִיר אֲבֵידָה בְּדָבָר חָשׁוּב, אוֹ שֶׁיּוֹצִיא טְרֵיפָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ בְּדָבָר חָשׁוּב מִשֶּׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Naḥman: **Perhaps he is employing artifice,** pretending to repent in order to be reinstated as a slaughterer. **Rather, what is his remedy?** It is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Idi bar Avin; as Rav Idi bar Avin says: One who is suspected of** selling ***tereifot*** to others **has no remedy** to restore his fitness to bear witness **until he goes to a locale where** they **do not recognize him and returns a lost item of substantial value** that he finds, **or removes his own *tereifa*** meat **of significant value from his possession.** These actions demonstrate that he has repented, as he is willing to lose money for a mitzva. By contrast, if he does so in a place where he is recognized his fitness in not reinstated based on these actions, as perhaps he performed them only in order to be reinstated.

Rav Nachman considered reinstating him. Rava intervened: *dilma iyarumei ka-mairim* — perhaps this is mere subterfuge. A man who knows what contrition looks like can simulate it. If external signs of mourning are insufficient, how does one genuinely establish *teshuva*? Rava cites Rav Idi bar Abin: a *shochet* suspected of selling *tereifot* has no remedy until he goes to a place where nobody recognizes him — where performance for an audience is impossible. There, he must demonstrate changed character through action: either he finds a valuable lost object and returns it to its owner — thereby demonstrating that he is no longer unscrupulous with money — or he identifies a *tereifa* among his own animals, an expensive one, and comes forward to declare it non-*kosher*, thereby absorbing a substantial financial loss in the service of honesty. Either act, performed without audience or incentive, constitutes credible evidence of genuine *teshuva*.

## Pigeon Flyers

### Two Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַפְרִיחֵי יוֹנִים, מַאי מַפְרִיחֵי יוֹנִים? הָכָא תַּרְגִּימוּ: ״אִי תִּקְדְּמֵיהּ יוֹנָךְ לְיוֹן״. רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: ״אָרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** § Among the list of those who are disqualified from bearing witness the mishna teaches: **And those who fly pigeons.** The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by: **Those who fly pigeons? Here,** in Babylonia, the Sages **explained** that these are people who gamble on pigeon races, i.e., one says to another: **If your pigeon reaches** a certain destination **before my pigeon** I will give you such and such an amount of money. **Rabbi Ḥama bar Oshaya says:** It is referring to **an *ara*,** i.e., one who trains his pigeons to bring him pigeons from the property of others.

The Mishna lists *mafrichei yonim* among the disqualified. The Gemara asks: *mai mafrichei yonim* — what precisely is this? Two interpretations emerge. In Bavel, the term is understood to refer to pigeon racing: *i tadmi yonecha le-yon* — if your pigeon arrives before mine at a certain destination, you win a stipulated sum. This is a form of gambling.

### Arah Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר: ״אִי תִּקְדְּמֵיהּ יוֹנָךְ לְיוֹן״, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר ״אָרָא״? אָמַר לָךְ: ״אָרָא״ – מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **the one who says** that it is referring to those who say: **If your pigeon reaches** a certain destination **before my pigeon** I will give you such and such an amount of money, **what is the reason** that **he does not say** that it is referring to **an *ara*?** The Gemara answers: He could **say to you** that **an *ara*** is not considered a robber, as the pigeons that he has his pigeons bring do not actually belong to those from whom he takes them. Rather, they dwell on the property of these individuals, and it is prohibited to take them **merely due to the ways of peace.**

Rabbi Chama bar Oshaya, from Eretz Yisrael, interprets the term as referring to an *ara* — a trained bird used to lure and attract other people's pigeons to the owner's home against their will. Rashi explains that this practice is not technically *gezel de-oraita*, since pigeons cannot be formally owned in the way that other property can, but the *chachamim* prohibited it as a form of theft — either as *tikkun olam* or as *darchei shalom*. Because it is only a rabbinic prohibition, the Mishna had to state it explicitly; had it been full biblical theft, the person would already be disqualified on those grounds without special mention.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אָרָא״, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר: ״אִי תִּקְדְּמֵיהּ יוֹנָךְ לְיוֹן״? אָמַר לָךְ: הַיְינוּ מְשַׂחֵק בְּקוּבְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the one who says** that the mishna is referring to **an *ara*, what is the reason** that **he does not say** that it is referring to one who says: **If your pigeon reaches** a certain destination **before my pigeon** I will give you such and such an amount of money? The Gemara answers: He could **say to you** that this individual **is** the same as one who **plays with dice;** they both gamble on games of chance. This type of disqualification is already listed in the mishna.

The Gemara then asks: why does the Mishna list both *masachek be-kubiya* and *mafrichei yonim*? If *mafrichei yonim* means pigeon racing, it is essentially the same as gambling — why mention it separately? And conversely, if *mafrichei yonim* refers to *ara*, why mention *masachek be-kubiya* separately when pigeon racing would be subsumed under gambling? Each opinion responds symmetrically: the proponent of pigeon racing says that *ara* is only a rabbinic prohibition rooted in *darchei shalom* and would not on its own suffice to generate full disqualification — thus the Mishna would have had no reason to include it; the proponent of *ara* says that pigeon racing is no different from *kubiya* gambling and the Mishna would not have needed to repeat it.

### Dependency Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנָא תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת עַצְמוֹ, וּתְנָא תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת יוֹנוֹ. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי תְּנָא תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת עַצְמוֹ – הָתָם הוּא דְּלָא גָּמַר וּמַקְנֵי, דְּאָמַר:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that it is necessary for the mishna to teach that both types of gamblers are disqualified. The mishna **taught** that one who bets on dice, **making it dependent on his own decision,** as he believes he has a method by which he will win, is disqualified, **and** the mishna **taught** that one who bets on pigeon races, **making it dependent on the decision of his pigeon,** is also disqualified. The Gemara explains: **And** both are **necessary. As had** the mishna **taught** this *halakha* only with regard to one who bets on dice, **making it dependent on his own decision,** one might reason that **it is** specifically **there** that a gambler is considered a thief. The reason for this is **that he** presumably **does not resolve to transfer** the money if he loses; **as he says** to himself:

The Gemara adds that even if both interpretations were accepted simultaneously, it would be necessary to teach both cases. The gambler at dice depends entirely on his own judgment and skill — *toleh be-da'at atzmo* — and therefore never fully resolves to relinquish his money, since he trusts in his own superiority. The pigeon racer depends on his pigeon — *toleh be-da'at yonah* — but he too may believe he knows how to bang the instruments that urge his pigeon on better than his opponent: *ana yad'ana le-nakushei tefei*. Conversely, if we knew only about the pigeon-dependent case, we might think the self-dependent gambler does fully resolve to transfer ownership since he is genuinely uncertain about his own capacities. And if we knew only about the self-dependent case, we might think the pigeon case involves a more genuine transfer. Both must therefore be taught: *tzricha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִים לִי בְּנַפְשַׁאי דְּיָדַעְנָא טְפֵי. אֲבָל תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת יוֹנוֹ – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי תְּנָא תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת יוֹנוֹ, דְּאָמַר: בְּנַקָּשָׁא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא, וַאֲנָא יָדַעְנָא לְנַקּוֹשֵׁי טְפֵי. אֲבָל תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת עַצְמוֹ – אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **I am certain of myself that I know better** than my competitor how to win. **But** with regard to one who **makes it dependent on the decision of his pigeon, say** that he is **not** disqualified from bearing witness, as he is aware that he cannot guarantee the results and therefore resolves to transfer the money if he loses. **And** conversely, **had** the mishna **taught** this *halakha* only with regard to one who **makes it dependent on the decision of his pigeon,** one might assume that only this type of gambler is disqualified, **as he** presumably **says: The matter,** i.e., the race, **is determined by knocking** on trees and other objects to speed up the pigeons, **and I know** how **to knock better** than my opponent. Therefore, he does not resolve to transfer the money if he loses. **But** with regard to one who **makes it dependent on his own decision, say** that he is **not** disqualified from bearing witness, as the roll of the dice is pure chance. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the mishna to teach both cases.

### Animal Fighting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמְשַׂחֵק בְּקוּבְיָא – אֵלּוּ הֵן הַמְשַׂחֲקִים בִּפְסֵיפָסִים. וְלֹא בִּפְסֵיפָסִים בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ קְלִיפֵּי אֱגוֹזִים וּקְלִיפֵּי רִימּוֹנִים. וְאֵימָתַי חֲזָרָתָן? מִשֶּׁיְּשַׁבְּרוּ אֶת פְּסֵיפָסֵיהֶן וְיַחְזְרוּ בָּהֶן חֲזָרָה גְּמוּרָה, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּחִנָּם לָא עָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion that the expression: Those who fly pigeons, refers to an *ara*, from a *baraita*: With regard to the expression **one who plays with dice, these are ones who play with *pispasim*,** which are dice of marble or other types of stone. **But** the Sages **did not** mean to **say** that **only** one who plays **with *pispasim*** is disqualified from bearing witness, but **rather even** one who plays with **nutshells or pomegranate shells** is disqualified. **And when is their repentance** accepted, so that they may resume being fit to bear witness? **Once they break their *pispasim* and repent of them completely,** abandoning this occupation entirely, **where they do not do** this **even for nothing,** i.e., they do not play even without betting.

A *beraita* from the Tosefta extends the categories. *Masachek be-kubiya* is defined as those who play with *fisfasin* — pieces of wood — but also with nutshells and pomegranate shells, reflecting various gambling implements. Repentance (*teshuva*) requires that they break their gambling tools and undertake *chazara gemura* — complete return — such that they would not even play the game for free, without money at stake.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַלְוֶה בְּרִיבִּית: אֶחָד הַמַּלְוֶה וְאֶחָד הַלֹּוֶה. וְאֵימָתַי חֲזָרָתָן? מִשֶּׁיְּקָרְעוּ אֶת שְׁטָרֵיהֶן, וְיַחְזְרוּ בָּהֶן חֲזָרָה גְּמוּרָה, דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְגוֹי לָא מוֹזְפִי. וּמַפְרִיחֵי יוֹנִים – אֵלּוּ שֶׁמַּמְרִין אֶת הַיּוֹנִים. וְלֹא יוֹנִים בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וָעוֹף. וְאֵימָתַי חֲזָרָתָן? מִשֶּׁיְּשַׁבְּרוּ אֶת פְּגָמֵיהֶן, וְיַחְזְרוּ בָּהֶן חֲזָרָה גְּמוּרָה, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בַּמִּדְבָּר נָמֵי לָא עָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The expression: **One who lends with interest,** is referring to **both the lender and the borrower.** Both are disqualified. **And when is their repentance** accepted? **Once they tear their** promissory **notes and repent of them completely,** abandoning this occupation entirely, where **they do not lend** with interest **even to a gentile.** The expression: **And** those **who fly pigeons,** is referring to **those who induce the pigeons** to behave in this manner, i.e., they train them. **And** the Sages **did not** mean to **say** that **only** those who fly **pigeons** are disqualified; **rather, even** those who do this with **a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or** any type of **bird** are disqualified. **And when is their repentance** accepted? **Once they break their fixtures [*pigmeihen*]** upon which they stand the competing animals, **and repent completely,** abandoning this occupation entirely, **where they do not do** this **even in the wilderness,** where there is no one from whom to steal.

*Milveh be-ribit*, the interest-bearing loan, applies to both creditor and debtor. Repentance requires tearing up the loan documents and resolving not to lend on interest even to a *nochri* — to whom lending with interest is technically permitted. Similarly, *mafrichei yonim* who fight pigeons — and the *beraita* extends the prohibition to those who fight other animals as well: *behema*, *chaya*, and *of*, domesticated animals, wild animals, and birds — must break their fighting implements (*pigmeihen*) and resolve not to engage in animal fighting even in the *midbar*, the wilderness, where there is no settled community to harm.

### Rashi's Caveat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹחֲרֵי שְׁבִיעִית – אֵלּוּ שֶׁנּוֹשְׂאִין וְנוֹתְנִין בְּפֵירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית. וְאֵימָתַי חֲזָרָתָן? מִשֶּׁתַּגִּיעַ שְׁבִיעִית אַחֶרֶת וְיִבָּדְלוּ. וְאָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: לֹא חֲזָרַת דְּבָרִים בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא חֲזָרַת מָמוֹן. כֵּיצַד? אוֹמֵר: אֲנִי פְּלוֹנִי בַּר פְּלוֹנִי, כִּינַּסְתִּי מָאתַיִם זוּז בְּפֵירוֹת שְׁבִיעִית, וַהֲרֵי הֵן נְתוּנִין בְּמַתָּנָה לַעֲנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The expression: **Merchants** who trade in the produce **of the Sabbatical** Year, is referring to **those who do business with** the **produce of the Sabbatical** Year. **And when is their repentance** accepted? **Once another Sabbatical** Year **occurs and they refrain** from selling its produce or from assuming ownership of such produce. The *baraita* continues: **And Rabbi Neḥemya said:** The Sages **did not say** that **verbal repentance alone** is sufficient for a merchant who traded in the produce of the Sabbatical Year to be reinstated as a valid witness; **rather, returning** the **money** is also necessary. **How** can one return the money he gained from selling produce of the Sabbatical Year? **He says: I, so-and-so the son of so-and-so, gathered,** i.e., profited, **two hundred dinars** from trading **in** the **produce of the Sabbatical** Year, **and** as I gained it improperly, this sum is **hereby given as a gift to the poor.**

Rashi notes that the *chazara gemura* clauses — requiring the offender to go beyond the letter of the law to demonstrate repentance — are not original parts of the *beraita*. They are later additions by the *stam* of the Gemara. Rashi's evidence for this is linguistic: the word *mozfei*, used in connection with the interest-lender's repentance (not lending even to a *nochri*), is Aramaic rather than Hebrew — it is not the vocabulary of a Tannaitic source. Similarly, the clause about not fighting animals even in the *midbar* is, Rashi observes, added to defend the interpretation of *ara*: if one races or fights pigeons, the locale would be irrelevant to the prohibition, but if the issue is luring other people's birds to one's home, then even in the *midbar* — where there are no other people's animals to steal — one must demonstrate restraint as a sign of full repentance.

## Shviit & Taxes

### Shviit Traders

The *beraita* continues with *sochrei shevi'it* — those who trade in *shemita* produce. Their repentance requires waiting until the next *shevi'it* year arrives and then refraining from the prohibited commerce. The wait of up to seven years reflects the severity of the offense and the need to demonstrate that the change in behavior is genuine rather than circumstantial.

### Monetary Repentance

Rabbi Nechemia adds that repentance in this domain requires not merely behavioral change but *chazarat mamon* — monetary restitution. The offender must declare explicitly: "I, so-and-so, have collected two hundred *zuz* through trading in *shevi'it* produce, and these funds are hereby given as a gift to the poor." The rationale is that *shevi'it* produce is designated for the poor to eat freely; the money derived from its illicit sale must therefore be directed to *aniyim*.

### Thieves & Chasmans

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: בְּהֵמָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אִי תִּקְדְּמֵיהּ יוֹנָךְ לְיוֹן״, הַיְינוּ דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּהֵמָה. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אָרָא״, בְּהֵמָה בַּת הָכִי הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **In any event, it is taught** in the *baraita* that the status of one who flies pigeons applies to one who uses **a domesticated animal** in the same manner. **Granted, according to the one who says** that the term: One who flies pigeons, is referring to those who race pigeons, saying: **If your pigeon reaches** a certain destination **before my pigeon** I will give you such and such an amount of money, **this is how you find** a parallel case of one who races **a domesticated animal** against another animal. **But according to the one who says** that the term pigeon flyer means **an *ara*, is a domesticated animal capable of** luring other domesticated animals?

The *beraita* further records that the Sages added *gazlanim* — thieves — and *chamsanim* — violent takers — to the list of the disqualified. A *chamsan* is one who takes an item from another even when the owner objects, but pays for it nonetheless. The *gazlan* disqualification requires explanation: a *gazlan* commits an *issur de-oraita* and would obviously be *pasul* without any new ruling.

### Finding from Incapacitated…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הוֹסִיפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן הַגַּזְלָנִין וְהַחַמְסָנִין. גַּזְלָן? דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא! לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לִמְצִיאַת חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה, וְקָטָן. מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר: מְצִיאַת חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה, וְקָטָן לָא שְׁכִיחָא. אִי נָמֵי, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם בְּעָלְמָא. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּסוֹף סוֹף מָמוֹנָא הוּא דְּקָא שָׁקְלִי, פַּסְלִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § It was **taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages **added the robbers and those who force transactions,** i.e., who compel others to sell to them, **to** the list of those who are disqualified from bearing witness. A **robber is** disqualified **by Torah law;** why is it necessary for the Sages to add such an individual to the list? The Gemara answers: It **is necessary only to** add one who steals **an item found by a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** who acquire those items by rabbinic law only (see *Gittin* 59b). Since these people are not considered halakhically competent, by Torah law they do not acquire an item that they find, and consequently one who steals such an item from them is not in violation of a prohibition by Torah law. One possibility is that taking such an item is prohibited by rabbinic law because it constitutes robbery. Nevertheless, **initially** the Sages did not disqualify such an individual from bearing witness, as they **assumed** that the case of **an item found by a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor is uncommon.** Therefore, it was not deemed necessary to disqualify one who robs them of such an item. **Alternatively,** the Sages may have reasoned that taking such an item is prohibited **merely on account of the ways of peace,** i.e., to foster peace and prevent strife and controversy, and is not considered actual robbery. **When they realized that ultimately** these people **were taking property** from others and were likely to perform actual robbery, **the Sages disqualified them.**

The answer is that the ruling is necessary specifically for the case of one who takes a found object belonging to a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan* — a deaf person, a fool, or a minor. Since none of these three can halachically perform an act of *kinyan*, they technically cannot acquire a found object, and taking it from them would not constitute biblical theft. Nevertheless, the *chachamim* instituted a rabbinic prohibition against this *mipnei darchei shalom* — for the sake of social peace. Originally, the Sages did not include such a person among the disqualified for testimony, reasoning that the situation was uncommon. Ultimately, however, since this person is demonstrably unscrupulous with money, the Rabbis extended the disqualification to him as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחַמְסָנִין, מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר דְּמֵי קָא יָהֵיב (אַקְרַאי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא). כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא חָטְפִי, גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to **those who force transactions, initially** the Sages did not disqualify them, as **they assumed** that their behavior could be excused for two reasons: **They would pay money** for the items they took, and their forcing transactions **was merely occasional;** it was not a common practice. **When they realized that** these people **were snatching** items regularly, **the Sages issued a decree that they** are disqualified from bearing witness.

As for the *chamsan*: originally the Sages reasoned that since he pays for what he takes, his behavior is merely objectionable — *akrai be-alma hu* — but not an expression of financial unscrupulousness. Once they observed that people were brazenly grabbing others' property and forcing payment — *kachatfei* — the Rabbis decreed the disqualification.

### Shepherds Added

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: עוֹד הוֹסִיפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן הָרוֹעִים, הַגַּבָּאִין, וְהַמּוֹכְסִין. רוֹעִים, מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר אַקְרַאי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא מְכַוְּונִי וְשָׁדוּ לְכַתְּחִילָּה, גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן. הַגַּבָּאִין וְהַמּוֹכְסִין, מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר מַאי דְּקִיץ לְהוּ קָא שָׁקְלִי. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דְּקָא שָׁקְלִי יַתִּירָא, פַּסְלִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It is **taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages **further added** the following **to** the list of those disqualified from bearing witness: **The shepherds,** who shepherd their animals in the fields of others and are therefore considered like robbers; **the collectors** of government taxes, who collect more than the amount that people are legally liable to pay; **and the customs officials,** who collect customs in an illegal manner. The Gemara explains: **Shepherds** were not disqualified at first, as the Sages **initially assumed it was merely incidental** that they would let their animals graze in the fields of others. **When they realized that they would intentionally send** the animals to the fields of others **from the outset, the Sages issued a decree that they** are disqualified from bearing witness. **The collectors** of taxes **and the customs officials** were not disqualified at first, as the Sages **initially assumed they would take the set amount they** were instructed to take. **When they realized that** these officials **were taking more** than that, **they disqualified them.**

The *beraita* records that shepherds (*ro'im*) were added to the disqualified list as well, along with two categories of tax collectors (*gabba'im* and *mochsin*). Regarding shepherds: originally the Sages assumed that when a shepherd's flock grazed in another's field, it was accidental — *akrai be-alma hu*. Once they observed that shepherds were deliberately guiding their animals into others' fields — as Rabbeinu Chananel explains, even throwing food ahead of the animals to lead them there — the Rabbis imposed disqualification.

Tax collectors were originally trusted: the assumption was that they collected only what the ruler had assessed. Once it became clear that they were routinely taking more than their authorized quota — essentially stealing under cover of official authority — the Rabbis decreed them *pasul* for testimony.

## Shepherds Geography

### Rava's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רוֹעֶה שֶׁאָמְרוּ, אֶחָד רוֹעֵה בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה וְאֶחָד רוֹעֵה בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The **shepherd that** the Sages **said** is disqualified from bearing witness is referring to **both a shepherd of small livestock and a herder of large livestock.**

Rava rules that the disqualification of shepherds applies equally to shepherds of small animals (*behema dakka*) and shepherds of large animals (*behema gassa*).

### Contradiction Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רָבָא הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: רוֹעֵה בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – פְּסוּלִין, בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – כְּשֵׁרִין. רוֹעֵה בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה, אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – כְּשֵׁרִין. הָהוּא בִּמְגַדְּלִים אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rava say this? But doesn't Rava say: Shepherds of small livestock in Eretz Yisrael are disqualified** from bearing witness, as besides grazing in others' fields they also ruin the land? **Outside of Eretz** Yisrael **they are fit** to bear witness. By contrast, **herders of large livestock, even in Eretz Yisrael, are fit** to bear witness. The Gemara answers: **That was stated with regard to** those **who raise** their animals on their own land, without herding them on land in the public domain.

A contradiction is immediately raised: Rava himself stated elsewhere that a shepherd of small animals in Eretz Yisrael is *pasul*, whereas outside of Eretz Yisrael such a shepherd is *kasher*. And regarding large animals, Rava said that even in Eretz Yisrael such a shepherd is *kasher*. This appears to contradict his blanket ruling above.

### Resolution via Context

The resolution: Rava's second, more lenient statement — which permits the shepherd of small animals outside Eretz Yisrael and the shepherd of large animals everywhere — was stated in the context of *megadalim*, those who raise their animals domestically, within the home or city. A person who raises an animal in his own home is far less likely to allow it to graze in others' fields, and so the concern for unscrupulousness does not arise in the same way. The stricter ruling — disqualifying shepherds who take animals out to pasture — applies to those who actually work in the field.

### Rashi's Reason

Rashi explains why the rules differ between Eretz Yisrael and *chutz la-aretz* for small-animal shepherds: *be-Eretz Yisrael chamira tefei mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael* — in Eretz Yisrael the matter is more serious because of the importance of *yishuv Eretz Yisrael*, the settled and flourishing habitation of the Land. Preventing the invasion of neighbors' fields preserves the quality of life and the integrity of land use in the Land of Israel, making the Rabbis more stringent about whom they regard as trustworthy.

### Mishna Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי: נֶאֱמָנִים עָלַי שְׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָקָר. מַאי לָאו, לְעֵדוּת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a proof for Rava's opinion that a herder of large livestock is also disqualified: **This too stands to reason, from** the fact **that** the mishna (24a) **teaches** that a litigant may state: **Three cattle herders are trusted for me** in court; by inference, cattle herders are generally disqualified. **What, is it not with regard to bearing witness** that cattle herders are disqualified, in accordance with Rava's statement?

The Gemara finds support for Rava's position in the previous Mishna, which stated: *ne'emanim alai sheloshah ro'ei vakar* — three cattle herders are trusted by me. The Gemara asks: trusted for what purpose? For bearing witness? The Gemara answers: no — *lo le-dina* — for serving as *dayanim*, judges.

### Three Herders Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְדִינָא. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: שְׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָקָר. וְאִי לְעֵדוּת, שְׁלֹשָׁה לְמָה לִי? וְאֶלָּא מַאי, לְדִינָא? מַאי אִירְיָא שְׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָקָר? כֹּל בֵּי תְלָתָא דְּלָא גְּמִרִי דִּינָא נָמֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ הָנֵי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי בַּיִּישּׁוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** it is **with regard to** sitting in **judgment.** The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise** according to this interpretation, **as it teaches: Three cattle herders** are trusted for me. **And if** it is **with regard to bearing witness, why do I** need **three** witnesses? Two are enough. The Gemara asks: **But rather,** with regard to **what** are cattle herders disqualified? If it is **with regard to** sitting in **judgment, why** does the mishna mention **specifically three cattle herders? Any three** people **who did not study *halakha* are also** disqualified from serving as a court. The Gemara answers: **This** is what the mishna **is saying:** The litigants can accept as judges **even those** cattle herders **who** dwell in the fields and **do not frequent the settled area,** and are therefore not proficient in the ways of business.

The implication of the Mishna's language is that these three herders are technically *pasul* — for otherwise the Mishna would not have needed to say they are "trusted." The phrase signals that despite their technical disqualification, the litigants may accept them. And the Gemara notes that the number three is specified because it is *dayanim* under discussion: for *edut*, bearing witness, you would not need three; but for *dayanim* you need a court of three. Furthermore, the Mishna's particular mention of these *ro'ei vakar* teaches that even individuals who are maximally removed from settled life, rarely exposed to monetary transactions, and likely unversed in the law can be accepted as a court of three — *afilu hanei de-la shechichi be-yishuv* — if the litigants so agree.

## Stam Roeh vs Gabbai

### Default Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: סְתָם רוֹעֶה – פָּסוּל, סְתָם גַּבַּאי – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: An ordinary shepherd** is **disqualified** from bearing witness unless the court recognizes him as one who does not let his animals graze in the fields of others. **An ordinary** tax **collector** is **fit** unless the court determines he is one who collects more than people are obligated to pay.

Rav Yehuda concludes with a practical ruling governing presumptive status: *stam roeh pasul* — a random, undifferentiated shepherd is presumed *pasul* for testimony unless proven otherwise. *Stam gabbai kasher* — a random tax collector, by contrast, is presumed *kasher* until the opposite is demonstrated. The burden of proof runs in opposite directions for the two figures: the shepherd begins with a presumption of disqualification, while the tax collector begins with a presumption of validity.
