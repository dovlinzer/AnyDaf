# Sanhedrin 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Minor Fathering Child

### Rav Chisda vs Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: קָטָן שֶׁהוֹלִיד, אֵין בְּנוֹ נַעֲשֶׂה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ בֵּן״ – לְאִישׁ בֵּן, וְלֹא לְבֵן בֵּן. הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: ״כִּי יִהְיֶה בֵּן לְאִישׁ״. מַאי ״כִּי יִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ בֵּן״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְכִדְרַב חִסְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:** In the case of **a minor who fathered** a child, **his son cannot become a stubborn and rebellious son, as it is stated: "If a man has** a stubborn and rebellious **son,"** which indicates that the *halakha* applies only if **a man has a son, but not** if **a son,** i.e., one who is not yet a man, **has a son.** The Gemara asks: How can Rav Ḥisda derive his *halakha* from this verse? Doesn't **he require this** verse **to** teach us that **which Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says,** namely, that a minor is exempt from the punishment imposed upon a stubborn and rebellious son? The Gemara answers: This verse teaches two *halakhot*, as, **were** the intention to teach only the *halakha* that a minor is exempt, **let the verse say: If there be a son to a man.** For **what** reason does the verse introduce a change into the normal word order and state: **"If a man has a son"? Conclude from it** that the verse serves **to** teach the ruling **of Rav Ḥisda.**

The *daf* opens four lines from the top of 69a, at the words *etvei Abaye*. The context is the beginning of the laws of *ben sorer u'moreh*, the wayward and rebellious son. We have already positioned a debate between two *amoraim*: Rav Chisda taught that if a minor fathers a child, that child cannot himself become a *ben sorer u'moreh*, because the *pasuk* reads *le'ish ben*—a man has a son—implying that the father must be a man, not a child. The *gemara* understands this teaching to presuppose that an underage boy, someone under thirteen, is at least capable of fathering a child. This is understood to be in conflict with the position of Rava, who teaches—based on the case of *gezel ha'ger*, stealing from a convert and swearing falsely, whereupon the convert dies—that one need not be concerned about whether the convert left heirs if he was a minor, because a minor cannot father a child. So we have a direct debate between Rav Chisda and Rava over whether a minor is biologically capable of fathering offspring.

### Shifcha Charufa Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: ״אִישׁ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ. בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁרָאוּי לְבִיאָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rabba from a *baraita* discussing a designated maidservant, about whom the verse states: "And if a man lies carnally with a woman who is a maidservant designated to a man, and not fully redeemed, nor freedom given her, inquiry shall be made; they shall not be put to death, because she was not free" (Leviticus 19:20). From the word **"man," I have** derived **only** that this *halakha* applies to an adult **man.** But as for a minor **aged nine years and one day, who is fit for** engaging in **intercourse, from where** is it derived that he too is subject to this *halakha*? **The verse states: "And** if **a man."** The extraneous letter *vav*, meaning "and," serves to include a minor who is nine years old, as already at that age he can perform complete intercourse.

Abaye challenges this framework. The *pasuk* in the case of *shifcha charufa*—a slave woman who is partly betrothed to a slave—reads *ve'ish ki yishkav et isha shichvat zera*, "and a man who lies with a woman as a seminal emission." Rashi notes that this is one of the places where the Torah explicitly describes the act as *shichvat zera*, and it is accordingly one of the contexts where *halacha* requires *gemar biah*, full penetration, rather than mere *ha'ara*, simple insertion.

From the word *ish*, "a man," one might think the law applies only to an adult man. The *talmud lomar*, the additional *vav* in *ve'ish*, teaches that even a boy nine years and a day old—whom we recognize as capable of an act of intercourse—is included. But the *gemara* notes that this *pasuk* speaks of *shichvat zera* in a context requiring that the act be *ra'uy l'hazri'a*, appropriate for seminal emission. From here, Abaye argues, we see that an underage child can indeed emit seed. Does this not demonstrate that a minor's seed is potent?

### Seed Potency Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יֵשׁ לוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מוֹלִיד, כִּתְבוּאָה שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיאָה שְׁלִישׁ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to** Abaye: There is no proof from here, as even though a nine-year-old boy **has** sperm, **he cannot father** a child. His sperm is **like** the seed of **grain that** was cut even though it **had not** yet **reached one-third** of its growth. Such seed, even if planted, will not grow.

The *gemara* concedes the point about emission but refuses the conclusion: *yesh lo v'eino molid*—he has seed but it cannot procreate. The semen exists but is not yet fertile. The analogy given is *kitzvu'a shelo heivia shlish*: grain that has not yet reached a third of its ripening. There is grain there, but were you to plant the kernel, it would not yield new growth. So too, the minor's seed is present but lacks generative potency.

### Yazid Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה תָּנָא: ״וְכִי יָזִד אִישׁ״ – אִישׁ מֵזִיד וּמַזְרִיעַ, וְאֵין קָטָן מֵזִיד וּמַזְרִיעַ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״מֵזִיד״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּבַשּׁוֹלֵי הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּזֶד יַעֲקֹב נָזִיד״.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **of the school of Ḥizkiyya taught:** The verse states: **"But if a man comes intentionally [*yazid*]** against his neighbor, to slay him with guile, you shall take him from My altar, that he may die" (Exodus 21:14). The use of the term *yazid* in this context and its juxtaposition to the word "man" teaches that **a man can heat [*mezid*]** himself up **and produce** viable **sperm, but a minor cannot heat** himself up **and produce** viable **sperm.** Therefore, even though a minor can engage in full intercourse with a woman, he cannot father a child. **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: From where may** it **be inferred that this** word ***mezid* is a term** meaning **heating** up? **As it is written** in a different verse: **"And Jacob cooked [*vayyazed*] pottage"** (Genesis 25:29).

The *beis Chizkiyah* offers a *drasha* in support. The *pasuk* in *Parshat Mishpatim* reads *vechi yazid ish al re'ehu lehorgo be'arma*, "and when a man acts with premeditation against his fellow to kill him with cunning." The *drashah* turns on the word *yazid*. The *beis Chizkiyah* connects it to the verb found in *vayazed Yaakov nazid*, Yaakov cooked a stew—*yazid* carries the sense of cooking, of ripening. The teaching thus reads: *ish mazid u'mazria*, only a man whose seed has "cooked" and ripened within him can be fertile; *ve'ein katan mazid u'mazria*, a minor's seed has not yet undergone that ripening process.

Rav Mordechai asked Rav Ashi: what is the source that the word *mazid* carries the connotation of cooking and ripening? He answered: *dichsiv vayazed Yaakov nazid*—precisely the verse about Yaakov's stew. This is consistent with Rava's position that only once a person is an adult, after *bar mitzvah*, is his seed fertile.

### Ben v'Lo Av

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״בֵּן״ – וְלֹא אָב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't the school of Rabbi Yishmael teach** the following *baraita* concerning a stubborn and rebellious son: The verse that states: "If a man has a stubborn and rebellious son," teaches that **a son** can become a stubborn and rebellious son, **but not a father,** so that one who has a child cannot be sentenced as a stubborn and rebellious son.

The *gemara* now introduces a teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *ben v'lo av*—the *ben sorer u'moreh* must be a son, not yet a father. This excludes a case where the *ben sorer u'moreh* has himself already fathered a child. This is distinct from what we had been discussing, which was excluding the case where a *ben sorer u'moreh* was fathered by a minor. Here we are excluding the case where the *ben sorer u'moreh* is himself already a father.

But a question arises: since we have established that a *ben sorer u'moreh* is already *bar mitzvah*, is there even enough time—within the narrow window of his eligibility—for him to have fathered a child, assuming that a boy's seed only becomes potent at *bar mitzvah*? The *gemara* will now investigate this tension.

## Three-Month Window

### Conception Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיעַבַּר בָּתַר דְּאַיְיתִי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, וְאוֹלֵיד מִקַּמֵּי דְּלַקֵּיף זָקָן – מִי אִיכָּא שְׁהוּת כּוּלֵּי הַאי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי כְּרוּסְפָּדַאי: כׇּל יָמָיו שֶׁל בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים בִּלְבַד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say that** his wife **conceived after he grew two** pubic **hairs and** the baby **was born before he grew a beard around** his genitals, **is there such a long interval** between these two times to allow for carrying the child to term? **But doesn't Rabbi Kruspedai say: The entire time** during which it is possible to judge and sentence **a stubborn and rebellious son is only three months,** the time between the appearance of two pubic hairs and the growth of a beard around the genitals? Consequently, it is impossible for a child to be born to the stubborn and rebellious son during this period.

The *gemara* asks: *heichei dami*, what exactly is the case that the teaching of *ben v'lo av* comes to exclude? If we say the woman became pregnant *basar d'aisei shtei searos*—after he brought two pubic hairs, meaning after he reached *bar mitzvah* and his seed was potent—and she gave birth *mikamei d'leikef zakan*, before the lower beard fills out (which marks the upper end of his eligibility as a *ben sorer u'moreh*), then *mi ka shechiach kulei hai*? Is there really time for all of that to happen within his window of eligibility?

### Rav Chisda Three Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאִיעַבַּר מִקַּמֵּי דְּלַיְיתֵי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, וְאוֹלֵיד מִקַּמֵּיהּ דְּלַקֵּיף זָקָן, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: קָטָן מוֹלֵיד!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not that** his wife **conceived before he grew two** pubic **hairs, and** the baby **was born before he grew a beard around** his genitals? **And you can learn from it** that **a minor can,** in fact, **father** a child.

This is where the teaching of Rav Chisda becomes critical. Rav Chisda states—and it is worth noting, as an aside, that the name Chisda is the Aramaic cognate of *tzitzit*—*kol yamav shel ben sorer u'moreh eino ela gimel chodashim bilvad*: the entire period of eligibility for *ben sorer u'moreh* is only three months. Given that constraint, if conception could only occur after *bar mitzvah*, the woman would not give birth within the three-month window. So it would seem that the *drasha* of *ben v'lo av* has no practical application.

The *gemara* therefore suggests the only viable reading: the conception took place before he was *bar mitzvah*, *d'ibar mikamei d'laisi shtei searos*, and the woman gave birth while he was still within his three-month window. And from this, the *gemara* attempts to derive: *u'shma mina katan molid*—we see that a minor can indeed father a child, since the only way to construct the case is if the conception occurred before he was thirteen.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיעַבַּר בָּתַר דְּאַיְיתִי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, וְאוֹלִיד בָּתַר דְּאַקֵּיף זָקָן. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי כְּרוּסְפָּדַאי – כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אֲמַר: אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא ״בֵּן״ – וְלֹא הָרָאוּי לִקְרוֹתוֹ אָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this reasoning: **No, actually,** you can explain **that** his wife **conceived** only **after he grew two** pubic **hairs, and** the baby **was born after he grew a beard around** his genitals. **And** as for **that which** is **difficult** for you based on the statement **of Rabbi Kruspedai** that the *halakha* governing a stubborn and rebellious son applies for only three months, it can be explained as follows: **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **they say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, that the term "son" teaches that only **a son** can become a stubborn and rebellious son, **but not one who is fit to be called a father.** That is to say, the verse does not exclude someone whose child was born during this period, but rather one whose wife conceived during this time, so that he is fit to be called a father.

But the *gemara* rejects this inference. It says: *u'leolam d'ibar basar d'aisei shtei searos v'olid basar d'akef zakan*—the conception was after *bar mitzvah*, and the birth was after the three months, after the lower beard filled in. In that case, he is already no longer eligible as a *ben sorer u'moreh* on other grounds, and the exclusion of *ben v'lo av* is indeed without a practical case—and the *gemara* acknowledges this.

When Rav Dimi came from *Ma'arava*, he explained the teaching of *ben v'lo av* more precisely. What they say in the West is: *ben v'lo hara'ui likroso av*—he is excluded not when he has already become a father in fact, but once he has become someone who is *fit to be called a father*, i.e., someone who is *ra'uy lehachriso av*. This is precisely the conceptual foundation for Rav Chisda's three-month window.

### Conception Timing (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי כְּרוּסְפָּדַאי אָמַר רַבִּי שַׁבְּתַי: כׇּל יָמָיו שֶׁל בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה אֵינָן אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים בִּלְבַד. וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: מִשֶּׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת וְעַד שֶׁיַּקִּיף זָקָן!
>
> **Translation:** § Returning to **the** matter **itself: Rabbi Kruspedai says** that **Rabbi Shabbtai says: The entire time** during which it is possible to judge and sentence **a stubborn and rebellious son is only three months.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that a boy can be judged as a stubborn and rebellious son **from when he grows two** pubic **hairs until he grows a beard around** his genitals? This seems to indicate that his liability depends on his physical maturity, and not on any specific time period.

The reasoning is as follows: starting from the day a boy becomes *bar mitzvah*, his seed is potent. If he were to have intercourse that very day, and if we allow the possibility of a seven-month pregnancy, the fetus might be visible—*halachically* showing—after three months. At that point, one can already point to him and say: this man is the father of the child this woman is carrying. He is therefore already *ra'uy lehachriso av*, fit to be called a father. So the window of *ben sorer u'moreh* closes three months after *bar mitzvah*, not because he has necessarily become a father, but because he has reached the stage where he is theoretically capable of being identified as one.

The *drasha* of *ben v'lo av* is thus not excluding an actual father but someone who has entered the theoretical category of fatherhood. And that is precisely the limit Rav Chisda defines.

### Three Months vs Beard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקִּיף זָקָן – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מְלוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים. מְלוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא הִקִּיף.
>
> **Translation:** If **he grew a beard around** his genitals, then **even if three months have not passed,** he can no longer become liable as a stubborn and rebellious son. And if **three months passed,** then **even if he has not grown** a beard **around** his genitals, he is similarly exempt.

The *gemara* now raises an apparent conflict: *v'hanan tnan misheyavi shtei searos v'ad sheyakef*—our *mishnah* teaches that the window extends until the full beard fills out, *yikef zakan*. That could be longer than three months. How does Rav Chisda's three-month limit coexist with the *mishnah*'s criterion of the beard?

### Whichever Comes First

The *gemara* resolves this cleanly: whichever criterion is reached first closes the window. *Hikif zakan af al gav delo malu gimel chodashim*—if the lower beard fills out before three months have elapsed, the window closes even if three months have not yet passed. *Malu gimel chodashim af al gav delo hikif*—if three months pass before the beard fills out, the window closes even if the beard has not yet filled in. Each criterion reflects a distinct reason for closure. The beard filling in marks that the boy is no longer a *ben* teetering on the edge of manhood—he is a full adult. And three months marks the point at which he is already *ra'uy lehachriso av*. Whichever comes first applies.

## Rav Kuspai Teaching

### Seven-Month Pregnancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב מִנְּהַר פְּקוֹד קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבִּי כְּרוּסְפָּדַאי אָמַר רַבִּי שַׁבְּתַי, יוֹלֶדֶת לְשִׁבְעָה אֵין עוּבָּרָהּ נִיכָּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov from Nehar Pekod sat before Ravina and sat and said in the name of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: Learn from** the statement **that Rabbi Kruspedai** says that **Rabbi Shabbtai says** that when a woman **gives birth at seven** months, **her fetus cannot** yet **be discerned after one-third of her days** of pregnancy. In a nine-month pregnancy, the fetus can be discerned after three months, which is one-third of the pregnancy. In the case of a pregnancy that lasts seven months, the fetus cannot be discerned at the end of one-third of the pregnancy, i.e., after two and one-third months, but after three months, as in a standard nine-month pregnancy.

Rav Yaakov from Har Pakod was sitting before Ravina and said in the name of Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: we can learn from the teaching of Rav Kuspai, who said in the name of Rav Shabtai that *kol yamav shel ben sorer u'moreh eino ela gimel chodashim bilvad*, that the window is only three months. From this limit, we can infer something about seven-month pregnancies: *yoledet leshiva ein ubra nikar leshlish yameha*—for a woman who gives birth after seven months, the fetus is not visible at a third of the gestational period, but only after three full months.

The *gemara*'s background assumption is that viable births occur either after nine months or after seven months, but not in the eighth month—the eighth-month infant was understood not to survive, which is a distinct discussion. The question is: if a pregnancy is only seven months, does the fetus become visible proportionally sooner than in a nine-month pregnancy?

### Proportional Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ: עוּבָּרָהּ נִיכָּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ, לְמָה לִי שְׁלֹשָׁה? בִּתְרֵי וְתִילְתָּא סַגִּיא!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ya'akov explains the inference: **As if it enters your mind** that when a woman gives birth at seven months, **her fetus can** already **be discerned after one-third of her days** of pregnancy, i.e., after two and one-third months, **why do I** need **three** months from the time the boy reaches adulthood until the end of the time that he can become liable as a stubborn and rebellious son? A period of **two and one-third** months should **suffice.** If he engaged in intercourse with a woman immediately upon reaching adulthood and the intercourse resulted in a seven-month pregnancy, the fetus would be able to be discerned after two and one-third months, and he would be fit to be called a father already from then. From the statement of Rabbi Kruspedai, citing Rabbi Shabbtai, it is clear that the earliest time the fetus can be discerned is after three months of the pregnancy have passed.

The argument runs as follows. In a normal nine-month pregnancy, we say the fetus is visible after three months—a third of the gestational period. If the same proportion applied to a seven-month pregnancy, the fetus would be visible after two and a third months. If so, why is the window of *ben sorer u'moreh* set at three full months? The very day after *bar mitzvah*, a boy could theoretically have impregnated someone with a seven-month pregnancy, and that fetus would be visible after two and a third months—making him *ra'uy lehachriso av* at two and a third months, not three. Since Rav Kuspai sets the window at three full months, it must be that even for a seven-month pregnancy, the fetus only becomes visibly apparent after three months—not proportionally earlier.

### Majority in Capital

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ, עוּבָּרָהּ נִיכָּר לִשְׁלִישׁ יָמֶיהָ. זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to** Rabbi Ya'akov: **Actually, I** could **say to you** that even when a woman gives birth at seven months, **her fetus can** already **be discerned after one-third of her days** of pregnancy. But the *halakha* with regard to a stubborn and rebellious son was not adjusted accordingly because of the principle that one **follows the majority.** Most women give birth at nine months, and their fetuses are discernible only after three months. Therefore, the fact that one would be fit to be called a father in the case of a seven-month pregnancy is disregarded.

The response given is: *zil batar ruba*—go by the majority. Even if there might be a seven-month pregnancy where the fetus becomes visible earlier, when defining the parameters of *ra'uy lehachriso av*, we go by the majority of cases. In most circumstances, a woman will not show until three months. So the window is defined as three months by majority, even if the minority case might push it earlier.

### Rav Huna Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. אָמַר לֵיהּ: וּבְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת מִי אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוּבָּא? הַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״וְשָׁפְטוּ הָעֵדָה וְהִצִּילוּ הָעֵדָה״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **stated this** answer **before Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** whereupon **he said to** them: **But do we** blindly **follow the majority in** cases of **capital law** and not judge each case on its own merits? Doesn't **the Torah state: "And the congregation shall judge…and the congregation shall deliver"** (Numbers 35:24–25), from which it is derived that the court must make every effort to find exculpatory arguments in support of the accused; **and** yet **you say** that one **follows the majority?** If it is possible that already after two and one-third months the stubborn and rebellious son will be fit to be called a father, from that time on he should be exempt from punishment.

This answer was then brought before Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, and he objected sharply: *uvdinei nefashot mi azlinan batar ruba?* Do we go by *rov* in cases of capital law? *Ben sorer u'moreh* is a capital crime, and the Torah says *v'shafetu ha'eda vehitzilu ha'eda*—the court has an obligation to try to save the person. Surely when defining the *shiurim* of eligibility for a capital offense, we should try to narrow the window as much as possible and not rely on majority to extend it. If there is even a minority possibility that a boy could be *ra'uy lehachriso av* at two and a third months—because of a seven-month pregnancy—we should adopt that shorter window to protect him. Going by *rov* here seems to work *against* the accused.

### Ravina Responds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהְדְּרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּבְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת לָא אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוּבָּא? וְהָתְנַן: אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בִּשְׁנַיִם בַּחֹדֶשׁ״ וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה״, עֵדוּתָן קַיֶּימֶת, שֶׁזֶּה יוֹדֵעַ בְּעִיבּוּרוֹ שֶׁל חֹדֶשׁ וְזֶה אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages then **brought** Rav Huna's analysis **back to Ravina** and presented it **before** him. Ravina **said to** them: **And do we not follow the majority in** cases of **capital law? But didn't we learn** in the mishna (*Sanhedrin* 40a): If **one** witness **says** that the event occurred **on the second of the month, and one** witness **says** that the event occurred **on the third** of the month, this is not regarded as a contradiction and **their testimony stands,** since it is possible to say **that this** witness **knows of** the **addition of** a day to the previous **month,** and according to his tally the event occurred on the second of the month, **and that** witness **does not know** of the addition of a day to the previous month, and according to his tally the event occurred on the third of the month.

Ravina responded to Rav Huna's objection: do you think we never go by *rov* in *dinei nefashot*? We most certainly do. And the proof is from the *mishnah*: if one witness says the murder occurred on the second of the month and another says it was on the third, their testimony is not considered a contradiction—*eydutan kayemet*. The reason is that it is common for people to be uncertain about which day *Rosh Chodesh* fell, depending on whether the prior month was full or deficient. We therefore assume, by *rov*, that both witnesses are referring to the same event and one of them is simply mistaken about the calendar—a normal, prevalent error. We do not treat this as a *hakhasha*, a contradiction, that would disqualify both witnesses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ לָא אָמְרִינַן זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא, נֵימָא: הָנֵי דַּוְקָא קָא מַסְהֲדִי, וְאַכְחוֹשֵׁי הוּא דְּקָא מַכְחֲשִׁי אַהֲדָדֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא, וְרוּבָּא דְאִינָשֵׁי עֲבִדִי דְּטָעוּ בְּעִיבּוּרָא דְּיַרְחָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind** that **we do not say** that one **follows the majority** in cases of capital law, **let us** then **say** that **these** witnesses **are testifying with precision, and** that **they contradict each other,** and therefore the accused should be acquitted. **Rather, is it not because we say** that one **follows the majority, and the majority of people are apt to err with regard to the addition** of an extra day **to the month?**

Now, if we never applied *rov* in capital cases, we would be compelled to say the witnesses are actually contradicting one another—*ve'achshishei hu d'kamakhashishi ahadadi*—and we would have to disqualify the testimony. But we do not do that. Instead, we go by the majority pattern of human behavior and say: most people make calendar errors of this type, and so they are not contradicting each other. This shows that we do go by *rov* even in *dinei nefashot*.

Ravina's reasoning makes an important conceptual distinction. Going by *rov* in capital cases does not mean we execute someone on probabilistic evidence. Rather, the application of *rov* here is structural: it defines what counts as a contradiction, what parameters establish eligibility, what we regard as the normal course of events when setting up legal categories. When we ask "at what point is a boy *ra'uy lehachriso av*?"—that is a definitional question, answered by majority. It is not asking whether we will convict someone on probability alone.

### Three-Year-Old Girl

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִדִּפְתִּי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִיאָה, וְאִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ יָבָם – קְנָאָהּ, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti says: We learn** in another mishna (*Nidda* 44b) **as well** that one follows the majority even in cases of capital law: A girl who is **three years and one day old** whose father arranged her betrothal **can be betrothed with intercourse,** as, despite her age, the legal status of intercourse with her is that of full-fledged intercourse. **And** in a case where the childless husband of a girl three years and one day old dies, **if** his brother, the ***yavam*, engages in intercourse with her, he acquires her** as his wife. **And** if a girl of that age is married, a man other than her husband is **liable** for engaging in intercourse **with her due to** violation of the prohibition against adultery, as despite her age she is legally considered to be **a married woman.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְטַמְּאָה אֶת בּוֹעֲלָהּ, לְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב הַתַּחְתּוֹן כְּעֶלְיוֹן. נִישֵּׂאת לַכֹּהֵן – אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה. בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶחָד מִן הַפְּסוּלִין – פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה. וְאִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶחָד מִכׇּל הָעֲרָיוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בְּתוֹרָה – מוּמָתִין עָלֶיהָ, וְהִיא פְּטוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And** if she is impure due to menstruation, **she transmits impurity to one who engages in intercourse with her,** who then **renders** all the items designated for **lying beneath** him **impure like** the items designated for lying **above** him. If **she marries a priest, she may partake of *teruma*** like any other wife of a priest. If she is unmarried and **one** of the men **who is unfit** for the priesthood, e.g., a *mamzer* or *ḥalal*, **engaged in intercourse with her, he has disqualified her from** marrying into **the priesthood,** and if she is the daughter of a priest, she is disqualified from partaking of *teruma*. **And if one of any of those with whom relations are forbidden, which are enumerated in the Torah, engaged in intercourse with her,** e.g., her father or father-in-law, the man is **executed by** the court for engaging in intercourse with **her, and she is exempt** because she is a minor.

Rabbi Yirmiyah of Difti adds a further proof. We have taught in the *mishnah*—and it appears as well in *Mishnah Niddah*—that *bat shalosh shanim v'yom echad miskadeshet b'biah*: a girl of three years and one day can be betrothed through *biah*, an act of intercourse, which is considered a legally effective act. This has several *halachic* ramifications. If her *yavam* performed *yibum* with her, it is effective. If someone had intercourse with her after such a marriage, he would be liable as one who committed adultery with a married woman, *aishes ish*, and would be executed. If she was a *niddah*, the man who had intercourse with her becomes an *av hatumah* himself, and the objects beneath him are rendered *tamei* in the manner of a *mishkav* of a *niddah*, one degree removed. If she is married to a *kohen*, she may eat *terumah*, since she is fit for *biah*. Intercourse with someone invalid for *kehunah* renders her unfit to marry a *kohen*. And if one of the *arayos* had intercourse with her—a father, a case of *aishes ish*, or whatever the applicable category—that person is executed, while she is exempt as a minor.

Now, why does all of this apply already at three years old? One might ask: should we not be concerned that she will turn out to be an *aylonis*—a woman with certain infertile and masculine characteristics—in which case the original *kiddushin* would retroactively be *kiddushei ta'us*, a mistaken betrothal, and the *kiddushin* was never valid? If so, the man should not be liable for *aishes ish* at all. The answer is: *zil batar ruba*—the vast majority of women are not an *aylonis*, and we go by that majority. We do not suspend the legal effect of the *kiddushin* out of concern for a rare exception.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? אֵימָא: אַיְילוֹנִית הִיא, וְאַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי לָא קַדֵּישׁ! אֶלָּא, לָאו דְּאָמְרִינַן: זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא, וְרוֹב נָשִׁים לָאו אַיְילוֹנִית נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya of Difti explains how this mishna demonstrates that one follows the majority even in cases of capital law: **Why** is a man who engaged in intercourse with a three-year-old girl who was married to another man liable to receive the death penalty? **Say** that perhaps it will turn out that **she is a sexually underdeveloped woman [*ailonit*]** who is incapable of bearing children, **and** her husband **did not betroth her with this understanding;** and consequently the marriage is null, as it was entered into in error. Therefore, a man who engaged in intercourse with her should not be liable to receive the death penalty for adultery. **Rather, is it not that we say** that one **follows the majority, and the majority of women are not sexually underdeveloped women,** and therefore the assumption is that the betrothal was valid? This is proof that even in cases of capital law one follows the majority.

The *gemara* pushes back: when the *mishnah* says one is *chayav* for intercourse with her, perhaps it means only liable to bring a *korban* in a case of *shogeg*, not liable to execution—and so there is no proof that *rov* applies in capital cases specifically. But the *mishnah* explicitly says *humatu aleha*, they are executed on her account. So we are dealing with *dinei nefashot* after all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״מוּמָתִין עַל יָדָהּ״ קָתָנֵי? בְּבָא עָלֶיהָ אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it taught** in the mishna: And if one of any of those with whom relations are forbidden, which are enumerated in the Torah, engaged in intercourse with her, the man is **executed by** the court for engaging in intercourse with **her?** The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case **where her father** or some other close relative **engaged in intercourse with her,** so that the prohibition is incest, rather than adultery.

The *gemara* then tries another evasion: perhaps the case of *aishes ish* in the *mishnah* is not about *kiddushin* where there is a concern of *kiddushei ta'us*, but rather about a case of the father himself having intercourse with her—which is incest, *arayos*, and has nothing to do with the validity of *kiddushin*. The *gemara* acknowledges that the *mishnah* refers to *kol ha'arayos*, all the forbidden relationships, which does include cases of *aishes ish* through valid *kiddushin*. Therefore, we are not concerned about the minority possibility of *aylonis*, and we see that *rov* applies even in *dinei nefashot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״אִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶחָד מִכׇּל הָעֲרָיוֹת״ קָתָנֵי? אֶלָּא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן: דְּקַבְּלַהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it taught: If one of any of those with whom relations are forbidden engaged in intercourse with her,** the man is executed by the court for engaging in intercourse with her? This seems to indicate that the death penalty is imposed for all types of forbidden intercourse with a three-year-old girl, even if the intercourse is forbidden as a result of her being married. The Gemara refutes this claim: **Rather, what are we dealing with here?** With a case **where** the husband explicitly **accepted her upon himself** as his wife even if she turns out to be a sexually underdeveloped woman. Therefore, another man who engages in intercourse with her is liable to receive the death penalty even if he is not one of her close relatives.

The *gemara* leaves this as a live possibility.

## Beis Shammai/Hillel

### Mother-Son Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְסוֹלֶלֶת בִּבְנָהּ קָטָן, וְהֶעֱרָה בָּהּ – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If a woman **was acting lewdly with her minor son and he performed the initial stage of intercourse with her, Beit Shammai say** that **he has** thereby **disqualified her from** marrying into **the priesthood. And Beit Hillel deem her fit** to marry into the priesthood, because they maintain that the intercourse of a minor is not regarded as intercourse.

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraisa*: *hamisuleles bifnei bna katan*, a woman who is engaged in physically intimate contact with her minor son—Rashi describes the phrase as *lashon pritzus*, language of licentiousness—and her son performed *ha'ara'ah*, mere insertion. *Beis Shammai* says she is now *pasul min hakehunah*, disqualified from marrying a *kohen*, since she has had an act of intercourse with her son. *Beis Hillel* says she remains *kasher*. The central question is what the dispute turns on, since we know that an act of intercourse with a minor is only considered legally significant when the minor is at least nine years old.

### Nine vs Eight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר נַחְמָנִי: אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר זְעֵירִי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁבִּיאָתוֹ בִּיאָה, פָּחוֹת מִבֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה שֶׁאֵין בִּיאָתוֹ בִּיאָה. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּבֶן שְׁמֹנֶה, דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: גָּמְרִינַן מִדּוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: לָא גָּמְרִינַן מִדּוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rabba bar Naḥmani, says** that **Rav Ḥisda says, and some say** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Ze'eiri says: All,** i.e., both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, **concede with regard to** a boy **nine years and one day old that his intercourse** is regarded as **intercourse** and disqualifies a woman from marrying into the priesthood as well as results in her liability to receive the death penalty, even though he himself is not liable to receive it. And they also all concede concerning a boy **less than eight years old that his intercourse** is **not** regarded as **intercourse** vis-à-vis these *halakhot*. **They disagree only about** a boy who is **eight years old, as Beit Shammai maintain** that **we learn from earlier generations,** when people were able to father children at that age, and we apply that reality to the present; **and Beit Hillel maintain** that **we do not learn from earlier generations.**

Rav Chiya the son of Rava bar Nachmani said in the name of Rav Chisda—and some attribute it as Rav Chisda said in the name of Ze'iri—that everyone agrees on two extreme points: once the son is nine years and a day old, *she'biaso bia*, his act is definitely considered an act of intercourse, and whether intentional or not, she is disqualified from a *kohen*. And everyone agrees that if the son is under eight years old, *ein biaso bia*, his act is not considered intercourse at all, and she remains eligible.

### Eight-Year Dispute

The dispute is specifically about a boy who is eight years old. *Beis Shammai* says *gamina midoros harishonim*—we learn from the earlier generations, and the *gemara* will demonstrate from *pesukim* that earlier generations could father children at eight years old. Therefore, an eight-year-old's act is considered intercourse. *Beis Hillel* says *lo gamina midoros harishonim*—we do not extrapolate from the earlier generations to our present reality. Whatever was possible in biblical times, when people lived hundreds of years and human physiology may have differed, does not define the *halacha* for us today. For us, the threshold is nine years and a day.

It is worth noting that the framing of this debate specifically in the context of a mother and her minor son is somewhat striking, since the same question of when a minor's act is considered intercourse arises in many other contexts. Nonetheless, this is how the *gemara* chooses to present the dispute.

## Earlier Generations

### Batsheva Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים מְנָלַן דְּאוֹלִיד? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב: ״הֲלוֹא זֹאת בַּת שֶׁבַע בַּת אֱלִיעָם אֵשֶׁת אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי״, וּכְתִיב: ״אֱלִיעָם בֶּן אֲחִיתֹפֶל הַגִּלֹנִי״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח בְּיַד נָתָן הַנָּבִיא וַיִּקְרָא אֶת שְׁמוֹ יְדִידְיָהּ בַּעֲבוּר (כִּי) ה׳ (אֲהֵבוֹ)״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** in **earlier generations** men **fathered** children at this age? **If we say** that we know this **from** the following calculation: **It is written: "Is this not Bathsheba, daughter of Eliam, wife of Uriah the Hittite?"** (II Samuel 11:3). **And it is** also **written: "And Eliam, son of Ahithophel the Gilonite"** (II Samuel 23:34), which teaches that Bathsheba was the granddaughter of Ahithophel. **And it is written** with reference to the birth of Solomon: **"And he sent by the hand of Nathan the prophet, and he called his name Jedidiah, for the Lord's sake"** (II Samuel 12:25).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי לִשְׁנָתַיִם יָמִים וַיִּהְיוּ גֹזְזִים לְאַבְשָׁלוֹם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאַבְשָׁלוֹם בָּרַח וַיֵּלֶךְ גְּשׁוּרָה וַיְהִי שָׁם שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** later **it is written: "And it came to pass after two years, that Absalom had sheepshearers"** (II Samuel 13:23), and at that time Amnon was killed (see II Samuel 13:23–29), this being at least two years after Solomon was born. **And** afterward **it is written: "So Absalom fled, and went to Geshur, and was there three years"** (II Samuel 13:38), so that this was five years after Solomon was born.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּשֶׁב אַבְשָׁלוֹם בִּירוּשָׁלִַים שְׁנָתַיִם יָמִים וּפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ לֹא רָאָה״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה וַיֹּאמֶר אַבְשָׁלוֹם אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ אֵלְכָה נָּא וַאֲשַׁלְּמָה אֶת נִדְרִי אֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתִּי לַה׳ בְּחֶבְרוֹן״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲחִיתֹפֶל רָאָה כִּי לֹא נֶעֶשְׂתָה עֲצָתוֹ וַיַּחֲבֹשׁ אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וַיָּקׇם וַיֵּלֶךְ אֶל בֵּיתוֹ וְאֶל עִירוֹ וַיְצַו אֶל בֵּיתוֹ וַיֵּחָנַק״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written: "So Absalom dwelt two years in Jerusalem, and did not see the king's face"** (II Samuel 14:28), bringing the tally to seven years after Solomon was born. **And it is written: "And it came to pass after forty years, that Absalom said to the king, I pray you, let me go and pay my vow, which I have vowed to the Lord, in Hebron"** (II Samuel 15:7). This was the beginning of Absalom's rebellion against David. Accordingly, at that time Solomon was at least seven years old. **And** at some point during the rebellion **it is written: "And when Ahithophel saw that his counsel was not followed, he saddled his donkey, and arose, and went to his house, to his city, and put his household in order, and strangled himself** and died" (II Samuel 17:23).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים וּמִרְמָה לֹא יֶחֱצוּ יְמֵיהֶם״. וְתַנְיָא: כׇּל שְׁנוֹתָיו שֶׁל דּוֹאֵג אֵינָן אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאַרְבַּע, וְשֶׁל אֲחִיתֹפֶל אֵינָן אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשָׁלֹשׁ. כַּמָּה הָוְיָא לְהוּ? תְּלָתִין וּתְלָת. דַּל שְׁבַע דַּהֲוָה שְׁלֹמֹה, פָּשׁ לְהוּ עֶשְׂרִים וְשֵׁית. דַּל תַּרְתֵּי שְׁנֵי לִתְלָתָא עִבּוּרֵי, אִשְׁתְּכַח דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד בְּתַמְנֵי אוֹלֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written: "Bloody and deceitful men shall not live out half their days"** (Psalms 55:24). **And** in keeping with this verse, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **All of Doeg's years were only thirty-four and Ahithophel's were only thirty-three.** Neither reached the age of thirty-five, half of the normal life span of seventy years. Based on this, one can calculate: **How many** years did Ahithophel live? **Thirty-three. Subtract seven** years, **Solomon's** age at the time of Ahithophel's death, which **leaves** Ahithophel **twenty-six** years old at the time of Solomon's birth. **Subtract two** more **years for three pregnancies,** one preceding the birth of Eliam the son of Ahithophel, one preceding the birth of Bathsheba, daughter of Eliam, and one preceding the birth of Solomon, son of Bathsheba. **It turns out** that three generations were born in twenty-four years, and **that each and every** parent **begot** a child **at** the age of **eight.**

The *gemara* asks: *vedoros rishonim minalan de'olid*, where do we know that the earlier generations could father children at eight years old?

The first attempt is from the genealogy of Batsheva and Achitofel. The *pasuk* identifies Batsheva as the daughter of Eliam (*halo zos Batsheva bas Eliam eshes Uriya Hachiti*), and a second *pasuk* identifies Eliam as the son of Achitofel the Gilonite (*Eliam ben Achitofel ha'Giloni*). So Batsheva is the granddaughter of Achitofel. Another *pasuk* records the birth of Shlomo to Batsheva, who was named Yedidya because God loved him (*vayishlach beyad Nasan Hanavi vayikra shemo Yedidya ba'avur Hashem*).

From there, the *gemara* strings together a series of chronological data points. Two years after Shlomo was born, *Avshalom*'s shearers were at work—so Shlomo is two years old. Then Avshalom fled to Geshur and remained there three years—Shlomo is now five. Then Avshalom returned to Yerushalayim and did not see his father's face for two years—Shlomo is seven. At that point, Achitofel saw that his counsel had not been followed, saddled his donkey, went home, gave his final instructions, and hanged himself. So Shlomo was seven years old when Achitofel died.

How old was Achitofel when he died? The *pasuk* states *anshei damim u'mirma lo yechetzu yemeihem*—men of blood and deceit will not live half their days. And a *baraisa* teaches: the lifetime of Do'eg was only thirty-four years, and the lifetime of Achitofel was only thirty-three years. The reasoning is *yemei shnoseinu bahem shiv'im shana*—seventy years is the norm, and these men did not reach half of that.

Now the arithmetic: Achitofel died at thirty-three, at which point Shlomo was seven. Subtract seven from thirty-three: Achitofel was twenty-six when Batsheva gave birth to Shlomo. Over the course of that twenty-six years, three generations of births occurred—Achitofel to Eliam, Eliam to Batsheva, Batsheva to Shlomo—accounting for approximately two years of pregnancy time in total. Subtract two from twenty-six, leaving twenty-four years divided over three generational births. That is eight years per generation: each one fathered or bore a child at age eight.

### Objection Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בִּתְשַׁע אוֹלֵיד, וּבַת שֶׁבַע אוֹלִידָא בְּשֵׁית, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתְּתָא בָּרְיָא. תֵּדַע, דְּהָא הֲוַי לַהּ וָלָד מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **From where** do you prove this? **Perhaps both** Ahithophel and his son Eliam **fathered** children **when** they were each **nine** years old, **and Bathsheba gave birth** to Solomon **when** she was **six, because a woman is stronger** and can conceive at an earlier age. **Know** that this is true that women conceive at an earlier age, **as** Bathsheba **had already** given birth to **a child** from David before giving birth to Solomon (see II Samuel 11:27). Therefore, no proof can be derived from here.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this calculation. Perhaps the two men in this chain—Achitofel and Eliam—each fathered at nine, not eight. And perhaps Batsheva was even younger than eight when she gave birth to Shlomo—perhaps as young as six. After all, we know *mishum d'itita baria*, a woman is biologically more robust and could theoretically mature earlier. Moreover, there was already a child born to Batsheva before Shlomo—the child who died shortly after birth—so the timeline becomes even more compressed. This means Batsheva would have had to be even younger than six, which only reinforces that the two men might have been nine and Batsheva extremely young—and you cannot prove from this chain that anyone gave birth at eight.

### Abraham Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״אֵלֶּה תּוֹלְדֹת תֶּרַח תֶּרַח הוֹלִיד אֶת אַבְרָם אֶת נָחוֹר וְאֶת הָרָן״. וְאַבְרָהָם גָּדוֹל מִנָּחוֹר שָׁנָה, וְנָחוֹר גָּדוֹל מֵהָרָן שָׁנָה. נִמְצָא אַבְרָהָם גָּדוֹל שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים מֵהָרָן. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח אַבְרָם וְנָחוֹר לָהֶם נָשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **from here** that one can deduce that in earlier generations men fathered children at the age of eight, as it is written: **"And these are the generations of Terah; Terah begot Abram, Nahor, and Haran"** (Genesis 11:27). **And Abraham was** at least **one year older than Nahor, and Nahor was one year older than Haran,** so **it turns out** that **Abraham was two years older than Haran. And it is written: "And Abram and Nahor took wives for themselves;** the name of Abram's wife was Sarai and the name of Nahor's wife was Milka, daughter of Haran, father of Milka and father of Iscah" (Genesis 11:29).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״יִסְכָּה״ זוֹ שָׂרָה, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״יִסְכָּה״? שֶׁסּוֹכָה בְּרוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״כֹּל אֲשֶׁר תֹּאמַר אֵלֶיךָ שָׂרָה שְׁמַע בְּקֹלָהּ״. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״יִסְכָּה״ – שֶׁהַכֹּל סָכִים בְּיוֹפְיָהּ. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּפֹּל [אַבְרָהָם] עַל פָּנָיו וַיִּצְחָק וַיֹּאמֶר בְּלִבּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. כַּמָּה קַשִּׁישׁ אַבְרָהָם מִשָּׂרָה? עֲשַׂר שְׁנִין. וְקַשִּׁישׁ מֵאֲבוּהּ תַּרְתֵּין שְׁנִין. אִשְׁתְּכַח כִּי אוֹלְדַהּ הָרָן לְשָׂרָה בְּתַמְנֵי אוֹלְדַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Iscah is** in fact **Sarah. And why was she called Iscah? Because she envisioned [*shesokha*]** hidden matters **by means of divine inspiration. And this** explains **what is written: "In all that Sarah has said to you, hearken to her voice"** (Genesis 21:12). **Alternatively,** Sarah was also called **Iscah, because all gazed [*sokhim*] upon her beauty. And it is written: "And Abraham fell upon his face, and laughed, and said in his heart:** Shall a child be born to him that is a hundred years old? And shall Sarah, who is ninety years old, give birth?" (Genesis 17:17). **How much older was Abraham than Sarah?** He was **ten years** older than her **and,** as stated above, he was **two years older than her father,** Haran. **It turns out** then that **when Haran begot Sarah, he begot her at** the age of **eight.**

The *gemara* therefore turns to a second passage. *Eileh toldot Terach, Terach holid et Avram et Nachor v'et Haran*. Assuming these sons are listed in birth order, and assuming each is one year apart from the next, Avram is two years older than Haran. Another *pasuk* tells us that Avram and Nachor took wives, and Nachor's wife was Milkah, the daughter of Haran—*avi Milkah v'avi Yiskah*. The *pasuk* also mentions a daughter of Haran named Yiskah. Rabbi Yitzchak identifies Yiskah with Sarah: *Yiskah zo Sarah*. Why is she called Yiskah? Because she could see with *ruach hakodesh*. Alternatively, *yiskah* connotes that all would look upon her beauty. Either way, Sarah is identified as a daughter of Haran, Avram's younger brother.

Now the calculation: Avram was a hundred years old and Sarah was ninety when Yitzchak was born, making Avram ten years older than Sarah. But Avram is also two years older than Haran, Sarah's father. If Haran is two years younger than Avram, and Avram is ten years older than Sarah, then when Sarah was born, Avram was ten. That means Haran at that moment was eight—making Haran eight years old when Sarah was born. Or, more precisely, given that conception precedes birth by nine months, Haran would have been about seven when he impregnated Sarah's mother—which the *gemara* acknowledges is a slight imprecision, though the logic points toward eight.

### Birth Order Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא אַבְרָהָם זוּטָא דַּאֲחוּהּ הֲוָה, וְדֶרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **From where** do you prove this? **Perhaps Abraham was the youngest of the brothers, and** not the oldest among them. The fact that Abraham is listed first is no proof that he was the oldest, as perhaps the verse **listed them in the order of their wisdom** and therefore Abraham, being the wisest, was mentioned first.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּדַע דְּקָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ קְרָא דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי נֹחַ בֶּן חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה וַיּוֹלֶד נֹחַ אֶת שֵׁם אֶת חָם וְאֶת יָפֶת״. שֵׁם גָּדוֹל מֵחָם שָׁנָה, וְחָם גָּדוֹל מִיֶּפֶת שָׁנָה. נִמְצָא שֵׁם גָּדוֹל מִיֶּפֶת שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים. וּכְתִיב: ״וְנֹחַ בֶּן שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה וְהַמַּבּוּל הָיָה מַיִם עַל הָאָרֶץ״. וּכְתִיב: ״(וְאֵלֶּה) תּוֹלְדוֹת שֵׁם שֵׁם בֶּן מְאַת שָׁנָה וַיּוֹלֶד אֶת אַרְפַּכְשָׁד שְׁנָתַיִם אַחַר הַמַּבּוּל״. בֶּן מְאָה שָׁנָה? בַּר מְאָה וְתַרְתֵּין שְׁנִין הֲוָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Know** that it is true **that the verse** sometimes **lists** brothers not according to their birth order, but **in the order of their** degrees of **wisdom, as it is written: "And Noah was five hundred years old; and Noah begot Shem, Ham, and Japheth"** (Genesis 5:32). According to this, **Shem was** at least **one year older than Ham, and Ham one year older than Japheth,** so **it turns out** that **Shem was two years older than Japheth. And it is written: "And Noah was six hundred years old when the flood of waters was upon the earth"** (Genesis 7:6). **And it is written: "These are the descendants of Shem; Shem was one hundred years old, and begot Arpachshad two years after the flood"** (Genesis 11:10). If Shem was the oldest brother, how could he be only **100 years old? He must have been** at least **102 years old,** as Noah was 500 years old when his third son was born, and he was 600 years old at the time of the flood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, דֶּרֶךְ חׇכְמָתָן קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse **listed them in the order of their** degrees of **wisdom,** Shem being the wisest. With regard to his age, Shem was the youngest of the brothers, having been born when Noah was 502 years old. Shem begot his son 100 years later, which was two years after the flood. **Here too,** then, with regard to the sons of Terah, it can be argued that the verse **lists them in the order of their** degrees of **wisdom.**

The *gemara* objects: *dilma Avraham zuta de'achu hava*—perhaps Haran was the oldest son and Avram was the younger, and they are not listed in birth order at all. *V'derech chochmatam kachashev lehu*—the Torah sometimes enumerates children according to their wisdom or stature rather than their birth order. And proof that such non-chronological ordering occurs is from Noach's sons. The *pasuk* says Noach was five hundred years old when he gave birth to Shem, Cham, and Yafet—listed in that order. If we assume they are one year apart in birth order, Shem is two years older than Yafet. The *mabul* occurred when Noach was six hundred, making Shem a hundred years old at that time. But a subsequent *pasuk* says: *Shem ben mea shana vayoled et Arpachshad shnatayim achar ha-mabul*—Shem was a hundred years old and fathered Arpachshad two years *after* the flood. If Shem was a hundred at the time of the flood, he would have been a hundred and two two years after—yet the *pasuk* says he was a hundred when he fathered Arpachshad. *Bar mea ve'tartein shnin havei*—he should have been a hundred and two. The resolution: *ela derech chochmatam kachashev lehu*—Shem, Cham, and Yafet are listed in order of their stature, not their birth. Shem was actually two years younger than the *pasuk*'s listing implies if read as birth order, which resolves the arithmetic. And so here too by Avram, Nachor, and Haran—the listing may reflect stature, not age—and you cannot prove that Avram was the oldest.

### Bezalel Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב זְבִיד מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא. אָמַר לִי: אַתּוּן מֵהָכָא מַתְנִיתוּ, וַאֲנַן מֵהָכָא מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ: ״וּלְשֵׁם יֻלַּד גַּם הוּא אֲבִי כׇּל בְּנֵי עֵבֶר אֲחִי יֶפֶת הַגָּדוֹל״. יֶפֶת הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּאֶחָיו הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: I stated this discussion before Rav Zevid of Neharde'a.** When he heard it, **he said to me: You learn** that Shem was not Noah's oldest son **from there, and we learn it from here: "And to Shem, the father of all the children of Eber, the brother of Japheth the elder, to him also were children born"** (Genesis 10:21). This verse indicates that **Japheth,** rather than Shem, **was the oldest of the brothers.**

Since neither the Achitofel-Batsheva chain nor the Avraham-Haran genealogy provides an airtight proof, Rav Kahana said: I presented these arguments before Rav Zvid of Nehardea, and he said to me—you try to prove it from there, but we prove it from here, from the chain of Bezalel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְנָלַן? מֵהָכָא: ״וּבְצַלְאֵל בֶּן אוּרִי בֶן חוּר לְמַטֵּה יְהוּדָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַתָּמׇת עֲזוּבָה אֵשֶׁת כָּלֵב וַיִּקַּח לוֹ כָלֵב אֶת אֶפְרָת וַתֵּלֶד לוֹ אֶת חוּר״. וְכִי עֲבַד בְּצַלְאֵל מִשְׁכָּן בַּר כַּמָּה הָוֵי? בַּר תְּלֵיסַר, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ אֲשֶׁר הֵמָּה עֹשִׂים״. וְתַנְיָא: שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה עָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה מִשְׁכָּן, שְׁנִיָּה הֵקִים מִשְׁכָּן וְשָׁלַח מְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, from where do we** derive that in earlier generations men fathered children at the age of eight? **From here,** as it is written: **"And Bezalel, son of Uri, son of Hur, of the tribe of Judah,** made all that the Lord commanded Moses" (Exodus 38:22). **And it is written: "And Azubah died, and Caleb took for himself Ephrat, who bore him Hur.** And Hur begot Uri, and Uri begot Bezalel" (I Chronicles 2:19–20). **And when Bezalel made the Tabernacle how old was he?** He must have been at least **thirteen years old, as it is written:** "And all the wise men that carried out all the work of the sanctuary, came **every man from his work that they did"** (Exodus 36:4), and one who is less than thirteen is not called a man. **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **In the first year** following the exodus from Egypt **Moses made the Tabernacle; in the second** year **he erected the Tabernacle and sent out the spies.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה אָנֹכִי בִּשְׁלֹחַ מֹשֶׁה עֶבֶד ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״, ״וְעַתָּה הִנֵּה אָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם בֶּן חָמֵשׁ וּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה״. כַּמָּה הָוְיָא לְהוּ? אַרְבְּעִין. דַּל אַרְבֵּיסַר דַּהֲוָה בְּצַלְאֵל, פָּשָׁא לְהוּ עֶשְׂרִים וְשֵׁית. דַּל תַּרְתֵּי שְׁנֵי דִּתְלָתָא עִיבּוּרֵי, אִשְׁתְּכַח דְּכֹל חַד וְחַד בְּתַמְנֵי אוֹלֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written** that Caleb, Bezalel's great-grandfather, said to Joshua: **"I was forty years old when Moses the servant of the Lord sent** me from Kadesh Barnea to spy out the land" (Joshua 14:7). And he added: **"And now, behold, I am this day eighty-five years old"** (Joshua 14:10). **How many** years old **was** Caleb when he was sent off with the spies? He was **forty. Subtract fourteen** years, **as Bezalel was** at least fourteen years old when Caleb was sent to spy out the land. This is known because that mission took place a year after the Tabernacle was erected. This **leaves twenty-six** years. **Subtract two** more **years for three pregnancies,** one preceding the birth of Hur, son of Caleb, one preceding the birth of Uri, son of Hur, and one preceding the birth of Bezalel, son of Uri. **It turns out** that three generations were born in twenty-four years, and **that each and every** parent **begot** a child **at** the age of **eight.**

The *pasuk* states: *u'Vetzalel ben Uri ben Chur le'mateh Yehuda*—Bezalel was the son of Uri, the son of Chur. Another *pasuk* records that Caleb married Efrat and she bore him Chur. So the chain is: Caleb fathered Chur, Chur fathered Uri, Uri fathered Bezalel.

When did Bezalel build the *mishkan*? He had to have been at least thirteen years old, as derived from the *pasuk* *ish ish mi'melachto asher hem osim*—the word *ish* requiring full majority. A *baraisa* teaches: in the first year after leaving Egypt, Moshe constructed the *mishkan*; in the second year, the *mishkan* was erected and the *meraglim* were sent. So when the *meraglim* were sent, Bezalel was at minimum fourteen years old.

Now, the *pasuk* records Caleb saying: *ben arba'im shana anochi bishlo'ach Moshe eved Hashem*—I was forty years old when Moshe sent me as a spy. So when the *meraglim* were sent, Caleb was forty. At that point, Bezalel was fourteen. Subtract fourteen from forty: Caleb was twenty-six when Bezalel was born. Over those twenty-six years, three births occurred—Caleb to Chur, Chur to Uri, Uri to Bezalel—accounting for approximately two years of pregnancy time in total. Subtract two from twenty-six, leaving twenty-four years divided across three generations. That is eight years per generation: *ishtakach de'kol chad v'chad bitamnei aolid*—each one fathered his child at age eight.

This chain, unlike the previous attempts, is not susceptible to the objections about birth order or alternative arithmetic. It is this proof from Bezalel that the *gemara* accepts as establishing that *doros harishonim* did indeed father children at eight years old—which is the foundation for *Beis Shammai*'s position that an eight-year-old's act of intercourse is *halachically* significant.

## Not a Daughter

### Rabbi Shimon Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּן, וְלֹא בַּת. תַּנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בְּדִין הוּא שֶׁתְּהֵא בַּת רְאוּיָה לִהְיוֹת כְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, שֶׁהַכֹּל מְצוּיִין אֶצְלָהּ בַּעֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the penalty for rebelliousness is imposed upon **a son, but not** upon **a daughter. It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says: It would be reasonable** that **a daughter should be fit to be** treated **like a stubborn and rebellious son,** and to be punished like him if she sins in the same way as he does. The reason is **that all manner of sin is found in her vicinity.**

The *mishnah* states: *ben v'lo bas*—the *ben sorer u'moreh* must be a son, not a daughter. A *baraisa* records Rabbi Shimon's reasoning: *b'din hu she'tei bas re'uya lihyos k'ben sorer u'moreh*—by logical reasoning, a daughter should equally qualify. Why? *She'kol metzuyin etzla b'aveira*—because all manner of sin is found in her vicinity. If the concern behind *ben sorer u'moreh* is that someone who is heading down a destructive path should be put to death before he becomes a full-fledged criminal who murders for his indulgences, then the same concern applies to a girl who is on such a path, since she could turn to prostitution and cause great harm. The logical grounds are arguably even stronger.

### Ben v'Lo Bas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גְּזֵירַת הַכָּתוּב הִיא: ״בֵּן״, וְלֹא בַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **But it is a Torah edict** that the penalty for rebelliousness is imposed only upon **a son, and not** upon **a daughter.**

Yet despite Rabbi Shimon's logic, the conclusion is unambiguous: *ela gezeras ha'kasuv hu, ben v'lo bas*—it is a divine decree, the *pasuk* says *ben*, a son, and not *bas*, a daughter. No matter how compelling the logical argument in favor of including a daughter, the explicit language of the Torah determines the *halacha*. The category of *ben sorer u'moreh* applies only to a boy.
