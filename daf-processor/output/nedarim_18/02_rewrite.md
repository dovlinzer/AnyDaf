# Nedarim 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Neder Betoch Neder

### Rav Huna vs. Shmuel

The daf begins at 18a, approximately four lines down, with *mative rav hamnuna* — a challenge posed by Rav Hamnuna. We are in the midst of the debate between Rav Huna and Shmuel concerning what the *mishnah* means when it says *yesh neder betoch neder* — that there is a neder within a neder.

The classic illustration involves the acceptance of *nezirut*. If someone says *hareini nazir hayom* and then says *hareini nazir lemachar*, Rav Huna requires that the two *neziruyot* not be identical — that there be some non-overlapping period. The Ran explains that when one day does not overlap between the two acceptances, that day — day thirty-one — causes an entirely new thirty-day *nezirut* to take effect. That is Rav Huna's position. Shmuel, reading the *mishnah* in its simpler sense, holds that even if the second acceptance is word-for-word identical to the first — *hareini nazir hareini nazir* — it nonetheless takes effect: not immediately, but held in abeyance until the first *nezirut* concludes, at which point the second kicks in automatically.

What emerged in the prior session is that even Rav Huna would concede that if someone accepted both *neziruyot* simultaneously — saying, for example, *harei alai shtei nezirut* — the phenomenon of one taking effect while the other hovers in abeyance is valid. His objection applies specifically to the sequential case, where one *nezirut* is already in force: attempting to accept the identical state upon oneself after it is already operative, he maintains, is a meaningless act.

### Ran's Approaches

It is worth noting that the Ran actually presents two distinct ways of reading the entire *sugya*, and we have been following only one of them. The approach we have been working with understands *neder betoch neder* as the second *nezirut* being suspended and held in abeyance, to take effect when the first concludes. This explains naturally why the phenomenon is unique to *nezirut*: only *nezirut* has a finite, time-bound character, which creates conceptual space for the second to hover.

However, the Ran also acknowledges a quite different reading: that *yesh neder betoch neder* means both *neziruyot* take effect simultaneously, making one a double *nazir*. Under this reading, if someone were to drink wine, he would incur two sets of lashes. The Ran does not favor this reading, but he recognizes it as a legitimate interpretation of the *Gemara*. More significantly, under this reading Rav Huna's insistence that the two acceptances differ makes excellent sense, because if they are absolutely identical, the principle of *ein issur chal al issur* — one prohibition cannot take effect upon another — would prevent the second from having any force. If, however, they differ slightly, the second qualifies as an *issur mosif* or *issur kolel*, and that distinction permits one to take effect upon the other within the *Gemara*'s framework for *ein issur chal al issur*. Crucially, this second reading would not confine the phenomenon to *nezirut* at all. The *mishnah*'s use of *nezirut* would be merely illustrative, and in principle one could say *konam kikar zeh alai konam kikar zeh alai* and create two stacked prohibitions on that loaf of bread, with the person incurring two sets of lashes for eating it.

These are genuinely two very different frameworks for understanding the *sugya*.

### Why Verse Needed

The *mishnah* says *yesh neder betoch neder* but goes directly to the case of *nezirut* without explaining what makes *nezirut* distinctive. Once we operate within the hovering framework — that the second *nezirut* waits and takes effect when the first ends — we understand the uniqueness of *nezirut*. But the question arises: why does a *pasuk* need to teach us this? The *Gemara* asks *heichi dami* — precisely which case requires the scriptural derivation?

### Qal VaChomer

To appreciate the need for a *pasuk*, the *Gemara* first reconstructs the *qal vachomer* one might have made in the opposite direction. The *Gemara* states: *ma shvua chamura, she'ein shvua chala al shvua* — a *shvua* is weightier, yet a *shvua* cannot take effect upon a *shvua*. If you take an oath not to eat a loaf of bread and then take a second oath to the same effect, the second one does not take effect, because you are already *mushba v'omed* — already obligated — and you cannot obligate yourself anew in the same matter. Therefore, reasons the *qal vachomer*, *nezirut kala lo kol sheken* — how much more so should the lighter category of *nezirut* be unable to double up?

The *Gemara* then states *talmud lomar nazir l'hazir* — the doubled language in the verse teaches that *nezirut chala al nezirut*, that *nezirut* does indeed stack. So the *pasuk* overrides what the *qal vachomer* would have predicted.

Now, *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case requiring that override? If it is Rav Huna's case — *hareini nazir hayom hareini nazir lemachar* — why would you even need a *pasuk*? Since there is a day of non-overlap, it is perfectly obvious that the second should take effect; no *pasuk* is needed. The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ela lav d'amar hareini nazir hayom hareini nazir hayom* — it must be the case where someone said the identical thing twice, where the second acceptance completely coincides with the first. And the *Gemara* notes that since the *mishnah* teaches *nezirut chala al nezirut* — that even this works — it implies we are dealing with the non-obvious case. This seems to be a strong proof against Rav Huna.

The *Gemara* deflects: *hacha b'mai askinan k'gon shekibel alav shtei neziruyot b'vat achat* — here we are dealing with a case where the person accepted two *neziruyot* at once. That too is not obvious: since both are accepted for the same period beginning today, the second has no time in which it alone is operative. It is not obvious that the second one should be meaningful. Yet the *pasuk* teaches that it is — that the second hovers and will take effect when the first concludes. Crucially, Rav Huna agrees to this case. His objection is specifically to the sequential case where one is already in force when the attempt is made to add the second. When they are created together as a package, even he accepts the hovering phenomenon. And it is precisely that non-obvious case — *bat achat* — that requires a *pasuk*.

### Weight of Shvua

The *Gemara* then asks: *umai chumra d'shvua* — in what sense is a *shvua* said to be weightier, such that the *qal vachomer* runs from *nezirut* (the *kal*) to *shvua* (the *chamur*)? The *Gemara* first raises a possible answer and immediately complicates it: perhaps *shvua* is weightier because it takes effect even on matters that are non-physical — it obligates you to perform an action rather than conferring status on an object. But then *neder nami chamir* — a *neder* has its own weightiness: *sheken chal al mitzva k'reshut* — it can take effect on *mitzvah* objects and prevent you from performing a *mitzvah*. For instance, one can forbid all *sukkot* or all *tefillin* upon oneself, which a *shvua* cannot do, because a *shvua* that directly contradicts a Torah obligation is invalid, whereas a *neder* accomplishes the same end-result through a different mechanism.

Since each has an advantage over the other, the *Gemara* cannot identify a clean technical superiority. Instead, it concludes: *ela mishum d'chtiv ba bishvua lo yenakeh* — the reason *shvua* is considered weightier is that the Torah writes regarding it *ki lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa shemo lashav* — God will not cleanse one who takes His name in vain. This is not a technical distinction about the formal scope of each instrument; it is a statement about weight and gravity. A *shvua* invokes the divine name, and its violation carries a dimension of offense against God that a *neder* does not.

### Tosafot on Lo Yenakeh

There is a point worth flagging here, which Tosafot addresses. The *pasuk* of *lo yenakeh* is written in the context of *shvuat sheker* — a false oath about the past, where one invokes God's name to testify to the truth of a statement that is in fact false. That is the paradigmatic desecration of God's name: calling on God to bear witness to a lie. The *mishnah*'s case, however, involves *shvuat bituy* — an oath about future behavior, which creates an obligation. These are conceptually distinct: *shvuat bituy* is not primarily about calling God to witness a falsehood; it creates a positive obligation on the person. When you fail to fulfill it, retroactively you have backed your word with God's name and then not delivered on it — which resembles *shvuat sheker*, but the conceptual emphasis is on the obligation created rather than the invocation of the divine name.

Tosafot acknowledges that the *pasuk* of *lo yenakeh* is technically written in the context of *shvuat sheker*, and the *mishnah* deals with *shvuat bituy*, but concludes: *mikol makom chumra chashivuta shava hi* — nevertheless, as a category, *shvua* as a whole is weightier than *neder*, and the *Gemara* simply uses the most accessible expression of that weightiness. There are other respects in which *shvua* is more serious, which the *Gemara* in Shvuot elaborates; here the *Gemara* takes the straightforward one.

### Logic of Qal VaChomer

This is a good moment to clarify the logical structure at play. A *qal vachomer* runs from the *kal* to the *chamur*: if something is true for the lighter category, it certainly holds for the weightier one. Here, you might have wanted to argue: since *neder betoch neder* works by *nezirut*, which is *kal*, it should certainly work by *shvua*, which is *chamur*. But we know it does not work by *shvua*. The *Gemara*'s move is the logical contrapositive: if it does not hold for the *chamur*, it certainly should not hold for the *kal*. If *shvua* — which is *chamur* — does not permit *ein shvua chala al shvua* to be overridden, then *nezirut* — which is *kal* — certainly should not permit it. This is the structure of *if P then Q; therefore if not-Q then not-P*. You would have expected that just as *shvua* cannot double up, neither should *nezirut* be able to — and the *pasuk* comes to teach otherwise.

## Issur Cheftza vs. Gavra

### Order of Neder/Shvua

Before moving further in the *Gemara*, there is an important and conceptually rich discussion that the Ran develops on the basis of the *Yerushalmi*, concerning the interaction between *neder* and *shvua* across different categories — specifically, what happens when someone makes a *neder* following a *shvua*, or a *shvua* following a *neder*, on the same object.

The question: *shvua shelo ochal kikar ze* — I take an oath I will not eat this loaf — followed by *kikar ze konam alai* — I now make this loaf a *konam*, forbidden. Or conversely: first *konam*, then *shvua*. Does the second instrument take effect on top of the first?

### Cheftza Implies Gavra

The Ran's answer hinges on a fundamental conceptual distinction: *neder* operates as an *issur cheftza* — a prohibition that attaches to the object itself — while *shvua* operates as an *issur gavra* — a prohibition that attaches to the person. The Ran's core principle is that *issur cheftza* implies *issur gavra*, but not the reverse.

### Sukkah Example

The Ran illustrates with a paradigmatic case. If I have a *mitzvah* to sit in a *sukkah* — which is an *issur gavra*-style obligation upon me as a person — I can nonetheless subsequently make the *sukkah* forbidden to myself by declaring it a *konam*. The personal obligation and the object's status operate on different planes and do not directly contradict one another. Practically, the result is that I will be unable to honor both simultaneously, but the act of conferring a status on the object is not in direct conflict with the personal obligation. They are not in conversation with one another.

### Asymmetry of Order

From this emerges an asymmetry that is, at first glance, counterintuitive. If someone first says *shvua shelo ochal kikar ze* — I take an oath I will not eat this loaf — and then says *konam kikar ze alai*, the *neder* takes effect. Why? Because the *shvua* says nothing about the status of the loaf. The loaf is entirely neutral; the *shvua* is purely a statement about what I will do. Since the loaf retains neutral status, I can subsequently confer a prohibited status upon it through a *neder*. Similarly, if the *shvua* is in the reinforcing direction — I swear I will eat the loaf, and then I make it a *konam* — the *neder* still takes effect, even though it now creates a direct conflict between the *neder* and the *shvua*. The point is structural: the *shvua* made no claim about the object's nature, so the *neder* finds the object in a neutral state and can act upon it.

Reverse the order, however, and the result changes entirely. If someone first declares *konam kikar ze* — making the loaf forbidden — and then attempts to take a *shvua* on that loaf, whether to eat it or not to eat it, the *shvua* does not take effect. This is the surprising direction.

### Status Speaks to Person

Why? Because when the loaf is declared forbidden through a *neder*, that status is not merely a claim about the object in isolation. The Ran argues: what does it mean for an object to be forbidden if there are no people in the world to be affected by it? The forbidden status of the object is inherently a statement *to* the person — it says, you may not eat this. Once the *neder* has conferred a forbidden status on the loaf, that status has already implicated and addressed the person. It has already generated, as a direct consequence, an *issur gavra* — a personal prohibition upon the individual. Therefore, when the person subsequently attempts to make a *shvua* — a personal statement about what he will or will not do — the *shvua* finds that the personal dimension has already been spoken to. It is *lo chal*: the *shvua* cannot take effect, because there is no open space for it. The *issur gavra* generated by the *neder* preempts it.

This is the Ran's formulation: *issur cheftza* of *nedarim* generates *issur gavra* as well — *issur gavra nami ika bei* — because once the loaf is forbidden, the prohibition of *lo yachel devaro* applies, which is a personal obligation not to violate the *neder*. So the *neder* both establishes a status on the object and, as a consequence, speaks to the person. Whereas a *shvua* speaks only to the person and leaves the object's status entirely untouched. Hence the asymmetry: *neder* before *shvua* forecloses the *shvua*; *shvua* before *neder* does not foreclose the *neder*.

### Torah Lav Laws

This leads to a broader and genuinely important question about *issurim* in the Torah. When one first encounters the categories of *issur gavra* and *issur cheftza*, the natural case to consider is something like *chazir*. Our intuitive religious sense is that there is something intrinsically problematic about pork — that it is not merely neutral matter with a personal directive not to eat it, but that the thing itself has a problematic status. Yet the Torah's language often says *lo tochal* — you shall not eat — which is directed at the person and sounds like an *issur gavra*.

There are indeed those who argue that every prohibition in the Torah is technically an *issur gavra*, since the Torah always speaks to what you are not permitted to do, not about some inherent ontological status of the object. But the Ran's position, and the more nuanced view, is that these two categories are not mutually exclusive. A prohibition can start with an *issur cheftza* — the object has a problematic status — and then translate into an *issur gavra*: *and therefore* you may not eat it. Any *issur cheftza* carries with it an *issur gavra*. The converse, however, is not true: an *issur gavra* does not necessarily imply any *issur cheftza*.

The practical implication the Ran draws: since every *issur cheftza* entails an *issur gavra*, once you have started with a *neder* — which is an *issur cheftza* — you have, by the same act, already generated an *issur gavra*. There is therefore no room for a *shvua* to add anything on the personal level. But if you start with a *shvua* — which is purely *issur gavra* — the object remains wholly untouched, and a *neder* can still act upon it.

Furthermore, the Ran explains, every *lav* in the Torah is an *issur gavra* — that is, it is addressed to the person and tells them what not to do — but this does not mean it is *only* an *issur gavra*. Some *lavim* begin with the recognition that the object itself has a problematic character, and the prohibition on the person follows from that. The Ran's statement that a *lav* functions *kechol lo sa'aseh sheba-torah* — like any negative commandment in the Torah — does not collapse the distinction; it means the Torah's prohibitions always land, at minimum, on the person, but they may simultaneously reflect a status of the object.

### Ran's Text Analysis

Let us read the relevant passage in the Ran carefully. The Ran frames the question this way: one might have thought that just as an *issur cheftza* of *nedarim* can take effect upon an *issur gavra* of *shvuot* — since they are operating on different planes — so too should an *issur gavra* of *shvuot* be able to take effect upon an *issur cheftza* of *nedarim*. After all, if the *sukkah* argument works in one direction, why not in the other?

The Ran's answer: *mishum de-vinedarim ke-heichi de-ishei issur cheftza, issur gavra nami ika bei* — because in *nedarim*, once the *issur cheftza* exists, the *issur gavra* is already embedded in it. The moment the loaf is forbidden, the halacha of *lo yachel devaro* — you shall not violate your word — kicks in and is already speaking to the person. *Ve-hai lav issur gavra hu kechol lo sa'aseh sheba-torah* — this is a personal prohibition just like any negative commandment in the Torah. Thus the *neder*, as an *issur cheftza*, carries its *issur gavra* along with it. *Hilkach ein makom lishevuah shetachol al nedarim* — therefore there is no room for a *shvua* to take effect once a *neder* is already in place.

The Ran then cites the *Yerushalmi* in the first chapter of our tractate, which states: *bekikar echad mikeivan shehizkir alav shevuah keneveilah hu* — once someone declares a *neder* on a loaf, it is like a *neveilah*. From that point forward, if one attempts to make a *shvua* on it, it is as if one is trying to make a *shvua* take effect on top of an existing *issur* — and *ein shvuah chala al issurin*. Then *Rav Yudan* in the *Yerushalmi* states: this is specifically when the person first mentioned the *neder* and then the *shvua* — the *shvua* does not take effect. But if the *shvua* came first and the *neder* second, the *neder* does take effect, since the *shvua* left the object's status entirely open.

### Hekdesh Example

A question arises: what about *hekdesh*? When one is *makdish* something — consecrates it — it is simultaneously an *issur cheftza* and generates an *issur gavra* for any individual who might benefit from it. Is this not the same principle?

Precisely — the Ran's point applies equally to *hekdesh*. Every *issur cheftza* translates into an *issur gavra*. What is distinctive about *hekdesh*, one might note, is that the *issur cheftza* is capable of generating an *issur gavra* not just for the one who sanctified it but for any person who might interact with it. But this is not categorically different from the Ran's point; it simply illustrates the broader reach of an *issur cheftza*. A *neder* can similarly forbid a loaf to the entire world if the person who makes the *neder* owns that loaf. The principle that *issur cheftza* implies *issur gavra* holds across these cases. Indeed, the very concept of *kedushah* carries this inherently: the object's sanctified status is not merely an internal metaphysical fact about the object but speaks directly to what any person may or may not do with it.

### Meat and Milk

The conceptual framework of *issur gavra* versus *issur cheftza* opens onto a rich set of questions about Torah prohibitions generally. Take the prohibition of *basar b'chalav* — meat and milk. One approach holds that the primary prohibition of the Torah is simply not to eat them together; everything else — the prohibition on cooking them, on deriving benefit — is secondary and constructed to protect the eating prohibition. Under this view, the orientation is fundamentally personal and action-centered: there is nothing problematic about the combination per se, only about what you do with it. This is closer to a pure *issur gavra* orientation.

The opposing approach holds that the very phenomenon of meat and milk cooked together is intrinsically problematic — something akin to *kilayim* or *shatnez*, a fundamental disruption of natural categories. Under this view, the prohibition is not merely about protecting me from eating; it reflects a status of the *combined entity* itself, which then generates a series of downstream prohibitions: against cooking, against benefiting, against eating. This is the *issur cheftza* orientation.

The difference has concrete implications. The scope of the prohibition on *hana'ah* — benefit — is more naturally explained if there is something fundamentally objectionable about the object's status, which demands complete hands-off treatment, rather than merely a personal directive not to perform a specific action. The *issur achilah* alone suggests a narrower focus: it is about what you may or may not do, not about the thing being categorically tainted. These distinct orientations can be mapped onto the *issur gavra* / *issur cheftza* distinction and carry consequences for how we analyze the *halachot* of *basar b'chalav* throughout the tractate of Chullin.

For the purposes of *Nedarim*, the operative question is not whether a Torah prohibition is *issur gavra* or *issur cheftza* in the abstract, but whether the object in question is a *davar hanadur* — something whose forbidden status derives from speech-act dedication — or not. That is the criterion for whether a *neder* can use it as a reference point.

## Retroactive Effect

### Rava's Retroactivity

The *Gemara* now transitions to a related question. The *mishnah* establishes that when someone makes a *shvua* and then makes a second *shvua* on the same matter — *shvua shelo ochal v'achal eino chayav ela achat* — he violates only once. The second *shvua* does not take effect because one is already *mushba* and *omed*. Rava, however, asserts: *nish'al al rishona, shniya chala alav* — if the person petitions a *chacham* and has the first *shvua* dissolved through *heter*, the second *shvua* takes retroactive effect. Even though the second *shvua* was meaningless at the moment of utterance — since the first was already operative — once the first is removed, the second comes into force.

This is a striking principle. One might have argued that a statement which is entirely without force at the moment it is uttered is simply null — a *davar she'ein bo mamash*, a nothing. How can it hover and then retroactively take effect?

### Revach Concept

The *Gemara*'s language is illuminating. It does not say the second *shvua* is null; it says *revacha hu de-leit lei* — it has no *revach*, no space, no room in which to operate. This language is remarkable because it treats the *shvua* as something concrete — an obligation-object that requires physical space in which to exist. The first *shvua* occupies that space entirely, leaving no room for the second. But the second is nonetheless present, hovering, awaiting an opening. When the first is dissolved, the space opens, and the second takes effect. The obligations and statuses created by speech are being conceptualized with something close to physicality — they occupy space, they compete for room, they hover.

### Two Neziruyot

The *Gemara* attempts to derive Rava's principle from the case of two *neziruyot*. If someone accepted two *neziruyot* — *mi she-nadar shtei nezirot u-manat et ha-rishona ve-hifrish korban* — counted the first *nezirut*, separated a *korban*, and then obtained a *heter* on the first *nezirut*, *ala lo shniya bi-rishona* — the second *nezirut* is considered to have been operative from the start, and the days already counted apply retroactively to the second. The second *nezirut* hovered and, upon dissolution of the first, retroactively took its place. This seems to be strong support for Rava's principle.

### Bat Achat Status

The *Gemara* deflects the proof: *hacha b'mai askinan k'gon shekibel alav shtei neziruyot b'vat achat* — perhaps the case of two *neziruyot* refers specifically to the scenario where both were accepted simultaneously in a single utterance. In that case, we have already accepted that the simultaneous acceptance creates the hovering phenomenon: one takes effect, the other hovers, and if the first is dissolved, the second retroactively fills its place. This is structurally different from the sequential case, where one *shvua* was already in force before the second was uttered. In the *bat achat* scenario, because they were created together as a single package, the hovering status is constitutive and built-in. The *Gemara* is suggesting that perhaps Rava's extension to the sequential *shvua* case is not proven from the *nezirut* example.

### Sequential vs. Simultane…

And yet Rava's assertion stands: even in the sequential case, where the second *shvua* was uttered after the first was already operative, the second one nonetheless hovers — *revacha hu de-leit lei* — and will take effect if the first is dissolved. The *lishna achrina* of the *Gemara* reinforces this: *chiyuva hu de-leka, ha-shvuah ika* — the obligation to bring a *korban* or the liability for violation may not exist, but the *shvua* itself is still present. The practical implication — *le-mai hilchata* — is precisely *de-Rava*: if you dissolve the first *shvua*, *chala lo shniya tachteiha*, the second takes its place. Even by *shvua*, where we say *ein shvua chala al shvua*, the focus remains on the obligation-creating dimension of the *shvua*, and even there the second one hovers with just no room to operate, until the first is removed.

## Issur Cheftza vs. Gavr…

### Power of Words

It is worth pausing over what *Masechet Nedarim* reveals about the nature of speech. The *Gemara*'s language — that the second *shvua* has no *revach*, no space — treats obligations and statuses created by words as if they were concrete, quasi-physical entities that occupy space, hover, and compete. This is not incidental to the *sugya*; it is, in a sense, its deepest teaching. When one says *konam* or takes a *shvua*, one is not merely expressing an intention. One is creating a status — of an object, of oneself — that now has an independent existence in the world of *halacha*. It competes with other statuses, it occupies conceptual space, it can hover waiting for room to take effect. The tractate of *Nedarim* is, among other things, a meditation on the creative power of speech: how words construct religious realities, generate obligations, transform the status of objects, and build what might be called a personal religious world alongside the pre-existing structure of Torah obligations. Much as a *bamah* was a personal, do-it-yourself site of worship — the *Gemara* elsewhere compares *nedarim* to this — so one who makes *nedarim* is in a sense constructing his own overlay of sanctity and prohibition on a neutral world. The statuses thus created are real, weighty, and interact with one another in all the ways we have been tracing.

## Stam Nedarim Lehachmir

### Opening Principle

The *mishnah* now presents a principle that addresses a question that has been hovering throughout the tractate: *stam nedarim lehachmir u-peirusham lehakel* — unspecified *nedarim* are given the stricter interpretation, while an articulated clarification is given the more lenient one. This principle speaks directly to the recurring question of why we do not simply ask the person what he meant. The answer embedded in the *mishnah* is that we do ask — and when he tells us, we follow the lenient interpretation. But when he does not tell us, or cannot tell us, we default to strict.

### Sacrificial Examples

*Keitzad* — what are the examples? The *mishnah* says: *harei alai k'basar maliach k'yayin nesech* — let this be forbidden to me like salted meat or like wine of libation. Two possible meanings present themselves. Salted meat is associated with sacrificial meat, since *kol korban chovatcha takriv melach* — all your obligatory offerings require salt. *Yayin nesech* — wine of libation — can refer either to *nesachim*, the wine poured on the altar as part of the Temple service, or to wine poured as a libation to *avodah zarah*. These are radically different referents with opposite halachic consequences.

*Im b'shel shelamim nadar* — if he meant like a sacrifice to God, *assur*: it is forbidden, because this is a *davar hanadur*, an object made forbidden through human speech-dedication. *Im b'shel avodat kochavim nadar* — if he meant like idolatrous libation or sacrifice, *mutar*: it is permissible. Why? Although food offered to *avodah zarah* is prohibited, it is not a *davar hanadur*. Its forbidden status does not derive from a speech-act of dedication; it derives from another mechanism entirely. Therefore it cannot serve as a reference point for a *neder*. *V'im stam* — and if he did not specify — *assur*: we give it the stricter interpretation and treat it as if he meant the sacrificial-to-God meaning.

The *mishnah* thus teaches both components of the principle simultaneously. The strict default (*stam lehachmir*) operates when nothing else is known. The lenient interpretation (*peirusham lehakel*) operates when the person clarifies that he meant the lenient referent — and his clarification is accepted and binding.

One might ask: what if the person says he had no particular meaning in mind, that he simply heard the phrase and used it without thinking of either the *shelamim* or the *avodah zarah* referent? The Ran and Tosafot address this. If someone says he intended to create the *issur* but had no idea what exactly the words meant, we would still apply the strict default. The requirement of intent (*piv velibbo shavin*) pertains primarily to the intention to create the status — the intention to obligate — not necessarily to detailed knowledge of precisely which referent is meant by the words chosen.

### Cherem Example

*Harei alai kecherem* — let this be upon me like *cherem*. *Cherem* admits of two referents: *cherem shel shamayim* — consecrated to the *Beit HaMikdash* — and *cherem shel kohanim* — dedicated to the *kohanim* for their use. The former is forbidden to all; the latter is permissible to those who receive it. *Im kecherem shel shamayim, assur* — that is a *davar hanadur* and forbidden. *V'im kecherem shel kohanim, mutar* — that is not forbidden to people, so it cannot serve as a *neder* reference point. *V'im stam, assur* — unspecified, we give it the strict meaning.

### Ma'aser Example

*Harei alai kema'aser* — let this be upon me like *ma'aser*. Again, two referents: *ma'aser behemah* — the animal tithe — and *ma'aser goren* — the grain tithe. *Im kema'aser behemah nadar, assur*: the animal tithe is a *korban* and is forbidden to be eaten by ordinary people; it is both a *davar hanadur* and a *davar ha'assur*. *V'im shel goren, mutar*: the grain tithe (*ma'aser rishon* or *ma'aser sheni*), while it is a *davar hanadur* in the sense that it is set aside through designation, it is not a *davar ha'assur* — it is permissible for consumption. Therefore it cannot serve as a reference point for creating a prohibition. *V'im stam, assur* — without clarification, the strict meaning governs.

### Terumah Example

*Harei alai kiterumah* — let this be upon me like *terumah*. Two referents: *terumat halishkah* — the annual donation to the *Beit HaMikdash* treasury (the *machatzit hashekel*) — and *terumah goren* — the priestly tithe separated from grain. *Im kiterumat halishkah nadar, assur*: this is forbidden to all and dedicated to the *Beit HaMikdash*, a clear *davar hanadur*. *V'im shel goren, mutar*: *terumah* of the grain is not a *davar hanadur* for the purposes of *neder* — either because it is not forbidden to all (being permitted to *kohanim*) or, as the Rambam suggests, because it is not a freely undertaken vow but a Torah-imposed obligation, and thus does not qualify as a *davar hanadur*. *V'im stam, assur* — the last statement is attributed specifically to Rabbi Meir, and it is this attribution that opens the door for Rabbi Yehudah's qualification.

## Rabbi Yehudah

### Terumah Regional

Rabbi Yehudah says: the strict default for unspecified *terumah* applies only in *Yehudah*, not in the *Galil*. *Stam terumah bi'Yehudah asurah, ba'Galil muteret* — in the *Galil* it is permitted even without clarification. The reason: *she'ein anshei Galil makirim et terumat halishkah* — the people of the *Galil* are not familiar with *terumat halishkah*. The *Beit HaMikdash* is not in their environment; the concept of *terumat halishkah* as a donation to the Temple treasury is not part of their daily consciousness.

### Default Modifier

This raises the deeper principle of what counts as a *stam* — a default meaning — and what requires a modifier. A word's default meaning is determined by its sociological and linguistic reality in a given context. In the *Galil*, when someone says *terumah*, they mean *terumah goren* — that is what the word means to them. If they want to say *terumat halishkah*, they will use the full phrase; the modifier is necessary. *Terumat halishkah* is not a *stam* meaning in the *Galil* because it is not part of the ordinary linguistic repertoire.

The phenomenon of what becomes the default and what requires modification is always a function of usage and familiarity. The default is what the unmodified word naturally evokes in its context. When digital watches were introduced, *digital watch* required the modifier — *watch* was *stam* the analog kind. Eventually, if enough shifts occur, the defaults can reverse, and the formerly default meaning needs a modifier. *Stam nedarim lehachmir* applies when the stricter meaning is a genuine, equally likely candidate for what the word means — when it could plausibly be what someone saying that word intends. Where one meaning has so dominated the word's use that the other meaning requires a modifier to convey, *stam* simply means the default meaning, and if the default is lenient, the result is *lehakel*.

### Cherem Regional

Rabbi Yehudah then applies the same logic in the opposite direction for *cherem*: *stam charamim bi'Yehudah mutarin, baGalil asurin*. In *Yehudah*, unspecified *cherem* is permissible; in the *Galil*, it is forbidden. Why? *She'ein anshei Galil makirin et charmei kohanim* — the people of the *Galil* are not familiar with the concept of *cherem* given to *kohanim*. In the *Galil*, the word *cherem* evokes only *cherem* consecrated to the *Beit HaMikdash*, which is the strict, forbidden category. *Cherem* given to *kohanim* would need a modifier there. Therefore *stam cherem* in the *Galil* is the strict meaning and the result is *assur*.

In *Yehudah*, by contrast, proximity to the *Beit HaMikdash* and familiarity with the *kohanim* means the word *cherem* commonly evokes *charmei kohanim* — the lenient category. Therefore *stam cherem* in *Yehudah* is treated as *mutar*.

### Logical Tension

The *Gemara* will press what seems to be a logical inconsistency in Rabbi Yehudah's ruling on *cherem* in *Yehudah*. Even granting that *cherem* in *Yehudah* commonly evokes *charmei kohanim*, the word can also mean *cherem haMikdash* in *Yehudah* — the Temple *cherem* is not unknown there. If both meanings are available in *Yehudah*, why does *stam lehachmir* not apply, as it does when there are two possible interpretations, one strict and one lenient? The principle of *stam nedarim lehachmir* would seem to demand that we take the strict meaning whenever both meanings are live options. Why does the existence of the lenient meaning (*charmei kohanim*) tip the entire default in *Yehudah* toward *mutar*, rather than triggering *lehachmir* precisely because both are possible?

The answer the *Gemara* will develop is that *stam lehachmir* presupposes that both meanings are roughly equally available and plausible. Where one meaning so dominates the sociological and linguistic reality that the other, while technically possible, is not the word's natural default meaning, we follow the actual default — whether strict or lenient. In *Yehudah*, *cherem* is simply what people mean when they say *charmei kohanim*; the *mikdash* meaning requires the fuller phrase. That dominance controls the *stam*, and no *lehachmir* override applies where the default itself is the lenient reading. This is not merely a logical rule but a sociological and linguistic reality that the *halacha* incorporates: what a word means — in its actual usage, in its actual community — determines what *stam* means.
