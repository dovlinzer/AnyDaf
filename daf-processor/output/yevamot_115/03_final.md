# Yevamot 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chaotic Circumstances

### Peace vs. War

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלְכָה הִיא וּבַעְלָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם. שָׁלוֹם בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, וְשָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם. וּבָאָה וְאָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — תִּנָּשֵׂא. ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — תִּתְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman who went, she and her husband, overseas,** if there was **peace between him and her,** i.e., the couple were not fighting at the time, **and** there was also **peace in the world,** i.e., there was no war at that time, **and** the woman **came** back by herself **and said: My husband died, she may marry** on the basis of her own testimony. Likewise, if she said: **My husband died,** and they did not have children, but her husband had a brother, **she may enter into levirate marriage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלוֹם בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, וּמִלְחָמָה בָּעוֹלָם. קְטָטָה בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, וְשָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם. וּבָאתָה וְאָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי״ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If there was **peace between him and her** when they left **but** there was **war in the world,** or if there was **a quarrel between him and her and peace in the world, and she came and said: My husband died, she is not deemed credible,** as she may be mistaken or lying.

The *mishnah* establishes that a woman who reports her husband's death is believed, provided there is no known conflict between them and the world is at peace. The condition of domestic harmony will be addressed elsewhere; the condition of peace in the world is what the *Gemara* takes up first. When the world is in a state of war, a woman might report that her husband is dead based on her interpretation of events — *k'didami*, as things appeared to her — rather than on concrete, verified knowledge. She may see her husband struck down and assume he died when he has not necessarily done so. The *Gemara* therefore qualifies the *mishnah*'s grant of credibility: in a chaotic or dangerous situation, her testimony is suspect not because she is lying, but because she may be drawing incorrect conclusions in good faith.

### Famine vs. War

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמִלְחָמָה — מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרָה בִּדְדָמֵי: סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּא בְּכֹל הָנֵי דְּאִיקְּטוּל, הוּא פָּלֵיט? אִם תִּימְצָא לוֹמַר, כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁלוֹם בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ נָטְרָא עַד דְּחָזְיָא, זִימְנִין דְּמָחוּ לֵיהּ בְּגִירָא אוֹ בְּרוּמְחָא וְסָבְרָא וַדַּאי מֵת, וְאִיכָּא דְּעָבֵד סַמְתָּרִי וְחָיֵה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason** that one does not rely on her testimony **when** there is **war?** It is **because she says** what **she imagines** to be the case: Can it **enter one's mind** that **among all these** people **who were killed,** her husband alone **is saved? If you say: Since there was peace between him and her, she guards herself** and waits **until she** actually **sees** that he died; even so, **at times** it might happen **that** his enemies **strike him with an arrow or spear and she thinks** that **he is certainly dead** due to the wound, **and** yet this is no proof that he is dead, as **there are** instances **when** someone **prepares medicine [*samterei*]** for the wounded person **and he survives,** despite his apparently fatal wound.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר רָבָא לְמֵימַר רְעָבוֹן אֵינוֹ כְּמִלְחָמָה, דְּלָא אָמְרָה בִּדְדָמֵי. הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: רְעָבוֹן הֲרֵי הוּא כְּמִלְחָמָה. דְּהָהִיא דַּאֲתָת לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״בַּעְלִי מֵת בָּרָעָב״. אֲמַר לַהּ: שַׁפִּיר עֲבַדְתְּ דְּשֵׁיזִבְתְּ נַפְשִׁיךְ? סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ דִּבְהָהוּא פּוּרְתָּא דִּנְפָפִיתָא דִּשְׁבַקְתְּ לֵיהּ הֲוָה חָיֵי? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: מָר נָמֵי יָדַע דְּכִי הַאי גַוְונָא לָא חָיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava thought to say** that **famine is not like war, as** in the case of a famine **she will not say** and infer based on what **she imagines** to be the case. **Rava then** retracted and **said: Famine is like war.** Why did he change his mind? This happened **because a certain woman came before Rava,** and **said to him: My husband died in a famine.** Seeking to cross-examine her, Rava **said to her:** Did **you do well to save yourself,** by running away and leaving him? Did it **enter your mind that with that small amount of sifted flour that you left him he could have survived? She said to him: The Master also knows that in a case like this he could not survive.** Rava understood from her comment that she did not actually see her husband die, but merely saw that he was weak from hunger, and yet she testified with certainty that he died.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: רְעָבוֹן גְּרִיעָה מִמִּלְחָמָה. דְּאִילּוּ מִלְחָמָה, כִּי אָמְרָה: ״מֵת בַּעְלִי בַּמִּלְחָמָה״ הוּא דְּלָא מְהֵימְנָא, הָא ״מֵת עַל מִטָּתוֹ״ — מְהֵימְנָא. וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי רְעָבוֹן, עַד דְּאָמְרָה: ״מֵת וּקְבַרְתִּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava then** retracted again and **said: Famine is worse than war** in this respect. **As** in **wartime,** it is only **if she said: My husband died in the war, that she is not deemed credible.** This indicates that if in a time of war she says: **He died upon his bed,** or in some other unrelated manner, **she is deemed credible,** as she would not err in this case, **whereas with regard to a famine she** is deemed credible only **if she says: He died and I buried him.** In other words, during a famine it must be clear that she is testifying about his actual death, and is not basing her claim on an assumption.

Rava introduces a distinction that is not at all obvious from the *mishnah* itself. In a case of war, he says, she is only disbelieved if she reports that her husband died *in the war*. But if she says that during a time of war he died in his bed — a natural death — she would be believed. The existence of warfare in the world is irrelevant to a claim of natural death; there is no reason to suspect confusion there.

Rava then adds a striking observation: *ra'avon gariya mimilchama* — famine is in some ways worse than war. In a case of famine, even a report that the husband died naturally is suspect, because people may faint from starvation and appear dead when they have not actually died. The precise nature of the confusion during a *ra'avon* is not fully spelled out, but Rava's point is that the circumstances create a context in which what looks like natural death may not be death at all. Thus, the woman would not be believed even in a situation that might otherwise seem less fraught than active combat.

### Related Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַפּוֹלֶת — הֲרֵי הוּא כְּמִלְחָמָה, דְּאָמְרָה בִּדְדָמֵי. שִׁילּוּחַ נְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּמִלְחָמָה, דְּאָמְרָה בִּדְדָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **a rockslide is like war, as she will say** what **she imagines** to be the case, and she might not meticulously examine the facts to see if he was possibly saved. Furthermore, **an outbreak of snakes and scorpions is like war, as she will say** what **she imagines** to be the case.

The *Gemara* extends this principle to additional chaotic circumstances. A case of *mapolet* — a building collapse in which the couple was trapped — is treated like war: she will report based on perception rather than certain knowledge, and is therefore not believed. Similarly, a situation of *shiluach nechanishim ve'akravim* — an outbreak of dangerous snakes and scorpions — is likewise classified as equivalent to war, with the same concern that her report is based on how things appeared rather than on definitive fact.

### Pestilence Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דֶּבֶר — אָמְרִי לַהּ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּמִלְחָמָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אֵינוֹ כְּמִלְחָמָה. אָמְרִי לַהּ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּמִלְחָמָה, דְּאָמְרִי בִּדְדָמֵי. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אֵינוֹ כְּמִלְחָמָה, דְּסָמְכִי אַדְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: שַׁב שְׁנִין הָוֵה מוּתָנָא, וְאִינָשׁ בְּלָא שְׁנֵי לָא אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** In a case of **pestilence** or a similar plague, **some say it is like war, and some say it is not like war.** The Gemara explains: **Some say it is like war, as she will say** what **she imagines** to be the case, because she assumes that if most everyone died in the plague her husband could not have survived. Conversely, **some say it is not like war, because we rely on that which people say** in the common expression: For **seven years there was pestilence and not a person left,** i.e., died, **before his time.** In other words, with regard to natural disasters of this kind it is known sometimes that one can avoid harm, and therefore if a woman testifies that her husband died she certainly witnessed his death.

The case of *dever* — pestilence — generates a dispute. One opinion holds that it is like war: since people are dying all around, she may assume her husband has died when he has not. A second opinion holds that pestilence is *not* like war, because *samchi ada'ata* — she places her confidence in a well-known folk saying: *shev shanin havah moseina ve'ish belo shneih lo azil* — pestilence may last seven years, yet a person does not die before his time. Because of this widely held belief, she will not draw premature conclusions even in the midst of a plague; she knows better than to assume death simply because the disease is present. In the other categories — war, famine, building collapse, dangerous animals — no such folk wisdom insulates her from making assumptions, so the concern remains.

## Migo & War Report

### War as Migo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֶחְזִיקָה הִיא מִלְחָמָה בָּעוֹלָם, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן מָה לָהּ לְשַׁקֵּר, דְּאִי בָּעֲיָא אָמְרָה: ״שָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם״. אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיחַזְקָה — אָמְרָה בִּדְדָמֵי, וְלָא אָתֵי ״מָה לִי לְשַׁקֵּר״ וּמַרַע חֶזְקָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the scholars: If **she maintains** that there is **a war in the world,** i.e., if the court was not aware of a war in that place, but the wife comes and claims that there was a war, and she went on to say that her husband died in this war, **what is** the *halakha* in this case? **Do we say: Why should she lie?** In other words, if she was lying she would have issued a more advantageous claim. Since she herself informed the court that there was a war, which undermines her claim that her husband died, there is no legitimate reason to suspect her of lying. **Because if she wanted** to lie **she** would have **said:** There was **peace in the world,** and the court would have accepted her testimony. **Or perhaps** it can be argued: **Since she has maintained** that there was a war and this claim of hers has already been accepted, with regard to her report concerning her husband, **she will say** what **she imagines** to be the case, **and** the argument of: **Why would I lie, does not come and undermine the** established **presumption** that there was a war.

The *Gemara* raises a further question at the end of 114b: suppose the woman is the one who first informs us that a war broke out — we had no independent knowledge of it. She comes from a distant country and says: war broke out there, and my husband died in it. Should she be believed on the grounds of *migo*? After all, she did not have to report the existence of war. She could have claimed her husband died during peacetime; had she wished to deceive, that simpler story was available to her.

### Migo Limitations

The *Gemara* at the top of 115a frames the question precisely. On one hand: *mah lo leshaker* — she had no reason to lie; *i ba'ei amar shalom ba'olam* — she could have said there was peace in the world. On the other hand: *keivan de'ichzaka, amra bidedamei* — now that we have established that there was a war, we are still concerned that she is reporting based on perception, drawing conclusions that may not reflect what actually happened. The *migo* principle can only establish that she is not *intentionally* lying. It cannot establish that her conclusions are accurate. Since the entire concern is not that she is fabricating a story but that she is sincerely misreading the situation, the *migo* does nothing to resolve that problem.

### Migo Analysis

Tosafot sharpens this point by noting that in fact the woman *always* has a *migo* available to her. Even in the standard case where we know there is a war, she could have said her husband died naturally — on his bed — rather than in combat. And yet we do not believe her in such a case either. This demonstrates conclusively that the *migo* provides no advantage here. Whether or not she could have told a different, less suspicious story, the underlying concern — *amra bidedamei*, that her report is based on impression rather than certainty — remains unaddressed by the *migo* argument. The *Gemara* ultimately concludes that even where the report of war comes entirely from her, this does not strengthen her credibility in any meaningful way.

## Smoke & Fire

### Smoke Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״עִישְּׁינוּ עָלֵינוּ בַּיִת״, ״עִישְּׁינוּ עָלֵינוּ מְעָרָה״, ״הוּא מֵת, וַאֲנִי נִצַּלְתִּי״ — אֵינָהּ נֶאֱמֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a *baraita* in an attempt to resolve this dilemma. **Come** and **hear:** It was taught that if a woman comes and says: **They set our house** on fire and the house became filled with **smoke,** or: **They set our cave** on fire to **smoke** us out, and she adds: My husband **died and I was saved, she is not deemed credible.**

A *baraita* presents the following case: a woman comes and says, *ashinu aleinu bayis* — smoke filled our house — or *ishnu aleinu me'ara* — smoke filled our cave — *hu meis va'ani nitzalti* — he died in the smoke and I escaped. She is *einan ne'emenes* — not believed. The *Gemara* notes that this is analogous to the war situation: everything we know about the chaotic circumstances comes from her own testimony, yet that does not strengthen her report, because we remain concerned that she is drawing incorrect conclusions. The circumstance being reported by her, rather than independently known, does not help.

### Miracle Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר לָהּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דִּלְדִידָךְ אִיתְרְחִישׁ נִיסָּא, לְדִידֵיהּ נָמֵי אִיתְרְחִישׁ נִיסָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this is no proof. **There,** in the case of the fire, **it is different, as one says to her: Just as a miracle occurred for you** and you were saved, **likewise a miracle** might have **occurred for** your husband and he too survived.

The *Gemara* then asks whether this case could serve as a proof that reporting the dangerous circumstances oneself does not help — but answers that it is different: *shani hasam*. The reason she is not believed in the smoke case is not merely that she reported the circumstances herself. Rather, there is a specific argument: *keheichi delididi itrachish nisa ledidei nami itrachish nisa* — just as a miracle occurred for her and she escaped the smoke-filled house, so too a miracle may have occurred for him. The very fact of her escape is concrete evidence that someone can survive such a situation. Her escape undermines any confident claim that he could not have survived as well.

## Attack Scenario

### Bandits Attack

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״נָפְלוּ עָלֵינוּ גּוֹיִם״, ״נָפְלוּ עָלֵינוּ לִיסְטִים״, ״הוּא מֵת, וְנִצַּלְתִּי״ — נֶאֱמֶנֶת. הָתָם, כִּדְרַב אִידִי. דְּאָמַר רַב אִידִי: אִשָּׁה כְּלֵי זַיְינָהּ עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from another *baraita*. If a woman comes and says: A group of **gentiles attacked us,** or: A group **of bandits attacked us,** and she adds: My husband **died and I was saved, she is deemed credible.** This indicates that her testimony is accepted due to the argument of: Why should she lie? The Gemara rejects this proof. **There,** in that *baraita*, her testimony is accepted because it stands to reason that she is speaking the truth, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Idi. As Rav Idi said:** With regard to **a woman, her weapons are upon her.** In other words, a woman is generally not killed by thieves, because the very fact of her being a woman protects her. They would most likely rape her and not kill her. Consequently, it is reasonable to assume that she is speaking the truth.

Another *baraita* presents a contrasting case: *nafalu aleinu ovdei kochavim* — non-Jews attacked us — *nafalu aleinu listim* — or bandits attacked us — *hu meis venitzalti* — he died and I was saved. Here, remarkably, she *is* believed. This is surprising. One might think that even here, running away during an attack, she could have drawn wrong conclusions about whether her husband actually died.

### Woman's Weapons

The *Gemara* explains: *hatam kerav idi* — there, the ruling follows Rav Idi. *De'amar rav idi: isha klei zaynah aleha* — a woman's weapons are upon her. This is a euphemistic expression meaning that a woman may be specifically targeted to be kept alive by attackers — bandits or enemies who wish to assault her — while a man would simply be killed. Therefore, the fact that she survived does not suggest that he survived. We have a coherent explanation for why she alone escaped: she was spared for a violent purpose she managed to evade, while he was simply killed.

### Why Believed

Rashi identifies two distinct reasons why she is believed in this case, each of which distinguishes it from the general concern about *amra bidedamei*. First, the attack was not a large-scale war or pestilence — it was an event that happened specifically to the two of them. When the situation is not broadly chaotic but rather a discrete incident affecting a small group, the circumstances are less confusing and her reporting is correspondingly more reliable. Second, even though she escaped — which ordinarily would suggest that perhaps he escaped too — the principle of *isha klei zaynah aleha* provides a concrete explanation for why her survival is consistent with his death. These two factors together make this case fundamentally different from war or famine.

## Wedding Fire

### Fire Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּבְשִׁילְהֵי הִלּוּלֵיהּ אִיתְּלַי נוּרָא בֵּי גְּנָנֵיהּ. אֲמַרָה לְהוּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ: ״חֲזוֹ גַּבְרַאי! חֲזוֹ גַּבְרַאי!״ אֲתוֹ, חֲזוֹ גַּבְרָא חָרוֹכָא דִּשְׁדֵי, וּפִסְּתָא דִּידָא דְּשַׁדְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who** got married. **At the end of his wedding a fire broke out in the bridal chamber,** where the bride and groom were standing, during the ceremony. **His wife** screamed and **said to them: Look at my husband, look at my husband! They went and saw** an unrecognizable **burnt man fallen down, and a palm** of a hand **lying** there.

The *Gemara* now presents a concrete incident. There was a man at whose wedding, as the celebration concluded, *itli nura be-ganenei* — fire caught under the wedding canopy. His wife called out to the bystanders: *chazu gavrai chazu gavrai* — look at my husband, come quickly! People came. When they arrived, they found *gava charucha deshadi* — a charred body lying there — and *pisa deyada deshadi* — a severed, charred hand lying separately nearby.

### Smoke Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין לְמֵימַר: הַיְינוּ ״עִישְּׁינוּ עָלֵינוּ בַּיִת, עִישְּׁינוּ עָלֵינוּ מְעָרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin thought to say: This is** the same as the case of: **They set our house** on fire and the house became filled with **smoke,** or: **They set our cave** on fire to **smoke** us out, i.e., we cannot rely on her claim that her husband died.

Rav Chiyya bar Avin considered ruling that this case is comparable to the smoke-filled house — *hainu ishnu aleinu bayit ishnu aleinu me'ara* — meaning that she should not be believed, since there was fire and it is not certain that the husband died.

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לָא קָאָמְרָה ״חֲזוֹ גַּבְרַאי, חֲזוֹ גַּבְרַאי״. וְעוֹד, גַּבְרָא חָרוֹכָא דִּשְׁדֵי, וּפִסְּתָא דִּידָא דְּשַׁדְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Is** this case **comparable** to those? **There she did not say: Look at my husband, look at my husband. And furthermore,** there is another difference: Here, there is **a burnt man who has fallen down and a palm that is lying** there. In other words, her statement is substantiated by facts.

Rava rejected this comparison. *Mi dami?* — how can you compare them? Two differences distinguish this case from the smoke case. First, in the smoke case she was not calling witnesses; here she is crying out *chazu gavrai* — look at my husband — summoning people to observe what is happening, which suggests she is reporting something concrete and visible rather than drawing a conclusion after the fact. Second, and more significantly, there is actual physical evidence: a charred body and a severed charred hand. This is not a case of drawing conclusions from chaos — there is something to point to. The combination of her calling witnesses and the presence of these remains constitutes a much stronger basis for belief.

### Alternative Theories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין — גַּבְרָא חָרוֹכָא דִּשְׁדֵי, אֵימָא אִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא אֲתָא לְאַצּוֹלֵי, וַאֲכַילְתֵּיהּ נוּרָא. וּפִסְּתָא דִידָא דְּשַׁדְיָא — נוּרָא אִיתְּלַיא וְאִתְיְלִיד בֵּיהּ מוּמָא, וּמֵחֲמַת כִּיסּוּפָא אֲזַל וַעֲרַק לְעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** why didn't **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin** accept her testimony? In his opinion, **a burnt man who has fallen down** is not conclusive proof, as one might still **say:** Perhaps **another person came to the rescue and the fire burned him. And** as for **the palm lying** there, perhaps **the fire burned him and caused a deformity** through which he lost his hand, **and due to his embarrassment he went and ran away to** somewhere else **in the world,** but he is still alive. Consequently, Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin did not want to rely upon the testimony of the wife.

Rav Chiyya bar Avin, however, pressed back with alternative explanations for the physical evidence. Regarding the charred body: *eima inish achrina ata le'atzolei ve-achlei nura* — perhaps it is not the husband's body at all, but rather someone else who came to rescue the husband and was consumed by the fire. And regarding the severed hand: perhaps the husband himself caught fire, *ve-italya be-muma* — and became disfigured, his hand burning and falling off — *u-machamat kisufa azal ve-arak le-alma* — and out of shame at his disfigurement he fled and is still alive somewhere.

This exchange illuminates a broader principle running through the entire *Gemara*. The concern about *amra bidedamei* is not limited to questions about the woman's state of mind. Even when we have physical evidence — a charred body, a hand — we face the independent question of what conclusions we can reasonably draw from those facts. The woman may be reporting accurately what she observed, yet the question remains: do the facts she has accurately reported actually establish that the husband is dead? Cases of *agunah* arising from uncertainty about a husband's fate often turn not on doubting the witness's sincerity but on precisely this kind of evidentiary ambiguity. What does it mean to "see" someone die when what one observed were circumstances that strongly implied death without confirming it absolutely?

## Single Witness War

### Ed Echad Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עֵד אֶחָד בַּמִּלְחָמָה מַהוּ? טַעְמָא דְּעֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן — מִשּׁוּם דְּמִילְּתָא דַּעֲבִידָא לְאִיגַּלּוֹיֵי הוּא לָא מְשַׁקַּר, הָכָא נָמֵי לָא מְשַׁקַּר. אוֹ דִלְמָא: טַעְמָא דְּעֵד אֶחָד מִשּׁוּם דְּהִיא גּוּפָא דָּיְיקָא וּמִינַּסְבָא, וְהָכָא [כֵּיוָן דְּזִימְנִין דְּסָנְיָא לֵיהּ], לָא דָּיְיקָא וּמִינַּסְבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before them:** In the case of **one witness** who testifies to the death of someone **during a war, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: **The reason that one witness is deemed credible** when he provides testimony concerning the death of a husband is **because** the husband being alive **is a matter that is likely to be revealed,** and one would **not lie** in a case of this kind. **Here, too,** one witness would **not lie. Or perhaps the reason that one witness** is trusted **is because** his account is supported by the fact that **she herself is exacting** in her investigation before she **marries** again. **And here, since sometimes** she **hates him,** and war is a situation that requires especially careful investigation and it is tempting for her to rely on the witness, **she is not exacting** in her investigation before she **marries** again, and therefore the testimony of one witness is not accepted.

The *Gemara* now poses a new question: *ed echad b'milchama mahu* — what is the law if a third-party witness — not the woman herself — reports that the husband died in war? The question is somewhat surprising. If the concern is not intentional deception but rather the drawing of incorrect conclusions in chaotic circumstances, why would a third party be any more reliable than the woman herself? Both were presumably present in the same chaotic environment and subject to the same perceptual limitations.

One answer is that a third party, being less emotionally invested and not a direct subject of the events he is narrating, will be more careful and precise. When one presents oneself explicitly as a witness rather than as a party to the events, there is a greater awareness of the implications of testimony, a greater compunction about stating things as certain that are not fully verified.

### Why Believe Him

The *Gemara* presents the argument for believing the single witness. The ordinary basis for accepting *ed echad* in cases of a husband's death is *milta de'avida le'igloyei hu* — the matter is one that will eventually become known — *lo meshaker* — and therefore he will not lie, since truth will surface in any case. The argument applies here too: *hacha nami lo meshaker* — here too he will not lie. Furthermore, the general basis for accepting a single witness's report of a husband's death is reinforced by the assumption that the woman herself will investigate before remarrying, knowing the grave consequences if the husband is still alive.

### Why Not Believe

The argument against believing him in a war situation is as follows. If the ordinary basis for trusting a single witness is that she herself — *hi gufa daika uminisba* — will investigate and verify before remarrying, then in a war situation that backstop is undermined. The *Gemara* explains: *lo daika uminisba de'amra bidedamei* — she will not investigate carefully enough, because what the witness has told her coheres with what she herself perceived. She heard the witness say her husband died in war; this fits her own impressions; and so she proceeds to remarry without the careful independent investigation that normally underwrites the trust we extend to a single witness. The risk of drawing incorrect conclusions is therefore compounded: the witness may have made assumptions, and the woman may not independently verify because the witness's account confirms her own perceptions. Rashi similarly explains that the concern about *bidedamei* applies not just to the woman's testimony but to this entire chain of inference.

## Akiva's Report

### Nehardea Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כְּשֶׁיָּרַדְתִּי לִנְהַרְדְּעָא לְעַבֵּר הַשָּׁנָה, מְצָאַנִי נְחֶמְיָה אִישׁ בֵּית דְּלִי, וְאָמַר לִי: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁאֵין מַשִּׂיאִין אֶת הָאִשָּׁה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד, אֶלָּא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא. וְנַמְתִּי לוֹ: כֵּן הַדְּבָרִים. אָמַר לִי, אֱמוֹר לָהֶם מִשְּׁמִי: אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִים הַמְּדִינָה הַזּוֹ מְשׁוּבֶּשֶׁת בִּגְיָיסוֹת? כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מֵרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן, שֶׁמַּשִּׂיאִין הָאִשָּׁה עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma. **Rabbi Akiva said: When I descended to Neharde'a** in Babylonia **to intercalate the year, I found** there the Sage **Neḥemya of Beit D'li, and he said to me: I heard that** the Sages **do not** allow **a woman to marry in Eretz Yisrael based on** the testimony of **one witness, apart from Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava,** as the other Sages are reluctant to rely on his opinion. **And I said [*namti*] to him: This is so. He said to me: Say to them in my name:** Do **you know that this country is riddled with troops? This is** the tradition that **I received from Rabban Gamliel the Elder, that** the Sages do allow **a woman to marry based on one witness.**

Rami bar Chama brings a *tanna'itic* account as a potential proof. Rabbi Akiva reports: when I went to Nehardea *le'aber shanah* — to intercalate the year — I found Nechemia, a man from the house of Beit Dali, who told me: I have heard that in the Land of Israel they do not allow a woman to remarry on the basis of one witness, except for Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava, who permits it alone. Rabbi Akiva confirmed this. Nechemia then said: go back and tell them in my name — *atem yode'im hamedina hazzo meshubeshet begayasot* — you know that this country is disrupted by army troops — and this is the tradition I have received from Rabban Gamliel the Elder: *shemashi'in et ha'isha al pi ed echad* — that a woman may be allowed to remarry on the basis of one witness.

### Troops Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְדִינָה מְשׁוּבֶּשֶׁת בִּגְיָיסוֹת? לָאו, אַף עַל גַּב דִּמְדִינָה זוֹ מְשׁוּבֶּשֶׁת, כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי שֶׁמַּשִּׂיאִין עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד. אַלְמָא עֵד אֶחָד מְהֵימַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita* in relation to the issue at hand. **What** is the significance of his comment that this **country is riddled with troops? Isn't** he saying: **Even though this country is riddled** with troops, **this is** the tradition that **I received, that** the Sages do allow **a woman to marry based on one witness,** notwithstanding the war. **Apparently, one witness is deemed credible** in the case of a missing husband during a time of war.

The question is how to understand the mention of troops. Does Nechemia mean to say: even though this country is in a state of war and troops are passing through, the tradition from Rabban Gamliel the Elder is that a single witness is believed even under such circumstances? If so, this would be a direct answer to our question — a single witness *is* accepted in a war situation. *Alma ed echad meheman* — one witness is believed.

### Rava Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, מַאי שְׁנָא ״מְדִינָה זוֹ״? ״כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ גְּיָיסוֹת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִים שֶׁמְּדִינָה זוֹ מְשׁוּבֶּשֶׁת בִּגְיָיסוֹת, וְלָא אֶפְשָׁר לִי לְמִשְׁבַּק אִינָשֵׁי בֵיתִי וּמֵייתֵי קַמֵּי רַבָּנַן. כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מֵרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שֶׁמַּשִּׂיאִין הָאִשָּׁה עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If this is** how you interpret the matter, in **what** way **is** this **country different** from any other? In other words, why did Neḥemya of Beit D'li mention a particular place? **He should have** said: **Any place where there are troops. Rather, Rava said: This is what he said: You know that this country is riddled with troops, and I cannot leave the members of my household and come before the Sages,** due to the danger. Therefore, I cannot testify in person that **this is** the tradition that **I received from Rabban Gamliel** the Elder, **that** the Sages allow **a woman to marry based on one witness.** According to this interpretation, his statement has no bearing on the matter at hand.

Rava rejects this reading. If the point were that one witness is accepted in time of war, he argues, Nechemia should have stated the rule in universal terms: *kol makom sheyesh gayasot* — wherever there are troops, a single witness is believed. Why would he refer specifically to *this* country? Moreover, the discussion was focused on the halakhic practice of *Eretz Yisrael* — Nechemia was delivering a tradition about what Rabban Gamliel the Elder ruled. The mention of troops, Rava explains, is entirely incidental. Nechemia was explaining why he could not go deliver this tradition to the authorities in *Eretz Yisrael* himself: *ve'i efshar li lemishbak inshi beiti umaytei kamei rabbanan* — it is impossible for him to travel, because the region is in chaos due to military activity. He was therefore asking Rabbi Akiva to carry the message back. The ruling itself — that a woman may remarry on the basis of one witness — has nothing to do with war. It is simply the general *halakha* according to Rabban Gamliel the Elder, and the mention of troops explains only why Nechemia could not convey it in person. The incident is therefore not a proof regarding *ed echad b'milchama*.

## Drowned Scholars

### Drowned Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מַעֲשֶׂה בִּשְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁהָיוּ בָּאִים עִם אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן סִימַאי בִּסְפִינָה, וְטָבְעָה, וְהִשִּׂיא רַבִּי נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן עַל פִּי נָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues. **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* that relates **an incident involving two Torah scholars who were coming with Abba Yosei ben Simai by boat, and** that boat **sank. And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi allowed **their wives to marry based on** the testimony given by **women** that these men were dead.

The *Gemara* presents another account. Two Torah sages were traveling with Abba Yossi ben Simai on a boat. *Ve-tavu* — they drowned. *Ve-hesi Rabbi nishoteihan al pi nashim* — Rabbi permitted their wives to remarry on the basis of women's testimony. Women who had been present or had witnessed the sinking reported the deaths, and Rabbi acted on those reports.

### Water = War

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מַיִם כְּמִלְחָמָה דָּמוּ. וְנָשִׁים, אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה — כְּעֵד אֶחָד דָּמוּ, וְקָתָנֵי: הִשִּׂיא!
>
> **Translation:** **But** consider: **Water,** i.e., the sea, **is like war** with respect to this *halakha*, as there is room in both cases for conjecture and error. **And women, even one hundred** of them, **are considered as one witness** with regard to their testimony about a husband's death. **And** yet the *baraita* **taught:** He allowed them **to marry,** which indicates that one may rely on one witness even during a war.

The *Gemara* observes: *mayim kemilchama dami* — water is treated like war in the relevant sense. In chaotic and dangerous circumstances on the water, those reporting what happened may be drawing conclusions based on perception rather than certainty. Furthermore, the testimony here is not that of two formal male witnesses. A hundred women, the *Gemara* notes, have the legal status of one witness — *bi'nashim afilu me'ah k'echad dami* — because women do not constitute formal *edut* in the technical sense. So Rabbi was relying on what is legally equivalent to *ed echad* testimony, in a situation analogous to war. This seems to be a strong proof that such testimony is accepted.

### Water Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? מַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף נִינְהוּ, וּמַיִם שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם סוֹף — אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה. אֶלָּא הֵיכִי דָּמֵי, דְּאָמְרִי: אַסְּקִינְהוּ קַמַּן וַחֲזֵינְהוּ לְאַלְתַּר, וְקָאָמְרִי סִימָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof. **And** how can **you understand** it that way? Apparently, the women testified only that the boat sank, **and this is** a case of **an endless body of water,** as the boat sank at sea in a spot from which it is impossible to see the shore. **And** the *halakha* is that if a man was on a boat that sank in **an endless body of water his wife is prohibited** to marry, as there is no proof that he actually drowned and didn't emerge from the water on a different shore. **Rather,** one must say: **What are the circumstances? That** those women **said: Those** drowned men **were brought up before us and we saw them immediately** when they came out of the water, **and** the women **stated distinguishing marks** that identified these people.

But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this reading. *Mayim she'ein lahem sof ninhu* — this was open water without visible boundaries. And *mayim she'ein lahem sof isha asura* — in the case of water with no visible end, a woman may not remarry even if two formal witnesses testify that they saw her husband drown. The logic is counterintuitive: rather than reasoning that without a visible shore the man had nowhere to swim, the concern runs the other direction. If the water has no visible end, then somewhere beyond the witnesses' sight the man may have surfaced and survived. No testimony about drowning in such conditions is ever absolutely conclusive. If that is so, how could Rabbi have permitted remarriage at all? The case seems impossible to rely upon as a proof for anything.

### Simanim Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלָאו עֲלַיְיהוּ סָמְכִינַן, אֶלָּא אַסִּימָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As,** in this case **we do not rely upon** the women, **but** upon **the distinguishing marks.**

The *Gemara* resolves this: *ela heichi dami* — what must the situation actually have been? The witnesses must have said *ashkachinhu kaman* — we found them before us, meaning the bodies were retrieved — *ve-chazinahu l'altar* — and we saw them immediately, before the water had time to completely disfigure the faces — *ve-amri simanim* — and they identified the bodies by specific bodily marks. Tosafot raises the question of why facial recognition alone would not suffice; the answer appears to be that even brief immersion can alter appearance enough that identification by *simanim* — distinctive physical marks such as a mole, a birthmark, or a physical abnormality — is needed to reinforce the identification. In that case, the *Gemara* concludes, *d'lav aleiyhu samchinan ela a'simanim* — we are not relying on the witnesses' general impression; we are relying on the concrete identifying marks they specified. There is therefore no concern of *amri bidedamei* — the testimony is grounded in specific, verifiable physical evidence, not in perception or assumption.

The *Gemara* has thus not definitively resolved whether *ed echad* is accepted in a war situation without additional grounding. The drowned scholars case, which seemed to offer a proof, turns out instead to rest on the presence of *simanim* rather than on the testimony of informal witnesses under chaotic conditions per se.

## Sesame Seeds

### Seeds Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַפְקֵיד שׁוּמְשְׁמֵי גַּבֵּי חַבְרֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי שׁוּמְשְׁמַי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁקַילְתִּינְהוּ: וְהָא כֵּן וְכֵן הָוַיִין, וּבְחָבִיתָא רַמְיִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּידָךְ שְׁקַלְתִּינְהוּ, וְהָנֵי — אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who deposited sesame plants with his friend.** Sometime later **he said to him: Give me the sesame plants.** The friend **said to him: You** already **took them.** The owner replied: **But they were** of **such-and-such** an amount, **and placed in a barrel;** go and check that barrel and you will see that I am right. The bailee **said to him: You took your** sesame plants, **and these** in the barrel at my house **are other ones.**

The *Gemara* shifts from the weighty terrain of death and remarriage to a *dinei mamonot* case involving sesame seeds — but the analogy it draws will illuminate the broader question of when we may connect circumstantial dots. A certain man had deposited a quantity of sesame seeds with his friend for safekeeping. When he later asked for them back, the friend said: you already took them back. The first man pointed to a barrel and said: I gave you exactly this measure, and here is the same measure of sesame seeds sitting in this barrel — *ha kaila ve-kaila havyan* — the amounts match precisely. The friend replied: those are mine; yours you already took.

### Chisda's Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבַר רַב חִסְדָּא לְמֵימַר: הַיְינוּ שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן: הָנָךְ אֲזַלוּ לְעָלְמָא, וְהָנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The case came before the Sages for a ruling. **Rav Ḥisda thought to say: This** situation **is** the same as the situation involving the **two Torah scholars** who drowned, when they used distinguishing marks to identify them. **And we do not say** in that case: **Those** men **went** elsewhere **in the world, and these** men who floated up **are different people.** Here too one may rely upon the distinguishing marks of the sesame plants given by the owner, and there is no reason to think that these sesame plants are different ones.

Rav Chisda sought to resolve the case by analogy to the drowned scholars. Just as in that case we matched two bodies to two men and assumed they were the same, so here — two matching quantities, same container, reasonable to assume it is the same deposit. The application from a case about remarrying widows to a property dispute over seeds raises immediate questions: do we apply different standards of proof in *dinei mamonot* versus cases of personal status? And how much circumstantial convergence is needed in each context to draw a confident conclusion?

### Rava Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם, קָאָמְרִי סִימָנִים. הָכָא, שׁוּמְשְׁמֵי מַאי סִימָנָא אִית לְהוּ? וּדְקָאָמַר ״כֵּן וְכֵן הָוַיִין״ — אֵימַר חוּשְׁבָּנָא אִיתְרְמִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Ḥisda: **Is it comparable? There they said distinguishing marks** that identified the victims. **Here,** in the case of **sesame plants, what distinguishing marks might they have,** by which they could be identified? **And** as for **that which he said: They were** of **such-and-such** an amount, one can **say it happened by chance** that this second time it **was** the same **amount,** and there is no proof that these are the same sesame plants.

Rava rejected the analogy: *mi dami* — how can you compare? *Hatam ka'amar simanim* — in the drowned case, the witnesses identified specific bodily marks. That is what made the identification reliable. *Hacha shumshumei mai simanei it lehu* — here, sesame seeds have no identifying marks whatsoever. One measure of sesame seeds looks exactly like any other measure of sesame seeds. The analogy simply does not hold.

### Weight Not Enough

The *Gemara* then asks: is not the matching quantity itself a kind of identifying mark? *Ha kaila ve-kaila havyan* — they are the same measure. The *Gemara* answers: *chushbana itremi* — it may simply be a coincidence that the amounts match. Just as one cannot identify a person by height alone — many people share the same height — one cannot identify a deposit by quantity alone. Many quantities happen to be identical, and identical weight or volume does not constitute a meaningful *siman*. The claim fails.

## Labeled Vessels

### Vessel Labels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וּמִי חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא פִּינָּן? וְהָתְנַן: מָצָא כְלִי וְכָתוּב עָלָיו קוֹף — קׇרְבָּן, מֵם — מַעֲשֵׂר, דָּלֶת — דִּמּוּעַ, טֵית — טֶבֶל, תָּיו — תְּרוּמָה. שֶׁבִּשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה הָיוּ כּוֹתְבִין תָּיו תַּחַת תְּרוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the same issue, **Mar Kashisha, son of Rav Ḥisda, said to Rav Ashi: And are we concerned** that **perhaps** the one guarding the plants **moved them** from their place? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 4:11): If one **found a vessel on which** the letter ***kuf* was written,** all objects inside the vessel are designated for a ***korban*,** an offering; if the letter ***mem*** was written on it, it is ***ma'aser*,** tithes; if it was the letter ***dalet*,** it is ***dimua*,** a mixture of *teruma* and non-sacred produce; if ***tet*,** it signifies ***tevel*,** untithed produce; and finally, if it is a ***tav*,** it indicates ***teruma*. As during a time of danger,** i.e., religious persecution against Jews, **they would write,** for example, ***tav* instead of *teruma*.** In this case, no concern is expressed that someone might have moved the *teruma* from that vessel to somewhere else.

Mar Kashisha bar Rav Chisda raises a question to Rav Ashi: are we really concerned, in the sesame seeds case, that the borrower might have emptied the barrel and refilled it with different seeds? He cites a *mishnah*: if a vessel is found with the letter *kuf* written on it, one assumes it contains *korban* — something consecrated to the Temple; *mem* indicates *maaser*; *dalet* indicates *dimua* — a mixture of *chullin* and *terumah*; *tet* indicates *tevel* — untithed grain; and *tav* indicates *terumah*. The *mishnah* notes that in times of danger, people wrote *tav* in place of spelling out *terumah* in full. The principle behind all of this is that one assumes the vessel still contains what its label indicates — that is, we do not ordinarily suppose that a person emptied a labeled vessel and reused it for something else.

### Label Principle

The rationale is intuitive: the whole point of labeling a vessel is to maintain consistent use. A person who has gone to the trouble of marking a storage container with its contents is unlikely to repurpose that container while leaving the old label in place. From this, one might conclude that we never assume a vessel was emptied and reused, which would support the view that the sesame seeds in the barrel are the original deposit.

### Yosei Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְלָא חָיְשִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא פִּינָּן? אֵימָא סֵיפָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ מָצָא חָבִית וְכָתוּב עָלֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוּלִּין, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אֶשְׁתָּקַד הֲוָה מָלֵא תְּרוּמָה, וּפִינָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** In response to this claim, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: And aren't we concerned** that **perhaps** someone **moved** the *teruma* from their place? **Say the latter clause** of that same mishna **Rabbi Yosei says: Even if** one **found a barrel on which** the full word ***teruma* was written, these** contents **are non-sacred. As I say: Last year it was full of *teruma*, and** someone **removed** the contents and replaced them with non-sacred produce. This proves that the possibility that someone moved the original contents is taken into consideration.

But Ravina draws attention to the end of the *mishnah*: Rabbi Yosei says that even if one finds a whole barrel explicitly labeled *terumah*, one may still say that its current contents are *chullin*. His reasoning: *shani omer eshtakad havei meleiah terumah u-pina* — I can say that last year it was filled with *terumah*, it was then emptied, and now it is being used for ordinary produce. Rabbi Yosei explicitly entertains the possibility of emptying and reusing a vessel.

### Label Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא חָיְשִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא פִּינָּן, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: אִם אִיתָא דְּפִינָּהוּ — מִיכְפָּר הֲוָה כָּפַר. וְאִידַּךְ: אֵימַר אִישְׁתְּלוֹיֵי אִישְׁתְּלִי. אִי נָמֵי, לְפַנְחַיָּא שַׁבְקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say as follows: **Everyone** agrees that **we are concerned** that **perhaps** someone **moved** the contents of a container from their place, **and here,** with regard to the marked vessels, **they disagree about this** issue: **The Sage** who claims that one may rely on the inscription **holds** that **if it is so, that he moved** the *teruma*, **he would have erased** the inscription. **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Yosei, responds that one can **say he forgot** to do so. **Alternatively, he left the label to preserve [*panaḥya*]** the contents, so that people would mistakenly think that it contained *teruma* and would refrain from taking the produce.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Yosei's view and the earlier assumption of consistency. The dispute turns on a specific question: *im issa de-panya michpar hava kafar* — if you were going to reuse the vessel for something else, would you not have erased the label? One position holds that no one would leave a misleading label on a vessel; therefore, the presence of the label proves continued consistent use. Rabbi Yosei, however, holds that there are two plausible ways the label could remain despite a change of contents: either the owner forgot the vessel was labeled — *ishtaluyei ishtali* — or he intentionally kept the *terumah* label as a deterrent, knowing that people will not touch something marked *terumah* and thus using the label to protect ordinary produce from being eaten.

### Seeds Application

The implication for the sesame seeds case: when it comes to explicitly labeled vessels, there is at least a basis for arguing that the label proves continuity of use — that is the majority position against which Rabbi Yosei dissents. But an unlabeled barrel of sesame seeds lacks even that basis. With no label, there is no reason to presume that the contents are original. It is entirely plausible that the barrel was emptied, the original seeds returned to their owner as the depositor claims, and then refilled with the friend's own seeds. The sesame seeds case therefore cannot be resolved on the basis of continuity of possession.

## Reish Galuta

### Death Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִצְחָק רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּרַב בִּיבִי, הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל מִקּוּרְטָבָא לְאַסְפַּמְיָא, וּשְׁכֵיב. שְׁלַחוּ מֵהָתָם: יִצְחָק רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּרַב בִּיבִי, הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל מִקּוּרְטָבָא לְאַסְפַּמְיָא, וּשְׁכֵיב. מִי חָיְשִׁינַן לִתְרֵי יִצְחָק, אוֹ לָא? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: חָיְשִׁינַן. רָבָא אָמַר: לָא חָיְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates a story that deals with the permission of a woman to remarry. **Yitzḥak the Exilarch, son of the sister of Rav Beivai, was walking from Cortva to Spain and died** along the way. **They sent** this message **from** Spain: **Yitzḥak the Exilarch, son of the sister of Rav Beivai, was walking from Cortva to Spain and died.** The Gemara asks: **Are we concerned about** the possibility of **two** men named **Yitzḥak or not?** Perhaps there is someone else with the same name, and therefore the mention of his name is not a sufficiently distinguishing mark. **Abaye said: We are concerned** about this possibility. **Rava said: We are not concerned.**

The *Gemara* introduces a new story and a new question about identification. Yitzchak the *Reish Galuta* — the political head of the Jewish diaspora in Babylonia — who was the son of the sister of Rabbi Bibi, was traveling from Cordoba to Spain. *Ve-shakhiv* — he died en route. A message was sent back: Yitzchak the *Reish Galuta*, son of Rabbi Bibi's sister, was traveling from Cordoba to Spain and died.

### Name Worry

The question posed: *mi chayishinan le-trei Yitzchak o lo* — must we be concerned that there might be two people named Yitzchak who fit these identifying details? At first glance this seems almost absurd. How many people named Yitzchak could there be who simultaneously hold the position of *Reish Galuta*, are nephews of Rabbi Bibi, and were traveling on that same route? And yet the *Gemara* treats this as a live question, because the broader principle at stake is one of general importance: when we receive a report about a person identified by name and other particulars, how much specific identification is required before we may act on the assumption that we know who is meant?

### Abaye's View

Abaye holds: *chayishinan* — we are concerned. Even with multiple identifying details, one must verify that no other person fits the same description before relying on the report for a matter of consequence.

### Rava's View

Rava holds: *lo chayishinan* — we are not concerned. Once a person fits the specific identifying details provided, there is no need to investigate further for potential duplicates. Rava's position is that context and convergence of identifying information are, in principle, sufficient.

## Androlinai Get

### Get Found

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דְּהָהוּא גִּיטָּא דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא וּכְתִיב: בְּצַד קְלוֹנְיָא מָתָא אֲנָא אַנְדְּרוֹלִינַאי נְהַרְדָּעָא פְּטַרִית וְתָרֵכִית יָת פְּלוֹנִית אִנְתְּתִי. וְשַׁלְחַהּ אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה. וּשְׁלַח לֵיהּ: תִּיבָּדֵק נְהַרְדְּעָא כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: From where do I say** my reasoning that there might be another man with the same name? **As a certain bill of divorce was found in** the city of **Neharde'a, and** this passage was **written** on it: **On the colonial [*kelonya*] side of the city, I, Androlinai of Neharde'a, excused, sent away,** and divorced **my wife so-and-so.** Androlinai's wife requested permission to remarry based on this bill of divorce, but they did not know if he was the man who gave the divorce or if it was given by another man of the same name. **And the father of Shmuel sent this** question **before Rabbi Yehuda Nesia** in Eretz Yisrael. **And** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **sent** a message **to him: All of Neharde'a must be examined,** to see whether there is another man by that name. This shows that one must be concerned that there might be two people with the same name.

Rava supports his position with the following proof. A *get* was found in Nehardea, written as follows: I, Androlinai, from Nehardea, hereby divorce and dismiss so-and-so my wife. The father of Shmuel sent this document to Rav Yehuda Nesi'ah — the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — asking: we know a man in Nehardea named Androlinai whose wife is named Sarah. May we assume this *get* refers to him and allow his wife to remarry?

### Investigation Need

Rashi notes that the *get* contained not only the husband's unusual name but also the wife's name and the city — all three identifying details matching someone known to the community. And yet the question was raised: must we verify that no other person in Nehardea shares these same particulars?

### Investigation Order

Rav Yehuda Nesi'ah responded: *zil bedok bi-Nehardea kullah* — go and check the entire Nehardea — meaning the entire district — to determine whether anyone else has the same name as this husband and the same name as his wife. He thus apparently required active investigation before assuming that the *get* referred to the known Androlinai.

### Rava's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, מַאי שְׁנָא ״מְדִינָה זוֹ״? ״כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ גְּיָיסוֹת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ דַּאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל הוּא דִּשְׁלַח הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This story provides no proof. **If it is so,** that there were grounds to suspect that someone else of the same name wrote the bill of divorce, Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **should have** said: **The entire world must be examined,** in case there is someone else with the same name somewhere. Since he did not say this, evidently there was no legitimate reason for this suspicion at all. Why, then, did Rabbi Yehuda Nesia send instructions to examine all of Neharde'a? **Rather,** Rava added, **it was due to respect for the father of Shmuel that he sent this** message. He did not want to write explicitly that Shmuel's father had inquired unnecessarily, and therefore he wrote his reply in a manner which indicated his partial agreement with the concern.

Rava, however, argues that this cannot be the actual *halakhic* ruling. If we truly could not draw logical inferences from identifying information, then *yidbok kulei alma mibei leh* — one would have to search the entire world. The *get* says the man is from Nehardea, but Rashi notes that perhaps he was merely *in* Nehardea when he wrote it; perhaps he had since moved on; perhaps there was once another Androlinai who lived there and left. There would be no end to such concerns. Rava therefore concludes: *mishum kevodo de-avuh de-Shmuel hu de-shalach hakhi* — Rav Yehuda Nesi'ah's instruction to investigate was not a true halakhic ruling but a gesture of respect to the father of Shmuel, who had raised the question. Rather than dismissing it as unnecessary, he gave him work to do. In reality, Rava maintains, once the known Androlinai fits all the identifying details, we do not require a search for hypothetical duplicates.

## Mirror Names

### IOU Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ — דְּהָנְהוּ תְּרֵי שְׁטָרֵי דְּנָפְקִי בְּמָחוֹזָא וּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: חָבֵי בַּר נַנַּאי וְנַנַּאי בַּר חָבֵי, וְאַגְבִּי בְּהוּ רָבָא בַּר אֲבוּהּ זוּזֵי. וְהָא חָבֵי בַּר נַנַּאי וְנַנַּאי בַּר חָבֵי בְּמָחוֹזָא שְׁכִיחִי טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I say** my reasoning that we are not concerned about two people with identical names? **As there were two** promissory **notes produced in Meḥoza, and** these names of the creditors were **written on them: Ḥavai bar Nanai and Nanai bar Ḥavai, and Rava bar Avuh collected dinars** for them **with these** promissory notes, without concerning himself with the possibility that they might be referring to other people. **And** the names **Ḥavai bar Nanai and Nanai bar Ḥavai are very common in Meḥoza,** i.e., there are certainly other people with these names, and yet he was not worried about this matter.

Rava offers an additional proof from a case in Mechoza. Two loan documents — IOUs — were found from Mechoza, and the names on them were *Chavi bar Nigai* and *Ninai bar Chavi*: almost mirror images of each other, each component of one name appearing inverted in the other. These were very common names in that city, making the prospect of confusion quite real.

### Collection Allowed

Rava bar Avuha nevertheless permitted collection on the basis of these IOUs, notwithstanding the similar names and the possibility of confusion about which document referred to which party.

### Context Works

Rashi and Rava's conclusion is that context and circumstances are sufficient to identify the correct parties. Despite the commonality of these names and despite the mirror-image similarity between them, the combination of circumstances surrounding who holds the document, who is being approached for payment, and what the overall situation is allows us to draw a reliable conclusion about which document refers to which transaction and which parties.

## IOU Safety

### Three Protections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נֵיחוּשׁ לַהּ? אִי לִנְפִילָה — מִזְהָר זְהִיר בֵּיהּ. אִי לְפִקָּדוֹן — כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמֵיהּ כִּשְׁמֵיהּ לָא מַפְקֵיד גַּבֵּיהּ. מַאי אָמְרַתְּ, דִּלְמָא מְסַר לֵיהּ? אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what** should we **be concerned** in the case of the promissory note? **If** we are concerned **about** the possibility of **falling,** i.e., that the promissory note might have dropped from this person's hand and the other one found it, the former is certainly **careful with it** so as not to lose it, as he knows there is someone else in the city with the same name. **If** we are concerned **about** the possibility that it was given as **a deposit** for safekeeping, i.e., that the actual owner might have given it to the one in possession of it, **since his name is the same as the name** of the bailee, the owner **would not deposit** his contract **with him** without some insurance. **If you say** that **perhaps** the actual owner **passed,** i.e., transferred the promissory note **to this** person whose name is the same as his own, i.e., he gave or sold it to him so he may collect it for himself, in that case the one in possession is entitled to collect the money, as **letters** of credit **are acquired through passing.** There is no need for an additional act of acquisition here, which means that the promissory note belongs to the one in possession of it, despite the fact that it was not originally written for him.

Abaye frames his own understanding of why the creditor holding these IOUs may be trusted, offering three reasons not to worry that the wrong person is presenting the document. First, Abaye raises the possibility that the creditor might have *dropped* the IOU — *ei l'nefilla* — and someone else found it. But creditors are careful: *mizahar beh* — they safeguard such documents precisely because their entire claim rests on them. The concern about accidental loss is therefore negligible. Second, one might worry the creditor gave the IOU to someone else for *pikadon* — safekeeping — who now misuses it. But as Abaye explains, *keivan d'shmei k'shmei lo mafkad gabei* — no creditor would be so foolish as to entrust a document bearing his debtor's name to another person who happens to share that exact name, since that person could then fraudulently use it to collect. Third, one might argue the IOU was transferred — *mesar leh* — sold or assigned to another — but the transfer was not completed and therefore the holder has no valid claim. But this too fails: *osiyos niknos b'mesira* — the transfer of written documents is effected by the very act of handing them over. If the creditor gave the document to someone, that act itself completes the acquisition, and the holder has a valid claim.

### Creditor Logic

The logic threading through all three answers is that the circumstances themselves constrain the universe of plausible explanations. A creditor would not drop his IOU carelessly; a creditor would not entrust it to someone with an identical name; and if it was intentionally transferred, the transfer was legally complete. These considerations together mean that the person presenting the IOU is almost certainly the legitimate creditor, and we may rely on context to establish that identification without demanding additional proof.

## Context & Conclusions

### Spectrum

Stepping back, the cases discussed across 115a–b form a spectrum. At one extreme is the Yitzchak *Reish Galuta* situation — a case with an extraordinary concentration of identifying information, where Abaye nonetheless insists on concern that another individual might match all the same particulars. In the middle is the Androlinai *get* — a document with multiple identifying details including an unusual name, a wife's name, and a city, where the question of possible duplicates arises and Rava argues that practical logic forecloses the need for further investigation. At the near end is the mirror-name IOU case from Mechoza, where the names are common, the possibility of confusion is real, and yet collection is allowed based on the totality of circumstances.

### Connecting Dots

This spectrum is worth reading alongside the earlier discussion of *amra bidedamei*. The concern running through that entire section is that a woman — or any witness — might connect dots incorrectly, reasoning from circumstances to a conclusion that seems warranted but is not actually certain. The cases in this later section demonstrate that such dot-connecting is unavoidable. In the charred body case, in the drowned scholars case, and now in the name-identification cases, some inference from context is always required. The question is not whether we may ever reason from circumstances to conclusions — we must — but rather how many inferential steps we may take, what quality of identifying information we require, and how much weight we give to explanations that would undermine the most natural reading of the facts.

### Investigation Missing

What runs through all of this as a striking omission is the question of why the *Gemara* so consistently treats the woman's testimony as a fixed datum to be believed or disbelieved, rather than as a starting point for investigation. If the concern is that she is drawing incorrect conclusions — that she saw her husband shot in battle and assumed he died, or saw smoke and assumed he perished — the obvious response would be to ask her detailed questions: what exactly did you see, when, from what distance, what happened immediately after? A careful interrogation might well reveal whether her report reflects direct observation or inference. The *Gemara* does not explain why this kind of probing examination is not the standard response, and that absence remains a notable gap in the analysis.
