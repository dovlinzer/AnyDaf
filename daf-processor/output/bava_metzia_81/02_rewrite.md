# Bava Metzia 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Borrowing Return Status

### Mishnah Contradiction

The daf opens at the bottom of 81a, picking up from the *mishnah* that establishes: *kol ha-umanim shomrei sachar hen* — any craftsman who has an object entrusted to him for work becomes a *shomer sachar*, a paid bailee. As a *shomer sachar*, he is liable for *geneivah va-aveidah*, loss and theft, though he is exempt from *onsin*, accidents and damages beyond his control.

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *tenan hatam* — a *mishnah* elsewhere appears to contradict this ruling. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* from 98b dealing with borrowing, which bears directly on the liability of a *sho'el* and the status of objects while in transit.

### Mishnah 98b Citation

The *mishnah* in 98b reads: if a borrower says to the lender, "send it to me by way of my son, my slave, or my agent," or "by your son, your slave, or your agent," and the lender complies and the animal dies in transit — the borrower is *chayav*, liable. The reasoning is that once both parties agreed on the method of transmission, the object effectively entered the borrower's *reshut*, his legal domain, from the moment that agreement was made.

Likewise, the *mishnah* continues: *vechen beshaah shemachzirah* — the same principle governs the moment of return. If there was no prior agreement about how the object would be returned, then the object has not left the borrower's *reshut* until it physically reaches the lender's hands. If it is damaged in transit without such an agreement, the borrower remains liable.

This creates an apparent contradiction with our *mishnah*. In the case of the *uman*, the craftsman, we rule that once he declares the object ready for pickup and demands payment — *tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot*, take your thing and give me the money you owe me — he is immediately downgraded to a *shomer chinam*, an unpaid bailee. Yet the *mishnah* from 98b suggests that a borrower retains liability until the object physically returns to the lender's hands. The two rulings seem to pull in opposite directions.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi, both in his commentary here and in his notes to 98b, clarifies the mechanism of the *mishnah* from 98b. The rule that the object "enters the borrower's *reshut*" upon an agreed-upon method of transmission means that the borrower has taken on full *sho'el* liability from that point forward. Conversely, when returning the animal, if no agreement was made about the return method, the borrower's *reshut* extends all the way until the object physically arrives back in the lender's hands. The point of tension with our *mishnah* is therefore real: in one case, a declaration by the craftsman terminates his elevated liability immediately; in the other, mere physical possession during transit sustains it.

### Efrayim bar Pappa

The *gemara* resolves this tension through an *okimta* attributed to Efrayim bar Pappa citing Rav Chisda: *lo shanu ela shemachzirah betoch yemei she'elatah* — the *mishnah* from 98b applies only when the borrower is returning the object *within* the agreed borrowing period. If I borrowed Michael's cow for a month and I am returning it on the fifteenth, then during that transit period the object is still in my *reshut* and I remain liable until it reaches Michael's hands.

However, if the borrowing period has already concluded — if it is the first of the following month and I am now returning the cow because I am obligated to — then we say the borrower is *patur* from the moment the return journey is underway. After the term expires, everyone understands the return is necessary, and the object is treated as already partially back in the lender's *reshut*. On this reading, there is no contradiction: the *mishnah* from 98b addresses mid-term returns, while our *mishnah* about the *uman* addresses an analogous situation where the craftsman's completion declaration marks the natural end of the relationship.

### Rav Nachman Challenge

Rav Nachman bar Pappa raises a challenge from a *baraita*: *vechullan she'amru tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot shomer chinam — hagamarti shomer sachar?* The *baraita* implies that saying *tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot* makes the craftsman a *shomer chinam*, but what if he merely said *gamarti*, "I am done with your object"? Does a simple declaration of completion also terminate his status as *shomer sachar* and reduce him to *shomer chinam*?

### Take vs. Completed

The *gemara* draws a distinction. *Tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot* — "take your thing and give me the money" — is a specific formulation that explicitly acknowledges both the completion of the work and the demand for payment. *Gamarti* — "I am finished" — is a simpler statement that leaves the ownership transfer implicit. One might have thought these function differently in law.

The *gemara* then considers a further refinement: what if the *uman* reverses the order and says *havei li ma'ot vetol shelcha* — "give me my money and then take your thing"? Here, the craftsman is explicitly withholding the object pending payment, holding it as a kind of lien. In that case, as we learned in the previous *sugya*, the *uman* retains the status of *shomer sachar*, because he is essentially holding the object on account of his right to payment.

### Formulation Logic

If *gamarti* and *tol shelcha* produce the same legal result — both reducing the craftsman to *shomer chinam* — why does the *mishnah* choose the more elaborate formulation *tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot*? The *gemara* asks: *ei hachi adtane vechullan she'amru tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot shomer chinam nashmi'inan gamarti vechol sheken tol shelcha* — if *gamarti* suffices, teach us *gamarti* and we would know all the more so that *tol shelcha* works as well. Why teach the more complex case?

### Tosefta Versions

One answer the *gemara* entertains involves the parallel *Tosefta*. The *Tosefta* in this context does not use the formulation *tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot* at all. It simply states *tol shelcha* or *gamarti*. In the *Tosefta*, the precise order of the words does not matter — the key is simply that the craftsman communicated completion in some form. The *mishnah*'s specific phrasing, then, is not necessarily presenting a more stringent or different rule; it may simply reflect the version in use in a particular tradition.

### Textual Transmission

Examining the manuscript evidence, there is reason to believe that the formulation *havei li ma'ot vetol shelcha* entered our *mishnah* by a process of textual migration. Chapter five of the *mishnah* contains the phrase *havei li ma'ot vetol shelcha* in a discussion of *ribbit*, where the order of the demand genuinely matters — there, it is the sequence of payment-first and release-second that creates the problematic structure. It appears that this precise phrasing was then absorbed into *mishnah* 6 — perhaps by accident in transmission — and was subsequently reordered in *Bavel* to read *tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot*, either because the order seemed insignificant in this context or because the reversed order was needed to generate the *gemara*'s *diyuk*.

### Explicit Formula Teaching

The *gemara* ultimately explains why the specific formulation *tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot* is necessary as the *mishnah*'s teaching point. One might have thought that in the case of the *uman* who declares both completion and a payment demand, he would not even rise to the level of *shomer chinam* — that is, he would have no *shemirah* obligations whatsoever and could not even be required to take an oath. *Tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot* teaches us that he *does* remain a *shomer chinam*, with the attendant obligations of that status, even after declaring completion. Simple *gamarti* alone does not convey this teaching.

The *ikka de'amri* version of the *sugya* traces the same path through a different entry point: Rav Nachman bar Pappa notes that the *mishnah*'s ruling — all *umanim* who say *tol shelcha vehavei ma'ot* are *shomer chinam* — might seem to imply the same for *gamarti*. The *gemara* rejects the inference: *tol shelcha shani*, "take your thing" is a distinct case, and the *mishnah* is needed to teach it specifically. The operative *halacha*, however, is that *gamarti* does indeed carry the same consequence as *tol shelcha* — both reduce the craftsman to *shomer chinam* — while *havei li ma'ot vetol shelcha*, retaining the object pending payment, keeps him as *shomer sachar*.

## Borrower Liability Status

### Exempt Accident Status

A new *sugya* now opens, introduced by the late *amora* Huna Mar bar Amemar, who presents the contradiction between the two *mishnayot* before Ravina and resolves it in precisely the same fashion as above — noting that this very late *amora*, who is himself the student of an *amora*, engages in the same method as the *stam* Talmud: raising a contradiction and deploying an *okimta* from an earlier *amora* to resolve it. There is a certain wry irony in this observation, insofar as it suggests that Huna Mar bar Amemar may himself be part of the *stam* in this passage.

Following this, the *gemara* raises a distinct question through *ibaya lehu*: once a borrower has been declared exempt from liability for accidents — because, say, the borrowing period has ended and the return is underway — what precisely is his status? Is he *patur misho'el* but still *chayav keshomer sachar*, remaining liable for theft and loss but not for accidents? Or is he not even a *shomer sachar*, but rather a *shomer chinam*, such that he could take an oath and be exempt even from theft and loss?

### Amemar Benefit Theory

Amemar addresses this directly: *mistabra patur misho'el vechayav keshomer sachar* — it stands to reason that the borrower loses only his *sho'el* liability for accidents, but retains the elevated status of a *shomer sachar* for theft and loss. The basis for this position is *ho'il veka neheni minei* — because the borrower still derives some benefit from the object, that ongoing *hana'ah* functions as the "payment" that classifies him as a paid bailee. The precise nature of this benefit during the return journey is admittedly puzzling — one might ask what concrete benefit accrues to someone returning a borrowed donkey — but the principle, as Amemar formulates it, is that the *hana'ah* inhering in the very act of borrowing persists even into the return phase.

### Artisan Baraita

A *baraita* is then cited in support of Amemar's position. The case involves someone who goes to an *uman*'s shop to acquire vessels as a gift for his father-in-law, with the arrangement that if the father-in-law accepts them he will pay the full price, and if not, he will pay only *lefi tovat hana'ah*, an amount reflecting the modest benefit he received from his father-in-law's goodwill toward him. The *baraita* rules: *ne'ensu behalicha chayav* — if the vessels are damaged on the way to the father-in-law's house, he is liable as a *sho'el*. But *uvichazira patur mipnei she-hu ke-nosei sachar* — if they are damaged on the return journey, he is exempt from *onsin* because he is classified as a *nosei sachar*, another term for *shomer sachar*.

### Hana'ah Return Phase

This *baraita* thus makes explicit that the return-journey status is *shomer sachar*, not *shomer chinam* — precisely as Amemar ruled from *sevara*. The *hana'ah* on the return leg, though difficult to quantify precisely, is understood as the benefit the father-in-law's increased goodwill generates, and that *hana'ah* is treated as sufficient "payment" to classify the carrier as a paid bailee. The *baraita* confirms that the borrower is neither fully liable as a *sho'el* on the return, nor fully exempt as a *shomer chinam* — he occupies an intermediate position: *patur* from *onsin* but *chayav* for *geneivah va-aveidah*.

## Donkey Sale Case

### Donkey Injury Setup

The *gemara* now presents a related case. A certain man was given a donkey by his friend with instructions: take it to such-and-such a place and try to sell it there; if you manage to sell it, you pay me the price; if not, bring it back and I'll take it back from you. He went, failed to sell the donkey, and on the return journey the animal was injured.

### Rav Nachman Liable

The case came before Rav Nachman, who ruled the man *chayav* — liable to pay for the injury despite the fact that it occurred on the return journey, where we might have expected reduced liability.

### Rava Challenge

Rava challenged Rav Nachman: but the *baraita* we just cited teaches *uvichazira patur mipnei she-hu ke-nosei sachar* — on the return journey, the bailee is exempt from damage as a *shomer sachar*, not liable as a *sho'el*. Why, then, does Rav Nachman rule *chayav*?

### Continuous Mission

Rav Nachman clarifies: *chazara de-hai halicha hi* — this return is not a return at all in the relevant sense; it is a continuation of the going. The reason is straightforward: *de-chazara de-hai ilu ashkach lezivunei mi la zavna* — had he encountered a buyer on his way back, would he not have sold the donkey? Of course he would. He had not mentally or legally concluded his sale mission. He remained in the posture of someone trying to sell the animal throughout the entire journey, including the return leg. Therefore, his status is that of a *sho'el* on the outbound journey — fully liable for *onsin* — and this status continues unbroken. The "return" is in a physical sense only; legally, he is still traveling outbound.

### Rental Analogy

This principle can be illuminated by analogy to modern rental arrangements. When you rent a car for thirty days and return it on the thirty-first, you are charged for the additional day — not because the rental company treats the return journey more generously, but because the liability period has simply extended. What the *gemara* is articulating here is a related but distinct idea: when the very purpose of the mission is ongoing — when the seller is still in selling mode — the legal character of his possession does not change merely because he is physically heading home.

### Period Boundaries

The broader principle emerging from this section is that the boundary between *sho'el* liability and *shomer sachar* liability is determined not simply by the direction of travel, but by the legal status of the period. If a borrowing term has expired, the return is treated as the lender's *reshut* drawing the object back, and the borrower's liability is reduced accordingly. If, by contrast, the mission is still active — as in the donkey-sale case where the seller could have concluded the sale at any moment — then the full liability of the outbound status persists throughout. The agreement between the parties, and the continuing purpose of the arrangement, define where one period ends and another begins.

## Guard-for-Guard Setup

### Mishnah Reciprocal

The *mishnah* states: *shmor li ve'eshmor lach shomer sachar* — "guard for me and I'll guard for you" creates the status of *shomer sachar* for each party with respect to the other's property. This ruling, however, immediately generates a difficulty.

### Owner Working Problem

The problem is the principle of *shmira biv'alim* — when the owner of an object is in the employ of the bailee at the very moment of the bailment, the bailee's status is reduced to *shomer chinam*. The logic is that the owner's labor for the bailee constitutes a kind of offset or subsidy that eliminates the "payment" element of the *shomer sachar* relationship. If the two parties are simultaneously guarding each other's property, then each is the *ba'al* of his own object while simultaneously laboring for the other party. One might therefore expect each to be no more than a *shomer chinam* — not *shomer sachar*. Why, then, does the *mishnah* call this arrangement *shomer sachar*?

The difficulty is not immediately obvious from the face of the *mishnah*, and working through it is central to understanding the cases that follow. If two people say to each other, "you guard mine, I'll guard yours," right now simultaneously, then at the very moment I am guarding your property, you — the owner of that property — are guarding mine, meaning you are working for me. That is precisely the structure of *shmira biv'alim* that should disqualify the elevated *shomer sachar* status.

### Rav Papa Sequential

Rav Papa resolves the difficulty by giving the *mishnah* a specific reading: *de'amar lei shmor li hayom ve'eshmor lach lemachar* — the arrangement is sequential, not simultaneous. "Guard mine today and I will guard yours tomorrow." At the moment I am guarding your property today, you are not laboring for me at all — you are simply benefiting from the arrangement we made. The "payment" for my guardianship is your guardianship of my property tomorrow, and those two periods of guardianship are non-overlapping. Therefore, the *shmira biv'alim* exemption does not apply, and each party is correctly classified as a *shomer sachar*.

### Baraita Parallels

A *baraita* extends the same logic to mixed arrangements: *shmor li ve'eshmor lach, hash'ileini ve'ash'ilecha, shmor li ve'ash'ilecha, hash'ileini ve'eshmor lach* — guard for me and I'll guard for you; lend to me and I'll lend to you; guard for me and I'll lend to you; lend to me and I'll guard for you. All such combinations, the *baraita* rules, create *shomrei sachar* for one another: *kulan na'asu shomrei sachar zeh lazeh*. The same question arises — *shmira biv'alim hi?* — and Rav Papa's answer applies equally: the arrangements are sequential, so that at no moment is the owner of an object simultaneously laboring for the one guarding it.

One might ask: does this apply even to *hash'ileini ve'ash'ilecha*, a reciprocal lending arrangement? If I borrow your object and you borrow mine, and something happens, am I liable as a *sho'el* — fully liable for *onsin* — or as a *shomer sachar*? The answer, on Rav Papa's reading, is that the sequential structure of the arrangement creates *shomer sachar* status, not *sho'el* status, for each party. The mutual exchange of benefit is treated as the equivalent of payment.

### Non-Overlapping Times

The crucial principle crystallized by Rav Papa is that the *kula* of *shomer chinam* — the exemption from liability for *geneivah va-aveidah* that arises from *shmira biv'alim* — operates only when the owner of the object is *actually working* for the bailee at the precise moment the *shmira* is occurring. It is not enough that the owner has contracted to work for the bailee at some other time, or that there exists a general reciprocal relationship. The exemption requires contemporaneous, simultaneous employment. This point becomes critical in the cases that follow.

## Perfume Makers Coat

### First Theft Case

The *gemara* now presents a practical scenario that tests this principle of simultaneity. There was a group of perfume makers (*aloi*) who had a rotating arrangement: each day, one of them would do the baking for the group. On a particular day, they said to one of their number: go do the baking today. He replied: then you all guard my coat (*glimai*) while I am baking. Before he returned with the bread, his coat was stolen. The case came before Rav Papa, who ruled the others *chayav* — they were *shomrei sachar* with respect to the coat, and therefore liable for the theft.

### Rabbis Challenge

The Rabbis challenged Rav Papa: how could he rule *chayav*? This is *pshi'ah biv'alim* — the owner of the coat was working for the guardians at the very moment of the *shmira*. He was baking for them. That should reduce their status to *shomer chinam* and exempt them from liability for theft.

### Drinking Break

Rav Papa was discomfited by the objection. But the situation was resolved when the facts were investigated further: *iglai milta d'hahi sha'ata shichra hava ka shati* — it emerged that at the very moment the coat was stolen, the baker was not in fact baking. He had paused to have a drink and had not yet begun the work. He was therefore not, at that precise moment, in the employ of the other perfume makers. The theft occurred during this interlude, and so *shmira biv'alim* did not apply. The other perfume makers were indeed *shomrei sachar* at the moment of the theft, and Rav Papa's ruling of liability was correct after all.

The *gemara* observes: *hanicha leman d'amar pshi'ah biv'alim patur* — this story only makes sense as a vindication of Rav Papa on the assumption that we hold *pshi'ah biv'alim* exempts from liability. The reason Rav Papa was embarrassed by the Rabbis' objection is precisely that he accepts this principle: if the baker had been baking at the moment of theft, the others would indeed be *patur*. Once it emerged he was on a break, the case shifted, and Rav Papa's original ruling was justified.

### Explicit Condition

The *gemara* then adds a second scenario that sharpens the point. In this version, the day in question was not normally the baker's day to bake — it was an out-of-turn assignment. He agreed to bake, but explicitly conditioned his agreement on reciprocal guarding of his coat: *behai agra d'ka apeina lechu natru glimai* — "in consideration of my baking for you today, you will guard my coat." Here the *shomer sachar* arrangement is explicit and formally articulated. Again the coat was stolen before he returned, again the case came before Rav Papa, and again he ruled *chayav*.

### Break Resolution

Once again the Rabbis objected on grounds of *shmira biv'alim*, and once again the investigation revealed the same resolution: at the moment of the theft, the baker was drinking and had not yet entered into the employ of the other perfume makers. The theft thus occurred outside the window of *shmira biv'alim*, leaving the others fully liable as *shomrei sachar*.

### Simultaneity Principle

The second scenario is necessary, the *gemara* explains, in order to make Rav Papa's ruling defensible according to the opinion that *pshi'ah biv'alim chayav* — that even when the owner is working for the bailee, if the bailee was negligent he is still liable. On that opinion, the first case does not require any *iglai milta*: even if the baker were baking at the moment of theft, the bailee would be *chayav* for negligence. So the story only needs the "drinking break" resolution if we are operating according to the opinion that *pshi'ah biv'alim patur*. The second scenario, with its explicit wage-condition, is constructed to show that Rav Papa's embarrassment and subsequent vindication make sense even according to that stricter opinion.

### Contractor Examples

The operative principle is that *shmira biv'alim* requires not merely that an employment relationship exists, but that the owner of the object be *actually employed* by the bailee at the very moment that the *shmira* occurs. If I am your contractor fixing your house for a week and I ask you to watch my dog, you are a *shomer chinam* for my dog throughout the working hours — because at every moment that you are guarding my dog, I am working for you. But if the theft or loss occurs during my lunch break, at that precise moment I am not in your employ, and the *shmira biv'alim* exemption does not apply. Borderline cases involving breaks, meal times, and pauses in work are governed by whether the owner's labor was actually ongoing at the critical moment.

## Tall and Short Travelers

### Exchange Setup

The next case involves two travelers on the road. One was tall (*aricha*) and riding a donkey; he had a linen sheet (*sadina*). The other was short (*gutza*) and walking; he was wearing a heavy woolen coat (*sarbala*).

### Item Exchange Action

When they reached a river, the short traveler removed his woolen coat, placed it on the donkey, and took the tall traveler's linen *sadina* to wrap himself in while crossing. During the crossing, the water carried the *sadina* away — it was lost.

At the moment of the crossing, each traveler was effectively holding something belonging to the other: the short traveler had borrowed the tall traveler's *sadina*, while the tall traveler's donkey was carrying the short traveler's *sarbala*. The short traveler was thus simultaneously the *sho'el* of the *sadina* and in a position where his property was being "guarded" by the tall traveler.

### Rabba Liable

The case came before Rabba, who ruled the tall traveler *chayav* for the loss of the *sadina*. The reasoning was that the short traveler was guarding the tall traveler's *sarbala* while the tall traveler had the *sadina* — the short traveler was, in a sense, "in the employ" of the tall traveler, providing a service by safeguarding his coat in exchange for use of the *sadina*.

### Rabbis Objection

The Rabbis objected: this is a case of *sheila biv'alim* — a borrowing that occurs while the lender is in the employ of the borrower (or, analogously, while the owner of the borrowed object is simultaneously working for the borrower). Under that principle, the tall traveler should be *patur*, not *chayav*, for the loss of the *sadina*.

### Without Consent

Rabba's ruling was vindicated when the facts were clarified: *belo datei shaklei* — the short traveler had placed his *sarbala* on the donkey without asking, without the tall traveler's knowledge or consent. The tall traveler never agreed to guard the *sarbala*. Therefore, the arrangement by which the tall traveler was supposedly "working for" the short traveler — the arrangement that would have created *shmira biv'alim* — never existed from the tall traveler's perspective.

### Unwitting Bailee

This yields an important principle: a *shomer she-lo mi-da'at* — an unwitting bailee, someone whose "guardianship" was imposed without his knowledge — does not acquire the protected status that *shmira biv'alim* would otherwise confer. The tall traveler was never actually in the short traveler's employ in any legally cognizable sense, because he did not know the coat was on his donkey. Consequently, the *sheila biv'alim* analysis cannot apply, and the tall traveler is fully liable as a *sho'el* for the loss of the *sadina*. The *baraita*'s vindication through the *iglai milta* device — the same structure we saw in the perfume makers' case — teaches the rule: guardianship without knowledge creates no *shmira biv'alim* protection.

## Wrong Road Case

### Instruction Breach

A man hired a donkey to his friend. The owner instructed the renter explicitly: do not take the road by *nahar Pakod* (*d'ika maya* — because there is water there that will endanger the donkey), and go instead by the road of *Narash* (*d'leika maya* — where there is no water). The renter ignored the instruction and took the road by *nahar Pakod*.

### No Water Claim

The donkey died. When the renter came before the court, he argued: yes, I took the road by *nahar Pakod*, but there was no water there when I traveled it. The donkey did not die because of any water hazard. I essentially followed the spirit of the owner's instruction even if I took the wrong road; I was not exposed to the danger he warned me about.

### Miggo Analysis

Rava initially found this argument compelling on the basis of *miggo*: *mah li l'shaker* — why would the renter lie? If he wanted to fabricate an excuse, he could simply have told the court that he went by the road of *Narash* and the donkey died there for no discernible reason. Instead, he admitted to taking the forbidden road while claiming there was no water. The admission of the route combined with the denial of the hazard creates a *miggo*: since he could have claimed a more convenient lie and didn't, his more costly admission deserves credibility.

### Miggo Refutation

Abaye rejected this reasoning. *Mah li l'shaker b'makom eidim lo amrinan* — we do not apply the *miggo* principle when public knowledge contradicts the claim. The *miggo* argument presupposes that the alternative claim would have been believable. But here, everyone knows that *nahar Pakod* is filled with water. It is publicly established fact — *anan sahadei*.

### Public Knowledge

*Anan sahadei* — "we are all witnesses" — functions as a form of public knowledge so pervasive and certain that it overrides the logical inference of *miggo*. If the renter claims there was no water on the road of *nahar Pakod*, his claim is directly contradicted by common knowledge, and the *miggo* collapses. The Rif is noted to address this passage as well, and the principle articulated here — that *anan sahadei* overrides *miggo* in situations of bailment disputes where the facts are matters of common knowledge — is the operative ruling.

## Acceptance Statements

### Mishnah Put Down

A new *mishnah* presents three scenarios involving verbal acceptance or rejection of a guardianship obligation. In the first case, one person says to another, "guard this for me," and the other responds, "put it down in front of me (*hanach lefanai*)." The *mishnah* rules that this response creates the status of *shomer chinam*: the respondent has accepted the object for guarding, but only as an unpaid bailee.

### Rav Huna Rejection

Rav Huna introduces a variant: if instead of *hanach lefanai* the respondent says *hanach lefanecha* — "put it down in front of you" — that is not acceptance of any guardianship obligation at all. *Eino lo shomer chinam v'lo shomer sachar* — he is neither an unpaid bailee nor a paid bailee. The response "put it down in front of yourself" is a rejection, tantamount to saying: I am not taking responsibility for this.

### Unqualified Put

The *gemara* then poses a further question: *ibaya lehu hanach stam mai* — what is the status when someone simply says *hanach*, "put it down," without qualification? He says neither "in front of me" nor "in front of you." Is this an acceptance of guardianship, or is it effectively a rejection? The question is left open — *teiku* — and the *gemara* proceeds to seek resolution from other sources.

### Mishnah Contrast

A resolution is proposed by reading the *mishnah* itself as implicitly contrasting the two cases. Since the *mishnah* uses the specific phrase *hanach lefanai* — "put it in front of me" — and rules that this creates *shomer chinam* status, the implication (*ha stam v'lo klum*) might be that a mere *hanach* without qualification would be no different — or, conversely, from Rav Huna's ruling that *hanach lefanecha* is a rejection, one can infer that *hanach* alone — without the distancing "*lefanecha*" — constitutes acceptance as *shomer chinam*. The *gemara* concludes that *hanach stam* is functionally equivalent to *hanach lefanai*: a qualified acceptance of responsibility as unpaid bailee.

## Courtyard Guardianship

### Tannaitic Dispute

The *gemara* now considers a *machloket tannai'm* — a dispute between the Sages — on the related question of what happens when someone brings objects into another's courtyard.

### Rabbanan Position

The Rabbis hold: *im hichnis bireshut chayav* — if someone brings objects into the courtyard of another person with permission, the owner of the courtyard becomes a *shomer chinam* for those objects. *Deki amar lei o'ol v'antar lach* — when the owner of the courtyard says "come in," he is implicitly saying "come in and I will guard your things." Permission to enter the courtyard carries with it implicit acceptance of guardianship responsibility. The *ba'al hachatzer* therefore bears *shomer chinam* obligations for the items stored there, and if they are damaged due to negligence he is liable.

### Rabbi Position

Rabbi disagrees: *eino chayav ad she'yikabel alav ba'al habayit lishmor* — the owner of the house or courtyard incurs no guardianship liability unless he explicitly accepts it with a statement of guardianship. Merely granting permission to enter or to deposit items is not sufficient.

### Guarded Courtyard

The *gemara* explores two ways of explaining this dispute. The first explanation focuses on the nature of the courtyard. A *chatzer de-bat netirah* — a courtyard that is inherently guarded — is a place that is always under the owner's watch. When the owner of such a courtyard grants someone permission to place items there, his granting of permission carries an implicit guarantee of safety: the very act of saying "you may put things here" is understood as saying "and I, who guard this space, will guard your things as well." On this reading, the *rabbanan* and Rabbi agree that what matters is what the owner communicates by his permission; they differ on whether such a courtyard's inherent guardedness makes the implicit guarantee automatic.

### Permission to Enter

The second explanation approaches the dispute from the perspective of the permission itself. A courtyard requires the owner's permission to enter. When the owner grants permission to enter *and* to deposit items, the granting of that permission is itself a statement of assumed responsibility — *tiv venatar lach ka'amar lei*, "sit and I will guard for you." The owner could not have meant merely "enter" without any accompanying commitment, because in a guarded space, permission to enter implies the full range of hosting obligations. Rabbi, however, reads the permission as narrowly as possible: it grants access, nothing more, and explicit acceptance of guardianship must be stated separately.

### Marketplace Contrast

The contrast with a *shuk*, a marketplace or public space, illuminates the logic further. In a public market, there is no permission required to put something down. No one needs Moshe's authorization to place a package on the bench next to him. Therefore, when someone in the *shuk* says to another "put it down (*hanach*)," the words cannot mean merely "you have permission to set it here" — because such permission was never needed. The statement *hanach* in the public context can only mean "put it down and I will guard it." By this reasoning, a simple *hanach* in the *shuk* functions as acceptance of *shomer chinam* status, consistent with the *gemara*'s conclusion in the previous *sugya*.

### Location-Based Rules

The *gemara* thus arrives at a conclusion that turns on the nature of the location and the content of the permission granted. In a courtyard, where permission to enter is necessary and where the space is inherently guarded, granting permission carries an implicit guardianship commitment according to the *rabbanan*; Rabbi requires it to be made explicit. In a marketplace, where no permission is needed, the bare utterance *hanach* already implies acceptance. The *halacha* appears to follow Rabbi's view — that explicit acceptance of guardianship is necessary in the courtyard context — but both explanations offered by the *gemara* converge on the principle that different rules apply depending on whether the location is inherently guarded and whether the act of permission itself communicates a guardianship commitment.
