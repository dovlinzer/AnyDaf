# Hullin 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bird Simanim Review

### Simanim Recap

The present daf continues a multi-day discussion of the kosher and non-kosher status of birds. By way of review: the *gemara* concluded that any bird possessing none of the kosher *simanim* — or, stated differently, possessing all four non-kosher *simanim* — is definitively non-kosher. The *nesher* (the eagle, or more probably the griffon vulture) was cited as the clearest example. A bird possessing all four kosher *simanim* is definitively kosher. Where a bird exhibits a mixture of kosher and non-kosher *simanim*, its status depends on whether it appears on the Torah's enumerated list of forbidden species. The preceding session refined this framework further, analyzing specific birds with only one or two kosher *simanim* and asking whether, by process of elimination, one could determine that such a bird was nonetheless kosher. The general conclusion was that possessing all four kosher *simanim* is sufficient to establish permissibility, a ruling with real practical significance today.

### Swamp Hen & Mesorah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תַּרְנְגוֹלָא דְּאַגְמָא חַד מִשְּׁמוֹנָה סְפֵיקוֹת הוּא, וְהַיְינוּ מַרְדּוּ. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תַּרְנְגוֹלָא דְּאַגְמָא אֲסִירא, תַּרְנְגוֹלְתָּא דְּאַגְמָא שַׁרְיָא, וְסִימָנָיךְ – ״עַמּוֹנִי וְלֹא עַמּוֹנִית״. דְּרַשׁ מָרִימָר: תַּרְנְגוֹלְתָּא דְּאַגְמָא אֲסִירָא, חַזְיוּהָ דְּדָרְסָה וְאָכְלָה, וְהַיְינוּ גֵּירוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: The swamp rooster is one of the eight uncertain** cases, **and this is** the ***mardu*. Rav Pappa says: The swamp rooster is forbidden, but the swamphen is permitted. And your mnemonic** to remember this is the statement of the Sages with regard to the verse: "An Ammonite or a Moabite shall not enter into the assembly of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:4), that **an Ammonite man** is unfit to enter the assembly, **but not an Ammonite woman. Mareimar taught: The swamphen is forbidden,** because the Sages **saw that it claws** its prey **and eats** it. **And this is** the ***giruta*,** a non-kosher bird (see 109b).

A case raised at the end of the prior discussion deserves closer attention. The *gemara* cited the instance of the *tarnegolta de-agma*, the hen of the swamp, which had been presumed kosher on the basis of its apparent *simanim*. Upon subsequent observation, however, it was discovered that this bird was in fact a *dores* — a bird of prey — a characteristic that had not previously been detected. The ruling was accordingly reversed, and the bird was declared non-kosher.

From this brief episode, Rashi draws a sweeping conclusion: one cannot eat any bird without an established *mesorah*, a reliable tradition of identification. Since one of the kosher *simanim* is the absence of predatory behavior, and since this characteristic may not always be evident, any assumption of permissibility based solely on observed *simanim* remains vulnerable to being overturned by later discovery. Rashi's position is not stated explicitly in the *gemara* itself, and it raises the question regarding birds of the New World, such as the turkey, for which no *mesorah* existed at the time of first contact. Even within Rashi's framework, one might ask whether a centuries-long, well-documented record of a bird's behavior could substitute for traditional *mesorah* — particularly if it conclusively established that the bird was not a *dores*. The question is complicated further by observations, such as the fact that wild turkeys are known to consume rodents and small animals, which raises the subsidiary question of precisely how *dores* is to be defined.

### Tosafot: Birds of Prey

A further clarification is needed regarding a point that had been stated imprecisely in an earlier session. One might have inferred from the *gemara*'s discussion that a bird of prey could theoretically still be kosher under certain circumstances. Tosafot, however, is emphatic on this point and derives from an explicit statement in the *mishnah* that every bird of prey, without exception, is non-kosher. This is a categorical rule for Tosafot: *dores* status renders a bird definitively forbidden regardless of what other *simanim* it may possess. Conversely, if a bird is not a *dores* but exhibits some non-kosher *simanim* without all four, its status then depends on whether it figures in the Torah's list. This represents an important corrective to a reading that might have emerged from the *gemara* alone.

## Shakitna & Species ID

### Shakitna Characteristics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁקִיטָנָא, אֲרִיכֵי שָׁקֵי וְסוּמָּקֵי – שַׁרְיָא, וְסִימָנָיךְ מוּרְזְמָא. גּוּצֵי וְסוּמָּקֵי – אֲסִירִי, וְסִימָנָיךְ ״נָנוּס פָּסוּל״. אֲרִיכֵי שָׁקֵי וִירוּקֵּי – אֲסִירִי, וְסִימָנָיךְ ״יְרוּקִּין פְּסוּלִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says:** There are several types of ***shekitena*. The** **long-shanked red** ones are **permitted. And your mnemonic** to remember this is the ***murzema*** bird, which is similar in appearance and known to be kosher. The **little red** ones are **forbidden, and your mnemonic** for this is the *halakha* that **a dwarf** priest is **unfit** for Temple service. The **long-shanked green,** i.e., yellow, ones are **forbidden, and your mnemonic** for this is the mishna (56a): Innards that have turned **green** render an animal a *tereifa* and **unfit** for consumption.

The *gemara* at the second line of 63a opens with a statement of Rav Yehuda regarding a bird called the *shakitna*. Whether this bird is kosher or non-kosher depends on a set of observable characteristics. If it has *arichi shaki* — long legs, literally long thighs — and is *sumki*, reddish in color, it is *shari*, permissible. If, however, it has *gutzei*, short legs, even though it is reddish, it is *assiri*, forbidden. If it has long legs but is *yeroki* — green or yellow, depending on how that term is understood in context — it is likewise forbidden.

### Mnemonics: Murzma

Each ruling is accompanied by a mnemonic. For the long-legged, reddish bird that is permissible: *murzma murzma*, the name of a familiar kosher bird that was reddish and long-legged, providing a mental image of the permissible type. For the short-legged bird that is forbidden: *nanos pasul*, a midget is disqualified from performing the *avodah* in the *Beis Hamikdash*, since short stature constitutes a physical blemish. For the long-legged but greenish or yellowish bird: *yerokin pesulin*, recalling the rule that if a bird falls into fire and its internal organs turn greenish or yellowish, it is rendered a *treifa* and invalidated. Crucially, these are not reasons but memory devices — mnemonics designed to aid retention, not to supply legal justification.

### Mixed Simanim Birds

A broader difficulty underlies the entire discussion. If a bird has some kosher and some non-kosher *simanim*, its permissibility depends on whether it appears on the forbidden list — and that in turn depends on its name. This is why slight variations among similar-looking birds can matter: variations in leg length or coloration might be precisely what determines whether a bird falls under one species name or another, and thus whether it is included on the list.

### Species Definition

One might ask whether the variations described here — long versus short legs, reddish versus greenish coloration — genuinely distinguish different species or merely represent natural variation within a single species. The *gemara* does not address this directly, but one possibility is that these distinguishing traits correlate with behavioral differences, perhaps including whether the bird is in some sense a *dores*. Another possibility is simply that they are what define the species name, which is the operative category for determining the bird's listing status.

## Torah Birds ID

### Nesher ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״שָׁלָךְ״, זֶה הַשּׁוֹלֶה דָּגִים מִן הַיָּם. ״דּוּכִיפַת״, שֶׁהוֹדוֹ כָּפוּת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: דּוּכִיפַת, שֶׁהוֹדוֹ כָּפוּת, וְזֶהוּ שֶׁהֵבִיא שָׁמִיר לְבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says:** As for the ***shalakh*,** listed as a non-kosher bird (see Leviticus 11:17), **this is** the bird **that scoops [*sholeh*] fish out of the sea.** The ***dukhifat*** (see Leviticus 11:19) is the bird **whose comb** seems **bent [*hodo kafut*]** due to its thickness. The Gemara notes: **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: The ***dukhifat*** is the bird **whose comb** seems **bent, and this is** the bird **that brought the *shamir* to the Temple.** As recounted in tractate *Gittin* (68b), King Solomon required a unique worm called the *shamir* to carve stones of the Temple, as the verse states: "There was neither hammer nor ax nor any tool of iron heard in the house, while it was in building" (I Kings 6:7).

The *gemara* now turns to identifying the specific birds mentioned in the Torah's own list. This task carries halachic urgency: since the status of a bird with mixed *simanim* depends on whether it appears on the list, one must know what the listed names actually refer to. The *nesher* has already been mentioned throughout this discussion. Although it is conventionally translated as "eagle," Yehuda Feliks, in his authoritative work on the flora and fauna of the Torah, identifies it with the griffon vulture — the largest bird found in the Land of Israel — based on multiple converging indicators.

### Shalach ID

Rav Yehuda states that the *shalach* is *zeh ha-sholeh dagim min ha-yam* — the one that scoops up fish from the sea. The root *shal* is embedded in the name itself. As to the precise identification: Feliks suggests a fish owl; others propose the cormorant, a bird well known for diving into water to catch fish; Aryeh Kaplan in *The Living Torah* translates it as the cormorant. Artscroll's translation, both here and in its Torah commentary, declines to translate these bird names at all, so as not to mislead readers regarding the halachic implications. The caution is well founded, given the difficulty of mapping ancient nomenclature onto modern ornithological taxonomy.

### Duchifat & Shamir

The *duchifat* is identified through a play on its name: *she-hodo kaffut*, its glory or crest of feathers is folded or bent over. This likely refers to the hoopoe, a bird whose distinctive crown of feathers can be erected or folded flat. A *baraita* confirms this derivation and adds an aggadic note: *ve-zeh she-hevi shamir l'Beis Ha-Mikdash*. The *gemara* recalls the account in Tractate Gittin of the *shamir*, a worm capable of cutting through stone, which was needed for the construction of the *Beis Hamikdash* because the Torah prohibits using iron tools on the stones of the altar. The *duchifat* made use of the *shamir* to break open stones in order to access prey concealed within them, and thus served as the means by which the *shamir* was brought. Onkelos, in his Aramaic translation of the Torah, renders *duchifat* as "breaker of stones," reflecting this tradition.

### Yochanan's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, כִּי הֲוָה חָזֵי שָׁלָךְ, אָמַר: ״מִשְׁפָּטֶיךָ תְּהוֹם רַבָּה״. כִּי הֲוָה חָזֵי נְמָלָה, אָמַר: ״צִדְקָתְךָ כְּהַרְרֵי אֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts: **When Rabbi Yoḥanan would see a *shalakh*, he** would **say: "Your judgments are like the great deep"** (Psalms 36:7), as God exacts retribution even upon the fish in the sea. **When he would see an ant, he** would **say** the first half of the same verse: **"Your righteousness is like the mighty mountains,"** as God provides sustenance for the tiny ant just as He does for the largest creatures.

Rashi offers two interpretations of this pairing. The first, which he attributes to his teachers: the fish dwelling in the depths of the sea are nonetheless subject to divine judgment and cannot escape it; even the smallest creatures, the ants, receive divine sustenance, and this reflects God's great righteousness. The second interpretation, which Rashi himself prefers: the *mishpat*, the strict justice, is reflected in the *shalach*, which must labor intensely, diving into the sea for its food — it earns what it receives. The ant, by contrast, finds its food more easily, raiding a pantry or a picnic, as it were — and this reflects divine *tzedaka*, a more generous, charitable dispensation. Rashi himself notes a certain tension in the word *tzedaka* here, observing that *tzedek* and *mishpat* are closely related terms, often paired in Scripture.

## Ambiguous Birds

### Custom & Peres

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: לַקְנִי וּבָטְנִי – שַׁרְיָין, שַׁקְנַאי וּבָטְנַאי – מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לֶאֱכוֹל אוֹכְלִין, מְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לֶאֱכוֹל אֵין אוֹכְלִין. אַטּוּ בְּמִנְהֲגָא תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא? אִין, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּאַתְרָא דִּשְׁכִיחִי פֶּרֶס וְעׇזְנִיָּה, הָא בְּאַתְרָא דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי פֶּרֶס וְעׇזְנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says:** The ***laknei* and *batnei*** birds are **permitted.** As for the ***sakna'ei* and *batna'ei*** birds, in any **place that it is customary to eat** them, **one may eat** them; in any **place that it is customary not to eat** them, **one may not eat** them. The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that the matter** of whether it is permitted **depends on custom?** The Gemara responds: **Yes, but** it is **not difficult: This** place where they are forbidden is **a place where the *peres* and *ozniyya* are found.** Since they are similar to these birds, one must be concerned that people will confuse them, even though the *sakna'ei* and *batna'ei* are themselves kosher. **That** place where they are permitted is **a place where the *peres* and *ozniyya* are not found.**

The *gemara* shifts to a new statement: *likni u-vitni sharyin* — birds called *likni* and *bitni* are kosher. But what of *shiknai* and *bitnai*? The answer given is: *makom she-nahagu le-echol ochlin, makom she-nahagu she-lo le-echol ein ochlin* — where there is a local custom to eat these birds one may eat them; where the custom is to refrain, one may not. The *gemara* pushes back immediately: *atu be-minhaga talya milta?* Is this really a matter of custom? This is a legal question, not merely a matter of practice. The *gemara* answers: yes, and it is not difficult to explain.

### Peres ve-Ozniya

The key to understanding this ruling lies in the *peres* and *ozniya*, two species of non-kosher birds that Feliks identifies as variants of the vulture family. These birds are extremely rare in inhabited areas. Because they possess only a single kosher *siman*, and because they are so rarely encountered in settled areas, there is ordinarily no reason to worry that a bird one has identified as kosher might in fact be a *peres* or *ozniya*.

However, where *peres* and *ozniya* are locally common — *b'atra de-shechiach peres ve-ozniya* — the concern arises that a bird believed to be a kosher species might actually be one of these forbidden birds. In such a location, one must not eat the *shiknai* or *bitnai*, because they might be confused with the *peres* or *ozniya*. In a place where these non-kosher species are not found — *b'atra de-lo shechiach peres ve-ozniya* — there is no basis for concern, and one may eat the bird without restriction.

### Geography & ID

This raises a question regarding the formulation *where there is a custom to eat*. If the concern is purely geographic — the presence or absence of the *peres* and *ozniya* — why is it phrased as a matter of custom rather than of fact? Some *rishonim* address this by suggesting that perhaps one location had a *mesorah* regarding the bird's identity and another did not. But that explanation has a difficulty: if one location possessed a reliable *mesorah*, that *mesorah* should suffice for others as well, and the ruling should not appear to be a mere matter of local custom. The fact that it is framed in terms of *minhag* suggests that the distinction is indeed geographic, not testimonial.

## Abaye's Lists

### Kuai Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: קוּאֵי וְקָקוֹאֵי אֲסִירִי, קָקוֹאֲתָא שַׁרְיָא. בְּמַעְרְבָא מַלְקוּ עִילָּוַהּ, וְקָרוּ לַהּ תַּחְוָותָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** The birds called ***kevai* and *kakvai* are forbidden,** but the ***kakvata* is permitted.** Still, **in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they flog** one who eats it **on its** account, **and they call it *taḥveta*.**

Abaye now introduces his own list of specific birds. Birds called *kuai* and *kakuai* are forbidden. The *kakuasa*, however, is permissible.

### Regional Disputes

Strikingly, in *Eretz Yisrael* — *be-ma'arava* — the *kakuasa* was not merely considered forbidden but was considered so definitively forbidden that those who ate it were given *malkot*, lashes. Furthermore, the bird was known by a different name there, *tachvasa*. This radical discrepancy — a bird considered clearly permissible in one region and clearly forbidden in another, to the point of warranting physical punishment — is difficult to explain if the dispute hinged solely on the *simanim*. One would expect that the *simanim* would be equally observable in both locations. The more likely explanation is that the dispute concerned whether this bird was subsumed under the name of a forbidden species on the Torah's list, with the different regional nomenclature (*kakuasa* versus *tachvasa*) perhaps driving different legal conclusions.

## Tinshemet

### Bird & Sheretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״תִּנְשֶׁמֶת״ בָּאוָת שֶׁבָּעוֹפוֹת. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בָּאוָת שֶׁבָּעוֹפוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בָּאוָת שֶׁבַּשְּׁרָצִים? אָמַרְתָּ: צֵא וּלְמַד מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה נִדְרֶשֶׁת בָּהֶן, דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָנוֹ. בְּמָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? בְּעוֹפוֹת, אַף כָּאן בְּעוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The ***tinshemet*,** listed in the Torah as non-kosher (see Leviticus 11:18), is the ***ba'ut* among birds.** One might ask: **Do you say** that it is **the *ba'ut* among birds, or is it only the *ba'ut* among creeping animals?** The *tinshemet* is also listed among the creeping animals (see Leviticus 11:30). **Say: Go out and learn from the thirteen hermeneutical principles,** of which one is: **A matter derived from its context. What are the** adjacent **verses speaking about?** They are speaking **about birds. So too here,** the word *tinshemet* is referring **to birds.**

The *gemara* now turns to the *tinshemet*, one of the birds listed in the Torah's roster of forbidden species. A *baraita* defines it as *ba'ut she-ba-ofot* — the "screecher" or "howler" among birds. Rashi elsewhere identifies this term as meaning something that shrieks, and renders the *tinshemet* variously as an owl or a bat, with some inclination toward the bat. The word *tinshemet* also appears in the Torah's list of forbidden *sheratzim*, the creeping creatures. Rashi there identifies that creature as a mole, and notes the resemblance: a bat, he observes, looks somewhat like a mole with wings, which may explain why the same name attaches to both.

There is also a possible textual variant to consider: the word *ba'ut* may in some versions be *bava'at*, reflecting the Aramaic *bava'at* used by Onkelos in translating this word, which involves the reversal of an *alef* and a *vav*. Feliks identifies the *tinshemet* bird as a barn screech owl and the *tinshemet* *sheretz* as a chameleon. One etymology connects the name to the word *neshamah* — breath — suggesting that the creature breathes in a distinctive manner; another, cited in the *Arukh*, takes *ba'ut* to mean something fearful or repugnant, suggesting that both animals — the owl or bat, and the mole or chameleon — inspire a particular revulsion.

### Context Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי גַּבֵּי שְׁרָצִים כְּהַאי גַּוְונָא: ״תִּנְשֶׁמֶת״ בָּאוָת שֶׁבַּשְּׁרָצִים. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בָּאוָת שֶׁבַּשְּׁרָצִים, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בָּאוָת שֶׁבָּעוֹפוֹת? אָמַרְתָּ: צֵא וּלְמַד מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה נִדְרֶשֶׁת בָּהֶן, דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָנוֹ – בְּמָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? בִּשְׁרָצִים, אַף כָּאן בִּשְׁרָצִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It is also taught** in a *baraita* **in this way with regard to** the *tinshemet* listed among the **creeping animals:** The ***tinshemet*** here is the ***ba'ut* among creeping animals.** One might ask: **Do you say** that it is the ***ba'ut* among creeping animals, or it is only** the ***ba'ut* among birds? Say: Go out and learn from the thirteen hermeneutical principles,** of which one is: **A matter derived from its context. What are the** adjacent **verses speaking about?** They are speaking **about creeping animals. So too here,** the word *tinshemet* is referring **to creeping animals.**

The *baraita* poses a question: since the word *tinshemet* appears in both the list of forbidden birds and the list of forbidden *sheratzim*, how do we know which is being referred to in each context? The answer invokes one of the thirteen hermeneutical principles — *davar ha-lamed me-inyano*, a matter is interpreted according to its context. When *tinshemet* appears in the portion dealing with birds, it refers to a bird; when it appears in the portion dealing with *sheratzim*, it refers to a *sheretz*. This same reasoning is then applied in the reverse direction by an analogous *baraita* dealing with *sheratzim*: one might have thought the *tinshemet* mentioned there refers to the bird of the same name, but context establishes that it refers to a creeping creature.

### Tinshemet = Kipuf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בָּאוָת שֶׁבָּעוֹפוֹת – קִיפוֹף, בָּאוָת שֶׁבַּשְּׁרָצִים – קוּרְפְּדַאי. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״קָאָת״ – זוֹ הַקּוּק, ״רָחָם״ – זוֹ שְׁרַקְרַק.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** The ***ba'ut* among birds** is commonly called the ***kifof*.** The ***ba'ut* among creeping animals** is commonly called the ***kurpedai*. Rav Yehuda says:** As for the ***ka'at*** listed in the Torah as non-kosher (see Leviticus 11:18), **this is the** bird called **a *kuk*.** As for the ***raḥam*, this is** the ***sherakrak*.**

Abaye then provides identifications in the vernacular known to his contemporaries. The *tinshemet* among birds is the *kipuf*, which again Rashi renders as either a bat or an owl. The *tinshemet* among *sheratzim* is the *korpadai*, which Rashi identifies as a mole.

Rav Yehuda then identifies the *ka'at* — another bird on the list — as the *kuk* or *kik*, which Feliks identifies as the tawny owl. Rashi supplies a French gloss whose precise meaning is difficult to recover. The *racham* is identified as the *sherakrak*, the shrieker or crier.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ רָחָם? כֵּיוָן שֶׁבָּא רָחָם בָּאוּ רַחֲמִים לְעוֹלָם. אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: וְהוּא דְּיָתֵיב אַמִּידֵּה וְעָבֵיד ״שְׁרַקְרַק״, וּגְמִירִי דְּאִי יָתֵיב אַאַרְעָא וְשָׁרֵיק – אֲתָא מְשִׁיחָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶשְׁרְקָה לָהֶם וַאֲקַבְּצֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Why is it called the *raḥam*?** Because **when the *raḥam* comes** to Eretz Yisrael, **mercy [*raḥamim*] comes to the world,** as it appears at the beginning of the rainy season. **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said: And it is** a sign of rain only **when it sits on something and makes a *sherakrak*** sound. And it **is learned** as a tradition **that if it sits on the ground and hisses [*veshareik*],** this is a sign that **the Messiah is coming, as it is stated: "I will hiss [*eshreka*] for them, and gather them"** (Zechariah 10:8).

Rabbi Yochanan then asks: why is this bird called *racham*? Because *keivan she-ba racham ba'u rakhamim la-olam* — when the *racham* arrives, compassion (*rakhamim*) comes to the world. The bird is a harbinger of the rainy season, and rain in the Land of Israel is understood as a divine expression of mercy and providential care. Identifying the specific bird has proven difficult: Feliks says it is an Egyptian vulture; Aryeh Kaplan and some traditional commentaries suggest the magpie; Artscroll has proposed the roller, partly on the basis of Rashi's description. Rav Bibi bar Abaye adds a qualification: the sign of impending rain is specifically when the bird sits on something elevated and performs its characteristic cry. But there is an even more extraordinary tradition: if the bird sits on the ground itself and cries out, that is a sign not merely of rain but of the coming of *mashiach*, as the verse in the prophets says *eshreka lahem va-akabtzem* — "I will call out to them and gather them." Rav Adda bar Shimi raised an apparent objection to Mar bar Rav Idai: there was a case of a bird sitting among cabbages and crying out, and rather than presaging the *mashiach*, a boulder came and crushed its head. The response: *hahu kadba hava* — that was a false messenger, a false herald. The boulder crashing down was itself the evidence that the signal was fraudulent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר שִׁימִי לְמָר בַּר רַב אִידַאי: וְהָא הָהוּא דְּיָתֵיב בֵּי כְרָבָא וּשְׁרַק, וַאֲתָא גָּלָל אַפְסְקֵיהּ לְמוֹחֵיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָהוּא בַּיָּידָא הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Shimi said to Mar bar Rav Idai: But** wasn't there **a certain** *raḥam* **that sat on a plowed field and hissed, and a stone came and broke its head?** Mar bar Rav Idai **said to him: That** *raḥam* **was a liar** and was punished for prophesying falsely.

## Raven & Kite ID

### Orev Varieties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עוֹרֵב״ – זֶה עוֹרֵב, ״אֶת כׇּל עוֹרֵב״ – לְהָבִיא עוֹרֵב הָעִמְקִי, ״לְמִינוֹ״ – לְהָבִיא עוֹרֵב הַבָּא בְּרָאשֵׁי יוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: "Every *orev* after its kinds" (Leviticus 11:15), **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: As for the ***orev*, this is the crow.** When the verse states: **"Every *orev*,"** this serves **to include the valley crow** as non-kosher. And the verse states: **"After its kinds," to include the crow that comes at the heads of pigeons.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״עוֹרֵב״ – זֶה עוֹרֵב. אַטּוּ קַמַּן קָאֵי? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: ״עוֹרֵב״ – זֶה עוֹרֵב אוּכָּמָא, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״קְוֻצּוֹתָיו תַּלְתַּלִּים שְׁחֹרוֹת כָּעוֹרֵב״. הָעִמְקִי – חִיוָּורָא, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּמַרְאֵהוּ עָמֹק מִן הָעוֹר״, כְּמַרְאֵה חַמָּה הָעֲמוּקָּה מִן הַצֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The Master said:** As for the ***orev*, this is the crow. Is that to say** that the particular crow **stands before us,** such that one immediately knows which one it is? **Rather, say:** As for the ***orev*, this is the black crow, and so** the verse **states: "His locks are curled, and black as a crow"** (Song of Songs 5:11). The Gemara continues to explain the *baraita*: **The valley** crow **[*ha'amaki*]** is the **white** crow. **And so** the verse **states** with regard to leprosy: **"If the appearance thereof be deeper [*amok*] than the skin"** (Leviticus 13:30), and the Sages explained: **As the appearance of** an area lit by the **sun, which** seems **deeper than the shade,** which appears to cover it. There is therefore an association between a valley and the color white.

The *gemara* now proceeds to a *baraita* identifying further species. *Orev zeh orev* — the *orev* is the *orev*. The *gemara* finds this unhelpfully circular, noting that the *baraita* ought to be more specific since it is distinguishing the canonical *orev* from related subspecies. The *gemara* therefore amends the reading: the *orev* referred to is specifically *orev uchma*, the black raven or crow, as implied by the verse in *Shir Ha-Shirim*: *kvutsotav taltalim shekhorot ke-orev*, "his locks are curled, black as a raven."

The *orev ha-amaki*, "the one of the valleys" or "the deep one," is described as white or whitish. The connection to the word *amok* (deep) here draws on a verse concerning the *tsara'at* affliction: a leprous spot whose appearance is *amok min ha-or*, deeper than the surrounding skin. Rashi and other commentators explain that a lighter-colored spot appears visually deeper than darker skin surrounding it — a claim that the Ramban famously challenges, noting that it contradicts ordinary visual experience, where a darker area appears closer and a lighter area further away. The *gemara* draws an analogy: the appearance of sunlit pavement appears deeper, further receding from the viewer, than the shade nearby — though this too is a counterintuitive claim that resists easy verification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹרֵב הַבָּא בְּרָאשֵׁי יוֹנִים, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא תֵּימָא דְּאָתֵי בְּרֵישׁ יוֹנֵי, אֶלָּא דְּדָמֵי רֵישֵׁיהּ לִדְיוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **the crow that comes at the heads of pigeons, Rav Pappa said: Do not say** that the *baraita* means **that it comes at the head of pigeons,** i.e., it dwells with them; **rather,** it means that this crow's **head resembles that of a pigeon.**

The third variety, *orev ha-ba be-roshei yonim*, might seem to mean a raven that flies at the head of a flock of doves. Rav Pappa corrects this: *de-dami reshei le-yona*, its head resembles that of a dove.

Raven and crow are not the same species, though they are closely related. Feliks identifies the *orev afur* as the hooded crow and the *orev shachor* as the raven — and it is possible that exactly these two categories, one darker and one lighter, correspond to the *orev uchma* and *orev ha-amaki* discussed in the *gemara*.

### Hatz & Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הַנֵּץ״ – זֶה הַנֵּץ, ״לְמִינֵהוּ״ – לְהָבִיא אֶת בַּר חִירְיָא. מַאי בַּר חִירְיָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שׁוּרִינְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: "And the *netz* after its kinds" (Leviticus 11:16), **the Sages taught:** As for **the *netz*, this is the hawk.** The verse states: **"After its kinds," to include** the bird called ***bar ḥireya*.** The Gemara asks: **What is the *bar ḥireya*? Abaye said:** It is the bird commonly called the ***shurineka*.**

The *baraita* then addresses the *netz*, the sparrowhawk according to Feliks. The text states simply *zeh ha-netz* — this is the *netz* — and then adds that its species (*l'minehu*) includes the *bar chirya*, a related species. Abaye identifies the *bar chirya* in the vernacular as the *shurinka*.

### Chasida = Daya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״הַחֲסִידָה״ – זוֹ דַּיָּה לְבָנָה. לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ חֲסִידָה? שֶׁעוֹשָׂה חֲסִידוּת עִם חַבְרוֹתֶיהָ. ״הָאֲנָפָה״ – זוֹ דַּיָּה רַגְזָנִית. לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ אֲנָפָה? שֶׁמְּנָאָפֶת עִם חַבְרוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: "And the *ḥasida*, and the *anafa* after its kinds" (Leviticus 11:19), **Rav Yehuda says:** As for **the *ḥasida*, this is the white *dayya*. And why is it called *ḥasida*? Since it performs charity [*ḥasidut*] for its fellows,** giving them from its own food. As for **the *anafa*, this is the irritable *dayya*. And why is it called *anafa*? Since it quarrels [*mena'efet*] with its fellows.**

Rav Yehuda identifies the *chasida* as the *daya levana*, the white *daya*, or white kite. This identification immediately introduces a terminological complexity that will drive the remainder of the day's discussion, since the Torah's list includes both *daya* and *ayah*, and the *gemara* will need to determine how these terms relate to one another. Feliks identifies the *chasida* as the stork, while identifying the *da'ah* and *dayah* as the black kite — which would make the *chasida* and the *dayah* distinct species, though both fall under the general family name.

### Chasida Meaning

Why is this bird called *chasida*, a name derived from the root *chesed*, kindness? Because *she-osa chasidut im chavroteha* — it performs acts of kindness with its companions, sharing its food with members of its own group. A well-known homiletical observation notes the apparent paradox: if the reason non-kosher birds are forbidden is because they embody predatory or morally deficient traits, why is this bird — which is explicitly named for its kindness — among the forbidden species? The answer given is that its *chesed* is confined to its own kind; it shares food only with members of its own flock, not with outsiders. Kindness that extends only to one's own circle, rooted in group interest rather than genuine moral concern, is not true *chesed* — and indeed may be understood as a form of self-interest in communal form.

### Anafa Meaning

The *anafa* is identified as the *daya ragzanit*, the angry *daya*. Why is it called *anafa*? Because *she-mena'efet im chavroteha* — it quarrels with or becomes enraged at its companions. A textual variant — *she-mna'anefet*, from the root *alef-nun-peh*, meaning anger — may actually be more coherent with the name *anafa*, since *ve-hi'anef* means to become wrathful (as in Moshe's words that God became angry with him). The current received text, *mna'efet*, carries the meaning of adultery, which is also attested as a behavior among certain birds — but the linguistic connection to *anafa* is cleaner if one reads *mna'anefet*.

## 24 Birds Count

### Count Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב חָנָן בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר רַב: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה עוֹפוֹת טְמֵאִין הֵן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: דְּהֵיכָא? אִי דְּוַיִּקְרָא – עֶשְׂרִים הָווּ, אִי דְּמִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה – עֶשְׂרִים וְחַד הָווּ, וְכִי תֵּימָא דָּאָה דִּכְתִיבָא בְּוַיִּקְרָא וְלָא כְּתִיבָא בְּמִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה שִׁדְיַיהּ עֲלַיְיהוּ – אַכַּתִּי עֶשְׂרִין וּתְרֵין הָווּ!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥanan bar Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav Ḥanan, son of Rava, says** that **Rav says: There are twenty-four non-kosher birds. Rav Ḥanan bar Rav Ḥisda said to Rav Ḥisda,** his father: **From where** in the Torah is this number obtained? **If** you are referring to the list **of Leviticus** (11:13–19), **there are** only **twenty** birds listed there. **If** you are referring to the list **of Deuteronomy** (14:12–18), **there are** only **twenty-one** there. **And if you would say: Add** the ***da'a*, which is written in Leviticus but is not written in Deuteronomy, to** the others in Deuteronomy, **still there are** only **twenty-two.**

Rav Chanan bar Rav Chisda cites a tradition in the name of Rav Chisda, who cites Rav, which holds that there are twenty-four species of non-kosher birds. Now that the *gemara* has done its best to identify what is on the list, it turns to the problem of actually counting to twenty-four and resolving apparent discrepancies in the number by conflating certain species.

### List Discrepancy

Rav Chanan, who had transmitted this tradition from his father, turns back to ask him directly: *v'heicha?* How do you actually arrive at twenty-four? If one counts the names in the list in Vayikra, there are twenty. If one counts the list in Mishneh Torah — the list in Devarim which we encountered in the preceding day's portion — there are twenty-one. Even if one adds the one bird, *da'ah*, that appears in Vayikra but not in Mishneh Torah, and adds it to the twenty-one in Devarim, the total is only twenty-two. How does one reach twenty-four?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּאִמָּךְ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: ״לְמִינָהּ״ ״לְמִינָהּ״ ״לְמִינוֹ״ ״לְמִינֵהוּ״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע. אִי הָכִי עֶשְׂרִין וְשֵׁית הָווּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דָּאָה וְרָאָה אַחַת הִיא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ תַּרְתֵּי אִינּוּן,
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: This** is what **your mother's father,** Rav Ḥanan, son of Rava, **said in the name of Rav:** The phrases **"after its kinds," "after its kinds," "after its kinds,"** and **"after its kinds,"** that appear in each list indicate additional cases. **Here,** then, **are four** more. The Gemara objects: **If so, there are twenty-six,** not twenty-four. **Abaye said:** The ***da'a*** mentioned in Leviticus **and** the ***ra'a*** mentioned in Deuteronomy **are one** bird. **As, if it enters your mind that they are two** different birds,

His father responds with what he says was transmitted from Rav: the word *leminehu* ("of its kind"), which appears multiple times in the lists, carries halachic weight, extending each category to include related species. Counting the instances of *leminehu* in Devarim — four occurrences — and adding them to the base count of twenty yields twenty-four. (The *Toldot Aharon* cites three from Vayikra and one from Devarim, but the *gemara* here is counting from Devarim.)

### Da'ah = Ra'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי מִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה לְאוֹסוֹפֵי הוּא דַּאֲתָא, מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דִּכְתִיב ״דָּאָה״ וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דִּכְתִיב ״רָאָה״ וְלָא כְּתִיב ״דָּאָה״? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִין רָאָה וְדָאָה אַחַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** one might ask: **Since** it is assumed that the list in **Deuteronomy comes to add** to the list in Leviticus, **what is different here,** in Leviticus, **that it is written: "*Da'a*,"** and **what is different here,** in Deuteronomy, **that it is written: "*Ra'a*," and *da'a* is not written? Rather, conclude from** the presence of each on only one list that the ***ra'a* and *da'a* are one species.**

The *gemara* immediately objects: if there are twenty-one base species in Devarim and one adds the *da'ah* from Vayikra, that is twenty-two base species, plus four *leminehus*, which is twenty-six — not twenty-four. How does one return to twenty-four?

Abaye answers: *da'ah ve-ra'ah achat hi* — the *da'ah* and the *ra'ah* are one and the same species. The alignment of the two lists makes this easy to see: Vayikra has *da'ah* and *ayah*, while Devarim has *ra'ah*, *ayah*, and *dayah*. The *da'ah* and *ra'ah* occupy the same position on their respective lists, and the words differ only in that one begins with a *dalet* and the other with a *resh*. They are different names for the same bird.

### Mishneh Torah Adds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי עֶשְׂרִין וְחַמְשָׁה הָווּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁרָאָה וְדָאָה אַחַת הִיא, כָּךְ אַיָּה וְדַיָּה אַחַת הִיא. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ תַּרְתֵּי אִינּוּן, מִכְּדֵי מִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה לְאוֹסוֹפֵי הוּא דַּאֲתָא, מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דִּכְתִיב ״לְמִינָהּ״ אַאַיָּה, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דִּכְתִיב ״לְמִינָהּ״ אַדַּיָּה? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אַיָּה וְדַיָּה אַחַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But still, there are twenty-five** birds, not twenty-four. **Abaye said: Just as** the ***ra'a* and *da'a* are one** species, **so** too, the ***ayya* and** the ***dayya*,** the latter of which is mentioned only in Deuteronomy, **are one** species. **As, if it enters your mind that they are two** different species, one might ask: **Since** it is assumed that the list in **Deuteronomy comes to add** to the list in Leviticus, **what is different here,** in Leviticus, **that it is written: "After its kinds," about** the ***ayya*,** prohibiting some other kind of *ayya*, **and what is different there,** in Deuteronomy, **that it is written: "After its kinds," about** the ***dayya*?** Why is the *ayya* not mentioned? **Rather, learn from** the use of the same phrase with regard to the ***ayya* and *dayya*** that they **are one** species.

This conflation is further supported by a structural principle: *Mishneh Torah le-osufi hu de-ata* — the list in Devarim is understood as coming to add to the list in Vayikra, not to diminish it. That is, everything in the Vayikra list must also be present in the Devarim list, and the Devarim list may only expand upon it. If *da'ah* and *ra'ah* were distinct species, then since Devarim replaces *da'ah* with *ra'ah* and does not include both, the Devarim list would contain *less* than the Vayikra list — which violates this principle. Therefore, they must be the same species. The Devarim list simply uses a different name for the same bird — presumably so that someone who heard the bird called by one name would not think that only that name was forbidden while the other was permissible.

### Aya = Daya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁאַיָּה וְדַיָּה אַחַת הִיא, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִיכְתַּב אַיָּה וְדַיָּה? כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֶקְרָא אֲנִי אַיָּה, דַּיָּה לָמָּה נֶאֶמְרָה? כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּתֵּן פִּתְחוֹן פֶּה לְבַעַל דִּין לַחְלוֹק, שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אַתָּה קוֹרֵא אַיָּה וְהוּא קוֹרֵא דַּיָּה, אַתָּה קוֹרֵא דַּיָּה וְהוּא קוֹרֵא אַיָּה, לְכָךְ כָּתַב בְּמִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה: ״וְהָרָאָה וְאֶת הָאַיָּה וְהַדַּיָּה לְמִינָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that** the ***ayya* and *dayya* are one** species, **why did** the Torah need **to write** both ***ayya* and *dayya*** in Deuteronomy? The Gemara responds: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** Given that the two are one species, **I will read *ayya*** and know that it is forbidden. **Why is *dayya* stated?** It is **so as not to give a claim to a litigant to disagree,** and **it should not occur** that **you call** it **an *ayya* and he calls** it **a *dayya*** and eats it. Likewise, the Torah did not write only *dayya* so that it will not occur that **you call** it **a *dayya* and he calls** it **an *ayya*** and eats it. **Therefore,** the Torah **writes in Deuteronomy: "And the *ra'a*, and the *ayya*, and the *dayya* after its kinds"** (Deuteronomy 14:13). Consequently, both the list in Leviticus and that in Deuteronomy enumerate twenty-four birds, in accordance with the statement attributed to Rav.

Abaye continues: just as *ra'ah* and *da'ah* are the same, so too *ayah* and *dayah* are the same. This is a bolder conflation: both *ayah* and *dayah* appear on the same list in Devarim — *ra'ah*, *ayah*, *dayah* — so here the Torah uses two different names for the same species within a single list.

The proof again comes from the principle of addition. In Vayikra, the word *leminah* ("of its kind") is appended to *ayah*, indicating that the *ayah* category is broad and encompasses related species. In Devarim, *leminah* is attached to *dayah*, but *not* to *ayah*. If *ayah* and *dayah* are distinct species, then the Devarim list has applied *leminah* to *dayah* while removing it from *ayah*, leaving *ayah* as a narrower category than it was in Vayikra. That would mean Devarim reduces the scope of a prohibition established in Vayikra — again violating the addition principle. The only solution is that *ayah* and *dayah* are the same species, and so it makes no difference to which of them *leminah* is attached; they are interchangeable.

Why, then, does the Torah use two names for the same species within a single list? The *gemara* cites Rebbi: *ekra ani ayah, dayah lama ne'emar?* Since *ayah* was already stated, why was it necessary to add *dayah*? The answer: *kedei she-lo titen pitchon peh le-va'al din lachlok* — so as not to give grounds for a disputant to draw distinctions. One person calls the bird *ayah*, another calls it *dayah*, and the disputant claims that only one of those names appears in the Torah's prohibition. By including both names, the Torah forecloses that argument and makes clear that whatever one calls this bird, it is forbidden.

### Addition Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: לָמָּה נִשְׁנוּ? בִּבְהֵמָה – מִפְּנֵי הַשְּׁסוּעָה, וּבָעוֹפוֹת – מִפְּנֵי הָרָאָה. מַאי לָאו, מִדִּבְהֵמָה דְּהָתָם לְאוֹסוֹפֵי, עוֹפוֹת נָמֵי לְאוֹסוֹפֵי? לָא, הָתָם לְאוֹסוֹפֵי, הָכָא לְפָרוֹשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Why** is the list **of** non-kosher **animals** in Leviticus **repeated?** It is **due to** the necessity of adding **the *shesua*** (Deuteronomy 14:7), which was not listed in Leviticus. **And** the list **of** non-kosher **birds** is repeated **due to the *ra'a*. What, is it not** understood **from** the fact **that** the extra list of **animals there,** in Deuteronomy, is **to add** animals, that the list of **birds** is **also** repeated **to add** birds? The Gemara responds: **No, there,** i.e., with regard to animals, the list is repeated **to add,** but **here,** with regard to birds, it is repeated only **to explain.**

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from the *baraita* that discusses why the Torah repeated the list in Devarim at all. By mammals, the repetition was to introduce the *shisua*, a novel animal — an apparently new species of cloven-hoofed creature — thereby genuinely adding to the Vayikra list. By birds, the *baraita* states that the repetition was *mipnei ha-ra'ah* — because of the *ra'ah*, implying that the *ra'ah* is itself a new species not found in Vayikra. This directly contradicts Abaye's conflation of *ra'ah* and *da'ah* as a single species.

The *gemara* resolves this: by mammals, the repetition was genuinely additive (*le-osufi*); by birds, however, the repetition was explanatory (*le-frushei*). The Devarim list does not introduce the *ra'ah* as a new species but clarifies that what Vayikra called *da'ah*, Devarim refers to as *ra'ah* — they are the same creature, and the second mention serves to prevent future ambiguity or legal dispute.

## Rav Abbahu

### Ra'ah = Aya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: רָאָה זוֹ אַיָּה, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ רָאָה? שֶׁרוֹאָה בְּיוֹתֵר, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״נָתִיב לֹא יְדָעוֹ עָיִט וְלֹא שְׁזָפַתּוּ עֵין אַיָּה״, תָּנָא: עוֹמֶדֶת בְּבָבֶל וְרוֹאָה נְבֵלָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the opinion that the *da'a* and *ra'a* are one species, and that the *ayya* and *dayya* are another species, **differs from** the opinion of **Rabbi Abbahu, as Rabbi Abbahu says:** The ***ra'a* is** the ***ayya*. And why is it called** the ***ra'a*? Since it sees [*ro'ah*] most** vividly. **And so** the verse **states: "That path no bird of prey knows, neither has the eye of the *ayya* seen it"** (Job 28:7). And a Sage **taught:** The *ra'a* can **stand in Babylonia and see a carcass in Eretz Yisrael.**

Rav Abbahu offers an alternative harmonization that diverges significantly from Abaye's. Where Abaye identified *da'ah*/*ra'ah* as one pair and *ayah*/*dayah* as another, Rav Abbahu says: *ra'ah zu ayah* — the *ra'ah* is the *ayah*. His basis is etymological and descriptive: why is this bird called *ra'ah* (which can mean "seeing")? Because *she-ro'ah bi-yoter* — it can see at an extraordinary distance. A verse from Iyov lends support: *nesiv lo yeda'o ayit ve-lo shezafatto ein ayah* — "a path unknown to the *ayit* (vulture), unseen by the eye of the *ayah*" — invoking the *ayah*'s renowned visual acuity. Thus the name *ra'ah* is simply a descriptor applied to the *ayah* because of this characteristic, and the two names refer to a single bird. A *baraita* confirms the remarkable sharpness of this bird's vision: *omedet be-Bavel ve-ro'ah neveilah be-Eretz Yisrael* — it can stand in Babylonia and spot a carcass all the way in the Land of Israel, then swoop down to consume it. This aligns with what we know of large raptors and vultures, which soar at great heights and can detect carrion from extraordinary distances.

### Addition Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרָאָה הַיְינוּ אַיָּה, מִכְּלָל דְּדָאָה לָאו הַיְינוּ רָאָה. מִכְּדֵי מִשְׁנֵה תוֹרָה לְאוֹסוֹפֵי הוּא דַּאֲתָא, מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דִּכְתִיב ״דָּאָה״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״דָּאָה״? אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דָּאָה וְרָאָה וְאַיָּה אַחַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses Rabbi Abbahu's statement: **Since** the ***ra'a* is** the same as the ***ayya*, by inference,** one may conclude **that** the ***da'a* is not the** same as the ***ra'a*;** otherwise, there are not twenty-four non-kosher birds. But **since** it is assumed that the list in **Deuteronomy comes to add** to the list in Leviticus, **what is different here,** in Leviticus, **that it is written: "*Da'a*,"** and **what is different there,** in Deuteronomy, **that *da'a* is not written? Rather,** must one **not conclude from** the discrepancy that the two are the same? If so, one must conclude that according to Rabbi Abbahu, the ***da'a* and *ra'a* and *ayya* are** all **one** species.

The *gemara* examines Rav Abbahu's position against the same addition principle it applied to Abaye. If *ra'ah* and *ayah* are the same, then Rav Abbahu implicitly leaves *da'ah* and *ra'ah* as distinct species — he has not claimed that *da'ah* equals *ra'ah*, only that *ra'ah* equals *ayah*. But this creates the same problem: Vayikra has *da'ah*, Devarim does not — it has *ra'ah* in its place. If they are not the same, then Devarim's list is missing something from Vayikra's, violating the addition principle. Rav Abbahu must therefore also accept that *da'ah* and *ra'ah* are the same — and since *ra'ah* equals *ayah*, it follows that all three, *da'ah*, *ra'ah*, and *ayah*, are the same species.

### All One Species

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּרָאָה הַיְינוּ אַיָּה, מִכְּלַל דְּדַיָּה לָאו הַיְינוּ אַיָּה. מַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דִּכְתִיב ״לְמִינֵהוּ״ אַאַיָּה, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״לְמִינֵהוּ״ אַאַיָּה אֶלָּא אַדַּיָּה? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דָּאָה וְרָאָה דַּיָּה וְאַיָּה אַחַת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, **from** the fact that Rabbi Abbahu holds that the ***ra'a* is** the same as the ***ayya*, by inference,** one may conclude that the ***dayya* is not** the same as the ***ayya*.** But if so, one may ask again: **What is different there,** in Leviticus, **that it is written: "After its kinds," about** the ***ayya*, and what is different here,** in Deuteronomy, **that it is not written: "After its kinds," about** the ***ayya* but about** the ***dayya*? Rather,** the *ayya* and *dayya* must be one species. And one may **learn from** the combination of the two disputes that according to Rabbi Abbahu, the ***da'a* and *ra'a*, *dayya* and *ayya* are** all **one** species. Consequently, according to Rabbi Abbahu, there are only twenty-three non-kosher species.

The *gemara* then presses further. If *ra'ah* and *ayah* are the same, then presumably *dayah* remains a distinct species. But we saw in Abaye's discussion that *ayah* and *dayah* must also be the same, on account of the *leminah* problem: Vayikra attaches *leminah* to *ayah*; Devarim attaches it only to *dayah*; they cannot be distinct, or Devarim would narrow the *ayah* category below what Vayikra established. Therefore, Rav Abbahu too must conclude that *dayah* is the same as *ayah* — and since *ayah* equals *ra'ah* which equals *da'ah*, all four names — *da'ah*, *ra'ah*, *ayah*, *dayah* — refer to a single species.

This means Rav Abbahu's position yields a list of only twenty-three non-kosher species, one fewer than the standard count of twenty-four. By combining four names into one species rather than two pairs of two, he arrives at a smaller total than the framework Abaye constructed.

## Kosher Infinite

### Infinite Varieties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵאָה עוֹפוֹת טְמֵאִין יֵשׁ בַּמִּזְרָח, וְכוּלָּן מִין ״אַיָּה״ הֵן. תָּנֵי אֲבִימִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שְׁבַע מֵאוֹת מִינֵי דָגִים הֵן, וּשְׁמוֹנֶה מֵאוֹת מִינֵי חֲגָבִים, וּלְעוֹפוֹת אֵין מִסְפָּר. עוֹפוֹת עֶשְׂרִין וְאַרְבָּעָה הָווּ! אֶלָּא: וּלְעוֹפוֹת טְהוֹרִים אֵין מִסְפָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the phrase: "The *ayya* after its kinds" (Leviticus 11:14), **it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Isi ben Yehuda says: There are one hundred non-kosher birds in the East, and they are all species of *ayya*. Avimi, son of Rabbi Abbahu, taught: There are seven hundred types of** non-kosher **fish, and eight hundred types of** non-kosher **grasshopper, and there are countless birds.** The Gemara protests: Are there countless non-kosher **birds?** But **there are** only **twenty-four** non-kosher birds mentioned in the Torah. **Rather,** Avimi must have meant: **And there are countless kosher birds.**

A *baraita* records the view of Avimi bar Rav Abbahu: there are seven hundred species of kosher fish, eight hundred species of kosher grasshoppers, and of kosher birds there is no number — they are infinite. This is not an exaggeration from the standpoint of modern taxonomy, which recognizes thousands of species in many animal families.

### Why 24 Works

The *gemara* challenges this: we have just spent considerable effort establishing that there are exactly twenty-four species of non-kosher birds — so how can kosher birds be infinite? The answer is direct: the twenty-four refers to non-kosher birds; kosher birds, being everything that does not appear on that list, are indeed without number, because they comprise the totality of all bird species minus the twenty-four forbidden ones. The structure of the Torah's enumeration — listing only the forbidden species — is precisely what makes this possible: by bounding the non-kosher category at twenty-four, the kosher category is left open and unlimited. Whether Avimi's statements about fish and grasshoppers similarly refer to the kosher varieties of those species is left as an open question.

## Torah Lists

### Shorter List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לִפְנֵי מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם שֶׁבְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מְרוּבָּה מִן הַטְּהוֹרוֹת, לְפִיכָךְ מָנָה הַכָּתוּב בַּטְּהוֹרָה. גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לִפְנֵי מִי שֶׁאָמַר וְהָיָה הָעוֹלָם שֶׁעוֹפוֹת טְהוֹרִין מְרוּבִּין עַל הַטְּמֵאִין, לְפִיכָךְ מָנָה הַכָּתוּב בַּטְּמֵאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: It is revealed and known before the One Who spoke and the world came into being that** the species of **non-kosher animals are more numerous than the kosher** ones. **Therefore, the Torah lists the kosher** animals, teaching that all the rest are non-kosher. On the other hand, **it is revealed and known before the One Who spoke and the world came into being that** the species of **kosher birds are more numerous than the non-kosher** ones. **Therefore, the Torah lists the non-kosher** birds.

A *baraita* records Rebbi's explanation for what might otherwise seem like an inconsistency: by mammals, the Torah lists the kosher species; by birds, it lists the non-kosher ones. Why the difference? *Galui ve-yadu'a lifnei mi she-amar ve-hayah ha-olam* — it is known to God that non-kosher mammals far outnumber kosher ones. Therefore the Torah provides the shorter list, which is the list of kosher mammals. Conversely, it is known that kosher birds vastly outnumber non-kosher ones. Therefore, by birds, the Torah provides the shorter list, which is the list of the twenty-four non-kosher species. In each case, the Torah teaches by enumerating the smaller category and leaving the larger one to inference.

### Short Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? כִּדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: לְעוֹלָם יִשְׁנֶה אָדָם לְתַלְמִידוֹ דֶּרֶךְ קְצָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this *baraita* **teaching us?** The Gemara responds: **As Rav Huna says** that **Rav** says, **and some say** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says in the name of Rabbi Meir: A person should always teach his student in a concise manner,** just as the Torah is concise in its language.

The pedagogical principle underlying this practice is stated explicitly by Rav Huna in the name of Rav — and some versions of the attribution add "in the name of Rabbi Meir" — *le-olam yishaneh adam le-talmido derekh ktzarah*: one should always teach one's student in the shortest way possible. The Torah's alternating methodology — listing kosher by mammals, non-kosher by birds — is itself a demonstration of this principle: in each domain, it says as little as necessary to convey the maximum amount of information.

## Mesorah Suffices

### Tradition Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: עוֹף טָהוֹר נֶאֱכָל בְּמָסוֹרֶת, נֶאֱמָן הַצַּיָּיד לוֹמַר: עוֹף זֶה טָהוֹר, מָסַר לִי רַבִּי. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁבָּקִי בָּהֶן וּבִשְׁמוֹתֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: A kosher bird may be eaten on** the strength of **a tradition** that it is kosher, without inspecting for the signs listed in the mishna. And **the hunter is deemed credible to say: My teacher conveyed to me** that **this bird is kosher. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And this** is the *halakha* only **when** the teacher **is familiar with** the non-kosher birds **and with their names.**

Rav Yitzchak states: *of tahor ne'echal be-mesoret* — a kosher bird may be eaten on the basis of tradition. This statement must be understood carefully. Rav Yitzchak is not requiring a *mesorah* as an absolute precondition; he is affirming that a *mesorah* is sufficient. The context is practical: all of the preceding discussion about identifying species, conflating names, and knowing which birds appear on the forbidden list makes clear how technically demanding the determination of a bird's permissibility can be. When one does not know with certainty whether a bird has all four kosher *simanim*, and when one faces the question of whether a bird with some non-kosher *simanim* might nonetheless be permissible because it does not appear on the list — the answer is that a reliable tradition of identification suffices to establish permissibility. This is the *gemara*'s position; it is only in the later period, beginning with Rashi, that the *mesorah* becomes not merely sufficient but necessary, on the grounds that one can never be fully certain whether a bird is a *dores* without a long-standing tradition.

### Hunter Testimony

The *gemara* applies this principle to a specific case: *ne'eman ha-tzayad lomar of zeh tiher li rabbi* — a hunter is believed when he testifies that a given bird is kosher, provided that his master taught him so. The hunter's tradition — received from someone with practical expertise in bird identification — constitutes a valid basis for consuming that species.

### Know Birds & Names

Rabbi Yochanan qualifies this rule: *ve-hu she-baki bahen u-vi-shmotseihen* — the ruling applies only if the master who transmitted the tradition was genuinely expert in both the birds themselves and their names. Both components are essential. Knowledge of the bird's physical characteristics is necessary to know what one is dealing with; knowledge of its name is necessary because, as established earlier in this discussion, a bird bearing the name of a forbidden species may be forbidden even if its *simanim* appear kosher — on the grounds that it may be a taxonomic extension of that forbidden species. Without knowledge of both appearance and nomenclature, the tradition cannot be relied upon. This caveat also addresses a perennial concern about oral traditions: that knowledge transmitted without grounding in empirical reality gradually loses accuracy as each generation repeats what it has heard without being able to verify it against the facts on the ground.

### Teacher vs Hunter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: רַבּוֹ חָכָם, אוֹ רַבּוֹ צַיָּיד? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁבָּקִי בָּהֶן וּבִשְׁמוֹתֵיהֶן. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבּוֹ צַיָּיד – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבּוֹ חָכָם, בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁמַיְיהוּ גְּמִיר לְהוּ, אֶלָּא אִינְהוּ מִי יָדַע לְהוּ? אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: רַבּוֹ צַיָּיד. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma:** Was Rabbi Yoḥanan referring to the hunter's **teacher the Sage, or** to **his teacher the hunter,** i.e., the one who taught him how to hunt? The Gemara responds: **Come** and **hear** proof from that **which Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And this** applies only **when** the teacher **is familiar with them and with their names. Granted, if you say** this is referring to **his teacher the hunter,** this works out **well. But if you say** it is referring to **his teacher the Sage, granted,** a Sage will know **their names,** since **he has learned them, but does he recognize** the birds **themselves? Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that Rabbi Yoḥanan referred to **his teacher the hunter?** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is so.

Rabbi Zeira raises a pointed question: when the *gemara* says "his master taught him," does this refer to a Torah teacher — a *chacham*, a *talmid chacham* — or to a master hunter?

### Hunter's Tradition

The resolution comes from Rabbi Yochanan's qualification itself. If we require that the transmitter of the tradition be *baki bahen u-vi-shmotseihen* — expert in the birds and in their names — then consider: if it were a Torah teacher being discussed, one could readily understand that he knows the *names*, since names are transmitted through textual study. But *baki bahen*, expert in the birds themselves, in their physical characteristics and appearance? *Ela inhu mi yada?* — do Torah scholars typically possess that kind of practical, empirical expertise in birds? The phrasing of the requirement — that the master must know both the birds and their names — only makes sense if the master in question is a hunter, precisely the kind of person one would not normally assume to be expert in the legal and textual dimension of the names. The requirement that he know *both* aspects, the empirical and the nominal, is what makes the specification necessary, and it only makes sense when applied to a hunter. Therefore: *shema mina rabo tzayad* — the tradition in question is that of a master hunter.

The conclusion is both surprising and significant. The operative tradition that establishes a bird's permissibility need not come from a *talmid chacham* or rabbinic authority. It suffices if it comes from a knowledgeable, God-fearing craftsman — a hunter who truly knows his birds and has received a tradition regarding their status from those who came before him. The tradition is reliable not because of the scholarly credentials of its bearer, but because of his practical mastery and the authenticity of what he received.

This brings the day's discussion to its natural conclusion. To summarize the full framework: a bird with all four kosher *simanim* is definitively kosher. A bird with a mixture of *simanim* may still be kosher if it does not appear on the list of twenty-four forbidden species, and a reliable tradition — even one stemming from a master hunter rather than a rabbinic sage — suffices to establish that it does not appear on that list. And according to Rashi, given the difficulty of ever being fully certain that a bird is not a *dores*, a *mesorah* is ultimately not merely sufficient but necessary.
