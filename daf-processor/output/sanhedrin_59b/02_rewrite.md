# Sanhedrin 59b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Repetition at Har Sinai

### Ever Min Hachai Setup

The discussion on this *daf* begins at the very bottom of 59a, arising from a question about why the *Torah* needed to repeat the prohibition of *ever min hachai* — the limb of a living animal — once it had already been included among the laws of *bnei Noach*. The *Gemara*'s response establishes a principle that is both important and, on closer inspection, quite striking.

### Core Principle

The *Gemara* articulates the following rule: anything that was said to *bnei Noach* and then repeated at *Har Sinai* applies to both Jews and non-Jews. That much is straightforward. But the second half of the principle is more surprising: anything said only to *bnei Noach* and *not* repeated at *Har Sinai* applies only to *Yisrael*. This is why *ever min hachai* had to be repeated — so that it would apply to both. The lone example of something said to *bnei Noach* that was *not* repeated and therefore applies only to *Yisrael* is *gid hanasheh*, though this is itself somewhat puzzling, since the verse explicitly states *al ken lo yochlu bnei Yisrael* — "therefore the children of Israel shall not eat."

### Gemara Challenge

The *Gemara* immediately presses the obvious difficulty: if something was said to *bnei Noach* and not repeated at *Har Sinai*, why should the absence of repetition mean it shifts to apply *only* to Jews? On the contrary — *aderaba* — one might argue the opposite: since it was not repeated at *Sinai*, perhaps it was given only to *bnei Noach* and not to *bnei Yisrael* at all.

### Higher Kedusha

The *Gemara* rejects this possibility by invoking a principle seen elsewhere: *leika mida d'l'Yisrael sharei u'l'Akum assur* — there is nothing that could be permitted to a Jew and forbidden to a non-Jew. Since Jews move toward a higher level of *kedusha*, the system cannot permit to Jews what it forbids to non-Jews. If something was forbidden to *bnei Noach*, it must equally — or even more stringently — be forbidden to Jews. Therefore, rather than concluding that unrepeated laws apply only to *bnei Noach* and not to us, we say the reverse: they apply to *Yisrael* as well.

## Exceptions & Mechila

### Yefat Toar

The *Gemara* now tests this principle against apparent counter-examples. The first is *yefat to'ar* — the beautiful captive taken in war. The *Torah* permits this for *bnei Yisrael*, yet it had previously been categorized under the prohibition of *gezel* (theft) for *bnei Noach*. Is this not a case of something forbidden to them and permitted to us?

The *Gemara* responds: *hasam mishum d'lav bnei kibush ninhu* — the mechanism of conquest, whereby captured territory legally becomes the possession of the conquering nation, applies to the wars of *bnei Yisrael* but not to the wars of gentiles. For Israel, the conquest creates a legal transformation of ownership; for non-Jews, that mechanism does not operate, and therefore what would otherwise be *gezel* remains *gezel* for them. The distinction is not in the underlying concept but in the absence of the operative mechanism. One might note — and indeed this was raised — that the *Torah* itself contains explicit verses indicating that God gave certain territories to various nations. This makes the *Gemara*'s answer difficult to fully resolve, and it must be left as a tension in the text.

### Pruta & Mechila

The second challenge concerns theft of less than a *perutah* — the minimum monetary unit. The *Gemara* had previously stated that *bnei Noach* are liable even for stealing less than a *perutah*, whereas for Jews such an act does not constitute actionable theft. Is this not something forbidden to them and permitted to us?

The *Gemara* answers: *hasam mishum d'lav bnei mechila ninhu* — the difference is not a fundamental legal distinction in the law of theft but rather a function of *mechila*, forgiveness or remission. In practice, Jews tend to forgive such trivial amounts, and once the victim has forgiven the offense, the act retroactively loses its character as *gezel*. For non-Jews, this dynamic of *mechila* does not similarly operate, and therefore the theft retains its prohibited character.

*Rashi* reads this somewhat differently, emphasizing that even Jews are commanded on *gezel*, and that the smaller amount fails to register as *gezel* only because people naturally forgive it. The underlying prohibition is the same; what changes is whether the practical reality of *mechila* transforms the nature of the act.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* raises a probing question: given that these apparent exceptions have been explained away, why does the *Gemara* not also raise the issue of *hashchatat zera* related to *peru u'rivu* as a similar counter-example? *Tosafot* also notes a more pressing case — abortion, where a *ben Noach* is liable for killing a fetus (treating it as full *retzichah*), whereas for a Jew there is an exemption from the death penalty and the act is not classified as murder. Is this not a genuine case of something more stringently forbidden to them than to us?

*Tosafot*'s resolution is that the principle only prohibits something being *fully permitted* to Jews while being forbidden to non-Jews. As long as the act is not completely permitted — even if it is less weighty for Jews than for non-Jews — the principle is not violated. The *Gemara* therefore does not raise abortion as a challenge, because abortion is not actually *permitted* to Jews; it is merely categorized differently.

## Abortion & Murder

### Tosafot Questions

The question *Tosafot* raises is: why does the *Gemara* not ask about abortion as a counter-example to the rule that nothing forbidden to *bnei Noach* can be permitted to *Yisrael*? For *bnei Noach*, killing a fetus is considered murder and punishable by death. For Jews, the fetus does not have the status of a full life in terms of capital liability, and one who destroys a fetus is *patur* from execution. Is this not a case where something is forbidden to them yet effectively permitted to us?

### Not Fully Permitted

*Tosafot* answers this by distinguishing between degrees of prohibition. The rule that nothing can be forbidden to non-Jews and permitted to Jews does not require that both groups be equally liable; it only requires that the act not be *fully mutar* for Jews. *Tosafot* draws a parallel to *Shabbat*: gentiles are forbidden to rest on a *Shabbat* day, and for Jews it is a *mitzvah* — yet that is not a violation of the principle, because *mitzvah* represents an elevation of obligation, not a relaxation into permissibility.

Similarly, *Tosafot* argues that abortion is not truly *mutar* for Jews. The act remains *assur* — it is simply that the classification and the consequences differ. This resolution implies that the prohibition on abortion operates at a *d'Oraisa* level, since if it were only a *d'Rabbanan* prohibition, it would not adequately resolve the conceptual difficulty. If abortion were fundamentally permitted by *Torah* law and only restricted by rabbinic decree, one could still say that the *Torah* itself placed Jews in a more permissive position than non-Jews — which is precisely what the principle rules out.

The most stringent position in modern *halachic* literature on abortion is that of Rav Moshe Feinstein, who characterizes it as *retzichah* — murder — though without the accompanying death penalty. This formulation is consistent with *Tosafot*'s framework: the act is *assur d'Oraisa*, a serious biblical prohibition, even though it does not carry capital punishment for Jews. One should note that another *Tosafot*, discussing this issue in a different context, uses the word *mutar* rather than *patur* — and Rav Moshe Feinstein found this so untenable that he concluded it must be a *ta'us sofer*, a scribal error, and that the text must have originally read *patur*.

### Mother's Life Priority

*Tosafot* raises one further difficulty. The *Gemara* in the chapter of *Ben Sorer u'Moreh* rules that once the baby's head has emerged, one may not sacrifice the infant's life to save the mother, since one may not set aside one life in favor of another. However, before the head emerges, the *halacha* permits — indeed requires — performing an abortion to save the mother's life. In this case, the act is not merely *patur* for Jews; it is an actual *mitzvah*. Yet for *bnei Noach*, who treat the fetus as a full life, performing an abortion would constitute *retzichah* even to save the mother. Here, then, is a genuine case where something is a *mitzvah* for *Yisrael* and forbidden for *bnei Noach*.

*Tosafot* responds that when an act is a *mitzvah* for Jews, it does not fall under the rubric of the problematic principle at all. The principle addresses what is *mutar* for Jews and *assur* for non-Jews; a *mitzvah* represents an entirely different category — not leniency but obligation. We are not comparing a relaxation with a prohibition; we are comparing a divine command with a prohibition. These are incommensurable, and the principle does not speak to such cases.

### Ranking Even in Murder

*Tosafot* adds a further observation: even granting that *bnei Noach* treat abortion as *retzichah*, it is possible that even for them, saving the mother's life might be permitted. Even within the category of murder, one may recognize rankings — acknowledging that a fetus, though classified as a living person whose killing constitutes *retzichah*, is nonetheless not a fully formed life in the same sense as the mother. The *heter* of *rodef* — the permission to kill a pursuer to save a victim — seems to apply to *bnei Noach* as well, as an earlier passage in the *Gemara* suggests. *Tosafot* therefore entertains the possibility that even for non-Jews, choosing the mother's life over the fetus's life may be permitted, or even obligatory, notwithstanding the classification of the act as *retzichah* in principle.

## Milah & Avraham

### Milah Exception

The *Gemara* now examines *milah* as a potential counter-example to the general principle. *Milah* was given to *bnei Noach* — specifically, as it says, *v'atah et briti tishmor*, "and you shall keep My covenant," referring to *Avraham*, who is considered a representative of *bnei Noach* — and it was repeated at *Har Sinai*, where the verse states *u'vayom hashemini yimol b'sar orlato*, "on the eighth day the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised." According to the principle, something given to *bnei Noach* and repeated at *Sinai* should apply to both. Yet *milah* applies only to Jews, not to non-Jews generally. How does this fit?

### Repetition for Detail

The *Gemara* answers: *hahu l'mishrei Shabbat hu d'ata* — the verse in the *Torah* that mentions *milah* at *Sinai* is not there to repeat or re-command the *mitzvah* itself; its purpose is solely to teach that *milah* overrides *Shabbat* (*bayom*, "on the day," meaning even on *Shabbat*). Since the repetition serves only to convey a legal detail — a *prat* about the *mitzvah* — it does not qualify as a genuine repetition in the sense that would bring it under the principle. True repetition, for purposes of this rule, means that the *mitzvah* was re-stated at *Sinai* for its own sake, as a *tzivui*. A repetition that exists only to teach a subsidiary *halacha* does not count.

This approach effectively narrows what qualifies as "repeated at *Har Sinai*," providing a mechanism by which the *Gemara* can consistently maintain its structural principle while accounting for all the apparent exceptions.

### Peru V'Rivu

The *Gemara* then raises the case of *peru u'rivu* — the commandment to procreate. This was originally given to *bnei Noach*, as the verse says *v'atem peru u'revu*; and it appears to have been repeated at *Sinai*, since *Moshe* told the people *shuvu lachem l'ohaleichem* — "return to your tents" — which the *Gemara* reads as a re-issuance of the *mitzvah* of *peru u'rivu*. Yet *peru u'rivu* applies only to Jews, not to *bnei Noach*. This is another apparent violation of the principle.

The *Gemara*'s resolution in this case is different. The statement *shuvu lachem l'ohaleichem* was not an independent repetition of the *mitzvah* of *peru u'rivu* at all. Rather, it served a technical legal purpose: to lift a temporary prohibition. Leading up to *matan Torah*, the Jewish people were forbidden from conjugal relations — *al tigshu el isha*. Since a decree enacted by a *beit din* through formal enactment requires an explicit act to reverse it — *kol davar she'be'minyan tzarich minyan acher le'hatiro* — *Moshe*'s statement served as that explicit retraction. The verse was functioning as a cancellation of the earlier edict, not as a new statement of the *mitzvah* of *peru u'rivu*. Therefore, it does not qualify as a repetition of the *mitzvah* at *Sinai*.

*Tosafot* notes a significant difficulty in this entire discussion: why should we assume that *peru u'rivu* does not apply to *bnei Noach* at all? The verses in *Bereishit* in which God commands *pru u'revu* seem to apply to all humanity, and the fact that the obligation is not listed among the *sheva mitzvot* might simply reflect that the *sheva mitzvot* lists prohibitions rather than positive commands (*kum asei*). However, *Tosafot* points out that *peru u'rivu* carries within it a prohibition dimension as well: one who is commanded in *peru u'rivu* is thereby commanded not to destroy seed (*hashchatat zera*), which is a *shev v'al ta'aseh* — an act of omission constituting a violation. If *peru u'rivu* applied to *bnei Noach*, *hashchatat zera* would have been enumerated among the *sheva mitzvot*. Since it is not, *Tosafot* infers that *peru u'rivu* was not given to *bnei Noach*. Some *Rishonim* dispute this and hold that *bnei Noach* are indeed commanded in *peru u'rivu*, but this *Gemara* assumes they are not — a conclusion that is admittedly difficult to reconcile with a plain reading of the *pesukim*.

### Avraham's Line

The *Gemara* offers an alternative and more satisfying answer specifically for *milah*: *mila me'ikara l'Avraham hu d'ka mazhar lei Rachmana* — *milah* was never given to all of *bnei Noach* in the first place. It was given exclusively to *Avraham* and his descendants: *v'atah et briti tishmor ata v'zar'acha acharecha l'dorotam* — you and your seed after you. The word *inshi achareini lo* — nobody else was included. Since *milah* was given not to *bnei Noach* as a category but only to a subset — *Avraham*'s specific line — it was never subject to the general principle.

This raises an interesting conceptual question: when Jews today perform *bris milah*, which *tzivui* are they fulfilling — the command given to *Avraham*, or the verse that appears later in the *Torah* specifying the eighth day? This *Gemara* seems to imply that the primary source is the command to *Avraham*, which carries forward specifically through his designated line.

The *Gemara* then asks: if *milah* belongs to *Avraham* and his seed, does that include *bnei Yishmael*? The *Gemara* answers with the verse *ki v'Yitzchak yikarei lcha zara* — "for through Yitzchak shall your seed be called." *Yishmael*, though biologically *Avraham*'s son, is not the designated *zera* of *Avraham* in this covenantal sense. The *Gemara* then asks about *bnei Esav*: the answer is *b'Yitzchak v'lo kol Yitzchak* — within *Yitzchak*, but not all of *Yitzchak*'s descendants are included. The covenant narrows further to *Yaakov*, so that the family of *Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov* — the ancestral line of *Bnei Yisrael* — constitutes the intended recipients of the covenant of *milah*.

### Keturah Question

Rav Oshaya challenges this resolution by asking: if *milah* applies only to *Avraham*'s line through *Yitzchak* and *Yaakov*, why did *Avraham* circumcise the children of *Keturah*? *Rashi* understands this question as referring to *Avraham*'s actual children from *Keturah* — not their descendants as a nation — and the assumption is that *Avraham* did in fact circumcise them.

### Eternal vs. Temporary

Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Avin and Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina answer: the verse *es briti hafar* indicates that at the moment of the original command, *Avraham* was personally required to circumcise all his children then living. This was an immediate, one-time obligation — "you shall observe My covenant" applied to *Avraham*'s household at that time. The eternal, ongoing *mitzvah* applying to future generations, however, is limited to the line of *Yitzchak*. There is thus a distinction between the temporary scope of the original command — which covered all of *Avraham*'s sons at that moment — and the permanent covenantal obligation that carries forward only through the Abrahamic family narrowly defined.

## Adam & Meat Eating

### Adam Vegetarian

The *Gemara* now turns to a different subject. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *Adam HaRishon lo hutar lo basar le'achila* — *Adam HaRishon* was not permitted to eat meat. The *Gemara* derives this from the verse in *Bereishit* which says that all vegetation shall be for food — for you and for all the animals — implying that you and the animals alike share in the vegetation, but you do not get to eat the animals: *v'lo chayat ha'aretz lachem*.

It is worth noting that *Rashi* interprets this *Gemara* more narrowly: the prohibition was not on eating meat as such, but on killing an animal for the purpose of eating it. According to *Rashi*, if an animal died on its own, *Adam* would have been permitted to eat it — and it is precisely for that reason that the prohibition of *ever min hachai* needed to be stated, since without it one might eat a limb from a living animal. But the straightforward reading of the *Gemara*'s statement — and the *pshat* of the verse — is that *Adam* was not permitted to eat meat at all. On this reading, the commandment of *ever min hachai* would belong to *Noach*'s generation, as an earlier opinion in the *Gemara* had suggested.

### Meat Permitted

When *bnei Noach* arrived — that is, with the generation of *Noach* — meat was permitted: *keyerek esev natati lachem et hakol* — "like the green herb I have given you everything." Just as you eat vegetation, so now everything, including animals, is given to you for food.

### Ever Min Hachai Details

The *Gemara* then derives the scope of *ever min hachai* from the verse *ach basar benafsho damo lo socheilu* — "but flesh with its life-force, its blood, you shall not eat." This establishes the prohibition of *ever min hachai* for *bnei Noach*. The *Gemara* then asks whether this prohibition might extend even to *sheratzim* — rodents and creeping things. From the word *ach* — "but" — which serves as a limiting term, the *Gemara* derives that *sheratzim* are excluded. The reason is offered by Rabbi Huna: *mi shedamo choluk mibesaro* — an animal to which the prohibition applies is one in which blood and flesh are distinguishable from each other; *yatzeu sheratzim she'ein damam choluk mibesaram* — rodents are excluded because their blood is not seen as distinct from their flesh.

## Mastery & Serpent

### Fish & Birds Work

The *Gemara* raises a challenge based on the verse *uredu bidgat hayam* — "and have dominion over the fish of the sea." The word *uredu*, "have dominion," might seem to imply that *Adam* was permitted to eat fish. The *Gemara* responds: *lo limlacha* — no, the dominion refers to using them for labor. The *Gemara* then asks with evident irony: can fish actually perform labor? The answer given is yes — and an example offered is a question posed by Rechava: if one were to harness a goat and a *shivuta* (a type of fish) together to pull a plow, would that constitute a violation of *kilayim*? The question itself presupposes that fish could in principle be used to do work.

The same challenge is raised from the verse *u've'of hashamayim* — "and over the birds of the heaven." Birds too are said to be under *Adam*'s dominion, which might imply permission to eat them. Again the *Gemara* responds: *lo limlacha* — for labor. And again the question: can birds do labor? Here the *Gemara* answers affirmatively with more confidence: Rabba bar Rav Huna describes using ducks or roosters to thresh grain. This occasions a brief discussion involving Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that an animal's right to eat while working depends on whether it performs labor with all four of its limbs — raising the question of what to make of an animal that works only with its lower limbs.

The verse *b'chol chayah haromeset al ha'aretz* — mastery over all the wild creatures that creep on the earth — is then cited, and the *Gemara* explains that this phrase serves specifically to include the *nachash*, the serpent. This introduces a *Baraita* from Rabbi Shimon ben Menasia.

### Serpent Servant

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasia laments: *chaval al shamash gadol she'avad min ha'olam* — what a loss, that such a great servant has been lost to the world. Before the serpent sinned, it walked upright on legs. Had it not been for the serpent's role in the sin of *Adam*, it could have served as an extraordinary attendant. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasia describes the lost potential in vivid terms: every individual Jew could have had two fine serpents to serve him — one sent to the north and one to the south — to bring precious stones and gems. And furthermore, since the serpent moved along the ground, it would overturn the soil beneath it with a strap attached to its tail, providing usable earth for one's garden. This passage is sometimes read as containing a degree of gentle humor: the greatest creature that was lost would have fetched the most precious jewels in the world — and also turned our garden soil.

### Meat from Heaven

This leads into a challenge from a *Baraita*: Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima stated that *Adam HaRishon* was reclining in *Gan Eden* like a king at a banquet, with heavenly angels roasting meat for him and filtering wine. The serpent saw all this splendor and was seized with jealousy — and this was the motivation for seducing *Chava*. But does this not contradict the ruling that *Adam* was not permitted to eat meat?

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *hasam b'vasar hayored min hashamayim* — the meat at that banquet had not come from a living animal; it was a miraculous meat that descended directly from heaven. The *Gemara* then pauses to ask whether such a thing is possible — *mi ika basar hayored min hashamayim?* — and answers affirmatively with the story of Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta. While traveling, he was confronted by roaring lions that threatened to devour him. He recited the verse *hakefirim sho'agim lateref* — "the young lions roar for prey" — as a kind of prayer. In response, two large portions of meat descended from heaven. The lions ate one and left the other. Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafta brought the remaining portion to the *beit medrash* and asked whether it was *tahor* or *tamei* — kosher or otherwise. The scholars ruled: *ein davar tamei yored min hashamayim* — nothing *tamei* descends from heaven.

Rabbi Zeira then posed the question to Rabbi Abahu: what if something in the shape of a donkey were to descend from heaven — would it be permitted? Rabbi Abahu responded with a tone suggesting the question was self-evidently unnecessary: since nothing *tamei* ever descends from heaven, even if it descended in the form of a donkey, it would be kosher. This brief exchange — combining the miraculous with questions of *kashrut* — gives rise to a brief but illuminating digression touching on the contemporary issue of laboratory-grown meat, which some *poskim* address by beginning with this very *sugya* about meat whose origin bypasses the life of an actual animal.

## Blasphemy Setup

### Witnesses Testify

The *Gemara* now returns to the major topic of the *Mishna*: the laws governing blasphemy (*birkat Hashem*). Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha had described the court procedure for trying a blasphemer. The witnesses testify using a euphemism — *yake Yose es Yosi*, "may Yose smite Yosi" — substituting the name "Yosi" in place of God's actual name, so that the explosive content of the curse is never directly repeated in court until the very end, when the final testimony must be delivered with God's name stated explicitly.

### Four-Letter Name

Rav Achai bar Yaakov teaches: *eino chayav ad sheyivarech shem bein arba osios* — one is not liable for the death penalty unless the blasphemy was uttered using God's four-letter name, the *Yud-Kei-Vav-Kei*. A *kinui* — a substitute name of God, such as *El* or *Elohim* — would not suffice to incur capital liability, even if it might constitute a lesser transgression.

The *Gemara* asks whether this principle is even necessary to state, since the *Mishna*'s use of the example *yake Yose es Yosi* — where the name *Yosi* carries four letters — already implies it. The *Gemara* responds that one might have thought *Yosi* was simply chosen arbitrarily as a convenient placeholder, with no implication about the four-letter name. Rav Achai bar Yaakov therefore makes explicit that the four-letter name is required. An alternative reading of Rav Achai bar Yaakov's statement is that from *yake Yose es Yosi* we learn that even the four-letter name — as opposed to the longer expanded name of 42 letters — is sufficient. *Rashi* explains that the *shem hamiforash* refers to the 42-letter name, and the question was whether the four-letter name alone suffices. The *Gemara* concludes that it does.

### Standing Requirement

After all the testimony has been heard and the verdict reached, everyone who was present stood when God's name was finally uttered. Where do we derive the obligation to stand?

Rav Yehuda bar Ami derives it from the story of *Ehud* and *Eglon*, King of Moav. The verse says: *vayomer Ehud dvar Elohim li eilecha* — "Ehud said, I have a word of God for you." *Vayakom me'al hakisay* — and Eglon rose from his throne. From here the *Gemara* constructs a *kal vachomer*: if *Eglon*, a non-Jew, who knew God only by a *kinui* — the name *Elohim* — nonetheless stood up when God's name was enunciated, then surely a Jew, upon hearing God's explicit name, is obligated to stand.

This is a striking *Gemara*. One might have assumed from the context of the *Mishna* that the standing was connected specifically to the horror of hearing God being cursed. But this derivation suggests that the obligation to stand is connected simply to the enunciation of God's name — which would seem to imply a much broader requirement to stand whenever God's name is articulated. Yet we do not, in practice, maintain such a rule. It is worth noting, then, that the *Gemara* derives this *din* specifically from the context of blasphemy proceedings.

## Keriah & Tears

### Ravshakeh Story

The *Gemara* then asks the source for *keriah* — the tearing of garments — when one hears God's name being blasphemed. The verse states that *Elyakim ben Chilkiyahu*, *Shevna the scribe*, and *Yoach ben Asaf* came to King Chizkiyahu *keruei begadim* — with torn garments — after hearing Ravshakeh, the envoy of the King of Assyria, curse and blaspheme God. They then reported the words of Ravshakeh to the king, and the verse records that *hamelech Chizkiyahu vayikra et begadav* — the king also tore his garments. This establishes the practice of *keriah* upon hearing blasphemy.

### Gezera Shava

The *Gemara* then asks how we know that the tear made for blasphemy is never sewn up — as is the rule with *keriah* over the death of a parent. The connection is made through a *gezeirah shavah* by Rabbi Abahu. The verse here uses the word *keruei vegdeihem* — "with torn garments" — and an analogous scene involving *Elisha*, who tore his garments upon the departure of *Eliyahu* in the fiery chariot. There, the verse says *vayikra'em lishnayim kera'im* — "he tore them into two tears." The *Gemara* notes: we already know from *vayikra'em lishnayim* that he made tears; why does the verse add the word *kera'im*? *Melamed shehem keruim le'olam* — to teach that they remain torn forever, never to be sewn up.

Since in the *Elisha* narrative the tear is effectively a *keriah* over a father-figure — *Eliyahu* in relation to *Elisha* — the *gezeirah shavah* links the unsewn tear of *keriah* over a parent to the *keriah* performed upon hearing blasphemy. Both are permanent and may not be repaired.

## Keriah Conditions

### Firsthand Secondhand

The *Gemara* now details who is obligated to perform *keriah* and under what conditions. A *Baraita* teaches: *echad hashomeia* — both one who directly hears the blasphemy, *ve'echad shomeia mipi shomeia* — and one who hears it secondhand — are obligated to tear their garments. We can infer this from the court proceeding itself, where the *dayanim* heard the blasphemy only through the testimony of the witnesses and still performed *keriah*.

However, the witnesses themselves are not required to repeat their *keriah*: *shekevar kare'u besha'ah sheshame'u* — they already tore their garments at the time they first heard the offense. The *Gemara* presses: so what if they already tore once — they are hearing it again now, so should they not tear again? The answer comes from the verse itself: it was *hamelech Chizkiyahu* who tore — implying that those who reported the incident to him, and who had already torn, did not tear again. This establishes the principle: once *keriah* has been performed for a particular incident of blasphemy, one need not tear again upon hearing it repeated in the context of testimony. But if the same individual were to blaspheme on a different occasion, *keriah* would again be required.

### Jew & Explicit

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *hashomeia azkarah mipi oved kochavim eino chayav likroa* — one who hears a *kinui* — a substitute name of God — from a non-Jew is not obligated to perform *keriah*. The question is raised: why then did they tear their garments over Ravshakeh? The *Gemara* answers: *Yisrael mumar haya* — Ravshakeh was not a gentile; he was a Jewish apostate.

Rav Yehuda further states in the name of Shmuel: *ein korin ela al shem hameyuchad bilvad* — *keriah* is performed only when God's four-letter, explicit name is used; a *kinui* does not trigger the obligation.

### Rabbi Chiya View

Rabbi Chiya disputes both of Shmuel's rulings. He states: *hashomea azkara bazman hazeh eino chayav likroa* — in our time, one who hears God's name invoked in the context of cursing is not obligated to tear, *she'ilmalei atah omer kein, nitmalu kol hab'gadim kera'im* — because if you were to rule otherwise, our garments would be filled with tears.

The *Gemara* analyzes this statement. If Rabbi Chiya is speaking of Jews blaspheming constantly, that is implausible. The context must therefore be non-Jews cursing God — and if we are speaking of non-Jews, and they are doing so constantly, they could hardly be using God's explicit four-letter name, since non-Jews do not generally know or pronounce it. The only coherent context is non-Jews using a *kinui*. Yet Rabbi Chiya exempts us from *keriah* only because it is *bazman hazeh* — in our time, where it is so prevalent. The implication is that *b'ikara* — in principle — even hearing a non-Jew curse God using a *kinui* would have required *keriah*. This is the exact opposite of Shmuel's position, which required both a Jewish blasphemer and God's explicit name. Rabbi Chiya's position requires neither, though the practical exemption in our time renders the distinction moot for the common case.

## Second & Third Witness

### Reish Lakish

The *Mishna* describes the court procedure: the second witness says *af ani kamohu* — "I also heard the same as him" — and the third witness says the same. This raises a significant legal question about the validity of testimony.

Reish Lakish derives from this procedure a ruling about testimony in general: *af ani kamohu kasher b'dinei mamonot u'v'dinei nefashot* — technically, saying "me too" — i.e., affirming the content of a previous witness's testimony without independently restating it — is valid testimony in both monetary cases and capital cases. This is the *d'Oraisa* standard. The rabbis normally require a more explicit and independent statement of testimony from each witness. But how do we know that "me too" works *d'Oraisa*? *Ve'hacha keivan d'lo efshar okmuha ad'raita* — here, since we cannot ask the second witness to repeat the curse in full — doing so would require re-uttering the blasphemy, which is itself deeply problematic — the *Gemara* allows the testimony to stand on its minimum *Torah*-valid form. *De'i salka daitach pasul* — if you were to think that "me too" is wholly invalid as testimony, we would have a situation where the perpetrator escapes execution simply because we cannot ask the witnesses to repeat a curse. Clearly this cannot be the intent of the law. Therefore the *Gemara* concludes that "me too" is technically valid *d'Oraisa*, even if rabbinic practice generally demands more explicit testimony.

### Me Too Testimony

The validity of *af ani kamohu* as testimony thus has implications beyond the specific case of blasphemy. It establishes that the minimum *Torah* standard for testimony does not require each witness to independently narrate the full content of what they observed, provided the first witness has already done so and the subsequent witness affirms it. Rabbinic enactment raises this standard, but the underlying *d'Oraisa* rule is as stated.

### Third Witness

The requirement that the third witness also say *af ani kamohu* reflects the position of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that where there are three witnesses and one is found invalid, the entire testimony is invalidated — *stama k'Rabbi Akiva d'makish gimmel l'bet*. Rabbi Akiva applies an equation between a group of three witnesses and a group of two, such that disqualification of any member disqualifies the whole. This is why the court takes care to elicit testimony from all the witnesses present: once all three have testified formally, if any one of them is later found to be invalid, the consequences of that invalidity — namely, the invalidation of the entire testimony — follow accordingly.

## Avoda Zara Intro

### Classic Forms

The *Gemara* now transitions to the *Mishna* dealing with *avoda zara* — worship of foreign gods. The *Mishna* enumerates the acts that constitute *avoda zara* punishable by death. These are the four classic forms of divine worship: *shechita* (slaughter/sacrifice), *haktara* (burning incense), *nisuch* (pouring libations), and *hishtachavaya* (prostration). These four acts constitute the normative forms of service in the *Beit HaMikdash*, and performing any of them before a foreign deity is *chayav misa*.

### Verbal Acceptance

Beyond these four acts, the *Mishna* adds a fifth category: *hamekabelo alav l'eloah* — one who accepts a foreign god as his deity. This is highly significant. *Avoda zara* typically connotes a physical act of worship, yet here the *Mishna* rules that a purely verbal declaration of acceptance — "you are my god" — also constitutes *avoda zara* punishable by death.

*Rashi* notes a further distinction within this category: *mekabelo alav l'eloah* may refer to acceptance even when not in the presence of the idol, while the phrase *ve'omer lo eli ata* — "and says to it, you are my god" — refers to addressing the idol directly in its presence. These are two distinct manifestations of the same underlying offense.

This has considerable historical and halachic importance. During forced conversions in the medieval period, Jews were often compelled to make verbal declarations of acceptance of another religion without necessarily performing any physical act of worship. The *Mishna*'s ruling here establishes that such a verbal acceptance alone falls within the category of *avoda zara*.

### Adoration Acts

The *Mishna* then delineates acts that are prohibited but do not reach the threshold of capital punishment. These are acts of adoration — *gifeuf* (hugging), *nishuk* (kissing), *nichbad* (sweeping before the idol), *marbetz* (settling the dust), *rachitza* (washing the idol), *sacha* (anointing with oil), *malbish* (clothing it), and *man'il* (putting shoes on it). All of these are transgressive — *over b'lo ta'aseh* — but the negative prohibition violated is *v'lo ta'aseh k'ma'aseihem*, which *Rashi* reads as a general prohibition against engaging in acts that imitate or pay homage to idol worship, even when those acts fall short of formal *avoda*.

### Vows & Oaths

Similarly, one who vows in the name of a foreign god or swears by its name transgresses a *lo ta'aseh* — *shem elohim acherim lo tazkiru, lo yishama al picha* — "the names of foreign gods shall not be mentioned; they shall not be heard upon your lips." Taking an oath or vow in the name of another deity violates this prohibition, though it does not constitute *avoda zara* in the technical sense and is not punishable by death.

### Unique Worship

Finally, the *Mishna* addresses unique or idiosyncratic forms of worship specific to a given idol. Two examples are given. The first is *Ba'al Pe'or*: one who exposes himself and defecates before *Ba'al Pe'or* — which was apparently that idol's established form of worship — is liable to the death penalty, *zo hi avodato*, because this is the manner in which it is worshiped, however degrading the act might appear in any other context. The second is *Markolis*: a deity associated with travel and roadways, worshiped by throwing a stone onto a configuration of three stones. *Hazorek even l'markolis* — one who throws a stone to *Markolis* — is liable, again because this constitutes the established form of worship for that deity.

The principle that emerges is: any act that constitutes the genuine, established form of worship for a particular idol renders one liable for *avoda zara*, regardless of whether that act would ordinarily seem worshipful or even degrading. Similarly, the four classic forms of worship (*shechita*, *haktara*, *nisuch*, *hishtachavaya*) render one liable before any idol, even if that is not the specific form of worship associated with it — because these are the normative forms of divine service. All other acts, however intimate or deferential, constitute at most negative prohibitions, not capital *avoda zara*.
