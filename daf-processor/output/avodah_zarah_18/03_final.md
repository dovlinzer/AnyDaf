# Avodah Zarah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Eliezer ben Prata

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁנִּתְפְּסוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן: אַשְׁרֶיךָ שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתָּ עַל דָּבָר אֶחָד, אוֹי לִי שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתִּי עַל חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: When Rabbi Elazar ben Perata and Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon were arrested** by the Romans during the time of the religious persecution of the Jewish people, **Rabbi Elazar ben Perata said to Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon: Fortunate are you, as you were arrested on one charge** only, of teaching Torah publicly; **woe is me, as I have been arrested on five charges.**

### Context of Persecution

The eighteenth daf of Avodah Zarah sits at the intersection of two major themes that the *Gemara* has been carefully interweaving. The immediate context arose from the prohibition against building stadia, scaffolding, and platforms to serve Roman courts of justice — structures that facilitated the persecution and execution of Jews, whether on trumped-up charges or under patently unjust laws. That discussion naturally introduced a series of narratives about the *harugei malkhut*, the great Rabbis who were martyred during the Hadrianic persecutions and beyond.

It is worth pausing to note a crucial corrective the *Gemara* itself offers: Roman persecution was not a constant condition. For the most part, the Roman Empire — whatever its other features — permitted Jewish life to proceed. It was during specific, intense periods of persecution, above all the Hadrianic decrees, that the threat became existential. The rest of the time, as one might put it, the roads were built and the bathhouses were open, and being part of the Roman world was perfectly tolerable — so long as one was not being targeted.

### Heresy vs. Idolatry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אַשְׁרֶיךָ שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתָּ עַל חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים וְאַתָּה נִיצּוֹל, אוֹי לִי שֶׁנִּתְפַּסְתִּי עַל דָּבָר אֶחָד וְאֵינִי נִיצּוֹל, שֶׁאַתָּה עָסַקְתָּ בַּתּוֹרָה וּבִגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, וַאֲנִי לֹא עָסַקְתִּי אֶלָּא בַּתּוֹרָה [בִּלְבַד].
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina** ben Teradyon **said to him: Fortunate are you, as you were arrested on five charges but you will be saved; woe is me, as I have been arrested on one charge, but I will not be saved.** You will be saved **because you engaged in Torah** study **and in acts of charity, and I engaged in Torah** study **alone.**

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* introduced a striking and recurring theme: what are the real threats to Jewish life in the present era? The answer the *Gemara* gives is pointed. *Avodah zarah*, idol worship in the classical sense, is not where the danger lies. The genuine threats are *minut* — heresy, intellectual seduction, ideas that are compelling but corrosive to Jewish commitment — as well as *rashut*, positions of governmental power that can pull a person away from his moorings, and *zenut*, sexual license. It was precisely the story of Rabbi Eliezer ben Prata that launched this line of thinking: he was charged with heresy, likely connected to his encounters with Jewish-Christians, and he managed to escape. His escape, however, prompted the *Gemara* to reflect on what actually endangers Jewish integrity in any given generation.

### Rabbi Elazar ben Durdaya

The *Gemara*'s concern with *zenut* as one of these central threats found its most dramatic expression in the story of Rabbi Elazar ben Durdaya, who had spent his life pursuing prostitutes and a life of promiscuity. When he finally attempted to turn away from that life, the rupture was so total, the effort so overwhelming, that he died in the attempt. This is not a story with a simple moral lesson about repentance; it is a stark illustration of how deeply and lethally the pull of *zenut* can ensnare a person, and how the path back can itself be agonizing. That story then ties back, with a certain structural elegance, to the narratives about the Roman government's persecution of Rabbis who refused to abandon *Torah* and *mitzvot* — because, as the *Gemara* will make clear, it is precisely the commitment to a life of *Torah u'mitzvot* that keeps one anchored against all of these centrifugal forces.

## Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon

### Torah Teaching Charge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְיוּהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא, אֲמַרוּ: מַאי טַעְמָא תָּנֵית, וּמַאי טַעְמָא גָּנְבַתְּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי סַיָּיפָא לָא סָפְרָא, וְאִי סָפְרָא לָא סַיָּיפָא, וּמִדְּהָא לֵיתַהּ הָא נָמֵי לֵיתַהּ. וּמַאי טַעְמָא קָרוּ לָךְ ״רַבִּי״? רַבָּן שֶׁל תַּרְסִיִּים אֲנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Romans **brought Rabbi Elazar ben Perata** for his trial and **said: What is the reason** that **you taught** Torah, **and what is the reason** that **you stole,** as these were the crimes of which he was accused. Rabbi Elazar ben Perata **said to them: If one is an armed robber [*sayafa*], he is not a scholar [*safra*], and if one is a scholar he is not an armed robber,** i.e., I am accused of two mutually exclusive crimes; **and from** the fact **that this** characterization **is not** true, one may also conclude that **that** characterization **is also not** true. They asked him: **But** if you do not teach Torah, then **what is the reason** that **they call you rabbi?** He answered: **I am the master [*rabban*] of weavers [*tarsiyyim*].**

The *Gemara* now turns to the story of Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon, and — in a characteristic narrative move — we already know the ending before we arrive at it. On the previous *amud* (17b), a *baraita* reported that Rabbi Hanina told Rabbi Eliezer ben Prata that he himself knew he would be found guilty by the Romans, and his reason was telling: *askata baTorah v'lo askata baTorah u'vegmilus chasadim* — you involved yourself in Torah and in acts of loving-kindness, while I involved myself only in Torah. This self-assessment — that his singular focus on *Torah* study, to the exclusion of practical good deeds, would cost him — must be kept in mind throughout what follows. It is not, strictly speaking, the cause of his death; it is the reason he will not be saved. His lack of *gemilut chasadim* means insufficient merit to protect him. And, as will become clear, there is also a deeply practical dimension to this failing: a life devoted exclusively to *Torah* in the most public and unaccommodating way is precisely what brought him into fatal conflict with Rome.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְיוּהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי קָא עָסְקַתְּ בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוַּנִי ה׳ אֱלֹהָי״. מִיָּד גָּזְרוּ עָלָיו לִשְׂרֵיפָה, וְעַל אִשְׁתּוֹ לַהֲרִיגָה, וְעַל בִּתּוֹ לֵישֵׁב בְּקוּבָּה שֶׁל זוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Romans **brought Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon** for judgment, and they **said to him: Why did you occupy yourself with the Torah?** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to them,** citing a verse: **"As the Lord my God commanded me"** (Deuteronomy 4:5). **They immediately sentenced him to** death by means of **burning, and** they sentenced **his wife to execution** by decapitation, **and his daughter** was condemned **to sit in a brothel [*kubba shel zonot*].** The Gemara explains the Divine decree that he should receive this punishment: **He** was sentenced **to** death by **burning, as he would**

The narrative proper begins three lines from the bottom of 17b. *Atiyuhu l'Rabbi Chanina ben Teradyon* — they brought Rabbi Chanina ben Teradyon before the Roman authorities. The *Maharsha* notes that the language directed at him differs from what was said to Rabbi Eliezer ben Prata. To the latter they said *tanita* — you have taught, a word more associated with Rabbinic literature and suggesting perhaps a somewhat more private context. To Rabbi Hanina they say *amai kaasakt b'Oraisa* — why did you involve yourself with Torah? — and as the narrative unfolds, the emphasis falls unmistakably on the public nature of his teaching, the *sefer Torah* sitting visibly in his lap.

His response to their charge is unambiguous: *ka'asher tzivani Hashem Elokai* — I am doing as the Lord my God commands me. The contrast with Rabbi Eliezer ben Prata is instructive. Rabbi Eliezer, when confronted, spoke about God in terms carefully calibrated so that the Romans thought he was paying them a compliment. He said *ne'eman alai hadayan* — I trust the judge — and they understood it as a reference to themselves. Rabbi Hanina does no such thing. Where Rabbi Eliezer was subtle, diplomatic, and practically intelligent, Rabbi Hanina is openly defiant, making no effort whatsoever to accommodate the reality of Roman power or to avoid its consequences.

The result is immediate. *Miyad gazru alav lisreifah* — they immediately decreed burning for him. *V'al ishto l'harigah* — his wife would be executed. *V'al bito lasheves b'kubbah shel zonah* — and his daughter would be consigned to a house of prostitution. His entire household was swept into the punishment.

### God's Name & Public Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹגֶה אֶת הַשֵּׁם בְּאוֹתִיּוֹתָיו. וְהֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָתְנַן: אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא — הָאוֹמֵר אֵין תּוֹרָה מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, וְאֵין תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַהוֹגֶה אֶת הַשֵּׁם בְּאוֹתִיּוֹתָיו!
>
> **Translation:** **pronounce the** ineffable **name** of God **with** all of **its letters,** i.e., as it is spelled. The Gemara asks: **And how could he do that? But didn't we learn** in the mishna (*Sanhedrin* 90a): **These** are the people **who have no share in the World-to-Come: One who says** that **the Torah is not from Heaven or** that **there is no** source **from the Torah** for **the resurrection of the dead. Abba Shaul says: Also one who pronounces the** ineffable **name** as it is written, **with** all of **its letters,** has no share in the World-to-Come.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהִתְלַמֵּד עֲבַד, כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תִלְמַד לַעֲשׂוֹת״, אֲבָל אַתָּה לָמֵד לְהָבִין וּלְהוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **did it to teach himself, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the prohibition against sorcery: **"You shall not learn to do"** (Deuteronomy 18:9); this indicates: **But you may learn to understand and to teach.** In other words, certain prohibitions do not apply when one is acting only in order to acquire knowledge of the subject.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמָא אִעֲנַשׁ? מִשּׁוּם (הוגה) [דְּהוֹגֶה] אֶת הַשֵּׁם בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא [הֲוָה], וְעַל אִשְׁתּוֹ לַהֲרִיגָה דְּלָא (מַיחָה) [מַחַתָה] בֵּיהּ. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: כָּל מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ לִמְחוֹת וְאֵינוֹ מוֹחֶה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what is the reason** that **he was punished?** The Gemara answers: He was punished **because he would pronounce the** ineffable **name** of God **in public,** instead of privately. **And his wife** was condemned **to execution** by decapitation **because she did not protest his** doing so. **From here** the Sages **stated: Anyone who has the capability to protest** effectively the sinful conduct of another **and does not protest is punished for** that person's sin.

The *Gemara* now asks what specifically warranted Rabbi Hanina's punishment, since we have established that the reason he was not saved is his insufficiency in *gemilut chasadim*, but that is not the same as why he deserved punishment in the first place. The answer: *shehayah hogeh et ha-shem b'osiyosav* — he would articulate God's name exactly as it is spelled. Rashi understands this to refer to the forty-eight letter name of God, with its extraordinary powers. Tosafot, however, understand it more simply: he would say the divine name *Yud-Kei-Vav-Kei* explicitly as written, rather than substituting *Adon* or another form. Tosafot's reading fits better with the broader thematic arc of what is happening here.

This immediately raises a difficulty. *V'heichi avid hachi* — how could he have done something so clearly prohibited? The *Mishnah* cited by the *Gemara* lists among those who have no share in the World to Come, in the view of Abba Shaul, one who articulates God's name as it is spelled. The *Gemara* resolves this: *l'hislamed avid* — he did so for purposes of study and understanding, not for any improper use of divine power or for promiscuous invocation. The support is a *baraita* that distinguishes between learning about how idolaters worship their gods *in order to do so* — which is forbidden — and learning about it purely for *Torah* purposes, which is permitted. The same principle applies here: using the divine name explicitly for purposes of learning and teaching is permissible.

If so, the *Gemara* presses, why was he punished at all? *Mishum d'hogeh et ha-shem b'farhesya d'havah* — because he did so in public. And here the thread connecting everything comes into focus. The problem was not the act itself but its public character. A small measure of discretion — a sensitivity to the consequences of performing one's *Torah* life without any accommodation to the surrounding world — was all that was required. Rabbi Hanina could not provide it. Whether in his teaching, his responses to the Romans, or his invocation of the divine name, everything about him was public, overt, entirely without concession to practical reality.

### Wife's Punishment & Enablement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל אִשְׁתּוֹ לַהֲרִיגָה דְּלָא (מַיחָה) [מַחַתָה] בֵּיהּ. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: כָּל מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ לִמְחוֹת וְאֵינוֹ מוֹחֶה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And his wife** was condemned **to execution** by decapitation **because she did not protest his** doing so. **From here** the Sages **stated: Anyone who has the capability to protest** effectively the sinful conduct of another **and does not protest is punished for** that person's sin.

The *Gemara* then addresses why his wife was sentenced to death. *D'lo michaya* — because she could have protested and stopped him, and she did not. *Mikan amru: kol mi sheyesh b'yado limchos v'eino mocheh ne'enash alav* — from here the rabbis derived that anyone who has the capacity to protest wrongdoing and does not do so is punished alongside the one who committed the act.

This is a striking principle. His wife is not a perpetrator; she is a passive enabler. Yet the punishment she receives is nearly as severe as his own, the difference being in the manner of execution rather than its occurrence. The principle resonates far beyond this story. It speaks to the moral culpability of bystanders, of those who know that injustice or wrongdoing is occurring and choose silence. The principle that passive enablement carries near-equivalent moral weight to active commission has obvious applications across any number of contexts in which people stand by while harm is done, whether out of self-interest, cowardice, or simple inertia.

### Daughter's Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל בִּתּוֹ לֵישֵׁב בְּקוּבָּה שֶׁל זוֹנוֹת, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פַּעַם אַחַת הָיְתָה בִּתּוֹ מְהַלֶּכֶת לִפְנֵי גְּדוֹלֵי רוֹמִי, אָמְרוּ: כַּמָּה נָאוֹת פְּסִיעוֹתֶיהָ שֶׁל רִיבָה זוֹ, מִיָּד דִּקְדְּקָה בִּפְסִיעוֹתֶיהָ. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״עֲוֹן עֲקֵבַי יְסוּבֵּנִי״? עֲוֹנוֹת שֶׁאָדָם דָּשׁ בַּעֲקֵבָיו בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה מְסוּבִּין לוֹ לְיוֹם הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why was **his daughter** condemned **to sit in a brothel? As Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Once, the daughter of** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **was walking before the nobles of Rome,** and they **said** to each other: **How pleasant are the steps of this young woman.** Upon hearing this, **she immediately took care** to keep walking in such a fashion that **her steps** would continue to be pleasing to them. **And this is** the same as that **which Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "The iniquity of my heel encircles me"** (Psalms 49:6)? It means that **the sins that a person tramples with one's heel in this world,** i.e., dismisses and pays no attention to them as they seem to lack importance, e.g., the way that one walks, come and **encircle him on the Day of Judgment.**

The punishment assigned to his daughter — to be placed in a house of prostitution — requires its own explanation, which Rabbi Yochanan provides. On one occasion, she happened to be walking before prominent Roman officials. They noticed her and remarked among themselves: *kamah na'os p'siyoseiha shel riva zu* — how beautiful are the footsteps of this young woman. She was not acting provocatively; she was not dressed immodestly; there was no deliberate display. But when she heard their notice, *miyad dikdekah b'p'siyoseiha* — she immediately began to be very careful about how she walked, refining and emphasizing the very feature that had drawn their attention.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish then connects this to the verse: *avoni akeivai yesubeni* — the sins of my heels surround me. *Avonot she'adam dash b'akeivav ba'olam hazeh mesubin lo leyom hadin* — sins that people treat as trivial, trampling on them as if with their heels, will wrap around them on the day of judgment. The play on the word for footsteps and heels is deliberate. She wanted a little more attention, she sought it through a subtle vanity, and the measure-for-measure response is that she will now receive male attention of the most degrading kind. It seems — and it is worth saying directly — extraordinarily harsh. The *Gemara* does not shy away from that sense of disproportion; indeed, the very harshness is what makes the *tziduk hadin* that follows so powerful.

### Tziduk HaDin Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁיָּצְאוּ שְׁלָשְׁתָּן, צִדְּקוּ עֲלֵיהֶם אֶת הַדִּין, הוּא אָמַר: ״הַצּוּר תָּמִים פׇּעֳלוֹ [וְגוֹ׳]״, וְאִשְׁתּוֹ אָמְרָה: ״אֵל אֱמוּנָה וְאֵין עָוֶל״, בִּתּוֹ אָמְרָה: ״גְּדֹל הָעֵצָה וְרַב הָעֲלִילִיָּה אֲשֶׁר עֵינֶיךָ פְקֻחוֹת עַל כׇּל דַּרְכֵי וְגוֹ׳״, אָמַר רַבִּי: [כַּמָּה] גְּדוֹלִים צַדִּיקִים הַלָּלוּ, שֶׁנִּזְדַּמְּנוּ לָהֶן שָׁלֹשׁ מִקְרָאוֹת שֶׁל צִדּוּק הַדִּין בִּשְׁעַת צִדּוּק הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When the three of them went out** after being sentenced, **they accepted the** justice of God's **judgment.** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said: "The Rock, His work is perfect;** for all His ways are justice" (Deuteronomy 32:4). **And his wife said** the continuation of the verse: **"A God of faithfulness and without iniquity." His daughter said: "Great in counsel, and mighty in work; whose eyes are open upon all the ways** of the sons of men, to give every one according to his ways" (Jeremiah 32:19). **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: How great are these righteous people, that** these **three verses,** which speak **of** the **acceptance of** God's **judgment, occurred to them at the time of accepting the** righteousness of His **judgment.**

*Beshah sheyatzeu shloshtan* — when the three of them went out to face their respective punishments, they each accepted the justice of the divine decree. *Tzidku aleihem et hadin* — they affirmed the righteousness of God's judgment.

Rabbi Hanina himself declared: *Hatzur tamim po'alo* — the Rock, perfect are His deeds. His wife continued with the next phrase of the same verse: *El emunah ve'ein avel* — a God of faithfulness and without iniquity. And his daughter quoted from Jeremiah: *Gadol ha'eitza verav ha'aliliyah asher einecha pekuchot al kol darchei ish legagid le'ish kefo'alo* — great in counsel and mighty in deed, whose eyes are open upon all the ways of men, to give each one according to his deeds.

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi then comments: *kamah gedolim tzaddikim hallalu shenizdamnu lahen shloshah mikra'ot shel tziduk hadin beshaas tziduk hadin* — how great are these righteous ones, that at the very moment of accepting divine judgment, the precise verses appropriate for the *tziduk hadin* presented themselves to them. This is remarkable not only because they found the right verses but because they said them about themselves, while still alive and on the way to their deaths — a form of *tziduk hadin* ordinarily associated with the *viduy* of the dying or the mourners' acceptance after a death.

The *Gemara* continues to call them *tzaddikim* even while acknowledging that they sinned in ways that were worthy of punishment. Both realities are held simultaneously: they are wholly righteous people, the punishments seem wildly disproportionate, and yet they accept God's judgment without qualification. That acceptance is itself part of what makes them *tzaddikim*. The *tziduk hadin* is not a formality; it is a genuine spiritual posture in the face of divine inscrutableness.

## Yossi ben Kisma Warning

### Divine Mandate Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁחָלָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן קִיסְמָא, הָלַךְ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן לְבַקְּרוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ: חֲנִינָא אָחִי, (אָחִי), אִי אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאוּמָּה זוֹ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם הִמְלִיכוּהָ? שֶׁהֶחְרִיבָה אֶת בֵּיתוֹ וְשָׂרְפָה אֶת הֵיכָלוֹ, וְהָרְגָה אֶת חֲסִידָיו וְאִבְּדָה אֶת טוֹבָיו, וַעֲדַיִין הִיא קַיֶּימֶת. וַאֲנִי שָׁמַעְתִּי עָלֶיךָ שֶׁאַתָּה יוֹשֵׁב וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה [וּמַקְהִיל קְהִלּוֹת בָּרַבִּים], וְסֵפֶר מוּנָּח לְךָ בְּחֵיקֶךָ!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: When Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma fell ill, Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon went to visit him.** Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma **said to him: Ḥanina my brother, do you not know that this nation has been given reign by** a decree from **Heaven?** The proof is **that** Rome has **destroyed** God's **Temple, and burned His Sanctuary, and killed His pious ones, and destroyed His best ones, and it still exists.** Evidently, all of this is by Divine decree. **And** yet **I heard about you that you sit and engage in Torah** study, **and convene assemblies in public, and have** a Torah **scroll placed in your lap,** thereby demonstrating complete disregard for the decrees issued by the Romans.

The *Gemara* now introduces an earlier episode that, in narrative terms, preceded the execution but sheds retrospective light on it. *Tanu Rabbanan: keshechalah Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma* — the rabbis taught: when Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma fell ill, Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon went to visit him. Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma, lying on his deathbed, offered his visitor a rational argument of considerable force.

*Chanina achi* — Chanina, my brother — *i ata yodeia she'umah zu min ha-shamayim himlichuha?* — do you not understand that this nation, Rome, has been given its dominion from heaven? The evidence is overwhelming. They destroyed the *Beit HaMikdash*; they burned the Temple; they slaughtered the *chasidim* and destroyed the finest of the Jewish people — and still they stand, still they endure. If God had not sanctioned their power, none of this would have been possible. The theological underpinning is exactly the same as *tziduk hadin*: we may not understand it, but this is manifestly part of God's plan.

*Ve'ani shamati alecha she'ata yoshev v'osek baTorah umakhil kehillot b'rabbim* — and I have heard about you that you sit and occupy yourself with Torah, gathering the multitudes in public — *v'sefer Torah munach lecha b'cheikecha* — with the *sefer Torah* lying right there in your lap. This last detail is significant. Rabbi Hanina is not merely teaching privately or even just teaching publicly in words. He is symbolically and visibly identified with the *sefer Torah*. He is not disguising his activity in any way. He is, in every possible sense, refusing to accommodate himself to the political reality around him.

### Rejection of Practical Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם יְרַחֲמוּ. אָמַר לוֹ: אֲנִי אוֹמֵר לְךָ דְּבָרִים שֶׁל טַעַם, וְאַתָּה אוֹמֵר לִי ״מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם יְרַחֲמוּ״? תָּמֵהַּ אֲנִי אִם לֹא יִשְׂרְפוּ אוֹתְךָ וְאֶת סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בָּאֵשׁ! אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, מָה אֲנִי לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to him: Heaven will have mercy** and protect me. Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma **said to him: I am saying reasonable matters to you, and you say to me: Heaven will have mercy? I wonder if** the Romans **will not burn** both **you and** your **Torah scroll by fire.** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to him: My teacher, what** will become of **me?** Am I destined **for life in the World-to-Come?**

Rabbi Hanina's response is three words: *min ha-shamayim yerachamu* — from heaven they will have mercy. Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma's reply is one of the most pointed lines in these *Gemarot*: *ani omer lecha devarim shel ta'am v'ata omer li min ha-shamayim yerachamu* — I am presenting you with a rational argument, a serious and cogent case, and you respond to me with "heaven will have mercy"? The rebuke is not merely practical; it is theological. Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma is not a Roman sympathizer or an accommodationist for its own sake. He is a Torah giant who has found a way to work within the structures of power — and who understands that expecting miraculous divine intervention as a substitute for practical wisdom is not *bitachon*, trust in God, but irresponsibility.

Rabbi Hanina is not willing to adjust. He is not going to accommodate himself to Roman power in his practice, in his manner of response to their prosecutors, or in his use of the divine name. He lives, as it were, in a different world — one in which the future of *Olam Ha-Ba* has already arrived, in which a person clutches the *sefer Torah* to his chest as God Himself does in the opening *midrash* of this *masechet*, and in which such public, total identification with *Torah* is simply what one does. The practical world can sort itself out.

It is important to note that miracles do sometimes save rabbis in these narratives — but not when the rabbi has acted irresponsibly and brought unnecessary danger upon himself. Rabbi Eliezer ben Prata was rescued precisely because he was subtle and careful and did not unnecessarily provoke. Rabbi Hanina, by contrast, is doing everything possible to ensure that he cannot be saved.

### Torah Without Deeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לְיָדֶךָ? אָמַר לוֹ: מָעוֹת שֶׁל פּוּרִים נִתְחַלְּפוּ לִי בְּמָעוֹת שֶׁל צְדָקָה וְחִלַּקְתִּים לַעֲנִיִּים. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, מֵחֶלְקְךָ יְהִי חֶלְקִי וּמִגּוֹרָלְךָ יְהִי גּוֹרָלִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma **said to him: Did any** special **incident occur to you** which might serve as an indication? Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to him: I confused** my own **coins** that I needed **for** the festivities of **Purim with coins of charity, and I distributed them** all **to the poor** at my own expense. Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma **said to him: If** that is **so, may my portion be of your portion, and may my lot be of your lot.**

Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma asks Rabbi Hanina whether he will merit the *Olam Ha-Ba*, and Rabbi Hanina's question in return — *mah ani l'chayei Olam Ha-Ba?* — is itself revelatory. He genuinely is uncertain. The earlier *amud* has already told us why: he himself acknowledged that he had focused only on *Torah* and not on *Torah u'gemilut chasadim*. He is the mirror image of the compulsively idealistic figure of Ben Azzai, who also could not tear himself away from *Torah* study to fulfill other obligations. Rabbi Hanina knows, at some level, that his singular passion has come at a cost.

*Amar lo: klum ma'aseh ba l'yadcha?* — did any deed come to hand? Did you ever do something practically good, a *ma'aseh*, an act? The question is almost jarring given everything Rabbi Hanina has done in the public sphere. But all that public Torah teaching does not, in Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma's framework, substitute for a *ma'aseh*. Rabbi Hanina answers: on one occasion, *ma'ot shel Purim* — Purim money — became mixed up with *tzedakah* money, and rather than sort out the confusion in a way that would disadvantage the poor, he distributed the *tzedakah* money generously and drew on his own funds to cover the *Purim* obligations. *Amar lo: im ken mechalkecha yihiyeh chelki umigoralcha yihiyeh gorali* — if so, may your portion be mine and my lot be yours.

### Contrast: Yossi vs. Hanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ: לֹא הָיוּ יָמִים מוּעָטִים עַד שֶׁנִּפְטַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן קִיסְמָא, וְהָלְכוּ כׇּל גְּדוֹלֵי רוֹמִי לְקׇבְרוֹ וְהִסְפִּידוּהוּ הֶסְפֵּד גָּדוֹל, וּבַחֲזָרָתָן מְצָאוּהוּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן שֶׁהָיָה יוֹשֵׁב וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה וּמַקְהִיל קְהִלּוֹת בָּרַבִּים וְסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה מוּנָּח לוֹ בְּחֵיקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: Not** even **a few days** passed **before Rabbi Yosei ben Kisma died** of his illness, **and all of the Roman notables went to bury him, and** they **eulogized him** with **a great eulogy. And upon their return, they found Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon, who was sitting and engaging in Torah** study **and convening assemblies in public, with a Torah scroll placed in his lap.**

Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma died shortly thereafter, and all the great men of Rome came to his burial and delivered a great eulogy for him. He had known how to navigate the world of Roman power; they recognized his greatness and respected it even in death. The contrast with what immediately follows could not be starker.

*Uvachazartan matzu l'Rabbi Chanina ben Teradyon shehayah yoshev v'osek baTorah* — when the Romans returned from the funeral, they found Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon sitting and occupying himself with Torah, gathering the masses in public, the *sefer Torah* lying in his lap, just as before. He had gone nowhere. He had changed nothing. The Roman dignitaries returning from the funeral of the great Rabbi who had managed his relationship with them so effectively walked directly into the sight of the Rabbi who refused any such accommodation whatsoever.

There is also a small but telling detail: Rabbi Hanina was not at Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma's funeral. He was teaching Torah in public while the man who had just visited him, questioned his judgment, predicted his fiery death, and expressed a wish to share his *Olam Ha-Ba* — was being buried. He was, in every sense, single-minded.

## Execution & Sefer Torah

### Discovery with Sefer Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֱבִיאוּהוּ וּכְרָכוּהוּ בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, וְהִקִּיפוּהוּ בַּחֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת וְהִצִּיתוּ בָּהֶן אֶת הָאוּר, וְהֵבִיאוּ סְפוֹגִין שֶׁל צֶמֶר וּשְׁרָאוּם בְּמַיִם וְהִנִּיחוּם עַל לִבּוֹ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תֵּצֵא נִשְׁמָתוֹ מְהֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **They brought him** to be sentenced, **and wrapped him in the Torah scroll, and encircled him with bundles of branches, and they set fire to it. And they brought tufts of wool and soaked them in water, and placed them on his heart, so that his soul should not leave** his body **quickly,** but he would die slowly and painfully.

*V'hevi'uhu v'karchuhu b'sefer Torah* — they took him and wrapped him in the *sefer Torah*. The Romans, having found him publicly identified with the *sefer Torah* and unwilling to separate himself from it in any practical sense, now enforced that identification in a grotesque and literal way: he would die wrapped in the *sefer Torah* he had refused to put down.

### Cruel Execution Method

*V'hikifuhu b'chavilei zmorot* — they surrounded him with bundles of branches. *V'hitzitu bahen et ha'or* — and they lit the fire. *V'hevi'u sfugin shel tzemar v'shara'um b'mayim v'henichum al libo* — they brought wool sponges, soaked them in water, and placed them on his heart, *kedei shelo tetzei nishmato meherah* — so that his soul would not depart quickly. It was to be a prolonged, agonizing death.

His daughter, witnessing this, cried out: *Abba, ere'acha b'kach?* — Father, is this how I must see you? The anguish is almost impossible to articulate.

### Unity with Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרָה לוֹ בִּתּוֹ: אַבָּא, אֶרְאֲךָ בְּכָךְ? אֲמַר לַהּ: אִילְמָלֵי אֲנִי נִשְׂרַפְתִּי לְבַדִּי הָיָה הַדָּבָר קָשֶׁה לִי, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאֲנִי נִשְׂרָף וְסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה עִמִּי, מִי שֶׁמְבַקֵּשׁ עֶלְבּוֹנָהּ שֶׁל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה הוּא יְבַקֵּשׁ עֶלְבּוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **His daughter said to him: Father, must I see you like this?** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to her: If I alone were being burned, it would be difficult for me,** but **now that I am burning along with a Torah scroll, He who will seek** retribution for **the insult** accorded **to the Torah scroll will also seek** retribution for **the insult** accorded **to me.**

Rabbi Hanina's response to his daughter is the theological core of his entire existence: *Ilmalei ani nisrafti l'vadi, hayah davar kasheh li* — had I been burned alone, that would have been difficult for me, because it would have meant being torn from the *Torah*. *Achshav she'ani nisraf v'sefer Torah immi* — but now that I am being burned together with the *sefer Torah* — *mi shemivakesh elbonah shel sefer Torah, hu yivakesh elboni* — the one who will come to vindicate the *sefer Torah*'s honor will come to vindicate mine as well.

The theological claim here is not that he will be saved. He knows he will not. The claim is that his life and the *Torah* are so wholly unified that the divine vindication of the *Torah*'s honor will encompass him. He is not separable from it. Even at the moment of death, he does not say: perhaps I should have been more accommodating, perhaps Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma had a point. He has accepted his death, but he has not reconsidered his life. He is at peace, because he understands his entire existence as one of radical unity with *Torah*.

### Ascending Letters Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: רַבִּי, מָה אַתָּה רוֹאֶה? אָמַר לָהֶן: (גִּלְיוֹן) [גְּוִילִין] נִשְׂרָפִין וְאוֹתִיּוֹת פּוֹרְחוֹת. אַף אַתָּה פְּתַח פִּיךָ וְתִכָּנֵס בְּךָ הָאֵשׁ. אָמַר לָהֶן: מוּטָב שֶׁיִּטְּלֶנָּה מִי שֶׁנְּתָנָהּ, וְאַל יְחַבֵּל הוּא בְּעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **His students said to him:** Our **teacher, what do you see?** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to them:** I see the **parchment burning, but** its **letters are flying** to the heavens. They said to him: **You too should open your mouth and the fire will enter you,** and you will die quickly. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to them: It is preferable that He who gave** me my soul **should take it away, and one should not harm oneself** to speed his death.

His students, sensing something extraordinary was occurring, asked him: *Rebbi, mah atah ro'eh?* — what do you see? He answered: *gilyonin nisrafin v'otiyot porchot* — I see the parchment burning and the letters flying upward. The physical scroll is being consumed; the letters themselves ascend. It is not difficult to understand the implication: his body too is being consumed, and his soul rises with those letters, inseparable from them.

The students then urged him: *af atah p'tach picha v'tikanes becha esh* — open your mouth and let the fire enter you, to hasten death. His response is a *halachic* principle of the first order: *mutav sheyitlenah mi shenatanah v'al yechabell hu b'atzmo* — better that the one who gave the soul should take it, and a person should not inflict injury upon himself to speed his own death. This is one of the very few talmudic sources that bear directly on the entire field of Jewish medical ethics concerning the end of life. The *Gemara* does not elaborate on it here, but the principle stands clearly: one may not hasten death, even one's own, even in extremis. The departure of the soul belongs to God.

### Roman Officer Intervention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ קְלַצְטוֹנֵירִי: רַבִּי, אִם אֲנִי מַרְבֶּה בַּשַּׁלְהֶבֶת וְנוֹטֵל סְפוֹגִין שֶׁל צֶמֶר מֵעַל לִבְּךָ, אַתָּה מְבִיאֵנִי לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא? אָמַר לוֹ: הֵן. הִשָּׁבַע לִי, נִשְׁבַּע לוֹ. מִיָּד הִרְבָּה בַּשַּׁלְהֶבֶת וְנָטַל סְפוֹגִין שֶׁל צֶמֶר מֵעַל לִבּוֹ, יָצְאָה נִשְׁמָתוֹ בִּמְהֵרָה. אַף הוּא קָפַץ וְנָפַל לְתוֹךְ הָאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The executioner [*kaltzatoniri*] said to him: My teacher, if I increase the flame and take off the tufts of wool from your heart,** so that you will die sooner and suffer less, **will you bring me to the life of the World-to-Come?** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **said to** the executioner: **Yes.** The executioner said: **Take an oath for me,** that what you say is true. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **took** the **oath for him,** and the executioner **immediately increased the flame and took off the tufts of wool from his heart,** causing **his soul** to **leave** his body **quickly.** The executioner **too leaped and fell into the fire** and died.

A Roman officer — a *kaltztoniri*, described by Rashi as one responsible for the execution of punishments — now approaches Rabbi Hanina with a proposal. *Rebbi, im ani marbeh bashalhevet v'notel sfugin shel tzemar me'al libcha, atah mevi'ani l'chayei ha'olam haba?* — Rabbi, if I increase the flame and remove the wet sponges, will you bring me to the World to Come? Rabbi Hanina answers: *hen* — yes. *Hashava li* — swear to me. *Nishba lo* — and he swore. Immediately the officer raised the flame and removed the sponges, and *yatz'ah nishmato bimherah* — his soul departed quickly.

The irony embedded in this exchange merits attention. Rabbi Hanina refused to hasten his own death, citing the principle that one must not injure oneself. Yet he consented to — and indeed encouraged — a non-Jew's intervention that would do exactly that. There is also something almost paradoxical in the fact that Rabbi Hanina, who earlier was uncertain about his own *Olam Ha-Ba*, here confidently promises it to someone else. One possible reading is that at the moment of death, a certain clarity descended upon him. Another is that the narrative is simply holding up the richness and strangeness of the moment without resolving every tension.

*Af hu kafatz v'nafal l'toch ha'or* — the Roman officer himself then leaped into the fire, presumably knowing that what he had done would bring Roman retribution, and choosing death on his own terms.

### Heavenly Voice Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן וּקְלַצְטוֹנֵירִי מְזוּמָּנִין הֵן לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. בָּכָה רַבִּי וְאָמַר: יֵשׁ קוֹנֶה עוֹלָמוֹ בְּשָׁעָה אַחַת, וְיֵשׁ קוֹנֶה עוֹלָמוֹ בְּכַמָּה שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **A Divine Voice emerged and said: Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon and the executioner are destined for the life of the World-to-Come.** Upon hearing this, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **wept and said: There is** one who **acquires his** share in the **World**-to-Come **in one moment,** such as the executioner, **and there is** one who **acquires his** share in the **World**-to-Come only **after many years** of toil, such as Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon.

*Yatz'ah bat kol v'amrah: Rebbi Chananya ben Teradyon v'kaltztoniri mezumanim heim l'chayei ha'olam haba* — a heavenly voice came forth and declared: Rabbi Chananya ben Teradyon and the Roman officer are both destined for the World to Come.

*Bacha Rebbi v'amar: yesh koneh olamo b'sha'ah achat v'yesh koneh olamo b'kamah shanim* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi wept and said: there are those who acquire their world in one moment, and there are those who acquire it over many years. This is almost verbatim the same line cited earlier in connection with Rabbi Elazar ben Durdaya. The Roman officer, with a single act of compassion and courage in the final moments of his life, is placed in the same *Olam Ha-Ba* as the great Rabbi.

The *bat kol* declares their names together, as equals. This is striking, and it circles back to the *Gemara*'s overall assessment of Rabbi Hanina. The *Gemara* has not been uniformly laudatory. It noted that he was not saved because of his insufficient *gemilut chasadim*. It showed him being rebuked by Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma for failing to heed rational argument. He himself was uncertain about his *Olam Ha-Ba*. And now the heavenly declaration places him alongside a Roman officer who did one good deed at the end of his life. None of this diminishes Rabbi Hanina's greatness or his martyrdom. But the *Gemara* seems to be carefully signaling that the way he navigated his life — the refusal to accommodate, the single-minded identification with *Torah* to the exclusion of everything else — was not simply held up as the ideal. He earned his *Olam Ha-Ba*, but *Olam Hazeh* required something more.

## Bruriah's Sister Rescue

### Request for Rescue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרוּרְיָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, בְּרַתֵּיה דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן תְּרַדְיוֹן הֲוַאי, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: זִילָא בִּי מִלְּתָא דְּיָתְבָא אֲחָתַאי בְּקוּבָּה שֶׁל זוֹנוֹת. שְׁקַל תַּרְקַבָּא דְּדִינָרֵי וַאֲזַל, אֲמַר: אִי לָא אִיתְעֲבִיד בַּהּ אִיסּוּרָא — (מִיתְעֲבִיד) [מִתְרְחִישׁ] נִיסָּא, אִי עֲבַדָה אִיסּוּרָא — לָא (אִיתְעֲבִיד) [מִתְרְחִישׁ] לַהּ נִיסָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Berurya, the wife of Rabbi Meir, was a daughter of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon. She said to** Rabbi Meir: **It is a disrespectful matter for me that my sister is sitting in a brothel;** you must do something to save her. Rabbi Meir **took a vessel [*tarkeva*]** full **of dinars and went. He said** to himself: **If no transgression was committed with her, a miracle will be performed** for her; **if she committed a transgression, no miracle will be performed for her.**

*Beruriah deveitehu deRebbi Meir, berateih deRebbi Chananya ben Teradyon* — Beruriah, the wife of Rabbi Meir, was the daughter of Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon. The *Gemara* has told us that his daughter was assigned to a house of prostitution; that must have been an unmarried daughter, since Beruriah, as the wife of Rabbi Meir, had escaped that fate. Nevertheless, she was deeply distressed. *Amra leh: zil b'milta d'yatba achasai b'kubah shel zonos* — it is a disgrace to me that my sister is confined to a house of prostitution.

Rabbi Meir took a large bag of *dinari* and set out. Before attempting to bribe anyone, he posed as a Roman soldier seeking to hire the sister-in-law. *Amra leh: dishtana ana* — she told him she was a *niddah* and could not comply. He said he would wait. She replied that there were many women there far more attractive than she was, implicitly discouraging him from pursuing the matter. From this, Rabbi Meir drew his conclusion: *shma mina lo avdas issura* — I understand from this that she has not engaged in the prohibited act. *Kol d'ati amar lah hachi* — it must be that with every man who approaches her, she deflects him in just this way.

This moment in the narrative raises a profound discomfort that should not be glossed over. The idea that the sister's worthiness of rescue depends on whether she had been sexually violated — in a situation where she is being held against her will and assigned to prostitution — places the moral burden on her in a way that the *Gemara* itself does not pause to interrogate. The story seems to understand that her active resistance to compliance is morally significant, perhaps indicating that she had not willingly participated. But the question of what it would mean if she had been violated by force, without any capacity to resist, is left unaddressed.

### Meir's Disguised Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל נְקַט נַפְשֵׁיהּ כְּחַד פָּרָשָׁא, אֲמַר לַהּ: (הַשְׁמִיעִנִי) [הִשָּׁמְעִי] לִי. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: דַּשְׁתָּנָא אֲנָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: מִתָּרַחְנָא (מִרְתַח). אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: נְפִישָׁן טוּבָא (וְאִיכָּא טוּבָא הָכָא) דְּשַׁפִּירָן מִינַּאי. אָמַר: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא עֲבַדָה אִיסּוּרָא, כֹּל דְּאָתֵי אָמְרָה לֵיהּ הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir **went and dressed as** a Roman **knight,** and **said to her: Accede to my** wishes, i.e., engage in intercourse with me. **She said to him: I am menstruating [*dashtana*]** and cannot. **He said to her: I will wait. She said to him: There are many** women in the brothel, **and there are many** women **here who are more beautiful than I. He said** to himself: I can **conclude from** her responses that **she did not commit a transgression,** as **she** presumably **said this to all who come.**

### Bribing the Guardian

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל לְגַבֵּי שׁוֹמֵר דִּידַהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲבַהּ נִיהֲלַי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִיסְתְּפֵינָא מִמַּלְכוּתָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁקוֹל תַּרְקַבָּא דְּדִינָרֵי, פַּלְגָא פְּלַח וּפַלְגָא לֶהֱוֵי לָךְ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי שָׁלְמִי מַאי אֶיעְבֵּיד?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir went over to her guard,** and **said to him: Give her** to me. The guard **said to him: I fear** that if I do so, I will be punished **by the government.** Rabbi Meir **said to him: Take** this **vessel** full **of dinars; give half**  to the government as a bribe, **and half will be for you.** The guard **said to him: But when** the money **is finished, what shall I do?**

*Azal legabei shomra didah* — Rabbi Meir went to her guardian. *Amar leh: hava nihali* — give her to me, meaning he offered to buy her freedom. The guardian responded: *mistafina mimalchusa* — I fear the government. I am responsible for keeping these women in line, and I cannot simply release one. Rabbi Meir produced the bag of *dinari*. *Palga plach* — use half of it to bribe the Roman officials who will investigate. *U'palga le'hava lach* — and keep the other half for yourself.

The guardian's next question reveals his practical concern: *v'chi shalmi, mai avidna?* — and when the bribe money runs out and they keep asking for more, what do I do then? The *Maharsha* offers a fascinating reading here. He suggests that the arrangement was not merely one of individual extortion, but that the house of prostitution was in some sense state-run, with the government taking a percentage of the earnings — a *machas*, a tax. In that case, the guardian's worry was not simply about bribery of individual officials but about a structural obligation: what happens when the *dinari* are spent and the expected revenue from this woman is still being demanded?

### Prayer & Divine Rescue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימָא ״אֱלָהָא דְּמֵאִיר עֲנֵנִי״ וּמִתַּצְּלַתְּ, אָמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִי יֵימַר דְּהָכִי אִיכָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא חָזֵית. הֲווֹ הָנְהוּ כַּלְבֵי דַּהֲווֹ קָא אָכְלִי אִינָשֵׁי, שְׁקַל קָלָא שְׁדָא בְּהוּ. הֲווֹ קָאָתוּ לְמֵיכְלֵיהּ, אֲמַר: ״אֱלָהָא דְּמֵאִיר עֲנֵנִי!״, שַׁבְקוּהּ, וְיַהֲבַהּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir **said to him: Say: God of Meir answer me! And you will be saved.** The guard **said to him: And who can say that this is** the case, that I will be saved by this utterance? Rabbi Meir **said to him: You will now see. There were these** carnivorous **dogs that would devour people;** Rabbi Meir **took a clod** of earth, **threw** it **at them,** and when **they came to devour him, he said: God of Meir answer me!** The dogs then **left him** alone, **and** after seeing this the guard **gave** the daughter of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Teradyon **to** Rabbi Meir.

Rabbi Meir's solution to this problem is unexpected: *eima: elaha de'Meir aneni* — say, "God of Meir, answer me," and you will be saved. The guardian is skeptical: *u'mi yemar d'hachi ika?* — who says that will actually do anything? Rabbi Meir provides a demonstration on the spot. There were savage dogs in the vicinity that had been known to attack people. Rabbi Meir took a clod of earth, threw it toward the dogs, they lunged at him — and he said, *elaha de'Meir aneni*, and they relented. The guardian was persuaded. He took the *dinari*, he released the sister, and the rescue was accomplished.

### Government Discovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אִשְׁתְּמַע מִילְּתָא בֵּי מַלְכָּא, אַתְיוּהּ אַסְּקוּהּ לִזְקִיפָה. אֲמַר: ״אֱלָהָא דְּמֵאִיר עֲנֵנִי!״ אַחֲתוּהּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ultimately the matter was heard** in **the king's court,** and the guard, who **was brought and taken to be hanged, said: God of Meir answer me!** They then **lowered him down,** as they were unable to hang him. **They said to him: What is this? He said to them: This was the incident** that occurred, and he proceeded to relate the entire story to them.

*Lesof ishtama milta b'malka* — eventually the matter became known to the government. They took the guardian and prepared to execute him — apparently to crucify or hang him. In his extremity, he cried out: *elaha de'Meir aneni*, and the execution somehow failed, the *Gemara* leaving the precise mechanism unspecified. When they demanded to know what had happened, he told them the entire story. They then went and had Rabbi Meir's likeness carved at the gates of Rome, with orders that anyone who recognized the face should bring him in.

## Rabbi Meir's Escape

### Most Wanted Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתוֹ חֲקַקוּ לִדְמוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַפִּיתְחָא דְרוֹמִי, אָמְרִי: כֹּל דְּחָזֵי לְפַרְצוּפָא הָדֵין לַיְיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They** then **went** and **engraved the image of Rabbi Meir at the entrance of Rome** where it would be seen by everyone, and they **said: Anyone who sees** a man with **this face should bring him** here.

*Atu chakaku lidmutei deRebbi Meir apitcha deRomi* — they engraved Rabbi Meir's face at the entrance to Rome, making him, in modern terms, one of the most wanted men in the empire. *Amri: kol dechazi lepartzufa hadin, leyatyei* — anyone who sees someone matching this face should bring him. Rabbi Meir was effectively a fugitive with a price on his head.

### Prostitution House Refuge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חֲדָא חַזְיוּהִי, רְהוּט אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, רְהַט מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ, עָל לְבֵי זוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **One day,** Romans **saw** Rabbi Meir and **ran after him,** and **he ran away from them** and **entered a brothel** to hide.

*Yoma chad chazyuh* — one day they spotted him. *Rahatu batrei* — they ran after him. *Rahat kamaihu* — he ran from them. *Al l'vei zonot* — and he ducked into a house of prostitution. The calculation was simple: Rabbi Meir would never be found in a house of prostitution.

### Food Deception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בִּשּׁוּלֵי גוֹיִם חֲזָא, טְמַשׁ בְּהָא וּמְתַק בְּהָא. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲתָא אֵלִיָּהוּ אִדְּמִי לְהוּ כְּזוֹנָה, כְּרַכְתֵּיהּ. אָמְרִי: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם, אִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר הֲוָה לָא הֲוָה עָבֵיד הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** he then escaped capture because **he saw** food **cooked by gentiles** and **dipped [*temash*] this** finger **in** the food **and tasted** it **with that** other finger, and thereby fooled them into thinking that he was eating their food, which they knew Rabbi Meir would not do. And **some say** that he escaped detection because **Elijah came, appeared to them as a prostitute** and **embraced** Rabbi Meir. The Romans who were chasing him **said: Heaven forbid, if this were Rabbi Meir, he would not act** in **that** manner.

A second account describes a different escape. There was food cooked by non-Jews nearby — *bishulei ovdei kochavim*. *Tamash beha* — Rabbi Meir dipped one finger into the pot. *U'metach beha* — and licked his other finger. Onlookers, seeing him dip one hand and bring the other to his lips, assumed he was tasting the food. They concluded this could not possibly be Rabbi Meir. The contrast with Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon could not be more pointed. Here is a great rabbi, dipping his finger in non-Jewish cooked food to make it appear that he is eating it, precisely in order to survive. This is what it looks like to be practical. This is what it looks like to accommodate the realities of the world in order to preserve one's life and continue one's mission.

### Elijah's Intervention

A third account introduces a miraculous intervention. *Ata Eliyahu, idmi lehu ke'zona, kerachtei* — Elijah appeared, looking like a prostitute, and embraced Rabbi Meir. The Romans who were pursuing him said to one another: *chas veshalom, ei Rabbi Meir hava lo hava avid hachi* — God forbid, if this were Rabbi Meir he would never act this way. And so they let him go.

### Flight to Babylon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָם עֲרַק, אֲתָא לְבָבֶל. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מֵהַאי מַעֲשֶׂה, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מִמַּעֲשֶׂה דִּבְרוּרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir **arose, fled,** and **arrived in Babylonia.** The Gemara notes: **There are** those **who say** that he fled because **of this incident, and there are** those **who say** that he fled due to embarrassment **from the incident involving** his wife **Berurya.**

However the precise sequence of events, the cumulative pressure became untenable. *Kam arak, ata l'Bavel* — Rabbi Meir rose and fled to Bavel. It may come as a surprise to many that Rabbi Meir — one of the central figures of the *Mishnah*, the greatest student of Rabbi Akiva in the generation after the Bar Kokhba revolt — took refuge in Babylon. *Ika deamri mehai ma'aseh* — some say it was this episode that drove him to flee. *V'ika deamri mimaaseh deBeruriah* — and some say it was a different episode involving Beruriah, a deeply troubling story cited by Rashi from tractate Kiddushin, in which Rabbi Meir, wanting to disprove Beruriah's dismissal of the rabbinic statement that *nashim da'atan kalot* — women's minds are light — arranged for a student to seduce her, with terrible consequences.

## Theater & Circus Bans

### Stadium & Camp Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַהוֹלֵךְ לְאִיצְטָדִינִין וּלְכַרְקוֹם, וְרָאָה שָׁם אֶת הַנְּחָשִׁים וְאֶת הַחַבָּרִין, בּוּקְיוֹן וּמוּקְיוֹן וּמוּלְיוֹן וְלוּלְיוֹן, בְּלוּרִין סַלְגּוּרִין — הֲרֵי זֶה מוֹשַׁב לֵצִים, וַעֲלֵיהֶם הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״אַשְׁרֵי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא הָלַךְ וְגוֹ׳ כִּי אִם בְּתוֹרַת ה׳ חֶפְצוֹ״, הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁדְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ מְבִיאִין אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי בִּיטּוּל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who goes to stadiums [*le'itztadinin*]** where people are killed in contests with gladiators or beasts, **or to a camp of besiegers [*ulkharkom*]** where different forms of entertainment are provided for the besieging army, **and** he **sees there** the acts of **the diviners and those who cast spells,** or the acts of the clowns known as ***bukiyon*, or *mukiyon*, or *muliyon*, or *luliyon*,** or ***belurin*,** or ***salgurin*, this is** categorized as **"the seat of the scornful"; and with regard to** such places **the verse states: "Happy is the man that has not walked** in the council of the wicked, nor stood in the way of sinners, nor sat in the seat of the scornful. **But his delight is in the Torah of the Lord"** (Psalms 1:1–2). **You learn** from here **that these matters bring a person to dereliction** of the study **of Torah,** since had he not sat in "the seat of the scornful," he would delight in the study of Torah.

Having traced the narratives of martyrdom and escape, the *Gemara* returns to the theme introduced at the outset of the *daf*: the threats posed by the larger world to Jewish integrity. These themes are, as noted, deliberately intertwined. The Roman venues of entertainment and the Roman venues of governance and violence are part of the same cultural apparatus, and the *Gemara* now addresses several specific prohibitions concerning attendance at these venues.

*Tanu Rabbanan: haholech l'itztadinin ulcharkum* — one who goes to the stadium or to the siege camp, and sees there the snake charmers, the magicians, the mimes, and all the various entertainments catalogued in the *Gemara* by a string of terms — *bukin mukin mulin lulin belurin selgurin* — whatever these refer to specifically, the general category is clear: jugglers, performers, curiosity acts, entertainers. *Harei zeh moshav leitzim* — this is the dwelling of mockers, the *moshav leitzim*.

The *Gemara* connects this to the famous opening verse of Psalms: *Ashrei ha'ish asher lo halach ba'atzas resha'im u'v'derech chata'im lo amad u'v'moshav leitzim lo yashav, ki im b'torat Hashem cheftzo* — praised is the person who did not walk in the counsel of the wicked, nor stand in the path of sinners, nor sit in the dwelling of scornful mockers; rather his desire is in the Torah of God. The point the *Gemara* draws is explicit: *halamadeta she-devarim hallalu mevi'im et ha'adam lidei bittul Torah* — these things lead a person to *bittul Torah*, the neglect of Torah. Not merely at the moment of attendance, but in their broader effect on a person's orientation toward life and its serious purposes.

### Exceptions & Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: לְאִיצְטָדִינִין מוּתָּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁצּוֹוֵחַ וּמַצִּיל, וּלְכַרְקוֹם מוּתָּר, מִפְּנֵי יִשּׁוּב מְדִינָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְחַשֵּׁב עִמָּהֶם, וְאִם נִתְחַשֵּׁב עִמָּהֶם — אָסוּר. קַשְׁיָא אִיצְטְדִינִין אַאִיצְטָדִינִין, קַשְׁיָא כַּרְקוֹם אַכַּרְקוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: **One is permitted** to **go to stadiums, because he can scream and save** the life of a Jew who would otherwise be killed there; **and it is permitted** to go **to a camp of besiegers, because** at times one can provide for the public **welfare** by petitioning the besiegers and saving the residents of the **town, provided that he is not counted** as one **of them; but if he is counted** as one **of them, it is prohibited.** This is **difficult,** as there is a contradiction between the statement about attending **stadiums** in the first *baraita* and the statement **about** attending **stadiums** in the second *baraita*, and is similarly **difficult** as there is a contradiction between the statement about **a camp of besiegers** in the first *baraita* and the statement **about a camp of besiegers** in the second *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא כַּרְקוֹם אַכַּרְקוֹם לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן — בְּמִתְחַשֵּׁב עִמָּהֶן, כָּאן — בְּשֶׁאֵין מִתְחַשֵּׁב עִמָּהֶן, אֶלָּא אִיצְטָדִינִין אַאִיצְטָדִינִין קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Granted,** the apparent contradiction between one statement about **a camp of besiegers** and the other statement **about a camp of besiegers** is **not difficult,** as **here,** the first *baraita* is referring to a case **where he is counted** as one **of them,** and **there,** the second *baraita* is referring to a case **where he is not counted** as one **of them. But** with regard to the contradiction between the ruling about attending **stadiums** in the first *baraita* and the ruling **about** attending **stadiums** in the second *baraita*, it is **difficult.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין הוֹלְכִין לְאִיצְטָדִינִין מִפְּנֵי ״מוֹשַׁב לֵצִים״, וְרַבִּי נָתָן מַתִּיר מִפְּנֵי שְׁנֵי דְבָרִים: אֶחָד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁצּוֹוֵחַ וּמַצִּיל, וְאֶחָד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמֵּעִיד עֵדוּת אִשָּׁה לְהַשִּׂיאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This issue **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not go to stadiums, because** they are considered **"the seat of the scornful." And Rabbi Natan permits** attending stadiums **due to two** reasons; **one** is **because he can scream and save** the life of someone who would otherwise be killed, **and** the other **one** is **because** even if he cannot save the man's life, **he can provide testimony** that **a woman's** husband died, which will enable her **to marry** again.

A *Tosefta* complicates the picture. *Holchin l'itztadinin mutar* — going to the stadium is permitted. The permission is grounded in two rationales: *mipnei shetzovei'ach umatzil* — because one may cry out and thereby save a life (as when the crowd's reaction at a gladiatorial contest determines whether the defeated fighter lives or dies), and *mipnei yishuv medinah* — attending the siege camp is permitted because it may allow one to exercise political influence, perhaps tempering how the army treats a city once it falls. Rashi reads this as the possibility of cultivating relationships with officers that could be used to protect Jewish populations.

The condition attached to the permission for the siege camp is significant: *u'vilvad shelo yitchasheiv imahem* — provided one does not become unified with them, does not become, as it were, one of them. This echoes exactly the warning from the *Gemara*'s discussions of *rashut* — positions of governmental power — and from Rabbi Yossi ben Kisma's model of how to engage with Roman authority: proximity for purposeful ends, but not absorption into their world. *V'im nitchasheiv imahem — assur* — if one does become unified with them, that is forbidden.

*Kashya itztadinin a'itztadinin, kashya charkum a'charkum* — the *Gemara* notes apparent contradictions regarding both the stadium and the siege camp. The contradiction about the siege camp resolves straightforwardly: *bishlama charkum a'charkum lo kashya* — the two statements refer to different cases. When it is permitted, it refers to going without becoming unified with them; when it is forbidden, it refers to going and becoming one of them.

The stadium presents a harder problem. *Tanna'ei hi* — it is a dispute between *Tannaim*. The first position prohibits going to the stadium categorically, *mipnei moshav leitzim* — because of the *moshav leitzim* concern. Rabbi Natan permits it for two reasons: one may cry out and save a life, and one may serve as a witness to someone's death, thereby enabling the widow to remarry. Why would the *Rabbanan* refuse to accept these considerations? The most persuasive reading is that the *Rabbanan* do not take the stated justifications at face value. They understand that people go to these venues to be entertained, and any claim of going purely to save a life or bear witness is, in their assessment, a rationalization. If someone were truly going solely for life-saving purposes, one imagines the *Rabbanan* would not object; but that, they suggest, is not actually why people attend.

### Theaters & Circuses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין הוֹלְכִין לְטֵרַטְיָאוֹת וּלְקִרְקְסִיאוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּזַבְּלִין שָׁם זִיבּוּל לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מָקוֹם שֶׁמְּזַבְּלִין — אָסוּר מִפְּנֵי חֲשַׁד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וּמָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מְזַבְּלִין שָׁם — אָסוּר מִפְּנֵי ״מוֹשַׁב לֵצִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One may not go to theaters [*letarteiot*] or circuses [*ulkirkaseiot*] because they sacrifice offerings there to** objects of **idol worship;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: It is prohibited** to go to **a place where they sacrifice** offerings, **due to a suspicion of idol worship, and it is** also **prohibited** to go to **a place where they do not sacrifice** offerings, **due to** it being considered **"the seat of the scornful."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא: נָשָׂא וְנָתַן אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the opinion of the Rabbis and that of Rabbi Meir? After all, according to both opinions it is prohibited to attend theaters or circuses. **Rabbi Ḥanina of Sura said:** The difference **between them** arises in the case of one who **engaged in business** there. According to Rabbi Meir, the profits are forbidden as the proceeds of idol worship, as Rabbi Meir maintains that the gentiles certainly worship idols at theaters or circuses. Conversely, according to the Rabbis, the profits are forbidden only if it is established that they worshipped idols there.

*Tanu Rabbanan: ein holchin l'tertiyot u'l'kirkasiyot* — one does not go to theaters or circuses. *Mipnei she-mezavlin sham zivul la-avodat kochavim* — because they conduct offerings for *avodah zarah* there. This is the view of Rabbi Meir. Tosafot make an important philological observation here: the word *mezavlin* / *zivul* is almost certainly a rabbinic dysphemism — deliberately derogatory wordplay on *mizavchim* / *zivuach* (sacrifices), substituting a word from the root *zevel* (dung). Rather than saying they sacrifice to idols, the rabbis say they heap dung for idols. This is a classic rabbinic rhetorical technique of verbal contempt.

The *chachamim* refine the prohibition. *Makom she-mezavlin — assur, mipnei chashad avodat kochavim* — a place where they do sacrifice: forbidden, because it appears as though one is participating in idol worship. *Makom she-ein mezavlin — assur, mipnei moshav leitzim* — a place where they do not sacrifice: also forbidden, but for the different reason of *moshav leitzim*.

*Mai beinaihu?* — what practical difference does this distinction make, since both are forbidden? Rabbi Chanina of Sura answers: *nasa v'natana ikka beinaihu* — the difference lies in the case of commercial transactions conducted at these venues. If the prohibition is because of *avodah zarah*, then proceeds from business done there are subject to the *Gemara*'s rules about the profits of idolatry, which must be destroyed. If the prohibition is only because of *moshav leitzim*, then while going was wrong, the proceeds of legitimate business conducted there are not subject to those special penalties.

## Progression to Mockery

### Escalation Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרָשׁ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אַשְׁרֵי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא הָלַךְ בַּעֲצַת רְשָׁעִים וּבְדֶרֶךְ חַטָּאִים לֹא עָמָד וּבְמוֹשַׁב לֵצִים לֹא יָשָׁב״? וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁלֹּא הָלַךְ, הֵיכָן עָמַד? וּמֵאַחַר שֶׁלֹּא עָמַד, הֵיכָן יָשַׁב? וּמֵאַחַר שֶׁלֹּא יֵשֵׁב, הֵיכָן לָץ?
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the earlier discussion of the evils of scornfulness, the Gemara cites several statements that criticize such behavior. **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Happy is the man that has not walked in the counsel of the wicked, nor stood in the way of sinners, nor sat in the seat of the scornful"** (Psalms 1:1)? **Since he did not walk** in the counsel of the wicked, **how** could **he stand** with them? **And since he did not stand, how** could **he sit** with them? **And since he did not sit** with them, **how** could **he** have **scorned?** Since he never joined the company of the wicked, he would have no reason to be involved with them in any manner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ, שֶׁאִם הָלַךְ — סוֹפוֹ לַעֲמוֹד, וְאִם עָמַד — סוֹפוֹ לֵישֵׁב, וְאִם יָשַׁב — סוֹפוֹ לָלוּץ, וְאִם לָץ — עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״אִם חָכַמְתָּ חָכַמְתָּ לָּךְ וְאִם לַצְתָּ לְבַדְּךָ תִשָּׂא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse serves **to say to you that if he walked** with the wicked, **he will ultimately stand** with them. **And if he stood** with them, **he will ultimately sit** in their company, **and if he sat, he will ultimately scorn** along with them. **And if he scorned, the verse says about him: "If you are wise, you are wise for yourself; and if you scorn, you alone shall bear it"** (Proverbs 9:12).

Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi expounded: mah dektiv: ashrei ha'ish asher lo halach ba'atzas resha'im u'v'derech chata'im lo amad u'v'moshav leitzim lo yashav?* — Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi expounded: what does the verse mean when it catalogues not walking, not standing, and not sitting with progressively worse company?

At first glance, the verse seems to present a redundant progression. If a person did not walk in the way of the wicked, how would he ever have stood with sinners? And if he never stood there, how would he ever have sat in the company of mockers? *Ki mei-achar she-lo halach, heichan amad? U-mei-achar she-lo amad, heichan yashav? U-mei-achar she-lo yashav, heichan lutz?* — the terms are presented in escalating form, but if the earlier stage is avoided, the later stages simply do not follow.

The *drasha* resolves this by inverting the reading: the verse is describing not a person who avoided all of these, but what happens to the person who does *not* avoid the first stage. *Lomar lach she-im halach, sof la-amod* — if you start by just walking by, by just checking it out, you will end up staying. *V'im amad, sof lashev* — and if you stay, you will end up sitting down for a while. *V'im yashav, sof lalutz* — and if you sit, you will end up absorbed in the mockery and frivolity. The walk-past becomes a stop; the stop becomes an extended stay; the stay becomes an identification with a culture of levity that corrodes seriousness of purpose.

This is not a trivial concern. The *leitz*, the mocker, is the antithesis of a person with *seriousness of purpose* — someone who understands that life is weighty, that there are things worth standing for, that not everything is a joke. A culture saturated with entertainment and irony produces a particular kind of person, one for whom nothing is quite real and nothing quite matters. This is what the *Gemara* identifies as the danger of *moshav leitzim* in the deepest sense: not merely a waste of time, but a corruption of one's fundamental orientation toward existence.

### Mockery Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כׇּל הַמִּתְלוֹצֵץ יִסּוּרִין בָּאִין עָלָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעַתָּה אַל תִּתְלוֹצָצוּ פֶּן יֶחְזְקוּ מוֹסְרֵיכֶם״. אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא לְרַבָּנַן: בְּמָטוּתָא בָּעֵינָא מִינַּיְיכוּ דְּלָא תִּתְלוֹצְצוּ, דְּלָא לֵיתוֹ עֲלַיְיכוּ יִסּוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Concerning **anyone who scoffs, suffering will befall him, as it is stated: "Now therefore do not be scoffers, lest your suffering be made strong"** (Isaiah 28:22). Similarly, **Rava said to the Sages** who were sitting before him: **Please, I ask of you that you not scoff, so that suffering will not befall you.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: כָּל הַמִּתְלוֹצֵץ מְזוֹנוֹתָיו מִתְמַעֲטִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מָשַׁךְ יָדוֹ אֶת לֹצְצִים״. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כׇּל הַמִּתְלוֹצֵץ נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֵד יָהִיר לֵץ שְׁמוֹ עוֹשֶׂה בְּעֶבְרַת זָדוֹן״, וְאֵין עֶבְרָה אֶלָּא גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יוֹם עֶבְרָה הַיּוֹם הַהוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ketina says:** Concerning **anyone who scoffs, his sustenance is lessened, as it is stated: "He stretches out his hand with scorners"** (Hosea 7:5), meaning that God withdraws His providence from scoffers and does not provide for them. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: Anyone who scoffs falls into Gehenna, as it is stated: "A proud and haughty man, scorner is his name, he acts in arrogant wrath"** (Proverbs 21:24). **And wrath** means **nothing other than Gehenna, as it is stated** with regard to the Day of Judgment: **"That day is a day of wrath"** (Zephaniah 1:15).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: כׇּל הַמִּתְיַיהֵר נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֵד יָהִיר לֵץ שְׁמוֹ עוֹשֶׂה בְּעֶבְרַת זָדוֹן״, וְאֵין עֶבְרָה אֶלָּא גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יוֹם עֶבְרָה הַיּוֹם הַהוּא״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִילַאי בַּר חֲנִילַאי: כׇּל הַמִּתְלוֹצֵץ גּוֹרֵם כְּלָיָיה לָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעַתָּה אַל תִּתְלוֹצָצוּ פֶּן יֶחְזְקוּ מוֹסְרֵיכֶם כִּי כָלָה וְנֶחֱרָצָה שָׁמַעְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya says,** based on the same verse: **Anyone who is haughty falls into Gehenna, as it is stated: "A proud and haughty man, scorner is his name, he acts in arrogant wrath"** (Proverbs 21:24). **And wrath means nothing other than Gehenna, as it is stated: "That day is a day of wrath"** (Zephaniah 1:15). **Rabbi Ḥanilai bar Ḥanilai says: Anyone who scoffs causes extermination** to be wrought **upon the world, as it is stated: "Now therefore do not be scoffers, lest your suffering be made strong; for an extermination wholly determined have I heard** from the Lord, the God of hosts, upon the whole land" (Isaiah 28:22).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: קָשָׁה הִיא שֶׁתְּחִילָּתוֹ יִסּוּרִין וְסוֹפוֹ כְּלָיָיה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Scoffing **is a severe** sin, **as at first** one is punished with **suffering, and ultimately** one is punished with **extermination.**

The *Gemara* then assembles several rabbinic statements about the consequences of becoming a *leitz*. Rava addresses his students directly: *bimatusa ba'ina mineichu d'lo tislotzatzu* — please, do me a favor, do not be frivolous. *D'lo leisu aleichu yisurin* — so that bad things do not come upon you. The setting — a great *Amora* pleading with his *talmidim* to drop the levity — is vivid.

Rav Ketina: *kol hamitzlotzeitz mezonosav mitmatin* — anyone who indulges in mockery and frivolity will find their income reduced. *Mashach yado es lotzim* — God withdraws His sustaining hand from the *lotzim*. The argument is not merely moralistic: a person who lacks seriousness of purpose cannot be effectively productive even in worldly terms. Entertainment and frivolity eat away not only at *Torah* but at one's capacity for sustained, directed effort in any domain.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish pushes the stakes still higher: *kol hamitzlotzeitz nofel b'gehinnom* — anyone who becomes a *leitz* will ultimately fall into *Gehinnom*. The source is a verse in which the words for "wicked," "haughty," and "mocker" appear together, and the word for "transgression" — *evrah* — is connected to *Gehinnom* by the phrase *yom evrah hayom hahu* — the day of wrath is that day. Rabbi Oshaya extends this to arrogance (*yahir*) as well, noting that the same verse links the haughty one to the same consequences. Rabbi Chanilai ben Chanilai draws out yet another dimension: *kol hamitzlotzeitz goreim kalyah l'olam* — the *leitz* brings destruction not only upon himself but upon the world. *Ki chalah v'necheratzah shamati* — because complete destruction I have heard decreed. Rabbi Elazar summarizes the trajectory: it begins with personal affliction (*yisurin*) and ends with communal destruction (*kalyah*).

### Final Drasha & Positive Engagement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: ״אַשְׁרֵי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא הָלַךְ״ לְטֵרַטְיָאוֹת וּלְקִרְקְסִיאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, ״וּבְדֶרֶךְ חַטָּאִים לֹא עָמָד״ — זֶה שֶׁלֹּא עָמַד בְּקִנִגְיוֹן, ״וּבְמוֹשַׁב לֵצִים לֹא יָשָׁב״ — שֶׁלֹּא יָשַׁב בְּתַחְבּוּלוֹת. שֶׁמָּא יֹאמַר אָדָם: הוֹאִיל וְלֹא הָלַכְתִּי לְטֵרַטְיָאוֹת וּלְקִרְקְסִיאוֹת וְלֹא עָמַדְתִּי בְּקִנִגְיוֹן, אֵלֵךְ וְאֶתְגָּרֶה בְּשֵׁינָה, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבְתוֹרָתוֹ יֶהְגֶּה יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi **taught that "Happy is the man that has not walked** in the counsel of the wicked," this is referring **to** the **theaters and circuses of gentiles; "nor stood in the way of sinners," this** is referring to **one who has not stood** as an observer **at bestial contests [*bekinigiyyon*]; "nor sat in the seat of the scornful,"** this is referring to **one who has not sat in the bad company** of people who engage in scoffing and jeering. **Lest a person say: Since I did not go to theaters and circuses, and did not stand in bestial contests, I will go and indulge in sleep, the verse states: "And he meditates in His law day and night"** (Psalms 1:2). This demonstrates that it is not sufficient simply to avoid transgressions; rather, it is necessary to engage actively in Torah study.

Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi's final *drasha* weaves the prohibitions against theatrical and circus attendance directly into the verse of *Ashrei ha'ish*: *Ashrei ha'ish asher lo halach l'varkita'os u'l'kirkasa'os shel ovdei kochavim* — praised is the person who did not go to the theaters and circuses of idol worshippers. *U'v'derech chata'im lo amad* — who did not stand *b'kangiyon* — at the animal hunts, a popular Roman entertainment involving wild animals. *U'v'moshav leitzim lo yashav* — who did not sit in the *tachbulos*, another form of public entertainment gathering.

But the verse does not end with the negative. And here Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi addresses a potential misunderstanding. Someone might hear all these prohibitions and conclude: fine, I will not go to the theaters or the circuses. I will not attend the games. I will simply sleep away my time at home. *Talmud lomar u'v'soraso yehegeh yomam va-lailah* — the verse teaches: it is not sufficient to refrain from bad. One must do good. One must invest one's life in purposeful engagement with *Torah* day and night.

This is the *Gemara*'s final word on the subject and, in a sense, the organizing principle of the entire *daf*. The threats to Jewish integrity are real and varied: *minut* and intellectual seduction, *rashut* and the corrupting allure of power, *zenut* and sexual license, and the more subtle but no less insidious erosion of seriousness through entertainment and frivolity. The answer to all of them is not merely avoidance but positive commitment — a life actively oriented toward *Torah* and *gemilut chasadim*. And the execution of that commitment, as the stories of Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon and Rabbi Meir so powerfully illustrate, requires not only passion and devotion but also practical wisdom, an ability to navigate the world as it actually is. The ideal is the *sefer Torah* held close to the chest. The imperative, in *olam hazeh*, is to know how to carry it without making oneself a target needlessly — and to be, unlike Rabbi Hanina, a little more like Rabbi Meir.
