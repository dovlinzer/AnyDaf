# Zevachim 93 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Ricochet

### Ricochet Question

The daf opens at the very bottom of 92b, four lines from the end, with a question posed by Levi to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The setting is the ongoing discussion of the *pesukim* in the *Torah* prescribing that if blood of a *chatat* falls upon a garment, that garment must be laundered *b'makom kadosh* — within the *Azarah*. Earlier the *Gemara* had been treating special cases involving the blood at the neck of a bird *chatat*; now it returns to the standard *chatat*.

Levi's question is: *nitas mi'beged l'beged mahu* — if blood flies out of the vessel in which it was caught, strikes a garment, and then ricochets off that first garment onto a second garment, does the second garment require this special laundering? The underlying tension is articulated by the *Gemara* itself: *mi'beged kama idchi leh l'kibbus od dilma lo* — once the blood struck the first garment, it became obligated in *kibbus*, which means it could no longer be placed on the *mizbe'ach*. And if it is no longer fit for *hazayah*, it ought not to impose any obligation on a subsequent garment.

On the other hand, one could argue that the first garment is no different from blood spilling onto the floor: if blood caught in a *kli* then spills on the floor, one may gather it up and it remains fit for the *mizbe'ach*. Similarly, since the blood ricocheted — meaning it did not fully absorb into the first garment — the surface blood could conceivably be taken from that garment and placed on the *mizbe'ach*. It is even possible to argue that the obligation of *kibbus* attaches only to blood absorbed into the garment, not to blood on the surface that will ricochet off. That is the heart of the question.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi responds: *zo she'elah* — this is indeed a genuine question — and then rules that the second garment does require laundering. His reasoning is a *mimah nafshach*: go either way. If striking the first garment does not render the blood unfit — that is, if one could still take that blood and place it on the *mizbe'ach* — then obviously the blood remains fit and the second garment is obligated. And if striking the first garment does render it unfit, *ana k'Rabbi Akiva sevira li* — he follows Rabbi Akiva, whose position we will see cited explicitly shortly: *hayta lo sha'at hakoasher v'nifsela damah ta'un kibbus* — as long as the blood was once fit, having been caught in a valid vessel in a valid state, it retains the power to obligate laundering even if it subsequently becomes invalid. Therefore, even under the assumption that the blood became unfit upon striking the first garment, the second garment is still obligated. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's answer fully resolves the case for one who holds like Rabbi Akiva, though it leaves open how the ruling would fall for one who does not.

## Tamei Garment

### Simultaneous Tumah

At the top of 93a, Rami bar Chama poses a question to Rav Chisda: *nitas al beged tamei mahu* — what is the law if the blood strikes a garment that is itself *tamei*? The force of this question lies in timing. Had the blood become *tamei* before striking the garment, there would be no obligation, since *tamei* blood cannot be placed on the *mizbe'ach* and therefore lacks the fitness required to impose a laundering obligation. But here the blood became *tamei* precisely at the moment of impact — through contact with the *tamei* garment. The question is whether to assess the blood's status by the instant before impact, when it was still fit, or by the instant of impact, when it simultaneously became *tamei*. Does simultaneity follow the same rule as sequential unfitness, or does it constitute a distinct category?

### Rav Huna's Answer

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua answers with a methodological observation: the question is only meaningful if one does not hold like Rabbi Akiva. *Midek'miba'ya leh hachi shema mina hayta lo sha'at hakoasher v'nifsela ein damah ta'un kibbus* — the very fact that the question is posed implies that we are working within a framework where blood that was once fit but is now unfit does not obligate laundering. For one who follows Rabbi Akiva, all these scenarios obviously require laundering, since the blood was initially fit. The question thus belongs to those who require the blood to be currently fit at the moment of impact.

And the specific *sha'alah* is: is there a distinction between *zeh achar zeh* — where the blood first becomes unfit and then strikes the garment, in which case there is no obligation — and *b'vas achas* — where unfitness and impact are simultaneous? Perhaps in the simultaneous case, since the blood was still fit in the moment before impact, it retains enough fitness to impose the obligation. Or perhaps the simultaneity of becoming *pasul* and striking the garment means they are legally inseparable, and the blood should be treated as unfit at impact. The *Gemara* identifies this as a debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis, as explained by Rava and subsequently reframed by Abaye.

## Prior Tumah Debate

### Intro Debate

The question of whether to assess the blood's status as it was prior to impact or as it becomes at the moment of impact maps onto a debate not about blood at all, but about the *mei chatas* — the waters of the *parah adumah*. The *Gemara* says: *plugta d'Rabbi Eliezer v'Rabanan aliba d'Raba v'cheha matritz Abaye* — this is the debate of Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis as explained by Rava, with Abaye offering his own reinterpretation.

### Rabbi Eliezer

A *Baraita* states: *Rabbi Eliezer omer mei chatas shenitmu metaharim shehrei nidah mazin aleha* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that *mei chatas* which have become *tamei* are still effective for purification, because we see that one may sprinkle *parah adumah* waters on a *niddah* who is *tamei* from *tumas meis*. When those waters strike her, they immediately become *tamei* — she is a *niddah* and her contact renders the water a *rishon l'tumah* — and yet the sprinkling works. Rabbi Eliezer infers from this that *tamei* water is not disqualified from functioning as *mei chatas*. The critical point is that the water became *tamei* only upon contact, not before — yet Rabbi Eliezer evidently treats the simultaneous-*tumah* case and the prior-*tumah* case as governed by the same rule.

### Rabbi Akiva Airspace

Rava's initial explanation of this debate is as follows: *Rabbi Eliezer b'shitas Rabbi Akiva rabo omer* — Rabbi Eliezer follows his teacher Rabbi Akiva, who holds that passing a vessel of *parah adumah* waters over the airspace of something *tamei* renders those waters unfit. The principle is *havaras kli agabei makom tamei k'munach dami* — conveying a vessel over a *tamei* place is as if one set it down there, bringing it into contact with the *tumah*.

This position of Rabbi Akiva is sourced from a *Mishnah*: *ha'kometz chutz l'tanur* — there was a clay oven (*tanur*) positioned against a wall, and inside the oven was a *sheretz* (a dead rodent), making the interior of the oven *tamei* in the manner of *kli cheres*. On the window ledge on the other side of the wall from the person stood flasks (*lagim*) of *parah adumah* waters. The person stretched his hand through the window, took the flasks, and in bringing them across, passed them over the opening of the oven. *Rabbi Akiva m'tamei v'chachamim m'taharim* — Rabbi Akiva declares the waters unfit; the Sages declare them *tahor*. Rava explains: *Rabbi Akiva savar k'munach dami v'Rabbanan savri lav k'munach dami* — Rabbi Akiva holds that passing through airspace is as if the vessel was placed in that space; the Rabbis disagree. This is understood as a special *halacha* particular to *parah adumah* waters, since ordinarily the concept of *tumah* via airspace is limited to a *mes*.

Given that framework, one can understand Rabbi Eliezer's derivation from the *niddah* case. If Rabbi Akiva holds that passing a flask of *mei chatas* over a *tamei* space renders it *tamei* — and Rabbi Eliezer follows him — then when one sprinkles the water on the *niddah*, the water enters her airspace before striking her, and according to Rabbi Akiva, that itself would make the water *tamei*. Yet it still functions. From this, Rabbi Eliezer learns that *tamei* waters remain effective.

### Oven Flask Case

The reasoning only works, however, if the same airspace principle applies both when conveying the flask and when actually sprinkling. This is where Abaye intervenes with a challenge.

But first, one might ask: if the water becomes *tamei* upon entering the *niddah*'s airspace — even before touching her — and yet it still works, does this not mean that even waters rendered *tamei* beforehand should work? Rabbi Eliezer's inference seems to presuppose that the waters are *tamei* before they actually touch the *niddah*, which he treats as functionally equivalent to the case where they were *tamei* from the outset. This is precisely the equivalence that Rava's reading of the debate builds upon: the airspace *tumah* happens before contact, yet Rabbi Eliezer says the water still works — and from this he infers that prior *tumah* in general does not disqualify *mei chatas*.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges this entire framework with a *baraita*: *modeh Rabbi Akiva b'hazayah she-he'evira al kli cheres tamei agav mishkav u'moshav shehi tehora* — Rabbi Akiva himself concedes that when one is actually performing the *hazayah* — actually sprinkling the *parah adumah* waters — if the water passes over a *tamei kli cheres*, or over a *mishkav u'moshav* (a seat rendered *tamei* by a *niddah*, *zavah*, or *zav*), it does not disqualify the water. This stands in direct contrast to the flask case, where Rabbi Akiva ruled the water *tamei*.

The principle stated is: *she'ein lecha davar shem'tamei l'ma'ala k'l'mata ela k'zayit min hames* — the only thing that creates *tumah* through the airspace above or below it is a *k'zayit* of a corpse (and certain analogous cases like *even hamenuga*). In all other cases there is no *tumah* via airspace. Therefore, when sprinkling, there is no concern — the water passes through the airspace of a *tamei* object and is unaffected.

If so, the flask case must be governed by a different logic entirely. When you are conveying the flask with your hand over the airspace of the *tamei* oven, it is not a matter of airspace *tumah* at all.

### Abaye's Reframe

Abaye therefore rejects Rava's explanation and offers his own: *dekulei alma lav k'munach dami* — everyone agrees that passing through airspace is not legally equivalent to setting down; there is no airspace *halacha* here. Rather, *Rabbi Akiva savar gazrinan shema yaniach v'Rabbanan savar lo gazrinan* — the debate in the flask case is a rabbinic *gezeirah*: Rabbi Akiva decrees that passing the flask over the *tamei* airspace is prohibited lest one set it down there; the Rabbis do not issue that decree. And Rabbi Akiva agrees that by actual sprinkling there is no concern — *keivan de-nafik nafik*, once the water leaves the hand it is beyond your control and cannot be set down.

With this reframing, Rabbi Eliezer's derivation from the *niddah* case takes on an entirely different character. There is no airspace *tumah* involved. The water becomes *tamei* only upon actually striking the *niddah*, not a moment before. And yet Rabbi Eliezer says it still works. His inference is therefore: since water that becomes *tamei* at the very moment of impact is still effective, we can derive that water which was *tamei* beforehand should equally be effective.

### Blood Application

This is the crux of the application to the blood case: *b'danim tumah khedumah mi-tumah she-be-osah sha'ah kamiflagi* — the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis is precisely whether one may infer from the *halacha* of *tumah* that arises simultaneously with impact the *halacha* of *tumah* that preceded impact. *Mar savar danim* — Rabbi Eliezer says: since water that becomes *tamei* at the moment of impact still functions, clearly water that was *tamei* beforehand functions too. *V'mar savar ein danim* — the Rabbis say: no, one cannot infer prior *tumah* from simultaneous *tumah*. In the simultaneous case, we assess the water's status from the moment just before impact, when it was still *tahor*, and that *tahor* status is what gives it its efficacy. Prior *tumah* is a categorically different situation.

Transposed to the blood case: if blood strikes a *tamei* garment and becomes *tamei* at impact, according to Rabbi Eliezer the garment is not obligated in laundering — since he treats simultaneous *tumah* like prior *tumah*, and prior *tamei* blood cannot obligate. According to the Rabbis, the garment is obligated — they assess the blood's status just before impact, when it was still *tahor* and fit, and that fitness is sufficient to create the obligation. This is a clean and elegant resolution of the original question.

Tosafos raises the question of why the *Gemara* frames this as the debate of Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis "according to Rava and as reframed by Abaye," rather than simply attributing it to Abaye alone, given that Abaye essentially rejected Rava's explanation. Tosafos answers that Abaye was Rava's student and the reinterpretation was carried out in dialogue with Rava; at the end of the day, Rava assented to Abaye's reading, so the analysis remains properly attributed to both.

### Raba's Position

Rava offers an alternative approach: *dekulei alma ein danim* — everyone agrees that what occurs simultaneously cannot be used to infer the law of prior status. The two situations are categorically distinct, and this point is not in dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis. Their debate must therefore be about something else entirely.

### Minimum Amount

Rava's explanation is that Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis debate whether *hazayah tzerichah shiur* — whether there is a minimum amount required for an effective sprinkling of *parah adumah* waters. Rabbi Eliezer holds that there is a *shi'ur*, and consequently, the required amount may sometimes be reached only by combining two successive applications. He therefore envisions a case where the first half-amount strikes the *niddah*, becomes *tamei* through contact with her, and then the second half-amount joins it. In that combined moment, half of the water is *tamei* yet the sprinkling is valid — and Rabbi Eliezer learns from this that *tamei* water can still function. The Rabbis hold there is no minimum amount for *hazayah*, and therefore no such combined scenario arises, and no inference can be drawn. According to Rava, the question of inferring prior *tumah* from simultaneous *tumah* is not the debate at all — everyone recognizes them as distinct categories.

## Invalid Blood

### Invalid Chattat

The *Gemara* turns to the next discussion of the *Mishnah*: *chatat pesulah* — if the *chatat* is invalid, its blood does not obligate the garment in laundering.

### Rabbi Akiva Fit

A *Baraita* derives this from the verse: *midamah kesheirah v'lo midamah pesulah* — the obligation applies to the blood of a valid *chatat*, not to that of an invalid one. Rabbi Akiva qualifies: *im haytah l'sha'at kosher v'nifselah damah ta'un kibbus* — if the blood was at some point fit, having been caught while the *chatat* was still valid, it retains the power to obligate laundering even after the *chatat* becomes invalid. Conversely, *lo haytah l'sha'at kosher v'nifselah ein damah ta'un kibbus* — if it was never fit, it does not obligate.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon disagrees and states the rule of the *Mishnah*: *echad zeh v'echad zeh ein damah ta'un kibbus* — in both cases, whether the blood was once fit or was never fit, as long as it is currently invalid, it does not obligate the garment in laundering.

### Verse Support

The basis for Rabbi Shimon's position is textual. Two limiting words appear in the relevant *pesukim*: *osah* and *midamah*. Each word serves to exclude one category: one excludes the case where the blood was previously fit but is now invalid, and the other excludes the case where it was never fit. Together they establish that only currently fit blood can create the laundering obligation.

### Akiva Counter

Rabbi Akiva counters that *osah prat l'trumah* — the word *osah* is not a limiting term relating to blood at all, but rather refers to the cooking of the *chatat* in a pot. The verse states *kol zachar bakohanim yochal osah*, and in that context discusses the pot used to cook the sacrifice: the pot must be rinsed and scoured. The word *osah* teaches that this applies to a *chatat* cooked in the pot, but not to *terumah* — if *terumah* is cooked in a pot, no such scouring is required. Since Rabbi Akiva uses *osah* to exclude *terumah* from the cooking obligation, he cannot simultaneously use it to limit the blood case. He therefore has only one limiting word — *midamah* — which he uses to exclude blood that was never fit, leaving the once-fit case still obligated.

Rabbi Shimon, for his part, does not need the verse *osah* to exclude *terumah* from the cooking obligation, because he holds on independent grounds that only *kodshei kodashim* require the scouring of the pot — not even *kadashim kalim* like *shelamim*, and certainly not *terumah*. Since he does not need *osah* for that purpose, he can deploy it to limit the blood case, and in conjunction with *midamah*, he excludes both the never-fit and the once-fit categories.

## Vessel & Fitness

### Neck Blood

The *Mishnah* continues: *nitzas min hatzavar al habeged* — if blood flew directly from the neck of the animal (in the case of the standard *chatat*, not a bird) and struck a garment, *eino ta'un kibbus* — it does not require laundering. The reason is that the blood was never caught in a *kli shareis*, and therefore was never fit for *hazayah*.

### Applied Blood

*Min hakeren umin hayesod* — if the blood was already applied to the corner of the *mizbe'ach* or spilled to its base, and then some of it flew onto a garment, it likewise does not require laundering. At that point the blood is clearly no longer fit for *hazayah*. The *Gemara* had already noted earlier in the tractate that once blood has been applied to any corner such that the remaining blood becomes *shirayim*, even that remaining blood — though not itself placed on a corner — is no longer fit for *hazayah*, since the essential sprinkling has been completed. Therefore even blood that has not itself been placed on the *yesod* but has been reduced to *shirayim* status would not obligate laundering.

### Floor Blood

*Nishpach al haritzpa ve'asafo* — if the blood spilled directly onto the floor from the animal and was then gathered into a *kli shareis*, *eino ta'un kibbus*. This differs from the case of blood properly caught in a vessel that subsequently spills: blood first caught properly and then spilled on the floor may be gathered and is still fit. But blood that went directly from the animal to the floor was never initially caught in a valid vessel, and therefore was never fit in the required manner.

### Summary Principle

The *Mishnah* summarizes: *ein ta'un kibbus ela dam sheniskabel b'kli v'rauy lehazayah* — the only blood that obligates laundering is blood that was caught in a vessel and is fit for sprinkling. This formulation gives the underlying rationale for all the preceding cases.

## Verse Derivations

### Never Caught

A *Baraita* derives the exclusion of blood that was never caught in a vessel from the verse *asher yizeh midamah al habeged* — "which shall be sprinkled from its blood upon the garment." The word *yizeh* — sprinkled — teaches that the verse speaks only of blood that is fit for *hazayah*: *lo amarti lecha ela b'rauy lehazayah* — I have imposed the laundering obligation only upon blood that is eligible to be sprinkled. Since blood that went directly from the neck to the garment without being caught in a *kli* was never fit for sprinkling, it is excluded.

### Already Applied

A second *Baraita* uses the same phrase *asher yizeh* to exclude blood that was already placed on the *mizbe'ach*: *prat lezeh shekvar huzah* — to the exclusion of blood that has already been sprinkled. This case is different from the case of *pesul* — the blood is not invalid; it was perfectly valid and properly applied. But it is no longer fit for *hazayah* because the sprinkling has already occurred. The verse *asher yizeh*, in the sense of "blood that is destined to be sprinkled" or "eligible to be sprinkled," excludes this case as well.

### Fitness Summary

One might ask why the *Mishnah* appends the summary principle — *ein ta'un kibbus ela dam sheniskabel b'kli v'rauy lehazayah* — since everything it says has already been covered by the preceding cases. The *Gemara* explains that the summary line is not adding a new case but rather stating the underlying rationale: *mah ta'ama nishpach al haritzpa ve'asafo ein ta'un kibbus* — why does blood spilled on the floor and then gathered not obligate? Because the rule is that only blood caught in a vessel and fit for sprinkling obligates laundering. The line thus functions as an explanatory principle. However, the *Gemara* immediately pivots and asks *lemiyutei mai* — what new case does the requirement of *rauy lehazayah* come to exclude beyond what has been stated? And this leads directly into the next section.

## Minimum Amount

### Mixed Vessels

The question is: what if one caught less than the required *shi'ur* in one vessel and less than the required *shi'ur* in a second vessel, and now combines them? Is the combined amount considered valid blood "caught in a vessel and fit for sprinkling," or is it disqualified because no single vessel ever contained the full minimum amount? This is the new case that the summary principle of *rauy lehazayah* comes to exclude. The premise — that there is a *shi'ur* required for *hazayah* and that it must be caught in a single vessel — is established first for *parah adumah*, and then the *Gemara* asks whether the same applies to the blood of a *korban*.

### Chalafta Bar Shaul

A *Baraita* is cited: Rabbi Chalafta bar Shaul rules regarding *parah adumah* that *kiddesh pachot mikhedi haza'ah b'kli zeh ufachot mikhedi haza'ah b'kli zeh lo kiddesh* — if one mixed the ashes with water in amounts less than the required *shi'ur* in each of two separate vessels, and then combined them, the water is not valid *mei chatas*. The full required amount must have been present in a single vessel at the time of mixing. The parallel to *hazayah* of blood is direct: just as one must catch the necessary amount in a single vessel, so too must the ashes be mixed with sufficient water all at once.

### Blood Question

The question is then extended to blood: *b'dam mahu* — does the same principle apply to the blood of a *chatat* or other *korban*? If there is a *shi'ur* for the sprinkling of blood, must the full amount have been caught in a single vessel, or may one catch less in each of two vessels and combine them?

The question is whether the rule for *parah adumah* is *hilkheta* — an arbitrary, unlearned *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* specific to *parah adumah* and inapplicable elsewhere — or whether it is derived from a *pasuk* and would therefore apply to blood as well. The verse cited for *parah adumah* is *v'taval bamayim*, from the procedure of dipping the hyssop: the Torah says to dip it "in the water," meaning *the* water — the same water that was present from the moment of mixing, in the full required amount. If this is a *pasuk*-based ruling, then the analogous verse for blood — *v'taval badam*, the *kohen* shall dip his finger in the blood — would teach the same requirement for blood.

### Unique to Parah

Rav Safra in the name of Rabbi Elazar rules: *af b'dam lo kiddesh* — the rule does not apply to blood. The requirement that the full *shi'ur* be present in a single vessel from the outset is unique to *parah adumah* and is not extended to the blood of *korbanot*.

### Rava's Blood

Rava disagrees and cites a *Baraita*: *v'taval v'lo mesappeg badam ad she-yehei badam shi'ur tevilah me-ikkara* — one must dip the finger into the blood, not merely wipe it along the walls of the vessel to collect blood that way, and the blood into which one dips must contain the full required amount from the outset. This directly supports the claim that our *Mishnah*'s summary phrase — *rauy lehazayah* — comes to exclude blood combined from two vessels, each containing less than the *shi'ur*. The two words *v'taval* and *badam* are each independently necessary: *v'taval* teaches that one must actually dip into a pooled collection of blood rather than wiping it up (*mesappeg*); *badam* teaches that the blood into which one dips must be the blood that was initially caught — meaning the full *shi'ur* must have been present from the moment of collection.

A third phrase, *min hadam shebe'inyan* — the blood that was being referred to in context — is also derived. The *Gemara* asks what this phrase excludes, and Rava answers: *lema'utei shirayim sheba'etzba* — it excludes the remainder blood left on the *kohen*'s finger after a sprinkling. This launches the final section of the daf.

## Finger Blood

### Baraita Finger

The *Baraita* states: *haya maze v'hinaza haza miyado* — if one was engaged in the sprinkling and some blood came from one's hand onto a garment: *ad shelo hiza ta'un kibbus* — before the sprinkling, the garment must be laundered; *mishehiza ein ta'un kibbus* — once the sprinkling has been done, it does not require laundering.

### Two Readings

This *Baraita* is susceptible to two distinct interpretations, and which reading one accepts has direct consequences for the question of whether remainder blood on the finger may be used.

The first reading: *ad shelo gamar mil'hazot* — the *Baraita* refers to blood that ricochets from the finger while one is still in the middle of the sprinkling process, before all four applications have been completed (in the case of an inner *chatat*, all four corners are required). Under this reading, blood that leaves the finger between applications still obligates laundering because the process is ongoing and the blood is still considered fit — the remaining blood on the finger may be used for the subsequent applications. Once all applications are complete, any remaining blood on the finger is no longer fit and would not obligate laundering. This reading supports the view that remainder blood on the finger is valid for subsequent applications.

The second reading: *ad shelo yatzta haza'ah miyado* — the *Baraita* refers to blood that ricochets from the finger before the first sprinkling with that fingerful of blood has been performed. Blood dipped onto the finger and not yet applied — that is, blood currently fit to be placed on the *mizbe'ach* in this very application — obligates a garment it strikes. Once that application has been performed, any remaining blood on the finger cannot be used and would not obligate. Under this reading, the *Baraita* does not at all address whether remainder blood on the finger may serve subsequent applications; it simply says that blood about to be applied in the current application is fit, while blood leftover after an application is not.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye raises a challenge from the *Mishnah* in the context of *parah adumah*: *gamar mil'hazot mekane'ach yado begufa shel parah* — once one has finished all seven sprinklings of the *parah adumah* blood toward the *Beit Hamikdash*, one wipes the remaining blood on the finger onto the body of the cow itself (which is then burned in its entirety). The implication of *gamar* — only once finished — is that one does not wipe between sprinklings. If remainder blood on the finger cannot be used for the next sprinkling, one would expect to wipe between each one. The fact that one does not wipe suggests that the remaining blood on the finger is still usable.

### Rava Response

Rava replies: *gamar mekane'ach yado begufa shel parah* — once finished, one wipes on the cow because all remaining blood must be burned along with the cow, as the Torah requires. But *lo gamar mekane'ach etzba'o* — before finishing, one does wipe the finger, just not on the cow itself. This preserves the position that remainder blood on the finger is not usable for subsequent sprinklings. One must wipe between applications, thereby starting each application with freshly dipped blood.

### Vessel Rim

Abaye then raises a natural follow-up: if you wipe your finger between sprinklings but not on the cow, what do you wipe it on? *Bishlama gamar mekane'ach yado begufa shel parah, shenemar v'saraf et haparah l'einav* — when finished, the verse requires the entire cow to be burned in one's sight, which accounts for wiping on the cow. But before finishing, *ella etzba'o b'mai mekane'ach* — on what does one wipe? Abaye answers: *b'ispat mizrak* — on the rim of the vessel in which the blood was caught. The verse *kipporei zahav* — golden cleansers — is cited as a textual support, the word *kipporei* connoting cleansing or wiping.

One might press further: if one is wiping the finger on the rim of the vessel that still contains blood, and then dipping back in for the next application, there is a question of whether the wiped-off blood — now deemed unusable — might mix back into the usable blood in the vessel. Ideally one would wipe on the outer edge of the vessel rather than the inner rim so as not to contaminate the remaining blood with blood that can no longer be used. This is the natural tension with which the daf concludes.

By way of summary: the discussion of the *Mishnah*'s phrase *rauy lehazayah* drew us into the question of whether blood caught in two separate vessels and then combined meets the fitness standard. The resolution, supported by Rava's *Baraita*, is that even by blood there is a requirement that the full *shi'ur* be caught initially in a single vessel. This then gave rise to the question of remainder blood on the finger: whether it may be used for subsequent applications, both from the standpoint of validly applying the blood to the *mizbe'ach* and from the standpoint of obligating a garment in *kibbus* — questions that, as we have seen throughout the daf, are directly linked to one another.
