# Sukkah 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arba Minim for Agud

### Lulav Knot Debate

The daf opens at the very top of 37a, picking up from the *mishna*'s debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim* regarding the material from which the knot of the *lulav* must be fashioned. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the binding must be made exclusively from the *arba'ah minim* themselves — from *min mino*, of the same species. An interesting question arises in passing: could one use a part of the *etrog* tree for this purpose? After all, the *eged* consists of three *minim*, not four, which would make a binding from the *etrog* tree somewhat anomalous. This is worth pausing over, though the *gemara* does not resolve it explicitly here.

### Rava's Logic

Rava explains that Rabbi Yehuda's requirement is not an aesthetic point. Rather, it flows from the conceptual principle that the *lulav* requires a knot, and therefore to use anything extraneous to the *arba'ah minim* would be to add something foreign to the *mitzvah* object itself. Once we understand that this is the underlying rationale — and not aesthetics — it follows that even parts of the *lulav* tree that are not themselves beautiful, such as a strip of bark, may be used to tie the *lulav*. The bark of the palm tree is still part of that species and therefore not extraneous.

### Tree Parts & Minim

Rava goes further to demonstrate that even non-standard parts of the relevant trees — trunk, bark, and imperfect specimens — are halachically classified as part of the *arba'ah minim*. His proof comes from a fascinating debate about the *sukkah* itself, a debate that did not surface during the earlier chapters of the tractate. It is to this debate that we now turn.

## Sukkah & Arba Minim

### Baraita: Schach Rules

Near the bottom of 36b, Rava introduces a *baraita* that bears directly on his point. The verse *basukkot teshvu* — you shall dwell in *sukkot* — is interpreted by Rabbi Meir as permitting *sechach* made of any material, provided it meets the standard criteria of not being susceptible to *tumah* and growing from the ground. Rabbi Yehuda, however, takes a far more restrictive position: *ein sukkah noheget ela b'arba minim she'balulav* — the *sukkah* may only be made from the same *arba'ah minim* used for the *lulav*. That is, the *sechach* itself must consist of the four species.

### Kal VaChomer Issue

Rabbi Yehuda supports this position with a *kal vachomer*: if the *lulav*, which is not observed at night as it is during the day, nonetheless requires the *arba'ah minim*, then a *sukkah*, which is observed equally by night and by day, should certainly require the *arba'ah minim*. This is, admittedly, a peculiar *kal vachomer*. One can construct almost any stringency through such reasoning. Still, the argument has some intuitive pull, and we will shortly see *p'sukim* in Ezra that seem to link the two *mitzvot* and push in this direction.

### Rabbinic Pushback

The *chachamim* respond with a pointed methodological objection: *kol davar she'attah datcha techillato le'hachmir vesofo le'hakel eino din* — any *kal vachomer* that begins with a stringency but ends in a leniency is not a valid argument. The stringency here is obvious, but where is the leniency? The *gemara* transitions to 37a to answer this.

### Torah Overrides

*Lo matzah arba minim, yaishiv u'batel?* — If one cannot find sufficient *arba'ah minim* to cover a *sukkah* with *sechach*, should one simply not fulfill the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* at all? But the Torah says *basukkot teshvu shivat yamim* — you shall dwell in *sukkot* for seven days — and a *sukkah* made of any valid material satisfies that obligation. Rabbi Yehuda's stringency, taken to its logical conclusion, would cause people to forfeit the *mitzvah* entirely when they cannot source the required species. This is the *kula* embedded within the *chumra*.

This exchange carries an important broader lesson: nearly every *chumra* has an associated *kula*. One cannot simply assume that being *machmir* is automatically the safer course. Stringencies can exclude people, waste their time and money, and — most critically — push them away from performing the *mitzvah* altogether. A notable *teshuva* from Rabbi Yehuda Herzl Henkin in *Bnei Banim* makes precisely this point: one cannot reflexively play it safe by adopting a *chumra* without accounting for the *kulos* it necessarily produces.

## Ezra Pasuk Analysis

### Ezra's Plant List

The *gemara* adduces a verse from Ezra in support of both positions. When the returned exiles built their *sukkot*, the text instructs them: *tzu ha-har* — go out to the mountain — *ve-heviu alei zayit* — bring olive leaves — *ve-alei etz shemen* — and oil-tree leaves, presumably also a type of olive — *ve-alei hadas* — myrtle leaves — *ve-alei tmarim* — date leaves — *ve-alei etz avot* — which is also a variety of *hadas* — *la'asot sukkot ka-katuv* — to make *sukkot* as it is written. The verse is describing the construction of *sukkot* and it identifies specific plant materials, materials strikingly associated with the *lulav*. This lends real texture to Rabbi Yehuda's position.

### Olive Leaves Problem

And yet the very same verse undermines him. Among the materials listed is the olive branch — *alei zayit* — which is plainly not one of the *arba'ah minim* as Rabbi Yehuda understands them. If Rabbi Yehuda holds that *peri etz hadar* refers to the *etrog* and not the olive, then olive branches should not be appearing in this list as *sukkah* material. The *chachamim* seize on this: you see, they argue, the verse in Ezra proves that you may use materials *outside* the *arba'ah minim* for the *sukkah*.

### Tzadukim & Pasuk

It is worth noting at this point that certain Second Temple-era groups — most likely the *Tzedukim*, though possibly also the Karaites — read the verse *u-lekachtem lachem ba-yom ha-rishon peri etz hadar kapot tmarim* not as establishing a separate *mitzvah* of taking the *lulav* and *etrog*, but as instructing the people to gather these species and use them to construct the *sukkah*. On their reading, *u-lekachtem lachem* means: take these things and build your *sukkah* with them. There is no independent *mitzvah* of *netilat lulav* in their framework.

Some scholars suggest this may actually reflect the plain sense of that verse, and that the passage in Ezra-Nehemiah — originally one book — may have been written with precisely that reading in mind. On this interpretation, *peri etz hadar* would refer to the olive, with its beautiful, fruit-bearing tree producing oil. This would explain why olive branches appear in Ezra's list alongside the other species: they are all there as *sukkah*-building materials. That reading is itself a fascinating possibility for understanding the *peshat* of the Ezra passage.

### Yehuda's Solution

How does Rabbi Yehuda respond to the mention of *alei zayit*? He resolves the problem by partitioning the list: *savar hani le-dfanot* — he maintains that the olive wood mentioned in Ezra was used for the *walls* of the *sukkah*, not the *sechach*. Everything else — *alei hadas*, *alei tmarim*, *ve-alei etz avot* — was used for the *sechach* itself. With this move, Rabbi Yehuda insulates his position: the *sechach* in Ezra is still composed entirely of species associated with the *lulav*, while the olive is accounted for by its use in the walls, which have no *arba'ah minim* requirement.

## Yehuda & Planks

### Contradiction Setup

Now that Rabbi Yehuda's position has been articulated — *sechach* must come from the *arba'ah minim* — the *gemara* identifies an apparent internal contradiction. In another *mishna*, we find the ruling *mesachchin be-nesarim* — one may make *sechach* with planks — and this is attributed to Rabbi Yehuda. But planks are not *hadas* twigs or *arava* branches. How can Rabbi Yehuda permit *sechach* from planks while simultaneously insisting that *sechach* must be made from the *arba'ah minim*?

### Trunk Solution

The resolution is precisely the point Rava sought to establish from the outset: planks can be cut from the trunks of *hadas* trees or palm trees. If Rabbi Yehuda permits such planks as *sechach*, it is because he regards the trunk of these trees as halachically part of their respective *min*. The trunk of a palm tree is classified as part of the *lulav* species; the trunk of a *hadas* tree is part of the *hadas* species. This is *the* proof Rava was building toward.

### Eikra Definition

The *gemara* states: *amar siv ve-ikra de-dikla mina de-lulav hu shma mina* — the *siv* (a vine or fibrous material attached to the palm) and the *ikra de-dikla* (the trunk of the palm tree) are considered part of the species of *lulav*. Rashi explains that *ikar* here means the essence or core of the tree — i.e., the trunk. Although *akarin* ordinarily refers to roots, the context and Rashi's explanation together support reading *ikra* as the trunk. The same Rabbi Yehuda who demands that the *lulav* be bound *be-mino* — with material from its own species — and that the *sechach* be from the *arba'ah minim*, does not restrict this requirement to the decorative, leafy portions of those species. The woody core of the tree qualifies as well.

### Cedar & Hadass

A further challenge arises from the *braita* about planks: *sechachah binesarim shel erez* — if one covered a *sukkah* with planks of *erez* (cedar) — *sheyesh bahen arba'ah tefachim*, planks four *tefachim* wide, *divrei hakol pesulah* — all opinions agree it is invalid. *Ein bahen arba'ah tefachim* — if the planks are less than four *tefachim* — that is the dispute: Rabbi Meir invalidates and Rabbi Yehuda validates. The *braita* continues: Rabbi Meir concedes that if there is a gap between the planks equal to the width of one plank, and one fills that gap with valid *sechach*, the *sukkah* is *kasher*. The key question for us is: how can Rabbi Yehuda validate cedar planks if cedar is not one of the *arba'ah minim*?

The *gemara* answers: *mai erez hadas* — when the *braita* says *erez*, it actually refers to a species of myrtle tree. This is not a claim that cedar and myrtle are botanically identical, but rather that there exists a variety of *hadas* tree called *erez*. The proof comes from the teaching of Rabbah bar Rav Huna: *amri bei Rav asarah minei arazim hem* — there are ten species of *erez* — and the verse cited (*eten bamidbar erez shitah vehadas*) lists *erez* and *hadas* in the same breath, implying they share a botanical classification. Whether the *gemara* means that cedar *is* a type of *hadas* or that there is a *hadas*-type tree called *erez* remains somewhat ambiguous, but the former is a bold claim that invites scrutiny. Rashi's view and the overall thrust of the *sugya* support the latter reading.

Taken together, Rabbi Yehuda's position is a remarkable and internally consistent system: the *lulav* requires a binding *be-mino*; the *sechach* must be drawn from the *arba'ah minim*; and the *arba'ah minim* encompass not only the canonical leafy components but the trunks of the relevant trees as well — all of which finds a kind of quasi-support in the *pasuk* in Ezra, save for the complicating presence of the olive, which Rabbi Yehuda deftly reroutes to the walls.

## Lulav Binding Rules

### Rava to Builders

The *gemara* transitions to practical instructions about the construction and wrapping of the *lulav* bundle. Rava addressed the craftsmen who would assemble the *hoshana* — the bundled combination of *hadas*, *lulav*, and *arava* — for the house of the *Reish Galusa*. The practice was apparently to wrap some decorative material, perhaps silk, around the bundle to hold it together. Rava instructed them: *shari bei beis yad* — leave an unwrapped space at the bottom — *ki heichi d'lo tehavi chatzitza* — so that it will not constitute an obstruction between the hand and the *lulav* itself.

The concern is straightforward: if the entire lower portion of the bundle is covered in wrapping, the person holding it is not actually gripping the *lulav*, *hadas*, and *arava* directly — he is gripping the silk. That interposition would constitute a *chatzitza*, an obstruction, potentially invalidating the *lekicha*. By leaving some unwrapped space at the bottom, the holder can make direct physical contact with the *arba'ah minim* themselves.

This is analogous to the modern practice, where the accordion-like holder used today is itself made from the *arba'ah minim* — which raises a distinct point addressed momentarily. But for now, the operative concern is the wrapping material acting as an obstruction.

### Kol L'Naoso

Rava then articulates a general principle: *kol l'naoso eino chotzetz* — anything that is present in order to beautify the *lulav* does not constitute an obstruction. If a material is there to enhance the appearance of the *mitzvah* object, it is not treated as foreign matter interposed between the hand and the *lulav*. It is, in some sense, part of the *lulav* as a beautified *mitzvah* object.

### Rabba's Objection

Rabba raises a separate objection. He rules: *lo lincub inish hoshana besudara* — one should not hold the *hoshana* with a cloth — *d'beina lekicha tamah v'leika* — because the Torah requires a complete act of taking, and you do not have it. The *lekicha* must be direct; holding the bundle via an interposed cloth means you are not truly taking the *lulav* but rather taking the cloth that happens to be holding the *lulav*. This is not merely a *chatzitza* concern — it is a concern about whether the act of taking has been performed at all.

### Rava: Indirect OK

Rava disagrees: *lekicha al yedei davar acher sh'ma lekicha* — taking something indirectly through another object still constitutes a valid act of taking. These are two distinct principles — the *chatzitza* concern and the *lekicha al yedei davar acher* concern — and Rava accepts both as non-disqualifying, while Rabba rejects both. The proof Rava adduces for indirect taking comes from an altogether different domain, to which the *gemara* now turns.

## Hyssop Precedent

### Hyssop Extension

Rava sources his principle of *lekicha al yedei davar acher* from the laws of *Para Aduma*. The *eizov* — hyssop — would be placed into a thin applicator tube containing the mixture of ashes and water from the *parah adumah*. This was the standard method for transporting and deploying the purifying mixture: the tube protected the ashes, and one would insert the *eizov*, allow it to absorb the mixture, withdraw it, and sprinkle it on the person requiring purification.

The *baraita* presents a case where the stem of the *eizov* has broken and is now too short to reach the water at the bottom of the tube. The solution: *metapko bechut uv'kush* — one extends it with a string or with a spindle, some additional piece affixed to the short stem — *ve'tovel v'ma'aleh* — dips the extended bundle into the tube and withdraws it — *v'ochez be'eizov u'mazeh* — grasps the *eizov* itself and sprinkles. Rashi notes that the instruction to grasp the *eizov* directly at the point of sprinkling is for practical control and accuracy; you want to sprinkle it directly on the person, and holding a string gives you poor aim. But the act of dipping — the *tevilah* — was never performed by direct contact; the *eizov* was lowered into the tube through the medium of the string.

### Indirect Taking

The Torah requires *v'lakach v'taval* — he shall take and dip. Yet the hyssop was dipped not through a direct taking but through the medium of the string. Why is this valid? *Ela lav shma mina l'kichah al yedei davar acher sh'ma l'kichah* — learn from this that taking something indirectly is considered a valid act of taking.

### Ashes Proof

The *gemara* initially deflects this proof: *dilma shani hatam* — perhaps it is different there — *kivan d'chavreih k'gufeih dami* — since the string was *attached* to the *eizov*, the string becomes like the *eizov* itself. An attached extension becomes part of the object; an unattached tool does not. So perhaps the ruling permitting the dipped *eizov* rests not on a general principle of indirect taking but on the specific fact of attachment.

The *gemara* therefore turns to a second proof from *Para Aduma*: *nafla mishfoperet l'shoket* — if ashes fell from their container into the vessel of water by accident, the mixture is *pasul*, because the Torah requires intentional human agency — *v'lakchu v'natnu*, he shall take and place. Accidental spillage does not constitute taking and placing. But: *hepilo hu*, if one *intentionally* tilted the container and poured the ashes in, *kasher* — it is valid — even though one never touched the ashes directly. One took them by holding the container and pouring. *Ela lav shma mina l'kichah al yedei davar acher sh'ma l'kichah* — here too, indirect taking counts as taking.

### Attached vs. Unattached

The *gemara* draws the distinction: the first hyssop proof could be deflected on the grounds that attachment makes the string part of the *eizov*. The ashes proof cannot be so deflected — the container is not attached to the ashes in any integral way — and it therefore stands as a clean demonstration that *lekicha al yedei davar acher sh'ma lekicha*.

## Two Principles

### Redundancy Question

Having established both that *chatzitza* is a concern and that *lekicha al yedei davar acher* is a concern, a natural question presents itself: why are these two separate discussions necessary? If Rabba's first objection — that there must be no *chatzitza* — already requires direct contact between hand and *lulav*, then his second objection — that indirect taking is invalid — adds nothing. The *chatzitza* rule already mandates the directness that the *lekicha* rule is also demanding. And conversely, if Rava's response to the *chatzitza* concern (*kol l'naoso eino chotzetz*) already dispenses with the requirement for unobstructed contact, why does he need a second response about *lekicha al yedei davar acher* being valid?

### Tosafot Resolution

Tosafot addresses this at length and arrives at a nuanced resolution. The two principles operate in distinct conceptual registers. The *chatzitza* concern arises when one intends to take the *lulav* directly — that is, one's act is defined as direct gripping — but something stands between the hand and the object. In that case, the intervening material is an obstruction to the act one is trying to perform. Rava's response: if that intervening material is there to beautify the *lulav*, it is not classified as foreign matter and therefore does not obstruct.

The *lekicha al yedei davar acher* concern is categorically different. Here, one is not even attempting to take the *lulav* directly — one is using some tool or holder to lift it. The question is whether this type of act — indirect taking — counts as *lekicha* at all. Rabba says it does not; Rava says it does.

To illustrate the distinction: imagine wrapping the *lulav* in silk and gripping it. One's intent is to hold the *lulav* directly through one's hand, but the silk is in the way. That is a *chatzitza* question. Now imagine instead fashioning some kind of holder, placing the *lulav* in it, and lifting the entire holder. Here one is not trying to grip the *lulav* directly — one is using the holder as an instrument of taking. That is a *lekicha al yedei davar acher* question.

One can further distinguish: in the *chatzitza* scenario, one might argue that the interposing material, by being attached in some sense to the *lulav* or being *l'naoso*, becomes like part of the *lulav* — akin to the string attached to the *eizov*, which became like the *eizov* itself. But in the *lekicha al yedei davar acher* scenario, where one uses tongs or a holder that is not attached to the *lulav*, perhaps the tool is an extension of one's hand rather than part of the *lulav*. Rabba rejects even this, and Rava permits it.

A practical example helps crystallize the issue: carrying the *lulav* inside its bag. Taking the *lulav* out of its bag while reciting the *bracha* is an excellent instance of *lekicha al yedei davar acher* — the bag is certainly not there to beautify the *lulav*, but one is lifting it through the bag. Whether one thereby fulfills the *mitzvah* at that moment depends on one's *kavanah*, but the structural question is real, and it is worth noting that if one *does* already discharge the *mitzvah* in this fashion, one can still recite the *bracha* afterward according to certain *rishonim* if one has not yet performed the *nanuim*.

### Min B'Mino

Tosafot raises yet a third principle: *min b'mino eino chotzetz* — an object of the same type does not constitute an obstruction. If *hadas* leaves or *lulav* leaves are interposed between the hand and the *lulav* bundle, they do not count as foreign matter.

### Context Matters

These three principles — *kol l'naoso*, *lekicha al yedei davar acher*, and *min b'mino eino chotzetz* — do not operate identically in every context. In the laws of *mikvah*, for instance, the baseline requirement is *v'rachatz besaro bamayim* — the body must contact the water — and consequently the stakes for *chatzitza* are higher. An argument that something is there to beautify, or that it is the same type of material, may carry far less weight when the Torah explicitly demands bodily contact with the purifying water. Similarly, in the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *kohanim*'s feet must be in contact with the ground, since that physical connection is part of the sanctity of service. The *chatzitza* standards there are correspondingly more demanding.

In the context of *lulav*, however, the Torah never says the *lulav* must touch one's flesh. The requirement is *u-lekachtem lachem* — you shall take for yourselves. The question is whether the act constitutes a *lekicha*. Here, arguments about what does and does not count as an obstruction to that act can afford to be more accommodating, because the underlying norm is about the *action* of taking, not about the *physical contact* between body and object. The final words of the relevant Tosafot capture this exactly: *v'ein l'damot kol had'varim* — one cannot simply transpose these principles from one context to another; each domain has its own governing logic.

## Assembly Warnings

### No Cramping

The *gemara* records a series of practical warnings from Rabba regarding the physical assembly of the *lulav* bundle. Rabba warns: *lo raduz inish lulava be-Hoshana* — one should not cram the *lulav* into the *hoshana* (the bound *hadas*-and-*arava* bundle). The concern is *d'dilma natri tarfei ve-havei chatzitza* — that in the process of forcing the *lulav* in, leaves of the *hadas* or *arava* will be torn off and become loose, lying between the hand and the still-attached portions of the bundle, thereby creating a *chatzitza*.

### Rava Counters

Rava counters: *min be-mino eino chotzetz* — leaves of *hadas*, *arava*, or *lulav* that have come loose are not foreign matter; they are the same species as the species one is holding. Therefore, they cannot function as an obstruction. It is also worth noting in passing that Rabba's warning assumes a certain number of leaves can fall without rendering the *lulav* itself *pasul* — which implicitly acknowledges that some leaf loss does not disqualify the *hadas* or *arava*, though the *gemara* does not spell out exactly how many leaves may fall.

### No Cutting

Rabba issues a second warning: *lo ligoz inish lulava be-Hoshana* — one should not cut the *lulav* shorter while it is already assembled within the *hoshana* bundle. Trimming the bottom of the *lulav* to reduce its length severs the lowest junction points, where leaves are attached. Once those junctions are cut, leaves that were previously attached at the very bottom are now free and floating — *de-mishtari hutza ve-havei chatzitza* — and they will constitute a *chatzitza* between the hand and the intact *lulav* above.

### Rava Again

Again Rava responds with the same principle: *min be-mino eino chotzetz*. Severed *lulav* leaves lying at the base of the bundle are not classified as obstructing matter, since they are of the same species as the *lulav* one is holding. The *chatzitza* category, in this context, is reserved for genuinely foreign and heterogeneous material.

## Smelling Rules

### Huktza Principle

The *gemara* now moves beyond *chatzitza* to a distinct but related topic: the status of *mitzvah* objects with respect to other uses during the period of the *mitzvah*. Rava rules: *hadas shel mitzvah assur le-hariach bo* — it is forbidden to smell the *hadas* designated for the *mitzvah*. The operative concept is *huktza le-mitzvato* — an object set aside for a *mitzvah* is considered fully given over to that sacred purpose and may not be used for other purposes, even incidental ones that do not detract from the *mitzvah* at all.

### Etrog Analysis

The same principle applies to the *etrog*, but with an inverse result: *etrog shel mitzvah mutar le-hariach bo* — one may smell the *etrog* of the *mitzvah*. Why? *Etrog le-achila kai* — the *etrog's* primary use is eating. Therefore, *ki aktzyei me-achila aktzyei* — when it is designated for *mitzvah* use, it is the eating that is made off-limits, not the smelling. Smelling is an incidental benefit, not the central function of an *etrog*, and so smelling it remains permitted.

### Hadass Analysis

By contrast, *hadas le-reicha kai* — the primary use of *hadas* is for its fragrance. People smell *hadas*; that is what one does with myrtle. Therefore, *me-reicha aktzyei* — when the *hadas* is set aside for the *mitzvah*, it is the smelling that is made off-limits. The *mitzvah* object's central identity determines which use is foreclosed.

### Rashi's Source

Rashi's comment here is significant. Rather than invoking a general principle of *huktza le-mitzvato* applicable across all *mitzvot*, Rashi derives this ruling specifically from the *sukkah*: *de-gamrinan mi-sukkah de-yalfinan be-perek kama de-chal shem shamayim al atzei sukkah ve-asiri be-hana'ah kol shiva ve-havei huktza le-mitzvah* — we learn from the *sukkah*, where we established in the first chapter that the name of God rests upon the wood of the *sukkah* and it is therefore forbidden for personal benefit for all seven days — that same principle of *huktza le-mitzvah* extends to the *lulav* as well. This is a notably specific derivation. It is not merely a general legal category but one rooted in the elevated, *Mikdash*-infused sanctity of the *sukkah* and, by extension, its associated *mitzvot*. This reinforces a broader thematic pattern in *masechet Sukkah*: the *sukkah* and its *mitzvot* are understood through a vocabulary of divine presence and sanctity that approaches the language of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

## Shabbat Smelling

### Hadass Permitted

The *gemara* now pivots to a related but distinct scenario: not the *hadas* or *etrog* of the *mitzvah*, but *hadas* or *etrog* growing on a tree during *Shabbat* — any *Shabbat* of the year, entirely apart from *Sukkot*. Rava rules: *hadas bimchubar mutar le-hariach bo* — one may smell *hadas* growing on a tree on *Shabbat*. There is no concern that smelling it will lead to detaching it.

### Etrog Banned

The ruling is reversed for *etrog*: *etrog bimchubar assur le-hariach bo* — one may not smell an *etrog* growing on a tree on *Shabbat*.

### Prevention Logic

The rationale follows directly from the identities established above. *Hadas dile-ariach ka'ei* — the entire point of *hadas* is to smell it. *Sharei lei lo ati lemigzyei* — if you let someone smell it right there on the branch, they have obtained the benefit they sought and have no reason to pluck it. But *etrog de-achila ka'ei* — the *etrog's* purpose is eating. *Ei shreit lei* — if you permit smelling it, the person smells its fragrance, desires to taste it, and *ati lemigzyei* — will come to pluck it or, as Rashi notes, bite into it directly from the tree, which is itself a *Shabbat* violation of detachment. *Ein lecha talush gadol mizeh* — there is no more complete form of detachment than biting. The primary use determines the risk: give someone access to the incidental use and they will pursue the primary use, which on *Shabbat* requires severing a growing thing.

## Hand Placement

### Placement Rule

Rava rules: *lulav biyamin ve'etrog bismoal* — the *lulav* is held in the right hand and the *etrog* in the left. For a left-handed person, the halacha reverses: the stronger hand takes the *lulav*. The principle is that the dominant hand should hold the *lulav*.

### 3 vs 1 Logic

The reasoning: *hanei telat mitzvot ve-hai chada mitzvah* — the *lulav* bundle represents three of the four species (the *lulav* itself, *hadas*, and *arava*), while the *etrog* represents only one. Three species outweigh one, and the stronger hand is given the larger share of the *mitzvah*.

### Practical Custom

It is worth noting that even though we do not rule like Rabbi Yehuda that the *lulav tzarich oged* — that the *lulav* strictly requires an *eged* — all of the preceding discussions about assembly, *chatzitza*, cutting, and holding clearly assume that one *does* assemble the *lulav*, *hadas*, and *arava* together into a bound bundle. Even according to the *chachamim*, this is the universal custom, and the combined bundle of three species, held in the right hand, constitutes the *lulav* side of the four-species taking. The *etrog* is held separately in the left hand and brought together with the bundle for the *lekicha*.

## Bracha Formulation

### RY Question

Rabbi Yirmiya poses a question to Rabbi Zerika: *mai ta'ama lo mevarchinan ela al netilat lulav?* — why does the *bracha* mention only *netilat lulav* and not reference all four species? Why not say *al netilat arba minim* or spell out all four — *peri etz hadar, kapot tmarim, anaf etz avot, ve-arvei nachal*?

### RZ Answer

Rabbi Zerika answers: *hu degavo'a mikulam* — the *lulav* is naturally the tallest of the four species. It visually dominates the bundle. When people look at the assembled *mitzvah* object, their eye is drawn to the *lulav*, and thus the *bracha* is named after what stands out.

### Height Insufficient

Rabbi Yirmiya then pushes back: *veligbeihye le'etrog ulevarech* — one could simply raise the *etrog* high above the rest and then make the *bracha* in its name. Rabbi Zerika responds: *hu bemino gavo'a mikulam* — it is naturally taller than all the others. The *lulav's* dominance is intrinsic and organic, not a function of how one happens to be holding things at a given moment. Artificially elevating the *etrog* does not override the natural prominence of the *lulav*.

### Rambam Option

This discussion about the *bracha* and the *lulav's* dominance touches on a striking *halakha* noted by the Rambam in connection with the principle that *arba'ah minim she'balulav me'akvim zeh et zeh* — all four species are required to discharge the *mitzvah*. The *Geonim*, whose position the Rambam accepts, hold that one need not take all four species simultaneously. If, after the fact, one took each species separately at different times during the day, one has been *yotze*. Tosafot disagrees, arguing that the Torah's formulation implies a single act of taking encompassing all four.

Within the Rambam's position, however, the *brachot* would be differentiated accordingly: upon taking the *lulav*, one would say *al netilat* (or *lekichat*) *kapot tmarim*; upon taking the *etrog*, *al lekichat peri etz hadar*; upon taking the *hadas*, *al lekichat anaf etz avot*; and so on. The Rambam presumably draws on Geonic tradition for these formulations, given that most *brachot* were crystallized during the Geonic period and the principle of not introducing new *brachot* extends through that era. It is a striking contrast to our practice of a single *bracha* of *al netilat lulav* covering all four species in one act.

## Shaking Location

### Mishna Q

The *gemara* introduces a new *mishna*: *heichan hayu m'na'anim?* — where did one shake the *lulav*? The question arises somewhat abruptly, since the *mishna* has not yet formally introduced the topic of *nanuim* at all. The *gemara* notes that the *nanuim* were already implied earlier, where it was established that a *lulav* must be three *tefachim* in length in order to be shakeable — *k'dei l'na'anea bo kasher* — which presupposed that shaking is an inherent part of the *mitzvah*. The current *mishna* now addresses the question of when and where during *Hallel* this shaking takes place.

### BH Position

Beit Hillel rules: *be'Hodu laShem techila v'sof* — at *Hodu laShem* at the beginning and at the end of the relevant *perek* of *Tehillim*.

### Psalm Structure

Rashi draws attention to something that is easy to overlook: what we experience as separate chapters in *Tehillim* — 117 and 118 — are, according to the *masora*, actually a single *perek*. *Tehillim* 117 opens *Hallelu et Hashem kol goyim*, and *Tehillim* 118 begins *Hodu laShem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo*, continues through *yomar na Yisrael*, *yomar na veit Aharon*, *yomar na yirei Hashem*, *min hametzar karati Yah*, and concludes with *Hodu laShem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo*. It is all one *perek*. The *Hodu* at the start of what we call chapter 118 and the *Hodu* at the very end of that chapter — which is also the end of *Hallel* — are the *techila v'sof* referred to by Beit Hillel. The centrality of *Hodu laShem* to *Hallel* is so self-evident to those who recite it that the *gemara* can refer to it simply as the beginning and the end and be immediately understood.

### BS Addition

Beit Shammai adds: *af be'anna Hashem hatzlicha na* — one should also shake at *Anna Hashem hatzlicha na*, the verse that follows *Anna Hashem hoshi'ah na*. The precise rationale for Beit Shammai's addition is not entirely transparent from the *sugyat* itself.

## R. Akiva Observation

### Eyewitness

Rabbi Akiva reports: *tzofeh hayiti b'Rabban Gamliel v'Rabbi Yehoshua* — I was observing Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua during *Hallel* on *Sukkot*. Such first-person eyewitness accounts of how great *tannaim* conducted themselves in practice are a recurring and distinctive feature of *masechet Sukkah* — more so, it seems, than in many other *masechetot*. Rabbi Akiva says that he watched carefully how these two figures held and shook their *lulavim*.

### Practice Contrast

What he observed was a striking divergence between popular practice and expert practice: *she'kol ha'am hayu m'na'anim et lulaveihem* — the entire crowd was shaking their *lulavim* at various points throughout *Hallel* — *v'hem lo na'anu ela b'Ana Hashem Hoshi'ana v'Ana Hashem Hatzlicha Na* — but Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua shook only at *Ana Hashem Hoshi'ah Na* and *Ana Hashem Hatzlicha Na*, and not at additional points. The masses shook broadly; the *gedolim* were precise and limited.

## Tosafot Custom

### Rite Differences

The *gemara's* discussion of where to shake prompts an examination of current practice, which Tosafot addresses. The Sephardic custom is for the entire congregation to recite *Hodu laShem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo* together simultaneously, performing the *nanuim* once at *Hodu laShem ki tov* — no responsive recitation, simply a unified communal declaration with shaking. The Ashkenazic custom involves a responsive format: the *shaliach tzibbur* leads, the *kahal* responds, and the shaking accompanies the congregational responses. These represent genuinely different practices, both of which Tosafot engages with.

### Trees Burst Forth

Tosafot explains the underlying scriptural logic of Beit Hillel's positions — specifically, why one shakes both at *Hodu laShem ki tov* and at *Ana Hashem Hoshi'ah Na*. A verse in *Divrei HaYamim* reads: *az yerannenu atzei haya'ar milifnei Hashem ki va lishpot et ha'aretz* — then the trees of the forest will burst forth in song before God, for He comes to judge the earth. The immediately following verse says *Hodu laShem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo*, and the verse after that reads *v'imru hoshi'enu elohei yishenu*. The shaking of the *lulav* — the trees, as it were, expressing themselves — accompanies precisely these two moments of praise: *Hodu laShem* and *Ana Hashem Hoshi'ah Na*. The trees bursting into song are the *arba'ah minim* being waved; the words they accompany are these two key verses. This is, in Tosafot's own assessment, a beautiful explanation of Beit Hillel's ruling.

### Responsive Format

Returning to the Ashkenazic practice: the *shaliach tzibbur* recites *yomar na Yisrael* and the *tzibbur* responds *Hodu laShem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo*, shaking at each *Hodu* response. This responsive format gives a congregational, antiphonal quality to the *Hallel* at this juncture, and the shaking accompanies each of the *kahal's* *Hodu* responses throughout the sequence.

### Chazan Timing

As for the *shaliach tzibbur* himself: when does he perform the *nanuim*? Tosafot presents two views. The primary view is that the *shaliach tzibbur* does not shake during his own portions — the *yomar na Yisrael*, *yomar na veit Aharon*, and *yomar na yirei Hashem* — since those are his leading parts, not the congregational responses. However, Tosafot also records a second view: *yesh mefarshim* — some explain — that since the *tzibbur* responds with *Hodu* and shakes at each repetition, the *shaliach tzibbur* also shakes during his *yomar na* verses in concert with the congregation's ongoing participation. On this latter view, the *shaliach tzibbur* shakes throughout the responsive sequence — his verses and the congregational responses together form a continuous unit of praise and waving.

The discussion of the end of Tosafot on the relationship between shaking the *lulav* at the time of the *bracha* versus shaking it during *Hallel*, and the interaction between *lekicha* and *nanuim*, is left for the following day's shiur.
