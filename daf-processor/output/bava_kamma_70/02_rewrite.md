# Bava Kamma 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Harsha'ah Overview

### Rabbi Yochanan & Harsha'…

The daf opens by returning to the principle of *Rabbi Yochanan* — *gezel velo nityashu hab'alim sheneihem einam yekholim lehakdish* — that once an item has been stolen and the owners have not yet despaired of recovering it, neither party can sanctify it: the thief because it is not his, and the owner because it is not under his control. The Gemara had extended this principle broadly to cover selling, gifting, redeeming, leasing (*maskir*), and declaring ownerless (*mafkir*). The core insight is that ownership alone is insufficient to exercise the full rights of an owner; the item must also be under one's actual control (*birshuto*).

With that principle firmly established, the Gemara now draws out a striking implication — one that introduces a legal instrument most readers would otherwise barely know existed: the *harsha'ah*, a form of power of attorney.

### Harsha'ah vs. Agency

The Gemara introduces the *orchata* (also written *adrachta* in some *Rishonim*) — a written power of attorney, derived from *leharshot*, to authorize or give permission. This instrument is fundamentally different from ordinary *shelichut* (agency). Appointing an agent to buy or sell something on one's behalf is a recognized *halakhic* institution. But a power of attorney is something qualitatively distinct: it confers the right to litigate in court, to represent one's interests, and to render decisions that are binding on the principal. This resembles in some respects the institution of *apotropus*, but the *harsha'ah* raises its own pressing question: what is the *halakhic* mechanism that makes it operative?

### Standing Problem

The practical problem the *harsha'ah* is designed to solve is the problem of standing. If Reuven claims that Shimon is wrongfully holding his iPhone, Reuven might wish to authorize David to litigate on his behalf. But Shimon can simply turn to David and say: *lav ba'al devarim didi at* — you are not my litigant; this is not your dispute. In secular court, we would call this lack of standing. The only way David can bring Shimon to court is if he has some personal stake or ownership interest in the property. This is the crux of how the *harsha'ah* must work: Reuven must somehow transfer to David a degree of ownership — at least a fiction of ownership — so that David can claim the matter as his own.

Why, then, would land present no problem while *metaltelin* (chattel) does? Because land, even if contested, is always "there." The concept of *eino birshuto* — not under one's control — does not apply to land in the same way. But chattel held by a denier is effectively in the other party's possession, and Reuven cannot grant what is not under his control.

## Harsha'ah on Chattel

### Kafrei Restriction

The *Nahardai* academy ruled: *lo kasvinan orchata amitaltelin* — we do not write a power of attorney on chattel. If the dispute concerns land, one may write a *harsha'ah*, because land is never truly removed from one's *reshut*. But for movable property — the iPhone in Shimon's pocket — such a document cannot be issued. The reason invoked is Rabbi Yochanan's principle: since Reuven cannot freely transfer something that is not under his control, the *harsha'ah* over *metaltelin* would be halakhically inoperative.

### Michzi Kesikra

A second version (*ika d'amri*) of the Nahardai position refines this significantly: *lo kasvinan orchata amitaltelin dekafrei* — we do not write a *harsha'ah* on chattel only when the other party is *denying* (*kafrei*) that the item belongs to Reuven. If Shimon says "I know it's yours, but I'm holding it as collateral for a debt you owe me" (and Reuven denies the debt), that is not *kafrei* — Shimon concedes the underlying ownership, and in that case a *harsha'ah* may be written. But if Shimon says "it's mine, not yours," then writing a document that says "I hereby transfer my iPhone to David" looks like an outright fabrication — *michzi kesikra* (appearing as a lie). Even if Reuven is telling the truth, the document on its face is absurd: the item is in Shimon's pocket and Shimon claims it is his. Courts would refuse to recognize such a document.

### Rishonim Challenge

This refined position, however, immediately draws a challenge from the *Rishonim*. The second version of the Nahardai ruling states: *aval lo kafrei, kasvinan* — but if Shimon is not denying ownership, we do write the *harsha'ah*. Yet the *Rabbi Yochanan* problem of *eino birshuto* presumably applies here too! Even when Shimon admits the item is Reuven's but refuses to return it, the property is still not under Reuven's control. How can Reuven transfer it to David?

The *Rishonim* answer that this concession is telling: fundamentally, the *harsha'ah* really cannot work perfectly within Rabbi Yochanan's framework. It is, at its core, a *takkanah d'rabbanan* — a rabbinic enactment, a legal fiction. The Sages recognized that the legal system requires such an instrument, and they permit it despite the technical problem. The next section of the Gemara will go further in clarifying exactly what kind of fiction is being constructed.

---

## Ownership Fiction

### Written Harsha'ah

The Gemara now presents the Nahardai academy's second statement: *orchata d'la ksiv bei zil dun v'zachi v'apik l'nafshach* — a *harsha'ah* document that does not contain the clause "go litigate and extract the property for yourself" is worthless (*leit bei mashasha*). Why? Because Shimon can respond to David with *lav ba'al devarim didi at* — you are not my litigant. Without the clause authorizing David to act on his own behalf, David has no claim against Shimon; he is merely Reuven's messenger, and Reuven's dispute is not David's to press.

The only thing that grants David standing is a degree of transferred ownership, coupled with the framing that he is acting in his own interest. Yet the paradox is obvious: if Reuven had truly and fully transferred ownership to David, there would be no need to write "do it on your own behalf" — David would simply be suing for his own property and would have no obligation to return anything to Reuven. The *harsha'ah* is thus walking a tightrope: enough transferred ownership to grant standing, but not so much that David is the true and exclusive owner.

### Abaye: Partial Ownership

Abaye refines this. He says: *i katav lei lemachatza leshlish uleravia* — if the *harsha'ah* document stipulates that David is entitled to keep half, a third, or a quarter of whatever he wins (analogous to a contingency fee arrangement), then the clause "do it on your own behalf" is unnecessary. David clearly has his own stake in the matter, and his interest in his portion gives him standing to press the entire case: *migo demishtayei dina apalga, mishtayei dina akula* — since he can litigate for his own portion, he can litigate for the whole.

Abaye's formulation makes explicit what was already implicit: in most real-world uses of the *harsha'ah*, David does not actually pocket the proceeds. He is paid a fee, or nothing beyond the principal's gratitude, and returns whatever he recovers. The fiction of acting "on one's own behalf" is therefore largely just that — a fiction. And this actually helps dissolve the Rabbi Yochanan problem: since Reuven is not truly transferring ownership, the principle that one cannot transfer what is not under one's control is not genuinely being violated. The *harsha'ah* functions as rabbinic fiction of ownership just sufficient to confer standing.

One might ask: could Reuven simply give David a token share — a *perutah's* worth — to establish his ownership stake minimally? This is a reasonable question, and it is not directly resolved here, but it sharpens the underlying principle: at some point the ownership stake becomes so nominal that it looks like a farce rather than a genuine transfer.

### Ameimar: Retention Risk

Ameimar draws out the uncomfortable logical consequence of the ownership fiction. If the *harsha'ah* works by genuinely (even if partially) transferring ownership to David — if the document says "go litigate on your own behalf" without any contingency clause — then if David wins the case and simply keeps the money, Reuven is stuck. *I tafas, lo mapkinan mineih* — if David seizes the proceeds, we do not remove them from him, because the fiction of ownership that enabled him to litigate also entitles him to retain what he won.

This is a sobering consequence: whoever is named in a *harsha'ah* must be deeply trusted, because the legal mechanism that empowers him also enables him to retain the proceeds against the principal's wishes. The practical response — and this is a reasonable one — is to execute a separate written agreement alongside the *harsha'ah* by which David commits to returning whatever he recovers. The *harsha'ah* says "take it for yourself," and the side agreement creates the enforceable obligation to return it. This dual-document structure was indeed part of how the institution functioned in practice.

## Rav Ashi: Agent Only

### Court Judgment Clause

Rav Ashi offers a resolution to Ameimar's concern. He says: *keivan dikhtiv lei kol dimistanei mindina kabileti alai* — since the *harsha'ah* document contains the clause "any judgment rendered by the court I accept upon myself," this language proves that the arrangement is not a true transfer of ownership. If Reuven had really transferred the property to David, why would Reuven declare that he accepts the court's judgment? The court's judgment would be David's concern, not Reuven's. The very fact that Reuven declares his acceptance of the outcome reveals that this is still fundamentally Reuven's case, and David is his agent — *shaliach shaveih* — not a new owner.

Therefore, Rav Ashi concludes, even if David were to attempt to keep the recovered property, Reuven could retrieve it. The court judgment clause in the *harsha'ah* is the textual signal that David has been constituted as an agent, not an owner.

### Teiku & Compromise

The Gemara records a middle position: *shutaf shaveih* — David has been made a partner (*shutaf*), not merely an agent. This corresponds to the *machatza, shelish, u'revia* formulation of Abaye — David has been given a genuine partial ownership stake, which entitles him to his portion but not to the whole. The practical question distinguishing *shutaf* from *shaliach* is *lemitpas palga* — could David seize his half? As a partner he could claim his share; as a mere agent he could not retain anything.

### Final Halakha

The *halakha* is *shaliach shaveih* — David is constituted as an agent only. Significantly, if one reads the *Rif*, he transmits that this final ruling was not actually found in the Gemara text itself but was a ruling of the *Geonim* — specifically attributed to Rav Hai Gaon — and was recorded in the language of the Gemara by the *Hilkhot Gedolot*, from which it was absorbed into the Gemara text. This is a well-known phenomenon: the *Geonim* quote Aramaic *sugyot* in Geonic works, append their rulings in the same style, and later readers assume the ruling is part of the original Gemara.

The ruling that David is *shaliach* solves the concern about retention — David cannot keep the property — but it reopens the original standing problem. If he is only an agent, how does he have standing? Shimon can again say *lav ba'al devarim didi at*. The answer the *Rishonim* give is that the *harsha'ah* is ultimately a *takkanah*, a juridical fiction: David is fundamentally an agent (*shaliach*), but the *harsha'ah* document creates a fictional element of ownership just sufficient to grant him standing. Since it is only a fiction — not a true transfer — we need not worry overly about Rabbi Yochanan's principle of *eino birshuto*, because no genuine ownership transfer is occurring. And since he remains fundamentally an agent, he cannot retain the proceeds.

What the *harsha'ah* creates, then, is a new legal instrument that does not exist natively in *halakha*. *Halakha* knows *shelichut* and it knows ownership; it does not natively know power of attorney. The *harsha'ah* blends the two — fundamentally an agency relationship, with a dash of fictive ownership to resolve the standing problem — and the *takkanah* framework legitimates this construct.

## Tosafot Practice

### Kafrei Expansion

Rabbeinu Chananel records, and Tosafot cites approvingly, that in the practice of subsequent generations the *harsha'ah* was written even on *metaltelin dekafrei* — chattel that the opposing party denies. This directly contradicts the Nahardai ruling as initially understood, which prohibited the *harsha'ah* in exactly such cases due to *michzi kesikra*. Rabbeinu Chananel himself acknowledges that he does not know the reason for this expanded practice; Rabbeinu Tam provides the explanation.

### Agency Rationale

Rabbeinu Tam argues that the operative *halakha* follows the second version (*lishna batra*) of the Nahardai position — the one that anchors the restriction in *michzi kesikra* rather than in Rabbi Yochanan's principle. And then, as Tosafot proceeds to demonstrate from another Gemara in Ketubot, *ulmichezi kesikra lo chaishinan* — we are ultimately not concerned with something that merely *appears* to be a fabrication, as long as it is not actually false. Since Reuven genuinely claims the property is his, writing the *harsha'ah* is not a *sheker*; it only superficially resembles one. Moreover, since the Gemara has concluded that the *harsha'ah* is fundamentally *shelichut* — agency — the ownership-transfer element is a minor technical fiction, not the core mechanism. One need not worry so much about appearances when the underlying legal structure rests on agency rather than a contested ownership claim.

### Malve Extension

The expansion of *harsha'ah* practice raised a further question in the era of the *Rishonim*: can a *harsha'ah* be written for a loan (*malve*) case, where there is no tangible property to transfer at all, even fictively? If Reuven lent Michael a thousand dinars and Michael denies the debt, there is no object to "give" to David as a way of conferring standing. The entire fictive-ownership mechanism seems unavailable.

The *Rishonim* debate this, and in practice — particularly as the need expanded with communities living at great distances and parties unable to appear in court themselves — many *Rishonim* permitted the *harsha'ah* even for *malve* cases. The reasoning is that if the *halakhic* foundation is ultimately *shelichut* sustained by a rabbinic *takkanah*, and the *takkanah* is enacted to accommodate a genuine systemic need, then the *takkanah* can extend even to cases where the ownership-transfer fiction is formally unavailable. The *harsha'ah* as an institution, in other words, outgrew its original mechanical justification and came to rest on its *takkanah* foundation alone.

## Mishnah: Two Witnesses

### Standard Cases

The Mishnah on 70a opens: *ganav al pi shnayim vetavach umachar al pihem* — if someone broke in and stole, as established by two witnesses, and then slaughtered or sold the animal, also witnessed by those same two, he pays the fourfold or fivefold penalty. Remarkably, the Mishnah adds: *o al pi shnayim acherim* — the testimony of slaughter or sale may come from an entirely different pair of witnesses. The two sets of witnesses combine to produce liability for the enhanced penalty.

### Exemption Cases

The Mishnah then catalogues a series of cases in which the thief is *chayav* to pay the fourfold or fivefold penalty, alongside implicit contrasts with cases in which he would be exempt. Several need brief explanation.

*Ganav umachar beShabbat* — if he stole and *sold* on Shabbat, he is liable, because selling involves no *melakhah d'oraita* and presents no issue of *qam leih* (the principle exempting from monetary liability when one's act carries the death penalty). *Ganav umachar l'avodah zarah* — selling to idolatry is similarly not a capital offense. But *ganav vetavach beShabbat* — stealing and *slaughtering* on Shabbat is a *melacha d'oraita*, potentially *chayav misa*, and would be exempt. *Ganav vetavach l'avodah zarah* — slaughtering as an act of idolatrous worship is *chayav misa* and similarly exempt.

*Ganav vetavach beyom hakippurim* — he stole and slaughtered on Yom Kippur. The *chiddush* here is significant: slaughtering on Yom Kippur is *chayav kareit*, not *chayav misa*. The Mishnah assumes — and this is its innovative ruling — that *qam leih* (which exempts from monetary liability) applies only when the act is *chayav misa*, not merely *chayav kareit*. Hence he still pays.

*Ganav mishel aviv vetavach umachar ve'achar meisu aviv* — he stole from his father, then slaughtered or sold, and only afterward did the father die. The sequence matters because had the father died before the slaughter or sale, Reuven would have inherited the animal and would be slaughtering his own property. Since the slaughter preceded the father's death, liability is intact.

Other cases include stealing and then sanctifying (*hikdish*) the animal before slaughtering — in which case the slaughter is of *hekdesh* and the verse (*mishel rei'eihu*, "of his fellow") excludes it. And *ganav vetavach ve'achar hikdish* — stole, slaughtered, and then sanctified — where the sequence preserves liability.

### Rabbi Shimon

The Mishnah concludes with cases in which slaughter took place but not for ordinary consumption: for medicinal purposes (*lerefu'ah*), for dogs (*leklavim*), or where the animal was slaughtered and found to be a *treifah*, or where *chulin* were slaughtered in the *azarah* (temple courtyard). In all these cases the Sages hold the thief liable. *Rabbi Shimon poter bishnei elu* — Rabbi Shimon exempts in the last two cases. His reasoning: *shechitah she'einah re'uyah lo shemah shechitah* — a slaughtering that does not render the meat fit for human consumption is not legally considered an act of *shechitah*. Slaughtering for dog food could be eaten by a person; that is *shechitah*. But if the animal was already a *treifah* internally, the slaughter did not make it permissible, and it is therefore not *shechitah* in the relevant sense. Similarly, *chulin ba'azarah* renders the meat forbidden despite the act. The Sages disagree: as long as the act of *shechitah* was performed, liability follows regardless of the outcome.

## Davar Rule Explained

### Davar Principle

The Gemara opens its analysis of the Mishnah with a challenge: *leima matnitin dela k'Rabbi Akiva* — perhaps our Mishnah does not accord with the position of Rabbi Akiva. For Rabbi Akiva says *davar velo chatzi davar* — testimony must concern a complete matter, not half a matter. The verse (*al pi shnayim eidim yakum davar*) specifies that what "stands" by the testimony of two witnesses must be a *davar* — a whole thing.

The Gemara illustrates this from a *baraita* concerning *chazakah*: the presumption of ownership established by three years of uncontested possession. The case: in year one, two witnesses saw Reuven eating the produce of a contested field; in year two, two different witnesses saw the same; in year three, another two witnesses saw it again. Does this combination constitute a *chazakah*? The Sages say yes. Rabbi Akiva says no. His reasoning: *al pi shnayim eidim yakum davar* — each pair of witnesses must testify to a complete matter. No single pair is testifying to a *chazakah* — only to one year of possession. And critically, taken in isolation, each pair is actually testifying to a *gezeilah*: if it is known that the field was previously mine, and you saw Reuven on it for one year, that is testimony that he is stealing from me, not that he owns it. Only when all three years are combined does the picture change. Rabbi Akiva holds that witnesses cannot combine in this fashion.

### Abaye Exception

Abaye rescues the Mishnah from the contradiction: *afilu teima Rabbi Akiva* — even according to Rabbi Akiva, the Mishnah can be valid. Rabbi Akiva's restriction applies only when each set of witnesses, taken individually, fails to establish any complete legal fact. But Rabbi Akiva would concede in certain cases — and Abaye offers the paradigmatic example.

### Marriage & Adultery

*Mi lo modeh Rabbi Akiva bishnayim omrim kiddesh u'vishnayim omrim ba'al?* — Does not Rabbi Akiva concede in the case where one pair of witnesses says "she was betrothed to Reuven" and another pair says "she had sexual relations with Shimon"? Together these establish adultery. Separately, the first pair establishes a complete legal fact — *kiddushin* — independently of the second pair's testimony. The second pair's testimony is incomplete without the first, but the first pair's testimony stands perfectly well on its own.

*Da'af al gav d'edei bi'ah tzrichin l'edei kiddushin* — even though the testimony of the sexual act is meaningless without the testimony of the prior marriage, *keivan d'edei kiddushin lo tzrichi l'edei bi'ah* — since the testimony of the marriage does not require the testimony of the act to be complete, *davar karinan bei* — the first testimony is a *davar*. Once it is accepted by the court as a complete legal fact establishing the woman's married status, the second testimony can be received, because in that established context it too is saying something legally complete and consequential.

The same logic applies to the Mishnah. *Af al gav d'edei tvichah tzrichin l'edei geneivah* — the witnesses to the slaughter are meaningless without the established theft, *keivan d'edei geneivah lo tzrichi l'edei tvichah* — but the witnesses to the burglary stand on their own; they establish *chayav kefel* (twofold liability) without needing the slaughter testimony. Once the court accepts the theft as an established fact, the testimony of slaughter or sale speaks to a legally complete matter. Rabbi Akiva can therefore accept such combined testimony.

## Pubic Hair Case

### Hair Locations

The Gemara then asks: if Rabbi Akiva's restriction is so limited, what *does* it exclude according to the Rabbis? *L'matu'ei echad b'gavah v'echad b'kreisa* — what is excluded is a case where one witness saw one pubic hair on the girl's back and another witness saw one hair on her abdomen. Together these might seem to establish that she has the two hairs required to be considered *halachically* an adult (*gedolah*). But the Gemara immediately objects: that is not merely *chatzi davar* — half a matter; it is *chatzi davar v'chatzi eidus* — half a matter and half testimony, meaning each witness is only one of the two required, which is a different and more basic problem than the one Rabbi Akiva is addressing.

### Years vs. Hair

The Gemara refines: *ella l'matu'ei shnayim omrim echad b'gavah u'shnayim omrim echad b'kreisa* — the excluded case is where two witnesses saw one hair in one location and two different witnesses saw one hair in a different location. Each pair has full testimonial standing, but together they are trying to establish majority by combining two separate observations. This, the Gemara says, does not work, because *hani amri ketanah hi v'hani amri ketanah hi* — each pair, taken individually, is actually testifying that she is still a minor. Neither pair establishes a complete legal fact; each is, on its own terms, evidence of minority, not majority.

This raises the obvious question Tosafot flags: why is this different from the three years of *chazakah*, where the Rabbis allow combination? Each year's testimony, taken alone, would equally suggest the person is trespassing. Yet the Rabbis permit combining those three testimonies.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot offers two explanations. The first: the witnesses who testified about each year saw everything there was to see in that year — a complete inspection of a year's worth of evidence. In the case of the hairs, each pair of witnesses saw only one hair and evidently did not conduct a complete inspection; they left the other location to another pair. That incompleteness of examination is what makes it a *chatzi davar*. The second explanation attributed to Rav Ashi's *peirush*: the testimony of each year's witnesses has independent evidentiary weight — specifically, it establishes liability for that year's produce, making the person who was on the land *chayav* to pay restitution for that year. Because each pair's testimony is actionable on its own terms (even if the conclusion they support shifts when all three are combined), it has the independent standing required to be a *davar*. In the case of the hairs, each pair's testimony, taken alone, is not actionable toward any legal conclusion; it only becomes meaningful in conjunction with the other.

## Shabbat Sale Issue

### Baraita Sale

The Mishnah includes the case of *ganav umachar beShabbat* — he stole and sold on Shabbat — holding him liable for the fourfold or fivefold payment. This is straightforward: selling involves no *melakha d'oraita* and incurs no *chayav misa*. But a *baraita* teaches the opposite — *patur* — that he is exempt. The Gemara must explain the *baraita*.

### Fig Transaction

Rami bar Chama provides the scenario the *baraita* has in mind. The case is: *hakos teina mite'anti ukni lach gneivotai* — the buyer says to the thief, "Go pick figs from my fig tree, and in that act of picking (which constitutes your payment), you simultaneously sell me the stolen animal." The thief goes and picks the figs on Shabbat, thereby violating Shabbat through *melacha d'oraita*. In this case, the act of *mechira* (sale) — specifically the kinyan that completes the transaction — takes place at the same moment as the Shabbat violation. The question of what constitutes the *kinyan* here (whether it is *chalipin*, a barter-exchange, or a condition on a pre-existing arrangement) is discussed by Tosafot, though the Gemara does not resolve it explicitly.

### Validity Objection

The Gemara raises a powerful objection. If the transaction occurred through an act of *chillul Shabbat* that makes the actor *chayav misa*, should the sale even be legally valid? *Vekeivan deki tava lei kaman bedina, lo amrinan lei zil shlim* — if one of the parties were to bring this transaction to court, we would not enforce it, because *demichayav benafshei hu* — the actor is liable with his life for the act through which the transaction was completed. This seems to imply that *qam leih bederabbah mineih* — the principle that one who incurs the death penalty is exempt from monetary consequences — voids the transaction entirely, not merely the monetary liability.

## Qam Leih Analysis

### Rav Pappa Answer

Rav Pappa offers a different scenario in which the *baraita*'s exemption can apply. The buyer says to the thief: *zrok gneivotayikh l'chatzeiri v'tikni li gneivotayikh* — throw the stolen animal into my yard, and once it enters, I acquire it. The thief throws the animal from *reshut harabbim* into the buyer's *reshut hayachid* — a private domain — thereby performing *hotza'ah* (transferring between domains on Shabbat), a *melakhah d'oraita*.

The Gemara asks: according to which authority does this work? *K'Rabbi Akiva, d'amar k'luta k'mi shehunchah damya* — it works according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds that once an object is contained within the boundaries of a private domain (*keluta*), it is as if it has come to rest, and the Shabbat violation is complete at the moment of entry — the same moment as the *kinyan*. But if we follow the Rabbis, who hold that the *chillul Shabbat* is not complete until the object actually comes to rest on the ground (*ad d'mati l'ara*), the *kinyan* (entry into the yard) precedes the *chillul Shabbat* (landing on the ground), and the two do not coincide.

To make it work according to the Rabbis, the Gemara suggests: *be'omer lo tikni li gneivotayikh ad shetanuach* — the buyer stipulates that he will not acquire the animal until it lands on the ground. This way, the *kinyan* and the *chillul Shabbat* occur simultaneously, *qam leih* applies, and the sale is — at least superficially — tainted.

Yet the problem persists: if *qam leih* voids the sale, how is there a sale at all? If there is no sale, why is the thief exempt from fourfold or fivefold? The exemption in the *baraita* presupposes a valid sale.

### Rava Etnan

Rava proposes a far more elegant and conceptually important resolution, arguing from the principle of *le'olam kerami bar Chama* — the fig scenario is entirely valid as the case. Rava invokes the law of *etnan* (a fee paid to a prostitute): *esnan asra Torah va'afilu ba al imo* — the Torah prohibited an *etnan* even if the man had relations with his own mother. The case is deliberately extreme: even where the act was *chayav misa* — incest — the fee paid to the prostitute retains its legal status as a fee (*etnan*), not a gift, and cannot be brought as a sacrifice on the *mizbe'ach*.

The implication is precise: although a court cannot compel the man to pay the agreed-upon fee — since the obligation arose simultaneously with an act that is *chayav misa*, and *qam leih* prevents enforcement — if the man nevertheless voluntarily pays the fee, the payment is classified as *etnan*, not as a voluntary gift. This proves that the underlying obligation exists even though the court cannot enforce it. The obligation is *bidei shamayim* — before Heaven — even if it is not judicially actionable.

### Qam Leih Nuance

Rava's argument articulates a crucial distinction that runs through this entire discussion: *qam leih bederabbah mineih* does not extinguish an obligation — it prevents the court from enforcing it. The two are not the same. This is directly parallel to the principle of *bidei shamayim* discussed in the Gemara of *Hakones*: certain liabilities exist and carry genuine moral weight before Heaven even when they are not enforceable in a human court.

Applied to the *baraita*: the thief's sale of the stolen animal on Shabbat generated a genuine obligation — a real *mechira*. *Qam leih* means that if the buyer tried to sue in court to recover his payment or the animal, we would not enforce the transaction. *Hacha nami af al gav d'le'inyan tashlumim, i tav'ah bedina kaman lo amrinan lei zil shalim* — even so, since at the end of the day the thief delivers the animal to the buyer based on that transaction, *haveh mechira* — it is a *mechira*, because the underlying obligation is real. The sale is not voided by *qam leih*; it is merely rendered unenforceable. And since it is a genuine sale, the thief is exempt from the fourfold or fivefold penalty — which requires a sale — even though the court would not adjudicate the transaction itself.

### iPhone Application

To apply this concretely: imagine the thief stole an iPhone and conducted the sale through an act of *chillul Shabbat* as described. The buyer can come to court and try to enforce the transaction — the court will decline, because the sale came through a *chiyuv misa*. But if the thief ultimately hands over the iPhone based on that understanding, the handover is a *mechira*, because the obligation underlying the sale never disappeared; it was only unenforceable. The *Meiri* has an extended and interesting discussion about whether contractual obligations — as opposed to sale obligations — might be even more robustly enforceable in this setting, noting that the mechanism of *qam leih* was designed for punitive sanctions and may operate differently with respect to interpersonal agreements.
