# Gittin 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmitat Kesafim

### Creditor's Obligation

The Gemara opens by citing a *mishnah* from *Shevi'it*: *hamachzir chov lechaveiro bishmita tzarich she'yomar lo meshameit ani* — one who returns a debt to his fellow during *shmita* must be told by the creditor, "I release you." If the debtor then says *af al pi chen* — "nevertheless, I still wish to repay" — the creditor is permitted to accept the money, as derived from the verse *v'zeh dvar hashmita*: this is the "matter" of *shmita*, referring to speech — the creditor must verbally articulate the release.

This raises an immediate question about the mechanics of *shmitat kesafim*. We have generally assumed that *shmita* automatically annuls debts — *shamot kol ba'al masheh yado*. So what precisely is the relationship between the creditor's verbal declaration of *meshameit ani* and the automatic annulment? Is the debt erased by force of law, with the declaration serving merely as a reinforcement? Or is there some other structure at work?

### Rosh's Two-Stage View

The *Rosh* offers what is perhaps the most compelling resolution. He reads the term *bishmita* — during the *shmitta* year — with precision. According to the *Rosh*, during the year of *shmita* itself, the underlying debt still legally exists; what the Torah prohibits is the active demanding or exacting of that debt. The obligation of *meshameit ani* is thus the creditor's active participation in that prohibition: you declare "I release you," and your verbal release reflects the commandment not to press for payment. Only at the conclusion of *shmita* — at the onset of the eighth year — is the debt automatically and permanently annulled.

This reading elegantly explains the *mishnah*'s scenario. During *shmita*, the debt is real but uncollectable. If the debtor voluntarily appears and wishes to pay, the debt still technically exists, which is precisely why the creditor may accept the payment once the debtor says *af al pi chen*. An alternative reading — that the debt has already been erased and repayment is simply a moral gesture, like reciprocating a Shabbos invitation — is possible but less legally satisfying. The *Rosh*'s framework gives the entire structure a cleaner internal logic.

### Pressuring for Repayment

The *Gemara* pushes further on the concept of *af al pi chen* as a vehicle for recovering the debt. Rabba states: *talvei lei ad d'amar hachi* — one may "string him up" until he says *af al pi chen*, meaning one may apply pressure to get the debtor to voluntarily declare his intention to repay. The creditor is not demanding payment as a debt; he is urging the debtor to understand his moral responsibility.

### Abaye's Objection

Abaye challenges this directly. The *mishnah* elsewhere specifies that when the debtor does repay after the creditor has said *meshameit ani*, he should not say "I am giving this to you on account of my debt" — *be-chovi ani noten lach* — but rather "this is mine, and I am giving it to you as a gift" — *sheli hen u-v'matana ani noten lach*. If the very framing of the repayment must avoid any acknowledgment of debt, how can one pressure the debtor to pay at all? That pressure itself seems to acknowledge that a real debt is being pursued.

Rava responds: *talyuhu v'yahiv* — you pressure him until he gives it as a gift. The pressure is not framed as debt collection; it is an appeal to moral conscience. You are not saying "pay me what you owe me" but rather nudging him to understand that it is the right thing to do, and then he gives — voluntarily, framed as a gift. This is admittedly a fine line, and even a somewhat counterintuitive one. As the lecturer notes, it sits in obvious tension with the spirit of *shmitat kesafim*, much as the *pruzbul* does — it is the *Gemara* trying to balance the ideal of debt release with the practical reality that creditors have legitimate interests and debtors have genuine moral obligations. According to the *Rosh*'s reading, however, this is somewhat less strained: if the debt truly still exists during *shmita*, then the debtor is being encouraged to honor an obligation that is real, even if uncollectable.

### Abba bar Marta Story

The *Gemara* illustrates this tension through a vivid narrative. Abba bar Marta — also known as Abba bar Minyumi — owed Rabba money. During *shmita*, he came to repay the loan. Rabba did the right thing and said *meshameit ani* — "I release you." The debtor took Rabba at his word, pocketed the release, and left with his money. Abaye later found Rabba in a state of anguish and asked what was wrong. Rabba explained what had happened: he had done exactly what he was supposed to do, and the debtor had simply left without understanding the script he was meant to follow.

Abaye — the same Abaye who had challenged the legitimacy of applying pressure — went to the debtor and, with notable subtlety, walked him through the exchange. He asked: "Did you bring your money to the Master?" Yes. "What did he say?" *Meshameit ani*. "And did you say to him *af al pi chen*?" No. "Had you said *af al pi chen*, he would have happily accepted it." Abaye then instructed him: go back now, bring the money, and say *af al pi chen*. The debtor did so, and Rabba accepted it.

This story is striking on multiple levels. Rabba himself, after the fact, said that the debtor — a Torah scholar — should have known better, since the mechanism of *af al pi chen* is explicitly stated in a *mishnah* in *Shevi'it*. But more striking is Abaye's conduct: having challenged the very principle of pressuring debtors, he himself applied exactly such pressure — albeit gentle and indirect — in service of his own *rebbe*'s financial interests. The *Gemara* does not comment on this irony, but it is unmistakable.

## Pruzbul

### Rav Nachman's Leniency

The *Gemara* now pivots to the *pruzbul* — the mechanism instituted by Hillel to circumvent *shmitat kesafim* by transferring debts to a court, which is not subject to the *shmitta* release. Rav Nachman rules: *ne'eman adam lomar pruzbul hayah be-yadi ve-avad* — a person is believed to claim that he had a *pruzbul* and lost it, without having to produce the document. This is a significant leniency: previously we had seen opinions suggesting that certain *talmidei chakhamim* might not even need a formal written *pruzbul* or could rely on a verbal declaration; Rav Nachman does not go quite that far, but he does rule that production of the document in court is not required.

### Rationale: Presumed Dili…

The reasoning is straightforward: *keivan de-tikinu rabanan pruzbul* — since the Rabbis instituted the *pruzbul* as the legitimate and permitted path for collecting debts after *shmita*, *lo shevik heteира ve-achil issurah* — we presume that people would not abandon the permissible path and proceed in a transgressive manner. In other words, anyone who is claiming to collect a debt almost certainly wrote a *pruzbul*; we simply presume they did. This is a generalized principle used elsewhere in halakhic reasoning: where a straightforward permissible path exists, we assume people availed themselves of it.

### Rava's Suggestion

Rava applied this principle in practice. When a case came before him and the creditor had not produced a *pruzbul*, Rava asked him directly: *midi pruzbul havah lach ve-avad?* — "Is it possible that you had a *pruzbul* and you lost it?" This is the principle of *ptach picha le-ilem* — "open up your mouth to the mute" — the idea that someone who had indeed written a *pruzbul* and lost it might not even think to assert that fact. Rava's prompt invited the creditor to recall and state what was presumably the truth. There is, the lecturer acknowledges, something of a wink and nod in this exchange — Rava is actively facilitating the creditor's claim — but the underlying principle is sound: we presume people acted in the permitted manner.

### Mishnah Contradiction

A difficulty arises from a *mishnah* that seems to state the opposite: *ha-motzi shtaro ve-ein imo pruzbul harei elu lo yifare'u* — a creditor who produces his loan document without an accompanying *pruzbul* may not collect. This appears to require production of the *pruzbul*.

### Evolving Standards

The *Gemara* resolves this as a *tannaitic* dispute: a *baraita* cites the Sages as saying that the *pruzbul* need not be produced alongside the loan document. Beyond this formal resolution, there is a broader historical explanation available: the *pruzbul* at the time of the *mishnah* was a weightier and more formal institution, and its requirements were accordingly more stringent. Over time, as the institution developed and became more routine, the standards relaxed — consistent with the general pattern of the *pruzbul* becoming progressively easier to utilize, including the loosening of requirements around land ownership and other formal conditions. The principle of *lo shevik heteира ve-achil issurah* thus becomes ever more operative as the *pruzbul* becomes ever more accessible.

## Enslaved Captive

### Mishnah Case

The *Gemara* transitions to a *mishnah* in an entirely different domain, though it remains within the broader framework of *tikkun olam* — the organizing theme of this chapter. Interestingly, the *mishnah* itself does not mention *tikkun olam*, and it is not immediately clear from the *mishnah's* language which ruling constitutes the *takkanah* and what its social purpose is. That unpacking is left entirely to the *Gemara*.

The *mishnah* states: *eved she-nishba u-fadahu* — a slave who was taken captive and redeemed. *Im le-shum eved* — if he was redeemed for the purpose of returning to servitude — *yishtabed*, he goes back into servitude. *Im le-shum ben chorin* — if he was redeemed for the purpose of freedom — *lo yishtabed*, he does not return to slavery and goes free.

This is intuitively sensible. The slave is someone's property. Non-Jewish forces have captured him. If his own master or someone acting on the master's behalf ransoms him back, he returns to his master. But if third parties — people unconnected to the master — encounter this captive slave, take pity on him, and redeem him with the intention of setting him free, he goes free.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel dissents: *bein kach u-vein kach yishtabed* — either way, he remains a slave. The rationale behind his position, and the question of which view constitutes the *tikkun olam*, will be developed by the *Gemara*.

### Ye'ush Question

The *Gemara* asks: *bemai askinan?* — what precisely is the case? The analysis turns on the concept of *ye'ush* — the master's renunciation of hope of recovering his slave. Since the slave is legally property, the same principles that govern lost or stolen objects apply. Before *ye'ush*, the master has not relinquished his claim; after *ye'ush*, he has, and the object can pass into the possession of a third party. The question is: in which of these two states does the *mishnah*'s case take place?

If it is before *ye'ush*, then when the slave is redeemed *le-shum ben chorin* — for freedom — why does he go free? He is still fully the master's property. A third party who purchases stolen property from a thief before the owner's *ye'ush* cannot claim ownership; similarly, why should these redeemers be able to grant the slave his freedom? The slave is still mine, regardless of what you intended when you paid the captors.

Conversely, if it is after *ye'ush*, then when the slave is redeemed *le-shum eved* — for servitude — why is he still enslaved? After *ye'ush*, he arguably should already be a free person, since no one owns him. These two questions create the central dilemma that Abaye and Rava each resolve in their own ways.

### Abaye: Before Ye'ush

Abaye says: *le-olam lifnei ye'ush* — the case is before *ye'ush*. Before *ye'ush*, *le-shum eved ishtabed le-rabo rishon* — obviously, if redeemed for servitude, he returns to the first master. He is still the master's property and the redemption simply restores him.

The harder question is *le-shum ben chorin*: if he is redeemed for freedom before *ye'ush*, why does he go free? *Lo ishtabed lo le-rabo rishon ve-lo le-rabo sheni* — he is enslaved neither to the first master nor to the second. That the redeemers cannot enslave him is straightforward — *de-ha le-shum ben chorin parkei* — they redeemed him to be free. But why does he not return to the first master? He was never subject to *ye'ush*; he remains the master's property. The answer lies in *tikkun olam*: *dilma mimnei ve-lo parkei* — we are concerned that if people know that a slave redeemed for freedom will simply be returned to his master, they will hold back and not redeem him at all. We wish to incentivize redemption. People may feel the pull of *pidyon shvuyim* — the obligation to redeem captives — but only if they perceive the captive as a potential free person do they feel sufficiently motivated to act. Rashi captures this: once a person knows that his redemption will result in the slave's freedom, he begins to see the slave as a would-be free man and feels the weight of *pidyon shvuyim* more acutely.

Therefore, as a *tikkun olam*, we rule that if someone redeems the slave *le-shum ben chorin* before *ye'ush*, the slave goes free — even though by strict law he should be returned to the first master.

### Rava: After Ye'ush

Rava takes the opposite position: *le-olam le-achar ye'ush* — the case is after *ye'ush*. After *ye'ush*, the first master has relinquished his claim. The slave does not automatically become free merely because of *ye'ush*, however; a third party who takes possession of him — *shinui reshut* combined with *ye'ush* — can acquire rights to him.

Thus, *le-shum eved ishtabed*: if redeemed for servitude after *ye'ush*, the redeemer acquires him through the mechanism of *ye'ush ve-shinui reshut*. *Le-shum ben chorin, lo yishtabed*: if redeemed for freedom, he goes free — the redeemer did not intend to acquire him, and the first master has been *meya'esh*. *Le-rabo sheni lo* — the redeemer cannot enslave him because that was not his intent. *Le-rabo rishon lama lo — de-ha le-achar ye'ush hu* — and the first master cannot reclaim him because *ye'ush* has already transpired.

On Rava's reading, the Sages (*chakhamim*) are simply stating the straightforward *din*: after *ye'ush*, the outcome depends on the redeemer's intent. There is no *tikkun olam* in the Sages' position; it is the plain law of *ye'ush ve-shinui reshut*. Rabban Gamliel, by contrast, is the one introducing a *tikkun olam*: *bein kach u-vein kach yishtabed* — either way, the slave goes back to the first master. This is not because of any legal logic, but because of the principle articulated by Chizkiyah.

As Chizkiyah explains: *shelo yehei kol echad ve-echad holech u-mapil atzmo le-gayyasot u-mafki'ah atzmo mi-yad rabo* — lest every slave deliberately allow himself to be captured by marauding troops as a strategy for escaping his master. If slaves know that capture leads to *ye'ush* and *ye'ush* leads to freedom, they have an incentive to manufacture the situation. The *tikkun olam* of Rabban Gamliel is therefore to ensure that whatever happens, the slave returns to his first master — according to Rashi, *le-rabo rishon* specifically, even if the second party intended to enslave him to himself.

This is a striking *Gemara* precisely because *tikkun olam* here is not the expansive, progressive concept it is sometimes romanticized to be. One version of *tikkun olam* incentivizes redemption by granting freedom. The other version of *tikkun olam* ensures runaway slaves are always returned to their masters, because social order depends on the stability of that institution. Both are *tikkun olam* in the technical sense: rabbinic enactments for the sake of societal welfare. They point in opposite directions.

### Baraita Challenge

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Rabban Gamliel himself says to the Sages: *keshem she-mitzvah lifdot bnei chorin, kach mitzvah lifdot ha-avadim* — just as there is a *mitzvah* to redeem free persons, so too there is a *mitzvah* to redeem slaves. This statement fits naturally with Abaye's reading: Abaye says the Sages' position is a *tikkun olam* to incentivize redemption, and Rabban Gamliel challenges it by saying people need no special incentive — there is already an obligation to redeem slaves, just as there is to redeem free persons. On Rava's reading, however, this statement seems out of place. According to Rava, the Sages are simply stating the *din* of *ye'ush*, and Rabban Gamliel is introducing a *takanah* to prevent runaways; in that context, his invocation of the *mitzvah* to redeem slaves seems entirely beside the point.

### Rava's Response

Rava resolves this by saying: *Rabban Gamliel lo hava yada mai kamri rabbanan* — Rabban Gamliel did not know what exactly the Sages were arguing. He was not responding to a specific position but rather laying out a comprehensive rebuttal to any possible rationale: if you are arguing that the slave goes free before *ye'ush* in order to incentivize redemption, you are wrong — there is already a *mitzvah* to redeem slaves, so no extra incentive is needed. And if you are arguing that after *ye'ush* the slave goes free because that is the plain *din*, I also disagree — we should override that *din* because of Chizkiyah's concern about slaves engineering their own capture. Either way, *bein kach u-vein kach yishtabed*.

## Non-Jewish Slave Ownersh…

### Captors' Rights

With the basic framework of the *mishnah* established, the *Gemara* turns to a foundational question: how do the non-Jewish captors acquire any ownership rights over the slave at all? This matters because, according to Rava, it is only after *ye'ush* that a third party can redeem the slave and enslave him to themselves through *ye'ush ve-shinui reshut*. For this to work, the captors must have some degree of recognized ownership over the slave. If the captors have no rights whatsoever, then the master's *ye'ush* would leave the slave simply ownerless — and one might argue he would go free at that very moment, with no mechanism for the second party to acquire him.

The *Gemara* answers: *in, kanei lei le-ma'aseh yadav* — yes, the captors do acquire him, but only for his labor — *ma'aseh yadav* — not as full property. This partial ownership is precisely the concept of *kinyan geneivah* or *kinyan gezeila*: when a thief or violent aggressor takes possession of someone's property, they acquire a de facto partial ownership. A well-known consequence of this principle is that a thief who steals an object and is later caught does not owe the owner rental fees for the period of use, because that de facto possession granted him some right of use, however illegitimate his acquisition. The same structure applies here: the captors, by seizing the slave, acquire rights to his labor, even without purchasing him and even without the master's consent. This partial ownership prevents the slave from being automatically freed by *ye'ush* and enables the subsequent transfer of possession to a Jewish buyer who wishes to enslave him.

### Reish Lakish

This raises a broader question about the structure of slave ownership in halakhic thought. Reish Lakish articulates a general principle: *minayin le-oved kochavim she-kanah oved kochavim le-ma'aseh yadav* — how do we know that a non-Jew who acquires a non-Jew does so only for his labor (*ma'aseh yadav*) and not as full bodily property (*kinyan guf*)? This is a striking halakhic distinction. Jews can own non-Jewish slaves as full property — *kinyan guf*. Non-Jews can own other non-Jews, or own Jews, only for their labor — *ma'aseh yadav*. Only a Jewish master holds full property rights over a non-Jewish slave.

This is deeply unsettling on its face. But one suggested rationale, offered in the course of discussion, is that Jewish law does not trust pagan society to exercise the kind of restrained mastery over another human being that full property ownership would entail. The experience of *shibud mitzrayim* — Egyptian bondage — and the ethical framework that follows from it means that the only context in which full property ownership of a person can be sanctioned is one governed by Torah law. It remains a troubling framework by any measure, but it is the halakhic structure the *Gemara* is operating within.

### Verse Derivation

The derivation for this distinction comes from the verse in *Vayikra*: *v'gam mi-bnei ha-toshavim ha-garim imachem, me-hem tiknu eved va-amah* — and also from the children of the resident aliens who dwell among you, from them you may purchase slaves. The *Gemara* reads this as a limiting verse: *atem konim me-hem* — you may purchase them as full property, *ve-lo hem konim mi-kem* — but they may not purchase you as full property. And *ve-lo hem konim zeh mi-zeh l'gufo* — nor may they purchase one another as full property. Non-Jews may own non-Jews and may own Jews, but only for *ma'aseh yadav*, not *kinyan guf*.

The implications of *kinyan guf* — full bodily ownership — go beyond mere economic rights. As Rashi emphasizes, *kinyan guf* is a matter of personal status. If a slave is owned *le-gufo*, then to change that status, a formal document of manumission — a *get shichrur* — is required. Merely renouncing one's economic claim is insufficient to alter the slave's halakhic identity. Furthermore, a slave owned *le-gufo* cannot simply choose to undergo conversion and thereby redefine his status; his identity is determined by the ownership relationship, and only the master can change it through formal manumission. A slave owned only *le-ma'aseh yadav*, by contrast, has a more fluid status: his labor belongs to another, but his person does not, and if that labor-obligation ends, his status adjusts accordingly.

### Kal va-Chomer

A *kal va-chomer* is then constructed. If a non-Jew can own a Jew for *ma'aseh yadav* — as we know from the *pesukim* in the Torah describing a Jew who sells himself into servitude to a resident alien — then certainly a non-Jew can own another non-Jew for *ma'aseh yadav*. The argument is: the *chumra* case (a Jew owned by a non-Jew for his labor) establishes the category; the *kal* case (a non-Jew owned by a non-Jew for his labor) follows *a fortiori*. 

A complication arises: we know a non-Jew can own a Jew for *ma'aseh yadav* through the mechanism of purchase (*be-kaspa*) — the Jew sells himself. But in our case, the captors did not purchase the slave; they seized him by force. What is the source for acquisition through forceful seizure — *chazakah* — rather than purchase?

### Kibbush Milchamah

The *Gemara* introduces the concept of *kibbush milchamah* — forceful conquest as a mode of acquisition. Rav Papa demonstrates this from the episode of Sichon: Sichon captured the lands of Ammon and Moab through military conquest. When the Jewish people subsequently conquered Sichon's territories, they were permitted to take those lands even though the Torah had commanded *al tetzer et Moav* — do not distress Moab. The resolution was that those territories were no longer Moab's; Sichon had acquired them through *kibbush milchamah*, and they had become Sichon's lands. When Israel conquered Sichon, they inherited lands that were legitimately Sichon's. This demonstrates that forceful seizure in war constitutes a recognized form of acquisition. Tosafot identifies this principle explicitly as *kibbush milchamah*.

If this is true of land, the same principle extends to people: a non-Jewish force that captures a slave by force acquires rights to him through *kibbush milchamah*, even without any purchase transaction.

### Captives Verse

The final step extends this principle from non-Jews seizing non-Jewish property to non-Jews seizing property belonging to Jews. For the former, the case of *Amon u-Moav* and Sichon is sufficient. For the latter — *akum mi-Yisrael minayin?* — the *Gemara* derives it from the verse *va-yishbi mimenu shevi* — and he took captives from them. The fact that the verse uses the noun *shevi* (captive) rather than merely saying that a person was taken implies that the captured person acquired the status of *shevi* — captive — as a result of being seized. The seizure itself changes the person's status, giving the captors recognized rights to him. This establishes that non-Jewish captors acquire rights — *ma'aseh yadav* — over Jewish-owned slaves whom they capture by force, and that those rights persist even after the master's *ye'ush*, enabling the subsequent transfer of possession.

## Escape & Manumission

### Escape Goes Free

Rabbi Yochanan, cited by Rabbi Shimon bar Abba, teaches: *eved she-barach mi-beit ha-asurin yatza le-cheirut* — a slave who escapes from captivity goes free. Moreover, *koфin rabo ve-kotev lo get shichrur* — we compel his former master to write him a writ of manumission, to ensure his freedom is formally and publicly established.

### Apparent Contradiction

This creates an apparent contradiction with Rabbi Yochanan's own stated position — recorded in the name of Rabbah bar bar Chana — that wherever Rabban Gamliel appears in the *mishnah*, the *halacha* follows him (with three specified exceptions, none of which is our case). Since our *mishnah* features Rabban Gamliel ruling *bein kach u-vein kach yishtabed* — he is always returned to slavery — and we rule like Rabban Gamliel, how can Rabbi Yochanan simultaneously hold that a slave who escapes goes free?

### Abaye Resolves

According to Abaye, the resolution is straightforward. Abaye's reading of the *mishnah* is *lifnei ye'ush*: before *ye'ush*, the slave is fully the master's property and Rabban Gamliel's ruling — that he always remains enslaved — is correct and we rule accordingly. But the case of Rabbi Yochanan — where the slave escapes his captors — is *le-achar ye'ush*. After *ye'ush*, the master has no claim, the captors own him only for *ma'aseh yadav*, and when he escapes, no one retains ownership over him. He goes free.

The need for a *get shichrur* even after *ye'ush* is not self-evident, since the master's monetary claim has lapsed. But this is precisely where the distinction between monetary ownership and personal status matters. Even if the master no longer owns the slave in any economic sense — cannot demand his labor, has no property rights — the slave's *halakhic status* may still be that of a slave. Personal status is not automatically altered by the lapse of property rights; it may require a formal document — the *shtar shichrur* — to effect the change.

### Rava: Escape Different

For Rava, the resolution is more complex, since on his reading, even *le-achar ye'ush* we apply the *takanah* of Chizkiyah — slaves are always returned to their masters, lest they engineer their own capture. If we rule like Rabban Gamliel based on Chizkiyah's concern, and Rabbi Yochanan says we always rule like Rabban Gamliel, how can Rabbi Yochanan simultaneously teach that an escaped slave goes free?

Rava answers: *barach shani* — escape is different. The reason we send slaves back to their masters after *ye'ush* is to disincentivize deliberate self-captivity. But a slave who escapes his captors has demonstrated the opposite: he risked his life — *le-katla masar nafsheih* — to get out of captivity. Presumably the penalty for escape was death. A person willing to accept mortal risk to flee captivity clearly did not seek out captivity as a means of liberation. The entire concern underlying Chizkiyah's *takanah* simply does not apply to this person. Therefore, even according to Rava, when a slave escapes rather than being redeemed, he goes free — and Chizkiyah's *takanah* does not operate.

## Ownership vs. Status

### Shmuel's Case

The *Gemara* now introduces a case involving the maidservant of Shmuel. She was taken captive — *ishtava'i* — and a group of people redeemed her *le-shum amahuta*, intending to restore her to Shmuel's service as a gift to him. They sent Shmuel a message: we follow the ruling of Rabban Gamliel (*ke-Rabban Gamliel sevira lan*), and even if you hold like the Sages, we redeemed her specifically for servitude — so please accept her back.

The difficulty is that they had assumed the case was *lifnei ye'ush* — before Shmuel had given up hope. But in fact, *le-achar ye'ush hava* — Shmuel had already been *meya'esh* from her. Once this is established, Shmuel had no interest in retaining her in any case, and no one wished to press a claim to her.

### Shmuel's Ruling

The *Gemara* records that Shmuel went further: not only would he not retain her in servitude, but she did not even require a *get shichrur* — a writ of manumission. This reflects Shmuel's consistent position: *ha-mafkir avdo yatza le-cheirut ve-eino tzarich get shichrur* — one who renounces ownership over his slave, the slave goes free without needing a formal writ. The renunciation of monetary ownership is sufficient to change the slave's halakhic status entirely.

### Verse Interpretation

Shmuel derives this from the verse *ve-chol eved ish miknat kasef* — the slave of a man. The phrase *eved ish* — slave of a man — implies: *eved she-yesh lo reshut rabo alav karui eved* — a slave is only called a slave when the master retains authority over him. *She-ein lo reshut rabo alav, ein karui eved* — when no such authority exists, he is no longer designated a slave, and his personal status changes accordingly without need for any further document. Monetary status and personal status are thus intertwined; one automatically implies the other.

### Rabbi Abba Case

The *Gemara* then brings the parallel case of the maidservant of Rabbi Abba bar Zutra. She was taken captive and redeemed by a non-Jew — *tarmuda'ah* — who intended to take her as a wife. Jewish community members sent word to Rabbi Abba: if you have been *meya'esh* from her, send her a *get shichrur*. But the *Gemara* asks: what would the *get shichrur* accomplish? If these community members have the financial means to redeem her, they can simply do so and return her to Rabbi Abba; there is no need for a writ. And if they cannot afford to redeem her, how does sending a writ of manumission help?

### Writ Purposes

The *Gemara* resolves this with two alternative explanations. The first: they do have the means to redeem her, but the point of the *get shichrur* is to incentivize broader communal participation. *Keivan de-mashdar lah gita de-cheiruta* — once a writ of manumission is sent and it becomes known that she is a free woman, people will feel a stronger obligation of *pidyon shvuyim* — redeeming a free person is more compelling than redeeming a slave — and they will pool their resources to buy her back. This parallels Abaye's *tikkun olam* reasoning from the *mishnah*: the prospect of freedom is what mobilizes people.

The second approach: they cannot afford to redeem her. But if Rabbi Abba sends a *get shichrur*, it becomes publicly known that she had been a slave. This embarrasses the non-Jew who wishes to take her as a wife — publicly known as the wife of a former slave is not a dignity he wishes — and he will therefore lower his asking price to a level they can afford. The *get shichrur* here is not about changing her status for its own sake, but about creating public information that changes the non-Jew's leverage. The *Gemara* qualifies: non-Jews may be privately happy to take advantage of opportunities involving Jewish-owned property, but *be-farhesia* — publicly — *zila behu milta* — such things are embarrassing to them. Public exposure thus becomes a tool for reducing the ransom price.

## Branded Woman Story

### Wedding Discovery

The *Gemara* concludes with a remarkable narrative drawn from *Bava Kamma* (daf 114). A man in Lod married off his son. Guests came to the celebration, and as the host's wife poured wine for the assembled guests, her sleeves shifted and her arms were exposed. The guests saw that she had a *kivuyah* — a brand — on her arm, the kind of mark used to identify slaves as property of their owner. The implication was immediate and devastating: if she was a branded slave who had never been formally freed, she could not legally marry a Jew. Her husband's marriage was no marriage. Their son, whose wedding was at that very moment being celebrated, was himself a slave. The entire gathering fell under a cloud.

### Martyrdom Explanation

The host addressed the whispers directly. He acknowledged the brand but offered an explanation: his wife had been taken captive by non-Jews who sought to compel her to serve their idols. She refused — categorically, repeatedly. In response, they tortured her; they took a red-hot iron and burned her arms to break her will. She endured the torture and still did not submit to *avodah zarah*. The brand on her arms was not a slave's mark; it was the mark of a *tzadeket*, a holy woman who had accepted physical torment rather than violate her faith.

### Acceptance

The guests believed him entirely. And the *Gemara* records that they not only accepted the explanation but honored him more after hearing it — contributing additional money and expressing greater esteem for him than they had before.

### Gemara's Lesson

The *Gemara* draws a careful lesson. Ordinarily, a woman who claims she was captured and mistreated is not simply believed to explain away evidence of slave status. The *mishnah* elsewhere makes clear that such claims require substantiation. Why was she believed here? Because there was tangible physical evidence that required explanation. The brand *existed*; it was visible to everyone. Given that evidence, the question was not whether to believe a claim out of nowhere, but how to interpret an observable fact. The choice was between two explanations — slave branding or torture for refusing idolatry — and given that she was a Jewish woman of apparent standing and reputation, the more favorable explanation was accepted. The underlying principle is that credible, corroborating physical evidence creates a context in which alternative explanations can be credited. Without such evidence — if a woman simply makes an unsubstantiated claim — she is not believed. The physical reality of the brand is what opens the door to the charitable reading. Without it, the door remains closed.
