# Yevamot 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yibum Rights & Exemption

### Mishnah Clarification

The daf opens with a corrective gloss on the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* speaks of four brothers, and the *gemara* immediately notes that the formulation "four brothers" raises an obvious difficulty: if they all died, who remains to perform *yibum*? The *gemara* therefore emends the reading — *ela eima arba me-achim*, say rather that four of the brothers died, leaving four widows, while other brothers survive. This is, as the lecturer observes, one of those *mishnayot* in this *masechet* that would have made an excellent opening for the tractate had it appeared earlier.

### Oldest Brother Priority

With the corrected reading in place, the *mishnah* establishes that the oldest surviving brother holds the right — *ha-reshut beyado* — to perform *yibum* with all four widows before any other brother may act. He is not limited to one; if he wishes, he may marry all of them before the younger brothers have any opportunity to intervene.

### One Exempts All

The *mishnah* then addresses the exemption principle that has underlain the entire tractate from its opening: *chalitzah* or *yibum* performed with any one wife exempts all the others from further obligation. Though this has been assumed throughout, the *gemara* now derives it explicitly from scriptural sources, which are examined in detail in the following section.

---

## Four Wives Limit

### Elders' Advice

The *gemara* raises a challenge: would we simply allow this brother to marry four women simultaneously? Surely that is an extraordinary domestic situation. The *gemara* invokes a *baraita* on the verse *ve-karu lo ziknei iro* — "and the elders of his city shall call to him." The *gemara* understands *ve-dibru eilav*, "and they shall speak to him," as mandating substantive guidance, not mere ritual formality. *Melamed she-masiin lo eitzah hogenes lo* — they give him advice that is appropriate and fitting for him specifically. Crucially, the elders must address him directly, *hen ve-lo sheluchan*, not through an intermediary.

### Age Matching

The content of that guidance is elaborated: if he is young and the *yevamah* is elderly, or he is elderly and she is young, the elders say to him — *mah lecha etzel yaldah*, what do you want with such a young woman, or *mah lecha etzel zekeinah*, what do you want with such an old woman? *Kelech etzel she-kamotecha*, go and find someone of your own age, *ve-al tasim ketatah be-veitecha* — do not bring strife into your house. The advice is non-binding; he is free to reject it. And notably, the woman's own voice is not heard in this passage, a theme that recurs throughout the tractate.

### Math of Conjugal Rights

The immediate question is why the *mishnah* limits the brother to four wives. If he is a man of means who can support multiple wives — the *gemara* answers *lo tzericha de-efshar lei*, this is indeed a case where he has the resources — why not permit ten or twenty? The *gemara* answers: *eitzah tovah ka mashma lan*, this is good advice even for a wealthy man. *Arba in, tfeih lo* — four wives yes, more no. The reasoning is mathematical: *ki heichi de-timtei onah be-chodesh*, the *mitzvah* of *onah* for a *talmid chacham* is once per week. With four wives rotating, each wife receives conjugal attention once a month. Any more than four wives and a woman would receive her *onah* less than once a month, which is an unacceptable diminishment of her rights. Four, therefore, is both the maximum permitted and the outer limit of practical wisdom.

### Senegal Digression

It is an interesting coincidence that Islamic law similarly limits a man to four wives, a number said to reflect the personal practice of Muhammad. The parallel with the Talmud's reasoning is worth noting, though the justifications differ. By way of illustration: on a rabbinic mission to Senegal — a society in which the overwhelming majority of the population practices polygamy, with only a small Christian minority living monogamously — the lecturer had occasion to observe a kind of living parallel to the world of *Avraham, Yitzchak, ve-Yaakov*: village compounds in which co-wives occupied separate quarters, maintained their own domestic rotations, and shared child-rearing responsibilities. A question from one of the visiting rabbinical students — "what is it like living in a polygamous society?" — met with understandable puzzlement from the Senegalese women, who might equally have asked what it is like to be monogamous. The tension was dissolved with a joke: asked what the penalty for polygamy is, the answer was — two mothers-in-law. The laughter that followed confirmed that mother-in-law humor requires no translation across cultures.

## Yibum vs. Chalitzah

### Bayis Echad Proofs

Having addressed the practical question of how many wives a *yavam* may take in *yibum*, the *gemara* now establishes the scriptural basis for the principle that *yibum* or *chalitzah* with one wife exempts all the others — and that it must be one or the other, not both. *Amar Rabbi Chama berabi Chanina amar Rabbi Yochanan amar kra*: the verse reads *asher lo yivneh et beit achiv*, describing the man who will not build his brother's house. The *gemara* derives: *bayis echad hu boneh, v'ein boneh shenei batim* — he builds one house, not two. And since *beito zu ishto* — "his house" is his wife — this limits *yibum* to a single wife. The equation of *bayis* and *ishah* is a well-established trope in the *gemara*.

### Chalitzah Exclusivity

The same principle is derived for *chalitzah* from a different verse. *Amar Mar Zutra bar Tuvia amar kra*: the verse *beit chalutz ha-na'al*, "the house of the withdrawn shoe," similarly uses the singular — *bayis echad hu choletz, v'ein choletz shenei batim* — he performs *chalitzah* with one house, not two.

### Mixed Option Question

The *gemara* now raises a question that it acknowledges is somewhat unusual: if one cannot perform *yibum* with two wives and cannot perform *chalitzah* with two wives, perhaps he should do *yibum* with one and *chalitzah* with the other? After all, neither verse individually prohibits this combination. Why assume the obligation is purely either/or?

### Either/Or Resolution

The *gemara* resolves this from a third verse: *im lo yachpotz* — if he does not wish to perform *yibum*, he does *chalitzah*. The implication is: *ha im chafetz, yavam* — if he wants to, he performs *yibum* and does not perform *chalitzah*. The governing principle follows: *kol ha-oleh l'yibbum oleh l'chalitzah, v'chol she-eino oleh l'yibbum eino oleh l'chalitzah* — whoever is eligible for *yibum* is eligible for *chalitzah*, and whoever is ineligible for *yibum* is equally ineligible for *chalitzah*. Once *yibum* has been performed with one wife, the others are no longer candidates for *yibum*; they are therefore also not candidates for *chalitzah*.

The *gemara* then adds a rabbinic rationale as well: *she-lo yomru bayis miktzaso banuy u-miktzaso chalutz* — lest people say that one may build half a house through *yibum* and release the other half through *chalitzah*. This seems circular at first — why should we care if people say that? — but the concern is practical: if it were permitted to do *yibum* first and then *chalitzah*, people might attempt the reverse, performing *chalitzah* first and then *yibum*. That sequence is forbidden, because *keivan shelo banah shuv lo yivneh* — once he has declined to build, he may not build again. The prohibition of *asher lo yivneh* applies retroactively.

### Tzarat Ervah Issue

The *gemara* entertains a further *hava amina*: perhaps the entire *yibum* obligation applies only when there is a single *yevamah*, since all the relevant verses are phrased in the singular — *bayis echad*, one house. When there are two *yevamot*, perhaps there is no *yibum* obligation at all. The *gemara* dismisses this forcefully: *im kein tzaras ervah d'asar rachmana lamah li*? If the obligation simply dissolves whenever there are two *yevamot*, the entire first chapter's discussion of *tzaras ervah* — the co-wife of a forbidden relative — would be superfluous. If two wives already eliminate the *yibum* obligation, there is no need to specify that it is eliminated when one of the two is an *ervah*.

The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps *tzaras ervah* is still necessary. One might have thought that since the *ervah* herself is outside the *yibum* framework — *ervah abara'i kaima* — her co-wife should still be subject to *yibum*. The verse therefore tells us that even the co-wife of an *ervah* is forbidden. This remains a meaningful ruling even within the framework of the *hava amina*.

### Multiple Wives Apply

The *gemara* resolves the question of how *yibum* can apply at all when there are multiple *yevamot* by invoking the doubled formulation of *yevimto yevimto* — the word *yevimto* appears more than once in the Torah, teaching by way of expansion (*ribuy*) that the *yibum* framework applies even when there are multiple *yevamot*. Only one wife need perform *yibum* or *chalitzah*, but the obligation is real and the exemption is comprehensive.

## Preferential Choice

### Valid vs. Invalid Wife

The *gemara* addresses a refinement: what if among the *yevamot* one woman is *kesheirah* — of full valid status — and another is *pesulah*, disqualified in some way (not an *ervah*, but perhaps already a *grushah* from a previous marriage)? The *halacha* differentiates based on whether *chalitzah* or *yibum* is being performed. If the *yavam* intends to perform *chalitzah*, he should do so with the woman who is already *pesulah*. If he intends *yibum*, he should do so with the *kesheirah* — or at minimum, the *Rambam* understands, he may do *yibum* with whichever he wishes, but the preference is for the *kesheirah*.

### Don't Waste Wells

The rationale for performing *chalitzah* specifically with the *pesulah* is articulated by *Rav Yosef*: *amar Rav Yosef kan shanah Rebbi* — here the *Rebbi* taught the principle of *lo yishpoch adam mei boro v'acherim tzrichim lahem* — a person should not pour out the water of his well when others need it. In other words, do not casually disqualify someone when the act of doing so serves no purpose. If *chalitzah* is to be performed anyway, and it will disqualify a woman from marrying a *kohen* by conferring upon her the status of *chalutzah*, perform it with the woman who already cannot marry a *kohen*. Do not needlessly foreclose the options of the *almanah* who retains her full status.

## Personal Status Issues

### Three Cases Intro

Following the conclusion of the *yibum* discussions, the *mishnah* pivots to questions of personal status and *mamzer*. These topics — *geirusin*, *mamzer*, and *psul l'kehunah* — are deeply connected to *Masechet Yevamot*, which serves as a natural home for these fundamental questions of Jewish identity and status.

### Machzir Gerushah

The first case is *hamachzir grushaso* — a man who remarries a woman he previously divorced, after she had in the interim married another man. This is a violation of a negative prohibition in the Torah, but it is not categorized as one of the *arayot* — the cardinal sexual prohibitions. It is an *issur lav*, a simple negative commandment.

### Chalutzah Marriage

The second case is *ve-honosei chalutzaso* — a man who marries a woman with whom he performed *chalitzah*. This too is a simple negative prohibition, grounded in the principle of *keivan she-banah shuv lo yivneh*: once the bond of *yibum* has been formally dissolved, it cannot be rebuilt. This prohibition applies even if the woman subsequently married and divorced another man entirely.

### Relative of Chalutzah

The third case is *ve-honosei kerovas chalutzaso* — a man who marries the relative of his *chalutzah*, for example her sister or her mother. This prohibition appears on its face to be purely rabbinic in character: just as a man may not marry his divorced wife's sister while that prohibition persists, so too the rabbis extended that restriction to the relative of a woman with whom he performed *chalitzah*, treating the *chalutzah* as if she were his divorced wife. The *gemara* will subject this assumption to considerable scrutiny.

---

## Mamzer Debate

### Rabbi Akiva Strict

The *mishnah* records the dispute regarding the offspring of these unions. *Divrei Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva holds that the child is a *mamzer*. His position is striking in its severity: any offspring of any prohibited relationship, even a violation of a simple *lav*, even a case as remote from incest as a man remarrying his own ex-wife, renders the child a *mamzer*. Equally, the child of a man who married his *chalutzah* or the relative of his *chalutzah* is a *mamzer* according to Rabbi Akiva.

### Sages' Standard

The *chachamim* disagree. *Chachamim omrim ein ha-vlad mamzer* — the child is not a *mamzer*. In the Sages' view, the *mamzer* status arises only from the offspring of a relationship that carries *issur kares*, not from a mere *issur lav*. This is the authoritative ruling.

### Divorcée Relatives

Nevertheless, the *chachamim* concede one case: *u-modim be-nosei kerovas gerushaso she-ha-vlad mamzer* — they agree that if a man marries the relative of his actual *grushah*, his divorced wife — her sister, daughter, or mother — the child is a *mamzer*. The prohibitions that attach to relatives of one's wife are not dissolved by divorce (with the single exception of the wife's sister, whose prohibition lapses upon the wife's death). All other prohibited relatives remain forbidden even after divorce, and those prohibitions carry the weight of *ervah* and *kares*. It is therefore a genuine *ervah* violation, and the Sages accordingly agree that a *mamzer* results.

---

## Text Emendation

### Chalutzah Problem

A sharp textual question now emerges: does Rabbi Akiva really hold that marrying the *relative* of a *chalutzah* produces a *mamzer*? That prohibition, as established in an earlier *mishnah*, is only rabbinic. *Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish* had already stated explicitly: *achot gerushah midivrei Torah, achot chalutzah midivrei sofrim* — the sister of a *grushah* is a biblical prohibition, while the sister of a *chalutzah* is only rabbinic. Surely Rabbi Akiva would not generate a biblical *mamzer* status from a rabbinic prohibition.

### RSBL's Solution

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish's formulation thus creates a difficulty for reading the *mishnah* at face value. If the prohibition of marrying a *chalutzah*'s relative is only rabbinic, Rabbi Akiva cannot have intended to declare such a child a *mamzer* in the full biblical sense.

### Text Amendment

The *gemara*'s response is to propose an emendation: *tani kerovas gerushaso* — the text of Rabbi Akiva's ruling should be corrected to read "relative of his *grushah*," not "relative of his *chalutzah*." On this reading, Rabbi Akiva's third case is the relative of a woman he genuinely divorced — a biblical prohibition — and the Sages, while disagreeing with his first two cases, concede this third one.

### Akiva's Intent

The *gemara* marshals an argument in favor of this emendation. The *mishnah* closes with the concession of the *chachamim*: *u-modim be-nosei kerovas gerushaso she-ha-vlad mamzer*. One asks: why do the Sages frame their concession specifically around the case of the relative of a *grushah*, rather than simply stating in the abstract that *mamzer* status applies wherever there is *issur kares*? That latter formulation would appear in a later *mishnah* in any case. If all the Sages wished to convey was the principle that *kares* produces a *mamzer*, there was no need to single out this particular case. *Ela shema mina airy bah* — it must be that Rabbi Akiva himself mentioned this case, and the Sages responded to it specifically, conceding it while rejecting his other claims. This strongly implies that Rabbi Akiva's three cases were: remarrying a *grushah*, marrying a *chalutzah*, and marrying the relative of a *grushah* — not the relative of a *chalutzah*.

### Counter Argument

Yet the *gemara* acknowledges that this inference is not airtight. The Sages might have chosen the case of the relative of a *grushah* not because Rabbi Akiva mentioned it, but because Rabbi Akiva *did* mention the relative of a *chalutzah*, and the Sages wished to contrast that case — which they reject — with the stronger case of the relative of a *grushah*, which they accept. On this reading, the *mishnah* text stands as written: Rabbi Akiva genuinely spoke of *kerovas chalutzaso*, and the Sages replied by conceding only *kerovas gerushaso*. The *gemara* concludes: *ve-dilma le-olam lo airy bah* — perhaps Rabbi Akiva was never discussing the case of the *grushah*'s relative at all, and the plain reading of the *mishnah* — that Rabbi Akiva's three cases include the relative of a *chalutzah* — is in fact the correct one.

---

## Chalutzah Status

### Beit Chalutz

If the *mishnah* is taken at face value — that Rabbi Akiva genuinely holds the relative of a *chalutzah* produces a *mamzer* — one must explain the basis for treating a *chalutzah*'s relatives as biblically forbidden. *Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Avin amar Rabbi Yochanan hayinu ta'ama de-Rabbi Akiva*: the reason for Rabbi Akiva's position is derived from the very verse that describes the *chalitzah* ceremony. The Torah calls the result *beit chalutz ha-na'al* — "the house of the withdrawn shoe." *Ha-kasuv kera'o bayis* — the verse itself designates the *chalutzah*'s household as his *bayis*, his house. This is the same word used to denote his wife. Rabbi Akiva therefore understands the *chalutzah* as possessing a biblically grounded equivalence to a divorced wife.

### Biblical Equivalence

The implication is significant and marks a genuine departure from the assumed framework: although *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) taught that the prohibition of marrying a *chalutzah*'s relative is rabbinic — and this has been the operating assumption throughout — Rabbi Akiva holds it to be biblical. In Rabbi Akiva's reading, *beit chalutz ha-na'al* creates a full scriptural equivalence between a *chalutzah* and a *grushah*, making the relatives of a *chalutzah* as forbidden as those of an ex-wife at the level of *de-Oraisa*. As the *gemara* notes: *ein hachi nami kerovas chalutzaso de-Oraisa* — and that is indeed Rabbi Akiva's view.

## Lav vs. Kares

### Machzir Consensus

The *gemara* now turns to the specific question of what status attaches to the *offspring* of these forbidden unions. *Amar Rav Shimon bar Rebbe: ha-kol modim be-machzir grushaso she-ha-vlad pagum li-kehunah* — everyone agrees that in the case of *machzir grushaso*, even though the child is not a *mamzer*, the child is *pagum li-kehunah*, disqualified for priestly purposes. For a female child, this means she may not marry a *kohen*. The question, then, is whether the offspring of a simple *issur lav* — a union that produces no *mamzer* — nonetheless carries a taint of *psul l'kehunah*.

This is not an abstract question. Its most practically significant application, as the *gemara* will make clear, is the case of a non-Jewish father and a Jewish mother.

### Almana Counterpoint

To establish that the offspring of an *issur lav* carries *pagum* status for *kehunah*, the *gemara* constructs a *kal v'chomer* from the case of *almana l'kohen gadol* — a widow who marries a *kohen gadol*. That union is a violation of a narrowly scoped *lav*, applying only to the *kohen gadol*, not to all priests. Yet the offspring of such a union is *pagum*. If so narrow a prohibition produces *pagum* offspring, how much more so should a universal *issur lav* — like *machzir grushaso*, which applies to all Jewish men — produce *pagum* offspring?

### Almana Unique

The *gemara* rejects this *kal v'chomer*: *mah le-almana she-kein hi atzmah mischaleles* — the *almana* case is unique in that the woman herself becomes a *chalalah* through that act of union. It is specifically because the act of sex constitutes a violation of a *kohen*-specific prohibition that the woman acquires a changed status, and it is that changed status which transmits *psul* to the offspring. This dynamic does not apply in the case of *machzir grushaso*.

### Woman's Status Proof

The *gemara* elaborates on this distinction. A woman's status is affected by an act of prohibited sex only when that act is a prohibition specific to *kehunah* — for example, a *kohen gadol* marrying a widow, or an ordinary *kohen* marrying a *grushah*. In such cases, the woman becomes a *chalalah* and is barred from *kehunah* benefits even if the *kohen* subsequently dies. By contrast, *machzir grushaso* is not a prohibition rooted in *kehunah*. The woman in that case is already a *grushah* and already disqualified from marrying a *kohen*; the act of remarriage does not further diminish her status — she does not, for example, lose her entitlement to eat *terumah* if she is a *bat kohen* (as *Rashi* and *Tosafot* note). Since the woman's status is unchanged by the act, there is no basis for arguing that the child's status is affected.

### To'evah Proof

An additional proof had been raised from the verse *hi to'eivah* — the Torah describes the act of remarriage as "an abomination." A *derashah* reads: *hi to'evah, v'ein baneha to'evim* — she is the abomination, but her children bear no problematic status. This verse seems to explicitly exempt the children of this prohibition from any stigma. The *gemara*, however, notes that this *derashah* can be redirected: *hi to'evah, v'ein tzaratah to'evah* — she is the abomination but not her co-wife. The verse excludes the co-wife from the taint of the prohibition, but says nothing about the children. The *phat* in the verse thus does not definitively resolve the question of the offspring's status.

### Three Cases Baraita

A *baraita* revisits all three cases from the *mishnah* in light of this framework. Rabbi Akiva holds that in each of these *issur lav* cases — *machzir grushaso*, *nosei chalutzaso*, and *nosei kerovas chalutzaso* — *ein lo bakiddushin*, the *kiddushin* is not binding, no *get* is required, the woman becomes invalid, and the child is *pasul*. The *chachamim* take the opposite position on every point: *kiddushin* is binding, a *get* is required if she wishes to leave, *hi kesheirah* — the woman's status is unchanged — and *vlada kasher* — the child is *kasher*.

## Context Specificity

### Akiva vs. Sages

The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the Sages on these three points — the validity of *kiddushin*, the woman's status, and the child's status — is now examined carefully. When the Sages say *vlada kasher*, how comprehensive is that *kashrut*? Does it mean the child is fully valid, including for *kehunah* purposes? Or does it mean only that the child is not a *mamzer*, while still being *pagum li-kehunah*?

### Grammatical Parsing

The *gemara* parses the language precisely. According to Rabbi Akiva, the baraita states *hi pesulah* — she is disqualified. To whom is she disqualified? It cannot mean disqualified from marrying other Jews, since no act of forbidden sex can render the woman herself a *mamzer* — that category applies only to offspring. The only meaningful disqualification the woman could acquire is disqualification from *kehunah* — either from marrying a *kohen* or from eating *terumah* as a *bat kohen*. So when Rabbi Akiva says *hi pesulah*, he means: this *issur lav* is so weighty that it retroactively makes the woman invalid for *kehunah* purposes. When he then says *vlada pasul*, the *gemara* notes that we know from elsewhere that Rabbi Akiva declares such a child a *mamzer*; therefore *pesul* here means *mamzer* status, disqualified from the congregation of Israel.

Turning to the Sages' parallel formulation — *hi kesheirah, vlada kasher* — the *gemara* applies the same logic. *Hi kesheirah* cannot mean that the woman is *kasher* to marry other Jews in the general sense, since that would never have been in doubt; forbidden sex does not create *mamzer* status in the woman herself. Therefore *hi kesheirah* must mean she remains *kesheirah li-kehunah* — the act of sex with an *issur lav* did not alter her *kehunah* eligibility. And since the woman's status remains unchanged by the act, the parallel reading of *vlada kasher* must mean the child too is *kasher li-kehunah*.

Yet the *gemara* resists this inference. *Midi irya? Ha kide'ita, ve-ha kide'ita* — each statement exists in its own context. When the Sages say *hi kesheirah*, they address her particular vulnerability: could she have become a *chalalah*? No, she remains *kesheirah*. When they say *vlada kasher*, they address the child's particular vulnerability: could the child be a *mamzer* as Rabbi Akiva claims? No, the child is *kasher* — meaning, not a *mamzer*. It does not follow that the child is also *kasher li-kehunah*, since that question was never the focus of the Sages' statement about the child.

### No Almana Proof

The *gemara* thus concludes that the *kal v'chomer* from *almana li-kohen gadol* provides no valid proof, and the linguistic analysis of the *baraita* also provides no proof. The underlying question — whether the offspring of a simple *issur lav* is *pagum li-kehunah* — remains genuinely unresolved on the basis of these sources. The *gemara* acknowledges: *almanah kashya* — the attempted proof from *almanah* is problematic — and the entire structure built upon it collapses. In the case of *machzir grushaso*, a simple *issur lav* in which the woman's *kehunah* status is not altered by the act, there is no demonstrated basis for saying the child is *pagum li-kehunah*.

## Kares Only Pagum

### Kares Pagum

The *gemara* now shifts the framework. *Amar Rav Yosef bar Shimon bar Rebbe: ha-kol modim be-ba al chayavei kerisos she-ha-vlad pagum* — everyone agrees that if a man had relations with a woman whose prohibition carries *issur kares*, the child is *pagum*. Having abandoned the claim that an *issur lav* makes a child *pagum*, the *gemara* asks instead about *issur kares*.

### Teimani Concession

Who is the "everyone" who concedes this point? The *gemara* identifies the concession as belonging to Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua holds an even narrower definition of *mamzer* than the *chachamim* — he requires not merely *kares* but *chayavei misos beis din* (capital offenses by a court, such as adultery) to produce a *mamzer*. Even he concedes that the offspring of an *issur kares* is at minimum *pagum*, even if not a full *mamzer*.

### Kal V'Chomer

This concession is again derived via *kal v'chomer* from *almana l'kohen gadol*: if the offspring of that narrow, *kohen*-specific *lav* is *pagum*, then certainly the offspring of a universal *issur kares* should be *pagum*. This time, the *gemara* does not dismiss the argument.

### Zonah Transfer

The reason the *kal v'chomer* succeeds here, where it failed for *issur lav*, is that a woman who engages in a sexually prohibited act carrying *issur kares* is rendered a *zonah* — a category explicitly disqualified from *kehunah* marriage. *Keivan she-ba'alah, asa'ah zonah* — the act of union itself confers *zonah* status upon the woman. Since the woman herself becomes disqualified, that disqualification flows to the child as well. The mechanism thus mirrors that of the *almana*-*kohen gadol* case: it is always the woman's changed status that serves as the conduit for the child's *psul*. When the woman's status does not change — as in a simple *issur lav* — the child is unaffected. When the woman becomes a *zonah* through *issur kares*, that status transmits to the offspring.

## Non-Jew Father

### Consensus Claim

*Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan: ha-kol modim she-eved ve-oved kochavim ha-ba al bas Yisrael she-ha-vlad mamzer* — everyone agrees that a slave or a non-Jew who has relations with a Jewish woman produces a *mamzer*. This is a striking claim, and the *gemara* immediately asks: who is the "everyone" here? The answer is *Shimon Ha-Teimani* — even he who insists that *mamzer* status requires *issur kares* concedes this case.

### Lav But No Kiddushin

The concession is puzzling, because relations with a non-Jew are only an *issur lav*, not an *issur kares*. On Shimon Ha-Teimani's own framework, there should be no *mamzer*. The *gemara* resolves the apparent inconsistency: *hani mili* — the rule that *issur lav* does not produce a *mamzer* applies specifically to *lav*-prohibitions where *kiddushin* is nonetheless valid and binding. In ordinary *issur lav* cases, the man could in principle marry the woman through *kiddushin*, and if he does, a *get* is required to dissolve the marriage. It is within that framework of potential marital bindingness that we say the child is not a *mamzer*.

### Kiddushin Theory

But in the case of a non-Jew or a slave, *kiddushin* is categorically impossible — *keivan d'lo tafsi bu kiddushin, k'chayavei krisos dami* — since marriage cannot be binding, the case is treated as if it were *issur kares*. This raises a broader theoretical question: perhaps the operative criterion for *mamzer* is not *lav* versus *kares* at all, but rather whether *kiddushin* could be binding. When *kiddushin* is binding, the union carries a marital framework, and even a severe prohibition like *kares* does not produce a *mamzer* (for this we shall see the *niddah* counterexample shortly). When *kiddushin* cannot be binding, the offspring lacks any legitimate familial framework, and *mamzer* status results.

### Niddah Comparison

The *niddah* case presents itself as a powerful illustration. Relations with a *niddah* carry *issur kares*, yet the offspring of a husband and wife during a period of *niddah* is universally held not to be a *mamzer*. Why? Because *kiddushin* is fully binding in that marriage — the couple is validly married. The *kares*-level prohibition is severe, but it occurs within the context of a legally recognized marriage, and therefore the *mamzer* criterion is not triggered. This inverts the logic: it is not the *kares* per se that creates a *mamzer*, it may be the absence of bindable *kiddushin*. And if so, the non-Jew case — where *kiddushin* can never bind — would produce a *mamzer* even though it is only a *lav*. This is, the *gemara* acknowledges, a coherent and reasonable argument — though we shall see that the *halacha* does not follow it.

## Non-Jew Non-Mamzer

### RSBY Baraita

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *baraita*: *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda omer ein mamzer ela mi-she-issuro issur ervah ve-onush kares* — a *mamzer* results only from a union that is both a sexual prohibition (*issur ervah*) and punishable by *kares*. Intercourse with a non-Jew, being only an *issur lav*, would not produce a *mamzer* on this view. The *baraita* thus directly contradicts the claim that "everyone" agrees a non-Jew-fathered child is a *mamzer*.

### Rabbi's Possible Excepti…

*Rav Yosef* responds: *Ella amar Rav Yosef man d'chula modei Rabbi* — the "everyone" who concedes this point is *Rebbi*, Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi himself. Although *Rebbi* in another context says the lenient positions apply only according to Rabbi Akiva, and his own ruling aligns with the view that *mamzer* requires *issur kares* — nevertheless, in the specific case of a non-Jewish or slave father, *Rebbi* may concede that the child is a *mamzer*. The rationale is that even one who normally reserves *mamzer* status for *issur kares* might make an exception when *kiddushin* is categorically impossible, treating the non-Jew case as functionally equivalent to an *issur kares* situation in terms of its legal consequences. This remains the position being tested.

## Captive Woman Case

### Ami's Consultation

A narrative is now introduced. *Rabbi Acha Sar Ha-Birah* and *Rabbi Tanchum* the son of *Rabbi Chiya* of *Kfar Akko* redeemed a group of captive women traveling from Armon to Tiberias. One of these captive women had become pregnant through a non-Jew. The practical question of the child's status was brought before *Rabbi Ami*.

### Three Say Mamzer

*Rabbi Ami* reported the opinions he had received: *Rabbi Yochanan, Rabbi Elazar*, and *Rabbi Chanina* all held that a non-Jewish or slave father together with a Jewish mother produces a *mamzer*. These are three of the most authoritative voices in the generation of Palestinian *amoraim*.

### Four Say Kasher

*Rav Yosef* is unimpressed with the weight of three authorities and responds in kind: *ravusa le-mechashev gavrei* — is it such an achievement to count up names? He provides a counter-list of four: *Rav* and *Shmuel* in Bavel, and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* and *Bar Kappara* in Eretz Yisrael — or, in an alternative formulation, *Bar Kappara* is replaced by the *ziknei darom*, the elders of the south. All four (or this variant group of four) hold that the child of a non-Jewish father and Jewish mother is *kasher*.

### Rabbi's Mamzer

The *gemara* does not resolve the standoff by simple head-counting. Instead, *Rav Yosef* himself declares: *ella amar Rav Yosef: Rebbe hi* — it is *Rebbi*'s position that is operative. The basis is the tradition brought by *Rav Dimi* in the name of *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi* in the name of *Rabbeinu* — *Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi* — that *oved kochavim ve-eved ha-ba al bas Yisrael, ha-vlad mamzer*. So we have an explicit ruling attributed to *Rebbi* himself: the child born of a non-Jewish father and a Jewish mother is a *mamzer*.

### Kehunah Status TBD

The daf closes with this contested ruling standing unresolved in terms of final *pesak*. The *gemara* notes, and leaves for the following daf, the second question that flows from this discussion: even granting that we ultimately rule the child is *not* a *mamzer* — as the majority of *poskim* conclude — what is the child's status for *kehunah*? May a daughter born of a non-Jewish father marry a *kohen*? That question, deeply relevant in practice and tightly connected to everything that has been established in today's analysis, will be taken up on the next *amud*.
