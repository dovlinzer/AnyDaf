# Yevamot 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yibum Rights & Exemption

### Mishnah Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבָּעָה אַחִין נְשׂוּאִין אַרְבַּע נָשִׁים וָמֵתוּ, אִם רָצָה הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶם לְיַיבֵּם אֶת כּוּלָּן — הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **four brothers married to four women and** some of the brothers **died** childless, their wives thereby become *yevamot*. **If the eldest of** the brothers who survived **wished to consummate the levirate marriage with all of** his *yevamot*, **he has permission** to do so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַרְבָּעָה אַחִין סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אַרְבָּעָה מֵאַחִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The simple reading of the mishna's first clause implies that all four brothers died. The Gemara questions this: Can it **enter your mind** to say that all **four brothers** died? If they all died, then who remains to consummate the levirate marriage? **Rather,** emend the mishna and **say** instead: **Four** married men **of** a set of more than four **brothers** died.

The daf opens with a corrective gloss on the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* speaks of four brothers, and the *gemara* immediately notes that the formulation "four brothers" raises an obvious difficulty: if they all died, who remains to perform *yibum*? The *gemara* therefore emends the reading — *ela eima arba me-achim*, say rather that four of the brothers died, leaving four widows, while other brothers survive. This is, as the lecturer observes, one of those *mishnayot* in this *masechet* that would have made an excellent opening for the tractate had it appeared earlier.

### Oldest Brother Priority

With the corrected reading in place, the *mishnah* establishes that the oldest surviving brother holds the right — *ha-reshut beyado* — to perform *yibum* with all four widows before any other brother may act. He is not limited to one; if he wishes, he may marry all of them before the younger brothers have any opportunity to intervene.

### One Exempts All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁהָיָה נָשׂוּי לִשְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים וּמֵת — בִּיאָתָהּ אוֹ חֲלִיצָתָהּ שֶׁל אַחַת מֵהֶן פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who was married to two women and died** childless, the **intercourse or *ḥalitza* of** either **one of** the wives with the *yavam* **releases her rival wife** from the levirate bond, and the rival wife need not enter into levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza*.

The *mishnah* then addresses the exemption principle that has underlain the entire tractate from its opening: *chalitzah* or *yibum* performed with any one wife exempts all the others from further obligation. Though this has been assumed throughout, the *gemara* now derives it explicitly from scriptural sources, which are examined in detail in the following section.

## Four Wives Limit

### Elders' Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ. וְשָׁבְקִי לֵיהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְקָרְאוּ לוֹ זִקְנֵי עִירוֹ״ — הֵן וְלֹא שְׁלוּחָן. ״וְדִבְּרוּ אֵלָיו״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמַּשִּׂיאִין לוֹ עֵצָה הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the eldest of them wished to consummate the levirate marriage with all of his *yevamot*, **he has permission** to do so. The Gemara asks: **Do they** actually **leave him** to do so? **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And the Elders of his city shall call him"** (Deuteronomy 25:8), which indicates that **they,** the Elders, **and not their agent,** should call him. The verse continues: **"And they speak to him";** this phrase **teaches that they offer him advice that is appropriate for him.**

The *gemara* raises a challenge: would we simply allow this brother to marry four women simultaneously? Surely that is an extraordinary domestic situation. The *gemara* invokes a *baraita* on the verse *ve-karu lo ziknei iro* — "and the elders of his city shall call to him." The *gemara* understands *ve-dibru eilav*, "and they shall speak to him," as mandating substantive guidance, not mere ritual formality. *Melamed she-masiin lo eitzah hogenes lo* — they give him advice that is appropriate and fitting for him specifically. Crucially, the elders must address him directly, *hen ve-lo sheluchan*, not through an intermediary.

### Age Matching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאִם הָיָה הוּא יֶלֶד וְהִיא זְקֵנָה, הוּא זָקֵן וְהִיא יַלְדָּה, אוֹמְרִין לוֹ: מָה לְךָ אֵצֶל יַלְדָּה, מָה לְךָ אֵצֶל זְקֵנָה? כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל שֶׁכְּמוֹתְךָ, וְאַל תָּשִׂים קְטָטָה בְּבֵיתֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: Appropriate advice means **that if he was a young man and she an elderly woman** or if **he was an elderly man and she a young woman, they say to him: What do you** want **with a young woman** when you are elderly? Or: **What do you** want **with an elderly woman** when you are young? **Go after your own kind,** i.e., a woman of a similar age, **and do not place discord in your household** that could be caused by marrying a woman of a significantly different age. From the *baraita* it is apparent that if consummating the levirate marriage will ultimately lead to contention between the couple, it is preferable to perform *ḥalitza*. Similarly, in the case of the mishna, marrying four women will likely lead to contention since it is difficult to support so many people, and poverty will lead to strife. Therefore, the *yavam* should not be allowed to consummate levirate marriages with all of them.

The content of that guidance is elaborated: if he is young and the *yevamah* is elderly, or he is elderly and she is young, the elders say to him — *mah lecha etzel yaldah*, what do you want with such a young woman, or *mah lecha etzel zekeinah*, what do you want with such an old woman? *Kelech etzel she-kamotecha*, go and find someone of your own age, *ve-al tasim ketatah be-veitecha* — do not bring strife into your house. The advice is non-binding; he is free to reject it. And notably, the woman's own voice is not heard in this passage, a theme that recurs throughout the tractate.

### Math of Conjugal Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאֶפְשָׁר לֵיהּ. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא נָמֵי! עֵצָה טוֹבָה קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: אַרְבַּע — אִין, טְפֵי — לָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִמְטְיֵיהּ עוֹנָה בְּחֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara qualifies the mishna's case: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach that he has permission to consummate the levirate marriage with all of his *yevamot* in the case **where it is possible for him** to provide for all four women. The Gemara asks: **If so,** then the same should be true **even** if there are **many** more women **as well;** why does the mishna specifically discuss a case of four women? The Gemara explains: The mishna **teaches us good advice;** in a case of up to **four** women, **yes,** if he can provide for them then it is acceptable to marry all of them. But if there are any **more** than that, **no,** he should not, **in order that he will** be able to **meet the conjugal rights** of each woman at least once **in** each **month.** A Torah scholar is expected to provide conjugal relations once a week. If he marries no more than four women, then that will ensure that each of his wives will receive their conjugal rights at least once a month.

The immediate question is why the *mishnah* limits the brother to four wives. If he is a man of means who can support multiple wives — the *gemara* answers *lo tzericha de-efshar lei*, this is indeed a case where he has the resources — why not permit ten or twenty? The *gemara* answers: *eitzah tovah ka mashma lan*, this is good advice even for a wealthy man. *Arba in, tfeih lo* — four wives yes, more no. The reasoning is mathematical: *ki heichi de-timtei onah be-chodesh*, the *mitzvah* of *onah* for a *talmid chacham* is once per week. With four wives rotating, each wife receives conjugal attention once a month. Any more than four wives and a woman would receive her *onah* less than once a month, which is an unacceptable diminishment of her rights. Four, therefore, is both the maximum permitted and the outer limit of practical wisdom.

### Senegal Digression

It is an interesting coincidence that Islamic law similarly limits a man to four wives, a number said to reflect the personal practice of Muhammad. The parallel with the Talmud's reasoning is worth noting, though the justifications differ. By way of illustration: on a rabbinic mission to Senegal — a society in which the overwhelming majority of the population practices polygamy, with only a small Christian minority living monogamously — the lecturer had occasion to observe a kind of living parallel to the world of *Avraham, Yitzchak, ve-Yaakov*: village compounds in which co-wives occupied separate quarters, maintained their own domestic rotations, and shared child-rearing responsibilities. A question from one of the visiting rabbinical students — "what is it like living in a polygamous society?" — met with understandable puzzlement from the Senegalese women, who might equally have asked what it is like to be monogamous. The tension was dissolved with a joke: asked what the penalty for polygamy is, the answer was — two mothers-in-law. The laughter that followed confirmed that mother-in-law humor requires no translation across cultures.

## Yibum vs. Chalitzah

### Bayis Echad Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְיַיבֵּם לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ! אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִבְנֶה אֶת בֵּית אָחִיו״, בַּיִת אֶחָד הוּא בּוֹנֶה, וְאֵין בּוֹנֵה שְׁנֵי בָתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions why the mishna considers only the possibility that he would do so with only one of the women: **But let him** instead **consummate levirate marriages with both of them. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The verse states** that a *yavam* who performs *ḥalitza* is referred to as: "The man **who does not build his brother's house"** (Deuteronomy 25:9). The fact that the word "house" appears in the singular indicates that even had he instead chosen to consummate a levirate marriage, only **one house may he build,** by consummating a levirate marriage with one of his brother's wives, **but he may not build two houses.**

Having addressed the practical question of how many wives a *yavam* may take in *yibum*, the *gemara* now establishes the scriptural basis for the principle that *yibum* or *chalitzah* with one wife exempts all the others — and that it must be one or the other, not both. *Amar Rabbi Chama berabi Chanina amar Rabbi Yochanan amar kra*: the verse reads *asher lo yivneh et beit achiv*, describing the man who will not build his brother's house. The *gemara* derives: *bayis echad hu boneh, v'ein boneh shnei batim* — he builds one house, not two. And since *beito zu ishto* — "his house" is his wife — this limits *yibum* to a single wife. The equation of *bayis* and *ishah* is a well-established trope in the *gemara*.

### Chalitzah Exclusivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַחְלוֹץ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ?! אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה, אָמַר קְרָא: ״בֵּית חֲלוּץ הַנַּעַל״, בַּיִת אֶחָד הוּא חוֹלֵץ, וְאֵין חוֹלֵץ שְׁנֵי בָתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But let him perform *ḥalitza* with both of them;** why does the mishna state that he does so with only one woman? **Mar Zutra bar Toviya said: The verse states** that following the *ḥalitza* the man is called: **"The house of him who had his shoe removed"** (Deuteronomy 25:10). The fact that the word "house" appears in the singular indicates that **he performs *ḥalitza* with** only **one house,** i.e., only one of his brother's wives, **and he does not perform *ḥalitza* with two houses.**

The same principle is derived for *chalitzah* from a different verse. *Amar Mar Zutra bar Tuvia amar kra*: the verse *beit chalutz ha-na'al*, "the house of the withdrawn shoe," similarly uses the singular — *bayis echad hu choletz, v'ein choletz shnei batim* — he performs *chalitzah* with one house, not two.

### Mixed Option Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְיַיבֵּם לַחֲדָא וְנַחְלוֹץ לַחֲדָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But let him consummate the levirate marriage with one and perform *ḥalitza* with the other.**

The *gemara* now raises a question that it acknowledges is somewhat unusual: if one cannot perform *yibum* with two wives and cannot perform *chalitzah* with two wives, perhaps he should do *yibum* with one and *chalitzah* with the other? After all, neither verse individually prohibits this combination. Why assume the obligation is purely either/or?

### Either/Or Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״אִם לֹא יַחְפּוֹץ״, הָא חָפֵץ — יְיַבֵּם. כׇּל הָעוֹלֶה לְיִבּוּם — עוֹלֶה לַחֲלִיצָה, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין עוֹלֶה לְיִיבּוּם — אֵין עוֹלֶה לַחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **the verse states: "If** the man **does not wish** to take his *yevama*" (Deuteronomy 25:7), which implies that with regard to anyone who performs *ḥalitza*, **were he to wish** to, **he could consummate the levirate marriage.** This teaches the principle that only **one who is eligible for levirate marriage is eligible for *ḥalitza*,** but **one who is ineligible for levirate marriage is ineligible for *ḥalitza*.** And since, in the case of the mishna, if the *yavam* consummated the levirate marriage with one of his brother's wives he may not do so with the second, consequently he may not perform *ḥalitza* with her either.

The *gemara* resolves this from a third verse: *im lo yachpotz* — if he does not wish to perform *yibum*, he does *chalitzah*. The implication is: *ha im chafetz, yavam* — if he wants to, he performs *yibum* and does not perform *chalitzah*. The governing principle follows: *kol ha-oleh l'yibbum oleh l'chalitzah, v'chol she-eino oleh l'yibbum eino oleh l'chalitzah* — whoever is eligible for *yibum* is eligible for *chalitzah*, and whoever is ineligible for *yibum* is equally ineligible for *chalitzah*. Once *yibum* has been performed with one wife, the others are no longer candidates for *yibum*; they are therefore also not candidates for *chalitzah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ: בַּיִת מִקְצָתוֹ בָּנוּי וּמִקְצָתוֹ חָלוּץ. וְיֹאמְרוּ! אִי דִּמְיַיבֵּם וַהֲדַר חָלֵיץ — הָכִי נָמֵי. אֶלָּא, [דִּלְמָא] חָלֵיץ וַהֲדַר מְיַיבֵּם, וְקָם לֵיהּ בְּ״לֹא יִבְנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** an additional reason that one may not do so is so **that** people **should not say** that the brother's **house was partially built** through levirate marriage **and partially released through *ḥalitza*.** The Gemara asks: **And** even if **they will say** that, what of it? The Gemara explains: **If he** first **consummated the levirate marriage** with one wife **and subsequently performed *ḥalitza*** with the other, **indeed,** there is no reason not to do this. **However, perhaps he** might first **perform *ḥalitza*** with one wife **and subsequently consummate levirate marriage** with the other, **and** by doing so **he is liable for** a violation of the prohibition **of: "He does not build** his brother's house" (Deuteronomy 25:9). The verse indicates that one who performs *ḥalitza* has not built his brother's house and is therefore subsequently prohibited from attempting to do so by consummating a levirate marriage with either the *ḥalutza* or any of her rival wives.

The *gemara* then adds a rabbinic rationale as well: *she-lo yomru bayis miktzaso banuy u-miktzaso chalutz* — lest people say that one may build half a house through *yibum* and release the other half through *chalitzah*. This seems circular at first — why should we care if people say that? — but the concern is practical: if it were permitted to do *yibum* first and then *chalitzah*, people might attempt the reverse, performing *chalitzah* first and then *yibum*. That sequence is forbidden, because *keivan shelo banah shuv lo yivneh* — once he has declined to build, he may not build again. The prohibition of *asher lo yivneh* applies retroactively.

### Tzarat Ervah Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: כִּי אִיכָּא חֲדָא — תִּתְקַיֵּים מִצְוַת יִבּוּם, כִּי אִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי — לֹא תִּתְקַיֵּים מִצְוַת יִבּוּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks. **But** since the Torah's description of levirate marriage mentions only a case in which the deceased brother had only one wife, **say: When there is only one** wife, **the mitzva of levirate marriage exists,** but **when there are two** wives, **the mitzva of levirate marriage does not exist.**

The *gemara* entertains a further *hava amina*: perhaps the entire *yibum* obligation applies only when there is a single *yevamah*, since all the relevant verses are phrased in the singular — *bayis echad*, one house. When there are two *yevamot*, perhaps there is no *yibum* obligation at all. The *gemara* dismisses this forcefully: *im kein tzaras ervah d'asar rachmana lamah li*? If the obligation simply dissolves whenever there are two *yevamot*, the entire first chapter's discussion of *tzaras ervah* — the co-wife of a forbidden relative — would be superfluous. If two wives already eliminate the *yibum* obligation, there is no need to specify that it is eliminated when one of the two is an *ervah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, צָרַת עֶרְוָה דְּאָסַר רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? הַשְׁתָּא תַּרְתֵּי בְּעָלְמָא אָמְרַתְּ לָאו בְּנֵי חֲלִיצָה וְיִבּוּם נִינְהוּ — צָרַת עֶרְוָה מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a proof: **If** that were **so, why do I** need the *halakha* concerning **a rival wife of a forbidden relation, which is forbidden by the Merciful One** in the Torah? **Now** that even in the case of **two** women in **general,** where neither woman is a forbidden relation, **you say** that **they are not eligible for *ḥalitza* and levirate marriage, is it necessary** to say **a rival wife of a forbidden relation** is also forbidden? The fact that the Torah does prohibit a rival wife of a forbidden relation indicates that the mitzva of levirate marriage does exist in a general case of two wives in which neither is a forbidden relation.

The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps *tzaras ervah* is still necessary. One might have thought that since the *ervah* herself is outside the *yibum* framework — *ervah abara'i kaima* — her co-wife should still be subject to *yibum*. The verse therefore tells us that even the co-wife of an *ervah* is forbidden. This remains a meaningful ruling even within the framework of the *hava amina*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְּמָה לָא? אִצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: עֶרְוָה אַבָּרַאי קָיְימָא, וְתִתְיַיבֵּם צָרָתַהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Why not?** Even if one assumes that there is no mitzva of levirate marriage when the deceased brother had two wives, **it is** still **necessary** to teach the *halakha* concerning a rival wife of a forbidden relation because it could **enter your mind to say** that since there is no possibility of consummating the levirate marriage with her, **the forbidden relation stands outside** the calculation such that her presence is disregarded, **and her rival wife should enter into levirate marriage** as though she were the only wife. Therefore, the verse needs to **teach us that** the rival wife **is forbidden.**

### Multiple Wives Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: ״יְבִמְתּוֹ״ ״יְבִמְתּוֹ״ — רִיבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the repeated use of the phrase **"his *yevama*," "his *yevama*"** in the verses concerning levirate marriage **amplifies** the mitzva of levirate marriage so that it applies even when the deceased brother had more than one wife.

The *gemara* resolves the question of how *yibum* can apply at all when there are multiple *yevamot* by invoking the doubled formulation of *yevimto yevimto* — the word *yevimto* appears more than once in the Torah, teaching by way of expansion (*ribuy*) that the *yibum* framework applies even when there are multiple *yevamot*. Only one wife need perform *yibum* or *chalitzah*, but the obligation is real and the exemption is comprehensive.

## Preferential Choice

### Valid vs. Invalid Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה אַחַת כְּשֵׁרָה וְאַחַת פְּסוּלָה, אִם הָיָה חוֹלֵץ — חוֹלֵץ לַפְּסוּלָה, וְאִם הָיָה מְיַיבֵּם — מְיַיבֵּם לַכְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **one** of these women **was fit** to marry into the priesthood **and one was unfit,** then **if he performs *ḥalitza*, he should perform *ḥalitza* with the unfit** woman rather than with the one who is fit for the priesthood, since doing so with the woman who is fit would needlessly disqualify her from marrying into the priesthood. **But if he consummates the levirate marriage, he may consummate the levirate marriage with** the one who is **fit.**

The *gemara* addresses a refinement: what if among the *yevamot* one woman is *kesheirah* — of full valid status — and another is *pesulah*, disqualified in some way (not an *ervah*, but perhaps already a *grushah* from a previous marriage)? The *halacha* differentiates based on whether *chalitzah* or *yibum* is being performed. If the *yavam* intends to perform *chalitzah*, he should do so with the woman who is already *pesulah*. If he intends *yibum*, he should do so with the *kesheirah* — or at minimum, the *Rambam* understands, he may do *yibum* with whichever he wishes, but the preference is for the *kesheirah*.

### Don't Waste Wells

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, כָּאן שָׁנָה רַבִּי: לֹא יִשְׁפּוֹךְ אָדָם מֵי בוֹרוֹ, וַאֲחֵרִים צְרִיכִים לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** that **here,** through this mishna, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught** the principle that one should not perform an action that will needlessly disadvantage others, and so **a person should not spill out water** collected in **his pit** that he does not need **when others are in need of it.**

The rationale for performing *chalitzah* specifically with the *pesulah* is articulated by *Rav Yosef*: *amar Rav Yosef kan shanah Rebbi* — here the *Rebbi* taught the principle of *lo yishpoch adam mei boro v'acherim tzrichim lahem* — a person should not pour out the water of his well when others need it. In other words, do not casually disqualify someone when the act of doing so serves no purpose. If *chalitzah* is to be performed anyway, and it will disqualify a woman from marrying a *kohen* by conferring upon her the status of *chalutzah*, perform it with the woman who already cannot marry a *kohen*. Do not needlessly foreclose the options of the *almanah* who retains her full status.

## Personal Status Issues

### Three Cases Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּחְזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ, וְהַנּוֹשֵׂא חֲלוּצָתוֹ, וְהַנּוֹשֵׂא קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ — יוֹצִיא, וְהַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who remarries his divorcée** after she had been married to another man from whom she was then widowed or divorced, **or one who marries the woman with whom he performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*], or one who marries a relative of his *ḥalutza*,** since all such marriages are forbidden he **must divorce** her, **and the offspring** born from such unions **is a *mamzer*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.** He holds that even the offspring from relations forbidden by a prohibition punishable by lashes is a *mamzer*.

Following the conclusion of the *yibum* discussions, the *mishnah* pivots to questions of personal status and *mamzer*. These topics — *geirusin*, *mamzer*, and *psul l'kehunah* — are deeply connected to *Masechet Yevamot*, which serves as a natural home for these fundamental questions of Jewish identity and status.

### Machzir Gerushah

The first case is *hamachzir grushaso* — a man who remarries a woman he previously divorced, after she had in the interim married another man. This is a violation of a negative prohibition in the Torah, but it is not categorized as one of the *arayot* — the cardinal sexual prohibitions. It is an *issur lav*, a simple negative commandment.

### Chalutzah Marriage

The second case is *ve-honosei chalutzaso* — a man who marries a woman with whom he performed *chalitzah*. This too is a simple negative prohibition, grounded in the principle of *keivan she-banah shuv lo yivneh*: once the bond of *yibum* has been formally dissolved, it cannot be rebuilt. This prohibition applies even if the woman subsequently married and divorced another man entirely.

### Relative of Chalutzah

The third case is *ve-honosei kerovas chalutzaso* — a man who marries the relative of his *chalutzah*, for example her sister or her mother. This prohibition appears on its face to be purely rabbinic in character: just as a man may not marry his divorced wife's sister while that prohibition persists, so too the rabbis extended that restriction to the relative of a woman with whom he performed *chalitzah*, treating the *chalutzah* as if she were his divorced wife. The *gemara* will subject this assumption to considerable scrutiny.

## Mamzer Debate

### Rabbi Akiva Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. וּמוֹדִים בְּנוֹשֵׂא קְרוֹבַת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ — שֶׁהַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis say: The offspring** in those cases **is not a *mamzer*, but they concede with regard to one who marries a relative of his divorcée,** a union forbidden by a prohibition entailing *karet*, **that the offspring is a *mamzer*.** They hold that only the offspring from relations forbidden by a prohibition entailing *karet* is a *mamzer*.

The *mishnah* records the dispute regarding the offspring of these unions. *Divrei Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva holds that the child is a *mamzer*. His position is striking in its severity: any offspring of any prohibited relationship, even a violation of a simple *lav*, even a case as remote from incest as a man remarrying his own ex-wife, renders the child a *mamzer*. Equally, the child of a man who married his *chalutzah* or the relative of his *chalutzah* is a *mamzer* according to Rabbi Akiva.

### Sages' Standard

The *chachamim* disagree. *Chachamim omrim ein ha-vlad mamzer* — the child is not a *mamzer*. In the Sages' view, the *mamzer* status arises only from the offspring of a relationship that carries *issur kares*, not from a mere *issur lav*. This is the authoritative ruling.

### Divorcée Relatives

Nevertheless, the *chachamim* concede one case: *u-modim be-nosei kerovas gerushaso she-ha-vlad mamzer* — they agree that if a man marries the relative of his actual *grushah*, his divorced wife — her sister, daughter, or mother — the child is a *mamzer*. The prohibitions that attach to relatives of one's wife are not dissolved by divorce (with the single exception of the wife's sister, whose prohibition lapses upon the wife's death). All other prohibited relatives remain forbidden even after divorce, and those prohibitions carry the weight of *ervah* and *kares*. It is therefore a genuine *ervah* violation, and the Sages accordingly agree that a *mamzer* results.

## Text Emendation

### Chalutzah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְסָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הַנּוֹשֵׂא קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר? וְהָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, כָּאן שָׁנָה רַבִּי: אֲחוֹת גְּרוּשָׁה מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֲחוֹת חֲלוּצָה מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים! תְּנִי: קְרוֹבַת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But does Rabbi Akiva** actually **hold** that with regard to **one who marries a relative of his *ḥalutza*, the offspring is a *mamzer*? Didn't Reish Lakish say** that through the mishna **here, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught** that **a sister of** one's **divorcée** is forbidden **by Torah law** whereas **a sister of** one's ***ḥalutza*** is forbidden **by rabbinic law?** If a relative of one's *ḥalutza* is forbidden by rabbinic law, how can the offspring of that union be a *mamzer*? The Gemara concedes: Emend the mishna and **teach** instead: **A relative of his divorcée.**

A sharp textual question now emerges: does Rabbi Akiva really hold that marrying the *relative* of a *chalutzah* produces a *mamzer*? That prohibition, as established in an earlier *mishnah*, is only rabbinic. *Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish* had already stated explicitly: *achot gerushah midivrei Torah, achot chalutzah midivrei sofrim* — the sister of a *grushah* is a biblical prohibition, while the sister of a *chalutzah* is only rabbinic. Surely Rabbi Akiva would not generate a biblical *mamzer* status from a rabbinic prohibition.

### RSBL's Solution

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish's formulation thus creates a difficulty for reading the *mishnah* at face value. If the prohibition of marrying a *chalutzah*'s relative is only rabbinic, Rabbi Akiva cannot have intended to declare such a child a *mamzer* in the full biblical sense.

### Text Amendment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וּמוֹדִים בְּנוֹשֵׂא קְרוֹבַת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ שֶׁהַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אַיְירִי בָּהּ — הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״וּמוֹדִים״. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָא אַיְירִי בַּהּ, מַאי ״וּמוֹדִים״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **So, too, it is reasonable** that this is the correct version of the mishna, as **the latter clause teaches: But they concede** in the case of **one who marries a relative of his divorcée that the offspring is a *mamzer*. Granted, if you say** that Rabbi Akiva **was talking about that** case, **this is** consistent with **that which** the mishna **teaches: They concede,** which implies that they agree to a case already mentioned. **However, if you say** that he **was not talking about that** case, **what** could the phrase: **They concede,** possibly be referring to?

The *gemara*'s response is to propose an emendation: *tani kerovas gerushaso* — the text of Rabbi Akiva's ruling should be corrected to read "relative of his *grushah*," not "relative of his *chalutzah*." On this reading, Rabbi Akiva's third case is the relative of a woman he genuinely divorced — a biblical prohibition — and the Sages, while disagreeing with his first two cases, concede this third one.

### Akiva's Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּיֵשׁ מַמְזֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת. הָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ לְקַמַּן: אֵיזֶהוּ מַמְזֵר — כׇּל שְׁאֵר בָּשָׂר שֶׁהוּא בְּ״לֹא יָבֹא״ — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם. וַהֲלָכָה כִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But perhaps this** phrase **teaches us that** the Rabbis concede **that** the offspring **from** forbidden relations for which one **is liable to** receive ***karet* is a *mamzer*.** The Gemara rejects this option: **This is already taught later on** in a mishna (49a): **Which** offspring of forbidden relations has the status of **a *mamzer*?** It is the offspring of a union with **any next of kin that is subject to** a Torah prohibition that one **should not engage in sexual relations** with them; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Shimon HaTimni says:** It is the offspring of a union with **any** forbidden relative **for which one is liable to** receive ***karet* at the hand of Heaven. And** the ***halakha* is in accordance with his statement.** Since the mishna explicitly rules in accordance with Rabbi Shimon HaTimni, it would be unnecessary for the mishna here to teach that fact.

The *gemara* marshals an argument in favor of this emendation. The *mishnah* closes with the concession of the *chachamim*: *u-modim be-nosei kerovas gerushaso she-ha-vlad mamzer*. One asks: why do the Sages frame their concession specifically around the case of the relative of a *grushah*, rather than simply stating in the abstract that *mamzer* status applies wherever there is *issur kares*? That latter formulation would appear in a later *mishnah* in any case. If all the Sages wished to convey was the principle that *kares* produces a *mamzer*, there was no need to single out this particular case. *Ela shema mina airy bah* — it must be that Rabbi Akiva himself mentioned this case, and the Sages responded to it specifically, conceding it while rejecting his other claims. This strongly implies that Rabbi Akiva's three cases were: remarrying a *grushah*, marrying a *chalutzah*, and marrying the relative of a *grushah* — not the relative of a *chalutzah*.

### Counter Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָ[א] לְעוֹלָם לָא אַיְירִי בַּהּ, וְאַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא מַחֲזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ וְנוֹשֵׂא חֲלוּצָתוֹ וּקְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ — תָּנֵי נָמֵי קְרוֹבַת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara persists: **But perhaps he was not actually talking about that** case, **but since** Rabbi Akiva **taught** the cases of **one who remarries his divorcée, or one who marries his *ḥalutza* or a relative of his *ḥalutza*,** the mishna **also taught** in the name of the Rabbis the case of one who marries **a relative of his divorcée** because it is a similar case. The Gemara concedes that this would be an acceptable reading of the mishna.

Yet the *gemara* acknowledges that this inference is not airtight. The Sages might have chosen the case of the relative of a *grushah* not because Rabbi Akiva mentioned it, but because Rabbi Akiva *did* mention the relative of a *chalutzah*, and the Sages wished to contrast that case — which they reject — with the stronger case of the relative of a *grushah*, which they accept. On this reading, the *mishnah* text stands as written: Rabbi Akiva genuinely spoke of *kerovas chalutzaso*, and the Sages replied by conceding only *kerovas gerushaso*. The *gemara* concludes: *ve-dilma le-olam lo airy bah* — perhaps Rabbi Akiva was never discussing the case of the *grushah*'s relative at all, and the plain reading of the *mishnah* — that Rabbi Akiva's three cases include the relative of a *chalutzah* — is in fact the correct one.

## Chalutzah Status

### Beit Chalutz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הָוֵי מַמְזֵר. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״בֵּית חֲלוּץ הַנַּעַל״, הַכָּתוּב קְרָאוֹ ״בֵּיתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the mishna should not be emended, and with regard to the Gemara's original question as to how Rabbi Akiva could claim that the offspring from one's union with a relative of his *ḥalutza* is a *mamzer* if that union is forbidden only by rabbinic law, one must conclude that **a relative of one's *ḥalutza*** is forbidden by Torah law **according to Rabbi Akiva** and therefore the offspring **is a *mamzer*. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **this is Rabbi Akiva's reasoning: As the verse states: "The house of he who had his shoe removed"** (Deuteronomy 25:10). **The verse called** his relationship with the *ḥalutza* **his house,** indicating that the *ḥalutza* is considered as though she had been married to the *yavam*, and therefore her relative is forbidden to him by Torah law.

If the *mishnah* is taken at face value — that Rabbi Akiva genuinely holds the relative of a *chalutzah* produces a *mamzer* — one must explain the basis for treating a *chalutzah*'s relatives as biblically forbidden. *Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Avin amar Rabbi Yochanan hayinu ta'ama de-Rabbi Akiva*: the reason for Rabbi Akiva's position is derived from the very verse that describes the *chalitzah* ceremony. The Torah calls the result *beit chalutz ha-na'al* — "the house of the withdrawn shoe." *Ha-kasuv kera'o bayis* — the verse itself designates the *chalutzah*'s household as his *bayis*, his house. This is the same word used to denote his wife. Rabbi Akiva therefore understands the *chalutzah* as possessing a biblically grounded equivalence to a divorced wife.

### Biblical Equivalence

The implication is significant and marks a genuine departure from the assumed framework: although *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) taught that the prohibition of marrying a *chalutzah*'s relative is rabbinic — and this has been the operating assumption throughout — Rabbi Akiva holds it to be biblical. In Rabbi Akiva's reading, *beit chalutz ha-na'al* creates a full scriptural equivalence between a *chalutzah* and a *grushah*, making the relatives of a *chalutzah* as forbidden as those of an ex-wife at the level of *de-Oraisa*. As the *gemara* notes: *ein hachi nami kerovas chalutzaso de-Oraisa* — and that is indeed Rabbi Akiva's view.

## Lav vs. Kares

### Machzir Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּמַחֲזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** that **Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: All agree in** the case of **one who remarries his divorcée**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהַוָּלָד פָּגוּם לַכְּהוּנָּה. מַאן הַכֹּל מוֹדִים — שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי: אֵין מַמְזֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין, נְהִי דְּמַמְזֵר לָא הָוֵי, פָּגוּם מִיהָא הָוֵי,
>
> **Translation:** **that the offspring,** even if not a *mamzer*, **has flawed** lineage and, if the offspring is a girl, is unfit **to** marry into the **priesthood.** The Gemara clarifies: **Who** is included by saying: **All agree?** It is **Shimon HaTimni, as although Shimon HaTimni said** that the offspring of relations **for which one is liable for** violating **a prohibition is not a *mamzer*,** nevertheless, **granted** that Shimon HaTimni holds that **he is not a *mamzer*;** he would agree, **however,** that **he is** of **flawed** lineage.

The *gemara* now turns to the specific question of what status attaches to the *offspring* of these forbidden unions. *Amar Rav Shimon bar Rebbe: ha-kol modim be-machzir grushaso she-ha-vlad pagum li-kehunah* — everyone agrees that in the case of *machzir grushaso*, even though the child is not a *mamzer*, the child is *pagum li-kehunah*, disqualified for priestly purposes. For a female child, this means she may not marry a *kohen*. The question, then, is whether the offspring of a simple *issur lav* — a union that produces no *mamzer* — nonetheless carries a taint of *psul l'kehunah*.

This is not an abstract question. Its most practically significant application, as the *gemara* will make clear, is the case of a non-Jewish father and a Jewish mother.

### Almana Counterpoint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר מֵאַלְמָנָה. וּמָה אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרָהּ שָׁוֶה בַּכֹּל — בְּנָהּ פָּגוּם, זוֹ, שֶׁאִיסּוּרָהּ שָׁוֶה בַּכֹּל — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁבְּנָהּ פָּגוּם?
>
> **Translation:** This is derived **through an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* pertaining to **a widow** who is married **to a High Priest, where the prohibition** that pertains **to her is not equally applicable to all,** i.e., only a High Priest is prohibited from marrying a widow, and nevertheless **her child** from that union will **have flawed** lineage, then so too with regard to **this** divorcée, who married another man and is therefore prohibited from marrying her original husband, **where the prohibition** that pertains **to her is equally applicable to all** Jews, as the prohibition to remarry one's divorcée after she was married to another man applies to all Jews, **isn't it logical that her child** from that union will have **flawed** lineage?

To establish that the offspring of an *issur lav* carries *pagum* status for *kehunah*, the *gemara* constructs a *kal v'chomer* from the case of *almana l'kohen gadol* — a widow who marries a *kohen gadol*. That union is a violation of a narrowly scoped *lav*, applying only to the *kohen gadol*, not to all priests. Yet the offspring of such a union is *pagum*. If so narrow a prohibition produces *pagum* offspring, how much more so should a universal *issur lav* — like *machzir grushaso*, which applies to all Jewish men — produce *pagum* offspring?

### Almana Unique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְאַלְמָנָה שֶׁכֵּן הִיא עַצְמָהּ מִתְחַלֶּלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a number of objections: The logic of this *a fortiori* inference **can be refuted: What** is true with regard **to a widow, where** her union with a High Priest is what makes **her herself disqualified** from subsequently marrying any priest and, if she is the daughter of a priest, from eating *teruma*, is not true with regard to a remarried divorcée, where her remarriage to another man is permitted and is not the cause of any further disqualification. Therefore, one case cannot be deduced from the other.

The *gemara* rejects this *kal v'chomer*: *mah le-almana she-kein hi atzmah mischaleles* — the *almana* case is unique in that the woman herself becomes a *chalalah* through that act of union. It is specifically because the act of sex constitutes a violation of a *kohen*-specific prohibition that the woman acquires a changed status, and it is that changed status which transmits *psul* to the offspring. This dynamic does not apply in the case of *machzir grushaso*.

### Woman's Status Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד: ״הִיא תּוֹעֵבָה״ כְּתִיב, וְאֵין בָּנֶיהָ תּוֹעֲבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, "She is an abomination"** (Deuteronomy 24:4) **is written** with regard to remarrying with one's divorcée, and the emphasis on the word "she" teaches that only she is considered so, **but her children are not abominations** and they have unflawed lineage.

The *gemara* elaborates on this distinction. A woman's status is affected by an act of prohibited sex only when that act is a prohibition specific to *kehunah* — for example, a *kohen gadol* marrying a widow, or an ordinary *kohen* marrying a *grushah*. In such cases, the woman becomes a *chalalah* and is barred from *kehunah* benefits even if the *kohen* subsequently dies. By contrast, *machzir grushaso* is not a prohibition rooted in *kehunah*. The woman in that case is already a *grushah* and already disqualified from marrying a *kohen*; the act of remarriage does not further diminish her status — she does not, for example, lose her entitlement to eat *terumah* if she is a *bat kohen* (as *Rashi* and *Tosafot* note). Since the woman's status is unchanged by the act, there is no basis for arguing that the child's status is affected.

An additional proof had been raised from the verse *hi to'evah* — the Torah describes the act of remarriage as "an abomination." A *derashah* reads: *hi to'evah, v'ein baneha to'evim* — she is the abomination, but her children bear no problematic status. This verse seems to explicitly exempt the children of this prohibition from any stigma. The *gemara*, however, notes that this *derashah* can be redirected: *hi to'evah, v'ein tzaratah to'evah* — she is the abomination but not her co-wife. The verse excludes the co-wife from the taint of the prohibition, but says nothing about the children. The *phat* in the verse thus does not definitively resolve the question of the offspring's status.

### Three Cases Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא: הַמַּחֲזִיר גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ וְהַנּוֹשֵׂא חֲלוּצָתוֹ וְהַנּוֹשֵׂא קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין לוֹ בָּהּ קִדּוּשִׁין, וְאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט, וְהִיא פְּסוּלָה, וּוְלָדָהּ פָּסוּל, וְכוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לְהוֹצִיא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ קִדּוּשִׁין, וּצְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט, וְהִיא כְּשֵׁרָה וּוְלָדָהּ כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who remarries his divorcée, and one who marries his *ḥalutza*, and one who marries a relative of his *ḥalutza*, Rabbi Akiva says** that **one's betrothal of such** women **is not** effective. **And** therefore, even if he attempts to do so, it is meaningless and **she does not require a bill of divorce from him** in order to separate from him. **And** if she has relations with him **she is** rendered **unfit and her child is unfit, and we force him to send** her **away. The Rabbis say** that **one's betrothal of such** women **is** effective, **and** therefore if he betrothed her **she requires a bill of divorce from him, and** if she has relations with him **she** remains **fit and her child is fit.**

A *baraita* revisits all three cases from the *mishnah* in light of this framework. Rabbi Akiva holds that in each of these *issur lav* cases — *machzir grushaso*, *nosei chalutzaso*, and *nosei kerovas chalutzaso* — *ein lo bakiddushin*, the *kiddushin* is not binding, no *get* is required, the woman becomes invalid, and the child is *pasul*. The *chachamim* take the opposite position on every point: *kiddushin* is binding, a *get* is required if she wishes to leave, *hi kesheirah* — the woman's status is unchanged — and *vlada kasher* — the child is *kasher*.

## Context Specificity

### Akiva vs. Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן, לָאו לִכְהוּנָּה? לָא, לַקָּהָל. אִי הָכִי, הִיא כְּשֵׁרָה, לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לַקָּהָל — פְּשִׁיטָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּזַנְּיָא אִפַּסְלָה לַקָּהָל?! אֶלָּא לָאו לִכְהוּנָּה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: When the *baraita* states she and her child are rendered unfit, **to whom** are they unfit to be married? **Is it not to the priesthood? No,** it is **to the congregation** of Israel. **If so,** when the *baraita* states **she is fit, to whom** is she fit to be married? **If we say** it is **to the congregation** of Israel, **this is obvious;** could it be that **because she engaged in forbidden sexual relations that she is rendered unfit from** marrying into **the congregation** of Israel? Although by doing so she is rendered a *zona*, that can disqualify her only from marrying a priest. **Rather, is it not** that the intention is that she is fit **to** marry into **the priesthood?**

The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the Sages on these three points — the validity of *kiddushin*, the woman's status, and the child's status — is now examined carefully. When the Sages say *vlada kasher*, how comprehensive is that *kashrut*? Does it mean the child is fully valid, including for *kehunah* purposes? Or does it mean only that the child is not a *mamzer*, while still being *pagum li-kehunah*?

### Grammatical Parsing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּהִיא לִכְהוּנָּה, וְלָדָהּ נָמֵי לִכְהוּנָּה! מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? הָא כִּדְאִיתָא וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And since** in reference **to her** the word fit in the *baraita* means fit **for the priesthood,** it follows that for **the child as well,** the word fit means fit **for the priesthood.** The Gemara objects: **Are these cases comparable? This** case is **as it is, and that** case is **as it is,** and although the two cases are juxtaposed in the *baraita* they need not be relating to the same issue. Rather, when referring to the mother the *baraita* mentions fitness for the priesthood, and when referring to the child it mentions fitness for the congregation of Israel.

The *gemara* parses the language precisely. According to Rabbi Akiva, the baraita states *hi pesulah* — she is disqualified. To whom is she disqualified? It cannot mean disqualified from marrying other Jews, since no act of forbidden sex can render the woman herself a *mamzer* — that category applies only to offspring. The only meaningful disqualification the woman could acquire is disqualification from *kehunah* — either from marrying a *kohen* or from eating *terumah* as a *bat kohen*. So when Rabbi Akiva says *hi pesulah*, he means: this *issur lav* is so weighty that it retroactively makes the woman invalid for *kehunah* purposes. When he then says *vlada pasul*, the *gemara* notes that we know from elsewhere that Rabbi Akiva declares such a child a *mamzer*; therefore *pesul* here means *mamzer* status, disqualified from the congregation of Israel.

Turning to the Sages' parallel formulation — *hi kesheirah, vlada kasher* — the *gemara* applies the same logic. *Hi kesheirah* cannot mean that the woman is *kasher* to marry other Jews in the general sense, since that would never have been in doubt; forbidden sex does not create *mamzer* status in the woman herself. Therefore *hi kesheirah* must mean she remains *kesheirah li-kehunah* — the act of sex with an *issur lav* did not alter her *kehunah* eligibility. And since the woman's status remains unchanged by the act, the parallel reading of *vlada kasher* must mean the child too is *kasher li-kehunah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: הִיא פְּסוּלָה וּוְלָדָהּ פָּסוּל. הִיא פְּסוּלָה לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לַקָּהָל, מִשּׁוּם דְּזַנְּיָא אִפַּסְלָה לֵיהּ לַקָּהָל?! אֶלָּא לָאו לִכְהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **So, too, it is reasonable** to assume that the two clauses concern different issues, **as the first clause teaches** in the name of Rabbi Akiva: **She is unfit and her child is unfit.** When that first clause states: **She is unfit, to whom** is she unfit to be married? **If we say** it is **to the congregation** of Israel, could it be that **because she engaged in forbidden sexual relations she is rendered unfit to** marry into **the congregation** of Israel? **Rather, is it not** that the intention is that she is rendered unfit **to** marry into **the priesthood?**

Yet the *gemara* resists this inference. *Midi irya? Ha kide'ita, ve-ha kide'ita* — each statement exists in its own context. When the Sages say *hi kesheirah*, they address her particular vulnerability: could she have become a *chalalah*? No, she remains *kesheirah*. When they say *vlada kasher*, they address the child's particular vulnerability: could the child be a *mamzer* as Rabbi Akiva claims? No, the child is *kasher* — meaning, not a *mamzer*. It does not follow that the child is also *kasher li-kehunah*, since that question was never the focus of the Sages' statement about the child.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּוְלָדָהּ פָּסוּל, לְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לִכְהוּנָּה: הָא לַקָּהָל כָּשֵׁר? הָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לַקָּהָל.
>
> **Translation:** And then when the first clause continues and states: **And her child is unfit, to whom** is she unfit to be married? **If we say** that it is **to** the **priesthood, that** would imply that the child **is fit to** marry into **the congregation** of Israel. However, this is untenable because **didn't Rabbi Akiva** himself **say** in the mishna here that **the offspring is a *mamzer*** and is unfit from entering the congregation of Israel? **Rather, it is obvious** that the intention of the *baraita* is that the child is unfit even **to** marry into **the congregation** of Israel.

### No Almana Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַלְמָנָה קַשְׁיָא: מָה לְאַלְמָנָה שֶׁכֵּן הִיא עַצְמָהּ מִתְחַלֶּלֶת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **However,** although two of the objections were resolved, the objection to the logic of the *a fortiori* inference from the case of **a widow** remains **difficult,** as the Gemara noted earlier: **What** is true with regard **to a widow, where** her union with a High Priest is what makes **her herself disqualified** from subsequently marrying any priest and, if she is the daughter of a priest, from eating *teruma*, is not true with regard to a remarried divorcée, where her remarriage to another man is permitted and is not the cause of any further disqualification. Therefore, there is no basis for assuming that the child of one who remarries his divorcée after she had been married to another man will have flawed lineage.

The *gemara* thus concludes that the *kal v'chomer* from *almana li-kohen gadol* provides no valid proof, and the linguistic analysis of the *baraita* also provides no proof. The underlying question — whether the offspring of a simple *issur lav* is *pagum li-kehunah* — remains genuinely unresolved on the basis of these sources. The *gemara* acknowledges: *almanah kashya* — the attempted proof from *almanah* is problematic — and the entire structure built upon it collapses. In the case of *machzir grushaso*, a simple *issur lav* in which the woman's *kehunah* status is not altered by the act, there is no demonstrated basis for saying the child is *pagum li-kehunah*.

## Kares Only Pagum

### Kares Pagum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבָא עַל חַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת שֶׁהַוָּלָד פָּגוּם. מַאן הַכֹּל מוֹדִים, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אֵין מַמְזֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת, נְהִי דְּמַמְזֵר לָא הָוֵי — פָּגוּם מִיהָא הָוֵי,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** this **was stated,** it **was stated as follows: Rav Yosef said** that **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said: All agree with regard to one who engages in intercourse with** a forbidden relation **for which one is liable to** receive ***karet* that the offspring has flawed** lineage. The Gemara clarifies: **Who** is included by saying: **All agree?** It is **Rabbi Yehoshua, as although Rabbi Yehoshua said** in the mishna (49b) **that** the offspring born **from** a union **for which one is liable to** receive ***karet* is not a *mamzer*,** nevertheless, **granted** that Rabbi Yehoshua holds that **he is not a *mamzer*;** he would agree, **however,** that **he is of flawed** lineage.

The *gemara* now shifts the framework. *Amar Rav Yosef bar Shimon bar Rebbe: ha-kol modim be-ba al chayavei kerisos she-ha-vlad pagum* — everyone agrees that if a man had relations with a woman whose prohibition carries *issur kares*, the child is *pagum*. Having abandoned the claim that an *issur lav* makes a child *pagum*, the *gemara* asks instead about *issur kares*.

### Teimani Concession

Who is the "everyone" who concedes this point? The *gemara* identifies the concession as belonging to Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua holds an even narrower definition of *mamzer* than the *chachamim* — he requires not merely *kares* but *chayavei misos beis din* (capital offenses by a court, such as adultery) to produce a *mamzer*. Even he concedes that the offspring of an *issur kares* is at minimum *pagum*, even if not a full *mamzer*.

### Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר מֵאַלְמָנָה: וּמָה אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרָהּ שָׁוֶה — בַּכֹּל בְּנָהּ פָּגוּם, זוֹ שֶׁאִיסּוּרָהּ שָׁוֶה בַּכֹּל — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁבְּנָהּ פָּגוּם.
>
> **Translation:** This is derived **through an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* of **a widow,** as follows: **Just as** in the case of **a widow** who is married **to High Priest, where the prohibition** that pertains **to her is not equally applicable to all,** i.e., only a High Priest is prohibited from marrying a widow, and nevertheless **her child** from that union will **have flawed** lineage, then so too with regard to **this** woman, **where the prohibition** that pertains **to her is equally applicable to all** Jews, **isn't it logical that her child** from that union should **have flawed** lineage?

This concession is again derived via *kal v'chomer* from *almana l'kohen gadol*: if the offspring of that narrow, *kohen*-specific *lav* is *pagum*, then certainly the offspring of a universal *issur kares* should be *pagum*. This time, the *gemara* does not dismiss the argument.

### Zonah Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: מָה לְאַלְמָנָה, שֶׁכֵּן הִיא עַצְמָהּ מִתְחַלֶּלֶת — הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן שֶׁבְּעָלָהּ, עֲשָׂאָהּ זוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the logic of this *a fortiori* inference could be refuted in a manner similar to the suggestion above that **what** is true with regard **to a widow, who is herself disqualified** from marrying a priest, is not true with regard to a woman who is forbidden by a prohibition for which one is liable to *karet*, that is not correct because **here, too, once he has engaged in intercourse with her he renders her a *zona*,** and as such she is disqualified from marrying into the priesthood.

The reason the *kal v'chomer* succeeds here, where it failed for *issur lav*, is that a woman who engages in a sexually prohibited act carrying *issur kares* is rendered a *zonah* — a category explicitly disqualified from *kehunah* marriage. *Keivan she-ba'alah, asa'ah zonah* — the act of union itself confers *zonah* status upon the woman. Since the woman herself becomes disqualified, that disqualification flows to the child as well. The mechanism thus mirrors that of the *almana*-*kohen gadol* case: it is always the woman's changed status that serves as the conduit for the child's *psul*. When the woman's status does not change — as in a simple *issur lav* — the child is unaffected. When the woman becomes a *zonah* through *issur kares*, that status transmits to the offspring.

## Non-Jew Father

### Consensus Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּעֶבֶד וְגוֹי הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara considers the status of other children born from forbidden unions: **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: All agree with regard to a slave or a gentile who engaged in intercourse with a Jewish woman that the offspring** born from such a union **is a *mamzer*.**

*Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan: ha-kol modim she-eved ve-oved kochavim ha-ba al bas Yisrael she-ha-vlad mamzer* — everyone agrees that a slave or a non-Jew who has relations with a Jewish woman produces a *mamzer*. This is a striking claim, and the *gemara* immediately asks: who is the "everyone" here? The answer is *Shimon Ha-Teimani* — even he who insists that *mamzer* status requires *issur kares* concedes this case.

### Lav But No Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן ״הַכֹּל מוֹדִים״ — שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי. דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמַר שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי אֵין מַמְזֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין — הָנֵי מִילֵּי
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Who** is included by saying: **All agree?** It is **Shimon HaTimni, as although Shimon HaTimni said** that the offspring of relations for **which one is liable for** violating **a prohibition is not a *mamzer*, this applies** only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין דְּתָפְסִי בְּהוּ קִדּוּשִׁין, אֲבָל הָכָא, גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא תָּפְסִי בְּהוּ קִדּוּשִׁין — כְּחַיָּיבֵי כָּרֵיתוֹת דְּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **to** forbidden relations **for which one is liable for** violation of **a prohibition** concerning **which a betrothal** between the couple **would take effect. However, here,** with regard to **a gentile and a slave, since their betrothal** of a Jewish woman **would not take effect,** a union with them **is comparable to** forbidden relations **for which one is liable to** receive ***karet*,** and therefore the offspring of such a union will be a *mamzer*.

The concession is puzzling, because relations with a non-Jew are only an *issur lav*, not an *issur kares*. On Shimon Ha-Teimani's own framework, there should be no *mamzer*. The *gemara* resolves the apparent inconsistency: *hani mili* — the rule that *issur lav* does not produce a *mamzer* applies specifically to *lav*-prohibitions where *kiddushin* is nonetheless valid and binding. In ordinary *issur lav* cases, the man could in principle marry the woman through *kiddushin*, and if he does, a *get* is required to dissolve the marriage. It is within that framework of potential marital bindingness that we say the child is not a *mamzer*.

### Kiddushin Theory

But in the case of a non-Jew or a slave, *kiddushin* is categorically impossible — *keivan d'lo tafsi bu kiddushin, k'chayavei krisos dami* — since marriage cannot be binding, the case is treated as if it were *issur kares*. This raises a broader theoretical question: perhaps the operative criterion for *mamzer* is not *lav* versus *kares* at all, but rather whether *kiddushin* could be binding. When *kiddushin* is binding, the union carries a marital framework, and even a severe prohibition like *kares* does not produce a *mamzer* (for this we shall see the *niddah* counterexample shortly). When *kiddushin* cannot be binding, the offspring lacks any legitimate familial framework, and *mamzer* status results.

### Niddah Comparison

The *niddah* case presents itself as a powerful illustration. Relations with a *niddah* carry *issur kares*, yet the offspring of a husband and wife during a period of *niddah* is universally held not to be a *mamzer*. Why? Because *kiddushin* is fully binding in that marriage — the couple is validly married. The *kares*-level prohibition is severe, but it occurs within the context of a legally recognized marriage, and therefore the *mamzer* criterion is not triggered. This inverts the logic: it is not the *kares* per se that creates a *mamzer*, it may be the absence of bindable *kiddushin*. And if so, the non-Jew case — where *kiddushin* can never bind — would produce a *mamzer* even though it is only a *lav*. This is, the *gemara* acknowledges, a coherent and reasonable argument — though we shall see that the *halacha* does not follow it.

## Non-Jew Non-Mamzer

### RSBY Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַמְזֵר אֶלָּא מִמִּי שֶׁאִיסּוּרוֹ אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה וְעָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: In the case of **a gentile or a slave who engaged in intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring** born from such a union **is a *mamzer*. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says:** The offspring is **a *mamzer* only** if born **from** relations with **one who is forbidden by a prohibition of forbidden relations** that are **punishable by *karet*.** It is apparent from the *baraita* that one who holds, as does Shimon HaTimni, that only the offspring from forbidden relations for which one is liable to receive *karet* is a *mamzer*, nevertheless holds that the offspring of a slave or gentile and a Jewish woman is not a *mamzer*.

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *baraita*: *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda omer ein mamzer ela mi-she-issuro issur ervah ve-onush kares* — a *mamzer* results only from a union that is both a sexual prohibition (*issur ervah*) and punishable by *kares*. Intercourse with a non-Jew, being only an *issur lav*, would not produce a *mamzer* on this view. The *baraita* thus directly contradicts the claim that "everyone" agrees a non-Jew-fathered child is a *mamzer*.

### Rabbi's Possible Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאן ״הַכֹּל מוֹדִים״ — רַבִּי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּרַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵין הַדְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ אֲמוּרִים אֶלָּא לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, שֶׁהָיָה עוֹשֶׂה חֲלוּצָה כְּעֶרְוָה, וְלֵיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, בְּגוֹי וְעֶבֶד מוֹדֶה. דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ: גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said: Who** is included by saying: **All agree?** It is **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, as **although Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** in a *baraita* (52b) concerning the mishna (50a–51b) that states that a levirate betrothal between a *yavam* and a *yevama* with whom he had already performed *ḥalitza* is ineffective: **This statement was said only according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva, as he would consider a *ḥalutza* like a forbidden relative** such that if the *yavam* betrothed her it would not take effect. **And** although Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **himself does not hold accordingly** with regard to that issue, **with regard to** the offspring of a union with **a gentile or a slave he concedes** that the offspring is a *mamzer*. **As, when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, he said that **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi said in the name of our Master,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: With regard to **a gentile or a slave who engaged in intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring is a *mamzer*.**

*Rav Yosef* responds: *Ella amar Rav Yosef man d'chula modei Rabbi* — the "everyone" who concedes this point is *Rebbi*, Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi himself. Although *Rebbi* in another context says the lenient positions apply only according to Rabbi Akiva, and his own ruling aligns with the view that *mamzer* requires *issur kares* — nevertheless, in the specific case of a non-Jewish or slave father, *Rebbi* may concede that the child is a *mamzer*. The rationale is that even one who normally reserves *mamzer* status for *issur kares* might make an exception when *kiddushin* is categorically impossible, treating the non-Jew case as functionally equivalent to an *issur kares* situation in terms of its legal consequences. This remains the position being tested.

## Captive Woman Case

### Ami's Consultation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַחָא שַׂר הַבִּירָה וְרַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ פְּרוּק הָנְהוּ שְׁבוּיָיתָא דַּאֲתוֹ מֵאַרְמוֹן לִטְבֶרְיָא. הֲוָה חֲדָא דְּאִעַבַּרָא מִגּוֹי, וַאֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא דְּאָמְרִי: גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related incident: **Rabbi Aḥa, lord of the capital, and Rabbi Tanḥum, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** the **man of Akko village, redeemed those captives who came from Armon to Tiberias. One of them had been impregnated by a gentile, and they came before Rabbi Ami** to ask what the offspring's status would be when born. **He said to them** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Ḥanina** all **say:** With regard to **a gentile or a slave who engaged in intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring is a *mamzer*.**

A narrative is now introduced. *Rabbi Acha Sar Ha-Birah* and *Rabbi Tanchum* the son of *Rabbi Chiya* of *Kfar Akko* redeemed a group of captive women traveling from Armon to Tiberias. One of these captive women had become pregnant through a non-Jew. The practical question of the child's status was brought before *Rabbi Ami*.

### Three Say Mamzer

*Rabbi Ami* reported the opinions he had received: *Rabbi Yochanan, Rabbi Elazar*, and *Rabbi Chanina* all held that a non-Jewish or slave father together with a Jewish mother produces a *mamzer*. These are three of the most authoritative voices in the generation of Palestinian *amoraim*.

### Four Say Kasher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רְבוּתָא לְמִחְשַׁב גַּבְרֵי?! הָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל בְּבָבֶל, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי וּבַר קַפָּרָא בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ חַלּוֹפֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא וְעַיֹּילֵי זִקְנֵי דָרוֹם, דְּאָמְרִי: גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this, **Rav Yosef said: Is it so great to enumerate men?** The fact that several great Sages held this opinion does not prove that their opinion is the accepted *halakha*. **But** there are **Rav and Shmuel in Babylonia, and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and bar Kappara in Eretz Yisrael, and some say** to **remove bar Kappara** from this list **and insert** instead **the Elders of the South, who** all **say:** With regard to **a gentile or a slave who engaged in intercourse with a Jewish woman,** the lineage of **the offspring is unflawed,** and he or she may marry into the congregation of Israel.

*Rav Yosef* is unimpressed with the weight of three authorities and responds in kind: *ravusa le-mechashev gavrei* — is it such an achievement to count up names? He provides a counter-list of four: *Rav* and *Shmuel* in Bavel, and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* and *Bar Kappara* in Eretz Yisrael — or, in an alternative formulation, *Bar Kappara* is replaced by the *ziknei darom*, the elders of the south. All four (or this variant group of four) hold that the child of a non-Jewish father and Jewish mother is *kasher*.

### Rabbi's Mamzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי הִיא. דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי: מִשּׁוּם רַבֵּינוּ אָמְרוּ: גּוֹי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said** the *halakha* is in fact that the offspring is a *mamzer* because this **is Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's opinion, **as when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael he said that **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi said in the name of our Master,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, that **they say:** With regard to **a gentile or a slave who engaged in intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring is a *mamzer*.**

The *gemara* does not resolve the standoff by simple head-counting. Instead, *Rav Yosef* himself declares: *ella amar Rav Yosef: Rebbe hi* — it is *Rebbi*'s position that is operative. The basis is the tradition brought by *Rav Dimi* in the name of *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi* in the name of *Rabbeinu* — *Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi* — that *oved kochavim ve-eved ha-ba al bas Yisrael, ha-vlad mamzer*. So we have an explicit ruling attributed to *Rebbi* himself: the child born of a non-Jewish father and a Jewish mother is a *mamzer*.

### Kehunah Status TBD

The daf closes with this contested ruling standing unresolved in terms of final *pesak*. The *gemara* notes, and leaves for the following daf, the second question that flows from this discussion: even granting that we ultimately rule the child is *not* a *mamzer* — as the majority of *poskim* conclude — what is the child's status for *kehunah*? May a daughter born of a non-Jewish father marry a *kohen*? That question, deeply relevant in practice and tightly connected to everything that has been established in today's analysis, will be taken up on the next *amud*.
