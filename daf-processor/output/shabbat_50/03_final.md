# Shabbat 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 39 Melachot Overview

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּגִיזֵּי צֶמֶר וְאֵין מְטַלְטְלִין. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא טָמַן בָּהֶן, אֲבָל טָמַן בָּהֶן — מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: One may insulate food **in wool fleece, and he may not move it. Rava said:** This *halakha* that fleece may not be moved on Shabbat **applies only** to a case **where one did not insulate** food **in it.** Only in that case is it set-aside. **However, if one insulated** cooked food **in it, he may move it.** By insulating food in the fleece, he indicated that he intends to use it on Shabbat.

We pick up today on daf 50, beginning four lines from the bottom of 49b.

### 39 Melachot Source

The preceding *gemara* addressed a question of fundamental importance: the basis for the thirty-nine *melachot*. Two approaches emerged. The first concerns the number itself — how it is derived from the occurrences of the words *melacha*, *melacha*, and *melechet* in the Torah. The second is a striking reading from Rav Yoel Bin-Nun, who proposed that the source lies in the specific items constructed for the *Mishkan*. More radically, the *gemara* raised the possibility that the *Mishkan* does not even serve as the formal source for the thirty-nine *melachot* at all — a reading derived from the *yereiot*, the curtains — which stands in tension with everything we have traditionally been taught. That was a crucial *gemara*, and it now gives way to the practical discussions of what one may use as insulation on *Shabbat*.

### Insulation & Muktzeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ הַהוּא מֵרַבָּנָן בַּר יוֹמֵיהּ לְרָבָא: טוֹמְנִין בְּגִיזֵּי צֶמֶר וְאֵין מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן. כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? נוֹטֵל אֶת הַכִּיסּוּי, וְהֵן נוֹפְלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain Sage** for whom it was **his first day** in that study hall **raised an objection to Rava** from our mishna: **One may insulate** food **in wool fleece, and one may not move** it. **How,** then, **does he act** if he insulated food in wool fleece and now wishes to remove the pot? **He lifts the cover,** which he is permitted to move, **and** the wool fleece **falls** by itself.

The *mishnah* states: *tomnin be-gizei tzemer ve-ein metaltelin otam* — one may insulate a pot using wool shearings, but one may not move them. These shearings do not add heat; they only preserve it. Nevertheless, they are *muktzeh* on *Shabbat*, as they are the sort of item one keeps in a sewing kit and thus set aside for a purpose incompatible with *Shabbat* use. The immediate practical question this raises is: how does one retrieve the pot from within them? The *mishnah* will address that shortly. But first, the *gemara* introduces a qualification.

## Rava: Designation

### Rava's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רָבָא לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא יִחֲדָן לְהַטְמָנָה. אֲבָל יִחֲדָן לְהַטְמָנָה — מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if it was stated, it was stated as follows: Rava said:** This *halakha* that wool fleece may not be moved on Shabbat **applies only** in a case **where one did not designate it for insulating** food. **However, if he designated it for insulating** food, **one may move it,** as in that case, it is no longer set-aside.

*Amar Rava: lo shanu ella she-lo taman bahem* — Rava states that wool shearings are considered *muktzeh* only if one has not used them to insulate before *Shabbat*. But *aval taman bahem* — if one has already used them as insulation on *erev Shabbat*, then *metaltelin otam* — they are no longer *muktzeh*, because they have been put to a legitimate purpose and their identity and designation have thereby been changed. This connects to earlier discussions about pieces of cloth normally kept in a sewing kit: the *gemara* established that unless one permanently changes their use, they remain *muktzeh*. Rava here appears to take a more lenient position — but the *gemara* will immediately challenge it.

### Student Challenge

The *mishnah* goes on: *keitzad hu oseh?* — what does one do? *Notel et ha-kisuy ve-hen noflot* — one lifts the lid and the shearings fall off incidentally. The *mishnah* is working out how to retrieve the pot precisely because even after having been used as insulation, the shearings remain *muktzeh*. This directly contradicts Rava's stated position.

*Eitvei* — a challenge is raised to Rava by *ha-hu me-rabbanan bar yomei*, a student on his very first day at the *yeshiva*. The challenge comes from our own *mishnah*: *tomnin be-gizei tzemer ve-ein metaltelin otam* — you may insulate in wool shearings and you may not move them. One might have argued that this refers only to the case where one has not yet insulated with them. But the *mishnah* goes on: *keitzad hu oseh?* — what does one do? *Notel et ha-kisuy ve-hen noflot* — one lifts the lid and the shearings fall off incidentally. The *mishnah* is working out how to retrieve the pot precisely because even after having been used as insulation, the shearings remain *muktzeh*. This directly contradicts Rava's stated position.

### Rava Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי בֶּן שָׁאוּל אָמַר רַבִּי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא יִחֲדָן לְהַטְמָנָה, אֲבָל יִחֲדָן לְהַטְמָנָה — מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן. רָבִינָא אוֹמֵר: בְּשֶׁל הֶפְתֵּק שָׁנוּ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, גִּיזֵּי צֶמֶר שֶׁל הֶפְתֵּק אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן. וְאִם הִתְקִינָן בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן — מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** that **when Rabin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Ya'akov said** that **Rabbi Asi ben Shaul said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said:** This *halakha* that wool fleece may not be moved on Shabbat **applies only** in a case **where one did not designate it for insulating** food. **However, if one designated it for insulating** food, **he may move it.** **Ravina said:** In fact, Rava's statement can be understood as it was originally understood, i.e., one who insulated food in wool fleece may move it because it is considered designated for insulating food. In the mishna that indicates otherwise **they taught about** wool fleece taken from a merchant's **shelves [*heftek*].** That wool was certainly not designated for insulating food. It will be returned to those shelves to be sold. Therefore, it is set-aside for that purpose and may not be moved on Shabbat, even if it is used to insulate food. **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **wool fleece** taken from a merchant's **shelves, one may not move it** on Shabbat. **And if a homeowner prepared** the fleece **to use it, one may move it.**

*Ella im hachi itmar* — Rava's ruling must therefore be reformulated. His actual statement was: *lo shanu ella she-lo yichdam le-hatzmana* — the shearings are *muktzeh* only if one has not formally re-designated them for use as insulation. *Aval yichdam le-hatzmana* — if one explicitly set them aside, declaring that they would no longer serve as sewing materials but would from now on be used as insulation, then *metaltelin otam* — their identity has changed and they may be moved. The case in the *mishnah*, where they remain *muktzeh* despite having been used for insulation, must be referring to *gizei tzemer* that are stored in a particularly prominent manner — what Ravina calls *be-shel heftek*. The *geonim* explain *heftek* as referring to items displayed on a shelf or clearly set aside for commercial or business use, like a drawer of samples. In such a case, a single use is not sufficient to transform their identity; a formal re-designation is required. But for wool shearings that have no such fixed designation, a single use as insulation is enough to change their status.

### Tannaitic Support

*Itmar nami* — this ruling was also transmitted in a chain of tradition: when Rabin came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported that Rabbi Yaakov said in the name of Rabbi Assi ben Shaul, who said in the name of Rabbi: *lo shanu ella she-lo yichdam le-hatzmana* — it is only *muktzeh* if not designated for insulation; *aval yichdam le-hatzmana, metaltelin otam*. Tannaitic support for the same principle appears as well: *tzimri ligizei tzemer shel hefke* — wool items set aside in this fixed manner remain *muktzeh*. *Ve-im hitkinam ba'al ha-bayit le-hishtamesh bahem* — but if the owner took some action, fixing or preparing them for use, then *metaltelin otam* — they may be moved.

This brings into focus the central question that will occupy the *gemara* for much of the daf: what exactly is required to change the status of a *muktzeh* object? Three possible models have already begun to emerge implicitly. First, a single use — employing the item once for its new purpose is enough. Second, designation — either mental or verbal re-dedication to the new purpose. Third, a physical change — actually doing something to transform the object. All three are about to be examined directly.

## Three Models

### Palm Beams Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: חֲרִיּוֹת שֶׁל דֶּקֶל שֶׁגְּדָרָן לְעֵצִים, וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן לִישִׁיבָה — צָרִיךְ לְקַשֵּׁר. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַשֵּׁר. הוּא תָּנֵי לַהּ וְהוּא אָמַר לַהּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the question of what can be done to permit use of items ordinarily set-aside on Shabbat, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana taught** the following *baraita* **before Rav:** With regard to **hard branches of a palm** tree **that one cut for** fire **wood** or for construction, **and** then **he reconsidered their** designation and decided to use them **for sitting, he must tie** the branches together on Shabbat eve. This allows him to move them on Shabbat like any other household utensil. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He need not tie** them together and, nevertheless, he is permitted to move them. Rabba bar bar Ḥana **taught** the *baraita*, **and he said** about **it** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

*Tana Rava bar bar Chana kamei de-Rav* — Rava bar bar Chana taught the following before Rav. If one has *chariyot shel dekel* — hard, leaf-stripped rods cut from a palm tree, something like a *lulav* that has hardened — and they were originally cut to serve as firewood or for construction purposes, but then one changed one's mind (*venimlach aleihen li-yeshiva*) and decided to use them as a kind of improvised floor covering or mat to sit on — what must one do before *Shabbat* to render them non-*muktzeh*? *Tzarich le-kasher* — one must physically weave or knot them together into a mat. It is not enough to think "I intend to sit on these on *Shabbat*." A physical act is required to transform their identity.

### R. Shimon b. Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר רַב אָמַר: קוֹשֵׁר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: חוֹשֵׁב, וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: יוֹשֵׁב, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא קִישֵּׁר וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא חִישֵּׁב.
>
> **Translation:** On that same topic, **it was stated** that **Rav said: He ties** the branches together on Shabbat eve. **And Shmuel said:** If he merely **has in mind** on Shabbat eve that he wishes to sit on them on Shabbat, he need not tie them together. **And Rav Asi said:** If he even briefly **sits** on them on Shabbat eve, sitting on the branches is permitted the next day, **even though he did not tie** them together **and even though he did not have** that **in mind.**

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer: ein tzarich le-kasher* — one need not tie or weave them. Presumably, it suffices to have them in mind and designate them mentally for the new use. This must all be done before *bein ha-shmashot*, so that as *Shabbat* enters the objects already carry their new identity. *Hu tani lah ve-hu amar lah* — Rava bar bar Chana taught this *beraita* and then himself ruled: *halacha ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the ruling follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and no physical weaving is required; mental designation suffices. We thus have two positions: physical transformation versus mental designation.

### Amoraim Positions

The *gemara* confirms: *af al pi shelo kishar, ve-af al pi shelo chishev* — even without knotting and even without mental designation for the following day. Rashi notes that *chishev* in Shmuel's formulation specifically means planning what to do with the object *le-machar* — for tomorrow.

The *gemara* notes that Rav corresponds to the first *tanna* in the *beraita* and Shmuel corresponds to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The question then becomes: who is the *tanna* that supports Rav Assi's position, that a single use suffices even without planning? *Hu d'amar ke-hai tanna* — he holds like a different *tanna* altogether.

### Bandages Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּ דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא דְּתַנְיָא: יוֹצְאִין בְּפָקוֹרִין וּבְצִיפָא, בִּזְמַן שֶׁצְּבָעָן בְּשֶׁמֶן וּכְרָכָן בִּמְשִׁיחָה. לֹא צְבָעָן בְּשֶׁמֶן וְלֹא כְּרָכָן בִּמְשִׁיחָה — אֵין יוֹצְאִין בָּהֶם. וְאִם יָצָא בָּהֶם שָׁעָה אַחַת מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא צָבַע וְלֹא כְּרָכָן בִּמְשִׁיחָה מוּתָּר לָצֵאת בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rav Asi **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **One may go out** into a public domain on Shabbat **with combed flax [*pakorin*] or combed wool** covering a wound, **when he** previously **dipped them in oil and tied them** to the wound **with twine.** If **he did not dip them in oil** or **tie them with twine, he may not go out** into the public domain **with them. And if he went out with them for a brief period** on Shabbat eve **while it was still day, even if he did not dip** them in oil or **tie them with twine, he is permitted to go out with them** on Shabbat. Apparently, there is a *tanna* who maintains that using an item before Shabbat enables one to use it on Shabbat as well. No additional steps are necessary.

*De-tanya: yotzin be-pakkurin u-ve-tzipa* — one may go out on *Shabbat* wearing *pakkurin* and *tzipa*. Two interpretations are offered for what these objects are. Rashi understands them as types of bandages placed on wounds. This immediately raises a question relevant to the upcoming chapters on carrying: even wearing something on one's body is not automatically permissible in a domain without an *eruv*. To be permitted, an item must function as a garment or as jewelry. A bandage that merely holds a poultice against a wound — holding mashed herbs mixed with oil or vinegar in place — is not functioning as clothing. But if the bandage is present to protect the wound from friction against an outer garment, or to shield it from the elements, it does perform a clothing-like function and would be permissible. According to Rashi, then, the *gemara* here is using the clothing/carrying question as an analogy for the *muktzeh* question: what transforms the identity of an object?

Tosafot, citing Rabbeinu Chananel (with Steinsaltz noting that he in turn may have relied on Rav Hai Gaon), offers an entirely different explanation: *kippa she-yesh ba sei'ar, ve-notno be-rosh ha-kereiach* — it is a type of skullcap with hair sewn into it, a toupee or wig, placed on the head of a bald person. This is distinct from the *pea nochrit*, the woman's wig discussed elsewhere in the *gemara*. A brief tangent is worth noting here: the question of whether a wig constitutes *beged isha* depends heavily on social norms. The *gemara* elsewhere considered even a man looking into a mirror to be a *beged isha* concern because at that time only women did so. For Tosafot, however, a wig worn by a bald man occupies a different category — it is not an affectation but a practical necessity, and *beged isha* determinations are inherently contextual and era-dependent.

Returning to the *beraita*: *yotzin be-pakkurin u-ve-tzipa bizman she-tzva'an be-shemen u-kshar'an be-meshicha* — one may go out with these items when they have been treated with oil and bound with a cord. According to Rashi, this shows that the item is not merely there to hold a medical preparation in place; it provides some form of protection for the body and thereby functions as clothing. According to Tosafot, treating and binding them makes it function more fully as a wig. *Lo tzava'an be-shemen ve-lo karchan be-meshicha* — if these treatments were not done, *ein yotzin bahem* — one may not go out wearing them. *Ve-im yatza bahem sha'ah achat mi-be'od yom* — but if one actually wore them for even a single hour on the day before, *mutar latzet bahem* — one may then go out wearing them on *Shabbat*.

The analogy to *muktzeh* is explicit: just as wearing something once, even without performing the full preparatory treatment, is sufficient to transform its identity for the purposes of carrying — so too a single use is sufficient to change the *muktzeh* status of an object. This *tanna*, then, is the source for Rav Assi's position.

The *gemara* thus presents three distinct positions: tying (physical transformation), mental designation, and single use. The single use appears to be the most lenient — and from the various sources the *gemara* has marshaled, it seems the *gemara* is inclining toward this view, though Tosafot raises the question of exactly which opinion constitutes the final *halacha*.

One further methodological point is worth noting. The earlier case of the insulation raised the question of whether the wool shearings, when they were used to insulate a pot on *erev Shabbat*, would thereby lose their *muktzeh* status. There we saw that use did not always suffice — particularly for items designated *be-shel heftek*. Yet in the general framework now being laid out, use once seems to suffice. The resolution is that items with a strong, fixed designation for some other clearly demarcated purpose require more to break that designation. For items without such a fixed designation, even a single use is enough.

A side observation on the question of *dvarim she-be-lev*: one might ask whether mental designation can have any legal force, given the principle *dvarim she-be-lev einam dvarim* — what is in one's heart is not legally operative. The answer is that this principle applies almost exclusively to civil and interpersonal matters, where one party cannot be bound by what is in another's mind. In the domain of one's obligations to God — and *muktzeh* is a *din* between the person and *Shabbat* — *machshava* plays a significant and well-established role. The distinction is fundamental.

## Action vs Thought

### Straw Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: הַקַּשׁ שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּטָּה לֹא יְנַעְנְעֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ, אֲבָל מְנַעַנְעוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ. אֲבָל אִם הָיָה עָלָיו מַאֲכַל בְּהֵמָה, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה עָלָיו כַּר אוֹ סָדִין מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — מְנַעַנְעוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. שְׁמַע מִינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We too have also learned** in a mishna: **Straw that is** piled **on a bed** to be used for fuel or mixed with clay is set aside for that purpose and may not be moved. Therefore, one who seeks to lie on the bed **may not move** the straw **with his hand, but he may move it with his body,** as this is not the typical way of moving straw. **However, if that** straw had been designated as **animal feed, or if there was a pillow or sheet** spread **over it** on Shabbat eve **while it was still day** and he lay on it before Shabbat, **he may move it with his hand.** Apparently, even brief use before Shabbat suffices to permit use on Shabbat as well. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from it** that there is a tannaitic opinion in accordance with which Rav Asi stated his opinion.

*Amar Rav Ashi anan nami tanina* — Rav Ashi observes that we have learned something similar in a *beraita*. *Ha-taven she-al gabei ha-mita* — if one has straw on a bed (presumably serving as a kind of mattress), it is *muktzeh* on *Shabbat*. *Lo yenaneney be-yado* — one may not move it with one's hand. *Aval menaneha be-gufo* — one may lie on it and shift it with one's body, because this constitutes *tiltul be-gufo* — indirect moving with one's body — which is permissible even with *muktzeh* objects.

However: *aval im haya ma'achal behema* — if the straw was designated as animal fodder, then since one may feed one's animals on *Shabbat*, animal fodder is not *muktzeh*. *O she-haya alav kar o sadin mi-be'od yom* — or if one had placed a pillow and blanket on the straw from the day before, it is no longer *muktzeh* and may be moved with one's hand. Placing a pillow and blanket on top of the straw before *Shabbat* clearly demonstrates that one has designated this straw as one's mattress — it was not merely incidentally present on the bed. In that case *menan'o be-yado* — one may move it freely. *Shma mina* — this confirms that a preparatory act done before *Shabbat*, even without explicitly designating the object for the following day, is enough to change its status.

The *chiddush* here is precisely this: one might have thought that placing a pillow and blanket on the straw is a lower-level act than formally designating the straw for *Shabbat* use. But the *gemara* holds it is sufficient. There was no declaration of "I am re-dedicating this straw as my mattress for *Shabbat*." Rather, the act of making a bed out of the straw on *erev Shabbat* was itself enough to transform its identity.

### Feasibility Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תְּנָא דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל — רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן עֲקִיבָא. דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר זְעֵירִי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַךְ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן עֲקִיבָא לְמָקוֹם אֶחָד, וּמָצָא חֲרִיּוֹת שֶׁל דֶּקֶל שֶׁגְּדָרוּם לְשׁוּם עֵצִים, וְאָמַר לָהֶם לְתַלְמִידָיו: צְאוּ וְחַשְּׁבוּ כְּדֵי שֶׁנֵּשֵׁב עֲלֵיהֶן לְמָחָר. וְלָא יָדַעְנָא אִי בֵּית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה הֲוָה, אִי בֵּית הָאֵבֶל הֲוָה. מִדְּקָאָמַר אִי בֵּית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה הֲוָה אִי בֵּית הָאֵבֶל הֲוָה — דַּוְקָא בֵּית הָאֵבֶל אוֹ בֵּית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה דִּטְרִידִי, אֲבָל הָכָא קָשַׁר — אִין, לֹא קָשַׁר — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And who is the** unnamed ***tanna* who disagrees with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** in the *baraita* cited above? He holds that in order to use palm branches for sitting, one must perform an action, e.g., tie them together, before Shabbat? The Gemara answers: It is **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Akiva, as when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Ze'iri said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Rabbi Ḥanina ben Akiva once went to a certain place** on Shabbat eve **and found** there **hard branches of a palm** tree **that they had cut for** fire **wood. And he said to his disciples: Go out and have in mind** that you will use them **so that we** will be permitted to **sit on them tomorrow,** on Shabbat. **And,** Ze'iri added, **I do not know if** the house where Rabbi Ḥanina ben Akiva went **was the house of** a wedding **feast or if it was the house of mourning.** **From** the fact **that** Ze'iri **said:** I do not know **whether it was the house of** a wedding **feast or the house of mourning,** it may be inferred that this *halakha* applies **specifically** to **the house of mourning or the house of a feast because** they **are preoccupied** with other matters and do not have time to tie the wood. **However, here,** in ordinary circumstances, if **he tied** the branches together, **yes,** it is permitted to sit on them on Shabbat; if **he did not tie** them together, **no,** it is not permitted.

*U-man tanna de-falig alei de-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — who is the *tanna* that disagrees with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and demands a physical act? Rav is the *amora* who holds this position; the underlying *tanna* is Rabbi Chanina ben Akiva.

*De-ki ata Rav Dimi amar Zeiri amar Rabbi Chanina:* when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported that Rabbi Chanina ben Akiva had once gone to a certain place and found *chariyot shel dekel* — exactly the palm-rod case — that had been cut to serve as wood. It was *erev Shabbat*, and he said to his students: *tze'u ve-chishvu ke-dei she-neshev aleihem le-machar* — go and think about them, designate them mentally, so that we may sit on them tomorrow. This seems at first to be the more lenient position — mental designation suffices. But the postscript is what tells the full story: *ve-lo yadna* — and it was reported that this was only because it was a special circumstance, either a wedding celebration or a funeral (*bei mishteh* or *bei avel*), when everyone was so distracted and pressed for time (*de-treidi*) that weaving the rods together was not feasible.

This is a revealing moment. The question is not one of strict versus lenient standards in the abstract. The underlying principle is: physical action is required when it is the meaningful and expected way to transform an object's identity. When a mat can be woven, weaving it is what demonstrates genuine re-designation. Merely thinking about using the sticks as a mat, when one could have woven them, is an insufficient expression of intent. But in extraordinary circumstances — a funeral, a wedding — when it was simply not possible to do the weaving, the mental designation was accepted. The logic is relative: what falls short is not thinking per se, but thinking when a more complete act was available and not performed.

*Davka bei avel u-vei mishteh de-treidi* — it is only because of the distraction of the special occasion that designation sufficed. *Aval hacha: kesher — in, lo kesher — lo* — in ordinary circumstances, only physical tying transforms the identity; without it, the rods remain *muktzeh*.

### Dirt Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מַכְנִיס אָדָם מְלֹא קוּפָּתוֹ עָפָר, וְעוֹשֶׂה בָּהּ כׇּל צָרְכּוֹ. דָּרֵשׁ מָר זוּטְרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּמָר זוּטְרָא רַבָּה: וְהוּא שֶׁיִּחֵד לוֹ קֶרֶן זָוִית. אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: כְּמַאן — כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. דְּאִי כְּרַבָּנַן — הָאָמְרִי בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן דְּבָעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה אֶלָּא מִידֵּי דְּבַר מֶיעְבַּד בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה, אֲבָל מִידֵּי דְּלָא בַּר מֶיעְבַּד בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: A person may bring a basket full of earth into** his house on Shabbat eve, pour it on the floor, **and use it for all his needs** on Shabbat, e.g., to cover excrement. **Mar Zutra taught in the name of Mar Zutra Rabba: That** applies **only if he designated a** specific **corner** in his house for the earth. **The Sages said before Rav Pappa: In accordance with whose** opinion was this last ruling taught, that designating a place for the earth is sufficient to permit its use on Shabbat? It must have been taught **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** with respect to palm branches, **as if it was** taught **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, didn't they say** that in order to permit use of an object that is set-aside on Shabbat, **we require an action,** e.g., tying the palm branches together? Thought alone is insufficient. **Rav Pappa said to them: Even if you say that** the *halakha* was taught in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, the Rabbis stated** their opinion **that we require an action, only with regard to something with which it is possible to perform a** preparatory **action. However,** with regard to **something with which it is not possible to perform a** preparatory **action, no,** they did not require an action. Since it is not possible to perform a preparatory action with the earth, one is permitted to use the earth by means of thought alone.

*Amar Rav Yehuda: machnis adam me-lo kupato afar* — a person may bring in a full box of dirt on *erev Shabbat*, *ve-oseh bo kol tzarcho* — and use it for all one's needs throughout *Shabbat*. Dirt that would ordinarily be *muktzeh* becomes usable because one has brought it inside with clear intent to use it — for covering something on an earthen floor, for hygienic purposes, and the like.

*Darash Mar Zutra* transmitted in the name of Mar Zutra the Elder: *ve-hu she-yichad lo keren zavit* — this applies only if one designated a specific corner of the room for it. According to Rashi, the dirt is apparently dumped out on the floor; without designating a specific space, the dirt blends into the floor itself. The designation of a corner preserves its identity as usable dirt, distinct from the general flooring.

*Amru rabanan kumei de-Rav Pappa* — the rabbinic students asked Rav Pappa: is this ruling consistent only with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that mental designation suffices? *De-ei ke-rabanan, ha amri beinan ma'aseh* — if we follow the rabbis who require a physical act, how can mere mental designation of a corner suffice?

Rav Pappa replied: *afilu teima rabanan* — even according to the rabbis who require a physical act, this case would be permitted. *Ad kan lo ka'amri rabanan de-veinan ma'aseh* — the rabbis only require a physical act *ela midi de-var avida be-ma'aseh* — in cases where a physical act is a natural and meaningful option, and therefore anything less than that is an insufficient demonstration of intent. *Aval midi de-lav bar avida be-ma'aseh* — but for something like a box of dirt, where no physical act of transformation is applicable, *lo* — no act is required. The principle is explicitly articulated: physical transformation is required only when such transformation is both meaningful and feasible; when it is not, designation alone suffices.

### Sand Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: בַּכֹּל חֹפִין אֶת הַכֵּלִים חוּץ מִכְּלֵי כֶסֶף בִּגְרַתְקוֹן. הָא נֶתֶר וָחוֹל מוּתָּר. וְהָתַנְיָא: נֶתֶר וָחוֹל — אָסוּר. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה, וּמָר סָבַר לָא בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה. לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין — אָסוּר. הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us say that** this issue, whether or not an action is required in that case, is **parallel** to a dispute among **the *tanna'im*.** As it was taught in one *baraita*: **One may clean utensils** on Shabbat **with any** type of cleaning agent, **except for silver utensils with cream of tartar [*gartekon*],** as that not only polishes the silver, but also smooths it. By inference: Cleaning with **natron and sand is permitted.** **Wasn't it taught** in the *Tosefta*: Cleaning with **natron and sand is prohibited** on Shabbat? **What, is it not that they disagree with regard to this** following point? **That** one **Sage,** who prohibits use of sand on Shabbat, **holds** that **an action is required** in order to permit the use of items that would otherwise be set-aside on Shabbat. Since it is impossible to perform an action with sand, its use is prohibited. **And** the other **Sage,** who permits use of sand, **holds** that **an action is not required.** The Gemara rejects this argument: **No, everyone agrees** that **an action is not required; and,** nevertheless, it is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which prohibits use of sand and natron, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda; that** *baraita*, which permits their use, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** The Gemara elaborates: **This** *baraita*, which prohibits use of sand and natron, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said** with regard to the laws of Shabbat in general that **an unintentional act is prohibited.** It is prohibited to perform an otherwise permitted action from which an unintended prohibited labor ensues. Therefore, cleaning a silver utensil with sand or natron is prohibited because he thereby unintentionally smooths the utensil, which is prohibited on Shabbat. **That** *baraita*, which permits the use of sand and natron, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that **an unintentional act is permitted.**

*Nema ke-tanna'ei* — the *gemara* proposes that this very principle may underlie a *tannaitic* dispute. The *mishnah* permits cleaning vessels with virtually any material, *chutz mi-kli kesef be-gartikon* — except silver vessels with *gartikon*, an abrasive substance that not only removes dirt but actually polishes and smooths the metal surface. Rashi explains that this smoothing constitutes the *melacha* of *memarek* — burnishing — which is derived from the *Mishkan* and is an *av melacha*. Therefore the *gartikon* is universally forbidden.

The *gemara* cites a *beraita* that permits using *netar* (natron, a washing soda) and sand to clean vessels, while another *beraita* prohibits them. One might think these two *beraitot* disagree about whether sand and natron count as *muktzeh* — whether, as objects that have no prior designated use for *Shabbat*, they require some kind of physical preparation to be usable. But the *gemara* rejects this: *dechulei alma lo beinan ma'aseh* — everyone agrees that where physical transformation is meaningless (as with dirt, and similarly with sand), no such act is needed. *Ve-lo kashya* — the apparent contradiction is easily resolved. The dispute between the two *beraitot* is not about *muktzeh* at all; it is the well-known disagreement between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon regarding *davar she-eino mitkavein* — an action performed without intent to bring about a prohibited consequence.

## Cleansers & Hair

### R. Yehuda vs Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לְהָא דְּשָׁרֵי — כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֲבָל לֹא יָחוֹף בָּהֶם שְׂעָרוֹ, וְאִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — מִשְׁרָא קָשָׁרֵי. דִּתְנַן: נָזִיר חוֹפֵף וּמְפַסְפֵּס, אֲבָל לֹא סוֹרֵק! אֶלָּא הָא וְהָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וּתְרֵי תַנָּאֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **In what** manner **did you establish that** *baraita*, which **permits** the use of sand and natron? You established it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** If so, **say the latter clause** of that same *baraita*: **However, one may not wash his hair with them** on Shabbat. **And, if** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, he permits** doing so. **As we learned** in a mishna: **A nazirite,** for whom it is prohibited to cut his hair, **may wash** his hair with sand and natron **and separate** it with his fingers; **however, he may not comb** it, as combing will certainly cause hair to fall out. Apparently, Rabbi Shimon permits washing hair even in a case where it is prohibited to cause hair to fall out; in his opinion, the fact that washing one's hair might inadvertently cause that to happen is not a source of concern. **Rather,** both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita,* which disagree with regard to cleaning silver utensils with sand and natron, are in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that an unintentional act is prohibited. **And** there are **two *tanna'im* in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gartikon* is forbidden by all because it will certainly smooth the silver. But with sand and natron, the smoothing is not inevitable — it might happen, or it might not. This is precisely the terrain of the *davar she-eino mitkavein* debate. Rabbi Yehuda holds *davar she-eino mitkavein assur* — one may not perform an action that may incidentally produce a prohibited result. Rabbi Shimon holds *davar she-eino mitkavein muttar* — since the prohibited consequence is not certain, and one does not intend it, the action is permitted.

The *beraita* that permits sand must therefore follow Rabbi Shimon. But the *gemara* points to a problem with this reading: *eima seifa* — look at the end of that same *beraita*, which rules *aval lo yachof bahem sa'aro* — one may not use these same cleansers to wash one's hair on *Shabbat*. The concern is that it will cause hair to fall out. If the *beraita* follows Rabbi Shimon, who permits *davar she-eino mitkavein*, why does it prohibit washing one's hair? The *gemara* answers: this is because the hair-falling concern is not analogous to the silver-smoothing concern in terms of probability. To establish this, the *gemara* invokes a case of a *nazir*, whose hair is under a strict prohibition: *Nazir chofef u-mefasfes, aval lo sorek* — a *nazir* may scrub and run fingers through his hair but may not comb with a fine comb, because combing will certainly pull out hairs. This demonstrates that scrubbing with these cleansers is not considered certain to cause hair loss. If that is so, then on *Shabbat* the concern about hair falling out is merely a possibility — which means it cannot be Rabbi Shimon who prohibits it, since Rabbi Shimon permits *davar she-eino mitkavein*.

Therefore: *ela ha ve-ha Rabbi Yehuda* — both the permissive and restrictive *beraitot* follow Rabbi Yehuda. *Ve-trei tanna'ei aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda* — they represent two different *tannaitic* traditions within Rabbi Yehuda's view. One *tanna* in the name of Rabbi Yehuda holds that sand is capable of smoothing silver — *garir* — and therefore prohibits it. The other holds it cannot smooth silver — *lo garir* — and therefore permits it. Both hold like Rabbi Yehuda that even a possible unintended consequence is forbidden; they differ only on the empirical question of whether sand actually has the capacity to polish silver.

This resolves the apparent contradiction cleanly. Both *beraitot* follow Rabbi Yehuda. The permissive *beraita* permits sand on silver because that *tanna* holds sand cannot smooth silver — so there is no possible prohibited consequence to worry about. Yet the same *beraita* forbids using sand on hair because hair loss is a real possibility. The restrictive *beraita* prohibits sand on silver because that *tanna* holds it can smooth the metal — a possibility that Rabbi Yehuda prohibits.

### Olive Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי תַּנָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר גָּרֵיר, וְהַאי תַּנָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לָא גָּרֵיר. בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא — כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֲבָל פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו מוּתָּר. הָא מְעַבַּר שֵׂיעָר! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּקָטָן, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בָּאִשָּׁה, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּסָרִיס.
>
> **Translation:** **This *tanna*, in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, holds** that sand and natron **scrape** and smooth the utensils. Therefore, their use on Shabbat is prohibited. **And that *tanna*,** also **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, holds** that sand and natron **do not scrape** and smooth the utensils. Therefore, their use on Shabbat is permitted. **How did you establish** that *baraita*? It was **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** If so, **say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **But his face, his hands, and his feet,** it is **permitted** to wash with sand and natron. **Doesn't he** thereby **cause hair to fall out?** It should be prohibited according to Rabbi Yehuda. **The Gemara answers: If you wish, say** that the permission to wash one's face with sand and natron refers to **a child; and if you wish, say** instead that it refers to **a woman; and if you wish, say** instead that it refers to **a eunuch.** All of them have no facial hair, and that is why there is no concern that use of sand and natron to clean their faces will cause hair to fall out.

*Bei minei Rav Sheshet: mahu lifzo'ach zeitim be-Shabbat* — the question is raised to Rav Sheshet: may one crush olives on a hard surface on *Shabbat*? Rashi understands the purpose as making the olives more palatable, but notes that oil will be wasted in the process. Tosafot argues that the purpose is to use the crushed olive as a hand cleanser, which explains why this question appears in the middle of a discussion about cleansers. In either case, oil is lost and the question of *hefsed ochlin* — wasting food — is implicated.

Rav Sheshet responds: *ve-chi be-chol mi hutra* — who said it is even permitted during the weekday? The questioner was asking about *Shabbat*, but Rav Sheshet challenges the premise: even without the *Shabbat* context, one should not be doing this, because *mishum hefsed ochlin* — the waste of oil is objectionable. The *gemara* briefly considers whether this conflicts with Shmuel's rule that *oseh adam kol tzarcho be-pat* — one may use bread for any purpose without concern for wasting food. The distinction offered is that bread used for other purposes does not become repulsive and remains edible; the olive oil lost in the crushing process, however, is ruined — *meisi* — and the two cases are not comparable.

### Mar Zutra's Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: עֲפַר לְבֵינְתָּא שְׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כּוּסְפָּא דְיַסְמִין שְׁרֵי. אָמַר רָבָא: עֲפַר פִּלְפְּלֵי שְׁרֵי. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בַּרְדָּא שְׁרֵי. מַאי בַּרְדָּא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תִּילְּתָא אַהֲלָא וְתִילְתָּא אָסָא וְתִילְתָּא סִיגְלֵי. אָמַר רַב נְחֶמְיָה בַּר יוֹסֵף: כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא רוּבָּא אַהֲלָא — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. אַמֵּימָר וּמָר זוּטְרָא וְרַב אָשֵׁי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי, אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ בַּרְדָּא. אַמֵּימָר וְרַב אָשֵׁי מְשׁוֹ, מָר זוּטְרָא לָא מְשָׁא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בַּרְדָּא שְׁרֵי?! אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי: בַּר מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּחוֹל נָמֵי לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. סָבַר לַהּ כִּי הָא דְּתַנְיָא: מְגָרֵר אָדָם גִּלְדֵי צוֹאָה וְגִלְדֵי מַכָּה שֶׁעַל בְּשָׂרוֹ בִּשְׁבִיל צַעֲרוֹ, אִם בִּשְׁבִיל לְיַפּוֹת — אָסוּר. וְאִינְהוּ כְּמַאן סַבְרוּהָ? — כִּי הָא דְּתַנְיָא: רוֹחֵץ אָדָם פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו בְּכׇל יוֹם בִּשְׁבִיל קוֹנוֹ, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּל פָּעַל ה׳ לַמַּעֲנֵהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Rav Yehuda said:** Washing one's face with **powdered frankincense** (Rav Hai Gaon) **is permitted** on Shabbat, even if he has a beard, as it does not cause hair to fall out. **Rav Yosef said:** Washing with the **solid residue of jasmine** from which its fragrant oil was squeezed **is permitted. Rava said:** Washing with **ground pepper is permitted. Rav Sheshet said:** Washing with ***berada* is permitted** on Shabbat. **What is *berada*? Rav Yosef said:** It is a mixture of **one-third aloe, one-third myrtle,** and **one-third violets. Rav Neḥemya bar Yosef said: Everywhere that there is** a mixture with **no majority of aloe,** it may **well** be used. Even if the mixture contains more than a third aloe, as long as it constitutes less than a majority, it does not cause hair to fall out. **Ameimar, Mar Zutra, and Rav Ashi were sitting** on Shabbat, **and they brought *berada* before them** for washing. **Ameimar and Rav Ashi washed** with it; **Mar Zutra did not wash. They said to him: Doesn't the Master hold in accordance with that which Rav Sheshet said:** Washing with ***berada* is permitted** on Shabbat? **Rav Mordekhai,** who was also there, **said to them: Except for him, the Master;** i.e., do not draw conclusions from Mar Zutra, **as he does not hold** that one is permitted to use *berada*, **even on a weekday.** Mar Zutra **holds in accordance with that which was taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may scrape off** dried **excrement crusts and scabs of a wound that are on his flesh because of** the **pain** that they are causing **him.** However, **if** he does so in order **to** clean and **beautify himself, it is prohibited.** According to the *tanna* of this *baraita*, it is prohibited to adorn or beautify oneself, as the verse: "Neither shall a man put on a woman's garment" (Deuteronomy 22:5) prohibits dressing or conducting oneself in the manner of women. **And** Ameimar and Rav Ashi, who permit use of *berada*, **in accordance with whose** opinion **do they hold?** They hold **in accordance with that which was taught** in a *baraita*: **A person must wash his face, his hands, and his feet every day for** the sake of **his Maker, as it is stated: "The Lord has made everything for His own purpose"** (Proverbs 16:4). Every beautiful thing that exists in the world sings the praise of God Who created beautiful things. Therefore, it is appropriate for one to beautify himself in praise of God.

*Amemar u-Mar Zutra ve-Rav Ashi hava yatvi* — Amemar, Mar Zutra, and Rav Ashi were sitting together when the *barda* cleanser was brought before them. Amemar and Rav Ashi used it to wash. Mar Zutra did not. When they asked him whether he did not accept Rav Sheshet's ruling that *barda shari* — the *barda* formula is permitted, since it poses no real risk of causing hair loss — Rav Mordechai answered on his behalf: *bar minei de-Mar* — set aside what he holds on the *Shabbat* question entirely, because *de-afilu be-chol nami lo sevira lei* — even during the weekday he does not use cleansers.

The reasoning is rooted in a *beraita*: *magar adam glidei tzoa ve-glidei maka she-al besaro bishvil tza'aro* — one may scrape excrement or wound-scabs from one's skin if they cause discomfort. *Aval im bishvil le-yafo — assur* — but scraping one's skin to improve one's appearance is forbidden. This is because beautifying the skin by abrasion was understood to fall under the concern of *beged isha* — behavior categorized as uniquely feminine. Mar Zutra adopted the most stringent position: any use of cleansers whatsoever, even during the week, risks crossing into this territory, and he therefore avoided them entirely.

The other two *amoraim*, Amemar and Rav Ashi, disagreed with this stringency. Their position rests on a different *beraita*: *rocheitz adam panav yadav ve-raglav be-chol yom bishvil kono* — a person should wash his face, hands, and feet every day for the honor of his Creator. Human beings were created *be-tzelem Elokim*, and maintaining cleanliness is therefore an expression of respect for God. *Kol po'al Hashem le-ma'anehu* — everything God made is for His glory, and that includes the human form. On this reading, personal hygiene is not merely permitted but affirmatively valued. The concern about *beged isha* does not extend to basic cleanliness.

Against this background, the *gemara* records a series of opinions about which substances may be used to wash hair on *Shabbat* — all of this within the framework of Rabbi Yehuda's position, where the operative question is whether a given cleanser poses any real risk of causing hair loss. *Afar levinta shari* — powdered brick-dust or, according to the *geonim*, powdered frankincense, is permitted, as it is not sufficiently abrasive to pose a risk. *Kuspa de-yasmon shari* — the residue or powder from jasmine (or according to Rashi, sunflower), a fine and non-abrasive substance, is also permitted. *Afar pilpeli* — ground pepper-dust is similarly permissible. *Barda shari* — the *barda* compound is permitted.

*Mai barda? Amar Rav Yosef: tilta ahala, ve-tilta asa, ve-tilta sigli* — it is one third of *ahala* (a plant identified by Rashi as violet-like, possibly a crystalline or aromatic substance), one third *hadas* (myrtle), and one third *sigli* (violet). Rashi specifies that *sigli* is the plant called *viol*, which has three leaves. The myrtle and violet are understood to be non-abrasive; only the *ahala* component poses any concern. Since it constitutes only a third of the mixture, the formula is permitted.

*Amar Rav Nechemya bar Yosef: kol heicha de-leika ruba ahala, shapir dami* — even more leniently, as long as *ahala* does not constitute a majority of the mixture, it is fine. This extends the range of permissible formulas beyond the specific one-third proportion.

The *gemara* notes the connection between this discussion and the earlier one: the entire digression on cleansers was triggered by the question of whether sand — one of the candidate cleaning substances — is *muktzeh* or not, and then opened into the broader question of whether such cleansers might cause unintended violations of *Shabbat*.

## Pot Removal

### Mishnah Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: קוּפָּה, מַטָּהּ עַל צִדָּהּ וְנוֹטֵל, שֶׁמָּא יִטּוֹל וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם נִתְקַלְקְלָה הַגּוּמָּא — שֶׁאָסוּר לְהַחֲזִיר. תְּנַן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטֵל וּמַחֲזִיר. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא נִתְקַלְקְלָה הַגּוּמָּא — שַׁפִּיר קָא אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן. אֶלָּא לָאו, אַף עַל פִּי דְּנִתְקַלְקְלָה הַגּוּמָּא. לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא נִתְקַלְקְלָה, וְהָכָא בְּחוֹשְׁשִׁין קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says:** If he placed the pot in **a basket** filled with fleece, **he leans** the basket **on its side** so that the fleece will fall to the side of the pot, **and takes** the pot. Otherwise, there is room for concern **lest** the wool collapse when **he lifts** the pot from the basket. Then he will be unable to replace the pot. It is prohibited to move the fleece to make room for the pot, since the fleece is set-aside. However, the Rabbis disagree and say: He may lift the pot and afterward replace it. **Rabbi Abba said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: Everyone agrees,** even the Rabbis, **that if the cavity** in which the pot had been placed **was destroyed,** its walls having collapsed inward, **it is prohibited to return** the pot to the basket. The Gemara asks, based on what **we learned** in the mishna. **And the Rabbis say: He may lift** the pot **and** afterward **replace it.** The Gemara elaborates: **What are the circumstances? If the cavity** in which the pot had been placed **was not destroyed, the Rabbis say fittingly** that it is permitted to replace the pot; why would Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya prohibit the practice? **Rather, is it not** that the Rabbis permit returning the pot **even though the cavity was destroyed?** Apparently, that is the subject of the dispute in the mishna. The Gemara rejects this: **No, actually,** everyone agrees that if the cavity was destroyed, it is prohibited to return the pot to the basket. The mishna is dealing with a case **where** the cavity **was not destroyed, and here** the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to** whether or not **one** need be **concerned** lest, if one is allowed to remove the pot from the basket without tilting it to the side, the cavity be destroyed and he will come to return the pot to the basket anyway.

We return now to the practical problem that began the discussion: the pot insulated in *muktzeh* shearings — how does one retrieve it on *Shabbat*? The *mishnah* records a dispute. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: *kuppa matta al tzidah* — one may tip the entire box on its side and take out the pot. However, one may not simply remove the pot and return it, because tipping and removing the shearings entirely sidesteps the problem, while returning the pot after removal would require dealing with the hole. The rabbis, by contrast, permit removing the pot and returning it: *chachamim omrim notlo u-machziro*.

What is the underlying concern? Two readings emerge from the *rishonim*. Rashi understands the problem as one of *muktzeh*: when the pot is replaced, it will push the shearings aside to make room, and this amounts to actively moving *muktzeh* material with intent — not the incidental movement permitted by *tiltul min ha-tzad*. Tosafot offers a different analysis rooted in the laws of *hatmana* (insulation): one may not create a new act of insulation on *Shabbat* itself, even in substances that merely preserve heat (as opposed to those that add heat, where the prohibition applies already from *erev Shabbat*). If the hole fills up and one must now form a new cavity to re-insert the pot, that constitutes a fresh act of insulation — which is prohibited on *Shabbat*.

### Hole Filling Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא נִתְקַלְקְלָה הַגּוּמָּא, וּמָר סָבַר אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַאי סְלִיקוּסְתָּא — דָּצַהּ, שָׁלְפָה, וַהֲדַר דָּצַהּ — שְׁרֵי. וְאִי לָאו, אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, **holds** that **one** need be **concerned lest the cavity be destroyed** and he return the pot anyway; **and** the other **Sage,** a reference to the Rabbis, **holds** that **one** need **not** be **concerned** about that. The Gemara records several rulings with regard to placing an object into another object that is set-aside. **Rav Huna said:** With regard to **this** fragrant **daffodil branch** that was kept in a pot of moist earth in the house; if on Shabbat eve one **inserted it** into the earth, then **pulled it out, and then inserted it** again into the earth, **it is permitted** to pull it out again on Shabbat. By inserting it and then pulling it out, he has already widened the cavity in which the branch was placed. There is no room for concern that when he pulls it out again on Shabbat he will cause earth to shift from its place. **And if** he did **not** do so on Shabbat eve, **it is prohibited** to pull it out on Shabbat.

*Ve-amar Rabbi Abba amar Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi amar Rav: hakol modim she-nitkalkela ha-guma she-assur le-hachzir* — all agree that if the hole actually collapsed and was ruined, it is forbidden to return the pot. The question is then what exactly the *mishnah* dispute is about. One might read the rabbis as permitting return even after the hole collapses, but the *gemara* rejects this. *Heichi dami?* — what exactly is the case? *Ee delo nitkalkela ha-guma, shapir ka'amri rabanan* — if the hole is intact, the rabbis' position is perfectly understandable. But then what is Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's concern? The *gemara* first suggests the debate is about a case where the hole did collapse, but then clarifies: *le'olam delo nitkalkela* — the hole did not collapse. *Ve-hacha be-chosheshin ka-miflegi* — the dispute is about whether one must be concerned that it might collapse. One master holds *chosheshin* — we are concerned about that possibility — and therefore forbids returning the pot. The other holds *ein chosheshin* — one need not be concerned — and permits it. This is a notably stringent reading of the *mishnah*: even Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's position does not address a case where the hole actually caved in (which would be universally forbidden), but only a precautionary concern that it might.

### Fragrant Plant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי סַכִּינָא דְּבֵינֵי אוּרְבֵי — דָּצַהּ, שַׁלְפָהּ, וַהֲדַר דָּצַהּ — שְׁרֵי. וְאִי לָאו אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: This knife that is** stored **between bricks; if one stuck it** between the bricks on Shabbat eve, **pulled it out, and then stuck it** between the bricks, **it is permitted** to pull it out on Shabbat. **And if** he did **not** do so on Shabbat eve, **it is prohibited** to pull it out on Shabbat.

*Amar Rav Huna:* a case involving *slikusta* — a fragrant plant that would be brought in a pot of dirt to important personages to smell. The practice was to insert it into the dirt, withdraw it for use, and then re-insert it. On *Shabbat*, the question is whether one may pull it out, present it for smelling, and then re-insert it into the dirt. The concern is not *kotzet* — harvesting — since the plant has already been cut. Rather, the problem is making or enlarging a hole in the dirt, which Rashi understands as a *muktzeh* issue (the dirt being *muktzeh*). *De-atza shalfa ve-hadar de-atza* — if one had already inserted it, pulled it out, and re-inserted it before *Shabbat*, then the hole is sufficiently established, and *sharya* — it is permitted on *Shabbat*. *Ve-ee lo — assur* — if not, it is forbidden, because one would effectively be creating a new hole in *muktzeh* dirt.

### Knife in Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מָר זוּטְרָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּגוּרְדְּיָתָא דִּקְנֵי שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, and some say Rav Ashi, said:** Placing a knife **between** the branches of **a hedge of reeds** (*ge'onim*) may **well** be done and there is no concern lest one come to cut the reeds when he removes it.

*Amar Shmuel: hai sakina de-veini ourbi* — people would store knives in holes between the bricks of a wall. *De-atza shalfa ve-hadar de-atza sharya* — if one had pre-made the hole by inserting the knife, removing it, and re-inserting it before *Shabbat*, one may take the knife out and return it on *Shabbat*. *Ve-ee lo — assur* — if not, it is forbidden, because taking out the knife and returning it would mean enlarging or forming the hole anew on *Shabbat*. The *gemara* will continue to explore these cases. Rashi reads the concern as *muktzeh*-based — the dirt or mortar between the bricks is *muktzeh*, and one must not move it to make space. But there is also a possible concern of *binyan* — that by making the knife-shaped opening in a wall, one is creating a useful structural feature, which would be a form of building. The two explanations are left in tension, but the unifying thread is clear: whatever the precise prohibition, one must establish the opening before *Shabbat* enters, so that no new act of hole-making is required on *Shabbat* itself.
