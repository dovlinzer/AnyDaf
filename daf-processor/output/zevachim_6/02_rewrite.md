# Zevachim 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Asham vs. Chatas

### Reish Lakish Challenge

The daf opens in the middle of 5b, at the line beginning with the word *mosarah*. The subject is Reish Lakish's pointed observation about what seems to be a paradox: an *asham*, which is fundamentally bound up with its *ba'alim* — its owners — how can it be brought in a valid state when it is no longer connected to those owners? Specifically, the case at hand is one in which the animal was slaughtered *shelo lishmah*, not for its proper designation, and yet the *gemara* has been treating it as *kasher*. The challenge is to explain how a *korban* so intimately tied to its owners can function validly when that connection is severed.

### Asham Reincarnation

The *gemara*'s answer proceeds by way of analogy. Just as a *chatas* whose owners have died undergoes a kind of reincarnation — the money is transferred and ultimately used to purchase an *olah* — so too an *asham* whose owners have died can be similarly reincarnated and brought as an *olah*. There exists, therefore, a genuine and recognized phenomenon in which an *asham* is brought without being connected to its original owners. That is precisely what gives the *shelo lishmah* case its foothold: if an *asham* can in principle be brought entirely detached from its owners, then the fact that a *shelo lishmah asham* is brought without counting for the owners is not categorically impossible but simply an extension of a known reality.

A *chatas*, by contrast, presents greater difficulty. On the one hand, the *gemara* acknowledges that by the case of *mosarah* — where someone separated two animals as backup for each other — there is at least some theoretical analogue in which a *chatas* might be brought apart from its original purpose. So the possibility is not entirely impossible. Yet the Torah's own language — *chatas hi*, "it is a *chatas*" — places a limiting constraint: only when performed properly, in its intended manner, fully connected to the owners, does it count as a *chatas*. The *gezeiras hakasuv* forecloses any latitude. The *asham*, by contrast, has no such limiting *pasuk* — or so it seems momentarily.

### Hu Post-Emurim

At this point the *gemara* introduces a complication: the word *hu* — "it is" — does in fact appear by the *asham* as well. The verse reads *v'hiktiru hamizbeiach isheh lashem asham hu*, "they shall burn it on the altar as a fire-offering to God; it is a guilt offering." One might therefore think that the same logic applies — that *asham hu* restricts the *asham* to only its proper, owner-connected mode.

The *gemara* rejects this. The *pasuk* speaks of *asham hu* at the stage *le'achar haktaras emurim* — after the *emurim* have already been burned on the *mizbeiach*. At that late stage, there is nothing left that could possibly invalidate the *korban*; the *emurim* have already been offered. Thus the word *hu* at that juncture cannot be a limitation on *kashrus*, because by that point any defect in procedure is irrelevant. A *tanna* makes this explicit: the word *hu* by an *asham* is stated only with reference to the post-*emurim* stage, and a *shechita* of an *asham* without the *emurim* having been brought is nonetheless *kasher*. The limiting force of *hu* simply cannot be functioning as a restriction on *lishmah* when applied to a stage at which no such restriction could have any effect.

### Pasture Transition

This raises the natural follow-up: if *asham hu* is not limiting the *kashrus* of the *shechita*, what is it teaching? The *gemara* answers: it is the source for the ruling of Rav Huna in the name of Rav. When an *asham* has been *nitak l're'iyah* — put out to pasture, meaning the animal has been set aside to graze until it develops a *mum* and can be redeemed, its sanctity ultimately destined to fund an *olah* — and it is slaughtered *stam*, without explicit intent for any particular *korban*, it is valid. Since the animal's sanctity is headed toward that of an *olah*, a generic *shechita* at that transitional stage is treated as an *olah* and is *kasher*. However, if the animal has *not* yet been put out to pasture, this leniency does not apply. The word *hu* teaches that the *asham* identity has a certain stickiness; it does not simply dissolve on its own. Only once the formal transition to the *re'iyah* stage has occurred is the identity sufficiently shifted that an unspecified *shechita* can ride along with it. But a *shelo lishmah asham* is *kasher* and simply does not count for the owners — that much has been established.

## Heirs & Ownership

### Setup Discussion

The *gemara* now shifts to a new discussion. Rav Nachman and Rav Sheshet were sitting together, with Rav Adda bar Matna also present among them, and they were analyzing Rabbi Elazar's response to Reish Lakish. Rabbi Elazar had pointed to the phenomenon of *korbanos* being brought after the owner's death — particularly *olah* and *shlamim* — as a precedent for why it should not be surprising that an *asham* can be brought in a valid state without being connected to the owners.

One might press back on behalf of Reish Lakish: granting that this phenomenon exists by *olah* and *shlamim*, why not simply argue that those too should be *mirtze* — that they should actually count on behalf of whoever brings them? If you insist that *korbanos* must be connected to someone, why not let the *yorsehim*, the heirs, receive some benefit from bringing them? What exactly is the nature of the *heir's* relationship to a *korban* they have inherited? This becomes the central question of the section.

### Yoledet Proof

Rav Adda bar Matna provides what he considers a decisive answer. Consider a *yoledet* — a woman who has given birth and incurred the obligation to bring an *olah* as part of her *tahara* process — who then dies before bringing it. Her children inherit the obligation and bring the *olah*. But the *olah* of a *yoledet* is specifically connected to childbirth. If we were to say the heirs receive some form of *kapara* or merit from bringing it, we would face an absurdity: the sons who are bringing it never gave birth. The *korban* is not an atonement for a general *mitzvat aseh* that they too might have violated; it is organically tied to a biological event that the heirs simply did not experience. Rav Adda bar Matna therefore concludes: *im hi yalda, baneha mi yaldu* — "if she gave birth, did her sons give birth?" The very impossibility of the *korban*'s purpose applying to the heirs demonstrates that there can be no assumption that the heirs receive *kapara* from it, and thus the phenomenon of a *korban* being brought without being *mirtze* for anyone is simply a settled *halacha*.

### Rav Ashi Counter

Rav Ashi, however, challenges this. Even the *olah* of a *yoledet*, he argues, carries within it a general atoning function. The *gemara* establishes elsewhere that an *olah* atones for *mitzvot aseh* — positive commandments — that one has failed to fulfill. This function belongs to the *olah* as such, regardless of the occasion for which it was brought. A *yoledet* who brings her *olah* would simultaneously achieve atonement for any *mitzvot aseh* she had neglected — that is simply what an *olah* does. If that is so, then even the sons who bring the *olah* on her behalf might gain some *kapara* for their own unfulfilled *mitzvot aseh*, since the *korban* carries that general atoning capacity regardless of whose name it is technically brought under. The argument of Rav Adda bar Matna is therefore not conclusive: even in the *yoledet* case one could in principle argue that the heirs receive secondary atonement.

### Ownership Core Q

This brings the *gemara* to the core question: do heirs actually *own* the *korban* they inherit? The suggestion that heirs might receive atonement from a *korban* implies that they must have some ownership stake — *kapara* is not something one receives from a stranger's *korban*. And if ownership is the criterion, then the question of whether heirs truly become the *ba'alim* of an inherited *korban* must be resolved before anything else can follow.

## Mincha-Temurah Tension

### Mincha No-Partnership

The *gemara* adduces the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan as a starting point. If a person sanctified a *mincha* and died, leaving it to his two sons, can that *mincha* be brought? The general *halacha* is that all *korbanos* may be owned and brought in partnership — *b'shutfut* — with one exception: a *mincha*. A *mincha* cannot be a *minchat shutafim*, a partnership meal-offering. The Torah's use of the word *nefesh* — "a soul" — in connection with the *mincha* is interpreted to limit it to an individual's offering, excluding any jointly-owned *mincha* from being valid. Rabbi Yochanan rules that when two sons inherit a *mincha*, it may nonetheless be brought, and it does not carry the disqualifying status of a *minchat shutafin*. The implication seems clear: the heirs do not become the owners of the *korban*. It remains, in some sense, their father's *mincha* — brought by them but not owned by them — and therefore the partnership concern simply does not apply.

### Behema Temurah Block

Yet the same Rabbi Yochanan says something that appears to cut in the opposite direction. If a person left a *behema*, an animal set aside as a *korban*, and died, leaving it to his two sons, Rabbi Yochanan rules that the *korban* may be brought — but neither son can perform *temurah* on it. *Temurah* is the act of substituting another animal for the *korban*, and the entire *parasha* of *temurah* is formulated in the singular, excluding jointly-owned *korbanos*. If the two sons collectively own the *korban* as partners, they are in the position of *shutafin* — joint owners — and *shutafin* cannot perform *temurah*. This ruling only makes sense if the heirs are considered the actual owners, with joint ownership that prevents *temurah*. But that directly contradicts the *mincha* ruling, which implies they are not owners at all.

### Contradiction Framed

The contradiction is now squarely before us. The *mincha* case indicates the heirs do not own the *korban* — otherwise it would be an invalid *minchat shutafin*. The *behema* case indicates they do own it — otherwise they should each individually be able to perform *temurah*, since two non-owners could not be blocked by the *shutafin* rule. How can both rulings be ascribed to the same Rabbi Yochanan?

### Hamir Yamir Include

The *gemara* resolves the tension through a careful reading of the verse governing *temurah*. The Torah's doubled language — *im hamir yamir* — is interpreted as a deliberate inclusion: it comes to include the *yoresh*, the heir, within the scope of those who can perform *temurah*. This is the operative principle that makes an heir's *temurah* possible at all. Once the inclusion of the heir is established through the *pasuk*, the *gemara* then applies the parallel rule: *v'ein shnayim m'mirin* — two people cannot jointly perform *temurah*. Just as two genuine joint-owners cannot do *temurah*, two heirs — even though they are not genuine owners — likewise cannot. The verse that includes the heir also implicitly carries the single-person limitation that accompanies *temurah*.

The *gemara* then addresses a further challenge from Rav Yaakov of Nehar Pekod, who asks whether the same logic should not apply to *maaser sheni*. By *maaser sheni* the *pasuk* similarly uses doubled language — *gaol yigal* — to include the heir in the obligation of adding a fifth when redeeming. Should we then say that two heirs cannot jointly redeem? The *gemara* dismisses this: *maaser* is fundamentally different, because even with genuine joint-owners by *maaser*, partnership does not affect the status of ownership. Partners in *maaser* add a fifth just like sole owners. Since the *shutafut* concern has no purchase by *maaser* even in ordinary cases of real ownership, it certainly does not affect the status of heirs. By *temurah* and *mincha*, on the other hand, partnership is definitionally relevant — those *halachot* are stated in terms that exclude joint-owners — and heirs track that structure accordingly.

## Ownership Definitions

### Multi-Halacha Principle

The *gemara* now develops a more precise conceptual framework. Rather than asking whether heirs do or do not "own" the *korban* in some univocal sense, it recognizes that different *halachot* within the laws of *kodshim* are keyed to different relationships one might have to the *korban*. Ownership, sanctification, and atonement are not always vested in the same person, and the *halacha* tracks each relationship independently.

### Sanctifier Add Fifth

The *gemara* cites Rav and Rabbi Yochanan on this point. Take a case where someone else sanctifies an animal on another person's behalf — your friend has the *korban* obligation, you own the animal, and you declare it sanctified for your friend's sake. Who adds a fifth when redeeming it, should it develop a *mum*? The Torah says *ve'im gaol yigal hamakdish* — if the *makdish*, the one who sanctified, redeems it, he must add a fifth. Thus: *hamakdish mosif chomesh* — the sanctifier adds the fifth, not the person whose *korban* it functionally is.

### Atone-for Temurah

In that same configuration, who can perform *temurah*? Here the rule runs the other way: *umitkapeir oseh tmurah* — the one for whom the *korban* atones has the power to perform *temurah*. There is a *pasuk* from which this is learned. The capacity to perform *temurah* belongs to the one for whom the *korban* has atoning function, not to the one who technically sanctified it. Additionally, a third *halacha*: if someone takes *terumah* from his own grain on behalf of his fellow's grain, the right to designate to which *kohen* that *terumah* goes belongs to the one who took it from his own produce — *tovat hana'ah shelo*.

### Heir Temurah Logic

This tripartite framework now creates a pointed logical problem. We have established that the *mincha* case demonstrates heirs do not genuinely own the *korban* — it remains their parent's. But if they do not own it and it does not atone for them in any direct sense, how can a single heir perform *temurah*? The power of *temurah* belongs to the one for whom the *korban* atones. If the *korban* does not atone for the heir — since it is fundamentally their parent's — then the heir should have no *temurah* power at all, regardless of whether there is one heir or two.

## Mikufya Atonement

### Hybrid Status

The *gemara*'s resolution is elegant and conceptually precise. It introduces the distinction: *mikiva lo mechapra, mikufya mechapra* — "from its fixed identity it does not atone, from what floats above it, it does atone." The heir occupies a hybrid status: not an owner, not the designated beneficiary of the *korban*'s primary function, and yet the recipient of a secondary, ripple-effect *kapara*.

### Fixed-Identity Ripple

What does this mean concretely? The *korban* was sanctified by the mother — call her the primary identity of the *korban*. It was designated to atone for her *mitzvot aseh*. That is *kiva* — its fixed, fundamental identity. She has since died. The heirs bring the *korban* because that is the *halacha* — an *olah* that was sanctified must be brought even after the owner's death. The *korban*'s core purpose remains keyed to her. Yet once the *korban* is brought, the heir who participated in bringing it gains something — a secondary atonement, a halo effect, a ripple from the act. It is not the primary function of the *korban*, but it is a genuine *kapara* that attaches to the heir by virtue of his role in bringing it.

### Non-Owner Temurah

This secondary level of atonement is sufficient, the *gemara* concludes, to vest in a single heir the capacity to perform *temurah*. The rule is that the one for whom the *korban* atones can do *temurah*. The heir receives atonement — not at the *kiva* level, but at the *mikufya* level — and that is enough. Two heirs, however, cannot do *temurah* together, because the *pasuk* of *im hamir yamir* includes only a single heir, and the structural exclusion of joint performance applies by analogy to the *shutafin* rule. The *mincha* remains perfectly coherent: since the heirs do not own it and it is not their *korban* at the *kiva* level, two heirs bringing an inherited *mincha* do not constitute a *minchat shutafin* and the *mincha* is valid. The entire system is now internally consistent.

## Shelo Lishmah Kippur

### Atone Question

Having established the parameters of when a *korban* is brought and for whom it functions, the *gemara* turns to a new question regarding *shelo lishmah*. We have already been told that if an *olah* was slaughtered *shelo lishmah*, the *korban* is *kasher* but does not fulfill the owner's obligation — *lo ala l'be'alim l'shem chova* — and the owner must bring another *korban* to discharge his *neder* or obligation. The new question is whether, setting aside the formal obligation question, the *korban* achieved any metaphysical effect at all. Did it *kippeir*? Did it accomplish the spiritual function of atonement, even if it did not discharge the formal *chova*?

### No Atonement Answer

Rav Sheshet the son of Rav Idi argues that it makes sense to say it did not atone — *mistavra d'lo kipro*. His reasoning is a *kal v'chomer*-style inference: if the *korban* did achieve atonement, then what is the purpose of bringing the second one? The owner has already been atoned for. To say that a *korban* both fully atoned and yet the obligation to bring a replacement remains is difficult to accept. The simpler conclusion is that no atonement was achieved, which is precisely why the replacement is necessary.

### Valid Start Logic

Rav Ashi reframes the question. He clarifies what Rav Sheshet's difficulty actually was: if the *korban* did not achieve atonement, one might wonder why it is brought at all — what is the point of completing a *korban* that accomplishes nothing? To this, Rav Ashi says: *shelo lishmo mikoach lishmo ka'ati* — the *shelo lishmah* korban comes from the strength of a *lishmah* korban. It began its life as a fully valid *korban* that was supposed to be brought. An error occurred in the process — it was slaughtered *shelo lishmah* — but you do not simply abandon something that began as a legitimate *korban*. You finish bringing it. The obligation to complete it derives not from any atonement it will achieve, but from the fact that it was properly sanctified and initiated as a *korban*. The *shechita* was a mistake, but the *korban* must nonetheless be brought to completion.

### Finish Obligation

The *gemara*'s conclusion is thus: no atonement is achieved by the *shelo lishmah korban*, but it must still be brought because it began as a valid *korban* and the process must be completed. The obligation to bring a second *korban* — to actually discharge the *neder* and achieve atonement — remains fully in force. The interesting conceptual point the *gemara* considered along the way is whether it is theoretically possible to separate the question of *kippur* from the question of *yotzei b'chova* — and the *gemara* shows that while they often track each other, they are in principle distinct questions, even if the conclusion here is that the *shelo lishmah korban* fails on both counts.

## Post-Separation Aveiros

### New Q Setup

The *gemara* now pivots to a question that stands entirely on its own, independent of *shelo lishmah*. It is a general question about when an *olah*'s atoning power is temporally defined. The principle is well known that an *olah* atones for *mitzvot aseh* — positive commandments one has failed to fulfill. The question is: which *mitzvot aseh* does it atone for? Specifically, if a person sanctified an animal as an *olah* and then, between the moment of sanctification (*hafrashah*) and the moment of bringing it, he failed to perform some *mitzvat aseh* — say he neglected to put on *tefillin* or failed to eat in a *sukkah* — does the *olah* atone for that post-*hafrashah* transgression?

### Chatas Comparison

One position reasons by analogy to the *chatas*. A *chatas* atones only for a sin committed before the *hafrashah*; it does not extend its atoning reach to sins committed after the animal was sanctified. One might therefore say the same about an *olah*: what was done before you set the animal aside is covered; what happens after is not.

### Olah Generic

The opposing position distinguishes the *olah* from the *chatas*. A *chatas* is inherently particular and concrete — every separate sin requires its own separate *chatas*. Because it is tied to a specific sin, its identity is fixed at the moment of sanctification in relation to that specific transgression. An *olah*, however, is generic. It is not linked to any particular *mitzvat aseh*; it works for *mitzvot aseh* generally, as a class. And precisely because it is not anchored to one specific act, *keivan de'ika kammah asei gabeih mechapera* — since any number of *mitzvot aseh* are potentially within its scope — it may well atone for those that come after the *hafrashah* as well.

### Vesamach Proof

To adjudicate between these positions, the *gemara* invokes a *beraisa*: *vesamach venirtza* — "and he shall lay his hands and it shall be accepted." The *beraisa* asks: does the *semichah* itself achieve atonement? Surely not — *ein kaparah ela bedam*, atonement comes through the blood, not the laying of hands. Rather, the juxtaposition of *vesamach venirtza* — the laying of hands alongside the acceptance — teaches that if one treats the *semichah* as a mere *shiyurei mitzvah*, an afterthought, then the *katuv* treats him *ke'ilu lo kibeir* — as though he did not fully achieve atonement. The phrase used is *ke'ilu lo kibeir vechibeir* — it is as though he did not atone, yet he did atone.

The *gemara* attempts to read this *beraisa* as bearing on the post-*hafrashah* question. Reading *lo kibeir* as referring to a specific failure and *kibeir* as referring to prior coverage, the *gemara* suggests: *lo kibeir* means the *olah* did not work for the *asei* of *semichah*, while *kibeir* means it did work for the *mitzvot aseh* committed prior to the *hafrashah*. If that reading is correct, it implies that the *olah* does not atone for the failure to do *semichah* specifically because *semichah* was omitted after the *hafrashah* — which would prove that post-*hafrashah* transgressions are generally excluded.

## Kailuah Reading

### Part Works Read

The initial reading of the *beraisa* thus proposes a clean split: the *olah* atones for *mitzvot aseh* committed before the *hafrashah*, but does not atone for the failure to do *semichah*, which occurred after the *hafrashah*. The phrase *ke'ilu lo kibeir vechibeir* is parsed as: part A was covered (*kibeir*), part B was not (*lo kibeir*) — not because *semichah* is uniquely excluded, but because it is paradigmatically a post-*hafrashah* failure.

### Rava Counter

Rava challenges this reading. He is willing to grant, in principle, that an *olah* works generically for post-*hafrashah mitzvot aseh* — that is exactly his position — but he insists that *semichah* is different and would be excluded even on that theory. Why? *Shani hatam de-chol kamma de-lo shachit be-amor ve-smoch ka'i* — as long as the animal has not yet been slaughtered, you can always still do *semichah*. The moment you transgress the *asei* of *semichah* is only the instant the *shechita* is performed — at which point it is too late. So the *asei* of *semichah* is not really a post-*hafrashah* transgression at all in the sense under discussion; it is a transgression that occurs at the very moment of *shechita* itself, or immediately after. The *beraisa* excluding *semichah* therefore tells us nothing about whether the *olah* covers sins committed in the period between *hafrashah* and *shechita*. Rava's conclusion remains: the *olah* atones generically for *mitzvot aseh*, including those violated after the *hafrashah*, with the one natural exception being the *asei* of *semichah* itself, which arises only at *shechita*.

### C-Minus Read

Huna bar Yehuda offers a simpler reading of the *beraisa* that eliminates the problem entirely. The phrase *ke'ilu lo kibeir vechibeir* does not mean the *olah* worked for some things and failed for others. It means: the person technically achieved *kapara* — *kibeir gavra* — but did not achieve *kaparah shleimah*, a complete and ideal atonement. He got a *kapara*, but not the way it was supposed to be done. As Rashi formulates it: *le-nachat ruach le-kono* — he did not bring satisfaction to his Creator. The *korban* was technically valid, but was not performed at its highest level. This reading makes the *beraisa* entirely irrelevant to the post-*hafrashah* question.

### Metzora Oil Case

To support this reading, Huna bar Yehuda points to a parallel case in the *tahara* process of the *metzora*. Part of that process requires the *kohen* to take the remaining oil and pour it on the head of the *metzora*. This oil is called *ha-notar* — the remainder — suggesting it is not the central act. Yet it is juxtaposed to the language *lechapper alav lifnei Hashem* — to atone for him before God. Rabbi Akiva reads the juxtaposition stringently: *im natan kipper ve-im lo natan lo kipper* — if the oil was poured on the head, it worked; if not, it did not. But Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri says it is only *shiyurei mitzvah* — a supplementary part of the *mitzvah* — and therefore *bein nittan bein she-lo nittan kipper*: whether or not it was poured, the *tahara* is effective. Yet he adds: *u-ma'alin alav ke-ilu lo kipper* — we treat it as though it did not work.

### Akiva-Ben Nuri

The *gemara* asks: what does Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri mean by *ke-ilu lo kipper*? If it truly did not work, he would have to bring another *korban* — but he explicitly said it did work. *Ella kipper gavra lo kipper kaparah shleimah* — technically the *metzora* is *tahor*, but the *mitzvah* was not performed optimally. This is the same structure Huna bar Yehuda proposes for the *semichah beraisa*. The *beraisa* then uses the same language back in the *semichah* context: *ke'ilu lo kibeir vechibeir* simply means the *korban* was technically valid but suboptimal — a C-minus — with no implication about which category of sins was or was not covered.

The *gemara*, however, notes that the parallel is not perfectly clean. One could still read the *metzora* case as a split — the oil placement on the ear worked (*kibeir*), but the oil placement on the head did not (*lo kibeir*) — rather than reading it as a unified C-minus grade. The question of whether *ke-ilu lo kibeir vechibeir* always means suboptimality or can sometimes mean "worked for A, not for B" remains open, and Rashi and Tosafos debate the practical implications.

## Shavuot Two Goats

### Second Goat Tumah

The *gemara* now seeks a more direct proof from the *Shavuot* offerings. Rabbi Shimon teaches: the two *se'irei l'chatas* brought on *Shavuot* are both for *tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — for the ritual impurity of the sanctuary and its sancta. The question is: once the blood of the first *se'ir* is sprinkled, why is there a need to bring the second? Rabbi Shimon answers: *al tumah she'eira bein zeh l'zeh* — to cover any *tumah* that occurred between the bringing of the first *se'ir* and the second. He then elaborates: in principle, Bnei Yisrael should be obligated to bring a *korban* every few minutes, given how easily *tumat mikdash* occurs inadvertently. It is only the Torah's *rachamim* — its mercy — that does not burden us with that frequency, stretching the coverage from one *Yom Tov*'s *korban* to the next. Here on *Shavuot*, the Torah deliberately includes two *se'irim* to demonstrate this principle and cover even the brief interval between them.

### Post-Sep Implication

This teaching carries a significant implication for the question at hand. The *korban tzibur* of *Shavuot* is, in its nature, more analogous to the *atonement for mitzvot aseh* scenario than to a specific *chatas* for a specific sin. And critically, it appears that the second *se'ir* — which covers the *tumah* between the two offerings — atones for *tumah* that occurred *after* the *korban* was sanctified. If both animals were sanctified simultaneously at the outset, the *tumah* that occurred between the first and second offering would be post-*hafrashah* relative to the second one. If so, this is precisely the proof being sought: a generic *korban* can atone for transgressions occurring after its sanctification.

### Beit Din Retcon

Rav Pappa objects to drawing this conclusion. He argues that *korbanos tzibur* operate under special rules. *Beit din* — the rabbinic court that oversees communal *korbanos* — has the authority to stipulate regarding *korbanos tzibur* in ways that individuals cannot do for their own *korbanos*. Applied here: even if the two *se'irim* were sanctified simultaneously, *beit din* can retroactively stipulate that the sanctity of the second *se'ir* took effect only immediately before it was brought. Under that stipulation, the second *se'ir* is not really a post-*hafrashah* atonement at all — its *hafrashah* effectively occurs right before its *shechita*, after all the relevant *tumah* had already occurred. Therefore, nothing can be proven from *Shavuot* about whether individual *korbanos* atone for post-*hafrashah* transgressions.

This power of *beit din* is illustrated by the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *korbanos tzibur, sachin meshachtan l'ma shehein* — communal *korbanos* follow the designation determined at the moment of their *shechita*, meaning that through *beit din*'s authority, the identity and sanctity of a communal *korban* can be determined at the time of slaughter, not the time of sanctification.

### Shimon No Retcon

Rav Yosef the son of Rav Shmuel challenges Rav Pappa's recourse to *beit din*'s power. Can one really attribute to Rabbi Shimon — who is the source of this entire teaching about the two *se'irim* — the position that *beit din* has this authority? The *gemara* cites a ruling transmitted through a long chain: Rav Idi bar Avin, Rav Amram, Rav Yitzchak, all in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *temidin shelo hutzrechu l'tzibur* — if there were *temidin* that were left over from one year and now cannot be used with the new year's communal funds, may they be redeemed without a *mum*?

### Temidim Redemption

The answer depends on one's position regarding *beit din*'s power. *L'divrei Rabbi Shimon ein nifdin temimim* — according to Rabbi Shimon, they cannot be redeemed while still unblemished. For Rabbi Shimon, the ordinary rules apply: a *korban* can only be redeemed once it has developed a *mum*. *Aval l'divrei chachamim nifdin temimim* — but the *chachamim* permit redemption without a *mum* in this case, precisely because *beit din* can stipulate retroactively that the *korban* was never truly meant to be sanctified under these circumstances, effectively treating it as though it was never sanctified and allowing it to be redeemed as if unblemished. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, does not accept this *beit din* stipulation mechanism — the *korban* was sanctified and that sanctity is real and follows normal rules.

### Shimon Final

Since Rabbi Shimon does not hold that *beit din* can retroactively manipulate the *hafrashah*, Rav Pappa's defense cannot be invoked on his behalf. It was Rabbi Shimon who taught about the two *se'irim* of *Shavuot* in the first place, and since Rabbi Shimon rejects *lae beit din masneh aleihem*, the second *se'ir* truly does cover *tumah* that occurred after its sanctification — not by virtue of any retroactive *beit din* stipulation, but simply because it is a generic *korban* that atones for a generic category of *aveira*. The proof therefore stands: generic *korbanos* — analogous to the *olah* that atones for *mitzvot aseh* — do have the capacity to atone for transgressions occurring after the moment of *hafrashah*. The *gemara* leaves the discussion at this point, noting that the full resolution will be completed the following day.
