# Arakhin 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Mishna

### Yesterday's Nida

We begin Daf 9 by picking up with the *mishna* at the bottom of 8b. For those who were present yesterday, the preceding *mishna* dealt with a woman whose *nida* status is uncertain — specifically the complex question of *petach betoch pitchai nida* — which represents one of the most mathematically demanding areas of *halacha*. This connects naturally to a well-known *mishna* at the end of the third *perek* of *Pirkei Avot*, which states: *kinim upitchai nida hen hen gufei halachot* — that *kinim* and *pitchai nida* are the very substance of *halacha*, genuinely difficult and requiring rigorous analysis. The *mishna* adds that *tkufot ugmatriot parparot lachochma* — questions of astronomical seasons and numerical calculations are more like spices, not as essential. This is worth noting both because it acknowledges the difficulty of yesterday's *mishna* and because the topic of *tkufot* touches directly on the *mishna* before us today.

### Rosh Chodesh Topic

Today's *sugya* concerns *Rosh Chodesh* and the permissible range of variation in the number of *malei* and *chaseir* months — full and lacking months — within a given year. This is another challenging piece of Talmudic reasoning, and the entire *gemara* on this *daf* will be devoted to it.

### Mishna Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מֵאַרְבָּעָה חֳדָשִׁים הַמְעוּבָּרִים בְּשָׁנָה, וְלֹא נִרְאָה יָתֵר עַל שְׁמוֹנָה. שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם אֵין נֶאֱכָלִין פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם, וְלֹא יָתֵר עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה. לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים אֵין נֶאֱכָל פָּחוֹת מִתִּשְׁעָה, וְלֹא יָתֵר עַל אַחַד עָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **No fewer than four full** thirty-day **months** may be established **during** the course of **a year, and it did not seem** appropriate to establish **more than eight. The two loaves** that are brought to the Temple on *Shavuot* **are eaten** by the priests **not before the second and not after the third** day from when they were baked. **The shewbread is eaten not before the ninth** day from when it was baked, which is the situation in a regular week when the bread is baked on Friday and eaten on the following Shabbat; **and not after the eleventh** day, when the two Festival days of Rosh HaShana occur on Thursday and Friday, as the shewbread is baked on Wednesday and not eaten until the following Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין קָטָן נִימּוֹל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁמוֹנָה, וְלֹא יָתֵר עַל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **A minor boy is not circumcised before the eighth** day after his birth **and not after the twelfth** day. Normally a newborn is circumcised on his eighth day. If he was born during twilight, which an uncertain period of day or night, he is circumcised on what would be the eighth day of his birth if he is was born at night, which is the ninth day if he was born during the day. If he was born during twilight on Shabbat eve, the circumcision cannot be performed on Friday, as he might have been born on Shabbat and therefore Friday is only the seventh day. And the circumcision cannot be on Shabbat, as perhaps he was born on Friday and only circumcision performed on the eighth day overrides Shabbat. Therefore, it is postponed until after Shabbat. If two days of Rosh HaShana occur on Sunday and Monday, the circumcision is postponed until Tuesday, the twelfth day after birth.

The *mishna* states: *ein pochin me'arba'a chodashim hame'ubarim bashana* — one may not have fewer than four full (*me'ubarim*, literally "pregnant") months in a year. The *mishna* continues: *velo nireh yeter al shmona* — and it did not appear appropriate, as the *gemara* will explain (*lo nireh lachachamim*), to have more than eight. So the range is: no fewer than four full months, no more than eight. This permitted variation — two on each side of the standard — is the entire focus of the *gemara* that follows.

### Lunar Structure

The underlying structure of the lunar calendar must be understood first. A lunar month is approximately 29 and a half days — slightly more, as *Rashi* notes. Since a month must be a whole number of days, months alternate between 29 and 30 days in order to approximate this 29.5-day cycle.

### Standard Year

In a standard Hebrew year, there are six months of 29 days (*chaseir*, lacking) and six months of 30 days (*malei*, full, also called *me'ubar*). This six-and-six structure perfectly represents the 29.5-day average.

### Permitted Range

The *mishna* permits a variation of two from that standard. You may have as few as four full months — two fewer than the standard six — and no more than eight full months — two more than the standard six. The *gemara* will explain why exactly this degree of variation is tolerable and why going beyond it is not.

### Secondary Cases

The rest of the *mishna* enumerates other cases following the same "no fewer than X, no more than Y" pattern, though the *gemara* does not analyze them in depth. The *shtei halechem* — the two loaves brought on *Shavuot* — may not be eaten fewer than two days after baking, nor more than three days after. The reasoning: one does not bake the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuot* itself, even though it is *Yom Tov*, because the baking does not override *Shavuot*. Therefore, in a standard year, it is baked on *erev Shavuot* — day one — and brought on *Shavuot* — day two. If *erev Shavuot* falls on *Shabbat*, then it is baked on *erev Shabbat* — day one — and brought on *Shavuot* — day three. Hence the range of two to three days.

The *lechem hapanim* — the showbread placed on the *shulchan* in the *Beit Hamikdash* — is not eaten fewer than nine days after baking, nor more than eleven. One does not bake the *lechem hapanim* on *Shabbat*. In the standard case, it is baked on Friday (day one), placed on the *shulchan* on *Shabbat* (day two), and eaten the following *Shabbat* (day nine, counting the day of baking as day one). How does one reach eleven? If the two days before *Shabbat* are the two days of *Rosh Hashanah* — which carries two days of *Yom Tov* even in *Eretz Yisrael* — then since one does not bake on *Yom Tov* for bread that will only be eaten nine days later, the baking is pushed back to *erev Yom Tov*, adding two days and yielding a total of eleven.

One should note a sharp question that arises here: if Thursday is *Rosh Hashanah*, then the following *Shabbat* would be *Yom Kippur*, on which eating the *lechem hapanim* would be problematic. The resolution is that the two-day *Rosh Hashanah* in the time of the *Beit Hamikdash* was structured differently from our own: the second day was the actual 1st of *Tishrei*, while the first day was the 30th of *Elul*. Accordingly, Thursday as the first day of *Rosh Hashanah* means it is the 30th of *Elul*, making *Yom Kippur* fall on *Shabbat* — not on the Friday immediately following. The question was very nearly perfect.

### Brit Milah Rules

A *katan* is not given a *brit milah* fewer than eight days after birth — the day of birth itself being counted as day one, so the *milah* occurs on the same day of the week one week later. The maximum is twelve days. How does one reach twelve? If a baby is born during *bein hashmashot* on Friday night — the twilight period between Friday and *Shabbat* — the *brit milah* cannot be performed on Friday, because perhaps he was born on *Shabbat* and Friday would be too early; nor on *Shabbat*, because perhaps he was born on Friday and a *brit* on any day other than the eighth does not override *Shabbat*. Therefore, the *brit* is performed on Sunday. Counting Friday as day one, *Shabbat* is day two, the following *Shabbat* is day nine, and Sunday is day ten. If that Sunday and Monday happen to be two days of *Yom Tov*, the *brit* cannot be performed then either, pushing it to day twelve. That is the range of eight to twelve.

## Why Not Nine?

### Rav Huna's Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״לֹא נִרְאָה יָתֵר עַל שְׁמֹנָה״? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא נִרְאָה לַחֲכָמִים לְעַבֵּר יָתֵר עַל שְׁמוֹנָה. מַאי שְׁנָא תִּשְׁעָה דְּלָא? אִם כֵּן קָדֵים אָתֵי סֵיהֲרָא תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the statement of the mishna that **it did not seem** appropriate to establish **more than eight? Rav Huna says: It did not seem** appropriate **to the Sages to extend more than eight** months in a year and establish them as full, thirty-day months. The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **nine** months, in **that** the Sages **did not** deem it appropriate to establish that many full months in one year? Apparently, the Sages were concerned **that if so,** i.e., if there were that many full months, then the new **moon** of Tishrei would **arrive three days before** Rosh HaShana. A lunar month is twenty-nine and a half days, and therefore a twelve-month period should have six full months of thirty days and six deficient months of twenty-nine days. If there were nine full months, this would cause subsequent months to start three days after the new moon. This in turn would lead people to deride the rabbinical court and accuse it of declaring the new month whenever it wanted, without regard for the astronomical facts.

The *gemara* opens its analysis by clarifying the *mishna*'s ambiguous phrase *lo nireh*. *Rav Huna* states: *lo nira lachachamim le'aber yater al shmonah* — it did not appear proper to the Sages to have more than eight full months. This is the plain reading: the permissible range is four to eight.

### Nine Full Problem

The *gemara* immediately asks: *mai shna tisha delo* — why not nine? The answer: *de'im ken kadim asi sira tlata yomei* — because then the new moon would appear three days before *Rosh Hashanah*. The arithmetic is straightforward. The standard is six full months. If you have nine full months — three more than standard — you have pushed *Rosh Hashanah* forward three days relative to when the *molad* actually occurs. The new moon would thus be visible three days before *Rosh Hashanah*, making a mockery of the calendar: people would be observing *Rosh Hashanah* three days after seeing the new moon.

### Two Days & Leap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי קָא קָדֵים אָתֵי תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי. כִּדְאָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, הָכָא נָמֵי כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה שָׁנָה שֶׁלְּפָנֶיהָ מְעוּבֶּרֶת, וְעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה חוֹדֶשׁ. דַּל יַרְחָא מַלְיָא בַּהֲדֵי יַרְחָא חַסִּירָא, וְאַכַּתִּי חַד יוֹמָא! לָא אַדַּעְתַּיְיהוּ דְּאִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** But **now, too,** when there are eight full months in one year, the new moon of Tishrei will **arrive two days before** Rosh HaShana, which will also cause people to disparage the court. It is **as Rav Mesharshiyya says,** with regard to a different question: This is referring to a case **where** the year before **was a leap year. Here, too,** one can answer that it is a case **where the year before it was a leap** year, **and the extension** of the **year** was accomplished with an added **month** of twenty-nine days. Accordingly, **remove a full,** thirty-day **month** from the calculation of the current year **corresponding to the** extra **deficient,** twenty-nine-day **month** of the previous year, and the result is that only seven thirty-day months have an impact on the appearance of the new moon relative to the following Rosh HaShana. The Gemara asks: **But** even in a year with only seven thirty-day months the new moon would **still** precede Rosh HaShana by **one day.** The Gemara answers: **People do not pay attention** to a difference of one day, and therefore they will not deride the court.

But the *gemara* presses further: if three days is clearly unacceptable, what about two extra full months — i.e., eight full months — which would push *Rosh Hashanah* two days past the *molad*? *Kadimati terei yomei* — you would still see the new moon two days before *Rosh Hashanah*. Is that acceptable?

### Mesharshiya Fix

The *gemara* answers by citing *Rav Mesharshiya*: *kegon shehayeta shana me'uberet* — we are speaking of a case where the year (or the previous year) was a leap year, a *shana me'uberet*. The significance of this is as follows: when a leap year is declared and an extra month is added, that extra month must represent a 29.5-day lunar cycle. But it can only be expressed as either 29 or 30 days. If the extra month is made 29 days, the calendar is effectively pulled back by half a day relative to where it would have been with a precise 29.5-day cycle. This half-day correction partially offsets the two-day error produced by the eight full months.

### Offset Correction

The practical result: what would have been a two-day discrepancy between *Rosh Hashanah* and the *molad* is reduced to one and a half days. Is a day-and-a-half error tolerable? The *gemara* acknowledges there is still a discrepancy — *lo adatei de'inshei* — but people are not paying close enough attention for an error of a day or a day and a half to be glaringly obvious. Three days would be unmistakable; a day and a half can pass unnoticed.

It is important to clarify what is meant by "correction" here. The system is not saying: if you have too many full months, simply add more lacking months. That would merely be restoring the six-and-six balance, which is what we started with. The question is how much deviation from six-and-six is permissible. The answer is that two days of deviation is permissible precisely because in a leap year scenario — where the extra month is made 29 days rather than 30 — the true discrepancy is only one and a half days, which falls within the range of human tolerance. There is no way to correct for the remaining half-day until the next leap year, since one cannot express a half-day difference through any single month's adjustment.

## Ulla Position

### Ulla Reformulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: לֹא נִרְאָה לַחֲכָמִים לְחַסֵּר יָתֵר עַל שְׁמוֹנָה, וּמַאי טַעְמָא קָאָמַר — מָה טַעַם אֵין פּוֹחֲתִים מֵאַרְבָּעָה חֳדָשִׁים הַמְעוּבָּרִים בְּשָׁנָה — מִשּׁוּם דְּלֹא נִרְאָה לַחֲכָמִים לְחַסֵּר יָתֵר עַל שְׁמוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Until this point the Gemara has discussed Rav Huna's interpretation of the mishna, that the statement: It did not seem appropriate to establish more than eight, means that the Sages did not wish to establish more than eight full months in one year. The Gemara presents an alternative opinion. **Ulla says:** The mishna means that **it did not seem** appropriate **to the Sages to establish more than eight deficient,** twenty-nine-day months. **And** the mishna **is saying what** the **reason** is for the previous statement in the mishna, as follows: **What is the reason** that **no fewer than four full,** thirty-day **months** may be established **during a year?** It is **because it did not seem** appropriate **to the Sages to establish more than eight deficient,** twenty-nine-day months, so that the shortest possible year would comprise four full months and eight deficient months.

*Ulla* offers a reformulation: *lo nira lachachamim lechaser yater mishmona* — the Sages did not see fit to have more than eight *chaseir* (lacking) months. This is not a new ruling but a restatement of the *mishna*'s other limit — the minimum of four full months — expressed from the opposite direction. One cannot have fewer than four full months because that would mean having more than eight lacking months.

### Eight Lacking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא תִּשְׁעָה דְּלָא, דְּאִם כֵּן מְיַיתְּרָא סֵיהֲרָא תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי קָא מְיַיתְּרָא תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי! כִּדְאָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, הָכָא נָמֵי כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה שָׁנָה שֶׁלְּפָנֶיהָ מְעוּבֶּרֶת, דַּל יַרְחָא חַסִּירָא בַּהֲדֵי יַרְחָא מַלְיָא. וְאַכַּתִּי אִיכָּא חַד יוֹמָא, סָבְרִי: אִיתְחֲזוֹיֵי אִיתְחֲזִי וַאֲנַן לָאו אַדַּעְתִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **nine** deficient months, in **that** the Sages **did not** deem it appropriate to have so many deficient months in one year? Apparently, the Sages were concerned **that if so,** i.e., if there were nine deficient months, the new **moon** of Tishrei would appear **three days after** Rosh HaShana. But **now, too,** if there are eight deficient months in one year, the new moon will appear **two days after** Rosh HaShana. It is **as Rav Mesharshiyya says** with regard to a different question: This is referring to a case **where** the year before **was a leap year. Here, too,** one can answer that it is a case **where the year before it was a leap** year, and the added month was a thirty-day month. Accordingly, **remove a deficient,** twenty-nine-day month from the calculation of the current year **corresponding to the** extra **full,** thirty-day **month** of the previous year, and the result is that only seven deficient months have an impact on the appearance of the new moon relative to the following Rosh HaShana. The Gemara asks: **But** even in a year with seven deficient months **there is still one day** between Rosh HaShana and the appearance of the new moon, and therefore people will deride the court. The Gemara answers: People will **assume** that the new moon **was sighted** by witnesses and reported to the court, and they will say: The new moon was visible yesterday **but we did not pay attention** and did not notice it.

The *gemara* explains why more than eight lacking months is problematic: *de'im ken lihavei saira tlata yomei* — the moon would then appear three days after *Rosh Hashanah*. Just as too many full months push *Rosh Hashanah* ahead of the *molad*, too many lacking months push *Rosh Hashanah* behind it. With nine lacking months — three more than the standard six — *Rosh Hashanah* would occur three days before the new moon actually appears.

Eight lacking months, by the same logic, would produce a two-day gap — *Rosh Hashanah* occurring two days before the new moon is visible. The *gemara* again applies *Rav Mesharshiya*'s correction: *kegon dehavya shana shelifneha meuberet* — if the previous year was a leap year, and this time the extra month was made 30 days (rather than 29), then the leap month pushed the calendar forward by half a day beyond the precise cycle. This partially offsets the two-day deficit, reducing it to one and a half days.

In this scenario, the situation is actually somewhat more tolerable than in the previous case. Here, *Rosh Hashanah* occurs before the new moon is visible. On the night of *Rosh Hashanah*, one looks up at the sky and sees — nothing. The old moon has set and the new crescent has not yet appeared. This is not as jarring as seeing a bright new moon the night before *Rosh Hashanah*, because the absence of the moon on *Rosh Hashanah* night can be explained: perhaps in *Yerushalayim* witnesses did see it, perhaps clouds obscured it elsewhere. *Savri itchazi itchazyei ve'anan lav adatin* — people assume it was seen and they simply did not notice. This explains why the *gemara* frames this case as more forgivable: a one-day-early *Rosh Hashanah* (where the sky is dark) is more easily rationalized than a one-day-late one (where a new crescent is already visible).

### Tolerance Rationale

This also clarifies the asymmetry between the two tolerance explanations offered. In the case of too many full months (eight *malei*), the new moon has already been seen for a day before *Rosh Hashanah* — this requires the weaker explanation of *lav adatei de'inshei*, that people are generally inattentive. In the case of too many lacking months (eight *chaseir*), the sky on *Rosh Hashanah* night is dark — this is explained by *savri itchazi*, a more natural and plausible rationalization. The *gemara* thus provides different but complementary justifications for why each direction of error remains within tolerable bounds, so long as the discrepancy does not exceed a day and a half.

A sharp question arises here: what happens if *beit din* finds itself already at the limit of eight *malei* or eight *chaseir* months, and the witnesses continue to arrive late? Can *beit din* simply declare the month on its own authority, without witnesses, to prevent the calendar from exceeding the permitted range? The *gemara* implies that in extremis, *beit din* may indeed do so — which is, of course, the basis of our fixed calendar today. The witnesses and their testimony are the preferred mechanism, and the system is largely self-correcting (a late declaration one month tends to be offset the following month), but when genuine correction is needed and the testimony cannot be relied upon, *beit din* acts on its own authority.

## Leap Year Dispute

### 30-Day Leap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? בְּעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה, דְּתַנְיָא: כַּמָּה עִיבּוּר שָׁנָה — שְׁלֹשִׁים, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: חוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rav Huna and Ulla **disagree?** The Gemara answers: They disagree **about** the length of the month added in the **intercalation of the year, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **How many** days are added in the **intercalation of the year? Thirty** days. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** A deficient **month,** i.e., one of twenty-nine days. Rav Huna accepts the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, whereas Ulla accepts the opinion of the first *tanna*.

At this point the *gemara* asks: *b'mai kamifligi* — what exactly is the dispute between *Rav Huna* and *Ulla*? After all, both seem to be saying the same thing — variation between four and eight — just from opposite ends. The *gemara* points to a dispute in a *baraita* about the length of the leap month. The first opinion: *kama ibur shana* — how long is the month added in a leap year? *Lamed* — 30 days.

### RSbG Flexible

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says: it can simply be a *chodesh* — a month — meaning either 29 or 30 days, as with any other month.

### Seven vs Eight

This dispute is precisely what divides *Rav Huna* and *Ulla*. The entire argument for tolerating eight full months rests on the correction provided by a leap year whose extra month is made 29 days: that 29-day representation of a 29.5-day cycle pulls the calendar back half a day, reducing the apparent two-day error to one and a half days. But if the extra month of a leap year must always be 30 days — as the first opinion holds — then this half-day correction is never available. Making a 30-day month for the leap year pushes the calendar in the other direction. Accordingly, if the leap month is always 30 days, the maximum tolerable deviation is not eight full months but seven. Seven full months, combined with a 30-day leap correction pushing half a day forward, reduces the net error to half a day above one — that is, one and a half days — which is tolerable. But eight would produce a two-day error with no available correction, which is not. *Ulla*, who holds that the maximum is effectively seven full months (since eight can never be corrected), reads the *mishna*'s statement about not going beyond eight as referring to *chaseir* months — because only on that side of the equation does a correction mechanism exist in his framework.

Why does the first opinion require the leap month to always be 30 days? The *baraita* does not elaborate, though one might suggest it reflects a presumption that a standard month, by default, is 30 days — hence the terminology *malei* for a 30-day month and *chaseir* for a 29-day month. A 29-day month is the "lacking" one; when adding an extra month, one adds a full one. But the precise rationale is not discussed in the *gemara*.

## RH & Pesach

### Shavuot Fixed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אִין עֲצֶרֶת חָלָה אֶלָּא בְּיוֹם הָנֵף, וְאֵין רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה חָלָה אֶלָּא אוֹ בְּיוֹם הָנֵף אוֹ לְאוֹר עִיבּוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Huna from a *baraita*: The festival of ***Shavuot* occurs only on** the same day of the week as **the day of waving** the *omer* offering, i.e., the sixteenth of Nisan, which is the second day of Passover. **And Rosh HaShana** also **occurs only on** the same day of the week as **the day of waving** the *omer* offering of the preceding year, **or** on the day of the week that occurs **on the night after** the waving, i.e., the seventeenth of Nisan, which is the third day of Passover.

The *baraita* continues: *Rosh Hashanah* falls only on *yom hanaf* — the same day of the week as the second day of *Pesach* — or on *le'or iburo* — the day following the 30th of *Nissan*, meaning the day after. In other words, *Rosh Hashanah* falls either on the same day of the week as the second day of *Pesach* or one day later — i.e., on the second or third day of *Pesach*.

### Math Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְעוּלָּא, דִּשְׁמוֹנָה חֲסֵירִין עָבְדִינַן, מֵעוּבָּרִין לָא עָבְדִינַן — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ. שְׁנֵיהֶן חָסֵר — בְּיוֹם הָנֵף. אֶחָד מָלֵא וְאֶחָד חָסֵר — לְאוֹר עִיבּוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Ulla,** who says **that we may establish eight deficient** months in a year but **we may not establish** eight **full** months in a year, **you find** that Rosh HaShana can occur on either of the two days mentioned in the *baraita*. There are six ends of months between the *omer* offering and Rosh HaShana. In an ordinary year the months alternate in length, so these six months will comprise three full and three deficient months. If so, Rosh HaShana will occur on the same day of the week as the third day of Passover. If a year has eight deficient months, one of the **two** extra **deficient** months will presumably occur in the summer, between the day of waving and Rosh HaShana, which would mean that Rosh HaShana would occur one day earlier, **on** the same day of the week as the second day of Passover, **the day of the waving** of the *omer* offering. And if no extra deficient months are added that year, but the standard format of alternating lengths of months, **one full and one deficient,** is applied, Rosh HaShana will occur on the day of the week that occurs **on the night after** the waving of the *omer* offering.

The arithmetic is straightforward. The first day of *Nissan* falls on the same day of the week as the second day of *Pesach* (since the 15th of *Nissan* is *Pesach*, and 14 days before is the 1st — both fall on the same day of the week). Between *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* and *Rosh Hashanah* are six months. On average, each month deviates from a perfect number of weeks by one and a half days (since months alternate between 29 and 30 days, averaging 29.5, while a full week is 7 days: 28 is 4 weeks, leaving 1.5 days). Six months times one and a half days equals nine days. Subtract one full week (seven days) and you are left with two days. *Rosh Hashanah* therefore falls two days after the day of *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* — which is the same as the third day of *Pesach*. If, however, one of those six months is a *chaseir* rather than a *malei* month, it pulls the total back by one day, landing *Rosh Hashanah* on the second day of *Pesach*. In our fixed calendar, those six months always consist of exactly three *malei* and three *chaseir*, producing a standard two-day offset — that is, *Rosh Hashanah* on the third day of *Pesach*.

### At Bash Gar

*Rashi* records a powerful mnemonic that captures this relationship and extends it across the entire Jewish calendar year. The device is *at bash gar* — an *at-bash* pairing of the days of *Pesach* with the holidays they correspond to:

The first day of *Pesach* corresponds to *Tisha B'Av* — they always fall on the same day of the week. The second day of *Pesach* is *Shavuot*. The third day of *Pesach* is *Rosh Hashanah* — as proven above. The fourth day of *Pesach* is *Kriat HaTorah*, meaning *Simchat Torah*: this follows because *Rosh Hashanah* (day 1 of *Tishrei*) is the third day of *Pesach*, and the eighth day of *Sukkot* falls on the same day of the week as *Rosh Hashanah*, so the ninth day — *Simchat Torah* — is one day later, which is the fourth day of *Pesach*. The fifth day of *Pesach* corresponds to *Yom Kippur*: *Rosh Hashanah* is the third day of *Pesach*, and *Yom Kippur* is ten days later — which lands on the fifth day of *Pesach* plus seven (one week), equaling the fifth day plus three, i.e., the fifth day of *Pesach* itself. The sixth day of *Pesach* is *Purim*, which can be derived by counting back from *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*. And to complete the picture: the seventh day of *Pesach* corresponds to *Yom Ha'atzmaut*.

## Acheirim Fixed

### 2nd or 3rd Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַב הוּנָא דְּאָמַר: עָבְדִינַן מְעוּבָּרִין, הָא זִימְנִין דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְאוֹר אוֹר עִיבּוּרוֹ! אָמַר לְךָ רַב הוּנָא: וּלְעוּלָּא מִי נִיחָא? שְׁמוֹנָה הוּא דְּלָא עָבְדִינַן, הָא שִׁבְעָה עָבְדִינַן. שִׁבְעָה זִימְנִין דְּלָא עָבְדִינַן בְּסִיתְוָא וְעָבְדִינַן בְּקַיְיטָא, וְהָא הֲוָה לְאוֹר אוֹר עִיבּוּרוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rav Huna, who says: We may establish** eight **full,** thirty-day months in a year, **there are** also **times when you find** that Rosh HaShana occurs **on the night** after **the night** after **the extra day,** i.e., the thirtieth of Nisan, two days after the day of the week of the *omer* offering. This would occur on a Rosh HaShana following a year with eight full months, i.e., two more full months than usual, when one of the extra full months would occur between Passover and Rosh HaShana. In such a case, Rosh HaShana would be delayed from occurring on the same day of the week as the third day of Passover, which is the usual situation, to the same day as the fourth day of Passover. The Gemara responds with a counter objection. **Rav Huna** could **say to you: And does** the *baraita* really **work out well according to Ulla?** Even according to Ulla, **it is eight** full months **that we may not establish** in one year, **but we may establish seven** full months in a single year, one more than the usual. Consequently, there may be **times when we do not establish** the extra full month **in the winter, but we establish** it **in the summer,** between Passover and Rosh HaShana. In such a case, Rosh HaShana would **be two days after** the day of the week of the *omer* offering. Consequently, even according to Ulla, the *baraita* should have mentioned this possibility.

The *baraita*'s limitation of *Rosh Hashanah* to the second or third day of *Pesach* — and not the fourth — creates a problem for both *Rav Huna* and *Ulla*. Even *Ulla*, who limits full months to seven, would allow one extra full month; if that extra full month falls in the six months between *Pesach* and *Rosh Hashanah*, it would push *Rosh Hashanah* a day forward to the fourth day of *Pesach*. And *Rav Huna* would permit two extra full months, potentially shifting *Rosh Hashanah* even further. So how can the *baraita* flatly restrict *Rosh Hashanah* to only two possible days?

### Four Days Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָא מַנִּי? אֲחֵרִים הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין בֵּין עֲצֶרֶת לַעֲצֶרֶת, וְאֵין בֵּין רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לְרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, אֶלָּא אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים בִּלְבַד, וְאִם הָיְתָה שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת — חֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with ***Aḥerim*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: ***Aḥerim* say** that **the difference between *Shavuot*** of one year **and *Shavuot*** of the following year, **and** similarly, the difference **between Rosh HaShana** of one year **and Rosh HaShana** of the following year, **is only four days** of the week. In an ordinary year, when full and deficient months alternate, there are 354 days. Therefore, every year is exactly fifty weeks and four days long, which means that any calendar date one year would occur four days later in the week in the following year. **And if it was a leap year,** in which case the year contains 383 days, or fifty-four weeks and five days, there is a difference of **five** days between them.

The *gemara* resolves this: *ha mani acherim hi* — this *baraita* follows the position of *Acheirim*, elsewhere identified as *Rebbi Meir*. The *baraita* of *Acheirim* states: *ein bein atzeret l'atzeret v'ein bein Rosh Hashanah l'Rosh Hashanah ela arba'ah yamim bilvad* — between one *Shavuot* and the next, and between one *Rosh Hashanah* and the next, there are only exactly four days. *Acheirim* holds that the calendar is entirely fixed, with no permissible variation from the six-and-six norm. On this view, there are never extra *malei* or extra *chaseir* months — the calendar is precise — and therefore *Rosh Hashanah* can only fall on the second or third day of *Pesach*, corresponding to one lacking month or zero extra months in the intervening six.

### Four Day Math

The figure of four days follows from the mathematics of a perfect lunar year. A lunar month averages 29.5 days. Over twelve months, that yields 354 days. Dividing 354 by 7 gives 50 weeks and 4 days. So from one year to the next, the calendar advances exactly four days of the week. This is derived more simply as follows: each month deviates from an exact number of weeks by 1.5 days on average. Twelve months times 1.5 days equals 18 days. Subtract two full weeks (14 days) and the remainder is four. Hence the four-day shift from one *Rosh Hashanah* to the next.

*Acheirim*'s system is thus entirely fixed: the calendar never deviates from six *malei* and six *chaseir*, and the four-day rule always holds. This implies, as *Rav Adda bar Abba* points out to *Rava*, that *Acheirim* is teaching something significant: *d'lo ba'inan mitzva l'kadesh al pi re'iya* — we do not require, as an absolute necessity, that the month be sanctified through the testimony of witnesses. *Acheirim* holds that *beit din* will set the month correctly on its own authority when necessary, ensuring that the calendar never deviates. This is, of course, the very principle underlying our fixed calendar today.

### Adar Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, לַאֲחֵרִים, בְּיוֹם הָנֵף — לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ! אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, וְעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, דַּל יַרְחָא לְבַהֲדֵי יַרְחָא, דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לָהּ בְּיוֹם הָנֵף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately,** the *baraita* remains difficult, because **according to *Aḥerim* you do not find** that Rosh HaShana ever occurs **on** the same day of the week as **the day of waving** the *omer* offering. This is because *Aḥerim* maintain that the months always alternate between full and deficient months and therefore there is never an additional full month between Passover and Rosh HaShana. **Rav Mesharshiyya said** in response: Rosh HaShana can occur on the same day of the week as the day of the *omer* offering, in a case **where it was a leap year and** the intercalated month added to create the **leap year** was **thirty days.** This would cause Rosh HaShana to occur six days of the week later than the year before, but *Aḥerim* maintain that it must occur exactly five days of the week later. Consequently, one of the months after Passover must be changed from a full month to a deficient one: **Remove** from the calculation one full **month** after Passover **corresponding to** the extra full **month** added before Passover in order to ensure that Rosh HaShana occurs five days of the week later than the previous year. This ensures **that you** will **find** a case where Rosh HaShana occurs **on** the same day of the week as **the day of waving** the *omer* offering.

But how does *Acheirim* handle a leap year? If the variation from one *Rosh Hashanah* to the next in a regular year is exactly four days, adding a leap month (*Adar Sheni*) will add more days. *Rav Mesharshiya* explains: *kegon shehayta shana me'uberet ibur shana lamed yom* — if it was a leap year and the extra *Adar* was made 30 days. According to *Acheirim*, this 30-day extra month, combined with what should be a standard year, would create a six-day gap from one *Rosh Hashanah* to the next. To correct this, *dal yarcha l'vadei yarcha* — one removes a month for a month: one of the subsequent 30-day months is made 29 days instead. This pulls *Rosh Hashanah* back by one day, from the third day of *Pesach* to the second day. This is precisely why the *baraita* says *Rosh Hashanah* falls on *yom hanaf* (the second day of *Pesach*) — this is the corrected scenario following a 30-day leap month. *Acheirim*'s system is thus not absolutely rigid in every single year, but it maintains strict control: deviations introduced by leap years must be immediately corrected in the months that follow, so that the gap between *Rosh Hashanahs* never exceeds five days. The key principle is that *beit din* will actively manage the calendar rather than waiting for witnesses to determine it.

## Ravina Objection

### Exact Cycle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא וְהָאִיכָּא יוֹמָא דְּשָׁעֵי, וְיוֹמָא דִּתְלָתִין שְׁנֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בְּכֹל שַׁתָּא לָא קָא חָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina objects to** the opinion of *Aḥerim* for a different reason: **But** how is it possible that there is always a difference of four weekdays between one Rosh HaShana and the next? The length of a lunar month is not precisely twenty-nine and a half days, but twenty-nine days, twelve hours and 793/1080 of an hour. This extra fraction beyond twenty-nine and a half days is compounded month after month and year after year, and as a result, eventually **there is an** additional **day** that must be added to the calendar every three years due to **the** accumulation of **hours, and** an additional **day** that must be added every **thirty years** due to a further accumulation of the extra parts of an hour. The Gemara answers: **Since** these days do **not** appear on the calendar **every year,** the *baraita* **did not account** for them.

*Ravina* raises a fundamental objection to the claim that the gap between one *Rosh Hashanah* and the next is always exactly four days. As *Rashi* notes — counting seven lines from the bottom of the relevant passage — the true gap between years is not four days precisely, but four days, eight hours, and 876 *chalakim*. A *chelek* is one 1080th of an hour, so 876 *chalakim* represents approximately 876/1080 of an hour — roughly 0.8 of an hour. The reason 1080 is used as the base unit is that it is highly divisible, allowing clean fractional representation. The total annual remainder beyond four days is therefore approximately 8.8 hours. This arises because the true lunar month is not exactly 29.5 days but slightly more, and the accumulated excess over twelve months, beyond the four-day figure, comes to this additional 8-plus hours.

### Long Cycles

These extra hours accumulate over time. Since the daily remainder beyond four days is roughly 8 hours, every three years the accumulated excess reaches approximately 24 hours — an additional full day. Every 30 years, the smaller fractional excess (the *chalakim* beyond the eight hours) likewise accumulates to an additional day. So over longer cycles, the gap between one *Rosh Hashanah* and the next is not always four days — sometimes it is five.

## Shmuel Year

### 354 Days Base

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? שְׁנֵיהֶם מְלֵאִין — שִׁשָּׁה, שְׁנֵיהֶם חֲסֵירִין — שְׁנַיִם, אֶחָד מָלֵא וְאֶחָד חָסֵר — אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** An ordinary year, consisting of alternating full and deficient months, consists of 354 days. If **two** months that are generally deficient are made **full,** there are three hundred and fifty-**six** days in the year. If **two** months that are generally full are made **deficient,** there are three hundred and fifty-**two** days in the year. If the months alternate as usual, **one full and one deficient,** there are three hundred and fifty-**four** days in the year. This shows that according to Shmuel it is permitted to change two months from deficient to full months, which leads to a total of eight full months in one year.

*Shmuel* holds like *Rav Huna* — that the calendar may vary between four and eight full months — and his statement about the length of a lunar year confirms this. The calculation: twelve months times 30 days each gives 360 days, but since six of those months are *chaseir* and lose one day each, the true standard is 360 minus 6 equals 354 days. A perfect lunar year is 354 days.

### 352-356 Range

*Shmuel* states: *ein shenat levana pechuta mishlosh me'ot vachamishim ushnayim yom velo yeteira al shlosh me'ot vachamishim veshisha yom* — a lunar year is no fewer than 352 days and no more than 356. Two extra *malei* months add two days to the standard 354, yielding 356. Two extra *chaseir* months subtract two days, yielding 352. One extra of each cancels out, leaving 354. The range of 352 to 356 directly corresponds to the permissible variation of four to eight full months in a year.

### Nazir 354

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר ״כִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה״ — מוֹנֶה נְזִירוּת שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה יָמִים, כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה. ״כִּימוֹת הַלְּבָנָה״ — מוֹנֶה נְזִירוּת שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וַחֲמִשִּׁים וְאַרְבָּעָה יָמִים, כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַלְּבָנָה. וְאִם אִיתָא, זִימְנִין דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ שִׁשָּׁה! בִּנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְרוֹב שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against Shmuel from a *baraita*. If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite** for a duration that is **like the** number of **days** in **a solar** year, **he counts 365 days** for his term of **naziriteship, corresponding to the number of days** in **a solar** year. If he says: **Like the days** in **a lunar** year, i.e., a year comprising twelve lunar months, **he counts 354 days** for his term of **naziriteship, corresponding to the number of days** in **a lunar** year. The Gemara explains the objection: **And if it is so,** that there can be eight full months in a year, there are **times when you find** that a lunar year has three hundred and fifty-**six** days. Why, then, shouldn't the period of naziriteship last for 356 days? The Gemara explains: The *halakha* is that **with regard to vows,** you **follow the** ordinary **language of people.** The meaning of a vow is understood in accordance with the way its words are used in common speech. **And** when people speak of a lunar year, they are generally referring to one that is the length of **most years,** i.e., 354 days.

A *baraita* tests this: if someone takes a vow of *nezirus* for the duration of a solar year (*kiyemot hachama*), he counts 365 days; if he vows for a lunar year (*kiyemot halevana*), he counts 354 days. But if a lunar year can be as long as 356 days, why does he not count 356 to be safe? The *gemara* answers: *binedarim halach achar leshon benei adam* — in matters of vows, one follows common speech and the majority of years. Most people, when referring to a lunar year, mean the standard 354-day year, and so that is the operative figure for purposes of *nedarim*.

## Rabbi's 9 Lacking

### Nine Lacking Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב הוּנָא, דְּתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה וְעָשָׂה רַבִּי תִּשְׁעָה חֲסֵירִים, וְנִרְאָה חוֹדֶשׁ בִּזְמַנּוֹ, וְהָיָה רַבִּי תָּמֵיהַּ וְאוֹמֵר: עָשִׂינוּ תִּשְׁעָה חֲסֵירִין וְנִרְאֶה חוֹדֶשׁ בִּזְמַנּוֹ! אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי: שֶׁמָּא שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת הָיְתָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **also holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna,** that it is permitted to establish eight full months in one year. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: There was an **incident** in which **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **established nine deficient** months in one year, **and** nevertheless **the new moon was sighted in its** proper **time,** on Rosh HaShana, and not later. **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **expressed astonishment and said: We made nine** months **deficient** in one year **and** yet **the new moon was sighted in its** proper **time.** How could this be? **Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said before him: Perhaps** last year **was a leap year**

The *gemara* records a striking historical incident: *ma'aseh ve'asa Rebbi tish'a chaseirim* — there was an incident in which *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) implemented nine lacking months in a single year. This exceeded the stated limit of eight. Yet remarkably, *venira chodesh bizmano* — the new moon appeared precisely at the right time: *Rosh Hashanah* coincided exactly with the *molad*. *Rebbi* was astonished. How could nine lacking months have produced a perfectly timed *Rosh Hashanah*?

### Two Full + Leap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאִישְׁתָּקַד עָשִׂינוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם מְלֵאִים, דַּל תְּלָתָא לְבַהֲדֵי תְּלָתָא וְקָם לֵיהּ בְּדוּכְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נֵר יִשְׂרָאֵל! כֵּן הָוֵה.
>
> **Translation:** **and** the month that was **intercalated,** added for the **leap year,** was **thirty** days, and **last year we turned two** months that are generally deficient into **full** months, which meant that it had eight full months instead of the usual six. Consequently, **remove** from consideration the **three** months that were made deficient this year **corresponding to the three** extra months that were made full last year, **and** the moon is **restored to its place,** i.e., it is properly aligned with the months. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Lamp of Israel!** Indeed, **so it was.**

*Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi*, his son, offered the explanation: *shema shana me'uberet hayta* — perhaps last year was a leap year, and *ibur shana lamed yom* — the added month of that leap year was made 30 days. Furthermore, in the previous year, *asinu shenayim mele'im* — two extra full months had been declared. The result: last year's calendar was running two and a half days ahead. Two extra full months pushed *Rosh Hashanah* two days forward; a 30-day leap month (representing a true 29.5-day cycle) pushed it forward an additional half day. The cumulative error was two and a half days ahead of where the *molad* actually fell.

### 3 Lacking Fix

To correct this accumulated error, *Rebbi* declared nine lacking months in the current year — three more *chaseir* months than the standard six, rather than the usual maximum of two. Three extra lacking months pulled the calendar back by three days. *Da telasa levahadei telasa* — three against three: the three days of correction exactly offset the two-and-a-half-day excess of the previous year, with the slight remainder absorbed within the system's tolerance. *Vekam lei beduchta* — and the calendar was restored precisely to its correct position, which is why the new moon appeared exactly on *Rosh Hashanah*.

### RSbR Confirms

*Rebbi* responded to his son: *ner Yisrael, kein haya* — "Light of Israel, that is indeed what happened." The incident demonstrates two important principles. First, nine lacking months — while beyond the ordinarily stated limit — is permissible when correction of the calendar demands it; the limits of four to eight describe normal operation, not an absolute constraint in cases of deliberate calendrical correction. Second, the incident confirms *Rav Huna*'s position that a calendar can legitimately include two extra full months in a year, since *Rebbi* accepted last year's declaration of *shenayim mele'im* as valid. The parameters of 352 to 356 days, and the range of four to eight full months, reflect the real boundaries of the lunar calendar as it operates — boundaries that *Rebbi* himself navigated with mastery.
