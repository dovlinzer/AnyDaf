# Ketubot 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 50 Shekel Fine & Other Payments

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵיזֶהוּ בּוֹשֶׁת? הַכֹּל לְפִי הַמְבַיֵּישׁ וְהַמִּתְבַּיֵּישׁ. פְּגָם — רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא שִׁפְחָה נִמְכֶּרֶת בַּשּׁוּק, כַּמָּה הָיְתָה יָפָה, וְכַמָּה הִיא יָפָה. קְנָס — שָׁוֶה בְּכׇל אָדָם, וְכֹל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ קִצְבָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה — שָׁוֶה בְּכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **What is humiliation?** How is the payment for humiliation during rape or seduction assessed? It is **all based on the one who humiliated and the one who was humiliated.** The price will vary depending on the lineage of the family of the rape victim and the nature of the attacker. How is her **degradation** assessed? **One considers her as though she were a maidservant sold in the marketplace,** and assesses **how much she was worth** beforehand **and how much she is** currently **worth,** after the rape or seduction. The sum of the **fine is equal for all people, and** the principle is: **Any** payment **that has a fixed** sum **by Torah law is equal for all people,** regardless of the lineage and the physical state of the attacker or the victim.

The *mishnah* with which this *gemara* opens enumerates the full range of monetary obligations that arise in the case of rape. Beyond the well-known fixed payment of fifty *shekel*, the *mishnah* specifies *boshet* (embarrassment) and *pegam* (devaluation), calibrated to the persons involved, as well as *tzaar* (suffering), though this last item will not be addressed in the *gemara*'s analysis. *Boshet* is assessed according to the degree of shame — a function of both the perpetrator and the victim — while *pegam* is determined by recourse to the slave market. The *knas*, the fifty *shekel* fine, however, is fixed for everyone. With this framework established, the *gemara* immediately presses forward with a series of questions about the logical and scriptural basis of these rulings.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: חֲמִשִּׁים סְלָעִים אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, מִכֹּל מִילֵּי! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא יֹאמְרוּ: בָּעַל בַּת מְלָכִים חֲמִשִּׁים, בָּעַל בַּת הֶדְיוֹטוֹת חֲמִשִּׁים?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that **the Merciful One said** that the payment is **fifty *sela* from all** these **matters,** i.e., the fine, degradation, humiliation, and pain. **Rabbi Zeira said:** That cannot be, as **they will say:** If **one who engaged in forced intercourse with a daughter of kings** pays a sum of **fifty** *sela*, does **one who engaged in forced intercourse with the daughter of commoners [*hedyotot*]** also pay **fifty** *sela*?

The *gemara*'s first question is foundational: how do we know that the additional payments of *boshet* and *pegam* are owed at all, beyond the fifty *shekel*? Perhaps the fifty *shekel* was intended to cover everything — the physical violation, the shame, the devaluation — as a flat, comprehensive payment. The *gemara* poses this as a sharp logical challenge: *bat melachim chamishim, bat hedyot chamishim* — should the daughter of a king and the daughter of a commoner each yield the same fifty *shekel* to the rapist? The intuitive sense of fairness rebels against such a result, suggesting that there must be additional variable payments layered on top of the fixed amount.

### Rabbi Zeira's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי עֶבֶד נָמֵי, יֹאמְרוּ: עֶבֶד נוֹקֵב מַרְגָּלִיּוֹת — שְׁלֹשִׁים, עֶבֶד עוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה מַחַט — שְׁלֹשִׁים?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** This is not a decisive argument, as **if so, with regard to a** Canaanite **slave** killed by an ox, the Torah says that the owner of the ox pays the master of the slave thirty *sela*. There too, **they will say:** For **a slave who pierces** precious **pearls [*margaliyyot*],** a valuable skill, the fine is **thirty** *sela*, and for **a slave who performs the needlecraft** of a tailor, a common, less valuable skill, is the fine also **thirty** *sela*?

Rabbi Zeira reframes the question. The problem, he argues, is not the difference in social standing but rather a difference in physical state. Consider the following scenario: two men successively violate the same woman — the first in an unnatural manner (*shelo ke-darkah*), and the second naturally. Because the first act, being anal rather than vaginal, did not rupture the hymen, the woman retains the status of a *betulah* for purposes of the fifty-*shekel* obligation. Thus both men owe fifty *shekel* for violating the same woman. Surely it would be absurd to say, *ba shleimah chamishim, ba pegumah chamishim* — that the man who violated a fully intact woman and the man who violated a woman already compromised by a prior assault each pay an identical amount. This physical distinction, Rabbi Zeira argues, demonstrates that the fifty *shekel* cannot be all-inclusive, and additional variable payments must exist.

One might note that from a moral perspective this reframing is, if anything, more troubling than the original question about social standing. Why it should be "obvious" that the second man's payment ought to be less — merely because the woman had previously been violated — is not self-evident. Yet the *gemara*'s logic rests on the premise that the physical state of the woman's body renders the disparity manifest in a way that social status does not.

### Slave Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי עֶבֶד נָמֵי, יֹאמְרוּ: עֶבֶד בָּרִיא — שְׁלֹשִׁים, עֶבֶד מוּכֵּה שְׁחִין — שְׁלֹשִׁים?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** This too is no proof, as **if so, with regard to a slave as well, they will say** if one whose ox gored **a healthy slave** pays **thirty** *sela*, does one whose ox gored **a slave afflicted with boils** also pay **thirty** *sela*?

Abaye, however, refuses to concede even on these terms. He returns with a counter-analogy: by the case of an ox that gores a slave, the *Torah* likewise fixes a payment of thirty *shekel* regardless of the slave's earning potential or physical condition. *Eved nokev margaliyot shloshim, eved oseh ma'aseh machat shloshim* — the slave who could pierce pearls and the slave fit only for menial work each yield the same payment. That the *Torah* can set a fixed amount regardless of individual circumstance is therefore not logically incoherent. The same might be true of the fifty *shekel* in our case. Abaye's challenge thus stands: you cannot derive additional variable payments from logic alone.

### Scriptural Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר עִינָּהּ״, הָנֵי תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר עִינָּהּ, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** a different proof. **The verse says:** "Fifty shekels of silver…**because he tormented her"** (Deuteronomy 22:29); **these** fifty *sela* are the fine paid **because he tormented her.** From which it **may be inferred that there are** the additional payments of **humiliation and degradation** beyond that sum mentioned in the verse.

Abaye ultimately concedes that the derivation cannot rest on logic and instead grounds it in a verse. The *Torah* states, *tachat asher ina* — he shall pay in exchange for his having oppressed and violated her. Abaye reads this as indicating that the fifty *shekel* is specifically payment for the act of forced intercourse itself. *Michlal d'ika boshet u'fgam* — by implication there must be other payments for other dimensions of the harm, namely the embarrassment and the devaluation, which are not subsumed within the fifty.

### Rava's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָתַן הָאִישׁ הַשּׁוֹכֵב עִמָּהּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה חֲמִשִּׁים כָּסֶף״. הֲנָאַת שְׁכִיבָה חֲמִשִּׁים, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** an alternative proof. **The verse says: "And the man who lay with her shall give to the father of the young woman fifty shekels of silver"** (Deuteronomy 22:29). For **the pleasure of lying** with her he pays **fifty** *sela*; from which it **may be inferred that there are** the additional payments of **humiliation and degradation,** beyond payment of the fine.

Rava arrives at the same conclusion from a different verse: *v'natan ha'ish hashochev imah l'avi hana'arah chamishim kasef* — the man who lies with her shall give to the father of the young woman fifty pieces of silver. The verse explicitly links the act of lying with her to the payment of fifty. From this, Rava reads *hana'at shchiva* — the pleasure of intercourse — as the specific object of the fifty-*shekel* payment. Since that payment covers only that pleasure, *michlal d'ika boshet u'fgam*, the attendant harms of embarrassment and devaluation must generate separate obligations. The label *hana'at shchiva* — the pleasure of the sex act — applied in the context of rape is admittedly arresting, but the *gemara*'s legal point is clear: the fifty *shekel* is narrowly targeted, and everything else falls outside it.

## Who Receives Payment

### Question of Recipient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְדִידַהּ? אָמַר קְרָא: ״בִּנְעוּרֶיהָ בֵּית אָבִיהָ״, כׇּל שֶׁבַח נְעוּרֶיהָ — לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that these additional payments are paid **to her?** The Gemara answers that **the verse says:** "Between a father and his daughter, **being in her young womanhood, in her father's house"** (Numbers 30:17), from which it is derived that **all profits** of **her young womanhood** go **to her father.**

With the existence of *boshet* and *pegam* payments established, the *gemara* turns to the next question: to whom do these payments go? The *Torah*'s explicit statement that the fifty *shekel* goes to the father does not answer this question, since *boshet* and *pegam* are inferred obligations without a stated recipient. Perhaps they should go to the woman herself, who after all is the one violated.

### Seduction Objection

The immediate rejoinder — already flagged by *Tosafot* — is a practical one. In the case of *pituy*, seduction, the *Torah* also mandates *boshet* and *pegam* payments. But if these payments belong to the woman, there is a difficulty: she consented. On what basis would she collect payment for shame and devaluation she voluntarily incurred? The very existence of these payments in the context of seduction proves that they belong to the father, not the daughter. The father did not consent to the seduction of his daughter; his interests were violated regardless of her willingness. Hence the payments must flow to him. This observation also yields the curious implication that even a consenting daughter is considered to have been shamed — since the shame payment is hers and she receives it via her father, not because she personally objects, but because the legal categories operate independently of her consent.

### Shevach Neurim

The *gemara* then asks: even accepting that the payments go to the father, what is the textual basis for this? It attempts to ground it in the verse *b'neureha beit aviha*, written in connection with the father's power to annul his daughter's vows: in her youth, in the father's house. From this the *gemara* tries to extract a general principle — *kol shevach neurim l'aviha*, all benefits that accrue from her youth belong to her father. If that principle were sound, any monetary right arising during her minority would belong to him automatically.

### Earnings to Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִן שֶׁמַּעֲשֵׂה הַבַּת לְאָבִיהָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִי יִמְכּוֹר אִישׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ לְאָמָה. מָה אָמָה, מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ לְרַבָּהּ — אַף בַּת, מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However,** with regard to **that** which **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: From where** is it derived **that the handiwork of the daughter** goes **to her father?** It is **as it is stated: "And if a man sells his daughter as a maidservant"** (Exodus 21:7), juxtaposing his daughter to a maidservant: **Just as a maidservant, her handiwork** belongs **to her master,** as she was sold for that purpose; **so too a daughter, her handiwork** goes **to her father.**

As a parallel case, Rav Huna in the name of Rav asks: from where do we know that a daughter's earnings belong to her father while she is still a *ktana* or *na'arah*? The derivation is by way of analogy to a female slave: just as a slave's handiwork belongs to her master — *mah amah ma'aseh yadeha lerabbah* — so too a daughter's earnings belong to her father — *af bat ma'aseh yadeha le'aviha*. Underlying this is the logic of the father's power to sell his daughter as a maidservant: if he can transfer her labor to another master, that is precisely because he himself is her current master and already owns her productive output. The sale does not create the entitlement; it transfers it.

### Vows Verse Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִ״בִּנְעוּרֶיהָ בֵּית אָבִיהָ״! אֶלָּא, הַהִיא בַּהֲפָרַת נְדָרִים הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Why do I** need this complicated proof? **Let him derive it** directly from the verse **"Being in her young womanhood, in her father's house." Rather,** this cannot be derived from that verse because **that** verse **is written with regard to the nullification of vows.** A young woman is subject to her father's authority with regard to her vows, which he has the right to nullify. The matter of monetary rights is not addressed in that verse.

The *gemara* then turns this against the *shevach neurim* argument. If the verse *b'neureha beit aviha* already established a sweeping principle that all benefits from the daughter's youth belong to the father, then we would never have needed the separate derivation from the slave-sale verse to establish that her earnings belong to him. The very necessity of that second derivation proves that *b'neureha beit aviha* does not function as a general umbrella rule.

### Monetary vs. Ritual Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִינֵּיהּ, מָמוֹנָא מֵאִיסּוּרָא לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say: Let us derive** monetary matters **from** vows, i.e., just as she is subject to her father's authority with regard to vows the same is true with regard to monetary matters, **we do not derive monetary** matters **from ritual** matters.

The reason for this limitation is now given: *mamona me'issura lo yalfinan* — we do not derive monetary rights from ritual obligations. The father's power to annul vows is a matter of *issur*, of religious obligation and its nullification. That power cannot be extended by analogy into the domain of monetary entitlement. Accordingly, *b'neureha beit aviha* tells us only about vows, the slave-sale verse tells us only about earnings, and neither reaches the question of payments for personal injury.

### Fines Cannot Establish Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִקְּנָסָא, מָמוֹנָא מִקְּנָסָא לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say: Let us derive** that her father receives payment **from** the *halakha* of **a fine,** i.e., just as the fine is paid to her father, as explicitly stated in the Torah, so too, other payments are also paid to her father, **we do not derive monetary** matters **from fines.**

The *gemara* then forecloses yet another avenue: *vechi teima neilef miknasa* — perhaps we can learn the father's right to *boshet* and *pegam* from the fact that he already receives the fifty-*shekel knas*? This too fails. *Mamona miknasa lo yalfinan* — monetary compensation cannot be derived from a fine. A *knas* is always treated as a legal exception, not as a model from which general monetary principles can be extrapolated. Thus we know the father receives the *knas*, we know he receives her earnings, and we know he may annul her vows — but none of these sources establishes his right to payments for injury done to her person.

### Father's Control Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּאָבִיהָ הָוֵי, דְּאִי בָּעֵי מָסַר לַהּ לִמְנֻוּוֹל וּמוּכֵּה שְׁחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara explains that **it is reasonable that** the payments of humiliation and degradation are paid **to her father, as if he wished** to do so he could **give her** hand in marriage **to a repulsive** man **or one afflicted with boils,** thereby humiliating her.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty not through scriptural derivation but through a rational inference: *mistavra de'aviha havu* — it stands to reason that these payments belong to the father. The reasoning is as follows. The father has full practical control over precisely those aspects of his daughter's existence that generate *boshet* and *pegam*. He may marry her off to whomever he chooses — even, as the *gemara* notes starkly, to a repulsive or diseased man — and in doing so he controls whether she will suffer shame and whether she will lose her virginity. Since *pegam* arises from the loss of virginity and *boshet* from the experience of shame, and since the father has legal dominion over both, it follows that the associated monetary payments belong to him.

One might note the tension here. The shame paid under the category of *boshet* is presumably the daughter's shame, not the father's — yet she, in the case of consensual seduction, is held to have been shamed even when she consented. And the payment for *tzaar*, physical suffering, is not cleanly addressed by this rationale at all: the father's power to marry her off does not equate to a right to compensation for pain she experiences during an act of rape. The *gemara* does not fully resolve this tension, resting instead on the broader framework of his comprehensive control over her person and status during this period.

## Pegam Valuation

### Virgin vs. Non-Virgin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּגָם — רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא שִׁפְחָה נִמְכֶּרֶת. הֵיכִי שָׁיְימִינַן לַהּ? אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן בֵּין שִׁפְחָה בְּתוּלָה לְשִׁפְחָה בְּעוּלָה לְשַׁמְּשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: How is her **degradation** assessed? **One considers her as though she were a maidservant sold** in the marketplace, and assesses how much she would have been worth beforehand and how much she would be worth currently. The Gemara asks: **How do we assess her** value? **Shmuel's father said: One estimates** the difference **between how much a person is willing to give** to purchase **a virgin maidservant and** how much he is willing to give **to** purchase **a non-virgin maidservant to serve him.**

Having established both that *pegam* exists and that it flows to the father, the *gemara* turns to its practical calculation. The *mishnah* states: *pegam ones k'ilu hi shifcha nimkeret* — the devaluation in the case of rape is assessed as if she were a maidservant being sold. The *gemara* asks how this evaluation is actually conducted. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel explains: we estimate *kamah adam rotzeh liten bein shifcha betulah l'shifcha beulah l'shamshah* — how much more would a person pay for a virgin maidservant than for a non-virgin one, in order to employ her as a household servant.

### Marriage to Slave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ? אֶלָּא בֵּין שִׁפְחָה בְּעוּלָה לְשִׁפְחָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּעוּלָה, לְהַשִּׂיאָה לְעַבְדּוֹ. וּלְעַבְדּוֹ מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ? בְּעֶבֶד שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ לְרַבּוֹ קוֹרַת רוּחַ הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **a non-virgin maidservant to serve him;** if he purchases her for service, **what difference is there to him** whether or not she is a virgin? **Rather,** the difference **between a maidservant who engaged in intercourse and a maidservant who did not engage in intercourse** is with regard to how much one is willing **to marry her to his slave.** The Gemara further asks: **And** with regard to marrying her **to his slave, what difference is there to him** whether or not she is a virgin? The Gemara answers: It is **with regard to a slave from whom his master has** a sense of **satisfaction,** and he seeks a virgin for the slave in order to reciprocate.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this formulation. If the maidservant is being bought merely to work in the household — cooking, cleaning, and so forth — what does it matter to the buyer whether she is a virgin? *Shifcha beulah l'shamshah, mai nafkali minei* — for purposes of domestic service, her prior sexual history is irrelevant. The *gemara* therefore revises the scenario: the valuation is based not on domestic service but on the buyer's wish to marry her to his slave. The master is paying to acquire a wife for his servant, and for that purpose, whether she is a virgin matters. He would pay more for a virgin. The *gemara* then asks why the slave himself cares — the master's goal, one might think, is simply to produce more slaves. The *gemara* answers that the case is one where the master wishes to please a slave he is fond of, and such a master would spend more to give his cherished slave a virgin wife. The entire valuation, then, is constructed around this scenario: the difference in price between a virgin and a non-virgin maidservant, as assessed in the context of being married to a slave.

The application of the slave market to calculate the monetary value of personal injury is of course deeply disquieting. The *halakhah* has no other instrument for assigning a monetary value to a human being, so it employs the only market that treats human beings as commodities — and then applies the differential values that market generates. Even granting that framework, one may ask whether it approximates a fair measure of actual loss.

### Tosafot's Objection

*Tosafot* presses precisely this concern. The problem with the slave market valuation here is that it fails to capture the actual magnitude of the victim's loss. Consider a woman of high social standing who was a virgin: she stood to marry a man of comparable status. Having been raped, that prospect is now compromised. That loss is enormous — far beyond what the difference in price between a virgin and non-virgin maidservant would reflect. *Tosafot* frames the question pointedly: *teima d'mai shuma hi zu v'chi isha chashuvah mimishpacha gedolah einah nifsedet b'fagma ella shaveh shifcha haniset l'eved?* — is this a valuation at all? Does a distinguished woman from a prominent family suffer no greater loss to her *pegam* than the price difference of a maidservant being married to a slave? The slave-market calculation may at least roughly approximate a loss in earning capacity when, say, a limb is severed — because a more able-bodied slave would command a higher price, and that price differential tracks, however crudely, the value of what was lost. But the value of a woman's virginity to her own matrimonial prospects is not mirrored by what it would mean for the same woman to be matched with a slave.

### Tosafot's Solution

*Tosafot* proposes a partial resolution: *kol isha shamin l'fi mah shehi b'inyan zeh* — we assess each woman according to who she is. The slave-market analogy is not applied generically. Rather, one asks: how much more would a person pay for a woman like this one — a woman of her precise social standing and background — in the virgin versus non-virgin state, for the purpose of marrying her to his slave? By factoring in her actual status, the evaluation at least partially accounts for the differences between women. *Tosafot* candidly acknowledges the difficulty remains, but at least places the problem on the table. The slave would still have to be the reference point, and that measure still falls far short of capturing the full social loss — but the intent is to calibrate the slave-market differential to the particular woman in question.

## Sale vs. Fine Dispute

### Rabbi Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מֶכֶר — אֵין קְנָס. וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קְנָס — אֵין מֶכֶר. קְטַנָּה — יֵשׁ לָהּ מֶכֶר, וְאֵין לָהּ קְנָס. נַעֲרָה — יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס, וְאֵין לָהּ מֶכֶר. הַבּוֹגֶרֶת — אֵין לָהּ לֹא מֶכֶר וְלֹא קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any place where there is sale** by a father of his minor daughter as a Hebrew maidservant, **there is no fine** if she is raped. **And any place where there is a fine,** when a young woman is raped; **there is no sale** by the father. The Gemara specifies: **A minor is subject to sale** by her father, **and she is not entitled to a fine** if she is raped. **A young woman is entitled to a fine** if she is raped **and is not subject to sale. A grown woman is neither subject to sale nor entitled to a fine.**

The *gemara* now presents the position of Rabbi Meir, which had been quoted earlier: *kol makom she'yesh mecher ein knas, v'chol makom she'yesh knas ein mecher* — wherever there is sale, there is no fine; wherever there is fine, there is no sale. The two categories are mutually exclusive. This principle structures the entire dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages that follows.

### Mishnaic Enumeration

The *mishnah* in the *braita* spells out Rabbi Meir's position in full. A *ktana*, a minor girl, can be sold by her father as a maidservant but bears no *knas*. A *na'arah* — a girl from the age of twelve until twelve and a half — has a *knas* but cannot be sold. A *bogeret*, a girl past twelve and a half who has reached full maturity, has neither: she cannot be sold nor does any *knas* apply to her.

### Sages' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס בִּמְקוֹם מֶכֶר. דְּתַנְיָא: קְטַנָּה מִבַּת יוֹם אֶחָד וְעַד שֶׁתָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — יֵשׁ לָהּ מֶכֶר וְאֵין לָהּ קְנָס. מִשֶּׁתָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת עַד שֶׁתִּיבְגַּר — יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס וְאֵין לָהּ מֶכֶר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מֶכֶר — אֵין קְנָס, וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קְנָס — אֵין מֶכֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: קְטַנָּה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד וְעַד שֶׁתִּיבְגַּר — יֵשׁ לָהּ קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: This** *halakha* in the mishna is **the statement of Rabbi Meir, but the Rabbis say: She is entitled to a fine** even **where** there is **a sale, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A minor** girl, **from one day old until she grows two** pubic **hairs, is subject to sale and is not entitled to a fine. From when she grows two** pubic **hairs** and becomes a young woman **until she matures** into a grown woman, **she is entitled to a fine and she is not subject to sale;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir, as Rabbi Meir would state** the principle: **Any place where there is a sale there is no fine, and any place where there is a fine there is no sale. And the Rabbis say: A minor** girl **from the age of three years and one day until she matures** into a grown woman **is entitled to a fine.**

The Sages disagree sharply. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav introduces the *braita* establishing this: the Sages hold that sale and *knas* can overlap. A *ktana* from the age of three years and one day — the age at which an act of intercourse has legal significance — through the onset of the two pubic hairs that mark her as a *na'arah* can indeed be sold and also bears a *knas*. The *knas* therefore extends downward to cover minors, overlapping with the period in which sale is possible. According to the Sages, *knas* exists from three years and one day all the way through the *bogeret* stage, without the sharp break Rabbi Meir insists upon.

### Rabbi Meir's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלוֹ תִּהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה״ — בִּמְהַוָּה עַצְמָהּ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir?** It is as **the verse says** with regard to a rapist: "And the man who lay with her shall give to the father of the young woman fifty *sela*, **and to him she shall be as a wife"** (Deuteronomy 22:29). **The verse is speaking with regard to one** halakhically competent to **marry** by **herself,** which means she must be a young woman.

Rav Chisda then examines the scriptural basis for Rabbi Meir's position. The verse in the context of rape states *v'lo tihiyeh l'ishah* — to him she shall be as a wife. Rabbi Meir understands that the verb *tihiyeh* is addressed to the woman herself, indicating that she is someone who can "make herself" a wife — that is, someone who can participate in the act of *kiddushin* in her own right. That describes specifically a *na'arah*, who can accept *kesef kiddushin* on her own behalf, not a *ktana*, who cannot act autonomously in matters of marriage. Thus the verse limits the *knas* to the case of a *na'arah*.

### Sages' Spelling Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״נַעֲרָ״, אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what is the rationale for the opinion of **the Rabbis? Reish Lakish said** that **the verse says "young woman [*na'ara*]";** however, although the term is pronounced *na'ara*, it is written as *na'ar*, without the letter *heh*, and **even a minor girl is indicated** by that term.

Rish Lakish then presents the Sages' counter-derivation. The word *na'arah* in the verse concerning rape is written without the letter *hei* — it appears in a defective spelling, as *naar* without the terminal vowel letter. The Sages read this defective spelling as a deliberate signal that the verse is not restricting itself to a full *na'arah*. Even a *ktana*, who is not a complete *na'arah*, falls within the scope of the *knas*. The absence of the *hei* thus expands rather than restricts.

### Hei Letter Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמְעַהּ רַב פָּפָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חָנָן מִבֵּי כְלוֹחִית, אֲזַל אַמְרַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתּוּן, אַהָא מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ. אֲנַן, אַהָא מַתְנֵינַן לָהּ, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע עַל הַקְּטַנָּה — פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתְנוּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה״, ״נַעֲרָה״ מָלֵא דִּיבֵּר הַכָּתוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa, son** of **Rav Ḥanan, from** a place called **Bei Keloḥit, heard** this *halakha* and **went and said it before Rav Shimi bar Ashi.** Rav Shimi **said to him: You teach this** statement **concerning that** matter. **We,** based on our traditions, **teach it concerning this** matter, as **Reish Lakish said: One who slanders a minor girl,** falsely claiming that she was not a virgin on the wedding night, **is exempt** from paying the fine, **as it is stated: "And they shall give them to the father of the young woman [*na'ara*]"** (Deuteronomy 22:19). **The verse spoke of *na'ara*,** not only pronounced but written **in full,** indicating that this payment is in effect only with regard to a young woman, not a minor girl.

This raises an obvious difficulty: if the absence of the *hei* always implies inclusion of a minor, how do we know that in the case of *motzi shem ra* — where a husband falsely claims his wife was not a virgin — the law applies only to a *na'arah* and not a *ktana*? After all, Rish Lakish himself states *hamotzi shem ra a-ktanah patur* — one who spreads ill repute about a minor is exempt from those special penalties.

The *gemara* addresses this with a two-step argument. In the passage of *motzi shem ra*, the word *na'arah* is written *malei* — in full, with the *hei*. And in any case, as Rav Adda bar Ahava points out, it is obvious that the laws of *motzi shem ra* apply only to an adult woman, because the passage itself contemplates the possibility that if she was found to have committed adultery, she is executed — *v'ktanah lav bat onshin hi*, a minor is not subject to capital punishment. So we know she must be an adult from the content of the passage itself, independent of any spelling. The spelling with the *hei* is not what tells us she is a *na'arah*; we learn that from the reference to execution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״נַעֲרָה״, הָא לָאו הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְאִם אֱמֶת הָיָה הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה לֹא נִמְצְאוּ בְתוּלִים לַנַּעֲרָה. וְהוֹצִיאוּ אֶת הַנַּעֲרָה אֶל פֶּתַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ וּסְקָלוּהָ״, וּקְטַנָּה לָאו בַּת עוֹנָשִׁין הִיא! אֶלָּא: כָּאן נַעֲרָה, הָא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״נַעֲרָ״ — אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava** strongly **objects to this: The reason is that the Merciful One writes "*na'ara*"** with a *heh*. Is that to say that **if that were not the case I would have said that** this *halakha* applies **even** to **a minor girl?** How is that possible? **But isn't it written: "And if this matter was true, that the hymen of this young woman was not found intact; then they shall remove the young woman to the entrance of her father's house and stone her"** (Deuteronomy 22:20–21). **And** this verse clearly refers to a young woman old enough to be punished, as **a minor is not subject to punishment. Rather, here,** with regard to the payment of a slanderer, the verse speaks of **a *na'ara*,** written with a *heh*, and a minor is excluded. This is a paradigm from which **it may be inferred** that in any place **that it is stated "*na'ara*"** without a *heh*, **even a minor girl is indicated.**

What the contrast in spelling does teach us, now that we know from context that *motzi shem ra* applies only to an adult, is how to read the spelling in the rape passage. There the word is spelled without the *hei* — and since we know that a full *hei* corresponds to a full *na'arah*, the absence of the *hei* signals that even a *ktana* is included. *Afilu ktanah bemashma* — even a minor is implied. The two passages thus illuminate each other: the full spelling in *motzi shem ra* confirms adult status there, and that confirmation, read back against the defective spelling in the *knas* passage, yields the Sages' expansive ruling.

## Fines vs. Payments

### Mishnah Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר: ״פִּתִּיתִי אֶת בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ — מְשַׁלֵּם בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says: I seduced the daughter of so-and-so, pays** compensation for **humiliation and degradation** based **on his own** admission, **but does not pay** the **fine.**

The *mishnah* establishes a foundational distinction between the *knas* and the monetary payments of *boshet* and *pegam*. It states: *ha-omer pititi et bitto shel peloni* — one who says "I seduced so-and-so's daughter" — *meshalem boshet u-pegam al pi atzmo*, he pays for the embarrassment and the devaluation on the basis of his own admission. However, *ein meshalem knas* — he does not pay the fine. This ruling instantiates a broader principle of *halakhah* that must be examined carefully.

### Fine Rule

The rule is that a *knas* can only be imposed by a *beit din* on the basis of external testimony. It cannot be self-generated by confession. More than that: even if a person freely admits to an act that would normally carry a *knas*, not only does his admission fail to obligate him — there is a further principle that such an admission may actually exempt him from the *knas* even if witnesses subsequently appear. Our *mishnah* addresses only the narrower point: self-admission is insufficient to obligate payment of a fine. The *boshet* and *pegam* payments, by contrast, are classified as *mamon* — genuine monetary compensation — and therefore the normal rules of monetary obligation apply. Just as one who admits to owing a debt is bound to pay it, one who admits to causing shame and devaluation is bound to compensate for it.

### Domain Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר: ״גָּנַבְתִּי״ — מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרִי אֶת פְּלוֹנִי״, אוֹ ״שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ — הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרִי עַבְדּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ — אֵין מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַמְשַׁלֵּם יָתֵר עַל מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק — אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **one who says: I stole, pays the principal,** the value of the stolen goods, **based on his own** admission, **but does not pay** the **double payment and** the **payment four and five** times the principal for the slaughter or sale of the sheep or ox that he stole. Likewise if he confessed: **My ox killed so-and-so, or:** My ox killed **an ox** belonging to **so-and-so, this** owner **pays** based **on his own** admission. However, if he said: **My ox killed a slave** belonging to **so-and-so, he does not pay** based **on his own** admission as that payment is a fine. **This is the principle: Anyone who pays more than what he damaged,** the payments are fines and therefore **he does not pay** based **on his own** admission. He pays only based on the testimony of others.

The *mishnah* extends this principle into adjacent legal domains to illustrate its scope. *Ha-omer ganavti* — one who says "I stole" — *meshalem et ha-keren al pi atzmo*, he pays the principal on his own admission. He thereby acknowledges that he wrongfully took someone else's money, and that is straightforward monetary liability. However, he does not pay *tashlumei kefel v'tashlumei arba'ah va-chamishah* — the double payment for theft, or the four- and fivefold payment for slaughtering or selling a stolen ox or sheep — because those additional amounts are *knas* and cannot be imposed through self-admission.

Similarly, *hamit shori et peloni* — if someone says "my ox killed a certain person" — he pays on his own admission, because the payment for a goring death (the *kofer*, a ransom-type payment for the loss of life attributable to one's animal) is treated as monetary compensation, not as a fine. If the ox gored another ox, likewise, the owner pays on his own admission.

But *hamit shori avdo shel peloni* — if my ox killed someone's slave — the case is different. The payment for a slave killed by an ox is a fixed thirty *shekel*, regardless of the slave's actual value. This fixed amount means that in cases where the slave was worth less than thirty *shekel*, the payment exceeds the actual damages.

### Fine Criterion

This brings us to the *mishnah*'s governing criterion: *zeh haklal — kol ham'shalem yater al mah she'hizik ein m'shalem al pi atzmo* — this is the rule: whoever pays more than the actual damage does not pay on his own admission. The determinative question is not whether the payment is fixed, but whether it can ever exceed the actual damages. A fixed payment that always falls short of or equals actual damages might still be understood as partial compensation — the *Torah* capping or limiting what you owe. But once there exist scenarios in which the payment exceeds the actual loss, it is clear that the payment is functioning as a fine, a punitive measure imposed by the court, not as compensation. Since some slaves are worth less than thirty *shekel*, the thirty-*shekel* payment for a gored slave sometimes exceeds actual damages — and therefore the entire framework of that payment is treated as a *knas*.

## Seduction Admission

### Rape vs. Seduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי ״אָנַסְתִּי״! לָא מִבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִבַּעְיָא ״אָנַסְתִּי״, דְּלָא קָא פָּגֵים לַהּ, דִּמְשַׁלֵּם בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, אֲבָל ״פִּתִּיתִי״, דְּקָא פָּגֵים לַהּ, אֵימָא: לָא מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And let** the *tanna* **teach** this *halakha* with regard to one who said: **I raped** the daughter of so-and-so. Why did the mishna cite the case of seduction? The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **is speaking** employing the style: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** for the mishna to cite the case of one who says: **I raped** her, **where he does not tarnish her** reputation and merely incriminates himself, as it is obvious that **he pays** compensation for **humiliation and degradation** based **on his own** admission. **However,** in the case of one who says: **I seduced** her, **where he tarnishes her** reputation as he testifies that she willingly engaged in relations with him, and he is not deemed credible to do so, **say** that **he does not pay** based **on his own** admission. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that even in the case of seduction he pays compensation for humiliation and degradation based on his own admission.

Rava raises a question about the *mishnah*'s choice of case. Why does the *mishnah* illustrate the distinction between fine and monetary payment by means of the seduction case rather than the rape case? *Lo mibaya anasti* — if the *mishnah* used rape, it would go without saying that the rapist pays *boshet* and *pegam* on his own admission. After all, by claiming to have raped her, he is placing the wrongdoing entirely on himself — *d'lo pagim lah* — he is not spreading any ill repute about her. There is nothing problematic about accepting such an admission. The case of seduction, however, is more striking and therefore more instructive. When a man admits to seduction, he is simultaneously claiming that the woman consented — and in doing so, *kapagim lah*, he is spreading ill repute about her, implying that she was willing to violate her own modesty. This raises the possibility that perhaps the court should refuse to accept the testimony altogether, to prevent the ill repute from being reinforced by a legal proceeding in which money changes hands. The *mishnah* teaches us, *kama'shma lan*, that we do accept such an admission for purposes of the monetary payments — the court is willing to bracket the accompanying narrative and process the monetary obligation.

### Rabbi Shimon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אַף בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ — לֹא כׇּל הֵימֶנּוּ שֶׁיִּפְגּוֹם בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Even** the payments of **humiliation and degradation, he does not pay** them based **on his own** admission as **it is not within his power to tarnish** the reputation of **the daughter of so-and-so** based merely on his confession.

However, a *tanna* in a *braita* — Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon — takes a more stringent position. *Af poteh she-be-pgam eino meshalem al pi atzmo* — even in the seduction case, he does not pay *boshet* and *pegam* based on his own admission. *Lo kol heimenu she-yifgom bito shel peloni* — we do not extend credibility to someone who comes to court and, by his very admission, devalues and besmirches another man's daughter. In the case of rape, the man's admission does not cast aspersions on her; it condemns only himself, and therefore is accepted. But in the case of seduction, any testimony he offers necessarily implies her willing participation. According to Rabbi Shimon, the court will not be made an instrument for broadcasting that kind of claim, even at the cost of allowing him to pay money he genuinely owes.

### Family Shame Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: נִיחָא לַהּ לְדִידַהּ, מַאי? דִּלְמָא לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְאָבִיהָ. נִיחָא לֵיהּ לְאָבִיהָ, מַאי? דִּלְמָא לָא נִיחָא לְהוּ לִבְנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה. נִיחָא לְהוּ לִבְנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה, מַאי? אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלֵיכָּא חַד בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם דְּלָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** According to Rabbi Shimon, if **she herself is amenable** to his claim, and admits that his version of the events is accurate, **what** is the *halakha*? Is he exempt from payments of humiliation and degradation in that case as well? Abaye responded: **Perhaps her father is not amenable** to his daughter's reputation being tarnished. We therefore do not rely on his statement even in this case. Rav Pappa continued: If **her father is** also **amenable** to his claim, **what** is the *halakha*? Abaye responded: **Perhaps her** other **family members are not amenable,** as the reputation of the entire family would be tarnished. Rav Pappa asked: If the **family members** too are **amenable, what** is the *halakha*? Abaye answered: Even if all the local relatives are amenable, **it is impossible that there** will **not** be at least **one** relative **in a country overseas who is not amenable** to his claim.

Rav Papa then puts a series of escalating questions to Abaye. Suppose the woman herself says she does not mind — she consents to the admission and wants the money. Does that change matters? The *gemara* responds: *dilma lo nicha lei le-avuhah* — perhaps the father is not agreeable to having this said publicly. His honor is also at stake. Suppose then that both she and her father say they are content to accept the money. The *gemara* responds: *dilma lo nicha lehu li-vnei mishpacha* — perhaps other members of the family would not be pleased. Their reputations are implicated even if the immediate parties have made peace with it.

And even if the entire family agrees? The *gemara* concludes that one cannot confidently say there is no family member anywhere who would be harmed: *ee efshar de-leika chad bi-mdinas hayam de-lo nicha lei* — it is impossible that there is not someone, even somewhere distant, who would object. The *beit din* therefore has a broader responsibility, rooted in policy considerations that transcend the preferences of the immediate parties. Even where the father and daughter both consent to the proceedings, the court protects against the spread of reputational harm to the wider family.

This is a striking intersection of policy and *halakhah*. As a matter of pure *halakhah*, one who admits to owing money is obligated to pay. Yet here, in order to shield innocent third parties from the collateral reputational damage caused by airing the admission in court, the *beit din* may decline to act on testimony it would otherwise be obligated to process.

### Tosafot's Distinction

*Tosafot* draws a critical limiting distinction. The restriction articulated by Rabbi Shimon — and the policy concerns the *gemara* elaborates — apply specifically when the perpetrator is the one who comes forward and volunteers the admission. In that scenario, he is the one initiating the spread of ill repute, and the court refuses to be complicit. But *Tosafot* rules that if the father or the daughter is the one who brings the claim, initiating the lawsuit and effectively certifying that the event occurred, the situation is entirely different. In that case, the father himself has chosen to make this public, and the admission cannot be said to harm the family in a way they have not already accepted. *Hainu d'mishum d'kadam tva'u ha-av techilah, ein tzarich lomar lav kol heimenu* — if the father sued first, the rule of "we do not extend him such credibility" simply does not apply, and the perpetrator would be obligated to pay even on his own admission in such circumstances.

## Half Damages Debate

### Rav Papa's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא, רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא — מָמוֹנָא. רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא — קְנָסָא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the payment of **half the damage** that the owner of an innocuous ox, which was not yet witnessed goring a person or an ox three times, must pay to the owner of the ox that he gored. **Rav Pappa said: Half the damage** is considered a payment of **money,** compensation for the damage caused. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said** that **half the damage** is considered payment of a **fine.**

The *gemara* now pivots to a debate that has broad structural implications: whether the half-damages payment for a *shor tam* — a non-habitual goring ox — constitutes monetary compensation or a fine. Rav Papa holds that *palga nizka mamona* — half damages is a monetary payment, genuine compensation. His reasoning is that ordinary unwarned oxen cannot be presumed to be naturally guarded against going out and goring. The owner therefore bears some baseline responsibility to watch his animal. In principle, given that negligence, full damages would be appropriate. The *Torah*, however, had mercy — *Rachmana hu d'chas alei* — because the animal had not yet been officially warned. The owner is barely over the line of negligence, and so the *Torah* reduces the liability to half. But it remains compensation, not punishment.

### Rav Huna's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא מָמוֹנָא, קָסָבַר: סְתָם שְׁוָורִים לָאו בְּחֶזְקַת שִׁימּוּר קָיְימִי. וּבְדִין הוּא דִּמְשַׁלֵּם כּוּלֵּיהּ, וְרַחֲמָנָא הוּא דְּחָיֵיס עִלָּוֵיהּ, דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא אִיַּעַד תּוֹרָא. רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא קְנָסָא, קָסָבַר: סְתָם שְׁוָורִים בְּחֶזְקַת שִׁימּוּר קָיְימִי. וּבְדִין הוּא דְּלָא לִישַׁלֵּם כְּלָל, וְרַחֲמָנָא הוּא דְּקַנְסֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִינְטְרֵיהּ לְתוֹרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **Rav Pappa said: Half the damage** is considered a payment of **money,** as **he maintains: Standard oxen do not exist in the presumptive status of safety,** and therefore are likely to cause damage. **And by right,** the owner **should pay the entire** damage caused by his animal, **and it is the Merciful One that has compassion on him, as his ox is not yet forewarned** until it has gored a person or an animal three times. Fundamentally, the payment is for damage that the animal caused. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said** that **half the damage** is payment of a **fine,** as **he maintains: Standard oxen exist in the presumptive status of safety,** and are not dangerous. **And by right,** the owner **should not pay at all,** as the ox goring could not have been anticipated, and therefore the owner bears no responsibility. **And it is the Merciful One that penalized him so that he would guard his ox.** The sum that he pays is a fine.

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua takes the opposing view: *palga nizka knasa* — half damages is a fine. In his view, unwarned oxen should indeed be presumed to be self-regulated and safe. The owner of a *tam* was therefore not negligent; he had no real reason to believe his animal would gore. *U'vdin hu d'lo l'shalem klal* — strictly speaking, he should owe nothing at all. Nevertheless the *Torah* imposed a fine — *ki heichi d'nintrei l'torei* — to incentivize owners to be more careful with their animals. The half-payment is thus not calibrated to actual fault or loss; it is a deterrent penalty. At its core, the dispute concerns which side of the negligence line the owner of an unwarned goring ox falls on. Rav Papa places him just over the line, making the half-damages a partial compensation. Rav Huna places him just under the line, making the half-damages a fine to encourage greater care.

### Textual Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִימָן: הִיזִּיק, מָה, וְהֵמִית, כְּלָל. תְּנַן: הַנִּיזָּק וְהַמַּזִּיק בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא מָמוֹנָא, הַיְינוּ דְּשָׁיֵיךְ נִיזָּק בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא קְנָסָא, הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָאו דִּידֵיהּ קָא שָׁקֵיל, בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין אִיתֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** for the proofs cited with regard to this dispute: **Damaged; what; and killed; principle. We learned** in a mishna that if an innocuous ox gored and killed another's ox, both **the damaged and the damager** share **in the payments. Granted, according to the one who said** that **half the damage** is a payment of **money; that** is how the **damaged party shares in the payments.** By right, the owner of the dead ox should be compensated for his entire loss. However, since the ox that gored his ox was innocuous, the owner of the gored ox bears half the costs. The mishna characterizes him as sharing in the payments. **However, according to he who said** that **half the damage** is payment of **a fine,** by right, the injured party himself is entitled to nothing. **Now,** the owner **takes** half the damage **that** by right **is not his;** can **he** be characterized as sharing **in the payments?**

The *gemara* brings a textual challenge from the *mishnah* in *Bava Kamma*, which states *ha'nizak ve'hamazik betashlumin* — the person damaged and the damager share in the monetary payments. This language of "sharing" seems to reflect the perspective of compensation: the victim suffers the full loss of the animal but receives only half payment, so he is in effect bearing half the cost himself. The *gemara* notes that this framing is intelligible if half damages is compensation — *bishlama lema'an d'amar palga nizka mamona, hainu d'shaich nizak betashlumin* — there is a meaningful sense in which the victim participates in bearing the financial burden. But if half damages is a fine, the situation is reversed: the victim is already receiving money he is not technically owed, since the owner was not negligent. To speak of the victim "sharing in the cost" when he is in fact receiving a windfall seems incoherent.

### Resolution on Carcass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִפְחַת נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** This *halakha* is **necessary only for the degradation of the carcass.**

The *gemara* resolves this by limiting what the *mishnah*'s language actually teaches. *Lo nitzrecha ella l'pchat neveila* — the statement that both parties share in the outcome addresses specifically the depreciation of the carcass. When the ox is killed by the goring, the victim takes possession of the carcass. If that carcass then depreciates in value over the subsequent days, the loss falls entirely on the victim, not on the owner of the goring ox. In this sense, and only in this sense, does the victim "share" in the financial outcome: the calculation of damages freezes at a certain point, and any subsequent depreciation of the carcass is the victim's problem. This narrow reading of the *mishnah*'s language neutralizes the apparent support for Rav Papa's position, since the *gemara* shows the language can be explained equally well under Rav Huna's fine-based view.

### Omitted Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָה בֵּין תָּם לְמוּעָד, שֶׁהַתָּם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק מִגּוּפוֹ, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה. וְלָא קָתָנֵי שֶׁהַתָּם אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues. **Come** and **hear** an additional proof from a *baraita*: **What is** the difference **between an innocuous and a forewarned** ox? The difference is **that** the owner of **an innocuous** ox **pays half** of the **damage from its body.** Compensation for the damage may be collected only from the body of the ox that gored another ox. If the ox that gored another ox is worth less than half the damage, e.g., if an inexpensive ox killed an expensive one, the injured party receives less than half the damage. **And** the owner of **a forewarned** ox **pays the entire damage from the** owner's **property,** and the value of the ox that gored another ox has no effect on the payment. The *tanna* **did not teach** an additional difference that the owner of **an innocuous** ox **does not pay** based **on his own** admission **and** the owner of **a forewarned** ox **pays** based **on his own** admission. Apparently, the half damage is a payment of money and not a fine.

The *gemara* brings a further challenge from another *mishnah* in *Bava Kamma* that lists the differences between a *tam* and a *muad*. The *tam* pays half damages from the body of the goring animal itself, capped by the animal's own value; the *muad* pays full damages from the owner's best property with no ceiling. Conspicuously absent from this list is any mention of the distinction that the *tam* payment cannot be collected on self-admission while the *muad* payment can — a distinction that would obviously apply if *tam* damages were a *knas* while *muad* damages were *mamon*. This omission appears to support Rav Papa's position that both are *tashlumin* and the fine-versus-payment distinction simply does not exist between them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנָא וְשַׁיַּיר: מַאי שַׁיֵּיר דְּהַאי שַׁיַּיר! שַׁיַּיר חֲצִי כוֹפֶר. אִי מִשּׁוּם חֲצִי כוֹפֶר — לָאו שִׁיּוּרָא הוּא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this claim: This *baraita* is no proof, as the *tanna* **taught** some cases **and omitted** others and did not list all the differences between innocuous and forewarned oxen. The Gemara asks: **What** else **did he omit that he omitted this?** The failure to include an item in a list can be deemed insignificant only if it is one of at least two omissions. If there is only one omission, apparently it was omitted advisedly. The Gemara replies: **He omitted** the *halakha* of the **half ransom** as well.

The *gemara* deflects this with the principle *tana veshayer* — the *tanna* taught the list incompletely and left items off. The *gemara* then asks *mah shayer de-hai shayer* — if something was left off, what else was omitted? The answer: *shayer chatzi kofer* — the question of half-*kofer* was also left off the list. When an unwarned ox kills a person, the question of whether the owner owes half of the *kofer* ransom payment is itself disputed, and that difference between *tam* and *muad* was not listed either. Since the list is demonstrably incomplete in at least one other respect, its silence about the fine-versus-payment distinction carries no probative weight.

The *gemara* adds one further point: even if one sought to use the *chatzi kofer* omission to demonstrate incompleteness, the response would be that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yose HaGelili, who holds that a *tam* does pay half-*kofer*, that distinction between *tam* and *muad* would not exist at all — and therefore its absence from the list might not indicate an omission. In sum, the list of *tam-muad* differences is either incomplete or, under certain opinions, covers the relevant cases, and in neither event does it resolve the underlying debate between Rav Papa and Rav Huna.
