# Meilah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pigul & Me'ilah Status

### Rav Gidal Zrika Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: זְרִיקַת פִּיגּוּל אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא מִידֵי מְעִילָה בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא לִידֵי מְעִילָה בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the misuse of offerings that were rendered unfit, **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav said: Sprinkling** the blood of **an offering** with **the intent to consume it after its appointed time [*piggul*],** in a case where there was *piggul* intent during the slaughter as well, **does not remove** the meat of the offering **from** the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse, in** the case of **offerings of the most sacred order.** The reason is that its meat is still classified as "the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15), since there was no proper sprinkling that permitted the priests to partake of the offering, as it is *piggul*. Similarly, this sprinkling **does not bring** the sacrificial portions **of offerings of lesser sanctity into** the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** as only a valid sprinkling confers that status.

We open on daf 4a, four or five lines from the bottom, in the midst of the position of Rav Gidal amar Rav. The Mishnah had already established that when the sacrificial *avodot* are performed with a *psul* — whether through *machshavas pigul*, *chutz lizmano*, *chutz limkomo*, or a similar defect — two consequences follow. First, the *korban* retains its sanctified status and the principle of *im ala lo yerad* applies. Second, and more directly relevant here, the meat of *kodshei kodashim* does not exit the category of *me'ilah*. Under ordinary circumstances, a valid *zrika* renders the meat *mutar l'kohanim*, which removes it from *me'ilah*. But when the *zrika* is performed *b'psul*, the meat never becomes *mutar l'kohanim* and therefore retains its *me'ilah* status. Rav Gidal amar Rav extends this principle to the case where the *zrika* was performed with *machshavas pigul*: such a *zrika* is not considered a legitimate *zrika*, and consequently the meat remains subject to *me'ilah*. Similarly, in the case of *kodashim kalim*, the *emurim* do not become fit for the *mizbeach*.

### Tosafos Mertzeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּרָא דְּמַיְיתֵי לִידֵי פִיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Ashi: This is not a difficulty, as Ulla did not mean that the intent during the removal of the handful alone rendered the meal offering full-fledged *piggul*. Rather, he meant that **a prohibited act** was performed upon it **which brings** it to a status of ***piggul*,** but the full status of *piggul* is attained only if there is also improper intent at the time of the sacrificing of the handful, which is equivalent to sprinkling in the case of an animal.

The Gemara then asks at length why Rav Gidal amar Rav's ruling requires any special statement at all — does it not follow obviously from the Mishnah itself? Tosafos addresses this by noting that the case of *pigul* is qualitatively different from other *psulim*. By *zrika*, there is a principle of *mertzeh l'pigulo* — the act is in some sense treated as if the *korban* were properly received, at least insofar as it generates the status of *pigul*. There is a theoretical dimension of legitimacy to the *korban* even when it is disqualified by *pigul*. One might therefore have thought that a *zrikas pigul* counts as a legitimate *zrika* for the purposes of *me'ilah* — and Rav Gidal amar Rav comes to tell us that it does not.

### Pigul Early Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָתָנֵי: אִם אֲחֵרִים מֵבִיא לִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, הוּא עַצְמוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Rava's answer to Rav Ashi, the Gemara objects: **But didn't** Ulla **teach** in justification of his opinion: **If** the taking of this handful **brings others,** i.e., the remainder of the meal offering, **into** a status of ***piggul*,** by means of the intent to consume it after its appointed time, with regard to the handful **itself,** is it **not all the more so** that it should be rendered *piggul* by this intent? If so, the same should likewise apply in the case of an offering of lesser sanctity that was slaughtered with the intent to consume it after its appointed time, i.e., it should be rendered *piggul* by this intent alone, regardless of intent during sprinkling.

Challenges were raised against this position, and in the end it was conceded that *pigul* status does exist even at stages prior to the *zrika* — a *shechitas pigul* can, for instance, sanctify the bread of a *todah*. Nevertheless, despite this acknowledgment, a *zrikas pigul* still does not have the effect of changing the *me'ilah* status of the *korban*, because a change in *me'ilah* status requires a genuine *zrika* performed *b'hechsher*. Only a properly executed *zrika* renders the meat fit for *kohanim* or, in the case of *kodashim kalim*, makes the *emurim* fit for the *mizbeach*. Nothing short of that will alter *me'ilah* status.

### Braisa Pigul Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הַפִּיגּוּל לְעוֹלָם מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיזְּרִיק דָּם, וּמְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rav Giddel: With regard to an offering that is ***piggul*,** one who derives benefit **from it** is **always** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. Does this **not** mean that the meat of an offering of the most sacred order is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse **even though** its **blood was sprinkled** with the intent to partake of it or to burn the portions consumed on the altar beyond its designated time? **And** if so, this **supports** Rav Giddel's opinion.

The Gemara now attempts to bring a proof at the bottom of 4a. A *braisa* states: *ha'pigul l'olam mo'alin bo* — with *pigul*, one always remains liable for *me'ilah*. The plain reading is that we are dealing with *kodshei kodashim* in which a *pigul* thought was entertained, and the meat never exits its *me'ilah* status because it never becomes *mutar*. This appears to support Rav Gidal amar Rav straightforwardly — whether *pigul* occurs at an early stage or at the *zrika* itself, it does not change the *me'ilah* status.

### Challenge Zrika Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּלֹא זָרַק הַדָּם. אִי דְּלֹא זָרַק הַדָּם, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** one cannot cite a proof from this *baraita*, as it is possible that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the priest did **not** yet **sprinkle the blood,** and that is why the offering is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. But once the blood is sprinkled with improper intent, the offering is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. The Gemara asks: **If** the *baraita* is dealing with a case **where** the priest did **not** yet **sprinkle the blood, what** is the purpose **of stating** it? Certainly before its blood is sprinkled the offering is included in the category of "the sacred items of the Lord," and is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

The Gemara asks: does *ha'pigul l'olam mo'alin bo* not mean that even where *zrikas hadam* was performed — albeit as a *zrikas pigul* — the *me'ilah* status is still retained? And would that not be a support to Rav Gidal amar Rav, since even a *zrikas pigul* proves insufficient to alter *me'ilah* status?

### Response Legit Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: לְעוֹלָם דְּזָרֵיק, וְכִי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא, בְּעוֹלָה. אִי בְּעוֹלָה, פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּגָבוֹהַּ הִיא כּוּלַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggest an alternative reason for rejecting the suggestion: **Rather,** the *baraita* is **actually** referring to a case **where** the blood was **sprinkled. But** nevertheless, it does not support Rav Giddel's opinion, as **when that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is referring **to a burnt offering.** A burnt offering is always subject to the *halakhot* of misuse even after its blood is sprinkled, as it is never permitted to the priests in consumption. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If** the ruling of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to a burnt offering,** then it is **obvious** that it is still subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, **as it entirely** belongs **to the Most High,** i.e., the entire offering is burned on the altar and none of it is consumed by the owners or priests.

The Gemara deflects: perhaps the *braisa* is speaking of a case where no *zrikas hadam* took place at all — where the *pigul* thought occurred at an earlier stage and there was never a *zrika*. But this answer itself invites an objection: if there was simply no *zrika*, why mention *pigul* at all? Without any *zrika*, *me'ilah* status obviously does not change, whether *pigul* is present or not, and whether one is dealing with *kodashim kalim* or *kodshei kodashim*. The word *l'olam* would be entirely without content.

The Gemara therefore suggests that perhaps the *braisa* is discussing an *olah*. By an *olah*, the meat is always designated for the *mizbeach* both before and after *zrika* — there is never a change in *me'ilah* status regardless of what *machshava* was entertained. Of course, *peshita d'gavoha hi kulla* — it is self-evident that for an *olah* the entire matter belongs to the Divine, before and after *zrika*, and the mention of *pigul* adds nothing. The Gemara therefore finds this reading unsatisfying as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: לָן דָּמָהּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחָזַר וּזְרָקוֹ – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** one may cite proof for the opinion of Rav Giddel from that *baraita*, **as the latter clause** of the *baraita* **teaches:** If **its blood was left overnight** instead of being sprinkled on the altar on the day it was slaughtered, **even though** the priest **later sprinkled it** on the following day, nevertheless, one who derives benefit **from it** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property.

The *seifa* of the *braisa* then enters the discussion: if the blood was left overnight (*linas hadam*) and you subsequently went ahead and performed the *zrika*, there is still *me'ilah* — *af al pi shechazar v'zarka, mo'alin bah*. This is plainly because no valid *zrika* was performed on the original blood, and therefore the meat cannot become *mutar ba'achilah*. Crucially, the implication is that had the blood been thrown in time, the *me'ilah* status *would* have changed — which means we are obviously not dealing with an *olah*, where *me'ilah* never changes. The *seifa* thus makes clear that the *braisa* is discussing a *chatat*, where proper *zrika* would indeed remove *me'ilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּחַטָּאת – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּעוֹלָה – צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement of the *baraita*: **Granted, if you say** that the *baraita* is referring **to a sin offering,** then it is **well.** Since the blood was disqualified for sprinkling by being left overnight, it does not become permitted to the priests even after the blood is sprinkled, and is therefore subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. But if the blood had not been left overnight, the offering would not be subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, as the sprinkling would have removed the sin offering from that category due to the fact that it became permitted for the priests. **But if you say** that the *baraita* is referring **to a burnt offering,** does it **need to be said** that it is still subject to the *halakhot* of misuse? A burnt offering remains subject to the *halakhot* of misuse even if the blood had not been left overnight and the sprinkling was performed properly, as it never becomes permitted to the priests.

Since the *seifa* is clearly discussing a *chatat*, the Gemara asks whether the *reisha* might be read by analogy as also discussing a *chatat* in the context of *pigul* — and would that not support Rav Gidal amar Rav? The Gemara resists this inference, however, noting that the cases are not perfectly parallel. Overnight blood involves an objectively defective act: the blood is physically old, and there is an active failure. By contrast, *machshavas pigul* involves nothing physically wrong — the entire defect is in the *kohen*'s thought. One might therefore have distinguished the two and argued that where everything is physically correct and only a thought is at issue, the *zrika* should still be sufficiently valid to change *me'ilah* status. The Gemara articulates this counterintuitive point: leaving blood overnight, though it seems passive, is in a certain sense more active, because it occurs in the realm of external action — something was supposed to be done and was not done, or was done at the wrong time. A mental defect, by contrast, leaves the physical world undisturbed, and perhaps a *zrika* that is physically perfect, marred only by *machshava*, is meaningful enough to change the status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא לָאו וַדַּאי מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא הֲלָנָה – דְּקָעָבֵיד בְּיָדַיִם, לָא מַהְנֵי זְרִיקָה לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִידֵי מְעִילָה. מַחְשָׁבָה – לָא קָא עָבֵיד בְּיָדַיִם, מַהְנֵי לֵיהּ זְרִיקָה לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִידֵי מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: It is **not** even **certain** that **the latter clause supports** Rav Giddel, as it is possible that although the sprinkling of blood that was left overnight does not remove an offering from being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, nevertheless, sprinkling after *piggul* does achieve this. The Gemara clarifies: **What is the difference** between the two cases? The difference is with regard to **leaving** the blood **overnight, where it is performed in** relation to **an action,** i.e., the priest failed to act in the proper manner, **sprinkling** the blood **is not effective to remove** the offering from the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** By contrast, in the case of sprinkling after *piggul*, where the disqualification is merely a function of **intent,** which **is not performed in** relation to **an action,** perhaps **sprinkling** the blood **is effective to remove** the offering from the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.**

Nonetheless, the *braisa*'s simple sense still appears to be that it discusses a *chatat* with a *pigul* thought and that *me'ilah* status does not change — constituting genuine support for Rav Gidal amar Rav.

### Braisa Kodshei Kadashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא הָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הַפִּיגּוּל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּזָרַק וּמְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ! לָא, דְּלֹא זָרַק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let** us **say** that **this** *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rav Giddel: With regard to **an offering of the most sacred order that** is ***piggul*,** one who derives benefit **from it** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. Does this **not** mean that it is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse **even though** the priest **sprinkled** its blood, **and** if so this *baraita* **supports** Rav Giddel's opinion? The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** one cannot cite a proof from this *baraita*, as it is possible that it is referring to a case **where** the priest did **not** yet **sprinkle the blood.**

The Gemara attempts the proof once more from a second *braisa*: *ha'pigul b'kodshei kodashim mo'alin bo* — with *pigul* in *kodshei kodashim*, there is *me'ilah*. The same structure of argument follows: does this not mean that even after *zrikas hadam* with *machshavas pigul*, the *me'ilah* status is retained? The Gemara again deflects — perhaps no *zrika* was performed. And if no *zrika* was performed, again the question arises: what is the *chiddush*? Without any *zrika*, of course there is *me'ilah*.

### Before After Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל זָרַק, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ? אַמַּאי קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if that is so, **what,** then, is the *halakha* if the priest did **sprinkle** the blood? Is the *halakha* **indeed that** one is **not** liable for **misusing it?** If so, **why is it taught** in **the latter clause** of the *baraita*: Unlike an offering of the most sacred order, **in** the case of **an offering of lesser sanctity,** one who derives benefit **from it** is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property?

The *seifa* of this second *braisa* states that by *kodashim kalim* with *pigul*, there is no *me'ilah* — *ein mo'alin bo*. The Gemara presses: if one is looking for a case where there is no *me'ilah*, why go all the way to *kodashim kalim*? One could have drawn the distinction within *kodshei kodashim* itself: before *zrika*, there is *me'ilah*; after *zrika* — even a *zrikas pigul* — there is no *me'ilah*, because the *zrikas pigul* changes the status. Why was this example not used, if indeed *zrikas pigul* is sufficient to change status?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִיפְלוֹג בְּרֵישָׁא, וְלִיתְנֵי: לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ! הָהוּא וַדַּאי מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: If there is a difference between whether or not the priest sprinkled the blood, **let** the *baraita* **distinguish and teach** the distinction **in the first clause,** with regard to the case of offerings of the most sacred order itself, as follows: **Before** the **sprinkling** of the blood one is liable for **misusing** the offering, but **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** It is not necessary to mention offerings of lesser sanctity at all. The Gemara concludes: **That** statement in the latter clause of the *baraita* **certainly supports** Rav Giddel's opinion.

The answer the Gemara reaches is that *kodashim kalim psika lei* — the case of *kodashim kalim* is categorical. With a *pigul* thought and *kodashim kalim*, there is never *me'ilah* under any circumstances: not before *zrika* because it is *kodashim kalim* and the *emurim* were not yet fit for the *mizbeach*, and not after *zrikas pigul* because that *zrika* does not change the status of the *emurim*. By contrast, one might theoretically argue that by *kodshei kodashim* after *zrikas pigul*, the status does change — and therefore the example does not work there as cleanly. So the *braisa* chose *kodashim kalim* because there *me'ilah* never applies across the board when *pigul* is present. But this does not actually prove what the *reisha* was talking about.

### Pigul Not Meaningful

The conclusion at this stage of the Gemara is this: these *braisot* make it quite clear that a *pigul* thought does not alter *me'ilah* status — whether one is speaking of raising the level of *emurim* of *kodashim kalim* or of lowering the level of meat in *kodshei kodashim*. The whole *Mishnah* already teaches that *avodot* done *b'psul* do not change the status. The reason the Gemara invested so much effort in exploring this is precisely because *pigul* paired with *zrika* seemed to occupy a special category: since *zrika* performed with *machshavas pigul* is treated as if it were *meratzeh* for the purpose of generating *pigul* status, perhaps it should be considered a meaningful enough *zrika* to change *me'ilah* status as well. The Gemara ultimately concludes that it does not — the *emurim* of *kodashim kalim* are not raised, and the meat of *kodshei kodashim* is not lowered out of *me'ilah*, unless a genuinely valid *kesherah zrika* was performed.

## Me'ilah Status Change

### Yehoshua General Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כֹּל שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ, וְכֹל שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehoshua said a principle** with regard to misuse of disqualified sacrificial animals: With regard to **any** sacrificial animal **that had a period of fitness to the priests** before it was disqualified, one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** Misuse fundamentally applies to items consecrated to God, which are not permitted for human consumption at all. Once the offering was permitted for consumption by the priests, it is no longer in that category. **And** with regard to **any** sacrificial animal **that did not have a period of fitness for the priests** before it was disqualified, one is liable for **misusing it,** as it remained consecrated to God throughout.

This brings us seamlessly into the next section of the Mishnah. Having established that *me'ilah* status changes only through a valid *zrika*, the Mishnah now articulates the precise moment at which that change takes effect. Rabbi Yehoshua states a general principle: *kol she-yesh lah she'at heter la-kohanim ein mo'alin bah* — wherever the meat had a moment during which it was permitted to the *kohanim*, *me'ilah* no longer applies. And conversely, where the meat never had such a moment of permissibility, it retains its *me'ilah* status.

### Fit Then Defective

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזוֹהִי שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים? שֶׁלָּנָה, וְשֶׁנִּטְמֵאת, וְשֶׁיָּצָאת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna clarifies: **Which is** the sacrificial animal **that had a period of fitness for the priests?** This category includes a sacrificial animal **whose** meat **remained overnight** after it was properly sacrificed, and was therefore disqualified as *notar*, **and** one **that** was disqualified when it **became ritually impure** after it was properly sacrificed, **and** one **that left** the Temple courtyard after it was properly sacrificed and was thereby disqualified. All of these disqualifications transpired after consumption of the sacrificial meat was permitted, and therefore one who derives benefit from these offerings is not liable for misuse.

Rabbi Yehoshua's principle is illustrated with examples of meat that was originally fit and then became defective. *She-lanah* — the meat lay overnight and became *notar*. *She-nitme'ah* — it became *tamei* after slaughter. *She-yatzah* — it left the *Beis HaMikdash* and became disqualified through *psul yotzei*. In each of these cases, there was a moment during which the meat was fit to be eaten, and therefore *me'ilah* no longer applies even after the subsequent *psul*.

### Never Fit Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזוֹהִי שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר לַכֹּהֲנִים? שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ, וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמָהּ, וְשֶׁקִּבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִין וְזָרְקוּ אֶת דָּמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And which is** the sacrificial animal **that did not have a period of fitness for the priests?** It is a sacrificial animal **that was slaughtered** with the intent to partake of it or sprinkle its blood or sacrifice its sacrificial portions **beyond its** designated **time, or outside its** designated **area, or** one **that** those **unfit** for Temple service **collected and sprinkled its blood.** All of these disqualifications occurred before consumption of the sacrificial meat was permitted by means of the sprinkling of the blood. Therefore, these offerings remain consecrated to God and one is liable for misuse if one derives benefit from them.

The counterexamples — where the meat never had a moment of permissibility — include *she-nishcheta chutz lizmana ve-chutz limkoma*: slaughter with an intent of *pigul*, either for the wrong time or the wrong place. From the very moment of *shechita* the meat was unfit. Similarly included is the case of *u-she-kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah* — where *psulim* caught the blood or sprinkled it. In all such cases the meat was never *mutar* and retains its *me'ilah* status.

### Bar Kappara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּר קַפָּרָא לְבַר פְּדָת: בֶּן אֲחוֹתִי, רְאֵה מָה אַתָּה שׁוֹאֲלֵנִי לְמָחָר בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ: הֶיתֵּר שְׁחִיטָה שָׁנִינוּ,
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this matter, the Gemara relates that **bar Kappara said to bar Pedat: My sister's son, look** into the topic of the question **that you will ask me tomorrow in the study hall.** The question involved the interpretation of Rabbi Yehoshua's statement: Any sacrificial animal that had a period of fitness to the priests. Was it **fitness** of **slaughtering** that **we learned,** i.e., provided that it was slaughtered properly, even if the offering was later disqualified, it is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

At this point, Bar Kappara feeds his nephew Bar Padda a question to ask the following day in the *Beis HaMidrash*. The question, which Bar Kappara clearly intends to use as a prompt for discussion, is: when the Mishnah says the meat had a *she'at heter*, what precise moment does this refer to? Three possibilities are proposed. *Heter shechita*: once the *shechita* was performed correctly, that is sufficient — even if the *psul* came immediately afterward. *Heter zerika*: once the blood was in a state where it could have been sprinkled — that is, once the *kabbalah* was properly performed — that is the relevant threshold, based on the principle that *kol ha'omed lizrok ke-zruk dami*. *Heter achilah*: only once the meat actually became fit for consumption in practice. The plain reading of the Mishnah seemed to point toward *zerika*, but this question is now placed under scrutiny.

### Chizkiya vs Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ הֶיתֵּר זְרִיקָה שָׁנִינוּ, אוֹ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ? חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: הֶיתֵּר שְׁחִיטָה שָׁנִינוּ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** was it **fitness** of **sprinkling** of the blood that **we learned,** i.e., once the blood is ready to be sprinkled, even if it was not sprinkled yet, the offering is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse? **Or** was it **fitness** of **consuming** the meat of the offering that **we learned?** In other words, is it only after the blood was actually sprinkled properly, and the meat of the offering is permitted to be consumed by the priests, that the offering is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse? The Gemara cites the different opinions with regard to this matter: **Ḥizkiyya says:** It was **fitness** of **slaughtering** that **we learned,** whereas **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It was **fitness** of **consuming** the meat of the offering that **we learned.**

When the question is posed in the *Beis HaMidrash*, Bar Kappara presents it as a dispute between two *amoraim*. Chizkiya says: *heter shechita shaninu* — the critical moment is the valid *shechita*. Rabbi Yochanan says: *heter achilah shaninu* — only when the meat actually became fit for eating has the threshold been crossed. Notably, no *amora* explicitly advances the position of *heter zerika*, and it will emerge that this term, in the language of the Gemara, actually refers to the moment of *kabbalah* — the blood having been properly received — rather than to the *zrika* proper. *Heter zerika* means the blood was in a state fit for being *nizrak*, which is determined by whether the *kabbalah* was done correctly.

### Zeira Not Precise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: לָא דַּיְיקָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּחִזְקִיָּה וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further discusses this matter. **Rabbi Zeira says: The mishna is not precisely in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥizkiyya nor** precisely **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** i.e., a close reading of the mishna does not conform to either of their interpretations of the phrase: A period of fitness.

Rabbi Zeira weighs in and observes that the Mishnah does not support either position precisely: *lo daika masnisin d'lo ke-Chizkiya u'd'lo ke-Rabbi Yochanan*. This is already somewhat surprising, since one might have assumed the Mishnah's language clearly supports Rabbi Yochanan's position — the meat had to actually be *mutar ba'achilah*. Rabbi Zeira argues otherwise.

### Meat or Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: שֶׁלָּנָה, וְשֶׁנִּטְמֵאת, וְשֶׁיָּצָאת. לָאו דְּלָן דָּם, וְקָתָנֵי: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר זְרִיקָה שָׁנִינוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira elaborates: **We learned** in the mishna, in its list of examples of offerings that were disqualified after a period of fitness: A sacrificial animal **that remained overnight** after its blood was sacrificed on the altar and was therefore disqualified as *notar*, **and** one **that** was disqualified when it **became ritually impure, and** one **that left** the Temple courtyard and was thereby disqualified. **Isn't** the case of remaining overnight referring to a situation **where the blood remained overnight** and was not sprinkled after it was collected in a cup? **And** yet the mishna **teaches:** One is **not** liable for **misusing it,** as there was a period of fitness. **And** therefore, one can **learn from** the mishna that it was **fitness** of **sprinkling** of the blood that **we learned.**

Rabbi Zeira's argument is based on close reading of the Mishnah's language. The Mishnah lists *she-lanah, she-nitme'ah, she-yatzah* as examples of meat that once had a *she'at heter*. One might assume the natural reading is that the *meat* lay overnight and became *notar*, or the *meat* became *tamei* — in which case the meat was first *mutar* and then became *pasul*, fitting Rabbi Yochanan's *heter achilah* position. But Rabbi Zeira argues that *lav delan dam* — perhaps what lay overnight was the *blood*, not the meat. Under this reading, the *kabbalah* was performed correctly, and then the blood spent the night and became *lina*; the *zrika* was therefore impossible. Yet the Mishnah says *ein mo'alin bah* — *me'ilah* no longer applies. Why? Because the *kabbalah* was done correctly — and if *heter zerika* (i.e., valid *kabbalah*) is the threshold, once that is reached, subsequent *psul* to the blood is no longer the meat's concern for purposes of *me'ilah*. This would not support Rabbi Yochanan's *heter achilah* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּלָן בָּשָׂר, אֲבָל דָּם אִיזְדְּרִיק, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָתָנֵי: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **No,** the mishna is not referring to a case where the blood had not been sprinkled. Rather, it is dealing with a case **where the meat was left overnight, but the blood had** already **been sprinkled** properly. It is **due to this** that the mishna **teaches:** One is **not** liable for **misusing it,** as there was a period of fitness of consuming the meat, as stated by Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The Gemara acknowledges that the Mishnah can also simply be read as discussing the *meat* — the meat became *tamei*, the meat went out — and under that reading the *zrika* was done correctly, the meat was briefly *mutar*, and then the *psul* occurred. But it emphasizes that this ambiguity means the Mishnah does not clearly confirm either position.

### Stock Phrase Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי, אִילֵּימָא דִּזְרָקוּהוּ פְּסוּלִים וְקִבְּלוּהוּ פְּסוּלִים – לְמָה לִי עַד דְּאִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּקִבְּלוּהוּ פְּסוּלִים וּזְרָקוּהוּ כְּשֵׁרִים, וְקָתָנֵי מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר זְרִיקָה שָׁנִינוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** referred to in the mishna, with regard to people who are unfit for collecting and sprinkling the blood? **If we say** that the mishna is dealing with a case **where** the blood was both **sprinkled** by those who are **unfit** for Temple service **and** the blood was also **collected** by those who are **unfit** for Temple service, this is difficult: **Why do I** need to say that it is not disqualified **until there are two** actions, collecting and sprinkling, which were performed improperly? After all, the offering is disqualified as soon as those who are unfit for Temple service collect the blood. **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where** the blood was **collected** by those who are **unfit** for Temple service **but sprinkled** by those who are **fit? And** the mishna thereby **teaches** us that it is only in this case that one is liable for **misusing it.** This is due to the fact that there was no period of fitness for sprinkling the blood. By contrast, had the blood been collected by those who are fit for Temple service, where the blood is fit for sprinkling, the offering would no longer be subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, even if it was sprinkled by those who are unfit for Temple service. If so, one can **learn from** the mishna that it is **fitness** of **sprinkling** of the blood that **we learned.**

The same ambiguity applies to the Mishnah's case of *she-kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah*. The natural reading might present two independent scenarios: either *psulim* caught the blood, or *psulim* sprinkled the blood. Under either reading, the blood was never valid, and *me'ilah* is retained. This would suggest that the *zrika* stage is itself critical. But the Gemara argues that the *vav* should be read conjunctively — as one scenario: *psulim* caught the blood *and* then it was sprinkled — with the implication that the *psulim* caught it and *kesherim* performed the *zrika*. The only problem in this reading is the *kabbalah*, and yet *me'ilah* still applies. This would suggest that *kabbalah* is the critical moment: if the *kabbalah* was defective, it does not matter that the subsequent *zrika* was valid. The Gemara acknowledges, however, that this is a forced reading; one could just as naturally read it as *kiblu psulim o zarku psulim* — two separate *or* scenarios. It is not at all a decisive proof.

## Psul Shirayim

### Rav Yosef Zevachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אִיכָּא לְפַלּוֹגֵי הָכִי, הָא דִּתְנַן הָתָם: חַטָּאת פְּסוּלָה – אֵין דָּמָהּ טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס, בֵּין שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר וְנִפְסְלָה, וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר וְנִפְסְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this** interpretation of the mishna: **But if** it **enters your mind** that **there is** a way **to make this distinction** in the mishna, between a case where the blood was collected by those who are unfit for Temple service and sprinkled by those who are fit and a situation where it was collected by those who are fit for Temple service and sprinkled by those who are unfit, then this would present a difficulty with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna **there** (*Zevaḥim* 92a). The Gemara cites the relevant mishna: With regard to **a disqualified sin offering, its blood does not** cause a garment to **require laundering whether** the offering **had a period of fitness** when its blood was fit for presentation **or whether it did not have a period of fitness.** In either case, as the offering is currently unfit, its blood does not require laundering.

Rav Yosef challenges this entire line of argument. He points out that if one is prepared to read *she-kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah* with such precision — honing in on the *kabbalah* as the determining moment — then one must reckon with the same phrase appearing in a completely different *halacha* in Zevachim, where it manifestly cannot be read precisely. The *halacha* discussed there is *chatat pesula ein dammah ta'un kibus* — if a *chatat* is *pasul*, its blood, should it spatter onto a garment, does not require the special ritual of laundering mandated by the Torah for the blood of a valid *chatat*.

### Stock Phrase Alert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי זוֹ הִיא שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר וְנִפְסְלָה? שֶׁלָּנָה, וְשֶׁנִּטְמֵאת, אוֹ שֶׁיָּצְאָה. אֵיזוֹהִי שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר? שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה חוּץ לִמְקוֹמָהּ, חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ, וְשֶׁקִּבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִים וְזָרְקוּ אֶת דָּמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna proceeds to explain: **Which** offering **is** the disqualified sin offering **that had a period of fitness and was** then **disqualified?** It is one **that was left overnight** and then became disqualified as *notar*; **or** it is one **that became ritually impure; or** it is one **that left** the Temple courtyard. **Which** offering **is** the disqualified sin offering **that did not have a period of fitness?** It is one **that was slaughtered** with the intent to eat it or present its blood **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area; or** it is one whose blood was **collected** by people **disqualified** for Temple service **and** they **sprinkled its blood.**

Rav Yosef argues that in the context of that *braisa* about *kibus*, the phrase *ve-kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah* cannot possibly be honing in on the *kabbalah* stage to the exclusion of the *zrika* stage. Why? Because there is a separate *derasha* — *keri ka-an asher yize mi-dammah* — that teaches that once the blood of a *chatat* has already been sprinkled on the *mizbeach*, any remaining blood no longer has the power to affect a garment. This means that if both the *kabbalah* and the *zrika* were performed by *kesherim*, the blood of a *chatat* that splatters afterward would not require *kibus* regardless — not because the *chatat* was *pasul*, but simply because the *zrika* already took place and the remaining blood is *shirayim*. Therefore, in the *braisa* about *kibus*, the moment of *zrika* is entirely irrelevant to the question of whether *kibus* applies. The phrase *ve-kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah* cannot therefore be read as two carefully distinguished stages; it must simply be a *stock phrase* that was used loosely.

### Rav Assi Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַשִּׁי לְרָבִינָא: אִי בְּדִקְנָא דַּאֲנַן עֲבֵיד, אָמַר רָבִינָא, אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי:
>
> **Translation:** [This section is not directly part of the Rav Assi objection but relates to the broader discussion. The next substantive response from Rav Assi regarding the stock phrase comes shortly:]

Rav Assi objects to the stock phrase theory. *Im ken lama li lematni tartei?* — if both appearances of *kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah* are stock phrases, where did the phrase originate? A stock phrase must be precise somewhere before it can be used loosely elsewhere. You cannot have two imprecise uses of the same phrase without at least one precise use. Therefore, he argues, it must be that in our Mishnah on *me'ilah* the phrase is used precisely, and that it was then borrowed and applied more loosely in the *braisa* on *kibus*. From this it follows that our Mishnah is indeed honing in on the *kabbalah* stage, as the *zrika* would already have changed the analysis.

### Kri'i Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכִי מִיתְנַן הָתָם בִּצְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר דִּמִשּׁוּם הַכִּנּוּס בִּשְׁלָמָא וּמִשּׁוּם הַזְּרִיקָה וְאִי בִּשְׁלָמָא הָכִי דִּכְתִיב קְרִי בַּתּוֹרָה. אָבָל אִם לָא הָיָה הִנָּצֶל בַּדָּם וְנִשְׁמַר בַּדָּם טוֹב וְקִיְּמוֹ הַדָּם, שֶׁנִּשְׁמַר אִם הָיָה בִּקְנַת הַדָּם קַיָּם, הַדָּם נִשְׁמַר אִם הָיָה קַיָּם בְּשׁוּם צוּרָה דְּשׁוּם צּוּרָה שֶׁהוּא דָּם צָרִיךְ הִנָּצֶל בַּדָּם, אָז הַשְׁמָרוֹת וּבַדָּם הַיָּשָׁן קַיָּם וְהַדָּם קַיָּם בִּשׁוּם צוּרָה צָרִיךְ הִנָּצֶל.

The Gemara, however, does not concede that the *braisa* about *kibus* is imprecise even on its own terms. Rather, it argues that we actually do want to introduce *zrika* into the *braisa* — not to distinguish the *kabbalah* from the *zrika* as stages of *psul*, but to convey a separate principle: *d'psul oseh shirayim* — that even a *zrika* performed by a *psul* person makes the remaining blood into *shirayim*.

Here is the scenario. The blood is caught by a *psul* person, who then performs the *zrika*. A second *kohen* — a *kasher* one — also caught some of the blood. The question is whether the *zrika* of the *psul* person is considered a *zrika* at all for the purpose of rendering the remaining blood in the second *kohen*'s possession as *shirayim* — remainder blood that is no longer usable for *matir* purposes and may not be thrown on the *mizbeach*. If the *psul* person's *zrika* is treated as a nullity, the *kasher kohen*'s blood is still valid, potentially usable blood; if the *psul* person's *zrika* has some impact, that blood in the *kasher kohen*'s possession is now *shirayim*. The Gemara teaches that even the *zrika* of a *psul* person has this effect — *d'psul oseh shirayim*. And when the *braisa* in *kibus* states *ve-kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah*, it is not casually appending the *zrika*; it is specifically telling us that even where the *psul* person sprinkled the blood, any other blood caught by a *kasher kohen* would be *shirayim* and therefore not *ta'un kibus*.

This is why Rav Assi insists we do not need to collapse the *braisa* into a stock phrase at all.

### Reish Lakish Question

Reish Lakish posed this question to Rabbi Yochanan directly: *pasul mahu she-ya'aseh shirayim?* — if a *psul* person performs the *avoda*, does that render the remaining blood *shirayim*?

### Yochanan Pigul Shirayim

Rabbi Yochanan's answer is striking: *ein lecha davar she-ya'aseh shirayim ela chutz mi-zmano ve-chutz mi-mkomo* — the only thing that makes remaining blood into *shirayim* is a *psul* of *pigul*, i.e., *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo*. Any other *psul* does not make *shirayim*.

### Pigul Mertzeh Logic

The reason is precisely the *mertzeh l'pigulo* principle that anchored so much of the earlier discussion. By *pigul*, the *avoda* is treated as if it were accepted — it is, in a sense, as though a valid *avoda* took place, for the very purpose of triggering *pigul* status. Because of this quasi-legitimacy, a *zrikas pigul* is treated as if it were a valid *zrika* for the purpose of rendering remaining blood *shirayim*. With any other *psul*, no such quasi-legitimacy exists, the *avoda* is a complete nullity, and the remaining blood is not *shirayim*.

### Clarification Tzibbur

But if that is so, then Rabbi Yochanan's statement that "nothing" makes *shirayim* except *pigul* requires qualification — what about *tamei*? The answer is that Rabbi Yochanan's formulation must be understood more precisely: *ein lecha davar she-eino meratzeh ba-tzibbur ve-oseh shirayim ela chutz lizmano ve-chutz limkomo*. Nothing that is *never* overridden for the community makes *shirayim* — except *pigul*. This is the operative distinction.

### Tamei Exception

*Tuma* stands as the exception to the rule, because it is a *psul* that is overridden in communal contexts — whether through the principle of *hutra ba-tzibbur* or *d'chuya ba-tzibbur*. Since *tamei* is sometimes permitted, the *avoda* of a *tamei* person retains some measure of validity and therefore has the power to make remaining blood *shirayim*. Thus Rabbi Yochanan's statement that only *pigul* makes *shirayim* excludes all the standard *psulim* that are never overridden for the community — a *ba'al mum*, an *onen*, and similar — but includes both *pigul* (because of *mertzeh l'pigulo*) and *tuma* (because of *hutra* or *d'chuya ba-tzibbur*). The *avoda* of any other *pasul kohen* is a complete nullity, leaves the remaining blood untouched, and does not make *shirayim*.

This clarification, while presented as something of a digression, beautifully crystallizes the conceptual terrain of the entire sugya: not every defective *avoda* is equal. Some *psulim* leave the *avoda* with residual halakhic significance — *pigul* because of *mertzeh l'pigulo*, *tuma* because of its conditional permissibility — while others render the *avoda* a complete non-event.

## Braisa Zrika Proof

### Ha'Pigul L'olam

The Gemara now returns to the central question: does the *braisa* support that *zrika* — rather than *kabbalah* — is the critical moment for *me'ilah* status change? The *braisa* in question is the one cited at the very start of today's daf: *ha'pigul l'olam mo'alin bo* — with *pigul*, there is always *me'ilah*.

### Inference Kabbalah

The Gemara attempts to draw an inference: perhaps the phrase *l'olam mo'alin bo* implies that even if the *pigul* occurred at the stage of *zrika*, the *me'ilah* status is unchanged. This in turn would suggest that had the *pigul* occurred only at the *zrika*, one might have thought the *me'ilah* status could change — which would imply that the critical moment is *kabbalah*, not *zrika*. Under this reading, the *braisa* would be evidence that once the *kabbalah* is proper, the *me'ilah* status can be affected, even if the *zrika* is subsequently defective through *pigul*.

### Did Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, זָרַק. לֵימָא הוֹאִיל וּמְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ סֵיפָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ נָמֵי רֵישָׁא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this inference and asks: **No, you did do** *zrika*. **Shall** we **say** that **since the latter clause supports** the opinion of Rav Giddel, **the first clause also supports** his opinion?

The Gemara rejects this inference. *Lo, zarka* — no, you did do *zrika*. The *braisa* is stating that even when *zrika* was performed with *machshavas pigul*, the *me'ilah* status does not change. The *psul* at the *zrika* stage is sufficient to prevent any status change, and the *braisa* is not implying anything about *kabbalah* being the threshold.

### L'olam Emphasis

The question then becomes: if so, what does the word *l'olam* add to the phrase *ha'pigul l'olam mo'alin bo*? The Gemara answers: it adds exactly what Rav Gidal amar Rav came to teach. *L'olam* emphasizes that even a *zrikas pigul* — which one might have thought should count as a quasi-*zrika* by virtue of the *mertzeh l'pigulo* principle — does not change *me'ilah* status. *Pigul will always be a problem*, even at the *zrika* stage. One is not permitted to say that because *pigul* renders the *korban* as if it were *meratzeh*, therefore the *zrika* was as if properly performed and therefore *me'ilah* status changed. *L'olam* closes that door.

### Rav Gidal Position

This brings us back to the precise formulation of Rav Gidal amar Rav: *zrikas pigul eina motzi midei me'ilah ve-eina meivi lidei me'ilah* — a *zrikas pigul* neither removes *me'ilah* status nor brings something into *me'ilah* status. It is halakhically inert for the purposes of *me'ilah* change in both directions. By underscoring *l'olam* in precisely this way, the *braisa* is aligned with and indeed supports the position of Rav Gidal amar Rav. And notably, by emphasizing *zrikas pigul* as the problem being addressed, the word *l'olam* simultaneously underscores that *zrika* — not *kabbalah* — is the critical moment, since it is specifically the defect at *zrika* that is being declared insufficient.

### Critical Moment Unclear

That said, the question of the precise critical moment — whether *shechita*, *kabbalah*, *zrika*, or *achilah* determines when *me'ilah* status changes — remains unresolved at this point in the Gemara. The plain sense of the Mishnah points toward *zrika*, meaning the moment the meat actually becomes *mutar*. But the Gemara has been seriously entertaining the possibility that the critical moment is *kabbalah*, based on a close reading of the phrase *kiblu psulim ve-zarku et dammah* as pointing to the *kabbalah* as the decisive threshold. While neither the *braisot* nor the Mishnah decisively resolve the debate between Chizkiya's position (*heter shechita*) and Rabbi Yochanan's (*heter achilah*), and while the *kabbalah* position has significant difficulties of its own, the discussion will continue on the next *daf*.
