# Avodah Zarah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nations' Defense

### Aggadata Framework

We take up the *daf* at the bottom of 3b, in the middle of the opening *aggadata*. This opening *aggadata* is significant because it functions as a framing device for the tractate as a whole: it addresses the broad question of the Rabbis' attitude toward non-Jews and the non-Jewish world. The scenario it depicts is one of a future day of cosmic judgment, in which all the nations of the world come before God to justify their actions, claiming that everything they did was somehow connected to the *Torah* or done for the sake of enabling the Jewish people to study it. God rejects all of these claims entirely.

### Idolatry vs. Torah

One point worth noting at the outset — and it is easy to overlook — is that the nations in this *aggadata* are not being held accountable for their idolatry. In the *Torah* itself, the central concern is the proclamation of a single transcendent God who is not worshipped through images, and the great struggle against the idolatry of the surrounding nations. The people of Israel entering the land were warned above all else against being led astray by the idolatrous practices of the inhabitants. The verse in *Yeshayahu* that serves as the textual basis for this *midrash* speaks of how, in the future, all idols will be exposed as falsehoods. Yet by the time of the Rabbis, the framing has shifted entirely. The dispute here is not about idolatry versus belief in God. It is about *mitzvot* and *Torah* — have you kept them, are you connected to them? The *Maharsha* observes that the image of God holding a *Sefer Torah* in his bosom captures precisely this: every nation claims that its faith is the true one, and the revelation is that it is the *Torah* that stands closest to God, not any other faith. But the central point remains that this is not a polemic about paganism — it is about what it means to live a life of *Torah* and *mitzvot*.

### Non-Jewish Works

A second theme concerns whether credit is extended to the accomplishments of the non-Jewish world — the roads, the bridges, the bathhouses, the marketplaces. In this *midrash*, those accomplishments are acknowledged, but only in order to be dismissed. They are dismissed on two grounds: first, that the nations did it all for their own sake, not for any higher purpose; and second, that God declares it all to be his anyway. The acknowledgment, in other words, is purely instrumental — it is introduced so that it can be set aside.

### Never Given Torah

The nations now bring their first claim before God. The *gemara* locates this approximately fifteen lines from the bottom of the page, beginning at *omrim lefanav*, near the citation of *Devarim* 33. They say: *Ribono Shel Olam*, how can you hold us responsible for not observing the *Torah* when you never gave it to us? Had you given it to us, we would have accepted it.

### Torah Offered

The *gemara* immediately interjects: how could they possibly make such a claim? The verse says *Vayomar Hashem miSinai ba*, God came from Sinai, and also *v'zarach miSeir lamo*, he shone from *Seir*, and *Eloah miTeiman yavo*, he came from *Teiman*. What was God doing in *Seir*, the territory of *Esav*, and in *Paran*, the territory of *Yishmael*? Rabbi Yochanan explains: this teaches that before giving the *Torah* to the Jewish people, God brought it around and offered it to every nation and tongue — *kol umah v'lashon* — and none of them accepted it, until he came to Israel and they received it. So the nations cannot honestly claim they were never given the opportunity.

### Would Have Kept

The nations revise their argument: *klum kiblanuha v'lo kiyamanuha* — if only you had given it to us, we would have fulfilled it. The *gemara* is dismissive: *al da'at tavar tuhu* — this claim shatters them. You cannot say "I would have done it had I accepted it" when you chose not to accept it in the first place.

The nations then try yet again. *Ribono Shel Olam*, did you not force it upon Israel? The verse says they stood at the foot of the mountain — *vayisyatzvu b'tachtit hahar* — and Rav Dimi bar Chama teaches that God held the mountain over them like a barrel and said: if you accept the *Torah*, good; if not, this is where you will be buried. So if you had only forced it upon us the way you forced it upon them, we would have committed ourselves to a life of *Torah u'mitzvot*.

## Noachide Mitzvot

### Track Record Test

God responds with a challenge: *harishonot yashmiunu* — let us look at the early record and see whether your claim that you would have upheld the *Torah* had any merit. You were given the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*. *Sheva mitzvot she'kibaltem heichan kiyamtem* — the seven *mitzvot* you did accept, where is your track record in fulfilling them? The *midrash* thus moves from dismissing the nations' claim about the *Torah* to an even more pointed indictment: they did not even fulfill the basic ethical-religious minimum that was given to them.

### Habakkuk Verse

To demonstrate that the nations did not fulfill the seven Noachide laws, the *gemara* cites a teaching of Rav Yosef from the verse in *Chabakuk*: *amad vayimodad eretz ra'ah vayater goyim* — God stood up and measured out the land, he saw and he *yatar* the nations. The word *vayater* is read as related to *mutar*, permitted. What did God see? He saw the *sheva mitzvot* that the *bnei Noach* had accepted upon themselves — and the verse notes that they accepted them, *she'kiblu aleihem*, a phrase that is itself slightly ironic given that they were never fully willing participants. God saw they did not fulfill them, and therefore declared it all *mutar* — permissible to them, as it were, releasing them from obligation.

The *gemara* pauses over this: does that mean the sinner is rewarded — *chotei niskar* — that by failing to observe, one is now relieved of the obligation entirely?

### Obligation & Reward

Mar b'rei d'Ravina clarifies. Since the nations failed to uphold the seven *mitzvot* and began living as though they were not obligated, when they subsequently do perform those same actions, they do so not from a sense of *chiuv*, of obligation, but rather out of their own inclination or personal ethic. Therefore, *af al pi she'mekaymim otam ein mekabelin aleihem sachar k'metzuveh v'oseh* — even if they observe these *mitzvot*, they are not rewarded as one who is commanded and fulfills, *metzuveh v'oseh*. Rather, they receive only the lesser reward of *eino metzuveh v'oseh*, one who performs a deed without being obligated to do so. This is grounded in the principle stated by Rabbi Chanina: *gadol hametzuveh v'oseh yoter mi-mi she'eino metzuveh v'oseh* — greater is the one who acts from obligation than the one who acts without it.

### Rabbi Meir Teaching

The *gemara* challenges this framework: but surely non-Jews do receive some reward? The *braita* is then introduced: *hayah Rabbi Meir omer minayin she'afilu oved kochavim ve'osek baTorah shehu k'kohen gadol* — Rabbi Meir taught that we learn from the verse *asher ya'aseh otam ha'adam v'chai bahem* — which specifies not *kohanim*, *leviyim*, or *Yisraelim*, but simply *ha'adam*, a human being — that even a non-Jew who involves himself in *Torah* is like a *kohen gadol*.

This is a striking teaching, and its full force requires some unpacking. The phrase *harei hu k'kohen gadol* is borrowed from a famous passage in the *Mishna* in *Horayot*, which contains what is essentially an anti-hierarchical statement about the nature of *Torah* society. The traditional structure is ordered by class: *kohen*, *levi*, *Yisrael*, and so on, with a further hierarchy of man over woman, Jew over non-Jew. But the *Mishna* there says that when all other things are equal, *Torah* learning overrides everything. A *mamzer* who is a *talmid chacham* takes precedence over a *kohen gadol am ha'aretz*. The *kohen gadol* represents the apex of the class hierarchy, and *Torah* supersedes it entirely. This is grounded in the verse *yekarah hi mipeninim* — *Torah* is dearer than pearls — read as *yekarah hi mikohen gadol shenichnas lifnai velifnim*, dearer even than the *kohen gadol* who enters the innermost sanctum. The larger *midrash* from which this teaching derives, found in the *Sifrei* (or *Sifra*), elaborates further: it reads verses that say *adam*, it reads verses that say *goy*, and in each case draws the conclusion that whoever is *osek baTorah* rises to the highest level. One particularly powerful line reads *veyavo goy tzaddik* — and notes that it does not say *kohanim leviyim veyisraelim* will enter but rather *goy tzaddik*, a righteous nation; even a non-Jew *osek baTorah* will enter. So Rabbi Meir has taken the existing principle — that *Torah* breaks class hierarchy — and extended it to its ultimate conclusion: it does not even matter whether you are a Jew.

This is a profound counter-voice to everything the *midrash* has been building until this point. It does not speak about non-Jews who build roads or bathhouses, but it does disrupt the sharp division of the world into Jew and non-Jew. It raises the possibility that among the nations there may be individuals who are greater even than Jews — certainly greater than a *kohen gadol am ha'aretz*.

One might also ask whether *osek baTorah* here means learning *Torah* or performing *mitzvot*. The verse on which the teaching is grounded — *asher ya'aseh otam ha'adam v'chai bahem* — emphasizes doing, *ya'aseh*, not learning. And indeed, a parallel formulation elsewhere reads *afilu goy v'oseh et haTorah*, a non-Jew who *does* the *Torah*. This version fits both the verse and the broader context of the *gemara*, which has been discussing the performance of *mitzvot*. The *Rambam* in fact rules that if a non-Jew performs any of the 613 *mitzvot* of the *Torah* — even *brit milah*, which one might assume to pertain exclusively to the Jewish covenant — he receives reward as an *eino metzuveh v'oseh*. So the question of whether Rabbi Meir's teaching pertains to Torah study or to the performance of *mitzvot* is itself a substantive one.

### Diffusing Radicalism

The *gemara* immediately works to contain the force of Rabbi Meir's position. Rather than allowing the teaching to stand in its radical form — a non-Jew *osek baTorah* being compared to a *kohen gadol* — the *gemara* re-reads it through the lens of *metzuveh v'oseh* versus *eino metzuveh v'oseh*. They don't receive reward equal to a *kohen gadol*; they receive the lesser reward of one who performs without obligation. This is in tension with the entire point of the *harei hu k'kohen gadol* formulation, which was meant to assert equivalence, not hierarchy. Other *gemarot* push back further still: a *goy she-shavas* — a non-Jew who observes *Shabbat* — is *chayav mita*, and accordingly some *gemarot* limit Rabbi Meir's statement to mean that a non-Jew may only study the *mitzvot* that pertain to him — the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* — not the *Torah* that belongs to Israel. The *gemara* here has introduced the radical voice of Rabbi Meir and then systematically bracketed and diminished it.

It is worth noting parenthetically that certain *poskim* read the *midrash*'s phrase *amad vehitiran lahem* — God declared it permitted to them — in a more *pshat* direction, invoking an argument analogous to *tinok shenishba*. A Jew who grew up with no Jewish knowledge and violates *Shabbat* cannot be held to the same standard of responsibility as one who knew and chose otherwise. Some *poskim* apply a similar framework to non-Jews regarding the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*: one cannot hold them accountable for obligations they have no awareness of. This reading looks more sympathetically at the very passage the *gemara* here cites in order to be dismissive.

### Learning vs. Doing

The dominant orientation of this *sugya*, however, remains one of dismissal. The question of what a non-Jew's positive actions are worth — whether *osek baTorah* means learning or doing, whether the reward is that of a *kohen gadol* or merely of *eino metzuveh v'oseh* — all of it is resolved in the direction of minimizing credit to the nations. The lens through which the opening of this tractate invites us to view the non-Jewish world is one of fundamental skepticism: they did not fulfill their minimal obligations, and even when they appear to do so, it is not from a place of genuine *chiuv* and therefore yields only attenuated reward. That is how this *sugya* is shaping the reader's perception of the larger world — not as a simple polemic about idolatry versus monotheism, but as a carefully constructed argument about religious obligation, meritocracy, and who is genuinely connected to a life of *Torah u'mitzvot*.

## Counter-Claim

### Why Israel Different

The nations, having been told that their track record with the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* disqualifies their claim, now press a further argument. Fine, they say — but *Yisrael shekibluah, heichan kiyamuah*? The Jewish people who received the *Torah*, where is their record of fulfilling it? Why should they be rewarded if they too failed to keep it?

### Israel's Fulfillment

God responds: *ani me'id bahem shekiyamu et haTorah kulah* — I will testify that they fulfilled the entire *Torah*.

### Bias Challenge

The nations immediately challenge this: *Ribono Shel Olam, klum yesh av sheme'id al bno?* Is there a father who testifies on behalf of his son? You are *pasul edut* — disqualified as a witness. You are *noge'a b'davar*, a party with vested interest. You have already shown favoritism, as it is written *beni bechori Yisrael* — Israel is my firstborn son.

### Heaven & Earth

God replies: very well, let us bring impartial witnesses — *shamayim va'aretz ya'idu bahem*. Heaven and earth are frequently invoked in *Tanach* as cosmic witnesses to solemn commitments: *ha'azinu hashamayim ve'adabera*, *shimu shamayim*, and so on. Based on rain and the produce of the land, they hold people accountable and can testify to whether Israel has kept the *Torah*.

### Vested Interests

The nations object again: *shamayim va'aretz nog'im be'edutam* — heaven and earth also have a vested interest. The verse states *im lo briti yomam valayla, chukot shamayim va'aretz lo samti* — were it not for my covenant, meaning the *Torah*, I would not have set the laws of heaven and earth in place. Their very existence is conditional on Israel keeping the *Torah*.

### Creation Purpose

This is the teaching of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: why does the *Torah* say *yom hashishi*, the *the* sixth day, with the definite article, rather than simply *yom shishi*? This teaches that the entire six days of creation were predicated on the sixth day of *Sivan*, the day of *matan Torah*. God stipulated with the act of creation: *im Yisrael mekablim et haTorah mutav, ve'im lav ani achazir etchem l'tohu vavohu* — if Israel accepts and keeps my *Torah*, good; if not, I will return the world to the primordial void. Since the continued existence of heaven and earth depends on Israel having fulfilled the *Torah*, they cannot serve as impartial witnesses — their testimony in Israel's favor is self-serving. Chizkiyah adds a supporting verse: *mishamayim hishmata din, eretz yerah v'shakata* — from heaven the judgment was heard, the earth feared and then became tranquil. If it feared, why did it become tranquil? Because it feared that Israel would not accept the *Torah* and creation would be undone — and when Israel accepted it at *Har Sinai*, the earth was tranquil.

The recurrence of this theme is worth pausing over. The entire *sugya* continues to frame what is at stake not as a struggle between monotheism and idolatry, but as the question of whether the Jewish people live a life of *Torah* and *mitzvot*. That is why the world exists. That is the lens through which this tractate — which governs the Jewish community's relationship with the surrounding non-Jewish world — asks us to approach everything that follows.

## Historical Witnesses

### Rulers Testify

Since heaven and earth are disqualified, God turns to a different category of witnesses: *mikem yavo'u ve'ya'idu bahem b'Yisrael* — from among you, the nations, witnesses will come and testify on Israel's behalf that they fulfilled the entire *Torah*.

### Nimrod & Abraham

*Yavo Nimrod ve'ya'id b'Avraham shelo avad avodah kochavim* — let Nimrod come and testify regarding Abraham that he did not worship idols. According to the *midrash*, Abraham was cast into the furnace at *Ur Kasdim* precisely because he refused to bow to idols, and Nimrod himself witnessed this.

### Laban & Jacob

*Yavo Lavan ve'ya'id b'Yaakov shelo nechshad al hagezel* — let Laban come and testify regarding Jacob that he was never suspected of theft. Laban pursued Jacob and accused him of stealing his *terafim*, and yet, as Rashi notes, Jacob could say: show me someone who worked for his employer for twenty years and did not take so much as a pin or a scissors. Laban searched and found nothing. The irony that Rachel had in fact taken the *terafim* is duly noted — but Jacob himself did not, and it is Jacob who is the subject of this testimony.

### Potiphar & Joseph

*Yavo eshet Potifar ve'ta'id b'Yosef shelo nechshad al aveirah* — let the wife of Potiphar come and testify that Joseph did not commit sexual sin. Here too we are choosing whom to look at; many of the *bnei Yisrael* worshiped the *egel* at the foot of *Har Sinai*, but those are not the subjects of this *midrash*.

### Pre-Sinai Laws

Rashi makes a point worth highlighting: Abraham, Jacob, and Joseph all lived before *matan Torah*. The *mitzvot* for which they are praised — *avodah zarah*, *gezel*, and *giluy arayot* — correspond to three of the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach*. The *midrash* is thus making a two-stage argument: in the pre-Sinaitic period, when Israel's ancestors were still in the legal status of *bnei Noach*, they kept the *mitzvot bnei Noach*. The witnesses testifying to this are themselves non-Jews, which is precisely the point — they are the ones who observed it firsthand.

### Post-Sinai Witnesses

After *matan Torah*, a second set of non-Jewish witnesses is called. *Yavo Nevuchadnetzar ve'ya'id b'Chananyah Mishael v'Azaryah shelo hishtachavu latzelem* — let Nebuchadnezzar come and testify that Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah did not bow down to the statue. *Yavo Daryavesh ve'ya'id b'Daniel shelo bitel et hatfillah* — let Darius come and testify that Daniel did not cease from his daily prayers. Then the friends of *Iyov* — Bildad, Tzofar, and Eliphaz — are called; as non-Jewish prophets, they can testify through prophetic knowledge that the Jewish people kept the entire *Torah*. The verse *yitnu edeihem veyitzdaku* is cited in support: let them give their testimony and be found righteous. Notably, the original verse in *Yeshayahu* was part of a challenge to idolaters; here it has been entirely reframed as a declaration about who kept the *Torah*.

### Statue Question

Tosafot raises an important distinction regarding Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah. The *gemara* says they did not bow to *latzelem* — the statue — which is not the same formulation used for Abraham, who is said not to have been *oved avodah zarah*. Tosafot suggests that the statue Nebuchadnezzar demanded they bow to may not have been an actual idol but rather a symbol of fealty to the king — and that bowing to it would not technically have constituted *avodah zarah* under *halacha*. Yet they gave up their lives rather than do even that. This leads Tosafot into an important discussion relevant to its own historical context: the Jews of the Crusade period who died as martyrs in circumstances that were not technically required by *halacha* — can the idea of *kiddush Hashem* extend to such cases? Tosafot is also troubled by the *midrash* that suggests Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah would have bowed had they been subjected to torture. If the statue was not real *avodah zarah*, that claim becomes more understandable — one cannot be obligated to die for something that is not technically *yehareg v'al ya'avor*.

## Sukkah Test

### Final Chance

The nations, having been outmaneuvered at every turn, make one last appeal: *Ribono Shel Olam, tenah lanu merosh vena'asena* — give us one final chance and we will do it.

### Friday-Shabbat

God's reply is blunt: *shotim shebaolam* — fools! *Mi shetarach beerev Shabbos yochal beShabbos* — whoever worked on *erev Shabbat* gets to eat on *Shabbat*; whoever did not work on *erev Shabbat* — where will his food come from? The world to come is like *Shabbat*: a time to receive reward, not to perform *mitzvot*. The work must precede it. The *gemara* cites Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's reading of *asher anochi metzavcha hayom* — *hayom la'asotam velo lemachar la'asotam*, today is the time to do them, not tomorrow; *hayom la'asotam velo hayom litol secharchem*, today you do, but today is not when you collect your reward.

Strictly speaking, of course, the *gemara* acknowledges that God should not be offering them any chance at all — *ein HaKadosh Baruch Hu ba bitrunya im briyotav* — God does not wish to leave his creatures with any legitimate basis for complaint. Even an opportunity they do not deserve will be granted, to ensure they have no grounds to protest.

### Sukkah Offered

So God says: *mitzvah kalah yesh li, vesukkah shemah* — I have an easy *mitzvah* for you, called *sukkah*. *Lechu va'asu osah* — go and do it.

### Zechariah Vision

The choice of *sukkah* is not arbitrary. The *haftarah* read on *Sukkot* is from *Zechariah*, and it envisions precisely this scenario: *vehayah kol hanotar mikol hagoyim habaim al Yerushalayim ve'alu midei shanah beshanah lehishtachavot leMelech Hashem Tzeva'ot velachog et chag hasukkot* — all the remaining nations will come up to Jerusalem year after year to bow before God and to celebrate the festival of *Sukkot*. And those who do not come will receive no rain; and if the family of Egypt does not come, they will be struck with plague. *Sukkot* thus carries a universalist dimension — it is connected to rain, to sustenance, to the basic conditions of life for all humanity — which makes it a fitting test case.

### Sukkah Failure

The nations hear the offer and are enthusiastic. *Miyad kol echad ve'echad notel veholech ve'oseh sukkah berosh gago* — immediately, each one goes and builds a *sukkah* on the roof. But then God causes the sun to beat down upon them with the heat of summer — some textual variants read *kitkufat Tammuz*, like the summer period, but the standard text has *mitkufat Tammuz*, during the summer period. In any case, the point is clear: the sun is blazing.

### Sun Exemption

The *gemara* pauses: but is this fair? God said he does not come with complaints against his creatures — and here he is making things unreasonably difficult. The answer given is that for Israel too, sometimes the summer heat extends into *Sukkot*, depending on the alignment of the lunar and solar calendars. The Jews manage to sit in their *sukkot* even then. So God was not imposing an impossible standard — he was testing whether the nations could do what Jews have managed to do.

Furthermore, Rava points out: *mitzta'er patur min hasukkah* — one who is genuinely uncomfortable is *halachically* exempt from the *sukkah*. So there was actually a legitimate basis for leaving.

### Rejecting Bonds

But the nations do not simply leave. *Vekol echad ve'echad meva'et besukkato veyotzei* — each one kicks his *sukkah* in frustration and walks out. That is the crucial point. Leaving the *sukkah* when genuinely uncomfortable is permitted; kicking it as you leave is not. The verse cited is *nenatkah et mosroteimo venashlichah mimenu avoteimo* — let us cast off his bonds and throw his ropes away from us. The *Maharsha* notes that *avot* echoes *anaf etz avot*, the braided branch used for *lulav*, lending the image a *Sukkot*-flavored resonance. The nations' reaction is not simply one of incapacity — it is one of hostility. They are constitutionally unsuited to a life of regulated obligation, and the moment conditions become difficult, their response is not to seek an exemption but to reject the entire enterprise. This is how the *midrash* concludes its characterization of the nations: not merely that they fail to observe, but that they are fundamentally not the kind of people who can live this type of life.

## Divine Laughter

### God Laughs

*Miyad HaKadosh Baruch Hu yoshev umesachek aleihem* — immediately God sits up in heaven and laughs at them. The verse is *Yoshev Bashamayim Yischak* — he who dwells in the heavens will laugh — drawn from the same chapter of *Tehillim* that spoke of casting off bonds.

### One Day Laughter

Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: *ein schok lifnei HaKadosh Baruch Hu ela oto hayom bilvad* — God has laughter only on that one day, the future moment when all of the nations' claims and pretensions are exposed as folly.

### Future Converts

The *gemara* notes that some teach Rabbi Yitzchak's statement in connection with a different teaching. A *braita* states: Rabbi Yosi teaches that in the future, non-Jews will come and convert — *ba'in ovdei kochavim umitgayrin*.

### No Converts

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: would we accept them? The rule is *ein mekabelin gerim limot haMashiach* — we do not accept converts in the Messianic era, because such converts would be motivated by self-interest rather than sincere commitment. The precedent cited is that we similarly did not accept converts in the days of David and Solomon, when it was too advantageous to be part of the Jewish people.

### Dragged Converts

Nevertheless, Rabbi Yosi's point still holds, because these individuals will present themselves as converts even without formal acceptance: *shena'asu gerim grurim* — they become, in an alliterative phrase, *gerim grurim*, dragged-along converts. To demonstrate their Jewish identity, they take on the visible markers of male Jewish practice: *tefillin* on their heads and arms, *tzitzit* on their garments, and a *mezuzah* on their doorposts.

### Gog-Magog Wars

Then the wars of *Gog uMagog* approach. The timing is somewhat curious: one would generally expect the Messianic period to follow the wars of *Gog uMagog*, yet here the Messianic era has arrived and the wars have not yet occurred. In any case, these *gerim grurim* see the armies massing against the Jewish people and ask: why are you coming? *Ve'omrim lo al Hashem ve'al Meshicho* — they answer: we are coming to fight against God and his Messiah. Immediately, each of these fair-weather converts strips off his *mitzvot* and departs. *Vekol echad ve'echad menatek mitzvotav veholech*. The verse again: *nenatkah et mosroteimo venashlichah mimenu avoteimo*. Tosafot notes that *mosroteimo* — the bonds — alludes to the *tefillin* straps wound around the arm and head, and *avoteimo* — the braided cords — to the *tzitzit*. They cast it all off, and God laughs: *VehaKadosh Baruch Hu yoshev umesachek, Yoshev Bashamayim Yischak*. Rabbi Yitzchak's teaching applies here as well: *ein lo leHaKadosh Baruch Hu schok ela oto hayom bilvad* — only on that one day.

## Divine Schedule

### Twelve Hours

The *gemara* presses: is it really true that God only laughs on that single day? But Rav Yehuda, citing Rav, teaches that the day is divided into twelve hours, and God's schedule throughout the day involves something that might be characterized as a form of pleasure or laughter.

### Study Torah

In the first three hours, *HaKadosh Baruch Hu yoshev ve'osek baTorah* — God sits and engages in *Torah* study.

### Divine Judgment

In the second set of three hours, from roughly nine to noon, *yoshev vedan et kol ha'olam kulo* — God sits in judgment of the entire world. When he sees that the world's conduct would merit destruction, he does not carry it out: *miyad omed mikise hadin veyoshev al kise rachamim* — he rises from the throne of judgment and takes his seat on the throne of compassion. The world must continue.

### Sustains World

In the third set of three hours, from noon to three in the afternoon, *yoshev vezan et kol ha'olam kulo* — God provides sustenance to the entire world. The *gemara* describes the scope of this: *mikarnei re'eimim ad beitzei kinim* — from the horns of the great beast (suggested by some to be the reindeer, with its magnificent antlers) to the eggs of lice. Rashi suggests that *beitzei kinim* refers to a species of small creature rather than lice eggs literally, though the plain sense of the phrase seems to convey the extremity of God's providential reach: from the most lofty creature to the most minute, God sustains all.

### Leviathan Play

In the fourth and final set of three hours, from three to six in the afternoon, *yoshev umesachek im livyatan* — God sports with the Leviathan. The verse cited is *livyatan zeh yatzarta lisachak bo* — the Leviathan you created to play with. This is, admittedly, a strikingly anthropomorphic image, with something of a mythological flavor. But the structural point it makes is clear: God has laughter every day, *im briyotav*, with his creatures, taking pleasure in his creation. What God does *not* do every day is laugh *at* his creatures, *al briyotav*. That happens only on the one day at the end of time when the nations' folly is finally exposed.

### Post-Destruction

Rav Acha objects to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *mi-yom she-charav Beit ha-Mikdash ein schok lifnei HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — from the day the *Beit ha-Mikdash* was destroyed, God no longer laughs at all.

### Jerusalem Mourning

Is this provable from a verse? The verse *vayikra Hashem Elokim Tzeva'ot bayom hahu livchi u-lemisped u-lekorchah* — God called that day one of weeping and lamentation and mourning — might be limited to the day of the destruction itself. But the verse *im eshkachech Yerushalayim tishkach yemini, tidbak leshoni le-chiki im lo ezkerechi* — if I forget you, Jerusalem, may my right hand wither; may my tongue cleave to my palate if I do not remember you — shows that the mourning is perpetual, not confined to a single day. Could this not mean simply that God will never *forget* Jerusalem while still being capable of joy? The verse *hechesheiti me-olam acharish et'apak* — I have long been silent, held myself back — implies that God continually restrains himself, maintaining a posture of perpetual restraint rather than open laughter.

### Teaching Infants

If God no longer sports with the Leviathan in the fourth quarter of the day, what does he do instead? *Yoshev u-melamed tinokot shel beit raban Torah* — God sits and teaches *Torah* to the schoolchildren. These are not living children in their classrooms who need God as their teacher. They are infants and young children who died before their time. They are up in heaven, and God himself teaches them *Torah*. It is a striking and moving image: the child who is taken too young sits in a heavenly *cheder* with God as the teacher. The verse cited is *et mi yoreh dei'ah, ve'et mi yavin shmu'ah — g'mulei me-chalav, atikei mi-shadayim* — to whom will he teach knowledge, who will understand the teaching? — those weaned from milk, those torn away from the breast.

One might ask: who taught these infants before the destruction of the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, when God was occupied with the Leviathan? The *gemara* offers two answers: either it was the angel *Metatron*, who serves in some traditions as a heavenly surrogate for divine activity, or alternatively God was doing both simultaneously — teaching *Torah* and playing with the Leviathan. After the destruction, the play ceased and only the teaching remains.

### Night Tour

Finally, the *gemara* addresses what God does at night, having described his daytime schedule in such detail. Two answers are given. The first is that nighttime activity parallels daytime activity in some analogous way — *me'ein yamama*. The second is more specific and no less vivid: *rochev al kruv kal shelo veshat* — God mounts his swift cherub and tours the world, surveying creation. More expansively: *bishmonah asar elef olamot* — through 18,000 worlds, based on the verse *rechev elohim ribotayim*, which yields a figure of 20,000, from which 2,000 are subtracted to give 18,000. A third possibility is offered: *yoshev veshome'a shira mipi chayot* — God sits and listens to the celestial beings singing songs of praise, as the verse says: *yomam yetzaveh Hashem chasdo, uvelayla shiro imi* — by day God commands his kindness, and at night his song is with me.
