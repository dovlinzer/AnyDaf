# Sanhedrin 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamei Kohen & Avoda

### Kanaim Pogim Bo Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא מִשּׁוּם זָרוּת, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם רִחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **neither due to non-priesthood,** if a non-priest performs one of those rites in the Temple, he is not liable to receive the death penalty; **nor due to** the performance of any of these rites in a state of **ritual impurity; nor due to** the performance of any of these rites as a priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments; nor due to** the performance of any of these services without the **washing of his hands and feet.** The reason for the lack of liability is that in all these cases, additional rites must be performed in order to complete the service. One is liable for performing the service outside the Temple, or performing the service as a non-priest, in a state of ritual impurity, etc., only when performing a ritual that completes the service.

The daf begins at the bottom of 82b, picking up in the midst of the *mishna*'s discussion of *kana'im pogim bo* — cases where zealots may strike down a transgressor without formal trial. One example that emerged, though not placed explicitly under the *kana'im pogim bo* rubric, concerns a *kohen* who performs the *avoda* while in a state of *tumah*. The *mishna* relates that his fellow *kohanim* would remove him and crack his skull open. The question the *gemara* is wrestling with is what the jurisprudential basis for this extreme extrajudicial response is: does it reflect an underlying *chiyuv misa bidei shamayim* — that heaven regards this transgressor as deserving death — and therefore the zealots are given license to act? Or does the opposite inference follow — if God is going to punish him, why must human beings intervene?

### Gemara's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מְקַטֵּר – חַיָּיב. מַאי לָאו, מִיתָה? לָא, בְּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** From the *baraita*, one may infer: **But** one who **burns** the offering on the altar in one of these circumstances, including in a state of ritual impurity, is **liable. What, is it not** teaching that he is liable to receive **death** at the hand of Heaven, and there is proof from the *baraita* to resolve Rav Aḥa bar Huna's dilemma? The Gemara rejects that proof: **No,** he is liable **for** violating **a prohibition,** without the punishment of death.

The *gemara*'s inclination is firmly toward the former reading: the very fact that extra-legal killing is permitted, or that colleagues may strike the offender down, signals that at some level we recognize the act as one deserving of death — specifically, death at the hands of heaven. The burden now falls on the *gemara* to defend this position.

### Intermediary Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל זָר נָמֵי לְאַזְהָרָה? וְהָכְתִיב: "וְהַזָּר הַקָּרֵב יוּמָת"! הָא כִּדְאִיתַאּ וְהָא כִּדְאִיתַאּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to that understanding, as for the **non-priest** who is mentioned in the *baraita* together with the priest who performs the Temple service in a state of impurity, is he too **liable for** violating **a prohibition,** and not death, if he burns the offering on the altar? **But isn't it written: "And the non-priest who approaches shall be put to death"** (Numbers 18:7)? The Gemara answers: Do not extrapolate the liability of a non-priest from the liability of a ritually impure priest, as **this** case of ritual impurity is **as it is, and that** case of a non-priest is **as it is;** each situation is discussed independently.

A *breita* is introduced as a challenge. The *breita* enumerates a series of *avodot* that are not *gmar avoda* — not a final, completed act of service, but intermediate stages in the offering process: *hayotzek*, pouring the oil on a *mincha*; *habolel*, mixing the oil in; *hapotet*, breaking the baked *mincha* into pieces; *hamoleach*, applying salt before placing it on the altar; *hemenif*, waving it; *hamagish*, pressing the *mincha* to the corner of the altar; *vehamessader et hashulchan*, arranging the showbread on the table (whose actual offering consists of burning the incense atop it each *Shabbat*); *hameitiv et hanerot*, cleaning out the *menorah* lamps — notably not the lighting itself, which for a separate reason is not classified as *avoda* at all; *vehakomet*, taking the handful of the *mincha*; and *vehamikabel hadam*, catching the blood of the *korban* before it is thrown.

For all of these intermediary acts performed *bechutz* — outside the *Beis Hamikdash* — one is *patur*, exempt from the punishment of *shchutei chutz* and its *karet*. And now, at the top of 83a, the *breita* continues: one is similarly not liable *lo mishum zarut velo mishum tumah* — neither because a *zar*, a non-*kohen*, performed the *avoda*, nor because a *tamei kohen* performed it — and *velo mishum mechusar begadim* and *velo mishum rachatzat yadayim veraglayim*. In all these cases, when the act is merely an intermediate stage, there is no liability.

## Responses & Defenses

### Negative Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּיוֹצֵק וּבוֹלֵל לָאו נָמֵי לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: אַזְהָרָה לְיוֹצֵק וּבוֹלֵל מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "קְדֹשִׁים יִהְיוּ וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ". מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is that to say, **by inference,** that a priest **who pours** oil onto a meal-offering **and one who mixes** the oil into the meal-offering, in a state of ritual impurity, **does not** violate **even a prohibition? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is **a prohibition against pouring** oil **and** against **mixing** oil in a state of ritual impurity derived? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "They shall be sacred to their God and they shall not profane** the name of their God; for the fire offerings of the Lord, the bread of their God, they offer" (Leviticus 21:6). The Gemara answers: No proof may be cited from that verse, as the prohibition against performing Temple rites that do not complete the service in a state of impurity is **by rabbinic law, and the verse is a mere support,** not a Torah prohibition.

The *gemara* presses the inference: if the *breita* says that when an *avoda tama* — a final, complete act of service — is performed, one *is* liable, what exactly is that liability? *Mai lav misa?* Presumably *misa bidei shamayim*, since the whole list revolves around *misa* or *karet*. The *gemara* attempts to deflect: *lo belav* — perhaps it means only a simple negative prohibition, not death. The listing in the *breita* of liability for a completed act does not necessarily prove that the specific case of a *tamei kohen* rises to *misa bidei shamayim*.

### Secondary Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: וְאֵלּוּ הֵן שֶׁבְּמִיתָה – טָמֵא שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ. תְּיוּבְתָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Zevaḥim* 12:17): **And these are they who** are liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven: Among those enumerated is **an impure** individual **who performed** the Temple **service.** The Gemara concludes: This is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Rav Sheshet, and proof that an impure individual who performs the Temple service is liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven, and young priests and zealots may kill only one who is liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven.

But the *gemara* immediately recognizes a problem with this deflection. If the liability even for a completed *avoda* performed by a *tamei kohen* is only a *lav*, then the *breita*'s statement that there is *no* liability for an intermediary act implies that there would not even be a negative prohibition for such an intermediate act — no violation whatsoever. Yet another *braisa* directly addresses this: *azharah leyotzek ubolel minayin* — how do we know there is a negative prohibition for pouring oil and mixing, specifically when the *kohen* is *tamei*? The answer given is the verse *kedoshim yihyu velo yechalelu* — they shall be holy and they shall not desecrate. When *tamei*, they lack the status of *kedoshim*, and this verse thus supplies a negative prohibition even for the intermediate acts.

The *gemara* attempts another retreat: perhaps this *azharah* is only rabbinic in force, with the verse functioning as a mere *asmachta*. But the *gemara* is not satisfied to leave the matter there.

### Explicit Braita

A direct challenge is lodged: *meisivei* — we have an explicit *braisa* that states *ve'eilu hein shebemisa*, the following incur *misa bidei shamayim*, and that list explicitly includes *tamei sheshimesh* — a *tamei kohen* who performs the *avoda*. The *gemara* concedes: *tiyuvta*. This is a definitive refutation of the position that the *tamei kohen*'s liability is only a *lav*. The *kohen* who is *tamei* and performs the *avoda* is indeed *chayav misa bidei shamayim* — which simultaneously justifies the license given to his colleagues to intervene and coheres with the broader structure of the list, in which completed *avoda* incurs *karet* or *misa bidei shamayim* while intermediary acts incur only a negative prohibition.

## Full Braita List

### Braita Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁבְּמִיתָה – הָאוֹכֵל אֶת הַטֶּבֶל, וְכֹהֵן טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה, וְזָר שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, וְזָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, וְטָמֵא שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, וּטְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, וּמְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים, וּמְחוּסַּר כַּפָּרָה, וְשֶׁלֹּא רָחַץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, וּשְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן, וּפְרוּעֵי רֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § In order to discuss **the** matter **itself,** the Gemara cites the *Tosefta*: **And these** are they **who** are liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven: **One who partakes of untithed produce, and a ritually impure priest who partook of ritually pure *teruma*, and a non-priest who partook of *teruma*, and a non-priest who performed** the Temple **service, and a ritually impure** priest **who performed** the Temple **service, and** a priest who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed, **who performed** the Temple **service, and** a priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments, and** a priest **who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering to complete his purification process as a leper or *zav*, **and** a priest **who did not wash his hands and feet** before beginning the Temple service; **and** priests who performed the Temple service while **intoxicated with wine, and** priests who performed the Temple service with **overgrown** hair on their **heads.**

The *gemara* now turns to examine the entire *braisa* in full — *gufa*, let us look at the body of the text. The *braisa* opens: *ve'eilu shebemisa* — the following carry the penalty of *misa bidei shamayim*.

### Teruma Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל עָרֵל, וְאוֹנֵן, וְיוֹשֵׁב – אֵינָן בְּמִיתָה אֶלָּא בְּאַזְהָרָה. בַּעַל מוּם – רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּמִיתָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בָּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **But** a priest who performs the Temple service while **uncircumcised or** as **an acute mourner** on the day that one of his close relatives dies, **or** a priest who performs the Temple service while **seated, are not** punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven; **rather,** they are liable **for** violating **a prohibition.** With regard to **a blemished** priest who performs the Temple service, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** He is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, **and the Rabbis say:** He is liable only **for** violating **a prohibition.**

The first two entries concern eating *teruma*. *Ha'ochel et hatevel* — one who eats *tevel*, grain from which *teruma* has not yet been separated, is *chayav misa bidei shamayim*. Then, *vekohen tamei she'achal terumah tehorah* — a *kohen* who is *tamei* and eats *teruma* that is *tehorah* is likewise *chayav misa bidei shamayim*, for although a *tahor kohen* may eat *teruma*, a *tamei kohen* eating *tehorah teruma* is a serious violation. The third entry is *vezar she'achal et hateruma* — a non-*kohen* who eats *teruma*. The parallel between a *zar* and a *tamei kohen* is already emerging here as a structural motif.

### Avodah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵזִיד בִּמְעִילָה, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּמִיתָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בָּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** As the Torah states the *halakha* only in the case of one who misuses consecrated property unwittingly, there is a dispute with regard to one who **intentionally performed** an action of **misuse** of consecrated property. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** He is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, **and the Rabbis say:** He is liable only **for** violating **a prohibition.**

Continuing the parallel: *vezar sheshimesh* — a non-*kohen* who performs the *avoda* in the *Beis Hamikdash*, and *vetamei sheshimesh* — a *tamei kohen* who performs the *avoda*. Both are on the list.

### Extended Cases

The *braisa* then extends to more attenuated forms of *tumah*: *vetvul yom sheshimesh* — even a *tvul yom*, one who has immersed in the *mikvah* but for whom nightfall has not yet come, so the *tumah* has not been fully removed, still carries a residual *tumah*, and performing the *avoda* in this state is *chayav misa*. Then *umechusar begadim* — a *kohen* who is not wearing the *bigdei kehuna*, and *umechusar kapparah* — one whose *tumah* requires not only immersion and nightfall but also a *korban* the following morning, such as a *zav*. Until that *korban* is brought, he retains a status of *tumah*, and performing *avoda* renders him *chayav misa*. Finally, *veshelo rachatz yadayim veraglayim* — a *kohen* who has not washed his hands and feet before performing the *avoda*.

### Non-Mita Violations

The *braisa* then pivots to a category of violations that *do* desecrate the *avoda* — rendering the service invalid — yet do not incur *misa bidei shamayim*, only a simple negative prohibition. These are *shtuyei yayin* — a *kohen* who is intoxicated, *parua rosh* — a *kohen* whose hair has grown wild beyond thirty days, and — as a separate group — performing the *avoda* as an *arel* (uncircumcised *kohen*), as an *onen* (one in the immediate state of mourning after a family death), or while sitting rather than standing. All of these invalidate the *avoda* and constitute a *lav*, but the *kohen* is not *chayav misa bidei shamayim*.

### Baal Mum & Meila

Two additional cases are subjects of dispute. Regarding *ba'al mum* — a *kohen* with a physical blemish — *Rebbi* holds that performing the *avoda* is *bemisa*, for the blemish removes him from the category of a valid officiating *kohen* much as *tumah* does. The *chachamim*, however, maintain that it is only *be'azhara*, a simple negative prohibition. A second dispute concerns *hameizid bemeila* — one who intentionally commits *meila*, misappropriating consecrated objects. This is somewhat distinct from the *kohen*-service cases, but *Rebbi* again holds it is *bemisa*, while the *chachamim* say it is only *be'azhara*. As the *gemara* notes, this dispute is relevant to an earlier discussion of *hagonev et hakasva* — stealing a *kli sharet* — which also appeared under the *kana'im pogim bo* framework, and sits more comfortably with *Rebbi*'s position.

## Ochel Tevel Derivation

### Tevel Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹכֵל אֶת הַטֶּבֶל, מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִנַּיִין לְאוֹכֵל אֶת הַטֶּבֶל שֶׁהוּא בְּמִיתָה? דִּכְתִיב: "וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ אֶת קׇדְשֵׁי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל [אֵת] אֲשֶׁר יָרִימוּ לַה׳". בַּעֲתִידִים לִתְרוֹם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: It was taught in the *baraita* that **one who partakes of untithed produce** is liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **As Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: From where** is it derived **that one who partakes of untithed produce** is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven? It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "They shall not desecrate the sacred items of the children of Israel, which they will set apart unto the Lord"** (Leviticus 22:15). From the fact that it is formulated in the future tense: "Which they will set apart," it is clear that **the verse is speaking** about items **that are destined to be separated** in the future, i.e. untithed produce from which *teruma* and tithes will be separated.

Having enumerated the full *braisa*, the *gemara* proceeds to examine each entry and establish its scriptural basis. The first: *ha'ochel et hatevel minalan* — how do we know that eating *tevel* incurs *misa bidei shamayim*?

### Lo Yechalelu Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיָלֵיף "חִילּוּל" "חִילּוּל" מִתְּרוּמָה, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּמִיתָה – אַף כָּאן בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *tanna* **derives** the punishment by means of a verbal analogy: The term of **desecration** written with regard to untithed produce is derived **from** the term of **desecration** written with regard to an impure priest who partakes of ***teruma*** (see Leviticus 22:9). **Just as there,** with regard to *teruma*, he is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, **so too here,** with regard to untithed produce, he is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven.

*D'amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Eliezer* — Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer, directly addressing this: the verse states *velo yechalelu et kodshei benei Yisrael et asher yarimu laHashem* — they shall not desecrate the sanctified things of Israel, that which they will separate as *teruma* to God. The context of the verse is *teruma*, but the *derasha* pivots on the word *yarimu* in the future tense: *ba'atidim litrom hakatuv medaber* — the verse is speaking about that which is *yet to have teruma taken from it*, i.e., *tevel*. Eating *tevel* is thus a violation of this verse. This also accounts for the *Rambam*'s ruling that the prohibition of *misa* applies to *tevel* only before *teruma* has been separated, not to grain from which *teruma* has been taken but *ma'aser* has not yet been separated.

### Chillul Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף "חִילּוּל" "חִילּוּל" מִנּוֹתָר: מָה לְהַלָּן בְּכָרֵת, אַף כָּאן בְּכָרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let us derive** the punishment by means of a different verbal analogy: Derive the term of **desecration** written with regard to untithed produce from the term of **desecration** written with regard to meat **leftover from an offering after the time allotted for its consumption [*notar*]** (see Leviticus 19:6, 8). **Just as there,** with regard to *notar*, he is punished **with *karet*, so too here,** with regard to untithed produce, he is punished **with *karet*.**

But this verse establishes only that there is a prohibition. How do we derive *misa bidei shamayim* specifically? *Veyalif chillul chillul miteruma* — through a *gezera shava* on the word *chillul*: the verse here uses *yechalelu*, while a verse concerning *teruma* eaten by a *tamei kohen* states *umetu vo ki yechalleluhu* — they shall die when they desecrate it. Since both verses use the language of *chillul* and both concern *teruma* (one actual, one yet to be separated), we derive: *ma halan bemisa af kan bemisa* — just as there it is *misa*, here too it is *misa*.

### Teruma vs Notar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִתְּרוּמָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן תְּרוּמָה: חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, הוּתְּרָה, בְּרַבִּים, פֵּירוֹת, פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is reasonable** to say that **he should have derived** the punishment for partaking of untithed produce **from *teruma*, as** there are many elements common to *teruma* and untithed produce. Unlike *notar*, they are germane to the *halakhot* of ***teruma*;** they are not in effect **outside of Eretz** Yisrael; both of them can become **permitted,** as an impure priest can immerse and partake of *teruma*, and untithed produce can become permitted by taking *teruma* and tithes; in both cases, the term of desecration is formulated **in the plural,** while with regard to *notar* it is formulated in the singular; both apply to **produce;** and neither the *halakha* of **an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its designated time [*piggul*], nor** the *halakha* of ***notar*,** apply in either case.

A challenge is immediately raised: why learn *tevel* from *teruma*? There is also a *chillul* connection to *notar* — the verse states *ki et kodesh Hashem chillel* regarding leftover sacrificial meat, and *notar* carries *karet*, not *misa bidei shamayim*. One might therefore argue: *mah halan bekaret af kan bekaret* — perhaps the *ochel tevel* incurs *karet*, not *misa*.

The *gemara* responds that learning from *teruma* is more cogent — *mistabra miterumah hava lei lemilaf* — because of the multiple points of correspondence between *tevel* and *teruma*. First, *tevel* and *teruma* share the same name: one is actual *teruma*, the other is grain destined for *teruma*. Second, neither applies in *chutz la'aretz*, whereas *notar* arises in the context of *korbanot* which are not brought outside *Eretz Yisrael*. Third, both admit of *heter* — *tevel* can be fixed by separating *teruma*, and the *tamei kohen* can be fixed by immersing in a *mikvah*; *notar*, once it has become *notar*, cannot be rectified. Fourth, in both cases the verse uses *yechalelu* in the plural (*berabbim*), whereas *notar* uses the singular *chillel*. Fifth, both apply to grain (*peirot*) and not to *korbanot*, whereas *notar* is specific to sacrificial contexts. Finally, neither *tevel* nor *teruma* can acquire the status of *pigul* or *notar*, as they are not *korbanot*.

### Ravina's Gezerah Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִנּוֹתָר הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן פְּסוּל אוֹכֶל, אֵין לוֹ הֶיתֵּר בְּמִקְוֶה. הָנָךְ נְפִישָׁן. רָבִינָא אָמַר: חִילּוּל דְּרַבִּים מֵחִילּוּל דְּרַבִּים עָדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that conclusion: **On the contrary, he should have derived** the punishment for the partaking of untithed produce **from *notar*, as** there are elements common to *notar* and untithed produce. Both cases relate to the **unfitness of the food,** while in the case of an impure individual partaking of *teruma*, it is the person who is unfit; and in both cases **there is no permission** granted by means of immersion **in the ritual bath,** while in the case of an impure individual partaking of *teruma*, immersion is effective. The Gemara answers: **Those** elements common to *teruma* and untithed produce **are more numerous,** therefore that is the preferred derivation. **Ravina says:** There is no need for a tally of common elements, as deriving **desecration in the plural** by means of a verbal analogy **from desecration in the plural is preferable.**

Ravina adds an additional — and in a sense dispositive — consideration: *chillul derabbim mechillul derabbim adif*. Setting aside all the substantive comparisons, the words are identical: *lo yechalelu* appears both in the *tevel* verse and in the *teruma* context. That lexical identity constitutes a proper *gezera shava*. This is what anchors *tevel* to *misa bidei shamayim* rather than to the *karet* of *notar*.

## Tamei Kohen Teruma

### Veshomru Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹהֵן טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מִנַּיִין לְכֹהֵן טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה שֶׁהוּא בְּמִיתָה בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם? דִּכְתִיב: "וְשָׁמְרוּ אֶת מִשְׁמַרְתִּי וְלֹא יִשְׂאוּ עָלָיו חֵטְא וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to analyze the *baraita*. **From where do we** derive **that an impure priest who partook of pure *teruma*** is liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven? It is derived **as Shmuel says: From where** is it derived with regard **to an impure priest who partakes of pure *teruma* that** he is punished **with death at the hand of Heaven?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is written** with regard to a priest who partakes of *teruma*: **"And they shall keep my charge lest they bear a sin for it** and die therein if they desecrate it: I am the Lord" (Leviticus 22:9).

The *gemara* now addresses the second entry in the *braisa*: *vekohen tamei she'achal terumah tehorah minalan* — how do we know that a *tamei kohen* eating *tehorah teruma* is *chayav misa bidei shamayim*? Again, Shmuel provides the source: *minayin lekohen tamei she'achal terumah tehorah shehi bemisa bidei shamayim?* The verse states *veshamru et mishmarti velo yisu alav cheit umetu vo* — they shall keep My charge, they shall not bear sin on account of it, and they shall die because of it — and the continuation is *ki yechalleluhu*. The context of this passage is *tumah* and the keeping of *mishmarti*, which the *gemara* associates with *teruma* (based in part on Rashi's observation that the *pesukim* describe the progression from *tvila* and *ha'arev shemesh* to eating *teruma*, and that after sunset but before the morning *korban*, the only thing newly permitted is *teruma*, not *korbanot*). The verse thus explicitly supplies the punishment of *misa* for a *tamei kohen* who eats *teruma*.

It is worth noting the full arc of this *limud*: it was precisely this verse's *chillul* language — *ki yechalleluhu* — that served as the anchor for the *gezera shava* through which we derived *misa* for eating *tevel*. So the *tevel* derivation above was itself dependent on establishing the *teruma* case here, which is now being derived directly from its own verse.

### Tehorah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְהוֹרָה – אִין, טְמֵאָה – לָא. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִנַּיִין לְכֹהֵן טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּמִיתָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וּמֵתוּ בוֹ כִּי יְחַלְּלֻהוּ",
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: From where is it derived that for partaking of **ritually pure** *teruma*, **yes,** the impure priest is liable, but for partaking of **impure** *teruma*, **no,** the priest is not liable? It is derived **as Shmuel says** that **Rabbi Eliezer says: From where** is it derived **that an impure priest who partakes of impure *teruma* is not** punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated** in that verse: **"And die therein if they desecrate it,"**

The *gemara* notes a critical limitation embedded in the verse: *tehorah ein tmeiah lo* — the *misa* penalty applies only when the *teruma* itself is *tehorah*. If both the *kohen* and the *teruma* are *tamei*, there is no *misa*. This follows from Shmuel citing Rabbi Elazar: *umetu vo ki yechalleluhu* — the death penalty is triggered by *desecration*, and *perat lazu shemechulelet ve'omedet* — the verse excludes the case where the *teruma* is already desecrated, already *tamei* in its own right. The act of a *tamei kohen* eating already-*tamei teruma* does not constitute a new act of *chillul*, and therefore the *misa* penalty does not apply. One might wonder how the *tamei kohen* can eat *tehorah teruma* without rendering it *tamei* through contact — the *gemara* implies scenarios such as someone else placing it in his mouth, or grain that never came into contact with liquid after harvest and therefore could not contract *tumah* in the first place.

## Zar Eats Teruma

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזָר שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, אָמַר רַב: זָר שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה לוֹקֶה. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: לֵימָא מָר בְּמִיתָה, דִּכְתִיב "וְכׇל זָר לֹא יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ"?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues in its analysis of the *baraita*: **A non-priest who partook of *teruma*** is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven. **Rav says: A non-priest who partook of *teruma* is flogged. Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav: Let the Master say** that he is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, **as it is written:** "And they shall keep my charge lest they bear a sin for it and die therein if they desecrate it: I am the Lord Who sanctifies them. **No non-priest shall eat of the sacred,** a tenant of a priest, or a hired servant, shall not eat of the sacred" (Leviticus 22:9–10). Based on the juxtaposition between them, one should say that just as an impure priest who partakes of *teruma* is punished with death at the hand of Heaven, so too a non-priest who partakes of *teruma* is punished with death at the hand of Heaven.

The next entry in the *braisa* is *vezar she'achal et haterumah* — a non-*kohen* who eats *teruma*. Here a striking dispute emerges. Rav states: *zar she'achal et haterumah lokeh* — a *zar* who eats *teruma* receives lashes. The use of *lokeh* rather than any heavier formula implies that *misa bidei shamayim* is not incurred — the punishment is only the negative prohibition and its associated flogging.

### Challenging Rav

Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenge Rav directly: why not say *bemisa*? They point to the very verse that just established the *tamei kohen*'s liability. The *pesukim* read: *veshamru et mishmarti velo yisu alav cheit umetu vo* — carrying the explicit penalty of *misa* — and the very next verse states *vechol zar lo yochal kodesh*, no non-*kohen* shall eat *kodesh* (i.e., *teruma*). The juxtaposition of these two *pesukim* would seem to extend the *misa* of the first verse to the prohibition stated in the second.

### Rav's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "אֲנִי ה׳ מְקַדְּשָׁם" – הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן. מֵיתִיבִי: וְאֵלּוּ הֵן שֶׁבְּמִיתָה – זָר הָאוֹכֵל אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה. מַתְנִיתָא אַדְּרַב קָא רָמֵית? רַב תַּנָּא הוּא וּפָלֵיג!
>
> **Translation:** Rav responded: The phrase **"I am the Lord Who sanctifies them" ends the matter** of death, so the matter of a non-priest who partakes of *teruma* is not juxtaposed to it. The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav from the *baraita*: **And these are they who** are liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven: **A non-priest who partakes of *teruma*.** The Gemara explains: **Are you raising a contradiction** from **a *baraita* to** the opinion **of Rav? Rav is a *tanna*, and** as such has the authority to **dispute** the determination of the *baraita*.

Rav deflects by pointing to the verse separating these two *pesukim*: *ani Hashem mekadeisham* — I am God who sanctifies them. This phrase, Rav argues, *hifsik ha'inyan* — creates a paragraph break, closing out the discussion of the *tamei kohen* and beginning a new independent topic. The *zar*'s prohibition against eating *teruma* therefore stands alone, without any *misa* appended to it from the prior *pasuk*. This is, as the *gemara* acknowledges, a reasonable reading of the text.

## Avodah Performers

### Zar Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: "וְהַזָּר הַקָּרֵב יוּמָת".
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The punishment for **a non-priest who performed** the Temple **service** is death at the hand of Heaven, **as it is written:** "You and your sons with you shall safeguard your priesthood with regard to every matter of the altar…**and any non-priest who approaches shall die"** (Numbers 18:7).

The *gemara* turns to *vezar sheshimesh* — the non-*kohen* who performs the *avoda* in the *Beis Hamikdash*. Here there is no need for derivation or *gezera shava*: *dichsiv vehazar hakarev yumos* — the Torah states in three explicit words that a *zar* who draws near to perform the service shall die. This is among the most direct statements of *misa* in the entire *parasha* of *avodah*.

### Tamei Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטָמֵא שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, כְּדִבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין מֵרַב יוֹסֵף: מִנַּיִין לְטָמֵא שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ שֶׁהוּא בְּמִיתָה? דִּכְתִיב: "דַּבֵּר אֶל אַהֲרֹן וְאֶל בָּנָיו וְיִנָּזְרוּ מִקׇּדְשֵׁי בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ אֶת שֵׁם קׇדְשִׁי".
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The punishment for **an impure** priest **who performed** the Temple **service** is death at the hand of Heaven. The source for this is explained: **As Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin asked of Rav Yosef: From where** is it derived **that an impure** priest **who performed** the Temple **service is** punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven? Rav Yosef answered: It is derived from a verse, **as it is written** in the context of admonishing priests to avoid impurity: **"Speak to Aaron and his sons that they shall separate themselves from the sacred items of the children of Israel** that they sanctify to Me, **and they shall not desecrate My sacred name:** I am the Lord" (Leviticus 22:2).

The case of *vetamei sheshimesh* — a *tamei kohen* who performs the *avoda* — now receives its derivation in the *gemara*'s own voice, as elaborated by Rav Chiya bar Avin in a question posed to Rav Yosef. *Minayin letamei sheshimesh shehu bemisa?* The relevant verse is *dabru el Aharon ve'el banav veyinazru mikodshei benei Yisrael velo yechalelu et shem kodshi* — they shall separate themselves from the holy things of Israel and not desecrate My holy name. The context is explicitly *tumah*: the verse forbids *avodah* while *tamei* but does not itself state the penalty. However, the verse uses the language of *chillul* — *yechalelu* — and *veyalif chillul chillul miteruma*: we have already established that by *teruma* the verse states *umetu vo ki yechalleluhu*, linking *chillul* to *misa*. Thus: *mah halan bemisa af kan bemisa* — just as the *tamei kohen* eating *teruma* is *chayav misa*, so too a *tamei kohen* performing the *avoda* is *chayav misa*. And the logic is compelling: if eating *teruma* while *tamei* is *misa bidei shamayim*, all the more so is performing the *avoda* while *tamei*.

### Notar Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיָלֵיף "חִילּוּל" "חִילּוּל" מִתְּרוּמָה, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּמִיתָה – אַף כָּאן בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef continues: **And** the *tanna* **derives** the punishment by means of a verbal analogy. He derives the meaning of the term of **desecration** written with regard to an impure priest who performs the Temple service **from** the term of **desecration** written with regard to an impure priest who partakes of ***teruma*** (see Leviticus 22:9). **Just as there,** with regard to *teruma*, he is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, **so too here,** with regard to an impure priest who performs the Temple service, he is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven.

The *gemara* again raises the challenge: *veneilaf chillul chillul minotar* — why not derive from *notar*, where the verse similarly states *ki et kodesh Hashem chillel*, yielding *karet* rather than *misa*? The concern is *mah halan karet af kan karet*.

### Teruma Superior

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף "חִילּוּל" "חִילּוּל" מִנּוֹתָר: מָה לְהַלָּן כָּרֵת, אַף כָּאן כָּרֵת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us derive** the punishment by means of a different verbal analogy: Derive the term of **desecration** written with regard to an impure priest who performs the Temple service **from** the term of **desecration** written with regard to ***notar*.** This would teach that **just as there,** with regard to *notar*, he is punished with ***karet*, so too here,** with regard to an impure priest who performs the Temple service, he is punished with ***karet*.**

The *gemara* again resolves that learning from *teruma* is superior — *mistabra miterumah hava lei lemilaf*. The points of comparison are the same as before but now tailored to the *avodah* context: *sheken guf tamei* — both cases involve the *person* being *tamei* (as opposed to *notar* where the issue is with the object), and *mikveh berabbim* — both can be resolved by *mikvah* immersion, and both use *yechalelu* in the plural. The counter-argument — that *notar* and *avoda* share the quality of *kodesh panim* (inner sanctity of the *Beis Hamikdash*), and that *pigul* and *notar* are both relevant in the *avoda* context but not in *teruma* — is noted. But as before, the *gemara* resolves: *chillul derabbim chillul derabbim adif* — the identical language of *yechalelu* connecting the *avoda* verse and the *teruma* verse is the decisive anchor.

## Extended Tamei Cases

### Tvul Yom Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִתְּרוּמָה הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן: גּוּף, טָמֵא, מִקְוֶה, בְּרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is reasonable** to say that it is **from *teruma*** that **he should have derived** the punishment for an impure priest who performs the Temple service, **as** there are many elements common to an impure priest who partakes of *teruma* and an impure priest who performs the Temple service. In both of these cases, unlike *notar*, the problem relates to the **body** of the priest; both relate to **impurity;** in both, the issue can be resolved through immersion in **a ritual bath;** and in both, the term of desecration is formulated **in the plural,** while with regard to *notar* it is formulated in the singular.

With the core cases established, the *gemara* extends the analysis to progressively more attenuated forms of *tumah*. *Tvul yom sheshimesh* — a *kohen* who has immersed in the *mikvah* but for whom nightfall has not yet arrived, rendering him a *tvul yom* with only residual *tumah* — is also *chayav misa bidei shamayim* for performing the *avoda*. The source is a *braisa* in which Rabbi Simai asks: *remez letvul yom she'im avad chilel minayin?* — how do we know that a *tvul yom* who performed the *avoda* has *desecrated* it, rendering the service invalid? The verse cited is *kedoshim yihyu leloheihem velo yechalelu*. The *gemara* notes that we do not need this verse to establish the basic prohibition against a fully *tamei kohen* performing *avoda*, since that is derived more explicitly from *veyinazru mikodshei benei Yisrael*. The surplus verse — *kedoshim yihyu velo yechalelu* — is therefore applied: *tenahu inyan letvul yom*, it extends the prohibition to the *tvul yom*. The verse establishes that even the *tvul yom* desecrates the *avoda* by performing it.

From there, *veyalif chillul chillul miteruma*: since the verse again employs *yechalelu*, we connect it to the *teruma* verse's *umetu vo ki yechalleluhu*, and derive *mah halan bemisa af kan bemisa* — the *tvul yom* who performs *avoda* is *chayav misa bidei shamayim*. The *gemara* does not revisit the *notar* alternative here, having already established the priority of the *teruma*-based *gezera shava*.

### Begadim Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִנּוֹתָר הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן קֹדֶשׁ, פְּנִים, פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר! חִילּוּל דְּרַבִּים מֵחִילּוּל דְּרַבִּים עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that conclusion: **On the contrary,** it is **from *notar*** that **he should have derived** the punishment for an impure priest who performs the Temple service, **as** there are elements common to *notar* and an impure priest who performs the Temple service. Unlike *teruma*, both are cases involving **sacrificial** matters; both involve acts performed **inside** the Temple; and in both cases, the disqualification of ***piggul* and** the disqualification of ***notar*** are applicable. The Gemara explains: Although the number of elements common to an impure priest who partakes of *teruma* equals the number of elements common to *notar*, deriving **desecration in the plural** by means of a verbal analogy **from desecration in the plural is preferable.**

The *braisa*'s inclusion of *mechusar begadim* — a *kohen* not wearing the prescribed *bigdei kehuna* — is derived from a verse cited by Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, with some transmitting it in the name of Rabbi Elazar berabbi Shimon. The verse is *vechagarta osam avnet* — you shall gird them with the sash — followed by *vehaysa lahem kehunah lechukat olam* — and it shall be for them an eternal priestly ordinance. From this, the *gemara* draws the foundational principle: *bizman shebigdeihem aleihem kehunasam aleihem* — when their garments are upon them, their *kehuna* is upon them; *ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunasam aleihem* — when their garments are not upon them, their *kehuna* is not upon them. A *kohen* performing *avoda* without his vestments is therefore treated as a *zar* — *vehavu lehu zarim* — and since a *zar* who performs *avoda* is *chayav misa*, so too is the *mechusar begadim*.

## Extended Tamei Cases…

### Tosafot on Begadim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי סִימַאי אוֹמֵר, רֶמֶז לִטְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "קְדֹשִׁים יִהְיוּ לֵאלֹהֵיהֶם וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ".
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The punishment for a priest who was ritually impure, **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed, and **who performed** the Temple **service,** is death at the hand of Heaven. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: It is derived **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Simai says: From where** in the Torah is the **allusion with regard to** a priest **who immersed that day, that if he performed** the Temple **service he desecrated** that service? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "They shall be sacred to their God and they shall not desecrate** the name of their God" (Leviticus 21:6).

Tosafot raises a pointed question here (*hiksha Rav Yaakov deOrleans*): there is an explicit verse in *Parshas Tetzaveh* that seems to state directly that a *kohen* performing *avoda* without his *begadim* is *chayav misa* — why does the *gemara* need the circuitous derivation via the *ein kehunasam aleihem* principle? Tosafot answers that the explicit verse in *Tetzaveh* mentioning death is written in the immediate context of the *michnasayim* — the linen undergarments protecting against exposure of *ervah* — and could be read as pertaining specifically to that, not to the general principle of *mechusar begadim*. Rashi on the *pesukim* there actually reads the verse as referring to *misa* for lacking *bigdim* generally, but the Ramban sides with the *gemara*'s understanding as reflected in Tosafot: the *gemara* clearly derived the *mechusar begadim* case through the *ein kehunasam aleihem* principle, implying that the explicit verse was not taken to cover this case, and was instead understood as referring narrowly to the *michnasayim* context.

### Kappurim Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְטָמֵא שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ, דְּנָפְקָא לַן מִ"וְיִנָּזְרוּ", תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לִטְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **If** this verse **is not** written with regard to the **matter of an impure** priest **who performed** the Temple **service, as** that *halakha* is **derived for us from** the verse: **"That they shall separate themselves** from the sacred items of the children of Israel" (Leviticus 22:2), then **apply it** to the **matter of** a priest **who immersed that day who performed** the Temple **service.** Although he is no longer impure in every sense, the priest remains impure in terms of the prohibitions of partaking of *teruma* and sacrificial food, and of entering the Temple.

The final *tumah*-related entry is *mechusar kippurim* — one whose *tumah* requires not only *mikvah* immersion and nightfall but also a *korban* brought the following morning, such as a *zav* or *zava*. Even after immersion, until the *korban* is brought, this person retains a status of *tumah*. The derivation is from the verse *vechiper aleha hakohen vetahera* — the *kohen* shall atone for her and she shall become *tehorah*. Rav Huna explains: *vetahera miklal shehi tmeiah* — the verse's statement that she becomes *tehorah* only upon atonement implies that prior to bringing the *korban* she retains a *tamei* status. And since we have already established that a *tamei kohen* performing *avoda* is *chayav misa bidei shamayim*, it follows that a *mechusar kippurim* who performs *avoda* is likewise *chayav misa*.

### Tosafot on Kappurim

Tosafot raises a natural question: why not simply say that *mechusar kippurim* is *tamei* and therefore — by the already-established rule that *tamei* incurs *misa* — he is *chayav misa*? The answer is that the *mechusar kippurim*'s *tumah* is even more attenuated than that of the *tvul yom*, and so one might have thought it insufficient to trigger the full *misa* liability. Tosafot therefore explains that we would not have been able to make this argument for *mechusar kippurim* had we not first derived via the *kedoshim yihyu* verse that even the *tvul yom*'s diminished *tumah* is sufficient. Once we know that a *tvul yom* — despite having already immersed — is still considered *tamei* enough for *misa*, the verse of *vetahera* that reveals a residual *tumah* for the *mechusar kippurim* can similarly be treated as establishing a cognizable *tumah* status, and the general rule applies.

## Final Mita Cases

### Washing Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיָלֵיף חִילּוּל חִילּוּל מִתְּרוּמָה, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּמִיתָה – אַף כָּאן בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *tanna* **derives** the punishment by means of a verbal analogy. He derives the meaning of the term of **desecration** written with regard to a priest who immersed that day who performed the Temple service **from** the term of **desecration** written with regard to an impure priest who partakes of ***teruma*** (see Leviticus 22:9). **Just as there,** with regard to *teruma*, he is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven, **so too here,** with regard to an impure priest who performs the Temple service, he is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven.

The *braisa*'s entry of *shelo rachatz yadayim veraglayim* — a *kohen* who has not washed his hands and feet before performing the *avoda* — has an explicit source: *dichsiv bevo'am el ohel moed yirchatzu mayim velo yamutu* — when they come to the Tent of Meeting they shall wash with water so that they shall not die. The verse itself states the penalty unambiguously.

### Wine Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וּמָטוּ בָּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: "וְחָגַרְתָּ אֹתָם אַבְנֵט".
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** the punishment for a priest **who is lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments** and who performed the Temple service is death at the hand of Heaven. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that this is his punishment? **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and** there are those who **determined** that this *halakha* is a tradition that was cited **in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon:** It is stated: **"You shall gird them with a belt,** Aaron and his sons, and you shall wrap the headdresses upon them; and they shall have the priesthood as an eternal statute, and you shall inaugurate Aaron and his sons" (Exodus 29:9).

Equally explicit is the source for *shtuyei yayin* — a *kohen* who is intoxicated and performs the *avoda*: *yayin vesheichar al tesht atah uvanecha itcha bevo'achem el ohel moed velo yamutu* — do not drink wine or strong drink when you enter the Tent of Meeting, so that you shall not die. Here again the *misa* is written into the verse directly.

### Hair Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁבִּגְדֵיהֶם עֲלֵיהֶם – כְּהוּנָּתָם עֲלֵיהֶם; אֵין בִּגְדֵיהֶם עֲלֵיהֶם – אֵין כְּהוּנָּתָם עֲלֵיהֶם. וְהָווּ לְהוּ זָרִים, וְאָמַר מָר: זָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ – בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** From that verse it is derived: **At a time when their vestments are upon them, their priesthood is upon them;** but when **their vestments are not upon them, their priesthood is not upon them.** Therefore, priests who are lacking the requisite priestly vestments **assume** the status of **non-priests** in this respect, **and the Master says** earlier in the *baraita*: **A non-priest who performs** the Temple **service** is punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven.

The case of *parua rosh* — a *kohen* who has allowed his hair to grow wild beyond thirty days — is not derived from the *Torah* itself but from a verse in Yechezkel: *rosham lo yegalechu ufera lo yeshaleichu* — they shall not shave their heads nor let their hair grow wild. The *gemara* then notes that the very next verse in Yechezkel states *veyayin lo yishtu* — and they shall not drink wine, juxtaposing the *parua rosh* prohibition with the *shtuyei yayin* prohibition: *itkesh parua rosh leshtuei yayin* — *parua rosh* is thereby linked to *shtuyei yayin* via *hekesh*. Since we know that *shtuyei yayin* is *bemisa*, by the same token *parua rosh bemisa* as well. The *gemara* is aware that ordinarily one does not derive *halacha* from the *nevi'im*, but notes that *chiyuv misa bidei shamayim* is not a practical *halacha* that we enact, and moreover, Yechezkel in such contexts is typically understood as articulating principles that were already established or implicit in the earlier tradition.
