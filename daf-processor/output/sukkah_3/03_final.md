# Sukkah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sechach & Height

### Sechach Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלְּהוּ כְּרַבָּה לָא אָמְרִי, הָהוּא יְדִיעָה לְדוֹרוֹת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why each of the Sages cited his own source and did not accept the sources cited by the others. **All of them,** Rabbi Zeira and Rava, **did not say** that the fact that a *sukka* more than twenty cubits high is unfit is derived from the verse: "So that your future generations will know that I caused the children of Israel to reside in *sukkot* when I took them out of the land of Egypt" (Leviticus 23:43), **as** did **Rabba,** because in their opinion **that** verse does not mandate one to be aware that he is sitting in a *sukka*; rather, it mandates **knowledge for** future **generations** of the exodus from Egypt.

By way of review, today's *daf* — *daf* 3 of *Sukkah* — continues from the central themes introduced in the previous session. The governing insight established at the outset is that the concept of *sukkah* is primarily defined by its *sechach*. This was clear from the fact that several *amoraim*, when explaining the problem of a *sukkah* too tall, focused specifically on the *sechach* being too high, and they grounded their positions in *pesukim* that use the word *sukkah* — a word understood to denote the *sechach* itself. Three distinct explanations were offered for why excessive height is problematic, each rooted in a different understanding of the *sechach*'s role.

### Three Key Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי זֵירָא — אִם כֵּן לֵימָא קְרָא: ״וְחוּפָּה תִּהְיֶה לְצֵל יוֹמָם״, וּמַאי ״וְסוּכָּה תִּהְיֶה לְצֵל יוֹמָם״ — שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Zeira,** how would he respond to this objection? The Gemara answers that Rabbi Zeira could say: **If** it is **so** that the verse is merely a metaphor, **let the verse say: And there shall be a canopy for shade in the daytime from the heat,** which is the term used in the previous verse. **And what is** the meaning of: **"And there shall be a *sukka* for shade in the daytime from the heat"? Learn from it two** matters: One is the plain meaning of the verse, that God will be a canopy of glory for the Jewish people, and the second is that the essence of a *sukka* is to have the roofing provide shade.

The first issue is the question of permanence versus temporariness: one reason the *sukkah* must not be too tall is that extreme height renders the structure less temporary in character. The second issue is visibility — the importance of being able to see the *sechach*, perhaps connected to *lema'an yedu doroteichem*, the requirement that the generation know and recognize the *sukkah* for what it is. The third issue concerns the *sechach*'s capacity to provide shade: according to one opinion, the *sechach* must not merely possess the intrinsic quality of blocking sunlight, but must actually cast shade within the *sukkah*, and if the *sechach* is too high, it cannot do so effectively.

### Tosafot & Rain

On the theme of temporariness, the *Gemara* makes the notable point that *dirat arai* — temporary dwelling — is not defined by building material; one may construct a *sukkah* from brick. Yet Tosafot raise a sharper question: can the *sechach* itself become so permanent that the structure loses its character as a *sukkah*? Tosafot conclude that it can. Their primary criterion for *sechach* permanence is impermeability to rain — if rain cannot penetrate the *sechach* at all, it has crossed into a category of permanent roofing. There is also the related notion, apparently found in the *Yerushalmi*, of being able to see the stars through the *sechach*, though Tosafot do not treat this as a strict requirement. Additional markers of permanence — *sechach* nailed down with planks and sealed with plaster — are also raised, even if not precisely quantified. The identity of the *sukkah* is, in any case, bound up intimately with the character of its *sechach*.

## Hilni Case

### Hilni Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּהֵילֶנִי הַמַּלְכָּה בְּלוֹד שֶׁהָיְתָה סוּכָּתָהּ גְּבוֹהָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה, וְהָיוּ זְקֵנִים נִכְנָסִין וְיוֹצְאִין לְשָׁם וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לָהּ דָּבָר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָיה? אִשָּׁה הָיְתָה וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַסּוּכָּה. אָמַר לָהֶן: וַהֲלֹא שִׁבְעָה בָּנִים הֲווֹ לָהּ, וְעוֹד: כׇּל מַעֲשֶׂיהָ לֹא עָשְׂתָה אֶלָּא עַל פִּי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said:** There was **an incident involving Queen Helene in Lod where her *sukka* was more than twenty cubits high, and the Elders were entering and exiting** the *sukka* **and did not say anything to her** about the *sukka* not being fit.  The Rabbis **said to him:** Is there **proof from there? She was,** after all, **a woman and** therefore **exempt from the** mitzva of ***sukka*.** Consequently, the fact that her *sukka* was not fit did not warrant a comment from the Elders.  Rabbi Yehuda **said to them** in response: **Didn't she have seven sons** and therefore require a fit *sukka*? **And furthermore, she performed all of her actions only in accordance with** the directives of **the Sages.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the story of Queen Hilni, who sat in a *sukkah* higher than twenty *amot*. This case had been cited by Rabbi Yehuda as a proof, to which the Rabbis retorted that it was no proof at all — a woman is exempt from *sukkah*, and her personal practice carries no normative weight. Rabbi Yehuda replied that she had sons, and as the *Gemara* elaborated, at least her sons were obligated at a rabbinic level, by virtue of the obligation of *chinuch*.

### Walls Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּשֶׁאֵין דְּפָנוֹת מַגִּיעוֹת לַסְּכָךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת, דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל מַלְכָּה לֵישֵׁב בְּסוּכָּה שֶׁאֵין דְּפָנוֹת מַגִּיעוֹת לַסְּכָךְ, מִשּׁוּם אַוֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explores the statements of the *amora'im* who quoted Rav in light of this *baraita*. **Granted, according to the one,** Rabbi Yoshiya, **who said that** it is specifically **in** a case **where the walls** of the *sukka* **do not reach** up to **the roofing** that there is a **dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, the *baraita* can be explained as dealing with a *sukka* of that type, as **it is customary for a queen to reside in a *sukka* in which the walls do not reach** up to **the roofing,** **due to** the fresh **air** that circulates through the openings in the wall.

The *Gemara* now uses the Hilni case to test which of the three explanations for the twenty-*amah* limit is correct. It begins: *bishlama* — this works well according to the view that the entire problem of excessive height arises only when the walls do not reach the *sechach*. According to that position, the concern is whether one will be able to perceive the *sechach*: if the walls reach the *sechach*, the eye is naturally drawn upward to where the wall meets the roof, and there is no problem regardless of height. The debate is therefore only relevant when the walls fall short of the *sechach*. This fits the Hilni case perfectly, since it is entirely plausible that a queen's *sukkah* would be constructed with walls not extending all the way to the *sechach* — built that way, the *Gemara* suggests, *mishum avira*, to allow for air circulation and a pleasant breeze. The case is therefore squarely within the terms of the debate.

### Small Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה מַחְלוֹקֶת, וְכִי דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל מַלְכָּה לֵישֵׁב בְּסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to the one who said** that it is specifically in the case of **a small *sukka*** that there is a **dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, in which case the *baraita* is referring to a case where Queen Helene resided in a small *sukka*, **is it customary for a queen to reside in a small *sukka*** whose area is less than four cubits squared?

However, the explanation presents difficulties for the second opinion — namely, that the problem is the *sechach*'s practical capacity to provide shade. According to this view, the debate about the twenty-*amah* limit only arises in a very small *sukkah*: because the *sechach* covers a small area, if it is too high it will fail to provide shade below. A large *sukkah* poses no such problem even with elevated *sechach*. But then the *Gemara* asks: *vachi darkah shel malka leishev besukkah ketana* — would a queen sit in such a tiny *sukkah*, a mere four by four *amot*? Surely Hilni's *sukkah* was large, and if so, the case is simply irrelevant to the debate about a small *sukkah*.

### Compartments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב אַדָּא: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא סוּכָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה קִיטוֹנִיּוֹת קִיטוֹנִיּוֹת. וְכִי דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל מַלְכָּה לֵישֵׁב בְּסוּכָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה קִיטוֹנִיּוֹת קִיטוֹנִיּוֹת? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְקִיטוֹנִיּוֹת שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Adda said:** This ruling **is necessary only** in the case of **a *sukka* that is constructed** with several **small rooms [*kitoniyyot*].** The *sukka* was large, but it was subdivided into many small rooms, each of which was smaller than four square cubits. Again, the Gemara asks: **Is it** then **customary for a queen to reside in a *sukka* constructed** with several **small rooms** without leaving a large room in which she could assemble her family and servants? **Rav Ashi said:** This ruling **is necessary only** with regard **to the compartments in** the *sukka*. It was indeed a large *sukka* with a large central room; however, there were many small rooms adjacent to the main room. It is with regard to this type of *sukka* that there is a tannaitic dispute.

Rabba bar Rav Adda resolves the difficulty: *lo nitzrecha ela l'sukkah asuyah kitoniyot kitoniyot* — the case concerns a *sukkah* fashioned with small interior compartments. The outer structure may indeed be large, as befits a queen, but inside it contained private eating chambers. Halachically, it is not the overall outer structure that defines the *sukkah* — the *sukkah* is the space in which one actually sits, surrounded by its four walls. The *sechach* directly above a small chamber would therefore cover a small area, and at excessive height it would fail to provide shade. One might ask whether the walls of those chambers would need to reach the *sechach*. That concern, however, belongs to the first opinion; the present discussion concerns the second opinion, and here the question is only the size of the area covered by the *sechach*, not whether the walls reach it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: בָּנֶיהָ בְּסוּכָּה מְעַלְּיָא הֲווֹ יָתְבִי, וְאִיהִי יָתְבָה בְּקִיטוֹנִיּוֹת מִשּׁוּם צְנִיעוּתָא, וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי לָא אָמְרִי לַהּ דָּבָר. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: בָּנֶיהָ גַּבָּהּ הֲווֹ יָתְבִי, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא אָמְרִי לַהּ דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis hold: Her sons were residing in a full-fledged *sukka*** with a large central room, which everyone agrees was fit. **However, she** often **resided in the small rooms due to modesty,** to avoid being in the public eye. **And for that** reason the Elders **did not say anything to her,** as even if the small rooms were too small relative to the height of the *sukka*, there was no problem since her sons did not sit in them. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds: Her sons would** occasionally **reside with her** in the small room, **and even so,** the Elders **did not say anything to her,** indicating that a *sukka* more than twenty cubits high is fit even in a small *sukka*.

The *Gemara* then raises a further objection: *vachi darkah shel malka leishev besukkah asuyah kitoniyot kitoniyot* — would a queen sit in a *sukkah* composed entirely of tiny little chambers, with no general sitting room? That seems implausible.

Rav Ashi refines the answer: *lo nitzrecha ela l'kitoniyot shebah* — there was indeed a grand, spacious outer dining room where guests could gather, but the *sukkah* also had small side chambers, and the queen took her private meals in one of those chambers. Rashi adds the following clarification of how Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis read the incident: the Rabbis held that the sons (*b'nah*) sat in the large dining room, and since a large *sukkah* poses no problem of height under this theory, the Hilni case proves nothing. Rabbi Yehuda held that the sons ate together with her in one of the tiny side chambers, and since they raised no objection, he takes this as proof that even in a small chamber — where height would be a problem — the *sukkah* was nonetheless valid.

Rashi then raises a difficulty with Rabbi Yehuda's reading: how could all seven sons fit inside a tiny chamber? If the minimum size is four by four *amot*, it seems impossibly cramped for seven people, and all the more so if one holds the minimum size to be only seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim*.

### Both Dimensions

Tosafot resolve Rashi's difficulty with an important principle: for a *sukkah* to satisfy the minimum size requirement, it is not sufficient that it have the minimum *area* — it must have the minimum measurement in *both* dimensions independently. Therefore, Tosafot suggest, the chamber could have been, for instance, three and a half *amot* wide but twenty *amot* long. Such a chamber is halachically *not* a minimum *sukkah* — because in one dimension it falls short — yet it could easily accommodate seven sons. And accordingly, the shade-provision concern would also apply: for the *sechach* to provide shade effectively, it would need to span the minimum in both directions; being very long but narrow would not suffice.

## Minimum Size

### Shmuel Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יִצְחָק: הֲלָכָה, צְרִיכָה שֶׁתְּהֵא מַחְזֶקֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak said:** The ***halakha*** is that one's *sukka* **must be** large enough to **hold his head, and most of his** body, **and his table.**

With the debate over the twenty-*amah* height having been examined through various lenses, the *Gemara* now shifts to a distinct question that arose incidentally: what is the minimum size of a *sukkah*? Setting aside questions of height entirely, the inquiry is what the smallest valid *sukkah* must measure. Shmuel bar Yitzchak states: *halacha tzricha kidei machzeket rosho verubo veshulchano* — the *halacha* requires a *sukkah* large enough to contain one's head, the majority of one's body, and one's table. This is significantly smaller than the position of Rebbe, who as we will see requires four by four *amot*. The logic of *rosho verubo* — head and majority of body — reflects the posture of reclining: one must be able to enter not merely one's head but the upper portion of one's body into the *sukkah*. And one's *shulchan* — the serving tray or small table from which one eats — was approximately seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim*, roughly two feet by two feet.

### Rabbi Abba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: כְּמַאן, כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא כְּמַאן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to him,** astonished: **In accordance with whose** opinion did you rule? Was it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai?** This is the subject of a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, and according to Beit Hillel it is sufficient for the *sukka* to be large enough to hold one's head and most of his body; it need not be large enough to hold his table as well. The halakhic ruling that you issued is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai, despite the fact that in disputes between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel. Rav Shmuel bar Yitzḥak **said to him: Rather, in accordance with whose** opinion should I rule? Yes, my ruling is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai because in this case that is the *halakha*.

Rabbi Abba immediately challenges Shmuel bar Yitzchak: *keman* — whose opinion are you following? *kevet Shammai* — is this the position of Bet Shammai? Shmuel bar Yitzchak replies: *ela keman* — well, who else would I be following? The answer is frank and perhaps surprising: yes, this ruling accords with Bet Shammai, and nevertheless it is the *halacha*.

### Bet Shammai/Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁהָיָה רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בַּסּוּכָּה וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי פּוֹסְלִין וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one whose head and most of his** body **were in the *sukka* and his table was in the house, Beit Shammai deem it unfit and Beit Hillel deem it fit.**

To understand what this means, it is necessary to turn to the *Mishna* on 28a. The *Mishna* there reads: *mi shehaya rosho verubo besukkah veshulchano betoch habayit* — one whose head and the majority of his body was in the *sukkah*, but whose table was inside the house. *Bet Shammai poslin uvet Hillel machshirin* — Bet Shammai invalidate and Bet Hillel validate. Bet Hillel then adduces a proof: was it not the case that the elders of both Bet Shammai and Bet Hillel went to visit Yochanan ben haChoranis and found him sitting with his head and the majority of his body in the *sukkah* while his table was in the house, and they said nothing to him? Bet Shammai retorts: *misham raya* — you want to bring a proof from there? In fact, the elders did speak — they told him: *im ken hayita noheg lo kiyamta mitzvat sukkah miyamecha* — if this is how you have been conducting yourself, you have never in your life fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשָּׁם רְאָיָיה? אִשָּׁה הָיְתָה וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַסּוּכָּה. אָמַר לָהֶן: וַהֲלֹא שִׁבְעָה בָּנִים הֲווֹ לָהּ, וְעוֹד: כׇּל מַעֲשֶׂיהָ לֹא עָשְׂתָה אֶלָּא עַל פִּי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** Is there **proof from there? She was,** after all, **a woman and** therefore **exempt from the** mitzva of ***sukka*.** Consequently, the fact that her *sukka* was not fit did not warrant a comment from the Elders.  Rabbi Yehuda **said to them** in response: **Didn't she have seven sons** and therefore require a fit *sukka*? **And furthermore, she performed all of her actions only in accordance with** the directives of **the Sages.**

A word on the nature of the *shulchan* in question: it is not necessarily a large serving table but rather the small tray or board from which one eats — essentially, the surface from which one takes one's food. The scenario is one in which a person sits in the *sukkah* while the food is in the adjoining house, requiring them to step in and out repeatedly to retrieve each portion. One can readily understand why this practice would be problematic: a person who must constantly reach back into the house for food will eventually find himself eating inside the house.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה פְּלִיגִי? דִּלְמָא בְּסוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה פְּלִיגִי, וּכְגוֹן דְּיָתֵיב אַפּוּמָּא דִּמְטוּלְּתָא וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת. דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: גָּזְרִינַן שֶׁמָּא יִמָּשֵׁךְ אַחַר שׁוּלְחָנוֹ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: לָא גָּזְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak strongly objects to this** assumption: **From where** do you conclude **that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree with regard to** the minimum measure of **a small *sukka*? Perhaps** it is **with regard to a large *sukka* that they disagree, and** in a case **where one is sitting at the entrance of the *sukka* and his table is inside the house. As Beit Shammai hold that we issue a decree** to prohibit one from sitting that way **lest he be drawn after his table** while eating, to the extent that his head and most of his body will be inside the house and not inside the *sukka*. **And Beit Hillel hold that we do not** issue that **decree.**

The *Gemara* initially understands the *Mishna* as debating not the minimum size of the *sukkah* but rather the act of eating in this divided manner — whether one is permitted, in a large *sukkah*, to sit inside while the food remains in the house. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak presses the point: *mimmay d'Bet Shammai uvet Hillel besukkah ketana pligi* — why do you assume the debate concerns a small *sukkah* and its minimum dimensions? *Dilma besukkah gedolah pligi* — perhaps they are debating a perfectly normal, large *sukkah*, where the person sits at the opening (*apumma d'metulta*) and the table is just inside the house. On that reading, Bet Shammai's concern is a *gezeirah*: *shema yimashekh achar shulchano* — we worry that the person will be drawn after his table and find himself eating indoors. Bet Hillel is not concerned and therefore does not enact such a decree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: מִי שֶׁהָיָה רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בַּסּוּכָּה וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי פּוֹסְלִין וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין. וְאִם אִיתָא, מַחְזֶקֶת וְאֵינָהּ מַחְזֶקֶת מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches:** In the case of **one whose head and most of his** body **were in the *sukka* and his table was in the house, Beit Shammai deem it unfit and Beit Hillel deem it fit. And if it is so** that the dispute is with regard to the minimum measure of the *sukka*, the formulation of the mishna is missing the essential point. The distinction between a *sukka* that **holds and** a *sukka* that **does not hold** his head and most of his body is **what** the mishna **needed** to say. Since the mishna does not make that distinction, apparently the dispute is not with regard to the minimum measure of a *sukka*.

Rav Nachman reinforces this reading from the language of the *Mishna* itself: the *Mishna* describes a *scenario* — someone whose head and body are in the *sukkah* and whose table is in the house — rather than describing the *dimensions* of the *sukkah*. If the debate were about minimum size, the *Mishna* should have framed the question as whether the *sukkah* can or cannot contain a table.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה לָא פְּלִיגִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: מַחְזֶקֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ — כְּשֵׁרָה. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this conclusion: **And in the case of a small *sukka* do** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **not disagree? But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: A *sukka* that **holds his head, and most of his** body, **and his table is fit. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** It is unfit **until it measures** at least **four cubits by four cubits.**

### Rav Nachman

Yet the *Gemara* immediately challenges this reading: does Rav Nachman mean to say they do not *also* debate the minimum size? But a *braita* explicitly states: *machzeket rosho verubo veshulchano kesheira* — if it contains one's head, body, and table, it is valid. And Rabbi Yehuda says it must be four by four *amot*. This is plainly a debate about dimensions. A second *braita* states the same dispute with Rebbe's position but in the opposing view says only *rosho verubo* — head and body — without mentioning the table at all. So there are two *braitot*, and between them a contradiction: one requires head, body, and table; the other requires only head and body. The natural resolution is that the *braita* requiring the table reflects Bet Shammai, and the one requiring only head and body reflects Bet Hillel. And both are clearly debating minimum size — they speak in the language of *machzeket*, what the *sukkah* can contain, not in the language of what scenario is occurring.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל סוּכָּה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פְּסוּלָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ אֵינָהּ מַחְזֶקֶת אֶלָּא רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ — כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאִילּוּ שׁוּלְחָנוֹ לָא קָתָנֵי. קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי. אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, הָא בֵּית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught in** yet **another** *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Any *sukka* that does not have** an area of at least **four cubits by four cubits is unfit. And the Rabbis say: Even if it holds only his head and most of his** body, **it is fit. However,** the term: **His table, is not taught** in this *baraita*. If so, these two tannaitic sources **contradict each other,** as each attributes a different opinion to the Rabbis. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it that this** *baraita*, in which: His table, is taught, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** while **that** *baraita*, in which: His table, is not taught, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel?** Apparently, they do dispute the minimum measure of a small *sukka*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בְּתַרְתֵּי פְּלִיגִי: פְּלִיגִי בְּסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה, וּפְלִיגִי בְּסוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה. וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: מִי שֶׁהָיָה רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בַּסּוּכָּה וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יָצָא, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יָצָא. וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ מַחְזֶקֶת אֶלָּא כְּדֵי רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בִּלְבַד, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי פּוֹסְלִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, they disagree with regard to two** issues; **they disagree with regard to** the minimum measure of **a small *sukka*, and they disagree with regard to** where one may sit in **a large *sukka*.** And the mishna **is incomplete and this** is what **it is teaching: One whose head and most of his** body **were in the *sukka* and his table was in the house, Beit Shammai say** that **he did not fulfill** his obligation **and Beit Hillel say** that **he fulfilled** his obligation. **And** with regard to a *sukka* **that holds only his head and most of his** body, **Beit Shammai deem it unfit and Beit Hillel deem it fit.**

### Two Debates

The *Gemara* concludes: *le'olam bitarti pligi* — in truth, they are debating two distinct cases simultaneously. *Pligi besukkah ketana, pligi besukkah gedolah* — they debate both the small *sukkah* and the large *sukkah*. The text of the *Mishna* must therefore be understood as containing two interwoven debates, with some words missing: *chisurei mechsara vehachi katani* — the text is deficient and should be read as follows. First debate: one whose head and body is in a large *sukkah* but whose table is in the house — Bet Shammai says *lo yatza*, he has not fulfilled his obligation; Bet Hillel says *yatza*, he has. Second debate: a *sukkah* that can contain only one's head and body but not one's table — Bet Shammai invalidate (*poslin*), Bet Hillel validate (*machshirin*).

The use of *chisurei mechsara* — asserting that words are missing from the *Mishna* — is always somewhat forcing of the text, and some *acharonim*, including the *Gra*, note that when the *Gemara* invokes this formula, it is often not literally claiming a different *girsa* but rather acknowledging that the text does not fit its position and is consciously adjusting it. Here, however, the claim is relatively persuasive, since, as the *Gemara* itself observes, the language of the *Mishna* points in two directions: on the one hand, it describes a behavioral scenario (the table in the house), and on the other hand it uses the language of *poslin* and *machshirin* — terms that describe the status of an *object*, in this case the *sukkah* itself, not the fulfillment of an act. If the entire debate were about the scenario of eating in a divided way, Bet Shammai should have said *lo yatza* — you did not fulfill your obligation — rather than *poslin*, which implies a problem with the *sukkah* as an object.

### Habitation Link

Are these two debates logically connected? They appear to be, and the linkage runs through the concept of what constitutes minimal habitation. What does it mean to inhabit a *sukkah*? Primarily, to eat — and later we will see that sleeping is also included. If the *sukkah* can contain one's head and the majority of one's body, one can sleep in it; the majority of one's body being inside is sufficient to consider the person as dwelling within. But can such a space also support eating in a natural way? This depends on whether one considers it natural and stable to eat in a space that cannot accommodate one's table. Bet Shammai, who holds that having the table inside the house is problematic, reasons that people cannot remain present and grounded in a space while their food is elsewhere — they will inevitably be drawn out. If that is true, then a space that cannot contain the table is not a space in which one can genuinely inhabit and eat with any permanence or stability, and is therefore below the minimum. Bet Hillel, who holds that one can eat in the *sukkah* even with the table outside, is not concerned that one will be drawn after it. Accordingly, even a space that cannot contain the table may constitute minimally valid dwelling. The two debates are thus expressions of the same underlying principle.

### Geonic Rule

Tosafot, citing the *Seder* of Rav Amram Gaon, note that there are six places in *halacha* where we rule like Bet Shammai, and this is one of them — the minimum size of the *sukkah* must contain *rosho verubo veshulchano*, in accordance with Bet Shammai.

It is also worth noting that a number of *Rishonim* rule like Bet Shammai for the minimum size but hold that in a large *sukkah*, even if the table is in the house, one fulfills the *mitzvah*. Others maintain that the two debates are so logically intertwined that they must be ruled on together. Tosafot themselves address this *machloket Rishonim*.

### Rabbinic Authority

The statement of Bet Shammai to Yochanan ben haChoranis — *lo kiyamta mitzvat sukkah miyamecha*, you have never in your life fulfilled the *mitzvah of sukkah* — raises a striking and fundamental question. If this person was sitting in a large *sukkah* with his table in the house, he was at most violating a rabbinic stricture. Surely he fulfilled his obligation at a *d'Oraisa* level. Can the Rabbis, through a *gezeirah*, prevent a person from fulfilling a biblical obligation?

The question is not foreign to the *Gemara*; a parallel issue arises at the very beginning of *Brachos*, where the *Mishna* instructs one not to recite *Shema* after midnight — yet the *d'Oraisa* obligation may be fulfilled the entire night. If one recited *Shema* after midnight despite the rabbinic restriction, is one *yotzei* or not?

Tosafot address this directly at the end of their discussion. The relevant passage reads: *heicha d'Beit Hillel machmirin mid'rabbanan — ee asa kedivrei Beit Shammai, lo yatza yedei chovaso afilu d'Oraisa* — where Bet Hillel is the stricter position rabbinically and one acted according to Bet Shammai's more lenient position, one has not fulfilled one's obligation even at the biblical level. The proof is the *sukkah* case: one who sits at the entrance of the *sukkah* (*afischa d'matalaltah*) with the table in the house, and Bet Shammai's concern is the *gezeirah* lest one be drawn after the table — Bet Shammai tells him he has not fulfilled *mitzvat sukkah*, including, Tosafot conclude, *afilu mid'Oraisa*, even on the biblical level.

What is the precise mechanism by which a rabbinic decree can prevent fulfillment of a biblical obligation? Several possibilities present themselves: *mitzvah haba'ah ba'aveirah* — a *mitzvah* accomplished through a transgression — or the principle of *yesh koach beyad chachamim* — that the Rabbis possess the authority to uproot a positive commandment under certain circumstances. The exact mechanism is left open, but the phenomenon itself is strikingly illustrated here.

It is important not to overstate the principle. There are many rabbinic enactments surrounding *sukkah* — concerning what constitutes *kosher sechach d'Oraisa*, what constitutes *kosher sechach d'rabbanan*, and so forth. It does not follow that every rabbinic dimension of *sukkah* law, if violated, would prevent fulfillment of the *d'Oraisa* obligation. The present case represents a particular and notable instance where that outcome is possible, not a general rule applicable to all rabbinic requirements relating to positive commandments.

## 4x4 Standard

### House Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: בַּיִת שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פָּטוּר מִן הַמְּזוּזָה, וּמִן הַמַּעֲקֶה, וְאֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא בִּנְגָעִים, וְאֵינוֹ נֶחְלָט בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, וְאֵין חוֹזְרִין עָלָיו מֵעוֹרְכֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין בּוֹ, וְאֵין מִשְׁתַּתְּפִין בּוֹ, וְאֵין מַנִּיחִין בּוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the above discussion, the Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught that which the Sages taught:** The halakhic status of **a house in which there is not** an area of **four cubits by four cubits** is not that of a house? Therefore, *halakhot* in the Torah or the mishna that are relevant to a house do not apply to a house that size. Consequently, it is **exempt from** the mitzva of placing a ***mezuza*** on its doorpost; **and** it is exempt **from the** obligation of establishing a **parapet** around its roof; **and it does not become ritually impure with leprosy** of the house. **And** its sale is **not rendered final** in the same manner as the sale of **houses within walled cities.** The owner of a house in a walled city who sells his house has the option to buy it back from the purchaser within one year of the sale. If he fails to do so, the sale is rendered final and the house does not return to the original owner during the Jubilee Year (see Leviticus 25:29–31). **And one does not return from** the ranks **of** soldiers **waging war for** a house that size, as would one who built a house with an area greater than four by four cubits (see Deuteronomy 20:5). **And one** need **not join** the houses in the courtyards **for** a house with that area. If there is more than one house in a courtyard, it is prohibited by rabbinic law to carry in that courtyard unless the residents of each of the houses contribute food that is placed in one of the houses, thereby rendering them joint-owners of the courtyard. The resident of a house with an area of less than four by four cubits need not participate in this joining of courtyards. **And one** need **not merge** the courtyards that open into an alleyway **for** a courtyard in which the area of its only house is less than four by four cubits. In this case, too, the resident of that courtyard need not participate in the merging of alleyways. **And one does not place the** food collected for the aforementioned **joining** [***eiruv***] of courtyards **in** this house but rather in a house with an area of at least four by four cubits.

The *Gemara* now asks: *man tana l'ha d'sanu rabbanan* — who is the *tanna* of the following *baraisa*? The *baraisa* establishes that a house smaller than four *amot* by four *amot* does not qualify as a *bayit* — a house — for the purposes of any *halacha* that requires a house. The four-by-four *amot* standard is the universal minimum for a structure to bear the legal identity of a house.

## Permanent vs. Temp

### Temp Dwelling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן סוּכָּה — דְּדִירַת עֲרַאי הִיא, אֲבָל לְגַבַּי בַּיִת — דְּדִירַת קֶבַע הוּא, אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ דְּאִי אִית בֵּיהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — דָּיְירִי בֵּיהּ אִינָשֵׁי, וְאִי לָא — לָא דָּיְירִי בֵּיהּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Even** if **you say** that the *tanna* of the *baraita* is **the Rabbis, the Rabbis say** that a structure with an area smaller than four by four cubits is fit **only there, with regard to a *sukka*, which is a temporary residence,** because in a temporary residence one is willing to confine himself to a small area. **However, with regard to** *halakhot* relating to **a house, which is a permanent residence, even the Rabbis concede that if it has** an area of **four cubits by four cubits, people reside in it,** as it is a functional house, **and if not, people do not reside in it,** and its legal status is not that of a house at all.

The *Gemara* answers that this *baraisa* need not be attributed exclusively to Rebbe. Even the *Rabbanan* can accept the four-by-four standard for a house, because when they said a *sukkah* can be as small as *rosho verubo veshulchano*, they were speaking of a *sukkah* — which is a *dirat arai*, a temporary dwelling. The entire rationale for permitting a smaller minimum is rooted in the *sukkah*'s character as something temporary, not a permanent home.

### Rabbi 4x4

Rebbe, by contrast, requires four by four *amot* for a valid *sukkah* as well. This raises the natural question: how does Rebbe understand a *sukkah*? If the minimum of four by four *amot* corresponds to a permanent dwelling, and Rebbe demands this of a *sukkah*, it appears that Rebbe holds a *sukkah* must have the character of *dirat keva* — a permanent dwelling.

### Permanence Debate

This may be the precise point of contention between Rebbe and the *Rabbanan*. Everyone agrees that four by four *amot* defines a permanent house and that *rosho verubo veshulchano* is sufficient for something temporary. The debate is which category the *sukkah* falls into. The *Rabbanan* hold that the *sukkah* is defined as *dirat arai*, and therefore a smaller minimum is appropriate. Rebbe may hold that *teshvu* — you must *dwell* in the *sukkah* — demands genuine habitation, which requires the standards of a *dirat keva*, and therefore the minimum must be four by four *amot*. This is, as the *Gemara* notes, a genuinely fascinating question, because earlier in the *masechet* we discussed how the *sukkah* must not be so large or permanent as to be confused with one's house. Now we see that the question runs in the other direction as well: must a *sukkah* be *arai* or may it — or must it — have some quality of *keva*?

## 4x4 Standard (II)

### Exemptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: פָּטוּר מִן הַמְּזוּזָה, וּמִן הַמַּעֲקֶה, וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא בִּנְגָעִים, וְאֵינוֹ נֶחְלָט בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, וְאֵין חוֹזְרִין עָלָיו מֵעוֹרְכֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּ״בַיִת״ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ בְּכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara briefly discusses the *halakhot* listed in the *baraita*: **The Master said** that a house in which there is an area of less than four by four cubits it **is exempt from** the mitzva of placing a ***mezuza*** on its doorpost, **and** it is exempt **from the** obligation of establishing a **parapet** around its roof, **and it does not become ritually impure with leprosy** of the house. **And** its sale is **not rendered final** in the same manner as the sale of **houses within walled cities, and one does not return from** the ranks **of** soldiers **waging war for** a house that size. **What is the reason** for these *halakhot*? It is due to the fact **that "house" is written** in the Torah **with regard to all** these *halakhot*. The legal status of a structure with an area of less than four by four cubits is not that of a house.

The *Gemara* now unpacks each item in the *baraisa*. A structure under four by four *amot* is exempt from *mezuzah* — because the verse says *al mezuzot beitecha*, and this is not a *bayit*. It is exempt from *ma'akeh*, the railing required around a flat roof — because the verse says *ki sivneh bayit chadash*, and this too requires a house. It does not become *tamei* with *nega'im* — because the verse says *kenega nir'ah li babayit*, and again the term *bayit* is required. In each of these cases, the common denominator is that the Torah employs the word *bayit*, and a structure under four by four *amot* does not carry that designation.

### Property Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ עִיבּוּר בֵּין שְׁתֵּי עֲיָירוֹת, וְאֵין הָאַחִין וְהַשּׁוּתָּפִין חוֹלְקִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And one does not render it an extension** of the city limits when it is located **between two cities.** Two cities between which there is a distance of more than 141⅓ cubits cannot be joined and considered as a single city for the purpose of measuring the Shabbat limit for one city from the edge of the second city. However if there is a house equidistant between the two cities, i.e., a bit more than seventy cubits from each town, the house joins the two cities together for the purpose of measuring the Shabbat limit. A house in which there is an area of less than four by four cubits cannot serve this function; **and brothers and partners do not divide it,** as it is too small to be divided.

The *baraisa* continues: such a structure does not become permanently transferred in the sale of houses in a walled city (*batei arei chomah*). The law of *batei arei chomah* is that when a house in a walled city is sold, the seller has one year to redeem it; after that year, it passes irrevocably to the buyer. A structure under four by four *amot*, however, is not treated as a house but as a yard or open field, and therefore it may always be redeemed and reverts to its original owner at *Yovel* — the special rules of *batei arei chomah* do not apply. Similarly, building such a structure does not exempt one from military service; the Torah exempts *asher banah bayit chadash* — one who built a new house — and this does not qualify as a house.

### Eruv Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין בּוֹ, וְאֵין מִשְׁתַּתְּפִין בּוֹ, וְאֵין מַנִּיחִין בּוֹ עֵירוּב. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּלָא חֲזֵי לְדִירָה. עֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת — אֵין מַנִּיחִין בּוֹ, אֲבָל שִׁיתּוּף מַנִּיחִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** by rabbinic law, **one** need **not join** the houses in the courtyards **for** a house with that area, **and one** need **not merge** the courtyards that open into an alleyway **for** a courtyard in which the area of its only house is less than four by four cubits. **And one does not place the** food collected for the **joining** of courtyards **in** this house. **What is the reason** for these *halakhot*? It is due to the fact **that it is not fit for residence.** The point of the joining of courtyards is to transform the courtyard into a residence shared by the residents of all its member households, and this can be accomplished only by placing the joint food in a place whose legal status is that of a house. The Gemara infers this from the fact that it is taught in the *baraita*: **And one does not place** the food of **the joining of courtyards in** this house, **but the** food of the **merging** of alleyways, **one places in it.**

The *baraisa* further states that a structure under four by four *amot* does not need to participate in the *eiruv chatzeiros* — the joining of houses in a courtyard — and cannot serve as the repository for the *eiruv*. The reason is *d'lo chazi l'dira* — it is not fit for dwelling. The *halacha* of *eiruv chatzeiros* requires that all those who dwell in a shared courtyard join together; someone who occupies a tiny structure this size is not considered to genuinely dwell there in any ongoing sense, but merely to pass through. However, the *Gemara* distinguishes: for *shituf mavo'os* — the joining of courtyards in an alleyway — the *eiruv* may be placed in this structure. The reason is that the *shituf mavo'os* may be placed in the *chatzer* itself, and this structure is no worse than a courtyard (*d'lo gara michatzer shebamavoi*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּלָא גָּרַע מֵחָצֵר שֶׁבְּמָבוֹי. דִּתְנַן: עֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת בֶּחָצֵר, שִׁיתּוּפֵי מָבוֹי בַּמָּבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this distinction? It is due to the fact **that it is no less** a residence **than a courtyard in the alleyway.** An unroofed courtyard is not fit for residence, and nevertheless the food for the merging of alleyways may be placed there, **as we learned** in a *baraita* in tractate *Eiruvin* (85b): **The joining of courtyards** may be placed **in the courtyard** and **the merging of alleyways** may be placed **in the alleyway.**

The *Gemara* clarifies by citing a *Mishna*: *eiruv chatzeiros bachatzer veshitufei mavo'os bamavoi* — the *eiruv chatzeiros* goes in the courtyard and the *shituf mavo'os* goes in the *mavoi*. But the *Gemara* questions this: can the *eiruv chatzeiros* really be placed in the courtyard itself? A *baraita* rules that if one places the *eiruv* in a gatehouse, a portico (*achsadra* — Rashi explains this as what is called a *portico*), or a porch (*mirpeset*), it is invalid, because those are places people pass through, not places they dwell. Rather, the *Mishna* must be understood as: the *eiruv chatzeiros* goes in one of the *houses* of the courtyard, and the *shituf mavo'os* goes in one of the *courtyards* of the *mavoi*. Since the *shituf mavo'os* can go in an actual courtyard, a small structure is no worse and may serve as its repository.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ, עֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת בֶּחָצֵר? וְהָתְנַן: הַנּוֹתֵן עֵירוּבוֹ בְּבֵית שַׁעַר אַכְסַדְרָה וּמִרְפֶּסֶת — אֵינוֹ עֵירוּב, וְהַדָּר שָׁם אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר. אֶלָּא אֵימָא: עֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת בְּבַיִת שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר, וְשִׁיתּוּפֵי מְבוֹאוֹת בְּחָצֵר שֶׁבַּמָּבוֹי. וְהַאי לָא גָּרַע מֵחָצֵר שֶׁבְּמָבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed** this *halakha*: How can **the joining of courtyards** be placed **in the courtyard? Didn't we learn** in the mishna: With regard to **one who placed his joining** of courtyards **in a gatehouse** or in **a portico [*akhsadra*],** a roofed structure without walls or with incomplete walls, **or** on **a balcony,** it **is not a** fit ***eiruv*. And one who resides there,** in any of these structures, **does not render it prohibited** for the homeowner and the other residents of the courtyard to carry, even if he did not contribute to the *eiruv*, as the legal status of these places is not that of a house. **Rather,** emend the mishna and **say: The joining of courtyards** is placed **in** one of **the** full-fledged **houses that is in the courtyard, and the merging of alleyways** is placed even **in** one of **the courtyards that** opens **into the alleyway. And this** house whose area is less than four by four cubits **is no less** a residence **than** one of **the courtyards that** open **into the alleyway.**

### City Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ עִיבּוּר בֵּין שְׁתֵּי עֲיָירוֹת. דַּאֲפִילּוּ כְּבוּרְגָּנִין לָא מְשַׁוֵּינַן לֵיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא — בּוּרְגָּנִין חֲזוּ לְמִילְּתַיְיהוּ, וְהַאי לָא חֲזֵי לְמִילְּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the *baraita*: **And one does not render it an extension** of the city limits when it is located **between two cities.** The Gemara explains: This means that **we do not even render its** halakhic status **like** that of **huts [*burganin*]** used by grain watchmen in the fields, which join the two cities between which they are located for the purpose of measuring the Shabbat limit. **What is the reason** that it is considered less a residence than a watchman's hut? The Gemara answers: Watchmen's **huts,** even though they are not sturdy, **are suited for their matters, while this** house with an area less than four by four cubits **is not suited for its matter,** as it is not fit for residence.

The *baraisa* also states that a structure under four by four *amot* cannot serve as the connective structure between two cities (*ibur bein shtei ayarot*). The relevant principle is that two cities can be treated as a single city — with their *techum* of two thousand *amot* measured from the outer edge of the combined unit — if they are within seventy and two-thirds *amot* of each other. If they are further apart but a house exists at the midpoint, that house connects them: it is within seventy and two-thirds *amot* of one city and seventy and two-thirds *amot* of the other, and the cities are thereby considered one. However, the connecting structure must qualify as a house. A structure under four by four *amot*, the *baraisa* rules, does not serve this function.

One might ask: why require a house at all? Even a *burganin* — a lean-to or watchman's hut erected to guard a field — suffices to connect two cities. If a lean-to works, why not this small structure? The *Gemara* answers: *burganin chazu l'milsaihu* — a lean-to is fit for what it is built for. *Vehai lo chazi l'milsai* — but this structure, built in the form of a house, is not fit for what it purports to be. The distinction is between a condemned house and a tent: both might offer the same degree of physical shelter, but a condemned house lacks any legitimate identity as a dwelling, while a structure purpose-built as a shelter for temporary use — such as a lean-to — genuinely fulfills its function. Something constructed as a house but too small to serve as one is not merely deficient; it is categorically illegitimate as a house. Rashi adds that a *burganin* is made for a single night's sleep, while something built in the form of a house implicitly aspires to more regular dwelling — and if it falls short of that aspiration, it does not qualify even for the lesser connecting function.

### Property Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הָאַחִין וְהַשּׁוּתָּפִין חוֹלְקִין בּוֹ. טַעְמָא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, הָא אִית בֵּיהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חוֹלְקִין, וְהָתְנַן: אֵין חוֹלְקִים אֶת הֶחָצֵר עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה וְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לָזֶה!
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the *baraita*: **And brothers and partners do not divide** a house that does not measure at least four by four cubits, as it is too small to be divided. The Gemara infers: **The reason** that a house that size is not divided **is** due to the fact **that there is not** an area of four by **four cubits in it; however, if there is** an area of four by **four cubits in it, they divide it.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna: **One divides the courtyard** at the request of one of the heirs or partners **only** if its area is sufficient so **that there will be in it** four by **four cubits for this** partner or heir **and** four by **four cubits for that** partner or heir? Apparently, in order to divide a courtyard it must be at least four by eight cubits.

Finally, the *baraisa* states that brothers or partners (*achin veshutfin*) cannot divide a structure under four by four *amot*. The *Gemara* initially takes this to mean that its small size makes it impossible to divide in a meaningful way. But then it raises a challenge from a *Mishna* that rules that a courtyard cannot be divided unless each party receives at least four by four *amot*. If even a four-by-four structure cannot be divided without each party losing their minimum share, how could any house of exactly four by four be divided? The *Gemara* therefore amends the formulation: *ein bo din chalukah k'chatzer* — a structure under four by four *amot* is not subject to division *as a courtyard is divided*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אֵין בּוֹ דִּין חֲלוּקָּה כְּחָצֵר. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חָצֵר לְפִי פְּתָחֶיהָ מִתְחַלֶּקֶת. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: נוֹתֵן לְכׇל פֶּתַח וָפֶתַח אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְהַשְּׁאָר חוֹלְקִין אוֹתוֹ בְּשָׁוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** emend the *baraita* and **say** that **the *halakha* of division like** that of **a courtyard** does **not** apply **to it. As Rav Huna said: A courtyard is divided according to** the number of **its entrances.** When the residents of the houses in a courtyard divide the courtyard between them, the division is not based on the number of houses in the courtyard, nor is it based on the size of the houses. Instead, it is divided based on the number of entrances that open into the courtyard. **Rav Ḥisda said: One gives** the homeowner **for each and every entrance four cubits, and the rest** of the courtyard **is divided equally** among the residents of the courtyard.

The *Gemara* explains this in light of the rules for dividing a courtyard. Rav Huna holds that a courtyard is divided proportionally according to its doorways — the number of doors opening into it. Rav Chisda holds that each doorway is allotted four *amot* of courtyard space in front of it, and the remainder is divided equally. In either approach, the doorways of the houses opening onto the courtyard are the determining factor. A structure under four by four *amot*, however, does not factor into this calculation at all. As the *Gemara* puts it: *d'leimeha vei kai* — for a structure that is built to remain as it is, one gives it the appropriate portion of the courtyard. *D'limistar kai* — but for a structure that is essentially designated to be demolished, too small to function as a house, one does not allocate it any portion of the courtyard, for it carries no significance. A small condemned-in-character structure simply does not enter the calculation by which a courtyard is apportioned among its residents.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָנֵי מִילֵּי בַּיִת, דִּלְמֶהֱוֵי קָאֵי — יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ חָצֵר. הַאי, דִּלְמִיסְתַּר קָאֵי — לָא יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ חָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The principle that entrances are factored into the division of a courtyard **applies only** with regard to **a house that stands to endure,** as the owner needs use of the yard to ease access to his house, so **we provide him** with four cubits according to Rav Ḥisda, or part of the **courtyard** according to Rav Huna. However, in the case of **this** small house, **which stands to be leveled,** its owner has no need for the adjacent courtyard, so **we do not provide him** with any part of the **courtyard,** as if it were not even there.
