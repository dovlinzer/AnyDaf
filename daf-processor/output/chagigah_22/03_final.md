# Chagigah 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodesh vs. Terumah

### Standards Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מְקַבְּלִינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ. קוֹדֶשׁ נָמֵי, לָא נְקַבֵּיל מִינַּיְיהוּ! הָוְיָא לֵיהּ אֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** We do not accept *teruma* **from** *amei ha'aretz*, as they are not trustworthy with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual purity, and therefore it does not matter if the vessels he uses for *teruma* are not immersed properly. Therefore, the Sages were not concerned that the *am ha'aretz* may come to a misunderstanding when observing a *ḥaver* immersing vessels within vessels. The Gemara continues its line of questioning. If so, **we should likewise not accept sacrificial** food **from** *amei ha'aretz*, since they are not sufficiently meticulous with ritual purity, and we should therefore not care if they immerse their vessels improperly. The Gemara responds: The *am ha'aretz* **will have** feelings of **antagonism** if sacrificial food is not accepted from him, and this would lead to internal discord and conflict within Israel.

The daf opens by recapitulating the distinction already established between the standards required for *kodesh* and those required for *terumah*. The *chaveirim* — those who are scrupulous in matters of *tumah ve-taharah* — need not themselves be confused about what practices pertain to *taharah*, what to *terumah*, and what to *kodesh*. The concern is rather with the *amei ha-aretz*, who observe the behavior of the *chaveirim* and pattern themselves accordingly. Since the *amei ha-aretz* are sufficiently attentive to distinguish between the different levels of practice being modeled for them, the *chaveirim* must be more exacting by *kodesh* than by *terumah*.

The reason the greater stringency falls precisely on *kodesh* is rooted in an irony: although we do not accept the *terumah* of the *am ha-aretz* — directing him instead to give it to a *kohen* who is himself an *am ha-aretz* — when it comes to *kodshim* we have no such option. There is only one *Beit HaMikdash*, and therefore we must accept what the *am ha-aretz* brings. This will be the theme that closes the entire *perek*: at the *regalim*, when all of Israel converges on that single central *Beit HaMikdash* with their *korbanot*, the principle of unity overrides these distinctions entirely. Because we are ultimately going to be accepting their *korbanot*, we must during the rest of the year set the highest possible standard — a model sufficiently rigorous that our acceptance of their offerings remains defensible. That is why the degree of care displayed in matters touching *kodesh* must exceed what we exhibit for *terumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי, הָוְיָא לֵיהּ אֵיבָה! לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ, דְּאָזֵיל יָהֵיב לֵיהּ לְכֹהֵן עַם הָאָרֶץ חַבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, in the case of ***teruma* he will also have** feelings of **antagonism** if *teruma* is not accepted from him. Why were the Sages concerned about this factor only with regard to sacrificial food and not *teruma*? The Gemara replies: An *am ha'aretz* **does not care** if his *teruma* is not accepted by *ḥaverim*, **as** he can always **go** and **give** his *teruma* **to an *am ha'aretz* priest** who is **his friend** and who will accept it from him. In the case of sacrificial food, however, there is only one Temple, and care must be taken not to make the *amei ha'aretz* feel they are being rejected.

The Gemara had further noted that this same logic of inclusion — of not pushing the *am ha-aretz* away so severely that he breaks off and goes to build his own *bamot* — underlies the practice of accepting testimony from an *am ha-aretz* in certain contexts. What begins as a principle specific to the *Mikdash* becomes a broader mandate: the social and communal costs of extreme stratification must figure into *halachic* decision-making.

### Borrowing Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיחוּשׁ לִשְׁאֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § We have established that the reason for leniency with regard to immersing one vessel inside another for *teruma* is based on the fact that we do not care if the vessels *amei ha'aretz* use for *teruma* are improperly purified, since we do not accept *teruma* from them in any event. The Gemara asks: **But let us be concerned about borrowing** vessels from them.

The Gemara now raises a fresh question: *ve-neichosh li-she'ilah* — should we not be concerned about the vessels we borrow from the *am ha-aretz*? The *am ha-aretz* was not placed in *cherem*. Their clothing, to be sure, carries the status of *midras* and must be treated accordingly, particularly by those eating in *taharah*. Yet regular interaction continued; the *am ha-aretz* were neighbors, storekeepers, laborers. Vessels were borrowed from them as a matter of course. If we are required to assume their vessels are *tamei*, how can we interact with them at all?

### Pottery Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: כְּלִי חֶרֶס מַצִּיל עַל הַכֹּל, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מַצִּיל אֶלָּא עַל אוֹכָלִים וְעַל הַמַּשְׁקִים וְעַל כְּלֵי חֶרֶס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proves that it is acceptable to borrow vessels from an *am ha'aretz*: **As we learned** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 1:14): **An earthenware vessel** of an *am ha'aretz* **shields** all kinds of items from the ritual impurity imparted by a corpse. There are two applications of this fact: If there are objects or foods inside a tightly sealed earthenware vessel located inside a room containing a corpse, the vessel prevents the impurity from reaching the items inside it. Also, if there is a corpse in the first floor of a house and food or vessels are located in a second story of the same building, with an opening such as a skylight in the floor between the two stories, an earthenware vessel plugging up the opening will prevent the ritual impurity from spreading to the second story. This is **the statement of Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai say: It shields only food, drink, and earthenware vessels,** but not utensils of metal, wood, cloth, etc.

The Gemara resolves this question by citing a *mishnah* in *Eiduyot*. The scenario: a corpse lies in a house, causing *tumah* to radiate throughout the enclosed space. If there is an opening of a *tefach* in the ceiling or wall, the *tumah* passes through and spreads to the upper story as well. However, if a *kli cheres* — a pottery vessel — is positioned so that its underside seals the opening, and its cavity faces downward, the *tumah* cannot penetrate it. A pottery vessel becomes *tamei* only through its interior; *tumah* striking its exterior has no effect. The inverted vessel therefore constitutes a barrier, protecting whatever is above.

On this point, *Beit Hillel* rules: *kli cheres matsil al hakol* — the pottery vessel protects everything in the upper story. *Beit Shammai*, however, rules that it protects only *ochlin u-mashkin* — food and drink — and other pottery vessels above. Everything else, they hold, remains *tamei*.

## Hillel vs. Shammai

### Shammai's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: מִפְּנֵי מָה? אָמְרוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא עַל גַּבֵּי עַם הָאָרֶץ, וְאֵין כְּלִי טָמֵא חוֹצֵץ. אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל: וַהֲלֹא טִיהַרְתֶּם אוֹכָלִין וּמַשְׁקִין שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ! אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: כְּשֶׁטִּיהַרְנוּ אוֹכָלִין וּמַשְׁקִין שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ —
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: For what** reason do you make this distinction? It is clear that a closed, pure earthenware vessel serves as a barrier, blocking the spread of impurity in a room with a corpse (see Numbers 19:15). **Beit Shammai said** to them: **Because** the earthenware vessel itself **is impure on account of** its contact with **an *am ha'aretz*.** The Sages decreed that anything touched by an *am ha'aretz* is impure, since such people are not meticulous or sufficiently knowledgeable about the *halakhot* of purity; therefore his vessels are considered impure. **And** the principle is that **an impure vessel does not serve as a barrier** from impurity. **Beit Hillel said to them: But didn't you declare the food and drink inside** the earthenware vessel to be **pure?** If the vessel of an *am ha'aretz* cannot serve as a barrier, why do you say that some items inside it are pure? **Beit Shammai said to them: When we declared the food and drink inside it to be pure,**

*Beit Hillel* challenges *Beit Shammai*: how can the pottery vessel protect some things but not others? Either it functions as a barrier or it does not. *Beit Shammai* responds: *mipnei she-hu tamei al gabei am ha-aretz* — because this pottery vessel must be assumed *tamei*, as we are dealing with the house of an *am ha-aretz*. A *tamei* vessel cannot function as a barrier; *tumah* passes through it. Therefore everything in the upper story should be *tamei*. But if everything is *tamei*, why save the food and drink?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעַצְמוֹ טִהַרְנוּ. אֲבָל נְטַהֵר אֶת הַכְּלִי, שֶׁטׇּהֳרָתוֹ לְךָ וָלוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **we declared** them **pure for** the *am ha'aretz* **himself,** not for *ḥaverim*, since *ḥaverim* in any event do not consider anything touched by an *am ha'aretz* to be pure. A *ḥaver* would therefore never relate to the food of an *am ha'aretz* as pure, and he would not use the earthenware vessels of an *am ha'aretz* for preparation of pure food, since an earthenware vessel cannot be purified through immersion. However, he might borrow a metal vessel, for instance, and purify it through immersion before using it for pure food. **But** how **can we purify a vessel** of metal and other materials, **whose purity** would be relevant both **for you and for him?** A *ḥaver* may one day borrow a metal vessel from the *am ha'aretz*, and not realize that it was once inside an earthenware vessel in the same room as a corpse and as a result requires extensive purification from corpse contamination through the ashes of the red heifer, rather than mere immersion. Therefore, the *am ha'aretz* is informed that his vessels of metal and other materials have contracted impurity from the corpse, and must undergo the extensive purification process, and after this is done these vessels will now be fit for use of the *ḥaver* after mere immersion.

The distinction *Beit Shammai* draws is the following. The *tumah* does indeed pass through the assumed-*tamei* pottery vessel and enters the upper story. But within that context, we can still say: *ke-she-tiharnu ochlin u-mashkin she-betokho le-atzmo tiharnu* — when we declare the food and drink *tahor*, we do so only for the *am ha-aretz* himself. We do not eat the food of the *am ha-aretz*. The primary dimension along which these distinctions of *tumah ve-taharah* played out in daily life was the question of with whom one eats, whose food one handles, and who may sit at whose table. Since we will never eat the food of the *am ha-aretz* in any case, we can tell him his food is *tahor* without exposing ourselves to any risk.

The vessels above, however, are another matter entirely: *nitaher kli she-tihart lach ve-lo* — you would be making those vessels *tahor* not only for him but for yourself, since we do borrow his vessels. Therefore the vessels must be declared *tamei* to protect us.

### Hillel's Logic

*Beit Hillel*'s position — that the pottery vessel protects everything — is striking in light of the concession that the *am ha-aretz*'s vessel is presumed *tamei*. The Gemara does not fully reconcile this within the immediate exchange, but the underlying logic emerges from the thrust of the debate: *Beit Hillel* maintains the protective function of the pottery vessel even in the house of an *am ha-aretz*, treating it as *tahor* precisely to avoid the social consequences of declaring everything *tamei* — consequences that *Beit Shammai*'s student will shortly spell out.

## Yehoshua Critique

### Logical Absurdity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: בּוֹשַׁנִי מִדִּבְרֵיכֶם, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי! אֶפְשָׁר אִשָּׁה לָשָׁה בַּעֲרֵיבָה — אִשָּׁה וַעֲרֵיבָה טְמֵאִין שִׁבְעָה, וּבָצֵק טָהוֹר? לוֹגִין מָלֵא מַשְׁקִין — לוֹגִין טָמֵא טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה, וּמַשְׁקִין טְהוֹרִין?!
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to this debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel: **Rabbi Yehoshua said: I am ashamed of your words, Beit Shammai,** for they are illogical. Is it **possible** that there should be a corpse on the first floor, with an earthenware vessel blocking the opening to the second story, and **a woman** is standing upstairs **kneading** dough **in a** metal **bowl,** and the **woman and the bowl are impure** for **seven** days owing to the impurity of the corpse, **while** the **dough** inside the trough **is pure?** For that would be the result according to Beit Shammai, who distinguishes between food and earthenware vessels on the one hand and metal vessels on the other. Similarly: Is it possible that there is **a** metal **pitcher [*login*] full of liquid** in the second story, and the **pitcher** should be **impure with impurity of seven** days, **while the liquids** remain **pure?**

*Tanya amar Rabbi Yehoshua*: Rabbi Yehoshua declared that he is embarrassed — not socially, but logically — by the ruling of *Beit Shammai*. His embarrassment is intellectual: the distinction *Beit Shammai* draws produces absurd results. Consider a woman kneading dough in the upper story; she and her mixing bowl are *tamei shivah* — *tamei* for seven days through *tumat meit* — yet the dough inside the bowl is *tahor*? Or: vessels filled with liquid are *tamei shivah* and the liquids inside them are *tahor*? The very objects in direct physical contact with the food are *tamei* at the most severe level, while the food itself is *tahor*. The logical incoherence is acute.

### Shammai's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְפַּל לוֹ תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד מִתַּלְמִידֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, אָמַר לוֹ: אוֹמַר לְךָ טַעְמָן שֶׁל בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. אָמַר לוֹ: אֱמוֹר. אָמַר לוֹ: כְּלִי טָמֵא — חוֹצֵץ אוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ? אָמַר לוֹ: אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ. כְּלִי שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ — טָמֵא אוֹ טָהוֹר? אָמַר לוֹ: טָמֵא. וְאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״טָמֵא״, כְּלוּם מַשְׁגִּיחַ עָלֶיךָ? וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״טָמֵא״, אוֹמֵר לְךָ: ״שֶׁלִּי טָהוֹר וְשֶׁלְּךָ טָמֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** After Rabbi Yehoshua posed this question, **one student from among the students of Beit Shammai approached him** and **said to him: I will tell you Beit Shammai's reasoning. He said to him: Speak. He said to him: Does an impure vessel serve as a barrier** to corpse contamination **or does it not serve as a barrier?** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: It does not serve as a barrier.** The student asked further: And **is a vessel of an *am ha'aretz* pure or impure? He said to him: Impure.** The student responded: **And if you tell him** that his vessel **is impure, will he pay attention to you at all?** Clearly he will not. **What is more, if you say to him** that it is **impure, he will say to you:** On the contrary, **my** vessel **is pure and yours is impure.**

*Nitpal lo talmid echad mi-talmidei Beit Shammai* — one of the disciples of *Beit Shammai* attached himself to Rabbi Yehoshua and asked permission to explain the reasoning. Rabbi Yehoshua invited him to speak.

The student posed the question in logical sequence. Does a *tamei* vessel serve as a barrier against the spread of *tumah*? Rabbi Yehoshua conceded: no, it does not. Is the vessel of an *am ha-aretz* to be presumed *tamei*? Again Rabbi Yehoshua conceded: yes. Then — the student pressed — if the pottery vessel is *tamei*, the *tumah* passes through it and enters the upper story, and everything should be *tamei*. Everything, that is, except the food and drink.

Why the exception for food and drink? Because the *am ha-aretz* will never accept a ruling that he is eating *tamei* food. He knows his food is prepared in his own way; he regards it as *tahor*. We are working within his own system. According to his own *shittah* — that his pottery vessel is *tahor* — his food is *tahor*. We may affirm this without risk, since we do not eat with him or eat his food.

### Social Cost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶהוּ טַעְמָן שֶׁל בֵּית שַׁמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is Beit Shammai's reasoning:** Food, drink, and earthenware vessels inside a sealed earthenware vessel remain pure, as, since they belong to an *am ha'aretz*, a *ḥaver* will not eat the food or borrow the earthenware vessel. Vessels of metal or similar materials may one day be borrowed by a *ḥaver*, however, and therefore Beit Shammai declared these to be impure.

This leads to the more pointed argument. *Ve-im atah omer lo tamei* — if you were to tell the *am ha-aretz* that everything in the upper story is *tamei*, including his food, *klum mashgi'ach alecha* — he will not pay any attention to you. And worse: he will turn the ruling back on you. *She-lecha tahor ve-shelo tamei* — he will say your things are *tamei* and his are *tahor*. The cost of absolute stringency, imposed on someone who has not accepted your premises, is total rejection. He will reconstruct the classification in his own favor, and you will have lost all credibility and influence.

The only workable approach, then, is to concede where the concession costs nothing — the food, which we do not eat — and hold the line where it costs something — the vessels, which we do borrow. This is not a comfortable ruling. We are declaring something *tahor* that is in fact *tamei*, on the grounds that the declaration does not endanger us and that full honesty would be completely counterproductive. The context also bears noting: what is under discussion here is *tumat meit*, which is far weightier than the ordinary presumed *tumah* of an *am ha-aretz*. We would normally already treat his vessels as *tamei*; here, the *tumah* carries the full weight of *tumat shivah*.

## Yehoshua's Submission

### Prostration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד הָלַךְ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְנִשְׁתַּטַּח עַל קִבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, אָמַר: נַעֲנֵיתִי לָכֶם עַצְמוֹת בֵּית שַׁמַּאי! וּמָה סְתוּמוֹת שֶׁלָּכֶם כָּךְ, מְפוֹרָשׁוֹת — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אָמְרוּ: כׇּל יָמָיו הוּשְׁחֲרוּ שִׁינָּיו מִפְּנֵי תַּעֲנִיּוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Once he heard the logic behind Beit Shammai's opinion, **Rabbi Yehoshua immediately went and prostrated himself on the graves of Beit Shammai,** i.e., the students and proponents of Shammai, and **said: I humble myself before you, bones of Beit Shammai. If such** clarity and wisdom is **found in your** rulings that you stated and left **unexplained, all the more so** must this be the case **in your** rulings when they were stated and **explained.** People **said** of Rabbi Yehoshua: **Throughout his days his teeth darkened because of** all **his fasts** that he undertook to atone for having spoken inappropriately of Beit Shammai.

*Mi-yad halach Rabbi Yehoshua ve-nishtate'ach al kivrei Beit Shammai*. Immediately, Rabbi Yehoshua went and prostrated himself on the graves of *Beit Shammai*. He declared: *na'aneti lachem atzmot Beit Shammai* — I have humbled myself before you, bones of *Beit Shammai*. *U-mah stumot she-lachem kach* — if even your unexplained, seemingly inexplicable rulings rest on such profound and well-grounded reasoning, *meforashot al achat kama ve-chama* — how much more weight and seriousness must I attach to your rulings that are explicit and transparent.

The reversal is total. Rabbi Yehoshua began by declaring himself *embarrassed* at *Beit Shammai*'s distinction. He ends prostrate at their graves. *Kol yamav hushcharu shinav mipnei ta'aniyotav* — all his days, his teeth were blackened from his fasts, undertaken as *teshuvah* for having expressed that embarrassment. The fast was not for questioning the ruling itself, but for publicly proclaiming that the ruling embarrassed him — an expression of disrespect for *Beit Shammai*'s depth that he later recognized was entirely unwarranted.

What also emerges from this episode is a portrait of the social texture of the relationship between *chaveirim* and *amei ha-aretz*. They were not completely separated. They did not eat together, and neither ate the other's food. Clothing presented a problem because of *midras*. But they borrowed vessels from each other, had commercial and neighborly interactions, and even — as we saw at the end of the previous *perek* — at times had *amei ha-aretz* transport *tahor* food. The stratification was real and consequential, centered overwhelmingly around eating and food-handling, but it did not constitute total ostracism.

### Dunking Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת ״לְךָ וָלוֹ״, אַלְמָא שָׁאֲלִינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ! כִּי שָׁיְילִינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ מַטְבְּלִינַן לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its main point. **In any event,** this mishna **teaches** that the status of the vessels of an *am ha'aretz* is relevant both **for you and for him. Apparently,** then, **we** *ḥaverim* **may borrow** vessels **from** *amei ha'aretz*. The question therefore arises: Why are the Sages not concerned that *amei ha'aretz* may immerse vessels inside of other vessels in an inappropriate way, so that they will remain unpurified when borrowed by a *ḥaver*? The Gemara answers: **For when we** *ḥaverim* **borrow** vessels **from them we immerse them** before using them.

*Katani miha* — the upshot of all of this is that we borrow vessels from them. The Gemara accepts this as a given and asks: given that we borrow their vessels, and given that those vessels are presumed *tamei*, how does this affect the earlier discussion about whether a different standard applies for *terumah* vs. *kodesh*? If we borrow their vessels and then use them for *terumah*, it matters little that we declined to take *terumah* directly from them; the vessels we borrowed may be just as problematic.

The Gemara answers: *ki she'alinan mineihu, matbilinan lehu* — when we borrow their vessels, we immerse them in the *mikveh* first. This is the standard practice: borrow, then immerse. Therefore, we need not refuse to borrow; we take the vessels and we *tovel* them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נַיהְדְּרוּ לְהוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵּית שַׁמַּאי: כִּי שָׁאֲלִינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ מַטְבְּלִינַן לְהוּ! טְמֵא מֵת בָּעֵי הַזָּאָה שְׁלִישִׁי וּשְׁבִיעִי, וּמָנָא לְשִׁבְעָה יוֹמֵי לָא מוֹשְׁלִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, let Beit Hillel respond to Beit Shammai.** They can respond as follows: **When we borrow** vessels **from them we immerse them,** and that is why we rule that vessels of metal or similar materials are pure. The Gemara explains: That dispute is referring to the impurity of a corpse. **And that which becomes impure by** proximity to **a corpse requires sprinkling** of the red heifer's ashes on the **third and seventh** days of its purification, **and people do not** generally **lend vessels for seven days.** The solution the *ḥaver* implements of immersing vessels that he borrows from an *am ha'aretz* is effective only for other impurities, but not for the impurity imparted by a corpse.

One category of vessel, however, cannot be taken to the *mikveh*: *kli cheres*. A pottery vessel that has become *tamei* on the inside cannot be purified through immersion alone. This is precisely why *Beit Shammai* could declare the food and drink in the upper story *tahor*: we can affirm this freely because we would never borrow a *kli cheres* from an *am ha-aretz* in the first place — we can never restore it to *taharah* in any practical way. For other vessels — metal, wood — we borrow and we *tovel*, and that suffices.

But in the case of *tumat meit*, immersion alone is not enough. *Tamei meit ba'ei haza'ah shelishi u-shevi'i* — one who has become *tamei* through contact with a corpse requires sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes on the third and seventh day. *Ve-mi luzi le-zayin yomei lo mushali inshi* — people do not generally borrow a vessel for seven days, and it would be entirely unworkable to require that every borrowed vessel be brought to a *parah adumah* distribution point and subjected to the full seven-day purification process. Therefore, in the case of *tumat meit* — which is what the scenario of the house with the corpse involves — we cannot simply declare the vessels *tahor* by reference to our standard practice of immersion. Hence *Beit Shammai*'s ruling stands: the vessels must be treated as *tamei*, while the food, which we would never eat anyway, may be declared *tahor* for the *am ha-aretz* himself.

To summarize the resulting picture: we do not eat the *am ha-aretz*'s food — once food is *tamei*, it cannot be restored, and in any case the entire structure of these distinctions centered on with whom one eats. We treat their clothing as *midras*. But vessels we do borrow, and we immerse them before use. *Terumah* we do not accept from them; their *korbanot* we must accept, and therefore in matters touching *kodesh* we must model the highest standard throughout the year.

## Trust Conditions

### Immersion Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַטְּבִילָה לָא מְהֵימְנִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: נֶאֱמָנִים עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ עַל טׇהֳרַת טְבִילַת טְמֵא מֵת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question with regard to the *halakha* that a *ḥaver* must immerse vessels that he borrows from an *am ha'aretz*: **But** is it so that *amei ha'aretz* **are not trusted with regard to immersion? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: ***Amei ha'aretz* are trusted with regard to the purification** process **of immersion of that which has become impure by** contact with **a corpse?** In addition to being sprinkled with purification water on the third and seventh days, a person or article that has been in contact with a corpse must also undergo immersion on the seventh day. An *am ha'aretz* is believed when he says that he has performed this immersion.

The Gemara now asks: *ve-atvilah le-meheimni* — are we in fact prepared to trust the *am ha-aretz* to immerse vessels properly? The question arises in connection with the *ma'alah* discussed earlier — that a vessel must not be immersed inside another vessel — which implies concern that the *am ha-aretz* will not perform *tevilah* with the necessary care. But we also have a *baraita*: *ne'emanim amei ha-aretz al taharat tevilat tamei meit* — the *amei ha-aretz* are trusted to say that they immersed themselves after becoming *tamei* through contact with a corpse. If they are trusted to report their own *tevilah*, how can we doubt the quality of their immersion?

### Body vs. Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּגוּפוֹ, הָא בְּכֵלָיו. רָבָא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּכֵלָיו, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאָמַר ״מֵעוֹלָם לֹא הִטְבַּלְתִּי כְּלִי בְּתוֹךְ כְּלִי״, וְהָא דְּאָמַר ״הִטְבַּלְתִּי, אֲבָל לֹא הִטְבַּלְתִּי בִּכְלִי שֶׁאֵין בְּפִיו כִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת הַנּוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents two answers for this question. **Abaye said** one answer: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which teaches that an *am ha'aretz* is trusted, is referring **to** the immersion of **his body,** whereas **this** teaching of the Gemara that *amei ha'aretz* are not trusted concerning immersion deals **with his vessels. Rava said** a different answer: Both **this and that,** both the *baraita* and the Gemara's teaching, refer **to** the **vessels** of an *am ha'aretz*, **and it is** nevertheless **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which teaches that an *am ha'aretz* is trusted, is referring to an *am ha'aretz* **who said: I never immersed one vessel inside another,** which is a statement that we accept. **And this** teaching of the Gemara that *amei ha'aretz* are not trusted deals with one **who said: I have immersed** vessels inside of other vessels, **but I did not immerse with a vessel whose mouth does not have** the width of **the tube of a wineskin.** It is with regard to such details that an *am ha'aretz* cannot be trusted.

*Amar Abaye: lo kashya. Ha be-gufo, ha be-keilav.* The question is not difficult: the two cases are distinct. The *baraita* speaks of trusting an *am ha-aretz* regarding his own bodily immersion. The concern with the *ma'alah* is about the immersion of vessels. One may exercise greater care and attentiveness in matters relating to one's own *tumah ve-taharah* than in matters of vessel-handling, and perhaps the particular problem of one vessel inside another — *kli betoch kli* — is less likely to arise with human immersion.

### Rava's View

Rava disagrees with Abaye's framing. *Idi ve-idi be-keilav* — both cases are about vessels. The distinction, however, is not between body and vessels but between two different types of claims. *Ve-lo kashya:* if the *am ha-aretz* says *me-olam lo hitbalti kli betoch kli* — I have never immersed a vessel inside another vessel — we believe him. We do not assume he is lying; we assume he may lack attentiveness or knowledge of certain details, but we do not suspect outright falsehood. If he says he never did the problematic thing, we take him at his word.

But if he says: I did immerse one vessel inside another, but I was careful to ensure the mouth was wide enough, or that the inner vessel was positioned so water could circulate freely — that we do not accept. The issue is no longer honesty but competence and attentiveness. Can we trust that an *am ha-aretz*, even with good intentions, applied the necessary care and knowledge to a situation that required careful monitoring? That trust we cannot extend.

This distinction resonates well beyond this context. In matters of *hashgacha*, the question of whether someone is reliable is not a single question but two: Is the person honest? And does the person have sufficient knowledge and attentiveness to deliver what they sincerely intend to deliver? The Gemara locates the limit of trust in *amei ha-aretz* precisely at the point where intent and execution may diverge.

### Fruit Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: נֶאֱמָן עַם הָאָרֶץ לוֹמַר פֵּירוֹת לֹא הוּכְשְׁרוּ, אֲבָל אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן לוֹמַר פֵּירוֹת הוּכְשְׁרוּ אֲבָל לֹא נִטְמְאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And so it was taught** in a *baraita* to this effect: **An *am ha'aretz* is trusted to say** that **produce has not been made susceptible** to impurity, i.e., that it has never come into contact with water, **but he is not trusted to say** that the **produce has been made susceptible** to impurity **but has not** actually **become impure.** This *baraita* shows that *amei ha'aretz* are trusted concerning basic facts, but not concerning matters that require detailed knowledge and scrupulous care.

A parallel *baraita* reinforces this principle: *ne'eman am ha-aretz lomar peirot lo huchsharu* — an *am ha-aretz* is believed to say that fruit never came into contact with liquid and therefore never became susceptible to *tumah*. But *eino ne'eman lomar peirot huchsharu aval lo nitme'u* — he is not believed to say that the fruit became susceptible but he then ensured it did not contract *tumah*. Once again: if the problematic circumstance never arose, he is believed. If it arose and he claims to have managed it correctly, he is not believed. The concern about honesty is absent; the concern about whether he monitored a live risk with sufficient care is present.

### Parah Adumah Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַגּוּפוֹ מִי מְהֵימַן? וְהָתַנְיָא: חָבֵר שֶׁבָּא לְהַזּוֹת — מַזִּין עָלָיו מִיָּד, עַם הָאָרֶץ שֶׁבָּא לְהַזּוֹת — אֵין מַזִּין עָלָיו עַד שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה בְּפָנֵינוּ שְׁלִישִׁי וּשְׁבִיעִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question with regard to Abaye's opinion: **And is** an *am ha'aretz* really **trusted** concerning **his body,** when he claims to have immersed? **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning **a *ḥaver* who comes** before those in charge of sprinkling water of purification **to be sprinkled** with that water, and claims that the requisite three days have passed since his contamination by a corpse, **they may sprinkle upon him immediately. But** concerning **an *am ha'aretz* who comes** before them and claims that three days have passed, **they may not sprinkle upon him until he performs** and counts **in our presence** the **third** day **and** the **seventh** day? This shows that an *am ha'aretz* is not trusted concerning the purity of his own body.

The Gemara then complicates the earlier picture. A *baraita* teaches: *chaver she-ba lehazzot, mazim alav mi-yad* — if a *chaver* comes to be sprinkled with the *parah adumah* ashes, saying it has been three days since he contracted *tumat meit*, we sprinkle immediately. But *am ha-aretz she-ba lehazzot* — if an *am ha-aretz* comes, we do not trust his count. We make him wait until the third day from when he arrives before us, complete the first sprinkling, then wait a full seven days from that point for the second. We do not credit his claim about where he stands in the seven-day process.

This appears to contradict the *baraita* cited earlier, which had stated that *amei ha-aretz* are trusted regarding their immersion after *tumat meit*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִתּוֹךְ חוֹמֶר שֶׁהֶחְמַרְתָּ עָלָיו בִּתְחִילָּתוֹ, הֵקַלְתָּ עָלָיו בְּסוֹפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said,** modifying his previous explanation: **Because of the stringency that you applied to** the *am ha'aretz* **in his beginning,** i.e., at the beginning of the purification process, by not allowing him to purify himself without first ensuring that he has not been in contact with a corpse for three days, **you** may **be lenient with him in his end,** in that he is trusted regarding having immersed at the end of the seven days, removing the impurity contracted through contact with a corpse.

*Ella amar Abaye* — Abaye resolves this: *mitoch chomer she-hichmarta lo bi-techillato, hikalta lo be-sofo* — because of the stringency we impose at the beginning of the process, we are able to be lenient at the end. Since the *am ha-aretz* knows that we are suspicious of him and will not credit his count, and since he has seen what the cost of that suspicion is — he must wait through the full process again from the start — he will be exceedingly careful to immerse properly once the process is complete, so as not to have to repeat it. The trust we extend at the end regarding his *tevilah* is not naive; it is enabled by the very strictness we apply at the beginning.

*Rashi* explains: *le-fi she-hu nizhar be-tevilah kedei she-lo yitztarech lo od* — he is careful about the *tevilah* precisely so that he will not need to go through the ordeal again. The general trust we extend is thus conditioned on the demonstrated consequences he faces when we do not trust him.

## Back vs. Inside

### Vessel Sections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחוֹרַיִם וָתוֹךְ. מַאי אֲחוֹרַיִם וָתוֹךְ? כְּדִתְנַן: כְּלִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא אֲחוֹרָיו בְּמַשְׁקִין — אֲחוֹרָיו טְמֵאִין, תּוֹכוֹ, אוֹגְנוֹ, אׇזְנוֹ, וְיָדָיו — טְהוֹרִין. נִטְמָא תּוֹכוֹ — כּוּלּוֹ טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: The *halakhot* of **the back** of a vessel **and** its **inside** apply to vessels used for *teruma*, but not for sacrificial food. The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of: **The back and** its **inside?** The Gemara explains. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Keilim* 25:6): **A vessel whose back** part, as opposed to its inside, **was defiled by** contact with impure **liquid. Its back is impure,** while its other parts, such as **its inside, its rim, its ear-shaped handles, and its** straight **handles are pure.** By Torah law, foods and liquids cannot impart ritual impurity to a vessel at all, but by rabbinic law liquids can. In order to clarify that it is only a rabbinic decree, they instituted that the impurity thereby imparted, if the liquid touched the outside of the vessel, should affect only the part touched by the liquid, but not its inside or the other parts of its outside. However, if **its inside was defiled,** even by impurity only according to rabbinic law, **it is all impure.** This *halakha* applies only to *teruma*, but regarding offerings, the defilement of any part of the vessel renders it all impure.

The Gemara turns to another of the *ma'alot* discussed in the *mishnah*: the distinction between the *achorayim* — the back or outer surface of a vessel — and the *toch* — the inner cavity, along with the lip, ears, and handle. Biblically, if any part of a vessel contracts *tumah*, the entire vessel is *tamei*. Rabbinically, however, a distinction was introduced: if the *back* of a vessel comes into contact with *tamei* liquid, only the back becomes *tamei*; the inside, the lip (*ogen*), the ears (*oznav*), and the handle (*yadav*) remain *tahor*.

This distinction is rooted in the nature of the rabbinic extension that generated it. The only liquid that is an *av ha-tumah* — a primary source of *tumah* — is the discharge of a *zav* or *zavah*, including their saliva and urine. The rabbis extended the *tumah*-transmitting power of *tamei* liquid generally, treating all *tamei* liquids as capable of making vessels *tamei*, even though ordinarily such liquids are at most *sheini* and would lack that power. Because this generalization was entirely rabbinic and represented a major stringency, the rabbis deliberately marked it as such by introducing an internal incoherence: only the surface that actually touched the *tamei* liquid becomes *tamei*, while the rest of the vessel remains *tahor*. The absurdity of the outer wall being *tamei* while the inner wall is *tahor* signals to any observer that this is a rabbinic construct, not a biblical rule.

Conversely: if the inside of the vessel becomes *tamei*, *nitma tocho kulo tamei* — everything becomes *tamei*. This parallels the rule for *kli cheres*, where *tumah* entering the airspace of the interior affects everything within.

### Handles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הַצְּבִיטָה וְכוּ׳. מַאי בֵּית הַצְּבִיטָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מָקוֹם שֶׁצּוֹבְטוֹ, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּצְבׇּט לָהּ קָלִי״. רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָקוֹם שֶׁנְּקִיֵּי הַדַּעַת צוֹבְעִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And its place for gripping.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of: **Place for gripping [*beit hatzevita*]? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** It is the **place** where **he** grips the vessel in order to **pass [*tzovet*]** it, a kind of indentation used for grasping the vessel. **And similarly it states: "And he pinched [*vayitzbat*] some parched corn for her"** (Ruth 2:14), which means that he gave her a little of the corn. Therefore, the term is referring to a place on the vessel used to grasp it. **Rabbi Asi** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is the **place that fastidious people** use for **dipping.** A small receptacle for spices and the like would be attached to the sides of vessels for dipping one's food.

The *beit ha-tzvitah* — the handle — is similarly treated as a distinct zone that does not become *tamei* along with the outer surface. The Gemara asks: what exactly is the *beit ha-tzvitah*? *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: mekom she-tzovtin* — the place one grasps or by which one presents the vessel. The word may relate to the verse *va-yitztbot lah kali*, where something is extended forth. *Rav Asi amar Rabbi Yochanan: mekom she-nekiyei ha-da'at tzovim* — the place where fastidious people would dip, referring, according to *Rashi*, to a small separate insert or indentation in the vessel designed to hold oil or a condiment into which one dips food — a kind of built-in dipping vessel within the larger one.

### Terumah Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב בִּיבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: כׇּל הַכֵּלִים אֵין לָהֶם אֲחוֹרַיִם וָתוֹךְ, אֶחָד קׇדְשֵׁי הַמִּקְדָּשׁ וְאֶחָד קׇדְשֵׁי הַגְּבוּל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קׇדְשֵׁי הַגְּבוּל מַאי נִינְהוּ — תְּרוּמָה, וְהָתְנַן: אֲחוֹרַיִם וָתוֹךְ וּבֵית הַצְּבִיטָה לִתְרוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai taught** the following *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman: No vessels have** this difference between **the back and inside, whether** they are vessels used for **consecrated** foods **of the Temple or** those used for **consecrated** foods **of outlying areas,** i.e., outside the Temple. In these cases, if one of the parts of the vessel was defiled with impure liquids the entire vessel becomes impure. Rav Naḥman **said to him: What are these consecrated** foods **of outlying areas** mentioned in the *baraita*? This term is usually applied to ***teruma*,** but if so the *baraita* contradicts the mishna. For **didn't we learn** in the mishna: The *halakhot* of **the back** of a vessel **and** its **inside and its place for gripping** apply **to** vessels used for ***teruma*?**

*Tanei Rav Bibi kamei de-Rav Nachman: kol ha-kelim ein lahem achorayim ve-toch* — all vessels are treated as a single unit, without the back-inside distinction. *Echad kodshei mizbei'ach, echad kodshei mikdash, ve-echad kodshei ha-gvul* — whether the *kodshim* brought to the altar, or those involving the *Beit HaMikdash* generally, or those of the outer provinces. *Kodshei gvul*? What is that? *Terumah*, it would seem. But our own *mishnah* states explicitly that the *achorayim ve-toch* distinction *does* apply by *terumah*. How can Rav Bibi's formulation be correct?

### Kodesh Differ

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ קָאָמְרַתְּ. אַדְכַּרְתַּן מִילְּתָא דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲלוֹת שָׁנוּ כָּאן; שֵׁשׁ רִאשׁוֹנוֹת — בֵּין לַקּוֹדֶשׁ, בֵּין לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ, אַחֲרוֹנוֹת — לַקּוֹדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לְחוּלִּין שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל טׇהֳרַת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman continued: **Perhaps** when you said the consecrated foods of outlying areas you were not referring to *teruma*, but rather **you are speaking of non-sacred** food **prepared according to the** standards of **purity of sacrificial** food, and you called them consecrated foods of outlying areas because this level of purity can be observed outside the Temple as well. In the course of this discussion Rav Naḥman said to Rav Beivai: **You have** now **reminded me** of something that **Rabba bar Avuh said** concerning this issue: **They taught eleven stringencies** of sacrificial food **here** in the mishna. The **first six** apply **both to sacrificial** food **and to non-sacred** food **prepared according to the** standards of **purity of sacrificial** food, whereas **the last** five **apply** only **to sacrificial** food**s but not to non-sacred** food **prepared according to the** standards of **purity of sacrificial** food. Rabba bar Avuh's statement therefore corroborates the interpretation of the *baraita* as dealing with non-sacred food prepared according to the standards of purity of sacrificial foods.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: perhaps *kodshei gvul* does not refer to *terumah* at all, but to *chullin she-na'asu al taharat ha-kodesh* — ordinary food that one treats with the *taharah* standards of *kodesh*. This is corroborated by a teaching of *Rabba bar Avuha*: *achas esrei ma'alot shanu kan* — there are eleven levels enumerated in the *mishnah*. *Shesh bi-rishonah bein la-kodesh bein le-chullin she-na'asu al taharat ha-kodesh* — the first six apply equally to *kodesh* and to *chullin* treated as *kodesh*. *Chamesh acharonot la-kodesh ve-lo le-chullin she-na'asu al taharat ha-kodesh* — the final five apply only to *kodesh* proper, not to *chullin she-na'asu al taharat ha-kodesh*. The *achorayim ve-toch* distinction belongs among the *ma'alot* that apply to *terumah* but not to *kodesh* — for *kodesh*, the entire vessel is treated as a single unit and no partial-*tumah* distinctions are made.

The category of *chullin she-na'asu al taharat ha-kodesh* — called here *kodshei gvul*, the sanctity of the outer provinces — also invites a broader reflection. Even while the *Beit HaMikdash* stood, people sought to bring a dimension of *kedusha* into their ordinary eating by treating their *chullin* with the *taharah* standards of *kodesh*. The designation *kodshei gvul* — the sacred things of the territory outside — suggests that this practice was understood not merely as fastidiousness or habit-formation for *kodesh* occasions, but as a genuine attempt to extend *kedushah* outward from the *Mikdash* into the ordinary domain. After the *churban*, *Chazal* channeled this same impulse through forms of *kedushah* that were egalitarian rather than stratifying: *netilat yadayim*, *brachot* before and after eating, the principle that eating without a *bracha* is *ke-ilu ma'al be-hekdesh* — as though one misappropriated something sacred. These forms preserved the sanctification of eating without recreating the social divisions that the *taharah* system had generated.

## Midras Story

### Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת הַמִּדְרָס — נוֹשֵׂא אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ. קוֹדֶשׁ, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מַעֲבִיר חָבִית שֶׁל יֵין קוֹדֶשׁ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם,
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One who carries an object trodden** by a *zav* may **carry *teruma*** at the same time, if he is careful that neither he nor the impure object come into contact with the *teruma*, **but** this may **not** be done with **sacrificial** food. The Gemara asks: Concerning **sacrificial** food, **what is the reason** that he may **not** carry it? As long as contact with the food is prevented, why should he not carry sacrificial food as well? The Gemara answers: This enactment was made **due to an incident that occurred. As Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** There was once **an incident involving someone who was transferring a barrel of sacrificial wine from one place to another,**

*Ha-nosei et ha-midras nosei et ha-terumah aval lo et ha-kodesh* — one who is carrying a *midras* — the seat, sandal, or garment of a *zav*, which is an *av ha-tumah* — may simultaneously carry *terumah* but not *kodesh*.

The logic of the *terumah* case: the carrier of the *midras* is himself a *rishon le-tumah*. A *rishon* cannot render a vessel a *sheni*; only an *av ha-tumah* can initiate that chain of transmission. *Rashi* specifies that the *terumah* is in a *kli cheres* whose exterior is touching the carrier, so since a *kli cheres* becomes *tamei* only through its interior, the *terumah* inside is protected. But for *kodesh* — why is it not equally protected?

The answer: *mishum ma'aseh she-hayah* — because of a specific incident.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִפְסְקָה רְצוּעָה שֶׁל סַנְדָּלוֹ, וּנְטָלָהּ וְהִנִּיחָהּ עַל פִּי חָבִית, וְנָפְלָה לַאֲוִיר הֶחָבִית, וְנִטְמֵאת. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת הַמִּדְרָס — נוֹשֵׂא אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **and the strap of his sandal,** which had been rendered ritually impure by being trodden by a *zav*, **broke off, and he picked up** the strap **and placed it on** top of **the mouth of** the **barrel, and** it **fell into the airspace of the barrel,** thereby **rendering** the entire barrel **impure. At that time** the Sages **said** and issued a permanent decree that **one who carries an object that is impure by having been trodden** by a *zav* **may carry *teruma*** at the same time, **but not sacrificial** food.

*D'amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: a man was transporting a barrel of *yayin kodesh* — wine designated for use in the *Beit HaMikdash* — from one place to another. A strap of his sandal broke. He picked up the strap and placed it on the mouth of the barrel. It fell into the airspace of the barrel, and since a *kli cheres* becomes *tamei* through objects entering its airspace, the entire contents became *tamei*. *Be-otah sha'ah amru* — from that moment they ruled: one who carries a *midras* may carry *terumah* alongside it, but not *kodesh*.

### Ruling

What the man was doing carrying the sandal of a *zav* is not explained by the Gemara. *Rashi* simply notes that it was a *midras ha-zav* — a sandal that had been worn by a *zav* — and that the man had taken it in his hand for reasons unspecified. In any case, the ruling derived from the incident is clear: the same care that would seem sufficient for *terumah* is not sufficient for *kodesh*, and the story of the barrel enshrined that distinction as law.

## Chananya Principle

### Jordan River Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי! הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לֹא אָסְרוּ אֶלָּא בַּיַּרְדֵּן וּבִסְפִינָה, וּכְמַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if they prohibited doing so due to that incident, they should have made the same decree with regard to ***teruma* also,** because if the same incident would occur with *teruma* it would defile the *teruma* as well. The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** stated? **It is** that of **Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya, who said** concerning a similar issue: **They prohibited** it **only in the Jordan** River, **and** only **in a boat,** in a situation **similar to the incident that occurred,** which triggered the enactment.

The Gemara now asks why the prohibition derived from the barrel incident does not extend to *terumah* as well. The answer invokes the opinion of Rabbi Chananya ben Akavia: *lo asru ela be-Yarden u-ve-sefina u-che-ma'aseh she-hayah* — they only prohibited in the exact parallel case of the incident, and did not generalize.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם מֵי חַטָּאת וְאֵפֶר חַטָּאת וְיַעֲבִירֵם בַּיַּרְדֵּן וּבִסְפִינָה, וְלֹא יַעֲמוֹד בְּצַד זֶה וְיִזְרְקֵם לְצַד אַחֵר, וְלֹא יְשִׁיטֵם עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם, וְלֹא יִרְכַּב עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי חֲבֵירוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיוּ רַגְלָיו נוֹגְעוֹת בַּקַּרְקַע. אֲבָל מַעֲבִירָן עַל גַּבֵּי הַגֶּשֶׁר וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ; אֶחָד הַיַּרְדֵּן וְאֶחָד שְׁאָר הַנְּהָרוֹת. רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא אָסְרוּ אֶלָּא בַּיַּרְדֵּן וּבִסְפִינָה, וּכְמַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents the background to Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya's statement: **What is that** enactment that Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya was referring to? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may not carry the water of purification and the ashes of purification and transport them across the Jordan** River, **and** this is if he is **on a boat. Nor may he stand on one side** of the river **and throw them to the other side. Nor may he float them** across the river. **Nor may he** ford the river **riding on an animal or on his friend** and carrying the water or ashes of purification, **unless his feet are touching the ground** as he fords the river. **But he may transfer them** across the river **over a bridge without concern** about transferring them improperly. This decree applies **both** to **the Jordan and** to **other rivers. Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya says:** The Sages **prohibited** these acts **only in the Jordan** River, **and** only if he transports them **in a boat, and** in circumstances exactly **like** those of **the incident that occurred.**

The *baraita* underlying this principle: a person may not carry the water of *Parah Adumah* and its ashes and transport them across the Jordan River by boat. Nor may one toss them across a river, float them across the water, or transport them while riding on an animal or on another person's back — unless one's feet are touching the ground. One may, however, carry them across a bridge, since a bridge is considered an extension of the ground. This prohibition applies equally to the Jordan and to all other rivers.

Rabbi Chananya ben Akavia dissents: *lo asru ela be-Yarden u-ve-sefina u-che-ma'aseh she-hayah* — they only prohibited specifically the case that arose: crossing the Jordan River in a boat. The broader prohibition, which extends to all rivers and to any transport not grounded, represents an abstraction that Rabbi Chananya ben Akavia refuses to endorse.

### Abstraction Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה? דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מַעֲבִיר מֵי חַטָּאת וְאֵפֶר חַטָּאת בַּיַּרְדֵּן וּבִסְפִינָה וְנִמְצָא כְּזַיִת מֵת תָּחוּב בְּקַרְקָעִיתָהּ שֶׁל סְפִינָה. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם מֵי חַטָּאת וְאֵפֶר חַטָּאת וְיַעֲבִירֵם בַּיַּרְדֵּן בִּסְפִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What** was the **incident that occurred? Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** There was once **an incident involving a person who was transferring water of purification and ashes of purification in the Jordan, and** he was **on a boat, and an olive-bulk** from a **corpse was discovered stuck in the floor of the boat,** over which the water of purification had passed, thereby rendering them impure and invalid. **At that time** the Sages **said: A person may not carry water of purification and ashes of purification and transport them across the Jordan,** and this is if he is **on a boat.** Just as in that case, Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya stated that the decree was limited to the specific circumstances of the original incident, here too, he would say that since the original incident involved sacrificial food and not *teruma* the Sages did not apply the decree to *teruma*.

What was the original incident that generated the *Parah Adumah* transport prohibition? *D'amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: a man was transporting *mei chatas* and *efer chatas* across the Jordan in a boat, and a *ke-zayit* of a corpse was found embedded in the floor of the boat. This rendered everything in the boat *tamei*, ruining the *Parah Adumah* materials. *Be-otah sha'ah amru* — at that moment they prohibited transporting *mei chatas* and *efer chatas* across the Jordan by boat.

The mainstream position generalized from this case to prohibit any transport of *Parah Adumah* materials that is not rooted in the ground. Rabbi Chananya ben Akavia insists on limiting the prohibition to exactly the scenario that occurred.

In the parallel barrel case: there is a specific incident involving a *midras* sandal and an open barrel of *kodesh* wine. The generalization that follows is that carrying a *midras* alongside *kodesh* is prohibited. Rabbi Chananya ben Akavia would confine this prohibition to *kodesh*, and not extend it to *terumah*, because the incident involved *kodesh*. Others might argue that the underlying risk — a *midras* contaminating nearby contents — is identical for *terumah*, and that the prohibition should follow. The Gemara accepts Rabbi Chananya ben Akavia's limiting approach as the operative principle, which is why the ruling ends at *kodesh* and does not reach *terumah*.

### Disputed Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: סַנְדָּל טָמֵא, סַנְדָּל טָהוֹר מַהוּ? חָבִית פְּתוּחָה, חָבִית סְתוּמָה מַהוּ? עָבַר וְנָשָׂא מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Several **dilemmas were raised before** the scholars: It is clear that this *halakha*, like the incident itself, applies to an object that has actually contracted impurity by being trodden by a *zav*, such as an **impure sandal.** But if it is **a pure sandal, what is** the *halakha*? Is the decree so far-reaching as to include a prohibition against carrying even a pure sandal together with sacrificial food? Similarly, the *halakha* certainly applies to **an open barrel,** where there is a danger that the sandal may enter the airspace of the barrel, as in the original incident, but if it was **a closed barrel,** where there is no such danger, **what is** the *halakha*? Does the decree apply in this case as well? Another question: If someone **transgressed** this decree **and carried** an impure sandal together with sacrificial food, **what is** the *halakha*?

On the basis of this framework, the Gemara raises a series of unresolved questions. *Sandal tamei, sandal tahor mahu* — what if the sandal being carried is *tahor*, not *tamei*? Should one never carry any sandal alongside *kodesh*, as a generalization of the barrel incident? *Chavit pesucha* — we know the problem arose with an open barrel; what of a sealed barrel, *chavit stuma mahu*? Perhaps the concern does not extend to a sealed barrel, into whose airspace nothing can fall. These questions remain in the category of *teiku*.

### Ila vs. Zeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אִילָא אָמַר: אִם עָבַר וְנָשָׂא — טָמֵא. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: עָבַר וְנָשָׂא — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a conclusion for the last dilemma. **Rabbi Ila said: If one transgressed and carried** sacrificial food together with an impure sandal, **it is** indeed rendered **impure. Rabbi Zeira said:** If **he transgressed and carried** sacrificial food in this manner, **it is** still **pure.**

Finally: *avar ve-nasa mahu* — what is the status *bedi'avad*? Perhaps the prohibition applies only *lechatchila*; if one violated it and transported *kodesh* while carrying a *midras*, is the *kodesh* thereby *tamei* or does it remain *tahor*?

*Amar Rabbi Ila: avar ve-nasa tamei* — Rabbi Ila holds that even *bedi'avad* the *kodesh* is rendered *tamei*. The prohibition reflects an actual *tumah* risk, and violation of it produces *tumah* in practice. *Rabbi Zeira amar: avar ve-nasa tahor* — Rabbi Zeira holds that *bedi'avad* the *kodesh* remains *tahor*. The prohibition is a *lechatchila* precaution, but after the fact, no *tumah* has actually been transferred. Here the daf concludes.
