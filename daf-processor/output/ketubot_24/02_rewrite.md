# Ketubot 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Captive Women & Single W…

### Captive Women Testimony

The daf opens by picking up from the *mishna* on the bottom of 23b, which addressed two women who had been taken captive. The fundamental *chiddush* established there is that although each woman is not believed to testify about her own status — if witnesses already established that she was taken captive — she is believed to testify about her fellow captive. This holds even where there exists a possible concern of reciprocity, a concern the *Gemara* will make explicit in the course of today's discussion.

### Single Witness Despite R…

Beyond the mutual testimony of the two women, the *mishna* also establishes a broader principle regarding *ed echad*: even a single witness is believed in matters relating to captivity. Moreover, the *Gemara* indicated that even in a case of one witness against one — where one *ed* says she was violated and another says she was not — we follow the witness who says she was not violated, even if that witness would normally occupy a weaker evidentiary position under standard *halacha*, as in the case where the exonerating witness is a woman testifying against a man's claim. This represents a particular leniency, parallel to principles encountered in Yevamos, in extending credibility to the *ed* who exonerates. Further implications of this principle will emerge as the daf proceeds.

## Kohanim Witness Dispute

### Kohanim Testifying Mutual

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which presents a parallel case in a different domain. Two men each claim to be a *kohen* — *zeh omer kohen ani v'zeh omer kohen ani*. Standing alone, neither man is believed about himself in the absence of an established *chazaka* that he is a *kohen*, and therefore neither would be given *terumah* at the threshing floor. However, when each testifies about the other — each saying his fellow is a *kohen* — they are believed, and *terumah* is given to both. This is the *chiddush* of the *mishna* on two levels: first, that *ed echad* suffices to establish *kehuna* for purposes of *terumah*; second, that this is accepted even where there is a concern about collusion or reciprocity between the two.

### Rabbi Yehuda Rejects

*Rabbi Yehuda* dissents: *ein ma'alin l'kehunah al pi ed echad* — *kehuna* status may not be established on the basis of a single witness. This is a categorical position that will be examined against what appears to be a contradictory ruling of his elsewhere.

### Rabbi Eliezer Conditional

*Rabbi Eliezer* qualifies: when is it true that a single witness is insufficient? Only *bimkom she'yesh orerim* — where a concrete challenge has been raised against the person's *kehuna* status. But where there is no such concrete challenge, *ma'alin l'kehunah al pi ed echad* — one witness suffices to establish *kehuna*. The *Gemara* will later note that *Rabbi Eliezer* may also decline to accept the specific case of the *tanna kamma* — the case where two men testify about one another and reciprocity is a concern — and may only accept *ed echad* in the standard, uncontested case.

### Rabban Shimon Position

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says, citing *Rabbi Shimon ben HaSgan*: *ma'alin l'kehunah al pi ed echad* — a single witness suffices to establish *kehuna* status. The difficulty is that his position is not immediately distinguishable from the preceding opinions. The *tanna kamma* permits even the case of mutual testimony despite the concern of collusion; *Rabbi Eliezer* permits *ed echad* where there is no concrete challenge; *Rabbi Yehuda* forbids *ed echad* entirely; and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* restates the permissive view without specifying how he diverges from either camp. The *Gemara* will work through these distinctions in the course of the analysis that follows.

## Peh She'Asar Analysis

### Multiple Cases Needed

The *Gemara* asks: *kol hanei lama li* — why are all of these *mishnayyot* necessary? The reference is to the series of cases across the preceding *mishnayyot* involving *ha'peh she'asar hu ha'peh she'hitir*, as well as the subsequent *mishnayyot* about *ne'emanut* without a *peh she'asar* component — namely, trusting *ed echad* regarding *shvuya* and trusting *ed echad* regarding *kohen* status. The *Gemara* proceeds to explain why each case is independently necessary.

The first case in the *perek* — where *Rabbi Yehoshua* agrees that the man who says "this field was your father's and I purchased it from you" is believed — is accepted because *de'ika drara demamona*, there is a *drara demamona* present. The *Gemara* relies on this concept to explain the conditions under which *peh she'asar* operates, and it is precisely around this term that a significant dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* emerges.

### Drara Definition Debate

*Rashi* explains *drara demamona* as meaning loss of money — *hefsed mamon*. The significance for *Rashi* is that because the first half of the man's statement would cause him to lose the field if left unqualified, it is manifest that the second part of the statement — "but I purchased it from him" — is intended as part of the same continuous utterance. This bears on the implicit requirement, never stated explicitly by the *Gemara* but understood by the *Rishonim*, that the two halves of the *peh she'asar* statement must constitute a single statement, perhaps even *toch k'dei dibbur* of one another. When money is at stake, *Rashi* argues, it is clear that the qualifying clause is organically linked to the initial admission, eliminating any concern that the admission was made at one time and the qualification asserted separately later.

*Tosafot* understands *drara demamona* differently: it means a *shaychus l'mamon*, a connection to money, rather than an actual loss. The simpler point, in *Tosafot*'s reading, is that the man is already in possession of the field, and it is therefore more readily understandable that we would apply *peh she'asar* — he is already physically present and connected to the subject matter of his claim. The *Rosh* also notes that *Rashi* seems to read *demuchzak basadeh* into the term. In any case, the dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* revolves around whether the operative concept is *hefsed mamon* or *shaychus mamon*. The *Gemara* does not resolve this explicitly, and it has remained a subject of debate among the *Rishonim*.

### Captivity Case Necessity

The *Gemara* then explains why the case of *edim* — witnesses who say "yes, we signed this document, but we were *ketanim*" or "*anusim*" or "*krovim*" — was needed alongside the case of the field. In the *edim* case there is no *drara demamona* in *Rashi*'s sense: the money has not changed hands, and the witnesses themselves are not the ones at risk of losing anything. One might have thought that *peh she'asar* would not apply there. Conversely, had only the case of the *edim* been taught, one might have thought that witnesses are believed only because they are helping someone else — the *loveh* — and the second half of their statement is therefore more readily credited. But in the case of the man claiming to have purchased the field, the second part of his statement benefits himself — *le'nafshei* — and one might have argued that *peh she'asar* should not apply there. Hence both cases are needed.

It is also worth noting, as *Tosafot* points out, that the case of the *edim* carried its own independent *chiddushim*: once their statement triggered the validity of the *shtar* in the first half, one might argue they cannot then be believed to undermine the *shtar* with the second half of their statement. Both elements — the field case and the *edim* case — were therefore required.

The *Gemara* further explains: having established *peh she'asar* in monetary contexts, one might still have wondered whether it would apply in a case involving *issur* — such as a woman who says "I was married and then divorced." The weight of that *issur ve'hetter* is more severe, and one might say we would not accept the second half of the statement in so grave a matter. Thus the *mishna* about the divorced woman was needed as well.

## Captivity Case Purpose

### Gomlin Concern Address

The *Gemara* then asks: why was the case of *shtei nashim she'nishbu* — two captive women testifying about one another — necessary? The *mishna* about captivity had already appeared in the context of *peh she'asar*. The *Gemara* answers that the unique *chiddush* of the two-women case is the ruling that we are not concerned about *gomlin* — reciprocity. The concern would be that they have colluded: I will say you were not violated if you say the same of me. The *mishna* teaches *ka mashma lan* that we do not suspect *gomlin* in this context, which is a *chiddush* beyond the mere principle of believing *ed echad* or a woman's testimony in captivity matters.

### Kohanim Case Purpose

Why was the case of two men each claiming *kehuna* needed? The *Gemara* answers: *mishum d'kaba'i le'misni plugta de-Rabbi Yehuda ve-Rabbanan* — because the *Gemara* wanted to record the dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Rabbanan*. Even if one accepted that *ed echad* suffices for *shvuya*, it does not automatically follow that *ed echad* suffices for *kehuna* status. *Rabbi Yehuda* is stricter by *kohen* than by *shvuya*, and this divergence requires explanation.

### Kehuna Special Status

Two reasons are offered for why *kehuna* may warrant greater scrutiny than *shvuya*. First, by *shvuya* the principle of *shvuya hekilu* applies — the Rabbis were specially lenient regarding a captive woman, and *Rabbi Yehuda* himself accepts that leniency. The case of *kehuna* does not benefit from any such blanket rabbinic dispensation. Second, and more consequentially, allowing a man to eat *terumah* has downstream effects. People will come to treat him as a fully established *kohen*, and that status will be translated into other domains: into *yuchsin* considerations, into questions about whom he may marry, and potentially into his eligibility to serve in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *kehuna* context therefore carries a kind of ripple effect that makes it qualitatively different from the *shvuya* context, and this justifies greater caution.

## Donkey Drivers Baraita

### Grain Quality Testimony

The *Gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that appears to present a striking reversal of the positions of *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Rabbanan*. The case involves *chamarim* — merchants who travel from city to city with their goods loaded on donkeys — who enter a town. One of them declares: "My grain is *chadash* and my friend's is *yashan*; my grain has not had *terumot u'ma'asrot* taken, while my friend's has." In other words, he testifies entirely in his friend's favor on all relevant counts: the friend's grain is of better quality and is halachically prepared for use.

On the question of *chadash* and *yashan*, both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* conclude that the reference is not to the halachic *issur* of eating *chadash* grain before the *omer* — because the *Gemara* will establish shortly that we are dealing with *d'rabbanan* concerns here. Rather, *chadash* and *yashan* refer in a general sense to the quality of the grain: old, dried grain is better, much as old wine is considered superior, while fresher grain has not yet dried sufficiently. The *metukkan* component — the grain from which *terumot u'ma'asrot* have been taken — is the direct halachic concern.

### Collusion Concern Test

The difficulty with accepting such testimony is obvious: the concern about collusion operates differently here than in the *mishna*'s case. In the *mishna*, the two men are simultaneously before us and each testifies about the other. Here, however, the merchant testifies in one city, and the implicit arrangement is that in the next city his friend will reciprocate, each gaining by the exchange across different locations and different audiences. This presents a particularly stark concern about collusion, and the *baraita* records a dispute: one opinion holds he is *eino ne'eman* — not believed — and *Rabbi Yehuda* holds he is *ne'eman* — believed.

This seems to flatly reverse the positions as we know them: in our *mishna*, *Rabbi Yehuda* was the one who did not believe, while the *Rabbanan* were more lenient. Here, *Rabbi Yehuda* is the lenient one and the unnamed *tanna* takes the stricter view. Everybody's positions appear reversed.

### Reversed Positions

*Rav Adda bar Ahava* says, citing *Rav*: *muchlefet hashita* — the attributions in the *baraita* are switched. *Rabbi Yehuda* should be read as saying *eino ne'eman*, and the *Rabbanan* should be read as saying *ne'eman*, which would be consistent with their positions in the *mishna*.

### Demai & Shvuya

*Abaye* disagrees: *l'olam lo teihafuch* — there is no need to reverse the attributions. The positions can be reconciled if we attend to the specific subject matter of each case. The *baraita* concerns *demai* — grain purchased from an *am ha'aretz* — and *b'demai heikeilu*: the Rabbis were lenient regarding *demai*. The halachic reality is that most *amei ha'aretz* do in fact tithe their grain, and the concern about *demai* is only rabbinic in character. Just as we observed that *shvuya hekilu* — the Rabbis were lenient regarding a captive woman — so too *d'mai hekilu*: *Rabbi Yehuda*, who is strict by *kohen* status because of the weighty ramifications, is prepared to be lenient regarding *demai* because the only thing at stake is a *d'rabbanan* concern, and there the stakes do not justify the same caution.

*Rava* then presses the question: even if *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is coherent in this framework, the *Rabbanan* are now the ones who appear contradictory — lenient toward *kohen* status in the *mishna* and stricter about *demai* in the *baraita*. The *Gemara* responds: *d'Rabbi Yehuda a'd'Rabbi Yehuda lo kashya k'de'shaninan* — *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is consistent for the reason just stated. As for the *Rabbanan*, their apparent inconsistency is resolved by invoking the principle that *Rabbi Chama bar Ukva* stated: *b'khlei uminuto b'yado* — when the instruments of his profession are in his hand.

## Klei Umanto Status

### Potter's Vessels Case

The *Gemara* traces the origin of the principle of *khlei uminuto b'yado* to a case involving a potter who leaves his jugs and pitchers unattended while he goes to drink water. The question is whether an *am ha'aretz* may have touched them and rendered them *tamei*. There are three *baraitot* on this question, and *Rabbi Chama bar Ukva*'s statement is the key to reconciling them.

### Store Open/Closed Status

The first *baraita* holds that the innermost vessels — those not exposed to the passing public — are *tahor*, while the outermost ones are *tamei*. A second *baraita* holds that everything, even the innermost vessels, is *tamei*. A third *baraita* holds the opposite: everything is *tahor*, including the outermost ones. *Rabbi Chama bar Ukva* resolves the contradiction between the first two *baraitot*: when *khlei uminuto b'yado* — when the store is open for business and his instruments are visibly deployed — then *mipnei she'yad hakol memash'mesh bahen*, since everyone will be handling and inspecting the wares, even the innermost vessels are *tamei*, yielding the second *baraita*. When the store is not open for business, only the outermost vessels that are directly exposed present a concern, yielding the first *baraita*. When the instruments of the trade are entirely absent and the setup gives no indication of a store open for business, everything remains *tahor*, yielding the third *baraita*.

### Edge Alignment Resolution

This still leaves a question: what is the case of the first *baraita*, where the outer vessels are *tamei* and the inner ones are *tahor*? If the store is open, everything is *tamei*; if the store is closed, everything is *tahor*. *D'smicha l'reshut harabbim* — the answer is a case where the store is not yet open for business, but the vessels are arranged right at the edge of the public domain. Even without the full display of open commerce, the outermost vessels are pressed up against the *reshut harabbim*, and *u'mishum chifufei reshut harabbim* — because of the incidental rubbing of passersby in the public domain — people will inadvertently touch them even without intending to inspect them. The inner vessels, shielded from this incidental contact, remain *tahor*.

The application to the *baraita* about the donkey drivers follows: when *khlei uminuto b'yado*, when the merchant has his scales and commercial equipment visibly deployed, it is apparent that he is preparing to sell his grain in the next city. In that circumstance, the concern about reciprocal collusion is heightened and credibility is withheld. *Tosafot* reads the application of *khlei uminuto b'yado* in the opposite direction — as explaining why we do believe the *kohen* in the *mishna*'s case: the *kohen* carries the vessels and apparatus associated with *kehuna* practice, which independently serves as circumstantial evidence of his status.

## Terumah to Yichus Transl…

### Elevation Question

The *Gemara* returns to the central issue raised toward the end of the discussion of the *kohen* case: *ma'alin mitrumah leyuchsin* — whether eating *terumah* halachically translates into establishing *yichus*. The underlying point is that *yichus* carries the highest evidentiary standard. If eating *terumah* is halachically treated as establishing full *kohen* status for *yichus* purposes as well, then one cannot allow *ed echad* to permit *terumah* while maintaining a stricter standard for *yichus*, because the two have been collapsed into a single status. Conversely, if we hold *ein ma'alin mitrumah leyuchsin* — that eating *terumah* does not halachically translate into *yichus* — the two standards remain distinct and can be applied independently.

### Document Evidence

A related *iba'ia* is now posed: *mahu le'haalos mishtaros leyuchsin* — can a document establish *yichus* status? The *Gemara* first considers a case where the person himself is identified as a *kohen* among the signatories: *ani ploni Kohen chasamti eid*. This is rejected immediately — *man kamsahid lei* — he is merely testifying about himself and his self-identification carries no evidentiary weight for *yichus* purposes.

The *Gemara* therefore reformulates the case: a loan document in which the borrower is identified as *ploni Kohen* — *ani ploni Kohen lavisi maneh miploni* — and which bears the signatures of witnesses at the bottom. Does the witnesses' signature constitute testimony that all the contents of the document are accurate, including the borrower's identification as a *kohen*? The *Gemara* raises two possibilities. On one hand, *a'maneh shebashtar kamsahadei* — the witnesses are testifying only to the loan itself, not to incidental details of nomenclature; they sign to confirm a transaction took place and should not be understood to be corroborating every description in the document. On the other hand, perhaps *achula milsa kamsahadei* — once witnesses affix their signatures, they are treated as testifying to all aspects of the document's contents.

*Rav Huna* and *Rav Chisda* dispute this: one holds *ma'alin* — the document suffices to establish *yichus*, and one holds *ein ma'alin*. This question has direct practical relevance for tractate Ketubot, where documents such as a *ketuba* may identify a woman as a *gerusha* or *giyores*, carrying implications for whether she may marry a *kohen*. The sensitivity around these identifications has given rise to the practice of using subtle formulations — *matrachta* instead of *besulta*, or *giyorta* rather than *besulta* — so that personal status is indicated without being read aloud.

### Duchaning Evidence

A further *iba'ia* is raised: *mahu le'haalos mi'nesiat kapayim leyuchsin* — if someone performs *duchaning*, may that serve as sufficient evidence to establish *yichus* status? The *Gemara* notes that this question can be asked regardless of one's position on whether *terumah* translates into *yichus*, because *nesiat kapayim* may be distinct from *terumah* in ways that cut in either direction.

### Public vs Private Acts

Even according to the view that eating *terumah* translates into *yichus*, one might argue that *nesiat kapayim* does not. Why? *Hani mili terumah, de'avon mita hi* — eating *terumah* is a capital offence for a non-*kohen* who does so knowingly, and therefore a man would not eat *terumah* unless he genuinely believed himself to be a *kohen*. By contrast, a non-*kohen* who performs *duchaning* transgresses only a positive commandment — *koh sevarchu et bnei Yisrael* is directed only at *kohanim* — and the *issur asei* is considerably lighter. One might therefore conclude that a person would be less deterred from performing *duchaning* improperly, making it a weaker indicator of genuine *kehuna* status.

Conversely, even according to the view that eating *terumah* does not translate into *yichus*, one might argue that *nesiat kapayim* does. The reason is that *terumah de'mitachla b'tzina'* — *terumah* is eaten privately, without public communal recognition. A person might eat *terumah* given to him by a few sympathetic farmers without the broader community acknowledging him as a *kohen*. But *nesiat kapayim defarhesya* — *duchaning* takes place publicly, before the entire congregation. *Ilav Kohen hu, kulei hai lo machtzif inish lenafshai* — if a person were not truly a *kohen*, he would not have the *chutzpa* to ascend the *duchan* before the entire community and pronounce the *birkat kohanim*. The public nature of the act, combined with the community's implicit acquiescence, gives it greater evidentiary weight than the private eating of *terumah*. *Rav Chisda* and *Rav Avina* dispute this question as well, one holding *ma'alin* and the other holding *ein ma'alin*.

## Kohanim Return & Chazaka

### Exile Return Case

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* posed the question of *nesiat kapayim* directly to *Rava*: *mahu le'haalos mi'nesiat kapayim leyuchsin*? *Rava* replied that it is the subject of the dispute between *Rav Chisda* and *Rav Avina*. *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* pressed: what is the practical *halacha*? *Rava* responded that what he knew was a *baraita*.

*Rabbi Yosei* says: *gedola chazaka* — the principle of *chazaka* is powerful, as demonstrated by the following *pasuk* from *Ezra*. The *pasuk* describes *kohanim* who returned from exile and who could not produce genealogical documentation: *u'mibnei hakohanim bnei Chavaya ben Hakotz bnei Barzillai* — this family had intermarried into the non-priestly family of *Barzillai HaGiladi*, taking their name, and when they sought their genealogical records — *eileh bikshu kesavam hamityachasim* — they could not be found. *Vayigalu min hakehuna* — they were removed from the *kehuna*.

### Nechemia's Leniency

*HaTirshatha* — identified by *Rashi* as *Nechemia* — then addressed them: *harei atem b'chezkatchem*, you retain your presumptive status. He asked: what were you eating in exile? *B'kodshei gvul* — *terumah*, the *kodshim* eaten outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. Very well, *af kan b'kodshei gvul* — you may continue eating *terumah*. But *asher lo yochlu mikodesh hakodashim* — they may not eat from the *korbanot*, and this restriction remains *ad amod kohen l'urim v'tumim* — until *mashiach* comes, since there was no *urim v'tumim* in the second *Beit HaMikdash* to verify status with certainty. The principle being illustrated is *gedola chazaka*: the fact that they had been eating *terumah* in exile serves as a *chazaka* that allows them to continue doing so, despite their inability to produce documentation.

### Chazaka Prevents Escalat…

The *Gemara* then asks: if we hold *ma'alin mi'nesiat kapayim leyuchsin*, and these *kohanim* were presumably also performing *duchaning* in exile as part of their established practice, then by allowing them to continue their practice upon their return, we would be allowing them to *duchan* — and through *duchaning* they would be elevated to full *yichus* status, thereby allowing them to work in the *Beit HaMikdash*. But that is precisely what *Nechemia* was trying to prevent. The very structure of *Nechemia*'s ruling — allowing *terumah* but withholding full *kehuna* recognition — therefore implies that *duchaning* does not translate into *yichus*. Or does it?

### Terumah Levels Distincti…

The *Gemara* responds: *shani hacha d'raiah chezkaihu* — the case of *bnei Barzillai* is different, because their status had been openly stigmatized and thrown into question. Since everyone knew their status was doubtful — and indeed the *pasuk* says explicitly that they were excluded from the *kehuna* — no one would translate their *duchaning* into full *yichus*, because the stigma was communally recognized. This does not prove that in ordinary circumstances *duchaning* cannot translate into *yichus*.

The *Gemara* then notes that the same question applies to the view that eating *terumah* translates into *yichus*: if *Nechemia* allowed these *kohanim* to continue eating *terumah*, why would that not suffice to restore them to full *yichus* status and allow them to work in the *Beit HaMikdash*? The same answer applies: *d'raiah chezkaihu* — their status was publicly known to be in question, and therefore no translation from *terumah* to *yichus* would be inferred.

### No Gezeirah Applied

This raises a further question: if *gedola chazaka* allows these *kohanim* to continue eating *terumah*, what precisely is the *chiddush*? The *Gemara* answers: *meikara achol bitrumah d'rabbanan v'hasha achol bitrumah d'oraita* — in exile they had been eating only *terumah d'rabbanan*, since *terumah* outside *Eretz Yisrael* is only of rabbinic force. The *chiddush* of *gedola chazaka* is that upon their return to *Eretz Yisrael*, we allow them to continue eating *terumah*, even though the *terumah* there is *d'oraita*.

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative resolution: *hacha nami bitrumah d'rabbanan achol* — even in *Eretz Yisrael* we only allow them to eat *terumah d'rabbanan*, such as *terumah* of fruits, which is rabbinic in character. *Bitrumah d'oraita lo achol* — biblical *terumah* of grain, oil, and wine is not permitted to them. And the corollary for the broader debate: *ki maskinu mitrumah leyuchsin, bitrumah d'oraita* — the principle of translation from *terumah* to *yichus* operates only when the *terumah* in question is *d'oraita*; *bitrumah d'rabbanan lo maskinan* — rabbinic *terumah* does not trigger the elevation to *yichus* status.

If that is so, the *chiddush* of *gedola chazaka* here is not that we permit *terumah d'oraita*, but that we permit rabbinic *terumah* despite the possibility that this might, through some chain of reasoning, eventually lead to eating biblical *terumah*. One might have argued that we should apply a *gezeirah* — prohibiting even rabbinic *terumah* lest it lead to biblical *terumah*. *De'af al gav d'ika lemigzar mishum terumah d'oraita, lo gazrinan* — nevertheless, no such *gezeirah* is made. The power of *chazaka* is that it overrides even a preventive decree that might otherwise have been reasonable. The discussion continues on 25a.
