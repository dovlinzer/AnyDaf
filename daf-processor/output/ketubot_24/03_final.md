# Ketubot 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Captive Women & Single Witness

### Captive Women Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁנִּשְׁבּוּ, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת ״נִשְׁבֵּיתִי וּטְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״, וְזֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת ״נִשְׁבֵּיתִי וּטְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״ — אֵינָן נֶאֱמָנוֹת. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מְעִידוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נֶאֱמָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where witnesses testify that there are **two women who were taken captive,** and **this** woman **says: I was taken captive but I am pure, and that** woman **says: I was taken captive but I am pure, they are not deemed credible. And when this** woman **testifies about that** woman that she is pure and vice versa, **they are deemed credible.**

The daf opens by picking up from the *mishna* on the bottom of 23b, which addressed two women who had been taken captive. The fundamental *chiddush* established there is that although each woman is not believed to testify about her own status — if witnesses already established that she was taken captive — she is believed to testify about her fellow captive. This holds even where there exists a possible concern of reciprocity, a concern the *Gemara* will make explicit in the course of today's discussion.

### Single Witness Despite Reciprocity

The *mishna* also establishes a broader principle regarding *ed echad*: even a single witness is believed in matters relating to captivity. Moreover, the *Gemara* indicated that even in a case of one witness against one — where one *ed* says she was violated and another says she was not — we follow the witness who says she was not violated, even if that witness would normally occupy a weaker evidentiary position under standard *halacha*, as in the case where the exonerating witness is a woman testifying against a man's claim. This represents a particular leniency, parallel to principles encountered in Yevamos, in extending credibility to the *ed* who exonerates. Further implications of this principle will emerge as the daf proceeds.

## Kohanim Witness Dispute

### Kohanim Testifying Mutual Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְכֵן שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים, זֶה אוֹמֵר ״כֹּהֵן אֲנִי״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר ״כֹּהֵן אֲנִי״ — אֵינָן נֶאֱמָנִין. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מְעִידִין זֶה אֶת זֶה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ נֶאֱמָנִין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַעֲלִין לִכְהוּנָּה עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵימָתַי — בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ עוֹרְרִין. אֲבָל בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין עוֹרְרִין — מַעֲלִין לִכְהוּנָּה עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן הַסְּגָן: מַעֲלִין לִכְהוּנָּה עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And likewise,** with regard to **two men** whose lineage is unknown, and **this** man **says: I am a priest, and that** man **says: I am a priest, they are not deemed credible. And when this** man **testifies about that** man that he is a priest and vice versa, **they are deemed credible. Rabbi Yehuda says: One does not elevate** a man **to priesthood on the basis of one witness.** Two witnesses are required for that purpose. **Rabbi Elazar says: When** is that the ruling? In a case **where there are challengers** to his claim that he is a priest. **However,** in a case **where there are no challengers, one elevates** a man **to priesthood on the basis of one witness. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says in the name of Rabbi Shimon, son of the deputy** High Priest: **One elevates** a man **to priesthood on the basis of one witness.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which presents a parallel case in a different domain. Two men each claim to be a *kohen* — *zeh omer kohen ani v'zeh omer kohen ani*. Standing alone, neither man is believed about himself in the absence of an established *chazaka* that he is a *kohen*, and therefore neither would be given *terumah* at the threshing floor. However, when each testifies about the other — each saying his fellow is a *kohen* — they are believed, and *terumah* is given to both. This is the *chiddush* of the *mishna* on two levels: first, that *ed echad* suffices to establish *kehuna* for purposes of *terumah*; second, that this is accepted even where there is a concern about collusion or reciprocity between the two.

### Rabbi Yehuda Rejects Single Witness

*Rabbi Yehuda* dissents: *ein ma'alin l'kehunah al pi ed echad* — *kehuna* status may not be established on the basis of a single witness. This is a categorical position that will be examined against what appears to be a contradictory ruling of his elsewhere.

### Rabbi Eliezer Conditional Position

*Rabbi Eliezer* qualifies: when is it true that a single witness is insufficient? Only *bimkom she'yesh orerim* — where a concrete challenge has been raised against the person's *kehuna* status. But where there is no such concrete challenge, *ma'alin l'kehunah al pi ed echad* — one witness suffices to establish *kehuna*. The *Gemara* will later note that *Rabbi Eliezer* may also decline to accept the specific case of the *tanna kamma* — the case where two men testify about one another and reciprocity is a concern — and may only accept *ed echad* in the standard, uncontested case.

### Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's Position

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says, citing *Rabbi Shimon ben HaSgan*: *ma'alin l'kehunah al pi ed echad* — a single witness suffices to establish *kehuna* status. The difficulty is that his position is not immediately distinguishable from the preceding opinions. The *tanna kamma* permits even the case of mutual testimony despite the concern of collusion; *Rabbi Eliezer* permits *ed echad* where there is no concrete challenge; *Rabbi Yehuda* forbids *ed echad* entirely; and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* restates the permissive view without specifying how he diverges from either camp. The *Gemara* will work through these distinctions in the course of the analysis that follows.

## Peh She'Asar Analysis

### Multiple Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כֹּל הָנֵי לְמָה לִי! צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי תְּנָא מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא דְּרָרָא דְמָמוֹנָא. אֲבָל עֵדִים, דְּלֵיכָּא דְּרָרָא דְמָמוֹנָא — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **all these** different cases cited in the *mishnayot* in this chapter? Aren't they all based on the principle: The mouth that prohibited is the mouth that permitted? The Gemara answers: These cases are all **necessary, as, if** the *tanna* had **taught** only the case where **Rabbi Yehoshua concedes, in** a case where one says to another: This field, which is currently in my possession, belonged to your father, and I purchased it from him, then one might have thought that his claim is deemed credible **due to** the fact **that there is financial significance [*derara*]** in his contention that it belonged to the other's father, and he would not have made that claim if it were not true. However, in the case of witnesses authenticating their signatures, **where there is no financial significance** for them in their testimony, **say no,** their claim is not accepted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא עֵדִים, מִשּׁוּם דִּלְעָלְמָא, אֲבָל אִיהוּ, דִּלְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, מִשּׁוּם דְּמָמוֹנָא, אֲבָל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, דְּאִיסּוּרָא — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the *tanna* **taugh**t only the case of **witnesses,** one might have thought that their claim is deemed credible **due to** the fact **that** their testimony is relevant **to others. However,** with regard to **him, whose** testimony is relevant **to himself,** as he claims that he purchased the field from the other's father, say no, his claim is not accepted. **And if** the *tanna* **taught us these two** cases, one might have thought that the claim is deemed credible **due to** the fact **that** the cases involve **monetary** matters; **however,** in the case of **a married woman** who claims that she was divorced, **which is a ritual** matter, **say no,** she is **not** deemed credible. Therefore, it was necessary for the *tanna* to teach us all three cases.

The *Gemara* asks: *kol hanei lama li* — why are all of these *mishnayyot* necessary? The reference is to the series of cases across the preceding *mishnayyot* involving *ha'peh she'asar hu ha'peh she'hitir*, as well as the subsequent *mishnayyot* about *ne'emanut* without a *peh she'asar* component — namely, trusting *ed echad* regarding *shvuya* and trusting *ed echad* regarding *kohen* status. The *Gemara* proceeds to explain why each case is independently necessary.

The first case in the *perek* — where *Rabbi Yehoshua* agrees that the man who says "this field was your father's and I purchased it from you" is believed — is accepted because *de'ika drara demamona*, there is a *drara demamona* present. The *Gemara* relies on this concept to explain the conditions under which *peh she'asar* operates, and it is precisely around this term that a significant dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* emerges.

### Drara Definition Debate

*Rashi* explains *drara demamona* as meaning loss of money — *hefsed mamon*. The significance for *Rashi* is that because the first half of the man's statement would cause him to lose the field if left unqualified, it is manifest that the second part of the statement — "but I purchased it from him" — is intended as part of the same continuous utterance. This bears on the implicit requirement, never stated explicitly by the *Gemara* but understood by the *Rishonim*, that the two halves of the *peh she'asar* statement must constitute a single statement, perhaps even *toch k'dei dibbur* of one another. When money is at stake, *Rashi* argues, it is clear that the qualifying clause is organically linked to the initial admission, eliminating any concern that the admission was made at one time and the qualification asserted separately later.

*Tosafot* understands *drara demamona* differently: it means a *shaychus l'mamon*, a connection to money, rather than an actual loss. The simpler point, in *Tosafot*'s reading, is that the man is already in possession of the field, and it is therefore more readily understandable that we would apply *peh she'asar* — he is already physically present and connected to the subject matter of his claim. The *Rosh* also notes that *Rashi* seems to read *demuchzak basadeh* into the term. In any case, the dispute between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* revolves around whether the operative concept is *hefsed mamon* or *shaychus mamon*. The *Gemara* does not resolve this explicitly, and it has remained a subject of debate among the *Rishonim*.

### Captivity Case Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״נִשְׁבֵּיתִי וּטְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״ לְמָה לִי? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי: ״וְאִם מִשֶּׁנִּשֵּׂאת בָּאוּ עֵדִים — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תֵּצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the case where one says: **I was taken captive and I am pure, why do I need** the *tanna* to teach that case? There is no novel element in that ruling, as it is merely another application of the same principle. The Gemara answers: The *tanna* taught that case **due to** the fact **that** the *tanna* **sought to teach** based on it: **And if the witnesses came after she married, this** woman need **not leave** her husband.

The *Gemara* then explains why the case of *edim* — witnesses who say "yes, we signed this document, but we were *ketanim*" or "*anusim*" or "*krovim*" — was needed alongside the case of the field. In the *edim* case there is no *drara demamona* in *Rashi*'s sense: the money has not changed hands, and the witnesses themselves are not the ones at risk of losing anything. One might have thought that *peh she'asar* would not apply there. Conversely, had only the case of the *edim* been taught, one might have thought that witnesses are believed only because they are helping someone else — the *loveh* — and the second half of their statement is therefore more readily credited. But in the case of the man claiming to have purchased the field, the second part of his statement benefits himself — *le'nafshei* — and one might have argued that *peh she'asar* should not apply there. Hence both cases are needed.

It is also worth noting, as *Tosafot* points out, that the case of the *edim* carried its own independent *chiddushim*: once their statement triggered the validity of the *shtar* in the first half, one might argue they cannot then be believed to undermine the *shtar* with the second half of their statement. Both elements — the field case and the *edim* case — were therefore required.

The *Gemara* further explains: having established *peh she'asar* in monetary contexts, one might still have wondered whether it would apply in a case involving *issur* — such as a woman who says "I was married and then divorced." The weight of that *issur ve'hetter* is more severe, and one might say we would not accept the second half of the statement in so grave a matter. Thus the *mishna* about the divorced woman was needed as well.

## Captivity Case Purpose

### Gomlin Concern Address

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁנִּשְׁבּוּ״ לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא נֵיחוּשׁ לְגוֹמְלִין, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need the *tanna* to teach the case of **two women who were taken captive?** What novel element is introduced in that case that did not exist in the case of one woman? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say: Let us be concerned for collusion** between the women, that each would testify for the benefit of the other, the *tanna* therefore **teaches us** that this is not a concern.

The *Gemara* then asks: why was the case of *shtei nashim she'nishbu* — two captive women testifying about one another — necessary? The *mishna* about captivity had already appeared in the context of *peh she'asar*. The *Gemara* answers that the unique *chiddush* of the two-women case is the ruling that we are not concerned about *gomlin* — reciprocity. The concern would be that they have colluded: I will say you were not violated if you say the same of me. The *mishna* teaches *ka mashma lan* that we do not suspect *gomlin* in this context, which is a *chiddush* beyond the mere principle of believing *ed echad* or a woman's testimony in captivity matters.

### Kohanim Case Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכֵן שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים״ לְמָה לִי? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need the *tanna* to teach the following case: **And likewise two men,** each testifying that the other is a priest? He already taught that if two women each testify that the other is pure, they are deemed credible. The Gemara answers: The *tanna* taught the superfluous *halakha* with regard to two men as an introduction, **due to** the fact **that he sought to** subsequently **teach the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis,** concerning whether or not the testimony of a single witness is deemed credible to establish another's presumptive status as a priest.

Why was the case of two men each claiming *kehuna* needed? The *Gemara* answers: *mishum d'kaba'i le'misni plugta de-Rabbi Yehuda ve-Rabbanan* — because the *Gemara* wanted to record the dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Rabbanan*. Even if one accepted that *ed echad* suffices for *shvuya*, it does not automatically follow that *ed echad* suffices for *kehuna* status. *Rabbi Yehuda* is stricter by *kohen* than by *shvuya*, and this divergence requires explanation.

### Kehuna Special Status

Two reasons are offered for why *kehuna* may warrant greater scrutiny than *shvuya*. First, by *shvuya* the principle of *shvuya hekilu* applies — the Rabbis were specially lenient regarding a captive woman, and *Rabbi Yehuda* himself accepts that leniency. The case of *kehuna* does not benefit from any such blanket rabbinic dispensation. Second, and more consequentially, allowing a man to eat *terumah* has downstream effects. People will come to treat him as a fully established *kohen*, and that status will be translated into other domains: into *yuchsin* considerations, into questions about whom he may marry, and potentially into his eligibility to serve in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *kehuna* context therefore carries a kind of ripple effect that makes it qualitatively different from the *shvuya* context, and this justifies greater caution.

## Donkey Drivers Baraita

### Grain Quality Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲנִי כֹּהֵן וַחֲבֵרִי כֹּהֵן״ — נֶאֱמָן לְהַאֲכִילוֹ בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן לְהַשִּׂיאוֹ אִשָּׁה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה: שְׁנַיִם מְעִידִין עַל זֶה, וּשְׁנַיִם מְעִידִין עַל זֶה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן לְהַאֲכִילוֹ בִּתְרוּמָה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה, שְׁנַיִם מְעִידִין עַל זֶה וּשְׁנַיִם מְעִידִין עַל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara elaborates: **The Rabbis taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of two men, each of whom says: **I am a priest and my counterpart is a priest,** each **is deemed credible** with regard **to enabling his** counterpart **to partake of *teruma*. But he is not deemed credible** with regard to establishing his presumptive status as a priest of unflawed lineage **for** the purpose of **his marrying a woman until there are three** people, the two claiming to be priests and an additional witness, so **that** there are **two** witnesses **testifying** with regard **to** the status of **this** person **and two** witnesses **testifying** with regard **to** the status of **that** person. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Each **is not deemed credible even** with regard **to enabling his** counterpart **to partake of *teruma* until there are three** men, so **that** there are **two** witnesses **testifying** with regard **to this** person **and two** witnesses **testifying** with regard **to that** person.

The *Gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that appears to present a striking reversal of the positions of *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Rabbanan*. The case involves *chamarim* — merchants who travel from city to city with their goods loaded on donkeys — who enter a town. One of them declares: "My grain is *chadash* and my friend's is *yashan*; my grain has not had *terumot u'ma'asrot* taken, while my friend's has." In other words, he testifies entirely in his friend's favor on all relevant counts: the friend's grain is of better quality and is halachically prepared for use.

On the question of *chadash* and *yashan*, both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* conclude that the reference is not to the halachic *issur* of eating *chadash* grain before the *omer* — because the *Gemara* will establish shortly that we are dealing with *d'rabbanan* concerns here. Rather, *chadash* and *yashan* refer in a general sense to the quality of the grain: old, dried grain is better, much as old wine is considered superior, while fresher grain has not yet dried sufficiently. The *metukkan* component — the grain from which *terumot u'ma'asrot* have been taken — is the direct halachic concern.

### Collusion Concern Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה חָיֵישׁ לְגוֹמְלִין, וְרַבָּנַן לָא חָיְשִׁי לְגוֹמְלִין? וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ, דִּתְנַן: הַחַמָּרִין שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לָעִיר, וְאָמַר אֶחָד מֵהֶן: שֶׁלִּי חָדָשׁ, וְשֶׁל חֲבֵרִי יָשָׁן: שֶׁלִּי אֵינוֹ מְתוּקָּן, וְשֶׁל חֲבֵרִי מְתוּקָּן — אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that Rabbi Yehuda is concerned for collusion** between them, **and the Rabbis are not concerned for collusion? But didn't we learn** that **they** said the **opposite? As we learned** in a mishna: In a case where there were **donkey drivers who entered a city, and one of them said: My** produce **is new** from this year's crop, and it is not yet completely dry and therefore of lower quality, **and the** produce **of my counterpart is old** and dry and therefore more durable; or if he said: **My** produce **is not tithed and the** produce **of my counterpart is tithed, he is not deemed credible.** Presumably, there is collusion between the two merchants. In this city, one denigrates the quality of his own produce, enhancing his credibility, while praising the quality of the produce of his counterpart; and his counterpart says the same in the next city that they enter. **And Rabbi Yehuda says: He is deemed credible,** as apparently he is not concerned for collusion between the merchants.

The difficulty with accepting such testimony is obvious: the concern about collusion operates differently here than in the *mishna*'s case. In the *mishna*, the two men are simultaneously before us and each testifies about the other. Here, however, the merchant testifies in one city, and the implicit arrangement is that in the next city his friend will reciprocate, each gaining by the exchange across different locations and different audiences. This presents a particularly stark concern about collusion, and the *baraita* records a dispute: one opinion holds he is *eino ne'eman* — not believed — and *Rabbi Yehuda* holds he is *ne'eman* — believed.

This seems to flatly reverse the positions as we know them: in our *mishna*, *Rabbi Yehuda* was the one who did not believe, while the *Rabbanan* were more lenient. Here, *Rabbi Yehuda* is the lenient one and the unnamed *tanna* takes the stricter view. Everybody's positions appear reversed.

### Reversed Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר רַב: מוּחְלֶפֶת הַשִּׁיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that **Rav said:** The attribution of **the opinions is reversed** in one of the *mishnayot*, so that the opinions of the *tanna'im* are consistent in both the case of the priests and the case of the donkey drivers.

*Rav Adda bar Ahava* says, citing *Rav*: *muchlefet hashita* — the attributions in the *baraita* are switched. *Rabbi Yehuda* should be read as saying *eino ne'eman*, and the *Rabbanan* should be read as saying *ne'eman*, which would be consistent with their positions in the *mishna*.

### Demai & Shvuya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ. בִּדְמַאי הֵקֵילּוּ, רוֹב עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ מְעַשְּׂרִין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Actually, do not reverse** the attribution, and the fact that Rabbi Yehuda accepts the claim of the donkey driver is because **with regard to doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*]** the Sages **were lenient,** because **most *amei ha'aretz* tithe** their produce. The ordinance of the Sages classifying produce purchased from an *am ha'aretz* as doubtfully tithed produce and requiring its tithing is based on a far-fetched concern. Therefore, testimony of any sort is sufficient to permit its consumption. However, as a rule, Rabbi Yehuda is concerned about collusion.

*Abaye* disagrees: *l'olam lo teihafuch* — there is no need to reverse the attributions. The positions can be reconciled if we attend to the specific subject matter of each case. The *baraita* concerns *demai* — grain purchased from an *am ha'aretz* — and *b'demai heikeilu*: the Rabbis were lenient regarding *demai*. The halachic reality is that most *amei ha'aretz* do in fact tithe their grain, and the concern about *demai* is only rabbinic in character. Just as we observed that *shvuya hekilu* — the Rabbis were lenient regarding a captive woman — so too *d'mai hekilu*: *Rabbi Yehuda*, who is strict by *kohen* status because of the weighty ramifications, is prepared to be lenient regarding *demai* because the only thing at stake is a *d'rabbanan* concern, and there the stakes do not justify the same caution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קַשְׁיָא, דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן לָא קַשְׁיָא? אֶלָּא: דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא קַשְׁיָא, כִּדְשַׁנֵּינַן. דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן לָא קַשְׁיָא, כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר עוּקְבָא, בְּשֶׁכְּלֵי אוּמָּנוּתוֹ בְּיָדוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Is that to say that the contradiction between one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** and another statement **of Rabbi Yehuda is difficult,** but the contradiction between one statement **of the Rabbis and** another statement **of the Rabbis is not difficult?** Clearly, the contradiction between the rulings of the Rabbis in the respective *mishnayot* is difficult. **Rather,** the contradiction between one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** and another statement **of Rabbi Yehuda is not difficult, as we explained** above that Rabbi Yehuda was lenient with regard to doubtfully tithed produce. The contradiction between one statement **of the Rabbis and** another statement **of the Rabbis is** also **not difficult.** Fundamentally, the Rabbis are not concerned for collusion between the two parties. However, in the case of donkey drivers they are concerned, **as Rabbi Ḥama bar Ukva said** in another context that it is referring to a case **where one has the tools of his trade in his hand.**

*Rava* then presses the question: even if *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is coherent in this framework, the *Rabbanan* are now the ones who appear contradictory — lenient toward *kohen* status in the *mishna* and stricter about *demai* in the *baraita*. The *Gemara* responds: *d'Rabbi Yehuda a'd'Rabbi Yehuda lo kashya k'de'shaninan* — *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is consistent for the reason just stated. As for the *Rabbanan*, their apparent inconsistency is resolved by invoking the principle that *Rabbi Chama bar Ukva* stated: *b'khlei uminuto b'yado* — when the instruments of his profession are in his hand.

## Klei Umanto Status

### Potter's Vessels Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי בְּשֶׁכְּלֵי אוּמָּנוּתוֹ בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** it is referring to a case **where** the second donkey driver comes **with the tools of his trade in his hand,** clearly indicating that he too came to sell his produce. Therefore, when the other driver praises his produce, there is room for concern that there was collusion and that in the next city their roles will be reversed. However, when there is no proof of collusion, the Rabbis were not concerned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא אִתְּמַר דְּרַבִּי חָמָא בַּר עוּקְבָא — אַהָא, דִּתְנַן: הַקַּדָּר שֶׁהִנִּיחַ קְדֵירוֹתָיו וְיָרַד לִשְׁתּוֹת (מַיִם מִן הַיְאוֹר) — הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת טְהוֹרוֹת, וְהַחִיצוֹנוֹת טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where** is the solution **of Rabbi Ḥama bar Ukva stated?** It is stated **concerning that which we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 7:1): In the case of **a potter who** fashioned his vessels in ritual purity, and **abandoned his pots, and descended to drink water from the river,** there is concern that in his absence ritually impure people came into contact with his pots. **The inner** pots **are pure, and the outer** pots **are impure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ טְמֵאוֹת! אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר עוּקְבָא: בְּשֶׁכְּלֵי אוּמָּנוּתוֹ בְּיָדוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיַּד הַכֹּל מְמַשְׁמֶשֶׁת בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Both these** inner pots **and those** outer pots **are ritually impure? Rabbi Ḥama bar Ukva said:** That *baraita* is referring to a case where **the tools of** the potter's **trade are in his hand,** indicating that he intends to sell his pots. **Since all** potential buyers **touch** pots when examining them before purchase, the concern is that among those people there is one who is ritually impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ טְהוֹרוֹת! אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר עוּקְבָא: בְּשֶׁאֵין כְּלֵי אוּמָּנוּתוֹ בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **Both these** inner pots **and those** outer pots **are ritually pure? Rabbi Ḥama bar Ukva said:** That *baraita* is referring to a case where **the tools of** the potter's **trade are not in his hand,** indicating that he does not intend to sell his pots. Therefore, strangers will not touch them at all.

The *Gemara* traces the origin of the principle of *khlei uminuto b'yado* to a case involving a potter who leaves his jugs and pitchers unattended while he goes to drink water. The question is whether an *am ha'aretz* may have touched them and rendered them *tamei*. There are three *baraitot* on this question, and *Rabbi Chama bar Ukva*'s statement is the key to reconciling them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת טְהוֹרוֹת וְהַחִיצוֹנוֹת טְמֵאוֹת, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, the ruling in the mishna: **The inner** pots **are pure and the outer** pots **are impure, under what** circumstances **can** this case **be found?** When the tools of the potter's trade are in his hand, all the vessels are impure, and when the tools of the potter's trade are not in his hand, all the vessels are pure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּסְמִיכָא לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּמִשּׁוּם חִיפּוּפֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *tanna* of the mishna is referring to a case **where** the tools of the potter's trade are not in his hand. However, he leaves his pots **adjacent to the public domain, and** in an area demarcated from the public domain **due to the stakes** or other objects that are placed on the sides of **the public domain** to distance passersby from the walls of the private domain, and due to crowding, people will be pushed to the sides and inadvertently render the outer pots impure. They do not pass close to the inner pots and will not touch the pots to examine them, because they are not for sale.

### Edge Alignment Resolution

This still leaves a question: what is the case of the first *baraita*, where the outer vessels are *tamei* and the inner ones are *tahor*? If the store is open, everything is *tamei*; if the store is closed, everything is *tahor*. *D'smicha l'reshut harabbim* — the answer is a case where the store is not yet open for business, but the vessels are arranged right at the edge of the public domain. Even without the full display of open commerce, the outermost vessels are pressed up against the *reshut harabbim*, and *u'mishum chifufei reshut harabbim* — because of the incidental rubbing of passersby in the public domain — people will inadvertently touch them even without intending to inspect them. The inner vessels, shielded from this incidental contact, remain *tahor*.

The application to the *baraita* about the donkey drivers follows: when *khlei uminuto b'yado*, when the merchant has his scales and commercial equipment visibly deployed, it is apparent that he is preparing to sell his grain in the next city. In that circumstance, the concern about reciprocal collusion is heightened and credibility is withheld. *Tosafot* reads the application of *khlei uminuto b'yado* in the opposite direction — as explaining why we do believe the *kohen* in the *mishna*'s case: the *kohen* carries the vessels and apparatus associated with *kehuna* practice, which independently serves as circumstantial evidence of his status.

## Terumah to Yichus Translation

### Elevation Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן בְּמַעֲלִין מִתְּרוּמָה לְיוּחֲסִין קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to cite an additional resolution to the contradiction between the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, with regard to collusion in terms of the presumptive status of priests and in terms of produce merchants. **And if you wish, say** instead that the dispute between **Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis** in the matter of presumptive priestly status is unrelated to collusion. Rather, it is **with regard to** whether **one elevates** one who eats ***teruma* to** the presumptive status of priesthood **for** the purpose of **lineage** that **they disagree.** Rabbi Yehuda holds that one elevates from *teruma* to lineage and therefore requires full-fledged testimony by two witnesses to enable the person to partake of *teruma*. The Rabbis maintain that one does not elevate from *teruma* to lineage, and each matter is considered separately. For the purpose of partaking of *teruma*, any testimony is sufficient; for the purpose of lineage, full-fledged testimony by two witnesses is required.

The *Gemara* returns to the central issue raised toward the end of the discussion of the *kohen* case: *ma'alin mitrumah leyuchsin* — whether eating *terumah* halachically translates into establishing *yichus*. The underlying point is that *yichus* carries the highest evidentiary standard. If eating *terumah* is halachically treated as establishing full *kohen* status for *yichus* purposes as well, then one cannot allow *ed echad* to permit *terumah* while maintaining a stricter standard for *yichus*, because the two have been collapsed into a single status. Conversely, if we hold *ein ma'alin mitrumah leyuchsin* — that eating *terumah* does not halachically translate into *yichus* — the two standards remain distinct and can be applied independently.

### Document Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לְהַעֲלוֹת מִשְּׁטָרוֹת לְיוּחֲסִין? הֵיכִי דָּמֵי: אִילֵּימָא דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״אֲנִי פְּלוֹנִי כֹּהֵן חָתַמְתִּי עֵד״ — מַאן קָא מַסְהֵיד עִילָּוֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard **to elevating from documents** indicating that one is a priest, **to** priestly **lineage?** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If you say that it is written in** the document: **I, so-and-so, a priest, signed** as **a witness,** in that case **who is testifying about him** that he is a priest? He is the only source asserting his priesthood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״אֲנִי פְּלוֹנִי כֹּהֵן לָוִיתִי מָנֶה מִפְּלוֹנִי״ וַחֲתִימוּ סָהֲדִי, מַאי? אַמָּנֶה שֶׁבַּשְּׁטָר קָא מַסְהֲדִי, אוֹ דִלְמָא אַכּוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא קָא מַסְהֲדִי. רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא, חַד אָמַר: מַעֲלִין, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין מַעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** this *halakha* is **necessary** in a case **where it is written in** the document: **I, so-and-so, a priest, borrowed one hundred dinars from so-and-so, and witnesses signed** the document, **what** is the *halakha*? **Do** the witnesses **testify** only **concerning the** loan of **one hundred dinars in the document? Or, perhaps they testify concerning the entire matter** and confirm with their signatures that every detail written in the document is true, including the fact that the borrower is a priest. With regard to the halakhic ruling, there is a dispute between **Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda. One said: One elevates** from documents to priestly lineage, **and one said: One does not elevate.**

A related *iba'ia* is now posed: *mahu le'haalos mishtaros leyuchsin* — can a document establish *yichus* status? The *Gemara* first considers a case where the person himself is identified as a *kohen* among the signatories: *ani ploni Kohen chasamti eid*. This is rejected immediately — *man kamsahid lei* — he is merely testifying about himself and his self-identification carries no evidentiary weight for *yichus* purposes.

The *Gemara* therefore reformulates the case: a loan document in which the borrower is identified as *ploni Kohen* — *ani ploni Kohen lavisi maneh miploni* — and which bears the signatures of witnesses at the bottom. Does the witnesses' signature constitute testimony that all the contents of the document are accurate, including the borrower's identification as a *kohen*? The *Gemara* raises two possibilities. On one hand, *a'maneh shebashtar kamsahadei* — the witnesses are testifying only to the loan itself, not to incidental details of nomenclature; they sign to confirm a transaction took place and should not be understood to be corroborating every description in the document. On the other hand, perhaps *achula milsa kamsahadei* — once witnesses affix their signatures, they are treated as testifying to all aspects of the document's contents.

*Rav Huna* and *Rav Chisda* dispute this: one holds *ma'alin* — the document suffices to establish *yichus*, and one holds *ein ma'alin*. This question has direct practical relevance for tractate Ketubot, where documents such as a *ketuba* may identify a woman as a *gerusha* or *giyores*, carrying implications for whether she may marry a *kohen*. The sensitivity around these identifications has given rise to the practice of using subtle formulations — *matrachta* instead of *besulta*, or *giyorta* rather than *besulta* — so that personal status is indicated without being read aloud.

### Duchaning Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לְהַעֲלוֹת מִנְּשִׂיאוּת כַּפַּיִם לְיוּחֲסִין? תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מַעֲלִין מִתְּרוּמָה לְיוּחֲסִין, וְתִיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מַעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* with regard **to elevating from the lifting of hands** for the Priestly Benediction **to** priestly **lineage?** Is the presumptive status of a person who recites the Priestly Benediction in the synagogue that of a priest in terms of lineage as well? The Gemara notes: **Raise the dilemma** according **to the one who said: One elevates from *teruma* to lineage; and raise the dilemma** according **to the one who said: One does not elevate** from *teruma* to lineage.

A further *iba'ia* is raised: *mahu le'haalos mi'nesiat kapayim leyuchsin* — if someone performs *duchaning*, may that serve as sufficient evidence to establish *yichus* status? The *Gemara* notes that this question can be asked regardless of one's position on whether *terumah* translates into *yichus*, because *nesiat kapayim* may be distinct from *terumah* in ways that cut in either direction.

### Public vs Private Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מַעֲלִין: הָנֵי מִילֵּי תְּרוּמָה, דַּעֲוֹן מִיתָה הִיא. אֲבָל נְשִׂיאוּת כַּפַּיִם, דְּאִיסּוּר עֲשֵׂה — לָא. אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Raise the dilemma** according **to the one who said: One elevates** from *teruma* to lineage. In his opinion, perhaps **this applies only** to ***teruma*, where** a non-priest who partakes of *teruma* performs **a transgression** whose punishment **is death** at the hand of Heaven. One would not partake of *teruma* and risk that punishment if he were not a priest. **However,** with regard to **the Priestly Benediction,** where a non-priest who recites it violates **a prohibition** stated as **a positive mitzva, no,** one cannot be certain that one would not recite the blessing if he were not a priest. Therefore, one does not elevate from the lifting of hands to priestly lineage. **Or, perhaps** there is **no difference,** and in both cases because there is a transgression involved one can assume that he would not risk performing a transgression were he not a priest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מַעֲלִין: הָנֵי מִילֵּי תְּרוּמָה דְּמִיתְאַכְלָא בְּצִנְעָא, אֲבָל נְשִׂיאוּת כַּפַּיִם, דִּבְפַרְהֶסְיָא — אִי לָאו כֹּהֵן הוּא, כּוּלֵּי הַאי לָא מַחְצִיף אִינָשׁ נַפְשֵׁיהּ. אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Raise the dilemma** according **to the one who said: One does not elevate** from *teruma* to lineage. In his opinion, perhaps **this applies only** to ***teruma* that is eaten in private.** Therefore, a non-priest might partake of *teruma* when he believes that no one is watching. **However,** with regard to **the lifting of hands, which is** recited **in public [*parhesya*], if one is not a priest, he would not be insolent to** the **extent that** he would comport himself like a priest in public. Therefore, although partaking of *teruma* is not a clear indicator that he is a priest, reciting the Priestly Benediction is a clear indicator. **Or perhaps,** there is **no difference,** and due to the concern lest a non-priest partake of *teruma* in private and recite the Priestly Benediction in public, neither action can facilitate elevating the person to the presumptive status of a priest in terms of lineage.

Even according to the view that eating *terumah* translates into *yichus*, one might argue that *nesiat kapayim* does not. Why? *Hani mili terumah, de'avon mita hi* — eating *terumah* is a capital offence for a non-*kohen* who does so knowingly, and therefore a man would not eat *terumah* unless he genuinely believed himself to be a *kohen*. By contrast, a non-*kohen* who performs *duchaning* transgresses only a positive commandment — *koh sevarchu et bnei Yisrael* is directed only at *kohanim* — and the *issur asei* is considerably lighter. One might therefore conclude that a person would be less deterred from performing *duchaning* improperly, making it a weaker indicator of genuine *kehuna* status.

Conversely, even according to the view that eating *terumah* does not translate into *yichus*, one might argue that *nesiat kapayim* does. The reason is that *terumah de'mitachla b'tzina'* — *terumah* is eaten privately, without public communal recognition. A person might eat *terumah* given to him by a few sympathetic farmers without the broader community acknowledging him as a *kohen*. But *nesiat kapayim defarhesya* — *duchaning* takes place publicly, before the entire congregation. *Ilav Kohen hu, kulei hai lo machtzif inish lenafshai* — if a person were not truly a *kohen*, he would not have the *chutzpa* to ascend the *duchan* before the entire community and pronounce the *birkat kohanim*. The public nature of the act, combined with the community's implicit acquiescence, gives it greater evidentiary weight than the private eating of *terumah*. *Rav Chisda* and *Rav Avina* dispute this question as well, one holding *ma'alin* and the other holding *ein ma'alin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבִּי אֲבִינָא, חַד אָמַר: מַעֲלִין, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין מַעֲלִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרָבָא: מַהוּ לְהַעֲלוֹת מִנְּשִׂיאוּת כַּפַּיִם לְיוּחֲסִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבִּי אֲבִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** There is a dispute with regard to the halakhic ruling between **Rav Ḥisda and Rabbi Avina. One said: One elevates** from the Priestly Benediction to lineage, **and one said: One does not elevate. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rava: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to elevating from the lifting of hands to lineage? Rava said to him:** There is **a dispute between Rav Ḥisda and Rabbi Avina.**

## Kohanim Return & Chazaka

### Exile Return Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכְתָא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא מַתְנִיתָא יָדַעְנָא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה חֲזָקָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִבְּנֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים בְּנֵי חֳבַיָּה בְּנֵי הַקּוֹץ בְּנֵי בַרְזִילַּי אֲשֶׁר לָקַח מִבְּנוֹת בַּרְזִילַּי הַגִּלְעָדִי אִשָּׁה וַיִּקָּרֵא עַל שְׁמָם. אֵלֶּה בִּקְשׁוּ כְתָבָם הַמִּתְיַחְשִׂים וְלֹא נִמְצָאוּ וַיְגֹאֲלוּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה. וַיֹּאמֶר הַתִּרְשָׁתָא לָהֶם אֲשֶׁר לֹא יֹאכְלוּ מִקֹּדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים עַד עֲמוֹד כֹּהֵן לְאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak asked Rava: **What is the *halakha*?** Rava **said to him: I know** and base the halakhic ruling on **a *baraita*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: Great is** the legal authority of **presumptive status,** as one may rely upon it in determining halakhic practice, **as it is stated: "And of the children of the priests: The children of Habaiah, the children of Hakkoz, the children of Barzillai, who took a wife of the daughters of Barzillai the Gileadite, and was called after their name. These sought their register of the genealogy, but it was not found; therefore were they deemed tainted and put from the priesthood. And the Tirshatha said unto them that they should not partake of the most sacred items, until there stood up a priest with Urim VeTummim"** (Ezra 2:61–63).

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* posed the question of *nesiat kapayim* directly to *Rava*: *mahu le'haalos mi'nesiat kapayim leyuchsin*? *Rava* replied that it is the subject of the dispute between *Rav Chisda* and *Rav Avina*. *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* pressed: what is the practical *halacha*? *Rava* responded that what he knew was a *baraita*.

*Rabbi Yosei* says: *gedola chazaka* — the principle of *chazaka* is powerful, as demonstrated by the following *pasuk* from *Ezra*. The *pasuk* describes *kohanim* who returned from exile and who could not produce genealogical documentation: *u'mibnei hakohanim bnei Chavaya ben Hakotz bnei Barzillai* — this family had intermarried into the non-priestly family of *Barzillai HaGiladi*, taking their name, and when they sought their genealogical records — *eileh bikshu kesavam hamityachasim* — they could not be found. *Vayigalu min hakehuna* — they were removed from the *kehuna*.

### Nechemia's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: הֲרֵי אַתֶּם בְּחֶזְקַתְכֶם. בַּמֶּה הֱיִיתֶם אוֹכְלִים בַּגּוֹלָה — בְּקׇדְשֵׁי הַגְּבוּל, אַף כָּאן בְּקׇדְשֵׁי הַגְּבוּל. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַעֲלִין מִנְּשִׂיאוּת כַּפַּיִם לְיוּחֲסִין, הָנֵי כֵּיוָן דְּפָרְסִי יְדַיְיהוּ — אָתֵי לְאַסּוֹקִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei explains: Nehemiah **said to** the priests whose status was uncertain: **You** maintain **your presumptive status. Of what did you partake in the** Babylonian **exile?** It was **the consecrated items** eaten in **the outlying areas,** i.e., *teruma*. **Here too,** you may continue to partake of the **consecrated items** eaten in **the outlying areas,** but you may not partake of offerings, with regard to which you have no presumptive status of priesthood. The Gemara asks: **And if it enters your mind** to say **that one elevates from the lifting of hands to lineage, they will come to elevate those** priests whose status was uncertain to priestly status in terms of lineage, **since they lifted their hands** and recited the Priestly Benediction in exile.

*HaTirshatha* — identified by *Rashi* as *Nechemia* — then addressed them: *harei atem b'chezkatchem*, you retain your presumptive status. He asked: what were you eating in exile? *B'kodshei gvul* — *terumah*, the *kodshim* eaten outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. Very well, *af kan b'kodshei gvul* — you may continue eating *terumah*. But *asher lo yochlu mikodesh hakodashim* — they may not eat from the *korbanot*, and this restriction remains *ad amod kohen l'urim v'tumim* — until *mashiach* comes, since there was no *urim v'tumim* in the second *Beit HaMikdash* to verify status with certainty. The principle being illustrated is *gedola chazaka*: the fact that they had been eating *terumah* in exile serves as a *chazaka* that allows them to continue doing so, despite their inability to produce documentation.

### Chazaka Prevents Escalation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּרִיעַ חֶזְקַיְיהוּ. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מַעֲלִין מִתְּרוּמָה לְיוּחֲסִין: כֵּיוָן דְּאָכְלִי בִּתְרוּמָה אָתֵי לְאַסּוֹקִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא לָאו, מִשּׁוּם דְּרִיעַ חֶזְקַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here it is different.** There was no concern lest they elevate them to lineage **because their presumptive status was undermined** by the fact that they did not partake of offerings like the other priests. **As, if you do not say that** one relies on the fact that their status was undermined, **according to the one who said: One elevates from *teruma* to lineage, since they partake of *teruma*,** there should be concern lest **they come to elevate them** to lineage. **Rather,** is the reason that this is not a concern **not due to** the fact **their presumptive status was undermined,** and it is clear to all that there is uncertainty with regard to their status as priests? However, one may not infer from the time of Nehemiah to a time when all priests eat *teruma* and recite the Priestly Benediction, and there is no factor that indicates that they are anything less than full-fledged priests. Perhaps, then, one elevates from *teruma* and from the lifting of hands to lineage.

The *Gemara* then asks: if *gedola chazaka* allows these *kohanim* to continue eating *terumah*, why would that not suffice to restore them to full *yichus* status and allow them to work in the *Beit HaMikdash*? The same answer applies: *d'raiah chezkaihu* — their status was publicly known to be in question, and therefore no translation from *terumah* to *yichus* would be inferred.

### No Gezeirah Applied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, מַאי גְּדוֹלָה חֲזָקָה? מֵעִיקָּרָא אֲכוּל בִּתְרוּמָה דְּרַבָּנַן, הַשְׁתָּא אֲכוּל בִּתְרוּמָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what, then,** is the meaning of: **Great is** the legal authority of **presumptive status?** This is a standard case of presumptive status, as the practice of the priests remained as it was. There is nothing novel in the application of the principle of presumptive status in this case. The Gemara answers: **Initially,** in the Babylonian exile, they would **partake of *teruma*** taken from produce obligated **by rabbinic** law. **Now,** upon their return to Eretz Yisrael, **they partake of *teruma*** taken from produce obligated **by Torah** law: Grain, wine, and oil, based on their presumptive status.

This raises a further question: if *gedola chazaka* allows these *kohanim* to continue eating *terumah*, what precisely is the *chiddush*? The *Gemara* answers: *meikara achol bitrumah d'rabbanan v'hasha achol bitrumah d'oraita* — in exile they had been eating only *terumah d'rabbanan*, since *terumah* outside *Eretz Yisrael* is only of rabbinic force. The *chiddush* of *gedola chazaka* is that upon their return to *Eretz Yisrael*, we allow them to continue eating *terumah*, even though the *terumah* there is *d'oraita*.

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative resolution: *hacha nami bitrumah d'rabbanan achol* — even in *Eretz Yisrael* we only allow them to eat *terumah d'rabbanan*, such as *terumah* of fruits, which is rabbinic in character. *Bitrumah d'oraita lo achol* — biblical *terumah* of grain, oil, and wine is not permitted to them. And the corollary for the broader debate: *ki maskinu mitrumah leyuchsin, bitrumah d'oraita* — the principle of translation from *terumah* to *yichus* operates only when the *terumah* in question is *d'oraita*; *bitrumah d'rabbanan lo maskinan* — rabbinic *terumah* does not trigger the elevation to *yichus* status.

If that is so, the *chiddush* of *gedola chazaka* here is not that we permit *terumah d'oraita*, but that we permit rabbinic *terumah* despite the possibility that this might, through some chain of reasoning, eventually lead to eating biblical *terumah*. One might have argued that we should apply a *gezeirah* — prohibiting even rabbinic *terumah* lest it lead to biblical *terumah*. *De'af al gav d'ika lemigzar mishum terumah d'oraita, lo gazrinan* — nevertheless, no such *gezeirah* is made. The power of *chazaka* is that it overrides even a preventive decree that might otherwise have been reasonable. The discussion continues on 25a.
