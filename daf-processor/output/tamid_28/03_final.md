# Tamid 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Cup Lip & Respect

### Cup Cleaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׁתָה מַיִם וְלֹא שָׁפַךְ מֵהֶן, וְנָתַן לְתַלְמִידוֹ, וְאוֹתוֹ תַּלְמִיד אִיסְטְנִיס הָיָה, וְלֹא רָצָה לִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמֵת בַּצָּמָא. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם מַיִם וְיִתֵּן לְתַלְמִידוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן שָׁפַךְ מֵהֶן. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הִילְכָּךְ, הַאי תַּלְמִידָא דְּשָׁפֵיךְ קַמֵּי רַבֵּיהּ – לֵית בֵּיהּ מִשּׁוּם אַפְקִירוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** there was **an incident involving a certain individual who drank water and did not pour some of it out, and he gave** the remaining water **to his student. And that student was a delicate person [*istenis*], and he did not want to drink** from the cup, **and he died of thirst. At that time,** the Sages **said: A person should not drink water and give** the remaining water **to his student unless** he first **poured some of it out. Rav Ashi said: Therefore,** in the case of **this student who pours** water from the cup that his teacher drank from first, even if he does so **in the presence of his teacher, his actions are not** prohibited **due to disrespect [*afkiruta*].**

We pick up at the very bottom of 27b, approximately six lines from the end, at the small *dalet* in parentheses. Rav Ashi rules: *hai talmida d'shafich chamra kammai rabbeh* — a student who pours out some drink in front of his teacher, specifically to clean the lip of the cup, is not guilty of *afkivuta*, disrespect toward a Torah scholar. The context is the preceding discussion about a student who was so fastidious about the lip of a shared cup that he preferred to die of thirst rather than drink from it. Rav Ashi is clarifying that this kind of fastidiousness, pouring out a little liquid to cleanse the rim, does not constitute degradation of a Torah scholar, because the intent is cleanliness, not contempt. The general rule that follows is *kol mili lo tiphut b'apei rabach* — in general, you should not spit or pour things out in front of your master.

### Squash & Porridge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל מִילֵּי לָא תִּיפְלוֹט בְּאַפֵּי רַבָּךְ, בַּר מִקָּרָא וְדַיְיסָא, דְּכִפְתִילָה שֶׁל אֲבָר דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to eating, these *amora'im* instructed their sons: In the case of **anything** that you are eating, if the food causes you to salivate and you need to spit out the saliva, **do not spit** it **out in the presence of your teacher,** as it is disrespectful, **except** in the case **of** a dish of **gourd or porridge. If one is eating gourds or porridge he may spit out the saliva even in the presence of his teacher, as** the saliva generated by these items is **like** a molten **bar of lead,** and refraining from spitting it out would be dangerous.

There is, however, an exception beyond the cup of water already discussed: *mi-kra v'daisa* — squash and *daisa*, a type of porridge or oatmeal-like dish. The reason given is *d'kaptila shel avar damu* — they are likened to a strand of molten lead, an allusion to the method of execution by *sreifa*, in which fiery lead would be poured down the throat. The point is that saliva mixed with these particular foods is considered especially dangerous, so there is genuine justification to remove someone else's spit before consuming them. Thus, even in front of one's master, spitting out such food is permitted and appropriate.

## Watch Enforcement

### Officer Circulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: אִישׁ הַר הַבַּיִת הָיָה מְחַזֵּר עַל כָּל מִשְׁמָר וּמִשְׁמָר, וַאֲבוּקוֹת דּוֹלְקוֹת לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Middot* 1:2): **The man [*ish*]** in charge of overseeing the watches **of the Temple Mount would circulate** nightly **among each and every watch post,** to ascertain that the watchmen were awake and performing their duty properly. **And** there were **lit torches** carried **before him,** so that the watchmen would see him approaching.

The Gemara transitions with *tnan hatam* — we learned in a Mishnah elsewhere. This is a Mishnah from Middot, cited here because, as the Rosh notes, since there is no Gemara on Middot, the Gemara in Tamid takes the opportunity to explicate certain Mishnayos from that tractate. The Mishnah reads: *Ish Har HaBayit hayah mechazer al kol mishmar u-mishmar* — the officer of Har HaBayit would make his rounds among all the watches. The reference to Har HaBayit is significant: recall that half the watches of the *Levi'im* were stationed around Har HaBayit itself rather than inside the *Mikdash* proper. He would go from watch to watch *v'avukas dolkos lefanav* — with torches burning before him, presumably carried by his attendants.

### Sleeping Detection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מִשְׁמָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״אִישׁ הַר הַבַּיִת שָׁלוֹם עָלֶיךָ״! – נִיכָּר שֶׁהוּא יָשֵׁן
>
> **Translation:** **And** at **every watch post where** the watchman would **not stand** up, the man would test whether the watchman was sleeping; **and the man of the Temple Mount** would **say to him: "Peace be upon you." If he would not reply, it was evident that he** was **sleeping.**

The Mishnah continues: *v'chol mishmar she'eino omed v'omer lo, Ish Har HaBayit shalom alecha* — any watch that, upon his approach, did not immediately stand and say "Ish Har HaBayit, shalom alecha," demonstrated that they had been asleep. One might ask how he could sneak up on anyone while carrying large torches. The answer is implicit: the test was not surprise but speed of response — the moment he became visible, the watchman was expected to jump to his feet. Failure to do so *nikar shehu yashen* — revealed that he had been sleeping. It is worth noting that sitting appears to have been the accepted norm for the *Levi'im* on watch, at least in certain positions; what was forbidden was sleep, not sitting.

### Burning Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹבְטוֹ בְּמַקְלוֹ. וּרְשׁוּת הָיְתָה לוֹ לִשְׂרוֹף אֶת כְּסוּתוֹ. וְהֵם אוֹמְרִים: מָה קוֹל בָּעֲזָרָה? קוֹל בֶּן לֵוִי לוֹקֶה וּבְגָדָיו נִשְׂרָפִין, שֶׁיָּשַׁן לוֹ עַל מִשְׁמָר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: פַּעַם אַחַת מָצְאוּ אֶת אֲחִי אִמִּי יָשֵׁן, וְשָׂרְפוּ אֶת כְּסוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The man of the Temple Mount would therefore **strike him with his staff, and he** even **had license to burn** the watchman's **garment,** in order to discipline him. **And those** overhearing the watchman being rebuked would **say** to one another: **What is** that **sound in the** Temple **courtyard?** It is the **sound of a Levite** being **flogged and** having **his clothing burned, as he was** caught **sleeping during** his **watch. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: Once,** the supervisors **found my mother's brother sleeping** on his watch, **and they burned his garment.**

*Chovto b'maklo* — the officer would strike the offending watchman with his stick. Beyond that physical punishment, *reshut hayeta lo lisrof et kesuto* — he had explicit permission to burn his garment. The Gemara then describes the reaction of those nearby: *mah kol ba'azara?* — what is that sound in the *azara*? And the answer: *kol ben Levi lokeh ubigadav nisrafim sheyashen lo mimishmaro* — it is the sound of a Levite being struck and his garments burned because he fell asleep at his post.

This passage is genuinely disturbing. It is one thing to impose physical discipline; it is another to set someone's clothing on fire, potentially while it is still on his person. The phrase *kol ba'azara* is also slightly puzzling, since the *Levi'im* were not in the *azara* proper and were presumably sitting — they would certainly not be in the inner courtyard. Perhaps it means the *Kohanim* who were inside the *azara* were hearing sounds emanating from outside, and so the phrase reflects their vantage point. As for the burning of garments on a person's body — one suggestion is that perhaps the watchman, having fallen asleep unintentionally, had not removed his clothing as the *Kohanim* did when they slept intentionally in the *Beis HaMoked* (where they would remove their *bigdei kehuna* and place them beside their heads). Another reading, perhaps more charitable, is that the burning was figurative — a kind of shaming or symbolic defroking — and that *kol ba'azara* reflects how people would gossip about the incident afterward. That said, the story of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's own uncle strongly suggests the account is entirely literal: *pa'am achat matzu es achi imi yashen vesarfu es kesuso* — once they found my mother's brother sleeping and burned his garment. No concern is expressed about danger to his person, which is itself striking.

It is worth remembering that in the ancient world, army discipline was severe and unambiguous. The Romans, for example, were known to execute soldiers summarily for dereliction of duty. The text appears to reflect a similarly uncompromising culture of military-style order in the Temple service.

### Ancient Discipline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: כִּי מָטֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּהָא מַתְנִיתָא, אָמַר הָכִי: אַשְׁרֵיהֶם לָרִאשׁוֹנִים, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ עַל אוֹנֶס שֵׁינָה עוֹשִׂין דִּין. שֶׁלֹּא עַל אוֹנֶס שֵׁינָה – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: When Rabbi Yoḥanan would reach this mishna** in his studies, he **would say this: The early** generations **were praiseworthy, as they meted out judgment even in** the case of an offense caused by **unavoidable sleep;** and **all the more so** would they mete out judgment **in** the case of offenses that were **not** caused by **unavoidable sleep.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba reports: *ki mati Rabbi Yochanan bahai matnisa* — when Rabbi Yochanan reached this Mishnah, he said: *ashreihem larishonim* — praiseworthy are the earlier generations, who maintained such discipline that they would even punish people for *onesh sheina* — falling asleep — which is not a willful act; sleep overcomes a person involuntarily. *Shelo al onesh sheina al achat kama v'chama* — how much more rigorous must their discipline have been when dealing with fully willful violations. The implicit contrast is with Rabbi Yochanan's own era, where even voluntary transgressions went unchecked. Whether one reads this as sincere admiration or as tinged with irony, it reflects a clear reckoning with the passage of a certain kind of institutional discipline.

## Rebuke & Feedback

### Rebbi's Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵיזוֹ הִיא דֶּרֶךְ יְשָׁרָה שֶׁיָּבוֹר לוֹ הָאָדָם? יֶאֱהַב אֶת הַתּוֹכָחוֹת, שֶׁכׇּל זְמַן שֶׁתּוֹכָחוֹת בָּעוֹלָם – נַחַת רוּחַ בָּאָה לָעוֹלָם, טוֹבָה וּבְרָכָה בָּאִין לָעוֹלָם, וְרָעָה מִסְתַּלֶּקֶת מִן הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלַמּוֹכִיחִים יִנְעָם וַעֲלֵיהֶם תָּבֹא בִּרְכַּת טוֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Which** way of life **is an upright path that a person should select for himself;** what should be his guiding principle? One **should love admonition, for as long as** statements of **admonition** from the wise are heard **in the world, pleasantness comes into the world, goodness and blessing come into the world, and evil departs from the world, as it is stated: "But to those who admonish shall be delight, and a good blessing shall come upon them"** (Proverbs 24:25).

The Gemara cites a *braita*: *Rebbi omer, eizehi derech yeshara sheyavor lo ha'adam — ahavat hatochechot* — Rebbi says, what is the proper path a person should choose for himself? Love of remonstration. The *masores ha-Shas* points to Pirkei Avot for a parallel statement by Rebbi. The placement here is deliberate: having just described a regime of strict discipline and physical punishment for failures, the Gemara pivots to the spiritual dimension of correction and rebuke. Rebbi is not speaking of physical punishment but of welcoming critical feedback. The connection is clear — people being disciplined should not react with resentment; rather, a person who genuinely loves *tochachah* understands that it comes from a place of care and serves growth.

### Rebuke Blessing

*Shekol zman shetochachot ba'olam* — as long as people are giving genuine *mussar* to one another, and presumably also willingly receiving it, *nachat ruach ba'ah la'olam* — pleasantness comes to the world — *tovah u'vrachah ba'im la'olam v'ra'ah mistaleket min ha'olam* — goodness and blessing come to the world and evil is removed from it. The proof text cited is *v'lamochichim yinam* — to those who give rebuke it will be pleasant — *va'aleihem tavo birchat tov* — and upon them will come the blessing of good. The *mefaresh* notes that since the context is about loving to *receive* rebuke, he reads the verse as referring to those who accept rebuke — though grammatically, the verse speaks of those who *give* it.

The deeper point is profound. A society in which people earnestly want to hear what they could do better is a society in which everyone is working on self-improvement, and in which honor and pride do not become obstacles to growth. Many people are fundamentally decent, yet so protective of their dignity that when confronted with criticism they dig in their heels and bad behavior perpetuates. Rebbi's teaching is a call to cultivate the desire to hear honest and constructive feedback.

It is worth noting, as the Gemara elsewhere acknowledges, that not only does no one today want to receive *mussar* — no one even knows how to give it properly. Rebuke delivered from anger, wounded pride, or a love of criticizing is not true *tochachah* and will not be received as such. Part of the responsibility lies with the giver as much as the receiver. If the recipient senses that the feedback comes from genuine care, it becomes far easier to hear.

There is a well-known story — attributed either to the Vilna Gaon and the Dubna Maggid, or to another pairing of that era — of a great Torah personality who would invite a *maggid* regularly, whether weekly or monthly, specifically to receive *mussar*. This is the very spirit Rebbi is describing: turning toward someone and deliberately asking to hear what one might do better.

### Emunah Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: יַחְזִיק בֶּאֱמוּנָה יְתֵירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵינַי בְּנֶאֶמְנֵי אֶרֶץ לָשֶׁבֶת עִמָּדִי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say:** The path one **should** select is to **adhere to utmost faithfulness** in business and interpersonal relations, **as it is stated: "My eyes are upon the faithful of the land, that they may dwell with Me;** he who walks in a way of integrity, he shall serve Me" (Psalms 101:6).

*V'yesh omrim* — some say the proper path is *yachazik b'emunah* — to hold fast to *emunah*. Here *emunah* should be understood not primarily as theological faith but as trustworthiness in one's dealings with others, in the sense of *noseh v'noten b'emunah* — conducting business and human interactions with the greatest rectitude. The *mefaresh* explains it this way, and this reading fits the broader context of Rebbi's statement in Pirkei Avot as well. The proof text cited is *einai b'ne'emnei aretz lashevet imadi* — my eyes are toward the trustworthy of the land, that they may dwell with me — meaning, with God. *Ba'emunah yeteirah* — through an abundant measure of trustworthiness in one's dealings.

### Rebuke Heaven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הַמּוֹכִיחַ אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם – זוֹכֶה לְחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מוֹכִיחַ אָדָם אַחֲרַי״. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁמּוֹשְׁכִין עָלָיו חוּט שֶׁל חֶסֶד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חֵן יִמְצָא מִמַּחֲלִיק לָשׁוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: Anyone who rebukes another for the sake of Heaven is privileged to** dwell in **the portion of the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "He that rebukes a man shall be behind Me"** (Proverbs 28:23), i.e., with Me. **Moreover,** the heavenly court **extends over him a cord of** divine **grace, as it is stated** in the same verse: **"He will find more favor than he who flatters with the tongue."**

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *kol ha-mochiach et chaveiro l'shem shamayim* — whoever rebukes his friend for the sake of heaven alone, not out of personal offense or to feel superior, but out of genuine concern — *zocheh l'chelko shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — will merit to be in the portion of the Holy One Blessed Be He in *Olam Haba*, as the verse says, *mochiach adam acharai* — one who gives rebuke will come after me, meaning into God's inner circle.

*V'lo od ela she-moshchin alav chut shel chesed* — not only that, but a thread of *chesed* is drawn over such a person. This phrase, *chut shel chesed*, appears in several places in the Gemara — in connection with one who learns Torah by day and night, and in the *Aggadata* of Esther, where she is described as having this quality. It suggests an aura or halo of divine loving-kindness that surrounds the person. The proof text is *chen yimtza mi-machalik lashon* — favor will be found by one with a smooth tongue — meaning someone who knows how to speak in a way that is heard, that lands well, that is accessible and palatable to the recipient.

This is also a kind of encouragement for those who give *mussar* and face pushback: even if the receiver — or bystanders — reacts with "who are you to tell me what to do?", the one who gave rebuke in the right spirit will find divine favor and will be remembered well. The arc of the discussion moves naturally from the harsh physical discipline of the Temple watch to the verbal, interpersonal, and ultimately spiritual dimensions of correction and feedback — each step refining the previous one.

## Trumat HaDeshen Lottery

### Lottery Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה לִתְרוֹם אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ [וְכוּ׳]. הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ: מִי שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה לִתְרוֹם אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – מַשְׁכִּים וְטוֹבֵל עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָבֹא הַמְמוּנֶּה. אַלְמָא לָאו בְּפַיִיס תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא. וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: יָבֹא וִיפַיֵּיס, אַלְמָא בְּפַיִיס תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches (26a): If one **found** the bathroom door **closed, he would know** that there was a person there. The mishna then describes the commencement of the daily service in the Temple: **Whoever wants to remove** the ashes **from the altar** rises early and immerses himself before the appointed priest arrives. The Gemara objects: **This** mishna **itself** is **difficult. You** first **said: Whoever wants to remove** the ashes **from the altar rises early and immerses** himself **before the appointed** priest **arrives. Evidently, the matter** of determining who removes the ashes **is not dependent upon a lottery;** rather, whoever is ready first is entitled to remove the ashes. Otherwise, only the one selected would then immerse. **But afterward,** the mishna **teaches** that the appointed priest says to the other priests: Whoever immersed **may come and participate in the lottery. Evidently, the matter is dependent upon a lottery.** How can this contradiction be resolved?

The Gemara now turns to the actual *avodah* of *trumat hadeshen*. The Mishnah states: *mi she-rotze litrom et ha-mizbe'ach mashkim v'tovel ad she-lo yavo ha-memuneh* — whoever wishes to perform *trumat hadeshen* should rise early and immerse before the appointed officer arrives. Then the Mishnah continues and says *yavo v'yapayes* — the officer arrives and says, let us draw lots. The Gemara identifies a tension: *ha-gufa kashya* — the passage is internally contradictory. The first clause says "whoever wishes to do *trumat hadeshen*" — implying it is not determined by lottery at all, just by desire or perhaps by some other mechanism. Yet the second clause explicitly introduces a lottery. *Alma lav b'payes talya milta* — it sounds like the selection is not lottery-based. And yet: *alma b'payes talya milta* — it sounds exactly like it is.

### Before/After Edict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן – קוֹדֶם תַּקָּנָה. כָּאן – לְאַחַר תַּקָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** when the mishna indicates that whoever is ready first is entitled to remove the ashes, it is describing the procedure **before** the Sages enacted the **ordinance** of a lottery. **There,** when the mishna states that the appointed priest conducts a lottery, it is describing the procedure **after** they enacted the **ordinance.**

Abaye resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — kan kodem takkana, kan l'achar takkana* — the two clauses refer to two different historical periods, before and after a rabbinic enactment. Before the *takkana*, the practice was: *kol mi she-rotze litrom et ha-mizbe'ach, torem* — whoever wished to perform *trumat hadeshen* could simply do so, as perhaps only one person would want to on a given morning. When multiple *Kohanim* sought the privilege, *ratzin v'olin b'keves* — they would race up the ramp of the *mizbe'ach*, and *kol ha-kodem et chaveiro b'toch arba amot zachah* — whoever preceded his fellow within four *amot* of the top won the right. There is something symbolically apt about this: *trumat hadeshen* is the very first act of the day, and *zerizin makdimin l'mitzvot* — those who are eager precede others in the performance of *mitzvot*. A race up the *mizbe'ach* is itself a demonstration of that very eagerness. In the event of a tie, the appointed officer would say *hatzbi'u* — extend your fingers — and they would hold out one or two fingers (but not the thumb; the Mishnah specifies *v'ein motzi'in godel ba-Mikdash*). The most creative explanation offered for excluding the thumb is that two fingers clearly emerge from a single hand and count as one, whereas a thumb extended alongside a finger might be misread as coming from two separate people — a way of preventing a *Kohen* from being counted twice in the count.

### Broken Leg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה כׇּל מִי שֶׁרוֹצֶה לִתְרוֹם אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, תּוֹרֵם. בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מְרוּבִּין – רָצִין וְעוֹלִין בַּכֶּבֶשׁ, כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בְּתוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – זָכָה. הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם שָׁוִין, הַמְמוּנֶּה אוֹמֵר לָהֶם: ״הַצְבִּיעוּ״. וּמָה הֵן מוֹצִיאִים? אַחַת אוֹ שְׁתַּיִם. וְאֵין מוֹצִיאִין גּוּדָּל בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. מַעֲשֶׂה שְׁנַיִם הָיוּ שָׁוִין, רָצִין וְעוֹלִין בַּכֶּבֶשׁ, וְדָחַף אֶחָד מֵהֶם אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנִשְׁבְּרָה רַגְלוֹ. וּכְשֶׁרָאוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁהָיוּ בָּאִים לִידֵי סַכָּנָה, הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ תּוֹרְמִין אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ אֶלָּא בְּפַיִיס.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Yoma* 22a): **Initially,** the practice was as follows: **Whoever wants to remove** the ashes **from the altar removes** them. **When there are many** priests who wish to perform that task, the matter is determined by a race: The priests **run and ascend on the ramp** leading to the top of the altar. **Any** priest **who precedes the other** and reaches **within four cubits** of the top of the altar first **is privileged** to remove the ashes. If **two** priests **were equal** and neither preceded the other, **the appointed** priest **says to them: Raise** your **fingers,** and a lottery is performed. **And what** fingers **do they extend** for the lottery? They may extend **one or two** fingers. The lottery was then conducted when the priests chose an arbitrary number, after which the appointee counted the raised fingers of the priests, who stood in a circle. He would go around the circle counting the fingers until he reached the chosen number, and the priest at whom the count ended won the lottery. **And** the priests **do not extend a thumb in the Temple,** as using the thumb might enable someone to manipulate the lottery. As the count progressed, a priest could calculate the result and surreptitiously extend his thumb and an additional finger. Since there is separation between the thumb and the forefinger it might appear as though they belonged to two different priests, and in this manner one could cause the lottery to conclude with a different priest. **An incident** occurred in which **two** priests **were equal** as they were **running and ascending on the ramp; and one of them shoved the other** and he fell, **and his leg was broken. And when the court saw that** people **were coming to** potential **danger, they instituted that** the priests **would remove** the ashes **from the altar only by** means of **a lottery.**

The reason the system changed is recorded in the Mishnah: *ma'aseh she-hayu shavin, ratzin v'olin b'keves, v'dachaf echad mehen et chaveiro v'nishberah raglo* — there was an incident where two *Kohanim* were neck and neck racing up the ramp, and one pushed the other off, and his leg broke. *U-chesherau Beis Din she-hayu ba'im lidei sakanah* — when the court saw that things were reaching the point of genuine danger, *hitkinu shelo yitramu et ha-mizbe'ach ella b'fayas* — they enacted that *trumat hadeshen* would henceforth be determined only by lottery. The racing was abolished entirely.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לְאַחַר תַּקָּנָה, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: מִי שֶׁהוּא רוֹצֶה לָבֹא וּלְפַיֵּיס – מַשְׁכִּים וְטוֹבֵל עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָבֹא הַמְמוּנֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Both **this** clause, which states that to remove the ashes the priests must rise early and be prepared, **and that** clause, which states that a lottery is conducted, are describing the procedure **after the ordinance. And this** is what the mishna **is teaching: Whoever wants to come and to participate in the lottery rises early and immerses before the appointed** priest **arrives** to conduct the lottery among those priests who are ready to participate.

Rava disagrees with Abaye's reading. *Idi v'idi achar takkana* — both clauses of the Mishnah are post-enactment, and there is no contradiction. When the Mishnah says "whoever wishes to do *trumat hadeshen*," it means whoever wishes to *be part of the lottery* for *trumat hadeshen* should immerse early. The word "*litrom*" does not mean he proceeds directly to perform the act; it means he wants a chance at it. This, it should be said, is quite clearly the plain *pshat* of the Mishnah.

## Service Order

### Pispas Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָטַל אֶת הַמַּפְתֵּחַ וּפָתַח אֶת הַפִּשְׁפָּשׁ, וְנִכְנַס מִבֵּית הַמּוֹקֵד לָעֲזָרָה, וְנִכְנְסוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים אַחֲרָיו, וּשְׁתֵּי אֲבוּקוֹת שֶׁל אוֹר בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: At some time near dawn the appointed priest **took the key** that was kept beneath a marble tablet set in the floor of the Chamber of the Hearth **and opened** with it **the wicket [*hapishpesh*]** in the gate of the Chamber of the Hearth. **And he entered** through the wicket **from the Chamber of the Hearth to the** Temple **courtyard; and the priests** of the patrilineal family **entered after him, and two torches of fire** were **in their hands,** to light the way.

Having resolved the question of the lottery, the Mishnah now describes the actual sequence of events. The winning *Kohen* takes the key — which, as we learned in an earlier Mishnah, was kept under the custody of the elders of the *Kohanim* in the *Beis HaMoked* — *u'fasach et ha-pispas* — and opens the small door. The *pispas* is a small doorway inset within the large gate, analogous to a dog door set within a full-sized door, or a postern gate within a castle wall. At this pre-dawn hour, opening the massive main gate to the *azara* is premature and inappropriate. Instead, a small door that is built into the gate allows passage. This is the passage from the *Beis HaMoked* into the *azara* proper.

*V'nichnasu ha-Kohanim acharav* — the *Kohanim* entered behind him into the *azara*, *u'shtei avukot shel ur b'yadaim* — with two torches of light in hand among them.

### Group Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶחְלְקוּ לִשְׁתֵּי כִיתּוֹת, אֵלּוּ מְהַלְּכִין בָּאַכְסַדְרָה דֶּרֶךְ מִזְרָח, וְאֵלּוּ מְהַלְּכִין בָּאַכְסַדְרָה דֶּרֶךְ מַעֲרָב. הָיוּ בּוֹדְקִין וְהוֹלְכִים עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעִים לִמְקוֹם עוֹשֵׂה חֲבִיתִּים. הִגִּיעוּ – אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אָמְרוּ: ״שָׁלוֹם, הַכֹּל שָׁלוֹם״. הֶעֱמִידוּ עוֹשֵׂה חֲבִיתִּים לַעֲשׂוֹת חֲבִיתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The priests **divided into two groups; these** priests would **walk along the portico** that surrounded the Temple courtyard, starting in the **direction of east, and those** priests would **walk along the portico** starting in the **direction** of **west.**The priests would ensure that all the service vessels were in place, ready for use in the daily service. Both groups **would continue inspecting** the vessels **until they reached the place** where the Chamber of **the Preparer of the** High Priest's daily **Griddle-Cake** Offering was located. When **they reached** that place, **these** priests **and those** priests **said** to each other: It is **well; all is well,** and all the vessels are in place. **They** then **set the preparer of** the **griddle-cake** offering **to prepare** the **griddle-cake** offering.

*Necheleku lishtei kitot* — they divided into two groups. *Eilu mehalchim ba'achsadra derech mizrach, v'eilu mehalchim ba'achsadra derech ma'arav* — one group went along the portico to the east, and the other went along the portico to the west. The *achsadra* was a colonnaded walkway with a balcony-like structure above — and the Gemara will later clarify that they were walking beneath it at floor level, not above. *Hayu bodkin v'holchin* — they went checking as they walked. The *meforshim* explain this as an inspection for misplaced *keilim* or anything out of order, a kind of early-morning sweep of the entire *azara*. One might think such a check would be done at the end of the night rather than the beginning of the morning, but perhaps the analogy is a cleaning or inspection crew that arrives in the very early hours precisely because everyone from the previous night's service has long since finished.

Those going to the east had only a short stretch to cover; those going to the west had to traverse nearly three-quarters of the inner perimeter before meeting the eastern group. Both groups converged at the eastern wall — specifically, at the chamber of the *Chavitin*, located near *Sha'ar Nikanor*, the main gate into the *azara* proper.

### Chavitin Start

*Higi'u, higi'u* — having arrived, *eilu v'eilu omrim: shalom, hakol shalom* — both groups would declare to one another: peace, all is well. The parallel to the earlier scene — where the officer of Har HaBayit would receive *shalom alecha* or, failing that, would reach for his stick — is noticeable: both scenes are structured around a greeting that signals all is in order. Here, however, *hakol shalom* is the expected and happy result. *He'emidu osei chavitin la'asot chavitin* — they would then announce: let those who make the *chavitin* arise and begin preparing. The *chavitin* is the *Kohen Gadol*'s morning *mincha*, a meal-offering he would bring after the *Tamid shel shachar*. It would not be the first offering placed on the *mizbe'ach*, but the preparation — heating water, starting the process — had to begin early. The announcement at this moment is formulaic: it signals that the morning has begun and each department should move to its task.

It is worth pausing to note the distinctly military character of the entire sequence: the torches, the two-column march, the password-style greeting, the division of responsibilities, the immediate and ordered response to the announcement. The *Ramban* observes that the system of *mishmarot* in the Temple was modeled on the manner in which royal palaces were guarded and run. Every element of this morning procedure reflects that sensibility: the Temple is the palace of the King, and it is run accordingly.

## Kohen Trumat

### Washing First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁזָּכָה לִתְרוֹם אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, הוּא יִתְרוֹם. וְהֵם אוֹמְרִים: הֱוֵי זָהִיר שֶׁלֹּא תִּגַּע בַּכְּלִי עַד שֶׁתְּקַדֵּשׁ יָדֶיךָ וְרַגְלֶיךָ מִן הַכִּיּוֹר. וַהֲרֵי הַמַּחְתָּה נְתוּנָה בַּמִּקְצוֹעַ בֵּין הַכֶּבֶשׁ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, בְּמַעֲרָבוֹ שֶׁל כֶּבֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The priest **who won** the lottery **to remove** the ashes **from the altar shall** then **remove** the ashes. **And** the other priests **say** to him: **Be careful that you do not touch the vessel** with which you perform the rite **until you sanctify your hands and your feet from the Basin,** as a priest may not perform any service in the Temple before sanctifying his hands and feet. The priests would continue their reminders: **The coal pan** with which the ashes are removed **is placed in** the **corner between the ramp and the altar, on the western** side **of** the **ramp.**

The Mishnah now addresses the *Kohen* who has won the lottery for *trumat hadeshen*. *Mi she-zacha litrom et ha-mizbe'ach, hu yitrom* — he shall perform it. Before he proceeds, the others instruct him: *hevei zahir she-lo tiga b'kli ad she-tekadesh yadecha v'raglecha min ha-kiyor* — be careful not to touch any *kli sharet* until you have washed your hands and feet from the *kiyor*, the copper laver described in the Torah at the beginning of *Ki Tisa*. The relevant verse: *v'rachatzu yedeihem v'ragleihem v'lo yamutu b'vo'am el ohel mo'ed o b'gishtam el ha-mizbe'ach lesharet* — they shall wash their hands and feet so that they do not die when they come to the *Ohel Mo'ed* or when they approach the *mizbe'ach* to serve. The concern is *linah*: whatever *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* was performed the previous night is invalidated by the passage of the night. He must wash anew this morning. The walk-around the *azara* that the group just performed does not itself constitute *avodah*, so those *Kohanim* had not yet been obligated to wash; but now, before touching any sacred vessel, the *Kohen* designated for *trumat hadeshen* must first go to the *kiyor*.

They also point out to him: *v'harei ha-machtah netunah b'miktzoa bein ha-kevesh la-mizbe'ach b'ma'aravo shel kevesh* — behold, the silver shovel is placed at the corner between the ramp and the *mizbe'ach*, at the western side of the ramp. This is in the same general zone as the *kiyor*. The instruction is explicit: you will see the *machtah*; do not touch it. First go and wash.

### Alone Walk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין אָדָם נִכְנָס עִמּוֹ. וְלֹא נֵר בְּיָדוֹ, אֶלָּא מְהַלֵּךְ לְאוֹר הַמַּעֲרָכָה. לֹא הָיוּ רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **No person would enter with** the priest who was removing the ashes, as it was permitted to enter the area between the Entrance Hall of the Sanctuary and the altar only when performing the Temple service. **And** there was **no lamp in his hand** when he went to fetch the coal pan. **Rather, he would walk by the light of the arrangement** of wood on the altar, upon which the portions of the offerings sacrificed the previous day were burned during the night. The other priests **would not see him,** as the altar hid him from their sight,

*Ein adam nichnas imo, v'lo ner b'yado* — no one accompanies him, and he carries no candle. He goes alone to the *kiyor*, and then alone up to the *mizbe'ach*. *Ella mehalech l'or ha-ma'arachah* — he makes his way by the light of the fire burning on the *mizbe'ach*. The reason he carries no candle is not entirely explained, though practically, one cannot hold a candle while washing hands and then handling the shovel, and there is no obvious place to set it down. As for why no one accompanies him — it is simply the prescribed manner of performing this particular *avodah*; he walks alone.

*V'lo hayu ro'im oto* — the other *Kohanim* could not see him, because the *mizbe'ach* and its ramp were very tall and he was passing between the ramp and the *ulam*, on the side away from where the others stood. *V'lo hayu shom'im et kolo* — they could not even hear him moving.

### Kiyor Machinery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין אֶת קוֹלוֹ, עַד שֶׁהָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל הָעֵץ שֶׁעָשָׂה בֶּן קָטִין מוּכְנִי לַכִּיּוֹר. וְהֵן אוֹמְרִים: הִגִּיעַ עֵת! קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו מִן הַכִּיּוֹר, נָטַל מַחְתַּת הַכֶּסֶף וְעָלָה לְרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **nor could they hear the sound of his** steps. They were therefore unaware of his progress **until they heard** the **sound of the wood that ben Katin crafted** into **a mechanism [*mukhani*]** of pulleys that was used **to** sink **the Basin** into flowing water during the night, so that its water would not be disqualified by remaining overnight. When the priests heard the sound of the pulleys raising the Basin from the water, **they said** to each other: **The time** for sanctifying hands and feet **has come.** The priest **sanctified his hands and his feet** with water **from the Basin** after he raised it. **He** then **took the silver coal pan** from the corner between the ramp and the altar, **and ascended to the top of the altar.**

The *Kohanim* outside knew he had reached the *kiyor* when *ad she-hayu shom'im kol ha-etz she-asah Ben Katin muchani la-kiyor* — they heard the sound of the wooden gear-mechanism that Ben Katin had constructed for the *kiyor*. The problem Ben Katin's device solved was this: any water left standing overnight in the *kiyor* — a *kli sharet* — would become *pasul b'linah*, invalidated by overnight exposure. To prevent this, a system of levers and gears was built that lowered the *kiyor* into a pit beneath the *azara* floor each night. Once submerged, the water was considered connected to the ground and did not become *pasul*. Each morning, the first *Kohen* to use the *kiyor* would operate the mechanism to raise it back out. When the other *Kohanim* heard the sound of that machinery — the creaking and turning of the wooden gears — they knew: *higi'a eit kiddush yadayim v'raglayim min ha-kiyor* — the time for washing hands and feet from the *kiyor* has come.

### Altar Ascent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּינָּה אֶת הַגֶּחָלִים הֵילָךְ, חוֹתֶה אֶת הַמְאוּכָּלוֹת הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת. יָרַד הִגִּיעַ לָרִצְפָּה, הָפַךְ פָּנָיו לַצָּפוֹן, הוֹלֵךְ בְּמִזְרָחוֹ שֶׁל כֶּבֶשׁ כְּעֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת. צָבַר אֶת הַגֶּחָלִים עַל גַּבֵּי הָרִצְפָּה רָחוֹק מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים, מָקוֹם שֶׁנּוֹתְנִים מוּרְאַת הָעוֹף, וְדִישּׁוּן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, וְדִישּׁוּן הַמְּנוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The priest **cleared the** upper layer of **coals to this side** and to that side and **scooped** into the coal pan **the inner** coals that were completely **consumed.** He then **descended** the ramp. When **he reached the floor,** in the southeast of the Temple courtyard, **he turned his face toward the north. He** would **walk along the east** side **of the ramp** toward the south side of the altar, walking a distance of **about ten cubits** from the bottom of the ramp, which was twenty cubits from the altar. **He** then **heaped the coals upon the floor** in a location **three handbreadths distant from the ramp,** in the **place where** the priests would **place the crop of the bird** burnt offering, **the ashes** removed **from the inner,** golden **altar, and the ashes** removed **from the Candelabrum.**

*Natal machtas ha-kesef* — he took the silver shovel that had been left in its designated spot between the ramp and the *mizbe'ach*. *V'alah l'rosh ha-mizbe'ach* — he ascended to the top of the *mizbe'ach*. *Pina et ha-gechalim haylach va'haylach* — he moved the live coals aside, clearing space to access the inner ash deposits. *Choteh me-hachmot ha-pnimiyot* — he scooped from the innermost ashes, those that had been fully consumed, not the coals. He then descended the ramp on the other side, facing away from the *mizbe'ach*. *Hafach panav la-tzafon* — he turned his face to the north, turning back toward the face of the *mizbe'ach*. *Halach b'mizrachah shel keves ke-eser amot* — he walked about ten *amot* down the eastern side of the ramp, roughly a third of its length.

### Ash Piling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵחֵלּוּ מַעֲלִין בָּאֵפֶר עַל גַּב תַּפּוּחַ. תַּפּוּחַ הָיָה בְּאֶמְצַע הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, פְּעָמִים שֶׁהָיוּ עָלָיו כִּשְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת כּוֹר. וּבָרְגָלִים לֹא הָיוּ מְדַשְּׁנִין אוֹתוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נוֹי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ. וּמִיָּמָיו
>
> **Translation:** The priests then **began raising the ashes onto** the **circular heap** upon which the ashes were piled. The **circular heap was in the middle of the altar. Sometimes there was** as much **as three hundred *kor*** of ashes **upon it.** When the heap of ashes became excessively large, the priests would remove the ashes and pour them outside the city. **But during the Festivals they would not remove the ashes** from the altar, **as** the ashes **were** considered **an adornment to the altar,** since they were a sign of the great number of offerings that were sacrificed on it. **In all the days** of the altar, even when there was an abundance of ashes upon it,

*Tzavar et ha-gechalim al gabei ha-ritzpa* — he piled up the ashes and coals on the floor of the *azara*. This is the act of *trumat hadeshen* itself: removing a symbolic scoop of ash from the *ma'aracha gedolah*, the main fire where the *olah* and fats had burned through the night, and depositing it at the designated spot alongside the ramp. *Rachok min ha-keves shlosha tefachim* — about three *tefachim* removed from the ramp. This location is the same spot where the innards of the bird-*olah* were deposited (*morat ha-of*), as well as the ash-cleanings of the inner *mizbe'ach* where the *Ketoret* was burned, and the ash-cleanings of the *Menorah*. Notably, the *mefaresh* here distinguishes between these latter items — which he holds were miraculously absorbed into the ground where they were placed, as Abaye's statement in Yoma implies — and the *trumat hadeshen* itself, which he believes was not absorbed. His proof: the Gemara elsewhere states that one may not derive *hana'ah* from the *trumat hadeshen* even after it has been placed. If it were absorbed, why would such a prohibition be necessary? This is a striking observation, since we generally assume a unified miraculous treatment of all these deposits, yet this *mefaresh* argues the *trumat hadeshen* stood apart.

## Architecture & Order

### Balcony Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמִי הֲווֹ אַכְסַדְרָאוֹת בָּעֲזָרָה? וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין אַכְסַדְרָאוֹת בָּעֲזָרָה – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תִטַּע לְךָ אֲשֵׁרָה כָּל עֵץ אֵצֶל מִזְבַּח ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the priests would walk along the portico surrounding the Temple courtyard. Since porticoes were usually made of wood, the Gemara asks: **And were there porticoes in the courtyard? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: From where** is it derived **that one may not build** wooden **porticoes in the** Temple **courtyard? The verse states: "You shall not plant for yourself an *ashera* of any kind of tree beside the altar of the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 16:21).

The Gemara asks: *umi havu achsadra'ot ba'azara?* — could there really have been balconies or colonnaded structures in the *azara*? A *braita* cites Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: *minayin she'ein osim achsadra'ot ba'azara?* — how do we know that one may not construct *achsadra'ot* in the *azara*? The verse is *lo tita lecha ashera kol etz etzel mizbe'ach Hashem Elokecha* — you shall not plant any tree beside the altar of Hashem. Read expansively: beyond the prohibition of an *ashera*, there is a prohibition against any wooden structure (*kol etz*) near the *mizbe'ach* — including wooden balconies in the *azara*. The resolution is straightforward: Rav Chisda rules *b'achsadra'ot shel binyan* — the *achsadra'ot* of the *azara* were built of stone, not wood. There is no reason to assume they were made of wood, and therefore no violation.

### Service Sequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״לֹא תִטַּע לְךָ אֲשֵׁרָה״, ״לֹא תִטַּע לְךָ כׇּל עֵץ אֵצֶל מִזְבַּח ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּאַכְסַדְרָאוֹת שֶׁל בִּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **this** is what the verse **is saying: You shall not plant for yourself an *ashera*,** i.e., a tree used as part of idolatrous rites. Moreover, **you shall not plant** or otherwise place **for yourself any kind of tree** or wood **beside the altar of the Lord your God. Rav Ḥisda says:** The mishna is referring **to porticoes** made **of building** materials, i.e., stone, whereas the prohibition of erecting porticoes in the Temple courtyard applies only to porticoes of wood.

The Mishnah's instruction to get the *chavitin* makers started seems to imply the *chavitin* was among the first things offered after *trumat hadeshen*. The Gemara addresses this: *minayin she'ein davar kodem la-Tamid shel shachar?* — how do we know that nothing precedes the morning *Tamid* in terms of offerings on the *mizbe'ach*? The verse says *v'arach aleha ha-olah* — everything goes up upon the *olah*, meaning the *Tamid* as the *olah* comes first. Rava adds: *ha-olah olah rishona* — the *olah* is placed first before everything. The *chavitin*, though it required early preparation, was not offered before the *Tamid*.

### Water Heating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ בּוֹדְקִין וְהוֹלְכִין כּוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דַּחֲבִיתִּין הֲווֹ קְדִימִי? וְהַתַּנְיָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין דָּבָר קוֹדֵם לְתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָרַךְ עָלֶיהָ הָעֹלָה״, וְאָמַר רַבָּה: ״הָעֹלָה״, עוֹלָה רִאשׁוֹנָה! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְהָחֵם חַמִּין לִרְבִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Both groups **would continue inspecting** the vessels until they reached the place where the Chamber of the Preparer of the Griddle-Cake Offering was located. They would then set the preparer of the Griddle-Cake Offering to prepare the griddle-cake offering. The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that** the **griddle-cake** offering **was** sacrificed **before** the other rites of the Temple service were performed? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that there is nothing** that is sacrificed on the altar **prior to the daily morning offering? The verse states:** "And the fire on the altar shall be kept burning on it, it shall not be extinguished; and the priest shall kindle wood upon it every morning, **and he shall prepare the burnt offering upon it** and shall cause the fats of the peace offerings to go up in smoke upon it" (Leviticus 6:5). **And Rabba says** that the term: **The burnt offering,** with the definite article, indicates that the **burnt offering** is important and is the **first** offering sacrificed each day. **Rav Yehuda says:** The griddle-cake offering was not burned on the altar at that early hour. Rather, it was necessary to set the preparers of the griddle-cake offering **to heat hot water for** preparing **the parboiled** griddle cakes.

Rav Yehuda resolves the apparent inconsistency: *le-hachaim chammin l'revicha* — the instruction to get started was not about offering the *chavitin* first, but about heating the water needed to make it. The *chavitin* required boiling water and a process that took time. While the actual offering would come only after the *Tamid shel shachar*, the preparation had to begin much earlier. Thus, the announcement at the chamber — *he'emidu osei chavitin la'asot chavitin* — was a call to begin the preparatory work, not the offering itself.

With this the first *perek* concludes: *Hadran alach bishloshoh mekomot.*

## Morning Tending

### Kohanim Rush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רָאוּהוּ אֶחָיו שֶׁיָּרַד, וְהֵם רָצִים וּבָאִים. מִהֲרוּ וְקִדְּשׁוּ יְדֵיהֶם וְרַגְלֵיהֶם מִן הַכִּיּוֹר, נָטְלוּ אֶת הַמַּגְרֵפוֹת וְאֶת הַצִּינּוֹרוֹת וְעָלוּ לְרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna described the performance of the removal of the ashes by the priest who was selected to perform this task. This mishna continues: **The brethren** of the priest who removed the ashes, i.e., the other members of the patrilineal family, **saw that he had descended** from the altar with the coal pan, **and they** would **run and come** to the Basin. **They made haste and sanctified their hands and their feet with** the water in **the Basin,** and then **they took the shovels and the forks and ascended** with them **to the top of the altar.** The shovels were for shoveling the ashes to the center of the altar, while the forks were required to remove from the altar those limbs that had not been consumed.

The second *perek* opens immediately. *Ra'u echad she-yarad* — the other *Kohanim* saw the *Kohen* designated for *trumat hadeshen* descending the ramp, indicating he was nearly done. *V'hen ratzin u'ba'in* — they came running. *Maharu v'kidshu yedeihem v'ragleihem min ha-kiyor* — they hurried and washed their hands and feet from the *kiyor*, because now that the *trumat hadeshen* is complete, the full morning *avodah* is about to begin and they must be prepared to handle the *keilim*.

### Clear Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאֵבָרִים וְהַפְּדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא נִתְעַכְּלוּ מִבָּעֶרֶב – סוֹלְקִין אוֹתָן עַל צִדֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אִם אֵין צְדָדִים מַחְזִיקִין – סוֹדְרִים אוֹתָם בַּסּוֹבֵב עַל הַכֶּבֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the limbs** of burnt offerings **and the fats** of other offerings **that had not been consumed** and burned to ashes during the time **from the** previous **evening,** the priests would **clear them to the sides of the altar. If** the remaining limbs and fats were so abundant that the **sides** of the altar were **unable to hold** them, the priests would **arrange them on the ramp, opposite the surrounding ledge** of the altar.

*Natlu et ha-magrefot v'et ha-tzinorot* — they took the various shovels and pitchforks. *V'alu l'rosh ha-mizbe'ach* — they ascended to the top of the *mizbe'ach*. The task before them: *evarim u-fedarim shelo nit'aklu mibe'od erev* — the limbs of the *olah* and the fats that had not been fully consumed overnight. These had been placed on the *ma'aracha gedolah*, the main fire, but may not have burned through entirely by morning. They had to be cleared away so that the ash-tending could proceed. But they could not simply remove this material from the *mizbe'ach*: any *evarim* or *fedarim* from the previous day, if taken off the *mizbe'ach*, would be *pasul b'linah* and could never be returned. So long as they remained on the *mizbe'ach*, they were valid; once removed, they could not be put back.

### Side Placement

*Sokin otan al tzidei ha-mizbe'ach* — they pushed them to the sides of the *mizbe'ach*, getting them out of the way without removing them. *Im ein tzadadim machzikin* — if the sides were too crowded, *sodrin otan ba-sovev* — they arranged them on the *sovev*, the walkway that ran around the mid-level of the *mizbe'ach*. If even that was insufficient, the *evarim* could be placed on the ramp itself. In every case, the material stayed on the *mizbe'ach* or its ramp — never off it.

### Ash Pile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵחֵלּוּ מַעֲלִין בָּאֵפֶר עַל גַּב תַּפּוּחַ. תַּפּוּחַ הָיָה בְּאֶמְצַע הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, פְּעָמִים שֶׁהָיוּ עָלָיו כִּשְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת כּוֹר. וּבָרְגָלִים לֹא הָיוּ מְדַשְּׁנִין אוֹתוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נוֹי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ. וּמִיָּמָיו לֹא נִתְעַצֵּל הַכֹּהֵן מִלְּהוֹצִיא אֶת הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The priests then **began raising the ashes onto** the **circular heap** upon which the ashes were piled. The **circular heap was in the middle of the altar. Sometimes there was** as much **as three hundred *kor*** of ashes **upon it.** When the heap of ashes became excessively large, the priests would remove the ashes and pour them outside the city. **But during the Festivals they would not remove the ashes** from the altar, **as** the ashes **were** considered **an adornment to the altar,** since they were a sign of the great number of offerings that were sacrificed on it. **In all the days** of the altar, even when there was an abundance of ashes upon it, **the priest** tasked with removing the ashes from the circular heap **was never indolent in removing the ashes.**

*Hechelu ma'alin ba-eifer al gabei tapuach* — they began transferring the accumulated ashes up to the *tapuach*, the great ash pile in the center of the *mizbe'ach* surface. This is the real and practical tending of the fire, as distinct from the symbolic single-scoop *trumat hadeshen*. The ashes from the *ma'aracha gedolah* would build up; they needed to be moved to the central pile. The *tapuach* stood in the middle of the *mizbe'ach*. *Pe'amim she-haya alav ke-shlosh me'ot kur* — at times there were 300 *kur* of ash on it, an enormous quantity, representing a veritable mountain.

### Festival Ashes

*U-varegalim lo hayu medashnin oto* — on the *Regalim*, the pilgrimage festivals, when all of Israel was present and the volume of sacrifices was extraordinary, they would deliberately not clear out the *tapuach*. *Mipnei she-hu noy la-mizbe'ach* — because the great ash mound was considered beautiful, a dignified aesthetic feature, and a visible testament to the abundance of *korbanos* being offered. To see an enormous ash pile was to understand the scale of the nation's service. Nevertheless, *u-miyamav lo nitatzel ha-kohen milhotzi et ha-deshen* — never in all the days of the Temple did a *Kohen* prove lazy about removing the ashes when the time came to do so. However large the pile, however demanding the task, no one ever said: it is someone else's turn. We will continue with the tending of the fire in the next session.
