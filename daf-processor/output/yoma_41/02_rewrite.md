# Yoma 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hagralah Me'akev

### Hagralah Overview

The *daf* begins with a question that carries over from the preceding discussion: is the *hagralah* — the actual drawing of lots on Yom Kippur to designate the two goats — *me'akev*, meaning essential to the validity of the ceremony? If the *kohen gadol* simply designated the goats verbally, without performing the lottery, would the service be acceptable *bedi'avad*?

To orient the question: Rabbi Shimon holds a uniquely expansive position. Based on his reading of the verse *yaamad chai lifnei Hashem lechaper alav*, as Tosafot understands it, Rabbi Shimon maintains that the *sheir hamishtaleach* is not linked to the fate of the other *sheir* at all — the two animals function independently. Consequently, for Rabbi Shimon, not only is the *hagralah* not *me'akev*, even the entire pairing is not *me'akev*; *bedi'avad*, each goat stands on its own. But Rabbi Shimon is the outlier. The question under discussion concerns everyone else: granted that the pairing is required, must the designation specifically emerge from the lottery, or could a verbal declaration suffice? This debate is attributed to Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yannai, and connects as well to the broader tannaitic dispute — involving Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Nechemiah, and others — over which procedural steps are *me'akev* in general.

### Goral Oseo Chatat

The *gemara* brings a *braita* as evidence. The verse reads: *asher alah alav hagoral l'Hashem ve'asahu chatat* — "upon which the lot fell for God, and he shall make it a *chatat*." The *braita* reads *ve'asahu chatat* not as a future act of Aaron designating it verbally, but as an already completed act of the *goral*: the *goral* is what had already made it a *chatat*. From this the *braita* derives: *hagoral oseo chatat ve'ein hashem oseo chatat* — the lot makes it a *chatat*, and not the name. The "name" here refers both to the invocation of God's name *l'Hashem* and, more pointedly, to verbal designation. No verbal declaration by itself can consecrate the goat as the *chatat*; only the act of the lot falling upon it achieves that designation.

This is a striking conclusion. If the *hagralah* were not *me'akev*, then verbal designation would be the only conceivable alternative for specifying which goat is the *chatat* and which is the *sheir la'azazel*. The *braita* forecloses that alternative entirely.

### Kal Vachomer

The *braita* itself, however, anticipates a counterargument. One might reason through a *kal vachomer*: consider the case of birds (*kinnim*), where a pair of birds — one to be a *chatat* and one to be an *olah* — can be designated through verbal declaration, while drawing lots plays no role whatsoever. If verbal designation works in a context where lots are entirely ineffective, how much more so should verbal designation work in the context of the Yom Kippur goats, where the lot *does* function? The *talmud lomar* answers: *ve'asahu chatat* — the *goral* makes it a *chatat*, and the verbal name does not.

The *gemara* then raises a technical difficulty with this *kal vachomer*. The *kal vachomer* draws a comparison between the moment of the *hagralah* (by the goats) and the moments when verbal designation works (by the birds). But as we will see shortly, verbal designation of birds is effective only at two specific times: at the moment of purchase (*lekichah*) or at the moment the *kohen* performs the *melikah* (*asiyah*). The drawing of lots for the goats occurs at neither of those moments — it is not the time of *lekichah* and it is not the time of *asiyah*. So how can the *braita* compare them?

Rava resolves this by re-reading the *kal vachomer* more carefully. The argument should be understood as follows: in the case of birds, lots *never* work — not at *lekichah*, not at *asiyah*, not at any point. And yet verbal designation does work at those two specific moments. Now contrast this with the goats: there the lot *does* work — not at the time of *lekichah* or *asiyah*, but at some point. Surely, then, verbal designation should work at *lekichah* or *asiyah*, the moments where it demonstrably functions by birds. The *braita* responds: no — because we have the verse explicitly telling us that only the *goral* makes it a *chatat*, not the verbal designation, at any moment. The verse is the *talmud lomar* that overrides the *kal vachomer*.

### Sifra Proof

Having established the *braita*'s position, the *gemara* bolsters it further. *Stam Sifra mani* — the anonymous voice of the *Sifra* (Torat Kohanim, the tannaitic halakhic commentary on Vayikra) is, by tradition, Rabbi Yehuda. He is the unnamed author of the *Sifra*. And the *Sifra* teaches: *hagoral oseo chatat ve'ein hashem oseo chatat* — the lot makes it a *chatat* and not the verbal designation. *Alma hagrala me'akva* — this demonstrates that the *hagralah* is *me'akev*. Because if verbal designation cannot work, there is no other mechanism available; the *goral* is the only path, which means it is indispensable.

### Conclusion

The *gemara* concludes: *tyuvta deman d'amar hagrala lo me'akva tyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of the view that the *hagralah* is not *me'akev*. The position that it is *me'akev* prevails for all opinions. Earlier in the discussion there was uncertainty about whether this was a debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemiah; now the *gemara* concludes that even Rabbi Yehuda — the most permissive voice on *me'akev* questions — would hold that the *hagralah* is *me'akev*.

One detail remains worth noting. The *hagralah* is *me'akev* even though it is not categorized as an *avodah* in the technical sense: it need not be performed by a *kohen*, and the *kohen gadol*'s left hand or the *segan* can perform it. This is a striking combination — something that is *me'akev* yet is not an *avodah*. The question of what precisely gets labeled an *avodah* — and what the halakhic consequences of that designation are (who may perform it, whether it must be done *be'yamin*, and the like) — is a broader discussion. The *hagralah* illustrates how *me'akev* and *avodah* are not coextensive categories. And Rabbi Shimon, of course, remains the outlier: for him, not only is the *hagralah* not *me'akev*, but the entire pairing is not *me'akev* either, and *bedi'avad* the two goats are independent of one another.

## Birds Designation

### Rav Chisda

Having drawn in the case of birds as part of the *kal vachomer* discussion, the *gemara* now completes that topic. Rav Chisda states: *ein hakinnim mitparshot* — a pair of birds (*kinnim*) does not become designated, meaning one as a *chatat* and one as an *olah*, except at one of two moments: *o bi-lekichat ba'alim* — at the time of the owner's purchase, or *o be-asiyat ha-kohen* — at the time of the *kohen*'s act, that is, the *melikah*.

This is a remarkable *halakha*. A person — typically a *yoledet*, a *zav*, a *metzora*, or another individual completing a purification process — must bring two birds, one a *chatat* and one an *olah*. Ordinarily one would assume that consecration must accompany acquisition, or at least that designation must be made early. Here, the pair can be acquired as a unit, entirely undesignated, and the specification of which bird is which can be deferred. But if it is deferred past the moment of purchase, it can only then be established at the moment of *melikah* itself. There is no intermediate window.

The *chiddush* of Rav Chisda's ruling, as one might note, is not the *lekichah* moment — one can designate any *korban* at the time of purchase. The *chiddush* is rather the other side: if you did *not* designate at the time of purchase, you do not lose the ability to designate forever; you retain it until *asiyah*. Nothing in between counts.

### Two Moments

Rav Shimi bar Ashi asks: what is the source for Rav Chisda's ruling? He answers from two verses. By the *yoledet*, the verse says *ve-lakacha shtei torim o shnei bnei yonah* — "and she shall take two turtledoves or two young pigeons" — and specifies *echad le-olah ve-echad le-chatat*, one for an *olah* and one for a *chatat*. The verb of taking (*lekichah*) thus anchors designation to the moment of purchase. By contrast, the verse concerning the *zav* says *ve-asah ha-kohen echad chatat ve-echad olah* — "the *kohen* shall make one a *chatat* and one an *olah*" — which implies that until the *kohen*'s act, it has not yet been determined which is which. Similarly by the *metzora*. These two verses establish the two and only two valid moments of designation.

### Purification Birds

The birds in question are those brought as part of the purification process for the *yoledet*, the *zav*, and the *metzora* at the completion of their *tahara*. These *korbanot* consist of pairs of birds — either turtledoves or young pigeons — one designated as a *chatat* and one as an *olah*. The prevalence of bird *korbanot* in this context reflects a practical reality: pigeons and doves required minimal upkeep, were inexpensive, and were a readily available source for sacrifice. The *korban* of birds served as the *korban ani* option in the sliding-scale obligations of these individuals, making it accessible across economic circumstances.

The *gemara* then raises a challenge. The *braita* we encountered earlier — *ve'asahu chatat*, *hagoral oseo chatat ve'ein hashem oseo chatat* — is now being examined again. The *kal vachomer* embedded in that *braita* compares the case of the goats to the case of birds. But the *gemara* objects: *deha hacha, delav shat lekichah velav shat asiyah hi* — by the Yom Kippur goats, the drawing of lots occurs at a moment that is neither *shat lekichah* nor *shat asiyah*. The comparison is therefore flawed unless we re-read what the *kal vachomer* is actually arguing, which Rava proceeds to do, as discussed above.

### Money Designation

A further challenge is introduced. The *gemara* brings a case from the laws of *tum'at mikdash v'kodashav* — the sin of someone who, while ritually impure, entered the *Mikdash* or consumed consecrated food unknowingly. This transgression falls under the *korban oleh ve-yored*, the sliding-scale offering whose form depends on the offender's economic status. A poor person brings birds; a middle-tier person brings birds as well; a wealthy person brings a sheep.

The case: a poor person (*ani*) commits this transgression and sets aside money (*hefrish ma'ot l'kino*) for the pair of birds he is obligated to bring. He then becomes wealthy (*ve-he'ashir*), which means his obligation has shifted to bringing a sheep. He then verbally divides his pile of money, saying: this half is for my *chatat* and this half is for my *olah*. What is the *halakha*?

The *braita* rules: the half he designated as *chatat* money can be supplemented with additional funds to purchase the sheep *chatat* he now owes — *mosif u-meivi chovato midmei chatato*. However, the half designated as *olah* money cannot be used toward his *chatat* obligation — *ve-ein mosif u-meivi chovato midmei olato* — since he declared it for an *olah*, and a sheep *chatat* requires no accompanying *olah*. That leftover *olah* money would be used to purchase a bird *olah* as a freewill offering (*nedavah*).

### Rich vs Poor

Rav Sheshet raises a pointed difficulty with this *braita*, setting aside entirely the question of *when* designation can occur. He cites a tradition in the name of Rabbi Eliezer citing Rabbi Yehoshua: if a wealthy person who was obligated to bring a sheep instead brought the bird offering of a poor person, *lo yatza* — according to Rabbi Yoshiya, he has not fulfilled his obligation. If that is so, asks Rav Sheshet, once this man became wealthy, the pile of money he set aside for birds is no longer relevant to his *korban* at all — he cannot be *yotzei* with what it would purchase. How, then, can any designation of that money into *chatat* and *olah* portions have any halakhic significance? He is no longer in the category of one who brings these birds. *Kevan delo yatza, heichi kava* — since he would not fulfill his obligation, how can the designation be operative?

## Yatzei Obligation

### Yoshiya: Lo Yatzei

The *gemara* now presents the two positions directly. Rabbi Yoshiya holds: *lo yatza* — a wealthy person who brings the lesser offering of a poor person does not fulfill his obligation. Going down the scale is simply invalid. This is the position attributed earlier to Rabbi Eliezer in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua, and Rabbi Yoshiya endorses it.

### Chaga: Yatzei

Rabbi Chaga, however, transmits a tradition also in the name of Rabbi Yoshiya — creating two conflicting traditions attributed to the same authority — that *yatza*: a wealthy person who brought the lesser offering does fulfill his obligation *bedi'avad*. According to Rabbi Chaga's version, once a person has actually brought the lesser *korban*, it is accepted after the fact.

If Rabbi Chaga's position is correct, then the wealthy person who set aside money for birds while poor is not necessarily removed from the category. *Bedi'avad*, had he brought those birds, he would have been *yotzei*. Therefore, when he divides the pile and designates half for *chatat* and half for *olah*, the designation is meaningful — and we are not forced to read the *braita* as imprecise or as describing a pre-enrichment event.

## Birds Designation…

### Resolution

With the two positions on the table, the *gemara* resolves the question about the *braita*'s scenario. If one follows Rabbi Yoshiya's stricter view that *lo yatza* — that a wealthy person cannot be *yotzei* with a poor person's offering — then the person who became rich is entirely out of the category, and his designation of the money into *chatat* and *olah* portions is meaningless. The *gemara* therefore says we must read the *braita* as imprecise (*shekvar amar me'inyuto*): the *braita* actually means that he made the designation while still poor, and then became rich. The *braita* presents the two events in the wrong order or runs them together loosely.

Furthermore, once we are already reading the *braita* as imprecise about the order of events, we can also say: not only did the designation precede the enrichment, but the designation itself occurred at the moment of *hafrasha* — at the time he set aside the money, the same act of separation constituted the designation. It was one stage, not two.

Under Rabbi Chaga's view that *yatza*, the *braita* need not be read as imprecise at all, and the scenario can be taken literally: he designated the money after becoming rich, and this was effective at some intermediate moment — which would pose the original difficulty about *lekichah* and *asiyah*.

The *gemara*, even under Rabbi Chaga's view, ultimately re-reads the *braita* to say: *ela eima ve-achar kach lakach ve-amar* — the "afterwards" in the *braita* refers not to a verbal splitting of money, but to actually going and purchasing a bird and *at that moment* declaring the other half of the money for the other *korban*. The act of purchasing one bird (*preda achas*) serves as the *shat lekichah* for the designation of the remaining money.

Rav Pappa fills in the practical details. Rather than taking the entire pile of money and buying two birds simultaneously, the person takes half the money and buys a single bird — say, designating it as an *olah*. The very act of purchasing that one bird at *shat lekichah* automatically designates the remaining pile of money as *chatat* money. Now, since there is still money remaining (not a bird), it can be supplemented and used to buy the sheep *chatat*. This is the meaning of *mosif u-meivi chovato midmei chatato*. The bird purchased as an *olah* would simply be brought as a *korban nedavah*.

Conversely, if the bird he purchased was the *chatat*, the remaining money is *olah* money — and that cannot be redirected toward his *chatat* obligation. That is the second part of the *braita*, *ve-ein mosif u-meivi chovato midmei olato*. And the *chatat* bird itself? *Azla le-misa* — it must be left to die, since it was designated as a *chatat* that can no longer be brought and birds have no mechanism of *pidyon* (redemption): unlike livestock, a blemished bird cannot have its *kedusha* transferred to money.

The term *preda* used by the *gemara* for a single bird from the pair is a technical rabbinic term: just as *kan* refers to the pair (a "nest"), *preda* refers to one of the two, derived from the notion of separation.

## Yatzei Obligation…

### Metzora Proof

Returning to the dispute between the two traditions attributed to Rabbi Hoshaya — one via Rabbi Elazar saying *lo yatza* (a rich person bringing a poor person's offering does not fulfill the obligation), the other via Rabbi Chaga saying *yatza* — the *gemara* introduces a *braita* from the laws of *metzora* to clarify.

The *metzora* also has a sliding-scale offering. The *braita* rules: if a poor *metzora* brought the richer *korban*, *yatza* — he has fulfilled his obligation. If a rich *metzora* brought the lesser offering of a poor person, *lo yatza* — he has not. This is presented as a clear ruling. Presumably the same logic would apply to the *korban oleh ve-yored* in general, which would support the *lo yatza* position.

### Oleh V'Yored

However, the *gemara* then distinguishes: *metzora* is a special case. The Torah uses the word *zot* — "this shall be the law of the *metzora* on the day of his purification" — which is an exclusionary term indicating precise adherence to the specified form. Therefore, going down on the scale is invalid. Yet the same verse contains the word *torat* — "the *torat* of the *metzora*" — which is inclusive, extending the law to cover someone who goes *up* the scale. Hence by *metzora*: going up works, going down does not.

The *gemara* confirms this from a *braita*: the word *torat* comes to include a poor *metzora* who brought the richer *korban* — *yatza*. One might think that even going down should work. The *talmud lomar zot* — the word *zot* excludes that possibility.

Why not apply this principle to *korban oleh ve-yored* generally? Because the additional word by *metzora* — *ve-im dal hu*, "and if he is impoverished" — is taken as a limiting term, indicating that these precise rules (both the inclusion of going up and the exclusion of going down) apply specifically to *metzora* and are not meant to be generalized. For *korban oleh ve-yored*, lacking these words, Rabbi Chaga's tradition would hold that *bedi'avad* even going down on the scale fulfills the obligation.

## Red String

### String on Scapegoat

The *gemara* returns to the *mishnah*'s description of the Yom Kippur service. After the lots are drawn and the goats are designated, the *mishnah* records: *kashar lashon shel zehorit be-rosh sheir hamishtaleach* — he tied the *lashon shel zehorit* (the red string) on the head of the *sheir hamishtaleach*, the goat to be sent over the cliff. Notably, the *mishnah* says "head," not "horns." *Ve-he'emido keneged beit shilucho* — he then stood the goat facing the gate from which it would be sent away. *Ve-la-nishchat keneged beit shechitato* — and for the one that would be slaughtered, he placed it opposite the place of slaughter.

The *mishnah* thus describes two actions: the tying of a red string (mentioned explicitly for the *sheir hamishtaleach*) and the placement of each goat at its respective station.

### Second String Question

A question arises from the *mishnah*'s language. When it says *ve-la-nishchat keneged beit shechitato*, does this phrase go back to the first verb — meaning that a red string was also tied on the goat designated for slaughter, placed at the point around its neck from which it would be slaughtered — or does it go back only to the second verb, meaning that only the placement of the animal opposite its slaughter site is described, with no red string on this goat at all?

The more grammatically proximate reading would attach this clause to the verb of standing/placing. But the *gemara* explores both possibilities.

### No Mixing

A *braita* taught by Rav Yosef provides the evidentiary basis. It repeats the *mishnah*'s language but adds: *shelo yitarev zeh be-zeh* — so that the two goats should not become confused with one another, *ve-lo yitarev be-acherim* — and should not become confused with other goats.

### Neck String

The *gemara* now reasons from this addition. If *ve-la-nishchat* refers to a red string tied around the neck of the goat designated for slaughter — *ei amart akshira kai* — then both goats bear red strings, and the two strings distinguish them from each other and from other animals. This works perfectly: the *sheir hamishtaleach* has its string on its head (between the horns), and the *sheir la-Hashem* has its string around its neck. Different placements; same color — but placement suffices to distinguish them.

But if *ve-la-nishchat* refers only to placement — *ela ei amart ahamada kai* — then only one goat (the *sheir hamishtaleach*) has a string. *Nehi de-va-chavreih lo mi'arev* — granted, the one designated for slaughter won't be confused with the one being sent away, since that one has a string and this one does not. *Be-hai katar beih u-ve-hai lo katar beih*. But *u-ve-achrinei mi'arev* — it could still be confused with other goats, since nothing on it marks it as distinct. The requirement *shelo yitarev be-acherim* would then remain unsatisfied for the goat designated for slaughter.

Therefore: *ela lav, shma mina akshira kai* — the clause about the slaughtered goat must refer to tying a string. The *gemara* concludes: *shma mina* — there is indeed a red string tied around the neck of the *sheir la-Hashem*, the goat designated to be slaughtered as the *chatat*.

This is a striking conclusion — one that does not appear to be reflected in the standard liturgical descriptions of the *avodah* found in the *machzor*. Yet the *gemara* concludes it definitively. The practical logic is asymmetrical: the *sheir hamishtaleach* needs its string both to be identifiable and because that string will later be split when the goat reaches the cliff — requiring sufficient length for that purpose. The goat that remains in the *azarah* and is slaughtered would not encounter any other goats, so practically speaking it is less in need of the marking, but the *gemara*'s reading of the *braita* requires the string nonetheless.

## Red String…

### Two Strings

Rabbi Yitzchak's statement that follows addresses two types of *lashon shel zehorit* — and this connects back to the strings just discussed. One of those two strings is the one of the *sheir hamishtaleach*; the other is the red string (*sheni tola'at*) thrown into the pyre of the *parah adumah* — the Red Heifer. Both are called *lashon shel zehorit* in rabbinic tradition, and Rabbi Yitzchak notes that he received a tradition about these two strings: one requires a *shiur* (minimum size) and the other does not — but he does not know which is which.

---

## String Minimums

### Two String Types

Rabbi Yitzchak states: *shtei leshonot shamati* — I have received teachings about two strings. *Achat shel parah ve-achat shel sheir hamishtaleach* — one pertaining to the *parah adumah* and one pertaining to the *sheir hamishtaleach*. *Achat tzericha shiur ve-achat einah tzericha shiur* — one requires a minimum measure and the other does not. *Ve-lo yadana he minayhu* — and I do not know which is which.

### Scapegoat Minimum

Rav Yosef proposes a resolution. *Shel sheir hamishtaleach deva'i chaluka, ba'i shiur* — the string of the *sheir hamishtaleach* requires a minimum measure, because that string must be split in two. As we will see later in the tractate, the string is divided: one half remains tied between the horns of the goat as it is sent off, and the other half is kept at the cliff, enabling those present to observe when it turns white — the traditional sign that the Yom Kippur atonement has been accepted. Since the string must be divided and still function in both capacities, it requires a minimum length sufficient for this purpose.

### Parah No Minimum

By contrast, *shel parah delo va'i chaluka, lo ba'i shiur* — the string of the *parah adumah* does not need to be split and thus has no minimum measure. Any quantity, however small, satisfies the requirement of throwing *sheni tola'at* into the fire.

### Weight Question

Rami bar Chama challenges this conclusion. Even if the *parah adumah*'s string has no minimum *length*, it may still require minimum *weight*. The verse by the *parah adumah* uses the language *ve-hishlich* — "and he shall cast it" — which implies a throwing with force and trajectory. If the string is so light and wispy that it flutters rather than falls into the fire, can that be called *ve-hishlich*? There may therefore be a minimum *weight* requirement, even if not a minimum length.

### Tanna Debate

Rava responds: *koved tanna'i hi* — the question of whether the string must have weight sufficient to fall into the fire is itself a dispute among *tanna'im*. According to the *tanna* who holds that the string need not fall in a directed fashion, even a string that flutters into the fire is valid, and no minimum weight is required. According to that view, the *parah adumah* string truly has no minimum — neither of length nor of weight. The full range of opinions on this question, and how they map onto the various types of red strings involved in the Yom Kippur and *parah adumah* rituals, will be addressed in the discussion that follows.
