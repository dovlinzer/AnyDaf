# Nazir 55b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Metzora & Nazir

### Metzora Stages

The *mishna* on this *daf* discusses the status of a *nazir* who finds himself simultaneously subject to another form of ritual impurity or process that prevents him from completing his *nezirut*. Before engaging with the *Gemara's* analysis, it is worth clarifying the three distinct stages of a *metzora's* progression, since the *mishna's* ruling depends critically on understanding them.

When a person develops a suspicious skin condition, a *kohen* must examine it. If the *kohen* cannot render an immediate determination, he declares the individual a *musgar* — quarantined for observation — and returns after a week to reassess. During this first stage, the individual is *tamei* at a full level of *tumah*, yet the situation remains tentative: the *kohen* has not rendered a definitive verdict. If upon the second inspection the condition has not resolved, the *kohen* declares him a *muchlat* — a confirmed, definitive *metzora*. The term *muchlat* conveys the absolute, locked-in nature of the status: there is no longer any ambiguity about his condition. This period of confirmed *metzora* status, which continues until the condition clears, is referred to in the *mishna* as *yemei gamro*. During *yemei gamro* the *metzora* is sent outside the camp entirely.

Once the condition clears, the *kohen* examines the individual and initiates the *tahara* process. On the day the condition is found to have healed, the *kohen* performs the bird ritual — taking two living, *tahor* birds, cedar wood, crimson thread, and hyssop; slaughtering one bird over an earthenware vessel containing spring water; and sprinkling the blood seven times upon the person being purified. The individual then shaves all his hair, immerses in a *mikveh*, and is permitted to re-enter the camp, but must remain outside his tent for seven days. This seven-day counting period that follows the initial ritual is called *yemei sifro*. On the seventh day of *yemei sifro*, he shaves all his hair a second time, immerses again, and on the eighth day brings his *korbanot*. Only then is he fully *tahor*.

The structure, then, is as follows: *musgar* — the first tentative quarantine stage; *muchlat* and *yemei gamro* — the confirmed indefinite period of *metzora* status until the condition clears; and *yemei sifro* — the seven-day counting period following the initial *tahara* ritual, culminating in a second shaving and the offering of *korbanot*.

### Mishna Rule

Our *mishna* establishes that during *yemei gamro* and *yemei sifro*, the days do not count toward a *nazir's* tally of *nezirut*, though they do not destroy (*soter*) the days already counted beforehand. This parallels the rule for a *nazir* who is *tamei meis* in a way that does not trigger *giluch* and *korban* — such as one who contracted a rabbinic-level *tumas meis*, or who touched a *golel*, *dofek*, or *kelim* that had been in contact with a corpse. In those cases, the days of *tumah* do not count, but neither do they nullify the earlier days.

By contrast, if the *metzora's* condition was caught at the *musgar* stage and cleared without advancing to *muchlat*, the episode is treated like a *zav* or *zava* — it has no effect whatsoever on the *nazirut*. Similarly, a *nazir* who becomes a *zav* or *zava* experiences no interruption to his *nezirut*. The *mishna's* ruling of *ein olim lo* — the days do not count — applies specifically to *yemei gamro* and *yemei sifro*, the stages characterized by *giluach* and *korban*.

## Rav Chisda

### Rav Chisda's Theory

The *Gemara* records the opinion of Rav Chisda, who distinguishes between a short and a long *nezirut*: *lo shanu ela binezirut mu'etet, aval binezirut merubah misak nami salikan lei* — the rule that those days do not count was stated only regarding a short *nezirut*, but in the case of a long *nezirut*, those days do in fact count. This is a striking position, because it essentially challenges the plain reading of the *mishna*.

Rav Chisda's reasoning is rooted in the requirement of *gidul se'ar* — thirty days of hair growth — that must precede the final *giluach* of a *nazir tahor*. A *nazir* who goes through the *metzora* process will have his hair shaved not once but twice before emerging from that process. By the time he is *tahor*, his head is bare. He will therefore require a full thirty days of hair growth regardless of how many days he counted beforehand. In a standard thirty-day *nezirut*, then, this practical reality means that the days spent as a *metzora* cannot contribute anything meaningful — even if they "count" in principle, you will still need a full thirty days at the end. Hence the *mishna's* language of *ein olim lo* is, in Rav Chisda's view, not a statement of fundamental law but a practical consequence of the *gidul se'ar* requirement.

For a long *nezirut*, however — say, one of fifty or one hundred days — if a *nazir* had already completed twenty days before becoming a *metzora*, and the *metzora* process occupied an additional stretch of time, he may still have enough days remaining that, even accounting for thirty days of *gidul se'ar* at the end, those middle days can effectively be credited. In such a case, Rav Chisda holds that they fundamentally do count, and the *mishna* simply does not address this scenario.

### Rav Mesharsheya

Rav Mesharsheya raises a challenge to Rav Chisda's position. He observes that the *mishna* states *ve'eino mevatel bahen et hakodmin* — these days do not destroy the earlier ones. He argues: if the *mishna* were speaking of a short *nezirut*, then it should have said the opposite — that the earlier days *are* destroyed — because in practice, a *nazir* with a thirty-day *nezirut* who undergoes this entire process and has his hair shaved twice will need the full thirty days thereafter regardless of what he accumulated beforehand. Saying merely that the middle days *do not count* without nullifying the earlier ones presupposes a scenario where one already has days that are worth preserving. That is only meaningful, Rav Mesharsheya argues, in the context of a long *nezirut* — *nezirut merubah* — yet even there, the *mishna* rules *ein olim lo*. This directly contradicts Rav Chisda, who held that in a *nezirut merubah* the days should count.

### Fifty-Day Case

Rav Chisda responds by proposing a specific scenario that fits the *mishna's* language of neither *soter* nor *oleh*: a fifty-day *nezirut* in which the *nazir* had completed twenty days before becoming a *metzora*, then undergoes the *metzora* process — involving two shavings — and afterward must count another thirty days of *gidul se'ar* before the final *giluach*. In this case, the twenty days beforehand are preserved and not destroyed, but the middle days of the *metzora* process cannot be used either, because the *nazir* will need those thirty days at the end regardless. This accounts for the *mishna's* formulation.

Rav Chisda concedes that in a truly long *nezirut* — one where enough days remain that the middle days could be credited and still leave thirty days for *gidul se'ar* at the end — those middle days would in principle count. He maintains that the *mishna's ein olim lo* is not a fundamental halakhic statement but rather a practical limitation born of the *gidul se'ar* requirement. The rest of the *Gemara* will proceed to demonstrate that this position is untenable, and that the days fundamentally do not count.

## Double Safek

### Double Safek Setup

The *Gemara* now raises a complex case: a *nazir* who may have become *tamei meis* in a way that would require *giluach* and *korban* — that is, a *tumah* of the *soter* variety — and who is also a *muchlat b'safek*, a doubtful confirmed *metzora*. The intersection of these two *safekot* creates a situation where multiple *giluchim* may be required under different conditions, but performing any one of them without justification could violate the *nezirut* itself.

One might ask how a person can be a *muchlat b'safek*, since the *halacha* of *tzaraas* is that the status of *metzora* is determined entirely by the *kohen's* declaration — once the *kohen* renders a ruling according to the established procedures, the status is definitive. The *rishonim* acknowledge this difficulty and note that a precise scenario is hard to identify; one possible construction is that there was doubt about the *kohen's* own qualifications. Regardless of the exact framing, the *Gemara* treats the double *safek* as a valid case and proceeds to work through its consequences.

### Sixty to 120 Days

The *Gemara* rules that such a *nazir* may eat *kodshim* only after sixty days, and may drink wine and contract *tumas meis* — that is, fully complete his *nezirut* — only after one hundred and twenty days.

The logic is as follows. In order to eat *kodshim*, the *nazir* need not yet fully resolve his *nazir tamei* status — from a standpoint of *tumah v'taharah*, once he has undergone the sprinkling of *parah adumah* on days three and seven, he is fully *tahor* and may eat *kodashim* even without yet bringing his *korbanot* of *nezirut*. What must be resolved first is his possible *metzora* status, since a *metzora* cannot eat *kodshim*. It takes sixty days to exit the *metzora* status in light of the *safek*, for reasons the *Gemara* will explain. Completing the *nezirut* entirely, however — which requires resolving the *nazir tamei* and *nazir tahor* obligations — takes a full one hundred and twenty days.

### Conditional Korbanos

The *Gemara* and *Tosafot* work through the mechanics in considerable detail. The procedure unfolds across multiple thirty-day intervals, with each *giluach* separated by thirty days because of the need for *gidul se'ar*.

Initially, the individual cannot shave immediately upon the clearing of the condition, because he may be a *nazir tahor* — and a *nazir tahor* may not have his hair cut before the completion of his *nezirut*. Therefore one must wait thirty days before performing the first *giluach*, ensuring that even if he were a *nazir tahor*, the minimal period required has elapsed. At that point, a conditional *korban olah* is brought: if he is a *nazir tahor*, this serves as his *korban* for *nezirut*; if not, it is a voluntary *nedava*. The *halacha* that *gilach al echad min hadamim yatza* — even a single *korban* can serve *bedi'eved* to complete the *giluach* of a *nazir tahor* — allows this to work. After thirty days, the first *giluach* is performed, covering both the possible *metzora giluach* and the possible *nazir tahor* or *nazir tamei giluach* as applicable.

However, the matter is not resolved. One cannot proceed to the second *metzora giluach* — which would normally take place seven days after the first — because the *nazir* may actually have been a *nazir tamei* rather than a *nazir tahor*. If so, the first *giluach* served only the *nazir tamei* role, and thirty more days are needed before the second *giluach* can take place for the *nazir tahor*. Thus a second *giluach* with its own conditional *korban* occurs at day sixty.

After sixty days and two *giluchim*, the *metzora* process — if it was ever real — is fully complete, and the individual can eat *kodshim*. But the *nezirut* is not yet done. If the individual was actually both a *metzora* and a *nazir tamei*, then the *halacha* is that the *giluach* of a *metzora* does not count toward the *giluach* of a *nazir*; they cannot serve double duty. This means the first sixty days of *giluachim* may not have counted for the *nazir* at all in such a scenario. The individual must therefore wait another thirty days and perform a third *giluach*, and then yet another thirty days with a fourth *giluach*, to cover all possible configurations. In each interval, conditional *korbanot* are brought with the appropriate *tnai*.

The key principle underlying this entire structure, and the one the *Gemara* intends to prove through it, is *ein olim lo* — the days of *yemei gamro* and *yemei sifro* do not count toward the *nezirut*. The fact that the concluding period requires not a mere thirty days but a full doubling of the *nezirut* period demonstrates that those middle days contribute nothing. This point is pressed against Rav Chisda's position in the next section.

## Braita Proof

### Rav Ashi's Query

The *Gemara* introduces a further proof — and, more fundamentally, the textual source for the entire principle — when Rav Ashi poses the question: *ein li ela yemei tumah she'ein olim lo min haminyan* — I know only that the days of *tumah* do not count; *yemei chaluto minayin* — what is the source that the days of *yemei gamro* do not count?

It is important to clarify what is meant here. The baseline case that does not count is a full *tumas meis* with *giluch* — and that case is actually *soter*, not merely *ein olim lo*. The question Rav Ashi is asking is grounded in a *Midrash Halacha* from the *Sifrei*: given that we have a source for the *nazir tamei* not counting days, what is the derivation that the *metzora's* days in *yemei gamro* also do not count?

### Kal Vachomer

A *kal vachomer* is proposed. A *nazir* who accepts *nezirut* upon himself while standing in a graveyard — a full *tamei meis* condition — does not need to perform the *giluach* and *korban* of a *nazir tamei*, because he never transitioned from *tahor* to *tamei* during the *nezirut*. Crucially, his hair at that point is preserved: it will not need to be shaved as a result of his *tumah*, and it remains available for the final *giluach* of his *nezirut taharah*. Yet even this person, whose hair is intact and whose case appears less disruptive than a *metzora's*, has his days of *tumah* not count. How much more so, then, should the days of a *nazir muchlat* not count — for the *metzora's* hair will inevitably be shaved off before the *nezirut* is complete, representing a direct contradiction to the integrity of the *nezirut*.

The argument runs: *kal vachomer* — just as the *nazir bakever* whose hair is *raui letiglachat nezirut* (fit for the final shaving of *nezirut*) nonetheless has his days not count, the *muchlat* whose hair is *she'ein saro raui letiglachat nezirut* (not fit, because it will be shaved off prematurely) should *a fortiori* have his days not count.

This *kal vachomer* is then challenged: *tumas meis* is weightier because it destroys the earlier days — that is why it doesn't count. But *yemei gamro* does not destroy the earlier days, suggesting it is less disruptive and perhaps its days should count. The *braita* responds: *lo, kal vachomer* — and reframes the *kal vachomer* to rest on the comparison of *giluach* and *korban*.

### Braita Categories

A *braita* then organizes the categories systematically. *Yemei gamro* and *yemei sifro* — the periods characterized by *giluach* and *korban* obligation — do not count toward the *nezirut*. The analogy to *nazir tamei* holds: both involve shaving and *korban*, and in both cases those days are *ein olim lo min haminyan*.

By contrast, the days of a *zav*, a *zava*, and the *musgar* stage of a *metzora* all count toward the *nezirut*. The distinguishing criterion is the absence of *giluach* and *korban*. A *zav* and *zava* do bring a *korban* at the conclusion of their status, but they do not shave. The *musgar* has neither *giluach* nor *korban* — if the condition clears at that stage, he simply immerses in a *mikveh* and is *tahor*. Therefore, *harei elu olim lo* — those days count.

The *braita* addresses the question of whether *yemei hasgiro* — the *musgar* stage — should also not count, given that it too involves the same level of *tumah* as *yemei gamro*. The answer is decisive: it is the *giluach* and *korban* that render *yemei gamro* and *yemei sifro* analogous to *nazir tamei* and generate the rule of *ein olim lo*. Without them, there is no basis to exclude those days.

The *braita* thus provides the *midrash halacha* foundation for the *mishna's* rule: *yemei sifro viyemei gamro ein olim lo min haminyan* — because they bear the halakhic signature of *giluach* and *korban* that marks days of significant ritual disruption to the *nezirut*.

### Fundamental Non-Counting

With the *braita* in hand, the *Gemara* now delivers its decisive refutation of Rav Chisda. The *braita* had considered the possibility that *yemei gamro* might be *oleh min haminyan*. At what point would such a position be coherent? Clearly not in the case of a *nezirut mu'etet* — a standard thirty-day *nezirut* — because there one would need thirty days of *gidul se'ar* regardless, and the days could never practically count. The only scenario in which one could meaningfully *consider* those days as counting is in the case of a *nezirut merubah*, where there would be more than thirty days remaining and the *gidul se'ar* requirement could still be satisfied at the end.

Yet the *braita* entertains precisely this scenario and then concludes: *ein olim lo min haminyan* — they do not count. *Alma lo salik leh* — the *braita's* conclusion is that fundamentally, as a matter of principle, those days do not count. This has nothing to do with *gidul se'ar*. The *braita* is operating in a *nezirut merubah* context — the very scenario Rav Chisda claimed would allow the days to be credited — and rules that they do not count regardless. *Shema mina*: this is a conclusive proof that the *halacha* of *ein olim lo* is not a practical consequence of the *gidul se'ar* requirement, as Rav Chisda maintained, but a fundamental halakhic principle in its own right.
