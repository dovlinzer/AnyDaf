# Zevachim 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ra'ui Lepetach Ohel Moed

### Opening & Review

Daf 109 continues the discussion of when a person is liable for offering *korbanot* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. The previous daf addressed questions concerning which *avodot* trigger liability — *shechitah* and *ha'alah* are explicit in the verse, and the Gemara added *zrikat hadam* as well. There was also discussion about whether one who slaughters a *korban* for the sake of the meat, rather than as an act of divine offering, is nonetheless liable — a genuine *chidush*, given that the burning must be expressly for God. The present daf turns to the specifics of which types of *korbanot* fall within the scope of this prohibition.

### Ra'ui Principle

The Mishnah opens with the phrase *echad kodshim kesherim ve-echad kodshim pesulim* — liability applies whether the offering is valid or invalid. But not every category of *pasul* is included. The governing principle is *ra'ui lepetach ohel moed*: the offering must be of a type that is fit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. This is derived from the verse *el petach ohel moed lo hevi'o* — "he did not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Vayikra 17:4). If someone were to sanctify a duck or a deer — animals that are categorically ineligible as *korbanot* — and were to slaughter or burn them outside, he would not be liable, since these animals are not *ra'ui lepetach ohel moed*. The *chiyuv kares* for *shichutei chutz* or *ha'ala'as chutz* requires that the act be performed upon something that could, in principle, have been brought to the *Mikdash*.

### Psulan Bakodesh

The Mishnah then teaches *shehayah psulan bakodesh* — that the category of *pasul* offerings which does generate liability is specifically those whose disqualification arose within the sacred precincts, *psulan bakodesh*. The underlying principle is that if such an offering were placed on the *mizbeach*, it would remain there and would not be removed. This "stays-on-the-altar" standard is what qualifies them as sufficiently *ra'ui lepetach ohel moed* to generate liability when offered outside. The most intuitive understanding of the phrase is that the *psul* occurred after the animal entered the *Beit HaMikdash* — for instance, if the *shechitah* was performed *shelo lishmah*, if the blood was received by a *zar*, if the blood was taken outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, and so on.

### Baal Mum Exception

A *baal mum* — a blemished animal — does not qualify as *psulan bakodesh*, even if the blemish was acquired inside the *Beit HaMikdash* (say, if someone scratched the animal after it entered). The reason is that a blemish is not a *psul* generated by some deficiency in the ritual process within the *Mikdash*; it is entirely circumstantial, not arising from anything that went wrong in the performance of the *avodah*. Consequently, one who slaughters or burns a *baal mum* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* is not liable for *kares* for *shichutei chutz* or *ha'ala'as chutz*.

The case of *zarut* — a *zar* performing the *avodah* — is treated differently, because *zarut* was *hutrah b'bamah*: performing the *avodah* as a non-*kohen* was entirely permitted on a private *bamah*. So *zarut* does not generate a prior *psul* that would raise these questions.

## Ha'alah & Items

### Baraita Expansion

The Mishnah then states: *hamaleh kezayit min ha'olah umin ha'imurin bachutz chayav* — one who burns a *kezayit* from the *olah* or from the *imurim* outside is liable. The *baraita* begins by noting that the verse says *asher ya'aleh olah o zavach* — "who brings up an *olah* or a sacrifice" (Vayikra 17:8). One might think liability is limited to an *olah*. How do we know to include the *imurim* of an *asham*, the *imurim* of a *chatat*, the *imurim* of *kodshei kodashim*, and the *imurim* of *kodashim kalim*? The word *zavach* in the verse expands the prohibition. The implicit logic is that the *zavach* is read in parallel to the *olah*: just as an *olah* is paradigmatically the portion placed entirely on the *mizbeach*, so too with a *zevach* — only that portion which would normally ascend to the *mizbeach* is included. One is liable for the *imurim* of the other *korbanot*, but not for their meat.

### Non-Meat Items

The *baraita* then asks how we know to include non-animal items. *Minayin l'rabos hakometz vehalevonah* — how do we include the *kometz*, the handful of flour taken from the *mincha*, and the *levonah*, the frankincense that accompanies the *mincha*? Both of these are burnt on the *mizbeach*. The *baraita* continues: *vehaketores* — the daily *ketores* burnt on the golden inner *mizbeach* twice each day — and *minchas kohanim* and *minchas kohen mashiach*, the *minchah* offerings of *kohanim* that are burnt entirely. All of these are burnt on the altar, and the *baraita* establishes that they fall under the prohibition of *ha'alah* outside.

### Libations

The *baraita* then introduces something genuinely novel: *vehamenasech sheloshet lugin yayin vesheloshet lugin mayim* — one who pours a libation of three *lugin* of wine or three *lugin* of water. This is not a burning at all; it is a *nisuch*, a libation. Yet it falls within the scope of *ha'alah*. The three *lugin* of wine is the minimum quantity brought as a wine libation with a *korban*; the three *lugin* of water refers to the water libation performed during *Sukkos* as part of *nisuch hamayim b'chag*.

Rashi notes that for all of these items — wine, *mincha*, and water alike — it is not sufficient that one simply takes them from home and pours them. Just as one who slaughters an unsanctified animal outside is not liable, so too here the item must be sanctified. Rashi specifies regarding the water: *be-chag ka'amar umekudashim l'chag* — the law applies on *Sukkos*, and the water must have been sanctified for the *nisuch hamayim b'chag*. The principle of *ra'ui lepetach ohel moed* must be satisfied: only sanctified items, appropriate to their specific sacred context, are included.

The source for this expansion is the verse *v'el petach ohel moed lo yevienu* — "and he did not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" — from which we derive: *kol haba lepetach ohel moed chayavin alav bachutz* — anything that would be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, one is liable for performing outside. The scope of *ha'alah* extends beyond animals to any item that ascends to the *mizbeach*, whether by fire or by libation.

### Psulan Extension

Having established the scope of valid items, the *baraita* turns to the *pesulim*. *V'ein li ela kodashim kesherim minayin lerabos pesulim* — we have so far only established liability for valid offerings; how do we know to include certain *pesulim*? The *baraita* enumerates the qualifying categories of *psulan bakodesh*: *kegon halan* — meat or *imurim* that remained overnight (*nosar*); *vehayotzei* — portions that were taken outside the *Beit HaMikdash*; *vehatamai* — items that became ritually impure; those involving thoughts of *pigul*; cases where *pesulim* or a *zar* received or threw the blood; blood thrown in the wrong location — above or below the prescribed demarcation; blood of inner-altar offerings placed on the outer altar or vice versa; and a *pesach* or *chatat* slaughtered *shelo lishman* on the third day. All of these are *psulan bakodesh* — their *psul* arose from a defect in the ritual process itself.

How do we know these generate liability when burned outside? The verse says *lo yevienu la'asos* — "he shall not bring it to do." Rashi points out that *la'asos* implies an active role: these items, if found on the *mizbeach*, are not merely left in place — they are actively offered. One turns them on the fire, completes the burning. The word *la'asos* thus establishes that even these disqualified items retain a "doing" quality when on the *mizbeach*, and therefore if one performs this act outside, he is liable.

## Meat & Imurim

### Olah vs Shelamim

The Mishnah teaches: *hamaleh kezayit min ha'olah umin ha'imurin bachutz chayav* — if one burns a *kezayit* consisting of half a *kezayit* of the meat of an *olah* and half a *kezayit* of its *imurim*, he is liable, because both portions are burnt on the *mizbeach* and thus combine. This implicitly excludes the *shelamim*: a half *kezayit* of the meat of a *shelamim* and a half *kezayit* of its *imurim* do not combine, because one is not liable for burning the meat of a *shelamim* outside — the meat of a *shelamim* is not placed on the *mizbeach* in the *Beit HaMikdash*, so performing that act outside generates no liability.

The *baraita* then states: *olah ve'emureha mitztarfin lekezayit leha'alotan bachutz ulchayev aleihen mishum pigul notar vetamei* — an *olah* and its *imurim* combine to a *kezayit* for the purposes of *ha'ala'as chutz*, and also to generate liability for the prohibitions of *pigul*, *notar*, and *tamei*. The implication is that this combining applies to an *olah* and its *imurim* but not to a *shelamim* and its *imurim*.

### Pigul Question

The Gemara raises an objection. Granted that the meat of a *shelamim* does not combine with its *imurim* for *ha'alah* — the meat is never burnt on the *mizbeach*, so there is no act of *ha'alah* to speak of. But for *pigul*, *notar*, and *tamei*, which are prohibitions applicable to the entire *korban*, why shouldn't the meat and the *imurim* of a *shelamim* combine? Furthermore, there is an explicit Mishnah elsewhere stating *kol hapigulin mitztarfin v'chol hanos'arin mitztarfin* — all *pigul* items combine with one another and all *notar* items combine — which would seemingly permit even a half *kezayit* of *shelamim* meat and a half *kezayit* of *shelamim imurim* to combine for the prohibition of *pigul* or *notar*. This generates a direct contradiction: *kashya pigul apigul, kashya notar anotar*.

### Machshevet Pigul

The Gemara resolves the *pigul* contradiction with an elegant distinction. The *baraita* that implies no combining for a *shelamim* is referring to *machshevet pigul* — the disqualifying intention formed during the *shechitah* that the offering will be eaten or burnt outside its allotted time. In such a case, the entire animal is rendered *pigul*, and if one then eats a half *kezayit* of the meat and a half *kezayit* of the *imurim*, he is liable — he has consumed a full *kezayit* of *pigul* matter. That is the ruling of the Mishnah that says *kol hapigulin mitztarfin*.

What the *baraita* is addressing is a different scenario entirely: one formed the intention during *shechitah* to eat half a *kezayit* of the *shelamim* meat in three days and to burn half a *kezayit* of the *imurim* in three days. These two half-*kezayitim* do not combine to constitute a single *machshevet pigul*, because eating by a person (*achilas adam*) and burning on the *mizbeach* (*achilas mizbeach*) are categorically distinct acts, and a *machshevah* about one does not combine with a *machshevah* about the other. By contrast, if it were an *olah*, and one formed the intention to burn half a *kezayit* of the meat and half a *kezayit* of the *imurim* outside their time, these would combine — because both are acts of *achilas mizbeach*, burning on the altar. This elegantly parallels the rule of *ha'ala'as chutz*: in both cases, the *olah* and its *imurim* combine precisely because both are destined for the *mizbeach*, while for a *shelamim*, the meat and the *imurim* serve different functions and do not combine.

## Notar & Blood

### Notar Contradiction

The *pigul* question having been resolved, the *notar* contradiction remains. The Gemara's answer is as follows. The Mishnah that teaches *kol hanos'arin mitztarfin* — all *notar* items combine — refers to a case where the animal was already rendered *notar* and one ate half a *kezayit* of the meat and half a *kezayit* of the *imurim*. In that case they combine, since both are *notar* and the prohibition applies to the entire *korban*. What the *baraita* means when it implies that the meat and *imurim* of a *shelamim* do not combine for *notar* is a different scenario: *k'an bishenisosru ad shelo nizrak hadam* — where they became "left over" before the blood was thrown. That is: the animal was slaughtered, and somehow the bulk of the meat and *imurim* were lost or destroyed, leaving only half a *kezayit* of meat and half a *kezayit* of *imurim*, and the blood has not yet been thrown. The question now is whether the *kohen* may still throw the blood.

### Rabbi Yehoshua View

At this point the Gemara introduces the view of Rabbi Yehoshua: *kol hazevachim shebaTorahshenishta'yer bahen kezayit basar o kezayit chelev zorek es hadam* — for any sacrifice in the Torah, if at least a *kezayit* of meat or a *kezayit* of *chelev* (*imurim*) remains, one throws the blood. The basis is the principle, derived from various verses, that throwing the blood is only meaningful when it serves as a *matir* — permitting either the eating of the meat or the burning of the *imurim* on the *mizbeach*. If there is nothing left of substance — no *kezayit* of meat to eat and no *kezayit* of *chelev* to burn — the blood throwing serves no purpose and should not be done.

### Olah Blood Allowance

The Mishnah continues: *chatzi zayit basar v'chatzi zayit chelev eino zorek es hadam* — if only half a *kezayit* of meat and half a *kezayit* of *chelev* remain (in the context of a *shelamim*), one does not throw the blood. There is no full *kezayit* of anything that the blood throwing would permit. However, *be'olah chatzi zayit basar v'chatzi zayit chelev zorek es hadam mipnei shekulo kalil* — in the case of an *olah*, one does throw the blood even with only half a *kezayit* of each, because the entire animal is burnt. Both the meat and the *imurim* of an *olah* are destined for the *mizbeach* and belong to the same category — *achilas mizbeach* — so together they constitute a full *kezayit* of something the blood throwing renders permitted.

This is precisely parallel to everything discussed above. The three areas in which an *olah*'s meat and *imurim* combine — *ha'ala'as chutz*, *machshevet pigul*, and the question of throwing blood — are all rooted in the same principle: since both the meat and the *imurim* of an *olah* are burned on the *mizbeach*, they form a unified category. For a *shelamim*, they do not: the meat is for human consumption and the *imurim* are for the *mizbeach*, and therefore they never combine in any of these three contexts.

The *baraita* adds: *umincha afilu kula kayemes lo yizrok* — if only the *mincha* that accompanied the sacrifice remains (because all the meat and *imurim* were lost), one does not throw the blood even though there is something left to be done with. As Rav Papa explains, *mincha* refers here to the *minchas nesachim* that accompanies a *korban* — but this is not the sacrifice itself. Throwing the blood must function as a *matir* for the animal's own meat or *imurim*; the *mincha* that accompanies it is a separate item and does not fulfill that role.

### Rashi Resolution

The *baraita* stated that an *olah* and its *imurim* combine *lechayev aleihen mishum notar* — to generate liability for *notar*. On the analysis now developed, what this means is not simply that eating them generates *notar* liability. Rather, it means the following: if one had half a *kezayit* of *olah* meat and half a *kezayit* of *imurim* remaining, and went ahead and threw the blood, then the blood-throwing was *permitted* — since for an *olah* those half-*kezayitim* combine. Because the blood throwing was permitted and valid, it changes the halachic status of the remaining meat, elevating it so that it can subsequently become *notar* and generate liability. For a *shelamim*, by contrast, throwing the blood in such a circumstance was not legitimate — because a half *kezayit* of meat and a half *kezayit* of *imurim* do not combine. Even if the blood was thrown and one is perhaps *yotzei b'di'avad*, since the throwing was not properly connected to the meat, it does not change the meat's status. Therefore, if the meat subsequently became *notar* and was eaten, one is not liable — because the blood throwing never meaningfully "activated" the meat. This is Rashi's resolution: the license to throw the blood, when present, changes the meat's status in ways that have downstream consequences for *notar* and *tamei* liability.

## Non-Meat Shiur

### Burnt Items

The Mishnah then addresses non-meat items: *hakometz vehalevonah vehaketores uminchas kohanim uminchas kohen mashiach uminchas nesachim* — the *kometz*, the *levonah*, the *ketores*, the *minchah* of *kohanim*, the *minchah* of the *kohen mashiach*, and the *minchah* of libations. For all of these, if one burns a *kezayit* of any of them outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, he is liable, in accordance with the view of the *tanna kamma*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Full

Rabbi Eliezer dissents: *poter ad sheyakriv es kulan* — he exempts unless the entire amount is burned. The question naturally arises: why does Rabbi Eliezer agree that by the meat of an *olah* one is liable for a *kezayit* alone, yet by the *kometz* of a *mincha* he requires the full amount?

The answer lies in the concept of *matir*. The *kometz* is the *matir* for the remainder of the *mincha* — it is the act by which the *shirayim* are permitted to the *kohanim*. As a *matir*, it only functions when the full *kometz* is brought; a partial offering does not discharge the function. For the meat of an *olah*, by contrast, there is no separate *matir* — each *kezayit* of meat constitutes an independent meaningful act of placing it on the *mizbeach*. But for items that function as *matirim*, the entire amount must be brought to constitute a meaningful halachic act.

### Act vs Matir

Rabbi Eliezer therefore draws this distinction: where one is performing an act of *ha'alah* that functions as a *matir*, the only meaningful act is the complete one, and one is liable only for performing the complete act outside. Where one is performing *ha'alah* that is simply an act of burning — not tied to unlocking anything else — each *kezayit* is independently meaningful, and liability attaches to each *kezayit*.

However, Rabbi Eliezer agrees to the following: *vechulan shehikriv bifnim veshayar mehem kezayit vehikrivan bachutz chayav* — if one burned the bulk of the *kometz* or *ketores* inside and left over at least a *kezayit*, which he then burned outside, he is liable. Why? Because that remaining *kezayit* was, at that point, the last piece needed to complete the *matir* function. Had it been done inside, it would have been the culminating act that validated the *korban*. The remaining *kezayit* thus carried the full weight of the *matir* function, and performing it outside generates liability. Conversely, if even a minute quantity was missing from the offering — *vechulan shechosru kol shehu vehikrivan bachutz patur* — one is exempt, because such a diminished offering would not have been a valid *hakravah* inside the *Beit HaMikdash*, and what would not be valid inside does not generate liability when done outside.

## Ketores Debate

### Ketores Baraita

A *baraita* is now cited: *hamaktir kezayit bachutz chayav* — one who burns a *kezayit* of *ketores* outside is liable. *Chatzi pras bifnim patur* — if inside the *Beit HaMikdash* half the standard amount (i.e., less than the full *pras*) was burned, one is exempt. On first reading, this appears to generate a contradiction: if a *kezayit* outside makes one liable, why is a *zar* who brings less than the full amount inside exempt?

### Contradiction Noted

The apparent reading of *patur* would be *patur zar* — a non-*kohen* who burns less than the full amount inside is exempt from penalty. But this is strange: if the minimum for liability outside is a *kezayit*, why would a *zar* burning a *kezayit* inside be exempt? The *kezayit* is presumably meaningful enough to generate liability.

### Patur Tzibur

Rabbi Zeira, citing Rav Chisda citing Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba citing Rav, resolves this by reinterpreting *patur*: *mai patur — patur tzibur* — "exempt" here does not mean the *zar* is exempt from punishment; it means the *tzibur* has discharged its obligation. In other words, *chatzi pras bifnim patur* means: if only half the prescribed amount was burned inside, the community has nonetheless fulfilled its *mitzvah*. Thus the *baraita* is saying that one is liable outside for a *kezayit*, and one is *yotzei* inside with a *kezayit* as well.

This is a striking reading, and it raises a further question addressed by Tosafot: what exactly is the position of the *tanna kamma* who disagrees with Rabbi Eliezer? There are two possible ways to understand the *tanna kamma*. The simpler reading would be that he agrees with Rabbi Eliezer that the full amount is needed to be *yotzei* inside — the *ketores* must be complete for the *mitzvah* to be fulfilled — yet he holds that a *kezayit* is sufficient to generate liability when burned outside, since it constitutes a meaningful act even without fulfilling the *matir* function. This is certainly how the *tanna kamma* operates with respect to the *kometz* of a *mincha*, where all agree the full *kometz* is required inside, yet a *kezayit* outside triggers liability. The Gemara, however, shifts in its understanding when it comes to *ketores*: it reads the *tanna kamma* as maintaining that a *kezayit* of *ketores* is sufficient to be *yotzei* inside as well, and therefore one is liable for a *kezayit* outside.

### Daily Ketores

Rava articulates the consensus underlying this reading: *behaktara deheichal de'chulei alma lo peligi* — regarding the daily *ketores* performed in the *Heichal*, everybody agrees that one is *yotzei* with a *kezayit*, and therefore everybody agrees one is liable for a *kezayit* outside. The reason is that the Torah does not specify a precise *shiur* for the daily *ketores*; the Rabbis established the amount of a *pras* in the morning and a *pras* in the afternoon, but *mid'oraisa* a *kezayit* suffices. The debate in the Mishnah, accordingly, does not concern the daily *ketores* at all — both Rabbi Eliezer and the *tanna kamma* agree that one is liable for a *kezayit* of the daily *ketores* burned outside. The disagreement, on this reading, concerns the *ketores* of Yom Kippur brought into the *Kodesh Kodashim*.

## Yom Kippur Ketores

### Abaye Resolution

Abaye, however, rejects this framing. The Torah states *v'hayesah zos lachem le'chukkas olam* specifically in the context of the Yom Kippur *avodah* in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, signaling that the *haktarah* inside there is defined by the Torah as a *chok* — a fixed requirement. And the Torah specifies *melo chofnav* — a full double handful — for the Yom Kippur *ketores*. There is no room to say that Yom Kippur *ketores* is *yotzei* with a *kezayit* inside; the requirement is incontrovertibly *melo chofnav*. Therefore the debate cannot be about Yom Kippur.

Abaye offers his own resolution: *behaktaras bifnim chulei alma lo peligi* — regarding the *ketores* of Yom Kippur burned in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, everybody agrees you need *melo chofnav* inside, and therefore everybody agrees that one is liable outside only for *melo chofnav*. *Ki peligi behaktaras chutz* — the debate is about what one is liable for when burning outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, specifically in connection with the daily *ketores*. Since everybody agrees one is *yotzei* with a *kezayit* for the daily *ketores* inside, everybody agrees one is liable for a *kezayit* of daily *ketores* burned outside. The debate is whether, given that the *tanna kamma* holds one is liable for a *kezayit* of daily *ketores* burned outside, he also says one is liable for a *kezayit* of Yom Kippur *ketores* burned outside — reasoning that the outside is linked to what makes one *yotzei* for the corresponding inside *avodah*. Rabbi Eliezer rejects this linkage: since the inside *avodah* on Yom Kippur requires *melo chofnav*, one is liable outside for Yom Kippur *ketores* only if the full *melo chofnav* is burned.

### Daily Debate

The structure of the debate can be mapped as follows. For the Yom Kippur *ketores*, everyone agrees: *melo chofnav* is required inside, and *melo chofnav* is required to generate liability outside. For the daily *ketores*, everyone agrees: a *kezayit* is sufficient inside, and a *kezayit* generates liability outside. The debate is about one specific case: Yom Kippur *ketores* burned outside. The *tanna kamma* argues that since the daily *ketores* generates liability for a *kezayit* outside, and the Yom Kippur *ketores* is still *ketores*, one is liable for a *kezayit* of Yom Kippur *ketores* burned outside as well, drawing a link between the two categories of *ketores*. Rabbi Eliezer refuses this link: since the inside Yom Kippur *avodah* requires *melo chofnav*, the outside requires *melo chofnav* as well.

### Rava Rejection

Rava, however, rejects Abaye's framework entirely. His objection is methodological: *hashta chutz m'chutz lo yalfei rabbanan pnim m'chutz miba'ei* — we do not apply *shiurim* from one category to another even within the category of *ha'ala'as chutz* itself; how much more so can we not apply the *shiur* of one type of *ketores* to another type? The proof is from the *baraita*: *yachol hama'aleh pachos mikezayit kometz... hayei chayav?* — perhaps one who burns less than a *kezayit* of *kometz* or less than three *lugin* of wine outside would be liable? The answer is no — *al hashalem hu chayav ve'eino chayav al hachaser* — one is liable only for the complete minimum amount relevant to that specific offering. Three *lugin* of wine is many times a *kezayit* in volume, yet we do not say that since one is liable for a *kezayit* of meat outside, one should therefore be liable for a *kezayit* of wine outside. Each category carries its own *shiur*. By the same logic, we cannot say that because one is liable for a *kezayit* of daily *ketores* outside, one is therefore liable for a *kezayit* of Yom Kippur *ketores* outside. The categories are distinct, and *shiurim* do not transfer across them.

## Kli Shares Solution

### Vessel Interpretation

Having rejected Abaye's framework, Rava proposes his own solution to the underlying difficulty. The question remains: Rabbi Eliezer agrees that one is *yotzei* with a *kezayit* of daily *ketores* inside, and therefore agrees one is liable for a *kezayit* outside. What then is the subject of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *tanna kamma*? Rava answers: *kegon dikav'inhu shenei chatzi pras bemana* — the case is where one placed two portions of *ketores*, each a half *pras*, into a single *kli shareis* together.

The scenario is as follows. A *kohen* prepares to offer the daily *ketores*. He places his material in the *kli shareis* — let us say two *kezayitim*, or two half-*prasim* — together in a single vessel. Had he only placed one *kezayit* in the vessel, everyone would agree he is *yotzei* if he burns it inside, and liable if he burns it outside. But because he placed more than a *kezayit* together in a single *kli shareis*, a question arises as to whether the vessel configuration changes the *shiur*.

### Chachamim View

The *chachamim* hold that placing multiple *kezayitim* together in a *kli shareis* is not halachically significant with respect to the minimum *shiur*. One is still *yotzei* if one burns only a *kezayit*, even when more was placed in the vessel together, and one is correspondingly liable for a *kezayit* burned outside.

### Rabbi Eliezer Vessel

Rabbi Eliezer holds that once two half-*prasim* are placed together in the *kli shareis*, the vessel configuration is significant — *keviusa demana milsa hi*. The act of placing them together in the vessel establishes the full combined amount as the operative minimum. Therefore, when the full amount has been placed in the vessel, one must burn all of it to be *yotzei*, and similarly, one is liable outside only for the full amount that was placed in the vessel together.

### Keviusa Demana

The dispute thus turns on the question of *keviusa demana* — whether the configuration of the *kli shareis* is halachically meaningful. The *tanna kamma* says it is not: *lav milsa hi*. The *shiur* remains a *kezayit* regardless of what else was placed in the vessel. Rabbi Eliezer says it is: *milsa hi*. Once the vessel was loaded with the combined amount, that becomes the operative *shiur*, and one is liable outside only for the full amount thus configured.

It is worth noting, as Tosafot observes, something unusual in the way this sugya has developed. Normally the *tanna kamma* holds that one is liable outside for a *kezayit* even when the full amount is required inside for the *mitzvah* to be fulfilled — as is clearly the case with the *kometz* of a *mincha*. Yet for *ketores*, the Gemara assumes throughout that the *tanna kamma* links the *shiur* of liability outside to the minimum *shiur* with which one is *yotzei* inside. Rava's final solution preserves this assumption — both Rabbi Eliezer and the *tanna kamma* agree that a *kezayit* is the *shiur* for the daily *ketores* inside when only a *kezayit* was placed in the *kli shareis*, and both agree this generates liability for a *kezayit* outside. The debate arises only when the *kli shareis* was filled with more, and turns entirely on the halachic significance of *keviusa demana*.
