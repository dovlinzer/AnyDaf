# Shabbat 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Shimon & Muktza

### Intro & Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: וְאַתְּ לָא תִּסְבְּרַהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא רַבִּי אַבָּא וְרַבִּי אַסִּי אִיקְּלַעוּ לְבֵי רַבִּי אַבָּא דְּמִן חֵיפָא, וּנְפַל מְנָרְתָּא עַל גְּלִימֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי וְלָא טַלְטְלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: And you** yourself **did not hold that Rabbi Yoḥanan** rules **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** even before Rav Yitzḥak came and cited this statement in his name? **Didn't Rabbi Abba and Rabbi Asi happen** to come **to the house of Rabbi Abba who was from** the city **Haifa, and a candelabrum fell onto Rabbi Asi's cloak and he did not move it?**

The daf begins at the bottom of 45b, continuing the extended discussion that has occupied the Gemara for nearly two pages: which cases of *muktza* does Rabbi Shimon concede are prohibited, and do we rule like him? As we saw previously, Rabbi Shimon agrees that a burning lamp presents a problem — though this may be a case of *huksa l'mitzvaso* (set aside for its commandment) rather than standard *muktza l'shabbos v'yomtov*. He similarly concedes the case of raisins that were pushed aside and are *aino raoy* (unfit), and beyond that he agrees to additional cases: animals on Yom Tov that are normally kept outside the house, newly born chicks, and so on. The Gemara now continues this inquiry.

### Rav Assi Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי תַּלְמִידֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲוָה, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאִית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that he did not lift it? **Is it not because Rabbi Asi was a student of Rabbi Yoḥanan, and Rabbi Yoḥanan holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who holds** that there is a prohibition of **set-aside?**

Abaye poses a challenge to Rav Yosef. The Gemara reads: *amar lei Abaye l'Rav Yosef* — Abaye said to Rav Yosef: *v'at lo tisbor d'Rabbi Yochanan k'Rabbi Yehuda s'vira lei?* Don't you also agree that Rabbi Yochanan holds like Rabbi Yehuda? The background is that Rabbi Yochanan had been reported as saying "they say the *halacha* is like Rabbi Shimon," while the Gemara yesterday concluded on the basis of other reports that Rabbi Yochanan himself does not hold like Rabbi Shimon but rather like Rabbi Yehuda, and that "they say" refers to others. Abaye now presses Rav Yosef: *v'ha Rav Abba v'Rav Assi ikla l'vei Rabbi Abba d'min Cheifa* — Rav Abba and Rav Assi came to the house of Rabbi Abba from Cheifa, *v'nafal m'norta al glima d'Rav Assi* — and a candelabrum fell onto Rav Assi's coat, *v'lo tiltelah* — and he refused to move it. This was presumably on Shabbat, and the candelabrum, though burning when Shabbat began, had apparently since gone out. Now, in the Mishna, the position that a lamp remains forbidden even after burning out belongs to Rabbi Yehuda (or Rabbi Meir), not to Rabbi Shimon, who permits moving it once extinguished. So why did Rav Assi refuse? *Lav mishum d'Rav Assi talmida d'Rabbi Yochanan hava* — must it not be that Rav Assi is a student of Rabbi Yochanan, *v'Rabbi Yochanan k'Rabbi Yehuda s'vira lei* — and Rabbi Yochanan holds like Rabbi Yehuda? This seems to confirm that Rabbi Yochanan does not rule like Rabbi Shimon.

### Lamp vs. Menorah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְנָרְתָּא קָאָמְרַתְּ? מְנָרְתָּא שָׁאנֵי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: הוֹרָה רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בְּצַיְדָּן: מְנוֹרָה הַנִּיטֶּלֶת בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ, בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו — אָסוּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא בְּנֵר כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אֲבָל מְנוֹרָה, בֵּין נִיטֶּלֶת בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת בֵּין נִיטֶּלֶת בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו — אָסוּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: **Candelabrum you say;** are you citing a proof from the case of a candelabrum? **A candelabrum is different** because there is a unique *halakha* in that case. **As Rabbi Aḥa bar** Rabbi **Ḥanina said** that **Rabbi Asi said** that **Reish Lakish issued a ruling in** the city of **Sidon: A candelabrum that can be moved in one of his hands, one is permitted to move it** on Shabbat. However, if it is so heavy that one must move it **with his two hands, it is prohibited to move it. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: We have** permission to carry **only in the case of an** oil **lamp, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** However, with regard to a candelabrum, **both** one **that is carried in one hand** and one **that is carried in two hands, it is prohibited to move it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי? רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: הוֹאִיל וְאָדָם קוֹבֵעַ לָהּ מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that there is a unique prohibition in the case of a candelabrum? **Rabba and Rav Yosef both said: Since a person** usually **designates** a fixed **place for it** due to its size and its use, it is considered a built-in part of the house, and moving the candelabrum is like dismantling the house.

The Gemara responds: *k'Rabbi Yehuda s'vira lei d'is lei muktza* — he holds like Rabbi Yehuda that *muktza* applies, and that is why he did not move the *menorah*. But then the Gemara counters: *m'norta ka'amart? m'norta shani* — you're talking about a *menorah*? A *menorah* is different. This is not a reference to a Chanukah *menorah* but rather to a standing candelabrum. As Reish Lakish ruled in Tzidan: *m'norta haniteles b'yado achas mutar l'taltela* — a candelabrum held in one hand may be moved on Shabbat; *b'shtei yado ossur l'taltela* — one requiring two hands may not. Rabbi Yochanan, however, states: *anu ein lanu ela b'ner k'Rabbi Shimon* — the only leniency we have is with a lamp, ruling like Rabbi Shimon; *aval m'norta, bein b'yado achas bein b'shtei yado ossur l'taltela* — but a *menorah*, whether it requires one hand or two, may not be moved. Why this distinction? Rabba and Rav Yosef both offer the same explanation: *ho'il v'adam koveia lo makom* — since a *menorah* is the kind of object to which a person designates a fixed place, it acquires a hands-off quality. One does not ordinarily move one's candelabrum around the house, and even Rabbi Shimon might agree that such an item is restricted. The point stands: the fact that Rav Assi, a student of Rabbi Yochanan, did not move the *menorah* cannot prove that Rabbi Yochanan holds like Rabbi Yehuda in general, since the *menorah* has its own special rationale.

## Menorah Components

### Canopy Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: וַהֲרֵי כִּילַּת חֲתָנִים דְּאָדָם קוֹבֵעַ לוֹ מָקוֹם, וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כִּילַּת חֲתָנִים מוּתָּר לִנְטוֹתָהּ וּמוּתָּר לְפָרְקָה בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: A groom's canopy** is an object **for** which **a person designates a** set **place, and,** nevertheless, **Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya:** With regard to a **groom's canopy,** **it is permitted to assemble it and it is permitted to dismantle it on Shabbat.**

Abaye, however, presses further: *ve'harei kilat chatanim* — what about a canopy bed? *de'adam kove'a lo makom* — no one regularly moves the canopy of a bed; it too is *kove'a makom*. And yet, Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Chiya: *kilat chatanim mutar lintota u'mutar leparka be'Shabbat* — you may both spread the canopy and take it down on Shabbat. Ordinarily, erecting a flat overhead structure constitutes an *ohel* (tent) and is forbidden on Shabbat, but a bridal-canopy bed of the type described was fashioned like a teepee — coming to a point at the top — and therefore does not meet the technical definition of an *ohel*, which requires a flat surface of at least a *tefach* in width. So the spreading and collapsing is permitted from the standpoint of *ohel*. But if the canopy is then left in place for months or years without being moved, why may one dismantle it on Shabbat? Should it not be *muktza* on the grounds that it is *kove'a makom*? The objection undermines the proposed rationale for the *menorah*: if *kove'a makom* were itself a sufficient category to prohibit handling, it should apply to the canopy bed as well. Tosafot responds that conceptually such a category does exist — related to *muktza machmat chisaron kis*, precious objects one designates as untouchable — but the *menorah* does not reach the threshold of that category with sufficient force. A floor lamp that one might occasionally need to reposition is not at the same level of "hands-off" as, say, a Ming vase placed permanently on a mantle. So the *kove'a makom* rationale cannot be the reason a *menorah* is treated differently.

### Chulyot & Assembly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּשֶׁל חוּלְיוֹת. אִי הָכִי, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ דְּשָׁרֵי? מַאי "חוּלְיוֹת" — כְּעֵין חוּלְיוֹת, דְּאִית בַּהּ חִידְקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** Here it is referring to a special candelabrum made **of joints,** removable parts, and there is concern lest it fall and break into its component parts when it is moved, and one may come to reassemble it, which would be tantamount to crafting a vessel on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **If so,** if it is referring to that type of candelabrum, **what is the reason** for the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish who permits** moving the candelabrum? The Gemara replies: It is not referring to a candelabrum that can actually be dismantled. Rather, **what** is the meaning of **joints? Similar to joints,** i.e., **there are grooves in it** and it appears as if it is made of different components.

Rather, Abaye offers the true explanation: *ella amar Abaye be'shel chulyot askinan* — we are dealing with a *menorah* made of screwable component sections (*chulyot*). Such candelabra could have their arms removed and reattached, allowing one to vary the number of arms depending on how much light was desired. The problem is that screwing arms into a candelabrum on Shabbat would constitute a forbidden act of construction. While the precise halakhic category is debated — whether it is *binyan* (building) in the technical sense, or whether we say *ein binyan b'keilim* (construction does not apply to vessels) and instead apply *makeh b'patish* (the "finishing blow" that completes an object) — either way, assembling such a fixture is prohibited on Shabbat. Therefore, even simply carrying such a *menorah* is forbidden, lest one come to tighten or adjust its parts. As Rashi explains: *shema tipol v'titparek v'yachzirena v'nimtza oseh keli* — one might drop it, it might come apart, and one would reassemble it, thereby constructing a vessel.

### Gezeirah Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ: חוּלְיוֹת, בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה בֵּין קְטַנָּה — אֲסוּרָה לְטַלְטְלָהּ. גְּדוֹלָה נָמֵי דְּאִית בָּהּ חִידְקֵי — גְּזֵירָה אַטּוּ גְּדוֹלָה דְּחוּלְיוֹת. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּקְטַנָּה דְּאִית בָּהּ חִידְקֵי: מָר סָבַר גָּזְרִינַן, וּמָר סָבַר לָא גָּזְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** in summary: With regard to a candelabrum made of actual **joints, both** one that is **large** and one that is **small, it is prohibited to move it. In addition,** a **large** candelabrum **that has grooves,** everyone agrees that it is prohibited to move it by rabbinic **decree,** which was issued **due to** a **large** candelabrum made of **joints.** Because it is common for a large candelabrum to be made of joints, if one saw someone carrying a large, grooved candelabrum, he would mistakenly assume that it had joints due to the similarity between them, and would mistakenly permit carrying a large candelabrum actually composed of joints. **Where** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish **disagree** is **in** the case of a **small** candelabrum **that has grooves. This Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **we issue a decree** prohibiting moving even a small, grooved candelabrum due to a large one. **And this Sage,** Reish Lakish, **holds** that **we do not issue a decree.** Because a small candelabrum is not typically made of joints, everyone realizes that the grooves are strictly decorative.

The Gemara now works through a careful gradation. If a *menorah* genuinely consists of separate *chulyot*, it is forbidden to move, whether large or small, since there is a realistic concern one will come to fix it. But what if the *menorah* is actually a single piece, merely engraved with circular markings (*chidkei*) that make it look as though it is composed of screwable sections — a *marat ayin* problem? Here the Gemara introduces a *gezeirah*: a large such *menorah*, even if not actually made of pieces, is forbidden because it too closely resembles the problematic case. The concern is more pronounced with a large candelabrum, since component-piece *menorahs* are typically large impressive fixtures; a small handheld one is generally constructed compactly and is less likely to involve interchangeable parts. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ki pligi* — where the dispute lies — is by a small *menorah* that has engraved markings but is not actually made of *chulyot*. One opinion (*mar savar gazrinan*) maintains that even this is forbidden, extending the *gezeirah* two degrees from the core concern. The other opinion (*u'mar savar lo gazrinan*) holds that the *gezeirah* does not extend that far. This is a striking Gemara — even a position that in principle accords with Rabbi Shimon's leniency may still prohibit handling a *menorah* for an independent rabbinic reason entirely unrelated to *muktza* in the standard sense.

## Rabbi Yochanan's View

### Stam Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתָם מִשְׁנָה. וּתְנַן, מוּכְנִי שֶׁלָּהּ בִּזְמַן שֶׁהִיא נִשְׁמֶטֶת — אֵין חִיבּוּר לָהּ, וְאֵין נִמְדֶּדֶת עִמָּהּ, וְאֵין מַצֶּלֶת עִמָּהּ בְּאֹהֶל הַמֵּת, וְאֵין גּוֹרְרִין אוֹתָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת בִּזְמַן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלֶיהָ מָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say that** the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda? **Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan state** the following principle: **The *halakha* is in accordance with an unattributed mishna? And we learned** in the mishna that discusses ritual impurity of a wagon with a detachable undercarriage: The wagon's **undercarriage, when it is detachable** from the wagon, it is **not** considered **connected to it** and they are considered independent units as far as the *halakhot* of ritual impurity are concerned. **And it is not measured with it.** This refers to calculating the volume of forty *se'a*, as a vessel with a volume larger than forty *se'a* does not have the legal status of a vessel and cannot become ritually impure. **And** the undercarriage likewise **does not protect** together **with** the wagon **in a tent** over **the corpse.** A large wagon is considered a tent in and of itself, and the vessels inside the wagon do not become impure if the wagon is over a dead body. However, the undercarriage is not included with the wagon in this regard. If a hole in the wagon is sealed by the undercarriage, it is not considered to be sealed with regard to preventing ritual impurity. **And,** likewise, **one may not pull** the wagon **on Shabbat when there is money upon it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אֵין עָלֶיהָ מָעוֹת — שַׁרְיָא, וְאַף עַל גַּב דַּהֲווֹ עָלֶיהָ בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** By inference: **If there is not money on it,** one is **permitted** to move the wagon **even though there was** money **on it at twilight.**

The status of Rabbi Yochanan's position remains unresolved. We have now established from multiple angles: Rabbi Yochanan was reported saying "they say the *halacha* is like Rabbi Shimon," which the Gemara interpreted as others say, not his own view; separately, he appeared to hold like Rabbi Yehuda; yet at the bottom of this very page he stated that the only leniency "we" have is *b'ner k'Rabbi Shimon* — by the lamp, ruling like Rabbi Shimon — suggesting at minimum some acceptance of Rabbi Shimon in the lamp case, though perhaps not more broadly. The Gemara now poses a fresh challenge: *umi amar Rabbi Yochanan hachi* — did Rabbi Yochanan really hold like Rabbi Yehuda? *V'ha'amar Rabbi Yochanan halacha kestam mishna* — but Rabbi Yochanan has a general principle that the *halacha* follows a *stam mishna* (an anonymously authored Mishna, presumed to represent the majority view). And consider what the Mishna teaches about the *mukhni shela* — an object that, following Tosafot's reading, refers to a dresser with a detachable base. The Mishna establishes that such a base, being removable, is not considered a single unit with the dresser for purposes of *tuma*: it does not create *chibur* (connection) with it, is not measured together with it to determine whether the dresser is large enough to be exempt from *tuma*, and does not seal the dresser's opening to protect contents from the *tuma* of a corpse in the same room — since the detachable base cannot constitute a proper seal. None of that, incidentally, is our concern; the Mishna belongs to tractate Keilim, and one may be grateful that tractate Keilim has no Talmud Bavli. The relevant portion is the final clause: *ve'ein gorrin ota beshabbat bizman sheyesh aleha maot* — one may not drag the dresser on Shabbat when there is money resting on the base. The implication — which the Gemara draws — is that if there is no money on the base now, one may drag it even if there was money on it at *bein hashmashot* (twilight, i.e., the onset of Shabbat). That is the position of Rabbi Shimon: the status at the onset of Shabbat does not permanently determine an object's status throughout Shabbat. If this *stam mishna* reflects Rabbi Shimon's view, and Rabbi Yochanan rules according to *stam mishnaot*, how can Rabbi Yochanan hold like Rabbi Yehuda?

### Rav Zeira's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: תְּהֵא מִשְׁנָתֵינוּ שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ עָלֶיהָ מָעוֹת כׇּל בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁבּוֹר דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Let our mishna** apply only to a case **in which there was no money on** the wagon **throughout** the entire duration of **twilight.** This strained interpretation is accepted **so as not to contradict** and reject **Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement.**

Rav Zeira resolves the difficulty: *tehei mishnateinu dehava aleha maot bein hashmashot* — the Mishna should be read as addressing a case where there was money on the base at *bein hashmashot*. The inference that "only when money is actively on it now may one not drag it" is incorrect. The Mishna is describing one scenario: money is currently on the base, so you cannot drag it. A separate and equally valid scenario — not addressed by this Mishna but equally valid — is that money was on it at *bein hashmashot* and is no longer there now. That too would be forbidden. Thus no proof to Rabbi Shimon's position can be derived from this Mishna, and Rabbi Yochanan need not be in conflict with himself. The Gemara then uses a striking phrase: *shelo lishbor devarav shel Rabbi Yochanan* — so as not to "break" the words of Rabbi Yochanan, i.e., lest we create a self-contradiction in his rulings. The phrase *lishbor* — to shatter — is an unusual formulation, and it reflects how much is at stake in harmonizing Rabbi Yochanan's position. The overall picture emerging across these two pages is a sustained push away from Rabbi Shimon: Rav holds like Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yochanan holds like Rabbi Yehuda, and even Rabbi — who sounded at one point like he accepted Rabbi Shimon's leniency — may in fact hold like Rabbi Yehuda.

## Historical Practices

### Rabbi's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַךְ רַבִּי לִדְיוֹסְפָרָא, וְהוֹרָה בִּמְנוֹרָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּנֵר. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הוֹרָה בִּמְנוֹרָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּנֵר — לְהֶיתֵּרָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא: הוֹרָה בִּמְנוֹרָהּ לְאִיסּוּרָא — וּכְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּנֵר לְהֶיתֵּרָא. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: One time, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **went to** the town of **Deyosfera, and issued a ruling with regard to a candelabrum in accordance with** the ruling that **Rabbi Shimon** made **with regard to an** oil **lamp.** This description is insufficiently clear, therefore **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does this mean that **he issued a ruling in** the case of a candelabrum, **like** the ruling that **Rabbi Shimon** made **in** the case of **an** oil **lamp, to permit** moving it? **Or, perhaps, he issued a ruling in** the case of **a candelabrum to prohibit** moving it, **and** in another case he ruled **in accordance with** the ruling that **Rabbi Shimon** made **in** the case of **an** oil **lamp, to permit** moving it. There was no resolution found to this dilemma and therefore it **stands** unresolved.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi reports: *pa'am achat halach Rabbi l'Diospora* — Rabbi once traveled to Diospora (which Rashi understands as a place name, though it sounds remarkably like "diaspora"), *v'horah b'menorah k'Rabbi Shimon b'ner* — and he ruled regarding a *menorah* the way Rabbi Shimon rules regarding a lamp, i.e., leniently. The Gemara immediately raises an ambiguity: *ibaya lehu* — a question was left unresolved. Did Rabbi rule that a *menorah* is like Rabbi Shimon's lamp — meaning both are permitted — or did he issue two separate rulings: a *menorah* is forbidden (as we have been saying throughout), and by a lamp we follow Rabbi Shimon's leniency? The Gemara concludes *teiku* — the question stands unresolved. Similarly unresolved is whether Rabbi's ruling concerning the lamp was a narrow ruling limited to the lamp case, or a broader acceptance of Rabbi Shimon's position across the board.

### Criticized Practices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מַלְכִּיָּא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי וְטַילְטֵל שְׁרָגָא, וְאִיקְּפַד רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי גָּלִילָאָה אִיקְּלַע לְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, טַילְטֵל שְׁרָגָא וְאִיקְּפַד רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is told that **Rav Malkiya happened** to come **to the house of Rabbi Simlai and moved an** extinguished oil **lamp, and Rabbi Simlai became angry,** as in his opinion it is prohibited to move an oil lamp because it is set-aside. Likewise, **Rabbi Yosei the Galilean happened** to come **to the place of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, and moved an** oil **lamp, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, became angry.**

The Gemara records two parallel incidents illustrating the tension. Rav Malkia came to the house of Rav Simlai, *v'tiltal shraga* — and moved an extinguished lamp — *v'ikpad Rav Simlai* — and Rav Simlai was upset, indicating that in his domain this was not done. Similarly, a figure from the Galilee came to the house of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina, moved an extinguished lamp, and Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina was displeased. These incidents show that the position of Rabbi Shimon continued to resurface and find practitioners, but it was consistently met with disapproval by more senior figures. The Gemara's trajectory is unmistakably toward sidelining Rabbi Shimon's broader leniency, even as it cannot fully eliminate it.

### Rabbi Abahu's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ כִּי אִיקְּלַע לְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הֲוָה מְטַלְטֵל שְׁרָגָא. כִּי אִיקְּלַע לְאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא הֲוָה מְטַלְטֵל שְׁרָגָא. מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ — לֶיעְבַּד כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אִי כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ — לֶיעְבַּד כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן! — לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וּמִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ דְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הוּא דְּלָא הֲוָה עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara also relates that **Rabbi Abbahu, when he happened** to come **to the place of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, he would move an** oil **lamp.** However, **when he happened** to come **to the place of Rabbi Yoḥanan, he would not move an** oil **lamp.** The Gemara wondered: **Whichever** way **you** look at it there is a difficulty. **If he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, let him act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** everywhere and refrain from moving the lamp. **And if he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, let him act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** everywhere and move the oil lamp. The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it can be explained that Rabbi Abbahu **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon; however, in deference to Rabbi Yoḥanan he did not act** accordingly, so as not to act contrary to his ruling in the place where he was the authority.

A more nuanced case involves Rabbi Abahu: *ki ika le'asrei de'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, havie metalteil shraga* — when he was in the house of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi he would move an extinguished lamp; *aval ki ata le'asrei de'Rabbi Yochanan, lo havie metalteil shraga* — but when he came to the house of Rabbi Yochanan, he would not. The Gemara asks: *ato mima nafshach* — make up your mind. If he holds like Rabbi Yehuda, let him act like Rabbi Yehuda everywhere and not move it; if he holds like Rabbi Shimon, let him move it everywhere. The resolution: *le'olam ke'Rabbi Shimon sevira lei* — he actually held it was permitted, *umishum kevodo de'Rabbi Yochanan, hu dela havie avid* — but out of deference to Rabbi Yochanan, he refrained when in his presence. This is logically coherent: if something is forbidden, one cannot permit oneself to do it even in another's house; but if something is fundamentally permitted, one may choose voluntarily to refrain out of respect. This behavior itself is evidence of Rabbi Abahu's underlying view.

One might ask why Rav Malkia or the Galilean scholar would perform an act in another scholar's home that the host considered forbidden. Perhaps they did not know the host's position in advance, or acted out of habit. In any case, this passage makes clear that the question being debated at this juncture is quite narrow: not whether we accept Rabbi Shimon's global rejection of *muktza*, which nobody is proposing, but merely whether we can follow him in the specific case of the lamp after it has gone out.

This is also the point at which Tosafot and Rashi diverge significantly on what these discussions actually concern. Rashi reads the debate as potentially including even the case where the lamp was burning at *bein hashmashot* — Rabbi Shimon's full position. Tosafot restricts the discussion drastically: he argues that even the most lenient voices in this passage are only addressing a lamp that was not in use when Shabbat began at all, and whose only potential *muktza* problem is *muktza machmat miyus* (set aside by reason of disgust). According to Tosafot, the possibility of ruling like Rabbi Shimon on the case where something was burning at *bein hashmashot* and is now extinguished is not even on the table here — everyone agrees that case remains forbidden. As Tosafot reads it, the upcoming discussion of *muktza machmat miyus* is thus the real subject of the preceding material as well. This matters greatly for practical *halacha*, and reflects a broader methodological point: Rashi provides the baseline reading against which Tosafot argues, but Rashi's reading is not necessarily closer to *p'shat*. It is simply the necessary starting point for the conversation.

## Muktza Machmat Miyus

### Oil vs. Naphtha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁרָגָא דְּמִשְׁחָא שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלַהּ, דְּנַפְטָא — אֲסִיר לְטַלְטוֹלַהּ. רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: דְּנַפְטָא נָמֵי שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלַהּ, דְּהוֹאִיל וַחֲזֵי לְכַסּוֹת בֵּיהּ מָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakhot* of moving lamps on Shabbat, **Rav Yehuda said:** With regard to **an** extinguished **oil lamp, it is permitted to move it,** whereas a **naphtha** lamp, **it is prohibited to move it.** Since the smell of naphtha is unpleasant, the lamp is used exclusively for lighting. Therefore, moving it is prohibited. **Rabba and Rav Yosef both said:** With regard to **a naphtha** lamp, **too, it is permitted to move it.**

The Gemara now takes up the category of *muktza machmat miyus* — *muktza* by reason of disgust or repulsiveness — directly. Rav Yehuda rules: *sharga d'mishcha shari l'tiltula* — an oil lamp may be moved (after it is no longer burning); *d'nafta assur l'tiltula* — but a lamp of naphtha, a strongly malodorous pitch-like fuel, may not, because everyone would agree that qualifies as *ma'us* (disgusting). The category of *muktza machmat miyus* is not in dispute; what is in dispute is whether an ordinary oil lamp falls within it. Rava and Rav Yosef both hold: *d'nafta nami shari l'tiltula* — even a naphtha lamp may be moved. Tosafot explains this not as a wholesale rejection of the *miyus* category but as a narrower definition: there is a distinction between something that is malodorous and something that is genuinely disgusting to handle. A naphtha lamp may smell unpleasant, but it is not so disgusting to handle that one would refuse to touch it, and therefore it does not meet the threshold for *muktza machmat miyus*.

### Rav Avya Anecdote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַוְיָא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רָבָא. הֲוָה מְאִיסָן (בֵּי) כַּרְעֵיהּ בְּטִינָא. אִתֵּיב אַפּוּרְיָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אִיקְּפַד רָבָא, בְּעָא לְצַעוֹרֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ שְׁרָגָא דְנַפְטָא נָמֵי שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Avya happened** to come **to Rava's house. His feet were dirty with clay** and **he put them on the bed before Rava. Rava became angry** at him for dirtying the bed and, therefore, **sought to torment him** with questions that he could not answer. Rava **said to him: What is the reason** that **Rabba and Rav Yosef both said** that with regard to **a naphtha lamp, too, that it is permitted to move it?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוֹאִיל וְחַזְיָא לְכַסּוֹיֵי בַּהּ מָנָא. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, כׇּל צְרוֹרוֹת שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר מִטַּלְטְלִין, הוֹאִיל וְחַזְיִין לְכַסּוֹיֵי בְּהוּ מָנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אִיכָּא תּוֹרַת כְּלִי עָלֶיהָ. הָנֵי לֵיכָּא תּוֹרַת כְּלִי עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Avya **said to him: Since it is suitable to cover a vessel with it.** Rava **said to him: But if that is so, all pebbles** in the yard **may** also **be carried** *ab initio* on Shabbat, **since it is suitable to cover a vessel with them.** Rav Avya **said to him:** There is a distinction between these cases. **This,** the lamp, **the status of a vessel** applies **to it** and there are leniencies that apply to vessels with regard to the *halakhot* of set-aside. **These,** the pebbles, **the status of a vessel does not** apply **to it,** as they are a raw material. Carrying them is prohibited unless designated for a specific purpose before Shabbat.

Rav Avya came to the house of Rava with mud-caked legs and sat down on the couch, apparently without wiping his feet. Rava, displeased at his furniture being muddied, sought to call him to account. With characteristic indirection, Rava asked: *mai ta'ama Rava v'Rav Yosef d'amri duvayhu* — why do Rava and Rav Yosef both rule that even a naphtha lamp may be moved? The implication of the question was presumably not lost on anyone in the room, as the theme of *miyus* — disgust and its consequences — was clearly pointed. Rav Avya, whether willfully or obliviously, answered on the merits: *hoil ve-chazei le-khisuyei bei mana* — since even a disgusting lamp can still be used to cover or seal a vessel, it retains a use and is therefore not *muktza*. Rava pressed back: *ella meiata, kol tzerorot shebe-chatzer metaltelin, hoil ve-chazei le-khisuyei bei mana?* — by that logic, all rocks in a courtyard should be movable, since one could always use a rock to plug up a hole in a vessel! And rocks are certainly *muktza*; one cannot eliminate *muktza* simply by imagining some marginal use.

### Vessel Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תָּא שְׁמַע: הַשֵּׁירִים וְהַנְּזָמִים וְהַטַּבָּעוֹת, הֲרֵי הֵן כְּכׇל הַכֵּלִים הַנִּטָּלִים בֶּחָצֵר. וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: מַה טַּעַם? — הוֹאִיל וְאִיכָּא תּוֹרַת כְּלִי עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Was it not taught** in a *baraita* that **bracelets, nose-rings and rings,** although it is prohibited to go out into the public domain wearing them on Shabbat, **they are like all the vessels that may be moved in the courtyard;** in the private domain, one may move them and they are not set-aside. **And Ulla said: What is the reason** that it is permitted to move nose-rings in the yard? It is **because the status of a vessel** applies **to it.**

Rav Avya's response articulates a fundamental principle: *hai ika torat keli aleiha, hani leika torat keli aleiha* — this lamp has the status of a vessel; rocks do not. A vessel is by definition something created for human use. To strip a vessel of its usable status requires that it become so thoroughly repulsive or restricted that no legitimate use for it on Shabbat can be identified. A rock, by contrast, never entered the category of human utility in the first place; absent a deliberate act of designation (turning it into a paperweight, for instance), it begins and remains *muktza*. A vessel, however, requires far more to cross back out of the category of usable objects.

This principle is then supported by a *baraita*: *hashirim ve-hanezamim ve-hatabaot* — various bracelets, nose rings, and rings. An upcoming chapter of the tractate will establish that certain types of jewelry may not be worn on Shabbat outside of an *eruv*-enclosed space, out of concern that a woman will remove the jewelry to show it to someone, hold it in her hand, and inadvertently carry it in the public domain. Yet, the *baraita* rules: *harei hen ke-khol ha-kelim ha-nitalin be-chatzer* — these items are like all other vessels that may be moved in a courtyard. And Ulla explains: *hoil ve-ika torat keli aleiha* — since they still have the status of a vessel. Even though you may not wear them, you may move them, because the prohibition on wearing them is insufficient to strip them of their vessel-status for purposes of *muktza*. This is contrasted with genuinely precious items — *muktza machmat chisaron kis*, like an extraordinarily valuable object one designates as permanently untouchable — where the vessel-status is overridden by the degree of restriction. But the general principle holds: vessel-status creates a strong presumption against *muktza* that requires a great deal to overcome.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: בְּרִיךְ רַחֲמָנָא דְּלָא כַּסְּפֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב אַוְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Thank God** that **Rava did not embarrass Rav Avya** and Rav Avya managed to successfully answer Rava's questions.

The Gemara concludes with Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's remark: *barich rachmana dela chasfyei Rava le-Rav Avaya* — blessed be God that Rava did not embarrass Rav Avaya. Whether this is gratitude for the preservation of Rav Avaya's dignity, or relief that the exchange produced a sound halakhic answer, or both, is left characteristically open.

## Vows & Firstborn

### Vow Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַבָּה. תַּנְיָא: מוֹתַר הַשֶּׁמֶן שֶׁבַּנֵּר וְשֶׁבַּקְּעָרָה — אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. אַלְמָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה. וּרְמִינְהוּ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין מוּמוֹ נִיכָּר מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב — אֵין זֶה מִן הַמּוּכָן!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised a contradiction before Rabba** citing two sources with regard to set-aside on Shabbat. **It was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **the remaining oil that is in an** oil **lamp and in a bowl** in which a wick was lit, it **is prohibited** to use it on Shabbat **and Rabbi Shimon permits** using it. **Apparently, Rabbi Shimon is not of** the opinion that there is a prohibition of **set-aside. And a contradiction is raised** from a parallel source, in which the Sages discussed the *halakha* of the firstborn of a kosher animal that developed a blemish on a Festival. The firstborn must be examined to determine whether or not that type of blemish disqualifies the animal from being sacrificed as an offering. If it is disqualified, it may be redeemed, slaughtered, and eaten as non-sacred meat on the Festival. **Rabbi Shimon says: Any** firstborn animal **whose blemish is not perceptible before the Festival is not among** the animals **prepared** prior to the Festival for use on the Festival, and it is prohibited to slaughter it. Apparently, an item not prepared in advance has set-aside status according to Rabbi Shimon.

Abaye presents a challenge to Rava from a *baraita*: *mafirin nedarim be'Shabbat ve-nish'alin l'nedarim shehen l'tzorech Shabbat* — a husband may annul his wife's vows on Shabbat, and one may seek the voiding of vows by a sage on Shabbat, provided there is a Shabbat-related need. The *baraita* thus permits both husband-annulment and sage-voiding on Shabbat. The *muktza* question here is real: if a woman has made a vow forbidding herself from eating a particular food, that food was *assur* to her at *bein hashmashot*, and even if the vow is later annulled during Shabbat, how does it escape *muktza*? Tosafot raises a related and subtle issue: a vow creates a personal prohibition — it is the person, not the object, that is restricted. If only she is forbidden and others are not, can the food be genuinely *muktza*? And even if it can be *muktza* to her, may it not be moved by others? These questions about whether *muktza* attaches to the object or the person, and whether something *muktza* to one person is *muktza* to all, are raised by Tosafot and remain without a clean resolution, though Tosafot suggests that if the vow explicitly forbade the food to all people, or if *migo d'itkatzai bein hashmashot* — since it was restricted at the onset of Shabbat — it might become broadly *muktza*.

### Firstborn Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם, אָדָם יוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה אֵימָתַי תִּכְבֶּה נֵרוֹ. הָכָא, אָדָם יוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה מָתַי יִפּוֹל בּוֹ מוּם? מֵימָר אָמַר: מִי יֵימַר דְּנָפֵיל בֵּיהּ מוּמָא, וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר דְּנָפֵיל בֵּיהּ מוּמָא — מִי יֵימַר דְּנָפֵיל בֵּיהּ מוּם קָבוּעַ. וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר דְּנָפֵל בֵּיהּ מוּם קָבוּעַ — מִי יֵימַר דְּמִזְדְּקֵק לֵיהּ חָכָם?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba said to him: **How can you compare** these cases? **There,** in the case of the lamp, **a person sits and anticipates when his candle will be extinguished.** It is clear to him that it will be extinguished, and he can safely assume that a certain amount of oil will remain in the lamp or the bowl. **Here,** does **a person sit and anticipate when a blemish will befall** his animal? The owner of the animal **says: Who says that a blemish will befall** his animal? **And** even **if you say that a blemish will befall it, who says that a permanent blemish** that would enable it to be slaughtered **will befall it? And** even **if you say that a permanent blemish will befall it, who says that a Sage will** agree to **engage** in examining the blemish? Since there are so many uncertainties involved, if the blemish is not perceptible before the Festival, the possibility of the firstborn animal becoming available does not enter a person's mind at all.

The Gemara's sharper challenge involves the *bechor* (firstborn animal). Rabbi Shimon rules: *kol she'ein mumo nikar me'erev Yom Tov, ein ze min hamuchan* — if a firstborn animal's blemish was not evident before Yom Tov, it is not considered *muchan* (designated and available) for Yom Tov use. The background: a firstborn animal that lacks a blemish must be brought as a *korban* and may not be slaughtered for ordinary consumption. If it has a blemish, however, a *kohen* may slaughter and eat it without the need for redemption. On Yom Tov, slaughtering is generally permitted. So if a blemish appears on the animal on Yom Tov itself — may the *kohen* slaughter it? Rabbi Shimon says: if the blemish was not visible before Yom Tov, it is *muktza* for the entire Yom Tov, even if the blemish is discovered during the day. This appears to be in direct tension with Rabbi Shimon's leniency by the lamp: just as oil left over when the lamp burns out becomes permitted, why does the *bechor* not become permitted once the blemish appears?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: מְפֵירִין נְדָרִים בְּשַׁבָּת, [וְנִשְׁאָלִין לִנְדָרִים שֶׁהֵן] לְצוֹרֶךְ הַשַּׁבָּת. וְאַמַּאי? — לֵימָא: מִי יֵימַר דְּמִיזְדְּקֵק לַהּ בַּעַל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised an objection** to this last point from that which we learned in a mishna: **One may nullify vows on Shabbat.** A woman who vowed that certain food is prohibited to her, her husband can nullify her vow on Shabbat. **And** likewise **one may request** that a Sage find an opening **to** dissolve his **vows,** i.e., a factor that the one taking the vow failed to take into account or an element of regret, if that nullification or dissolution is **for the purpose of Shabbat.** The question arises: **And why,** after a man has nullified his wife's vow, should she be permitted to eat that food? When the woman vowed not to eat that food, she consciously set it aside. Even if some way to dissolve the vow is found, the food should remain set-aside. On the basis of the same uncertainty that was raised above, **say: Who says that** her husband **will** agree to **engage** in nullifying her oath? Perhaps he will refuse to nullify it.

The Gemara responds sharply: *hachi hashta* — how can you compare these cases? By the lamp, *adam yoshev umitzappe imatai tichbe nero* — a person sits and anticipates that his lamp will burn out. He knows when Shabbat begins that the lamp will eventually go out and oil will remain; his mental designation of the oil is entirely reasonable. But by the *bechor*, *adam yoshev umitzappe imatai yippol bo mum?* — does a person actually sit and expect a blemish to fall on his animal? Three independent contingencies would all have to materialize: *mi yeimar denafal bei muma* — who says a blemish will occur at all? *Mi yeimar denafal bei mum kavua* — even if a blemish appears, who says it will be a permanent blemish, since only a permanent blemish permits slaughter? And *mi yeimar dimizdakek leh chacham* — who says a sage will be available and willing to render a ruling on the blemish?

Tosafot adds a sharpening observation: Rabbi Yehuda, who generally holds of more *muktza*, actually rules more leniently on the *bechor* than Rabbi Shimon does. This is because Rabbi Yehuda permits a sage to rule on a *bechor*'s blemish on Yom Tov. Since for Rabbi Yehuda the third contingency effectively disappears — the ruling is permitted — the *bechor* is not as thoroughly set aside. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds that it is forbidden for a sage to rule on a *bechor* on Yom Tov, making the third contingency a genuine obstacle and rendering the *bechor* fully *muktza*. This observation also reflects a general principle that the Gemara articulated earlier in the tractate: Rabbi Shimon does not hold that *muktza* applies in every case where something was unavailable at the onset of Shabbat, but he does hold that when the object was genuinely set aside under circumstances where a reasonable person would have expected it to remain unavailable, the *muktza* status is maintained.

### Annulment Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְרַב פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא. דְּאָמַר רַב פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: כׇּל הַנּוֹדֶרֶת — עַל דַּעַת בַּעְלָהּ הִיא נוֹדֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of vows, it can be explained **in accordance with** that which **Rav Pineḥas** said **in the name** of **Rava,** who came to explain some of the fundamentals of the *halakhot* of vows, **as Rav Pineḥas said in the name** of **Rava: Every** woman **who takes a vow,** it is from the outset contingent **on her husband's consent that she takes the vow.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִשְׁאָלִין לִנְדָרִים שֶׁל צוֹרֶךְ הַשַּׁבָּת בְּשַׁבָּת. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא: מִי יֵימַר דְּמִזְדְּקֵק לֵיהּ חָכָם? — הָתָם — אִי לָא מִיזְדְּקֵק לֵיהּ חָכָם סַגְיָא לֵיהּ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה הֶדְיוֹטוֹת. הָכָא — מִי יֵימַר דְּמִיזְדְּקֵק לֵיהּ חָכָם.
>
> **Translation:** Moreover, the Gemara cites proof for this from that which was taught: **Come** and **hear: One may request** that a Sage dissolve his **vows for the purpose of Shabbat on Shabbat,** i.e., one who vowed on Shabbat that eating on that day is prohibited for him. **And why** is he permitted to eat something that was prohibited to him by his vow? **Say** again: **Who says that** the **Sage will** agree to **engage** in dissolution of **his** vow? Consequently, one has certainly diverted his attention from the food, set it aside, and it should be prohibited to eat it. The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, there is a difference, as **there,** in the *halakhot* of vows, even **if** the **Sage does not** agree to **engage** in dissolution of **his** vow, **he** can **suffice** with renouncing the vow **before three common people.** Even though it is preferable to have a Sage dissolve his vow, in exigent circumstances one may turn to a court of three common people to dissolve it. He will certainly find a way to dissolve his vow. However, **here,** in the case of the firstborn animal, **who says that** the **Sage will** agree to **engage** in examination of the blemish? In the *halakhot* of firstborn animals only an ordained Sage, who received special license to do so, is authorized to verify that it is a permanent blemish and permit redemption and slaughter of the animal as a non-sacred animal.

The Gemara now asks why the vow case does not suffer from the same problem as the *bechor*: even if the husband might annul the vow, who says he will? And even if a sage could void the vow, who says one will be found? The response distinguishes the two mechanisms carefully. For husband-annulment, the Gemara — on the basis of the principle stated by Rav Pinchas in the name of Rava — explains: *kol hanoderes al daas baala hi noderes* — when a wife makes a vow, she implicitly makes it subject to her husband's consent. The vow was never fully unconditional; it always carried the understood possibility that the husband might refuse to ratify it. The food was therefore never truly and definitively forbidden in the way the *bechor* was; there was always the standing possibility of the husband's non-acceptance. As a result, the analogy to *adam yoshev umitzappe* — the person who anticipates the lamp burning out — holds here. Just as the oil is quasi-expected to become available, so the vow-forbidden food is quasi-expected to be annulled.

For sage-voiding, a different answer applies: even if one cannot find a trained sage, *sagi lei b'gimmel hedyotot* — three laypeople suffice to void a vow. The availability of three unqualified laypeople is far more assured than the availability of a qualified expert willing to rule on a *bechor*. And here Tosafot's point becomes decisive: for the *bechor*, not only must one find a sage, but one must find a sage who is willing to transgress — since ruling on a *bechor* on Yom Tov is itself forbidden according to Rabbi Shimon. No such obstacle exists with voiding a vow. The difference in the practical availability and halakhic permissibility of the relevant adjudication produces the decisive asymmetry between the two cases.

## Unintended Acts

### Challenge to Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מִי אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כָּבְתָה מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָהּ. כָּבְתָה — אִין, לֹא כָּבְתָה — לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? — דִילְמָא בַּהֲדֵי דְּנָקֵיט לַהּ כָּבְיָא? הָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין — מוּתָּר. דְּתַנְיָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: גּוֹרֵר אָדָם כִּסֵּא מִטָּה וְסַפְסָל, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לַעֲשׂוֹת חָרִיץ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised a contradiction before Rav Yosef: Did Rabbi Shimon** actually **say** that when a lamp **is extinguished,** it **is permitted to move it** on Shabbat? By inference: After **it is extinguished, yes,** moving it is permitted; so long as it **is not extinguished, no,** moving it is prohibited. **What is the reason** that it is prohibited to move a burning candle? It is due to concern that **perhaps, as he moves** the lamp, the flame **will be extinguished.** However, is Rabbi Shimon really concerned that a flame will be extinguished under those circumstances? **Didn't we learn** that **Rabbi Shimon stated** a principle: **An unintentional act,** a permitted action from which an unintended prohibited labor ensues on Shabbat, since he did not intend to perform the prohibited action, **is permitted? As it was taught** in a *baraita*, **Rabbi Shimon says: A person may drag a bed, chair, and bench** on the ground, **as long as he does not intend** to **make a furrow** in the ground. Even if a furrow is formed inadvertently, one need not be concerned. Since that was not his intention, there is no prohibition according to Rabbi Shimon. Consequently, according to Rabbi Shimon there should be no prohibition in moving a burning candle, even though it may be extinguished. Since that is not the intention of the one moving it, no prohibition would be violated.

The final section of the daf circles back to what was presented at the outset as the one thing Rabbi Shimon concedes: a burning lamp. The Gemara asks with fresh urgency — *mi amar Rabbi Shimon kavta mutar l'taltela, kavta in, lo kavta lo* — is it really Rabbi Shimon's position that when the lamp is extinguished it may be moved but when burning it may not? Why exactly? *Dilma bahadi d'nakat lei kavta* — perhaps as you carry it, it will happen to go out. So the concern when moving a burning lamp is that the act of carrying will cause extinguishing. But then the Gemara objects: *ha shamna lei l'Rabbi Shimon davar she'eino mitkavein mutar* — but we know Rabbi Shimon holds that an unintended act is permitted! He is not intending to extinguish the lamp; he merely wants to move it. And the extinguishing is not even inevitable — it is only a possibility. Rabbi Shimon should permit this.

The Gemara cites support from a *baraita*: *gorer adam kissei mitta v'safsal, ubilvad shelo yiskayven la'asos charitz* — one may drag a bench or bed across the ground, provided one does not intend to create a furrow, even though dragging will likely create one. Rabbi Shimon permits this precisely because the furrow-making is unintended. Why then should he not similarly permit moving a burning lamp, with the unintended — and uncertain — possibility of extinguishing?

### Biblical vs. Rabbinical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּכִי מִיכַּוֵין אִיכָּא אִיסּוּרָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, כִּי לָא מִיכַּוֵין — גָּזַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מִדְּרַבָּנַן. כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּכִי מִיכַּוֵין אִיכָּא אִיסּוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן — כִּי לָא מִיכַּוֵין שָׁרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that there is a distinction between the cases: In **every** case **where if** he **intends** to perform the action, **there is a prohibition by Torah law,** e.g., extinguishing a candle; even **when he does not intend** to do so, **Rabbi Shimon issued a decree** prohibiting it **by rabbinic law.** However, in **every** case **where** even **if** he **intends** to perform the action, **there is** merely **a prohibition by rabbinic law,** e.g., digging a furrow which is not a full-fledged act of plowing that is prohibited by Torah law, but is prohibited only by rabbinic law, **when he does not intend** to perform the action, **Rabbi Shimon** even **permits** performing this action ***ab initio*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: מוֹכְרֵי כְסוּת מוֹכְרִין כְּדַרְכָּן, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין בַּחַמָּה מִפְּנֵי הַחַמָּה וּבַגְּשָׁמִים מִפְּנֵי הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְהַצְּנוּעִין מַפְשִׁילִין בְּמַקֵּל לַאֲחוֹרֵיהֶן. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּכִי מִיכַּוֵין — אִיסּוּרָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא אִיכָּא, כִּי לָא מִיכַּוֵין — שָׁרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְכַתְּחִילָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection** to this distinction from that which we learned in a mishna: **Clothing merchants** who sell garments made of diverse kinds, a prohibited mixture of wool and linen, **may sell them as they normally would** to gentiles. A merchant may place the garments he is selling on his shoulders and need not be concerned about the prohibition against wearing diverse kinds, **as long as** the merchant **does not intend** to benefit from the garments **in the sun** as protection **from the sun, or in the rain** as protection **from the rain. However, the modest** people, those who are particularly meticulous in their performance of mitzvot, would **suspend** the wool and linen garments **on a stick behind them. And here, isn't it** a case where **if** one **intends** to wear the clothing, **there is a prohibition by Torah law,** and even so **when** he **does not intend** to wear it, **Rabbi Shimon permits** it ***ab initio*.** Apparently, Rabbi Shimon does not distinguish between cases on that basis.

The Gemara's proposed resolution draws a principled distinction: *kol heicha dechi michaven ika issur d'Oraisa, ki lo michaven gazar Rabbi Shimon mi'd'rabbanan* — wherever the intended act would constitute a Torah-level violation, Rabbi Shimon does not permit the unintended version; he imposes a rabbinic prohibition even when one lacks the intention. *Kol heicha dechi michaven ika issur d'rabbanan, ki lo michaven shari Rabbi Shimon l'chatchila* — but wherever the intended act would be only a rabbinic violation, then Rabbi Shimon permits the unintended act entirely, even *lechatchila*. The bench-dragging falls into the second category: creating a furrow in this manner is not the standard method of making a furrow, and doing it deliberately would itself be only a rabbinic violation — a *shinui* (irregular method). Since the prohibition is rabbinic even when intentional, Rabbi Shimon permits the unintended version. Extinguishing a lamp, however, is a Torah-level prohibition when done deliberately. Therefore Rabbi Shimon applies a rabbinic fence around even the unintended version and prohibits carrying the burning lamp.

This resolution will not survive intact. The Gemara will return tomorrow to challenge this distinction, since Rabbi Shimon is in fact universally understood to permit *aino mitkavein* — unintended acts — even where the intended version would be biblical. The Gemara's answer at this stage is a preliminary position that the next *daf* will address. We will resume there with the question: if Rabbi Shimon truly permits all unintended acts regardless of biblical or rabbinic status, what is his actual reason for prohibiting the moving of a burning lamp?
