# Yoma 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah Hutra/Dechuya

### Hutra vs. Dechuya

The discussion on this *daf* opens with one of the classic questions in the laws of *korbanot*: what is the nature of *tumah* in the context of a *korban tzibur*? The two positions are *tumah hutrah b'tzibur* and *tumah dechuya b'tzibur*. Is *tumah* simply permitted for a *korban tzibur* — meaning the Torah's prohibition against bringing a *korban b'tumah* was never stated for *korbanot tzibur* in the first place, so that bringing such a *korban* while *tamei* constitutes no violation whatsoever? That is the position of *hutra*: there is no prohibition, no impropriety, and nothing to apologize for. Or is the position *tumah dechuya*? According to this view, there is in fact a prohibition, and one is technically violating it, but the mandate to bring the *korban tzibur* overrides the prohibition. The violation is acknowledged even as it is permitted — and that acknowledgment carries practical consequences, because in an ideal world one would prefer to avoid the violation entirely, and that preference would shape certain decisions about minimizing the degree of *tumah* involved.

### Pikuach Nefesh Analogy

A structurally parallel question applies to *pikuach nefesh* on *Shabbos*. When one breaks *Shabbos* to save a life, is this *chilul Shabbos* that one is permitted to commit, or is it not considered *chilul Shabbos* at all — meaning it is precisely what one is supposed to do on *Shabbos* when a life is at stake, regardless of what activities are required? The question is not merely theoretical. If one believes that saving a life overrides *Shabbos* but still constitutes a technical violation, one might be tempted to minimize the violation even at the cost of increasing risk to the patient. The literature on *pikuach nefesh* is unequivocal: do not hesitate, do not make calculations, go ahead and do whatever is necessary. The dominant message is to act without delay. An important qualification does apply — if it is possible to accomplish the same goal in a manner that does not involve *chilul Shabbos* and this involves no additional difficulty or risk, one should certainly do so. But that caveat does not undercut the primary principle; it refines it at the margins.

### Tumah Meis vs. Yotzei

This question connects directly to the *Kohen Gadol*'s service on *Yom Kippur*. If he were to become *tamei*, he would proceed with the *avodah* regardless — the question is how much effort must be invested in preventing that outcome, and that depends precisely on whether we say *hutra* or *dechuya*. One further clarification is important at the outset: this entire debate concerns *tumas meis* or a similar externally contracted *tumah*, that is, *tumah* resulting from contact with something outside the person's body. A *tumah yotzei migufo* — such as *ba'al keri* for a man, or contact with a *niddah* — involves the transfer of a personal status rather than external contact, and such *tumah* is not overridden under any circumstances. Those cases must simply be avoided. The debate is limited to *tumas meis*.

### Melachot on Shabbat

A related question concerns the performance of *melachot* on *Shabbos* within the *Mikdash* for the sake of a *korban tzibur*. Is *Shabbos* overridden — *nidcheh* — for the *korban tzibur*, or does it simply not apply at all? While the same derivations in the *Gemara* that address *tumah* are applied to the *Shabbos* question as well, and the *pesukim* that permit bringing a *korban Pesach* or a *korban tzibur* on *Shabbos* are the same as those relevant to *tumah*, the *Gemara* does not appear to contain a direct discussion of whether the performance of *melachot* for a *korban tzibur* on *Shabbos* constitutes *hutra* or *dechuya*. There seems to be an implicit assumption that it is *hutra*.

## Rav Nachman vs. Sheshet

### Rav Nachman Extreme

The debate is between *Rav Nachman*, who holds *hutra*, and *Rav Sheshet*, who holds *dechuya*. To appreciate the full force of their disagreement, one must consider the more extreme formulation of their positions, which appears toward the end of 6b. *Ika de'amrei* — some say — that when *Rav Nachman* says *hutra*, he means it even in the most extreme case. The scenario: the *kohanim* who constitute today's shift, the *beis av*, include both *temeim* and *tehorim*, and they need to bring the *tamid*, which is a *korban tzibur* with a fixed time. *Rav Nachman*'s extreme position is that even so, one may have the *temeim* bring the *tamid*. Why bother asking the *tahor kohen* to do it? It is a *korban tamid*, a *korban tzibur* — there is simply no problem at all. This is a genuinely radical formulation: even when a *tahor* alternative is trivially available, *hutra* means there is no reason to prefer it.

### Minchat Ha-omer

*Rav Sheshet* asks: where do I derive my position of *dechuya*? He cites a *braita*: a *kohen* was standing and bringing the *minchat ha-omer* — a flour offering — and as he was holding it, it became *tamei*, perhaps because a *sheretz* fell into it. The *braita* states that he announces the problem and a substitute is brought. The text has an interesting marginal variant (*girsa*): rather than the *kohen* himself calling out, the people around him check whether a substitute is available; if so, they inform him, and if not, they tell him to proceed quietly. Either way, the operative point is the same — one prefers to obtain a *tahor* substitute. This demonstrates that even for a *korban tzibur* with a fixed time, there is a preference for *taharah*, which is precisely what *dechuya* would predict.

### Pikeach Ve-Shtok

The curious instruction — *hevei pikeach ve-shtok*, "be wise and keep quiet" — demands explanation. If bringing the *tamei* offering is fully permitted, why the instruction to remain silent? And if it is not permitted, how can silence make it acceptable? The *Gemara* notes the tension embedded in this phrase with the word *miha* — *ketani miha* — which signals that something here is not entirely straightforward. The phrase itself will be revisited repeatedly as the *sugya* develops.

### Sheyirayim Exception

*Rav Nachman* concedes one point. He says *modina* — he would admit — *heicha de'ika sheyirayim le'achilah*: where the *korban* has remnants (*sheyirayim*) that are intended to be eaten, he acknowledges the preference for *taharah*. The principle of *tumah hutrah b'tzibur* permits bringing the *korban b'tumah*, but it does not extend to *eating* it *b'tumah* — with one exception. One might ask: which *korban* may even be eaten *b'tumah*? The *korban Pesach*, as established in *Pesachim*, because the essential purpose of the *korban Pesach* is the *achilah*, not the *hakravah*. Every other *korban* — even when the *hakravah* is permitted *b'tumah* — may not be eaten *b'tumah*. Therefore, if the *korban* in question has *sheyirayim* that are eaten, there is good reason to prefer a *tahor* substitute: not because the *hakravah* itself demands it, but because one wants the *sheyirayim* to be available for eating rather than discarded. Importantly, *Rav Nachman* notes that in this context the discussion involves the *korban* becoming *tamei*, not the *kohen* — though for present purposes the practical difference is not yet significant.

### Minchat Nesachim

A second *braita* is cited in parallel. A *kohen* was bringing the *minchat nesachim* — the flour, oil, and wine that accompany certain *olot* and *shelamim* — for bulls, rams, and sheep, and it became *tamei* in his hand. The *braita* states that a substitute is to be sought, and if none is available, he is told *hevei pikeach ve-shtok* — keep quiet and proceed. This case is actually sharper than the *minchat ha-omer*: the *minchat nesachim* is entirely burned on the *mizbeach* and no *kometz* is taken; there is no *sheyirayim* for eating. The preference for *taharah* here cannot be explained by the *sheyirayim* rationale. The *Gemara* raises the question of which *korban* is under discussion. Is this perhaps the *musaf* of *Sukkot*, with its full complement of bulls, rams, and sheep — a paradigmatic *korban tzibur*? That would be a formidable challenge to *Rav Nachman*: if it is a *korban tzibur* with a fixed time, and we still prefer *taharah*, that supports *dechuya*.

## Fixed Time Key

### Par Avodah Zarah

*Rav Nachman* responds: no, we are not discussing a *korban musaf*. He recategorizes the three animals. The *parim* — the bulls — refer to the *par avodah zarah*, the bull brought as an *olah* when the entire *Klal Yisrael* sins with *avodah zarah*. This is indeed a *korban tzibur*, since it is brought on behalf of the community. Yet *af al gav de-tzibur hu* — even though it is a communal *korban* — *keivan delo kevi'a lei zman*, since it has no fixed time, *mahadrinan* — we pursue a *tahor* alternative. This is the critical point, stated explicitly: what determines whether *tumah* is overridden is not that a *korban* is a *korban tzibur*, but that it has a fixed time. A *korban tzibur* without a fixed time does not override *tumah*. This can be demonstrated from the opposite direction as well: a *korban Pesach* is brought by individuals, yet because it has a fixed time — and indeed because the entire Jewish people bring it simultaneously — it carries the character of a *korban tzibur*. Even more strikingly, the *korban* of *Aharon ha-Kohen* on *Yom Kippur* — which is personal, possibly funded from his own resources — overrides *tumah* entirely, because it must be brought that very day.

### Aaron's Ram

The *eilim* — rams — in the *braita* refer to *eilo shel Aharon*, Aaron's ram on *Yom Kippur*. This is a *korban yachid* with a fixed time. *De-af al gav de-kevi'a lei zman* — though it has a fixed time, *keivan de-yachid hu*, since it is a *korban yachid* and not a *korban tzibur*, *mahadrinan* — we prefer to bring it *b'taharah*. The *korban* does override *tumah* because of its fixed time, but because it lacks the additional dimension of being a *korban tzibur*, it does not receive the full force of *hutra*, and we would prefer *taharah* when possible.

### Sheep with Omer

The *kevasim* — sheep — are identified as the sheep brought together with the *omer* on the second day of *Pesach*. The discussion thus circles back to the *minchat ha-omer* discussed earlier. Here the rationale for preferring *taharah* is *de'ika sheyirayim le'achilah* — as *Rav Nachman* already conceded, when there are *sheyirayim* to be eaten, the preference for *taharah* exists even within his position of *hutra*.

### Fixed Time Principle

The structure of *Rav Nachman*'s response can now be stated as a general principle: the override of *tumah* — whether framed as *hutra* or *dechuya* — is predicated on a fixed time, not on the *tzibur* character of the *korban*. A non-fixed-time *korban* does not override *tumah* or *Shabbos*, even if it is a *korban tzibur*. A fixed-time *korban* does override *tumah* and *Shabbos* — whether or not it is a *korban tzibur*, though a *korban yachid* with a fixed time earns somewhat less deference than a *korban tzibur* with a fixed time. The explanations for the *braita*'s preference for *taharah* are therefore subsidiary: in one case the *korban* has *sheyirayim*; in another it is a *yachid* *korban* even if fixed-time; and in the third it is a *korban tzibur* without a fixed time. None of these cases presents the clean scenario of a *korban tzibur* with a fixed time for which *Rav Nachman* maintains *hutra* without qualification.

This still leaves a pressing question, however. The *par avodah zarah* is not a fixed-time *korban*. If the override of *tumah* depends entirely on fixed time, and not on *tzibur* status alone, why does the *braita* instruct *hevei pikeach ve-shtok* and permit it to be brought *b'tumah* at all? By *Rav Nachman*'s own logic, a non-fixed-time *korban* should simply be invalid when brought *b'tumah*. This is the question that opens the next discussion.

## Ritzuy Tzitz

### Person Tamei Rule

*Tosafot* provides the resolution. There is a fundamental distinction between two types of *tumah* situations: cases in which the *person* — the *kohen* performing the *avodah* — is *tamei*, and cases in which the *object* — the *korban* itself — is *tamei*. When the *kohen* is *tamei*, the only mechanism that permits the *avodah* is the override rooted in fixed time. If the *korban* does not have a fixed time, there is no override, no *hutra*, no *dechuya* — the *kohen* simply cannot perform the *avodah*.

### Object Tamei Exception

When the *korban* itself is *tamei* — the blood, the meat, the flour offering — a distinct principle applies: *ritzuy tzitz*. The *tzitz* that the *Kohen Gadol* wears on his forehead, inscribed *Kodesh l'Hashem*, functions to validate *korbanot* that were brought while *tamei*. This principle applies regardless of whether the *korban* is a *korban tzibur* or a *korban yachid*, and regardless of whether the *korban* has a fixed time. The *tzitz* operates specifically when the *objects* are *tamei* — the blood, the meat, the fat — not when the *people* are *tamei*. For the *par avodah zarah*, then, when its *minchat nesachim* became *tamei*, the *ritzuy tzitz* is what permits the *avodah* to proceed and validates the *korban bedieved*.

### Keep It Quiet

This also explains the instruction *hevei pikeach ve-shtok*. The *korban* is not supposed to be brought *b'tumah*; for a non-fixed-time *korban*, there is no override when the objects are *tamei*. But *bedieved*, once the *korban* is in the process of being brought, the *ritzuy tzitz* will validate it after the fact. The instruction "keep quiet" reflects precisely this *bedieved* character: the impropriety is acknowledged — one does not announce that a *tamei korban* is being brought — but the validity of the *korban* is secured through the *tzitz*. The principle of *sha'at ha-dechak k'bedieved dami* — a situation of constraint functions like *bedieved* — may further explain why the *kohen* is permitted to proceed rather than halt entirely. An important distinction must be maintained here: the *ritzuy tzitz* does not atone for a sin the way a *korban* atones; rather, it validates the *korban*, declaring it acceptable. The concern of the *Beis HaMikdash* is whether *bedieved* the *avodah* stands, and the *tzitz* answers that question in the affirmative.

## Fixed Time Key…

### Korban Categories

The framework that emerges from this discussion can be organized into four categories. First, a *korban tzibur* with a fixed time — such as the *tamid*, the *musaf*, or the *Yom Kippur* communal offerings — fully overrides both *tumah* and *Shabbos*. According to *Rav Nachman*, this is *hutra* in the fullest sense: even *lechatchila* there is no need to prefer a *tahor kohen* over a *tamei* one. Second, a fixed-time *korban yachid* — such as the *eilo shel Aharon* on *Yom Kippur* or the *asiris ha-eifah* of the *Kohen Gadol* — also overrides *tumah* and *Shabbos* by virtue of its fixed time, but because it lacks the *tzibur* dimension, there is a preference for *taharah* even according to *Rav Nachman*. Third, a *korban tzibur* without a fixed time — such as the *par avodah zarah* — does not override *tumah* or *Shabbos*. If the *kohen* is *tamei*, the *avodah* is simply invalid. If the *korban* object is *tamei*, the *ritzuy tzitz* will validate it *bedieved*, and the instruction *hevei pikeach ve-shtok* applies. Fourth, a non-fixed-time *korban yachid* — the vast majority of individual *korbanot* — similarly cannot override *tumah* or *Shabbos*, and if the person is *tamei*, the *korban* is invalid. If the object is *tamei* and the *ritzuy tzitz* is functioning, there may be *bedieved* validity, though in practice the communal dimension present even in the *par avodah zarah* case does not exist here.

One might ask how *temei* *kohanim* can be present in the *azarah* at all in the scenario where *Rav Nachman* says the *tamei kohanim* may bring the *tamid*. One plausible construction: the *kohanim* begin as *tehorim*, and one becomes *tamei* in the midst of performing the *tamid* — perhaps through an accidental *neveila* when the *shechita* goes wrong — and the question is whether the others must stop or whether he may continue alongside them. The *heter* to be in the *azarah* while *tamei* would be coextensive with the *heter* to bring the *korban*, and only applies for the purpose of that specific *avodah*.

## Braita: Dam Tamei

### Dam Shitma

The *Gemara* now introduces a *braita* dealing with a case of *dam she-nitma*: the blood of an *olah* became *tamei* and was thrown on the *mizbeach b'shogeg*, unintentionally — either because the *tumah* was unintentional or because the throwing itself was inadvertent, a question debated in the *Gemara*. If *b'shogeg*, the *braita* rules *hurtzah* — it is valid. If *b'meizid*, intentionally, *lo hurtzah* — it is not valid. The surface meaning of *lo hurtzah* is that the *korban* is entirely invalid. However, as the *Gemara* will clarify, the *ritzuy tzitz* ensures *bedieved* validity even in a case of *b'meizid*, and *lo hurtzah* is interpreted elsewhere to mean that the owners or the *kohanim* are penalized — they do not derive benefit from the *korban* — even though the *korban* itself stands. The straightforward question is: if we are dealing with a *korban tzibur*, why should the distinction between *shogeg* and *meizid* matter at all? If *tumah hutrah b'tzibur*, nothing problematic happened. The *Gemara* resolves this immediately: *ki tanya hahee d'yachid* — this *braita* is about a *korban yachid*. The validity *bedieved* in the *shogeg* case is because of the *tzitz*.

### Tzitz Works Objects

The *Gemara* introduces the principle of *ritzuy tzitz* directly: the *tzitz* functions only when the objects — the blood, the meat, the fat — are *tamei*, not when the people are *tamei*. The *korban* objects that have become *tamei* are validated by the *tzitz bedieved*. When the *kohen* himself is *tamei*, the *tzitz* cannot help; only the override from fixed time applies in that scenario.

### Tzitz Braita

The *Gemara* cites a *braita* that elaborates the *ritzuy tzitz* principle: it applies to the blood, the meat, the fat — all the constituent parts of *korbanot* that may become *tamei* — whether the *korban* is *yachid* or *tzibur*, whether the *tumah* was *b'shogeg* or *b'meizid*, *b'ones* or *b'ratzon*. In all these cases, the *tzitz* renders the *korban* valid. Note that this *braita* states the *tzitz* works *bein b'yachid bein b'tzibur* — for both individual and communal *korbanot*.

### Non-Fixed Tzibur

This raises an immediate question: why does the *braita* find it necessary to state that the *tzitz* works for a *korban tzibur*? If *tumah hutrah b'tzibur*, then there is no problem in the first place — the *korban tzibur* brought *b'tumah* is perfectly valid without any assistance from the *tzitz*. Who needs the *tzitz* for a *korban tzibur*? *Rav Nachman* responds: *amar lach Rav Nachman — ki tanya ha-tzitz mirtzeh ad'yachid*. The mention of *korban tzibur* in the *braita* was incidental; the primary purpose of the *tzitz* rule is for the *korban yachid* case. Once the *braita* was stating that the *tzitz* validates *tamei* *korbanot*, it included *korban tzibur* for completeness — but the *tzibur* does not actually need the *tzitz*. Alternatively — *ve-iba'is eima* — even if you want to say the mention of *korban tzibur* is meaningful, it refers specifically to *korban tzibur* without a fixed time, such as the *par avodah zarah*. That category does not have *tumah hutrah* or *tumah dechuya* — it has no override at all based on its own character — and therefore when the *korban* objects become *tamei*, the *tzitz* is what validates them *bedieved*.

## Pigul/Notar

### Pigul Disqualified

Having established that the *tzitz* validates *korbanot* that were brought *b'tumah*, the *Gemara* asks: how do we know the *tzitz* applies specifically to *tumah* and not to other forms of invalidity? The *pasuk* that describes the *tzitz* — *v'nasa Aharon et avon ha-kodashim* — uses the language of sin and atonement without specifying what type of invalidity it addresses. One might suppose the *tzitz* renders acceptable all invalidly brought *korbanot*. The *Gemara* systematically eliminates alternatives. *Pigul*: a *korban* brought with the intent to eat it at the wrong time — the *pasuk* states explicitly *lo yeratzeh*, it shall not be accepted. There is no *ritzuy* possible for *pigul*; the *pasuk* forecloses it. The *tzitz* cannot override an explicit *lo yeratzeh*.

### Notar Issue

*Notar*: the sin of leaving meat from a *korban* past the permitted time of eating. The *pasuk* states *lo yechashev* — it shall not be reckoned. This is from the same *parasha* as *pigul*. Moreover, *notar* arises only after the *korban* was already valid and accepted — it is a post-hoc problem, occurring after the *avodah* was completed. There is no need for the *tzitz* to validate the *korban* for a problem that arises after the *korban* is already good. Both *pigul* and *notar* here likely encompass, between them, the two related forms of invalid intent: an intention of the wrong time (*pigul* proper, or *chutz lizmano*) and an intention of the wrong place (*chutz limkomo*), which are addressed by the same *pesukim* of *lo yeratzeh* and *lo yechashev*, as discussed extensively in *Rashi* and *Tosafot*.

### Tumah Only

By process of elimination, *tumah* is the only remaining category of invalidity to which *ritzuy tzitz* can apply. *Pigul* is explicitly excluded by *lo yeratzeh*. *Notar* either arises too late or is similarly excluded. *Tumah* alone is left as the form of invalidity that the *tzitz* can address.

### Hutrah Logic

The *braita* does not merely stop with this logical conclusion but adds a substantive reason why *tumah* in particular is the appropriate domain for *ritzuy tzitz*. The *braita* states: *she-hutrah mi-klalah b'tzibur* — because we know that *tumah* has instances where it is set aside entirely, namely the *korban tzibur* context. There is an established precedent, a category within the law, where *tumah* does not stand as an absolute barrier. Given that *tzitz* is going to function for some category of invalidity, and given that *pigul* and *notar* are explicitly excluded, it makes sense — indeed it is fitting — that *tumah* should be the category where *tzitz* provides its assistance, precisely because *tumah* is the type of invalidity that the Torah itself has shown some flexibility about in the context of *korban tzibur*.

One significant observation: this *braita* uses the term *hutrah* — *she-hutrah mi-klalah b'tzibur* — and the *Gemara* recognizes that this precise vocabulary, in a context where the *Rav Nachman / Rav Sheshet* debate uses those same technical terms, constitutes apparent support for *Rav Nachman*. The *Gemara*'s response is *tannai hi* — it is itself a debate among *tannaim*. *Rav Sheshet* holds that he has *tannaim* who use the language of *dechuya*, as evidenced by the *braitot* on the earlier *amud*. The *braita* here that says *hutrah* represents the other side of that *tannaitic* debate. It is only the specific use of the word *hutrah* in the *braita* that constitutes a challenge to *Rav Sheshet* — not the halakhic mechanics of how the *tzitz* functions, which are unaffected by whether one holds *hutra* or *dechuya*.

## Abaye's Framework

### Implication Analysis

*Abaye* now enters to frame the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* — which concerns whether the *tzitz* must be worn to function — in terms of the *hutra/dechuya* debate. If one holds *hutra*, the *korban* brought *b'tumah* for a *korban tzibur* is valid without any assistance; the *tzitz* is not needed in that scenario and its wearing or non-wearing is irrelevant to the outcome. If one holds *dechuya*, the *korban* is valid but remains in a liminal state — there was something problematic about it — and the *tzitz* can play a meaningful supporting role. *Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds the *tzitz* must be worn on the forehead to function, implies a certain understanding: since the *tzitz* must actively be worn to have an effect, its contribution is a real and necessary one. This is more consistent with a *dechuya* framework in which the *tzitz* is doing actual work. *Rabbi Shimon*, who holds the *tzitz* always functions even when not being worn, suggests a different picture: the very existence of the *tzitz* in proper condition suffices, pointing perhaps to a *dechuya* understanding in which, even though the validity is acknowledged to be in a somewhat lower register, the *tzitz* functions as a constant structural feature of *korban* validity rather than a moment-by-moment active intervention.

### Broken Tzitz

*Abaye* states that one point of agreement between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* is clear: *venishbar ha-tzitz — de-chulei alma lo peligi de-lo mirtzeh*. If the *tzitz* is broken — if it no longer exists as a functional object — all agree that it does not make *korbanot* acceptable. A broken *tzitz* has no *ritzuy* function.

### On Peg Debate

The debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* is located precisely in the case where *detalui be-kuchta* — the *tzitz* is hanging on its peg, sitting in the *Kohen Gadol*'s storage, intact but not being worn. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that only when it is on the forehead does it function. *Rabbi Shimon* holds that even when it is hanging on its peg, so long as it is fit to be worn, it functions.

## Wearing Tzitz

### Yehuda: Must Wear

*Rabbi Yehuda* derives his position from the juxtaposition of two phrases in the *pasuk*: *al mitzcho* — on his forehead — and *venasa Aharon et avon ha-kodashim* — he will lift off the sin of the sanctified things. Reading these two phrases together, *Rabbi Yehuda* understands that the *tzitz* lifts off the *avon* of the *korbanot* specifically when it is on the forehead. Only the act of wearing the *tzitz* on the forehead activates its function.

### Shimon: Always Works

*Rabbi Shimon* derives his position from a different part of the same *pasuk*: *tamid leratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* — it shall always be for acceptance before God. He reads *tamid leratzon* as a standalone statement: the *tzitz* always makes *korbanot* acceptable, regardless of whether it is being worn at any given moment. On his reading, *al mitzcho* tells us the placement of the *tzitz* — on the forehead — while *tamid leratzon* independently declares its constant efficacy.

### Constant Consciousness

*Rabbi Yehuda* must account for the word *tamid*, which on its face seems to imply something constant even when not being worn. He explains: *hahu tamid she-lo yassiach da'ato mimeno* — *tamid* means that when the *tzitz* is worn, the *Kohen Gadol* must be constantly conscious of it, never allowing his attention to lapse. It does not mean the *tzitz* must be worn continuously, but that wearing it must be accompanied by uninterrupted awareness.

### Tefillin Analogy

The *Gemara* draws an explicit parallel to *tefillin*. *Rabba bar Rav Huna* teaches: *chayav adam lemashesh be-tefillin be-chol sha'ah ve-sha'ah* — a person wearing *tefillin* is obligated to touch them constantly, continuously invoking his awareness of them. This is learned *kal va-chomer* from the *tzitz*: if the *tzitz*, which contains only one divine name — *Kodesh l'Hashem* — demands *al mitzcho tamid*, understood as constant consciousness when wearing, then *tefillin*, which contain many divine names, certainly demand constant awareness when worn. *Tosafot* notes that this is not a formally rigorous *kal va-chomer* — the divine names of *tefillin* are concealed within boxes while the name on the *tzitz* is exposed, and the *bigdei kehunah* may carry distinct demands from personal *mitzvot* — but the conceptual comparison between *tzitz* and *tefillin* is genuine and significant. There is a broader parallel worth noting: just as *tefillin shel rosh* corresponds in certain respects to the *tzitz*, the *tzitzit* parallels the *bigdei kehunah* more broadly. These analogies suggest that even in the absence of the *Beis HaMikdash*, the personal *mitzvot* of *tefillin* and *tzitzit* carry forward something of the *kedushah* of the *Mikdash*'s service.

### Shimon: Fitness

*Rabbi Shimon* responds to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position by clarifying what *al mitzcho venasa* means in his own reading. He does not dispute that the *tzitz* has a connection to the forehead; he argues that the *pasuk* establishes fitness, not active wearing, as the operative criterion. *Ra'ui le-metzach mirtze — she-ein ra'ui le-metzach eino mirtze*: when the *tzitz* is fit to be placed on the forehead — meaning it is whole and intact — it functions. When it is not fit — meaning it is broken — it does not function. The phrase *al mitzcho venasa* thus tells us the condition of fitness rather than the requirement of actual wearing. This is precisely the line that *Abaye* identified: all agree that a broken *tzitz* does not function (*la-apukei nishbar ha-tzitz de-lo mirtze*), while the debate is about a *tzitz* that is intact but not being worn.

*Rabbi Shimon* reinforces his position with the case of the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. There are times during the *Yom Kippur avodah* when the *Kohen Gadol* is not wearing the *tzitz* — specifically during the *avodah* performed in the inner sanctuary, the *Heichal*, where he wears only the white garments and not the golden ones. If a *korban* were to become *tamei* during those moments, *Rabbi Shimon* argues, the *korban* would still be valid, because the *tzitz* is fit to be worn even though it is not being worn at that moment. *Rabbi Yehuda* responds that the *Yom Kippur* case is not evidence for the *tzitz*'s function at all: *hanach le-Kohen Gadol be-Yom Kippurim she-tumah hutrah lo b'tzibbur* — the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* operates under the principle of *hutra b'tzibbur* for a fixed-time *korban*. The validity of those *korbanot* when *tamei* does not depend on the *tzitz* in any way; it follows from the *tumah hutrah b'tzibur* principle itself. Therefore *Rabbi Yehuda* cannot be refuted by that case — it provides no information about whether the *tzitz* functions when not worn.

The *Gemara* draws out the implication: *micklal de-Rabbi Yehuda — Rabbi Shimon savar tumah dechuya hi b'tzibbur*. If *Rabbi Shimon* thought the *Yom Kippur* case was a valid proof for the *tzitz* functioning when not worn, that means *Rabbi Shimon* holds *dechuya*. If it were *hutra*, the *Yom Kippur* case is trivially valid and the *tzitz* is irrelevant; there would be nothing to prove. The fact that *Rabbi Shimon* thinks the *tzitz* has a role to play even there — even for the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* — reflects his view that *dechuya* applies: there is something slightly in need of correction, and the *tzitz* provides that correction. *Rabbi Yehuda*, on the other hand, says the *tzitz* has no role on *Yom Kippur* for that *avodah*, because *hutra* makes the whole question moot.

This settles the alignment *Abaye* identified: *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *hutra*, and therefore the *tzitz* must be actively worn when it does function, since the *tzitz* is not needed in the main *korban tzibur* scenario at all. *Rabbi Shimon* holds *dechuya*, and therefore the *tzitz* has a perpetual structural role — it is always doing something, even when not actively worn, because the validity of *korbanot* brought *b'tumah* always remains in a *dechuya* register that the *tzitz* continuously addresses. The requirement of fitness — being fit to be placed on the forehead — is what *Rabbi Shimon* derives from *al mitzcho venasa*, and this fitness, not active wearing, is what activates the *ritzuy*.
