# Menachot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shirayim After Kemitza

### Debate Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שִׁירַיִם שֶׁחָסְרוּ בֵּין קְמִיצָה לְהַקְטָרָה מַקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ עֲלֵיהֶן, אוֹתָן שִׁירַיִם מָה הֵן בַּאֲכִילָה?
>
> **Translation:** **As a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: According **to the statement of** the one **who says that** in a case where the **remainder** of a meal offering **became lacking between the removal of the handful and** its **burning** upon the altar, the priest **burns** the **handful on** account of this remainder, concerning the **remainder itself, what** is its status **with regard to consumption,** i.e., may the remainder be eaten?

The daf opens in the middle of a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning a *mincha* in which some of the *shirayim* — the remainder of the grain offering left after *kemitza* — went missing between the time of *kemitza* and *haktara*. Reish Lakish holds that the offering is invalid, deriving this from a special *pasuk*, even though in an analogous case involving an animal sacrifice, where some of the meat or fat remained, everyone would agree the offering is valid. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees, holding that the case is indeed comparable to the animal offering, and that one may proceed to offer the *kometz* even if not all of the *shirayim* is present. As will emerge later, there remains a separate question about whether the *shirayim* may be eaten in such a case — but at minimum, Rabbi Yochanan maintains that the *haktara* of the *kometz* may proceed.

### Rav Pappa's Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב פָּפָּא וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר שְׁמַעְיָה לְרַב פָּפָּא: מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בְּמִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר דְּצִיבּוּר הִיא, וּפְלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa** was once **sitting and saying this *halakha*,** and he noted that Reish Lakish was silent and did not respond to Rabbi Yochanan's objections by differentiating between a communal offering and that of an individual, and that Rav Adda bar Ahava derived from Reish Lakish's silence that a lack in the measure of a meal offering is tantamount to a blemish. **Rav Yosef bar Shemaya said to Rav Pappa: Are we not dealing** with a disagreement **that** exists between **Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish** even **in** the case of **the *omer* meal offering, which is a communal** offering? **And** even so, they **disagree.** Accordingly, Reish Lakish could not have responded to the objections of Rabbi Yochanan by distinguishing between a communal and an individual offering, and therefore one cannot derive from his silence that a meal offering lacking in its measure is like a blemished animal.

The *Gemara* relates that Rav Pappa was sitting and recounting this dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Rav Yosef bar Shemaya challenged him: might not the entire debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concern specifically the *minchat ha'omer*, which is a *korban tzibur*? This question connects to a discussion that had concluded the previous daf, where the *Gemara* had raised a proof to Rabbi Yochanan from the case of *Lechem HaPanim*.

### Lechem HaPanim Proof

The proof from *Lechem HaPanim* was as follows: if some of the loaves went missing, it is nonetheless evident that the *bazichin* — the bowls of *levona* which function analogously to the *kometz* — may still be offered. Reish Lakish might have responded that *Lechem HaPanim* is a *korban tzibur* and thus subject to different rules, but the *Gemara* rejected this: a *korban tzibur* receives special dispensation only in cases of *tuma*, not in cases of a deficient offering. The present passage adds a further reason why Reish Lakish could not have offered that distinction — namely, that the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish may itself have concerned a *korban tzibur*, specifically the *minchat omer*. If so, invoking the *korban tzibur* status of *Lechem HaPanim* would not have answered anything. The *Gemara* leaves this without a definitive resolution but notes it as an additional reason the distinction could not have been made. The conclusion stands: *Lechem HaPanim* provides a sound proof in support of Rabbi Yochanan, that even when some of the offering is missing, the *kometz* may be brought.

### Two Braitot on Chaser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַלְכִּיּוֹ, תָּנָא חֲדָא: ״מִסׇּלְתָּהּ״ – שֶׁאִם חָסְרָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא פְּסוּלָה, ״מִשַּׁמְנָהּ״ – שֶׁאִם חָסְרָה כׇּל שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וְהַנּוֹתֶרֶת מִן הַמִּנְחָה״ – פְּרָט לַמִּנְחָה שֶׁחָסְרָה הִיא, וְשֶׁחָסַר קוּמְצָהּ, וְשֶׁלֹּא הִקְטִיר מִלְּבוֹנָתָהּ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Malkiyu says** that **one** *baraita* **taught:** The verse states: "And he shall remove…**of its fine flour"** (Leviticus 2:2). This indicates **that if** the meal offering **lacks any amount** of its flour, it is **disqualified** and the priest may not remove a handful from it. Similarly, the term: **"Of its oil"** (Leviticus 2:2), teaches **that if** the meal offering **lacks any amount** of its oil, it is **disqualified. And it is taught** in **another** *baraita* that the verse that states: **"But that which is left of the meal offering** shall be Aaron's and his sons'" (Leviticus 2:3), teaches that the remainder of the meal offering is given to the priests only if there was initially a whole meal offering, **to the exclusion of a meal offering that was lacking, or whose handful was lacking, or from which** the priest **did not burn any frankincense.** In such cases, the meal offering is disqualified and its remainder may not be consumed by the priests.

Rav Malkio introduces a new layer of analysis. One *braita* teaches from the words *misolta* — "from its fine flour" — and *mishemena* — "from its oil" — that if any amount of the flour or oil of the *mincha* is lacking, it is invalid. A second *braita* teaches from the words *vehanoteret min hamincha* — "and what remains of the *mincha* shall be for Aaron and his sons" — that this comes to exclude a case where some of the *mincha* went missing, whether it was the main offering itself that was lacking, or the *kometz* that was deficient, or the *levona* that was not offered at all. (In the case of *levona*, the *Gemara* notes, the rule runs in the opposite direction from the *kometz*: even a minimal amount of *levona* suffices, and this will be addressed later.) The question thus arises: why does the Torah need two separate *psukim* to convey the problem of *chaser*?

### Pesuk Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי בַּחֲסֵרוֹת לְמָה לִי? לָאו חַד לְמִנְחָה שֶׁחָסְרָה קוֹדֶם קְמִיצָה, וְחַד לְשִׁירַיִם שֶׁחָסְרוּ בֵּין קְמִיצָה לְהַקְטָרָה, לָא, חַד לְמִנְחָה שֶׁחָסְרָה קוֹדֶם קְמִיצָה, דְּאִי מֵבִיא מִבֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנָּה – אִין, וְאִי לָא – לָא, וְחַד לְשִׁירַיִם שֶׁחָסְרוּ בֵּין קְמִיצָה לְהַקְטָרָה, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּמַקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ עֲלֵיהֶן – אוֹתָן שִׁירַיִם אֲסוּרִים לַאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Malkiyu asks: **Why do I** need **two verses to** disqualify meal offerings that are **lacking?** Is it **not** correct to say that **one** verse is referring **to a meal offering that became lacking before the removal of the handful,** i.e., that one may not refill it, **and one** verse is referring **to** the **remainder** of a meal offering **that became lacking between the removal of the handful and** its **burning** upon the altar, and it teaches that one may not burn a handful on account of such a remainder? The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No; one** verse is indeed referring **to a meal offering that became lacking before the removal of the handful,** but it is not teaching that this meal offering is disqualified. Rather, this verse indicates **that if he brings** flour **from his home and fills it** so that the meal offering is no longer lacking, then **yes,** it is fit, **but if** he does **not** bring more flour, it is **not** fit. **And one** verse is indeed referring **to** the **remainder** of a meal offering **that became lacking between the removal of the handful and** its **burning** upon the altar; nevertheless, it does not indicate that the handful may not be burned. Rather, it teaches **that even though** the priest **burns** the **handful on** account of such a remainder, **that remainder is prohibited for consumption.**

If this reading is correct, it would appear to contradict Rabbi Yochanan in both directions. The first *pasuk* would seem to contradict his position, discussed earlier, that if the *mincha* was lacking before *kemitza* one may refill it and proceed — for the *pasuk* appears to invalidate *kemitza* from a deficient *mincha*. But the *Gemara* resolves this: what the first *pasuk* teaches is that if one performed *kemitza* while the *mincha* was still lacking, the *kemitza* is invalid. Rabbi Yochanan never permitted taking *kemitza* from a deficient *mincha* — he only permitted *refilling* the deficiency beforehand. Both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree that *kemitza* from a deficient quantity is invalid; they dispute only whether one may restore the missing amount prior to *kemitza*.

As for the second *pasuk*, one might think it proves Reish Lakish correct that when *shirayim* go missing between *kemitza* and *haktara*, the *kometz* may not be offered. The *Gemara* resolves this as well: the second *pasuk* is not invalidating the *haktara* of the *kometz*. Rather, it is teaching that even according to Rabbi Yochanan, who permits the *haktara*, there is a consequence for the *shirayim*: *vehanoteret min hamincha* — "what remains of the *mincha*" — implies the *shirayim* must come from a full and intact *mincha* in order to be *yochlu Aharon u'vanav*, eligible for priestly consumption. If some of the *shirayim* went missing before the *kometz* was offered, the remaining *shirayim* cannot be eaten, even though the *haktara* of the *kometz* itself is permitted.

### Eating Shirayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַנּוֹתֶרֶת״, וְלֹא הַנּוֹתֶרֶת מִן הַנּוֹתֶרֶת. וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: ״מֵהַמִּנְחָה״ – מִנְחָה שֶׁהָיְתָה כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ze'eiri said** that **the verse states: "But that which is left** of the meal offering shall be Aaron's and his sons'," which teaches that the priests may consume the remainder of a meal offering, **but not the remainder of the remainder,** i.e., if the remainder became lacking before the handful was burned, the remaining part of the remainder may not be consumed. Alternatively, **Rabbi Yannai says** that **"of the meal offering** shall be Aaron's and his sons'" indicates that the priests may consume only the remainder of **a meal offering that was previously** whole when the handful was burned, not the remainder of the remainder.

The *Gemara* now explores this question directly: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that the *kometz* may be offered even when *shirayim* went missing between *kemitza* and *haktara*, what is the status of those remaining *shirayim* with respect to eating?

Zeiri rules that they may not be eaten. He derives this from *vehanoteret* — "what remains shall be eaten" — and not *hanoteret min hanoteret*, "what remains from what remains." Once the *shirayim* are themselves a reduced remainder, they no longer qualify for priestly consumption.

Rav Yannai offers an additional formulation: *mehamincha*, from the *mincha* that already was — meaning the *mincha* as it stood in its full state. Rashi provides two readings of Rav Yannai's position. In one reading, Rav Yannai disputes Zeiri and holds that as long as the *mincha* was intact at the time of *kemitza*, the *shirayim* may be eaten even if the offering of the *kometz* occurred when the *shirayim* were no longer complete — with Rabbi Yochanan then siding with Zeiri against eating the *shirayim*. In the other reading, Rav Yannai agrees with Zeiri that the *shirayim* cannot be eaten but simply emphasizes a different *drasha* from the *pasuk*.

In sum: Reish Lakish holds that when *shirayim* go missing between *kemitza* and *haktara*, the *kometz* may not be offered at all. Rabbi Yochanan holds the *kometz* may be offered — but the *Gemara*'s analysis suggests that even Rabbi Yochanan would agree the remaining *shirayim* cannot be eaten.

## Right Hand Kemitza

### Mishnah: Left Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָמַץ בִּשְׂמֹאל – מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If *kemitza* was performed with the left hand, the offering is invalid. The discussion that follows will occupy the remainder of the daf, tracing the requirement to use the right hand for *kemitza* and for other priestly acts.

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah*, which states that if *kemitza* was performed with the left hand, the offering is invalid. The discussion that follows will occupy the remainder of the daf, tracing the requirement to use the right hand for *kemitza* and for other priestly acts.

### Kaf Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיַּקְרֵב אֶת הַמִּנְחָה וַיְמַלֵּא כַפּוֹ מִמֶּנָּה״ – כַּף זֶה אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַהוּ, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן מִלּוֹג הַשָּׁמֶן וְיָצַק עַל כַּף הַכֹּהֵן הַשְּׂמָאלִית״ – כָּאן שְׂמָאלִית, הָא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כַּף״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that it is derived from **that** which **the verse states: "And the meal offering was presented; and he filled his hand from it"** (Leviticus 9:17). Rabbi Zeira explains: **This hand, I do not know what it is,** i.e., the left hand or the right. **When** the verse **states** with regard to the atonement of a leper: **"And the priest shall take of the *log* of oil, and pour it into the palm of his own left hand"** (Leviticus 14:15), it mentions the left hand. One can therefore derive that **here,** where the verse specifies, the priest must use his **left** hand, whereas in **any place where it is stated** in a verse **"hand"** without specification, it is referring **only** to the **right** hand.

From where do we derive that *kemitza* requires the right hand? Rabbi Zeira cites the *pasuk*: *vehikriv et hamincha vemilei kapo mimena* — "he shall bring the *mincha* forward and fill his *kaf* from it." The word *kaf* — palm — is unmodified. Rabbi Zeira argues: I do not know on my own whether *kaf* means the right or left palm. But when the Torah speaks of the *metzora* offering, it states *veyatzak al kaf hakohen hasmalit* — the oil is poured onto the *left* palm of the *kohen*. Since the Torah was compelled to specify "left" in that context, we derive that *kaf* without a modifier means the right hand. This is the same logical structure the *Gemara* employs elsewhere — as in the case of witnesses, where the Torah had to say "a *single* witness," demonstrating that *eid* alone implies two — so here, specifying "left" implies that the unmodified term defaults to the right.

### Left Hand Redundancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ? ״שְׂמָאלִית״ אַחֲרִינָא כְּתִיב. וְאֵימָא: אֵין מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט אֶלָּא לְרַבּוֹת? ״שְׂמָאלִית״ אַחֲרִינָא כְּתִיב. כָּאן שְׂמָאלִית, וְאֵין אַחֵר שְׂמָאלִית. וְאֵימָא: אַדְּרַבָּה, מָה כָּאן שְׂמָאלִית – אַף בְּעָלְמָא נָמֵי שְׂמָאלִית! אַרְבָּעָה ״שְׂמָאלִית״ כְּתִיבִי: תְּרֵי בְּעָנִי, וּתְרֵי בְּעָשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** how can this conclusion be derived from the verse discussing the atonement of a leper? This verse **is necessary for itself,** i.e., to teach that the oil must be poured on the priest's left hand, not his right. The Gemara answers that **another** mention of the **left** hand **is written** in the same passage (see Leviticus 14:16). From this additional mention of the left hand it may be derived that any unspecified reference to a hand is referring to the right hand. The Gemara challenges: **But say** that according to the hermeneutical principle that one **restriction after** another **restriction** serves **only to amplify,** it should be inferred from the additional specification of the left hand that the oil may be poured onto the priest's right hand. The Gemara responds: Yet **another** specification of the **left** hand **is written** in that passage (see Leviticus 14:26). Accordingly, the hermeneutical principle that one restriction after another serves only to amplify does not apply. Instead, the verse indicates that **here** the **left** hand is required, **and there is no other** instance where the Torah requires the **left** hand. The Gemara challenges: **But** one can **say** that **on the contrary, just as here** the **left** hand is required, **so too generally,** the **left** hand is required **as well.** The Gemara responds: **Four** mentions of the **left** hand **are written** in the passage, **two with regard to a poor** leper (Leviticus 14:26–27), **and two with regard to a wealthy** one (Leviticus 14:15–16). Accordingly, three of the specifications serve to indicate that the oil may be poured only onto the left hand, while the additional mention serves to restrict the use of the left hand to the case of a leper alone.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this derivation. The word *smalit* — left — in the *metzora* passage is needed simply to establish that *this particular act* requires the left palm; it cannot simultaneously serve as an inference about the general default meaning of *kaf*. The *Gemara* responds that *smalit* appears a second time in that passage, a redundancy that allows one instance to establish the requirement for that act and the second to serve as the inference. The *Gemara* then raises a further objection: perhaps two mentions of *smalit* should be read as a *mi'ut achar mi'ut*, an exclusion following an exclusion, which classically comes to *broaden* — in this case, to teach that even the right hand is acceptable for catching the oil. The *Gemara* replies that *smalit* actually appears a *third* time — in the parallel passage describing the *metzora* who brings the less expensive bird offering — and a third mention refutes the reading of broadening. Then a further question arises: perhaps three times is not refutation but emphasis, establishing that left is the paradigm *everywhere*, not merely here. To this, the *Gemara* replies that *smalit* is in fact written a *fourth* time — two mentions in the passage of the wealthy *metzora* and two in that of the poor one — and the accumulation of four repetitions establishes both that left is strictly required *here* and that *kaf* elsewhere defaults to the right.

### Yirmiya's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: ״עַל בֹּהֶן יָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית וְעַל בֹּהֶן רַגְלוֹ הַיְמָנִית״ דִּכְתִיב בְּשֶׁמֶן דִּמְצוֹרָע עָשִׁיר לְמָה לִּי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zeira:** According to your opinion, one interprets homiletically the additional specifications in the passage discussing the purification of a leper. If so, there is another apparently superfluous verse: **"Upon the thumb of his right hand, and upon the big toe of his right foot"** (Leviticus 14:17), **which is written with regard to** the placement of the **oil** upon the right thumb and big toe **of a wealthy leper. Why do I** need the verse to specify that the oil is placed on the leper's right thumb and big toe?

Rabbi Yirmiya poses a sharp challenge to Rabbi Zeira. If the derivation from *smalit* teaches that an unmodified *kaf* means the right hand, what do we make of the fact that the Torah itself repeatedly uses the modifier *yemanis* — right — in the *metzora* passages? The Torah specifies that blood is placed on the *right* earlobe, the *right* thumb, and the *right* big toe, and then repeats those specifications again when describing the application of oil. If the general rule is that "hand" already means "right," why does the Torah need to say "right" so many times?

### Rava's Yad Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (דְּהָא כְּתִב ״עַל דַּם הָאָשָׁם״!), חַד לְהַכְשִׁיר צְדָדִין, וְחַד לִפְסוֹל צִידֵּי צְדָדִין. ״עַל דַּם הָאָשָׁם״, (על) [וְ״עַל] מְקוֹם דַּם הָאָשָׁם״ – לְמַאי אָתוּ? הָנֵי צְרִיכִי, אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״עַל דַּם הָאָשָׁם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אִיתֵיהּ – אִין, נִתְקַנֵּחַ – לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״עַל מְקוֹם״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״עַל מְקוֹם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: דַּוְקָא נִתְקַנֵּחַ, אֲבָל אִיתֵיהּ – אֵימָא הָוֵי חֲצִיצָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן ״עַל דַּם הָאָשָׁם״. אָמַר רָבָא: מֵאַחַר דִּכְתִיב ״עַל דַּם הָאָשָׁם״ וְ״עַל מְקוֹם [דַּם] הָאָשָׁם״, וּכְתִיבָא יְמָנִית בְּדָם ״עַל בֹּהֶן יָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית וְעַל בֹּהֶן רַגְלוֹ הַיְמָנִית״, וּכְתִיבִי בְּשֶׁמֶן דִּמְצוֹרָע עָשִׁיר וְעָנִי, לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: ״יָד״ ״יָד״ לִקְמִיצָה. ״רֶגֶל״ ״רֶגֶל״ לַחֲלִיצָה. ״אוֹזֶן״ ״אוֹזֶן״ לִרְצִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** After all, a verse already indicates that the oil must be placed on the right thumb and big toe, **as** it is **written: "Upon the blood of the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 14:17). Since the Torah has already specified that the blood is placed upon the right thumb and big toe (Leviticus 14:14), it is clear that the oil is placed there as well. Similarly, why must the verse specify with regard to a poor leper that the oil is placed on the right thumb and big toe? Isn't it already clear from the verse where the oil must be placed, as it states: "Upon the place of the blood of the guilt offering" (Leviticus 14:28)? The Gemara responds: **One** specification, stated with regard to a wealthy leper, serves **to permit** the placement of the oil on the **sides** of the thumb and sides of the big toe in addition to the nail side of the thumb and big toe, **and one,** stated with regard to a poor leper, serves **to disqualify the sides of sides,** i.e., their undersides. The Gemara inquires with regard to the verse: **"Upon the blood of the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 14:17), stated with regard to the purification of a wealthy leper, and the verse: **"Upon the place of the blood of the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 14:28), stated with regard to the purification of a poor leper. **For what** purpose **do they come,** i.e., why are both verses necessary? The Gemara responds: **These** verses **are necessary, because if the Merciful One had written** only: **"Upon the blood of the guilt offering," I would say:** If the blood **is** still on the right thumb and big toe of the leper, **yes,** the priest places the oil upon the blood. But if it **was wiped** from there, he does **not** place the oil. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "Upon the place** of the blood of the guilt offering," indicating that the oil is placed upon the location of the blood, not necessarily upon the blood itself. **And** conversely, **if the Merciful One had written** only: **"Upon the place** of the blood of the guilt offering," **I would say:** The oil is placed on his right thumb and big toe **specifically** when the blood **was wiped** from there. **But** if the blood **is** still there, I will **say** that the blood **is an interposition** between the oil and the thumb or toe. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that the oil is placed **"upon the blood of the guilt offering,"** and the blood is not considered an interposition. **Rava said: Since it is written** that the priest places the oil **"upon the blood of the guilt offering," and: "Upon the place of** the blood of **the guilt offering," and it is** also **written** with regard to a wealthy leper (see Leviticus 14:14) and a poor one (see Leviticus 14:25) that the **right** hand and foot are required **for** the placement of **the blood,** as the verses state: **"Upon the thumb of his right hand, and upon the big toe of his right foot," and** this **is** also **written with regard to** the **oil of a wealthy leper** (see Leviticus 14:17) **and a poor** one (see Leviticus 14:28), one can ask: **Why do I** need all of these verses? **Rather, Rava said:** The verses that specify that the oil must be placed on the right thumb and big toe do not teach a *halakha* with regard to a leper, as it is clear that the oil must be placed on the right thumb and big toe, as it states: "Upon the place of the blood of the guilt offering." Rather, these verses are the source of verbal analogies for other *halakhot*. When the verse states with regard to a wealthy leper: "Of his right hand" (Leviticus 14:17), this teaches a verbal analogy between the term **"hand"** written here and **"hand"** written **with regard to the removal of a handful,** as the verse states about the removal of a handful: "And he filled his hand from it" (Leviticus 9:17). The verbal analogy teaches that the removal of the handful must also be performed with the right hand. Similarly, when the verse states: "Of his right foot" (Leviticus 14:17), with regard to a wealthy leper, this teaches a verbal analogy between the term **"foot"** written here and **"foot"** written **with regard to the ritual through which the *yavam*, a man whose married brother died childless, frees his brother's widow, the *yevama*, of her levirate bonds [*ḥalitza*],** as the verse states with regard to *ḥalitza*: "And remove his shoe from upon his foot" (Deuteronomy 25:9). The verbal analogy teaches that the shoe is removed from his right foot. Additionally, when the verse states: "Upon the tip of the right ear of him that is to be cleansed" (Leviticus 14:17), with regard to a wealthy leper, this teaches a verbal analogy between the term **"ear"** written here and **"ear"** written **with regard to** the **piercing** of a Hebrew slave's ear with an awl, as the verse states: "And his master shall bore his ear through with an awl" (Exodus 21:6). The verbal analogy teaches that the slave's right ear is pierced.

The *Gemara* addresses this by noting two distinct textual formulations: *al dam ha'asham* — "on the blood of the *asham*" — and *al mekom dam ha'asham* — "on the place of the blood of the *asham*." Both formulations are necessary. Had the Torah written only *al dam ha'asham*, one might think the blood must still be physically present — that if it had been wiped away, the oil application would be invalid. The addition of *al mekom* teaches that even if the blood was wiped away, the *place* suffices. Conversely, had the Torah written only *al mekom*, one might have thought the blood must be wiped away first, lest it constitute a *chatzitza* between the oil and the thumb. Both formulations together teach that either condition — blood present or blood wiped away — is acceptable.

As for the repetition of the body parts and the word "right" in the context of the oil application: the *Gemara* explains that since the Torah had already said to apply the oil where the blood of the *asham* is, restating the body parts — thumb, toe — comes to teach that the requirement encompasses even the *sides* of these body parts, but not the underside. This accounts for one set of repetitions. The second set (in the passage of the poor *metzora*) parallels the first.

### Yirmiya's Challenge (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׂמָאלִית״ (הֶעָנִי) לְמַאי אֲתָא? אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: לִיפְסוֹל יָמִין דְּכֹהֵן בִּמְצוֹרָע, שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאמַר: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא נִתְרַבְּתָה שְׂמֹאל נִתְרַבְּתָה יָמִין, בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁנִּתְרַבְּתָה שְׂמֹאל אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנִּתְרַבְּתָה יָמִין? וְאִידָּךְ ״שְׂמָאלִית״ (וְיָד וָרֶגֶל יְמָנִית דְּעָנִי), לְמַאי אֲתָא? לְכִדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כׇּל פָּרָשָׁה שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרָה וְנִשְׁנֵית, לֹא נִשְׁנֵית אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּתְחַדֵּשׁ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the additional mention of the **left** hand in the verse dealing **with the poor** leper, **for what** purpose **does** it **come? Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said:** It comes **to disqualify** the **right** hand **of a priest for** the purification of **a leper.** This teaches **that you should not say: And if in a place where** the **left** side **is not included,** as sacrificial rites in general are disqualified when performed with the left hand, the **right** hand **is included,** i.e., those rites must be performed with the right hand, then **in a place where** the **left** hand **is included,** in the case of a leper, **isn't it logical that** the **right** hand should also be **included?** Therefore, the verse repeats that the oil is poured into the priest's left hand, in order to disqualify the right hand. The Gemara asks: **And** concerning **the other** verses that specify the **left** hand of a poor leper (Leviticus 14:26–27) as well as the **right hand and foot of a poor** leper (Leviticus 14:25–28), **for what** purpose do they **come?** The Gemara responds: These verses come **for that which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Any passage that was stated** in the Torah **and was** then **repeated, was repeated only for** the sake of **a matter that was introduced** for the first time **in** the repeated passage. That is, sometimes the Torah repeats an entire passage just to teach a single new detail. In this case, the verses that discuss the purification of a poor leper were repeated only for the sake of the differences in the offerings between a wealthy leper and a poor one. No additional *halakha* should be derived from them.

The larger question — why *yemanis* is written so many times — remains to be answered, and sets up Rava's resolution.

Rava offers an elegant resolution that reorganizes the entire derivation. Rather than learning the requirement of the right hand for *kemitza* from an inference about the unmodified word *kaf*, Rava derives it directly from the repeated use of *yemanis* — right — in the *metzora* passages. These repetitions, where they are otherwise superfluous, function as a *gezeira shava* that extends the requirement of the right body part to other contexts in the Torah.

Rava formulates it as follows: *yad yad le'kmitza* — the reference to the right *hand* (*yado hayemanit*) in the *metzora* passages, where it was redundant, teaches that wherever the Torah requires the use of a *yad* elsewhere, such as in *kemitza* of the *mincha*, it must be the right hand. *Regel regel le'chalitza* — the reference to the right foot (*raglo hayemanit*), redundant in context, teaches that when the Torah says to perform *chalitza* with the *regel*, it means the right foot. *Ozen ozen le'retziah* — the reference to the right earlobe, redundant in context, teaches that when the Torah speaks of boring the ear of the perpetual slave, it means the right ear.

This is an economical and far-reaching derivation. One set of superfluous *yemanis* expressions — from either the rich or the poor *metzora* passage — teaches *kemitza*, *chalitza*, and ear-piercing each require their respective right body part. The *Gemara* then asks what the corresponding set of *yemanis* expressions — from whichever *metzora* passage remains — comes to teach. Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi answers that the expressions of *smalit* — left — which appear by the *kohen*'s palm are not being used to infer the general default of *kaf* as right (since Rava accomplishes that differently), but rather to establish a positive requirement exclusive to that act: the oil must be received specifically in the *left* palm of the *kohen*, and not the right. The left here does not mean "even left is acceptable"; it means *only* left is acceptable, because the *kohen* is simultaneously pouring with his right hand.

The remaining repetitions — right, right, right and left, left, left in the second *metzora* passage — are accounted for by the principle taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *kol parsha shenemra venishnis lo nishnis ela bishvil davar shenischadesh bah* — whenever a passage is repeated in the Torah, it is formally restated even if no new law is being derived from the restatement itself. Having already taught everything once, the Torah restates the passage in its full form for the second *metzora* type, without requiring that each element teach something new.

## Rabbi Shimon Right Hand

### Finger & Kehuna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרָה אֶצְבַּע וּכְהוּנָּה, אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: Any place** in the Torah **in which** it **is stated** that an action is performed with **a finger or** by the **priesthood,** i.e., that one uses his finger to perform the action or that a priest performs it, this teaches that it is performed **only** with the **right** hand.

Rabbah bar bar Chana reports in the name of Reish Lakish a distinct principle: wherever the Torah mentions both *etzba* — finger — and *kehuna* — priestly service — in connection with an act, that act must be performed with the right finger. This principle derives from the *chatat* passage, where the *kohen* dips his finger and places the blood on the corners of the *mizbe'ach*, and where the word *right* is explicit. When the *chatat* passage is combined with the *metzora* passage — which similarly mentions *kohen*, *etzba*, and *yemanit* — the two together teach that any *halacha* stated with both *etzba* and *kehuna* requires the right.

### Limbs Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין, אֶצְבַּע וּכְהוּנָּה בָּעֵינַן, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן מִדַּם הַחַטָּאת בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ״, וְגָמַר מִמְּצוֹרָע, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְטָבַל הַכֹּהֵן אֶת אֶצְבָּעוֹ הַיְמָנִית״. הֲרֵי קְמִיצָה דְּלָא כְּתִיבָא בָּהּ אֶלָּא כְּהוּנָּה, וּתְנַן: קָמַץ בִּשְׂמֹאל פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It might **enter our mind** to say that this means that **we require both a finger and** the **priesthood** to be stated together in the verse in order to mandate use of the right hand, e.g., **as it is written: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger"** (Leviticus 4:25). **And** the fact that this verse is referring to a finger from his right hand **is derived from a leper, as it is written: "And the priest shall dip his right finger"** (Leviticus 14:16). This cannot be correct, **as** there is the verse that addresses **the removal of a handful** from a meal offering, **in which only** the **priesthood is written, and** yet **we learned** in a mishna (6a): If the priest **removed the handful with** his **left** hand the meal offering is **unfit.**

A challenge is immediately raised: the act of bringing the limbs of the *olah* to the ramp (*holachat evarim lakevesh*) is mentioned in connection with *kehuna* — the verse states *vehikriv hakohen et hakol hamizbeacha*, and the *Gemara* identifies this as referring to bringing the limbs. Yet the *Mishnah* in this regard explicitly describes the *kohen* holding the head in his right hand and the right leg in his left hand, demonstrating that this act is performed with both hands, not exclusively the right. This would seem to disprove the principle.

### Essential Avoda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אוֹ ״אֶצְבַּע״ אוֹ ״כְּהוּנָּה״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי הוֹלָכַת אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ, דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ כְּהוּנָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִקְרִיב הַכֹּהֵן אֶת הַכֹּל הַמִּזְבֵּחָה״, וְאָמַר מָר: זוֹ הוֹלָכַת אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ, וּתְנַן: הָרֶגֶל שֶׁל יָמִין בִּשְׂמֹאל וּבֵית עוֹרָהּ לַחוּץ. כִּי אָמְרִינַן ״אוֹ אֶצְבַּע אוֹ כְּהוּנָּה״ – בְּדָבָר הַמְעַכֵּב כַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **Rava said:** This statement means that if the verse mentions **either a finger or** the **priesthood,** only the right hand may be used. **Abaye said to** Rava: But this is contradicted by the verse discussing **the conveyance of** the **limbs** of the daily burnt offering **to the ramp** of the altar, **as priesthood is written with regard to it, as it is written: "And the priest shall sacrifice the whole** and make it smoke **upon the altar"** (Leviticus 1:13), **and the Master said** that **this** verse is referring to **the conveyance of** the **limbs to the ramp. And** yet **we learned** in a mishna (*Tamid* 31b): When the priest conveys the limbs to the ramp, **the foot of** the **right** side of the offering is carried **in** the **left** hand of the priest, **and the place of its skin,** i.e., the side of the limb covered in skin, is held facing **outward.** Clearly, use of the left hand does not disqualify the conveyance of the limbs. The Gemara responds: **When we say** that if the verse states **either finger or priesthood** then the left hand is disqualified, this is only **with regard to a matter that precludes atonement,** i.e., a rite whose performance is indispensable to the atonement, similar to the sprinkling of the oil on the leper (see Leviticus 14:16). The conveyance of the limbs, by contrast, is not indispensable to atonement.

The *Gemara* resolves the challenge by qualifying the principle: it applies only to a *davar hame'akkeiv kapparah* — an essential act whose omission invalidates the *korban*. Bringing the limbs to the ramp is not such an act; even if the *korban* was slaughtered near the altar, the conveyance is not strictly required for the offering to be valid. The principle therefore holds only for those core *avodot* that are indispensable to the *kapparah*. On this basis we understand *kemitza* — an essential act — as requiring the right hand, and similarly any other essential priestly service.

### Kabbalat Hadam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי קַבָּלָה (דבר) [דְּדָבָר] הַמְעַכֵּב כַּפָּרָה הוּא, וּכְתִב בָּהּ כְּהוּנָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִקְרִיבוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֲנִים אֶת הַדָּם״ – זוֹ קַבָּלַת הַדָּם, וּתְנַן: קִבֵּל בִּשְׂמֹאל פָּסַל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't there the **collection** of the blood in a service vessel, **which is a matter indispensable to atonement, and about which priesthood is written? As it is written: "And Aaron's sons, the priests, shall present the blood"** (Leviticus 1:5), and **this** is referring to **the collection of the blood. And** yet **we learned** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 15b): If **one collected** the blood **with** his **left** hand, the blood is **disqualified** for offering, **and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **fit,** despite the fact that priesthood is mentioned in the verse.

The *Gemara* tests this refined principle against *kabbalat hadam* — catching the blood. This is clearly an essential act, *me'akkeiv kapparah*, and it does mention *kehuna*: *vehikrivu bnei Aharon hakohanim et hadam*, which the *Gemara* identifies as referring to *kabbalat hadam*. As expected, the *Mishnah* in Zevachim teaches *kibel bismol pasul* — if the blood was caught with the left hand it is invalid. So far this is consistent.

The complication is that Rabbi Shimon rules it is valid even when done with the left hand. This is striking — Rabbi Shimon appears to directly contradict the principle that *kehuna* plus essential *avoda* demands the right hand.

### Rabbi Shimon Etzba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן קָאָמְרַתְּ? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תַּרְתֵּי בָּעֵי. וּמִי בָּעֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תַּרְתֵּי? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרָה ״יָד״ – אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא יָמִין, ״אֶצְבַּע״ – אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא יָמִין! אֶצְבַּע לָא בָּעֲיָא כְּהוּנָּה, כְּהוּנָּה בָּעֲיָא אֶצְבַּע. אֶלָּא כֹּהֵן לְמָה לִי? בְּכִהוּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **You are saying** that there is a difficulty **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon? Rabbi Shimon requires** that **both** matters appear in the verse, i.e., both finger and priesthood. The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Shimon** really **require both? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says:** In **any place** in the Torah in **which** the word **hand is stated,** the verse **is** referring **only** to the **right** hand, and whenever a verse mentions the word **finger,** it **is** referring **only** to a finger of the **right** hand? The Gemara responds: According to Rabbi Shimon, if the verse mentions only the word **finger,** it **does not require** a mention of the **priesthood** as well for the limitation to apply. But if the verse mentions only the **priesthood,** it **requires** mention of the term **finger** for the limitation to apply. The Gemara asks: **But** according to Rabbi Shimon, if the mention of the priesthood alone does not suffice to disqualify the right hand, then **why do I** need the superfluous reference to **a priest** with regard to the collection of the blood? After all, the verse already states that the collection must be performed by the sons of Aaron. The Gemara responds: The additional mention of the priesthood indicates that a priest must perform the collection of the blood **in his priestly state,** i.e., while wearing the priestly vestments.

The *Gemara* defends the principle by noting that Rabbi Shimon operates with a fundamentally different framework. For the *Chachamim*, either *etzba* alone or *kehuna* alone — in connection with an essential act — is sufficient to require the right hand. Rabbi Shimon holds that *etzba* alone does require the right, but *kehuna* alone does not. For Rabbi Shimon, the word *kohen* in a verse does not by itself establish a requirement of the right hand; only an explicit mention of *etzba* or *yad* carries that implication.

This is confirmed by the *braita* which records Rabbi Shimon's own formulation: *kol makom she'ne'emar yad eino ela yamin, etzba eino ela yamin* — wherever the Torah says *yad* it means right, wherever it says *etzba* it means right. The *Gemara* refines this: for Rabbi Shimon, *etzba lo ba'ei kehuna* — the word *etzba* triggers the right-hand requirement even without *kehuna* — but *kehuna ba'ei etzba* — *kehuna* alone does not trigger it; you need *etzba* as well. Effectively, for Rabbi Shimon the word *kohen* is halachically immaterial for determining which hand is required. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, treat *kehuna* by itself — in an essential act — as sufficient to mandate the right hand.

This accounts for their dispute regarding *kabbalat hadam*: the verse mentions *kohen* but not *etzba*. The *Chachamim* say *kohen* in an essential act demands right; Rabbi Shimon says without *etzba* the left is permitted.

One might ask why the Torah mentions *kohen* at all in such verses if, for Rabbi Shimon, it carries no implication about which hand is used. The *Gemara* answers: *bi'kehuno* — it comes to require that the *kohen* perform the act while wearing his *bigdei kehuna*, his priestly vestments. The mention of *kehuna* is not about handedness but about the performer's proper ritual state.

### Zrika Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי זְרִיקָה דְּלָא כְּתִיב בַּיהּ אֶלָּא כְּהוּנָּה, וּתְנַן: זָרַק בִּשְׂמֹאל פָּסוּל, וְלָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פְּלִיג בְּבָרַיְיתָא, דְּתַנְיָא: קִבֵּל בִּשְׂמֹאל פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר; זָרַק בִּשְׂמֹאל פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't there the **sprinkling** of the blood, **concerning which only** the **priesthood is written** in the verse, **and we learned:** If **one sprinkled** the blood **with** his **left** hand it is **disqualified; and Rabbi Shimon does not disagree** with this ruling, indicating that Rabbi Shimon holds that a mention of the priesthood does not require a mention of the word finger? **Abaye says: He disagrees** with this ruling **in a *baraita*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one collected** the blood **with** his **left** hand it is **disqualified, and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **fit.** Additionally, if **one sprinkled** the blood **with** his **left** hand it is **disqualified, and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **fit.**

The *Gemara* carries this further. What about *zrika* — the throwing of the blood against the altar, the most central act of the blood service? This act also mentions only *kehuna* and not *etzba*. The *Mishnah* rules *zarak bismol pasul* — if *zrika* was done with the left hand it is invalid — and apparently Rabbi Shimon does not dispute this.

But in fact, Abbaye reports, Rabbi Shimon *does* dispute it — in a *braita*. That *braita* records: *kibel bismol pasul ve'Rabbi Shimon machshir; zarak bismol pasul ve'Rabbi Shimon machshir* — both catching the blood and throwing the blood with the left hand are valid according to Rabbi Shimon. This is striking: even the core act of *zrika*, which is universally treated as requiring the right hand, is permitted by Rabbi Shimon with the left, because the verse does not use the word *etzba*.

Tosafot note a difficulty here: the *Gemara* attributes the rule that *zrika* with the left is invalid to a *Mishnah*, yet Tosafot observe that no such *Mishnah* exists anywhere. Tosafot accordingly explore the possibility of an alternative textual reading of the *Gemara*.

### Two Derivations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, הָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״יָד״ ״יָד״ לִקְמִיצָה, לְמָה לִי? מִכְּהוּנָּה נָפְקָא! חַד לְקוֹמֶץ, וְחַד לְקִידּוּשׁ קוֹמֶץ. לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּלָא בָּעֵי קִידּוּשׁ קוֹמֶץ, וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר נָמֵי דְּבָעֵי קִידּוּשׁ קוֹמֶץ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וּבִשְׂמֹאל אַכְשׁוֹרֵי מַכְשַׁר – ״יָד״ ״יָד״ דְּרָבָא לְמָה לִי? אִי לִקְמִיצָה גּוּפַהּ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא נָפְקָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים הִיא כַּחַטָּאת וְכָאָשָׁם״. בָּא לְעוֹבְדָהּ בַּיָּד – עוֹבְדָהּ בְּיָמִין, כְּחַטָּאת; בָּא לְעוֹבְדָהּ בִּכְלִי – עוֹבְדָהּ בִּשְׂמֹאל, כְּאָשָׁם. לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְקוֹמֶץ דְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא קׇרְבָּנוֹ מְהוּדָּר, כִּי קָמֵיץ לַהּ נָמֵי בִּשְׂמֹאל תִּתַּכְשַׁר, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But that which Rava says** with regard to the superfluous terms in the passage discussing a leper: One derives a verbal analogy between the word **"hand"** written in that passage and the word **"hand"** written **with regard to the removal of a handful** from a meal offering, to indicate that the latter must also be performed with the right hand, **why do I** need this verbal analogy? One can **derive** that the handful must be removed with the right hand **from** the verse's mention of the **priesthood,** as it is stated: "And the priest shall remove his handful" (Leviticus 5:12). The Gemara responds: Both derivations are necessary, **one for** the removal of the **handful** from a meal offering, **and one for the sanctification of** the **handful,** i.e., placing it into a second service vessel. Both must be performed with the right hand. The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Shimon, who does not require sanctification of** the **handful, or according to the one who says that Rabbi Shimon also requires the sanctification of** the **handful but** that **he deems** the sanctification **fit** when performed **with** the **left** hand (see 26a), **why do I** need the verbal analogy **of Rava** between **"hand"** and **"hand"?** **If** one suggests that it is necessary **to** indicate that the **removal of the handful itself** must be performed with the right hand, this cannot be, since this is **derived from** the verse cited by **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya. As Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says: What is the reason that Rabbi Shimon** does not require that the handful be sanctified in a service vessel? **As the verse states** with regard to the meal offering: **"It is most holy, as the sin offering, and as the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 6:10). Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, elaborates: The verse compares the meal offering to a sin offering and a guilt offering. Therefore, if the priest **comes to perform** the burning of the handful **with** his **hand, he performs it with** his **right** hand, **like** in the case of **a sin offering,** whose blood is sprinkled with the hand. And if he **comes to perform it with a vessel,** i.e., if he first sanctifies the handful in a service vessel, then **he** may **perform it with** his **left** hand, **like** in the case of **a guilt offering,** whose blood is sprinkled from a vessel. Since the removal of the handful is performed with the hand, the verse indicates that it must be performed with the right hand, and the verbal analogy is unnecessary. The Gemara responds: The verbal analogy **is necessary only for the handful of the meal offering of a sinner,** to teach that it must be removed with the right hand. It might **enter your mind to say: Since Rabbi Shimon says** that this offering does not require oil and frankincense **so that** a sinner's **offering will not be of superior quality,** perhaps **when** the priest **removed the handful with** his **left** hand, which is a manner of inferior quality, **it should be fit as well.** The verbal analogy therefore **teaches us** that the handful must always be removed with the right hand, even in the case of the meal offering of a sinner.

This raises a question about Rava's *yad yad le'kmitza* derivation — which teaches that *kemitza* must be done with the right hand — and the principle of *Rabbah bar bar Chana* — which teaches the same from the conjunction of *etzba* and *kehuna*. If the latter principle is sufficient, why do we need Rava's derivation? And conversely, if Rava's derivation is sufficient, what need is there for the *kehuna* principle?

The *Gemara* reconciles the two: *chad le'kometz ve'chad le'kidush kometz* — one derivation applies to the act of taking the *kometz* from the *mincha*, and the other applies to the subsequent act of placing the *kometz* into the *kli shares*, the sanctifying vessel. Both acts require the right hand, and both have an independent derivation.

For Rabbi Shimon, this analysis must be modified. Rabbi Shimon holds that placing the *kometz* into a *kli* need not be done with the right hand — indeed, some versions of his position hold that he does not require placement into a *kli* at all, and others hold that he requires it but permits the left hand for this step. In either case, Rabbi Shimon cannot use both derivations the way the *Chachamim* do.

The *Gemara* therefore asks: if Rabbi Shimon does not need Rava's *yad yad* for the *kli* step, and the taking of the *kometz* with the right hand is already established for Rabbi Shimon by another derivation — the teaching of Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Chiya — then what purpose does the *yad yad* serve for Rabbi Shimon at all?

### Minchat Choté

The *Gemara* answers: for Rabbi Shimon, the *yad yad* derivation is needed specifically for the *kometz* of a *minchat choteh* — the grain offering of a sinner. Without it, one might have thought that the *kemitza* of this particular *mincha* could be performed with the left hand. The reasoning would be as follows: Rabbi Shimon teaches that the *minchat choteh* lacks oil and *levona* so that the sinner's offering should not be *mehudar* — enhanced or beautified. One might extend this logic and suppose that the offering should be treated in a diminished manner throughout, including allowing *kemitza* with the left hand. The *yad yad* derivation comes to exclude this: even the *minchat choteh* requires *kemitza* with the right hand.

The broader teaching that undergirds this discussion is Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Chiya's derivation from the verse *kodesh kodashim hi, ke'chatat uke'asham* — the *mincha* is *kodesh kodashim*, like a *chatat* and like an *asham*. Rabbi Shimon reads this verse as establishing a two-track system. If one wishes to offer the *kometz* directly with the hand — without placing it into a *kli* — then the hand itself functions as the vessel, and the model is the *chatat*: the act must be done with the right hand. If one does place the *kometz* into a *kli*, then the *kli* serves as the vessel, and the model is the *asham* — specifically the *metzora asham* procedure, in which oil is caught in the left palm. In this case, while the *kemitza* itself must still be taken with the right hand, the transfer into the *kli* may be done with the left.

The picture that emerges is a rich and internally consistent set of positions. For the *Chachamim*, the requirement of the right hand attaches to any essential priestly act in which the verse mentions either *kehuna* or *etzba* — which effectively means virtually every essential *avoda*. For Rabbi Shimon, the word *kehuna* alone carries no implication about which hand is required; right hand is demanded only when *etzba* or *yad* is explicitly mentioned. This accounts for Rabbi Shimon's remarkable ruling that both *kabbalat hadam* and *zrika* may be performed with the left hand. And within the domain of *kemitza*, Rabbi Shimon introduces an additional nuance: whether a *kli* is used determines not only the procedural form of the act but also which hand is required for its component steps.
