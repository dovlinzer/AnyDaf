# Menachot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shirayim After Kemitza

### Debate Setup

The daf opens in the middle of a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning a *mincha* in which some of the *shirayim* — the remainder of the grain offering left after *kemitza* — went missing between the time of *kemitza* and *haktara*. Reish Lakish holds that the offering is invalid, deriving this from a special *pasuk*, even though in an analogous case involving an animal sacrifice, where some of the meat or fat remained, everyone would agree the offering is valid. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees, holding that the case is indeed comparable to the animal offering, and that one may proceed to offer the *kometz* even if not all of the *shirayim* is present. As will emerge later, there remains a separate question about whether the *shirayim* may be eaten in such a case — but at minimum, Rabbi Yochanan maintains that the *haktara* of the *kometz* may proceed.

### Rav Pappa's Report

The *Gemara* relates that Rav Pappa was sitting and recounting this dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Rav Yosef bar Shemaya challenged him: might not the entire debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concern specifically the *minchat ha'omer*, which is a *korban tzibur*? This question connects to a discussion that had concluded the previous daf, where the *Gemara* had raised a proof to Rabbi Yochanan from the case of *Lechem HaPanim*.

### Lechem HaPanim Proof

The proof from *Lechem HaPanim* was as follows: if some of the loaves went missing, it is nonetheless evident that the *bazichin* — the bowls of *levona* which function analogously to the *kometz* — may still be offered. Reish Lakish might have responded that *Lechem HaPanim* is a *korban tzibur* and thus subject to different rules, but the *Gemara* rejected this: a *korban tzibur* receives special dispensation only in cases of *tuma*, not in cases of a deficient offering. The present passage adds a further reason why Reish Lakish could not have offered that distinction — namely, that the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish may itself have concerned a *korban tzibur*, specifically the *minchat omer*. If so, invoking the *korban tzibur* status of *Lechem HaPanim* would not have answered anything. The *Gemara* leaves this without a definitive resolution but notes it as an additional reason the distinction could not have been made. The conclusion stands: *Lechem HaPanim* provides a sound proof in support of Rabbi Yochanan, that even when some of the offering is missing, the *kometz* may be brought.

### Two Braitot on Chaser

Rav Malkio introduces a new layer of analysis. One *braita* teaches from the words *misolta* — "from its fine flour" — and *mishemena* — "from its oil" — that if any amount of the flour or oil of the *mincha* is lacking, it is invalid. A second *braita* teaches from the words *vehanoteret min hamincha* — "and what remains of the *mincha* shall be for Aaron and his sons" — that this comes to exclude a case where some of the *mincha* went missing, whether it was the main offering itself that was lacking, or the *kometz* that was deficient, or the *levona* that was not offered at all. (In the case of *levona*, the *Gemara* notes, the rule runs in the opposite direction from the *kometz*: even a minimal amount of *levona* suffices, and this will be addressed later.) The question thus arises: why does the Torah need two separate *psukim* to convey the problem of *chaser*?

### Pesuk Division

The answer offered is that the two *psukim* address two distinct scenarios of *chaser*. The first *pasuk* — *misolta umishemena*, which frames the act of *kemitza* itself — addresses the case where the *mincha* was lacking *before* the *kemitza* was taken. The second *pasuk* — *vehanoteret min hamincha*, which speaks of what remains after — addresses the case where the *shirayim* went lacking *between kemitza* and *haktara*.

If this reading is correct, it would appear to contradict Rabbi Yochanan in both directions. The first *pasuk* would seem to contradict his position, discussed earlier, that if the *mincha* was lacking before *kemitza* one may refill it and proceed — for the *pasuk* appears to invalidate *kemitza* from a deficient *mincha*. But the *Gemara* resolves this: what the first *pasuk* teaches is that if one performed *kemitza* while the *mincha* was still lacking, the *kemitza* is invalid. Rabbi Yochanan never permitted taking *kemitza* from a deficient *mincha* — he only permitted *refilling* the deficiency beforehand. Both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree that *kemitza* from a deficient quantity is invalid; they dispute only whether one may restore the missing amount prior to *kemitza*.

As for the second *pasuk*, one might think it proves Reish Lakish correct that when *shirayim* go missing between *kemitza* and *haktara*, the *kometz* may not be offered. The *Gemara* resolves this as well: the second *pasuk* is not invalidating the *haktara* of the *kometz*. Rather, it is teaching that even according to Rabbi Yochanan, who permits the *haktara*, there is a consequence for the *shirayim*: *vehanoteret min hamincha* — "what remains of the *mincha*" — implies the *shirayim* must come from a full and intact *mincha* in order to be *yochlu Aharon u'vanav*, eligible for priestly consumption. If some of the *shirayim* went missing before the *kometz* was offered, the remaining *shirayim* cannot be eaten, even though the *haktara* of the *kometz* itself is permitted.

### Eating Shirayim

The *Gemara* now explores this question directly: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that the *kometz* may be offered even when *shirayim* went missing between *kemitza* and *haktara*, what is the status of those remaining *shirayim* with respect to eating?

Zeiri rules that they may not be eaten. He derives this from *vehanoteret* — "what remains shall be eaten" — and not *hanoteret min hanoteret*, "what remains from what remains." Once the *shirayim* are themselves a reduced remainder, they no longer qualify for priestly consumption.

Rav Yannai offers an additional formulation: *mehamincha*, from the *mincha* that already was — meaning the *mincha* as it stood in its full state. Rashi provides two readings of Rav Yannai's position. In one reading, Rav Yannai disputes Zeiri and holds that as long as the *mincha* was intact at the time of *kemitza*, the *shirayim* may be eaten even if the offering of the *kometz* occurred when the *shirayim* were no longer complete — with Rabbi Yochanan then siding with Zeiri against eating the *shirayim*. In the other reading, Rav Yannai agrees with Zeiri that the *shirayim* cannot be eaten but simply emphasizes a different *drasha* from the *pasuk*.

In sum: Reish Lakish holds that when *shirayim* go missing between *kemitza* and *haktara*, the *kometz* may not be offered at all. Rabbi Yochanan holds the *kometz* may be offered — but the *Gemara*'s analysis suggests that even Rabbi Yochanan would agree the remaining *shirayim* cannot be eaten.

## Right Hand Kemitza

### Mishnah: Left Invalid

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah*, which states that if *kemitza* was performed with the left hand, the offering is invalid. The discussion that follows will occupy the remainder of the daf, tracing the requirement to use the right hand for *kemitza* and for other priestly acts.

### Kaf Derivation

From where do we derive that *kemitza* requires the right hand? Rabbi Zeira cites the *pasuk*: *vehikriv et hamincha vemilei kapo mimena* — "he shall bring the *mincha* forward and fill his *kaf* from it." The word *kaf* — palm — is unmodified. Rabbi Zeira argues: I do not know on my own whether *kaf* means the right or left palm. But when the Torah speaks of the *metzora* offering, it states *veyatzak al kaf hakohen hasmalit* — the oil is poured onto the *left* palm of the *kohen*. Since the Torah was compelled to specify "left" in that context, we derive that *kaf* without a modifier means the right hand. This is the same logical structure the *Gemara* employs elsewhere — as in the case of witnesses, where the Torah had to say "a *single* witness," demonstrating that *eid* alone implies two — so here, specifying "left" implies that the unmodified term defaults to the right.

### Left Hand Redundancy

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this derivation. The word *smalit* — left — in the *metzora* passage is needed simply to establish that *this particular act* requires the left palm; it cannot simultaneously serve as an inference about the general default meaning of *kaf*. The *Gemara* responds that *smalit* appears a second time in that passage, a redundancy that allows one instance to establish the requirement for that act and the second to serve as the inference. The *Gemara* then raises a further objection: perhaps two mentions of *smalit* should be read as a *mi'ut achar mi'ut*, an exclusion following an exclusion, which classically comes to *broaden* — in this case, to teach that even the right hand is acceptable for catching the oil. The *Gemara* replies that *smalit* actually appears a *third* time — in the parallel passage describing the *metzora* who brings the less expensive bird offering — and a third mention refutes the reading of broadening. Then a further question arises: perhaps three times is not refutation but emphasis, establishing that left is the paradigm *everywhere*, not merely here. To this, the *Gemara* replies that *smalit* is in fact written a *fourth* time — two mentions in the passage of the wealthy *metzora* and two in that of the poor one — and the accumulation of four repetitions establishes both that left is strictly required *here* and that *kaf* elsewhere defaults to the right.

### Yirmiya's Challenge

Rabbi Yirmiya poses a sharp challenge to Rabbi Zeira. If the derivation from *smalit* teaches that an unmodified *kaf* means the right hand, what do we make of the fact that the Torah itself repeatedly uses the modifier *yemanis* — right — in the *metzora* passages? The Torah specifies that blood is placed on the *right* earlobe, the *right* thumb, and the *right* big toe, and then repeats those specifications again when describing the application of oil. If the general rule is that "hand" already means "right," why does the Torah need to say "right" so many times?

The *Gemara* addresses this by noting two distinct textual formulations: *al dam ha'asham* — "on the blood of the *asham*" — and *al mekom dam ha'asham* — "on the place of the blood of the *asham*." Both formulations are necessary. Had the Torah written only *al dam ha'asham*, one might think the blood must still be physically present — that if it had been wiped away, the oil application would be invalid. The addition of *al mekom* teaches that even if the blood was wiped away, the *place* suffices. Conversely, had the Torah written only *al mekom*, one might have thought the blood must be wiped away first, lest it constitute a *chatzitza* between the oil and the thumb. Both formulations together teach that either condition — blood present or blood wiped away — is acceptable.

As for the repetition of the body parts and the word "right" in the context of the oil application: the *Gemara* explains that since the Torah had already said to apply the oil where the blood of the *asham* is, restating the body parts — thumb, toe — comes to teach that the requirement encompasses even the *sides* of these body parts, but not the underside. This accounts for one set of repetitions. The second set (in the passage of the poor *metzora*) parallels the first.

### Yirmiya's Challenge

The larger question — why *yemanis* is written so many times — remains to be answered, and sets up Rava's resolution.

### Rava's Yad Solution

Rava offers an elegant resolution that reorganizes the entire derivation. Rather than learning the requirement of the right hand for *kemitza* from an inference about the unmodified word *kaf*, Rava derives it directly from the repeated use of *yemanis* — right — in the *metzora* passages. These repetitions, where they are otherwise superfluous, function as a *gezeira shava* that extends the requirement of the right body part to other contexts in the Torah.

Rava formulates it as follows: *yad yad le'kmitza* — the reference to the right *hand* (*yado hayemanit*) in the *metzora* passages, where it was redundant, teaches that wherever the Torah requires the use of a *yad* elsewhere, such as in *kemitza* of the *mincha*, it must be the right hand. *Regel regel le'chalitza* — the reference to the right foot (*raglo hayemanit*), redundant in context, teaches that when the Torah says to perform *chalitza* with the *regel*, it means the right foot. *Ozen ozen le'retziah* — the reference to the right earlobe, redundant in context, teaches that when the Torah speaks of boring the ear of the perpetual slave, it means the right ear.

This is an economical and far-reaching derivation. One set of superfluous *yemanis* expressions — from either the rich or the poor *metzora* passage — teaches *kemitza*, *chalitza*, and ear-piercing each require their respective right body part. The *Gemara* then asks what the corresponding set of *yemanis* expressions — from whichever *metzora* passage remains — comes to teach. Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi answers that the expressions of *smalit* — left — which appear by the *kohen*'s palm are not being used to infer the general default of *kaf* as right (since Rava accomplishes that differently), but rather to establish a positive requirement exclusive to that act: the oil must be received specifically in the *left* palm of the *kohen*, and not the right. The left here does not mean "even left is acceptable"; it means *only* left is acceptable, because the *kohen* is simultaneously pouring with his right hand.

The remaining repetitions — right, right, right and left, left, left in the second *metzora* passage — are accounted for by the principle taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *kol parsha shenemra venishnis lo nishnis ela bishvil davar shenischadesh bah* — whenever a passage is repeated in the Torah, it is formally restated even if no new law is being derived from the restatement itself. Having already taught everything once, the Torah restates the passage in its full form for the second *metzora* type, without requiring that each element teach something new.

## Rabbi Shimon Right Hand

### Finger & Kehuna

Rabbah bar bar Chana reports in the name of Reish Lakish a distinct principle: wherever the Torah mentions both *etzba* — finger — and *kehuna* — priestly service — in connection with an act, that act must be performed with the right finger. This principle derives from the *chatat* passage, where the *kohen* dips his finger and places the blood on the corners of the *mizbe'ach*, and where the word *right* is explicit. When the *chatat* passage is combined with the *metzora* passage — which similarly mentions *kohen*, *etzba*, and *yemanit* — the two together teach that any *halacha* stated with both *etzba* and *kehuna* requires the right.

The *Gemara* clarifies the initial assumption: we might have thought both terms — *etzba* and *kehuna* — must appear together to trigger the requirement of right. The *chatat* verse indeed contains both: *velakach hakohen* (*kohen*) and *be'etzbao* (*etzba*). Rashi notes that from the *metzora* passage we already know that this combination means *right*, since that passage uses *etzba* and *kohen* alongside *yemanit*.

### Limbs Challenge

A challenge is immediately raised: the act of bringing the limbs of the *olah* to the ramp (*holachat evarim lakevesh*) is mentioned in connection with *kehuna* — the verse states *vehikriv hakohen et hakol hamizbeacha*, and the *Gemara* identifies this as referring to bringing the limbs. Yet the *Mishnah* in this regard explicitly describes the *kohen* holding the head in his right hand and the right leg in his left hand, demonstrating that this act is performed with both hands, not exclusively the right. This would seem to disprove the principle.

### Essential Avoda

The *Gemara* resolves the challenge by qualifying the principle: it applies only to a *davar hame'akkeiv kapparah* — an essential act whose omission invalidates the *korban*. Bringing the limbs to the ramp is not such an act; even if the *korban* was slaughtered near the altar, the conveyance is not strictly required for the offering to be valid. The principle therefore holds only for those core *avodot* that are indispensable to the *kapparah*. On this basis we understand *kemitza* — an essential act — as requiring the right hand, and similarly any other essential priestly service.

### Kabbalat Hadam

The *Gemara* tests this refined principle against *kabbalat hadam* — catching the blood. This is clearly an essential act, *me'akkeiv kapparah*, and it does mention *kehuna*: *vehikrivu bnei Aharon hakohanim et hadam*, which the *Gemara* identifies as referring to *kabbalat hadam*. As expected, the *Mishnah* in Zevachim teaches *kibel bismol pasul* — if the blood was caught with the left hand it is invalid. So far this is consistent.

The complication is that Rabbi Shimon rules it is valid even when done with the left hand. This is striking — Rabbi Shimon appears to directly contradict the principle that *kehuna* plus essential *avoda* demands the right hand.

### Rabbi Shimon Etzba

The *Gemara* defends the principle by noting that Rabbi Shimon operates with a fundamentally different framework. For the *Chachamim*, either *etzba* alone or *kehuna* alone — in connection with an essential act — is sufficient to require the right hand. Rabbi Shimon holds that *etzba* alone does require the right, but *kehuna* alone does not. For Rabbi Shimon, the word *kohen* in a verse does not by itself establish a requirement of the right hand; only an explicit mention of *etzba* or *yad* carries that implication.

This is confirmed by the *braita* which records Rabbi Shimon's own formulation: *kol makom she'ne'emar yad eino ela yamin, etzba eino ela yamin* — wherever the Torah says *yad* it means right, wherever it says *etzba* it means right. The *Gemara* refines this: for Rabbi Shimon, *etzba lo ba'ei kehuna* — the word *etzba* triggers the right-hand requirement even without *kehuna* — but *kehuna ba'ei etzba* — *kehuna* alone does not trigger it; you need *etzba* as well. Effectively, for Rabbi Shimon the word *kohen* is halachically immaterial for determining which hand is required. The *Chachamim*, by contrast, treat *kehuna* by itself — in an essential act — as sufficient to mandate the right hand.

This accounts for their dispute regarding *kabbalat hadam*: the verse mentions *kohen* but not *etzba*. The *Chachamim* say *kohen* in an essential act demands right; Rabbi Shimon says without *etzba* the left is permitted.

One might ask why the Torah mentions *kohen* at all in such verses if, for Rabbi Shimon, it carries no implication about which hand is used. The *Gemara* answers: *bi'kehuno* — it comes to require that the *kohen* perform the act while wearing his *bigdei kehuna*, his priestly vestments. The mention of *kehuna* is not about handedness but about the performer's proper ritual state.

### Zrika Dispute

The *Gemara* carries this further. What about *zrika* — the throwing of the blood against the altar, the most central act of the blood service? This act also mentions only *kehuna* and not *etzba*. The *Mishnah* rules *zarak bismol pasul* — if *zrika* was done with the left hand it is invalid — and apparently Rabbi Shimon does not dispute this.

But in fact, Abbaye reports, Rabbi Shimon *does* dispute it — in a *braita*. That *braita* records: *kibel bismol pasul ve'Rabbi Shimon machshir; zarak bismol pasul ve'Rabbi Shimon machshir* — both catching the blood and throwing the blood with the left hand are valid according to Rabbi Shimon. This is striking: even the core act of *zrika*, which is universally treated as requiring the right hand, is permitted by Rabbi Shimon with the left, because the verse does not use the word *etzba*.

Tosafot note a difficulty here: the *Gemara* attributes the rule that *zrika* with the left is invalid to a *Mishnah*, yet Tosafot observe that no such *Mishnah* exists anywhere. Tosafot accordingly explore the possibility of an alternative textual reading of the *Gemara*.

### Two Derivations

This raises a question about Rava's *yad yad* derivation — which teaches that *kemitza* must be done with the right hand — and the principle of *Rabbah bar bar Chana* — which teaches the same from the conjunction of *etzba* and *kehuna*. If the latter principle is sufficient, why do we need Rava's derivation? And conversely, if Rava's derivation is sufficient, what need is there for the *kehuna* principle?

The *Gemara* reconciles the two: *chad le'kometz ve'chad le'kidush kometz* — one derivation applies to the act of taking the *kometz* from the *mincha*, and the other applies to the subsequent act of placing the *kometz* into the *kli shares*, the sanctifying vessel. Both acts require the right hand, and both have an independent derivation.

For Rabbi Shimon, this analysis must be modified. Rabbi Shimon holds that placing the *kometz* into a *kli* need not be done with the right hand — indeed, some versions of his position hold that he does not require placement into a *kli* at all, and others hold that he requires it but permits the left hand for this step. In either case, Rabbi Shimon cannot use both derivations the way the *Chachamim* do.

The *Gemara* therefore asks: if Rabbi Shimon does not need Rava's *yad yad* for the *kli* step, and the taking of the *kometz* with the right hand is already established for Rabbi Shimon by another derivation — the teaching of Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Chiya — then what purpose does the *yad yad* serve for Rabbi Shimon at all?

### Minchat Choté

The *Gemara* answers: for Rabbi Shimon, the *yad yad* derivation is needed specifically for the *kometz* of a *minchat choteh* — the grain offering of a sinner. Without it, one might have thought that the *kemitza* of this particular *mincha* could be performed with the left hand. The reasoning would be as follows: Rabbi Shimon teaches that the *minchat choteh* lacks oil and *levona* so that the sinner's offering should not be *mehudar* — enhanced or beautified. One might extend this logic and suppose that the offering should be treated in a diminished manner throughout, including allowing *kemitza* with the left hand. The *yad yad* derivation comes to exclude this: even the *minchat choteh* requires *kemitza* with the right hand.

The broader teaching that undergirds this discussion is Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Chiya's derivation from the verse *kodesh kodashim hi, ke'chatat uke'asham* — the *mincha* is *kodesh kodashim*, like a *chatat* and like an *asham*. Rabbi Shimon reads this verse as establishing a two-track system. If one wishes to offer the *kometz* directly with the hand — without placing it into a *kli* — then the hand itself functions as the vessel, and the model is the *chatat*: the act must be done with the right hand. If one does place the *kometz* into a *kli*, then the *kli* serves as the vessel, and the model is the *asham* — specifically the *metzora asham* procedure, in which oil is caught in the left palm. In this case, while the *kemitza* itself must still be taken with the right hand, the transfer into the *kli* may be done with the left.

The picture that emerges is a rich and internally consistent set of positions. For the *Chachamim*, the requirement of the right hand attaches to any essential priestly act in which the verse mentions either *kehuna* or *etzba* — which effectively means virtually every essential *avoda*. For Rabbi Shimon, the word *kehuna* alone carries no implication about which hand is required; right hand is demanded only when *etzba* or *yad* is explicitly mentioned. This accounts for Rabbi Shimon's remarkable ruling that both *kabbalat hadam* and *zrika* may be performed with the left hand. And within the domain of *kemitza*, Rabbi Shimon introduces an additional nuance: whether a *kli* is used determines not only the procedural form of the act but also which hand is required for its component steps.
