# Rosh Hashanah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Delayed Korban Valid

### Becha Chet Baraita

The *gemara* on 6a continues its sustained treatment of *bal te'acher* — the prohibition against delaying the fulfillment of one's vow to bring a *korban*. To orient ourselves: the preceding discussion had introduced a *baraita* that interprets the phrase *becha chet* ("the sin shall be upon you") as teaching that *becha chet v'lo b'korban'cha chet* — the sin is yours, but it does not invalidate the *korban* itself. The *gemara* did not dispute this ruling; rather, it observed that the point seemed obvious, since we already know from the case of a *bechor* that a *korban* brought after the proper time remains *kasher*. The *gemara* then raised a possible distinction: perhaps the *korban* is technically *kasher* but lacks *hartza'ah* — it is not found acceptable, not *b'nei hartza'ah*. Both Rashi and Tosafot note the centrality of *kappara* here, but the concept extends more broadly to the notion that a *korban* must find favor before God, that there is an aspect of *yehi ratzon* — of the offering being desired — which is central to the very act of *avodah*. The *bechor*, by contrast, is primarily about the act of separation and sanctification; the actual offering is secondary. But for other *korbanot*, the question of *hartza'ah* is fundamental.

So the *gemara* sought to resolve the matter by pointing out that the *baraita*'s teaching — that the *korban* is not invalidated — is needed to address precisely this concern. Yet the *gemara* then presses further: even granting that, we might have derived the same ruling from another source.

### Ben Azzai & Pigul

The *gemara* notes that the ruling could have been derived from Ben Azzai. A *baraita* teaches: Ben Azzai says, the verse by *pigul* states *lo yeratzeh hamakriv oto* — the one who brings a *pigul* offering, it shall not be considered favorably for him. The word *oto*, "it," singles this out as the problem. One might have thought, the *baraita* reasons, that since the Torah says *lo te'acher leshalmo* — do not delay fulfilling your vow — someone who delays his *neder* would likewise be in the category of *belo yeratzeh*, his *korban* rendered unacceptable like *pigul*. Therefore the verse says *oto*: it is *pigul* that is *belo yeratzeh*, but the one who merely postpones his vow is not in this category. His delay is a sin upon him, but it does not compromise the acceptability of the *korban*.

Thus, the *gemara* observes, we already know from Ben Azzai's *drasha* that postponing a vow does not invalidate the offering. One might ask why the *gemara* insists that different Tannaitic sources cannot simply derive the same *halacha* from different *drashot* — and indeed, the *gemara*'s move here is to press that every source must be teaching something new. So if the point about the *korban*'s validity is already known, what does the phrase *becha chet* come to teach us?

### Sin on Person

The *gemara* reinterprets the *drasha* entirely. Rather than *becha chet v'lo b'korban'cha chet* — the sin is on you, not on your *korban* — the verse is now read as *becha chet v'lo b'ish'techa chet*: the sin falls upon you, not upon your wife.

### Wife's Death Context

This interpretation requires context. The *gemara* invokes a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan — or, some say, Rabbi Elazar — that *ein ishto shel adam meitah ela im ken mevakshin mimenu mamon v'ein lo*: a man's wife does not die unless heaven demands from him a monetary obligation that he is not fulfilling. What does this mean in context? Rashi understands it as referring to stolen money left unrepaid. Tosafot, more attuned to the passage at hand, explains it as referring to an unfulfilled vow — *mevakshin mimenu mamon*, heaven is calling in the pledge he made and has not satisfied. The consequence of non-fulfillment is rendered in vivid terms: the one who sleeps beside you is taken from you. One might then have assumed — *havah amina* — that the same punishment applies even to *bal te'acher*, the mere postponement of a vow. Therefore the verse teaches: *becha chet*, the sin focuses on you alone, it is not collective punishment. Non-fulfillment is one matter; postponement is another — and postponement does not trigger collective consequences.

### Collective Punishment Context

This, of course, is a striking and theologically charged teaching. The original *baraita* had said the sin falls on you and not on your *korban*; the *gemara* now reads it as saying the sin falls on you and not on your wife. That the two readings coexisted and the *gemara* chose to pursue the second is itself noteworthy.

It is worth acknowledging the discomfort this teaching provokes. The *Torah* itself seems to deny collective punishment in the starkest terms: *lo yumtu avot al banim u-vanim lo yumtu al avot* — fathers shall not be put to death for sons, nor sons for fathers; *ish b'chet'o yumatu* — each person is put to death for his own sin.

### Collective Punishment

And yet the tradition is not entirely uniform on this point. There are contexts — particularly around *avodah zarah* — where something resembling collective punishment appears. The verse in Devarim states, in the context of one who gives his children to *Molech*, *v'natati et panai ba-ish ha-hu u-ve-mishpachto* — I shall set My face against that man and against his family. What did his family do? Interestingly, Onkelos there translates *mishpachto* not as "his family" but as "his supporters," evidently seeking to preserve the principle of individual culpability by reading the collective as a community of willing participants. But the plain sense of the verse does point toward something collective. By *ir hanidachat*, the *gemara* resolves the difficulty by saying that the innocent are separated out and only the guilty are killed — but the Rambam famously rules that the guilty men are executed together with their wives and children, which is a position the other *rishonim* find deeply troubling, and which even pertains to a court-imposed punishment, not merely divine retribution.

One student offered a more structural reading: in the case of *avodah zarah*, what is at stake is not personal guilt spreading to family members, but rather the need to eradicate every trace of the corrupt influence. The family, on this reading, is not guilty — but the entire ecosystem connected to the *avodah zarah* must be cleansed, just as *shabbor teshabber matzevoteihem* demands the destruction of all vestiges. This is why *ir hanidachat* exists as a category — not because everyone in the city is equally culpable, but because the contamination must be fully excised. The same logic may explain why the concern extends to the family by *Korach* as well: one does not leave behind a four-year-old who will grow up to seek vengeance and carry on the rebellion. Rashi on the verse *v'ish hu b'mishpachto* takes a different approach, suggesting that the family is implicated because a corrupt figure — Rashi's example is a tax collector — typically has the complicity of the entire household. Rashi thus attempts to reframe the collective punishment as collective guilt.

In any event, the *gemara* here is working within a tradition that acknowledges, particularly in connection with *avodah zarah* and unfulfilled vows, some notion of collective consequences radiating outward from the individual. The *mishna* in Ketubot lists *noderet v'eina mekayyemet* — a wife who makes vows and does not fulfill them — as grounds for divorce without the *ketubah*. One might ask what her unfulfilled vows have to do with the husband. The *gemara* there explains it through this very same idea: unfulfilled vows can bring punishment upon the family. The same theme runs through our passage.

What the *gemara* here accomplishes is to narrow it: whatever applies in the case of outright non-fulfillment of a vow, it does not apply to mere postponement. *Becha chet* — the sin is on you, and not visited collectively. Though even the tradition acknowledges that *ba'avon nedarim*, for the sin of violating vows, a person's children may die — a statement embedded in the *halacha* of Ketubot — the *gemara* limits the collective dimension. Postponement is a sin, but it is an individual one.

## Motza Sefatecha Dras (II)

### Why Need Motza

The *gemara* now turns to a *baraita* on the closing verse of the *bal te'acher* passage: *motza sefatecha tishmor v'asita ka'asher nadarta l'Hashem Elokecha nedavah ka'asher dibarta beficha* — what comes forth from your lips you shall observe and do, as you vowed to Hashem your God, a *nedavah*, as you spoke with your mouth.

The *baraita* proceeds to identify multiple obligations within this verse. *Motza sefatecha* — this is a *mitzvat asei*, a positive commandment. *Tishmor* — this is a *mitzvat lo ta'asei*, a negative prohibition. And *v'asita* — this constitutes an *azharah l'veit din she'yasucha*, a charge to the court to compel the person to act.

But the *gemara* immediately challenges each element. Why do we need a *mitzvat asei* from *motza sefatecha*? We already have *u'vata shama v'havatem shama* — the verse commanding one to come to the place God chooses and bring all one's sacrifices there; that is already an explicit positive obligation. Why do we need *tishmor* as a negative prohibition? We already have the explicit *lo te'acher leshalmo* — do not delay fulfilling it — at the beginning of the passage; that is *bal te'acher* in its plainest form. And why do we need *v'asita* to teach that the court must compel someone to bring his *korban*? This too we already know: *yakriv oto*, as expounded in a *baraita* from Vayikra, teaches *she'kofin oto ad she'yomar rotzeh ani* — you compel him until he says "I am willing." *Yakriv oto* is read not as "he brings it" but as "you cause him to bring it" — the court presses the person into compliance. The resolution of the apparent contradiction between compulsion and *l'rtzono* — that it must be according to his will — is the famous principle that deep down a person wishes to fulfill his obligations; the *yeitzer hara* stands in the way, and the court beats the *yeitzer hara* out of him until his true desire is revealed. The Rambam discusses this principle at length in the context of the coerced *get*.

Tosafot notes that there are other *gemarot* indicating that the court generally compels performance of positive *mitzvot* — one thinks of the case of someone refusing to don *tefillin* or sit in a *sukkah* — and so the question of why we need a special verse for *korbanot* is pointed. The answer is that by *korbanot* one might have thought the Torah's explicit insistence on *l'rtzono* would preclude compulsion; the verse therefore tells us that compulsion applies even here.

The *gemara* then notes that since we apparently derive all three elements from other sources, the passage *motza sefatecha* must be doing something different — namely, extending these obligations beyond the domain of *korbanot*, to include *kodshei bedek habayit* and *tzedakah* as well.

## Tzedakah & Bedek (III)

### Categories Drasha

The *baraita* reads the entire clause of *motza sefatecha... ka'asher nadarta... nedavah... ka'asher dibarta beficha* as a series of categories to which the obligations of *bal te'acher* apply. *Ka'asher nadarta l'Hashem Elokecha* — this is a *neder*, the formula *harei alai*, a personal obligation. *L'Hashem Elokecha* — these are *chatat, asham, olah*, and *shlamim*, the various *korbanot* brought to God, whether obligatory or voluntary. *Nedavah kemashmao* — a *nedavah* in the straightforward sense, as distinguished from a *neder*: whereas a *neder* is *harei alai* — I obligate myself — a *nedavah* is *harei zu* — I sanctify this object. *Ka'asher dibarta* — what you spoke: these are *kodshei bedek habayit*, donations to the *beit hamikdash* in a non-*korban* form, where one sanctifies the monetary value of an object and transfers its ownership to the Temple treasury. And finally, *beficha* — with your mouth: *zu tzedakah*, this refers to *tzedakah*.

On the connection of *beficha* to *tzedakah*, Tosafot cites the verse *yatza mipi tzedakah* — "righteousness went forth from My mouth" — which explicitly links the word for *mouth* with *tzedakah*. This conjunction, *beficha zu tzedakah*, carries enormous resonance in later halachic literature and becomes a standard formula in discussions of whether *bal te'acher* applies to charitable pledges.

It should be noted that this list, as the *gemara* presents it here, is somewhat shorter than the list as it appeared in the preceding sugya. Conspicuously absent is *leket, shikcha, u'peah* — a category that Tosafot had already flagged as difficult from multiple angles: there is no act of speech that creates the obligation (making *beficha zu tzedakah* inapplicable), and the very concept of *bal te'acher* sits uneasily in that context, since either you left the *leket* in the field or you did not. The fact that this category does not appear in the *gemara*'s current formulation is notable.

The court's role in enforcement applies across this entire list, not only to *korbanot*. The Rambam rules that one who fails to give the appropriate amount of *tzedakah* is subject to compulsion by the court — a ruling that flows directly from this sugya's extension of *kofin oto* to the full scope of *bal te'acher*. Much of the *halacha* of *tzedakah* is also elaborated at the beginning of Bava Batra.

One question left open by the *gemara* is how to categorize a pledge made to a synagogue or communal institution that does not fit neatly as *tzedakah* to the poor. Does such a pledge fall under *bedek habayit* as an extension of that category, or does it fall under *tzedakah*, or perhaps simply under the general rubric of *neder*? The *gemara* does not address this directly.

## Motza Sefatecha Dras (II)

### Two Stages

The *gemara* now introduces a crucial distinction. After exhausting the question of why each element of *motza sefatecha* is needed when we already have other sources, the *gemara* offers its definitive answer: *chad amar v'lo afrish v'chad afrish v'lo akriv* — one verse speaks to the case where the person has taken a vow but has not yet separated an animal, and the other speaks to the case where the person has already sanctified the animal but has not yet brought it as a *korban*. Both stages involve *bal te'acher*, and each requires its own source.

And *u'tzricha* — you need both. If we only knew that *bal te'acher* applies when the person has taken a vow without yet separating an animal — *mishum delo kaim l'diburei*, because he has not yet acted on his words, his vow remains unsatisfied — one might have thought that once the animal is sanctified, the obligation is effectively in the Temple's possession already, and there is no further *bal te'acher* for the delay in physically delivering it. *Kol heicha de'isa b'igaza de'rachmana isa* — wherever the animal is, it is as if it sits in the divine storehouse. So we need the verse to tell us there is *bal te'acher* even in the second stage.

Conversely, if we only knew that *bal te'acher* applies when the animal has been sanctified and is being held without delivery — *dekamoshi gabei*, because there is something concrete that belongs to *hekdesh* and is being withheld — one might have thought that mere words, a vow without any physical action, are not sufficient to trigger *bal te'acher*. *Dibura lo kelum hu* — words alone amount to nothing in this context; *bal te'acher* requires that there be an actual object being held back from the *beit hamikdash*, somewhat analogous to *lo ta'ashok sechar sachir* — withholding a laborer's wages. Therefore both verses are necessary.

This raises an interesting question that the *gemara* itself does not address: if someone says *harei alai olah* — taking a vow — and then two *regalim* pass, and then he sanctifies an animal, and then two more *regalim* pass, and then he brings it — has he violated *bal te'acher*? Are these two distinct forms of *bal te'acher* such that sanctifying the animal resets the clock on the second stage, or does the original vow define the entire period from start to finish? The *gemara* does not say the clock resets, and presumably it does not, but the existence of two distinct dimensions of *bal te'acher* — the unfulfilled vow and the unsacrificed animal — is worth marking.

### Neder & Nedavah

The *gemara* raises a sharp difficulty with this two-stage framework. It notes that the word *nedavah* appears in both of the relevant verses — and by definition, a *nedavah* involves saying *harei zu*, thereby immediately sanctifying the animal. So if a *nedavah* always comes with a sanctified animal, how can one of the verses be addressing the case where the person has only spoken and not yet sanctified anything? *U'nedavah ksiv* — *nedavah* is written in both verses.

The *mishnah* makes the distinction explicit: *eizehu neder? ha'omer harei alai olah* — a *neder* is when one says "I take upon myself an *olah*." *V'eizehu nedavah? ha'omer harei zu olah* — a *nedavah* is when one says "this animal shall be an *olah*," immediately sanctifying the object. The difference has practical consequences: if it is a *neder* and the animal dies or is stolen, the person remains obligated and must bring another, since his personal commitment (*harei alai*) is still outstanding. If it is a *nedavah* and the animal dies or is stolen, he is exempt, since he never took on a personal obligation — he only sanctified the animal, and once it is gone, so is the obligation.

So the *gemara* asks: how can one verse be addressing the pre-sanctification stage when *nedavah* — written in both verses — means the animal has already been sanctified?

Rava resolves this. *Mashkachat lah kegon d'amar: harei alai olah al m'nat she'eini chayav b'achrayushah* — the case is where a person says: I accept upon myself the obligation of an *olah* on the condition that I will not be responsible for it if something happens to it after I sanctify it. This is technically a *neder* — it involves a personal obligation, *harei alai* — but it carries the halachic consequence of a *nedavah* with respect to *achrayut*: if the animal dies, he is not obligated to replace it. Rava is thus saying that the word *nedavah* in the verse that addresses the pre-sanctification stage refers not to a classic *nedavah* — *harei zu* — but to a *neder* that has assumed *nedavah*-like consequences through this stipulation.

In summary: the first verse addresses a *neder* in the classic sense (*harei alai*) or a *neder* qualified to resemble a *nedavah* in its consequences — in both cases, no animal has yet been physically sanctified. The second verse addresses the case where the animal has already been sanctified, whether through a classic *nedavah* (*harei zu*) or through a *neder* that has been actualized by designating a specific animal. Both stages trigger *bal te'acher*, and both require their own scriptural source.

## Tzedakah & Bedek (III)

### Rava Tzedakah

Having established the framework of *bal te'acher*, the *gemara* now turns to Rava's striking position on *tzedakah*. Rava declares: *utzedakah mechayev alah le'alter* — regarding *tzedakah*, one immediately becomes obligated. The violation of *bal te'acher* for *tzedakah* does not wait for three *regalim*; it is instantaneous. *Mai ta'ama? Dekaimi aniyim* — the reason is that the poor are present. The *regel* functions as the defining window of opportunity for *korbanot* — when one is *oleh l'regel* is when one has the opportunity to bring the offering. By *tzedakah*, every day that the poor are around constitutes such an opportunity. Failure to act is therefore an immediate transgression.

The *gemara* responds *pshita* — this is obvious — and one might expect that to end the discussion. But the *gemara* clarifies that it was not obvious: one might have thought that since *tzedakah* appears in the same *drasha* as *korbanot*, it should follow the same schedule — *ad de'avrei alei gimmel regalim ke'korbanot*. Therefore Rava tells us: *hatam hu de'leitnehu de'talinehu Rachmana b'regalim* — there, by *korbanot*, the Torah pegged the obligation to the *regalim*, which is when the opportunity arises. *Aval hacha*, here by *tzedakah*, it is not about *regalim* — *de'shchichi aniyim* — the poor are always present.

---

## Rava Severity (III)

### Rava I: Immediate

This is the first of three escalating statements from Rava about the severity and scope of *bal te'acher*. The immediate obligation for *tzedakah* — *mechayev alah le'alter* — is Rava's most extreme formulation, and it raises practical questions about what "immediately" means. Does one have even a short period to get one's affairs in order before writing the check, as it were? And does the obligation exist continuously, since the poor are always around?

---

## Tzedakah & Bedek (III)

### Tosafot Limitation

Tosafot, commenting on 4a, pulls back from the full force of Rava's position. Tosafot writes: *hani mili dekaimi aniyim, aval lo mechayev lehadori basreihu kol zman she'lo avru aleha shalosh regalim* — this applies when the poor are right in front of you. But if poor people are not directly present, one is not required to go searching for them; the obligation would still be governed by the three-*regalim* framework. Tosafot thus preserves Rava's principle while containing it: the immediacy of the obligation is activated by direct encounter with the poor, not by the abstract existence of poverty in the world.

## Rava Severity (III)

### Rava II: One Regel

The second of Rava's statements: *keivan she'avar alav regel echad, over b'asei* — once a single *regel* has passed without the person fulfilling his vow, he transgresses a positive commandment. The *mitzvat asei* of *u'vata shama v'havatem shama* — the obligation to bring one's sacrifices to the place God chooses — is violated as soon as the first opportunity is missed. Three *regalim* must pass to trigger *bal te'acher* proper, the *lo ta'asei*, but even one *regel* triggers a violation of the *asei*.

The *gemara* challenges this from a *baraita*: the case of the *vlad shlamim*, the offspring of a *shlamim* sacrifice. A witness testified: *hayesa lanu parah shel zivchei shlamim*, we once had a cow that was a *shlamim*, *v'achalnuhu b'Pesach*, we ate it at Pesach, *v'achalnu valdah shlamim b'chag*, and we ate its offspring as a *shlamim* on *Sukkot*. The *gemara* asks: why was the offspring not brought by *Shavuot*? According to Rava, one *regel* was already enough to trigger a violation of the *asei*. Did they not transgress by letting *Shavuot* pass?

Mar Zutra, in the name of Rava, answers: *kegon she'hayesa cholah b'Atzeret* — perhaps the animal was ill at *Shavuot*, and an ill animal may not be brought as a *korban*; therefore the delay was justified. Rav Ashi offers a different resolution: *mai v'achalnu valdah shlamim b'chag* — what does "on the *chag*" mean? He reads *chag* as referring to the next holiday, which would be *Shavuot* itself, not *Sukkot*. The *gemara* objects that this reading is linguistically strained: *kol heicha de'tani Pesach tani Atzeret* — wherever the *tanna* mentions Pesach and then wishes to refer to *Shavuot*, he calls it *Atzeret*. If he simply says *chag*, he means *Sukkot*. So Rav Ashi's reading is rejected, and Mar Zutra's answer — illness at *Shavuot* — stands.

### Rava III: Daily

The third of Rava's statements: *v'amar Rava, keivan she'avru alav gimmel regalim, b'chol yom va-yom over b'bal te'acher* — once three *regalim* have passed, one transgresses *bal te'acher* anew every single day. The rationale is practical as well as moral: if the transgression were a one-time event, once the person is already *over*, there is no further urgency to bring the *korban*. By declaring a fresh violation each day, the Torah maintains constant pressure on the person to remedy the situation.

---

## Rava Severity (III)

### Baraita Challenge

The *gemara* challenges Rava's third statement from the same *baraita* cited earlier: *echad bechor v'echad kol ha-kodashim, keivan she'avru aleihem shana belo regalim, regalim belo shana, over b'val te'acher* — whether it is a *bechor* or any consecrated animal, once a year without *regalim* or *regalim* without a year have passed, one transgresses *bal te'acher*. The *gemara* reads this as implying that the transgression happens once, at the threshold, not repeatedly each day. So is there a contradiction with Rava?

Rav Kahana responds: *man dekamosi shapir kamosi* — whoever raises this question raises it well. *Mikhdei, tana alavai kamahadar* — the *tanna* of that *baraita* is trying to find every possible scenario in which one transgresses. If you were trying to exhaust all the cases, you should have added: and after three *regalim*, every single day. Since he did not say that, presumably the *tanna* does not hold like Rava.

The *gemara* pushes back: *v'idakh, tana le-mikvei b'lav kamahadar* — the other side responds that the *tanna* is only interested in defining the parameters of the *lav*, when it first takes effect. *B'lav yeteira lo kamahadar* — counting the accumulation of subsequent violations is not his concern. He defines the threshold; Rava defines the ongoing consequence. The two positions are not contradictory — they address different questions. Rava's three statements therefore stand unchallenged: *tzedakah* is immediate, an *asei* is violated after one *regel*, and after three *regalim* a new violation accrues every day.

## Calendar Problem

### Lesser Measure

The *gemara* now turns to analyze the *baraita* itself in more detail. *Gufa*: the *baraita* teaches that *bal te'acher* is triggered by the lesser of three *regalim* or one year. *Bishlama regalim belo shana meshakchat lah* — the case of three *regalim* without a full year is easily constructed: if one makes a vow just before Pesach, then *Shavuot* and *Sukkot* follow within the same calendar year, giving three *regalim* in less than twelve months.

### Year Without Regalim

But *ela shana belo regalim heichi meshakchat lah* — how do you construct the inverse case, a full year without three *regalim*? A normal year always contains three *regalim*. The *gemara* notes that this is easy *l'man de'it lei kesidran* — for the opinion that requires the *regalim* to pass in their proper sequence (*Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot*). Under that view, a vow taken between *Pesach* and *Sukkot* could result in a year passing before completing the sequence *kesidran*. But for the opinion that any three *regalim* suffice, regardless of order — *l'man deleit lei kesidran* — any three *regalim* in any sequence count. How would you then construct a full year without three *regalim*?

### Rabbi Solar Year

The *gemara* answers: *bishlama le-Rebbi, de'meshakchat lah b'shana me'uberet* — for Rebbi, there is a ready answer, derived from a *baraita* about the redemption of a sold house in a walled city, which must be redeemed within a "full year." Rebbi holds: *gimel me'ot v'shishim v'chamisha yamim keminian yemos hachama* — 365 days, according to the count of the solar year. The Sages hold: *monei yud-bet chodesh miyom l'yom* — twelve months from day to day, and if the year was a leap year, the seller gains the extra month.

Under Rebbi's view, a year is 365 solar days, irrespective of the Hebrew calendar. In a leap year, the Hebrew calendar adds an extra month, making the lunar year 383 or 384 days. If one sanctifies an animal right after *Pesach* in a leap year — say, on the first of *Iyar* — and defines the year as 365 days, that span of 365 days will end before the next *Pesach* arrives. Since the leap year pushed the next *Pesach* further away (because it takes approximately 384 days to reach the same date again), 365 days will expire while one is still in *Adar Beit*, before the next *Pesach*. Three *regalim* have not yet recurred — the new *Pesach* has not come — yet 365 days have passed. So according to Rebbi, one can have a full year without three *regalim*.

### Shavuot Fluctuation

But for the Sages, who define a year by the same calendar date twelve (or thirteen) months later, the problem remains: under a normal calendar, the same date always brings you back after three *regalim*. The *gemara* resolves this by citing a teaching of Rabbi Shemaya: *Atzeret pe'amim heh, pe'amim vav, pe'amim zayin* — *Shavuot* can fall on the 5th, 6th, or 7th of Sivan, depending on whether *Nisan* and *Iyar* are both full months (30 days each, giving the 5th), both deficient (29 days each, giving the 7th), or one full and one deficient (giving the 6th).

This fluctuation creates the possibility of a full year without *Shavuot* occurring again on or before its previous date. If one took a vow on the 6th of *Sivan* in a year when *Shavuot* fell on the 5th, and the following year *Shavuot* falls on the 7th, then when the anniversary arrives — the 6th of *Sivan* of the following year — *Shavuot* has not yet occurred that year. The 6th of *Sivan* has come again (completing a full lunar year from the same date), but the festival of *Shavuot* is still one day away. A full year has elapsed without three *regalim* having passed through that date.

### Acherim Fixed

The *gemara* then asks: who argues with Rabbi Shemaya's position that the date of *Shavuot* can fluctuate? The answer is *Acherim* — identified as Rabbi Meir — who hold that the holidays shift by a fixed number of days each year. A *baraita* teaches: *Acherim omrim, ein bein Atzeret l'Atzeret, ein bein Rosh Hashanah l'Rosh Hashanah ela arba yamim bilvad* — from one year's *Shavuot* to the next, the calendar shifts by exactly four days. This is because a Hebrew year contains 354 days, and dividing by seven leaves a remainder of four. So each subsequent year's holidays fall four weekdays later than the previous year's. *V'im haysa shanah me'uberet, chamisha* — in a leap year, the shift is five days rather than four, because the added month is always a fixed 29-day month, and 29 divided by seven leaves a remainder of one, adding one more day to the shift.

The elegance of the *Acherim* system lies in its perfect regularity — no month-length variation, no fluctuation in the date of *Shavuot*. One might ask why such a clean calendar was not adopted in practice. The answer, as noted, is that other constraints — such as the prohibition against *Rosh Hashanah* falling on certain days of the week — require the flexibility of variable month lengths, and that system is what prevents adoption of the fully fixed calendar that *Acherim* envision.
