# Bava Batra 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Taluhu ve-Zavin

### Gezel Background

The *sugya* of Bava Batra 48 is a self-contained and richly layered discussion of *taluhu ve-zavin* — the question of whether a sale conducted under coercion is legally valid. To appreciate the full arc of the argument, we return briefly to the bottom of 47b, where the Gemara had just established that if a *gazlan* produces a signed *shtar* purporting to show that he purchased a field, the document is inadmissible. The reasoning is straightforward: we presume that the original owner agreed to write the *shtar* only out of fear of the consequences of refusing. The coercion underlying the transaction, according to this earlier discussion, strips the document of any legal standing. It is precisely against this backdrop that Rav Huna's general principle must be understood.

### Rav Huna Statement

At the bottom of 47b, Rav Huna states: *taluhu ve-zavin, zevineih zevinei* — if someone was suspended, meaning compelled under duress, and sold his field or some other property, it is nevertheless a legitimate sale. This is a striking position. Rav Huna is not carving out an exception for mild social pressure; he is declaring that coerced sales, as a category, are valid.

### All Sales Forced

The rationale Rav Huna offers is equally striking: *kol demazbin inish, ee lav de-anis lo hava mazbin* — anything that people sell, they would not have sold were they not in some sense forced to do so. This reflects the economic reality of a pre-commercial, agrarian society in which property is not a commodity to be traded for profit. People sell only when circumstances compel them — when they need to raise funds, when a debt presses, when a family must be fed. In such a world, every sale is in some sense an act born of necessity, and the seller, though driven by circumstance, nonetheless makes the decision himself. He weighs his options, determines that selling is the lesser hardship, and proceeds. That decision, however reluctant, is still *his* decision. Therefore, Rav Huna argues, even when an external party is the source of the coercive pressure, if the seller ultimately chooses to go through with the transaction, that choice is real and the sale is binding.

### Self vs External

The Gemara immediately registers the obvious objection, at the bottom of 47b: *v'dilma shani unsa denafshei me-unsa de-achrinei* — perhaps there is a fundamental difference between pressure that originates from within one's own circumstances and coercion imposed by another person. When I decide to sell because my family needs food, I am the author of my decision. I weigh the competing demands on me and I choose. That is very different from a situation in which someone holds a weapon to my head, in which case the decision is not really mine at all — it is simply the coercer's will being executed through my body. This distinction makes enormous intuitive sense, and the Gemara takes it seriously enough to set aside the analogy to ordinary commercial pressure and seek a more direct proof for Rav Huna's principle.

## Korban Proof

### Korban & Lirtzono

The Gemara turns to a *baraita* at the top of 48a. The verse concerning the *olah* says *yakriv oto* — "he shall bring it." The *baraita* reads this as implying not only that the individual must bring his vowed *korban*, but that *beis din* may compel him to do so: *melamed shekofin oto*. Yet an adjacent phrase says *lirtzono* — it must be his will, his desire. The *baraita* itself poses the question, *ha katzad*, how can both be true simultaneously?

### Coercion Process

The resolution is: *kofin oto ad sheyomar rotzeh ani* — we compel him until he himself says "I want it." The coercion does not replace his will; it continues until his will is extracted from him, expressed in his own words. At that point, the act is considered genuinely his. The Gemara attempts to derive from this case a proof for Rav Huna's principle: just as external compulsion does not invalidate the *korban* once the individual declares his consent, so too external compulsion should not invalidate a sale once the seller goes along with the transaction.

### Korban Different

The Gemara deflects this proof: *v'dilma shani hasam denichla lei d'teihavei lei kaparah* — perhaps that case is different because the person actually wants the atonement the *korban* provides. There is something he is receiving, something of immense personal value, and that benefit makes it plausible that when he says *rotzeh ani* he genuinely means it. Furthermore, it should be noted — as the *Rashbam* observes — that nobody obligated him to take this vow in the first place. He expressed from the outset his desire for the *kaparah* the *korban* would bring. Compelling him to follow through is not so much imposing something external as reconnecting him to his own prior commitment. For these reasons, the *korban* case may be uniquely suited to genuine reconciliation in a way that a purely secular coerced sale is not. The proof therefore fails.

## Get Divorce Case

### Get & Kofin

The Gemara pivots to the second case, introduced with *ve'ela meseifa* — let us try to derive the proof from the following *Mishna*. By *gitei nashim*, the same rule applies: *kofin oto ad sheyomar rotzeh ani*. When a husband is obligated to divorce his wife but refuses, *beis din* can compel him, applying physical pressure until he declares *rotze ani*. Since the husband must be the one to issue the *get* — *beis din* cannot issue it in his stead — and since the *get* must be issued willingly, the mechanism of compelling him until he says *rotze ani* is the operative solution.

### Get Religious Aspect

The Gemara again objects: *v'dilma shani hasam demitzvah lishmoa divrei chachamim* — perhaps this case too is special, because there is a religious dimension to the compulsion. *Chazal* have determined that this man is obligated to divorce his wife. When he is coerced and ultimately says *rotze ani*, we are prepared to accept that declaration as genuine because, at some level, he does want to do the right thing. He wants to align himself with the ruling of the *chachamim*. He wants to fulfil his religious obligation. The coercion is not introducing something wholly alien to his will; it is removing the *yetzer hara* that had been obstructing what his deeper self already wanted. This is qualitatively different from a case where someone is simply being pressured to part with property for purely secular reasons, with no religious obligation in the background. The proof from the *get* therefore likewise fails to establish Rav Huna's principle in the general case.

It is worth pausing here to note a point raised by *Tosafot* that addresses both of these attempted proofs. *Tosafot* cites a resolution by the *Ri*: *de-chol davar she-hu mechuyav la'asos, havei k'mo mecher* — anything one is obligated to do is analogous to a sale. By the *korban*, the person is obligated to bring it; by *gitei nashim*, the husband is obligated to give the *get*. The analogy to a coerced sale thus holds not because both involve getting something in exchange, but because in both situations the compulsion is not purely external — in each case, there is something the person is already bound to do. When one is obligated to act and is then compelled to follow through, we can say, combined with that compulsion, that he has genuinely made his peace with the act. This is structurally analogous to *taluhu ve-zavin*, where what reconciles the seller is the compensation he receives. *Tosafot*'s point is that obligation performs the same reconciling function as money. Nonetheless, the Gemara itself notes that the *korban* and *get* cases may represent an even stronger form of reconciliation — rooted in religious obligation and the mitzvah to heed the *chachamim* — and so they remain distinguishable from the purely monetary case.

### Tosafot Obligation

*Tosafot*'s resolution, then, is that the criterion for a coerced act to be valid is not merely receiving compensation, but more broadly that the actor has some basis — whether financial compensation or pre-existing obligation — upon which to reconcile himself to the transaction. Money provides that basis in the sale context; religious obligation provides it in the *korban* and *get* contexts. The Gemara, while acknowledging the parallel, concludes that the cases of *korban* and *get* cannot serve as direct proofs for our case because their religious dimension makes them categorically stronger. The question of *taluhu ve-zavin* in the purely secular context must therefore rest on independent reasoning.

## Rambam Framework

### Rambam Anuss

Before proceeding to that independent reasoning, it is worth examining what the *Rambam* says about the conceptual underpinnings of this entire discussion, particularly in the context of *get*. The *Rambam* in Hilchot Gerushin 2:20 addresses the question directly: why is this *get* not nullified? After all, the husband is plainly *anus* — coerced. The *Rambam*'s answer is definitional: *she-ein omrim anus ela le-mi she-neilchatz ve-nidechak la'asos davar she-eino mechuyav bo min ha-Torah* — we only apply the category of *anus* to someone who is being compelled to do something he is not obligated in from the Torah. In that case, the coercion is wholly external, there is nothing inside the person generating any pull toward the act, and so the act truly is not his. Like someone who was beaten until he sold or gave something — that is a case of genuine *ones*. That is exactly the case of *taluhu ve-zavin*, and the *Rambam* explicitly includes *machar*, selling, as an instance of coercion.

### Yetzer Hara/Tov

But then the *Rambam* introduces a contrasting category: *aval mi she-takfo yitzro ha-ra le-vatel mitzvah o la'asos aveirah ve-huka ad she-asa davar she-chayav la'asoso* — but someone whose *yetzer hara* seized him to violate a commandment or commit a transgression, and he was beaten until he did what he was obligated to do or distanced himself from what he was forbidden to do — *ein zeh anus mimenu*, this is not coercion imposed upon him from outside. Rather, *hu anas atzmo be-da'ato ha-ra'ah* — he himself had been coercing himself through his own corrupt inclination. This is a remarkable psychological and philosophical move. The *Rambam* is saying that the *yetzer hara* is itself a form of self-imposed coercion, an internal tyrant that overrides what the person's deeper, truer will actually wants.

Applied to the husband who refuses to give a *get*: since he wants to be part of the Jewish people, *rotzeh la'asos kol ha-mitzvos u-le-hisrachek min ha-aveiros*, and it is only his *yetzer hara* that has seized him — *ve-yitzro hu she-takfo* — when he is beaten until his *yetzer hara* weakens, *v'amar rotzeh ani*, he is in truth being liberated to do what he genuinely wanted all along. The beating does not impose an alien will upon him; it removes the internal obstruction that had been preventing his true will from expressing itself. It is loosely analogous to the hardening of Pharaoh's heart — there, one might say, the heart was hardened enough to withstand the pressure of the *makkot*, allowing the underlying dynamic to play out; here, in the reverse direction, the *yetzer hara* is weakened so the *yetzer hatov* can emerge.

### Get Lirtzono

The *Rambam*'s framework also illuminates the significance of the term *lirtzono*. The verse by *korban* requires *lirtzono*, and the *Rambam* identifies *lirtzono* as the first of ten Torah requirements for a *get* — the husband must divorce *lirtzono*, an expression rooted in the verse *ve'im lo timtza chen be'einav*, that he no longer finds favor in his wife's eyes. The *acharonim* note that every transaction requires *leda'ato* — awareness and agreement — but *lirtzono* adds something: not merely agreement, but genuine desire. This explains why, in the *Rambam*'s treatment, *rotzeh ani* is specified for *korban* and *get* but not necessarily for monetary sales. By a sale, what is required is that the seller genuinely agrees and commits to the transaction, even if he does not desire it in any deep sense. No one truly desires to sell a beloved field to put food on the table; but they agree, they decide, and that suffices. By *korban* and *get*, however, *lirtzono* demands something closer to genuine desire, and therefore *rotzeh ani* must be articulated explicitly.

## Sikrikon Field Sale

### Sikrikon Mishna

With the theoretical foundations established, the Gemara turns to what would constitute direct evidence for *taluhu ve-zavin* in the purely monetary context. The vehicle is the *Mishna* in *Gittin* concerning the *sikrikon* — the non-Jewish strongman, a kind of robber baron, who had seized fields from their Jewish owners. The case: someone purchased a field from the *sikrikon*, and then went back and re-purchased it from the original owner. The *Mishna* rules *mikcho batul* — the purchase is invalid. This seems puzzling at first. The original owner's field had already been stolen; he might as well sell it to this Jew and receive some money for what he had already lost. The *sikrikon* presumably loomed in the background as an implicit or explicit threat. These are all circumstances that were coercing the original owner. And yet the *Mishna* says the sale is invalid.

### Validity Question

The Gemara presses: *ve'amai*? Why should this not be valid under the principle of *taluhu ve-zavin*? The original owner was coerced by the circumstances of the *sikrikon*, but *agav ontzei gamar umakni* — because of the coercion, he ultimately decided and transferred — so the sale should be good. This is precisely where the direct proof for or against Rav Huna's principle can be tested: if the *Mishna* says the coerced sale to the buyer is invalid, does that contradict *taluhu ve-zavin*?

### Rav Commitment

Rav's answer resolves the apparent contradiction by introducing a crucial condition. *Lo shanu ella de'amar lei lech chazek ukeni* — we only say the sale is invalid when the original owner merely said verbally, "go take possession." A verbal declaration alone is insufficient to demonstrate genuine reconciliation with the transaction. *Aval bishtar kana* — but when the original owner goes so far as to write a *shtar*, that act is sufficient evidence of genuine intent, and the sale is valid. Writing a formal document is a deliberate, effortful act that goes well beyond passive compliance. Even if he felt coerced to write it, the very act of committing the transaction to writing demonstrates that he did choose to go through with it. The *Mishna*'s ruling of invalidity, then, is confined to the case where only a verbal authorization was given, and it poses no challenge to Rav Huna's principle where a *shtar* was written — or, as we will see, where money was actually received.

---

## Shmuel Money

### Shmuel Achrayus

The Gemara now introduces Shmuel's more stringent position. Shmuel says that even the writing of a *shtar* is insufficient — *af bishtar nami lo kana* — unless the *shtar* includes an *achrayus* clause, a commitment of personal liability for the sale. Only when the seller takes on personal liability does it become clear that he is truly owning the transaction and not merely going through the motions under duress. An *achrayus* clause is the strongest possible signal that the seller really means what he is doing.

### Shmuel Agreement

One might then ask: if Shmuel requires *achrayus* even beyond a *shtar*, how does he relate to Rav Huna's principle that a coerced sale is valid? The Gemara resolves this: *modeh Shmuel heika de'yahiv zuzei* — Shmuel agrees that the sale is valid when money was actually given. And here the Gemara at last makes explicit what has been implied throughout: *taluhu ve-zavin* is categorically different from *taluhu ve-yahiv*. A coerced *sale* is valid; a coerced *gift* is not. The reason is precisely that money changes the character of the transaction. Even under duress, a person who receives payment has something concrete that reconciles him to the decision. He may not have desired to sell, but he is getting compensated, and that compensation provides the psychological basis for genuine acquiescence. In the *sikrikon* case, when money was not actually received — only a verbal agreement or even a *shtar* was produced — Shmuel holds that the threshold of genuine reconciliation has not been met. But once money actually changes hands, even Shmuel agrees that *gamar umakni* — the seller has made his peace, and the sale stands.

There remains the contrary voice of Rav Bibi, who states in the name of Rav Nachman that the *shtar* of a *gazlan* is not recognized even after money has changed hands — *karka ein lo, maos yesh lo*, the land remains with its original owner but the *gazlan* gets his money back. This position seemingly contradicts Rav Huna outright, holding that even when money was paid, a coerced sale remains invalid. The Gemara resolves the contradiction simply: *memra de-Rav Huna lo svira lei* — Rav Bibi is presenting an Amoraic tradition, and Rav Huna is not bound to agree with it. The two positions are simply in dispute, and the halacha will need to be determined accordingly.

---

## Rava Limitations

### Field Choice

The Gemara now introduces Rava's qualification of Rav Huna's ruling. Rava states: *hilchasa, talyuhu ve-zavin, zivinei zivinei* — the halacha follows Rav Huna, a coerced sale is valid. But Rava immediately proceeds to limit the scope of this ruling with a series of conditions. The first limitation: *ola amran ela besadeh stam* — we only say this when the buyer said "sell me one of your fields" without specifying which, and the seller chose which field to transfer. Since the seller played an active role in selecting the property being sold, he exercised a meaningful degree of choice. The *Rashbam* notes that presumably he would have sold his worst field in such a circumstance, which makes it even more plausible that he arrived at genuine acquiescence. *Aval besadeh zo lo* — but if the buyer said "sell me *this* field," leaving the seller no discretion whatsoever, then the seller is merely executing the coercer's will and the transaction lacks the element of personal decision-making that would demonstrate reconciliation.

### Money Counting

The second limitation refines even the *sadeh zo* case: *ola amran ela d'lo artzei zuzei* — even when the seller had no choice of field, the sale is invalid only if the money was not counted out. If the seller received the payment and then counted it out, verifying the amount, that act of counting is itself evidence of engagement and acquiescence. Counting the money is not the action of someone who is being wholly manipulated; it reflects a degree of active participation in the exchange. *Aval artzei zuzei* — if he did count the money, even in the *sadeh zo* case, the sale is valid.

### Escape Routes

The third limitation applies even where money was not counted: *v'lo amran ela d'hava lei l'ishtamutei* — we only declare the sale invalid if the seller had no available avenue of escape, no excuse he could have offered, no way to buy time or maneuver out of the situation. If, on the other hand, there were options available to him — ways he could have deflected or delayed — and he did not avail himself of them, that passivity is itself evidence of acquiescence. He could have tried to get out of it and did not, and that suggests he resigned himself to the sale and ultimately agreed to it. *Aval hava lei l'ishtamutei v'lo ishtamit* — if he had an escape route and didn't use it, the sale is valid. Only when all three negative conditions converge — he was told *sadeh zo*, the money was not counted, and he had no avenue of escape — can we say he was wholly passive and the sale is invalid.

---

## All Valid Conclusion

### General Valid

After laying out these three conditions, the Gemara makes a decisive ruling: *v'hilchata, b'kulhu d'hava zvineih zvini* — the halacha is that in *all* of these cases the sale is valid. Even *sadeh zo*, even without counting money, even without an available escape — a coerced sale with compensation is valid. Rava's attempted limitations do not survive as halakhic conditions.

### Woman Marriage

The Gemara demonstrates this through a comparison: *d'ishah k'sadeh zo dami* — a woman in the context of marriage is analogous to *sadeh zo*. When a man forces a woman to accept *kiddushin*, she is in the position of the seller given no choice of which field to transfer. The buyer, so to speak, has specified exactly what he wants — her, not her sister, not someone else. She has no discretion whatsoever.

### Forced Kiddushin

Yet Meimar teaches: *talyuha v'kadesh, kidushav kidushin* — if a man coerced a woman and she accepted *kiddushin*, the *kiddushin* is valid. This is the *sadeh zo* scenario par excellence — she had no choice of "field" — and the ruling is that it is valid. This validates the general conclusion that *taluhu ve-zavin* holds even in the most constrained circumstances, across all the cases Rava had sought to limit.

## Woman Exception

### Woman Invalid

However, Mar bar Rav Ashi immediately objects: *b'ishah vadai kidushin lo havu* — regarding a woman forced into marriage, he is certain the *kiddushin* is not valid. He does not challenge the general principle of *taluhu ve-zavin* in property transactions; his objection is specific to this case. Even if by other coerced transactions the rule holds, forcing a woman into marriage is different.

### Improper Act

The reason Mar bar Rav Ashi gives is moral and jurisprudential: *hu asah shelo khogan, lefikach asu imo shelo khogan, v'afki'inhu rabanan l'kidushin minei* — he acted improperly, therefore they deal with him improperly, and the *rabanan* nullify his *kiddushin*. This is the principle of *hafkaat kiddushin* — the power of *chazal* to annul a marriage. Because he committed a moral violation in forcing this woman, the rabbis respond by removing the legal effect of his act. The *kiddushin* is void.

### Zenut Conversion

Ravina poses the technical challenge to Rav Ashi: *teinach de-kadesh be-chaspa* — I understand the mechanism if he performed *kiddushin* with money, because *beis din* can deploy *hefker beis din hefker*, declare the money ownerless, and without valid money there is no *kiddushin*. But *kadesh be-viah mai ika le-meimar* — what if he performed *kiddushin* through *biah*? How can the rabbis nullify an act that physically occurred? The Gemara's answer: *shavinhu rabbanan le-vilato beilat zenut* — the rabbis declare that his act of *biah* is reclassified as *beilat zenut*, fornication, not as *kiddushei biah*.

### Annulment Theory

Three distinct theoretical frameworks emerge from the *Rishonim* for understanding this mechanism. The *Rashbam* invokes the principle *kol ha-mekadesh adaita de-rabbanan mekadesh* — everyone who performs *kiddushin* does so implicitly conditioned on the approval of the rabbis, so that retroactively, when the rabbis withhold approval, the *kiddushin* was never valid. However, *Tosafot* points out that this principle is invoked in cases where the act was initially proper and is being retroactively annulled — where one can plausibly say the person had always implicitly stipulated rabbinic approval. Here, the man is committing a flagrant moral violation, acting demonstrably against the will of the rabbis. One cannot say he is acting *adaita de-rabbanan*. That is precisely why, *Tosafot* notes, the Gemara does not use that phrase here. The second framework, cited by *Tosafot*, is *yesh koach be-yad chachamim la-akor davar min ha-Torah* — the rabbis possess the power, granted by the Torah itself, to override even a Torah-level reality when circumstances warrant it. The third framework understands marriage as a social construct: for an act of *biah* to be contextualized as *kiddushei biah*, it requires not merely the subjective intention of the actor but a societal framework willing to recognize it as such. When the man acts in a manner that is demonstrably *asah shelo khogan* — a clear moral violation — society, through its rabbinic institutions, refuses to contextualize the act as *kiddushin*. It is seen instead as *beilat zenut*, as rape. There is no social recognition of this as marriage, and therefore it is not marriage.

## Moda'ah Remedy

### Tavi Papi

The Gemara concludes with a practical case that ties the entire *sugya* together. Tavi coerced Papi in connection with a *kidra* — some type of property — and Papi sold it under duress.

### Both Documents

Rabbah bar bar Chana found himself as a witness in this situation and signed both the *moda'ah* — the formal declaration by Papi that he was being coerced and did not genuinely consent to the transaction — and subsequently the *ashkelta*, the bill of sale that formalized the coerced transaction. The *moda'ah* is the crucial legal remedy available to a coerced party: before or during the coercion, the person can go before witnesses, declare that he is being compelled, state that any documents he will sign or acts he will perform are not expressions of genuine will, and have that declaration witnessed and recorded. Such a declaration, properly executed, nullifies any subsequent coerced transaction.

### Both Signings Valid

Rav Huna asserts: *man dechatim amoda'ah shapir chatim, uman dechatim a-ashkaltah shapir chatim* — the witness who signed the *moda'ah* acted properly, and the witness who signed the bill of sale also acted properly. Both signings were legitimate.

### Logical Problem

The Gemara objects immediately: how can both be legitimate? *Mimmah nafshach* — take either position and one undermines the other. If the *moda'ah* is valid, then the bill of sale is nullified by it, and the witness to the sale was signing a meaningless document. If the bill of sale is valid, then the *moda'ah* should have been irrelevant, and signing it accomplished nothing. The two instruments are logically in tension with each other.

### Moda'ah Purpose

The Gemara resolves this with a formulation that also provides the conceptual key to understanding *moda'ah* in the framework of *taluhu ve-zavin*: *ilav moda'ah, man dechatim a-ashkaltah shapir chatim* — were it not for the *moda'ah*, the person who signed the bill of sale would have done perfectly fine, because absent a *moda'ah*, the coerced sale would have been valid under Rav Huna's principle. The bill of sale was therefore in principle a legitimate document. The *moda'ah*, however, exists precisely to neutralize that presumptive validity. When Papi declared before witnesses that he was being coerced, he pre-empted the *agav ontzei gamar umakni* logic — he made clear in advance that he was *not* reconciling himself to the transaction, that whatever words or signatures might follow were not expressions of genuine consent. The *moda'ah* thus does not contradict the bill of sale because it precedes and conditions it; it says: "When I sign that document, do not read it as genuine consent, because I am telling you now that it will not be." This is why the Gemara says *ilav moda'ah* — the *moda'ah* is the operative instrument that changes the legal character of everything that follows.

The Gemara adds: *Rav Huna l'taimei* — this resolution is perfectly consistent with Rav Huna's own position. He holds that *taluhu ve-zavin* is valid precisely because *agav ontzei gamar umakni*. The *moda'ah* is the mechanism by which a coerced party can demonstrate prospectively that he is *not* making his peace, that reconciliation has not occurred and will not occur, thereby removing the very foundation upon which Rav Huna's principle rests.

### Moda'ah Limits

This, however, immediately opens a practical problem that *Tosafot* raises: what if, at the time of the coerced sale itself, the coercer compels the seller to declare *bitul moda'ah* — a nullification of any prior *moda'ah* he may have issued? If such a declaration is effective, the escape hatch of *moda'ah* is closed. And can one then pre-empt even *that* by declaring at the time of the *moda'ah* that any subsequent *bitul moda'ah* he may be forced to utter is itself not genuine? The question enters a kind of legal infinite regress — like viruses and anti-viruses, each measure generating a counter-measure. The Gemara itself does not resolve this fully in the present passage, but the structure of the problem is clear: the tension between *taluhu ve-zavin* on one side and *moda'ah* on the other creates a system in which the coerced party has at least *some* avenue of protection — provided the *moda'ah* remains operative and is not itself nullified by further coercion. Within the framework of this *sugya*, the *moda'ah* stands as the principal, and perhaps only, remedy available to a seller who has been subjected to the kind of coercion that, under Rav Huna's ruling, would otherwise validate the transaction against him.
