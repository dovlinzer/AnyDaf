# Ketubot 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Framework of Ketubot

### Women's Status & Transit…

The daf we take up today — picked up in the middle of 47b, roughly ten lines below the two dots — sits at the heart of *masechet Ketubot*, the section that deals with the *ketubah* proper: the reciprocal rights and responsibilities between husband and wife. Before arriving at those obligations, however, it is worth situating the *masechet* within a broader framework.

The earlier chapters of *Ketubot* focused on the father's rights over his daughter: his power to annul her vows, to marry her off, and to claim her earnings — most of which are biblical. Notably, the father bears almost no reciprocal responsibilities. He has no legal obligation even to feed his daughter; he could, in principle, send her out of the house to earn her own keep.

This reflects a portrait of women in the *Torah* that is striking to the modern reader: the woman never appears as a fully autonomous agent directing her own fate. She moves from one man's house to another — from her father's house to her husband's house. The *Torah*'s language bears this out explicitly; when a *kohen*'s daughter is widowed or divorced, the verse says *v'shava el beit aviha kine'ureha* — she returns to her father's house. It is the Rabbis who open up the space for a woman's legal independence, ruling that the father's authority ceases when she becomes a *bogeret* at twelve and a half, at which point she becomes her own legal agent. That emancipation is a rabbinic innovation, not a biblical one; the baseline framework of the *Torah* is the transit from father's house to husband's house, and that is precisely the framework *masechet Ketubot* addresses.

### Erusin vs. Nissuin

Within that framework, there is an important distinction between *erusin* — betrothal — and *nissuin* — the actual consummation of marriage and cohabitation. After *erusin*, the woman remains essentially in her father's domain. It is only with *nissuin*, when she begins living together with her husband, that the primary obligations and rights of the *ketubah* fully take effect. The base amount of the *ketubah* — 100 or 200 *zuz*, depending on whether she is a widow or a virgin — exists from the time of *erusin*, but whether the additional amounts, the *tosefes* and the *nedunya* (dowry), take effect before *nissuin* was a matter of debate, as was discussed in the preceding material.

### Three Main Obligations

The ongoing rights and responsibilities during marriage bear a distinct name: *t'nai ketubah* — the stipulations of the *ketubah*. This term refers not to conditions in the contractual sense, but to the ongoing obligations that govern the marriage itself, as distinct from the *ikar ketubah* (the base lump-sum payment of 100 or 200) and the *tosefes* (any additional amount the groom undertakes). The *ikar* and *tosefes* are payable at divorce or the husband's death, functioning essentially as alimony or, in the case of his death, as a substitute for inheritance — since a wife does not inherit her husband — protecting her when she is left without his support. The *t'nai ketubah*, by contrast, are the live, ongoing obligations.

Three primary reciprocal obligations structure this discussion: the husband's duty to provide *mezonot* (food and sustenance) in exchange for his wife's earnings; his duty to redeem her from captivity (*pirkyon*) in exchange for his right to the *peiros* — the fruits and earnings from her *nichsei milug* (property to which she retains title but whose income accrues to him); and his duty to bury her should she predecease him, in exchange for the *nedunya*, the dowry property she transfers to his estate at marriage (*nichsei tzon barzel*).

## Reciprocal Obligations

### Tanu Rabanan

The *Tanu rabanan* at 47b articulates these exchanges formally: *tiknu mezonoteha tachat maaseh yadeha* — they established his obligation to feed her in exchange for her earnings; *upirkona tachat perot* — his obligation to redeem her from captivity in exchange for his right to the *peiros*; and *ukvorata tachat ktuvata* — his obligation to bury her in exchange for the *ketubah*, which here means the *nedunya*, the dowry property.

### Whose Rights Matter?

The *Gemara* then asks a pointed question: *lefikach baal ochel perot* — "therefore the husband eats the fruit" — why is this stated with the word *lefikach* ("therefore"), as if it were the conclusion to be derived, when all three obligations have already been stated? The question exposes an important ambiguity in how the entire structure is framed: is the husband the entitled party — in which case he holds the rights and the obligations are what he concedes in exchange — or is the wife the entitled party, in which case her rights are primary and his benefits are what he receives in exchange?

This framing question matters enormously for practical *halakha*. If the husband is the entitled party — if we read the *braita* as saying, essentially, he has a right to her earnings, and in exchange he feeds her — then it is *he* who can opt out: he could say, keep your earnings, I will not feed you. If, on the contrary, the wife is the entitled party — she has a right to be supported, and in exchange he takes her earnings — then *she* is the one who can opt out: she could say, don't support me, and I will keep my own earnings.

### Fruit & Redemption

The *Gemara* resolves the specific question of the *lefikach* as follows. One might think that since the husband's right to the *peiros* exists specifically to ensure he will redeem his wife from captivity, we should place those earnings in escrow — a dedicated emergency fund — rather than allow him to spend them freely. If the money is segregated, the reasoning goes, he will feel his obligation is discharged once that fund is exhausted, and if the ransom demanded exceeds the amount saved, he will simply refuse to pay the difference. *Lefikach baal ochel perot* — therefore we let him eat the fruit freely, without strict accounting — because if he has used the earnings over the years without segregating them, no exact accounting is kept in either direction: he is not limited to a fixed sum when the time comes to redeem her, and he understands that the obligation to redeem her is open-ended. *Zimnin d'lo malu* — sometimes the accumulated earnings will not be sufficient — and precisely because he has enjoyed them without limit, he will reach into his own pocket to make up the difference and redeem her fully.

This exchange may be difficult for the modern reader to fully appreciate on two levels. First, it presupposes that capture and the need for ransom were not rare occurrences — the *masechet* returns to the topic repeatedly — though that reality is remote from contemporary experience. Second, the *Gemara*'s assumption that a husband might hesitate to redeem his wife is jarring. Yet the discussion notes a real concern: if a wife taken captive might have been raped, or might even willingly engage in sexual relations in order to secure her freedom, a husband might pause before paying the ransom. The *Gemara*'s frank acknowledgment of this dynamic is a striking window into the social and moral realities that shaped these laws.

### Modern Ketubah Reform

The framing question — whose rights are primary — is not merely academic. Returning to it: against the plain sound of the *braita*, which makes the husband appear to be the entitled party, the *halakhic* conclusion is that the wife is the protected party. She is the one entitled to support, and in exchange he receives her earnings. This means she is the one who can opt out of the exchange: she may declare that she will retain her own earnings and forego his support obligation.

This conclusion has direct modern application. *Kolech*, a women's Orthodox organization in Israel, has created a rider to the standard *ketubah* in which the wife first waives her right to be supported and retains her own earnings and the income from her property, thereby severing the exchange on which the standard *t'nai ketubah* rest. Having done so, both parties are then free to enter a new, mutual set of obligations — pooling their earnings and accepting joint responsibility for one another's financial well-being. This restructuring is only legally available to the wife, and only because *halakha* recognizes her, not the husband, as the fundamentally protected party in the original exchange.

Since the *ketubah* in Israel occupies a dual status — simultaneously a ritual document and a legally operative instrument before the rabbinical courts — *Kolech* issues this restructuring as a separate rider rather than altering the traditional *ketubah* text itself, preserving the ritual formula while achieving the legal goal.

## Common & Uncommon

### Abaye's Principle

Having established the three paired exchanges, the *Gemara* asks why specifically these obligations are matched to these rights — why *mezonot* for earnings, *pirkyon* for *peiros*, and burial for *nedunya*, and not some other pairing. *Abaye* supplies the organizing principle: *tiknu matzui l'matzui ve'she'eino matzui l'she'eino matzui* — common things are matched with common things, and uncommon things with uncommon things.

### Classification of Obliga…

The matching follows accordingly. *Mezonot* — the daily obligation to feed his wife — is an everyday, ongoing expense. It is matched against *maasei yadeha* — her earnings — which are likewise an ongoing, regular matter, whether from labor outside the home, or more commonly, from the domestic work she performs within it: cooking, sewing, running the household. Common is matched with common.

*Kevurah* — burial — is a one-time event, as is the *nedunya* the wife brings into the marriage: she brings it once, and it is transferred once. Uncommon is matched with uncommon.

*Pirkyon* — redemption from captivity — and *peiros* — the ongoing earnings from her property holdings — present a more complex case. The *peiros* are an ongoing accumulation, while the ransom may be a one-time and potentially very large demand. The *Gemara* already explained why the lack of exact accounting between these two serves both parties: the husband enjoys the earnings freely, and therefore is expected to redeem her without limit when the moment comes.

### Historical Context

*Rashi* notes a further dimension relevant to the matching of *pirkyon* with *peiros*: while a *nedunya*, a dowry of transferred property, was extremely common — virtually every woman brought one — property that the wife *already owned* at the time of marriage, to which she retained title and from whose income the husband benefited (*nichsei milug*, producing *peiros*), was less common in the *Gemara*'s world. In the contemporary context, by contrast, a professional woman is far more likely to bring personal assets and investments into a marriage than to bring a formal dowry, which inverts the historical frequency. This does not change the legal structure, but it does illuminate why *Rashi* treats *peiros* as the less common item in its pair.

## Ketubah Document

### Ketubah Formulas

It is worth pausing to examine the actual text of the *ketubah* document, which translates these abstract obligations into a formal written formula. The *ketubah* opens with the date — *b'echad b'shabbat*, on the first day of the week, in year such-and-such — and the groom's declaration: *amar lah l'hada betulta, havi li l'intu* — he said to this woman, be for me as a wife. This declaration corresponds to the moment of *kiddushin*; the *ketubah* obligations, however, take effect at *nissuin*.

The groom then undertakes the central obligations: *eflach v'okir ve'ezon ve'afarnes yatich ke'hilchat guvrin yehuda'in* — I will work, honor, sustain, and provide for you in the manner of Jewish husbands. *Eflach* — I will work — means that he commits to going out and earning a living for her benefit. *Okir* — I will honor — reflects the principle *chayav adam l'chaved es ishto yoser m'gufo*, a man must honor his wife more than himself; this is a personal rather than financial obligation. *Ezon* — I will feed — corresponds to *mezonot*. *Afarnes* — I will provide — likely refers to *ksus*, clothing. The phrase *ke'hilchat guvrin yehuda'in* — in the manner of Jewish husbands — signals that he commits to doing all of this properly, without cutting corners.

Later in the document comes the formula *u'me'ayel lavasayichi k'orach kol ar'a* — I will enter upon you in the way of all the earth — which refers to *bi'ah*, the sexual relation, the *mitzvah* of *onah*. The phrase *k'orach kol ar'a* may be read in parallel with *ke'hilchat guvrin yehuda'in* — not merely as a bare description of intercourse, but as a commitment to fulfill this obligation in the proper, respectful manner.

### Listed & Unlisted

The *ketubah* formula thus mentions *mezonot*, *ksus*, and *onah* — sustenance, clothing, and conjugal relations. What is conspicuously absent from the explicit text is *refuah* (the obligation to pay for his wife's medical care) and *kevurah* (burial). These obligations exist, but they are not spelled out in the formula, presumably because they do not require a fixed monetary amount to be stipulated in the document.

Similarly, the husband's corresponding *rights* — his entitlement to her earnings, the *peiros* of her *nichsei milug*, and so forth — are not enumerated in the *ketubah* text. The *ketubah* is primarily a document of his obligations to her, not a ledger of mutual exchanges.

### Fixed Amounts

The *ketubah* does specify the *nedunya* — the dowry — listing what she brings from her father's house and fixing a monetary value for it. It is this fixed value that the estate must return to her in the event of divorce or the husband's death. In practice, rather than enumerating the specific assets — two sheep, ten garments, one camel — the document fixes the amount as a round sum: *hakol kibel alav chasan dena b'me'ah zkukan kesef tzaruf* — the groom accepted upon himself the equivalent of one hundred *zekukim* of refined silver as the value of the *nedunya*.

Beyond the *nedunya*, the *ketubah* also specifies the *tosefes ketubah* — the additional amount the groom undertakes above the base *ikar ketubah*. These two amounts — the fixed *nedunya* and the *tosefes* — together constitute the sum the wife collects at the dissolution of the marriage.

### Zuz to Kesef Tzaruf

The historical background to these numbers deserves explanation. The biblical *ketubah* amount — *ikar ketubah* — is 200 *zuz* for a virgin and 100 for a widow. By the time of the *Rishonim*, however, the *zuz* had become so debased in value that 200 *zuz* offered virtually no protection to the woman. To remedy this, the *Rishonim* effectively replaced 200 *zuz* with 100 *kesef tzaruf* — refined silver coins — a sum of substantially greater real value. They accomplished this through two mechanisms.

First, the *nedunya* was fixed at 100 *kesef tzaruf*, even for a woman who brought nothing to the marriage — the document simply states that as the agreed valuation. Second, the *tosefes ketubah* was standardized at an additional 100 *kesef tzaruf*, making it a boilerplate requirement rather than a voluntary addition. The two amounts together — 100 for the *nedunya* and 100 for the *tosefes* — total 200 *kesef tzaruf*, and the document closes with *sach hakol mas'ayim zekukim kesef tzaruf*, the total being 200 *zekukim* of refined silver, effectively replacing the 200 *zuz* of the biblical standard with a figure of real monetary weight. The combination of a fixed *nedunya* and a fixed *tosefes* into a single total is admittedly an unusual conflation, but the purpose — protecting the wife — is transparent.

The *Kolech* *ketubah* rider, mentioned earlier, represents the most recent significant modification to this framework, though no formal communal fix has been enacted since the *Rishonim*'s standardization roughly six hundred years ago.

## Biblical & Rabbinic

### Rava's Question

Having outlined the structure of the reciprocal obligations, the *Gemara* turns to a fundamental question: which of these obligations are biblical (*m'deoraisa*) and which are rabbinic enactments (*m'derabanan*)? *Rava* raises the question: the earlier *braita* framed the husband's *mezonot* obligation as a rabbinic enactment — the Rabbis established his duty to feed her in exchange for her earnings, implying it was not a pre-existing biblical duty. But the following *braita* appears to treat *mezonot* as biblical. Which is correct?

### Three Torah Obligations

The *braita* invoked is derived from the verse in Exodus describing the case of a man who acquires a female slave with the intention of marrying her. If he subsequently takes a second, free-born wife of higher social standing, the *Torah* warns: *she'erah kesutah ve'onatah lo yigra* — he may not diminish the first wife's *she'er*, *kesut*, and *onah*. The concern is that the husband, preferring his second wife, will treat the first as a second-class wife in every dimension. The *Torah* prohibits this, and from this prohibition we derive the affirmative obligations owed to every wife.

The classic reading, familiar from *Rashi* on the *Chumash*, maps the three terms as follows: *she'erah elu mezonot* — *she'er* (flesh) refers to food, derived from the verse *va'asher achalu she'er ami*, "they ate the flesh of my people," meaning that flesh requires food to sustain it. *Kesutah k'mashma'an* — *kesut* means clothing, simply as the word implies. *Onatah zeh onah ha'amurah ba-Torah* — *onatah* refers to the regular conjugal times, derived from the verse *im ta'aneh benotai* — if you afflict my daughters — where withholding is the *inuy* and the obligation is its positive counterpart.

So according to this first reading, all three — food, clothing, and conjugal relations — are biblical obligations derived from the *Torah*.

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

*Rabbi Eliezer* disagrees with the assignment of terms. He reads *she'erah* not as food but as sexual relations: *she'er* in the *Torah* typically denotes flesh in the sense of bodily closeness — *ish ish el kol she'er besaro lo tikrevu l'galot ervah* — a man shall not draw close to any who is *she'er*, his close flesh, to uncover nakedness. Thus *she'erah* signals the fleshly, sexual relation.

In his reading, then, *she'erah* is *onah*, *kesutah* remains clothing, and *onatah* becomes *mezonot* — sustenance — derived from *vay'anecha vayarchivcha*, God caused Israel to suffer hunger (*inuy*) in the wilderness before feeding them with the *man*. The same root that denotes the withholding of sex also denotes the withholding of food, and from either withholding we derive an obligation to provide.

The conclusion is the same trio — food, clothing, sex — but the words from which each is derived are rearranged.

### Rabbi Eliezer b. Yaakov

*Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* offers a substantially different reading. He treats the verse not as a list of three separate obligations, but as two paired phrases: *she'er kesut* together, and *kesut ve'onah* together. *She'er* functions as an adjective qualifying *kesut*: *le'fum she'erah ten kesutah* — according to her flesh, give her clothing. That is, provide her with clothing appropriate to her body and stage of life. *Shelo yiten lo shel yaldah le'zekenah ve'lo shel zekenah le'yaldah* — do not give an old woman a young woman's garments or a young woman an old woman's garments. Similarly, *kesutah ve'onatah* — according to the season's time, give her clothing: *shelo yiten chadashim biyemot ha'chamah ve'lo shechakim biyemot ha'geshamim* — do not give new, thick garments in summer, nor worn, thin garments in winter.

According to *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*, the entire verse speaks only of clothing, with two specifications: that the clothing be appropriate to her age and that it be appropriate to the season. This is a striking position. It would seem to leave *mezonot* and *onah* entirely without a biblical source, at least from this verse. Several *Rishonim* note that this need not mean those obligations are nonexistent or unimportant, but rather that they understand them differently: the sexual relationship is not merely one item among a list of obligations, but is constitutive of the very identity of marriage. A husband who cannot fulfill this dimension of the marriage is not simply failing one obligation among many — he is undermining what marriage fundamentally is. This has halakhic implications when, for example, a husband is physically incapable of conjugal relations: the question is whether the wife can sue for divorce not merely on the grounds that one obligation is unmet, but on the grounds that there is, in some sense, no marriage at all.

### Kiruv Basar

*Rav Yosef* cites yet another tradition: *she'erah zu kiruv basar* — *she'erah* means the coming-together of flesh. *Shelo yinhag bah minhag Parsiyim she'meshamshin mitosaihen belivushaihen* — he should not conduct himself with her as the Persians do, who have marital relations while clothed. This *braita* uses the exotic figure of the Persians — the *Gemara*'s analogue, apparently, of the various cultural stereotypes every society projects onto others — to make the point that conjugal relations must involve *kiruv basar*, actual bodily union, not merely the act of intercourse while remaining clothed.

This supports a ruling of *Rav Huna*: if a husband declares *ee efshi ela ani be'vigdi vehi be'vigdah* — I refuse to have relations unless we are both clothed — he must give a divorce and pay the *ketubah*. The intimacy of marriage requires nakedness; the *Gemara* assumes that a husband and wife share a bed unclothed. This is why the *Gemara* elsewhere considers whether, when a wife is a *niddah*, it suffices for the couple to wear nightclothes rather than having separate beds — since the assumption is that normally they are unclothed together, the wearing of clothes might itself constitute sufficient *hekker* (marking of separation). The *Gemara* ultimately rejects this and requires separate beds, but the very framing of the question reveals the baseline assumption.

### Halakhic Consensus

The consensus position among the *Rishonim*, notwithstanding *Rashi*'s well-known commentary on the *Chumash*, is that the only obligation that is definitively biblical (*m'deoraisa*) is *onah* — the conjugal relation. *Mezonot* and *ksus* — food and clothing — are rabbinic obligations. This is consistent with the plain language of the first *braita*, which framed *mezonot* as a rabbinic enactment in exchange for her earnings. What *Rashi*'s reading captures is the content of the three obligations, not their legal source; the *halakhic* derivation of which is biblical and which is rabbinic is a separate question.

### Ramban's Framework

The *Ramban*, in his *Torah* commentary, offers a reading that both accommodates the *halakhic* consensus and gives depth to all three terms. If the only biblical obligation is *onah*, and *she'erah* and *kesutah* cannot straightforwardly be read as food and clothing in this context, then what do all three words mean?

The *Ramban* proposes that the verse describes three dimensions of the *sexual* relationship between husband and wife — particularly with reference to the first wife whom the husband might be inclined to treat contemptuously. *She'erah* refers to *kiruv basar* — actual bodily union, the quality of the intimate act itself, not merely its occurrence. The husband may not treat the first wife as a purely instrumental sexual partner while reserving emotional and physical tenderness for the second; even with the first wife, the intimacy must be real. *Kesutah* — "her covering" — the *Ramban* reads as referring to the setting and environment: the marital bed, the linens, the physical context in which intimacy occurs. Relations must not be conducted in a degrading or perfunctory manner, as one might visit a prostitute, but with the full dignity of the marital environment. *Onatah* — "her times" — refers to the regularity and ongoing rhythm of conjugal life: it must occur at appropriate intervals and reflect a sustained connection, not a sporadic or arbitrary one.

On this reading, the three words all speak to *onah*, but they articulate its three necessary dimensions: the quality of bodily union, the dignity of the environment, and the regularity of the relationship. This also explains, the *Ramban* argues, why the *halakhic* conclusion places only *onah* in the biblical category — because all three terms are ultimately about *onah*, not about separate obligations of food and clothing.

One might observe that in the *ketubah* text itself, *onah* appears somewhat separately — *u'me'ayel lavasayichi k'orach kol ar'a* — and the formula *ke'hilchat guvrin yehuda'in* and *k'orach kol ar'a* may both carry the sense of the *Ramban*'s understanding: not merely that the act occurs, but that it is carried out in the proper, dignified, and fully human manner.

## Burial Standards

### Rabbi Yehuda's Minimum

The *Gemara* now returns to the *mishnah*, which addresses the standard for the husband's obligation to bury his wife when she predeceases him. *Rabbi Yehuda* rules that even the poorest man in Israel must provide his wife with a burial that includes no fewer than two flutes (*chalilim*) and a wailer (*mekonenes*) — professional mourners of a minimum standard.

### Family Practice vs. Mini…

The *Gemara* reads this ruling in contrast to the *Tanna Kamma*, who appears to accept a lower standard. The *Gemara* clarifies the precise case of dispute: everyone agrees that the husband must provide for the burial at *at least* the standard to which the wife's family is accustomed. The question is what happens when his family standard is higher than hers. In that case, does he owe her the higher standard?

The *Tanna Kamma* holds that he need only meet her family's customary standard. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that he must provide whatever is the *higher* of the two standards — his or hers.

### Ascent & Non-Descent

This dispute reflects the broader principle of *olah immo ve'einah yoredes immo* — she ascends with him but does not descend with him. That is, if the husband is of higher social or economic standing than his wife, she is entitled to be maintained at *his* standard, not merely her own. Conversely, if he falls into poverty, he cannot reduce her maintenance to below what she was accustomed to before marriage. The principle governs his obligations of food, clothing, and housing during the marriage; the question here is whether it also governs the funeral.

### Living vs. Deceased

The *Gemara* frames this as follows: everyone may agree that *olah immo ve'einah yoredes immo* applies during the couple's shared life — while both are alive, she is maintained at the higher of the two standards. The question is whether it continues to apply *after death*, when there is no longer a shared life to speak of. *Rav Chisda* rules in the name of *Mar Ukva*: *halakha ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — we follow *Rabbi Yehuda*. Even after death, the burial must be conducted at the higher of the husband's and wife's family standards. The principle of *olah immo ve'einah yoredes immo* is not extinguished by her death.

## Absent/Incapacitated Hus…

### Deranged Husband

*Rav Chisda* further rules in the name of *Mar Ukva* regarding a husband who has become a *shoteh* — who is in a state of mental incapacity, unable to manage his own affairs or provide for his household. *Beit Din* intervenes actively: *Beit Din yordin l'nechasav v'zanin u'mefarnesin es ishto u'vanav u'vnosav* — *Beit Din* descends to his property, selling it off as necessary, and uses the proceeds to provide *mezonot* and *parnasah* for his wife, his sons, and his daughters, as well as for a *davar acher* — another matter — to be specified below.

### Conscious Abandonment

This is contrasted with a different *braita*: a man who has gone abroad — who left on his own initiative, consciously, without making arrangements for his family. His wife comes before *Beit Din* demanding *mezonot*. Here too *Beit Din yordin l'nechasav* and sells off property to sustain his wife — but *aval lo vanav u'vnosav*, not his sons and daughters — *v'lo davar acher*, nor for the other matter. The children and the additional obligations are not covered when the husband has consciously abandoned his responsibilities.

### Legal vs. Intent

*Ravina* raises the question to *Rav Ashi*: what is the operative distinction between these two cases? The answer lies in a difference between legal obligation and presumed intent. A husband has a legal obligation to support his wife; he has no such legal obligation to support his children. *Beit Din* can enforce legal obligations against his estate in any case. But when *Beit Din* acts on behalf of a *shoteh* — a man who is incapacitated and not consciously choosing to abandon his family — it acts not merely to discharge legal obligations but also to fulfill what the man himself would presumably have wanted: to care for his children, to provide the additional comforts for his wife that go beyond the strict legal minimum. We assume that a man who has not made a conscious choice to abandon his family would, if he could, continue to care for them fully.

When, however, a man has left consciously and deliberately — even if he may have expected to return — the fact that he did not provide for his children before leaving is read as a communication: he is not attending to those responsibilities. Since he is not legally obligated, *Beit Din* cannot override this. Only the wife's strict legal entitlement can be enforced against his estate.

### Additional Matters

The *davar acher* — the additional matter mentioned in both the case of the *shoteh* and the absent husband — is now identified. *Rav Chisda* says: *ze tachshit* — this refers to jewelry or personal adornments for the wife (or, according to *Rashi*, to perfume — something that is an ongoing personal expense but not within the strict legal category of *ksus*/clothing). *Rav Yosef* says: *tzedaka* — charitable donations that the husband regularly made.

The relationship between these two opinions: if one holds that even *tachshit* is provided for the wife even when the husband has consciously abandoned the family — because *delo nicha lei d'titnaval*, he does not want his wife to be degraded — then *tzedaka* would certainly be provided in the case of incapacity. But if one holds that *tachshit* is provided only in the case of incapacity, not conscious abandonment, then *tzedaka* would certainly not be provided in the case of conscious abandonment: if *Beit Din* will not even provide for his own family, it will surely not make charitable donations on his behalf. *Tzedaka* is provided only in the case of a *shoteh*, not when the husband has voluntarily left.

## Husband Predeceases

### Husband Abroad

*Rabbi Chiya bar Avin* cites *Rav Huna*: if a man has gone abroad and his wife dies during his absence, *Beit Din* again descends to his property and buries her — *lefi kvodo*, according to his status.

### Higher Status Standard

The *Gemara* immediately asks: why *lefi kvodo* — his status — and not *lefi kvodah*, her status? What if her family's standard is higher than his? The *Gemara* corrects: read it as *af lefi kvodah* — even according to her status, meaning the *higher* of the two. This is the application of *olah immo ve'einah yoredes immo* even after death: *Beit Din* uses the estate to bury her at the higher of his and her family standards. As *Rav Chisda* ruled in the name of *Mar Ukva* earlier, *halakha ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — the principle of ascent without descent applies even posthumously.

### Deathbed Refusal

*Rav Matna* addresses a final case. A man on his deathbed declares: *im meta ishti lo tikberuha minichasai* — when my wife dies after I am gone, do not bury her from my estate. Is such a declaration effective?

The *Gemara* initially analyzes the case where the husband is instructing his heirs not to use the estate to bury his wife after he dies. Since the husband's obligation to bury his wife exists only if *she* predeceases *him*, and here *he* is the one dying first, his estate has no such obligation to begin with — the estate passes to the heirs, and the heirs owe her no burial obligation. So the declaration seems redundant; the heirs would not be obligated even without it.

### Community Obligation

The *Gemara* therefore reformulates: the operative case is where a man says, before his own death: *im meis hu lo tikberuhu minichasav* — when I die, do not bury me from my estate. He is telling his heirs to save the estate funds and let the community bear the cost of his funeral. Here, *ein shom'in lo* — we do not listen to him. The heirs may be compelled to use estate funds for his burial, regardless of his expressed wish.

The reason is stated as a principle: *lav kol heimenu sheya'ashir et banav ve'yapil atzmo al hatzibur* — it is not within his power to enrich his sons by transferring his own burial costs to the community. A man cannot, by declaration, disinherit the community of its right not to bear avoidable expenses, and cannot enrich his heirs at communal expense. The burial costs are properly charged to the estate, and the community's obligation arises only when the estate is genuinely insufficient — not when it has been artificially preserved by a deathbed instruction.
