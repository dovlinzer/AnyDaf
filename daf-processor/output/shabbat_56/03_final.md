# Shabbat 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reuven & Bnei Eli

### Opening & Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְאוּבֵן. בְּנֵי עֵלִי. בְּנֵי שְׁמוּאֵל. דָּוִד וּשְׁלֹמֹה. וְיוֹאָשׁ. סִימָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara prefaces the following statements of Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani with a **mnemonic: Reuben, the sons of Eli, the sons of Samuel, David, Solomon, and Josiah.**

Today's learning begins on daf 55b, approximately fifteen lines from the bottom, at the passage that reads: *Reuven, bnei Eli, bnei Shmuel, David, Shlomo, veYoash* — a compact summary of the figures who appear to have sinned, and who will be shown, one by one, to have not sinned in the manner the plain reading of the text suggests. *Yoash* may be the least prominent of the group, but he is included. The *daf* itself is the last of the fifth *perek*.

### Reuven Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר רְאוּבֵן חָטָא אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא טוֹעֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּהְיוּ בְנֵי יַעֲקֹב שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכּוּלָּן שְׁקוּלִים כְּאֶחָד. אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וַיִּשְׁכַּב אֶת בִּלְהָה פִּילֶגֶשׁ אָבִיו״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבִּלְבֵּל מַצָּעוֹ שֶׁל אָבִיו, וּמַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ שָׁכַב עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the sins of some of the significant ancestors of the Jewish people, the Gemara now addresses several additional ancestors. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: Anyone who says that Reuben sinned** with Bilhah **is nothing other than mistaken, as it is stated:** "And it came to pass, when Israel dwelt in that land, that Reuben went and lay with Bilhah his father's concubine; and Israel heard of it. **Now the sons of Jacob were twelve"** (Genesis 35:22). The fact that the Torah stated the number of Jacob's sons at that point in the narrative **teaches that,** even after the incident involving Bilhah, **all** of the brothers **were equal** in righteousness. Apparently, Reuben did not sin. **How** then **do I establish** the meaning of the verse: **"And he lay with Bilhah his father's concubine"?** The plain understanding of the verse indicates sin. **This** verse **teaches that** Reuben **rearranged his father's bed** in protest of Jacob's placement of his bed in the tent of Bilhah and not in the tent of his mother Leah after the death of Rachel. **And the verse ascribes to him** liability for his action **as if he had** actually **lain with** Bilhah.

The case of *Reuven* was treated yesterday, and the *gemara* here simply refers back to that discussion — anyone who says *Reuven* sinned is only making a mistake. Yet it is important to keep in mind that this is not the unanimous position of the *gemara*; the discussion yesterday made clear that the matter is in fact a *machlokes*. That caveat is worth carrying forward as we proceed through the rest of the *daf*.

### R. Shmuel bar Nachmani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר בְּנֵי עֵלִי חָטְאוּ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא טוֹעֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁם שְׁנֵי בְנֵי עֵלִי עִם אֲרוֹן בְּרִית הָאֱלֹהִים חׇפְנִי וּפִנְחָס כֹּהֲנִים לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: Anyone who says that the sons of Eli sinned is nothing other than mistaken, as it is written: "And the two sons of Eli, Hophni and Pinehas, were there priests of the Lord"** (I Samuel 1:3).

The *gemara* continues with the same voice that opened the earlier discussion. *Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol ha'omer bnei Eli chat'u eino ela to'eh* — whoever says that the sons of *Eli* sinned is only making a mistake. The verse states: *vesham shnei vnei Eli Chofni uPhinehas Kohanim LaHashem* — there were the two sons of *Eli*, *Chofni* and *Phinehas*, priests to God. If one continues reading in *Shmuel*, it appears unmistakably that they abused the power of their religious office — using it as an avenue to sleep with women who came to the *Mishkan* to bring their sacrifices. Sadly, that kind of phenomenon has not disappeared from the world.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב: פִּנְחָס לֹא חָטָא. מַקִּישׁ חָפְנִי לְפִנְחָס: מַה פִּנְחָס לֹא חָטָא — אַף חָפְנִי לֹא חָטָא. אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכְּבֻן אֶת הַנָּשִׁים״ — מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁשִּׁהוּ אֶת קִינֵּיהֶן, שֶׁלֹּא הָלְכוּ אֵצֶל בַּעֲלֵיהֶן, מַעֲלֶה עֲלֵיהֶן הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ שְׁכָבוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yonatan **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav, as Rav said: Pinehas did not sin.** And the verse **juxtaposes Hophni to Pinehas; just as Pinehas did not sin, so too Hophni did not sin.** The Gemara asks: **How,** then, **do I establish** the meaning of the verse: "Now Eli was very old, and heard all that his sons did to all Israel; and **how they lay with the women** that assembled at the door of the Tent of Meeting" (I Samuel 2:22), which indicates otherwise? The Gemara answers: **Since** the sons of Eli **delayed** sacrificing **the bird-offerings** of women who had given birth, a pair of doves brought as part of the purification process, and this delay caused the women **not to go to their husbands** in timely fashion, **the verse ascribes to** Hophni and Pinehas liability **as if they had lain with them.** They were guilty of nothing more than negligence and carelessness.

So how does the *gemara* respond to those *pesukim*? It says *savar lah k'Rav* — this view holds like *Rav*. *D'amar Rav: Pinchas lo chata* — as *Rav* said, *Phinehas* did not sin. Once it can be established that *Phinehas* didn't sin, the *gemara* applies a *hekesh*, comparing *Chofni* to *Phinehas*: just as *Phinehas* didn't sin, *Chofni* didn't sin. But then what does one do with the verse *asher yishkvun et hanashim* — that they slept with the women who came to offer? The *gemara* answers: *mitoch sheshiheu et kineihen* — because they delayed bringing the bird offerings, primarily the post-childbirth *korbanot*, that women would bring. Now, technically a woman is permitted to her husband before bringing her sacrifice; the sacrifice determines whether she may enter the *Beit HaMikdash* and eat sacred food, not the *tum'ah* and *taharah* of her marital relationship. Nevertheless, by dilly-dallying and forcing women to wait until the following day, *shelo halchu etzel ba'aleihen* — they could not go home to their husbands. *Ma'aleh aleihem HaKatuv ke'ilu shachvu imahen* — Scripture treats it as if they had slept with these women, because in effect they had inserted themselves between the women and their husbands.

### Lecturer's Concerns

This reinterpretation raises serious concerns that deserve to be named directly. The *gemara* is attempting to paper over what the *pesukim* seem to state quite clearly. *Ein mikra yotze miyedei pshuto* — a verse does not depart from its plain meaning, and many great *rishonim* have noted that one need not take the *gemara*'s homiletical readings as the literal truth of what happened. But beyond that question of *peshat*, there is a deeper issue. Reading the *pesukim* as they stand — that even great religious figures can sin, and that the measure of religious greatness is in part how one deals with sin and *teshuvah* — yields a picture that is honest and attainable. When we paint every Biblical figure as an ideal, we may be holding up a model that is unreachable, and the question of what benefit that provides is genuine. Then again, one might argue that the *gemara* is restoring a certain balance — preserving some sense of these figures as holy, so that we are not left with the equally distorted impression that they are nothing special, that their lapses are no different from our own.

But the more serious concern is this: the overall thrust of these reinterpretations signals that if someone in a position of religious authority appears to have committed a terrible act, we should look for a way to explain it away, because surely a person of such stature could not have done it. That instinct, whatever its intellectual merits, is genuinely dangerous — particularly now, as the abuse of religious authority, including sexual abuse, continues to be a live and painful issue.

One positive dimension does emerge, however, from the *gemara*'s specific reframing of the sin of *Chofni* and *Phinehas* — and it connects to the theme from yesterday's *gemara*, which established that a person who could have protested and did not is held accountable. By saying that delaying a woman's sacrifice is treated as if one had committed adultery with her, the *gemara* establishes a remarkably high standard of accountability for those in positions of religious authority. Even what might seem like a minor sin — a delay, a negligence, a failure to act promptly when a husband and wife are separated — is weighted in the gravest possible terms. *Ma'aleh alav HaKatuv ke'ilu shachvam.* That weight of responsibility on leaders, and the way even small failures can take on enormous moral significance, is the message worth carrying forward.

A student noted that the *navi* itself says *chafetz Hashem l'hamitam* about the sons of *Eli*, and that immediately following, *Eli* is told his family's hold on the *kehunah* will be removed — precisely because *Eli* himself failed to protest. That observation is apt: the focus could just as easily have fallen on *Eli*'s silence, not on reinterpreting his sons' conduct. It is also worth noting that nearly every figure in this *gemara* — *Reuven*, *bnei Eli*, *Shlomo* — attained their position by virtue of birth, as sons of someone important. *David* is the notable exception.

## Bnei Eli: Sacrifice Delay

### Rav: Chofni Sinned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב: פִּנְחָס לֹא חָטָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַאֲחִיָּה בֶן אֲחִטוּב אֲחִי אִי כָבוֹד בֶּן פִּנְחָס בֶּן עֵלִי כֹּהֵן ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״ — אֶפְשָׁר חֵטְא בָּא לְיָדוֹ וְהַכָּתוּב מְיַיחֲסוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now examines **the matter itself** of Rav's statement cited in the course of the previous discussion. **Rav said: Pinehas did not sin, as it is stated: "And Ahijah, the son of Ahitub, Ichabod's brother, the son of Pinehas, the son of Eli, was the Lord's priest** in Shiloh, wearing an ephod" (I Samuel 14:3). **Is it possible that sin came to** Pinehas' **hand and,** nevertheless, **the verse traces the lineage** of his grandson, Ahijah, back **to him?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״יַכְרֵת ה׳ לָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂנָּה עֵר וְעֹנֶה מֵאׇהֳלֵי יַעֲקֹב וּמַגִּישׁ מִנְחָה לַה׳ צְבָאוֹת״. אִם יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא — לֹא יִהְיֶה לוֹ עֵר בַּחֲכָמִים וְלֹא עוֹנֶה בַּתַּלְמִידִים. וְאִם כֹּהֵן הוּא — לֹא יִהְיֶה לוֹ בֵּן מַגִּישׁ מִנְחָה. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: פִּנְחָס לֹא חָטָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Wasn't it was already stated** with regard to those who engage in promiscuous relations: **"The Lord will cut off from the man that does this, him that is awake and him that answers from the tents of Jacob, or any to present an offering to the Lord of hosts"** (Malachi 2:12). The Sages interpreted the verse homiletically: **If** the sinner is **an Israelite, he will not have** among his descendants **one who is** sharp and **awake among the Sages, or** even **one among** their **disciples who can answer** questions. **And if he is a priest, he will not have a son who will present a meal-offering.** If Pinehas had sons and grandsons serving as priests, **conclude from it that Pinehas did not sin.**

The *gemara* now examines the underlying position. *Amar Rav: Pinchas lo chata* — *Rav* asserts that *Phinehas* did not sin. The proof is from the verse: *V'Achiyah ben Achituv achi Ichavod ben Pinchas ben Eli kohen Hashem* — a certain *Achiyah*, who is a grandson of *Pinchas ben Eli*, is identified as *kohen Hashem*, a priest of God. *Efshar chata ba l'yado v'hakatuv meyachaso* — is it possible that *Phinehas* committed this terrible sin and yet the verse would establish a laudable figure's *yichus* back to him? You would not invoke the lineage of a *rasha*.

Furthermore, the verse from *Malachi* specifies the punishment for this sin: *yichrat Hashem la'ish asher ya'asennah er v'oneh me'oholei Yaakov umagish minchah l'Hashem tzvaot* — God will cut off that person. The *gemara* reads *er v'oneh* not as "childless" (*ariri*) but as "presence" — *lo yiheyeh lo er bachachamim v'lo oneh b'talmidim* — he will have no presence among the sages and no echo among the students. And if he is a *kohen*: *lo yiheyeh lo ben magish minchah* — he will have no son who brings a *minchah* to God. Since *Achiyah* was bringing *korbanot* and serving as *kohen*, this curse was clearly not operative, which proves that *Phinehas* did not sin. *Ela lav shema minah: Pinchas lo chata.*

### Delaying Sacrifices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא כְתִיב ״אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכְּבֻן״! — ״יִשְׁכָּבֵן״ כְּתִיב. וְהָכְתִיב: ״אַל בָּנָי כִּי לֹא טוֹבָה הַשְּׁמֻעָה״! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: ״בְּנִי״ כְּתִיב. וְהָכְתִיב ״מַעֲבִרִים״! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: ״מַעֲבִירָם״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, isn't it written: "And how they lay [*yishkevun*]** with the women." The verb *yishkevun* is in the plural, indicating that both sons were guilty. The Gemara answers: It **is written** without a *vav* so that it can be read as ***yishkeven*** in the singular, i.e., how he lay, indicating that only one of them sinned. The Gemara asks further: **Isn't it written** that Eli said: **"No, my sons [*banai*]; for it is not a good report** that I hear; you make the Lord's people to transgress" (I Samuel 2:24). The fact that Eli referred to his sons in the plural indicates that they both sinned. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: It is written** in a manner that can be read as **my son [*beni*]** in the singular. The Gemara raises another question: **Isn't it written: "You make** the Lord's people **to transgress [*ma'avirim*]** in the plural, indicating that both sons were guilty. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** Here too, the word **is written** without a *yod* so that it can be read as: **You cause** the Lord's people **to transgress [*ma'aviram*]** in the singular, indicating that only one of them sinned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב ״בְּנֵי בְלִיָּעַל״! מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לְפִנְחָס לְמַחוֹת לְחׇפְנִי וְלֹא מִיחָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִלּוּ חָטָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises one last challenge: **Isn't it written:** "Now the sons of Eli were **scoundrels;** they knew not the Lord" (I Samuel 2:12), indicating that they were both sinners. The Gemara answers: **Since Pinehas should have protested Hophni's** conduct, **but he did not protest, the verse ascribes to him** liability **as if he** too **had sinned.**

According to *Rav*, then, *Phinehas* did not sin, but *Chofni* did — and what *Chofni* did was the sin literally attributed to him in the text. It is only Rabbi *Shmuel bar Nachmani* who reinterprets both brothers' conduct as the delay of sacrifices rather than actual immorality. *Rav*'s position, by contrast, is not a full whitewash; it preserves real accountability. The citation of that verse in *Malachi* is particularly significant: it does not deny that such sins are possible, it simply demonstrates that *Phinehas* was not among those who committed them. *Chofni*, according to *Rav*, did the very thing the *Torah* attributes to him.

### Textual Issues

The *gemara* then raises several textual challenges. The verb *yishkevun* in the verse is plural — how can *Rav* read it as referring only to *Chofni*? The *gemara* answers: *yishkevan ktiv* — it is written in the singular form, without the plural spelling. Similarly, the verse says *al banai ki lo tovah hashemuah* — "my sons," in the plural — and the *gemara* resolves: *beni ktiv*, it is written as the singular. The word *ma'avirim* presents a more substantial difficulty. The *gemara* reads it as *ma'aviram*, without a *yud*, suggesting a singular reference. But both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* are troubled by this. *Rashi* notes that in all our *sefarim*, the word is indeed spelled *ma'avirim* with a *yud*, and moreover, the *Masora* — which catalogues every instance of a word written deficiently — does not include this word in its lists. *Rashi* therefore appears to have had a different text, and offers an alternative explanation. *Tosafot* addresses the discrepancy directly: *ha-Shas she-lanu cholek al ha-sefarim she-lanu* — our *Talmud* has a different reading than our *Tanakh*. *Tosafot* also notes a parallel case in the *Yerushalmi*, where it reads that *Shimshon* judged Israel for forty years, whereas our *Tanakh* says twenty — illustrating that the *Bavli* and *Yerushalmi* occasionally had *Tanakh* texts that differed from one another and from what we have. Both *Rashi* and *Tosafot*, then, are fully aware of this textual divergence. And curiously, *Rashi* here is defending the integrity of the *Masora* even as there are places in his *al ha-Torah* commentary where his own readings diverge from our received *Masorah*.

### Phinehas Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לְפִנְחָס לְמַחוֹת לְחׇפְנִי וְלֹא מִיחָה, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִלּוּ חָטָא.
>
> **Translation:** Since **Pinehas should have protested his brother** **Chofni's behavior and did not do so, Scripture treats it as if he himself sinned.**

Returning to the main argument: *mitoch shehayah lo l'Pinchas limchot b'Chofni v'lo michah, ma'aleh alav hakatuv k'ilu chata* — since *Phinehas* had the ability to protest his brother *Chofni*'s behavior and did not do so, Scripture treats it as if he himself sinned. According to *Rav*, then, this is not nearly as sweeping an exoneration as one might think. *Chofni* genuinely committed the act the *Torah* attributes to him. *Phinehas* is held culpable not for what he did, but for what he failed to prevent. The citation of *Malachi* serves not to minimize the possibility of such sins, but precisely to establish that *Phinehas* personally was not among those who committed them — while leaving fully intact the principle that such things do and did happen.

## Bnei Shmuel: Neglect

### Sons Not Sinful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי שְׁמוּאֵל חָטְאוּ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא טוֹעֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי (כִּי זָקֵן שְׁמוּאֵל וּבָנָיו לֹא הָלְכוּ) בִּדְרָכָיו״ — בִּדְרָכָיו הוּא דְּלֹא הָלְכוּ, מִיחְטָא נָמֵי לָא חָטְאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **that the sons of Samuel sinned is nothing other than mistaken, as it is stated: "And it came to pass, when Samuel was old** that he made his sons judges over Israel…**And his sons walked not in his ways** but sought after unjust gain, and took bribes, and perverted justice" (I Samuel 8:1–3). By inference: **In his ways they did not walk,** however, **they did not sin either.** They were not the equals of their father, but they were not sinners.

The same formula appears for the sons of *Shmuel*. *Amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol omer bnei Shmuel chat'u eino ela to'eh* — whoever says the sons of *Shmuel* sinned is only making a mistake. The verse says: *vayehi ka'asher zaken Shmuel velo halchu vanav bidrachav* — when *Shmuel* grew old, his sons did not walk in his ways.

### Samuel's Circuit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וַיִּטּוּ אַחֲרֵי הַבָּצַע״ — שֶׁלֹּא עָשׂוּ כְּמַעֲשֵׂה אֲבִיהֶם, שֶׁהָיָה שְׁמוּאֵל הַצַּדִּיק מְחַזֵּר בְּכָל מְקוֹמוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל וְדָן אוֹתָם בְּעָרֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָלַךְ מִדֵּי שָׁנָה בְּשָׁנָה וְסָבַב בֵּית אֵל וְהַגִּלְגָּל וְהַמִּצְפָּה וְשָׁפַט אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וְהֵם לֹא עָשׂוּ כֵּן, אֶלָּא יָשְׁבוּ בְּעָרֵיהֶם כְּדֵי לְהַרְבּוֹת שָׂכָר לְחַזָּנֵיהֶן וּלְסוֹפְרֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **However, how** then **do I establish** the meaning of the verse: **"And they sought after unjust gain,"** indicating that they were sinners? It means **that they did not conduct** themselves **in accordance with the actions of their father. As Samuel the righteous would travel to all places** where the people of **Israel** were located **and** sit in **judgment in their towns, as it is stated: "And he went from year to year in circuit from Beth-El, and Gilgal, and Mitzpa, and judged Israel** in all those places" (I Samuel 7:16). **And,** however, **they did not do so** and travel from place to place. **Rather, they sat in their** own **cities in order to enhance the fees** collected **by their attendants and scribes.** Therefore, the verse ascribes to them liability as if they sinned by seeking ill-gotten gains and bribes.

The *gemara* reads this precisely: *bidrachav hu de'lo halchu* — it was only in his specific *ways* that they did not follow him. But from that alone one could not say they actually sinned. They simply did not attain his level. The verse would have been a harsher indictment had it said they sinned outright. So what then is the meaning of the verse's end, *vayitu acharei habatza* — that they went after profit? The *gemara* explains: *shelo asu kema'aseh avihem*. Their father *Shmuel*, the righteous one, would *mechazer bechol mekomot Yisrael* — he would travel a circuit through all of Israel, *vedan otam be'areihem* — judging people in their own cities, as the verse makes explicit: he went each year to *Beit El*, *Gilgal*, and *Mitzpah*, and judged Israel throughout. His sons did not do this.

### Gain Reframed

Instead, *ela yasvu be'areihem* — they sat in their cities and made the people come to them. The motivation the *gemara* identifies is: *kedei leharbot sachar lechazaneihem ulesofreichem* — to increase income for their attendants and their scribes. If you travel from city to city, you rely on local personnel. But if everyone comes to you, you can sustain your own entourage — the *chazanim* who summon litigants, the *soferim* who record proceedings. This is how the word *batza* — profit — applies without amounting to outright bribery. Their interest in sustaining their patronage network led them to prioritize convenience over justice. The *gemara* here is making a pointed observation that incompetence and self-interest, even without explicit corruption, produce the same injustice as a bribe. The verse does not let them off the hook merely because what they did was subtle. And indeed, the continuation of the *pasuk* — that they perverted judgment — reflects exactly this: once you are concerned with your own financial interests, you will compromise your standards, consciously or otherwise.

### Tannaic Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי. ״וַיִּטּוּ אַחֲרֵי הַבָּצַע״, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר חֶלְקָם שָׁאֲלוּ בְּפִיהֶם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְלַאי הִטִּילוּ עַל בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: קוּפָּה יְתֵירָה שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר נָטְלוּ בִּזְרוֹעַ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מַתָּנוֹת נָטְלוּ בִּזְרוֹעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this matter is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** As it was taught in a *baraita* that the verse states: **"And they sought after unjust gain." Rabbi Meir says:** This means that **they vocally demanded their portions** of the tithe due them as Levites, abusing their position to the detriment of other Levites. **Rabbi Yehuda says: They imposed** upon local **homeowners** to sell their **merchandise** and support them. **Rabbi Akiva says: They took an extra basket of tithes,** beyond that which was their due, **by force. Rabbi Yosei says: They took** only **the gifts** due them; however, they took them **by force.** They acted improperly, as a Levite is required to wait until he is given his gifts and may not take them.

A *braita* then introduces a *machlokes tana'im* about the precise nature of their conduct. *Rabbi Meir* says they demanded *ma'aser* for themselves by name: as *Levi'im*, they would approach a person and say they wanted their *ma'aser* — which the person was technically entitled to direct as they chose, but could hardly refuse when the judge himself was asking. *Rabbi Yehuda* says they placed their money with homeowners to invest on their behalf — using their position to obtain favorable investment arrangements, and obviously affecting their objectivity when those same people came before them in judgment. *Rabbi Akiva* says they forcibly took an extra box of *ma'aser* beyond what was due them. *Rabbi Yosi* says they took their gifts by force. These positions shade into one another — variations on the theme of leveraging authority to obtain financial benefit — but notably, not one of these *tanna'im* describes outright bribery. Even at the *tanna'im*'s most critical, the conduct was rationalized as lying within the envelope of their formal entitlements, abused rather than simply stolen. Yet the *gemara* refuses to accept that rationalization: an abuse of authority that serves one's financial interests, however dressed up, is precisely what *vayitu acharei habatza* condemns.

## David & Bathsheba

### Bathsheba Not Adultery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר דָּוִד חָטָא אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא טוֹעֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי דָּוִד לְכׇל דְּרָכָיו מַשְׂכִּיל וַה׳ עִמּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. אֶפְשָׁר חֵטְא בָּא לְיָדוֹ וּשְׁכִינָה עִמּוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: Anyone who says that David sinned** with Bathsheba **is nothing other than mistaken, as it is stated: "And David succeeded in all his ways; and the Lord was with him"** (I Samuel 18:14). **Is it possible that sin came to his hand and** nevertheless **the Divine Presence was with him?**

*Amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol ha'omer David chata eino ela to'eh* — whoever says *David* sinned is only making a mistake. The proof text: *vayehi David lechol drachav maskil vaHashem imo* — in all his ways *David* was wise and God was with him. *Efshar cheit ba l'yado u'Shchina imo* — is it possible that he committed such a sin and yet the *Shechina* remained with him?

This is not the only sin attributed to *David* — there was also the census — and the *gemara* will address that shortly. The focus here is specifically on the claim that the relationship with *Bat Sheva* constituted adultery. *Ela ma ani mekayem: madua bazita et dvar Hashem la'asot hara* — what does one do with the *pasuk* that says, "Why did you denigrate the word of God to do this evil?" The *gemara*'s answer: *shebikesh la'asot v'lo asa* — he intended to do it, but he did not technically commit the sin itself. He slept with *Bat Sheva*, but it was not, in the legal sense, adultery. How that is possible requires explanation, and the *gemara* will now provide it.

Before proceeding, a structural observation: as with *Reuven*, *bnei Eli*, and *bnei Shmuel*, this is not a unanimous position. *Rav*, who said that *Phinehas* was innocent but *Chofni* was not, is a consistently more critical voice throughout this *daf* — and, as we will see, he pushes back here as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״מַדּוּעַ בָּזִיתָ אֶת דְּבַר ה׳ לַעֲשׂוֹת הָרַע״ — שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת וְלֹא עָשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **However, how** then **do I establish** the meaning of the rebuke of the prophet Nathan: **"Why have you despised the word of the Lord, to do that which is evil** in My sight? Uriah the Hittite you have smitten with the sword, and his wife you have taken to be your wife, and him you have slain with the sword of the children of Ammon" (II Samuel 12:9), indicating that David sinned? The Gemara answers: David **sought to do** evil and have relations with Bathsheba while she was still married to Uriah **but did not do** so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: רַבִּי דְּאָתֵי מִדָּוִד מְהַפֵּךְ וְדָרֵישׁ בִּזְכוּתֵיהּ דְּדָוִד. ״מַדּוּעַ בָּזִיתָ אֶת דְּבַר ה׳ לַעֲשׂוֹת הָרַע״ — רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מְשׁוּנָּה רָעָה זוֹ מִכׇּל רָעוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁכָּל רָעוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה כְּתִיב בְּהוּ ״וַיַּעַשׂ״, וְכָאן כְּתִיב ״לַעֲשׂוֹת״ — שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת וְלֹא עָשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who descends from** the house of **David, seeks to teach** the verse **in favor of David.** With regard to that which is written: **"Why have you despised the commandment of the Lord to do evil," Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: This evil** mentioned with regard to David **is different from all** other **evils in the Torah; as with regard to all** other **evils in the Torah, it is written: And he did** evil, **and here it is written: To do evil.** This unique phrase indicates that David **sought to do** evil **but did not** actually **do** so. His intentions were improper; however, his actions were proper.

*Rebbi*, who was a descendant of *David* and is identified as trying to defend him, offers a reading of the word *la'asot*. He notes that wherever else Scripture attributes evil to the kings of Israel, it uses the completed form: *vaya'as hara b'einei Hashem* — he did evil. Here the text uses the infinitive *la'asot* — "to do." *Mishunah ra zo mikol ra'ot sheba'Torah* — this evil is different from all other evils in the *Torah*. *Shebikesh la'asot v'lo asa* — he desired to do it but did not complete the act as a technical violation.

### Get & Conditional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ לָקַחְתָּ לְּךָ לְאִשָּׁה״ — לִיקּוּחִין יֵשׁ לְךָ בָּהּ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הַיּוֹצֵא לְמִלְחֶמֶת בֵּית דָּוִד, כּוֹתֵב גֵּט כְּרִיתוּת לְאִשְׁתּוֹ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵת עֲשֶׂרֶת חֲרִיצֵי הֶחָלָב הָאֵלֶּה תָּבִיא לְשַׂר הָאָלֶף וְאֶת אַחֶיךָ תִּפְקֹד לְשָׁלוֹם וְאֶת עֲרֻבָּתָם תִּקָּח״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that which is written: **"And his wife you have taken to be your wife";** it means that **you have** rights of **marriage with her,** as by law Bathsheba was already divorced from Uriah. **As Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: Anyone who goes to a war** waged by the royal **house of David writes a** conditional **bill of divorce to his wife.** That was done to prevent a situation in which the soldier's wife would be unable to remarry because the soldier did not return from battle and there were no witnesses to his fate. The conditional bill of divorce accorded her the status of a divorcee and freed her to remarry. **As it is stated: "And carry these ten cheeses to the captain of their thousand, and to your brothers bring greetings and take their pledge [*arubatam*]**" (I Samuel 17:18).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״עֲרֻבָּתָם״? — תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: דְּבָרִים הַמְעוֹרָבִים בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the meaning of ***arubatam*? Rav Yosef taught:** It refers to **matters that are shared [*hame'oravim*] between him,** the husband, **and her,** the wife, i.e., marriage. The verse should be read: Take the bill of divorce that determines the status of the relationship between husband and wife. As, apparently, it was customary for men at war to send their wives a conditional divorce, since Uriah later died, Bathsheba retroactively assumed divorced status from the time that he set out to war. She was not forbidden to David.

The *gemara* now explains how sleeping with *Bat Sheva* was not adultery. *D'amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol hayotzei lemilchemet Beit David kotev get kritut l'ishto* — anyone who went out to war in the time of *David* wrote a *get kritut*, a bill of divorce, for his wife. The reason: a soldier going to war might not return, and if no one knew whether he was dead or alive, his wife would be an *agunah*. To prevent this, soldiers would give a *get* in advance.

The proof text is drawn from when *Yishai* sends *David* to his brothers at the front: *ve'et aruvatam tikach* — take their *aruvah*. *Tani Rav Yosef: devarim hame'uravim beino leveina* — something that intertwines him to her, meaning their bond of marriage. Take the *gittin* for their wives — because they had left in such haste they had forgotten to give them before departing.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* debate the nature of this *get*. *Tosafot* holds it was given unconditionally, in which case *Bat Sheva* was fully free the moment *Uriah* left for war, with no conditions attached. *Rashi* holds it was a conditional *get* — effective only upon certain circumstances — and *Tosafot* raises the difficulty that if the condition was not yet fulfilled when *David* slept with *Bat Sheva*, the technical *issur* might still apply. A student noted that this connects to contemporary discussions about conditional marriage or conditional *gittin* as solutions to the *agunah* problem — arrangements in which a *get* would be given now or a *shaliach* appointed, to take effect under defined conditions. These are indeed proposals that have been raised, though they differ from the precise mechanism described here.

### Uriah: Mored Malchus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֹתוֹ הָרַגְתָּ בְּחֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן״ — מַה חֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן אִי אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו, אַף אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי אִי אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו. מַאי טַעְמָא — מוֹרֵד בַּמַּלְכוּת הֲוָה. דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וַאדֹנִי יוֹאָב וְעַבְדֵי אֲדֹנִי עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה חֹנִים״. וְהָכְתִיב: ״אֵת אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי הִכִּיתָ בַחֶרֶב״ — שֶׁהָיָה לְךָ לְדוּנוֹ בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין וְלֹא דַּנְתָּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that which is written: **"And him you have slain with the sword of the children of Ammon,"** it means: **Just as you are not punished for** soldiers killed by **the sword of the children of Ammon** in the course of the war, **so too you are not punished for** the death of **Uriah the Hittite.** **What is the reason** that David was not liable for the death of Uriah? Because Uriah **was a traitor against the throne. As he said to** David: **"And my lord Joab and the servants of my lord are encamped in the open fields"** (II Samuel 11:11). In the presence of the king, one may not refer to another as his lord. Doing so is treason. That which is written: **"Uriah the Hittite you have smitten with the sword,"** means **that you could have judged him before the Sanhedrin** as one guilty of treason against the throne, **and you did not judge** him in that manner.

Having addressed the question of *Bat Sheva*, the *gemara* turns to the killing of *Uriah*. *Et Uriah HaChitti hikita va'cherev* — you smote him with the sword. But *she'haya lecha ladun b'Sanhedrin v'lo danto* — your sin was that you should have brought him before the *Sanhedrin* and you did not. He deserved to die — the *gemara* will establish this momentarily — but the manner of his death was extra-legal.

*V'et ishto lakachta lecha l'isha — shelikuchin yesh lecha ba* — and you took his wife as your wife, but marriage to her was in fact binding, because of the *get*.

Why did *Uriah* deserve to die? *Mored bemalchus hava* — he was a rebel against the king. The evidence: *Uriah* said to *David*, *adoni Yoav ve'avdei adoni al pnei hasadeh chonim* — my master *Yoav* and the servants of my master are encamped in the field; how should I go home to my wife? *Rashi* explains that *Uriah*'s reference to *Yoav* as "my master" in the very presence of the king demonstrates that his primary loyalty was to *Yoav*, not to *David* — an act of subordination that constituted rebellion. *Tosafot* explains it differently: *David* instructed him to go home to his wife, and *Uriah* flatly refused. Defiance of a direct royal command is *mored bemalchus* in itself.

The principle of *mored bemalchus* is itself derived elsewhere in the *gemara* from *Sefer Yehoshua*, where the people say to *Yehoshua* that anyone who disobeys his command shall be put to death — a principle the *gemara* extrapolates not from divine legislation but from the consent of the people. What is notable here is that the *gemara* holds *David* accountable for not having processed *Uriah*'s execution through the *Sanhedrin*. One might have thought that *mored bemalchus* is by definition extra-legal — that royal authority operates independently of the court system. *Tosafot* addresses this directly: *kashya le'Ri*, how can the *gemara* fault *David* for not bringing this to *Sanhedrin*, given that elsewhere the *gemara* says *mored bemalchus lo ba'ei midainei* — rebellion against the king requires no formal adjudication? *Tosafot* answers: *v'yesh lomar d'bari tzarich ladun v'lada'at im haya mored bemalchus* — one does in fact need to go to court to determine and prove that the person was indeed a *mored bemalchus*. The king cannot act entirely outside any legal framework; the factual determination must be made through a judicial process, even if the full procedural protections of a capital case are not required. That is where *David* was at fault. The question of how much a sovereign may act extra-legally — whether even *mored bemalchus* requires some adjudicative process — is, as one might note, not only a historical question.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: כִּי מְעַיְּינַתְּ בֵּיהּ בְּדָוִד לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ בֵּיהּ בַּר מִדְּאוּרִיָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״רַק בִּדְבַר אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: When you analyze** the matter of **David, no** sin that he committed **is found in his** lifetime, **except for that** involving **Uriah. As it is written**: "Because David did that which was right in the eyes of the Lord, and turned not aside from any thing that He commanded him all the days of his life, **save only in the matter of Uriah the Hittite"** (I Kings 15:5).

Having laid out the case, the *gemara* cites *Rav*'s summary: *amar Rav ki me'aynat bei b'David, lo meshkachat lei bar mide'Uriya* — when you examine the story of *David* closely, you will not find any sin other than the matter of *Uriah*. The verse itself says *rak bidvar Uriya HaChitti* — only in the matter of *Uriah HaChitti*. This is *Rav*'s most sweeping formulation about *David* — not only excusing the *Bat Sheva* episode as not technically adultery, but asserting there are no other sins whatsoever. *Tosafot* notes the difficulty: what about the census? *Tosafot* answers: *v'yesh lomar d'lo chashiv lei d'lo havi kol kach avon gadol* — it can be said that the census did not rise to the level of a grave enough sin to be counted here.

### Lashon Hara & Tziva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי קַשִּׁישָׁא רָמֵי דְּרַב אַדְּרַב: מִי אָמַר רַב הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב: קִיבֵּל דָּוִד לָשׁוֹן הָרָע. קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye the Elder raised a contradiction between** one statement **of Rav and** another statement **of Rav: Did Rav** actually **say this? Didn't Rav say: David accepted a** slanderous **report?** Just as it is prohibited to relate a slanderous report, it is similarly prohibited to accept it. This contradiction remains unresolved, and it is **difficult.**

Then *Abaye Kashisha* — the elder *Abaye*, presumably not the standard *Abaye* — points out a contradiction in *Rav*'s own statements: *rami d'Rav a'Rav* — he shows that *Rav* contradicts himself. *Ve'ha'amar Rav: kibel David lashon hara* — but *Rav* himself said that *David* accepted *lashon hara*! The *gemara* simply notes: *kashya*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. רַב אָמַר: קִיבֵּל דָּוִד לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ הַמֶּלֶךְ אֵיפוֹא הוּא וַיֹּאמֶר צִיבָא אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ הִנֵּה הוּא בֵּית מָכִיר בֶּן עַמִּיאֵל בְּלֹא דְבָר״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיִּקָּחֵהוּ מִבֵּית מָכִיר בֶּן עַמִּיאֵל מִלֹּא דְבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now examines **the matter itself** with regard to Rav's statement cited in the course of the previous discussion. **Rav said: David accepted a slanderous report, as it is written** with regard to David's search for a surviving son of Jonathan: **"And the king said to him,** to Ziba, Saul's slave: **Where is he? And Ziba said to the king: Behold, he is in the house of Machir, the son of Ammiel, in Lo-Devar [*belo devar*]"** (II Samuel 9:4). Ziba indicated to David that Jonathan's son was inconsequential, lacking any matter [*lo devar*] of Torah. **And it is written: "Then King** David **sent, and fetched him out of the house of Machir, the son of Ammiel, from Lo-Devar [*milo devar*]"** (II Samuel 9:5). That verse can be read that after sending for him, David found him filled with matters [*melo devar*] of Torah.

What, then, is the story of *David* and *lashon hara*? The *gemara* examines it. *Rav amar kibel David lashon hara* — *David* accepted slander. The context is the well-known episode involving *Tziva*, the servant of the house of *Shaul*, and *Mefivoshet*, the son of *Yehonatan*. When *David* came to power, he sought out any remaining members of *Shaul*'s house in order to treat them well. *Tziva* informed him that *Yehonatan* had a son, *Mefivoshet*, and *David* brought him in and granted him all the property of *Shaul*'s house.

Later, as *David* was fleeing *Avshalom*, *Tziva* appeared carrying donkeys laden with food and provisions. *David* asked: *v'ayo ben adonecha* — where is your master's son? *Tziva* replied that *Mefivoshet* was waiting in *Yerushalayim*, hoping that the chaos of *Avshalom*'s rebellion would allow him to reclaim his grandfather *Shaul*'s kingdom. *David* immediately transferred all of *Mefivoshet*'s property to *Tziva* — he believed the slander.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי חַזְיֵיהּ דְּשַׁקָּרָא הוּא, כִּי הֲדַר אַלְשֵׁין עִילָּוֵיהּ מַאי טַעְמָא קַבֵּיל מִינֵּיהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ אֶל צִיבָא אַיֵּה בֶּן אֲדוֹנֶיךָ וַיֹּאמֶר צִיבָא אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ הִנֵּה הוּא יוֹשֵׁב בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם וְגוֹ׳״. וּמְנָא לַן דְּקַבֵּיל מִינֵּיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ הִנֵּה לְךָ כֹּל אֲשֶׁר לִמְפִיבוֹשֶׁת וַיֹּאמֶר צִיבָא הִשְׁתַּחֲוֵיתִי אֶמְצָא חֵן בְּעֵינֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Now, after** David **saw that** Ziba **was a liar, when** Ziba **once again slandered** Jonathan's son, Mephibosheth, **why did David accept** his report? **As it is written** that when David fled from Absalom, he met Ziba: **"And the king said: And where is your master's son? And Ziba said to the king: Behold, he is staying in Jerusalem,** as he said: Today shall the house of Israel restore to me the kingdom of my father" (II Samuel 16:3). **And from where do we** derive **that David accepted** Ziba's slanderous report? **As it is written: "Then said the king** to Ziba: **Behold, all that belongs to Mephibosheth is yours. And Ziba said: I humbly beseech you that I may find favor in your sight,** my lord, **O king**" (II Samuel 16:4).

The *gemara* points out: *chazyei d'shakra hu* — *David* had already seen that *Tziva* was a liar. How? Because *Tziva* had described *Mefivoshet* as being *b'lo davar* — without word — meaning, the *gemara* reads, that he had no *Torah*. Yet when *David* went to retrieve him, he found *Mefivoshet* to be *melo davar* — filled with words, a great *talmid chacham*. Other *gemarot* (notably in *Berachot*) describe *Mefivoshet* as *David*'s teacher, one who would challenge and correct him in *halacha*. So *Tziva*'s earlier characterization was demonstrably false. *Heikhi hadar alshin ilavei* — why then, when *Tziva* came back with the slander about *Mefivoshet* waiting to reclaim the throne, did *David* accept it? *Mai ta'ama kibel mineih* — that is the question. And the answer, according to *Rav*, is that he had no good reason to — he simply believed the slander.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לֹא קִיבֵּל דָּוִד לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, דְּבָרִים הַנִּיכָּרִים חֲזָא בֵּיהּ. דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמְפִיבוֹשֶׁת בֶּן שָׁאוּל יָרַד לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ וְלֹא עָשָׂה רַגְלָיו וְלֹא עָשָׂה שְׂפָמוֹ וְאֶת בְּגָדָיו לֹא כִבֵּס וְגוֹ׳״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי כִּי בָא יְרוּשָׁלִַם לִקְרַאת הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ הַמֶּלֶךְ לָמָּה לֹא הָלַכְתָּ עִמִּי מְפִיבוֹשֶׁת. וַיֹּאמַר אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ עַבְדִּי רִמָּנִי כִּי אָמַר עַבְדְּךָ אֶחְבְּשָׁה לִּי הַחֲמוֹר וְאֶרְכַּב עָלֶיהָ וְאֵלֵךְ אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ כִּי פִסֵּחַ עַבְדְּךָ. וַיְרַגֵּל בְּעַבְדְּךָ אֶל אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ וַאדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ כְּמַלְאַךְ הָאֱלֹהִים וַעֲשֵׂה הַטּוֹב בְּעֵינֶיךָ״. ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ הַמֶּלֶךְ לָמָּה תְּדַבֵּר עוֹד דְּבָרֶיךָ אָמַרְתִּי אַתָּה וְצִיבָא תַּחְלְקוּ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה. וַיֹּאמֶר מְפִיבֹשֶׁת אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ גַּם אֶת הַכֹּל יִקָּח אַחֲרֵי אֲשֶׁר בָּא אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ בְּשָׁלוֹם אֶל בֵּיתוֹ״. אָמַר לוֹ: אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי ״מָתַי תָּבֹא בְּשָׁלוֹם״, וְאַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה לִי כָּךְ? לֹא עָלֶיךָ יֵשׁ לִי תַּרְעוֹמוֹת אֶלָּא עַל מִי שֶׁהֱבִיאֲךָ בְּשָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: David did not accept** Ziba's **slanderous report** without substantiation. Rather, **he** himself **saw conspicuous matters in** Mephibosheth that indicated that Ziba was right. **As it is written: "And Mephibosheth, the son of Saul, came down to meet the king, and he had neither dressed his feet, nor trimmed his beard, nor washed his clothes** from the day the king departed until the day he came back in peace" (II Samuel 19:25). David thought that he was mourning the fact that he had returned in peace. **And it is written: "And it came to pass, when he came to Jerusalem to meet the king, and the king said to him: Why did you not go with me, Mephibosheth? And he answered: My lord, O king, my servant deceived me; for your servant said: I will saddle me a donkey, and I will ride on it, and go to the king; because lame is your servant. And he slandered your servant to my lord the king; but my lord the king is like an angel of God: Do therefore what is good in your eyes"** (II Samuel 19:26–28). **"And the king said to him: Why do you speak any more of your matters? I have said: You and Ziba shall divide the estate. And Mephibosheth said to the king: Let him even take all, seeing that my lord the king is come back in peace to his own house"** (II Samuel 19:30–31). Mephibosheth **said to** David as follows: **I** had hoped for your return, **saying: When will he come in peace, and** yet **you do this to me,** giving Ziba half of my estate? **It is not against you that I have grievances, but against He who brought you back in peace.** Mephibosheth's own statement substantiates Ziba's report about him.

*Rav* and *Shmuel* disagree on this point. *Rav* holds that *David* accepted *lashon hara* without adequate basis — he knew *Tziva* had lied before, yet believed him again. *Shmuel*, however, says *lo kibel David lashon hara* — *David* did not accept *lashon hara*. *Devarim hanikarin chaza bei* — he saw supporting evidence that suggested *Mefivoshet* was not entirely in his corner.

What was that evidence? When *David* returned to power after *Avshalom*'s defeat, *Mefivoshet* came out to meet him in a noticeably disheveled state — *lo asa raglav v'lo asa sfamo v'et begadav lo kibes* — he had not washed his feet, had not trimmed his mustache, had not laundered his clothes. *David* asked: *lama lo halachta imi Mefivoshet* — why did you not come with me when I was fleeing? *Mefivoshet* replied that *Tziva* had deceived him — *avdi rimani* — he had promised to saddle the donkeys so *Mefivoshet* could ride, since he was lame, but then stole the donkeys and left, and proceeded to slander him to the king.

*Mefivoshet* then said: *adoni ha'melech kema'lach ha'elohim, va'aseh hatov b'einecha* — my master the king is like an angel of God, do as you see fit. *David* responded not with a full reversal, but with a split: *amarti atah v'Tziva techalku et hasadeh* — you and *Tziva* divide the field. *Mefivoshet* replied: *gam et hakol yikach, acharei asher ba adoni ha'melech b'shalom el beito* — let him take everything, since my master the king has returned home in peace.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבֶן יְהוֹנָתָן מְרִיב בָּעַל״, וְכִי ״מְרִיב בַּעַל״ שְׁמוֹ? וַהֲלֹא מְפִיבֹשֶׁת שְׁמוֹ! אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁעָשָׂה מְרִיבָה עִם בְּעָלָיו. יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: נַצָּא בַּר נַצָּא. ״נַצָּא״ — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. ״בַּר נַצָּא״ — דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּבֹא שָׁאוּל עַד עִיר עֲמָלֵק וַיָּרֶב בַּנָּחַל״, אָמַר רַבִּי מָנִי: עַל עִסְקֵי נַחַל.
>
> **Translation:** **This is what is written: "And the son of Jonathan was Meriv-Baal"** (I Chronicles 8:34). The Gemara asks: **And was Meriv-Baal his name? Wasn't his name Mephibosheth? However, since he entered into a quarrel [*meriva*] with his Master [*ba'al*],** i.e., God, and complained about God having saved David, **a Divine Voice emerged and said to him: Quarrelsome** one, **the son of a quarrelsome** one; you are just like your father, Saul. The Gemara explains: **Quarrelsome** one; **that which we** just **said** that Mephibosheth complained to God about His salvation of David. **The son of a quarrelsome** one; **as it is written: "And Saul came to a city of Amalek and quarreled in the valley"** (I Samuel 15:5). And **Rabbi Mani said:** Saul quarreled with God **with regard to matters of the valley,** saying: For the murder of even a single person, there is a commandment to break the neck of a heifer in a valley to atone for the crime (see Deuteronomy 21:1–9); why then must all these Amalekites be killed?

The *gemara*, however, reads *Mefivoshet*'s words quite differently from what they seem on the surface. *Mefivoshet* was not nobly conceding — he was expressing anger at God. *Amar lo: ani amarti matai tavo b'shalom* — I was so worried about when you would return safely. *V'atah oseh li kach* — and this is what I get in return, you split the field? *Lo alecha yesh li taromet, ela al mi sheheviacha b'shalom* — my complaint is not against you, it is against the One who brought you back in peace. If this is what peace brings, I could do without it. The *gemara* connects this to the later verse that calls him *Meriv Baal* — contentious one: *mitoch she'asah merivah im be'alav*, he made a quarrel with his Master, meaning God. *Yatzeta bat kol v'amrah: natza bar natza* — a heavenly voice declared: contentious one, son of a contentious one. And *bar natza* refers back to *Shaul*, of whom the verse says *vayariv banachal* — he waged contention, which the *gemara* in *Yoma* connects to *Shaul*'s famous argument with God over why he should have to destroy all of *Amalek* including its innocents.

According to *Shmuel*, then, it was only after this exchange that *David*'s decision to divide the property was finalized with full legal force — since a king fleeing a rebellion who gives away assets does not do so with the full force of royal authority. By the time he returned to his throne and observed *Mefivoshet*'s disheveled state and then heard his veiled complaint against God, *David* had supporting evidence for doubting him. The decision was not made on the basis of slander alone.

### Kingdom Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר דָּוִד לִמְפִיבֹשֶׁת: אַתָּה וְצִיבָא תַּחְלְקוּ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה, יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לוֹ: רְחַבְעָם וְיָרָבְעָם יַחְלְקוּ אֶת הַמְּלוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** To the matter at hand: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: When David said to Mephibosheth: You and Ziba shall divide the estate, a Divine Voice emerged and said to him: Rehoboam and Jeroboam shall divide the kingdom.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אִילְמָלֵי לֹא קִיבֵּל דָּוִד לָשׁוֹן הָרָע לֹא נֶחְלְקָה מַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד, וְלֹא עָבְדוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְלֹא גָּלִינוּ מֵאַרְצֵנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav** said: **Had David not accepted** Ziba's **slanderous report** about Mephibosheth, **the kingdom of the house of David would not have been divided, Israel would not have worshipped idols** because of Jeroboam, **and we would not have been exiled from our land.**

*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* draws the consequence of *David*'s acceptance of slander: *besha'ah she'amar David leMefivoshet atah v'Tziva techalku et hasadeh, yatzah bat kol v'amrah: Rechavam v'Yerovam yechaleku et hamalchus* — at the moment *David* said "you and *Tziva* divide the field," a heavenly voice declared that *Rechavam* and *Yerovam* would divide the kingdom. You divided the property of a master between the master and his servant; so too your kingdom will be divided between a king and his servant, between *Rechavam* and *Yerovam*. The parallel to *Shaul* is striking: *Shaul* returned from *Amalek* and *Shmuel* rent his garment, declaring that God had torn the kingship away. Here *David*, by accepting *lashon hara*, triggers the eventual tearing of his own dynasty's kingdom.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: ilmalei lo kibel David lashon hara, lo nechleku malchus Beit David* — had *David* not accepted the slander, the kingdom of the house of *David* would never have been divided. *V'lo avdu Yisrael avodah zarah* — and Israel would not have worshipped *avodah zarah*, because the split of the kingdom led *Yerovam* to erect the golden calves in his territory. *V'lo galinu me'artzeinu* — and we would never have gone into *galus*. All of this — the split of the kingdom, the *avodah zarah*, the exile itself — flows from *lashon hara*. This is as powerful a statement about the consequences of believing slander as the *gemara* ever makes.

## Solomon & Idolatry

### Solomon Not Sinful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר שְׁלֹמֹה חָטָא אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא טוֹעֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא הָיָה לְבָבוֹ שָׁלֵם עִם ה׳ אֱלֹהָיו כִּלְבַב דָּוִד אָבִיו״ — כִּלְבַב דָּוִד אָבִיו הוּא דְּלָא הֲוָה, מִיחְטָא נָמֵי לָא חֲטָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: Anyone who says that** King **Solomon sinned is nothing other than mistaken, as it is stated: "And his heart was not perfect with the Lord his God, as was the heart of David, his father"** (I Kings 11:4). By inference: Solomon's heart **was not equal to the heart of David, his father;** however, **he also did not sin.**

*Amar Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol ha'omer Shlomo chata eino ela to'eh* — whoever says *Shlomo* sinned is only making a mistake. The verse: *velo hayah levavo shaleim im Hashem Elokav kilvav David aviv* — his heart was not whole with God as the heart of *David* his father. The *gemara* reads this precisely: it was only in comparison to *David* that his heart fell short. *Michta nami lo chata* — but he did not actually sin. Then what does one do with *vayehi l'eit ziknat Shlomo nashav hitu et levavo* — that his wives caused his heart to go astray, and they worshipped *avodah zarah*?

### Wives Led Astray

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וַיְהִי לְעֵת זִקְנַת שְׁלֹמֹה נָשָׁיו הִטּוּ אֶת לְבָבוֹ״ — הַהִיא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּרַבִּי נָתָן רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וַיְהִי לְעֵת זִקְנַת שְׁלֹמֹה נָשָׁיו הִטּוּ אֶת לְבָבוֹ״, וְהָכְתִיב: ״כִּלְבַב דָּוִד אָבִיו״ — כִּלְבַב דָּוִד אָבִיו הוּא דְּלָא הֲוָה מִיחְטָא נָמֵי לָא חֲטָא! הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״וַיְהִי לְעֵת זִקְנַת שְׁלֹמֹה נָשָׁיו הִטּוּ אֶת לְבָבוֹ לָלֶכֶת אַחֲרֵי אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים״ — וְלֹא הָלַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **However, how** then **do I establish** the meaning of the verse: **"For it came to pass, when Solomon was old, that his wives turned away his heart after other gods"** (I Kings 11:4)? **That** verse is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Natan; as Rabbi Natan raised a contradiction** between the two parts of the verse. On the one hand, **it is written: "For it came to pass, when Solomon was old, that his wives turned away his heart** after other gods." On the other hand, **isn't it written:** "And his heart was not perfect with the Lord his God, **as was the heart of David his father,"** indicating that Solomon's heart **was not equal to the heart of David his father;** however, **he also did not sin?** Rather, the verse **says as follows: For it came to pass, when Solomon was old, that his wives turned away his heart,** in an attempt to spur him to go **after other gods; however, he did not go** after them.

The *gemara* answers: *hahi k'Rabbi Natan* — that verse follows the position of *Rabbi Natan*. *Rabbi Natan* addresses the apparent contradiction between two verses — one saying the wives led *Shlomo*'s heart astray, the other saying his heart was not fully with God but he did not sin — and resolves it: *nashav hitu et levavo laleches acharei elohim acheirim, v'lo halach* — his wives caused his heart to go toward following other gods, but he did not actually go. They tried to lead him, but he did not commit the *avodah* itself.

### Future Tense Exegesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״אָז יִבְנֶה שְׁלֹמֹה בָּמָה לִכְמוֹשׁ שִׁקֻּץ מוֹאָב״ — שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לִבְנוֹת וְלֹא בָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't it written: "Then did Solomon build [*yivne*] an altar for Kemosh, the abomination of Moab,** in the hill that is before Jerusalem, and for Molech, the abomination of the children of Ammon" (I Kings 11:7), indicating that Solomon did in fact stray after idols? The Gemara answers: This can be understood to mean **that** Solomon **sought to build** an altar for idols **but did not build** it.

What then of the verse *az yivneh Shlomo bamah l'Chamosh shikutz Moav* — "*Shlomo* will build an altar to *Kemosh* the abomination of *Moav*"? The *gemara* reads the future tense *yivneh* as indicating intention rather than completed action: *shebikesh livnot v'lo vanah* — he desired to build but did not actually build.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״אָז יִבְנֶה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מִזְבֵּחַ לַה׳״ — שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לִבְנוֹת וְלֹא בָּנָה?! אֶלָּא דִּבְנָה, הָכָא נָמֵי דִּבְנָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But now** if that is the way to understand the future tense verb form *yivne*, what of another instance where the same form is employed: **"Then did Joshua build [*yivne*] an altar to the Lord God** of Israel in Mount Eival" (Joshua 8:30)? There too, would you say that Joshua **sought to build** an altar **but did not build** one? **Rather,** there, in the case of Joshua, it means that **he** actually **built it. Here too,** with regard to Solomon, it means that **he** actually **built** the altar.

An objection is raised: the verse in *Sefer Yehoshua* also uses the future tense *yivneh*, saying that *Yehoshua* "will build" an altar to God — and surely no one would claim *Yehoshua* merely intended but did not build. *Ela debana* — he built it. So the future tense cannot in itself prove that the action was not completed. *Hacha nami debana* — so here too *Shlomo* built it. The future-tense argument collapses.

### Reb Natan: Not Protesting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶת הַבָּמוֹת אֲשֶׁר עַל פְּנֵי יְרוּשָׁלִַים אֲשֶׁר מִימִין לְהַר הַמִּשְׁחָה אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה שְׁלֹמֹה מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְעַשְׁתֹּרֶת שִׁקֻּץ צִידֹנִים וְגוֹ׳״. אֶפְשָׁר בָּא אָסָא וְלֹא בִּיעֲרָם, יְהוֹשָׁפָט וְלֹא בִּיעֲרָם, עַד שֶׁבָּא יֹאשִׁיָּה וּבִיעֲרָם? וַהֲלֹא כׇּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל אָסָא וִיהוֹשָׁפָט בִּיעֲרוּם! אֶלָּא מַקִּישׁ רִאשׁוֹנִים לָאַחֲרוֹנִים, מָה אַחֲרוֹנִים לֹא עָשׂוּ וְתָלָה בָּהֶן לְשֶׁבַח, אַף רִאשׁוֹנִים לֹא עָשׂוּ וְתָלָה בָּהֶן לִגְנַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this verse should be understood **as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says:** The verse states: **"And the altars that were before Jerusalem, which were on the right hand of the Mount of Olives, which Solomon the king of Israel had built for Ashtoret the abomination of the Zidonim,** and for Kemosh the abomination of Moab, and for Milkom the abomination of the children of Ammon, did the king defile" (II Kings 23:13). All these were destroyed by Josiah. The Gemara asks: **Is it possible that** the righteous king **Asa came and did not destroy them,** and the righteous king **Jehosaphat** came **and did not destroy them until Josiah came and destroyed them? Didn't Asa and Jehosaphat destroy all the idolatry in Eretz Yisrael? Rather,** the **verse juxtaposes the earlier ones,** i.e., Solomon, **to the later ones,** i.e., Josiah. **Just as the later ones,** Josiah, **did not effect** the destruction of the altars, as it was done by his predecessors, **and** nevertheless **the verse attributes** it **to him to praise** him as if he had destroyed all those altars, **so too, the earlier ones,** Solomon, **did not effect** the construction, **and** nevertheless **the verse attributes** it **to him to disgrace** him as he did not prevent their construction.

The *gemara* therefore turns to a different textual basis. *Ela kedetanya* — it is as the *braita* taught. *Rabbi Yosi* notes that the verse describing *Yoshiyahu*'s reforms says he destroyed *"the altars on the face of Yerushalayim to the right of the Mount of the Destroyer, which Shlomo Melech Yisrael built for Ashtoreth the abomination of the Sidonians."* This verse credits *Shlomo* with building them. But *efshar* — is it possible? *Asa* came and destroyed all *avodah zarah* in the land. Then *Yehoshafat* came and destroyed what had cropped up since. *Kol avodah zarah shebe'eretz Yisrael, Asa v'Yehoshafat bi'arum* — all *avodah zarah* in the land of Israel, *Asa* and *Yehoshafat* wiped out. The only altars *Yoshiyahu* needed to destroy were new ones that had appeared in his time. If *Shlomo* had actually built altars, they would have been eliminated by *Asa* or *Yehoshafat* centuries earlier. *Yoshiyahu* would have had nothing left from *Shlomo*'s time to destroy.

*Ella makish rishonim l'acharonim* — the verse connects the earlier figures to the later ones. *Mah acharonim lo asu v'tala vahen l'shvach* — just as *Yoshiyahu* is credited with destroying altars that technically no longer existed, because his will was to uproot all *avodah zarah* everywhere, so too *shlomo* is credited with building those altars even though he did not personally erect them. *Rishon* and *acharon* are held symmetrically accountable and credited: *Yoshiyahu* gets credit for what he intended to uproot even if it was already gone; *Shlomo* bears responsibility for what he intended to permit even though others built it. What *Shlomo* actually did was fail to protest — *mipnei shehayah lo limchot b'nashav v'lo micha, ma'aleh alav hakatuv k'ilu chata* — since he had the opportunity to intervene and did not, Scripture treats it as if he himself sinned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעַשׂ שְׁלֹמֹה הָרַע בְּעֵינֵי ה׳״! אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לִמְחוֹת בְּנָשָׁיו וְלֹא מִיחָה מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִלּוּ חָטָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another question. **Isn't it written: "And Solomon did evil in the sight of the Lord"** (I Kings 11:6), clearly indicating that Solomon sinned? **Rather, since he should have protested against** the conduct of **his wives,** i.e., their involvement in idolatry, **but he did not protest, the verse ascribes to him** liability **as if he had sinned.**

### Active vs. Passive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נוֹחַ לוֹ לְאוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק שֶׁיְּהֵא שַׁמָּשׁ לְדָבָר אַחֵר, וְאַל יִכָּתֵב בּוֹ ״וַיַּעַשׂ הָרָע בְּעֵינֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: It would have been preferable for that righteous man,** Solomon, **to be a servant** tasked with drawing water and hewing wood **for another matter,** i.e., idolatry, **and not** have the verse **write about him: "And he did evil in the sight of the Lord,"** even though he did not.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: noach lo l'oto tzadik sheyehei shamash l'davar acher* — it would have been better for that *tzaddik* — note that *Shlomo* is here called a *tzaddik* — to have personally performed some menial service for *avodah zarah*, to have swept the floor of a pagan temple, *v'al yikatev bo vaya'as hara b'einei Hashem* — rather than to have it written of him that he did evil in the eyes of God.

This is a striking formulation that goes directly against the instinct of keeping one's hands clean. The conventional religious impulse is: as long as I am not personally doing anything wrong, I have not sinned. *Shlomo* thought: I am not worshipping; my wives are. But the *gemara* says that this passive non-intervention, when one had the authority and obligation to protest, is treated as the full commission of the act — *vaya'as hara b'einei Hashem*. And *Shmuel* says: it would have been better to personally dirty your hands with a minor act of *avodah zarah* than to be held morally and textually responsible for the entire edifice of it by looking away. The weight of the obligation to be *mocheh* — to protest, to intervene — could not be more starkly expressed.

### Rome Founded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנָּשָׂא שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת בַּת פַּרְעֹה, הִכְנִיסָה לוֹ אֶלֶף מִינֵי זֶמֶר, וְאָמְרָה לוֹ כָּךְ עוֹשִׂין לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה פְּלוֹנִית, וְכָךְ עוֹשִׂים לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה פְּלוֹנִית, וְלֹא מִיחָה בָּהּ. יָרַד גַּבְרִיאֵל וְנָעַץ קָנֶה בַּיָּם וְעָלָה בּוֹ שִׂירְטוֹן, וְעָלָיו נִבְנָה כְּרַךְ גָּדוֹל שֶׁל רוֹמִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: When Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter, she brought to him a thousand musical instruments and said to him: This is the way we do** it **for this idolatry, and this is the way we do** it **for that idolatry, and he did not protest** that talk. **The angel Gabriel descended** from heaven **and implanted a reed into the sea, and a sandbar grew around it,** growing larger each year, **and upon it the great city of Rome was built,** which became God's instrument to punish Israel.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: basha'ah shenasah Shlomo et Bat Paroh* — when *Shlomo* married the daughter of *Paroh*, *hichnisah lo elef minei zemer* — she brought in a thousand kinds of musical instruments, and for each one she would demonstrate how it was used in the worship of a particular *avodah zarah*. *V'lo micha bah* — and he did not protest. The consequence: *yarad Gavriel v'naatz kaneh bayam* — the angel *Gavriel* descended and drove a reed into the sea; *v'alah bo sirton v'nivnah bo krach gadol shelo Romi* — a sandbar formed around it, which ultimately became the foundation of Rome.

The parallel to the *David* episode is clear: *David*'s acceptance of *lashon hara* split the kingdom; *Shlomo*'s passivity in the face of *avodah zarah* planted the seed of Rome — the power that would destroy the very *Mikdash Shlomo* had built. *Steinsaltz* notes that the Greek consolidation of influence over southern Italy occurred roughly contemporaneously with *Shlomo*'s reign, though Rome itself was founded some two centuries later. The *gemara*'s framework is not historical chronology but moral causation: the external influence *Shlomo* allowed to enter through his marriage to *Bat Paroh* set in motion the rise of the power that would ultimately undo his greatest achievement.

## Jeroboam & Josiah

### Jeroboam's Calves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁהִכְנִיס יָרָבְעָם שְׁנֵי עֶגְלֵי זָהָב, אֶחָד בְּבֵית אֵל וְאֶחָד בְּדָן, נִבְנָה צְרִיף אֶחָד, וְזֶהוּ אִיטַלְיָאה שֶׁל יָוָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita*: On that very day that Jeroboam introduced two golden calves, one in Beth-El and the other in Dan, a single** small **hut was constructed,** which was the first house constructed there. **And that was** the inauguration of **Italy of Greece.**

A *braita* adds the next stage: *oto hayom shehichnis Yerovam shnei eglei zahav, beBeit El echad uveDan, nivnah tzrif echad* — on the day that *Yerovam* introduced the two golden calves, one in *Beit El* and one in *Dan*, a small hut was built on that emerging sandbar in the sea. *V'zo hi Italya shel Yavan* — and this is Italy of Greece, meaning the stage at which the Greeks extended their influence over the southern Italian peninsula. First *Shlomo*'s marriage to *Bat Paroh* formed the island; then *Yerovam*'s institutionalized *avodah zarah* allowed the first habitation. Rome is built incrementally, corresponding to Israel's moral deterioration. As *Yerushalayim* descends, Rome rises.

### Josiah's Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ חָטָא — אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא טוֹעֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַשׂ הַיָּשָׁר בְּעֵינֵי ה׳ וַיֵּלֶךְ בְּכׇל דֶּרֶךְ דָּוִד אָבִיו״. אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְכָמֹהוּ לֹא הָיָה לְפָנָיו מֶלֶךְ אֲשֶׁר שָׁב וְגוֹ׳״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: Anyone who says that Josiah sinned is nothing other than mistaken, as it is stated: "And he did that which was right in the sight of the Lord, and walked in all the way of David his father,** and turned not aside to the right hand nor to the left" (II Kings 22:2). **However, how** then **do I establish** the meaning of the verse: **"And like him was there no king before him that returned** to the Lord with all his heart, and with all his soul, and with all his might, according to all the Torah of Moses; neither after him arose there any like him" (II Kings 23:25)? The verse states: Josiah returned to God. Apparently, in his early days he was a sinner, and later he returned to God.

The final figure in the list is *Yoshiyahu. Amar Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol omer Yoshiyahu chata eino ela to'eh* — whoever says *Yoshiyahu* sinned is only making a mistake. *Yoshiyahu* was righteous — the verse says *vaya'as hayashar b'einei Hashem v'haleich bechol derech David aviv*. So what is the problem? The verse also says *vechamohu lo hayah lefanav melech asher shav el Hashem bechol levavo* — there was no king before him who *returned* to God with his whole heart. The word *shav* — returned — implies he had gone somewhere first, that there was something to return from.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכָּל דִּין שֶׁדָּן מִבֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה עַד שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה הֶחְזִירָן לָהֶן. שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר נָטַל מִזֶּה וְנָתַן לָזֶה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּכָל מְאֹדוֹ״: שֶׁנָּתַן לָהֶם מִשֶּׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The reference here is not to repentance for actual sins that Josiah committed. Rather, in **every judgment that he issued from the age of eight,** when he was crowned, **until** the age of **eighteen,** he **returned** the money to the parties whom he judged liable, due to concern that in his youth he may not have judged the cases correctly. **And lest you say** that **he took from this** one, whom he exonerated, **and gave to that** one, whom he found culpable, therefore **the verse states:** Returned to the Lord **with all his might [*me'odo*],** i.e., with all his money. It means **that he gave** those he judged liable in his youth **from his own** money.

The *gemara*'s answer: it does not mean he sinned and repented. Rather, *shekol dayan shedan miben shemoneh ad shemoneh esreh, hechziran lahen* — every judgment he had issued from the time he became king at age eight until eighteen years later, he reversed. Why? Not because he had been a minor — a king may adjudicate. But eighteen years into his reign he found the *Sefer Torah*, and realized he had not been applying the full weight of *Torah she'bikhsav* and *Torah she'ba'al peh*. *Shav el Hashem* — he returned to God by revisiting and correcting all his earlier judicial decisions.

One might think this involved taking money from those who had won under his earlier rulings and redistributing it. The *gemara* says *shema tomar natal mizeh v'natan lazeh* — perhaps he simply reversed the decisions, transferring from one litigant to the other. The verse says *bechol me'odo* — with all his strength, meaning wealth: *shenatan lahen mishelo* — he compensated those who had been wronged out of his own pocket. Those who had won under his earlier rulings kept what they had received; those who deserved to win but had not were made whole from the king's personal resources.

### Rav: Ba'al Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב: אֵין לְךָ גָּדוֹל בְּבַעֲלֵי תְּשׁוּבָה יוֹתֵר מִיֹּאשִׁיָּהוּ בְּדוֹרוֹ וְאֶחָד בְּדוֹרֵנוּ. וּמַנּוּ? אַבָּא אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אַחָא אֲחוּהּ דְּאַבָּא אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא. דְּאָמַר מָר: רַבִּי אַבָּא וְאַחָא אַחֵי הֲווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yonatan **disagrees with Rav, as Rav said: There is no greater penitent than Josiah in his generation, and** there is **one in our generation.** The Gemara asks: **And who** is the great penitent in Rav's generation? The Gemara answers: He is **Abba, father of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba, and some say** it is **Aḥa, brother of Abba, father of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba, as the Master said: Rabbi Abba and Aḥa were brothers.**

*Rav* disagrees with this entire framing. *D'amar Rav: ein lecha gadol b'ba'alei teshuvah yoter miYoshiyahu* — there is no greater *ba'al teshuvah* than *Yoshiyahu*. Once again, *Rav* is prepared to say the person actually sinned — that *Yoshiyahu* began his life following in the ways of his father's idolatry — and that his greatness was in the magnitude of his *teshuvah*. *Bedoro* — in his generation, he was the greatest *ba'al teshuvah*.

### Later Ba'alei Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: וְעוֹד אֶחָד בְּדוֹרֵנוּ. וּמַנּוּ? — עוּקְבָן בַּר נְחֶמְיָה רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא. וְהַיְינוּ ״נָתָן דְּצוּצִיתָא״. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא בְּפִירְקָא, וַהֲוָה קָא מְנַמְנַמְנָא, וַחֲזַאי בְּחֶילְמָא דְּקָא פְּשַׁט יְדֵיהּ וְקַבְּלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: And there is another** great penitent **in our generation. And who is he?** He is **Okvan, son of Neḥemya the Exilarch. And that is** the one also known as **Natan *detzutzita*,** i.e., from whose head sparks emerged. **Rav Yosef said: I was** once **sitting at the lecture delivered on the Festival [*pirka*] and I was dozing. And I saw in a dream how** an angel **stretched out his hands and received** Natan *detzutzita*, demonstrating that his repentance was accepted.

*V'echad bedoreinu* — and there is one in our generation. *Rav Yosef* then adds: *ve'od echad bedoreinu* — and there is another in our generation. *Umanu? Ukva bar Nechemiah Reish Galusa* — who is it? *Mar Ukva*, the *Reish Galusa*, the *Exilarch*. This is striking: just yesterday the *gemara* subjected the *Reish Galusa* to severe criticism, holding him accountable for allowing injustice in his court. Here *Rav Yosef* counts him among the great *ba'alei teshuvah*. He is also identified as *Natan deTzitzisa* — Nathan of the sparks, though the exact meaning of the epithet remains uncertain.

*Amar Rav Yosef: hava yasvina b'pirka v'hava ka menamnem* — I was sitting in the learning session and I was dozing off. *V'chazai b'chilma d'kapshi yadei v'kablei* — and I saw in my dream a hand reach down from heaven and receive this *Ukva bar Nechemia*, signifying that his *teshuvah* had been fully accepted before Heaven.

*Hadran alach Behemah.*
