# Bava Kamma 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Worker Negligence

### Intro & Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַנּוֹתֵן צֶמֶר לַצַבָּע, וְהִקְדִּיחוֹ יוֹרָה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי צַמְרוֹ. צְבָעוֹ כָּאוּר – אִם הַשֶּׁבַח יָתֵר עַל הַיְּצִיאָה, נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַיְּצִיאָה; וְאִם הַיְּצִיאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַשֶּׁבַח, נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who gives wool to a dyer and it was burned in the cauldron** during the dyeing process, thereby completely ruining the wool so that there is no enhancement, only loss, the dyer **gives** the owner **the value of his wool.** If he **dyed it unattractively [*ka'ur*]** so that the dye is not absorbed well by the wool, **if the enhancement,** i.e., the amount that the value of the wool has increased by being dyed, **exceeds the** dyer's **expenses,** the owner of the wool **gives** the dyer **the expenses. And if the expenses exceed the enhancement, he gives him** the value of **the enhancement.**

Daf 101 marks a milestone: one hundred daf completed in Bava Kamma. We pick up with the *mishna* on 100b. The preceding *mishna* had addressed workers who damage objects entrusted to them and are liable to pay. That discussion touched on an interesting nuance — whether a person's primary role vis-à-vis an object is that of a workman or a *shomer*, which might place the situation in a different category than ordinary *adam hamazik*, even when damage occurs. That was the backdrop. Now we return to a case involving *shinui*, but *shinui* not in the context of *gezeila*, rather in the context of a craftsman or worker operating on someone else's property.

### Destroyed Wool

The *mishna* opens: *hanotein tzemer latzaba ve-hikdicho yoreh* — someone gave wool to a dyer, and the pot scalded it, destroying the wool. *Noten lo d'mei tzemaro* — he must pay the cost of the wool. Whether the case assumes negligence, and what degree of negligence is required, is not the focus here; the point is simply that the wool was destroyed in the course of the work, and the craftsman must pay. That is the straightforward case.

### Inferior Dye

The *mishna* then turns to a more nuanced scenario: *tzeva'o ka'ur* — the craftsman dyed it with an inferior dye. The word rendered *ka'ur* is written with an *alef* in place of an *ayin*, and means "inferior." The correct color was used, but the quality of the dye was substandard, and the resulting quality of the wool was diminished accordingly. In that case: *im hashevach yoter al hayetzia noten lo et hayetzia; im hayetzia yoter al hashevach noten lo et hashevach* — if the improvement exceeds the expenses, he pays the craftsman only his expenses; if the expenses exceed the improvement, he pays only the improvement.

At the outset, it is worth noting that no one raises the possibility of *shinui* here. The craftsman was told to dye it a certain color and did so — it is therefore not a *shinui* in the sense of taking possession of the object. The question is rather: what recourse does the owner have when the work was done poorly?

### Pshat Expenses

To appreciate the *pshat* of the *mishna*, consider a representative scenario. The owner brings wool worth one hundred dollars, contracts a fee of fifty dollars, and anticipates receiving wool worth two hundred dollars. The craftsman, however, does inferior work, and the end result is wool worth only one hundred fifty dollars. Of the fifty-dollar fee, say twenty-five dollars represented out-of-pocket expenses and twenty-five dollars represented the craftsman's labor. The *mishna* says: pay the lesser of the expenses or the improvement. In this case, pay only the expenses — twenty-five dollars — and forfeit the labor. The owner receives wool worth one hundred fifty dollars and has paid only twenty-five, netting a profit of twenty-five instead of the anticipated fifty.

### Profit Analysis

The same framework applies across different valuation scenarios. If the resulting wool is worth only one hundred twenty dollars, then the improvement — twenty dollars — is less than the expenses — twenty-five dollars — and so only the improvement is paid. The owner breaks even: he neither gains nor loses. The principle is consistent: as long as there is some improvement, however modest, the craftsman is paid the lesser of the two figures, and the owner is guaranteed at minimum to break even. Only in the first half of the *mishna*, where the wool is actually destroyed and rendered worth less than its original value, is the craftsman a *mazik* who must pay full damages.

The mechanism behind this arrangement is worth examining. The craftsman is not a *mazik* — the object is worth more than it was. He is not a *gazlan*, and he has not effected a *shinui* that would make him *koneh* the object. He is a worker who failed to perform as contracted. The response of *chazal* is effectively to re-read the terms of the contract: since you did not perform your labor as required, you are not entitled to be paid for your labor; but since you did incur out-of-pocket expenses, those are recoverable. The result is a form of *peshara* — a reasonable settlement. The owner may not realize the full profit he anticipated, but he will not lose money, and the craftsman loses his labor as the consequence of his inferior work.

A student raised the observation that under secular law, a contractor who completely fails to perform might recover nothing at all — not even expenses. The response is instructive: this is a case of inferior performance, not a complete departure from the contracted work. The craftsman used the right color but an inferior grade of dye. That is not the radical variation addressed in the next section of the *mishna*. The question of exactly what a contractor is owed when work is substandard — as distinct from entirely wrong — is genuinely complex in any legal system.

### Contract Reinterpretation

It bears emphasis that *chazal* are not finding the craftsman liable as a *mazik*, nor are they finding a *kinyan* through *shinui*. They are reinterpreting the contractual arrangement itself. Because the work was below standard, the craftsman loses his labor; because he did have legitimate out-of-pocket costs, those are compensable. This framework ensures the owner does not lose money and the craftsman bears the cost of his poor workmanship in the form of forfeited wages. Whether this is a strict *din* or something *chazal* imposed as a form of equitable adjustment is an interesting question, but that is the structure.

*Tosafot* offers an alternative reading, drawing on the *Yerushalmi* as interpreted by the *Ri*, though *Tosafot* acknowledges this is not the *pshat* of the *Yerushalmi*. The conceptual approach is different: rather than simply docking the craftsman's labor, one first determines what profit the owner would have made had everything gone correctly, and that profit is deducted from whatever would otherwise be owed to the craftsman. Any remainder is then paid.

In the scenario described: the owner would have made a fifty-dollar profit had the work been performed properly. So from the value of the dyed wool, fifty dollars is first allocated to the owner as his rightful profit. Only if something remains is the craftsman paid. He does not receive compensation directly for lost profit — the owner does not recover damages for anticipated gains — but the accounting begins with restoring the owner's expected profit before the craftsman sees a penny.

This reading places considerably more pressure on the craftsman. Under the *pshat* reading, the craftsman at least recovers his expenses in most scenarios; under *Tosafot*'s reading, even his expenses might be absorbed by the owner's priority claim to profit. *Tosafot* is starting from the premise that the owner is entitled to the profit he contracted for, and the entire arrangement follows from that premise. Practically speaking, the *pshat* reading is almost certainly the *psak halacha*, and even *Tosafot* acknowledges that this is not the straightforward reading. But the conceptual distinction is meaningful: one approach protects the craftsman's out-of-pocket costs; the other prioritizes the owner's expected return above all else.

## Shinui & Possession

### Wrong Color Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִצְבּוֹעַ לוֹ אָדוֹם וּצְבָעוֹ שָׁחוֹר, שָׁחוֹר וּצְבָעוֹ אָדוֹם – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי צַמְרוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הַשֶּׁבַח יָתֵר עַל הַיְּצִיאָה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַיְּצִיאָה, וְאִם הַיְּצִיאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַשֶּׁבַח – נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח.
>
> **Translation:** If the owner gave wool to a dyer **to dye** it **red for him and** instead **he dyed it black,** or to dye it **black and he dyed it red, Rabbi Meir says:** The dyer **gives** the owner of the wool **the value of his wool. Rabbi Yehuda says:** Here too, **if** the value of **the enhancement exceeds the** dyer's **expenses,** the owner of the wool **gives** the dyer **the expenses. And if the expenses exceed the enhancement, he gives him** the value of **the enhancement.**

The *mishna* proceeds to a more radical case: *litzboa lo adom v'tzava'o shachor, shachor v'tzava'o adom* — he was told to dye it red and dyed it black, or told black and dyed it red. Here we are no longer discussing inferior quality — the craftsman produced a different color entirely, in violation of his instructions. *Rabbi Meir omer noten lo d'mei tzemaro* — Rabbi Meir says this is equivalent to a *mazik*: the craftsman is *koneh* through *shinui* and pays for the wool.

### Yehuda's Position

*Rabbi Yehuda*, however, disagrees. *Im hashevach yeter al hayetzia noten lo es hayetzia; v'im hayetzia yeter al hashevach noten lo es hashevach* — the same rule as before applies. Even producing the wrong color is not a *shinui* sufficient to effect *kinyan*. The craftsman simply failed to do the job as instructed; he is treated the same as the inferior-dye case, and the lesser of expenses or improvement is paid.

The debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is a conceptual one: does *shinui* apply in the context of a craftsman who violates his instructions, or is *shinui* a category reserved for the *gazlan*? It is worth noting, as was pointed out in discussion, that it is not necessarily clear which ruling is more favorable to the owner of the wool. If the wrong color happens to be more valuable, Rabbi Meir's ruling — which makes the craftsman *koneh* and requires him to pay only for the raw wool — could actually leave the owner worse off than Rabbi Yehuda's approach. The debate is therefore not about protecting one party over another; it is a purely conceptual question about whether *shinui* is operative here.

### Bench vs Chair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנּוֹתֵן עֵצִים לֶחָרָשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת מֵהֶן כִּסֵּא, וְעָשָׂה מֵהֶן סַפְסָל; סַפְסָל, וְעָשָׂה מֵהֶן כִּסֵּא – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי עֵצָיו. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הַשֶּׁבַח יָתֵר עַל הַיְּצִיאָה – נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַיְּצִיאָה, וְאִם הַיְּצִיאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַשֶּׁבַח – נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 10:8): With regard to **one who gives wood to a carpenter to fashion a chair from it and he fashioned a bench from it** instead, or one who gives him wood to fashion **a bench and he fashioned a chair from it** instead, **Rabbi Meir says:** The carpenter **gives him the value of his wood** and keeps the chair or bench, as he has acquired it due to the change. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** He does not acquire the item. Rather, **if** the value of the wood's **enhancement exceeds the** carpenter's **expenses** the owner **gives** the carpenter **the expenses, and if the expenses exceed the enhancement** of the wood **he gives him** the value of **the enhancement.** Since the carpenter deviated from the customer's order, he is entitled to either his expenses or the enhancement, the smaller amount of the two.

The *gemara* extends the same debate to a more radical scenario. A *baraita* teaches: *hanotein etzim lecharash la'asot mehem kisei v'asa mehem safsal* — one gave wood to a carpenter to make a chair, and he made a bench instead; or gave it to make a bench, and he made a chair. Rabbi Meir says: *noten lo d'mei eitzav* — pay for the wood, the craftsman is *koneh* through *shinui*. This is an even more dramatic departure than wrong color — the very form of the object has changed — yet Rabbi Meir applies the same principle. Rabbi Yehuda again disagrees: *im hashevach yeter al hayetzia noten lo es hayetzia* and so on. The craftsman did not fulfill his commission, but *shinui* does not apply, and the lesser-of-two rule governs.

### Meir's Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אִם נָתַן עֵצִים לֶחָרָשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת מֵהֶן כִּסֵּא נָאֶה, וְעָשָׂה מֵהֶן כִּסֵּא כָּעוּר; סַפְסָל נָאֶה, וְעָשָׂה סַפְסָל כָּעוּר – אִם הַשֶּׁבַח יָתֵר עַל הַיְּצִיאָה, נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי הַיְּצִיאָה; וְאִם הַיְּצִיאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַשֶּׁבַח, נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי הַשֶּׁבַח.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And Rabbi Meir concedes** that **if he gave wood to a carpenter to fashion a beautiful chair from it and he fashioned an ugly chair from it,** or to fashion **a beautiful bench** from it **and he fashioned an ugly bench** from it, then **if** the value of the wood's **enhancement exceeds the** carpenter's **expenses** the owner **gives** the carpenter **the value of the expenses, and if the expenses exceed the enhancement he gives him the value of the enhancement.** Since the carpenter did not deviate significantly from the customer's instructions, Rabbi Meir concedes that the carpenter does not acquire the item due to the change.

Importantly, the *baraita* records that Rabbi Meir himself concedes a limitation on his own position. *U'modeh Rabbi Meir im natan etzim lecharash la'asot mehem kisei na'eh v'asa mehem kisei ka'ur* — if one gave wood to make a beautiful, high-quality chair, and the craftsman made a plain, inferior one; or was asked to make a fine bench and made a substandard one — even Rabbi Meir agrees this is not *shinui*. The object is of the same type; it is merely of lesser quality. In that case, *im hashevach yeter al hayetzia noten lo es hayetzia; v'im hayetzia yeter al hashevach noten lo es hashevach* — the lesser-of-two rule applies. So the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is confined to cases where the craftsman produces a genuinely different type of object or a genuinely different color: Rabbi Meir says that is *shinui* effecting *kinyan*; Rabbi Yehuda says it is not. Where it is merely inferior work of the same type, everyone agrees it falls under the *mishna*'s lesser-of-two framework.

## Dye Presence Theory

### Color Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: יֵשׁ שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל הַצֶּמֶר, אוֹ אֵין שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל הַצֶּמֶר?
>
> **Translation:** § In connection with the discussion of the halakhic implications of the enhancement of a garment quoted above, the Gemara records that **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: What is the *halakha*? That **the enhancement** which **dye** provides **to the wool** is **substantive,** i.e., the dye is considered a separate entity from the wool it dyed? **Or is** the *halakha* that **the enhancement** which **dye** provides **to the wool** is **not substantive,** i.e., the dye does not remain an independent entity, and it is viewed as if there is only colored wool?

The *gemara* now pivots to a broad conceptual question: *yesh shevach samanim al hatzemer o ein shevach samanim al hatzemer* — is the improvement of the dye still considered to be present in the wool, or not? The *gemara* later reformulates this as *chazuta milta hi o lo* — is the color, the appearance, of a dyed object itself considered a substantive presence of the original material? To put it concretely: when I look at a blue shirt, do I say there is a shirt and there is also blue dye still present within it, or do I say there is simply a blue shirt, with no dye remaining as a discrete entity?

The practical significance of this question — the *nafka mina* — depends on the case, and the *gemara* works through several scenarios to isolate one in which the answer truly makes a difference.

### Stolen Dye Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דִּגְזַל סַמָּנִין וְדַקִּינְהוּ וּתְרָנְהוּ וּצְבַע (בָּהֶן) [בְּהוּ], תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּקְנָנְהוּ בְּשִׁינּוּי! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּגְזַל סַמָּנִין שְׁרוּיִין, וּצְבַע בְּהוּ. מַאי? יֵשׁ שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי צֶמֶר, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי סַמָּנַאי דִּשְׁקַלְתִּינְהוּ״; אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי הַצֶּמֶר, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵית לָךָ גַּבַּאי וְלָא מִידֵּי״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which this dilemma is relevant? **If we say that** the dilemma arises **where one robbed** another of **pigments, and ground them** up **and soaked them** in water, converting them into dye, **and** then **dyed** wool **with them,** then the dilemma would be concerning how the robber returns the pigments. In this case, **derive** the *halakha* **due to** the fact that the robber **acquired** the pigments **due to a change** of form, which occurred when he ground the pigments, and in any event will be obligated to return the value of the pigments at the time of the robbery. **No,** it is **necessary** to resolve the dilemma in a case **where** one **robbed** another of **pigments** that had already been **soaked** in water, **and** then **dyed** wool **with them. What** is the *halakha* here? Is **the enhancement** which **dye** provides **to** the **wool substantive,** i.e., a separate entity, so **that** the owner can **say to** the robber: **Give me my dyes that you took? Or perhaps the enhancement** which **dye** provides **to the wool** is **not substantive,** but becomes one with the wool, so **that** the robber can **say to** the owner: **I have nothing at all of yours,** since the dyes are no longer considered extant.

The *gemara* initially considers a case where someone steals dye (*samanim*, or more precisely the raw pigment ingredients), processes them — stomping and immersing them to produce liquid dye — and uses the dye to color some wool. The *gemara* notes this is not the right framing: by processing the raw materials, the thief already acquired possession through *shinui* at that earlier stage, before the dye even touched the wool. So the question of whether the dye is present in the wool is irrelevant in that version.

The *gemara* then tries: what if the thief stole already-prepared liquid dye and used it to dye his wool? Even here, the scenario is difficult. If the dye is not considered present, the owner demands payment for his stolen dye. If it is present, the owner could theoretically demand his dye back — but how? The *gemara* notes that any attempt to extract the dye (*tzappan*) would destroy it entirely, so saying "just take it" is not a meaningful remedy. Either way, the thief cannot escape liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: וְאִי אֵין שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי צֶמֶר, מִי מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵית לָךָ גַּבַּאי וְלָא מִידֵּי״?! נֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי סַמָּנַאי דְּאַפְסֵדְתִּינְהוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this understanding of the application of the dilemma. The Sages **say: And** even **if** the **enhancement** which **dye** provides **to** the **wool is not substantive, can** the robber really **say to him: I have nothing at all of yours? Let** the owner **say to him: Give me my dyes that you have** caused to be **lost.** Even if the dyes are no longer considered extant, the owner is still entitled to monetary compensation for the robbery.

The *gemara* therefore settles on a case with a genuine *nafka mina*: the thief stole both the wool and the dye from the same person, dyed the wool with the stolen dye, and then has a change of heart and returns the dyed wool. Has he returned everything he stole? If *yesh shevach samanim* — the dye is still considered present in the wool — then yes, by returning the dyed wool he has returned both the wool and the dye, and his obligation is discharged. If *ein shevach samanim* — the dye is no longer present — then he has returned only the wool, and he still owes for the dye separately.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דִּגְזַל צֶמֶר וְסַמָּנִין דְּחַד, וְצַבְעֵיהּ לְהָהוּא צֶמֶר בְּהָנָךְ סַמָּנִין, וְקָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְצֶמֶר. יֵשׁ שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי הַצֶּמֶר, וְקָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ סַמָּנִין וְצֶמֶר; אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי צֶמֶר, וְצֶמֶר מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, סַמָּנִין לָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another scenario: **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where he robbed one** person of **wool and dyes, and dyed that wool with those dyes, and he is returning the** dyed **wool to him.** The dilemma is: Is the *halakha* that **enhancement** which **dye** provides **to the wool is substantive, and** he **is** therefore **returning** both the **dye and** the **wool to him? Or perhaps enhancement** which **dye** provides **to** the **wool is not substantive, and** it is only **the wool** that **he is returning to him,** but **he is not returning the dye to him,** since it is no longer extant.

The *gemara* then tries: what if the thief stole both the wool and the dye from the same person, dyed the wool with the stolen dye, and then returns the dyed wool. Has he returned everything?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּאִיַּיקַּר לֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ בִּדְמֵי! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּזַל צִיבְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this understanding of the application of the dilemma as well. The Sages **say: Derive** the *halakha* in this case based on the fact **that he increased the value** of the wool **for him,** as dyed wool is more expensive than undyed wool. Consequently, even if the robber has not returned the dye, he reimburses the owner for it when he returns the appreciated wool. The Gemara suggests: **No,** it is **necessary** to resolve the dilemma in a case **where** the cost of **the dye has depreciated.**

The *gemara* raises a complication: even under *ein shevach samanim*, would the thief not benefit from the rule that a *gazlan* who improves a stolen object and returns it is entitled to compensation for the improvement? If the dye was worth twenty-five dollars and the improvement to the wool is worth fifty dollars, then even if the dye is not present and he owes twenty-five for the dye, he is owed fifty for the improvement, and on net the owner owes him the difference. This is a recurring theme from earlier in the tractate — improving stolen property, whether under *takanas hashavim* or *kinyan gezeila*, entitles the improver to compensation.

### Market Value Drop

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁצָּבַע בְּהוּ קוֹפָא.
>
> **Translation:** If the dye is viewed as an independent entity the thief is returning both the wool and the dye. If it is not, he must reimburse the robbery victim for the value of the dye at the time of the robbery. **And if you wish, say** instead: The dilemma is relevant in a case **where** he robbed another of dye and a basket, and **dyed** the **basket with** the dye, in which case the dye does not increase the value of the dyed item.

As noted above, the market-value-drop scenario is the case the *gemara* identifies as cleanly isolating the *nafka mina*. The thief returns the dyed wool when the market has declined. Under *yesh shevach samanim*, the physical dye is present in the returned wool, the return is complete, and the market loss falls on the owner just as it would had no theft occurred — the thief has returned the object as it stands. Under *ein shevach samanim*, the dye has vanished; only colored wool remains. The thief owes for the dye but can offset only the modest improvement in the wool's value, leaving a net obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּצֶמֶר דְּחַד וְסַמָּנִין דְּחַד, וְקָאָתֵי קוֹף וְצַבְעֵיהּ לְהָהוּא צֶמֶר בְּהָנָךְ סַמָּנִין. יֵשׁ שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי צֶמֶר, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי סַמָּנַאי דְּגַבָּךְ נִינְהוּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין שֶׁבַח סַמָּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי צֶמֶר, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵית לָךָ גַּבַּאי כְּלוּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents an alternative understanding of the case where the dilemma applies. **Ravina said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where there** was **wool that** belonged to **one** person **and dyes that** belonged to **one** other person, **and a monkey came and dyed that wool with those dyes.** The dilemma is as follows: Is the *halakha* that **enhancement** which **dye** provides **to** the **wool is substantive,** so **that** the owner of the dye can **say to** the owner of the wool: **Give me my dyes that are with you? Or perhaps enhancement** which **dye** provides **to** the **wool is not substantive and** the owner of the wool can **say to** the owner of the dye: **There is nothing** of yours **with me,** and he is exempt from liability because the dyes are no longer extant.

*Ravina* introduces an entirely different version of the scenario, one without any theft at all: *kegon de-tzemer de-chad ve-samanim de-chad* — Michael owns wool; David owns dye; *ve-ata kof ve-tzavya lehu tzemer be-hanei samanim* — a monkey came along and, in the manner of curious monkeys, mixed Michael's wool with David's dye, dyeing the wool with it. This is why *Rashi* explains *kupa* as a monkey — the scenario we have just reached involves a monkey (*kof*) as the actor.

There is no *gazlan* here. The monkey is wild; there is no human tortfeasor (setting aside the separate *Bava Kamma* discussion of the owner of an animal that causes damage, which is inapplicable to a wild monkey). The question is therefore purely: *yesh shevach samanim al gabei tzemer* — is Michael's dye still considered present in David's wool, such that Michael can say to David, "Give me back my dye; it's still yours in your wool"? If David cannot extract it physically — which he cannot, since any dye remover would destroy the dye entirely — he would have to compensate Michael for it. Or: *ein shevach samanim al gabei tzemer* — the dye has ceased to exist; David holds nothing belonging to Michael; Michael has no claim.

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question about this scenario: even if the dye is not considered present, David's wool is now worth more. Should David not owe Michael something for the benefit he derived — analogous to the earlier *Bava Kamma* principle that one who benefits from another's property at a loss to the owner owes some compensation, even without fault? *Tosafot* notes the tension but suggests that where neither party nor their agent acted — where it was purely an act of nature, or in this case an act of monkey — perhaps the obligation of *neheneh* (benefiting at another's loss) does not arise. In any event, the *gemara*'s framing is focused on the question of physical presence, not the broader *neheneh* analysis, and the distinction between "the physical dye is present" and "a benefit was derived" is central to the structure of the discussion.

## Orlah Proof

### Orlah Must Burn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בֶּגֶד שֶׁצְּבָעוֹ בִּקְלִיפֵּי עׇרְלָה – יִדָּלֵק. אַלְמָא חֲזוּתָא מִילְּתָא הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** Having determined the scenario in which the dilemma is relevant, the Gemara proceeds to resolve the dilemma. **Come** and **hear** a solution from a mishna (*Orla* 3:1): **A garment that one dyed with** dye extracted from **peels of *orla*,** i.e., fruit that grows on a tree during its first three years, **must be burned,** as it is prohibited to derive benefit from *orla*. **Apparently,** the enhancement of an item's **appearance is** considered a significant **matter,** and the dye therefore remains an independent substance even after having been absorbed by the wool.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve the question of *yesh* or *ein shevach samanim* by bringing a *ta shema*: *beged she-tziva'o b'klipei orlah yidalek* — a garment dyed with the shells of *orlah* fruit must be burned. *Orlah* — the fruit of a tree during its first three years — is *assur b'hana'ah*, forbidden for any benefit. The question is whether a garment dyed with *orlah*-derived dye is itself *assur b'hana'ah*. The argument runs as follows: if the *orlah* were no longer present after dyeing, then wearing the garment would not constitute deriving benefit from *orlah* — it would be merely a colored garment, and the original prohibition would have been exhausted in the dyeing process. But if the garment must be burned, this implies the *orlah* is still considered present in the garment, and wearing it continues to constitute *hana'ah* from *orlah*. *Alma chazuta milta hi* — apparently, even mere appearance, mere color, counts as the physical presence of the original material.

The question of requiring destruction is sharper than the mere prohibition on wearing, as was observed in discussion. That the garment must be *burned* — not merely discarded or sold — indicates that the *orlah* is treated as actually present within it, not merely that an aesthetic pleasure is being forbidden. The ruling about destruction is therefore a stronger proof for *yesh shevach samanim* than the *issur hana'ah* alone.

### Special Orlah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲנָאָה הַנִּרְאֶה לָעֵינַיִם אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה – דְּתַנְיָא: ״עֲרֵלִים לֹא יֵאָכֵל״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִיסּוּר אֲכִילָה; מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יֵהָנֶה מִמֶּנּוּ, וְלֹא יִצְבַּע [בּוֹ], וְלֹא יַדְלִיק בּוֹ אֶת הַנֵּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַעֲרַלְתֶּם עׇרְלָתוֹ אֶת פִּרְיוֹ״, ״עֲרֵלִים לֹא יֵאָכֵל״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת כּוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this resolution. **Rava said:** The reason the garment must be burned is not because the dye remains an independent substance. Rather, it is because **the Torah prohibited benefit that is visible to the eye, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And shall have planted all manner of trees for food, then you shall count the fruit thereof as forbidden; three years shall it be as **forbidden [*areilim*]** to you; **it shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 19:23). From this verse **I have** derived **only a prohibition** against **eating** it; **from where** is it derived that one **may not** even derive **benefit from it, and** that **one may not dye with** dyes extracted from the fruit, **and** that **one may not light a lamp with** oil extracted from the fruit? The *baraita* answers: **The verse states: "Then you shall count the fruit thereof [*orlato*] as forbidden [*araltem*].** Three years shall it be as **forbidden [*areilim*]** to you; **it shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 19:23), **to include all these** types of benefit in the prohibition.

*Rava* responds: *hana'ah ha-nir'eh la'einayim assrah Torah* — the Torah specifically forbade even aesthetic pleasure in the case of *orlah*. He grounds this in the verse *ve-araltem orlato et piryo, arelim lo ye'achel* (Vayikra 19), from which the *gemara* derives through the doubled language — *ve-araltem, orlato, arelim* — that not only eating but also dyeing and using *orlah* as fuel for a lamp are forbidden. The Torah actively extended the prohibition of *orlah* to cover *hana'ah ha-nir'eh la'einayim*, pleasure that is merely visible or aesthetic. This is not, Rava argues, evidence of a general principle that absorbed material remains physically present; it is a special *halacha* specific to *orlah*, established by its own *drasha*, and cannot be generalized to monetary cases or other contexts.

It is worth noting the conceptual layering here. There are in fact two distinct questions: first, whether aesthetic pleasure constitutes *hana'ah* at all (a separate discussion involving *avodah zarah*, where aesthetic pleasure from gazing at an idol is ruled only *derabbanan*); and second, whether the *orlah* remains present after dyeing such that wearing the garment continues to derive benefit from it. Rava's formulation — *hana'ah ha-nir'eh la'einayim assrah Torah* — appears to address both, locating the ruling in a specific Torahic extension to *orlah* rather than in any general rule about presence. The upshot: the *orlah* proof cannot be leveraged to answer the general *yesh* or *ein shevach samanim* question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בֶּגֶד שֶׁצְּבָעוֹ בִּקְלִיפֵּי שְׁבִיעִית – יִדָּלֵק! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״תִּהְיֶה״ – בַּהֲוָיָתָהּ תְּהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** § Having cited a contradiction raised by Rava, the Gemara proceeds to cite another proof. **Come** and **hear** a resolution from that which was taught in a *baraita*: **A garment that one dyed with** dye extracted from **the peels of** produce from the **Sabbatical** Year must be **burned** when the time for the elimination of Sabbatical Year produce arrives. This indicates that the dye evidently remains an independent substance. The Gemara rejects this proof: It **is different there, as the verse states: "Shall** all the increase thereof **be"** (Leviticus 25:7), and the phrase: "Shall be," teaches that it **will** always **remain as it is,** i.e., it retains its status as Sabbatical Year produce despite any change to its form.

The *gemara* then raises a parallel case: *beged she-tziva'o b'klipei shevi'is yidalek* — a garment dyed with shells having *shemittah* status (after the time of *bi'ur*) must also be burned. Again this seems to suggest the dye remains present. The *gemara* answers with a verse: *ve-hayetah shabbat ha-aretz lachem le-ochlah* — *kodesh tihyeh lachem, tihyeh b'havyatah tehei* — it retains its status. This is again a specific *drasha* by *shemittah*, not a general principle. So both the *orlah* case and the *shemittah* case are deflected as exceptions grounded in specific verses, and the underlying question of *yesh* or *ein shevach samanim* remains unresolved by these proofs.

## Blood & Tumah

### Blood Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא רָמֵי, תְּנַן: בֶּגֶד שֶׁצְּבָעוֹ בִּקְלִיפֵּי עׇרְלָה – יִדָּלֵק; אַלְמָא חֲזוּתָא מִילְּתָא הִיא. וּרְמִינְהִי: רְבִיעִית דָּם שֶׁנִּבְלְעָה בַּבַּיִת – הַבַּיִת טָמֵא. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: הַבַּיִת טָהוֹר. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי, הָא בְּכֵלִים דַּהֲווֹ מֵעִיקָּרָא, הָא בְּכֵלִים דַּאֲתוֹ לְבַסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states that **Rava raises a contradiction: We learned** in a mishna (*Orla* 3:1): **A garment that one dyed with** dye extracted from **peels of *orla* must be burned. Apparently,** the change in **appearance** precipitated by the *orla* peels **is** considered a significant **matter,** and the dye retains its status as *orla*. **And raise a contradiction** from another mishna (*Oholot* 3:2): With regard to **a quarter**-*log* **of blood** from a corpse **that was absorbed in** the floor of **a house,** every vessel in **the house is ritually impure** by virtue of being under the same roof as the blood. **And some say** that any vessel in **the house is ritually pure. And** these two statements **do not disagree,** as **this** first statement was **in reference to vessels that were** in the house **at the outset,** before the blood was absorbed; and **this** second statement was **in reference to vessels that came** into the house **at the end,** after the blood had already been absorbed.

*Rava* now raises a direct contradiction. The *mishna* on *orlah* implies *chazuta milta hi* — appearance constitutes presence, and the *orlah* is still there. But consider: *revi'is dam she-nivle'ah ba-bayis, ha-bayis tamei* — a *revi'is* of blood (approximately three and a half ounces, the minimum quantity of a corpse's blood that generates *ohel* *tumah*) was absorbed into the floorboards of a house. One *tanna* says the house is *tamei*; another says *tahor*. This appears to be a contradiction about whether absorbed blood is still considered present.

### Blood Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִבְלְעָה בִּכְסוּת – רוֹאִין; אִם מִתְכַּבֶּסֶת הַכְּסוּת וְיוֹצָא מִמֶּנָּה רְבִיעִית דָּם – טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו – טְהוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the blood **was absorbed in a garment,** it is **examined,** and **if the garment is washed and a quarter**-*log* **of blood emerges from it,** it **is ritually impure,** and the garment imparts ritual impurity to the vessels in the house as well. **But if not,** i.e., if less than a quarter-*log* of blood emerges, then it **is pure,** and it does not impart impurity. Apparently, only the blood that can be removed from the garment is considered blood, while the blood absorbed in the garment is insignificant. If this is the case, the change in appearance precipitated by the blood is not considered a significant matter, and the blood absorbed in the garment does not remain an independent substance.

The *gemara* resolves these two positions: they do not actually conflict. *Ha b'keilim de-val mei'ikara* — vessels that were present in the house before the blood was absorbed are *tamei*, because at the moment of absorption, the blood was a real, present quantity of *tumah*, and those vessels were exposed to it. *Ha b'keilim de-atu l'vasof* — vessels brought into the house after the blood was absorbed do not become *tamei*, because at that point the blood is no longer considered physically present. The principle emerging from the *tumah* cases is that absorbed substances are generally not considered present in their new substrate — which would suggest *ein shevach samanim*, cutting against the *orlah* ruling.

### Extractable Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִקּוּלֵּי רְבִיעִיּוֹת שָׁנוּ כָּאן; בְּדַם תְּבוּסָה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents an answer. **Rav Kahana said:** A *halakha* **from** among **the leniencies** applied to the measurements **of a quarter**-*log* was **taught here,** as the mishna is written **in reference to the blood of submission** that is discharged from a body at the time of death, and such blood is ritually impure **by rabbinic law,** but in general, a change in appearance precipitated by blood is significant.

A *baraita* then addresses blood absorbed into a garment: *im mitkabeset ha-ksut ve-yotzei mimenah revi'is dam, tamei hi* — if you could launder the garment and extract a *revi'is* of blood in the wash water, the garment is *tamei* (and the blood is considered present and capable of extending *tumah* to objects under the same roof). *V'im lav, tehorah* — if the blood is not extractable, the garment is *tahor* on that basis. The principle is one of extractability: if the absorbed substance can be drawn back out, it is still present; if it cannot, it is considered gone. This standard — extractable versus non-extractable — maps onto the dye question in an interesting way, since absorbed dye, like absorbed blood, generally cannot be extracted without destroying it or the substrate.

### Dam Tvuza

*Rav Kahana* offers a resolution to the apparent contradiction between the *orlah* ruling (implying presence) and the *tumah* ruling (implying absence): *mi-kulei revi'yot shanu kan, b'dikei dam tvuza de-rabbanan* — the leniency in the *tumah* case applies specifically to *dam tvuza*, the blood of a dying person. Because it is uncertain whether the full *revi'is* minimum was discharged after death (some may have flowed during life), the *tumah* of *dam tvuza* is only *derabbanan*. Where the *tumah* is only rabbinic, we are lenient and rule that absorbed blood is not considered present. But for ordinary post-mortem blood of a full *revi'is*, absorbed into floorboards, the blood would in fact remain present and continue to cause *tumah*. 

This means the two *amoraim* — the one who said *chazuta milta hi* (appearance constitutes presence, *orlah* is the rule) and the one who said the *orlah* case is the exception — are in a genuine dispute. On 101a the *gemara* had leaned toward *ein shevach samanim* with *orlah* as an exception grounded in a specific verse; now on 101b the argument runs the other way — *chazuta milta hi* is the default rule, and the *tumah* leniency is the exception, limited to *dam tvuza de-rabbanan*. The question of *yesh* or *ein shevach samanim* is left without a definitive resolution, and the conceptual parallel to other contexts — *ta'am k'ikar*, the principle that a flavor can constitute the substance itself — is noted as genuinely related, even if this *sugya* is not the place to pursue it.

## Shemittah Dyes

### Woad Safflower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא רָמֵי, תְּנַן: מִמִּין הַצּוֹבְעִין – סְפִיחֵי סְטִיס וְקוֹצָה, יֵשׁ לָהֶן שְׁבִיעִית וְלִדְמֵיהֶן שְׁבִיעִית, יֵשׁ לָהֶן בִּיעוּר וְלִדְמֵיהֶן בִּיעוּר. אַלְמָא עֵצִים יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשַּׁת שְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** § Having cited a contradiction raised by Rava, the Gemara proceeds to cite another. **Rava raises** another **contradiction. We learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 7:1): Concerning plants **from** among **the species that** are used as **dyes,** for example the ***sefiḥin*,** i.e., produce that grew without being intentionally planted, **of woad and safflower; they have** sanctity of the **Sabbatical** Year **and money** exchanged for **them has** sanctity of the **Sabbatical** Year. Additionally, **they are** subject to the *halakha* of **eradication, and money** exchanged for **them is** subject to the *halakha* of **eradication. Apparently, wood,** a type of inedible growth, **is subject to the sanctity of the Sabbatical** Year despite the fact that it is not edible.

Because the discussion has touched on *shemittah*, the *gemara* turns to a related *mishna* addressing which plants carry *kedushat shemittah*. *Mi-min ha-tzovim* — plants of the dyer's trade — *sefichei satin ve-kotza* — wild growths of woad (which produces a dye chemically identical to indigo) and safflower — *yesh lahem shevi'it u-demeihem shevi'it* — have *shemittah* sanctity, and money received in exchange for them also has that sanctity. Moreover, *yesh lahem bi'ur u-demei bi'ur* — they are subject to the obligation of *bi'ur*, destruction once the corresponding season has passed in the fields, and money received for them carries the same obligation.

The noteworthy feature of this ruling is that these plants are inedible. Unlike *shemittah* grain or fruit, which have sanctity because they are food, these dye plants cannot be eaten. Yet they carry full *kedushat shemittah*. This implies that inedible plants — wood, broadly speaking — have *kedushat shemittah*, which seems to create a problem.

### Grape Leaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: עֲלֵי קָנִים וַעֲלֵי גְפָנִים שֶׁגִּיבְּבָן בְּחֵבֶא עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה, לִקְּטָן לַאֲכִילָה – יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשַּׁת שְׁבִיעִית, לְעֵצִים – אֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם קְדוּשַּׁת שְׁבִיעִית!
>
> **Translation:** **And raise a contradiction** from a *baraita*: With regard to **reed leaves and vine leaves that one piled for storage upon the field,** if he **gathered them for eating, they are subject to the sanctity of the Sabbatical** Year; if he gathered them **for** use as **wood,** e.g., for kindling a fire, **they are not subject to the sanctity of the Sabbatical** Year. Apparently, wood or any other non-food product is not subject to the sanctity of the Sabbatical Year.

A *baraita* appears to contradict this: *alei kanim ve-alei gefanim* — leaves of reeds and grape leaves — *liktan le-achilah, yesh bahem mishum kedushat shevi'it* — if gathered for eating, they have *shemittah* sanctity; *le-etzim, ein bahem mishum kedushat shevi'it* — if gathered as firewood, they do not. The principle seems clear: non-food use of vegetation does not generate *shemittah* sanctity, which directly contradicts the ruling about dye plants.

### Lesorah Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁנֵּי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְאׇכְלָה״ – בְּמִי שֶׁהֲנָאָתוֹ וּבִיעוּרוֹ שָׁוִין, יָצְאוּ עֵצִים שֶׁהֲנָאָתָן אַחַר בִּיעוּרָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **answers** the contradiction, as **the verse states:** "And the Sabbath produce of the land shall be **for food** for you" (Leviticus 25:6), indicating that the sanctity of the Sabbatical Year takes effect only **with regard to those** items **whose benefit and whose consumption coincide,** as is the case with regard to food. **Wood is excluded, as its benefit follows its consumption.** The primary purpose of kindling wood is not accomplished with the burning of the wood; rather, it is with the charcoal that heats the oven. Therefore, wood is not subject to the sanctity of the Sabbatical Year.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction through a verse: *ve-hayetah shabbat ha-aretz lachem le-ochlah* — the resting of the land shall be for you to eat. The word *le-ochlah* limits *shemittah* sanctity to things whose benefit is comparable to the experience of eating. This does not mean only literal food; it means any use whose benefit comes *in the manner* of eating.

### Benefit Timing

What characterizes eating as a mode of benefit? *Be-hana'ato u-vi'uro shavim* — the pleasure and the destruction occur simultaneously. When you eat food, you derive your benefit at the very moment the food is being consumed and destroyed. Firewood fails this test: *hana'atan achar bi'uran* — the primary benefit from firewood comes after the wood has been destroyed. When wood burns, the useful heat for cooking comes from the coals — from the part of the wood that has already been consumed — not from the wood that is presently burning. The benefit is displaced from the moment of destruction.

Dye plants, by contrast, pass the test. As the raw plant material is processed — boiled, immersed, broken down — the vegetation is being destroyed at the very moment the dye is being produced. The benefit (obtaining the dye) and the destruction (of the plant) are simultaneous. This is analogous to eating. Therefore, despite being inedible, dye plants have *kedushat shemittah*.

### Rashi Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: וְעֵצִים לְהַסָּקָה – תַּנָּאֵי הִיא
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: And** the matter of whether **kindling wood,** whose benefit follows its consumption, is subject to the sanctity of the Sabbatical Year **is** a dispute among the ***tanna'im*.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* differ on exactly when the benefit of dyeing occurs, and therefore on precisely how the simultaneity principle is satisfied. *Rashi* says: *be-sha'at retichat ha-yoreh, kalah ha-shoresh ve-koleit ha-tzeva* — at the moment the dyepot boils, the plant material is destroyed and the dye is produced. The benefit is having the dye; the destruction is the dissolution of the raw ingredient; these happen at the same time. The benefit of dye plants is therefore realized during the production of the dye itself, at the moment of processing.

*Tosafot* offers a different explanation: *hana'ato u-vi'uro shaveh* — the benefit and the destruction are simultaneous — *de-hana'ato k'she-lovshan* — because the benefit of the dye comes when one wears the garment. As the garment is worn, the dye is gradually being used up or worn away. Therefore, at the moment of wearing, both the benefit and the gradual destruction are occurring together. *Tosafot*'s position is that the benefit is not the production of the dye but the act of wearing the colored garment.

This last position strikes many as counterintuitive. In practice, dye tends to outlast the garment itself; it is difficult to say that wearing a garment constitutes the simultaneous destruction of the dye within it. *Rashi*'s understanding — that the benefit comes at the moment of processing — aligns more naturally with how dyes are actually produced. But the underlying logic in either case serves to distinguish dye plants from firewood and to bring them within the scope of *le-ochlah*, explaining why they carry *kedushat shemittah* despite being inedible.
