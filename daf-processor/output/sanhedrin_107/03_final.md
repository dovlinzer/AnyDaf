# Sanhedrin 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Doeg's Three Judgments

### Doeg's Three Punishments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה מַלְאֲכֵי חַבָּלָה נִזְדַּמְּנוּ לוֹ לְדוֹאֵג, אֶחָד שֶׁשִּׁכַּח תַּלְמוּדוֹ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׂרַף נִשְׁמָתוֹ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁפִּיזֵּר עֲפָרוֹ בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבְבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Three angels of destruction encountered Doeg: One who caused him to forget his Torah** knowledge, **one who burned his soul, and one who dispersed the ashes of** his soul **in synagogues and in study halls** to be trampled beneath the feet of the righteous.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *shlosha malachei chavala nizdamnu lo le'Doeg* — three destructive angels chanced upon Doeg the Edomite. One caused him to forget his Torah learning, one burned his soul, and one scattered his ashes in *batei knesiyot uvavatei midrashot*. The imagery is striking — Doeg, who so thoroughly undermined David's life, ends with his very dust scattered throughout the holiest spaces. There is something almost paradoxical in this: the element that was Doeg was not erased from the world but dispersed into its holiest spaces.

### Doeg & Achitofel Apart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דּוֹאֵג וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל לֹא רָאוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה. דּוֹאֵג בִּימֵי שָׁאוּל, וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל בִּימֵי דָּוִד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Doeg and Ahithophel did not see one another,** as both died at a young age. **Doeg** lived **in the days of Saul, and Ahithophel** lived **in the days of David,** toward the end of David's life.

Rabbi Yochanan further notes: *Doeg ve'Achitofel lo rau ze et ze* — Doeg and Achitofel never encountered one another. This is fortunate for David, because had these two forces converged simultaneously, he would not have survived. Doeg operated during the lifetime of Saul, while Achitofel appeared later, during David's own reign.

### Half-Lives of Evil Men

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דּוֹאֵג וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל לֹא חָצוּ יְמֵיהֶם. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים וּמִרְמָה לֹא יֶחֱצוּ יְמֵיהֶם״. כׇּל שְׁנוֹתָיו שֶׁל דּוֹאֵג לֹא הָיוּ אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאַרְבַּע, וְשֶׁל אֲחִיתוֹפֶל אֵינָן אֶלָּא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Doeg and Ahithophel did not reach half of their** allotted **days,** as they died before the age of thirty-five, half of the standard lifetime mentioned in the verse: "The days of our years are seventy years" (Psalms 90:10). **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **"Bloody and deceitful men shall not live half their days"** (Psalms 55:24); **all the years of Doeg were only thirty-four, and** the years **of Ahithophel were only thirty-three.**

Rabbi Yochanan adds that neither Doeg nor Achitofel lived beyond half their years. A *baraita* corroborates this: *anshei damim umirma lo yechetzu yemeihem* — men of bloodshed and deceit will not live out half their days. Doeg lived only thirty-four years; Achitofel only thirty-three.

This teaching is, of course, complicated. We are fully aware that righteous people sometimes die young, and that wicked people sometimes live long lives. The Gemara's intent is worth noting without pressing too hard for a tidy resolution: there is some understanding that righteousness carries with it a blessing of longevity, and its absence is reflected in a shortened life. Let us note it and proceed.

## David & Achitofel

### Three Titles Descent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִלָּה קָרָא דָּוִד לַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל רַבּוֹ, וּלְבַסּוֹף קְרָאוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ, וּלְבַסּוֹף קְרָאוֹ תַּלְמִידוֹ. בַּתְּחִלָּה קְרָאוֹ רַבּוֹ – ״וְאַתָּה אֱנוֹשׁ כְּעֶרְכִּי אַלּוּפִי וּמְיֻדָּעִי״. וּלְבַסּוֹף קְרָאוֹ חֲבֵרוֹ – ״אֲשֶׁר יַחְדָּו נַמְתִּיק סוֹד בְּבֵית אֱלֹהִים נְהַלֵּךְ בְּרָגֶשׁ״. וּלְבַסּוֹף קְרָאוֹ תַּלְמִידוֹ – ״גַּם אִישׁ שְׁלוֹמִי אֲשֶׁר בָּטַחְתִּי בוֹ
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Initially, David called Ahithophel his teacher, and eventually, he called him his colleague, and ultimately, he called him his student. Initially,** David **called** Ahithophel **his teacher,** as it is stated: **"But it was you, a man my equal, my master [*alufi*], and my familiar friend"** (Psalms 55:14); a teacher is known as *aluf* as he trains [*me'alef* ] his students. **And eventually, he called him his colleague,** as it is stated: **"We took sweet counsel together, and walked to the house of God with the throng"** (Psalms 55:15); the term together indicates that they were equals. **And ultimately, he called him his student,** as it is stated: **"Even my own familiar friend, in whom I trusted,**

Rabbi Yochanan observes a striking descent in how David refers to Achitofel across three Biblical verses. Initially David called him *rabo* — his teacher and master. Later he called him *chaveiro* — his friend. And still later he called him *talmido* — his student.

The *pasuk* supporting the first: *ata enosh ke'erki alufi umyudai* — you are like my teacher, the one who gives me knowledge. The second title, "friend," emerges from the continuation of that same *pasuk*: *asher yachdav namtik sod beveis elokim nehalech beragesh* — together we share secrets and walk in the house of God. And for the third, "student," the Gemara cites: *gam ish shlomi asher batachti vo ochel lachmi higdil alay akev* — the man of my peace whom I trusted, who ate my bread.

### Betrayal of Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹכֵל לַחְמִי הִגְדִּיל עָלַי עָקֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **who did eat of my bread, has lifted his heel against me"** (Psalms 41:10). Bread is a metaphor for Torah knowledge.

The end of that final *pasuk* is telling: *higdil alay akev* — he raised his heel against me. This is the sensitive spot David identifies — Achitofel had such intimate access, such a deep place in David's confidence, that when he betrayed that trust, the wound was uniquely devastating. Of course David had trusted him so completely; otherwise Achitofel's counsel would never have carried such weight in his life.

## David's Trial Request

### Temptation Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם אַל יָבִיא אָדָם עַצְמוֹ לִידֵי נִסָּיוֹן, שֶׁהֲרֵי דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל הֵבִיא עַצְמוֹ לִידֵי נִסָּיוֹן וְנִכְשָׁל. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִפְּנֵי מָה אוֹמְרִים ״אֱלֹהֵי אַבְרָהָם, אֱלֹהֵי יִצְחָק וֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״, וְאֵין אוֹמְרִים ״אֱלֹהֵי דָוִד״? אָמַר: אִינְהוּ מִינְּסוּ לִי וְאַתְּ לָא מִינַּסֵּית לִי. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, בְּחָנֵנִי וְנַסֵּנִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּחָנֵנִי ה׳ וְנַסֵּנִי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos Ahithophel, the Gemara relates the events that led to his death. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A person should never bring himself to** undergo **an ordeal, as David, king of Israel, brought himself to** undergo **an ordeal and failed.** David **said before** God: **Master of the Universe, for what** reason **does one say** in prayer: **God of Abraham, God of Isaac, and God of Jacob, and one does not say: God of David?** God **said** to David: **They have undergone ordeals before Me, and you have not undergone an ordeal before Me.** David **said before Him: Examine me and subject me to an ordeal, as it is stated: "Examine me, Lord, and subject me to an ordeal;** try my kidneys and my heart" (Psalms 26:2).

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *le'olam al yavi adam atzmo lidei nisayon* — a person should never bring himself to be tested. The proof: David, King of Israel, brought himself to be tested and he failed. This is the lens through which the Gemara examines the entire episode that follows.

It is very common, when approaching the story of David and Bathsheba, to read it with knowing eyes — as though we already understand what happened and why. The effort here will be to resist that reflex and to follow the commentaries in searching for a more nuanced understanding of what the *nisayon* was and where exactly David's *chet* lay. Perfect answers may not be available, but the questions are worth holding.

### Nisayon Etymology

What is this word *nisayon*? One explanation connects it to the Hebrew word *nes* — a flag or banner. A *nisayon* is meant to be something that elevates and displays a person's greatness, not something that causes failure. A teacher does not quiz students on material they do not know; questions are posed so that students can demonstrate what they have mastered, raising their esteem and strengthening the teacher's reputation. The *nisayon* of the Patriarchs — *v'HaElokim nisa es Avraham* — was of this character. It was meant to show their success, not to expose them.

### David's Prayer Question

How did David bring the *nisayon* upon himself? The Gemara relates: David said before God, *mipnei mah omrim Elokei Avraham Elokei Yitzchak v'Elokei Yaakov v'ein omrim Elokei David?* Why does the prayer invoke the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob — but not the God of David?

The *chutzpah* of this request is already a kind of self-endangerment. Some suggest David could foresee that his name would appear somewhere in the *tefillah* — he is associated with *Magen David*, and appears in the *Haftarah* blessings — but the request here is more specific. David wants to be the fourth wheel of that *merkavah*, so to speak: if Abraham is *chesed*, Isaac is *gevurah*, and Jacob is *tiferet*, then *malchus* — the fourth *sefirah* — should be his.

### God's Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: מְינַסֵּינָא לָךְ, וְעָבֵידְנָא מִילְּתָא בַּהֲדָךְ, דִּלְדִידְהוּ לָא הוֹדַעְתִּינְהוּ, וְאִילּוּ אֲנָא קָא מוֹדַעְנָא לָךְ דִּמְנַסֵּינָא לָךְ בִּדְבַר עֶרְוָה. מִיָּד, ״וַיְהִי לְעֵת הָעֶרֶב וַיָּקׇם דָּוִד מֵעַל מִשְׁכָּבוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** God **said** to him: **I will subject you to an ordeal, and I will perform a matter for you** that I did not perform for the Patriarchs, **as for them, I did not inform them** of the nature of the ordeal, **while I am informing you that I will subject you to an ordeal involving a matter of** a married woman, with whom **relations** are **forbidden. Immediately,** it is written: **"And it came to pass one evening that David rose from his bed"** (II Samuel 11:2).

God responds: the Patriarchs were tested by Me, and you have not yet been tested. David then says: *bachaneni Hashem venaseni* — O God, test me and try me. And the Gemara reports that God agreed — but did something for David that He had not done for the Patriarchs: He told David in advance both that a test was coming and that it would be in the domain of *ervah*, of sexual matters. Every possible forewarning was given.

The human side of us acknowledges that even knowing a test is coming, one can still fail. But when we speak of David HaMelech — the author of *Tehillim*, the founder of our Messianic dynasty — we must recognize that we are not dealing with an ordinary person, and that our reading of what follows must accordingly be more careful.

## David & Bathsheba

### Evening Rising

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שֶׁהָפַךְ מִשְׁכָּבוֹ שֶׁל לַיְלָה לְמִשְׁכָּבוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם, וְנִתְעַלְּמָה מִמֶּנּוּ הֲלָכָה. אֵבֶר קָטָן יֵשׁ בָּאָדָם, מַשְׂבִּיעוֹ – רָעֵב, וּמַרְעִיבוֹ – שָׂבֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says:** Once David heard the nature of his ordeal, he sought to prevent himself from experiencing lust. **He transformed his nighttime bed into his daytime bed,** i.e., he engaged in intercourse with his wives during the day, in an attempt to quell his lust. **But a *halakha*,** i.e., a Torah statement, **escaped him: There is a small limb in man** that he employs in sexual intercourse. If **he starves** the limb, and does not overindulge, it **is satiated; but** if **he satiates** the limb and overindulges in sexual intercourse, it **is starving,** and desires more. Therefore, his plan had the opposite effect.

Immediately following, the Gemara cites the *pasuk* from Shmuel: *vayehi le'et ha'erev vayakom David me'al mishkavo vayit'halech al gag beit hamelech vayar isha rochetzet me'al hagag veha'isha tovat mareh me'od* — it was at the time of evening and David rose from his bed and walked upon the roof of the king's house, and he saw a woman bathing from the roof, and the woman was very beautiful.

Several features of this *pasuk* deserve attention if we wish to read it charitably. Why is David rising in the evening? One favorable explanation is that the moment David learned he would be tested in matters of *ervah*, he resolved to invert his schedule entirely — to be awake and moving through the world at the hour when others sleep, deliberately placing himself in a situation of constant disorientation so as to avoid the ordinary circumstances that might lead to temptation.

What is he doing on the roof? He is the king — he could walk in the streets to great acclaim. Yet he chooses the roof, perhaps seeking solitude, perhaps preparing to compose *Tehillim*.

### Forgotten Halakha

Rav Yehuda says that David *hafach mishkavo lailah lemishkavo shel yom* — he switched his sleep schedule, converting the nighttime to his daytime and vice versa. But in doing so, he forgot one *halakha*: *eivar katan yesh ba'adam masbi'o ra'ev umar'ivo savei'a* — there is a small limb in a person: when it is satiated it is hungry, and when it is starved it is satisfied. The Gemara's reference, of course, is to the sexual drive. Rashi, in his second comment on the page, understands that David had relations with his wife as part of this switched schedule — and yet, as the Gemara implies, he had still not fully reckoned with this dimension of human nature.

### Roof Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּתְהַלֵּךְ עַל גַּג בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיַּרְא אִשָּׁה רוֹחֶצֶת מֵעַל הַגָּג וְהָאִשָּׁה טוֹבַת מַרְאֶה מְאֹד״. בַּת שֶׁבַע הֲוָה קָא חָיְיפָא רֵישָׁא תּוּתֵי חַלְּתָא. אֲתָא שָׂטָן, אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּצִיפַּרְתָּא. פְּתַק בֵּיהּ גִּירָא, פַּתְקַהּ לְחַלְּתָא, אִיגַּלַּיה וְחַזְיַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And he walked upon the roof of the king's house; from the roof he saw a woman bathing, and the woman was very fair to look upon"** (II Samuel 11:2). **Bathsheba was shampooing her head behind a beehive,** which concealed her from sight. **Satan came and appeared to** David **as a bird.** David **shot an arrow at** the bird, the arrow **severed the beehive,** Bathsheba **was exposed, and** David **saw her.**

The Gemara then addresses the vision on the roof. *Vayit'halech al gag beit hamelech* — another strange detail. David is the king; his palace roof would not ordinarily afford him a close view of another person bathing on an adjacent rooftop. What exactly does he see?

The Gemara relates: *ata Satan idmei lei k'tziporta* — Satan appeared to David in the form of a bird. *Patak bei gira patka l'chalta* — David shot an arrow at the bird, missed, and the lattice or screen below was knocked away — and through the opening, he sees the woman.

There is obviously more here than can be fully unpacked. The arrow is itself a striking image: elsewhere in *Tanakh*, David's weapon is a sling and a stone. When offered a sword, he declines. The imagery of David with an arrow shooting at a bird is not the ordinary David. One bird. One arrow. This does not read as an incidental scene. The image is chosen deliberately to signal that something deeply unusual is occurring here, something that strains the ordinary categories of reading. Satan appearing as a bird, a specific shot at a specific creature — there is prophetic or visionary texture to the scene that our simple reading misses.

### Woman Identified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח דָּוִד וַיִּדְרֹשׁ לָאִשָּׁה וַיֹּאמֶר הֲלוֹא זֹאת בַּת שֶׁבַע בַּת אֱלִיעָם אֵשֶׁת אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי. וַיִּשְׁלַח דָּוִד מַלְאָכִים וַיִּקָּחֶהָ וַתָּבוֹא אֵלָיו וַיִּשְׁכַּב עִמָּהּ וְהִיא מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת מִטֻּמְאָתָהּ וַתָּשׇׁב אֶל בֵּיתָהּ״. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״בָּחַנְתָּ לִבִּי פָּקַדְתָּ לַּיְלָה צְרַפְתַּנִי בַל תִּמְצָא זַמֹּתִי בַל יַעֲבׇר פִּי״. אָמַר: אִיכּוֹ זְמָמָא נְפַל בְּפוּמֵּיהּ דְּמַאן דְּסָנֵי לִי, וְלָא אֲמַר כִּי הָא מִילְּתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately,** it is written: **"And David sent and inquired after the woman. And one said: Is not this Bathsheba, daughter of Eliam, the wife of Uriah the Hittite? And David sent messengers, and took her, and she came to him, and he lay with her, for she was purified from her impurity, and then she returned to her house"** (II Samuel 11:3–4). **And that is** the meaning of that **which is written: "You have proved my heart; You have visited me in the night: You have tried me, but You find nothing; let no presumptuous thought pass my lips"** (Psalms 17:3). David **said: Oh, that a muzzle would have fallen upon the mouth of the one who hates me,** a euphemism for his own mouth, **and I would not have said anything like that** and I would have withstood the ordeal.

After this, the *pasuk* continues: *Vayishlach David vayidrosh l'isha* — David sent someone to inquire about the woman. This too is worth pausing over. A king who simply wanted a beautiful woman he had seen would not bother making it a public matter; he would simply act. The inquiry itself suggests something more deliberate, more conflicted. The messengers return with the answer: *halo zot Batsheva bat Eliam eshet Uriah haChitti* — is this not Bathsheba, daughter of Eliam, wife of Uriah the Hittite?

### Achitofel Connection

Not mentioned directly in the *pasuk*, but introduced here by the Gemara: who is Bathsheba's grandfather? Bathsheba is the granddaughter of Achitofel. The lineage connects the entire story back to the betrayal discussed earlier. This context is not incidental — it is part of why the Gemara has woven these threads together.

### Bathsheba Brought

The narrative continues: *Vayishlach David malachim vayikacheha vatavo eilav vayishkav imah v'hi mitkadeshet mitumatah vatashov el beitah* — David sent messengers and took her; she came to him and he lay with her; she was purifying herself from her ritual impurity; and she returned to her home.

Several issues press in here simultaneously: the question of how old Bathsheba actually is at this point, the fact that her being *mitkadeshet mitumatah* establishes she was not a *niddah*, and the very public character of what David has done. The Gemara does not let these pass without notice.

## David's Confession

### Psalm 139 Tested

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לַמְנַצֵּחַ לְדָוִד בַּה׳ חָסִיתִי אֵיךְ תֹּאמְרוּ לְנַפְשִׁי נוּדִי הַרְכֶם צִפֹּר״? אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מְחוֹל לִי עַל אוֹתוֹ עָוֹן שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ ״הַר שֶׁבָּכֶם צִפּוֹר נִדְּדַתּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "To the leader, of David. In the Lord I put my trust; how can you say to my soul: Flee like a bird to your mountain"** (Psalms 11:1)? **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, pardon me for that sin** with Bathsheba so **that** the wicked people **will not say: The mountain that is among you,** i.e., the luminary of the generation, David, **was driven** from the world due to **a bird** that led to his transgression.

The Gemara now turns to David's own response, expressed through *Tehillim*. The verse: *bachanta libi pakadta layla tzeraftani bal timtza zamoti bal yaavor pi* — You examined my heart, You came to me at night, You put me through fire as one purifies gold, You did not find me wanting — and yet: I wish a muzzle had fallen upon my mouth and I had not said what I said.

What is it that David wishes he had not said? He wishes he had never asked to be tested. *Eiko zamama nafal b'pumia demantsani li velo amar ki hamiltah* — I wish something had blocked my mouth so that I would not have made that request.

### Bird Imagery

Rava expounds another verse from *Tehillim*: *lamnatzeiach ledavid bashem chasiti eich tomeru lenafshi nudi herchem tzippor* — in You, God, I take refuge; how can you say to my soul, flee like a bird to the mountain? Here David is asking God for forgiveness for that transgression, *shelo yomru har shebachem tzippor nadedato* — so that people will not say, the great mountain among you, a bird managed to overcome him.

### To You Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״לְךָ לְבַדְּךָ חָטָאתִי וְהָרַע בְּעֵינֶיךָ עָשִׂיתִי לְמַעַן תִּצְדַּק בְּדׇבְרֶךָ תִּזְכֶּה בְשׇׁפְטֶךָ״? אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: גַּלְיָא וִידִיעָא קַמָּךְ, דְּאִי בְּעַאי לְמִכְפְּיֵיהּ לְיִצְרַי הֲוָה כָּיֵיפְינָא, אֶלָּא אָמֵינָא דְּלָא לֵימְרוּ ״עַבְדָּא זַכְיֵהּ לְמָרֵיהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Against You, only You, have I sinned, and done what is evil in Your eyes; that You are justified when You speak, and right when You judge"** (Psalms 51:6)? **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: It is revealed and known before You that if I sought to suppress my** evil **inclination, I would have suppressed it; but I said:** I will sin, so **that they will not say a servant overcame his master** and withstood the ordeal even though God said that he would not.

Rava expounds yet another verse: *lecha levadecha chatati vehara beeinecha asiti lemaan titzdak bedvarecha tizke beshoftecha* — to You alone have I sinned, and what is evil in Your eyes I have done, so that You will be justified in Your word and pure in Your judgment.

This is an intense passage. David says, "to You alone I have sinned" — he could have said he sinned against Bathsheba, against Uriah, against others. Instead he declares that his transgression is before God alone: *lecha levadecha chatati*. And he adds: *vehara beeinecha asiti lemaan titzdak* — I did this so that You would be right in Your judgment.

### Overcame Inclination

David's reasoning here is remarkable: had he suppressed his inclination and resisted the test despite God's declaration that he would fail, it would appear as though the servant had overcome his Master. So he intentionally failed to preserve God's credibility and word.

Each morning we pray *bechof yitzri* — bend my *yetzer* — and David maintained that he had the capacity to do exactly that. Yet he refrained, so as not to appear greater than God's word.

### Destined Through Pain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב, ״וּבְצַלְעִי שָׂמְחוּ וְנֶאֱסְפוּ נֶאֶסְפוּ עָלַי נֵכִים [וְלֹא יָדַעְתִּי] קָרְעוּ וְלֹא דָמּוּ״? אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, גָּלוּי וְיָדוּעַ לְפָנֶיךָ שֶׁאִם הָיוּ קוֹרְעִין בְּשָׂרִי לֹא הָיָה דָּמִי שׁוֹתֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And when I limped they rejoiced and gathered, the wretched gather themselves together against me, and those whom I know not; they tore and did not cease [*dammu*]"** (Psalms 35:15)? **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe. It is revealed and known before you that if** my enemies **were to tear my flesh, my blood [*dami*] would not flow to the ground,** due to excessive fasting (see II Samuel 12:16–17).

Rava expounds a verse from *Tehillim*: *ki ani letzela nachon umachovi negdi tamid* — I am prepared for stumbling, and my pain is always before You. What is this *machov*, this pain? The Gemara answers: *reuyah hayetah Batsheva bat Eliam leDavid misheshet yemei bereshit ella sheba'ah elav bemachov* — Bathsheba was destined for David from the six days of Creation, but she came to him through pain. If that is not a heartbreaking statement, it is hard to know what is.

### Too Early

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: רְאוּיָה הָיְתָה לְדָוִד בַּת שֶׁבַע בַּת אֱלִיעָם, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֲכָלָהּ פַּגָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise, the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Bathsheba, daughter of Eliam, was** designated as **fit for David, but he partook of her unripe,** before the appointed time. David would have ultimately married her in a permitted manner after the death of Uriah.

A related teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *reuyah hayetah leDavid Batsheva bat Eliam ella she'achalah pagah* — she was indeed destined for David, but he consumed the fruit before it was ripe. There was a right time; he did not wait for it.

## Eighteen Wives

### Eighteen Duties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת חׇלְיוֹ שֶׁל דָּוִד קִיֵּם שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה עוֹנוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָגַעְתִּי בְּאַנְחָתִי אַשְׂחֶה בְכׇל לַיְלָה מִטָּתִי בְּדִמְעָתִי עַרְשִׂי אַמְסֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Even during the time of his illness he fulfilled** the mitzva of **conjugal rights for eighteen** wives, **as it is stated: "I am weary with my groaning; every night I speak in my bed; I melt away my couch with tears"** (Psalms 6:7). Even when he was weary and groaning he still spoke in his bed, a euphemism for sexual intercourse.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: even during the period of David's illness, he fulfilled *shemona esreh onot* — eighteen *onot*, meaning he maintained his marital obligations to his eighteen wives. This is stated even while he was ill — a testament to his sustained commitment.

### Test Question

This raises an obvious question: if David had eighteen wives and was diligently fulfilling his marital obligations even while ill, how could Bathsheba have held any pull over him? This is precisely the point. The test was not about deprivation; David was not lacking. The *nisayon* was something else — and the question of exactly what that something else was remains deliberately open.

It is worth noting, almost as an aside, that while the Gemara speaks of eighteen wives, *Tanakh* records only six. The discrepancy is unresolved here, but the thrust of the teaching stands: David was not a man in want.

## Idolatry Attempt

### Chushai Dissuades

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בִּקֵּשׁ דָּוִד לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי דָּוִד בָּא עַד הָרֹאשׁ אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה שָׁם לֵאלֹהִים״, וְאֵין ״רֹאשׁ״ אֶלָּא עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוּא צַלְמָא רֵישֵׁיהּ דִּי דְהַב טָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: David sought to engage in idol worship** during Absalom's coup, **as it is stated: "And it came to pass when David was at the top [*rosh*] of the ascent, where he would bow to God"** (II Samuel 15:32), **and *rosh*** means **nothing other than idol worship, as it is stated: "As for that image, its head [*reishei*] was of fine gold"** (Daniel 2:32).

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *bikesh David la'avod avoda zara* — David sought to engage in *avodah zarah*. Some understand this as David reasoning through worst-case scenarios rather than as a genuine theological defection, but the Gemara derives it from a *pasuk*: *Vayehi David ba ad harosh asher yishtachave sham l'elohim* — David came to the summit where he would bow down to God. And the Gemara notes that *rosh* is associated with *avodah zarah*, as in the verse about the golden statue whose head was of fine gold.

### Offering Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהִנֵּה לִקְרָאתוֹ חוּשַׁי הָאַרְכִּי קָרוּעַ כֻּתׇּנְתּוֹ וַאֲדָמָה עַל רֹאשׁוֹ״. אָמַר לוֹ לְדָוִד: יֹאמְרוּ מֶלֶךְ שֶׁכְּמוֹתְךָ יַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? אָמַר לוֹ: מֶלֶךְ שֶׁכְּמוֹתִי יַהַרְגֶנּוּ בְּנוֹ? מוּטָב יַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְאַל יִתְחַלֵּל שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"Behold Hushai the Archite came to meet him with his coat rent and earth upon his head"** (II Samuel 15:32). Hushai **said to David: Shall they say a king like you will engage in idol worship?** David **said to him:** Is it preferable that they say with regard to **a king like me,** known to be righteous, that **his son will kill him?** David continued, referring to himself in third person: **It is preferable that he shall engage in idol worship and the name of Heaven shall not be desecrated in public** through the murder of a righteous king in this manner.

Coming toward David was *Chushai HaArchi*, with his garment torn and earth upon his head. Chushai said to him: *yomru melech shekmotcha ya'avod avoda zara?* — is it fitting that they should say a king of your stature engaged in idolatry? No conceivable sin is a good option for a king like David; he is the founder of the Messianic dynasty, the one whose descendant will build the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Lesser Evil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: מַאי טַעְמָא קָנְסִיבַתְּ יְפַת תּוֹאַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יְפַת תּוֹאַר רַחֲמָנָא שַׁרְיַיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא דָּרְשַׁתְּ סְמוּכִין, דִּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״כִּי יִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה״? כׇּל הַנּוֹשֵׂא יְפַת תּוֹאַר יֵשׁ לוֹ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Hushai **said** to him: **What is the reason** that **you married a beautiful woman,** the mother of Absalom? David **said to him:** With regard to **a beautiful woman, the Merciful One permitted** marrying **her.** Hushai **said to him:** But **you did not interpret the juxtaposed** verses, **as juxtaposed to** the portion of the beautiful woman is the portion beginning: **"If a man has a stubborn and rebellious son"** (Deuteronomy 21:18). From that juxtaposition it is derived: **Anyone who marries a beautiful woman has a stubborn and rebellious son.** Therefore, even if Absalom kills you, there will be no desecration of God's name, as the people will attribute his actions to his mother.

David responds to Chushai: *melech shekmoti yehargenu bno?* — is it fitting that a son should kill a king like me? He reasons: *mutav ya'avod avoda zara v'al yitchalel shem shamayim b'farhesya* — better that there be some private explanation for what is happening to him, rather than that God's name be desecrated publicly through the spectacle of a son murdering his father the king.

David then suggests another possible explanation for the people to latch onto: *mai tama kenesivat yifat to'ar* — perhaps they will say it all traces back to the *yifat to'ar*, the beautiful captive woman that the Torah permits a soldier to take. Chushai responds: *yifat to'ar rachmana sharyei* — but God permitted the *yifat to'ar*. David then presses: did you not learn the principle of *smuchin*, of adjacent passages? The Torah's passage of *yifat to'ar* is immediately juxtaposed with the passage of *ben sorer umoreh* — the rebellious son. *Kol hanose yifat to'ar yesh lo ben sorer umoreh* — whoever takes a *yifat to'ar* may end up with a rebellious son. David has a rebellious son — Absalom — and perhaps the *yifat to'ar* has something to do with the chain of events.

### Unerasable Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי דְּמִן בֵּירֵי: לְמָה דָּוִד דּוֹמֶה? לְסוֹחֵר כּוּתִי. אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, ״שְׁגִיאוֹת מִי יָבִין״! [אֲמַר לֵיהּ:] שְׁבִיקִי לָךְ. ״וּמִנִּסְתָּרוֹת נַקֵּנִי״! שְׁבִיקִי לָךְ. ״גַּם מִזֵּדִים חֲשֹׂךְ עַבְדֶּךָ״! שְׁבִיקִי לָךְ. ״אַל יִמְשְׁלוּ בִי אָז אֵיתָם״, דְּלָא לִישְׁתַּעוֹ בִּי רַבָּנַן. שְׁבִיקִי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Dostai from Biri taught: To what is David comparable?** He is comparable **to a Samaritan merchant,** who incrementally lowers the price until the buyer agrees to purchase the merchandise. **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe: "Who can discern his errors"** (Psalms 19:13), i.e., forgive me for the unwitting sins that I committed. God **said to him: They are forgiven for you.** David asked more: **"Cleanse me from hidden faults"** (Psalms 19:13), i.e., pardon me for transgressions that I committed in private, even if I performed them intentionally. God said to him: **They are forgiven for you.** David requested: **"Keep back your servant also from intentional sins"** (Psalms 19:14). God said to him: **They are forgiven for you.** David requested: **"Let them not have dominion over me, then I shall be faultless"** (Psalms 19:14), and I further request **that the Sages will not speak of me** and condemn me. God said to him: **They are forgiven for you.**

David pleads before God: *shegi'ot mi yavin* — who can understand his own errors? God responds: *shviki lach* — forgiven. He continues: *u'mistarot nakeni* — cleanse me of hidden transgressions. *Shviki lach* — forgiven. *Gam mi'zeidim chasoch avdecha* — hold back Your servant also from intentional sins. *Shviki lach* — forgiven. *Al yimshelu vi* — let them not rule over me — he asks that the rabbis in future generations not endlessly rehearse his sins. *Shviki lach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנִקֵּיתִי מִפֶּשַׁע רָב״, שֶׁלֹּא יִכָּתֵב סִרְחוֹנִי. אָמַר לוֹ: אִי אֶפְשָׁר. וּמָה יוֹד שֶׁנָּטַלְתִּי מִשָּׂרַי עוֹמֵד וְצוֹוֵחַ כַּמָּה שָׁנִים, עַד שֶׁבָּא יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְהוֹסַפְתִּי לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקְרָא מֹשֶׁה לְהוֹשֵׁעַ בִּן נוּן יְהוֹשֻׁעַ״. כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כּוּלָּהּ – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** David requested: **"And I shall be clear from great transgression"** (Psalms 19:14), meaning **that my transgression** with Bathsheba and Uriah **will not be written** in the Bible. God **said to him:** That is **impossible. And just as** the letter ***yod* that I removed from** the name of **Sarai,** wife of Abraham, when I changed her name to Sarah, was **standing and screaming several years** over its omission from the Bible **until Joshua came and I added** the *yod* **to his** name, **as it is stated: "And Moses called Hosea, son of Nun, Joshua [*Yehoshua*]"** (Numbers 13:16); **the entire portion** of your transgression, which is fit to be included in the Bible, **all the more so** it cannot be omitted.

Then David adds: *v'nikiti mi'pesha rav* — let me be clean of great transgression, let my iniquity not be written down, let my *sirchon* not be made famous across the generations. And here God says: *iy efshar* — this I cannot do. Things leave marks that endure across time. Consider: the single letter *yud* taken from Sarai when her name was changed to Sarah — that little *yud* stood and cried out for many years, until it was finally restored when Moses renamed Hoshea as Yehoshua, as the verse states: *vayikra Moshe l'Hoshea bin Nun Yehoshua*. If a single letter of the *Torah* cries out for years, how much more so this entire episode — *kol ha'parsha kula al achat kama v'chama*.

### Solomon's Proverb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנִקֵּיתִי מִפֶּשַׁע רָב״. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מְחוֹל לִי עַל אוֹתוֹ עָוֹן כּוּלּוֹ! אָמַר, כְּבָר עָתִיד שְׁלֹמֹה בִּנְךָ לוֹמַר בְּחׇכְמָתוֹ: ״הֲיַחְתֶּה אִישׁ אֵשׁ בְּחֵיקוֹ וּבְגָדָיו לֹא תִשָּׂרַפְנָה. אִם יְהַלֵּךְ אִישׁ עַל הַגֶּחָלִים וְרַגְלָיו לֹא תִכָּוֶינָה. כֵּן הַבָּא עַל אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ לֹא יִנָּקֶה כׇּל הַנֹּגֵעַ בָּהּ״. אָמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל הָכִי נִטְרַד הָהוּא גַּבְרָא? אָמַר לוֹ: קַבֵּל עָלֶיךָ יִסּוּרִין. קִבֵּל עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And I shall be clear from great transgression"** (Psalms 19:14). David **said before** God: **Master of the Universe, pardon me for this sin.** God **said** to him: **Your son Solomon is already destined to say with his wisdom: "Can a man take fire in his lap and his garments not be burned? Can one walk on hot coals and his feet not be scorched? So too one who lies with his neighbor's wife; anyone who touches her shall not go unpunished"** (Proverbs 6:27–29). David **said to Him: Will that man,** David, **be expelled** for **that entire** transgression, with no remedy? God **said to** David: **Accept upon yourself afflictions,** and that will atone for your sins. **He accepted** afflictions **upon himself.**

David presses further: *mechol li al oto avon kulo* — please forgive me entirely. God responds: your son Solomon is going to say in his wisdom — *hayichteh ish esh b'cheiko u'vegadav lo tisarena? im yehalech ish al gechalim v'raglav lo tichvena? ken ha'ba al eshet re'ehu lo yinakeh kol ha'nogei'a bah* — can a man place fire in his pocket without his clothes catching flame? Can a man walk on hot coals without his feet being burned? So too, one who comes to another man's wife — he will not be acquitted. Much is being cleaned, God implies — after all, Solomon himself, the one who will speak these very words, is Bathsheba's son. That itself is a form of rectification. But not the whole thing.

God then tells David: *kabel alecha yissurin* — accept afflictions upon yourself. And David accepted.

## Six Months Leprosy

### Metzora Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים נִצְטָרַע דָּוִד, וְנִסְתַּלְּקָה הֵימֶנּוּ שְׁכִינָה, וּפֵירְשׁוּ מִמֶּנּוּ סַנְהֶדְרִין. נִצְטָרַע – דִּכְתִיב: ״תְּחַטְּאֵנִי בְאֵזוֹב וְאֶטְהָר תְּכַבְּסֵנִי וּמִשֶּׁלֶג אַלְבִּין״. נִסְתַּלְּקָה הֵימֶנּוּ שְׁכִינָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״הָשִׁיבָה לִּי שְׂשׂוֹן יִשְׁעֶךָ וְרוּחַ נְדִיבָה תִסְמְכֵנִי״. וּפֵרְשׁוּ מִמֶּנּוּ סַנְהֶדְרִין – דִּכְתִיב: ״יָשׁוּבוּ לִי יְרֵאֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** For **six months David was afflicted with leprosy and the Divine Presence abandoned him and** the members of **the Sanhedrin dissociated** themselves **from him. He was afflicted with leprosy, as it is stated: "Purge me with hyssop and I shall be clean; wash me, and I shall be whiter than snow"** (Psalms 51:9), indicating that he required purification like a leper. **The Divine Presence abandoned him, as it is stated: "Restore me to joy of Your salvation; and uphold me with a willing spirit"** (Psalms 51:14). **And** the members of **the Sanhedrin dissociated** themselves **from him, as it is stated: "Let those who fear You turn to me,** and those who have known Your testimonies" (Psalms 119:79).

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *shisha chodashim nitztarei David v'nistalka heimeinu Shechina u'farshu heimeinu Sanhedrin* — for six months David was a *metzora*, the *Shechina* departed from him, and the Sanhedrin withdrew from him.

The evidence for the *tzara'at*: the verse in *Tehillim*, *techat'eini b'eizov v'et'har techabseini u'misheleg albin* — purge me with hyssop and I will be clean, wash me and I will be whiter than snow — employs the very terminology and procedure used for the *metzora*, making clear that David underwent that process. The departure of the *Shechina* is derived from: *hashiva li sason yishecha v'ru'ach nediva tismacheini* — return to me the joy of Your salvation and support me with a generous spirit — which implies that the spirit had left. And the withdrawal of the Sanhedrin is derived from: *yashuvu li yerei'echa* — let Your fearers return to me — implying they had gone away.

### Reign Discrepancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַיָּמִים אֲשֶׁר מָלַךְ דָּוִד עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive that this lasted for **six months?** It is derived **as it is written: "And the days that David reigned over Israel were forty years;**

How do we know the duration was precisely six months? The verse states that David reigned over Israel forty years. Yet elsewhere it specifies that he reigned in Hebron for seven years and in Jerusalem for thirty-three years — seven plus thirty-three equals only forty. But another verse reads that he reigned in Hebron seven years *and six months*. Those six months are not counted in the total of forty. *Shema mina nitztarei* — we derive from this that those are the months he was a *metzora*, the months that simply did not count.

## Idolatry Attempt (continued)

### Future Forgiveness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מְחוֹל לִי עַל אוֹתוֹ עָוֹן! מָחוּל לָךְ. ״עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי אוֹת לְטוֹבָה וְיִרְאוּ שֹׂנְאַי וְיֵבֹשׁוּ כִּי אַתָּה ה׳ עֲזַרְתַּנִי וְנִחַמְתַּנִי״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּחַיֶּיךָ אֵינִי מוֹדִיעַ, אֲבָל אֲנִי מוֹדִיעַ בְּחַיֵּי שְׁלֹמֹה בִּנְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** David **said before Him** after this: **Master of the Universe, pardon me for this sin.** God said to him: **It is forgiven for you.** David requested: **"Perform on my behalf a sign for good, that they that hate me may see it and be put to shame"** (Psalms 86:17); show me a sign in my lifetime so that everyone will know that You have forgiven me. God **said to him: In your lifetime I will not make** it **known** that you were forgiven, **but I will make** it **known in the lifetime of your son, Solomon.**

Again David pleads before God: *mechol li al oto avon* — please forgive me. God answers: *machul lach* — forgiven. David then asks for a sign — *asei imi ot l'tova v'yiru sonai v'yevoshu* — give me a visible indication that You have forgiven me, so that my enemies will see and be ashamed. God answers: *eini modia b'chayecha aval ani modia bechayei Shlomo bincha* — I will not make it known in your lifetime, but in the lifetime of your son Solomon it will be known.

## Temple Building Sign

### Gates Clung

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁבָּנָה שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, בִּיקֵּשׁ לְהַכְנִיס אָרוֹן לְבֵית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים. דָּבְקוּ שְׁעָרִים זֶה בָּזֶה. אָמַר עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה רְנָנוֹת וְלֹא נַעֲנָה. אָמַר: ״שְׂאוּ שְׁעָרִים רָאשֵׁיכֶם וְהִנָּשְׂאוּ פִּתְחֵי עוֹלָם וְיָבוֹא מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד. מִי זֶה מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד ה׳ עִזּוּז וְגִבּוֹר ה׳ גִּבּוֹר מִלְחָמָה״. וְנֶאֱמַר: ״שְׂאוּ שְׁעָרִים רָאשֵׁיכֶם וּשְׂאוּ פִּתְחֵי עוֹלָם וְיָבוֹא מֶלֶךְ הַכָּבוֹד וְגוֹ׳״, וְלֹא נַעֲנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **When Solomon built the Temple** and **sought to bring the Ark into the Holy of Holies,** the **gates clung together** and could not be opened. Solomon **uttered twenty-four songs** of praise, **and** his prayer **was not answered. He said: "Lift up your heads, you gates, and be you lifted up, you everlasting doors, that the King of glory may come in. Who is the King of glory? The Lord strong and mighty, the Lord mighty in battle"** (Psalms 24:7-8). **And it is stated: "Lift up your heads, you gates, yea, lift them up, you everlasting doors, that the King of glory may come in.** Who then is the King of glory? The Lord of hosts; He is the King of glory. Selah" (Psalms 24:9-10), **and he was not answered.**

The Gemara now narrates the fulfillment of that promise. When Solomon built the *Beit HaMikdash* and sought to bring the Ark into the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, the gates clung together — *davku shearim zeh bazeh* — and refused to open.

### Twenty-Four Songs

Solomon sang twenty-four *renanot* — songs of praise — and received no answer.

### King of Glory Psalm

He then invoked the verse: *s'u she'arim rasheichem v'hinas'u pitchhei olam v'yavo Melech HaKavod* — lift up your heads, O gates, and be lifted, O eternal openings, and let the King of Glory enter.

### Angels' Confusion

The angels mistook Solomon's meaning and thought he was referring to himself — that he, Solomon, was declaring himself *Melech HaKavod*. Solomon immediately clarified with the question: *mi zeh Melech HaKavod?* — who is the King of Glory? — and answered: *Hashem izuz v'gibbor Hashem gibbor milchamah* — it is God, powerful and mighty, God the warrior. He repeated the call — *s'u she'arim rasheichem* — and again received no answer.

### David's Merit Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר: ״ה׳ אֱלֹהִים אַל תָּשֵׁב פְּנֵי מְשִׁיחֶךָ זָכְרָה לְחַסְדֵי דָּוִיד עַבְדֶּךָ״, מִיָּד נַעֲנָה. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נֶהְפְּכוּ פְּנֵי שׂוֹנְאֵי דָּוִד כְּשׁוּלֵי קְדֵירָה, וְיָדְעוּ כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁמָּחַל לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל אוֹתוֹ הֶעָוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Once he said: "O Lord God, turn not away the face of Your anointed; remember the good deeds of David Your servant"** (II Chronicles 6:42), **he was immediately answered,** and the gates opened (II Chronicles 7:1). **At that moment, the faces of all of David's enemies turned** dark **like the** charred **bottom of a pot. And all of the Jewish people knew that the Holy One, Blessed be He, had forgiven him for that sin,** as it was only by David's merit that Solomon's prayer was answered.

Only when Solomon turned to a verse in *Divrei HaYamim* — *Hashem Elokim al tashev pnei meshichcha zachrah l'chasdei David avdecha* — do not turn away the face of Your anointed one, remember the *chesed* of Your servant David — *miyad ne'enah*: he was answered immediately. All of Solomon's own invocations, even those that identified God as *Melech HaKavod*, were not sufficient. It was only the invocation of his father David's merit that opened the gates.

### Forgiveness Known

*B'otah sha'ah nehapchu pnei sonei David k'shulei k'deirah v'yadu kol Yisrael shemachal lo HaKadosh Baruch Hu al oto avon* — at that moment the faces of David's enemies turned dark as the bottom of a pot, and all of Israel knew that the Holy One had forgiven David for that sin.

Consider the weight of what David endured: six months as a *metzora*, the *Shechina* withdrawn, the Sanhedrin dispersed from around him, denied any sign of forgiveness in his own lifetime — not through the restoration of his health, not through being recognized as the fourth pillar of the *merkavah*, nothing. The confirmation came only through his son, through the building of the Temple, through the miracle of the gates. And even then, it came not through Solomon's own merit or his invocations of God's glory, but through the *zechus* of David himself.

One should also pause over a question that the Gemara leaves conspicuously open. If the relationship between David and Bathsheba was as straightforwardly wrong as a surface reading suggests, then the son born of that union would have been a *mamzer*. Yet David prays fervently for the revival of that very son. A *mamzer* cannot become king; a *mamzer* cannot be the progenitor of the continuing Messianic line. David himself was descended from converts, which complicates things further. The Gemara does not resolve this, and perhaps it is not meant to. The text invites us to leave this section with more questions than we arrived with.

## Gechazi's Sin

### Elisha's Mission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּחֲזִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ אֱלִישָׁע דַּמֶּשֶׂק״. לְהֵיכָא אֲזַל? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁהָלַךְ לְהַחֲזִיר גֵּחֲזִי בִּתְשׁוּבָה, וְלֹא חָזַר. אָמַר לוֹ: חֲזוֹר בָּךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִמְּךָ: הַחוֹטֵא וּמַחְטִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים אֵין מַסְפִּיקִין בְּיָדוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **Gehazi,** the attendant of Elisha, has no share in the World-to-Come. The Gemara explains that this is **as it is written: And Elisha went to Damascus** (see II Kings 8:7). **Where did he go,** and for what purpose? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He went to cause Gehazi to repent, but he did not repent.** Elisha **said to him: Repent.** Gehazi **said to him: This** is the tradition that **I received from you: Whoever sins and causes the masses to sin is not given the opportunity to repent.**

The Gemara now turns to Gechazi, the last in the list of individuals who forfeited their share in *olam haba*, and asks what brought him there. The verse states: *vayelech Elisha l'Damesek* — Elisha went to Damascus. Why? Rabbi Yochanan explains: *she'halach le'hachzir Geichazi bi'teshuva ve'lo chazar* — he went to bring Gechazi back in *teshuva*, and Gechazi did not return.

To appreciate the weight of this story, one must recognize who Gechazi was. He stood to Elisha as Joshua stood to Moses — the trusted right hand of a prophet, the person closest to one through whom God worked miracles and spoke directly. How rare a position. How high a person.

### No Repentance

Elisha said to Gechazi: *chazor becha* — repent, return. And Gechazi responded with a teaching he had received from Elisha himself: *kach mekabulani mimcha — ha'chotei u'machti et ha'rabbim ein maspikin be'yado la'asot teshuva* — this I have received from you: one who sins and causes the multitude to sin has no capacity for *teshuva*.

### Carried Sins

On one hand, we affirm that *teshuva* is always available to every person. What is the problem here? The Gemara's image is instructive. Every good deed generates a positive spiritual presence, every bad deed a negative one. One who causes others to sin — *machti et ha'rabbim* — accumulates not only his own negative forces but those generated by all the people he has led astray. Even if he were to fully rectify his own *aveiros*, he would still be carrying the weight of every bad deed done by every person he had misled. That accumulated burden, belonging to others, cannot be shed through his own *teshuva* alone. *Ha'chotei u'machti et ha'rabbim* — it is not that God withholds forgiveness capriciously, but that the spiritual arithmetic makes full rectification inaccessible.

## Naaman & Gifts

### Naaman Arrives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְעוֹלָם תְּהֵא שְׂמֹאל דּוֹחָה וְיָמִין מְקָרֶבֶת, לֹא כֶּאֱלִישָׁע שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְגֵחֲזִי בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, וְלֹא כִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן פְּרַחְיָה שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְיֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Always have the left** hand **drive** sinners **away and the right draw** them **near,** so that the sinner will not totally despair of atonement. This is **unlike Elisha, who pushed away Gehazi with his two hands** and caused him to lose his share in the World-to-Come, **and unlike Yehoshua ben Peraḥya, who pushed away Jesus the Nazarene with his two hands.**

The Gemara recounts the underlying events. Naaman, commander of the Aramean army, suffered from *tzara'at*. A young Israelite girl who had been taken captive told him: if you go to Elisha, he will heal you.

### Jordan Immersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּחֲזִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר נַעֲמָן הוֹאֵל וְקַח כִּכָּרָיִם וַיִּפְרׇץ בּוֹ וַיָּצַר כִּכְּרַיִם כֶּסֶף וְגוֹ׳״. ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו אֱלִישָׁע מֵאַיִן גֵּחֲזִי וַיֹּאמֶר לֹא הָלַךְ עַבְדְּךָ אָנֶה וָאָנָה. וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו לֹא לִבִּי הָלַךְ כַּאֲשֶׁר הָפַךְ אִישׁ מֵעַל מֶרְכַּבְתּוֹ לִקְרָאתֶךָ הַעֵת לָקַחַת אֶת הַכֶּסֶף וְלָקַחַת בְּגָדִים וְזֵיתִים וּכְרָמִים וְצֹאן וּבָקָר וַעֲבָדִים וּשְׁפָחוֹת״. וּמִי שְׁקַל כּוּלֵּי הַאי? כֶּסֶף וּבְגָדִים הוּא דִּשְׁקַל!
>
> **Translation:** Elisha drove **Gehazi** away, **as it is written: "And Naaman said: Be content, take two talents. And he urged him, and bound two talents of silver** in two bags, with two changes of garments" (II Kings 5:23). Naaman offered Gehazi payment for the help Elisha had given him. The verse states: **"And Elisha said to him: Where from, Gehazi? And he said: Your servant went nowhere at all. And he said to him: Went not my heart with you, when the man turned back from his chariot to meet you? Is it the time to receive silver and to receive garments, and olive groves, and vineyards, and sheep and cattle, and menservants and maidservants?"** (II Kings 5:25-26). The Gemara asks: **And did** Gehazi **take all that? It is** merely **silver and garments that he took.**

Elisha directed Naaman to go and immerse in the Jordan. Naaman protested: *achuchai ka mechaychat bi?* — are you mocking me? He had rivers in Damascus; what made the Jordan special? His own attendants counseled him: what do you have to lose? We are already near the Jordan — go, try it. *Azal ve'taval ba'Yarden ve'itnasi* — he went, immersed in the Jordan, and was healed.

### Refused Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה הָיָה אֱלִישָׁע יוֹשֵׁב וְדוֹרֵשׁ בִּשְׁמוֹנָה שְׁרָצִים. נַעֲמָן שַׂר צְבָא מֶלֶךְ אֲרָם הָיָה מְצוֹרָע. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ הָהִיא רָבִיתָא דְּאִישְׁתְּבַאי מֵאַרְעָא יִשְׂרָאֵל: אִי אָזְלַתְּ לְגַבַּי אֱלִישָׁע מַסֵּי לָךְ. כִּי אֲתָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל טְבוֹל בְּיַרְדֵּן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַחוֹכֵי קָא מְחַיְּיכַתְּ בִּי? אָמְרִי לֵיהּ הָנְהוּ דַּהֲווֹ בַּהֲדֵיהּ: מַאי נָפְקָא לָךְ מִינַּהּ? זִיל נַסִּי. אֲזַל וּטְבַל בְּיַרְדְּנָא וְאִיתַּסִּי. אֲתָא אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ כֹּל הָנֵי דְּנָקֵיט. לָא צְבָא לְקַבּוֹלֵי מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** This was the incident involving Gehazi: **At that moment, Elisha was sitting and teaching** the *halakhot* of the **eight** impure **creeping animals.** Now **Naaman, the general of the army of Aram, was a leper. A certain young Jewish woman who had been taken captive from Eretz Yisrael said to him: If you go to Elisha, he will heal you. When** Naaman **came** to him, Elisha **said to him: Go immerse in the Jordan.** Naaman **said to him: Are you mocking me** by suggesting that this will cure me? **Those** companions **who were with** Naaman **said to him: What is the difference to you? Go, try** it. Naaman **went and immersed in the Jordan and was healed.** Naaman **came** and **brought to** Elisha **all those** items **that he had** taken with him from Aram, and Elisha **did not agree to receive** them **from him.**

Naaman then brought all his gifts to Elisha in gratitude. Elisha refused: *lo tzvi lekabulei minei* — he declined to accept any of them. The reasoning, as the Gemara frames it, is that had Naaman come to Elisha as one goes to a physician, Elisha would have had no power to heal him. He came instead as one approaches a prophet — and a prophet channels God's power, not his own. Payment for such healing is impossible to accept; it would be, in effect, collecting payment for something God did.

Rabbi Yitzchak notes that at that very time, Elisha was sitting and expounding the laws of the eight *sheratzim* — the eight loathsome creatures designated by the Torah. The eight items in Naaman's offer that Gechazi subsequently took correspond to those eight: *kesef, begadim, zeitim, kramim, tzon, bakar, avadim, shfachot* — money, garments, olive trees, vineyards, sheep, cattle, male servants, female servants.

### Gechazi Takes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּחֲזִי אִיפְּטַר מִקַּמֵּיהּ אֱלִישָׁע, אֲזַל שְׁקַל מַאי דִּשְׁקַל וְאַפְקֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Gehazi took leave from before Elisha** and **went after Naaman. He collected the gifts that had been offered and deposited them. Gehazi took leave from before Elisha** and **went** and **took** from Naaman **what he took, and** he **deposited** them.

*Gechazi iftar mi'kamei Elisha* — Gechazi took leave of Elisha and went after Naaman. He collected the gifts that had been offered and deposited them. *Azal shakal mai shakal ve'afkid* — he went, took what he took, and stored it away.

### Eight Rewards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא, חַזְיַיהּ אֱלִישָׁע לְצָרַעַת דַּהֲוָה פָּרְחָה עִלָּוֵיה רֵישֵׁיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָשָׁע! הִגִּיעַ עֵת לִיטּוֹל שְׂכַר שְׁמֹנָה שְׁרָצִים. ״וְצָרַעַת נַעֲמָן תִּדְבַּק בְּךָ וּבְזַרְעֲךָ לְעוֹלָם וַיֵּצֵא מִלְּפָנָיו מְצֹרָע כַּשָּׁלֶג״.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Gehazi **came, Elisha saw the leprosy that had grown on** Gehazi's **head.** Elisha **said to him: Wicked one! The time has arrived to take** your **reward for** studying the matter of **the eight creeping animals.** Since the silver Gehazi received was his reward for studying the matter of the eight creeping animals, Elisha enumerated eight items that Gehazi sought to purchase with the silver that he took. Then Elisha said to Gehazi: **"The leprosy of Naaman shall cleave to you and to your seed forever. And he went out of his presence a leper as white as snow"** (II Kings 5:27).

When Gechazi returned, Elisha had a vision: *ki ata chazei Elisha letzaraat d'havah parcha ilu reishah* — Elisha saw, in what appears to have been prophetic sight, *tzara'at* blooming over Gechazi's head. Something was deeply wrong.

## Gechazi's Curse

### Eternal Leprosy

Elisha pronounced to Gechazi: *rasha* — you are wicked. *Higia et litol sachar shmonah sheratzim* — you have taken the payment of those eight despicable creatures. And then: *vetzaraat Naaman tidbak becha u'bezarcha ad olam* — the *tzara'at* of Naaman will cling to you and to your offspring forever.

### Leper Departure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאַרְבָּעָה אֲנָשִׁים הָיוּ מְצֹרָעִים פֶּתַח הַשָּׁעַר״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גֵּחֲזִי וּשְׁלֹשֶׁת בָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And there were four men afflicted with leprosy at the entrance of the gate"** (II Kings 7:3), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** These were **Gehazi and his three sons,** as he and his descendants were cursed.

*Vayetzei lefanav metzora kashelag* — he departed from Elisha's presence white with *tzara'at*. The verse later read as the *haftarah* for *Metzora* mentions: *ve'arba'ah anashim hayu metzoraim petach hasha'ar* — four men with *tzara'at* were sitting at the gate.

Who are these four? Rabbi Yochanan identifies them: *Gechazi u'shloshet banav* — Gechazi and his three sons. The curse of *vetzaraat Naaman tidbak becha u'vezarcha ad olam* — it will cling to you and your offspring forever — extended to his children. It was not only Gechazi himself who bore the consequences, but those who came after him as well.
