# Yoma 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Korban Division

### Tamid Distribution

We pick up with the *mishnah* on 26b, continuing our survey of how the various *avodot* of the morning were distributed among the *kohanim* through the lottery system. We have already discussed the lot drawings for the *tamid* and the *ketoret*, but have not yet addressed the other *korbanot* brought throughout the day.

Before turning to the next *mishnah*, there is one final point from the previous discussion worth revisiting. The *mishnah* had established that the *tamid* was brought by nine *kohanim*, rising to twelve in certain circumstances — on Shabbat, on Sukkot, and the like — depending on what accompanied the animal to the altar. The *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya teaches that sometimes it could reach thirteen, because four *avodot* preceded the carrying of the limbs: the *shechita*, the throwing of the blood, the cleaning of the gold altar, and the cleaning of the *menorah*. These four preceded the carrying of the limbs, so the base of nine-to-twelve in the *mishnah* becomes nine-to-sixteen when those four are added in.

One *baraita* goes even further: *ve-tanya*, it teaches that the number reached seventeen. The *gemara* notes that this view follows Rabbi Yehuda rather than Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that the same *kohanim* who brought the limbs to the *kevesh* (the ramp) would also carry them from the *kevesh* to the top of the altar — no additional personnel. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that there was a fourth lottery, in which different *kohanim* would take the limbs from the ramp to the top. Yet this disagreement only adds one person, not nine. Rashi explains accordingly: the carrying from the ramp to the top of the altar was performed not by nine new *kohanim* but by a single *kohen*. One person went back and forth, carrying everything from the *kevesh* to the *mizbeach*. Rashi states this explicitly: *v'hu tanna is lei od kohen echad hama'aleh evarim min hakevesh lamizbe'ach harei kan yod-zayin* — this *tanna* holds that there is one additional *kohen* who brings the limbs from the ramp to the altar, giving us seventeen. This is somewhat startling: nine to twelve *kohanim* carry the limbs and their accompaniments to the ramp — a large and elaborate procession — yet the final stage, from the ramp to the top of the altar, according to Rashi, was handled by a single *kohen* making multiple trips.

### Ram Sacrifice

The next *mishnah* turns from the *tamid* — which is a lamb — to the *ayil*, the ram. *Ayil karev b'echad-asar*: a ram is offered by eleven *kohanim*. Five carry the meat, the various sections of the animal. Then the *kirvayim* (inner organs), the *solet* (flour), and the *yayin* (wine) are each assigned to two *kohanim* apiece — *bishnayim bishnayim*. That is how eleven is reached rather than nine.

This distribution is worth pausing over, because it seems almost inverted in proportion. One *kohen* carries an enormous limb of the animal, yet because a ram requires slightly more wine and flour than a lamb, we assign two *kohanim* to carry the wine and two to carry the flour. The reason, as with the two *kohanim* who would carry the small cups of incense for the *shulchan*, appears to be largely symbolic: the doubling of personnel for the wine and flour signals that the ram's accompanying offerings are of a greater quantity than those of the lamb. It is a visual and ceremonial statement about the elevated nature of the *korban*, not a practical necessity of weight or volume.

### Cow Sacrifice

When it comes to a *par* — a cow — the number rises dramatically: *par karev b'esrim ve'arba*, a cow requires twenty-four *kohanim*. The distribution is as follows. The head is carried by one *kohen*, and the right hind leg by two — they would carry the whole leg together. The rump and tail, which in a lamb were handled by one, are here assigned two each. The chest is carried by one and the throat area by three. The two forearms receive one *kohen* apiece, and the two flanks similarly two *kohanim* total. Then, paralleling what we saw with the ram, the *kirvayim*, the *solet*, and the *yayin* are each carried by three *kohanim* — *bishloshsh shloshsh* — rather than the two assigned to a ram. The arithmetic works out to twenty-four.

### Communal vs Individual

*Bameh devarim amurim* — when does this elaborate division apply? Only for *korbanot tzibur*, the communal sacrifices. In such offerings the aim is to involve as many *kohanim* as possible, visibly demonstrating that the *korban* is being offered on behalf of *Klal Yisrael*. By contrast, *aval bekorban yachid* — for an individual's *korban*, *im ratzah lehakriv makriv*: any *kohen* who wishes to perform the offering may do so. There is no prescribed division, no lottery, and the distribution happens on an entirely ad hoc basis.

Rashi adds a point that, while perhaps predictable, is worth making explicit. His comment to *bameh devarim amurim* notes that even for *korbanot tzibur*, what we are describing requires both a designated number of *kohanim* per animal and a *payis* — a lottery — to assign them. This is significant, because up to this point in the *gemara* the *payis* has been discussed exclusively in connection with the *tamid*. Here Rashi implies that *korbanot tzibur* more broadly — the *musaf* offerings of Shabbat, *Rosh Chodesh*, *Sukkot*, and so on — would also be distributed by lottery. Indeed, *masechtah Sukkah* discusses the division of the *korbanot* of *Sukkot* being done with a *payis*, including the famous system by which the seventy *parim* were distributed among the *mishmarot* during the festival. But in the *mishnayot* before us this has not been stated explicitly, and Rashi's comment fills in what the *mishnah* leaves implicit.

The broader significance of the *payis* for *korbanot tzibur* is worth reflecting on. The lottery ensures that the offering is not simply handled by one *kohen* acting as an individual agent. Rather, the entire corporate body of *kohanim* participates in the process — if everyone gathers for the lottery, in some sense everyone is involved, even if only a small number physically carry the limbs. This mirrors the nature of the *korban* itself: it belongs to and is brought on behalf of the entire community.

### Hefshet v'Nituach

The *mishnah* concludes: *hafshatan venituachan shel elu ve'elu shavin* — whether it is a *korban tzibur* or a *korban yachid*, the flaying and the dismembering are performed equally. At first glance this simply means there is no prescribed system for these acts, no lottery and no fixed number of *kohanim* — it is done on an ad hoc basis, whoever is available. But as the *gemara* will make clear, the *mishnah* is saying something more far-reaching: *hefshet venituach* can be performed by a *zar*, a non-*kohen* entirely.

## Flaying Permitted?

### Mishna Intro

The *gemara* homes in on the final clause of the *mishnah* — *hafshatan venituachan shavin b'zar* — and reads it not merely as a statement about the distribution of labor but as a substantive halakhic ruling: the flaying and dismembering of the sacrifice may be performed by someone who is not a *kohen* at all. This is a striking departure from the general presumption, grounded in the verse *ve'atem uvenayich itcha tishmru et kehunatckem, vehazar hakarev yumat* — the *kehunah* is your exclusive domain, and any outsider who approaches shall die. Against that baseline assumption, the *gemara* will now seek the textual source that exempts *hefshet venituach* from the requirement of *kehunah*.

A practical question presents itself immediately: if a *zar* performs the flaying, where in the *Mikdash* does this occur? The *Ezrat Kohanim* is of a higher sanctity than the *Ezrat Yisrael*, and a *yisrael* may enter it only when necessary — for *smicha*, *shechita*, or *tenufah*, as the *mishnah* in *Keilim* records. In principle, however, the flaying could be done in the *Ezrat Yisrael*, which was technically part of the *azarah*, and which in practice was quite accessible. Indeed, the hooks for hanging the animals, the tables for washing the *kirvayim*, and the whole apparatus for processing the offering were arrayed in that area. Moreover, during the *regalim* the entire space was filled with *Bnei Yisrael*, and there was even a gate known as the Women's Gate through which women could enter the *azarah* — the notion that the space was exclusively restricted to *kohanim* does not reflect the full picture.

### Chizkiyah's Logic

*Amar Chizkiyah*: the source for *hefshet venituach* not requiring *kehunah* is derived from the verse *ve-natnu bnei Aharon ha-kohen esh al ha-mizbe'ach* — "the sons of Aaron the *kohen* shall place fire on the altar." From this the *gemara* infers: *netinat esh bikhuna*, placing the fire requires a *kohen*; *hafshat venituach lo bikhuna*, the flaying and dismembering do not require *kehunah*.

This is, on its face, a peculiar derivation. The verse is telling us that the fire requires a *kohen* — but how do we identify what is being implicitly excluded? Why should the implicit exclusion be *hefshet venituach* rather than something else entirely? The *gemara* recognizes this difficulty and will spend the remainder of the *daf* probing the structure of the *psukim* to place this exclusion on firmer ground.

### Abaye's Teaching

At this point the *gemara* introduces a scene: *ashkachei Abaye de-hava masbar leh li-breh* — Abaye was found explaining to his son how we know that *hefshet venituach* does not require a *kohen*. What follows is, in its essence, a straightforward *pshat* reading of the opening *psukim* of *Vayikra* (chapter 1), beginning from the *pasuk* describing an *olah* brought from the cattle.

The passage opens with the owner bringing his animal to the entrance of the *Ohel Mo'ed*, performing *smicha*, and then — *ve-shachat et ben habakar lifnei Hashem* — slaughtering it. Immediately following, the verse shifts: *ve-hikrivu bnei Aharon ha-Kohanim et ha-dam ve-zarku et ha-dam al ha-mizbe'ach saviv* — the sons of Aaron, the *kohanim*, shall bring the blood and dash it on the altar all around. The pattern is unmistakable: *smicha* and *shechita* are stated in the singular, without reference to *bnei Aharon*, attributing those acts to the owner. *Kabalah* and *zerikah*, by contrast, are stated in the plural and explicitly attributed to *bnei Aharon ha-Kohanim*.

Then comes *ve-hifshit et ha-ola ve-nitach otah li-ntacheha* — singular again, no mention of *bnei Aharon*. This is the flaying and dismembering. Then: *ve-natnu bnei Aharon ha-kohen esh al ha-mizbe'ach ve-archu ha-etzim al ha-esh* — the fire and the wood arrangement return to *bnei Aharon*. The pattern speaks clearly: where the *pasuk* does not say *bnei Aharon* and uses the singular, it is not requiring *kohanim*; where it says *bnei Aharon*, it does.

*Ve-shachat et ben habakar lifnei Hashem — ve-hikrivu bnei Aharon ha-Kohanim et ha-dam*: from *kabalah* onward, the *mitzvat kehunah* begins. *Smicha* is the owner's, *shechita* is permitted to a *zar* — *limad al ha-shechita she-kesherah b'zar*. And the *hefshet venituach*, appearing in the singular without *bnei Aharon*, likewise does not require *kehunah*.

What makes this reading striking is that it is essentially *pshat* in the *psukim* — the text itself, read plainly, tells you who does what. The *gemara*, however, is going to approach it differently, reading the inference not from the overall literary pattern but from the principle that a superfluous *bnei Aharon* must be teaching something. The tension between Abaye's *pshat* approach and the *gemara*'s *drasha* framework will drive the analysis that follows.

## Flaying Permitted?…

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* raises a difficulty with the derivation from *ve-natnu bnei Aharon ha-kohen esh al ha-mizbe'ach*. If the rule is that from *kabalah* onward everything is *mitzvat kehunah*, then the repetition of *bnei Aharon* in connection with placing the fire on the altar should be superfluous — and it is precisely from that superfluity that Chizkiyah inferred the exclusion of *hefshet venituach*. But the *gemara* objects: *ha-mibaye leh le-gufeh* — perhaps this verse is not superfluous at all. One might have thought that since placing fire on the altar is not itself an act that renders the *korban* valid or constitutes the essential *kapara* — it is not *kabalah* or *zerikah* — perhaps it would not require *kehunah*. Therefore the verse comes to tell us explicitly that even this requires *kohanim*: *ka mashma lan d'ba'i kehunah*. If the verse is needed for that purpose, it is not superfluous, and we cannot derive anything from it about *hefshet venituach*. The derivation from Chizkiyah's source thus collapses.

## Benei Aharon Extra

### Fire Requires Kohen

The *gemara* now attempts a fresh source. *Ela meihacha* — perhaps from here: *ve-archu bnei Aharon ha-Kohanim et ha-netachim et ha-rosh ve-et ha-pader* — "the sons of Aaron, the *kohanim*, shall arrange the limbs, the head, and the fat on the fire." This is the second instance of *bnei Aharon* in close succession: *ve-natnu bnei Aharon* for the fire, and then *ve-archu bnei Aharon ha-Kohanim* for the arranging of the limbs on the fire. Since we have already established from the first verse that even non-essential *avodot* require *kehunah*, the second *bnei Aharon* here is genuinely superfluous — and the *gemara* argues it is from this superfluity that we derive: *lemi'utei hefshet venituach*, to exclude *hefshet venituach* from the requirement of *kehunah*.

### Arranging Limbs

The logic runs as follows. *Michdi mikabala ve'eilach mitzvat kehunah* — I already know that from *kabalah* onward it is the *kohen*'s domain. *Ve-archu lama li*? Why must the verse repeat *bnei Aharon ha-Kohanim* when instructing the *kohanim* to arrange the limbs on the fire? I already know we are talking about *kohanim* at this stage of the service. The answer is that this repetition is not superfluous within its own section — it leapfrogs back over the *hefshet venituach*, which appeared without *bnei Aharon*, and re-establishes *kohanim* as the required agents. The very fact that it must return to say *bnei Aharon* demonstrates that what came between — the flaying and dismembering — did not require *bnei Aharon*.

The *gemara* now raises an objection: *ve-eima lemi'utei shnei gizeirei etzim*? Perhaps the superfluity of *bnei Aharon* here is not coming to exclude *hefshet venituach* but to exclude the two logs of wood placed on the altar in the afternoon *tamid* — teaching that *that* act does not require a *kohen*. This seems like an odd suggestion, since the placing of logs on the altar already requires *kehunah* as we have established. The *gemara* responds: *mistabra divcha d'chvatah ka m'ma'et* — it stands to reason that what is being excluded is something of the same category as what is being discussed, namely a step in the sacrificial process. To which the *gemara* replies with a counter-argument: *sidra d'chvatah memma'et* — on the contrary, since this verse deals with placing things on the fire, what it is excluding might be a different act of placing on the fire, namely the afternoon wood. The *gemara* then rules this out — *lo salka da'atach* — because of a further derivation: *ve-hikriv ha-kohen et ha-kol ha-mizbecha*, "the *kohen* shall bring it all to the altar," teaches that it is the bringing of the limbs to the *kevesh* that requires *kehunah*, but the bringing of wood does not require *kehunah*. From this it is already implicit that the placing of wood on the fire — whether in this context or in the afternoon *tamid* — does require *kehunah* (since only the bringing of wood is excluded, not the placing). Therefore, the second *bnei Aharon* is not needed to tell us that — it must be excluding *hefshet venituach*.

### Rabbi Elazar Obj.

*Amar Rav Hamnuna: kashya lei l'Rabbi Elazar* — Rabbi Elazar raised an objection to this entire derivation. *Ha-i be-ven ha-bakar ktiv* — the passage being analyzed is written in the context of a *ben habakar*, a cow! And a cow, as we have just learned in the *mishnah*, requires twenty-four *kohanim*. How can the repetitions of *bnei Aharon* in this passage teach us anything about the number of *kohanim* required for the *tamid* — a lamb — when the passage is discussing a fundamentally different type of animal?

### Tamid Context

The resolution comes from the phrase *al ha-etzim asher al ha-esh asher al ha-mizbe'ach* — "on the wood that is on the fire that is on the altar." This appears redundant: why enumerate *etzim*, *esh*, and *mizbe'ach* separately? The answer is that this combination — fire, wood, and altar mentioned together — is specifically characteristic of the *tamid*. As the Torah says elsewhere: *esh al ha-mizbe'ach tukad bo lo tichbeh, ubi'er aleha ha-Kohen etzim ba-boker ba-boker* — the fire on the altar shall burn continuously, and the *kohen* shall arrange wood upon it each morning. It is specifically in the context of the daily *tamid* that all three elements — wood, fire, and altar — appear together. Therefore, the *drashot* derived from the repetitions of *bnei Aharon* in this passage speak to the *tamid*, not to the cow, and the derivation is valid.

## Non-Kohen Wood

### Yochanan's Rule

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: a *zar* who performed *sidur hama'aracha* — who arranged the woodpile — is *chayav*, he has transgressed and incurred liability. *Keitzad hu oseh*? What is to be done? *Porkah ve-chozer ve-sodrah* — it can be dismantled and rearranged. The act can be corrected.

### Why Redo?

The *gemara* immediately objects: *mai ahani lei*? What does redoing it accomplish? He has already transgressed by doing it the first time. If the *zar* now puts it back on, has he not simply committed the same transgression a second time? This is analogous to someone who inadvertently turned on a light on Shabbat — turning it back off only compounds the violation.

### Sidur vs Natan

The *gemara* resolves this: *ella porkah zar ve-sodrah kohen* — what Rabbi Yochanan means is that the *zar* removes it, and then a *kohen* comes and rearranges it properly. The *zar* has done his transgression, but the *avodah* itself can be rectified: take the wood down and have a *kohen* replace it.

At this point it is important to clarify a terminological distinction that the *gemara* will rely upon going forward. *Sidur hama'aracha* — the arranging of the woodpile — corresponds to *ve-archu bnei Aharon etzim al ha-mizbe'ach*, the organizing of the existing woodpile. This is distinct from *ve-natnu bnei Aharon ha-kohen esh al ha-mizbe'ach* — the placing of new logs on the fire. These are two separate stages: first the arranging of the existing wood (*aricha*), and then the adding of new wood. The discussion about the *zar* undoing his act and having a *kohen* redo it pertains specifically to *sidur hama'aracha* — the *aricha* stage — where there is a genuine *avodah* of arranging that is important to be done by a *kohen*, and which can therefore be undone and properly redone.

## Night vs Day Work

### Beivai Question

*Mative Rav Beivai bar Abaye*: Rav Beivai challenges the entire framework. If *sidur hama'aracha* is a night *avodah* — and it is, since it is performed together with *trumat hadeshen* before daybreak — then it does not constitute a real *avodat hayom*. And if it is not a genuine *avodat hayom*, he asks, why should a *zar* be liable for performing it? The general principle is that *avodot* performed at night are, so to speak, of a lesser halakhic status. Why, then, does this require a *kohen* at all, let alone render a *zar chayav*?

### Two Stages

The *gemara* responds with two examples to test the premise. First: *ve-harei eivarim u-fedarim* — the burning of the limbs continues into the night, yet it certainly requires a *kohen* and a *zar* would be liable. The *gemara* answers: *sof avodat yemama hi* — the burning of the limbs is fundamentally a daytime *avodah*; it simply carries over into the night when not completed in time. Second: *ve-harei trumat hadeshen* — the removal of the ashes takes place before daybreak and is unquestionably a nighttime act, yet a *zar* is *pasul* for it. The *gemara* answers: *techilat avodat yemama hi* — the *trumat hadeshen* is the opening act of the day's service; it is conceptually linked to the daytime *avodot* that follow.

This resolution leads to a significant halakhic reorientation. The *gemara* effectively says: precisely because *sidur hama'aracha* is a nighttime *avodah* — not the opening act of the day's service in the way *trumat hadeshen* is — it follows the status of nighttime *avodot* generally, and is *kesherah b'zar*. Counterintuitively, then, the *psukim* that explicitly assign *bnei Aharon* to *sidur hama'aracha* do not render a *zar chayav* for performing it, because the halakhic category of nighttime *avodah* overrides the textual indication. The liability of a *zar* attaches only to *natan etzim* — the placing of new logs — which is considered a daytime *avodah*.

### Rava's Question

*Matkif lah Rava*: if *shnei gizeirei etzim* — the placing of the two logs — is genuinely an *avodat hayom*, why is there no lottery for it? Surely daytime *avodot* of the *tamid* require a *payis*. This seems like a meaningful lacuna in the lottery system.

### Lottery Principle

The *gemara* answers: *ishtamiteti* — Rava momentarily forgot what had been taught in a *baraita*: *mi she-zacha bi-trumat hadeshen zacha b'sidur hama'aracha u-v'sidur shnei gizeirei etzim*. There is indeed a lottery — the winner of the *trumat hadeshen* lottery also receives the right to perform *sidur hama'aracha* and to place the two logs. So all three acts are covered by the first *payis*, and there is no gap in the lottery system.

From Rava's question, the *gemara* extracts a general principle: *avodat yom* requires a *payis*; *avodat laila* does not. This implied rule is then tested. *Ve-harei eivarim u-fedarim* — the burning of the limbs finishes at night, yet there was a *payis* for it (specifically, the fourth lottery for bringing the limbs from the ramp to the altar). The *gemara* answers: *sof avodat yemama hi* — it is a daytime *avodah* at its core, even if its conclusion may extend into the night. And *ve-harei trumat hadeshen* — that occurs at night, yet there is a *payis* for it? Here the *gemara* gives a different answer than before: *mishum ma'aseh she-hayah* — the lottery for *trumat hadeshen* was instituted because of the incident, the dangerous race among *kohanim* that led to injury and even death. The lottery in that case was not a reflection of the general rule but a special enactment responding to a particular historical circumstance.

## Day vs Night Avodot

### Burning Limbs

The *gemara* refines the conceptual framework. The burning of the limbs — *eivarim u-fedarim* — is *sof avodat yom*: the conclusion of the daytime service. Even when it is completed at night because the day ran short, it retains its classification as a daytime *avodah*. It is not, in any fundamental sense, a nighttime act.

### Trumat HaDeshen

By contrast, *trumat hadeshen* occupies a unique position. It is performed before daybreak — in the deep of the night, or at least well before dawn — and yet it is classified as *techilat avodat yemama*, the opening of the day's service. It inaugurates the sequence of *avodot* that will unfold through the day. For this reason, despite occurring at night, it is conceptually anchored to the day.

### Hand Washing

The evidence that *trumat hadeshen* is the first of the day's *avodot* is derived from a ruling of Rabbi Yochanan (transmitted by Rabbi Aptoriki): *kidesh yadav li-trumat hadeshen, le-machar eino tzarich lekadesh* — if a *kohen* sanctified his hands before performing *trumat hadeshen*, he does not need to sanctify them again at daybreak. Ordinarily, the transition from night to day requires renewed sanctification of the hands. The fact that the *trumat hadeshen* sanctification carries over through daybreak demonstrates that it counts as the first of the daytime *avodot*. It is not a nighttime act that concludes and is then followed by a fresh start at dawn — it is itself the beginning of the day's *avodah*, even though it occurs before the sun rises.

## Timing Rules

### Shechita Timing

The *gemara* now turns to a practical question. We have established that the *shechita* of the *tamid* required the formal announcement of daybreak — the lookout would call out that it was time for *shechita*. *Amar lahem ha-memuneh: tze'u ur'u im higi'a zman ha-shechita* — the officer would instruct them to go see whether daybreak had arrived. This careful timing was essential, because if the *shechita* was performed before daybreak it could not be corrected. The animal would be dead; there would be no remedy.

### Wood Flexibility

The two logs of wood, by contrast, were not subject to the same strict protocol. *Va-ilu zman shnei gizeirei etzim lo katni* — the *mishnah* does not state that one had to wait for the announcement before placing the logs. The *gemara* explains: *hach d'leis lei takanta katni, hach d'is lei takanta lo katni* — what has no remedy, the *mishnah* teaches us to be careful about; what has a remedy, it need not specify. If the logs were placed before daybreak, they can simply be removed and replaced after daybreak. No irreversible harm has been done. In practice, as Rashi notes, the *kohanim* were generally able to judge the time without the formal announcement; the announcement was instituted to prevent error, but since the wood could always be corrected, there was no need to wait for it in that case.

### Final Conclusion

The *gemara* thus arrives at its final conclusion. The *sidur hama'aracha* — the arranging of the woodpile — is classified as a nighttime *avodah*. Although the *psukim* attribute it to *bnei Aharon*, it is *kesherah b'zar*: performed at night, it carries the leniency that attaches to all *avodat laila*. A *zar* who performs it does not incur the liability that attaches to a *zar* who violates a genuine *avodat hayom*. The *natan etzim* — the actual placing of new logs on the fire — is of a different character. It is associated with the daytime *avodah*, a *zar* who performs it is *chayav*, and it is subject to the lottery system through which the *kohen* who won the *trumat hadeshen* lottery receives the honor of performing it as well. In this way the *gemara* reconciles the textual evidence of the *psukim*, the practical realities of the Temple service, and the conceptual categories of *avodat hayom* and *avodat laila*.
