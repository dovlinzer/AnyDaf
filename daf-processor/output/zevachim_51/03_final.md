# Zevachim 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Drashot in Kodshim

### Four Methods Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּבִנְיַן אָב, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ וּבִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה וּבְקַל וָחוֹמֶר וּבְבִנְיַן אָב?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via a paradigm** can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a juxtaposition or via a verbal analogy or via an *a fortiori*** inference **or via a paradigm?**

Today's daf is daf 51a, and we are wrapping up the meta-discussion of what types of doubly-derived *drashot* are legitimate in the area of *kodshim* — a domain in which more restrictions govern when one may apply certain hermeneutical methods. Two rules apply in *kodshim* that do not apply elsewhere. First, while in other areas of *halacha* one generally assumes that a stated requirement is binding — as with *u-lekachtem lachem pri etz hadar*, where no one debates whether a *pri* or an *etz* is truly indispensable — in the world of *korbanot* there are so many requirements that the default assumption is that unless something is explicitly repeated, it is not *me'akev*, meaning it does not invalidate the *korban* if omitted. Second, the question of "chaining" derivations — learning from A to B, and then from B to C — which is generally permitted in other areas of *halacha*, is restricted in *korbanot*, presumably because of the sheer proliferation of details that would result.

The *gemara* previously identified four methods by which connections between laws can be established: *hekesh* (juxtaposition), *gezeirah shavah* (verbal analogy), *kal va-chomer* (an a fortiori argument), and *binyan av* (paradigmatic analogy). It then proceeded through a 4×4 chart exploring which of these methods may serve as a starting point for a further derivation within *kodshim*. To recall the conclusions reached: *hekesh* to *hekesh* — no; *hekesh* to *gezeirah shavah* — no; *hekesh* to *kal va-chomer* — yes; *hekesh* to *binyan av* — left open. We now turn to the final row of that chart: what happens when one starts with a *binyan av*?

### Binyan Av Starting Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשׁוֹט מִיהָא חֲדָא – מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ: לָן בַּדָּם – כָּשֵׁר? שֶׁהֲרֵי לָן בָּאֵימוּרִין כָּשֵׁר. לָן בְּאֵימוּרִין כָּשֵׁר – שֶׁהֲרֵי לָן בַּבָּשָׂר כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: **Resolve at least one** of those questions. The Gemara cites a lengthy *baraita* before stating the resolution inferred from that *baraita*. **For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that **in** the case of **blood left overnight** it is **fit,** i.e., if blood of an offering had been left overnight and was then placed on the altar it need not be removed? This is **as** it is **in** the case of **sacrificial portions,** where if they are **left overnight** they are **fit.** From where is it derived that **in** the case of **sacrificial portions** which are **left overnight,** they are **fit?** This is **as** it is **in** the case of **meat,** where if it is **left overnight** it is **fit,** because the meat of a peace offering may be eaten for two days and one night.

The *gemara* poses the question on daf 51a: *davar ha-lamed be-binyan av, mahu she-yihei lamed be-hekesh u-ve-gezeirah shavah u-ve-binyan av* — when a law has been derived through a *binyan av*, may one then proceed to derive further laws from it via *hekesh*, *gezeirah shavah*, *kal va-chomer*, or another *binyan av*? Because all of these are asked together, the *gemara* addresses them in a fairly summary fashion. An additional layer of the question concerns how to determine whether one is even operating "within the world of *kodshim*": when you have three links in a chain — A, B, and C — which of them must be in *kodshim* for the restrictive rules to apply?

The *gemara* states: *pashat mi-achada* — we can at least resolve one of these. It proceeds to bring a proof.

### Dam Kasher Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹצֵא – הוֹאִיל וְיוֹצֵא כָּשֵׁר בְּבָמָה. טָמֵא – הוֹאִיל וְהוּתַּר בַּעֲבוֹדַת צִיבּוּר. חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – הוֹאִיל וּמְרַצֶּה לְפִיגּוּלוֹ. חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – הוֹאִיל וְהוּקַּשׁ לְחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ. שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִין וְזָרְקוּ דָּמָן – בְּהָנָךְ פְּסוּלִין דַּחֲזוּ לַעֲבוֹדַת צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that if an offering that has **left** the Temple courtyard is then placed on the altar it need not be removed? This is derived by a paradigm, **since** an offering that **leaves** its area is **fit in** the case of an offering brought on a private **altar.** From where is it derived that if an offering that has become **ritually impure** is placed on the altar it need not be removed? This is derived by a paradigm, **since** one is **permitted** to offer an impure offering **in** the case of **communal rites,** i.e., communal offerings. In cases of necessity, the communal offerings may be sacrificed even if they are ritually impure. From where is it derived that in the case of an offering that was disqualified due to the intention of the priest who slaughtered it to consume it **beyond its** designated **time [*piggul*],** if it was placed on the altar it need not be removed? The *halakha* applies there **since** the sprinkling of its blood **effects acceptance** of the offering notwithstanding **its** status of ***piggul*.** The status of *piggul* takes effect only if the sacrificial rites involving that offering were otherwise performed properly. This indicates that it still has the status of an offering, so it need not be removed from the altar. From where is it derived that in the case of an offering that was disqualified due to the intention of the priest who slaughtered it to consume it **outside its** designated **area,** if it was placed on the altar it need not be removed? This is derived by a paradigm, **since** it is **juxtaposed to** an offering that was slaughtered with intent to consume it **beyond its** designated **time.** From where is it derived that in the case of an offering for **which** priests who are **disqualified collected and sprinkled its blood,** if it was placed on the altar it need not be removed? This is derived **from** the *halakha* of **these** priests who are generally **disqualified** because they are impure, yet **who are fit to** perform the **communal rites,** i.e., to sacrifice communal offerings, when all the priests or the majority of the Jewish people are impure. In any event, the *halakha* of the sacrificial portions was derived via a paradigm from the *halakha* of meat that was left overnight, and then the *halakha* of blood was derived via a paradigm from the *halakha* of the sacrificial portions. Evidently, a matter derived via a paradigm can teach its *halakha* to another matter via a paradigm.

The relevant *halacha* is as follows: if something *pasul* (disqualified) is placed upon the *mizbe'ach*, one is generally permitted to leave it there — with certain exceptions. According to Rabbi Yehuda's position, which defines a larger category of things that must be removed, items such as blood that was slaughtered at night, blood that was spilled, or blood that left the *Beit Hamikdash* must come down. The question is: where does one draw the line? What determines whether something *pasul* on the *mizbe'ach* may remain?

A *braita* addresses this through a series of comparisons arranged as a *binyan av*. The first link: why do we know that blood which has stayed overnight (*lan*) and is then placed on the *mizbe'ach* may remain there? *She-harei lan be-imurin kasher* — because we know that *imurim* (the sacrificial fats and portions designated for the altar) that have stayed overnight are valid if placed on the *mizbe'ach*. And how do we know *that*? *She-harei lan be-basar kasher* — because in the case of a *shelamim*, the meat is permitted for eating for two days. Thus, since the meat can be valid even a day later, we extend that principle to the *imurim* as well: if the *imurim* slept overnight, they too are still valid on the *mizbe'ach*. And since this is true of the *imurim*, we extend it further to the blood.

### Weak Comparisons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּנִים דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ, מִדָּבָר שֶׁבְּהֶכְשֵׁירוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the derivations of the *baraita*: **But can one deduce** the *halakha* of **a matter that is not fit,** i.e., sacrificial portions that are disqualified due to having been left overnight, **from** the *halakha* of **a matter that is fit,** i.e., the peace offering, which is permitted to be eaten for two days and one night? Similarly, how can the *baraita* derive the *halakha* of meat that was removed from the Temple courtyard from the *halakha* of a private altar, which has no sacred area surrounding it?

The *braita* continues with a series of additional comparisons, each establishing that another category of *pasul* material placed on the *mizbe'ach* may remain there.

*Yotze*: how do we know that something that has left the *Beit Hamikdash* and been placed on the *mizbe'ach* may remain? *Ho'il ve-yotze kasher be-bamah* — because on a *bamah* (a temporary altar outside the *Mikdash*), the concept of *yotze* does not apply; the correct location for the service is simply wherever one happens to be. So something that has gone outside the *Mikdash* can still remain on the *mizbe'ach*.

*Tamei*: how do we know that meat which is *tamei* and placed on the *mizbe'ach* may remain? *Ho'il ve-hutar be-avodat tzibur* — because there are occasions when *tamei* is permitted for communal service (*tumah hutrah be-tzibur*). This is admittedly an imperfect comparison, since *tumah hutrah be-tzibur* applies when the *people* are *tamei*, not when the *meat* is *tamei*. One could have invoked *ritzuy tzitz* (the atoning power of the priestly forehead-plate) instead, but that is a direct *halacha*, not a comparison.

*Chutz lizmano*: how do we know that *pasul* meat offered with the intent for the wrong time (*pigul* thoughts) may remain on the *mizbe'ach*? *Ho'il u-mratze le-pigulo* — because when *pigul* thoughts are entertained, the remainder of the *korban* is still treated as having been performed correctly in order to establish the *korban* as *pigul*. Since within the framework of *pigul*, those very *pigul* thoughts do not constitute an additional invalidation, the *korban* retains enough validity to remain on the *mizbe'ach*.

*Chutz limkomo*: an intent for the wrong place (*chutz limkomo*) is derived from its linkage to *chutz lizmano*, as they are compared to each other.

Finally, the case of *pasul kohanim* who received and threw the blood: *be-hanach psulim de-chazu le-avodat tzibur* — this applies specifically to the type of *psulim* who would be valid for a communal offering, which in practice limits this to kohanim who are *tamei*.

### Challenge: Kosher to Pas…

The *gemara* now raises a sharp objection — one that had been lurking throughout the *braita*'s entire presentation: *ve-chi danim davar she-lo be-hechsheiro mi-davar she-be-hechsheiro?* — how can one draw an inference from something that is fundamentally *kasher* (meat on the second day of a *shelamim*, or something outside the *Mikdash* by a *bamah*) to establish that something which is fundamentally *pasul* may remain on the *mizbe'ach*? The comparison is not genuinely analogous: one case is inherently valid, the other is inherently disqualified.

### Zot Torat Ha-Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא מִ״זֹּאת תּוֹרַת הָעוֹלָה״ רִיבָּה סָמֵיךְ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The ***tanna* relied on** the verse: "Command Aaron and his sons, saying: **This is the law of the burnt offering:** It is that which goes up on its firewood upon the altar all night until the morning; and the fire of the altar shall thereby be kept burning" (Leviticus 6:2), which **amplified** it, teaching that many types of disqualified offerings may be left upon the altar. The derivations written in the *baraita* are mere supports for those *halakhot*.

This reframes the entire preceding discussion. The *braita*'s comparisons were never meant to serve as the *positive* basis for concluding that these *pasul* items may remain. We do not begin from the premise that everything must come down and then use weak analogies to carve out exceptions. Rather, we begin from the opposite premise: *zot torat ha-olah* establishes that everything which goes up remains on the *mizbe'ach*. The question is only which categories are so severely disqualified that they fall outside this blanket rule — those are the few things that must come down. And how do we establish that these problematic categories are not among the worst exceptions? By showing that in some loosely associated scenario, something similar would be *kasher*. The comparisons are not logically rigorous proofs; they are simply indicators that these cases are not egregious enough to be expelled from the general rule of *zot torat ha-olah*.

### No Proof of Binyan Av

This resolution carries an important implication for the meta-discussion. Once we understand that the real source for these laws is *zot torat ha-olah* — and not a chain of *binyan av* derivations — the *braita* no longer constitutes a proof that one can apply a *binyan av* on top of a *binyan av*. The *halacha* that *pasul* items may remain on the *mizbe'ach* is not actually being learned through a *binyan av* built upon a *binyan av*; it derives from the general principle of *zot torat ha-olah*, with the comparisons serving only a subsidiary function of classifying which cases fall outside the rule. It is therefore possible that the *gemara* has, in the end, not resolved any of the questions about what one may derive from a starting point of *binyan av*.

## Shirei Hadam

### Source Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעֹלָה אֲשֶׁר פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – הַהוּא דְּפָגַע בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to the sin offerings whose blood is presented inside the Sanctuary: As to **the remainder of the blood** which is left after the sprinklings, a priest would pour it onto the western base of the external altar. But if he did not place the remainder of the blood on the western base, it does not disqualify the offering. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that it must be poured on the western base? The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to the bull offering of the High Priest: "And the priest shall sprinkle the blood upon the corners of the altar of sweet incense before the Lord, which is in the Tent of Meeting; and all the remaining blood of the bull he shall pour out **at the base of the altar of burnt offering, which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 4:7). This means that he must pour it on **that** base **which he encounters first** when he leaves the Tent of Meeting, which is the western base.

Having concluded — or at least set aside — the meta-discussion of hermeneutical methods, the *gemara* now turns to the next *halacha* of the *mishnah* concerning *chata'ot*: *shirei hadam* — the remnant blood. The *mishnah* states that the remnant blood of inner *chata'ot* is spilled on the western base of the outer *mizbe'ach*. To situate this: an inner *chatat* involves bringing blood into the inner precincts and applying it there — on the *paroches* or on the inner *mizbe'ach* — and the remainder of the blood is then brought back out and spilled on the base of the outer *mizbe'ach*, specifically the western base. The *gemara* asks: *shirei hadam mai ta'ama?* — what is the source for this *halacha*?

### Pesach Ohel Moed

The *gemara* answers: *amar kra, el yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah asher pesach ohel moed* — the verse says to pour it at the base of the *mizbe'ach ha-olah* which is at the opening of the *Ohel Moed*. Since the opening of the *Ohel Moed* faces west, this identifies the western base as the place for pouring the remnant blood.

Interestingly, there is a variant reading of the *gemara* where the answer is phrased as *mai ta'ama* — asking for a *reason* rather than a *pasuk* — and the response would be *hahu depaga bereisha*: as one exits the inner area, the first part of the base that one encounters is the western one. This alternative reading raises an important conceptual question, discussed at length by Tosafot: is the rule that *shirei hadam* goes on the western base a *gzeiras hakasuv* — a direct scriptural decree — or is it grounded in the principle of *ein ma'avirin al ha-mitzvot*, the rule that one does not bypass a *mitzvah* opportunity in order to do it elsewhere? Tosafot ultimately sides with the reading preserved in our *gemara* (as Rashi edited it), which bases the rule directly on the *pasuk*, setting aside the *hahu depaga bereisha* formulation.

Tosafot notes an important reason why *ein ma'avirin al ha-mitzvot* would be inapplicable here. That principle governs a situation where one encounters a *mitzvah* and is tempted to bypass it in order to perform a different *mitzvah*. But the question here is not whether to perform the *mitzvah* of spilling blood — one is going to do that regardless — but rather *where* on the base the *mitzvah* should be performed. The question of which location is the correct one is not answered by the principle of *ein ma'avirin al ha-mitzvot*.

### Outer Chata'ot Placement

This debate has a practical ramification for the outer *chata'ot*. When offering an outer *chatat*, the blood is applied to the corners of the outer *mizbe'ach* and the *kohen* descends. Where does he then pour the *shirayim*? If the rule is based on a *gzeiras hakasuv* — the explicit verse *pesach ohel moed* pointing to the west — then one would move to the western base. But if the rule is based on *hahu depaga bereisha*, the principle of taking the first base one encounters as one descends, then one would pour it on the southern base, since that is what one reaches first upon coming down from the corner of the outer *mizbe'ach*. This is thus a genuine *machlokes* with practical consequences.

### Pesukim Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעֹלָה״ – וְלֹא יְסוֹד מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי. ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעֹלָה״ – אֵין לוֹ יְסוֹד לַפְּנִימִי עַצְמוֹ. ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעֹלָה״ – תֵּן יְסוֹד לַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁל עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: There are three verses that contain the same phrase. With regard to pouring the remainder of the blood of a bull offering of the High Priest, the verse states: "All the remaining blood of the bull he shall pour out **at the base of the altar of burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:7). This teaches that it must be on the base of the external altar, **but not** on **the base of the inner altar,** where he had sprinkled the blood. The *baraita* continues: With regard to the bull sacrificed for an unwitting communal sin the verse states: "And he shall sprinkle the blood upon the corners of the altar which is before the Lord, that is in the Tent of Meeting, and all the remaining blood shall he pour out **at the base of the altar of burnt offering,** which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 4:18). This teaches that the **inner altar itself has no base** at all. Finally, the verse states with regard to the sin offering of a king: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger and place it upon the corners of the altar of burnt offering, and the remaining blood thereof shall he pour out **at the base of the altar of the burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:25). This teaches that you must **give a base to the altar of** the **burnt offering,** i.e., that the remainder of any blood placed on the altar must be poured on the base.

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita*: *tanu rabanan — el yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah*. Rashi notes that there are five passages in *Vayikra* dealing with *chata'ot*, each of which describes the spilling of blood at the *mizbe'ach* base. In three of these passages, the text reads explicitly *mizbe'ach ha-olah*; in the other two, it reads simply *el yesod ha-mizbe'ach* without the word *olah*. The *baraita* derives three distinct *drashot* from these three occurrences of *mizbe'ach ha-olah*.

### Rashi Classification

Rashi identifies the five *parshiyot* as follows: the *par mashiach* (the anointed Kohen Gadol's bull), the *par helam davar* (the communal sin offering for an unknowingly erroneous ruling), and the *se'ir nasi* (the prince's goat) — these three have the phrase *mizbe'ach ha-olah* in the context of spilling their remnant blood. The remaining two — the *kivsah shel yachid* (the individual's ewe) and the *se'irah shel yachid* (the individual's she-goat) — have only *el yesod ha-mizbe'ach*. Rashi classifies the first two (*par mashiach* and *par helam davar*) as inner *chata'ot* — *penimiyot* — and the latter two (*kivsah* and *se'irah*) as outer — *chitzoniyot*. The *se'ir nasi* presents a more interesting case that will figure in the *gemara*'s analysis below.

## Three Drashot

### First Drasha

The *baraita* states: *el yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah — ve-lo yesod mizbe'ach ha-penimi*. The first *drasha* derived from *mizbe'ach ha-olah* is that the remnant blood of inner *chata'ot* must be spilled on the base of the outer *mizbe'ach* — the *mizbe'ach ha-olah* — and not on the base of the inner *mizbe'ach*. This explains why the Torah specifically had to write *mizbe'ach ha-olah* by the inner *chata'ot*: to make clear that even though the blood service took place inside, the *shirayim* must be carried out and poured on the outer altar's base.

### Second Drasha

The *baraita* continues: *el yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah — ein lo yesod le-penimi atzmo*. The second *drasha* derived from the same phrase teaches that the inner *mizbe'ach* does not even have a *yesod* (a base) at all. Unlike the outer *mizbe'ach*, which has a protruding base around its lower perimeter, the inner *mizbe'ach* is a simple rectangular solid — no ledge, no protruding base. This is derived from the implication of *mizbe'ach ha-olah*: only the *mizbe'ach ha-olah* (the outer altar) has a *yesod*; the inner *mizbe'ach* does not.

### Third Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מִזְבְּחָהּ שֶׁל עוֹלָה – יְהֵא לַיְסוֹד? אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; מָה שִׁירַיִים, שֶׁאֵין מְכַפְּרִין – טְעוּנִין יְסוֹד; תְּחִלַּת עוֹלָה, שֶׁמְּכַפֶּרֶת – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטְּעוּנָה יְסוֹד?!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Or** perhaps it **is not** so, but **rather** the verse serves to teach that any sprinkling of blood on the corners of the **altar of** the **burnt offering will be** done on a part of the altar where there is **a base. Rabbi Yishmael said:** There is no need for the verse to teach that *halakha*, because it can be derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** the **remainder** of the blood, **which does not effect atonement, requires** pouring on the **base** of the altar, with regard to **the initial** sprinkling of the blood **of a burnt offering, which effects atonement, is it not logical that it requires** a part of the altar where there is **a base?**

The third *drasha* is the most complex and, frankly, the most difficult to pin down: *el yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah — ten yesod le-mizbe'ach shel olah*. The phrase is deliberately opaque — "give a base to the *mizbe'ach* of the *olah*" — and the difficulty of interpreting it is reflected in the extensive Tosafot on the following page, which does not represent the usual Tosafot trying to reconcile contradictory *sugyot* but rather a sustained effort to figure out the *pshat* of this single phrase.

According to Rashi, this third *drasha* teaches a general principle: all *korbanot* offered on the outer *mizbe'ach*, if there is any remnant blood (*shirayim*), must have that blood poured at the *yesod*. The Torah only explicitly mentioned spilling the blood at the base in the context of *chata'ot* — which makes sense, since with most other *korbanot* the blood is thrown (*zrikah*) in a manner that leaves little or nothing remaining. A *chatat* involves applying blood with a finger at the corners, leaving substantial remainder, but an *olah* or *shelamim* involves a broad throw, after which there is typically nothing left. Nevertheless, if there *is* remainder blood, one needs to know what to do with it. The Torah's silence on this point by other *korbanot* could leave one without guidance. The third *drasha* therefore establishes that the principle of *yesod* applies to the entire outer *mizbe'ach*: any remnant blood of any outer *korban* goes to the base.

Tosafot offers an alternative reading. According to Tosafot, the phrase *ten yesod le-mizbe'ach shel olah* means: whenever you throw blood on the *mizbe'ach ha-olah* — other than at the corners where a *chatat* is applied — it must be thrown opposite a place where there is a *yesod* below. The outer *mizbe'ach* did not have a *yesod* running along its entire perimeter; one corner was without a protruding base. The *halacha* is that blood thrown against the wall of the *mizbe'ach* must be thrown where there is a base beneath it. According to Tosafot, this is what the third *drasha* teaches. Notably, both Rashi and Tosafot agree on the underlying *halachot*; the question is only from which *pasuk* each is derived.

### Initial vs. Remnant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מָה שִׁירַיִים, שֶׁאֵין מְכַפְּרִין וְאֵין בָּאִין לְכַפָּרָה – טְעוּנָה יְסוֹד; תְּחִלַּת עוֹלָה, שֶׁמְּכַפֶּרֶת וּבָאָה לְכַפָּרָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטְּעוּנָה יְסוֹד?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Akiva said: Just as** the **remainder** of the blood, **which does not effect atonement and does not come for atonement,** nevertheless **requires** pouring on the **base** of the altar, with regard to **the initial** sprinkling of the blood **of a burnt offering, which effects atonement and comes for atonement, is it not logical that it requires** a part of the altar where there is **a base?**

The *gemara* now raises a question about the third *drasha* itself. Before accepting that it teaches about the spilling of *shirayim* of all outer *korbanot*, perhaps the *drasha* is teaching something else entirely: that the initial application of blood for an *olah* must be placed where there is a base below it. This is itself a genuine *halacha*, and the *gemara* entertains the possibility that the verse *el yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah* is its source. Two Amoraim — Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva — will now argue that this is not what the verse is teaching, and in doing so will reveal a dispute between themselves.

---

## Yishmael vs. Akiva

### Yishmael Kal Vachomer

Rabbi Yishmael argues: *mah shirayim she-ein mechapperim te'unim yesod* — the remnant blood, which does not itself create *kaparah* (atonement), nonetheless must be poured at the base. *Techilat olah she-mechapperes — eino din she-te'una yesod?* — then certainly the initial blood of an *olah*, which *does* achieve atonement, must be applied where there is a base below it. This is a *kal va-chomer*: if the less significant act (pouring the remnant) requires a *yesod*, certainly the more significant act (the initial atoning application) requires one. Therefore, Rabbi Yishmael concludes, we do not need the verse to tell us that the initial blood of an *olah* goes opposite a base — we know that from a *kal va-chomer*. The verse *mizbe'ach ha-olah* must therefore be teaching us something else.

### Why Extra Drasha?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעֹלָה״? תֵּן יְסוֹד לְמִזְבְּחָהּ שֶׁל עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **If so, why** must **the verse state: "At the base of the altar of burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:25)? It is to teach that you must **give a base to the altar of** the **burnt offering,** i.e., that the remainder of the blood of the offering must be poured on the base.

The *gemara* presses: if one can derive from a *kal va-chomer* that the initial blood must be placed opposite the base, why would I have thought otherwise? What was the *hava amina* (initial assumption) that required the *kal va-chomer* to dispel? And conversely, once the *kal va-chomer* suffices to teach us about the initial blood, what remains for the verse *mizbe'ach ha-olah* to teach?

### Mizbeach Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר מָר: ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח״ – וְלֹא יְסוֹד מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי. הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ! מֵ״אֲשֶׁר פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses this *baraita*. **The Master says:** The verse states: **"At the base of the altar** of burnt offering, which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 4:18). This teaches that it must be on the base of the external altar, **but not the base of the inner altar.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't that necessary for the matter itself,** to teach that the remainder of the blood must be poured onto the base of the external altar? The Gemara answers: That *halakha* is **derived from: "Which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 4:18), referring to the external altar. Therefore, the verse mentions the altar of burnt offering to exclude the base of the inner altar.

Rashi's explanation addresses both questions. Had the verse said only *el yesod ha-olah* (without the word *mizbe'ach*), one might have concluded that the initial blood of an *olah* goes on the base specifically — meaning on the *gag ha-yesod*, the top surface of the protruding ledge. The word *mizbe'ach* changes the focus: rather than speaking about a specific *korban*'s application of blood, the verse is making a statement about the *mizbe'ach* itself — that the *mizbe'ach* and its *yesod* are always a relevant destination for blood. This shifts the *drasha* from being about the initial application of blood to being a general principle about the *yesod* as a receptacle for all blood.

Because the word *mizbe'ach* introduced this ambiguity, one might still have thought the verse is teaching about initial blood application (only now understood differently — not about the top of the *yesod* but about the *mizbe'ach* and its base in a more general sense). Rabbi Yishmael therefore steps in with the *kal va-chomer*: since I already know from a *kal va-chomer* that initial blood must go opposite a base, the verse cannot be teaching me that. It must instead be teaching me that all *shirayim* of all outer *korbanot* go to the *yesod*.

### Counter-Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי כְּתִיב ״אֶל יְסוֹד הָעוֹלָה״?! ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה״ כְּתִיב! אִי הֲוָה כְּתִיב ״אֶל יְסוֹד הָעוֹלָה״ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בִּזְקִיפָה אֶל יְסוֹד; הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה״ – אַגַּגּוֹ דִּיסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** How can the verse mean that? **Is it written: At the base of the burnt offering?** This would indicate that the blood of the burnt offering must be placed where there is a base. **It is written** in the verse: **"At the base of the altar of burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:25). The Gemara answers: Even if the verse were to be speaking about sprinkling the blood of the burnt offering and is not referring to pouring the remainder of the blood, the term "altar of" is necessary, because **if it were written: At the base of the burnt offering, I would say** that the priest must sprinkle the blood **on the upright** wall **of the base** of the altar, i.e., the side of the base, rather than on the upper surface of the base. **Now that it is written: "At the base of the altar of the burnt offering,"** it means that the blood must be sprinkled **on the upper surface of the base.**

The *gemara* makes this logic explicit. *Mi kesiv al yesod ha-olah? Al yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah kesiv* — it does not say *el yesod ha-olah*; it says *el yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah*. The inclusion of the word *mizbe'ach* signals that the verse is making a statement about the *mizbe'ach* as an institution rather than about a particular *korban*. If the Torah had simply said *el yesod ha-olah*, one might have read it as: when offering an *olah*, the blood must go opposite the *yesod*. The word *mizbe'ach* instead conveys: the *mizbe'ach ha-olah* — the outer *mizbe'ach* in its entirety — has the quality that all blood remnants are poured at its base.

The *gemara* adds: had it said *el yesod ha-olah*, one might have thought the verse was telling me where to place the initial blood, and I would have interpreted it as meaning even on the *zkifa* — the vertical face of the *yesod* — or perhaps even on the *gag ha-yesod*, the top of the protruding ledge. Since *mizbe'ach* is written instead, it teaches us a principle about the *mizbe'ach* itself: the *yesod* of the *mizbe'ach* is always a destination for blood, referring to spilling the *shirayim*.

### Shirayim on Yesod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כְּדִכְתִיב; הָנֵי לְמָה לִי קְרָא – לְשִׁירַיִם?! שִׁירַיִם הָא בָּרַאי עָבֵיד לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Because if it enters your mind** that the verse states this simply to teach **as it is written,** concerning this offering alone, **why do I** need **these verses** with regard to the sin offering of a king? If you would answer: The verses are needed **to** teach the *halakha* that the **remainder** of the blood must be poured on the external altar rather than on the inner altar, then the question remains: Is there any need for the Torah to teach this about the **remainder? But** the sprinklings of blood themselves are **performed** on the **external** altar, so why would one think that the remainder of the blood should be poured on the inner altar?

At this point, the *gemara* asks another question about the third *drasha*. One might have thought the verse is needed to teach not the *shirayim* of all outer *korbanot*, but something far simpler: that the outer *chatat* — namely the *se'ir nasi*, which is an outer *chatat* and for which the verse *mizbe'ach ha-olah* is written — has its *shirayim* poured at the outer base, just as the inner *chata'ot* do. After all, the first two *drashot* established the rule for inner *chata'ot*; perhaps the third *drasha* is simply confirming the rule for the outer *chatat* as well.

The *gemara* rejects this reading. If we only needed to know that the outer *chatat*'s *shirayim* go at the outer base, *no verse would be necessary* — it follows by *kal va-chomer* from the inner *chata'ot*. If the inner *chatat*, whose entire service was performed inside, still has its *shirayim* poured outside at the outer base, then certainly the outer *chatat*, whose service is entirely outside, has its *shirayim* poured at the outer base. Moreover, one might have supposed some chiasmic logic — that inner *korbanot* pour blood outside and outer *korbanot* pour blood inside — but this is impossible once the second *drasha* established that the inner *mizbe'ach* has no *yesod* at all. Since there is no base on the inner *mizbe'ach*, there is no possibility of pouring there; the *kal va-chomer* works perfectly and no verse is needed to establish the outer *chatat*'s *shirayim* on the outer base. Therefore, the third *drasha* must be teaching something new: that the *shirayim* of *all* outer *korbanot* — not just *chata'ot* — go to the *yesod*. This includes an *olah*, a *shelamim*, and any other *korban* offered on the outer *mizbe'ach*, if remnant blood remains.

### Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva's formulation sounds nearly identical to Rabbi Yishmael's, but the *gemara* discerns a meaningful difference in phrasing. Rabbi Akiva says: *mah shirayim she-ein mechapperim ve-ein ba'in le-chapper te'unim yesod* — the remnant blood neither achieves atonement (*mechapperim*) nor is it brought for the purpose of atonement (*ba'in le-chapper*), yet it still requires the *yesod*. *Techilat olah she-mechapperes — eino din she-te'una yesod?* — certainly the initial blood of an *olah*, which *does* achieve atonement, must be applied where there is a base below it. The conclusion is the same as Rabbi Yishmael's: we know the initial blood's placement from a *kal va-chomer*, so the verse teaches us about the *shirayim*. *Im kein mah talmud lomar yesod mizbe'ach ha-olah — ten yesod le-mizbe'ach shel olah*: all *shirayim* of all outer *korbanot* go to the *yesod*.

### Me'akev Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: שִׁירַיִם מְעַכְּבִים אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – מָר סָבַר מְעַכְּבִי, וּמָר סָבַר לָא מְעַכְּבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the explanations of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva? **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The difference **between them** is whether failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood **disqualifies** the offering, so that all the sprinklings must be done again. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yishmael, **holds** that failure to pour the remainder of the blood does **disqualify** the offering. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, who adds the words: Which does not come for atonement, **holds** that failure to pour the remainder of the blood does **not disqualify** the offering.

What, then, distinguishes Rabbi Yishmael from Rabbi Akiva if they reach the same conclusion? Rav Ada bar Ahava explains: *sheyariyim me'akvin ika beinaihu* — the *me'akev* status of the *shirayim* is the point of contention between them.

Rabbi Yishmael's formulation emphasizes that the *shirayim* are not *mechapper*. He does not say they are not *ba'in le-chapper*. This implies that while the pouring of *shirayim* at the *yesod* does not accomplish atonement in itself, the act of pouring is nonetheless *me'akev* — it is an indispensable element of the service whose omission would retroactively undermine the validity of the *korban's* atonement.

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, adds the phrase *ve-ein ba'in le-chapper*: the *shirayim* neither atone nor even *come* for the purpose of atonement. By emphasizing this double negation — that the *shirayim* have no atoning function whatsoever, not even instrumentally — Rabbi Akiva implies that the pouring of *shirayim* at the *yesod* is not *me'akev*. It is a required act, but its omission does not invalidate the *korban*. *Mar savar me'akvin, u-mar savar lo me'akvin* — one holds it is *me'akev*, the other holds it is not.

This connects directly to the position of our *mishnah*, which assumes that *shikhfas shirayim* of inner *chata'ot* is not *me'akev*. The *gemara* will continue to explore the debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva and consider further ways of articulating their disagreement.
