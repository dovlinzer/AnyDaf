# Zevachim 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Ch. 2 Overview

### Opening & Topic Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ דָּמָן זָר, אוֹנֵן, טְבוּל יוֹם, וּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים, וּמְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים, שֶׁלֹּא רָחַץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, עָרֵל, טָמֵא, יוֹשֵׁב, עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבֵּי כֵּלִים, עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, עַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלֵי חֲבֵרוֹ – פָּסַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **all slaughtered offerings,** if the one **who collected their blood** was **a non-priest;** or a priest who was **an acute mourner,** i.e., one whose relative has died and has not yet been buried; or **one** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed; **or one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, e.g., a *zav* or leper after the seventh day of the purification process; **or** a priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments;** or one **who did not wash his hands and feet** from the water in the Basin prior to performing the Temple service; or **an uncircumcised** priest; or **a ritually impure** priest; or if the one who collected the blood was **sitting;** or if he was **standing** not on the floor of the Temple but **upon vessels,** or **upon an animal,** or **upon the feet of another,** he has **disqualified** the offering.

Daf 16 opens at the beginning of the second *perek*, which moves beyond the discussion of *shelo lishma* and into a broader inquiry about the various circumstances — primarily relating to the personal status of the officiating *kohen* — that invalidate a sacrifice. This chapter presents a whole list of categories of persons who, if they perform the *avodah*, render the *korban* invalid.

### Kabbalat HaDam Review

Because *shechita* is technically valid even when performed by a non-*kohen*, the Mishnah focuses on *kabbalat hadam* as the first *avodah* under discussion, so that the full list of disqualifying statuses can be surveyed properly. The Gemara has established — through a *tzad hashaveh*, a common denominator argument — that just as a *kohen* who is *tamei* or a *baal mum* invalidates if he performs the *avodah*, so too does a non-*kohen*. The general principle emerging is: anyone who is prohibited from performing the *avodah* in a given state will, if he performs it anyway, invalidate the *korban*. The Gemara now examines each case to establish both the prohibition and the invalidation.

## Zar & Tzad HaShaveh

### Source of Zar Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ זָר מְנָלַן? דְּתָנֵי לְוִי: "דַּבֵּר אֶל אַהֲרֹן וְאֶל בָּנָיו לֵאמֹר, וְיִנָּזְרוּ מִקׇּדְשֵׁי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳" — "בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל" לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי נָשִׁים – קׇרְבַּן נָשִׁים בְּטוּמְאָה קָרֵב?!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From where do we** derive that **a non-priest** who performs sacrificial rites disqualifies an offering? **As Levi teaches:** The verse states: **"Speak to Aaron and to his sons, that they separate themselves from the sacred items of the children of Israel [*benei Yisrael*]"** (Leviticus 22:2). Levi continues: **"The children of Israel"** serves **to exclude what** from the prohibition against sacrificing an offering in a state of impurity? **If we say** that it serves **to exclude** the offerings of **women,** and the verse states "*benei*" in the masculine to indicate sons but not daughters, is it conceivable that **an offering of women is sacrificed in** a state of **impurity?**

With the general principle in place, the Gemara asks: where do we know that a non-*kohen* is prohibited from performing the *avodah* in the first place? The *tzad hashaveh* requires a proven prohibition as its starting point, so the source of the *zar*'s prohibition must be identified independently.

### Veyinnazru vs. Vezar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי גּוֹיִם? הַשְׁתָּא צִיץ לָא מְרַצֵּה, דְּאָמַר מָר: וּבַגּוֹיִם – בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד לֹא הוּרְצָה; בְּטוּמְאָה קָרֵב?! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: "וְיִנָּזְרוּ מִקׇּדְשֵׁי, בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps it serves **to exclude** offerings of **gentiles. Now,** if the **frontplate** of the High Priest, which normally effects acceptance for offerings of Jews sacrificed in a state of impurity, **does not effect acceptance** for the offerings of gentiles, **as the Master said: And with regard to** an offering of **gentiles, whether** it was offered in a state of impurity **unintentionally or intentionally, it was not accepted;** can it be, then, that such an offering **is sacrificed in** a state of **impurity** *ab initio*? **Rather, this** is what the verse **is saying,** i.e., it should be read in the following manner: **That they separate themselves from the sacred items; the children of Israel, and that they not profane** My holy name. That is, the verse states that they, the priests, must separate themselves from the sacred items when they are ritually impure, and that the children of Israel must separate themselves from performing the sacrificial rites, so that they will not profane, i.e., disqualify, the offerings.

One might have thought the source is the verse *veyinnazru mikodeshei benei Yisrael* — "they shall be separated from the holy things of the children of Israel" — which could, on one reading, include a *zar*. But the Gemara immediately sets this aside: the plain sense (*pshat*) of that verse concerns a *kohen* who is *tamei*, not a non-*kohen*. More decisively, if we were to read the verse as referring to a *zar*, the verse itself already contains the word *yechalelu* — it would explicitly state both the prohibition and the invalidation, making the entire *tzad hashaveh* unnecessary. Since we are constructing a *tzad hashaveh* precisely because we need to derive the invalidation, we must be reading that verse as referring to *tumah* alone. The source for the *zar*'s prohibition is therefore the explicit verse *vezar lo yikrav aleichem* — "a stranger shall not come near you" — which plainly bars a non-*kohen* from performing the Temple service.

### Bamah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא, אָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִבַּעַל מוּם: מָה בַּעַל מוּם, שֶׁאוֹכֵל – אִם עָבַד חִילֵּל; זָר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל?!
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* of the **school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The *halakha* of a non-priest who performs the sacrificial rites **is derived via an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **a blemished** priest: **Just as** with regard to **a blemished** priest, **who may partake** of the meat of offerings, **if he performed** sacrificial **rites** he has nevertheless **desecrated** the service,

With the prohibition of the *zar* established, the *tzad hashaveh* from *tamei* and *baal mum* would seem to yield the rule that the *zar* also invalidates. But the Gemara raises a challenge: *mah letzad hashaveh shebahen sheken lo hutru bebama* — what is distinctive about the common denominator, the cases of *tamei* and *baal mum*, is that neither of them is permitted to perform sacrifices outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. A *tamei* person and a *baal mum* remain disqualified even at a private altar (*bamah*). But a *zar* is permitted to bring a *korban* on a *bamah* — he is only barred in the *Beit HaMikdash* itself, where the *kohen* functions as part of the Temple apparatus. Perhaps, then, the *zar*'s disqualification is categorically different: since he is sometimes permitted, maybe he does not rise to the level of invalidating the *korban*.

### Onen & Baal Mum Fix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְזָר – שֶׁאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל?! מָה לְבַעַל מוּם – שֶׁכֵּן עָשָׂה בּוֹ קָרֵב כְּמַקְרִיב! טָמֵא יוֹכִיחַ. מָה לְטָמֵא – שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא! בַּעַל מוּם יוֹכִיחַ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין. לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה; הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁמּוּזְהָרִין, וְאִם עָבְדוּ חִילְּלוּ; אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא זָר – שֶׁהוּא מוּזְהָר, וְאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** then with regard to **a non-priest, who may not partake** of the meat of offerings of the most sacred order, **is it not right that if he performed** sacrificial **rites he has desecrated** the service? The Gemara rejects the inference: One cannot draw an *a fortiori* inference from a blemished priest, as **what** is notable **about** the case of **a blemished** priest? It is notable **in that** the Torah **rendered** an animal that **is sacrificed like** the priest who **sacrifices** it, i.e., both blemished animals and blemished priests are disqualified. Since there is an added element of stringency with regard to the case of a blemished priest, one cannot draw an *a fortiori* inference from it. The case of **an impure** priest **will prove** that this is no reason to reject the *a fortiori* inference. While an animal and the priest are not equated with regard to ritual impurity, as an animal cannot become impure while alive but a priest can, an impure priest desecrates the service. The Gemara rejects this as well: **What** is notable **about** the case of **an impure** priest? It is notable **in that** an impure priest **imparts impurity** to others. The Gemara responds: **A blemished** priest **will prove** that this is no reason to reject the inference, as he cannot impart his blemish to others. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point. Therefore, one may derive the *halakha* of a non-priest from the combination of the case of a blemished priest and that of an impure priest: **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case, **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case. **Their common element** is **that they are prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites **and if they performed** these **rites they have desecrated** the service. Therefore, **I will also include a non-priest, who is prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites, **and** conclude that **if he performed** sacrificial **rites he has desecrated** the service.

To address this challenge, the Gemara introduces the *onen* — a *kohen* who has just suffered the death of an immediate relative — alongside the *baal mum*. Rather than using *tamei* and *baal mum* as the two poles of the *tzad hashaveh*, the Gemara substitutes *onen* for *tamei*. The critical question is whether an *onen* is prohibited on a *bamah*. Rav Samra son of Rava pointedly asks: *u'man leima lan de'onen assur bebama* — who says an *onen* cannot bring on a *bamah*? There is no explicit basis for that restriction. Since we have no evidence that an *onen* is barred from a *bamah*, we assume he is permitted there. This allows the *tzad hashaveh* to proceed: both *onen* and *baal mum* are prohibited in the *Beit HaMikdash* and invalidate — and crucially, at least one of them (the *onen*) may be permitted on a *bamah*, just like the *zar*. Therefore, the fact that a *zar* can bring on a *bamah* cannot be used to exempt him from the rule that prohibited persons invalidate.

### Bamah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן דְּמוּזְהָר? אִי מִ"וְּיִנָּזְרוּ" – חִילּוּל בְּגוּפֵיהּ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ! אֶלָּא מִ"וְּזָר לֹא יִקְרַב אֲלֵיכֶם" – אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתְּרוּ בְּבָמָה! לָא תֵּימָא: טָמֵא יוֹכִיחַ, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אוֹנֵן יוֹכִיחַ. מָה לְאוֹנֵן – שֶׁכֵּן אָסוּר בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר! [בַּעַל מוּם] יוֹכִיחַ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁמּוּזְהָרִין כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that** a non-priest **is prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites? **If** it is derived **from** the verse: "Speak to Aaron and to his sons, **that they separate themselves** from the sacred items of the children of Israel, and that they not profane My holy name" (Leviticus 22:2), then the *a fortiori* inference is unnecessary, since **profanation itself is written in** the verse. **Rather,** it must be that it is derived **from** the verse: "Keep the charge of the Tent of Meeting, whatever the service of the Tent may be; **but a common man shall not draw close to you"** (Numbers 18:4). The Gemara asks: Still, the *a fortiori* inference drawn from the cases of a blemished priest and an impure priest **can be refuted: What** is notable **about their common element?** It is notable **in that** a blemished priest and an impure priest **were not permitted** to perform sacrificial rites **on** a private **altar** during times when there was no Temple or permanent Tabernacle. Since it was permitted for non-priests to perform rites on private altars, perhaps non-priests do not desecrate the sacrificial rites performed in the Temple. The Gemara responds: **Do not say** that the case of **an impure** priest **will prove** the *a fortiori* inference with the case of a blemished priest; **rather, say** that the case of **an acute mourner will prove** it, as it is prohibited for him to perform the service and, if he were to perform it, he would desecrate it. This, too, is rejected: **What** is notable **about** the case of **an acute mourner?** It is notable **in that** he **is prohibited from** partaking of second **tithe,** whereas a non-priest may partake of second tithe. The Gemara responds: **A blemished** priest **will prove** the inference, as he may partake of second tithe. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point. **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case. **Their common element** is **that they are prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites *ab initio*, and they desecrate the service if they do so. Therefore, with regard to a non-priest, who is prohibited from performing sacrificial rites, if he performed sacrificial rites he has desecrated the service.

### Common Denominator Acceptance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי לִפְרוֹךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתְּרוּ בְּבָמָה! מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב סַמָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: וּמַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּאוֹנֵן אָסוּר בְּבָמָה? דִּלְמָא שְׁרֵי בְּבָמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Here, too, let one refute** the inference: **What** is notable **about their common element?** It is notable **in that** both an acute mourner and a blemished priest **were not permitted** to perform sacrificial rites **on** a private **altar,** unlike a non-priest. **Rav Samma, son of Rava, objects to this: And who shall say to us that an acute mourner** was **prohibited** from performing rites **on** a private **altar? Perhaps** it was **permitted** for him to perform the rites **on** a private **altar.**

The resulting *tzad hashaveh* is simply that both *onen* and *baal mum* are prohibited in a given state and, if they perform the *avodah* in that state, they invalidate. Since the *zar* is equally prohibited, he equally invalidates — and one cannot narrow the rule by pointing to the *bamah* permissibility of the *zar*, given that the *onen*, who shares in the common denominator, may himself be permitted on a *bamah*.

## Yoshev Kal VaChomer

### Yoshev Kal VaChomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא אָמַר, אָתְיָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִיּוֹשֵׁב: מָה יוֹשֵׁב, שֶׁאוֹכֵל – אִם עָבַד חִילֵּל; זָר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya says:** The *halakha* that a non-priest desecrates the service **is derived** by **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of a priest who performed sacrificial rites while **sitting: Just as** with regard to a priest who was **sitting, who may partake** of the meat of offerings, **if he performed** sacrificial **rites he has desecrated** the service, then with regard to **a non-priest, who may not partake** of the meat of offerings of the most sacred order, **is it not right that if he performed** sacrificial **rites he has desecrated** the service?

Rav Mesharshiya offers an independent derivation: a *kal vachomer* from the case of *yoshev* — a *kohen* who performs the *avodah* while sitting. The *kal vachomer* runs as follows: a *kohen* who sits is permitted to eat the *korbanot* — even *kodshei kodashim* need not be eaten while standing — yet if he performs the *avodah* while sitting, he invalidates it. A *zar*, who is not permitted to eat *kodshei kodashim* at all, should certainly invalidate if he performs the *avodah*.

### Yoshev Edus Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְיוֹשֵׁב – שֶׁכֵּן פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת! מִיּוֹשֵׁב תַּלְמִיד חָכָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **What** is notable **about** the case of **a sitting** priest? It is notable **in that** one who sits **is disqualified from bearing witness,** as witnesses must stand when testifying. Since there is an added aspect of stringency with regard to the case of a sitting priest, one cannot derive the *halakha* with regard to a non-priest from it. The Gemara responds: Learn instead **from** the *halakha* of **a sitting Torah scholar,** as the court may allow a Torah scholar to sit while testifying.

The Gemara challenges this *kal vachomer*: *mah leyoshev sheken pasul le'edut* — sitting entails a disqualification for testimony as well. Perhaps sitting is a uniquely weighty category of invalidity, and therefore one cannot draw an inference from it to the case of a *zar*, whose disqualification does not extend to testimony.

### Talmid Chacham Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְשֵׁם יוֹשֵׁב, שֶׁכֵּן פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת! שֵׁם יוֹשֵׁב לָא פָּרֵיךְ. וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר פָּרֵיךְ – אָתְיָא מִיּוֹשֵׁב וּמֵחֲדָא מֵהָנָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: Still, one cannot derive the *halakha* from this, as **what** is notable about **the category of a sitting** priest? It is notable **in that** generally speaking, one who sits **is disqualified from bearing witness,** even though there are exceptions. The Gemara responds: **The category of a sitting** priest **cannot refute** the derivation. One can refute a derivation only from concrete cases, not general concepts. **And** even **if you say it can refute** the derivation, the *halakha* with regard to a non-priest **can be derived from** the case of **a sitting** priest **and from one of those** other cases, i.e., a blemished priest, an impure priest, or an acute mourner, all of whom may testify.

The Gemara deflects this challenge: *v'afilu teima yoshev talmid chacham* — there are cases where a *talmid chacham* is permitted to give testimony while seated, as a mark of respect accorded to him. Since there are instances of sitting that are valid for testimony, one cannot categorically characterize *yoshev* as more stringently invalid than other disqualified states. The Gemara states *shem yoshev lo parach* — the sitting-equals-testimony-invalidity objection does not hold, because the existence of cases where a sitting person's testimony is accepted means that *yoshev* cannot be bracketed as uniquely severe.

### Tzad HaShaveh Alternative

Even if one insists that the *yoshev* category is weightier because some *yoshev* cases are invalid for testimony, the Gemara offers an alternative: construct a *tzad hashaveh* from *yoshev* together with one of the other disqualified categories — *baal mum*, *tamei*, *onen* — and the common denominator of those cases will yield the same conclusion without relying on *yoshev* alone.

### Lifnei Hashem Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹשֵׁב דְּכָשֵׁר בְּבָמָה – מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: "לַעֲמֹד לִפְנֵי ה׳ לְשָׁרְתוֹ" – לִפְנֵי ה׳ וְלֹא לִפְנֵי בָּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** one who is **sitting is fit** to perform sacrificial rites **on** a private **altar?** If he is not, one can refute the common element in the same manner as above. The Gemara responds: **The verse states:** "The Lord separated the tribe of Levi to bear the Ark of the Covenant of the Lord, **to stand before the Lord to minister to Him"** (Deuteronomy 10:8). The verse indicates that the sacrificial rites must be performed while standing only **before the Lord,** i.e., in the Temple, where the Divine Presence resides, **and not before** a private **altar,** which is a mere place of worship.

The discussion of *yoshev* raises the question of how we know a *kohen* who performs the *avodah* sitting is *mutar* on a *bamah*, a point required to make the *tzad hashaveh* work properly (so that not all members of the common denominator are disqualified on a *bamah*). The source is the verse *la'amod lifnei Hashem lesharto* — "to stand before God to serve Him." The requirement of standing is derived from the phrase *lifnei Hashem*, which specifically designates the *Beit HaMikdash* as the place of the divine presence in this legal sense. A *bamah*, by contrast, is not *lifnei Hashem* in this technical sense — one may offer sacrifices there without the standing requirement applying. Therefore, *yoshev* is valid on a *bamah*, which is precisely what the *tzad hashaveh* needs.

### General Rule Established

The upshot is a broad general principle: any person who is in a prohibited status invalidates the *korban* if he performs the *avodah*, and one cannot escape this rule by pointing to the exceptions each category enjoys in certain contexts. *Baal mum*, *tamei*, *onen*, and *yoshev* all have situations in which they are permitted — yet all invalidate. The principle therefore extends uniformly: prohibition entails invalidation.

## Onen Invalidation

### Kohen Gadol Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹנֵן מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: "וּמִן הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לֹא יֵצֵא, וְלֹא יְחַלֵּל" – הָא אַחֵר שֶׁלֹּא יָצָא חִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that rites performed by **an acute mourner** are disqualified. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As it is written** with regard to a High Priest who is an acute mourner: **"And he shall not leave the Sanctuary, and he will not profane** the Sanctuary of his God" (Leviticus 21:12). One can infer: **But** any **other** ordinary priest **who did not leave** the Sanctuary while he was an acute mourner and continued to perform the service **has desecrated** the rites he performed.

The Gemara turns to the *onen* specifically and asks: *onein minalan* — from where do we know that an *onen* invalidates? The first proposed source is a verse concerning the *Kohen Gadol*: *u'min hamikdash lo yetzei v'lo yechalel* — "and from the Sanctuary he shall not go out, and he shall not desecrate." The *Kohen Gadol*, even as an *onen*, must remain in the *Beit HaMikdash* and does not desecrate the sacrifices. The inference is: *ha'acher shelo yatza chillel* — an ordinary *kohen*, who is not commanded to remain, would invalidate if he stayed and performed the service while an *onen*.

### Hen HaKrivu Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר מֵהָכָא: "הֵן הִקְרִיבוּ" – אֲנִי הִקְרַבְתִּי, מִכְּלָל דְּאִי אִינְהוּ אַקְרִיב – שַׁפִּיר אִישְׂתְּרוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** One can derive it **from here** instead: When Moses asked Aaron why the sin offering that was sacrificed on the day that Nadav and Avihu died was burned and not eaten, he suspected that Aaron's other sons, Eleazar and Itamar, had sacrificed the sin offering while acute mourners, which disqualified it and forced them to burn it. Aaron responded to Moses: "This day have **they offered** their sin offering?" (Leviticus 10:19). Rather, **I offered** it. As High Priest, I do not desecrate the Temple service even when I am an acute mourner; I burned it only because, as acute mourners, my sons and I are prohibited from partaking of the meat. Rabbi Elazar continues: **By inference,** one can derive **that if they had offered** the sin offering they would have disqualified it, and it would have been **proper** that **it was burned.**

Rabbi Eliezer derives the same principle from a different verse: *hen hikrivu* — the exchange between Moshe and Aharon following the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, recorded in the Torah. The Gemara presents a reading of that exchange in which Moshe raises the possibility that Elazar and Itamar, as *kohanim hedyotim* who were *onanim* after their brothers' deaths, may have brought the *chatat* — and if so, as *kohanim hedyotim* performing the *avodah* as *onanim*, they would have invalidated it. Aharon responds that it was he — the *Kohen Gadol* — who brought it, and the *Kohen Gadol* may perform the *avodah* as an *onen*. From this exchange one infers that had the *kohanim hedyotim* brought it, it would indeed have been invalidated.

### Nadav & Avihu Context

The underlying factual context is that Nadav and Avihu died, rendering Elazar and Itamar *onanim*. Aharon, as *Kohen Gadol*, is governed by the rule *kohen gadol makriv onein v'eino ochel* — the *Kohen Gadol* brings sacrifices as an *onen* but does not eat them as an *onen*. The *kohen hedyot*, by contrast, may neither bring nor eat as an *onen*. So Aharon's response — "I was the one who brought it" — serves to explain that no invalidation occurred, since the *Kohen Gadol* may perform the *avodah* in a state of *aninut*.

There is a secondary question about why the meat was burned at all, if Aharon's bringing it was valid. One answer: the verse's emphasis is *hen hayom* — "they brought it today." Being *onanim*, Elazar and Itamar could not eat the *chatat* during the day, and if one holds *aninus layla* applies through the night as well, the meat remained uneaten until it became *notar*, requiring burning. The burning was thus the consequence of the *onen*'s inability to eat, not of any invalidation of the offering itself.

### Gemara Rejects Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מִ"וּמִן הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לֹא יֵצֵא"? אָמַר לָךְ, מִי כְּתִיב: הָא אַחֵר שֶׁלֹּא יָצָא – חִילֵּל?! וְאִידַּךְ – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מֵ"הֵן הִקְרִיבוּ"? קָסָבַר: מִפְּנֵי טוּמְאָה נִשְׂרְפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Rabbi Elazar does not say** that this *halakha* is derived **from** the verse: **"And he shall not leave the Sanctuary"?** The Gemara responds: He could have **said to you: Is it written: But** any **other** ordinary priest **that did not leave** the Sanctuary **has desecrated** the rites he performed? It is only an inference, and so it is not conclusive. The Gemara asks: **And the other** Sage, who derives it from the verse: "And he shall not leave the Sanctuary," **what is the reason** that **he did not say** to derive it **from** the verse: "This day have **they offered** their sin offering?" The Gemara responds: He **holds** that the sin offering brought by Aaron **was burned because** it became **ritually impure,** not because Aaron and his sons were acute mourners.

The Gemara then asks why Rabbi Eliezer does not simply use the *min hamikdash lo yetzei* verse, and conversely, why the first opinion does not use *hen hikrivu*. The answer to the first question: *mi ktiv ha'acher shelo yatza chillel* — the verse does not explicitly state that a different (*hedyot*) *kohen* who stays would invalidate. The verse tells us only that the *Kohen Gadol* who stays does not desecrate. One might equally read the verse as saying the same is true of a *kohen hedyot* — just as the *Kohen Gadol*'s remaining does not invalidate, so too a *kohen hedyot*'s remaining does not invalidate. The negative implication (that the *kohen hedyot* would invalidate) is not logically entailed by the verse; it is a possible reading but not a forced one.

The answer to the second question: the reading of *hen hikrivu* as implying that the *kohanim hedyotim* would have invalidated is rejected by the second opinion because, on that opinion's reading, the reason the meat was burned was not *aninut* at all but *tumah*. If the meat became *tamei*, that explains the burning independently, and the inference from the exchange between Moshe and Aharon collapses. The *onen* issue becomes irrelevant to the burning on this reading, and therefore one cannot derive from the exchange that an *onen hedyot* invalidates. The derivation from *hen hikrivu* then proceeds through a *gezeirah shavah* rather than a direct inference from the *pshat* of the verse.

### Kal VaChomer Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: אָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִבַּעַל מוּם; וּמָה בַּעַל מוּם, שֶׁאוֹכֵל – אִם עָבַד חִילֵּל; אוֹנֵן, שֶׁאֵין אוֹכֵל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל?!
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* of the **school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The *halakha* **is derived by an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of **a blemished** priest: **And just as** with regard to **a blemished** priest, **who may partake** of the meat of offerings, **if he performed** sacrificial **rites he has desecrated** the service, then with regard to **an acute mourner, who may not partake** of the meat of offerings, **is it not right that if he performed** sacrificial **rites he has desecrated** the service?

The school of Rabbi Yishmael (*d'vei Rabbi Yishmael*) takes yet a different approach, deriving through a *kal vachomer* from *baal mum*: if a *baal mum*, who is permitted to eat the *korban*, nonetheless invalidates when he performs the *avodah*, then an *onen*, who is not permitted to eat, should certainly invalidate.

### Baal Mum vs. Zar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְבַעַל מוּם, שֶׁכֵּן עָשָׂה בּוֹ קְרֵיבִין כְּמַקְרִיבִין! זָר יוֹכִיחַ. מָה לְזָר, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה! בַּעַל מוּם יוֹכִיחַ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין. לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה; הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁהֵן מוּזְהָרִין, וְאִם עָבְדוּ חִילְּלוּ; אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אוֹנֵן – שֶׁמּוּזְהָר, וְאִם עָבַד חִילֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the inference: One cannot derive this *halakha* from the case of a blemished priest, as **what** is notable **about a blemished** priest? It is notable **in that** the Torah **rendered** animals that **are sacrificed like** the priests who **sacrifice** them. Since this stringency does not exist with regard to acute mourning, perhaps an acute mourner may perform sacrificial rites as well. The Gemara responds: The case of **a non-priest will prove** that this is no reason to reject the *a fortiori* inference, since there is no equivalence between priest and sacrifice in this regard, yet a non-priest desecrates the service. The Gemara rejects this as well: One cannot cite a proof from the case of a non-priest, as **what** is notable **about** the case of **a non-priest?** It is notable **in that he has no remedy,** i.e., a non-priest may never perform the sacrificial rites. By contrast, an acute mourner will eventually become permitted to perform the service. The Gemara responds: **A blemished** priest **will prove** that this is no reason to reject the inference, as even though the priest may perform the service if the blemish is healed, he desecrates the service so long as he remains blemished. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point. Therefore, one learns the *halakha* from the combination of the cases of a blemished priest and a non-priest: **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case, **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case. **Their common element is that they are prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites, **and if they performed** such **rites they have desecrated** the service. Therefore, **I will also include an acute mourner, who is prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites, **and** conclude that **if he performed** sacrificial **rites he has desecrated** the service.

The Gemara challenges: *mah l'baal mum sheken asah bo korbanin kemakrivin* — perhaps the *baal mum* has greater disqualifying power because the concept of *mum* applies not only to the *kohen* but to the animal itself. An *onen* has no such extension of disqualification to the *korban*. The Gemara addresses this by introducing the *zar* alongside the *baal mum*: the *zar*'s disqualification does not apply to the animal at all, so the combination of *zar* and *baal mum* neutralizes this distinctive feature.

### Takana Challenge

A further challenge: *mah l'zar sheken ein lo takana* — a *zar* can never stop being a *zar*; his status is permanent. An *onen*, by contrast, has a *takana* — his status is temporary, lasting only through the day. Perhaps only permanent disqualifications generate invalidation. The Gemara responds: *baal mum yochiach* — the *baal mum* proves otherwise, since a *mum* can be temporary (*mum over*) and the *baal mum* nonetheless invalidates.

### Zar & Mum Tzad

The Gemara then brings the full *tzad hashaveh*: *lo rei zeh kirei zeh v'lo rei zeh kirei zeh* — each case has its own unique characteristic that the other lacks. The *zar* has no exceptions but his disqualification does not extend to the animal; the *baal mum*'s disqualification extends to the animal but may be temporary. Neither encompasses the other's distinctive feature. The *hatzad hashaveh shebahen* — the common denominator — is simply that both are prohibited from performing the *avodah* in their respective states and, if they do so, they invalidate. An *onen*, who is equally prohibited, therefore equally invalidates.

### Onen Prohibition Source

To deploy this *tzad hashaveh*, however, one must first establish the prohibition independently. Where is the *onen* prohibited? If we say it is from the *min hamikdash lo yetzei* verse, then the invalidation itself is already packaged in that verse (*chillul begufei ketiv bei*), and a *tzad hashaveh* is unnecessary. If we say the prohibition is derived from *hen hikrivu* — the exchange of Moshe and Aharon — then the *pshat* of that passage implies prohibition but not necessarily invalidation as its explicit content.

### Kohen Gadol Source Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָן מוּזְהָר? אִילֵּימָא מִ"וּמִן הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לֹא יֵצֵא", חִילּוּל בְּגוּפֵיהּ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ! אֶלָּא מֵ"הֵן הִקְרִיבוּ" – וְקָסָבַר מִפְּנֵי אֲנִינוּת נִשְׂרְפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Where is** an acute mourner **prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites, as asserted in the inference? **If we say** that it is derived **from** the verse: **"And he shall not leave the Sanctuary,** and he will not profane the Sanctuary of his God" (Leviticus 21:12), then the above *a fortiori* inference is unnecessary, since **profanation itself is written in** the verse. **Rather,** it must be that it is derived **from** the verse: "This day have **they offered** their sin offering?" (Leviticus 10:19), **and** this *tanna* **holds** that the sin offering brought by Aaron **was burned because** Aaron and his sons were in **acute mourning.**

The Gemara makes the point sharply: from *hen hikrivu* one can at most learn the *issur* — the prohibition on the *kohen hedyot* to perform the *avodah* as an *onen*. The actual rule that he *invalidates* if he does so is derived through the *gezeirah shavah*, not from the verse's plain meaning. This is why both derivations — from the *Kohen Gadol* verse and from *hen hikrivu* — are needed, each contributing a different element.

### Tamei Exception Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִפְרַךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלוֹ! טָמֵא יוֹכִיחַ. מָה לְטָמֵא, שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא! הָנָךְ יוֹכִיחוּ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין כּוּ׳, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁמּוּזְהָרִין כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The inference from the common element of the cases of a blemished priest and a non-priest **can be refuted: What** is notable **about their common element?** It is notable **in that** there are **no** circumstances in which **its general prohibition was permitted.** There is an exception to the prohibition against an acute mourner performing the Temple service, namely the High Priest, who may perform the sacrificial rites while an acute mourner. The Gemara responds: The case of **an impure** priest **will prove** that this is no reason to reject the inference, as there is an exception to the prohibition against performing the service while impure, namely that the prohibition against performing the Temple service in a state of impurity is permitted in cases involving the public, yet an impure priest desecrates the service. The Gemara asks: **What** is notable **about** the case of **an impure** priest? It is notable **in that he imparts impurity** to others. Perhaps it is only for this reason that an impure priest desecrates the service. The Gemara responds: **Those** other cases, i.e., a blemished priest and a non-priest, **will prove** that this does not reject the inference, since they do not impart their status to others. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point. Therefore, one can derive the *halakha* from the combination of the cases of an impure priest, a blemished priest, and a non-priest: The aspect of this case is not like the aspect of that case; **their common element is that they are prohibited** from performing sacrificial rites and they desecrate the service. Therefore, I will also conclude that since an acute mourner is prohibited from performing sacrificial rites, he desecrates the service.

The Gemara next confronts a challenge to the *tzad hashaveh* itself: *mah lehatzad hashaveh shebahen sheken lo hutru miklalan* — both *zar* and *baal mum* are never permitted in the *Beit HaMikdash* in their respective states; there are no exceptions. An *onen*, however, does have an exception — the *Kohen Gadol*. Perhaps the rule "prohibited entails invalidation" applies only where there is an absolute prohibition with no exceptions, and since the *onen* prohibition has an exception (the *Kohen Gadol*), it does not generate invalidation for the *kohen hedyot*.

The Gemara answers: *tamei yochiach* — throw *tamei* into the *tzad hashaveh*. *Tumah* has an exception: *tumah hutra betzibur* — ritual impurity is permitted for communal offerings. Yet a *tamei* person who performs the *avodah* when not permitted to (i.e., for a private offering) nonetheless invalidates. Since the exception does not eliminate the invalidation by *tamei*, the same logic applies to the *onen*: the existence of an exception for the *Kohen Gadol* does not mean that when the *onen hedyot* transgresses and performs the *avodah*, he fails to invalidate. *Hanakh yochichu v'chazar hadin* — combining all the cases, no single distinctive feature can be used to bracket the *onen* out of the general principle.

## Rava's Korban Yachid/Tzibur Position

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹנֵן פָּסוּל. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד, אֲבָל בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר – מְרַצֶּה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִטּוּמְאָה;
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **an acute mourner** collects the blood of an offering, the offering is **disqualified. Rava says: They taught** this **only with regard to an individual's offering. But with regard to a communal offering,** a rite performed by an acute mourner does **effect acceptance.** One can derive this by ***a fortiori*** inference **from ritual impurity.**

Rava now proposes a significant qualification: the rule that an *onen* invalidates applies only to a *korban yachid* — a private individual offering. With respect to a *korban tzibur* — a communal offering — an *onen hedyot* who performs the *avodah* does not invalidate; rather, it is *meratzeh*, acceptable.

### Rava's Logic Grid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה טוּמְאָה, שֶׁלֹּא הוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד – הוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר; אֲנִינוּת, שֶׁהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקָרְבַּן יָחִיד – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר?!
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** the prohibition against performing rites in a state of **ritual impurity, whose general prohibition was not permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering,** as no individual offering may be sacrificed in a state of impurity, **was** nevertheless **permitted in** the case of **an ordinary [*hedyot*]** priest performing rites **for a communal offering,** as communal offerings may be sacrificed by an impure priest when necessary; then so too, with regard to the prohibition against performing rites while in a period of **acute mourning, whose general prohibition was permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering,** as the High Priest may perform all rites while an acute mourner, **is it not right that** this prohibition **was permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering?**

Rava's reasoning proceeds through a *kal vachomer* from *tumah*. The structure requires holding several parameters simultaneously. By *tumah*: a *Kohen Gadol* performing the *avodah* for a *korban yachid* in a state of *tumah* is not permitted — there is no exception for a *Kohen Gadol* with *tumah* by a private offering. Yet a *kohen hedyot* performing the *avodah* for a *korban tzibur* in a state of *tumah* is permitted — *tumah hutra betzibur*. Now by *aninut*: a *Kohen Gadol* performing the *avodah* for a *korban yachid* as an *onen* is permitted — the *Kohen Gadol* may bring even a private offering while an *onen*. The *kal vachomer* then argues: if *tumah*, which is not permitted even by the *Kohen Gadol* for a *korban yachid*, is nonetheless permitted by a *kohen hedyot* for a *korban tzibur*, then *aninut*, which is more lenient in that it is permitted by the *Kohen Gadol* even for a *korban yachid*, should certainly be permitted by a *kohen hedyot* for a *korban tzibur*.

## Rava Bar Ahilai Refutes

### Multiple QV Paths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא בַּר אֲהִילַאי: לֹא תּוּתַּר אֲנִינוּת אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד, מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה טוּמְאָה, שֶׁהוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּצִבּוּר – לֹא הוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד; אֲנִינוּת, שֶׁלֹּא הוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תּוּתַּר אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Ahilai objects to this:** On the contrary, if such an inference can be made, then perhaps one can make the following mistaken inference: The performance of rites in a state of **acute mourning should not be permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering, by *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** the prohibition against performing rites in a state of **ritual impurity,** whose general prohibition **was permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering, was not permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering;** then so too, with regard to the prohibition against performing the rites during a period of **acute mourning,** whose general prohibition **was not permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering, is it not right that** this prohibition **should not be permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering?**

Rava bar Ahilai challenges Rava's *kal vachomer* by demonstrating that the same grid of parameters admits multiple alternative inferences, each of which yields a different — and equally unacceptable — conclusion. The problem is that Rava's argument assumes certain cells of the grid as fixed and derives the unknown from them. But there is no principled reason why those particular cells should be the fixed ones; other assignments of fixed and variable yields contradictory results.

### Alt 1: Onen Limits

One could instead run the inference in the opposite direction regarding *oninut* by the *Kohen Gadol*: since *tumah*, which is permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by *korban tzibur*, is not permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by *korban yachid*, perhaps *aninut*, which is not permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by *korban tzibur* (starting from that assumption), is also not permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by *korban yachid*. In other words, rather than deriving that the *kohen hedyot* is permitted as an *onen* for *korban tzibur*, one could derive that the *Kohen Gadol* is *not* permitted as an *onen* for a *korban yachid* — which directly contradicts the explicit *pshat* of *min hamikdash lo yetzei*.

### Alt 2: Tumah Extends

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּתַּר טוּמְאָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד, מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֲנִינוּת, שֶׁלֹּא הוּתַּר אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר – הוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד; טוּמְאָה, שֶׁהוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד?!
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, one may claim: The performance of rites in a state of **ritual impurity should be permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering, by *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** the prohibition against performing rites in a state of **acute mourning,** whose general prohibition **was not permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering,** still **was permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering;** then so too, with regard to the prohibition against performing the rites in a state of **ritual impurity,** whose general prohibition **was permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering, is it not right that** this prohibition **was permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering?**

Alternatively, one could argue in the opposite direction for *tumah* by the *Kohen Gadol*: since *aninut* is permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid* (which is a place where *tumah* is not permitted), and since *tumah* is permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by a *korban tzibur* (where *aninut* is not permitted), perhaps *tumah* should also be permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid*. This contradicts the well-established rule that *tumah* has no exception for private offerings regardless of who performs the *avodah*.

### Alt 3: Tumah Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא תּוּתַּר טוּמְאָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר, מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֲנִינוּת, שֶׁהוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד – לֹא הוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר; טוּמְאָה, שֶׁלֹּא הוּתְּרָה אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּקָרְבַּן יָחִיד – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תּוּתַּר בְּכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט בְּקׇרְבַּן צִבּוּר?!
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, one may claim: The performance of rites in a state of **ritual impurity should not be permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering, by *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** the prohibition against performing rites in a state of **acute mourning,** whose general prohibition **was permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual's offering,** still **was not permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering;** then so too, with regard to the prohibition against performing the rites in a state of **ritual impurity,** whose general prohibition **was not permitted in** the case of **a High Priest** performing rites **for an individual offering, is it not right that** this prohibition **should not be permitted in** the case of **an ordinary priest** performing rites **for a communal offering?**

A third alternative runs the *kal vachomer* so as to restrict *tumah* even by *korban tzibur*: since *aninut* is permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid* and yet is not permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by a *korban tzibur*, perhaps *tumah*, which is not permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid*, should equally not be permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by a *korban tzibur*. This would overturn the entire principle of *tumah hutra betzibur* as applied to ordinary *kohanim*.

### Pshat Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ הָכִי וְאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ הָכִי, כֹּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא תֵּיקוּ בְּדוּכְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one **can derive this** way **and** one **can derive that** way. Since these derivations contradict one another, **each and every** *halakha* **shall stand in its place** and not modify the other by *a fortiori* inference.

Since any of these inferences is logically as valid as Rava's original *kal vachomer*, and since each leads to a conclusion that contradicts established law, the Gemara concludes: *ika l'mifrach hachi v'ika l'mifrach hachi* — you can challenge in this direction and you can challenge in that direction. The grid-based *kal vachomer* reasoning breaks down entirely because there is no way to determine which parameter to hold fixed and which to derive. The Gemara therefore rules: *kocha d'hacha tichu b'duchtei* — let each law stand as it is, in its own place. The rule about *tumah* belongs to the category of *tzibur*; the rule about *onen* belongs to the category of *Kohen Gadol*. These are independently derived principles, not nodes in a logical grid from which further inferences can be pressed. Attempting to construct *kal vachomers* across these categories leads only to confusion, and Rava's proposed leniency for an *onen* by *korban tzibur* is accordingly rejected.
