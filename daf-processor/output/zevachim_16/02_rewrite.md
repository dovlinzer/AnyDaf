# Zevachim 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Ch. 2 Overview

### Opening & Topic Setup

Daf 16 opens at the beginning of the second *perek*, which moves beyond the discussion of *shelo lishma* and into a broader inquiry about the various circumstances — primarily relating to the personal status of the officiating *kohen* — that invalidate a sacrifice. This chapter presents a whole list of categories of persons who, if they perform the *avodah*, render the *korban* invalid.

### Kabbalat HaDam Review

Because *shechita* is technically valid even when performed by a non-*kohen*, the Mishnah focuses on *kabbalat hadam* as the first *avodah* under discussion, so that the full list of disqualifying statuses can be surveyed properly. The Gemara has established — through a *tzad hashaveh*, a common denominator argument — that just as a *kohen* who is *tamei* or a *baal mum* invalidates if he performs the *avodah*, so too does a non-*kohen*. The general principle emerging is: anyone who is prohibited from performing the *avodah* in a given state will, if he performs it anyway, invalidate the *korban*. The Gemara now examines each case to establish both the prohibition and the invalidation.

---

## Zar & Tzad HaShaveh

### Source of Zar Prohibition

With the general principle in place, the Gemara asks: where do we know that a non-*kohen* is prohibited from performing the *avodah* in the first place? The *tzad hashaveh* requires a proven prohibition as its starting point, so the source of the *zar*'s prohibition must be identified independently.

### Veyinnazru vs. Vezar

One might have thought the source is the verse *veyinnazru mikodeshei benei Yisrael* — "they shall be separated from the holy things of the children of Israel" — which could, on one reading, include a *zar*. But the Gemara immediately sets this aside: the plain sense (*pshat*) of that verse concerns a *kohen* who is *tamei*, not a non-*kohen*. More decisively, if we were to read the verse as referring to a *zar*, the verse itself already contains the word *yechalelu* — it would explicitly state both the prohibition and the invalidation, making the entire *tzad hashaveh* unnecessary. Since we are constructing a *tzad hashaveh* precisely because we need to derive the invalidation, we must be reading that verse as referring to *tumah* alone. The source for the *zar*'s prohibition is therefore the explicit verse *vezar lo yikrav aleichem* — "a stranger shall not come near you" — which plainly bars a non-*kohen* from performing the Temple service.

### Bamah Challenge

With the prohibition of the *zar* established, the *tzad hashaveh* from *tamei* and *baal mum* would seem to yield the rule that the *zar* also invalidates. But the Gemara raises a challenge: *mah letzad hashaveh shebahen sheken lo hutru bebama* — what is distinctive about the common denominator, the cases of *tamei* and *baal mum*, is that neither of them is permitted to perform sacrifices outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. A *tamei* person and a *baal mum* remain disqualified even at a private altar (*bamah*). But a *zar* is permitted to bring a *korban* on a *bamah* — he is only barred in the *Beit HaMikdash* itself, where the *kohen* functions as part of the Temple apparatus. Perhaps, then, the *zar*'s disqualification is categorically different: since he is sometimes permitted, maybe he does not rise to the level of invalidating the *korban*.

### Onen & Baal Mum Fix

To address this challenge, the Gemara introduces the *onen* — a *kohen* who has just suffered the death of an immediate relative — alongside the *baal mum*. Rather than using *tamei* and *baal mum* as the two poles of the *tzad hashaveh*, the Gemara substitutes *onen* for *tamei*. The critical question is whether an *onen* is prohibited on a *bamah*. Rav Samra son of Rava pointedly asks: *u'man leima lan de'onen assur bebama* — who says an *onen* cannot bring on a *bamah*? There is no explicit basis for that restriction. Since we have no evidence that an *onen* is barred from a *bamah*, we assume he is permitted there. This allows the *tzad hashaveh* to proceed: both *onen* and *baal mum* are prohibited in the *Beit HaMikdash* and invalidate — and crucially, at least one of them (the *onen*) may be permitted on a *bamah*, just like the *zar*. Therefore, the fact that a *zar* can bring on a *bamah* cannot be used to exempt him from the rule that prohibited persons invalidate.

### Common Denominator Accep…

The resulting *tzad hashaveh* is simply that both *onen* and *baal mum* are prohibited in a given state and, if they perform the *avodah* in that state, they invalidate. Since the *zar* is equally prohibited, he equally invalidates — and one cannot narrow the rule by pointing to the *bamah* permissibility of the *zar*, given that the *onen*, who shares in the common denominator, may himself be permitted on a *bamah*.

## Yoshev Kal VaChomer

### Yoshev Kal VaChomer

Rav Mesharshiya offers an independent derivation: a *kal vachomer* from the case of *yoshev* — a *kohen* who performs the *avodah* while sitting. The *kal vachomer* runs as follows: a *kohen* who sits is permitted to eat the *korbanot* — even *kodshei kodashim* need not be eaten while standing — yet if he performs the *avodah* while sitting, he invalidates it. A *zar*, who is not permitted to eat *kodshei kodashim* at all, should certainly invalidate if he performs the *avodah*.

### Yoshev Edus Challenge

The Gemara challenges this *kal vachomer*: *mah leyoshev sheken pasul le'edut* — sitting entails a disqualification for testimony as well. Perhaps sitting is a uniquely weighty category of invalidity, and therefore one cannot draw an inference from it to the case of a *zar*, whose disqualification does not extend to testimony.

### Talmid Chacham Exception

The Gemara deflects this challenge: *v'afilu teima yoshev talmid chacham* — there are cases where a *talmid chacham* is permitted to give testimony while seated, as a mark of respect accorded to him. Since there are instances of sitting that are valid for testimony, one cannot categorically characterize *yoshev* as more stringently invalid than other disqualified states. The Gemara states *shem yoshev lo parach* — the sitting-equals-testimony-invalidity objection does not hold, because the existence of cases where a sitting person's testimony is accepted means that *yoshev* cannot be bracketed as uniquely severe.

### Tzad HaShaveh Alternative

Even if one insists that the *yoshev* category is weightier because some *yoshev* cases are invalid for testimony, the Gemara offers an alternative: construct a *tzad hashaveh* from *yoshev* together with one of the other disqualified categories — *baal mum*, *tamei*, *onen* — and the common denominator of those cases will yield the same conclusion without relying on *yoshev* alone.

### Lifnei Hashem Source

The discussion of *yoshev* raises the question of how we know a *kohen* who performs the *avodah* sitting is *mutar* on a *bamah*, a point required to make the *tzad hashaveh* work properly (so that not all members of the common denominator are disqualified on a *bamah*). The source is the verse *la'amod lifnei Hashem lesharto* — "to stand before God to serve Him." The requirement of standing is derived from the phrase *lifnei Hashem*, which specifically designates the *Beit HaMikdash* as the place of the divine presence in this legal sense. A *bamah*, by contrast, is not *lifnei Hashem* in this technical sense — one may offer sacrifices there without the standing requirement applying. Therefore, *yoshev* is valid on a *bamah*, which is precisely what the *tzad hashaveh* needs.

### General Rule Established

The upshot is a broad general principle: any person who is in a prohibited status invalidates the *korban* if he performs the *avodah*, and one cannot escape this rule by pointing to the exceptions each category enjoys in certain contexts. *Baal mum*, *tamei*, *onen*, and *yoshev* all have situations in which they are permitted — yet all invalidate. The principle therefore extends uniformly: prohibition entails invalidation.

## Onen Invalidation

### Kohen Gadol Source

The Gemara turns to the *onen* specifically and asks: *onein minalan* — from where do we know that an *onen* invalidates? The first proposed source is a verse concerning the *Kohen Gadol*: *u'min hamikdash lo yetzei v'lo yechalel* — "and from the Sanctuary he shall not go out, and he shall not desecrate." The *Kohen Gadol*, even as an *onen*, must remain in the *Beit HaMikdash* and does not desecrate the sacrifices. The inference is: *ha'acher shelo yatza chillel* — an ordinary *kohen*, who is not commanded to remain, would invalidate if he stayed and performed the service while an *onen*.

### Hen HaKrivu Debate

Rabbi Eliezer derives the same principle from a different verse: *hen hikrivu* — the exchange between Moshe and Aharon following the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, recorded in the Torah. The Gemara presents a reading of that exchange in which Moshe raises the possibility that Elazar and Itamar, as *kohanim hedyotim* who were *onanim* after their brothers' deaths, may have brought the *chatat* — and if so, as *kohanim hedyotim* performing the *avodah* as *onanim*, they would have invalidated it. Aharon responds that it was he — the *Kohen Gadol* — who brought it, and the *Kohen Gadol* may perform the *avodah* as an *onen*. From this exchange one infers that had the *kohanim hedyotim* brought it, it would indeed have been invalidated.

### Nadav & Avihu Context

The underlying factual context is that Nadav and Avihu died, rendering Elazar and Itamar *onanim*. Aharon, as *Kohen Gadol*, is governed by the rule *kohen gadol makriv onein v'eino ochel* — the *Kohen Gadol* brings sacrifices as an *onen* but does not eat them as an *onen*. The *kohen hedyot*, by contrast, may neither bring nor eat as an *onen*. So Aharon's response — "I was the one who brought it" — serves to explain that no invalidation occurred, since the *Kohen Gadol* may perform the *avodah* in a state of *aninut*.

There is a secondary question about why the meat was burned at all, if Aharon's bringing it was valid. One answer: the verse's emphasis is *hen hayom* — "they brought it today." Being *onanim*, Elazar and Itamar could not eat the *chatat* during the day, and if one holds *aninus layla* applies through the night as well, the meat remained uneaten until it became *notar*, requiring burning. The burning was thus the consequence of the *onen*'s inability to eat, not of any invalidation of the offering itself.

### Gemara Rejects Both

The Gemara then asks why Rabbi Eliezer does not simply use the *min hamikdash lo yetzei* verse, and conversely, why the first opinion does not use *hen hikrivu*. The answer to the first question: *mi ktiv ha'acher shelo yatza chillel* — the verse does not explicitly state that a different (*hedyot*) *kohen* who stays would invalidate. The verse tells us only that the *Kohen Gadol* who stays does not desecrate. One might equally read the verse as saying the same is true of a *kohen hedyot* — just as the *Kohen Gadol*'s remaining does not invalidate, so too a *kohen hedyot*'s remaining does not invalidate. The negative implication (that the *kohen hedyot* would invalidate) is not logically entailed by the verse; it is a possible reading but not a forced one.

The answer to the second question: the reading of *hen hikrivu* as implying that the *kohanim hedyotim* would have invalidated is rejected by the second opinion because, on that opinion's reading, the reason the meat was burned was not *aninut* at all but *tumah*. If the meat became *tamei*, that explains the burning independently, and the inference from the exchange between Moshe and Aharon collapses. The *onen* issue becomes irrelevant to the burning on this reading, and therefore one cannot derive from the exchange that an *onen hedyot* invalidates. The derivation from *hen hikrivu* then proceeds through a *gezeirah shavah* rather than a direct inference from the *pshat* of the verse.

### Kal VaChomer Approach

The school of Rabbi Yishmael (*d'vei Rabbi Yishmael*) takes yet a different approach, deriving through a *kal vachomer* from *baal mum*: if a *baal mum*, who is permitted to eat the *korban*, nonetheless invalidates when he performs the *avodah*, then an *onen*, who is not permitted to eat, should certainly invalidate.

### Baal Mum vs. Zar

The Gemara challenges: *mah l'baal mum sheken asah bo korbanin kemakrivin* — perhaps the *baal mum* has greater disqualifying power because the concept of *mum* applies not only to the *kohen* but to the animal itself. An *onen* has no such extension of disqualification to the *korban*. The Gemara addresses this by introducing the *zar* alongside the *baal mum*: the *zar*'s disqualification does not apply to the animal at all, so the combination of *zar* and *baal mum* neutralizes this distinctive feature.

### Takana Challenge

A further challenge: *mah l'zar sheken ein lo takana* — a *zar* can never stop being a *zar*; his status is permanent. An *onen*, by contrast, has a *takana* — his status is temporary, lasting only through the day. Perhaps only permanent disqualifications generate invalidation. The Gemara responds: *baal mum yochiach* — the *baal mum* proves otherwise, since a *mum* can be temporary (*mum over*) and the *baal mum* nonetheless invalidates.

### Zar & Mum Tzad

The Gemara then brings the full *tzad hashaveh*: *lo rei zeh kirei zeh v'lo rei zeh kirei zeh* — each case has its own unique characteristic that the other lacks. The *zar* has no exceptions but his disqualification does not extend to the animal; the *baal mum*'s disqualification extends to the animal but may be temporary. Neither encompasses the other's distinctive feature. The *hatzad hashaveh shebahen* — the common denominator — is simply that both are prohibited from performing the *avodah* in their respective states and, if they do so, they invalidate. An *onen*, who is equally prohibited, therefore equally invalidates.

### Onen Prohibition Source

To deploy this *tzad hashaveh*, however, one must first establish the prohibition independently. Where is the *onen* prohibited? If we say it is from the *min hamikdash lo yetzei* verse, then the invalidation itself is already packaged in that verse (*chillul begufei ketiv bei*), and a *tzad hashaveh* is unnecessary. If we say the prohibition is derived from *hen hikrivu* — the exchange of Moshe and Aharon — then the *pshat* of that passage implies prohibition but not necessarily invalidation as its explicit content.

### Kohen Gadol Source Limits

The Gemara makes the point sharply: from *hen hikrivu* one can at most learn the *issur* — the prohibition on the *kohen hedyot* to perform the *avodah* as an *onen*. The actual rule that he *invalidates* if he does so is derived through the *gezeirah shavah*, not from the verse's plain meaning. This is why both derivations — from the *Kohen Gadol* verse and from *hen hikrivu* — are needed, each contributing a different element.

### Tamei Exception Analogy

The Gemara next confronts a challenge to the *tzad hashaveh* itself: *mah lehatzad hashaveh shebahen sheken lo hutru miklalan* — both *zar* and *baal mum* are never permitted in the *Beit HaMikdash* in their respective states; there are no exceptions. An *onen*, however, does have an exception — the *Kohen Gadol*. Perhaps the rule "prohibited entails invalidation" applies only where there is an absolute prohibition with no exceptions, and since the *onen* prohibition has an exception (the *Kohen Gadol*), it does not generate invalidation for the *kohen hedyot*.

The Gemara answers: *tamei yochiach* — throw *tamei* into the *tzad hashaveh*. *Tumah* has an exception: *tumah hutra betzibur* — ritual impurity is permitted for communal offerings. Yet a *tamei* person who performs the *avodah* when not permitted to (i.e., for a private offering) nonetheless invalidates. Since the exception does not eliminate the invalidation by *tamei*, the same logic applies to the *onen*: the existence of an exception for the *Kohen Gadol* does not mean that when the *onen hedyot* transgresses and performs the *avodah*, he fails to invalidate. *Hanakh yochichu v'chazar hadin* — combining all the cases, no single distinctive feature can be used to bracket the *onen* out of the general principle.

## Rava's Korban Yachid/Tzi…

### Rava's Position

Rava now proposes a significant qualification: the rule that an *onen* invalidates applies only to a *korban yachid* — a private individual offering. With respect to a *korban tzibur* — a communal offering — an *onen hedyot* who performs the *avodah* does not invalidate; rather, it is *meratzeh*, acceptable.

### Rava's Logic Grid

Rava's reasoning proceeds through a *kal vachomer* from *tumah*. The structure requires holding several parameters simultaneously. By *tumah*: a *Kohen Gadol* performing the *avodah* for a *korban yachid* in a state of *tumah* is not permitted — there is no exception for a *Kohen Gadol* with *tumah* by a private offering. Yet a *kohen hedyot* performing the *avodah* for a *korban tzibur* in a state of *tumah* is permitted — *tumah hutra betzibur*. Now by *aninut*: a *Kohen Gadol* performing the *avodah* for a *korban yachid* as an *onen* is permitted — the *Kohen Gadol* may bring even a private offering while an *onen*. The *kal vachomer* then argues: if *tumah*, which is not permitted even by the *Kohen Gadol* for a *korban yachid*, is nonetheless permitted by a *kohen hedyot* for a *korban tzibur*, then *aninut*, which is more lenient in that it is permitted by the *Kohen Gadol* even for a *korban yachid*, should certainly be permitted by a *kohen hedyot* for a *korban tzibur*.

---

## Rava Bar Ahilai Refutes

### Multiple QV Paths

Rava bar Ahilai challenges Rava's *kal vachomer* by demonstrating that the same grid of parameters admits multiple alternative inferences, each of which yields a different — and equally unacceptable — conclusion. The problem is that Rava's argument assumes certain cells of the grid as fixed and derives the unknown from them. But there is no principled reason why those particular cells should be the fixed ones; other assignments of fixed and variable yields contradictory results.

### Alt 1: Onen Limits

One could instead run the inference in the opposite direction regarding *oninut* by the *Kohen Gadol*: since *tumah*, which is permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by *korban tzibur*, is not permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by *korban yachid*, perhaps *aninut*, which is not permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by *korban tzibur* (starting from that assumption), is also not permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by *korban yachid*. In other words, rather than deriving that the *kohen hedyot* is permitted as an *onen* for *korban tzibur*, one could derive that the *Kohen Gadol* is *not* permitted as an *onen* for a *korban yachid* — which directly contradicts the explicit *pshat* of *min hamikdash lo yetzei*.

### Alt 2: Tumah Extends

Alternatively, one could argue in the opposite direction for *tumah* by the *Kohen Gadol*: since *aninut* is permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid* (which is a place where *tumah* is not permitted), and since *tumah* is permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by a *korban tzibur* (where *aninut* is not permitted), perhaps *tumah* should also be permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid*. This contradicts the well-established rule that *tumah* has no exception for private offerings regardless of who performs the *avodah*.

### Alt 3: Tumah Limits

A third alternative runs the *kal vachomer* so as to restrict *tumah* even by *korban tzibur*: since *aninut* is permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid* and yet is not permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by a *korban tzibur*, perhaps *tumah*, which is not permitted for the *Kohen Gadol* by a *korban yachid*, should equally not be permitted for the *kohen hedyot* by a *korban tzibur*. This would overturn the entire principle of *tumah hutra betzibur* as applied to ordinary *kohanim*.

### Pshat Conclusion

Since any of these inferences is logically as valid as Rava's original *kal vachomer*, and since each leads to a conclusion that contradicts established law, the Gemara concludes: *ika l'mifrach hachi v'ika l'mifrach hachi* — you can challenge in this direction and you can challenge in that direction. The grid-based *kal vachomer* reasoning breaks down entirely because there is no way to determine which parameter to hold fixed and which to derive. The Gemara therefore rules: *kocha d'hacha tichu b'duchtei* — let each law stand as it is, in its own place. The rule about *tumah* belongs to the category of *tzibur*; the rule about *onen* belongs to the category of *Kohen Gadol*. These are independently derived principles, not nodes in a logical grid from which further inferences can be pressed. Attempting to construct *kal vachomers* across these categories leads only to confusion, and Rava's proposed leniency for an *onen* by *korban tzibur* is accordingly rejected.
