# Makkot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eidim Zomemim Unit

### Two vs. Three Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים אוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה עֵדִים יוּמַת הַמֵּת״ – אִם מִתְקַיֶּימֶת הָעֵדוּת בִּשְׁנַיִם לָמָּה פָּרַט הַכָּתוּב בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה? אֶלָּא לְהַקִּישׁ שְׁלֹשָׁה לִשְׁנַיִם: מָה שְׁלֹשָׁה מְזִימִּין אֶת הַשְּׁנַיִם, אַף הַשְּׁנַיִם יָזוֹמּוּ אֶת הַשְּׁלֹשָׁה. וּמִנַּיִן אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֵדִים״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It is written: **"At the mouth of two witnesses or three witnesses shall he who is to die be executed"** (Deuteronomy 17:6). The question is: **If the testimony is valid with two** witnesses, **why did the verse specify** that it is valid **with three? Rather,** it is **to juxtapose** and liken **three to two: Just as three** witnesses **can render the two** witnesses **conspiring** witnesses, **so too, the two** witnesses **can render the three** witnesses **conspiring** witnesses. **And from where** is it derived that two witnesses can render **even one hundred** witnesses conspiring witnesses? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states:** "Three **witnesses."** Since the verse is obviously discussing witnesses, the term witnesses is superfluous, as it could have stated: Two or three. The term "witnesses" teaches that two witnesses can render a set of witnesses conspiring witnesses irrespective of their number.

The daf opens at 5b, at the *mishna* that continues the discussion of *eidim zomemim* while also establishing the general parameters defining a *kat eidim* — a unit of witnesses. The *mishna* begins by citing the verse *al pi shnayim eidim o shlosha eidim yumet hamet*: by the mouth of two witnesses or three witnesses shall the condemned be put to death. The *mishna* asks: if two witnesses are already sufficient to establish a matter, why did the Torah need to specify three? Surely that is obvious.

### Equal Weight Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַקִּישׁ שְׁלֹשָׁה לִשְׁנַיִם: מָה שְׁלֹשָׁה מְזִימִּין אֶת הַשְּׁנַיִם, אַף הַשְּׁנַיִם יָזוֹמּוּ אֶת הַשְּׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** to juxtapose and liken **three to two: Just as three** witnesses **can render the two** witnesses **conspiring** witnesses, **so too, the two** witnesses **can render the three** witnesses **conspiring** witnesses.

The answer is that the Torah places two and three on equal halakhic footing: *lehakkish shnayim lishlosha v'shlosha lishnayim*. Just as three witnesses can render two witnesses *zomemim* — by declaring *immanu hayitem b'oto hamakom*, you were with us in that place, you could not have witnessed what you claimed — so too can two witnesses render three witnesses *zomemim*. Two carries the same weight as three. Once the minimum threshold of two is met and a *kat eidim* is formed, the number beyond that is irrelevant.

### Extension to Many

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֵדִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived that two witnesses can render **even one hundred** witnesses conspiring witnesses? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states:** "Three **witnesses."** Since the verse is obviously discussing witnesses, the term witnesses is superfluous, as it could have stated: Two or three. The term "witnesses" teaches that two witnesses can render a set of witnesses conspiring witnesses irrespective of their number.

The *mishna* then extends this principle further: *u'minayin afilu me'ah?* — how do we know this applies even to a hundred witnesses? The answer is *talmud lomar eidim*: the Torah uses the general term *eidim*, witnesses, rather than specifying the number. Any group of *eidim*, regardless of size, has equal halakhic weight.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מָה שְׁנַיִם אֵינָן נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם זוֹמְמִין – אַף שְׁלֹשָׁה אֵינָן נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שְׁלׇשְׁתָּם זוֹמְמִין. וּמִנַּיִן אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֵדִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** that three witnesses are mentioned in the verse in order to teach: **Just as two** witnesses who testified that a person is liable to be executed **are not killed** for this testimony **unless both of them are** found to be **conspiring** witnesses, **so too, three** witnesses who testified together **are not killed unless** all **three of them are** found to be **conspiring** witnesses. **And from where** is it derived that the same *halakha* applies **even** to **one hundred** witnesses? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states:** "Three **witnesses."** The superfluous term "witnesses" teaches that the status of all witnesses who come to court as a single set of witnesses is that of one testimony with regard to this *halakha*.

*Rabbi Shimon* adds a related principle: just as when there are two witnesses, both must be proven *zomemim* before the law of *ka'asher zamam* can be applied — neither one alone is the full unit — so too when there are three, all three must be proven *zomemim* together. One might have thought that if two out of three are proven *zomemim*, those two could be punished on their own, since after all two witnesses are sufficient to convict and these two are the ones whose testimony carried the verdict. Rabbi Shimon rejects this: the entire group is treated as a single unit, and you must invalidate the whole unit before the *halakha* of *eidim zomemim* applies. And once the principle is established for three, it extends to a hundred as well.

### Rabbi Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַשְּׁלִישִׁי לְהָקֵל, אֶלָּא לְהַחֲמִיר עָלָיו וְלַעֲשׂוֹת דִּינוֹ כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּאֵלּוּ. וְאִם כֵּן עָנַשׁ הַכָּתוּב לַנִּטְפָּל לְעוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה כְּעוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה יְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר לַנִּטְפָּל לְעוֹשֵׂי מִצְוָה כְּעוֹשֵׂי מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: The third** witness mentioned in this verse **does not come** for the judges **to be lenient** concerning him; **rather,** its mention comes for the judges **to be stringent concerning him and to render his halakhic status like** that of **these** two witnesses who testified with him. One could claim that since the testimony of the third witness is superfluous, as the testimony of the other two witnesses sufficed, the third witness and any other witnesses beyond the first two should be exempt. Therefore, the verse teaches that since he testified with them and was rendered a conspiring witness with them, he too is executed. One can learn a moral from this *halakha*: **And if the verse punished one who associates with transgressors** with a punishment **like** the one received by the **transgressors,** even though his role in the transgression is ancillary, **all the more so will** God **pay a reward to one who associates with those who perform a mitzva like** the reward of those **who perform** the **mitzva** themselves, even though his role in performing the mitzva is ancillary.

*Rabbi Akiva* makes a further point: *lo ba hashlishi lehakel ela lehakhmir alav vela'asot dino k'yotzei b'eilu* — the Torah's mention of the third witness comes not to be lenient but to be stringent, teaching that the third witness receives the same punishment as the first two. The reasoning is this: the first two carried the full weight of the testimony and would have been sufficient to convict the defendant on their own. When all three are found *zomemim*, one might argue that the third, who contributed nothing beyond what was already in place, should not receive the full punishment. Rabbi Akiva says otherwise — because he joined himself to the group, his *din* is identical to theirs.

*Rashi* clarifies that Rabbi Akiva does not dispute the principle that all three must be proven *zomemim* together. His *chidush* is specifically about the third's punishment, not about when the *halakha* applies. The *mishna* then draws a moral inference: *al achat kamah vechamah yeshaleim sachar lenitpal le'osei mitzvah ke'osei mitzvah* — if one who attaches himself to wrongdoers is punished like them, all the more so will one who attaches himself to those performing a *mitzvah* be rewarded as though he were the primary actor, since *middat hatov* is greater than *middat hadin*.

## Pasul Invalidates

### One Disqualifies All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה שְׁנַיִם נִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵלָה, אַף שְׁלֹשָׁה נִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵלָה. מִנַּיִן אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֵדִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cites another derivation based on the juxtaposition of two to three: **And just as** with regard to **two** witnesses, if **one of them is found** to be **a relative or** is otherwise **disqualified, their** entire **testimony is voided,** as it is no longer the testimony of two witnesses, **so too,** with regard to **three** witnesses who came to testify as one set, if **one of them is found** to be **a relative or** is otherwise **disqualified, their** entire **testimony is voided,** even though two valid witnesses remain. **From where** is it derived that the same *halakha* applies **even** in the case of **one hundred** witnesses? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "Witnesses."**

The *mishna* now establishes another principle that flows from the equation of two and three. Just as when two witnesses testify and one is found to be a *karov* — a relative of a litigant — or *pasul* — disqualified by virtue of a transgression — the entire testimony collapses, because without him only one valid witness remains and one witness is nothing, so too if one of three witnesses is *karov* or *pasul*, *eidusan beteilah*: the entire testimony of the group is void. One bad apple spoils the entire unit. This is a significant *chidush*: one might have thought that the disqualified witness simply does not combine, leaving two valid witnesses intact. The *halakha* rejects this — he is part of the unit and his *pesul* disqualifies everyone. And again, this extends to a hundred.

### Capital vs. Civil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, אֲבָל בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – תִּתְקַיֵּים הָעֵדוּת בַּשְּׁאָר. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְאֶחָד דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: In what** case **is this statement,** that if one of the three witnesses is disqualified the entire testimony is voided, **said?** It is said **with regard to** cases of **capital law,** which are adjudicated stringently. **But with regard to** cases of **monetary law,** which are adjudicated more leniently, even if one of the witnesses is disqualified, **the testimony will be validated with** the testimony of **the rest** of the witnesses, and if it is sufficient the case can be adjudicated on that basis. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi disagrees and **says:** If one of the three witnesses is disqualified the entire testimony is voided in **both** cases of **monetary law and** cases of **capital law.**

*Rabbi Yosi* then limits this rule: *bameh devarim amurim bedinei nefashos* — this principle, that one invalid witness voids the entire group, applies only to capital cases. But in *dinei mamonos*, civil and monetary cases, *tiskayeim ha'eidus bash'ar*: the invalid witnesses are simply set aside and the valid ones retain their standing.

### Tosafot Reasoning

*Tosafot* offers a sharp explanation for this distinction. The reason two witnesses are a unit at all is that one witness alone counts for nothing in *dinei nefashos* — *lo yakum eid echad be'ish*. Therefore, if one of two is disqualified, you are left with nothing. But in *dinei mamonos*, a single witness does carry halakhic weight: he can obligate an oath, a *shvuah*. Accordingly, there is no foundational reason in *dinei mamonos* to treat one remaining valid witness as equivalent to nothing. The *chidush* of one invalidating all simply does not apply there, and the remaining valid witnesses stand.

### Rabbi's Position

*Rabbi* disagrees. He maintains that the principle applies equally to both *dinei mamonos* and *dinei nefashos*: *echad dinei mamonos ve'echad dinei nefashos*. However, *Rabbi* introduces a different qualification.

### Hatra'ah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁהִתְרוּ בָּהֶן. אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁלֹּא הִתְרוּ בָּהֶן, מַה יַּעֲשׂוּ שְׁנֵי אַחִין שֶׁרָאוּ בְּאֶחָד שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ?!
>
> **Translation:** **And when** does one disqualified witness void the entire testimony? Only **when** the witnesses **forewarned them** before they performed the transgression, thereby demonstrating their desire to fill the role of witnesses in that case. **But when they did not forewarn them, what shall two brothers do** in a case **where** they, together with others, **saw someone who killed a person?** Will the murderer escape punishment because two relatives happened to be there at the time of the murder and their presence voids the entire testimony? No, the testimony is voided by the presence of relatives or disqualified witnesses only when their intent was to testify. If that was not their intent, they do not void the testimony.

*Ve'eimasai* — when does the disqualified individual actually become part of the group and thereby invalidate it? Only *bizman she'hisru bahen*: when the *pasul* witness actively gave *hatra'ah*, warning the perpetrator of the consequence of his act. By giving *hatra'ah*, he makes himself a participant in the *eidut* and thereby becomes part of the unit in a way that can disqualify it. But if he merely happened to be present — if, as *Rabbi* puts it, two brothers happened to witness the same murder — we cannot simply declare that their presence voids the testimony of the entire assembled group of witnesses. *Mah ya'asu shnei achim shera'u b'echad sheharag et hanefesh?* What would two brothers do if they happened to witness someone commit murder? The system cannot function if the accidental presence of any *pasul* observer brings down every *eidut*.

## Toch Kedei Dibbur

### Rava Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: וְהוּא שֶׁהֵעִידוּ כּוּלָּם בְּתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Apropos the statement in the mishna that the halakhic status of one hundred witnesses equals that of two witnesses with regard to the *halakha* of conspiring witnesses among other *halakhot*, **Rava says: And that** is their status only in a case **where all** the witnesses **testified within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase. If their testimony extended over a longer period, the testimony of the witnesses does not constitute one testimony.

*Rava* adds a condition governing when multiple witnesses form a single *kat*: *vehu she'he'idu kulam betoch kedei dibbur* — it is only when all the witnesses testify within *toch kedei dibbur* of one another that they are considered a unified group.

### Time Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: מִכְּדִי תּוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר הֵיכִי דָּמֵי – כְּדֵי שְׁאֵילַת תַּלְמִיד לְרַב. מֵאָה טוּבָא הֲווֹ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כׇּל חַד וְחַד בְּתוֹךְ כְּדֵי דִיבּוּר שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: Now, what are the circumstances of the period of time indicated by the words: **Within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase? It is a period whose duration is **equivalent** to the duration of **the greeting of a student to a rabbi:** *Shalom alekha*, *rabbi*, which means: Greetings to you, my teacher. The duration of the testimony of **one hundred** witnesses **is** always **greater** than the duration of that greeting. How, then, can one hundred witnesses testify within the time required for speaking a short phrase? Ravina **said to him:** Rava means that **each and every** witness must begin testifying **within** the time required **for speaking** a short phrase **after the other** witness completes his testimony. The result is an uninterrupted series of testimonies that combine into a single testimony.

*Rav Acha miDifti* raises a question to *Ravina*: what is the measure of *toch kedei dibbur*? The standard measure is the time it takes a student to greet his teacher — *shalom alecha Rebbe u'mori* — which is roughly a second. If that is the standard, how can a hundred witnesses ever form a single unit? Each one's testimony would take far longer than that, and by the time the hundredth witness testified, they would be far outside the window of the first. The answer is: *kol chad ve'chad betoch kedei dibbur shel chaveiro* — each successive witness must begin testifying within *toch kedei dibbur* of the conclusion of the previous witness's testimony. The chain links continuously, each to the one before, and in this way even a large group can form a unified unit.

### Tosafot Limitation

*Rashi* understands *Rava*'s requirement to apply generally — that for witnesses to constitute a single *kat eidim* for any purpose, they must testify within *toch kedei dibbur* of one another. *Tosafot* disagrees sharply. *Tosafot* notes that it is clear from the *hatra'ah* discussion, and from later *sugyot*, that *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* does not require *toch kedei dibbur*. For that *halakha*, witnesses form a unit at the moment they witness the event, not the moment they testify. Indeed, the *mishna*'s discussion of what happens when two brothers witness a murder presupposes that one becomes part of a group of witnesses by being present at the scene — not by testifying in *toch kedei dibbur*. *Tosafot* therefore limits *Rava*'s rule: the requirement of *toch kedei dibbur* applies specifically to *eidim zomemim*, to the question of whether all witnesses must be proven *zomemim* together before *ka'asher zamam* can be applied.

### Why Different Standards

Why should *eidim zomemim* require *toch kedei dibbur* to form a unit while other *eidut* does not? *Tosafot* explains: ordinary witnesses become a unit at the moment they witness an actual event. The event is what binds them. But *eidim zomemim*, by definition, did not witness an event — they fabricated testimony. There is no external event that created their bond as a group. Therefore, for *eidim zomemim*, the constitutive act that makes them a unit is the act of testifying in *beit din* itself, and that testimony must be linked by *toch kedei dibbur* for them to be treated as a single *kat*.

## Perpetrators/Victims

### Murderer Disqualifies?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים כּוּ׳ מַה יַּעֲשׂוּ שְׁנֵי אַחִים וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, הָרוּג יַצִּיל! כְּשֶׁהֲרָגוֹ מֵאֲחוֹרָיו. נִרְבָּע יַצִּיל! כְּשֶׁרְבָעוֹ מֵאֲחוֹרָיו. הוֹרֵג וְרוֹבֵעַ יַצִּילוּ! אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** The *Gemara* poses a question: **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: But if that is so,** that a relative or other disqualified witness who witnesses an incident voids the entire testimony, the **murdered** victim **should spare** the accused from execution because the victim himself witnessed his own murder, and he is considered a disqualified witness because of his personal stake in the verdict. Therefore, the entire testimony should be voided. Abaye said to Rav Pappa: A murderer is executed only **when he killed** the victim **from behind him** and the victim did not witness his murder. Rav Pappa said to Abaye: It is not uncommon for a murder to take place without the knowledge of the victim. But in a case where one is sentenced to death for sodomizing a male, why is he executed? **The one who was sodomized should spare** the accused from execution. Here too, since the victim witnessed the sodomy, and he is disqualified from bearing witness due to his personal stake in the verdict, the entire testimony should be voided. The Gemara answers: The sodomizer, too, is executed only **when he sodomized** the victim **from behind him,** and the victim did not witness the act of sodomy. Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Although it is possible to construct a scenario where the victim did not witness the act, it is difficult to construct a scenario where the perpetrator did not witness the act. Therefore, the **murderer and the one who sodomizes should spare** themselves from execution and void the testimony because they witnessed the act, and they are disqualified from bearing witness on the grounds that a person is not capable of testifying about himself. Abaye **was silent** and was unable to respond.

The *Gemara* now returns to the principle of *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* and the question *Rabbi* raised: what about two brothers who happen to be present? *Rav Pappa* poses the problem to *Abaye* in its sharpest form. According to *Rabbi Yosi*, who does not require *hatra'ah* for a *pasul* witness to combine with the group and disqualify it, is it not the case that in virtually every capital case the testimony should be thrown out? *Ela me'ata harog yatzil* — let the murder victim save the murderer. The victim was present, is a *karov* to himself, and perhaps a *soneh* — an enemy — of the murderer. According to *Rabbi Yosi*, should not his presence disqualify the entire *eidut*?

The *Gemara* answers: the victim was killed *keshehorgo meiachorav* — from behind, and therefore did not see the act. What about the perpetrator of forbidden *bi'ah*? Let the passive partner save the active one. Again: *kesheravo meiachorav* — it occurred from behind, and he did not see. But *Rav Pappa* presses further: let the perpetrator himself — the murderer, the one committing the act — save himself. He saw the event. He is a *karov* to himself. *Ishtik* — *Abaye* had no answer.

### Rava Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״יָקוּם דָּבָר״ – בִּמְקַיְּמֵי דָּבָר הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Rav Pappa **came before Rava** with this question, Rava **said to him:** It is written: "According to two witnesses or three witnesses **shall a matter be established"** (Deuteronomy 19:15); it is **with regard to** those who **establish a matter** as legal fact in court that **the verse is speaking.** It is only witnesses who are relatives or otherwise disqualified who void the entire testimony, not subjects of the matter. Therefore, there is no basis for the questions of Rav Pappa.

When *Rav Pappa* came before *Rava*, *Rava* resolved the difficulty: *yakum davar bemekayemei davar hakatuv medaber* — when the Torah says the matter shall be established, it speaks of those who *establish* the matter, those who stand outside the action and give it its legal standing.

### Who Counts as Witness

*Rashi* explains most clearly: perpetrators and victims occupy categorically different roles from witnesses. An *eid* is someone external to the act itself — someone who observes and establishes what occurred. The perpetrator and the victim are not *bemekayemei davar*; they are the actors. They never enter the category of *eid* at all, and therefore their presence as *karov* or *pasul* is simply irrelevant to the *eidut*. This resolves the problem entirely: *Rabbi Yosi*'s principle about *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* operates only within the class of witnesses, not across the boundary into the class of actors.

## Witness vs. Spectator

### Rava's Inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ? אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ: לְמִיחְזֵי אֲתִיתוּ אוֹ לְאַסְהוֹדֵי אֲתִיתוּ? אִי אָמְרִי לְאַסְהוֹדֵי אֲתוֹ, נִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵלָה, אִי אָמְרִי לְמִיחְזֵי אֲתוֹ, מַה יַּעֲשׂוּ שְׁנֵי אַחִין שֶׁרָאוּ בְּאֶחָד שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **How do we,** the members of the court, formulate what we **say to** the witnesses in order to ascertain whether their intent was to testify? **Rava says: This** is what **we say to** the witnesses who come to the court: **Did you come to observe** the proceedings **or did you come to testify? If** the witnesses **say they came to testify,** then if **one of them is found** to be **a relative or** otherwise **disqualified, their** entire **testimony is voided. If** the witnesses **say** that **they came to observe,** in that situation, **what shall two brothers do** in a case **where they saw someone who killed a person?** It is certainly unusual for those who witnessed the murder to not even attend the court hearing.

The *Gemara* now returns to *Rabbi Yosi*'s unanswered problem: what happens when two brothers simply happen to be present among a crowd of witnesses to a murder? *Rabbi Yosi* never required *hatra'ah*, so he has no mechanism to distinguish an active participant in the *eidut* from a passive bystander. *Rava* provides the answer: *heichi amrinan lehu* — this is what we say to the witnesses: *lemechzei asitu o la'asudei asitu?* Did you come to watch, or did you come to testify?

### If Testifying Intent

If they respond that they came to testify — that they positioned themselves with awareness that they might be called upon to bear witness — then they are treated as *eidim*. They are *mitztaref*, they join the group, and if one is *karov* or *pasul*, *eidusan beteilah*.

### If Spectating Intent

If they say they merely came to observe — they were bystanders, not intending to testify — then they are not classified as *eidim* and do not combine with the group. Consequently, their *pesul* does not disqualify the others.

### Two Brothers Problem

This distinction resolves the problem of two brothers who happened to witness a murder. *Mah ya'asu shnei achim shera'u b'echad sheharag et hanefesh?* They were spectators, not witnesses. Their mere presence cannot bring down a valid *eidut*, and the system is not paralyzed every time relatives happen to be in the vicinity of a crime.

### Tosafot Civil Cases

*Tosafot* notes that this discussion of intent — did you come to testify or to spectate — is addressing *Rabbi*'s position specifically in the context of *dinei mamonos*, where there is no *hatra'ah* to serve as the external marker of a witness's active participation. In *dinei nefashos*, according to *Rabbi*, the act of giving *hatra'ah* itself establishes whether someone has assumed the role of witness. But in *dinei mamonos*, there is no *hatra'ah*, and so the court must inquire directly into the person's intent.

## Practical Rulings

### Rav Yehuda Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וְרַב נַחְמָן אוֹמֵר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dispute in the mishna, **it was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and Rav Naḥman says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

*Rav Yehuda* rules in the name of *Shmuel*: the *halakha* follows *Rabbi Yosi* — the principle that one *pasul* witness voids the entire *eidut* applies only in *dinei nefashos*, not in *dinei mamonos*, and no *hatra'ah* is required as a precondition for the *pasul* witness to be counted as part of the group.

### Rav Nachman Ruling

*Rav Nachman* rules: the *halakha* follows *Rabbi* — the principle applies in both *dinei nefashos* and *dinei mamonos*, and in *dinei nefashos* at least, *hatra'ah* by the *pasul* witness is required for him to be considered part of the group.

### Wedding Problem

This immediately raises a practical difficulty that all the *rishonim* grapple with: why is every wedding not invalidated? At every wedding there are relatives present among those who witness the *kiddushin*, and if the *halakha* follows *Rabbi* that this principle applies even in *dinei mamonos* — and *kiddushin* is certainly not less serious than a monetary matter — then *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* should void every marriage contracted in the presence of relatives.

### Wedding Solutions

Several answers are offered. One is that the relatives are simply spectators, not witnesses, and the *Gemara*'s principle that intent determines whether one is classified as an *eid* applies here: people attending a wedding are presumed to come to celebrate, not to testify. The formal designation of specific witnesses — *eidim meyuchadim* — to the exclusion of all others present serves to confirm this status explicitly, marking everyone else as spectators. *Tosafot* raises an additional consideration: a relative may not even know the *halakha* of *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul*, and therefore cannot be said to have consciously presented himself as a witness in a way that activates the rule. *Tosafot* also suggests that perhaps the *halakha* of *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* requires actually appearing and testifying before *beit din*, rather than merely being present at the scene — though this sits in some tension with the earlier discussion that suggests the group is formed at the moment of witnessing.

## Two Windows Mishna

### Setup of Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ, מֵחַלּוֹן זֶה, וּשְׁנַיִם רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ מֵחַלּוֹן זֶה, וְאֶחָד מַתְרֶה בּוֹ בָּאֶמְצַע, בִּזְמַן שֶׁמִּקְצָתָן רוֹאִין אֵלּוּ אֶת אֵלּוּ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ עֵדוּת אַחַת, וְאִם לָאו – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שְׁתֵּי עֵדֻיוֹת. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם נִמְצֵאת אַחַת מֵהֶן זוֹמֶמֶת – הוּא וָהֵן נֶהֱרָגִין, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה פְּטוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where there **were two** witnesses **observing** an individual violating a capital transgression **from this window** in a house, **and two observing him from that window** in a house, **and one** person was **forewarning** the transgressor **in the middle** between the two sets of witnesses, the *halakha* depends on the circumstances. In a situation **where some of** the witnesses observing from the two windows **see each other,** the testimony of all **these** witnesses constitutes **one testimony, but if** they do **not** see each other, the testimony of **these** witnesses constitutes **two** independent **testimonies. Therefore,** as two independent sets of witnesses, **if one of** the sets **was found** to be a set of **conspiring** witnesses, while the testimony of the other set remained valid, both **he,** the one accused of violating the capital transgression, **and they,** the conspiring witnesses, **are executed, and the second** set, whose testimony remained valid, **is exempt.**

The next *mishna* on 6b presents a case: two witnesses observe a murder from one window, and two other witnesses observe the same murder from a second window across the street, while one additional person standing in the middle of the street below gives *hatra'ah* to the perpetrator.

### Visibility Requirement

The governing condition is whether the witnesses on opposite sides can see one another. The *mishna* requires that some members of each group see some members of the other group — the seeing must be mutual, going in both directions. It is not necessary that all four witnesses see all four others, but at minimum one witness from each group must see at least one from the other group.

### Unified Testimony

If that condition is met — if one witness from each side sees one witness from the other — then all four are considered a single *kat* of four. To make any of them *eidim zomemim*, all four must be proven *zomemim* together, and if one is found *pasul*, all four are disqualified.

### Separate Testimony

If the condition is not met — if the witnesses on one side cannot see any witness on the other side — then they constitute two separate *katot*, two independent groups. In that case, one group can be made *zomemim* without the other group being affected.

### Paradoxical Outcome

This produces a striking result. If one *kat* of two is proven *zomemim* — *immanu hayitem b'oto hayom* — those two are executed under *ka'asher zamam*. But the other *kat* remains valid. They testified truthfully about a murder that actually occurred. Accordingly, the murderer is also executed. The irony is stark: both the murderer and the *eidim zomemim* are put to death. One might ask — if he actually committed the murder, why are the *eidim zomemim* executed? As the *Gemara* addressed earlier, the answer is that when they testified, the defendant had not yet been found guilty in court and was not yet *chayav mita*. Their testimony sought to impose a death sentence on someone not yet legally subject to it. They are therefore *eidim zomemim* regardless of what the other group's testimony subsequently establishes.

## Rabbi Yosi Witnesses

### Person in Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁנֵי עֵדָיו מַתְרִין בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים״. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים״ – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִין שׁוֹמַעַת מִפִּי הַתּוּרְגְּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** Transgressors **are never executed unless his two witnesses are** the ones **forewarning him, as it is stated: "At the mouth of two witnesses…**he who is to be put to death shall die" (Deuteronomy 17:6), from which it is derived that it is from the mouths of the two witnesses that the accused must be forewarned, and forewarning issued by someone else is insufficient. **Alternatively,** from the phrase **"at the mouth of two witnesses"** one derives **that** the judges must hear the testimony directly from the witnesses, and the **Sanhedrin will not hear** testimony **from the mouth of an interpreter.**

The *mishna* then turns to the role of the person in the middle giving *hatra'ah*, and *Rabbi Yosi* offers a ruling about a broader requirement.

### Both Must Warn

*Rabbi Yosi omer: le'olam ein neheragim ad she'yihyu shnei edav matrin bo* — a defendant can never be executed unless the witnesses themselves — the two who will testify against him — are the ones who gave him the *hatra'ah*. The idea behind *hatra'ah* is not merely that someone warned the perpetrator; it is that the very people who observed the act and will testify about it must be the ones who warned him. The perpetrator must know that those observing him are witnesses, he must hear their warning, understand the consequence, reject it, and commit the act under their gaze. If the warning came from an external person — the man in the middle of the street — while the actual witnesses were looking on from their windows, that does not constitute sufficient *hatra'ah* according to *Rabbi Yosi*.

### Pasuk Source

*Rabbi Yosi* derives this from the verse *al pi shnayim eidim*: it is by the *mouth* of the two witnesses — they must be the ones speaking the warning.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* clarifies an important point about the *mishna*'s structure. In the *reisha* of the *mishna* — the case where the witnesses from the two windows do see one another and form a single *kat* — the man in the middle giving *hatra'ah* does not play any combining role. The combination of the two groups is achieved entirely through their visual contact with one another. The only function of the man in the middle becomes apparent in *Rabbi Yosi*'s ruling at the *seifa*: he represents the problematic scenario in which the *hatra'ah* was not given by the witnesses themselves but by an outside party. Whether the witnesses can see the *matreh* or hear his voice, and whether that suffices for the *tanna kamma* who does not share *Rabbi Yosi*'s requirement, is a question *Tosafot* explores — but for *Rabbi Yosi*, that external *hatra'ah* is simply insufficient regardless.

## Rava on Warning

### Visibility Combines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם הָיוּ רוֹאִין אֶת הַמַּתְרֶה, אוֹ הַמַּתְרֶה רוֹאֶה אוֹתָן – מִצְטָרְפִין. אָמַר רָבָא: מַתְרֶה שֶׁאָמְרוּ, אֲפִילּוּ מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִפִּי הַשֵּׁד.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos witnesses joining to constitute a set of witnesses, **Rava says:** Even if the witness in either window is unable to see the witness in the other window, **if** the witness in each window **sees the one who is forewarning** the accused, **or** if **the one who is forewarning** the accused **could see** the two disjointed witnesses, **they join** to constitute a set of witnesses. **Rava says:** The one **forewarning** the accused of **whom** the Sages **spoke** need not be a third witness, but **even** if the victim forewarns the murderer **from his own mouth, and even** if the forewarning emerged **from the mouth of a demon,** meaning the source of the forewarning is unknown, the forewarning is legitimate.

*Rava* states: *im hayu ro'in et hamatra o hamatra ro'eh osan mitztarfin* — if the witnesses see the *matreh* or the *matreh* sees them, they combine. At first this seems difficult to reconcile with the *mishna*, where combination of the two groups of witnesses required mutual visibility between members of the groups themselves.

### Tosafot Application

*Tosafot* explains that *Rava* is addressing a different scenario from the *mishna*: not two groups of two, but two individual witnesses — one from each side — who are not yet a valid *kat* on their own. In that case, if each of the two individual witnesses sees the *matreh*, or the *matreh* sees them, the *matreh* functions as a connecting link that draws them into a single unit of two. *Tosafot* further reasons that if this combining role applies to one-and-one, it would logically apply to two-and-two as well — making all four into a single *kat* of four through the *matreh*'s mediation, even though the *mishna* did not explicitly address this mechanism. One might distinguish the cases — two-and-two is each already a valid *kat*, and arguably no further combination is needed — but *Tosafot* treats *Rava*'s principle as applicable in both settings.

### Warning Sources

*Rava* adds a further statement: *matra she'amru afilu mipi atzmo va'afilu mipi hashed* — the *matreh* whose warning suffices under the *tanna kamma*'s view (as opposed to *Rabbi Yosi*'s stricter requirement) need not be a qualified witness or even a recognized authority. The warning is valid even if it comes *mipi atzmo* — from the victim himself — or *mipi hashed* — from a voice with no identified source.

### Rashi Explanation

*Rashi* explains: *mipi atzmo* refers to the victim of the act — if *Shimon* is about to be killed and cries out to *Reuven*, "Do not kill me! If you do, you will be *chayav mita*!" — that constitutes valid *hatra'ah*. *Mipi hashed* means some unidentified voice — whether a minor, a woman, a non-Jew, or simply an unrecognized source — that delivers the warning. This is a significant *chidush*, because ordinarily one might think that *hatra'ah* must come from a source of authority to have legal standing. *Rava* teaches that the function of *hatra'ah* — at least according to the *tanna kamma* — is to establish that the perpetrator acted in willful defiance of a known consequence, and any warning sufficient to convey that consequence can serve that purpose.

## Separate Testimony

### Civil Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: עֵדוּת מְיוּחֶדֶת כְּשֵׁירָה בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יוּמַת עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד״ – בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת הוּא דְּאֵין כְּשֵׁירָה, אֲבָל בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: Disjointed testimony** of two witnesses, each of whom observed an incident independent of the other, **is valid in** cases of **monetary law, as it is written: "He shall not die at the mouth of one witness"** (Deuteronomy 17:6). This indicates that **it is** only **with regard to** cases of **capital law that** disjointed testimony **is not valid, but with regard to** cases of **monetary law** that testimony **is valid.**

*Rav Nachman* rules: *eidus miyuchedes kesheira b'dinei mamonos* — testimony from separate, individual witnesses who did not see one another is valid for monetary cases. The verse *lo yamus al pi ed echad* — he shall not die by one witness — uses the language of *death*, applying the disqualification of *eidus miyuchedes* specifically to *dinei nefashos*. For *dinei mamonos*, one-and-one who each witnessed the same event from separate vantage points without seeing each other do combine into a valid pair of witnesses.

### Challenge to System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב זוּטְרָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת תַּצִּיל! אַלְּמָה תְּנַן ״הוּא וָהֵן נֶהֱרָגִין״? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zutra objects to this: But if that is so,** and disjointed testimony is effective in certain cases, **in** cases of **capital law** disjointed testimony **should spare** the accused from execution. Since one must exploit every avenue possible to prevent executions, in a case where some of the disjointed witnesses were rendered conspiring witnesses, the entire testimony should be voided on their account. **Why,** then, **did we learn** in the mishna that if one set witnessed the capital transgression from one window and one set from the other window, and one set was found to be a set of conspiring witnesses, **he,** the accused, **and they,** the conspiring witnesses, **are executed?** The Gemara comments: Indeed, that is **difficult** according to Rav Naḥman.

*Rav Zutra* challenges this: if *eidus miyuchedes* can combine for *dinei mamonos*, why not use the same combination to benefit the defendant in *dinei nefashos*? For instance, in the *mishna*'s case of two groups of two witnesses who do not see one another — where one group is proven *zomemim* and executed while the other's testimony stands — why not combine all four into a single *kat* and then declare *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul*, voiding the entire *eidut* and sparing the murderer? We have a principle of *v'hitzilu ha'edah* — the court should seek to acquit in capital cases. *Alma tnan hu v'hen neheragim* — but the *mishna* explicitly says both the murderer and the *zomemim* are executed, which means we do not use such combination. *Kashya.*

### System Integrity

The response is that while the principle of seeking to acquit in *dinei nefashos* is real, it does not license distorting the halakhic structure itself. If the foundational rule is that in *dinei nefashos* separate witnesses who did not see one another do not combine, then they simply do not combine — one cannot invoke that non-combination selectively to generate a *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* that would not otherwise exist.

### Distinctions Explained

*Tosafot* addresses why *eidus miyuchedes* combines in *dinei mamonos* but not in *dinei nefashos*. The answer follows the same reasoning established earlier: in *dinei mamonos*, a single witness has halakhic standing — he can obligate a *shvuah*. He is not a legal nullity. Therefore, one witness plus one witness produces two witnesses. In *dinei nefashos*, a single witness is legally nothing — *lo yakum eid echad be'ish*. One plus one remains one, and no valid *kat* is formed.

## Two Rabbi Yosis

### Questioning Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַאי סְבָרָא? וְהָתְנַן רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הַשּׂוֹנֵא – נֶהֱרָג, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמוּעָד וּמוּתְרֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said to Abaye: And is Rabbi Yosei of the opinion that **this** line of **reasoning** is correct, and forewarning by the witnesses is indispensable? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (9b): **Rabbi Yosei says: An enemy** who commits murder cannot claim that he killed the victim unwittingly. Rather, **he is executed** even if there was no forewarning, **due to** the fact **that his** halakhic status is **like** that of one who is **cautioned and forewarned.** Apparently, Rabbi Yosei does not always require that there be forewarning.

*Rav Pappa* raises a difficulty against *Rabbi Yosi*'s requirement that the witnesses themselves give the *hatra'ah*. The *mishna* elsewhere records that *Rabbi Yosi* holds: *hasoneh neherag mipnei she'hu k'muad u'mushra* — a known enemy who kills the person he hates is executed even without *hatra'ah*, because his hatred demonstrates his willfulness. If *Rabbi Yosi* is willing to dispense with *hatra'ah* entirely in that case, how can he insist that the *hatra'ah* must specifically be given by the witnesses?

### Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָהוּא – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חָבֵר אֵין צָרִיךְ הַתְרָאָה, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה הַתְרָאָה אֶלָּא לְהַבְחִין בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג לְמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him: That** statement in the mishna you cited that is attributed to Rabbi Yosei **is** actually the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda says: A *ḥaver* does not require forewarning, as forewarning was instituted only to distinguish between** one who commits a transgression **unwittingly and** one who does so **intentionally.** A *ḥaver*, who is a Torah scholar, does not require forewarning to distinguish between them. Rabbi Yosei ben Ḥalafta, whose opinion is cited in the mishna here, is of the opinion that forewarning is a necessary prerequisite to executing someone who is judged liable, and that forewarning must be issued by the witnesses.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *hahu Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the *Rabbi Yosi* who can waive *hatra'ah* for a *soneh* is not the same *Rabbi Yosi* as in our *mishna*. That position belongs to *Rabbi Yosi ben Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Scholar Exception

*Detanya*: *Rabbi Yosi ben Rabbi Yehuda omer: chaver ein tzarich hatra'ah* — a *talmid chacham* does not require *hatra'ah*, *lefi she'lo nitna hatra'ah ela lehavchin bein shogeg le'meizid* — because the entire purpose of *hatra'ah* is to distinguish between unintentional and intentional violation. A scholar who knows the law cannot claim ignorance of the prohibition, and therefore *hatra'ah* for that purpose is unnecessary. The same logic underlies the case of the *soneh*: his demonstrated hatred establishes that his act was willful. This view treats *hatra'ah* purely as a tool for determining the defendant's state of mind.

### Authority Requirement

The *Rabbi Yosi* of our *mishna* holds a fundamentally different conception of *hatra'ah*. For him, *hatra'ah* is not merely about establishing *meizid* versus *shogeg*. It is an act of formal authority — the witnesses, who represent the legal system, must confront the perpetrator directly, warn him, and he must defy them under their observation. This is why it must be the witnesses themselves who deliver the warning: the act of rejection must be a rejection of the witnesses' authority specifically, not merely a demonstration of willfulness in general. Accordingly, there is no contradiction: *Rabbi Yosi ben Rabbi Yehuda*'s leniency operates within a framework where *hatra'ah* is purely about frame of mind, while *Rabbi Yosi*'s stringency reflects a broader conception of what *hatra'ah* must accomplish.

A subtle distinction also applies regarding the two types of *shogeg* at stake. A *talmid chacham* cannot claim ignorance of the law — he knows that *chilul Shabbos* is prohibited. But he could still claim ignorance of the facts — perhaps he did not realize the walls had fallen and it was *reshut harabim*, or that the substance was *chametz* rather than *matzah*. Similarly, the *soneh* who kills is presumed willful in his act, but one might ask whether he necessarily knew the legal consequence of *mita*. These distinctions point to the complexity underlying both positions.

## Interpreter Requirement

### Pasuk Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים״ – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִין שׁוֹמַעַת מִפִּי הַתּוּרְגְּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** from the phrase **"at the mouth of two witnesses"** one derives **that** the judges must hear the testimony directly from the witnesses, and the **Sanhedrin will not hear** testimony **from the mouth of an interpreter.**

The *mishna* adds a second *drasha* on the phrase *al pi shnayim eidim*: *shelo tehei Sanhedrin shoma'as mipi hameturgeiman* — the *Sanhedrin* may not hear witness testimony through an interpreter. The witnesses must testify directly, *al pi* — by their own mouths — to the court.

### Rava Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים״ – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִין שׁוֹמַעַת מִפִּי הַתּוּרְגְּמָן. הָנְהוּ לָעוֹזֵי דַּאֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אוֹקִי רָבָא תּוּרְגְּמָן בֵּינַיְיהוּ. וְהֵיכִי עֲבִיד הָכִי? וְהָתְנַן: שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא סַנְהֶדְרִין שׁוֹמַעַת מִפִּי הַתּוּרְגְּמָן! רָבָא מִידָּע הֲוָה יָדַע מָה דַּהֲווֹ אָמְרִי, וְאַהְדּוֹרֵי הוּא דְּלָא הֲוָה יָדַע.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the statement in the mishna that the judges must hear the testimony directly from the witnesses, not through an interpreter, the Gemara relates: There were **certain** people who spoke **a foreign** language **who came before Rava** for judgment. **Rava installed an interpreter between them** and heard the testimony through the interpreter. The Gemara asks: **And how did he do so? But didn't we learn** in the mishna **that** the **Sanhedrin will not hear** testimony **from the mouth of an interpreter?** The Gemara answers: **Rava knew what they were saying,** as he understood their language, **but he did not know** how to **respond** to them in their language. He posed questions through the interpreter but understood the answers on his own, as required by the mishna.

The *Gemara* relates that foreign litigants once came before *Rava*, and he appointed a *turgeiman* — an interpreter — to mediate. The *Gemara* asks: how could he do this? Has not the *mishna* prohibited exactly this? The answer: *Rava meida hava yada mah dehavu amri* — *Rava* understood their language and heard them directly.

### Hearing vs. Speaking

*V'ahadurei hu d'lo hava yada* — what *Rava* could not do was respond to them in their language, and so the interpreter was used to convey *Rava*'s responses back to the witnesses, not to relay the witnesses' words to *Rava*. The *mishna*'s prohibition is one-directional: the *beit din* must hear the witnesses' testimony directly. There is no formal requirement that the court communicate back to witnesses in a language they understand without intermediary assistance, though questions of accuracy in the interpreter's transmission of the court's words remain a matter of trust rather than formal *halakha*.

## Guarantor Relatives

### Facts of Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילְעָא וְטוֹבִיָּה, קָרִיבֵיהּ דְּעָרְבָא הֲוֹה. סְבַר רַב פָּפָּא לְמֵימַר: גַּבֵּי לֹוֶה וּמַלְוֶה רַחִיקִי נִינְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: אִי לֵית לֵיהּ לְלֹוֶה, לָאו בָּתַר עָרְבָא אָזֵיל מַלְוֶה?
>
> **Translation:** Apropos disqualified witnesses, the Gemara relates: Two people called **Ile'a and Tuviyya,** who signed as witnesses on a promissory note, **were relatives of the guarantor** of the loan. **Rav Pappa thought to say** that since **vis-à-vis the borrower and lender** these witnesses **are distant** and are not related, their testimony on the document is valid. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rav Pappa: If the borrower does not have** the means to repay the loan, **doesn't** the **lender pursue the guarantor** to claim his debt? Therefore, the guarantor is party to the loan, and his relatives are not eligible to serve as witnesses on the promissory note.

The *Gemara* closes by presenting a case: *Ilai* and *Tuvya* are relatives of an *areiv* — a guarantor on a loan between a borrower and a lender — and they are called to testify about the terms of the loan transaction.

### Initial Analysis

*Rav Pappa* was inclined to accept their testimony. His reasoning: *gabei loveh u'malveh rechikei ninhu* — they are not related to the borrower or to the lender. The case before the court is ostensibly between those two parties, and *Ilai* and *Tuvya* have no familial tie to either. The *halakha* of disqualification by kinship should not apply to them.

### Rav Huna Objection

*Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua* challenged *Rav Pappa*: *ei leit lei l'loveh, lav batar arva azil malveh?* — if the borrower cannot repay, does the creditor not turn to the guarantor to collect? The guarantor is not a peripheral figure; he is potentially the primary target of collection. This litigation is therefore also a litigation that directly affects the guarantor's financial interests. A case involving the guarantor's money is a case the guarantor is a party to, and those related to him are as compromised as if they were related to the borrower or lender directly.

### Ruling Applied

The *halakha* therefore follows: relatives of the *areiv* are disqualified from testifying in the case, just as relatives of the primary litigants are disqualified. The *areiv*'s stake in the outcome is sufficient to render the case *his* case as well, and the rules of kinship-based disqualification apply accordingly.
