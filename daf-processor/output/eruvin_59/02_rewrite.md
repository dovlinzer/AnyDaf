# Eruvin 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overlapping Squares

### NYC Tchumim Problem

Before turning to the daf itself, a practical question arising from the preceding sugya on measuring *tchumim* deserves attention — one with direct relevance to contemporary metropolitan life. Consider the geography of New York City: a sprawling, multi-borough urban area whose irregular shape poses a genuine challenge for the laws of *tchumim*. If one squares off Manhattan, the resulting rectangle may extend into New Jersey or Queens but still fail to encompass Riverdale in the Bronx. And even squaring off the Bronx separately may not bridge the gap to lower Manhattan. How, then, do people routinely travel across these distances on Shabbos without violating the *tchumim*?

### Brooklyn Bridge Tshuva

The most obvious theoretical solution — though one stated nowhere explicitly in the *Gemara* — is that if the squared-off areas of two cities overlap, they should be treated as a single city. This would resolve the geometric problem elegantly: the process of squaring off (*ibbur*) occurs before the 2,000 *amos* are even measured, and if two squares overlap, the cities merge into one for the purpose of *tchumim*. Whether this is actually the *halacha*, however, is not self-evident. A number of *teshuvos* in the responsa literature address this question in the specific context of the Brooklyn Bridge — asking whether a *beis dira* located on the bridge, particularly a toll booth or some small habitable structure situated not immediately adjacent to the city but somewhere in the middle of the bridge, suffices to connect the two shores for purposes of *tchum* measurement. It seems from Rashi that as long as there is a functional residential presence near the bridge, it would count. Several of these *teshuvos* conclude, however, somewhat cryptically, that when you look at a map, "it all works out" — a phrase that presumably points to the overlapping-squares principle as the operative rationale.

### Chazon Ish Overlap

There is in fact an explicit source for this principle. The Chazon Ish, in his commentary on *Eruvin* (at the beginning of siman 16), writes: *im yesh shivim amos v'shiyayrim oseh karpaf l'shnayim l'hiyot k'echad* — if there are 70-and-a-fraction *amos* between two settlements, the *karpaf* measurement joins them as one. He then raises the further question: *yesh l'ayein im yesh ibbur hapgumim hayotzim dimotzi es hamidah k'negdam v'ose oso k'min tavla* — one must examine whether, when the irregular protrusions of a city are squared off, and *v'avir hazeh shemitaber ima pogea b'ir acheres* — the air of that squared-off area impinges upon another city — *mahu sheibbur hazeh yaaseh shteihen k'ir achas* — whether that overlapping squared area causes both cities to be treated as one. Drawing on Rashi, the Chazon Ish argues affirmatively: if squaring off one city causes its squared area to overlap with another city, the two are treated as a single city for purposes of *tchumim*.

### Contemporary Practice

This ruling of the Chazon Ish is not extensively discussed in the classical *poskim* and *teshuvos*, yet it is evidently assumed in contemporary *eruv* and *tchum* practice. No authority appears to dispute it. Its practical significance is considerable: it is presumably the reason why people travel without concern from one New York borough to another on Shabbos, relying on the principle that once the respective squares overlap, all the boroughs constitute a single continuous city. This is a striking case where a ruling of the Chazon Ish quietly shapes widespread practice without having generated significant formal debate.

## Competing Markers

### Greater Measurement

Turning now to the top of 59a: the *Mishna* had established that when there are competing measurements of a *tchum* — one marker extending further than another — we follow the more lenient, i.e., the greater, measurement. The *Gemara* opens by parsing the language of the *Mishna*: *b'makom she'ribu hein b'makom she'miatu lo* — in the place where they gave more, that is where you are limited by the lesser. Then the *seifa* adds: *af l'makom she'ribu* — you may even go to the further marker.

### Rashi v. Tosafos

Rashi and Tosafos differ sharply on what question is being asked and answered here. According to Rashi, the *Gemara*'s question is somewhat trivial: if you are going toward the further marker, you obviously pass the nearer one along the way. The principle of *yesh bichlal masayim maneh* applies — the greater includes the lesser — so the answer is obvious. You may go to the further one, and certainly to the nearer one along the same path.

Tosafos rejects this reading and identifies a more substantive question. Recall that the preceding material dealt at length with the complications of measuring *tchumim* over hills, valleys, and uneven terrain. Tosafos therefore understands the *Gemara* as asking about two markers on the same side of a city — say, the western side — but at different positions: one marker here, and another further along the same side but at a different point. The question is whether one must respect the nearer marker when traveling in one direction, even if the further marker is legitimate for a different position along that side, owing to different topographical conditions. Perhaps the more sloped terrain in one area accounts for a closer marker, while flatter terrain elsewhere justifies a farther one. The *Gemara*'s answer — *af l'makom she'ribu* — means that yes, you may go to the further marker from your current position, but from the other position you must stop at the nearer marker. Both markers are legitimate, each reflecting the topography at its location, and both must be respected. This reading, Tosafos notes, connects directly to the preceding discussion of how terrain affects measurement.

### Two Surveyors

The *Gemara* then continues: *ribu l'echad u'miatu l'echad* — one measurement was greater, the other lesser — and the ruling is to follow the greater one. This sounds identical to the earlier case in the *Mishna*, and the *Gemara* itself asks: *hainu hach* — is this not exactly the same thing? The *Gemara* answers: *hachi ka'amar* — the intent is different. Here we are speaking not of a single surveyor who produced different markers at different points along the same side, but of two different surveyors whose measurements conflict. One surveyor's marker falls further out; the other's falls closer in. Even according to Rashi, the novelty here is that these are competing measurements from different people. According to Tosafos, the case is even more pointed: the two competing markers are for the exact same location — one surveyor placed his marker here, the other placed his there, on precisely the same side and roughly the same spot. In that case you might think each marker's legitimacy is simply negated by the other, yet the ruling is still to follow the greater one.

It is worth noting a difference in the *girsa*: the *Gemara* here reads *ribu echad miat echad* — one person gave more, one gave less — while the *Mishna* reads *ribu l'echad*, which Rashi reads as referring to one of the measurers. The grammatical variation supports Rashi's understanding that the novelty lies in the two-surveyor scenario.

### Diagonal Limit

Abaye adds a qualification: *u-vilvad shelo yarbeh yoter mi-midat ha-ir be-alakhsonah* — provided that the further marker does not exceed the diagonal of the city. The logic of squaring off a city means that instead of 2,000 *amos* in each direction, one can potentially reach up to 2,828 *amos* along the diagonal. If the further surveyor's marker falls within this expanded range, it retains legitimacy. If it extends beyond even this, it is simply too far off to be credited.

This raises an interesting question: if we are now capable of remeasuring and can see that the further marker is significantly off, why follow it at all? Why not simply remeasure and discard the erroneous marker? The *Gemara* seems to envision a scenario in which established signposts carry authority so long as they retain some plausible basis — as long as the error is not so gross as to exceed even the diagonal. Tosafos appears to read this as specifically addressing the case where the two competing markers are themselves located at the diagonal, which makes the reference to the diagonal measurement pointed. A simpler reading of the *Gemara*, however, would be that the markers are not necessarily at the diagonal, and the *Gemara* is simply saying that any marker within 2,800 *amos* has a residual claim to legitimacy even if we now know it may not be perfectly precise.

One might ask why the *Gemara* allows established markers to override a current remeasurement. This seems to be one of those cases where a *halakhic* reality is maintained even when it may not align perfectly with physical reality — and the rationale for this leniency is stated explicitly at the close of the *Mishna*: *lo amru chakhamim et ha-davar le-hachmir ela le-hakel* — the Sages said this matter only to be lenient, not to be strict. Since *eruvin* and *tchumim* are rabbinic, a degree of leniency in their administration is not only permitted but intended.

## Informal Witnesses

### Why Rabbinic

A *Baraita* now seems to contradict the *Mishna*: while the *Mishna* said the Sages spoke *le-hakel*, the *Baraita* says *lo amru chakhamim et ha-davar le-hakel ela le-hachmir* — they said it only to be strict, not lenient. Ravina resolves the contradiction: the Sages were not being lenient with respect to a Torah obligation. Rather, the entire institution of *tchumim* was itself a rabbinic *chumra* imposed where the Torah did not require it. Once established as rabbinic, however, the administration of *tchumim* proceeds with leniency — including the acceptance of testimony from those who do not qualify as formal witnesses.

The *Mishna* at its close trusts even a male or female slave to testify about where the *tchum* marker was. Normally, slaves are excluded from formal *edus*. The *Gemara*'s emphasis that *techumim derabbanan* — the Sabbath boundary laws are rabbinic in origin — functions here not only to explain why we follow the more lenient marker, but also to explain why we trust categories of testimony that would otherwise be inadmissible.

### Ed Echad Principle

This prompts Tosafos to raise a broader question: if the reason we trust slaves (and, by extension, women and children, since Tosafos includes them in this discussion) is specifically because *tchumim* are rabbinic, would we *not* trust them for a matter that is biblical? What about everyday reliance on women for matters of biblical prohibition?

### Women Beyada

Tosafos notes — as the first line of that *Tosafos* makes explicit — that *mashma i havei de-oraita lo mehumni*: it sounds like that if *tchumim* were biblical, these informal witnesses would not be believed. Yet in practice, *ma'aseh be-khol yom she-ma'aminim nashim* — every single day we trust women: *bi-shechitah u-venikkur* — we eat meat that our wives or household members tell us was properly slaughtered and *nikkured* of *chelev*, and we trust them that *challah* was separated. How is this consistent?

The answer lies in the principle of *beyada* — control over outcome. When something carries a *chezkat issur*, as a live animal does before *shechita*, a person is trusted to report that it was rendered permissible only if they were in a theoretical position of control — *beyada* — to have effected the permissible outcome. A woman is believed about *shechita* not because she necessarily performed it herself, but because she could theoretically have done so or arranged for it to be done. Women are indeed eligible to perform *shechita*, as the opening *Mishna* of *Chullin* establishes: *hakol shochtin*. Even if this particular woman does not know how, she could in principle have brought the animal to a *shochet*. The theoretical control is sufficient.

### Tosafos Women

Tosafos then goes further and asks about cases where *beyada* may not apply — where the woman is not in a position of theoretical control. The case of *eruvin* itself is given as an example: one cannot control where a *tchum* boundary falls, so the *beyada* framework does not apply there. This is why the *Gemara* must fall back on the rabbinic nature of *tchumim* as the justification for trusting informal witnesses.

The remainder of Tosafos extends this analysis to *bedikat chametz* and to the question of whether women are trusted for it because of the rabbinic status of the obligation or because of *beyada* — a discussion that becomes increasingly complex and somewhat less straightforward. The broader point that emerges from Tosafos is that there are at least two distinct grounds on which informal witnesses may be trusted: (1) the principle of *ed echad ne'eman be'issurin*, which does not require formal *edus* and under which women are generally believed, especially when *beyada* applies; and (2) the rabbinic status of the underlying law, which independently permits reliance on testimony that would not suffice for a Torah obligation. By *tchumim*, the *Gemara* appears to rely on the second ground, since the first may not cleanly apply.

## Private/Public Cities

### Mishna Rule

The next *Mishna* introduces a new topic: the *halakhic* significance of a city's history — whether it began as private (*shel yachid*) or public (*shel rabbim*) — and how that history affects the permissibility of making an *eruv* for the entire city. The rule is as follows: *ir sheyachid vena'asit shel rabbim* — a city that was private and became public — *me'arvin et kullah* — one may make an *eruv* for the entire city. But *shel rabbim vena'asit shel yachid* — if it was public and became private — *ein me'arvin et kullah* — one may not make an *eruv* for the whole city. Rather, one must leave out a section equivalent to a small city — the *Mishna* uses the example of *Ir Chadashah*, an actual town in Judah (not "new city" as a description, but a proper name) which Rabbi Yehuda says had 50 residents. That is the minimum that must be left off. Rabbi Shimon is more lenient: three courtyards of two houses each is sufficient.

### 600,000 Criterion

Rashi uses this *Mishna* as the anchor for the principle — which originates with the *Ge'onim* rather than being stated explicitly in the *Gemara* — that 600,000 people are required for a street to constitute a *reshus harabbim* from the Torah. For Rashi, "public city" (*shel rabbim*) means one with 600,000 inhabitants, and "private city" (*shel yachid*) means one without. This gives the history-of-the-city criterion a concrete meaning: a city that once had 600,000 residents had the *potential* for a Torah-level *reshus harabbim*, even if no actual street met all the formal criteria; one that never reached that population did not.

Within this framework, Rashi introduces an important nuance: what does 600,000 mean? Does it mean 600,000 people living in the city, or 600,000 people actively using a particular road on a daily basis? Rashi here says it means 600,000 *living in the city*, not necessarily using the road. He also raises another possibility: that a city becomes "public" not through permanent residents but through the influx of 600,000 on market days — a city that becomes a major commercial hub. This matters considerably for contemporary *eruv* discussions, since the definition of the 600,000 criterion significantly affects which streets in modern cities might theoretically qualify as a *reshus harabbim*.

With this framework, Rashi's explanation of the *Mishna* runs as follows. If a city started private — fewer than 600,000 — and now has become public — 600,000 — it nonetheless never actually had a functioning *reshus harabbim* even now, since a *reshus harabbim* requires not only population but a street of 16 *amos* wide running through the full length. Given that it started private, we permit making an *eruv* over the entire city. This seems counterintuitive — the city is *now* public, and surely that should be more restrictive — but Rashi's logic is that since it started private, there is no concern that people will mistake the *eruv* for a permissible enclosure of an actual *reshus harabbim*. Conversely, if the city started as public, even though it is now private, we require leaving over a section. The reason: people remember the city's former grandeur and may still think of it as a large public city, so we create a *heker* — a reminder — lest someone come to make an *eruv* around an actual *reshus harabbim*.

### Ownership Model

The Ramban reads the *Mishna* very differently, and is notably skeptical of the 600,000 criterion altogether. For the Ramban, "public" and "private" mean precisely what they say: ownership. A *shel yachid* is a city literally owned by one person; *shel rabbim* is a city owned by the public. This is not a demographic question but a proprietary one. The Ramban holds that one of the formal requirements for a Torah-level *reshus harabbim* is that the thoroughfare be publicly owned. A privately owned road, however busy, cannot constitute a *reshus harabbim*. Accordingly, a city that starts private — owned by one person — and then becomes public — ownership transfers to many — presents one set of concerns, while the reverse presents another.

### Logical Problem

Both Rashi and the Ramban, however, struggle with the same logical problem: why should we be *more lenient* when the city is now public than when it is now private? Intuitively, we should be governed by current conditions: if the city is now public, now is when a *reshus harabbim* might exist, and now should be more restrictive. The *Mishna* seems to invert this expectation.

Rashi's answer, as noted, appeals to historical perception: people recall the former status of the city and may confuse it with its current one. A city that was once great but is now diminished still carries its former identity in the public consciousness, and we must account for the possibility that someone will treat it as if it still has a *reshus harabbim*. A city that started small and is now large presents less risk — people are aware of the growth and will be appropriately careful.

The Ramban's answer is more technical. When the *Mishna* says the city "became public," this need not mean it became *fully* publicly owned. Perhaps the inhabitants are renting the land while the owner retains title, or perhaps the residential plots were sold while the major thoroughfares remained in private ownership. In that case, the roads — the potential *reshus harabbim* — are still privately owned, and the *reshus harabbim* concern is mitigated. This is admittedly not the simple reading of the *Mishna*, but the Ramban is deliberately drawing the *Gemara*'s subsequent discussion into the analysis to address the logical problem.

The *Gemara* then asks: *heichi dami yachid vena'aseh rabbim* — what is the actual scenario? Rav Yehuda gives the example of *Iskurta*, the city of the *Resh Galuta* — the exilarch — which was presumably once his private domain and has now become public. Rav Nachman challenges this: if the reason the city qualifies as public is that many people come to it, say, to receive authorization from the *Resh Galuta*, then the same logic should apply to any city where Jews gather on Shabbos morning — *deshabta shchichi ahadadi lishmo'a drasha*, as Rashi explains: they gather to hear the weekly *drasha*, at which point they will all remind one another not to carry. If that is the reason we are lenient, then we should be lenient in every city. The Gemara then revises the example to a city of *Natzu'ei*, a private individual — suggesting, as the Ramban reads it, that the point is that the city, while nominally public, retains enough private character (the owner still holds the major roads, or inhabitants are renters) that the leniency is justified. This answer is not entirely satisfying as *pshat* of the *Mishna*, and the Ramban acknowledges it, but at minimum he brings this logical tension explicitly into the *Gemara*'s discussion.

## Eruv in Reshus

### Rabbi Yehuda Lechi

Having established the rules about private and public cities, the *Gemara* now examines a *Baraita* that pushes the analysis further. The *Baraita* says: *ir shel yachid vena'asit shel rabbim* — a city that was private and became public — *reshus harabbim overes besochah* — and a real *reshus harabbim* passes through it — *keitzad mearvin osah* — how does one make an *eruv* for it? *Oseh lechi mikan ulechi mikan o korah mikan vekorah mikan venoise u'nosein be'emtza* — one places a *lechi* on each end of the street, or a *korah* on each end, and one may then carry in the middle.

A word on terminology: the *Gemara* here uses the verb *le'areiv* to refer to the construction of the physical enclosure, not merely the combining of domains through the bread loaf. This represents an evolution in the use of the word *eruv* — from its original, technical meaning of joining through the loaf, to encompassing the structures that permit carrying. It is worth noting this semantic development, as it reflects how the term entered the broader vocabulary of the *halacha*.

Now, *keitzad mearvin osah* — making an *eruv* around a *reshus harabbim* with a mere *lechi* or *korah* — is a striking claim. The *Gemara* immediately asks: who is the *Tanna* that permits this? The answer given by Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua is: *Rabbi Yehuda hi* — it is Rabbi Yehuda. A *Baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Yehuda says: *mi sheyesh lo shenei batim mishenei tziddei reshus harabbim* — one who has two houses on opposite sides of a *reshus harabbim* — *oseh lechi mikan ulechi mikan o korah mikan vekorah mikan venoise u'nosein be'emtza* — places a *lechi* or *korah* on each end, and may carry in the middle. The language is identical to the *Baraita* above, confirming the attribution.

The *Baraita* then records the Sages' rejection: *ein me'arvin reshus harabbim bekach* — one cannot use this method to close off a *reshus harabbim*. A *lechi* or *korah* is insufficient to transform a *reshus harabbim* into a domain where carrying is permitted. We do not rule in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda on this point.

This explains a great deal about the structure of contemporary *eruv* discussions. Those *Rishonim* and *poskim* who require 600,000 for a *reshus harabbim* are effectively saying that almost no modern city ever has a genuine *reshus harabbim*, and therefore the question of enclosing one with a *lechi* or *korah* never arises. This is crucial, because if there were a real *reshus harabbim*, we rule like the Sages — *ein me'arvin reshus harabbim bekach* — and no amount of *lechin* or *koros* would suffice. The entire practical edifice of the contemporary *eruv* depends on the position that the streets being enclosed do not qualify as a *reshus harabbim* at the Torah level to begin with.

Furthermore, the *Baraita* adds: *ve'ein me'arvin osah lachatza'in* — one may not make an *eruv* for only half the city. Either the whole city is enclosed as one *eruv*, or each individual alleyway (*maboi*) makes its own. This raises further questions, addressed immediately below.

### Sages Reject

Before turning to the question of partial *eruvin*, it is worth pausing to absorb the significance of the *Baraita*'s structure. The entire distinction between starting *shel yachid* and starting *shel rabbim* — which the *Mishna* presented as a rule about leaving a portion off — is here shown to have a deeper implication: when it started *shel yachid*, one may (according to Rabbi Yehuda) make a full *eruv* even around a genuine *reshus harabbim*, placing *lechin* or *koros* at the two ends of the through-street. When it started *shel rabbim* and remains *shel rabbim*, Rabbi Yehuda himself is more limited: the city may be enclosed only if the major street is a dead-end — that is, only if the street does not pass fully through the city from one end to the other, so that it is not a true *reshus harabbim*. A street with two open ends that begins and ends *shel rabbim* cannot be enclosed under even Rabbi Yehuda's lenient view.

What emerges is that the history of the city's status confers a kind of *halakhic* identity. A city's origin, whether private or public, shapes how the *halacha* categorizes its current configuration, even when the current conditions have changed. This is admittedly counterintuitive from a purely logical perspective — the *halacha* typically focuses on present conditions — yet both Rashi and the Ramban acknowledge this feature and offer their respective explanations for it.

### Length v. Width

The *Baraita*'s prohibition against making an *eruv* for half the city — *ve'ein me'arvin osah lachatza'in* — is now analyzed. Rav Pappa distinguishes between length and width. *Lo amru ella le'orkah* — this prohibition applies only lengthwise, i.e., splitting the city into an eastern half and a western half along the length of the through-street. *Aval lerachvah me'arvin* — but one may divide it widthwise, i.e., splitting the city into a northern half and a southern half across the width of the street.

The scenario is as follows. The city has a major through-street running, say, east to west. The courtyards line up on either side of the street. If one attempts to make an *eruv* for the eastern half of the city as one unit and the western half as another, the problem is that both halves are competing for the same road. Since the entire city was once *shel yachid*, there is a presumption that it is all unified, and any division that creates competing claims over the same stretch of road is untenable. One person cannot use the eastern portion of the road under one *eruv* while another uses the western portion under a different *eruv*, especially when the road is the single artery serving both — or when there is no way to draw a dividing line across the road without both communities needing to cross it.

But if the division is widthwise — say, the north side of the street makes its own *eruv* and the south side makes its own — the problem is less acute, because in principle the north can exit through the northern end of the road and the south through the southern end. They are not competing over the same stretch of road in the same direction.

### Rabbi Akiva Rule

The *Gemara* now asks: according to whose view is this? *Dlo ke-Rabbi Akiva* — this must not be according to Rabbi Akiva, because Rabbi Akiva states in another context: *regel hamutteres bimkomah oseres afilu shelo bimkomah* — a foot that is permitted in its own place can nonetheless make forbidden even a place where it is not normally located. What is Rabbi Akiva's case? If two courtyards are arranged with courtyard B interior to courtyard A — meaning the only way out of B is through A — and each makes a separate *eruv*, Rabbi Akiva says A cannot carry, even though B is *mutteres* in its own domain, because B's presence in A's space (which B must traverse to exit) renders A's domain forbidden.

By analogy, one might think that in our case of the city divided widthwise, the northern half and the southern half each effectively intrude into one another's territory by both needing the through-street, such that Rabbi Akiva would forbid even the widthwise division.

The *Gemara* responds: *afilu teima Rabbi Akiva* — one may explain this even according to Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva's ruling applies specifically to *shtei chatzerot zu lifnim mizu* — two courtyards one interior to the other — where *dipnimit leit lei pitcha achrina* — the inner courtyard has no other exit. In that case there is no alternative, and B must inevitably traverse A. Here, however, *hani nafki be'hai pitcha vehani nafki be'hai pitcha* — the north can exit one way and the south another. They are not forced into one another's space, and therefore even Rabbi Akiva would permit the widthwise division.

A second version of Rav Pappa's position is then cited, more stringent than the first: not only may one not divide the city lengthwise, one may not divide it widthwise either. Even the widthwise division is forbidden. The *Gemara* asks: is this like Rabbi Akiva? The answer again is no — even the Sages who disagree with Rabbi Akiva can explain the stringency. The Sages' leniency in the courtyard case rests on the fact that there is a door between the courtyards that can be conceptually closed — *achdi ledasha umishtamsha* — the inner courtyard could shut its door, and it is therefore viewed as if they are fully separate. Here, however, where the dividing line runs across the middle of a public street, *mi matzit mesalekat reshus harabbim mehacha* — one cannot simply remove the *reshus harabbim* from the middle. There is no door to shut, no partition to interpose. The two halves of the city remain interconnected by the open street, and any attempt to treat them as separate *eruv* domains is undermined by their unavoidable shared access to that road. According to this version of Rav Pappa's ruling, the only options are to enclose the whole city under one *eruv*, or to designate each alleyway individually with additional measures.

## Alleyways v. City

### Daka Barrier

The *Baraita* had permitted, as an alternative to a citywide *eruv*, making each *maboi* its own *eruv* — *mavoi mavoi bifnei atzmo*. But the *Gemara* asks: if the problem with dividing the city in half is that the two halves compete for the through-street, how do individual alleyways avoid the same problem? After all, each *maboi* opens onto the common through-street, and they would seem to be competing with one another for that shared space just as much as any two halves of the city would.

The answer: *hacha bamai askinan kegon de'avad daka* — the case here is where each *maboi* constructs a *daka*, a small physical barrier at its opening. This is supported by a ruling cited in the name of Rav Chisda: *echad mibnei maboi she'asa daka lepitcho eino oser bnei maboi* — one of the residents of an alleyway who constructs a small barrier (*daka*) at his opening does not thereby create a problem for the rest of the alleyway's residents. The *daka* here is not a full *lechi* or *korah* — it is an actual, even if small, physical barrier — and it signals that this household has effectively withdrawn from the shared domain of the alleyway.

### Courtyard Analogy

This ruling illuminates the mechanics by analogy. In a standard *maboi* configuration, all the *chatzeros* open into the alleyway, and all must join in the *eruv*. If one *chatzer* refuses to participate, it renders the entire alleyway's *eruv* problematic, because its residents remain in a separate domain that impinges on the shared alleyway space. However, if that *chatzer* constructs a *daka* — a real, if modest, physical barrier — it effectively signals that it is not participating in the alleyway's shared space at all. It has removed itself, and the remaining residents of the alleyway may carry without interference.

The same principle applies here. Since the city started *shel yachid*, there is a presumption of unity that causes all domains within it to be treated as if they pull together — meaning each *maboi* is viewed as sharing in the common through-street, and therefore competing with every other *maboi* for it. Under ordinary conditions, of course, individual *maboyim* make their own *eruvin* without difficulty. The problem here is specifically the *shel yachid* origin, which imposes an artificial unity. To overcome this, each *maboi* must make a *daka* at its opening — a real physical barrier — to signal its separation from the common street and from the other *maboyim*. Only then can it make its own independent *eruv* without the concerns that otherwise arise from the city's private origins.

## Garbage Dump Case

### Rabbi Zeira Case

The *Gemara* now presents a case study. Rabbi Zeira made an *eruv* for the city of Rabbi Chiyya — *areiv lemasa devei Rebbi Chiyya* — and did not leave over any portion of the city. Abaye questioned him: why did you not leave a section off, as required for a city that started *shel rabbim*? Rabbi Zeira replied that the elders of the city told him that Rav Chiyya bar Ashi used to make an *eruv* for the entire city without leaving anything over. Rabbi Zeira reasoned from this precedent: the only way one can make an *eruv* for the whole city without leaving a section is if the city started as *shel yachid* — private. Therefore, this city must be *shel yachid*, and I followed suit.

### Ashpa Dump

Abaye corrected him. The elders had not told Rabbi Zeira the full story. What they had told *Abaye* was different: *hai ashpa hava lei michad gisa* — on one side of the city there used to be a garbage dump (*ashpa*). A garbage dump is not a proper opening; it effectively closes off one end of the through-street, rendering it a dead-end rather than a full *reshus harabbim*. With only one functional opening, the street was not a genuine *reshus harabbim*, and therefore Rav Chiyya bar Ashi could make an *eruv* for the whole city without difficulty, regardless of whether it started *shel yachid* or *shel rabbim*.

But *vahashta de'afneiyeh le'ashpa* — now the garbage dump has been cleared away. *Havei lei kishnei pesachim va'asir* — the street now has two open ends, constituting a genuine *reshus harabbim*, and making an *eruv* for the whole city is therefore problematic. Rabbi Zeira responded: *lav ada'atai* — I had not considered that; you make a good point.

### Opening Definition

The case thus illustrates that the requirement to leave a section off the *eruv*, and indeed the entire question of whether one may make an *eruv* for the whole city, is not primarily driven by whether the city historically was *shel yachid* or *shel rabbim*. It is driven by whether there is an actual, genuine *reshus harabbim* within the city — a street open at both ends, 16 *amos* wide, through which the public passes. When a garbage dump closed off one end, the street lacked the requisite second opening and was not a *reshus harabbim* at all. Only when both ends are open does the problem arise in earnest. The history of the city's public or private character compounds or mitigates this concern, but the existence of a genuine *reshus harabbim* is the foundational question.

## Wall & Ladder

### Rav Nachman Dual

The case of Rabbi Zeira raises the broader question: what constitutes a genuine opening for purposes of *reshus harabbim*? This brings us to a question posed by Rav Ami bar Adda to Rabbah: *sula mikan ufesach mikan mahu* — what is the law if there is a wall on one side and a regular opening (*pesach*) on the other? If the wall effectively closes one end, the street might not be a true *reshus harabbim*. But what if the wall has a ladder (*sulam*) leaning against it — does the ladder count as an opening, making it as if both ends are open?

One view reported in Rav's name is that *sulam torat petach alav* — a ladder has the legal status of an opening. If so, the wall-with-ladder functions like a full opening, the street has openings at both ends, and it is a genuine *reshus harabbim* — with all the attendant stringencies.

Rav Nachman rejected this. He cited a more complete tradition in Rav's name: *sulam torat petach alav vetorat mechitzah alav* — a ladder functions both as an opening and as a partition. It is simultaneously a *petach* and a *mechitzah*. How can it be both? The two functions apply in different contexts.

*Torat mechitzah alav* — it counts as a partition: for purposes of determining whether the street has genuine openings at both ends, the wall with a ladder counts as a wall, not an opening, because the ladder does not remove the wall's status as a physical barrier. Therefore, a street that ends in a wall — even one with a ladder — does not have two open ends, and is not a genuine *reshus harabbim*. This makes it *easier* to make a full-city *eruv*, since the street is treated as a dead-end.

### Two Courts Ladder

*Torat petach alav* — it counts as an opening: this function applies in the context of two courtyards separated by a wall, where the question is whether the two courtyards may optionally join in a single *eruv* or must remain separate. *Ratzu echad me'arev ratzu shenayim me'arvin* — if the residents prefer to treat the two courtyards as one, they may make one *eruv*; if they prefer to treat them as separate, they make two *eruvin*. The ladder allows passage between the two courtyards, and because of this possibility of access, it is treated as an opening — which means the courtyards are legally connected enough to join in one *eruv* if they choose, yet also separate enough to maintain independent *eruvin* if they prefer. The ladder grants the residents this choice.

The elegance of Rav Nachman's ruling lies in its recognition that the *sulam* with a *lechi* serves a dual function simultaneously, and the applicable function depends entirely on the legal question being asked. When the question is: does this constitute a thoroughfare that creates a *reshus harabbim*? — the wall (even with a ladder) counts as a *mechitzah*, closing off the street. When the question is: may these two courtyards join in one *eruv*? — the ladder counts as a *petach*, connecting them. The ladder thus yields the most lenient possible ruling in both directions: it is a *mechitzah* when being a wall helps, and a *petach* when being an opening helps. The *Gemara* will continue to develop this principle further on the next *daf*.
