# Yoma 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Entry

### Holy of Holies Entry

Daf 53 opens at precisely the moment we have been building toward throughout the tractate: the *Kohen Gadol* stands inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. He has passed through the curtains, he stands near the rock — or, in the *Bayit Rishon*, beside the *Kodesh Kodashim* itself. He sets down the fire pan, and through a process debated earlier — whether he needed to perform *chofin v'chozer v'chofin*, scooping and re-scooping — he managed to transfer the incense from the ladle back into his hands and place it onto the fire pan. The chamber fills with smoke, and he prepares to leave.

### Mishnah Drama

Before entering the *sugyot* proper, it is worth pausing on something that might seem self-evident but is genuinely worth naming: the *Mishnayot* of this tractate have employed a persistent rhetorical technique of pulling out and pulling in. Each time the reader expects to finally arrive at the *avodah*, the Mishnah steps back — "and the rest of the year they would do it this way" — before drawing the narrative back in toward the center. The effect is dramatic. Had the Mishnah simply recited the procedure in sequence, the moment would not feel as momentous. By constantly supplying the larger context and then narrowing back toward the singular, we are made to feel the full weight of what the *Kohen Gadol* is doing: an act so utterly unique that it can only be appreciated against the backdrop of everything it is not.

## Tzedokim Dispute

### Incense Verse

A *Tanu Rabbanan* at the top of 53a introduces a *beraita* anchored in the verse *v'natan et haketoret al ha'esh lifnei Hashem* — "and you shall place the *ketoret* on the fire before God." The *beraita* understands *lifnei Hashem* as a directive: when you are in the presence of God, when you are inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, do not place the *ketoret* on the fire pan outside and then carry it in already burning. This is the *lehotzi mi'liban shel Tzedukim* — the verse is meant to uproot the position of the *Tzedukim*.

It is worth recalling the context: during the seven days of preparation before *Yom Kippur*, the *Kohen Gadol* was instructed in the proper procedure and made to take an oath that he would not follow the *Tzedukim* practice. Much of the anxiety surrounding the *Kohen Gadol*'s potential death inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* was historically connected to concern that he might conduct the *avodah* according to *Tzedukim* custom.

### Tzedokim Position

The *Gemara* now asks: *mai darosh* — what did the *Tzedukim* actually derive? Their position is rooted in the verse *ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet*, which appears in the opening of *Acharei Mos*: *v'al yavo b'chol et el hakodesh mibeit laparochet el pnei hakaporet asher al ha'aron v'lo yamut ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet*. The *Tzedukim* read this as follows: since God appears upon the *kaporet* in a cloud, if you wish to enter, you must enter enveloped in a cloud. *Ki b'anan* is not a description of God's presence but a condition on the *Kohen Gadol*'s entry — he must come in already surrounded by the cloud of burning *ketoret*. Hence their ruling: *yitaken mibachutz v'yachnis*, prepare it outside with the incense already burning, and then carry it in.

### Chazal Refutation

The *Chachamim* respond: has it not already been stated explicitly — *v'natan et haketoret al ha'esh lifnei Hashem*? The *ketoret* is placed on the fire in the presence of God, which clearly means inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. If so, what do we do with *ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet*?

### Maaleh Ashan

Rather than reading *anan* as a reference to the cloud of God's normal theophanic appearance, *Chazal* concede — and this is notable — that the *anan* here indeed refers to the cloud produced by the *ketoret*. But *melamed she-noten bah ma'aleh ashan*: the verse is teaching that the *ketoret* must contain the herb called *ma'aleh ashan*, that ingredient which causes the smoke to rise in a straight column rather than dispersing. The *ki b'anan eraeh* means: when you come to encounter Me, ensure that the *ketoret* creates a proper *anan* — but it must be created inside, not brought in from outside.

One might ask: would the *ketoret* not produce smoke regardless, even without *ma'aleh ashan*? A reasonable reading of the *Gemara* is that *ma'aleh ashan* is a specific plant — indeed a proper name, not a generic descriptor — and that without it there would still be smoke, just not the ideal upward-streaming column. Alternatively, the verse may be understood more broadly as requiring any substance that ensures a robust cloud: *v'chisa mikkol makom*, the cloud must cover the *kaporet* by whatever means works. Both readings remain in play, and the *Gemara* will revisit this question.

## Ki BeAnan Erah

### First Source

Having established that *ki b'anan eraeh* teaches the requirement for *ma'aleh ashan*, the *beraita* then adds: *u'minayin she-noten bah ma'aleh ashan?* — and from where do we know this requirement? *Talmud lomar v'chisa anan haketoret et hakaporet*: the second verse makes it explicit that the cloud of the *ketoret* must cover the *kaporet*. The *Gemara* immediately questions this structure.

### Second Source

*Kra bi kra?* — do you need one verse to prove another? You already cited *ki b'anan eraeh* as the source for *ma'aleh ashan*, and now you cite a second verse to confirm it?

*Amar Rav Yosef hachi ka'amar*: the two verses are not redundant. *Ein li ela aleh ma'aleh ashan* — I might have thought only the leaf of the *ma'aleh ashan* plant suffices. *Ikar ma'aleh ashan minayin* — from where do I know the root of the plant may also be used? *Talmud lomar v'chisa*: the additional verse extends the obligation to include even the root, which is perhaps less potent than the leaf.

*Abaye* challenges this: but the *beraita* itself teaches the opposite — the root is *better* than the leaf! As it says: *natan b'ikar ma'aleh ashan haya mitamer v'oleh kemakel* — when one placed the root in the *ketoret*, the smoke would rise straight as a pole *ad shemagia lishmei kora*, until it reached the ceiling beams, and then *memashmesh v'yored bakotalim* — it would feel its way downward along the walls, *ad shenitmaleh habayit ashan*, until the chamber was filled with smoke, as it says in *Yeshayahu*, *v'habayit yimalei ashan*. So the root produces the superior result.

*Ela amar Abaye hachi ka'amar*: reverse it. The first verse establishes that the root suffices — that is already known to be the ideal. The second verse, *v'chisa*, comes to extend the requirement to the leaf as well, even though it is the lesser option, teaching that even with the leaf, provided the cloud ultimately covers the *kaporet*, the *avodah* is valid.

## Death Penalty

### Empty Entry

The *beraita* notes that if one failed to include *ma'aleh ashan*, or omitted even one of the ingredients of the *ketoret* — *shechisar achat mikol samaneha* — the offender is *chayav mita*. Many will recognize this formulation from the *Pitum HaKetoret* recited on *Shabbos*. But the *Gemara* presses: *v'teipuk lei de-kamayeh biah reikanit* — why is the death penalty derived from the improper burning? Let the offender be liable for *biah reikanit*, purposeless entry, since without a valid *ketoret* he has no legitimate reason to be inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* at all.

The background is the verse *v'al yavo b'chol et el hakodesh... v'lo yamut*, from which *Chazal* understand two separate liabilities: the first, *al yavo b'chol et v'lo yamut*, prohibits any unauthorized, purposeless entry — this is *biah reikanit*; the second, *v'lo yamut ki b'anan eraeh*, teaches that if the *ketoret* is not done properly, the offender is liable as well. The *Gemara*'s question is: these two liabilities will typically coincide — anyone who burns the *ketoret* improperly also lacks a valid *avodah* purpose and thus has committed a *biah reikanit*. Why do we need a separate liability for the burning?

*Amar Rav Sheshet*: the two liabilities exist independently to cover cases of partial knowledge. If someone was *shogeg* — unaware — of the prohibition of *biah reikanit* but fully intentional (*mezid*) regarding the improper burning, he cannot be held liable under the first rubric but can be held liable under the second. The point is not to find a scenario where one occurs without the other in practice, but to ensure liability attaches to whichever violation he was *mezid* about.

### Two Incense

*Rav Ashi* pushes further: *afilu teima hizid bazu uvazu* — even if he was *mezid* about both, it is still possible to burn the *ketoret* improperly without any *biah reikanit*. How? *Kegon de'ayeil shtei haktarot* — a case where he brought in two *ketorot*, *achat shleima v'achat chasera*: one complete and one lacking. *Abi'ah lo mechayev* — he is not liable for *biah reikanit*, because his entry was justified by the valid *ketoret* he carried in. His presence inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* was legitimate. But once he has burned the valid one, he then proceeds to burn the deficient one. *Ahaktara mechayev* — for that burning he is liable, because he burned a lacking *ketoret*, *de-kamaktir ketoret chasera*. His entry was legitimate; his subsequent action was not.

## Ki BeAnan Erah…

### Two Verses

Having established the two-verse framework, the *Gemara* now returns to examine the four *amoraic* explanations of why both *ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet* and *v'chisa anan haketoret et hakaporet* are necessary. *Amar Rav Yosef*: as discussed above, one verse teaches the leaf and the other extends to the root — or, in *Abaye*'s reversal, one establishes the root and the other extends to the leaf.

### Mishkan Mikdash

*Rav Sheshet amar*: the two verses serve an entirely different purpose. *Ein li ela Ohel Moed* — I know only that this *avodah* applies in the *Ohel Moed* of the desert, since the *psukim* throughout this section center on *v'chein ya'aseh l'Ohel Moed hashochein itam b'toch tumatam*. How do I know the same requirement applies in the permanent *Mikdash* — in *Shilo* or in the *Beis Olamim* on *Har HaBayis*? *Talmud lomar v'chisa*: the additional verse extends the cloud-covering requirement to every place where God's presence dwells.

The *Gemara* objects: we already derive the equivalence between *Ohel Moed* and *Mikdash* from the words *hashochein itam b'toch tumatam* — wherever God causes His presence to dwell, the obligation applies. So the extra verse is not needed for that. *Ela hacha kamar*: try again. *Ein ela b'Yom HaKippurim* — I know only that this requirement applies on *Yom Kippur*. *B'sha'ar yemei hashanah minayin* — from where do I know it applies throughout the rest of the year? *Talmud lomar v'chisa*: the additional verse extends the obligation to the daily *ketoret* burned in the *Heichal*.

This is a striking expansion. The natural assumption has always been that the emphasis on *ma'aleh ashan* and the cloud of the *ketoret* is specifically a *Yom Kippur* concern, connected to the *Kohen Gadol*'s entry into the *Kodesh Kodashim*. But *Chazal* now establish that the requirement — including the attendant *chiyuv mita* for failing to fulfill it — applies equally to the *ketoret* burned twice daily on the golden altar in the *Heichal*. Indeed, when the *Gemara* discussed the family that guarded the secret knowledge of producing the *ketoret*, that discussion concerned the *daily* offering. We say every *Shabbos* in *Pittum HaKtoret*: *chisar achat mikol samaneha chayav mita* — and now we understand why this applies even outside the context of *Yom Kippur*.

There is something deeply meaningful here. The encounter with God that the *ketoret* is meant to facilitate — what the verse *ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet* points toward — is not confined to the singular moment of *Yom Kippur*. Even in the daily offering, some shadow of that encounter, some echo of the intense divine presence, is being sought and required. The daily *ketoret* is in some sense modeled after, and reaches toward, the *Yom Kippur* experience. The same conditions, the same standards, and the same consequences attach — not with the same intensity, but with the same underlying orientation.

### Obligation Contingency

*Rav Ashi amar*: *chad lemitzvah v'chad le'akeiv* — one verse establishes the obligation *lechatchila*, and the second tells you that the requirement is *me'akeiv* even *bedi'eved*. Without the second verse, one might have thought that failure to achieve the proper cloud is a deficiency but does not invalidate the *avodah*. *Tosafos* adds: since everything in *Yom Kippur* is generally *me'akeiv* by virtue of the word *chukkah*, this reading may be more naturally applied to the extension of the requirement to the rest of the year — both verses together teach that the daily *ketoret* too must have the cloud in order to be valid.

### Punishment Warning

*Rava amar*: *chad le'onash v'chad le'azhara* — one verse provides the punishment, the other the prohibition. *V'lo yamut* is the *onash*, the consequence; *ki b'anan eraeh* serves as the *azhara*, the warning: do not enter this way unless you have the proper cloud. *V'al yavo ki b'anan eraeh*: the prohibition is not to enter without the *anan*; *v'lo yamut* is the consequence if one does.

## Death Penalty…

### When Applies

A *tanya* elaborates: *Rebbi Eliezer omer* — *v'lo yamut* is the *onash*, *ki b'anan eraeh* is the *azhara*. The verse structure supports this: *v'al yavo bechol et el hakodesh... v'lo yamut ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet* — do not enter without the *anan*, and if you do, you will die.

### Acharei Mos

The *beraita* then poses a series of questions about the *parsha*'s temporal position. *Yachol yiheyu shneihem amurim kodem misat bnei Aharon* — perhaps both the prohibition and the consequence were communicated to *Moshe* and *Aharon* before the sons of *Aharon* died? After all, one might reason, it would only be just that they were warned beforehand if they were to be punished. *Talmud lomar acharei mos shnei bnei Aharon* — the heading of the *parsha* tells us it was said afterward.

But then perhaps *yachol yiheyu shneihem amurim achar misat shnei bnei Aharon* — maybe both the prohibition and the consequence were given only after they died, meaning they had received no warning beforehand at all? *Talmud lomar ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet* — the *lashon asid*, the future tense "I will appear," indicates that this was given before God's presence had yet appeared on the *kaporet*.

*Amar Rava amar kra ki b'anan eraeh — v'adayin lo nireh*: I will appear, and I have not yet appeared. Since by the time of *Acharei Mos* God had already appeared in the *Mishkan* on *yom hashmini*, this must have been stated before *yom hashmini* — before the *Mishkan* was erected. *Ha keitzad*? How to reconcile? *Azharah kodem misah*: the warning — *ki b'anan eraeh* — was given before they died, before the *Mishkan* was erected. The prohibition was communicated beforehand. But *v'lo yamut*, the articulation of the *onash*, was stated only *achar misah*, after they died.

This is a striking *Gemara*, and it is worth pausing on several dimensions. First, it has effectively read *ki b'anan eraeh* not as referring to the *anan haketoret* — as *Chazal* argued against the *Tzedukim* earlier — but as referring to God's ordinary theophanic cloud. If the verse meant "I will appear [in the cloud of *ketoret*]," the future tense would not prove that it was said before *yom hashmini*; it would simply mean that on a future *Yom Kippur*, when you perform the *avodah*, I will appear. The proof only works if *anan* refers to God's standard *anan* of divine presence, and the claim is that God had not yet appeared on the *kaporet* specifically. But even then, the end of *Sefer Shemos* describes God's *anan* filling the entire *Mishkan* — and the *Gemara* accepts that this refers to the whole structure, not specifically *al hakaporet*. So the verse's emphasis on the *kaporet* is what allows it to be read as pointing to something yet to occur.

The additional dimension is halachic: the *Gemara* presupposes that punishment would be unjust without prior warning of both the prohibition and the consequence. The visceral biblical account — the fire going forth from before God and consuming *Nadav* and *Avihu* — is being fully halachized. The deaths cannot be understood merely as a collision with overwhelming divine power; they must be framed within the architecture of prohibition, forewarning, and willful violation.

### Paskening

*V'ela mai ta'ama i'anush* — but if the punishment required prior warning of the consequence, and that was only given after their death, why were they punished at all?

*Keditanya, Rebbi Eliezer omer*: they died *al she'horu halacha bifnei Moshe Rabban* — because they issued a *halachic* ruling in the presence of their teacher. *Mah darash*? The verse states that the sons of *Aharon* placed fire on the altar. From this they derived: *af al pi she'esh yoredes min hashamayim, mitzvah lehavoi min ha'hedyot* — even though fire descends from Heaven, one is still obligated to add fire from a mundane source. This is in fact the correct *halacha*. But they ruled on it independently, *bifnei rabban*, without deferring to *Moshe*. And the connection to the earlier theme is unmistakable: on *yom hashmini*, immediately before their act, fire had come down from Heaven — *vatetzei esh milifnei Hashem*. They then brought their own *esh* in addition, ruling by their own authority that this was required even in the face of the divine fire. It is also worth noting that the verse says *esh zarah asher lo tziva osam* — a foreign fire which He had not commanded them — which suggests the problem lay not merely in the *ketoret* itself but in the nature of their action.

### Multiple Explanations

*Tosafos* notes that in *Toras Kohanim* there is a wide *machloket* among the *tannaim* as to why they died. *Ika man d'amar misinai natlu* — they finally received their punishment for the improper behavior when they saw God at *Har Sinai*, where it says *vayochlu vayishtu*, eating and drinking while beholding the divine presence. *V'is de'amri*: they used to say, "when will these two old men die, and we two will lead the generation" — an impatient ambition regarding *Moshe* and *Aharon*. Others say *parua rosh hayu* — they entered with disheveled hair. Others say *shtuyei yayin hayu* — they were intoxicated, which the juxtaposition with *yayin veshichar al tesht* immediately following their death suggests. And others say *mechusarei begadim hayu* — they lacked some of the required priestly vestments. *Uviferek Arba Misos nami maisi lah* — these explanations are also cited in the chapter of the four capital punishments.

## Walking Backwards

### Shlomo's Journey

Having concluded that the *Kohen Gadol* exits *derech kenisato*, walking backwards the way he came in, the *Gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — from where is this derived?

*Amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: the source is the verse concerning *Shlomo*, *vayavo Shlomo labamah asher beGivon Yerushalayim* — Shlomo came to the altar which is in *Givon*, Jerusalem. The verse oddly appends "Jerusalem" to a journey that went *to* Gibeon — it should have said he went *from* Jerusalem. *Vekhi mah inyan Givon etzel Yerushalayim*? The *Gemara* explains: *makish yetziato miGivon liYerushalayim levi'ato miYerushalayim liGivon* — by linking Gibeon and Jerusalem without the word "from," the verse connects his departure from Gibeon back to Jerusalem with his original approach from Jerusalem to Gibeon. Just as he went *panav klapei bamah* — facing the altar as he traveled toward it — so too when he departed from Gibeon back to Jerusalem, he continued to face the altar, walking backwards.

### Daily Application

This practice is not confined to the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. Just as we saw the *ketoret* requirement extended from the singular *Yom Kippur* moment into a daily obligation, the same pattern operates here with the backwards departure. *V'khein kohanim ba'avodatam* — the *kohanim* when performing their *avodah*, *uleviyim beduchnam* — the *levi'im* at the *duchan* as they sing, *v'Yisrael bema'amadam* — and the *Yisrael* representatives who stood in the *azarah* daily to represent the people for whose sake the *korbanot* were brought — when they leave, *lo hayu machzirim peneihem veholechim*, they would not turn their backs and walk away. Rather *metzadedim peneihem veholechim* — they would angle themselves sideways. Perhaps a full backwards walk is more appropriate when one is alone, as when leaving the *Kodesh Kodashim*; in the more crowded *azarah* one angles the face to the side rather than walking directly backward, but the back is never turned to the altar.

### Students Leaving

*V'khein talmid haniftar merabbo*: a student taking leave of his *rebbe* similarly does not turn his back, but faces to the side as he departs. The movement from *Mikdash* to *Torah* is an unmistakable and pointed one. In the absence of the *Mikdash*, *Torah* — embodied in the *rebbe* — is our center. The same reverence that governed departure from the *Kodesh Kodashim* and from the *azarah* now governs departure from the presence of one's teacher.

### Rabbi Stories

*Ki ha d'Rabbi Elazar*: when *Rabbi Elazar* would take leave of *Rabbi Yochanan*, the procedure varied depending on who was departing first. If *Rabbi Yochanan* wished to walk away, *Rabbi Elazar* would prostrate himself and remain in place until *Rabbi Yochanan* was no longer visible. If *Rabbi Elazar* himself was taking leave, *havei ka'azil l'achoreih* — he would walk backwards, *ad d'michsi minei d'Rabbi Yochanan* — until he had passed out of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s sight. The logic is clear: once the *rebbe* can no longer see him, turning his back would not be disrespectful.

### Rava Backwards

*Rava*, similarly, *kad havei miptar mineih d'Rav Yosef* — when taking leave of *Rav Yosef* — *havei azil l'achoreih*, he would walk backwards. The consequence: *ad d'minadp'in karaei* — his legs would strike against objects, *u'mitpayan askofta d'vei Rav Yosef dama* — and the doorposts of *Rav Yosef*'s house would be smeared with blood. He was willing to absorb that cost. This detail carries an unmistakable resonance — blood on the doorpost — though perhaps unintended.

*Amru leih l'Rav Yosef hachi avid Rava*: people reported this to *Rav Yosef*, who was blind and could not see it himself. Notably, even though *Rav Yosef* could not see *Rava* departing, *Rava* continued to walk backwards — because others could see, and because respectful conduct in the presence of one's *rebbe* is not simply a matter of being observed by him. *Amar Rav Yosef: yehei ra'ava d'tairem reishach a'kula karcha* — may it be God's will that your head be raised over the entire city, which *Rashi* understands as a blessing to become *Rosh Yeshiva*. *Maharsha* reads it as a blessing to lead both competing *yeshivot* — both his own and *Abaye*'s.

### Prayer Steps

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: the same principle extends to *tefillah*. One who prays must take three steps backward at the conclusion of *Shemoneh Esrei* — *v'achar kach yiten shalom* — and only afterward recite the *oseh shalom* farewell. Note the order: first the steps, then *oseh shalom*. One might have expected the farewell to precede the departure, but there is a logic here: by saying goodbye one implicitly casts oneself as already outside the encounter. Instead, one first demonstrates the distancing — the physical stepping back — and then, once that breach has been created, sums up the experience with *oseh shalom bimromav hu ya'aseh shalom*. This too models after our central *avodah*: just as the *Kohen Gadol* exits the *Kodesh Kodashim* before completing his benediction to the assembled people, here the physical withdrawal precedes the formulaic conclusion.

### Dog Vomit

*Amar leih Rav Mordechai*: once one has taken those three steps back, one must stand there — *hatam miba'ei leih l'meikam* — and not immediately return to where one was standing. *Mashal l'talmid haniftar merabo* — the comparison returns to the *rebbe*-student relationship: *im chozer l'altar, domeh l'kelev sheshav al ke'o* — if he steps back and immediately returns, it is like a dog returning to its vomit. The image is jarring, and its exact significance is not entirely transparent. One reading: the act of stepping back is itself a kind of self-abasement, an *anochi afar va'efer* — an acknowledgment of one's smallness before the divine or before the *rebbe*. To immediately return negates that act of abasement, reversing the debasement just performed. Whatever the precise valence of the image, the practical instruction is clear: after the three steps back, one stands for a moment. One does not simply stride back to one's seat or to where one was standing before. The leave-taking must mean something.
