# Yoma 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Entry

### Holy of Holies Entry

Daf 53 opens at precisely the moment we have been building toward throughout the tractate: the *Kohen Gadol* stands inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. He has passed through the curtains, he stands near the rock — or, in the *Bayit Rishon*, beside the *Kodesh Kodashim* itself. He sets down the fire pan, and through a process debated earlier — whether he needed to perform *chofin v'chozer v'chofin*, scooping and re-scooping — he managed to transfer the incense from the ladle back into his hands and place it onto the fire pan. The chamber fills with smoke, and he prepares to leave.

### Mishnah Drama

Before entering the *sugyot* proper, it is worth pausing on something that might seem self-evident but is genuinely worth naming: the *Mishnayot* of this tractate have employed a persistent rhetorical technique of pulling out and pulling in. Each time the reader expects to finally arrive at the *avodah*, the Mishnah steps back — "and the rest of the year they would do it this way" — before drawing the narrative back in toward the center. The effect is dramatic. Had the Mishnah simply recited the procedure in sequence, the moment would not feel as momentous. By constantly supplying the larger context and then narrowing back toward the singular, we are made to feel the full weight of what the *Kohen Gadol* is doing: an act so utterly unique that it can only be appreciated against the backdrop of everything it is not.

## Tzedokim Dispute

### Incense Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנָתַן אֶת הַקְּטוֹרֶת עַל הָאֵשׁ לִפְנֵי ה׳״ — שֶׁלֹּא יְתַקֵּן מִבַּחוּץ וְיַכְנִיס. לְהוֹצִיא מִלִּבָּן שֶׁל צַדּוּקִין, שֶׁאוֹמְרִים יְתַקֵּן מִבַּחוּץ וְיַכְנִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: **"And he shall put the incense upon the fire before the Lord"** (Leviticus 16:13); this means that **he** should **not prepare** by placing the incense **outside,** in the Sanctuary, **and bring** into the Holy of Holies a coal pan holding the burning incense. This was emphasized in order **to exclude the opinion of the Sadducees, who say** that **he** should **prepare** the incense **outside** and then **bring** it in.

A *Tanu Rabbanan* at the top of 53a introduces a *beraita* anchored in the verse *v'natan et haketoret al ha'esh lifnei Hashem* — "and you shall place the *ketoret* on the fire before God." The *beraita* understands *lifnei Hashem* as a directive: when you are in the presence of God, when you are inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, do not place the *ketoret* on the fire pan outside and then carry it in already burning. This is the *lehotzi mi'liban shel Tzedukim* — the verse is meant to uproot the position of the *Tzedukim*.

It is worth recalling the context: during the seven days of preparation before *Yom Kippur*, the *Kohen Gadol* was instructed in the proper procedure and made to take an oath that he would not follow the *Tzedukim* practice. Much of the anxiety surrounding the *Kohen Gadol*'s potential death inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* was historically connected to concern that he might conduct the *avodah* according to *Tzedukim* custom.

### Tzedokim Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? ״כִּי בֶּעָנָן אֵרָאֶה עַל הַכַּפּוֹרֶת״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁיְּתַקֵּן מִבַּחוּץ וְיַכְנִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did** the Sadducees **interpret;** what verse do they cite as the basis for their opinion? The Gemara answers that it was the verse: **"For I will appear in a cloud upon the Ark cover"** (Leviticus 16:2), which the Sadducees say **teaches that he should prepare** it **outside,** so there would already be a cloud of incense, and only then should **he bring** it inside the Holy of Holies.

The *Gemara* now asks: *mai darosh* — what did the *Tzedukim* actually derive? Their position is rooted in the verse *ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet*, which appears in the opening of *Acharei Mos*: *v'al yavo b'chol et el hakodesh mibeit laparochet el pnei hakaporet asher al ha'aron v'lo yamut ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet*. The *Tzedukim* read this as follows: since God appears upon the *kaporet* in a cloud, if you wish to enter, you must enter enveloped in a cloud. *Ki b'anan* is not a description of God's presence but a condition on the *Kohen Gadol*'s entry — he must come in already surrounded by the cloud of burning *ketoret*. Hence their ruling: *yitaken mibachutz v'yachnis*, prepare it outside with the incense already burning, and then carry it in.

### Chazal Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים, וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וְנָתַן אֶת הַקְּטוֹרֶת עַל הָאֵשׁ לִפְנֵי ה׳״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי בֶּעָנָן אֵרָאֶה עַל הַכַּפּוֹרֶת״ —
>
> **Translation:** The Sages said to them: Wasn't it already stated explicitly: **"And he shall put the incense upon the fire before the Lord"** (Leviticus 16:13), which indicates that the High Priest should present the incense only when he stands before God in the Holy of Holies? **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "For I will appear in a cloud upon the Ark cover"** (Leviticus 16:2)? This verse does not mean that there should already be an incense cloud before he enters.

The *Chachamim* respond: has it not already been stated explicitly — *v'natan et haketoret al ha'esh lifnei Hashem*? The *ketoret* is placed on the fire in the presence of God, which clearly means inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. If so, what do we do with *ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet*?

### Maaleh Ashan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנּוֹתֵן בָּהּ מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן. וּמִנַּיִין שֶׁנּוֹתֵן בָּהּ מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִסָּה עֲנַן הַקְּטוֹרֶת אֶת הַכַּפּוֹרֶת״. הָא לֹא נָתַן בָּהּ מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן, אוֹ שֶׁחִיסַּר אַחַת מִכׇּל סַמָּנֶיהָ — חַיָּיב מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** Instead, this verse is referring to the shape of the incense cloud, and it **teaches that he includes a smoke-raiser in its** spices, a plant that causes the smoke to rise straight up like a staff. **And from where** is it derived **that he includes a smoke-raiser in its** spices? **As it is stated: "And the incense cloud shall cover the Ark cover"** (Leviticus 16:13). Therefore, if **he did not include a smoke-raiser, or if he omitted any one of its spices, he is liable** to receive the **death** penalty.

Rather than reading *anan* as a reference to the cloud of God's normal theophanic appearance, *Chazal* concede — and this is notable — that the *anan* here indeed refers to the cloud produced by the *ketoret*. But *melamed she-noten bah ma'aleh ashan*: the verse is teaching that the *ketoret* must contain the herb called *ma'aleh ashan*, that ingredient which causes the smoke to rise in a straight column rather than dispersing. The *ki b'anan eraeh* means: when you come to encounter Me, ensure that the *ketoret* creates a proper *anan* — but it must be created inside, not brought in from outside.

One might ask: would the *ketoret* not produce smoke regardless, even without *ma'aleh ashan*? A reasonable reading of the *Gemara* is that *ma'aleh ashan* is a specific plant — indeed a proper name, not a generic descriptor — and that without it there would still be smoke, just not the ideal upward-streaming column. Alternatively, the verse may be understood more broadly as requiring any substance that ensures a robust cloud: *v'chisa mikkol makom*, the cloud must cover the *kaporet* by whatever means works. Both readings remain in play, and the *Gemara* will revisit this question.

## Ki BeAnan Erah

### First Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִין שֶׁנּוֹתֵן בָּהּ מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִסָּה עֲנַן הַקְּטוֹרֶת אֶת הַכַּפּוֹרֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **that he includes a smoke-raiser in its** spices? **As it is stated: "And the incense cloud shall cover the Ark cover"** (Leviticus 16:13).

Having established that *ki b'anan eraeh* teaches the requirement for *ma'aleh ashan*, the *beraita* then adds: *u'minayin she-noten bah ma'aleh ashan?* — and from where do we know this requirement? *Talmud lomar v'chisa anan haketoret et hakaporet*: the second verse makes it explicit that the cloud of the *ketoret* must cover the *kaporet*. The *Gemara* immediately questions this structure.

### Second Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרָא לִקְרָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עֲלֵה מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן, עִיקַּר מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִסָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** One **verse for** another **verse?** The requirement of a smoke-raiser was already proven from the verse: "For I will appear in a cloud upon the Ark cover" (Leviticus 16:2); why cite a second verse in support of the first one? **Rav Yosef said: This is** what the *baraita* **said;** this is how to understand the matter: **I have** derived **nothing other** than the obligation to bring **a leaf of a smoke-raising** plant. **From where** is it derived that he must also bring **a root of a smoke-raiser? The verse states: "And it shall cover,"** which indicates the requirement for more of the smoke-raiser than might have been supposed from the other verse.

*Kra bi kra?* — do you need one verse to prove another? You already cited *ki b'anan eraeh* as the source for *ma'aleh ashan*, and now you cite a second verse to confirm it?

*Amar Rav Yosef hachi ka'amar*: the two verses are not redundant. *Ein li ela aleh ma'aleh ashan* — I might have thought only the leaf of the *ma'aleh ashan* plant suffices. *Ikar ma'aleh ashan minayin* — from where do I know the root of the plant may also be used? *Talmud lomar v'chisa*: the additional verse extends the obligation to include even the root, which is perhaps less potent than the leaf.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא אִיפְּכָא תַּנְיָא! דְּתַנְיָא: נָתַן בָּהּ עִיקַּר מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן, הָיָה מִתַּמֵּר וְעוֹלֶה כְּמַקֵּל עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לִשְׁמֵי קוֹרָה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לִשְׁמֵי קוֹרָה, מְמַשְׁמֵשׁ וְיוֹרֵד בַּכּוֹתָלִים עַד שֶׁנִּתְמַלֵּא הַבַּיִת עָשָׁן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַבַּיִת יִמָּלֵא עָשָׁן״!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** to Rav Yosef: Your interpretation indicates that the leaf of the smoke-raiser raises smoke better than its root, as the requirement for a root is merely considered an addition. **But wasn't the opposite taught** in a *baraita*? **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **He put into** the incense **a root of a smoke-raiser,** and the smoke **would rise in a column like a staff until it reached** the Temple **ceiling. When it reached** the **ceiling** there was nowhere for the smoke to exit the building, and therefore it slowly **descended the walls until the** entire **House was filled with smoke, as it is stated: "And the House was filled with smoke"** (Isaiah 6:4). This *baraita* indicates that the root of the smoke-raiser raises smoke better than its leaf.

*Abaye* challenges this: but the *beraita* itself teaches the opposite — the root is *better* than the leaf! As it says: *natan b'ikar ma'aleh ashan haya mitamer v'oleh kemakel* — when one placed the root in the *ketoret*, the smoke would rise straight as a pole *ad shemagia lishmei kora*, until it reached the ceiling beams, and then *memashmesh v'yored bakotalim* — it would feel its way downward along the walls, *ad shenitmaleh habayit ashan*, until the chamber was filled with smoke, as it says in *Yeshayahu*, *v'habayit yimalei ashan*. So the root produces the superior result.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עִיקַּר מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן, עֲלֵה מַעֲלֵה עָשָׁן מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִסָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that **this is** what the *baraita* **said;** this is how it should be understood: **I have** derived **nothing other** than the obligation to bring **a root of a smoke-raising** plant. **From where** is it derived that he must also bring **a leaf of a smoke-raiser? The verse states: "And it shall cover."**

*Ela amar Abaye hachi ka'amar*: reverse it. The first verse establishes that the root suffices — that is already known to be the ideal. The second verse, *v'chisa*, comes to extend the requirement to the leaf as well, even though it is the lesser option, teaching that even with the leaf, provided the cloud ultimately covers the *kaporet*, the *avodah* is valid.

### Rav Sheshet on Mishkan and Mikdash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד שֶׁבַּמִּדְבָּר. שִׁילֹה וּבֵית עוֹלָמִים מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִסָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** that the *baraita* means the following: **I have** derived **nothing other** than the inclusion of a smoke-raiser amongst the other spices in **the Tent of Meeting that** was **in the wilderness; from where** is it derived that this obligation extends to the Tabernacle at **Shiloh and** in **the eternal Temple** in Jerusalem? **The verse states: "And it shall cover,"** which indicates that wherever the High Priest enters the Holy of Holies to burn incense, he must include a smoke-raiser in its ingredients.

## Ki BeAnan Erah (Extended)

### Expanded Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִסָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is** what the *baraita* **said: I have** derived **nothing other** than the obligation to include a smoke-raiser in the spices of the incense offered **on Yom Kippur** in the Holy of Holies; **from where** is it derived that this obligation applies to the incense offered **on the rest of the days of the year** in the Sanctuary? **The verse states: "And it shall cover,"** to teach that every incense burned in the Temple must include a smoke-raiser.

This is a striking expansion. The natural assumption has always been that the emphasis on *ma'aleh ashan* and the cloud of the *ketoret* is specifically a *Yom Kippur* concern, connected to the *Kohen Gadol*'s entry into the *Kodesh Kodashim*. But *Chazal* now establish that the requirement — including the attendant *chiyuv mita* for failing to fulfill it — applies equally to the *ketoret* burned twice daily on the golden altar in the *Heichal*. Indeed, when the *Gemara* discussed the family that guarded the secret knowledge of producing the *ketoret*, that discussion concerned the *daily* offering. We say every *Shabbos* in *Pittum HaKtoret*: *chisar achat mikol samaneha chayav mita* — and now we understand why this applies even outside the context of *Yom Kippur*.

There is something deeply meaningful here. The encounter with God that the *ketoret* is meant to facilitate — what the verse *ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet* points toward — is not confined to the singular moment of *Yom Kippur*. Even in the daily offering, some shadow of that encounter, some echo of the intense divine presence, is being sought and required. The daily *ketoret* is in some sense modeled after, and reaches toward, the *Yom Kippur* experience. The same conditions, the same standards, and the same consequences attach — not with the same intensity, but with the same underlying orientation.

### Multiple Hermeneutical Frameworks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: חַד לְמִצְוָה, וְחַד לְעַכֵּב. רָבָא אָמַר: חַד לְעוֹנֶשׁ, וְחַד לְאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: One** verse teaches us **the mitzva** itself, **and one** verse teaches that this requirement is **indispensable,** i.e., the rite is invalid if one omits this ingredient. **Rava said: One** verse teaches that the omission of the smoke-raiser renders one liable to receive the **punishment** of death, **and one** verse serves **as a warning** that it is prohibited to omit it, as the Torah does not require punishment without issuing a warning.

*Rav Ashi amar*: *chad lemitzvah v'chad le'akeiv* — one verse establishes the obligation *lechatchila*, and the second tells you that the requirement is *me'akeiv* even *bedi'eved*. Without the second verse, one might have thought that failure to achieve the proper cloud is a deficiency but does not invalidate the *avodah*.

*Rava amar*: *chad le'onash v'chad le'azhara* — one verse provides the punishment, the other the prohibition. *V'lo yamut* is the *onash*, the consequence; *ki b'anan eraeh* serves as the *azhara*, the warning: do not enter this way unless you have the proper cloud. *V'al yavo ki b'anan eraeh*: the prohibition is not to enter without the *anan*; *v'lo yamut* is the consequence if one does.

## Death Penalty and Acharei Mos

### Temporal Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: ״וְלֹא יָמוּת״ — עוֹנֶשׁ, ״כִּי בֶּעָנָן אֵרָאֶה״ — אַזְהָרָה. יָכוֹל יִהְיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲמוּרִין קוֹדֶם מִיתַת בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַחֲרֵי מוֹת שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״. יָכוֹל יִהְיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲמוּרִים אַחַר מִיתַת שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי בֶּעָנָן אֵרָאֶה עַל הַכַּפּוֹרֶת״. הָא כֵּיצַד? אַזְהָרָה קוֹדֶם מִיתָה, וְעוֹנֶשׁ אַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** "Speak to Aaron your brother that he should not come at all times into the sacred place within the curtain, before the Ark cover that is upon the Ark; **that he not die"** (Leviticus 16:2). This verse teaches the **punishment** for the omission of the smoke-raiser from the spices of the incense. With regard to the verse: **"For I will appear in a cloud** upon the Ark cover" (Leviticus 16:2); this is the **warning** that it is prohibited to omit the smoke-raiser, which causes the cloud to rise. One **might** have thought that **both** verses **were said before the deaths of Aaron's sons,** who died upon entering the sacred place. Therefore, **the verse states: "After the deaths of the two sons of Aaron"** (Leviticus 16:1). One **might** have thought that **both of them,** the punishment and the warning, **were said after the deaths of Aaron's two sons.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For I will appear in a cloud upon the Ark cover."** **How so;** how should these verses be understood? The **warning** was said **before** the **death** of Aaron's sons, **and the punishment** associated with it was stated **after the death** of his sons.

A *tanya* elaborates: *Rebbi Eliezer omer* — *v'lo yamut* is the *onash*, *ki b'anan eraeh* is the *azhara*. The verse structure supports this: *v'al yavo bechol et el hakodesh... v'lo yamut ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet* — do not enter without the *anan*, and if you do, you will die.

The *beraita* then poses a series of questions about the *parsha*'s temporal position. *Yachol yiheyu shneihem amurim kodem misat bnei Aharon* — perhaps both the prohibition and the consequence were communicated to *Moshe* and *Aharon* before the sons of *Aharon* died? After all, one might reason, it would only be just that they were warned beforehand if they were to be punished. *Talmud lomar acharei mos shnei bnei Aharon* — the heading of the *parsha* tells us it was said afterward.

But then perhaps *yachol yiheyu shneihem amurim achar misat shnei bnei Aharon* — maybe both the prohibition and the consequence were given only after they died, meaning they had received no warning beforehand at all? *Talmud lomar ki b'anan eraeh al hakaporet* — the *lashon asid*, the future tense "I will appear," indicates that this was given before God's presence had yet appeared on the *kaporet*.

### The Proof from Future Tense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי בֶּעָנָן אֵרָאֶה״ — וַעֲדַיִן לֹא נִרְאָה. וְאֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמָא אִיעֲנוּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses puzzlement at Rabbi Eliezer's statement: **What is the** biblical **derivation?** How does Rabbi Eliezer learn from the phrase "I will appear" that the warning was stated earlier? **Rava said** that **the verse states: "For I will appear in a cloud";** the future tense indicates that the cloud over the Ark cover **had not yet appeared,** as the Tabernacle had not been built, which means that this command was issued before Aaron's sons entered and died. The Gemara asks: **Rather, what is the reason** that Aaron's sons **were punished** with death, if the punishment had not been stated? The Torah does not punish one who is unaware of the sin's punishment.

*Amar Rava amar kra ki b'anan eraeh — v'adayin lo nireh*: I will appear, and I have not yet appeared. Since by the time of *Acharei Mos* God had already appeared in the *Mishkan* on *yom hashmini*, this must have been stated before *yom hashmini* — before the *Mishkan* was erected. *Ha keitzad*? How to reconcile? *Azharah kodem misah*: the warning — *ki b'anan eraeh* — was given before they died, before the *Mishkan* was erected. The prohibition was communicated beforehand. But *v'lo yamut*, the articulation of the *onash*, was stated only *achar misah*, after they died.

This is a striking *Gemara*, and it is worth pausing on several dimensions. First, it has effectively read *ki b'anan eraeh* not as referring to the *anan haketoret* — as *Chazal* argued against the *Tzedukim* earlier — but as referring to God's ordinary theophanic cloud. If the verse meant "I will appear [in the cloud of *ketoret*]," the future tense would not prove that it was said before *yom hashmini*; it would simply mean that on a future *Yom Kippur*, when you perform the *avodah*, I will appear. The proof only works if *anan* refers to God's standard *anan* of divine presence, and the claim is that God had not yet appeared on the *kaporet* specifically. But even then, the end of *Sefer Shemos* describes God's *anan* filling the entire *Mishkan* — and the *Gemara* accepts that this refers to the whole structure, not specifically *al hakaporet*. So the verse's emphasis on the *kaporet* is what allows it to be read as pointing to something yet to occur.

The additional dimension is halachic: the *Gemara* presupposes that punishment would be unjust without prior warning of both the prohibition and the consequence. The visceral biblical account — the fire going forth from before God and consuming *Nadav* and *Avihu* — is being fully halachized. The deaths cannot be understood merely as a collision with overwhelming divine power; they must be framed within the architecture of prohibition, forewarning, and willful violation.

### Alternative Explanations for Their Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא מֵתוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן אֶלָּא עַל שֶׁהוֹרוּ הֲלָכָה בִּפְנֵי מֹשֶׁה רַבָּן. מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? ״וְנָתְנוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן אֵשׁ עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהָאֵשׁ יוֹרֶדֶת מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, מִצְוָה לְהָבִיא מִן הַהֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: They were not punished for their entry into the sacred place but for a different reason, **as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Aaron's sons died only because they taught a *halakha* before Moses their teacher;** they should have asked him for his ruling, but they neglected to do so. The Gemara asks: **What did they interpret** from the verse that led them to enter the sacred place with fire? The verse states: **"And the sons of Aaron the priest shall put fire upon the altar"** (Leviticus 1:7), which indicates that **although fire descends from the heavens,** it is a **mitzva to bring man-made** fire. To fulfill this mitzva, Aaron's sons entered and brought fire to the altar. Although their ruling was in accordance with the *halakha*, they incurred the death penalty for failing to ask Moses.

*V'ela mai ta'ama i'anush* — but if the punishment required prior warning of the consequence, and that was only given after their death, why were they punished at all?

*Keditanya, Rebbi Eliezer omer*: they died *al she'horu halacha bifnei Moshe Rabban* — because they issued a *halachic* ruling in the presence of their teacher. *Mah darash*? The verse states that the sons of *Aharon* placed fire on the altar. From this they derived: *af al pi she'esh yoredes min hashamayim, mitzvah lehavoi min ha'hedyot* — even though fire descends from Heaven, one is still obligated to add fire from a mundane source. This is in fact the correct *halacha*. But they ruled on it independently, *bifnei rabban*, without deferring to *Moshe*. And the connection to the earlier theme is unmistakable: on *yom hashmini*, immediately before their act, fire had come down from Heaven — *vatetzei esh milifnei Hashem*. They then brought their own *esh* in addition, ruling by their own authority that this was required even in the face of the divine fire. It is also worth noting that the verse says *esh zarah asher lo tziva osam* — a foreign fire which He had not commanded them — which suggests the problem lay not merely in the *ketoret* itself but in the nature of their action.

## Walking Backwards

### Shlomo's Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא וּבָא לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ כְּנִיסָתוֹ. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיָּבֹא שְׁלֹמֹה לַבָּמָה אֲשֶׁר בְּגִבְעוֹן יְרוּשָׁלִַם״, וְכִי מָה עִנְיַן גִּבְעוֹן אֵצֶל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the High Priest **exits and comes** out **the way** that **he entered,** i.e., he does not turn around, but walks backward with his face toward the Ark. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived; what is the source in the Torah for this *halakha*? **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said** that **the verse states: "And Solomon came to the high place that was at Gibeon, Jerusalem"** (II Chronicles 1:13). **Now, what does Gibeon have to do with Jerusalem?** Either he came to Gibeon or to Jerusalem; these are two different places.

Having concluded that the *Kohen Gadol* exits *derech kenisato*, walking backwards the way he came in, the *Gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — from where is this derived?

*Amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: the source is the verse concerning *Shlomo*, *vayavo Shlomo labamah asher beGivon Yerushalayim* — Shlomo came to the altar which is in *Givon*, Jerusalem. The verse oddly appends "Jerusalem" to a journey that went *to* Gibeon — it should have said he went *from* Jerusalem. *Vekhi mah inyan Givon etzel Yerushalayim*? The *Gemara* explains: *makish yetziato miGivon liYerushalayim levi'ato miYerushalayim liGivon* — by linking Gibeon and Jerusalem without the word "from," the verse connects his departure from Gibeon back to Jerusalem with his original approach from Jerusalem to Gibeon. Just as he went *panav klapei bamah* — facing the altar as he traveled toward it — so too when he departed from Gibeon back to Jerusalem, he continued to face the altar, walking backwards.

### Comparative Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מַקִּישׁ יְצִיאָתוֹ מִגִּבְעוֹן לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, לְבִיאָתוֹ מִירוּשָׁלַיִם לְגִבְעוֹן: מָה בִּיאָתוֹ מִירוּשָׁלַיִם לְגִבְעוֹן — פָּנָיו כְּלַפֵּי בָמָה, כְּדֶרֶךְ בִּיאָתוֹ, אַף יְצִיאָתוֹ מִגִּבְעוֹן לִירוּשָׁלַיִם — פָּנָיו כְּלַפֵּי בָמָה, כְּדֶרֶךְ בִּיאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse **compares his exit from Gibeon to Jerusalem to his arrival from Jerusalem to Gibeon. Just as** in the case of **his arrival from Jerusalem to Gibeon his face was** necessarily **turned toward the high place, in the usual manner** of a person's **arrival, so too,** upon **his exit from Gibeon to Jerusalem his face** was still **turned toward the high place, in the manner of his arrival.** This teaches that one does not turn his face away from a sacred place; rather, he must walk backward.

### Daily Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן כֹּהֲנִים בַּעֲבוֹדָתָן, וּלְוִיִּם בְּדוּכָנָם, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַעֲמָדָם, כְּשֶׁהֵן נִפְטָרִין — לֹא הָיוּ מַחֲזִירִין פְּנֵיהֶן וְהוֹלְכִין, אֶלָּא מְצַדְּדִין פְּנֵיהֶן וְהוֹלְכִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise,** with regard to **priests in their service; and Levites on their platform** in the Temple, where they recited songs; **and Israelites at their watches,** where they observed the sacrifice of the daily offering: **When they departed** from the sacred place, **they would not turn their faces and walk but would turn their faces sideways and walk,** so as not to turn their backs on the sacred place.

This practice is not confined to the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. Just as we saw the *ketoret* requirement extended from the singular *Yom Kippur* moment into a daily obligation, the same pattern operates here with the backwards departure. *V'khein kohanim ba'avodatam* — the *kohanim* when performing their *avodah*, *uleviyim beduchnam* — the *levi'im* at the *duchan* as they sing, *v'Yisrael bema'adamam* — and the *Yisrael* representatives who stood in the *azarah* daily to represent the people for whose sake the *korbanot* were brought — when they leave, *lo hayu machzirim peneihem veholechim*, they would not turn their backs and walk away. Rather *metzadedim peneihem veholechim* — they would angle themselves sideways. Perhaps a full backwards walk is more appropriate when one is alone, as when leaving the *Kodesh Kodashim*; in the more crowded *azarah* one angles the face to the side rather than walking directly backward, but the back is never turned to the altar.

### Students Leaving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן תַּלְמִיד הַנִּפְטָר מֵרַבּוֹ לֹא יַחֲזִיר פָּנָיו וְיֵלֵךְ, אֶלָּא מְצַדֵּד פָּנָיו וְהוֹלֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise, a student who takes leave of his teacher** should **not turn his face and walk but turn his face sideways and walk.** 

*V'khein talmid haniftar merabbo*: a student taking leave of his *rebbe* similarly does not turn his back, but faces to the side as he departs. The movement from *Mikdash* to *Torah* is an unmistakable and pointed one. In the absence of the *Mikdash*, *Torah* — embodied in the *rebbe* — is our center. The same reverence that governed departure from the *Kodesh Kodashim* and from the *azarah* now governs departure from the presence of one's teacher.

### Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר כַּד הֲוָה מִיפְּטַר מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, כַּד הֲוָה בָּעֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְסַגּוֹיֵי, הֲוָה גָּחֵין קָאֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אַדּוּכְתֵּיהּ עַד דַּהֲוָה מִיכַּסֵּי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **in accordance with that** practice **of Rabbi Elazar when he took leave of his teacher, Rabbi Yoḥanan. When Rabbi Yoḥanan wanted to leave** him, **Rabbi Elazar would bend down and stand in his place** as a sign of respect and humility, **until Rabbi Yoḥanan disappeared from his sight;** only then would Rabbi Elazar turn to leave.

*Ki ha d'Rabbi Elazar*: when *Rabbi Elazar* would take leave of *Rabbi Yochanan*, the procedure varied depending on who was departing first. If *Rabbi Yochanan* wished to walk away, *Rabbi Elazar* would prostrate himself and remain in place until *Rabbi Yochanan* was no longer visible. If *Rabbi Elazar* himself was taking leave, *havei ka'azil l'achoreih* — he would walk backwards, *ad d'michsi minei d'Rabbi Yochanan* — until he had passed out of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s sight. The logic is clear: once the *rebbe* can no longer see him, turning his back would not be disrespectful.

### Rabbi Elazar Walking Backwards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַד הֲוָה בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְסַגּוֹיֵי, הֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ עַד דְּמִכַּסֵּי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And when Rabbi Elazar wanted to leave, he would walk backward until he disappeared from Rabbi Yoḥanan's sight,** and only then would he walk normally, so as not to turn his back on his teacher.

### Rava Backwards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא כַּד הֲוָה מִיפְּטַר מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, הֲוָה אָזֵיל לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ עַד דְּמִנַּגְּפָן כַּרְעֵיהּ וּמִתַּוְּוסָן אִסְקוּפָּתָא דְּבֵי רַב יוֹסֵף דְּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rava took leave of Rav Yosef, he would walk backward,** paying no attention to the obstacles in his path, **until his legs were bruised and the threshold of Rav Yosef's house was bloodied** from Rava's wounds.

*Rava*, similarly, *kad havei miptar mineih d'Rav Yosef* — when taking leave of *Rav Yosef* — *havei azil l'achoreih*, he would walk backwards. The consequence: *ad d'minadp'in karaei* — his legs would strike against objects, *u'mitpayan askofta d'vei Rav Yosef dama* — and the doorposts of *Rav Yosef*'s house would be smeared with blood. He was willing to absorb that cost. This detail carries an unmistakable resonance — blood on the doorpost — though perhaps unintended.

### Rav Yosef's Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הָכִי עָבֵיד רָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יְהֵא רַעֲוָא דִּתְרוּם רֵישָׁךְ אַכּוּלֵּהּ כַּרְכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to Rav Yosef: This is what Rava does.** Rav Yosef was blind and could not see for himself. Rav Yosef **said to him: May it be** God's **will that you lift your head over the entire city,** in reward for honoring your teacher.

*Amru leih l'Rav Yosef hachi avid Rava*: people reported this to *Rav Yosef*, who was blind and could not see it himself. Notably, even though *Rav Yosef* could not see *Rava* departing, *Rava* continued to walk backwards — because others could see, and because respectful conduct in the presence of one's *rebbe* is not simply a matter of being observed by him. *Amar Rav Yosef: yehei ra'ava d'tairem reishach a'kula karcha* — may it be God's will that your head be raised over the entire city, which *Rashi* understands as a blessing to become *Rosh Yeshiva*.

### Prayer Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּפְסִיעַ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּסִיעוֹת לַאֲחוֹרָיו, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִתֵּן שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the obligation of a student to walk backward when taking leave of his teacher, the Gemara discusses a similar topic. **Rabbi Alexandri said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: One who prays must take three steps backward** upon concluding his prayer **and then recite: Peace,** in a manner befitting one who departs from before the Holy One, Blessed be He.

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: the same principle extends to *tefillah*. One who prays must take three steps backward at the conclusion of *Shemoneh Esrei* — *v'achar kach yiten shalom* — and only afterward recite the *oseh shalom* farewell. Note the order: first the steps, then *oseh shalom*. One might have expected the farewell to precede the departure, but there is a logic here: by saying goodbye one implicitly casts oneself as already outside the encounter. Instead, one first demonstrates the distancing — the physical stepping back — and then, once that breach has been created, sums up the experience with *oseh shalom bimromav hu ya'aseh shalom*. This too models after our central *avodah*: just as the *Kohen Gadol* exits the *Kodesh Kodashim* before completing his benediction to the assembled people, here the physical withdrawal precedes the formulaic conclusion.

### Standing After Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי: כֵּיוָן שֶׁפָּסַע שָׁלֹשׁ פְּסִיעוֹת לַאֲחוֹרָיו — הָתָם אִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְמֵיקַם. מָשָׁל לְתַלְמִיד הַנִּפְטָר מֵרַבּוֹ, אִם חוֹזֵר לְאַלְתַּר, דּוֹמֶה לְכֶלֶב שֶׁשָּׁב עַל קִיאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Mordekhai **said to him: Since he has taken three steps backward, he should stand there** and not return to his place immediately. This is **analogous to a student who takes leave of his teacher. If he returns immediately** to the place where was first standing, **he is similar to a dog who returns to its vomit,** and his previous action is spoiled.

*Amar leih Rav Mordechai*: once one has taken those three steps back, one must stand there — *hatam miba'ei leih l'meikam* — and not immediately return to where one was standing. *Mashal l'talmid haniftar merabo* — the comparison returns to the *rebbe*-student relationship: *im chozer l'altar, domeh l'kelev sheshav al ke'o* — if he steps back and immediately returns, it is like a dog returning to its vomit. The image is jarring, and its exact significance is not entirely transparent. One reading: the act of stepping back is itself a kind of self-abasement, an *anochi afar va'efer* — an acknowledgment of one's smallness before the divine or before the *rebbe*. To immediately return negates that act of abasement, reversing the debasement just performed. Whatever the precise valence of the image, the practical instruction is clear: after the three steps back, one stands for a moment. One does not simply stride back to one's seat or to where one was standing before. The leave-taking must mean something.
