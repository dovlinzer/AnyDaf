# Menachot 28b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Menorah Materials

### Temple Replicas

The Gemara at the bottom and middle of 28b continues its discussion of the *menorah*, turning from the question of what materials may be used for it to the broader prohibition against fashioning replicas of Temple vessels. A *braita* states: *lo yaaseh adam bayit tavnit heichal, achsadrah keneged ulam* — a person may not construct his house according to the dimensions of the *heichal*, nor may he build his *achsadrah* corresponding to the *ulam*. The *achsadrah*, a Roman-era portico with three walls open on the fourth side, functions like the *ulam* in that it serves as an entry-hall; the *ulam*'s opening was twenty *ammot* wide and thirty *ammot* high, effectively giving it the character of such a space. Similarly, one may not make his courtyard according to the dimensions of the Temple *azarah*, nor a table according to the dimensions of the Temple *shulchan*, nor a *menorah* corresponding to the Temple *menorah*.

Several practical questions arise from this prohibition. If one fashions a *menorah* not of gold, there is an irony at work: a non-gold *menorah*, if used in the *Mikdash*, would require far fewer of the ornamental features mandated for the golden *menorah*. This actually makes the prohibition more stringent in a subtle way — a non-gold *menorah* is a closer functional analog to a valid *Mikdash menorah* because it needs so few conditions to be acceptable. One might therefore need to be more careful that it not be considered something fit for Temple use. The exact threshold of the prohibition — whether proportions must match precisely, or whether a change in size suffices to avoid the prohibition — is a question the *poskim* address at length.

The *braita* then draws an important distinction: one may make a candelabra with five, six, or eight arms — but not seven. Furthermore, the prohibition applies even to a *menorah* made of other metals, *vaafilu shel sh'ar minei matachot*, since the *menorah* in the *Mikdash* itself would be *kasher* if made from other metals.

An intriguing tradition connects this passage to *Chanukah*. The prohibition against fashioning a seven-branched candelabra is derived not from the *braita* alone but from the *pasuk* in *Rosh Hashanah*: *lo ta'asu iti*, read as "do not make things that are associated with Me." This is understood to prohibit replicating objects of the *Mishkan* for lay use. One opinion cited in the literature holds that precisely this is why *Chanukah* was established for eight days rather than seven: had it been seven, people would have fashioned small *chanukiot* that would amount to *menorot*, thereby violating this prohibition. That is a striking derivation.

Regarding the *menorah* constructed by *Machon HaMikdash* and displayed near the *Kotel*, the position of the institution is that the prohibition applies only when the object is intended for ordinary use — since they intend theirs for use in the future *Beit HaMikdash*, no prohibition applies.

### Metals Debate

The Gemara's focus in this passage is on the debate among the *Tannaim* about what materials are acceptable for the *menorah*. Rav Yosef maintains that the dispute is not between precious metals and all metals, but rather between all metals on one side and non-metallic materials on the other — and that no one requires specifically precious metals. The *braita* cited supports this framing: the debate is about whether wood and similar materials would suffice.

### Historical Evidence

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda argues that even wood is a valid material for the *menorah*, and cites historical evidence: *kederekh she'asu malkhei beit Hashmonai* — as the Hasmonean kings did when, upon rededicating the *Beit HaMikdash* without the resources for a proper *menorah*, they fashioned one from wood. Since a wooden *menorah* would therefore be valid, one may not make a wooden seven-branched candelabra even at home.

The Sages respond: *misham ra'aya?* — can you really bring proof from there? Those were not wood at all, they say. *Shappudin shel barzel hayu* — they were iron rods, covered with tin, *bavatz*. When the Hasmoneans grew wealthier, they replaced the iron-and-tin *menorah* with one of silver, and eventually with one of gold.

What is striking about this exchange is that throughout the tractates of *Zevachim* and *Menachot* — pages of rigorous *halakhic* analysis — it is almost unheard of to see the *Tannaim* debating not a *pasuk* or a logical argument but a piece of collective historical memory. Yet here, both sides are appealing to what people remembered of the Hasmonean *menorah*. Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda remembered it as wood; the Sages maintained it was iron coated with tin. It is also worth noting that iron coated with tin would look more lustrous than bare iron — tin gives a more polished appearance. One could perhaps understand how a rusted iron object might be mistaken for wood, but how a tin-coated object would look like wood is less clear.

## Menorah Dimensions

### Height Debate

The Gemara now turns to the dimensions of the *menorah*. Shmuel, in the name of an elder (*mishmei desaba*) whose identity is not preserved, states: *gova shel menorah shemonah asar tefachim* — the height of the *menorah* was eighteen *tefachim*. Since a *tefach* is approximately three inches, eighteen *tefachim* comes to roughly 54 inches — about four and a half feet. This is considerably shorter than most people envision the *menorah*. The relief on Titus's Arch shows the *menorah* being carried, and a rough scaling against the human figures depicted suggests it was closer to the height of a person — perhaps somewhat taller. Yet the Gemara's figure, taken literally, gives a much more modest object.

### Structure Breakdown

The Gemara proceeds to give a *tefach*-by-*tefach* breakdown of the *menorah*'s structure from base to top. Beginning at the bottom: the base and the flower (*ha-raglayim ve-ha-parach*) occupy three *tefachim*. Then two *tefachim* of smooth, unornamented shaft. Then one *tefach* containing a cup (*gavia*), a sphere (*kaftor*), and a flower (*perach*). Then two more *tefachim* of smooth shaft, followed by a *tefach* that is a sphere — a *kaftor* — from which two arms branch outward (*shenei kanim yotzim mimenu, echad eilech ve-echad eilech*), reaching the full height of the *menorah*. The pattern continues: one *tefach* of smooth shaft, a sphere from which two more arms branch, one smooth *tefach*, and a third sphere from which the final two arms branch. At the top of the structure, two more *tefachim* of smooth shaft bring one to the topmost section, where three *tefachim* are reserved for the uppermost ornaments of each arm: *she-bahem gimmel gvi'im ve-kaftor va-perach* — three cups, a sphere, and a flower.

As for the appearance of these ornaments: the cups (*gvi'im*) are described as Alexandrian goblets (*kousot Alexandriyim*); the spheres (*kaftorim*) resemble Cretan apples — apparently a variety of olive-shaped or apple-shaped fruit, described in some sources as what might be called Ceracean apples; and the flowers (*prachim*) resemble the floral capitals of columns.

The *mishnah*'s language is notably terse, simply stating that the arms "go out" and "reach the height of the *menorah*" without specifying whether they arch. A number of *Rishonim* consequently assume they were angular — extending outward at an angle and then rising straight up — but this conflicts with the visual evidence from Titus's Arch, coins, and mosaics, all of which consistently depict arched arms in the form we typically envision.

### Contemporary Evidence

The pictures that survive from antiquity — Titus's Arch, coins, mosaics — are broadly consistent with one another in the appearance of the arms and the general shape of the *menorah*, though they diverge considerably in their depictions of the base. Some show a triangular base, some a pyramidal form, some a rectangular one. It may be that most artisans simply did not have access to a clear view of the base, or that the base was partially obscured, and so they reconstructed it from imagination. This divergence is itself historically interesting.

One might also note that virtually all the contemporary images appear to show ornamentation distributed along the full length of the arms, not merely concentrated at the junction points and the top, as the Talmudic description implies. While the *Torah* is explicit that there are *shelosha gvi'im* on each arm, the number of *kaftorim* and *prachim* on those arms is not stated with equal clarity — the *pasuk* says *kaftor u-farach*, and the *Rabbis* derive a minimum of two of each. The surviving artistic depictions may reflect a tradition that there were additional ornaments along the shaft, or they may simply reflect artistic elaboration.

### Cups & Flowers Count

The Gemara now enumerates the total number of cups, spheres, and flowers. There are twenty-two cups, eleven spheres, and nine flowers. The Gemara derives each figure from *pesukim*.

For the cups: the *pasuk* states *u-va-menorah arba'ah gvi'im meshukadim, kaftoreiha u-feracheiha* — on the *menorah* (meaning the central shaft) there are four cups. These four consist of three at the top and one in the lower section. Each of the six side arms has three cups, for *shisha* times three equals eighteen. Adding the four of the central shaft gives twenty-two.

For the spheres: the central shaft has *kaftoreiha* — the plural indicating two — one at the lower-middle juncture and one at the top. Each of the six arms has one *kaftor*, giving six more, for a total of eight. Adding the three junction-point spheres (*kaftor, kaftor, kaftor*) gives eleven.

For the flowers: the central shaft has *feracheiha* — two — and the six arms contribute one each, for eight. The ninth flower is derived from the *pasuk*, *ad yerechah ad pirchah mikshah hi* — meaning that from its very base to its very flower it is one beaten piece — which implies there is a flower at the very base, yielding the ninth.

All of these components are *me'akvei zeh et zeh* — each holds the others back. If any of the twenty-two cups is missing, the *menorah* is not valid for the *Mikdash* when made of gold. The same applies to the spheres and flowers taken individually, and furthermore all three categories are interdependent: the full complement of all three is required. This holds, however, only for a gold *menorah*; for a *menorah* of other materials, these ornamental requirements do not apply.

The Gemara then turns to a dispute about the height. Rav says *govhah shel menorah tisha tefachim* — the height was only nine *tefachim*. Rav Shimi bar Chiya challenges this: does the *mishnah* not teach that there was a stone before the *menorah* with three steps, upon which the *kohen* would stand to tend the lamps? If the *menorah* were only nine *tefachim*, why would anyone need to ascend a pedestal to reach it? Rav responds somewhat sharply — *Shimi at?* — saying that his nine *tefachim* was measured only from where the branches begin, not from the ground.

### Pedestal & Steps

The mention of the pedestal raises a question in its own right. Even at eighteen *tefachim* — four and a half feet — it is not immediately obvious why a *kohen* would need to stand on a raised platform to tend the lamps. The top of the *menorah* at that height would be within easy reach of an adult standing at floor level. Several possible explanations arise: people in antiquity were, on average, meaningfully shorter than today, perhaps six inches to a foot shorter; and the *kohen* may have needed to be at a certain height not to reach the top, but to pour oil without spilling. The Gemara itself states that the base and flower are three *tefachim* — it is not entirely clear whether the stone pedestal is included within the eighteen *tefachim* or is additional.

The *tefach* measurement itself is independently attested by archaeological evidence. The *mikvaot* found at Masada, for example, allow one to calculate the size of a *se'ah* and thereby derive the size of a *tefach*, consistently yielding approximately 3.2 inches. This figure is independent of the height of the people who built them.

## Gold Purity

### Solomon's Menorot

The Gemara now cites a *pasuk* from the account of *Shlomo*'s construction of the *Beit HaMikdash*: *ve-ha-perach ve-ha-nerot ve-ha-melkachayim zahav, michalot zahav* — the flower, lamps, and tongs were of gold, *michalot zahav*. What does the phrase *michalot zahav* mean? Rav Ami explains: *she-kilato le-chol zahav sagur shel Shlomo* — making the *menorah* used up all of the *zahav sagur*, the purest and most precious form of gold, belonging to Shlomo. Rashi explains that *zahav sagur* is a gold so precious that it would "close the markets" — its mere appearance would cause the gold market to shut down, as no other gold could compare.

But how could making one *menorah* — which requires only one *kikar* — exhaust all of Shlomo's store of *zahav sagur*? The answer lies in the process. The Gemara teaches that Shlomo made not one *menorah* but ten, and for each one *ve-le-chol achat ve-achat heivi elef kikar zahav* — he began with one thousand *kikar* of gold, *ve-hichnisuha elef pe'amim la-kur* — and smelted it one thousand times in the furnace to remove all impurities — *ve-he'imiduha al kikar* — until only one *kikar* remained. Starting with ten thousand *kikar* total and reducing each to one *kikar* would exhaust a vast store of gold.

One must note the difficulty with this literally. Gold as an element does not comprise only one part in a thousand of raw ore — the ratio imagined here would represent an almost inconceivably low grade of material. The Gemara itself appears aware that this is hyperbolic or metaphorical, emphasizing through exaggeration just how utterly pure the gold of the *menorah* was. There is an additional technical puzzle: pure gold is extremely malleable, and it is not clear that an object of pure gold without any alloy would have the structural integrity needed to stand unsupported. Nonetheless, this is the picture the Gemara paints.

A further challenge arises: another *pasuk* states that all of Shlomo's drinking vessels and all the vessels of the House of the Forest of Lebanon were *zahav sagur*, and that silver was considered worthless in Shlomo's time — implying that *zahav sagur* was abundant. How then could the *menorot* have exhausted all of it? The Gemara resolves this: the statement that all the gold was used up refers specifically to the *zahav sagur*, not to all forms of gold. Shlomo had abundant ordinary gold; it was this singular, supremely pure grade that was entirely consumed in the making of the *menorot*.

### 80 Smeltings

The Gemara then questions whether the figures given — one thousand smeltings reducing a thousand *kikar* to one — are plausible at all. A *braita* is cited: Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda reports that once a *menorah* in the *Beit HaMikdash* was found to be slightly heavier than Moshe's *menorah* by the weight of one *dinar* — specifically, a Gordian *dinar* (*kordaikini*). Since the *menorah* must weigh exactly one *kikar*, no more and no less, something had to be done. The solution: *ve-hichnisuha peh pe'amim la-kur* — they smelted it eighty times — *ve-he'imiduha al kikar* — until it was reduced to exactly one *kikar*.

This raises an immediate practical question: the smelting presumably involved heat and refashioning the metal. But if the *menorah* had already been formed, why would one smelt it rather than simply remove a small amount directly? And why eighty smeltings to remove the weight of a single *dinar*? The Gemara's answer is that after the initial thousand-fold purification process, the gold was already nearly perfectly pure — *keivan de-kai kai* — there was almost no remaining impurity. At that stage, even tiny residual impurities required many passes through the furnace to extract. The eighty smeltings were thus not inefficiency but a consequence of starting from an already-purified state, in which even the smallest remaining dross is exceedingly difficult to remove.

### Meaning of Pure

All of this elaborate discussion of smelting and purity is, in the end, a *drasha* on a single word: *zahav tahor*, pure gold. The *menorah* is uniquely described in the *Mikdash* as *zahav tahor*, and the Gemara elaborates the full weight of that term.

The Gemara now shifts to consider the word *tahor* as applied not only to the gold but to the *menorah* as a whole. Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan asks: what does it mean when the *pasuk* in *Vayikra* refers to the *menorah ha-tehorah*, the pure *menorah*, in the context of Aharon lighting the lamps? Everything in the *Mikdash* is meant to be *tahor* — so what is this word singling out?

The Gemara first proposes: *she-yardu ma'aseha mi-makom tahara* — its form descended from a place of purity, meaning the vision of it came from heaven. The *menorah*'s design was divinely revealed. The Gemara then asks: but the *pasuk* also refers to the *shulchan ha-tahor*, the pure table — does the same apply there? Should we say that the design of the *shulchan* also descended from heaven?

The Gemara responds: by the *shulchan*, *tahor* means something quite different and quite practical — *miklal she-hu tamei*, it implies that the *shulchan* can become *tamei*. This is a matter of real *halakhic* significance. The *shulchan* was a wooden vessel plated with gold. Ordinarily, a wooden vessel made to remain permanently in place — *kli etz ha'asuy le-nachat* — does not contract *tumah*, because such a vessel is treated as a fixture rather than a chattel. The *shulchan* would seem to fall into that category, and one might therefore think it cannot become *tamei*. The *pasuk* therefore teaches that it can — because, as Resh Lakish explains, the *shulchan* was not actually left permanently in place: it was lifted and shown to the pilgrims on the *shalosh regalim*.

## Divine Image

### Heavenly Vision

The Gemara now turns to why *tahor* applied to the *menorah* is treated differently from *tahor* applied to the *shulchan*. By the *shulchan*, as established, *tahor* conveys a *halachic* ruling about *tumah*. By the *menorah*, however, the same rationale does not apply — the *menorah* is a metal vessel, and metal vessels contract *tumah* regardless of whether they are permanently positioned. One does not need a *pasuk* to tell us that the *menorah* can become *tamei*. Therefore, *tehorah* as applied to the *menorah* must be teaching something else entirely. The conclusion: *she-yardu ma'aseha mi-makom tahara* — its form came down from a place of purity, from heaven.

The Gemara then brings an extended discussion of what it means that things "came down from heaven." A *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches: *aron shel esh ve-shulchan shel esh u-menorah shel esh yardu min ha-shamayim* — an *aron* of fire, a *shulchan* of fire, and a *menorah* of fire descended from heaven, *ve-ra'ah Moshe ve-asah kemotam* — and Moshe saw and made like them. The word *tahor* applied to the *menorah* thus points to this fiery heavenly vision.

But the Gemara challenges this: surely the same applies to other Temple vessels and to the *Mishkan* structure itself? Another *pasuk* states *ve-ha-kemota et ha-Mishkan ke-mishpato asher horeita ba-har* — you shall erect the *Mishkan* according to its law, as you were shown on the mountain. Does this not suggest that Moshe was also shown a heavenly vision of the entire *Mishkan*?

The Gemara answers with a careful textual distinction: by the *Mishkan* the language is *ke-mishpato* — "according to its law" — indicating primarily verbal instruction, even if some visual element was present. By the *menorah*, the language is *kazeh* — "like this," pointing to a specific image. And similarly in the *pasuk u-re'eh ve-aseh be-tavnitam asher atah mor'eh ba-har* — look and make, according to the image which you are shown on the mountain. The language of seeing and of *tavnit*, image, makes clear that Moshe was shown not merely a verbal description but an actual visual form. The *menorah* in particular thus carries the quality of being *tehorah* in the deepest sense — its design is of heavenly origin.

Regarding the *shulchan* and the pilgrims: when the *shulchan* was displayed during the *regalim*, it was carried into the *azarah* and the people were shown the *lechem ha-panim* with the words *re'u chibatkhem lifnei ha-makom* — see how beloved you are before God. The miracle associated with this, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches, was that *siluko ke-siduro* — when the bread was removed, it was as warm and fresh as the day it was placed on the table. Because the *shulchan* was moved during the *regalim*, it was not a permanent fixture, it was not *asuy le-nachat*, and it therefore could contract *tumah* — and the *pasuk* saying *tahor* comes precisely to teach this.

### Gabriel & Three Hard Thi…

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *Gavriel*, the angel of fire, appeared *chagur ke-min pesikya* — wearing what appears to be a smelter's or craftsman's apron — and showed Moshe the construction of the *menorah*, *ve-her'ah lo le-Moshe ma'aseh menorah*. The proof-text is *ve-zeh ma'aseh ha-menorah* — "and this is the construction of the *menorah*" — where the word *zeh*, "this," implies a pointed, demonstrative act of showing: here, look at this.

A *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches that there were three things Moshe found difficult — *kashin lo* — that required God, as it were, to show him directly with His finger: the *menorah*, *rosh chodesh*, and *sheratzim* (the categories of impure insects). The *menorah*, as established, was shown through *ve-zeh ma'aseh ha-menorah*. *Rosh chodesh* was shown through *ha-chodesh ha-zeh lachem* — this month — where *zeh* points to the precise sliver of the new moon that constitutes the beginning of the month and must be recognized by witnesses. *Sheratzim* are identified through *ve-zeh lachem ha-tamei* — these are the impure creatures — pointing to the precise identification of each category. Some add a fourth: *shechita*, based on *ve-zeh asher ta'aseh al ha-mizbe'ach* — this is what you shall do on the altar — since the details of the act of slaughter are intricate and demanding enough that a direct visual demonstration was necessary.

The common thread in this list is worth noting: these are cases where verbal description alone was insufficient, where the complexity or subtlety of the matter required a direct, demonstrative showing. The word *zeh* in each case marks that moment of pointing. A related tradition in the *Gemara* mentions the *machatzit ha-shekel* as another thing Moshe found hard to grasp, but *Tosafot* distinguishes the two: by the *machatzit ha-shekel*, Moshe's difficulty was philosophical — what is the significance of half a shekel? — whereas in the cases listed here, the difficulty was purely practical — what does this actually look like?

There is a play on words underlying this entire discussion: the *menorah* was made *mikshah* — beaten from a single piece of gold — and Moshe was *mitkasheh*, found himself struggling (*kasheh*) to understand it. The difficulty of grasping the *menorah* was such that, according to some *midrashim* (not in this *Gemara* itself), Moshe ultimately gave up and God told him simply to throw the gold into the fire, whereupon the *menorah* emerged formed on its own. This *Gemara* does not go that far, but it does insist that the image itself had to descend from heaven, shown by Gavriel in the visual form of fire.

## Mezuzah Portions

### Kotzo Shel Yud

The *Gemara* now transitions to the laws of *mezuzah*, beginning with the principle that *shtei parshiyot she-ba-mezuzah me'akvot zu et zu* — the two portions of the *mezuzah*, *Shema* and *Ve-haya Im Shamoa*, prevent one another from being valid. Even one letter that is defective or missing invalidates the *mezuzah*. The *Gemara* initially treats this as self-evident — *peshita* — though, notably, in the case of the *Megillah* a much higher degree of incompleteness is tolerated. Here the *Gemara* takes it for granted.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav adds a striking specification: *lo nitzrecha ela le-kotzah shel Yud* — the ruling that even one defective element invalidates the *mezuzah* is needed particularly for the *kotzah shel Yud*, the tiny tapered point of the letter *Yud*. Rashi explains that this refers to the small left leg of the *Yud* that gives the letter its distinctive shape; without it, the letter might resemble a square and could be unrecognizable as a *Yud*. *Tosafot* disputes this, arguing that without a left leg the letter would indeed be unrecognizable and obviously invalid — and identifies the *kotzah shel Yud* instead as the tiny point on the upper right of the letter, which a reader would still intuitively read as a *Yud* even without it, yet which *halakha* requires for validity. The precise identification is debated, but the phrase *kotzah shel Yud* has become a lasting idiom in rabbinic discourse for the smallest, most easily overlooked detail — in the same spirit as Rabbi Akiva's *drasha* on the *kitrei otiyot*, the crowns atop letters.

The *Gemara* again asks *peshita* — is this not also obvious? — and the answer points to a deeper principle: *kol ot she-ein gevil mukaf lah me-arba ruchoteha pesulah* — any letter that does not have parchment surrounding it on all four sides is invalid.

### Parchment Around Letters

This principle, *mukaf gevil*, requires that every letter be fully surrounded by blank parchment — it may not be attached to or touching an adjacent letter on any side. Even a letter that is perfectly formed but rests against another letter without a clear break is invalid. The ruling about the *kotzah shel Yud* now becomes contextually richer: we are not simply saying that a missing stroke invalidates the letter. We are establishing that even the most apparently minor element of a letter's form — even a tiny point that any reader would still recognize as a *Yud* — carries the full weight of the *halakha*, and its absence disqualifies the *mezuzah*.

### He Letter Rules

The *Gemara* concludes with a ruling transmitted by Rav Zeira in the name of Rav Huna, and corroborated by Rav Yaakov in the name of Rav Yehuda: *nikev tocho shel He kasher, yerekho pasul* — if a hole forms in the interior of the letter *He*, it is valid; if the defect is in the leg of the *He*, it may be invalid.

The *He* has a distinctive feature: a small freestanding leg (*yerekh*) that hangs from the upper portion of the letter but does not reach the base. If a hole forms within the enclosed body of the letter, the *mukaf gevil* concern does not apply to interior space — the letter's surrounding parchment is unaffected by an interior hole. But if the hole is in the leg itself, two problems arise: first, the *mukaf gevil* question — the remnant of ink on the leg may not be fully surrounded by blank parchment — and second, and perhaps more fundamentally, the structural integrity of the letter itself is compromised.

The ruling is: *im nishtayer bo ke-shi'ur ot ketanah kasher* — if what remains of the leg is at least the size of a small letter (that is, the size of a *Yud*, the smallest letter), the *He* is still valid. *Ve-im lav, pasul* — if less remains, it is invalid.

A practical case is recorded: Iggra, the father-in-law of Rabbi Abba, found that the leg of a *He* in a *Sefer Torah* — or, according to Rashi, in *Tefillin* — had been compromised by a hole in the word *ha-hem*. The question was brought before Rabbi Abba, who applied exactly this standard: if enough of the leg remains to constitute the size of a small letter, the text is *kasher*; if not, it is *pasul*. The discussion of the precise standards for the *He* and other letters will continue in the following *shiur*.
