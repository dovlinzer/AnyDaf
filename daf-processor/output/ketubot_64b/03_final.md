# Ketubot 64b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Penalties & Reduction

### Marital Disputes Intro

The present *sugya* continues the discussion of marital dysfunction — specifically, the mechanisms by which we encourage compliance when a marriage is in trouble. The issues at stake include whether the husband can be compelled to go out and find work in order to meet his financial obligations (since his obligation is to pay the bills, but the question of whether he can be forced to find employment — *ve'ana eflach* — is debated), and, more significantly for *agunah* concerns, the degree to which we compel a husband to give a *get* when the wife declares the marriage intolerable. Related to that, *Tosafos* addresses the question of whether we may even "encourage" a husband to perform *chalitzah* when he refuses. The *daf* now turns to the financial penalties and why they differ between husband and wife.

### Penalty Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁתָּבַע עַד מָתַי הוּא פּוֹחֵת וּמוֹסִיף — בְּמַתְנִי׳, כִּתְנִיא: הַמּוֹרֶדֶת עַל בַּעֲלָהּ — פּוֹחֲתִין לָהּ מִכְּתוּבָּתָהּ שִׁבְעָה דִּינָרִים בְּשַׁבָּת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה טַרְפְּעִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A woman who **rebels against her husband** is fined; **we reduce her marriage contract** by **seven dinars** each **week.** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Seven *terapa'ikin*.** 

The *mishnah* established the following asymmetric penalty framework: when the wife is not fulfilling her responsibilities, we reduce her *kesuvah* by seven *dinarin* per week; when the husband is not fulfilling his responsibilities, we increase the *kesuvah* (for the wife's benefit) by three *dinarin*. A second opinion substitutes *tarpikin* for *dinarin* in both cases — seven *tarpikin* per week for her and three *tarpikin* per week for him.

### Currency Conversions

To understand the penalty's practical significance, one must first be oriented in the relevant currency equivalences. A *dinar* equals a *zuz*, and four *zuz* equals a *sela*, which is equivalent to a biblical *shekel*. The Torah's fifty-*shekel* bride price (*mohar*) accordingly equals two hundred *zuz* — which is precisely the basis of the standard *kesuvah* of two hundred *zuz*.

### Penalty Duration Math

If the *kesuvah* stands at two hundred *zuz* and is reduced by seven *zuz* per week, the base amount will be exhausted in approximately twenty-eight weeks. If the rate is instead three *zuz* per week — as is the case when the husband is being penalized — the base amount would last approximately sixty-seven weeks. These calculations apply only to the base *kesuvah* of two hundred *zuz*; the *tosefes* (additional sum) and *nidunya* (dowry) would extend the process considerably.

### Tarpikin Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַרְפְּעִיקִין? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אִסְתֵּירָא. וְכַמָּה אִסְתֵּירָא — פַּלְגָא דְזוּזָא. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה טַרְפְּעִיקִין, שֶׁהֵן תֵּשַׁע מֵעֵין, מָעָה וַחֲצִי לְכׇל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are *terapa'ikin*? Rav Sheshet said: An *asteira*,** a small coin. **And how much is an *asteira*? A half of a dinar. This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: Three *terapa'ikin*, which are nine *ma'as*, a *ma'a* and a half for each day.**

The *gemara* asks: *mai tarpikin* — what exactly is a *tarpikin*? Rav Sheishes answers: *asteira*, a half-*zuz*. A *baraita* corroborates this: Rabbi Yehuda says that three *tarpikin* — which is three half-*zuz*, or one and a half *zuz* — equal nine *ma'in*, which works out to a *ma'ah* and a half per day (excluding *Shabbos*). Accordingly, when the *mishnah* says seven *tarpikin* for her, that is three and a half *zuz* per week; when it says three *tarpikin* for him, that is one and a half *zuz* per week.

## Asymmetrical Penalties

### Mored/Moredet Gap

Setting aside the distinction between the *dinarin* and *tarpikin* opinions, the structural asymmetry is this: for every day the wife is not fulfilling her responsibility, the *kesuvah* is reduced by one unit (whether that unit is a *zuz* or a *tarpikin*) — and this applies to all seven days of the week, including *Shabbos*. For every day the husband is not fulfilling his responsibility, the *kesuvah* is increased by only a half unit, and *Shabbos* is excluded. There are thus two distinct questions: why is she penalized at double the rate of him, and why is *Shabbos* counted against her but not against him?

### Shabbat Fine Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף לִשְׁמוּאֵל: מַאי שְׁנָא אִיהוּ דְּיָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ דְּשַׁבָּת. וּמַאי שְׁנָא אִיהִי דְּלָא יָהֲבִינַן לַהּ דְּשַׁבָּת? אִיהִי דְּמִיפְחָת קָא פָחֵית, לָא מִיחֲזֵי כִּשְׂכַר שַׁבָּת, אִיהוּ דְּאוֹסוֹפֵי קָא מוֹסְפָא, מִיחֲזֵי כִּשְׂכַר שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef said to Shmuel: What is different** when she is the one rebelling against **him, that we give him** compensation **for Shabbat,** as her marriage contract is reduced by seven dinars a week, which is one dinar per day including Shabbat, and **what is different** for **her that we do not give her** compensation **for Shabbat** but rather only for six days? The Gemara explains: When it is **she who** is fined and her marriage contract **is reduced,** it **does not appear to be Shabbat wages,** money paid for services rendered on Shabbat, which is prohibited. Whereas when it is **he who** is fined and compelled to **add** additional money every day to her marriage contract, it does **appear to be Shabbat wages.**

Rabbi Chiyya bar Rav Yosef posed the question to Shmuel: *mai shna ihu de-yavnan leh de-Shabbas* — why does she get penalized even for *Shabbos*, while he does not? The *gemara* answers: when the *kesuvah* is being *reduced* because the wife is withholding, the benefit to the husband takes the form of a debt reduction — it does not *appear* as if he is earning wages on *Shabbos*. But when the *kesuvah* is being *increased* because the husband is withholding, if we were to include *Shabbos*, it would appear as though the wife is effectively earning money on *Shabbos*, which is impermissible. The reduction of a debt is categorically different from the payment of wages, and the law tracks that distinction.

### Prostitute Market Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף לִשְׁמוּאֵל: מָה בֵּין מוֹרֵד לְמוֹרֶדֶת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: צֵא וּלְמַד מִשּׁוּק שֶׁל זוֹנוֹת, מִי שׂוֹכֵר אֶת מִי? דָּבָר אַחֵר: זֶה יִצְרוֹ מִבַּחוּץ, וְזוֹ יִצְרָהּ מִבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** On the same issue, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef said to Shmuel: What is** the reason for the difference in *halakha* **between a rebellious** man **and a rebellious** woman? According to all opinions, a rebellious wife's fine is greater than that of a rebellious husband. **He said to him: Go and learn from the market of prostitutes. Who hires whose** services? Clearly, a man suffers more from lack of sexual intercourse, and therefore the penalty for a rebellious wife is greater. **Alternatively,** when **he** desires sexual relations, **his inclination** is noticeable **on the outside,** and therefore he feels shame as well as pain. **But** for **her, her inclination is on the inside,** and is not obvious.

The *gemara* then addresses why her penalty rate is double his: *tze le-mad mi-shuk shel zonot* — go and learn from the marketplace of prostitutes. There, the men hire the women — meaning the market for female prostitutes vastly exceeds the market for male prostitutes. This demonstrates that men are regarded as the more injured party when sexual intimacy is withheld.

### Rashi's Explanations

Rashi offers two explanations. The first is straightforward: the *gemara* assumes that the *mored/moredes* framework is primarily about withholding marital intimacy (*tashmish*), and men are seen as having a significantly greater sexual drive and therefore suffering greater harm from its denial. The second, *davar acher*: *ze yitzro mi-bachutz ve-ze yitzro mi-bifnim* — one's anatomy is external and the other's internal. Rashi explains: *kishuyav nikar u-mitganeh* — a husband's physical arousal is visible and thus a source of shame if unsatisfied, making his injury more apparent. Both explanations converge on the conclusion that the husband is the more acutely injured party when intimacy is denied, and accordingly, when the *wife* is the one withholding, she is fined at double his rate.

### Penalty Asymmetry Summary

To summarize the imbalances: the wife is penalized at twice the daily rate as the husband; she is penalized on *Shabbos* while he is not (since her withholding generates a debt reduction, while his generates something that would resemble earnings on *Shabbos*); and, as noted from the previous discussion, the husband always retains the ability to initiate a *get*, which the wife does not. As for the practical timeline — depending on whether the unit is a *zuz* or a *tarpikin*, the process of running down the base two-hundred-*zuz kesuvah* spans roughly between a half year and a year, not counting *tosefes* and *nidunya*. The later *gemara* traditions, as was noted, prefer a much faster resolution leading directly to the *get* rather than a prolonged attrition of the *kesuvah*.

## Separate Maintenance

### Intermediary Provision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּשְׁרֶה אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִישׁ — לֹא יִפְחוֹת לָהּ מִשְּׁנֵי קַבִּין חִטִּין, אוֹ מֵאַרְבָּעָה קַבִּין שְׂעוֹרִין. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לֹא פָּסַק לָהּ שְׂעוֹרִין אֶלָּא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, שֶׁהָיָה סָמוּךְ לֶאֱדוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If someone **feeds his wife by means of a third party** serving as a trustee, while the husband himself is not living with her for some reason, he may **not** give **her less than two *kav*** of **wheat or four *kav*** of **barley** a week for her sustenance. **Rabbi Yosei said: Only Rabbi Yishmael, who was near Edom, allotted her barley.**

The *mishnah* now addresses a related situation: *hamashre ishto al yedei shaliach* — a husband who is providing for his wife through an intermediary rather than sharing a home with her. The reasons for this arrangement could be various — a business trip, an unresolved marital dispute, or other circumstances — but the halakhic question is the same: what are the minimum provisions he must supply?

### Grain Allowance

He may not give her less than two *kav* of wheat or, alternatively, four *kav* of barley per week. Rabbi Yosi notes that the option of barley was stipulated only by Rabbi Yishmael, who lived near Edom — as the *gemara* will clarify, the Edomite barley was of inferior quality, which is why double the measure of barley was required to match the nutritional value of the wheat. Elsewhere, the ratio would differ.

### Consumables List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ חֲצִי קַב קִיטְנִית, וַחֲצִי לוֹג שֶׁמֶן, וְקַב גְּרוֹגְרוֹת אוֹ מָנֶה דְּבֵילָה. וְאִם אֵין לוֹ — פּוֹסֵק לְעוּמָּתָן פֵּירוֹת מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And he** must **give her half a *kav*** of **legumes, and half a *log*** of **oil, and a *kav*** of **dried figs or** the weight of **a *maneh*** of **fig cakes. And if he does not have** these fruits, **he** must **apportion** for her **a corresponding** amount of **fruit from elsewhere.**

In addition to grain, the husband must provide: a half *kav* of legumes (*kitnit*); a half *log* of oil (approximately seven ounces); and a *kav* of dried figs (*grogrot*) or, alternatively, a *maneh* (a weight measurement) of pressed dates. If those specific fruits are unavailable, he substitutes an equivalent amount of other available fruit.

### Durable Goods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ מִטָּה, מַפָּץ וּמַחְצֶלֶת. וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ כִּפָּה לְרֹאשָׁהּ, וַחֲגוֹר לְמׇתְנֶיהָ, וּמִנְעָלִים מִמּוֹעֵד לְמוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **And he** must **give her a bed, a soft mat, and** a hard **mat. And he** must **give her a cap for her head, and a belt for her waist, and** new **shoes from Festival to Festival.**

Beyond consumables, the *mishnah* specifies a bed, a mat, and a cushion, along with a head covering, a belt for her waist, and shoes provided seasonally — new shoes each *regel* (festival).

### Seasonal Clothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵלִים שֶׁל חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז מִשָּׁנָה לְשָׁנָה. וְאֵין נוֹתְנִין לָהּ לֹא חֲדָשִׁים בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, וְלֹא שְׁחָקִים בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים. אֶלָּא נוֹתֵן לָהּ כֵּלִים שֶׁל חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְהִיא מִתְכַּסָּה בִּבְלָאוֹתֵיהֶן בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה. וְהַשְּׁחָקִים — שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he must purchase **garments** for her with a value **of fifty dinars from year to year.** The mishna comments: **And he may not give her new** clothes, which tend to be thick and warm, **in the summer, nor worn** garments **in the rainy season,** as these are too thin and she will be cold. **Rather, he should give her clothes** at a value **of fifty dinars in the rainy season, and she covers herself with** these same **worn** garments **in the summer** as well. **And** the leftover, **worn** clothes belong **to her.**

Clothing is allocated at fifty *zuz* worth per year. Importantly, the *mishnah* specifies that one does not give her *new* clothing in the summer — since new garments had not yet been worn thin and were therefore too warm — nor worn, thin clothing in the winter when warmth is needed. Rather, the new clothes of fifty *zuz* are purchased in winter; she wears them during the cold months, and as they become worn over the course of the year, she uses the now-thinned garments in the summer. The worn-out garments at year's end remain hers to keep.

### Pin Money & Meals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹתֵן לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לְצוֹרְכָּהּ. וְאוֹכֶלֶת עִמּוֹ מִלֵּילֵי שַׁבָּת לְלֵילֵי שַׁבָּת. וְאִם אֵין נוֹתֵן לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לְצוֹרְכָּהּ — מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In addition to the above, **he** must **give her** another **silver *ma'a*** coin **for** the rest of **her needs. And she eats with him from Shabbat evening to Shabbat evening.** Although he may provide for her sustenance via a third party throughout the week, on Shabbat evening she has the right to eat together with him. **And if he does not give her a silver *ma'a*** coin **for her needs, her earnings** belong **to her.**

Each week she also receives a *ma'ah kesef* — a small coin of silver — as pin money for miscellaneous personal needs. Additionally, even when living apart, the husband must arrange for her to eat the Friday night *Shabbos* meal together with him. This is striking: just as the *gemara* mandates conjugal relations at fixed intervals, it similarly mandates a minimum of shared table time — specifically the Friday night meal. The underlying logic appears to be that *parnasah* encompasses not merely financial provision but a certain degree of continued personal relationship.

### Work Income Clause

If the husband fails to provide the *ma'ah kesef*, she retains the income from her work (*ma'aseh yadayim*). The *mishnah* thus links the pin money to her earnings: if he does not give her even this small personal allowance, she keeps what she produces.

## Woman's Work

### Work Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה הִיא עוֹשָׂה לוֹ? מִשְׁקַל חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שְׁתִי בִּיהוּדָה, שֶׁהֵן עֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים בַּגָּלִיל. אוֹ מִשְׁקַל עֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים עֵרֶב בִּיהוּדָה, שֶׁהֵן עֶשְׂרִים סְלָעִים בַּגָּלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** is the fixed amount **that she** must **earn for him?** She must spin wool in the **weight** of **five *sela*** of threads of the **warp in Judea, which are** equivalent to **ten *sela*** according **to** the measurements of the **Galilee, or the weight** of **ten *sela*** of the threads of the **woof,** which are easier to prepare, **in Judea, which are** equivalent to **twenty *sela*** according **to** the measurements used in the **Galilee.**

The *mishnah* now asks: *u-mah hi osah lo* — what work does she perform for him? The term *ma'aseh yadayim* is potentially ambiguous: does it refer to domestic duties performed within the home, or to the productive labor by which she generates income from outside the house? The *mishnah* here focuses on the latter — a fixed, measurable quota of weaving that she must produce each week. The distinction between basic domestic responsibilities and income-generating labor was never fully resolved in the earlier *gemara* discussion, though the *peshat* of the *mishnah* appears to treat the weaving quota as the measurable *ma'aseh yadayim* that is offset by the *ma'ah kesef*.

### Weaving Quotas

The quota is: five *sela'im* of warp (*sheti*) per week in Judea, which equals ten *sela'im* by Galilean weight — since Galilean weights were half those of Judea — or alternatively, ten *sela'im* of woof (*erev*) in Judea, equaling twenty *sela'im* in Galilee. The woof is the easier of the two to prepare, which accounts for the higher numeric quota assigned to it.

## Separate Maintenance (II)

### Nursing Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיְתָה מְנִיקָה — פּוֹחֲתִין לָהּ מִמַּעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וּמוֹסִיפִין לָהּ עַל מְזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if she is nursing** at the time, the required amount **is reduced from her earnings and is added** to the sum she receives **for her sustenance.**

If the wife is nursing, her weaving obligation is reduced — both because she is expending energy in another demanding activity, and more significantly because excessive physical labor might compromise her ability to nurse effectively. In addition, her food allowance is increased because she is eating for two.

### Status Qualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּעָנִי שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל בִּמְכוּבָּד הַכֹּל לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement,** i.e., all these amounts and measurements, **said? With** regard to **the poorest of Jews,** i.e., these are the minimum requirements. **However, in the case of** a financially **prominent man, all** the amounts are increased **according to his prominence.**

The *mishnah* concludes: *b'mah devarim amurim — b'ani she'b'Yisrael* — all of the above minimums apply to the poorest standard of Israelite living. However, *aval b'mechubad — hakol l'fi k'vodo*: for a person of higher status, everything scales according to his standing.

## Two-Meals Standard

### Eruv Techumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין, לָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא, וְלָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דִּתְנַן: וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּרוֹ — מְזוֹן שְׁתֵּי סְעוּדוֹת לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. מְזוֹנוֹ לַחוֹל וְלֹא לַשַּׁבָּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לַשַּׁבָּת וְלֹא לַחוֹל. וְזֶה וָזֶה מִתְכַּוְּונִין לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?** It is **not Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka and** it is **not Rabbi Shimon, as we learned** in a mishna (*Eiruvin* 82b): **What is the measure** for a joining of Shabbat boundaries [*eiruv*]? It consists of a quantity of **food** sufficient for **two meals for each and every one** of those included in the *eiruv*. The *tanna'im* disagree with regard to the definition of these two meals: It is referring to **one's food** that he eats **on a weekday and not on Shabbat;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is referring to the amount he eats **on Shabbat and not on a weekday. And** both **this** Sage, Rabbi Meir, **and that** Sage, Rabbi Yehuda, **intend to be lenient.**

Having established a weekly grain allocation of two *kav* of wheat, the *gemara* asks whose opinion this *mishnah* reflects — and finds that it corresponds neither to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka nor to Rabbi Shimon. The *gemara* first establishes the basis for the calculation by invoking the law of *eruv techumin*: the standard food measure deposited for an *eruv techumin* must be *mazon shtei seudot l'chol echad v'echad* — two meals' worth per person.

### Bread Measure Dispute

A dispute governs how much bread constitutes "two meals": Rabbi Meir says the standard is the amount of bread consumed at a weekday meal (not a *Shabbos* meal), while Rabbi Yehuda says it is the amount consumed at a *Shabbos* meal. The *gemara* notes that each opinion is attempting to be lenient — Rabbi Meir because *Shabbos* meals involve more varied dishes and therefore less bread per meal, and Rabbi Yehuda because weekday meals involve a larger bread intake. Thus each opinion arrives at a smaller figure by its own reasoning.

### RYbB Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא אוֹמֵר: כִּכָּר הַלָּקוּחַ בְּפוּנְדְּיוֹן, מֵאַרְבַּע סְאִין לְסֶלַע. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּי יָדוֹת לַכִּכָּר, מִשָּׁלֹשׁ כִּכָּרוֹת לַקַּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says:** Food for two meals is the size of **a loaf bought with a *pundeyon*,** which is one forty-eighth of a *sela*, **when four *se'a*** of wheat are sold **for a *sela*.** According to this calculation, a *pundeyon* can purchase one-twelfth of a *se'a* of wheat, which is equivalent to half of a *kav*, as there are six *kav* in a *se'a*. Therefore, according to Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, one quarter of a *kav* is sufficient for a single meal. **Rabbi Shimon says:** Food for two meals is **two** of three **parts of a loaf, when three loaves** are prepared **from a *kav*** of wheat. According to Rabbi Shimon, therefore, one-ninth of a *kav* of wheat is sufficient for a meal.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka says: the standard is a loaf purchased for a *pundion* — a small coin — when the going rate of wheat is four *se'ah* per *selah*. Since a *selah* equals 48 *pundion*, and a *se'ah* equals six *kav*, four *se'ah* is twenty-four *kav*, and 24 *kav* for 48 *pundion* means one *kav* equals two *pundion*. Therefore, a loaf costing one *pundion* is made from a half-*kav* of wheat — and that loaf, he says, constitutes two meals. His measure is thus one half-*kav* of wheat per two meals.

### R. Shimon Measure

Rabbi Shimon states: two-thirds of a loaf, when three loaves are baked from one *kav*, constitutes two meals. Since three loaves per *kav* means each loaf is one-third of a *kav*, two-thirds of a loaf is two-thirds of one-third of a *kav* — that is, two-ninths of a *kav* for two meals. This is considerably less than half a *kav*, making Rabbi Shimon's standard significantly more lenient.

A supplementary *gemara* notes that half of the two-meal loaf — a *pras* — is the amount used to define other legal measures.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶצְיָהּ — לְבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע. וַחֲצִי חֶצְיָהּ — לִפְסוֹל אֶת הַגְּוִיָּיה. וַחֲצִי חֲצִי חֶצְיָהּ — לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Having discussed the various opinions with regard to the size of a loaf of bread sufficient for a meal, the mishna states that **half** of this loaf is the amount called a half [*peras*], a measure relevant **for** the *halakhot* of **a leprous house.** If one enters a house afflicted with leprosy and remains there long enough to eat this amount of food, the clothes he is wearing become ritually impure. **And half of its half,** one quarter of a loaf this size, is the amount of ritually impure food that **renders the body unfit.** In other words, impure food of this amount imparts ritual impurity to the body of the eater and disqualifies him by rabbinic law from eating *teruma*. **And half of one half of its half,** one-eighth of this loaf, is the minimum measure of food that is **susceptible to ritual impurity as food.**

*Kedei achilat pras* (the time it takes to eat half a loaf, i.e., one meal's worth of bread) is the standard duration used in many other halakhic contexts, such as the time one must remain inside a *metzora*-afflicted house to contract *tum'ah*. Half of a meal's worth renders food susceptible to *tum'ah* of food, and so on, though the *gemara* does not pursue those applications here.

### Meals Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא, תַּמְנֵי הָוְיָין! וְאִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, תַּמְנֵי סְרֵי הָוְיָין!
>
> **Translation:** After the citing the mishna, the Gemara returns to its question: **Who is** the author of **the mishna** here, which says a husband must provide two *kav* of wheat per week for his wife's sustenance? **If** it is **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka,** who maintains that one quarter of a *kav* is sufficient for a single meal, there **are** only **eight** meals in two *kav*, and the wife requires at least fourteen meals for a week, as it was customary to eat two meals each day. **And if** it is **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that one-ninth of a *kav* is sufficient for a meal, two *kav* **are** enough for **eighteen** meals, and therefore the mishna requires more than she actually needs.

The *mishnah* allocates two *kav* of wheat per week. The problem is now evident: according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, a half-*kav* equals two meals, so two *kav* yields only eight meals — far fewer than the fourteen meals (two per day over seven days) required for a week. According to Rabbi Shimon, two-ninths of a *kav* equals two meals, meaning one *kav* yields nine meals and two *kav* yields eighteen — more than the fourteen needed. Neither tanna's measure maps cleanly onto the *mishnah*'s two-*kav* allocation.

### Baker's Profit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא, וְכִדְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: צֵא מֵהֶן שְׁלִישׁ לַחֶנְווֹנִי. הָכָא נָמֵי: אַיְיתִי תִּילְתָּא שְׁדִי עֲלַיְיהוּ. אַכַּתִּי תַּרְתֵּי סְרֵי הָוְיָין! אוֹכֶלֶת עִמּוֹ לֵילֵי שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, and** this is **as Rav Ḥisda said** in explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka's opinion: **Deduct one-third for the grocer's** markup, as he takes one-third as profit. This adds one half to the total cost. **Here, too, bring one-third** and **add** it **to** the total amount of meals that can be provided by two *kav* of wheat. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Still,** after adjusting the calculation by adding an additional half, a measurement known by the term: Outside third, to the amount of meals that can be eaten from two *kav* of wheat, **they are** equal to **twelve meals.** This is still not sufficient, as the wife requires fourteen. The Gemara answers: **She eats with him on Shabbat evening.** Consequently, this meal is not included in the amount that must be provided through the third party.

The *gemara* resolves the problem on the side of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka by invoking a principle of Rav Chisda: *tze me-hen shelish le-chanvani* — deduct a third as the storekeeper's profit. Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka was not stating how much wheat actually goes into the loaf; he was stating the market price. When you pay a *pundion* for a loaf, the baker is not putting the entire value of that *pundion* into raw wheat — a third of his revenue is profit. Therefore, the actual wheat content per loaf is higher than the simple price calculation suggests. Once one adds back the baker's markup, the two *kav* of wheat actually yield twelve meals, not eight.

The *gemara* then entertains a second version in which the markup is one-half rather than one-third, which would yield sixteen meals from two *kav*. The dispute between the two figures — one-third versus one-half — depends on local conditions: specifically, whether the supplier of grain also provides firewood at no extra cost. Where firewood is included, the baker's overhead is lower and his profit margin higher, yielding the half-markup figure; where firewood must be purchased separately, his overhead is higher and his profit is the smaller third.

### Shabbat & Guests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֲכִילָה מַמָּשׁ. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֲכִילָה תַּשְׁמִישׁ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? וְעוֹד, תְּלֵיסַר הָוְיָין! אֶלָּא כִּדְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, צֵא מֵהֶן מֶחֱצָה לַחֶנְווֹנִי. הָכִי נָמֵי אַיְתִי פַּלְגָא וּשְׁדִי עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that when the mishna is referring to eating, it means **literal eating. However, according to the one who says** that this **eating** on Shabbat evening is a euphemism, and it is actually referring to **conjugal relations, what can be said? And furthermore,** even if the meal on Shabbat evening is omitted, **they are** still **thirteen** meals that she requires but she has enough for only twelve. **Rather,** this is **as Rav Ḥisda said,** with regard to Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Broka's opinion: **Deduct one-half for the grocer's** markup. **So too** here, **bring a half and add** it **to** the total amount, which means she has enough for sixteen meals, not eight.

The *gemara* then reconciles the various meal counts with the *mishnah*'s two-*kav* allocation. Under the first version of Rav Chisda's principle (one-third markup), two *kav* yields twelve meals. Adding the Friday-night *Shabbos* meal that the *mishnah* already mandates — which the wife eats with her husband — brings the total to thirteen. The *gemara* notes that even this falls short of fourteen. A further solution: perhaps the *mishnah* in saying *ochla imo lel Shabbas* refers not to eating together but, as some suggest, to the euphemistic *tashmish* — in which case the Friday night meal still counts in her weekly bread ration. Still, thirteen does not equal fourteen.

Under the second version (half markup), two *kav* yields sixteen meals.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, שִׁיתַּסְרֵי הָוְיָין. כְּמַאן, כְּרַבִּי חִידְקָא, דְּאָמַר: אַרְבַּע סְעוּדוֹת חַיָּיב אָדָם לֶאֱכוֹל בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** After reconciling the apparent contradiction between the two statements of Rav Ḥisda, the Gemara returns to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka. **If so,** according to the above calculation, there **are sixteen** meals, which is more than a woman requires in a week. The Gemara suggests: In that case, **who is** the author of **the mishna?** Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥidka, who said** that **a person is obligated to eat four meals on Shabbat?** Since two meals are eaten on an ordinary weekday, this results in a total of sixteen meals a week.

If we follow Rabbi Chidka, who requires four *Shabbos* meals rather than three, a weekly total of sixteen is arrived at naturally (twelve weekday meals plus four on *Shabbos*). Alternatively, even according to the rabbis who require only three *Shabbos* meals, one arrives at fifteen, with one additional meal allotted for *orchei u-farchi* — guests who might drop by unexpectedly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, דַּל חֲדָא לְאָרְחֵי וּפָרְחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who maintain that one is obligated to eat only three meals on Shabbat, as you should **remove one meal for guests and wayfarers.** In other words, the husband cannot give his wife the absolute minimum amount she requires for herself and no more. He must give her enough to provide for the occasional visitor. Consequently, the total sum is somewhat more than was originally assumed.

Turning to Rabbi Shimon, under whose measure two *kav* yields eighteen meals: according to the rabbis (three *Shabbos* meals), that is fifteen meals needed, with three extra portions allocated for guests. According to Rabbi Chidka (four *Shabbos* meals), there are sixteen needed, leaving two extra for guests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לְרַבָּנַן — דַּל תְּלָת לְאָרְחֵי וּפָרְחֵי, לְרַבִּי חִידְקָא — דַּל תַּרְתֵּי לְאָרְחֵי וּפָרְחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Now that you have arrived at this** answer, **you** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that two *kav* is sufficient for eighteen meals. This can be explained either by saying that Rabbi Shimon agrees with the opinion **of the Rabbis,** that one eats three meals on Shabbat, if you **remove three** meals **for guests and wayfarers,** or that Rabbi Shimon agrees with the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥidka,** that four meals are eaten on Shabbat, in which case you must **remove two** meals **for guests and wayfarers.** In this manner, the mishna can be reconciled with all opinions.

One observation is worth noting: conspicuously absent from this entire weekly allocation is any protein — no meat, fish, eggs, or dairy — as well as any vegetables. The provision described truly reflects the *ani she'b'Yisrael* minimum: starch, a small amount of legumes, oil, and dried fruit.

## Wine Allocation

### No Wine Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ חֲצִי קַב קִיטְנִית, וַחֲצִי לוֹג שֶׁמֶן, וְקַב גְּרוֹגְרוֹת אוֹ מָנֶה דְּבֵילָה. וְאִם אֵין לוֹ — פּוֹסֵק לְעוּמָּתָן פֵּירוֹת מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר. וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ מִטָּה, מַפָּץ וּמַחְצֶלֶת. וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ כִּפָּה לְרֹאשָׁהּ, וַחֲגוֹר לְמׇתְנֶיהָ, וּמִנְעָלִים מִמּוֹעֵד לְמוֹעֵד, וְכֵלִים שֶׁל חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז מִשָּׁנָה לְשָׁנָה. וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לְצוֹרְכָּהּ. וְאוֹכֶלֶת עִמּוֹ מִלֵּילֵי שַׁבָּת לְלֵילֵי שַׁבָּת. וְאִם אֵין נוֹתֵן לָהּ מָעָה כֶּסֶף לְצוֹרְכָּהּ — מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ שֶׁלָּהּ. וּמָה הִיא עוֹשָׂה לוֹ? מִשְׁקַל חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שְׁתִי בִּיהוּדָה, שֶׁהֵן עֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים בַּגָּלִיל. אוֹ מִשְׁקַל עֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים עֵרֶב בִּיהוּדָה, שֶׁהֵן עֶשְׂרִים סְלָעִים בַּגָּלִיל. וְאִם הָיְתָה מְנִיקָה — פּוֹחֲתִין לָהּ מִמַּעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וּמוֹסִיפִין לָהּ עַל מְזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּעָנִי שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל בִּמְכוּבָּד הַכֹּל לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna lists legumes and oil among the weekly provisions but conspicuously omits wine.

The *mishnah* lists legumes and oil among the weekly provisions but conspicuously omits wine. The *gemara* notes this omission and reads it as supporting Rabbi Elazar's ruling:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵין פּוֹסְקִין יֵינוֹת לָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** This supports the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar said: Wines are not allotted to a wife.**

The *gemara* notes this omission and reads it as supporting Rabbi Elazar's ruling: *ein poskin yayin le'isha* — *beis din* does not allocate wine for a woman who is being maintained separately from her husband. The term *poskin* here refers specifically to the court's directive: when *beis din* must determine provisions — whether because the husband is absent and has not made arrangements, or because his estate must provide for a widow or a woman awaiting *yibbum* — wine is not included in what they mandate.

### Chana Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִישׁ כְּפַר נְבִירְיָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אִישׁ כְּפַר נְפוֹר חַיִל: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין פּוֹסְקִין יֵינוֹת לָאִשָּׁה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַתָּקׇם חַנָּה אַחֲרֵי אׇכְלָה בְשִׁילֹה וְאַחֲרֵי שָׁתֹה״. "שָׁתָה״, וְלֹא "שָׁתָת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda of the village of Neviraya, and some say of the village of Nefor Ḥayil, interpreted** a verse: **From where** is it derived **that one does not allot wines for a woman? As it is stated: "So Hannah rose up after she had eaten in Shiloh and after he had drunk"** (I Samuel 1:9). It states: **"He had drunk," and not: She had drunk.** This teaches that although she ate, she did not drink wine.

Rabbi Yehuda ish Kfar Nevoria (some say ish Kfar Nafor Chayya) expounds: how do we know that *beis din* does not allocate wine for a woman? From the verse describing Chana in Shilo: *vatakom Chana acharei achlo v'acharei shato* — Chana arose after *he* ate and after *he* drank. The verse uses the masculine singular forms for both eating and drinking. The drasha: *shato v'lo shatata* — he drank, but she did not drink. The *gemara* immediately challenges this:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה "אָכְלָה״ וְלֹא ״אׇכְלוֹ״, הָכִי נָמֵי?! אֲנַן מִדְּשַׁנִּי קְרָא בְּדִבּוּרֵיהּ קָאָמְרִינַן. מִכְּדֵי בְּגַוַּהּ קָא עָסֵיק וְאָתֵי, מַאי טַעְמָא שַׁנִּי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ "שָׁתָה״ וְלֹא "שָׁתָת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, if that is so,** by the same reasoning, should the phrase **"she had eaten,"** which is in the feminine, **indeed** be interpreted to mean that only she ate, **and** that **he did not eat?** The Gemara answers: **We say** this interpretation **from** the fact **that the verse changed its language. Since** the verse **was already dealing with her, what is the reason** that it **changed** the terminology and did not state: And had drunk, in the feminine? One can **learn from this** that **"he had drunk"** means that he drank, **but she did not drink.**

The response: the verse changes the expected formulation (*achla v'shata* would have been expected) and it is precisely that deviation that the *midrash* is drawing upon. Since the verse's subject was primarily Chana, the shift to male forms for drinking signals something meaningful — that she did not drink.

### Accustomed Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רְגִילָה — נוֹתְנִין לָהּ! רְגִילָה שָׁאנֵי, דְּאָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר כָּהֲנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: רְגִילָה — נוֹתְנִין לָהּ כּוֹס אֶחָד. שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְגִילָה — נוֹתְנִין לָהּ שְׁנֵי כּוֹסוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If a woman is **accustomed** to wine, **she is given** wine. The Gemara explains: If the woman is **accustomed** to wine it **is different, as Rav Ḥinnana bar Kahana said** that **Shmuel said:** If a woman is **accustomed** to drinking wine, **she is given one cup,** and if she **is not accustomed** to wine, **she is given two cups.**

A *baraita* challenges this: *regila notnin lah* — if a woman is accustomed to drinking wine, we do allocate wine for her. The *gemara* resolves the apparent conflict: *regila shani* — one who is accustomed is different; she has developed a tolerance. Rav Chinana bar Kahana then cites Shmuel: if she is accustomed, one cup is given; if she is not accustomed, two cups are given. This formulation is perplexing — why give *more* wine to someone unaccustomed?

### Abaye's Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר?! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: רְגִילָה, בִּפְנֵי בַּעְלָהּ — שְׁנֵי כּוֹסוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַעְלָהּ — נוֹתְנִין לָהּ כּוֹס אֶחָד. אֵינָהּ רְגִילָה, בִּפְנֵי בַּעְלָהּ — אֶלָּא כּוֹס אֶחָד, שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בַעְלָהּ — אֵין נוֹתְנִין לָהּ כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Shmuel **saying?** His statement is the opposite of what one would logically expect. **Abaye said: This is what** he **is saying:** If **she is accustomed** to wine, then **in the presence of her husband** she is given **two cups,** and if she **is not in the presence of her husband she is given one cup.** If **she is not accustomed** to drinking wine, then **in the presence of her husband** she is given **only one cup,** and if she **is not in the presence of her husband she is not given** wine **at all.**

Abaye clarifies: what Shmuel's statement means is as follows. If she is normally accustomed to drinking two cups in her husband's presence, then when separated from him *beis din* allocates only one cup. If she normally drinks only one cup in her husband's presence, then when separated from him she receives nothing at all. The logic is that when the husband is absent, we are more cautious about wine consumption regardless of her accustomed level, and so we always reduce by at least one cup — and if she was already at the minimum of one, we reduce to nothing.

### Cooking Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: רְגִילָה, נוֹתְנִין לָהּ לְצִיקֵי קְדֵירָה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּכַלָּתוֹ שֶׁל נַקְדִּימוֹן בֶּן גּוּרְיוֹן שֶׁפָּסְקוּ לָהּ חֲכָמִים סָאתַיִם יַיִן לְצִיקֵי קְדֵרָה מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת. אָמְרָה לָהֶן: כָּךְ תִּפְסְקוּ לִבְנוֹתֵיכֶם. תָּנָא: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם הָיְתָה, וְלֹא עָנוּ אַחֲרֶיהָ אָמֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: If **she is accustomed** to wine, **she is given** wine, but not for drinking, rather **for** meat **pudding [*tzikei*],** made with wine, flour, and leftover meat **in a pot. As Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: An incident** occurred **involving the daughter-in-law of Nakdimon ben Guryon,** whose husband had died. **The Sages apportioned for her** from his estate **two *se'a*** of **wine for pudding, from one Shabbat eve to another. She said to them,** as a blessing out of gratitude: **So may you apportion for your** own **daughters** an amount as large as this. It was **taught: She was a widow waiting for her *yavam*, and** consequently, **the Sages did not answer amen after her** blessing, as they did not want their daughters to reach her unfortunate state.

The *gemara* adds an important exception: even where the general rule would deny her wine, *notnin lah l'tziki kdera* — wine used as a cooking ingredient is still permitted and allocated. Wine for cooking (*tziki kdera*) does not implicate the concerns that motivate the general restriction. This is supported by a story: Rabbi Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the daughter-in-law of Nakdimon ben Guryon was allocated by the rabbis two *se'ah* of wine per week — from *erev Shabbos* to *erev Shabbos* — for use in cooking. She responded: *kach tifsaku livnoteichem* — may you allocate the same for your daughters. It is then noted that she was a *shomeret yavam* at the time, and the rabbis refrained from answering *amen* to her wish, lest it become an ill omen — for her blessing would mean their daughters' husbands would die.

This narrative also illuminates the broader context: the allocation mechanism described in the *mishnah* governs not only cases where the husband is away, but also where he is deceased. A widow or *shomeret yavam* drawing support from the estate would be subject to exactly these provisions — making the *mishnah* of critical ongoing practical importance.

### Wine & Behavior

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: כּוֹס אֶחָד — יָפֶה לָאִשָּׁה. שְׁנַיִם — נִיוּוּל הוּא. שְׁלֹשָׁה — תּוֹבַעַת בַּפֶּה. אַרְבָּעָה — אֲפִילּוּ חֲמוֹר תּוֹבַעַת בַּשּׁוּק וְאֵינָהּ מַקְפֶּדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught** in a *baraita*: **One cup** of wine is **good for a woman; two** cups **is a disgrace,** as she will start to become drunk; after **three** cups, she will become lustful and **verbally request** sexual intercourse, which is unseemly; after **four** cups of wine, she will **even request** intercourse from **a donkey in the marketplace, as** at this stage she is so drunk that **she is not particular** about with whom she has relations.

A tannaitic teaching underlies the caution: one cup of wine is good for a woman; two is unseemly (*nivul*); three, and she will verbally proposition men for sex (*tova'at b'peh*); four, and she will proposition even a donkey in the marketplace and will be completely without inhibition. This is, as the *gemara* itself suggests, a statement composed from a male perspective, and likely reflects a world in which women had far lower habitual exposure to wine. Nonetheless, the *halakha* flows from this assessment.

### Husband Present

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין בַּעְלָהּ עִמָּהּ. אֲבָל בַּעְלָהּ עִמָּהּ, לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: They taught** that a woman should not drink much wine **only if her husband is not with her. However,** if **her husband is with her, we have no** problem **with it.** If she feels an urge for intercourse her husband is available.

Rava limits the entire discussion: *lo shanu ela she'ein ba'alah imah* — all of the above restrictions apply only when the husband is not present. *Aval ba'alah imah — leit lan bah* — when she is with her husband, we have no concern. The rationale is twofold: first, the husband's presence is presumed to provide a check on behavior; second, and more fundamentally, it is simply not *beis din*'s business what a couple does privately in their own home. The wine restrictions are a function of *beis din*'s authority to set the terms of court-supervised maintenance — not a general restriction on women drinking wine.

The *gemara* raises an apparent challenge: but Chana was with her husband Elkanah in Shilo — the very setting we used to prove that she did not drink — so why did the restriction apply there?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חַנָּה, דְּבַעְלָהּ עִמָּהּ הֲוַאי! אַכְסְנַאי שָׁאנֵי. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִנַּיִן לְאַכְסְנַאי שֶׁאָסוּר בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיַּשְׁכִּימוּ בַבֹּקֶר וַיִּשְׁתַּחֲווּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ וַיָּשׁוּבוּ וַיָּבֹאוּ אֶל בֵּיתָם הָרָמָתָה וַיֵּדַע אֶלְקָנָה אֶת חַנָּה אִשְׁתּוֹ וַיִּזְכְּרֶהָ ה׳״, הַשְׁתָּה — אִין, מֵעִיקָּרָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** the case of **Hannah was** one **in which her husband was with her,** and yet this episode is cited as a source for the *halakha* that a woman should not drink wine. The Gemara answers: The case of **a guest is different, as Rav Huna said: From where** is it derived **that a guest is prohibited from** engaging in **conjugal relations? As it is stated: "And they rose up in the morning early, and worshipped before the Lord, and returned, and came to their house to Ramah; and Elkanah knew Hannah his wife; and the Lord remembered her"** (I Samuel 1:19). This verse indicates that **now,** after they returned home, **yes,** they engaged in relations; **at the outset,** when they were still in Shiloh, **no,** they did not. Therefore, Hannah did not drink wine in Shiloh.

The *gemara* answers: *achsanai shani* — guests are different. Rav Huna teaches that guests in someone else's home are forbidden from engaging in marital relations, derived from the fact that Elkanah and Chana only had marital relations after returning home to Ramah (*vayeida Elkanah et Chana ishto* — after their return, not while they were guests). The same elevated standard of propriety that restricts intimacy applies to wine consumption as well when one is a guest.

## Narrative Examples

### Choma's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּמָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּאַבָּיֵי אֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: פְּסוֹק לִי מְזוֹנֵי! פְּסַק לַהּ. פְּסוֹק לִי חַמְרָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יָדַעְנָא בֵּיהּ בְּנַחְמָנִי דְּלָא הֲוָה שָׁתֵי חַמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Abaye's wife, Ḥoma, came before Rava** after Abaye died, as Rava was the local judge. **She said to him: Apportion sustenance for me,** as I am entitled to be sustained by Abaye's heirs. Rava **apportioned** sustenance **for her.** She subsequently said to him: **Apportion wine for me** as well. Rava **said to her: I know that Naḥmani,** i.e., Abaye, **did not drink wine.** Since you were not accustomed to drinking wine during your husband's lifetime, you are not entitled to it after his death.

The *gemara* now transitions to a series of vivid narratives that illustrate the wine-allocation principles in practice. Choma, the wife of Abaye — described as a woman of striking beauty — came before Rava to have her provisions allocated. Rava assigned her *mezonos*.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: חַיֵּי דְּמָר דַּהֲוָה מַשְׁקֵי לִי בְּשׁוּפְרָזֵי כִּי הַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **She said to him: By the Master's life,** this is not correct. In fact, **he would give** me wine **to drink in cups [*shufrazei*] as large as this.** She gestured with her hands to show how large the cups were.

She then asked him to allocate wine as well. Rava replied: *yada'ana beih b'Nachmani d'lo hava shati chamra* — I know Nachmani (a name by which Abaye was also known), and he did not drink wine; therefore I cannot establish her accustomed level as including wine. She responded: *chayei d'mar* — by your life, he would pour wine for her in a large elongated cup (*shufrizi*) — *this* big — and she stretched out her arms to demonstrate the size of the vessel.

### Choma's Arm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא מַחְוְיָא לֵיהּ אִיגַּלִּי דְּרָעַאּ, נְפַל נְהוֹרָא בְּבֵי דִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **While she was showing him** the size of the cups, **her arm became uncovered,** and she was so beautiful that it was as though **a light had shined in the courtroom.**

As she gestured to indicate the size of the cup, her sleeve fell back and her arm became exposed. Her skin was so luminously beautiful that a radiant light (*nehora*) filled the *beis din*.

### Rava Aroused

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָם רָבָא, עָל לְבֵיתֵיהּ תַּבְעַהּ לְבַת רַב חִסְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava arose, went home,** and **requested** intercourse **from** his wife, the **daughter of Rav Ḥisda.**

Rava was overcome — aroused and agitated. He rose abruptly, went home, and immediately initiated marital relations with his wife, the daughter of Rav Chisda.

### Wife's Reaction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ בַּת רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאן הֲוַי הָאִידָּנָא בְּבֵי דִּינָא? אֲמַר לַהּ: חוּמָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּאַבָּיֵי. נָפְקָא אַבָּתְרַהּ, מָחֲתָא לָהּ בְּקוּלְפֵי דְשִׁידָּא עַד דְּאַפְּקַהּ לַהּ מִכּוּלֵּי מָחוֹזָא. אָמְרָה לַהּ: קְטַלְתְּ לִיךְ תְּלָתָא, וְאָתֵת לְמִיקְטַל אַחֲרִינָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The **daughter of Rav Ḥisda said to him: Who was** just **now in the courtroom?** Noticing his unusual behavior, she suspected that there must have been a woman in the court. **He said to her: Ḥoma, Abaye's wife,** was there. Upon hearing this, Rava's wife **went after** Ḥoma and **struck her with the lock of a chest [*kulpei deshida*] until she drove her out of the entire** city of **Meḥoza, saying to her: You have** already **killed three** men, as Abaye was your third husband, **and** now **you come to kill another** one, my husband Rava? Since you showed him your beauty, he will want to marry you.

His wife asked what had happened in court that day. He told her: Choma, Abaye's wife, had come. His wife went out after Choma and struck her with a strap from a chest (*kulfi d'shidei*), chasing her out of the entire city of Mechuza. She said to Choma: you have already killed three husbands — *u-ba'ita lemiktal achrina* — and now you want to kill a fourth? The reference to three dead husbands raises the possibility, though the text leaves it unresolved, that Abaye himself was among the deceased, which would explain why Choma had come to *beis din* to claim *mezonos* from the estate.

### Rav Yosef Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נְחֶמְיָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: פְּסוֹק לִי מְזוֹנֵי! פְּסַק לַהּ. פְּסוֹק לִי חַמְרָא! פְּסַק לַהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: יָדַעְנָא בְּהוּ בִּבְנֵי מָחוֹזָא דְּשָׁתוּ חַמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the wife of Rav Yosef, son of Rava, came before Rav Neḥemya, son of Rav Yosef,** for judgment. **She said to him: Apportion sustenance for me.** Rav Neḥemya **apportioned** a certain amount of sustenance **for her. She said to him: Apportion wine for me** as well. **He apportioned** wine **for her. He said to her: I know that the residents of Meḥoza** are accustomed to **drinking wine,** and therefore you, too, are entitled to wine, in accordance with the local custom.

A second case follows immediately: the wife of Rav Yosef the son of Rava came before Rav Nechemya the son of Rav Yosef for *mezonos* allocation. She requested wine. Rav Nechemya allocated it, explaining: *yeda'na behu b'vnei Mechuza deshatau chamra* — I know the people of Mechuza, that they drink wine routinely. In a locale where wine is a normal part of daily meals — as common as it is in, say, France — there is nothing unusual or dangerous about allocating wine to the wife; it is simply part of her established accustomed standard of living.
