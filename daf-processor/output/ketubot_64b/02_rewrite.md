# Ketubot 64b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Penalties & Reduction

### Marital Disputes Intro

The present *sugya* continues the discussion of marital dysfunction — specifically, the mechanisms by which we encourage compliance when a marriage is in trouble. The issues at stake include whether the husband can be compelled to go out and find work in order to meet his financial obligations (since his obligation is to pay the bills, but the question of whether he can be forced to find employment — *ve'ana eflach* — is debated), and, more significantly for *agunah* concerns, the degree to which we compel a husband to give a *get* when the wife declares the marriage intolerable. Related to that, *Tosafos* addresses the question of whether we may even "encourage" a husband to perform *chalitzah* when he refuses. The *daf* now turns to the financial penalties and why they differ between husband and wife.

### Penalty Structure

The *mishnah* established the following asymmetric penalty framework: when the wife is not fulfilling her responsibilities, we reduce her *kesuvah* by seven *dinarin* per week; when the husband is not fulfilling his responsibilities, we increase the *kesuvah* (for the wife's benefit) by three *dinarin*. A second opinion substitutes *tarpikin* for *dinarin* in both cases — seven *tarpikin* per week for her and three *tarpikin* per week for him.

### Currency Conversions

To understand the penalty's practical significance, one must first be oriented in the relevant currency equivalences. A *dinar* equals a *zuz*, and four *zuz* equals a *sela*, which is equivalent to a biblical *shekel*. The Torah's fifty-*shekel* bride price (*mohar*) accordingly equals two hundred *zuz* — which is precisely the basis of the standard *kesuvah* of two hundred *zuz*.

### Penalty Duration Math

If the *kesuvah* stands at two hundred *zuz* and is reduced by seven *zuz* per week, the base amount will be exhausted in approximately twenty-eight weeks. If the rate is instead three *zuz* per week — as is the case when the husband is being penalized — the base amount would last approximately sixty-seven weeks. These calculations apply only to the base *kesuvah* of two hundred *zuz*; the *tosefes* (additional sum) and *nidunya* (dowry) would extend the process considerably.

### Tarpikin Definition

The *gemara* asks: *mai tarpikin* — what exactly is a *tarpikin*? Rav Sheishes answers: *asteira*, a half-*zuz*. A *baraita* corroborates this: Rabbi Yehuda says that three *tarpikin* — which is three half-*zuz*, or one and a half *zuz* — equal nine *ma'in*, which works out to a *ma'ah* and a half per day (excluding *Shabbos*). Accordingly, when the *mishnah* says seven *tarpikin* for her, that is three and a half *zuz* per week; when it says three *tarpikin* for him, that is one and a half *zuz* per week.

## Asymmetrical Penalties

### Mored/Moredet Gap

Setting aside the distinction between the *dinarin* and *tarpikin* opinions, the structural asymmetry is this: for every day the wife is not fulfilling her responsibility, the *kesuvah* is reduced by one unit (whether that unit is a *zuz* or a *tarpikin*) — and this applies to all seven days of the week, including *Shabbos*. For every day the husband is not fulfilling his responsibility, the *kesuvah* is increased by only a half unit, and *Shabbos* is excluded. There are thus two distinct questions: why is she penalized at double the rate of him, and why is *Shabbos* counted against her but not against him?

### Shabbat Fine Logic

Rabbi Chiyya bar Rav Yosef posed the question to Shmuel: *mai shna ihu de-yavnan leh de-Shabbas* — why does she get penalized even for *Shabbos*, while he does not? The *gemara* answers: when the *kesuvah* is being *reduced* because the wife is withholding, the benefit to the husband takes the form of a debt reduction — it does not *appear* as if he is earning wages on *Shabbos*. But when the *kesuvah* is being *increased* because the husband is withholding, if we were to include *Shabbos*, it would appear as though the wife is effectively earning money on *Shabbos*, which is impermissible. The reduction of a debt is categorically different from the payment of wages, and the law tracks that distinction.

### Prostitute Market Proof

The *gemara* then addresses why her penalty rate is double his: *tze le-mad mi-shuk shel zonot* — go and learn from the marketplace of prostitutes. There, the men hire the women — meaning the market for female prostitutes vastly exceeds the market for male prostitutes. This demonstrates that men are regarded as the more injured party when sexual intimacy is withheld.

### Rashi's Explanations

Rashi offers two explanations. The first is straightforward: the *gemara* assumes that the *mored/moredes* framework is primarily about withholding marital intimacy (*tashmish*), and men are seen as having a significantly greater sexual drive and therefore suffering greater harm from its denial. The second, *davar acher*: *ze yitzro mi-bachutz ve-ze yitzro mi-bifnim* — one's anatomy is external and the other's internal. Rashi explains: *kishuyav nikar u-mitganeh* — a husband's physical arousal is visible and thus a source of shame if unsatisfied, making his injury more apparent. Both explanations converge on the conclusion that the husband is the more acutely injured party when intimacy is denied, and accordingly, when the *wife* is the one withholding, she is fined at double his rate.

### Penalty Asymmetry Summary

To summarize the imbalances: the wife is penalized at twice the daily rate as the husband; she is penalized on *Shabbos* while he is not (since her withholding generates a debt reduction, while his generates something that would resemble earnings on *Shabbos*); and, as noted from the previous discussion, the husband always retains the ability to initiate a *get*, which the wife does not. As for the practical timeline — depending on whether the unit is a *zuz* or a *tarpikin*, the process of running down the base two-hundred-*zuz kesuvah* spans roughly between a half year and a year, not counting *tosefes* and *nidunya*. The later *gemara* traditions, as was noted, prefer a much faster resolution leading directly to the *get* rather than a prolonged attrition of the *kesuvah*.

## Separate Maintenance

### Intermediary Provision

The *mishnah* now addresses a related situation: *hamashre ishto al yedei shaliach* — a husband who is providing for his wife through an intermediary rather than sharing a home with her. The reasons for this arrangement could be various — a business trip, an unresolved marital dispute, or other circumstances — but the halakhic question is the same: what are the minimum provisions he must supply?

### Grain Allowance

He may not give her less than two *kav* of wheat or, alternatively, four *kav* of barley per week. Rabbi Yosi notes that the option of barley was stipulated only by Rabbi Yishmael, who lived near Edom — as the *gemara* will clarify, the Edomite barley was of inferior quality, which is why double the measure of barley was required to match the nutritional value of the wheat. Elsewhere, the ratio would differ.

### Consumables List

In addition to grain, the husband must provide: a half *kav* of legumes (*kitnit*); a half *log* of oil (approximately seven ounces); and a *kav* of dried figs (*grogrot*) or, alternatively, a *maneh* (a weight measurement) of pressed dates. If those specific fruits are unavailable, he substitutes an equivalent amount of other available fruit.

### Durable Goods

Beyond consumables, the *mishnah* specifies a bed, a mat, and a cushion, along with a head covering, a belt for her waist, and shoes provided seasonally — new shoes each *regel* (festival).

### Seasonal Clothing

Clothing is allocated at fifty *zuz* worth per year. Importantly, the *mishnah* specifies that one does not give her *new* clothing in the summer — since new garments had not yet been worn thin and were therefore too warm — nor worn, thin clothing in the winter when warmth is needed. Rather, the new clothes of fifty *zuz* are purchased in winter; she wears them during the cold months, and as they become worn over the course of the year, she uses the now-thinned garments in the summer. The worn-out garments at year's end remain hers to keep.

### Pin Money & Meals

Each week she also receives a *ma'ah kesef* — a small coin of silver — as pin money for miscellaneous personal needs. Additionally, even when living apart, the husband must arrange for her to eat the Friday night *Shabbos* meal together with him. This is striking: just as the *gemara* mandates conjugal relations at fixed intervals, it similarly mandates a minimum of shared table time — specifically the Friday night meal. The underlying logic appears to be that *parnasah* encompasses not merely financial provision but a certain degree of continued personal relationship.

### Work Income Clause

If the husband fails to provide the *ma'ah kesef*, she retains the income from her work (*ma'aseh yadayim*). The *mishnah* thus links the pin money to her earnings: if he does not give her even this small personal allowance, she keeps what she produces.

## Woman's Work

### Work Definition

The *mishnah* now asks: *u-mah hi osah lo* — what work does she perform for him? The term *ma'aseh yadayim* is potentially ambiguous: does it refer to domestic duties performed within the home, or to the productive labor by which she generates income from outside the house? The *mishnah* here focuses on the latter — a fixed, measurable quota of weaving that she must produce each week. The distinction between basic domestic responsibilities and income-generating labor was never fully resolved in the earlier *gemara* discussion, though the *peshat* of the *mishnah* appears to treat the weaving quota as the measurable *ma'aseh yadayim* that is offset by the *ma'ah kesef*.

### Weaving Quotas

The quota is: five *sela'im* of warp (*sheti*) per week in Judea, which equals ten *sela'im* by Galilean weight — since Galilean weights were half those of Judea — or alternatively, ten *sela'im* of woof (*erev*) in Judea, equaling twenty *sela'im* in Galilee. The woof is the easier of the two to prepare, which accounts for the higher numeric quota assigned to it.

## Separate Maintenance…

### Nursing Mother

If the wife is nursing, her weaving obligation is reduced — both because she is expending energy in another demanding activity, and more significantly because excessive physical labor might compromise her ability to nurse effectively. In addition, her food allowance is increased because she is eating for two.

### Status Qualification

The *mishnah* concludes: *b'mah devarim amurim — b'ani she'b'Yisrael* — all of the above minimums apply to the poorest standard of Israelite living. However, *aval b'mechubad — hakol l'fi k'vodo*: for a person of higher status, everything scales according to his standing.

## Two-Meals Standard

### Eruv Techumin

Having established a weekly grain allocation of two *kav* of wheat, the *gemara* asks whose opinion this *mishnah* reflects — and finds that it corresponds neither to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka nor to Rabbi Shimon. The *gemara* first establishes the basis for the calculation by invoking the law of *eruv techumin*: the standard food measure deposited for an *eruv techumin* must be *mazon shtei seudot l'chol echad v'echad* — two meals' worth per person.

### Bread Measure Dispute

A dispute governs how much bread constitutes "two meals": Rabbi Meir says the standard is the amount of bread consumed at a weekday meal (not a *Shabbos* meal), while Rabbi Yehuda says it is the amount consumed at a *Shabbos* meal. The *gemara* notes that each opinion is attempting to be lenient — Rabbi Meir because *Shabbos* meals involve more varied dishes and therefore less bread per meal, and Rabbi Yehuda because weekday meals involve a larger bread intake. Thus each opinion arrives at a smaller figure by its own reasoning.

### RYbB Measure

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka says: the standard is a loaf purchased for a *pundion* — a small coin — when the going rate of wheat is four *se'ah* per *selah*. Since a *selah* equals 48 *pundion*, and a *se'ah* equals six *kav*, four *se'ah* is twenty-four *kav*, and 24 *kav* for 48 *pundion* means one *kav* equals two *pundion*. Therefore, a loaf costing one *pundion* is made from a half-*kav* of wheat — and that loaf, he says, constitutes two meals. His measure is thus one half-*kav* of wheat per two meals.

### R. Shimon Measure

Rabbi Shimon states: two-thirds of a loaf, when three loaves are baked from one *kav*, constitutes two meals. Since three loaves per *kav* means each loaf is one-third of a *kav*, two-thirds of a loaf is two-thirds of one-third of a *kav* — that is, two-ninths of a *kav* for two meals. This is considerably less than half a *kav*, making Rabbi Shimon's standard significantly more lenient.

A supplementary *gemara* notes that half of the two-meal loaf — a *pras* — is the amount used to define other legal measures. *Kedei achilat pras* (the time it takes to eat half a loaf, i.e., one meal's worth of bread) is the standard duration used in many other halakhic contexts, such as the time one must remain inside a *metzora*-afflicted house to contract *tum'ah*. Half of a meal's worth renders food susceptible to *tum'ah* of food, and so on, though the *gemara* does not pursue those applications here.

### Meals Calculation

The *mishnah* allocates two *kav* of wheat per week. The problem is now evident: according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, a half-*kav* equals two meals, so two *kav* yields only eight meals — far fewer than the fourteen meals (two per day over seven days) required for a week. According to Rabbi Shimon, two-ninths of a *kav* equals two meals, meaning one *kav* yields nine meals and two *kav* yields eighteen — more than the fourteen needed. Neither tanna's measure maps cleanly onto the *mishnah*'s two-*kav* allocation.

### Baker's Profit

The *gemara* resolves the problem on the side of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka by invoking a principle of Rav Chisda: *tze me-hen shelish le-chanvani* — deduct a third as the storekeeper's profit. Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka was not stating how much wheat actually goes into the loaf; he was stating the market price. When you pay a *pundion* for a loaf, the baker is not putting the entire value of that *pundion* into raw wheat — a third of his revenue is profit. Therefore, the actual wheat content per loaf is higher than the simple price calculation suggests. Once one adds back the baker's markup, the two *kav* of wheat actually yield twelve meals, not eight.

The *gemara* then entertains a second version in which the markup is one-half rather than one-third, which would yield sixteen meals from two *kav*. The dispute between the two figures — one-third versus one-half — depends on local conditions: specifically, whether the supplier of grain also provides firewood at no extra cost. Where firewood is included, the baker's overhead is lower and his profit margin higher, yielding the half-markup figure; where firewood must be purchased separately, his overhead is higher and his profit is the smaller third.

### Shabbat & Guests

The *gemara* then reconciles the various meal counts with the *mishnah*'s two-*kav* allocation. Under the first version of Rav Chisda's principle (one-third markup), two *kav* yields twelve meals. Adding the Friday-night *Shabbos* meal that the *mishnah* already mandates — which the wife eats with her husband — brings the total to thirteen. The *gemara* notes that even this falls short of fourteen. A further solution: perhaps the *mishnah* in saying *ochla imo lel Shabbas* refers not to eating together but, as some suggest, to the euphemistic *tashmish* — in which case the Friday night meal still counts in her weekly bread ration. Still, thirteen does not equal fourteen.

Under the second version (half markup), two *kav* yields sixteen meals. If we follow Rabbi Chidka, who requires four *Shabbos* meals rather than three, a weekly total of sixteen is arrived at naturally (twelve weekday meals plus four on *Shabbos*). Alternatively, even according to the rabbis who require only three *Shabbos* meals, one arrives at fifteen, with one additional meal allotted for *orchei u-farchi* — guests who might drop by unexpectedly.

Turning to Rabbi Shimon, under whose measure two *kav* yields eighteen meals: according to the rabbis (three *Shabbos* meals), that is fifteen meals needed, with three extra portions allocated for guests. According to Rabbi Chidka (four *Shabbos* meals), there are sixteen needed, leaving two extra for guests.

One observation is worth noting: conspicuously absent from this entire weekly allocation is any protein — no meat, fish, eggs, or dairy — as well as any vegetables. The provision described truly reflects the *ani she'b'Yisrael* minimum: starch, a small amount of legumes, oil, and dried fruit.

## Wine Allocation

### No Wine Rule

The *mishnah* lists legumes and oil among the weekly provisions but conspicuously omits wine. The *gemara* notes this omission and reads it as supporting Rabbi Elazar's ruling: *ein poskin yayin le'isha* — *beis din* does not allocate wine for a woman who is being maintained separately from her husband. The term *poskin* here refers specifically to the court's directive: when *beis din* must determine provisions — whether because the husband is absent and has not made arrangements, or because his estate must provide for a widow or a woman awaiting *yibbum* — wine is not included in what they mandate.

### Chana Drasha

Rabbi Yehuda ish Kfar Nevoria (some say ish Kfar Nafor Chayya) expounds: how do we know that *beis din* does not allocate wine for a woman? From the verse describing Chana in Shilo: *vatakom Chana acharei achlo v'acharei shato* — Chana arose after *he* ate and after *he* drank. The verse uses the masculine singular forms for both eating and drinking. The drasha: *shato v'lo shatata* — he drank, but she did not drink. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: why not also read the eating verb as indicating *he* ate but *she* did not — *achlo v'lo achla*? The *gemara* responds: the verse changes the expected formulation (*achla v'shata* would have been expected) and it is precisely that deviation that the *midrash* is drawing upon. Since the verse's subject was primarily Chana, the shift to male forms for drinking signals something meaningful — that she did not drink.

### Accustomed Exception

A *baraita* challenges this: *regila notnin lah* — if a woman is accustomed to drinking wine, we do allocate wine for her. The *gemara* resolves the apparent conflict: *regila shani* — one who is accustomed is different; she has developed a tolerance. Rav Chinana bar Kahana then cites Shmuel: if she is accustomed, one cup is given; if she is not accustomed, two cups are given. This formulation is perplexing — why give *more* wine to someone unaccustomed?

### Abaye's Refinement

Abaye clarifies: what Shmuel's statement means is as follows. If she is normally accustomed to drinking two cups in her husband's presence, then when separated from him *beis din* allocates only one cup. If she normally drinks only one cup in her husband's presence, then when separated from him she receives nothing at all. The logic is that when the husband is absent, we are more cautious about wine consumption regardless of her accustomed level, and so we always reduce by at least one cup — and if she was already at the minimum of one, we reduce to nothing.

### Cooking Wine

The *gemara* adds an important exception: even where the general rule would deny her wine, *notnin lah l'tziki kdera* — wine used as a cooking ingredient is still permitted and allocated. Wine for cooking (*tziki kdera*) does not implicate the concerns that motivate the general restriction. This is supported by a story: Rabbi Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the daughter-in-law of Nakdimon ben Guryon was allocated by the rabbis two *se'ah* of wine per week — from *erev Shabbos* to *erev Shabbos* — for use in cooking. She responded: *kach tifsaku livnoteichem* — may you allocate the same for your daughters. It is then noted that she was a *shomeret yavam* at the time, and the rabbis refrained from answering *amen* to her wish, lest it become an ill omen — for her blessing would mean their daughters' husbands would die.

This narrative also illuminates the broader context: the allocation mechanism described in the *mishnah* governs not only cases where the husband is away, but also where he is deceased. A widow or *shomeret yavam* drawing support from the estate would be subject to exactly these provisions — making the *mishnah* of critical ongoing practical importance.

### Wine & Behavior

A tannaitic teaching underlies the caution: one cup of wine is good for a woman; two is unseemly (*nivul*); three, and she will verbally proposition men for sex (*tova'at b'peh*); four, and she will proposition even a donkey in the marketplace and will be completely without inhibition. This is, as the *gemara* itself suggests, a statement composed from a male perspective, and likely reflects a world in which women had far lower habitual exposure to wine. Nonetheless, the *halakha* flows from this assessment.

### Husband Present

Rava limits the entire discussion: *lo shanu ela she'ein ba'alah imah* — all of the above restrictions apply only when the husband is not present. *Aval ba'alah imah — leit lan bah* — when she is with her husband, we have no concern. The rationale is twofold: first, the husband's presence is presumed to provide a check on behavior; second, and more fundamentally, it is simply not *beis din*'s business what a couple does privately in their own home. The wine restrictions are a function of *beis din*'s authority to set the terms of court-supervised maintenance — not a general restriction on women drinking wine.

The *gemara* raises an apparent challenge: but Chana was with her husband Elkanah in Shilo — the very setting we used to prove that she did not drink — so why did the restriction apply there? The *gemara* answers: *achsanai shani* — guests are different. Rav Huna teaches that guests in someone else's home are forbidden from engaging in marital relations, derived from the fact that Elkanah and Chana only had marital relations after returning home to Ramah (*vayeida Elkanah et Chana ishto* — after their return, not while they were guests). The same elevated standard of propriety that restricts intimacy applies to wine consumption as well when one is a guest.

## Narrative Examples

### Choma's Case

The *gemara* now transitions to a series of vivid narratives that illustrate the wine-allocation principles in practice. Choma, the wife of Abaye — described as a woman of striking beauty — came before Rava to have her provisions allocated. Rava assigned her *mezonos*.

### Rava's Response

She then asked him to allocate wine as well. Rava replied: *yada'ana beih b'Nachmani d'lo hava shati chamra* — I know Nachmani (a name by which Abaye was also known), and he did not drink wine; therefore I cannot establish her accustomed level as including wine. She responded: *chayei d'mar* — by your life, he would pour wine for her in a large elongated cup (*shufrizi*) — *this* big — and she stretched out her arms to demonstrate the size of the vessel.

### Choma's Arm

As she gestured to indicate the size of the cup, her sleeve fell back and her arm became exposed. Her skin was so luminously beautiful that a radiant light (*nehora*) filled the *beis din*.

### Rava Aroused

Rava was overcome — aroused and agitated. He rose abruptly, went home, and immediately initiated marital relations with his wife, the daughter of Rav Chisda.

### Wife's Reaction

His wife asked what had happened in court that day. He told her: Choma, Abaye's wife, had come. His wife went out after Choma and struck her with a strap from a chest (*kulfi d'shidei*), chasing her out of the entire city of Mechuza. She said to Choma: you have already killed three husbands — *u-va'ita lemiktal achrina* — and now you want to kill a fourth? The reference to three dead husbands raises the possibility, though the text leaves it unresolved, that Abaye himself was among the deceased, which would explain why Choma had come to *beis din* to claim *mezonos* from the estate.

### Rav Yosef Case

A second case follows immediately: the wife of Rav Yosef the son of Rava came before Rav Nechemya the son of Rav Yosef for *mezonos* allocation. She requested wine. Rav Nechemya allocated it, explaining: *yeda'na behu b'vnei Mechuza deshatau chamra* — I know the people of Mechuza, that they drink wine routinely. In a locale where wine is a normal part of daily meals — as common as it is in, say, France — there is nothing unusual or dangerous about allocating wine to the wife; it is simply part of her established accustomed standard of living.
