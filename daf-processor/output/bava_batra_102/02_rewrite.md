# Bava Batra 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Finding Bodies

### Intro & 1-2 Bodies

The *gemara* on today's daf picks up from a *braita* at the bottom of 101b, which quotes a *mishna* from *Oholot*. The *mishna* opens with the case of *hamotzei meit mushkav kedarko* — one who finds in a field a body that has been laid down in the manner consistent with intentional burial, as opposed to a body that has fallen in battle or been murdered and placed in some irregular position. The distinction is significant: the proper positioning of the body indicates deliberate burial rather than accidental or hasty disposal.

The *mishna* then addresses what one may do with such a body. If only one or two bodies are found, the ruling is *notlo ve'et tefusato* — one removes the body together with the soil directly beneath it, the *tefusah*, into which bodily fluids may have seeped and which is buried together with the body. This right of relocation exists because the landowner has an interest in removing the source of *tumah* from his field — a practical concern given that *tumah* restricts *kohanim* from the area and complicates agricultural activities. It bears mention that this removal of the surrounding soil applies only when the body was buried; if the deceased fell in battle and was not interred, the halakha does not require taking the soil beneath, a distinction that the *gemara* leaves somewhat unexplained. The rule for two bodies is the same: *notlan ve'et tefusan*, one removes them together with their respective soil.

The primary concern driving this entire discussion is *tumah*. It should be clarified that walking on top of ground beneath which bodies lie buried deep below does not necessarily carry a prohibition for the landowner; there is no *issur* in using the land above such caves. The concern is rather one of practical consequence — the *tumah* that permeates the area.

### 3 Bodies = Graveyard

The *mishna* then addresses the case of *matza sheloshah* — finding three bodies. One might argue that even three bodies in a field could reflect a tragic incident, such as a caravan attacked by bandits whose members were hastily buried at the site before the group moved on. The *mishna* therefore specifies two conditions that together warrant an assumption of a graveyard: the bodies must be laid out in a manner consistent with proper burial, and they must be spaced at the intervals one would expect in a genuine burial site. When three bodies satisfy both conditions, one must assume the existence of an established graveyard beneath the field.

Once a graveyard is assumed, one may not relocate the bodies. The rationale has two dimensions. First, the bodies may already be in an established public burial ground, which the individual landowner has no right to disturb. Second, if there is a graveyard here, there may well be additional graves extending outward in multiple directions, and a broader investigation is required to determine the full extent of the *tumah*.

A question naturally arises about *kavod ha-met* — the honor owed to the dead — in a case where a buried *beit ha-kevarot* lies beneath what is functionally a wheat field. The distinction seems to lie in whether the nature of the space is visibly that of a graveyard. A field with a *beit ha-kevarot* buried below it does not present itself as a graveyard in any perceptible sense, and the obligations that flow from the sanctity of a cemetery may not attach in the same way to ground that simply happens to have graves beneath it.

### 4-8 Amot Spacing

The *mishna* specifies that if there are between four and eight *amot* encompassing the three bodies — *im yesh beinehen me'arba ve'ad shmoneh* — then *harei zu shechunat kvarot*, this constitutes a neighborhood or district of graves. Reading the language carefully, *yesh beinehen* might seem to indicate spacing between individual bodies, but it is being understood as the total distance that encompasses all three — the outer edges of the first and third bodies span between four and eight *amot*.

The language *shechunat kvarot* is notable: it designates not quite a full *beit ha-kevarot* but a recognized area of burial, which then triggers the investigation described next.

### 20 Amah Search

Once three bodies are found within the qualifying range, the *mishna* requires: *ubodek mimeno ulehalan esrim amah* — one must search outward from there in twenty *amot*, presumably in multiple directions, as will be discussed. The logic is that if a graveyard has been established, there may be additional graves extending beyond the three discovered.

If a body is found at the end of twenty *amot* — *matza echad besof esrim amah* — the search does not terminate there. Rather, *bodek mimeno ulehalan esrim amah*, one continues checking another twenty *amot* from that newly found body, and so on, until twenty *amot* are cleared with no additional findings.

The reason one cannot simply stop upon finding a body at the outer edge of the first twenty *amot* is grounded in a principle of *sheraglayim ladavar* — substantiation. Ordinarily, a single grave standing alone would be treated as an isolated burial and could be relocated. But once that grave is found in the context of an established graveyard, it must be presumed part of that graveyard. And since it is part of the graveyard, one cannot declare the graveyard to have ended at that point; perhaps it continues further. The *mishna* itself notes this: *she'ilu*, had this single body been discovered in isolation, *notlo ubitfusato*, it would have been relocated. But found after establishing that a graveyard exists in the area, it must be treated as part of that graveyard, and the search must continue.

## Cave Dimensions

### Rashbam: 4-8 Amot

The *gemara*'s treatment of this *mishna* is somewhat surprising: it reads the case through the lens of cave burial, not through the more common model of straight burial in the ground. This requires explanation, because the *mishna* does not mention caves, and historically, burial in caves with niches — *kokhim* — was largely a practice of the wealthy. Ordinary people were generally buried directly in the ground. Nevertheless, the *gemara* insists on interpreting this *mishna* through the framework of cave burials, and the discussion of four-to-eight *amot* flows from that interpretive choice.

Where does the four-to-eight range come from? According to the *Rashbam*, the minimum of four *amot* is achieved by assuming that the spacing between the individual niches is less than a full *amah* — perhaps half an *amah* or even less. With compressed spacing, three graves can fit within four *amot*.

### Tosafot Corner

*Tosafot* offers a more elegant explanation. The minimum of four *amot* can be understood if the three bodies are arranged around a corner of the cave. In the earlier *gemara*, a small margin was assumed at the corner — about half an *amah*. With three bodies, two along one wall and one turning the corner, the total linear distance measured along the outer edge can fall within four *amot*. This accounts for the minimum without requiring any unusual compression of spacing.

### R. Shimon: 6×8

The maximum of eight *amot* is explained by reference to Rabbi Shimon's dimensions for a burial cave: six *amot* by eight *amot*. According to Rabbi Shimon, a cave wall could be as long as eight *amot*. If a wall is eight *amot* long and accommodates four niches, a cave with only three occupied niches would have three bodies within eight *amot*. The fact that one niche is unoccupied is not extraordinary; the *mishna* is describing a scenario in which not every available space has been filled.

### Why Not Beyond 8

The upper limit of eight is therefore tied directly to Rabbi Shimon's understanding of cave dimensions: since no wall exceeds eight *amot* according to the standard, finding three bodies spread across more than eight *amot* would not warrant an assumption of graveyard — the spacing would be too wide to reflect a normal cave configuration. Although the *mishna* is setting a standard and not a maximum for the size of any individual's burial plot, the practical assumption is that beyond eight *amot*, the natural inference of a unified graveyard no longer applies. Either they are not all affiliated or they are simply not close enough to one another to compel that conclusion.

Thus: four *amot* as the minimum because any more compressed would suggest accidental or emergency placement rather than an organized graveyard; eight *amot* as the maximum because that corresponds to Rabbi Shimon's longest cave wall and the standard spacing that a legitimate burial arrangement would produce.

## 20 Amah Analysis

### Multi-Directional

The *gemara* now turns to the question of why the search must extend twenty *amot* and in what directions. Consider a typical cave complex under Rabbi Shimon's dimensions: a cave six *amot* wide and eight *amot* long, with niches along the walls. When three bodies are found, one does not know which wall they occupied or which part of the complex they represent. The three discovered might be along the longer wall, or they might be along one of the shorter walls, or they might represent bodies from different walls.

If the three bodies were on one of the longer walls, then on the opposite longer wall — eight *amot* away — there could be additional bodies. Beyond the far wall there may be another cave complex. In all, the total distance to check along the main axis could reach the length of the first cave plus the width of the courtyard plus the length of a second cave — a significant span. Similarly, one does not know if the three bodies found are at the near end or the far end of the cave, which means the search must also proceed in the opposite direction.

Because the exact configuration is unknown, the search must be conducted in multiple directions — not just along the axis of the wall where the bodies were found, but laterally as well — since the discovered bodies might have been on a transverse wall.

### Rashbam Diagonal

The *Rashbam* explains why the twenty-*amah* search protocol requires checking at an angle — *be-alakhsona* — in addition to a straight-line search. Since not only is the cave six *amot* wide, but each niche runs four *amot* deep into the wall, the searcher must account for bodies buried at varying depths. A straight-line search directly down the cave's axis might miss a body buried at a different depth. By checking at an angle, one covers more ground relative to depth, increasing the likelihood of intersecting a burial regardless of how far into the niche it lies. According to the *Rashbam*, one would make this diagonal check on both sides. The intuition is that if all burials are at the same depth but that depth is unknown, a diagonal pass will intersect the burial plane at some point regardless of its exact position.

### Configuration

The deeper problem is that the searcher cannot know the configuration of the cave complex. The direction of the search — and how far one needs to go — depends on which wall the discovered bodies were on and which direction the remaining cave extends. Since one does not know which of the bodies found is at the head or foot of the grave, and since the *mishna* does not require that this be determined, the search must account for all plausible configurations. This means going twenty *amot* in multiple directions even though a precise knowledge of the cave's orientation might reduce the required scope.

The *Rashbam*'s explanation of the twenty *amot* as deriving from one cave complex does not fully resolve why one would have to search in all directions equally. If the bodies are pointing in one direction, that should indicate which wall they are on and thus which direction is relevant. The *gemara* nevertheless proceeds on the assumption that the twenty-*amah* search is required in multiple directions since the orientation cannot be determined with certainty.

One further point: the *mishna*'s rule that finding a single body at the end of twenty *amot* requires continuing the search can be understood, under the cave-complex reading, as follows. If the cave complex extends from one group of bodies to another, finding a body at the end of twenty *amot* might seem to mark the boundary of a single cave complex — one could argue the complex has been fully identified and no further search is needed. But the *mishna* rejects this: once one cave complex has been established, finding another body nearby does not allow one to say the graveyard ends there. That body might be the beginning of an entirely separate cave complex. Only when twenty *amot* are cleared with no findings can the search end.

## 22 to 20 Resolution

### Problem Stated

The *gemara* now raises a sharp problem. *Amar Mar*: the *mishna* specifies a range of four to eight *amot* and a search radius of twenty *amot*. *Me'arba ve'ad shmona mani* — whose opinion does this reflect? *I Rabbanan* — if it is the *rabbanan*, who hold that a cave wall is four *amot* by six *amot*, one would not find three bodies in a wall of eight *amot*, since eight exceeds the *rabbanan*'s maximum wall length. *I Rabbi Shimon* — if it is Rabbi Shimon, who holds that the cave is six *amot* by eight *amot*, then you could have three bodies within eight *amot* with one niche empty. That accounts for the maximum of eight.

The *gemara* then turns to the twenty *amot*. *Eima seifa: bodek heimenu ulehalan esrim amah. Mani?* — whose opinion does this reflect? *I Rabbi Shimon esrim ve-tarten havyan* — according to Rabbi Shimon with his six-by-eight cave, the total distance to check should be twenty-two *amot*, not twenty: eight for the first wall, six for the width, and eight for the opposite wall. *I Rabbanan tmoneh esrei havyan* — according to the *rabbanan* with a four-by-six cave, the total would be eighteen: six plus six plus six. Neither figure matches twenty.

At this stage it should also be noted that the *Rashbam* observes a difficulty in his own reading: if we are going to now argue that the cave is six *amot* wide (to fit the *rabbanan*'s dimensions) as a way of approaching the twenty-*amah* number, we cannot simultaneously claim that three bodies can fall within eight *amot*, since under the *rabbanan* the wall is only six *amot* long. The *gemara* is aware of the tension but proceeds.

### Rabbanan & Diagonal

The *gemara*'s first answer: *le-olam Rabbanan hee* — the *mishna* follows the *rabbanan*, whose cave is six *amot* by four *amot* (or rather, whose wall length is six *amot*). Under the *rabbanan*, the straight-line calculation yields eighteen *amot*, not twenty. But the *mishna*'s protocol calls for checking *be-alakhsona* — at a diagonal — and by going at a diagonal rather than straight, the effective distance covered is longer. Specifically, the *gemara* argues that a diagonal across a six-by-four rectangle is eight *amot*, producing eight plus six plus six equals twenty.

The *Rashbam* explains the rationale for the diagonal check: because the searcher is uncertain of his exact position within the cave complex, and because the niches run four *amot* deep, going at an angle covers more ground and reduces the risk of missing a buried body.

### Tosafot Math

*Tosafot* challenges the *Rashbam*'s mathematics. The *gemara*'s claim is that the diagonal of a six-by-four rectangle is eight *amot*. This is what the *Rashbam* accepts. But *Tosafot* demonstrates that this cannot be correct. The *gemara* elsewhere operates with the principle that the diagonal of any square is approximately 1.4 times the side — this is the rabbinic approximation of the square root of two. A four-by-four square therefore has a diagonal of approximately 5.6 *amot*. A six-by-four rectangle can be divided into a four-by-four square and a two-by-four remainder. The diagonal of the four-by-four portion is 5.6, and adding the remaining two *amot* gives 7.6 — not eight. *Tosafot* thus proves geometrically, without recourse to any formula, that the *Rashbam*'s claim that the diagonal of a six-by-four equals eight is incorrect.

For those who prefer the precise calculation: by the Pythagorean theorem, the square root of six squared plus four squared equals the square root of thirty-six plus sixteen equals the square root of fifty-two, which is approximately 7.2 *amot*. In either approach — *Tosafot*'s rabbinic method or the Pythagorean formula — the diagonal falls short of eight. *Tosafot*'s proof is impressive precisely because it is made without a formula, relying instead on the *gemara*'s own approximation method.

The *gemara* itself anticipates part of this problem. *U-midde-ha be-alakhsona hai nami be-alakhsona esrim ve-tartei havyan* — if one is going to do a diagonal check at one end, consistency would require doing a diagonal at the other end too, which would bring the total back to twenty-two rather than twenty. The *gemara* responds: *chada be-alakhsona amrinan tartei be-alakhsona lo amrinan* — we require a diagonal check in one direction but not in both. The rationale is that the diagonal check, which demands greater care and coverage, is required closer to where the original bodies were found, but not necessarily at the far end of the search. This is first answer to the problem of twenty versus twenty-two.

### Infant Graves

The *gemara* then offers a second answer through Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi: *le-olam Rabbi Shimon hi* — the *mishna* actually follows Rabbi Shimon, with his eight-*amah* wall. *Ve-hacha be-mai askinan bi-nefili* — the case involves infant graves (*nefili*). If the original three bodies discovered were infants, one might assume the entire cave complex was dedicated to infant burials. Infants' bodies are smaller, so the niches and the overall cave would be proportionally smaller — perhaps only six *amot* rather than eight. This brings the math from twenty-two back toward twenty.

The *gemara* then asks: *u-midehava bi-nefili ha nami bi-nefili?* — if we have established that the first set of graves involved infants, why not assume that the graves found at the far end of the search are also infant graves, and therefore shrink that end as well? The answer is: *chada bi-nefili amrinan tartei bi-nefili lo amrinan* — we apply the infant-grave assumption once, to the first set, but we do not extend it to both ends of the search. This parallels the logic of the diagonal answer: leniency in one direction, not both.

All of this analysis rests on the *gemara*'s unusual insistence on reading the *mishna* through the framework of cave burial rather than straightforward ground burial — an interpretive choice that the *gemara* does not explicitly justify, and which generates considerable difficulty throughout.

## Extra Bodies Logic

### Compressed Burials

The final portion of the *mishna* addresses a case of *retzufim* — bodies buried with more compression than normal, so that four bodies fall within less than four *amot*. The *tana kamma* rules that such compactly arranged bodies do not constitute a graveyard: they are not laid out with the standard spacing, and therefore the presumption of an established burial ground does not apply. The three or four bodies found together may nonetheless be removed and reinterred elsewhere, along with the soil beneath them.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: *ro'in et ha-emtza'in ke'ilu einam* — one looks at the middle bodies as if they are not there. That is, if four bodies are buried within, say, five *amot* — too condensed to qualify as a graveyard under normal rules — Rabbi Shimon instructs us to disregard one of the middle bodies. The remaining three would then fall within four to eight *amot* and the site would qualify as a *shechunat kvarot*.

### Vineyard Parallel

The *gemara* notes that precisely this type of reasoning — whether to ignore extra or intermediate items when determining whether a legal threshold has been met — appears in a parallel dispute involving vineyard spacing, and that Rabbi Shimon and the *rabbanan* take the opposite positions there. A vineyard planted with fewer than four *amot* between the vines — *kerem hanatu'a pachot me'arba amot* — is not a proper vineyard, and the laws of *kil'ayim* do not apply to it in the same way. Rabbi Shimon says: *eino kerem* — it is not a vineyard, period; the compressed spacing disqualifies it. The *chachamim* say: *harei zeh kerem ve-ro'in et ha-emtza'iyim ke'ilu einam* — it is a vineyard, and we look at the intermediate vines as if they are not there. So in the vineyard case, the *chachamim* apply the principle of ignoring extras to establish a legal classification, while Rabbi Shimon refuses; whereas in the grave case, Rabbi Shimon applies that principle and the *tana kamma* refuses.

### Emergency vs Intent

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *kashya de-Rabbanan a-de-Rabbanan kashya de-Rabbi Shimon a-de-Rabbi Shimon* — both the apparent self-contradiction of the *rabbanan* and the apparent self-contradiction of Rabbi Shimon are not difficult. *De-Rabbi Shimon a-de-Rabbi Shimon la kashya* — Rabbi Shimon's positions are in fact consistent. In the vineyard case, Rabbi Shimon refuses to ignore the extra vine because *lo nata inish ada'ata le-me'ekar* — one does not plant a vine with the intention of uprooting it. The act of planting was intentional and deliberate; it cannot be treated as an accident or a temporary measure. Therefore the vine is counted, the spacing is too tight, and it is not a vineyard.

By contrast, in the grave case, *zimnin de-mishtarmi lei bein hashmashot* — sometimes it happens that someone has to bury a body on *erev Shabbos*, rushed and unable to prepare the proper spacing, *u-mikrei u-manach lei* — and he simply places it wherever he can. The implied understanding is that he intended to return after *Shabbos* and reinter the body in its proper place. Because the extra burial was not meant to be permanent, one can treat it as though it is not really part of the graveyard, ignore it for purposes of determining spacing, and conclude that the remaining three bodies do constitute a *shechunat kvarot*.

For the *rabbanan* the logic is the inverse. In the grave case, *de-minaval* — because it is deeply disrespectful to bury a body with such compressed spacing — *lo mikrei kever*, it does not constitute a proper grave. The disrespectful treatment itself disqualifies the site from being recognized as an established graveyard, regardless of the emergency circumstances. But in the vineyard case, one can easily imagine planting an extra vine with the intention of keeping only the best ones — *d'shapir shapir, de-la shapir lihevei le-tzivei*, a good vine remains and a weak vine will become firewood. Since that extra vine was always a candidate for removal, the *chachamim* are willing to look past it and treat the remaining vines as a proper vineyard.

### Respect Argument

The *Rashbam* presses a counter-argument against the *gemara*'s explanation of Rabbi Shimon's position in the grave case. The *gemara* had said that the extra body was placed there under emergency conditions — *erev Shabbos* — with the intent to relocate it afterward. The *Rashbam* argues that this logic fails: even in an emergency, no one would bury a body in a manner that is so disrespectful of the deceased. *Kavod ha-met* is a serious obligation, and the very fact that the body ended up compressed into this space shows that its placement was not an emergency measure — it was simply done incorrectly, by someone who did not understand the proper way to establish a burial site. The placement was therefore entirely intentional, not a temporary accommodation, and there is no basis to treat it as though the body is not really there. On the *Rashbam*'s reading, Rabbi Shimon's ruling — that we ignore the extra body and recognize the site as a graveyard — must rest on some other logic than the emergency-intent rationale the *gemara* offers.

## Land Measurement

### Beis Kor Measure

With *hadran lach ha-mocher et ha-peiros*, the *gemara* transitions to an entirely new *mishna*. The new perek opens with a case of land sale. *Ha-omer la-chaveiro: beis kor afar ani mocher lach* — one says to his fellow: I am selling you a *beis kor* of land. The *beis kor* is a measure of area derived from volume: a *kor* is a volumetric measure for grain, and a *beis kor* denotes the area of land required to sow one *kor* of seed. More concretely, a *beis se'ah* — fifty by fifty *amot* — is a standard unit. There are six *kavim* in a *se'ah*, and a *kor* equals thirty *se'ahs*, making a *beis kor* equal to thirty *beis se'ahs*. This is an enormous area — approximately 150,000 square feet — and the halakhic rules governing its sale are the subject of the new *mishna*.

### Boulder Exclusion

The *mishna* addresses a scenario in which the sold parcel contains obstructions: *hayu sham neka'im amukim asarah tefachim* — deep crevices of ten *tefachim* in depth — *o sla'im gevohim asarah tefachim* — or boulders protruding ten *tefachim* in height. The *Rashbam* understands these obstructions to be four *amot* by four *amot* in their horizontal dimensions as well, approximating the area of an independent domain. When such a feature — a crevice or boulder that is ten *tefachim* deep or tall — sits within the parcel, the ruling is *ein nimdadin imah*: it is not included in the measured area. The seller cannot count the space occupied by this boulder or crevice as part of the *beis kor* being sold; he must make up that space elsewhere in the parcel or adjust accordingly.

If the obstruction is *pachos mikan* — less than ten *tefachim* in height or depth — the ruling is *nimdadin imah*: it is included in the measured area, and the buyer receives no additional land to compensate.

### Precise vs Approx

The *mishna* distinguishes between a precise sale and an approximate one. If the seller said not *beis kor* but *ki-beis kor* — *like* a *beis kor*, approximately a *beis kor*'s worth — the word *ki* introduces an element of approximation. Under this formulation, *afilu hayu sham neka'im amukim yoser me'asarah tefachim o sla'im gevohim yoter me'asarah tefachim, harei elu nimdadin imah* — even if there are crevices deeper than ten *tefachim* or boulders taller than ten *tefachim*, they are all included in the measured area. The approximate formulation signals that the buyer is acquiring the land as it stands, with all its features, and the stated measure is a rough approximation rather than a precise guarantee. The lack of precision does not mean the buyer receives less than a full *beis kor*; it means the full area including all obstructions counts toward the promised measure.
