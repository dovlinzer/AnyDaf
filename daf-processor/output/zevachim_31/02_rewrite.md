# Zevachim 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul w/ Intervening Th…

### Half-portions Case

The daf opens three lines down from the top, continuing the discussion of *piggul* thoughts and the effect of an intervening thought of *chutz l'mkomo*. As a brief orientation: the position of the *Rabbanan* is that any extraneous invalid thought interjected during the sacrificial service prevents the offering from becoming *piggul*. Rabbi Yehuda disagreed, holding that once *piggul* has taken effect, a subsequent bad thought cannot undo it — but if the bad thought came first, the offering is invalidated before *piggul* can form, and the later *chutz l'zmano* thought is therefore inoperative. With that context, the Gemara introduces the following case.

The case is: *chatzi zayis chutz lizmano, v'chatzi zayis chutz limkomo, v'chatzi zayis chutz lizmano* — the *kohen* entertains three consecutive thoughts: first, that he will consume half a *k'zayis* outside its designated time; second, that he will consume half a *k'zayis* outside its designated place; and third, that he will consume another half a *k'zayis* outside its designated time. This case is being analyzed within the framework of the *Rabbanan*.

The underlying conceptual issue requires some unpacking. A thought about a mere half-*k'zayis* is legally inconsequential — only a thought involving a full *k'zayis* carries halachic weight. Had the *kohen* thought about a full *k'zayis* in the wrong place, that would constitute a meaningful invalidating thought capable of preventing *piggul*. But a half-*k'zayis* thought of *chutz l'mkomo* is not significant enough to serve as an *eruv machshavos* on its own.

Now, consider the progression. The first thought — half a *k'zayis chutz lizmano* — is, taken alone, insufficient for *piggul*. When combined with the second thought — half a *k'zayis chutz limkomo* — the two halves together constitute a full *k'zayis* of disordered intent, and by the principle of *tzeiruf* established in our *Mishnah* (derived from the word *yihyeh*), the *korban* becomes *pasul*. So by the time the second thought has been entertained, the offering is already *pasul*. The question is whether the third thought — another half *k'zayis chutz lizmano* — can now interact retroactively with the first half-*k'zayis chutz lizmano* to produce *piggul*, or whether we say the offering is already *pasul* and cannot now become *piggul*.

One might argue: since the two *chutz lizmano* half-thoughts are more naturally aligned with each other, we should step back, reassess, and say that the dominant thought is a full *k'zayis* of *chutz lizmano*, rendering the offering *piggul*. The half-thought of *chutz limkomo* is not sufficiently weighty to disrupt that pairing. Alternatively, one might argue: by the time the third thought arrives, the *korban* is already *pasul* from the combination of the first two thoughts, and *piggul* cannot take hold in something already *pasul*.

### Rava: Piggul Wakes Up

Rava rules: *vayikatz k'yoshen ha'piggul* — "and the *piggul* wakes up, like a sleeping person." The *piggul*, though temporarily dormant due to the intervening half-thought of *chutz limkomo*, reasserts itself. We step back, assess the totality, recognize the more natural pairing of the two *chutz lizmano* half-thoughts, and declare the offering *piggul*.

### Tosafot on Thoughts

Rashi invokes a closely related phrase — *chozer v'nei'or*, "it wakes up again" — drawn from the domain of physical mixture. The classic case: sixty parts of chicken soup into which half a unit of forbidden matter (*chazir*) falls and is *batel*. If another half-unit of forbidden matter subsequently falls in, the question arises whether the first half "wakes up" — *chozer v'nei'or* — and combines with the second to form a full prohibited unit no longer subject to *bitul*. Rava's formulation borrows this imagery.

Tosafot, however, flags an important disanalogy. In the physical case, at the moment the second half-unit is added, both halves are *physically present simultaneously* — there is an actual full unit of forbidden substance co-existing in the mixture at one moment in time. In the case of *piggul*, we are dealing with thoughts that existed sequentially and have passed. There is no moment at which a full *k'zayis* of *piggul* thought exists simultaneously in any coherent sense. These are distinct mental acts distributed across time, not physical quantities occupying space together. Tosafot therefore regards Rava's analogy as illuminating but imperfect, and maintains that in the domain of thought, one should not so readily say the *piggul* "wakes up."

### Rav Hamnuna: Erev M'chav…

Rav Hamnuna takes the opposing position: *erev machshavos havei* — the thought of *chutz limkomo* constitutes an *erev machshavos*, an extraneous thought that invalidates the sequence, and the offering remains *pasul* rather than becoming *piggul*. His reasoning, it may be presumed, turns on the fact that by the time the third thought arrives, the *korban* has already become *pasul* through the combination of the first two half-thoughts, and once *pasul*, it cannot be reconstituted as *piggul*.

One might ask: what if the intervening thought had been only a quarter-*k'zayis* of *chutz limkomo* — insufficient, even in combination with the first half-thought, to render the offering *pasul*? Presumably Rav Hamnuna would agree that in that case *piggul* does take effect. The critical factor for Rav Hamnuna is not the mere presence of an intervening thought but whether that intervening thought succeeded in rendering the offering *pasul* before the *piggul* configuration could fully form.

## Tumah Analogy

### Rava's Tumah Proof

Rava now seeks a proof for his position from a *Mishnah* in the laws of *tumah*. The *Mishnah* states: *kebeitzah ochel rishon u'kebeitzah ochel sheni* — a *kebeitzah*-worth of food that is a *rishon l'tumah* and a *kebeitzah*-worth of food that is a *sheni l'tumah*. To set up the scenario concretely: imagine one lump of dough that came into contact with a *neveilah* — it is now a *rishon l'tumah*, first degree of contamination. A separate lump of dough touched the first lump and is now a *sheni l'tumah*, second degree of contamination.

These two lumps are then mixed together — *shebelalan zeh bazeh* — into one large two-*kebeitzah* piece of dough. Within that larger piece, there is a full *kebeitzah*-worth of *rishon l'tumah* distributed throughout. When this combined dough touches another food item, say an apple, the apple becomes a *sheni l'tumah*, because there is a full *kebeitzah* of *rishon* present capable of transmitting contamination. A *kebeitzah* of substance is required for food to function as a *metamei*, to render other things *tamei*.

Now the *Mishnah* continues: *chilkan* — if you divide the combined dough back in half, then *zeh sheni v'zeh sheni* — each half now operates only as a *sheni l'tumah*. Why? Because in each half there is only a half-*kebeitzah* of *rishon l'tumah* and a half-*kebeitzah* of *sheni l'tumah*. Since you need a full *kebeitzah* of *rishon* to render something else a *sheni*, and no single half contains that full amount, the halves each operate at the lower common level of *sheni*.

Then: *hechezir v'irvan* — if you recombine the two halves, *rishon havei* — it reverts to functioning as a *rishon*. And indeed the *Mishnah* makes this explicit in its *seifa*: if both halves fall separately on a loaf of *terumah*, each falling alone can only make the *terumah* into a *shlishi* (*terumah* can attain a *shlishi* status, which is called *pasul*). But — *im naflu shnehem ke'echad* — if both halves fall on the *terumah* simultaneously, or if one falls and remains while the second falls upon it, then at that moment there is a full *kebeitzah* of *rishon l'tumah* in contact with the *terumah*, and the *terumah* becomes a *sheni l'tumah*. *Asa sheni.*

How does this serve as a proof for Rava? The argument is as follows: when the dough was divided, each half was a mixture of half-*kebeitzah rishon* and half-*kebeitzah sheni*. Despite that internal mixture — despite the fact that the *rishon* component was thoroughly blended with *sheni* material — when both halves are reunited on the *terumah*, the *rishon* component within each half does not lose its identity. It reconnects with the *rishon* component in the other half, and together they form a full *kebeitzah* of *rishon* capable of transmitting *tumah*. The *rishon*, so to speak, wakes up. This is Rava's model for the *piggul* case: the *chutz lizmano* thought, though interrupted by the *chutz limkomo* half-thought, retains its identity and can combine with the other *chutz lizmano* half-thought to form a full *piggul*.

Tosafot's objection, noted above, applies here again: in the *tumah* case, at the critical moment when both halves lie simultaneously on the *terumah*, there is a physically present full *kebeitzah* of *rishon*. This is not an abstraction — both halves coexist at one instant. In the *piggul* case, however, the thoughts existed at different moments in time and are no longer present. Moreover, the temporal sequencing is itself part of what defines *piggul*. These differences render the *tumah* analogy genuinely strained.

### Rashi: Full Measure Start

The Gemara now records Rav Hamnuna's rebuttal: *hatam ika shiura, hacha leka shiura* — "there a *shiur* is present, here no *shiur* is present." But what exactly does this mean?

Rashi explains the distinction as follows: in the *tumah* case, *before* any mixing occurred, you began with a full *kebeitzah* of *rishon* and a full *kebeitzah* of *sheni*. Each component started at its full measure. Even after they were mixed and split, since the *rishon* began as a complete *kebeitzah*, it retains its identity more readily and can reconstitute itself as a full measure upon recombination. The identity of the *rishon* was never genuinely compromised — it simply passed through a stage of physical distribution. In the *piggul* case, by contrast, you never had a full *k'zayis* of *chutz lizmano* thought to begin with. You only ever had a half, and a half is not a full *shiur*. A half cannot wake up the way a temporarily dispersed full measure can.

### Tosafot: Tight Bonding

Tosafot offers a different reading. The phrase *leka shiura* does not mean that the *rishon* started at a full measure; rather, it refers to the *sheni l'tumah* present in the mixed dough. Because the *rishon* component is incomplete — only a half-*kebeitzah* — it bonds more tightly with whatever it is mixed with. Incompleteness creates a stronger attachment. In the *piggul* scenario, by analogy, the half-thought of *chutz limkomo* that falls between the two *chutz lizmano* thoughts forms a tight bond with the adjacent half-thought, making it harder for the *chutz lizmano* thoughts to reassert themselves and combine over that intervening thought. In the *tumah* case, the *rishon* component, when present at a full *kebeitzah*, does not need the *sheni* to complete it, and so the bond between them is weaker — allowing the *rishon* to separate out and reconstitute itself when the two halves are reunited. Tosafot thus focuses on the bonding dynamics of the intermediate component rather than the starting quantity of the dominant component.

### Rav Hamnuna: No Shiur

Rav Hamnuna himself brings a counter-proof from a parallel *Mishnah*: *ha'ochel she'nitma b'av hatumah* — food that became *tamei* through an *av hatumah* (such as a *neveilah*), rendering it a *rishon*, *v'shenitma b'vlad hatumah*, and food that became *tamei* through a derivative of *tumah* (a *rishon*), rendering it a *sheni* — *mitztarfin zeh im zeh l'tamei b'kala shebishneihem*, these combine to operate at the lower of the two levels, that of a *sheni*. This is the same basic scenario but now the two components start as halves from the outset — you never had a full *kebeitzah* of either *rishon* or *sheni* separately. You have half a *kebeitzah* of *rishon* and half a *kebeitzah* of *sheni*.

Rav Hamnuna wants to infer from the language of this *Mishnah* — which says simply that they combine to act as a *sheni* — that even if you then added a completing half-*kebeitzah* of *rishon*, the combined mass would still operate only as a *sheni*, because the *Mishnah*'s ruling is stated definitively without any qualification. This would parallel the *piggul* case: even if you add the third thought, the offering remains in its *pasul* state.

The Gemara pushes back: *lo dilma delo hadar malyei* — perhaps the *Mishnah* only means that at the stage described, the mixture operates as a *sheni*; if you were to subsequently add the remaining half-*kebeitzah* of *rishon*, it might well revert to operating as a *rishon*. The *Mishnah* is not ruling on the subsequent scenario at all. So the debate remains unresolved from this source.

## Half-portions Debate

### Rav Dimi's Case

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he formulated a related case. His case is: *chatzi zayis chutz limkomo, v'chatzi zayis chutz lizmano, v'chatzi zayis chutz lizmano* — first half a *k'zayis chutz limkomo*, then half a *k'zayis chutz lizmano*, then another half a *k'zayis chutz lizmano*. Rav Dimi states that this case is obviously *pasul* but not *piggul*.

His point is that the novelty he is presenting is not this case — which all would agree is *pasul* and not *piggul*, since by the time you reach the first full *k'zayis* (combining the *chutz limkomo* thought and the first *chutz lizmano* thought), the *korban* is already *pasul*. Rather, Rav Dimi's implicit claim is that even a case that seems more naturally inclined toward *piggul* — where the two *chutz lizmano* thoughts appear side by side — is nonetheless not *piggul*, because the first full *k'zayis* was reached at the *pasul* stage rather than the *piggul* stage.

### Bar Kappara Position

Bar Kappara disagrees. He holds that in the arrangement *makom, zman, zman* (i.e., the case with *chutz limkomo* first), the offering is *pasul* and not *piggul* — obviously, since the *chutz limkomo* intervened before the two *chutz lizmano* thoughts could combine. But in the reverse arrangement — where the two *chutz lizmano* thoughts appear consecutively, with *chutz limkomo* following — Bar Kappara says that is *piggul*. His ruling: *ein chatzi kezayis mo'il b'makom kezayis* — a half-*k'zayis* thought cannot function in place of a full *k'zayis* thought. Since at the moment when the first full *k'zayis* is completed you have a combination of two *chutz lizmano* halves, that constitutes *piggul*. The subsequent half-*k'zayis* of *chutz limkomo* is too small to disrupt what has already formed.

### Ravin's Formulation

When Ravin came, he presented yet another arrangement: *chatzi kezayis chutz lizmano, v'chatzi kezayis chutz lizmano, v'chatzi kezayis chutz limkomo* — that is, *zman, zman, makom*. Bar Kappara holds that this arrangement is *piggul*: by the time you have reached the first full *k'zayis*, both halves are *chutz lizmano*, giving you a complete *piggul* thought, and the subsequent half-*k'zayis* of *chutz limkomo* is insufficient to retroactively undo what has already taken effect. *Ein chatzi kezayis mo'il b'makom kezayis.*

The three arrangements can now be laid out clearly. The arrangement *makom, zman, zman* is agreed by all to be *pasul* and not *piggul* — the *chutz limkomo* half-thought combined with the first *chutz lizmano* half-thought to produce *pasul* before any *piggul* could form. The arrangement *zman, zman, makom* is *piggul* according to Bar Kappara, since the first *k'zayis* is reached through two *chutz lizmano* thoughts. And the arrangement *zman, makom, zman* — Rava's original case — is where the real dispute between Rava and Rav Hamnuna lies: Rava says *piggul* wakes up; Rav Hamnuna says it remains *pasul*.

### Rav Ashi Restructured

Rav Ashi presents a further restructuring of the case. His formulation is: *chatzi kezayis chutz lizmano u'kezayis chetzyo chutz limkomo v'chetzyo chutz lizmano* — one half-*k'zayis chutz lizmano*, followed by a full *k'zayis* that the *kohen* declares to be split: half *chutz limkomo* and half *chutz lizmano*. Bar Kappara rules: *piggul*. *Ein chatzi kezayis mo'il b'makom kezayis.*

In this formulation, when you combine the initial half-*k'zayis chutz lizmano* with the second *k'zayis* (itself internally divided half-*chutz limkomo* and half-*chutz lizmano*), you cannot draw a boundary anywhere that gives you a full *k'zayis* of *pasul* without simultaneously producing a full *k'zayis* of *piggul*. The moment the two *chutz lizmano* half-thoughts combine, you have a full *k'zayis chutz lizmano* — there is no stage at which the offering settles as merely *pasul* before *piggul* is constituted. The half-*k'zayis chutz limkomo* embedded within the second *k'zayis* is not independent enough to serve as an *eruv machshavos*; it is structurally bound within the whole.

One might have supposed that in Rava's original case — *zman, makom, zman* — the intervention of the *chutz limkomo* half prevents *piggul*, while in Rav Ashi's case, because no such intervention is possible, *piggul* results. But Rav Ashi's point (according to Bar Kappara) is that even in Rava's case, the *piggul* holds — and this case is an even more striking instance, because here one might have thought the internally combined *k'zayis* of *pasul* (half-*makom* plus half-*zman* bound together) should prevent the two *zman* halves from combining. The ruling is that they do combine.

### Rashi Correction

At this point a significant correction is in order, because Rashi reads these cases in precisely the opposite direction from an initial reading.

On reflection, Rashi understands Rav Ashi's formulation not as moving along a spectrum from clearer cases of *pasul* toward a single case of *piggul*, but in the opposite direction: all the cases are *piggul*, and Rav Ashi's *chiddush* is that even the most challenging configuration — where the intervening half-*chutz limkomo* is not a standalone thought but is structurally embedded within a full *k'zayis* combined with a half-*chutz lizmano* — is still *piggul*.

According to Rashi, the concern in Rav Ashi's case is that one could view the second *k'zayis* as a fully constituted *pasul k'zayis* (half-*makom* and half-*zman* forming a complete *pasul* unit), in which case one might argue: the first half-*k'zayis chutz lizmano* paired with this complete *pasul k'zayis* should yield only *pasul*, not *piggul*. The *chiddush* is that even so, we disaggregate: the two *chutz lizmano* halves find each other and combine, the embedded *chutz limkomo* half is insufficient to hold them apart, and *piggul* results. This is a greater *chiddush* than Rava's case, because in Rava's case the *chutz limkomo* half-thought was independent and free-standing, whereas here it is wrapped together with a *chutz lizmano* half — and even so, it cannot prevent the *piggul*.

## Dogs & Fire Thoughts

### Rav Yanai: Dogs Eating

Rav Yanai rules: *chishiv sheyocheluhu klavim l'machar, piggul* — if the *kohen* thought during the service that the meat would be fed to dogs tomorrow, this constitutes a *piggul* thought. The consuming act of dogs is considered *achilah* — eating. His proof is from the verse: *v'es Izevel yochlu haklavim b'chelek Yizreel* — "and Jezebel shall be consumed by the dogs in the plot of Jezreel" (Melachim I 21:23). The verb *yochlu* is used, demonstrating that the Torah treats the consumption of dogs as an act of eating.

Tosafot raises an objection: many verses speak of animals eating, so what is the *chiddush* here? The deeper question, Tosafot notes, is not merely about whether the verb *achilah* applies to dogs, but whether the conceptual structure of *piggul* can be satisfied by such a thought at all. The logic of *piggul* is that the *kohen* is redirecting the intended act — human consumption of the sacrifice — to the wrong time or place. *Piggul* is premised on a *thought about the natural, designated act of consumption*. Feeding meat to dogs is not the natural, intended act for sacrificial meat; it is closer to destruction than to the proper consumption that *piggul* addresses. Yet despite this conceptual difficulty, the ruling stands that it does constitute a *piggul* thought.

### Rav Ami Challenge

Rav Ami challenges: *ella me'ata, chishev shetochluhu esh l'machar* — if that is so, what about a thought that fire would consume the meat tomorrow? The verse states: *tochlu esh lo nopach* — "a fire not blown will consume it" (Iyov 20:26). The word *tochlu* — consuming — is used of fire. Should we then say that planning for fire to consume the sacrifice tomorrow constitutes *piggul*?

The answer, one might suggest, is yes. But then the following problem emerges from the *Mishnah*: *le'echol k'chatzi zayis u'lehaktir k'chatzi zayis kasher, she'ein achilah v'haktarah mitztarfin* — if one planned to eat half a *k'zayis* and to burn half a *k'zayis* on the altar, it is valid — these two half-*k'zayis* thoughts do not combine. If burning on the altar were simply another form of *achilah*, why would this *Mishnah* not allow the two halves to combine?

### Language Matters

The Gemara's resolution is that the answer depends entirely on precise verbal formulation. *Ei de'afkei belashon achilah, hacha nami* — if you expressed the thought about burning using the language of *achilah* — saying, for example, that the fire would *consume* it, using the eating idiom — then even fire-consumption might combine. *Hacha bemai askinan de'afkei belashon haktarah* — the *Mishnah*'s case involves the standard language of *haktarah* (offering on the altar), not *achilah*. And *lashon achilah lachud v'lashon haktarah lachud* — the language of eating and the language of altar-burning are distinct categories.

This resolution is, to say the least, striking. The entire framework of *piggul* is built on redirecting the natural act of the sacrifice to the wrong time or place. That feeding dogs or burning by fire — acts that would never satisfy the obligations of the sacrifice — should generate *piggul* based solely on the verbal category one uses in formulating the thought is a deeply counterintuitive position. And indeed it is strange that whether one says "I will burn it" versus "I will have it consumed by fire" should determine whether the thought is a valid *piggul* thought. The Gemara nonetheless resolves the question in precisely this way, leaving the reader with a sense that the formal structure of *piggul* is being operated at a level of abstraction quite removed from the practical realities of the service.

It is also worth noting, as Tosafot elsewhere points out, that this stands in stark contrast to the rule that a thought of *teme'im* eating the sacrifice is *not piggul* — because *teme'im* are categorically excluded from eating *kodashim*, and the thought therefore cannot redress the natural eating that should occur. If tamei persons do not constitute a valid eating thought, the question of why dogs should — given that neither dogs nor fire would ever satisfy the obligation — becomes all the more acute.

## Two People Eating

### Rav Ashi Question

Rav Ashi poses the following question: *chishev le'echol kezayis bishnei bnei adam mahu* — if the *kohen* thought during the service that a full *k'zayis* of the sacrifice would be consumed tomorrow, but split between two people, each eating half a *k'zayis*, what is the ruling? Does the thought constitute *piggul*?

The question divides on two framings. On one view: *batar machshava azlinan d'ika shiura* — we follow the thought, and the thought was about a full *k'zayis* being consumed. Since the *kohen*'s mental intent encompassed a full *k'zayis* of consumption, it should constitute *piggul*. On the alternative view: *batar ochlin azlinan v'leika shiura* — we assess each act of eating independently, and each person's act involves only a half-*k'zayis*, which is below the threshold of significance. Since no single act of eating reaches the required *shiur*, there is no valid *piggul* thought.

### Abaye's Proof

Abaye resolves the question by invoking the *Mishnah*: *le'echol k'chatzi zayis u'lehaktir k'chatzi zayis kasher, she'ein achilah v'haktarah mitztarfin* — half a *k'zayis* for human eating and half a *k'zayis* for altar consumption do not combine. The reason they do not combine is that human eating and altar-burning are categorically distinct acts. But this implies — *ha le'echol v'le'echol mitztaref* — that eating and eating do combine. If the half-*k'zayis* intended for the altar were instead intended for a second human being, the two halves of eating would combine to form a valid *piggul* thought.

Abaye states this as a near-explicit inference: *v'heichi dami b'shnei bnei adam, mitztaref* — when two human beings are each to eat half a *k'zayis*, that combines. What makes human-and-altar fail to combine is not the division of the act between two parties, but the categorical difference between human eating and altar burning. Two instances of the same category — human eating — do combine. *Shma mina.*

## Time Duration Issue

### Rava Slow Eating

Rava poses a further question: *chishev le'echol kezayis b'yoser m'kdei achilas pras, mahu* — if the *kohen* thought that he would eat a full *k'zayis* of the sacrifice tomorrow, but at an extremely slow pace, over a duration longer than *kdei achilas pras* (the halachically defined time unit for eating, roughly the time required to consume half a loaf of bread, often understood as approximately five to ten minutes) — does this constitute *piggul*?

The principle of *kdei achilas pras* governs many areas of halacha: for *matzah* on Pesach, eating *cheilev* or *chametz* — the forbidden act or the fulfillment of the *mitzvah* is defined by whether consumption occurred within this timeframe. Eating spread across a longer period is not considered a unified act of eating. Rava's question is whether the *piggul* thought must envision consumption within *kdei achilas pras* to be valid, or whether a thought of extended, slow consumption suffices.

### Altar vs Human Time

Two possible frameworks present themselves. *L'achilas gavoah madminan lah* — perhaps we compare the act to altar-burning, which by its nature takes far longer than *achilas pras*, and therefore a thought of extended consumption is still valid. *O l'achilas hedyot madminan lah* — or perhaps we compare it to ordinary human eating, in which case the thought must envision consumption within *achilas pras*. Logically, the answer would seem to depend on what the *kohen* was planning: if planning for his own consumption, it should be measured by human eating norms; if planning for the altar, by altar norms.

Abaye attempts to bring a proof from the same *Mishnah*: *le'echol k'chatzi zayis u'lehaktir k'chatzi zayis kasher, she'ein achilah v'haktarah mitztarfin*. He argues: *ha le'echol v'le'echol mitztaref* — if instead of an altar-half, there had been a human-eating-half, they would combine. But altar-burning takes longer than *achilas pras* — *v'haktar b'yoter m'kdei achilas pras hu*. This suggests that even when the act will take longer than *achilas pras*, it remains a valid *piggul* thought — which would seem to support the view that extended duration does not invalidate the thought.

The Gemara deflects this proof: *dilma b'hesek gadol* — perhaps the case of altar-burning being analogized involves a large fire, one that would in fact consume the sacrifice quickly, within the standard timeframe. If so, the inference fails, because we may be comparing only to a fast altar-burning case. The Gemara leaves the question unresolved. One might have expected the answer to depend naturally on the nature of the plan — human or altar — but the Gemara does not provide a clear resolution and leaves it as an open *teiku*.

## Non-Standard Parts

### Meat + Altar Split

The Gemara now focuses directly on the *Mishnah* that has been cited throughout: *le'echol k'chatzi zayis u'lehaktir k'chatzi zayis kasher, she'ein achilah v'haktarah mitztarfin* — a thought to eat half a *k'zayis* plus a thought to burn half a *k'zayis* on the altar: valid (*kasher*). The two half-*k'zayis* thoughts do not combine, since eating and altar-burning are two categorically distinct acts.

The Gemara now draws an inference from the precise subject matter of the *Mishnah*. What is being burned on the altar in this scenario? The *emurim* — the sacrificial innards and fats designated for the altar. And what is being eaten? The meat, which is the portion designated for human consumption. The *Mishnah* tells us that planning to eat half a *k'zayis* of meat plus planning to burn half a *k'zayis* of *emurim* does not combine. One might infer from this: but if instead of burning the *emurim* on the altar, one had planned to eat that half-*k'zayis* of *emurim* oneself, that would combine with the half-*k'zayis* of meat — yielding a full *k'zayis* of human eating-intent — and constitute *piggul*.

But this inference would be significant, because it would imply that a *kohen*'s thought to eat the *emurim* — the parts not designated for human consumption — constitutes a valid *piggul* thought. This is a matter of explicit dispute.

### R. Eliezer Dispute

Rabbi Yirmiyah identifies the position implied by this inference as that of Rabbi Eliezer: *ha meni Rabbi Eliezer hi* — this follows Rabbi Eliezer's view. For Rabbi Eliezer holds: *mechashvin me'achilat adam le'achilat mizbe'ach u'me'achilat mizbe'ach le'achilat adam* — we extend *piggul* thought from the domain of human eating to that of altar-consumption, and vice versa. A thought to eat what is normally burned on the altar, or to burn on the altar what is normally eaten, still constitutes a relevant *piggul* thought, because all parts of the sacrifice are united in the framework of *k'vod hakorban*.

The source for Rabbi Eliezer's position is the *Mishnah* later in the tractate: *ha'shochet et hazevach le'echol davar she'ein darko le'echol, le'haktir davar she'ein darko le'haktir, kasher* — if one slaughters the sacrifice with the thought of eating what is not normally eaten (e.g., hooves, or *emurim*), or burning what is not normally burned, it is valid (*kasher*) — meaning no *piggul* is created. *Rabbi Eliezer posel* — Rabbi Eliezer says it is invalid, meaning such thoughts do create *piggul*. Rabbi Eliezer takes the more expansive view that any consumption-related thought regarding any part of the sacrifice is relevant.

### Abaye Clarification

Abaye intervenes with a crucial clarification: *afilu teima Rabbanan* — even according to the *Rabbanan*, the Gemara's inference need not be read as implying that eating the *emurim* would combine. Abaye reframes the inference entirely.

The *Mishnah* says that eating half a *k'zayis* plus burning half a *k'zayis* does not combine. Abaye says: do not read the implied positive case as "eating the *emurim* would combine." Rather, read the implied positive as: if instead of burning the *emurim*, there was a plan to *eat* another half-*k'zayis* of meat — that is, the normal food portion — those two human-eating halves would combine. The *Mishnah*'s contrast is between *eating and burning* (which do not combine) versus *eating and eating* (which do combine). The combining pair must consist of two acts of the same natural category, each natural in its own domain.

The Gemara asks: but then what *chiddush* does the *Mishnah* add? If the answer to the positive case is simply that two halves of human eating combine, we already knew that from the *reisha* of the *Mishnah*, which teaches that a half-*k'zayis* eaten outside its place plus a half-*k'zayis* eaten the next day (*pasul* for being outside its time) combine and render the offering *pasul*. The Gemara responds: the *chiddush* of the *Mishnah* is precisely the negative — *she'ein achilah v'haktarah mitztarfin* — human eating and altar-burning do not combine, even though each is the natural act for its respective portion of the sacrifice. One might have thought: since eating the meat is natural and burning the *emurim* is natural, and each half represents the appropriate designated act for its part of the sacrifice, they should combine. The *Mishnah* tells us they do not.

*Salka da'atach amina hasam hu d'lav k'orchei kamachshev* — one might have supposed that the reason eating the *emurim* does not form *piggul* (per the *reisha*) is because it is not the natural act for the *kohen*. But here, where burning the *emurim* is the natural act for the altar, and eating the meat is the natural act for the human, one might have said they should combine — *eima litztaref*. The *Mishnah* teaches us otherwise: *kamashmalan*. Two natural acts in two different categories do not combine. For combination (*tzeiruf*) of half-*k'zayis* thoughts to yield *piggul*, both halves must be of the same type of act.

## Third Perek Opens

### Invalid Servers OK

*Hadrach alach kol hazvachim shekiblu damam* — we have completed the second *perek* and now begin the third. The new *Mishnah* opens: *kol hapesulim sheshachtu, shchitatam ksheira* — all persons who are normally disqualified from performing the Temple service, if they performed the *shechita* (the slaughter of the sacrifice), their *shechita* is valid. The list includes: one who is *tamei*, an *onen* (one in acute mourning), one who is not standing on the ground, a non-*kohen* (*zar*), and others. The reason is that *shechita* is inherently valid even when performed by persons who would be disqualified from other aspects of the *avodah* — *shehashechita ksheira b'zarim u'vnashim u'vavadim u'vtme'im* — *shechita* is valid when performed by non-*kohanim*, by women, by servants, and by *teme'im*.

The consequence follows: *lefikach hein poslim bemachshava* — therefore, because their *shechita* is valid, their *piggul* thoughts are legally operative. The law of *piggul* does not require a *kohen*; it requires only that the person performing the act be one whose performance is valid. Since all these individuals may validly perform *shechita*, their intent during *shechita* has halachic consequence, including the capacity to generate *piggul*.

### Women & Zarim OK

The *Mishnah* adds that this applies *afilu bekodshei kodashim* — even for offerings of the highest sanctity, *kodshei kodashim*, such as the *olah* and *chatat*, which must be slaughtered in the inner courtyard (*azarah*) near the altar. One might have thought that the heightened sanctity and the specific spatial requirements of *kodshei kodashim* would restrict *shechita* to qualified *kohanim*. The *Mishnah* tells us this is not so. The *Mishnah* adds: *u'vivlad shelo yehu tme'im nogim babasar* — the one condition is that *teme'im* must not come into physical contact with the meat and render it *tamei*.

### Tosafot on Women

Before proceeding to the next section of the *Mishnah*, Tosafot raises an important point: *hagemara muvchach denashim shochtot afilu lechatchila* — the Gemara demonstrates that women may perform *shechita* even *lechatchila*, not merely *b'diavad*. The *Mishnah*'s language of "if they slaughtered" (*sheshachtu*) in the past tense refers specifically to the *tamei* persons — for whom *lechatchila* is indeed inappropriate, since they risk contaminating the meat. But women are not subject to that concern, and thus the *lechatchila* permission for women is unrestricted.

Tosafot then cites this as a *teshuvah* — a formal rebuttal — to a ruling in a work called *Hilchos Eretz Yisrael*, which stated that women should not perform *shechita* because their minds are unreliable (*da'atan kalah*). Tosafot notes that this work, whose authority and provenance were questionable — likely originating from a pietistic or Kabbalistic circle — also contained the ruling that a *shechita* performed without reciting a *berachah* is *pasul*, and that if the *shochet* was unclothed at the time, the *shechita* is invalid. Both of these rulings are plainly contrary to accepted *halacha* — proper *shechita* remains valid regardless of whether a *berachah* was recited, and the physical state of the *shochet* does not affect the validity of the act. Tosafot concludes that the rulings in that work are merely personal *chumros* of whatever scholar composed it, not binding *halacha*.

This discussion reflects a broader phenomenon in the Tosafot literature: the *Rishonim* of Ashkenaz wrote in a social context where women were active participants in commerce and public life, and Tosafot frequently engages with *halachic* questions arising from that reality — whether women may be in circumstances of potential *yichud* due to business necessity, whether women may recite a *berachah* on time-bound positive commandments (*mitzvas asei shehazman grama*), and so on. Whether women were in practice actually serving as *shochatat* is a separate historical question, but Tosafot clearly feels the need to address the matter on principle.

### Tamei Access Problem

A logistical difficulty arises regarding *tamei* persons and *kodshei kodashim*. The *Mishnah* says that even *teme'im* may perform *shechita* of *kodshei kodashim* — but *kodshei kodashim* must be slaughtered inside the *azarah*, and a *tamei* person is prohibited from entering the *azarah*. How, then, can a *tamei* person perform this *shechita*?

Tosafot engages with this problem and notes that the solution depends on the precise *level* of *tumah* involved. Some categories of *tamei* persons can stand in the *Ezrat Nashim* but not in the inner *azarah*; others cannot enter even the *Ezrat Nashim*. For those who cannot enter the *Ezrat Nashim* at all, Tosafot suggests that perhaps they could stand just outside the boundary of the *azarah* near a small opening and position the animal close enough to the entryway that *shechita* could be performed — with a sufficiently long instrument and the animal placed right at the threshold. The *azarah*'s boundaries are defined by its structural walls, not by a surrounding radius. The *tamei* person need not be inside; the animal, however, must be inside for the *shechita* to count as performed in the proper location. Tosafot works through the geometry of this logistical difficulty without arriving at a fully seamless resolution, but the principle stands: somehow, the *shechita* can be accomplished by a *tamei* person without that person entering the prohibited space.
