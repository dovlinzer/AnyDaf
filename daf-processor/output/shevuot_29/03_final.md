# Shevuot 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Daf 29 Overview

### Daf 29 Context

Daf 29 is the final daf of the third *perek* of Shevuot. With it, we wrap up the first two *perakim*, which focused on being *tamei* and entering the *Beis HaMikdash* — a theme we have discussed at length in terms of its place within this *masechet*. The third *perek* is concerned, at its core, with the *korban* that one brings when taking an oath and then violating it *beshogeg*, whether with respect to the future or to the past. But as we have seen, it also lays down the foundational parameters of oath law more broadly.

### Foundation Concepts

The *perek* has established the basic mechanics of *shevuot*: the difference between a *shevua* and a *neder*, the rule of *ein shevua chala al shevua* — that one oath cannot take hold upon another — and the concept of *mushba ve'omed*, that one who is already "standing under" an existing oath cannot create a new obligation by taking an additional one on the same matter. Similarly, we encountered the principle that a *shevua levatel es hamitzvah* — a *shevua* taken to negate a *mitzvah* obligation — does not take effect.

### Transition to Courts

Beginning with the next *perek*, our focus will shift toward the role of *shevuot* within *Seder Nezikin*: the use of oaths in the context of court deliberations, litigation, and the relationship between two parties. Questions will arise such as when one litigant is entitled to impose an oath upon the other, and what the consequences are if that oath is not taken. That discussion begins tomorrow. Today, however, we complete the third *perek*'s analysis of what makes an oath valid, binding, and consequential.

## Shevuat Shav

### Three Oath Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵיזוֹ הִיא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא? נִשְׁבַּע לְשַׁנּוֹת אֶת הַיָּדוּעַ לָאָדָם – אָמַר עַל הָעַמּוּד שֶׁל אֶבֶן שֶׁהוּא שֶׁל זָהָב, וְעַל הָאִישׁ שֶׁהוּא אִשָּׁה, וְעַל הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהִיא אִישׁ;
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Which** oath **is an oath** taken **in vain,** mentioned in the previous mishna (27b)? It is when one **takes an oath to deny that which is known to people** to be true, for example, one **says about a stone column that it is** made **of gold, or about a man that he is a woman, or about a woman that she is a man.**

The *Mishnah* on 29a opens with the question: *eizehi shevuat shav?* — what constitutes a *shevuat shav*, an oath taken in vain? To appreciate this, it helps to situate it against the categories we have already encountered. A *shevuat bituy* is future-oriented: if violated *beshogeg*, one brings a *korban*; if violated *bemezid*, one receives lashes. A past-oriented oath, when violated *bemezid*, constitutes a *shevuat sheker* — a false oath. If violated *beshogeg*, there is a debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael as to whether a *korban* is brought at all. What we have not yet examined is the third category: *shevuat shav*, against which the verse reads *lo tisa et shem Hashem Elokecha lashav* — do not take God's name in vain, as distinct from *lasheker*, which is false.

### Known Falsehoods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁבַּע עַל דָּבָר שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ – "אִם לֹא רָאִיתִי גָּמָל שֶׁפּוֹרֵחַ בָּאֲוִיר" וְ"אִם לֹא רָאִיתִי נָחָשׁ כְּקוֹרַת בֵּית הַבַּד";
>
> **Translation:** Another type of oath taken in vain is when one **takes an oath about a matter that is impossible,** e.g., if he says: **If I did not see a camel flying through the air, or: If I did not see a snake** as large **as the beam of the olive press.**

The *Mishnah* defines *shevuat shav* beginning with a case of *nishba lishanot es hayaduah l'adam* — swearing to claim something is different from what is well known to people. The example given is: *amar al amud shel even shehu shel zahav* — one has a pillar of stone and takes an oath that it is gold. One might have assumed this was a *shevuat sheker*, a false oath, but the distinction being drawn is that something *transparently* false is not a meaningful lie — it does not deceive — and therefore the oath serves no purpose. It is, in that sense, vain. A lie that cannot function as a lie is not *sheker*; it is *shav*. The *Mishnah* similarly includes swearing that a man is a woman or that a woman is a man.

### Impossible Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְעֵדִים: "בּוֹאוּ וְהַעִידוּנִי," "שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא נְעִידֶךָ"; נִשְׁבַּע לְבַטֵּל אֶת הַמִּצְוָה – שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲשׂוֹת סוּכָּה וְשֶׁלֹּא לִיטּוֹל לוּלָב וְשֶׁלֹּא לְהַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין; זוֹ הִיא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא, שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of one who **said to witnesses: Come and testify for me,** and they replied: On our **oath we will not testify for you,** that is an oath taken in vain, because it involves **taking an oath to refrain from** performing **a mitzva.** Other examples of this include an oath **not to build a *sukka*, or not to take a *lulav*, or not to don phylacteries. This** type of oath **is an oath taken in vain, for** which one is **liable** to receive **lashes** if he takes the oath **intentionally, and for** which he is **exempt** if he takes it **unwittingly.**

The *Mishnah* continues with *nishba al davar she'ee efshar lo* — swearing about something that is impossible. The example given is: *im lo ra'iti gamal porei'ach ba'avir* — if I did not see a camel flying through the air; or claiming to have seen a snake as thick as the beam of an olive press. These are cases of patently impossible claims, though the precise structure of what oath is being taken here will require the *Gemara*'s attention, as we will see shortly.

Worth noting is something conspicuously absent from this *Mishnah*: the case of swearing to something patently *true*. If one swears that two plus two is four, that is also a *shevuat shav* — the oath serves no purpose because the matter is self-evident. This case appears elsewhere and is assumed to be included in *shevuat shav*, but it is not enumerated here, a point to flag.

### Negating Mitzvot

A distinct and important category in the *Mishnah* is *nishba levatel es hamitzvah* — taking an oath to negate a *mitzvah* obligation. The examples given are: *shelo la'asot sukka*, *shelo litol lulav*, *shelo l'haniach tefillin*. Each of these cases falls under *shevuat shav*.

One notices immediately that the *Mishnah* says *shelo la'asot sukka* — not to *build* a *sukka* — rather than *shelo leishev basukka*, not to *dwell* in it. One does not have a technical obligation to build a *sukka* oneself. This phrasing prompted the *Netziv* to develop the concept of *hachshar mitzvah hakatuv baTorah*: the Torah's verse *chag hasukkot ta'aseh lecha* implies a value in the making of the *sukka* even absent a strict obligation, such that this too carries some weight as a *kiyum mitzvah*. Most *Rishonim*, however, treat the phrase as *lav davka* — not to be read precisely — and assume the *Mishnah* means *shelo leishev basukka*. Still, the observation is worth making.

### Penalties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאוֹכַל כִּכָּר זוֹ," "שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה" – הָרִאשׁוֹנָה שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא.
>
> **Translation:** If one said: On my **oath I will eat this loaf,** and later said: On my **oath I will not eat it, the first** oath is **an oath on an utterance, and the second** is **an oath** taken **in vain,** as he took an oath to perform an action that would violate his previous oath.

With regard to penalties: *zo hi shevuat shav shechayavin al zedonah malkot* — if one willfully takes a *shevuat shav*, one receives lashes. *Ve'al shigagatah patur* — if done in error or unawareness, one is exempt. Crucially, one does not bring a *korban* for *shevuat shav* under any circumstances. The *korban* is reserved for *shevuat bituy* in the future, and possibly the past — but not for *shevuat shav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲכָלָהּ – עָבַר עַל שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא. לֹא אֲכָלָהּ – עָבַר עַל שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** If **he ate it, he violated** the prohibition against taking **an oath in vain.** If **he did not eat it, he violated** the prohibition against breaking **an oath on an utterance.**

The *Mishnah* also addresses a paired case: *shevua she'ochal kikar zo, shevua shelo ochelenah* — first swearing to eat a loaf of bread, then swearing not to eat it. The *Mishnah* identifies the first as a *shevuat bituy* and the second as a *shevuat shav*, since the second oath negates the binding obligation created by the first. The *Mishnah* then states: *achalah* — if he ate it — he has violated the *shevuat shav* (though he fulfilled his *shevuat bituy*); *lo achalah* — if he did not eat it — he has violated the *shevuat bituy*. The implication of the *Mishnah* seems to be that the two oaths stand in tension and one will inevitably be violated. The *Gemara* will challenge this framing, and *Tosafot* raises a preliminary question about whether these two oaths are truly contradictory in the first place — after all, one might fulfill the first by eating a *kazayit* and fulfill the second by not eating the entire loaf. *Tosafot* does not resolve this cleanly but suggests that "I will eat it" may connote eating the whole thing, while "I will not eat it" may mean not eating any of it. In any case, it is the *Gemara* that will probe the deeper difficulty.

## Obviousness Standard

### Ulla's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עוּלָּא: וְהוּא שֶׁנִּיכָּר לִשְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to an oath taken in vain that denies that which is known to people, **Ulla says:** It is considered known to people when **it is** a fact **that is known to three people.**

The *Gemara* now takes up the question of what "well known" means for the purpose of *shevuat shav*. *Amar Ulla: vehu shenikar ligimel bnei adam* — Ulla teaches that it does not need to be transparently false to everyone; it suffices that three people know the truth of the matter. So if three people know that a pillar is made of stone — even though most observers believe it is gold because it has been painted to look so — and one swears it is gold, that is a *shevuat shav* rather than a *shevuat sheker*.

### Challenge to Rule

This is a striking ruling. One would naturally assume that if a person succeeds in deceiving most observers, the oath functions as a genuine lie and ought to constitute *shevuat sheker*. The apparent logic of the three-person standard is that facts known to three people are, in principle, publicly accessible — they can eventually come to light. But this seems deeply insufficient as a criterion. A lie can deceive a thousand people even though the facts are theoretically discoverable. The distinction between *shav* and *sheker* here seems to rest on an idealized notion of epistemic accessibility that may not correspond to social reality, particularly in an age where demonstrable falsehoods are repeated and believed with great conviction. The argument that "in theory, people could find out" is a thin basis for removing the offense from the category of *sheker*.

### What Counts as Known

One might further ask: how contextual is this three-person standard? If three members of one's family witnessed something and one swears publicly to the contrary, does that qualify as *shav* rather than *sheker* because three people know? The more defensible reading is that the three-person rule applies in a context where the knowledge is sufficiently available in the relevant setting — where one would ordinarily expect the truth to be discoverable in the community in which the oath is being taken. A purely private or confined awareness likely does not satisfy the threshold. That is how one would prefer to read the principle, though the *Gemara* does not spell this out explicitly.

## Impossible Oaths

### Oath Content Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁבַּע עַל דָּבָר שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ – "אִם לֹא רָאִיתִי גָּמָל פּוֹרֵחַ בָּאֲוִיר". "שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁרָאִיתִי" לָא קָאָמַר, מַאי "אִם לֹא רָאִיתִי"? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: תָּנֵי "שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁרָאִיתִי". רָבָא אָמַר: בְּאוֹמֵר "יֵאָסְרוּ כׇּל פֵּירוֹת שֶׁבְּעוֹלָם עָלַי, אִם לֹא רָאִיתִי גָּמָל פּוֹרֵחַ בָּאֲוִיר".
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna describes an oath taken in vain where one **takes an oath about a matter that is impossible,** e.g., where one says: **If I did not see a flying camel.** The Gemara points out: The *tanna* of the mishna **did not say:** On my **oath I saw. What** is the meaning of: **If I did not see? Abaye said:** Emend the language of the mishna and **teach:** On my **oath I saw. Rava said:** This is part of a larger statement **where one says: Let all the produce in the world be prohibited for me if I did not see a camel flying through the air.**

The *Gemara* now returns to the case of the camel flying. The difficulty is pointed: *shevua she'ra'iti lo ka'amar* — the person did not say "I swear I saw a flying camel." The language in the *Mishnah* is *im lo ra'iti* — "if I did not see." This is conditional language, not a direct oath. So what exactly is the *shevua* here, and why does it constitute *shevuat shav*?

### Abuye vs. Rava

*Abaye* resolves this by proposing an alternative *girsa* — a different textual reading: *tani shevua she'ra'iti* — simply emend the *Mishnah* to read that the person swore that he *did* see a flying camel. That is patently false, and the case straightforwardly fits the category of *shevuat shav*. *Rava*, by contrast, offers a different reading of the conditional formulation: *ye'asru kol peirot sheba'olam alai bishevua im lo ra'iti gamal porei'ach ba'avir* — what the person is saying is: "All the fruit of the world shall be forbidden to me by oath if I did not see a camel fly." Since in fact he did not see one, the condition is triggered, and he has taken a *shevua* never to eat any of the world's produce.

### Tossafot's View

*Tosafot* finds the *Gemara*'s treatment here difficult and clarifies the point: the truly significant element is not the flying camel — that is essentially incidental — but rather the oath to refrain from all fruit forever, which is practically impossible. The analogy offered in the *Gemara* elsewhere is an oath never to sleep: the person receives lashes for *shevuat shav* immediately, and is permitted to sleep immediately, because such an oath cannot be fulfilled. The *ee efshar* — the impossibility — refers not to the camel's flight but to the oath's subject matter, namely the commitment to never consume any food. As *Tosafot* acknowledges, this is a very strained reading of the *Mishnah*, which emphasizes the flying camel prominently and treats it as if it were the salient feature of the case. The real point, according to *Tosafot*, is the category of a *shevua* to do something humanly impossible.

### Neder Shav

This analysis in *Tosafot* prompts an interesting further question: what if the same impossibility were framed as a *neder* rather than a *shevua*? If one said "all fruit of the world is forbidden to me" in a *neder* formulation, the effect is indirect — it forbids the objects rather than directly speaking to one's capacities. This is analogous to a *neder levatel es hamitzvah* — a *neder* that makes a *sukka* forbidden, which takes effect because it operates on the object rather than directly contradicting the obligation. *Tosafot* presents two opinions: one holds that such a *neder* on all food would take effect for the same reason; the other argues that a *neder* whose fulfillment is humanly impossible is not a valid *neder* at all.

This raises a further question: if such a *neder* does not take effect, has the person done anything wrong? Is there a concept of *neder shav*? The *Gemara* itself states simply *nidrei shav lo matzinu* — we have not found the category of a *vow in vain*. The explanation lies in the structural difference between a *shevua* and a *neder*. The prohibition of *shevuat shav* derives from *lo tisa et shem Hashem Elokecha lashav* — it is the invocation of God's name, explicitly or implicitly, that defines the wrong. A *shevua* always involves calling upon God as a witness to one's word; even without pronouncing the divine name aloud, the invocation is present by implication. A *neder*, by contrast, operates by sanctifying or prohibiting objects, without any inherent invocation of God's name. Hence, if a *neder* fails to take effect, there is nothing further that has been violated.

## God's Name Role

### Shevua as God's Witness

This returns us to a theme that has recurred throughout the *perek*: the fundamental character of a *shevua* is not merely a commitment about one's future actions but an act of calling upon God to witness the truth of one's statement. Whether or not one explicitly pronounces the divine name, God's presence as guarantor is always implicit in the structure of a *shevua*. This is what makes *shevuat shav* a violation of *lo tisa et shem Hashem lashav* — one has invoked God's name, directly or by implication, in a vain or false context.

### Shevua vs. Neder

This distinction between *shevua* and *neder* is precisely why *neder shav* is not a recognized category. The *Rambam* reinforces this point when he rules that while an oath without explicit mention of God's name does create a binding obligation, the full range of consequences — *malkus* and *korban* — apply only when God's name or one of His appellations is explicitly invoked. Whether the *Rambam* means that without God's name the violation is only rabbinic, or that it is biblical but lacks sufficient weight to trigger the specific penalties — analogous to a *chatzi shi'ur* — is itself a question worth examining. But the centrality of God's name to the *shevua* is unmistakable throughout.

## Intent & Private Meaning

### Private Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְדִלְמָא הַאי גַּבְרָא צִיפּוֹרָא רַבָּא חֲזִי, וְאַסֵּיק לֵיהּ שְׁמָא "גַּמְלָא", וְכִי קָא מִשְׁתְּבַע – אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דִּידֵיהּ אִישְׁתְּבַע?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the oath: If I did not see a camel flying through the air, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Perhaps this man** who took this oath **saw a great bird and named it** for himself: **Camel, and when he took the oath, he took the oath according to his own understanding.**

The *Gemara* poses a sharp question via *Ravina*: how do we know in any given case that an oath about a camel flying is patently false? Perhaps the person had a large pet bird he nicknamed "Camel," and when he said he saw a camel fly, he was referring to his bird — *ufarkah* — and assigned that meaning perfectly consistently with his own private linguistic framework. *Ve'chi kamishtava ada'ata didei kamishtava* — when he takes an oath, perhaps he takes it on the basis of his own private meaning? This is a natural extension of the well-established principle that *shevuot* and *nedarim* require *piv ve'libo shavin* — that one's words and one's intention must be aligned — which implies that what one *means* by the words matters, not merely what the words standardly denote.

### Lips vs. Mind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: בָּתַר פּוּמֵּיהּ אָזְלִינַן, וְלָא אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר דַּעְתֵּיהּ – וְהָא תַּנְיָא: כְּשֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: "הֱוֵי יוֹדֵעַ, שֶׁלֹּא עַל דַּעְתְּךָ אָנוּ מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתְךָ, אֶלָּא עַל דַּעְתֵּנוּ וְעַל דַּעַת בֵּית דִּין". מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן: דִּלְמָא אִיסְקוּנְדְּרֵי יְהַיב לֵיהּ וְאַסֵּיק לְהוּ "זוּזֵי", דְּכִי קָא מִשְׁתְּבַע – אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דִּידֵיהּ קָא מִשְׁתְּבַע?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **we follow his mouth,** i.e., the generally accepted meaning of what one says, **and we do not follow his understanding,** i.e., his own private meaning, **but isn't it taught** otherwise in a *baraita*: **When they administer an oath to him** in court, the judges **say to him: Know that** it is **not according to your** own **understanding** that **we administer** this **oath to you, but according to our understanding and according to the understanding of the court? What is the reason** they say this? **Is it not because we say: Perhaps he gave tokens [*iskunderei*]** to his creditor **but he calls them** for himself: **Dinars,** so **that when he takes the oath, it is according to his own understanding that he takes the oath.** Since, in principle, it is possible for him to take an oath according to his private meaning of the words, the court insists that the oath it administers is to be understood according to the words' generally accepted meaning.

The response is: *batar pumi azlinan velo azlinan batar da'atei* — we follow the lips, not the mind. The principle that intention matters functions to contextualize and clarify; it does not permit a speaker to assign a meaning to words that stands in clear contradiction to what those words normally and standardly mean. There is a difference between private *context* that clarifies an ambiguity, and private *language* that simply overrides the shared meaning of words. The former may be permitted; the latter is not.

### Court Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, הָתָם מִשּׁוּם קַנְיָא דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi replies: **No,** the reason the court admonishes the oath taker is not that the oath can be interpreted according to a private meaning. **There,** when the court administers an oath, they explain that it is according to their meaning **due to** deceptions like that of **the reed** in **Rava's** court. In that incident, someone handed his creditor a hollow reed, which he had secretly filled with coins, to hold for him, and proceeded to take an oath that he had given him the money owed to him, whereupon he took back the reed, as the creditor was unaware of its contents. The court admonishes oath takers so that they do not think that they have fulfilled their obligation to take an oath if they engage in such chicanery.

This principle is reinforced by a *baraita*: *kshe'mashbi'in oto omrim lo hevei yodea shelo al da'atcha anu mashbi'im otcha ela al da'teinu v'al da'at Beit Din* — when *Beit Din* administers an oath, they explicitly warn the person: this oath is not being taken on the basis of your understanding of the words but on the basis of ours, and of the court's. The question is why this warning is necessary. Does the fact that we must preempt the possibility of private meanings not demonstrate that, absent such preemption, one would in fact be entitled to assign private meanings?

The *Gemara* answers: *lo, hasam mishum kanya d'Rava* — no; the warning is directed at a different concern, illustrated by the famous story of *Rava*. A person owed money and denied the debt. Before taking an oath, he secretly placed the money inside a hollowed-out walking stick, handed the stick to the creditor, and then swore that he had "given" the other party the money — which was literally true, in a manner of speaking, since the money was inside the stick now held by the creditor. Here the deception is not through private linguistic meaning but through *context* — the circumstances are constructed so that words true in one sense are understood entirely differently by everyone else. The court's warning addresses this kind of manipulation: not that you may assign idiosyncratic private meanings to words, but that you may not manufacture a context designed to make your words technically accurate while conveying a false impression.

### Context Not Meaning

The conclusion is thus that the concern is not private meaning but constructed context. The standard remains: we go by what the words normally and publicly mean, and no private redefinition overrides that standard.

## Moshe's Oath

### Torah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וְכֵן מָצִינוּ כְּשֶׁהִשְׁבִּיעַ מֹשֶׁה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, אָמַר לָהֶן: "דְּעוּ שֶׁלֹּא עַל דַּעְתְּכֶם אֲנִי מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֶתְכֶם, אֶלָּא עַל דַּעַת הַמָּקוֹם וְעַל דַּעְתִּי". וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא לְהוּ: "קַיִּימוּ מַאי דַּאֲמַר אֱלוֹהַּ"! לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּמַסְּקִי אַדַּעְתַּיְיהוּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **And so we found** that **when Moses administered an oath to Israel** that they would keep the Torah (see Deuteronomy 29:9–12), **he said to them: Know that I am not administering this oath according to your understanding but according to the understanding of the Omnipresent and according to my understanding. And why** not **let him** simply **say to them: Keep that which God said? Is it not** that he insisted on admonishing them **because** he was concerned lest **they direct** the oath **in their minds** to **idolatry,** saying that they are taking an oath of loyalty to God but privately intending the oath to be for idolatry? This indicates that the content of an oath can be affected by an oath taker's private meaning.

The *Gemara* cites a further proof from the account of *Moshe* at *Arvot Moav*, where *Moshe* imposes an oath upon *Bnei Yisrael* — not only upon those present but upon all future generations. *Amar lahen: de'u shelo al da'atchem ani mashbia etchem ela al da'at Hamakom ve'al da'ati* — know that I am not imposing this oath based on what you think the words mean; it is on God's understanding and on mine. The *Gemara* asks why *Moshe* needed to say this. If the concern were private linguistic meanings, he could simply have said: fulfill everything God commanded. But even the most explicit formulations — *kimu kol hamitzvot kulan* — might be given non-private but alternative recognized meanings. For instance, *kol haTorah kula* could be interpreted as the essence of the Torah, namely rejection of *avoda zara*, based on the rabbinic teaching that one who repudiates idolatry is as one who affirms the entire Torah. Similarly, *kimu kol hamitzvot* could be satisfied by adopting *tzitzit*, which the rabbis say is equivalent in weight to all the *mitzvot*. These are not private meanings but legitimate alternative interpretations of the language used.

### Tossafot Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה נָמֵי אִיקְּרִי "אֱלוֹהַּ", דִּכְתִיב: "אֱלֹהֵי כֶסֶף וֵאלֹהֵי זָהָב".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** Moses explicitly indicated that the oath was according to God's and his own understanding **because idolatry is also referred to** with the word: **God, as it is written:** "You shall not make with Me **gods of silver or gods of gold"** (Exodus 20:20). Therefore, the generally accepted meaning of the oath is ambiguous.

*Tosafot* raises a beautiful question here. If *Bnei Yisrael* were not going to keep the *mitzvot* commanded by God, why would a *shevua* to keep them make any difference? *Tosafot* phrases it: *ktzat kashe mai mahanya lehu hashevuah* — it is somewhat difficult: what does the oath accomplish? If they feared they would commit *avoda zara*, how much more so should they fear violating an oath? If they would transgress divine command, would they honor their own sworn word?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לְהוּ: "קַיִּימוּ תּוֹרָה"! חֲדָא תּוֹרָה. וְלֵימָא: "קַיִּימוּ שְׁתֵּי תּוֹרוֹת"! "תּוֹרַת חַטָּאת" "תּוֹרַת אָשָׁם". "קַיִּימוּ כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ"! עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דְּאָמַר מָר: חֲמוּרָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁכׇּל הַכּוֹפֵר בָּהּ כְּמוֹדֶה בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: If private meanings do not affect the content of an oath, **let Moses merely say to them: Keep the Torah.** The Gemara explains: They could then have taken an oath to keep only **one Torah,** either the Written or the Oral Torah. The Gemara suggests: **And let him say: Keep two Torahs.** The Gemara explains: The word: Torah, also has a narrower meaning of a set of halakhic procedures. The Jewish people could then have limited the oath to **the law of [*torat*] the sin-offering** or **the law of [*torat*] the guilt-offering.** The Gemara suggests: Let Moses administer an oath to **keep the entire Torah.** The Gemara replies: That could have been understood as referring only to the prohibition of **idolatry, as the Master says:** The prohibition of **idolatry is** so **severe that** with regard to **one who denies** idol worship, **it is as if he affirms the entire Torah.**

### Pachad Yitzchak

The answer, and it is a compelling one, is psychological and philosophical. The *Pachad Yitzchak* — Rav Yitzchak Hutner — extends this reasoning in a striking direction. He addresses the same question with respect to *Ma'amad Har Sinai* itself: why did *Bnei Yisrael* need to say "we will do" and "we will listen"? The *mitzvot* were being commanded by God regardless — what additional weight did their own acceptance carry? He argues that this points toward an irreducible category of self-imposed obligation that is independent of divine command. If all ethical obligation derived solely from God's command, then a person's own self-commitment would add nothing. But there is evidently something qualitatively distinct about the experience of standing by one's own word — of one's integrity being on the line — that is not reducible to obedience to an external command. The *shevua* does not merely replicate the divine command; it introduces a different register of obligation, one rooted in the person's own self-definition. This is why it can function as additional motivation even for someone already bound by *mitzvot*.

### Prevents Nullification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לְהוּ: "קַיִּימוּ מִצְוָה"! חֲדָא מִצְוָה. "קַיִּימוּ מִצְוֹת!" תַּרְתֵּי. "כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת כּוּלָּן"! מִצְוַת צִיצִית, דְּאָמַר מָר: שְׁקוּלָה מִצְוַת צִיצִית כְּנֶגֶד כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let him say to them: Keep** the **mitzva,** i.e., that which you are commanded. The Gemara replies: That could have been understood as referring to only **one mitzva.** The Gemara suggests: Let him say: **Keep mitzvot,** i.e., in the plural. The Gemara replies: That could be understood as referring to only **two** mitzvot. The Gemara suggests: Let him say: **All the mitzvot in their entirety.** The Gemara replies: That could be understood as referring to **the mitzva of ritual fringes, as the Master says: The mitzva of ritual fringes is equivalent to all the** other **mitzvot.**

The *Gemara* ultimately provides its own explanation for why *Moshe* said *al da'at Hamakom ve'al da'ati*: *ki heichi d'lo tehei hafara lishevu'asaihu* — so that there could be no *hatara*, no annulment, of the oath. The mechanism of *hatarat nedarim* works by demonstrating that, given the actual circumstances, one would not have taken the oath. But when the oath is declared to be binding on *God's da'at* and on *Moshe's da'at*, not merely on the swearer's own intent, the swearer cannot approach a *chacham* and claim "had I known, I would not have committed to this" — because the bindingness was never predicated solely on the swearer's intent. It was anchored in a will external to the swearer's own, and that external will cannot be undone by the swearer's claim of regret. This is the primary function of *al da'at Hamakom ve'al da'ati* — not to determine the *meaning* of the words but to constrain the *unwinding* of the commitment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לְהוּ: "קַיִּימוּ שְׁשׁ מֵאוֹת וּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִצְוֹת"! וּלְטַעֲמֵיךְ, לֵימָא לְהוּ: "עַל דַּעְתִּי"; "עַל דַּעַת הַמָּקוֹם" לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let him say to them: Keep 613 mitzvot.** The Gemara replies: **And according to your reasoning, let him** simply **say:** Take the oath **according to my understanding. Why do I** need for him to say: **According to the understanding of the Omnipresent?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּהְוֵי הֲפָרָה לִשְׁבוּעֲתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Moses administered the oath in this manner **so that there would not be** any possibility of the **nullification of their oath.** An oath taken with public consent cannot be dissolved. In this case, Moses and God constitute the public that gives its consent to the oath administered to the people. This was the reason for Moses' insistence that the oath was taken according to his and God's awareness; it was not because there was a possibility that the people could take the oath according to a private meaning.

## Cherem & Waivers

### Cherem Question

*Tosafot* raises the practical application of this principle in the context of medieval communal *cherem*. The classic formula used when communities enacted binding enactments — including *Cherem Rabbeinu Gershom* — employed the phrase *al da'as hakadosh baruch hu ve'al da'as hakahal*, on God's understanding and on the community's. Since this formula invokes *da'as rabim* — collective intent — the rule *neder she'hu al da'as rabim ein lo hafara* would seem to apply: a *neder* made on collective intent cannot be annulled. Yet *Cherem Rabbeinu Gershom* was in practice sometimes waived under extenuating circumstances. How is that possible?

### Waiver Discretion

*Tosafot* answers: *da'as hakahal kach hu* — this is precisely *what* the collective intent is. The invocation of *da'as rabim* achieves two things: first, it prevents the individual subject to the *cherem* from unilaterally seeking a *hatara* on his own; second, it vests complete discretion in the *kahal* — or the rabbis as their representatives — to waive the obligation whenever and however they see fit, without requiring any formal process of *charata*. It is not that the *Beit Din* must go through the usual process of demonstrating that had they known the circumstances they would not have imposed the *cherem*. The *da'as rabim* is precisely the authority of the collective to determine, at their discretion, when a waiver is appropriate. The individual cannot undo it; the collective can.

### God's Da'at Purpose

As for the invocation of God's name in the communal *cherem* — *tiknu hageonim lechumra be'alma* — the *Geonim* instituted the inclusion of *da'as Hamakom* to add weight and solemnity to the enactment. But unlike *Moshe's* invocation, which was made at God's explicit instruction, the communal *cherem* is ultimately grounded in *da'as hakahal* alone. The reference to God is a stringency, not essential to the legal mechanism. *Aval charamos shenigzaru hakol taluy beda'as hakahal lachud* — as far as the law is concerned, it is the community's intent that is dispositive.

## Contradictory Oaths

### Bread Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הָעוֹנֶה "אָמֵן" אַחַר שְׁבוּעָה – כְּמוֹצִיא שְׁבוּעָה בְּפִיו דָּמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: "וְאָמְרָה הָאִשָּׁה אָמֵן אָמֵן".
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says: Anyone who answers amen after** being administered **an oath is like one who expresses an oath with his** own **mouth, as it is written** in the context of the oath administered to a *sota*, a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful: **"And the woman shall say: Amen, amen"** (Numbers 5:22).

The *Mishnah* presents the case of *shevua she'ochal kikar zo, shevua shelo ochelenah* — swearing to eat a particular loaf of bread and then swearing not to eat it. The *Mishnah* identifies the first as a *shevuat bituy* and the second as a *shevuat shav* — since the second oath attempts to negate an obligation already created by the first. The *Mishnah* then states: *achalah* — if he ate it — he violated the *shevuat shav*; *lo achalah* — if he did not eat it — he violated the *shevuat bituy*.

### When Does It Become Shav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי נוֹהֶגֶת בַּאֲנָשִׁים וּבְנָשִׁים, בִּקְרוֹבִים וּבִרְחוֹקִים, בִּכְשֵׁרִין וּבִפְסוּלִין, בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ. וְחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ קׇרְבָּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: As opposed to the *halakhot* of an oath of testimony, which will be discussed in the following chapter, the *halakhot* of **an oath on an utterance apply to men and to women, to relatives and to non-relatives, to those who are fit** to testify **and to those who are disqualified,** whether the oath is taken **in the presence of a court or not in the presence of a court,** i.e., when one takes an oath **on his own,** at his own initiative. **And for** violating an oath **intentionally** one is **liable** to receive **lashes, and for** doing so **unwittingly** he is liable to bring **a sliding-scale offering.**

The *Gemara* presses the difficulty that Michael flagged: if the second oath was already a *shevuat shav* the moment it left his lips — *harei yatza shevua leshav* — then if he does not eat the bread, why has he violated anything? The *shevuat shav* was never binding, and all he has done is fail to eat what his first oath required. There should be no violation of the second oath because the second oath was always void. The *Gemara* thus asks: *mishum shevuat shav lo mechayev?* Did he not transgress anything with respect to the *shevuat shav*?

### Both Oaths Stand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא נוֹהֶגֶת בַּאֲנָשִׁים וּבְנָשִׁים, בִּרְחוֹקִים וּבִקְרוֹבִים, בִּכְשֵׁרִין וּבִפְסוּלִין, בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – וּמִפִּי עַצְמוֹ. וְחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Liability for **an oath** taken **in vain applies to men and to women, to relatives and to non-relatives, to those who are fit** to bear witness **and to those who are disqualified,** whether the oath is taken **in the presence of a court or not in the presence of a court, and** also when one takes an oath **on his own. And for** violating an oath **intentionally** one is **liable** to receive **lashes, and for** doing so **unwittingly** he is **exempt.**

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* resolves this: *tani af al shvuat bituy* — both oaths effectively stand and operate in parallel. One always has the transgression of the *shevuat shav* — it was uttered in vain the moment it was spoken — and the question is only whether one will *additionally* violate the *shevuat bituy*. If he eats the bread, he violates the *shevuat shav* and fulfills the *shevuat bituy*. If he does not eat it, he has the *shevuat shav* transgression regardless, and additionally violates the *shevuat bituy*. There is no situation in which he escapes both violations: the *shevuat shav* is violated simply by having been uttered, and the *shevuat bituy* is violated one way or the other depending on his action.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ, הַמּוּשְׁבָּע מִפִּי אֲחֵרִים חַיָּיב – "אִם לֹא אָכַלְתִּי הַיּוֹם" וְ"לֹא הִנַּחְתִּי תְּפִלִּין הַיּוֹם"; "מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי", וְאָמַר "אָמֵן" – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **both this,** an oath on an utterance, **and that,** an oath taken in vain, even if **he is administered the oath by others,** he is **liable.** For example, if one said: **If I did not eat today, or: I did not don phylacteries today,** and another said to him: **I administer an oath to you** that your statement is true, **and** the former **said: Amen,** he is **liable** if the statement was false.

## God's Name Role…

### Rambam's Categories

At this point it is worth citing the *Rambam*'s systematic account of *shevuat shav*, which he divides into four categories: *ha'achas shenishba al davar yadua she'ein kein* — swearing about something known to be untrue; *hashniya shenishba al davar yadua she'ein bo safek l'adam shehu kein* — swearing to affirm something that is entirely beyond doubt and requires no oath; *hashlishit shenishba levatel es hamitzvah* — swearing to negate a *mitzvah*; and *harevi'it shenishba al davar she'ein lo koach bah la'asoto* — swearing to do something beyond one's physical capacity, such as never to sleep or never to eat. The second category — the patently true oath — is notably absent from our *Mishnah* but is confirmed here by the *Rambam* as a genuine category of *shevuat shav*. Equally notable is that the *Rambam* does not enumerate *ain shevua chala al shevua* — the redundant oath — as a category of *shevuat shav*, which is itself an interesting omission.

On the question of God's name, the *Rambam* rules: if one says *alah*, *arur*, or *shevua* without mentioning God's name, the thing sworn is nevertheless prohibited. However, one does not receive *malkus* and does not bring a *korban* for violating such an oath unless it included an explicit divine name or one of God's appellations. This underscores how central God's name is to the full category of *shevua*: without explicit invocation, the oath may still be halakhically binding, but the severe penalties are reserved for the case where God's name is directly engaged. Whether this reflects a merely rabbinic level of obligation absent God's name, or a biblical obligation of lesser weight — insufficient to trigger *korban* or *malkus* but real nonetheless, in a manner analogous to *chatzi shi'ur* — remains a meaningful question.

## Shevuat Bituy

### Scope

The *Mishnah* turns to a summary of the scope and penalties of *shevuat bituy*. *Noheget beanshim uvenashim* — it applies to both men and women; *bikrovim uverechokim* — to relatives and non-relatives alike. The mention of relatives here anticipates the contrast with *shevuat ha'edut* in the next *perek*, where a relative cannot be a valid witness and an oath regarding testimony from a relative is therefore not binding as a *shevuat ha'edut*. *Rashi* explains that the example of relatives here means something like: whether you swear to give money to your brother or to a stranger, the oath is equally binding. *Bichsherim uvisulim* — whether the oath-taker is halakhically fit to give testimony or disqualified, as in the case of a *ganav*.

### Jurisdiction

*Bifnei Beit Din ushelo bifnei Beit Din* — the oath operates whether taken in the presence of the court or outside of it. *Mipi atzmo* — it applies only if the oath was self-initiated. The *Mishnah* adds this qualification, which will generate apparent tension with the end of the *Mishnah*, as the *Gemara* will note.

---

## Shevuat Bituy…

### Penalties

*Vechayavin al zedonah malkus ve'al shigagatah korban oleh veyored* — one who willfully violates a *shevuat bituy* receives lashes; one who unintentionally violates it brings the variable-cost *korban*.

---

## Shevuat Shav Scope

### Full Scope

A *shevuat shav* likewise applies to men and women, relatives and non-relatives, those who are *kasher* to testify and those who are *pasul*. In these respects it parallels *shevuat bituy* exactly.

### Jurisdiction

Similarly, *shevuat shav* applies both *bifnei Beit Din* and outside of *Beit Din*. And here too, it applies only when self-initiated — *mipi atzmo*.

### Penalties

*Vechayavin al zedonah malkus ve'al shigagatah patur* — willful violation of *shevuat shav* brings lashes; unintentional violation incurs no penalty at all — no *korban*, no lashes. This stands in direct contrast to *shevuat bituy*, where unintentional violation triggers the *korban*.

## Imposed vs. Self

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, תָּנֵי: אַף עַל שְׁבוּעַת בִּיטּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said: Teach** the mishna with this emendation: If he did not eat it, he **also** violated the prohibition **against** breaking **an oath on an utterance.**

Having stated in both contexts that oaths apply only *mipi atzmo* — when self-initiated — the *Mishnah* then appears to reverse course. It states: *achat zu ve'achat zu hamushba mipi acherim chayav* — whether the oath of *shevuat bituy* or of *shevuat shav*, one is *chayav* even if the oath was imposed by others. This seems to directly contradict the requirement of *mipi atzmo* that was just stipulated. Which is it?

### Shmuel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין וּבָרָיְיתָא נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: שְׁבוּעַת הָעֵדוּת נוֹהֶגֶת בַּאֲנָשִׁים וְלֹא בְּנָשִׁים, בִּרְחוֹקִים וְלֹא בִּקְרוֹבִים, בִּכְשֵׁרִין וְלֹא בִּפְסוּלִין; וְאֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא בִּרְאוּיִן לְהָעִיד, וּבִפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ; וּמִפִּי אֲחֵרִים אֵינָן חַיָּיבִין, עַד שֶׁיִּכְפְּרוּ בָּהֶן בְּבֵית דִּין. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava:** An inference from **the mishna and a *baraita* also** support this when they are read **precisely** to support this point. **As it is taught** in the mishna: The *halakhot* of **an oath on an utterance apply to men and to women, to relatives and to non-relatives, to those who are fit** to testify **and to those who are disqualified,** whether the oath is taken **in the presence of a court or not in the presence of a court,** i.e., when one takes an oath **on his own,** at his own initiative. Ravina states his inference: On his own, **yes,** but administered **by others, no. And** it is **taught** in **the last clause** of the mishna: With regard to **this,** an oath on an utterance, **and that,** an oath taken in vain, even if **he is administered the oath by others,** he is **liable.**

*Shmuel* resolves the tension: *kol ha'oneh amen achar shevua kemotzi shevua mipiv dami* — one who responds *amen* after an oath administered by another is considered as if the oath came from his own lips. Packed into the word *amen* is the full content of what the other person said. When you say *amen*, you have, in effect, pronounced the oath yourself. The proof-text is the *pasuk* regarding a woman who responds *amen amen* to an oath imposed upon her — this is treated as if she herself articulated the oath. The case in the *Mishnah* that describes someone being told "I impose an oath upon you" and responding *amen* thus does not contradict the requirement of *mipi atzmo*; rather, the word *amen* itself constitutes the speech act of self-imposition.

### Confirmation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא בְּבָרַיְיתָא: שְׁבוּעַת הָעֵדוּת כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לְעֵדִים: "בּוֹאוּ וְהַעִידוּנִי"; "שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לְךָ עֵדוּת," אוֹ שֶׁאָמְרוּ: "אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין לָךְ עֵדוּת"; "מַשְׁבִּיעַ אֲנִי עֲלֵיכֶם," וְאָמְרוּ (לוֹ) "אָמֵן" – בֵּין בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין, בֵּין מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ בֵּין מִפִּי אֲחֵרִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁכָּפְרוּ בָּהֶם – חַיָּיבִין. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Before stating the inference from the mishna, Rav Pappa cites the *baraita*. **And** it **is taught in a *baraita*:** With regard to **an oath of testimony, how** is one liable to bring a sliding-scale offering? A person **said to witnesses: Come and testify for me.** They replied: On our **oath we do not know any testimony that concerns you,** i.e., we have no knowledge of the matter. **Alternatively, they said: We do not know any testimony that concerns you,** and he said to them: **I administer an oath to you, and they said to him: Amen. Whether** this oath was taken **in the presence of a court** or **whether** it was **not in the presence of a court, whether** it was taken by each witness **by himself** or **whether** it was administered **by others, once they denied** knowing testimony that they in fact knew, they **are liable;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

*Rav Pappa* in the name of *Rava* adds that this same principle can be derived from the rules of *shevuat ha'edut*, the oath of testimony discussed at the outset of the next *perek*. That *mishnah-baraita* specifies: *noheget beanshim velo benashim* — it applies to men but not women; *berachokim velo bekrovim* — to non-relatives but not relatives; *bichsherim velo bifsulim* — to those fit to testify but not those disqualified. These are the inverse of the categories for *shevuat bituy*. The *baraita* further distinguishes between oath initiated by the witnesses themselves and oath imposed upon them: if the witnesses themselves swore, the oath is binding whether inside or outside the court; if the oath was imposed by others, it is binding only if done *bifnei Beit Din*. This distinction depends on the presence or absence of *amen*. When they say *amen*, the oath functions as self-imposed and operates anywhere; when no *amen* is said, a court-imposed oath requires the formal setting of *Beit Din*.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הָא דְּעָנָה "אָמֵן", הָא דְּלָא עָנָה "אָמֵן"? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** These statements in the mishna are **difficult,** as they contradict **each other. Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from it** that **this** latter clause of the mishna is referring to one **who answered amen** to the administered oath. **That** former clause is referring to one **who did not answer amen** and is therefore liable only in the presence of a court.

This provides the clean reconciliation: *ha deana amen, ha delo ana amen* — when the person said *amen*, it is as if it came from their own lips; when they did not say *amen*, only the court context supplies sufficient authority for the imposed oath to be binding. And a striking point emerges: even without *amen*, a court-administered oath appears to be effective — meaning the person does not need to affirmatively acquiesce in some cases. That is itself a *chiddush*, but it is the separate mechanism of *Beit Din*'s authority, not the principle of *amen* as self-imposition.

## Close Reading

### Diuk Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא שְׁמוּאֵל – מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? דּוּקְיָא דְּמַתְנִיתִין קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** since his statement can be derived from tannaitic sources, **what is Shmuel teaching us?** The Gemara answers: **He is teaching us the precise** reading **of the mishna.** Since that *halakha* is not stated explicitly in the mishna, Shmuel saw fit to state it.

After establishing this halakhic conclusion, the *Gemara* asks: given that the point follows so clearly from a close reading of the *Mishnah* itself — the example given is of someone who says *amen*, and it says *chayav* — what precisely is *Shmuel* teaching us? The answer: *diuka dematnitin ka mashma lan* — he is teaching us the method of *diuk*, close textual reading, and demonstrating that by attending carefully to the precise language of the *Mishnah*, one can extract substantive halakhic conclusions that might otherwise be missed.

### Rambam's Bracha

The *Rambam* applies this same principle — *kol ha'oneh amen kemotzi shevua mipiv dami* — to the laws of *Brachot* in *Hilchot Brachot*. He creates two distinct levels. *Kol ha'oneh amen kemotzi bracha mipiv dami*: if you hear a *bracha* and respond *amen*, that *amen* is considered as if you yourself pronounced the full *bracha* — all the words of the blessing are packed into the word *amen*. A distinct lower level is the *shomea*: *vehashomea harei zeh k'oneh* — one who merely listens, without saying *amen*, is considered as if he responded *amen*. Listening is equivalent to *amen*; *amen* is equivalent to having said the full *bracha*. There are thus two degrees of removal from actually pronouncing the words — and the *Rambam* distinguishes them, because the halakhic consequences may differ, even if both permit one to fulfill the obligation. The *Rambam* clearly derives this graduated structure from the *Gemara*'s discussion here, extending the principle of *amen* as equivalent speech from the domain of *shevuot* into the domain of *brachot*.
