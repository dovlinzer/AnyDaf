# Kiddushin 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiddushin Formulatio (II)

### Six Formulations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ אִשְׁתִּי״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ אֲרוּסָתִי״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ קְנוּיָה לִי״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ שֶׁלִּי״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ בִּרְשׁוּתִי״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ זְקוּקָה לִי״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְלִיתְנִינְהוּ כּוּלְּהוּ כַּחֲדָא! תַּנָּא תְּלָת תְּלָת, שַׁמְעִינְהוּ וְגַרְסִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that if a man says to a woman: **You are hereby my wife,** or: **You are hereby my betrothed,** or: **You are hereby acquired to me,** then **she is betrothed.** If he said to her: **You are hereby mine,** or: **You are hereby under my authority,** or: **You are hereby bound to me,** then **she is betrothed.** The Gemara asks: **But** as the *halakha* is that she is betrothed with regard to both sets of statements, **let** the *baraita* **teach all of them together.** Why does the *baraita* divide these statements into two groups? The Gemara answers: **The *tanna* heard them as two sets of three, and** consequently **he taught them** in that form. He heard each sequence of three cases as a separate *halakha* from his teachers, and therefore he preserved them as two sets of three.

The *gemara* on Kiddushin 6a resumes mid-discussion with a *tanna rabbanan*, continuing the examination of which linguistic formulations (*lashonot*) constitute a valid *ma'amar* of *kiddushin*. The *beraita* presents six formulations, grouped in two sets of three. In the first set: *harei at ishti* ("you are my wife"), *harei at arusati* ("you are my *arusa*"), and *harei at knuya li* ("you are acquired to me") — in any of these cases, she is *mekudeshet*. In the second set: *harei at sheli* ("you are mine"), *harei at birshuti* ("you are in my domain"), and *harei at zekuka li* ("you are bound to me") — these, too, effectuate *kiddushin*. The term *zekuka* carries connotations similar to *zika* in the context of *yibbum*, conveying a sense of being halachically bound or attached.

The *gemara* asks why these six formulations are presented as two separate groupings rather than a single unified list. The answer is simply that this is how the tradition was received: *tana telat shmi'ineihu ve-garsineihu* — he heard them in sets of three and transmitted them that way. Grouping teachings in threes is a familiar mnemonic convention, even when the internal logic might not demand the division.

### Ambiguous Formulations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״מְיוּחֶדֶת לִי״ מַהוּ? ״מְיוֹעֶדֶת לִי״ מַהוּ? ״עֶזְרָתִי״ מַהוּ? ״נֶגְדָּתִי״ מַהוּ? ״עֲצוּרָתִי״ מַהוּ? ״צַלְעָתִי״ מַהוּ? ״סְגוּרָתִי״ מַהוּ? ״תַּחְתַּי״ מַהוּ? ״תְּפוּשָׂתִי״ מַהוּ? ״לְקוּחָתִי״ מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If a man betrothing a woman said: You are hereby **unique to me, what is** the *halakha*? Is this woman betrothed? Similarly, if he said to her: You are hereby **designated to me, what is** the *halakha*? If he said: You are hereby **my helper, what is** the *halakha*? If he said: You are hereby **my counterpart, what is** the *halakha*? If he said: You are hereby **my gathered one, what is** the *halakha*? If he said: You are hereby **my rib, what is** the *halakha*? If he said: You are hereby **my closed one, what is** the *halakha*? If he said: You are hereby **beneath me, what is** the *halakha*? If he said: You are hereby **my seized one, what is** the *halakha*? Finally, if he said: You are hereby **my taken one, what is** the *halakha*?

The *gemara* then turns to a series of more ambiguous formulations and poses the question *mahu* — is this effective or not? The list includes: *meiyuchedet li* ("singled out to me"), *meyu'edet li* ("designated to me," drawing on the language of *ya'adah* from the passage about the *bat la'amah*), *ezrati* ("my helpmate," from *ezer kinegdo* in *Bereishit*), *negdati* ("alongside me"), *atzurati* (from *atzeret*, suggesting being gathered together with him at home), *tzalati* (from *vayikach achat mitzal'otav*, God's taking of the *tzela* from *Adam*), *segurati* (from *vayisgor basar tachtena*, the enclosing of flesh), *tachtai* ("beneath me," from the same verse), and *tefusati* (from the language of *nitpasha* in the context of a *na'ara me'orasa* — carrying connotations of being seized, which, rightly or wrongly, implies the married relationship).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשׁוֹט מִיהָא חֲדָא, דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר ״לְקוּחָתִי״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, מִשּׁוּם ״שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר כִּי יִקַּח אִישׁ אִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Resolve at least one** of these dilemmas, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who says** to a woman: You are hereby **my taken one, she is betrothed, because it is stated: "When a man takes a woman"** (Deuteronomy 24:1).

One of these ambiguous cases admits of a ready resolution. The formulation *lekuchati* — "my taken one" — is validated by a *beraita*: *omer lekuchati harei zu mekudeshet*, because the *Torah* itself uses the root *kicha* to describe betrothal: *ki yikach ish isha*. The term *likuchin* is the *Torah's* own word for this act, and so this formulation clearly works.

### Charufah Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״חֲרוּפָתִי״ מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר ״חֲרוּפָתִי״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁכֵּן בִּיהוּדָה קוֹרִין לָאֲרוּסָה ״חֲרוּפָה״. וִיהוּדָה הָוְיָא רוּבָּא דְּעָלְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If a man says to a woman: You are hereby **my espoused one [*ḥarufati*], what is** the *halakha*? **Come** and **hear, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that with regard to **one who says:** You are hereby **my espoused one, she is betrothed, as in Judea they call a betrothed woman a *ḥarufa*, an espoused woman.** The Gemara asks: **And is Judea most of the world?** Even if this is true in Judea, why should a *halakha* that applies in all locations be based on this local custom?

The *gemara* then raises the case of *charufati* — a term derived from the passage *shifcha nechereset l'ish*, which describes a slave-woman who is *nechereset*, or designated, to a man. Does using this term constitute a valid *ma'amar* of *kiddushin*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: הָאוֹמֵר ״חֲרוּפָתִי״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִיא שִׁפְחָה נֶחֱרֶפֶת לְאִישׁ״. וְעוֹד, בִּיהוּדָה קוֹרִין לָאֲרוּסָה ״חֲרוּפָה״. וִיהוּדָה וְעוֹד לִקְרָא? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: הָאוֹמֵר ״חֲרוּפָה״ בִּיהוּדָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁכֵּן בִּיהוּדָה קוֹרִין לָאֲרוּסָה ״חֲרוּפָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** With regard to **one who says:** You are hereby **my espoused one, she is betrothed, as it is stated: "Who is a maidservant espoused [*neḥerefet*] to a man"** (Leviticus 19:20). This verse means that she is betrothed to a certain man. **And furthermore,** the *baraita* adds another proof for this claim: **In Judea they call a betrothed woman a *ḥarufa*, an espoused woman.** The Gemara asks: **And** is it reasonable to introduce the custom in **Judea** with the term: **And furthermore,** as proof **to** a *halakha* derived from **a verse? Rather,** the Gemara explains that **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** With regard to **one who says:** You are hereby **espoused, in Judea, she is betrothed, as in Judea they call a betrothed woman a *ḥarufa*, an espoused woman.**

A *beraita* is cited: *omer charufati mekudeshet* — yes, it works. The *gemara* initially explains this on the basis that *bi-Yehudah korin l'arusa charufa* — in Judea, *arusa* is colloquially called *charufa*. The *gemara* challenges this: if we already have a *pasuk* supporting the term, why do we additionally need to appeal to regional Judean usage? And conversely, if the *pasuk* alone is sufficient, why does the regional usage matter?

After two rounds of revision, the *gemara* settles on the following reading: *omer charufa bi-Yehudah mekudeshet* — if one uses this formulation specifically *in Judea*, it is effective, because there the word is understood to mean *arusa*, likely by association with the *pasuk*. But if one uses this term outside of Judea, where the word does not carry that meaning, it would presumably not work.

### Context Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּשֶׁאֵין מְדַבֵּר עִמָּהּ עַל עִסְקֵי גִּיטָּהּ וְקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ – מְנָא יָדְעָה מַאי קָאָמַר לַהּ? וְאֶלָּא בִּמְדַבֵּר עִמָּהּ עַל עִסְקֵי גִּיטָּהּ וְקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אָמַר לַהּ נָמֵי,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a general question with regard to all the previously mentioned expressions: **With what are we dealing? If we say** that these dilemmas are referring to a case **where he was not speaking with her about matters of her bill of divorce or her betrothal,** but suddenly issued this statement to her, **from where does she know what he is saying to her?** Out of context, these statements are not necessarily referring to betrothal. **Rather,** they are referring to a case **where he was speaking to her about matters of her bill of divorce and her betrothal.** But if so, **even though he did not say** anything, she would **also** be betrothed if he gave her money for the purpose of betrothal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הָיָה מְדַבֵּר עִם אִשָּׁה עַל עִסְקֵי גִּיטָּהּ וְקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ, וְנָתַן לָהּ גִּיטָּהּ וְקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ וְלֹא פֵּירֵשׁ – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: דַּיּוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ לְפָרֵשׁ. וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 4:7): If **one was speaking with a woman about matters of her bill of divorce or her betrothal, and he gave her a bill of divorce or her betrothal,** i.e., the money or a document of betrothal, **but did not clarify** his action, **Rabbi Yosei says:** This **is sufficient for him,** i.e., it is a valid divorce or betrothal because she will understand his intention from the context. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He is required to clarify** the meaning of his behavior. **And Rav Huna says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** If so, even if he said nothing to her she would still be betrothed, and this should certainly be the case if he says one of the statements under discussion.

This leads into a broader structural question: all these ambiguous formulations — *tzalati*, *segurati*, *atzurati*, and the rest — in what kind of case are we evaluating them? If the parties were not already engaged in conversation about *kiddushin* and related matters (*iskei gitta ve-kiddusheha*), how would she even know what he means? A bizarre formulation like *harei at segurati* or *harei at atzurati* would be unintelligible to her without context. And since, unlike a *get*, which can be given against the woman's will, *kiddushin* requires her knowing consent — if she has no idea what he is saying, she cannot meaningfully agree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לְעוֹלָם בִּמְדַבֵּר עִמָּהּ עַל עִסְקֵי גִּיטָּהּ וְקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ, וְאִי דְּיָהֵיב לַהּ וְשָׁתֵיק הָכִי נָמֵי, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן דִּיהַב לַהּ וַאֲמַר לַהּ בְּהָנֵי לִישָּׁנֵי,
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in explanation of this matter: **Actually,** we are dealing with cases **where he was speaking to her about matters of her bill of divorce and her betrothal, and if** it was referring to a case **where he** had **given her** the money **and been silent, so too** it would have been a valid betrothal. **With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where he gave her** something **and said to her** one **of these expressions.**

But if they *are* already talking about marriage-related matters, then — the *gemara* points out — even if he said nothing at all when giving her the money, it would be valid. This is the ruling of the Mishnah: if a man was speaking with a woman about the matters of her *get* and *kiddushin* and gave her the document or money without making any verbal declaration (*velo peireish*), Rabbi Yose says *dayo* — it is sufficient. Rabbi Yehudah disagrees and holds that one must explicitly state what the act is for.

The *gemara* cites Rav Huna in the name of Shmuel: *halachah ke-Rabbi Yose*. And since even silence is sufficient according to Rabbi Yose, the question about these ambiguous formulations becomes sharper — if you don't need to say anything, what does it add to say something ambiguous?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: הָנֵי לִישָּׁנֵי לְקִידּוּשֵׁי קָאָמַר לַהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא לִמְלָאכָה קָאָמַר לַהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is the dilemma raised before** the Sages: **These expressions, did he say them to her for** the purpose of **betrothal, or perhaps he said** them **to her for** the purpose of **labor?** He might have intended to hire her, withdrawing his previous intention to betroth her. In other words, his statement in conjunction with his giving of an item renders the meaning of the expression less clear than if he had remained silent. The Gemara leaves most of these issues unanswered, and states that the dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* resolves this by identifying a middle case: the ambiguous formulation is actually more problematic than silence. When parties are engaged in conversation about marriage and one simply hands over the ring without speaking, an outside observer understands perfectly what is happening. But when one uses a strange, oblique phrase — *harei at segurati* or *harei at izmargadi* — one might wonder whether the speaker is trying to redirect the conversation. Perhaps he is now talking about hiring her for work. The ambiguous statement introduces doubt where silence would not.

## Silent Kiddushin

### Yossi vs. Yehudah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: הָיָה מְדַבֵּר עִם הָאִשָּׁה עַל עִיסְקֵי גִּיטָּהּ וְקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ, וְנָתַן לָהּ גִּיטָּהּ וְקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ וְלֹא פֵּירֵשׁ – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: דַּיּוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ לְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself:** If **he was speaking to the woman about matters of her bill of divorce or her betrothal, and he gave her bill of divorce or her betrothal to her and did not clarify** his intention, **Rabbi Yosei says:** This **is sufficient for him. Rabbi Yehuda says: He is required to clarify.** 

To review the Mishnah's dispute more carefully: *hayah medaber im ha'ishah al iskei gitta ve-kiddusheha ve-natan lah gitta ve-kiddusheha velo peireish* — he was speaking with the woman about matters of *get* and *kiddushin*, then gave her the document or the money, without specifying what it was for. Rabbi Yose says this is sufficient — *dayo*. Rabbi Yehudah says he must explicitly declare his intent — *tzarich le-fareish*.

### Halachah: Context Sufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְהוּא שֶׁעֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: וְהוּא שֶׁעֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן. כְּתַנָּאֵי: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: וְהוּא שֶׁעֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין עֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: And this** is the *halakha* provided **that they were discussing the same issue** and had not moved on to a different topic. **And likewise, Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says: And this** is the *halakha* provided **that they were discussing the same issue.** The Gemara comments: This disagreement is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** on this topic. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: And this** is the *halakha* provided **that they were discussing the same issue. Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon says:** This is the *halakha* **despite** the fact **that they were not discussing the same issue.**

Rav Huna reports in the name of Shmuel that *halachah ke-Rabbi Yose*. A further qualification is added by Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel: even Rabbi Yose concedes that this only applies *ve-hu she-asukin be-oto inyan* — provided they are still actively engaged in that same conversation when the act is performed. If they had moved on to other topics before he handed her the money, the contextual understanding that makes silent *kiddushin* effective is no longer present. Rav Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya concurs. A *beraita* also records Rabbi [Yehudah ha-Nasi] agreeing with this requirement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָאו דַּעֲסוּקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן, מְנָא יָדְעָה מַאי קָאָמַר לַהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵעִנְיָן לְעִנְיָן בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if they were not discussing the same issue, from where does she know what he is saying to her?** They have already changed the topic of conversation, and these expressions on their own are ambiguous. **Abaye said:** They have not changed to an entirely different topic; rather, they changed **from** discussing one **topic to** discussing another **topic within the same** general **topic.** In other words, they were no longer speaking directly about divorce or betrothal, but they were still discussing related matters. Therefore, his intention was clear to her when he made his statement.

Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, however, holds that even if they are no longer discussing the same matter, the *kiddushin* can still be valid. The *gemara* asks: if they have already moved on to a different topic, how does she know what he means? Abaye explains: *mitoch inyan le-inyan* — not a completely unrelated topic, but a related one. So for example, if they had been discussing the *kiddushin* itself and then shifted to discussing the financial arrangements of the marriage — the dowry and assets — that peripheral conversation is close enough that the subsequent act of giving money is still understood in context, per Rashi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יֵימַר לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּטִיב גִּיטִּין וְקִידּוּשִׁין לֹא יְהֵא לוֹ עֵסֶק עִמָּהֶם, אֲפִילּוּ לָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Rav Yeimar said to Rav Ashi: But** if so, with regard to **that** which **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Anyone who does not know the nature of bills of divorce and betrothals should have no dealings in them,** i.e., he may not serve as a judge in cases of this kind lest he permit that which is prohibited, does this principle apply **even** if that individual **did not hear this** *halakha* **that Rav Huna** says that **Shmuel says?** Since this type of case is uncommon, should such an individual be considered unfamiliar with the *halakhot* of bills of divorce and betrothals? Rav Ashi **said to him: Yes,** it **is indeed so;** this person is considered to lack the requisite knowledge to deal with these cases. This ruling is an important matter with regard to the *halakhot* of betrothal, and one who is unaware of it might err.

The *halachah* follows Rabbi Yose: *amar Rav Huna amar Shmuel halachah ke-Rabbi Yose*. Rav Ami and Rav Assi then apply this in connection with another statement of Shmuel — that anyone who does not have thorough command of the laws of *gittin* and *kiddushin* should not involve himself in adjudicating such cases. They suggest that even ignorance of this specific *halachah* of Rabbi Yose would qualify one as insufficiently expert to sit on such a *beit din*.

## Kiddushin Formulatio (II)

### Witnesses & Intent

The *gemara's* analysis of ambiguous formulations prompts a deep examination in *Tosafot ha-Rosh ha-Zaken*. The issue is not merely what the two parties understood between themselves, but what an objective third party — specifically, the *edim*, the witnesses — would have understood at that moment.

*Tosafot ha-Rosh ha-Zaken* makes the following point: even if both parties afterward declare that they understood the exchange to constitute *kiddushin* — even if the man says he intended it for *kiddushin* and the woman says she understood it as such and consented — this is still insufficient. The reason is *harei edim lo nitbarer lahem amitat ha-lashon be-et she-shamu* — at the moment the witnesses heard the words, the true meaning of the formulation was not clear to them. The validity of *kiddushin* cannot rest purely on the subjective agreement of the two parties; it requires that the act be objectively comprehensible to outside observers at the time it occurs.

This is not because the witnesses' understanding supersedes that of the principals, but because *kiddushin* is a legally constituted act that requires objectively intelligible expression. If a stranger were dropped into the middle of the scene at that moment, would they understand what was taking place? That is the standard.

*Tosafot ha-Rosh ha-Zaken* then offers a path by which even an ambiguous formulation could work: *im techila amru la-edim* — if, before the act, he informed the witnesses in advance, explaining "I am going to give her *kiddushin* by means of this particular formulation," and she subsequently confirmed that she understood it as *kiddushin*, then *vadai havu kiddushin* — it is certainly valid *kiddushin*. Why? Because at the moment the witnesses heard the formulation, *nitbarer lahem she-hem kiddushin* — it had already been made clear to them that this is what they would be witnessing. The context was established in advance, and the act is therefore objectively understood.

The practical upshot is that there is an objective but flexible standard. It cannot be entirely subjective — the two parties' private mutual understanding is not enough on its own. But the objective understanding can be established through advance disclosure to the witnesses. What cannot work is an ambiguous phrase taken in isolation, with nothing establishing its meaning for outside observers at the time of the act.

This explains why the *gemara* found silence to be less problematic than an ambiguous statement. When the parties are *asukim be-oto inyan* and the man silently gives money or a ring, any witness present understands perfectly what is occurring — the entire conversation has provided the context. But an odd phrase inserted into the flow of that conversation could, paradoxically, disrupt the clarity of the situation: it might suggest a shift of subject, introducing ambiguity where the silent act would have left none.

## Get Formulations

### Divorce Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּגֵירוּשִׁין, נָתַן לָהּ גִּיטָּהּ וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְשׁוּלַּחַת״ ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מוּתֶּרֶת לְכׇל אָדָם״ – הֲרֵי הִיא מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. פְּשִׁיטָא נָתַן לָהּ גִּיטָּהּ, וְאָמַר לָהּ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ בַּת חוֹרִין״ – לֹא אָמַר וְלֹא כְּלוּם. אָמַר לָהּ לְשִׁפְחָתוֹ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מוּתֶּרֶת לְכׇל אָדָם״ – לֹא אָמַר וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to Shmuel's statement. **And similarly, with regard to divorce:** If a husband **gave** his wife **her bill of divorce and said to her: You are hereby sent away,** or: **You are hereby divorced,** or: **You are hereby permitted to** marry **any man,** then **she is divorced.** The Gemara comments: It **is obvious** that if a husband **gave her a bill of divorce and said to his wife: You are hereby a free woman,** **he has said nothing,** as this statement is not a valid expression of divorce. Similarly, if a master **said to his female** Canaanite **slave** upon emancipating her: **You are hereby permitted to any man, he has not said anything.**

Parallel to the *kiddushin* formulations, the *gemara* now surveys valid *lashonot* for a *get*. If a man gives his wife a *get* and says *harei at meshulachet* ("you are sent away"), *harei at megureshet* ("you are divorced"), or *harei at muteret le-chol adam* ("you are permitted to any man"), she is divorced. These are straightforward and clearly effective.

### Reversed Language

The *gemara* then considers switched formulations. If a man gives his wife a *get* and says *harei at bat chorin* ("you are free") — a phrase appropriate for freeing a slave — he has said nothing effective. Similarly, if he says to his *shifchah* *harei at muteret le-chol adam* — a phrase from the *get* context — that too is ineffective. Each formula was misapplied: you used the slave's formula for your wife, and the wife's formula for your slave. Neither effects the intended legal change.

### Le'Atzmaych Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהּ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ לְעַצְמְךָ״ מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן לִמְלָאכָה קָאָמַר לַהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא לִגְמָרֵי קָאָמַר לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara addresses a less straightforward case: If a man **said to his wife: You are hereby for yourself, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **he said** this **to her only with regard to work?** In other words, he might have meant that she may keep her earnings. **Or perhaps he said to her** that she is on her own **entirely,** i.e., she is divorced.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר: ״הֲרֵי אַתָּה בֶּן חוֹרִין״, ״הֲרֵי אַתָּה לְעַצְמְךָ״. הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה עֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי דִּקְנֵי לֵיהּ גּוּפֵיהּ, כִּי אָמַר לֵיהּ ״הֲרֵי אַתָּה לְעַצְמְךָ״ – לִגְמָרֵי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, אִשָּׁה דְּלָא קְנֵי לֵיהּ גּוּפַהּ, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** a proof, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The essence of a bill of manumission** is the expression: **You are hereby a freeman,** or: **You are hereby for yourself. Now** consider, **if** in the case of **a Canaanite slave, whose body belongs to** the master, even so, **when** the master **says to him: You are hereby for yourself,** this is considered as though **he said to him** that he **is entirely** on his own and is freed, then **all the more so** is it **not** clear **that a wife, whose body is not owned** by her husband, is divorced by means of this expression?

The *gemara* then raises an ambiguous case in the context of *gerushin*: if a husband says to his wife *harei at le-atzmekh* ("you are unto yourself"), what does this accomplish? Is he divorcing her — meaning "you are now your own person, detached from me" — or is he merely relinquishing his rights to *ma'aseh yadeha*, the fruits of her labor, while remaining married? The latter would be a permissible arrangement, under which he agrees not to support her in exchange for not claiming her earnings.

Ravina raises a proof from a *beraita* before Rav Ashi: *gufo shel get shichrur* — the body of a *get shichrur* for a slave includes the formulation *harei ata ben chorin harei ata le-atzmecha* — "you are free, you are unto yourself." From here, a *kal va-chomer* is constructed: with a *eved Kena'ani*, whom the master owns bodily (*kani lei le-gufeih*), when he says *harei ata le-atzmecha*, this clearly means complete freedom — not merely that the slave can keep his own earnings. How much more so when one says this to a wife, over whose body he has no ownership — it must certainly mean complete release. Therefore, *harei at le-atzmekh* said to a wife constitutes a valid *get*.

## Slave Manumission

### Ein Li Esek Bakh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהּ לְעַבְדּוֹ ״אֵין לִי עֵסֶק בְּךָ״ מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן ״אֵין לִי עֵסֶק בְּךָ״ – לִגְמָרֵי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא לִמְלָאכָה קָאָמַר לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the same issue, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** If **one said to his** Canaanite **slave: I have no business with you, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say** that when **he said to him: I have no business with you,** he meant **entirely,** and therefore the slave is freed? **Or** did he **perhaps say** this **to him with regard to labor?** In other words, it is possible that the master is relieving the slave of his obligation to perform labor without actually emancipating him from slavery.

The *gemara* continues with a question about freeing a slave: if a master says to his slave *ein li esek bakh* — "I have nothing more to do with you" — does this constitute manumission? The question is whether the phrase means he is entirely releasing the slave (full *shichrur*, which changes the slave's status entirely, making him, in effect, a Jew in the full sense) or whether it merely means he is relinquishing control over the slave's labor while the slave remains legally enslaved. For an *eved Kena'ani*, the distinction is not merely practical but one of fundamental personal status.

### Ono Document

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב אָשֵׁי וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב חָנִין מָחוֹזָאָה לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְנׇכְרִי – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר מֵרַבּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁלֹּא כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ, אֲבָל כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ – זֶהוּ שִׁחְרוּרוֹ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי אוֹנוֹ? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: דִּכְתַב לֵיהּ: כְּשֶׁתִּבְרַח מִמֶּנּוּ – ״אֵין לִי עֵסֶק בְּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Rav Ashi, and some say Rav Ḥanin from Meḥoza** said **to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear:** With regard to **one who sells his** Canaanite **slave to a gentile,** the slave **is emancipated but** nevertheless **requires a bill [*get*] of manumission from his first master.** In this manner the Sages penalized this owner for preventing the slave from fulfilling the mitzvot in which he is obligated. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says** in addition to this: **In what** case **is this statement said?** This is referring to a situation **where he did not write a document [*ono*] for** the slave when he sold him to the gentile. **But** if **he wrote a document for him, this** itself **is his emancipation.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this **document? Rav Sheshet said** that **he writes to him: When you escape from him I have no business with you.** This indicates that the formula: I have no business with you, is a valid expression of emancipation.

Rav Nachman — and some report it as Rav Chanin from Chuza — raises this before Rav Ashi, citing a proof. The *mishnah* records that if a master sells his slave to a non-Jew, the slave goes free, but he still requires a *get shichrur* from his original master. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies: this applies only if the master did not write an *ono* at the time of sale. But if he wrote an *ono*, that document itself constitutes the *get shichrur*, and no separate document is needed.

Rav Sheshet explains: what is an *ono*? It is a document in which the master writes *kshe-tivrach mimeni, ein li esek bakh* — "when you flee from me, I have nothing to do with you." Since such a document is recognized as a *shtar shichrur* — proof that the slave goes free upon flight — it follows that the phrase *ein li esek bakh* does constitute full release. The question is thereby resolved.

## Kiddushin with Loan

### Loan Disqualified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוֶה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. בַּהֲנָאַת מִלְוֶה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן מִפְּנֵי הַעֲרָמַת רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye says:** With regard to **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan,** i.e., he previously lent this woman money and he now says that she is betrothed to him by means of that loan, **she is not betrothed.** The reason is that a woman can be betrothed only through her acceptance of money, or an item that has monetary value, at the time of the betrothal. Although this woman owes the man money, at the time the man states that she is betrothed to him, the loan is not in fact money but an obligation. Therefore, he does not actually give her anything at the time of the betrothal. By contrast, if he betroths her **by** means of **the benefit of the loan, she is betrothed. But it is prohibited to do so, due to** the fact that betrothing a woman via the benefit of a loan is **an artifice** used to circumvent the prohibition **of** receiving **interest,** as this enables the husband to gain an additional benefit from the loan.

Returning to *kiddushin*, Abaye presents a ruling that opens a substantial discussion: *ha-mekadesh be-malveh einah mekudeshet* — if a man attempts to be *mekadesh* a woman using a pre-existing loan (money he has already lent her), the *kiddushin* is not valid. The operative principle, derived from the model of *Sedei Ephron* — where Avraham gave Ephron money at the moment of the transaction — is that *kiddushin* must be effected by giving something at the time of the act. A loan is different: *hotza'ah nitnah, kevar hein shela* — once a loan is disbursed, the money is already hers. He no longer controls it; she merely owes him a debt. He cannot use money that is no longer his to effect *kiddushin*, for he is not giving her anything new. As Rashi explains the case: *de-amar hitkadshi li be-malveh she-yesh li be-yadeich* — he says "be betrothed to me through the loan I have in your hands." Since the money is already legally hers, he is not transferring anything, and the *kiddushin* fails.

### Extending Loan Term

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי הֲנָאַת מִלְוֶה, הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאַזְקְפַהּ, דְּאַמַר לַהּ אַרְבַּע בְּחַמְשָׁה – הָא רִבִּית מְעַלַּיְיתָא הוּא. וְעוֹד, הַיְינוּ מִלְוֶה! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאַרְוַוח לַהּ זִימְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is meant** by **this** term: **The benefit of the loan? If we say that** it means that **he established** interest **upon it** when she took the loan, e.g., **he said to her** that he is lending her **four** coins **in** exchange for the repayment of **five,** and he betroths her by releasing her from the obligation to pay this additional coin, **this is** a case of **full-fledged interest,** not merely an artifice used to circumvent the prohibition of receiving interest. He is receiving full payment of the loan and an additional benefit. **And furthermore,** when he releases her from the obligation to pay this additional coin, he is simply forgoing another obligation she has toward him; he is not giving her anything. **This is** like a regular case of a betrothal with **a loan,** and therefore she should not be betrothed. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where he extended the time** of the loan **for her.** When the time for her to repay the loan arrived he extended the deadline and betrothed her with the financial benefit she receives from the extra time he is giving her to use the money. In this case he does betroth her with the value he is giving her at that time, but it is similar to interest, as it is included in the prohibition of interest to pay the creditor for an extension of the time of a loan.

However, Abaye continues: *be-hana'at malveh mekudeshet* — if he is *mekadesh* her with the *hana'ah* (benefit) derived from extending the loan term, the *kiddushin* is valid. The *gemara* clarifies what *hana'at malveh* means. It is not *arba be-chamesh* — loaning four to be repaid as five — because that would simply be the loan itself (and would constitute actual *ribit*). Rather, *lo tzricha de-arvach lah zimna* — it means he extended the repayment deadline. She was obligated to pay him back by a certain date; he grants her additional time. The *hana'ah* she derives from this extension — she would theoretically have been willing to pay someone a *perutah* to intercede and secure that extension — constitutes the monetary benefit with which he is *mekadesh* her. Rashi articulates this explicitly: *amar lah hitkadshi li be-hana'ah zu she-at hayit notenet perutah le-adam she-yefayeseni alayich* — the extension is worth a *perutah* to her, and with that value he betroths her.

### Ribit Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְאֶלָּא אָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן מִפְּנֵי הַעֲרָמַת רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss Abaye's position. **But it is prohibited to do this,** i.e., give *teruma* in this manner, *ab initio*, **because** this creates the appearance of **an artifice** used to circumvent the prohibition **of** receiving **interest.**

While this *kiddushin* is valid, Abaye cautions: *aval asur la'asot kein mipnei ha'aramat ribit* — one may not do this, because it has the appearance of *ribit*. The technical distinction is that it is not *ribit gamur* — he is not receiving money from her; he receives a wife. He takes nothing directly from the loan transaction. Nevertheless, he derives benefit from the loan — the *hana'ah* of the extension is transformed into *kiddushin* — and this creates the appearance of a lender profiting from a loan, which the Torah prohibits. The principle is that a Jewish loan must be neutral: you lend a hundred and receive a hundred back. Here, the lender is getting something more — a wife — out of the loan relationship, even if it is not cash payment.

The *Rosh* agrees that *hana'at malveh* in the sense of extending the time constitutes real monetary value that can serve as the basis for *kiddushin*. And as noted, the extension need not be the exclusive path — forgiving the loan entirely also qualifies as *hana'at malveh* according to this view. The *Rashba* adds a further nuance: the effectiveness of the extension may depend on its timing. When the loan is *kol ha-omed ligbot ke-gavuy dami* — effectively ready for collection — then the creditor truly has the power to extend or demand, and the extension is a tangible new benefit. But if the loan is far from due, the extension is still too abstract to function as real *hana'ah*, and may not support *kiddushin*.

### Loan Forgiveness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן אִם מָחַל כׇּל הַמִּלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye then adds:** **And certainly if he forgives the entire loan,** this too functions as *hana'at malveh* and would constitute valid *kiddushin* (though the same *ribit* concern would apply).

Abaye then adds: *ve-kol sheken im machala kol ha-malveh* — certainly if he forgives the entire loan, this too functions as *hana'at malveh* and would constitute valid *kiddushin* (though the same *ribit* concern would apply). The reason is that *mechilah* creates a genuinely new benefit: where before she owed him a hundred dollars, now she owes nothing. That release of obligation — *hana'at mechilah* — is a fresh and real economic benefit conferred upon her at the moment of *kiddushin*, unlike the original loan disbursement, which is already spent. As Rashi frames it: *hashta hu de-kaya ve-lav afshata de-at malveh* — it is something that exists now, not merely the residue of the original loan.

The *Ran*, however, disputes whether forgiving the loan truly works. His position is that *mechilah* of the loan is not a genuinely *new* benefit — it is simply the erasure of a prior debt. The woman's mind is on the hundred dollars that was given, not on some additional, abstract gain. To be valid, the *kiddushin* must be effected with something above and beyond the original loan, something new introduced at the moment of betrothal. On this view, only the *hana'ah* of the extended repayment term — something genuinely incremental and new — would work, while outright forgiveness of the principal would not. This represents a substantive disagreement between Rashi and the *Ran* about the nature of what constitutes a valid new benefit in this context.

## Conditional Gifts

### Conditional in Other Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: ״הֵילָךְ מָנֶה עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחֲזִירֵהוּ לִי״ בְּמֶכֶר – לֹא קָנָה, בְּאִשָּׁה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, בְּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן – אֵין בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי, בִּתְרוּמָה – יָצָא יְדֵי נְתִינָה. וְאָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּכֹהֵן הַמְסַיֵּיעַ בְּבֵית הַגְּרָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** With regard to one who says to another: **Here are one hundred dinars for you** that I am giving you **on the condition that you return them to me,** if he gave these one hundred dinars as part **of a purchase, he does not acquire** the item, as he has not given the seller any money. And similarly, **with regard to a woman,** if he gave her money for her betrothal on the condition that she return it, **she is not betrothed.** If one gave money in this manner **for** the **redemption of** his firstborn **son,** for which a priest must receive five *sela*, **his son is not redeemed. If one does this with regard to *teruma*,** i.e., he gives produce to a priest as *teruma* on the condition that it will be returned, **he has** technically **fulfilled** his obligation of **giving.** Once he gets the *teruma* back it belongs to him, as he is the original owner, and although it is prohibited for him to partake of it, as he is a non-priest, he may sell it to a different priest. **But it is prohibited to do this,** i.e., give *teruma* in this manner, *ab initio*, **because** this priest receiving the *teruma* **appears like a priest who assists at the threshing floor,** as he presumably agrees to this arrangement in return for some gain.

Rava raises the case of *matanah al menat lehachzir* — a conditional gift given on the understanding that it will be returned. The paradigmatic instance is the *arba minim* on *Sukkot*: if one person says to another *heilach etrog zeh al menat she-tachzirehu li* — here is my *etrog*, but give it back — and the recipient takes it and uses it and then returns it, he has fulfilled his obligation. Rava himself ruled this way: *netalo ve-hachziro yatza*; if he did not return it, he did not fulfill the obligation.

Rava then applies this principle across several domains. In a sale (*mecher*), if a buyer says "here is payment on condition you return it," no acquisition takes place. For *kiddushin*, if he gives her money on condition she returns it, she is not *mekudeshet*. For *pidyon haben*, if he gives the *kohen* the five *sela'im* on condition they be returned, the son is not redeemed. But for *terumah*, if he gives *terumah* to the *kohen* on condition it be returned — *yatza yedei netinah* — the obligation to give *terumah* is fulfilled, though the practice is forbidden because it creates the appearance that the *kohen* has made a deal or purchased the *terumah*, compromising the dignity of the gift and smacking of *armah* — evasion of the spirit of the law.

### Why It Fails for Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָסָבַר רָבָא? אִי קָסָבַר מַתָּנָה עַל מְנָת לְהַחֲזִיר שְׁמָהּ מַתָּנָה – אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי, וְאִי קָסָבַר לֹא שְׁמָהּ מַתָּנָה – אֲפִילּוּ תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What does Rava maintain? If he maintains** that **a gift** given **on the condition that it is returned is called a gift,** this should apply not only to *teruma* but **even** to **all** the other cases, i.e., it should be considered a valid gift in all of the above cases. **And if he maintains** that a gift of this kind **is not called a gift,** then **even** with regard to ***teruma* it** should **not** be considered a legitimate form of giving.

The *gemara* finds this account internally inconsistent. Rava himself holds *matanah al menat lehachzir shemah matanah* — a conditional gift counts as a gift. If so, all these cases should work. And if it does not count as a gift, none of them should — including *terumah*, which the *beraita* says *does* work. The formulation in which some cases work and others do not seems untenable.

### Girsa Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא רָבָא הוּא דְּאָמַר: מַתָּנָה עַל מְנָת לְהַחֲזִיר שְׁמָהּ מַתָּנָה! דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״הֵילָךְ אֶתְרוֹג זֶה עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירֵהוּ לִי״ – נְטָלוֹ וְהֶחְזִירוֹ – יָצָא. וְאִם לָאו – לֹא יָצָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּכוּלְּהוּ קָנֵי לְבַר מֵאִשָּׁה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית בַּחֲלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rava is the one who says: A gift given on the condition that it is** later **returned is called a gift. As Rava said** that if one says to another on the first day of the festival of *Sukkot*: **Take this *etrog* on the condition that you return it to me,** and the recipient **takes it,** recites a blessing over it, **and returns it, he has fulfilled** his obligation, despite the fact that one must own the *etrog* he uses for the mitzva on the first day of *Sukkot*. **And if he does not** return it **he has not fulfilled** his obligation, as he gave him the gift only on the condition that it would be returned. This indicates that in the opinion of Rava, a gift that is given on the condition that it is returned is considered a gift. **Rather, Rav Ashi said: In all of these** cases the gift is **acquired, except for** the betrothal of **a woman, because a woman cannot be acquired by** means of symbolic **exchange.**

Rav Ashi resolves this by correcting the *girsa* of the *beraita*. The proper reading is not that *kiddushin*, *mecher*, and *pidyon haben* all fail while *terumah* alone works. Rather: *be-chulehu kani* — all of them work — *levar me-ishah* — except for the woman. *Mecher* works, *pidyon haben* works, *terumah* works (though one should not do it in practice for the reasons of *armah*), but *kiddushin* does not work. Why does *kiddushin* alone fail?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נְחֶמְיָה לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא כְּווֹתָיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna Mar, son of Rav Neḥemya, said to Rav Ashi: We say this in the name of Rava in accordance with your** opinion, not in accordance with the previous ruling.

*She-ein ishah nikneit be-chalippin* — a woman cannot be betrothed through the mechanism of *chalippin*. The *kicha* of *kiddushin* was derived from the model of *Sedei Ephron*, in which Avraham gave Ephron a definitive, unconditional transfer of money. *Chalippin* — the mode of acquisition by exchange or barter, typified by the *sudar* (handkerchief) — operates on a different and incompatible logic. The *sudar* in a *kinyan chalippin* is taken and immediately returned; the exchange is instantaneous and the physical object passes back at once, much as the *etrog* in the *matanah al menat lehachzir* case is returned. Saying to a woman "here is money, give it back" is structurally identical to *chalippin*, and *ein ishah nikneit be-chalippin*. Avraham's gift to Ephron was a clean, permanent, one-directional transfer, and *kiddushin* must replicate that model.

Rav Huna bar Rav Nechemia of Rav Ashi confirms: this was indeed the tradition he had in the name of Rava — the corrected *girsa* is the right one. All the cases except *kiddushin* work; only *kiddushin* fails due to the inherent incompatibility with *chalippin*.
