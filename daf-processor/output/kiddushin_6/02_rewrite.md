# Kiddushin 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kiddushin Formulations

### Six Formulations

The *gemara* on Kiddushin 6a resumes mid-discussion with a *tanna rabbanan*, continuing the examination of which linguistic formulations (*lashonot*) constitute a valid *ma'amar* of *kiddushin*. The *beraita* presents six formulations, grouped in two sets of three. In the first set: *harei at ishti* ("you are my wife"), *harei at arusati* ("you are my *arusa*"), and *harei at knuya li* ("you are acquired to me") — in any of these cases, she is *mekudeshet*. In the second set: *harei at sheli* ("you are mine"), *harei at birshuti* ("you are in my domain"), and *harei at zekuka li* ("you are bound to me") — these, too, effectuate *kiddushin*. The term *zekuka* carries connotations similar to *zika* in the context of *yibbum*, conveying a sense of being halachically bound or attached.

The *gemara* asks why these six formulations are presented as two separate groupings rather than a single unified list. The answer is simply that this is how the tradition was received: *tana telat shmi'ineihu ve-garsineihu* — he heard them in sets of three and transmitted them that way. Grouping teachings in threes is a familiar mnemonic convention, even when the internal logic might not demand the division.

### Ambiguous Formulations

The *gemara* then turns to a series of more ambiguous formulations and poses the question *mahu* — is this effective or not? The list includes: *meiyuchedet li* ("singled out to me"), *meyu'edet li* ("designated to me," drawing on the language of *ya'adah* from the passage about the *bat la'amah*), *ezrati* ("my helpmate," from *ezer kinegdo* in *Bereishit*), *negdati* ("alongside me"), *atzurati* (from *atzeret*, suggesting being gathered together with him at home), *tzalati* (from *vayikach achat mitzal'otav*, God's taking of the *tzela* from *Adam*), *segurati* (from *vayisgor basar tachtena*, the enclosing of flesh), *tachtai* ("beneath me," from the same verse), and *tefusati* (from the language of *nitpasha* in the context of a *na'ara me'orasa* — carrying connotations of being seized, which, rightly or wrongly, implies the married relationship).

One of these ambiguous cases admits of a ready resolution. The formulation *lekuchati* — "my taken one" — is validated by a *beraita*: *omer lekuchati harei zu mekudeshet*, because the *Torah* itself uses the root *kicha* to describe betrothal: *ki yikach ish isha*. The term *likuchin* is the *Torah's* own word for this act, and so this formulation clearly works.

### Charufah Usage

The *gemara* then raises the case of *charufati* — a term derived from the passage *shifcha nechereset l'ish*, which describes a slave-woman who is *nechereset*, or designated, to a man. Does using this term constitute a valid *ma'amar* of *kiddushin*?

A *beraita* is cited: *omer charufati mekudeshet* — yes, it works. The *gemara* initially explains this on the basis that *bi-Yehudah korin l'arusa charufa* — in Judea, *arusa* is colloquially called *charufa*. The *gemara* challenges this: if we already have a *pasuk* supporting the term, why do we additionally need to appeal to regional Judean usage? And conversely, if the *pasuk* alone is sufficient, why does the regional usage matter?

After two rounds of revision, the *gemara* settles on the following reading: *omer charufa bi-Yehudah mekudeshet* — if one uses this formulation specifically *in Judea*, it is effective, because there the word is understood to mean *arusa*, likely by association with the *pasuk*. But if one uses this term outside of Judea, where the word does not carry that meaning, it would presumably not work.

### Context Requirement

This leads into a broader structural question: all these ambiguous formulations — *tzalati*, *segurati*, *atzurati*, and the rest — in what kind of case are we evaluating them? If the parties were not already engaged in conversation about *kiddushin* and related matters (*iskei gitta ve-kiddusheha*), how would she even know what he means? A bizarre formulation like *harei at segurati* or *harei at atzurati* would be unintelligible to her without context. And since, unlike a *get*, which can be given against the woman's will, *kiddushin* requires her knowing consent — if she has no idea what he is saying, she cannot meaningfully agree.

But if they *are* already talking about marriage-related matters, then — the *gemara* points out — even if he said nothing at all when giving her the money, it would be valid. This is the ruling of the Mishnah: if a man was speaking with a woman about the matters of her *get* and *kiddushin* and gave her the document or money without making any verbal declaration (*velo peireish*), Rabbi Yose says *dayo* — it is sufficient. Rabbi Yehudah disagrees and holds that one must explicitly state what the act is for.

The *gemara* cites Rav Huna in the name of Shmuel: *halachah ke-Rabbi Yose*. And since even silence is sufficient according to Rabbi Yose, the question about these ambiguous formulations becomes sharper — if you don't need to say anything, what does it add to say something ambiguous?

The *gemara* resolves this by identifying a middle case: the ambiguous formulation is actually more problematic than silence. When parties are engaged in conversation about marriage and one simply hands over the ring without speaking, an outside observer understands perfectly what is happening. But when one uses a strange, oblique phrase — *harei at segurati* or *harei at izmargadi* — one might wonder whether the speaker is trying to redirect the conversation. Perhaps he is now talking about hiring her for work. The ambiguous statement introduces doubt where silence would not.

## Silent Kiddushin

### Yossi vs. Yehudah

To review the Mishnah's dispute more carefully: *hayah medaber im ha'ishah al iskei gitta ve-kiddusheha ve-natan lah gitta ve-kiddusheha velo peireish* — he was speaking with the woman about matters of *get* and *kiddushin*, then gave her the document or the money, without specifying what it was for. Rabbi Yose says this is sufficient — *dayo*. Rabbi Yehudah says he must explicitly declare his intent — *tzarich le-fareish*.

### Halachah: Context Suffic…

Rav Huna reports in the name of Shmuel that *halachah ke-Rabbi Yose*. A further qualification is added by Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel: even Rabbi Yose concedes that this only applies *ve-hu she-asukin be-oto inyan* — provided they are still actively engaged in that same conversation when the act is performed. If they had moved on to other topics before he handed her the money, the contextual understanding that makes silent *kiddushin* effective is no longer present. Rav Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya concurs. A *beraita* also records Rabbi [Yehudah ha-Nasi] agreeing with this requirement.

Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, however, holds that even if they are no longer discussing the same matter, the *kiddushin* can still be valid. The *gemara* asks: if they have already moved on to a different topic, how does she know what he means? Abaye explains: *mitoch inyan le-inyan* — not a completely unrelated topic, but a related one. So for example, if they had been discussing the *kiddushin* itself and then shifted to discussing the financial arrangements of the marriage — the dowry and assets — that peripheral conversation is close enough that the subsequent act of giving money is still understood in context, per Rashi.

The *halachah* follows Rabbi Yose: *amar Rav Huna amar Shmuel halachah ke-Rabbi Yose*. Rav Ami and Rav Assi then apply this in connection with another statement of Shmuel — that anyone who does not have thorough command of the laws of *gittin* and *kiddushin* should not involve himself in adjudicating such cases. They suggest that even ignorance of this specific *halachah* of Rabbi Yose would qualify one as insufficiently expert to sit on such a *beit din*.

## Kiddushin Formulations…

### Witnesses & Intent

The *gemara's* analysis of ambiguous formulations prompts a deep examination in *Tosafot ha-Rosh ha-Zaken*. The issue is not merely what the two parties understood between themselves, but what an objective third party — specifically, the *edim*, the witnesses — would have understood at that moment.

*Tosafot ha-Rosh ha-Zaken* makes the following point: even if both parties afterward declare that they understood the exchange to constitute *kiddushin* — even if the man says he intended it for *kiddushin* and the woman says she understood it as such and consented — this is still insufficient. The reason is *harei edim lo nitbarer lahem amitat ha-lashon be-et she-shamu* — at the moment the witnesses heard the words, the true meaning of the formulation was not clear to them. The validity of *kiddushin* cannot rest purely on the subjective agreement of the two parties; it requires that the act be objectively comprehensible to outside observers at the time it occurs.

This is not because the witnesses' understanding supersedes that of the principals, but because *kiddushin* is a legally constituted act that requires objectively intelligible expression. If a stranger were dropped into the middle of the scene at that moment, would they understand what was taking place? That is the standard.

*Tosafot ha-Rosh ha-Zaken* then offers a path by which even an ambiguous formulation could work: *im techila amru la-edim* — if, before the act, he informed the witnesses in advance, explaining "I am going to give her *kiddushin* by means of this particular formulation," and she subsequently confirmed that she understood it as *kiddushin*, then *vadai havu kiddushin* — it is certainly valid *kiddushin*. Why? Because at the moment the witnesses heard the formulation, *nitbarer lahem she-hem kiddushin* — it had already been made clear to them that this is what they would be witnessing. The context was established in advance, and the act is therefore objectively understood.

The practical upshot is that there is an objective but flexible standard. It cannot be entirely subjective — the two parties' private mutual understanding is not enough on its own. But the objective understanding can be established through advance disclosure to the witnesses. What cannot work is an ambiguous phrase taken in isolation, with nothing establishing its meaning for outside observers at the time of the act.

This explains why the *gemara* found silence to be less problematic than an ambiguous statement. When the parties are *asukim be-oto inyan* and the man silently gives money or a ring, any witness present understands perfectly what is occurring — the entire conversation has provided the context. But an odd phrase inserted into the flow of that conversation could, paradoxically, disrupt the clarity of the situation: it might suggest a shift of subject, introducing ambiguity where the silent act would have left none.

## Get Formulations

### Divorce Language

Parallel to the *kiddushin* formulations, the *gemara* now surveys valid *lashonot* for a *get*. If a man gives his wife a *get* and says *harei at meshulachet* ("you are sent away"), *harei at megureshet* ("you are divorced"), or *harei at muteret le-chol adam* ("you are permitted to any man"), she is divorced. These are straightforward and clearly effective.

### Reversed Language

The *gemara* then considers switched formulations. If a man gives his wife a *get* and says *harei at bat chorin* ("you are free") — a phrase appropriate for freeing a slave — he has said nothing effective. Similarly, if he says to his *shifchah* *harei at muteret le-chol adam* — a phrase from the *get* context — that too is ineffective. Each formula was misapplied: you used the slave's formula for your wife, and the wife's formula for your slave. Neither effects the intended legal change.

### Le'Atzmaych Question

The *gemara* then raises an ambiguous case in the context of *gerushin*: if a husband says to his wife *harei at le-atzmekh* ("you are unto yourself"), what does this accomplish? Is he divorcing her — meaning "you are now your own person, detached from me" — or is he merely relinquishing his rights to *ma'aseh yadeha*, the fruits of her labor, while remaining married? The latter would be a permissible arrangement, under which he agrees not to support her in exchange for not claiming her earnings.

Ravina raises a proof from a *beraita* before Rav Ashi: *gufo shel get shichrur* — the body of a *get shichrur* for a slave includes the formulation *harei ata ben chorin harei ata le-atzmecha* — "you are free, you are unto yourself." From here, a *kal va-chomer* is constructed: with a *eved Kena'ani*, whom the master owns bodily (*kani lei le-gufeih*), when he says *harei ata le-atzmecha*, this clearly means complete freedom — not merely that the slave can keep his own earnings. How much more so when one says this to a wife, over whose body he has no ownership — it must certainly mean complete release. Therefore, *harei at le-atzmekh* said to a wife constitutes a valid *get*.

## Slave Manumission

### Ein Li Esek Bakh

The *gemara* continues with a question about freeing a slave: if a master says to his slave *ein li esek bakh* — "I have nothing more to do with you" — does this constitute manumission? The question is whether the phrase means he is entirely releasing the slave (full *shichrur*, which changes the slave's status entirely, making him, in effect, a Jew in the full sense) or whether it merely means he is relinquishing control over the slave's labor while the slave remains legally enslaved. For an *eved Kena'ani*, the distinction is not merely practical but one of fundamental personal status.

### Ono Document

Rav Nachman — and some report it as Rav Chanin from Chuza — raises this before Rav Ashi, citing a proof. The *mishnah* records that if a master sells his slave to a non-Jew, the slave goes free, but he still requires a *get shichrur* from his original master. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies: this applies only if the master did not write an *ono* at the time of sale. But if he wrote an *ono*, that document itself constitutes the *get shichrur*, and no separate document is needed.

Rav Sheshet explains: what is an *ono*? It is a document in which the master writes *kshe-tivrach mimeni, ein li esek bakh* — "when you flee from me, I have nothing to do with you." Since such a document is recognized as a *shtar shichrur* — proof that the slave goes free upon flight — it follows that the phrase *ein li esek bakh* does constitute full release. The question is thereby resolved.

## Kiddushin with Loan

### Loan Disqualified

Returning to *kiddushin*, Abaye presents a ruling that opens a substantial discussion: *ha-mekadesh be-malveh einah mekudeshet* — if a man attempts to be *mekadesh* a woman using a pre-existing loan (money he has already lent her), the *kiddushin* is not valid. The operative principle, derived from the model of *Sedei Ephron* — where Avraham gave Ephron money at the moment of the transaction — is that *kiddushin* must be effected by giving something at the time of the act. A loan is different: *hotza'ah nitnah, kevar hein shela* — once a loan is disbursed, the money is already hers. He no longer controls it; she merely owes him a debt. He cannot use money that is no longer his to effect *kiddushin*, for he is not giving her anything new. As Rashi explains the case: *de-amar hitkadshi li be-malveh she-yesh li be-yadeich* — he says "be betrothed to me through the loan I have in your hands." Since the money is already legally hers, he is not transferring anything, and the *kiddushin* fails.

### Extending Loan Term

However, Abaye continues: *be-hana'at malveh mekudeshet* — if he is *mekadesh* her with the *hana'ah* (benefit) derived from extending the loan term, the *kiddushin* is valid. The *gemara* clarifies what *hana'at malveh* means. It is not *arba be-chamesh* — loaning four to be repaid as five — because that would simply be the loan itself (and would constitute actual *ribit*). Rather, *lo tzricha de-arvach lah zimna* — it means he extended the repayment deadline. She was obligated to pay him back by a certain date; he grants her additional time. The *hana'ah* she derives from this extension — she would theoretically have been willing to pay someone a *perutah* to intercede and secure that extension — constitutes the monetary benefit with which he is *mekadesh* her. Rashi articulates this explicitly: *amar lah hitkadshi li be-hana'ah zu she-at hayit notenet perutah le-adam she-yefayeseni alayich* — the extension is worth a *perutah* to her, and with that value he betroths her.

### Ribit Problem

While this *kiddushin* is valid, Abaye cautions: *aval asur la'asot kein mipnei ha'aramat ribit* — one may not do this, because it has the appearance of *ribit*. The technical distinction is that it is not *ribit gamur* — he is not receiving money from her; he receives a wife. He takes nothing directly from the loan transaction. Nevertheless, he derives benefit from the loan — the *hana'ah* of the extension is transformed into *kiddushin* — and this creates the appearance of a lender profiting from a loan, which the Torah prohibits. The principle is that a Jewish loan must be neutral: you lend a hundred and receive a hundred back. Here, the lender is getting something more — a wife — out of the loan relationship, even if it is not cash payment.

The *Rosh* agrees that *hana'at malveh* in the sense of extending the time constitutes real monetary value that can serve as the basis for *kiddushin*. And as noted, the extension need not be the exclusive path — forgiving the loan entirely also qualifies as *hana'at malveh* according to this view. The *Rashba* adds a further nuance: the effectiveness of the extension may depend on its timing. When the loan is *kol ha-omed ligbot ke-gavuy dami* — effectively ready for collection — then the creditor truly has the power to extend or demand, and the extension is a tangible new benefit. But if the loan is far from due, the extension is still too abstract to function as real *hana'ah*, and may not support *kiddushin*.

### Loan Forgiveness

Abaye then adds: *ve-kol sheken im machala kol ha-malveh* — certainly if he forgives the entire loan, this too functions as *hana'at malveh* and would constitute valid *kiddushin* (though the same *ribit* concern would apply). The reason is that *mechilah* creates a genuinely new benefit: where before she owed him a hundred dollars, now she owes nothing. That release of obligation — *hana'at mechilah* — is a fresh and real economic benefit conferred upon her at the moment of *kiddushin*, unlike the original loan disbursement, which is already spent. As Rashi frames it: *hashta hu de-kaya ve-lav afshata de-at malveh* — it is something that exists now, not merely the residue of the original loan.

The *Ran*, however, disputes whether forgiving the loan truly works. His position is that *mechilah* of the loan is not a genuinely *new* benefit — it is simply the erasure of a prior debt. The woman's mind is on the hundred dollars that was given, not on some additional, abstract gain. To be valid, the *kiddushin* must be effected with something above and beyond the original loan, something new introduced at the moment of betrothal. On this view, only the *hana'ah* of the extended repayment term — something genuinely incremental and new — would work, while outright forgiveness of the principal would not. This represents a substantive disagreement between Rashi and the *Ran* about the nature of what constitutes a valid new benefit in this context.

## Conditional Gifts

### Conditional in Other Cas…

Rava raises the case of *matanah al menat lehachzir* — a conditional gift given on the understanding that it will be returned. The paradigmatic instance is the *arba minim* on *Sukkot*: if one person says to another *heilach etrog zeh al menat she-tachzirehu li* — here is my *etrog*, but give it back — and the recipient takes it and uses it and then returns it, he has fulfilled his obligation. Rava himself ruled this way: *netalo ve-hachziro yatza*; if he did not return it, he did not fulfill the obligation.

Rava then applies this principle across several domains. In a sale (*mecher*), if a buyer says "here is payment on condition you return it," no acquisition takes place. For *kiddushin*, if he gives her money on condition she returns it, she is not *mekudeshet*. For *pidyon haben*, if he gives the *kohen* the five *sela'im* on condition they be returned, the son is not redeemed. But for *terumah*, if he gives *terumah* to the *kohen* on condition it be returned — *yatza yedei netinah* — the obligation to give *terumah* is fulfilled, though the practice is forbidden because it creates the appearance that the *kohen* has made a deal or purchased the *terumah*, compromising the dignity of the gift and smacking of *armah* — evasion of the spirit of the law.

### Why It Fails for Kiddush…

The *gemara* finds this account internally inconsistent. Rava himself holds *matanah al menat lehachzir shemah matanah* — a conditional gift counts as a gift. If so, all these cases should work. And if it does not count as a gift, none of them should — including *terumah*, which the *beraita* says *does* work. The formulation in which some cases work and others do not seems untenable.

### Girsa Dispute

Rav Ashi resolves this by correcting the *girsa* of the *beraita*. The proper reading is not that *kiddushin*, *mecher*, and *pidyon haben* all fail while *terumah* alone works. Rather: *be-chulehu kani* — all of them work — *levar me-ishah* — except for the woman. *Mecher* works, *pidyon haben* works, *terumah* works (though one should not do it in practice for the reasons of *armah*), but *kiddushin* does not work. Why does *kiddushin* alone fail?

*She-ein ishah nikneit be-chalippin* — a woman cannot be betrothed through the mechanism of *chalippin*. The *kicha* of *kiddushin* was derived from the model of *Sedei Ephron*, in which Avraham gave Ephron a definitive, unconditional transfer of money. *Chalippin* — the mode of acquisition by exchange or barter, typified by the *sudar* (handkerchief) — operates on a different and incompatible logic. The *sudar* in a *kinyan chalippin* is taken and immediately returned; the exchange is instantaneous and the physical object passes back at once, much as the *etrog* in the *matanah al menat lehachzir* case is returned. Saying to a woman "here is money, give it back" is structurally identical to *chalippin*, and *ein ishah nikneit be-chalippin*. Avraham's gift to Ephron was a clean, permanent, one-directional transfer, and *kiddushin* must replicate that model.

Rav Huna bar Rav Nechemia of Rav Ashi confirms: this was indeed the tradition he had in the name of Rava — the corrected *girsa* is the right one. All the cases except *kiddushin* work; only *kiddushin* fails due to the inherent incompatibility with *chalippin*.
