# Bekhorot 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ruach Ketzerit

### Ketzerit Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דַּדִּין שׁוֹכְבִין כְּשֶׁל אִשָּׁה, כְּרֵיסוֹ צָבָה, טִיבּוּרוֹ יוֹצֵא, נִכְפֶּה — אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת לְיָמִים, רוּחַ קְצָרָה בָּאָה עָלָיו, הַמְאוּשְׁבָּן, וּבַעַל גֶּבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna lists additional blemishes that disqualify a priest from performing the Temple service: One who has **breasts** so large that they **sag like** those **of a woman;** or if **one's belly is swollen** and protrudes; or if **one's navel protrudes;** or if one is **an epileptic, even** if he experiences seizures only **once in** a long **while;** or one **who is afflicted with a melancholy temper;** or **one whose scrotum is** unnaturally **long; or one whose penis is** unnaturally **long** is disqualified from performing the Temple service.

The daf begins, picking up from 44b, at the bottom of the amud where the text introduces *ruach ketzerit* as an additional *mum* — or disqualifying defect — applicable to *kohanim*. The term can be understood in more than one way. Rashi reads it as referring to some form of demonic spirit, but it is equally plausible to understand it as denoting a shortness of breath, something akin to asthma. What is striking here is that we continue to find ourselves in the broader category of *shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* — that is, defects measured not by visible disfigurement but by deviation from the normal range found among the sons of Aharon. It is notable how expansive this category turns out to be.

### Ben Nephalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: מַשְׁתִּינִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים, וְאֵין שׁוֹתִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים. וְתַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מַשְׁתִּינִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים, וְאֵין שׁוֹתִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ לְהַשְׁתִּין מַיִם וְלֹא הִשְׁתִּין, וְנִמְצָא כְּרֵיסוֹ צָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara cites a *halakha* involving a swollen belly. **Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, says** that Torah scholars **may urinate in public** and they need not be concerned with issues of modesty, because holding back from urinating causes bodily harm. **But they may not drink water in public,** as such conduct is unbefitting a Torah scholar. **And this is also taught** in a *baraita*: Torah scholars **may urinate in public, but they may not drink water in public. And there was an incident involving one who sought to urinate, and he did not urinate, and his belly was found to be swollen.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוּחַ קַצְרִית בָּאָה עָלָיו. מַאי נִינְהוּ? תָּנָא: רוּחַ בֶּן נְפָלִים בָּאָה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one **who is afflicted with a melancholy temper** is disqualified from performing the Temple service. The Gemara asks: **What is this** melancholy temper? **A *tanna*** taught: **A fallen spirit,** i.e., the spirit of a demon, **has come upon him.**

The Gemara asks *mani hu* — what exactly is *ruach ketzerit*? The answer offered is: *ruach ben nephalim ba'ah elav*, the spirit of the *ben nephalim* has come upon him. Again, Rashi identifies *ben nephalim* as a kind of evil spirit. However, one might also consider whether the term is related to *nephalim* in the sense of falling down — suggesting a condition like epilepsy, which would connect it thematically to the discussion of epileptic fits that precedes this passage on the daf.

### Rashi on Shtus

Rashi glosses *ben nephalim* as *ruach shtus* — a spirit of foolishness or frenzy — or alternatively as a *sheyd*, a demon. It is possible that for Rashi this condition resembles an epileptic episode, a kind of thrashing fit, consistent with the broader context of conditions discussed in this section of the *mishnah*.

## Genital Defects

### Mushuvano

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְאוּשְׁבָּן וּבַעַל גֶּבֶר. תָּנָא: מְשׁוּבָּן בַּבֵּיצִים, וּבַעַל גֶּבֶר בַּגִּיד. תָּנָא: מְשׁוּבָּן — זֶה הַקַּיָּין, הַגְרַבְתָּן — זֶה בַּעַל קִיק; קַיָּין — בַּבֵּיצִים, גְּרַבְתָּא — בַּגִּיד.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also teaches that **one whose scrotum is** unnaturally **long [*me'ushban*] and one whose penis is** unnaturally **long [*ba'al gever*]** are disqualified from performing the Temple service. The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: **A *meshuban*** is one **with** exceedingly large **testicles, and a *ba'al gever*** is one **with** an unnaturally long **penis.** Similarly, it is **taught** in a *baraita*: **A *meshuban*, this is** synonymous with **the *kayyan*. The *garbetan*, this is** synonymous with **a *ba'al kik*. A *kayyan*** is one with a condition **of the testicles,** while **a *garbeta*** is one with a condition **of the penis.**

Returning to the region of the body that was addressed more obliquely on the previous daf, the *mishnah* introduces the defect of *mushuvano ba'al gever*. A *tanna* explains that *mushuvano* refers to abnormally large testicles, while *ba'al gever* refers to an abnormally large penis. Additional synonyms are offered: *kayan* in relation to the testicles (*kayan b'beytzim*), and *garbtan* — or *garbta* — in relation to the penis (*garbta b'gid*). The root *gever*, denoting maleness, is clearly embedded in the terminology.

### Rukba Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כַּמָּה? מַחְוֵי רַב יְהוּדָה: עַד רְכוּבָּה. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: עַד רְכוּבָּה — פָּסוּל, לְמַעְלָה מִן רְכוּבָּה — כָּשֵׁר. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: עַד רְכוּבָּה — כָּשֵׁר, לְמַטָּה מִן הָרְכוּבָּה — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** is considered too long, which disqualifies the priest from performing the Temple service? **Rav Yehuda pointed** and said: **Until** the **knee. It is** likewise **taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** If one's penis reaches **until** his **knee** he is **disqualified** from performing the Temple service. But if it reaches until just **above** his **knee** he remains **fit. Some say** if his penis reaches **until** the **knee** he is **fit,** but if it passes **below the knee** he is **disqualified.**

The Gemara then asks: *ve'ad kama* — how large would these have to be to constitute a disqualifying defect? Rabbi Yehuda demonstrated that the measure is up to the knees. A *baraita* records Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov as ruling that if it reaches as far as the knees (*ad rukba*), the *kohen* is *pasul*, but if it extends above the knees he is *kasher*. An alternate version reverses this: up to the knees is *kasher*, below the knees is *pasul*. Either way, we are describing quite a significant deformity or deviation from the norm.

### Ein Lo Beytzim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין לוֹ בֵּיצִים, אוֹ אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא בֵּיצָה אַחַת — הֲרֵי זֶה ״מְרוֹחַ אָשֶׁךְ״ הָאָמוּר בְּתוֹרָה. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁנִּימּוֹחוּ אֲשָׁכָיו. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁרוּחַ בַּאֲשָׁכָיו. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁמַּרְאָיו חֲשׁוּכִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one has no testicles, or** if **he has only one testicle, that is** the ***mero'aḥ ashekh* that is stated in the Torah** (see Leviticus 21:20) among the blemishes that disqualify a priest from Temple service. **Rabbi Yishmael says:** A *mero'aḥ ashekh* is **anyone whose testicles were crushed. Rabbi Akiva says:** It is **anyone that** has **wind in his testicles,** i.e., they are swollen. **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says:** *Mero'aḥ ashekh* does not refer to the testicles; rather, the reference is to **anyone whose appearance [*marav*] is** especially **dark [*ḥashukhin*].**

The next *mishnah* addresses a case where a *kohen* has no testicles at all, or has only a single testicle. The ruling is that such a *kohen* falls under the category of *meruach ashech* mentioned in the Torah — which is a specific *mum* derived from a verse. Importantly, this is no longer in the category of *lo shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* but rather a *mum* explicitly grounded in the Torah text.

### Maruch Ashech Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: הָא ״חֲסַר אָשֶׁךְ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! תְּנִי: שֶׁנִּמְרְחוּ אֲשָׁכָיו. קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הַאי ״מַמְרוֹחַ אָשֶׁךְ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! תְּנִי: שֶׁהָרוּחַ בַּאֲשָׁכָיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains why the *tanna'im* interpret *mero'aḥ ashekh* in different ways. It is **difficult for Rabbi Yishmael:** If *mero'aḥ ashekh* is referring to one who lacks two testicles, then the Torah **should have** stated: ***Ḥasar ashekh*,** lacking a testicle, rather than *mero'aḥ ashekh*. Therefore, he **teaches** that it is referring to one **whose testicles were crushed.** This is **difficult for Rabbi Akiva:** If *mero'aḥ ashekh* means one with crushed testicles, then the Torah **should have** stated: ***Mimro'aḥ ashekh*,** crushed testicles, not *mero'aḥ ashekh*. Therefore, he **teaches** that the Torah is referring to one **who** has **wind in his testicles.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס: הַאי ״רוּחַ אָשֶׁךְ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! תְּנִי שֶׁמַּרְאָיו חֲשׁוּכִין, קָסָבַר גּוֹרְעִין וּמוֹסִיפִין וְדוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** This is **difficult for Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus:** If *mero'aḥ ashekh* is referring to one with wind in his testicles, then the Torah **should have** stated: ***Ruaḥ ashekh*,** wind in the testicles. Therefore, he **teaches** that the Torah is speaking of anyone **whose appearance is especially dark.** And how does he derive such an interpretation from the verse? **He holds** that the Sages **subtract and add and interpret homiletically.** Accordingly, the *ḥet* of *mero'aḥ* is removed along with the *alef* of *ashekh*. The *ḥet* is added to *ashekh* and the *alef* is added to *mero'aḥ*, and *mero'aḥ ashekh* is rearranged to state: *Marav ḥashukhin*, his appearance is dark.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ כּוּשִׁי! רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס לָא תָּנֵי ״כּוּשִׁי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But if so, **this is** identical to one whose skin is extremely **black,** and the mishna on 45b explicitly states that such a priest is disqualified from performing the Temple service. The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus** learns from the mishna only that a *mero'aḥ ashekh* is disqualified; he **does not teach** in that mishna the *halakha* that a person with extremely **black** skin is blemished.

What does *meruach ashech* actually mean? The word *ashech* is understood to refer to the testicles. The term *meruach* — related to the root *limroach*, to press or smooth — can mean crushed. That is the first opinion. Rabbi Yishmael objects: the first opinion spoke of a *kohen* who is simply missing a testicle, but the word *meruach* does not naturally connote absence — *chasar ashech mibaei lei*, the Torah should have said "lacking" if that were the meaning. Rabbi Yishmael therefore interprets *meruach* to mean that the testicles have been crushed or dissolved (*she'nimrechu* or *she'nimochu ashkav*).

Rabbi Akiva in turn challenges Rabbi Yishmael: the *mem* in *meruach* is part of the root *limroach*, so using it to form a passive participle meaning "crushed" would require an additional *mem* (something like *mimruach*). Rabbi Akiva instead derives *meruach* from *ruach* — wind — meaning the testicles are somehow inflated or swollen, perhaps from disease (*she'ruach b'ashkav*).

### Chanina Dark Skin

Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus offers an entirely different interpretation of *meruach ashech*, one unrelated to the testicles at all: *kol she'marav chashuchim* — whoever has a dark, swarthy appearance. He arrives at this reading by applying the principle of *gorin u'mosifin ve'dorshin* — rearranging letters in a scriptural phrase to yield a new meaning. Specifically, Rashi explains that by transposing the *chet* and *aleph* in *ashech*, one derives *maro* (appearance) and *choshech* (darkness).

The Gemara notes the consequence of this interpretation: *hainu Cushi* — this would correspond to what a later *mishnah* calls a *Cushi*, a person of Ethiopian or very dark complexion. Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus does not include *Cushi* in his list of defects because he has already addressed that condition here, under *meruach ashech*. According to him, there is therefore one fewer *mum* in the category relating to the testicles. But as he has introduced an entirely new concept — dark skin as a disqualifying *mum* — we are now dealing, as the Gemara itself moves toward, with racial characteristics rather than physical injuries or deformities. This is, as even the Gemara acknowledges, a striking and challenging development. The concern about framing an ethnic or racial characteristic as a *mum* is considerable, and it is a difficulty that the daf will continue to grapple with, touching on entire classes and races of people rather than individual conditions.

One might also note that a condition like inflated testicles — Rabbi Akiva's reading — might constitute a *mum avar*, a transient defect, since it could be caused by a passing infection. Yet one is *pasul* even with a *mum avar*, so this does not ultimately change the ruling.

## Lower Extremity

### Knocking Knees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַקִּישׁ בְּקַרְסוּלָּיו, וּבְאַרְכֻּבּוֹתָיו,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna lists additional blemishes that disqualify a priest from performing the Temple service: One whose legs are crooked and bend inward, causing him to **knock his ankles or his knees** into each other as he walks,

The *mishnah* continues moving down the body. The next defect listed is *makish bekarsulav* — a person whose knees knock together as he walks — or whose ankles knock, *u've'arkovotav*, striking one another. Also listed is *ba'al hapikas* and *ha'ikal*, terms whose meanings the *mishnah* itself goes on to explain.

### Ikal Bowlegged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעַל הַפִּיקִים, הָעִיקֵּל. אֵיזֶהוּ הָעִיקֵּל? כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מַקִּיף פַּרְסוֹתָיו וְאֵין אַרְכֻּבּוֹתָיו נוֹקְשׁוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ. פִּיקָּה יוֹצֵא מִגּוּדָּלוֹ, עֲקֵיבוֹ יוֹצֵא לַאֲחוֹרָיו, פַּרְסוֹתָיו רְחָבוֹת כְּשֶׁל אַוָּוז.
>
> **Translation:** **and a *ba'al happikim*,** and **the *ikkel*. What is the *ikkel*?** It is **anyone who places his feet together and his knees do not knock into each other,** i.e., he is bowlegged. A priest with **a protuberance emerging** alongside **the thumb of his** hand or the big toe of his foot,or one **whose heel emerges** and protrudes **back** from **his** foot, or one **whose feet are wide like** those **of a goose** are all disqualified from performing the Temple service.

*Eizehu ha'ikal* — who is the *ikal*? It is someone who, when seated with ankles together, has knees that point outward: bow-legged. This is distinct from knock-knees, which involves the knees striking one another inward. Like the other conditions in this section, the *ikal* does not appear among the defects listed for animals, suggesting that this is not a specific *mum* derived from a Torah verse, but rather falls under the category of *shaveh bish'ar echav* — not conforming to the standard form of one's priestly brethren.

### Pikas Growth

*Pika yotzei migudlo* — he has a spherical growth protruding from his thumb, or, given the context of the surrounding discussion, possibly from his big toe. The Gemara will shortly revisit this term in a *baraita* alongside *shufnar*. What is immediately striking is how this condition seems out of place amid a list of lower extremity defects — unless it refers to the big toe, in which case it fits more naturally. Artscroll indeed reads it in context as referring to the toe.

### Protruding Heel

*Akevo yotzei leachorav* — his heel protrudes outward. Normally, the lower leg bone connects to the rear of the foot, so the ankle is flush with the back of the heel. This case imagines a condition in which the leg bone is centered in the foot such that the heel protrudes behind it equally, like a post set in the center of a base — the foot extending both forward and backward from the point of attachment. Rabbi Elazar adds: *shoko yotzei b'emtza raglo* — his lower leg enters through the middle of his foot. This clarifies that the lower leg (*shok*), which genuinely means the lower leg and not the thigh, attaches at the midpoint of the foot rather than at the rear.

### Duck Feet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא תֵּימָא דִּטְרִיפָה וְלָא סְדִיקָא, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דִּטְרִיפָה, אַף עַל גַּב דִּסְדִיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: Do not say that** this is referring only to one whose feet are entirely like those of a goose, i.e., his feet **are flat and** his toes **are not split. Rather, once** one's feet **are flat** he is considered blemished, **even though** his toes **are split.**

*Parsotav rechavot keshel avaz* — his feet are wide like those of a goose or duck. Rav Pappa clarifies: do not say that the defect is specifically that they are flat (*treifa*) but not webbed (*sidika*), or that it requires both conditions. Rather, the decisive factor is the flat, wide shape — even if the toes are separated and not webbed, if the foot is flat and wide rather than arched and narrow, it constitutes a defect. Webbed feet would presumably also be problematic, connecting to the subsequent discussion of fused digits.

## Hand Defects

### Fused Fingers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו מוּרְכָּבוֹת זוֹ עַל זוֹ, אוֹ קְלוּטוֹת לְמַעְלָה עַד הַפֶּרֶק — כָּשֵׁר, לְמַטָּה מִן הַפֶּרֶק וַחֲתָכָהּ — כָּשֵׁר, הָיְתָה בָּהּ יְתֶרֶת וַחֲתָכָהּ, אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ עֶצֶם — פָּסוּל, וְאִם לָאו — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** A priest **whose fingers** or toes **are configured one upon the other, or** one whose fingers or toes are **attached,** is likewise disqualified. But if they were attached from **above** the palm of the hand or the bottom of the foot only **until the** middle **joint,** he is **fit.** If they were attached **below the joint,** higher up on the finger or toe, **and he cut** to separate them, he is **fit.** In a case where **there was an extra** finger or toe **on** his hand or foot **and he cut it, if** that extra appendage **contains a bone,** the priest is **disqualified** even after it was cut, **and if** there is **no** bone the priest is **fit.**

*Etzba'otav murkavot zo al gabi zo* — a person whose fingers grow one atop the other rather than lying side by side in normal alignment. Or, alternatively, *o klutot* — they are fused together. Rashi explains that *lema'alah* — "above" — refers to the region from the palm to the first knuckle joint.

### Fusion Levels

The *mishnah* rules *kasher* in a certain case of fused or overlapping digits, and this is where careful reading becomes important. If the fusion runs from the knuckle downward toward the fingertip — *lemata min haperek* — and one then severs the connection, *ve'chatachah kasher*, it is *kasher*. This implies that the condition before cutting may not have been *kasher*. The *kasher* ruling at the start of the *mishnah* appears to apply when the fusion runs from the palm to the knuckle — a case where, perhaps because the range of motion at the fingertips is preserved, or because the toes have less need for independent movement, it is not considered a serious defect.

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* on *shever yad* — a broken hand — and applies it to include *etzba'otav murkavot o klutot lema'alah min haperek*: fingers that are riding on one another or webbed from the knuckle to the nail. The Gemara asks: but does not the *mishnah* say *kasher* if you cut? Yes — that is precisely the point. *Ela belo chatachan* — if you did not cut them, the *baraita* derives from the verse *o shever yado* that it is a *mum*. If you cut them, the *mishnah* says *kasher*. The *kasher* stated in the *mishnah* for the upper portion — from palm to knuckle — means that even without cutting, it is *kasher*, and for the lower portion, cutting makes it *kasher*.

From a practical standpoint, this distinction between toes and fingers is worth noting. Since toes are less visible and require far less range of independent motion than fingers — one does not spread or individually extend one's toes the way one uses fingers — it is understandable that fusion nearer the base is not disqualifying. The webbed or overlapping condition near the tips requires correction, but base-fusion does not.

### Extra Digit Bone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״שֶׁבֶר יָד״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁבֶר יָד. אֶצְבָּעוֹת מוּרְכָּבוֹת לְמַעְלָה אוֹ קְלוּטוֹת לְמַעְלָה מִן הַפֶּרֶק, וְהָאָמְרַתְּ כָּשֵׁר! אֶלָּא וְלֹא חֲתָכָן, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ שֶׁבֶר יָד״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Broken-handed"** (Leviticus 21:19). **I have** derived **only** that **a broken hand** disqualifies a priest. From where do I derive that if **his fingers** or toes **are configured** one **upon** the other, **or** if they are **attached** from the hand or foot **above the** middle **joint and he cut them** to separate them, that he is disqualified? The Gemara interrupts its citation of the *baraita* to ask: **But didn't you say** in the mishna that if he separates his fingers he is **fit? Rather,** the *baraita* is referring to a priest whose fingers or toes are attached above the middle joint **and he did not cut them. From where** is it derived that he is disqualified? **The verse states: "Or broken-handed."**

*Hayta bo yeteret ve'chatachah* — if there was an extra digit and one cut it off: *im yesh bah etzem pasul ve'im lav kasher* — if it had a bone, the *kohen* is *pasul* after the cutting, because by removing a bony digit that was considered a limb, one has now rendered him lacking a limb. If it had no bone, the cut removes what is halachically merely a hanging piece of flesh, and the *kohen* is *kasher*. The implicit premise is that if the extra digit is left uncut, it is generally *pasul* regardless — as will be clarified further through the subsequent discussion about six-digit symmetry.

## Six Digits

### 24 Digits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר בְּיָדָיו וּבְרַגְלָיו, שֵׁשׁ וָשֵׁשׁ — עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹסְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** If there was **an extra** appendage **on his hands and on his feet, six on each** for a total of **twenty-four, Rabbi Yehuda deems** the priest **fit and the Rabbis deem** him **disqualified.**

*Yeterah beyado uv'raglav shesh shesh esrim ve'arba* — if a person has an extra digit on each hand and each foot, giving six on every extremity for a total of twenty-four digits.

### Yehuda Symmetry

Rabbi Yehuda rules this *kasher*. His reasoning, which emerges from the Gemara, is that since the extra digits appear symmetrically on every limb, the condition reflects a kind of internal consistency. Presumably, this is distinct from having a single extra digit on one hand only, where the asymmetry itself would be a problem independent of what the Sages rule here.

### Sages Six Digits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: וּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, וַתְּהִי עוֹד מִלְחָמָה, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי אִישׁ מָדוֹן אֶצְבַּע יָדָיו וְאֶצְבַּע רַגְלָיו שֵׁשׁ וָשֵׁשׁ״, מָר סָבַר בִּגְנוּתֵיהּ מִשְׁתַּעֵי, וּמָר סָבַר בִּשְׁבָחֵיהּ מִשְׁתַּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that verse, **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: And both derived** their opinions **from one verse,** as it is stated with regard to David's war against the Philistines: **"And there was again war** at Gath" (II Samuel 21:20), **and it is** also **written** in that same verse: **"And there was a champion, who had on every hand six fingers, and on every foot six toes,** twenty-four in number." One **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that the verse **speaks of negative** qualities of the champion, and therefore it may be derived from here that extra appendages are blemishes; and one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that the verse **speaks in his praise,** which means that extra appendages are not blemishes at all.

The Sages rule *pasul* even when the six digits appear symmetrically on all four extremities. The Gemara proceeds to analyze the scriptural verse in Chronicles that describes the Philistine warrior from Gat who had six fingers on each hand and six toes on each foot — twenty-four in total. One view reads the verse as disparaging this warrior, pointing to his extra digits as a sign of abnormality. The other reads it as praising him. Rabbi Yehuda draws support from a famous story in which Rabbi Tarfon, confronted with such a person, declared *kamoch yarbu be'Yisrael* — may there be many like you in Israel. Rabbi Yosei retorts: that is not what Rabbi Tarfon said. He said *kamoch yim'atu mamzerim ve'nesinim be'Yisrael* — may those like you cause a decrease in *mamzerim* in Israel. The story behind this remark is that there was a widespread belief that certain physical deformities result from sexual sin, and Rabbi Tarfon was implying that seeing such a result publicly would deter adultery and thus reduce the number of *mamzerim*. It was not a compliment to the man standing before him. The implication for Rabbi Yosei is clear: Rabbi Tarfon considered this a defect.

## Lower Extremity…

### Shufnar Calf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: בַּעַל הַפִּיקִּין וְהַשּׁוּפְנָר. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּעַל הַפִּיקִּין — שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ כְּסָתוֹת הַרְבֵּה, שׁוּפְנָר — שֶׁאֵין לוֹ כְּסָתוֹת כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** It was also **taught** in a *baraita*: **A *ba'al hakippin* and the *shufnar***  are blemished. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A *ba'al happikin*** is one **who has large protuberances** on his leg, while **a *shufnar*** is one **who has no protuberances at all.**

Returning to the *baraita* that lists various lower extremity conditions, the text mentions *ba'al hapikin* alongside *ha'shufnar*. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ba'al hapikin she'yesh lo kesutot harbeh* — he has an extremely large calf muscle, perhaps like what is seen in elephantiasis. *Shufnar she'ein lo kesutot kol ikar* — one who has essentially no calf at all, a leg as thin as a chicken leg with no muscular mass. Both extremes — the grossly enlarged calf and the entirely absent calf — constitute a defect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּיקָּה יוֹצָא מִגּוּדָּלוֹ, וַעֲקֵיבוֹ יוֹצֵא לַאֲחוֹרָיו. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שׁוֹקוֹ יוֹצֵא בָּאֶמְצַע רַגְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that one with **a protuberance emerging** alongside **the thumb of his** hand or the big toe of his foot, **and** one **whose heel emerges** and protrudes **back** from **his** foot are disqualified. **Rabbi Elazar says:** The description: One whose heel emerges and protrudes back from his foot, means **his calf,** i.e., his lower leg, **emerges from the middle** of **his foot.**

The *baraita* also restates *pika yotzei migudlo* — the spherical growth from the thumb or big toe — and *akev yotzei leachorav* — the heel protruding backward. Rabbi Elazar adds the condition of *shoko yotzei b'emtza raglo*, the lower leg bone entering the middle of the foot.

## Hand Defects…

### Digit Limb Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁנִּסְפֶּרֶת עַל גַּבֵּי הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And this** is the *halakha* only in a case **where it can be counted along the back of the hand** or foot, i.e., the extra digit is situated in a row with his other fingers or toes. Only in such a situation is an additional appendage considered a distinct limb to the extent that if it contains a bone he is disqualified even after it has been cut away.

Rabbah bar bar Chanah said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the extra digit has the status of a full limb — such that cutting it off renders the *kohen* lacking a limb — only if it is counted with the fingers of the hand, meaning it lies in a normal row alongside them. If it protrudes from an unusual location and is not aligned with the other fingers, it is never considered a genuine limb, and cutting it off does not create a deficiency, even if it contains a bone.

### Tuma Extra Digits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יְתֵירָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ עֶצֶם, וְאֵין בָּהּ צִפּוֹרֶן — מְטַמָּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, וּמְטַמָּא בְּאֹהֶל, וְעוֹלָה לְמִנְיַן מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּבְנִסְפֶּרֶת עַל גַּב הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to extra appendages **the Sages taught:** In the case of **an extra** appendage that was cut from a corpse, **which contains a bone,** then even if **it does not have a nail** it **imparts ritual impurity** both **through contact and by carrying** it, **and it imparts impurity in a tent,**as is the *halakha* with regard to the limb of a corpse. **And** if the extra appendage has no flesh surrounding the bone and therefore does not impart the impurity of a limb from a corpse, it is **included in the count of 125** bones, which constitute a majority of the number of bones in a human being. A majority of bones imparts impurity in a tent, even if there is no flesh on any of them. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And** it is considered a limb only **when it can be counted along the back of the hand** or foot.

A *tanna* teaches: an extra digit that contains a bone, even if it lacks a fingernail, causes *tumah* through *maga* and *massa* — touching and carrying — and also causes *tumas ohel*. Furthermore, its bone counts toward the number 125. This last point requires explanation.

The standard rabbinic enumeration of bones in the human body is 248 — *ramach eivarim* — as we invoke in the *Mi Sheberach* prayer. *Tumas ohel*, the transmission of corpse-impurity to everything beneath the same roof, is triggered not only by a minimum volume of flesh (*kezayis*) but also by the majority of the bones of a body. The majority of 248 is 125 (since 124 would be exactly half). Therefore, 125 bones from a single corpse under one roof transmit *tumas ohel*. Crucially, the number 125 is fixed and does not adjust based on the actual bone count of the individual. If a person had extra digits and thus more than 248 bones, one does not recalculate the threshold upward. The bone from that extra digit, if it was aligned with the fingers of the hand, counts toward the 125 even though the total body count exceeds 248. The principle is *zil batar ruba de'inshi* — we follow the standard of the majority of people.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּבָר זֶה רַבֵּינוּ הַגָּדוֹל אֲמָרוֹ, הַמָּקוֹם יִהְיֶה בְּעֶזְרוֹ: יְתֶרֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ עֶצֶם וְאֵין בָּהּ צִפּוֹרֶן — מְטַמָּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, וְאֵין מְטַמָּא בְּאֹהֶל. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּבְשֶׁאֵינָהּ נִסְפֶּרֶת עַל גַּב הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: This matter was stated by our great rabbi,** Rav, **may the Omnipresent** come **to his assistance. An extra** appendage **that contains a bone and does not have a nail imparts ritual impurity through contact and by carrying, but it does not impart impurity in a tent. Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And** it does not impart impurity in a tent only **when it is not counted along the back of the hand** or foot.

Rav Chisda then cites in the name of *Rabbeinu HaGadol* — identified by Rashi as Rav, and by Rabbeinu Gershom as Rebbi — a seemingly contradictory ruling: an extra digit with a bone but without a nail causes *tumah* through touch and carrying but *not* through *tumas ohel*. Rabbah bar bar Chanah reconciles this in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: this second case refers to a digit not aligned with the fingers of the hand. Not being in row with the fingers means it does not qualify as a limb, and therefore does not cause *tumas ohel*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עָשׂוּ דִּבְרֵיהֶם כְּדִבְרֵי נְבִיאוּת, מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי אֵבֶר הוּא — בְּאֹהֶל נָמֵי תְּטַמֵּא, וְאִי לָאו אֵבֶר הוּא — בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא נָמֵי לָא לִיטַמֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The Sages **rendered their statements** in this case **like statements of prophecy,** i.e., there is no rationale for this *halakha*, as **whichever way you** look at it there is a difficulty: **If** a digit that is not counted with the other digits **is** considered **a limb, it should impart impurity in a tent as well; and if it is not a limb, it also should not impart impurity through contact or by carrying.**

Rabbi Yochanan (or, according to one reading, Rabbi Chanina) remarks: *asu divreihem ke'divrei nevi'os* — they have spoken like prophets. As Tosafot notes, this is not a compliment; it means the ruling appears arbitrary, asserted without obvious logical foundation. The critique is: *mamah nafshach* — make up your mind. If the digit not aligned with the hand is considered a genuine limb, let it cause *tumas ohel*. If it is not considered a limb, why does it cause *tumah* even through touch and carrying?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: מִשּׁוּם עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה נָגְעוּ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ said in the name of Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika:** This appendage imparts impurity through contact and by carrying **because** the Sages **touched upon it,** i.e., issued a ruling concerning it, and determined that the *halakhot* of **a bone** the volume of **a barley grain** apply to it, i.e., it is considered a bone fragment and not a limb. Accordingly, it does not impart impurity in a tent.

Two answers are given. Rav Huna bar Manoach, citing Rav Acha son of Rav Ika, says: *mishum etzem kese'orah nago bah* — even if it is not a limb, it remains a bone of the body, and a bone of the body causes *tumah* through touch and carrying even at the size of a barley grain. So there is no contradiction: it causes *maga* and *massa* as a bone, not as a limb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ נִסְפֶּרֶת, אַטּוּ נִסְפֶּרֶת. אִי הָכִי, שֶׁאֵינָהּ נִסְפֶּרֶת בְּאֹהֶל נָמֵא תְּטַמֵּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** It is a rabbinic **decree** that an appendage **that is not counted** with the other digits imparts impurity through contact and carrying, **due to** a case where the appendage is **counted** with the other digits,in which case it is considered a limb and imparts impurity in a tent. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** then an appendage **that is not counted should impart impurity in a tent as well.**

Rav Pappa disagrees — or supplements. He says it is a *gezeira*: even though this digit does not qualify as a limb for purposes of *tumas ohel*, the Rabbis gave it *tumah* through touch and carrying as a safeguard against confusing it with the case where the digit *is* aligned with the hand and thus fully a limb. The Gemara then asks: if it is a safeguard, why not extend the rabbinic *gezeira* to *tumas ohel* as well? The answer: the Rabbis intentionally left a distinction recognizable, so that one would not come to burn *terumah* and *kodshim* based on rabbinic *tumah* alone — a classic technique of making rabbinic *tumah* slightly less severe than its biblical counterpart so the two remain distinguishable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַדוּ רַבָּנַן הֶיכֵּירָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִישְׂרְפוּ עֲלַיְיהוּ תְּרוּמָה וְקָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The Sages implemented a distinction** by decreeing this appendage imparts impurity only through contact and carrying, **in order that** people **will** know that this impurity applies by rabbinic law and not by Torah law. And therefore they will **not** come **to burn *teruma*,** i.e., the portion of produce designated for the priest, **or consecrated** items that have been rendered impure **on** account of this type of impurity, as by Torah law they are ritually pure and may not be burned.

The implication of Rav Pappa's position is particularly striking: he seems to hold that a digit not in line with the hand is not merely a non-limb — it may not even be considered truly part of the body at all, more like an external growth. The only *tumah* it generates is rabbinic, solely to prevent confusion with the aligned-digit case. This stands in contrast to the first answer, which treated the bone as genuinely part of the body even if not constituting a limb.

## Women's Bones

### 125 Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: רוֹב בִּנְיָנוֹ וְרוֹב מִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁל מֵת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ רוֹבַע — טְמֵאִין. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ רוֹב בִּנְיָנוֹ? שְׁנֵי שׁוֹקַיִם וְיָרֵךְ אֶחָד, הוֹאִיל וְרוֹב גּוֹבְהוֹ בְּגָדוֹל. אֵיזֶהוּ רוֹב מִנְיָנוֹ? מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to impurity imparted by extra appendages, **we learned** in a mishna **there** (*Oholot* 2:1): **A majority of the** skeletal **structure or a majority of the number** of bones in the corpse of a **dead** person, **even when there is not a quarter** of a *kav* of bones among them, **are impure.** Concerning the ruling of this mishna, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **What is** considered **a majority of the** skeletal **structure** of a corpse? It is **two calves and one thigh, since** this constitutes **a majority of the height of an adult. What is** considered **a majority of the number** of bones? **One hundred and twenty-five** bones.

A *mishnah* in *Ohalos* establishes that *rov binyano ve'rov minyano shel mes* — either the majority of the structural skeleton or the majority of the bone count of a corpse — causes *tumas ohel* even without a *rova* of volume. *Eizu rov binyano*: the structural majority is defined as two lower legs (*shokayim*) and one thigh (*yarech*), on the reasoning that most of a person's height is concentrated in the legs. *Eizu rov minyano*: the majority of the bone count is 125, since the standard total is 248. Ravina asks Rava: why does the *tanna* bother stating the number 125 — we know how to divide 248 by two. The Gemara answers that the *tanna* teaches this *l'ashmui'inan keditanya*: the number 125 is fixed regardless of the actual bone count of the individual.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: תַּנָּא מִנְיָינָא אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כִּדְתַנְיָא: חָסֵר, שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא מָאתַיִם, וְיָתֵר, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מָאתַיִם וּשְׁמוֹנִים וְאֶחָד — כּוּלָּן עוֹלִין לְמִנְיַן מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה, זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא דְאִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava:** Is the ***tanna* coming to teach us a number?** It is obvious that as there are 248 bones in the human body, 125 of them constitute a majority. Rava responded: The *tanna* **teaches us** that the *halakha* is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a corpse that **lacks** some bones and **has only 200** bones, **or** one that has **extra** and **has 281** bones, **all of them are included in the count of 125.** In other words, whether the number of 125 is in a majority or minority of that particular corpse, this number of bones imparts impurity, as one **follows the majority of people,** and most people have 248 bones.

A *baraita* spells this out explicitly: whether a person has as few as 200 bones (missing many digits) or as many as 281 (having extensive extra digits), the threshold for *tumas ohel* through majority of bone count remains 125. Rashi notes that if someone is missing eight digits, each finger and toe apparently counting as approximately six bones, one can arrive at a total as low as 200. For extra digits, Rashi acknowledges he cannot fully reconstruct the arithmetic, but the principle holds: *zil batar ruba de'inshi* — go by the majority of people.

### 252 Bones Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתַלְמִידָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל שֶׁשָּׁלְקוּ זוֹנָה אַחַת שֶׁנִּתְחַיְּיבָה שְׂרֵיפָה לַמֶּלֶךְ, בָּדְקוּ וּמָצְאוּ בָּהּ מָאתַיִם וַחֲמִשִּׁים וּשְׁנַיִם, אָמַר לָהֶם: שֶׁמָּא בְּאִשָּׁה בְּדַקְתֶּם, שֶׁהוֹסִיף לָהּ הַכָּתוּב שְׁנֵי צִירִים וּשְׁנֵי דְּלָתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the number of limbs or bones in the human body, **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** There was **an incident involving the students of Rabbi Yishmael, who boiled** the corpse of **a prostitute who was sentenced by the king to** death by **burning,** in order to separate her flesh from her bones. **They examined and found she** had **252** limbs. They were puzzled by their finding, as the Sages say that the human body has only 248 limbs. Shmuel **said to them: Perhaps you examined** the corpse of **a woman, as the verse added to her two hinges and two doors,** for a total of 252 limbs.

Rabbi Yehuda citing Shmuel introduces what has become a celebrated passage about empirical investigation in rabbinic medicine. The students of Rabbi Yishmael acquired the body of a woman who had been executed by the Roman government — a prostitute condemned to death. They boiled her body to strip away the flesh so as to examine the skeletal anatomy. They counted and found 252 bones, four more than the standard 248.

Rabbi Yishmael's response: *shema be'isha bedaktem* — perhaps you examined a woman? This was not a dismissal of their count but an explanation: women have additional bones, as the Torah has endowed them with *shenei tzirim ushnei delatot* — two hinges and two doors — understood as relating to the pelvic region and its role in childbirth. Modern anatomy records 206 bones in the adult human skeleton, with no consistent documented difference between men and women, though the Gemara clearly assumes otherwise. Whether the Gemara's figure of 248 might have included certain sesamoid bones or other structures counted differently is a question beyond the scope of this discussion.

### Tsirrim Hinges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁצִּירִים לַבַּיִת — כָּךְ צִירִים לָאִשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתִּכְרַע וַתֵּלֶד כִּי נֶהֶפְכוּ עָלֶיהָ צִרֶיהָ״. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁדְּלָתוֹת לַבַּיִת — כָּךְ דְּלָתוֹת לָאִשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא סָגַר דַּלְתֵי בִטְנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar says: Just as a house has hinges [*tzirim*], so too, a woman has hinges, as it is stated: "And she bowed herself and brought forth, for her pains [*tzireha*] came suddenly upon her"** (I Samuel 4:19). **Rabbi Yehoshua says: Just as a house has doors, so too, a woman has doors, as it is stated: "Because it did not shut the doors of my womb"** (Job 3:10).

Rabbi Eliezer employs the house metaphor that runs throughout rabbinic descriptions of female anatomy: *keshem she'tzirim labayit kach tzirim la'isha* — just as there are hinges on a house, so there are hinges on a woman. The verse cited is from the account of Rachel's labor: *vatichra vateleid ki nehepchu aleiha tzireiha* — she crouched and gave birth, for her *tzirim* had turned upon her. The word *tzir* here means birth pangs, but the *derasha* reads it as "hinges" — the hinges of the womb turned open.

### Delathim Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמַּפְתֵּחַ לַבַּיִת, כָּךְ מַפְתֵּחַ לָאִשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּפְתַּח אֶת רַחְמָהּ״. לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא קַשְׁיָא דְּתַלְמִידֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל! דִּלְמָא אַיְּידֵי דְּזוּטַר אִתְּמוֹחֵי מִתְּמַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: Just as** there is **a key to a house, so too,** there is **a key to a woman, as it is stated: "And He opened her womb"** (Genesis 30:22). The Gemara challenges: The incident **involving the students of Rabbi Yishmael,** who found that the woman had only four additional limbs rather than five, i.e., there was no key, poses **a difficulty for Rabbi Akiva.** The Gemara responds: **Perhaps since** it is **small, it dissolved** in the water when they boiled the corpse.

Rabbi Yehoshua extends the metaphor: *keshem she'delatot labayit kach delatot la'isha* — just as there are doors on a house, so there are doors on a woman. The verse is from Iyov: *ki lo sagar daltei bitni* — for You did not close the doors of my womb. The doors opened; the doors are an extra bone.

Rabbi Akiva adds a fifth structure to the count: *keshem she'mafteach labayit kach mafteach le'isha* — just as there is a key to a house, so there is a key for a woman. This fifth bone, perhaps understood as something that mechanically enables the opening of the birth canal, is derived from *vayiftach et rachmah* — God opened her womb. The *mafteach* is the key.

The Gemara raises an objection from the empirical evidence: the students of Rabbi Yishmael found only four extra bones, not five. The Gemara answers: *aidi d'zutra asmuchi mitmach* — because the key bone is very small, and since they boiled the body, it may have dissolved and been missed. The fifth bone exists but was not detected.

### Mafteiach Key

### Extra Bones Tuma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: וְכוּלָּן אֵין מְטַמְּאִין בְּאֹהֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אָדָם כִּי יָמוּת בְּאֹהֶל״, דָּבָר הַשָּׁוֶה לְכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: And all of them,** i.e., all the limbs found only in a woman, **do not impart impurity in a tent, as it is stated: "This is the law: When a person dies in a tent"** (Numbers 19:14), indicating that only **an item that is equal for all people,** male and female, imparts impurity in a tent.

Rav — or according to some readings Rava — rules: *ve'chulan ein metamim be'ohel* — none of these extra bones found in women cause *tumas ohel*. Rashi explains that without flesh, a bone does not attain the status of a limb for purposes of *tumas ohel*, and these bones are entirely internal. More importantly, the Gemara derives this from the verse *zot hatorah adam ki yamut be'ohel* — this is the law when a person dies in a tent. The implication is *davar hashaveh lechol adam* — it must apply to all human beings equally, to both men and women. Since these bones are unique to women — they are not part of the 248 bones shared across humanity — they do not trigger *tumas ohel*. They would presumably still count as bones of the body for other purposes, but they do not shift the 125 threshold, which remains anchored in the universal human count.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּבְאִישׁ לֵיכָּא? וְהָכְתִיב: ״צִירִים אֲחָזוּנִי כְּצִירֵי יוֹלֵדָה״! צִירֵי בָשָׂר. וְהָכְתִיב: ״אֲדֹנִי בַּמַּרְאָה נֶהֶפְכוּ צִירַי״! הָכָא נָמֵי צִירֵי בָשָׂר. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, מָאתַיִם וְאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? לָא בְּאִישׁ וְלָא בְּאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: And are there no** hinges **in a man? But isn't it written: "Pangs [*tzirim*] have taken hold upon me, as the pangs of a woman in travail"** (Isaiah 21:3)? The Gemara responds: The verse is referring specifically to **hinges of flesh,** which are not considered limbs, as they contain no bones. The Gemara persists: **But isn't it written: "My lord, by reason of the vision my pains [*tzirai*] come"** (Daniel 10:16)? The Gemara responds: **Here too,** the verse is referring to **hinges of flesh.** The Gemara notes: **So too, it is reasonable** that the hinges of a male are only of flesh, **as if you do not say so,** then as far as the **248** limbs that the Sages stated constitute a human body, **how can you find** them? **Neither in a man nor in a woman.**

This circles back to a broader point about the number 248. Since both Rabbi Akiva's five extra women's bones and Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua's four are distinctive to women, the standard of 248 *eivarim* — and by extension the 125 threshold — is defined by the male skeleton. This explains why we continue to recite *ramach eivarim u'shesah gidav* for all people, men and women alike, without adjusting the number for women. As Abaye notes, there is also a reference to *tzirim* in relation to a man in a prophetic vision (*adoni bemareh nehepchu tzirai*), but that usage describes only a *flesh* sensation, not a bone structure, so it does not undermine the asymmetry.

## Six Digits…

### Scriptural 6-6-24

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מָה לִי דִּכְתִיב ״שֵׁשׁ וָשֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע מִסְפָּר״? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״שֵׁשׁ וָשֵׁשׁ״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: שֵׁית דִּידֵיהּ וְשֵׁית דְּכַרְעֵיהּ, כְּתַב ״עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע״. וְאִי כְּתַב ״עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הוּא חֲמֵשׁ מֵהַאי גִּיסָא וּשְׁבַע מֵהַאי גִּיסָא, כְּתַב ״שֵׁשׁ וָשֵׁשׁ״. ״מִסְפָּר״ — בְּנִסְפֶּרֶת עַל גַּבֵּי הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that verse, **Rabba says: Why do I** need that **which is written** in the verse: "On every hand **six** fingers, **and** on every foot **six** toes, **twenty-four in number"?** Isn't it obvious that one who has six appendages on each limb will have twenty-four in total? Rabba explains: All of these details **are necessary, as, had the Merciful One written** only **six and six, I would say** there were **six of his hand and six of his foot,** i.e., he had only twelve appendages. Therefore, the Merciful One **wrote "twenty-four." And had** the Merciful One **written** only **"twenty-four," I would say** he had **five** fingers **on this side and seven** fingers **on that side.** Therefore, the verse **wrote six and six.** The verse concludes with the term **"in number" to** indicate that these fingers **were counted** in a single row **on the back of the hand.**

Returning to the question of six digits on hands and feet, the Gemara examines why the verse in Chronicles specifies *sheish sheish esrim ve'arba* — six, six, and twenty-four. Could the Torah not have simply stated one number and let us calculate? Rava explains: had the verse stated only "six and six," I might have assumed it meant six on one hand and six on one foot, not all four extremities. Had it stated only "twenty-four," I might have assumed an uneven distribution — five on one hand and seven on another. The triple specification — six, six, and twenty-four — conveys that every single extremity had exactly six. Furthermore, the word *mispar* in the verse is taken as an allusion to the concept of *benisperet al gabei hayad* — that the extra digit is counted in line with the other fingers, and therefore attains full limb status.

### Mamzerim Defect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁבָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, יָתֵר בְּיָדָיו וּבְרַגְלָיו שֵׁשׁ וָשֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע. אָמַר לוֹ: כְּמוֹתְךָ יִרְבּוּ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל! אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? כָּךְ אָמַר לוֹ: כְּמוֹתְךָ יִתְמַעֲטוּ מַמְזֵירֵי וּנְתִינֵי מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dispute concerning whether or not extra appendages are a blemish, it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** There was **an incident involving one man who came before Rabbi Tarfon,** and he had **extra** appendages **on his hands and on his feet, six and six,** for a total of **twenty-four** appendages. Rabbi Tarfon **said to him: There should increase among the Jewish people** those that are **like you,** i.e., strong men. Clearly, extra appendages are not a blemish but a praiseworthy condition. **Rabbi Yosei said to** Rabbi Yehuda: You seek to bring **a proof from there? This** is what Rabbi Tarfon actually **said to him:** All ***mamzerim* and Gibeonites should be like you,** i.e., physically weak, so that **they should decrease from the Jewish people.**

The story of Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Yosei, discussed above in the context of Rabbi Yehuda's position, bears re-examination here. The question of whether the biblical verse praises or disparages the Philistine warrior governs how one interprets the halachic implication. One view reads the verse as complimentary — *leshvachei mishtayei* — seeing twenty-four digits on a powerful warrior as an asset. This is Rabbi Yehuda's reading, and it coheres with his willingness to rule *kasher* when the extra digits are symmetrical. The other view reads it as disparaging — *lagnusei mishtayei* — a deformity, and this is the Sages' position, who rule *pasul* regardless. Rabbi Tarfon's statement, properly understood per Rabbi Yosei, was not *kamoch yarbu be'Yisrael* as a blessing of the man, but an invocation rooted in the assumption that such traits signal illegitimate birth — and thus more visible consequences of forbidden unions would lead to less adultery and fewer *mamzerim*. Rabbi Yehuda evidently reads the story differently, taking the compliment at face value.

## Handedness

### Iter Lefty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן — אִיטֵּר, בֵּין בַּיָּד בֵּין בָּרֶגֶל — פָּסוּל. הַשּׁוֹלֵט בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו — רַבִּי פּוֹסֵל וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין. מָר סָבַר — כְּחִישׁוּתָא אַתְחִלָא בְּיָמִין, וּמָר סָבַר — בְּרִיּוּתָא אַתְחִלָא בִּשְׂמֹאל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this dispute **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: One whose **left** side is dominant, **whether** his left **hand or** his left **foot,** is **disqualified** from performing the Temple service. Furthermore, in the case of **one who has control of both of his hands, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems** him **disqualified and the Rabbis deem** him **fit.** The Gemara explains this dispute: One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that he learned to control his left hand because **a weakness began in** his **right** hand. He is therefore disqualified, as his right hand is weak. **And** one **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **a strength began in** his **left** hand that allowed him to control both hands. Nevertheless, his right hand remains strong.

The *mishnah* concludes with *iter bein beyad bein beragel* — a left-handed person, whether the left-handedness manifests in the hand or the foot, is *pasul* for Temple service. How does one determine left-footedness? Rashi suggests: have the person stand and ask him to walk forward, and observe which foot he extends first. In most cases, foot dominance corresponds to hand dominance.

### Ambidextrous

One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that he learned to control his left hand because **a weakness began in** his **right** hand. He is therefore disqualified, as his right hand is weak. **And** one **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **a strength began in** his **left** hand that allowed him to control both hands. Nevertheless, his right hand remains strong.

*Ha'sholet bi'shtei yadav* — one who is ambidextrous. Rebbi rules *pasul*; the Sages rule *kasher*. The Gemara explains the dispute as follows: *mar savar tachishusha atchila beyamin* — one view holds that ambidexterity reflects a weakness of the right hand; *u'mar savar briyusa atchila bismol* — the other holds it reflects a strength of the left hand. The natural reading is that a weakness of the right is a defect and renders one *pasul*, while a strength of the left is not problematic in itself. The Sages are thus saying that ambidexterity, understood as a left-hand strength, does not disqualify. Rebbi holds that a weakened right renders one *pasul*.

### Rashi Right Dominance

Rashi's analysis of why left-handedness is disqualifying is important and conceptually distinctive. Rather than framing the problem as a simple deviation from the normal range — as one might say of, say, an *outie* belly button — Rashi locates the issue in the requirements of the *avodah* itself. The Torah repeatedly specifies the use of the right hand in priestly service: *utaval etzba'o* — he shall dip his finger — and wherever the Torah mentions the finger of a *kohen*, it means the right hand specifically. Rashi therefore says that *iter beyad pasul* because the *avodah* demands right-hand dipping, and that must also be the dominant hand. A left-handed *kohen* cannot properly perform this function.

For left-footedness, Rashi extends the logic: *la'amod uleshares* — there is a prescribed manner of standing and moving in service that is modeled on how the other *kohanim* stand, which involves leading with the right. A left-footed *kohen* does not conform to this.

What is notable about this framing is that it transforms the disqualification from a *mum* category or even a *shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* issue into a problem of the *ma'aseh avodah* — the act of service itself. This explains why ambidexterity is disputed: since an ambidextrous *kohen* still possesses a functional right hand and could in principle use it dominantly, the Sages hold that the *avodah* requirement is met. Rebbi, however, holds that if the right is weakened — or if neither hand is truly dominant — the condition of right-hand primacy required by the *avodah* is not satisfied. The practical consequence of Rashi's approach is also worth noting: it would seem that left-footedness alone should not be a problem unless one construes the service requirement as extending to the feet as well, which Rashi does. Notably, the *mishnah* explicitly includes left-footedness in the *pasul* ruling, and Rashi treats either condition as independently disqualifying.
