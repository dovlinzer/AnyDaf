# Bava Kamma 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tashlumei Nezek

### Mishna: Tashlumei Nezek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַבְתִּי בְּתַשְׁלוּמֵי נִזְקוֹ. ״חַבְתִּי בְּנִזְקוֹ״ לָא קָתָנֵי, אֶלָּא ״בְּתַשְׁלוּמֵי נִזְקוֹ״,
>
> **Translation:** In any case in which I facilitated part of the damage it caused, **I am liable for payments** of restitution **for damage it** caused, as if I were the one who facilitated the entire damage. The Gemara notes that the mishna **does not teach: I am liable for the damage it** caused, **rather:** I am liable **for payments** of restitution **for damage it** caused. The Hebrew terms: Payments of restitution [*tashlumim*], and: To complete [*lehashlim*], share the same Hebrew root. This alludes to the *halakha* that the payment of damages is required only in order to complete the injured party's compensation, which is already partially accounted for, as the injured party is able to recover his dead animal's current value by selling its carcass. Accordingly, the one liable for the damage is not required to pay the animal's prior value; rather, he must pay only the difference in its value from before it was damaged and its current state.

The *mishna* that enumerates the *avot nezikin* concludes with the phrase *chavti betashlumei nizko* — "I have incurred the obligation of *tashlumei nizko*," the compensation for the damage. The *gemara* immediately notices a subtle but significant linguistic choice: the *mishna* does not say *chavti benizko*, "I am liable for the damage itself," but rather *betashlumei nizko*, for the *payment* of the damage. This leads directly to a *beraita* that explicates the phrase.

### Owners & Carcass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנֵינָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: תַּשְׁלוּמֵי נֶזֶק – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מִטַּפְּלִין בַּנְּבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **We** already **learned this, as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The mishna uses the term: **Payments** of restitution **for damage,** as opposed to simply stating: One is liable for the damage caused, to allude to the *halakha* that the one who is liable must pay only for the decrease in the value of the animal. This assumes that the injured party is able to recover his animal's current value by selling the carcass. Therefore, the mishna **teaches that the owner** of the injured animal **attends to,** i.e., retains ownership of, **the animal carcass** so that, if he wishes to, he may sell it and keep the proceeds.

The *beraita* teaches: *tashlumei nezek melamed shehabealim metaplin benevelah* — the phrase "payment of damage" comes to teach that the owners, meaning the owners of the animal that was killed or injured, retain responsibility for and ownership of the carcass. The practical illustration is straightforward. If a bull gores a cow worth one hundred dollars, and the carcass is now worth ten dollars, the owner of the bull pays ninety dollars — the loss in value — and does not "buy" the dead animal. The original owner continues to own the carcass. It is not a case of "you break it, you buy it." The *mazik* caused a one-hundred-dollar cow to become a ten-dollar carcass; he pays the ninety-dollar difference, and the *nizak* retains the carcass.

### Makeh Behema Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״מַכֵּה נֶפֶשׁ בְּהֵמָה, יְשַׁלְּמֶנָּה״ – אַל תִּקְרֵי ״יְשַׁלְּמֶנָּה״, אֶלָּא ״יַשְׁלִימֶנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Ami said: As the verse states: "One who strikes an animal shall pay for it [*yeshallemenna*]"** (Leviticus 24:18). **Do not read** the final word ***yeshallemenna*,** meaning he shall pay for it; **rather,** read it as though it were vocalized as ***yashlimenna*,** meaning he shall complete it, to teach that he shall complete the injured party's compensation, which is already partially accounted for by the injured party's right to sell his animal's carcass.

The *gemara* asks: *mina hani milei* — from where is this derived? Perhaps the *mazik* would have to pay the full one hundred dollars and take the carcass in return. Rabbi Ami answers from the *pasuk* in Shemos: *makeh nefesh behemah yeshalmena* — one who strikes and kills an animal shall pay for it. The *gemara* reads this through the lens of *al tikrei yeshalmena ella yashlimena* — do not read "shall pay for it" but rather "shall complete" or "shall supplement" the value. That is, if a person kills a cow worth one hundred that is now worth ten, he does not pay the full hundred; he supplements the difference, paying ninety. The object itself remains with its original owner; the *mazik* pays only for the damage his actions caused.

### Shomer & Hatreifah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״אִם טָרֹף יִטָּרֵף יְבִאֵהוּ עֵד, הַטְּרֵפָה לֹא יְשַׁלֵּם״ – ״עַד טְרֵפָה יְשַׁלֵּם״, טְרֵפָה עַצְמָהּ לֹא יְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said** that this *halakha* is derived **from here:** The verse states with regard to a case where an animal was entrusted with a paid bailee who did not fulfill his duty to safeguard it, and the animal was attacked by a wild beast: **"If it be torn in pieces, let him bring a witness, the torn animal he shall not pay"** (Exodus 22:12). Rav Kahana expounds the verse to mean that **he shall pay** only up **until** the value of the **torn animal;** but **he shall not pay** for the **torn animal itself.** In other words, he pays only the difference in value between the animal before it was injured and its current torn state. If the owner of the injured animal wishes to fully recover his loss he must sell the animal's carcass and keep the proceeds.

Rav Kahana offers a different *pasuk* as the source. In the case of a *shomer* whose animal was killed by a wild beast, the *pasuk* reads: *im tarof yitaref yevi'eihu ed hatereifah lo yeshalem* — if it is torn up he shall bring testimony, and for the *tereifah* itself he shall not pay. Rav Kahana understands *yevi'eihu ad hatereifah yeshalem* — he pays up to the value of the dead animal — but *tereifah atzmah lo yeshalem* — for the carcass itself he does not pay. The *shomer* pays the difference between what the animal was worth and what the carcass is worth now, but the carcass itself belongs to the original owner and remains his.

### Goring & Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – לַנִּיזָּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya said** that this *halakha* is derived **from here:** The verse states with regard to a person's ox that fatally gores another ox: "He shall pay an ox for the ox, **and the carcass shall be his"** (Exodus 21:36), meaning the carcass belongs **to the injured** party, the owner of the gored animal.

Chizkiyah derives the same principle from a third *pasuk*, concerning an ox that gores another ox: *shallem yeshalem shor tachat hashor vahamet yihiyeh lo* — he shall pay an ox for an ox, and the dead animal shall be his. The question is: who is the *yihiyeh lo*, who gets the carcass? The *gemara* reads this as *hamet yihiyeh lo lanizak* — the carcass belongs to the *nizak*, the damaged party. This is admittedly a strained reading. The plain sense of *shor tachat hashor* sounds like full replacement — you break it, you buy it, you pay the whole ox and take the carcass. Chizkiyah and the *gemara* read it differently: pay for the damage you created, and the *hamet yihiyeh lo* tells us the carcass stays with the *nizak* rather than reinforcing the simple reading of complete replacement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה: ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – לַנִּיזָּק. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לַנִּיזָּק, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לַמַּזִּיק? אָמַרְתָּ: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, the school of Ḥizkiyya taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And the carcass shall be his"** (Exodus 21:36). The verse means the carcass belongs **to the injured** party. Do **you say** it belongs **to the injured** party? **Or** perhaps **it** belongs **only to the one liable for the damage?** To this suggestion, **you should say: It could not have been that.**

The *gemara* defends this reading through an argument raised by Abaye. If one were to understand *vahamet yihiyeh lo* as meaning the *mazik* takes the carcass and pays the full value, then the *pasuk shor tachat hashor* would have sufficed. Why add the phrase *vahamet yihiyeh lo* at all? The fact that it adds this phrase signals that it is not reinforcing the simple reading of full payment but rather modifying it — informing us that the carcass stays with the *nizak* and the *mazik* pays only the difference.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ נְבֵילָה דְּמַזִּיק הָוְיָא, לִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״שׁוֹר תַּחַת הַשּׁוֹר״, וְלִישְׁתּוֹק; ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ – לַנִּיזָּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: If it enters your mind** to say that the **animal carcass is** the property **of the one liable for the damage, let the Merciful One write:** "He shall pay **an ox for the ox," and** then **be silent** and state no more. **Why do I** need the verse to continue: **"And the carcass shall be his"? Conclude from it** that the carcass belongs **to the injured** party.

### Three Cases Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מַכֵּה בְּהֵמָה יְשַׁלְּמֶנָּה״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא; אֲבָל טְרֵפָה, דִּשְׁכִיחָא – אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it is **necessary** to have multiple sources for this *halakha*, **as if the Merciful One wrote** only: **"One who strikes an animal shall pay for it,"** I could claim that only in that case does one have to pay for only part of the damage **because it is an uncommon** occurrence. **But** in the case of **a torn animal, which is a common** occurrence, **I will say** that his liability should **not** be limited to the difference in value between what the animal had been worth and the carcass, but he should pay for the entire value of the injured animal. Therefore, it is **necessary** to explicitly state the *halakha* also in that case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן טְרֵפָה – מִשּׁוּם דְּמִמֵּילָא; אֲבָל מַכֵּה בְּהֵמָה, דִּבְיָדַיִם – אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Torah **had taught us only** the case of **a torn animal,** I could claim that only in that case does one have to pay for only part of the damage **because** the damage occurred **by itself,** i.e., it was not directly caused by the one liable for it. **But** in the case of **one who strikes an animal,** who does so **by** direct **action, I will say that** his liability should **not** be limited. Therefore, it is necessary to explicitly state the *halakha* also in that case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי – הָא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא, וְהָא מִשּׁוּם דְּמִמֵּילָא; אֲבָל ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – דִּשְׁכִיחָא וּבְיָדַיִם, אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Torah **had taught us only these two** cases, I could claim that only in those cases does one have to pay for only part of the damage, **this** one **because** it is **an uncommon** occurrence, **and that** one **because** the damage occurred **by itself. But** in a case where one's ox gores another's ox, of which the Torah states: **"And the carcass shall be his," which is a common** occurrence, and the damage is considered to have been inflicted **by** its owner's direct **action,** since it was under his guard, **I will say** his liability should **not** be limited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן ״הַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּמָמוֹנָא קָא מַזֵּיק; אֲבָל הָכָא, דִּבְגוּפָא מַזֵּיק – אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Torah **had taught us** only the case in the verse **"And the carcass shall be his,"** I could claim that only in that case does one have to pay for only part of the damage **because** it is one's **property** that **causes damage. But here,** in the case of one who strikes another's animal, **where one causes damage with** one's own **body, I will say** his liability should **not** be limited. Therefore, it is **necessary** to explicitly state the *halakha* in each case.

The *gemara* then asks: *utzricha* — is it really necessary that all three cases be stated? Could one not have been derived from another?

The *gemara* works through each possibility. The first *pasuk*, concerning a person who directly kills an animal (*makeh behemah*), involves a rare act. One might have thought that because it is uncommon, the Torah went easy on the *mazik* and left the carcass with the *nizak*, but that perhaps in the more common case of a *shomer* whose animal is torn by a wild beast, the Torah would be more protective of the *nizak* and require the *mazik* to pay everything, absorbing the carcass. The *tereifah* case therefore needed to be stated. Conversely, had only the *tereifah* case been stated — where the harm is indirect, *mimayla*, the *shomer* being passively rather than actively responsible — one might have thought the Torah goes easy on the *shomer* precisely because his culpability is passive. But when a person directly kills an animal *biyadayim*, perhaps the *mazik* should bear full responsibility for the carcass. Thus the *makeh behemah* case was also necessary.

Even granting both of those cases, there would still be need for the *pasuk* of the goring ox. Each of the first two cases has one mitigating factor: the *makeh behemah* is direct but rare; the *shomer* case is common but indirect. One might argue that in each, the Torah's leniency toward the *mazik* was a product of that particular mitigating factor. But an ox that gores another ox presents both aggravating features simultaneously — it is common (*shechicha*) and it is a direct act of the *mazik*'s property. One might then conclude that in such a case, with no mitigating factor present, the *mazik* should take the carcass. It needed to be stated explicitly that even here, the carcass remains with the *nizak*.

Similarly, had only the goring-ox case been stated, one might have thought that the principle applies specifically because the *mazik* in that case acts through his property — *mamona kamazik* — rather than through his own body. But when the *mazik* himself causes the damage, whether as a *shomer* or as an *adam hamazik*, one might think the personal nature of the culpability changes the result. All three cases were therefore indispensable, and the principle emerges comprehensively: regardless of whether the harm is common or rare, direct or passive, caused by the person himself or through his property, the *nizak* retains ownership of the damaged object and the *mazik* pays only the loss of value.

### Carcass as Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא לְרַב: אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״, הָא לָאו הָכִי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: נְבֵילָה – דְּמַזִּיק הָוְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said to Rav: But** according to the statement of Ḥizkiyya, as explained by Abaye, **the** only **reason** the injured party retains ownership of the carcass is **that the Merciful One wrote: "And the carcass shall be his,"** but were **it not for that I would say** that **the carcass is** the property **of the one liable for the damage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא אִי אִית לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ כַּמָּה טְרֵיפוֹת – יָהֵיב לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר מָר: ״יָשִׁיב״ – לְרַבּוֹת שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף, וַאֲפִילּוּ סוּבִּין; דִּידֵיהּ מִבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana questions the need for the Torah to teach this: **Now, if** the one liable for the damage **had** in his possession the carcasses of **several torn animals, he could give** the injured party a carcass as payment, **as the Master said** above (7a): The verse states: **"He shall recompense"** (Exodus 21:34), **to include** items **worth money, and even bran,** a relatively inferior commodity, as valid items with which to pay restitution. **Is it necessary** for the Torah to teach that he can pay restitution with **his,** i.e., the injured party's, animal carcass? Granting ownership of the carcass to the injured party seems pointless, because even had the Torah granted it to the one liable for the damage, he could give it to the injured party as payment.

### Depreciation Matter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לִפְחַת נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: It **is necessary only for** the issue of who sustains the loss due to the **diminishing** value **of the carcass** between its death and when the case is brought before the court. By granting ownership of the carcass to the injured party from the moment of the animal's death, the Torah limits the damages to the difference between the value of the animal when it was alive and its value immediately after it is killed, irrespective of what happens to the carcass afterward.

The *gemara* answers: *lo nitzrecha ella lephat nevelah* — the principle is necessary because of the depreciation of the carcass. When the animal is killed, the carcass is worth ten dollars. But by the time the case reaches *beis din* and a payment is assessed, the carcass has continued to deteriorate and may now be worth only five dollars. If we say the *mazik* fundamentally owes one hundred dollars and owns the carcass, then when he tenders the depreciated carcass as partial payment, he has paid only five, and must still make up the balance. The *nizak* absorbs none of the loss from the carcass's deterioration. But if we say from the outset that the *mazik* owes only ninety dollars and the *nizak* owns the carcass, then any further decline in the carcass's value is the *nizak*'s loss to bear. The conceptual distinction thus has real practical consequences wherever the carcass depreciates between the moment of damage and the moment of payment.

## Carcass Ownership

### Tannaim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא פְּחַת נְבֵילָה תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִם טָרֹף יִטָּרֵף יְבִיאֵהוּ עֵד״ –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that the issue of the **diminishing** value **of the carcass is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a case where an animal was entrusted with a paid bailee and was attacked by a wild beast: **"If it be torn in pieces, let him bring a witness [*ed*]"** (Exodus 22:12).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָבִיא עֵדִים שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה בְּאוֹנֶס, וּפָטוּר. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: יָבִיא עֲדוּדָה לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** This means that the bailee **should bring witnesses that** the animal **was mauled through circumstances beyond** his **control, and** based on their testimony he is **exempt** from liability. **Abba Shaul says:** The word *ed* should not be interpreted as witness, but as carcass. Accordingly, the bailee **should** immediately **bring** the **carcass [*aduda*] to court** in order to appraise its current value.

The *gemara* now raises the possibility that the question of *p'chas neveilah* — who bears the cost of the carcass's depreciation — is itself a *machlokes tanna'im*. This would mean that the underlying principle of whether the *mazik* owes ninety or one hundred is disputed at the *tanna'ic* level.

### All Agree Owner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: פְּחַת נְבֵילָה דְּנִיזָּק הָוֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: דְּמַזִּיק הָוֵי? לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא דְּנִיזָּק, וְהָכָא בְּטוֹרַח נְבֵילָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי;
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** so that **they disagree about this** matter: **That** one **Sage,** Abba Shaul, **holds** that the loss due to **the diminishing** value **of the carcass is** sustained **by the injured** party, and so there is a need to appraise its value immediately in order to correctly assess how much the one liable for the damage must pay, **and** the other **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the loss due to **the diminishing** value **of the carcass is** sustained **by the one liable for the damage** because the Torah granted him ownership of it. The Gemara rejects this: **No, everyone** agrees that the loss due to **the diminishing** value **of the carcass is** sustained **by the injured** party, since he owns it, **and here they disagree concerning** who must go to **the effort** of retrieving the **carcass** and transporting it to the court to be appraised.

The *gemara* resists this reading of the dispute, however. It insists: *d'chuli alma d'nizak* — everyone agrees that the carcass belongs to the *nizak*. The principle of *baalim metaplin benevelah* is not in dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Abba Shaul. The *gemara* refuses to allow that any *tanna* would hold the *mazik* is fundamentally owed the full value of the object.

### Bringing Carcass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: מִנַּיִין שֶׁעַל בַּעַל הַבּוֹר לְהַעֲלוֹת שׁוֹר מִבּוֹרוֹ – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֶּסֶף יָשִׁיב לִבְעָלָיו, וְהַמֵּת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** so **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Others say: From where** is it derived **that it is** incumbent **upon the owner of the pit to raise the ox from his pit** after it was killed by falling into it? **The verse states:** "The owner of the pit shall pay; **he shall restore money to its owner, and the carcass** shall be his" (Exodus 21:34). They read the term "and the carcass" as a second subject of the term "he shall restore." It therefore indicates that the one liable for the damage must restore the carcass to the injured party by retrieving it. The fact that this opinion is introduced with the phrase: Others say, suggests that it stands in opposition to another opinion. That other opinion apparently holds that the owner of the injured animal is responsible for retrieving the carcass.

If everyone agrees that the *nizak* owns the carcass, then what exactly is the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Abba Shaul? The *gemara* answers: *b'torach neveilah kamiflegi* — they disagree about who bears the burden of transporting the carcass. Abba Shaul's language — *yavi adudah l'beis din*, bring the carcass to *beis din* — is his *chiddush*: it is the *mazik*'s responsibility to bring the carcass to court, or more broadly, to bring it from the field to the city where it can be sold and the *nizak* can realize its value. A *beraita* is cited in support: *acheirim omrim minayin she'al ba'al habor l'haalos hashor miboro* — from where do we know that the owner of the pit must bring the dead ox up from the pit? The verse teaches: *kesef yashiv l'ba'alav vehamet* — money he returns to its owner, and the dead animal as well. He must return the carcass to the owner in a state where it can actually be sold, meaning he must extract it from the pit and bring it to a useful place.

### Effort & Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרָבָא: הַאי טוֹרַח נְבֵילָה, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵימָא דִּבְבֵירָא שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא, וְאַגּוּדָּא שָׁוְיָא אַרְבַּע; כִּי טָרַח – בִּדְנַפְשֵׁיהּ טָרַח! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּבְבֵירָא שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא, וְאַגּוּדָּא נָמֵי שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rava: What are the circumstances** in which the one liable for the damage is required to go to **this effort,** to retrieve the **carcass** from the pit? **If we say that** while still **inside the pit,** the carcass **is worth a dinar, and** when it is **at the edge** of the pit, since it is more accessible, its market value increases and **is worth four** dinars, then **when** the one liable for the damage **expends the effort** to retrieve the carcass, he is **expending the effort for his own sake,** since by increasing the value of the carcass he reduces his own liability. Therefore, he will certainly retrieve it of his own accord, and it is not necessary for the Torah to require him to do so. Rava **said to him: No,** it is **necessary** to require him to retrieve the carcass in a case **where,** while still **inside the pit** the carcass **is worth** one **dinar, and** when it is **at the edge** of the pit, despite being more accessible, it **is still worth** one **dinar.** Since it is of no benefit to him to retrieve it, the Torah had to require him to do so.

This raises a practical question. If at the bottom of the pit the carcass is worth one dollar, and once brought to the top it is worth four dollars, then who cares whether the *mazik* is required to bring it up? If he brings it up, his debt diminishes accordingly — he still owes the *nizak* the difference between the original value and the current value, accounting for the improvement. If he doesn't bring it up, he owes more. Either way the math adjusts. Why does it matter who has the formal obligation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא כְּהַאי גַוְונָא? אִין, דְּהָא אָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: כְּשׁוּרָא בְּמָתָא בְּזוּזָא, כְּשׁוּרָא בְּדַבְרָא בְּזוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is there** ever **a case like this,** where despite being more accessible its market value does not change? The Gemara answers: **Yes, as people say, a beam** of wood **in the city** sells **for a dinar,** and **a beam** of wood **in the field** also sells **for a dinar,** despite the fact that it needs to be transported from there to the city.

The *gemara* resolves this by positing a case where the value of the carcass is the same at the bottom of the pit as at the top — one dollar either way. Is such a case realistic? The *gemara* affirms: *de'amri inshi kashura be'mata be'zuza kashura be'dabra be'zuza* — people say, a beam in the city costs a dollar and a beam out in the field costs a dollar, even though you would need to transport it. Sometimes the effort invested in transportation does not translate into a corresponding increase in the sale price — much as home improvements often do not fully recoup their cost in resale value. In such a case, if the *mazik* is required to bring the carcass to a place where it can be sold, he may expend three dollars of effort but only reduce his debt by one dollar. It therefore matters significantly who bears the obligation to transport it. The conclusion is that the *mazik* bears that responsibility, even at a cost to himself.

## Shmuel Ganav/Gazlan

### Shamin Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין שָׁמִין לֹא לְגַנָּב וְלֹא לְגַזְלָן, אֶלָּא לְנִזָּקִין. וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אַף לְשׁוֹאֵל. וְאַבָּא מוֹדֶה לִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel says:** The practice of the court is that when an animal or other item is stolen and then is damaged or dies, the court **does not appraise** its current value and assign ownership of it to its owner, in order that the perpetrator should have to pay only the difference between its prior value and its current value, **neither for** the sake of **a thief nor for** the sake of **a robber.** Rather, the thief or robber acquires ownership of the carcass or damaged item and compensates the owner for its prior value. The court appraises the item or carcass **only for damages,** as the Gemara explained above. **And I say** that they appraise the item or animal **even for a borrower** who borrowed an item and while it was in his possession it became damaged, or who borrowed an animal and while it was in his possession it died, **and Abba,** i.e., Rav, **concedes to me.**

The *gemara* now introduces a teaching of Shmuel that both confirms and sharpens what has been established: *ein shamin lo leganav velo legazlan ella lenizikin* — this principle of *shamin*, of assessing the loss in value and paying only the difference, applies exclusively to cases of damage, *nezikin*, and not to cases of theft (*ganav*) or robbery (*gazlan*).

### Possession vs Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הָכִי קָאָמַר – אַף לְשׁוֹאֵל שָׁמִין, וְאַבָּא מוֹדֶה לִי; אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר – וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אַף לְשׁוֹאֵל אֵין שָׁמִין, וְאַבָּא מוֹדֶה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before them:** Is **this what** Shmuel **is saying:** The court **appraises** the value of an item or a carcass **even for** the sake of **a borrower, and Abba concedes to me; or perhaps, this is what** Shmuel **is saying: And I say** that the court **does not appraise** its value **even for a borrower, and Abba concedes to me?**

The conceptual basis for this distinction is compelling and is explained by Tosafos. In a case of damage, the *mazik* never took possession of the object. He broke it, diminished it, made it worth less — but it remained the *nizak*'s object throughout. The *mazik* therefore pays only for the damage he created: the difference between the object's original value and its current value. The object itself stays with the *nizak*. A thief or robber, however, *took* the object. That act of *geneiva* or *gezeila* made him liable for the full value of the cow the moment he took it. He owes one hundred dollars. If the cow subsequently dies, the question of what he can tender in payment — whether he can use the carcass as partial payment under the principle of *yashiv l'rabbos shaveh kessef afilu subbin* — is a separate discussion. But his fundamental liability is for the full value of the object he took, not merely for a diminution of value. Conceptually, he owes one hundred dollars, and the carcass may or may not serve as a means of discharging part of that debt, but it does not change the underlying obligation.

### Shoel Full Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּשְׁאֵיל נַרְגָּא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ, תַּבְרֵהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שַׁלֵּים לֵיהּ נַרְגָּא מְעַלְּיָא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין שָׁמִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution from the following incident: There was **a certain man who borrowed an ax from another and he broke it. He came before Rav** to rule if, and how much, he was liable to pay for it. Rav **said to him: Go and pay him with a full-fledged ax,** i.e., you must compensate the owner for the full value of the ax that you broke. The Gemara suggests: **Conclude from it** that the court **does not appraise** its value for the sake of a borrower.

Shmuel adds a further teaching — though a textual dispute affects how precisely it should be read. Tosafos preserves a *girsa* in which Shmuel says: *va'ani omer af leshoel* — and I say, even for a *shoel*, a borrower. His father, who is identified here as Rav, agrees with him on this point as well. The question is in which direction: does Shmuel mean that a *shoel* is like *nezikin* and one assesses only the damage, or that a *shoel* is like a *ganav* or *gazlan* and pays the full amount?

The conceptual tension here is genuine. A normal *shomer*'s liability arises from negligence — something went wrong on his watch and he is responsible for the resulting damage. This closely parallels *nezikin*, and accordingly a *shomer*'s liability should be measured by the diminution of value. A *shoel*, however, borrows the object and uses it as if it were his own. There is an argument that this taking-for-use creates a form of liability resembling *geneiva* — he has, in a sense, taken the object into his domain, and his obligation is to return the whole object. If the object breaks, he owes its full value, not merely the loss in value.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִדְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: ״דִּינָא הָכִי?!״ וּשְׁתֵיק, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שָׁמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **On the contrary, from** the fact **that Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav** on that occasion: **Is this the *halakha*? And** Rav **remained silent,** this suggests that he conceded to their opinion that the borrower should not have had to pay the full value of the ax. Accordingly, **conclude from it** that the court **appraises** an item's value for the sake of a borrower.

### Axe Case

A practical case is brought to test the question. A man borrowed an axe from his friend and broke it. He came before Rav, and Rav told him: *zil shalim leih narga ma'alya* — go pay him for a complete, full-value axe. This appears to support the reading that a *shoel* pays the full amount, not merely the assessed diminution — and that when Shmuel said Rav agreed with him, he meant they both hold a *shoel* pays like a *ganav* or *gazlan*.

### Challenge to Rav

The *gemara* pushes back. Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenged Rav directly, asking *dina hachi* — is that truly the *halacha*? Should it not be that the *shoel* pays only the assessed difference? Rav was silent. The *gemara* draws the inference: *shema mina shamin* — from Rav's silence it appears he conceded, and the *halacha* is that a *shoel* is assessed like other cases of damage, paying the diminution of value, not the full amount.

## Liability Categories

### Ulla's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שָׁמִין לְגַנָּב וּלְגַזְלָן. רַב פַּפֵּי אָמַר: אֵין שָׁמִין. וְהִלְכְתָא: אֵין שָׁמִין לֹא לְגַנָּב וְלֹא לְגַזְלָן, אֲבָל לְשׁוֹאֵל שָׁמִין – כִּדְרַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** An amoraic dispute **was stated: Ulla says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** The court **appraises** a stolen item's value **for a thief and for a robber. Rav Pappi says: It does not appraise** its value for them. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that the court **does not appraise** value **either for a thief or for a robber; but it appraises** value **for a borrower, in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Kahana and Rav Asi.**

The matter is then stated as a formal ruling. Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar taught: *shamin leganav ulegazlan* — one does assess the difference even for a *ganav* and a *gazlan*. This contradicts Shmuel's teaching.

### Rav Pappa Halakha

Rav Pappa rules explicitly: *ein shamin* — one does not assess the difference for a *ganav* or *gazlan*. The *hilchisa*, the accepted *halacha*, follows Shmuel: *ein shamin lo leganav velo legazlan* — neither for the thief nor the robber does one merely assess the loss in value. Both owe for the full object they took.

### Shoel Halakha

The *halacha* further specifies: *aval leshoel shamin*, but for a *shoel* one does assess the damage, following the position of Rav Kahana and Rav Assi, whose challenge to Rav was met with silence. Even though there is a conceptual argument that a *shoel* resembles a *ganav* or *gazlan* — having taken the object and used it as his own — the *halacha* does not follow that logic. A *shoel* is treated like other *shomrim* and like *nezikin*: his liability is for the diminution of value he caused, not for the full original value of the object. The *nizak* retains the damaged object, and the *shoel* pays the difference.

## Ulla's Teachings

### Birth Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שִׁלְיָא שֶׁיָּצְתָה מִקְצָתָהּ בְּיוֹם רִאשׁוֹן וּמִקְצָתָהּ בְּיוֹם שֵׁנִי, מוֹנִין לָהּ מִן הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites additional *halakhot* taught by Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar: When a woman gives birth or miscarries a fetus she is thereby rendered ritually impure (see Leviticus 12:1–5). Even if she delivers only the afterbirth, with no discernible fetus, she is rendered ritually impure due to the possibility that the fetus was dissolved in the afterbirth, and it is therefore considered as though she delivered it (*Nidda* 24b). The length of the period of impurity depends on the sex of the child. In the case where it is unclear what the sex is, she must observe the longer period of fourteen days (*Nidda* 29a). At the end of that period she may purify herself by immersing in a ritual bath. **And Ulla says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** If **part of** the **afterbirth [*shilya*] emerged on the first day** of a woman's miscarriage **and part of it** emerged **on the second day, one counts** the period of ritual impurity **from the first** day.

Having cited a ruling of Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar, the *gemara* now strings together a series of additional teachings in that same name. The first concerns a case where the amniotic sac (*shilya*) containing the embryo emerges partially on one day and partially on the next — for instance, some emerges on Sunday just before nightfall and the remainder after nightfall, which begins Monday. Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar rules: *monin lah min harishon* — she counts the seven or fourteen days of *tumas leida* beginning from Sunday.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מָה דַעְתָּךְ, לְחוּמְרָא?! חוּמְרָא דְּאָתֵי לִידֵי קוּלָּא הוּא – דְּקָא מְטַהֲרַתְּ לַהּ מֵרִאשׁוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him,** to Ulla: **What** is the rationale for **your opinion** to begin counting from the first day? It would appear to be based on the following: Ritual impurity is engendered only once the woman has delivered the fetus. This is defined as the emergence of the majority of the fetus or its head. Since in this case the fetus is not discernible, one must consider the possibility that it had already emerged on the first day, and therefore one is required **to act stringently** and regard her as impure from the first day. Rava questions the propriety of acting stringently in this case, as it is **a stringency that results in a leniency, because** if she begins counting from the first day, **you will** also **render her** fit to immerse and become **pure from** fourteen days **after the first** day. This is a leniency because it is possible that the majority of the fetus emerged only on the second day and therefore her period of impurity began only then. She will therefore remain impure until the fifteenth day.

Rava challenges this. Starting the count from Sunday seems like a *chumra* — being strict because she might have been *tamei* on Sunday. But it is a *chumra de'asi lidei kula hu* — a stringency that leads to a leniency. By counting from Sunday, the fourteen days may end earlier than if she had counted from Monday, and she could erroneously be declared *tehorah* while she is still in her period of *tum'ah*. Rava therefore rules: from Sunday she is concerned she may be *tamei* and acts accordingly, but she only begins counting the fourteen days from Monday, ensuring full coverage on both ends.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לָחוֹשׁ – חוֹשֶׁשֶׁת; מִימְנָא לָא (מ)מָנְיָא אֶלָּא לְשֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** With regard **to being concerned** for the possibility that she is impure from the first day, **she should be concerned.** But with regard to **counting** the period of impurity, **one counts only from the second** day.

### Fetus Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּאֵין מִקְצָת שִׁלְיָא בְּלֹא וָלָד? תְּנֵינָא: שִׁלְיָא שֶׁיָּצְתָה מִקְצָתָהּ – אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה; סִימָן וָלָד בְּאִשָּׁה, סִימָן וָלָד בִּבְהֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Ulla **teaching us** through this *halakha*? Does he mean to teach us **that part of the afterbirth** does not emerge **without** part of the **fetus** inside? But **we** already **learned** that in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 77a): When an animal is ritually slaughtered, the animal and everything inside it becomes permitted for consumption. This applies to an unborn fetus as well. If prior to the slaughter the majority of the fetus emerged, it is considered to have been born, and therefore the entire fetus, even the part that is still within its mother, is not rendered permitted for consumption by the slaughter. Accordingly, if **part of the afterbirth emerged** prior to the slaughter, **it is prohibited to eat** it, because an afterbirth is **a sign of a fetus in a woman** and **a sign of a fetus in an animal.**

The *gemara* then asks what *chiddush* is being conveyed by Ulla's teaching, since Rava has revised it. The answer is: *de'ein miktzas shilya belo velad* — the *chiddush* is that even when only part of the sac emerges, we assume some of the fetus was present within it. If it were possible that the partial emergence of the sac contained none of the fetus, there would be no basis for concern that childbirth began on Sunday. It is only because we assume the fetus was at least partially present in what emerged on Sunday that we are concerned at all. This is the novel principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִמַּתְנִיתָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא דְּיֵשׁ מִקְצָת שִׁלְיָא בְּלֹא וָלָד, וּגְזֵירָה מִקְצָתַהּ אַטּוּ כּוּלַּהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If** I would know this only **from the mishna, I would say** **that there is** a possibility that **part of the afterbirth** will emerge **without** part of **the fetus** inside, and the reason that the Sages forbid eating the afterbirth is due to a rabbinic **decree** prohibiting a case where **part of** an afterbirth emerges from the womb and part of it remains inside, **due to** the possibility that one may confuse it with a case where **all of** the afterbirth emerges. Therefore, Ulla **teaches us** that, in fact, part of the afterbirth does not emerge without part of the fetus inside.

The *gemara* challenges this as well, suggesting the principle might already have been taught in a *mishna*: *shilya sheyatza miktzasah asurah ba'akhilah* — if part of the amniotic sac of an animal emerged before *shechita*, what is within the sac is forbidden, because *shilya* is a sign of a *velad*. But the *gemara* deflects: one could read that *mishna* as a *gezeira* — a rabbinic fence — forbidding even the case where no fetus was in the partial sac, to prevent people from permitting cases where a fetus was present. It would not necessarily prove the underlying assumption that a fetus is always present with a partial sac. The *chiddush* of Ulla's teaching therefore stands.

### Bechor Treifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּכוֹר שֶׁנִּטְרַף בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – אֵין פּוֹדִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha* taught by Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar: **And Ulla says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** With regard to a male **firstborn** child **who was mauled** by an animal **within thirty days** of his birth and died, one is **not required to redeem him,** as the requirement to do so, by paying five *sela* to a priest, applies only once the child is thirty days old (see Numbers 18:15–16).

Another teaching of Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar: if a firstborn son (*bechor*) suffered a wound rendering him a *treifa* within the first thirty days of his life, *ein podin oso* — one does not perform *pidyon haben* when day thirty arrives. Even if the child was healthy at birth and even if he is still alive on day thirty, since he has become a *treifa* — his death is now inevitable from the wound — he does not have the status of a fully viable living person, and the *mitzva* of *pidyon hebben* does not apply. A *beraita* corroborates: the verse says *padoh tifdeh*, "you shall surely redeem," which one might think applies even to a child who became a *treifa* within thirty days; the word *ach* — "however" — limits the obligation and excludes such a case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פָּדֹה תִפְדֶּה״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ נִטְרַף בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַךְ״ – חִלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **Rami bar Ḥama taught** a *baraita*: **Since it is stated:** "Yet **you shall redeem"** (Numbers 18:15), one **might** have thought that **even** if a male firstborn child was **mauled** by an animal **within thirty days** of his birth one should redeem him. Therefore, **the verse states "yet** you shall redeem"; the addition of the word "yet" serves to **differentiate** and limit the requirement to redeem the firstborn male.

### Animal Meshicha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha* taught by Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar: **And Ulla says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: A large animal,** such as a cow or a horse, **is acquired by** the buyer **pulling** the animal.

Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar further teaches: *behema gassa nikneis bemeshicha* — a large animal such as a cow acquires a new owner through *meshicha*, pulling it toward oneself. The *gemara* notes a tension with what was taught in Kiddushin, where acquisition of a large animal is said to occur through *mesira*, handing over the reins. Ulla follows the view of the *Chachamim* in the *beraita* who hold that both large and small animals are acquired through *meshicha*. Rabbi Shimon in the same *beraita* holds that both types of animal require *hagbaha*, lifting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן בִּמְסִירָה! הוּא דְּאָמַר – כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא: דְּתַנְיָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זוֹ וָזוֹ בִּמְשִׁיכָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: זוֹ וָזוֹ בְּהַגְבָּהָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 25b): A large animal is acquired **by passing** the reins of the animal to the buyer? The Gemara explains: Rabbi Elazar **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*,** i.e., the Rabbis, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **And the Rabbis say: This and that,** i.e., both large and small animals, are acquired **through** the buyer **pulling** the animal. **Rabbi Shimon says: This and that** are acquired **through** the buyer **lifting** the animal.

### Estate Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ, מַה שֶּׁעֲלֵיהֶן – שָׁמִין, וּמַה שֶּׁעַל בְּנֵיהֶן וּבְנוֹתֵיהֶן – אֵין שָׁמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha* taught by Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar: **And Ulla says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** With regard to **brothers who divide** the estate they inherited, the court **appraises whatever** clothes are **upon them** if those clothes came from, or if they were purchased with money from, the deceased's estate, and that sum is considered part of the portion they receive. But the court does **not appraise whatever is upon their sons and daughters.** In order to save the children from the humiliation of having to appear in court, the brothers waive their rights to the clothes the children are wearing.

Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar rules: when brothers who have been living jointly off an estate come to divide it, *ma she'aleihem shamin* — one assesses the value of the clothing they themselves are wearing, counting it toward their respective shares. However, *ma she'al beneihem uvnoteihem ein shamin* — one does not assess the clothing worn by their children, even if those garments were paid for from the estate. Bringing all the children before *beis din* to have their clothing appraised would be undignified, and the brothers are presumed to have waived any claim on that point.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּעָמִים אַף מַה שֶּׁעֲלֵיהֶן אֵין שָׁמִין – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בִּגְדוֹל אַחֵי, דְּנִיחָא לְהוּ דְּלִשְׁתַּמְעוּן מִילֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Sometimes, it does not even appraise whatever is upon** the brothers. **You find such** a case **with** the **eldest brother** where the other brothers waive their rights to the value of his clothes, **as it is beneficial for them** to have the eldest brother appear well dressed in order **that** when he represents their interests in dealings with others, **his words will be listened to** and respected.

Rav Pappa adds a qualification: *pa'amim af ma she'aleihem ein shamin* — there are circumstances in which even the brothers' own clothing is not assessed. This applies when one of the brothers has been serving as the representative of the estate, managing business dealings on behalf of all of them. In that case, the others are presumed to have implicitly consented to his using estate funds for clothing appropriate to his representative role, for the benefit of all. Since he acted in their collective interest, his higher clothing expenditure is not counted against his share.

### Shomer Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שׁוֹמֵר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר – פָּטוּר. וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – דְּעַלּוֹיֵי עַלְּיַיהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – דְּהַשְׁתָּא גָּרוֹעֵי גָּרְעַיהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ, נָמֵי פָּטוּר, שֶׁהֲרֵי מָסַר לְבֶן דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha* taught by Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar: **And Ulla says** that **Rabbi Elazar says:** In the case of **a bailee who conveyed** a deposit that was entrusted to him **to** another **bailee,** the first bailee is **exempt** for any occurrence for which he would have been exempt had he kept the deposit with him. The Gemara adds: **And it is not necessary** to state this in the case of **an unpaid bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to a paid bailee, thereby increasing** the level of **its safeguarding,** since the paid bailee has a greater level of accountability than an unpaid bailee. **Rather,** this is the *halakha* **even** in a case of **a paid bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to an unpaid bailee, thereby decreasing** the level of **its safeguarding.** In this case, the first bailee is **exempt** for any occurrence for which he would have been exempt had he kept the deposit with him, **because he conveyed** it **to a mentally competent person** and thereby fulfilled his responsibility to ensure the deposit is safeguarded.

Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar teaches: *shomer shemassar l'shomer patur* — a *shomer* who transfers an object to another *shomer* does not thereby incur additional liability. If Reuven was given an object by Fred to watch, and Reuven asks Shimon to watch it in his place, this act of transfer does not make Reuven more liable than he was before. If Reuven was a *shomer chinam* he remains liable for *pshiah*; if he was a *shomer sachar* he remains liable for *geneiva v'aveida*. But the transfer itself does not constitute a new act of liability.

It goes without saying, says the *gemara*, that if Reuven was a *shomer chinam* and he paid Shimon to watch it as a *shomer sachar*, one would not fault him for raising the standard of *shmira*. More significantly, Ulla's ruling covers even the case where Reuven was a *shomer sachar* and transferred the object to Shimon as a *shomer chinam* — where one might expect the lower standard of care to constitute a form of negligence on Reuven's part. Even so, Reuven is *patur*. The reason: *sheharei masar l'ben da'at* — he transferred the object to a responsible adult, and that does not by itself violate the terms of his guardianship.

### Rava Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: שׁוֹמֵר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר – חַיָּיב. וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – דְּגָרוֹעֵי גָּרְעַיהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – חַיָּיב,
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** With regard to **a bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to** another **bailee,** the first bailee becomes **liable** to pay for any loss to the item, even for mishaps for which he would not have been liable had he kept the deposit with him. The Gemara adds: **And it is not necessary** to state this in a case of **a paid bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to an unpaid bailee, thereby decreasing** the level of **its safeguarding. Rather,** this is the *halakha* **even** in the case of **an unpaid bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to a paid bailee,** thereby increasing the level of its safeguarding; he is **liable.**

Rava disagrees sharply: *shomer shemassar l'shomer chayav* — a *shomer* who transfers to another *shomer* is liable. The act of transfer is itself a form of negligence or violation of the original agreement, and once a *shomer* has shirked his responsibility in this way, he becomes liable even for an *ones* that subsequently occurs — analogous to the principle of *techilato b'fshiah v'sofo b'ones*.

Rava's ruling likewise covers both directions. It goes without saying that if the *shomer sachar* transferred to a *shomer chinam* — thereby reducing the expected standard of care — that is negligence. Rava's ruling covers even the case where the *shomer chinam* transferred to a *shomer sachar*, where the *shomer* might argue he actually improved the *shmira*. Even so, he is *chayav*.

### Trust & Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ מְהֵימְנַתְּ לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה, הַאי לָא מְהֵימַן לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The reason he is liable is **that** the owner of the deposit **can say to him: You are credible to me with regard to** taking **an oath,** but **this** other bailee, to whom you conveyed my item, **is not credible to me with regard to** taking **an oath.** If an occurrence for which a bailee does not carry liability occurs, damaging the deposit, in order to release himself from an obligation to pay the bailee must take an oath to the item's owner that none of the types of occurrences for which he bears liability occurred. Rava rules that the owner is required to accept an oath only from the bailee with whom he entrusted his item, but not from anyone else. Accordingly, since the first bailee was not present when the event occurred, he is unable to attest to what happened, and even if the second bailee takes an oath to that effect, the owner is not expected to accept his oath. Consequently, the first bailee bears full liability for any loss.

The reason Rava gives is fundamental: *at meimant li bishvua hai lo meimant li bishvua* — the owner trusted *you*, and by placing his object with you, he implicitly agreed he would accept *your* oath if something happened to it. He never agreed to accept the oath of whoever you might subsequently choose to entrust it to. If the object is now in Shimon's hands and something goes wrong, Shimon's *shvua* is simply not one the owner agreed to credit. The *shomer* who transferred it has therefore violated the very basis of the trust placed in him. The *gemara* notes that in Bava Metzia an additional and perhaps more fundamental reason is given: *ein ritzoni she'yehei pikdoni b'yad acher* — the owner never agreed that anyone other than the original *shomer* should hold it. Regardless of whether the substitute would do better or worse, the original *shomer* had no right to make the transfer at all.

### Slave Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, הִלְכְתָא: גּוֹבִין מִן הָעֲבָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *halakha* taught by Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar: **And Ulla says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: The *halakha*** is that **one can collect from** the debtor **the slaves** that he owns as payment for a debt.

Ulla in the name of Rabbi Elazar further rules: *hilchata govin min ha'avadim* — the *halacha* is that one may collect a debt from slaves. This implies that slaves have the status of real estate (*karka*) for purposes of debt collection, meaning creditors hold a lien over a debtor's slaves just as they hold a lien over his land.

### Nachman Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְעוּלָּא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי? לָא, מִינֵּיהּ. מִינֵּיהּ?! אֲפִילּוּ מִגְּלִימָא דְּעַל כַּתְפֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Ulla:** Did **Rabbi Elazar say** that this *halakha* applies **even** when collecting **from** a debtor's **orphans?** A creditor can collect the debt from orphans only by taking the land the debtor bequeathed to his children. Rav Naḥman asked whether this also extends to collecting any slaves the children inherited. Ulla replied: **No,** a creditor collects a debt by taking slaves only when he collects directly **from** the debtor himself. Rav Naḥman challenges this: But when the creditor collects **from him,** he can collect the debt **even from the cloak that is upon his shoulders,** and therefore he can certainly also collect the debt by taking his slaves, so what is the novelty of this ruling?

Rav Nachman challenges Ulla directly: *amar Rabbi Elazar afillu b'yasmi* — did Rabbi Elazar really mean this applies even when collecting from orphans? Tosafos notes that in this context, the discussion of orphans is effectively equivalent to the question of *lakuchos* — third-party purchasers who acquired the property after the debt was incurred. The question is whether a lien on a slave follows the slave even after it is sold, so that the creditor can collect from the new owner or the heirs. Did Rabbi Elazar really go that far? Ulla withdraws: no, he meant only that one may collect from the debtor's own slaves. But Rav Nachman presses: *miminei? afilu miglima d'al katfei* — from the debtor himself? You can take the shirt off his back! Obviously one can collect from all of a debtor's assets directly. That is not a *chiddush* requiring a ruling.

### Slave Apotiki

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי. כִּדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: עָשָׂה עַבְדּוֹ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי, וּמְכָרוֹ – בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה הֵימֶנּוּ. שׁוֹרוֹ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי, וּמְכָרוֹ – אֵין בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla explains: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case in **which** the debtor **set aside** his slave **as designated repayment [*appoteiki*].** Consequently, if the slave was subsequently sold to a third party and the debtor was later unable to repay the debt, the creditor can seize the slave from the third party as payment of the debt. Even though generally only land can be seized from a buyer as payment for the seller's debt, a slave is considered to have similar status to land in this regard. This is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava, as Rava says:** If a master **set aside his slave** as **designated repayment** of a debt **and** then **sold him,** the master's **creditor collects** the debt **by** taking the slave; but if one set aside **his ox** as **designated repayment and** then **sold it,** the **creditor does not collect** the debt **by** taking the ox.

Under this pressure, Ulla revises the statement a second time: *hacha b'mai askinan she'asao apotiki* — the case we are discussing is where the debtor explicitly designated the slave as an *apotiki*, a formal lien. In that case, even if the slave is subsequently sold or passes to heirs, the creditor may collect from it. This is supported by Rava's ruling: if one makes his slave an *apotiki* and then sells the slave, the creditor may collect from the purchaser. This is distinct from the general case of slaves, which without explicit designation do not carry automatic liens the way real estate does.

### Slave Publicity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? הָא אִית לֵיהּ קָלָא, וְהָא לֵית לֵיהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this difference? The Gemara explains: The designation of **this** slave generates **publicity,** since a slave is significant and identifiable, therefore any prospective buyer is assumed to have been aware of the status of the slave and accepted the consequences of purchasing him. **But** the designation of **this** ox **does not** generate **publicity,** since one ox is not easily distinguishable from another, and a buyer cannot be assumed to be aware that the ox was set aside as designated repayment. Therefore it is unfair that one who purchases such an ox should have it seized from him. Therefore, the Sages enacted that a creditor cannot do so.

One might ask: if an explicit *apotiki* designation works for slaves, why does it not work equally for other chattel? If a person designates his ox as an *apotiki* and then sells the ox, can the creditor collect from the purchaser? The answer is no: *mai ta'ama hai it lei kala v'hai leit lei kala* — the reason is publicity. Slaves are known individuals. People know which slaves belong to whom, and if a lien is placed on a slave, that information can be known and the potential purchaser can investigate and protect himself. Real estate similarly carries publicly knowable liens, which is why automatic liens on real estate are enforceable against purchasers. Ordinary chattel — like a cow — is anonymous enough that nobody could reasonably be expected to know whether a particular animal carries a lien, and therefore even an explicit *apotiki* designation on chattel cannot be enforced against a third-party purchaser. Slaves occupy an intermediate position: they do not carry automatic liens like real estate, but an explicit *apotiki* on a slave is sufficiently public to be enforceable, because slaves are individuals whose status and encumbrances can be known. The discussion continues on the following *daf*.
