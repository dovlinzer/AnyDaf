# Kiddushin 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelichus Sources

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא – דִּטְעוּ, הָא – דְּלָא טְעוּ. אִי דְּלָא טְעוּ, מַאי יְכוֹלִים לְמַחוֹת? יְכוֹלִים לְמַחוֹת בְּרוּחוֹת. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ הֲרֵי הֵן כְּלָקוֹחוֹת. פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁתוּת – נִקְנֶה מִקָּח. יָתֵר עַל שְׁתוּת – בָּטֵל מִקָּח. שְׁתוּת – קָנָה, וּמַחְזִיר אוֹנָאָה. אָמַר רָבָא: הָא דַּאֲמַרַן ״פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁתוּת – נִקְנֶה מִקָּח״ – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא שַׁוְּיֵהּ שָׁלִיחַ, אֲבָל שַׁוְּיֵהּ שָׁלִיחַ, אָמַר: לְתַקּוֹנֵי שַׁדַּרְתָּיךָ וְלָא לְעַוּוֹתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** In **this** case, where Rav Naḥman ruled that their transaction is void, in accordance with the Rabbis, the court **erred** by one-sixth. But in **that** case, where Rav Naḥman ruled that the orphans cannot protest when they grow up, **they did not err** by one-sixth. The Gemara asks: **If** Rav Naḥman's ruling that the orphans cannot protest is referring to a case **where they did not err** by one-sixth, **why** did Shmuel say that **they can** later **protest;** what is the nature of their protest? The Gemara answers: **They can protest with regard to** the **locations.** One of the orphans can contend that he prefers property in a different location than he was given. **Rav Naḥman says: With regard to brothers who divided property received as an inheritance, they are considered like** they are **purchasers** from each other, and the *halakhot* of fraud are like those for regular transactions: If there was an error of **less than one-sixth** in the distribution, the **transaction is acquired,** i.e., valid. If it was **more than one-sixth,** the **transaction is void.** If the error was precisely **one-sixth,** it is **acquired, and** the one who received more than his fair share **must return** the amount of the **fraud.** **Rava says: That which we said,** that with regard to **less than one-sixth** the **transaction** is valid and the item **is acquired, we said only** in a case **where** the brother receiving a smaller share **did not appoint an agent** to deal with the distribution on his behalf. **But if** the brother receiving a smaller share **appointed an agent,** this *halakha* does not apply, as the one who appointed the agent can **say: I sent you to** act for **my benefit and not to my detriment.** The agent's right to act in this capacity did not extend to a case where it was to the detriment of the one who appointed him.

Today's *daf* is 43, picking up on 42b at the word *hasholeach*. At this point the *Gemara* has wrapped up its discussion of the basic sources for the principle of *shelichus* — derived from *terumah*, from *kiddushin* and *gittin*, and from *korbanos*. The *daf* now turns to a set of exceptions to the general rule of *shelichus*, centered on the concept of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

## Fire via Irresponsible

### Hasholeach Beyad Cheresh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ אֶת הַבְּעֵירָה בְּיַד חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – פָּטוּר מִדִּינֵי אָדָם, וְחַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם. שִׁילַּח בְּיַד פִּיקֵּחַ – פִּיקֵּחַ חַיָּיב. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא: שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאֵין שָׁלִיחַ לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה. דְּאָמְרִינַן: דִּבְרֵי הָרַב וְדִבְרֵי תַּלְמִיד – דִּבְרֵי מִי שׁוֹמְעִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss various aspects of agency. **And** there is a difficulty from **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 59b): In the case of **one who sends** an item that causes **a fire in the hands of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** the one who sent it **is exempt according to human laws but liable according to the laws of Heaven.** If **he sent** it **in the hands of** a halakhically **competent person,** only **the** halakhically **competent person is liable.** **But why** is the halakhically competent person liable? **Let us say** that the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself.** The Gemara answers: **There** it **is different, as there is no agency for transgression, as we say:** When there is a conflict between **the words of the Master,** i.e., God, **and the words of** the **student,** i.e., a human being, **whose words** should be **listened to?** Consequently, the agent is considered to have acted of his own accord, and the one who sent him bears no responsibility.

The *Gemara* on 42b begins with the *Mishnah*'s case: *hasholeach es habeeirah beyad cheresh shoteh vekatan* — if one sends a fire through the hands of a *cheresh* (a deaf-mute), a *shoteh* (someone with a psychiatric illness who does not process reality clearly), or a *katan* (a minor). All three of these individuals are not considered legally responsible for their actions. The ruling is *patur midinei adam vechayav midinei shamayim*: the one who sent the fire is exempt from human legal liability but bears responsibility before Heaven, since he clearly caused a conflagration and destruction of property.

### Liability Question

However, if the fire was transmitted through the hand of a *pikeach* — a mentally competent adult — then *pikeach chayav*: the adult who performed the act of burning is liable, and the one who sent him is exempt. This resembles the principle underlying organized criminal liability: the person who orchestrates and manipulates the scheme is, from a *halachic* standpoint, not directly responsible — only the person who actually lit the flame bears liability.

The *Gemara* then asks why the *meshaleach* is not liable through the principle of *shelichus*: *vamai, neima sheliach shel adam kemoso* — why not say that a person's *shaliach* is legally equivalent to himself? One might have thought that *shelichus* is simply irrelevant here because agency is about the delegation of legal authority, whereas the physical act of lighting a fire is not the kind of authority that can be transferred. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* engages the question directly.

### Ein Shaliach Lidvar

The *Gemara*'s answer is: *shani hasam de'ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — it is different there because there is no *shaliach* for a matter of sin. The reason is stated as *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid divrei mi shome'in*: between the words of the Master and the words of the student, whose words does one listen to? The point is that when you instruct a *shaliach* to commit a transgression, you cannot be held responsible for his action — he should have listened to God rather than to you. Ultimately, a person is responsible for his own choices, and the sin and the liability belong to the one who performed the act. This is an important principle that operates across the *halacha* of *shelichus*: *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid divrei mi shome'in*.

### Shaliach Awareness

An interesting question arises from this reasoning: does the principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* apply even when the *shaliach* was unaware that the act was a sin? If, for example, the *meshaleach* asked someone to take what he believed to be his own car to be destroyed, and the *shaliach* had no reason to suspect otherwise, perhaps in that case the *meshaleach* would in fact bear liability — since the *shaliach* had no grounds to refuse. The *Gemara* does not pursue this question directly at this point, but it is worth flagging as a genuine conceptual tension.

## Me'ilah & Shelichus

### Me'ilah Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַדְתַנְיָא: שָׁלִיחַ שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – שָׁלִיחַ מָעַל, עָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל. כִּי עָשָׂה שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מִיהָא מָעַל. אַמַּאי? נֵימָא: אֵין שָׁלִיחַ לִדְבַר עֲבֵירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** there is a difficulty from **that which is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the *halakhot* of misuse of consecrated property: In the case of **an agent who did not perform his agency** but deviated from the instructions of the one who appointed him and made use of consecrated property, the **agent has misused** consecrated property and is liable to bring the guilt-offering for that sin. In the case of an agent who **performed his agency, the owner has misused** consecrated property and is liable to bring the offering. The Gemara asks: The *baraita* states that **when** the agent **performed the agency of the owner, the owner** has **in any event misused** consecrated property. **Why? Let us say** that **there is no agency for transgression.**

The *Gemara* now cites a *beraita* regarding *me'ilah* — the inadvertent misappropriation of *hekdesh* funds. The ruling is: *shaliach shelo asah shelichuso shaliach ma'al, asah shelichuso ba'al habayis ma'al* — if the *shaliach* failed to carry out his mission (for instance, if sent to buy vegetables with *hekdesh* money he instead bought something else), then the *shaliach* himself committed *me'ilah*, since he was acting on his own rather than on behalf of the *meshaleach*. But if the *shaliach* carried out the mission as instructed, then the *ba'al habayis* committed *me'ilah* — the sin reverts to the one who sent him.

### Challenge to Principle

This creates an immediate difficulty: *vamai, neima ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — we should say that there is no *shaliach* for a matter of sin. How can the *ba'al habayis* be liable for *me'ilah* if the act was performed by the *shaliach*, and *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* should prevent attributing that act back to him? *Tosafos* raises a related point here: since *me'ilah* applies only in cases of *shogeg* (inadvertence), perhaps the principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* as expressed through *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid* does not apply in cases where the *shaliach* had no reason to refrain. Whether *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* is grounded in the principle that the *shaliach* should have known better, or whether it is a more fundamental rule that sin can only be attributed to the one who personally performs it, remains an open question — but the *Gemara* does not resolve it at this point.

### Gezeirah Shavah Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי מְעִילָה, דְּיָלְפָא ״חֵטְא״ ״חֵטְא״ מִתְּרוּמָה: מָה תְּרוּמָה מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ – אַף מְעִילָה מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ. וְנֵילַף מִינַּהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי מְעִילָה וּשְׁלִיחוּת יָד שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכֹל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The case of **misuse** of consecrated property **is different, as** it **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy of **"sin"** in this case and **"sin" from *teruma*,** as the verse states: "And sin through error" (Leviticus 5:15), with regard to misuse of consecrated property, and it states: "Lest they bear sin for it" (Leviticus 22:9) with regard to *teruma*: **Just as** with ***teruma*** one can **appoint an agent, so too** with **misuse** of consecrated property one can **appoint an agent,** although the latter is a transgression. The Gemara suggests: **And let us derive** a principle **from** misuse of consecrated property, that one can appoint an agent even to perform a transgression. The Gemara explains: This is not done **because misuse** of consecrated property **and misappropriation** of a deposit, i.e., a bailee using an item that was deposited with him, **are two verses that come as one,** i.e., they teach the same matter, that an agent can be appointed to perform a transgression. **And any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common aspect to apply to other cases.

The *Gemara* answers: *shani me'ilah diyalaf cheit cheit miTrumah* — *me'ilah* is different because the word *cheit* appears both in the laws of *terumah* and in the laws of *me'ilah*, creating a *gezeirah shavah*. Since *terumah* is one of the foundational sources for the institution of *shelichus* itself — *mah Trumah mishvach shliach af me'ilah mishvach shliach* — the same way *shelichus* applies for *terumah*, so too it applies for *me'ilah*, causing the liability to revert to the *meshaleach*.

The *Gemara* then asks: *veneilaf minah* — why not use *me'ilah* as a paradigm and extend *shaliach lidvar aveira* across all of *halacha*? The answer is: *mishum dehavei me'ilah veshlichus yad shnei kesuvin habayim ke'echad* — because *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad* together constitute *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad*, two scriptural instances coming as one, *ve'ein melamdin* — and two scriptural sources that appear together as exceptions do not generate a general rule.

## Shelichus Yad Exception

### Shelichus Yad Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעִילָה – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, שְׁלִיחוּת יָד מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע״, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לְחַיֵּיב עַל הַמַּחְשָׁבָה כְּמַעֲשֶׂה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁלַח בּוֹ יָד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם לֹא שָׁלַח יָדוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. אָמְרוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵית הִלֵּל: וַהֲלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע״! אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: וַהֲלֹא נֶאֱמַר ״אִם לֹא שָׁלַח יָדוֹ בִּמְלֶאכֶת רֵעֵהוּ״! אָמְרוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵית הִלֵּל: אִם כֵּן, ״עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע״ לְמָה לִי? שֶׁיָּכוֹל אֵין לִי אֶלָּא הוּא, אָמַר לְעַבְדּוֹ וְלִשְׁלוּחוֹ מִנַּיִן – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: The verse pertaining to **misuse** of consecrated property is **that which we said,** but **what is** the verse with regard to **misappropriation?** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah uses the inclusive term "every" with regard to one suspected of misappropriating a deposit: **"For every matter of trespass"** (Exodus 22:8). **Beit Shammai say:** This inclusive term "every" serves **to render** one **liable for** speech and **thought,** i.e., intent to misappropriate, **like action. And Beit Hillel say: One is liable only if he** actually **misappropriates it, as** it **is stated: "Whether he has not put his hand** unto his neighbor's goods" (Exodus 22:7). **Beit Shammai said to Beit Hillel: But isn't it stated: "For every matter of trespass,"** which indicates that one is liable without actually misappropriating the deposit? **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: But isn't it stated: "Whether he has not put his hand unto his neighbor's goods"? Beit Shammai said to Beit Hillel: If so,** if one is liable only for actual misappropriation, **why do I** need: **"For every matter of trespass"?** Beit Hillel replied: It is necessary, **as** one **might** have thought that **I have** derived liability **only** if **he** himself misappropriated it; **from where** do I derive that he is liable also if he **told his slave or his agent** to do so? **The verse states: "For every matter of trespass,"** to teach that the bailee is liable if one acting on his behalf misappropriates the deposit.

The *Gemara* introduces the second exception: *shelichus yad*. This refers to the case of a *shomer* — a watchman — who uses another person's property without permission. If one is watching someone's object and instead uses it for himself (the classic case being a *shomer* who drives someone's car rather than merely guarding it), that unauthorized use makes the *shomer* liable for any damage that subsequently occurs to the item. Now, what if the *shomer* did not use it himself but sent someone else to use it? That too creates liability — making this another instance where *shaliach lidvar aveira* applies, despite it being a prohibited act.

It is notable that in both *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad*, the operative concern is not only about religious transgression but also about civil liability — and it is precisely in those categories where the *Torah* carves out exceptions to *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

### Two Exceptions Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְבֵית הִלֵּל. אֶלָּא לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי, דְּמוֹקְמִי לֵיהּ לְהַאי קְרָא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה כְּמַעֲשֶׂה, נֵילַף מִינֵּיהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוָה מְעִילָה טְבִיחָה וּמְכִירָה שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכֹל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין. מְעִילָה – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, טְבִיחָה וּמְכִירָה מַאי הִיא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּטְבָחוֹ אוֹ מְכָרוֹ״ – מָה מְכִירָה עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר, אַף טְבִיחָה עַל יְדֵי אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains further: **This** answer, that misuse of consecrated property and misappropriation are two verses that come to teach the same matter, **works out well** according **to** the opinion of **Beit Hillel. But** according **to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who establish this verse** as rendering one liable **for thought like action** and do not learn from here that the bailee is liable if one acting on his behalf misappropriates the deposit, we should **derive** a principle from misuse of consecrated property, that an agent can be appointed to perform a transgression. The Gemara answers: This is not done, **because misuse** of consecrated property **and** the **slaughter or sale** of a stolen cow or sheep **are two verses that come as one, and any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common aspect to apply to other cases. The Gemara clarifies: The verse pertaining to **misuse** of consecrated property is **that which we said,** but **what is** the verse with regard to **slaughter or sale? The verse states: "And he slaughters it or sells it"** (Exodus 21:37), juxtaposing selling to slaughtering: **Just as a sale** is necessarily performed **by means of another,** since every transaction involves two parties, **so too slaughter** can likewise be performed **by means of another,** and the thief is liable even if the act of slaughtering is performed by his agent.

Since these two cases — *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad* — each appear independently in the *Torah* to establish *shaliach lidvar aveira*, and there are thereby two scriptural sources for the principle, they function as exceptions and not as a general rule. The logic is: had *shaliach lidvar aveira* been the rule, the *Torah* would have needed only one source. The fact that it appears twice signals that these are exceptional cases, and the governing principle across the rest of the *Torah* remains *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״אוֹ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשָּׁלִיחַ. דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה תָּנָא: ״תַּחַת״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשָּׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes two other explanations. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that the word **"or"** in the verse "and he slaughters it or sells it," serves **to include an agent. The school of Ḥizkiyya taught:** When the verse states with regard to sale or slaughter: "He shall pay five oxen for an ox, and four sheep for a sheep" (Exodus 21:37), repetition of the word **"for"** is superfluous and serves **to include an agent.** It teaches that the one who appointed the agent is liable for the action of the agent.

The *beraita* elaborates on the *shelichus yad* case through a debate between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel*. The *pasuk* says *al kol devar pesha* — on any matter of sin — and also *asher lo shalach yado bimleches reiehu* — that one did not cast his hand against his fellow's property. *Beis Shammai* holds that the word *devar* (which can mean speech or intention) implies that even expressing a desire to use the property — a *dibbur*, a thought or declaration of intent — is sufficient to create *shelichus yad* liability: *lechayav al hamachshavah kema'aseh*, thought is treated like action. *Beis Hillel* holds that one is not liable until *sheyishlach bo yad* — actual physical use — citing the language of the *pasuk* itself.

*Beis Shammai* challenges *Beis Hillel*: if literal *shlach yad* is required, what does *al kol devar pesha* add? *Beis Hillel* responds: *al kol devar pesha* addresses the case where you instructed your slave or your agent to use the property — *amar leavdo ushlucho* — so that even a *dibbur* in the form of directing another person creates liability. According to *Beis Hillel*, then, *al kol devar pesha* establishes the *shelichus yad* exception to *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — specifically the case of instructing an agent.

The *Gemara* then notes: this all works well for *Beis Hillel*, who has two exceptions — *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad* — giving two scriptural sources. But for *Beis Shammai*, who holds that *al kol devar pesha* refers to thought rather than agency, only *me'ilah* remains as a scriptural source for *shaliach lidvar aveira*. Perhaps, then, *me'ilah* should be the rule rather than the exception. The *Gemara* resolves this by introducing a third exception.

## Tevicha Umechira Case

### Stolen Livestock

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְלַמְּדִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? גַּלִּי רַחֲמָנָא בִּשְׁחוּטֵי חוּץ, ״דָּם יֵחָשֵׁב לָאִישׁ הַהוּא דָּם שָׁפָךְ״ – הוּא וְלֹא שְׁלוּחוֹ. אַשְׁכְּחַן גַּבֵּי שְׁחוּטֵי חוּץ, בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה מְנָלַן? דְּיָלֵיף מִשְּׁחוּטֵי חוּץ. אַדְּיָלֵיף מִשְּׁחוּטֵי חוּץ, נֵילַיף מֵהָנָךְ! הֲדַר כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְנִכְרַת הָאִישׁ הַהוּא״ – אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְגוּפוֹ, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who said** that **two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent that applies to other cases, **but according to the one who said** that two verses do **teach** a precedent, **what can be said?** Why not derive from the case of misuse of consecrated property and that of slaughter or sale that an agent can be appointed to perform a transgression? The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One reveals** in the Torah that an agent cannot be appointed to perform a transgression **in** the case of an offering **slaughtered outside** the Temple, as it states: **"It shall be considered blood for that [*hahu*] man; he has spilled blood"** (Leviticus 17:4). The emphasis of "that man" teaches that **he** is liable for his actions, **but** he is **not** liable for the actions of **his agent.** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that an agent cannot be appointed to perform a transgression **with regard to** the prohibition of offerings **slaughtered outside. From where do we** derive that this *halakha* applies **to the entire Torah?** The Gemara answers: It is **derived** as a principle **from** the case of offerings **slaughtered outside,** by means of analogy. The Gemara asks: But **before deriving** that an agent cannot be appointed to perform a transgression **from** the case of offerings **slaughtered outside, let us derive** the *halakha* **from these** other cases, i.e., misuse of consecrated property and slaughter or sale, where the *halakha* is that an agent can be appointed to perform a transgression. The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One again writes** in that same verse in connection to the prohibition of slaughtering offerings outside the Temple: **"And that [*hahu*] man shall be cut off** from among his people" (Leviticus 17:4). **If** the *halakha* derived from the emphasis of the word *hahu* written in this part of the verse **is not needed for** this **matter itself,** since the verse already taught that he is liable only for his own act and not for that of his agent, **apply it to the matter of the entire Torah,** so that one is held liable only for one's own actions and not for those of an agent.

The *Gemara* identifies a third category: *tevicha umechira*. If a *gannav* steals an ox or a sheep and then slaughters or sells it, he is liable to pay four or five times the value. The *Gemara* now establishes that this four- or fivefold liability applies even if the *gannav* had a *shaliach* perform the slaughter or the sale on his behalf. This is yet another case where *shaliach lidvar aveira* obtains despite the underlying act being forbidden.

### Deriving Shaliach

Two *tannaim* derive this inclusion of the *shaliach* from different *pesukim*. *Devei Rabbi Yishmael* derives it from the word *o* ("or") in the phrase *o utvacho o mechoro* — "or slaughter it or sell it." Just as selling inherently involves another party (the buyer), so too slaughtering can involve another party (a *shaliach*). *Devei Chizkiyah* derives it from the word *tachas* — the fourfold and fivefold payment is described as *tachas*, and this word is taken to include liability even when the *shaliach* performs the act.

### Exceptions Not Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִים, הָנֵי ״הָהוּא״ ״הָהוּא״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בְּהוּ? חַד, לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁנַיִם שֶׁאוֹחֲזִים בְּסַכִּין וְשׁוֹחֲטִים, וְחַד, ״הוּא״ – וְלֹא אָנוּס, ״הוּא״ – וְלֹא שׁוֹגֵג, ״הוּא״ – וְלֹא מוּטְעֶה. וְאִידָּךְ? מֵ״הוּא״ ״הָהוּא״. וְאִידָּךְ? ״הוּא״ ״הַהוּא״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that **two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent, and there is no reason to learn from the misuse of consecrated property and slaughter or sale that one can appoint an agent to perform a transgression, **what does he interpret those** terms: **"That** man" and: **"That** man" to be teaching? Since he does not need to counter a derivation from the two verses, he should not require them to teach that one cannot appoint an agent to perform a transgression. The Gemara answers: **One** of them serves **to exclude** from liability the case of **two who hold a knife and slaughter** an offering outside together. **And** the other **one** emphasizes: **"That** man" is liable, **but not one** who is **compelled** to slaughter the animal; **"that** man" is liable, **but not** one who does so **unwittingly;** and **"that** man" is liable, **and not** one who **was mistaken,** e.g., one who did not know that the animal was an offering. In order to be liable one must have acted with full awareness and completely willingly. The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** opinion, i.e., the one who holds that two verses that come as one do teach a precedent, which derives from the word "that [*hahu*]" that an agent cannot be appointed to perform a transgression, derive these *halakhot*? The Gemara answers: He derives them from the fact that the pronoun "*hahu*" is composed of two parts, the article *ha*, meaning: The, and the pronoun *hu*, meaning: He. He derives *halakhot* both **from "*hu*"** and from the additional article in **"*hahu*."** Therefore, he is able to derive both the principle that an agent cannot be appointed to perform a transgression and the *halakhot* of two that slaughter and one who acts without full awareness. **And the other** opinion, i.e., the one who holds that two verses that come as one do not teach a precedent, which derives these *halakhot* from the entire word "*hahu*," **does not interpret** the variation from **"*hu*"** to **"*hahu*."** He holds that one cannot derive separate *halakhot* from each part of this word.

With three exceptions now identified — *me'ilah*, *shelichus yad*, and *tevicha umechira* — the *Gemara* is satisfied that these are exceptions to the rule, not paradigm cases. For *Beis Hillel*, two exceptions (*me'ilah* and *shelichus yad*) suffice to establish them as non-normative by the principle of *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin*. For *Beis Shammai*, who requires more examples to classify sources as exceptions, the third case of *tevicha umechira* provides the necessary additional instance.

---

## General Rule Confirmed

### Shchutei Chutz Rule

The *Gemara* then asks: according to those who hold *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad melamdin* — that two scriptural instances do generate a general rule rather than marking exceptions — how do we know that *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* is the baseline? The answer lies in the case of *shchutei chutz*, slaughtering a *korban* outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. The *Torah* states *dam yeichashev la'ish hahu dam shafach* — the blood shall be reckoned to *that man*, the one who spilled it. The word *hahu* — "that man" — specifies that it is the doer himself who is liable, not one who instructed another to do it. This verse, cited only once, functions as a categorical statement of the principle: only the doer is liable.

### Double Hahu Analysis

The *Gemara* notes further that the *pasuk* actually uses *ha'ish hahu* twice — once in *dam yeichashev la'ish hahu* and once in *venichras ha'ish hahu* (that man is *kares*). The *Gemara* asks: why doesn't the *Gemara* instead learn from the exceptions (*tevicha umechira* and *me'ilah*) and treat those as the rule? The answer is that the doubled *hahu* is the decisive factor. Since the first *hahu* already establishes that only the doer of *shchutei chutz* is liable, the second *hahu* is superfluous to that specific ruling — *im einu inyan legufo, tanehu inyan lechol hatorah kullah* — and is therefore applied to the entire *Torah* as a general rule: only the doer himself is liable, and the cases of *me'ilah*, *shelichus yad*, and *tevicha umechira* are the exceptions.

### Alternative Drashim

For those who hold *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* — for whom the two or three exceptions already suffice to establish the rule — the two instances of *hahu* in the *shchutei chutz* passage must be put to other uses. The *Gemara* explains: one *hahu* teaches *lema'utei shnayim she'ochazin besakin veshochetin* — to exclude liability when two people hold the knife together and perform the act jointly, in which case neither is liable for *kares*. The other *hahu* teaches *hu velo anus, hu velo shogeg, hu velo mut'eh* — only one who acts willfully is liable for *kares*, not one who is coerced, acts inadvertently, or is mistaken. (In the case of *shogeg* one would still bring a *korban*, but there is no *kares*.) The *Gemara* notes that the other opinion does not independently derive these principles from the double *hahu* but arrives at them through other means.

## Shammai on Murder

### Murder Instruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתָנֵי: הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: ״צֵא הֲרוֹג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ״, הוּא – חַיָּיב, וְשׁוֹלְחָיו – פָּטוּר. שַׁמַּאי הַזָּקֵן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם חַגַּי הַנָּבִיא: שׁוֹלְחָיו חַיָּיב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֹתוֹ הָרַגְתָּ בְּחֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the statement that there is no agency for transgressions: **But** there is **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who says to his agent: Go kill a person, he,** the killer, **is liable** if he kills, **and the one who appointed him is exempt. Shammai the Elder says in the name of Haggai the prophet: The one who appointed him is liable, as it stated** with regard to David, who directed Joab to kill Uriah: **"Him you have slain with the sword of the children of Ammon"** (II Samuel 12:9). David was held responsible for the death of Uriah.

The *Gemara* cites a *beraita*: *ha'omer lishlucho tze veharog es hanefesh* — if one tells his *shaliach* to go and commit murder — *hu chayav umshalcho patur*: the murderer is liable and the one who sent him is exempt. This reflects the general principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

### Shammai's Ruling

However, the *beraita* continues: in the name of *Chaggai HaNavi*, *sholcho chayav* — even the one who sent the *shaliach* is liable. The proof text is *oso haragta b'cherev b'nei Amon* — when the *Navi* holds *David* accountable for the death of *Uriyah HaChiti*, the language used is "you killed him by the sword of *b'nei Amon*." Yet *David* did not personally kill *Uriyah*; he sent him to the front. Nevertheless, *David* is treated as the one who killed him. *Shammai HaZaken* therefore rules that the *meshaleach* is *chayav* even in a case of murder.

### Shammai's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּשַׁמַּאי הַזָּקֵן? קָסָבַר: שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד מְלַמְּדִין, וְ״הוּא״ ״הַהוּא״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם דָּרֵישׁ, וּמַאי חַיָּיב – חַיָּיב בְּדִינֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Shammai the Elder?** How can he say that there is agency for transgression? The Gemara answers: **He holds** that **two verses that come as one do teach** a precedent, and therefore he learns from the two cases of misuse of consecrated property and slaughter or sale that there is agency for transgression. **And** as for the derivation from one who slaughters an offering outside the Temple, which teaches that there is no agent for transgression, Shammai **does not interpret** the variation from **"*hu*"** to **"*hahu*."** And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually** it is possible that he does **interpret** the variation, and he agrees that there is no agent for transgression. **And what** is the meaning of Shammai's statement that the one who appoints him is **liable?** It means he is **liable according to the laws of Heaven,** although he cannot be punished by a human court.

What is the reasoning of *Shammai HaZaken*? *Kasavar shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad melamdim* — he holds that when two scriptural instances establish a principle, that principle is extended as a general rule rather than treated as an exception. For *Shammai HaZaken*, who aligns with the position of *Beis Shammai*, there are only two exceptions: *me'ilah* and *tevicha umechira* — and those two do function as a general rule, establishing *yesh shaliach lidvar aveira* across the *Torah*. He does not derive the limiting principle from the double *hahu* of *shchutei chutz*, and therefore holds that the *meshaleach* can bear liability.

### B'Dinei Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִדִּינֵי שָׁמַיִם נָמֵי פָּטוּר?! אֶלָּא: דִּינָא רַבָּה וְדִינָא זוּטָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **By inference,** does this mean **that the first *tanna* holds** that **he is exempt even according to the laws of Heaven?** The one who appointed him must bear some responsibility. **Rather,** the first *tanna* also agrees that the one who appointed the killer is liable according to the laws of Heaven, and **the** difference **between them** pertains to **a great judgment and a small judgment.** According to Shammai, his liability is great, to the extent that Heaven considers him fully responsible, whereas the first *tanna* holds that his liability is of a lesser degree.

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative interpretation of *Shammai HaZaken*'s position. Perhaps *Shammai HaZaken* does in fact agree that there is no *shaliach lidvar aveira* in human courts — and when he says *sholcho chayav*, he means *chayav b'dinei shamayim*: liable before Heaven, treated as a murderer in the eyes of God, just as the *Navi* addresses *David* as one who killed *Uriyah*. On this reading, the dispute is not about whether the principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* applies in *dinei adam*, but rather about the degree of heavenly accountability. The *tanna kamma* who says the *meshaleach* is *patur* would then be understood to be referring to human court liability only — surely everyone would agree that one who arranges a murder bears some degree of heavenly responsibility. The debate is therefore one of *dinei rabbah u'dinei zuta*: whether the *meshaleach* is treated in Heaven as an outright murderer or merely as one who incited murder.

### Murder Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּגַלִּי רַחֲמָנָא ״אֹתוֹ הָרַגְתָּ בְּחֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: Everyone agrees that there is no agent for transgression. Nevertheless, Shammai holds that **there,** with regard to killing, it **is different, since the Merciful One reveals: "Him you have slain with the sword of the children of Ammon,"** explicitly rendering David accountable for this transgression and indicating that killing is different from all other transgressions.

The *Gemara* offers yet a further interpretation: *shani hasam d'gali rachmana oso haragta b'cherev b'nei Amon* — perhaps the *pasuk* concerning *David* and *Uriyah* teaches a specific *halacha* unique to murder. One might have thought that of all prohibitions, murder would be the one most governed by *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — since *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid* is at its most compelling when the act is taking a human life. Yet precisely because of the immense gravity of murder, the *Torah* singles it out to hold the *meshaleach* liable as well, in addition to the one who performed the act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: הֲרֵי לְךָ כְּחֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן, מָה חֶרֶב בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן – אֵין אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו, אַף אוּרִיָּה הַחִתִּי – אִי אַתָּה נֶעֱנָשׁ עָלָיו. מַאי טַעְמָא – מוֹרֵד בַּמַּלְכוּת הֲוָה, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״וַאדֹנִי יוֹאָב וְעַבְדֵי אֲדֹנִי עַל פְּנֵי הַשָּׂדֶה חֹנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** opinion, i.e., the first *tanna*, who holds that the one who appoints the killer is exempt, explains the verse as follows: **Behold** this killing is **for you like the sword of Ammon. Just as you are not punished** for those killed by **the sword of Ammon** in the course of the war, **so too you are not punished for** the death of **Uriah the Hittite,** not even according to the laws of Heaven. **What is the reason** for this? Uriah **was a rebel against the monarchy** and was consequently liable to the death penalty, **as he said to** King David: **"And my lord Joab, and the servants of my lord, are encamped in the open field"** (II Samuel 11:11). By referring to Joab as his lord in front of the king, he indicated that he answered to Joab rather than to the king, which is tantamount to rebellion.

Those who disagree with *Shammai HaZaken* read the *pasuk* differently: *harei l'cha k'cherev b'nei Amon* — the comparison to the sword of *b'nei Amon* is meant to say the opposite. Just as one is not held liable when *b'nei Amon* kill people in the ordinary course of war, so too *David* should not be held liable for the death of *Uriyah*. The *pasuk* that *Shammai* uses to establish liability is turned on its head by his opponents to establish exemption. Moreover, those who hold *David* exempt argue that there was justification for *Uriyah*'s death: *mored b'malchus havah* — he was in a state of rebellion against the king. The evidence is *Uriyah*'s own words, in which he referred to *Yoav* as his master in the presence of *David*, thereby according authority to someone other than the king, or alternatively, by defying *David*'s explicit order to return home to his wife.

## Raba's Clarification

### Sexual & Food Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר סָבַר שַׁמַּאי שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד מְלַמְּדִין, וְ״הוּא״ ״הַהוּא״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ, מוֹדֶה בְּאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ ״צֵא בְּעוֹל אֶת הָעֶרְוָה״ וֶ״אֱכוֹל אֶת הַחֵלֶב״ שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, וְשׁוֹלְחָיו פָּטוּר. שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ זֶה נֶהֱנֶה וְזֶה מִתְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If you say** that **Shammai holds** that **two verses that come as one do teach** a precedent, **and he does not interpret** the variation from **"*hu*"** to **"*hahu*,"** the combination of which would result in him holding that there is agency for transgression in all cases, even he **concedes with regard to one who says to his agent: Go and engage in sexual intercourse with a forbidden relative,** or: Go **and eat** forbidden **fat, that** the agent **is liable and the one who appointed him is exempt, as we have not found in the entire Torah** a case where **this** person physically **benefits** from the transgression **but that** one **becomes liable.**

*Rava* adds an important clarification: even if one follows *Shammai HaZaken* and holds *yesh shaliach lidvar aveira* as a general principle, there remains a category of cases where *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* applies even according to *Shammai HaZaken*. That category is: if one instructs a *shaliach* to engage in sexual intercourse with a forbidden woman, or to eat *treif* food — *she'hu chayav v'sholcho patur* — the doer is liable and the sender is entirely exempt.

### Pleasure Theory

The reason is stated as a general principle: *she'lo matzinu b'chol haTorah kula ze neheneh v'ze mitchayev* — we have never found anywhere in the entire *Torah* that one person receives the *hana'ah* (the pleasure or benefit) while another person bears the liability. In these categories of sin, the transgression is not constituted merely by the physical act but by the *hana'ah* experienced in performing that act. An act can be attributed to a principal; pleasure cannot. The experience of *hana'ah* is irreducibly subjective and personal — it belongs only to the one who undergoes it, and there is no mechanism by which it can be transferred or attributed to another. This connects as well to the principle of *mita'sek b'chalavim v'arayos*: one cannot divorce the act from oneself when it is the *hana'ah* inherent in the doing that constitutes the prohibition.

## Shaliach as Witness

### Initial Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, רַב אָמַר: שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד. דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמְרִי: אֵין שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הֱוֵי לִי עֵד״ – אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה קִידֵּשׁ אִשָּׁה בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם וְלָא אָמַר לָהֶם: ״אַתֶּם עֵדַיי״ – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי? אֶלָּא, רַב אָמַר: שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד – אַלּוֹמֵי קָא מְאַלֵּימְנָא לְמִילְּתֵיהּ. דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמְרִי: אֵין שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד – כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: ״שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ״ – הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּגוּפֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that *amora'im* engaged in a dispute concerning the following issue: **Rav says: An agent can become a witness.** If one was appointed as an agent to perform a certain task, he can then serve as a witness that the action was performed. **In the school of Rabbi Sheila they say: An agent cannot become a witness.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of the school of Rabbi Sheila? If we say** it is **because** the one who appointed him **did not say to** the agent: **Be a witness for me,** and appointed him only as an agent; **if that is so,** if one **betrothed a woman in the presence of two** people **and did not say to them: You are my witnesses, so too** would they hold **that it is not a betrothal?** It is not necessary to explicitly appoint witnesses. **Rather,** the dispute is as follows. **Rav says: An agent can become a witness,** since **we strengthen his words** of testimony because he attests to a matter of which he is certain, as he is the one that performed the action. **In the school of Rabbi Sheila they say: An agent cannot become a witness. Since the Master said** that the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself,** the agent **is considered like** the one who appointed him **himself.** Just as one cannot testify with regard to a matter that concerns himself, the same applies to one's agent.

The *Gemara* now turns to an additional question within the laws of *shelichus*: *shaliach na'aseh eid* — can a *shaliach* serve as a witness to the very mission he was sent to perform? If, for example, two people are sent as *shluchim* to betroth a woman or to repay a debt, can those same individuals serve as *eidim* to attest that the act occurred? Or is the *shaliach* somehow considered equivalent to the principal — and just as the principal himself cannot be a witness to his own act, neither can the *shaliach*?

*Rav* holds *shaliach na'aseh eid*: a *shaliach* can indeed serve as a witness. *Beis Rabbi Shila* holds *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*: he cannot. The reasoning of *Beis Rabbi Shila* is grounded in the principle *shlucho shel adam kemoso* — the *shaliach* is considered equivalent to the principal himself. Just as a party to a transaction cannot serve as a witness to it, so too the *shaliach*, who stands in the principal's stead, cannot serve as an independent witness. He becomes, in legal terms, *k'gufei* — like the principal's own self.

One might initially suggest that the problem is simply that the *meshaleach* never explicitly designated the *shaliach* as a witness, only as an agent. But this cannot be the issue: if someone betroths a woman in the presence of two witnesses without explicitly saying "you are my witnesses," the *kiddushin* is nonetheless valid. Witnesses do not need formal designation when they are observing the act itself — the only context requiring explicit designation is an admission of debt, where otherwise one might claim he was speaking in jest. So the absence of formal appointment as *eid* cannot be the objection.

### Baraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אָמַר לִשְׁלֹשָׁה ״צְאוּ וְקַדְּשׁוּ לִי הָאִשָּׁה״ – אֶחָד שָׁלִיחַ וּשְׁנַיִם עֵדִים, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כּוּלָּם שְׁלוּחִין הֵן, וְאֵין שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד. עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנַיִם – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita* to the statement of Rav: If one **said to three** people: **Go out and betroth the woman for me,** then **one** of them is the **agent** for the betrothal **and two** of them serve as **witnesses;** this is **the statement of Beit Shammai. And Beit Hillel say: They are all** potential **agents, and** as **an agent cannot become a witness** none of them can be witnesses. Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **disagree only with regard to** a case where one said this to **three** people. **But with regard to two** people, **all agree** that agents are **not** able to serve as witnesses.

The *Gemara* challenges *Rav*'s position from a *beraita*: *amar l'shlosha tze'u v'kadshu li isha* — if one says to three people "go and betroth this woman for me," *Beis Shammai* holds that one of them serves as the *shaliach* and the other two as independent *eidim* — you are one or the other, not both. *Beis Hillel* holds *kulam shluchim hen* — all three are considered *shluchim*, and since they are all *shluchim*, *v'ein shaliach na'aseh eid* — none of them can simultaneously serve as *eidim*. The result under *Beis Hillel* is that you need to find other witnesses entirely. Both schools of thought, with *Beis Hillel* being the stricter, appear to hold *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*. How can *Rav* maintain the contrary?

### Position Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שָׁלִיחַ וְעֵד אֶחָד. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שָׁלִיחַ וּשְׁנֵי עֵדִים. וְרַב כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי?! אֵיפוֹךְ. וְרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנִי אִיפְּכָא: רַב אָמַר: אֵין שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד, דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אָמְרִי: שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד. וְהִילְכְתָא: שָׁלִיחַ נַעֲשֶׂה עֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It was **he,** Rav, **who said** his ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*,** i.e., the version of Beit Shammai as presented by Rabbi Natan. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says: Beit Shammai say** that **the agent and one witness** are sufficient to carry out the agency and testify, **and Beit Hillel say: The agent and two witnesses** are needed. The Gemara questions this explanation: **And** would **Rav** rule **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai?** The Gemara answers: **Reverse** the opinions, so that it is Beit Hillel who said that the agent and one witness suffice. **And Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, taught** the dispute of the *amora'im* in the **opposite** manner. **Rav says: An agent cannot become a witness,** and **in the school of Rabbi Sheila they say: An agent can become a witness.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that **an agent can become a witness.**

The *Gemara* answers that *Rav* rules like a different *tanna*: a *beraita* in the name of *Rabbi Natan* presents *Beis Shammai* as holding *shaliach v'ed echad* — a *shaliach* plus one additional witness suffice (meaning the *shaliach* himself can serve as one of the two required *eidim*), while *Beis Hillel* holds *shaliach u'shnei eidim* — the *shaliach* cannot be counted, and two additional witnesses are required. The *Gemara* then notes: but would *Rav* rule like *Beis Shammai*? The resolution is *ipech* — reverse the positions in that *beraita*: it is *Beis Hillel* who holds *shaliach na'aseh eid*, and *Beis Shammai* who holds *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*. On this reversal, *Rav* is in agreement with *Beis Hillel*.

### Final Halacha

*Rav Acha bar Rava* teaches the debate between *Rav* and *Beis Rabbi Shila* in the reverse of the original formulation: *Rav* holds *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*, while *Beis Rabbi Shila* holds *shaliach na'aseh eid*. Regardless of which formulation one follows, the *Gemara* concludes: *v'hilcheta shaliach na'aseh eid* — the *halacha* is that a *shaliach* can serve as a witness, though the theoretical grounding for this ruling requires considerable effort given the weight of the opposing sources.

## Shaliach Eid Applications

### Two Shluchim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: ״צְאוּ וְקַדְּשׁוּ לִי אֶת הָאִשָּׁה״ – הֵן הֵן שְׁלוּחָיו, הֵן הֵן עֵדָיו. וְכֵן בְּגֵירוּשִׁין, וְכֵן בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** It follows that if one **said to two** people: **Go and betroth the woman for me, the very same** people who are **his agents** for the betrothal **are his witnesses. And a similar** *halakha* is true **with regard to divorce:** If a man sent a bill of divorce to his wife with two people, they serve both as agents of delivery and as witnesses to the divorce. **And a similar** *halakha* applies **with regard to** cases of **monetary law.** If one appoints agents to perform a transaction for him, e.g., paying a debt to his creditor, they can testify that he has paid.

*Rava* in the name of *Rav Nachman* now states the operative *halacha* flowing from this principle: *amar l'shnayim tze'u v'kadshu li isha, hen shluchav v'hen eidav* — if one says to two people "go betroth this woman for me," they are his *shluchim* and they can also serve as his *eidim* to the act of *kiddushin*.

### Gerushin Concern

The same rule applies *b'gerushin* — in the case of divorce. If two people are sent to deliver a *get*, they can serve as witnesses to its delivery, with one caveat: there is a concern that one of them might have set his eye on the woman — that is, he may want to marry her himself — and this personal interest could compromise his reliability as a witness. Nevertheless, the concern is addressed, as will be explained.

### Mamonot Concern

The same rule applies *b'dinei mamonot* — if two people are sent to repay a debt, they too can serve as witnesses to the repayment. Here the concern is different: perhaps they will pocket the money and then falsely testify that they paid the creditor. Again, however, the concern is addressed.

### Why Concerns Addressed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּקִידּוּשִׁין – מִשּׁוּם דִּלְמֵיסְרַהּ קָאָתֵי, אֲבָל גֵּירוּשִׁין – נֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא עֵינָיו נָתַן בָּהּ. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גֵּירוּשִׁין – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתְּתָא לְבֵי תְרֵי לָא חַזְיָא, אֲבָל מָמוֹנָא – אֵימָא: הָנֵי מִיפְלָג פָּלְגִי. צְרִיכִי. מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר הַמַּלְוֶה חֲבֵירוֹ בְּעֵדִים צָרִיךְ לְפוֹרְעוֹ בְּעֵדִים – הָנֵי נוֹגְעִים בְּעֵדוּת נִינְהוּ, דְּאִי אָמְרִי: ״לָא פְּרַעְנֵיהּ״ אָמַר לְהוּ: ״פְּרָעוּנִי״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** for Rav Naḥman to teach this *halakha* in each of these legal domains, **as had he taught us** this *halakha* **only in** the case of **betrothal** one could say that the agents can serve as witnesses **because** they **are coming to render her forbidden** to everyone else, and therefore there is no reason to suspect them of lying, as their testimony renders her forbidden to them as well. **But** with regard to **divorce, we should be concerned** that **perhaps** the agent **cast his eyes upon her** and is testifying falsely so that he can marry her. **And had** Rav Naḥman **taught us** this *halakha* only in the case of **divorce,** it could have been said that the agents are not suspected of lying **because a woman is not fit for** marrying **two** people, and since they testify as a pair there is no concern that they both might have designs upon her. **But** with regard to **money,** one might **say** that **these** two can **divide** it between them, and perhaps they never paid the debt but kept the money themselves. Therefore, all the examples **are necessary.** The Gemara asks: **What does** Rav Naḥman **hold? If he holds** that in the case of **one who lends** money to **another in** the presence of **witnesses,** the debtor **must repay him in** the presence of **witnesses,** then **these** agents **are affected by their testimony. As, if they say: We did not repay him** but returned the money to the one who appointed us, then the one who appointed them will **say to them: Pay me** back the money I gave you to repay the debt. The agents are considered as the debtors of the one who appointed them, as they took money from him. They would not be deemed credible to state that they returned the money to the one who appointed them, as they do not have witnesses that they did so. Consequently, they have a financial incentive to testify falsely that they fulfilled their agency and repaid the debt.

The *Gemara* explains why all three cases remain valid despite these concerns, and why it was necessary to state all three. Had the ruling been stated only for *kiddushin*, one might have thought it applies specifically because the woman's becoming betrothed makes her forbidden to the *shaliach* himself — *l'issurei kasi* — thereby eliminating any personal interest he might have, and thus any concern of *nogea b'davar*. One could not have inferred from *kiddushin* that the same applies to *gerushin*, since in *gerushin* the *shaliach* may wish to marry the woman after her divorce and thus has a stake in the outcome. However, in *gerushin* there are two *shluchim* — and since neither can marry both parties, nor can both equally benefit, the concern is neutralized: *d'ita l'bei trai lo chazi* — she cannot go to both of them, so you cannot say both are equally *nogea b'davar*. And the case of *dinei mamonot* cannot be inferred from *gerushin*, since with money there is a more tangible concern that both witnesses will conspire to keep the funds for themselves. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* concludes *tzrichi* — all three must be stated, and in all three the *shluchim* are valid *eidim*.

The *Gemara* then deepens the analysis of the monetary case, asking: is there in fact a concern of *nogea b'eidus* — a disqualifying personal interest in the outcome? The issue turns on a separate *halachic* principle: *hamalveh et chaveiro b'eidim tzarich l'faro b'eidim* — if one lends money in the presence of witnesses, must he repay it in the presence of witnesses? If the answer is yes, then the *shluchim*, if they fail to deliver the money to the creditor, would have to repay it to the *meshaleach* — and they would not be believed to say they had already returned it. This means that by testifying that they paid the creditor, they are freeing themselves from liability to the *meshaleach*, making them *nogea b'eidus*. The *Gemara* resolves: *le'olam kasavar ein tzarich l'faro b'eidim* — the *halacha* is that one need not repay a debt-by-witnesses in the presence of witnesses. Therefore, if the *shluchim* claimed they had returned the money to the *meshaleach*, they would simply be believed. Since they could equally have said "we returned it to you," they gain nothing by testifying that they paid the creditor instead. They are thus not *nogea b'eidus*, and their testimony stands.

## Shevuas Hesess Impact

### Pre-Hesess Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בְּעֵדִים אֵין צָרִיךְ לְפוֹרְעוֹ בְּעֵדִים, וּמִגּוֹ דְּיָכְלִי לְמֵימַר: ״אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ לְלֹוֶה״, יְכוֹלִין לְמֵימַר: ״פְּרַעְנֵיהּ לְמַלְוֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rav Naḥman **actually holds** that in the case of **one who lends** money to **another in** the presence of **witnesses,** the debtor **does not need to repay him in** the presence of **witnesses. And since** the agents **are able to say: We returned** the money **to the debtor,** even without there being witnesses to substantiate their claim, **they can** also be deemed credible **to say: We repaid the creditor,** as they have no financial incentive to lie.

Before the rabbinic enactment of *shevuas hesess*, the analysis above held cleanly. The *shluchim* could serve as *eidim* in monetary matters because they had nothing to gain from their testimony: *malveh et chaveiro b'eidim ein tzarich l'faro b'eidim* — and they were equally believed whether they said they paid the creditor or returned the funds to the *meshaleach*. There was therefore no interest on their part, no disqualifying *nogea b'eidus*, and their testimony was valid.

### Post-Hesess Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּתַקּוּן רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעַת הֶיסֵּת – מִשְׁתַּבְעִי הָנֵי עֵדִים דְּיָהֵיבְנָא לֵיהּ, וּמִשְׁתְּבַע מַלְוֶה דְּלָא שָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ, וּפָרַע לֵיהּ לֹוֶה לְמַלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And now,** since the time of Rav Naḥman, **when the Sages instituted an oath of inducement,** an oath instituted by the Sages in a case where a defendant completely denies a claim, these witnesses are affected by their testimony. If they were to claim that they returned the money to the one who appointed them, they would be required to take an oath of inducement to that effect. Consequently, they have an incentive to lie and claim that they fulfilled their agency and repaid the loan. Therefore, their testimony that they fulfilled their agency is not deemed credible. Instead, **these witnesses take an oath** in court **that they gave** the money **to him,** i.e., the lender, **and** the **lender** in turn **takes an oath that he did not take** the money owed to him, **and** then **the debtor pays the lender** his debt a second time, as the Sages ruled in similar cases.

After *shevuas hesess* was introduced by the *Rabbanan* — under which virtually any monetary claim carries with it a requirement that the defendant take an oath — the calculus changed. Now, if the *shluchim* were to admit that they had not paid the creditor, the *meshaleach* could demand repayment, and they would be required to take a *shevuah* to establish that they had returned the funds to him. By testifying instead that they paid the creditor, they free themselves from that *shevuah* obligation. They thus have a tangible gain from their testimony — they are now *nogea b'eidus*, disqualified as witnesses.

### New Outcome

The practical result under the post-*hesess* regime is as follows: since the *shluchim* can no longer serve as *eidim*, their testimony is not accepted. Instead, the *shluchim* take an oath that they did in fact pay the creditor. The creditor in turn takes an oath that he never received payment. The consequence is that the *loveh* — the original debtor who sent the *shluchim* — must pay the *malveh* again. The irony is considerable: it is specifically their *shevuah* obligation to the *loveh* that disqualifies the *shluchim* from serving as witnesses — and it is precisely that disqualification that leaves the *loveh* without recourse and compelled to pay twice. That is the unfortunate outcome when *shevuas hesess* intersects with the institution of *shaliach na'aseh eid*.
