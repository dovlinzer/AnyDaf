# Kiddushin 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelichus Sources

### Daf Overview

Today's *daf* is 43, picking up on 42b at the word *hasholeach*. At this point the *Gemara* has wrapped up its discussion of the basic sources for the principle of *shelichus* — derived from *terumah*, from *kiddushin* and *gittin*, and from *korbanos*. The *daf* now turns to a set of exceptions to the general rule of *shelichus*, centered on the concept of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

---

## Fire via Irresponsible

### Hasholeach Beyad Cheresh

The *Gemara* on 42b begins with the *Mishnah*'s case: *hasholeach es habeeirah beyad cheresh shoteh vekatan* — if one sends a fire through the hands of a *cheresh* (a deaf-mute), a *shoteh* (someone with a psychiatric illness who does not process reality clearly), or a *katan* (a minor). All three of these individuals are not considered legally responsible for their actions. The ruling is *patur midinei adam vechayav midinei shamayim*: the one who sent the fire is exempt from human legal liability but bears responsibility before Heaven, since he clearly caused a conflagration and destruction of property.

### Liability Question

However, if the fire was transmitted through the hand of a *pikeach* — a mentally competent adult — then *pikeach chayav*: the adult who performed the act of burning is liable, and the one who sent him is exempt. This resembles the principle underlying organized criminal liability: the person who orchestrates and manipulates the scheme is, from a *halachic* standpoint, not directly responsible — only the person who actually lit the flame bears liability.

The *Gemara* then asks why the *meshaleach* is not liable through the principle of *shelichus*: *vamai, neima sheliach shel adam kemoso* — why not say that a person's *shaliach* is legally equivalent to himself? One might have thought that *shelichus* is simply irrelevant here because agency is about the delegation of legal authority, whereas the physical act of lighting a fire is not the kind of authority that can be transferred. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* engages the question directly.

### Ein Shaliach Lidvar

The *Gemara*'s answer is: *shani hasam de'ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — it is different there because there is no *shaliach* for a matter of sin. The reason is stated as *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid divrei mi shome'in*: between the words of the Master and the words of the student, whose words does one listen to? The point is that when you instruct a *shaliach* to commit a transgression, you cannot be held responsible for his action — he should have listened to God rather than to you. Ultimately, a person is responsible for his own choices, and the sin and the liability belong to the one who performed the act. This is an important principle that operates across the *halacha* of *shelichus*: *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid divrei mi shome'in*.

### Shaliach Awareness

An interesting question arises from this reasoning: does the principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* apply even when the *shaliach* was unaware that the act was a sin? If, for example, the *meshaleach* asked someone to take what he believed to be his own car to be destroyed, and the *shaliach* had no reason to suspect otherwise, perhaps in that case the *meshaleach* would in fact bear liability — since the *shaliach* had no grounds to refuse. The *Gemara* does not pursue this question directly at this point, but it is worth flagging as a genuine conceptual tension.

## Me'ilah & Shelichus

### Me'ilah Liability

The *Gemara* now cites a *beraita* regarding *me'ilah* — the inadvertent misappropriation of *hekdesh* funds. The ruling is: *shaliach shelo asah shelichuso shaliach ma'al, asah shelichuso ba'al habayis ma'al* — if the *shaliach* failed to carry out his mission (for instance, if sent to buy vegetables with *hekdesh* money he instead bought something else), then the *shaliach* himself committed *me'ilah*, since he was acting on his own rather than on behalf of the *meshaleach*. But if the *shaliach* carried out the mission as instructed, then the *ba'al habayis* committed *me'ilah* — the sin reverts to the one who sent him.

### Challenge to Principle

This creates an immediate difficulty: *vamai, neima ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — we should say that there is no *shaliach* for a matter of sin. How can the *ba'al habayis* be liable for *me'ilah* if the act was performed by the *shaliach*, and *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* should prevent attributing that act back to him? *Tosafos* raises a related point here: since *me'ilah* applies only in cases of *shogeg* (inadvertence), perhaps the principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* as expressed through *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid* does not apply in cases where the *shaliach* had no reason to refrain. Whether *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* is grounded in the principle that the *shaliach* should have known better, or whether it is a more fundamental rule that sin can only be attributed to the one who personally performs it, remains an open question — but the *Gemara* does not resolve it at this point.

### Gezeirah Shavah Answer

The *Gemara* answers: *shani me'ilah diyalaf cheit cheit miTrumah* — *me'ilah* is different because the word *cheit* appears both in the laws of *terumah* and in the laws of *me'ilah*, creating a *gezeirah shavah*. Since *terumah* is one of the foundational sources for the institution of *shelichus* itself — *mah Trumah mishvach shliach af me'ilah mishvach shliach* — the same way *shelichus* applies for *terumah*, so too it applies for *me'ilah*, causing the liability to revert to the *meshaleach*.

The *Gemara* then asks: *veneilaf minah* — why not use *me'ilah* as a paradigm and extend *shaliach lidvar aveira* across all of *halacha*? The answer is: *mishum dehavei me'ilah veshlichus yad shnei kesuvin habayim ke'echad* — because *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad* together constitute *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad*, two scriptural instances coming as one, *ve'ein melamdin* — and two scriptural sources that appear together as exceptions do not generate a general rule.

## Shelichus Yad Exception

### Shelichus Yad Concept

The *Gemara* introduces the second exception: *shelichus yad*. This refers to the case of a *shomer* — a watchman — who uses another person's property without permission. If one is watching someone's object and instead uses it for himself (the classic case being a *shomer* who drives someone's car rather than merely guarding it), that unauthorized use makes the *shomer* liable for any damage that subsequently occurs to the item. Now, what if the *shomer* did not use it himself but sent someone else to use it? That too creates liability — making this another instance where *shaliach lidvar aveira* applies, despite it being a prohibited act.

It is notable that in both *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad*, the operative concern is not only about religious transgression but also about civil liability — and it is precisely in those categories where the *Torah* carves out exceptions to *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

### Two Exceptions Principle

Since these two cases — *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad* — each appear independently in the *Torah* to establish *shaliach lidvar aveira*, and there are thereby two scriptural sources for the principle, they function as exceptions and not as a general rule. The logic is: had *shaliach lidvar aveira* been the rule, the *Torah* would have needed only one source. The fact that it appears twice signals that these are exceptional cases, and the governing principle across the rest of the *Torah* remains *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

The *beraita* elaborates on the *shelichus yad* case through a debate between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel*. The *pasuk* says *al kol devar pesha* — on any matter of sin — and also *asher lo shalach yado bimleches reiehu* — that one did not cast his hand against his fellow's property. *Beis Shammai* holds that the word *devar* (which can mean speech or intention) implies that even expressing a desire to use the property — a *dibbur*, a thought or declaration of intent — is sufficient to create *shelichus yad* liability: *lechayav al hamachshavah kema'aseh*, thought is treated like action. *Beis Hillel* holds that one is not liable until *sheyishlach bo yad* — actual physical use — citing the language of the *pasuk* itself.

*Beis Shammai* challenges *Beis Hillel*: if literal *shlach yad* is required, what does *al kol devar pesha* add? *Beis Hillel* responds: *al kol devar pesha* addresses the case where you instructed your slave or your agent to use the property — *amar leavdo ushlucho* — so that even a *dibbur* in the form of directing another person creates liability. According to *Beis Hillel*, then, *al kol devar pesha* establishes the *shelichus yad* exception to *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — specifically the case of instructing an agent.

The *Gemara* then notes: this all works well for *Beis Hillel*, who has two exceptions — *me'ilah* and *shelichus yad* — giving two scriptural sources. But for *Beis Shammai*, who holds that *al kol devar pesha* refers to thought rather than agency, only *me'ilah* remains as a scriptural source for *shaliach lidvar aveira*. Perhaps, then, *me'ilah* should be the rule rather than the exception. The *Gemara* resolves this by introducing a third exception.

## Tevicha Umechira Case

### Stolen Livestock

The *Gemara* identifies a third category: *tevicha umechira*. If a *gannav* steals an ox or a sheep and then slaughters or sells it, he is liable to pay four or five times the value. The *Gemara* now establishes that this four- or fivefold liability applies even if the *gannav* had a *shaliach* perform the slaughter or the sale on his behalf. This is yet another case where *shaliach lidvar aveira* obtains despite the underlying act being forbidden.

### Deriving Shaliach

Two *tannaim* derive this inclusion of the *shaliach* from different *pesukim*. *Devei Rabbi Yishmael* derives it from the word *o* ("or") in the phrase *o utvacho o mechoro* — "or slaughter it or sell it." Just as selling inherently involves another party (the buyer), so too slaughtering can involve another party (a *shaliach*). *Devei Chizkiyah* derives it from the word *tachas* — the fourfold and fivefold payment is described as *tachas*, and this word is taken to include liability even when the *shaliach* performs the act.

### Exceptions Not Rules

With three exceptions now identified — *me'ilah*, *shelichus yad*, and *tevicha umechira* — the *Gemara* is satisfied that these are exceptions to the rule, not paradigm cases. For *Beis Hillel*, two exceptions (*me'ilah* and *shelichus yad*) suffice to establish them as non-normative by the principle of *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin*. For *Beis Shammai*, who requires more examples to classify sources as exceptions, the third case of *tevicha umechira* provides the necessary additional instance.

## General Rule Confirmed

### Shchutei Chutz Rule

The *Gemara* then asks: according to those who hold *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad melamdin* — that two scriptural instances do generate a general rule rather than marking exceptions — how do we know that *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* is the baseline? The answer lies in the case of *shchutei chutz*, slaughtering a *korban* outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. The *Torah* states *dam yeichashev la'ish hahu dam shafach* — the blood shall be reckoned to *that man*, the one who spilled it. The word *hahu* — "that man" — specifies that it is the doer himself who is liable, not one who instructed another to do it. This verse, cited only once, functions as a categorical statement of the principle: only the doer is liable.

### Double Hahu Analysis

The *Gemara* notes further that the *pasuk* actually uses *ha'ish hahu* twice — once in *dam yeichashev la'ish hahu* and once in *venichras ha'ish hahu* (that man is *kares*). The *Gemara* asks: why doesn't the *Gemara* instead learn from the exceptions (*tevicha umechira* and *me'ilah*) and treat those as the rule? The answer is that the doubled *hahu* is the decisive factor. Since the first *hahu* already establishes that only the doer of *shchutei chutz* is liable, the second *hahu* is superfluous to that specific ruling — *im einu inyan legufo, tanehu inyan lechol hatorah kullah* — and is therefore applied to the entire *Torah* as a general rule: only the doer himself is liable, and the cases of *me'ilah*, *shelichus yad*, and *tevicha umechira* are the exceptions.

### Alternative Drashim

For those who hold *shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad ein melamdin* — for whom the two or three exceptions already suffice to establish the rule — the two instances of *hahu* in the *shchutei chutz* passage must be put to other uses. The *Gemara* explains: one *hahu* teaches *lema'utei shnayim she'ochazin besakin veshochetin* — to exclude liability when two people hold the knife together and perform the act jointly, in which case neither is liable for *kares*. The other *hahu* teaches *hu velo anus, hu velo shogeg, hu velo mut'eh* — only one who acts willfully is liable for *kares*, not one who is coerced, acts inadvertently, or is mistaken. (In the case of *shogeg* one would still bring a *korban*, but there is no *kares*.) The *Gemara* notes that the other opinion does not independently derive these principles from the double *hahu* but arrives at them through other means.

## Shammai on Murder

### Murder Instruction

The *Gemara* cites a *beraita*: *ha'omer lishlucho tze veharog es hanefesh* — if one tells his *shaliach* to go and commit murder — *hu chayav umshalcho patur*: the murderer is liable and the one who sent him is exempt. This reflects the general principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira*.

### Shammai's Ruling

However, the *beraita* continues: in the name of *Chaggai HaNavi*, *sholcho chayav* — even the one who sent the *shaliach* is liable. The proof text is *oso haragta b'cherev b'nei Amon* — when the *Navi* holds *David* accountable for the death of *Uriyah HaChiti*, the language used is "you killed him by the sword of *b'nei Amon*." Yet *David* did not personally kill *Uriyah*; he sent him to the front. Nevertheless, *David* is treated as the one who killed him. *Shammai HaZaken* therefore rules that the *meshaleach* is *chayav* even in a case of murder.

### Shammai's Logic

What is the reasoning of *Shammai HaZaken*? *Kasavar shnei kesuvim habaim ke'echad melamdim* — he holds that when two scriptural instances establish a principle, that principle is extended as a general rule rather than treated as an exception. For *Shammai HaZaken*, who aligns with the position of *Beis Shammai*, there are only two exceptions: *me'ilah* and *tevicha umechira* — and those two do function as a general rule, establishing *yesh shaliach lidvar aveira* across the *Torah*. He does not derive the limiting principle from the double *hahu* of *shchutei chutz*, and therefore holds that the *meshaleach* can bear liability.

### B'Dinei Shamayim

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative interpretation of *Shammai HaZaken*'s position. Perhaps *Shammai HaZaken* does in fact agree that there is no *shaliach lidvar aveira* in human courts — and when he says *sholcho chayav*, he means *chayav b'dinei shamayim*: liable before Heaven, treated as a murderer in the eyes of God, just as the *Navi* addresses *David* as one who killed *Uriyah*. On this reading, the dispute is not about whether the principle of *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* applies in *dinei adam*, but rather about the degree of heavenly accountability. The *tanna kamma* who says the *meshaleach* is *patur* would then be understood to be referring to human court liability only — surely everyone would agree that one who arranges a murder bears some degree of heavenly responsibility. The debate is therefore one of *dinei rabbah u'dinei zuta*: whether the *meshaleach* is treated in Heaven as an outright murderer or merely as one who incited murder.

### Murder Exception

The *Gemara* offers yet a further interpretation: *shani hasam d'gali rachmana oso haragta b'cherev b'nei Amon* — perhaps the *pasuk* concerning *David* and *Uriyah* teaches a specific *halacha* unique to murder. One might have thought that of all prohibitions, murder would be the one most governed by *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* — since *divrei harav vedivrei hatalmid* is at its most compelling when the act is taking a human life. Yet precisely because of the immense gravity of murder, the *Torah* singles it out to hold the *meshaleach* liable as well, in addition to the one who performed the act.

Those who disagree with *Shammai HaZaken* read the *pasuk* differently: *harei l'cha k'cherev b'nei Amon* — the comparison to the sword of *b'nei Amon* is meant to say the opposite. Just as one is not held liable when *b'nei Amon* kill people in the ordinary course of war, so too *David* should not be held liable for the death of *Uriyah*. The *pasuk* that *Shammai* uses to establish liability is turned on its head by his opponents to establish exemption. Moreover, those who hold *David* exempt argue that there was justification for *Uriyah*'s death: *mored b'malchus havah* — he was in a state of rebellion against the king. The evidence is *Uriyah*'s own words, in which he referred to *Yoav* as his master in the presence of *David*, thereby according authority to someone other than the king, or alternatively, by defying *David*'s explicit order to return home to his wife.

## Raba's Clarification

### Sexual & Food Cases

*Rava* adds an important clarification: even if one follows *Shammai HaZaken* and holds *yesh shaliach lidvar aveira* as a general principle, there remains a category of cases where *ein shaliach lidvar aveira* applies even according to *Shammai HaZaken*. That category is: if one instructs a *shaliach* to engage in sexual intercourse with a forbidden woman, or to eat *treif* food — *she'hu chayav v'sholcho patur* — the doer is liable and the sender is entirely exempt.

### Pleasure Theory

The reason is stated as a general principle: *she'lo matzinu b'chol haTorah kula ze neheneh v'ze mitchayev* — we have never found anywhere in the entire *Torah* that one person receives the *hana'ah* (the pleasure or benefit) while another person bears the liability. In these categories of sin, the transgression is not constituted merely by the physical act but by the *hana'ah* experienced in performing that act. An act can be attributed to a principal; pleasure cannot. The experience of *hana'ah* is irreducibly subjective and personal — it belongs only to the one who undergoes it, and there is no mechanism by which it can be transferred or attributed to another. This connects as well to the principle of *mita'sek b'chalavim v'arayos*: one cannot divorce the act from oneself when it is the *hana'ah* inherent in the doing that constitutes the prohibition.

## Shaliach as Witness

### Initial Debate

The *Gemara* now turns to an additional question within the laws of *shelichus*: *shaliach na'aseh eid* — can a *shaliach* serve as a witness to the very mission he was sent to perform? If, for example, two people are sent as *shluchim* to betroth a woman or to repay a debt, can those same individuals serve as *eidim* to attest that the act occurred? Or is the *shaliach* somehow considered equivalent to the principal — and just as the principal himself cannot be a witness to his own act, neither can the *shaliach*?

*Rav* holds *shaliach na'aseh eid*: a *shaliach* can indeed serve as a witness. *Beis Rabbi Shila* holds *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*: he cannot. The reasoning of *Beis Rabbi Shila* is grounded in the principle *shlucho shel adam kemoso* — the *shaliach* is considered equivalent to the principal himself. Just as a party to a transaction cannot serve as a witness to it, so too the *shaliach*, who stands in the principal's stead, cannot serve as an independent witness. He becomes, in legal terms, *k'gufei* — like the principal's own self.

One might initially suggest that the problem is simply that the *meshaleach* never explicitly designated the *shaliach* as a witness, only as an agent. But this cannot be the issue: if someone betroths a woman in the presence of two witnesses without explicitly saying "you are my witnesses," the *kiddushin* is nonetheless valid. Witnesses do not need formal designation when they are observing the act itself — the only context requiring explicit designation is an admission of debt, where otherwise one might claim he was speaking in jest. So the absence of formal appointment as *eid* cannot be the objection.

### Baraita Challenge

The *Gemara* challenges *Rav*'s position from a *beraita*: *amar l'shlosha tze'u v'kadshu li isha* — if one says to three people "go and betroth this woman for me," *Beis Shammai* holds that one of them serves as the *shaliach* and the other two as independent *eidim* — you are one or the other, not both. *Beis Hillel* holds *kulam shluchim hen* — all three are considered *shluchim*, and since they are all *shluchim*, *v'ein shaliach na'aseh eid* — none of them can simultaneously serve as *eidim*. The result under *Beis Hillel* is that you need to find other witnesses entirely. Both schools of thought, with *Beis Hillel* being the stricter, appear to hold *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*. How can *Rav* maintain the contrary?

### Position Reversal

The *Gemara* answers that *Rav* rules like a different *tanna*: a *beraita* in the name of *Rabbi Natan* presents *Beis Shammai* as holding *shaliach v'ed echad* — a *shaliach* plus one additional witness suffice (meaning the *shaliach* himself can serve as one of the two required *eidim*), while *Beis Hillel* holds *shaliach u'shnei eidim* — the *shaliach* cannot be counted, and two additional witnesses are required. The *Gemara* then notes: but would *Rav* rule like *Beis Shammai*? The resolution is *ipech* — reverse the positions in that *beraita*: it is *Beis Hillel* who holds *shaliach na'aseh eid*, and *Beis Shammai* who holds *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*. On this reversal, *Rav* is in agreement with *Beis Hillel*.

### Final Halacha

*Rav Acha bar Rava* teaches the debate between *Rav* and *Beis Rabbi Shila* in the reverse of the original formulation: *Rav* holds *ein shaliach na'aseh eid*, while *Beis Rabbi Shila* holds *shaliach na'aseh eid*. Regardless of which formulation one follows, the *Gemara* concludes: *v'hilcheta shaliach na'aseh eid* — the *halacha* is that a *shaliach* can serve as a witness, though the theoretical grounding for this ruling requires considerable effort given the weight of the opposing sources.

## Shaliach Eid Applications

### Two Shluchim Rule

*Rava* in the name of *Rav Nachman* now states the operative *halacha* flowing from this principle: *amar l'shnayim tze'u v'kadshu li isha, hen shluchav v'hen eidav* — if one says to two people "go betroth this woman for me," they are his *shluchim* and they can also serve as his *eidim* to the act of *kiddushin*.

### Gerushin Concern

The same rule applies *b'gerushin* — in the case of divorce. If two people are sent to deliver a *get*, they can serve as witnesses to its delivery, with one caveat: there is a concern that one of them might have set his eye on the woman — that is, he may want to marry her himself — and this personal interest could compromise his reliability as a witness. Nevertheless, the concern is addressed, as will be explained.

### Mamonot Concern

The same rule applies *b'dinei mamonot* — if two people are sent to repay a debt, they too can serve as witnesses to the repayment. Here the concern is different: perhaps they will pocket the money and then falsely testify that they paid the creditor. Again, however, the concern is addressed.

### Why Concerns Addressed

The *Gemara* explains why all three cases remain valid despite these concerns, and why it was necessary to state all three. Had the ruling been stated only for *kiddushin*, one might have thought it applies specifically because the woman's becoming betrothed makes her forbidden to the *shaliach* himself — *l'issurei kasi* — thereby eliminating any personal interest he might have, and thus any concern of *nogea b'davar*. One could not have inferred from *kiddushin* that the same applies to *gerushin*, since in *gerushin* the *shaliach* may wish to marry the woman after her divorce and thus has a stake in the outcome. However, in *gerushin* there are two *shluchim* — and since neither can marry both parties, nor can both equally benefit, the concern is neutralized: *d'ita l'bei trai lo chazi* — she cannot go to both of them, so you cannot say both are equally *nogea b'davar*. And the case of *dinei mamonot* cannot be inferred from *gerushin*, since with money there is a more tangible concern that both witnesses will conspire to keep the funds for themselves. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* concludes *tzrichi* — all three must be stated, and in all three the *shluchim* are valid *eidim*.

The *Gemara* then deepens the analysis of the monetary case, asking: is there in fact a concern of *nogea b'eidus* — a disqualifying personal interest in the outcome? The issue turns on a separate *halachic* principle: *hamalveh et chaveiro b'eidim tzarich l'faro b'eidim* — if one lends money in the presence of witnesses, must he repay it in the presence of witnesses? If the answer is yes, then the *shluchim*, if they fail to deliver the money to the creditor, would have to repay it to the *meshaleach* — and they would not be believed to say they had already returned it. This means that by testifying that they paid the creditor, they are freeing themselves from liability to the *meshaleach*, making them *nogea b'eidus*. The *Gemara* resolves: *le'olam kasavar ein tzarich l'faro b'eidim* — the *halacha* is that one need not repay a debt-by-witnesses in the presence of witnesses. Therefore, if the *shluchim* claimed they had returned the money to the *meshaleach*, they would simply be believed. Since they could equally have said "we returned it to you," they gain nothing by testifying that they paid the creditor instead. They are thus not *nogea b'eidus*, and their testimony stands.

## Shevuas Hesess Impact

### Pre-Hesess Rule

Before the rabbinic enactment of *shevuas hesess*, the analysis above held cleanly. The *shluchim* could serve as *eidim* in monetary matters because they had nothing to gain from their testimony: *malveh et chaveiro b'eidim ein tzarich l'faro b'eidim* — and they were equally believed whether they said they paid the creditor or returned the funds to the *meshaleach*. There was therefore no interest on their part, no disqualifying *nogea b'eidus*, and their testimony was valid.

### Post-Hesess Problem

After *shevuas hesess* was introduced by the *Rabbanan* — under which virtually any monetary claim carries with it a requirement that the defendant take an oath — the calculus changed. Now, if the *shluchim* were to admit that they had not paid the creditor, the *meshaleach* could demand repayment, and they would be required to take a *shevuah* to establish that they had returned the funds to him. By testifying instead that they paid the creditor, they free themselves from that *shevuah* obligation. They thus have a tangible gain from their testimony — they are now *nogea b'eidus*, disqualified as witnesses.

### New Outcome

The practical result under the post-*hesess* regime is as follows: since the *shluchim* can no longer serve as *eidim*, their testimony is not accepted. Instead, the *shluchim* take an oath that they did in fact pay the creditor. The creditor in turn takes an oath that he never received payment. The consequence is that the *loveh* — the original debtor who sent the *shluchim* — must pay the *malveh* again. The irony is considerable: it is specifically their *shevuah* obligation to the *loveh* that disqualifies the *shluchim* from serving as witnesses — and it is precisely that disqualification that leaves the *loveh* without recourse and compelled to pay twice. That is the unfortunate outcome when *shevuas hesess* intersects with the institution of *shaliach na'aseh eid*.
