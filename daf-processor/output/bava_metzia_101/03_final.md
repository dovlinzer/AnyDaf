# Bava Metzia 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Olive Trees for Wood

### Setup: Trees for Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר זֵיתָיו לְעֵצִים, וְעָשׂוּ פָּחוֹת מֵרְבִיעִית לַסְּאָה – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַזֵּיתִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who sells his olive trees** to another so he can chop them down and use them **for** their **wood, and** before he chopped them down **they yielded** olives, if the olives are of a quality that could provide the value of **less than a quarter**-*log* of oil **per *se'a*** of olives, **these** olives are the property **of** the new **owner of the olive trees,** i.e., the buyer.

The opening *sugya* of Daf 101 concerns a case involving the sale of olive trees designated for firewood. The *mishnah* states: *ha-mokher zeitav la-etzim ve-asu pachot me-revi'it la-se'ah harei elu shel ba'al ha-zeitim* — one who sells his olive trees to be used as wood, and the trees nonetheless produce olives yielding less than a *revi'it* (a quarter-log) of oil per *se'ah* of field, the oil belongs to the seller — that is, the original *ba'al ha-zeitim*, the owner of the trees.

The logic here is straightforward: someone who sells olive trees for lumber presumably does not place great value on whatever minimal fruit they still happen to bear. If the trees produce only a negligible quantity of oil — less than a *revi'it* per *se'ah* — the seller is presumed to have waived any interest in it, and the buyer acquires it along with the trees.

### Two Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשׂוּ רְבִיעִית לַסְּאָה, זֶה אוֹמֵר: "זֵיתַי גִּדְּלוּ", וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: "אַרְצִי גִּדְּלָה" – יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **they yielded** olives that could provide the value of **a quarter**-*log* or more of oil **per *se'a*** of olives, and **this** one, the buyer, **says: My olive trees yielded** the olives and so I have a right to them, **and that** one, the seller, **says:** The nourishment from **my land yielded** the olives and so I have a right to them, then **they divide** the olives between them.

The *mishnah* continues: *ha-zeitim asu revi'it la-se'ah* — but if the trees do produce the threshold amount of a *revi'it* per *se'ah* of oil, then we have competing claims. The buyer of the trees argues, *zeitai gadlu* — "my trees produced this oil; it belongs to me." The landowner counters, *artzi gadla* — "it is my land, with its soil and minerals, that actually developed this oil; it is mine." Since both parties advance legitimate claims, the *gemara* rules *yahalloku* — they divide the oil between them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁטַף נָהָר זֵיתָיו וּנְתָנָם לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ, זֶה אוֹמֵר: "זֵיתַי גִּדְּלוּ", וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: "אַרְצִי גִּדְּלָה" – יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the event that **a river swept away one's olive trees and deposited them in** the **field of another,** and they took root there and yielded olives, **this** one, i.e., the owner of the trees, **says: My olive trees yielded** the olives and so I have a right to them, **and that** one, i.e., the owner of the field, **says:** The nourishment from **my land yielded** the olives and so I have a right to them, then **they divide** the olives between them.

The same framework applies to the second case in the *mishnah*: *shataf nahar zeitav u-netanam le-tokh sdeh chaveiro* — a flood uproots someone's olive trees and deposits them in a neighbor's field. Here too, the original tree owner argues *zeitai gadlu*, and the field owner argues *artzi gadla*, and so again *yahalloku*.

### Deal Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ קוֹץ לְאַלְתַּר – אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מֵרְבִיעִית נָמֵי לְבַעַל הַקַּרְקַע. אִי דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ כֹּל אֵימַת דְּבָעֵית קוֹץ – אֲפִילּוּ רְבִיעִית נָמֵי לְבַעַל זֵיתִים!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the sale? **If** this is a case **where** before the sale the seller **said to** the buyer: **Cut down** the trees **immediately,** then clearly he is particular that the buyer not derive benefit from the nourishment provided by his land. Therefore, **even** if the olives yielded could provide **less than** the value of **a quarter**-*log* of oil per *se'a* of olives, they will belong **to the owner of the land,** i.e., the seller. Conversely, **if** the case is **where he said to him: Cut down** the trees **whenever you want,** then it is clear that he is not particular about the buyer deriving benefit from the nourishment provided by his land. Therefore, **even** if the olives yielded could provide the value of **a quarter**-*log* or more of oil per *se'a* of olives, they will belong **to the owner of the olive trees,** i.e., the buyer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ סְתָמָא: פָּחוֹת מֵרְבִיעִית – לָא קָפְדִי אִינָשֵׁי, רְבִיעִית – קָפְדִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** the ruling is **necessary** in a case **where** the seller **said to him** to cut down the trees, **without specification** about when he should do so. Accordingly, if the olives yielded produce **less than** the value of **a quarter**-*log* of oil per *se'a* of olives, then since **people are** generally **not particular** to receive their share of such olives, the buyer may keep them. But where the olives yielded produce the value of **a quarter**-*log* or more of oil per *se'a* of olives, **people are** generally **particular** to receive their share of such olives; consequently, they divide the olives between them.

This raises the question of how exactly these distinctions operate. When one buys a tree planted in someone else's land, the transaction is inherently ambiguous: the buyer does not acquire the underlying land — trees are *mechubar la-karka* and in some respects resemble land itself — yet neither does the buyer acquire all rights that would attend a purchase of the land. A tree planted in another's field creates a complex of partial rights, which is precisely what this *sugya* is working through.

The *gemara* therefore concludes that the *mishnah* is necessary to teach the intermediate case: *lo tzricha de-amar lei stama* — where the sale was made without specifying when the trees are to be cut. In this unstated case, the rule of *yahalloku* applies, conditioned on the amount of oil produced. *Pachot mirvi lo kapdei inshi* — below a *revi'it*, people do not typically care, so the buyer takes all. *Revi'it kapdei inshi* — at the threshold amount, people do care, and so the parties split.

### Amount vs. Ratio

The *sugya* thus draws an important distinction between a fixed minimum quantity and a ratio. The operative trigger for the *yahalloku* rule is a specific threshold — the *revi'it* per *se'ah* — not a proportional share. Below that minimum, the original seller's claim is simply presumed waived. At or above it, both parties have standing claims that require adjudication through division.

### Revi'it Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: וּרְבִיעִית שֶׁאָמְרוּ – חוּץ מִן הַהוֹצָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi says: And** the value of the **quarter**-*log* **that they mentioned** in the mishna **is exclusive of the expense** of processing the olives to produce oil.

Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi adds a technical clarification: *v'revi'it she'amru chutz min ha-hotza'ah* — the *revi'it* threshold specified by the *mishnah* is measured net of harvesting costs. The relevant question is not the gross quantity of oil produced, but whether the oil, after accounting for the expense of picking and pressing, yields a net *revi'it*. Anything less, even in gross terms, falls below the threshold of what people typically regard as worth contesting.

## Flood & Orlah

### Reish Lakish Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁטַף נָהָר זֵיתָיו. אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרוּ בְּגוּשֵׁיהֶן, וּלְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In the event that **a river swept away one's olive trees** and deposited them in the field of another, and they took root there and yielded olives, then the olives are divided between the owner of the trees and the owner of the field. **Ulla says** that **Reish Lakish says: They taught** this **only** about a case **where** the olive trees **were uprooted** and relocated together **with their clods** of earth in which they grew; **and** the ruling is with regard to olives that grew **after three** years since the trees took root in the new field. After three years there is no longer any prohibition of *orla* (see Leviticus 19:23) on the olives even if one presumes that the olives were nourished from the field owner's land.

The *mishnah*'s case of the flood — *shataf nahar zeitav u-netanam le-tokh sdeh chaveiro* — is now taken up in the *gemara*. Ulla cites Reish Lakish's position: *lo shanu ela she-ne'ekar be-gusham u-le'achar shalosh* — the *mishnah*'s rule of *yahalloku* applies only when the trees were uprooted together with a clump of soil (*ne'ekar be-gusho*) and only after three years have elapsed since the flood deposited them in the new field. *Aval be-tokh shalosh* — within the first three years — everything belongs to the *ba'al ha-zeitim*, the tree owner.

### Orlah Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ – הַכֹּל שֶׁל בַּעַל הַזֵּיתִים. דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אַתְּ נְטַעְתְּ בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ, מִי הֲוָה אָכְלַתְּ?
>
> **Translation:** **But during** the first **three** years after the trees took root in the new field, **everything** that grows **is** the property **of** the **owner of the olive trees, as he can say to** the owner of the field: Even **if you had planted** the trees yourself at the time that they took root in your field, **would you have eaten** the olives **during** the first **three** years? If you claim that the nourishment from your land yielded the olives, the olives will be prohibited as *orla*. The only reason for them to be permitted is if it is assumed that they were nourished only from the clods of earth in which they were initially planted over three years ago, in which case they should belong entirely to me, as the owner of both the trees and the clods.

The significance of the three-year period, as Rashi explains, is rooted in the laws of *orlah*. When a tree is newly planted, its fruit is forbidden for the first three years. A tree uprooted and replanted does not ordinarily retain its prior years of growth for *orlah* purposes — unless, critically, it was uprooted together with a substantial clump of soil (*gush*). When the tree is *ne'ekar be-gusho*, it is treated as if it were never fully replanted, and the *orlah* count is not reset. This is a striking and practically significant point: a mature olive tree displaced by a flood, arriving intact with its root-soil, imposes no *orlah* restriction on its fruit — it may be used immediately.

This generates the *yahalloku* dispute between the *ba'al ha-zeitim* and the *ba'al ha-karka*. The tree owner can argue: had you, the field owner, planted your own tree here, its fruit would have been forbidden under *orlah* for the first three years. You are benefiting from my tree precisely because it is free of *orlah*. Within those three years, therefore, all the fruit should be mine. That is Reish Lakish's original position: *be-tokh shalosh hakol le-ba'al ha-zeitim*.

### Field Owner Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: אִי אֲנָא נְטַעִי, לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ הֲוָה אָכֵילְנָא לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ. הַשְׁתָּא קָאָכְלַתְּ פַּלְגָא בַּהֲדַאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the owner of the field **say to him: If I had** uprooted your trees from my field and instead **planted** my own trees, then **after three** years **I could have consumed all of** the olives yielded by them. **Now that** I let your trees remain and so **you** have a right to **consume half with me,** in return I should be entitled to half the yield during the first three years as well.

The *gemara* raises a counterargument available to the field owner: *amar lei, ee ana nataie ve-achar shalosh shanim hava achilna leih le-chuleih* — had I planted my own tree here, after three years all the fruit would have been entirely mine. Under Reish Lakish's position, we split after three years. Why should your tree — which you did not plant here — end up giving you a share of my field's produce in perpetuity after three years? The field owner's contribution — his soil, his land — should ultimately prevail. *Hashta k'ha paga behadai* — you should be sharing with me right now, during the first three years when the *orlah* exemption is specifically benefiting you.

### Three-Year Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרוּ בְּגוּשֵׁיהֶן וּבְתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ, אֲבָל לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ הַכֹּל לְבַעַל הַקַּרְקַע. דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אֲנָא נְטַעִי לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ, מִי לָא הֲוָה אָכֵילְנָא לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another interpretation: **Rather, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said** in the name of **Reish Lakish: They taught** this **only** about a case **where** the olive trees **were uprooted** and relocated together **with their clods** of earth in which they grew, **and** the ruling is with regard to olives that grew **during** the first **three** years since the trees took root in the new field. **But after** the first **three** years, **everything** that grows is the property **of** the **owner of the land, as he can say to** the owner of the trees: **If I had** uprooted your trees from my field and instead **planted** my own trees, then **after three** years, **could I not have consumed all of** the olives produced by them?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: אִי אַתְּ נְטַעְתְּ – בְּתוֹךְ שָׁלֹשׁ לָא הֲוָה אָכְלַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּא קָא אָכְלַתְּ פַּלְגָא בַּהֲדַאי? מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אֲנָא נְטַעִי – הֲוָה קַטִּינֵי וּזְרַעְנָא תְּחוֹתַיְיהוּ סִילְקָא וְיַרְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the owner of the trees **say to him:** Even **if you had planted** trees yourself at the time my trees took root in your field, then **during** the first **three** years **you would not have consumed** their fruit at all. How, then, can you come **now** and claim you are entitled to **consume half** of the olives **together with me** during those first three years? The Gemara answers: He has a right to demand half of the olives **due to** the fact **that** the owner of the field **can say to** the owner of the trees: **If I had** uprooted your trees from my field and instead **planted** my own trees, **they would be slender** plants that do not cast heavy shadows, **and** I would have **planted chard [*silka*] and vegetables underneath** the trees and made a significant profit. Since, instead, I let your trees remain, in return I should be entitled to half of the fruit during the first three years.

When Ravin returned from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted a revised and more defensible version of Reish Lakish's position: *lo shanu ela she-ne'ekru be-gusheihen u-ve-tokh shalosh* — the *yahalloku* rule of the *mishnah* applies only within the first three years, when the *orlah* analysis is operative. *Aval le-achar shalosh hakol le-ba'al ha-karka* — after three years, everything belongs to the field owner. The field owner now argues: *de-amar lei, ee ana nataie, le-achar shalosh mi lo hava achilna leih le-chuleih?* — had I planted my own tree here, after three years all of it would have been mine. Once the *orlah* period has passed, the field owner's foundational claim reasserts itself fully.

The *gemara* then explains why the *mishnah*'s *yahalloku* applies during the first three years: *mishum de-amar lei, ee ana nataie, hava katini ve-zar'ana techoteihu silka ve-yarka* — had I, the field owner, planted my own tree here within the first three years, those saplings would have been small enough that I could have planted beets and vegetables beneath them. Your mature tree, however, provides considerably more shade, preventing me from utilizing the ground beneath it as I otherwise would have. This asymmetry gives the tree owner a counterclaim even within the first three years, justifying the split.

### Tree Retrieval

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אָמַר הַלָּה "זֵיתַיי אֲנִי נוֹטֵל" – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִשּׁוּם יִשּׁוּב אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּגוֹן דָּא צְרִיכָא רַבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: If **the other one,** the owner of the trees, **said: I am** uprooting and **taking my olive trees** back, the court **does not listen to him. What is the reason? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Due to** the desire to promote **the settling of Eretz Yisrael,** it is inappropriate to uproot trees. Upon hearing this ruling, **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** For rulings **such as this,** it is **necessary** to have **a great** Sage explain it, as that reason is not self-evident.

A related question is what happens if the original tree owner decides he wants his trees back — *amar hala zeitai ani natel*, "I will take back my trees." The *gemara* rules: *ein shom'in lo* — we do not accede to this request. The tree owner cannot simply reclaim his trees from the field where the flood deposited them.

### Yishuv Eretz Yisrael

Rabbi Yochanan explains the rationale: *mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael* — because we have a communal interest in the settlement and cultivation of the Land of Israel. The presence of a productive olive tree in any field in Eretz Yisrael is a positive development, and we therefore do not permit its removal. Rabbi Yirmiyah praises this reasoning as precisely the sort of principle that requires transmission from teacher to student — without a master, one might not have recognized it. Rashi notes that at the top of 101b an alternative explanation is offered for *ein shom'in lo* — that uprooting the tree would damage the field — but here the operative rationale is the imperative of *yishuv Eretz Yisrael*.

## Sharecropping Tithing

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה אֲבוֹתָיו מִן הַנׇּכְרִי – מְעַשֵּׂר וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Demai* 6:2): **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **one who receives a field of his ancestors from a gentile** under a sharecropping agreement, i.e., he is granted the right to cultivate the land and keep its produce in return for giving a portion of the produce to the gentile owner, must first **tithe** the produce grown in that field **and** only then **give** the gentile his portion from among the tithed produce.

The *gemara* now introduces a *mishnah* from tractate *Demai*, cited here in partial form. Rabbi Yehuda says: *ha-mikabbel sde avotav min ha-nochri me'asser ve-noten lo* — one who works his ancestral field (*sde avotav*) as a *mikabbel* (a sharecropper paying a fixed ratio of produce) from a non-Jew must tithe the produce before handing over the non-Jew's portion.

Some clarification of terminology is in order. A *choker* is a tenant farmer who owes a fixed *amount* of produce regardless of what is grown — say, forty bushels per year. A *mikabbel* owes a fixed *ratio* — say, twenty percent of whatever is produced. Rabbi Yehuda in the original *mishnah* adds *af ha-mikabbel* — even the *mikabbel*, not only the *choker*, is subject to this tithing requirement. The version cited here by the *gemara* simply states Rabbi Yehuda's position regarding the *mikabbel*, as is the way of the *gemara* when it excerpts only the portion of a *mishnah* relevant to the current discussion.

Why would we have thought otherwise? The question is whether the sharecropper may pay the non-Jew his share of the produce in *tevel* — untithed produce. The *mishnah* establishes that he may not: he must first tithe the entire harvest and only then convey the non-Jew's portion. This means he loses somewhat in the calculation — tithing reduces the net amount from which his own portion is derived — but the obligation stands.

One might ask: why does the non-Jew's portion of the produce require tithing at all? And why does a non-Jew own land in Eretz Yisrael to begin with? These are precisely the questions the *gemara* proceeds to address.

### Sde Avotav Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ מַאי "שְׂדֵה אֲבוֹתָיו" – אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לַהּ "שְׂדֵה אֲבוֹתָיו" – שְׂדֵה אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב. וְקָסָבַר: אֵין קִנְיָן לְנׇכְרִי בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַפְקִיעַ מִיַּד מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** Initially, when the Sages studied this mishna, **they assumed** the following interpretation: **What** is the meaning of: **A field of his ancestors?** It is a reference to any field in **Eretz Yisrael. And why did they call it: A field of his ancestors?** Because it is **a field of** his forefathers, **Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.** According to this interpretation, the mishna is referring to a field which was legitimately acquired by the gentile and to which the Jew has no claim. The Gemara explains why the Jewish sharecropper is required to tithe the produce he will give the landowner: **And** the reason the sharecropper tithes that produce is that the *tanna* **holds** that **a gentile's acquisition** of land **in Eretz Yisrael does not abrogate** its sanctity **with regard to** separating **tithes** from its produce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְקַבֵּל – כְּחוֹכֵר דָּמֵי. מָה חוֹכֵר, בֵּין עֲבַד וּבֵין לָא עֲבַד, בָּעֵי עַשּׂוֹרֵי וּמִיתַּן לֵיהּ, דְּכִי פּוֹרֵעַ חוֹבָתוֹ דָּמֵי. אַף מְקַבֵּל נָמֵי כִּי פּוֹרֵעַ חוֹבָתוֹ דָּמֵי, מְעַשֵּׂר וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Yehuda also holds that the obligation of one who **receives** a field of his ancestors, i.e., a sharecropper, to pay the landowner **is like** that of **a tenant farmer,** i.e., **just as a tenant farmer, whether** the field **produces** a crop **or whether it does not produce** a crop, **is required to procure produce from somewhere, tithe** it, **and** then **give** it **to** the landowner, **as he is like one paying his debt, so too,** one who **receives,** i.e., a sharecropper, is **also like one paying his debt,** and he must consequently first **tithe** the produce **and** then **give** it **to** the landowner. Since his obligation to the landowner is regarded as a debt, apparently before the produce is given to the landowner, it belongs to the tenant farmer or sharecropper, who is therefore required to tithe it.

The *gemara* initially considers the possibility that *sde avotav* in Rabbi Yehuda's statement simply means any land in Eretz Yisrael — "our forefathers' field," in the sense that Eretz Yisrael is the ancestral inheritance of the Jewish people from Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov. On this reading, *v'kasavar ein kinyan le-nochri be-Eretz Yisrael le-hafki'ah mi-yad ma'aser* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that a non-Jew cannot truly acquire land in Eretz Yisrael in a way that would exempt its produce from *ma'aser*. The land retains its sanctity and its tithing obligations regardless of who nominally holds it. Furthermore, on this reading, the *mikabbel* is treated like a *choker* in that his payment to the non-Jew is considered akin to paying a debt — *ki forei hovato dami* — and he therefore must pay with tithed produce, just as any valid monetary obligation must be discharged with properly separated produce.

### Stolen Ancestral Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא לְרַב פַּפִּי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְרַב זְבִיד: אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה אֲבוֹתָיו מִמֵּצִיק נׇכְרִי מְעַשֵּׂר וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ, מַאי אִירְיָא מֵצִיק? אֲפִילּוּ אֵין מֵצִיק – נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said to Rav Pappi, and some say** that he said it **to Rav Zevid: But** this explanation is challenged by **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **one who receives a field of his ancestors** under a sharecropping agreement, **from a gentile oppressor** who seized it, first **tithes** produce **and** then **gives** the gentile his portion. According to the Sages' initial understanding, **why** does the *baraita* **specifically** refer to **an oppressor** who stole the land? **Even** if the gentile was **not an oppressor,** the ruling of the *baraita* would **also** apply.

Rav Kahana raises a difficulty with this reading by citing a *baraita*: *Rabbi Yehuda omer, ha-mikabbel sde avotav mi-mazzik nochri me'asser ve-noten lo* — a *baraita* specifies *mi-mazzik nochri*, from a non-Jew who wrongfully seized this land. If *sde avotav* simply meant any land in Eretz Yisrael, why would the *baraita* specify a thief (*mazzik*)? The specification is unnecessary if the rule applies to any non-Jew who holds land in Eretz Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: לְעוֹלָם יֵשׁ קִנְיָן לְנׇכְרִי בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַפְקִיעַ מִיַּד מַעֲשֵׂר, וּמְקַבֵּל לָאו כְּחוֹכֵר דָּמֵי. וּמַאי "שְׂדֵה אֲבוֹתָיו" – שְׂדֵה אֲבוֹתָיו מַמָּשׁ. וּלְדִידֵיהּ הוּא דְּקַנְסוּהּ רַבָּנַן, דְּאַיְּידֵי דַּחֲבִיבָא עֲלֵיהּ טָפֵי וְאָזֵיל מְקַבֵּל לַהּ. אֲבָל אִינִישׁ דְּעָלְמָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *tanna* of the mishna holds that **a gentile's acquisition** of land **in Eretz Yisrael abrogates** its sanctity **with regard to** separating **tithes** from its produce, and he also holds that the obligation of one who **receives,** i.e., a sharecropper, to pay the landowner **is not like** that of **a tenant farmer.** Since under the sharecropping arrangement the landowner retains a percentage stake in the yield, when the sharecropper provides the landowner with a portion of the produce, he is not considered to be paying a debt to him, but simply to be providing the landowner with the latter's own produce. Therefore, fundamentally the sharecropper should not have to tithe the produce before giving it to the landowner. The requirement to do so is a fine the Sages imposed upon him, as the Gemara will explain. **And,** according to this explanation, **what** is the meaning of: **A field of his ancestors?** It is referring to **a field** that belonged to **his actual ancestors,** of whom he is their heir, and it was then seized by a gentile. **And it is** only **him whom the Sages penalized** and required to take tithes from produce before giving it to the gentile oppressor, **as, since** the land **is dear to him, he will more readily go and receive it** in a sharecropping arrangement from the gentile, despite the Sages' requirement. **But** with regard to another **person of the world** at large, the Sages did **not** penalize him, as no one else would be willing to receive the land under such disadvantageous terms.

Rav Kahana therefore proposes an alternative reading of the entire framework: in fact, *yesh kinyan le-nochri be-Eretz Yisrael le-hafki'ah mi-yad ma'aser* — a non-Jew *can* acquire land in Eretz Yisrael in a way that removes the tithing obligation from its produce. Furthermore, a *mikabbel* is *not* analogous to a *choker* — the distinction between a fixed ratio and a fixed amount is legally meaningful. Consequently, there would ordinarily be no obligation to tithe produce grown on a non-Jew's land and paid as rent to a non-Jew.

The exception is the specific case of *sde avotav mamash* — literally his own ancestral land, land that a non-Jew wrongfully seized from this person's own father or grandfather, and which the sharecropper is now effectively "renting back" from the usurper. In this case only, the Rabbis imposed a *d'rabbanan* obligation to tithe — *l'didi hu de-kansu Rabbanan* — as a special enactment applying to the person whose own ancestral land is involved.

### Redemption Incentive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְדִידֵיהּ מַאי טַעְמָא קַנְסוּהּ רַבָּנַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא בָּרָה בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This explains why the Sages limited the fine to a person who has an ancestral claim to the field, **but what is the reason** that **the Sages penalized him? Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It was **in order** to encourage him to purchase the field, so **that it will be clearly in his possession.** The disadvantageous conditions will make it preferable for him to purchase the land outright from the gentile instead of entering into a sharecropping arrangement with him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: כְּגוֹן דָּא צְרִיכָא רַבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this ruling, **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** For rulings **such as this,** it is **necessary** to have **a great** Sage to explain it.

This raises an obvious objection: why should the sharecropper be penalized — burdened with a tithing obligation — when someone else stole the land? Rabbi Yochanan explains: *k'dei she-tehei barah be-yado* — the Rabbis wanted to encourage the original owner to go and redeem his ancestral property. The tithing enactment is not truly a penalty; it is a mechanism to keep the original owner engaged with his land, to make its redemption feel urgent and personally meaningful. Someone renting another person's ancestral field, by contrast, would not be subject to this enactment, because such a person has no personal stake in recovering the land — and since no one else would actually comply with this rule in practice, it would serve no purpose to impose it on them.

Rabbi Yirmiyah again applies his characteristic observation: *kegon da tzaricha rabba* — for an explanation of this kind, one needs a master. The underlying logic — that the Rabbis deliberately made observance more demanding in order to incentivize redemption of ancestral property — is precisely the sort of insight one would not arrive at independently.

## Trespass Planting

### Tree Planting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַיּוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ וּנְטָעָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, אָמַר רַב: שָׁמִין לוֹ, וְיָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן בְּשָׂדֶה זוֹ לְנוֹטְעָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § An amoraic dispute **was stated** with regard to **one who entered another's field and planted** trees in **it without** the **permission** of the owner of the field. Since the owner of the field profits from the planter's actions, he is required to pay him. **Rav says:** The court **appraises** both the expenses **for** the one who planted the trees and the value of the improvements **and** the planter **is at a disadvantage,** i.e., the owner of the field pays the lesser of the two amounts. **And Shmuel says:** The court **estimates how much a person would be willing to give** for someone **to plant** trees **in this field,** and that is how much the owner of the field must pay.

The *gemara* introduces a new case: *ha-yored le-tokh sdeh chaveiro u-neta'ah shelo bi-reshut* — one who enters a neighbor's field and plants a tree there without permission. What does the field owner owe this unauthorized planter, and how is it calculated?

### Rav's Rule

Rav rules: *shamin lo ve-yado al ha-tachtonah* — we evaluate the situation and the planter receives the lesser of two amounts: either what it cost him in labor and materials (*hotza'ot*), or the *shvach* — the added value his planting conferred on the field. Because he acted without permission, he receives the more unfavorable of the two measures.

### Shmuel's Rule

Shmuel disagrees: *omdin kamah adam rotze li-tein be-sadeh zo li-nto'ah* — we evaluate how much a person would be willing to pay to have this field planted for him. This is a single, specific standard of market value for the planting service itself, without the disadvantageous *yado al ha-tachtonah* qualification.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: כָּאן – בְּשָׂדֶה הָעֲשׂוּיָה לִיטַּע, כָּאן – בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לִיטַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: And** Rav and Shmuel **do not disagree. Here,** Shmuel's ruling is **with regard to a field that is designated for planting,** while **there,** Rav's ruling is **with regard to a field that is not designated for planting.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּרַב לָאו בְּפֵירוּשׁ אִיתְּמַר, אֶלָּא מִכְּלָלָא אִיתְּמַר. דְּהָהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שׁוּם לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא בָּעֵינָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שׁוּם לֵיהּ, וְיָדוֹ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא בָּעֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And this** ruling **of Rav was not stated explicitly; rather, it was stated implicitly. As** there was **a certain** person **who came before Rav** after someone had planted trees in that person's field. Rav **said to him: Go** and have the court **appraise** both the expenses **for** the one who planted the trees and the value of the improvements. The owner of the field **said to him: I do not want** these trees; why should I pay for them? Rav **said to him: Go** and have the court **appraise** both the expenses **for** the one who planted the trees and the value of the improvements, **and he is at a disadvantage,** i.e., you are obligated to pay him only the lesser of the two amounts. The owner of the field again **said to him: I do not want** these trees; why should I pay for them? Rav did not persist with his ruling, indicating that he conceded that the owner of the field was not obligated to pay.

Rav Papa reconciles the two positions: *ve-lo peligi* — they do not actually disagree. Shmuel's rule applies to a *sadeh ha-asuyah li-nto'a* — a field that is suitable and intended for planting trees. In such a case, the field owner benefits from the planting in a straightforward way, and market rate for planting services is the appropriate measure. Rav's rule — with its less favorable *yado al ha-tachtonah* calculation — applies to a *sadeh she-einah asuyah li-nto'a*, a field not meant for trees, where the owner derives less benefit or may not have wanted trees at all.

Notably, Rav's position was not stated explicitly; it was derived from a practical case — *mikhalla itmar* — deduced from the story that follows.

## Trespass Planting (II)

### Fencing Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף חַזְיֵיהּ דְּגַדְרַהּ וְקָא מְנַטַּר לַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ גַּלִּית אַדַּעְתָּיךְ דְּנִיחָא לָךְ, זִיל שׁוּם לֵיהּ, וְיָדוֹ עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Eventually,** Rav **saw** that the owner of the field **had fenced in** the field **and was safeguarding** the trees. Rav **said to him:** By doing so **you have demonstrated your opinion that** having those trees in your field **is satisfactory to you.** Therefore, I rule that you should **go** and have the court **appraise** both the expenses **for** the one who planted the trees and the value of the improvements, **and he has the advantage,** i.e., you are obligated to pay him the greater of the two amounts.

The story behind Rav's stated position involves a case that came before Rav himself. Someone came to him in a situation where a tree had been planted in another's field without permission. Rav told the field owner: *zil shum lei* — go evaluate it and pay accordingly. The field owner responded: *lo ba'ina* — "I don't want the tree; I want it removed." Rav accordingly ruled *yado al ha-tachtonah* — the interloper receives the lesser of his costs or the added value.

Subsequently, it was observed that the field owner had fenced in the field and was actively guarding the tree. Rav said to him: *galita ada'atcha de-nicha lach* — you have revealed your true preference; it is clear that you actually want the tree. The ruling was reversed: *zil shum lei ve-yado al ha-elyonah* — now pay him the higher of the two amounts. The changed behavior — fencing and guarding — constituted compelling evidence that the original objection was either insincere or had since been abandoned, and the calculation shifted accordingly.

## Building in Ruin

### Building Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַיּוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ חוּרְבָּתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ וּבְנָאָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: עֵצַיי וַאֲבָנַיי אֲנִי נוֹטֵל, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § An amoraic dispute **was stated** with regard to **one who entered another's ruin and built it** up using his own materials but **without** the landowner's **permission, and** then later, the builder **said to** the landowner: **I will** dismantle the structure and **take my timber and my stones** back. **Rav Naḥman says:** The court **listens to him** and he is allowed to do so. **Rav Sheshet says:** The court **does not listen to him** and he is not allowed to do so.

A parallel case involves building rather than planting: *ha-yored le-tokh churbato shel chaveiro u-vanah shelo bi-reshuto* — one who enters another's ruin and constructs a building there without permission. Subsequently the builder has second thoughts and says: *atzai va-avnai ani natel* — "I want to take back my wood and my stones."

### Nachman Permits

Rav Nachman rules: *shamin lo* — we listen to him; the builder may retrieve his materials.

### Sheshet Opposes

Rav Sheshet disagrees: *ein shamin lo* — we do not permit the builder to reclaim his materials once they have been incorporated into the structure.

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. לֵימָא רַב נַחְמָן דְּאָמַר כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Naḥman's ruling from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 10:6) about the same case: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Beit Shammai say** that the court **listens to him, and Beit Hillel say** that the court **does not listen to him.** The Gemara explains the difficulty posed: **Shall we say** that **Rav Naḥman said** his ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai?** That is untenable, as this is not listed as one of the specific exceptions to the principle that the *halakha* is always in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Naḥman **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*,** i.e., Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: According to both Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai, the court **listens to him;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Beit Shammai say that** the court **listens to him, and Beit Hillel say that** the court **does not listen to him.**

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel: *Beit Shammai omrim shamin lo, u-Veit Hillel omrim ein shamin lo* — Beit Shammai holds that the builder may reclaim his materials; Beit Hillel holds he may not. This appears to put Rav Nachman in the camp of Beit Shammai, which is problematic since the *halacha* follows Beit Hillel.

Rav Nachman's response is that he does not hold according to the Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel dispute as framed by Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel. Rather, he holds like *hai tanna* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who maintains that *shomein lo* is not disputed at all: everyone agrees the builder may reclaim his materials. It is only Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel who frames a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel on the matter.

### Final Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּבַיִת – שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. בְּשָׂדֶה – אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? **Rabbi Ya'akov said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** **In the** case of **a house,** the court **listens to him,** but **in the** case of **a field,** the court **does not listen to him.**

The *gemara* asks: *mai havi alah?* — what is the final ruling? Rabbi Yaakov says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *be-bayit shomein lo* — with respect to a building constructed in another's ruin, we follow Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's uncontested position, and the builder may reclaim his materials. *U-ve-sadeh ein shomein lo* — but with respect to a tree planted in another's field without permission, we do not permit its removal.

### Two Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׂדֶה מַאי טַעְמָא – מִשּׁוּם יִשּׁוּב אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: מִשּׁוּם כַּחְשָׁא דְאַרְעָא. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In the** case of **a field, what is the reason** that the court does not listen to him? It is that **due to** the desire to promote **the settling of Eretz Yisrael,** it is inappropriate to uproot trees. **There are** those **who say** that it is for a different reason: It is **due to the weakening of the land** already caused by the tree roots, as it made the land unsuitable for other uses. Therefore, the owner of the trees may not just take them and leave the land in its worsened condition. The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between these** reasons? The practical difference **between them** is whether **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, the one who planted the trees may uproot them.

Why the difference between a building and a tree? Two explanations are offered. The first: *mishum yishuv Eretz Yisrael* — because we wish to encourage the cultivation and settling of Eretz Yisrael, and uprooting a productive tree works against that value. The second: *ika de-amri mishum kachash de-ara* — some say the reason is that uprooting damages the field itself, and we therefore do not allow it.

The practical difference between these two rationales: *ika beineihu chutz la-aretz* — the *yishuv Eretz Yisrael* rationale would not apply outside of Eretz Yisrael, permitting the tree owner to reclaim his tree there, while the field-damage rationale would apply equally inside and outside the land.

## House Rental

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ מִן הֶחָג וְעַד הַפֶּסַח. בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וּבַכְּרַכִּים, אֶחָד יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה וְאֶחָד יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים – שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. וּבַחֲנוּיוֹת, אֶחָד עֲיָירוֹת וְאֶחָד כְּרַכִּים – שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: חֲנוּת שֶׁל נַחְתּוֹמִים וְשֶׁל צַבָּעִים שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who rents out a house** in a town **to another in the rainy season,** the owner **cannot evict** the renter from the house **from the festival** of *Sukkot* **until Passover.** If the rental was **in the summer,** he must give **thirty days'** notice before he can evict him. **And** for a house located **in the cities [*uvakerakim*], both in the summer and in the rainy season** he must give **twelve months'** notice. **And for shops** that he rented out, **both in towns and in cities,** he must give **twelve months'** notice. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** For **a baker's shop or a dyer's shop,** one must give **three years'** notice.

A new *mishnah* governs the rental of houses: *ha-maskir bayit le-chavero bi-ymot ha-geshamim eino yachol le-hotzio min ha-chag ve-ad ha-Pesach* — one who rents a house to another during the winter months (*yemot ha-geshamim*) cannot evict the tenant from *Sukkot* through *Pesach*. *Bi-ymot ha-chama shloshim yom* — in the summer months, he must give thirty days' notice before eviction. These rules, as Rashi and the Rambam both explain, apply to an open-ended rental — where no fixed term was specified.

### Location Types

The *mishnah* distinguishes further by location type. *U-ve-krachim* — in large cities, *echad yemot ha-chama ve-echad yemot ha-geshamim shneim asar chodesh* — regardless of season, the tenant is entitled to a full twelve months. *U-ve-chanuyot* — for commercial stalls in the market, *echad ayarot ve-echad krachim shneim asar chodesh* — whether in a small town or a large city, the tenant also receives twelve months. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds: a baker's stall or a dyer's stall — *chanut shel nachtomim ve-shel tzava'im* — carries a three-year term, because the investment required to establish such an operation is substantial and the customary rental period reflects that.

### Gemara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, דְּכִי אָגַר אִינִישׁ בֵּיתָא בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים אָגַר לְכוּלְּהוּ יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה נָמֵי, דְּכִי אָגַר אִינִישׁ בֵּיתָא לְכוּלְּהוּ יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה אָגַר! אֶלָּא בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ בֵּיתָא לְמֵיגַר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the rainy season** that one cannot evict his renter? The Gemara suggests: **Because when a person rents a house during the rainy season,** it is presumed that **he rents it for the entire rainy season.** The Gemara challenges this: But in **the summer as well,** the same *halakha* should apply, **because when a person rents a house, he rents it for the entire summer.** The Gemara offers a different explanation: **Rather, in the rainy season, this is the reason** that he cannot evict him: It is **because** at that time, **houses for renting are not found** on the market. Since alternative housing is not available, even if he is renting the house month by month, one cannot evict him.

The *gemara* poses a sharp question on the *mishnah*'s distinction between summer and winter: *mai shna yemot ha-geshamim?* — why does the season matter at all? If someone rents a house in the summer, presumably he rents it for the entire summer season; if in the winter, for the entire winter. Why does the *mishnah* treat the winter as providing protection through *Pesach* while the summer provides only thirty days?

The *gemara* initially suggests an answer: *ela bi-ymot ha-geshamim hainu ta'ama de-lo shchiach beita le-meigar* — the winter protection exists because in winter it is difficult to find alternative housing, since everyone needs to be indoors. Eviction during winter would leave the tenant without recourse. In summer, housing is more available and thirty days is adequate notice.

However, the *gemara* challenges this answer from the latter part of the *mishnah*: in large cities (*krachim*), the tenant receives twelve months regardless of season — meaning that even a winter tenant in a large city could be evicted mid-winter with proper advance notice. But if the entire rationale for winter protection is the difficulty of finding alternative housing, why does this not apply equally to large cities in winter?

### Notice Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, לְהוֹדִיעַ קָתָנֵי. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ סְתָם – אֵין יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים מֵחַג וְעַד הַפֶּסַח, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הוֹדִיעוֹ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Given this difficulty, the Gemara offers a different interpretation of the mishna: **Rav Yehuda said:** The mishna **teaches** about the requirement **to give notice** before eviction, **and this** is what it **is saying:** Although in general in a case of **one who rents out a house to another without specification** of when the rental period will end, both the landlord and the renter can end the rental whenever they so decide, the landlord cannot **evict** the renter **during the rainy season,** i.e., **from the festival** of *Sukkot* **until Passover, unless he gives him notice** of **thirty days from the outset,** i.e., before the rainy season begins. Since it would still be summer, it would be possible for the renter to find alternate housing. But if a fixed rental period was agreed upon, the renter may be evicted upon its completion without prior notice.

Rav Yehuda resolves the difficulty by reframing the entire *mishnah*. *Le-hodia katani* — the *mishnah* is not discussing how long a tenant may stay; it is discussing how much advance notice (*hoda'ah*) the landlord must give before eviction. Read this way: one who rents a house without specifying a term cannot evict the tenant during the winter months between *Sukkot* and *Pesach* unless he gave thirty days' notice beforehand. During summer, thirty days' notice suffices. In large cities, twelve months' notice is required regardless of season. The tenant's protection is not in the duration of their stay per se, but in the requirement that notice be given sufficiently in advance.

### Mutual Notice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כְּשֶׁאָמְרוּ שְׁלֹשִׁים, וּכְשֶׁאָמְרוּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ – לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא לְהוֹדִיעוֹ. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁמַּשְׂכִּיר צָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ, כָּךְ שׂוֹכֵר צָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אוֹדַעְתַּן, הֲוָה טָרַחְנָא וּמוֹתֵיבְנָא בֵּיהּ אִינִישׁ מְעַלְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **When they said,** in the mishna: **Thirty days, and when they said: Twelve months, they said it only** with regard to the requirement **to give notice** before eviction. **And just as a landlord needs to give notice** to his renter before he evicts him, **so too a renter needs to give notice** to his landlord before he can terminate the rental. The Gemara explains why the renter must give notice: **As** the landlord **can say to him: Had you given me notice, I would have exerted myself** to find **and settle a respectable person in** my house.

A *baraita* extends this principle symmetrically: *u-khe-shem she-ha-maskir tzarich le-hodia, kach ha-socher tzarich le-hodia* — just as the landlord must give advance notice of his intention to reclaim the property, so too must the tenant give the landlord advance notice of his intention to vacate. The landlord who is not warned in time can claim: *ei hodatan, havina motivna bei inash malya* — had you told me you were leaving, I would have found a suitable tenant to replace you. Failure to give proper notice means the tenant is obligated to remain for the full notice period, or face the consequences.

### Winter Overlap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אִם נִכְנַס יוֹם אֶחָד בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים – אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ מִן הֶחָג עַד הַפֶּסַח. וְהָא אֲנַן שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם קָאָמַר! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם נִכְנַס יוֹם אֶחָד בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, מֵהָנֵי שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ מִן הַחַג וְעַד הַפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says: If one day** of the rental **enters into the rainy season** without notice being given, then the landlord **cannot evict** the renter **from the festival** of *Sukkot* **until Passover.** The Gemara challenges this: **But didn't we say** that he must be given **thirty days'** notice? Rav Asi's ruling indicates that if notice was given even a day before *Sukkot*, that would be sufficient. The Gemara explains: **This** is what Rav Asi **is saying: If one day of these thirty days** of notice **enters into the rainy season** without notice being given, i.e., if notice was given fewer than thirty days before *Sukkot*, then the landlord **cannot evict** the renter **from the festival** of *Sukkot* **until Passover.**

Rav Assi adds a practical ruling: *ei nichnas yom echad bi-ymot ha-geshamim, eino yachol le-hotzio min ha-chag ve-ad ha-Pesach.* If even one of the thirty days of the required notice period falls within the winter season (*yemot ha-geshamim*), the landlord cannot evict the tenant for the entire duration from *Sukkot* through *Pesach*. The practical implication: the landlord must issue notice no later than the 15th of Elul, so that the entire thirty-day notice period concludes by the onset of *Sukkot*. If notice is given even one day later — such that a single day of the notice period falls into the winter — the tenant has standing to remain until *Pesach*.

### Rent Increase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: וְאִם בָּא לְרַבּוֹת בְּדָמֶיהָ מְרַבֶּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: הַאי לִנְקְטֵיהּ בְּכוּבְסֵיהּ דְּלִשְׁבְּקֵיהּ לִגְלִימֵאּ?! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּאִיַּיקּוּר בָּתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: And if** the landlord **comes to increase** the rental **fee, he may increase** it without prior notice. **Rav Naḥman said to him:** One who does so is like **this** person **who grabbed** another **by his testicles so that he would relinquish his cloak,** i.e., he has not provided the person with a true choice. By increasing the rent, one is effectively evicting him and so he should have to give thirty days' notice. The Gemara defends Rav Huna's opinion: **No,** the ruling is **necessary** in a case **where** the rental of **houses became more expensive.** Since the landlord would lose out by preserving the rent, it is acceptable for him to increase the rent without prior notice.

Rav Huna rules: *ve-ei ba le-ribbuyei be-damei, marbeh* — if the tenant is being compelled to remain past what he would have chosen, the landlord may nonetheless raise the rent during this forced retention period.

### Coercion Objection

Rav Nachman objects sharply to Rav Huna's position. Using a vivid colloquial metaphor — *hai de-nakit be-chushyei de-lo shavkei le-glima* — he characterizes it as extortionate: I am holding you in a position where you have no choice, and now I am also demanding your coat? The combination of being unable to leave and being forced to pay more is fundamentally coercive.

### Market Price

The *gemara* resolves this tension: *lo tzricha de-okir batei* — the permission to raise rent applies only when housing prices in general have risen in the market. If the broader market has moved, the landlord is not being coercive in adjusting to prevailing rates; he is simply charging what any other landlord would charge. But absent a general rise in market prices, Rav Nachman's objection stands and the original rental price must be maintained.

### Peshita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא נְפַל לֵיהּ בֵּיתָא – אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא עֲדִיפַתְּ מִינַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** § It is **obvious** that if **the house** in which the landlord lives **fell down,** then he can evict his renter from the house that he is renting to him, without giving notice, as **he can say to him: You are no better than me.** Since the landlord needs to find a new house to live in, he can demand that he should move into his rental property and it should be his renter who must look for new housing. He cannot be expected to have given notice, as he could not have foreseen that his house would fall down.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זַבְּנֵיהּ אוֹ אוֹרְתֵיהּ אוֹ יַהֲבֵיהּ בְּמַתָּנָה – אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא עֲדִיפַתְּ מִגַּבְרָא דַּאֲתֵית מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If a landlord **sold** the rental property to another, **or bequeathed it** to his heirs, **or gave it** to another person **as a gift,** the renter **can say to** the new owner: **You are no better than the person from whom your** ownership of the property **came.** Since he was required to give me notice before evicting me, so must you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּלְּלֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ, חָזֵינַן: אִי הֲוָה אֶפְשָׁר לְאוֹדוֹעֵיהּ – אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאוֹדוֹעֵי, וְאִי לָא – אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא עֲדִיפַתְּ מִינַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** If a landlord **marries off his son,** and wishes to evict his current renter to provide a home for the newly married couple, then **we see: If it was possible for him to have given notice,** as the couple had already been engaged for some time, **then he is required to give notice** and cannot evict his renter otherwise, **but if** it was **not** possible to give timely notice, then **he can say** to his renter: **You are no better than me** and my needs. I need the property now for my son, so it should be you who should go and find alternate housing.

The *gemara* then works through several scenarios involving a landlord's need to reclaim his property. First, a clear case: if the landlord's own home collapses or becomes uninhabitable, he may evict his tenant — *lo adifat minai*, "you are no better than I am." The landlord does not have housing and owns this property; his own need prevails. This principle resonates with contemporary property law discussions as well.

Similarly, if the landlord sells the house, gives it away, or rents it to someone else, the new party acquires it subject to the existing tenancy. The tenant can say to the new owner: *lo adifat mi-gavra de-atit mineih* — "you are no better than the person from whom you received this property." The original landlord could not have evicted me; neither can you.

A more nuanced case: *klala le-vreih chazinan* — if the landlord intends to give the house as a gift to his son, we must examine the circumstances. *Ei hava efshar le-odi'ei, iba'ei lei le-odi'ei* — if it was possible for the landlord to have given advance notice, he was obligated to do so, and failure to give notice means the tenant may not be evicted. But if advance notice was genuinely impossible — Rashi offers the example of a son whose engagement was sudden and the bride's family insisted on an immediate wedding — then the landlord may reclaim the property even without prior warning. *Lo adifat minai* — in such circumstances of genuine urgency, the tenant cannot claim superior standing over the landlord's pressing personal need.

### Wine Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּזְבַן אַרְבָּא דְחַמְרָא. לָא אַשְׁכַּח דּוּכְתָּא לְאוֹתוֹבֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְהַהִיא אִיתְּתָא: אִית לָךְ דּוּכְתָּא לְאוֹגוֹרַי? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לָא. אֲזַל קַדְּשַׁהּ יְהַבָה לֵיהּ דּוּכְתָּא לְעַיֹּולֵיהּ. אֲזַל לְבֵיתֵיהּ כְּתַב לַהּ גִּיטָּא, שַׁדַּר לַהּ. אֲזַלָא אִיהִי, אֲגַרָא שָׁקוֹלָאֵי מִינֵּיהּ וּבֵיהּ, אַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ וְאוֹתְבֵיהּ בִּשְׁבִילָא. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: "כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה כֵּן יֵעָשֶׂה לּוֹ גְּמוּלוֹ יָשׁוּב בְּרֹאשׁוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who purchased a boat** laden **with wine. He was unable to find a place to store it. He said to a certain woman: Do you have place to rent to me? She said to him: No.** He was aware that she did own a suitable place, so **he went and betrothed her,** and then **she gave him** a lease on **the place** for him **to bring in** his wine there. **He went** back **to his home** and **wrote a bill of divorce for her,** which **he** then **sent to her.** Upon receiving the bill of divorce and realizing that the betrothal had been nothing more than a ruse, **she went and hired porters,** paying them **from** the wine **itself,** and instructed them to **take** the wine **out** of her place **and put it on the road.** Upon being presented with this case, **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said,** paraphrasing Obadiah 1:15: **Like he did,** so **shall be done to him, his repayment shall come back on his head;** she was entitled to do as she did.

The *gemara* concludes this section with an arresting story. A man purchased a shipload of wine but had no place to store it. He encountered a woman and asked whether she had space to rent him. She said no. He then proposed marriage, and she accepted. By virtue of being married to him, she provided him space for his wine. Once the wine was safely stored, he returned home, wrote a *get*, and sent it to her — an act of egregious bad faith.

She responded by hiring workers to remove the wine from the storage space and placing it in the street. The case came before Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, who ruled in her favor: *ka-asher asah kein ye'aseh lo, gemulo yashuv be-rosho* — as he did to her, so may be done to him; his recompense shall return upon his own head. Furthermore, the ruling applied not only to a case where the woman had no genuinely rentable space — *chatzer de-lo ka-ima le-agra* — but even to a case where she did have rentable space — *chatzer de-ka-ima le-agra*. In either scenario, she may remove the wine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא חָצֵר דְּלָא קָיְימָא לְאַגְרָא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חָצֵר דְּקָיְימָא לְאַגְרָא, אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נִיחָא לִי לְאוֹגוֹרֵי וְלָךְ לָא נִיחָא לִי, דְּדָמֵית עֲלַי כִּי אַרְיָא אָרְבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains Rav Huna's ruling: It is **not necessary** to state that the woman is entitled to evict the man if the place she rented out to him was **a courtyard that did not stand to be rented out,** in which case she is not expected to rent it to anyone; **rather, even** if it was **a courtyard that stands to be rented out, she** could **say to him:** It is **amenable for me to rent** the place **to everyone** else, **but it is not amenable to me** to rent it **to you, as you are to me like a preying lion.** Since you deceived me, I do not wish to have any dealings with you.

Her reasoning: *le-khulei alma nicha li le-ogurei, u-lakh lo nicha li, de-damit alai ki-aryah arva* — "I am happy to rent to anyone else, but not to you — you are like a predatory lion." The marriage was procured under false pretenses as a mechanism to obtain storage; the resulting "rental" cannot be enforced against her once the marriage was dissolved in bad faith. Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua's ruling underscores that the rights of tenancy do not extend to arrangements obtained through manipulation and betrayal.

## Maintenance Obligations

### Landlord Duties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ, הַמַּשְׂכִּיר חַיָּיב בַּדֶּלֶת, בַּנֶּגֶר וּבַמַּנְעוּל, וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁמַּעֲשֵׂה אוּמָּן. אֲבָל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַעֲשֵׂה אוּמָּן – הַשּׂוֹכֵר עוֹשֵׂהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one rents out a house to another, the landlord bears the responsibility for** providing **the door, for** providing **the bolt, for** providing **the lock, and for** providing **every item** in the house **that** is essential for normal living and requires **the work of a craftsman** to provide it. **But** with regard to **an item that does not** require **the work of a craftsman, the renter** is responsible to **make it.**

The final *mishnah* of the *daf* addresses the allocation of maintenance responsibilities between landlord (*maskir*) and tenant (*socher*). The *mishnah* rules: *ha-maskir chayav ba-delet, ba-nagar u-va-man'ul* — the landlord is responsible for providing a door, an interior bolt, and an exterior lock. More broadly, *ve-khol davar she-ma'aseh uman* — anything requiring skilled craftwork is the landlord's obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַזֶּבֶל שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְאֵין לַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶלָּא הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַתַּנּוּר וּמִן הַכִּירַיִם בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **The manure** found in the courtyard of a rented house is the property **of the landlord, and the renter has** rights **only** to the ashes **that come out of the oven and the stove,** which can also be used as a fertilizer.

### Tenant Duties

By contrast, *aval davar she-eino ma'aseh uman, ha-socher oseh-u* — anything that does not require a craftsman but can be done by an ordinary person is the tenant's responsibility. The *mishnah* also addresses ownership of the manure (*zevel*) found in the courtyard: it belongs to the landlord. The tenant, however, has rights to whatever ash or residue emerges from the oven and the double-burner stove — this belongs to the tenant, since it is a product of his use of the premises.

### Baraita Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּשְׂכִּיר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ, מַשְׂכִּיר חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ דְּלָתוֹת, לִפְתּוֹחַ לוֹ חַלּוֹנוֹת, לְחַזֵּק לוֹ תִּקְרָה, לִסְמוֹךְ לוֹ קוֹרָה. וְשׂוֹכֵר חַיָּיב לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ סוּלָּם, לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ מַעֲקֶה, לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ מַרְזֵב וּלְהָטִיחַ אֶת גַּגּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one rents out a house to another,** the **landlord bears the responsibility to install doors for it, to open windows** in its walls to provide light **for it, to strengthen its ceiling,** and **to support its** cross **beam. And the renter bears the responsibility to make a ladder for it** to provide access to the roof, **to erect a parapet for its** roof (see Deuteronomy 22:8), **to construct a gutter for it** to carry away rain which falls on the roof, **and to plaster its roof** so that rain does not leak through it.

A *baraita* expands on the landlord's responsibilities: *ha-maskir chayav le-ha'amid lo daltot* — doors, as established — *ve-chalonot*, windows; *ve-chazek et ha-tikrah*, reinforcing the roof; *li-smoch lo korah*, installing a beam. The tenant's obligations, in turn, include providing a ladder for roof access (*sulam*), constructing a parapet (*ma'akeh*) around the roof, installing a gutter (*marzev*), and plastering or thatching the roof (*latei'ach et gago*) when needed. Notably, roof plastering today would typically qualify as *ma'aseh uman* and would therefore fall on the landlord.

### Mezuzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מְזוּזָה עַל מִי? מְזוּזָה?! הָאָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: מְזוּזָה חוֹבַת הַדָּר הִיא. אֶלָּא: מְקוֹם מְזוּזָה עַל מִי? אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: תְּנֵיתוּהָ, דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מַעֲשֵׂה אוּמָּן – הַשּׂוֹכֵר עוֹשֵׂהוּ, וְהַאי נָמֵי לָאו מַעֲשֵׂה אוּמָּן הוּא, אֶפְשָׁר הוּא בְּגוּבְתָּא דְּקַנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The students in the study hall **raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: Upon whom** is the obligation to affix **a *mezuza*** (see Deuteronomy 6:9)? The Gemara expresses surprise at the question: Why did they ask about **a *mezuza*; doesn't Rav Mesharshiyya say:** Affixing **a *mezuza* is the obligation of the resident?** It is certainly the responsibility of the renter. The Gemara emends the dilemma: **Rather,** their dilemma was: **Upon whom** is the responsibility to prepare the **place** where the ***mezuza*** will be affixed, e.g., to bore a slit in a stone doorpost to insert the *mezuza* there? **Rav Sheshet said to them: You learned** this in the mishna: With regard to **an item that** does **not** require **the work of a craftsman, the renter** is responsible to **make it. And this** task **is also** something that does **not** require **the work of a craftsman,** as it **is possible** to insert a *mezuza* **inside** a hollow **reed** and then affix the whole arrangement to the doorpost.

The *gemara* closes with a question posed to Rav Sheshet: *mezuzah al mi?* — who bears the obligation for the *mezuzah*? Rav Mesharsheya's ruling is cited: *mezuzah chovat ha-dar hi* — the *mezuzah* is the obligation of the one who lives there, i.e., the tenant. This is unambiguous.

A more subtle question follows: *ela mekom mezuzah al mi?* — but who is responsible for the *mekom mezuzah*, the physical preparation of the doorpost to receive the *mezuzah*? Rav Sheshet responds by invoking the principle of the *mishnah*: *davar she-ein ma'aseh uman, ha-socher oseh-u* — since the preparation of the doorpost's housing is not a craftsman's task, it falls to the tenant. The *gemara* adds: *efshar hu be-gufta*, with a reference to a *baraita* — and the discussion continues into the next *amud*.
