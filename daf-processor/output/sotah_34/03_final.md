# Sotah 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Jordan Crossing

### Daf 34 Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת נָשְׂאוּ כֹּהֲנִים אֶת הָאָרוֹן: כְּשֶׁעָבְרוּ אֶת הַיַּרְדֵּן, וּכְשֶׁהֵסֵיבּוּ אֶת יְרִיחוֹ, וּכְשֶׁהֶחְזִירוּהוּ לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:2) that **Rabbi Yosei says: In three** different **places the priests carried the Ark.** They carried it **when** the Jewish people **crossed the Jordan, and when they surrounded Jericho** (Joshua 6:6), **and when they returned it to its** proper **place** in the Holy of Holies during the reign of King Solomon (I Kings 8:6).

Daf 34 picks up twenty lines down, at the words *odam ba-Yarden* — "while they were still in the Jordan" — in the midst of a *baraita* drawn from the *Tosefta*. The broader context is the *Mishnah*'s discussion of what may be said in any language versus what must be recited specifically in *lashon ha-kodesh*. The *Mishnah* had ruled that the blessings and curses pronounced on Har Gerizim and Har Eival could only be said in Hebrew, and the *Gemara* has been unfolding a detailed account of everything that accompanied the crossing of the Jordan into Canaan: the erection of the stones, the building of the *mizbe'ach*, and the proclamation of the *brachos* and *klalos*. Daf 34 continues from within that narrative.

### Joshua's Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹדָם בַּיַּרְדֵּן אָמַר לָהֶם יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: דְּעוּ עַל מָה אַתֶּם עוֹבְרִים אֶת הַיַּרְדֵּן — עַל מְנָת שֶׁתּוֹרִישׁוּ אֶת יוֹשְׁבֵי הָאָרֶץ מִפְּנֵיכֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוֹרַשְׁתֶּם אֶת כׇּל יוֹשְׁבֵי הָאָרֶץ מִפְּנֵיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״. אִם אַתֶּם עוֹשִׂין כֵּן — מוּטָב. וְאִם לָאו, בָּאִין מַיִם וְשׁוֹטְפִין אוֹתִיכֶם. מַאי ״אוֹתִיכֶם״ — אוֹתִי וְאֶתְכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** While the Jewish people **were still in the Jordan, Joshua said to them: Know for what** purpose **you are crossing the Jordan.** It is **in order to drive out the inhabitants of the land from before you, as it is stated: "And you shall drive out all the inhabitants of the land from before you"** (Numbers 33:52). **If you** will **do so,** then all is **well, but if not, water will come and drown *otikhem*.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the word ***otikhem*?** The Gemara explains: It is a combination of the words **me [*oti*] and you [*etkhem*].**

While the *kohanim* were still standing in the Jordan — and indeed all of *Bnei Yisrael* were still in the river — Yehoshua addressed them: *de'u al mah atem overim et ha-Yarden*, "understand for what purpose you are crossing the Jordan." He stated the condition explicitly: *al menat she-torishu et yoshvei ha-aretz mi-pneichem* — you are crossing on the condition that you commit yourselves to driving out the inhabitants of the land. The *baraita* cites the verse in Bamidbar, *ve-horashtem et kol yoshvei ha-aretz mi-pneichem*, and adds: if you fulfill this, well and good — but *im lav*, if you do not, *ba'u mayim u-shotefim etchem*, the waters will come and drown you. Whether this means that failure to agree at that moment would bring immediate catastrophe, or that failure to ultimately carry it out would lead to that consequence, the text leaves somewhat open; the latter reading seems more natural. The *Gemara* then notes the unusual phrasing *otichem* — "you" — and derives that Yehoshua was saying *oti ve-etchem*, the waters will come and destroy me together with you. He included himself in the threat.

### Twelve Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹדָם בַּיַּרְדֵּן אָמַר לָהֶן יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: ״הָרִימוּ לָכֶם אִישׁ אֶבֶן אַחַת עַל שִׁכְמוֹ לְמִסְפַּר שִׁבְטֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״לְמַעַן תִּהְיֶה זֹאת אוֹת בְּקִרְבְּכֶם כִּי יִשְׁאָלוּן בְּנֵיכֶם מָחָר לֵאמֹר מָה הָאֲבָנִים הָאֵלֶּה לָכֶם וְגוֹ׳״, סִימָן לַבָּנִים שֶׁעָבְרוּ אָבוֹת אֶת הַיַּרְדֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** While **they were still in the Jordan, Joshua said to them: "Pick up every man of you a stone upon his shoulder, according to the number of the tribes of the children of Israel"** (Joshua 4:5). **And it is written: "That this may be a sign among you, that when your children ask in time to come, saying: What do you mean by these stones?"** (Joshua 4:6). This will be **a sign for the children that** their **ancestors crossed the Jordan.**

Again while standing in the Jordan, Yehoshua instructed them: *harimu lachem ish even achat al shichmo le-mispar shivtei Yisrael* — each man should take up one stone on his shoulder, corresponding to the twelve tribes, taken from the Jordan itself, and erect them after crossing over. The verse states: *le-ma'an tihyeh zot ot be-kirbechem* — this shall be a sign in your midst — so that when future generations ask, *mah ha-avanim ha-eleh lachem*, "what are these stones?", they will be told that the forefathers crossed the Jordan. This language resonates with the *pasuk* about the *Korban Pesach* and the exchange recalled in the *Seder*. The purpose of the stones, the *Gemara* says, is as a *siman la-vanim she-avru avot ha-Yarden* — a sign for children that the forefathers crossed the Jordan.

A further instruction followed: *se'u lachem mizeh mi-toch ha-Yarden mi-matzav raglei ha-kohanim* — take from where the *kohanim* are standing twelve stones, carry them with you, and place them *ba-malon asher talinu vo ha-laila*, in the place where you will sleep that night. One might have thought this meant they should carry the stones to every encampment — but the *Talmud* derives from the phrase *asher talinu vo ha-laila*, "where we will sleep this night," that only the first encampment is meant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹדָם בַּיַּרְדֵּן אָמַר לָהֶן יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: ״שְׂאוּ לָכֶם מִזֶּה מִתּוֹךְ הַיַּרְדֵּן מִמַּצַּב רַגְלֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים הָכִין שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אֲבָנִים וְהַעֲבַרְתֶּם אוֹתָם עִמָּכֶם וְהִנַּחְתֶּם אוֹתָם בַּמָּלוֹן אֲשֶׁר תָּלִינוּ בוֹ הַלַּיְלָה וְגוֹ׳״. יָכוֹל בְּכׇל מָלוֹן וּמָלוֹן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר תָּלִינוּ בוֹ הַלָּיְלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** While **they were still in the Jordan, Joshua said to them: "Take out of the midst of the Jordan, out of the place where the priests' feet stood, twelve stones made ready, and carry them over with you, and lay them down in the lodging place, where you shall lodge this night"** (Joshua 4:3). One **might** have thought that they were required to place these stones **at each and every lodging place** where they stayed. Therefore, **the verse states: "Where you shall lodge this night,"** meaning only on that night.

Rashi's comment at the verse *seu mizeh mi-toch ha-Yarden mi-matzav* is worth noting: he explains that the people lifted up stones, the *kohanim* stood on them, and the stones were then taken from beneath the feet of the *kohanim*. Rashi further states that these were the very stones later erected on Har Eival, upon which the *mizbe'ach* was built and the *Torah* inscribed *be'er heitev* — and that they were subsequently taken down and re-erected in Gilgal. The precise relationship among the three distinct elements — the altar, the writing of the *Torah* on the stones *be'er heitev*, and the twelve commemorative stones — is not straightforward, and working out their interconnection is no simple matter.

### Grape Cluster

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אַבָּא חֲלַפְתָּא וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן מַתְיָא וַחֲנַנְיָא בֶּן חֲכִינַאי עָמְדוּ עַל אוֹתָן אֲבָנִים, וְשִׁיעֲרוּם כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת שְׁקוּלָה כְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה. וּגְמִירִי דִּטְעוּנָא דְּמַדְלֵי אִינִישׁ לְכַתְפֵּיהּ — תִּילְתָּא דִּטְעוּנֵיהּ הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Abba Ḥalafta, and Rabbi Eliezer ben Matya, and Ḥananya ben Ḥakhinai stood on those same stones** that the Jewish people took from the Jordan, **and** they **measured them** and found that **each and every one weighed about forty *se'a*. And** it **is learned** as a tradition **that a load that one can lift onto his shoulders is one-third of** the weight of the **load** that he can carry when others load it onto him.

The *baraita* records that Rabbi Yehuda — or according to another reading, Rabbi Yose — reported that Abba Chalafta, Rabbi Eliezer ben Matya, and Chananya ben Chakinai once stood on those very stones, and measured that each stone weighed the equivalent of forty *se'ah*, an enormous weight. A received tradition (*gemiri*) teaches that the load a person can lift and hoist onto his own shoulders is only one-third of what he can carry if another person loads him. Since Yehoshua instructed them to have the stones lifted onto their shoulders — implying they were loaded by others — this shows that each carrier could bear the equivalent of 120 *se'ah*. From this calculation, the *Gemara* derives: *mikan ata mitchashev la-eshkol* — from here you can reckon the size of the grape cluster.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אַתָּה מְחַשֵּׁב לָאֶשְׁכּוֹל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשָּׂאֻהוּ בַמּוֹט בִּשְׁנָיִם״. מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בַּמּוֹט״, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁבִּשְׁנַיִם? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בִּשְׁנָיִם״ — בִּשְׁנֵי מוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **From here you can calculate** the size of **the cluster** of grapes that the spies carried together from Eretz Yisrael, **as it is stated: "And they carried it upon a pole between two"** (Numbers 13:23). **From the fact that it is stated** that they carried the cluster of grapes **"on a pole" do I not know that** it was carried **by two** people? That is the only way it can be carried on a pole. **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Between two"?** It means that the spies carried it **on two poles,** and four people carried the cluster of grapes together. From here it can be deduced that the weight of the cluster was 480 *se'a*.

The verse says the spies carried the grape cluster *bamot bishnayim*, with a pole between two. But if a pole already implies two carriers, why does the verse add the word *bishnayim*? The *Gemara* answers: *bishnei motot* — there were two poles. Rabbi Yitzchak adds the term *turtani ve-turtani de-turtani* — poles upon poles — indicating not merely two poles but four. Rashi explains the arrangement: two poles running in parallel and two crossing beneath them in an X formation, providing additional support. Eight people in total carried it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: טוּרְטָנֵי, וְטוּרְטָנֵי דְטוּרְטָנֵי, הָא כֵּיצַד? שְׁמֹנָה נָשְׂאוּ אֶשְׁכֹּל, אֶחָד נָשָׂא רִימּוֹן, וְאֶחָד נָשָׂא תְּאֵינָה. יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכָלֵב לֹא נָשְׂאוּ כְּלוּם. אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲשִׁיבִי. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ בְּאוֹתָהּ עֵצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** They were configured like upper rods of **scales [*turtanei*]** that are balanced on the lower rods of **scales,** i.e., there were not two but four poles. **How so? Eight** of the spies **carried** the **cluster** of grapes, **one** of them **carried a pomegranate, and one carried a fig. Joshua and Caleb did not carry anything.** Why did Joshua and Caleb not carry anything? **If you wish, say** that it is **because they were** more **prominent** than the others and it was beneath their dignity to carry such a load. **And if you wish, say** instead **that they** did **not** take part **in the** wicked **counsel** of the spies, as the cluster of grapes was brought by the spies to scare the people.

This raises an obvious question about the sheer weight: if a single person, when loaded by another, can carry 120 *se'ah*, and eight people are carrying together with four poles, the cluster must have been of staggering proportions. The *Gemara* elaborates: one person carried a pomegranate, one carried a fig, eight carried the single cluster of grapes — and Yehoshua and Kalev carried nothing at all. Two explanations are offered: either *mishum de-chashivei*, because they were too distinguished to carry anything, or *she-lo hayu be-oto eitzah* — because they were not part of the scheme. The bringing back of the fruit was, in their view, part of the strategy to frighten the people and was not a genuine celebration of the land's bounty.

### Formation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא. חַד אָמַר: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — כַּחֲנִיָּיתָן עָבְרוּ. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה עָבְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa disagree with regard to** the dispute between the *tanna'im* as to the height of the water when the Jewish people crossed the Jordan. **One says: According to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** that the water stood at the height of twelve *mil*, the Jewish people **crossed** in the same formation **as they camped.** It was necessary for the water to stand only twelve *mil* high to allow for the entire encampment to pass through the Jordan. **According to the statement of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** who said the water stood at a height of over three hundred *mil*, the water had to reach these heights to allow for enough time for everyone to cross the Jordan, as **they crossed one after the other.**

The *Gemara* records a dispute between Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha concerning how the Israelites crossed the Jordan. This connects to the earlier debate — recalled from the previous daf — about how high the waters piled up. Rabbi Yehuda had held that the waters rose twelve *mil*, because the camp was twelve *mil* across, and the time it took the camp to cross was the time during which the waters accumulated. Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon disagreed sharply, saying the waters rose 300 *mil* — twenty-five times the camp's width — because water moves far faster than people walk.

One opinion among Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha holds: according to Rabbi Yehuda, *ke-chaniyatan avru* — they crossed just as they were encamped, in a twelve-by-twelve square formation — and that is why it took the length of time it did to traverse the Jordan, producing a twelve-*mil* accumulation of water. According to Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon, *be-zeh achar zeh avru* — they crossed one after another, in a more elongated procession, something closer to a marching line rather than a square — and therefore it took far longer to pass through, causing the waters to pile much higher. This reading is somewhat surprising, since Rabbi Eliezer b'Rabbi Shimon's stated position was simply that water travels faster than people, not that they marched in a different formation — but the *Gemara* suggests this may also be part of his position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד אָמַר: בֵּין מָר וּבֵין מָר כַּחֲנִיָּיתָן עָבְרוּ. מָר סָבַר: אָדָם קַל, וּמָר סָבַר: מַיִם קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And one says:** According to **both** this **Sage and** that **Sage, they crossed** in the same formation **as they camped.** However, one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **a person** moves **faster** than water, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **water** moves **faster** than a person does.

The other opinion holds that *bein mar u-vein mar ke-chaniyatan avru* — both authorities agree that they crossed in the same square formation as they were encamped. The debate is purely about speed: *mar savar adam kal*, one holds that a person travels as fast as the water (an admittedly difficult image), while *mar savar mayim kalim*, the other holds that water moves far faster than people. This second reading appears to be the more straightforward account of what the earlier dispute actually was.

Tosafot engages in an extended analysis arising from the fact that the Jordan was only fifty *amot* wide. If Yehoshua addressed all of *Bnei Yisrael* while they were standing in the river, and if the camp measured twelve *mil* — which equals 2,400 *amot* — then there would need to be 480 units of people in a fifty-*amot*-wide riverbed to accommodate the entire camp, meaning the people would have had to move steadily downstream as more entered behind them. Tosafot attempts to calculate whether the Jordan itself was even long enough to contain them all simultaneously, and the mathematics are far from trivial.

## Spies Mission

### Shlach vs. Devarim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁלַח לְךָ אֲנָשִׁים״, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״שְׁלַח לְךָ״ — מִדַּעְתֶּךָ. וְכִי אָדָם זֶה בּוֹרֵר חֵלֶק רַע לְעַצְמוֹ? וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּיטַב בְּעֵינַי הַדָּבָר״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״בְּעֵינַי״, וְלֹא בְּעֵינָיו שֶׁל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § Since the Gemara mentioned the cluster of grapes that the spies brought back from Eretz Yisrael, it continues discussing the story of the spies. It is stated in the Torah that God told Moses: **"Send you** men" (Numbers 13:2). **Reish Lakish says: "Send you"** means that you should send them **at your own discretion** and not as a divine command. As, if it were a divine command, **does a person choose a bad portion for himself?** Since God knew the nature of these spies and that they would ultimately slander the land, He certainly would not have sent them Himself. **And this is** the meaning of **that which is written** in the passage where Moses retold the story of the spies: **"And it was good in my eyes"** (Deuteronomy 1:23), and **Reish Lakish says:** The implication of these words is that it seemed good **"in my eyes," but not in the eyes of the Omnipresent.**

Since the discussion has been centered on Yehoshua, the crossing into the land, and the figures of Yehoshua and Kalev who brought back fruit from Canaan, the *Gemara* transitions naturally to the episode of the spies. The text now takes up the relationship between the two accounts of that mission: the version in *Shlach* and the retelling in Devarim.

In *Shlach* the Torah reads *shlach lecha anashim* — send men. Reish Lakish explains: *shlach lecha mada'atcha* — "go ahead, do it from your own mind," meaning God was granting permission, not issuing a command. This reconciles the two accounts: in Devarim, Moshe recalls that the people came to him requesting the mission; in *Shlach*, God responds by permitting it. The question is then implicit: would God have commanded something that ended so catastrophically? The answer is evidently no — *vachi adam zeh borer chelek ra le-atzmo*, would a person really choose a bad portion for himself? Clearly God would not have ordained this; it was only a concession to the people's request.

This is also the meaning, Reish Lakish continues, of the verse *va-yitav be-einai ha-davar* — "and the matter was good in my eyes." Reish Lakish reads it: *be-einai ve-lo be-einav shel makom* — it seemed good to me, but not in God's eyes. God gave allowance, but it was not God's own preference — as the catastrophic outcome made plain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיַחְפְּרוּ לָנוּ אֶת הָאָרֶץ״, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: מְרַגְּלִים לֹא נִתְכַּוְּונוּ אֶלָּא לְבוֹשְׁתָּהּ שֶׁל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְיַחְפְּרוּ לָנוּ אֶת הָאָרֶץ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וְחָפְרָה הַלְּבָנָה וּבוֹשָׁה הַחַמָּה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah relates that the people asked Moses to send spies so **"that they may search the land for us"** (Deuteronomy 1:22). **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says:** When the Jewish people asked to send **spies, their intention was only to shame Eretz Yisrael. It is written here: "That they may search [*veyaḥperu*] the land for us," and it is written there: "Then the moon will be embarrassed [*veḥafera*], and the sun will be ashamed"** (Isaiah 24:23).

The *Gemara* then adds: *vayachperu lanu et ha-aretz* — "and let them search out the land for us." Rabbi Chiya bar Abba comments: the spies intended from the outset nothing less than the shaming of Eretz Yisrael. He draws a verbal parallel: *vayachperu* here echoes the verse *ve-chafra ha-levanah u-vosha ha-chamah* — the moon shall be shamed and the sun shall be embarrassed — showing that *vayachpor* carries the connotation of shame. Their mission was aimed at disgracing the land.

### Spies' Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֵלֶּה שְׁמוֹתָם לְמַטֵּה רְאוּבֵן שַׁמּוּעַ בֶּן זַכּוּר״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דָּבָר זֶה מָסוֹרֶת בְּיָדֵינוּ מֵאֲבוֹתֵינוּ: מְרַגְּלִים עַל שֵׁם מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם נִקְרְאוּ. וְאָנוּ לֹא עָלְתָה בְּיָדֵינוּ אֶלָּא אֶחָד: ״סְתוּר בֶּן מִיכָאֵל״, ״סְתוּר״ — שֶׁסָּתַר מַעֲשָׂיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. ״מִיכָאֵל״ — שֶׁעָשָׂה עַצְמוֹ מָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah states with regard to the spies: **"And these were their names: Of the tribe of Reuben, Shammua the son of Zaccur"** (Numbers 13:4). **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: This statement** that follows **is a tradition of ours** that was passed down to us **from our ancestors:** The **spies were named after their actions, but we have obtained** the interpretation of **only one** name, the name of **"Sethur the son of Michael"** (Numbers 13:13). He is called **Sethur, as he hid [*satar*] the actions of the Holy One, Blessed be He.** In other words, he ignored the miracles that God performed for the Jewish people in Egypt and in the wilderness. He is called **Michael, as he made Him,** God, appear **weak [*makh*]** by saying that there was not enough food in the land for everyone.

The *Gemara* then lists the names of the spies as the Torah records them, beginning with *le-mateh Reuven Shamua ben Zakur*. Rabbi Yitzchak states: we have a received tradition going back through the generations — *masoret be-yadeinu me-avoteinu* — that the spies were called by the names they bore in the Torah because those names reflected their deeds, *meraglim al shem ma'aseihem nikre'u*. Whether this means their parents providentially gave them names that came to describe their actions, or whether the Torah assigns them names that function as epithets, the *Gemara* does not resolve definitively. But the principle stands: the names encode what they did.

However, Rabbi Yitzchak acknowledges, we have only managed to work this out for one of them: *Satur ben Michael*. *Satur* — from the root meaning to undermine or destroy — *satar ma'asav shel ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu*: he undermined God's design, which was to bring the people into the land. *Michael* — the name contains *El*, God — *she-asah atzmo mach*: he made God appear weak, as though God lacked the power to bring them in.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אַף אָנוּ נֹאמַר: ״נַחְבִּי בֶּן וׇפְסִי״, ״נַחְבִּי״ — שֶׁהֶחְבִּיא דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. ״וׇפְסִי״ — שֶׁפִּיסַּע עַל מִדּוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: We can also say** an interpretation of the name: **"Nahbi the son of Vophsi"** (Numbers 13:14): He is called **Nahbi, as he concealed [*heḥbi*] the statement of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** that the land is good, by delivering a distorted description of it. He is called **Vophsi, as he stomped [*pisse'a*] on the attributes of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** i.e., he did not believe in His promise to give Eretz Yisrael to the Jewish people.

Rabbi Yochanan then takes up the challenge and proposes an additional example: *Nachbi ben Vafsi*. *Nachbi* — *she-hechbi devarav shel ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — he concealed God's words, hiding the truth about what God could and would do. *Vafsi* — *she-pisa al midotav shel ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — he strode over, as it were, leaped past God's attributes, failing to acknowledge divine power.

### Caleb's Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּעֲלוּ בַנֶּגֶב וַיָּבֹא עַד חֶבְרוֹן״ — ״וַיָּבֹאוּ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁפֵּירַשׁ כָּלֵב מֵעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים, וְהָלַךְ וְנִשְׁתַּטַּח עַל קִבְרֵי אָבוֹת. אָמַר לָהֶן: אֲבוֹתַי! בִּקְּשׁוּ עָלַי רַחֲמִים שֶׁאֶנָּצֵל מֵעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** It is also stated with regard to the spies: **"And they went up into the south, and he came to Hebron"** (Numbers 13:22). Why is the phrase "and he came" written in the singular form? The verse **should have** said: **And they came. Rava says:** This **teaches that Caleb separated himself from the counsel of** the other **spies and went and prostrated himself on the graves of the forefathers** in Hebron. **He said to them: My forefathers, pray for mercy for me so that I will be saved from the counsel of the spies.**

The verse in the *parasha* reads *va-ya'alu ba-negev va-yavo ad Chevron* — they went up through the south and he came to Hebron. The singular *va-yavo* is striking; it should read *va-yavo'u*, in the plural. Rava derives from this: *melamed she-parash Kalev me-atzat ha-meraglim* — the singular teaches that Kalev broke away from the conspiracy of the spies. Not only did he refuse to participate in their scheme, but he physically separated himself from the group. *Va-halach ve-nishtate'ach al kivrei avot* — he went and prostrated himself on the graves of the forefathers — Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov — who are buried in Chevron, along with the *Imahos*. He prayed: *avosai bikshu alai rachamim* — my fathers, seek mercy for me, that I be saved from the conspiracy of the spies, for it was enormously difficult to stand up against that pressure.

The Torah's statement — *ve-avdi Kalev ekev hayta ruach acheret imo* — that Kalev possessed a different spirit — is thus explained by this act: he went to Chevron, he prayed, and that inner strength was what distinguished him. Of course there is a further point of connection: Kalev ultimately received Chevron as his portion when the land was divided. And Rashi notes on the verse *va-avi'osiv el ha-aretz asher ba shama* — "and I will bring him to the land he went to" — this language is purposefully specific. It is not a general promise of entry into Canaan; it is a promise of the very place, Chevron, that Kalev had visited to pray.

Tosafot raises a pointed question about this episode: the *Gemara* elsewhere teaches that the dead are unaware of what transpires in this world — *meisim lo yad'ei midi*. If so, what is the purpose of prostrating oneself and praying at the graves of the forefathers? Tosafot answers: when a person prays in this way, they inform the deceased in heaven that someone below is praying and seeking their intercession, and those holy souls can then themselves pray to God on that person's behalf. There is also support from the *baraita* that asks why people go out to cemeteries — the answer given is *k'dei she-yivakshu aleihem meisim rachamim*, so that the dead will pray for mercy on behalf of the living. The matter is theologically sensitive — rationalist authorities were wary of any formulation that might slide into the appearance of praying *to* the dead rather than asking them to pray — but the passage makes clear that there is a recognized tradition here, and both the metaphysical question (do the dead have awareness?) and the theological question (what is the proper role of an intermediary?) warrant careful handling.

### Joshua Protected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוֹשֻׁעַ כְּבָר בִּקֵּשׁ מֹשֶׁה עָלָיו רַחֲמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקְרָא מֹשֶׁה לְהוֹשֵׁעַ בִּן נוּן יְהוֹשֻׁעַ״, יָהּ יוֹשִׁיעֲךָ מֵעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְעַבְדִּי כָלֵב עֵקֶב הָיְתָה רוּחַ אַחֶרֶת עִמּוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Joshua** did not go to the graves of the forefathers because **Moses had already prayed for mercy for him, as it is stated: "And Moses called Hoshea son of Nun Joshua [*Yehoshua*]"** (Numbers 13:16), meaning: **God will save you [*Ya yoshiakha*] from the counsel of the spies. And this is** the meaning of **that which is written: "But My servant Caleb, because he had another spirit with him,** and has followed Me fully, him will I bring into the land where into he went" (Numbers 14:24), which implies that Caleb changed his mind over time. Joshua, however, was opposed to the intentions of the other spies from the outset.

Yehoshua did not need to go pray at the graves of the forefathers because Moshe had already prayed on his behalf. The verse states: *va-yikra Moshe le-Hoshea bin Nun Yehoshua* — and Moshe called Hoshea the son of Nun "Yehoshua." The name *Yehoshua* encodes the prayer: *Yah yoshia'acha me-atzat ha-meraglim* — may God save you from the plot of the spies. Moshe's *tefilla* for Yehoshua thus preceded and preempted the need Kalev felt to go seek intercession for himself.

The *Gemara* then returns to the verse *ve-avdi Kalev ekev hayta ruach acheret imo* and notes that the subsequent promise — *va-avi'osiv el ha-aretz asher ba shama* — refers precisely to Chevron, the land to which he came to pray. As Rashi underscores, the Torah does not say he will enter Canaan in general, but specifically the land he went to, which is Chevron.

### Giants Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְשָׁם אֲחִימַן שֵׁשַׁי וְתַלְמַי וְגוֹ׳״, ״אֲחִימַן״ — מְיוּמָּן שֶׁבְּאֶחָיו, ״שֵׁשַׁי״ — שֶׁמֵּשִׂים אֶת הָאָרֶץ כִּשְׁחִתוֹת, ״תַּלְמַי״ — שֶׁמֵּשִׂים אֶת הָאָרֶץ תְּלָמִים תְּלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues to state about Hebron: **"And Ahiman, Sheshai, and Talmai,** the children of Anak, were there" (Numbers 13:22). **Ahiman** was called by this name because he was the most **skilled [*meyumman*] among his brothers. Sheshai** was called by his name **because he would turn the land** that he treaded upon **into ditches [*sheḥatot*]** due to his large dimensions. **Talmai** was called this **because he would turn the land** that he treaded upon **into furrows** upon **furrows [*telamim*]** due to his weight.

The verse records: *ve-sham Achiman Sheshai ve-Talmai yelidei ha-anak* — there were Achiman, Sheshai, and Talmai, the children of the giant. The *Gemara* interprets each name. *Achiman* — *meyuman she-ba-echav* — the most capable among his brothers, the strongest. *Sheshai* — *she-mesim et ha-aretz ke-shechitut* — he caused the earth to collapse beneath him as he walked, leaving deep craters, he was so massive. *Talmai* — *she-mesim et ha-aretz telamim telamim* — he plowed furrows in the earth with each step. A second interpretation, framed as *davar acher*, shifts from etymology to historical record: *Achiman bana Anat*, Achiman built the city of Anat; *Sheshai bana Alash*, Sheshai built Alash; *Talmai bana Talbush*, Talmai built Talbush. As for the phrase *yelidei ha-anak*, the *Gemara* notes that *anak* relates to the word for neck: Rashi explains that their necks appeared to pierce through the heavens — or through the sun itself — so towering was their stature.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: אֲחִימַן בָּנָה עֲנָת, שֵׁשַׁי בָּנָה אָלֻשׁ, תַּלְמַי בָּנָה תַּלְבּוּשׁ. ״יְלִידֵי הָעֲנָק״ — שֶׁמַּעֲנִיקִין חַמָּה בְּקוֹמָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** their names signify another matter: **Ahiman** is the one who **built** the city of **Anat. Sheshai built** the city of **Alush. Talmai built** the city of **Talbush.** The verse describes them as **"the children of Anak"** because they were so tall and large that it appeared **as** if **they were wearing [*ma'anikin*] the sun** as a necklace **due to their height.**

## Hebron's Qualities

### Hebron vs. Zoan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְחֶבְרוֹן שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים נִבְנְתָה״. מַאי ״נִבְנְתָה״? אִילֵימָא נִבְנְתָה מַמָּשׁ, אֶפְשָׁר אָדָם בּוֹנֶה בַּיִת לִבְנוֹ קָטָן קוֹדֶם לִבְנוֹ גָּדוֹל?! דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי חָם כּוּשׁ וּמִצְרַיִם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The continuation of the verse states: **"Now Hebron was built seven years [*shanim*]** before Zoan of Egypt [*Mitzrayim*]" (Numbers 13:22). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"was built"? If we say** that **it was actually built** seven years before Zoan, **would a person build a house for his younger son before** he builds one **for his older son?** Canaan was the youngest son of Ham, **as it is written: "And the sons of Ham: Cush, and Mizraim,** and Put, and Canaan" (Genesis 10:6). How then could Hebron, a city in the land of Canaan, have been built before Zoan, a city in the land of Egypt, occupied by the descendants of Mizraim?

The verse states: *ve-Chevron sheva shanim nivnesa lifnei Tzoan Mitzrayim* — Chevron was built seven years before Tzoan of Egypt. The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mai nivnesa* — what does "built" mean here? If taken literally, it would mean that Chevron as a city was constructed before Tzoan. But this is difficult: Canaan was the youngest son of Cham, while Mitzrayim — after whom Egypt is named — was an older son, as the verse enumerates: *bnei Cham Kush u-Mitzrayim u-Put ve-Chanaan*. Would a father really build a city for his youngest son before his older son? And Tzoan was Mitzrayim's city.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיְתָה מְבוּנָּה עַל אֶחָד מִשִּׁבְעָה בְּצוֹעַן. וְאֵין לְךָ טְרָשִׁים בְּכׇל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל יָתֵר מֵחֶבְרוֹן, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבְרִי בָּהּ שָׁיכְבֵי. וְאֵין לְךָ מְעוּלָּה בְּכׇל הָאֲרָצוֹת יָתֵר מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כְּגַן ה׳ כְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם״, וְאֵין לְךָ מְעוּלָּה בְּכׇל אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם יָתֵר מִצּוֹעַן, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הָיוּ בְצֹעַן שָׂרָיו״, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי חֶבְרוֹן מְבוּנָּה אֶחָד מִשִּׁבְעָה בְּצוֹעַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the meaning of the verse is **that** Hebron **was seven times more fruitful [*mevunna*] than Zoan. And there is no stonier land in Eretz Yisrael than Hebron.** This is evident **because they would bury the dead there,** just as the forefathers were buried there. This was done only in land that was not suitable for agriculture. **And of all the lands, there is none of a higher quality than the land of Egypt, as it is stated: "Like the garden of the Lord, like the land of Egypt"** (Genesis 13:10). **And there was no higher-quality** land **in all of the land of Egypt than Zoan, as it is written** with regard to Pharaoh's ministers, who would certainly have lived on the finest land in the country: **"For his princes were in Zoan"** (Isaiah 30:4). **And even so, Hebron was seven times more fruitful than Zoan.**

The *Gemara* therefore reinterprets: *she-hayta mevunah echad mi-shiva be-Tzoan* — Chevron was seven times more *mevunah* — more built up, more developed — than Tzoan. The point is comparative quality, not chronological construction.

This is where the statement becomes striking. The *Gemara* observes: *ein lecha tarshin be-chol Eretz Yisrael yoter mi-Chevron* — there is no place in all of Eretz Yisrael that is more rocky and barren than Chevron — *de-kivrei bei shichvei* — which is precisely why the dead are buried there, since the ground is not useful for agriculture. Yet *ein lecha me'ulah be-chol ha-aratzot yoter mi-Eretz Mitzrayim* — there is no land in the world more fertile than Egypt, compared to the Garden of God, as the verse says — and *ein lecha me'ulah be-chol Eretz Mitzrayim yoter mi-Tzoan* — and within all of Egypt, there is no more fertile and distinguished region than Tzoan, where the high dignitaries of Israel resided during their dealings with Egypt.

And yet: *afilu hachi*, even so, Chevron is seven times more *mevunah* than Tzoan. The nature of that superiority — rocky Chevron surpassing the most fertile place in the most fertile land — suggests that Rashi's reading, *meyushevet be-peiros*, "settled with fruits," may itself be pointing toward something more than agricultural output. It is perhaps more natural to understand this as a spiritual statement about the quality of the place rather than a claim about its physical productivity.

### Rocky Land Grazing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחֶבְרוֹן טְרָשִׁים הֲוַי? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיְהִי מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה וַיֹּאמֶר אַבְשָׁלוֹם אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ אֵלְכָה נָּא וְגוֹ׳״, וְאָמַר רַב אַוְיָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָן: שֶׁהָלַךְ לְהָבִיא כְּבָשִׂים מֵחֶבְרוֹן. וְתַנְיָא, אֵילִים מִמּוֹאָב, כְּבָשִׂים מֵחֶבְרוֹן! מִינַּהּ, אַיְּידֵי דִּקְלִישָׁא אַרְעָא, עָבְדָה רִעְיָא וְשַׁמִּן קִנְיָינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** the land in **Hebron** in fact **stony? But isn't it written: "And it came to pass at the end of forty years, that Absalom said to the king: I pray, let me go** and pay my vow, which I have vowed to the Lord, in Hebron" (II Samuel 15:7)? **And Rav Avya says, and some say** that it was **Rabba bar bar Ḥanan:** This means **that** Absalom **went to bring sheep** specifically **from Hebron. And it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Menaḥot* 9:3): One must bring the choicest animals to the Temple as offerings. **Rams** are brought **from Moab,** and **sheep** are brought **from Hebron.** This indicates that Hebron has rich land where fat and healthy sheep are raised. The Gemara answers: **From this** very source it can be proven that Hebron is not suitable for agriculture. **Since the earth** there **is thin, it produces** only grass for **grazing and fattens the livestock.**

One might object: is it really true that Chevron is so rocky? The *Gemara* cites a verse — *va-yehi mi-ketz arba'im shanah va-yomer Avshalom el ha-melech elcha na* — and Avshalom, requesting leave to go to Chevron, said he wanted to bring a sacrifice. Rabbi Avyu, and some attribute this to Rabbah bar bar Chana, explains: *Chanan she-halach le-havi kevasim mi-Chevron* — he went to bring lambs from Chevron. And a *baraita* teaches: if you want fine rams, you get them from Moav; if you want the best lambs, you get them from Chevron. This appears to contradict the characterization of Chevron as barren and rocky: how can the best lambs come from such inhospitable terrain?

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction directly: *aidi de-klisha ar'a avda ra'aya, ve-shamin kinyana* — it is precisely because the land is thin and poor that it produces excellent grazing, and so you get fat, prized flocks. Rocky, sparse land is ideal grazing country; the animals forage over wide, lean terrain and produce superior quality. The paradox resolves itself: Chevron's very poverty as agricultural land is what makes it outstanding for flocks. And with that observation, the *Gemara* continues into the next daf.
