# Menachot 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Todah & Measures

### Todah Overview

Daf Ayin Zayin continues a discussion of the specifics of how *menachot* are made, but at this point we move on to the *todah*, which has not received much attention until now. One might even question whether the breads of the *todah* should be called a *minchah* at all — they do not come on their own accord. The same is true of the *omer* and the *shtei halechem*, though the *omer* does come together with its *korban* in a more independent fashion. And beyond that, the *todah* is *kodshei kodashim* and its meat is eaten by the owners throughout all of *yerushalayim*. Still, the breads of the *todah* are very much like a *minchah*, and our *mishnah* turns its attention to them.

The *todah* is essentially a *shelamim* eaten within one day, and it comes accompanied by forty loaves of *challah*, of four different types. One loaf of each type is given to the *kohen*, and the remainder is eaten by the owners throughout *yerushalayim*, just like the meat of the *shelamim*. As for when one brings a *todah* — the *torah* does not specify a particular occasion. It is defined as a thanksgiving *korban* (*im al todah yakrivenu*), and *chazal*, based on *pesukim* in *tehillim*, identify the *arba'ah tzrichim lehodot* — the four who are obligated to give thanks — which is the basis for the practice of reciting *birkat haGomel*, for instance after an overseas journey or recovery from serious illness. The *torah*, however, does not limit the *todah* to those circumstances.

### Five Se'ahs = Six

The *mishnah* at the bottom of *ayin vav amud bet* states: *haTodah hayetah ba'ah chamishah se'in yerushalmiyyot she-hen shishah midbariyyot* — the *todah* (meaning here its accompanying breads) would be brought with five *se'in* of Jerusalem measures, which were six of the wilderness measures.

What happened is this: whatever the measurement of a *se'ah* was at the time of the *torah* — the wilderness measure — when Israel entered the Land, the measures were changed at a certain point. What had formerly been called a *se'ah* and a fifth was now called a *se'ah*. The container was made larger; the same name, *se'ah*, was applied to a bigger quantity. So what used to be six measures in the desert became five of the new measures. An analogy would be the revaluation of the Israeli *shekel*, where the new *shekel* was set equal to ten of the old *shkalim*: the underlying value did not change, only the denomination.

It is important to note that the *se'ah* here refers not to wheat but to actual flour. This is a substantially greater quantity than one might initially assume. Six *se'in* of the original wilderness measure equal *shtei ephot*, because an *ephah* is three *se'ah* — so six original *se'in* constitute two *ephot*. Since an *issaron* is an *asirit ha-ephah*, a tenth of an *ephah*, two *ephot* yield twenty *issronot*.

This stands in contrast to what was learned previously, where one begins with *asarah issronot* of wheat in order to produce one *issaron* of flour. Here, we are speaking of twenty *issronot* of flour — the finished product.

### Se'ah Redefinition

The *mishnah* continues: *asarah le-chametz va-asarah le-matzah* — ten *issronot* for the *chametz* breads and ten for the *matzah* breads. There were forty loaves in total: ten loaves of *chametz* and thirty loaves of *matzah* (three different types of *matzah* of ten loaves each). Yet the division of flour is equal between *chametz* and *matzah*: the ten loaves of *chametz* receive ten *issronot*, and the thirty loaves of *matzah* also receive ten *issronot*. This means that each loaf of *chametz* receives one full *issaron*, while each loaf of *matzah* receives only a third of an *issaron*.

To appreciate the scale: a standard *minchah* consists of one *issaron* of flour, divided into ten loaves. The *omer* is one loaf made from a full *issaron*. The *shtei halechem* is two loaves, each from a full *issaron*. The *todah* is comparable in kind to the *omer* and *shtei halechem* — each of its ten *chametz* loaves is made from a full *issaron*, roughly the equivalent of a five-pound bag of flour. Each *chametz* loaf is thus the size of an entire *minchah*, and since there are twenty *issronot* in total, the *todah* brings twenty times the flour of a single *minchah*.

The *mishnah* then translates these quantities into new Jerusalem measures. The total of five new *se'in* equals thirty *kav* (since each *se'ah* is six *kav*). This total is divided equally: fifteen *kav* for *chametz* and fifteen *kav* for *matzah*. Each *chametz* loaf receives a *kav* and a half — which equals one old *issaron*. For the *matzah*, fifteen *kav* divided among thirty loaves across three types yields five *kav* per type of ten loaves, which is two *challot* per *kav*. All of this is equivalent to what was already expressed in the original measures; the new measures simply use familiar contemporary terms.

A student raised the question of whether the change in measurement meant that people in the time of the *beit hamikdash* were required to bring twenty percent more flour. The answer is no — the underlying amount was identical. The terminology changed, but not the quantity. What we formerly called *shesh se'ah* is now called *chamesh se'ah*, because the container referred to as a *se'ah* has become larger. The *asirit ha-ephah*, the *issaron*, remains a tenth of the *ephah* as defined in the wilderness. One is simply calibrating back to the *midbar* measures when determining the actual quantities involved.

### Se'ah to Ephah

The top of *ayin zayin amud alef* spells out the distribution in greater detail. There are ten *issronot* for the *chametz*, one *issaron* per *challah*. For the *matzah*, there are ten *issronot* divided among three types — *challot*, *rekikin*, and *revuchah* — yielding three and a third *issronot* per type, and since each type has ten loaves, each *matzah* loaf receives a third of an *issaron*. In new Jerusalem measures, each type of *matzah* has five *kav*, or two loaves per *kav*.

## Ephah = 3 Se'ahs

### Chametz & Matzah

The *gemara* returns to the opening line of the *mishnah* — *chamesh se'in Yerushalmiyyot* — and asks: *minah hani mili?* Where does this come from? The question is not simply how we know the flour quantity for the *todah*, but more specifically: how do we know the equivalence stated in the *mishnah* that an *ephah* equals three *se'ah*? This is critical because the *issaron*, the *torah*'s standard unit of flour measurement for *menachot*, is defined as an *asirit ha-ephah* — a tenth of an *ephah*. To translate that into familiar terms, one must know how large an *ephah* is.

Rav Chisda answers by citing a *pasuk* in *yechezkel*: *ha-ephah veha-bat tochen echad yihyeh* — the *ephah* and the *bat* shall have one measure. From this the *gemara* derives: just as a *bat* is three *se'ah*, so too an *ephah* is three *se'ah*. This immediately prompts the question: *u-bat gufah minayin?* And how do we know that a *bat* equals three *se'ah*?

### Matzah Types

The *gemara* considers whether the *bat* can be derived from the *pasuk* in *yechezkel* that states it is a tenth of a *chomer*. If a *chomer* is thirty *se'ah*, then a tenth of it — the *bat* and the *ephah* — would be three *se'ah*. But the *gemara* objects: *chomer lo yadana kama* — we do not actually know how large a *chomer* is. Knowing that both *bat* and *ephah* are a tenth of a *chomer* tells us their relationship to each other, but not their absolute size. So we remain stuck.

Similarly, the *pasuk* that says *va-asirit ha-chomer ha-ephah* tells us the *ephah* is a tenth of the *chomer*, and we already knew the *ephah* equals the *bat*. But if we do not know the size of the *chomer*, we cannot determine the size of the *ephah* from either *pasuk* alone.

### Kav Measures

The *gemara* therefore turns to a different *pasuk*: *ve-chok ha-shemen ha-bat ha-shemen uma-asar ha-bat min ha-kor, ve-ha-kor aseret ha-batim* — a tenth of a *kor* is a *bat*, and ten *batim* make a *kor*. What does this add? The *pasuk* here uses the word *kor* rather than *chomer*, and it tells us explicitly that ten *batim* make a *kor*. This confirms two equivalences: *chomer* and *kor* are the same measure, and *bat* and *ephah* are the same measure. We now know the ratio — the *kor*/*chomer* is ten times a *bat*/*ephah* — but we still need to know the absolute size.

The *gemara* proceeds on the assumption — cited in *rashi* and *tosafot* — that *chazal* had an established tradition that a *kor* equals thirty *se'ah*. With that anchor, the translation is complete: a *kor* is thirty *se'ah*, so a *bat* and an *ephah*, each being a tenth of a *kor*, are three *se'ah*. The *issaron*, a tenth of an *ephah*, is therefore three-tenths of a *se'ah*.

One might note that this *pasuk* from *yechezkel* involves oil — *shemen* — while we are ordinarily concerned with dry measures. The *gemara* tends to be careful in distinguishing *midas lach* from *midas yavash*, yet it uses this *pasuk* as its primary Rosetta Stone for converting *torah* measures into known quantities. The apparent inconsistency is noted but not fully resolved.

What the *gemara* is doing, at its core, is finding a point of translation. The *torah* says *asirit ha-ephah*, the *issaron*. We need to know what an *ephah* is in terms of measures we recognize. *yechezkel* provides the bridge: an *ephah* is a tenth of a *chomer*/*kor*, and a *kor* is thirty *se'ah* — a measure *chazal* knew. Now we can convert *torah* measures into something concrete.

### Measurement Source

The *gemara* having established that an *ephah* is three *se'ah* and an *issaron* is a tenth of an *ephah*, the practical upshot is that an *issaron* equals three-tenths of a *se'ah*. According to contemporary estimates — for instance the conversion chart in Yitzhak Frank's *practical talmudic dictionary* — an *issaron* is approximately 2.49 liters, which is slightly more than a two-liter bottle, and indeed roughly equivalent to a five-pound bag of flour. This is the consistent working estimate used throughout the discussion.

## Shmuel's Sixth Rule

### Ephah-Bat Equivalence

The *gemara* now introduces a famous ruling of Shmuel, one whose primary context is *bava batra* but which is directly relevant here. *amar shmuel: ein mosifin al ha-midot yoter mi-shish* — one may not add to measures by more than a sixth. This addresses precisely the phenomenon described in the *mishnah*: the transition from *midbar* measures to Jerusalem measures. When a community changes what it calls by a given name — for instance, what was formerly called a *se'ah* is now called by the same name but refers to a larger quantity — that change cannot exceed a sixth.

Shmuel adds two parallel rulings: *velo al ha-matbe'a yoter mi-shish* — the same applies to currency; one cannot revalue a *shekel* so that it is more than a sixth larger than the old *shekel*. And *velo ha-mistaker yoter mi-shish* — a person making a profit cannot profit by more than a sixth.

This last point requires careful clarification. *ona'ah* is a *torah* prohibition against overcharging — specifically, charging above or below the established market rate for a commodity. If there is no established rate, or if one is pricing an original creation, there is no *issur* of *ona'ah*. The *ona'ah* framework uses the threshold of a sixth: less than a sixth variance is presumed to be within the range of acceptable fluctuation and is *mochel*; exactly a sixth requires the overcharge to be returned but the sale remains valid; more than a sixth results in *bitul mekach*, annulment of the sale. That is the *ona'ah* framework.

Shmuel's ruling about profit is distinct: it addresses not overcharging relative to a market rate, but the profit margin itself. Even when there is no going rate to violate, Shmuel holds that one may not profit more than a sixth. The straightforward case Shmuel envisions is that of a middleman who purchases goods in one place and sells them in another. Whether and how this extends to creative works or situations without a clear cost basis is a subject that awaits fuller treatment in *bava batra*.

### Chomer-Kor Ratio

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for the limit of a sixth on measure changes? The first proposed explanation is *mishum afkuei tar'a* — out of concern for disruption of the *sha'ar*, the rate of exchange, meaning market prices. The concern is that during a transitional period, not everyone receives the new information simultaneously. If a seller is still quoting prices based on the old *se'ah* and the buyer is measuring with the new, larger *se'ah*, the buyer scoops out more than the seller intended to sell — effectively receiving a double *se'ah* for the price of one old *se'ah*. The resulting confusion could trigger what might look like sudden price doubling or market panic, even though no underlying change in value has occurred.

The *gemara* rejects this explanation: *ee hachi shtus nami lo* — if the concern is that people will panic at the new prices, then even a sixth is too large a change and should also be prohibited.

### Measure Translation

The *gemara* then proposes *mishum ona'ah* as the rationale: the concern is that the confusion between old and new measures will lead to situations of overcharging or undercharging. If the variance is kept to a sixth, it remains within the *ona'ah* framework where the purchase stands and the difference is simply returned. More than a sixth would produce *bitul mekach*.

But this too is rejected, on the basis of Rava's ruling: *kol davar she-be-midah u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan afilu pachas mi-kdei ona'ah chozer* — anything sold by precise measure, weight, or count is annulled even if the variance is less than the *ona'ah* threshold. When a precise measure is agreed upon, any deviation from that measure — however small — constitutes *bitul mekach*, because there is no tolerance for error where exactness is specified. If I agree to sell you exactly one *kav* and you receive a slightly different amount, the sale is void regardless of how close it comes. Therefore, if the concern were *ona'ah*, even a change of less than a sixth would be problematic, and fixing the limit at a sixth would not help.

### Dry-Wet Measures

The *gemara* then proposes the real reason: *mishum tigra* — out of concern for the merchant. The worry is that a merchant who buys goods in one town at the old price and travels to sell them in a town using the new, larger measure will unknowingly sell more goods than he intended for the same price, thereby suffering a loss. By limiting the measure change to a sixth, the worst-case scenario is that his profit margin is somewhat reduced, but he does not incur a net loss.

The *gemara* immediately asks: *revacha lo ba'ei?* But shouldn't he also need to make a profit? If even a small change in the measure can cut into his profit margin, why is a sixth the cutoff? And further — *zavin ve-zavin tigra ikrei?* Can someone who buys and sells without making any money even be called a merchant? Merely avoiding a loss is insufficient if there is no profit.

Ultimately, the *gemara* concedes that none of these explanations adequately accounts for why a sixth specifically is the threshold, as in each case the logic would seem to demand either more or less restriction. This leads to a different approach entirely.

### Isaron-Liter

The *gemara* concludes: *ela amar Rav Chisda, Shmuel kra ashkhach ve-darash* — Shmuel found a *pasuk* and expounded from it. The *pasuk* is again from *yechezkel*: *ha-shekel esrim gerah*, a *shekel* is twenty *gerah*, and then it enumerates — twenty *shkalim*, twenty-five *shkalim*, fifteen *shkalim* — *ha-maneh yiheyeh lachem*. So a *maneh* equals sixty *shkalim*. Since a *shekel* is four *zuz*, this makes a *maneh* equal to 240 *zuz*.

But a *maneh* is ordinarily one hundred — the word *maneh* in *gemara* usage means a hundred *zuz*. The *gemara* therefore observes: *maneh matan ve-arba'in havei?* Does this mean a *maneh* is 240 *zuz*? The resolution is that the measures of the *mikdash* are double those used outside it — a known principle. So a *maneh* of the *mikdash* is double the ordinary *maneh*. Half of 240 is 120. But a *maneh* is 100, so how do you get from 100 to 120?

Here the *gemara* applies the principle of measuring the sixth *milbar* — from the outside, from the final figure. From 100 to 120 is a twenty percent increase of the base. But 20 out of 120 — measuring from the end — is exactly a sixth. So the measure was increased by a fifth of the original amount, which equals a sixth of the final amount. That is the model: you may add up to a sixth measured from the new, larger figure, which corresponds to adding a fifth of the original.

From this single *pasuk* three conclusions are drawn: that the *maneh shel kodesh* was double the ordinary *maneh*; that measures are indeed changed from time to time; and that the change may not exceed a sixth (measured from the outside). The *mishnah* itself then serves as confirmation: *amar Ravina matnitin nami dika* — our *mishnah* is precise on this point. The *todah* used to require *shesh se'in* in the wilderness and now requires *chamesh se'in* of the Jerusalem measure. Five new *se'in* equal six old ones, meaning the new *se'ah* is one-and-a-fifth the old. Adding a fifth of the base is exactly adding a sixth of the new total. *shema mina.*

It is worth noting that while the *pasuk* provides the formal *shiur*, the analysis of the various concerns raised — inflation, *ona'ah*, merchant protection — likely informs the underlying rationale. The *pasuk* sets the ceiling; the concerns explain why a ceiling was needed at all.

## Terumah Division

### Shmuel's Rules

The next *mishnah* introduces the *terumah* of the *todah* loaves: *mikulam haya notel echad me-asarah truma* — from each group of ten loaves, one is taken as *terumah*. The result is that one loaf from each of the four types — one *chametz* and three types of *matzah* — is given to the *kohen*, for a total of four loaves. The *pasuk* says *ve-hikriv mimenu echad mikol korban trumah la-Hashem*: one from each *korban* group is given as *terumah*. From the word *echad* — one — we learn that it must be a whole loaf, not a partial one. From *mikol korban* we learn that all groups must be treated equally: one may not take two loaves from the *chametz* group in lieu of one from a *matzah* group. Each type yields exactly one loaf to the *kohen*. The *kohen* who performed the blood service of the *shelamim* receives those four loaves, and the remaining thirty-six are eaten by the owners throughout *yerushalayim*, as with the meat of the *shelamim*.

### Market Stability

The *beraita* then records a *tanna* asking: *eini yodeia mikamah hi* — from the word *trumah* alone, I do not know the proportion. Is *terumah* one out of ten, one out of fifty, one out of five? The *pasuk* says *ve-hikriv mimenu echad mikol korban trumah la-Hashem*, one from each group — but how large is the group?

### Ona'ah Risk

A *gezeirah shavah* is proposed. The word *trumah* appears here regarding the loaves, and the same word appears in the context of *trumat ma'aser* — the tithe of the tithe that the *levi* separates and gives to the *kohen*. Just as *trumat ma'aser* is one-tenth of the *levi*'s tithe, so here the one loaf is one-tenth of its group. This establishes that each group of ten loaves yields one loaf to the *kohen*, meaning each group contains ten loaves.

The alternative argument considers that *bikurim* are also called *trumah* (*trumat yadecha*), and *bikurim* have no fixed minimum — one may bring as little or as much as one wishes of the first fruits. Perhaps, then, the *terumah* of the *todah* loaves also has no fixed measure.

The *gemara* adjudicates between these two comparisons using the following principle: one should learn a *trumah* that has no subsequent *trumah* from another *trumah* that has no subsequent *trumah*. The loaves of the *todah*: once the one loaf is given to the *kohen*, there are no further *matnot kehunah* owed. Similarly with *trumat ma'aser*: once the *levi* gives it to the *kohen*, no further gifts remain. But with *bikurim*, the owner still owes *terumah* and *ma'asrot* on the remainder of his produce after separating *bikurim*. So we learn from *trumat ma'aser*, not from *bikurim*.

The counterargument proposes learning from *bikurim* based on a different criterion: both the *terumah* of the *todah* loaves and *bikurim* are eaten by *kohanim* in the *beit hamikdash* — the *makom kadosh* — whereas *trumat ma'aser* may be eaten anywhere. The *gemara* resolves this through the shared language *mimenu trumah* appearing in both the *todah* verse and the *trumat ma'aser* verse — a *gezeirah shavah* — which anchors the comparison to *trumat ma'aser* and confirms the one-in-ten proportion.

### Precise vs Imprecise

Having established that each group has ten loaves and that one-tenth is given to the *kohen*, the *beraita* turns to the next unknown: *eini yodeia mikamah hi challah* — I do not know how much flour goes into each *challah* loaf.

The *torah* uses the word *lechem* for the *chametz* loaves of the *todah*, and the same word is used for the *shtei halechem*. The *gezeirah shavah* of *lechem-lechem* suggests that just as each loaf of *shtei halechem* is an *issaron*, so too each *chametz* loaf of the *todah* is an *issaron*.

### Merchant Profit

The alternative comparison is to *lechem hapanim*, where each loaf is two *issronot*. One might argue: learn *lechem* from *lechem hapanim* and require two *issronot* per loaf. The *gemara* presents both sides and then adjudicates.

To learn from *shtei halechem*: the *todah* is (1) *chametz* and (2) accompanies a *korban*. The *shtei halechem* is also *chametz* and also accompanies a *korban*. These two characteristics are a strong basis for comparison. *lechem hapanim*, by contrast, is neither *chametz* nor does it accompany a *korban* — two reasons not to use it as the model.

The counterargument for *lechem hapanim*: the flour used for the *todah* loaves and for *lechem hapanim* may come from anywhere — old or new grain, from inside or outside of *eretz yisrael*. The *shtei halechem*, on the other hand, must come specifically from the new grain of *eretz yisrael*. In that respect, the *todah* resembles *lechem hapanim* more than it resembles *shtei halechem*.

### Maneh Doubling

The *gemara* resolves the ambiguity through an additional *drasha*. The *pasuk* by *shtei halechem* states *mimoshvoteichem tavi'u lechem tnufah*. The word *tavi'u* is deemed superfluous — the verse could simply have said *mimoshvoteichem lechem tnufah*. The extra *tavi'u* teaches that any other bread you bring that is comparable to *shtei halechem* will be like it: *mah l'halan issaron l'challah, af kan issaron l'challah* — just as there each loaf is an *issaron*, so here too. Furthermore, the word *tihyena* in the context of *shtei halechem* — containing the extra *yud*, which stands for ten — teaches that the standard is ten *issronot*, one *issaron* per loaf, rather than two *issronot* for the total pair. With this, we know: ten *chametz* loaves receive ten *issronot*, one per loaf.

For the *matzah* loaves, the *torah*'s phrasing in the *todah* passage places the *matzot* alongside the *chametz* and implies equality: *al chalot lechem chametz* — bringing the *matzot* together with the *chametz* as two equal halves. This establishes *chametz neged chametz havei matzah* — the *matzah* must be equal in total flour to the *chametz*. Since the *chametz* uses ten *issronot*, so too the *matzah* uses ten *issronot*.

### Sixth Calculation

Having established that the *matzah* receives ten *issronot* for thirty loaves — since there are three types and each type has ten loaves — one might think all thirty *matzah* loaves could be of one type. An explicit *pasuk* forecloses this: the *torah* enumerates three distinct categories of *matzah* — *challot matzot blulot ba-shemen*, *rekikei matzot meshuchim ba-shemen*, and *solet murbechet* — and all three must be present.

The totals therefore work out as follows. Ten *issronot* are divided among three types of *matzah*, yielding three and a third *issronot* per type. Since each type has ten loaves, that is three loaves per *issaron*. Combined with the ten *chametz* loaves at one *issaron* each, the *todah* totals twenty *issronot* of flour and forty loaves. From each group of ten, one loaf is given to the *kohen* — four loaves in total — and the remaining thirty-six are eaten by the owners. So concludes the *beraita*, which will be further unpacked in tomorrow's shiur.
