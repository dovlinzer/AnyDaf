# Nedarim 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi's Wedding & Bar Ka…

### Wedding Feast Setup

The Gemara on 50b concludes a narrative concerning a wedding feast that Rabbi — *Rabbeinu Ha-Kadosh*, Yehudah Ha-Nasi — made for his son, Rabbi Shimon Be-Rabbi. As the *meforesh* had already noted, Rabbi conspicuously did not invite Bar Kappara, one of the great *Tannaim* of the generation — a remarkable omission that the story itself helps explain.

The text records that twenty-four million *dinarin* were spent on the wedding hall: *ktav a-beit ginana esrin ve-arba alfin ribo'in dinarin nafkan a-beit ginana dein* — an enormous sum, whether this was an inscription by Rabbi proclaiming his expenditure or graffiti of protest by Bar Kappara himself. Upon learning of the celebration from which he had been excluded, Bar Kappara remarked: *im le-ovrei retzono kach* — if even those who displease him merit such wealth and grandeur, then those who genuinely fulfill God's will should be rewarded all the more. The phrase *ovrei retzono*, those who transgress His will, is notable: Bar Kappara applies it almost ironically to himself, inverting the usual theological register in which it describes those who transgress God's commands, not merely those who have fallen out of favor with Rabbi.

Eventually the two reconciled, and Bar Kappara was invited. At that point he shifted his language entirely: *osei retzono ba-olam ha-zeh kach, le-olam ha-ba al achat kama ve-khama* — those who truly do God's will in this world merit such blessing, and how much more so in the World to Come. The shift from *ovrei retzono* to *osei retzono* marks the resolution of the personal quarrel, though the underlying tension between the two figures becomes clearer as the story continues.

### Suffering & Joking

The Gemara explains the deeper reason Rabbi had been reluctant to include Bar Kappara: *yoma de-michayach bei Rabbi ati pur'anuta le-alma* — any day that Rabbi would smile or take pleasure, suffering would enter the world. This startling statement is explained by the *Rishonim*, particularly with reference to a tradition cited in the *Ein Yaakov* (from *sof perek Yakin Yaakov*), that Rabbi had accepted upon himself personal physical suffering in order to shield others — apparently in the context of a prolonged drought — so that the broader world would be spared. On any day that Rabbi permitted himself to enjoy life, his self-imposed suffering was interrupted, and its protective effect correspondingly lapsed.

This concept is not without theological precedent in *Chazal*: the idea that great *tzadikim* absorb suffering on behalf of their generation appears in various places throughout *Shas*. It differs from the Christian theological framework of vicarious atonement in important structural ways — not least that in the rabbinic model it applies to specific *tzadikim* of the generation rather than to a single universal atoner — though the apparent resemblance is worth acknowledging as a point of theological interest.

Bar Kappara, being something of a *badchan*, a comic and wit, was precisely the sort of person whose company Rabbi had reason to avoid. Knowing this, Rabbi made Bar Kappara an unusual offer: *la tivadchan vehavna lach arba'in grivey chittey* — if you do not make me laugh, I will pay you forty measures of wheat.

### Wheat Joke Attempt

Bar Kappara accepted this arrangement and then proceeded to collect. He took a very large basket, coated it with pitch, carried it upside down on his head, and came before Rabbi demanding the forty measures of wheat: *leiti li Mar arba'in grivey chittey dedarshina bach* — give me, Mar, the forty measures of wheat that I am owed on account of my dealings with you. Rabbi responded: *lav azhartach dela tivadchan* — did I not warn you not to make me laugh? Bar Kappara replied: *chittey dedarshina ka nasivna* — I am only here to take the wheat that was promised to me.

The exact nature of the humor here is somewhat opaque to modern sensibilities. The combination of the oversized basket, the pitch coating, and the upside-down carrying seems to have constituted a kind of visual practical joke — perhaps the point being that he had no intention of actually collecting the wheat and was doing the whole thing in a manner calculated to be absurd, while simultaneously protesting that he was acting in complete seriousness. The joke, like many pieces of ancient humor, may require having been there. In any event, Rabbi apparently found it amusing enough to react, which meant Bar Kappara had technically violated the terms of their agreement — a paradox Bar Kappara was exploiting with evident relish.

### Arayot Derashot

At some point proximate to the wedding — apparently while Ben Elasa, Rabbi's wealthy son-in-law, was also present — Bar Kappara turned to Rabbi's daughter and said: *lemachar shatina chamra berikuda deavuch uvekirkunei deimmach* — tomorrow I will drink wine while your father dances and your mother sings or cackles. This passage has been cited in discussions of *kol isha*, the prohibition on listening to a woman's voice in song: the *Ya'avetz* lists it among passages in *Shas* where *Tannaim* and *Amoraim* appear to conduct themselves in ways that stand in tension with norms accepted as normative by later authorities. The *Meforesh* attempts to read the passage as referring to pouring wine rather than singing, but the *Ran* understands it as singing — and the plain meaning of the language appears to support the *Ran*.

Bar Kappara then proceeded to put his plan into effect. He approached Rabbi with a series of *drashot* on the terminology of *arayot* drawn from Vayikra chapters 18 and 20. The appropriateness of such a topic at a wedding has been questioned, though one possible context is the reading of the *parasha* of *arayot* on *Yom Kippur* afternoon — an occasion described in the last *Mishna* of Masechet Taanit as one of the two happiest days of the year, when young people would go out and engage in flirtatious behavior. Just as *Yom Kippur*, paradoxically a day of great joy, required awareness of *arayot* precisely because of the festive atmosphere, so too at a wedding the topic may have been deemed relevant as a kind of cautionary counterpoint to the celebratory mood.

Bar Kappara asked Rabbi: *mai To'evah* — what does the word *to'evah* mean, as it appears in the context of *arayot*? Every explanation Rabbi offered, Bar Kappara shot down: *kol de-amar lei Rebbe de-hachin hu to'evah parcha Bar Kappara* — every interpretation Rabbi proposed, Bar Kappara rejected. Finally, Rabbi asked Bar Kappara to explain it himself. Bar Kappara then requested that Rabbi's wife bring him a cup of wine, and told Rabbi himself: *kum rakod li* — get up and dance for me. This was the fulfillment of the scenario he had pre-announced to Rabbi's daughter: the father dancing, the mother serving. Having established the stage, he delivered his *derasha*: *de-hachin amar Rachmana to'evah — to'eh ata bah* — the word *to'evah* means "you are making an error with this," a *derashah* on the letters of the word.

After a second cup of wine, Bar Kappara asked: *mai tevel* — what is the word *tevel*, used in the context of bestiality? He again deflected every answer Rabbi offered and insisted Rabbi do as he had done before — dance, and have the wine poured. His explanation: *tevel hu — tavlin yesh bah* — the word *tevel* asks rhetorically whether there are spices in this, whether this act is in some way more enjoyable than any other. The *derasha* frames the Torah's prohibition as a kind of ironic question directed at the would-be transgressor.

A third cup followed, and Bar Kappara asked: *u-mai zimah* — what is the word *zimah*, associated in the *pesukim* of Vayikra with promiscuity and harlotry, the verse *ve-lo tizneh ha-aretz u-malah ha-aretz zimah*. Again the same sequence played out, and Bar Kappara's answer was: *zo mah hi* — this one, who is she? The implication is that *zimah* evokes the consequence of widespread promiscuity — children of unknown parentage — a concern that forms the subject matter of the fourth *perek* of Kiddushin.

### Ben Elasa Leaves

Notably, the Gemara does not say that Rabbi himself grew frustrated or offended. Some observe that Rabbi possessed such a deep love of Torah that he was genuinely engaged by the *derashot*, however oddly timed. It was his son-in-law Ben Elasa who could not tolerate the spectacle: *lo yachil ben alasa lemisbal* — Ben Elasa could not bear it, and so *kam ve-nafak hu ve-ittateih metaman* — he arose and left together with his wife, Rabbi's daughter.

## Ben Elasa's Haircut

### Ben Elasa Identity

The Gemara pauses to ask: who was this Ben Elasa, and what was he known for? The answer comes from a *baraita*: *lo le-chinam pizer ben alasa et ma'otav* — it was not for nothing that Ben Elasa spent his money lavishly. Rather: *ela lehar'ot bahen tisporet shel kohen gadol* — he spent his wealth in order to demonstrate to people what the haircut of the *kohen gadol* looked like.

Some background: there are three categories of figures obligated to maintain distinctive hairstyles. The king must have his hair cut every day, befitting his need to appear dignified and presentable — a rule connected to the verse *yofyecha techzenah einecha* and to the ruling in Yoma that a king may even wash his face on *Yom Kippur*. The *kohen gadol* must have his hair cut once a week, in a specific prescribed style. Regular *kohanim* must have their hair cut once a month.

Ben Elasa, as the son-in-law of Rabbi — who flourished around the year 180 CE, well after the destruction of the *beit hamikdash* — was living in a world where the *kohen gadol's* haircut had not been seen in living memory. His motivation appears to have been a form of preservation and anticipation: by replicating that haircut at great personal expense, he was showing people what it had looked like, and implicitly affirming his expectation that the Temple service would one day be restored. Whether it is halachically proper to replicate the *keilim* or practices of the *beit hamikdash* under current conditions is itself a disputed question — some hold it constitutes an *aveira* — but Ben Elasa's gesture is at minimum a striking expression of *messianic* longing.

The *baraita* cites the verse: *kasom yichsemu et rosheihem* — they shall surely trim their heads — which describes the *kohanim's* distinctive manner of cutting their hair.

### Lulianit Haircut

The *baraita* teaches: *ke-ein lulinit* — it resembles the *lulinit* style. But what exactly is the *lulinit* haircut? *Mai lulinit?* Rav Yehuda — one of the *Amoraim* — did not understand it and asked for a description. Rava explains: *rosho shel zeh betzad ikaro shel zeh* — the tip of one hair lies alongside the root of the next. This appears to describe an extremely close, even cut — somewhat like a very precise buzz cut — in which each hair's tip meets the base of the one beside it, reminiscent of the way the *lulav* or *hadas* is ideally described as growing. Achieving such precision without modern instruments would have been extraordinarily difficult and consequently very expensive — which explains why Ben Elasa needed to spend so much money simply to replicate it.

The *Rif* offers a different interpretation: something more akin to cornrows, where the hair lies in close parallel rows. The name *lulinit* may derive from the Latin word for wool, suggesting a texture or layered appearance — though this remains speculative. Whatever its precise appearance, it was clearly a haircut of elaborate and costly workmanship, and Ben Elasa's willingness to fund it for himself speaks to the depth of his commitment to honoring the memory of the Temple service.

## Tavshil & Dilat

### Tavshil Exception

The *Mishna* had previously stated that among the things exempt from a *neder* against *tavshil* — a cooked dish — are the *beitza* (the very hard-boiled egg whose medicinal character was discussed on the previous daf) and the *dilat haramuza*, a type of gourd or squash. As a point of context, the *Geonim* issued a *cherem* prohibiting the application of medical remedies mentioned in the Gemara, on the grounds that what worked in that era may not work in ours — so the medicinal dimension of the *beitza* is of historical rather than practical interest. The question now before the Gemara is what exactly the *dilat haramuza* is.

### Shmuel vs. Rav Ashi

*Mai dilat haramuza?* — what is this *ramuza* gourd? Shmuel says: *kara karkuzai* — it is the *karkuzai* type of squash, i.e., it is a particular species of gourd identified by that name. Rav Ashi disagrees: *dilat hatmuna beremetz* — it is a gourd buried and pit-roasted in hot coals, and the word *ramuza* refers not to a species but to a method of preparation. On Rav Ashi's reading, the *dilat haramuza* is distinguished from ordinary *tavshil* by its unusual preparation method — it is cooked in embers rather than in a pot — and that is why it is treated as an exception.

### Ravina Refutes Ashi

Ravina challenges Rav Ashi's position: *itvei Ravina le-Rav Ashi*. He cites a passage from Masechet Kilayim in which Rabbi Nechemia rules that the Aramean pumpkin (*dilat arammit*), which is identical to the Egyptian pumpkin (*dilat hamitzrit*), constitutes *kilayim* if cross-bred with the Greek pumpkin (*ha-yevanit*) — and similarly constitutes *kilayim* with the *ramuza* pumpkin. The logic of *kilayim* requires that the two varieties be sufficiently similar to count as the same species for cross-breeding purposes, yet distinct enough that mixing them is prohibited. The first *perek* of Masechet Kilayim catalogs precisely these relationships. The critical point is that *kilayim* terminology only makes sense if *ramuza* is a species name — you cannot have *kilayim* between a method of preparation and a type of squash. This proves that *dilat haramuza* refers to a distinct variety of gourd, not to a preparation method, and Rav Ashi's position is therefore refuted: *tiyuvta*.

By implication, Shmuel's position — that *dilat haramuza* is simply the *karkuzai* squash, another species — is confirmed. In the late *Amoraic* period, disputes between Ravina and Rav Ashi are common; here, unusually, it is Rav Ashi who loses, because Ravina brought a text from Kilayim that Rav Ashi had apparently not accounted for.

## Methods of Preparation

### Tavshil Scope

The *Mishna* being quoted here was actually stated earlier — it is not a fresh *Mishna* introduced at this point, which is itself somewhat unusual, as the Gemara normally would mark such a repetition with *gufa* or a similar indicator. The clause under discussion is: *ha-noder mimaseh kedeirah* — one who takes a *neder* against *maseh kedeirah*, a dish prepared in a pot. The ruling is: *ein assur ela mimaseh retachto* — he is only forbidden to eat something that is the characteristic boiled-up, pot-liquidy result of cooking in a pot.

However, if a person says *amar konam ha-yored likdeirah* — using the formulation "whatever descends into a pot" — then *assur be-khol ha-na'aseh ba-kedeirah*, everything prepared in a pot is forbidden. This distinction is fundamental to the entire *perek*: *maseh kedeirah* is the name of a dish, while *ha-yored la-kedeirah* describes a method of preparation. A *neder* against a dish type applies only to that dish in its characteristic form; a *neder* against a method of preparation applies to anything processed by that method.

### Pot vs. Frying Pan

The *Tosefta* elaborates: if a person took a *neder* against *ha-yored la-kedeirah* — whatever goes into the pot — he is also forbidden to eat *yored la-ilpas*, whatever goes into a frying pan. Why? *Shekevar yarad la-kedeirah kodem sheyarad la-ilpas* — because the pot precedes the frying pan: the food is first parboiled in the pot, and only then finished in the *ilpas* to crisp the exterior. Since the food has already entered the pot in the course of its preparation, a *neder* against the pot catches it at an earlier stage of the process.

The reverse does not hold: if a person takes a *neder* against *ha-yored la-ilpas* — whatever goes into the frying pan — he is permitted to eat whatever was only boiled in a pot, because that preparation never passed through the frying pan. Similarly, *min ha-na'aseh ba-kedeirah* — a *neder* against whatever is *prepared* in a pot — permits eating *na'aseh ba-ilpas*, whatever is prepared in a frying pan, since the frying pan represents a later, distinct stage. And *min ha-na'aseh ba-ilpas* permits *na'aseh ba-kedeirah* for the same reason — the two methods, once separated, do not implicate each other.

The governing principle throughout this entire discussion is *lashon bnei adam* — we follow ordinary colloquial usage. This *perek* is built on the premise that *nedarim* are interpreted according to how people actually speak, not according to technical or textual precision.

### Oven & Bread

The *Tosefta* continues: *ha-noder min ha-yored la-tanur* — one who takes a *neder* against whatever goes into an oven — *ein assur ela be-pat*, is forbidden only regarding bread. The *tanur* of Talmudic times was a conical or cylindrical clay oven, similar to a tandoor, into which flatbread was stuck against the inner walls to bake. While meat could also be roasted in such an oven — as we know from discussions in Pesachim and from the laws of *Shabbat* — the essential purpose and primary use of the oven was bread-baking. Roasting meat in the same oven was, in effect, a secondary use of the heat already generated for bread.

Accordingly, when someone says *yored la-tanur*, what goes into the oven, the natural referent in *lashon bnei adam* is bread, and only bread is forbidden. But if a person says *kol maseh tanur alai* — everything prepared in an oven is forbidden to me — then *assur be-khol ha-na'asim ba-tanur*, he is forbidden everything prepared there, including anything else one might bake or roast in that oven.

## Pickled & Prepared Foods

### Kavush & Shaluk

The *Mishna* on 51b opens with a series of cases about particular preparation methods. First: *min ha-kavush* — if a person takes a *neder* against *ha-kavush*, the pickled thing, using the definite article — *ein assur ela min ha-kavush shel yerak* — he is forbidden only from pickled vegetables. The *Ran* notes the significance of the *hei ha-yedi'ah*: because he said "the pickled," he is referring to the thing that people characteristically pickle, which is vegetables. Had he said simply *kavush* without the article, the *neder* would be broader in scope. This points toward the principle of *stam nedarim le-hachmir, peirushan le-hakel*: where the language is ambiguous, we would need to ask what the person actually meant.

One might observe that what counts as "the" pickled thing varies considerably by community — cucumbers in one setting, radishes, kimchi, or salted turnips in another. Corned beef, for instance, is itself a form of pickled meat. In communities where a wide range of foods are customarily pickled, the referent of *ha-kavush* would need to be determined by local usage. But if a person explicitly says *kavush she-eini to'em* — I will not taste any pickled thing — *assur be-khol ha-kevushim*, every form of pickle is forbidden.

The second case: *min ha-shaluk* — from *shaluk*, a preparation method for meat, perhaps best described as braising or prolonged stewing. Using the definite article again, the *neder* is *eino assur ela min ha-shaluk shel basar* — it applies only to red meat prepared in this manner. But if the person says *shaluk she-eini to'em* — braised I will not taste — then it applies to all braised foods, whether meat, poultry, fish, or vegetables.

### Rav Akha's Challenge

Rav Akha son of Rav Avya raised a challenge to Rav Ashi: *amar dikhvish mai, deshalik mai, detzali mai, demaliakh mai* — what if someone used not the form *ha-kavush* or *kavush*, nor the possessive *neder* formulation, but rather the participial form *dikhvish* (that which has been pickled), *deshalik* (that which has been braised), *detzali* (that which has been roasted), or *demaliakh* (that which has been salted)? When someone uses this formulation — "the thing that was prepared in this manner" — does that mean specifically the item characteristically prepared that way, or does it mean anything prepared in that manner at all?

The Gemara responds: *tiba'ei* — the question stands unresolved. In the language of *nedarim*, this is effectively a *teiku* — a question left without a definitive ruling.

### Tzali & Roasted

The *Mishna* next addresses *tzali*: *min ha-tzali, eino assur ela min ha-tzali shel basar* — from the roasted, a person is forbidden only from roasted red meat. This ruling is attributed specifically to Rabbi Yehuda — *divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — which is somewhat puzzling, since the parallel clauses about *kavush* and *shaluk* are stated anonymously. The attribution to Rabbi Yehuda suggests there may be a *machloket* about this somewhere else in the *sugyot*, though no explicit dispute is evident in this immediate context. As before, if the person says *tzali she-eini to'em* — roasted I will not taste — then *assur be-khol ha-tzluyim*, it applies to anything roasted.

## Fish Nidrei

### Maliakh Fish

The *Mishna* continues: *min ha-maliakh* — from the salted thing. As is well known from other contexts throughout *Shas*, whenever *maliakh* is used without further qualification, it refers specifically to salted fish — the dried, salt-cured variety, something resembling salt cod or another hard-dried salted fish rather than a brined pickle. The distinction between *maliakh* and *kavush* is that *kavush* involves immersion in a brine solution, while *maliakh* involves dry-salting, producing a much harder and more concentrated product that requires soaking in changed water before eating. This is also why consuming very salty fish at certain times of year was considered a health concern. Accordingly: *eino assur ela min ha-maliakh shel dag* — the *neder* applies only to salt-cured fish.

But if a person says *maliakh she-eini to'em* — salted I will not taste — then *assur be-khol ha-melukhim*, it applies to anything salted.

The *Mishna* then addresses a *neder* against fish more broadly: if a person says *dag she-eini to'em* or *dagim she-eini to'em* — fish, I will not taste — *assur bahen bein gedolim bein katanim bein melukhim bein tfelim bein chayim bein mevushalim* — it is forbidden in every form: large or small, salted or fresh, raw or cooked. The inclusion of raw fish is of some halakhic interest: the question of whether *bishul akum* — the rabbinic prohibition on food cooked by non-Jews — applies to sushi has been debated by contemporary *poskim*, since the *gezeirah* was not applied to foods normally eaten raw, and raw fish has in recent years become a normative food category. Some authorities, including material published by the Star-K, have addressed this question, though a range of opinions exists. Rice, which commonly accompanies sushi, has its own complicated status under *bishul akum*, further complicating the analysis.

By contrast: *umutar bit-rayta trufa u-vetzir* — one is permitted to eat the minced or chopped fish dish along with its brine. The precise character of this dish is not entirely clear, but it appears to resemble something like chopped herring — a minced preparation with accompanying liquid — distinct enough from ordinary *dag* to fall outside the *neder's* scope.

### Dag vs. Dagah

A *baraita* introduces the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, which complicates the *Mishna's* apparent equivalence between *dag* and *dagim*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar rules: if a person says *dag she-eini to'em* — using the masculine singular — *assur ba-gedolim umuttar ba-katanim*, he is forbidden to eat large fish but permitted to eat small fish. Conversely, *daga she-eini to'em* — using the feminine or diminutive form — *assur ba-katanim umuttar ba-gedolim*, it is the small fish that are forbidden and the large fish that are permitted. And *dag daga she-eini to'em* — using both forms together — *assur bein ba-gedolim bein ba-katanim*, since the *neder* encompasses both forms, it applies to all fish regardless of size.

This *baraita* thus creates a grammatical distinction between *dag* and *dagah* based on the size of the fish in question: *dag* refers to large fish, *dagah* to small.

### Jonah Fish Story

Rav Pappa poses a challenge to Abaye: *mima'i de-dag she-eini to'em gadol hu* — what is the basis for saying that *dag* in the masculine form refers specifically to large fish? He points to the verse in Sefer Yonah: *vayeman Hashem dag gadol livloa et Yonah* — God prepared a large fish to swallow Yonah, which establishes *dag* as large. But then: *vayitpallel Yonah el Hashem Elohav mimi'ei ha-dagah* — Yonah prayed from within the innards of the fish, and there the word *dagah* is used. How can *dag* mean large and *dagah* mean small if the same fish is described by both words?

The Gemara answers that this apparent contradiction reflects a change in the fish itself: Yonah was initially swallowed by a large fish and remained there for three days without praying. When the fish then became more constricted — whether because God transferred him to a smaller fish or because the fish was otherwise diminished — Yonah found himself in a situation of *eit tzara*, genuine distress, and only then prayed. The *Maharsha* explains the transition differently: God transferred Yonah from a male fish (*dag*) to a female fish (*dagah*) that was filled with eggs, making it more crowded and prompting the prayer. Either reading explains why Yonah's prayer was elicited not by the initial swallowing but by a subsequent change in his situation — a point considered thematically relevant when the book of Yonah is read on *Yom Kippur* at *mincha*, evoking the image of approaching God from a place of constriction and need.

### Dagah Inclusive

Rav Pappa presses further: *dilma paltei dag gadol uvalei dag katan* — perhaps there is no size change at all; rather the large fish simply expelled Yonah underwater, and immediately a smaller fish swallowed him. In this reading there are two distinct fish in the narrative, and the change in terminology reflects a change in the fish rather than a change in its size. This would undermine the proof that *dag* means large and *dagah* means small, since both words in the Yonah narrative would simply describe whichever fish happened to be present.

The Gemara then introduces a different challenge to the *dag/dagah* distinction: *ella ve-ha-dagah asher ba-ye'or meta* — the verse describing the plague of blood says that the fish in the Nile died, using the word *dagah*. If *dagah* means only small fish, were only the small fish of the Nile killed while the large fish survived? Clearly that is not what the *pasuk* intends. The conclusion therefore is: *dagah mashma gedolim u-mashma katanim* — *dagah* is inclusive of all fish, both large and small.

This does not necessarily mean that *dag* cannot refer specifically to large fish — the masculine singular could still carry that meaning. But for practical purposes, the discussion is ultimately resolved on a different basis: *u-venedarim halach achar lashon bnei adam* — in matters of *nedarim*, we follow colloquial usage, and this holds *le-kulei alma*, by all opinions. Whatever the precise biblical semantics of *dag* versus *dagah*, what governs is how ordinary people use these words in their everyday speech. The *pesukim* from Tanach thus serve as evidence only insofar as they shed light on colloquial usage, and when they fail to do so consistently, we simply set them aside in favor of *lashon bnei adam*.

## Tzichna & Conclusions

### Tzichna Question

The *Mishna* states: *ha-noder min ha-tzachana* — one who takes a *neder* against *tzachana* (a form of prepared or pickled fish) — *assur be-tirya trufa*, is also forbidden the chopped minced fish preparation, *umuttar be-tzir u-ve-muryas*, but is permitted the fish brine and the *muryas* (a word of Latin origin meaning fish brine) which is the separately prepared pickling liquid.

On this, Ravina raises a question to Rav Ashi: *amar harei alai tzichin mai* — if a person declared forbidden upon himself *tzichin*, what is the law? Is *tzichin* simply the singular form of *tzachana*, such that one who is forbidden *tzachana* would obviously also be forbidden *tzichin*? Or are they distinct enough that a separate *neder* formulation would yield a different result? The Gemara offers no resolution: *tiba'ei* — the question is left open, a *teiku* on which we close the *daf*.
