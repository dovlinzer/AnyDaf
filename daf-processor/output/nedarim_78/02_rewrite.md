# Nedarim 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hafara & Hatara Basics

### Non-Judicial Status

The sugya opens by revisiting the foundational distinction between *hafara* — annulment of a vow by a husband — and *hatara* — annulment by a sage. Earlier discussion established that the panel performing *hatara* shares certain formal features with a *beit din*: it requires either three individuals or a single *mumche*, an expert of sufficient standing. Yet the *Gemara* makes clear that this body does not function as a proper *beit din* in the adjudicating sense. It may convene at night, its members may stand rather than sit, and relatives (*krovim*) are not disqualified — all departures from ordinary judicial procedure. This non-judicial character is not a peripheral detail; it is central to understanding several practical *halachos* that follow.

### Shabbat Permissibility

One immediate implication of the non-judicial status of *hafara* and *hatara* concerns performance on *Shabbos*. *Hafara* by a husband may always be performed on *Shabbos*, because the husband has only until the end of the day to annul — there is therefore always an immediate need (*tzorech*), and when there is a need it does not matter by what formal arrangement it is performed. *Hatara* may similarly be performed on *Shabbos* when it is needed for the sake of *Shabbos* itself (*letzorech Shabbos*). Moreover, precisely because the act of *hatara* is not a *maaseh beit din* in the formal judicial sense, there is no prohibition on performing it with three individuals rather than one, since even when three people act together they are not constituting a formal *beit din*. The non-judicial character thus dispenses with the concern that would otherwise make such an act on *Shabbos* problematic.

### Standard Language

The *Gemara* turns to the appropriate language for annulment, particularly on *Shabbos*. A man should not say to his wife on *Shabbos* the standard weekday formula — *mufar lichi*, *batul lichi*, "it is annulled to you, it is void to you" — as one would ordinarily say during the week. Rather, he should say to her: *tli ve-ochli*, *tli ve-shtei* — "take and eat, take and drink" — and the *neder* is automatically void (*veneder batel meilav*). This is instructive on two levels. First, it establishes what the standard, direct language of annulment is. Second, it demonstrates that even in cases where a more direct formulation would be appropriate, indirect language can suffice when circumstances call for it — in this case, the concern about performing an overtly legal verbal act on *Shabbos*.

### Intent Requirement

Rabbi Yochanan adds a critical qualification: *vetzarich sheyivatel belibo* — it is not sufficient to utter the indirect words alone; the husband must simultaneously hold in his heart an intention to annul (*bitul belibo*). The words spoken need not themselves be words of *bitul*, but the inner intention must be genuinely present. This formulation is reminiscent of the *bitul* of *chametz* before Pesach, where one who cannot perform *biur chametz* may instead annul it in the heart (*mevatel belibo vedaio*), rendering it ownerless. The parallel invites reflection, though the cases are distinct. Critically, as the *Ran* emphasizes and as the *Gemara* will clarify more explicitly, purely internal *bitul* without any accompanying verbal act would be insufficient. What is required is a combination: words that, while not explicit declarations of annulment, implicitly convey it — together with genuine inner intention. Neither element alone is adequate.

## Shammai vs. Hillel

### Shammai Position

A *baraisa* presents an explicit dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel on this question. Beit Shammai holds that on *Shabbos*, a husband should annul *belibo* — with inner intention accompanied by indirect language, as described above — but that on a weekday he must be more explicit, articulating the direct formula *mufar lichi*, *batul lichi*. The *Ran* clarifies that even Beit Shammai does not mean the husband annuls by thought alone on *Shabbos*; rather, the indirect verbal act combined with inner intention, as described in the previous passage, is what Beit Shammai permits when circumstances prevent direct articulation.

### Hillel Position

Beit Hillel holds that the same indirect approach suffices both on *Shabbos* and during the weekday: *echad ze ve-echad ze mevatel belibo ve-ein tzarich lehotzi bisfasav* — in both cases he annuls in his heart without needing to articulate the formula explicitly. On its face this sounds as though Beit Hillel requires no verbal act whatsoever, only internal intention. Yet as the *Ran* insists, and as a later passage in the *Gemara* makes clear, pure internal *bitul* without any verbal component would not be effective. Beit Hillel's position is therefore that the indirect verbal act is always sufficient — it is not a special dispensation limited to *Shabbos* — while Beit Shammai would prefer direct articulation when possible and treats the indirect form as an accommodation to *Shabbos* rather than an equally valid alternative.

### Heart Intent Synthesis

This raises the question of whether Beit Shammai would validate indirect annulment on a weekday after the fact (*bedi'avad*). It is reasonable to conclude that Beit Shammai would — their position is that direct language is preferred when there is no obstacle, not that indirect language is categorically invalid. Beit Hillel, by contrast, simply does not distinguish between the two modes: if it works, it works, regardless of when and under what circumstances it is employed. What is clear from both positions is that there is an irreducible combination at work: implicit verbal expression and genuine inner intention operating together. And of course, if one does say *mufar lichi* explicitly, that simultaneously fulfills whatever inner intention is required — the explicit formula carries the inner meaning by definition.

### Meifer vs. Mevatel

Checking the *Rambam* on this point reveals a striking distinction that requires closer examination. In *Hilchos Nedarim* 13:7 (*yud gimel*, *zayin*), the *Rambam* rules as follows: a husband who performs *hafara* of his wife's or daughter's vow must articulate it verbally — if he performs *hafara* only in his heart, it is not effective. But one who performs *bitul* — the annulment from a different angle — does not need to articulate it verbally; *bitul belibo* alone suffices, and the *neder* is thereby void. The *Rambam* in the passage there appears to treat *meifer* and *mevatel* as two distinct processes with differing requirements. The standard formula often cited — *mufar lichi*, *batul lichi* — may in fact be the *Rambam*'s way of referring to two separate acts, one of *hafara* and one of *bitul*, with different formal requirements for each. This distinction deserves further analysis, but at minimum the *Rambam* agrees with the *Ran* that some combination of verbal act and inner intention is operative, even if he parses the components differently.

## Language Specificity

### Explicit vs. Implicit

The *Ran* raises a pointed question. Earlier in the *Gemara*, a case was discussed in which a husband says to his wife: *konam, nedarim shetiduri, i efshi shetiduri* — "I do not wish you to make such vows" — or *ein ze neder* — "this is not a vow." The *Gemara* ruled that such language is ineffective: *lo amar klum*, he has said nothing of consequence. But if the *Ran* just established that indirect language is sufficient provided the husband has *bitul belibo*, why did that earlier formulation fail? After all, it too is a form of indirect expression.

### Vow Negation Problem

The resolution lies not in the directness of the language per se but in whether the language is *implicitly* an act of annulment at all. Consider the phrase "go ahead and eat it." If a wife has made a vow forbidding herself from eating something and her husband tells her "go ahead and eat it," there is only one coherent way to understand that instruction: the husband must have annulled the vow, because otherwise his instruction would be meaningless. The implication of annulment is thus inherent in and inseparable from the statement. By contrast, if the husband says "this is not a vow," he is making a factual claim — and a false one at that, since it plainly is a vow. He is not saying "I am making it not a vow" or "I am annulling it"; he is asserting a state of affairs that does not exist. Similarly, "I do not wish you to make vows" is a statement about his preferences, not an act of annulment. It leaves entirely open whether he intends to annul or merely to express displeasure. A statement that communicates annulment implicitly must actually communicate annulment — not every indirect locution accomplishes that. The crucial requirement, therefore, is not merely that the language be indirect but that the indirect language unambiguously implies the act of annulment. A statement of factual negation or personal preference does not clear that bar.

## Wrong Language Rule

### Language Swapping Invalid

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *chacham she-amar bi-leshon ba'al u-va'al she-amar bi-leshon chacham lo amar klum* — if a sage employs the language appropriate to a husband, or a husband employs the language appropriate to a sage, neither has accomplished anything. The case contemplated is not one of confusion about which process to perform; rather, it is a case where the actor knows what process he intends but uses the wrong terminology. A sage conducting a *hatara* who says *mufar lach* instead of the appropriate language of the sage has not effected *hatara*. A husband performing *hafara* who says *mutar lach* has not effected *hafara*. This ruling emphasizes that verbal articulation is not merely instrumental — it is constitutive of the act. Wanting to do the right thing while saying the wrong thing is insufficient.

One might wonder whether the principle of *yad la-neder* — where language that partially and ambiguously gestures toward a neder is nonetheless treated as binding — could apply here in reverse, validating an act whose language approximates but does not match the required formula. The answer appears to be no, precisely because the husband and the sage are performing fundamentally different processes. The *ba'al*'s act of *hafara* operates going forward — it dissolves an existing vow from the moment of annulment onward. The *chacham*'s act of *hatara* operates retroactively — it treats the vow as though it never existed. These are not merely formally different; they are conceptually opposed. Using the other's language does not create an approximate version of one's own process; it describes the other process entirely. There is no room for a *yad* construction when the language accurately describes something real, just the wrong thing.

### Torah Derivation

A *baraisa* grounds this in the *pasuk* from *Mattos*: *zeh hadavar asher tzivah Hashem — ish ki yidor neder* — "this is the matter that God commanded — a man who takes a vow." The phrase *zeh hadavar* — "this is the matter" — is read restrictively: *chacham matir ve-ein ba'al matir*, only the *chacham* performs *hatara*, not the husband. Now, one might have argued the contrary by a *kal va-chomer*: if a *chacham*, who lacks the power of *hafara*, can nevertheless perform *hatara*, then surely a *ba'al*, who possesses the greater power of *hafara*, should a fortiori be able to perform *hatara* as well. It is something of an irony — the husband's unique power is invoked as the basis for expanding his authority into the sage's domain. Nevertheless, *talmud lomar zeh hadavar*: no, only the *chacham* performs *hatara*, and not the husband.

### Hafara Authority

A second *baraisa* derives the mirror proposition. *Zeh hadavar* — again read restrictively — establishes that *ba'al meifer ve-ein chacham meifer*: only the husband can perform *hafara*, and the *chacham* cannot. Here too a *kal va-chomer* suggests itself in the other direction: if the husband, who lacks the power of *hatara*, can nevertheless perform *hafara*, then surely the *chacham*, who possesses the power of *hatara*, should a fortiori be able to perform *hafara*. Again, *talmud lomar zeh hadavar*: no. Each authority is confined to its own domain. The *Gemara* notes that these derivations do not prove quite as much as Rabbi Yochanan's rule might require — they establish that the actual *processes* are exclusive, not necessarily that using the other's language while performing one's own process would fail. But the broader point stands: since the two processes are conceptually distinct and the *devar* that defines each is specific to its actor, using the language that describes the other's process cannot constitute one's own.

### Ran Language Analysis

This raises a practical question: what exactly is the language of the *chacham*? The language of the *ba'al* is explicit in the *Torah* — *va-im hafer yafer osah me-ishah*, the language of *hafara* belongs to the husband. But where does the *Torah* specify that a *chacham* says *mutar lach*?

The *Ran* addresses this at the end of 77b. His first answer draws from the verse *lo yachel devaro* — "he shall not profane his word." From here we learn the role of the *chacham*: others can render his word *chullin*, ordinary and non-binding. Since *chullin* is the category of the non-sanctified and unrestricted, the term *mutar* — permissible — is the apt language for what the *chacham* does. This answer, as the *Ran* himself concedes, is not entirely persuasive.

At the very top of 78b, however, the *Ran* cites the *Yerushalmi* for a more compelling resolution. The *Yerushalmi* records: *ha-ba'al omer mufar lach, ve-ha-zaken omer ein kan neder ein kan shvu'a* — the husband declares "it is annulled to you," while the elder declares "there is no vow here, there is no oath here." This formulation clarifies the conceptual logic. *Mufar* — from *meifer* — means dissolved going forward: the vow existed and is now annulled from this point onward. *Mutar* — and equivalently *ein kan neder* — means it was always permissible; there never was a binding vow. This is the nature of the *chacham*'s act: *hatara* renders the vow as though it never came into existence. And this is precisely why the husband cannot use that language — to say *ein kan neder* or *mutar lach* is to claim that the vow never existed, but the husband's role presupposes that a vow did exist and is now being dissolved prospectively. He cannot retroactively undo its existence; he can only cut off its future effect.

This analysis casts light on the *nusach* of *hataras nedarim* recited on *erev Rosh Hashanah*. The formula includes *hakol mutar lach, hakol sharui lach* — *mutar* and its Aramaic equivalent *sharui*, both indicating that the thing was always permissible. It also includes *hakol mechulin lach*, which carries a somewhat different connotation — *mechila* suggests erasure of something that existed, somewhat closer in spirit to *hafara*. Whether *mechulin* was always part of the original *nusach* may be worth investigating. And crucially, the formula also includes *ein kan lo neder ve-lo shvu'a ve-lo nezirus ve-lo cherem ve-lo issur ve-lo konam* — there is no vow here, no oath, no *nezirus*, no *cherem*, no prohibition, no *konam*. All of these are the language of the *chacham*: retroactive annulment treating the original commitment as though it never took legal hold.

## Three Laypeople Source

### Zeh HaDavar Link

The *baraisa* continues with a *gezeira shava* — a textual analogy based on a shared phrase. The phrase *zeh hadavar* appears in *parshas Mattos* in the context of *nedarim*, and it appears again elsewhere in the *Torah* in the context of *shchutei chutz* — the prohibition against slaughtering a consecrated animal outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. The *baraisa* proposes linking them: just as by *shchutei chutz* the *Torah* states *daber el Aharon ve-el banav ve-el kol b'nei Yisrael* — applying the law to Aharon, his sons, and all of Israel — so too, *parshas nedarim* applies to all of Israel, not only to *rashei ha-matos*. And conversely, just as *parshas nedarim* emphasizes *rashei ha-matos* — heads of the tribes — so too *shchutei chutz* must be understood to involve *rashei ha-matos*.

### Three Hedyotos Valid

What does it mean for *parshas nedarim* to extend to *kol Yisrael* beyond *rashei ha-matos*? Rav Acha bar Yaakov explains: *lehachshir shlosha hedyotos* — to validate three laypeople as a qualified panel for *hataras nedarim*. The *parsha* focuses on *rashei ha-matos*, which establishes that a *yachid mumche* — a single qualified individual of sufficient stature — may annul vows. The inclusion of *kol Yisrael* then signals a second, broader category: ordinary people, provided there are three of them. The logic of choosing three, as the *Ran* explains, is that once we know one individual is not enough in this second category, the next coherent institutional form is that of a *beit din*, which requires three.

But this conception of a *beit din* deserves careful definition. The body that performs *hataras nedarim* is not a *beit din* in the adjudicating sense — it may convene at night, it may include relatives, its members need not be seated. How then is it meaningful to call it a *beit din*? Rav Soloveitchik observed in a number of contexts that *beit din* does not always function as an adjudicating body. The *Sanhedrin* that goes out in the case of *eglah arufah* and declares *yadeinu lo shafchu es hadam hazeh* is not adjudicating anything — it is representing the Jewish people. Similarly, when *beit din* sanctifies the new month or proclaims a leap year, it acts not as a court but as a representative leadership body. *Rashei ha-matos* is itself a leadership role, not a juridical one. The institution of *beit din*, understood as a representative leadership body rather than a judicial one, is the natural parallel. This may also explain why it is three specifically: not because three *dayanim* are needed, but because three is the constitutive number for a recognized communal body, and the *beit din* in its representative — as distinct from its adjudicating — capacity provides that form. The *Torah* elsewhere refers to the *beit din* using different terms at different points — *zekenim*, *shoftim*, *Elohim* — and it is the *zekenim* formulation, connoting communal leadership rather than strict adjudication, that applies here.

### Shchutei Chutz Link

Returning to the *gezeira shava*: what role do *rashei ha-matos* play in the context of *shchutei chutz*? Rav Sheshet explains: *lomar she-yesh she'eila b'hekdesh* — to teach that *rashei ha-matos* can perform *she'eila* regarding *hekdesh*, that is, a *chacham* can annul a consecration (*hekdesh*) just as he can annul a *neder*. This creates a significant practical opening: if someone slaughtered a consecrated animal outside the *Beis HaMikdash* and is thereby liable to *kareis*, he may go to a *chacham* and argue that had he been aware of certain circumstances he would never have consecrated the animal. The *chacham* performs *hatara* on the original act of *hekdesh*, which retroactively undoes the consecration, rendering the animal ordinary (*chullin*). The slaughter then turns out to have been of an unconsecrated animal, and no *aveira* was committed at all.

This, however, is the position of Beit Hillel, which holds that *she'eila* — the process of retroactive annulment on grounds of error — applies to *hekdesh*. Beit Shammai disagrees: *ein she'eila b'hekdesh*. Even if one consecrated an animal in error, the *hekdesh* is binding. Since the entire mechanism of *hatara* works by reclassifying the original act as having been made in error (*b'to'us*), and Beit Shammai holds that even *hekdesh b'to'us* is fully valid, Beit Shammai denies that *rashei ha-matos* play any role by *shchutei chutz*. Consequently, for Beit Shammai the *gezeira shava* linking *parshas nedarim* and *shchutei chutz* does not hold — they do not derive the law of three *hedyotos* from this source.

### Moshe Derivation

For Beit Shammai, then, where do three *hedyotos* come from? The *Gemara* answers: *nafka lehu mi-deRav Assi bar Nasn* — from the teaching of Rav Assi bar Nasn. The *pasuk* states: *vayedabeir Moshe es moadei Hashem el b'nei Yisrael* — "and Moshe spoke the appointed times of God to the children of Israel." A *baraisa* records a dispute. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili says: the *pasuk* speaks of the *moadim* and does not mention *Shabbos Bereishis* alongside them — which is puzzling, since in *parshas Emor* where the *moadim* are listed, *Shabbos* appears first, *sheshes yamim* and so on. Rebbi (*Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi*) says: the *pasuk* speaks of the *moadim* and does not mention *parshas nedarim* alongside them — which is equally puzzling, given that *parshas nedarim* in *Mattos* follows immediately after the *moadim* of *Pinchas*.

Rav Assi bar Nasn was troubled by this *baraisa* and went to find Rav Sheshet to ask him about it. He traveled first to Naharda'ah and, not finding Rav Sheshet there, followed him to Mechuza.

### Moadim Requirement

Rav Sheshet resolved the difficulty. The *baraisa* is not making a claim about what appears adjacent in the text; it is drawing a *halachic* distinction. *Moadei Hashem tzrichim kiddush beit din* — the appointed times require sanctification by *beit din*. The *moadim* are proclaimed through *kiddush hachodesh*, the *beit din*'s sanctification of the new moon. *Shabbos Bereishis ein tzricha kiddush beit din* — *Shabbos* requires no such sanctification; it has its sanctity independently, without any action by *beit din*. The verse *asher tikre'u osam bemo'adam* — "that you shall proclaim in their appointed time" — applies only to those occasions whose timing is declared by *beit din*, not to *Shabbos*.

Similarly — and this is the key point for our topic — *moadei Hashem tzrichim mumche*: the *moadim* require an expert panel, specifically a *beit din* of *mumchim*, for the sanctification of the new moon. *Ve-ein parshas nedarim tzricha mumche*: but *parshas nedarim* — the process of *hataras nedarim* — does not require a *mumche*-level panel. The word *eileh* — "these" — in *eileh moadei Hashem asher tikre'u osam* is restrictive: only *these* require a *beit din* of *mumchim* for their proclamation. *Shabbos* does not, and *parshas nedarim* does not. From here Beit Shammai derives that *hataras nedarim* is valid even when performed by *hedyotos*, laypeople without expert credentials.

The *Gemara* then raises the obvious difficulty: but *parshas nedarim* says *rashei ha-matos*, which implies people of stature and expertise — so how can laypeople suffice? The answer, attributed to Rav Chisda and alternatively to Rabbi Yochanan: *b'yachid mumche* — ​*rashei ha-matos* refers to the case of a *yachid mumche*, a single individual of sufficient standing. When it is a *yachid*, he must be a *mumche*. But the separate teaching about *kol Yisrael* — and for Beit Shammai, the teaching from *eileh* — establishes the alternative: three *hedyotos* without individual expert status.

## Mumche Definition

### Mumche Relativity

This raises the critical question of who qualifies as a *mumche* for the purpose of a *yachid* performing *hataras nedarim*. One might suppose that the *mumche* required here is parallel to the *mumche* required for *kiddush hachodesh* — a member of a *beit din* whose authority derives from an unbroken chain of *semicha* going back to Moshe Rabbeinu. On that reading, no *yachid mumche* could perform *hataras nedarim* today, since the chain of *semicha* has been severed and such a *beit din* no longer exists.

The *Rambam*, however, rejects this interpretation. In *perek* 12 of *Hilchos Shevuos*, the *Rambam* rules that any *rav muvhak* — a distinguished authority — may perform *hataras nedarim* as a *yachid*. The *mumche* required here is not the same *mumche* as the one required for *kiddush hachodesh*. The power to sanctify the new month derives from a specific authority transmitted from Moshe Rabbeinu through an unbroken chain; the power to annul vows as a *yachid* derives from something different — expertise, knowledge, standing in the realm of *halacha* — and that kind of *mumche* exists in every generation. There is no requirement of *semicha* in the technical genealogical sense.

### Knowledge Minimum

But what exactly is the knowledge threshold, and does it apply differently to a panel of three than to a *yachid*? Here one must distinguish between the requirements for a *yachid mumche* and the minimum competence required even for a *beit din* of three. The *Ran* asks pointedly: if laypeople suffice, why can one not convene three people who are entirely ignorant of the laws of *nedarim*? The answer, which the *Ran* derives from other passages in the *Gemara* — discussed earlier in the tractate — is that some minimum knowledge is required even from a panel of three. This is not a formal credential requirement but a functional one: if the members of the panel do not know what they are doing, they are not performing *hataras nedarim* at all. The act of *hatara* requires analysis — identifying a *petach* or a *charata*, understanding why the vow would not have been made had certain circumstances been known, and declaring the vow dissolved on that basis. A panel that mechanically recites words without any comprehension of the process is not conducting *hatara*; it is performing a meaningless verbal exercise.

The practical minimum for a panel of three is therefore that at least one member must genuinely understand the process and be capable of explaining it to the others. The other two must at minimum be able to understand the explanation when it is given — they need not be *gamir*, knowing the law independently, but they must be *savir*, capable of following reasoning and understanding the substance of what they are affirming. One must know and explain; the others must be able to understand. Below that threshold, the panel's declaration is without legal effect.

### Gadol HaDor Requirement

The *Ran* takes a stronger position regarding the *yachid mumche*. Citing *Rabbeinu Shmuel* in *perek Yayin Nesech*, the *Ran* notes that a *yachid mumche* must be *baki b'Gemara*, a thorough expert, *klomar rav muvhak* — that is, a distinguished rabbinic authority. But the *Ran* presses further. If being a *mumche* means simply possessing expert knowledge of the relevant laws, why does a *Gemara* elsewhere entertain the possibility that Rav Huna might not have the standing to annul a *neder* as a *yachid*? Rav Huna was certainly a great scholar; if expertise alone sufficed, he would plainly qualify. The *Ran* concludes from this that *mumche* for the purpose of *yachid* annulment requires more than expert knowledge. The *Torah* says *rashei ha-matos* — the heads of the tribes — and this implies that the annulment of vows was entrusted to those who are *yoshvim u-frukkim she-ba-dor*: the preeminent figures of their generation. It is not enough to be well-versed in the laws; one must be counted among the *gedolim* of the generation.

This produces a three-tiered analysis. The first possibility — that one must have *semicha* traceable to Moshe Rabbeinu, like the *beit din* of *kiddush hachodesh* — is rejected; that standard would make the role practically impossible and is not what the *Torah* intends here. The second possibility — that any expert scholar suffices — is also rejected by the *Ran*, since that would not explain the *Gemara*'s doubt about Rav Huna. The correct standard, according to the *Ran*, is a relative one: the *yachid mumche* must be the preeminent rabbinic authority of his generation, the *gadol ha-dor* in a real sense. This is a demanding but not impossible standard. Every generation has its *gedolim*, and among them, those at the very top are the ones who possess the standing to annul vows as a *yachid*. It is not an absolute metric but a generational one.

## Modern Application

### Rosh Hashanah Question

This analysis gives rise to a serious practical question about the widespread custom of *hataras nedarim* performed on *erev Rosh Hashanah*. The ceremony is conducted publicly, with groups of people serving as the panel before whom the petitioner declares nullification of vows made over the past year. Often the participants — both those serving as the *beit din* and those seeking *hatara* — have limited familiarity with the relevant laws and proceedings. Given the standards just established — that even a panel of three requires at minimum one member who genuinely understands the process, with the others capable of following the explanation — it is legitimate to ask whether the Rosh Hashanah ceremony typically meets halachic requirements.

### Petach vs. Charata

The ceremony as commonly practiced does not proceed through the formal *petach* model of *hatara*, in which the *chacham* identifies a specific new consideration that, had the person known it at the time of making the vow, would have caused him not to take it on at all — thereby establishing that the vow was made in error. Instead, the Rosh Hashanah ceremony operates primarily on the model of *charata* — remorse — where the petitioner expresses regret over vows made, and the panel affirms the nullification on that basis. There is a question among *poskim* as to whether the Rosh Hashanah practice constitutes a genuine *hataras nedarim* according to the stricter understanding, or whether it functions primarily as a *mesirat moda'ah* — a declaration of intent for the future — signaling that vows one may inadvertently take in the coming year should not be considered binding.

If *charata* alone suffices without going through the *petach* process, and if the *charata* model requires a less intensive analytical engagement, one might argue that the bar of *gamir ve-savir* is lower in that context, and that the ceremony thus has greater validity than the stricter analysis might suggest. Nevertheless, the concern remains that participants who do not understand the words being recited — or who recite them in a language they do not comprehend — are not meaningfully engaged in any process at all. The correct practice is for the words to be said in whatever language the participant understands. The broader question of whether the Rosh Hashanah ceremony reliably accomplishes genuine *hataras nedarim* is, by honest assessment, an open and serious one.
