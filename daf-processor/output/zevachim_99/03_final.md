# Zevachim 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fitness to Eat

### Rav Yosef's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הַכֹּהֵן הַמְחַטֵּא אֹתָהּ יֹאכְלֶנָּה״ – כֹּהֵן הַמְחַטֵּא יֹאכַל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחַטֵּא אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל. וּכְלָלָא הוּא?! וַהֲרֵי מִשְׁמָרָה כּוּלָּהּ – דְּאֵין מְחַטְּאִין, וְאוֹכְלִין! רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי קָאָמְרִינַן. הֲרֵי קָטָן – דְּאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי, וְאוֹכֵל! אֶלָּא מַאי ״יֹאכְלֶנָּה״ – יְחַלְּקֶנָּה. רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי – חוֹלֵק, שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי – אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק. הֲרֵי בַּעַל מוּם – דְּאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְחִיטּוּי, וְחוֹלֵק! בַּעַל מוּם רַחֲמָנָא רַבְּיֵיהּ: ״כׇּל זָכָר בַּכֹּהֲנִים״ – לְרַבּוֹת בַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that a priest who is unfit for the Temple service does not receive a share of the sacrificial meat. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Reish Lakish said:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states** about a sin offering: **"The priest who effects atonement shall eat it;** in a sacred place shall it be eaten, in the court of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 6:19). This teaches that only **a priest who effects atonement** by performing the rites of the offering **shall partake** of its meat, but a priest **who does not effect atonement does not partake** of its meat. The Gemara challenges: **And is** this an established **principle? But** there are **all the** priests of the **priestly watch** of that week in the Temple, **who do not effect atonement** for that offering, because the blood of a specific sin offering is presented by just one priest, **and** yet they all **partake** of its meat. The Gemara explains: **We** mean to **say** that any priest who is **fit for effecting atonement** may partake of it, even one who did not participate in the service. The Gemara objects: **But** there is the case of **a minor, who is unfit for effecting atonement, and** who nevertheless **partakes** of sacrificial meat. The Gemara explains: **Rather, what** is meant by the term: **"Shall eat it"?** It means that he **shall receive a share of it.** The *halakha* is therefore that a priest who is **fit for effecting atonement receives a share** of the meat, but a priest **who is unfit for effecting atonement does not receive a share** of the meat. Minors do not receive a share, though they may partake of meat given to them by others. The Gemara objects: But there is **a blemished priest, who is unfit for effecting atonement, and** yet he **receives a share** of its meat. The Gemara replies: **The Merciful One included a blemished** priest as an exception, as the verse that states: **"Every male among the priests** shall eat it" (Leviticus 6:22), serves **to include a blemished** priest.

The *mishnah* with which we have been engaged establishes a general rule: a *kohen* who was able to perform the *avodah* during the day is entitled to a portion of the sacrificial meat that night; one who was not able to do so receives no portion. The *ba'al mum* — a *kohen* with a permanent physical blemish — appears as an exception. The *mishnah* derives this from the verse *HaKohen HaMechattei Otah Yochalennah* ("the *kohen* who performs the sin-offering shall eat it"), reading *mechattei* not as the one who personally performed the offering but as the one who is *fit* to perform it, and reading *yochalennah* — "shall eat it" — as denoting entitlement, i.e., receipt of a portion. The *ba'al mum* is then included via an additional verse, *kol zachar bakohanim yochal otah*, on the grounds that, although he can never perform the *avodah*, at the time the *avodah* was being performed he at least possessed the capacity to eat from the meat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: מִכְּדֵי מַאי ״יֹאכְלֶנָּה״ – יְחַלְּקֶנָּה; לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״יְחַלְּקֶנָּה״! מַאי ״יֹאכְלֶנָּה״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה – חוֹלֵק, שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה – אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** another explanation: **Now what** is meant by the term: **"Shall eat it"?** It means: He **shall receive a share of it.** But if so, **let the Merciful One write: Shall receive a share of it. What** is the reason for writing: **"Shall eat it"? Learn from it** that only a priest who is **fit for partaking** of sacrificial meat, which includes a blemished priest, **receives a share** in the meat; but a priest **who is not fit for partaking** of sacrificial meat, e.g., one who immersed that day, **does not** receive a **share** in the meat.

Rav Yosef sharpens this analysis considerably. He asks: given that we have already determined that *yochalennah* means entitlement to a portion rather than merely permission to eat, why did the Torah not simply write *yechalkennah* — "shall receive a portion"? The deliberate use of the language of eating, Rav Yosef argues, is a *drasha*: *shema mina ra'uy l'achilah cholek she'eino ra'uy l'achilah eino cholek* — one who was fit to eat at the time of the *avodah* receives a portion; one who was not fit to eat does not. The operative criterion is not fitness to perform the *avodah* but fitness to eat at the time the *avodah* was performed. This reorients the entire framework. The *ba'al mum* is not an exception to a rule grounded in fitness for *avodah*; rather, he is a straightforward application of a rule grounded in fitness for eating. He can eat at all times, even though he can never perform the *avodah*, and so he receives a portion.

### Reish Lakish's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בַּעַל מוּם וְהוּא טָמֵא, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּחְלְקוּ לוֹ? כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חֲזֵי וְרַחֲמָנָא רַבְּיֵיהּ – לָא שְׁנָא, מָה לִי טָמֵא מָה לִי בַּעַל מוּם; אוֹ דִלְמָא, רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה חוֹלֵק, שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק?
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish raises a dilemma:** If a priest is **blemished and he is impure, what is** the *halakha*? Must the other priests **give him a share** of the meat? Perhaps we say that **since he is not fit** to perform the rite as a blemished priest **and** nevertheless **the Merciful One included him** to receive a share in the meat, there **is no difference: What** is the difference **to me** if he is **impure,** and **what** is the difference **to me** if he is only **blemished?** In any event he is not fit, yet the Torah allows him to receive a share in the meat. **Or perhaps** he may not receive a share in the meat, because only a priest who is **fit for partaking** of sacrificial meat **receives a share** of the meat, but a priest **who is not fit for partaking** of sacrificial meat **does not receive a share** of the meat.

Building on this framework, Reish Lakish poses a pointed question: what is the status of a *ba'al mum* who becomes *tamei* during the day? He always carries his dispensation as a *ba'al mum* — the Torah itself includes him despite his inability to perform the *avodah*. Does this standing permission carry over even when he is also *tamei*? *Keivan d'lo chazi v'rachmana rabyei lo shena ma li tamei ma li ba'al mum* — since he was ineligible and the Torah included him anyway, perhaps it makes no difference whether the additional disqualification is his *mum* or his *tumah*. Or do we say *ra'uy l'achilah cholek she'eino ra'uy l'achilah eino cholek*: the decisive question at every moment is whether he was fit to eat?

The question itself maps directly onto the dispute between Rav Yosef's approach and the older reading. If the *ba'al mum* is truly an *exception* — the Torah acknowledging his ineligibility and granting him a special pass — then one might argue that his special permission remains intact even when he becomes *tamei*. But if Rav Yosef is correct and there is no exception at all, only a reformulation of the rule, then a *ba'al mum* who is *tamei* is simply someone who is not fit to eat, and the rule denies him a portion straightforwardly.

### Kohen Gadol Onen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, תָּא שְׁמַע: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַקְרִיב אוֹנֵן, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק לֶאֱכוֹל לָעֶרֶב. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה בָּעֵינַן! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a *baraita*: If **a High Priest** is serving in the Temple and one of his immediate relatives dies, **he sacrifices** offerings even as **an acute mourner. But he does not partake** of sacrificial meat, **and he does not receive a share to partake** of it **in the evening. Conclude from** the *baraita* that in order for the priest to receive a share in sacrificial meat, **we require** that he be **fit for partaking** of it, and accordingly, a blemished priest who is impure does not receive a share. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that this is so.

Rabbah resolves both the question of Reish Lakish and the theoretical debate between the two approaches by citing a well-known *halakhah*: *kohen gadol makriv onen ve'eino ochel ve'eino cholek le'echol la'erev* — the *kohen gadol* who is an *onen* continues to perform the *avodah* (as the verse *u'min hamikdash lo yeitzei* requires him to remain and serve even in the face of bereavement) yet is forbidden to eat, and consequently does not receive a portion that night.

This is a decisive proof, and it is striking precisely because the *kohen gadol onen* is the mirror image of the *ba'al mum*. The *ba'al mum* can eat but cannot perform the *avodah*; the *kohen gadol onen* can perform the *avodah* but cannot eat. The ruling that the *kohen gadol onen* receives no portion at night — despite having performed the *avodah* that very day — demonstrates conclusively that fitness to perform the *avodah* is not what generates entitlement to a portion. Everything turns on fitness to eat. *Shema mina ra'uy le'achilah beinan shema mina* — we derive from this that fitness to eat is what we require, and from this we derive the answer.

Accordingly, there are no exceptions and no need for special passes. The rule applies uniformly: the *ba'al mum* receives a portion because he was fit to eat; the *kohen gadol onen* does not because he was not fit to eat; and a *ba'al mum* who becomes *tamei* does not, because a *tamei* person is not fit to eat.

### Tamei in Korbanos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: טָמֵא בְּקׇרְבְּנוֹת צִיבּוּר, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּחְלְקוּ לוֹ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: ״הַמְחַטֵּא״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – וְהַאי נָמֵי מֵחֵטְא הוּא; אוֹ דִלְמָא, רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה חוֹלֵק, שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Oshaya raises a dilemma:** If a priest is **impure,** then **in** a case of **communal offerings,** which may be offered by an impure priest, **what is** the *halakha*? Do the other priests **give him a share** of the meat, so that he may partake of it in the evening when he becomes pure? **Do we say** that **the Merciful One states:** "The priest **who effects atonement," and** therefore any priest who is fit for effecting atonement receives a share, as derived earlier, and **this** priest **is also** one who may **effect atonement,** since this is a communal offering? **Or perhaps** he may not, due to the principle that only a priest who is **fit for partaking** of sacrificial meat **receives a share** of the meat, but a priest **who is unfit for partaking** of sacrificial meat **does not receive a share.**

Rav Oshaya poses a parallel question from a different angle: *tamei bekorbanos tzibur mahu sheyechalku lo* — what is the rule for a *tamei kohen* when it comes to communal sacrifices? A *korban tzibur* brought in a state of *tumah* — when the majority of *kohanim* or the community at large is *tamei* — may be brought in *tumah*, and in such a circumstance a *tamei kohen* is technically fit to participate in the *avodah*. However, even when a *korban tzibur* is brought *betumah*, it is not eaten *betumah*; the override applies only to the act of bringing, not to eating. So we have a case where the *tamei kohen* is eligible to perform the *avodah* but not to eat. Does he receive a portion that night?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַקְרִיב אוֹנֵן, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק לֶאֱכוֹל לָעֶרֶב. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה בָּעֵינַן! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a *baraita*: If **a High Priest** is serving in the Temple and one of his relatives dies, **he sacrifices** offerings even as **an acute mourner, but he does not partake** of sacrificial meat **and he does not receive a share** of it **to partake in the evening. Conclude from** the *baraita* that in order for a priest to receive a share in sacrificial meat, **we require** that he be **fit for partaking** of it at the time of the service, without regard to whether he can perform the service. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that this is so.

The answer is provided by Ravina, who again cites the *kohen gadol onen*: *kohen gadol makriv onen ve'eino ochel ve'eino cholek le'echol la'erev*. The case is structurally identical. Ability to participate in the *avodah* without ability to eat generates no entitlement to a portion. *Shema mina ra'uy le'achilah beinan shema mina.* The principle is confirmed: there are no anomalies, no exceptions — every case is resolved by the single question of whether one was fit to eat at the time of the *avodah*.

## Onen Status

### Onen Touching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹנֵן נוֹגֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ מַקְרִיב כּוּ׳. אוֹנֵן נוֹגֵעַ?! וּרְמִינְהִי: אוֹנֵן וּמְחוּסַּר כִּיפּוּרִים – צְרִיכִין טְבִילָה לַקּוֹדֶשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: A priest who is **an acute mourner** is permitted to **touch** sacrificial meat, **but he may not sacrifice** offerings. The Gemara asks: Is it in fact permitted for **an acute mourner** to **touch** sacrificial meat? **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another mishna (*Ḥagiga* 21a): **An acute mourner and one who has not yet** brought **an atonement** offering, even after their respective disqualifications have expired, **require immersion** in order **to** eat **sacrificial** food. According to that mishna, an acute mourner who did not immerse may not touch sacrificial meat.

The *mishnah* now turns to the *onen* — one who has suffered the loss of an immediate relative — and states that an *onen* may touch *korbanos* but may not bring them or eat them, and therefore does not receive a portion. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah*, at least as it stands, is the opening clause: an *onen* is permitted to *touch* sacrificial offerings. He is not considered *tamei*.

But this is immediately challenged. A *mishnah* in *Chagigah* states that an *onen* and a *mechusar kippurim* require *tevilah* before touching *kodesh* — which implies that an *onen* has some degree of *tumah* that disqualifies him from contact with *korbanos*. How can our *mishnah* say he may touch?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בְּשֶׁטָּבַל, כָּאן בְּשֶׁלֹּא טָבַל. וְכִי טָבַל מַאי הָוֵי? הָא הָדְרָא עֲלֵיהּ אֲנִינוּת; דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: אוֹנֵן שֶׁטָּבַל – אֲנִינוּתוֹ חוֹזֶרֶת עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to** a case **where** the mourner **immersed** during his day of acute mourning. This is why he is permitted to touch the sacrificial meat. **There,** the ruling of the mishna in tractate *Ḥagiga* is stated **with regard to** a case **where** the mourner **did not immerse.** The Gemara asks: **And** even **if** he **immersed, what of it? But** doesn't his **acute mourning return to him? As Rabba, son of Rav Huna, says:** In a case of **an acute mourner who immersed** during his day of his acute mourning, **his acute mourning returns to him.**

Rav Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan resolves the apparent contradiction: *kan shetaval kan shelo taval* — our *mishnah* speaks of an *onen* who has already immersed in a *mikvah*; the *mishnah* in *Chagigah* speaks of one who has not. This would be a sufficient answer — except that the *Gemara* immediately objects: even if the *onen* immerses, what good does it do? *Hadra lei aninus* — his *aninut* returns to him. As Rabbah bar bar Chana teaches, an *onen* who immerses: his *aninut* comes back upon him. *Tovel ve'aninus beyado* — he emerges from the *mikvah* still an *onen*, so the immersion changes nothing. The *mikvah* referred to in *Chagigah* must mean after the *aninut* is over — that night, once the *aninut* has passed, he immerses and is permitted. But during the day, immersion does not help. We are back to the original problem.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דְּאַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ, הָא דְּלָא אַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ. הֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת – שְׁלִישִׁי וּשְׁבִיעִי בָּעֵי; דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסְטַאי בְּרַבִּי מָתוּן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת – צָרִיךְ הַזָּאָה שְׁלִישִׁי וּשְׁבִיעִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. That** case, in tractate *Ḥagiga*, is one **where he was distracted** from safeguarding his state of purity, so he may not touch sacrificial meat in the event that he is impure. **This** case, in the mishna here, is one **where he was not distracted.** The Gemara counters: If the mishna in tractate *Ḥagiga* is discussing a case of **distraction,** then his status is like that of one who contracted ritual impurity imparted by a corpse, who **requires** sprinkling with water of purification on the **third and seventh** days of his impurity. **As Rabbi Yustai, son of Rabbi Matun, says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** One who experienced **a distraction requires sprinkling** with water of purification on the **third and seventh** days.

The *Gemara* then proposes: *ha de'asiach da'ateih, ha delo asiach da'ateih* — the two sources reflect two different scenarios. The *onen* who may touch is one who has not experienced *hesach hadaat*; the *onen* who may not touch is one who has.

### Hesach Hadaat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דְּאַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ מִטְּמֵא מֵת, הָא דְּאַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ מִטְּמֵא שֶׁרֶץ. טְמֵא שֶׁרֶץ – טָמֵא מְעַלְּיָיא הוּא, הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ בָּעֵי! וְעוֹד, אֲפִילּוּ תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult. That** statement, that he requires sprinkling, is discussing a case **where he was distracted** and careless **about** contracting **impurity imparted by a corpse. This** mishna in tractate *Ḥagiga*, stating that he requires immersion but not sprinkling, is discussing a case **where he was distracted** and careless **about** contracting **impurity imparted by** the carcass of **a creeping animal.** The Gemara counters: One who was careless about becoming **impure due to** the carcass of **a creeping animal is fully impure,** and so he requires not only immersion to become pure, but he also **requires sunset. And furthermore,** if the mishna in tractate *Ḥagiga* is discussing this case, the mourner should be prohibited from touching **even *teruma*,** not just sacrificial meat. Why does the mishna mention only the latter?

The concept of *hesach hadaat* — a lapse of attentiveness — is now developed in some detail. The concern is that an *onen*, preoccupied with the death of a relative and knowing he will not be entering the *Beis HaMikdash* that day, may have relaxed his vigilance against *tumah*. The *Gemara* distinguishes between different degrees of carelessness.

If the *onen* has experienced *hesach hadaat* entirely — he has stopped being careful about contracting *tumah* from a *meis* — then *shelishi ve'shevii bo*: he requires sprinkling on the third and seventh day, as Rav Yostai the son of Rabbi Mattun said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *hesach hadaat tzarich hazayah shelishi ve'shevii*. For such a person, a *mikvah* is wholly insufficient; he is treated as having become *tamei meis*.

But if the *hesach hadaat* was only partial — he was not careful about contact with a *sheretz* or other lower-grade impurity but was still careful about *tumat meis* — then the concern is only that he may have become a *sheni*, not a *rishon*. In that case, the *mikvah* is the appropriate remedy, but it only works after his *aninut* concludes. During the day, as noted, the *aninut* returns upon him even after immersion.

This yields a three-way distinction: the *onen* who was wholly careless (*hesach hadaat mi'meis*) requires three-and-seven sprinkling; the *onen* who was partially careless (*hesach hadaat mi'tumei sheretz*) is the default case — *tamei* for the day, with the *mikvah* not helping until the *aninut* is over; and the *onen* who was fully attentive is the case of our *mishnah* — he may touch *korbanos*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בְּאוֹמֵר נִשְׁמַרְתִּי מִדָּבָר הַמְטַמְּאֵנִי, וְלֹא נִשְׁמַרְתִּי מִדָּבָר הַפּוֹסְלֵנִי. וּמִי אִיכָּא נְטִירוּתָא לְפַלְגָא?! אִין; וְהָתַנְיָא: עוֹדֵהוּ הַסַּל עַל רֹאשׁוֹ וּמַגְרֵיפָה בְּתוֹכוֹ, וְאָמַר: ״לִבִּי עַל הַסַּל וְאֵין לִבִּי עַל הַמַּגְרֵיפָה״ – הַסַּל טָהוֹר וְהַמַּגְרֵיפָה טְמֵאָה. וּתְטַמֵּא מַגְרֵיפָה לְסַל! אֵין כְּלִי מְטַמֵּא כְּלִי. וּתְטַמֵּא מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ! אָמַר רָבָא: בְּאוֹמֵר שְׁמַרְתִּיהָ מִדָּבָר הַמְטַמְּאָהּ, וְלֹא שְׁמַרְתִּיהָ מִדָּבָר הַפּוֹסְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where he says: I safeguarded myself from anything that would render me impure,** so I am certain that I did not contract impurity that requires waiting until sunset; **but I did not safeguard myself from anything that would render me unfit** for touching sacrificial meat. The Gemara asks: **But is there** such a concept of **partial care,** that one can claim to have safeguarded himself from one form of impurity but not another? The Gemara answers: **Yes, and it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one was carrying a basket, and **the basket was still on his head,** **and a shovel** was **in** the basket, **and he said: I am minding the basket,** that it not become impure, **but I am not minding the shovel,** then **the basket is pure, and the shovel is impure.** The Gemara challenges the ruling of the *baraita*: **But** wouldn't **the shovel render the basket impure?** The Gemara answers: There is a principle that **a vessel does not render** another **vessel ritually impure.** The Gemara challenges: **But** wouldn't **the shovel render that which is in** the basket, e.g., figs, **impure? Rava said:** The case is **where he says: I safeguarded it,** the shovel, **from anything that** would allow it to **render** another item **impure, but I did not safeguard it from anything that would render it** itself **unfit,** i.e., impure.

The Gemara, however, challenges this intermediate category. If the concern regarding the partially careless *onen* is that he may have touched a *sheretz* and become a *sheni*, he should be prohibited from touching even *terumah*, not merely *korbanos*. Why does the source in *Chagigah* imply only a prohibition against *kodesh*?

Rabbi Yirmiyah offers a further refinement: *netiruta lepalga* — one can maintain a calibrated, partial level of vigilance. The *onen* can say: *nishmarti midevar hamatmeini velo nishmarti midevar haposleni* — I was careful enough to ensure I did not become a *sheni* (which would disqualify me from touching *terumah*), but I was not careful enough to prevent becoming a *shlishi* (which disqualifies me only from *kodashim*). This is admittedly a peculiar and fine-tuned form of self-monitoring, but the *Gemara* demonstrates its plausibility from a parallel case: a man tending a basket of figs with a shovel declares that he is watching the basket carefully but not the shovel. Rava explains: *be'omer shimartiha midavar metame'ah velo shimartiha midavar haposlah* — he watched the shovel carefully enough to prevent it from becoming a *rishon* (which would make the figs a *sheni*), but not carefully enough to prevent it from becoming a *sheni* (which would only make the figs a *shlishi*, thereby disqualifying *terumah* figs, but not *chullin*). This demonstrates that one can indeed maintain precisely calibrated levels of vigilance.

The analogy is then applied to the *onen*: the default *onen* maintained enough vigilance not to touch anything that would make him a *sheni* — protecting his capacity to touch *terumah* — but not enough to prevent himself from becoming a *shlishi*, which is why he is disqualified only from *kodashim*.

### Onen as Shlishi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיגַּלְגַּל מִילְּתָא, וּמְטַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל. אֲמַר לְהוּ, לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רַבִּי: הָאוֹכֵל שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל וּמוּתָּר לִיגַּע? אַלְמָא בַּאֲכִילָה עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מַעֲלָה, בִּנְגִיעָה לָא עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מַעֲלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss the contradiction between the mishna, which permits an acute mourner to touch sacrificial meat, and the mishna in tractate *Ḥagiga*, which requires him to immerse. The Gemara relates: **The matter circulated and came before Rabbi Abba bar Memel. He said to** the Sages before him: **Have they not heard that which Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: One who partakes of *teruma*** that has **third-**degree impurity, i.e., *teruma* disqualified through contact with an item with second-degree impurity, **is prohibited from partaking** of *teruma*, **but permitted to touch** *teruma*. Rabbi Abba bar Memel continued: **Apparently, in** a case of **partaking, the Sages imposed a higher standard,** whereas **in** a case of **touching, the Sages did not impose a higher standard.** Similarly, in a case of an acute mourner, the Sages require him to immerse before he may partake of sacrificial meat, as taught in tractate *Ḥagiga*, but they do not impose this standard for touching the meat, as taught in the mishna here.

Rabbi Abba bar Mamal then offers a far simpler resolution that makes the entire *hesach hadaat* discussion unnecessary. As Rav (cited by Rabbi Yochanan) taught regarding one who eats a *shlishi* of *terumah*: he becomes, through the act of eating, a rabbinic *shlishi* himself — *assur le'echol* but *mutar liga*, prohibited from eating *terumah* but permitted to touch it. The principle is that rabbinic *maalot* concerning *tumah* operate differently for eating than for touching: *b'achilah avdu rabbanan maalah, b'negiah lo avdu rabbanan maalah* — the Sages enacted their stringencies with respect to eating, not with respect to touching.

Applying this to the *onen*: the Sages conferred upon an *onen* a rabbinic *tumah* status akin to a *shlishi* or *revi'i* — enough to prohibit him from eating *kodashim*, but not enough to prohibit him from touching them. There is no need for any discussion of *hesach hadaat* at all. A normal *onen* — not particularly careless, not particularly vigilant — may touch *korbanos* freely, as our *mishnah* says. He simply may not eat them. This is a clean, unified rule: the Sages enacted a stringency regarding the *onen's* eating of *kodashim* by treating him as a rabbinic *shlishi* (or *revi'i*) for the purpose of eating, without extending that stringency to touch. And because immersion during the day does not resolve his *aninut*, this status persists throughout the day regardless of whether he visits the *mikvah*.

## Onen's Portion

### Eating at Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק לֶאֱכוֹל כּוּ׳. מִיפְלָג הוּא דְּלָא פְּלִיג, וְכִי מְזַמְּנִי לֵיהּ – אָכֵיל; וּרְמִינְהִי: אוֹנֵן (וּמְחוּסַּר כִּיפּוּרִים) – טוֹבֵל וְאוֹכֵל אֶת פִּסְחוֹ לָעֶרֶב, אֲבָל לֹא בְּקָדָשִׁים!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to an acute mourner: **And he does not receive a share** of sacrificial meat in order **to partake** of it in the evening. The Gemara comments: The mishna indicates only that **he may not receive a share** of the meat, **but when** other priests **invite him** to join in their portions, **he may partake** of them in the evening. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 91b): **An acute mourner immerses and partakes of his Paschal offering in the evening, but** he may **not** partake **of** other **sacrificial** meat.

Having established the *onen's* status during the day, the *Gemara* turns to the night. Our *mishnah* implies that while the *onen* receives no portion — since he could neither perform the *avodah* nor eat during the day — if others were to invite him to eat from their portions that night, he would be permitted to do so. Is this actually correct? Is an *onen* permitted to eat *korbanos* the night following the death?

The *Gemara* cites a *mishnah* in *Pesachim*: *onen tovel ve'ochel pischo la'erev, aval lo b'kodashim* — an *onen* immerses and eats his *korban Pesach* that night, but not other *kodashim*. This passage cuts two ways. On one hand, it confirms that the *onen's* prohibition against eating *kodashim* is rabbinic in nature, since we are willing to waive it for *korban Pesach*. On the other hand, it establishes that for all other *kodashim* the prohibition extends into the night — so why does our *mishnah* imply the *onen* may eat at night?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בְּפֶסַח, כָּאן בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה. בְּפֶסַח – אַיְּידֵי דְּאָכֵיל פֶּסַח, אָכֵיל נָמֵי קָדָשִׁים. בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה – דְּלָא חֲזֵי, לָא חֲזֵי. וּמַאי ״אֲבָל לֹא בְּקָדָשִׁים״? אֲבָל לֹא בְּקָדָשִׁים שֶׁל כׇּל הַשָּׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya of Difti said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to** the first night of **Passover,** whereas **there,** in tractate *Pesaḥim*, the ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to the rest of the days of the year.** What is the reason for the distinction between the two? **On** the first night of **Passover, since he partakes of** the **Paschal offering, he may also partake of** other **sacrificial** meat. But **on the rest of the days of the year, when he is unfit** to partake of sacrificial meat, **he is unfit. And what** does the mishna in *Pesaḥim* mean when it states: **But** he may **not** partake **of** other **sacrificial** meat? It means: **But** he may **not** partake **of sacrificial** meat **of all** of the rest of **the year,** other than the first night of Passover.

Rabbi Yirmiyah of Difti offers one resolution: our *mishnah* is speaking specifically of the night of *Pesach*, when the *onen* is given blanket permission regarding *korban Pesach* and is permitted to eat other *kodashim* as well, since *pesach ayidi d'achil pesach achil nami kodashim*. For all other nights of the year, the *onen* indeed may not eat *kodashim*. This is admittedly a narrow reading of our *mishnah* — who would have assumed it was addressing the *Seder* night? — and the *Gemara* treats it as such.

### Death & Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן שֶׁמֵּת לוֹ מֵת בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר וּקְבָרוֹ בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, כָּאן שֶׁמֵּת לוֹ מֵת בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר וּקְבָרוֹ בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר; יוֹם קְבוּרָה לָא תָּפֵיס לֵילוֹ מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said** there is a different resolution to the contradiction between the *mishnayot*: This is **not difficult. Here,** in the ruling of the mishna in tractate *Pesaḥim*, which prohibits an acute mourner from partaking of sacrificial meat, it is referring to a case **where his** relative **died on the fourteenth** day of Nisan, **and he buried him on the fourteenth** itself, in which case he is still considered an acute mourner by rabbinic law that evening. **There,** in the ruling of the mishna in this chapter, it is referring to a case **where his** relative **died on the thirteenth** of Nisan, **and he buried him on the fourteenth** of Nisan. The reason the mourner may partake is that since **the day of burial** is not the day of death, it **does not take hold of its** following **night by rabbinic law.**

### Night is Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא אֲנִינוּת לַיְלָה מִדְּרַבָּנַן? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: אֲנִינוּת לַיְלָה מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אוֹנֵן אֵינוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אֶלָּא מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים. תֵּדַע – שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרוּ: אוֹנֵן טוֹבֵל וְאוֹכֵל אֶת פִּסְחוֹ לָעֶרֶב, אֲבָל לֹא בַּקֳּדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that **acute mourning** the following **night** is **by rabbinic law,** as opposed to by Torah law? This **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Acute mourning at night is by Torah law;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** His status as **an acute mourner** at night **is not by Torah law, but by rabbinic law. Know** that this so, **as** the Sages **said: An acute mourner immerses and partakes of his Paschal offering in the evening, but** he may still **not** partake **of** other **sacrificial** meat. If acute mourning at night were by Torah law, he would not be permitted to partake of the Paschal offering.

Rav Ashi provides a substantially cleaner resolution rooted in a distinction between biblical and rabbinic *onen* status.

Rav Ashi's resolution turns on the question of when biblical *aninut* applies. The *mishnah* in *Pesachim* that allows the *onen* to eat his *korban Pesach* that night but not other *kodashim* must reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that nighttime *aninut* — the extension of the *onen* status into the night — is only rabbinic: *ein onen midivrei Torah ela midivrei sofrim*. He proves this from the *mishnah* in *Pesachim* itself: if *aninut* at night were biblical, the permission to eat the *korban Pesach* would be inexplicable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֲנִינוּת לַיְלָה מִדְּרַבָּנַן?! וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אוֹנֵן אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּחַ קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו. מַאי, לָאו וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּפֶסַח? לָא; לְבַר מִפֶּסַח. וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״שְׁלָמִים״ – כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׁלֵם מֵבִיא, וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹנֵן. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַתּוֹדָה? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת הַתּוֹדָה, שֶׁכֵּן נֶאֱכֶלֶת בְּשִׂמְחָה כִּשְׁלָמִים. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָעוֹלָה? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת הָעוֹלָה, שֶׁכֵּן בָּאָה בְּנֶדֶר וּבִנְדָבָה כִּשְׁלָמִים. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר וָפֶסַח? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר וָפֶסַח, שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָן בָּאִין עַל חֵטְא. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֶבַח״. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת הָעוֹפוֹת וְהַמְּנָחוֹת וְהַיַּיִן וְהָעֵצִים וְהַלְּבוֹנָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שְׁלָמִים קׇרְבָּנוֹ״; כׇּל קׇרְבְּנוֹת שֶׁהוּא מֵבִיא – כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׁלֵם מֵבִיא, וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Shimon hold** that **acute mourning at night is by rabbinic law** and that consequently an acute mourner partakes of his Paschal offering in the evening? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says: An acute mourner does not send his offerings** to the Temple to be sacrificed? **What, is it not** referring **even to a Paschal offering?** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** the *baraita* is referring to all offerings **other than a Paschal offering.** The Gemara counters: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the verse: "And if his offering be a sacrifice of peace offerings [*shelamim*]" (Leviticus 3:1), **Rabbi Shimon says:** The offering is called ***shelamim*** to teach that **when** a person **is whole [*shalem*],** i.e., in a state of contentment, **he brings** his offering, **but he does not bring** it **when he is an acute mourner. From where** is it derived **to include** that an acute mourner does not bring even **a thanks offering? I include the thanks offering because it is consumed in** a state of **joy, like a peace offering. From where** is it derived that the verse also serves **to include a burnt offering? I include the burnt offering because it comes as a vow** offering **and as a gift** offering, **like a peace offering. From where** is it derived that the verse also serves **to include a firstborn** offering, **and** an animal **tithe** offering, **and a Paschal offering,** which are not brought voluntarily? **I include a firstborn** offering, **and** an animal **tithe** offering, **and a Paschal offering, because they** too, like a peace offering, **do not come** to atone **for a sin. From where** is it derived **to include a sin offering and a guilt offering,** which atone for sins? **The verse states:** "And if his offering be **a sacrifice [*zevaḥ*]** of peace offerings," which teaches that an acute mourner may not sacrifice any slaughtered offering [*zevaḥ*]. **From where** is it derived **to include** even **the bird offerings, and the meal offerings, and the wine, and the wood, and the frankincense** brought for the Temple service? **The verse states:** "And if **his offering** be a sacrifice of **peace offerings [*shelamim korbano*],"** teaching that for **all offerings [*korbanot*] that** a person **brings, he brings** them **when he is whole [*shalem*], but he does not bring** them **when he is an acute mourner.**

Rav Ashi adds a further structural refinement. Biblical *aninut* is created when the death and the burial occur on the same day — *yom mita* and *yom kvura* coincide. In that case, the *yom mita* generates biblical *aninut* during the day, and the rabbis extend a prohibition against eating *kodashim* into the night as well. But when the burial occurs on a day different from the day of death — the day of burial is a day of *aninut* only *miderabbanan* — then *yom kvura lo tafis leilo miderabbanan*: a day of burial that is itself only rabbinic does not generate a rabbinic nighttime extension. That night, the *onen* is free to eat *kodashim*.

The case of our *mishnah*, which implies that the *onen* may eat at night, is precisely this scenario: the death occurred on one day, the burial on the next. Since the burial day carried only rabbinic *aninut*, the following night carries no *aninut* at all — not even rabbinically — and the *onen* is fully permitted to eat *kodashim*. The narrow case in *Pesachim* where the *onen* eats only *korban Pesach* and not other *kodashim* refers to a night following a day of biblical *aninut* — death and burial on the same day — where the rabbis extended the prohibition into the night but waived it for *korban Pesach* alone.

## Shalem Requirement

### Shalem Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא פֶּסַח! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: פֶּסַח – כְּדִי נַסְבֵיהּ. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: מַאי פֶּסַח – שַׁלְמֵי פֶסַח. אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ שְׁלָמִים! תְּנָא שְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח, וּתְנָא שְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָן. דְּאִי לָא תְּנָא שְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּמֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח אָתֵי – כְּגוּפֵיהּ דְּפֶסַח דָּמֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **In any event,** Rabbi Shimon **teaches** that it is prohibited for an acute mourner to bring **a Paschal offering,** even though he will cease to be an acute mourner that night; this contradicts the first *baraita*. **Rav Ḥisda said:** The latter *baraita* mentions **a Paschal offering for no purpose.** In other words, the *halakha* that an acute mourner does not bring an offering does not actually apply to a Paschal offering, and the *baraita* mentions it only out of habit, since a firstborn-animal offering, the animal tithe offering, and a Paschal offering are frequently mentioned together. **Rav Sheshet said: What** is meant in this *baraita* by the term: **Paschal offering?** It is referring to **the peace offerings of Passover,** i.e., the peace offering that is sacrificed along with the Paschal offering. The Gemara objects: **If so,** that **is** the same as **a peace offering,** which Rabbi Shimon already mentioned. The Gemara answers: He **taught** the *halakha* with regard to **peace offerings that come on account of** the **Paschal offering, and** he **taught** separately the *halakha* with regard to **peace offerings that come on their own account.** The Gemara explains: Rabbi Shimon needed to teach both cases explicitly, **because if he did not teach** the *halakha* with regard to **peace offerings that come on account of** the **Paschal offering,** it would **enter your mind to say: Since they come on account of** the **Paschal offering, they are considered like** the **Paschal offering itself,** and the acute mourner offers them as well. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon **teaches us** that these peace offerings are also forbidden to an acute mourner.

Having established that Rabbi Shimon excludes an *onen* from having any *korban* brought on his behalf — including, apparently, a *korban Pesach* — the *Gemara* returns to the *mishnah* in *Pesachim*, which states that an *onen* does eat his *korban Pesach* that night. This presupposes that the *korban Pesach* was in fact brought for him during the day. But if Rabbi Shimon excludes an *onen* from having any *korban* brought, how is the *korban Pesach* brought?

The *Gemara* asks: is the exclusion in the *baraita* perhaps meant to include even *Pesach*? *Lo levar miPesach* — no, Rabbi Shimon means only other offerings; he agrees that a *korban Pesach* may be brought for an *onen*. Rav Chisda confirms: *pesach kedi nasba* — the inclusion of *Pesach* in the *baraita's* list was incidental, a byproduct of working through the standard catalogue of *korbanos* (*bechor*, *ma'aser*, *Pesach*), and does not reflect Rabbi Shimon's actual *halakhah* with respect to *Pesach*.

### Pesach Exception

This creates a conceptual tension: if Rabbi Shimon's principle of *shalem* is a genuine Torah-level requirement, what permits us to set it aside for *korban Pesach*?

Rashi resolves this by suggesting that the entire elaborate *drasha* of *shalem* is only an *asmachta* — a rabbinic ruling supported by scriptural allusion — and therefore flexible enough to be waived for *Pesach*.

Tosafot offer a more substantive and elegant resolution. All other *korbanos* have the bringing — the *zerikat ha-dam* and the sacrificial act — as their essential focus. For *korban Pesach*, however, the entire purpose is the eating. The *korban Pesach* is brought solely to enable the eating that night, which is why, unlike all other *korbanos* brought *betumah*, the *Pesach* itself is eaten even in a state of *tumah* when necessary. Because the *korban Pesach* is fundamentally defined by its eating, the requirement that the offerer be *shalem* at the time of bringing does not apply: the operative moment of *Pesach* is not the bringing during the day but the eating that night, and at night the *onen* is no longer in a state of *aninut*. He will be *shalem* when the *korban* is consumed — which is, for *Pesach*, the moment that matters.

This distinction carries through the entire sugyah. Rav Sheshet adds a final refinement: when the *baraita* says an *onen* may not send his *korban Pesach*, it means he may not bring the *shelamim* that accompany the *Pesach* — the *chagigah* offering — not the *korban Pesach* itself. The *Gemara* objects that this adds nothing to what was already said about *shelamim*, and resolves: the *baraita* teaches both *shelamim* brought independently and *shelamim* brought in the context of the *Pesach*, lest one think that since the *chagigah shelamim* comes as a package with the *Pesach* it is permitted — *ke-gufeih de-pesach dami* — as if it were part of the *Pesach* itself. The *baraita* therefore specifies that it is not. In all opinions, Rabbi Shimon holds that an *onen* may not have a *korban* brought on his behalf — with the sole exception of the *korban Pesach*, precisely because its essence lies not in the bringing but in the eating.
