# Zevachim 99 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fitness to Eat

### Rav Yosef's Drasha

The *mishnah* with which we have been engaged establishes a general rule: a *kohen* who was able to perform the *avodah* during the day is entitled to a portion of the sacrificial meat that night; one who was not able to do so receives no portion. The *ba'al mum* — a *kohen* with a permanent physical blemish — appears as an exception. The *mishnah* derives this from the verse *HaKohen HaMechattei Otah Yochalennah* ("the *kohen* who performs the sin-offering shall eat it"), reading *mechattei* not as the one who personally performed the offering but as the one who is *fit* to perform it, and reading *yochalennah* — "shall eat it" — as denoting entitlement, i.e., receipt of a portion. The *ba'al mum* is then included via an additional verse, *kol zachar bakohanim yochal otah*, on the grounds that, although he can never perform the *avodah*, at the time the *avodah* was being performed he at least possessed the capacity to eat from the meat.

Rav Yosef sharpens this analysis considerably. He asks: given that we have already determined that *yochalennah* means entitlement to a portion rather than merely permission to eat, why did the Torah not simply write *yechalkennah* — "shall receive a portion"? The deliberate use of the language of eating, Rav Yosef argues, is a *drasha*: *shema mina ra'uy l'achilah cholek she'eino ra'uy l'achilah eino cholek* — one who was fit to eat at the time of the *avodah* receives a portion; one who was not fit to eat does not. The operative criterion is not fitness to perform the *avodah* but fitness to eat at the time the *avodah* was performed. This reorients the entire framework. The *ba'al mum* is not an exception to a rule grounded in fitness for *avodah*; rather, he is a straightforward application of a rule grounded in fitness for eating. He can eat at all times, even though he can never perform the *avodah*, and so he receives a portion.

### Reish Lakish's Question

Building on this framework, Reish Lakish poses a pointed question: what is the status of a *ba'al mum* who becomes *tamei* during the day? He always carries his dispensation as a *ba'al mum* — the Torah itself includes him despite his inability to perform the *avodah*. Does this standing permission carry over even when he is also *tamei*? *Keivan d'lo chazi v'rachmana rabyei lo shena ma li tamei ma li ba'al mum* — since he was ineligible and the Torah included him anyway, perhaps it makes no difference whether the additional disqualification is his *mum* or his *tumah*. Or do we say *ra'uy l'achilah cholek she'eino ra'uy l'achilah eino cholek*: the decisive question at every moment is whether he was fit to eat?

The question itself maps directly onto the dispute between Rav Yosef's approach and the older reading. If the *ba'al mum* is truly an *exception* — the Torah acknowledging his ineligibility and granting him a special pass — then one might argue that his special permission remains intact even when he becomes *tamei*. But if Rav Yosef is correct and there is no exception at all, only a reformulation of the rule, then a *ba'al mum* who is *tamei* is simply someone who is not fit to eat, and the rule denies him a portion straightforwardly.

### Kohen Gadol Onen

Rabbah resolves both the question of Reish Lakish and the theoretical debate between the two approaches by citing a well-known *halakhah*: *kohen gadol makriv onen ve'eino ochel ve'eino cholek le'echol la'erev* — the *kohen gadol* who is an *onen* continues to perform the *avodah* (as the verse *u'min hamikdash lo yeitzei* requires him to remain and serve even in the face of bereavement) yet is forbidden to eat, and consequently does not receive a portion that night.

This is a decisive proof, and it is striking precisely because the *kohen gadol onen* is the mirror image of the *ba'al mum*. The *ba'al mum* can eat but cannot perform the *avodah*; the *kohen gadol onen* can perform the *avodah* but cannot eat. The ruling that the *kohen gadol onen* receives no portion at night — despite having performed the *avodah* that very day — demonstrates conclusively that fitness to perform the *avodah* is not what generates entitlement to a portion. Everything turns on fitness to eat. *Shema mina ra'uy le'achilah beinan shema mina* — we derive from this that fitness to eat is what we require, and from this we derive the answer.

Accordingly, there are no exceptions and no need for special passes. The rule applies uniformly: the *ba'al mum* receives a portion because he was fit to eat; the *kohen gadol onen* does not because he was not fit to eat; and a *ba'al mum* who becomes *tamei* does not, because a *tamei* person is not fit to eat.

### Tamei in Korbanos

Rav Oshaya poses a parallel question from a different angle: *tamei bekorbanos tzibur mahu sheyechalku lo* — what is the rule for a *tamei kohen* when it comes to communal sacrifices? A *korban tzibur* brought in a state of *tumah* — when the majority of *kohanim* or the community at large is *tamei* — may be brought in *tumah*, and in such a circumstance a *tamei kohen* is technically fit to participate in the *avodah*. However, even when a *korban tzibur* is brought *betumah*, it is not eaten *betumah*; the override applies only to the act of bringing, not to eating. So we have a case where the *tamei kohen* is eligible to perform the *avodah* but not to eat. Does he receive a portion that night?

The answer is provided by Ravina, who again cites the *kohen gadol onen*: *kohen gadol makriv onen ve'eino ochel ve'eino cholek le'echol la'erev*. The case is structurally identical. Ability to participate in the *avodah* without ability to eat generates no entitlement to a portion. *Shema mina ra'uy le'achilah beinan shema mina.* The principle is confirmed: there are no anomalies, no exceptions — every case is resolved by the single question of whether one was fit to eat at the time of the *avodah*.

## Onen Status

### Onen Touching

The *mishnah* now turns to the *onen* — one who has suffered the loss of an immediate relative — and states that an *onen* may touch *korbanos* but may not bring them or eat them, and therefore does not receive a portion. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah*, at least as it stands, is the opening clause: an *onen* is permitted to *touch* sacrificial offerings. He is not considered *tamei*.

But this is immediately challenged. A *mishnah* in *Chagigah* states that an *onen* and a *mechusar kippurim* require *tevilah* before touching *kodesh* — which implies that an *onen* has some degree of *tumah* that disqualifies him from contact with *korbanos*. How can our *mishnah* say he may touch?

Rav Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan resolves the apparent contradiction: *kan shetaval kan shelo taval* — our *mishnah* speaks of an *onen* who has already immersed in a *mikvah*; the *mishnah* in *Chagigah* speaks of one who has not. This would be a sufficient answer — except that the *Gemara* immediately objects: even if the *onen* immerses, what good does it do? *Hadra lei aninus* — his *aninut* returns to him. As Rabbah bar bar Chana teaches, an *onen* who immerses: his *aninut* comes back upon him. *Tovel ve'aninus beyado* — he emerges from the *mikvah* still an *onen*, so the immersion changes nothing. The *mikvah* referred to in *Chagigah* must mean after the *aninut* is over — that night, once the *aninut* has passed, he immerses and is permitted. But during the day, immersion does not help. We are back to the original problem.

The *Gemara* then proposes: *ha de'asiach da'ateih, ha delo asiach da'ateih* — the two sources reflect two different scenarios. The *onen* who may touch is one who has not experienced *hesach hadaat*; the *onen* who may not touch is one who has.

### Hesach Hadaat

The concept of *hesach hadaat* — a lapse of attentiveness — is now developed in some detail. The concern is that an *onen*, preoccupied with the death of a relative and knowing he will not be entering the *Beis HaMikdash* that day, may have relaxed his vigilance against *tumah*. The *Gemara* distinguishes between different degrees of carelessness.

If the *onen* has experienced *hesach hadaat* entirely — he has stopped being careful about contracting *tumah* from a *meis* — then *shelishi ve'shevii bo*: he requires sprinkling on the third and seventh day, as Rav Yostai the son of Rabbi Mattun said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *hesach hadaat tzarich hazayah shelishi ve'shevii*. For such a person, a *mikvah* is wholly insufficient; he is treated as having become *tamei meis*.

But if the *hesach hadaat* was only partial — he was not careful about contact with a *sheretz* or other lower-grade impurity but was still careful about *tumat meis* — then the concern is only that he may have become a *sheni*, not a *rishon*. In that case, the *mikvah* is the appropriate remedy, but it only works after his *aninut* concludes. During the day, as noted, the *aninut* returns upon him even after immersion.

This yields a three-way distinction: the *onen* who was wholly careless (*hesach hadaat mi'meis*) requires three-and-seven sprinkling; the *onen* who was partially careless (*hesach hadaat mi'tumei sheretz*) is the default case — *tamei* for the day, with the *mikvah* not helping until the *aninut* is over; and the *onen* who was fully attentive is the case of our *mishnah* — he may touch *korbanos*.

The *Gemara*, however, challenges this intermediate category. If the concern regarding the partially careless *onen* is that he may have touched a *sheretz* and become a *sheni*, he should be prohibited from touching even *terumah*, not merely *korbanos*. Why does the source in *Chagigah* imply only a prohibition against *kodesh*?

Rabbi Yirmiyah offers a further refinement: *netiruta lepalga* — one can maintain a calibrated, partial level of vigilance. The *onen* can say: *nishmarti midevar hamatmeini velo nishmarti midevar haposleni* — I was careful enough to ensure I did not become a *sheni* (which would disqualify me from touching *terumah*), but I was not careful enough to prevent becoming a *shlishi* (which disqualifies me only from *kodashim*). This is admittedly a peculiar and fine-tuned form of self-monitoring, but the *Gemara* demonstrates its plausibility from a parallel case: a man tending a basket of figs with a shovel declares that he is watching the basket carefully but not the shovel. Rava explains: *be'omer shimartiha midavar metame'ah velo shimartiha midavar haposlah* — he watched the shovel carefully enough to prevent it from becoming a *rishon* (which would make the figs a *sheni*), but not carefully enough to prevent it from becoming a *sheni* (which would only make the figs a *shlishi*, thereby disqualifying *terumah* figs, but not *chullin*). This demonstrates that one can indeed maintain precisely calibrated levels of vigilance.

The analogy is then applied to the *onen*: the default *onen* maintained enough vigilance not to touch anything that would make him a *sheni* — protecting his capacity to touch *terumah* — but not enough to prevent himself from becoming a *shlishi*, which is why he is disqualified only from *kodashim*.

### Onen as Shlishi

Rabbi Abba bar Mamal then offers a far simpler resolution that makes the entire *hesach hadaat* discussion unnecessary. As Rav (cited by Rabbi Yochanan) taught regarding one who eats a *shlishi* of *terumah*: he becomes, through the act of eating, a rabbinic *shlishi* himself — *assur le'echol* but *mutar liga*, prohibited from eating *terumah* but permitted to touch it. The principle is that rabbinic *maalot* concerning *tumah* operate differently for eating than for touching: *b'achilah avdu rabbanan maalah, b'negiah lo avdu rabbanan maalah* — the Sages enacted their stringencies with respect to eating, not with respect to touching.

Applying this to the *onen*: the Sages conferred upon an *onen* a rabbinic *tumah* status akin to a *shlishi* or *revi'i* — enough to prohibit him from eating *kodashim*, but not enough to prohibit him from touching them. There is no need for any discussion of *hesach hadaat* at all. A normal *onen* — not particularly careless, not particularly vigilant — may touch *korbanos* freely, as our *mishnah* says. He simply may not eat them. This is a clean, unified rule: the Sages enacted a stringency regarding the *onen's* eating of *kodashim* by treating him as a rabbinic *shlishi* (or *revi'i*) for the purpose of eating, without extending that stringency to touch. And because immersion during the day does not resolve his *aninut*, this status persists throughout the day regardless of whether he visits the *mikvah*.

## Onen's Portion

### Eating at Night

Having established the *onen's* status during the day, the *Gemara* turns to the night. Our *mishnah* implies that while the *onen* receives no portion — since he could neither perform the *avodah* nor eat during the day — if others were to invite him to eat from their portions that night, he would be permitted to do so. Is this actually correct? Is an *onen* permitted to eat *korbanos* the night following the death?

The *Gemara* cites a *mishnah* in *Pesachim*: *onen tovel ve'ochel pischo la'erev, aval lo b'kodashim* — an *onen* immerses and eats his *korban Pesach* that night, but not other *kodashim*. This passage cuts two ways. On one hand, it confirms that the *onen's* prohibition against eating *kodashim* is rabbinic in nature, since we are willing to waive it for *korban Pesach*. On the other hand, it establishes that for all other *kodashim* the prohibition extends into the night — so why does our *mishnah* imply the *onen* may eat at night?

Rabbi Yirmiyah of Difti offers one resolution: our *mishnah* is speaking specifically of the night of *Pesach*, when the *onen* is given blanket permission regarding *korban Pesach* and is permitted to eat other *kodashim* as well, since *pesach ayidi d'achil pesach achil nami kodashim*. For all other nights of the year, the *onen* indeed may not eat *kodashim*. This is admittedly a narrow reading of our *mishnah* — who would have assumed it was addressing the *Seder* night? — and the *Gemara* treats it as such.

### Death & Burial

Rav Ashi provides a substantially cleaner resolution rooted in a distinction between biblical and rabbinic *onen* status.

### Night is Rabbinic

Rav Ashi's resolution turns on the question of when biblical *aninut* applies. The *mishnah* in *Pesachim* that allows the *onen* to eat his *korban Pesach* that night but not other *kodashim* must reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that nighttime *aninut* — the extension of the *onen* status into the night — is only rabbinic: *ein onen midivrei Torah ela midivrei sofrim*. He proves this from the *mishnah* in *Pesachim* itself: if *aninut* at night were biblical, the permission to eat the *korban Pesach* would be inexplicable.

Rav Ashi adds a further structural refinement. Biblical *aninut* is created when the death and the burial occur on the same day — *yom mita* and *yom kvura* coincide. In that case, the *yom mita* generates biblical *aninut* during the day, and the rabbis extend a prohibition against eating *kodashim* into the night as well. But when the burial occurs on a day different from the day of death — the day of burial is a day of *aninut* only *miderabbanan* — then *yom kvura lo tafis leilo miderabbanan*: a day of burial that is itself only rabbinic does not generate a rabbinic nighttime extension. That night, the *onen* is free to eat *kodashim*.

The case of our *mishnah*, which implies that the *onen* may eat at night, is precisely this scenario: the death occurred on one day, the burial on the next. Since the burial day carried only rabbinic *aninut*, the following night carries no *aninut* at all — not even rabbinically — and the *onen* is fully permitted to eat *kodashim*. The narrow case in *Pesachim* where the *onen* eats only *korban Pesach* and not other *kodashim* refers to a night following a day of biblical *aninut* — death and burial on the same day — where the rabbis extended the prohibition into the night but waived it for *korban Pesach* alone.

## Shalem Requirement

### Shalem Drasha

The *Gemara* then asks: which *tanna* holds that nighttime *aninut* is rabbinic? *Mi-derabbi Shimon hi* — it is the position of Rabbi Shimon. A *baraita* is then cited in which Rabbi Shimon expounds the verse *v'im zevach shelamim korbano* ("and if his offering is a peace-offering"): *keshehu shalem mevi ve'eino mevi keshehu onen* — one brings offerings when he is *shalem*, complete, and does not bring them when he is an *onen*. This is a striking and novel *halakhah*. Rabbi Shimon derives that the *onen* is excluded not merely from performing the *avodah* within the *Beis HaMikdash* but from having any *korban* brought on his behalf at all — even by a *shaliach*, without his entering the sanctuary. The reasoning reflects a broader conception of *aninut*: one who has just suffered a devastating loss is, in a profound sense, not *shalem*, not whole. This is not the appropriate moment for the engagement of bringing a *korban* before the Almighty.

The *drasha* then extends this principle systematically. A *todah* is included — *sheken ne'echelet besimcha keshelamim* — because it is eaten in joy, like a *shelamim*, and joy is particularly incongruous with *aninut*. An *olah* is included — *sheken ba'ah beneder unedavah keshelamim* — because it is an act of generosity and dedication toward God, equally unsuitable for the *onen*. *Bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *Pesach* are included because *einam ba'im al chet* — they are not sin-offerings, and so their bringing reflects a positive relationship between the worshiper and God that presupposes a state of wholeness. A *chatas* and *asham* are included by the general derivation from *zevach*, every animal sacrifice requiring the offerer to be *shalem*. And finally, bird-offerings, *menachos*, wine, wood, and *levonah* are all included by the concluding term *shelamim korbano* — any *korban* whatsoever requires the one who brings it to be *shalem*. The formulation is sweeping: *kol ha-korbanot keshu mevi keshu shalem mevi ve'eino mevi keshu onen*.

### Pesach Exception

Having established that Rabbi Shimon excludes an *onen* from having any *korban* brought on his behalf — including, apparently, a *korban Pesach* — the *Gemara* returns to the *mishnah* in *Pesachim*, which states that an *onen* does eat his *korban Pesach* that night. This presupposes that the *korban Pesach* was in fact brought for him during the day. But if Rabbi Shimon excludes an *onen* from having any *korban* brought, how is the *korban Pesach* brought?

The *Gemara* asks: is the exclusion in the *baraita* perhaps meant to include even *Pesach*? *Lo levar miPesach* — no, Rabbi Shimon means only other offerings; he agrees that a *korban Pesach* may be brought for an *onen*. Rav Chisda confirms: *pesach kedi nasba* — the inclusion of *Pesach* in the *baraita's* list was incidental, a byproduct of working through the standard catalogue of *korbanos* (*bechor*, *ma'aser*, *Pesach*), and does not reflect Rabbi Shimon's actual *halakhah* with respect to *Pesach*.

### Tosafot on Pesach

This creates a conceptual tension: if Rabbi Shimon's principle of *shalem* is a genuine Torah-level requirement, what permits us to set it aside for *korban Pesach*?

Rashi resolves this by suggesting that the entire elaborate *drasha* of *shalem* is only an *asmachta* — a rabbinic ruling supported by scriptural allusion — and therefore flexible enough to be waived for *Pesach*.

Tosafot offer a more substantive and elegant resolution. All other *korbanos* have the bringing — the *zerikat ha-dam* and the sacrificial act — as their essential focus. For *korban Pesach*, however, the entire purpose is the eating. The *korban Pesach* is brought solely to enable the eating that night, which is why, unlike all other *korbanos* brought *betumah*, the *Pesach* itself is eaten even in a state of *tumah* when necessary. Because the *korban Pesach* is fundamentally defined by its eating, the requirement that the offerer be *shalem* at the time of bringing does not apply: the operative moment of *Pesach* is not the bringing during the day but the eating that night, and at night the *onen* is no longer in a state of *aninut*. He will be *shalem* when the *korban* is consumed — which is, for *Pesach*, the moment that matters.

This distinction carries through the entire sugyah. Rav Sheshet adds a final refinement: when the *baraita* says an *onen* may not send his *korban Pesach*, it means he may not bring the *shelamim* that accompany the *Pesach* — the *chagigah* offering — not the *korban Pesach* itself. The *Gemara* objects that this adds nothing to what was already said about *shelamim*, and resolves: the *baraita* teaches both *shelamim* brought independently and *shelamim* brought in the context of the *Pesach*, lest one think that since the *chagigah shelamim* comes as a package with the *Pesach* it is permitted — *ke-gufeih de-pesach dami* — as if it were part of the *Pesach* itself. The *baraita* therefore specifies that it is not. In all opinions, Rabbi Shimon holds that an *onen* may not have a *korban* brought on his behalf — with the sole exception of the *korban Pesach*, precisely because its essence lies not in the bringing but in the eating.
