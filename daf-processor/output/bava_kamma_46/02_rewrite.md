# Bava Kamma 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Right/Left Horn

### Rav's Position

The daf opens at the bottom of 45b with a ruling of Rav: *muad le-keren yamin eno muad le-keren smol* — an animal that has been established as a *muad* for goring with its right horn is not thereby a *muad* for goring with its left horn. If the ox gored three times and on each occasion used its right horn, it has not become habituated with respect to the left.

As both Rashi and Tosafot observe, Rav cannot merely be stating the obvious point that a *muad* for one type of goring is not a *muad* for another type. That general principle is already well established throughout the *mishnayos* and *Gemara* — if an animal is *muad* for goring cows, it is not thereby *muad* for goring sheep, and so on. It goes without saying that an ox that gores habitually with its right horn should not be assumed habitual with the left, and so if it then gores with the left, only half damages (*chatzi nezek*) would apply. Rav clearly does not need to teach us that. The presumption, therefore, is that Rav's ruling carries a specific implication about *shemira* — supervision — and that in some configuration, a difference in *shemira* obligations can produce a scenario where the owner is liable for one horn and exempt for the other.

### Gemara's Query

The *Gemara* asks: *aliba de-man* — according to which authority does Rav's distinction about right and left horns become halachically interesting in terms of *shemira*? The *Gemara* surveys the positions of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, which were the subject of the preceding discussion.

### Rabbi Meir's View

According to Rabbi Meir, both a *tam* and a *muad* require *shemira meula* — a high standard of supervision — for the owner to be exempt from liability. If that is so, then whether the animal gores with its right horn or its left horn makes no difference from a *shemira* standpoint: insufficient *shemira* renders the owner liable in either case, and sufficient *shemira* exempts him in either case. The distinction between right and left horns would carry no practical implication under Rabbi Meir's view.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

According to Rabbi Yehuda, the situation is more nuanced — and famously ironic. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a *muad* requires only a low level of *shemira* to exempt its owner, while a *tam* requires the higher standard. This opens up an interesting possibility with Rav's ruling. If an owner performs only a minimal level of *shemira* and the animal gores with its right horn — the horn for which it is *muad* — he is exempt. But if it gores with the left horn — for which it remains a *tam* — he would be liable, having failed to provide the higher standard of *shemira* required for a *tam*. Here, then, the right/left distinction generates a real and practical difference in liability.

### Gemara Objection

The *Gemara* raises an objection, however. If the goal is to demonstrate a scenario where a single level of *shemira* produces different outcomes for different aspects of the animal's status, one need not introduce two separate horns at all. Rabbi Yehuda's own position already contains a built-in mechanism for exactly this, through the principle of *tzad tamus be-mekoma omedes* — the *tam* aspect remains in place even within a *muad*. According to that doctrine, even an animal that has become fully *muad* retains an inner dimension of *tam* status, and so even for the right horn itself, a low level of *shemira* would still leave the owner liable for the *tam* component. There would be no need to invoke the distinction between two horns at all.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves this by confirming that Rav is indeed proceeding according to Rabbi Yehuda — but emphatically not according to the doctrine of *tzad tamus be-mekoma omedes* associated with Rav Adda bar Ahava. Rav rejects that principle entirely. In his view, once an animal is fully *muad*, it is *muad* through and through — there is no residual *tam* aspect lurking within it. The only genuine case where a single animal simultaneously presents a *muad* dimension and a *tam* dimension is precisely the situation of right horn and left horn: the right horn being *muad* and the left remaining *tam*. That is why Rav found it necessary and meaningful to articulate the distinction.

## Tam Aspect Debate

### Rav vs. Rav Adda

The *Gemara* makes explicit that Rav's ruling is a direct response to — and rejection of — the position of Rav Adda bar Ahava. Rav Adda bar Ahava had maintained the doctrine of *tzad tamus be-mekoma omedes*: that even within a fully *muad* animal, the *tam* dimension endures. This had the practical consequence that even for a full *muad*, performing only a low standard of *shemira* would still leave the owner liable for the residual *tam* component, and a variety of intermediate liability scenarios could arise.

### Full Muad Status

Rav flatly rejects this. *Ve-hachi ka'amar* — what Rav is really saying is: if you want to find a genuine case where an animal is simultaneously *muad* for one thing and *tam* for another, producing a scenario where *shemira* at a low level generates liability for one aspect but not the other, then the right horn and the left horn are the only scenario that achieves this. At the top of 46a, Rav states the principle: if an animal is a *muad legamrei* — fully and completely *muad* — there is no *tam* dimension embedded within it. The thing for which it is *muad* is one hundred percent *muad*. You cannot speak of a *tam* wrapped within every *muad*. The only way to obtain an animal that is half *tam* and half *muad* is through something structurally distinct — like a right horn established as *muad* and a left horn remaining *tam*. This is a sharp pushback against Rav Adda bar Ahava's conceptual framework.

## Rabbi Eliezer

### No Shemira Rule

The final portion of the *mishna* presents Rabbi Eliezer's striking position: *ein lo shemira ela sakin* — for a *shor muad*, there is no effective supervision except the knife. The only way the owner of a *muad* ox can free himself from liability is by performing *shechita*. Short of slaughter, the owner will always be considered liable when the animal causes damage.

### Rava's Reading

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Eliezer* — what is the reasoning behind Rabbi Eliezer's position? Rava answers by pointing to the verse *velo yishmrenu* — "and he did not watch it." Rava reads this phrase not as a description of negligent behavior but as a statement of ontological impossibility: once an animal has become a *muad*, it is in principle no longer watchable. The owner is therefore always in a state of fault — he can never be said to have genuinely supervised it. The only exit from this perpetual liability is to remove the animal from existence entirely through *shechita*.

### Pit Comparison

Abaye challenges Rava's reading. If *velo yishmrenu* means the ox is inherently incapable of being watched, then by the same token one should say *velo yichasenu* — the parallel language about a *bor*, a pit — means that covering a pit provides no exemption either, since covering it is similarly "impossible." Yet we have an explicit *mishna* ruling that if one covered the pit properly and an animal nevertheless fell in and died, the owner of the pit is exempt. We find no record of Rabbi Eliezer disputing this ruling. The analogy therefore breaks down: the textual logic Rava applies to the ox cannot extend uniformly to the pit.

### Hazard Principle

Abaye therefore offers an alternative grounding for Rabbi Eliezer's position, derived from a *braita* in the name of Rabbi Natan. The *braita* states: how do we know that one must not keep a vicious dog in his home, nor maintain a rickety ladder? From the verse *velo tasim damim beveitecha* — you shall not place blood in your house. The verse, which appears in the context of the obligation to build a parapet on one's roof, is read more broadly to prohibit maintaining any hazard in one's domain that could lead to another person's injury or death.

### Rashi's Logic

Rashi explains how this *braita* transforms the reading of *velo yishmrenu*. In isolation, the verse *velo yishmrenu* by the *shor muad* — or *velo yichasenu* by the *bor* — might simply mean: one failed to watch it, or failed to cover it. Read in that neutral descriptive sense, one who does watch it, or cover it, would be exempt. But once we know from *velo tasim damim beveitecha* that the Torah imposes an affirmative obligation to remove the *muad* ox entirely — not merely to supervise it — the language *velo yishmrenu* takes on a different meaning. Since the owner was under an obligation to eliminate the hazard and failed to do so, we read *velo yishmrenu* as expressing an impossibility: there is no form of supervision that discharges his duty. He remains liable until he performs *shechita*. The obligation to remove, in other words, interacts with the verse to rule out supervision as a viable defense.

The obvious follow-up question is why the same logic does not apply to a *bor*. A pit in one's domain is also a hazard under *velo tasim damim beveitecha*, and one should arguably be obligated to fill it — not merely cover it — in which case covering should provide no exemption either.

### Tosafot Answer 1

Tosafot's first answer draws a fundamental distinction between a living ox and a covered pit. An ox is a living creature; no matter how rigorously it is guarded, it will always strive to break free. The owner of a *muad* ox can never truly be said to have neutralized the danger in any lasting or definitive way. A covered pit, by contrast, can be rendered genuinely inert: once properly covered, it effectively ceases to exist as a pit at all. The hazard has been neutralized in a real sense. Starting from the obligation of *velo tasim damim beveitecha*, the *muad* ox therefore remains a perpetually present danger regardless of supervision, while the covered pit does not.

### Tosafot Answer 2

Tosafot's second answer operates on a different level entirely, distinguishing between civil liability and religious obligation. Tosafot observes that when Rabbi Eliezer states *ein lo shemira ela sakin*, we might initially have understood him to mean that one who fails to slaughter the *muad* ox is always civilly liable for its damages, regardless of how well he supervised it. But Tosafot suggests that once we ground the ruling in the *braita* of Rabbi Natan and the verse *velo tasim damim beveitecha*, Rabbi Eliezer may not actually be speaking about civil damages at all. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would concede that an owner who performs a genuinely high standard of *shemira* is exempt from civil liability in damages. What *velo tasim damim beveitecha* establishes is a separate religious obligation — an *issur* — to remove the hazard. Similarly, by a *bor*: covering it may fully exempt one from civil damages, yet one might still be under an independent obligation to eliminate it. The two tracks — civil liability and religious duty — need not rise and fall together. This second answer, though analytically elegant, does stretch the imagination somewhat when applied to pits in one's own home.

## Pregnant Cow Case

### Case Setup

With the declaration *hadran lach* marking the transition, the *Gemara* opens a new *perek*, still within the domain of goring. The *mishna* presents the following scenario: an ox gores a pregnant cow, and afterward one finds the fetus lying dead beside the cow. The goring clearly took place — there is sufficient evidence to establish the ox's liability for the cow. But a critical fact is unknown: *ein yadu'a im ad shelo nagcha yalda im mishe-nagcha yalda* — did the cow give birth before it was gored, or was it still pregnant at the time of the goring?

The practical stakes are evident. If the cow gave birth first and the fetus was already delivered when the goring occurred, the owner of the ox bears no responsibility for the fetus: the fetus was a separate entity, never struck by the ox, and its death cannot be attributed to the goring. But if the cow was still pregnant when gored, then the ox caused two losses simultaneously — the injury to the cow and the death of the fetus — and the owner owes damages for both.

### Mishnah Rule

The *mishna* rules: *meshalem chatzi nezek la-para u-reviah nezek la-vlad* — he pays half damages for the cow and a quarter of the damages for the fetus. The half damages for the cow is straightforward: this is a *shor tam*, and a *tam* pays half the damage it causes. The quarter damages for the fetus requires explanation. Had it been certain that the ox gored the fetus — i.e., that the cow was pregnant at the time — the owner would owe half the value of the fetus. Since there is genuine doubt as to whether he owes anything at all for the fetus, we take half of what he might owe: half of a half, yielding a quarter.

This ruling, as we shall see, reflects the position of Sumchus. But before exploring the *Gemara*'s analysis, it is worth appreciating the full structure of the case. The *mishna* also presents the reverse scenario — a cow gores an ox — but the mechanics of that case are considerably more complex and will require Abaye's clarification.

### Sumchus Principle

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel identifies the source of the *mishna*'s ruling: *zu divrei Sumchus* — this is the position of Sumchus. Sumchus maintains the principle of *mamon ha-mutal be-safek cholkin* — money suspended in doubt is divided. When there is a genuine and balanced uncertainty as to whether a financial obligation exists, and no external fact can resolve it, the parties split the difference rather than assigning the full loss to one side. Applied here: it is fifty-fifty whether the ox owes for the fetus, so the obligation is split — the owner pays half of the half he might owe, yielding the quarter the *mishna* specifies.

### Burden of Proof

The *Chachamim* disagree and articulate their opposing position in terms of a foundational principle: *ze klal gadol ba-din, ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* — this is a great rule in adjudication: the one who seeks to extract money from another bears the burden of proof. Under this principle, the uncertainty does not produce a split; instead, the owner of the ox retains his money unless and until the other party proves his liability. The owner of the cow wants to be paid for the fetus: he must prove the cow was pregnant when gored. Since he cannot, the claim fails entirely.

## Hamotzi Principle

### Principle Scope

The *Gemara* asks why the *Chachamim* found it necessary to introduce their ruling with the words *ze klal gadol ba-din* — "this is a great rule in adjudication" — rather than simply stating *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya*. The answer is that the expanded formulation signals the breadth of the principle's application: it applies even in circumstances that might seem like obvious exceptions, and the *Gemara* now enumerates several such cases.

### Bari vs. Shema

The first extension: *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* applies even in a case where the *nizak* — the injured party — says *bari*, meaning he asserts with certainty that the cow was still pregnant when gored, and the *mazik* — the damaging party — says *shema*, meaning he is uncertain whether it was pregnant or not. The *Chachamim* rule that *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* still governs: the certainty of the plaintiff's claim does not in itself constitute proof.

This invokes the broader *sugya* of *bari ve-shema* — whether a definite claim (*bari*) overcomes an uncertain response (*shema*) in monetary litigation, without any additional evidence. The question is a serious one: if I claim with certainty that you owe me one hundred coins and you respond that you simply do not remember whether you borrowed from me, does my confident assertion carry enough weight to obligate you? The *Gemara* here rules that *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* applies even in such a configuration — the plaintiff's certainty is not a substitute for proof.

Tosafot develops this analysis at some length, introducing the categories of *bari tov* and *bari garu'a*, and *shema tov* and *shema garu'a*. A *bari tov* — a strong *bari* — is a case where the defendant would know if the plaintiff were lying, and yet the plaintiff asserts the claim anyway: there is a check on fabrication. A *bari garu'a* — a weak *bari* — is where the defendant has no way of verifying the claim, so the plaintiff's certainty carries less epistemic weight. Similarly, a *shema tov* — a strong uncertainty — is where the defendant had no particular reason to know one way or the other; a *shema garu'a* — a weak uncertainty — is where the defendant should have known and his uncertainty is therefore suspect. A claim of certainty against someone who would know if they were lying, combined with a *shema garu'a*, may well tilt the analysis toward the plaintiff even under *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya*. These distinctions carry significant practical implications, though the general principle holds.

### Money vs. Rov

The *Gemara* introduces a further extension: *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* applies even in the face of *rov* — majority. In many areas of *halacha*, factual questions are resolved by *rov*: we follow the majority to determine the status of an object, an act, or a situation. One might have thought that if a statistical majority supports the plaintiff's position, that majority should function like evidence and shift the burden back to the defendant. The *Gemara* rejects this.

The discussion arrives through the case of *ha-mocher shor le-chavero ve-nimtza nagchan* — one who sells an ox to another and the ox turns out to be a habitual gorer. Rav rules that this is a *mekach ta'ut*, a sale in error, on the grounds that buyers generally purchase oxen for plowing, not slaughter, and had the buyer known about the goring nature he would not have bought it. Shmuel disagrees: the seller can say he assumed the buyer intended *shechita*, and since nothing was explicitly stated, the buyer has no recourse. Rav's reasoning rests on *rov* — the majority of ox purchases are for plowing — while Shmuel holds that in monetary matters we do not follow *rov*; the buyer must prove his expectation was betrayed.

The *Gemara* notes several layers of circumstantial evidence one might consult before even reaching this debate. Is this seller known to deal primarily with farmers or with butchers? What was the price of the ox? If plowing oxen command a higher price than slaughter animals, the sale price might indicate what both parties implicitly understood. The *Gemara* posits the case as one where no inference can be drawn from either the seller's identity or the price — because, for example, the market price of meat had risen to the point where slaughter animals were being sold at the same price as plowing oxen. In this situation of total ambiguity, the dispute between Rav and Shmuel is fully engaged.

Rav follows *rov*: the majority of ox sales are for plowing, and so the buyer had a legitimate expectation that gives rise to *mekach ta'ut*. Shmuel rules that *rov* does not apply in monetary cases: *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya*. This is explicitly offered as a separate *chidush* beyond the earlier cases. The earlier cases involved factual uncertainty about what actually happened. This case involves a question about the implicit meeting of minds in a transaction — a meaningfully different terrain. Shmuel's position is effectively a form of *caveat emptor*: unless the parties made explicit representations, no one bears a duty to disclose, and the buyer cannot extract money on the basis of unstated assumptions.

The *Gemara* distills the principle cleanly: *hanei milei ki azlinan batar ruba be-issura, aval be-mamona lo azlinan batar ruba* — the rule of following the majority applies when we are determining the halachic status of something in a domain of *issur*, but in monetary matters it does not. When one party seeks to extract money from another, *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* governs, and statistical majority does not discharge the burden of proof.

### Verse Source

Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani seeks a Biblical source for the principle of *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya*. He cites the verse *mi ba'al devarim yigash aleihem* — Moshe, upon ascending Har Sinai, instructed the people that whoever has a matter, *yigash aleihem*, let him bring it to Aharon and Chur. The *derasha* reads *yigash* not merely as "come forward" but as "bring his evidence" — *yagish ra'ayah aleihem*. The litigant, the *ba'al devarim*, is the one who must produce the proof.

### Sevara Basis

Rav Ashi challenges this derivation: *lamah li kra, sevara hu* — why do I need a verse? This is a matter of pure logic. As he puts it: *de-khaiv leih kevi azil le-vei asya* — the one who is in pain is the one who goes to the doctor. The party who wishes to collect, who is pressing the claim, is the one who bears the natural responsibility to substantiate it. The principle of *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* is an expression of basic rationality and does not require Scriptural support.

This observation itself carries some conceptual weight: the suggestion that a *sevara* carries no less force than a verse — that logical necessity is a source of law in its own right — is a significant methodological point in the structure of *Torah she-be'al peh*. The force of the *sevara*, to be sure, is not entirely self-evident: one might ask, once the plaintiff has brought his claim to court, why does the pressure to produce evidence not equalize? But Rav Ashi's intuition is that the natural asymmetry of the situation — the plaintiff goes to the defendant, not the other way around — carries through to the burden of proof.

Rav Ashi therefore holds that the verse *mi ba'al devarim yigash aleihem* teaches something else entirely: the ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha, which holds that when two litigants each have claims, the court first attends to the suit and only afterward to the countersuit — *nizkakin le-toveiah techilah*. The verse *mi ba'al devarim yigash aleihem* signals that the *ba'al devarim*, the one actively pressing a claim, comes first.

### Litigation Order

The scholars of Neharde'a add a qualification: *pe'amim she-nizkakin le-nitba techilah* — there are circumstances in which the court would first attend to the one being sued, even before the primary suit. Specifically, if the defendant's property is being devalued or his assets are being seized by creditors as a consequence of the delay in resolving his countersuit, the court may attend to his claim first in order to prevent irreversible loss. The basic order — suit before countersuit — is therefore a presumptive rule, not an absolute one.

A genuine analytical difficulty lurks here, as Rashi and Tosafot discuss: how exactly do we define which claim is the "suit" and which is the "countersuit"? From the perspective of the countersuit's plaintiff, he is every bit the *toveiah* and the other party the *nitba*. The designation of priority is not self-evidently determined merely by the sequence in which claims were filed. The basic principle is nonetheless clear, even if its precise application requires careful determination.

## Cow Gores Ox

### Parallel Setup

The *mishna* then presents the reverse of its opening case: *ve-chen para she-nagcha et ha-shor ve-nimtza vladah betzidah* — a cow gored an ox, and you find the cow's fetus lying beside it. This was a pregnant cow. The uncertainty mirrors the first case: did the cow give birth before the goring, or was it still pregnant when it gored? If it had already delivered the fetus, then the victim — the owner of the ox — can only collect from the cow itself, since only the cow was the gorers. But if the cow gored while still pregnant, the fetus was co-present within the cow at the moment of goring, and the question arises whether the fetus bears a share of the liability.

### Problem Stated

The *mishna* rules *chatzi nezek min ha-para u-reviah nezek min ha-vlad* — half damages from the cow and a quarter damages from the fetus. But this ruling is immediately puzzling. In the first case, the ambiguity concerned liability: perhaps the ox owes nothing for the fetus. Here, by contrast, the owner of the damaged ox is entitled to collect half damages from the cow without any doubt — the cow gored. The doubt is only about whether the fetus was also a contributing party to the goring, and therefore whether the fetus's value should also be available for collection. Why then does the *mishna* speak of collecting only *chatzi nezek* from the cow, as though even that amount were somehow uncertain?

### Abaye's Read

Abaye resolves this with a reconceptualization of what *chatzi nezek* and *reviah nezek* mean in this context. *Chatzi nezek — echad me-arba ba-nezek*: what the *mishna* calls "half damages" actually means one quarter of the full damages. And *reviah nezek — echad mi-shmoneh ba-nezek*: what the *mishna* calls "a quarter damages" actually means one eighth of the full damages.

To understand Abaye's reading, consider a concrete case: the ox was worth 200, and the owner is entitled to collect half damages — 100. Now, if the fetus was indeed present within the cow at the time of the goring, we would treat the cow and the fetus as two gorers who together caused the damage. Tosafot notes that in reality the division would be proportional — if the cow weighed ninety percent of the combined mass and the fetus ten percent, one would assign liability accordingly — but for simplicity the *mishna* treats them as equal contributors. So the cow accounts for half the goring and the fetus for the other half: of the 100 the victim is entitled to, 50 is attributable to the cow and 50 to the fetus.

Since there is now doubt whether the fetus was present at all, the victim can collect the full 50 from the cow — there is no doubt about the cow's role — but from the fetus only 25, splitting the 50 he might be owed because of *mamon ha-mutal be-safek cholkin*. So the victim collects 75 total: 50 from the cow and 25 from the fetus. Translating into the *mishna*'s language: *chatzi nezek* from the cow means half of the 100 he is entitled to, i.e., 50 — which is one quarter of the total damage of 200. And *reviah nezek* from the fetus means a quarter of the 100, i.e., 25 — which is one eighth of 200.

### Same Owner

This analysis, however, only applies when the cow and fetus belong to different owners. If they have the same owner — *paru ve-vlad de-chad ninhu* — the victim need not subdivide the goring between them at all. He can address the cow owner directly and say: your cow definitively gored me, and I am entitled to collect my full half damages from you. The cow and fetus together are simply one large animal under your ownership; I need not attribute separate fractions of the goring to each part. The victim can collect the entire 100 from the cow without any reduction.

### Equal Gorers

The logic of treating the cow and fetus as two equal gorers, then, only operates when they are held by separate owners. When the fetus has been sold and is the property of a different person — say, Reuven owns the cow and Shimon owns the fetus — it becomes necessary to divide the responsibility between them. The victim cannot collect his full 100 from Reuven alone while ignoring Shimon's potential involvement, nor can he simply extract everything from whichever party he chooses. The two are treated as co-gorers whose respective obligations must be assessed individually, and the doubt about the fetus's presence introduces the *mamon ha-mutal be-safek* analysis for Shimon's share.

### Separate Owners

When the cow belongs to Reuven and the fetus to Shimon, the victim faces a choice: whom does he approach first? The answer turns out to matter significantly for what each party can be made to pay.

### Defense Strategy

The *Gemara* considers whether the victim might simply go to Reuven first and demand the full 100. One could argue: Reuven, your cow definitely gored me. I am entitled to 100. If you want to claim that Shimon's fetus shares responsibility and you should only owe 50, you bear the burden of proving that the fetus was present. Since you cannot prove that, you owe me the full 100. This would be a straightforward application of *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* — though of course in this case we are operating under Sumchus's framework, since the *mishna* reflects Sumchus's view that *mamon ha-mutal be-safek cholkin*.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the scenario of *chatzi nezek* from the cow and *reviah nezek* from the fetus must reflect a situation where the victim went to Shimon — the fetus owner — first. Shimon naturally claims the fetus was delivered before the goring, so we split: the victim collects 25 from Shimon. He then goes to Reuven, who argues: *galeit da'atach de-shutafa it li* — by going to Shimon first, you have already revealed that you concede Shimon was involved. You collected from Shimon as though he were a co-gorer. Therefore, I owe you only the remaining half — 50 — not the full 100.

*Ika de-amri* — some say — an alternative reading holds that even if the victim went to Reuven first, Reuven can still limit his liability. Reuven could argue: *meida yad'ei de-shutafa it li* — it is established that I have a partner in this matter, namely the fetus owner. Since I know Shimon was involved, you cannot demand more than half from me, regardless of the order in which you pursued your claims. The victim cannot use the sequence of litigation to extract more than 50 from Reuven. On this reading, *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-raya* works in Reuven's favor: the victim cannot prove that Reuven was responsible for more than half the damage.

The *sugya* closes here with Abaye's analysis in place. As will emerge on the following daf, Rava will offer a substantially different reading of the *mishna* — one that challenges the entire framework Abaye has constructed.
