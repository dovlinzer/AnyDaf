# Menachot 64b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Omer Source & Karmel

### Intro: Omer from Karov

The *mishnah* opens with a fundamental principle governing the *omer* offering: *mitzvat ha-omer lehavi min ha-karov* — the obligation is to bring the barley for the *omer* from grain that grows near Yerushalayim. If the grain nearby has not yet ripened — *lo biker ha-karov bi-Yerushalayim* — then one brings it from anywhere in Israel, though the *mishnah* elsewhere makes clear that the grain must come from within the Land of Israel. The *beit din* did not maintain its own dedicated fields; rather, they drew from privately owned grain in the surrounding area. The *mishnah* then records two historical exceptions: *ma'aseh she-ba ha-omer mi-gagot srifin* — there was once an occasion when the *omer* had to be brought from a distant place called *Gagot Tsrifin*, meaning "the rooftops of the tent-structures," and the *shtei halechem* of Shavuot had to come from a valley known as *Ein Socher*.

### Karmel & Ma'avirin

The *Gemara* asks: what is the reason that one must ideally bring the *omer* from nearby grain? Two rationales are offered. The first is *mishum karmel* — because the Torah describes the *omer* offering using the word *karmel*, which the *Gemara* reads as a composite of *rach u-malei*, meaning soft and full. Grain brought from nearby Yerushalayim will be fresher, not yet hardened, and thus better satisfies the *karmel* quality requirement. The second rationale is *mishum de-ein ma'avirin al ha-mitzvot* — one does not pass over an available opportunity to perform a *mitzvah* in favor of a more distant or elaborate alternative.

### Ein Ma'avirin Principle

The principle of *ein ma'avirin al ha-mitzvot* has its own extensive *sugyot*. A classic example discussed earlier in Menachot concerns *tefillin*: one puts on the *tefillin shel yad* before the *tefillin shel rosh*. If one reaches into the bag and accidentally withdraws the *tefillin shel rosh* first, does *ein ma'avirin* require placing it on immediately, setting aside the proper order? In practice, one does set aside the *tefillin shel rosh* in order to first don the *tefillin shel yad*, because the importance of maintaining the correct sequence overrides the principle in that case — illustrating where these two considerations come into tension.

The application here is somewhat unusual. Normally, *ein ma'avirin* describes a scenario where one has physically encountered a *mitzvah* opportunity and must not pass it by. Here, however, the case may be that grain to the east of Yerushalayim is ripe and closer, while grain to the west is also ripe but farther away and perhaps of better quality. One has not yet physically encountered the eastern grain, yet the principle seems to require going toward the nearer source rather than passing it over for a more distant alternative. It is, in any event, a matter of *mitzvah* in the optimal sense — *be-geder mitzvah* — and not a question of *eikuva*, of whether the *omer* brought from a distant field would be disqualified after the fact. The *mishnah* presupposes that it would be valid; the obligation to bring from nearby is an ideal, not an invalidating condition.

## Hashmonean Siege Story

### Siege Context

Having established that the *omer* ordinarily must come from nearby grain, the *Gemara* now brings the *baraita* that explains the exceptional cases of *Gagot Tsrifin* and *Ein Socher*. *Tanu rabbanan*: when the kings of the *beit Chashmonai* were laying siege one against the other — *keshesharu malkhei beit Chashmonai ze al ze* — Horkanous was outside Yerushalayim conducting the siege, while Aristobolus was within the city walls. These were descendants of the Hasmoneans, two brothers contending over kingship, at various points enlisting outside alliances to gain the upper hand. The *Gemara*'s phrasing that they were *tzarim ze al ze* — besieging one another — is notably accurate: as Josephus records in the *Antiquities*, the situation reversed itself over time; at an earlier stage Aristobolus had been on the outside and had captured Yerushalayim, but at this point in the conflict Aristobolus held the city from within while Horkanous pressed from without.

Each day, those inside would lower a basket with coins down the wall — *meshalshelim lahen dinarei be-kupah* — and the troops outside, despite conducting a siege, would send up lambs for use as *temidim*. Whatever their political differences, both sides apparently recognized the sanctity of the *Beit Hamikdash* and continued to facilitate its worship.

### Traitor & Greek Wisdom

At this point, *hayah sham zaken echad she-hayah makir be-chokhmat Yevanit* — there was a certain elder who was versed in *chokhmat Yevanit*, Greek wisdom. From the context it is clear this refers to some mode of communication that could be used without detection — whether it meant the Greek language itself, or some system of signals akin to a code. *Tosafot* raises the possibility that it involved a form of visual signals. Whatever the precise meaning, this elder was a traitor operating from within, and he communicated to the besieging forces outside using this method: *kol zman she-asukim ba-avodah ein nimisarin be-yadkhem* — as long as those within are engaged in the Temple service, they will not be delivered into your hands. This is perhaps best understood not as a metaphysical secret but as a morale observation — the people inside drew their confidence and resilience from their ongoing worship, and so long as that continued, the siege would not succeed.

The very next day, those outside lowered the coins as usual, but instead of sending up a lamb, they sent up a pig. When the pig reached the midpoint of the wall, *naatz tzipornav ba-homah* — it dug its nails into the wall — and the entire Land of Israel shook four hundred *parsah* by four hundred *parsah*, that being the *Gemara*'s conventional measure for the dimensions of Eretz Yisrael.

### Consequences & Curses

At that moment, they declared: *arur she-yigadel hazir* — cursed be one who raises pigs — *ve-arur she-yelameid beno chokhmat Yevanit* — and cursed be one who teaches his son Greek wisdom.

### Burned Fields Context

*Rashi* explains the connection to our *mishnah*: a standard tactic in siege warfare was to burn the surrounding fields so that the besieged city could not draw sustenance from the area. Accordingly, all the nearby fields of barley and wheat were scorched, making it impossible to source the *omer* and *shtei halechem* from the usual nearby locations. The tradition recorded in the *mishnah* — that the *omer* once came from *Gagot Tsrifin* and the *shtei halechem* from *Ein Socher* — dates to this period. It is also possible that the distant sourcing occurred after the siege was resolved and the city was taken, at which point the besieging forces had already burned the nearby fields; this would better explain how those who brought the *omer* and *shtei halechem* could do so without depending on the cooperation of troops still outside the walls.

### Deaf-Mute & Gagot Tserif…

When it came time to bring the *omer*, no one knew where to find suitable barley — *lo havu yad'i meheika aytuyei omer* — as all the nearby grain was gone. An announcement was made: if anyone knows of a place where barley is available, please come forward. A mute person appeared — *ata hahu charesha*; the word *cheresh* can mean deaf-mute, but here the emphasis is on his inability to speak, though presumably he heard the announcement or was informed of it. He placed one hand on the roof of a nearby building — *oteiv chada yadei a-igra* — and his other hand on a *tsrifa*, a tent-like structure — *ve-chada yadei a-tsrifa*.

*Mordechai* — identified by *Rashi* as the original Mordechai of the Purim story, though *Tosafot* finds this chronologically difficult — interpreted the gesture and said to those around him: is there perhaps a place called *Gagot Tsrifin* or *Tsrifin Gagot*? For the gesture placed one hand on a roof (*gag*) and one on a tent-structure (*tsrif*). *Badku ve-ashkechu* — they investigated and found that indeed such a place existed — and that is where they brought the *omer* from.

### Ein Socher Discovery

When the time came for the *shtei halechem* and once again no one knew where to find ripe wheat, the same procedure repeated itself. Another announcement was made, and another mute person came forward. This one placed one hand over his eyes — *oteiv yadei a-einei* — and his other hand on a *sikhra*, which *Rashi* explains is the hole through which a door bolt passes, the mechanism known as a *socher*. Mordechai again interpreted: is there a place called *Ein Socher* — *ayin* meaning eye, *socher* meaning the bolt — or perhaps *Socher Ayin*? They checked and found such a place, and the *shtei halechem* was brought from there.

### Traitor vs. Mordechai

The structure of this passage is striking. Earlier in the narrative, a traitorous elder used *chokhmat Yevanit* — a covert mode of communication — to pass information to the enemy, which ultimately led to the pig being sent up and the desecration of the Temple service. Here, Mordechai uses gestural interpretation — a different but analogous form of unconventional communication — in order to restore and enable the sacred service. The methods of communication are in themselves neutral; the question is entirely how they are deployed. The traitor used such techniques to subvert and corrupt, while Mordechai used a comparable creative interpretive capacity to serve and repair.

There is also a symbolic resonance in the pig itself. *Chazal* famously note that the pig is the only non-kosher animal that possesses split hooves without chewing its cud — it displays the outward sign of *kashrut* while lacking the inward reality. The image of the pig digging its nails into the wall of Yerushalayim, pressing in as though to enter, evokes something of this deceptive quality. The Hasmonean brothers, descendants of the very heroes who fought against Antiochus's decrees, were now reenacting the central violation of those decrees: the sacrifice of swine. As the Book of Maccabees records, among the edicts of Antiochus was the command to sacrifice swine and other unclean animals. This context makes the episode all the more devastating — it is not merely a military stratagem but an embrace of the very desecration the Hasmoneans had once died to prevent.

The parallel with the Josephus account in *Antiquities* 14:2 is also notable. Josephus does not mention a pig; he describes the arrangement of exchanging coins for sacrificial animals during the Passover season, the betrayal when the animals were not delivered, and God's punishment through a destructive storm that destroyed the crops across the entire country — which accounts for the unavailability of nearby grain. The *Gemara*'s inclusion of the pig may reflect an interpretive layer that consciously connects the episode to the Antiochian decrees, placing the Hasmonean civil conflict within the same moral framework as the earlier persecution.

Regarding *chokhmat Yevanit* and the curse against it: the scholar Saul Lieberman, in his works *Greek in Jewish Palestine* and *Hellenism in Jewish Palestine*, argues that what the sources actually forbid is specifically teaching *chokhmat Yevanit* to one's son — to a young and impressionable child — because of the concern that it would open the gateway to Hellenistic culture and influence. For an adult, there was no prohibition beyond the question of *bittul Torah*. *Tosafot* notes the tension between this curse and the tradition permitting the study of *chokhmat Yevanit* to one who has already mastered all of Torah, and suggests that the curse may not have constituted a formal prohibition but rather an expression of strong disapproval, which only later developed into a more formal restriction.

## Mordechai & Bird Offerin…

### Three Women's Birds

The *Gemara* continues with another episode in which Mordechai's interpretive genius is on display. Three women arrived at the *Beit Hamikdash*, each bearing a pair of birds — *kinin* — as an offering. The first said she was bringing it *le-zivati*, for her flow; the second said *le-yamati*, for her ocean; and the third said *le-onati*, for her period. On the surface, all three seemed to be indicating conditions related to uterine bleeding and thus presenting their birds as the *korban* of a *zavah* — a woman who experiences a non-menstrual uterine flow and who, after seven clean days, brings one bird as a *chatat* and one as an *olah*.

### Mordechai's Interpretati…

Those present assumed that *zivati* meant she was literally a *zavah*, that *yamati* meant a heavy flow, and that *onati* referred to her menstrual period — all variants of the same *tum'ah*-based obligation, each requiring one *chatat* and one *olah*. Mordechai intervened. He suggested: *shema be-zov sakanah*, *shema be-yam sakanah*, *shema be-oness sakanah* — perhaps what each woman meant was not that she had suffered from a *tum'ah*-related flow, but that her flow had posed a health danger. She had been in peril and recovered, and was now bringing her birds not as an obligatory *korban* for *tum'ah*, but as a voluntary *olah* — a thanksgiving for her recovery. Under this reading, all six birds are *olot*, not one *chatat* and one *olah* per pair. *Badku ve-ashkechu* — they investigated and found that Mordechai's interpretation was correct.

### Petachiah/Balshan Identi…

This connects directly to the *mishnah* at the opening of 65a: *Petachyah al ha-kinin* — one of the officers of the *Beit Hamikdash* who oversaw the funds designated for bird offerings was named Petachyah. The *Gemara* identifies this Petachyah as Mordechai. Why was Mordechai called Petachyah? *She-poteach devarim ve-dorshanam* — because he would open up words and interpret them, as demonstrated in both the gestural communication stories and the women's bird offerings. Additionally, *veyodeia be-shivim lashon* — he knew all seventy languages — which presumably enabled this capacity for creative interpretation across linguistic registers.

### Seventy Languages

But the *Gemara* immediately raises an objection: the entire *Sanhedrin* knows all seventy languages. As Rabbi Yochanan teaches, one may only be seated on the *Sanhedrin* if one possesses wisdom, fine appearance, proper stature, seniority, and knowledge of *kshafim* — witchcraft — the last requirement understood by *Rashi* as enabling judges to counter the use of witchcraft by those being tried, and by *Tosafot* more practically as familiarity with such matters so as to properly evaluate testimony involving them. Critically, members of the *Sanhedrin* must also know all seventy languages, *shelo tehei Sanhedrin shoma'at mi-pi ha-torgeman* — so that the court need not rely on a translator, whose rendering might, whether deliberately or inadvertently, distort what was actually said. *Tosafot* acknowledges the difficulty of this requirement literally and suggests that perhaps only a portion of the members needed this knowledge, and one or two trusted members who knew the language could reliably relay the testimony to the rest.

If Mordechai was himself a member of the *Sanhedrin*, his knowledge of seventy languages was unremarkable. The *Gemara* therefore qualifies: *de-havei bala lishnei ve-darish* — what made Mordechai unique was that he could blend languages and interpret from them creatively. This is the meaning of the name *Balshan* by which Mordechai is also known in Nehemia — *bolel lashon*, one who mixes language. The capacity to think across linguistic boundaries, to hear in one language and understand what is being expressed through the conventions of another, was his distinctive gift. The *Gemara* thus identifies Mordechai of the Purim story with Balshan of Nehemia, and this identification becomes the interpretive key to his role in the *Beit Hamikdash* narratives above.

The larger thematic thread tying these stories together is the use and misuse of communicative skill. The traitorous elder deployed *chokhmat Yevanit* — whatever covert communication system it entailed — to pass damaging information to the enemy. Mordechai, by contrast, deployed his linguistic and interpretive gifts to locate grain for the *omer*, to identify a source of wheat for the *shtei halechem*, and to correctly identify the status of women's bird offerings. The methods of unusual communication are not inherently wrong; their moral valence depends entirely on the purpose to which they are put.

## Omer Harvesting Procedure

### Marking & Gathering

The following *mishnah* describes the actual harvesting of the *omer* in detail. *Shluchei beit din yotzim me-erev yom tov* — agents of *beit din* would go out on the eve of *yom tov*, the day before the *omer* is to be cut, *ve-osim otan kerichot mechubar la-karka* — and they would tie bands around stalks of barley while the grain was still attached to the ground. This was preparatory work: they identified the precise stalks they intended to cut, bound them together so they could be cut efficiently, and thereby eliminated any unnecessary delay once the actual cutting began that night. This is reminiscent of the custom of making a small incision in the *challah* before reciting *ha-motzi*, so that after the blessing one can begin cutting immediately without pausing to find the right spot — though in practice, as noted, one often forgets where the mark was made.

*Kol ha-ayarot ha-smuchot l'sham mitkansot l'sham* — all the villages near that field would gather there, *kedei she-yeikatzar be-esek gadol* — so that the harvesting would take place amid great public ceremony and tumult. The reason for this public gathering was to make unmistakably clear which day the *omer* was being cut and the counting begun, and consequently which day, fifty days hence, would be *Shavuot* — matters that were actively disputed by the *Baitusin* sectarians.

### Triple Affirmation

Once nightfall arrived — for the cutting had to take place at night, so that the *omer* could be brought as an offering the following morning, both the night and the following day being halachically the same calendar date — the procedure was conducted with elaborate public affirmation. The one designated to cut would ask the assembled crowd, *ba ha-shemesh?* — has the sun set? — and they would answer *hen*, yes. He would ask again, *ba ha-shemesh?* — and again they would answer *hen*. He would then hold up the scythe and ask *magal zu?* — shall I cut with this scythe? — *hen*. And again, *magal zu?* — *hen*. Then *kuppa zu?* — shall I use this basket? — *hen*, and again *kuppa zu?* — *hen*. If the harvesting fell on *Shabbat*, he would ask *Shabbat zu?* — shall I do this even though it is *Shabbat*? — *hen*, *Shabbat zu?* — *hen*. Finally, *ektzor?* — shall I now cut? — and the crowd would call back *katzor*, cut! *Ektzor?* — *katzor!* Each element of the procedure was affirmed three times by the assembled crowd: *ve-hem omrim lo hen hen hen*.

### Anti-Baytusin Framing

The *Gemara* explains this elaborate procedure: *kol kach lama? mipnei ha-Baitusin* — why all this? Because of the *Baitusin*, who maintained that the *omer* harvest should not take place on *motzaei yom tov* — the night following the first day of Pesach — but rather on *mimocharat ha-Shabbat*, on a Sunday. By assembling the entire surrounding population and conducting the harvesting with this repeated, unanimous public affirmation, the rabbinic authorities were not merely announcing their practice; they were framing the rabbinic position as universal consensus, with any alternative view rendered marginal and eccentric. The theatrical structure of the questioning and the crowd's affirmation was designed to convey: every normal person understands that this is how it is done.

## Tamid Individual/Communal

### Megillat Taanit Source

The *Gemara* now cites *Megillat Ta'anit*, an ancient text from the Second Temple period that records minor holidays — days on which fasting and, for the more significant ones, even eulogizing was prohibited. It is from *Megillat Ta'anit* that the eight days of Chanukah derive their status. The relevant passage reads: from *Rosh Chodesh Nisan* until the eighth of Nisan — *me-reish yarcha de-Nisan ad tamanya bei* — *itokam tamida d'lo l'mispad*: the proper practice of the *tamid* offering was established, and therefore one may not eulogize on those days. From the eighth of Nisan until the end of *Pesach* — *itosaf chaga de-shavuaya* — the holiday of *Shavuot* was established in its proper form, and one does not fast or eulogize during that period.

### Tzedukim Position

What was the dispute about the *tamid*? *She-hayu ha-Tzedukim omrim: yachid mitnadeiv u-meivi tamid* — the Sadducees held that an individual could personally donate and bring the *tamid*. Their textual basis was the verse: *et ha-keves ha-echad ta'aseh va-boker ve-et ha-keves ha-sheni ta'aseh bein ha-arbayim* — "you," in the singular, shall make the one lamb in the morning and the one lamb in the afternoon. The singular form of *ta'aseh* implies, in their reading, that an individual could sponsor and thereby "own" a given day's *tamid* offering — in effect, having today's *tamid* brought in his name.

### Pharisaic Counter

The Pharisaic response drew on another verse: *et korbani lachmi le-ishai tishm'ru* — "you," in the plural, shall observe my offerings. The plural *tishm'ru* indicates that the *tamid* is inherently a communal obligation, funded by communal *shekalim*, not by individual donation. This answer was considered decisive, though as a matter of textual argument one might equally note that singular language in the Torah can refer to the collective — "you" the nation — without implying individual sponsorship.

### Subtext: Individual vs.…

Beyond the textual argument, there is a notable subtext: it is consistently the *Tzedukim* who seek to break from the communal framework. Whether in the case of the *tamid* or, as we will see, in the calendar debates, their positions allow for individual assertion — a single donor imposing his identity on a communal sacrifice — at the expense of the shared, consensual reality. *Chazal*, writing as the victors in these debates, frame themselves as the mainstream and the *Tzedukim* and *Baitusin* as sectarians. One should bear in mind that each party considered itself the authentic carrier of tradition. The rhetorical framing — the overwhelming consensus, the dismissal of opposing positions as self-evidently absurd — is a polemical strategy, not necessarily a reflection of how evenly or unevenly matched the underlying arguments were. The debate over the *tamid*, whatever its substance, lasted eight days before resolution, which itself suggests it was not a foregone conclusion.

## Shavuot Day Debate

### Baytusin: Sunday Only

The second dispute recorded in *Megillat Ta'anit* concerns the date of *Shavuot*. The *Baitusin* held *atzeret achar ha-Shabbat* — that *Shavuot* falls on a Sunday, because the counting of the *omer* begins on *mimocharat ha-Shabbat* — the day after *Shabbat* — which they read as referring to the weekly *Shabbat*, not to *yom tov*. Under their reading, the *omer* is always cut on a Saturday night and *Shavuot* always falls on a Sunday, fifty days later.

### Ben Zakkai Dismissal

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai was assigned to engage them — *nitpal lahem Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — though the verb *nitpal*, from *tafel*, connotes something secondary or ancillary, already signaling that the engagement is not being taken entirely seriously. His opening was blunt: *shotim* — idiots — where do you derive this idea? The text reports that no one was willing to answer him: *ve-lo hayah adam echad she-hayah meshivo*. This is unsurprising given the rhetorical framing; when the presiding authority opens by calling the opposing position idiotic, few will volunteer to defend it.

### Elder's Defense

One elder did engage — *chutz mi-zaken echad she-hayah mitatpeit kenegdo* — though the verb *mitatpeit* is itself dismissive, suggesting sputtering or chattering rather than coherent argument. His position was this: *Moshe Rabbeinu ohev Yisrael hayah* — Moshe Rabbeinu loved the Jewish people — *ve-yodeia she-atzeret yom echad hu* — and he knew that *Shavuot* was only a single day. Therefore, *amad ve-tiknah achar Shabbat* — he arranged that *Shavuot* would always fall on a Sunday, *kedei she-yisrael mitangrin shnei yamim* — so that Jews would enjoy two consecutive days of celebration, the Sunday of *Shavuot* following immediately on *Shabbat*.

### Ben Zakkai Rebuttal

Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's response was to quote the verse *achad asar yom me-Chorev derech har Se'ir* — it is eleven days from Sinai to Eretz Yisrael. If Moshe Rabbeinu truly loved Israel and was willing to arrange the calendar for their pleasure, *lama ikevam ba-midbar arbaim shanah?* — why did he make them wander in the desert for forty years, when the journey was only eleven days? The argument from Moshe's love for Israel is therefore an absurd foundation for a calendrical claim. The elder's response was: *rabbi be-chach ata potrani?* — is that really how you dismiss me? — implying that the rebuttal was rhetorically clever but substantively inadequate. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's final word: *shoteh* — again, idiot — *ve-lo tehei Torah shleimah shelanu ke-sichah beteilah shelachem*: our complete Torah cannot be equated with your frivolous speech.

### Debate Assessment

The debate is troubling to read on its own terms. The *Baitusin* position is not obviously absurd: the plain meaning — *peshat* — of *mimocharat ha-Shabbat* does appear to refer to the day after the weekly *Shabbat*, and that reading is not unreasonable. The Pharisaic interpretation, that *Shabbat* here means *yom tov*, depends on a tradition that runs against the surface reading of the text. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's refusal to engage substantively — his reliance instead on the rhetoric of contempt — may reflect an awareness that the argument on purely textual grounds is not easily won. As was observed, this is a situation where one might say: argument weak, speak harshly. The deck is not simply loaded by superior logic; it is loaded by the exercise of institutional authority. The *Gemara* will spend the remainder of the *daf* attempting to supply the textual proofs that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai treated as self-evident — which in itself suggests they required more work than his dismissive posture implied.

## Textual Proofs: 50 Days

### 50 Days & 7 Weeks

The *Gemara* now turns to the textual arguments. One verse says *tisperu chamishim yom* — count fifty days; another says *sheva shabbatot temimot tihyena* — there shall be seven complete weeks. How are both fulfilled?

### Full Weeks Conditional

The resolution offered is: *kan be-yom tov she-chal lihyot be-Shabbat, kan be-yom tov she-chal lihyot be-emtza Shabbat* — one verse speaks to the case where *yom tov* falls on *Shabbat*, and the other to when *yom tov* falls on a weekday. *Sheva shabbatot temimot* — full weeks — means weeks that begin on Sunday. This is achievable only when *yom tov* itself falls on *Shabbat*, so that the *omer* counting begins on Sunday, producing seven complete weeks. When *yom tov* falls on any other day of the week, the counting begins on a day other than Sunday, and the weeks are not complete in that sense; in that case, only the day-count of fifty applies. This reconciliation makes clear that the counting is anchored to *yom tov* — to Pesach — and not to the weekly *Shabbat*. If it were always anchored to *Shabbat*, it would always start on Sunday and always produce full weeks, making the two verses redundant with each other rather than complementary.

### 50 vs. 49 Days

A further problem is that the *omer* is actually counted for forty-nine days, not fifty. Why does the verse say *chamishim yom*? This question is not addressed in the *Gemara* itself but is treated by the *Rishonim*. *Tosafot* offers two approaches: first, that the counting is *until* the fiftieth day — *ad ve-lo ad bi-khlal* — meaning the fiftieth day marks the endpoint but is not itself counted; second, reading the full verse — *ad mimocharat ha-Shabbat ha-shvi'it tisperu chamishim yom ve-hikravtem mincha chadasha la-Hashem* — the fiftieth day is *Shavuot* itself, the day of the new offering, but the counting of fifty terminates at that day rather than including it. The *Rosh* offers a third answer: the Torah engages in a form of rounding — just as the Torah prescribes forty lashes and *Chazal* derive that only thirty-nine are actually administered, so too fifty days is a rounded figure meaning forty-nine. It is notable that this question about the fifty versus forty-nine is addressed by the *Rishonim* but not explicitly by the *Gemara* here.

## R. Eliezer & Beit Din

### Singular vs. Plural Count

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees with the foregoing analysis, saying *eino tzarich* — this proof is unnecessary — and derives the same conclusion from a different verse. The verse *tispar lach* uses the singular form of the verb — count, for yourself in the singular — whereas elsewhere the verse uses *usfartem lachem*, the plural, which *Chazal* understand to mean that each individual Jew counts the *omer*. The plural implies individual, personal counting. The singular *tispar lach* points to something different: a counting that is the act of the collective as a single entity.

### Exclude Shabbat Rule

How does this counting function as a collective act? *Sefira tluya be-veit din* — the counting is dependent on *beit din*, specifically on *beit din*'s determination of when the new month begins. *She-hein yodein le-chadesh mimocharat ha-Shabbat mimocharat yom tov* — they know when *mimocharat ha-Shabbat* is, because it is *mimocharat yom tov*, the day after *yom tov*, and *yom tov* is determined by *beit din*'s establishment of the new month. This excludes the weekly *Shabbat bereishit* from playing any role — *yatzta Shabbat bereishit* — because *she-sefira be-chol adam*, the weekly *Shabbat* is not established by any act of *beit din*; it comes automatically, cycle after cycle, regardless of *beit din*.

### Pesach Dependency

The conclusion is that the counting is dependent on Pesach — on when *beit din* establishes Nisan — and not on the weekly *Shabbat* cycle. This is the point that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai treated as so obvious as to require no argument: *Shabbat* in *mimocharat ha-Shabbat* means *yom tov*, and *yom tov* is set by *beit din*, and the counting of the *omer* is therefore anchored to the determination of the Jewish calendar by rabbinic authority. The *Gemara* will continue on the following *daf* with additional proofs of this fundamental position.
