# Avodah Zarah 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Conditional Sale

### Sale Story

The daf opens, five lines from the top of 72a, with a series of cases involving the finalization of a sale — a digression triggered by the broader discussion of selling wine to a non-Jew and when such a transaction is considered complete. The first story: a man said to his friend, *hahu gavra d'amar lei l'chavrei*, "If I ever wind up selling this land, I will sell it to you" — and they performed some form of *kinyan*, presumably a *kinyan sudar*, on that agreement.

Two assumptions must be in place for this case to be legally meaningful. First, that a *kinyan* was indeed performed. Second — and more critically — that the parties did not merely make a promise, but rather effected an actual *kinyan* on the sale itself, structured as conditional: if and when I decide to sell, the transfer shall take effect retroactively from this moment. A bare promise to sell, even accompanied by a *kinyan*, would constitute *kinyan devarim* — a legally unenforceable commitment, as discussed at the beginning of *Bava Batra*. The case is only interesting if the *kinyan* was substantive.

### Rav Yosef Ruling

Against that backdrop, *azal zavnah l'inish achrina* — the seller subsequently went and sold the land to a third party, breaching his conditional commitment. Rav Yosef ruled: *kana kama* — the first buyer acquires the land. The sale to the second party is ineffective. On the assumption that the conditional *kinyan* was properly structured, once the seller decided to sell, the earlier *kinyan* snapped into place and the land was already transferred to the first buyer.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye challenged Rav Yosef: *vehalo lo pasak* — they never fixed a price. Even if the *kinyan* was otherwise properly executed, a sale cannot be final without an agreed-upon price — *psak damim*. The *ma'aseh kinyan* alone, without *psak damim*, is insufficient to finalize the transaction. No *kinyan* can be complete where the price remains undetermined.

### Wine Price Proof

Where does this principle derive from? Abaye cites our very own Mishnah: *hamocher yeino l'oved kochavim — pasak ad shelo madad, damav mutarin; madad ad shelo pasak, damav asurin.* One who sells his wine to a non-Jew: if the price was fixed before the non-Jew took possession (*meshichah*), the money is permitted — the sale is final before the non-Jew could touch the wine, rendering it *yayin nesech*. But if the non-Jew took possession before the price was fixed, the proceeds are forbidden — because the wine was not yet his when he was in a position to touch it, and it therefore became *stam yeinam*, producing a forbidden benefit. The Mishnah thus demonstrates clearly that no sale is finalized without a prior fixing of the price, regardless of whether physical possession has been taken.

### Yayin Nesech Exception

The Gemara deflects: *dilma chumra d'yayin nesech shani* — perhaps the Mishnah's ruling is a stringency unique to *yayin nesech*. In the laws of *mekach u'memkar* generally, a *kinyan* performed without a fixed price might well be final; but in the context of *yayin nesech*, the Sages treated the transaction as not fully consummated in order to impose the prohibition. One cannot therefore derive a general principle in monetary law from a Mishnah that may be operating under the special stringencies of *yayin nesech*.

### Meshicha & Price

The Gemara then seeks a proof from a source untainted by *yayin nesech* concerns. Rav Idi bar Avin reports that a case of this type arose in the academy of Rav Chisda — who was the son of Rav Huna — and was resolved from the following Mishnah: *mashach chammar u'poalav* — if a buyer led the donkey driver or the delivery workers carrying someone's goods and brought them into his house, the analysis is as follows. While the goods remain on the backs of the carriers, *shneihem yecholim lachzor bo* — either party may retract, regardless of whether the price was fixed before or after the act of drawing: the buyer has not yet completed a proper *kinyan*, since the goods are still in the possession of another person.

However, *parkan v'hichnisam l'toch beito* — once the buyer unloaded the goods from the carriers and brought them into his house, the outcome depends on whether the price was fixed: if the price was fixed before the goods entered the house, *ein shneihem yecholim lachzor* — neither party may retract. The *kinyan chatzer* combined with the *psak damim* finalizes the sale. But *madad ad shelo pasak* — if he brought them into his house before fixing a price — *shneihem yecholim lachzor* — either party may still retract. Here is an explicit Mishnah in the domain of monetary law, entirely unrelated to *yayin nesech*, establishing that even the most complete physical acquisition is insufficient to finalize a sale without a fixed price. Abaye's challenge to Rav Yosef therefore stands.

## Second Sale Story

### Rav Kahana Ruling

The Gemara presents a second, more refined case. A man said to his friend: *ee mizbanina lah l'ha ara mizbanina lach b'mea zuzei* — "If I ever sell this land, I will sell it to you for one hundred *zuz*." Here, crucially, a price was fixed. A conditional *kinyan* was performed. Subsequently, the seller went and sold the land to a third party for one hundred and twenty *zuz* — twenty more than the agreed price. Rav Kahana ruled: *kana kama* — the first buyer acquires the land. The condition was met — the seller decided to sell — and the *kinyan*, which had a fixed price, is now effective.

### Ones Challenge

Rav Yaakov mi'Nehar Pekod challenged this ruling: *hai zuzei ansuh* — the additional money coerced him. The seller argues that he was effectively forced by the higher offer. This is a striking claim. The condition of the sale was met in the most literal sense — the seller did decide to sell. And yet Rav Yaakov maintains that this form of financial pressure constitutes *ones*, coercion, sufficient to negate the effect of the conditional *kinyan*.

### Ones Analysis

The argument can be understood in two registers. The narrower reading: the seller's implicit intention when he said "if I ever sell it, I will sell it to you" was always limited to a case where he independently chose to sell at the market rate. It never occurred to him that someone would offer a dramatically above-market price, and he never intended his conditional commitment to cover such a scenario. The broader reading — captured by the word *ones* — is more absolute: the seller was not truly choosing to sell; rather, an irresistible offer compelled him. When he said "if I decide to sell," he meant a genuine, unforced decision. A sale driven by the overwhelming pressure of a windfall offer is not truly a free decision but a kind of financial coercion, and falls outside the scope of his original stipulation.

### Halacha Ruling

*Vehilchata k'Rav Yaakov mi'Nehar Pekod* — the halacha follows Rav Yaakov's position, which is a remarkable ruling: the *ones* of a dramatically higher financial offer is sufficient to allow the seller to retract from his conditional commitment to the first buyer.

## Assessment Dispute

### Three Assessors

The Gemara turns to a related case. A seller says to a buyer: *k'deshaimi bitlata* — "I will sell to you at whatever price three people assess it." The word *shaimi*, from *shuma*, is the technical language of *beit din* valuation. By using this term and specifying three people, the seller is implicitly constructing a *beit din* — a panel of three functioning as a court. Consequently, *afilu trei migu tlata* — even a majority of two out of the three suffices to determine the price, since this is the standard rule of judicial decision-making.

If instead the seller says *k'de'amri bitlata* — "as three people will say" — the verb *amri* ("will say") carries no *beit din* connotation. He is not structuring them as a court. Therefore, *ad de'amri bitlata* — all three must agree to the same price; majority vote is insufficient. Similarly, if he says *k'deshaimi b'arba'ah* — "as four assess it" — the word *shaimi* is *beit din* language, but four is not a standard *beit din* (which consists of three, and in any case must have an odd number). In that case too, *ad de'amri b'arba'ah* — all four must reach consensus. And certainly if he says *k'de'amri b'arba'ah* — neither *beit din* language nor a *beit din*-compatible number — full consensus of all four is required.

### Rav Pappa Objection

Now, assuming the seller said *k'deshaimi bitlata* — structured as a *beit din* — and three assessors came and set a price. The buyer objects: *leitu tlata achrinei d'kimlehu tfei* — bring a different panel of three who are more expert, because these assessors do not know what they are doing. This seems, on the surface, like a reasonable objection. After all, no particular *beit din* was specified in the agreement, and the buyer might reasonably argue that a *beit din* implies a competent one. Rav Pappa rules: *dina hu dema'akeiv* — the objection is legally valid; the buyer has a legitimate right to demand more qualified assessors.

### Rav Huna Counter

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua challenged this: *mimai dehani kimlu tefei* — on what basis do we presume the next panel would be any more expert than the first? The objection assumes that the replacement *beit din* will be superior, but that is far from established.

### Halacha Assessment

*Vehilchata k'Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua* — the halacha follows Rav Huna: a bare assertion that the assessors are unqualified, without concrete evidence, is legally insufficient. A mere claim, unsubstantiated, cannot override the assessment that was already performed. It appears, however, that this ruling is specifically addressed to objections made without backup support. If the buyer could demonstrate that the assessors lacked expertise and that a more qualified panel was available — for example, licensed professional appraisers — a more concrete basis for the objection might succeed. The ruling forecloses objections made out of thin air, not necessarily all objections whatsoever.

## Funnel Case

### Mishnah Setup

With the digression concluded, the Gemara returns to *yayin nesech*. The next Mishnah: *natal et hamashpach u'madad l'toch tzluchito shel oved kochavim* — a Jew took a funnel and poured wine through it into a vessel belonging to a non-Jew. To visualize the scenario: the funnel sits atop the non-Jew's wine bottle; wine from a larger pitcher flows through the funnel down into the non-Jew's vessel. When the wine reaches the bottom of the non-Jew's vessel, it becomes *yayin nesech* — either because it is now in the non-Jew's possession and susceptible to his touch, or because it mingles with drops already there. The wine in the upper portion of the funnel, however, has not yet entered the non-Jew's vessel. *V'chazar u'madad l'toch tzluchito shel Yisrael* — the Jew then reuses the same funnel to pour wine for a Jew.

### Funnel Remnants

*Im yesh bahen akavat yayin assur* — if there remain droplets of wine (*akavat yayin*, the "footsteps" of the wine) inside the funnel from the earlier pouring, it is forbidden. The problem is that those remnant drops were in contact with — or can be traced back to — the wine that became *yayin nesech* at the bottom of the non-Jew's bottle, and now those drops will mix with the wine being poured for the Jew.

### Vessel Pouring

The Mishnah then addresses a related case: *hame'areh mikli l'kli* — one who pours from one vessel into another. *Et she-me'areh mimenu mutar* — what remains in the pouring vessel is permitted. *V'et she-ira l'tocho assur* — what has been poured into the receiving vessel is forbidden. This ruling seems straightforward enough: once the wine lands in the non-Jew's vessel, it is forbidden; what remains in the Jewish pouring vessel is untainted. But the case of the funnel raises a deeper question. The wine in the funnel never entered the non-Jew's vessel directly — so why is it forbidden? This is the question that opens the central debate of the daf.

### Nitzok Intro

The answer lies in the concept of *nitzok* — a flowing stream of liquid connecting two bodies. Before examining how *nitzok* applies to *yayin nesech*, the Gemara grounds the discussion in its treatment in the laws of *tumah* and *taharah*, drawing from a Mishnah in *Taharot*. Three types of liquid connections are catalogued there: *nitzok* — a stream of liquid poured from one vessel into another; *katafres* — liquid flowing down at an angle over a surface; and *mashkeh tofe'ach* — a thin film of liquid connecting two pools.

A brief clarification on the last: *tofe'ach* means a thin layer of moisture sufficient to wet the hand on contact, but not thick enough that the wet hand could, in turn, wet a third object. *Tofe'ach al menat le'hatpi'ach*, by contrast, is a layer thick enough that touching it leaves enough moisture to wet yet another surface — liquid that is genuinely transferable. The Mishnah in *Taharot* rules that *nitzok*, *katafres*, and *mashkeh tofe'ach* are all *eino chibur lo le'tumah velo le'taharah* — they do not constitute a *chibur*, a legal connection, for purposes of *tumah* and *taharah*. Only *ashboren* — a collected, standing pool of water — constitutes a *chibur* for *tumah* and *taharah*.

What does *chibur le'tumah u'letaharah* mean in practice? Two examples: First, if a person who is *tamei* touches one pool of water connected to another only by *mashkeh tofe'ach*, does the *tumah* transfer to both? If it is not a *chibur*, it does not — they are legally distinct bodies. Second, for *mikvah*: if a full *mikvah* of forty *se'ah* and a smaller pool lacking forty *se'ah* are connected only by such a thin film, are they considered a single body of water? If not a *chibur*, one cannot be *tahor* by immersing in the smaller pool — they are two separate entities. The ruling that these are not a *chibur* means each body remains legally discrete.

There are precedents for connecting *tumah* and *taharah* concepts to *yayin nesech* — for example, earlier in the tractate, the question of whether the touch of a *zav* is the operative model for what makes wine *yayin nesech*. The question now is whether *nitzok* constitutes a *chibur* for purposes of *yayin nesech* even if it does not for *tumah* and *taharah*. If it does, then the wine remaining in the funnel is forbidden because the stream connects it to the *yayin nesech* at the bottom of the non-Jew's vessel, making them all one legal unit. If it does not, the funnel wine is entirely distinct from the *yayin nesech* below, and there is no reason to forbid it.

## Nitzok Chibur Debate

### Rav Huna Position

Rav Huna opens the formal debate: *nitzok ve'katafres u'mashkeh tofe'ach — chibur le'inyan yayin nesech*. Even if these forms of liquid connection are not a *chibur* for *tumah* and *taharah*, they may nonetheless constitute a *chibur* for *yayin nesech*, because *yayin nesech* is treated more stringently.

### Rav Nachman Challenge

Rav Nachman challenges: where does Rav Huna derive this? If the source is the Mishnah in *Taharot* — which says these are *eino chibur lo le'tumah velo le'taharah* — perhaps the inference is: *le'tumah u'letaharah* they are not a *chibur*, but *le'inyan yayin nesech* they are. Rav Nachman rejects this inference by pointing to the *sof* of that very Mishnah: it states that *ashboren* — collected water — *is* a *chibur le'tumah u'letaharah*. If we use the specification "for *tumah* and *taharah*" in the first clause to imply a *chibur* for *yayin nesech*, then symmetrically we would have to read the second clause to mean that *ashboren* is a *chibur* for *tumah* and *taharah* but *not* for *yayin nesech* — which is absurd. You cannot apply the same inferential logic to both clauses without reaching contradictory results.

### Inference Problem

*Ela me'ha leika le'mishma minah* — from that Mishnah there is simply nothing to prove. The Mishnah neither establishes nor refutes Rav Huna's position. What remains is to derive the answer from our own Mishnah in *Avodah Zarah*. The most natural explanation of why the wine in the funnel is forbidden — when the wine in the funnel never entered the non-Jew's vessel — is precisely that *nitzok* is a *chibur*: the stream connected them into one unit, and when the bottom became *yayin nesech*, the entire connected stream was contaminated.

### Rabbi Chiya Baraita

Against this reading, Rabbi Chiya teaches in a *baraita*: *she'pachastot tzluchito* — the wine vessel of the non-Jew "slapped" the wine in the funnel. That is, the reason the funnel wine is forbidden is not because *nitzok* created a connection retroactively from the moment the stream began. Rather, the non-Jew's vessel filled up to the point where wine sloshed or doubled back upward into the funnel itself. The funnel wine is forbidden because actual *yayin nesech* physically re-entered it — not because of any *chibur* created by the downward stream.

### Doubling Back Case

The Gemara now presses: if the wine doubled back, it is certainly forbidden — that is clear from Rabbi Chiya. *Aval lo pachsa tzluchito mai?* — but if the wine did not double back, what then? Can we infer that the funnel wine would be permitted, and thereby conclude that *nitzok* is not a *chibur*? The Gemara refuses this inference. Rabbi Chiya's *baraita* defines the case where the wine is *definitely* forbidden — when it doubled back. He does not rule on the case where it did not double back; that case remains undecided, *teiku*. One cannot infer permission from silence. Rabbi Chiya says: here it is definitely forbidden. He does not say: and only here.

### Second Mishnah

The Gemara turns to the second clause of the Mishnah — *hame'areh mikli l'kli, et she-me'areh mimenu mutar* — pouring from vessel to vessel; what remains in the pouring vessel is permitted. This raises two separate questions: (a) what is the status of the wine remaining in the source vessel, and (b) what is the status of the wine in midair — the stream itself? If *nitzok* is a *chibur*, the midair stream should be forbidden as part of the connected unit with the receiving vessel.

### Midair Stream Status

The Gemara attempts to read the Mishnah as proving *nitzok* is a *chibur*: the source vessel is permitted, which implies that the midair wine *is* forbidden — otherwise the Mishnah would have told us about the midair wine rather than only the source vessel. *Ha d'beini beini assur* — what is in midair is forbidden. *Shema mina nitzok chibur.* The Gemara objects: if *nitzok* is truly a *chibur*, then not only the midair wine but even the wine remaining in the source vessel should be forbidden — it is all one connected unit. Yet the Mishnah says the source vessel is permitted. This actually argues *against* *nitzok* being a *chibur*.

### Stream Breaking

The Gemara responds: *d'kamkatef kitufei* — the case of the Mishnah is one where the pourer broke the stream mid-pour. He began pouring; then, before the wine struck the receiving vessel, he righted the source vessel back up, breaking the stream. The wine already in midair then continued falling into the receiving vessel — but by then the connection to the source vessel had been severed. The source vessel is permitted because it was disconnected before the stream reached the *yayin nesech*. The midair wine, however, had already been released before the break and so remains part of the *nitzok*. *Ein hachi nami* — had the stream not been broken, even the source vessel would have been forbidden, because *nitzok* is a *chibur*.

### Ambiguity Conclusion

But the Gemara ultimately rejects the proof. The Mishnah states: *et she-ira l'tocho hu d'assur* — it is what was poured *into* the receiving vessel that is forbidden. This implies: *ha d'beini beini sharei* — the midair wine is permitted. If the midair wine were forbidden, the Mishnah would have said so. The Mishnah's silence about the midair wine, combined with this implied permission, undermines the earlier reading. The Mishnah does not tell us that the midair wine is forbidden; it tells us only that what ends up in the receiving vessel is forbidden. *Ela me'ha leika le'mishma minah* — from this Mishnah too, nothing can be proven. The question of whether *nitzok* is a *chibur* remains unresolved.

## Barrel to Vat

### Stream Forbidden

The Gemara attempts once more: *ta shema, hame'areh mi-chavit le-vor* — one who pours from a barrel into a large vat belonging to a non-Jew. *Kilu'ach ha-yored mi-sefas chavit le-mata assur* — the stream descending from the rim of the barrel downward is forbidden. This seems to be a direct and explicit statement: the midair stream itself is prohibited. Since the stream connects to the *yayin nesech* at the bottom of the vat, and since the stream is declared forbidden, this should prove that *nitzok* is a *chibur*. One might also note that this still leaves open the question of why the wine remaining in the barrel is not forbidden — but that question is set aside momentarily.

### Non-Jew Pouring

Rav Sheshes reinterprets the *baraita* entirely: *targema b'oved kochavim ha-me'areh* — the *baraita* is speaking of a case where it is the non-Jew who is doing the pouring, not the Jew. This changes everything. When a non-Jew pours *lo mevushal* wine, the concern is not *nitzok chibur* at all. The problem is *kocho* — the energy or physical causation of the non-Jew. Since his force is propelling the wine, it is treated as though he has directly handled it. This is precisely the halacha relevant to the question of whether a non-Jewish waiter may pour *lo mevushal* wine at the table: because he is causing the wine to move, it is as if he touched it.

### Kocho Concept

The Gemara then raises an objection to the *kocho* interpretation: if the non-Jew is pouring and his energy is moving all the wine, why is only the stream forbidden? The wine inside the barrel is also being moved by his actions — as the wine pours out, even the wine inside the barrel shifts toward the opening. Should the barrel wine not also be forbidden on account of *kocho*?

### Kocho Rabbinic

The Gemara answers: *koach d'oved kochavim mid'rabbanan hu d'assur* — the principle of *kocho* is entirely rabbinic in origin. Since it is a rabbinic enactment, the Sages defined its parameters. *Hu d'nafik l'varai gazru bei rabanan* — only the wine that actually exits the vessel, moving into the open, was subject to the rabbinic decree. *Hu d'ligavai lo gazru bei rabanan* — the wine that remains inside the barrel was not included in the decree. The Sages drew the line at the point of exit.

This has a striking practical implication. If a non-Jew pours your *lo mevushal* wine — say, at a table — the wine that has already been poured into the glass is forbidden on account of *kocho*. But the wine remaining in the bottle should, according to this analysis, be permitted: *kocho* does not extend to wine that has not yet left the vessel.

One might, however, argue the opposite on a different ground: once the poured wine in the glass is forbidden, and the glass is connected to the bottle by the stream of *nitzok*, perhaps the *nitzok* causes the bottle wine to be forbidden as well. But this is precisely the question at the heart of the entire sugya — whether *nitzok* is a *chibur*. If *nitzok* is not a *chibur*, then the *kocho* prohibition extends only to the poured wine, and the bottle wine is entirely permitted. The *baraita* about the barrel pouring into the vat therefore proves nothing about *nitzok chibur*, since Rav Sheshes has established that it speaks of a non-Jew pouring — a *kocho* case, not a *nitzok* case. The question of *nitzok chibur* remains unresolved.

## Practical Instructions

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda addressed the wine merchants with a practical ruling: *ki chailisu chamra l'ovdei kochavim — katfey kitufey* — when measuring out wine for non-Jews, break the stream as you pour. The concern is that the stream, once it contacts the non-Jew's vessel, will create a *nitzok* connection back to the barrel. If the barrel wine becomes connected to what has entered the non-Jew's vessel and turned *yayin nesech*, the entire barrel is at risk. By breaking the stream mid-pour, the connection is severed before it can trace back. Alternatively, Rav Chisda suggested throwing the wine into the vessel rather than pouring it in a continuous stream — eliminating *nitzok* entirely. From this instruction it is evident that Rav Chisda was concerned about *nitzok chibur* as a live halachic risk.

### Rava Warning

Rava issued a related warning to those who poured wine commercially into large vats: *lo l'karev oved kochavim l'sayaya b'hadaichu* — do not allow a non-Jew to assist you in the pouring. The concern is *dilma mishtalisu v'shadisu leh aleh* — you may become distracted and allow the non-Jew to take over the pouring entirely. Once he is doing all the pouring, *v'kasi m'kocho* — his energy is the operative cause, and the wine becomes forbidden. Even if his initial involvement was only as a helper, if he ends up performing the majority of the pour, it is treated as his action.

### Siphon Story

A practical case arose: a certain person was extracting wine from a barrel through a siphon — a *gishta* fitted with a *bas gishta*. A non-Jew came and placed his hand on the lower portion of the siphon tube, stopping the flow. Rava ruled: *asrei l'kulu chamra* — the entire barrel of wine is forbidden. 

### Siphon Challenge

Rav Pappa, Rav Adda bar Matna, and Ravina all challenged Rava: on what basis did he forbid everything? *Be-nitzok?* Is this based on *nitzok chibur* — treating the siphon as connecting the non-Jew's touch at the tip to all the wine in the barrel? If so, *shema mina nitzok chibur* — should we infer from Rava's ruling that *nitzok* is indeed a *chibur*? The very framing of the challenge suggests that the questioners did not accept *nitzok chibur* as settled, making Rava's ruling surprising.

Rava responded: *shani hatam d'kulei chamra agishta b'agishta garir* — this case is different. Here, all the wine in the barrel is being actively drawn through the siphon. A siphon functions by suction — a vacuum pulls the wine upward and through the tube. When the non-Jew places his hand on the siphon, it is not merely that wine is falling onto his hand, as in an ordinary *nitzok* case. Rather, because the entire barrel is being actively drawn through that tube, touching the tube is tantamount to inserting one's hand into the barrel itself. The mechanism of suction makes the wine throughout the tube and barrel a single, actively connected unit in a way that a gravitational stream is not.

Rashi elaborates: in a standard *nitzok* case, the wine falls downward onto the finger, but the finger is not reaching upward into the source. Here, the siphon is the agent pulling the wine out — by touching the siphon, the non-Jew is, in effect, intervening in the vessel itself. The implication of Rava's distinction is that without this special feature of the siphon, *nitzok* would not be a *chibur* — otherwise there would have been no need to distinguish the siphon case from the general rule.

### Kaniskanin Case

One final case: Mar Zutra son of Rav Nachman states that a *kaniskanin* — a communal drinking vessel from which multiple people drink simultaneously through separate straws — is permissible even when a Jew and a non-Jew drink from it at the same time. The non-Jew's mouth contacts only the tip of his own straw; the wine he draws travels toward him and does not return into the vessel. This is a kind of reverse *nitzok* — the non-Jew's contact does not travel back through the stream to contaminate the shared vessel.

*V'hanei milei d'kadim pasek Yisrael* — this is only permitted if the Jew stops drinking first. *Aval kadim pasek oved kochavim* — if the non-Jew stops drinking before the Jew, the small amount of wine at the tip of the non-Jew's straw — touched by his lips but not yet swallowed — will flow back into the vessel. That reversal renders the shared contents problematic. *Tosafot* notes the question of how those present knew who would stop first, and why they were not concerned about the possibility that the non-Jew might finish first.

## Tosafot Analysis

### Rashi vs. Rabbeinu Tam

*Tosafot* addresses the major dispute head-on. Rashi rules that *nitzok* is a *chibur* for *yayin nesech* — and indeed states explicitly in his commentary earlier in the *sugya* (*d'issur lehu b'nitzok, d'savira leh nitzok chibur, v'chein halacha*) that this is the normative halacha. Rabbeinu Tam disagrees, ruling that *nitzok* is not a *chibur* even for *yayin nesech*. This is not merely an academic debate; it has extensive practical consequences.

### Hefsed Merubeh Leniency

*Tosafot* rules that in a case of *hefsed gadol* — significant financial loss — one may rely on Rabbeinu Tam's lenient position and permit wine that would otherwise be questioned on account of *nitzok*. *Aval b'hefsed mu'at* — where the loss is small, it is better to be stringent in accordance with Rashi, treating *nitzok* as a *chibur*.

### Mixture Bittul

*Tosafot* then makes a critical structural observation. The Gemara's concern about *nitzok* is framed around *yayin nesech* — wine that is forbidden even in the smallest quantity (*b'mashehu*). But in the overwhelming majority of practical cases, the relevant prohibition is *stam yeinam* — ordinary wine that has been touched by a non-Jew. *Stam yeinam* follows the standard rules of *bittul*: it is *batel b'shishim*, nullified in a ratio of sixty to one.

*Tosafot* therefore rules: if wine is being poured from a barrel into a vessel containing *stam yeinam*, and the barrel contains sixty times the quantity of the problematic wine, then even if we were to accept that *nitzok* is a *chibur* — and the entire stream is treated as one unit — the mixture would be permitted. *Nitzok chibur* would mean only that the few drops of *stam yeinam* at the bottom are considered mixed throughout the entire barrel. But since the barrel contains sixty times that amount, the *stam yeinam* is nullified. *Tosafot* cites Rabbeinu Tam explicitly on this point: *stam yeinam* is *batel b'shishim*, and therefore the *chibur* of *nitzok* creates nothing more than a *ta'aroves* — a mixture — which is resolved by *bittul*.

### Touching Context

When, then, does *nitzok chibur* matter in practice? *Tosafot* answers: it matters when a non-Jew physically touches the flowing stream itself. In that scenario, the question is not one of *ta'aroves* — of drops mixing with a larger body — but of *maga*, touching. If *nitzok* is a *chibur*, the non-Jew's touch at one point in the stream is as if he touched the entire connected unit of wine, all the way back to the source vessel. The wine he touched is forbidden not because of mixture but because of contact. In that case, *bittul b'shishim* is irrelevant — *bittul* applies to mixtures, not to wine that has been directly touched.

### Stam Yeinam Leniency

*Tosafot* concludes with a layered practical ruling. First, in cases of *hefsed merubeh*, one may rely on Rabbeinu Tam that *nitzok* is not a *chibur* at all. Second, even where one is stringent and accepts Rashi's position, *stam yeinam* — the category most relevant today — is *batel b'shishim* in any mixture scenario, such that *nitzok chibur* only affects cases of direct touching, not ordinary ta'aroves. Third, the broader halachic context of *stam yeinam* — involving non-Jews who are not genuinely immersed in *avodah zarah*, questions of indirect contact, and the principle of *kocho* being rabbinic — provides additional grounds for leniency in many practical situations.

The upshot is that this *sugya* — which traverses *nitzok*, *kocho*, *chibur*, and the distinctions between *yayin nesech* and *stam yeinam* — carries enormous weight for practical halacha: for the question of a non-Jewish waiter pouring *lo mevushal* wine, for the status of wine remaining in a bottle after such pouring, and for the conditions under which a *nitzok* connection creates a prohibited *maga* rather than a permissible or neutralized mixture. Any practical case touching on these issues warrants careful consultation with a competent halachic authority.
