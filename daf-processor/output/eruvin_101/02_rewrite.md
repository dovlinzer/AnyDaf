# Eruvin 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Doors & Structures

### Hadeles shebe-muktza

The *mishna* at the top of daf 101 opens with the case of *hadeles shebe-muktza* — a door in a *muktza*, meaning not a door that renders objects *muktzeh*, but rather a door that opens into a back alley, an underused and largely neglected entrance to a building. At first glance this seems a surprising turn for *Masechet Eruvin*, since we appear to have left behind the domain-related questions of *eruvin* and carrying, finding ourselves instead in what looks like a random collection of *hilchos Shabbos* — not unlike the final chapter of *Masechet Shabbos*, to which there are striking parallels. Just as that tractate concluded with the *pkak hachalon*, the temporary window-stopper, this chapter addresses temporary door-like structures and the degree to which closing or opening them constitutes *boneh*, the prohibited act of construction.

The *mishna* presents several cases: *hadeles shebe-muktza*, the back-alley door; *chadakim shebepirtza*, bundles of thorns or sticks used to fill a breach in a wall; and *machatzalos*, mats similarly used to plug a hole. The principle governing all these cases is: *ein no'elin bahen* — one may not use them to close on *Shabbos* — *ella im kein gevohin min ha'aretz*, unless they are elevated off the ground. If such an object is dragging along the ground, it looks less like operating a door and more like adding a new component to the wall — an act that resembles *boneh*.

### Rashi on Back Doors

*Rashi* explains in the third line of his commentary that because this is a back door to a back alley, the homeowner does not bother to fashion a proper, hinged door for it. Rather, as *Rashi* puts it: *ein baal habayis choshesh laasos lo deles teluya kera'uy ukevua ella kefefa keneged hapessach* — the owner simply props a large plank against the opening. Such an object resembles more a construction addition to a wall than the operation of a genuine door, which is why the *mishna* demands it be elevated off the ground.

### Dragging Doors

The *gemara* opens with a challenge: *remini* — there is a contradiction. A *braita* discusses *deles hanigreret*, a dragging door; *machatzeles hanigreret*, a dragging mat; and *kankan hanigrar*, a dragging implement used to fill a hole. In each case, the *braita* states that *bizman shekshurim utluyim* — when they are tied and suspended — *no'elin bahen bein be-Shabbat bein be-Yom Tov*, one may close with them on both *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*.

The apparent contradiction with the *mishna* is this: the *braita* requires only that the item be tied and suspended, without explicitly demanding it be elevated off the ground. The *mishna*, by contrast, demands *gevohin min ha'aretz*. One can imagine an object tied by a rope to the doorframe but still lying flat on the ground — connected, like the pens chained to a bank counter, but not in any meaningful sense suspended. That distinction is not the same as *lavud*, which concerns physical proximity creating structural continuity; it is rather a question of whether the object appears genuinely integrated into the door or merely appended to the wall.

### Abaye's Hinge Rule

*Abaye* resolves the contradiction: *be-she-yesh lahem tzir* — the *braita*'s leniency applies when the door has an actual hinge. The presence of a *tzir* visually and functionally signals that the object is a genuine part of the building's door structure, removing the concern that one appears to be adding to the wall. As *Rashi* notes, *ve-lo mechezi ke-boneh* — it no longer looks like construction. This incidentally suggests that the prohibition in question is rabbinic in character, since the entire concern turns on visual appearance. Once there is a hinge, the additional requirement of being off the ground falls away; without a hinge, both conditions — tied and suspended, *and* elevated — are required.

### Rava on Former Hinges

*Rava* goes further: *be-she-hayah lahem tzir* — it suffices that there *was* once a hinge, even if the door has since fallen off its hinge and the hinge structure alone remains. So long as the remnant of the former hinge is visible, people understand that this object is conceptually a replacement door rather than a novel construction. In such a case, *Rava* holds that elevation off the ground is likewise unnecessary.

### Braita Contradiction

The *gemara* then raises *meitivi* — a further challenge from a *braita* that appears to combine all three criteria: *bizman she-kshurim u-tluyim u-gevohim min ha-aretz* — tied, suspended, *and* elevated off the ground, even if only *afilu melo nima*, a hair's breadth — *no'elin bahen*; if not, one may not. This seems to require all three conditions simultaneously, directly contradicting the positions of both *Abaye* and *Rava*.

The *gemara* resolves this by noting that *Abaye metaretz le-ta'ameh ve-Rava metaretz le-ta'ameh* — each authority reconciles the *braita* according to his own reasoning. According to *Abaye*: *o she-yesh lahem tzir o she-gevohim min ha-aretz* — either there is a hinge, or it is elevated. According to *Rava*: *o she-hayah lahem tzir o she-gevohim min ha-aretz* — either there formerly was a hinge, or it is elevated. The *braita*'s triple requirement thus applies only to the case where the item lacks any hinge, past or present.

A subsequent *braita* — *tanu rabbanan* — discusses *sochei kotzim ve-chavilin*, bundles of thorns or wood set aside to fill a breach in a courtyard wall. When these are *kshurim u-tluyim*, tied and suspended, one may close with them on *Shabbos*, and *ve-ein tzarich lomar be-Yom Tov*, needless to say on *Yom Tov*. This case presumably involves a *tzir* as well, which is why it does not separately invoke the requirement of elevation.

### Widowed Door

*Rabbi Chiyya* taught: *delet almanah ha-nigeret* — a widowed door that drags — *ein no'elin bah*, one may not close with it. The question is: what qualifies a door as "widowed"? Given that this ruling introduces an additional disqualification beyond dragging, the door is presumably otherwise compliant — it is tied, suspended, and perhaps even has a *tzir* — yet something about its "widowed" character renders it deficient.

The *gemara* offers two explanations. *Ika de-amri de-chad sheifa*: some say it means the door consists of a single plank of wood. Even if tied and suspended, a bare plank looks more like a construction material being installed than a fashioned door being operated. *Ika de-amri d'leit lei gashma*: others say it lacks a *gashma* — some structural feature. *Rashi* suggests this may refer to a bar or handle, though that reading fits awkwardly with the cases of thorn bundles and mats. A more compelling interpretation is that the door lacks a proper threshold or frame recess below, missing the structural element that would anchor it and mark it as a genuine door rather than a makeshift partition. In all interpretations, the "widowhood" of the door signals an absence — something once integral to its door-nature is missing — even if the object otherwise meets the other criteria.

## Binyan Hierarchy

### Fire Piles

*Rav Yehuda* teaches: *hai medurta* — a fire pile — *mimalla l'mata shari*, from top down is permitted; *mimatta l'mala assur*, from bottom up is forbidden. This teaching, connected to our *mishna*'s discussion of construction-like acts, launches a remarkable sequence of cases in which actions one would never expect to be problematic on *Yom Tov* turn out to carry a prohibition because they resemble *boneh*.

The principle at stake is *derech binyan* — the manner of building. When one stacks wood for a fire in the classic fashion, progressively building walls upward from the ground in a crisscross structure, that assembly-from-the-bottom constitutes *boneh* and is forbidden. The permitted alternative is to begin from the top and build downward — placing upper elements first and inserting lower ones beneath, rather than stacking upward. This is the general rule that will govern all subsequent cases: construction *derech binyan*, from the ground up, is prohibited; inverting that sequence removes the prohibition.

### Eggs & Grills

Similarly, *vchein beiza* — and so too with eggs. *Rashi* explains the case not as stacking eggs atop one another but as positioning eggs on a fire: one wishes to place eggs over a crisscross of twigs. The prescribed method is to hold the egg and construct the crisscross of twigs beneath it, rather than building the twig structure first and setting the egg on top. The structural act — creating the supportive framework — must follow, not precede, the placement of the item being supported.

### Pots & Beds

*Vchein kedira* — and similarly a pot. On *Yom Tov*, when one places a pot over a fire, the typical method employs two barrels as side walls with the pot resting in the hollow between them. The correct procedure is to hold the pot in position and bring the barrels to either side of it, rather than first setting up the barrels and then lowering the pot between them. Likewise, *vchein purya* — a collapsible bed: one must hold the mattress and place the frame structure beneath it, not first assemble the frame and then lay the mattress on top. And *vechen chavita* — barrels: placing two barrels on the ground and then a third atop them in a pyramidal arrangement constitutes building a structure from the ground up and is forbidden by the same logic.

### Tosafot Qualifications

One might ask — taken at face value, does this prohibition mean that any act of placing objects in a bottom-up sequence is forbidden on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*? Could one not pile books on a table, or arrange produce in a basket? *Tosafot* here provides essential qualifications that give the rule workable limits.

*Tosafot* begins by questioning *Rashi*'s interpretation of the egg case. To place an egg on an existing griddle — a prefabricated structure — does not constitute *boneh* at all, since one is not building walls but merely placing an object on a pre-existing implement: *ein derech binyan bekach*. The critical element, *Tosafot* argues, is not simply that items are placed bottom-up, but that one is building the walls themselves from the ground up: *lo damu lemchitzot upuraya demesader zu etzel zu*. This is the first criterion: the prohibition applies when one is constructing the enclosing walls of a structure in sequence, not merely placing objects in a stacked arrangement.

*Tosafot* further notes that it is universally accepted as straightforwardly permitted to place a pot on a tripod, and uses this as a springboard for his broader interpretation of the egg case: the forbidden act involves building up the enclosing walls *and* positioning an item within them. This leads to his second criterion: the prohibition applies when one intends to *use the space created beneath*, such as for a fire. When the space between the walls is functional — trapping heat, providing ventilation, serving the fire — the assembly has the character of constructing a functional enclosure. If one is merely piling objects with no intention of using the space between them, the resemblance to *boneh* dissipates.

*Tosafot* offers an additional, independent ground for leniency: *aval im machmirim machamat taut* — *ee nami lo damu lebinyan ela keshe'oseh al gabei karka velo al hashulchan* — the prohibition applies only when building on the ground, not when piling objects on a table. Piling books on a table, therefore, raises no concern, since it lacks the quality of ground-level construction that defines *binyan*.

### Piling Objects

The question of whether piling objects generally — including books arranged on a table — constitutes a forbidden act is thus squarely addressed by *Tosafot*'s qualifications: such acts are unproblematic because they lack both the wall-building character and the creation of an intentionally used enclosed space, and because they occur on a table rather than on the ground.

### Sadducee Debate

The section concludes with an interchange between a certain *Tzadoki* — likely a *min*, a sectarian or early Jewish-Christian — and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya*. The *Tzadoki* cites a verse: *tovam kechedek* — "their best are like thorns" — as a contemptuous description of the Jewish people, connecting *chedek* to the *chadakim* (thorns) mentioned in our *mishna*. *Rabbi Yehoshua* replied: *tipsha* — you have not read the end of the *pasuk*. The verse continues *yashar mi-mesukah* — "the most upright is more straight than a hedge." Just as *chadakim* — thorn bundles — protect a breach in a wall, so too the best among Israel protect their people, as a *sukkah* provides shelter. A further interpretation: *tovam kechedek she-machadikin et ha-resha'im l'Gehennom* — the righteous of Israel will ultimately trample the wicked, as expressed in the verse *kumi v'dushi bat Tziyon* — "Arise and thresh, O daughter of Zion," with the promise of iron horns and copper hooves to trample the nations. The connection to our *mishna* is thus not merely textual wordplay but a pointed halakhic and polemical exchange rooted in the specific terminology of the chapter.

## Cross-Domain Issues

### Rabbi Meir's Rule

The next *mishna* returns to domain-related concerns: *lo yaamod adam bi-reshut ha-yachid veyiftach bi-reshut ha-rabim* — a person may not stand in the private domain and use a key to open a door into the public domain, nor vice versa. The underlying issue is *Rabbi Meir*'s well-known position, encountered in previous discussions, that one may not stand in one domain and manipulate objects in another. Here is a concrete expression of that ruling: if one's front porch is enclosed and constitutes a *reshut ha-yachid*, and the key lies there, one may not lean in from the *reshut ha-rabim* to retrieve it and open the lock. The solution the *mishna* provides is to have a wall of ten *tefachim* — *ella im kein asa mechitzah gevohah asara tefachim* — so that one is standing within a *reshut ha-yachid* and the entire operation occurs within a single domain.

### Patamin Marketplace

The sages challenge *Rabbi Meir* with a precedent: *maaseh be-shuk shel patamin she-hayu b'Yerushalayim* — there was an established practice in the marketplace of the fat-sellers (*patamin* — dealers in fattened fowl or other goods) in Jerusalem, where the shopkeepers would lock up and leave the key in the window above the doorway, retrieving it on *Shabbos* to open their shops. *Rabbi Yose* adds a point of precision: it was in fact a wool marketplace, not a poultry market — but the principle is the same. Presumably these keepers were standing in what appeared to be a *reshut ha-rabim* while manipulating an object in the *reshut ha-yachid* zone of the lock and windowsill — a direct challenge to *Rabbi Meir*'s position.

### Jerusalem Status

The *gemara* responds that *Rabbi Meir* can deflect this evidence: *rabbanan amru Rabbi Meir reshut ha-rabim, u-mahadar inhu karmelis?* The sages are citing a *karmelis* in response to a ruling about a biblical *reshut ha-rabim*. Even the marketplaces of Jerusalem were at most a *karmelis*, not a true *reshut ha-rabim*, because Jerusalem was surrounded by walls with gates locked at night.

The basis for this is the statement of *Rabba bar bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *Yerushalayim ilmalei daltoteha nin'alot balailah chayavin aleha mishum reshut ha-rabim* — Jerusalem would otherwise be a *reshut ha-rabim*, but since its gates were locked at night, it was not. This implies that at the time the gates were intact and locked, the marketplaces were at most a *karmelis*; and so the precedent cited by the sages does not address *Rabbi Meir*'s concern about a true *reshut ha-rabim*.

*Rav Pappa* refines this further: *kan kodem she-nifretzu bo pratzot, kan l'achar she-nifretzu bo pratzot* — the distinction turns on whether Jerusalem's walls had yet been breached. Before the breaches — that is, in the period of the intact walls before the *churban* — the marketplaces were a *karmelis*. After the walls were breached by the enemy, the walls ceased to function as genuine enclosures, and the marketplaces reverted to the status of a true *reshut ha-rabim*. The *mishna*'s ruling thus pertains to the post-*churban* reality in which *Rabbi Meir* could indeed encounter real *reshut ha-rabim* conditions in what had been Jerusalem's markets.

*Rava* then draws out an additional implication: *seifa asya l'sharei gina* — the *mishna*'s closing clause actually extends *Rabbi Meir*'s ruling to a *karmelis*. The fuller reading of the *mishna* is: *lo yaamod adam bi-reshut ha-yachid veyiftach b'karmelis, b'karmelis veyiftach bi-reshut ha-yachid* — one may not stand in a *karmelis* and open into a *reshut ha-yachid*, or vice versa — unless one has erected a wall of ten *tefachim*, placing oneself entirely within a *reshut ha-yachid*. This is the evidence the sages are bringing from the Jerusalem marketplace — since that marketplace was a *karmelis*, they demonstrate that *Rabbi Meir* prohibits even standing in a *karmelis* and manipulating into a *reshut ha-yachid*.

### Garden Gates

The *gemara* then presents a *braita* about *pitchai shaarei gina* — the gates of a garden, which are typically a *karmelis* scenario. The governing rule is: *bizman sheyesh lahem beis shaar mipifnim* — when there is a gatehouse on the inside, one opens and closes from the inside; when on the outside, from the outside; and when gatehouses exist on both sides, one may operate from either direction. The reason in all these cases is that the gatehouse — itself a *reshut ha-yachid* — allows the person to be physically located within a *reshut ha-yachid* when performing the operation, so that everything occurs within a single domain.

*Ein lahem lo kan v'lo kan asurin* — if there is no gatehouse on either side, the operation is forbidden in both directions. *Rashi* explains: the lock mechanism on the door is itself a *reshut ha-yachid*, and the key placed upon it is therefore located in a *reshut ha-yachid*. What *Rabbi Meir* prohibits is standing in a *karmelis* — even a garden, not even a *reshut ha-rabim* — and taking a key from the *reshut ha-yachid* lock and operating it. Even though the key moves from *reshut ha-yachid* to *reshut ha-yachid* (lock to keyhole), *Rabbi Meir* is concerned that the person standing in the *karmelis* is engaged in manipulating objects across domains. In the absence of a gatehouse, the person unavoidably stands in the *karmelis*, and that is forbidden according to *Rabbi Meir*.

### Store Locks

A parallel case follows: *ve-chen chanuyos ha-petuchos li-reshut ha-rabim* — stores that open onto a *reshut ha-rabim*, where the lock mechanism is a contraption attached to or recessed into the door. The *braita* distinguishes based on the height of the lock mechanism:

*Bizman she-hamanul lemata me-asara* — when the lock is below ten *tefachim* in height, it is not a *reshut ha-yachid*. In this case one may bring the key from *erev Shabbos* and leave it on the *iskopa* — the porch or threshold — which is a *karmelis*. The next day one opens the lock and returns the key to the *iskopa*, all within the *karmelis* zone. This is permissible even according to *Rabbi Meir*.

*Bizman she-hamanul lemala me-asara* — when the lock is above ten *tefachim* and thus a *reshut ha-yachid*, *Rabbi Meir* says: bring the key from *erev Shabbos* and place it *in the lock itself*. The next day open and re-lock and return it to its place. This is counterintuitive at first — *Rabbi Meir* who objects so strenuously to cross-domain manipulation now appears to allow standing in a *karmelis* and using a key planted in a *reshut ha-yachid* lock. The *gemara* will need to address this.

*Ve-Chachamim omrim* — the sages say that even when the lock is above ten *tefachim*, one may bring the key from *erev Shabbos* and leave it on the *iskopa* (a *karmelis*), and the next day open and either return it to the *iskopa* or place it in the window above the doorway — *o be-chalon she-al gabei ha-petach* — provided the window is less than four by four *tefachim*. If the window is four by four, it is *assur*, because it constitutes a *reshut ha-yachid*, and taking the key from the *karmelis* into the window would be taking from a *karmelis* to a *reshut ha-yachid*.

*Rashi* notes that store lock mechanisms, unlike house gates, vary in height — sometimes low, sometimes high — which is precisely what makes them a useful vehicle for illustrating the full range of this ruling.

### Abaye's Lock Solution

The *gemara* presses the difficulty: if the *iskopa* is a *karmelis*, and if the lock mechanism — even when above ten *tefachim* — could theoretically be a *reshut ha-yachid*, how can the sages permit taking the key from the *iskopa* and inserting it into the lock? That would be *kamitaltel mi-karmelis li-reshut ha-yachid* — taking an object from a *karmelis* to a *reshut ha-yachid*, which is rabbinically prohibited.

*Abaye* resolves this: *le'olam d'leit bei arba'ah* — in fact the lock mechanism does not have the full four-by-four dimensions required for a *reshut ha-yachid*. However, *yesh bo lachok u-lehashlimo l'arba'ah* — it is the kind of recess in the wall that could in principle be carved out to reach the four-by-four threshold. This is a familiar *gemara* consideration: does theoretical capacity for four-by-four dimensions confer *reshut ha-yachid* status?

*B'ha pligi* — this is the precise point of dispute: *Rabbi Meir savar chokekim lehashlim* — *Rabbi Meir* holds that we treat a space that can be enlarged to four-by-four *as if* it already were, giving it *reshut ha-yachid* status. *Rabbanan savri ein chokekim lehashlim* — the sages hold that we do not apply this principle, and since the recess does not presently measure four by four, it is not a *reshut ha-yachid*, and the entire operation remains within a *karmelis* or *makom patur* zone, free of any transfer prohibition.

This analysis also resolves the apparent reversal in *Rabbi Meir*'s position. He does not in fact permit a *karmelis*-to-*reshut ha-yachid* manipulation in the case of the high lock; rather, he treats the lock as a *reshut ha-yachid* and requires that the key be placed there from *erev Shabbos*, so that the operation on *Shabbos* takes place entirely within the *reshut ha-yachid*. The sages, not treating the lock as a *reshut ha-yachid*, permit leaving the key on the *karmelis* and operating from there.

### Window Restrictions

The final clause of the *braita* concerns the window above the doorway. After opening the store, one may return the key either to the *iskopa* or to the window — but only if the window is less than four by four *tefachim*. If the window is four by four, placing the key there is *assur* — *mipnei she-hu ke-motzi me-reshut li-reshut* — because it would constitute carrying from the *karmelis* to the *reshut ha-yachid*.

The principle at work here is that even passing through a *makom patur* does not sanitize a transfer between two incompatible domains. So while one may move an object from the *karmelis* to the *makom patur* lock, and separately from the *makom patur* lock to the *karmelis* or to another *makom patur* window, one may not use the *makom patur* as a relay station to move an object from the *karmelis* to a *reshut ha-yachid* window.

### Rav Dimi Teaching

*Rabbi Bibi bar Abba* notes that three distinct conclusions (*shma mina telat*) emerge from this *braita*. First, *chokekim lehashlim* — we accept this principle as operative, at least according to *Rabbi Meir*. Second, *Rabbi Meir* has reversed himself from his earlier ruling about garden gates: here he permits standing in a *karmelis* and operating a key in a *reshut ha-yachid*, which is consistent only if one understands that *Rabbi Meir* restricts his prohibition to a true *reshut ha-rabim*, not a *karmelis*. Third, we accept the teaching of *Rav Dimi*.

*De-ki asa Rav Dimi amar Rabbi Yochanan*: when *Rav Dimi* came from *Eretz Yisrael* he reported that *Rabbi Yochanan* taught — *makom she-ein bo arba al arba, mutar li-bnei reshut ha-rabim u-li-bnei reshut ha-yachid le-katef alav* — a space that is less than four by four *tefachim* is a *makom patur* and may be used by people of either the *reshut ha-rabim* or the *reshut ha-yachid* — *u-bilvad she-lo yachlifu* — provided they do not use it as an exchange point between the two. One may not take something from the *karmelis*, set it down on the *makom patur*, and then take it into the *reshut ha-yachid*; that chain of transit violates the prohibition even though each individual step is technically permissible. This is confirmed by the *braita*'s ruling that returning the key to a window that is four by four is forbidden: moving from *karmelis* to *makom patur* to *reshut ha-yachid* is a prohibited sequence, even via the intermediate exempt space.

## Nagar & Glustra

### Rabbi Eliezer's Rule

The final *mishna* of the daf introduces the case of a *nagar she-yesh be-rosho glustra* — a locking bar with a bulbous head. The scenario is familiar in structure to the suspended-door cases that opened the daf: one wishes to use this bar to bolt the door on *Shabbos*, but the question is whether inserting it constitutes *boneh*. The bar hangs from the door, and in this regard it parallels the discussion of *neger ha-talui* from earlier in the tractate. The additional element here is the *glustra* — the bulbous protuberance at the head of the bar, which resembles a pestle and theoretically qualifies the bar as a *kli*, a vessel in its own right (something one could, for instance, use to pound spices).

*Rabbi Eliezer oser* — *Rabbi Eliezer* forbids using it unless it is *kashur ve-talui* — tied to the door and suspended — which, as established in the opening section of the daf, is what integrates the object into the door structure and removes the appearance of *boneh*.

### Rabbi Yose Lenient

*Rabbi Yose matir* — *Rabbi Yose* permits it even without being tied and suspended. His reasoning: since the bar has a *glustra* and thus the character of a *kli*, using it to bolt the door is clearly an ad-hoc, temporary act — as if one took a spoon and lodged it in the door brackets as a makeshift bolt. No one considers this the installation of a structural component; the object's vessel-nature makes its temporary use self-evident. According to *Rabbi Yose*, the *kli* character of the bar eliminates the concern that one is doing anything resembling *boneh*.

### Tiberias Synagogue

*Rabbi Eliezer* then cites a historical precedent: *maaseh bi-chneset she-be-Tverya* — a story from the synagogue in Tiberias. The community there had maintained a practice of permitting this bar (*she-hayu noghim bo heter*), until *Rabban Gamliel* and the elders came and declared it forbidden. This is *Rabbi Eliezer*'s evidence that the permissive practice was ultimately overturned by rabbinic authority.

*Rabbi Yose* disputes the facts: *issur nahagu bo* — they had actually been treating it as forbidden, and *Rabban Gamliel* and the elders came and declared it permitted. The same event, the same synagogue, but the opposite narrative. Both authorities invoke Tiberias; each claims that the final ruling of *Rabban Gamliel* and the elders supports his own position.

### Rope Lifting Test

The *gemara* resolves the dispute by introducing a functional criterion. *Bi-nittol be-agdo kulei alma lo pligi* — if the bar can be lifted by its rope (i.e., the rope is strong enough to bear it and raise it), all agree it is permitted, since it is clearly and functionally integrated into the door structure. *Ki pligi be-she-eino* — the dispute concerns the case where the rope cannot bear the bar's weight and would break if used to lift it.

In that case: *mar savar keivan de-ika berosha glustra torat kli aleiha* — one authority holds that since it has a *glustra* and the character of a *kli*, it retains its vessel-identity and its use is clearly temporary and non-constructive. *U-mar savar keivan de-la nital be-agdo lo* — the other holds that since it cannot be lifted by its rope, it is not genuinely integrated into the door mechanism, and inserting it into the bolt-brackets has the appearance of adding to the structure.

### Tosafot Chumra

*Tosafot* raises a question on the *Rabbi Yose* side of the Tiberias case. The *gemara* in *Nedarim* teaches that if a community has adopted a stringent practice regarding something otherwise permitted — *devarim hamutarim ve-chen nahagu bahen issur* — one may not come and declare it permitted in their presence, lest they come to treat forbidden things lightly. How, then, could *Rabban Gamliel* and the elders come and permit what the Tiberias community had been observing as forbidden?

*Tosafot* answers with a crucial distinction: *dehaynu keshe-yod'im she-mutar u-machmirim al atzman* — that rule applies only when the community *knows* the practice is permitted but has voluntarily adopted a stringency. In that case, you do not override their accepted *chumra*, because doing so risks training people to dismiss self-imposed stringencies indiscriminately, potentially leading to the violation of genuine prohibitions: *shelo yargilu bnei adam lehakeil veyavo'u lehatir issur*.

But *aval im machmirim machamat taut* — if they are observing the stringency because of a mistake, because they believe it to be genuinely required when it is not, then *mutar lehatir bifneihem* — it is not only permitted but appropriate to correct the error. One need not and should not buy into the ignorance of others. This principle extends beyond the Tiberias case: *Rav Moshe* articulates a parallel rule regarding *marit ayin* — the concern about appearances applies when people might misread the facts, not when they have a mistaken understanding of the law itself. Where the error is one of law, the correct response is correction and education, not accommodation of the misconception.
