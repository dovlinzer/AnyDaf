# Kiddushin 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eye/Tooth Damage

### Usability Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִכָּהוּ עַל עֵינוֹ וְכָהֲתָה, עַל שִׁינּוֹ וְנָדְדָה, אִם יָכוֹל לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עַכְשָׁיו – אֵין עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת, וְאִם לָאו – עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 9:9): In a case where the master **struck him on his eye and weakened** his vision without blinding him, or he struck him **on his tooth and loosened it, if he can use** the eye or the tooth **now,** the **slave is not emancipated by means of them. And if** he can **no** longer use them at all, then the **slave is emancipated by means of them.**

The daf opens on 24b, several lines before the text widens, with a *baraita* addressing the *eved kena'ani* and the question of when damage to an eye or tooth entitles him to freedom. The *tana* teaches: if the master struck the slave's eye and it became dim (*hikah lo eino*), or struck his tooth and it became loose (*shino venadeda*)—without totally knocking it out or gouging it entirely from the body—the measure is functional usability. *Im yachol lehishtamesh bahen achshav*, if it is still usable, he does not go free; *ve-im lav*, if it is no longer usable, he does go free. Precisely what threshold of residual vision or stability constitutes "usability" is left open, but the operative principle is clear: functionality, not structural completeness, governs.

### Pre-existing Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיְתָה עֵינוֹ כְּהוּיָה וְסִמְּאָהּ, שִׁינּוֹ נְדוּדָה וְהִפִּילָהּ, אִם יָכוֹל לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן כְּבָר – עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught **in another** clause in that *baraita*: In a case **where** the slave's **eye was weak** prior to this incident, **and** the master **blinded it** entirely, or if the slave's **tooth was loose and** his master **knocked it out, if he** was **able to use them before** the master struck him, the **slave is emancipated by means of them, and if not,** then the **slave is not emancipated by means of them.**

A second *baraita* addresses the mirror image: what if the slave's eye was already dim before the master's act, and the master then brought it from dim to totally blind? Or the tooth was already loose, and the master then knocked it out entirely? Again the measure is usability. *Im yachol lehishtamesh bahen kevar*—if the eye or tooth was still usable beforehand, then by taking it from usable to unusable the master has crossed the threshold and the slave goes free. *Ve-im lav*—if it was already unusable beforehand, he does not.

### Necessity of Both Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן הָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא נְהוֹרָא בְּרִיָּא וְהַשְׁתָּא נְהוֹרָא כְּחִישָׁא. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא נָמֵי נְהוֹרָא כְּחִישָׁא – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן הָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּסַמְּיַהּ לִגְמָרֵי, אֲבָל הָתָם, דְּלָא סַמְּיַהּ לִגְמָרֵי אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** to state both *halakhot*, despite the fact that they apparently teach the same *halakha* that a weakened eye is not considered a significant injury, **as, if** the *tanna* had **taught us** only **this first** *halakha*, I would say that he is emancipated, **because at the outset** his **vision was strong and now** his **vision is weak. But here,** in the second *baraita*, **where his vision was also weak at the outset,** I would **say** he is **not** emancipated if the master blinded him entirely, as his eye did not function properly even beforehand. **And** conversely, **if** the *tanna* had **taught us** only **this** second *halakha*, I would say that he is emancipated **because he blinded** him **entirely. But there,** where **he did not blind** him **entirely** I would **say no,** he is not emancipated, despite the fact that he weakened his vision. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state both *halakhot*.

The *gemara* asks why both directions need to be taught, since both reduce to the same principle. The answer is that each case is genuinely surprising without the other. Had we only heard the first case—going from full sight to functionally blind—one might reason that this applied only because the master caused a radical deterioration: *mishum d'me-ikara nehora bari ve-hashta nehora kakhisha*. But where the eye was already barely usable to begin with, perhaps even bringing it to total blindness would not suffice to free the slave. Conversely, had we only heard the second case—bringing an already-dim eye all the way to total blindness—one might think that applied only because he reduced the slave to absolute zero (*samya l'gamre*). But where the master merely brought a functioning eye down to a non-functional but not totally sightless state, one might think the slave does not go free. *Tzericha*: both cases are necessary.

## Intent & Act Nature

### Accidental vs. Intention…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה רַבּוֹ רוֹפֵא, וְאָמַר לוֹ לִכְחוֹל לוֹ עֵינוֹ וְסִמְּאָהּ, לַחְתּוֹר לוֹ שִׁינּוֹ וְהִפִּילָהּ – שִׂיחֵק בָּאָדוֹן, וְיָצָא לְחֵירוּת. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: ״וְשִׁחֲתָהּ״ – עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוֵּין לְשַׁחֲתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 9:9): In a case **where his master was a doctor and** the slave **said to him to paint his eye** with a medicament, **and** the master **blinded it** in the process, or he asked him **to drill his** aching **tooth, and** the master **knocked it out,** the slave **laughs at the master and is emancipated. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says** that the verse: "And if a man smites the eye of his slave, or the eye of his maidservant, **and destroys it,** to freedom shall he send him for his eye's sake" (Exodus 21:26), means that the slave is not emancipated **unless** the master **intends to destroy it.** If he intended to heal him, the slave is not freed, even if the master did, in fact, injure him.

The *gemara* next presents a *baraita*: what if the master himself was a doctor, and the slave asked him to apply an ointment to his eye (*lechkhol lo eino*), or to treat a tooth (*lachator lo shino*), and in the course of this therapeutic intervention the master accidentally blinded the eye or knocked out the tooth? The *baraita* rules: *sicheik b'adon ve-yatzah le-cheirut*—the slave laughs in his master's face and goes free. The unintended result governs; the therapeutic purpose does not shield the master.

### Intent Requirement

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents. He reads the word *ve-shichatah*—"and he destroys it"—in the relevant verse as describing not merely the result but the nature of the act: *ad she-yitkavein le-shachtah*, only when the master intended to destroy. This would exclude a therapeutic act that accidentally causes injury. Critically, an intentional act of violence—say, striking the slave in the face and thereby knocking out a tooth—would still qualify, even if the master was not specifically aiming at the tooth, because the act is fundamentally a destructive one directed at the slave's body. What is excluded is an act that is therapeutic in character and accidentally produces a harmful result.

### Fetus Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וְשִׁחֲתָהּ״, מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי שֶׁהוֹשִׁיט יָדוֹ לִמְעֵי שִׁפְחָתוֹ וְסִימֵּא עוּבָּר שֶׁבְּמֵעֶיהָ – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְשִׁחֲתָהּ״ – עַד שֶׁיְּכַוֵּין לְשַׁחֲתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis, what do they do with this** verse **"and destroys it"?** What do they derive from this phrase? The Gemara answers: **They require** it **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar says** that in a case **where** the master **stuck his hand into the womb of his maidservant** to assist her in giving birth **and he blinded the fetus in her womb, he is exempt** and the fetus is not emancipated when born. **What is the reason** for this? **As the verse states "and destroys it,"** meaning **unless he intends to destroy** it, and in this case he did not mean to touch the fetus's eye.

Rabbi Elazar teaches: if a man inserted his hand into the womb of his maidservant during childbirth, attempting to assist in delivering the baby, and in doing so accidentally blinded the eye of the fetus, he is *patur*—the child does not go free. The reason: *amar kra ve-shichatah, ad she-yitkaven le-shachtah*. Here the man was not relating to the eye at all—he was trying to extract the fetus and was not even aware of, let alone directing his actions toward, the eye of the unborn child.

This raises a subtle distinction. If a master punches a slave in the face and knocks out a tooth, he did not intend the tooth specifically—he intended the face. Yet this would presumably qualify because hitting the face in that region is, in the words of the *gemara*'s framework, something like a *psik reisha*: the tooth is within the scope of the violent act. By contrast, reaching into the womb to assist in a birth is not in any way directed at the eye of the fetus. One is not even aware of being near that area. That categorical difference is what the *Rabbanan* derive from *ve-shichatah*.

### Veshichatah Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ? מִ״וְשִׁחֵת״ ״שִׁחֲתָהּ״ נָפְקָא. וְאִידַּךְ? ״שִׁחֵת״ ״שִׁחֲתָהּ״ לָא דָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other** Sage, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, would say that that *halakha* is **derived from** the fact that the verse could have said: **And destroys,** and instead said **"destroys it." And the other** Sage, i.e., the Rabbis, how do they respond to this argument? **They do not expound** the difference between **"destroys"** and **"destroys it,"** i.e., they maintain that this is not a significant deviation from which one can derive a *halakha*.

The *gemara* clarifies the underlying dispute. The *Rabbanan* read *ve-shichatah* as establishing that even they require some connection between the act and the injured limb—the fetus case is exempt because there is no connection whatsoever. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel reads the same word as requiring positive destructive intent. *Ve-idach*: one position derives from the doubled form *shachat ve-shichatah* an additional teaching; the other holds that no such *derasha* from the doubled form is operative (*shachat ve-shichatah lo darish*). Rashi's treatment of the case where someone hit nearby and the slave was startled or frightened (*mav'it*) fills in the bracketing question of what it means to strike "in the vicinity" rather than directly.

## Visible Limbs

### Complete Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיְתָה עֵינוֹ סְמוּיָה, וַחֲטָטָהּ – עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? מְחוּסָּר אֵבֶר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says:** In a case **where** the slave's **eye was blind and** the master **removed it** entirely, the **slave is emancipated by means of** this injury. **What is the reason** for this? **He is** now **lacking a limb,** which is worse than a mere blemish.

Rav Sheshet introduces a further ruling: *harei she-hayeta eino smuya ve-chitatah*—if the slave's eye was already functionally blind and the master then gouged it out entirely (*chitatah*, removing it from the socket), the slave goes free. This is surprising, since there has been no functional change; the slave could not see before and cannot see now. Nevertheless, *mechusar eivar hu*—he is now missing a limb, and that structural change to the body is itself sufficient. The principle is that the loss of an external limb, even one that was already non-functional, effects the slave's freedom.

### 24 Limb Heads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא תּוּנָא: תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בִּבְהֵמָה, וְאֵין תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בְּעוֹפוֹת. יָכוֹל יָבְשָׁה גַּפָּהּ, נִקְטְעָה רַגְלָהּ, נֶחְטְטָה עֵינָהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִן הָעוֹף״ – וְלֹא כׇּל הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **And the *tanna*** of a *baraita* also **taught:** The *halakha* of **unblemished status,** i.e., that an offering must not contain a blemish, **and** the *halakha* of **male status** apply **to animal** offerings, **but** the *halakha* of **unblemished status and** the *halakha* of **male status** do **not** apply **to bird** offerings. The Gemara comments: One **might** have thought that if one brings a bird **whose wing is dried,** or whose **leg is severed,** or whose **eye was removed,** that it too is acceptable as an offering. Therefore, **the verse states:** "And if his offering to the Lord is a burnt-offering **from birds"** (Leviticus 1:14). This phrase indicates that only some birds can be sacrificed as offerings, **but not all birds.** This teaches that a bird can be disqualified due to a blemish. For example, if a significant part of the bird is missing, e.g., a limb, the bird is not acceptable as an offering. This indicates that the actual loss of an eye is considered a greater blemish than blindness. Consequently, if his master takes out the slave's blind eye, he is emancipated.

The *gemara* supports this from a *baraita* that itself draws on the laws of *tzara'at*: *tamim ve-zachrut be-vehemah ve-ein tamim ve-zachrut be-ofot*—the requirements of being unblemished and male apply to large animal sacrifices but not to bird sacrifices. One might therefore think that a bird with a withered wing, a severed leg, or a gouged-out eye would still be valid for a sacrifice. The verse says *min ha-of*—from the birds, implying not all birds—excluding a bird that is *mechusar eivar*, lacking a limb. Rashi emphasizes that this applies *af al gav de-sumya me-ikara*, even if the bird was already blind beforehand, since bird sacrifices may be blemished—yet the final act of removing the eye is still considered a substantive change that renders it *mechusar eivar*.

### 24 Limb Heads (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה רָאשֵׁי אֵבָרִים שֶׁבָּאָדָם כּוּלָּם אֵין מְטַמְּאִין מִשּׁוּם מִחְיָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵם: רָאשֵׁי אֶצְבָּעוֹת יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, וְרָאשֵׁי אׇזְנַיִם, וְרֹאשׁ הַחוֹטֶם, וְרֹאשׁ הַגְּוִיָּיה וְרָאשֵׁי דַדִּים שֶׁבָּאִשָּׁה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף שֶׁבָּאִישׁ. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: בְּכוּלָּם עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶם לְחֵירוּת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַסֵּירוּס. בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַלָּשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Nega'im* 6:7): There are **twenty-four extremities in a person, none of which can become ritually impure due to unaffected skin.** The Torah states that if a leprous spot contains some healthy flesh, the person is immediately rendered impure (Leviticus 13:14). The *halakha* of unaffected skin does not apply to the extremities because the priest must be able to see the entirety of the untainted area at once. Due to the shape of the twenty-four extremities, it is impossible to see the entirety of the area from a single vantage point. Consequently, the *halakha* of unaffected skin does not apply to them. **And these are** the twenty-four extremities: **The extremities of the fingers and toes,** twenty in total, **and the extremities of the ears, and the extremity of the nose, and the extremity of the penis, and the extremities of the nipples of a woman. Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** the nipples **of a man** are included. **And it is taught in that regard** in a *baraita*: **A slave is emancipated for** injuries to **all of them.** The body parts listed with regard to leprosy are the same ones that, when injured, lead to the emancipation of a slave. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Also, the castration** of a slave by his master entails his freedom. **Ben Azzai says: The tongue** is **also** considered an exposed body part, as it is exposed when one speaks. Consequently, if the master severs his slave's tongue, the slave goes free.

The *gemara* then introduces the twenty-four *rashei evarim*, the twenty-four limb heads of the human body that do not contract *tum'at michya* (the sign of *tzara'at* at a healed spot) because of their sloped or protruding nature, which prevents the entire spot from being visible to the *kohen* simultaneously. These are: the ten fingertips, the ten toetips (totaling twenty), the tips of the ears (twenty-two), the nose (twenty-three), and the top of the penis (twenty-four). By a woman, the nipples replace or supplement the last entry. Rabbi Yehuda adds the male nipples; the *chachamim* do not consider them sufficiently protruding.

The *baraita* then teaches: *u-vichulam eved yotzei bahen la-cheirut*—by all of these, a slave goes free if the limb is cut off or destroyed. These twenty-four, plus the eye and the tooth already taught in the *mishnah*, constitute the catalog of external limbs. The connection to the *eved* is not the *tzara'at* logic of sloped visibility, but the identification of what counts as an external, visible part of the body.

### Extra Finger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: הָיְתָה לוֹ יְתֶרֶת, וַחֲתָכָהּ – עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לְחֵירוּת. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: וְהוּא שֶׁנִּסְפֶּרֶת עַל גַּב הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi says** that **Rav says:** If the slave **had** **an extra** finger, i.e., six fingers on his hand, **and** the master **severed it,** the **slave is emancipated by means of** this injury. **Rav Huna says: And this** *halakha* applies **when** the finger **can be counted along the back of the hand,** i.e., the extra finger is on the same line as the others. If it protrudes from another spot, then it is not classified as a finger but a mere growth, and destroying it is not considered the removal of a limb.

Rabbi Hiyya bar Ashi says in the name of Rav: if the slave had an extra finger and the master cut it off, the slave goes free. Rav Huna qualifies: *ve-hu she-nisperet al gav ha-yad*—only if the extra finger is counted in continuity with the other fingers of the hand, so that one would count "one, two, three, four, five, six" running continuously. If the extra digit protrudes in a way that clearly places it outside the normal row of fingers, it is not considered a finger of the hand, and cutting it off would not free the slave.

### Testicles Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבֵי דְנָזוֹנְיָא לָא אֲתוֹ לְפִירְקֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא: זִיל צַנְּעִינְהוּ. אֲזַל אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן לְפִירְקָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי נֵיתֵי? דִּבְעֵינַן מִינֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא וְלָא פְּשַׁט לַן. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִי בְּעֵיתוּ מִינַּאי מִידֵּי וְלָא פָּשֵׁיטְנָא לְכוּ? בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ: עֶבֶד שֶׁסֵּרְסוֹ רַבּוֹ בַּבֵּצִים, מַהוּ? כְּמוּם שֶׁבַּגָּלוּי דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **The Elders of** the city of **Nezonya did not come to Rav Ḥisda's lecture.** Rav Ḥisda **said to Rav Hamnuna: Go** and **ostracize them [*tzaninhu*]** because they act disrespectfully toward the Sages. Rav Hamnuna **went** and **said to** the Elders of Nezonya: **What is the reason that the rabbis did not come to the lecture? They said to him: Why should we come, as we asked him** about **a matter and he did not resolve it for us.** We have nothing to learn from him. Rav Hamnuna **said to them: Have you asked me anything that I did not resolve for you?** Ask me your question. **They raised** the following **dilemma before him:** With regard to **a slave whose master castrates his testicles, what is** the *halakha*? **Is that considered an exposed blemish** that is sufficient to emancipate him **or not?**

This leads to the famous exchange. The elders of Nazunya stopped attending Rav Chisda's *shiur*. Rav Chisda sent Rav Hamnuna to inquire why, and rather than simply imposing a sanction, Rav Hamnuna asked what was troubling them. They said: we asked him a question and he did not resolve it—if he cannot answer our questions, why should we attend? Rav Hamnuna invited them to ask him their question.

They asked: *eved she-seiraso rabo be-beitzim*—if the master castrated the slave by crushing the testicles, does this qualify as *ke-mum she-ba-galuye*, a blemish on a visible limb? The question rests on the following tension: on one hand, the principle is that the master must have injured an external, visible limb. On the other hand, while the testicles are located externally in the sense of not being inside the body cavity, they are not fully visible in the way that a finger or an eye is—they are, as Rashi puts it, *tluyim be-kis ve-nikarim mi-bachutz*, suspended within the scrotal sac and discernible from outside, but not exposed in the way that would make the injury itself plainly visible.

### Hamnuna's Mission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַתְנִיתָא בְּעוֹ מִינָּךְ. דִּתְנַן: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה רָאשֵׁי אֵבָרִים שֶׁבָּאָדָם כּוּלָּם אֵין מְטַמְּאִין מִשּׁוּום מִחְיָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵם: רָאשֵׁי אֶצְבָּעוֹת יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, וְרָאשֵׁי אׇזְנַיִם, וְרֹאשׁ הַחוֹטֶם, וְרֹאשׁ הַגְּוִיָּיה וְרָאשֵׁי דַדִּים שֶׁבָּאִשָּׁה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף שֶׁבָּאִישׁ. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: בְּכוּלָּם עֶבֶד יוֹצֵא בָּהֶם לְחֵירוּת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַסֵּירוּס. בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַלָּשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** After this encounter Rav Hamnuna **came before Rav Ḥisda** and told him what had happened. Rav Ḥisda **said to him: They raised before you a dilemma** that can be resolved from **a *baraita*,** which was cited in connection to a mishna, and you did not know how to answer them. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Nega'im* 6:7): There are **twenty-four extremities in a person, none of which can become ritually impure due to unaffected skin.**... **And it is taught in that regard** in a *baraita*: **A slave is emancipated for** injuries to **all of them.** ... **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Also, the castration** of a slave by his master entails his freedom. **Ben Azzai says: The tongue** is **also** considered an exposed body part...

Rav Hamnuna returned to Rav Chisda with the elders' question unanswered. The irony was palpable: Rav Chisda had sent him saying "have you ever asked me something I didn't answer?"—and here was exactly such a question. Rav Chisda told him: *matnisa ba'u minach*—what they asked you is effectively answered by a *mishnah*.

### Castration & Tongue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר אַף הַסֵּירוּס. סֵירוּס דְּמַאי? אִילֵּימָא סֵירוּס דְּגִיד, הַיְינוּ גְּוִיָּיה. אֶלָּא לָאו: סֵירוּס דְּבֵיצִים?
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Also castration.** The Gemara asks: **Castration of what? If we say** that it is referring to **castration of** the **penis,** i.e., that the master severed the slave's penis, **this is** the same as the mishna that already mentioned **a penis.** What, then, does Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi add? **Rather, is it not** correct to say that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is referring to **castration of the testicles?** If so, this *baraita* resolves the dilemma raised by the Elders of Nezonya.

The *mishnah* cited lists the twenty-four *rashei evarim* and then adds: *Rabbi omer af ha-sirus*—even castration causes the slave to go free. *Ben Azzai omer af ha-lashon*—and cutting off the tongue.

The *gemara* asks: what castration does Rabbi mean? If it refers to the penis (*gid*), that was already included in the twenty-four *rashei evarim* as the *geviyah*. It must therefore be *sirus de-beitzim*—castration by the testicles. This is the answer to the elders' question: the *mishnah* itself treats the testicles as a visible limb, at least according to Rabbi.

### Tongue Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר אַף הַסֵּירוּס. וְרַבִּי, לָשׁוֹן לָא? אֶלָּא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: סֵירוּס, וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא לָשׁוֹן. בֶּן עַזַּאי אָמַר: לָשׁוֹן, אֲבָל סֵירוּס – לָא. וּמַאי ״אַף״? אַקַּמַּיְיתָא. אִי הָכִי, נַקְדְּמַהּ דְּבֶן עַזַּאי בְּרֵישָׁא! תַּנָּא שַׁמְעַהּ לִדְרַבִּי וְקַבְעַהּ, וְשַׁמְעַהּ לִדְבֶן עַזַּאי וְתַנְיַ[הּ], וּמִשְׁנָה לֹא זָזָה מִמְּקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further analyzes the *baraita*. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Also castration,** but he does not include the tongue, unlike ben Azzai. The Gemara inquires: **And** according to **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, is the **tongue not** considered exposed? **Rather,** the *baraita* should be explained as follows. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Castration** is included, **and it is not necessary** to say that if the slave's **tongue** is removed he is emancipated, as the tongue is exposed. **Ben Azzai says:** The loss of his **tongue** emancipates him, **but castration** does **not. And what** is the meaning of the term: **Also,** in the *baraita*, which indicates that ben Azzai is adding to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's statement? He is adding **to the first** statement of the first *tanna*, not to the immediately preceding ruling of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara asks: **If so,** the statement **of ben Azzai should be first,** as he adds one item, i.e., the tongue, to the ruling of the first *tanna*, while Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi further adds the case of castration to ben Azzai's opinion. The Gemara answers: The *baraita* should have been formulated in this manner, but the ***tanna*** first **heard** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and set it** in his version of the *baraita*, **and** afterward **he heard** the opinion of **ben Azzai and taught** it at the end. **And** although it would be appropriate to change the order of the statements, he did not do so because **a mishna does not move from its place.** Once it has been taught in a certain manner, the *tanna* will not change the text of a mishna, in order to avoid confusion.

The *gemara* then turns to the tongue. Rabbi said *af ha-sirus* and did not mention the tongue; Ben Azzai said *af ha-lashon*. Does this mean Rabbi would not free a slave whose tongue was cut off? The *gemara* answers: *Rabbi omer sirus ve-lo mibaye lashon*—when Rabbi says castration, cutting the tongue goes without saying, since the tongue is at least as visible as the testicles if not more so; you can actually see the tongue, whereas you never fully see the testicles themselves. Ben Azzai, by saying *af ha-lashon*, is disagreeing not with Rabbi but with the *tana kamma*'s list of twenty-four—he is adding the tongue to that list. The testicles, in Ben Azzai's view, do not qualify.

This produces an initially puzzling reading of the text, since Ben Azzai's statement comes after Rabbi's and uses the word *af*, "even." One might expect that Ben Azzai is adding to Rabbi's position, making him more inclusive. But the *gemara* reads it the opposite way: Ben Azzai is more restrictive than Rabbi (excluding the testicles), and *af ha-lashon* adds the tongue not to Rabbi's enumeration but to the *tana kamma*'s original twenty-four. The reason Ben Azzai was appended to the end rather than placed before Rabbi in the sequence is a principle about oral texts: *tana samakh le-de-Rebbe ve-kav'ah, ve-tani mishna lo zaz mi-mkomo*—once the text was learned and memorized in a certain form, it was not rearranged even when a later opinion needed to be integrated. Ben Azzai's view was tagged onto the end even though the resulting *af* is somewhat confusing.

## Tongue Status

### Tumah Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּלָשׁוֹן לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה דְּגָלוּי הוּא אֵצֶל הַשֶּׁרֶץ. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי נָמֵי בַּר נְגִיעָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says: All concede with regard to a tongue** that **in the matter of ritual impurity it is** considered **exposed with respect to a dead creeping animal** and other items that impart impurity. In other words, if an individual comes into contact with a source of ritual impurity with his tongue, he is rendered impure. **What is the reason** for this? **The Merciful One states: "Whom he touches"** (Leviticus 15:11), **and this** tongue **can also touch.** It is possible for one to touch objects with his tongue.

Having established that the tongue is treated as an external limb for purposes of freeing the slave—at least according to Rabbi—the *gemara* now examines whether the tongue is consistently classified as external or whether its classification shifts depending on the legal context. Ulla states: *hakol modim bi-lashon le-inyan tum'ah de-galuye hu etzel sheretz*—everyone agrees that for purposes of contracting *tum'ah* through contact with a *tamei* object, the tongue is considered external. The relevant principle is that *tum'at maga*, becoming *tamei* through contact, requires touching with an external part of the body. If one were to hold a piece of *neveilah* entirely inside one's mouth without any external surface touching it, one would not become *tamei*. But if the tongue touches the *tamei* object, that counts. The reason: *asher yiga bo amra rachmana*—the verse speaks of touching (*nega'*), and the tongue is a natural instrument of touching. From the perspective of *nega'*, the tongue is external—it is how one touches things.

### Tvilah Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעִנְיַן טְבִילָה – כְּטָמוּן דָּמֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא: ״וְרָחַץ בְּשָׂרוֹ בְּמַיִם״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, מָה בְּשָׂרוֹ מֵאַבָּרַאי – אַף כֹּל מֵאַבָּרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, all agree about a tongue with regard **to the matter of immersion** that the tongue **is considered concealed,** and therefore one need not open his mouth so that the water touches his tongue. For an immersion to be valid, the water must come into contact with the entire outside of one's body. Ulla teaches that this does not include the tongue. **What is the reason** for this? **The Merciful One states: "And he shall immerse his flesh in water"** (Leviticus 15:13). **Just as his flesh is on the outside, so too everything** that requires immersion is **on the outside,** and this does not include what is ordinarily on the inside.

By contrast, Ulla continues, for purposes of *tvilah* in the *mikvah*, everyone agrees that the tongue is considered internal (*ke-tamun dami*) and does not require direct contact with *mikvah* water. The reason: *ve-rachatz besaro ba-mayim amra rachmana*—the verse says one should wash one's *basar*, one's flesh, with water. Just as one's flesh is external (*mibar*), so everything that requires water contact must be external. The tongue, kept inside the mouth and not exposed the way the skin is, does not fall into that category.

The conceptual distinction emerges clearly: when the question is one of *nega'*—an act of touching—the tongue qualifies as external, because touching is something done with a tongue. When the question is one of the body's surface being contacted by water, analogous to skin, the tongue does not qualify. Rashi adds an additional practical dimension: one cannot expose the entire surface of one's tongue to water even if one tried, whereas any act of touching can be performed with any part of the tongue. This further explains why the two contexts yield opposite conclusions.

### Maidservant Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן הַזָּאָה. רַבִּי מְדַמֵּי לַהּ לְטוּמְאָה, וְרַבָּנַן מְדַמּוּ לַהּ לִטְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagreed only** with regard **to** whether the tongue is considered exposed or concealed in **the matter of sprinkling. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **compares** sprinkling **to impurity,** where the tongue is considered exposed, **and the Rabbis compare it to immersion,** where the tongue is considered concealed.

A challenge arises: does Rabbi truly hold that the tongue is internal for purposes of *tvilah*? After all, the *gemara* recounts: *ma'aseh be-shifchato shel beis Rebbe*—there was an incident with a maidservant in Rabbi's household who immersed in the *mikvah*, and when she emerged there was a bone (*etzem*) between her teeth. Rabbi required her to immerse again. This seems to imply that the interior of the mouth must be free of interposition—that the *mikvah* water must in some sense be capable of reaching inside the mouth.

### Natural Cavities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּבִיאַת מַיִם לָא בָּעֵינַן, מְקוֹם הָרָאוּי לָבוֹא בּוֹ מַיִם בָּעֵינַן,
>
> **Translation:** That is no proof, as it is **granted that we do not require immersion in water,** i.e., the water need not actually enter one's mouth. But **we require** that the mouth be **a place** that is **fit for water to enter.** If there is a foreign object, the water cannot enter that spot.

The *gemara* resolves this: *ne-hi de-vi'at mayim lo be'inan, makom ha-rauy la-vo bo mayim be'inan*—we do not require that *mikvah* water actually enter the oral cavity, but the cavity must be fit for water to enter (*rauy la-vo*), free of any *chatzitzah* that would impede it. This is the principle of *beit ha-starim*: concealed orifices and natural cavities—the inside of the ear, beneath the eyelid, the oral cavity, and similarly *beit ha-kamatin*, the natural folds of the body—do not require the water to actually make contact, but they must not be blocked. The bone between the maidservant's teeth was a *chatzitzah* preventing the cavity from being *rauy* for water contact, which is why Rabbi required a second immersion.

### Potential vs. Actual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי זֵירָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְבִילָּה – אֵין בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ. וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְבִילָּה – בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **in accordance with that** statement of **Rabbi Zeira. As Rabbi Zeira says** with regard to meal-offerings: For **any** amount of flour **suitable for mingling** with oil in a meal-offering, **mingling is not indispensable for it.** Although it is a mitzva to mingle the flour and oil *ab initio*, if they were not mingled the meal-offering is still valid. **But** for **any** amount of flour **not suitable for mingling, mingling is indispensable for it,** and such a meal-offering is invalid. The principle is: *Ab initio* requirements prevent the fulfillment of a mitzva in situations where they are not merely absent but impossible. Here too, although there is no need for the water to actually enter the concealed spaces of the body, it is still necessary that these places be fit for immersion without the interposition of a foreign object.

This leads to the broader question: when does *halakha* require that an act actually occur, and when is it sufficient that the act could occur? The parallel principle is that of Rabbi Zeira: *kol ha-rauy le-vilah ein vilah me'akevet bo*—anything capable of being mixed with oil (in the context of a *mincha* offering) does not require that the mixing actually occur; but anything incapable of being mixed requires division into separate offerings. The *gemara* applies this logic to *tvilah*: the *beit ha-starim* need not have water contact it, but it must be capable of being contacted.

Tosafot raises the pointed question: how do we know that "fit for" is sufficient rather than requiring the act itself? By analogy, one might argue that a *lulav* only needs to be "fit for" being held, without actually holding it—which is absurd. Tosafot answers that the distinction lies in how the Torah frames the obligation. Where the Torah says *you shall do* an act, the act must occur. Where the Torah describes the *nature* of an object—as when *mincha* is described as *balul ba-shemen*, mixed with oil, rather than saying "you must mix it"—it is sufficient that the object be of that character in potentia. Regarding *beit ha-starim* in *tvilah*, the tension between *kol besaro* (which seems to include even hidden areas) and the comparison to the hand (*ma yadav mi-bora'ei, af kol mi-bora'ei*, requiring external surfaces) is resolved by the intermediate category: the *beit ha-starim* must be *rauy* for water but need not actually be contacted.

Some *rishonim* find Tosafot's explanation insufficient, noting that the obligation of *tvilah* is phrased as an act (*ve-rachatz*), not a description of character. They therefore hold that the requirement of *beit ha-starim* being *rauy* is only *de-rabbanan*. This has significant practical implications: Rav Ovadia Yosef's leniencies in *sheilos* involving blocked orifices often rest on this position, allowing greater latitude when combined with other *shitot*, even though as a general rule a *de-rabbanan* concern is not automatically waived even *be-di'eved*.

## Testicles Visibility

### Blemish Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״וּמָעוּךְ וְכָתוּת וְנָתוּק וְכָרוּת״ – כּוּלָּן בַּבֵּיצִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments that the first question with regard to castration of the testicles is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** It is stated with regard to animals that cannot be used as offerings due to blemishes: **"That whose stones are bruised, or crushed, or torn, or cut** you shall not sacrifice to the Lord" (Leviticus 22:24). **All** of these blemishes **are** referring **to** the animal's **testicles;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* now returns to the question of whether crushing the testicles constitutes a visible *mum*, framing it as a dispute among *tanna'im*. The verse in *Vayikra* enumerates blemishes that disqualify an animal: *ma'uach, ve-katus, ve-nasuk, ve-karus*—crushed, bruised, severed, or cut. According to Rabbi Yehuda, all of these refer to injuries of the testicles (*kulam be-veitzim*).

### Testicle Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּבֵּיצִים וְלֹא בַּגִּיד? אֶלָּא: כּוּלָּן אַף בַּבֵּיצִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּם בַּגִּיד. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״מָעוּךְ וְכָתוּת״ – אַף בַּבֵּיצִים, ״נָתוּק וְכָרוּת״ – בְּגִיד – אִין, בְּבֵיצִים – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Could Rabbi Yehuda possibly mean that these blemishes apply only **to the testicles and not to the penis?** Certainly these should also be considered blemishes if they affect the penis, which is more exposed than the testicles. **Rather,** this is what the *baraita* is saying: **All of** these blemishes apply **to the testicles also;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: All of them** apply only **to the penis. Rabbi Yosei states** the following distinction: **"Bruised or crushed"** applies **to the testicles also.** Conversely, when there are areas that are **"broken or cut" on the penis, yes,** these are considered a blemish, but **on the testicles, no,** they are not a blemish.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees: *kulam be-gid*—all of these terms refer to the penis, not the testicles. For Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, an injury to the testicles does not constitute a visible blemish because the testicles themselves are not a visible limb in the relevant sense. While the scrotum is external, the testicles inside it are not exposed in the way that the penis is, and therefore injuries to them—not involving removal of the scrotum itself—do not register as blemishes on a visible limb.

### Yosi's Position

Rabbi Yosi takes a middle position: *ma'uach ve-katus af be-veitzim*—crushing and bruising apply even to the testicles, but *nasuk ve-karus be-gid, be-veitzim lo*—severance and cutting apply only to the penis, not to the testicles. At first glance this seems backward: severance (*nasuk*) and cutting (*karus*) seem more dramatic and visible than mere crushing. The resolution, as Rashi explains (*de-minkar tefei*), is that crushing produces a visible change in the shape and appearance of the scrotal area, while internal severance of the testicles within the scrotum may not produce any external sign at all. Rabbi Yosi is thus distinguishing not by severity of the injury but by whether the injury is externally discernible. The crushing is visible in its effect on the outside; the detachment is internal and therefore not a blemish on a visible limb.

This raises an important conceptual point about the two legal contexts. For the *eved*, the question is whether the *limb* is external and visible—if the testicles qualify as a visible limb, then any significant injury to them frees the slave. For the animal *mum*, the question may instead be whether the *injury* is externally visible. These are not the same question, and one must be careful about directly equating the *tanna'im*'s positions in one context with the other.

## Animals Kinyan

### Mishna Modes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה נִקְנֵית בִּמְסִירָה, וְהַדַּקָּה בְּהַגְבָּהָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A large domesticated animal is acquired by passing,** when its current owner transfers it to a buyer by giving him the reins or the bit. **And a small** domesticated animal is acquired **by lifting.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer. And the Rabbis say: A small domesticated animal can be acquired by pulling** also, and there is no need to lift it.

Having concluded the *sugyot* relating to human beings as objects of acquisition—whether as wives through *ishut* or as *eved kena'ani* who, though fully property in some respects, retain a human identity—the *gemara* transitions to the acquisition of animals. The *mishnah* teaches: *behema gasa nikneit be-mesira*, a large animal such as a cow or horse is acquired by *mesira*, the handing over of the reins or lead; *ve-ha-daka be-hagbaha*, a small animal such as a sheep or goat is acquired by lifting (*hagbaha*). This is the position of Rabbi Meir.

### Hagbaha Dispute

Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* disagree: *behema daka nikneit be-meshicha*—a small animal is acquired by *meshicha*, pulling or leading, not by *hagbaha*. This sets up a central analytical question about the hierarchy of *kinyanim*. *Hagbaha* is considered the most powerful form of acquisition: lifting an object demonstrates total physical mastery and control. *Meshicha*—causing the animal to move—is a lesser act. If *hagbaha* is possible with a small animal (one can lift a sheep), why does the *mishnah* according to Rabbi Meir specify *hagbaha*, and why do the *chachamim* prefer *meshicha*?

The key principle is one of hierarchy. If *hagbaha* is specified, it means *hagbaha* and not the weaker *meshicha*. If *meshicha* is specified, it means even *meshicha* suffices. The *chachamim*'s position that *meshicha* works for small animals implies that *meshicha* is sufficient even though *hagbaha* would certainly also work. The debate is whether we demand the superior *kinyan* when it is available, or whether a lesser one is adequate.

## Rava's Position

### Shmuel Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב בְּקִימְחוֹנְיָא: בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה. אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְתַלְמִידֵי דְּרַב, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִי אָמַר רַב בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה? וְהָאֲנַן בִּמְסִירָה תְּנַן! וְרַב נָמֵי בִּמְסִירָה אָמַר, הֲדַר בֵּיהּ מֵהַהִיא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav taught in** the town of **Kimḥonya: A large domesticated animal is acquired by pulling. Shmuel found Rav's students** and **said to them: Did Rav** actually **say that a large domesticated animal is acquired by pulling? But didn't we learn** in the mishna that it is acquired **by passing? And** several times I **also** heard **Rav say** that it is acquired **by passing.** Did **he retract that** ruling?

The *gemara* reports that Rava gave a public *drasha* in the town of Kimchunia stating that a large animal is acquired by *meshicha*. Shmuel encountered Rav's students and challenged this: did Rav really say *meshicha* for a large animal? The *mishnah* says *mesira*, and Rav himself was known to rule *mesira*. What is Rava doing?

### Alignment Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זוֹ וְזוֹ נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: זוֹ וְזוֹ בְּהַגְבָּהָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav's students replied: In fact, Rav retracted that ruling and **he states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*,** i.e., the Rabbis, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **And the Rabbis say: Both** small and large domesticated animals **are acquired by pulling. Rabbi Shimon says: Both** are acquired **by lifting.**

The *gemara* answers that Rava had retracted his earlier position. He now held *meshicha*, aligning himself with the *chachamim* as quoted in a *baraita*: *ve-chachamim omrim zo va-zo nikneit be-meshicha*—the *chachamim* rule that both large and small animals are acquired by *meshicha*.

### Shimon Opinion

Rabbi Shimon, however, takes the most demanding position: *zo va-zo be-hagbaha*—both large and small animals require *hagbaha*. Since *hagbaha* is at the top of the hierarchy, those who specify it mean *only* hagbaha. The practical difficulty this creates—particularly for animals that cannot be lifted—will be addressed shortly.

## Animals Kinyan (II)

### Rashi's Hierarchy

Rashi's analysis of the *mishnah*'s term *mesira* illuminates the broader debate about hierarchy. Rashi explains that *mesira* means specifically the handing over of reins or a saddle, and that *meshicha* would *not* constitute a valid *kinyan* for a large animal in this view: *aval meshicha lo maknya de-ein darkah be-chach lilech lefanav*. The reason Rashi gives is striking: large animals do not walk in front of you—they are led, with the owner handing control forward. *Meshicha*, in Rashi's understanding, means driving the animal from behind, which is not how large animals normally move. Accordingly, *mesira* is not merely a weaker form of *meshicha*; it is the uniquely appropriate act for large animals, while *meshicha*—pushing from behind—is the natural mode for small animals like sheep and goats.

Tosafot disagrees with this hierarchy. For Tosafot, *meshicha* is clearly the stronger act—you do more than merely hand over a lead, you actually cause the animal to move. *Mesira* without movement is a lesser demonstration of control. The debate between Rashi and Tosafot thus goes to the heart of how we define the hierarchy: Rashi holds *mesira* is higher for large animals because it is the appropriate and meaningful act; Tosafot holds *meshicha* is always higher because movement demonstrates greater mastery. On Tosafot's reading, when the *mishnah* says *mesira* for large animals, it means even *mesira* suffices—*meshicha* would certainly also work. On Rashi's reading, *mesira* means *mesira* and not *meshicha*, because the latter is simply not an appropriate act for a large animal.

It is worth noting that these positions need not entail a strict universal hierarchy. It is possible to hold that *mesira* is appropriate exclusively for large animals and *meshicha* exclusively for small ones, with neither strictly including the other—mutual exclusivity based on contextual appropriateness rather than rank. But the *gemara*'s general framework tends toward hierarchy, and the debate among *rishonim* largely proceeds on that assumption.

## Elephant Hagbaha

### Rav Yosef Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, פִּיל לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּמֶּה יִקָּנֶה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בַּחֲלִיפִין. אִי נָמֵי בְּשׂוֹכֵר אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to** this: **If that is so, by what** mode of acquisition **can an elephant be acquired, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon?** It is impossible to lift an elephant. **Abaye said to him:** It is possible to acquire it **by the** mode of acquisition of symbolic **exchange,** a legal act of acquisition formalizing the transfer of ownership of an article. **Alternatively,** one can acquire an elephant **by renting its place** temporarily and acquiring the elephant by means of the ground upon which it is standing.

The difficulty with Rabbi Shimon's position—requiring *hagbaha* for all animals—becomes acute when Rav Yosef challenges it: *ela me-ata, pil le-Rabbi Shimon be-meh yikneh?*—according to Rabbi Shimon, how would one acquire an elephant? No human being can lift an elephant, so *hagbaha* seems practically impossible.

### Abaye Solutions

Abaye offers two answers. First, *be-chalifin*—one could use *kinyan sudar*, the symbolic exchange of a kerchief, to effect the acquisition. Second, *i nami be-schirut mekomo*—one could rent the space the elephant occupies, a mechanism that somehow effects acquisition of the elephant itself. The second answer envisions something like placing stools or bundles beneath the elephant so that it stands upon objects belonging to the purchaser.

### Vessels & Bundles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: מֵבִיא אַרְבָּעָה כֵּלִים וּמַנִּיחָן תַּחַת רַגְלָיו. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ: כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל לוֹקֵחַ בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר – קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בְּסִימְטָא. אִי נָמֵי: בַּחֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says** that there is another method: One **brings four vessels and places them under** the elephant's **feet,** and he thereby acquires it like any other item that is inside the buyer's vessels. The Gemara asks: Can **you learn from** Rabbi Zeira's statement that if the **buyer's vessels,** being used to acquire an item from the seller, **are in the seller's domain, the buyer acquires** the item? The Gemara rejects this: This is no proof, as **with what are we dealing here?** The case in question is one where the vessels are not in the seller's domain but **in an alley [*simta*],** which is neither a public nor a private domain. In a place of this kind the buyer's vessels certainly effect acquisition for him. **Alternatively,** the buyer can lift an elephant **by** using **bundles of vines.** He leads the elephant to them, and when the elephant stands on the bundles of vines this is considered lifting the elephant.

The *gemara* explores whether the elephant standing on the buyer's objects constitutes a valid *kinyan*. This would require the buyer's vessels (*kelim*) to effect acquisition even while located in the seller's domain. The *gemara* answers that the case is one where the elephant is standing in a *simta*, a public alleyway, where a buyer's *kelim* are recognized as capable of effecting acquisition. Alternatively, the answer involves *chavilei zemorot*—bundles of vines placed for the elephant to step on.

### Rise vs. Lift

Rashi and Tosafot dispute what the bundle answer accomplishes. For Rashi, placing bundles under the elephant so that it steps on them and rises three *tefachim* off the ground constitutes a form of *hagbaha*: the buyer caused the animal to rise, even without physically lifting it. Rashi apparently holds that *hagbaha* does not require the buyer to personally hoist the animal, only that the animal is elevated by the buyer's act. Tosafot prefers a different reading: the bundles are elephant food placed high up, causing the elephant to rear up on its hind legs to reach the food. This too is a form of induced rising. However, Tosafot's answer raises its own difficulties: whether an elephant can meaningfully "jump," and more fundamentally, whether causing an animal to raise part of its body is equivalent to the *kinyan* of *hagbaha*. The conceptual problem is the same in both accounts—did the buyer perform *hagbaha*, or merely cause the animal to move in a way that resembles it?

## Kinyan Hierarchy

### One vs. Multiple

The concluding discussion raises a question that cuts across the entire preceding analysis: when multiple *kinyanim* are valid for a given transaction, does the existence of a superior *kinyan* preclude using an inferior one? Or do multiple valid modes coexist, any one of which may be used at will?

### Kinyan Mitzvot

The *Rashbam* articulates the challenge with characteristic sharpness: *ve-chi kinyanim mitzvot hen she-ein la'asot ela min ha-muvchar?*—are *kinyanim* like *mitzvot*, such that one is obligated to perform only the most distinguished option? The question is deliberately provocative. When we speak of performing a *mitzva* *min ha-muvchar*, from the finest available, we are talking about a religious obligation where the quality of the act reflects on one's service of God. But a *kinyan* is a legal mechanism for transferring ownership—a tool, not a religious duty in that sense. To say one must use the superior *kinyan* when available seems to misunderstand the nature of the institution. This striking formulation of the Rashbam is left for further discussion.
