# Bava Batra 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## One Witness & Chazaka

### Intro & Setup

The daf opens at the very last lines of 34a with the word *ela idamya*. The case under discussion involves a litigant who possessed a single witness testifying that he had eaten three years' worth of fruit from a given field — the basis for a claim of *chazaka*. The problem is that *chazaka* requires two witnesses; with only one, he cannot establish possessory title over the land. The question then becomes whether that lone witness can at least function as testimony against him — obligating him to pay for the three years of fruit he consumed. After all, the only basis for any claim against him regarding the fruit is that one witness, since without any witnesses at all, the litigant's own admission that he ate the fruit would be neutralized by his simultaneous claim of ownership. That combination would invoke the principle of *peh she'asar peh shehitir* — the same mouth that creates the liability also provides the exemption — and no payment would result. So the lone witness is doing all the work. The question is whether that witness can bear that weight.

### Supporting vs. Damning

The *gemara* draws a crucial distinction between a witness who supports a claim and one who damages it. The lone witness who testifies to three years of fruit consumption is not straightforwardly a damning witness — because if a second witness were to appear and corroborate him, that testimony would actually *help* the litigant establish his *chazaka* and win the land outright. In other words, this witness belongs to the category of supporting evidence for which a second corroborating witness has simply not yet been found. The *gemara* appears to conclude, though without stating it explicitly, that such a witness cannot therefore be used as damning testimony to compel payment for the fruit.

### Naska de-Rabbi Abba

This stands in contrast to the famous case known as *naska de-Rabbi Abba*, which the *gemara* had already invoked as a potential parallel. In that case, a person seized what appeared to be an ingot of silver belonging to someone else, claiming it was his own property. There was one witness who saw him take it. The analysis runs as follows: had there been two witnesses to the taking, he would be classified as a *gazlan* — a robber — and obligated to pay. Had there been no witnesses at all, he would be believed through a *migo*: he could have claimed he always had possession of the object, and since he could have made that stronger claim, his actual claim is similarly credited. With one witness, however, he finds himself in a middle position. That one witness is operating *against* him — it is a damning witness — because if a second were added, he would be liable. Accordingly, Rabbi Abba ruled that the single witness creates an obligation to take an oath contradicting the witness's testimony. Since the man cannot swear that he did not take the object (he openly admits he took it, insisting only that it was rightfully his), he is *chayav* in a *shvu'ah* he cannot fulfill, and therefore must pay.

### Two Years Parallel

The *gemara* now supplies the precise land-based analogue to *naska de-Rabbi Abba*. The equivalent case is not three years of fruit with one witness — which, as established, is a supporting rather than a damning configuration — but rather *two* years of fruit with one witness. If someone claims *chazaka* and a lone witness testifies to two years of fruit consumption, that witness is genuinely damning: two witnesses to two years would establish that the litigant ate the fruit without yet having established ownership, obligating him to pay. One such witness therefore triggers the same mechanism as *naska de-Rabbi Abba* — an obligation to swear against the witness's testimony. Since the litigant admits eating the fruit (claiming only that the land is his), he cannot take that oath, and he must pay. This is the precise structural parallel: just as in *naska de-Rabbi Abba* — "yes, I took it, but it was mine" — so too here, "yes, I ate the fruit, but the land is mine." The *shvu'ah* cannot be made, and payment follows.

## Boat & Holding Pattern

### Boat Dispute Setup

The *gemara* at the top of 34b introduces a new case: *hahu arba* — there was a boat. Two people were disputing ownership: *hai amar didi hu v'hai amar didi hu* — this one says it is mine, that one says it is mine. Before reaching the question of ultimate adjudication, the *gemara* first addresses a procedural matter. One of the claimants approaches the court and requests: *tifsuha ad d'maitina sahadei d'didi hu* — seize the boat and hold it while I go and collect my witnesses proving it is mine. The concern is practical: while he is gathering evidence, the other party might sell the boat or otherwise dispose of it, making recovery difficult or impossible. The request, then, is not for a final ruling but for a temporary holding pattern — essentially asking the court to serve as a neutral custodian until proof can be produced.

This case is deeply connected to the opening *sugya* of Bava Metzia, where two people are physically holding a *tallit* and the resolution is *yachloku* — they split it. That case, however, involves *metaltelin* — movable chattels in actual physical possession. Here, by contrast, the disputed objects (plots of land, boats) are not in either party's physical grip, and the resolutions will therefore differ.

### Tafsinan/Lo Tafsinan

The question is *tafsinan o lo tafsinan* — do we seize the disputed object as a holding measure or not? *Rav Huna amar tafsinan* — Rav Huna holds that the court should take possession, serving as a kind of referee to protect the integrity of the eventual adjudication. *Rav Yehuda amar lo tafsinan* — Rav Yehuda refuses. The reason for Rav Yehuda's position becomes clear only once the companion question is posed.

### Mafkinan/Lo Mafkinan

The companion question is *mafkinan o lo mafkinan* — if the court did take possession but the claimant subsequently cannot produce his witnesses, does the court then release the object or continue holding it indefinitely? *Rav Yehuda amar lo mafkinan* — once you take hold of it, you are responsible. You cannot simply release it back into an unresolved situation; you have assumed a judicial responsibility that cannot be walked back. *Rav Pappa amar mafkinan* — Rav Pappa maintains that even after taking possession, the court may release the object if no evidence is forthcoming.

The Rashbam makes an elegant observation: one's position on *mafkinan* directly determines one's position on *tafsinan*. Rav Yehuda, who holds that once you take possession you cannot release it, therefore refuses to take possession in the first place. If the claimant says "hold it while I find my witnesses" and then cannot produce them, Rav Yehuda would be stuck holding an unresolvable dispute indefinitely — a responsibility he has no way to discharge. He therefore declines from the outset. Rav Pappa, who permits releasing possession once no evidence materializes, is correspondingly more amenable to taking possession initially, since he has a viable exit.

The *halacha* follows a combination of both positions: *lo tafsinan* — we do not take possession in a holding pattern, because we do not wish to assume a responsibility we may be unable to fulfill. But *ve-heicha de-tafas lo mafkinan* — if the court did go ahead and take possession contrary to this ruling, it may not then release the object. Having chosen to get involved, it must remain responsible until the matter is resolved.

### Kol D'Alim Gavar

We now arrive at the underlying ruling for cases where neither party has any evidence. If both parties claim — *zeh omer shel avosai ve-zeh omer shel avosai* — this is my ancestral property, that is my ancestral property — and neither can bring proof, *Rav Nachman amar kol d'alim gavar*: whoever is stronger prevails. The Rashbam reads this to mean that the court declines to adjudicate at all. With no evidence, no physical possession, and nothing but unsubstantiated claims, the court simply steps away. The parties are left to resolve it themselves. Other *rishonim* read the phrase more actively — perhaps meaning that whoever can bring stronger circumstantial evidence will be favored — but following the Rashbam's reading, the court's message is: we have nothing to work with; do not bring this matter to us.

## Two Deeds Same Day

### Competing Deeds

The *gemara* now raises a comparison: *umai shena mi-shnei shtaros ha-yotzin be-yom echad* — how is the boat case different from the case of two *shtaros* dated on the same day? In this scenario, Reuven writes a deed gifting the same object to Shimon on a given date, and another deed gifting that same object to Levi on that very same date. Both cannot take effect simultaneously. One might suppose that we should determine which deed was written first, but another *gemara* establishes that in the absence of a locale where the hour is recorded in the document — which was not standard practice — deeds take effect at the end of the day, meaning both should have taken effect simultaneously. So what is to be done?

At the top of 35a, *Rav amar yachloku* — Rav says they split the contested object. *U-Shmuel amar shuda d'dayni* — Shmuel says the judges apply their best assessment of the circumstances and make a determination based on what Reuven most plausibly intended, even without definitive proof. Yet in neither option does the court say *kol d'alim gavar* and walk away. Why?

### Full Evidence Required

The *gemara* answers: *hasam leika le-mikam ala de-milsa, hacha ika le-mikam ala de-milsa* — there, there is nothing further to investigate; here, there is. In the case of the two *shtaros*, all available evidence is already before the court. The documents are equally valid, equally dated, addressed to different recipients — there is no additional proof that could ever be produced that would change the halachic picture. Given that this is the totality of the evidence, the court must engage and do the best it can, whether by splitting or by judicial assessment. In the boat case, by contrast, it is presumed that eventually — if the matter is sufficiently important — witnesses or documentation will emerge establishing ownership. The court therefore refuses to render judgment prematurely on the basis of pure claims, holding out for the possibility of real evidence.

## Cow/Donkey Birth Case

### Swap Dispute

The *gemara* raises a further comparison: *hamachalif parah ba-chamor v'yaldah* — someone swaps his cow for another person's donkey (via *chalipin*, an exchange that does not require both objects to be physically present), and when the new owner goes to collect the cow, he finds that she has given birth. Did the cow give birth before or after the swap? The calf's ownership turns on this question. Similarly, if someone sells a maidservant and she gives birth before she is physically collected: did it happen before the sale or after? *Zeh omer ad shelo macharti yaldah* — one says she gave birth before the sale; *ve-zeh omer mishekachti yaldah* — the other says it happened after the purchase. The ruling is *yachloku* — split it. Here, too, conceivably further evidence could surface, yet the court does not resort to *kol d'alim gavar* but instead divides the contested property. Why is this different from the boat case?

### Standing Doctrine

The *gemara* answers on 35b: *l'hai is lei drara d'mamona u-l'hai is lei drara d'mamona* — each litigant has *drara d'mamona*, a monetary connection or stake in the matter. Both parties have clear, established standing. We know the sequence of ownership: the cow belonged to one party at one point and passed to the other at a defined moment; the only question is where precisely the calf's birth falls relative to that moment. Each claimant has an objectively grounded basis for his claim — neither is simply making an unfounded assertion.

The boat litigants, by contrast, could be entirely without standing. Either one could be a complete stranger to the property making a baseless claim. The *gemara* captures this with the phrase *hacha ei d'mar lo d'mar v'ei d'mar lo d'mar* — in this case, if it belongs to one, the other has nothing; if it belongs to the other, the first has nothing. This is best read not as a logical observation about mutual exclusivity — which is equally true of the calf — but as a pointed remark about credibility and standing: according to each claimant's own account, the other party is a complete fabricator with zero claim. In the cow/donkey dispute, each party demonstrably had some connection to the cow at some point in time. That concrete monetary connection — *drara d'mamona* — compels the court to adjudicate. Where such standing is absent, the court steps back and declares *kol d'alim gavar*.

## Third Party Seizure

### Amri Neharda'a

Having established that the boat dispute defaults to *kol d'alim gavar*, the *gemara* asks what happens if a third party entirely unconnected to the original dispute seizes the boat. *Amri Neharda'a* — the Sages of Nehardea rule — *im ba echad min ha-shuk ve-hichzik bah ein motzi'in otah mi-yado*: if someone from the market came and took hold of it, we do not remove it from his possession. This ruling reinforces the logic of *kol d'alim gavar*: since the original two claimants have no demonstrated standing, a third party who physically seizes the object has no less a claim than they do. The very refusal of the court to adjudicate between Reuven and Shimon reflects the principle that *anyone's* claim is as weak as anyone else's. Why should only those two be considered?

### Gazlan Shel Rabim

This is supported by the teaching of Rabbi Hiyya: *gazlan shel rabim lav shmei gazlan* — one who is a *gazlan* of the masses is not called a *gazlan*. In this context, Rabbi Hiyya's rule applies because the object in question is effectively ownerless — it belongs to the *rabim* not in the sense of public property but in the sense that no individual has any demonstrated title. Taking something that belongs to no identifiable person does not constitute genuine *gezilah*, since there is no established owner from whom one could be stealing.

### Rav Ashi's View

Rav Ashi disagrees: *le'olam shmei gazlan* — this third party is indeed called a *gazlan*. The reasoning appears to be that Rav Ashi's case is somewhat different: the third party is not claiming that the object belongs to his own ancestors or making any affirmative claim of ownership. He is simply seizing it opportunistically, reasoning that since neither of the two disputants can prove ownership, he will take it for himself. That posture — seizure without any claim — stands on even weaker ground than the original two litigants. Rav Ashi therefore rules that even within the *kol d'alim gavar* framework, a claimless third-party seizure is impermissible. The object must be returned to the prior situation, and those with actual claims — however unsubstantiated — must argue it out.

### Gazlan Definition

The *gemara* then clarifies what Rabbi Hiyya's statement *gazlan shel rabim lav shmei gazlan* actually means in its primary sense — which turns out to be different from the boat case entirely. The teaching refers literally to a *gazlan* who has stolen from the general public: someone who overcharged customers systematically, or misappropriated communal resources — water from a shared well, for instance. In such a case, he is a *gazlan shel rabim* in a technical sense: he cannot make restitution, *shelo nitan lehishavon*, because the sheer number of victims makes it impossible to identify and repay each person individually. This is the primary meaning of Rabbi Hiyya's dictum. Its application to the boat case by the Sages of Nehardea is a secondary usage, drawing on the same conceptual structure — that where there is no identifiable owner to whom one owes restitution, the formal category of *gazlan* does not attach in the ordinary sense.
