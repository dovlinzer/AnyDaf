# Temurah 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lost Animal Dispute

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת, וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה — תָּמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who designates a sin offering, and** the animal **was lost, and he designated another in its stead** and sacrificed it, **and thereafter the first** animal **was found;** that is a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another animal, and **it shall** be left to **die.**

The daf opens in the middle of a large *mishnah* that spans the bottom of 22b into 23a. The subject is a *chatas* whose owner was *niskaper b'acher* — atoned for through another offering — and the question is under what circumstances the original animal is declared *mesa*, consigned to death. According to the *chachamim*, this outcome applies only when the missing animal is found after the owners have already been atoned for through the second offering. According to Rabbi Meir, it applies even if the missing animal is found after the second one has merely been sanctified, before it was actually brought.

### Rabbis' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּהִקְרִיב אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, הָא לֹא הִקְרִיב אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ — רוֹעָה. מַנִּי? רַבָּנַן הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה — רוֹעָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The first clause of the mishna states that if one designated an animal as a sin offering and the animal was lost, and he sacrificed another animal in its stead, and subsequently the first animal was found, it is left to die. The Gemara infers: The **reason** the animal is left to die is **that he sacrificed another** animal **in its stead; but** if **he did not sacrifice another in its stead,** and instead merely designated it, then the original sin offering is left to **graze.** In accordance with **whose** opinion is this statement? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that if a sin offering was **lost at the time** when another animal was **designated** in its stead, the original sin offering is left to **graze.**

The *gemara* begins its analysis by noting an apparent internal tension within the *mishnah* itself. The opening line of the *mishnah* reads: the reason the first animal must die is that when it was found, the second had already been brought in its stead. The implication is clear — had the second not yet been brought, but only sanctified, the first would merely graze (*ro'eh*) rather than die. This reflects the position of the *rabbanan*: that the animal is *avuda b'sha'as hafrasha* — lost at the time of designation — but is not subject to the death rule unless the replacement was actually brought (*hikriv*). If the animal was found after the second was sanctified but before it was brought, it simply grazes.

### Rabbi Meir Section

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת לְחַטָּאת, וְאָבְדוּ, וְהִפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת תַּחְתֵּיהֶן, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיקַּח חַטָּאת מֵהֶן עַד שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת — יָבִיא מֵאֵלּוּ וּמֵאֵלּוּ חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁאָר יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates money for** purchase of his **sin offering, and** the money **was lost, and he designated other money in its stead, and he did not manage to purchase a sin offering with** that money **before the original** money **was found, he should bring a sin offering from** a combination of **this** original money **and that** money designated in its stead, **and the remainder shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

The middle section of the *mishnah*, however, appears to operate on entirely different assumptions. It addresses the case where one designated money (*maos*) for a *chatas*, that money was lost, a second pile was designated, and then the first pile was found. The *mishnah* teaches that the solution is *yavi me'elu u'me'elu* — one purchases the *chatas* from both piles together, with the remainder going for a *nedava* in the form of an *olah*. The significance of this solution is that it presupposes there is a fundamental problem even if the money was found before the second pile was used. That is, the very act of designating a second pile has already created a situation requiring remediation — which sounds like the position of Rabbi Meir, not the *rabbanan*.

### Structure Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת, וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, לֹא הִסְפִּיק לְהַקְרִיבָהּ עַד שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, הָיוּ שְׁתֵּיהֶן תְּמִימוֹת — אַחַת מֵהֶן תִּיקְרַב חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תָּמוּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין חַטָּאת מֵתָה אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת מֵאַחַר שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים, וְאֵין הַמָּעוֹת הוֹלְכִים לְיָם הַמֶּלַח אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ מֵאַחַר שֶׁכִּיפְּרוּ הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who designates** his **sin offering and** the animal **was lost, and he designated another** animal **in its stead,** and **he did not manage to sacrifice** the sin offering **before the first** sin offering **was found,** and **both of** the animals **are unblemished** and fit for sacrifice, **one of them shall be sacrificed as a sin offering and the other shall** be left to **die;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: A sin offering is not** left to **die unless it was found after** its **owner achieved atonement; and the money is not taken to the Dead Sea unless it was found after its owner achieved atonement.**

This creates a structural difficulty: does the first section of the *mishnah* reflect the *rabbanan* while the middle section reflects Rabbi Meir? The *gemara* resolves this by observing that the *mishnah* first presents the straightforward law according to the *rabbanan* and then elaborates the interesting practical *halacha* according to Rabbi Meir — the solution of mixing the money — before concluding explicitly with the full text of the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *rabbanan*. The explicit statement of their disagreement appears at the end of the *mishnah*, where it states: Rabbi Meir says bring one and the other must die; the *chachamim* say a *chatas* is only put to death if it was found after the owners were atoned for, and similarly the money is only cast into the Dead Sea under those same conditions. The reason for presenting the intermediate cases before stating the dispute openly is to teach the substantive and surprising *halacha* of mixing the money — a solution that would never have been intuited without the *mishnah*'s detailed case presentation.

## Rav Huna Approach

### Consensus on Selection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם מָשַׁךְ אַחַת וְהִקְרִיבָהּ שֶׁהַשְּׁנִיָּה מֵתָה, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לָהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁמָּשַׁךְ אַחַת מֵהֶן וְהִקְרִיב, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **All concede,** even the Rabbis, **that if** the owner did not consult the court about which animal to sacrifice, but instead he **took one** of the animals **and sacrificed it,** he has demonstrated a lack of concern for the fate of the remaining animal, and **that** therefore **the second** animal is left to **die.** Consequently, **you find** that both clauses of the mishna are consistent with the opinion of the Rabbis, as one may interpret the latter clause as referring to a case **where** the owner **took one of the** two sums of money, used it to purchase an animal, **and sacrificed** the animal, **and** in such a case, **all agree** that the other sum of money is cast into the Dead Sea.

The *gemara* now turns to a detailed examination of the two *amora'ic* approaches that explain when the *chachamim* may agree with Rabbi Meir that the first animal must die even when found before the second has been brought. Rav Huna said in the name of Rav: *hakol modim* — everyone agrees — that if you found both animals present and on your own initiative *mashach* one of them, selected one, and then brought it as the offering, the second must die. The act of selection, made unilaterally, constitutes an effective rejection of the unchosen animal. In this, the *rabbanan* concede entirely to Rabbi Meir's result, even though the second animal had not yet been brought at the moment of finding.

### Beis Din Consultation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּבָא לִימָּלֵךְ, דְּרַבִּי סָבַר: לֹא עָשׂוּ תַּקָּנָה בְּקָדָשִׁים, וְאָמְרִינַן לָךְ הִתְכַּפֵּר בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ אֲבוּדָה, וַאֲבוּדָה מֵתָה. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: עָשׂוּ תַּקָּנָה בַּקֳּדָשִׁים, וְאָמְרִינַן לָךְ הִתְכַּפֵּר בַּאֲבוּדָה, וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ אֲבוּדָה תִּרְעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** They disagree only in** a case where the owner **comes to consult** the court. **As Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds:** The Sages **did not** care to **enact** a protective **ordinance with regard to consecrated** items, **and** it was of no concern to them that one of the animals would be left to die. **And** therefore, **we say** to the owner: **Go achieve atonement with** the sin offering **that was not lost, and the** one that was **lost** shall be left to **die. And the Rabbis hold:** The Sages **enacted** a protective **ordinance with regard to consecrated** items, **and we** therefore **say** to the owner: **Go achieve atonement with** the sin offering **that was lost, and the** one **that was not lost shall** be left to **graze.**

The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *rabbanan*, according to Rav Huna's reading, is confined to a very specific scenario: *b'ba l'imalech* — when one comes to ask *Beis Din* what to do. There are, conceptually, two considerations that might affect the outcome. The first is which animal is ultimately brought: the one that had been lost (*avuda*) or the replacement (*she'eina avuda*). The second is whether the selection was made unilaterally or through consultation with *Beis Din*. When a person approaches *Beis Din* and asks for guidance, the act of selecting is no longer a unilateral rejection of one animal over another — it becomes an attempt to navigate the situation constructively. In that context, the *rabbanan* hold that *Beis Din* can and should advise a path that salvages the outcome.

### Rabbi Rejects Salvage

According to Rav Huna's reading, Rabbi Meir's position is that *lo asu takana b'kodshim* — we do not enact remedial measures in matters of *kodshim*. Even when one comes to ask *Beis Din*, Rabbi Meir's position is that *Beis Din* is not interested in engineering a solution. He would advise: go, bring the second animal — the non-lost one — as the *korban*, and let the first, which had been lost, die. This is a striking position. The person is asking for help, the solution exists, and yet the instruction is to bring the replacement and allow the lost animal to die.

### Rabbis Salvage Effort

The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold *asu takana b'kodshim* — we attempt to salvage situations involving *kodshim*. Their advice to *Beis Din* is: tell the person to go and bring the *avuda*, the animal that had originally been lost, as the *korban*. Since that animal is now being brought, and since the replacement animal was never itself lost, the replacement never acquired the stigmatized status that triggers the death rule. It can therefore be allowed to graze, eventually develop a blemish (*mum*), be sold, and the proceeds used for an *olah*. This is the full content of the *rabbanan*'s position: the dispute is exclusively about whether *Beis Din* advises the salvage route. On all other points — including the case where a person acts on their own initiative — the *rabbanan* agree that the second animal must die.

This understanding also illuminates why the middle section of the *mishnah* works even according to the *rabbanan*. The cases there involve someone who did not consult *Beis Din*. Since they acted on their own, the *chachamim* concede to Rabbi Meir that there is a fundamental problem. The only self-help solution available in that posture — that is, without going to *Beis Din* — is to mix the two piles of money, thereby ensuring that neither pile alone is designated as the one that was used while the other was rejected. Mixing creates a situation where both piles are equally implicated in the *korban*, and the remainder is simply *mosar*, leftover funds for a *nedava*. Even a *talmid chacham* who knows the law well enough to realize that *Beis Din* would tell him to bring the *avuda* must still actually go and ask *Beis Din*, because the act of formally consulting them — rather than acting unilaterally — is itself what changes the halachic character of the selection.

### Tosafos Softening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עָשׂוּ תַּקָּנָה בְּקָדָשִׁים? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״יֹאכְלוּ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה אֲכִילָה מוּעֶטֶת, אוֹכְלִין עִמָּהּ חוּלִּין וּתְרוּמָה, כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא נֶאֱכֶלֶת עַל הַשָּׂבָע.
>
> **Translation:** **And** is there such an opinion that the Sages **did not** care to **enact** a protective **ordinance with regard to consecrated** items, to prevent them from being left to die? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a verse that deals with the remainder of a meal offering: "And that which is left thereof shall Aaron and his sons eat; it shall be eaten without leaven in a holy place; in the court of the Tent of Meeting they shall eat it" (Leviticus 6:9). **Why** must **the verse state** at the end the apparently redundant expression: **"They shall eat"?** The verse **teaches that if** the remainder constitutes **a small** amount for **consumption,** the priests **eat non-sacred** food **and *teruma* with it, so that** the remainder **will be eaten while satiated.**

*Tosafos* raises a pointed question against Rabbi Meir's position: how can Rabbi Meir simply not care about salvaging sanctified property, given the general principle that one may not destroy *kodshim* willfully (*ma'avdin kodshim b'yadayim*)? When a solution is readily at hand, why does Rabbi Meir not require that one take it? *Tosafos* answers by suggesting there is some benefit involved in bringing the non-lost animal. If, for example, the *avuda* is thin and weak while the replacement is fat and healthy, Rabbi Meir would instruct to bring the healthier animal as the *korban* — a more fitting offering for the *Beis HaMikdash* — and let the inferior one die. This framing presents Rabbi Meir not as indifferent to *kodshim* generally, but as prioritizing the quality of the offering over the possibility of preserving the rejected animal. This softens what would otherwise be a startling indifference to *takanas kodshim*. The *gemara* itself, however, states Rabbi Meir's position without any such qualification.

### Challenge to Rabbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יֹאכְלוּהָ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה אֲכִילָה מְרוּבָּה, אֵין אוֹכְלִין עִמָּהּ חוּלִּין וּתְרוּמָה, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא נֶאֱכֶלֶת עַל הַגַּסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **why** must **the verse state: "They shall eat it"?** The verse teaches **that if** the remainder constitutes **a large** amount for **consumption,** e.g., there are many remainders from the meal offerings, then **one does not eat non-sacred** food **or *teruma* with it, so that it will not be eaten** in **an excessive** manner. Otherwise, one might fail to consume the entire remainder, and some of it would be rendered leftover from an offering after the time allotted for its consumption [*notar*].

A challenge is now raised against the premise that Rabbi Meir never concerns himself with salvaging *kodshim*. A *beraisa* is cited in which a remedial instruction is given regarding the eating of a *mincha*: if there is a great deal of *mincha* and relatively few *kohanim* to eat it, they are told not to eat other foods beforehand, so that when they eat the *mincha* they will not be stuffing themselves (*achila gassa*), which would not constitute valid eating. This instruction is a kind of *takana* — an advisory arrangement to ensure the *kodshim* are handled properly. If Rabbi Meir genuinely held that no such remedial arrangements are ever made, this *beraisa* would appear to contradict him. The *gemara* responds that perhaps this *beraisa* is only according to the *rabbanan*, and Rabbi Meir would indeed not endorse even this kind of practical arrangement. This remains a somewhat difficult position.

### Mincha Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לַָאו אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי? לָא, רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Mesharshiyya concludes his objection: This *baraita* teaches that an ordinance was enacted in order to prevent the remainder of a meal offering from being rendered *notar*. The same reasoning should apply to other consecrated items, including a sin offering. **What, is it not** that this *baraita* is stated **even** according to the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? The Gemara responds: **No,** this *baraita* is stated specifically according to the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* works through the *beraisa* in detail. The verse states *yochluha* — they shall eat it — which is apparently a redundant phrase given that eating has already been mentioned. The redundancy teaches that if the *mincha* is plentiful and there are fewer *kohanim*, they should not fill themselves on ordinary food or *teruma* beforehand, so that the *mincha* should not end up being eaten in a state of *achila gassa*. Conversely, if the *mincha* is small and many *kohanim* are present, they may and should eat beforehand so that they have an appropriate appetite and the eating of the *mincha* will be meaningful. The *gemara* then asks: does this *beraisa* not reflect even Rabbi Meir's position? The answer given is that it may reflect only the *rabbanan*. This confirms the difficult implication that Rabbi Meir, at least as the *gemara* reads him here, would not endorse practical measures to ensure the proper treatment of *kodshim* even in simple, uncontested cases.

One further point arises tangentially: the question of whether the *kohanim* could eat non-*kodshim* food within the *azara* in order to prepare themselves to eat the *mincha* there. *Rashi* holds that they should go outside to eat, since bringing *chullin* into the *azara* is problematic. *Tosafos*, however, argues in several places that there is no categorical prohibition against bringing *chullin* into the *azara*; the prohibition concerns treating non-*kodshim* items with the ceremonial character of *avoda*, not merely their physical presence in that space.

## Rabbi Abba Approach

### Using Non-Lost Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּמִתְכַּפֵּר בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ אֲבוּדָה — שֶׁאֲבוּדָה מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** that which **Rabbi Abba** says that **Rav says,** the mishna cannot be interpreted in this manner. **As** Rabbi Abba **said: All concede** that **in** a case where **one achieves atonement through the one that was not lost, the one that was lost** is left to **die.**

Rabbi Abba said in the name of Rav: *hakol modim b'miskaper b'she'eina avuda she'avuda mesa* — everyone agrees that if you were atoned for through the non-lost animal, the number-two replacement, then the first animal, which had been lost, must die. This is not surprising: the first animal had already acquired the stigmatized status of *avuda*, and if it was not the one brought as the *korban*, that stigma is not remediated.

### Using Lost Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ? בְּמִתְכַּפֵּר בַּאֲבוּדָה, דְּרַבִּי סָבַר: מַפְרִישׁ לְאִיבּוּד כְּאִיבּוּד דָּמֵי, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָאו כְּאִיבּוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to what** case do the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **disagree?** They disagree **with regard to** the case of **one who achieves atonement with the one that was lost. As Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that if one **designates** a sin offering instead of one that was **lost, it is considered like the lost** sin offering. Accordingly, just as the lost animal is left to die if the second sin offering was sacrificed, the replacement animal is left to die if the original animal was sacrificed. **And the Rabbis hold** that if one designates a sin offering instead of one that was lost, **it is not considered like the lost** animal. Rather, if the original animal is sacrificed, the replacement animal is left to graze.

The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *rabbanan*, on this reading, concerns the opposite case: *b'miskaper b'avuda* — when you bring the animal that had been lost as the *korban*. Here the question is what happens to the replacement, number two, which had never been lost at all.

### Quasi-Lost Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי סָבַר: מַפְרִישׁ לְאִיבּוּד כְּאִיבּוּד דָּמֵי
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that if one **designates** a sin offering instead of one that was **lost, it is considered like the lost** sin offering.

Rabbi Meir holds *muchlefes ke-kedusha dami* — the replacement has a status equivalent to that of the original. Since it was designated specifically to stand in place of the lost animal, it takes on the same halachic character as the one it replaced, including a quasi-lost status. Just as the original, if not brought, would be *mesa* because it was once lost, so too the replacement — by virtue of its substitutive function — carries that same quasi-*avuda* status. Therefore, even if you bring the original lost animal, the replacement must die.

### No Quasi-Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָאו כְּאִיבּוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis hold** that if one designates a sin offering instead of one that was lost, **it is not considered like the lost** animal.

The *rabbanan* disagree: *lav ke-kedusha dami* — the replacement does not acquire the status of the original. The replacement was never lost. It never experienced the rejection and stigmatization that forms the foundation of the death rule. Designating it as a substitute does not transfer the original's history onto it. Therefore, if you bring the original *avuda* as the *korban*, the replacement — which was always a clean, unstigmatized animal — may simply graze, develop a *mum*, be sold, and the proceeds go toward an *olah*.

This understanding of the dispute is clean and analytically elegant. It explains the debate in terms of whether a substitutive designation transmits status, without invoking the separate question of *Beis Din* consultation at all.

One might then ask how this approach explains the middle section of the *mishnah*, which appears to offer mixing the piles as the only solution — implying there is no simpler alternative such as simply using the pile that had been lost. According to this approach, the *rabbanan* would in fact have a simpler solution available: bring the *chatas* from the pile that had been lost, and the other pile, never having had *avuda* status, may go for a *nedava* without requiring any mixing. The *gemara* raises this as an objection and responds, as before, that the *mishnah* states only the categorical solution — the one that always works under all circumstances. Mixing the piles is the *psika* case, the solution that admits of no complications regardless of which pile is used or how the selection was made. The *mishnah* does not purport to enumerate every possible solution, and the existence of alternative paths does not contradict the teaching that this particular path is always effective.

Regarding the analogy to the *Yom Kippur* goats: the *gemara* elsewhere discusses what happens when one of the two goats designated for *Yom Kippur* dies and a second pair must be brought. Rav rules that *ba'alei chayim einam nidchin* — live animals cannot be halachically rejected — and therefore the surviving goat from the first pair retains its validity. The *kohen gadol* is atoned for using that goat from the first pair. The extra goat from the second pair thus corresponds to one that was designated knowing full well it would not be brought — a case the *gemara* calls *mafrish le'ibud*, designating for non-use from the outset. This is argued to be analogous to the situation where an animal is designated as a replacement from the start. The *gemara* notes that this analogy is actually somewhat stronger than the ordinary *avuda* case, since there the owner intended to use the replacement but here the second-pair animal was always destined not to be brought.

## Money Mixing Solution

### Mixing Moneys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמַּפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת לְחַטָּאת, וְאָבְדוּ, וְהִפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת תַּחְתֵּיהֶן, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לִיקַּח חַטָּאת מֵהֶן עַד שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת — יָבִיא מֵאֵלּוּ וּמֵאֵלּוּ חַטָּאת, וְהַשְּׁאָר יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses another difficulty from that which **we learned** in the mishna: In the case of **one who designates money for** purchase of his **sin offering, and** the money **was lost, and he designated other** money **in its stead, and he did not manage to purchase a sin offering with** that money **before the original** money **was found, he should bring a sin offering from** a combination of **this** original money **and that** money designated in its stead, **and the remainder shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

The *gemara* now revisits the *mishnah*'s middle section in its own right. The *mishnah* teaches: *hamafrish ma'os l'chataso v'avdu* — one who designated money for a *chatas*, the money was lost, he designated a second pile, and then the first was found — the solution is *yavi me'elu u'me'elu*, to purchase the *chatas* from both piles combined. The remainder goes for a *nedava*, an *olah*. In this way, neither pile is identifiable as the one that was definitively chosen while the other was rejected.

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּמִתְכַּפֵּר מֵאֵלּוּ וּמֵאֵלּוּ, הָא מֵאֶחָד — יוֹלִיכֵם לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. לָא שְׁנָא מִתְכַּפֵּר בַּאֲבוּדָה, וְלָא שְׁנָא מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ אֲבוּדָה, וְלָא שְׁנָא מָשַׁךְ, וְלָא שְׁנָא לֹא מָשַׁךְ — תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ! הָכָא נָמֵי: מִילְּתָא דִּפְסִיקָא לֵיהּ קָתָנֵי, וּמִילְּתָא דְּלָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: The **reason** that the remainder is allocated for communal gift offerings is **that he atones** by bringing a sin offering **from** a combination of **this** original money **and that** money designated in its stead, **but** if the owner brought a sin offering **from one of** the two sums of money, **he** must **take** the other money and cast it **into the Dead Sea.** The mishna apparently indicates that there **is no difference** whether he **achieves atonement with the** money that was **lost and** there **is no difference** whether he **achieves atonement with the** money **that was not lost, and** there **is no difference** whether he **took** the money and brought a sin offering without consulting the court **and** there **is no difference** whether he **did not take** the money without consulting the court. In all cases, the other money is cast into the Dead Sea. If so, this is **a conclusive refutation of** the opinions of **both** Rav Huna and Rabbi Abba, as the mishna does not conform to either explanation. The Gemara responds: **Here too,** the *tanna* **taught a matter that was absolute,** i.e., a case where the owner achieved atonement with one of the two sums of money, **but he did not teach a matter that was not absolute.**

One might ask: according to the *rabbanan*, why is mixing necessary at all? Under Rav Huna's approach, you could consult *Beis Din* and be advised to bring from the *avuda* pile, thereby salvaging the replacement pile. Under Rabbi Abba's approach, you could simply use the pile that had originally been lost, and the replacement pile would never have had a problem in the first place. Either way, it appears that the *mishnah* is presenting mixing as if it were the only solution — which seems to contradict both Amoraic approaches.

### Clear vs. Unclear Cases

The *gemara* resolves this by applying the same principle it used earlier: *milta d'psika lei katani, milta d'lo psika lei lo katani*. The *mishnah* teaches only what is categorically true and admits of no exceptions. Mixing the piles always works, regardless of which pile is used for the *korban*, regardless of whether one acted unilaterally or consulted *Beis Din*, regardless of any other circumstance. The *mishnah* is not suggesting that no other solutions exist — it is teaching the one solution that is universally effective. The existence of situationally contingent alternatives is not denied; the *mishnah* simply confines itself to the unambiguous case.

## Surety Piles Case

### No Death for Backup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הַמַּפְרִישׁ שְׁנֵי צִיבּוּרֵי מָעוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת, מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְשֵׁנִי יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to designating money for purchasing a sin offering, **Rabbi Ami says:** In the case of **one who designates two piles of money as a guarantee,** so that in the event that one pile is lost he will purchase his sin offering with the other pile, **he achieves atonement with one of them, and the second** pile is **allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

Rabbi Ami introduces a distinct and clarifying case. If a person designated two piles of money *l'achrayos* — for surety, as a backup — meaning from the outset one pile was meant to serve as collateral in case the other was lost, and then one pile was used to purchase the *chatas*: in this case the second pile does not die and is not cast into the Dead Sea. Rather, *niskaper b'echad meihen v'hasheni yiplu l'nedava* — one was used for the *korban* and the other goes for a *nedava*, an *olah*. This is because nothing in this configuration ever had the status of *avud*, lost.

### Agreement Without Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי — פְּשִׁיטָא דְּיִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה, עַד כַּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלָּא בְּמַפְרִישׁ לְאִבּוּד, אֲבָל לְאַחְרָיוּת מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this statement? **If we say** that it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, it is **obvious that** the second pile of money is **allocated for** communal **gift** offerings, as **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that the surplus money is cast into the Dead Sea **only** in a case **where** one **designated** the money instead of money that was **lost. But** if one separated the two sums of money at the same time **as a guarantee,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **concedes** that the unused money is allocated for gift offerings.

The *gemara* asks: according to whom must this ruling be stated? If it is stated according to Rabbi Meir, it is obvious. And if it is stated according to the *rabbanan*, it is even more obviously true.

### Lost Status Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּיִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: הַשְׁתָּה מַפְרִישׁ לְאִיבּוּד אֲמוּר רַבָּנַן לָאו כְּאִיבּוּד דָּמֵי, לְאַחְרָיוּת מִיבַּעְיָא?! וְאֶלָּא, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא לֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נְדָבָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאִית לֵיהּ נְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** you will say that it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis.** But if so, isn't it **obvious that** the unused money is **allocated for** communal **gift** offerings, due to the following ***a fortiori*** inference: **Now** that **the Rabbis say** with regard to **one who designates** money to replace money that was **lost** that it **is not considered like** the **lost** money and is not cast into the Dead Sea, **is it necessary** to teach that money designated **as a guarantee** is allocated for communal gift offerings and is not cast into the sea? **Rather, it was necessary for** Rabbi Ami to teach this *halakha* **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that whenever the owner achieved atonement with another sin offering the remaining animal is left to die, even if it was initially designated as a guarantee. **Lest you say** that just as **Rabbi Shimon does not hold** that an animal is left to graze if the owner achieved atonement with another animal, he also does not maintain that the unused money is allocated for communal **gift** offerings, Rabbi Ami **teaches us that** even Rabbi Shimon **holds** that if one separates additional money as a guarantee, the unused money is allocated for communal **gift** offerings.

The reason it is obvious according to Rabbi Meir is that *ad kan lo ka'amar Rebbi ela b'makom sheyesh ibud* — Rabbi Meir's entire position, including his ruling that the replacement acquires a quasi-*avuda* status, is premised on there having been an actual *ibud* somewhere in the chain of events. The replacement is treated as quasi-lost only because it was designated to stand in for something that was genuinely lost. Here, nothing was ever lost. The two piles were set aside together from the beginning, one as the primary fund and one as backup. Since the foundation of the death rule — *avuda* — is entirely absent, even Rabbi Meir would agree that the second pile simply goes for a *nedava*. The ruling is obvious.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ דְּלֵית לֵיהּ נְדָבָה לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? וְהָתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שׁוֹפָרוֹת הָיוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְהָיָה כָּתוּב עֲלֵיהֶן: ״תִּיקְלִין חַדְתִין״ וְ״עַתִּיקִין״, ״קִינִּין״ וְ״גוֹזְלֵי עוֹלָה״, ״עֵצִים״ וּ״לְבוֹנָה״ וְ״זָהָב לְכַפֹּרֶת״, וְשִׁשָּׁה לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But how can you say** that one might think that **Rabbi Shimon does not hold** that the unused money is allocated for communal **gift** offerings? **Isn't it taught** in a mishna (*Shekalim* 18a): **There were thirteen** collection **horns in the Temple, and** the intended use of the funds **was written upon** each one, as follows: **New shekels,** for shekels given for the current year; **and old** shekels, for shekels given belatedly for the previous year; **pairs** of birds, whose funds were used to purchase obligatory bird offerings, e.g., for a woman who gave birth; **and fledglings** designated for voluntary **burnt offerings; wood** for the arrangement on the altar; **and frankincense** that accompanied meal offerings; **and gold** donated **for the Ark Cover. And** the remaining **six** horns were designated **for** the purchase of communal **gift** offerings.

The *gemara* concludes that the ruling is needed specifically *aliba d'Rabbi Shimon* — according to Rabbi Shimon's position. Rabbi Shimon is the *tanna* who elsewhere treats the *chamesh chatas hamesos* — the five categories of *chatas* that must die — as a unified group, such that if any one of them does not apply in a given context (*tzibbur*), none of them apply. Based on this categorical approach, one might have supposed that Rabbi Shimon holds the death rule operates without exceptions whenever the *halacha* applies at all — that is, once you are in the world of *chatas*, the animal always dies, never grazes, no *nedava* ever applies. Rabbi Ami therefore must state that even Rabbi Shimon agrees that *nedava* applies when the second pile was never in a position of *avuda* to begin with.

### Yehoyada Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: שִׁשָּׁה לִנְדָבָה — לְעוֹלָה הַבָּאָה מִן הַמּוֹתָרוֹת, שֶׁאֵין עוֹרָהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִם כֵּן, בָּטְלָה מִדְרָשׁוֹ שֶׁל יְהוֹיָדָע הַכֹּהֵן. דְּתַנְיָא: זֶה מִדְּרַשׁ דָּרַשׁ יְהוֹיָדָע הַכֹּהֵן — ״אָשָׁם הוּא״ כּוּלּוֹ, לְהָבִיא כׇּל דָּבָר הַבָּא מִמּוֹתַר חַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת, לִיקַּח בְּדָמָיו עוֹלוֹת — הַבָּשָׂר לַשֵּׁם, וְעוֹרוֹת לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught with regard to** that mishna: The funds contained in the **six** horns designated **for** communal **gift** offerings were used **to** purchase **burnt offerings that come from the surplus** funds which had been allocated for the purchase of sin offerings or guilt offerings and were not needed for those purposes. Such burnt offerings were brought in the absence of other offerings, so that the altar would not remain idle. Unlike other burnt offerings, the **hides** of these offerings were **not** given **to the priests;** rather, they were sold, and the money received from their sale was allocated for additional communal gift offerings. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** **Rabbi Neḥemya said to him, and some say** it was **Rabbi Shimon** who said to Rabbi Yehuda: **If so, the teaching of Jehoiada the** High **Priest** has been **negated. As it is taught** in the aforementioned mishna: **This teaching was taught** by **Jehoiada the** High **Priest:** There is an apparent contradiction in a verse. On the one hand, the verse states: **"It is a guilt offering"** (Leviticus 5:19), indicating that the meat of this guilt offering is eaten by the priests, as is the *halakha* with regard to all guilt offerings. On the other hand, the same verse concludes: "He is certainly guilty before the Lord," which indicates that the **entire** offering is for God, like a burnt offering. Rather, the verse comes **to include** the following *halakha*: With regard to **anything that comes from surplus** funds designated for **sin offerings or guilt offerings,** those **funds** should be used **to purchase burnt offerings. The meat** of these burnt offerings **is** entirely **for God, and the hides** are given **to the priests,** as is the *halakha* with regard to burnt offerings.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: was it not already obvious that Rabbi Shimon acknowledges *nedava* as a category? For Rabbi Shimon himself is quoted in a *beraisa* about the thirteen *shofros* — the charity boxes in the *Beis HaMikdash*. Thirteen boxes were stationed there, each designated for a different type of contribution. Seven were for specific purposes: *taklin chadatin* (new *shekalim* for the current year contributed late), *atikin* (old *shekalim* from the prior year contributed late), *kinin* (pairs of bird offerings for those obligated in *kinim* such as a *zav*, *zava*, *yoledes*, or *metzora*), *gozlei olah* (individual bird *olah* contributions), *eitzim* (wood for the altar fires), *levona* (frankincense), and *zahav lakapores* (gold for the *kapores* or other Temple vessels). Six were designated simply *l'nedava* — for voluntary contributions — specifically for the *olah* offerings purchased from leftover *chatas* and *asham* funds.

The relevant *beraisa* records a *midrash* taught by Yehoyada the *kohen*, based on the verse *asham hu asham asham laHashem* — the repeated word *asham* teaches that the money goes for *God* and the *kohanim*: the flesh of the *olah* is entirely for God (*olah* is entirely burnt), but the skin goes to the *kohanim*. Rav Nechemya — and some say Rabbi Shimon — objected: if the skins go to the *kohanim*, then you have effectively nullified Yehoyada's *midrash*, which teaches that everything from these leftover funds goes entirely for the *olah* without any priestly benefit. The point for our purposes is that Rabbi Shimon's objection demonstrates his clear acceptance that *nedava* applies in these cases of leftover *chatas* and *asham* funds. That is, Rabbi Shimon does hold that *nedava* is an operative category for leftover *kodshim* funds. Why then was Rabbi Ami's ruling necessary?

### One vs. Two Piles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא אִית לֵיהּ נְדָבָה לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כִּי אִית לֵיהּ נְדָבָה לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּחַד סִידְרָא, אֲבָל בִּתְרֵי סִדְרֵי לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Apparently, Rabbi Shimon does hold** that surplus funds which were designated for sin offerings are allocated for communal **gift** offerings. The statement of Rabbi Ami is therefore unnecessary even according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara responds: It **was necessary** for Rabbi Ami to teach this *halakha*, as it might **enter your mind to say: When Rabbi Shimon holds** that surplus money which was designated for a sin offering is allocated for communal **gift** offerings, that applies only **in** a case where the surplus is **from one set** of money, e.g., one allocated funds to purchase a goat and the price of goats depreciated, and therefore there is now a surplus from the money that was set aside. **But in** a case where one designates **two** distinct **sets** of money, such as an additional set of money as a guarantee, Rabbi Shimon does **not** state that the surplus money is allocated for communal gift offerings. Rabbi Ami therefore **teaches us** that even in this case, Rabbi Shimon agrees that the surplus money is allocated for communal gift offerings.

The *gemara* distinguishes the two scenarios. In the *Yehoyada midrash* case, there is one pile of money designated for a *chatas*, and after the *korban* is purchased, there is leftover money from that single designation. That leftover naturally and obviously goes for a *nedava* — no one would think otherwise. But in Rabbi Ami's case, there are two piles designated from the outset, one as backup for the other. One might have thought — at least according to Rabbi Shimon's categorical approach — that the second pile, having been designated as a correlate to the first, acquires a rejected or quasi-lost status and must be destroyed rather than used for *nedava*. That is the *chiddush* Rabbi Ami comes to teach: even in that case, since nothing was ever actually lost, neither pile ever acquired *avuda* status, and the second pile goes for a *nedava* just like simple leftover funds.

### Lost Status Foundation

The *gemara*'s conclusion thus articulates a foundational principle that has been operative throughout the entire sugya but is stated explicitly only now: the entire *halacha* of *chatas mesa* — including Rabbi Meir's expansive application of it, including his ruling that the replacement acquires a quasi-*avuda* status — is built entirely on the premise that something was *avud*, genuinely lost, at some point in the chain of events. Rabbi Meir does not disagree with this foundation; his innovation is only that *avuda* status can be transmitted derivatively to the replacement through the logic of *muchlefes ke-kedusha dami*. But where no *avuda* ever existed — where two piles were designated together for surety from the start — even Rabbi Meir agrees that *niskaper b'echad meihen v'hasheni yiplu l'nedava*: use one for the *korban* and the other goes simply and cleanly for a *nedava*.
