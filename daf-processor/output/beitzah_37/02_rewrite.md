# Beitzah 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shvut & Gezeirah

### Shvut Definition

The *mishnah* before us presents a category of rabbinic prohibitions described as *asur mishum shvut* — forbidden on account of *shvut*. This category applies broadly to *issurei d'rabbanan* on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, though the *mishnah* enumerates only a select collection of cases, which invites the question of what makes these particular instances distinctive.

As discussed in the previous session, the *Bavli* frames several of these prohibitions — climbing a tree, riding an animal, swimming, clapping, and dancing — as *gezeirot*: safeguards against performing a prohibited *melachah*. One must not ride an animal lest one break off a branch; one must not clap or dance lest one come to repair a musical instrument. Yet there is no indication in the *mishnah* itself that this is the conceptual basis of the prohibition, and neither the *Tosefta* nor the *Yerushalmi* frames these restrictions in terms of *gezeirah* at all.

What appears to be the original conception underlying the *mishnah* — and this bears repeating — is that these activities fall under the category of *uvda d'chol*, weekday-type conduct, and *shvut* in its most basic sense: the preservation of the character of *Shabbat*. Riding one's animal, tending to crops by chasing away birds, climbing trees to harvest fruit — these were quintessentially weekday activities, and prohibiting them reflects the imperative to distinguish the sacred day from ordinary life. The *Bavli*, however, characteristically reframes everything in terms of *gezeirah*, as a safeguard against some further prohibition.

### Tosafot Dancing

This *Bavli* framing is precisely what *Tosafot* engages when confronting the widespread practice of clapping and dancing — not in the agricultural context of scaring away birds, but in the context of the synagogue or a *simchah*. *Tosafot* argues that since the entire prohibition was formulated as a *gezeirah* lest one come to repair a musical instrument, and since in their time people did not routinely repair musical instruments themselves, the *gezeirah* no longer applies and the activity is therefore permitted.

This reasoning, however, stands in tension with the general principle established already at the opening of *Beitzah*: *af al pi shebatla hata'am lo batla hagezeirah* — even when the reason disappears, the *gezeirah* does not disappear — *d'kol davar she'b'minyan tzarich minyan acher l'hati'ro*, for anything enacted by a rabbinic assembly requires another assembly to annul it. Normally, one would need substantial evidence to demonstrate that a prohibition was intended to be conditional on the continuing relevance of its rationale.

It should be noted that *Tosafot* did not invoke this principle casually as a general license. *Tosafot* characteristically employed such reasoning when confronted with a divergence between established practice in the community and what the *Gemara* appeared to prohibit. Rather than declare the community in violation, *Tosafot* sought to interpret the *Gemara* in a way that harmonizes with observed practice. The possibility that a *gezeirah* lapses when its reason no longer applies was one such interpretive tool, deployed specifically when the facts on the ground demanded an explanation.

### Medicine Gezeirah

A striking parallel arises in a related area not found on our *daf* but directly relevant to this discussion: the prohibition against taking medicine on *Shabbat*. Here again, the *Bavli* formulates the restriction as a *gezeirah* — lest one come to grind the medicine. Yet earlier sources show no evidence that grinding was the underlying concern. It may well be that the prohibition was originally understood as intrinsic: going to a healer, involving oneself in a professional therapeutic process, engaging with remedies — all of this was simply the kind of weekday activity one does not conduct on *Shabbat*. The Gospel accounts of healing on *Shabbat*, incidentally, reflect precisely this sensibility: the Pharisees' objection appears to be to the intrinsic inappropriateness of the activity on *Shabbat*, not to any fear of grinding.

But by framing it as a *gezeirah*, the *Bavli* opens a door. On *Yom Tov*, for instance, grinding spices is explicitly permitted, and one might therefore argue that the *gezeirah* against medicine never applied to *Yom Tov* categorically. Similarly, some have argued that in contemporary circumstances — where patients do not grind their own medications — the *gezeirah* does not apply with the same force. It is rare to find a *posek* who will state outright that the prohibition has lapsed entirely, since the basic assumption remains *af al pi shebatla hata'am*. But the framing of the prohibition as a *gezeirah* has led *poskim* to interpret its parameters more creatively. The category of *choleh kol haguf*, for example — one whose entire body is affected — was excluded from the restriction from the outset, and many contemporary authorities define that category broadly in order to permit medication in a wide range of circumstances. This is the practical consequence of the tension between the stated rationale and the ongoing prohibition: even when the principle holds that the *gezeirah* survives its reason, the gap between reason and rule can lead to expansive readings of the exceptions.

### Af Al Pi Shebatla

This brings us to the broader methodological point. The *Bavli* consistently frames these *shvut* prohibitions as *gezeirot* rather than as intrinsic restrictions. With perhaps one exception — the separation of *terumot u'ma'asrot*, to which we will return — every prohibition in the *mishnah*'s catalogue is assigned a *gezeirah* rationale. This is a recurring and deliberate feature of *Bavli* legal reasoning. The framing is consequential: it invites the question of whether the prohibition survives a change in circumstances, even as the classical answer is that it does. *Tosafot* applies this possibility selectively, and only when practice has already diverged. *Poskim* apply it creatively, shaping the contours of the exceptions. But the underlying principle — *af al pi shebatla hata'am lo batla hagezeirah* — remains the default, and departing from it demands strong justification.

## Rashut Category

### Beit Din Debate

The *mishnah* then turns to a second category, introduced with the phrase *v'eilu hein mishum reshut* — and the following things are prohibited on account of *reshut*. The term *reshut* here does not mean something entirely discretionary or neutral. Rather, it designates activities that carry some *mitzvah* component, yet fall short of being obligatory *mitzvot* — and are nonetheless forbidden as *shvut*.

The first item is sitting in *beit din*: one may not convene a court on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. The obvious question is: *v'ha mitzvah ka avid* — is this not a *mitzvah*? There are numerous verses commanding judges to adjudicate. The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzricha d'ika d'adif minei* — there is always someone more qualified to sit in judgment. This is a significant observation. While there is certainly a *mitzvah* dimension to serving as a *dayan*, in any given case one can almost always point to someone more competent. This diminishes the obligatory character of the act without eliminating its *mitzvah* quality entirely.

### Kiddushin & Pru Urvu

The second item is *kiddushin* — one may not betroth a woman on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. Again the *Gemara* asks: is this not a *mitzvah*? And again the *Gemara* qualifies: *lo tzricha d'it lei isha u'vanim* — we are speaking of a case where the man already has a wife and children and has fulfilled the obligation of *pru u'rvu*.

This ruling raises a fascinating question that is the subject of a genuine *machloket rishonim*. The *Gemara*'s formulation seems to suggest that the entire *mitzvah* dimension of marriage lies in enabling the fulfillment of *pru u'rvu*. The *Rambam*, *Meiri*, and others among the *Rishonim* understand marriage as a *mitzvah* in the sense that it is a *hechsher mitzvah* — an enabling act for *pru u'rvu*. The *Rosh*, by contrast, argues that marriage is not even a *mitzvah* in its own right, since in principle *pru u'rvu* could be fulfilled outside of wedlock — and if the *mitzvah* is *pru u'rvu* alone, then the marriage itself carries no independent obligatory status.

This has practical implications. Once a man has fulfilled *pru u'rvu*, does he remain obligated to remarry if his wife dies or they divorce? The *Gemara* implies not — at that point the *kiddushin* falls into the *reshut* category. And yet it must be noted, as one present in the study rightly observed, that *reshut* does not mean the complete absence of a *mitzvah*. *Rashi* himself notes *k'tzat mitzvah ika* — there is still some *mitzvah* dimension, which he identifies with the verse *be-voker zera et zar'acha vela-erev al tanach yadecha*, the value of having more than the minimal number of children. The *reshut* category therefore denotes not the absence of *mitzvah* but its relative diminishment. Elsewhere the *Gemara* speaks of the intrinsic value of marriage quite apart from children — *lo tov heyot ha'adam levado*, *kol adam she-sharui belo ishah sharui belo simchah* — but for the specific purposes of this categorization, the *Gemara* focuses on the obligatory dimension, which is primarily tied to *pru u'rvu*.

### Tosafot Kiddushin

*Tosafot* raises an alternative and striking reading of the *Gemara*'s question. The *Gemara* asks why *kiddushin* is categorized as *reshut* rather than *mitzvah*. *Tosafot* suggests that this question carries a more radical implication: if *kiddushin* were truly a *mitzvah*, it ought to be permitted on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* outright. That is, *Tosafot* reads the *Gemara* as raising the possibility that a full-fledged *mitzvah* might override the *shvut* prohibition entirely — and therefore, when the man has not yet fulfilled *pru u'rvu*, the *kiddushin* should actually be permitted.

This is admittedly a difficult reading of the *Gemara*, because the final category of the *mishnah* — the *mitzvah* category — explicitly lists genuine *mitzvot* that are nonetheless prohibited. Still, *Tosafot*'s position is worth noting. In the spirit of this line of thinking, *Tosafot* elsewhere — citing *Rabbeinu Tam* — finds grounds to be lenient about *kiddushin* in pressing circumstances, such as when one party is in *avelut*. The argument runs that the urgency of fulfilling *pru u'rvu* or the social realities of ensuring timely marriage can generate leniency even where a prohibition would otherwise apply. This likely reflects the reality of medieval life, in which death and violence created frequent and pressing need to remarry quickly.

### Chalitzah Yibum

The third item is *chalitzah* and *yibum* — the levirate obligations — which are likewise forbidden. Again the question: *be-mitzvah ka avid*! And again the answer: *lo tzricha de-ika gadol* — there is an older brother, and *mitzvah be-gadol leyabem*, the obligation of *yibum* falls first on the eldest brother. So while there is certainly a *mitzvah* to perform *yibum* or *chalitzah*, if there is an older brother who bears the primary obligation, the younger brother's performance of it is not as full a *mitzvah*. The parallel to the *beit din* case is precise. In neither instance does the *reshut* designation imply that the *mitzvah* dimension is entirely absent; it means only that the obligation is attenuated by circumstances.

## Sanctification Gezeirah

### Mekach Umemkar

The *mishnah* continues with the category introduced by the phrase *v'eilu hein mishum mitzvah* — and the following are prohibited despite being genuine *mitzvot*. These include: *lo makdishin v'lo maarichin v'lo machrimin* — one does not consecrate objects, assess their value for redemption purposes, or declare them banned. The *Gemara* states: *gezeirah mishum mekach u'memkar* — this is a safeguard against business dealings.

Why is *mekach u'memkar* itself problematic? Here *Rashi* presents two distinct explanations that map precisely onto the conceptual tension discussed throughout this session. His first explanation is that consecrating an object — transferring it from one's domain to that of *hekdesh* — resembles a transaction. And *mekach u'memkar* is prohibited from the *Torah* itself on the basis of *mimtzo cheftzecha v'daber davar*: one may not pursue one's own affairs on *Shabbat*, and commercial activity is intrinsically a "worldly pursuit." On this reading, *mekach u'memkar* is an *issur* in its own right, and the prohibition against consecration is a *gezeirah* against something already intrinsically forbidden.

*Rashi*'s second explanation is that *mekach u'memkar* is itself a *gezeirah* — lest one come to write things down. On this reading, consecration leads to business, which leads to writing. The *Gemara* addresses the objection that this would constitute a *gezeirah l'gezeirah*: a safeguard against a safeguard. The response is that *kula chad gezeirah hi* — it is all part of a single integrated prohibition. Even so, the underlying question — is *mekach u'memkar* intrinsically forbidden, or only as a *gezeirah* against writing? — mirrors the same structural ambiguity we have traced throughout.

The verse *mimtzo cheftzecha v'daber davar* does ordinarily admit an exemption for *cheftzei shamayim* — the affairs of Heaven. One might therefore argue that *kiddushin* or *yibum*, which are *cheftzei shamayim*, should be free of the *mekach u'memkar* concern. But the *Gemara* identifies a further concern even in those cases — the concern of *shema yichtov*, that one will come to write — which applies regardless of whether the underlying activity is religious or commercial in character.

### Terumot Ma'asrot

The *mishnah* also prohibits *lo magbihin trumos u'maasros* — one may not separate *terumot* and *ma'asrot* on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*, even though this is a *mitzvah*. The *Gemara* responds: *pshita* — this is obvious. And presumably the reason it is obvious is that this prohibition is not cast as a *gezeirah* at all but is intrinsically problematic: separating *terumah* changes the status of the produce and "fixes" it, an act analogous to making or completing an object on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. This stands as a notable exception to the *Bavli*'s general tendency to frame everything in terms of *gezeirah*.

### Terumah to Kohen

*Rav Yosef* adds a qualification: *lo nitzrecha ela litnah l'kohen bo bayom* — the *chiddush* is that one may not even separate the *terumah* and give it directly to the *kohen* on the same day. One might have thought that reframing the act as giving a gift of food to the *kohen* — rather than fixing the produce — would render it permissible. After all, giving gifts of food is generally permitted on *Yom Tov*. Nevertheless, the prohibition stands.

### Challah Separation

There is, however, an important exception. The *Gemara* distinguishes between *pira d'tavil me'etmol* — produce that was *tevel* from before *Yom Tov*, for which the prohibition applies in full — and *pira d'tavil ha'idna* — produce that becomes *tevel* on *Yom Tov* itself. The only produce that can become *tevel* on *Yom Tov* is dough: on *Yom Tov* one may bake bread, and dough that is freshly made on *Yom Tov* becomes obligated in *challah* on that day. The *Gemara* rules that one may separate *challah* from such dough and give it to the *kohen*: *mafrishin u'mavilin l'kohein*.

Two points emerge. First, this falls under the *ochel nefesh* dispensation — since the baking of bread is permitted on *Yom Tov* for the sake of eating, separating *challah* from that dough is an integral part of the permitted food-preparation process. Second, the *Gemara* emphasizes that one should give the *challah* to the *kohen*, not merely separate it and set it aside. This presentation — transferring it as a gift to the *kohen* — helps reframe the act away from the impression of "fixing" the dough and toward the impression of gift-giving. That said, the *poskim* do not require giving the *challah* to the *kohen* as a strict condition for permissibility; in our time, *challat chutz la'aretz* is not given to the *kohen* at all, and one may still separate *challah* on *Yom Tov* from dough made on *Yom Tov*.

## Category Framework

### Shvut Levels

At this point the *Gemara* addresses an apparent difficulty in the *mishnah*'s structure. If the categories are labeled *reshut* and *mitzvah*, one might think the label implies these activities are not subject to *shvut* at all. Conversely, calling something *mishum mitzvah* seems to say the opposite of why it is forbidden. *Rabbi Yitzchak* clarifies: *lav mibaya ka'amar* — the *mishnah* is employing a *lav mibaya* formulation. It goes without saying that activities which are pure *shvut* with no redeeming quality — *shvus gerideta* — are forbidden. The *mishnah*'s contribution is to teach that even *shvut d'reshut* — activities that carry a quasi-*mitzvah* component — are likewise forbidden; and even further, *afilu shvus d'mitzvah nami assur* — even activities that are genuine *mitzvot* but whose performance entails a *shvut* violation remain prohibited. The categories do not denote the presence or absence of *shvut*; they denote ascending degrees of *mitzvah* quality, all of which nonetheless fall before the *shvut* prohibition.

### Shabbat Yom Tov

The final line of this section states that all of these prohibitions apply on *Yom Tov* — and *kal va'chomer* on *Shabbat*. The sole distinction between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* in this entire domain is *ochel nefesh* — food preparation. *Kol elu b'Yom Tov amru*: all these were declared with reference to *Yom Tov*, and they apply with equal force on *Shabbat*.

## Shabbat Yom Tov Diff

### Lowering Fruit

The *Gemara* immediately raises a contradiction. If the *mishnah* claims there is no difference between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* except for *ochel nefesh*, what of the ruling in a previous *mishnah* that *mashilin derech arubah b'Yom Tov aval lo b'Shabbat* — one may lower fruit through a skylight on *Yom Tov* but not on *Shabbat*? This is a *d'rabbanan* difference that has nothing directly to do with food preparation. *Rashi* had noted in the preceding *daf* that the concern driving that *mishnah* was *hefsed* — preventing loss — not eating, since the fruit was not going to be eaten that day. Yet the *mishnah* explicitly permits lowering it on *Yom Tov* and not on *Shabbat*. We have before us, then, a demonstrable rabbinic distinction between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* that does not fall under the *ochel nefesh* rubric.

### Abuye Resolution

*Rav Yosef* proposes resolving the contradiction by attributing the two *mishnayot* to different *Tannaim*: the ruling about the skylight follows *Rabbi Yehoshua*, while the ruling that there are no distinctions except *ochel nefesh* follows *Rabbi Eliezer*. A *beraita* records their dispute in the context of a mother animal and its calf that fell into a pit on *Yom Tov* — one cannot slaughter both on the same day due to the prohibition of *oto v'et beno*. *Rabbi Eliezer* rules that one may raise the first animal in order to slaughter it, and must leave the second in the pit with food provided below. *Rabbi Yehoshua* rules that one may raise the first, declare intent to slaughter it, and then change one's mind — raising the second and choosing which to slaughter. *Rabbi Yehoshua* thus permits a kind of creative flexibility to prevent loss, while *Rabbi Eliezer* allows only the minimum.

*Abaye* challenges this identification. Perhaps *Rabbi Eliezer* was strict in the animal case only because there was an alternative — one could lower food to the animal in the pit. But in the skylight case, where there is no alternative means of protection, *Rabbi Eliezer* might permit lowering the fruit. Conversely, *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s leniency in the animal case rested on the trick of declaring intent and retracting it — *de'efshar la'arumey*, one could make it appear as though each act was for the purpose of eating. In the skylight case, no such trick is available — it is evident that one is not going to eat a large quantity of drying fruit. So *Rabbi Yehoshua* might well be strict there. *Abaye*'s conclusion is that neither *mishnah* can be straightforwardly attributed to either *Tanna*, and the contradiction remains unresolved on this approach.

### Beit Shammai Hillel

*Rav Papa* offers a different resolution: the two *mishnayot* reflect the dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*. A *mishnah* records that *Beit Shammai* prohibits carrying a minor, a *lulav*, or a *Sefer Torah* into the public domain on *Yom Tov*, while *Beit Hillel* permits it. *Beit Shammai* maintained extensive restrictions on carrying on *Yom Tov*; *Beit Hillel* did not. On this reading, the *mishnah* permitting lowering fruit through the skylight follows *Beit Hillel*, who is lenient with respect to such movement, while the *mishnah* asserting no differences aside from *ochel nefesh* follows *Beit Shammai*, who restricts all non-food movement to mirror *Shabbat*.

At this point *Tosafot* raises a difficulty: the *mishnah*'s claim of no difference is explicitly about *d'rabbanan* distinctions. At the biblical level, obviously *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* differ — carrying and kindling fire are permitted on *Yom Tov* — but these permissions derive from or connect to *ochel nefesh*. The question, then, is whether there are any purely *d'rabbanan* differences, and our previous *mishnah* appeared to show that there are.

### Muktza Carrying

The *Gemara* then addresses how the *Beit Shammai* / *Beit Hillel* dispute on carrying connects to the question of lowering fruit. One might say *Beit Shammai* was strict about carrying but not about other forms of movement — after all, lowering fruit through a skylight might all occur within a *reshut hayachid*, without any carrying issue. The *Gemara* responds: *atu tiltul lav tsorech hotza'ah hu* — movement is the first step toward carrying. Because *Beit Shammai* was strict about *hotza'ah*, they were also strict about *tiltul* — general movement — lest it lead to carrying.

Many *rishonim* understand *tiltul* here as a reference to *muktza*: one of the functions of the *muktza* prohibition is to instill a general caution around the handling of objects, thereby reducing the risk of inadvertent *hotza'ah*. *Beit Shammai*'s greater stringency regarding carrying on *Yom Tov* generated corresponding stringency regarding *muktza*, while *Beit Hillel*'s greater permissiveness regarding carrying generated less restrictive *muktza* rules.

It should be noted, however, that the previous *mishnah* about the skylight was assumed not to involve *muktza* at all. *Rashi*'s entire discussion there centered on *tircha* — exertion — not on *muktza*. If the issue is *muktza*, the conceptual framework shifts. What must be said is that even in a case where *muktza* is not the concern, *Beit Shammai* would be stricter about *schlepping* in general — *tiltul* meaning simply the moving of objects — as a precautionary measure against *hotza'ah*.

### Unresolved Gaps

This resolution, while coherent on its own terms, leaves substantial gaps. As noted, yesterday's *daf* included an extended analysis that openly acknowledged multiple *d'rabbanan* differences between *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* entirely unrelated to *ochel nefesh* — for instance, the question of whether *Yom Tov* is treated more stringently than *Shabbat* because of *asi lezulzulei beh*, or whether it is treated more leniently. That passage assumed a whole system of differentiated rabbinic restrictions without limiting the differences to a single skylight case. The *Gemara* here addresses only one *mishnah* and declares it a *Beit Shammai* / *Beit Hillel* dispute — but many further differences remain unaddressed. The solution is incomplete, and a fully satisfying resolution of the tension between these two *mishnayot* is not reached.

One final observation: the *Gemara*'s insistence on framing *tiltul* as the first step toward *hotza'ah* is itself another instance of the *Bavli*'s preference for causal, *gezeirah*-style reasoning. It would have been equally natural to say that extensive *schlepping* is simply *mechazei uvdin d'chol* — it looks like weekday activity — without tethering it to the prohibition of *hotza'ah*. The *Gemara* declines that framing.

## Techumim Intro

### Behemah Keilim

A new *mishnah* introduces a concept that will dominate the remainder of the tractate: *ha-behemah ve-ha-keilim k'raglei ha-baalim* — animals and vessels follow the feet of their owner. This concerns the laws of *techumim*: the prohibition against traveling more than 2,000 *amot* from the edge of one's city on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*, unless one has placed an *eiruv techumim* to extend one's range in a particular direction.

The *mishnah* teaches that this restriction applies not only to one's own movement but to one's possessions. If another person wishes to use your vessels on *Yom Tov*, he may not take them to a location that falls within his *techum* but outside of yours. They are bound by your *techum*, not his. This raises a fascinating question — not addressed explicitly in the *Gemara* here, though it relates to the broader discussion of *shvitat keilim* — concerning objects located far from their owner. If one is abroad for *Yom Tov* and grants permission to someone at home to use one's property, it is not clear how the laws of *techum* apply, since the objects are already located entirely outside of the owner's *techum*. The answer to that case would require further investigation.

### Shepherd Animal

The *mishnah* continues: *ha-moser behemah livno o l'ro'eh* — if one hands over an animal to one's son or to a shepherd, and that shepherd's *eiruv* extends in a direction opposite to the owner's — the animal remains bound by the owner's *techum*. Even in the care of the shepherd, the animal follows the original owner's feet.

### Shared Estate

The *mishnah* then addresses the case of brothers who share an undivided estate. Where particular vessels have been designated for one brother's use — *keilim ha-myuchadim l'echad min ha-achim she-ba-bayit* — even though legal ownership remains undivided, those vessels follow the *techum* of the brother who uses them. Vessels that are not specifically designated for any one brother — *she-ein meyuchadim* — are bound to the place accessible to all, meaning they may be taken only within the intersection of all the brothers' *techumim*.

This can be visualized as follows. Suppose two brothers have made their *eiruvei techumim* in opposite directions. Each can travel up to 4,000 *amot* in one direction but may not step even a single *amah* in the opposite direction. Shared vessels may be taken only within the area accessible to both — the overlap between their respective ranges. If those ranges do not overlap at all, the shared vessels cannot be moved.

### Borrowing Timing

The *mishnah* addresses a further nuance: *hashoel kli mechavero me'erev Yom Tov* — if one borrows a vessel before *Yom Tov*, the vessel follows the borrower's *techum*, since when *Yom Tov* began the item was under the borrower's control. *BeYom Tov* — if the borrowing occurred on *Yom Tov* itself — the vessel follows the lender's *techum*, since at the onset of *Yom Tov* the item was still in the lender's domain. The *Gemara* therefore raises the interesting question of when the borrower's control is established: at the moment of physical possession, or at the moment permission is granted? The flexibility seen throughout this *mishnah* in defining "ownership" for purposes of *techum* suggests that the answer may not require formal physical *kinyan* — perhaps a grant of permission before *Yom Tov* suffices.

### Dough Techum

The *mishnah* extends the same principle to a woman who borrows spices, water, or salt from her neighbor to incorporate into her dough: *harei elu keraglei sheteihen* — the dough follows the *techum* of both women, since it contains components that, when *Yom Tov* began, were under the neighbor's ownership and therefore subject to the neighbor's *techum*. Even though the neighbor no longer has a claim to those specific particles in the finished dough — she will be repaid with equivalent spices after *Yom Tov* — the *techum* designation follows the status at the onset of *Yom Tov*. The dough therefore may be taken only to places accessible to both women simultaneously.

### Water Exemption

*Rabbi Yehudah* exempts water from this rule: *poter bamayim mipnei she-ein bahem mamash* — water is excluded because it is insubstantial. This requires some unpacking. Water is hardly trivial — it is essential for life, and so much of *Bereishit* revolves around wells and water sources. But the point is not that water lacks importance; it is that water loses its perceptible identity when incorporated into dough. Spices and salt can be tasted or, in some forms, seen. Water cannot be sensed in the finished product — its presence becomes invisible. Once a substance loses its perceptible identity within a mixture, *Rabbi Yehudah* rules that it no longer carries its *techum* restriction into the composite product.

## Rabbi Dosa

### Purchase Possession

The *Gemara* opens by noting that our *mishnah* does not accord with *Rabbi Dosa*. A *beraita* teaches: *Rabbi Dosa* (and some say *Abba Shaul*) holds that *halokeach behemah mechavero me'erev Yom Tov* — one who purchases an animal before *Yom Tov*, even though the animal was not actually transferred until *Yom Tov* itself, the animal follows the purchaser's *techum*. Formal legal possession — which in *halacha* typically requires a physical act of *kinyan* — has not yet been accomplished, yet the animal is treated as though it already belongs to the purchaser, because the transaction was concluded on *erev Yom Tov*.

This teaches something significant about the governing concept here. *Techum* restrictions are not triggered by strict legal ownership but by a more functional notion of under whose control or domain an object falls. One who has paid and agreed to a transaction but not yet physically acquired the object is already, for *techum* purposes, treated as the owner.

### Shepherd Transfer

The *beraita* continues: *vehamoseir behema lero'eh* — one who gives an animal to a shepherd *on Yom Tov* itself — the animal nonetheless follows the shepherd's *techum*. This directly contradicts our *mishnah*, which states that an animal given to a shepherd follows the owner's *techum*. The *mishnah* is therefore *dela keRabbi Dosa* — it does not accord with this view.

### Shepherd Choice

The *Gemara* reconciles the apparent contradiction: *kan bero'eh echad kan beshnei ro'im* — the difference is whether there is one shepherd in town or two. If there is only one shepherd, then even before the owner formally hands over the animal on *Yom Tov*, it was already understood — indeed, self-evident — that this animal would be entrusted to that shepherd. The assignment was pre-determined, and therefore when *Yom Tov* began, the animal was already effectively under the shepherd's domain. No formal act of transfer was needed, because there was no choice to be made.

If there are two shepherds, however, the recipient is not self-evident. A choice must be made, and that choice cannot retroactively define the status as of *Yom Tov*'s onset. In that case, the animal must have been formally transferred before *Yom Tov* in order for the shepherd's *techum* to govern it.

The *Gemara* notes that this reading is supported by the *mishnah* itself: *detani livno o lero'eh* — the *mishnah* presents a case of two potential recipients, one's son or the shepherd. Where there is a choice between two people, the transfer must be deliberate and explicit. This is consistent with the *Gemara*'s reading: the *mishnah* addresses the two-recipient scenario, while *Rabbi Dosa*'s ruling addresses the single-shepherd scenario. The *diyyuk* from the *mishnah* reinforces the reconciliation.

### Rav Yochanan Psak

*Rabbah bar bar Chana* cites *Rabbi Yochanan*: the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Dosa* — that in a case with a single predetermined shepherd, the animal follows the shepherd's *techum* even when formally transferred on *Yom Tov*. The *Gemara* then asks: but *Rabbi Yochanan* himself holds that the *halacha* follows the *stam mishnah* — the anonymously stated *mishnah* — and our *mishnah* appears to contradict *Rabbi Dosa*. The *Gemara* responds: the reconciliation already established — one shepherd versus two — resolves the tension. Our *mishnah* deals with two options; *Rabbi Dosa* deals with one. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling follows *Rabbi Dosa* for the single-shepherd case, and this is fully consistent with the *stam mishnah*, which addresses a different scenario.

## Two Borrowers

### Opposing Eiruv

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* concerning two people who borrowed a single shirt together as partners: one wished to wear it in the morning on *Yom Tov* to go to the *beit midrash*, the other wished to wear it in the evening of *Yom Tov* to go to a festive gathering. Crucially, the two borrowers placed their *eiruvei techumim* in exactly opposite directions. One placed his *eiruv* in the north, giving him access 4,000 *amot* to the north but no access whatsoever to the south. The other placed his *eiruv* in the south, giving him access to the south and none to the north. Their respective *techumim* do not overlap at all.

### Intersection Rule

The rule established by the *mishnah* and operative here is that jointly owned or borrowed objects may be taken only within the intersection of all relevant owners' *techumim*. Each co-owner's restriction applies to the object, and the object is bound by whichever restriction is more limiting in any given direction.

### Day Night

One might think that since the two borrowers intended to use the shirt at different times of day — one in the morning, one in the evening — the shirt could be treated as effectively belonging to each one during his respective period. This reasoning is rejected. *Yom Tov* is a single halakhic unit; the *techum* status of an object is fixed at the onset of *Yom Tov* and cannot be subdivided by time of day. There is no mechanism for dividing *Yom Tov* into morning and evening for the purpose of *techum* assignment.

### Zero Overlap

Since the two owners' *techumim* are in opposite directions with zero overlap, the intersection of their domains is zero. The conclusion is stark: *lo yezizenu mi-mkomo* — the shirt cannot be moved at all. It is fixed in its place for the entirety of *Yom Tov*. One cannot hand it off at the boundary either, since the moment it crosses from one *techum* into territory accessible only to the other, it has been moved beyond the first owner's permitted range.

## Partnership Property

### Rav Barrel

The *Gemara* presents a dispute: two people purchased a barrel of wine and an animal together as partners, with their *techumim* pointing in opposite directions. *Rav* rules that the barrel is permitted — *chavit muteret*. Each partner may take his half of the wine within his own *techum*, without being constrained by the other's direction. The animal, however, is forbidden — *behema asura* — and remains bound by the overlap of both owners' *techumim*.

### Shmuel Both

*Shmuel* disagrees: *chavit nami asura* — even the barrel is forbidden and must remain within the overlapping zone. On *Shmuel*'s view, when *Yom Tov* began the entire barrel was jointly owned, and the fact that it was subsequently divided does not alter the status fixed at the onset of *Yom Tov*.

### Bereira Theory

The *Gemara* analyzes *Rav*'s position. If *Rav* holds *yesh bereira* — that retroactive clarification is halakhically operative — then when the barrel is divided and each partner takes his half, we say retroactively that each partner always owned precisely that half. At the onset of *Yom Tov*, there was no joint ownership in the relevant sense — each half belonged to its eventual recipient from the beginning, definitionally. On this view, each partner may take his half wherever his *techum* allows, unconstrained by the other.

But if *Rav* holds *yesh bereira*, why is the animal different? And if *Rav* holds *ein bereira*, why is the barrel permitted? The apparent inconsistency demands explanation.

### Animal Nutrients

The *Gemara* resolves this by stating: *le-olam kasavar yesh bereira* — *Rav* does indeed hold *yesh bereira*, and this is why the barrel is permitted. The animal is different for a distinct biological reason: *dekaymi tchumei mehadadi* — the two halves of the living animal are mutually sustaining. *Tosafot* explains: even if we say retroactively that the front half always belonged to one partner and the back half always belonged to the other, the front half was, while the animal was alive at the onset of *Yom Tov*, drawing sustenance from the back half — blood, nutrients, oxygen — and vice versa. Each half contained within itself something belonging to the other owner. The situation therefore resembles the woman's dough that contained borrowed spices: even though the dough belongs fully to her, it contains components whose *techum* points in a different direction. Similarly, even if *bereira* establishes that each half of the animal "always" belonged to its eventual owner, each half physically contained something of the other's domain at the onset of *Yom Tov*. The barrel of wine presents no such difficulty: individual droplets do not organically sustain one another, and if *bereira* identifies which droplets belonged to whom, those droplets never derived anything from the other's share.

### Muktza Challenge

*Rav Kahana* and *Rav Assi* challenge *Rav* with an analogy: *le'issur muktza lo chasht ve'le'issur tchumin chasht*? If *Rav* is not concerned about a *muktza* problem with the animal, why is he concerned about a *techum* problem? The analogy runs as follows: if one is worried that the animal's halves are intermingled and that each half derives from the other, then by the same logic, any microscopic amount of meat or weight gained by the animal between the onset of *Yom Tov* and its slaughter ought to be *nolad* and *muktza* — newly created matter that was not in existence at the onset of *Yom Tov*. Yet clearly no one applies *muktza* to the imperceptible biological changes occurring within a living animal. Why then should the same microscopic biological interdependence generate a *techum* restriction? *Tosafot* acknowledges that the analogy is not entirely persuasive, but it is what the *Gemara* presents. *Rav* fell silent — *shtik Rav* — without offering a response.

### Rav Ushaya

The *Gemara* then reports the ultimate practical ruling: *Rav Oshaya amar yesh bereira* — there is *bereira*, and therefore both the barrel and the animal are permitted. Each partner may take his portion wherever his *techum* allows, since *bereira* retroactively establishes that each portion was always his. *Rabbi Yochanan amar ein bereira* — there is no *bereira*, and therefore both the barrel and the animal are forbidden and limited to the intersection of the two *techumim*.

## Corpse Tumah

### Multiple Exits

The *Gemara* now raises a challenge to *Rav Oshaya*'s position from an apparently contradictory ruling. A *mishnah* discusses the case of a corpse in a house with multiple exits. The concern is which exits become *tamei*: ordinarily, all exits through which the corpse might be removed are presumed to convey *tum'at ohel*. If one exit is opened and thereby identified as the definitive exit through which the corpse will be removed, that exit is *tamei* and the others are spared.

### Before Death

*Beit Shammai* rules: *vehu she'chishev alav ad she'lo yamus ha-meis* — the thought that identifies an exit as the intended removal point is effective only if it occurred before the person died. Once the person has died, all exits are simultaneously potential conduits for the corpse, and a subsequent thought cannot retroactively redefine one exit as the designated one.

### After Death

*Beit Hillel* is more lenient: *af mishe'yamus ha-meis* — even after the person has died, a mental designation of one exit can define it as *tamei* and spare the others. The identification need not be made before death; it can occur subsequently.

### Forward Only

*Rav Oshaya* comments on this *mishnah*: *le'taher ha-p'tachim mikan u'leha'ba* — the entire debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, and the mechanism of definition generally, operates only going forward from the moment of identification, *mikan u'leha'va*. *Lema'frea lo* — it does not work retroactively. That is, once one designates an exit, the other doorways are *tahor* going forward from that point. But the designation does not reach back in time to declare that those other exits were always *tahor*, retroactively protecting vessels that had been sitting in their thresholds before the designation was made.

### Contradiction

Here lies the apparent contradiction. *Rav Oshaya* has just been quoted as holding *yesh bereira* in the case of the barrel and the animal — meaning that dividing partnership property on *Yom Tov* retroactively establishes that each half was always the respective owner's. Yet in the *tumah* case, *Rav Oshaya* explicitly rules that the identification mechanism works only going forward and not retroactively — which is the essence of *ein bereira*. How can *Rav Oshaya* hold *yesh bereira* in one domain and *ein bereira* in another?

We leave this question open. The *daf* ends with this unresolved tension: *Rav Oshaya* in the context of *tumah* and doorways appears to hold *ein bereira* — no retroactive definition — while in the context of *techum* and partnership property he appears to hold *yesh bereira* — full retroactive clarification. Reconciling these positions will carry us into the next session.
