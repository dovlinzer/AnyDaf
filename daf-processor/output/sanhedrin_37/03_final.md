# Sanhedrin 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sanhedrin Layout

### Semicircular Seating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סַנְהֶדְרִין הָיְתָה כַּחֲצִי גּוֹרֶן עֲגוּלָּה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהוּ רוֹאִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. וּשְׁנֵי סוֹפְרֵי הַדַּיָּינִין עוֹמְדִים לִפְנֵיהֶם, אֶחָד מִיָּמִין וְאֶחָד מִשְּׂמֹאל, וְכוֹתְבִין דִּבְרֵי מְחַיְּיבִין וְדִבְרֵי מְזַכִּין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה, אֶחָד כּוֹתֵב דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּין, וְאֶחָד כּוֹתֵב דִּבְרֵי הַמְחַיְּיבִין, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי כּוֹתֵב דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּין וְדִבְרֵי הַמְחַיְּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A Sanhedrin** of twenty-three **was** arranged in the same layout **as half of a circular threshing floor, in order that** all the judges **will see one another** and the witnesses. **And two judges' scribes stand before** the court, **one on the right and one on the left, and they write the statements of those who find** the accused **liable and the statements of those who acquit** the accused. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** There were **three** scribes. **One writes** only **the statements of those who acquit** the accused, **one writes** only **the statements of those who find** him **liable, and the third writes** both **the statements of those who acquit** the accused **and the statements of those who find** him **liable,** so that if there is uncertainty concerning the precise wording that one of the scribes writes, it can be compared to the words of the third scribe.

The *mishnah* that opens our discussion actually begins at the bottom of daf 36, and it describes the physical arrangement of the *sanhedrin*: *sanhedrin hayetah kechatzi goren agulah kedei sheyihiyu ro'in zeh et zeh* — the *sanhedrin* was arranged like half of a circular threshing floor, so that all of the judges could see one another. The semicircular shape was not merely aesthetic; it served the functional purpose of ensuring mutual visibility among all who sat in judgment.

### Court Scribes

The *mishnah* continues: *ushenei sofrei hadayanin omdim lifneihem echad miyamin ve'echad mismol* — there were two court scribes standing before the judges, one on the right and one on the left. These scribes recorded the arguments on both sides: *vekotvin divrei mechaivin vedivrei mezakin* — they wrote the words of those arguing for conviction and the words of those arguing for acquittal.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and holds that there were in fact three scribes: one dedicated to recording the words of those arguing for acquittal, one dedicated to recording the words of those arguing for conviction, and a third who recorded both. Rashi explains that this third scribe served as a checker, providing a built-in safeguard against errors that might arise when a single scribe attempts to record both sides simultaneously — the workload being simply too great to do accurately.

### Three Rows of Scholars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁלֹשׁ שׁוּרוֹת שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים יוֹשְׁבִין לִפְנֵיהֶן, כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מַכִּיר אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ. הוּצְרְכוּ לִסְמוֹךְ – סוֹמְכִין מִן הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. אֶחָד מִן הַשְּׁנִיָּה בָּא לוֹ לָרִאשׁוֹנָה, אֶחָד מִן הַשְּׁלִישִׁית בָּא לוֹ לַשְּׁנִיָּה. בּוֹרְרִים לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד מִן הַקָּהָל וּמוֹשִׁיבִין אוֹתוֹ בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית. וְלֹא הָיָה יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן, אֶלָּא יוֹשֵׁב בְּמָקוֹם הָרָאוּי לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And three rows of Torah scholars sit before** the judges, and **each and every one** among those sitting **recognizes his place,** i.e., they are seated in accordance with their stature. When the court **must ordain** an additional judge, e.g., if a judge dies during the proceedings or in the case of a court without a decisive majority (see 40a), the court **ordains** the greatest Torah scholar **from the first** row. As a seat in the first row is now vacant, **one** Torah scholar **from the second** row **comes to the first** row, and **one** Torah scholar **from the third** row **comes to the second** row, and the court **selects another** Torah scholar **from among the assembled and they seat him in the third** row. **And** this Torah scholar who moves from the second row to the first row **would not sit in the place of the first** Torah scholar, who joined the court, **rather, he** would **sit in the place appropriate for him,** i.e., at the end of that row, in accordance with his stature.

Beyond the judges themselves and the scribes, the *mishnah* describes three rows of *talmidei chachamim* seated before the court: *veshalosh shurot shel talmidei chachamim yoshvin lifneihem kol echad ve'echad makir et mekomo* — three rows of scholars sit before them, and each individual knows his place.

Rashi clarifies that each of these three rows contained twenty-three scholars — the same number as the judges of the *sanhedrin ketanah* itself. The reason for maintaining this number, as Rashi explains, is rooted in the *halachic* mechanics of capital cases. In a capital case, a verdict cannot be rendered on the basis of a single-vote majority for conviction; at least two additional votes for conviction over acquittal are required. If the court of twenty-three becomes deadlocked in a configuration such as twelve for conviction and eleven for acquittal, they are unable to proceed. In such a case, they augment the court by bringing in additional judges from the first row of scholars seated before them.

### Adding Judges

The process of augmenting the court works as follows: *hutzrechu lismoch somchin min harishonah* — if they need to appoint additional judges, they draw from the first row. When someone from the first row is elevated to the bench, someone from the second row advances into the first, and someone from the third row advances into the second. And since one of the three rows now has a vacancy, *uvorerin lahem od echad min hakahal* — they select an additional individual from the general public watching the proceedings and seat him in the third row.

Importantly, *velo hayah yoshev bimkomo shel rishon ela yoshev bimkom haraui lo* — the newcomer does not take the specific seat vacated by the person who was elevated; rather, everyone shifts forward in an orderly fashion and the new person takes the position appropriate to him at the end of the row.

One might ask: why not simply maintain rows of twenty-three, twenty-three, and two, which would sum neatly to the maximum of seventy-one judges? Rashi addresses this: it would be disrespectful to seat only two people in what is meant to be a full row. The aesthetic and honorific consideration demands that all rows be equal in number. This admittedly creates the somewhat curious situation where most of the scholars seated in these rows — particularly those deeper in — will realistically never be called upon to serve as judges. Nevertheless, the rows must be maintained and filled.

## Biblical Derivation

### Shorerech as Navel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״שׇׁרְרֵךְ אַגַּן הַסַּהַר אַל יֶחְסַר הַמָּזֶג וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the Sanhedrin would sit in a semicircle. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina says: As the verse states: "Your navel is like a round goblet, let no mingled wine be wanting;** your belly is like a heap of wheat set about with lilies" (Song of Songs 7:3).

The *gemara* now asks: *mena hani milei* — what is the scriptural derivation for the semicircular arrangement? Rabbi Akha bar Chanina answers by citing a verse from *Shir Hashirim*: *shorerech aggan hasahar al yechsar hamazeg* — your navel is like a crescent basin; may its mixed wine never be lacking. The *gemara* proceeds to expound each term of this verse as a reference to the *sanhedrin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שׇׁרְרֵךְ״ – זוֹ סַנְהֶדְרִין. לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״שׇׁרְרֵךְ״? שֶׁהִיא יוֹשֶׁבֶת בְּטִיבּוּרוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם. ״אַגַּן״ – שֶׁהִיא מְגִינָּה עַל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. ״הַסַּהַר״ – שֶׁהִיא דּוֹמָה לַסַּהַר.
>
> **Translation:** This verse is interpreted as referring to the members of the Sanhedrin, who sit in a semicircle. **"Your navel"; this** is an allusion to the **Sanhedrin. And why is it called** by way of allusion **"your navel"?** It is **because it sits in the navel of the world,** in the Temple. **"Goblet [*aggan*]";** this teaches **that** the Sanhedrin **protects [*meginna*] the entire world** with its merit. **"Round [*hassahar*]";** this teaches **that** the Sanhedrin **is similar to the moon [*sahar*].** The court sits in a semicircle, like the shape of the moon.

*Shorerech* — your navel — *zo sanhedrin*: this refers to the *sanhedrin*. Why is the *sanhedrin* compared to the navel? *She-hi yoshevet be-tiburo shel olam* — because the *sanhedrin* sat in the navel of the world, a phrase associated with the Temple in Jerusalem, which other *midrashim* similarly describe as the world's center.

### Aggan & Hasahar

*Aggan* — basin — *she-hi megina al kol ha-olam kulo*: the word *aggan* is read as a reference to protection (*haganah*), because the *sanhedrin* protects the entire world. *Hasahar* — the crescent — *she-hi domah le-sahar*: this is the element most directly relevant to the *mishnah*, for it teaches that the *sanhedrin* is shaped like a crescent moon.

### Mazeg Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַל יֶחְסַר הַמָּזֶג״ – שֶׁאִם הוּצְרַךְ אֶחָד מֵהֶם לָצֵאת, רוֹאִין: אִם יֵשׁ עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה, כְּנֶגֶד סַנְהֶדְרִי קְטַנָּה – יוֹצֵא, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **"Let no mingled wine be wanting";** this compares the Sanhedrin to wine mixed with water, which typically involved mixing two parts water with one part wine. This teaches **that if one** member **of** the Great Sanhedrin **needed to leave,** they **see: If there are** still present in the Chamber of Hewn Stone **twenty-three** members, i.e., a third of the judges, **corresponding to** the number of **a lesser Sanhedrin, he may leave, but if not, he may not leave.**

The final clause of the verse — *al yechsar hamazeg*, may the mixing not be lacking — is interpreted as follows: *im hutsrach echad mehem latzet ro'in im yesh esrim u-shlosha keneged sanhedrei ketana yotze ve-im lav eino yotze*. If one of the judges must leave during the proceedings, they first check whether there are twenty-three individuals present in the crowd — corresponding to the number of a *sanhedrin ketanah*. Only if that minimum is present may the judge excuse himself; otherwise, he may not leave. The reference to *mazeg*, the mixing of wine, alludes to the maintenance of this quorum.

## Jewish Trustworthiness

### Wheat Mound

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בִּטְנְךָ עֲרֵמַת חִטִּים״ – מָה עֲרֵימַת חִטִּים הַכֹּל נֶהֱנִים מִמֶּנָּה, אַף סַנְהֶדְרִין הַכֹּל נֶהֱנִים מִטַּעֲמֵיהֶן. ״סוּגָה בַּשּׁוֹשַׁנִּים״ – שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ כְּסוּגָה שֶׁל שׁוֹשַׁנִּים לֹא יִפְרְצוּ בָּהֶן פְּרָצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"your belly is like a heap of wheat"** teaches that **just as** with regard to **a heap of wheat, all derive benefit from it, so too,** with regard to the **Sanhedrin, all derive benefit from their explanations** of the Torah. The phrase **"set about with lilies"** is said in praise of the Jewish people, **as they do not breach even a fence** made **of lilies,** since the Jewish people observe both Torah law as well as rabbinic ordinances and decrees.

The *gemara* continues to expound the verses of *Shir Hashirim* in connection with the *sanhedrin* and with the Jewish people more broadly. The verse *bitnech aremat chitim* — your stomach is like a mound of wheat — is interpreted: *mah aremat chitim ha-kol nehenin mimena af sanhedrin ha-kol nehenin mi-ta'amehem* — just as everyone benefits from a mound of wheat, so too everyone benefits from the reasoning and legal rulings of the *sanhedrin*.

### Rose Hedge

The verse *suga bashoshanim* — hedged with roses — is interpreted as a metaphor for the Jewish people's resistance to transgression: *she-afilu ke-suga shel shoshanim lo yifretzu bahen pratzot* — even a barrier as thin and penetrable as a hedge of roses, the Jewish people will not break through it.

### Niddah & Yichud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: אָמְרִיתוּ נִדָּה שְׁרֵי לְיַיחוֹדֵי בַּהֲדֵי גַּבְרָא. אֶפְשָׁר אֵשׁ בִּנְעוֹרֶת וְאֵינָהּ מְהַבְהֶבֶת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַתּוֹרָה הֵעִידָה עָלֵינוּ ״סוּגָה בַּשּׁוֹשַׁנִּים״, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ כְּסוּגָה בַּשּׁוֹשַׁנִּים לֹא יִפְרְצוּ בָּהֶן פְּרָצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** like an incident involving Rav Kahana, **as a certain heretic said to Rav Kahana: You say that** it **is permitted** for **a menstruating woman to seclude herself with a man,** i.e., her husband. Is it **possible** to set **fire to chips** of kindling **and not** have them **blaze** and burn? How can the couple be relied upon not to engage in sexual intercourse? Rav Kahana **said to him: The Torah testifies concerning us** that we are **"set about with lilies," as** the Jewish people **do not breach even a fence** made **of lilies.**

The *gemara* illustrates this principle with a remarkable exchange. A heretic approached Rav Kahana and challenged him: *amritu nida shari le-yichudi bahadei gavra* — you say that a *nidah* is permitted to be in *yichud* with her husband? *Efshar esh be-no'oret ve-eina mehabhevet* — is it possible that fire can be placed among flax and not singe it? Surely such proximity will inevitably lead to transgression.

Rav Kahana responded: *ha-torah he'idah aleinu suga bashoshanim* — the Torah has testified about us through this verse, that even a barrier as weak as a hedge of roses will not be breached. The Jewish people will not transgress even in circumstances that appear to invite it.

This exchange prompted a *halachic* observation by *Tosafot*, who notes a difficulty: if the prohibition of *yichud* is *de-oraita*, how can a verse from *Shir Hashirim* be invoked to override it? *Tosafot* resolves this by explaining that the *de-oraita* prohibition of *yichud* applies only in cases where the two individuals involved are categorically forbidden to one another — such as close relatives or other *arayot* — but not in the case of a husband and wife between whom *yichud* is not absolutely prohibited under Torah law in all circumstances.

### Pomegranate Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מֵהָכָא, ״כְּפֶלַח הָרִמּוֹן רַקָּתֵךְ״ – אֲפִילּוּ רֵיקָנִים שֶׁבָּךְ מְלֵאִים מִצְווֹת כְּרִמּוֹן. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: מֵהָכָא, ״וַיָּרַח אֶת רֵיחַ בְּגָדָיו״. אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״בְּגָדָיו״ אֶלָּא ״בּוֹגְדָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says** that the source to rely on them not to transgress is **from here: "Your temples [*rakkatekh*] are like a pomegranate split open"** (Song of Songs 6:7), which teaches that **even the empty** people **[*reikanim*] among you are** as **full of mitzvot as the pomegranate** is full of seeds. **Rabbi Zeira says** that the source is **from here:** The verse states concerning the occasion when Isaac blessed Jacob: **"And he smelled the smell of his garments,** and blessed him, and said: See, the smell of my son is as the smell of a field that the Lord has blessed" (Genesis 27:27). **Do not read "his garments [*begadav*]"; rather,** read: **His traitors [*bogedav*],** meaning that even traitors and sinners among the Jewish people have qualities "as the smell of a field that the Lord has blessed."

Resh Lakish offers an alternative source for the same principle. He cites the verse *ke-felach harimon rakatech* — like a wedge of pomegranate is your temple — and expounds: *afilu rikanim sheback male'im mitzvot karimon* — even those who are the most empty among you, the lowest of the low, are full of *mitzvos* like a pomegranate is full of seeds. This too serves as a basis for trusting in the essential spiritual integrity of every Jew.

### Fragrant Clothes

Rabbi Zeira derives the same idea from yet another source, citing the verse *vayarach et reiach begadav* — and he smelled the smell of his clothes — from the episode in which Yitzchak smells the garments of who he believes to be Eisav. Rabbi Zeira reads *begadav* not as "his clothes" but as *bogdav* — his traitors, or those who mislead. The point is striking: even when reading the verse as referring to deceivers, Yitzchak smells and finds them fragrant. The Torah makes explicit that the smell was pleasing — *kerei'ach sadeh asher beraco Hashem* — like the smell of a field that God has blessed. Even those who are in some sense dishonest or misleading carry within them something of genuine worth and beauty. The trust we extend to Jews even in compromised situations is grounded in this assurance.

### Rabbi Zeira & Thugs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ בִּרְיוֹנֵי דַּהֲווֹ בְּשִׁיבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, דַּהֲוָה מְקָרֵב לְהוּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנֶיהְדְּרוּ לְהוּ בִּתְיוּבְתָּא, וַהֲווֹ קָפְדִי רַבָּנַן. כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, אָמְרִי: עַד הָאִידָּנָא הֲוָה חֲרִיכָא קַטִּין שָׁקֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה בָּעֵי עֲלַן רַחֲמֵי, הַשְׁתָּא מַאן בָּעֵי עֲלַן רַחֲמֵי? הַרְהֲרוּ בְּלִבַּיְיהוּ וַעֲבַדוּ תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There were **certain hooligans [*biryonei*] who were** living **in the neighborhood of Rabbi Zeira. He brought them close,** i.e., treated them with friendship, **in order to cause them to repent** of their sins, **but the** other **Sages disapproved** of his actions. **When Rabbi Zeira died,** those hooligans **said: Until now, there was the short one with singed legs,** i.e., Rabbi Zeira, **who would pray for compassion for us. Who will pray for compassion for us now? They thought** about this **in their hearts and repented.** Ultimately, Rabbi Zeira's actions were proven correct, as they repented.

The *gemara* then recounts a story that illustrates this principle in practice. There were certain thugs (*biryonei*) in the neighborhood of Rabbi Zeira. Rather than avoiding them, *de-hava mekarev lehu* — he would associate with them, in the hope that his proximity would bring them to *teshuvah*. The *Rabbanan* were displeased with this conduct.

When Rabbi Zeira died, these same thugs said: *ad ha-idana hava haricha ketin shakei de-hava ba'ei alan rachamei hashta man ba'ei alan rachamei* — until now the singed small legs — their nickname for Rabbi Zeira, who according to Rashi had subjected his legs to heat as part of his ascetic practices, and who was known for his short, unimposing stature — would pray for us; who will pray for us now? *Hirhiru belibehu ve-avdu teshuvah* — they searched their hearts and did *teshuvah*. The connection to the theme of the section is clear: even the most apparently lowly and coarse among the Jewish people carry within them the capacity for genuine repentance and spiritual ascent.

## Judge Advancement

### Advancement Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלֹשׁ שׁוּרוֹת כּוּ׳. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כִּי נָיְידִי, כּוּלְּהוּ נָיְידִי. וְלֵימָא לְהוּ: עַד הָאִידָּנָא הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא בְּרֵישָׁא, הַשְׁתָּא מוֹתְבִיתוּ לִי בְּדַנְבֵי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ הָכִי, ״הֱוֵי זָנָב לָאֲרָיוֹת וְאַל תְּהִי רֹאשׁ לַשּׁוּעָלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that there are **three rows** of Torah scholars who sit before the court, and if one of the Torah scholars from the first row is elevated to a place on the court, the Torah scholar in the first position of the second row moves to the final position of the first row. **Abaye says: Learn from** the mishna that **when they move,** i.e., when the Torah scholars need to move as a result of one of them being elevated to the court, **they all move.** The Gemara inquires: **But let** the one being moved from the first position of the second row to the last position of the first row **say to** the court: **Until now I was sitting at the head** of the row, but **now you are seating me at the tail,** i.e., the end, of a row. **Abaye says** in explanation: That is not a valid claim, **as** the court **can say this to him: Be a tail to the lions and do not be a head to the foxes** (*Avot* 4:15), meaning that it is preferable to be the least among great people than the greatest among lesser people.

Returning to the three rows of scholars, *Abaye* derives a principle about how movement through the rows operates. When a scholar is elevated from the rows into the court, all others advance to fill the vacated positions, so that no empty space is left standing.

### Tail vs Head

This raises an apparent difficulty. Imagine a scholar who holds the first position in the second row — the most prominent seat in that row. When the court is augmented and everyone shifts forward, this individual is moved up into the second row of the first row, but he now occupies the last position there — the least prominent seat. He might reasonably object: *until now I was at the head; now you are placing me at the tail?*

Abaye responds with a well-known principle: *havei zanav la-arayot ve-al tehi rosh la-shu'alim* — be a tail among lions rather than a head among foxes. It is better to occupy the lowest position among a more distinguished group than the highest position among a lesser one. This resolves the objection: being last in the company of greater scholars is itself an elevation, not a demotion.

## Witness Warning

### Bringing Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מְאַיְּימִין אֶת הָעֵדִים עַל עֵידֵי נְפָשׁוֹת? הָיוּ מַכְנִיסִין אוֹתָן וּמְאַיְּימִין עֲלֵיהֶן: שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמְרוּ מֵאוֹמֶד וּמִשְּׁמוּעָה, עֵד מִפִּי עֵד וּמִפִּי אָדָם נֶאֱמָן, שֶׁמָּא אִי אַתֶּם יוֹדְעִים שֶׁסּוֹפֵנוּ לִבְדּוֹק אֶתְכֶם בִּדְרִישָׁה וּבַחֲקִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How does** the court **intimidate the witnesses in** giving **testimony for** cases of **capital law? They would bring** the witnesses **in and intimidate them** by saying to them: **Perhaps** what **you say** in your testimony is **based on conjecture, or** perhaps it is **based on a rumor,** perhaps it is **testimony** based on **hearsay,** e.g., you heard a witness testify to this in a different court, **or** perhaps it is **based on the statement of a trusted person. Perhaps you do not know that ultimately we examine you with inquiry and interrogation,** and if you are lying, your lie will be discovered.

The *mishnah* now turns to the procedure for warning witnesses in capital cases. *Al edei nefashot hayu machnisin otan u'me'aymin aleihem* — when dealing with witnesses in capital cases, they would bring them into the *sanhedrin* and impress upon them the gravity of their testimony.

### Direct Evidence

The witnesses are warned against several categories of invalid testimony: *shema tomru me'omed u'mishmua mipi ed v'ed mipi ed u'mipi adam ne'eman* — lest you base your testimony on an assumption, on something you heard, on what one witness told you another witness said, or on the word of someone you consider trustworthy. None of these suffice. Only direct personal observation constitutes valid testimony in a capital case. And the witnesses are reminded that the court will subject them to thorough *drisha u'vchakira* — the formal set of interrogatory questions designed to probe the precise circumstances of their claimed observation.

### Civil vs Capital

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֱווּ יוֹדְעִין, שֶׁלֹּא כְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת. דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – אָדָם נוֹתֵן מָמוֹן וּמִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ. דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – דָּמוֹ וְדַם זַרְעִיּוֹתָיו תְּלוּיִין בּוֹ עַד סוֹף הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The court tells them: **You should know that** cases of **capital law** are **not like** cases of **monetary law.** In cases of **monetary law, a person** who testifies falsely, causing money to be given to the wrong party, can **give** the **money** to the proper owner **and his** sin **is atoned** for. In cases of **capital law,** if one testifies falsely, **the blood of** the accused **and the blood of his offspring** that he did not merit to produce **are ascribed to** the witness's testimony **until eternity.**

The witnesses are further instructed: *havu yodim shelo k'dinei mamonot dinei nefashot* — know that capital cases are not like civil cases. In a civil case, *adam noten mamon u'mitkaper lo* — a person can give money and be atoned; the wrong can be undone materially. Moreover, even a witness who testified falsely in a civil case can make restitution. But in a capital case, *damo ve-dam zariyotav taluy bo ad sof ha-olam* — if someone is executed on the basis of your testimony, his blood and the blood of all of his potential descendants hang upon you forever.

### Cain & Abel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּקַיִן שֶׁהָרַג אֶת אָחִיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״דְּמֵי אָחִיךָ צֹעֲקִים״. אֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר ״דַּם אָחִיךָ״ אֶלָּא ״דְּמֵי אָחִיךָ״ – דָּמוֹ וְדַם זַרְעִיּוֹתָיו. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״דְּמֵי אָחִיךָ״ – שֶׁהָיָה דָּמוֹ מוּשְׁלָךְ עַל הָעֵצִים וְעַל הָאֲבָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The proof for this is **as we found with Cain, who killed his brother, as it is stated** concerning him: "The voice of **your brother's blood [*demei*] cries** out to Me from the ground" (Genesis 4:10). The verse **does not state: Your brother's blood [*dam*],** in the singular, **but** rather: **"Your brother's blood [*demei*],"** in the plural. This serves to teach that the loss of both **his** brother's **blood and the blood of his** brother's **offspring** are ascribed to Cain. The mishna notes: **Alternatively,** the phrase **"your brother's blood [*demei*],"** written in the plural, teaches **that his blood** was not gathered in one place but was **splattered on the trees and on the stones.**

The *mishnah* illustrates this with the story of Kayin: *sheken matzinu be-Kayin she-harag et achiv shene'emar dmei achicha tzo'akim* — as we see with Kayin who killed his brother, where the verse says "the bloods of your brother cry out." The deliberate use of the plural *dmei* — bloods — rather than the singular *dam* is significant. *Eino omer dam achicha ela dmei achicha* — it does not say "the blood of your brother" but "the bloods of your brother" — implying both his own blood and the blood of all his descendants who would never come into existence.

A *davar acher* adds: perhaps the plural refers to the fact that Hevel's blood was scattered across the trees and stones, sprayed in multiple places.

### Single Human Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ נִבְרָא אָדָם יְחִידִי, לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁכׇּל הַמְאַבֵּד נֶפֶשׁ אַחַת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ אִיבֵּד עוֹלָם מָלֵא. וְכׇל הַמְקַיֵּים נֶפֶשׁ אַחַת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ קִיֵּים עוֹלָם מָלֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The court tells them: **Therefore, Adam** the first man **was created alone, to teach you that** with regard to **anyone who destroys one soul from the Jewish people,** i.e., kills one Jew, **the verse ascribes him** blame **as if he destroyed an entire world,** as Adam was one person, from whom the population of an entire world came forth. **And** conversely, **anyone who sustains one soul from the Jewish people, the verse ascribes him** credit **as if he sustained an entire world.**

The *mishnah* then draws a profound theological lesson from the fact that all of humanity descends from a single ancestor, Adam HaRishon: *lefikach nivra adam yechidi* — therefore man was created singular. The lesson this conveys is that *kol ha-me'abed nefesh achat mi-Yisrael ma'aleh alav ha-katuv ke'ilu ibed olam malei* — anyone who destroys a single soul is treated by the Torah as though he has destroyed an entire world.

It is worth noting that the words *mi-Yisrael* — "from Israel" — do not appear in the earliest printings of the text, including the text as cited by the Rambam and in the old versions of Rashi. The original formulation appears to have applied universally to any human soul, without the specification of Jewish identity.

### Soul = World

Correspondingly, *ve-chol ha-mekayem nefesh achat mi-Yisrael ma'aleh alav ha-katuv ke'ilu kiyem olam malei* — anyone who sustains a single soul is treated by the Torah as though he has sustained an entire world. Rashi explains the practical implication of the statement *bishvili nivra ha-olam* — the world was created for my sake: *klomar chashuv ani ke-olam malei* — it means I consider myself to be like an entire world. This mindset carries with it a sense of cosmic responsibility: *lo etred et atzmi min ha-olam ba-aveira achat* — I will not cast myself out of the world through a single transgression.

### Creation Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי שְׁלוֹם הַבְּרִיּוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמַר אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״אַבָּא גָּדוֹל מֵאָבִיךָ״, וְשֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ הַמִּינִים אוֹמְרִים: ״הַרְבֵּה רְשׁוּיוֹת בַּשָּׁמַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cites another reason Adam the first man was created alone: **And** this was done **due to** the importance of maintaining **peace among people, so** that one **person will not say to another: My father,** i.e., progenitor, **is greater than your father. And** it was also **so that the heretics** who believe in multiple gods **will not say: There are many authorities in Heaven,** and each created a different person.

The *mishnah* enumerates additional reasons why God chose to create humanity through a single ancestor. *U-mipnei shalom ha-briyot shelo yomar adam le-chavero abba gadol mei-avicha* — for the sake of peace among human beings, so that no person could claim to his fellow that his lineage is superior, since all share the same first ancestor. *Ve-shelo yihyu ha-minim omrim harbeh rashuyot ba-shamayim* — so that heretics could not argue that multiple divine powers created different peoples, with each people's god responsible for their first ancestor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַגִּיד גְּדוּלָּתוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁאָדָם טוֹבֵעַ כַּמָּה מַטְבְּעוֹת בְּחוֹתָם אֶחָד – כּוּלָּן דּוֹמִין זֶה לָזֶה, וּמֶלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא טָבַע כׇּל אָדָם בְּחוֹתָמוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן, וְאֵין אֶחָד מֵהֶן דּוֹמֶה לַחֲבֵירוֹ. לְפִיכָךְ כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד חַיָּיב לוֹמַר: בִּשְׁבִילִי נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this serves **to tell of the greatness of the Holy One, Blessed be He, as** when **a person stamps several coins with one seal, they are all similar to each other. But the supreme King of kings, the Holy One, Blessed be He, stamped all people with the seal of Adam the first** man, as all of them are his offspring, **and not one of them is similar to another. Therefore,** since all humanity descends from one person, **each and every person is obligated to say: The world was created for me,** as one person can be the source of all humanity, and recognize the significance of his actions.

*U-lehagid gedulato shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — and to declare the greatness of God: whereas a human craftsman stamps many coins with the same mold and they all come out identical, God stamps every human being with the stamp of Adam HaRishon and yet no two are alike.

## Witness Warning (II)

### Personal Responsibility

The *mishnah* concludes: *lefikach kol echad ve'echad chayav lomar bishvili nivra ha-olam* — therefore each and every individual is obligated to say: the world was created for my sake. As Rashi makes clear, this is not an expression of arrogance but of moral seriousness — an awareness that one's own life is of infinite worth, and that the destruction of any life is therefore an act of cosmic devastation.

## Witness Concerns

### Fear of Involvement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמְרוּ: מָה לָנוּ וְלַצָּרָה הַזֹּאת? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וְהוּא עֵד אוֹ רָאָה אוֹ יָדָע אִם לוֹא יַגִּיד וְגוֹ׳״. וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמְרוּ: מָה לָנוּ לָחוּב בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל זֶה? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״בַּאֲבֹד רְשָׁעִים רִנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Why would we** want **this trouble?** Perhaps it would be better not to testify at all. **But** be aware, as **is it not already stated: "And he being a witness, whether he has seen or known, if he does not utter it,** then he shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 5:1)? It is a transgression not to testify when one can do so. **And perhaps you will say: Why would we** want **to be responsible for the blood of this** person? **But** be aware, as **is it not already stated: "When the wicked perish, there is song"** (Proverbs 11:10)?

After being told all of this, the witness might understandably recoil: *shema tomru ma lanu u-la-tzara ha-zot* — perhaps you will say, what do we want with all this trouble? The gravity of the situation — the weight of being responsible for a verdict that could result in someone's death — might cause a witness to retreat from testifying altogether.

### Rejoicing in Justice

The *mishnah* addresses this concern on two levels. First, there is a positive obligation: *halo kevar ne'emar ve-hu ed o ra'ah o yada im lo yagid* — the Torah has already stated that if one is a witness and has seen or knows something, he must come forward and testify. Second, the witness need not fear moral complicity in a justified execution: *halo kevar ne'emar be'avod resha'im rinah* — the Torah has already stated that when the wicked are destroyed, there is rejoicing. Participating in a just verdict against someone truly guilty is not a burden to be feared but an act of righteousness to be embraced.

## Circumstantial Evidence

### Assumption Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מֵאוֹמֶד? אוֹמֵר לָהֶן: שֶׁמָּא כָּךְ רְאִיתֶם? שֶׁרָץ אַחַר חֲבֵירוֹ לְחוּרְבָּה, וְרַצְתֶּם אַחֲרָיו, וּמְצָאתֶם סַיִיף בְּיָדוֹ וְדָמוֹ מְטַפְטֵף, וְהָרוּג מְפַרְפֵּר. אִם כָּךְ רְאִיתֶם – לֹא רְאִיתֶם כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **How does** the court describe testimony **based on conjecture?** The court **says to** the witnesses: **Perhaps you saw** this man about whom you are testifying **pursuing another into a ruin, and you pursued him and found a sword in his hand, dripping** with **blood, and the one** who was ultimately **killed** was **convulsing. If you saw** only **this,** it is as if **you saw nothing,** and you cannot testify to the murder.

The *gemara* now asks: *keitzad me'umad* — what exactly is an *umdana*, an assumption or circumstantial inference? The *baraita* explains with a concrete case: *omer lahem shema kach re'item sheratz echad achar chaveiro lechorba* — perhaps this is what you saw: one person running after another into a ruin. *Ve-ratztem acharav* — and you ran after him.

### Sword & Blood

*U-matzatem sa'if beyado u-damo metaptef ve-harug mefarfer* — and you found a sword in his hand, blood dripping from it, and a dead body nearby in its final convulsions. *Im kach re'item lo re'item klum* — if that is what you saw, you have seen nothing admissible. No matter how overwhelming the circumstantial picture, this does not constitute valid testimony in a capital case.

### Shimon ben Shatach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: אֶרְאֶה בְּנֶחָמָה אִם לֹא רָאִיתִי אֶחָד שֶׁרָץ אַחַר חֲבֵירוֹ לְחוּרְבָּה, וְרַצְתִּי אַחֲרָיו וְרָאִיתִי סַיִיף בְּיָדוֹ וְדָמוֹ מְטַפְטֵף וְהָרוּג מְפַרְפֵּר. וְאָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: רָשָׁע, מִי הֲרָגוֹ לָזֶה? אוֹ אֲנִי אוֹ אַתָּה. אֲבָל מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁאֵין דָּמְךָ מָסוּר בְּיָדִי, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם עֵדִים יוּמַת הַמֵּת״. הַיּוֹדֵעַ מַחְשָׁבוֹת יִפָּרַע מֵאוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ שֶׁהָרַג אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ. אָמְרוּ: לֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁבָּא נָחָשׁ וְהִכִּישׁוֹ וָמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Shataḥ said** as an oath: **I will** not **see the consolation** of Israel **if I did not** once **see one** person **pursue another into a ruin, and I pursued him and saw a sword in his hand, dripping** with **blood, and the one** who was ultimately **killed** was **convulsing. And I said to him: Wicked person, who has killed this man? Either you or I. But what can I do, since your blood is not given over to me, as the Torah states: "At the mouth of two witnesses,** or three witnesses, **shall he that is to die be put to death"** (Deuteronomy 17:6), and I did not witness you killing him. **The One Who knows** one's **thoughts shall punish this man who killed another.** The Sages **said: They did not move from there before a snake came and bit the** murderer, **and he died.**

The *baraita* then cites a first-person account from Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach. He attests — using the formula *er'eh be-nechama im lo ra'iti*, an oath of uncertain precise meaning — that he himself witnessed exactly this scenario: one man running into a ruin, the rabbi running after him, finding a sword in hand, blood dripping, and a dead body convulsing. He confronted the man directly: *amarti lo rasha mi harago lazeh o ani o ata* — wicked one! Who killed this person? Either I did or you did, and obviously it was not I. Yet, *aval ma e'eseh she-ein damcha masur beyadi* — what can I do? Your blood is not in my hands. *She-harei amra torah al pi shnayim edim yumat ha-met* — for the Torah has said: it is on the testimony of two witnesses that the death sentence is carried out.

This passage is somewhat puzzling, as the rishonic commentators note: the *mishnah*'s case concerns *umdana* — the requirement of direct observation versus circumstantial inference — while Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach's formulation invokes the requirement of two witnesses, a distinct principle. *Tosafot* addresses this by suggesting that Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach was not alone — there were other observers present — and that he is in fact deriving both the requirement of direct sight and the requirement of two witnesses from the same verse. The *gemara* acknowledges that this is somewhat strained (*ra'uh*), reflecting the genuine difficulty in the way the sources align.

### Divine Punishment

Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach concludes his account: *ha-yodea machshavot yipara me'oto ish she-harag et chaveiro* — may the One who knows all thoughts punish this man who killed his fellow. *Amru lo zazu misham ad sheba nachash ve-hikisho* — they said: they had not even moved from that place before a snake came and bit him, and he died. Divine justice was carried out where human justice could not reach.

## Post-Destruction Judgment

### Four Death Penalties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאִי בַּר נָחָשׁ הוּא? וְהָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, וְכֵן תָּנֵי דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁבָּטְלָה סַנְהֶדְרִי, אַרְבַּע מִיתוֹת לֹא בָּטְלוּ. לֹא בָּטְלוּ? וְהָא בָּטְלוּ! אֶלָּא, דִּין אַרְבַּע מִיתוֹת לֹא בָּטְלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this account: **But was this** murderer **fit** to die by being bitten **by a snake? But doesn't Rav Yosef say, and so the school of Ḥizkiyya** also **taught: From the day that the Temple was destroyed, although the Sanhedrin ceased** to be extant, the **four** types of court-imposed **capital punishment have not ceased.** The Gemara asks: **Have they** really **not ceased? But they have ceased,** as court-imposed capital punishment is no longer given. **Rather,** the intention is that **the *halakha* of** the **four** types of court-imposed **capital punishment has not ceased** to be applicable.

This brings the *gemara* to a remarkable principle stated by Rav Yosef and corroborated in the school of Chizkiyah: *miyom she-charav Beit HaMikdash af al pi she-batla Sanhedrin arba mitot lo batlu* — from the day the Temple was destroyed, even though the *sanhedrin* was nullified, the four death penalties were not annulled.

### Sanhedrin Cessation

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *lo batlu? ve-ha batlu!* — they were not annulled? But they clearly were nullified! The response is that the *din* — the legal principle and its implementation — was not annulled; it is simply that God now carries out these sentences through natural means rather than through a human court.

*Tosafot* raises a further difficulty: it is known that the *sanhedrin* ceased to adjudicate capital cases forty years *before* the destruction of the Temple. Why then does the *gemara* speak specifically of the day the Temple was destroyed rather than the earlier date?

### Special Cases

*Tosafot* explains, citing the passage in *Perek Hayu Bodkin*, that even after the *sanhedrin* went into a form of exile and ceased its regular functioning, it would still reconvene *letzorech sha'ah* — for special cases requiring it — and would return to the *Lishkat HaGazit* in the Temple precinct. *Tosafot* mentions in this connection a case that appears in uncensored versions of the text as *hahu uvda de-Yeshu* — the case of Jesus — which was subsequently removed by censors, likely to avoid provoking Christian hostility. Whether this reflects an independent Jewish tradition of such a trial or represents a textual intrusion from external sources is itself a question worth noting.

### Natural Death Means

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב סְקִילָה – אוֹ נוֹפֵל מִן הַגָּג, אוֹ חַיָּה דּוֹרַסְתּוֹ. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב שְׂרֵיפָה – אוֹ נוֹפֵל בִּדְלֵיקָה, אוֹ נָחָשׁ מַכִּישׁוֹ. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב הֲרִיגָה – אוֹ נִמְסָר לַמַּלְכוּת, אוֹ לִיסְטִין בָּאִין עָלָיו. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב חֶנֶק – אוֹ טוֹבֵעַ בַּנָּהָר, אוֹ מֵת בִּסְרוֹנְכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: How so? For **one who** would **be liable** to be executed by **stoning, either** he **falls from a roof or an animal mauls him** and breaks his bones. This death is similar to death by stoning, in which the one liable to be executed is pushed from a platform and his bones break from the impact of the fall. For **one who** would **be liable** to be executed by **burning, either** he **falls into a fire** and is burned **or a snake bites him,** as a snakebite causes a burning sensation. For **one who** would **be liable to** be executed by **slaying** through decapitation by the sword, **either** he **is turned over to the authorities** and they execute him with a sword, **or robbers come upon him** and murder him. **One who** would **be liable** to be executed by **strangling either drowns in a river** and is choked by the water **or dies of diphtheria [*bisronekhi*],** which causes his breathing to become constricted. According to this, a murderer, whose verdict in court would be death by slaying, should not be bitten by a snake.

The *gemara* then enumerates how each of the four death penalties continues to be administered through natural agency. One deserving death by stoning (*sekilah*) will either fall from a roof or be killed by a wild animal. One deserving death by burning (*sereifah*) will fall into a fire or be bitten by a snake. One deserving death by sword (*harigah*) will be handed over to the non-Jewish authorities or will be killed by bandits. One deserving strangulation (*chenek*) will drown in a river or die of *serunchi*, some form of disease.

### Multiple Penalties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָהוּא חֵטְא אַחֲרִיתִי הֲוָה בֵּיהּ, דְּאָמַר מָר: מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב שְׁתֵּי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין – נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in explanation: **That** murderer **had another sin** for which he deserved execution by burning, and **as the Master says: One who is found liable** by the court **to** receive **two** types of **court-imposed capital punishment is sentenced to the harsher** of the two, and burning is considered a harsher death than slaying (see 50a).

This raises a question about the snake in Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach's case. If the man was a murderer, his punishment should have been *harigah* — death by sword. Why then was he killed by snakebite, which corresponds to *sereifah*? *Amrei hahu chata acharini havah beih* — the answer is that he had another sin as well, one that carried the penalty of *sereifah*. *De-amar mar mi she-nitchayev shtei mitot beit din nidon ba-chamurah* — as the principle states, one who is liable for two different death penalties is judged by the more severe of the two. Since *sereifah* is considered more severe than *harigah*, it was the penalty of *sereifah* that was visited upon him through the snakebite.

## Evidence Standards

### Capital Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאוֹמֶד וְכוּ׳. בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת הוּא דְּלָא אָמְדִינַן, הָא בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת אָמְדִינַן. כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי אַחָא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אַחָא אוֹמֵר: גָּמָל הָאוֹחֵר בֵּין הַגְּמַלִּים וְנִמְצָא גָּמָל הָרוּג בְּצִידּוֹ – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁזֶּה הֲרָגוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that in cases of capital law the court warns the witnesses not to testify **based on conjecture.** The Gemara comments: One can infer that it is only **in** cases of **capital law that we do not** rule based on **conjecture, but in** cases of **monetary law, we do** rule based on **conjecture. In accordance with whose** opinion is the mishna taught? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Aḥa. As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Kamma* 3:6) that **Rabbi Aḥa says:** If there was **a rutting** male **camel** that was rampaging **among** other **camels, and** then **a camel was found killed at its side, it is evident that this** rampaging camel **killed it,** and the owner must pay for the damage caused. The *baraita* indicates that Rabbi Aḥa rules that cases of monetary law are decided based on conjecture.

The *gemara* now returns to analyze the *mishnah*'s broader evidentiary framework. The *mishnah* explicitly states that *umdana* is invalid in capital cases, which implies by inference that *umdana* is valid in civil cases.

### Camel Example

The *gemara* identifies this as aligning with the view of Rabbi Akha: *tanya Rabbi Acha omer gamal ha'ochez bein ha-gamalim ve-nimtza gamal harug be-tzido ve-yadu'a she-zeh harago* — Rabbi Akha taught that if there is a camel — described by a term (*ha'ochez*) that Rashi explains with some uncertainty, perhaps meaning one in heat or one that is agitated — in the midst of other camels, and a dead camel is found alongside it, it is known that this camel killed the other, and the owner bears civil liability accordingly. This is a straightforward application of *umdana* to civil cases.

### Evidence Consistency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, עֵד מִפִּי עֵד דְּקָתָנֵי בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת הוּא דְּלָא אָמְרִינַן, הָא בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת אָמְרִינַן? וְהָתְנַן: אִם אָמַר ״הוּא אָמַר לִי שֶׁאֲנִי חַיָּיב לוֹ״, ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי אָמַר לִי שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב לוֹ״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״בְּפָנֵינוּ הוֹדָה לוֹ שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב לוֹ מָאתַיִם זוּז״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to your reasoning,** with regard to **that** which the mishna **teaches,** that the court warns the witnesses not to provide **testimony** based on **hearsay,** should one infer that it is **in** cases of **capital law that we do not say** that testimony based on hearsay is allowed, **but in** cases of **monetary law, we do say** that testimony based on hearsay is allowed? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (29a): **If** the witness **said:** The defendant **said to me:** It is true **that I owe** the plaintiff, or if he says: **So-and-so said to me that** the defendant **owes** the plaintiff, the witness **has said nothing,** i.e., his testimony is disregarded. These two statements by witnesses are examples of testimony based on hearsay, yet they are not valid in cases of monetary law. A witness's testimony is not valid testimony **unless he says,** for example: The defendant **admitted in our presence to** the plaintiff **that he owes him two hundred dinars,** as by admitting the debt in the presence of witnesses he rendered himself liable to pay the amount that he mentioned.

A challenge is raised: if the logic of the *mishnah* is that whatever is invalid in capital cases is thereby valid in civil cases, this would imply that *ed mipi ed* — secondhand testimony — which the *mishnah* also lists as invalid in capital cases, should similarly be valid in civil cases. But this is clearly untenable.

### Secondhand Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, אַף עַל גַּב דִּפְסִילִי בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת. הָכָא נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דִּפְסִילִי בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Evidently, although** testimony based on hearsay **is invalid in** cases of **monetary law, we tell** the witnesses to be aware of this **in capital law. Here, too,** with regard to testimony based on conjecture, one can say that **although** testimony based on conjecture **is invalid in** cases of **monetary law, we tell** the witnesses to be aware of this **in** cases of **capital law.**

The *gemara* resolves this by rejecting the premise: *ed mipi ed* is invalid in *both* civil and capital cases. The *mishnah* cites it in the context of capital cases for completeness, not to imply that it is permissible in civil cases. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* from elsewhere: if a person claims that his fellow told him he owes money to a third party, that testimony is worth nothing — the obligation must be admitted directly before witnesses with a specific acknowledgment of the sum owed. This confirms that secondhand testimony is disqualified across the board. Consequently, one cannot derive from the *mishnah*'s treatment of *umdana* in capital cases a blanket rule that everything listed there is permissible in civil cases; each category must be analyzed independently.

## Bloods Plural

### Kayin's Strikes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֱווּ יוֹדְעִים כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָשָׂה קַיִן בְּהֶבֶל אָחִיו חַבּוּרוֹת חַבּוּרוֹת, פְּצִיעוֹת פְּצִיעוֹת, שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ מֵהֵיכָן נְשָׁמָה יוֹצְאָה, עַד שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְצַוָּארוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the court would say: **You should know** that cases of capital law are not like cases of monetary law, and would reference the murder of Abel by Cain. **Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says:** By employing the plural term for blood, "The voice of your brother's blood [*demei*] cries out to Me from the ground" (Genesis 4:10), the verse **teaches that Cain caused multiple wounds** and **multiple injuries to his brother Abel. As** Cain **did not know from where the soul departs,** he struck him multiple times. This continued **until he came to his neck** and struck him there, whereupon Abel died.

Returning to the phrase *dmei achicha* — the bloods of your brother — the *gemara* offers additional interpretations. Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Hiyya teaches: *melamed she-asa Kayin be-Hevel achiv chaburot chaburot petza'ot petza'ot* — this teaches that Kayin inflicted blow after blow and wound after wound upon his brother, *she-lo haya yodea me-heichan neshama yotzet* — because he did not know from where the soul departs, *ad she-higi'a le-tzavaro* — until he reached his neck and realized that was the means of death. The plural "bloods" thus reflects the multiple wounds inflicted in this act of primordial violence.

### Blood & Descendants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: מִיּוֹם שֶׁפָּתְחָה הָאָרֶץ אֶת פִּיהָ וְקִיבְּלַתּוּ לְדָמוֹ שֶׁל הֶבֶל, שׁוּב לֹא פָּתְחָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִכְּנַף הָאָרֶץ זְמִרֹת שָׁמַעְנוּ צְבִי לַצַּדִּיק״ – מִכְּנַף הָאָרֶץ וְלֹא מִפִּי הָאָרֶץ. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ חִזְקִיָּה אָחִיו: ״וַתִּפְתַּח הָאָרֶץ אֶת פִּיהָ״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְרָעָה פָּתְחָה, לְטוֹבָה לֹא פָּתְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says: From the day the earth opened its mouth and received the blood of Abel,** its mouth **has not opened again, as it is stated: "From the corner of the earth have we heard songs: Glory to the righteous"** (Isaiah 24:16): One can infer that the songs are heard **"from the corner of the earth," but not from the mouth of the earth,** as the earth never again opened its mouth. **Ḥizkiyya,** Rav Yehuda's **brother, raised an objection to** Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya: The verse states concerning Korah and his assembly: **"And the earth opened her mouth** and swallowed them up, and their households, and all the men that appertained unto Korah, and all their goods" (Numbers 16:32). Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, **said to him:** It **opened** again **for a deleterious** purpose; it **did not open** again **for a constructive** purpose.

Rav Yehuda bar Rav Hiyya offers a further teaching: *mi-yom she-patcha ha-aretz et piha ve-kibla et damo shel Hevel shuv lo patcha* — from the day the earth opened its mouth and received the blood of Hevel, it never opened its mouth again. The proof is from the verse *mi-kenaf ha-aretz zemirot shamanu tzevi la-tzaddik* — from the edges of the earth we heard song, glory to the righteous. The verse says *mi-kenaf ha-aretz* — from the edges — and not *mi-pi ha-aretz* — from the mouth of the earth — indicating that the earth's mouth was no longer available for this purpose.

### Earth's Mouth

His brother Chizkiyah challenged this: *va-tiftach ha-aretz et piha* — the earth did open its mouth, in the episode of Korach! The response: *le-ra'ah patcha le-tova lo patcha* — it opened its mouth to receive an evil person, not to benefit the righteous. That is a different matter entirely.

## Exile Atonement

### Half Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: גָּלוּת מְכַפֶּרֶת עָוֹן מֶחֱצָה. מֵעִיקָּרָא כְּתִיב: ״וְהָיִיתִי נָע וְנָד״, וּלְבַסּוֹף כְּתִיב: ״וַיֵּשֶׁב בְּאֶרֶץ נוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says: Exile atones** for **half** of **a sin.** As **initially** it **is written** in the verse concerning Cain that he said: **"And I shall be a fugitive [*na*] and a wanderer [*vanad* ]** in the earth" (Genesis 4:14), **and ultimately** it **is written:** "And Cain went out from the presence of the Lord, **and dwelt in the land of Nod"** (Genesis 4:16). Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, equates "Nod" with "*nad*," and understands that Cain was given only the punishment of being a wanderer. Exile atoned for half his sin, thereby negating the punishment of being a fugitive.

Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Hiyya further teaches that *galut mechaper avon mechtza* — exile atones for half of the sin. The proof is from Kayin's punishment: initially the verse describes his fate as *ve-hayiti na va-nad* — I will be both a wanderer (*na*) and a rover (*nad*). Yet subsequently it is written *va-yeshev be-eretz Nod* — and he settled in the land of Nod. Nod retains the root of *na* or *nad*, but the doubling is gone: only one form of wandering remains, suggesting that exile atoned for half of his sentence.

### Three Attone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: גָּלוּת מְכַפֶּרֶת שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ וְגוֹ׳ הַיֹּשֵׁב בָּעִיר הַזֹּאת יָמוּת בַּחֶרֶב בָּרָעָב וּבַדָּבֶר וְהַיּוֹצֵא וְנָפַל עַל הַכַּשְׂדִּים הַצָּרִים עֲלֵיכֶם וְחָיָה וְהָיְתָה לּוֹ נַפְשׁוֹ לְשָׁלָל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: Exile atones** for **three matters,** i.e., three types of death, **as it is stated: "So says the Lord:** Behold, I set before you the way of life and the way of death. **He that abides in this city shall die by the sword, and by the famine, and by the pestilence; but he that goes out, and falls away to the Chaldeans that besiege you, he shall survive, and his life shall be for him for a prey"** (Jeremiah 21:8–9), indicating that exile from Jerusalem will save one from those three deaths.

A distinct Rav Yehuda — a different *amora* — teaches that exile atones for three specific things, citing the verse: *ha-yoshev ba-ir ha-zot yamut ba-cherev u-va-ra'av u-va-dever ve-ha-yotzei ve-nafal al ha-kasdim ha-tzarim aleichem yichyeh ve-hayeta lo nafsho le-shalal* — he who dwells in this city will die by sword, by famine, and by pestilence, while he who goes out and surrenders to the besieging Kasdim will live, and his life will be as loot to him. The one who chooses exile — entering the hands of the besieging enemy — is spared from the three fates of sword, famine, and pestilence. Thus exile atones for three things: preservation from violent death, from starvation, and from plague.

### Complete Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: גָּלוּת מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל הַכֹּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ כִּתְבוּ אֶת הָאִישׁ הַזֶּה עֲרִירִי גֶּבֶר לֹא יִצְלַח בְּיָמָיו כִּי לֹא יִצְלַח מִזַּרְעוֹ אִישׁ יֹשֵׁב עַל כִּסֵּא דָוִד וּמֹשֵׁל עוֹד בִּיהוּדָה״. וּבָתַר דִּגְלָה כְּתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי יְכָנְיָה אַסִּר (בְּנוֹ) שַׁלְתִּיאֵל בְּנוֹ״. אַסִּר – שֶׁעִיבְּרַתּוּ אִמּוֹ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין. שַׁלְתִּיאֵל – שֶׁשְּׁתָלוֹ אֵל שֶׁלֹּא כְּדֶרֶךְ הַנִּשְׁתָּלִין: גְּמִירִי שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת מְעוּמָּד,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Exile atones for all** transgressions and renders a sinner like a new person, **as it is stated** concerning the king Jeconiah, a descendant of King David: **"So says the Lord: Write you this man childless, a man that shall not prosper in his days; for no man of his seed shall prosper, sitting upon the throne of David, and ruling anymore in Judah"** (Jeremiah 22:30). **And after** Jeconiah **was exiled it is written: "And the sons of Jeconiah, the same is Assir, Shealtiel his son"** (I Chronicles 3:17). The verse employs the plural "sons of" although he had only one son, Shealtiel. **"Assir,"** literally, prisoner, teaches **that his mother conceived him in prison. "Shealtiel,"** literally, planted by God, teaches **that God planted him in a way atypical of** most **plants [*hanishtalin*],** i.e., people. It **is learned** as a tradition **that a woman does not conceive** when she is **standing** during sexual intercourse,

Rabbi Yochanan goes further: *galut mechaperet al ha-kol* — exile atones for everything. His proof is from the case of Yechonya, the last king of Judah before the Babylonian exile. The prophet declared: *kitvu et ha-ish hazeh ariri* — write this man off as childless, and further that no descendant of his would sit on the throne of David or rule in Yehudah. His line appeared to be utterly and permanently severed. Yet after his exile, *ketiv uvnei Yechanya* — it is written that Yechonya had children. The exile atoned for the decree against him and restored what had been taken away.

### Shealtiel Birth

Why is one of Yechonya's sons called *Assir*? *She-ibrato imo be-veit ha-asurim* — because his mother conceived him while in jail, and *Assir* is connected to *asurin*, the imprisoned. The constraints of incarceration — according to Rashi, the confined space forced an unusual posture for conception, since a woman does not ordinarily conceive while standing — are reflected in this unusual name.

### Zerubbabel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִיא נִתְעַבְּרָה מְעוּמָּד. דָּבָר אַחֵר: שַׁלְתִּיאֵל – שֶׁנִּשְׁאַל עַל אָלָתוֹ אֵל. זְרוּבָּבֶל – שֶׁנִּזְרַע בְּבָבֶל. וּמָה שְׁמוֹ? נְחֶמְיָה בֶּן חֲכַלְיָה שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **and** the mother of Shealtiel **conceived** while **standing. Alternatively, "Shealtiel"** is interpreted as meaning **that God [*El* ] requested [*nishal* ]** dissolution **of His oath,** as it were, and allowed Jeconiah to father a child. In the continuation of that passage in Chronicles, where the verse refers to the grandson of Jeconiah, **Zerubbabel [*Zerubavel* ],** the Gemara interprets that his name teaches **that he was sown [*nizra*],** i.e., conceived, **in Babylonia [*Bavel*]. And what** was **his** true **name? Nehemiah, son of Hachaliah,** was **his** true **name.**

Yechonya's grandson Zerubbabel, who led the Jews back from Babylonian exile and oversaw the early stages of the Second Temple's construction, bears a name that the *gemara* unpacks: *she-nizra be-Vavel* — he was seeded, i.e., conceived, in Babylon. His very name encodes the geography of exile and return.

### Name Identities

*She'altiel* — Zerubbabel's father — is expounded in two ways. First: *she-lo kederech ha-neta'im* — he was not planted in the normal manner of plantings, reflecting his unusual circumstances of conception in jail. Second, a *davar acher*: *she-nish'al al alato el* — God dissolved or absolved the oath that had been made concerning his father Yechonya, releasing the curse.

The *gemara* then states that Zerubbabel's actual name was Nechemia ben Chachalya. This identification — equating Zerubbabel with Nechemia — is noted as puzzling by at least one of the commentators (*Margoliyot*), since they appear to be distinct figures elsewhere in Tanach. Yet here the *gemara* presents them as the same individual, and thus ends the discussion of daf 37.
