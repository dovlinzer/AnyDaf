# Sanhedrin 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sanhedrin Layout

### Semicircular Seating

The *mishnah* that opens our discussion actually begins at the bottom of daf 36, and it describes the physical arrangement of the *sanhedrin*: *sanhedrin hayetah kechatzi goren agulah kedei sheyihiyu ro'in zeh et zeh* — the *sanhedrin* was arranged like half of a circular threshing floor, so that all of the judges could see one another. The semicircular shape was not merely aesthetic; it served the functional purpose of ensuring mutual visibility among all who sat in judgment.

### Court Scribes

The *mishnah* continues: *ushenei sofrei hadayanin omdim lifneihem echad miyamin ve'echad mismol* — there were two court scribes standing before the judges, one on the right and one on the left. These scribes recorded the arguments on both sides: *vekotvin divrei mechaivin vedivrei mezakin* — they wrote the words of those arguing for conviction and the words of those arguing for acquittal.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and holds that there were in fact three scribes: one dedicated to recording the words of those arguing for acquittal, one dedicated to recording the words of those arguing for conviction, and a third who recorded both. Rashi explains that this third scribe served as a checker, providing a built-in safeguard against errors that might arise when a single scribe attempts to record both sides simultaneously — the workload being simply too great to do accurately.

### Three Rows of Scholars

Beyond the judges themselves and the scribes, the *mishnah* describes three rows of *talmidei chachamim* seated before the court: *veshalosh shurot shel talmidei chachamim yoshvin lifneihem kol echad ve'echad makir et mekomo* — three rows of scholars sit before them, and each individual knows his place.

Rashi clarifies that each of these three rows contained twenty-three scholars — the same number as the judges of the *sanhedrin ketanah* itself. The reason for maintaining this number, as Rashi explains, is rooted in the *halachic* mechanics of capital cases. In a capital case, a verdict cannot be rendered on the basis of a single-vote majority for conviction; at least two additional votes for conviction over acquittal are required. If the court of twenty-three becomes deadlocked in a configuration such as twelve for conviction and eleven for acquittal, they are unable to proceed. In such a case, they augment the court by bringing in additional judges from the first row of scholars seated before them.

### Adding Judges

The process of augmenting the court works as follows: *hutzrechu lismoch somchin min harishonah* — if they need to appoint additional judges, they draw from the first row. When someone from the first row is elevated to the bench, someone from the second row advances into the first, and someone from the third row advances into the second. And since one of the three rows now has a vacancy, *uvorerin lahem od echad min hakahal* — they select an additional individual from the general public watching the proceedings and seat him in the third row.

Importantly, *velo hayah yoshev bimkomo shel rishon ela yoshev bimkom haraui lo* — the newcomer does not take the specific seat vacated by the person who was elevated; rather, everyone shifts forward in an orderly fashion and the new person takes the position appropriate to him at the end of the row.

One might ask: why not simply maintain rows of twenty-three, twenty-three, and two, which would sum neatly to the maximum of seventy-one judges? Rashi addresses this: it would be disrespectful to seat only two people in what is meant to be a full row. The aesthetic and honorific consideration demands that all rows be equal in number. This admittedly creates the somewhat curious situation where most of the scholars seated in these rows — particularly those deeper in — will realistically never be called upon to serve as judges. Nevertheless, the rows must be maintained and filled.

## Biblical Derivation

### Shorerech as Navel

The *gemara* now asks: *mena hani milei* — what is the scriptural derivation for the semicircular arrangement? Rabbi Akha bar Chanina answers by citing a verse from *Shir Hashirim*: *shorerech aggan hasahar al yechsar hamazeg* — your navel is like a crescent basin; may its mixed wine never be lacking. The *gemara* proceeds to expound each term of this verse as a reference to the *sanhedrin*.

*Shorerech* — your navel — *zo sanhedrin*: this refers to the *sanhedrin*. Why is the *sanhedrin* compared to the navel? *She-hi yoshevet be-tiburo shel olam* — because the *sanhedrin* sat in the navel of the world, a phrase associated with the Temple in Jerusalem, which other *midrashim* similarly describe as the world's center.

### Aggan & Hasahar

*Aggan* — basin — *she-hi megina al kol ha-olam kulo*: the word *aggan* is read as a reference to protection (*haganah*), because the *sanhedrin* protects the entire world. *Hasahar* — the crescent — *she-hi domah le-sahar*: this is the element most directly relevant to the *mishnah*, for it teaches that the *sanhedrin* is shaped like a crescent moon.

### Mazeg Requirement

The final clause of the verse — *al yechsar hamazeg*, may the mixing not be lacking — is interpreted as follows: *im hutsrach echad mehem latzet ro'in im yesh esrim u-shlosha keneged sanhedrei ketana yotze ve-im lav eino yotze*. If one of the judges must leave during the proceedings, they first check whether there are twenty-three individuals present in the crowd — corresponding to the number of a *sanhedrin ketanah*. Only if that minimum is present may the judge excuse himself; otherwise, he may not leave. The reference to *mazeg*, the mixing of wine, alludes to the maintenance of this quorum.

## Jewish Trustworthiness

### Wheat Mound

The *gemara* continues to expound the verses of *Shir Hashirim* in connection with the *sanhedrin* and with the Jewish people more broadly. The verse *bitnech aremat chitim* — your stomach is like a mound of wheat — is interpreted: *mah aremat chitim ha-kol nehenin mimena af sanhedrin ha-kol nehenin mi-ta'amehem* — just as everyone benefits from a mound of wheat, so too everyone benefits from the reasoning and legal rulings of the *sanhedrin*.

### Rose Hedge

The verse *suga bashoshanim* — hedged with roses — is interpreted as a metaphor for the Jewish people's resistance to transgression: *she-afilu ke-suga shel shoshanim lo yifretzu bahen pratzot* — even a barrier as thin and penetrable as a hedge of roses, the Jewish people will not break through it.

### Niddah & Yichud

The *gemara* illustrates this principle with a remarkable exchange. A heretic approached Rav Kahana and challenged him: *amritu nida shari le-yichudi bahadei gavra* — you say that a *nidah* is permitted to be in *yichud* with her husband? *Efshar esh be-no'oret ve-eina mehabhevet* — is it possible that fire can be placed among flax and not singe it? Surely such proximity will inevitably lead to transgression.

Rav Kahana responded: *ha-torah he'idah aleinu suga bashoshanim* — the Torah has testified about us through this verse, that even a barrier as weak as a hedge of roses will not be breached. The Jewish people will not transgress even in circumstances that appear to invite it.

This exchange prompted a *halachic* observation by *Tosafot*, who notes a difficulty: if the prohibition of *yichud* is *de-oraita*, how can a verse from *Shir Hashirim* be invoked to override it? *Tosafot* resolves this by explaining that the *de-oraita* prohibition of *yichud* applies only in cases where the two individuals involved are categorically forbidden to one another — such as close relatives or other *arayot* — but not in the case of a husband and wife between whom *yichud* is not absolutely prohibited under Torah law in all circumstances.

### Pomegranate Verse

Resh Lakish offers an alternative source for the same principle. He cites the verse *ke-felach harimon rakatech* — like a wedge of pomegranate is your temple — and expounds: *afilu rikanim sheback male'im mitzvot karimon* — even those who are the most empty among you, the lowest of the low, are full of *mitzvos* like a pomegranate is full of seeds. This too serves as a basis for trusting in the essential spiritual integrity of every Jew.

### Fragrant Clothes

Rabbi Zeira derives the same idea from yet another source, citing the verse *vayarach et reiach begadav* — and he smelled the smell of his clothes — from the episode in which Yitzchak smells the garments of who he believes to be Eisav. Rabbi Zeira reads *begadav* not as "his clothes" but as *bogdav* — his traitors, or those who mislead. The point is striking: even when reading the verse as referring to deceivers, Yitzchak smells and finds them fragrant. The Torah makes explicit that the smell was pleasing — *kerei'ach sadeh asher beraco Hashem* — like the smell of a field that God has blessed. Even those who are in some sense dishonest or misleading carry within them something of genuine worth and beauty. The trust we extend to Jews even in compromised situations is grounded in this assurance.

### Rabbi Zeira & Thugs

The *gemara* then recounts a story that illustrates this principle in practice. There were certain thugs (*biryonei*) in the neighborhood of Rabbi Zeira. Rather than avoiding them, *de-hava mekarev lehu* — he would associate with them, in the hope that his proximity would bring them to *teshuvah*. The *Rabbanan* were displeased with this conduct.

When Rabbi Zeira died, these same thugs said: *ad ha-idana hava haricha ketin shakei de-hava ba'ei alan rachamei hashta man ba'ei alan rachamei* — until now the singed small legs — their nickname for Rabbi Zeira, who according to Rashi had subjected his legs to heat as part of his ascetic practices, and who was known for his short, unimposing stature — would pray for us; who will pray for us now? *Hirhiru belibehu ve-avdu teshuvah* — they searched their hearts and did *teshuvah*. The connection to the theme of the section is clear: even the most apparently lowly and coarse among the Jewish people carry within them the capacity for genuine repentance and spiritual ascent.

## Judge Advancement

### Advancement Process

Returning to the three rows of scholars, *Abaye* derives a principle about how movement through the rows operates. When a scholar is elevated from the rows into the court, all others advance to fill the vacated positions, so that no empty space is left standing.

### Tail vs Head

This raises an apparent difficulty. Imagine a scholar who holds the first position in the second row — the most prominent seat in that row. When the court is augmented and everyone shifts forward, this individual is moved up into the second row of the first row, but he now occupies the last position there — the least prominent seat. He might reasonably object: *until now I was at the head; now you are placing me at the tail?*

Abaye responds with a well-known principle: *havei zanav la-arayot ve-al tehi rosh la-shu'alim* — be a tail among lions rather than a head among foxes. It is better to occupy the lowest position among a more distinguished group than the highest position among a lesser one. This resolves the objection: being last in the company of greater scholars is itself an elevation, not a demotion.

## Witness Warning

### Bringing Witnesses

The *mishnah* now turns to the procedure for warning witnesses in capital cases. *Al edei nefashot hayu machnisin otan u'me'aymin aleihem* — when dealing with witnesses in capital cases, they would bring them into the *sanhedrin* and impress upon them the gravity of their testimony.

### Direct Evidence

The witnesses are warned against several categories of invalid testimony: *shema tomru me'omed u'mishmua mipi ed v'ed mipi ed u'mipi adam ne'eman* — lest you base your testimony on an assumption, on something you heard, on what one witness told you another witness said, or on the word of someone you consider trustworthy. None of these suffice. Only direct personal observation constitutes valid testimony in a capital case. And the witnesses are reminded that the court will subject them to thorough *drisha u'vchakira* — the formal set of interrogatory questions designed to probe the precise circumstances of their claimed observation.

### Civil vs Capital

The witnesses are further instructed: *havu yodim shelo k'dinei mamonot dinei nefashot* — know that capital cases are not like civil cases. In a civil case, *adam noten mamon u'mitkaper lo* — a person can give money and be atoned; the wrong can be undone materially. Moreover, even a witness who testified falsely in a civil case can make restitution. But in a capital case, *damo ve-dam zariyotav taluy bo ad sof ha-olam* — if someone is executed on the basis of your testimony, his blood and the blood of all of his potential descendants hang upon you forever.

### Cain & Abel

The *mishnah* illustrates this with the story of Kayin: *sheken matzinu be-Kayin she-harag et achiv shene'emar dmei achicha tzo'akim* — as we see with Kayin who killed his brother, where the verse says "the bloods of your brother cry out." The deliberate use of the plural *dmei* — bloods — rather than the singular *dam* is significant. *Eino omer dam achicha ela dmei achicha* — it does not say "the blood of your brother" but "the bloods of your brother" — implying both his own blood and the blood of all his descendants who would never come into existence.

A *davar acher* adds: perhaps the plural refers to the fact that Hevel's blood was scattered across the trees and stones, sprayed in multiple places.

### Single Human Value

The *mishnah* then draws a profound theological lesson from the fact that all of humanity descends from a single ancestor, Adam HaRishon: *lefikach nivra adam yechidi* — therefore man was created singular. The lesson this conveys is that *kol ha-me'abed nefesh achat mi-Yisrael ma'aleh alav ha-katuv ke'ilu ibed olam malei* — anyone who destroys a single soul is treated by the Torah as though he has destroyed an entire world.

It is worth noting that the words *mi-Yisrael* — "from Israel" — do not appear in the earliest printings of the text, including the text as cited by the Rambam and in the old versions of Rashi. The original formulation appears to have applied universally to any human soul, without the specification of Jewish identity.

### Soul = World

Correspondingly, *ve-chol ha-mekayem nefesh achat mi-Yisrael ma'aleh alav ha-katuv ke'ilu kiyem olam malei* — anyone who sustains a single soul is treated by the Torah as though he has sustained an entire world. Rashi explains the practical implication of the statement *bishvili nivra ha-olam* — the world was created for my sake: *klomar chashuv ani ke-olam malei* — it means I consider myself to be like an entire world. This mindset carries with it a sense of cosmic responsibility: *lo etred et atzmi min ha-olam ba-aveira achat* — I will not cast myself out of the world through a single transgression.

### Creation Reasons

The *mishnah* enumerates additional reasons why God chose to create humanity through a single ancestor. *U-mipnei shalom ha-briyot shelo yomar adam le-chavero abba gadol mei-avicha* — for the sake of peace among human beings, so that no person could claim to his fellow that his lineage is superior, since all share the same first ancestor. *Ve-shelo yihyu ha-minim omrim harbeh rashuyot ba-shamayim* — so that heretics could not argue that multiple divine powers created different peoples, with each people's god responsible for their first ancestor. *U-lehagid gedulato shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — and to declare the greatness of God: whereas a human craftsman stamps many coins with the same mold and they all come out identical, God stamps every human being with the stamp of Adam HaRishon and yet no two are alike.

## Witness Warning…

### Personal Responsibility

The *mishnah* concludes: *lefikach kol echad ve-echad chayav lomar bishvili nivra ha-olam* — therefore each and every individual is obligated to say: the world was created for my sake. As Rashi makes clear, this is not an expression of arrogance but of moral seriousness — an awareness that one's own life is of infinite worth, and that the destruction of any life is therefore an act of cosmic devastation.

## Witness Concerns

### Fear of Involvement

After being told all of this, the witness might understandably recoil: *shema tomru ma lanu u-la-tzara ha-zot* — perhaps you will say, what do we want with all this trouble? The gravity of the situation — the weight of being responsible for a verdict that could result in someone's death — might cause a witness to retreat from testifying altogether.

### Rejoicing in Justice

The *mishnah* addresses this concern on two levels. First, there is a positive obligation: *halo kevar ne'emar ve-hu ed o ra'ah o yada im lo yagid* — the Torah has already stated that if one is a witness and has seen or knows something, he must come forward and testify. Second, the witness need not fear moral complicity in a justified execution: *halo kevar ne'emar be'avod resha'im rinah* — the Torah has already stated that when the wicked are destroyed, there is rejoicing. Participating in a just verdict against someone truly guilty is not a burden to be feared but an act of righteousness to be embraced.

## Circumstantial Evidence

### Assumption Defined

The *gemara* now asks: *keitzad me'umad* — what exactly is an *umdana*, an assumption or circumstantial inference? The *baraita* explains with a concrete case: *omer lahem shema kach re'item sheratz echad achar chaveiro lechorba* — perhaps this is what you saw: one person running after another into a ruin. *Ve-ratztem acharav* — and you ran after him.

### Sword & Blood

*U-matzatem sa'if beyado u-damo metaptef ve-harug mefarfer* — and you found a sword in his hand, blood dripping from it, and a dead body nearby in its final convulsions. *Im kach re'item lo re'item klum* — if that is what you saw, you have seen nothing admissible. No matter how overwhelming the circumstantial picture, this does not constitute valid testimony in a capital case.

### Shimon ben Shatach

The *baraita* then cites a first-person account from Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach. He attests — using the formula *er'eh be-nechama im lo ra'iti*, an oath of uncertain precise meaning — that he himself witnessed exactly this scenario: one man running into a ruin, the rabbi running after him, finding a sword in hand, blood dripping, and a dead body convulsing. He confronted the man directly: *amarti lo rasha mi harago lazeh o ani o ata* — wicked one! Who killed this person? Either I did or you did, and obviously it was not I. Yet, *aval ma e'eseh she-ein damcha masur beyadi* — what can I do? Your blood is not in my hands. *She-harei amra torah al pi shnayim edim yumat ha-met* — for the Torah has said: it is on the testimony of two witnesses that the death sentence is carried out.

This passage is somewhat puzzling, as the rishonic commentators note: the *mishnah*'s case concerns *umdana* — the requirement of direct observation versus circumstantial inference — while Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach's formulation invokes the requirement of two witnesses, a distinct principle. *Tosafot* addresses this by suggesting that Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach was not alone — there were other observers present — and that he is in fact deriving both the requirement of direct sight and the requirement of two witnesses from the same verse. The *gemara* acknowledges that this is somewhat strained (*ra'uh*), reflecting the genuine difficulty in the way the sources align.

### Divine Punishment

Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach concludes his account: *ha-yodea machshavot yipara me'oto ish she-harag et chaveiro* — may the One who knows all thoughts punish this man who killed his fellow. *Amru lo zazu misham ad sheba nachash ve-hikisho* — they said: they had not even moved from that place before a snake came and bit him, and he died. Divine justice was carried out where human justice could not reach.

## Post-Destruction Judgment

### Four Death Penalties

This brings the *gemara* to a remarkable principle stated by Rav Yosef and corroborated in the school of Chizkiyah: *miyom she-charav Beit HaMikdash af al pi she-batla Sanhedrin arba mitot lo batlu* — from the day the Temple was destroyed, even though the *sanhedrin* was nullified, the four death penalties were not annulled.

### Sanhedrin Cessation

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *lo batlu? ve-ha batlu!* — they were not annulled? But they clearly were nullified! The response is that the *din* — the legal principle and its implementation — was not annulled; it is simply that God now carries out these sentences through natural means rather than through a human court.

*Tosafot* raises a further difficulty: it is known that the *sanhedrin* ceased to adjudicate capital cases forty years *before* the destruction of the Temple. Why then does the *gemara* speak specifically of the day the Temple was destroyed rather than the earlier date?

### Special Cases

*Tosafot* explains, citing the passage in *Perek Hayu Bodkin*, that even after the *sanhedrin* went into a form of exile and ceased its regular functioning, it would still reconvene *letzorech sha'ah* — for special cases requiring it — and would return to the *Lishkat HaGazit* in the Temple precinct. *Tosafot* mentions in this connection a case that appears in uncensored versions of the text as *hahu uvda de-Yeshu* — the case of Jesus — which was subsequently removed by censors, likely to avoid provoking Christian hostility. Whether this reflects an independent Jewish tradition of such a trial or represents a textual intrusion from external sources is itself a question worth noting.

### Natural Death Means

The *gemara* then enumerates how each of the four death penalties continues to be administered through natural agency. One deserving death by stoning (*sekilah*) will either fall from a roof or be killed by a wild animal. One deserving death by burning (*sereifah*) will fall into a fire or be bitten by a snake. One deserving death by sword (*harigah*) will be handed over to the non-Jewish authorities or will be killed by bandits. One deserving strangulation (*chenek*) will drown in a river or die of *serunchi*, some form of disease.

### Multiple Penalties

This raises a question about the snake in Rabbi Shimon ben Shatach's case. If the man was a murderer, his punishment should have been *harigah* — death by sword. Why then was he killed by snakebite, which corresponds to *sereifah*? *Amrei hahu chata acharini havah beih* — the answer is that he had another sin as well, one that carried the penalty of *sereifah*. *De-amar mar mi she-nitchayev shtei mitot beit din nidon ba-chamurah* — as the principle states, one who is liable for two different death penalties is judged by the more severe of the two. Since *sereifah* is considered more severe than *harigah*, it was the penalty of *sereifah* that was visited upon him through the snakebite.

## Evidence Standards

### Capital Restriction

The *gemara* now returns to analyze the *mishnah*'s broader evidentiary framework. The *mishnah* explicitly states that *umdana* is invalid in capital cases, which implies by inference that *umdana* is valid in civil cases.

### Camel Example

The *gemara* identifies this as aligning with the view of Rabbi Akha: *tanya Rabbi Acha omer gamal ha'ochez bein ha-gamalim ve-nimtza gamal harug be-tzido ve-yadu'a she-zeh harago* — Rabbi Akha taught that if there is a camel — described by a term (*ha'ochez*) that Rashi explains with some uncertainty, perhaps meaning one in heat or one that is agitated — in the midst of other camels, and a dead camel is found alongside it, it is known that this camel killed the other, and the owner bears civil liability accordingly. This is a straightforward application of *umdana* to civil cases.

### Evidence Consistency

A challenge is raised: if the logic of the *mishnah* is that whatever is invalid in capital cases is thereby valid in civil cases, this would imply that *ed mipi ed* — secondhand testimony — which the *mishnah* also lists as invalid in capital cases, should similarly be valid in civil cases. But this is clearly untenable.

### Secondhand Testimony

The *gemara* resolves this by rejecting the premise: *ed mipi ed* is invalid in *both* civil and capital cases. The *mishnah* cites it in the context of capital cases for completeness, not to imply that it is permissible in civil cases. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* from elsewhere: if a person claims that his fellow told him he owes money to a third party, that testimony is worth nothing — the obligation must be admitted directly before witnesses with a specific acknowledgment of the sum owed. This confirms that secondhand testimony is disqualified across the board. Consequently, one cannot derive from the *mishnah*'s treatment of *umdana* in capital cases a blanket rule that everything listed there is permissible in civil cases; each category must be analyzed independently.

## Bloods Plural

### Kayin's Strikes

Returning to the phrase *dmei achicha* — the bloods of your brother — the *gemara* offers additional interpretations. Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Hiyya teaches: *melamed she-asa Kayin be-Hevel achiv chaburot chaburot petza'ot petza'ot* — this teaches that Kayin inflicted blow after blow and wound after wound upon his brother, *she-lo haya yodea me-heichan neshama yotzet* — because he did not know from where the soul departs, *ad she-higi'a le-tzavaro* — until he reached his neck and realized that was the means of death. The plural "bloods" thus reflects the multiple wounds inflicted in this act of primordial violence.

### Blood & Descendants

Rav Yehuda bar Rav Hiyya offers a further teaching: *mi-yom she-patcha ha-aretz et piha ve-kibla et damo shel Hevel shuv lo patcha* — from the day the earth opened its mouth and received the blood of Hevel, it never opened its mouth again. The proof is from the verse *mi-kenaf ha-aretz zemirot shamanu tzevi la-tzaddik* — from the edges of the earth we heard song, glory to the righteous. The verse says *mi-kenaf ha-aretz* — from the edges — and not *mi-pi ha-aretz* — from the mouth of the earth — indicating that the earth's mouth was no longer available for this purpose.

### Earth's Mouth

His brother Chizkiyah challenged this: *va-tiftach ha-aretz et piha* — the earth did open its mouth, in the episode of Korach! The response: *le-ra'ah patcha le-tova lo patcha* — it opened its mouth to receive an evil person, not to benefit the righteous. That is a different matter entirely.

## Exile Atonement

### Half Atonement

Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Hiyya further teaches that *galut mechaper avon mechtza* — exile atones for half of the sin. The proof is from Kayin's punishment: initially the verse describes his fate as *ve-hayiti na va-nad* — I will be both a wanderer (*na*) and a rover (*nad*). Yet subsequently it is written *va-yeshev be-eretz Nod* — and he settled in the land of Nod. Nod retains the root of *na* or *nad*, but the doubling is gone: only one form of wandering remains, suggesting that exile atoned for half of his sentence.

### Three Attone

A distinct Rav Yehuda — a different *amora* — teaches that exile atones for three specific things, citing the verse: *ha-yoshev ba-ir ha-zot yamut ba-cherev u-va-ra'av u-va-dever ve-ha-yotzei ve-nafal al ha-kasdim ha-tzarim aleichem yichyeh ve-hayeta lo nafsho le-shalal* — he who dwells in this city will die by sword, by famine, and by pestilence, while he who goes out and surrenders to the besieging Kasdim will live, and his life will be as loot to him. The one who chooses exile — entering the hands of the besieging enemy — is spared from the three fates of sword, famine, and pestilence. Thus exile atones for three things: preservation from violent death, from starvation, and from plague.

### Complete Atonement

Rabbi Yochanan goes further: *galut mechaperet al ha-kol* — exile atones for everything. His proof is from the case of Yechonya, the last king of Judah before the Babylonian exile. The prophet declared: *kitvu et ha-ish hazeh ariri* — write this man off as childless, and further that no descendant of his would sit on the throne of David or rule in Yehudah. His line appeared to be utterly and permanently severed. Yet after his exile, *ketiv uvnei Yechanya* — it is written that Yechonya had children. The exile atoned for the decree against him and restored what had been taken away.

### Shealtiel Birth

Why is one of Yechonya's sons called *Assir*? *She-ibrato imo be-veit ha-asurim* — because his mother conceived him while in jail, and *Assir* is connected to *asurin*, the imprisoned. The constraints of incarceration — according to Rashi, the confined space forced an unusual posture for conception, since a woman does not ordinarily conceive while standing — are reflected in this unusual name.

### Zerubbabel

Yechonya's grandson Zerubbabel, who led the Jews back from Babylonian exile and oversaw the early stages of the Second Temple's construction, bears a name that the *gemara* unpacks: *she-nizra be-Vavel* — he was seeded, i.e., conceived, in Babylon. His very name encodes the geography of exile and return.

### Name Identities

*She'altiel* — Zerubbabel's father — is expounded in two ways. First: *she-lo kederech ha-neta'im* — he was not planted in the normal manner of plantings, reflecting his unusual circumstances of conception in jail. Second, a *davar acher*: *she-nish'al al alato el* — God dissolved or absolved the oath that had been made concerning his father Yechonya, releasing the curse.

The *gemara* then states that Zerubbabel's actual name was Nechemia ben Chachalya. This identification — equating Zerubbabel with Nechemia — is noted as puzzling by at least one of the commentators (*Margoliyot*), since they appear to be distinct figures elsewhere in Tanach. Yet here the *gemara* presents them as the same individual, and thus ends the discussion of daf 37.
