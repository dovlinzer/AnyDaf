# Ketubot 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Motzi Shem Ra

### Context & Setup

Today's daf is 46, picking up from the bottom of 45b. We are wrapping up the *parsha* of *motzi shem ra* before transitioning, God willing, to what many consider the core subject matter of *masechet Ketubot* itself.

The immediate context involves the *pesukim* concerning *petach beit aviha* — the Torah's specification that the proceedings of *motzi shem ra* take place at the doorway of the father's house — which the Gemara had established applies specifically in the case of after *nisuin*. A separate *pasuk*, concerning the *naarah meurasah* who is stoned, uses the expression *petach*, understood as *shaar ha'ir*, and this applies in a case of *erusin* rather than *nisuin*. Even in the classic case of *nisuin*, if there is no *petach beit ha'av*, the stoning is carried out at *shaar ha'ir*, and if there is no *shaar ha'ir* — for instance, in a city of non-Jews — it is done at *shaar beit din*. This parallel is drawn from the case of one found committing idolatry, who is executed in the city where the idolatry occurred rather than where judgment was rendered, presumably as a lesson to the inhabitants of that city.

### Lashes & Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע לוֹקֶה וְנוֹתֵן מֵאָה סֶלַע. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לִלְקוֹת — לוֹקֶה מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, מֵאָה סֶלַע, בָּעַל — נוֹתֵן, לֹא בָּעַל — אֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: One who defames his wife is flogged and gives one hundred *sela*. Rabbi Yehuda says: As for flogging, he is flogged in any case. However, with regard to the one hundred *sela*, if he defamed her after he had intercourse with her, he gives the money. If he did not yet have intercourse with her, he does not give her this sum.

The Gemara now presents a *baraita*: *ha-motzi shem ra lokeh v'nosein me'ah sela* — one who spreads false evil repute against his wife receives lashes and pays one hundred *sela* (equivalent to four hundred *zuz*). This is the standard formulation of the dual penalty in *motzi shem ra*.

### R. Yehuda's View

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies this. As far as lashes are concerned, *lokeh mikol makom* — he receives lashes under any circumstances. However, with respect to the hundred *sela*, *baal nosein, lo baal eino nosein*: only if he had intercourse with his wife and then brings the false claim does he pay; if he did not have intercourse, he does not pay.

One might wonder how he spreads evil repute without having had intercourse. The answer lies in how *chazal* understand the entire *parsha*. The plain sense of the *pesukim* — *v'avo eleha v'lo matzasi lah betulim*, "I came to her and found no signs of virginity" — sounds like a straightforward claim the husband makes the morning after the wedding. The burden then appears to fall on the girl and her family to produce the bedsheets. *Chazal*, however, require a proper judicial proceeding. There must be witnesses, and not just any witnesses: the husband must have been the one who initiated, urged, or hired them. Furthermore, the father and the girl counter with *eidim zomemim*, witnesses who refute and invalidate the first set. The claim thus becomes entirely a matter of witness testimony, which means it is possible for a husband to manufacture such a proceeding without the wedding night ever having occurred — bringing false witnesses to claim she committed adultery after betrothal, without the physical predicate of their own intercourse. In that scenario, Rabbi Yehuda says, he still receives lashes but does not pay the hundred *sela*.

The logic underlying Rabbi Yehuda's distinction is instructive. Spreading false and damaging repute about a young woman — ruining her standing — is a moral wrong regardless of whether it fits neatly within the *parsha* of *motzi shem ra*. But the hundred *sela* payment functions more like a *keifel*, a double payment, because the husband was in effect trying to rob the girl of her *mohar*, her bride price of fifty *shekel*. If they never had intercourse and the slander is exposed, she is still free to marry someone else and receive the standard bride price. The monetary penalty, then, is specifically anchored to the situation in which sex has occurred and the wedding-night claim is relevant — which is why Rabbi Yehuda limits it to that case.

### Sex Requirement

The Gemara identifies the underlying dispute as that between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *Rabbanan*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that the entire *parsha* of *motzi shem ra* in the Torah speaks only of a case in which intercourse has occurred. The *Rabbanan* hold that the *parsha* applies even without intercourse.

How does this map onto the dispute in the *baraita*? On one reading, the *Tanna Kamma* reflects the *Rabbanan*: whether or not there was intercourse, both lashes and the monetary penalty apply, because the *parsha* is not limited to cases of intercourse. Rabbi Yehuda then emerges as reflecting Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position that the *parsha* is only operative after intercourse, but extends lashes independently of the *parsha* — based on the prohibition of *lo telech rachil b'amecha*, the general prohibition against talebearing and slander. On this reading, anyone who spreads damaging false report, at least in a sufficiently provable and weighty manner, receives lashes based on this *lav*, regardless of whether they are technically within the *parsha* of *motzi shem ra*. The hundred *sela*, however, belongs exclusively to that *parsha* and is therefore conditioned on intercourse having occurred.

There is a second reading of the *baraita* as well: *ika d'amri kulah k'Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — perhaps the entire *baraita* assumes like Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov that the *parsha* operates only after intercourse. The *Tanna Kamma* says that in that scenario — one in which they have had intercourse — both lashes and payment apply. Rabbi Yehuda then adds that lashes are given even without intercourse, based on the general *lav* of *lo telech rachil*, while agreeing that payment requires the context of intercourse.

### Lashes Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לִלְקוֹת לוֹקֶה מִכׇּל מָקוֹם? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בָּעַל — לוֹקֶה, לֹא בָּעַל — אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: לוֹקֶה מַכַּת מַרְדּוּת מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** And does Rabbi Yehuda hold that with regard to flogging, he is flogged in any case? But isn't it taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yehuda says: If he had intercourse with his wife before he defamed her, he is flogged; but if he did not have intercourse with his wife before he defamed her, he is not flogged? In answer to this question, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: When Rabbi Yehuda said that he is flogged even if he had not yet had intercourse with his wife, he was referring to lashes for rebelliousness [*mardut*], which apply by rabbinic law.

The Gemara then challenges: does Rabbi Yehuda really hold that lashes are given under all circumstances? A *baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Yehuda himself says *ba'al lokeh, lo ba'al eino lokeh* — only if there was intercourse does he receive lashes, not otherwise. This appears to be a direct contradiction of the position just attributed to him, creating a *machlokes Tannaim* within Rabbi Yehuda's own rulings.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: when the first formulation says the husband receives lashes *mikol makom*, it means only *makas mardus*, rabbinic lashes — not Torah-level *malkot*. Rabbi Yehuda does not maintain a general biblical liability for talebearing. He holds that Torah-level lashes require being within the *parsha* of *motzi shem ra*, which itself requires intercourse. The earlier statement that he always receives lashes refers merely to a rabbinic disciplinary penalty for spreading evil repute, reflecting a societal interest in deterring such conduct.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מַאי ״בָּעַל לוֹקֶה״ דְּקָתָנֵי הָתָם — מָמוֹן. וְקָרֵי לֵיהּ לְמָמוֹן מַלְקוֹת? אִין, וְהָא תְּנַן: ״הָאוֹמֵר חֲצִי עֶרְכִּי עָלַי״ — נוֹתֵן חֲצִי עֶרְכּוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה וְנוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ שָׁלֵם. לוֹקֶה אַמַּאי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לוֹקֶה בְּעֵרֶךְ שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said: What of the statement that is taught there, in the *baraita*, that it is only if he had intercourse with her that he is flogged? It is referring to the money of the fine. The Gemara asks: And does one call monetary payment flogging? The Gemara answers: Yes, and we learned in a *baraita*: One who says: Half my valuation is upon me, he gives half his valuation, in accordance with the sum fixed by the Torah according to sex and age (see Leviticus 27:2–3). Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: He is flogged and gives his full valuation. The Sages inquired: Why is he flogged? What transgression did he commit? Rav Pappa said: He is flogged by having to pay a full valuation. This proves that monetary payment can be referred to as flogging.

Rav Pappa offers a different resolution. He maintains the original reading: Rabbi Yehuda does hold that lashes apply under all circumstances in the broad sense. When the second *baraita* says *ba'al lokeh, lo ba'al eino lokeh*, the word *lokeh* there does not mean lashes at all — it means monetary liability. *V'kari lei mamon malkut* — can a monetary payment really be called *malkut*? The Gemara answers: yes. A *baraita* records a dispute regarding one who says *chatzi erchi alai*, "I obligate myself to pay half my assessed value." Rabbi Yose berabbi Yehuda says *lokeh v'nosein erech shalem* — he is "*struck*" with the full bill. Rav Pappa explains that *lokeh* there means he is assessed the full amount, not that he receives lashes. The reason is a rabbinic decree: *gezeira chatzi ercho atu erech chatziyo* — saying "half my *erech*" might be confused with saying "the *erech* of half of me," and since the *erech* of half of one's body encompasses a vital organ and therefore equals the *erech* of the whole, one pays the full amount in either case. The word *lokeh* in that passage simply means "he is hit with the full payment." So too in the second *baraita*: *ba'al lokeh* means the husband is "hit" with the monetary obligation if there was intercourse, *lo ba'al eino lokeh* means no monetary obligation without intercourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְעָנְשׁוּ אוֹתוֹ״ — זֶה מָמוֹן, ״וְיִסְּרוּ״ — זֶה מַלְקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the *halakhot* of the defamer. The Sages taught the following *baraita*, based upon the following verses: "And the Elders of that city shall take the man and chastise him. And they shall fine him a hundred shekels of silver, and give them to the father of the young woman" (Deuteronomy 22:18–19). "And they shall fine [*ve'anshu*] him"; this is referring to money. "And chastise him"; this is referring to flogging.

The Gemara then turns to the derivation of the lashes themselves. A *baraita* states: *v'anshu oso* — "they shall punish him" — refers to the monetary fine, as the Torah makes explicit: *v'anshu oso me'ah kesef v'nasnu la'avi hana'arah*. But *veyisru* — "they shall chastise him" — refers to *malkot*. How do we know *veyisru* means lashes? Rabbi Abbahu derives it through a chain: the word *yisru* is linked to a parallel *yisru* in the *parsha* of *ben sorer umoreh* — *veyisru oso v'lo yishma aleihem* — and through the word *ben* appearing there, it connects to the *parsha* of *vehaya im ben hakot harasha*, the classic Torah passage prescribing lashes. Once *veyisru* means lashes in the context of the *ben sorer umoreh*, it likewise means lashes in *motzi shem ra*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לָמַדְנוּ ״יִסְּרוּ״ מִ״יִּסְּרוּ״, וְ״יִסְּרוּ״ מִ״בֵּן״, וּ״בֵן״ מִ״בִּן״ — ״וְהָיָה אִם בִּן הַכּוֹת הָרָשָׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu said: We learned the meaning of the word chastise in the case of a defamer by verbal analogy from the word chastise stated in the verse "if a man have a stubborn and rebellious son [*ben*], that will not listen to the voice of his father or the voice of his mother, and though they chastise him, will not listen to them" (Deuteronomy 21:18). And the implication of the word chastise in that verse is derived from the word son that appears in the same verse. And the implication of the word son [*ben*] with regard to a rebellious son is derived from the word ***bin*** in the verse **"Then it shall be if the wicked man deserve [*bin*] to be flogged"** (Deuteronomy 25:2).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַזְהָרָה לְמוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע מְנָלַן? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: מִ״לֹּא תֵלֵךְ רָכִיל״. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִ״וְּנִשְׁמַרְתָּ מִכֹּל דָּבָר רָע״.
>
> **Translation:** From where do we derive the warning, i.e., the prohibition that serves as the source for the flogging for a defamer? Rabbi Elazar says that the prohibition is derived from the verse "You shall not go up and down as talebearer" (Leviticus 19:16). Rabbi Natan says that it is derived from: "Then you shall keep yourself from every evil thing [*davar ra*]"** (Deuteronomy 23:10), which is expounded to mean *dibbur ra*, evil speech.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מֵהַאי? הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר. ״וְנִשְׁמַרְתָּ מִכֹּל דָּבָר רָע״, מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר: אַל יְהַרְהֵר אָדָם בַּיּוֹם וְיָבֹא לִידֵי טוּמְאָה בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** And what is the reason that Rabbi Elazar did not state that it is derived from this verse quoted by Rabbi Natan? He requires that verse for the statement of Rabbi Pineḥas ben Yair, as it was taught: "Then you shall keep yourself from every evil thing"; from here Rabbi Pineḥas ben Yair said: A person should not think impure thoughts by day and thereby come by night to the impurity of an emission.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי נָתָן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר מֵהַאי? הָהוּא: אַזְהָרָה לְבֵית דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא רַךְ לָזֶה וְקָשֶׁה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** And what is the reason that Rabbi Natan did not state that it is derived from that verse cited by Rabbi Elazar? Rabbi Natan explains that verse, which includes the term talebearer [*rakhil*], as a warning to the court that it should not be soft to [*rakh la*] this litigant and harsh to that one, but it must treat both sides as equals.

The Gemara then asks: *azhara lemotzi shem ra minlan* — what is the prohibitory *lav* that must be transgressed before lashes can be administered? Rabbi Elazar says: from *lo telech rachil b'amecha*. Rabbi Natan says: from *venishmarta mikol davar ra* — "guard yourself from every evil thing," interpreting *davar* as *dibbur*, speech, so that it means one must guard against spreading harmful words.

Why does Rabbi Elazar not use *venishmarta mikol davar ra*? Because that verse is needed for a teaching of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair: *al yeharher adam bayom*, a person should not entertain improper thoughts during the day lest they lead to a seminal emission at night — a teaching rooted in the contextual juxtaposition of that verse with the *parsha* of *ki yihye vecha ish asher lo yihye tahor mikre laila*.

Why does Rabbi Natan not use *lo telech rachil b'amecha*? Because that verse is needed to instruct a *beit din* not to be *rach* — gentle — toward one litigant and harsh toward the other. Rashi notes that *rachil* may be read as a compound: *rach li*, soft to me. This is an explicit *pasuk* against judicial partiality in conduct, not merely in judgment. As one student aptly observed, the verse *lo telech rachil b'amecha* follows immediately upon *lo ta'asu avel bamishpat, lo tisa pnei dal v'lo tehedar pnei gadol*, providing the contextual basis for reading it as an instruction to the court.

### Witness Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא אָמַר לְעֵדִים ״בּוֹאוּ וְהַעִידוּנִי״, וְהֵן מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ מֵאֲלֵיהֶם — הוּא אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן מֵאָה סְלָעִים. הִיא וְזוֹמְמֶיהָ מַקְדִּימִין לְבֵית הַסְּקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the husband did not say to witnesses: Come and testify for me that my wife committed adultery, but they testify for him of their own accord and are subsequently discovered to be liars, the husband is not flogged and does not give the one hundred *sela*, as he did not harm her. She and her conspiring witnesses are brought early to the place of stoning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִיא וְזוֹמְמֶיהָ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא: אוֹ הִיא אוֹ זוֹמְמֶיהָ מַקְדִּימִין לְבֵית הַסְּקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does it enter your mind to say that she and her conspiring witnesses are stoned? If she is liable to be stoned, they are not conspiring witnesses, and conversely, if they are conspiring witnesses, they are stoned and she is exempt. Rather, this must mean: Either she or her conspiring witnesses are brought early to the place of stoning. If they were telling the truth, she is stoned. If they conspired and offered false testimony, they are liable to be stoned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּלָא אֲמַר לְהוּ, הָא אֲמַר לְהוּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אַגְרִינְהוּ. לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂכּוֹר עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from the *baraita* that the reason the husband is not flogged or fined is that the husband did not tell them to testify, but if he told them to testify, although he did not hire them but merely persuaded them to testify that his wife had committed adultery as a betrothed woman, he is flogged and must pay the fine. This serves to exclude the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yehuda says: The husband is liable to the punishments of a defamer only if he hired witnesses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַתְיָא ״שִׂימָה״ ״שִׂימָה״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְשָׂם לָהּ עֲלִילוֹת דְּבָרִים״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״לֹא תְשִׂימוּן עָלָיו נֶשֶׁךְ״, מָה לְהַלָּן מָמוֹן, אַף כָּאן מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What is the reason of Rabbi Yehuda? Nowhere does the Torah explicitly state that the husband hired false witnesses. The Gemara answers that Rabbi Abbahu said: It is derived by a verbal analogy between the term placing, written with regard to a defamer, and the term placing, written with regard to the prohibition against charging interest. It is written here, with regard to a defamer: "And he place wanton charges against her" (Deuteronomy 22:14), and it is written there: "Neither shall you place upon him interest" (Exodus 22:24). Just as below, with regard to interest, the verse is referring to money, so too here, in the case of a defamer, it is referring to money, thereby indicating that the husband paid money in order to substantiate his false accusation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, וְכֵן תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף צִידוֹנִי בֵּי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: אָתְיָא ״שִׂימָה״ ״שִׂימָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Rav Yosef Tzidoni likewise taught in the school of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: It is derived from the verbal analogy between the term placing, written with regard to a defamer, and the term placing, written with regard to the prohibition against charging interest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: שְׂכָרָן בְּקַרְקַע, מַהוּ? בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, מַהוּ? שְׁנֵיהֶם בִּפְרוּטָה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma: According to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, if the husband hired the false witnesses with land instead of money, what is the *halakha*? If he hired them with less than the value of a *peruta*, what is the *halakha*? If he hired both witnesses with a *peruta*, what is the *halakha*? Since this *halakha* is derived from the case of interest, perhaps, like the prohibition against charging interest, it applies only with regard to money, rather than land, and only with money that is greater than the value of a *peruta*.

The *baraita* continues. If the husband did not tell the witnesses to come and testify — *lo amar l'eidim bo v'heiduni* — and they testified on their own accord, then *hu eino lokeh v'eino nosein me'ah sela*: he receives no lashes and pays nothing. He is effectively a victim. In that case, *v'hi v'zomemeha makdimin l'beis ha-skila* — either she is stoned (if the witnesses are truthful) or the *eidim zomemim* are stoned (if they are refuted).

The Gemara notes the implication: if he did tell them to come testify, even without hiring them, he bears liability. This brings us to Rabbi Yehuda's dissenting view: *eino chayav ad she-yiskor edim* — he is not liable unless he actually hired the witnesses. The Gemara's question is sharp: why should persuasion alone be insufficient? The principle of *ein shaliach l'dvar aveirah* — that there is no agency for transgression, since the agent should have heeded God rather than the sender — would seem to insulate the husband even if he hired them, let alone merely asked. The counter-intuition here is that by paying the witnesses, the husband transforms them into something closer to his employees and agents. The money concretizes his responsibility in a way that mere instigation does not.

Rabbi Abbahu derives Rabbi Yehuda's position through a *gezeira shava* of *sima sima*: the Torah uses the word *v'sam lah alilos devarim* — "and he placed upon her libelous words" — and elsewhere, *lo tasimun alav neshech* — "do not place interest upon him." Just as "placing" in the context of *neshech* involves monetary conduct, so too "placing" false words against her must be through money, through hiring. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak cites a parallel teaching of Rabbi Yose the Sidonian in the school of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai making the identical *gezeira shava*.

The Gemara then records a series of unresolved questions posed by Rabbi Yirmeyah. If he hired the witnesses with land (*karka*) rather than money — does that qualify? What if he hired them for less than a *perutah*'s worth? What if two witnesses together were hired for a single *perutah*, so that each individually received less than a *perutah*? These questions revolve around how formally the *gezeira shava* from *neshech* must be applied — whether the hiring must meet the technical minimum of *mammon* as defined in monetary law, or whether any act that concretizes the agency-like relationship suffices. The Gemara leaves these questions unresolved.

### Edge Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: הוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע עַל הַנִּישּׂוּאִין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, מַהוּ? עַל נִשּׂוּאֵי אָחִיו, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, Rav Ashi raised a dilemma concerning a defamer: If he defamed his wife with regard to their first marriage, what is the *halakha*? In other words, if a man married a woman, divorced her, remarried her, and subsequently defamed her by claiming that she had committed adultery during the period of betrothal before their first marriage, what is the *halakha*? Similarly, if he performed levirate marriage and then defamed her with regard to his brother's marriage to her, what is the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשׁוֹט מִיהָא חֲדָא. דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי יוֹנָה: ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ הַזֶּה״. לְזֶה — וְלֹא לַיָּבָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Resolve at least one of these dilemmas, as Rabbi Yona taught that the verse "And the father of the young woman shall say to the Elders: I gave my daughter to this man"** (Deuteronomy 22:16) serves to emphasize: I gave her to this man and not to the *yavam*, i.e., the brother of the original husband. Consequently, if one defames his *yevama* with regard to her original marriage to his brother, the unique *halakhot* of defamation do not apply.

Rav Ashi raises further questions about the scope of *motzi shem ra*. *Hotzi shem ra al nisuin harishonim mahu* — what if he is spreading false repute regarding the first marriage? The scenario is: he married her, divorced her, remarried her, and now — in the context of the second marriage — he is bringing false witnesses to claim she had committed adultery before the *nisuin* of the first marriage. The classic *motzi shem ra* is the morning after the wedding night, but surely the claim need not literally be made the next morning; the husband might simply explain that the witnesses only came to him now. Nevertheless, is a second marriage a separate enough context that the *parsha* no longer applies?

A related question: *al nisuei achiv mahu* — what if it is the brother making the claim? The entire *parsha* in the Torah is framed around the husband, and as the Gemara makes clear, if any other man did the identical thing — hired witnesses, spread evil repute — he would still be exempt from the penalties of *motzi shem ra*, even if the witnesses themselves would be *eidim zomemim*. What then of a *yavam*, a brother-in-law who inherits the marriage through *yibum*? If Reuven died and Shimon married Reuven's widow through *yibum*, and Shimon now claims that witnesses have told him she committed adultery prior to the *nisuin* with Reuven — is Shimon perhaps a continuation of Reuven's marriage, such that his claim falls within the *parsha*?

The tension here, as one student rightly noted, is fundamental. Once *chazal* have reconceived *motzi shem ra* as a judicial proceeding centered on witnesses rather than the concrete experience of the wedding night, the logic of limiting the *parsha* to the husband — and only to the original marriage — becomes less obvious. Why shouldn't any man who manufactures such a proceeding be equally liable? The Gemara is navigating the balance between reading the *parsha* as a normal judicial category capable of generalization and remaining tethered to the Torah's specific framing.

The Gemara resolves one of these questions. From the *baraita* taught by Rav Yona: *et biti nasati la'ish hazeh* — "I gave my daughter to this man" — *lazeh v'lo l'yavam*, to this one and not to the *yavam*. At least the case of the brother is excluded. The question of *nisuin harishonim* remains unresolved, and the Gemara notes *pashat mi'achada* — we can answer one question, not both.

### Pesuk Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבָּנַן וּמַאי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב? דְּתַנְיָא: כֵּיצַד הוֹצָאַת שֵׁם רַע? בָּא לְבֵית דִּין, וְאָמַר: פְּלוֹנִי, לֹא מָצָאתִי לְבִתְּךָ בְּתוּלִים. אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁזִּינְּתָה תַּחְתָּיו — יֵשׁ לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** In the course of the previous discussion, the Gemara mentioned a dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov. The Gemara asks: What is the opinion of the Rabbis and what is the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, referred to above (45b)? As it is taught in a *baraita*: How does the case of defamation proceed? It involves a situation where the husband came to the court and said to the father: So-and-so, I have not found indications of your daughter's virginity. If there are witnesses who testify that she committed adultery under his authority, i.e., while betrothed to him, she has a marriage contract of one hundred dinars.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁזִּינְּתָה תַּחְתָּיו יֵשׁ לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה מָנֶה?! בַּת סְקִילָה הִיא! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁזִּינְּתָה תַּחְתָּיו — בִּסְקִילָה. זִינְּתָה מֵעִיקָּרָא, יֵשׁ לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interrupts its citation of the *baraita*, as this last statement is very surprising: If there are witnesses who testify that she committed adultery under his authority, does she have a marriage contract of one hundred dinars? She is punished by stoning. The Gemara explains that this is what the *tanna* said: If there are witnesses who testify that she committed adultery under his authority, she is liable to receive the punishment of stoning. However, if she engaged in licentious sexual relations at the outset, before her betrothal, when she was still a single woman, she is merely guilty of deceiving her husband with regard to her virginity, and therefore she has a marriage contract of one hundred dinars, which is the standard marriage contract of a non-virgin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא (שֶׁ)שֵּׁם רַע [שֶׁ]אֵינוֹ שֵׁם רַע — הוּא לוֹקֶה וְנוֹתֵן מֵאָה סֶלַע, בֵּין בָּעַל וּבֵין לֹא בָּעַל. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֶמְרוּ דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁבָּעַל. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבָא אֵלֶיהָ״, ״וָאֶקְרַב אֵלֶיהָ״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its quotation of the *baraita*: If it was discovered that the bad name is not a bad name, i.e., the husband's accusation was false, he is flogged and gives her father one hundred *sela*, whether he had intercourse with her or whether he had not had intercourse with her. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: These matters were stated only in a case where he had intercourse with his wife before defaming her. The Gemara asks: Granted, according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, this is as it is written: "If a man take a wife and go in unto her"** (Deuteronomy 22:13), and: **"And when I came near to her,** I did not find in her the tokens of virginity" (Deuteronomy 22:14), as both expressions refer to sexual intercourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, מַאי ״וּבָא אֵלֶיהָ״, ״וָאֶקְרַב אֵלֶיהָ״? ״וּבָא אֵלֶיהָ״ — בַּעֲלִילוֹת, ״וָאֶקְרַב אֵלֶיהָ״ — בִּדְבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** However, according to the opinion of the Rabbis, what is the meaning of the phrases "and go in unto her," and "and when I came near to her," if the couple never engaged in intercourse? The Gemara explains that, according to the Rabbis, "and go in unto her" is referring to the wanton charges the husband leveled against his wife; "and when I came near to her" means that he came near with words, not intercourse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא מָצָאתִי לְבִתְּךָ בְּתוּלִים״. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, מַאי ״לֹא מָצָאתִי לְבִתְּךָ בְּתוּלִים״! לֹא מָצָאתִי לְבִתְּךָ כְּשֵׁרֵי בְתוּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Granted, according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, this is as it is written: "I did not find in your daughter the tokens of virginity"** (Deuteronomy 22:17), as Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov claims that the husband had relations with her and discovered that she was not a virgin. **However, according to the opinion of the Rabbis, what is the meaning of "I did not find in your daughter the tokens of virginity," if they did not have intercourse? The Gemara answers: The Rabbis explain that he means: I did not find for your daughter the fitness of virginity, i.e., I have discovered that she was unfaithful.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וְאֵלֶּה בְּתוּלֵי בִתִּי״. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, מַאי ״וְאֵלֶּה בְּתוּלֵי בִתִּי״? וְאֵלֶּה כְּשֵׁרֵי בְּתוּלֵי בִתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: Granted, according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, this is as it is written that the father replies: "And these are the tokens of my daughter's virginity"** (Deuteronomy 22:17). He presents a cloth that proves she was a virgin, in opposition to the husband's claim. **However, according to the opinion of the Rabbis, what is the meaning of "And these are the tokens of my daughter's virginity"?** The Rabbis answer that the father means: **And these are the** proofs of the **fitness of my daughter's virginity,** i.e., he either brings witnesses to counter the testimony of the husband's witnesses or provides some other proof that his daughter was a virgin at the time of her marriage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וּפָרְשׂוּ הַשִּׂמְלָה״. אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, מַאי ״וּפָרְשׂוּ הַשִּׂמְלָה״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses yet another question on the same lines: Granted, according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, this is at it is written: "And they shall spread the garment"** (Deuteronomy 22:17). The father brings the sheet on which the couple had intercourse and shows that it is stained with blood. **However, according to the opinion of the Rabbis,** who claim that a husband can defame his wife even if they have not engaged in intercourse, **what is the meaning of the phrase "And they shall spread the garment [*hasimla*]"?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: פָּרְשׂוּ מַה שֶּׁשָּׂם לָהּ. כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וּפָרְשׂוּ הַשִּׂמְלָה״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבָּאִין עֵדִים שֶׁל זֶה וְעֵדִים שֶׁל זֶה, וּבוֹרְרִין אֶת הַדָּבָר כְּשִׂמְלָה חֲדָשָׁה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: דְּבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן, שִׂמְלָה מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu said that the Rabbis interpret this expression as follows: They shall spread, i.e., examine, that which he placed against her [*sam la*]. In other words, they cross-examine the witnesses who testified against her, as it is taught in a *baraita*: "And they shall spread the garment"; this teaches that the witnesses of this husband come forward, and likewise the witnesses of that father come forward, and the court clarifies the matter like a new garment. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: The matters are as they are written, i.e., the verse refers to an actual cloth.

What is striking here is that the Gemara's own presentation reveals that Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov is not merely making a technical argument that intercourse is required while the rest of the *parsha* is about witnesses. He is reading *devarim kiksavam* — the *parsha* as written. He says *simla mamash* — a literal garment. The implication is that according to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, what happens is literally what the Torah describes: the husband makes a claim about the wedding night, the parents produce the bedsheet, and that is what is being contested. It is the *Rabbanan* who have transformed the entire *parsha* into a metaphor for judicial witness proceedings. This is an important observation because it means the gap between the two positions is not merely about whether intercourse is technically required — it is a gap about whether the entire structure of the case is literal or metaphorical.

This realization has a profound halachic dimension. Reading the *parsha* literally — as Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov appears to do in its fullest sense — creates a deeply troubling legal situation: the husband's unilateral claim, backed by no witnesses at all, would suffice to put the woman in jeopardy, with the burden of proof on her. *Chazal*'s reinterpretation transforms it into a normal judicial proceeding, which is precisely why the *Rabbanan*'s metaphorical readings, however strained, are halachically necessary.

### Kedarkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלַח רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר גִּיּוֹרֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַף עַל גַּב שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁחִלֵּק הַכָּתוּב בֵּין בִּיאָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ לְבִיאָה שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ לְמַכּוֹת וְלָעוֹנָשִׁין, אֲבָל מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע חִלֵּק: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּבְעוֹל שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְיוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Rav Ya'akov bar Giyyorei sent a message from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Although we have not found in the entire Torah that any verse distinguishes between sexual intercourse in a typical manner and sexual intercourse in an atypical manner, i.e., anal intercourse, with regard to flogging or any other punishment. However, in the case of the defamer, the Torah does distinguish in this manner, as the husband is obligated to pay the fine only if he had intercourse with his wife, even it was in an atypical manner, and he defames her by claiming that she had previously had intercourse in a typical manner with someone else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? אִי כְּרַבָּנַן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא בָּעַל. אִי כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי כְּדַרְכָּהּ בָּעֵינַן! אֶלָּא שְׁלַח רַב כָּהֲנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּבְעוֹל כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְיוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע בִּכְדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In accordance with whose opinion is this ruling of Rabbi Yoḥanan? If it is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, the husband should be liable even if he did not have intercourse with his wife. If it is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, we require both this, i.e., the act of intercourse between the husband and wife, and that, i.e., the woman's alleged act of intercourse with another man, to be performed in a typical manner in order for the laws of a defamer to apply, as Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov interprets the verses literally, meaning that the husband had intercourse with her and discovered she was not a virgin. Rather, Rav Kahana sent word in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan that the statement was as follows: He is obligated to pay the fine only if he had intercourse in a typical manner and defamed her with regard to intercourse performed in a typical manner, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.

Rav Yitzchak bar Rabbi Yaakov bar Giyori sent a ruling from Eretz Yisrael in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: even though throughout the entire Torah, when it comes to prohibited sexual acts, the Torah does not distinguish between *biah kedarkah* (vaginal intercourse) and *biah shelo kedarkah* (anal intercourse) — both being equally actionable for the purposes of lashes and capital punishment — the case of *motzi shem ra* is different. *Eino chayav ad she-yivo shelo kedarkah v'yotzi shem ra kedarkah*: he is not liable unless the intercourse was *shelo kedarkah* and the false claim is framed as if it were *kedarkah*.

Rashi notes that the phrase *she-yivo shelo kedarkah* means *afilu shelo kedarkah* — even if the intercourse was not the usual way. The Gemara will now explain the precise logic. The substance of the challenge is immediately apparent: according to whom does this ruling hold? If it follows the *Rabbanan*, then the whole case is about witnesses and what they testify — it has nothing to do with hymenal blood or the mode of intercourse. If it follows Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, then by his own logic *idi va-idi kedarkah beinan* — since the entire discovery is predicated on the absence of hymenal blood on the wedding night, both the intercourse and the claim must be about vaginal intercourse *kedarkah*, and the case of *shelo kedarkah* would never arise.

The Gemara therefore corrects: Rav Kahana sent a ruling in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — *eino chayav ad she-yivo kedarkah v'yotzi shem ra kedarkah*. He is not liable unless both the intercourse and the false claim are *kedarkah*. This follows Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov consistently: since the mechanism by which the husband discovers the claim is the absence of hymenal bleeding, both the intercourse that would have caused such bleeding and the claim about the absence of it must be *kedarkah*. The case requires that they had vaginal intercourse, that there was no blood, and that the claim is that she previously had vaginal intercourse with another man. The entire evidentiary structure revolves around the hymen.

The Rambam in *Hilchos Naarah Bisulah* (chapter 3, *halacha* 6) rules accordingly: *keitzad motzi shem ra* — the case begins with the husband's own experience: "I came to her and found no signs of virginity," after which he investigated and was then informed by witnesses that she had committed adultery after betrothal, and he brings those witnesses to *beit din*. The Rambam thus requires both elements: the concrete reality of intercourse and the judicial proceeding with witnesses — a kind of hybrid. Furthermore, the Rambam rules: *ba'alah shelo kedarkah v'amar lo matzasi betulim patur u-makin oso makas mardus* — if he had non-vaginal intercourse and claims she was not a virgin, he is exempt from the *motzi shem ra* penalties but receives *makas mardus*. As for hiring witnesses, the Rambam rules that he must have *heivi* the witnesses himself, but does not require that he actually hired them for payment. This raises a noteworthy question: if all he needed to do was produce them, could he in theory still have been acting in good faith, believing their testimony? The answer is not entirely clear, but presumably *heivi* assumes a knowing and malicious initiation.

## Ketubah Intro

### Bookend

With the conclusion of *motzi shem ra*, it is worth pausing to note the structural significance of what we have just completed. The opening *perek* of *Ketubot* began with the question of when a woman gets married and what happens if the husband makes a claim the morning after regarding her virginity — a scenario directly rooted in the *parsha* of *motzi shem ra* in the Torah. The motivation for the practice of marrying on Wednesday, so that the husband could appear before *beit din* the following Thursday while still in the heat of the moment, is precisely to replicate the conditions under which the *parsha* operates. The intervening material — about the fifty *shekel* payment, the *mohar* — prefigures the foundational financial structures of marriage. The daf now turns decisively from that framework toward what is in some respects the true subject matter of the *masechta*: the financial and legal relationship between husband and wife, structured around the *ketubah*.

### Paternal Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאָב זַכַּאי בְּבִתּוֹ בְּקִידּוּשֶׁיהָ בְּכֶסֶף, בִּשְׁטָר, וּבְבִיאָה. זַכַּאי בִּמְצִיאָתָהּ, וּבְמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וּבַהֲפָרַת נְדָרֶיהָ, וּמְקַבֵּל אֶת גִּיטָּהּ. וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת בְּחַיֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A father has authority over his daughter with regard to her betrothal through money, through a marriage document, or through intercourse. Likewise, a father is entitled to items she has found, and to her earnings, and to effect the nullification of her vows, i.e., a father may nullify his daughter's vows. And he accepts her bill of divorce on her behalf if she is divorced from betrothal before she becomes a grown woman. And although he inherits her property when she dies, e.g., property she inherited from her mother's family, he does not consume the produce of her property during her lifetime.

The *Mishnah* states: *ha'av zakai bivito b'kiddusheha b'kesef b'shtar uv'viah* — the father has legal rights over his daughter, whether she is a *ketanah* or a *naarah*, to betroth her through money, through a written document, or through an act of intercourse. That is, the father has the authority to give her in marriage by whatever of the three modes of *kiddushin* he chooses.

### Father's Control

Beyond the right to betroth her, the father is also legally entitled to *metziatah uma'aseh yadeha* — anything she finds and her earnings. He may annul her vows (*hafarat nedarim*), and if she is divorced while still a *nesuah* while an *arusah*, her *get* is given to him as her representative. The one thing the father is not entitled to, the *Mishnah* notes, is *peiros* — the earnings or produce from her property. If, for example, she inherits from a maternal grandfather with no surviving sons, the father does not collect the income from that inheritance. Importantly, this contrasts with the husband's rights once *nisuin* occurs.

### Husband's Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשֵּׂאת — יָתֵר עָלָיו הַבַּעַל, שֶׁאוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת בְּחַיֶּיהָ, וְחַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ, וּבְפִרְקוֹנָהּ, וּקְבוּרָתָהּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ עָנִי שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא יִפְחוֹת מִשְּׁנֵי חֲלִילִין וּמְקוֹנֶנֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If the daughter married, the husband has more rights and obligations than her father had before the marriage, as he consumes the produce of her property during her lifetime, and he is obligated to provide her sustenance, her redemption if she is captured, and her burial upon her death. Rabbi Yehuda says: Even the poorest man of the Jewish people may not provide fewer than two flutes and a lamenting woman, which it was customary to hire for a funeral, as these too are included in the duties of burial.

The *Mishnah* continues: once she enters into *nisuin* and passes from her father's control to her husband's, *yeseir alav ha-baal she'ochel peiros b'chayyeha* — the husband has all the rights the father had, and additionally he collects the produce of her property during her lifetime. This is the institution of *nichsei milog*: property that remains hers in title and returns to her upon dissolution of the marriage, but whose produce belongs to the husband throughout the marriage.

The transition from father to husband here reflects a model in which a woman's life between roughly the ages of three and twelve and a half is governed by her father's authority, and from *nisuin* onward by her husband's. The Mishnah frames this explicitly as the husband having *more* than the father — *yeseir alav* — not a different kind of authority but an extension of it. It should be noted, however, that *Masechet Kiddushin* fully recognizes the category of a *bogeret*, a young woman past the age of twelve and a half, who can betroth herself independently — a reality that creates a substantial window of personal autonomy entirely unaddressed by this Mishnah's framework.

### Obligations

The Mishnah also enumerates the husband's obligations. In exchange for these rights, he is *chayav b'mzonoteha* — obligated to feed and support her — *b'firkonah* — obligated to redeem her if she is taken captive — and *b'kvuratah* — obligated to pay for her burial. Rabbi Yehuda adds that even the poorest man in Israel may not stint on his wife's funeral below a bare minimum: *lo yifchos mishnei chalilin u-mkonenet* — he must provide at least two flutes and one wailer, the conventional minimum expression of public mourning.

## Father Pockets Money

### Money Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּכֶסֶף מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם אֵין כָּסֶף״, אֵין כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן זֶה, וְיֵשׁ כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן אַחֵר, וּמַנּוּ? אָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna indicates that a father receives the money of his daughter's betrothal. The Gemara asks: From where do we derive that the father is entitled to the money of her betrothal? Rav Yehuda said that the verse states, with regard to an emancipated Hebrew maidservant: "Then she shall go out for nothing, without money"** (Exodus 21:11), from which it is inferred: **There is no money for this master, i.e., her master does not receive money when she leaves him, but there is money for a different master, and who is this? Her father,** who also had authority over her, like her master. When she leaves her father's jurisdiction via betrothal, he is entitled to the betrothal money.

The Gemara now asks: *bechessef minalan* — from where do we derive that the father not only has the power to betroth his daughter but also pockets the money received? Rav Yehuda cites the verse *v'yatza chinam ein kesef* — when the father sells his daughter as a servant and the conditions of the sale are not fulfilled, she goes free without money. The analysis: *ein kesef la'adon zeh* — for this master, no money changes hands upon her release; *v'yesh kesef la'adon acher* — but there is another master who does receive money for her departure. *U-mano aviha* — and who is this other master? The father. The Gemara is explicit and unapologetic: the father's legal relationship to his daughter is described in terms parallel to a master's relationship to his servant. The Torah itself structures it that way, and the Gemara is simply making the implicit explicit.

### Father as Master

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְדִידַהּ! הַשְׁתָּא אָבִיהָ מְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ הַזֶּה״, אִיהִי שָׁקְלָא כַּסְפָּא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But one can say that the betrothal money should go to her, as one can derive from the verse that there is no money for this master but there is money for the woman herself when she leaves her father's domain. The Gemara refutes this suggestion: Now consider, her father accepts her betrothal, i.e., he can accept the money or document of betrothal from the man of his choice, as it is written: "I gave my daughter to this man"** (Deuteronomy 22:16). Can one then say that **she takes the money** when her father accepts the betrothal on her behalf?

Having established that the father functions as a kind of "master" who receives money when the daughter leaves his domain, the Gemara anticipates an objection: *v'eima ledidah* — perhaps while the father has the power to betroth her, the money goes to the daughter herself, not the father? The Gemara responds: *hatam aviha mikabel kiddusheha* — the father is the one who receives the *kiddushin*, as derived from *et biti nasati la'ish hazeh* — "I gave my daughter to this man." The very framing of the verse, in which the father is the active agent giving the daughter, establishes that the transaction runs through him and that the money is received by him.

### Ketanah vs Naarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי קְטַנָּה, דְּלֵית לַהּ יָד. אֲבָל נַעֲרָה, דְּאִית לַהּ יָד — אִיהִי תְּקַדֵּשׁ נַפְשַׁהּ וְאִיהִי תִּשְׁקוֹל כַּסְפָּא! אָמַר קְרָא: ״בִּנְעוּרֶיהָ בֵּית אָבִיהָ״ — כׇּל שֶׁבַח נְעוּרִים לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But one can say that this applies only to a minor, who does not have a hand, i.e., she is not legally competent to carry out transactions on her own behalf. However, in the case of a young woman, who does have a hand, she should betroth herself and she should also take her own betrothal money. The Gemara answers that there is a different exposition in this regard, as the verse states: "Being in her youth, in her father's house"** (Numbers 30:17), which teaches that **all gains that a daughter accrues in her youth, i.e., when she is a young woman, belong to her father.**

One might grant that this applies to a *ketanah*, a minor who has no independent legal agency at all. But what of a *naarah*, a girl who has reached the age of twelve and is to some degree her own person — perhaps she could both betroth herself and pocket the *kiddushin* money? The Gemara responds with a verse.

### Shevach Ne'urim

The Gemara refines further. *Amar kra bine'ureiha beit aviha — kol shevach ne'urim la'aviha*: while she is in her youth in her father's house, all the benefit and value of her youth (*shevach ne'urim*) belongs to her father. The Gemara notes, however, that this derivation will be examined further, as it does not yet fully satisfy the question. The underlying assumption being tested is that as a *naarah* she might have sufficient independent standing to act on her own behalf and retain the proceeds — and the verse is brought to foreclose that possibility. The discussion will continue on the following daf, where the Gemara will work through precisely how we know not only that the father controls the betrothal but that he is entitled to all the financial benefits that flow from his daughter's youth.
