# Sotah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Warning & God's Name

### Warnings & Erasure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְאַיְּימִין עָלֶיהָ וְכוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמְּאַיְּימִין עָלֶיהָ שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁתֶּה כָּךְ מְאַיְּימִין עָלֶיהָ שֶׁתִּשְׁתֶּה. אוֹמְרִים לָהּ: בִּתִּי, אִם בָּרוּר לָךְ הַדָּבָר שֶׁטְּהוֹרָה אַתְּ עִמְדִי עַל בּוּרְיִיךְ, וּשְׁתִי. לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מַיִם הַמָּרִים דּוֹמִין אֶלָּא לְסַם יָבֵשׁ שֶׁמּוּנָּח עַל בָּשָׂר חַי. אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם מַכָּה — מְחַלְחֵל וְיוֹרֵד, אֵין שָׁם מַכָּה — אֵינוֹ מוֹעִיל כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And they threaten her** in order that she admit her sin, to obviate the need to erase God's name. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from that which was taught in a *baraita* in the *Tosefta* (1:6): **In the same manner that they threaten her so that she will not drink, so too, they threaten her so that she will drink,** as **they say to her: My daughter, if the matter is clear to you that you are pure, arise for** the sake of **your clear** position **and drink.** If you are innocent you have nothing to fear, **because the bitter water is similar only to a dry poison placed on the flesh. If there is a wound** there, the poison will **penetrate and enter** the blood stream, but if **there is no wound there, it does not have any effect.** This teaches that the woman is warned not to drink if she is guilty, but if she is not guilty she is encouraged to drink. There is no mention of the latter in the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה. כָּאן — לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here** the mishna is referring to **before the scroll was erased,** and at that point the woman is warned only not to drink if she is guilty, so that the name of God will not be erased. **There** the *baraita* is referring to **after the scroll was erased.** Then she is warned that if she is innocent she should drink because if she now refuses to drink, it will turn out that the scroll was erased for no purpose.

The *gemara* opens on 7b with the phrase *uma'amin aleha* — the *beit din* impresses upon the *sotah* the gravity of what she is about to undergo. The *mishnah* had already stated that they warn her just as they warn witnesses in capital cases, and the reason given is *asur lishmo hagadol shenikhtav bikdusha shelo yimacheh al hamayim* — it is forbidden to erase God's name, which has been written in holiness, upon the water for no reason. The *megillas sotah* is inscribed with God's name, and the ink is then scraped into the bitter water. This is, in one sense, one of the most striking features of the *sotah* ritual: God is willing to have His name erased in order to restore *shalom bayit* to a couple. Nevertheless, erasing God's name carries a genuine *issur*, and so the *beit din* works strenuously to avoid bringing about that erasure unnecessarily.

### Drinking Paradox

The *gemara* then raises what appears to be a contradiction. A *baraita* from the *Tosefta* states that just as we scare her out of drinking, so too we scare her into drinking — *kederech shema'amin aleha shelo tishteh kach ma'amin aleha shetishteh*. This seems paradoxical: if the entire purpose of the warnings is to prevent the erasure of God's name, how can it also be our goal to encourage her to drink?

### Water as Medicine

The *baraita* resolves this by explaining that if she is certain of her innocence, she is told: *omrim lah bitti im barur lach hadavar shetehorab at omdi al boryich ushti* — if it is entirely clear to you that you are pure, stand firm and drink. The water, the *baraita* explains, is like a dry medicine placed upon raw flesh: *lefi she'ein mayim hame'arerim domim ela lesam yavesh shemunach al basar chai*. If there is a wound, the medicine dissolves into it and takes effect; if there is no wound, it does nothing at all. The implication is clear: if she is innocent, the water will have no effect and the ritual will demonstrate her innocence. If she is guilty, the ritual will run its course as designed. The *gemara* thus resolves the contradiction: there are two distinct stages of the ritual.

### Before/After Megillah

Before the *megillas sotah* has been erased, the goal is to encourage the woman to confess so that God's name need not be effaced at all. After the *megillah* has already been erased, that concern is moot — God's name is already gone — and it would now be wasteful and purposeless for her to refuse to drink. At that point, she is instead encouraged to drink: the water will prove her innocent if she is indeed innocent, and the erasure of God's name will have served its purpose. These are different encouragements suited to different moments in the proceeding.

## Judah & Reuben

### Aggadic Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר לְפָנֶיהָ וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוֹמֵר לְפָנֶיהָ דְּבָרִים שֶׁל הַגָּדָה, וּמַעֲשִׂים שֶׁאֵירְעוּ בִּכְתוּבִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, כְּגוֹן: ״אֲשֶׁר חֲכָמִים יַגִּידוּ וְלֹא כִחֲדוּ מֵאֲבוֹתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** the judge **says in her presence** matters that are not worthy of being heard by her and all her father's family in order to encourage her to admit her sin. The Gemara cites a *baraita* that details what was said. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The judge **says in her presence words of homiletical** interpretation **and** mentions **incidents that happened** to previous generations that are recorded **in the early** prophetic **writings. For example,** they expound the following verse: **"That wise men told and did not hide from their fathers"** (Job 15:18); this teaches that even during the time of the forefathers, there were people who admitted their sins despite the shame they incurred.

The *mishnah* had said that they tell her *devarim she'einam kedai lishmo'an* — things not fit to be heard — in order to move her emotionally. The *gemara* explains: *tanu rabbanan omer lefaneha devarim shel aggada uma'asim she'iru baksuvim harishonim* — they tell her *aggadot* and stories that occurred in earlier generations. The specific source cited is from *Iyov*: *asher chachamim yaggidu velo kichadu me'avosam* — that the wise of earlier times would tell of these things and not conceal them from their ancestors. As *Rashi* explains, these are things written in the *Torah* about events of ancient times — stories in which great and wise people were able to acknowledge their sins openly. The phrase *devarim she'einam kedai lishmo'an* refers to the fact that, under normal circumstances, one would not publicize these stories, as they could be embarrassing to figures of great stature whose full standing the tradition wishes to protect.

### Judah & Reuben

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה הוֹדָה וְלֹא בּוֹשׁ, מֶה הָיָה סוֹפוֹ — נָחַל חַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. רְאוּבֵן הוֹדָה וְלֹא בּוֹשׁ, מֶה הָיָה סוֹפוֹ — נָחַל חַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** For example, **Judah admitted** that he sinned with Tamar **and was not embarrassed** to do so, and **what was his end? He inherited the life of the World-to-Come. Reuben admitted** that he lay with his father's concubine Bilhah **and was not embarrassed,** and **what was his end? He** too **inherited the life of the World-to-Come.**

Two figures are given as examples. *Yehuda hoda velo bosh* — Judah admitted his sin and was not embarrassed. *Mah haya sofo? Nachal chayei olam haba* — and what came of it? He inherited a share in the *olam haba*. Similarly, *Reuven hoda velo bosh, mah haya sofo? Nachal chayei olam haba* — Reuben admitted his sin and was not embarrassed, and he too inherited the *olam haba*.

### Peshat & Derash

This passage is striking because the *gemara* here goes against what the tradition generally maintains in other contexts. As we know from Tractate *Megilla*, the rabbinic view — at least from the perspective of *Torah she-be'al peh* — tends to be that Judah and Reuben did not actually commit the transgressions that the *peshat* of the *Torah* seems to imply. When the *parshiyot* dealing with Reuben are read publicly, the *targum* is withheld, so that listeners will not be misled by the surface reading — for the *peshat* of the story of Reuben suggests that he literally violated his stepmother, while *Chazal* understand it as something considerably less severe. Yet here the *gemara* reads these stories according to their *peshat* in order to draw a moral lesson — that great people can admit wrongdoing. The reason these words are *she'einam kedai lishmo'an* is precisely because they are potentially embarrassing to figures who are elsewhere treated as towering *tzaddikim*.

This connects to the broader contemporary debate about approaching figures in *Tanach begova ha-einayim* — relating to biblical personalities as fully realized human beings — and how much that is permissible from the rabbinic perspective. The story of Judah and Tamar raises precisely this question: in the *peshat*, Judah says *tzadka mimeni*, acknowledging that Tamar was right and he was wrong. The *gemara* here appears to authorize reading the story as it reads, even if other contexts demand a more interpretive layer, in order to achieve the goal of inspiring the *sotah* to confess. The *Rema* in one of his *teshuvot* cites this passage as a proof that even embarrassing a *talmid chacham* can be part of what is legitimately permitted in the broader context of the *sotah* process — bringing people to confession and restoring *shalom bayit*.

### Reuben's Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רְאוּבֵן מְנָלַן דְּאוֹדִי? דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״יְחִי רְאוּבֵן וְאַל יָמֹת״, ״וְזֹאת לִיהוּדָה״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the source for Reuben's admission. **Granted, with regard to Judah we have found** a source **that he admitted** his sin with Tamar, **as it is written: "And Judah acknowledged them and said: She is more righteous than I"** (Genesis 38:26). Judah admitted that he was the one who had impregnated Tamar. **But from where do we** derive **that Reuben admitted** his sin? The Gemara answers: It is **as Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What is** the meaning of that **which is written** concerning Reuben and Judah in Moses' blessing of the tribes at the end of his life: **"Let Reuben live and not die in that his men become few"** (Deuteronomy 33:6), and immediately afterward, in the following verse, it is stated: **"And this for Judah,** and he said: Hear, Lord, the voice of Judah, and bring him in unto his people; his hands shall contend for him, and You shall be a help against his adversaries" (Deuteronomy 33:7). What is the connection between the blessing of Reuben and that of Judah, juxtaposed with the conjunction "and"?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל אוֹתָן שָׁנִים שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר, הָיוּ עַצְמוֹתָיו שֶׁל יְהוּדָה מְגוּלְגָּלִים בָּאָרוֹן, עַד שֶׁעָמַד מֹשֶׁה וּבִקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִי גָּרַם לִרְאוּבֵן שֶׁהוֹדָה — יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan says: **All those years that the Jewish people were in the desert, the bones of Judah,** which the Jewish people took with them from Egypt along with the bones of his brothers, **were rolling** around **in the coffin, until Moses arose and asked for compassion on** Judah's behalf. Moses **said before** God: **Master of the Universe, who served as the impetus for Reuben that he admit** his sin, through which he merited a blessing and was not excluded from the count of the twelve sons of Jacob (see Genesis 35:22)? It was **Judah,** as Reuben saw him confess his sin, and thereby did the same.

Having established that Judah admitted his sin, the *gemara* asks: *ela Reuven menalan de'hodi* — how do we know that Reuben admitted to having sinned? The proof comes from *Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* citing *Rabbi Yochanan*, who reads the connection between *yechi Reuven ve'al yamot* in Moses's blessing to the tribes at the end of *Devarim* and *vezot liYehudah*, which follows immediately. *Rashi* notes that the blessing of Judah is the only one that opens with the unusual word *vezot*, suggesting a textual linkage between Reuben's life-and-death fate and Judah's admission. The *gemara* understands this connection to mean that Reuben's fate depended upon Judah's own repentance and what it inspired.

### Judah's Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד: ״שְׁמַע ה׳ קוֹל יְהוּדָה״, עָל אֵיבְרֵיהּ לְשָׁפָא. וְלָא הֲוָה קָא מְעַיְּילִין לֵיהּ לִמְתִיבְתָּא דִרְקִיעָא, ״וְאֶל עַמּוֹ תְּבִיאֶנּוּ״. וְלָא הֲוָה קָא יָדַע מִשְׁקַל וּמִטְרַח בִּשְׁמַעְתָּא בַּהֲדֵי רַבָּנַן, ״יָדָיו רָב לוֹ״. לָא הֲוָה קָא סָלְקָא לֵיהּ שְׁמַעְתָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְהִילְכְתָא, ״וְעֵזֶר מִצָּרָיו תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately** after Moses prayed, the verse states: **"Hear, Lord, the voice of Judah"** (Deuteronomy 33:7). **His bones** then **entered** their **sockets [*shafa*],** and his skeleton was reassembled. **But** the angels still **did not elevate** him **into the heavenly study hall.** Moses then prayed: **"And bring him in unto his people"** (Deuteronomy 33:7), i.e., those in the heavenly study hall. This prayer was accepted, **but he** still **did not know** how **to deliberate** in Torah matters **with the** heavenly **sages.** Moses then prayed: **"His hands shall contend for him"** (Deuteronomy 33:7), meaning that he should have the ability to contend with them in study. But still **he was unable to draw** conclusions from **his discussion in accordance with the *halakha*.** Moses then prayed: **"And You shall be a help against his adversaries"** (Deuteronomy 33:7).

The *gemara* then describes what Judah suffered in the *midbar*. All forty years that *Bnei Yisrael* wandered in the desert, Judah's bones were rolling around in his coffin — *kol otan shanim she'hayu Yisrael bamidbar hayu atzmotav shel Yehuda mitgalglim ba'aron* — until Moses stood and prayed for him. Moses argued before God: *mi garam l'Reuven she'hoda* — who was it that inspired Reuben to do *teshuvah*? It was Judah. Thus Judah was rewarded not because of his own *teshuvah* alone, but because his character and his admission inspired Reuben to do *teshuvah* as well. That is why the next phrase in the blessing is *shema Adonai kol Yehudah* — God hears Judah's prayer and responds, releasing him from his posthumous suffering.

What was the precise nature of that suffering? *Rashi* here connects *evarai deshaffa* — his limbs were dislocated — to the *treifah* discussed in *Chullin* 52b, *buka de'atma deshaf biduchteh*, the hip socket displaced from the hip joint. The punishment of forty years of rolling bones was understood as a consequence of whatever connection existed between Judah and his conduct regarding Tamar — even as he had acknowledged his failing and been forgiven. *Tosafot* in the *gemara* raises the question of why Judah was still being punished for forty years, and *Rashi* connects it to a *niduy al tenai* — some form of conditional *cherem* — though this requires further investigation.

Beyond the rolling bones, Judah suffered additional penalties. *Velo hava ka'aylin lei lemtivta dirakia* — they would not admit him into the heavenly *yeshiva*. Even once he was admitted, *velo hava ka yada mishkal bishmayta bahadei rabanan* — he could not participate in the *shakla vetarya*, the back-and-forth of halakhic debate with the *rabbanim* in heaven. And then, *velo hava kasalik lei shemayta aliba dehilkhata* — he could not bring his *sugyot* to their proper halakhic conclusion. Moses's blessing *ve'ezer mitsarav tihyeh* — that the *Torah* should be a protection from his enemies — is read as the remedy for this final deficiency, enabling Judah to learn *sugyot aliba dehilkhata* at last.

### Reuben's Motivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא יְהוּדָה דְּאוֹדִי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֹא תִּישָּׂרֵף תָּמָר. אֶלָּא רְאוּבֵן, לְמָה לֵיהּ דְּאוֹדִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: חֲצִיף עֲלַי דִּמְפָרֵיט חֶטְאֵיהּ? כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיחַשְׁדוּ אֲחוֹהִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the propriety of admitting one's sins in public. **Granted,** with regard to **Judah,** it was proper **that he admitted** his sin in public, as he did so **in order that Tamar not be burned** innocently. **But why did Reuben admit** his sin in public? **But didn't Rav Sheshet say: I** consider one **who specifies his sins** in public to be **brazen,** as one who does so indicates that he is not embarrassed by his actions? The Gemara answers: The reason he admitted his sin in public was **in order that his brothers should not be suspected** of having committed the deed.

The *gemara* then distinguishes between Judah's case and Reuben's. *Bishlama Yehuda de'odi kehikhi delo tishraf Tamar* — Judah had an obvious reason to admit publicly: if he had not done so, Tamar would have been burned. But what motivated Reuben to confess? *Ve'amar Rav Sheshet*: *chatsifa alai demafrat chatatei kehikhi delo tishdu achohi* — he was concerned that if he did not publicly admit what he had done, his brothers would be suspected. Under normal circumstances, he might have gone to Jacob privately, but since others who knew something had happened would not know who was responsible, Reuben had to make a public declaration to clear his brothers' names.

### Public vs. Private

This leads to a broader principle. The *Rambam*, drawing on the *gemara*, distinguishes between *mitzvot bein adam lachaveiro* and *mitzvot bein adam laMakom* when it comes to the question of public versus private apology. For sins between a person and another person, one must publicize the apology, because another person was genuinely hurt and public acknowledgment is part of making it right. For sins between a person and God, it is preferable to keep the matter private — there is no reason to publicize a transgression of that kind, and doing so only adds another dimension of exposure without any remedial benefit. The case of Reuben illustrates that even when a public apology might magnify the problem in some ways, the concern of allowing others to remain under unjust suspicion can override that consideration.

## Breaking Ketubah

### Ketubah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמְרָה ״טְמֵאָה אֲנִי״ וְכוּ׳. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If** after the judge's warning **she says: I am defiled,** she writes a receipt for her marriage contract. The Gemara comments: **You** can **learn from this** mishna **that one writes a receipt** to serve as proof that a debt has been paid rather than tearing the promissory note. This matter is the subject of a dispute between the *tanna'im* in tractate *Bava Batra* (170b).

The *gemara* turns to the practical consequences if the woman admits to adultery during the *sotah* proceeding. *Im amart tameia ani* — if she says she is impure, *shomein sheovrim ketubatah* — the *gemara* infers that we break her *ketubah*. But what does *shovrim* mean here?

### Abbaye vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תְּנִי: ״מְקָרַעַת״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא ״שׁוֹבֶרֶת״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְבִין כְּתוּבָּה עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Teach** in the mishna differently. Rather than understanding that she writes a receipt, explain it to mean: **She tears** her marriage contract. **Rava said to him: But** the mishna **teaches** explicitly that **she writes a receipt. Rather,** to explain the mishna, **Rava said: We are dealing with a place in** which **they do not write a marriage contract,** as they rely on the rabbinical ordinance that all wives are entitled to the sum of a standard marriage contract upon divorce or being widowed, even if no marriage contract has been written. Because there is no marriage contract to tear, a receipt is written so that the man can prove that he no longer has a monetary obligation. However, generally, it is possible that the document would be torn, and no proof can be adduced from this mishna.

*Shma mina kotvim shovar* — one might think it means we write a receipt canceling the obligation of the *ketubah*. *Amar Abaye tani mikra* — Abaye says we should emend the *mishnah* to read *korei'a*, meaning we simply tear up the *ketubah* document itself. If a physical *shtar* is torn up, there is no way for the holder to claim the debt it represents, and the matter is closed. The question of which approach is better turns on where the burden lies: does the husband need a receipt proving the *ketubah* was discharged, or is it sufficient to simply destroy the document? *Amar lei Rava* — Rava objects that the *mishnah* says *shoveret*, not *koriat*, and one cannot simply amend a *mishnah*. Therefore the *mishnah* must be read as written.

*Ela amar Rava bimkom she'ein kotvim ketubah askinan* — Rava resolves the issue by saying the *mishnah* is discussing a case where no physical *ketubah* document was written at all. The *ketubah* obligation was undertaken orally. In such a case, there is no document to tear up, and so *shovrim* — breaking the *ketubah* — means writing some form of receipt or acknowledgment that the obligation has been discharged.

### No Document Cases

*Rashi* at the end of this passage offers two related scenarios in which a woman might claim her *ketubah* without a physical document. In a case of divorce, *mishenitagresha motzi'ah gittah ve'govah bah* — she produces the *get* itself as proof of the divorce and uses it to collect her *ketubah*. In a case of widowhood, *mevi'ah edei mitah ve'govah* — she brings witnesses who can testify to her husband's death, and on that basis she collects from the estate. The latter is a considerably more burdensome process, reflecting the general principle of *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah* — that the one seeking to extract money from another bears the burden of proof.

One can trace in these cases the historical development of the *ketubah* document itself. At an earlier stage, the *ketubah* was apparently an oral obligation, and the woman had to marshal independent evidence — witnesses to the divorce or death — in order to collect. The rabbis subsequently instituted the practice of writing a physical *ketubah* document precisely to ease this burden, enabling the woman to collect simply by presenting the *shtar* she held in her hand.

## Ritual Locations

### Eastern Gate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמְרָה ״טְהוֹרָה אֲנִי״ מַעֲלִין אוֹתָהּ לְשַׁעֲרֵי מִזְרָח.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But if** after the warning **she** maintains her innocence and **says: I am pure, they** would **bring her up to the Eastern Gate.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲלִין אוֹתָהּ?! הָתָם קָיְימָא! דְּמַסְּקִינַן לַהּ וּמַחֲתִינַן לַהּ כְּדֵי לְיַיגְּעָהּ. דְּתַנְיָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: בֵּית דִּין מַסִּיעִין אֶת הָעֵדִים מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּטָּרֵף דַּעְתָּן עֲלֵיהֶן וְיַחְזְרוּ בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Would **they bring her up?** **She is** already **standing there** in the Temple courtyard, as that is where the Sanhedrin sits. The Gemara answers: This teaches **that they** would **bring her up and** would **bring her down** repeatedly **in order to fatigue her,** with the hope that her worn-down mental state will lead to her confession. This was also done with witnesses testifying in cases of capital law, **as it is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Sanhedrin* 9:1): **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** In cases of capital law, the **court brings the witnesses from** one **place to** another **place in order to confuse them so that they will retract** their testimony if they are lying.

The *mishnah* states: *de'imar amart tehorah ani ma'alin osah lisha'arei mizrach* — if she says she is pure, they bring her up to the eastern gate, which is the *Sha'ar Nicanor*, the great gate standing between the *Ezrat Nashim* and the main *Azarah* of the *Beit Hamikdash*. The *Sha'ar Nicanor* featured two smaller doors on either side and fifteen steps ascending to it, and it constituted the central meeting point for those who had reason to be in the *Beit Hamikdash* but not in the *Azarah* proper — including women and other *Yisraelim* who gathered in the *Ezrat Nashim*.

The *gemara* asks: what does *ma'alin osah* mean — she was already there? The answer is that she had first been brought to the *Beit Din Hagadol*, the *Lishkat Hagazit*, which straddles the border between the *Azarah* and the *Ezrat Nashim*. From there she is brought up to the *Sha'ar Nicanor* for the actual drinking of the water.

### Walking Around

*Umakkifin osah kedei l'yage'ah* — they make her walk all around the *Har Habayit* to tire her out. A weary woman, if guilty, will be more inclined to admit it; and a woman who drinks the water despite exhaustion demonstrates more convincingly that she genuinely believes herself innocent. The *gemara* draws a parallel to a *baraita*: *Rebbe Shimon ben Elazar omer beit din masin es ha'edim mimakom lemakom kedei shetitaref da'atan aleihem veyachzeru bahen* — the *beit din* also makes witnesses in capital cases travel from place to place in order to wear them down. The goal is to create a genuine disincentive to false testimony — if you are lying, make it hard enough that you will reconsider — while simultaneously affirming that if you are telling the truth, it is a great *mitzvah* to do so regardless of the difficulty.

### Where She Drinks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁשָּׁם מַשְׁקִין אֶת הַסּוֹטוֹת וְכוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא סוֹטוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֶעֱמִיד הַכֹּהֵן אֶת הָאִשָּׁה לִפְנֵי ה׳״, מְצוֹרָעִין נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֶעֱמִיד הַכֹּהֵן הַמְטַהֵר וְגוֹ׳״, אֶלָּא יוֹלֶדֶת מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Because there,** at the Eastern Gate, **they give the *sota* women** the bitter water to drink, **and there the lepers and women who have given birth are purified. The Gemara asks: **Granted, the *sota* women** are given the bitter water to drink there, **as it is written: "And the priest shall stand the woman before the Lord"** (Numbers 5:18), and the Eastern Gate is directly opposite the Sanctuary, which is the area referred to as "before the Lord." Similarly, with regard to **lepers as well,** this is **as it is written: "And the priest that cleans** him shall set the man that is to be cleansed, and those things, before the Lord" (Leviticus 14:11). **But what is the reason that** a woman **who has given birth** must also be purified there?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתְיָין וְקָיְימָין אַקּוּרְבָּנַיְיהוּ, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם קָרֵב אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבָּיו. אִי הָכִי — זָבִין וְזָבוֹת נָמֵי! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְתַנָּא חֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ נְקַט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **If we say** it is **because** of the requirement **for** the women who have given birth to **come and stand over their offerings, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The offering of a person is brought only if he stands over it** while it is being sacrificed, and that is why they stand at this gate, which is as close to the sacrifice as they are permitted to be while they are ritually impure. **If that is so,** then the same *halakha* should apply to **men who experience a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zavim*] and women who experience a discharge of uterine blood after their menstrual period [*zavot*] as well.** They are also ritually impure while their offerings are sacrificed. Why would the mishna then specify women who have given birth? The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so, and the *tanna* cited one of them,** and the same *halakha* applies to all others in that category.

The *mishnah* establishes that the *sotah* drinks at the *Sha'ar Hamizrach*. The *gemara* asks: is this rule also applied to the *metzora* and the *yoledet*? For the *sotah*, the *pasuk* is explicit: *veha'emid hakohen es ha'ishah lifnei Adonai* — it must be done before God, meaning at the entrance of the *Beit Hamikdash*. For the *metzora*, the *pasuk* similarly says *veha'emid hakohen hamitaher* — the *kohen* must stand the *metzora* at the entrance of the *Mishkan*, and so the same location applies. The symbolism here is powerful: the *metzora* is excluded even from walled cities during the period of impurity and must dwell outside, and then the *taharah* process brings him back inside, to the very threshold of the sacred precincts.

What about the *yoledet*? The *gemara* asks: if the reason a *yoledet* must appear at the *Sha'ar Nicanor* is simply *de'ika mikaima akorbaneihu* — that anyone bringing a *korban* must be present for its offering — then by the same logic, *zavin vezavot* should also be listed. The *gemara* answers: *hachi nami vetana chada mineihu nakat* — indeed the same rule applies to them as well; the *mishnah* simply listed one representative example, the *yoledet*, but intended all *mechusarei kaparah*. *Tosafot* in multiple places in *Shas* grapples with the general problem of *mishnayot* that appear to make sweeping inclusive or exclusive statements but in practice admit of many exceptions. The principle of *tana veshayer* — the *tanna* left out cases — is one of the standard resolutions.

## Two Sotahs Rule

### No Two Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מַשְׁקִין שְׁתֵּי סוֹטוֹת כְּאַחַת, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא לִבָּהּ גַּס בַּחֲבֶירְתָּהּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִן הַשֵּׁם הוּא זֶה, אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֹתָהּ״ — לְבַדָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* in the *Tosefta* (1:6): **Two *sota* women are not given to drink simultaneously, in order that the heart** of each one **not be emboldened by the other,** as there is a concern that when one sees that the other woman is not confessing, she will maintain her innocence even if she is guilty. **Rabbi Yehuda says: This is not for** that **reason. Rather,** it is because **the verse states:** "And the priest shall bring **her [*ota*]** near and stand her before the Lord" (Numbers 5:16). Rabbi Yehuda explains his inference: The word "*ota*" indicates her **alone,** and therefore there is a Torah edict not to have two women drink the bitter water simultaneously.

The *gemara* introduces a *baraita*: *ein mashkim shtei sotot k'achat* — one cannot give two *sotot* the water simultaneously. The stated reason is *kedei shelo yehei libah gas bachaverta* — so that she should not become emboldened by the presence of another *sotah* alongside her. If two women who are both guilty were made to drink together, each might draw a kind of stubborn courage from the other and refuse to confess, even to the point of drinking the water despite her guilt.

One might observe from this rule that the *sotah* ritual is not deferred until the *regalim*. If it were, one would likely accumulate multiple *sotot* and be tempted to process them together — which is precisely what this rule prohibits.

### Rabbi Judah's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא, הָכְתִיב ״אֹתָהּ״? תַּנָּא קַמָּא — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּדָרֵישׁ טַעַם דִּקְרָא, וּמָה טַעַם קָאָמַר: מָה טַעַם ״אוֹתָהּ״ לְבַדָּהּ — כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא לִבָּהּ גַּס בַּחֲבֶירְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the first *tanna*, isn't it written "*ota*"?** The Gemara answers: **The first *tanna* is** actually **Rabbi Shimon, who interprets the reasons of** *halakhot* written in **verses, and he is saying: What is the reason? What is the reason** the Torah requires **her alone,** that each *sota* drink individually? **In order that the heart** of each woman **not be emboldened by the other.**

*Rabbi Yehudah omer lo min hashem hu zeh ela amar kra otah levadah* — Rabbi Judah disagrees with the reason. He says the rule is not grounded in the *sevara* of *libah gas bachaverta* at all. Rather, it is a *gezeirat hakatuv*: the *pasuk* simply says *otah levadah*, just her alone, and that suffices. This is a meaningful *machloket*: both *tanna'im* agree on the *halacha*, but they disagree about its source. The *tanna kama* treats the rule as a *sevara* — a logical inference — and Rabbi Judah treats it as a direct *pasuk*. As the *gemara* then clarifies, *vetana kama hinu Rabbi Shimon dedarish ta'ama d'kra* — the *tanna kama* is Rabbi Shimon, who agrees the *pasuk* says *otah levadah* but reads the *pasuk's* limitation as deriving from and coextensive with the underlying logic. This reflects a broader *machloket* between Rabbi Judah and Rabbi Shimon throughout *Shas* regarding *darshinan ta'ama d'kra* — whether understanding the reason behind a *pasuk* can expand or contract the parameters of the *halacha* derived from it.

### Fearful Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ רוֹתֶתֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between them?** Why should it matter if this *halakha* is due to a logical reasoning or due to a Torah edict? The Gemara answers: The difference **between them** is in a case where one of the women is **trembling** from fear. Since she has obviously not been emboldened by the presence of the other, Rabbi Shimon would allow her to be given to drink at the same time as the other.

*Mai beinaihu? Ika beinaihu rotetet* — what is the practical difference between the two positions? The case of a woman who is particularly fearful and trembling about the *sotah* water. According to Rabbi Shimon, whose reason is *libah gas bachaverta*, a *rotetet* woman would not be emboldened by the presence of another *sotah* — her extreme fear would override any such effect — and so two *sotot* could theoretically be processed together in such a case. According to Rabbi Judah, the *pasuk* says *otah levadah* regardless, and the prohibition is absolute. *Tosafot HaRosh* raises the question of what happens when one of the pair is *rotetet* but the other is not: even then, one might worry that the more composed woman will embolden the other, and therefore we remain *choshesh* in that case as well.

### Bundling Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרוֹתֶתֶת מִי מַשְׁקִין? וְהָא אֵין עוֹשִׂין מִצְווֹת חֲבִילוֹת חֲבִילוֹת. דִּתְנַן: אֵין מַשְׁקִין שְׁתֵּי סוֹטוֹת כְּאַחַת, וְאֵין מְטַהֲרִין שְׁנֵי מְצוֹרָעִין כְּאַחַת, וְאֵין רוֹצְעִין שְׁנֵי עֲבָדִים כְּאַחַת, וְאֵין עוֹרְפִין שְׁתֵּי עֲגָלוֹת כְּאַחַת, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין מִצְווֹת חֲבִילוֹת חֲבִילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if she is **trembling,** can the court **give her to drink** at the same time as the other? **But** there is a general principle that **one does not perform mitzvot in bundles,** as one who does so appears as if the mitzvot are a burden upon him, and he is trying to finish with them as soon as possible. **As we learned** in a *baraita*: **Two *sota* women are not given to drink simultaneously, and two lepers are not purified simultaneously, and two slaves are not pierced simultaneously, and two heifers do not have their necks broken simultaneously, because one does not perform mitzvot in bundles.** Accordingly, even Rabbi Shimon would agree that under no circumstances can a priest give two *sota* women to drink simultaneously. How, then, can the Gemara say that a trembling woman can be given to drink together with another *sota*?

The *gemara* asks: and can one really make a *rotetet* woman drink at all? And then it challenges even more fundamentally: *vaha ein osin mitzvot chavilot chavilot* — we have a principle that one may not bundle *mitzvot* together. Even without the specific concern of *libah gas bachaverta*, performing two *sotah* rituals simultaneously would violate this broader rule.

*Rashi* explains the reason for *ein osin mitzvot chavilot chavilot*: it appears as though the *mitzvot* are a heavy burden that one rushes to discharge. *Shenireh k'mo shehayah alav l'masoy umemaher l'farek masao* — it looks like someone hurrying to unload a heavy pack. Performing *mitzvot* hastily and in bulk conveys that they are merely obligations to be disposed of, rather than things of genuine value to be cherished. *Tosafot* in *Moed Katan* offers a different rationale: just as *ein me'arvin simcha b'simcha* — we do not hold a wedding on *Chol HaMoed* because we want each *simcha* to receive its own full attention — so too each *mitzvah* deserves its own dedicated focus and its own *simcha shel mitzvah*. One of the *Achronim*, *Rav Yosef Engel*, adds yet another conceptual lens: the principle of *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah* means that performing one *mitzvah* technically exempts one from another, and therefore bundling two *mitzvot* together may be not merely inadvisable but technically impossible.

### Bundling Examples

A *baraita* lists additional cases: *ein metaherin shtei metzoraim k'achas* — one cannot perform the purification ritual for two *metzoraim* simultaneously. *Ein rotzin shtei avadim k'achas* — one cannot pierce the ears of two *eved Ivri* together at the same ceremony. An *eved Ivri* who, after six years of service, declares *ahavti es adoni* and wishes to continue as a slave undergoes a formal ear-piercing, with the *pasuk* stating *va'avado l'olam*. A *machloket* exists between the *Chazon Ish* and the *Magen Avraham* about the nature of this: the *Chazon Ish* holds that even when the slave seeks to remain, the master may refuse, making it entirely a *reshut*; the *Magen Avraham* holds that once the slave actively wishes to be *nirtza*, it becomes obligatory for the master.

*Ein orfin shtei eglos k'achas* — one cannot perform two *egla arufa* rituals simultaneously. While it would be tragic indeed for a city to require two such rituals at once, if it did, each would have to be performed separately. The *baraita* concludes: *lefi she'ein osin mitzvot chavilot chavilot*. It is worth noting that all the examples in this *baraita* involve doing two instances of the *same* mitzvah at once — not two different *mitzvot* performed together. The concern is not that one cannot daven and shake a *lulav* simultaneously; it is the ceremonial repetition, the bundling of identical solemn procedures, each of which demands its own dedicated attention and process. The *eved Ivri* must be brought before the court, the *egla arufa* requires traveling out to a remote location — these are elaborate, ceremonial proceedings that cannot be rushed together.

### One vs. Two Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב כָּהֲנָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּכֹהֵן אֶחָד, כָּאן בִּשְׁנֵי כֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said, and some say** it was **Rav Kahana** who said: This is **not difficult. Here,** the second *baraita*, which says that it is prohibited to give two *sota* women to drink simultaneously because one does not perform mitzvot in bundles, is speaking **with regard to one priest. There,** Rabbi Shimon in the first *baraita*, who permits a trembling *sota* to be given to drink together with another *sota*, is speaking **with regard to two priests.** Since no individual priest is giving two women to drink simultaneously, mitzvot are not being performed in bundles.

*Amar Abaye ve'isayma Rav Kahana: lo kashya kan b'kohen echad kan bishnei kohanim* — the resolution is that a single *kohen* cannot administer the water to two *sotot*, but two *sotot* could theoretically be processed on the same day — perhaps even simultaneously — by two different *kohanim*, since in that case each *kohen* is performing a single, undivided act and there is no violation of *mitzvot chavilot chavilot*.

## Sotah Clothing

### Tearing Clothes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּהֵן אוֹחֵז בִּבְגָדֶיהָ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּפָרַע אֶת רֹאשׁ הָאִשָּׁה״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא רֹאשָׁהּ. גּוּפָהּ מִנַּיִן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הָאִשָּׁה״. אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וּפָרַע אֶת רֹאשָׁהּ״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַכֹּהֵן סוֹתֵר אֶת שְׂעָרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the priest grabs hold of her clothing** and pulls them until he reveals her heart, and he unbraids her hair. The Gemara cites the source for these acts. **The Sages taught:** The verse states: "And the priest shall stand the woman before the Lord **and uncover the woman's head"** (Numbers 5:18). From this verse **I have** derived **only** that he uncovers **her head; from where** do I derive that he uncovers **her body? The verse states: "The woman,"** rather than just stating: And uncovers her head. This indicates that the woman's body should be uncovered as well. **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states** specifically: **"And uncover her head"?** Once it has stated that he uncovers the woman, it is already apparent that she, including her hair, is uncovered. It **teaches that the priest** not only uncovers her hair but also **unbraids her hair.**

The *gemara* states: *vekohen ochez b'vgadeha* — the *kohen* holds onto her clothing. The procedure described in the *mishnah* involves tearing open her garments and then securing them with an Egyptian rope above her breasts. The *gemara* asks about this in relation to concerns of *hirhurin* — inappropriate sexual thoughts — that the ritual might occasion.

### Stoning Clothes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה לִבָּהּ וְכוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה חָיֵישׁ לְהִרְהוּרָא, וְרַבָּנַן לָא חָיְישִׁי? וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ, דְּתַנְיָא: הָאִישׁ, מְכַסִּין אוֹתוֹ פֶּרֶק אֶחָד מִלְּפָנָיו, וְהָאִשָּׁה, שְׁנֵי פְּרָקִים — אֶחָד מִלְּפָנֶיהָ וְאֶחָד מִלְּאַחֲרֶיהָ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ עֶרְוָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הָאִישׁ נִסְקָל עָרוֹם, וְאֵין הָאִשָּׁה נִסְקֶלֶת עֲרוּמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues by citing that **Rabbi Yehuda says: If her heart was** attractive he would not reveal it, and if her hair was attractive he would not unbraid it. The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that it is prohibited to uncover an attractive woman, **is concerned about** onlookers having sexual **thoughts, and the Rabbis,** who permit it, **are not concerned** about this? **But we have heard the opposite from them, as it is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Sanhedrin* 9:6): Although **a man** condemned to stoning is stoned unclothed, the court **covers him** with **one** small **piece** of material **in front of him,** to obscure his genitals, and they cover **a woman with two** small **pieces** of material, **one in front of her and one behind her, because all of her** loins are **nakedness,** as her genitals are visible both from the front and from the back. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say: A man is stoned** while **naked, but a woman is not stoned** while **naked,** but fully clothed. Apparently, Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned that the onlookers seeing the woman unclothed will lead to sexual thoughts, but the Rabbis are concerned about this.

*Lemimra d'Rabbi Yehuda chaish l'hirhur* — is Rabbi Judah concerned about *hirhur* while the *Rabbanan* are not? The *gemara* notes that this seems to be inverted from what we know elsewhere. A *baraita* states: when a man is executed by *skilah*, he is covered with *perek echad milfanav* — a single cloth in front. A woman, however, receives *shenai perakim*, one in front and one behind, *mipnei shekulah ervah* — and as *Rashi* explains, this is because *beit haboshet nireh mishenei tzedadin*, a woman's genitalia are exposed from two directions, requiring coverage on both sides. But in that ruling, the woman's entire torso would still be uncovered — and that is the position attributed to *Rabbi Yehuda*. *Vachachamim omrim ha'ish niskal arom ve'ein ha'ishah niskalet aromah* — the *Rabbis*, by contrast, hold that a man is stoned completely naked while a woman may not be stoned naked at all. So it appears that Rabbi Judah is *less* concerned about exposure and the *Rabbanan* are *more* concerned — the opposite of what one might have expected from the *sotah* context.

### Priestly Thoughts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הָכָא טַעְמָא מַאי — שֶׁמָּא תֵּצֵא מִבֵּית דִּין זַכָּאִית, וְיִתְגָּרוּ בָּהּ פִּרְחֵי כְהוּנָּה. הָתָם — הָא מִסְתַּלְּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: What is the reason here,** with regard to a *sota*, that Rabbi Yehuda is concerned? **Perhaps** the *sota* **will leave the court** having been proven **innocent, and the young priests** in the Temple who saw her partially naked **will become provoked by** the sight of **her. There,** in the case of a woman who is stoned, **she departs** from this world by being stoned and there is no concern for sexual thoughts.

*Amar Rava hacha ta'ama mai shema teitzi mibeit din zakai veyigaru bah pirchei kehuna* — Rava explains the concern in the *sotah* case differently. The worry is not about inappropriate thoughts during the ritual itself, but about afterward: if she leaves the *beit din* proven innocent, the young *kohanim* who are *metzuyin ba'azarah* — present in the *azarah* — will have seen her in a state of exposure and may develop inappropriate thoughts about her. An interesting related issue in later *sugyot* concerns whether a *kohen hedyot* who is not actively performing the *avodah* may remain in the *azarah* wearing *bigdei kehuna* if the *avneit* is woven of *shatnez*, and various *Achronim* address how to navigate the ensuing questions.

### Yetzer Hara Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: אָתֵי לְאִיגָּרוֹיֵי בְּאַחְרָנְיָיתָא — הָאָמַר רָבָא: גְּמִירִי דְּאֵין יֵצֶר הָרָע שׁוֹלֵט אֶלָּא בְּמַה שֶּׁעֵינָיו רוֹאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And if you would say** that the fact that she is killed is irrelevant to their sexual thoughts, as the onlookers **will be provoked with regard to other** women, this is not a concern. As **didn't Rava say:** It **is learned** as a tradition **that the evil inclination controls only that which** a person's **eyes see.**

*Vachi teima asi l'itgaru bah acharinsa* — but one might argue that even if she leaves fully clothed, these *kohanim* might pursue her anyway, so what is accomplished by keeping her clothed? *Amar Rava gemiri de'ein yetzer hara sholeit ela bamah she'einav ro'os* — Rava answers with a received principle: the *yetzer hara* has power only over what one's eyes have actually seen. A person does not develop inappropriate thoughts about someone they have not seen. *Rashi* explains the concern more precisely: the worry is that seeing her could cause these *kohanim* to entertain inappropriate thoughts about other women — thoughts kindled by what they have witnessed. *Rava* responds that this is not how the *yetzer hara* operates: one's illicit impulse is directed toward the specific person one has seen, not transferred toward others not seen.

### Beautiful Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא לַעֲבֵיד בַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי — אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״ — בְּרוֹר לוֹ מִיתָה יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say that two** forms of chastening, both stoning and humiliation, **should be done with her, Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** The **verse states: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18), teaching that even with regard to a condemned prisoner, **select a good,** i.e., a compassionate, **death for him.** Therefore, when putting a woman to death by stoning, she should not be humiliated in the process.

*Vachi teima la'avi bei tartei* — the *gemara* then asks: why not do both, that is, expose her in public *and* execute her if she is found guilty? *Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabba bar Avuha amar kra veahavta lere'acha kamocha beror lo mita yafa* — the principle of *mita yafa*, choosing the most appropriate death, derived from the verse *veahavta lere'acha kamocha*, governs all four *mitot beit din*. The *Rabbis* apply this *derasha* throughout *Sanhedrin* to scale back what the *Torah's* language might seem to imply and to ensure that capital punishment is carried out in a manner that preserves the physical integrity of the human body. Professor Lorberbaum has argued that the core rabbinic principle underlying capital punishment is the concept of *tzelem Elokim*: since the human form bears some representation of the divine, execution must be performed in a way that does not deform or despoil the body. *Mita yafa* thus means not necessarily the most pleasant death, but the death that leaves the body most intact.

One might ask: even granting that the woman who drinks the water and is found guilty deserves her punishment, does she deserve additional public humiliation? This is genuinely difficult. One consideration is that even a woman ultimately innocent of adultery has still violated her husband's explicit instructions by entering into *yichud* with the suspected man, with witnesses able to testify to it — so in some sense she has betrayed a responsibility of her marriage. But for a woman who is guilty, there remains the question of *mita yafa* placing a limit on how much additional shame can be imposed alongside the physical punishment.

### Pain vs. Shame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: בִּזְיוֹנֵיהּ עֲדִיף לֵיהּ טְפֵי מִצַּעְרָא דְגוּפֵיהּ, וּמָר סָבַר: צַעְרָא דְגוּפֵיהּ עֲדִיף לֵיהּ טְפֵי מִבִּזְיוֹנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that** the statement **of Rav Naḥman is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** and according to Rabbi Yehuda there is no mitzva to select a compassionate death. The Gemara refutes this: **No,** it may be **that everyone agrees with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, and here they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds:** Minimizing **one's degradation is preferable to him than** minimizing **his physical pain.** Therefore, the Rabbis view the more compassionate death as one without degradation, even if wearing clothes will increase the pain of the one being executed, as the clothes will absorb the blow and prolong death. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds that** minimizing **physical pain is preferable to a person than** minimizing **his degradation,** and therefore the one being executed prefers to be stoned unclothed, without any chance of the clothing prolonging the death, although this adds to the degradation.

*Lima deRav Nachman tannai hi* — the *gemara* asks whether *Rav Nachman*'s principle is itself subject to a *tannaic* dispute. *Mar savar bizyona adif lah tfei mitza'ara degufa* — one position holds that public embarrassment is preferable to physical pain, so a woman being executed should be unclothed, which causes the stones to strike more directly and kill her more quickly, reducing physical suffering even at the cost of greater shame. *Umar savar tza'ara degufa adif lei tfei mibizyonei* — the other position holds that physical pain is the greater burden, and therefore it is better to clothe the condemned person, reducing the velocity of death even if that means greater physical suffering, in order to spare them the humiliation of nakedness. Both positions invoke *mita yafa*; they simply disagree about what that requires when shame and pain pull in opposite directions.

### Rope & Clothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה מְכוּסָּה לְבָנִים וְכוּ׳. תָּנָא, אִם הָיוּ שְׁחוֹרִים נָאִים לָהּ — מְכַסִּין אוֹתָהּ בְּגָדִים מְכוֹעָרִים. הָיוּ עָלֶיהָ כְּלֵי זָהָב וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא, הַשְׁתָּא נַוּוֹלֵי מְנַוֵּויל לַהּ, הָנֵי מִיבַּעְיָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: בְּהָנֵי אִית לַהּ בִּזָּיוֹן טְפֵי, כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: שָׁלִיחַ עַרְטִיל וְסָיֵים מְסָאנֵי. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵבִיא חֶבֶל וְכוּ׳. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא מֵרַב הוּנָא: חֶבֶל הַמִּצְרִי מַהוּ שֶׁיְּעַכֵּב בְּסוֹטָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **she was dressed in white** garments, he would cover her with black garments. A Sage **taught: If black** garments **are becoming to her,** then **she is covered in unsightly garments.** The mishna teaches: If **she was wearing gold adornments** or other jewelry, they are removed from her. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious? Now** that the priest **renders her unattractive** by uncovering her and dressing her in unsightly garments, **is it necessary** to teach that they remove **these** adornments from her? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **with these** adornments on her, **she has more degradation, as people say** in a known aphorism: **Undressed, naked, and wearing shoes.** This means that a naked person who wears shoes emphasizes the fact that he is naked. Perhaps one would think that by a *sota* wearing jewelry, her nakedness is emphasized and her degradation is amplified. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that this is not so. The mishna continues: **And afterward** the priest **would bring** an Egyptian **rope,** and he would tie it above her breasts. **Rabbi Abba raised a dilemma before Rav Huna: What is** the *halakha* as to whether the lack of **an Egyptian rope will preclude** the performance of the rite **with regard to a *sota*?**

The *gemara* then addresses what happens if her clothes are particularly striking. *Im hayu shechorim na'in la mechassin ota bivgadim mecho'arim* — if she looks particularly attractive in black garments, they dress her in ugly or coarse clothing instead, so as not to attract attention or inspire *hirhurim* in those who see her. Similarly, *hayu aleha klei zahav* — if she is wearing gold jewelry, is that removed? One might think that jewelry would make it even *more* embarrassing for her — as the folk saying goes, *shlach artil uremi masanei*, a person walking around naked but wearing shoes is somehow more embarrassing than being entirely undressed, because the incongruity itself is humiliating. A woman going through the *sotah* ritual in her finest jewelry, stripped of her dignity but still wearing her gold, might be even more shamed by that contrast. The *gemara* therefore teaches that we do not apply that reasoning, and the jewelry is removed.

Then, *achar kach mevi chevel hamitzri* — the *kohen* brings the Egyptian rope and ties it above her breasts so that her clothing, though torn open, does not fall down entirely. The *Yerushalmi* explains the term *chevel mitzri* either because her conduct resembled *ke-ma'aseh eretz Mitzrayim*, or alternatively because *chevel matzui* — a common rope woven from palm fronds — was the standard coarse rope of the region.

### Egyptian Rope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם שֶׁלֹּא יִשָּׁמְטוּ בְּגָדֶיהָ מֵעָלֶיהָ הוּא, וּבְצִלְצוֹל קָטָן נָמֵי סַגִּי, אוֹ דִילְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר מָר ״הִיא חָגְרָה לוֹ בְּצִלְצוֹל, לְפִיכָךְ כֹּהֵן מֵבִיא חֶבֶל הַמִּצְרִי וְקוֹשֵׁר לָהּ לְמַעְלָה מִדַּדֶּיהָ״ — מְעַכֵּב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵבִיא חֶבֶל הַמִּצְרִי וְקוֹשְׁרוֹ לָהּ לְמַעְלָה מִדַּדֶּיהָ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִשָּׁמְטוּ בְּגָדֶיהָ מֵעָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Does any means of tying suffice? Perhaps the primary function of the rope **is so that her clothes will not fall off her, and** therefore even **a small ribbon [*tziltzul*] would also suffice. Or, perhaps** the rope is used **because** of what **the Master said: She girded** herself **with** a comely **ribbon** when she committed her transgression, and **therefore the priest brings** specifically **an Egyptian rope,** which is coarse, **and ties it above her breasts.** If that is the case, then the Egyptian rope should be **indispensable.** Rav Huna **said to him: You learned** the answer to this dilemma in a *baraita* that teaches: **And afterward** the priest **would bring an Egyptian rope** and he would tie it above her breasts, **so that her clothes would not fall off her.** The *baraita* states that the use of an Egyptian rope is primarily for holding up her clothing, and therefore use of specifically Egyptian rope is not essential.

A question is posed by *Rav Abba* to *Rav Huna*: is the *chevel hamitzri* indispensable to the *sotah* ritual — *me'akev* — or is it merely one way to accomplish the necessary task? If its purpose is simply *shelo yishmetu vgadeha me'aleha* — to prevent her clothes from falling completely off — then any cord would suffice, even a small one. But if the specific Egyptian rope is required because *hi chagra lo bitziltzul lefichach kohen mevi chevel hamitzri* — she girded herself with a fancy belt to entice her lover, and therefore the *kohen* must bring a qualitatively different material in its place — then it would be *me'akev*. The *gemara* resolves this from the *baraita* itself, which explains the reason as *kedei shelo yishmetu vgadeha me'aleha* — purely functional, preventing the clothing from falling — and therefore the specific Egyptian rope is not *me'akev*, and any comparable cord would do.

*Tosafot* raises the question of where the Egyptian rope is purchased from — the *Yerushalmi* indicates it comes from *misheyarei halishka*, purchased from public communal funds. *Tosafot* then extends the question: by the same token, are the sword used for *sayif* and the cloth used for *chenek* also purchased from public funds? In other words, how far does the parallel between *sotah* and capital punishment extend when it comes to the provision of materials?

## Witnesses to Ritual

### All vs. Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הָרוֹצֶה לִרְאוֹת בָּהּ יִרְאֶה וְכוּ׳. הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא. אָמְרַתְּ: כָּל הָרוֹצֶה לִרְאוֹת בָּהּ רוֹאֶה, אַלְמָא לָא שְׁנָא גַּבְרֵי וְלָא שְׁנָא נְשֵׁי, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים מוּתָּרוֹת לִרְאוֹתָהּ: נָשִׁים — אִין, אֲנָשִׁים — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And anyone who desires to watch her may watch,** except for her slaves and maidservants, who are not permitted to watch because her heart is emboldened by them. And all of the women are permitted to watch her. The Gemara comments: **This** matter **is itself difficult,** as there is an internal contradiction in the mishna. First **you say: And anyone who desires to watch her may watch. Apparently,** there **is no difference** whether the onlookers are **men and** there **is no difference** whether they are **women;** all are permitted to observe the rite. **And then** the mishna **teaches: And all of the women are permitted to watch her,** which indicates **women, yes,** they may watch her, but **men, no,** they may not.

The *mishnah* appears to contradict itself. First it states *vechol harotzeh liros ba yireh* — anyone who wishes to see may come and see, implying no distinction between men and women. Then it states *kol hanashim mutarot lirosa* — women are permitted to see, implying that men are excluded. *Abbaye* tries to resolve this by saying *targama anashim* — read the first clause as referring to men: even the first inclusive-sounding statement is actually restricted to men who choose to attend. But *Rava* objects: the *mishnah* says *kol harotzeh liros ba yireh* — *whoever* wishes, which is broad language that cannot be limited to men alone.

### Required Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תַּרְגְּמַהּ אֲנָשִׁים. אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא ״כָּל הָרוֹצֶה לִרְאוֹת בָּהּ רוֹאֶה״ קָתָנֵי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל הָרוֹצֶה לִרְאוֹת בָּהּ רוֹאֶה, לָא שְׁנָא גַּבְרֵי וְלָא שְׁנָא נְשֵׁי. וְנָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת לִרְאוֹתָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִוַּסְּרוּ כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂינָה כְּזִמַּתְכֶנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Interpret** the first statement, which permits all people to observe the *sota*, as pertaining **to women,** but men may not be onlookers. **Rava said to him: But** it **teaches** in that first statement that **anyone who desires to watch her may watch,** and one cannot limit this to women. **Rather, Rava said: Anyone who desires to watch her may watch,** there **is no difference** whether the onlookers are **men** and there **is no difference** whether they are **women. And** the next clause of the mishna teaches that **women are obligated to watch her, as is stated:** "Thus will I cause lewdness to cease out of the land, **that all women may be chastened not to do after your lewdness"** (Ezekiel 23:48).

*Ela amar Rava* — Rava resolves the contradiction differently. *Kol harotzeh liros ba yireh lo shena gavrei velo shena nashei* — anyone, male or female, who wishes to attend may do so. But *venashim chayavot lirosa* — women are *obligated* to come and witness the ritual. The source is *shene'emar venisru kol hanashim velo taasena kezimaschena* — from *Yechezkel*, the *pasuk* states that all women must be chastened and not act in the manner of *zimah*, inappropriate sexual conduct. The *sotah* ritual thus serves an educational and deterrent function for women specifically: they are required to witness it so that they will not engage in similar behavior. Men may attend if they choose; women must attend. The *kohanim* present, as discussed earlier, present a distinct consideration given the potential for *hirhurin*.

## Measure for Measure

### Midah K'neged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּמִדָּה שֶׁאָדָם מוֹדֵד — בָּהּ מוֹדְדִין לוֹ. הִיא קִשְּׁטָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ לַעֲבֵירָה — הַמָּקוֹם נִוְּולָהּ. הִיא גִּלְּתָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ לַעֲבֵירָה — הַמָּקוֹם גִּלָּה עָלֶיהָ. בַּיָּרֵךְ הִתְחִילָּה בַּעֲבֵירָה תְּחִילָּה וְאַחַר כָּךְ הַבֶּטֶן — לְפִיכָךְ תִּלְקֶה הַיָּרֵךְ תְּחִילָּה וְאַחַר כָּךְ הַבֶּטֶן, וּשְׁאָר כָּל הַגּוּף לֹא פָּלַט.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna teaches lessons that can be derived from the actions and treatment of a *sota*. **With the measure that a person measures, he is measured with it.** For example, **she,** the *sota*, **adorned herself to** violate **a transgression, the Omnipresent** therefore decreed that **she** be **rendered unattractive; she exposed herself for** the purpose of violating **a transgression,** as she stood in places where she would be noticed by potential adulterers, so **the Omnipresent** therefore decreed that **her** body be **exposed** publicly; **she began her transgression with** her **thigh and afterward with** her **stomach, therefore the thigh is smitten first and then the stomach, and the rest of all** her **body** does **not escape** punishment.

The *mishnah* at the close of the *daf* articulates the principle of *midah keneged midah* in the *sotah* ritual: *bemidah she'adam moded bah modedin lo* — the measure by which a person acts is the measure that is applied back to them. *Hi kishtah atzmah la'aveirah, hamakom nivalah* — she adorned herself for her sin, and God degrades her. *Hi giltah es atzmah la'aveirah, hamakom gilah aleiha* — she revealed herself for her transgression, and God reveals her.

### Order of Harm

*Beyerach hitchilah ba'aveirah techilah ve'achar kach habeten* — she began her sin with her thigh — understood as a reference to the genitalia — and then her womb became implicated, as the ultimate concern is the possibility of pregnancy from the adulterous relationship. *Lefichach palkah hayerech techilah ve'achar kach habeten* — and therefore the punishment follows the same order: the thigh first, then the womb. *Veshear kol haguf lo palat* — the rest of the body is unaffected; the *sotah* water acts only upon those parts of the body that were instruments of the sin. The *gemara* will go on from here to examine the precise relationship between the *psukim* and this ordering in considerable detail, but that belongs to the *sugyot* that follow.
