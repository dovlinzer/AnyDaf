# Menachot 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Minimizing Chilul Shabbos

### Rabbi Yishmael's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: עוֹמֶר הָיָה בָּא בַּשַּׁבָּת מִשָּׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, וּבַחוֹל מֵחָמֵשׁ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד שַׁבָּת וְאֶחָד חוֹל מִשָּׁלֹשׁ הָיָה בָּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yishmael says:** When the day of the sacrifice of the ***omer*** meal offering **would** occur **on Shabbat,** the labors performed that would otherwise be prohibited were kept to a minimum, and the one-tenth of an ephah of flour that was **brought** as an offering was processed **from three *se'a*** of reaped barley. **And** if it occurred **during the week,** the flour was processed **from five** *se'a* of reaped barley. **And the Rabbis say: Both on Shabbat and** during the **week,** the *omer* offering **would come from three** *se'a* of reaped barley.

The daf opens in the middle of a sustained discussion about *chilul Shabbos* in the context of Temple service. The *mishnah* had presented Rabbi Yishmael's position regarding the *omer* offering: when the *omer* falls on Shabbos, one should minimize the degree of Shabbos violation involved in its preparation. The *gemara* now attempts to identify other *tanna'im* who share an analogous approach — that even when Shabbos may legitimately be violated for certain *mitzvos* or Temple needs, one ought to do so in the way that minimizes the violation. The *gemara* frames this as a series of comparisons, each one an opportunity to examine not merely whether the positions are substantively identical, but what different types of calculations might govern the question of how much violation is warranted.

### Rava's Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קָסָבַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, עִשָּׂרוֹן מוּבְחָר בְּלָא טִירְחָא אָתֵי מֵחָמֵשׁ, בְּטִירְחָא אָתֵי מִשָּׁלֹשׁ. בַּחוֹל מַיְיתִינַן מֵחָמֵשׁ, דְּהָכִי שְׁבִיחָא מִילְּתָא. בְּשַׁבָּת – מוּטָב שֶׁיַּרְבֶּה בִּמְלָאכָה אַחַת בְּהַרְקָדָה, וְאַל יַרְבֶּה בִּמְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Yishmael holds** that **a select tenth** of an ephah of flour can come **without exertion from five** *se'a*, and **with exertion from three.** Therefore, **on a weekday we** reap and **bring** flour **from five** *se'a*, **as this** produces **a better** final **product,** since only the highest-quality flour of each *se'a* is selected. **On Shabbat,** it is **preferable that one** should **increase** the effort involved **in one prohibited labor,** that **of sifting** the flour numerous times, **and one** should **not increase** the number of prohibited acts involved in performing **many prohibited labors,** such as reaping and winnowing, which are required for the processing of five *se'a*.

Rava proposes that Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Chanina *segan ha-kohanim* said *davar echad* — the same thing. To establish this, the *gemara* invokes a *mishnah* that records Rabbi Chanina's position.

### Mishnah: Cutting & Processing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים אוֹמֵר: בַּשַּׁבָּת הָיָה נִקְצָר בְּיָחִיד, וּבְמַגָּל אֶחָד, וּבְקוּפָּה אַחַת, וּבַחוֹל – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, בְּשָׁלֹשׁ קוּפּוֹת, וְשָׁלֹשׁ מַגָּלוֹת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד שַׁבָּת וְאֶחָד חוֹל – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, בְּשָׁלֹשׁ קוּפּוֹת, וּבְשָׁלֹשׁ מַגָּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, says: On Shabbat** the barley **was reaped by an individual and with one sickle and with one basket** into which the barley was placed; **and during the week,** it was reaped **by three** people **with three baskets and three sickles. And the Rabbis say: Both on Shabbat and** during the **week,** it was reaped **by three** people **with three baskets and with three sickles.**

Rabbi Chanina *segan ha-kohanim* rules: when the *omer* is cut on Shabbos, it is cut by one person alone, using one scythe and one basket. On a weekday, three people cut it, using three scythes and three baskets. The Rabbis, by contrast, hold that even on Shabbos one uses three people with three scythes and three baskets.

### Rabbi Chanina's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר – לָא טָרְחִינַן, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר – לָא טָרְחִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains Rabba's comparison: **Doesn't Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, say there,** with regard to the process of gathering the barley, that **since it is possible** to reap by means of one person, **we do not exert** ourselves to reap it by means of three? **Here, too,** Rabbi Yishmael maintains that **since it is possible** to bring the *omer* meal offering from three *se'a* of barley, **we do not exert** ourselves on Shabbat to bring it from five *se'a*.

The *gemara* identifies the underlying principle: *keivan de'efshar lo trachinan* — since it is possible to accomplish the task with fewer people, we do not engage in the additional effort. Notably, the *gemara* uses the phrase *lo trachinan*, "we do not exert ourselves," even though the issue is not really about exertion but about the degree of *chilul Shabbos*. In fact, it might actually be less laborious to use more people — the point is simply that additional manpower means additional violation, and we minimize that wherever possible.

The parallel to Rabbi Yishmael is drawn: just as Rabbi Chanina minimizes the violation at the stage of cutting by using fewer personnel, Rabbi Yishmael minimizes violation at the stage of processing by cutting only three *se'ah* of grain rather than five, even though five *se'ah* yields finer flour for the *omer*. Each authority addresses a different stage — Rabbi Yishmael the quantity of wheat harvested, Rabbi Chanina the number of individuals involved — but both reflect the same underlying impulse to reduce *chilul Shabbos* even when it is technically permitted.

One might note that in terms of formal culpability, whether one cuts three *se'ah* or five in a single *shogeg* act would still produce only one *chatas* obligation, since the *shiur* has been met regardless of quantity. This question of whether continuous performance of a forbidden act constitutes one violation or many — depending on *hasra'ah* and other formal factors — is important and will resurface later in the *gemara*.

### Distinguishing Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָכָא, אֶלָּא דְּלֵיכָּא פַּרְסוֹמֵי מִילְּתָא, אֲבָל הָתָם, דְּאִיכָּא פַּרְסוֹמֵי מִילְּתָא – אֵימָא כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison: **From where** is this conclusion reached? **Perhaps Rabbi Yishmael states** his ruling **only here,** because **there is no** greater **publicity** of the **event** achieved by using five *se'a* rather than three. **But there,** in the case of reaping the barley, where **there is** greater **publicity** of the **event** through the involvement of more people, one can **say that he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* immediately objects: *mima'i* — how do we know these two positions are truly equivalent? Perhaps they should be distinguished.

### Pirsumei Milsa Factor

The *gemara* raises the possibility that Rabbi Yishmael's call to minimize *chilul Shabbos* applies specifically in contexts where there is no countervailing benefit to doing more. By the *omer*, however, there is a significant consideration on the other side: *pirsumei milsa* — publicizing the deed. The more people involved in the cutting, the more the act becomes a public event, which matters particularly in the context of the *omer* because of the *Tzedukim*. The *Tzedukim* disputed the rabbinic reckoning of when the *omer* is to be brought, and the elaborate public ceremony of the cutting was designed in part to assert the correct *halacha* openly. If so, perhaps Rabbi Yishmael would actually *endorse* using more people for the cutting — for the sake of *pirsumei milsa* — and only minimize at the sifting stage where no such benefit applies.

There is a deeper point here as well. The *mitzvah* of the *omer* inaugurates the count toward *Shavuos*, and the *Torah* itself frames this with the phrase *u'sefartem lachem mi'macharas ha-Shabbos* — you shall count from the day after the Shabbos. The very day on which the counting begins is contested, and thus publicly declaring and marking that day is not merely a polemical gesture against the *Tzedukim* but is organically connected to the *mitzvah* of *sefirah* itself. The *pirsumei milsa* of the *omer* cutting may therefore be understood as intrinsic to the *mitzvah*, not merely incidental politics.

It is admittedly somewhat striking that a value like *pirsumei milsa* — or even the goal of countering the *Tzedukim* — could tip the scales and justify *more* rather than less *chilul Shabbos*, particularly if one understands the obligation to minimize as a *de'oraisa* concern, stemming from the position that Shabbos violation for Temple needs is *dechuyah* rather than *hutrah*. Yet one can argue that when the publicizing of the act is itself religiously significant and connected to the content of the *mitzvah*, it enters into the calculation of what is halachically warranted.

### Tzorech Gavoa Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים הָתָם, דְּאִי בְּחַד אִי בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה – צוֹרֶךְ גָּבוֹהַּ מִיתְעֲבִיד כְּהִלְכָתוֹ, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּלָא אִיתְעֲבִיד כְּהִלְכָתוֹ צוֹרֶךְ גָּבוֹהַּ – אֵימָא כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** perhaps **Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, states** his ruling **only there, as whether one** individual **or three** people reap, the rite **is** still being **performed in its proper manner** for **the requirement of the Most High. But here, when** only three *se'a* are used, and the rite **is not** being **performed in its proper manner** for **the requirement of the Most High,** as five *se'a* are usually needed, **say that he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that on Shabbat the mitzva is performed in the same manner as on a weekday.

A second distinction is then raised: Rabbi Chanina *segan ha-kohanim* may have said to use only one person precisely because using one person versus three does not affect the end result. Whether one person cuts or three, the same quantity of grain is eventually harvested — it simply takes the one person three times as long. The *tzorech gavoah*, the Temple's religious interest, is served equally well either way. Here, however, the case of Rabbi Yishmael may be different: if one processes only three *se'ah* rather than five, the quality of the flour may be compromised. Fine flour is easier to obtain when you begin with a larger quantity; with only three *se'ah*, achieving the required fineness requires considerably more effort and may yield a less refined product. If so, the *tzorech gavoah* is better served by the five-*se'ah* approach, and Rabbi Chanina might concede this point — he would limit *chilul Shabbos* only when the end result is not thereby diminished, but not when the Temple's interest is materially affected.

The *gemara* thus leaves us with a rich and unresolved picture: we cannot simply say Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Chanina hold the same position in all cases. They share a general disposition toward minimizing *chilul Shabbos* where possible, but the specific calculations — whether *pirsumei milsa* is at stake, whether *tzorech gavoah* is affected — may yield very different conclusions in particular applications. As one student observed, we know what each authority said about his own case but cannot necessarily extrapolate to the other's. When we say they said *davar echad*, we mean they share the same underlying principle, not that they would necessarily rule identically in every scenario.

## Rabbi Yishmael & Rabbi Yosi

### Rav Ashi's Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד, דִּתְנַן: בֵּין שֶׁנִּרְאָה בַּעֲלִיל וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא נִרְאָה בַּעֲלִיל – מְחַלְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אִם נִרְאָה בַּעֲלִיל – אֵין מְחַלְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yosei said the same thing. As we learned** in a mishna (*Rosh HaShana* 21b): **Whether** the new moon **was seen clearly [*ba'alil*]** by everyone **or whether it was not seen clearly, one may desecrate the Shabbat** in order to testify about its appearance. **Rabbi Yosei says: If** the moon **was clearly seen,** witnesses **may not desecrate the Shabbat for it,** as other witnesses located nearer to the court will certainly testify. If these distant witnesses go to court to testify, they will desecrate Shabbat unnecessarily.

Having found the comparison between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Chanina insufficient, the *gemara* tries again. Rav Ashi proposes: Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yosi said *davar echad*. A *mishnah* is cited: whether the new moon was seen *ba'aliyah* — clearly, on a cloudless night — or *shelo nira ba'aliyah* — obscurely, under cloudy conditions — one violates Shabbos if necessary to travel to Jerusalem and testify before the *beis din*, thereby establishing *Rosh Chodesh* for that day. The urgency is real: if testimony is not delivered on the day the moon was sighted, that day cannot be sanctified as *Rosh Chodesh*. Riding a horse or being carried on a bed — whatever it takes — is warranted to arrive in time.

### Rabbi Yosi's Position

The text indicates that Rabbi Yosi dissents from the *tanna kamma* on one point: if the moon was seen *ba'aliyah* — clearly visible to all, on a bright night — then one need not violate Shabbos to come and testify. One can assume that many witnesses saw it, and this particular individual's testimony is not necessary. Rabbi Yosi's reasoning maps neatly onto *keivan de'efshar lo trachinan*: since the result can be achieved without this person's violation, we do not require that person to commit the violation.

### Keivan De'Efshar Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר – לָא טָרְחִינַן, הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר – לָא טָרְחִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains Rav Ashi's comparison: **Didn't Rabbi Yosei say there: Since it is possible** to receive testimony about the new moon without further witnesses, **we do not exert** ourselves and travel on Shabbat? **Here, too,** Rabbi Yishmael holds that **since it is possible** to bring the *omer* meal offering from three *se'a* of barley, **we do not exert** ourselves on Shabbat to bring it from five *se'a*.

The *gemara* draws the parallel to Rabbi Yishmael: just as Rabbi Yosi says that when testimony can be delivered without your Shabbos violation, you should not violate Shabbos, Rabbi Yishmael says that when the *omer* flour can be produced adequately with three *se'ah*, one should not harvest five. The underlying principle — *keivan de'efshar lo trachinan* — is the same in both cases.

### Case Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָכָא, אֶלָּא דְּלֵיכָּא נִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה מַכְשִׁילָן לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, אֲבָל הָתָם, דְּנִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה מַכְשִׁילָן לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, אֵימָא כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison: **From where** do you draw this conclusion? **Perhaps Rabbi Yishmael states only here that** three *se'a* are brought on Shabbat, **as there is no** concern that ultimately **you will cause** people **to stumble in the future** and refrain from bringing the *omer* offering. **But there,** in the case of witnesses testifying about the new moon, he concedes that all potential witnesses may travel on Shabbat because if not, **you will cause them to stumble in the future.** People will say: Why should we go to such trouble, as our testimony is unnecessary? Yet at some point they will be needed, and no witnesses will come to the court. Therefore, in that case one can **say** that Rabbi Yishmael agrees with this reasoning and **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* again probes whether this parallelism holds. Perhaps Rabbi Yishmael's position is limited to contexts where there is no further concern about what happens if we tell the witness not to come. Here, by *Rosh Chodesh*, there is precisely such a concern: if we discourage witnesses from coming on Shabbos when the moon was seen clearly, those witnesses may internalize the lesson incorrectly. The next time the moon is seen under partly cloudy conditions, a would-be witness might think, "They probably don't need me — surely others have seen it more clearly," and stay home. But if that person was in fact the only viable witness, *Rosh Chodesh* cannot be established. The policy concern is: *nimtze'as ata machshilo le'asid lavo* — you will end up tripping people up in the future. Rabbi Yishmael might therefore tell all witnesses to come, regardless of how clearly the moon was seen, in order to instill in them the habit of always coming forward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּלֵיכָּא צוֹרֶךְ גָּבוֹהַּ, וְלֹא נִיתְּנָה שַׁבָּת לִדָּחוֹת, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאִיכָּא צוֹרֶךְ גָּבוֹהַּ, וְנִיתְּנָה שַׁבָּת לִדָּחוֹת – אֵימָא כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** it is possible that **Rabbi Yosei states** his ruling **only there,** with regard to the new moon, **as this is not a requirement for the Most High, and** therefore **Shabbat may not be desecrated. But here,** as the *omer* meal offering **is a requirement for the Most High, and** therefore **Shabbat may be desecrated** for it, **say** that Rabbi Yosei holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that on Shabbat the mitzva is performed in the same manner as on a weekday.

A second distinction is introduced from the other direction: perhaps Rabbi Yosi's position is limited to cases where there is no *tzorech gavoah* at stake. When the moon is clearly visible and many witnesses are available, this individual's testimony contributes nothing to the Temple's interests — the day will be sanctified without him. Since no religious need is being served, *lo nitna Shabbos lidechos* — there is no basis to push aside Shabbos. But by the *omer*, the one already engaged in cutting three *se'ah* is already in the midst of a Shabbos violation. The person standing in the field cutting wheat is not analogous to the witness who has not yet taken a single step toward Jerusalem. For the one already involved, the question is only whether to do more or less of the very act already underway, and the *tzorech gavoah* consideration — that more grain yields finer flour — may be sufficient to justify doing more.

### Unified Principle

This distinction is striking. The witness who has not yet begun any violation is in an entirely different position from the Kohen already mid-harvest. For the witness, Rabbi Yosi says: you haven't started, others will do it, stay home. For the one already cutting, the question is only one of degree, and *tzorech gavoah* may tip that calculation toward doing more. The same principle — *keivan de'efshar lo trachinan* — generates different conclusions depending on whether one is already embedded in the act or is considering whether to begin it.

What emerges is that the *gemara*'s project of finding a *davar echad* is not about establishing doctrinal uniformity. Rather, it uses the exercise of comparing authorities to illuminate the different factors — *pirsumei milsa*, discouraging future witnesses, *tzorech gavoah*, and whether one is already mid-act — that enter into the question of how much *chilul Shabbos* is warranted when Shabbos is legitimately overridden. Whether one understands this as a *de'oraisa* regime of *dechuyah* — where only the minimum necessary violation is permitted — or as something more complex, the *gemara* makes clear that the calculations are subtle and highly context-dependent.

## Two Chataos Problem

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: שָׁחַט שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת שֶׁל צִיבּוּר, וְאֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ אֶלָּא אַחַת. אֲמַר רַבָּה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי אַמֵּי: חַיָּיב עַל הַשְּׁנִיָּה, וּפָטוּר עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. וַאֲפִילּוּ נִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ בַּשְּׁנִיָּה, וַאֲפִילּוּ נִמְצֵאת רִאשׁוֹנָה כְּחוּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Pesaḥim* 5:7): If one mistakenly **slaughtered two communal sin offerings,** e.g., the goat offerings of a Festival, on a Festival that occurred on Shabbat, **and only one was required, Rabba said, and some say** it was **Rabbi Ami** who said: He is **liable for the second,** superfluous communal sin offering, **but** is **exempt for the first. And** this is the *halakha* **even if he achieved atonement with the second** offering, e.g., if the blood of the first offering was spilled after the slaughter of the second. And this is the *halakha* **even if the first** offering **was found** to be **gaunt** and thereby disqualified as an offering, which meant that only the second offering was fit.

The *gemara* now introduces a distinct but related case: one slaughtered two *chataos* offerings belonging to the *tzibur* on Shabbos, when only one was required. According to Rashi's reading, one of the animals was the *korban* of the day — a *se'ir* for the *mussaf* — while the other was a different communal *chatat* that had not yet become obligatory to bring that day. The person accidentally slaughtered both.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava — or as the *gemara* notes, some attribute this to Rabbi Ami — rules: *chayav al ha-shniyah, patur al ha-rishonah*. One is liable for the second slaughter, which constituted an unjustified *chilul Shabbos*, but the first was entirely proper and one has in fact fulfilled the obligation of the day with it.

### No Retroactive Justification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבָּה הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבָּה: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת, אַחַת שְׁמֵינָה וְאַחַת כְּחוּשָׁה – שָׁחַט שְׁמֵינָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁחַט כְּחוּשָׁה – חַיָּיב, כְּחוּשָׁה וְאַחַר כָּךְ שְׁמֵינָה – פָּטוּר, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: הָבֵא שְׁמֵינָה לְכַתְּחִלָּה וּשְׁחוֹט!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in a case where the first animal was found to be gaunt, **did Rabba** really **say this? But doesn't Rabba say** that if one **had before him two** communal **sin offerings** on Shabbat, **one choice** animal **and one gaunt,** and he **slaughtered the choice** one **and then slaughtered** the **gaunt** one, he is **liable** for the gaunt animal, as it should not have been slaughtered. But if he first slaughtered the **gaunt** one **and subsequently** slaughtered the **choice** animal, he is **exempt** for slaughtering the choice animal. **And moreover,** members of the court **say to him** after he slaughtered the gaunt animal: **Bring** the **choice** animal **and slaughter** it ***ab initio*.** If so, in a case where the first animal was found to be gaunt, he certainly should not be liable for slaughtering the second.

The *gemara* then adds a striking corollary: even if the blood of the first animal spilled after the second was slaughtered, and the community therefore ended up using the second animal as its *korban*, this does not retroactively justify having slaughtered the second one. The determination is made as of the moment of the act. If the first animal's blood had spilled *before* the second was slaughtered, one would have been told to go ahead and slaughter a second — at that point it was necessary. But when both animals were slaughtered in sequence and only afterward the blood of the first spilled, the violation had already been committed when the second was slaughtered. Hindsight does not exonerate.

Similarly, the *gemara* adds: even if after slaughtering the first animal it was discovered to be *kachusha* — scrawny and inferior — one is still not retroactively justified for having slaughtered the second. The first animal, whatever its quality, was available and usable; the second slaughter was therefore unnecessary at the time it occurred.

### Nicer Korban Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: סְמִי כְּחוּשָׁה מִקַּמָּיְיתָא, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָהִיא – רַבִּי אַמֵּי אַמְרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say: Remove [*semei*]** the clause with regard to the **gaunt** animal **from the first** statement of Rabba. In other words, Rabba merely said that he is exempt for the slaughter of the first animal; he did not state a ruling about the second animal. **And if you wish, say** instead that the ruling **that** he is liable for the slaughter of the second animal **was stated** by **Rabbi Ami,** not Rabba.

The *gemara* then raises a pointed internal contradiction: did Rava really say this? For did Rava himself not also rule elsewhere that if one had two *chataos* before him — one fat and healthy, the other scrawny — and slaughtered the scrawny one first, one may not only go ahead and slaughter the fat one afterward without liability, but is in fact *instructed* to do so? *Havei shmena l'chatchila u'shchos* — bring a fat one and slaughter it. This is an astonishing ruling. Rava holds that even after the first animal has already been slaughtered and could technically satisfy the *chatat* obligation, if it is inferior, one is *permitted* — indeed encouraged — to violate Shabbos again by slaughtering a superior animal in its place.

This means that the license to violate Shabbos for Temple service extends not merely to fulfilling the bare minimum requirement, but encompasses bringing the *korban* in an ideal manner. The *mitzvah* of offering the *korban* properly — what we might call *hidur mitzvah* in the context of *korbanos* — is itself sufficient grounds for the additional Shabbos violation.

This is a genuinely large *chiddush*, and its moral and halachic implications are considerable. Not only does one violate Shabbos to bring a nicer *korban*, but the practical consequence is that the first animal — a *chatat* that has already been slaughtered — must now be set aside and destroyed, since a *chatat* shelamim cannot simply be reassigned. For the sake of a more aesthetically or halachically ideal offering, a validly slaughtered sacrificial animal is effectively wasted. One might ask whether any notion of *hidur mitzvah* can justify what is simultaneously a violation of the sanctity of *kodshim* and an obvious waste of animal life. These are not easy questions, and the *gemara* does not resolve them — it simply reports Rava's position as a *chiddush* of considerable scope.

### Attribution Question

The contradiction between Rava's two apparent positions is resolved in two ways. First, the earlier ruling that includes the *kachusha* case may simply need to be corrected: excise the clause about the scrawny animal from the first ruling, since Rava himself permits slaughtering a second animal in that scenario. Second, the *gemara* suggests: perhaps the stricter ruling was never Rava's to begin with. The original statement was attributed either to Rava or to Rabbi Ami — *ve'iseima Rabbi Ami*. Once it becomes clear that Rava permits slaughtering a superior animal even after a valid one has already been slaughtered, the stricter ruling must be attributed to Rabbi Ami alone.

### Moral Concerns

The moral dimension of Rava's position — permitting and even encouraging the discard of one *korban* in favor of a better one — was noted above. One might frame it as a concern purely internal to *kodshim*: does the value of bringing a nicer *korban* justify rendering a previously slaughtered *chatat* *pasul* in effect? And from a broader moral perspective: is the notion that "God deserves better" a sufficient justification for what is, by any ordinary reckoning, a significant waste of resources and of life?

The response one might offer is that *hidur mitzvah* is not merely about satisfying a divine preference in an anthropomorphic sense. Just as one spends money on a finer *esrog* rather than donating that sum to *tzedakah*, the religious value of beautifying the performance of a commandment is recognized as independently significant, even when it comes at a tangible cost. But the force of the question remains: choosing the finest available *esrog* before the fact is one thing; discarding what one has already consecrated and slaughtered because something better appeared is quite another. Rava's position is undeniably a *chiddush* of the highest order, and the discomfort it generates is not without basis.

## Intent vs. Reality Debate

### Ravina's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: נִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה כְּחוּשָׁה בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִין, מַהוּ? בָּתַר מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ אָזְלִינַן, וְגַבְרָא לְאִיסּוּרָא קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, אוֹ דִלְמָא בָּתַר מַעֲשָׂיו אָזְלִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** If the **first** offering is **found** to **be gaunt,** i.e., weakened, **in its intestines** after it was slaughtered, **what is** the *halakha*? Do **we follow his intention** and hold him accountable, **and this man,** who was unaware at the time of its slaughter that the first animal was gaunt, **intended** to transgress the Shabbat **prohibition** by slaughtering the second offering? **Or perhaps we follow his actions** and exempt him from liability, as the first offering had been disqualified when he brought the second.

Ravina poses a further question to Rav Ashi, pressing the *kachusha* case in a more nuanced direction. The previous ruling had addressed a case where the first animal was openly scrawny — visible to the eye before slaughter. What if the animal appeared fine at the time of slaughter but was only discovered to be scrawny *b'vnei me'ayim* — in its innards, upon being opened up? Rashi understands this either as a discovery made when examining the carcass closely, or more literally as a finding about the quality of the internal organs and fats — a small liver, insufficient fatty portions to place on the *mizbe'ach* — which are precisely the parts of the *olah* and *chatat* that go on the altar. These defects are only detectable after the slaughter is complete.

The question then is: one slaughtered the first animal, which appeared healthy; then slaughtered a second; then discovered that the first was actually of inferior quality internally. Is one liable for the second slaughter or not?

### Machshava vs. Ma'aseh

This is where the *gemara* introduces its central conceptual question: *batar machshava azlinan* or *batar ma'aseh azlinan* — do we follow the person's intention at the time of the act, or do we follow the objective reality of the act itself?

If we follow *machshava* — subjective intent — then one is liable, because at the moment of the second slaughter, the person had no awareness that the first animal was deficient. In his mind, he was committing an unjustified violation: he thought the first animal was adequate, and he slaughtered a second anyway. *De-gavra issura ka-michavein* — subjectively, the person was oriented toward performing a prohibited act, even if not with malicious intent. Rashi notes that the person was not actually trying to do something wrong — he was sincerely trying to bring a *korban* — but the point is that he did not have, in his subjective awareness, any justification for the second slaughter.

If we follow *ma'aseh* — the objective reality of the act — then one might be exempt, because at the time of the second slaughter, the fact that justified it was already present in the world, even if unknown to the slaughterer. The first animal *was* scrawny; the scrawniness existed; and therefore the act of slaughtering the second was objectively warranted, even if the person did not know it.

This distinguishes the *kachusha* case sharply from the spilled-blood case discussed earlier. When the blood spilled *after* the second slaughter, the fact that would have justified it did not exist at the time — it came into being only later. That is pure retrospect and cannot exonerate. Here, however, the inferior quality of the first animal was already a fact at the moment of the second slaughter. The only missing element was *awareness* of that fact. The question is therefore: does the objective presence of a justifying reality suffice, or must the actor be subjectively aware of it?

### Drowning Infant Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו הַיְינוּ דְּרַבָּה וְרָבָא? דְּאִיתְּמַר: שָׁמַע שֶׁטָּבַע תִּינוֹק בַּיָּם, וּפָרַשׂ מְצוּדָה לְהַעֲלוֹת דָּגִים וְהֶעֱלָה דָּגִים – חַיָּיב. לְהַעֲלוֹת דָּגִים, וְהֶעֱלָה דָּגִים וְתִינוֹק – רָבָא אָמַר: חַיָּיב, וְרַבָּה אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to** Ravina: **Isn't this** the same as a case subject to a disagreement between **Rabba and Rava,** and in fact both agree that in this case he is liable? **As it was stated:** If **one heard that a child was drowning at sea, and he spread a net to raise fish and** the result was that **he raised** only **fish,** he is **liable** for transgressing the Shabbat prohibition of trapping. If he intended **to raise fish,** and he **raised** both **fish and** the **child, Rava says:** He is **liable,** as his intention was to transgress a prohibition, **and Rabba says:** He is **exempt,** as his act saved a life and was therefore permitted on Shabbat.

The *gemara* responds that this is precisely the debate between Rabbah and Rava, and introduces the famous case: one heard that an infant was drowning in the sea, but instead of trying to save the infant, one cast out a net to catch fish. As a result, both fish and the infant were hauled up. Is one liable for *chilul Shabbos* or exempt?

Casting a net on Shabbos is a *melachah*. If one cast the net purely to catch fish — which one clearly intended — one has performed a prohibited act with prohibited intent. But the very same act also saved a life, which is not only permitted but obligatory on Shabbos. The act is objectively an act of *hatzalas nefesh* and simultaneously an act of *chilul Shabbos* motivated by prohibited intent. How do we characterize it?

### Rabbah vs. Rava

Rabbah says: *patur* — exempt. *Zil batar ma'aseh* — follow the objective reality of the act. The act saved a life; it cannot be characterized as a prohibited *chilul Shabbos*. Rava says: *chayav* — liable. *Zil batar machshava* — follow the subjective intent. The person intended only to catch fish; that intent defines the nature of the act, which is therefore a *ma'aseh chilul Shabbos* rather than a *ma'aseh hatzalas nefesh*.

### Reconciling Rabbah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כָּאן רַבָּה לָא קָא פָּטַר אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁמַע, אָמְרִינַן נָמֵי דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַתִּינוֹק, אֲבָל לֹא שָׁמַע – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why everyone would agree that one is liable in the case involving two offerings. **And Rabba deemed** him **exempt only there, since** the one who spread the net **heard** that a child had fallen in, and therefore **we say that his intention** in spreading the net **was also to** save **the child. But** had he **not heard** that the child had fallen in, he would **not** be exempt. This is comparable to the case of two offerings, where he could not have known before its slaughter that the first animal had weak intestines.

The *gemara* then offers a refinement of Rabbah's position. It suggests that Rabbah does not hold a purely objectivist view — he does not say the act is defined solely by what it accomplishes regardless of what the actor was thinking. Rather, Rabbah holds that even the most callous fisherman, if he *heard* that an infant was drowning, has some degree of concern for the infant in his heart — *amina nami da'tei a'tinok* — his mind is also on the infant. If so, even subjectively there is a dimension of *mitzvah* motivation. This is why Rabbah exempts: not because he ignores intent entirely, but because he believes that hearing of the drowning infant necessarily creates some minimal intention to help, even if the person refuses to admit it.

Consequently, if the person *did not hear* about the infant — if he cast his net in complete ignorance that anyone was drowning — then even Rabbah would hold him liable, because in that case there is no conceivable element of *mitzvah* motivation, and the act is purely a forbidden one that happened, by happenstance, to produce a beneficial result.

### Application to Animal

Applying this to Ravina's question: the person who slaughtered the second animal had no knowledge that the first was scrawny. He was entirely unaware of the fact that would have justified the act. Under the first reading of the *gemara*'s discussion, this means both Rabbah and Rava would hold him liable — Rava because subjective intent is what matters and the person had no awareness of any justification, and Rabbah because even on his more objective approach, some awareness is required for one's intent to be directed toward the *mitzvah*. Without any knowledge of the first animal's deficiency, neither the subjective nor the objective approach can exonerate him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַיְינוּ פְּלוּגְתַּיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּה וְרָבָא, דְּאִיתְּמַר: שָׁמַע שֶׁטָּבַע תִּינוֹק בַּיָּם, וּפָרַשׂ מְצוּדָה לְהַעֲלוֹת דָּגִים וְהֶעֱלָה דָּגִים – חַיָּיב, לְהַעֲלוֹת דָּגִים וְהֶעֱלָה תִּינוֹק וְדָגִים – רַבָּה אָמַר: פָּטוּר, וְרָבָא אָמַר: חַיָּיב. רַבָּה אָמַר פָּטוּר – זִיל בָּתַר מַעֲשָׂיו, וְרָבָא אָמַר חַיָּיב – זִיל בָּתַר מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that Rav Ashi **said to** Ravina conclusively: **This is** subject to the disagreement **between Rabba and Rava. As it was stated:** If **one heard that a child was drowning at sea, and he spread a net to raise fish and** the result was that **he raised** only **fish,** he is **liable** for transgressing the Shabbat prohibition of trapping. If he intended **to raise fish,** and he **raised** both **fish and** the **child, Rava says:** He is **liable, and Rabba says:** He is **exempt.** Rav Ashi adds that Rabba did not exempt him because he heard that a child was drowning. Rather, **Rabba says** that he is **exempt** because one **follows his actions,** whereas **Rava says** that he is **liable** because one **follows his intention.** Consequently, the same dispute applies to the case of two offerings.

Under the alternative reading — *ve'ika d'amri* — the case of the scrawny animal discovered in the innards is itself the very debate between Rabbah and Rava. Rabbah, on his purely objectivist reading, would exempt the slaughterer because the objective fact of the first animal's inferiority existed at the time of the second slaughter, regardless of whether the person knew it. Rava, who follows *machshava*, would hold him liable, since the person had no subjective awareness of any justification.

## Pikuach Nefesh Priority

### Multiple Helpers Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: חוֹלֶה שֶׁאֲמָדוּהוּ לִגְרוֹגֶרֶת אַחַת, וְרָצוּ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם וְהֵבִיאוּ עֲשָׂרָה גְּרוֹגְרוֹת בְּבַת אַחַת – פְּטוּרִין, אֲפִילּוּ בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, אֲפִילּוּ קָדַם וְהִבְרִיא בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion concerning performing extra prohibited labor on Shabbat in extenuating circumstances where the desecration of Shabbat is allowed. **Rabba says:** With regard to a dangerously **ill person** on Shabbat **whom** the doctors **evaluated** as needing **to** eat **one fig** to regain his health, **and ten men ran and** each cut and **brought ten figs simultaneously,** one each, **they are** all **exempt** from liability for transgressing the prohibition of reaping on Shabbat. This applies **even** in a case where the ten come **one after the other,** and **even** if the ill person **had already recovered his health by** eating **the first** fig.

The *gemara* now turns to the case of *pikuach nefesh* directly, a subject that had been raised earlier in discussion and that now receives formal treatment. Rabbah — or on some textual variants, Rava — states the following: an ill person (*choleh*) was assessed to need one unit of dried figs (*grogaros*) to save his life on Shabbos. Ten people ran to bring figs, each performing a *melachah* — perhaps *hotza'ah* without an *eruv* — and each brought one, so that ten were brought in total when only one was needed.

### All Exempt Rule

All ten are exempt. Even those whose figs were completely unnecessary — because others had already brought more than enough — are not liable for *chilul Shabbos*. The *gemara* goes further: even if they did not arrive simultaneously but sequentially, *beze achar ze*, each one is still exempt.

### Recovered Patient

And further still: even if the first person's fig was so quickly administered that the patient recovered *before* the second person even arrived — so that by the time the second person walked in with his fig there was no longer any sick person present — even that person is exempt.

### Intent Principle

The governing principle is clear: what defines the act is the actor's intention at the time of performing it. Each of the ten people, at the moment they performed their act of *melachah*, was doing so in order to save a life. That intention — sincere, reasonable, and directed toward a genuine *pikuach nefesh* — is sufficient to characterize the act as a permitted one, regardless of whether it turned out to be necessary.

This directly addresses the earlier question about the *machshava*-versus-*ma'aseh* debate. For *pikuach nefesh*, the subjective dimension — *intending* to save a life — is what matters. Even if objectively no life was at risk by the time the act was completed, the intention to save renders the act permissible.

### Permissive Standard

This case stands in stark contrast to the Temple-service cases discussed earlier, where the *gemara* was carefully calibrating exactly how much *chilul Shabbos* was warranted and asking whether minimization was required. By *pikuach nefesh*, the standard is dramatically more permissive: one does not minimize, one does not hold back because others are already handling it, and one does not reassess based on whether the effort turned out to be needed. Anyone who reasonably believed they were contributing to saving a life is fully exempt.

The question of whether the *gemara*'s ruling is *Rabbah* or *Rava* matters, because the two have just been presented as holding opposite positions on the objectivist-subjectivist spectrum. If the ruling is Rava's, it is straightforward: Rava holds that *machshava* governs, and since all ten people subjectively intended to save a life, all ten are exempt, even though objectively most or all were unnecessary. If it is Rabbah's ruling, it is particularly striking: Rabbah, who was shown to take the more objective view, here embraces a purely intentionalist standard. One way to reconcile this is to say that Rabbah applies his principle of leniency in both directions — wherever the act can be characterized as non-violative, whether because it objectively saves a life (the fish case) or because the person sincerely intended to save a life (this case), Rabbah will not call it *chilul Shabbos*. The practical upshot is that for *pikuach nefesh*, even Rabbah would say: if in your mind you are still engaged in saving this person, you act — you do not hesitate, you do not calculate whether others have already done enough, and you are not held responsible if it turns out to have been unnecessary.

This has direct practical significance. The *gemara*'s ruling that ten people running simultaneously are all exempt — each one performing a separate *melachah* — establishes that when it comes to saving a life, we do not want people holding back out of an excess of scruple. The danger of over-calculation in a *pikuach nefesh* situation is that someone who could have helped does not act, on the assumption that others will. The *gemara* forestalls that reasoning entirely.

## Multiple Fruit Locations

### Fruit Distribution Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: חוֹלֶה שֶׁאֲמָדוּהוּ לִשְׁתֵּי גְּרוֹגְרוֹת, וְיֵשׁ שְׁתֵּי גְּרוֹגְרוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי עוּקְצִין, וְשָׁלֹשׁ בְּעוֹקֶץ אַחַת – הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ מַיְיתִינַן? שְׁתַּיִם מַיְיתִינַן, דְּחָזוּ לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא שָׁלֹשׁ מַיְיתִינַן, דְּקָא מְמַעֲטָא קְצִירָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** In the case of a dangerously **ill person** on Shabbat **whom** the doctors **evaluated** as needing **to** eat **two figs** to regain his health, **and there are two figs** attached to a tree **by two stems [*okatzin*] and** another **three figs** attached to the tree **by one stem, which** one **of them** do **we bring?** Do **we bring two** figs, **as** that is the amount that **is fit for him,** i.e., this is the number of figs the ill person needs? **Or perhaps we bring** the **three** figs, **as** although he requires only two, this serves to **limit** the labor of **reaping,** as the three figs are attached to the tree by a single stem.

The *gemara* closes with Rabbah's final question, which returns to the theme of minimizing *melachah* acts and directly engages the issue of *shiurim* raised earlier. A sick person on Shabbos requires two units of fruit — two figs — to save his life. On the tree before us, there are two figs growing on two separate stems (*uktzim*), and a separate stem bearing three figs.

### Picking Strategy Question

The question: which do we pick? Do we pluck two figs from two separate stems — two acts of *ketzira*, harvesting — bringing exactly what is needed? Or do we pluck the three figs from the single stem — one act of *ketzira* — bringing one more than is strictly required?

### Minimize Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, שָׁלֹשׁ מַיְיתִינַן, דְּעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּכִי מְמַעֵט בַּאֲכִילָה קָא מְמַעֵט קְצִירָה, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּכִי קָא מְמַעֵט בַּאֲכִילָה קָא מַפְּשָׁא קְצִירָה, וַדַּאי שָׁלֹשׁ מַיְיתִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** It is **obvious** that **we bring** the **three** figs, **as Rabbi Yishmael states only there,** in the mishna, that three *se'a* of barley are reaped on Shabbat, **as** in that case **when** one **limits** the amount available **for eating,** he likewise **limits** the amount of **reaping. But here, when** one **limits** the amount **of eating** by bringing the two figs, he also **increases** the amount of **reaping.** Therefore is it **certain** that **we bring** the ill person the **three** figs attached by a single stem.

The *gemara* answers without hesitation: *peshita d'shalosh maitinan* — it is obvious that we bring three from the single stem. The goal is to minimize the number of acts of *melachah*, and a single act that brings three is preferable to two separate acts that bring only two.

### Distinguished from Omer

The *gemara* then carefully distinguishes this from Rabbi Yishmael's ruling about the *omer*. There, Rabbi Yishmael says to harvest *less* grain — three *se'ah* rather than five — even though that means more effort in sifting. Why? Because in that case, reducing the quantity of grain harvested also reduces the act of *ketzira* — less cutting corresponds to less grain. The reduction in quantity and the reduction in the number or extent of the prohibited act go hand in hand.

### Unified Principle

Here, by contrast, the relationship is inverted: bringing *less* quantity — two figs instead of three — would actually require *more* acts of *melachah*, since the two figs are on separate stems. When minimizing quantity and minimizing the number of prohibited acts pull in opposite directions, the correct calculation is to minimize the number of acts, even if that means bringing a slightly larger quantity than strictly necessary.

The principle that emerges is clean and important: a single act of *melachah*, even if it yields more than the minimum required quantity, is always preferable to multiple acts of *melachah* that together yield only the minimum. One violation, however substantial, is categorically less than two violations, however small. This also clarifies the question about *shiurim* that arose earlier: when the *gemara* discusses continuous acts — continuous sifting, continuous drinking by a *nazir* — the question of whether that constitutes one violation or many depends on how the act is divided and whether formal warnings intervene. But when we have clearly discrete, separate acts — two pluckings from two stems — those are unambiguously two violations, and one act producing more quantity is unambiguously preferable. Rabbi Yishmael's preference for more sifting over more cutting is perfectly consistent with this: more sifting is one extended act, while more cutting means engaging separately with more grain — conceptually closer to multiple acts. The distinction between quantity within a single act and multiplicity of acts is the key to resolving what might otherwise appear to be contradictory positions.
