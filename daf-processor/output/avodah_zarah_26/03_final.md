# Avodah Zarah 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Midwives & Nurses

### Jewish Midwife/Non-Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא תְּיַילֵּד אֶת הַנׇּכְרִית, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּיַלֶּדֶת בֵּן לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A Jewish woman may not deliver** the child of **a gentile woman, because** in doing so **she is delivering a child** who will engage in **idol worship.**

The *mishnah* on 26a opens by transitioning from the theme of fear of non-Jews in private settings to the question of rendering assistance to them. The *mishnah* states: *bat Yisrael lo tialeid et ovadet kochavim* — a Jewish woman may not serve as a midwife for a non-Jewish woman, *mipnei shemeyaledet ben la'avodat kochavim* — because she is helping bring a child into idolatry. The framing is noteworthy: the concern is not a generalized prohibition against helping non-Jews but is specifically tied to the idolatrous character of the society. The worry is that one is actively facilitating the perpetuation of *avodah zarah* by bringing more practitioners into the world.

### Non-Jew Midwife/Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל נׇכְרִית מְיַלֶּדֶת בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל
>
> **Translation:** **But** one **may** allow **a gentile woman** to **deliver** the child of **a Jewish woman.**

The *mishnah* then permits the reverse: *ovadet kochavim meyaledet bat Yisrael* — a non-Jewish woman may serve as a midwife for a Jewish woman. This immediately raises a question: given the concern about non-Jews and bloodshed that dominated the previous *mishnah*, why are we not worried here that the non-Jewish midwife might harm the Jewish mother or child? The *mishnah* itself does not explain this directly, but the *gemara* will clarify the conditions under which this is permitted.

### Jewish Nurse/Non-Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא תָּנִיק בְּנָהּ שֶׁל נׇכְרִית
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **a Jewish woman may not nurse the child of a gentile woman**

In parallel, the *mishnah* rules that *bat Yisrael lo teinik b'nah shel ovadet kochavim* — a Jewish woman may not nurse the child of a non-Jewish woman. Here again, the reasoning appears to track the same concern: one is helping raise a child destined for a life of idolatry, so the prohibition is similarly grounded in the idolatrous context rather than being a blanket prohibition against any assistance to non-Jews.

### Non-Jew Nurse/Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל נׇכְרִית מְנִיקָה בְּנָהּ שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּרְשׁוּתָהּ
>
> **Translation:** **but** one **may** allow **a gentile woman** to **nurse the child of a Jewish woman** while the gentile woman is **on** the Jewish woman's **property.**

The *mishnah* allows the inverse: *ovadet kochavim meinket b'nah shel Yisrael b'reshutah* — a non-Jewish woman may nurse the child of a Jewish woman, but specifically *b'reshutah*, in the Jewish woman's domain. This word is a hint at the condition of supervision that the *gemara* will spell out more explicitly: it must not be conducted in total privacy, but in circumstances where the non-Jewish nurse is in someone else's space and therefore subject to oversight.

### Ramban/Rabbeinu Yonah

One might ask how this *mishnah* applies in practice when a Jewish professional is called upon to help non-Jewish patients. While the *Rambam* is well known as a physician, the *Ramban* — Nachmanides — was also a doctor and specifically practiced in the area of gynecology, assisting Jewish and non-Jewish women alike in matters of childbirth and pregnancy. His cousin *Rabbeinu Yonah* famously wrote him a letter of critique, saying with mordant irony: *tavo alecha berachah she-ata mechayeh zaro shel Amalek* — may blessings come upon you, for you are giving life to the seed of Amalek. The choice of the word *Amalek* is significant. The critique was not framed primarily around whether Christians count as *ovdei avodah zarah*, but rather around the fact that these were the oppressors of Israel — the people actively threatening Jewish survival. How could one help bring more of them into the world?

We have no record of the *Ramban*'s formal reply, but the *Beit Yosef* and later *posekim* offer the principle of *mipnei eivah* — for the sake of preventing enmity — as the operative justification. This concept, which the *gemara* will develop at length, holds that fostering positive relations with the surrounding non-Jewish society is itself a halachic value that can, under certain conditions, override the reluctance to render assistance. Refusing to help when one is capable of doing so, especially in a professional capacity, breeds hostility that can be more dangerous than the assistance itself.

## Meir vs. Sages

### Baraita Stricter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא תְּיַילֵּד אֶת הַנׇּכְרִית, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּיַלֶּדֶת בֵּן לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְנׇכְרִית לֹא תְּיַילֵּד אֶת בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲשׁוּדִין עַל שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara cites a dispute related to the ruling of the mishna. **The Sages taught: A Jewish woman may not deliver** the child of **a gentile woman because** in doing so **she is delivering a child** who will engage in **idol worship. And** one **may not** allow **a gentile woman** to **deliver** the child of **a Jewish woman because** gentiles **are suspected of bloodshed;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that takes a more stringent position than the *mishnah*. The *baraita* opens similarly: *bat Yisrael lo tialeid et ovadet kochavim mipnei shemeyaledet ben la'avodat kochavim* — a Jewish woman may not serve as midwife for a non-Jewish woman because she helps bring a child into idolatry. So far, parallel to the *mishnah*. But then the *baraita* adds: *v'ovadet kochavim lo tialeid et bat Yisrael* — and a non-Jewish woman also may not serve as midwife for a Jewish woman, *mipnei shechashudah al shefichut damim* — because she is suspected of bloodshed. This is the position of *Rabbi Meir*, and it is fully consistent with the stringencies he expressed in the previous *mishnah* regarding private contact. The question then becomes: whose opinion does our *mishnah* represent, if it permits a non-Jewish midwife for a Jewish woman? The next line of the *baraita* answers: *v'Chachamim omrim*, the Sages say:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נׇכְרִית מְיַלֶּדֶת אֶת בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֲחֵרוֹת עוֹמְדוֹת עַל גַּבָּהּ, אֲבָל לֹא בֵּינָהּ לְבֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** One **may** allow **a gentile woman** to **deliver** the child of **a Jewish woman when other women are standing over her** watching her actions, **but not** when they are **alone together.**

*ovadet kochavim meyaledet et bat Yisrael* — a non-Jewish woman may serve as midwife — *b'zman she-acherot omdot agabah* — as long as other women are present and supervising, so that she cannot use the opportunity to harm the mother or child. But *lo beina l'veina* — not when it is entirely in private. This represents the majority view and explains the *mishnah*'s ruling.

*Tosafot* notes regarding the *mishnah*'s language of *b'reshutah* — in her domain — that perhaps this does not require literally standing over her in the manner the *baraita* describes, but rather a situation of *yotzei v'nichnas*, people coming and going, which provides a sufficient ambient level of supervision.

### Crushing Skull

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֲחֵרוֹת עוֹמְדוֹת עַל גַּבָּהּ נָמֵי לָא, דְּזִימְנִין דְּמַנְּחָא לֵיהּ יְדָא אַפּוּתֵאּ וְקָטְלָא לֵיהּ, וְלָא מִתְחֲזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir says: Even** when **other women are standing over her** one **may not** allow a gentile woman to deliver the child of a Jewish woman, **because** there are **times when** the midwife **places her hand on** the infant's **temple and kills him** by applying pressure to the area, **and** the act **is not seen.**

One might then ask: if other people are present and supervising, why does *Rabbi Meir* still object? The *gemara* explains: *de-zimnin de-mancha leh yadah a-pusa ve-katlah* — because sometimes during the delivery, she can place her hand on the soft spot of the infant's skull and kill him. *Ve-lo mitchell* — and it would not appear to onlookers that she had done so. The act could be accomplished in the very moment of delivery, indistinguishable from a stillbirth, even with people watching. Thus, according to *Rabbi Meir*, supervision is simply insufficient to address this particular form of potential harm.

### Infanticide Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּאֲמַרָה לַחֲבֶרְתַּהּ: מוֹלְדָא יְהוּדַיָּיתָא בַּת מוֹלְדָא יְהוּדַיָּיתָא! אֲמַרָה לַהּ: נְפִישִׁין בִּישָׁתָא דְּהַהִיא אִיתְּתָא, דְּקָא מַשְׁפֵּילְנָא מִינַּיְיהוּ דְּמָא כִּי אוּפְיָא דְּנַהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a story that validates this concern. This is **like** that incident involving **a certain** gentile **woman who said to her fellow** gentile: You are **the midwife of Jewish women, the daughter of a midwife of Jewish women.** This was meant as an insult. In response, the other gentile **said to her:** May as **many evils** befall **that woman,** i.e., you, as the number of Jewish infants that I have killed, **as I extract blood from them like the foam [*ufeya*] of a river.** This gentile midwife claimed to have caused the deaths of many Jewish children.

The *gemara* then illustrates this concern with a striking narrative. *Ke-hai itteta de-amra le-chavertah* — it is told of a woman who was being mocked by another non-Jewish woman for being a midwife to Jews. The mocker called her a "friend of the Jews," using it as a slur. The midwife shot back: *nefishin be-sha'ata de-hai itteta* — may you receive evil in proportion to what I have done to them — *de-ka-mashpilna mineihu dama ke-ofya de-nahara* — for I have spilled their blood like the flow of a river. She was boasting that she had killed many Jewish infants in the course of her midwifery work. This anecdote is brought as a proof for *Rabbi Meir*'s concern.

### Sages Dismiss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן אֲמַרוּ לָךְ: לָא הִיא, בְּמִילְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּאוֹקֵימְתַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the opinion of the Rabbis: **And the Rabbis,** who are not concerned to the same extent as Rabbi Meir, would **say to you:** That **is not so;** it was **merely** through **words that** the gentile midwife **was establishing herself.** In other words, she was bragging in order to intimidate her antagonist, whereas in reality she did not kill any infants.

The Sages, however, are not persuaded. *Lo hi* — that is not to be believed. *Be-milta de-amrah ve-kenisa* — she was responding in anger to someone who was mocking her, and the boast was an exaggeration born of that moment. In practice, where people are supervising, one can rely on the midwife's fear of being discovered, and the Sages maintain that supervision suffices.

### Poison Nipple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְקוּבִית לֹא תָּנִיק. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא תָּנִיק בְּנָהּ שֶׁל נׇכְרִית, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּגַדֶּלֶת בֵּן לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְנׇכְרִית לֹא תָּנִיק אֶת בְּנָהּ שֶׁל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲשׁוּדָה עַל שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נׇכְרִית מְנִיקָה אֶת בְּנָהּ שֶׁל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֲחֵרוֹת עוֹמְדוֹת עַל גַּבָּהּ, אֲבָל לֹא בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֲחֵרוֹת עוֹמְדוֹת עַל גַּבָּהּ נָמֵי לָא, דְּזִימְנִין דְּשָׁיְיפָא לֵיהּ סַמָּא לְדַד מֵאַבָּרַאי וְקָטְלָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **a Jewish woman may not nurse** the child of a gentile woman. **The Sages taught: A Jewish woman may not nurse the child of a gentile woman because** in doing so **she is** effectively **raising a child** who will engage in **idol worship. And** one **may not** allow **a gentile woman** to **nurse the child of a Jewish woman because she is suspected of bloodshed;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** One **may** allow **a gentile woman** to **nurse the child of a Jewish woman when other women are standing over her, but not** when the gentile woman is **alone together** with the child. **And Rabbi Meir says: Even** when **other women are standing over her** one **may not** allow a gentile woman to nurse the child, **because** there are **times when she smears poison** intended **for him upon** her **breast** when she is **outside** the house, **and** subsequently **kills him** with it while nursing.

The same tannaitic debate applies to nursing. The *baraita* states that *bat Yisrael lo tanik beno shel ovedet kochavim mipnei she-megadelet ben le-avodat kochavim* — a Jewish woman should not nurse a non-Jewish infant because she is helping raise a child to idolatry. And *Rabbi Meir* says that a non-Jewish woman also should not nurse a Jewish infant, *mipnei she-chashudah al shefichut damim* — because she is suspected of bloodshed. The Sages again permit it with supervision. *Rabbi Meir*'s concern in this case is: *de-zimnin de-shaifa leh samma le-dad mi-berai ve-katlah* — sometimes she will apply poison to her nipple before entering the Jewish home, outside where no one can see her do it, and the poison will already be in place by the time she arrives. Unlike the crushing-of-the-skull case, where the act occurs in full view, here the preparation happens in advance and in private, making even the most diligent supervision ineffective.

### Both Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מְיַלֶּדֶת, בְּהַהִיא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן דִּשְׁרֵי, דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲחֵרוֹת רוֹאוֹת אוֹתָהּ, אֲבָל מְנִיקָה דְּאֶפְשָׁר דְּשָׁיְיפָא לֵיהּ סַם לְדַד מֵאַבָּרַאי וְקָטְלָא לֵיהּ — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara remarks: **And it is necessary** for the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis to be stated with regard to both the case of a midwife and that of a wet nurse. The reason is **that if** the *tanna* had **taught us** only the *halakha* of **a midwife,** one might assume that it is only **in that** case that **the Rabbis said that it is permitted** to allow the gentile to deliver a Jewish infant, **because it is not possible** for her to kill him, **due to** the fact **that other women see her. But** with regard to **a wet nurse, as it is possible that she** might **smear poison upon** her **breast** while she is still **outside** the house **and** then **kill him** with it, one might **say** that the Rabbis **concede to Rabbi Meir** that a gentile woman may not be allowed to nurse the son of a Jewish woman under any circumstances.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מְנִיקָה, בְּהַהִיא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָסוּר, מִשּׁוּם דְּשָׁיְיפָא לֵיהּ סַם לְדַד מֵאַבָּרַאי וְקָטְלָא לֵיהּ, אֲבָל מְיַלֶּדֶת, דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר הֵיכָא דַּאֲחֵרוֹת עוֹמְדוֹת עַל גַּבָּהּ, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן — צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the *tanna* **had taught us** only the *halakha* of **a wet nurse,** one might assume that it is only **in that** case that **Rabbi Meir said that it is prohibited** for a gentile to nurse a Jewish infant, **due to** the fact **that she** might **smear poison upon** her **breast** while **outside** the house **and kill him** with it. **But** with regard to **a midwife, since it is not possible** for her to kill him **where other women are standing over her,** one might **say** that Rabbi Meir **concedes to the Rabbis.** The Gemara concludes: It is therefore **necessary** for this dispute to be stated with regard to both cases.

The *gemara* then asks: *u-tzricha* — why is it necessary for the *baraita* to state both debates, the one about midwifery and the one about nursing? Could we not have inferred one from the other?

The answer is that each case is necessary to prevent a mistaken inference. Had we only been told the debate about midwifery, one might have thought: the Sages permit it with supervision because the act of crushing the skull occurs right in front of witnesses, and even *Rabbi Meir*'s concern is marginal — she would be too afraid to act. But in the nursing case, where she can apply poison *mi-berai* — beforehand, outside — one might have thought the Sages would agree with *Rabbi Meir* that supervision is useless. Therefore the *baraita* must tell us that even in the nursing case, the Sages are not concerned, relying on the deterrent effect of supervision in some form.

Conversely, had we only been told the debate about nursing, one might have reasoned: *Rabbi Meir* is only concerned in the nursing case because of the possibility of preparation outside, which renders supervision ineffective. But in the case of childbirth, where she could not have prepared beforehand — the act would have to occur on the spot — one might think *Rabbi Meir* would concede to the Sages when others are supervising. Therefore the *baraita* must tell us explicitly that even in midwifery, *Rabbi Meir* does not permit it. Both cases are indispensable.

## Eivah Concept

### Paid vs. Free

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: יְהוּדִית מְיַלֶּדֶת אֲרַמִּית בְּשָׂכָר, אֲבָל לֹא בְּחִנָּם! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּשָׂכָר שְׁרֵי מִשּׁוּם אֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the above discussion, all agree that a Jewish woman may not deliver the child of a gentile woman. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **A Jewish woman may deliver** the child of **an Aramean woman** in exchange **for payment, but not for free. Rav Yosef said** in response: **It is permitted** in exchange **for payment due to** the **enmity** that would be engendered if Jews refused to deliver gentile infants despite being offered money to do so.

The *gemara* now raises a challenge against the *mishnah*'s apparent prohibition on serving as a midwife for non-Jewish women. A *baraita* states: *Yehudit meyeledes ovedet kochavim bischar aval lo bechinam* — a Jewish woman may serve as midwife for a non-Jewish woman for a fee, but not for free. This seems to contradict the rationale given in the *mishnah*: if the concern is that one is helping bring a child into idolatry, why does payment change anything? *Amar Rav Yosef*: *bischar shari mishum eivah* — when payment is involved, it is permitted on account of *eivah*, enmity. When someone offers to pay you for a professional service and you nonetheless refuse, that refusal will breed hatred and hostility. To decline an unpaid favor, however, is entirely natural — one's time has value and one is not obligated to provide free services — and so a refusal in that context does not generate *eivah*. Thus the distinction is not about the nature of the act but about the social dynamics of refusal.

### Eivah & Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר רַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר: אוֹלוֹדֵי נׇכְרִית בְּשַׁבְּתָא בְּשָׂכָר שְׁרֵי, מִשּׁוּם אֵיבָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: יָכְלָה לְמֵימַר לַהּ — דִּידַן, דִּמְינַטְּרִי שַׁבְּתָא — מְחַלְּלִינַן עֲלַיְיהוּ; דִּידְכוּ, דְּלָא מְינַטְּרִי שַׁבְּתָא — לָא מְחַלְּלִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef thought to say: Delivering** the child of **a gentile woman on Shabbat** in exchange **for payment is permitted due to enmity. Abaye said to him:** The concern of enmity does not apply here, because **she can say to** the gentile: With regard to **our** own women, **who keep Shabbat, we desecrate** Shabbat **for them;** with regard to **your** women, **who do not keep Shabbat, we do not desecrate** Shabbat for them.

*Rav Yosef* then sought to extend this principle further: *savar Rav Yosef lemeimar alodei ovedet kochavim be-Shabbat bischar shari mishum eivah* — he thought to say that one might even be permitted to assist a non-Jewish woman in childbirth on *Shabbat*, if paid, because of *eivah* concerns. The logic is that *eivah* can override rabbinic restrictions and resolve the concern expressed in the *mishnah* about aiding *avodah zarah*. Importantly, this reasoning only works if what is being overridden is a *d'Rabbanan* concern. *Eivah* is not a basis to permit a *d'Oraita* violation; for that, *pikuach nefesh* would be required. *Rav Yosef*'s position therefore implicitly assumes that the *Shabbat* activities involved in assisting childbirth in the case under discussion are, at most, rabbinic in character.

### Abaye Shabbat Reply

*Abaye* rejected this extension. He said the midwife can simply explain to the non-Jewish woman: *deedan demantrei Shabsah mechallelinan alei-hu* — for those of us who observe *Shabbat*, we are permitted to violate it on their behalf. *Deedchu d'lo mantrei Shabsah lo mechallelinan* — for those of you who do not observe *Shabbat*, I do not have the halachic authority to assist you on this day. In other words, she has a ready and honest explanation for her refusal that is rooted in religious observance rather than personal hostility, and therefore *eivah* should not result. The explanation is somewhat darkly amusing — it is not hard to imagine that a non-Jewish woman in labor receiving this particular theological explanation might not find it entirely satisfying, and one could argue it would exacerbate rather than defuse resentment. Nevertheless, that is *Abaye*'s answer.

### Chatam Sofer/Feinstein

Historically, Jews did assist non-Jewish women in childbirth on *Shabbat*, and there is a substantial responsa literature addressing how to ensure that any *Shabbat* activity involved would constitute only rabbinic rather than biblical violations, keeping the halachic concern at the level where leniency is possible. The *Chatam Sofer* engaged extensively with this question.

*Rav Moshe Feinstein* took the analysis a step further in addressing whether *Hatzalah* paramedics and Jewish doctors must respond to emergency calls from non-Jews on *Shabbat*. His position was that failing to answer such calls would generate such profound hatred and enmity that it would ultimately endanger Jewish lives — thus transforming the *eivah* itself into a *pikuach nefesh* concern for Jews. On this reasoning, even what would otherwise be a *d'Oraita* violation becomes permissible, not because *eivah* directly overrides *Shabbat* at the biblical level, but because the chain of consequences makes Jewish lives concretely at risk. This represents a significant extension of the *eivah* principle into territory the *gemara* itself does not explicitly reach.

### Paid Nursing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר רַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר: אוֹנוּקֵי בְּשָׂכָר שְׁרֵי מִשּׁוּם אֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef** also **thought to say: Nursing** the child of a gentile woman in exchange **for payment is permitted due to enmity.**

*Rav Yosef* then sought to apply the same logic to the nursing case: *anuka bischar shari mishum eivah* — a Jewish woman who is being paid to nurse a non-Jewish infant might be required to accept the arrangement because refusing payment without a good reason would create hostility.

### Abaye Nursing Excuses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: יָכְלָה לְמֵימַר, אִי פְּנוּיָה הִיא — ״בָּעֵינָא לְאִינְּסוֹבֵי״, אִי אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ הִיא — ״לָא קָא מִזְדַּהַמְנָא בְּאַפֵּי גַּבְרַאי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** The concern of enmity does not apply, because **she can say, if she is unmarried: I wish to get married.** And **if she is a married woman,** she may say: **I do not wish to become repulsive to my husband.**

*Abaye* again pushed back, arguing that here too a woman has ready-made excuses available to her. *Ei pnuya hi* — if she is unmarried, she can say: *ba'ina l'hinsuva* — I want to get married, and being seen nursing infants will make me appear either already a mother or less desirable as a wife, and men will not want to pursue me. *Ei eshet ish hi* — and if she is married, she can say: *lo migannina b'appei gavra* — I do not wish to become repulsive in the eyes of my husband, coming home smelling of milk and consumed with nursing another's child. Because plausible personal excuses are available, the refusal will not be interpreted as hostility, and therefore *eivah* does not compel her to accept even a paid nursing arrangement.

## Not Rescuing

### Idolaters & Shepherds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר רַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר, הָא דְּתַנְיָא: הַגּוֹיִם וְרוֹעֵי בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה לֹא מַעֲלִין וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef thought to say** a similar idea concerning **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **gentiles and shepherds of small domesticated animals, one may not raise** them out of a pit **and one may not lower** them into a pit.

A *baraita* is then cited that marks a sharp shift in tone and subject matter. *Ovdei kochavim v'ro'ei behemah dakkah* — idolaters and shepherds of small animals, such as sheep and goats that graze close to the ground and damage agricultural land — *lo ma'alin v'lo moridin* — one does not raise them from a pit and one does not lower them into one. That is: one takes no active steps to save their lives, but neither does one actively cause their death. The *Rambam* reads this passage as referring specifically to idolaters, not to non-Jews in general, which is a significant limiting interpretive move.

This is a deeply difficult passage. It sits in considerable tension with other *sugyot* throughout the *gemara* — such as the principle of *mefarnesin aniyei ovdei kochavim im aniyei Yisrael mipnei darchei shalom*, that we support the poor of non-Jews alongside the poor of Israel for the sake of peaceful relations. *Tosafot* engages at length with how these sources are to be reconciled. One consistent approach is the limiting one: these harsh rulings apply specifically to idolaters, not to all non-Jews, and that distinction does meaningful work in narrowing the scope.

These texts also have a history of censorship. Later manuscripts often read *lo hayu ma'alin* — they did not used to raise them — using the past tense to soften the force of the ruling for non-Jewish readers. The historical reality of the communities in which these texts were generated, living under oppressive conditions, shapes the rhetoric of the rabbinic voice here.

*Rav Yosef* thought to say that even in this case, *eivah* applies: *asukei b'sachar shari mishum eivah* — if they are offering you payment to rescue them, it is permitted because of *eivah*. The reasoning is that if someone in desperate circumstances offers you money for help and you refuse, that refusal — and any knowledge of it that spreads through the community — will breed hostility. Even if the person drowns, others who learn of the refusal may become enemies. Or perhaps the person manages to escape on their own and then bears a grievance against you. Either way, refusing a paid offer of rescue in a visible way creates *eivah*.

### Apostates Harsher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַגּוֹיִם וְרוֹעֵי בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה — לֹא מַעֲלִין וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין, אֲבָל הַמִּינִין וְהַמָּסוֹרוֹת וְהַמְשׁוּמָּדִים — מוֹרִידִין וְלֹא מַעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the notion of raising someone from or lowering him into a pit, the Gemara notes that **Rabbi Abbahu taught** the following while standing **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **gentiles and shepherds of domesticated animals, one may not raise** them from a pit, **and one may not lower** them into a pit. **But the heretics, and the informers, and the apostates [*vehameshummadim*] are lowered** into a pit, **but not raised** out of it.

The *baraita* then introduces a more severe category. *Rabbi Abbahu* taught before *Rabbi Yochanan*: for idolaters and shepherds of small animals, the rule is *lo ma'alin v'lo moridin* — no active rescue, no active killing. But *hamosrot vehamumrim* — the informers and the habitual violators — *hayu moridin velo ma'alin* — those one would actively throw into a well and not retrieve. These are figures who pose a direct existential threat to the Jewish community. Informers endanger Jewish lives by turning community members over to hostile authorities. The *mumer*, the habitual violator, is treated more severely than the ordinary non-Jew.

### Torah Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה ״לְכׇל אֲבֵדַת אָחִיךָ״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמְשׁוּמָּד, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מוֹרִידִין? סְמִי מִכָּאן מְשׁוּמָּד!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Abbahu: **I teach** that the verse: "And so you shall do **with every lost item of your brother"** (Deuteronomy 22:3), serves **to include the apostate** in one's obligation to return a lost item to another Jew; **and you say** that **one may lower** him into a pit? **Remove** the term **apostate from here.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* challenged *Rabbi Abbahu* on the inclusion of the *mumer* in this harsh category. He cited the verse *lekhol aveidat achikha* — for all the lost property of your brother — reading the word *lekhol* expansively, as extending the obligation of concern for another's well-being even to the *mumer*. You still have a relationship with this person; he is still *achikha*, your brother in some sense. So how can it be said that one would actively throw him into a well? *Rabbi Yochanan* accepts the ruling for the *min* and the *masor*, but insists the *mumer* is different. The *gemara* responds accordingly: *semi mikan mumer* — remove *mumer* from this list. The harsh ruling of active harm applies only to *minim* and *masorot*, and the *Rambam* adds *apikoros* to that list as well.

### Appetite vs. Spite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ: כָּאן בִּמְשׁוּמָּד אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת לְתֵיאָבוֹן, כָּאן בִּמְשׁוּמָּד אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת לְהַכְעִיס! קָסָבַר: אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת לְהַכְעִיס — מִין הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** Rabbi Abbahu **answer** Rabbi Yoḥanan as follows: **Here,** with regard to a lost item, the verse includes an apostate because it is referring **to an apostate** who **eats non-kosher meat due to** his **appetite,** i.e., he succumbs to the temptation. Conversely, **there,** with regard to raising an apostate from a pit, I am referring to **an apostate** who **eats non-kosher meat to express insolence.** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Abbahu **holds** that one who **eats non-kosher meat to express insolence is a heretic,** rather than an apostate.

The *gemara* then asks: why did *Rabbi Abbahu* not himself resolve the difficulty by distinguishing between two categories of *mumer*? There is the *mumer okhel neveilot lete'avon* — the person who eats forbidden food out of appetite, out of desire, or because it is cheaper or more convenient. He is not making a theological statement; his mundane desires simply override his religious commitments in a given moment. Such a person remains, in some meaningful sense, part of the community. He is a sinner, but sinners are still *achikha*. Then there is the *mumer okhel neveilot lehakh'is* — the person who eats forbidden food specifically to spite God. Even if you offer him perfectly good kosher food, he will *davka* choose the forbidden, as an act of defiance. This person has placed himself outside the community through intentional, principled rejection of its fundamental commitments.

Why couldn't *Rabbi Abbahu* have said: yes, a *mumer lete'avon* we have an obligation to, but a *mumer lehakh'is* is the one I said we throw into the well?

### Spite God Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר מְשׁוּמָּד, פְּלִיגִי רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא: חַד אָמַר: לְתֵיאָבוֹן — מְשׁוּמָּד, לְהַכְעִיס — מִין הָוֵי, וְחַד אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְהַכְעִיס נָמֵי מְשׁוּמָּד. אֶלָּא אֵיזֶהוּ מִין? זֶה הָעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** with regard to the definition of **an apostate** that **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree. One says** that someone who transgresses a prohibition **due to** his **appetite** is **an apostate,** while one who transgresses a prohibition in order **to express insolence is a heretic. And one says** that **even** one who sins **to express insolence** is considered **an apostate. Rather, who is** considered **a heretic? This is an idol worshipper.**

The *gemara* explains that *Rabbi Yochanan* holds *okhel neveilot lehakh'is min hu* — someone who violates *lehakh'is* is already in the *min* category. *Rabbi Yochanan* agrees that such a person is to be treated like a *min*; he simply classifies him that way. What he objects to is the routine use of the term *mumer* for such a person, as though a violator acting from spite is in the same category as one acting from appetite. So the ruling stands — *min* and *masor* are treated harshly — but the ordinary *mumer lete'avon* retains the status of *achikha*.

The *gemara* then records an *amora'ic* dispute: *Rav Aha* and *Ravina* disagreed about precisely where the line falls. One holds that *lete'avon* is a *mumer* and *lehakh'is* is a *min* — exactly as articulated above. The other holds that even *lehakh'is* is merely a *mumer*, and the *min* category is reserved for one who genuinely worships idols, *ha-oved elilei kochavim*. On this second, more lenient view, *lehakh'is* does not amount to apostasy; the person is still engaged with God, however perversely, and therefore remains within the *mumer* classification rather than being cast entirely outside the community.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot* disagree about the practical implications of this second position. Even according to the view that *lehakh'is* is a *mumer*, it does not necessarily follow that all the obligations toward a *mumer lete'avon* apply equally here.

### Insect Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אָכַל פַּרְעוֹשׁ אֶחָד אוֹ יַתּוּשׁ אֶחָד — הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּמָּד; וְהָא הָכָא דִּלְהַכְעִיס הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי מְשׁוּמָּד! הָתָם בָּעֵי לְמִיטְעַם טַעְמָא דְּאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* against the opinion that one who sins to express insolence is considered a heretic. The *baraita* teaches: If **one ate a single flea or a single mosquito, he is** considered **an apostate.** The Gemara clarifies the objection: **But here** it must be assumed **that** the insect was eaten **to express insolence,** as it is unappetizing, **and** yet this *baraita* **teaches** that one who eats a flea or a mosquito is **an apostate.** The Gemara answers: **There, he desires to experience the taste of forbidden** food, and therefore he is considered to be eating only due to temptation.

The *gemara* then challenges the first position — that *lehakh'is* already makes one a *min* — from a *baraita*: *akhal parosh ehad o yatush ehad harei zeh mumer* — if someone ate a gnat or a small insect, that person is a *mumer*. And presumably, who would eat a gnat except someone doing it *lehakh'is*, to be transgressive? Yet the *baraita* calls such a person a *mumer*, not a *min* — which seems to prove the more lenient position. The *gemara* rejects this inference: *hasam bitei'avon ta'ama de'issura* — in that case, the person may well be acting *lete'avon*, driven by the thrill of tasting something forbidden, curious about what the *issur* tastes like, rather than motivated by theological defiance of God. You therefore cannot prove from the insect case that *lehakh'is* falls within the *mumer* category.

## Prevention Methods

### Why State Lo Maalin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מוֹרִידִין אֲבָל לֹא מַעֲלִין. הַשְׁתָּא אַחוֹתֵי מַחֲתִינַן, אַסּוֹקֵי מִיבְּעֵי? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לֹא נִצְרְכָה, שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה מַעֲלָה בַּבּוֹר — מְגָרְרָהּ, דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ עִילָּא וְאָמַר: לָא תֵּיחוֹת חֵיוְתָא עִלָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the subject of lowering someone into a pit. **The Master said:** Heretics, informers, and apostates **are lowered** into a pit **but not raised** out of it. The Gemara analyzes this statement: **Now** that it is known that one actively **lowers** them into a pit, **is it necessary** to teach that one does not **raise** them from it? **Rav Yosef bar Ḥama said** that **Rav Sheshet said: No,** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* because it can be inferred from here **that if there was a ledge in the pit,** a Jew **scrapes it off** so that the one in the pit cannot ascend from it, **as** the Jew **employs a pretext and says** that he is removing the ledge so that **animals do not descend upon** the one in the pit while he is trapped in the pit.

The *gemara* now revisits the formulation of the *baraita* regarding *minim* and *masorot*: *moridin velo ma'alin* — one casts them down and does not bring them up. An obvious question presents itself: if one is permitted to actively throw them into a pit, of course one is not required to pull them out afterward. Why does the *baraita* need to state *lo ma'alin* separately? The *gemara* seeks to identify a meaningful independent application for the phrase.

### Ladder Removal

*Rav Yosef bar Chama* said in the name of *Rav Sheshes*: *lo nitzrecha* — the phrase is necessary for a different scenario. *She-im hayta maalah babor megarerah* — if there was a ladder inside the pit, one removes it. *Lo ma'alin* thus teaches not merely that one refrains from active rescue, but that one may actively prevent the person from being able to climb out on their own.

### Lion Excuse

And when doing so, one offers an excuse: *de-lo tichos chayusa le-iloi* — I am removing the ladder so that lions and wild animals should not come down it and attack. The cover story provides plausible deniability and prevents the act of passive harm from generating liability or conflict.

### Tosafot Authority

This formulation raises two questions in rapid succession. First: if one can actively throw a *min* or *masor* into the well to begin with, why does it require any explanation to remove a ladder? And second: why is an excuse even needed? *Tosafot* addresses this by distinguishing between two political situations. The harsh ruling of *moridin* — actively causing death — applies when the Jewish community is in a position of control, where such an act can be carried out without consequence. The ruling of *lo ma'alin* — preventing rescue while offering an excuse — reflects a situation of living under non-Jewish authority, where one must be more circumspect. In that context, the ruling teaches not merely passive nonrescue but active prevention with a cover story, because overt action against such individuals would be dangerous or impossible.

### Stone Cover

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לֹא נִצְרְכָה, שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה אֶבֶן עַל פִּי הַבְּאֵר, מְכַסָּהּ, אָמַר: לְעַבּוֹרֵי חַיּוּתָא עִילָּוַיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say** a different answer: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this, as it can be inferred from here **that if there was a stone at the mouth of the well** that one had fallen into, a Jew **covers it** and **says** that he is covering the opening in order **to pass his animals over it.**

*Rabbah* and *Rav Yosef* offer a parallel application: *she-im hayta even al pi habe'er mechaseh* — if there was a stone that could be placed over the mouth of the well, one covers it. The excuse offered is *le-vricha hu de-avdi lah* — I am covering the well so that my animals should not fall in. Perhaps one promises to remove the stone later and never does.

### Child Emergency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: שֶׁאִם הָיָה סוּלָּם, מְסַלְּקוֹ, אָמַר: בָּעֵינָא לְאַחוֹתֵי בְּרִי מֵאִיגָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** One can learn from here **that if there was a ladder** in the pit, a Jew **removes it** and **says: I require** the ladder **to lower my son from the roof.**

*Ravina* offers yet another variant: *she-im haya sulam mesalko ve-amar baina le-michas bri migra* — if there was a ladder, one removes it and explains that one needs it urgently to bring one's child down from the roof. The common thread across all these cases is the same: an act that prevents rescue is paired with a plausible innocent explanation to deflect suspicion and avoid generating hostile consequences.

## Non-Jew Mohel

### Purpose of Milah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל מָל אֶת הַגּוֹי לְשׁוּם גֵּר, לְאַפּוֹקֵי לְשׁוּם מוּרְנָא דְּלָא, וְגוֹי לֹא יָמוּל יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲשׁוּדִין עַל שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the subject of assisting or receiving aid from a gentile in the context of circumcision. **The Sages taught: A Jew may circumcise a gentile for the sake of** making him **a convert.** This is **to the exclusion of** circumcising a gentile **for the sake of** removing **a worm [*murna*], which** is **not** permitted, as it is forbidden to heal a gentile. **But** one **may not** allow **a gentile** to **circumcise a Jew** in any situation, **because** gentiles **are suspected of bloodshed.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *gemara* then introduces a *baraita* that pivots the discussion toward the laws of *brit milah*. *Yisrael mal oveid kochavim leshum ger* — a Jew may circumcise a non-Jew for the purpose of conversion, which is straightforward. But not *leshum murna* — not for the sake of a worm, a medical condition, according to *Rashi*. This prohibition is continuous with the theme of not rendering active assistance to non-Jews in ways that primarily benefit them without a corresponding Jewish interest.

### Meir: Danger

The *baraita* then turns to the question of a non-Jewish *mohel* circumcising a Jew. *Ve'oveid kochavim lo yamol Yisrael mipnei shechashudim al shefichut damim, divrei Rabbi Meir* — a non-Jew may not perform circumcision on a Jew, because non-Jews are suspected of bloodshed. This is entirely consistent with *Rabbi Meir*'s characteristic stringency throughout this chapter. What is interesting, however, is the implication: the ruling is framed entirely in terms of danger, not in terms of halachic invalidity. The circumcision performed by a non-Jew would apparently be *kosher* in principle — the only objection is the physical risk of submitting oneself to a non-Jew with a blade.

### Sages: Supervised OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: גּוֹי מָל אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֲחֵרִים עוֹמְדִין עַל גַּבּוֹ, אֲבָל בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינוֹ — לָא. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֲחֵרִים עוֹמְדִים עַל גַּבּוֹ נָמֵי לָא, דְּזִימְנִין דְּמַצְלֵי לֵיהּ סַכִּינָא וּמְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ כְּרוּת שׇׁפְכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** One **may** allow **a gentile** to **circumcise a Jew while others are standing over him** and observing his actions, **but not** when they are **alone together. And Rabbi Meir says: Even** where **others are standing over him** it is **also not** permitted, **as** there are **times when** a gentile might **tilt the knife and render** the Jew **one whose penis has been severed,** and he will be unable to father children.

*Chachamim* disagree, ruling: *oveid kochavim mal et Yisrael bizman she'acherim omdim al gabav* — a non-Jew may circumcise a Jew when other people are present and supervising, ensuring there is no opportunity for harm. *Aval beino leveino lo* — but not in private. This means the *Chachamim* accept a non-Jew as a halachically valid *mohel* for a Jew, which is a striking ruling that cuts against the intuition that entry into the covenant must be mediated by a covenantal member.

### Knife Slip

*Rabbi Meir*'s position is then explained: even with supervision, he says, there is a concern — *dezimnin dematzli bei sakina umashvei lei k'rut shofcha* — because sometimes the *mohel* may slip his knife and effectively render the person a *k'rut shofcha*, a eunuch. Even in front of witnesses, a subtle movement of the hand in the midst of the procedure could pass as an accident. Supervision, for *Rabbi Meir*, cannot eliminate this particular danger.

## Samaritan/Non-Jew

### No Jewish Doctor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר גּוֹי לָא? וּרְמִינְהוּ: עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ רוֹפֵא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיֵשׁ בָּהּ רוֹפֵא כּוּתִי וְרוֹפֵא אַרְמַאי — יָמוּל אַרְמַאי וְאַל יָמוּל כּוּתִי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יָמוּל כּוּתִי וְאַל יָמוּל אַרְמַאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does Rabbi Meir hold** that one **may not** allow **a gentile** to circumcise a Jew? **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** against this claim from the following *baraita*: In **a city in which there is no Jewish physician, and in which there is a Samaritan physician and an Aramean,** i.e., a gentile, **physician,** it is preferable that the **Aramean circumcise** the Jewish boys of the city **and** the **Samaritan not circumcise** them. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is preferable that the **Samaritan circumcise** the boys **and** the **Aramean not circumcise** them. Rabbi Meir holds that it is preferable for an Aramean gentile to perform circumcision despite the fact that Samaritans are considered Jewish to a certain extent.

The *gemara* now introduces another *baraita* that appears to contradict *Rabbi Meir*'s position. *Ir she'ein bah rofeh Yisrael* — in a city that has no Jewish doctor or *mohel* — *veyish bah rofeh Kuti verofeh oveid kochavim* — and there is both a Samaritan *mohel* and a non-Jewish *mohel* available — which of the two should perform the *brit milah*?

### Meir: Non-Jew Yes

*Yamol oveid kochavim ve'al yamol Kuti, divrei Rabbi Meir* — *Rabbi Meir* says: let the non-Jew do it rather than the Samaritan. This is initially puzzling on two counts. First, why not the Samaritan, who at least has some form of Jewish practice? *Rashi* explains that Samaritans performed *brit milah* with idolatrous intent, which disqualifies their *milah* on religious grounds. But the second and deeper problem: why does *Rabbi Meir* permit the non-Jew at all, given that he was just said to oppose it because of the danger of injury?

### Yehuda: Samaritan Yes

*Rabbi Yehuda* rules the opposite: *yamol Kuti ve'al yamol oveid kochavim* — let the Samaritan do it rather than the non-Jew. His reasoning is principled: Samaritans are halachically Jewish, and since a *brit milah* requires a member of the covenant to perform it, the Samaritan qualifies while the non-Jew does not.

### Gemara Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיפוֹךְ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יָמוּל כּוּתִי וְלֹא אַרְמַאי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַרְמַאי וְלֹא כּוּתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Reverse** their opinions, so that **Rabbi Meir says:** It is preferable that the **Samaritan circumcise** the boys **and not** the **Aramean,** and **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is preferable that the **Aramean** circumcise them **and not** the **Samaritan.**

The *gemara* considers reversing the attributions — assigning *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position to *Rabbi Meir* and vice versa — in order to achieve internal consistency: perhaps *Rabbi Meir* actually said Samaritan yes and non-Jew no, consistent with his danger concern about non-Jews. But this reversal fails. *Rabbi Yehuda* cannot be the one permitting non-Jews as *mohelim*, because we have an explicit *baraita* in which *Rabbi Yehuda* categorically excludes non-Jews from performing *brit milah* as a matter of halachic validity.

### Covenant Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַרְמַאי שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְמִילָה בְּגוֹי שֶׁהִיא פְּסוּלָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה אֶת בְּרִיתִי תִשְׁמֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yehuda** actually **hold** that it is **permitted** for circumcision to be performed by **an Aramean? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: From where** is it derived with regard **to circumcision** performed **by a gentile that** it **is not valid?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated:** "And God said to Abraham: **And as for you, you shall keep My covenant,** you, and your seed after you throughout their generations" (Genesis 17:9). "My covenant [*beriti*]" here is a reference to circumcision [*berit mila*], and therefore the verse is teaching that only Abraham and his descendants, i.e., Jews, are qualified to perform circumcision.

*Rabbi Yehuda*'s source is the verse *ve-ata et briti tishmor* — and you, Avraham, shall observe My covenant. The circumcision of Avraham and his descendants is a covenantal act, and the verse implies that only one who is himself within the covenant — a Jew — can perform it. A non-Jew stands outside the covenant entirely and therefore lacks the standing to perform the act that initiates others into it. This makes *Rabbi Yehuda*'s categorical exclusion of the non-Jew entirely coherent and textually grounded, and it means the reversal proposed by the *gemara* cannot work.

### Expert Removes Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּרוֹפֵא מוּמְחֶה
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara suggests, **actually do not reverse it.** As for the apparent contradiction between the two statements of Rabbi Meir, the Gemara explains: **And what are we dealing with here? [We are dealing] with an expert physician,**

The attributions therefore stand as originally stated. But this leaves the original question: how does *Rabbi Meir* permit the non-Jew, given his concern about danger? The *gemara* answers: *ve-hakha be-mai askinan be-rofe mumkhe* — here we are dealing with a professional expert. A *mohel* who has an established professional reputation would not risk that reputation by allowing something to go wrong, or by acting in any way that could be perceived as deliberate harm. The concern that a non-Jew with a knife might "accidentally" injure a patient does not apply to a recognized expert whose livelihood depends on his reliability and precision. Therefore, *Rabbi Meir*'s danger concern is not triggered in this case.

### Final Positions

To summarize the final positions: *Rabbi Meir* holds that a non-Jew is halachically valid as a *mohel* — he does not categorically exclude non-Jews from performing *brit milah* — but his concern about physical danger prevents him from permitting it in ordinary circumstances. When the non-Jew in question is a proven professional, that danger concern is resolved, and *Rabbi Meir* permits it. He objects to the Samaritan not on grounds of danger but on grounds of *avodah zarah* intent. *Rabbi Yehuda*, by contrast, does not share *Rabbi Meir*'s particular concern about physical danger from non-Jews, but holds that non-Jews are categorically excluded from performing *brit milah* as a matter of halachic principle, derived from the covenant language of the verse. He therefore permits the Samaritan, who is halachically Jewish, while categorically forbidding the non-Jew regardless of expertise.
