# Eruvin 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mazon Shtei Seudot

### R. Yochanan: Half Kav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: מִכִּכָּר בְּפוּנְדְּיוֹן, מֵאַרְבַּע סְאִין בְּסֶלַע. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּי יָדוֹת לַכִּכָּר מִשָּׁלֹשׁ לַקַּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says:** Food for two meals is the size **of a loaf** bought **with a *pundeyon*,** which is one-forty-eighth of a *sela*, **when four *se'a*** of wheat are sold **for a *sela*. Rabbi Shimon says:** Food for two meals is **two** of three **parts of a loaf, when three** loaves are prepared **from a *kav*** of wheat. In other words, the measure is two-thirds of a loaf the size of one-third of a *kav*.

The daf opens with a deceptively simple question: how much bread constitutes *mazon shtei seudot*, the two meals' worth of food that determines the minimum size of an *eruv*? The answer requires close attention to the coin-based pricing system by which the Mishnah defines loaf size.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's position is this: the relevant loaf is the kind that can be purchased for one *pundion* when four *se'in* of flour are going for one *sela* on the market. To unpack the arithmetic — one *se'ah* equals six *kav*, so four *se'in* equal twenty-four *kav*. A *sela* contains forty-eight *pundion*. Therefore, for every two *pundion* spent, one *kav* of bread is obtained. A single *pundion* thus buys half a *kav*. According to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, a half *kav* is the size of the loaf — and since the Mishnah is discussing two meals' worth, each *seuda* in his view is a quarter of a *kav*.

### R. Shimon: One-Third Kav

Rabbi Shimon's position, stated as *shtei yodot l'kikar mi-shalosh l'kav*, holds that a *kav* yields three loaves, so a single loaf is one-third of a *kav*. However, Rabbi Shimon specifies that *shtei yodot* — two-thirds — of that loaf constitutes the *eruv* measure. Two-thirds of one-third is two-ninths of a *kav*, and since this represents two *seudot*, a single *seuda* in his system equals one-ninth of a *kav*.

### Four vs. Nine Meals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וּקְרוֹבִים דִּבְרֵיהֶן לִהְיוֹת שָׁוִין. מִי דָּמֵי?! דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַרְבַּע סְעוֹדָתָא לְקַבָּא, דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תֵּשַׁע סְעוֹדָתָא לְקַבָּא!
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** in the *Tosefta*: **And their statements are close to being identical.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this: **Are they** really **identical?** According to the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** there are **four meals to a *kav*.** He maintains that food for two meals is equivalent to a loaf that can be purchased with a *pundeyon*, which is one-forty-eighth of a *sela*, when four *se'a* are bought for a *sela*. As there are twenty-four *kav* in four *se'a*, a loaf purchased with a *pundeyon* is half of a *kav*. This means that each meal is a quarter of a *kav*, or that there are four meals in a *kav*. In contrast, according to the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon,** there are **nine meals to a *kav*.** Rabbi Shimon maintains that food for two meals is two-thirds of a loaf equal in size to one-third of a *kav*. By this calculation, one meal is one-third of a loaf that itself is one-third of a *kav* in size, which amounts to nine meals in a *kav*.

The *Gemara* then cites a *Tannaitic* source stating *ukrovin divreihem l'hiyot shavin* — that their positions are approximately equal. The *Gemara* immediately objects: *mi dami*? How can this be? According to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, a half *kav* equals two *seudot*, so there are four *seudot* per *kav*. According to Rabbi Shimon, two-ninths of a *kav* equals two *seudot*, so there are nine *seudot* per *kav*. The gap between four and nine is not trivially close, and the *Gemara* presses on how the *Braita* could claim otherwise.

### Baker's Profit Margin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: צֵא מֵהֶן שְׁלִישׁ לַחֶנְוָנִי. וְאַכַּתִּי לְמָר תִּשְׁעָה. וּלְמָר שֵׁית! [אֶלָּא כְּאִידַּךְ] דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר: צֵא מֵהֶן מֶחֱצָה לַחֶנְוָנִי. וְאַכַּתִּי: לְמָר תֵּשַׁע, וּלְמָר תַּמְנֵי! — הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר ״וּקְרוֹבִים דִּבְרֵיהֶם לִהְיוֹת שָׁוִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** **Deduct one-third for the grocer's** markup, as he takes one-third as profit. This adds one half to the total cost. The Gemara asks: Even after allowing for this adjustment, it **remains** the case that **according to** this **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, there are **nine** meals to a *kav*, **whereas according to** the other **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, although it suffices for more than four meals, there are only **six** meals to a *kav*. According to the measure of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Broka, each meal is two-thirds of a quarter *kav*, i.e., one sixth of a *kav*, which still leaves a considerable discrepancy between their measures. **Rather,** we must explain **in accordance with** that **other** statement of **Rav Ḥisda, as he said: Deduct one half for the grocer's** markup. If the calculation is based on the assumption that the grocer takes half the sum as profit, the two measures are indeed close. The Gemara asks: **But** it **remains** the case that **according to** this **Sage,** there are **nine** meals to a *kav*, whereas **according to** the other **Sage,** there are only **eight** meals to a *kav*, as each meal is one half of a quarter *kav*, or one-eighth of a *kav*. The Gemara answers: **This is** the meaning of **that which was stated** in the *Tosefta*: **And their rulings are close to being identical.** Although their measures are not exactly the same, there is no great difference between them.

Rav Chisda resolves the difficulty by introducing the profit margin of the *chanvani*, the baker or retailer. The prices cited in the Mishnah reflect the wholesale cost of flour. A baker, however, does not sell at wholesale rates; he marks up the price. Rav Chisda's first formulation holds that the *chanvani* takes a one-third profit margin — meaning that for the same *pundion*, he sells only two-thirds of what the wholesale price would suggest. Applying this to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka: the nominal amount was a half *kav*, but the actual bread sold for a *pundion* is two-thirds of a half, which is one-third of a *kav*. One-third of a *kav* for two *seudot* means one *seuda* equals one-sixth of a *kav*, yielding six *seudot* per *kav*. Yet Rabbi Shimon still gives nine — so six and nine remain far apart.

The *Gemara* therefore invokes a second teaching of Rav Chisda: *tfei mehen palga l'chanvani*, the baker actually charges a fifty-percent markup. At a fifty-percent margin, the baker sells only half of the wholesale quantity for the same price. Half of a half *kav* is a quarter of a *kav* for two *seudot*, making one *seuda* one-eighth of a *kav*, or eight *seudot* per *kav*. Now eight and nine are genuinely close — and this, the *Gemara* explains, is what the *Braita* means when it says their positions are approximately equal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא אַדְּרַב חִסְדָּא! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — דְּקָא יָהֵיב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת צִיבֵי. הָא — דְּלָא יָהֵיב בַּעַל הַבַּיִת צִיבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Nonetheless, there is **a contradiction between** one statement of **Rav Ḥisda and** the other statement of **Rav Ḥisda** concerning the size of the grocer's profit. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as in **this** case, **the homeowner provides the wood** for baking bread, and the grocer therefore marks up the price by one-third as compensation for his efforts. Whereas in **that** case, **the homeowner does not provide the wood;** the grocer is entitled to reimbursement for the cost of the wood in addition to the work of baking, and consequently he marks up the price by one half.

The *Gemara* then asks: is there a contradiction between Rav Chisda's two statements — one-third markup versus fifty-percent markup? The resolution is that it depends on who supplies the wood for baking. If the homeowner provides the firewood, the baker's labor alone is compensated, and a one-third margin suffices. If the baker must supply the wood himself, his costs are higher and he charges a fifty-percent margin. In both cases, the Mishnah's stated price reflects the wholesale flour cost, not the retail bread price, and accounting for the baker's margin is essential to understanding how much bread actually changes hands.

## Pras & Tumat Ochlim

### Loaf to Eggs Conversion

Having established the size of two *seudot* in terms of *kav*, the *Gemara* now translates these quantities into *beitzim* — eggs — the standard sub-unit of volume measurement. A *kav* contains twenty-four *beitzim*. According to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, the loaf (representing two *seudot*) is a quarter of a *kav*, so one *seuda* — the *pras* — is one-eighth of a *kav*, which equals three *beitzim*. According to Rabbi Shimon, the loaf is one-third of a *kav* (the two-thirds measure being specific to the *eruv* context), so one *seuda* is one-sixth of a *kav*, which equals four *beitzim*.

One important clarification belongs here: Rabbi Shimon's *shtei yodot* — the two-thirds reduction — was applied only in the context of *eruv*. For all other halachic purposes, including the measurements that follow, the full loaf size is used. So the operative debate is whether the loaf itself is a quarter of a *kav* (Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka) or a third of a *kav* (Rabbi Shimon), and correspondingly whether a *pras* — half a loaf, one *seuda* — is three or four *beitzim*.

### Pras Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶצְיָיהּ, לַבַּיִת הַמְנוּגָּע. וַחֲצִי חֶצְיָיהּ, לִפְסוֹל אֶת הַגְּוִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** Having discussed measures with regard to a loaf of bread, the mishna states that **half** of this loaf is the amount called a half [*peras*], a measure relevant **for** the *halakhot* of **a leprous house.** If one enters a house afflicted with leprosy and remains there long enough to eat this amount of food, the clothes he is wearing become ritually impure. **And half of its half,** a quarter of this loaf, is the amount of ritually impure food that **disqualifies the body.** In other words, impure food of this amount imparts ritual impurity to the body of the eater, and disqualifies him by rabbinic law from eating *teruma*.

The *Gemara* now extends this framework to several other halachic domains. The text reads: *chetzi l'veit hamenuga, v'chatzi chetziyah lifsol et hageviyah*. Half of the loaf — one *seuda*, the *pras* — is the relevant measure for the *beit hamenuga*, the house afflicted with *tzara'at*: the time it takes to eat a *pras* determines how long one must remain inside before becoming *tamei*. This is the *kedai achilat pras* measure that appears across many areas of halachah.

Half again — half of the *pras* — is the measure for *pissul hageviyah*, rendering oneself invalid through eating forbidden food. This would be one-and-a-half or two *beitzim* depending on the position. And half of that — one-quarter of a *pras* — is the threshold for *tumat ochlin*, food impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וַחֲצִי חֲצִי חֶצְיָהּ לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין. וְתַנָּא דִּידַן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא תָּנֵי טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא שָׁווּ שִׁיעוּרַיְיהוּ לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** in the *Tosefta*: **And half of one half of its half,** one-eighth of this loaf, is the minimum measure of food that **contracts the** ritual **impurity of foods.** The Gemara asks: **And our *tanna*,** in the mishna, for **what reason** did **he did not teach** the measure of **the impurity of foods?** The Gemara answers: He did not state this *halakha* **because their measures are not** precisely **identical.** The measure for the impurity of foods is not exactly half the amount of ritually impure food that disqualifies one from eating *teruma*.

The *Gemara* at the top of 83a then introduces one further subdivision: *v'chatzi chatzi chetziyah l'tamei tumat ochlin* — a half of the half of the half is the measure for rendering food susceptible to or transmitting *tumah*. Working through the arithmetic, this yields either three-quarters of a *beitza* (following Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's three-*beitzim pras*) or one full *beitza* (following Rabbi Shimon's four-*beitzim pras*). The standard ruling of one *beitza* for *tumat ochlin* aligns with Rabbi Shimon's position.

The practical halachic stakes of this three-versus-four debate are significant. If one rules that *tumat ochlin* is one *beitza*, then by working backward, *kedai achilat pras* is four *beitzim* — Rabbi Shimon's position. Rashi understands two *zeitim* per *beitza*, making *kedai achilat pras* eight *kezayitim*. Rambam, however, in at least one formulation, rules by Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's three-*beitzim* measure, yet holds three *zeitim* per *beitza*, yielding nine *kezayitim*. The practical question of how quickly one must eat *matzah* on Pesach, or how much constitutes a halachically significant eating, turns on this unresolved debate between eight and nine *kezayitim*.

### Precision Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: כַּמָּה שִׁיעוּר חֲצִי פְרָס? שְׁתֵּי בֵיצִים חָסֵר קִימְעָא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּי בֵיצִים שׂוֹחֲקוֹת. שִׁיעֵר רַבִּי, שְׁתֵּי בֵיצִים וָעוֹד. כַּמָּה וָעוֹד? אֶחָד מֵעֶשְׂרִים בַּבֵּיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **How much is half a *peras*? Two eggs minus a little;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: Two large eggs,** slightly larger ones than average. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **measured** the amount of half a *peras* after calculating the number of *kav* in the *se'a* brought before him, and found it to be **a little more than two eggs.** The *tanna* asks: **How much** is this **little more? One-twentieth of an egg.**

Having laid out the full chain of subdivisions, the *Gemara* raises a pointed question: *u'tana didan mai ta'ama lo tana tumat ochlin*? Why did our *Tanna* stop before reaching *tumat ochlin*? If the subdivisions are this neat, why not carry them all the way through?

The answer is *mishum d'lo shavu shiuraya l'hadadi* — because the sizes do not precisely match up with one another. A *Braita* then demonstrates the imprecision: *bei beitzim chasar kima divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda says the relevant measure is two *beitzim* minus a little. The loaf size was never defined with the precision of a fixed number of eggs; it was defined by how much bread people actually eat, which is inherently approximate. The *tumat ochlin* measure, by contrast, is exact — it is defined as precisely one *beitza* (or three-quarters, depending on position), and is not derived from these meal-based calculations. Mixing an imprecise chain of subdivisions with a precise benchmark would be misleading, and that is why the *Mishnah* did not include *tumat ochlin* in this sequence.

### Egg with/without Shell

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי נָתָן וְרַבִּי דּוֹסָא אָמְרוּ: כְּבֵיצָה שֶׁאָמְרוּ כָּמוֹהָ וְכִקְלִיפָּתָהּ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כָּמוֹהָ בְּלֹא קְלִיפָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **In contrast, concerning the impurity of foods, it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Dosa said** that the measure of **an egg-bulk, which** the Sages **said** is the amount that contracts the impurity of foods, is **equivalent to it,** i.e., the egg, **and its shell. And the Rabbis say:** It is **equivalent to it without its shell.** These amounts are not precisely half of any of the measurements given for half a *peras*.

A separate dispute recorded in a *Braita* and cited by Rabbah bar Papa in the name of Rav Chisda addresses a related question: when the Sages said *kebeitza* for *tumat ochlin*, did they mean an egg including its shell or without? Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Dosa say with the shell; the Sages say without. This is a dispute about a precise measurement, not a derived one — which is precisely the *Gemara*'s point. *Tumat ochlin* is defined by the egg itself, exactly, while all the meal-based measures are approximations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּבֵיצָה וּמֶחֱצָה שׂוֹחֲקוֹת. וּמַאן חֲכָמִים — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה.
>
> **Translation:** As for the issue itself, **Rafram bar Pappa said** that **Rav Ḥisda said: This** *baraita* that clarifies the measure of half a *peras* is in accordance with **the statements of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei,** a measure that is identical to that of Rabbi Shimon in the mishna. **But the Rabbis say: One and one half large egg-bulks. And who are** these **Rabbis? Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka.**

The *Gemara* then notes that in Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's system, the *chatzi pras* comes out to one-and-a-half *beitzim sochakos* — also slightly larger than a bare number — and this too is slightly imprecise in the same direction as Rabbi's formulation.

## Se'ah Measurement

### 217 Eggs Puzzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: שִׁגֵּר בּוֹנְיוֹס לְרַבִּי מוֹדְיָא דְקוֹנְדֵּיס דְּמִן נְאוּסָא, וְשִׁיעֵר רַבִּי מָאתַן וּשְׁבַע עֶשְׂרֵה בֵּיעִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said:** A person named **Bonyos sent Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **a measure [***modya***] of a *se'a* from** a place called **Na'usa,** where they had a tradition that it was an ancient and accurate measure (Ritva). **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **measured** it and found it contained **217 eggs.**

The *Gemara* reports that when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he transmitted an account: Bunios sent Rabbi a *se'ah* measure from the town of Ni'usa, and Rabbi estimated that it held 217 eggs. This figure demands explanation, because the straightforward calculation gives a very different result. One *se'ah* equals six *kav*, and one *kav* equals twenty-four *beitzim*, so six times twenty-four yields 144 eggs. How does one arrive at 217?

### Three Se'ah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא סְאָה דְּהֵיכָא? אִי דְּמִדְבָּרִית, מֵאָה אַרְבָּעִים וְאַרְבַּע הָוְיָא! וְאִי דִּירוּשַׁלְמִית, מֵאָה שִׁבְעִים וְשָׁלֹשׁ הָוְיָא! וְאִי דְּצִיפּוֹרִית, מָאתַיִם וְשֶׁבַע הָוְיִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This *se'a*, from where** is it, i.e., on what measure is it based? **If** it is based on **the wilderness** *se'a*, the standard measure used by Moses in the wilderness, which is the basis for all the Torah's measurements of volume, the difficulty is that a *se'a* is composed of six *kav*, where each *kav* is equivalent to four *log* and each *log* is equivalent to six egg-bulks. This means that a *se'a* **is** equivalent to a total of **144** egg-bulks. **And if** it is **the Jerusalem** *se'a*, then the *se'a* **is** only **173** egg-bulks, as they enlarged the measures in Jerusalem by adding a fifth to the measures of the wilderness. **And if** it is a *se'a* **of Tzippori,** as the measures were once again increased in Tzippori, where another fifth was added to the Jerusalem measure, the *se'a* **is 207** egg-bulks. The *se'a* measured by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi does not correspond to any of these measures of a *se'a*.

The *Gemara* resolves the starting confusion by asking *ha se'ah deheicha* — which *se'ah* are we talking about? Three distinct measures went by the name *se'ah* in different eras and locales: the *se'ah midbarit* (the Wilderness measure from the Torah's time), the *se'ah Yerushalmit* (the Jerusalem measure from the period of the kings), and the *se'ah Tziporit* (the measure used in the Rabbinic period, associated with Tzippori). Each successive measure was larger than the previous one.

The *se'ah midbarit*, with the simple calculation of six *kav* at twenty-four *beitzim* each, yields 144 eggs. The *se'ah Yerushalmit* is one-sixth larger — that is, twenty percent larger — than the *midbarit*, so it contains 144 plus approximately 28.8, which rounds to 173 eggs. The *se'ah Tziporit* is in turn one-sixth larger than the *Yerushalmit*, another twenty percent increase, bringing it to approximately 207.6 eggs, which the *Gemara* rounds to 207. Even so, 207 is not 217. The *Gemara* is aware of this gap.

### Math Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּצִיפּוֹרִית, אַיְיתִי חַלְּתָא שְׁדִי עֲלַיְיהוּ. חַלְּתָא כַּמָּה הָוְיָין? תַּמְנֵי, אַכַּתִּי בָּצַר לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אַיְיתִי וְעוֹדוֹת דְּרַבִּי שְׁדִי עֲלַיְיהוּ. אִי הָכִי, הָוֵי לֵיהּ טְפֵי! כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הָוֵי כְּבֵיצָה, לָא חָשֵׁיב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** this measure is based **on the** *se'a* **of Tzippori,** but you must **bring** the amount of **the *ḥalla*** given to a priest, **and add it to them.** That is to say, although this measure is slightly larger than a *se'a*, if it is used for flour and you deduct the amount due as *ḥalla*, you are left with exactly one *se'a*, or 207 egg-bulks. The Gemara raises an objection: The amount of ***ḥalla*, how many** egg-bulks **is it?** Approximately **eight** egg-bulks, one-twenty-fourth of 207. Yet in that case, **it remains less** than 217 egg-bulks, for even if we were to add another eight egg-bulks for *ḥalla* to the 207 egg-bulks, we would have only 215 egg-bulks, almost 216 to be more precise, which is still less than 217. **Rather,** you must **bring** the **excess** amounts of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, the little more he included in his measure, **and add** these **to them.** In Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's calculations, he did not factor in the *ḥalla* that had to be separated. Instead, the egg-bulks he used to measure the *se'a* were small egg-bulks. Consequently, one-twentieth of an egg-bulk must be added for each egg-bulk. Since one-twentieth of 207 egg-bulks is roughly ten, the total amount equals 217 egg-bulks. The Gemara raises an objection: **If so, it is** still slightly **more** than 217 egg-bulks, by seven-twentieths of an egg-bulk, to be precise. The Gemara answers: **Since it is not** more than 217 egg-bulks by **a** whole **egg, he did not count it.**

The *Gemara* offers two additional factors that, combined, account for the remaining difference. First, *aiti chalta shadi aleihu* — add the *challah* portion. When baking bread from this quantity of flour, one must separate *challah*, which is one twenty-fourth of the dough. Adding one twenty-fourth of 207 gives approximately 8 more, bringing the total to roughly 215. This is still short of 217.

The second adjustment draws on Rabbi's own teaching that the *beitza* as a sub-unit is not perfectly divisible into larger measures — every *beitza* in this system is actually a *beitza* plus one-twentieth. That is, the *se'ah Tziporit* measured in terms of real eggs contains not 207 eggs but 207 eggs each of which is one-twentieth larger than a nominal *beitza*. One-twentieth of 207 is approximately 10, and adding this to 207 yields approximately 217. The *Gemara* notes that the result is slightly more than 217, but since the remainder is less than a full *beitza*, it is not counted, and 217 is the rounded figure.

### Growth Ratios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן סְאָה יְרוּשַׁלְמִית יְתֵירָה עַל מִדְבָּרִית שְׁתוּת. וְשֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִית יְתֵירָה עַל יְרוּשַׁלְמִית שְׁתוּת. נִמְצֵאת שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִית יְתֵירָה עַל מִדְבָּרִית שְׁלִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A Jerusalem *se'a* is larger than a wilderness** *se'a* **by one-sixth, and that of Tzippori is larger than a Jerusalem** *se'a* **by one-sixth. Consequently,** a *se'a* **of Tzippori is larger than a wilderness** *se'a* **by one-third.**

A *Braita* addresses the growth relationships among the three *se'ah* types directly: the *se'ah Yerushalmit* exceeds the *midbarit* by one-sixth (*shtos*), and the *se'ah Tziporit* exceeds the *Yerushalmit* by a further one-sixth. The *Braita* then states that the *Tziporit* therefore exceeds the *midbarit* by a full third — *shelish*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלִישׁ דְּמַאן? אִילֵּימָא שְׁלִישׁ דְּמִדְבָּרִית, מִכְּדִי שְׁלִישׁ דְּמִדְבָּרִית כַּמָּה הָוֵי — אַרְבְּעִין וּתְמָנְיָא, וְאִילּוּ עוּדְפָּא, שִׁיתִּין וּתְלָא. וְאֶלָּא שְׁלִישׁ דִּירוּשַׁלְמִית: שְׁלִישׁ דִּידַהּ כַּמָּה הָוֵי — חַמְשִׁין וּתְמָנְיָא נְכֵי תִּילְתָּא, וְאִילּוּ עוּדְפָּא, שִׁתִּין וּתְלָא. וְאֶלָּא דְּצִיפּוֹרִי: שְׁלִישׁ דִּידַהּ כַּמָּה הָוֵי — שִׁבְעִין נְכֵי חֲדָא, וְאִילּוּ עוּדְפָּא, שִׁשִּׁים וְשָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **One-third of which** measurement? **If you say** it means **one-third of a wilderness** *se'a*, **now** you must consider: **One-third of a wilderness** *se'a*, **how much is it? Forty-eight** egg-bulks, and **yet the difference** between the wilderness *se'a* and the Tzippori *se'a* is **sixty-three** egg-bulks. As stated above, a Tzippori *se'a* is 207 egg-bulks, whereas a wilderness *se'a* is only 144 egg-bulks. **But rather,** this one-third mentioned in the *baraita* is referring to **one-third of a Jerusalem** *se'a*, which is 173 egg-bulks, as stated above. The Gemara again examines the calculation: **One-third of that** *se'a*, **how much is it? Fifty-eight less one-third,** and **yet the difference** between the wilderness and the Tzippori *se'a* is **sixty-three. Rather,** you must say that it is referring to one-third of a **Tzippori** *se'a*. **One-third of that** *se'a*, **how much is it? Seventy less one-third,** and **yet the difference** between the wilderness *se'a* and the Tzippori *se'a* is **sixty-three** egg-bulks. The difference between the measures is not exactly one-third according to any of the known *se'a* measurements.

The *Gemara* subjects this claim to careful scrutiny. *Shelish de'man* — a third of which measure? If a third of the *midbarit* (144), that gives a difference of 48. But the actual difference between the *Tziporit* (207) and the *midbarit* (144) is 63, not 48. If a third of the *Yerushalmit* (173), that is approximately 57 and a fraction — still not 63. If a third of the *Tziporit* itself (207), that is approximately 69 — too large. No interpretation of the *Braita*'s "third" produces the actual difference of 63.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִמְצֵאת סְאָה שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי יְתֵירָה עַל מִדְבָּרִית קָרוֹב לִשְׁלִישׁ שֶׁלָּהּ. וּשְׁלִישׁ שֶׁלָּהּ קָרוֹב לְמֶחֱצָה דְּמִדְבָּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **this is** what the *tanna* **is saying: Consequently, a *se'a* of Tzippori is larger than a wilderness *se'a*** by sixty-three egg-bulks, which is **close to one-third of** a Tzippori *se'a* of sixty-nine egg-bulks. **And one-third of it,** sixty-nine egg-bulks, **is close to half of a wilderness** *se'a* of seventy-two egg-bulks.

Rav Yirmiah proposes a qualified reading: the *Braita* should be understood as saying the *Tziporit* exceeds the *midbarit* by *approximately* a third of its own size — *karov l'shelish*. A third of the *Tziporit* (approximately 69) is indeed in the vicinity of the actual difference. He further notes that this one-third of the *Tziporit* is itself *karov l'machtza d'midbarit* — approximately half of the *midbarit* measure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: מִידֵּי ״קָרוֹב״ ״קָרוֹב״ קָתָנֵי? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִמְצֵאת שְׁלִישׁ שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי בִּוְעוֹדָיוֹת שֶׁל רַבִּי יְתֵירָה עַל מֶחֱצָה שֶׁל מִדְבָּרִית שְׁלִישׁ בֵּיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raised an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yirmeya: **Does** the *baraita* **state** either: **Close** to one-third of a Tzippori *se'a* or: **Close** to half of a wilderness *se'a*? The wording of the *baraita* indicates an exact amount. **Rather, Ravina said** that **this is** what the *tanna* **is saying: Consequently, one-third of a Tzippori** *se'a* together **with the excess** amounts **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is greater than half** of **a wilderness** *se'a* of seventy-two egg-bulks by only **one-third of an egg.** In other words, a Tzippori *se'a* of 207 egg-bulks added to the excess amounts of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi of one-twentieth of an egg-bulk for each egg-bulk amounts to a total of 217 egg-bulks, one-third of which is seventy-two and one-third egg-bulks.

Ravina objects to the word *karov* being read into a *Braita* that does not contain it. He therefore offers a different resolution: taking a third of the *se'ah Tziporit* as corrected by Rabbi's *v'od* adjustment — a third of 217 — yields approximately 72, which is very close to half the *midbarit*'s 144. This is the *Gemara*'s best reconciliation of the *Braita*, though it requires incorporating Rabbi's fractional correction to make the numbers work.

## Challah & Meal Size

### Omer Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״רֵאשִׁית עֲרִיסוֹתֵיכֶם״, כְּדֵי עִיסּוֹתֵיכֶם, וְכַמָּה עִיסּוֹתֵיכֶם? כְּדֵי עִיסַּת הַמִּדְבָּר. וְכַמָּה עִיסַּת הַמִּדְבָּר? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָעוֹמֶר עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Our Sages taught** a *baraita*: The verse states: "You shall set apart a cake of **the first of your dough** as a gift; like the gift of the threshing floor, so shall you set it apart" (Numbers 15:20). What is the quantity of dough from which *ḥalla* must be separated? **The amount of "your dough." And how much is "your dough"?** This amount is left unspecified by the verse. The Gemara answers: It is **as the amount of the dough of the wilderness.** The Gemara again asks: **And how much is the dough of the wilderness?** The Torah states that the manna, the dough of the wilderness, was "an *omer* a head" (Exodus 16:16). A later verse elaborates on that measure, **as it is written: "And an *omer* is the tenth part of an *eifa*"** (Exodus 16:36). An *eifa* is three *se'a*, which are eighteen *kav* or seventy-two *log*. An *omer* is one-tenth of this measure.

The *Gemara* now turns to a *Braita* with direct practical implications for the *mitzvah* of *challah*. The verse says *reshit arisoteichem* — the first of your kneading — and the *Braita* asks: how large a quantity of dough is subject to the obligation of *challah d'orayta*? The answer is *kedei isat midbar*, the size of the dough prepared in the wilderness — meaning the amount of the *omer*.

The *omer* is defined by the Torah as one-tenth of an *ephah*, and an *ephah* is three *se'in*. So the *omer* equals three-tenths of a *se'ah midbarit*. Since the *se'ah midbarit* equals 144 *beitzim*, three-tenths of 144 yields 43.2 *beitzim*. This is the minimum quantity of dough that obligates one in *challah* according to Torah law — forty-three and one-fifth *beitzim*, a figure familiar to anyone who has looked into the practical laws of *challah* separation.

### Measure Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ, שִׁבְעָה רְבָעִים קֶמַח וָעוֹד חַיֶּיבֶת בְּחַלָּה, שֶׁהֵן שִׁשָּׁה שֶׁל יְרוּשַׁלְמִית, שֶׁהֵן חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **From here,** this calculation, Sages **said** that dough prepared from **seven quarters** of a *kav* of **flour and more is obligated in *ḥalla*. This is equal to six** quarter-*kav* of the **Jerusalem** measure, **which is five** quarter-*kav* of the **Tzippori** measure.

The *Braita* then expresses this same threshold in the larger measures of *kav*: *zayin reva'im kemach v'od chayevet b'challah* — seven quarter-*kav* portions of flour plus a little obligates in *challah*. A *reva* is one-quarter of a *kav*, and one *kav* equals twenty-four *beitzim*, so one *reva* is six *beitzim*. Seven *reva'im* is therefore forty-two *beitzim*, and the "little" extra accounts for the remaining 1.2, bringing the total to 43.2.

The *Braita* adds: *shehen vav shel Yerushalmiyot, shehen hey shel Tziporiyot* — this quantity is equivalent to six *reva'im* in the *Yerushalmit* measure and five *reva'im* in the *Tziporit* measure. The conversion is consistent: since the *Yerushalmit se'ah* is one-fifth larger than the *midbarit* in effective ratio, seven-and-change *reva'im midbariyim* convert cleanly to six *reva'im Yerushalmiyim*, and those in turn to five *reva'im Tziporiyim*.

### Health Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הָאוֹכֵל כְּמִדָּה זוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה בָּרִיא וּמְבוֹרָךְ. יָתֵר עַל כֵּן — רַעַבְתָּן. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — מְקוּלְקָל בְּמֵעָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **From here** the Sages also **said: One who eats roughly this amount** each day, **is healthy,** as he is able to eat a proper meal; **and** he is also **blessed,** as he is not a glutton who requires more. One who eats **more than this** is **a glutton,** while one who eats **less than this has damaged bowels** and must see to his health.

The *Braita* closes with a remarkable statement about the significance of this 43.2-*beitzim* measure beyond its legal context. *Ha-ochel k'middah zo harei zeh bari u'mvorach* — one who eats this amount is healthy and blessed. *Yaser kahn ra'avtan* — one who eats more is considered ravenous. *Pachos mikan mekukul b'mayav* — one who eats less will have digestive troubles.

Whether this refers literally to eating 43.2 *beitzim* worth of bread in a single meal or rather to consuming an amount proportional to one's *seuda* measure — the precise interpretation is not entirely clear. What the *Gemara* is expressing is the idea that there is a perfect, divinely calibrated quantity of bread for a meal, and that both excess and deficiency depart from that ideal.

## Courtyard vs. Balcony

### Physical Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר וְאַנְשֵׁי מִרְפֶּסֶת שֶׁשָּׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ, כׇּל שֶׁגָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — לְמִרְפֶּסֶת. פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — לֶחָצֵר. חוּלְיַית הַבּוֹר וְהַסֶּלַע, גְּבוֹהִים עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — לַמִּרְפֶּסֶת, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — לֶחָצֵר. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּסְמוּכָה, אֲבָל בְּמוּפְלֶגֶת — אֲפִילּוּ גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — לֶחָצֵר. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא סְמוּכָה — כׇּל שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְחוֹקָה אַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If both **the residents of** houses that open directly into **a courtyard and the residents of** apartments that open onto **a balcony** from which stairs lead down to that courtyard **forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*** between them, **anything** in the courtyard **that is ten handbreadths high,** e.g., a mound or a post, is part **of the balcony.** The residents of the apartments open to the balcony may transfer objects to and from their apartments onto the mound or post. Any post or mound that is **lower than this** height is part **of the courtyard.** A similar *halakha* applies to **an embankment** that surrounds **a cistern or a rock:** If the embankments that surround a cistern or rock are **ten handbreadths high,** they belong **to the balcony;** if they are **lower than this,** they may be used only **by** the inhabitants of **the courtyard.** **In what** case **are these matters,** the *halakha* that anything higher than ten handbreadths belongs to the balcony, **stated?** When the mound or embankment is **near** the balcony. **But in** a case where the embankment or mound is **distant** from it, **even** if it is **ten handbreadths high,** the right to use the embankment or mound goes **to** the members of **the courtyard. And what is** considered **near? Anything that is not four handbreadths removed** from the balcony.

The *Gemara* now pivots to the new *Mishna*, which introduces a scenario familiar in structure but novel in geometry: *anshei chatzer v'anshei mirpeset* — the residents of a courtyard and the residents of a balcony. The physical arrangement envisions a ground-level courtyard with residents below, a *mirpeset* — a balcony — above, and access between the balcony and the courtyard by means of a ladder. Each group made its own *eruv* but they did not establish a joint *eruv* together. The question that follows is which group has rights to the intermediate spaces — mounds, rocks, or structures — that occupy the space between the two levels.

### Ten-Tefachim Rule

The *Mishna* states: *kol shegavoha asarah tefachim l'mirpeset, pachos mikan l'chatzer* — anything ten *tefachim* or more in height belongs to the balcony; anything less than ten *tefachim* belongs to the courtyard. The same rule applies to a well-surround (*chuliyat habor*) or a rock (*sela*): if ten *tefachim* high, it is associated with the *mirpeset*; if less, with the *chatzer*.

The logic is that in halachic spatial reasoning, ten *tefachim* marks the boundary between what is part of the ground domain and what is elevated above it. Once a structure reaches ten *tefachim*, it is no longer considered part of the ground-level domain and is instead treated as belonging to the elevated domain of the balcony. This may seem counterintuitive — a mound of three feet in height would seem perfectly accessible from below — but the halachic threshold is fixed at ten *tefachim* regardless.

### Proximity Requirement

The *Mishna* adds an important qualification: *bameh devarim amurim bismucha* — this rule applies only when the mound is close to the balcony. The *Gemara* specifies how close: *kol she'eina rechoka arba'a tefachim* — it must be within four *tefachim*, approximately one foot. If the mound is further than four *tefachim* from the balcony, it cannot be associated with the balcony's domain regardless of its height, and it reverts to the *chatzer*. The combination of height (ten *tefachim* or more) and proximity (within four *tefachim*) must both be satisfied for the mound to belong to the *mirpeset*.

## Equal vs. Unequal Access

### Equal Easy Access

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, לָזֶה בְּפֶתַח וּלְזֶה בְּפֶתַח — הַיְינוּ חַלּוֹן שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת. לָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה — הַיְינוּ כּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת. לָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל וְלָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל — הַיְינוּ חָרִיץ שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It is **obvious** that if the residents of two courtyards established separate *eiruvin*, and the residents of both courtyards have convenient access to a certain area, the residents of **this** courtyard **through an entrance, and** the residents of **that** courtyard **through** another **entrance, this is** similar to the case of **a window between two courtyards.** If the residents did not establish a joint *eiruv*, the use of this window is prohibited to the residents of both courtyards. It is similarly obvious that if a place can be used by the residents **of this** courtyard only **by throwing** an object onto it **and** by the residents **of that** courtyard only **by throwing,** but it cannot be conveniently used by either set of residents, then **this is** equivalent to the case of **a wall between two courtyards.** If there is a wall between two courtyards, it may not be used by either courtyard. Likewise, if a place can be used by the residents **of this** courtyard only **by lowering** an object down to it **and** by the residents **of that** courtyard **by** a similar act of **lowering, this is** comparable to the *halakha* of **a ditch between two courtyards,** which may not be used by the residents of either courtyard.

The *Gemara* now abstracts the underlying principles by working through a series of cases. *L'zeh b'petach ul'zeh b'petach* — if one courtyard has easy access to an intermediate space and the other also has equally easy access — *hainu chalon shebein shtei chatzerot*, this is analogous to the well-known case of a window between two courtyards. In that case, neither courtyard can use the space for its own purposes, because each is an equal competitor. Both parties are forbidden from transferring objects through that space into their own domain.

### Equal Hard Access

The same logic governs equally difficult access. *L'zeh b'zirika ul'zeh b'zirika* — if both courtyards can only reach the space by throwing — *hainu kotel shebein shtei chatzerot*, this is the case of a wall between two courtyards that is equally hard to access from both sides, ten *tefachim* or more for both. Again, equal difficulty means equal competition, and neither side may use the space. *L'zeh b'shilshul ul'zeh b'shilshul* — if both must stoop down, as in a trench — *hainu charitz shebein shtei chatzerot*, this is the case of a ditch between two courtyards, and the same conclusion applies. Equality in either direction — equally easy or equally hard — yields mutual prohibition.

### Unequal Access

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָזֶה בְּפֶתַח וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה — הַיְינוּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן. לָזֶה בְּפֶתַח וְלָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל — הַיְינוּ דְּרַב שֵׁיזְבִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן. לָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** It is likewise obvious that in a place that can be conveniently used by the residents **of this** courtyard **through an entrance but** can be used by the residents **of that** courtyard only **by throwing** an object onto it, **this is** governed by the ruling **of Rabba bar Rav Huna,** who said that **Rav Naḥman said:** This place may be used only by those who have access to the area by way of an entrance. Likewise, a place that can be conveniently used by the residents **of this** courtyard **through an entrance but** can be used by the residents **of that** courtyard only **by lowering** an object down to it, **this is** governed by the ruling **of Rav Sheizvi,** who said **that Rav Naḥman said:** This place may be used only by those who have convenient access to it. The ruling in each of the aforementioned cases is clear. **What** is the *halakha* concerning a place that can be used by the residents **of this** courtyard only **by lowering** an object down to it **and by** the residents **of that** courtyard only **by throwing** an object on top of it? In other words, if an area is lower than one courtyard but higher than the other, so that neither set of residents has convenient access to it, which of them is entitled to use it?

When access is unequal, the result changes. *L'zeh b'petach ul'zeh b'zirika* — easy access for one, access only by throwing for the other — *hainu d'Rabbah bar Rav Huna amar Rav Nachman*, this is the ruling of Rabbah bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav Nachman about a wall that is less than ten *tefachim* on one side and more than ten on the other. The space goes to the one for whom access is easy. Similarly, *l'zeh b'petach ul'zeh b'shilshul* — easy access for one, stooping required for the other — *hainu d'Rav Shizbi amar Rav Nachman*, the case of a ditch that is less than ten deep on one side and ten or more on the other. Again, the space goes to the side for which it is more accessible.

### Shmuel vs. Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: שְׁנֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁבְּשִׁלְשׁוּל. שֶׁלָּזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת, וְלָזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּקָשֶׁה, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ לָזֶה בְּנַחַת וְלָזֶה בְּקָשֶׁה — נוֹתְנִים אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** It is **prohibited** for **both** sets of residents to use it. As the use of the area is equally inconvenient to the residents of both courtyards, they retain equal rights to it and render it prohibited for the other group to use. **And Shmuel said:** The use of the area **is granted to those who** can reach it **by lowering, as** it is relatively easy **for them** to lower objects to it, and therefore **its use is** more **convenient;** whereas **for the others,** who must throw onto it, **its use is** more **demanding. And** there is a principle concerning Shabbat: **Anything whose use is convenient for one** party **and** more **demanding for another** party, **one provides it to that** one **whose use** of it **is convenient.**

The genuinely novel case is *l'zeh b'shilshul ul'zeh b'zirika* — one side can access the space by stooping down into it, the other only by throwing up to it. Both are forms of difficult access, but they are different in kind. Is one more connected to the space than the other?

Rav rules *shneihem asurin* — both are forbidden, because the access is equally difficult in both directions. Shmuel rules *nosnim oso l'zeh sheb'shilshul* — we assign it to the one who must stoop down, reasoning that *tashmisho b'nachas* — access by lowering oneself is easier — while access by throwing is harder: *tashmisho b'kasha*. The general principle Shmuel derives is: whenever it is easy for one and hard for the other, we give the space to the one for whom it is easier.

### Application to Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר וְאַנְשֵׁי מִרְפֶּסֶת שֶׁשָּׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ, כָּל שֶׁגָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — לַמִּרְפֶּסֶת, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — לֶחָצֵר. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, מַאי מִרְפֶּסֶת — בְּנֵי עֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** In order to decide between these two opinions, the Gemara attempts to adduce a proof from the mishna: If **the residents of** houses that open directly into **a courtyard and the residents of** apartments that open onto **a balcony** from which stairs lead down to that courtyard **forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*** between them, **anything** in the courtyard **that is ten handbreadths high** belongs **to the balcony,** while anything that is **less than this** height belongs **to the courtyard.** The Gemara first explains: **It might have entered your mind** to say: **What is** the meaning of the **balcony** mentioned in the mishna? It is referring to **the residents of an upper story** above the balcony.

The *Gemara* now applies this dispute to our *Mishna*. The ruling that a mound of ten *tefachim* belongs to the *mirpeset* — rather than to neither party — would seem to support Shmuel. The mound is more than ten *tefachim* up from the courtyard level (so the courtyard residents must throw to reach it — *zirika*), while for the balcony residents it is below their level (they must lower something to reach it — *shilshul*). Giving it to the *mirpeset* is consistent with Shmuel's principle that *shilshul* beats *zirika*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמַאי קָרוּ לַהּ מִרְפֶּסֶת? דְּקָסָלְקִי בְּמִרְפֶּסֶת, אַלְמָא: כָּל לָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה — נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁבְּשִׁלְשׁוּל!
>
> **Translation:** It is referring to **the residents of an upper story** above the balcony; **and** if so, **why do we call** the upper story **a balcony?** Because the residents of the upper story **ascend** and descend to and from their apartments **by** way of **the balcony. From here** the Gemara infers: With regard to **any** place **that** can be used **by one** set of residents only **by lowering** an object down to it **and by another** set of residents only **by throwing** an object on top of it, **we grant** Shabbat use of **it to those who** can use it **by lowering,** as the residents of the upper story who use the area ten handbreadths high do so by means of lowering. Apparently, the mishna supports Shmuel and presents a difficulty to Rav.

Rav, however, is not contradicted by this *Mishna*. Rav Huna clarifies: *le'osam hadarim b'mirpeset* — the *Mishna* is speaking of the people who actually live on the balcony itself, not the residents of an upper story. For the balcony residents, the mound may actually be within less than ten *tefachim* of their floor level — within easy reach. In that case, the mound is simply less than ten *tefachim* away from them and more than ten away from the courtyard below, which is the ordinary case of unequal access that both Rav and Shmuel agree goes to the more accessible party. Rav's position — that when both sides face equally difficult access the space belongs to neither — remains intact. The *Mishna* does not force a contradiction, because the *Mishna*'s case is one of genuinely unequal access, not the ambiguous *shilshul-versus-zirika* scenario that divides Rav and Shmuel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לְאוֹתָן הַדָּרִים בַּמִּרְפֶּסֶת. הָכִי נָמֵי: לְאוֹתָן הַדָּרִין בַּמִּרְפֶּסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **As Rav Huna said** with regard to a different issue discussed in a subsequent mishna, that the *tanna* is referring **to those who live in** apartments that open directly onto **the balcony** rather than those who live in an upper story; **here too,** the *tanna* is speaking **of those who live in** apartments that open directly onto **the balcony.** In this case, the use of an area ten handbreadths high is convenient for the residents of the balcony, as it is on their level; whereas its use is relatively inconvenient for the residents of the courtyard. Consequently, the right to use this area is granted to the residents of the balcony.
