# Bava Batra 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tolerable Mixtures

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת לַחֲבֵירוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו רוֹבַע טִנּוֹפֶת לִסְאָה. תְּאֵנִים – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר מְתוּלָּעוֹת לְמֵאָה. מַרְתֵּף שֶׁל יַיִן – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר קוֹסְסוֹת לְמֵאָה. קַנְקַנִּים בַּשָּׁרוֹן – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עֶשֶׂר פִּיטָסוֹת לְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When selling a significant quantity of produce or a number of items, there is a possibility that there will be a certain proportion of impurities in it or that some of the product will be of substandard quality. The mishna delineates what proportion is considered acceptable, for which a buyer may not demand compensation. With regard to **one who sells produce,** i.e., grain, **to another, this** buyer **accepts upon himself** that up to **a quarter-***kav* of **impurities** may be present **in** each ***se'a*** of produce purchased. When purchasing **figs, he accepts upon himself** that up to **ten infested** figs may be present **in** each **hundred** figs purchased. When purchasing **a cellar** containing barrels **of wine, he accepts upon himself** that up to **ten** barrels of **souring** wine may be present **in** each **hundred** barrels purchased. When purchasing **jugs** of wine **in the Sharon** region, **he accepts upon himself** that up to **ten inferior-quality jugs [*pitasot*]** of wine may be present **in** each **hundred** jugs purchased.

We pick up at the very bottom of 93b, where the *mishnah* had addressed the question of tolerable defects in sold goods. The *mishnah* enumerates several categories: wine that has partially spoiled, figs that may contain worms, and grain that has some *tinofet* — some type of dross or extraneous material — mixed in. The *gemara* will now examine precisely what that extraneous material consists of and what the tolerable limits are for each type of commodity.

### Mekach Taos Framework

The *mishnah* establishes a threshold: up to a certain percentage of contamination or admixture, a buyer is presumed to accept the situation as normal, and it does not constitute a *mekach taos* — a fundamentally flawed transaction. Beyond that threshold, however, the transaction is problematic and the seller presumably must reimburse the buyer. A related question, which we will return to at length, concerns what happens when the contamination exceeds the tolerable limit by some amount: if, say, eleven barrels out of a hundred turned out to be spoiled wine when the tolerable limit is ten, must the seller reimburse the buyer for all eleven barrels, or only for the single barrel that exceeded the acceptable percentage?

### FDA Comparison

As a point of contrast, it is worth noting that contemporary regulatory frameworks — the FDA's standards, for example — similarly establish tolerable limits for extraneous matter, including microscopic animal and insect particles, in commercially sold food products. The permissible thresholds are surprisingly high. The Talmud's treatment of this question, of course, operates in the domain of *mekach umimkar* — commercial law — rather than *issur achilah*, and the framework and motivations are accordingly distinct.

## Rav Katina on Kitniyot

### Rova Kitniyot L'Se'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּאנֵי רַב קַטִּינָא: רוֹבַע קִטְנִית לִסְאָה. וְעַפְרוּרִית לָא?! וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר חִיָּיא קְטוֹסְפָאָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה: בּוֹרֵר צְרוֹר מִגׇּרְנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי חִטִּין!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ketina taught:** When the mishna states that a buyer accepts upon himself that a quarter-*kav* of impurities per *se'a* may be present, that means only that he accepts upon himself the presence of **a quarter-***kav* **of legumes,** but he does not accept upon himself the presence of a quarter-*kav* of dirt. The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that the buyer does **not** also accept upon himself that some quantity of **dirt** might be present in the produce? **But doesn't Rabba bar Ḥiyya Ketosfa'a say in the name of Rabba:** One who **picks out a pebble from** the wheat on **another's threshing floor** must **give him the value of wheat** of equal volume to the pebble that he removed. Had he not removed the pebble, the owner would have sold his wheat together with the pebble, all for the price of wheat. Accordingly, the removal of the pebble effectively caused the owner a small loss. It is apparent from this ruling that when selling produce, a buyer accepts upon himself that a quantity of dirt may be mixed in.

The *gemara* opens with a teaching of Rav Katina: *rova kitniyot l'se'ah* — a *rova* (a quarter of a *kav*, which is 1/24th of a *se'ah*, approximately four percent) of legumes per *se'ah* is the tolerable admixture referred to in the *mishnah*. The word *tinofet*, literally "filth" or "dross," used in the *mishnah*, Rav Katina understands as referring not to literal dirt but to wild legumes (*kitniyot*) that naturally grow alongside grain crops and become mixed in during processing.

### Dirt Tolerance Question

The *gemara* challenges Rav Katina: does his teaching imply that literal dirt — *afruris*, dust and grit — carries no tolerable threshold at all, that the grain must be entirely clean of it? That would seem overly demanding.

### Removing Dirt Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִטְנִית – רוֹבַע, עַפְרוּרִית – פָּחוֹת מֵרוֹבַע.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **legumes,** one accepts **a quarter-***kav* per *se'a*, but with regard to **dirt,** he accepts **less than a quarter-***kav*.

The *gemara* responds by citing a teaching of Rava bar Bar Chana in the name of Rava: if a person goes to a friend's threshing floor — where a mound of grain is being prepared for bagging and sale — and, intending to do the owner a favor, begins removing rocks and dirt, that person must pay the owner the equivalent weight in grain (*nosein lo dmei chitin*). The logic is straightforward: had the well-meaning helper not intervened, the grain would have been bagged as it was, with a small proportion of dirt making up part of the total weight of each bag. This admixture was acceptable and the buyer would have received it without objection. By removing the dirt, the helper has effectively forced the seller to fill those bags entirely with grain, replacing what could legitimately have been dirt. The result is a loss to the seller, and the helper must compensate.

This ruling makes clear that there is indeed a tolerable amount of dirt that may legitimately be sold along with grain — the *mishnah's* reference to *tinofet* cannot be limited to legumes alone to the exclusion of any dirt.

### Grama Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַפְרוּרִית – רוֹבַע לָא?! וְהָא תַּנְיָא: הַמּוֹכֵר פֵּירוֹת לַחֲבֵרוֹ, חִטִּין – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו רוֹבַע קִטְנִית לִסְאָה. שְׂעוֹרִים – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו רוֹבַע נִישּׁוֹבֶת לִסְאָה. עֲדָשִׁים – מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו רוֹבַע עַפְרוּרִית לִסְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** is it so that he does **not** accept **a quarter**-*kav* of **dirt? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who sells produce to another,** if he sells him **wheat,** the buyer **accepts upon himself** that **a quarter-***kav* **of legumes** may be present **in** each ***se'a*** of wheat purchased. When purchasing **barley, he accepts upon himself** that **a quarter-***kav* of **chaff** may be present **in** each ***se'a*** purchased. When purchasing **lentils, he accepts upon himself** that **a quarter-***kav* **of dirt** may be present **in** each ***se'a*** purchased.

The Rashbam and Tosafot debate the precise legal category of the helper's liability. The Rashbam characterizes it as *grama b'nezikin* — indirect causation of damage. The wheat itself remains unharmed; what the helper did was indirectly, through the rules of *mekach umimkar*, force the seller to supply more grain than he otherwise would have been obligated to. According to the view that certain forms of *grama* (or *garmi*) are actionable, the helper is liable. Tosafot, by contrast, frames it as direct damage (*mazik*): the pile of grain, viewed as a whole, is now worth less money than before the intervention, since it can no longer be sold at its original weight. Whether one conceptualizes the harm as a diminished pile or as an indirect compulsion to provide extra grain shapes how one categorizes the liability — but either way, the halachic conclusion is clear: there is an accepted and tolerable proportion of dirt that may accompany grain in a commercial sale.

## Crop-Specific Standards

### Baraita Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי, לָאו הוּא הַדִּין לְחִטִּים וְלִשְׂעוֹרִין? שָׁאנֵי עֲדָשִׁים, דְּמִיעְקָר עָקְרִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What, is it not** that just as the *baraita* rules with regard to lentils, **the same is true for wheat and for barley?** The Gemara answers: No, **lentils are different, because they are dug up** from the ground and dirt can easily get mixed in. Lentils often contain a higher percentage of dirt than do wheat and barley, which are harvested rather than dug up.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that specifies the tolerable admixtures for each type of grain. When selling grain (*chitin*, wheat), the buyer accepts up to a *rova* of *kitniyot* — legumes — per *se'ah*, again approximately four percent. When selling barley (*se'orim*), the tolerable admixture is a *rova* of *nishoves* per *se'ah* — straw blown by the wind and mixed in during threshing, which is a more typical contaminant for barley. When selling lentils (*adashim*), the tolerable admixture is a *rova* of *afruris* — dust and dirt — per *se'ah*.

### Lentil Distinction

The *gemara* asks: if a *rova* of dirt is acceptable for lentils, should we not infer that the same tolerance applies to wheat and barley? The *gemara* rejects this inference. Lentils are grown close to the ground and are pulled directly from the earth — *de'ma'aker akri lehu* — so dirt naturally and inevitably becomes mixed in during harvesting. Wheat and barley, by contrast, grow high above the ground; their grains do not ordinarily come into contact with soil during growth or harvesting. Accordingly, dirt is not the expected contaminant for wheat and barley, and the *baraita*'s leniency for dirt applies only to lentils.

This appears to be a strong support for Rav Katina's position: by wheat and barley, dirt is either not tolerable at all or at least not to the same degree as legumes.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דַעֲדָשִׁים דְּמִיעְקָר עָקְרִי לְהוּ, אֲבָל חִטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי – לָא; תִּפְשׁוֹט מִינַּהּ, חִטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי דְּעַפְרוּרִית לָא מְקַבֵּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But** according to this, **the** only **reason** that a buyer accepts that dirt may be present in lentils but not in wheat and barley is **that lentils are dug up** from the ground, **whereas wheat and barley** are **not.** If so, **resolve** the dilemma **from** this *baraita* and conclude that when purchasing **wheat and barley,** a buyer **does not accept** that a quarter-*kav* of **dirt** may be present in each *se'a*.

The *gemara* then attempts to resist this reading. Perhaps, it suggests, the same *rova* tolerance for dirt applies equally to wheat and barley as to lentils — and the *baraita* mentions dirt only in connection with lentils because the novel point (*chiddush*) is the opposite: one might have thought that since lentils are literally uprooted from the ground, an even greater proportion of dirt would be tolerable. The *baraita* teaches that no, even for lentils the limit is a *rova* and not more (*salka da'atach amina kivan de'mi'akar akri, yoter mi'rova nami mekabel, ka mashma lan* that this is not so).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם חִטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי מְקַבֵּל עַפְרוּרִית; עֲדָשִׁים אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ – דְּסָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כֵּיוָן דְּמִיעְקָר עָקְרִי לְהוּ, יוֹתֵר מֵרוֹבַע נָמֵי לְקַבֵּל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Actually,** perhaps when purchasing **wheat and barley** as well, a buyer **accepts** that a quarter-*kav* of **dirt** may be present in each *se'a*, but **it was necessary for** the *baraita* to state the *halakha* specifically with regard to **lentils.** This is **because** it might **enter your mind to say** that **since** lentils **are dug up** from the ground, the buyer would **also accept** even **more than a quarter-***kav* of dirt. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not the case.

The result is a degree of ambiguity: the plain reading of the *baraita* supports Rav Katina's distinction between legumes and dirt, while the *gemara*'s alternative reading holds that the *rova* tolerance may apply equally to dirt across all grain types, and the *baraita*'s placement of the dirt-tolerance rule by lentils is simply to teach the upper limit of what is acceptable even for the most dirt-prone crop.

## Overage Reimbursement

### Rav Huna's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אִם בָּא לְנַפּוֹת, מְנַפֶּה אֶת כּוּלּוֹ. אָמְרִי לַהּ דִּינָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ קִנְסָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says: If** the buyer **comes to sift** the produce to check if there is more than the acceptable proportion of impurities and he finds that there is too much, **he sifts all of it** and returns all the impurities to the seller, not just the amount in excess of a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*. The seller must instead provide produce that is free of any impurities. **Some say** that this is the strict ***halakha*, and some say** that it is **a penalty.**

Having established what the tolerable admixtures are, the *gemara* now turns to the central question about overage. Rav Huna states: *im ba lenapot, menafeh et kulo* — if one comes to sift out the grain, one sifts out all of it. The practical meaning is not merely that a buyer is permitted to sift his own grain — that goes without saying. Rather, the ruling addresses the case where the buyer sifts the grain and discovers that the actual proportion of contaminant exceeds the tolerable limit — say, five percent instead of four. Rav Huna's ruling is that the buyer is entitled to remove the entire amount of the foreign matter and demand full reimbursement from the seller. If the tolerable limit was four percent but five percent was present, the buyer need not accept four percent and seek reimbursement only for the extra one percent; he is entitled to reimbursement for the full five percent.

### Din vs. Knas

The *gemara* records two distinct rationales for Rav Huna's ruling: *amrei lah dina, v'amrei lah knasa* — some say it is a matter of strict legal entitlement (*din*), and others say it is a rabbinic fine (*knas*).

### Din Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לַהּ דִּינָא – מַאן דְּיָהֵיב זוּזֵי, אַפֵּירֵי שַׁפִּירֵי יָהֵיב; וְרוֹבַע לָא טָרַח אִינִישׁ, יוֹתֵר מֵרוֹבַע טָרַח אִינִישׁ; וְכֵיוָן דְּטָרַח – טָרַח בְּכוּלֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Some say** that this is the strict ***halakha*,** as **one who gives money** for produce **gives** it **for good-quality produce** containing no impurities at all. Even so, where there is just **a quarter**-*kav* of impurities per *se'a*, **a person will not take the trouble** to sift the grain to remove the impurities; instead, he accepts the small quantity of impurities that are present. By contrast, where there is **more than a quarter-***kav* of impurities per *se'a*, **a person will take the trouble** to sift the grain to remove the impurities, **and once he takes the trouble** to sift the grain, he does not stop once he reduces the proportion of impurities to a quarter-*kav* per *se'a*; rather, **he takes the trouble** to sift **all of it.** Accordingly, once he has sifted out the impurities, he never agrees to accept any quantity of impurities, and so the seller must take back all the impurities.

According to the *din* approach, the buyer is in principle entitled to receive the full weight of grain he paid for and nothing else — *man de'yeihiv zuzei apeirei shapir yeihiv*: when one pays money for grain, one is paying for grain, not for a mixture of grain and legumes. In theory, even a single grain of legume mixed in could be grounds for a claim. The reason the seller is not normally held to such a standard is *mechila* — the buyer implicitly waives his right to demand perfect purity, because the effort required to identify and remove a small proportion of admixture is simply not worth it for either party. Up to a *rova*, the effort of sifting is disproportionate to the small loss, so the buyer's *mechila* is presumed.

Once the admixture exceeds a *rova*, however, the calculus changes: *yoter mi'rova tarach inishi* — beyond that amount, the effort of sifting becomes worthwhile. And once it is worth the effort to sift — *dekivan de'tarach tarach bekulo* — the buyer will sift the entire batch, not just the excess. Since the buyer would rationally undertake the entire sifting operation once the threshold is crossed, the *mechila* that applied to the tolerable portion evaporates entirely. The buyer never truly forgave that amount; he merely declined to pursue it because the effort was too great. Once he is already sifting, no portion is forgivable, and the seller owes full reimbursement for everything.

The Rashbam's formulation captures this well. The buyer has no independent way of ascertaining how much contamination is present without actually sifting. For small amounts, it is simply not worth his time to sift, measure, demonstrate the contamination, and make a claim — so a *mechila* is presumed. But once the amount is substantial enough that sifting becomes worthwhile, the buyer is no longer presumed to forgive anything. He undertakes the entire process and holds the seller responsible for the entire admixture.

### Knas Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִי לַהּ קִנְסָא – רוֹבַע שְׁכִיחַ, יוֹתֵר לָא שְׁכִיחַ; וְאִיהוּ הוּא דְּעָרֵיב; וְכֵיוָן דְּעָרֵיב – קַנְסוּהוּ רַבָּנַן בְּכוּלֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** it is **a penalty.** They understand that it is **usual** for **a quarter-***kav* of impurities to be present in each *se'a* of produce, and so it is presumed that a buyer accepts that quantity. **More** than a quarter-*kav* **is unusual, and** consequently the seller is suspected of having deceitfully **mixed** additional impurities into the produce that he sold. **And since** the seller deceitfully **mixed** in impurities, **the Sages penalized him** by requiring him to pay for **all of** the impurities present, even those which he did not add.

According to the *knas* approach, the framing is quite different. In this view, one might actually say that the buyer is *mochel* — forgiving — even on whatever amount of contamination happens to be present, because he understands that some mixture is inevitable in agricultural goods and does not hold the seller to an exacting standard. Why, then, does excessive contamination trigger full reimbursement? Because *rova shchiach, yoter lo shchiach* — a *rova* is a common, expected occurrence; more than a *rova* is not common, and if there is that much contamination, *de'ihu de'ariv* — we must suspect that the seller deliberately adulterated the grain.

Moreover, even setting aside deliberate fraud, the *knas* functions as a preventative deterrent. If the only consequence of exceeding the threshold were reimbursement for the excess amount, there would be no real incentive for care or honesty: the worst-case outcome for a negligent or dishonest seller would be that he reimburses only the marginal overage, which is identical to what he would have owed anyway had he been honest. Just as the Torah's concern that a *gazlan* who merely returns what he stole is not sufficiently deterred, so too here: *v'keivan de'ariv, kansu'u rabbanan v'chulei* — once the seller has crossed the line, the Rabbis impose a fine of full reimbursement, extending liability back to cover even the tolerable portion, in order to create a meaningful deterrent.

## Kilayim Comparison

### Kilayim Rova Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָן: כֹּל תְּרֵי שְׁטָרֵי דְּרָאבִין בַּר רַב נַחְמָן, אוֹנָאָה וְקַבְּלָנוּתָא.) מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל סְאָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ רוֹבַע מִמִּין אַחֵר – יְמַעֵט. סַבְרוּהָ דְּרוֹבַע דְּכִלְאַיִם – כְּיוֹתֵר מֵרוֹבַע דְּהָכָא, וְקָא תָנֵי: יְמַעֵט!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **a mnemonic** for the cases it will cite: **All two documents of Ravin bar Rav Naḥman** are **exploitation and a contract.** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav Huna's ruling from a mishna (*Kilayim* 2:1): With regard to **any *se'a*** of seeds **that contains a quarter**-*kav* or more of seeds **of a different kind,** before sowing such seeds **one must reduce** the quantity of the other kind of seeds in the mixture so as not to violate the prohibition against growing a mixture of diverse kinds (see Leviticus 19:19). The Gemara explains: It can be **assumed that** the presence of **a quarter-***kav* of seeds of a different kind per *se'a* **with regard to** the prohibition of **diverse kinds is as** problematic as **more than a quarter-***kav* of impurities per *se'a* **with regard to** a sale, as discussed **here, and** therefore, since the mishna **teaches** with regard to diverse kinds only that **one must reduce** the additional amount, but not that one is required to remove all the seeds of a different kind, it follows that the same is true in the case of a sale, and the seller should not have to take back all of the impurities. This contradicts Rav Huna's ruling.

The *gemara* now presents a series of challenges designed to test these two approaches. The first comes from the laws of *kilayim*. If a person plants wheat and the seed contains a *rova* of a different variety of grain or legume seed mixed in, the *mishnah* in *kilayim* states *yema'it* — he must reduce that admixture. The ruling does not require complete removal; it is enough to bring the amount below the *rova* threshold.

### Equivalence Assumption

The *gemara* initially assumes that a *rova* of *kilayim* is comparable to the situation of *yoter mi'rova* in *mekach umimkar* — that is, a *rova* of foreign seed in a planted field is analogous to an unacceptable overage in a commercial sale, in that both represent the point at which the admixture is no longer tolerable. On this assumption, the parallel case for our question would be: when one has crossed the threshold, must one correct the full amount or only the excess?

Tosafot explains that this question in *kilayim* is being discussed according to the *knas* approach. The scenario, as Tosafot understands it, is one where the mixed seeds have already been planted and are growing together; the question is whether the grower — who has inadvertently or negligently caused *kilayim* to grow — must uproot everything or only enough to bring the field back within the tolerable ratio. It is worth noting, as Tosafot points out, that *kilay zera'im* (mixed seeds not involving vines) do not render the produce forbidden for eating or for benefit — unlike *kilay hakerem*. The prohibition is specifically against planting such mixtures, and by extension, the concept of *mekayem* — actively sustaining the mixed growth — applies when a person knowingly allows the *kilayim* to continue growing rather than removing it. Whether ceasing to remove the *kilayim* constitutes *mekayem* is part of what this discussion turns on.

### Removal Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, רוֹבַע דְּכִלְאַיִם – כִּי רוֹבַע דְּהָכָא דָּמֵי. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי יְמַעֵט? מִשּׁוּם חוּמְרָא דְכִלְאַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara deflects the challenge: **No,** this assumption is not necessarily correct; perhaps the presence of **a quarter-***kav* of seeds of a different kind per *se'a* **with regard to diverse kinds is comparable to a quarter-***kav* of impurities per *se'a* **with regard to** a sale, as discussed **here,** and both are considered acceptable levels of admixture. The Gemara asks: **If** it is **so** that a quarter-*kav* of seeds of a different kind per *se'a* is acceptable, **why** does the mishna teach that **one must reduce** the quantity of seeds of a different kind? The Gemara answers: The requirement is a rabbinic decree **due to the severity of** the prohibition of **diverse kinds.**

The *mishnah* records a dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yossi. The *Tanna Kamma* says *yema'it* — reduce the *kilayim* to below the threshold. Rabbi Yossi says *yivor* — one must completely remove it through *borer*, selecting out all of the foreign seed. If the scenario involves someone who has crossed the line of what is acceptable, this debate maps directly onto the *knas* question: when you have violated the threshold, is it enough to correct the violation by returning to within acceptable limits, or must you correct the entire amount?

On the surface, the *Tanna Kamma*'s position — that reducing to below the threshold suffices — would seem to argue against the *knas* approach in our case: even when someone has done something unacceptable, we do not fine them by requiring correction of the tolerable portion.

### Mechzi K'Mekayem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אִיתִּים יָבוֹר? הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כִּי מְקַיֵּים כִּלְאַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** why does Rabbi Yosei rule that **one must pick out** all the seeds of a different kind? The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to diverse kinds, **this is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosei:** One must pick out all the seeds, **because** once he is purifying the admixture of seeds, if he deliberately leaves a quantity of seeds of a different kind mixed in, **it appears as though he is** intentionally planting and **maintaining diverse kinds** in his field.

The *gemara* resolves this by rejecting the initial equivalence assumption. A *rova* of *kilayim* in a planted field is not comparable to *yoter mi'rova* in *mekach umimkar*; rather, it is comparable to exactly a *rova* in *mekach umimkar* — the limit itself, not an overage. A *rova* of foreign seed in a planted field is precisely at the boundary of what is tolerable, not beyond it. Since the grower has not actually violated the prohibition, the instruction to reduce is not a response to a violation at all — it is a stringency (*chumra d'kilayim*), a precautionary measure because the grower is right at the edge.

This reframing, however, creates a difficulty with Rabbi Yossi's position. If the grower has not yet violated anything — the field is still within tolerable limits — why does Rabbi Yossi demand complete removal of all the foreign growth? The *gemara* answers: *mishum de'mechzi k'mekayem kilayim* — because if a person begins removing some of the foreign growth and then stops, leaving the rest, this looks as though he is actively and willingly sustaining the *kilayim*. By deliberately removing some and leaving others, he signals acceptance of the remaining mixture, which takes on the character of *mekayem*, actively maintaining the forbidden combination. The *Tanna Kamma* does not share this concern; since one is not yet in violation, reducing below the threshold suffices. Rabbi Yossi, however, holds that any partial removal that leaves *kilayim* in place creates the appearance of *mekayem*, and complete removal is therefore required.

The upshot is that this *kilayim* dispute does not directly bear on the *knas* question in *mekach umimkar*, because the two scenarios are not actually parallel: one involves a field precisely at the tolerable boundary, while the other concerns a case where the boundary has already been crossed.

## Bava Metzia Deposit

### Deposit Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ אֵצֶל אֶחָד, זֶה מָנֶה וְזֶה מָאתַיִם; זֶה אוֹמֵר: ״מָאתַיִם שֶׁלִּי״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: ״מָאתַיִם שֶׁלִּי״ – נוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה, וְהַשְּׁאָר יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another challenge to Rav Huna's ruling from a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 37a): In the case of **two** people **who deposited** money **with one** person, and **this** one deposited **one hundred dinars and that** one deposited **two hundred** dinars, and when they return to collect their deposits, **this** one **says: My** deposit was **two hundred** dinars, **and that** one **says: My** deposit was **two hundred** dinars, the bailee **gives one hundred dinars to this** one **and one hundred dinars to that** one, **and the rest** of the money, i.e., the contested one hundred dinars, **will be placed** in a safe place **until Elijah comes** and prophetically determines the truth. In this case, one of the parties is certainly lying, but nevertheless, the Sages did not penalize the parties by placing all of the money in a safe place. Similarly, in the case of a sale, where a seller deceitfully mixed additional impurities into the produce he sold, he should not be penalized and required to take back all of the impurities.

The *gemara* turns to a case familiar from the first chapter of Bava Metzia. Two people deposit money with a third party for safekeeping: one deposits a hundred dollars and the other deposits two hundred dollars. No records are kept, and when both depositors come to reclaim their money, each claims to be the one who deposited the two hundred. The custodian cannot remember.

### Tanna Kamma Rule

The *Tanna Kamma* rules: *noten la'zeh maneh v'la'zeh maneh v'ha'she'ar yehei munach ad she'yavo Eliyahu* — give each depositor a hundred dollars, since that is the amount each is certainly owed, and hold the remaining disputed hundred in escrow until Eliyahu the Prophet comes to resolve the question, or until some other evidence comes to light or one party admits the truth. No one is fined; each person receives only what is certain, and the disputed sum is simply withheld.

At first glance, this would seem to argue against the *knas* approach: even though there is clearly a liar in this scenario, we do not penalize anyone's principal. One might expect that if we impose fines in the grain case to deter deception, we would similarly penalize the liar here by withholding his principal.

### Innocent Party Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, וַדַּאי מָנֶה לְמָר וּמָנֶה לְמָר; הָכָא, מִי יֵימַר דְּלָאו כּוּלֵּיהּ עָרוֹבֵי עָרֵיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this challenge: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the deposits, **it is certain** that at least **one hundred dinars** belongs **to** this **Master and one hundred dinars** belongs **to** that **Master. Here,** in the case of a sale where there is an unacceptable proportion of impurities mixed in, **who can say that** the seller **did not mix in the entire** amount intentionally? Accordingly, no proof can be drawn from the mishna.

The *gemara* rejects the comparison: *hachi hashta? hasam vadai maneh l'mar u'maneh l'mar* — the cases are fundamentally different. In the deposit case, each depositor is certainly owed a hundred dollars. If we were to withhold anything beyond the disputed hundred, we would be definitively penalizing an innocent party — the honest depositor who genuinely deposited two hundred and is entitled to the full amount. Imposing a fine that harms a known innocent party is categorically different from imposing a fine where no one is necessarily innocent.

Tosafot adds further clarity: in the grain case, even the tolerable four-percent portion may have been intentionally adulterated by the seller — we cannot say with confidence that any part of the contamination was innocent. In the deposit case, by contrast, both depositors are unambiguously innocent of any wrongdoing with respect to the hundred dollars each is owed. Furthermore, in the grain case, there is no definite innocent party whose loss is certain; it is possible that the seller adulterated everything. In the deposit case, a fine would certainly hurt the honest party.

### Rabbi Yossi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע מִסֵּיפָא – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אִם כֵּן, מָה הִפְסִיד הָרַמַּאי? אֶלָּא הַכֹּל יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a support for Rav Huna's ruling **from the latter clause** of that mishna: **Rabbi Yosei says: If so, what did the swindler lose?** He lost nothing by claiming the one hundred dinars that belongs to another, and he has no incentive to admit the truth. **Rather, the entire** deposit **will be placed** in a safe place **until Elijah comes.** Since through his lie the swindler risks losing even the one hundred dinars that he deposited, perhaps that will induce him to admit his deceit. According to Rabbi Yosei, the Sages did penalize one who acts deceitfully, which accords with Rav Huna's ruling.

Rabbi Yossi, however, goes further than the *Tanna Kamma*: *im kein mah hifsid harama'i* — if the liar suffers no consequence whatsoever, what deters future deception? His position is *hakol yehei munach ad she'yavo Eliyahu* — everything is withheld, including the hundred that both parties are certainly owed. This way, even if the innocent depositor is disadvantaged, the would-be deceiver is also penalized, and the deterrent effect is preserved.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם וַדַּאי אִיכָּא רַמַּאי, הָכָא מִי יֵימַר דְּעָרוֹבֵי עָרֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the deposits, **there is certainly a swindler,** and it is reasonable to penalize both parties in order to induce the swindler to admit his deceit. **Here,** in the case of a sale where there is an unacceptable level of impurities mixed in with the produce, **who can say that** the seller intentionally **mixed in** anything? Perhaps the impurities were inadvertently mixed in during the processing. Accordingly, no proof can be drawn from the mishna.

One might think that Rabbi Yossi's willingness to impose a broad penalty, even at the expense of an innocent party, aligns him with the *knas* approach in the grain case. But the *gemara* resists this inference as well. *Hachi hashta? hasam vadai ika ramai* — in the deposit case, there is certainly a liar; someone is definitively and knowingly lying about which deposit is his. When there is certainty of deception, a strong deterrent response is warranted. *Hacha mi yeimar de'rubo ariv* — in the grain case, however, we do not know that the seller did anything wrong at all. It may simply have happened that five percent got mixed in instead of four percent, with no intent and no negligence. Since the violation is uncertain, even Rabbi Yossi might not require full reimbursement here, and the analogy does not hold.

## Ribbis Loan Case

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁטָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רִבִּית – קוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְלֹא אֶת הָרִבִּית; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another support for Rav Huna's ruling from a *baraita*: In the case of a promissory **note in which** the details of a loan with **interest** were written, the court **penalizes** the creditor, **and he may collect neither the principal nor the interest;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** According to Rabbi Meir, the Sages did penalize one who acts improperly, which accords with Rav Huna's ruling.

The *gemara* now turns to the case of a *shtar* — a loan document — that contains *ribbis*, forbidden interest. Suppose the document states that a borrower received one hundred dollars at a five-percent annual interest rate. The lender has clearly violated the biblical prohibition of *ribbis*. Can she now collect the principal of one hundred dollars, or has the presence of *ribbis* in the transaction tainted the entire loan? Rabbi Meir's position is stark: *koniso v'eino goveh lo et ha'keren v'lo et ha'ribis* — the Rabbis fine her and she may collect neither the principal nor the interest. This appears to be precisely the *knas* model: because an unlawful element was present, we extend the penalty to cover the legitimate underlying transaction as well.

### Chachamim Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶת הָרִבִּית!
>
> **Translation:** **But the Rabbis say: He may collect the principal but may not collect the interest.** According to Rabbi Meir, the Sages did not penalize one who acts improperly, contrary to Rav Huna's ruling.

The *chachamim* disagree: *goveh et ha'keren v'eino goveh et ha'ribis* — the lender may collect the principal but not the interest. The violation is penalized, but the principal remains collectable.

### Loan vs. Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם הִיא וַדַּאי קַרְנָא דְּהֶתֵּירָא הוּא, הָכָא מִי יֵימַר דְּכוּלֵּיהּ לָא עָרוֹבֵי עָרֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this challenge: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of a loan with interest, according to strict *halakha* it **is certainly permitted** to collect **the principal,** so the Sages did not penalize him with regard to it. But **here,** in the case of a sale where there is an unacceptable proportion of impurities mixed in, **who can say that** the seller **did not mix in the entire** amount intentionally? Accordingly, no proof can be drawn from the mishna.

The *gemara* draws a distinction. According to Rabbi Meir's position, the entire loan might seem to be tainted — but is it? The *gemara* says: *hasam vadai karna deheteira hu* — the principal was entirely permissible. There is no prohibition against lending money. The prohibition arose specifically from attaching interest to the loan; the act of lending itself was not only permitted but, in fact, a *mitzvah* under other circumstances. One therefore does not look at the loan-with-*ribbis* as a single indivisible prohibited act. The loan was legitimate; the *ribbis* was the violation. They are conceptually separable, and the *chachamim* hold that the legitimate portion — the principal — may still be collected.

In the grain case, by contrast, the situation may be reversed: it is possible that the seller intentionally adulterated the entire amount, not just the excess. If the entire admixture is potentially the product of intentional fraud, then it may be appropriate to apply the fine to the whole — whereas in the *ribbis* case, the principal was never in violation at all.

### Tosafot Questions

Tosafot raises several sharp questions in connection with this case. First: does Rabbi Meir's ruling mean only that the *shtar* is invalidated — such that if other witnesses could testify to the loan independently, the principal would still be collectable? Or does it mean the loan itself is entirely uncollectable, regardless of what evidence is available? Tosafot concludes that the plain reading is the latter: the loan itself, not merely the document, is what Rabbi Meir renders uncollectable. And this makes conceptual sense: the prohibition of *ribbis* attaches to the act of lending with interest, whether or not a *shtar* was written. This distinguishes the *ribbis* case from the case of a *shtar* with a false date (*shtarei chov hamukdamin pesulin*): in that scenario, the problem is with the document — it creates a false lien — and if independent witnesses can testify to the loan, the principal remains recoverable. In the *ribbis* case, the problem is the loan transaction itself, and no independent testimony cures it.

Second, Tosafot asks: borrowing with *ribbis* is itself a prohibition — the borrower transgresses as well as the lender. Why, then, does Rabbi Meir's fine fall only on the lender? Should the borrower not benefit from being released from his obligation? Tosafot offers two answers. First, the borrower typically has no real choice — he borrows because he must, and his incentive to avoid the prohibition is weak, whereas the lender is the one who profits and who has options. Fining the party with the incentive and the power to avoid the violation is more effective as a deterrent. Second, practically speaking, the borrower has nothing from which to be fined except the debt itself — and making the borrower forfeit his debt would effectively reward him for being party to a *ribbis* transaction, producing the problematic result of a *choteh niskar*, one who benefits from his transgression.

Third, Tosafot raises the question of the witnesses who signed the *shtar*. Since they too participated in facilitating a prohibited loan, should they not be rendered *eidim pesulim* — disqualified witnesses — making the *shtar* invalid on that ground even according to the *chachamim*? Tosafot answers by invoking an important principle: even a genuine transgression does not disqualify a witness as *pasul* if the prohibition is not widely recognized as such by the general public. The *gemara* applies this principle to the prohibition of *lo tachmod*: even if one technically violates it by coveting and acquiring another's property while paying for it, one is not rendered *pasul* as a witness, because the public does not perceive that acquisition as a transgression of *lo tachmod*. Tosafot applies the same logic to witnesses who sign a *ribbis* document — if the prohibition is not commonly perceived in that context, the witnesses are not disqualified. This principle has obvious contemporary relevance to questions about the status of witnesses from communities where certain prohibitions are not widely understood.

## Land Sale Analogy

### Land Measurement Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רָבִין בַּר רַב נַחְמָן: לֹא אֶת הַמּוֹתָר בִּלְבַד הוּא מַחֲזִיר, אֶלָּא מַחֲזִיר לוֹ אֶת כָּל הָרְבָעִין כּוּלָּן. אַלְמָא הֵיכָא דְּבָעֵי אַהְדּוֹרֵי – כּוּלַּהּ מַהְדַּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** further support for Rav Huna's ruling from **that** which **Ravin bar Rav Naḥman teaches** (104b). Ravin bar Rav Naḥman's statement is with regard to a situation when land that was sold is later found to be larger than stated at the time of the sale. If the deviation is not more than an area required to sow a quarter-*kav* of seed per *beit se'a* of land, then the buyer need not return any land to the seller. If the proportion of extra land is larger than this, **not only must** the buyer **return the extra** land that is beyond the limit of a quarter-*kav* area per *beit se'a*, **but he must** also **return to him every one of the** extra **quarter-***kav* areas of land that he received beyond the stated area of a *beit kor*. The Gemara infers: **Apparently, when one is required to return** part of a sale because of a discrepancy that is beyond the acceptable limit of deviation, then **one is required to return** the **entire** discrepancy and not just the amount that is beyond the acceptable limit. This supports Rav Huna's ruling.

The final *ta shema* brings a case from a *mishnah* dealing with land sales. When a person sells a *beit kor* — an area of land equal to thirty *beit se'ah*, each *beit se'ah* being fifty by fifty *amot* — there is a question of how much measurement error is tolerable. If the seller specifies *mida b'chevel* — "by the measuring rope," meaning a precise measurement — then even a small discrepancy requires correction. If, however, the seller says the land is a *beit kor* *pachot o yeter* — "more or less" — then a certain degree of variation is acceptable. The tolerable variation, like the grain case, is one *rova* per *se'ah*: since a *beit kor* contains thirty *se'ah*, the total tolerable variation is thirty *rova'in*, which works out to approximately four percent of the total area.

### Rav Land Rule

Rav teaches: *lo et ha'mutar bilvad hu machzir ella machzir lo et kol ha'rivi'in kulam* — when the measurement exceeds the tolerable variation, the seller does not give back only the excess; he gives back all thirty-one *rova'in* (or however many the overage amounts to). This appears to be a direct parallel to our grain question: once the tolerable threshold is crossed, full reimbursement — not just reimbursement for the overage — is required.

### Land Significance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, ״הֵן חָסֵר הֵן יָתֵיר״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ, מִיהוּ רְבִיעַ לָא חֲשִׁיב; יָתֵר מֵרְבִיעַ – חֲשִׁיב, דְּכֵיוָן דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ לְאִיצְטְרוֹפֵי בְּתִשְׁעַת קַבִּין, הָוְיָא לַהּ אַרְעָא חֲשִׁיבְתָּא בְּאַפֵּי נַפְשָׁא; וְהָדְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the sale of land, the reason the buyer may keep the extra land when it is less than the acceptable limit is that the seller **said to him:** I am selling you this piece of land **whether it is** slightly **less or** slightly **more** than a *beit kor*, i.e., he agrees to accept a small deviation from the stated area. **But** while an extra **quarter**-*kav* area per *beit se'a* is **not significant,** and therefore the seller is willing to forgo it, **more than a quarter**-*kav* area per *beit se'a* **is significant,** and the seller is not willing to forgo any of it. Consequently, all of the extra land must be returned. The reason is **that since** all those extra areas of land **are fit to combine together to** form an area in which one could sow **nine *kav*** of seed, the extra land **is a significant plot of land in its own right, and** therefore **it must** all **be returned.** The land that was sold was stated to be a *beit kor*, which is thirty *beit se'a*. If the area of the extra land was of a proportion somewhat more than an area required to sow a quarter-*kav* of seed per *beit se'a* of land, then thirty such areas would collectively be about equal to an area required to sow nine *kav*.

The *gemara* again says *hachi hashta* — the parallel is not as clean as it appears. In the land case, the *gemara* explains, the reason full reimbursement is triggered is not simply that the threshold has been crossed, but that the total amount of overage, when aggregated, constitutes a significant and substantial plot of land in its own right. Thirty *rova'in* of overage combine to nearly nine *kav* of land — *havei lei ara chashivusa bifnei nafshah v'hadra* — and at that point, the combined excess is significant enough to stand as an independent parcel. No one is *mochel* on something of that significance, and so the entire overage must be returned.

In the grain case, by contrast, a small amount of legumes or dirt in a bag of wheat does not combine to form something independently significant in the same way. The *gemara*'s distinction is therefore not purely structural; it reflects a difference in the nature of what is at stake. A tolerable amount of extraneous matter in grain may remain subject to *mechila* even when there is some overage, whereas land, which accumulates into a recognizable plot, does not. The underlying question of how precisely the *mechila* threshold operates in the grain case — and whether full reimbursement there is a matter of *din* or *knas* — thus remains to be worked out in the subsequent discussion.
