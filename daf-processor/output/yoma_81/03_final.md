# Yoma 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Food Combination

### Kol HaOchlin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָאוֹכָלִין. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲכַל אוּמְצָא וּמִילְחָא — מִצְטָרֵף. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָאו אֲכִילָה הִיא, כֵּיוָן דְּאָכְלִי אִינָשֵׁי — מִצְטָרְפִין. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: צִיר שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי יָרָק מִצְטָרֵף לִכְכוֹתֶבֶת בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא מַשְׁקֶה הוּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: כׇּל אַכְשׁוֹרֵי אוּכְלָא — אוּכְלָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § We learned in the mishna: **All** types of **foods** combine to form a measure of liability with regard to eating on Yom Kippur. **Rav Pappa said:** If one **ate meat and** the **salt** that was on it, these **combine** to make the volume of a large date. **Although** consuming salt alone **is not** considered **eating, since people do eat** meat with salt together, **they combine** into one measure. Similarly, **Reish Lakish said: Brine on a vegetable combines** with the vegetable to make the **volume of a large date** with regard to the prohibition of eating **on Yom Kippur.** The Gemara expresses surprise at this: **It** is **obvious.** Why should the brine not combine with the vegetable, considering that it is itself food? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that brine is **a beverage,** and food and drinks do not combine, **it teaches us** that **any item that prepares food** for eating **is** considered **a food.**

The daf begins on 81b, approximately ten lines from the bottom, with the principle of *kol ha'ochlin mistarfin* — all foods combine for the minimum measure of a *kizayit*. The *kizayit*, it should be noted, refers to a measure equivalent in size to an olive, not to the literal consumption of an olive itself; that is what the comparative prefix *kaf* indicates.

### Salt & Meat

Rav Papa teaches: *achal umtza v'melach mistarfin* — if one ate raw meat together with salt, they combine toward the minimum measure. The case raises the question of what qualifies as food and what qualifies as liquid, given the rule, to be encountered shortly, that food and liquid do not combine. Salt on its own might not be considered food in the relevant sense. Nevertheless, the Gemara explains: *v'af al gav d'lav achila hi, kevan d'achli inshi mistarfin* — even though eating salt alone does not constitute eating, since people eat it together with meat as a normal manner of consumption, the salt is subsumed into the food and the two combine.

### Sauce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שׁוּרֵי אוּכְלָא — אוּכְלָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Any item that prepares food** for eating **is** considered **a food.**

Reish Lakish teaches: *tzir al gabi yerek mistaref* — the juice or sauce that rests upon a vegetable combines with it for the minimum measure of a *kigrogoret* on Yom Kippur. One might have thought the *tzir* is a liquid and therefore cannot combine with the solid vegetable. Rashi explains the *tzir* here as the cooking juices or sauce that enhances the food — *matka u'mateka d'ochla*, that which sweetens and improves the dish. The Gemara's principle is: *kol shurei ochla ochla hu* — anything whose function is to improve and enhance the food is itself considered food. Accordingly, meat served with sauce does not present a case of food and liquid; all of it is categorized as food and combines toward the minimum. A related question arises concerning Shabbat, where the issue of *bishul achar bishul b'lach* involves dishes that are part solid and part liquid, with determination made on the basis of *ikar v'tafel*. Similarly, the categorization of a dish like vegetable soup — whether its minimum measure is assessed as a solid (*kigrogoret*) or liquid (*melo lugmav*) — presents its own analytical questions, though the Gemara does not resolve them here.

## Achila Gassa

### Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הָאוֹכֵל אֲכִילָה גַּסָּה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא תְעוּנֶּה״ כְּתִיב, פְּרָט לְמַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish said: One who eats** in **an excessive manner on Yom Kippur,** to the degree that he forces himself to continue eating even when full **is exempt,** e.g., one who ate beyond being satiated on Yom Kippur eve and then ate something else as soon as the fast began. **What is the reason** for that? Because the Torah does not mention the prohibition of eating on Yom Kippur, but it **was written** "any soul which **shall not be afflicted** in that same day, he shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 23:29), **excluding one who harms** himself, e.g., one who does not enjoy his food at all.

Reish Lakish further teaches: *achal achila gassa b'Yom HaKippurim patur* — one who eats on Yom Kippur in the manner of *achila gassa*, that is, while already completely satiated and forcing additional food down, is exempt. The case envisions one who filled himself on *Erev Yom Kippur* and then continued to eat upon the arrival of Yom Kippur even though he was entirely full — the Yom Kippur eating constitutes a form of compulsive overindulgence rather than genuine eating.

### Mai Taama

The rationale: *asher lo te'uneh* — the prohibition is derived from the concept of affliction. *Prat l'mazik* — it excludes a form of consumption that does not abate any sense of affliction.

### Prat L'Mazik

Rashi's formulation here is particularly precise. He writes: *l'zeh she'eino mitbatel mimenu shum inuy al yedei achila* — this excludes the person whose eating accomplishes nothing in terms of removing the sense of affliction. And then Rashi adds a striking line: *mazik hu et ha'ochlin v'et atzmo* — this type of eating is damaging to the food and damaging to oneself. It does no good to the eater and it wastes the food. It is not eating; it is destruction.

### Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: זָר שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה אֲכִילָה גַּסָּה — מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ. ״כִּי יֹאכַל״ — פְּרָט לְמַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **Reish Lakish said: A non-priest who ate *teruma* in an excessive manner pays the principal,** that which he took, **and does not pay the** additional **fifth,** which one who illegally eats *teruma* pays to the priest as a penalty. This is because it states about one who eats *teruma*: "And **if** a man **eat** of the sacred thing in error, then he shall add a fifth to it, and give the priest the sacred thing" (Leviticus 22:14). The word "eat" **excludes** one who is not eating but **harming** himself. He does, however, pay the principal, since he caused a loss to the priest. The fifth is only paid by one who eats normally, not excessively.

This principle is applied to a parallel case in the laws of *terumah*. If a non-*kohen* eats *terumah* inadvertently (*bishogeg*), he is required to repay the value along with an additional fifth — though the mathematics should be noted: if the original value is one hundred, he pays twenty-five, and that twenty-five is a fifth of the resulting total of one hundred and twenty-five. However, this enhanced payment applies only when the act constitutes genuine eating. If a *zar* ate *terumah* in the manner of *achila gassa*, *meshalem et ha'keren v'eino meshalem et ha'chomesh* — he pays back only the principal and not the additional fifth. The verse states *ki yochal* — *when he eats* — and *prat l'mazik*: this disqualified form of consumption is categorized as damage, not eating.

The Gemara raises a broader implication: the same logic should presumably apply to other prohibited foods such as *chelev* or *chametz* eaten in the manner of *achila gassa*. With Yom Kippur, the analysis turns on whether the affliction has been abated; with other *ma'achalot assurot*, the severity of the prohibition is rooted in the problematic nature of the substance itself, and one is still placing the forbidden substance into one's body. Nevertheless, the tendency of the halachic literature is to assume that the exemption of *achila gassa* — understood through the lens of *achal prat l'mazik* — applies across these categories as well. Elsewhere in the Gemara there are additional applications of this principle, and the two cases presented here — Yom Kippur and *terumah* — represent classic examples from the domain of *ma'achalot assurot*.

## Non-Standard Eating

### Chewing Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכּוֹסֵס שְׂעוֹרִים שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה — מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ. ״כִּי יֹאכַל״ — פְּרָט לְמַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **who chews on barley of *teruma*** that has not been ground or baked **pays the principal and not** an extra **fifth.** The reason is that it states **"eats"** (Leviticus 22:14), **which excludes one who harms** himself. Consuming raw barley is considered self-harm, not eating.

Rabbi Yirmiya said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *zar she'koses se'orim shel terumah, meshalem et ha'keren v'eino meshalem et ha'chomesh*. If a non-*kohen* chews raw barley of *terumah*, he pays back the principal but not the additional fifth. The term *koses* refers specifically to chewing raw grain in a manner that is not a normal mode of eating. Since the verse says *ki yochal* — *when he eats* — and this is not a normal act of eating, it falls under *prat l'mazik*: it is not eating but a form of damage.

One might ask whether the same conclusion applies on Yom Kippur. The analysis there operates along two separate tracks: one concerns what constitutes a halachic act of eating, and the other concerns whether the act abates the sense of *inuy*. In principle, these could yield different results. Chewing raw grain is not a normal act of eating, yet the process might still fill one's stomach, which could be relevant to the question of affliction. The matter awaits the upcoming *mishnah* which addresses eating substances not normally consumed.

### Prunes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זָר שֶׁבָּלַע שִׁזְפִין שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה וֶהֱקִיאָן וַאֲכָלָן אַחֵר, רִאשׁוֹן — מְשַׁלֵּם (אֶת) קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ. שֵׁנִי — אֵין מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא דְּמֵי עֵצִים לָרִאשׁוֹן בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizvi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In the case of **a non-priest who swallowed plums of *teruma*** whole **and vomited them out,** whereupon **another** person **ate them, the first** one **pays the principal plus a fifth.** When the first one benefited from the plums, he acquired them and must therefore pay for them, and **the second** one must pay their worth to the first one. Since they are only fit for fuel after the first one vomited them out, the second person is considered to have damaged their value as fuel. Therefore, he **pays only the price of wood,** i.e., fuel, **to the first** person.

Rav Shizbi said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *zar she'bala shezifin shel terumah, veheki'an, va'achalan acher* — if a non-*kohen* swallowed prunes of *terumah* whole, then regurgitated them, and another person ate the regurgitated prunes. The example is constructed with prunes precisely because they can be swallowed whole and regurgitated while remaining intact, so the second person is eating something that still physically resembles a whole prune, making the case a genuine *chiddush*.

The ruling: *rishon meshalem keren ve'chomesh* — the first person pays both the principal and the fifth. Swallowing something whole, unlike *achila gassa* or chewing raw grain, constitutes a normal act of eating; people sometimes do swallow things whole. Regarding the second person: *eino meshalem ela d'mei etzim le'rishon bilvad* — he owes only the value of firewood, and only to the first person, not to the *kohen*.

### Regurgitated Status

Two independent principles drive the ruling regarding the second person. First, the moment the first person swallowed the prune, he acquired it. The laws of *Bava Kamma* establish that once a thief transforms a stolen object, he takes possession of it and must pay the *kohen* — but from that point on, it is his. Therefore, the second person's obligation runs to the first person, not to the *kohen*. Second, the object is no longer considered food. Even though it remains physically intact as a whole prune, once it has been swallowed and regurgitated, no normal person would eat it; it has lost its status as food and is now valued only as fuel — hence, firewood.

This raises a conceptually interesting question. The Gemara frames the second person's exemption from the enhanced payment in terms of the object no longer being food. But one might ask: is the reason the object is no longer considered food purely a function of the physical reality of its having been swallowed and regurgitated? Or is it because the halachic act performed by the first person constituted a genuine *achila*, and that halachic consumption is what changes the object's status vis-à-vis the second person? The way the Gemara frames the discussion suggests that the halachic classification of the first person's act — whether or not it is deemed an *achila* — may bear on how we assess the object's status for the second person. This remains an open point of analysis.

## Food & Drink

### Don't Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹכֵל וְהַשּׁוֹתֶה אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּמַחְלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא. דִּתְנַן, כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כֹּל שֶׁטּוּמְאָתוֹ וְשִׁיעוּרוֹ שָׁוֶה — מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that **food and drink do not combine.** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this? **Rav Ḥisda said:** This *halakha* **is taught as a dispute, and** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua. As we learned** in a mishna: **Rabbi Yehoshua stated a general principle: Any** item **that** is **identical** to another item in **its** type of **ritual impurity,** e.g., impurity that lasts only until evening, **and its measure** for impurity, e.g., an olive-bulk, **combines** with the other item to reach one measure to render other items impure. Therefore, two half olive-bulk pieces of two corpses or of two animal carcasses, or two half lentil-bulk pieces from two creeping animals all combine to render other items impure.

The *mishnah* states: *ha'ochel veha'shoteh ein mistarfin* — food and drink do not combine for the minimum measure. Rav Chisda raises the question of whose view this represents: *be'machloket shnuya* — it is actually a matter of dispute, and the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehoshua.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאָתוֹ וְלֹא שִׁיעוּרוֹ, שִׁיעוּרוֹ וְלֹא טוּמְאָתוֹ, לֹא טוּמְאָתוֹ וְלֹא שִׁיעוּרוֹ — אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** However, if one item is identical to another in **its ritual impurity but not its measure,** e.g., half a lentil-bulk of a creeping animal and half an olive-bulk of an animal carcass, which both impart impurity until nightfall; **or if its measure** is identical **but not its** type of **ritual impurity,** e.g., half an olive-bulk from a corpse, which imparts impurity for seven days, and half an olive-bulk from an animal carcass, which imparts impurity until nightfall; and all the more so, when **neither its ritual impurity nor its measure** are identical, then the items **do not combine.** Here, too, although both eating and drinking are prohibited on their own, they do not combine because they do not have the same measure that imparts impurity.

The source is a principle stated by Rabbi Yehoshua in a *mishnah* elsewhere: *kol she'tumato ve'shiyuro shaveh mitstareif* — whatever shares identical *tumah* status and identical minimum measure combines. This principle was articulated in the context of *tumah* laws, not Yom Kippur.

To illustrate: if one has half a *kezayit* of *neveilah* and half a *kezayit* from another *neveilah*, they combine because both the type of *tumah* (one day's impurity) and the minimum measure (*kezayit*) are identical. But if the *tumah* differs while the measure is the same — for example, half a *kezayit* of *neveilah* (one day's *tumah*) and half a *kezayit* of a human corpse (seven days' *tumah*) — they do not combine, even toward the more lenient one-day category, because the difference in the *tumah* itself places them in fundamentally distinct categories. Similarly, if the measure differs while the *tumah* is the same — say, a half-*kezayit* of *neveilah* and a half-lentil-sized piece of *sheretz*, where both cause one-day *tumah* but the *neveilah* requires an olive's measure while the *sheretz* requires only a lentil's measure — they likewise do not combine, because the differing minimums reflect a fundamental categorical distinction. And if both the *tumah* and the measure differ, they certainly do not combine.

The implicit view of the *chachamim* is more lenient: even across those lines, combination may be possible for the more lenient category, so that a half-*kezayit* of *neveilah* and a half-lentil piece of *sheretz* might combine for a one-day *tumah*, since both cause that degree of impurity. Only Rabbi Yehoshua insists that any difference along either dimension — *tumah* or measure — prevents combination entirely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא קָא אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם, אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה, דְּשֵׁם טוּמְאָה חַד הִיא. אֲבָל הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם יַתּוֹבֵי דַּעְתָּא הוּא, וְהַאי לָא מִיַּתְּבָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, until now we have** heard **the Rabbis say there** that items with different measures combine **only with respect to ritual impurity. Since the** phenomenon **of ritual impurity is one concept,** different types combine. **But here,** in the case of Yom Kippur, the measure that determines liability is set **due to settling the mind, and this** combination of eating and drinking together **does not settle the mind.**

Rav Nachman then argues that even the *rabanan* who disagree with Rabbi Yehoshua in the context of *tumah* would agree that food and drink do not combine on Yom Kippur. Their leniency in the *tumah* context rested on the reasoning that *shem tumah chad hi* — it is all one category of *tumah*, and the distinction between stricter and more lenient subcategories does not prevent combination toward the more lenient outcome. But Yom Kippur is categorically different.

### Psychological Reality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בְּמַחְלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דִּתְנַן: כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ כּוּ׳. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה, אֲבָל הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם יַתּוֹבֵי דַּעְתָּיהּ הוּא, וְהַאי לָא קָא מִיַּתְּבָא דַּעְתָּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, Reish Lakish said:** This question of whether food and drink combine to reach a measure that determines liability on Yom Kippur **is taught as a dispute.** The approach brought in the mishna that states that they do not combine is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, as we learned** in a mishna: **Rabbi Yehoshua stated a general principle** with regard to whether items with different types of impurity and measures combine, as Rav Ḥisda explained above. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** in accordance with the opinion of Rav Naḥman: **Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with **the** opinion of **the Rabbis, until now we have** heard **the Rabbis say there** that items with different measures combine **only with regard to ritual impurity. But here,** in the case of Yom Kippur, the measure that determines liability is set **due to settling the mind, and this** combination of eating and drinking together **does not** settle the mind.

On Yom Kippur, the operative question is not a formalistic one of halachic categories and minimum thresholds. It is a question of *yisuvay da'ata* — whether the affliction has been genuinely abated. *Va'hai lo ka meisyava da'ata* — an amount of food just below the threshold combined with an amount of drink just below the threshold does not achieve that psychological reality. A person who consumes both, each in a sub-threshold quantity, has not genuinely settled his mind and dispelled his discomfort. The threshold of each must be reached independently for the *inuy* to be considered removed.

Rabbi Yochanan takes the further position: *afilu teima rabbanan* — even according to the *rabanan* who disagree with Rabbi Yehoshua in *tumah* laws, here, on Yom Kippur, they would also require that each category reach its minimum independently. The Gemara's reasoning makes clear that this is not merely a technical debate about halachic categories; it reflects a substantive claim about the psychological reality that Yom Kippur law is tracking. This is the consistent theme the Gemara returns to across several discussions: the laws of *inuy* on Yom Kippur are rooted in an actual lived reality, not merely in formal legal classification. The standard, however, remains an objective one — what abates the sense of affliction for a person in normal circumstances — rather than a purely subjective assessment varying from individual to individual, which the Gemara reserves only for the question of *melo lugmav* (the minimum measure of liquid), and even there only for drink, not for food.

## Single Korban

### One Aveira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָכַל וְשָׁתָה בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא חַטָּאת אַחַת. אָכַל וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכָה — חַיָּיב שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת. אָכַל אוֹכָלִין שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִן לַאֲכִילָה, וְשָׁתָה מַשְׁקִין שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִן לִשְׁתִיָּה, וְשָׁתָה צִיר אוֹ מוּרְיָיס — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one ate and drank** unwittingly **within one lapse of awareness,** e.g., he forgot that it is Yom Kippur, **he is liable** to bring **only one sin-offering.** However, if **he ate and performed labor** unwittingly, **he is liable to bring two sin-offerings,** as by doing so he violated two separate prohibitions. If **he ate foods that are not fit for eating, or drank liquids that are not fit for drinking, or drank** fish **brine or** the briny **liquid** in which fish are pickled, **he is exempt,** as that is not the typical manner of eating or drinking.

The *mishnah* states: *achal ve'shata be'e'lem echad eino chayav ela chatat achat* — if one ate a minimum measure and drank a minimum measure on Yom Kippur in a single state of forgetfulness, forgetting that it was Yom Kippur, only one *korban* is brought. Although food and drink do not combine to constitute a minimum measure, they do share the same prohibition of *lo te'uneh* — the prohibition against abating one's affliction. When both acts occur within a single uninterrupted state of forgetfulness, they constitute a single commission of that one prohibition and therefore generate only one *korban*. The parallel case of a *nazir* who drank wine throughout an entire day without any intervening awareness of his *nezirut* illustrates the same principle: however many acts are performed, if there is no intervening awareness, they remain one transgression for purposes of the obligation.

It is worth noting that this convergence on a single *korban* does not require that the acts be performed within the time of *bechdei achilat pras* or *bechdei shtiyat revi'it*, which would make them one continuous act. Even acts at distinct moments are not separate enough — under the criterion of a single uninterrupted state of forgetfulness — to generate separate *korbanos*.

### Two Aveiros

One who eats and performs *melacha*, chayav shtei chatatot* — one who eats and performs *melacha* in a single state of forgetfulness is liable for two *chatatot*. These are two categorically distinct prohibitions: the eating violates *lo te'uneh*, while the *melacha* violates *lo ta'aseh kol melacha*. Even within one continuous forgetting of Yom Kippur, distinct categories of prohibition each require their own *korban*.

## Non-Fit Food

### Unfit Food

The *mishnah* continues: *achal ochlin she'einam re'uyim le'achila, ve'shata mashkin she'einam re'uyim lishtiya* — one who consumes foods unfit for eating or liquids unfit for drinking is exempt. This returns to the question raised earlier about whether normal suitability for consumption is a precondition for the prohibition.

### Fish Brine

The *mishnah* gives as examples of non-potable liquid: *tzir* (fish brine) and *moryes* (fish fat). These are liquids that pass through the body without genuinely satisfying thirst or hunger, and their consumption therefore does not constitute the kind of act that falls within the prohibition of *lo te'uneh*.

## Azharah

### No Explicit Lav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נֶאֶמְרָה אַזְהָרָה בְּעִינּוּי? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר. הֵיכִי נִכְתּוֹב? נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל״ — אֲכִילָה בִּכְזַיִת! נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא תְעוּנֶּה״ — קוּם אֱכוֹל מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Reish Lakish said: Why is there no warning stated about affliction;** why did the Torah not state explicitly that it is prohibited to eat and drink on Yom Kippur? It is **because it was not possible** to write it that way. The Gemara explains: **How could** the Merciful One **write it? Let the Merciful One write: One shall not eat** on Yom Kippur. The term eat prohibits **eating the amount of an olive-bulk,** whereas on Yom Kippur the actual prohibited measure is a large date-bulk. **Let the Merciful One write: Do not be afflicted.** That indicates the opposite of affliction, and would **mean: Get up and eat.** Therefore, it is written: "For whatsoever soul it be that shall not be afflicted on that same day, he shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 23:29).

Reish Lakish poses a fundamental question: *mipnei mah lo ne'emra azhara be'inuy* — why does the Torah never state an explicit negative prohibition (*lav*) concerning fasting? The question has practical halachic force. The general rule is that a *korban chatat* (and *malkot*) require the transgression of an explicit negative prohibition. Many severe prohibitions that carry *karet* — such as the failure to perform *brit milah* or *korban pesach* — do not generate a *korban* because the violation consists of failing to perform a positive *mitzvah* rather than transgressing a *lav*. Yom Kippur eating carries *karet*, but where is the *lav*? The Torah says *ve'initem et nafshoteichem* — a positive command to fast. If one fails to fast, he has not fulfilled a positive *mitzvah*. Where does the Torah say, in negative terms, "do not eat"?

### Grammar Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״הִשָּׁמֶר פֶּן לֹא תְעוּנֶּה״! אִם כֵּן נְפִישִׁי לְהוּ לָאוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hoshaya strongly objects to this: Let the Merciful One write** it in this manner: **Guard yourself lest you not be afflicted.** The Gemara answers: **If so, there would be** too **many negative mitzvot,** as the following are all expressions of prohibition: Guard, lest, and not. One would then say that there are three prohibitions against eating.

Reish Lakish's answer: *mishum dela efshar* — because it is grammatically impossible for the Torah to articulate such a prohibition. The Gemara walks through the options. Could the Torah have written *lo yochal*, "do not eat"? No — that would have implied the standard *kezayit* measure, whereas we know Yom Kippur operates by the measure of a *kigrogoret* and is organized around the concept of *inuy*. Could the Torah have written *lo te'uneh*, "do not fast"? That is the opposite of the intended meaning — it would be a command to eat. What is needed is something like "do not not fast" — a double negation that neither biblical idiom nor, as the Gemara demonstrates, rabbinic legal grammar can elegantly accommodate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״הִשָּׁמֶר בְּמִצְוַת עִינּוּי״! אִם כֵּן ״הִשָּׁמֶר״ דְּלָאו — לָאו, ״הִשָּׁמֶר״ דַּעֲשֵׂה — עֲשֵׂה! מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: נִכְתּוֹב: ״אַל תָּסוּר מִן הָעִינּוּי״! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye strongly objects to this: Let the Merciful One write: Guard yourself in the mitzva of affliction.** The Gemara rejects this: **If so,** there is a principle: If the words **guard yourself** are written **about a prohibition,** it is considered to be **a prohibition;** if the words **guard yourself** are written **about a positive mitzva,** it is considered to be **a positive mitzva.** Had the Torah written it that way, one would understand that there is a positive mitzva of affliction, but there is no element of prohibition. **Rav Ashi strongly objects to this: Let the Merciful One write: Do not stray from afflicting yourself,** which would imply a negative mitzva. The Gemara says: Indeed, this is **difficult.** The Gemara concedes that it could have been written in this manner.

Rav Hoshaia challenges: could the Torah have written *hishamer pen lo te'aneh* — "guard yourself lest you not fast"? The response is that *hishamer* and *pen* each independently function as signals of a negative prohibition, and using both would generate two *lavin* when only one is desired; and simply writing *hishamer lo te'aneh* is grammatically incoherent. Rav Bibi bar Abaye suggests: *hishamer b'mitzvat inuy* — "guard yourself regarding the commandment of fasting." The response: *hishamer* when appended to a positive *mitzvah* strengthens the positive obligation; it does not transform it into a negative prohibition. Only *hishamer* appended to a prohibitory statement generates a *lav*. Rav Ashi proposes: *al tasur min ha'inuy* — "do not deviate from the fast." The Gemara leaves this with *teiku*.

### Implied Lav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא: ״וְעִנִּיתֶם אֶת נַפְשׁוֹתֵיכֶם וְכׇל מְלָאכָה לֹא תַעֲשׂוּ״. יָכוֹל יְהֵא עָנוּשׁ עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת מְלָאכָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל הַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה כׇּל מְלָאכָה בְּעֶצֶם הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״ — עַל עִיצּוּמוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, וְאֵינוֹ עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the prohibitions against eating and labor, the Gemara says: **And a *tanna* cites** the prohibitions relating to affliction on Yom Kippur **from here:** It is stated: **"And you shall afflict your souls, you shall do no manner of labor"** (Numbers 29:7). The Gemara considers: One **might** have thought that performing labor during the **extension** of the period of prohibition **of labor,** added before Yom Kippur actually begins, **is punishable** by *karet*. Therefore, **the verse states: "And whatsoever soul it be that does any manner of labor on that same day"** (Leviticus 23:30), which teaches that performing labor **on that very day is punishable by *karet*, but** labor performed during **the extension** of the period of prohibition **of labor is not punishable by *karet*.**

Despite the inability to articulate the prohibition elegantly, the conclusion is that the negative prohibition is nonetheless implied. The Torah's intent to prohibit eating on Yom Kippur is assumed, and this implied *lav* is sufficient to ground the *korban* and the *malkot*. The Gemara will now show that the *baraita* takes a different approach, deriving the negative prohibition through an explicit textual mechanism rather than relying solely on implication.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Linking Punishments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת מְלָאכָה, אֲבָל יְהֵא עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת עִינּוּי — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כִּי כׇל הַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא תְעוּנֶּה בְּעֶצֶם הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה וְנִכְרְתָה״. עַל עִיצּוּמוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, וְאֵינוֹ עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת עִינּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that performing labor during the **extension** of the period of prohibition **of labor is not punishable by *karet*,** but not afflicting oneself during the **extension** of the period **of affliction is punishable by *karet*.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For whatsoever soul it be that shall not be afflicted on that same day, he shall be cut off** from his people" (Leviticus 23:29), which teaches that not afflicting oneself **on that very day** is **punishable by *karet*, but** not afflicting oneself during **the extension** of the period **of affliction is not punishable by *karet*.**

The *baraita* does not rest on the argument that the prohibition is implied despite being inexpressible; it derives the negative prohibition through a formal *derashah*. The verse reads: *ve'inisem et nafshoteichem ve'chol melacha lo ta'asu* — "afflict yourselves and do no *melacha*." From here the *baraita* proceeds through several stages.

First it asks: perhaps one would be punished for performing *melacha* during the period of *tosefet*, the added time at the beginning or end of Yom Kippur. The verse *ve'chol ha'nefesh asher ta'aseh kol melacha b'etzem ha'yom ha'zeh* — "whoever performs *melacha* on the essence of this day" — indicates by the word *b'etzem* ("the essence") that *karet* applies to *melacha* performed on the day itself, *v'eino anush karet al tosefet*, not during the added portion. Then: perhaps even without *karet*, one transgresses a negative prohibition by performing *melacha* during *tosefet*? The verse again says *lo ta'asu b'etzem* — the prohibition applies to the essence of the day. Similarly, by fasting, the verse *ki kol ha'nefesh asher lo te'uneh b'etzem ha'yom ha'zeh* uses the same *b'etzem* language — *karet* for failing to fast applies to the day itself, not to the *tosefet* period. And the exclusion of eating during *tosefet* from negative prohibition is derived through a *kal va'chomer*: *melacha*, which is a universal prohibition applying on Shabbat and *Yom Tov*, does not generate a transgression during *tosefet*; *inuy*, which is particular to Yom Kippur alone and thus a weaker prohibition, certainly does not generate a transgression during *tosefet*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא בִּכְלַל עוֹנֶשׁ, אֲבָל יְהֵא מוּזְהָר עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת מְלָאכָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל מְלָאכָה לֹא תַעֲשׂוּ בְּעֶצֶם הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״. עַל עִיצּוּמוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם הוּא מוּזְהָר, וְאֵינוֹ מוּזְהָר עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought **that** one who performs labor during the extension would **not be included in the punishment** of *karet*, **but he would be warned against** performing labor during the **extension** of the period of prohibition **of labor** with a prohibition incurring the punishment of lashes; therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall do no manner of labor on that same day"** (Leviticus 23:28). That teaches that **he is warned** not to perform labor on **that very day,** but **he is not warned against** performing labor during the **extension** of the period of prohibition **of labor,** and therefore he is not liable to receive lashes for doing so.

So through these steps we learn: no *karet* and no negative prohibition apply to either *melacha* or *inuy* during *tosefet*. But then the *baraita* states: *aval azhara l'inuy shel yom atzmo lo lamadnu* — with all of that established, we still have not derived the negative prohibition against eating on Yom Kippur itself. From where is it learned?

### Kal VaChomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אַזְהָרָה לְעִינּוּי שֶׁל יוֹם עַצְמוֹ לֹא לָמַדְנוּ מִנַּיִין. לֹא יֹאמַר עוֹנֶשׁ בִּמְלָאכָה, דְּגָמַר מֵעִינּוּי: וּמָה עִינּוּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיוֹם טוֹב עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, מְלָאכָה שֶׁנּוֹהֶגֶת בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר — מוּפְנֶה לְהַקִּישׁ וְלָדוּן מִמֶּנּוּ גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה: נֶאֱמַר עוֹנֶשׁ בְּעִינּוּי וְנֶאֱמַר עוֹנֶשׁ בִּמְלָאכָה, מָה מְלָאכָה — לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר, אַף עִינּוּי — לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* comments: **But the warning** about lashes **during the very day itself we have not learned. From where** is it derived? The Torah **need not state the punishment** of *karet* for performing **labor, since one can learn it** by logical derivation **from** the punishment of not being in a state of **affliction,** as follows: **Just as** the requirement of **affliction, which is not observed on *Shabbatot* and Festivals, is punishable by *karet*** on Yom Kippur, with regard to the prohibition of **labor, which is observed on** all ***Shabbatot* and Festivals,** is it **not all the more so?** If so, **why** is the punishment for labor **stated** explicitly? **It is available to compare and learn a verbal analogy from it: A punishment** with regard to **affliction is stated, and a punishment** with regard to **labor is stated. Just as** with regard to **labor one is punished only if he was warned** first with a negative mitzva, **so too,** with regard to **affliction, one is punished only if he was warned.**

The *baraita* attempts a *kal va'chomer*: since *karet* is prescribed for violating *inuy*, and *inuy* is the less universal prohibition (applying only on Yom Kippur, not on Shabbat or *Yom Tov*), then *melacha* — which is universal — certainly carries *karet*. Therefore, the statement of *karet* by *melacha* is superfluous; one could have derived it from *inuy*. Since it is superfluous, it is *mufneh* — available to serve as the basis of a *gezeirah shavah* — and we link the two: just as *melacha* combines punishment with a negative prohibition, so too does *inuy*.

The Gemara challenges: *ika mifrach* — this inference is rebuttable. *Melacha* has its own stringency — it is universally applicable, applying on Shabbat and *Yom Tov* — which *inuy* lacks. Therefore, one cannot say the *karet* for *melacha* is superfluous; perhaps without the explicit verse one would not have known *melacha* carries *karet*, precisely because *melacha* is sometimes overridden (as in the *Beit HaMikdash* on Shabbat or Yom Kippur). So the *karet* for *melacha* is not superfluous and cannot serve as the basis of the equation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְעִינּוּי שֶׁלֹּא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ, תֹּאמַר בִּמְלָאכָה שֶׁהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **This** comparison can **be refuted.** How so? **Whereas affliction** has **no permitted** exceptions **from its general** prohibition, as the mitzva to afflict oneself applies to all the Jewish people, what can **you say with regard to labor, which** has **permitted** exceptions **from its general** prohibition? It is permitted to perform the Temple services on Yom Kippur, which include prohibited labors, e.g., slaughtering animals and offering incense. Consequently, there is no proof that the mitzva of affliction is more lenient than the prohibition of labor.

The *baraita* then reverses direction: let us say instead that the *karet* for *inuy* is superfluous, derivable from *melacha* — since *melacha*, which is sometimes overridden in the *Beit HaMikdash*, nonetheless carries *karet*, how much more so should *inuy*, which is never overridden in that context. Therefore the *karet* for *inuy* is extra and *mufneh*, enabling the equation. The same challenge applies in reverse: *inuy* has its own stringency — it is never permitted in the *Beit HaMikdash* — while *melacha* is universal but sometimes allowed. Each prohibition has dimensions of stringency that the other lacks; neither the *karet* for *melacha* nor the *karet* for *inuy* is demonstrably superfluous given these competing considerations, and neither can be used as the basis of the *gezeirah shavah* on the grounds of being derivable from the other.

### Mufneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִלָּא: לֹא יֵאָמֵר עוֹנֶשׁ בְּעִינּוּי, דְּגָמַר מִמְּלָאכָה. מָה מְלָאכָה שֶׁהוּתְּרָה מִכְּלָלָהּ — עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, עִינּוּי שֶׁלֹּא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר — מוּפְנֶה, לְהַקִּישׁ וְלָדוּן מִמֶּנּוּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה: נֶאֱמַר עוֹנֶשׁ בְּעִינּוּי וְנֶאֱמַר עוֹנֶשׁ בִּמְלָאכָה, מָה מְלָאכָה עָנַשׁ וְהִזְהִיר — אַף עִינּוּי עָנַשׁ וְהִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say as follows: The Torah need **not say the punishment for** not being in a state of **affliction, since one can learn it from** the punishment for performing **labor** through an *a fortiori* inference. How so? **Just as labor, for which** exceptions **from the general** prohibition **are permitted,** e.g., performing the Temple service on Yom Kippur, and yet labor **is punishable by *karet*,** with regard to not being in a state of **affliction, which has no permitted** exceptions **from its general** prohibition, is it **not all the more so** that one who does not afflict himself should be liable to receive *karet*? If so, **why is it** nevertheless **stated? It is available to compare and learn a verbal analogy: Punishment is stated with regard to affliction, and punishment is stated with regard to labor. Just as for labor** the Torah **punished and warned, so too, for affliction** the Torah **punished and warned.**

At this point Ravina intervenes: *hai tanna etzem etzem g'mar* — this *tanna* is not trying to identify a superfluous *karet*; he is identifying a superfluous instance of the word *etzem*. The word *b'etzem ha'yom ha'zeh* already appeared to teach us the exclusion of *tosefet*. The *gezeirah shavah* works by finding a word that is *mufneh* — that is, not needed for its plain context, so that it is available to link two topics. The Gemara notes: *d'ilo mufneh, ika mifrach d'varchina* — without a truly superfluous word available, the equation could always be challenged along the lines just discussed. The question is therefore: which instance of *b'etzem* is extra?

### Five Instances

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הַאי תַּנָּא, ״עֶצֶם״ ״עֶצֶם״ גָּמַר. מוּפְנֶה, דְּאִי לָא מוּפְנֶה — אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ כִּדְפָרְכִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** We did not properly understand the teaching of this *baraita*, as **this *tanna* derived** a verbal analogy from the words "on that **same** day" (Leviticus 23:29) stated with regard to affliction, and "on that **same** day" (Leviticus 23:28) stated with regard to labor. The Gemara comments: We must say that **it is available,** meaning that in both places, the phrase "on that same day" is unnecessary within its own context and comes only to teach this verbal analogy; **as, if it were not available, it could be refuted, as we refuted it** above.

Ravina answers: *chamisha kra'ei k'tivei* — there are five separate verses stating the prohibition against *melacha*. One establishes the negative prohibition for the day; one for the night; one establishes the punishment for the day; one for the night; and the fifth is *l'afnu'ei* — it is extra, available as the basis of the *gezeirah shavah*, to link *inuy* to *melacha* and teach that just as the prohibition of *melacha* is accompanied by a negative prohibition, so too is the prohibition of *inuy*, whether for the day or night. The details of which five verses these are can be verified by examining the relevant passages in the *Chumash*, but the principle is clear: the redundancy in the *melacha* verses creates the textual space for the equation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאיֵי, אִפְּנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֶה. חַמְשָׁה קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי בִּמְלָאכָה, חַד לְאַזְהָרָה דִימָמָא, וְחַד לְאַזְהָרָה דְלֵילְיָא, וְחַד לְעוֹנֶשׁ דִּימָמָא, וְחַד לְעוֹנֶשׁ דְּלֵילְיָא, וְחַד לְאַפְנוֹיֵי לְמִגְמַר עִינּוּי מִמְּלָאכָה, בֵּין דִּימָמָא בֵּין דְּלֵילְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No, it is certainly available,** and we may learn from it, as **five verses are written** with regard to the prohibition of **labor** on Yom Kippur. **One** to teach **a warning** with regard to the prohibition of labor **on the day** of Yom Kippur itself; **and one for a warning** with regard to labor **on the night** of Yom Kippur; **and one for the punishment** of *karet* for performing labor **on the day; and one for the punishment** for performing labor **at night; and one** verse **to be available to derive** the requirement of **affliction from** the prohibition of **labor, both during the day and night,** using a verbal analogy.

A separate tradition then cites the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *ne'emar kan inuy ve'ne'emar sham inuy* — the word *inuy* appears here (on Yom Kippur) and there (in another context). The referent, per Rashi, is the verse describing the case of rape (*al devar asher ina et eshet re'ehu*), where the act of *inuy* — afflicting another — is backed by a negative prohibition. Just as there the prohibition is *against* performing the act of *inuy*, while here the *mitzvah* is *to* perform it — the *inuy* of fasting. This asymmetry limits how far the comparison can be pressed, though the *baraita* uses the formal link in terminology to establish the general principle that where *inuy* appears, a negative prohibition accompanies it.

## Alternatives

### Shabbat Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן עִינּוּי, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן עִינּוּי. מָה לְהַלָּן — לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר, אַף כָּאן — לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: יָלֵיף ״שַׁבַּת שַׁבָּתוֹן״ מִשַּׁבַּת בְּרֵאשִׁית: מָה לְהַלָּן — לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר, אַף כָּאן — לֹא עָנַשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** an alternative verbal analogy for the derivation: **"Affliction" is stated here,** with regard to Yom Kippur, **and "affliction" is stated later,** with regard to a man who rapes a woman: "Because he has afflicted his neighbor's wife" (Deuteronomy 22:24). **Just as there,** in the case of rape, the Torah **did not punish unless there was** prior **warning, so too here,** in the case of Yom Kippur, the Torah **did not punish unless there was** prior **warning. Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said** a different proof: **Derive** a verbal analogy from the words **"Shabbat of solemn rest"** (Leviticus 16:31) written with regard to Yom Kippur **from** the words "Shabbat of solemn rest" (Exodus 31:15, 32:5; Leviticus 23:3) written with regard to the weekly Shabbat, which commemorates the **Shabbat of Creation. Just as there,** in the case of Shabbat, the Torah **did not punish unless there was** prior **warning, so too here,** in the case of Yom Kippur, the Torah **did not punish unless there was** prior **warning.**

Rav Acha bar Yaakov offers a different derivation: *yalif Shabbat Shabbaton mi-Shabbat Bereishit* — the term *Shabbat Shabbaton* used for Yom Kippur is the same term used for the weekly Shabbat. Just as the weekly Shabbat is accompanied by a negative prohibition against violating it, so too is Yom Kippur. The shared language creates the equation.

### Intrinsic Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הוּא גּוּפֵיהּ שַׁבָּת אִיקְּרִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״תִּשְׁבְּתוּ שַׁבַּתְּכֶם״. בִּשְׁלָמָא רַב פָּפָּא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב — דִּקְרָא דִּכְתִיב בְּגוּפֵיהּ עֲדִיף. אֶלָּא רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב פָּפָּא?
>
> **Translation:** Yom Kippur **itself is called "Shabbat," as it is written:** "From evening until evening, **you shall rest on your Shabbat"** (Leviticus 23:32). The Gemara compares the various opinions. **Granted, Rav Pappa did not say as Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov** did because **a verse that is written about the matter itself is preferable** to a verbal analogy. **But what is the reason** that **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov did not state** his opinion **in accordance with the opinion of Rav Pappa?**

Rav Pappa argues differently: *hu gufei Shabbat ikrei* — the very fact that Yom Kippur is itself called *Shabbat* is sufficient. One need not make a formal comparison to Shabbat *Bereishit* through shared language; the application of the word *Shabbat* to Yom Kippur itself carries the intrinsic meaning of a negative prohibition against violating it.

### Word Meaning

The Gemara notes the difference between the two positions. Rav Pappa prefers not to derive the prohibition through an external analogy to Shabbat *Bereishit*; he would rather say that the word *Shabbat* as it appears on Yom Kippur itself carries the negative implication — *dekra dichtiv bei begufei atei*, the word as it stands in its own verse does the work. The challenge to this position is that the word *Shabbat* carries its meaning of prohibition only because of its association with *Shabbat Bereishit* to begin with; intrinsically it does not seem to function as an independent source of a *lav*. The question of why Rav Acha bar Yaakov did not simply adopt Rav Pappa's approach is answered by the Gemara: Rav Acha bar Yaakov needs the verse *tishbetu shabbatchem* for an entirely separate teaching, as the *baraita* cited next demonstrates.

## Tosefet

### Ninth Evening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״וְעִנִּיתֶם אֶת נַפְשׁוֹתֵיכֶם בְּתִשְׁעָה לַחוֹדֶשׁ״. יָכוֹל יַתְחִיל וְיִתְעַנֶּה בְּתִשְׁעָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בָּעֶרֶב״. אִי בָּעֶרֶב — יָכוֹל מִשֶּׁתֶּחְשַׁךְ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּתִשְׁעָה״. הָא כֵּיצַד? מַתְחִיל וּמִתְעַנֶּה מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, מִכָּאן שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין מֵחוֹל עַל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He requires this** verse of "keep your Shabbat" **for that which was taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall afflict your souls on the ninth day of the month** at evening, from evening until evening, you shall rest on your Shabbat" (Leviticus 23:32). One **might** have thought that **one should start to afflict oneself on the ninth** of Tishrei; therefore, **the verse states "at evening." If** the Torah had stated only **"at evening,"** one **might** have thought that the fast starts only **when darkness falls;** therefore, **the verse states "on the ninth,"** implying that one begins to fast on the ninth of Tishrei. **How** can these verses be reconciled? **One begins to fast while it is still daytime; from here** it is derived **that one** sanctifies and **extends from** the **non-sacred** weekday **to** the **sacred** day of Yom Kippur.

A *baraita* is cited: *ve'inisem et nafshoteichem be'tisha la'chodesh* — "you shall afflict yourselves on the ninth of the month." The verse continues: *ba'erev me'erev ad erev* — "in the evening, from evening until evening." The *baraita* notes an apparent problem: do we actually fast on the ninth? The fast begins on the tenth! Why does the verse say "the ninth"?

### Evening to Evening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בִּכְנִיסָתוֹ, בִּיצִיאָתוֹ מִנַּיִן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵעֶרֶב עַד עֶרֶב״. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, (יָמִים טוֹבִים) מִנַּיִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּשְׁבְּתוּ״. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא (יָמִים טוֹבִים, שַׁבָּתוֹת) מִנַּיִן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שַׁבַּתְּכֶם״. הָא כֵּיצַד? כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר שְׁבוּת, (מִכָּאן שֶׁ)מוֹסִיפִין מֵחוֹל עַל הַקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived **only** that one must add time **at the beginning** of Yom Kippur. **From where** do I derive that one adds time **at the conclusion of** Yom Kippur? **The verse states: "From evening until evening"** (Leviticus 23:32), implying that one adds at the end as well, just as he does at the beginning. **And I** have derived **only** the mitzva of adding to **Yom Kippur; from where** is it derived that one must also sanctify and append time before and after **Festivals? The verse states: "You shall rest"** (Leviticus 23:32), to teach that this rule applies even to Festivals, on which one is commanded to rest. **I have** derived **only** that one adds an extension to **Festivals; from where** do I derive that one must also sanctify and append to ***Shabbatot*? The verse states: "Your Shabbat"** (Leviticus 23:32). **How so? Every place the term: Rest [*shevut*] is stated,** it teaches **from here that one** sanctifies and **appends from** the **non-sacred** weekday **to** the **sacred.**

One answer is *tosefet*: the verse conveys that the fast already begins before nightfall on the ninth — one adds a portion of the ninth day to the tenth. The words *me'erev ad erev*, "from evening to evening," teach that the addition applies at both ends: just as Yom Kippur begins slightly before sunset on the ninth, so it continues slightly beyond nightfall at the conclusion of the tenth. This is the basis of *mosifin me'chol al ha'kodesh* — the principle of adding from the ordinary day to the sacred one.

### Extension

The verse *tishbetu shabbatchem* then extends this principle beyond Yom Kippur. *Ve'ein li ela Yom Ha'Kippurim; shabbatot minayin? Talmud lomar tishbetu* — I know only Yom Kippur; whence do I know that *tosefet* applies to Shabbat? From the word *tishbetu*. *Ve'ein li ela shabbatot; yamim tovim minayin? Talmud lomar shabbatchem* — and whence for *Yom Tov*? From the word *shabbatchem*. Wherever the language of *shevut* (rest) appears, the principle of adding from the weekday to the sacred day applies.

It should be noted that according to the Rambam, the biblical obligation of *tosefet* applies only to Yom Kippur, derived from the verse *be'tisha la'chodesh ba'erev*. The extension to Shabbat and *Yom Tov* via *tishbetu shabbatchem* is, in his view, only *derabbanan*. The earlier *tanna* in the *baraita* who used the word *etzem* to exclude *tosefet* from punishment implicitly assumed there is a recognized period of *tosefet*, and the question raised is what remains for the verse *be'tisha la'chodesh* to teach once the concept of *tosefet* is already established through the *etzem* derivation. The answer is the teaching of Rav Chiyya bar Rav of Difti, which follows.

## Eating Counts

### Ninth Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא דְּ״עֶצֶם״ ״עֶצֶם״, הַאי ״בְּתִשְׁעָה לַחוֹדֶשׁ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנֵי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב מִדִּיפְתִּי. דְּתָנֵי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב מִדִּיפְתִּי: ״וְעִנִּיתֶם אֶת נַפְשׁוֹתֵיכֶם בְּתִשְׁעָה״, וְכִי בְּתִשְׁעָה מִתְעַנִּין? וַהֲלֹא בֶּעָשׂוֹר מִתְעַנִּין! אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לְךָ: כָּל הָאוֹכֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה בִּתְשִׁיעִי, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ הִתְעַנָּה תְּשִׁיעִי וַעֲשִׂירִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the *tanna*** who learns a verbal analogy from the words "that **same** day," "that **same** day," **what does he do with** the phrase: **"On the ninth day of the month"?** The Gemara answers: **He requires it, in accordance with** that **which Ḥiyya bar Rav of Difti taught. As Ḥiyya bar Rav of Difti taught:** It states: **"And you shall afflict your souls on the ninth** day of the month" (Leviticus 23:32). **But does one afflict oneself on the ninth** of Tishrei? **Doesn't one** in fact **afflict oneself on the tenth** of Tishrei? **Rather,** the verse comes **to tell you: Anyone who eats and drinks on the ninth** of Tishrei and then fasts on the tenth, **the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he fasted on** both the **ninth and the tenth.** The verse alludes to this when it states that the fast is on the ninth.

*De'tani Rav Chiyya bar Rav mi'Difti: ve'inisem et nafshoteichem be'tisha* — you shall afflict yourselves on the ninth. The obvious difficulty: *ve'chi be'tisha misa'nin? Ve'halo be'asura misa'nin?* Do we really fast on the ninth? The fast is the tenth! So if *tosefet* is already known, what does "the ninth" add? The answer: *kol ha'ochel ve'shoteh be'tisha, ma'aleh alav ha'katuv ke'illu his'anna tishii va'asiri* — whoever eats and drinks on the ninth, Scripture accounts it as though he fasted both the ninth and the tenth.

### Concept

This teaching opens a significant conceptual question about the nature of Yom Kippur fasting. Earlier in the tractate, the Gemara raised the question of whether *inuy* — affliction — is fundamentally about causing oneself pain and suffering as part of *teshuvah*, a kind of self-chastisement, or whether it is about something else. The Rambam formulates the alternative: fasting on Yom Kippur is about *shevut*, cessation and release from the physical world, freeing oneself from material concerns in order to engage in spiritual matters.

### Enhancement

The teaching of Rav Chiyya bar Rav supports the Rambam's understanding. If fasting were purely about pain and suffering, one would expect that eating heavily on the ninth would undermine the fast by making it easier. The logic would run: the more you eat beforehand, the less it hurts, and the less it hurts, the less effective the *inuy*. But the teaching says the opposite — eating on the ninth is itself credited as a form of fasting. *Ke'illu his'anna* — it is as though one afflicted oneself. The better explanation is that eating appropriately before Yom Kippur enables a person to enter the day without being preoccupied with physical discomfort and hunger, free to focus fully on *teshuvah* and the spiritual work of the day. On this reading, the fast is not about how much one suffers; it is about the state of freedom from the physical world that one achieves. Eating well beforehand, far from undermining that state, actively prepares one for it — and that is why it is itself counted as a form of *inuy*.
