# Menachot 29b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Letter Formation & Tinok

### Mukaf Gvil Intro

The *mishnah* concerning the *parshiyot* of the *mezuzah* establishes that even a single letter can render it invalid — and the Gemara proceeds to clarify what that means in practice. It is taken for granted that a missing letter invalidates the document; what requires elaboration are the more subtle and specific requirements. One such requirement is *mukaf gvil* — the letter must be surrounded by parchment and cannot be connected to neighboring letters.

### Hole in Hei

The Gemara addresses a related case: what if there is a hole in the middle of the *hei*, such that the letter's inner leg is touching the hole rather than the parchment? The Gemara rules this is kosher, because *mukaf gvil* is a concept about the outer perimeter of the letter. The inner area of the letter is not governed by this requirement.

### Broken Letters

A separate question arises when part of a letter is broken — for instance, when the outer or inner leg of the *hei* is not a complete continuous line. The Gemara's standard is that the remaining form must look like an *ot ktana*, the smallest letter, which means something at least the size of a *yud*. There is a dispute among the *rishonim* on this point. The Rosh holds that the requirement of *yud*-size applies specifically to the large right leg of the *hei*, whereas the inner leg need only be a tiny dot. Many other *rishonim*, however, require that even the inner leg be at least the size of a *yud* — or, more precisely, at least half the size of a *yud*, depending on how one measures the relevant font.

### Kotzo Shel Yud

The Gemara does not, in general, provide an exhaustive table of the precise requirements for every letter. The focus is broader: the letter must look like the letter. The *kotzo shel yud* — which may refer to the right leg of the letter or to a small tapered point at its top — represents one of the few specific formal criteria the Gemara enumerates. Beyond such particulars, the standard is one of recognition: does the form presented match what we understand the letter to be?

### Mishnah Berurah Letters

The *Mishnah Berurah* in *Hilkhot Tefillin*, *Siman* 36, does go letter by letter and enumerate specific requirements for each one — what an *alef* must look like, what a *bet* must look like, what a *gimel* must look like, and so on. A trained *sofer* would have even more detailed knowledge beyond what the printed *Mishnah Berurah* conveys. But this level of specificity is largely a post-Talmudic development in practical guidance; the Gemara itself does not provide most of these detailed criteria.

### Tinok Test

Four lines from the top of 29b, the Gemara presents a concrete case. Rami bar Khami — the father-in-law of Rami bar Dikuli — found that the leg of the *vav* in the word *vayeharog* (which appears in one of the four *parshiyot* of *tefillin*) had been broken by a hole. He came before Rabbi Zeira for a ruling. Rabbi Zeira instructed him: go and bring a child — *yanuka d'lo chakhim v'lo tipesh* — neither too clever nor too dull. Not too clever, because you do not want the child to reconstruct the word from context rather than from the shape of the letters; not too dull, because you need a child who genuinely recognizes what letters look like. If the child reads it as *vayeharog*, it is kosher; if not — if the *vav* has been shortened to resemble a *yud* — it is invalid.

This is the *tinok* test, and it applies specifically to questions of whether a letter's form is sufficiently recognizable. The issue here is whether the leg of the *vav* is long enough to distinguish it from a *yud*: does this look like a *vav* or does it look like a *yud*?

### Formal vs. Appearance

It is important to understand the limits of this test. There are certain formal invalidities to which the *tinok*'s reading is entirely irrelevant. If a letter is touching a neighboring letter and thus lacks *mukaf gvil*, no child's reading can remedy that — the formal criterion has simply not been met, regardless of what the letter looks like to an innocent observer. The *tinok* test applies only where the question is one of appearance and recognition: is this form sufficiently close to the standard letter? The distinction between formal invalidities and questions of appearance is a major area of *halakha* in practice. When a question arises in a *shul*, one sometimes brings in a child to read the relevant word — sometimes covering the surrounding letters so the child cannot reconstruct it from context.

### Shulchan Aruch Rules

The *Shulchan Aruch* rules on this matter as follows. If one of the letters — the *Rema* specifies particularly the straight letters, such as a *vav* or a *zayin* — is broken (not necessarily through a hole; it suffices that the ink is not continuous), then if a child of average intelligence reads it correctly, it is kosher; if not, it is invalid. The *Rema* adds that it is not strictly necessary to cover the surrounding letters, though that is common practice.

The *Rema* then adds a significant qualification: *mihu im anu ro'im shelo nikar tzurat ha'ot k'tikuno pasul af al pi she'hatinok kora'o k'hogen* — if we can see that the letter's proper form is not recognizable, it is invalid even if the child manages to read it correctly. This reveals the underlying tension: is the standard one of objective form, or of perceived recognition? The *Rema*'s position suggests that the child's reading is a useful indicator in ambiguous cases, but cannot override our own clear judgment that the letter's form is deficient. Perhaps what the *Rema* means is that if the child identifies it, we may be looking at a correct guess rather than a genuine reading — particularly if no other letter is plausible and the form is closest to the one being identified.

### Defect After Writing

The *Shulchan Aruch* adds one further ruling: *ha de'machshirinan keshenifsak ot davka keshenichtav b'kashrut v'achar kach nifsak* — the lenient ruling regarding a broken letter applies only when the letter was originally written correctly and the defect occurred afterward. If, however, there was a hole in the parchment at the time of writing, the letter was never *mukaf gvil* to begin with — the end of the ink line was always touching a hole rather than parchment — and it is invalid from the outset. Thus one must verify not only the current state of the letter but also its history: was it written correctly and subsequently damaged, or was it defective from the moment of writing? This is a well-established and practically significant area of *halakha*; cases involving multiple *sifrei Torah* have turned on exactly these questions.

## Moshe & Tagin Midrash

### God Tying Tagin

Rav Yehudah says in the name of Rav: *b'shaah she'alah Moshe lamarom* — when Moshe ascended to the heavens to receive the Torah — *matza'o l'ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu she'yoshev v'kosher k'tarim l'otiyot* — he found God sitting and tying crowns to the letters. Moshe said before Him: *Ribbono shel Olam, mi me'akev al yadecha?* — Who is holding back Your hand? The phrase is striking: God's hand is not being held back at all; it is actively engaged in tying the crowns. What Moshe presumably means is: who is preventing You from giving the Torah already? All the letters are written — why are You adding these crowns?

### Shatnez Getz Letters

Before continuing with the narrative, it is worth identifying what these crowns are. Rava (discussed at 27:44 below) teaches that seven letters require three-pointed ornamental crowns: the letters of *shatnez getz* — *shin*, *ayin*, *tet*, *nun*, *zayin*, *gimel*, *tzadi* — whether they appear in their standard or final form. These are small crowns placed at the top of the letters, and there is some discussion in *Tosafot* about their precise arrangement. The three points do not spread out uniformly; rather, the middle point is the tallest, and the two flanking points extend somewhat to the sides, though not as dramatically spread as certain illustrations suggest. This is visible in examples from different scripts of *ksav*, where — taking the *ayin* as a clear example — the ornamentation is centered, with the middle of the three points elevated and the two others close by. These ornaments are called *karin*.

A natural question arises: if the crowns appear on a fixed, predictable set of letters — every *gimel*, every *nun*, every *shin* — how can Rabbi Akiva derive mountains of laws from them? If the placement is entirely systematic, one can predict in advance where every crown will appear. This difficulty points toward the deeper meaning of the *midrash*, which we will develop shortly.

### Moshe's Question

Returning to the narrative: God responds to Moshe's question by saying, *adam echad yesh she'asid lihyot b'sof kama doros, va'Akiva ben Yosef shemo* — there is a person who will live many generations hence, Akiva the son of Yosef is his name — *she'asid lidrosh al kol kotz v'kotz tili tilim shel halakhot* — who will one day interpret from every *kotz* and *kotz*, every jot and tittle, mountains and mountains of laws. This is why the crowns are being added: Rabbi Akiva will need them.

This passage is, of course, one of the most celebrated *aggadot* in the Talmud, appearing also in the tractate *Shabbat*. The difficulty about the systematicity of the crowns remains. If we know in advance which letters will receive the *karin*, the element of arbitrary derivation that might generate new meaning is absent — one cannot claim that a *nun* has its crown because of some particular hidden teaching, when every *nun* always receives one. Perhaps the resolution lies in understanding this as a metaphor: what the *midrash* is expressing is not so much the literal content derivable from any given crown, but the capacity — embodied by Rabbi Akiva — to find meaning in the entire texture of the Torah's transmission, to treat every formal element as potentially significant.

### Classroom & Halacha

God tells Moshe: *chazor l'achorecha* — go behind yourself, turn around. Moshe enters Rabbi Akiva's *beit midrash* and goes to sit at the back of the eight rows. There he cannot follow what is being discussed. The learning that Rabbi Akiva and his students are engaged in does not look familiar to him; he had thought he knew the Torah thoroughly, and yet this teaching is opaque. *Tashash kocho* — his strength failed him; he was distressed and despondent.

Then, when the discussion reaches a particular matter, the students ask Rabbi Akiva: *Rebbi, minayin lach?* — from where do you derive this? Rabbi Akiva replies: *Halacha L'Moshe miSinai* — it is a tradition going back to Moshe at Sinai. At that point, *nityashva da'ato* — Moshe's mind settled; he was reassured.

The image of *chazor l'achorecha* — turn around — carries a resonance that goes beyond the physical gesture. It appears twice in this passage and once more later, and seems to mean more than simply "sit down" or "step back." It evokes the idea that Moshe must take a step back from his sense of centrality: he is no longer *Mr. Big Shot*; he has to go to the back of the class. There is something humbling in recognizing that what ultimately becomes authoritative is in part how the Torah is interpreted after you, not only the original transmission itself.

### Torah She'be'al Peh

The deeper theme of this *midrash* is the nature and power of *Torah she'be'al peh*. If Moshe Rabbeinu himself were to enter a *daf yomi shiur* today and hear what is being discussed, he would not recognize much of it — and that is precisely the point. *Torah she'be'al peh* grows through human interpretation, inference, and development, in ways that go beyond the original intent. Rabbi Akiva's *tili tilim shel halakhot* — mountains of laws — were not necessarily the *pshat* of the text, not necessarily what the *Borei* literally intended, but the nature of the partnership that God established with Israel is that this interpretive creativity is itself legitimate and authoritative.

Rav Moshe Feinstein, in the introduction to his *Igros Moshe*, draws on precisely this *gemara* to articulate the self-understanding of a *posek*. He argues that a decisor who approaches his responsibility with genuine integrity — searching truthfully, consulting all relevant sources, maintaining intellectual honesty — need not be afraid that his *psak* is incorrect, because by definition the *Torah she'be'al peh* so produced becomes God's Torah. What Rabbi Akiva says ultimately becomes God's Torah; that is the nature of the process.

One commentator, the *Mei HaShiloach*, frames the distinction between Moshe and Rabbi Akiva in terms of two orientations in religious life: the positive commitment expressed in *Anochi Hashem Elokecha* — I am the Lord your God — versus the protective, boundary-setting thrust of *lo yihiyeh lecha* — you shall have no other gods. He suggests that Rabbi Akiva's characteristic contribution was the building of *takkanot* and *gezeirot*, the rabbinic safeguards of normative behavior, and that *davar echad* — the particular matter on which the students asked for a source — was itself the unity of God, which Rabbi Akiva traced back to Moshe. The *drash* is interesting, but it perhaps takes us further afield from what the *midrash* is fundamentally about.

The more central reading is this: Moshe, having heard that all of Rabbi Akiva's teaching traces back to *Halacha L'Moshe miSinai*, understands that he is the root and source of everything — even what he cannot follow or recognize. The Torah's authority derives from the original revelation communicated through Moshe; whatever is developed interpretively draws its legitimacy from that source. *Nityashva da'ato* — he settled. Whether he was positively happy or merely made his peace with it, the text is not entirely explicit. But the point is that the entire system of *Torah she'be'al peh* legitimizes itself by tracing back to its origin, even when its content has grown far beyond what the original transmission contained. This, too, is analogous in some ways to constitutional interpretation: the authority of what the Supreme Court finds in the constitution derives from the constitution itself, even when the content discovered there was not literally explicit in the original text. The legitimacy flows from the source; the meaning is developed by the interpreters.

The question of why this capacity was given to Rabbi Akiva rather than to Moshe is addressed partly by the observation that when Moshe lived, there was not yet the need for a vast *Torah she'be'al peh*. As time progressed and circumstances multiplied, the oral tradition had to grow. It is something like a parent who knew all the presidents by heart when the child could not recite them — "but when you were young, there were only a few." When Moshe stood at Sinai, you did not yet need *tili tilim shel halakhot*.

### Akiva Martyrdom

Moshe returns to God. He says: *Ribbono shel Olam, yesh lecha adam kaze v'ata noten Torah al yadai?* — You have a person like this, and yet You are giving the Torah through me? God's response, *shtok — kach ala b'machshava lefanai*, is: be silent; this is what arose in My intention; this was My plan.

Moshe then asks: *Ribbono shel Olam, he'reitni Torato, hareini secharo* — You have shown me his Torah; now show me his reward. Again: *chazor l'achorecha*. Moshe looks behind him. He sees the Romans weighing out Rabbi Akiva's flesh in the marketplace — his body had been martyred and his meat was being sold by the pound. Moshe cries out: *Ribbono shel Olam, zu Torah v'zu sechara?* — This is the Torah, and this is its reward? God responds again: *shtok — kach ala b'machshava lefanai*. Be silent. This arose in My intention.

### Theodicy & Justice

This is a striking *gemara* — and not only as a *midrash* about *Torah she'be'al peh*. It is among the most powerful passages in Rabbinic literature about *theodicy* and about the limits of the categories of *sachar v'onesh*, reward and punishment. So much of the Rabbinic enterprise involves explaining the economy of divine justice: the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed because of this sin; this calamity befell us because of that transgression. The entire intellectual architecture of *din v'cheshbon* assumes that history is legible, that suffering can be accounted for in some systematic way.

And yet, at certain moments — moments that carry particular weight in a post-Holocaust world — the Rabbis acknowledged that the categories break. The most honest and theologically serious thing one can sometimes say is: *we do not understand*. Rather than construct a framework of *sachar v'onesh* that does violence to the reality of what happened, the *gemara* simply says *shtok*. Be silent.

One participant in the learning observed that *chazor l'achorecha* here echoes the passage where Moshe asked to see God's face and was told *u'fanai lo yeira'u* — My face cannot be seen. You may see only My back; you can grasp only a glimpse of how God operates in the world. You cannot see the full picture. Similarly here: God says, turn around, look behind you — you get a glimpse of human history, but not full comprehension of how providence operates within it.

There is also a structural observation worth making about the *midrash* as a whole. It begins with an insistence on meaning at the level of the smallest detail: every crown on every letter is there because of the mountains of *halakhot* that Rabbi Akiva will derive from it — everything is significant, everything is interpretable. And then it pivots to an encounter with radical unintelligibility: the greatest Torah scholar of the generation, someone for whom every jot and tittle carried meaning, meets a death that makes no sense at all within human categories of justice. The tension between total meaning-making and total incomprehensibility is written into the structure of the *midrash* itself. Perhaps that is one of its deepest points: we are given the license, even the obligation, to interpret the Torah — to impose human meaning and order through *Torah she'be'al peh* — precisely because we cannot do the same for the world.

## Letter Symbolism

### Rava on Tagin

Returning to *halakha*: Rava teaches that seven letters require three *ziyunin* — three ornamental points — and they are the letters of *shatnez getz*: *shin*, *ayin*, *tet*, *nun*, *zayin*, *gimel*, *tzadi*. This applies whether the letter appears in its standard form or in its final form.

### Hump in Het

Rav Ashi says: *hazina lehu le-safrei dukani d'vei Rav* — I have seen the precise *sefarim* in the house of Rav. These were not ordinary scrolls but especially careful, exact ones. In these precise scrolls, *dekhattu lehu le-gagei d'het* — they made a bump at the top of the *het*. The word used is *chutra*, meaning a protrusion or hump at the crown of the letter.

### Rashi vs Tosafot

Rashi and *Tosafot* differ on what this *chutra* looks like. Rashi understands it as analogous to the *kotzo shel yud* — a small pointed element rising from the top left of the *het*, comparable to the little tapered point we have discussed in other letters. *Tosafot*, drawing on another *gemara*, understands it differently: *chutra* means a camel hump. According to *Tosafot*, the *het* is supposed to look like two *zayins* joined at the top with a camel hump connecting them. If you were to remove the hump, you would have two *zayins* standing separately. In practice, the samples of *ksav* that have been preserved follow *Tosafot*'s understanding, though in actual writing the hump is rendered somewhat subtly — it is present but not as dramatically exaggerated as *Tosafot*'s illustration suggests. The *het* in those examples clearly reflects the structure of two *zayins* united by the hump.

The word *dukani* — precise — is key here. Not all *sefarim* were written with the same degree of care; what Rav Ashi observed were the more precise ones, in which even these fine features were attended to.

### Suspended Hei Leg

Rav Ashi further notes: *de-tali le-karei de-hei* — and they suspended the leg of the *hei*. The inner left leg of the *hei* must not touch the roof of the letter; if it does, the *hei* becomes a *het*. The leg must hang in mid-air, suspended and not connected to the top. This matters halakhically: a *hei* whose inner leg connects to the top is a different letter entirely.

The symbolic meaning is then supplied: *ke-lomar Chai hu b'rumo shel olam* — to indicate that the Living One dwells in the heights of the world. The *het* — a letter whose name evokes *chayim*, life — with its bump pointing upward toward the heavens is thus a symbolic gesture toward the divine presence above. 

### Open Door Metaphor

As for the suspended leg of the *hei* — why is it suspended? This question was posed by Rav Yehudah Nesi'ah to Rav Ami in the context of interpreting the verse *bitchu b'Hashem adei ad, ki be-Yah Hashem tzur olamim* — trust in God forever and ever, for with *Yah* God is the rock of the ages. The question was: what is the significance of *bi-Yah* — with the *yud* and the *hei* of God's name? Rav Ami first misunderstood the question as asking about the word *olamim* — worlds in the plural — and answered that one should place one's trust in God as a refuge in both this world and the world to come. But the questioner clarified: the real question is why the verse says *be-Yah* — with the *beit* prefix, indicating *by means of* — rather than simply *Yah*.

The answer, as expounded by Rav Yehudah the son of Rabbi Ilai, is that *be-Yah* does not refer to God's name at all; it refers to the two letters *yud* and *hei* as the instruments of creation. God fashioned the worlds by means of the letters *yud* and *hei*. Specifically: one world was created with the *hei*, and one world was created with the *yud*. The evidence that this world was created with the *hei* comes from the verse *eileh toldot ha-shamayim v'ha-aretz be-hibaram* — and the reading *al tikrei be-hibaram, ela be-hei bara'am*: do not read "when they were created" but rather "He created them with the letter *hei*." Thus, *olam ha-zeh* was created with the *hei*, and *olam ha-ba* — the world to come, understood in the traditional sense of the future messianic age — was created with the *yud*.

Now why was this world created with the *hei*? *Mipnei she-domeh le-achsadrah* — because the *hei* resembles a vestibule, a portico. The *hei* has, in effect, three walls and a large open exit at the bottom. This open door is for anyone who wants to leave — *kol ha-rotzeh la-tzet yetzei* — any person who wishes to squander this world and effectively walk straight into *Gehenom* is welcome to go. The door is wide open. God will not forcibly stop anyone from wasting what this world offers.

### Open Door Metaphor

Yet the *gemara* asks: *u-mai ta'ama talya kareia?* — and why then is the inner leg of the *hei* suspended, creating that small opening at the top? *De-hadar bi-tshuvah* — for the one who returns in *teshuvah*. If a person has left through the great open door and wishes to return — if they genuinely wish to make a serious recommitment and re-enter this world's possibilities — *me-aylei leih* — they can slip back in through that narrow back door at the top. The wide door is an exit; the small suspended gap is a back entrance for *teshuvah*.

The *gemara* then asks: why not simply re-enter through the same large door one exited? *Saya de-milta* — things will not work out well that way. This is given force by Resh Lakish's teaching: *im la-leitzim hu yalitz, va-anivim yiten chen* — for those who mock, God will mock; for those who are humble, God will give grace. *Ba litaher, mesayyin oto* — if one comes to purify oneself, Heaven helps. *Ba litame, potchin lo* — if one comes to do wrong, the door is opened. God does not compel evil but does not block it either.

Rashi applies this to the question of why one cannot re-enter through the same large door: because genuine *teshuvah* requires divine assistance — *mesayyin oto* — and that assistance comes when one approaches through the harder, narrower path. There is also a psychological dimension here. If one genuinely wants to transform one's life, the answer is not merely to return to the exact same scenarios and surroundings one left, relying on willpower alone to make everything different. Real renewal requires approaching things from a new angle, and that requires God's help — *mesayyin oto*.

### Crown on Hei

*U-mai ta'ama it lei taga?* — and why does the *hei* have a small crown, a *taga*, at its top? Note that this is not the three-pointed *shatnez getz* crown; it is a smaller ornamental point, comparable to the *kotz* of the *yud* or the *kotz* of the *het*. God says: *im chozer bo, ani kosher lo kesher* — if this person returns in *teshuvah* and is trying to climb back through that upper door, I will tie him a rope. The image is vivid: the small opening high up at the back of the *hei* requires scaling the wall to get in, and God reaches out and throws a rope to help the penitent climb up. An alternative reading in the textual tradition has *ani kosher lo keter* — I will tie him a crown — bringing us full circle to the imagery of God tying crowns to letters at the beginning of the *midrash*, now recast as the reward granted to the one who returns.

### Yud Symbolism

The *gemara* then addresses the *yud* and *olam ha-ba*. *Mipnei mah nivra olam ha-ba be-yud?* — why was the world to come created with the *yud*? *Mipnei she-ha-tzaddikim she-bo mu'atim* — because the righteous who will inhabit it are few. The *yud* is the smallest letter, and it represents the small number of people who will attain the world to come. The world is capacious; the world to come is narrow and small.

*U-mipnei mah kaffuf rosho?* — and why is the head of the *yud* bent? If one imagines the *yud* as a figure, the entire form tilts and bends downward. *Mipnei she-ha-tzaddikim she-bo kaffuf rosheihem* — because the righteous there have their heads bent. This is not merely humility in the general sense, but something more pointed. Even among the *tzaddikim* who reach *olam ha-ba*, there are differences in achievement. Some are greater than others, and some will feel ashamed in the presence of those who accomplished more — *mitbayesh me-chaveiro*. One who was accounted among the few who merited *olam ha-ba* might still look at a fellow *tzaddik* of greater stature and feel embarrassed by the comparison. The *yud*'s bent posture captures this: not arrogance at having arrived, but a kind of humble self-awareness, even among those who made it.

## Sefer Torah Errors

### Rav on Errors

Rav Yosef notes that Rav made two teachings regarding *sifrei Torah* that appear to be contradicted by a *braita*. The first teaching: *sefer Torah sheyesh bo shtei ta'uyot bechol daf vadaf yitaken* — a *sefer Torah* that has two errors in every column may be corrected. A clarification of terminology is useful here: *daf* in this context does not mean a full page (that would be a *yeriyah*) but a column — what we would normally call an *amud*. Indeed, the word *amud* properly means a column, like the column of a building, which is why in the traditional printing of the *Yerushalmi*, references are given as column one, two, three, or four — two columns on each side of the page.

So: two errors per column — correctable. But if there are three errors in every column, the *sefer Torah* should be put in *sheimos* — it is not fitting for such a heavily error-laden scroll to continue being used for ritual purposes.

### Braita Contradiction

The *braita* presents a different standard. *Shalosh yitaken* — three errors per column may still be corrected. *Arba yiganez* — four errors per column requires *geniza*. This directly contradicts Rav's ruling, which drew the line at three. The Gemara treats this as a genuine contradiction requiring resolution.

### Perfect Column

The *braita* adds a further principle: *im yesh bo daf achat shleimah, matelet al kulah* — if there is one perfect column in the *sefer Torah*, that column can carry great weight for the whole scroll. Even a scroll that would otherwise be borderline can be corrected if one complete column is entirely free of errors. The imperfection of the rest is rendered more tolerable when at least one column is pristine.

One of the participants in the learning offered a lovely homiletical reading: even a person who has much in need of repair, if there is one genuinely beautiful *daf* within them — one domain of their life that is truly *shleimah* — that can be what saves everything else. *Dayenu.*

### Imperfect Column

The Gemara qualifies this further. Rav Zvid bar Shmuel said in the name of Rav: *vehu dechtiv rubya desaphra shapir* — this leniency applies only when most of the *sefer* is written correctly. That is, it is not sufficient merely to have one perfect column; the majority of the scroll must also be free of error. Only then, when the majority is fine and there is additionally one fully perfect column, do the principles of correction and saving apply. This hemming-in from both directions — requiring *rov* of the *sefer* to be correct, and requiring at least one perfect column — reflects a careful layering of standards.

### Imperfect Column

The Gemara then poses the question of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta: *ee hach daf shalosh ta'uyot mai?* — what if that one saving column itself has three errors? Three errors per column is within the correctable range — it does not rise to the level of requiring *geniza* — but it is not perfect. Does a column with three errors still function as the saving column for the rest of the *sefer*?

The answer given is: *hoil ve-ishtarei letikuni, matkanei* — since it falls within the permissible range for correction, since three errors is correctable rather than grounds for *geniza*, it is considered sufficient to save the rest of the *sefer Torah*. We are thus operating with a dual standard: on one side we require the majority of the *sefer* to be correct; on the other, we allow the one saving column to have up to three errors, as long as it remains within the range of what can be corrected.

### Yeterot vs Chaserot

The Gemara then specifies: *ve-hanei mili be-chaserot* — all of this discussion about the difficulty of errors, and the conditions under which a *sefer Torah* must be sent to *geniza*, applies specifically to missing letters, *chaserot*. If a letter is missing, the word must be scraped away entirely and rewritten in a smaller size to fit the space — a major intervention that leaves visible evidence of correction.

But *aval be-yeterot* — if the problem is extra letters — *leit lan bah*, it is not treated as severely. This does not mean, as *Tosafot* is careful to point out, that extra letters do not need to be corrected — they do. The point is that scraping away an extra letter is a far less radical operation than scraping away an entire word and rewriting it. The result is far less visually disruptive, and therefore the threshold for when such a *sefer Torah* must be set aside is much higher.

The practical reality of modern *sofer* work is striking here. When a trained *sofer* makes a correction — scraping away ink and rewriting — the result can be entirely invisible. The parchment and ink are prepared in such a way that, once corrected, there is no visible trace of what had been written before. But the Gemara was concerned that there would be visible marks, and recognized that the marks left by scraping away an entire word and rewriting it in reduced letters are far more significant than those left by removing a single extraneous letter.

### Leopard Spots

If a *sefer Torah* is filled with missing letters throughout, and one is required to scrape away word after word across multiple columns and rewrite them, the result looks like a spotted leopard — *kemenumar* — as Rav Chananel says in the name of Rav. The numerous patches of correction distributed across the scroll make it unfit for dignified use, and therefore the entire scroll should be sent to *geniza* rather than subjected to that level of repair.

A related question was raised: do these same standards of letter formation — the *kotzo shel yud*, the *tinok* test, *mukaf gvil* — apply equally to *tefillin* and *mezuzot*, or only to *sifrei Torah*? A ruling of *Tosafot* on 29a is instructive: *Tosafot* records that a case arose involving a *get* in which the *dalet* almost looked like a *yud*. The case was brought before Rabbeinu Eliyahu, who brought in a child of average intelligence and had him read the relevant word — not just identify the letter, but read the whole word in context. The child read it correctly, and Rabbeinu Eliyahu declared the *get* valid on the basis of this very *gemara*. This demonstrates that the *tinok* test and related criteria of letter recognition apply not only to *sifrei Torah* but across the domain of *STeM* generally.

## Sefer Torah Ending

### Rav Middle Column

The second teaching of Rav that Rav Yosef identified as contradicted by a *braita*: *ha-koseiv sefer Torah ba ligmor, gomer va-afilu be-emtza ha-daf* — when writing a *sefer Torah* and reaching the final *pasuk*, one may end even in the middle of a column. A *sofer* writing a *sefer Torah* must plan far in advance, because *leinei kol Yisrael* — the final words of the Torah — must land at the bottom of a column, and sections like *Shirat ha-Yam* and *Ha'azinu* have their own requirements about where they begin within a column. If the *sofer* did not plan ahead and arrives at *leinei kol Yisrael* at the top of a column, Rav says it is permissible to end there, in the middle of the column.

### Braita Objection

A *braita* from *Masekhet Sofrim* contradicts this: *ha-koseiv sefer Torah ba ligmor, lo yigmor be-emtza ha-daf, kederech she-gomar be-Chumashin* — when ending a *sefer Torah*, do not end in the middle of a column, as one might do in a *chumash*. By *chumash* here the *braita* means a codex — a bound handwritten volume, as opposed to a scroll. Before the printing press, a *chumash* was a parchment manuscript bound as a book rather than assembled as a scroll. It was not used for ritual *kriah* in *shul*, though the *Rambam* held that in the absence of a *sefer Torah*, one might read from such a handwritten *chumash* on *klaf* at least. Because such a *chumash* could end wherever the text ended without regard to column structure, the *braita* is warning: do not treat a *sefer Torah* the same way. You must end *ela mekatzer v'holech ad sof ha-daf* — tapering the lines gradually shorter and shorter until you reach the bottom of the column. This, of course, requires planning from the outset; if the *sofer* is only a few lines from the top of a new column when the text runs out, no amount of tapering will get the last word to the bottom.

### Middle Line Only

The Gemara resolves the contradiction between Rav and the *braita* by reinterpreting Rav's statement. When Rav said one may end *be-emtza ha-daf* — in the middle of the column — he did not mean the middle of the column at all. He meant *be-emtza ha-shita* — in the middle of the line. The rule is that one must end at the bottom of the column, but one need not end at the end of the final line; *leinei kol Yisrael* can conclude partway across the last line, leaving the remainder of that line blank, as long as the line itself is the bottom line of the column. One is familiar with this from contracts and legal documents, where the final line often has the text centered or pulled to one side, not stretching the full width — the point being that the column ends, but the last line need not be fully filled.

### Rabbanan vs Rav Ashi

The Gemara records a further debate. The *rabbanan* say: even ending in the middle of a line is permitted — which seems to imply that ending in the middle of a column is also acceptable. Rav Ashi, however, says: *be-emtza shita davka* — only in the middle of a line is permitted, but one is absolutely required to reach the bottom of the column. This is a matter of aesthetics and *kavod*: the line need not be justified to its full width, but the column must reach its bottom edge.

### Final Halacha

The Gemara concludes: *v'hilkheta, be-emtza shita davka* — the *halakha* follows Rav Ashi. *Leinei kol Yisrael* may end in the middle of a line, but the *sefer Torah* must end at the bottom of a column. The *sofer* must plan accordingly from well in advance. What happens if the *sofer* must write unusually large letters in order to stretch the text down to the bottom of the column — that question remains for the continuation of the *daf*.
