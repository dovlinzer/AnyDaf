# Pesachim 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Derech Hana'ah

### Practical Parameters

The daf opens by turning from the theoretical question of *how* we derive that certain objects are forbidden in benefit to the practical parameters governing that prohibition. The preceding discussion had established two distinct conceptual models for the source of *issur hana'ah*: the model rooted in *achilah* (eating), associated with Chizkiyahu's and Rabbi Abbahu's derivations from *lo yochal*, and the model rooted in *kodesh* — something completely taboo and off-limits, akin to consecrated property. As we will see, this distinction between the *achilah* world and the *kodesh* world turns out to have significant practical consequences.

### Rabbi Abbahu

Rabbi Abbahu, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, had already made two interconnected statements. First, for things one is forbidden to eat, one is liable only if one consumes them in the normal way of eating — eating raw forbidden fat, for example, would not constitute a transgression, since eating in a normal fashion involves a certain kind of pleasurable engagement with the act. Second — and this is a considerably more radical claim — even with respect to things that are forbidden in *hana'ah* (benefit), one must derive that benefit in a normal way. If one were to apply the fat of a *shor haniskal* (an ox condemned to be stoned) to a wound in order to heal it, one would not transgress, even though healing a wound is a recognizable and legitimate benefit. The reason is that just as eating is defined by a process with specific parameters — including the requirement that it be a normal, pleasurable act — so too deriving benefit, particularly insofar as it is conceptually linked to eating, must follow a normal process. The end result of having received benefit is insufficient; the manner in which the benefit was obtained must itself be a recognized form of *hana'ah*.

### Orlah Proof

Rav Zeira proposes a proof for this principle from a teaching about *orlah*. The rule is that one receives lashes for *orlah* only for juice that comes from olives and grapes — not for juice pressed from strawberries, figs, or pomegranates. The inference Rav Zeira draws is that one would not transgress by drinking pomegranate juice from *orlah* fruit, because drinking the juice of a pomegranate is not the normal way in which people derive pleasure from that fruit. In the period of the Gemara, only olive oil and wine were recognized as authentic fruit juices; all other fruit liquids were not considered a normal form of enjoying the fruit. This, Rav Zeira argues, demonstrates that at least by eating, one must act in the normal, pleasure-deriving manner.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges whether this constitutes a valid proof. He notes that if the case had involved the fruit itself — if someone had eaten the pomegranate in some abnormal fashion and thereby failed to derive normal pleasure from the act — that would indeed prove that the act of eating requires a normal mode of benefit. But the case of pomegranate juice is different. The reason one does not transgress is not that the process of deriving benefit was abnormal. The reason is a problem with the *object* itself.

### Zei'ah Be'alma

The term Abaye uses is *zei'ah be'alma* — it is merely like sweat, a runoff of the fruit that does not carry the legal status of the fruit itself. Halacha does not recognize fruit juices other than olive oil and wine as a genuine expression of the fruit. The Gemara elsewhere illustrates this: when one squeezes an olive before the true oil has separated, what runs off first is a watery liquid called *mohal*, which is not yet considered olive oil and does not legally represent the fruit. Similarly, on *Shabbos*, the melachah of squeezing fruit and extracting its juice is a biblical transgression only for olives and grapes; squeezing an orange or lemon is not biblically prohibited, because the extracted liquid is not considered a substance of significance — it is, in halachic terms, nothing.

The question, then, is not whether the person derived benefit in a normal way, but whether what they extracted is even legally considered "the fruit" at all. If the object is not the forbidden thing, there is no transgression, regardless of how one benefited from it.

### Taam Ke'ikar

Tosafot sharpens this difficulty considerably by raising the principle of *taam ke'ikar* — that the taste of a forbidden substance is treated as equivalent to the substance itself. If one cooks *chazir* with a potato, the potato absorbs the taste of the *chazir* and becomes forbidden. Why, then, should the liquid pressed from an *orlah* orange not be forbidden as the *taam* of the *orlah* fruit, even if it lacks the full legal status of the fruit? Tosafot acknowledges this is a genuine difficulty without a fully satisfying resolution. One possibility is that *taam ke'ikar* creates a secondary, derivative prohibition rather than the primary prohibition of the fruit itself — one may not transgress the specific *issur* of *orlah*, but one might still violate a secondary *taam* prohibition. The practical stakes of this question are real: one of the disputes among the *acharonim* regarding grain alcohol on *Pesach* turns precisely on this axis. Some hold that since it is derived from grain and water and constitutes a form of *chametz*, it is fully forbidden. Others argue, based on reasoning parallel to this Gemara, that it is *zei'ah be'alma* — it lacks the legal status of *chametz* since it is not olive oil or grape juice — and is therefore permissible on *Pesach*. The tension between *zei'ah be'alma* and *taam ke'ikar* is thus a live and unresolved issue with significant halachic consequences.

## Kilayim & Basar

### Kilayim Exception

The Gemara now records a statement of Abaye: everyone agrees that with respect to *kilayim* (diverse kinds planted together in a vineyard), one receives lashes even if one derived benefit in an abnormal way — *afilu shelo k'derech hana'atan*.

### Kodesh Model

The reason Abaye gives is precisely the conceptual distinction established earlier: *mishum d'lo ktiv behu achilah* — the Torah does not prohibit *kilayim* through the language of eating. What the Torah says is *pen tikdash* — lest it become sanctified. This language invokes the *kodesh* model, the world of something completely taboo and off-limits, rather than the *achilah* model of a normal act of eating extended to benefit. When a prohibition is rooted in *achilah*, it inherits the process-parameters of eating: the benefit must be derived in a normal fashion. When a prohibition is rooted in the *hekdesh* framework — something categorically forbidden and set apart — there are no such process parameters. Any benefit, however derived, violates the taboo. A straightforward illustration of the *achilah* framework's process-dependence is the case of an intravenous drip on Yom Kippur: one does not transgress the prohibition of eating, because eating is defined by a specific process, and an IV does not constitute that process.

### Basar Proof

The Gemara now tests Abaye's assertion by examining a *baraita* that attempts to derive the prohibitions of *basar bechalav* through a *kal vachomer*. The *baraita* opens with the view of Issi ben Yehuda: how do we know that *basar bechalav* is forbidden to *eat*, given that the Torah only explicitly prohibits *cooking* meat and milk together? The *baraita* establishes this through a *gezeirah shavah*: the prohibition of *basar bechalav* is introduced with the phrase *ki am kadosh atah l'Hashem Elokecha* — "for you are a holy nation to the Lord your God" — and another verse uses similar language introducing the prohibition of *treifah*: *v'anshei kodesh tihyun li uvasar basadeh treifah lo tocheilu* — "you shall be holy people to Me, and flesh torn in the field you shall not eat." The parallel implies that just as the *kodesh* formulation by *treifah* means you must abstain from eating it, so too by *basar bechalav*. Having established that *basar bechalav* is forbidden to eat, the *baraita* then asks: from where do we know that *hana'ah* (benefit) is also forbidden? A *kal vachomer* is proposed: *mah orlah shelo ne'evdah bah aveirah asurah bahana'ah* — *orlah*, for which no transgression was committed in its production, is nonetheless forbidden in benefit. *Basar bechalav*, for which a transgression was actually committed (the act of cooking), should certainly be forbidden in benefit.

### Orlah vs Chametz

The Gemara challenges the *kal vachomer* on two fronts. First, *orlah* may be more severe in one respect: *sha'at hachosher* — *orlah* never had a permissible state. From the moment the fruit grew it was forbidden. *Basar bechalav*, by contrast, begins with permitted meat and permitted milk; perhaps that prior permitted status mitigates the severity of the resulting prohibition, and therefore one cannot derive a *kal vachomer* from *orlah* to *basar bechalav*. The rejoinder is to invoke *chametz b'Pesach* as a comparison point: here is something that begins as permitted, becomes forbidden, and is nonetheless forbidden in benefit. But *chametz b'Pesach* is itself vulnerable to a counterargument: it carries the penalty of *kares*, making it uniquely severe, and thus cannot serve as a *kal vachomer* basis for *basar bechalav*, which carries no such penalty. The Gemara then turns to *kilayim* from the vineyard (*kilayei hakerem*, the only variety that is *assur bahana'ah*) as a possible model: it carries no *kares*, and one has committed a transgression in its production. This appears to be a valid comparison to *basar bechalav*, allowing the inference that *basar bechalav* is likewise forbidden in benefit.

### Abaye Responds

But this is precisely where Abaye's assertion about *kilayim* becomes problematic for the *kal vachomer*. If *kilayim* is *assur bahana'ah* even *shelo k'derech hana'ah* — even in an abnormal mode of benefit — then *kilayim* is more severe than an ordinary *issur hana'ah*. The challenge would then be: how can we learn a *kal vachomer* to *basar bechalav* from *kilayim*, if *kilayim* is uniquely stringent in this regard and *basar bechalav* would presumably be limited to normal modes of benefit? The response attributed to Abaye is striking: *atu b'vasar bechalav achilah kesivah bei?* Is the word *achilah* written in connection with *basar bechalav* itself? It is not. The Torah says nothing about eating *basar bechalav*; it speaks only of cooking it. We infer the prohibition of eating from other sources, but the Torah does not use the act-language of *achilah* directly with *basar bechalav*. Therefore, Abaye argues, the same logic that applies to *kilayim* applies here: because the Torah does not ground the prohibition in the language of eating, *basar bechalav* also is transgressed even when one derives benefit in an abnormal way. This is in fact the *halacha*: both *kilayim* and *basar bechalav* are transgressed *shelo k'derech hana'ah*, provided the object still retains its status as food. At some point, if the food becomes so altered or contaminated that it is no longer recognizable as food, one would not transgress — but as long as it remains food, the prohibition applies regardless of the mode of benefit.

### Kodesh Framework

The opposing view — that *basar bechalav* should be limited to normal benefit — had been grounded in the argument that we learn the prohibition of *basar bechalav* from *neveila* or *treifah*, and those prohibitions do use the language of *achilah*. What is prohibited vis-à-vis *treifah* is an act of eating, so by extension what is prohibited vis-à-vis *basar bechalav* should also be defined as an act — normal *derech hana'ah*. Abaye's rejoinder is that even though we may derive the *issur* of *basar bechalav* from *treifah*, the Torah itself never uses the language of *achilah* directly in connection with *basar bechalav*. The Torah formulates *basar bechalav* as something generically off-limits, not as a prohibited *act* of eating. What one learns from *treifah* is the category of the prohibition, but the specifics — whether it must be a formal act with process-parameters — one does not derive from *treifah*. Because *basar bechalav* is formulated as categorically forbidden rather than as a prohibited act, the prohibition does not require a normal mode of benefit. This is the same principle at work in *kilayim*, and the conceptual parallel between the two is thus complete. A practical illustration of this principle is discussed in the *Shulchan Aruch*, where the *Rema* addresses the use of ashes from a fire on which meat and dairy pots had been used, for purposes such as washing one's hair — a case where the question of *shelo k'derech hana'ah* of *basar bechalav* arises as a genuine halachic concern.

## Object Transformation

### Flower Pot Case

With the foregoing analysis of *kilayim* and *basar bechalav* established, the Gemara turns to a new challenge bearing on the scope of *kilayim*. Rav Shemaya raises a difficulty from a *mishna*: the case of *hamavir atzitz nakuv b'cherem* — one who passes a flower pot with a hole through a vineyard. A flower pot with a hole is treated halachically as if the plant within it is planted in the ground, since the only separation between the soil in the pot and the ground below is air, and the hole establishes a kind of legal continuity. If one places such a flower pot containing, say, a growing stalk of grain, on the floor of a vineyard, one has effectively brought grain into contact with grapevines — the classic configuration of *kilayei hakerem*.

### Bitul 1:200

The *mishna* rules: *im hosif matayim, assur* — if the grain stalk grew by one two-hundredth of its prior volume, it is forbidden. The underlying principle is that *kilayim* is subject to *bitul* (nullification) at a ratio of one in two hundred — more stringent than ordinary *bitul* at one in sixty and even more stringent than *terumah* at one in one hundred, reflecting the severity of *kilayim* as a prohibition that is *assur bahana'ah*. The practical application: suppose the grain in the flower pot had a volume of 199 units before placement in the vineyard, and upon removal it had grown to 200 units. The one unit of new growth is *kilayim*. One unit of *kilayim* against 199 units of *heter* is a ratio of one to 199 — insufficient to achieve *bitul*, which requires at least one to 200. Therefore the mixture is forbidden. If less than one unit had grown, *bitul* would be achieved and there would be no prohibition.

### Two Kilayim Forms

Crucially, the structure of this *mishna* implies that only the new growth is *kilayim* — not the original stalk of grain that was already growing before the flower pot was placed in the vineyard. The original stalk is treated as pure *heter*, and only what grew while situated in the vineyard carries the status of *kilayim*. This appears to directly contradict the earlier discussion, in which it was suggested that when seeds are crossed and planted together, even the original seed becomes forbidden. The *mishna* here seems to say the opposite: the original material retains its permissible status, and only the new growth is forbidden.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between two forms of *kilayim*, supported by a close reading of the relevant verse, which contains two distinct expressions: *hazera* (the seed) and *hamlei'ah* (the fullness, i.e., the growth). The verse thus encompasses both the original seed and the new growth, but applies each to a different case. *Zaru'a mei'ikaro* — something that was planted together from the outset, where both seeds were placed in the ground at the same time — in that case, *behashrasha*, as soon as the seeds take root together, the original seed itself becomes forbidden. *Zaru'a uva* — something that was already planted and was subsequently brought near the other plant, as in the flower pot case, where the grain stalk was already growing and was merely moved into the vineyard — in that case, *hosif in, lo hosif lo*: only what grows subsequently becomes forbidden; the original material does not take on *kilayim* status. The logic reflects the act emphasized by the Torah: *lo tizra karmecha kilayim* — "do not plant your vineyard with diverse kinds." When one plants two kinds together, the very act of planting creates the forbidden entity, and the original seeds are transformed by that act. When, however, one plant already exists and is simply brought into proximity with the other, no single act of joint planting occurred, and therefore the original material is not implicated — only the subsequent growth, which actually grew in the condition of *kilayim*, acquires that status. An interesting edge case left open by the Gemara is what happens when one seed is already growing and one plants a new seed next to it — neither the pure case of joint planting nor the pure case of the pre-existing flower pot. The Gemara does not resolve this.

## Healing & Martyrdom

### Healing Exception

Rabbi Yaakov, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, states: *b'khol mitrappin chutz me-atzei Asherah* — one may heal oneself using all types of things, even those that are otherwise forbidden in benefit, with the sole exception of wood from an *asherah*, a tree that has been worshipped as *avodah zarah*. The Gemara immediately asks: what is the case? If there is a genuine risk to life, then even *asherah* wood should be permissible — one may transgress any prohibition to save one's life. If there is no risk to life, then even ordinary forbidden objects should not be permissible for use in healing, since their benefit remains biblically prohibited. The Gemara resolves: we are speaking of a case where there is genuine *sakanah* (risk to life), and even so, *asherah* wood may not be used. All other prohibitions yield to *pikuach nefesh*; this one does not.

### Love Midrash

The basis for this exception is a *baraita* of Rabbi Eliezer interpreting the verse commanding love of God *b'khol nafshekha* (with all your soul) and *b'khol me'odekha* (with all your might). Rabbi Eliezer asks: if the Torah says love God with all your soul, why does it add all your might? And if it says all your might, why add all your soul? The answer: for a person whose body is more precious to him than his property, the Torah says *b'khol nafshekha* — you must be prepared to sacrifice even your life for God. For a person whose money is more precious to him than his body, the Torah says *b'khol me'odekha* — you must be prepared to sacrifice even your wealth. From this we derive the principle that one is obligated to give up one's life rather than violate certain categories of prohibition — the principle of *yehareg v'al ya'avor*.

### Three Sins

When Ravin transmitted the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan, he formulated it as follows: one may heal oneself with all things except *avodah zarah*, *gilluy arayot*, and *shfikhut damim* — idolatry, forbidden sexual relations, and murder. These are the three categories for which one must give up one's life rather than transgress. The principle is bidirectional: just as one cannot violate these three even when coerced at the cost of one's life, one cannot violate them even when doing so would cure an illness or save one from disease. Saving one's own life does not override these three prohibitions.

### Avizrayhu Debate

The case of *asherah* wood raises a pointed question: using *asherah* wood is a transgression of the prohibition against deriving benefit from *avodah zarah*, but it is not itself an act of *avodah zarah*. Should it be treated as equivalent to actual idol worship for purposes of *yehareg v'al ya'avor*? Tosafot argues that in principle the prohibition on deriving benefit from *asherah* wood would not in itself require martyrdom. What the Gemara prohibits, says Tosafot, is specifically the case where the healing power attributed to the *asherah* tree is bound up with belief in its sacred or supernatural properties — where the tree is worshipped precisely because of its reputed powers, and one is seeking to harness those same powers. In that situation, using the wood for healing is a gateway to genuine belief in the *avodah zarah*, and therefore one must give up one's life rather than do it. If, however, the *asherah* tree merely happens to provide useful bark or fruit, and the benefit sought is entirely incidental to its status as a worshipped object, Tosafot would permit it even under *sakanah*, despite the technical *issur hana'ah*. The Ran takes a fundamentally different position. He holds that any prohibition that belongs to the constellation of *avodah zarah* — even if it is not itself an act of worship and does not lead to worship — carries the status of *yehareg v'al ya'avor*. This is the principle of *avizrayhu d'avodah zarah*: the appurtenances of idolatry share in the severity of idolatry itself. The Ran's position extends this framework to the other two capital prohibitions as well: *avizrayhu d'gilluy arayot* — prohibitions within the constellation of forbidden sexual relations — are likewise *yehareg v'al ya'avor*. This principle, it should be noted, is the basis on which some authorities have argued that certain sexual prohibitions short of outright *gilluy arayot* also carry this status, though Tosafot explicitly disagrees with the broader extension. The Rosh was asked a related question during the Crusades, when Jews sought refuge inside churches to escape violent mobs. The Rosh ruled that entering a church for protection was permitted, since the Jews were using the walls of the building, not seeking salvation through any religious power — precisely the distinction Tosafot draws.

## Murder & Rape

### Torah Comparison

The Gemara now examines how each of the three categories is derived. *Avodah zarah* has already been established through the verse of *b'khol nafshekha*. For *gilluy arayot* and *shfikhut damim*, the Gemara cites a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi. The *baraita* begins from the verse discussing a *na'arah me'orasah* (a betrothed young woman) who was raped: *ki ka'asher yakum ish al re'ehu u'retzacho nefesh ken hadavar hazeh* — "for just as a man rises against his fellow and murders him, so is this matter." The Torah is declaring that the woman bears no guilt — she is like a murder victim, not a perpetrator. The Torah's point is moral and exculpatory, but the Gemara notes that the comparison, *hekesh*, also teaches law bidirectionally.

### Kill Rapist

From the case of the *na'arah me'orasah*, we learn that one may — indeed must — intervene to save her, even at the cost of the attacker's life. This is derived from the verse stating that she called out and no one came to save her: the implication is that had rescue been possible, it would have been not merely permitted but obligatory. Thus: *nitnah lehatzilah benafsho* — she may be saved through the life of the attempted rapist. Correspondingly, from the case of the murderer, one learns the same: *nitan lehatzilo benafsho* — one may save the intended murder victim through the life of the attempted murderer.

### Extension to Acts

The *hekesh* also runs in the other direction: from murder to the case of forbidden sexual relations. Just as *shfikhut damim* requires *yehareg v'al ya'avor* — one must be killed rather than commit murder — so too *gilluy arayot*: a woman being coerced into a forbidden sexual act must, in principle, allow herself to be killed rather than comply. The grammatical form of the ruling (*tehareg v'al ta'avor*) is significant, and the Rishonim debate its precise reading.

### Rava's Case

The basis for the *yehareg v'al ya'avor* of murder itself is not a verse but a *sevara* — pure logic — as illustrated by the famous episode of the man who came before Rava. The local authority had commanded him: go kill so-and-so, or I will kill you. What should he do? Rava answered: let him kill you — *likatlukha v'lo tiktol*. Why? *Mai chazit dedama didakh sumak tfei?* — What makes you think your blood is redder? *Dilma dama de'ahu gavra sumak tfei?* — Perhaps this other person's blood is redder than yours? There is no basis in logic or morality to privilege one's own life over an innocent person's such that one would be licensed to commit the sin of murder. Rashi emphasizes: the act of choosing to murder constitutes the sin itself. One might imagine that the principle of *chayecha v'chayei achikha* — the general rule that one's own life takes precedence over another's — could apply here. But that principle governs cases of passive competition for limited resources, not the active commission of murder. No license to commit the sin exists merely because one's own life is at stake.

### Unresolved Issues

Several significant questions are left open. First, regarding the case of the woman: some Rishonim read the relevant *girsa* as *tehareg v'al ta'avor* — she must allow herself to be killed — while others read it as *yehareg v'al ya'avor* referring to a man commanded to commit a forbidden sexual act. Connected to this is the Gemara's discussion elsewhere about *Esther* and the concept of *karka olam* — passivity. The Ramban makes a pointed observation: in a case of rape, even if a woman were willing to give up her life rather than submit, she might be physically unable to prevent the act. Her choice to sacrifice her life would not necessarily prevent the violation. This introduces a fundamental asymmetry that may affect how the rule applies to her case. Second, there is the question of what exactly is included in *gilluy arayot* for purposes of *yehareg v'al ya'avor*. The Gemara derives this law from the case of rape of a betrothed woman, which involves both violence and a violation against another person (the woman's betrothed). The question of whether less obviously "victimizing" prohibited sexual acts — such as a prohibition of *niddah* — would equally require martyrdom is not fully settled. The general assumption among the Rishonim is that anything classified as *arayot* is included, but the issue is worth noting. Third, on the question of *shfikhut damim*: Rava's logic rests on the inability to establish whose blood is "redder" — that is, whose life is objectively more valuable. But this raises the further question of whether, if killing one person would save multiple lives, the calculus changes. The Talmud's framework treats each life as infinite in value, making such arithmetic impossible — but the question of whether *yehareg v'al ya'avor* admits of any exception when one murder prevents many murders is left as a profound open question.

## Fever Healing

### Orlah Application

The final case on the daf involves Mar bar Rav Ashi, who encountered Ravina rubbing his daughter with *guharki de'orlah* — some form of juice or liquid from *orlah* fruit, perhaps unripe olives with their sediment intact, applied externally for what is understood to be a health purpose. The case raises immediate questions, since *orlah* is forbidden in *hana'ah*, and *orlah* does use the language of *achilah* — meaning, as established earlier in the daf, that the prohibition ordinarily requires *derech hana'ah*, the normal way of benefiting.

### Fever as Sakanah

Mar bar Rav Ashi challenges: when did the Rabbis say one may derive benefit from forbidden objects? Only in a situation of *sakanah* — genuine risk to life. When there is no such risk, deriving benefit from *orlah* remains prohibited. Ravina responds with the first answer: *hai ishta tzamirta nami ke-sha'at sakanah damya* — this high fever is itself the equivalent of *sakanah*. It is not merely discomfort; high fevers carry the potential for serious danger, and therefore this situation falls within the category of life-threatening conditions that permit use of otherwise forbidden items.

### Not Derech Hana'ah

A second answer is offered: *midi derech hana'ah ka avidna?* — Am I doing this in the normal way of benefit? The juice being applied is from unripe olives, or perhaps mixed with sediment, and is being rubbed onto the skin rather than consumed. This is not *derech hana'ah* for *orlah*. Since *orlah* prohibitions are tied to the language of *achilah* and therefore require a normal mode of benefit, using *orlah* fruit in this unripe, sediment-laden, externally applied form does not constitute a biblically prohibited act of benefit.

### Refuah Exception

The Rishonim note that the absence of a biblical transgression does not mean the act is entirely without restriction — a rabbinic prohibition may still apply. But the key ruling that emerges is that for purposes of *refuah* — healing — using something *shelo k'derech hana'ah* is permissible. This provides a fitting closure to the arc of the daf: the discussion opened with the principle that *shelo k'derech hana'ah* does not incur lashes, and concludes with the practical ruling that such abnormal use of forbidden objects is permissible in a medical context. The shiur will continue after the break with further discussion of these themes of *hana'ah* and *refuah*.
