# Sukkah 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achilat Arai

### Broader Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אוֹכְלִין אֲחִילַת עֲרַאי חוּץ לַסּוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may eat a casual meal outside the *sukka*.**

The daf we take up today is 27a, though we begin slightly earlier, near the bottom of 26b, with the mishnah's ruling that *ochlin achilat arai chutz lasukkah* — one may eat a temporary eating outside the *sukkah*. We spent considerable time previously examining the broad range of exemptions granted to the sick, to those in discomfort (*mitzta'er*), and to travelers, and we looked there at the larger conceptual issue: the *mitzvah* in its core is not a narrow technical matter of eating a precise quantity inside a *sukkah*, but a broader concept of fundamentally living there. Because it is that broader, more abstract obligation, there is correspondingly greater latitude in determining what kinds of circumstances genuinely constitute "living" in a place and what do not. This stands in sharp contrast to the way we have come to experience the obligation today — translated into a very limited, narrowly defined technical sense, focused on eating a particular quantity, with sleeping effectively set aside entirely. In that contemporary framing, the wide range of exemptions can seem surprising. But the more all-encompassing the obligation is, the more those exemptions make intuitive sense.

### Shiur Achilat Arai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְכַמָּה אֲחִילַת עֲרַאי? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תַּרְתֵּי וּתְלָתֵי בֵּי. אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וַהֲוָיא לֵיהּ סְעוּדַת קְבַע! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּדֵי טְעִימַת בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּאָזֵיל לְבֵי כְּנִישְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara now begins to discuss what constitutes a casual meal. **And how much is** considered **a casual eating?** **Rav Yosef said: Two or three** egg-bulks' worth. **Abaye said to him: But that would be a fixed meal** for some people! **Rather, Abaye said: It is** equivalent to **the taste of a *yeshiva* student who goes to study hall,** i.e., a very small amount.

With that context in place, the *gemara* turns to quantifying exactly how much food constitutes *achilat arai*. The text reads: *vekamah achilat arai* — how much is considered only a temporary eating, up to what amount?

*Amar Rav Yosef*: *tarti u'tlat bei* — two or three eggs' worth. Rashi, it should be noted, did not have the word *bei* in his version of the text; his reading is *tarti u'tlat*, meaning two or three morsels, though the amount one can swallow in a single go is generally assumed to be an egg's worth, making his reading consistent with two to three eggs.

*Amar leih Abaye*: Abaye objects, pointing out that for some people, two or three eggs' worth is already enough to constitute a full meal — *ve'havia leih seudat keva*. That would be a *keva* meal, a fixed, permanent eating, not a temporary one. *Ella amar Abaye*: rather, the measure is *kedei te'imat bar bei rav de'azeil l'bei kallah* — like the taste a *yeshiva bachur* takes before going in to his learning for the day. The precise quantity is not fully specified, but it is understood to be what one can swallow in a single go — approximately one egg's worth. Up to that amount is considered temporary.

### Food Type Categories

Interestingly, the *gemara* here does not address what *type* of food is in question, only the quantity. There is a *gemara* elsewhere in Yoma that takes up the question of *peiri* — whether fruit requires a *sukkah* at all — and the conclusion there is that fruit does not. The question then becomes which foods fall into the category of fruit and which do not. The general assumption is that bread and *mezonot* are the paradigmatic cases requiring a *sukkah*, while fruit clearly does not. Where meat and potatoes or similar foods fall is a matter of debate among the *rishonim*. The practical tendency is to adopt a narrow definition of what is technically obligated in a *sukkah*, focusing on bread, *mezonot*, and to some extent wine. But the fact remains: in our *gemara* here, the nature of the food is not discussed at all — only the quantity.

### Sleeping in Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוֹכְלִין אֲחִילַת עֲרַאי חוּץ לַסּוּכָּה, וְאֵין יִישְׁנִים שֵׁינַת עֲרַאי חוּץ לַסּוּכָּה. מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may eat a casual meal outside the *sukka*, but one may not take even a brief nap outside the *sukka*. What is the reason?**

The *gemara* then introduces a *braitah*: *ochlin achilat arai chutz lasukkah* — one may eat a temporary eating outside the *sukkah* — *v'ein yeshenin shenat arai chutz lasukkah* — but one may not take even a temporary nap outside of it. *Mai ta'ama* — why is a nap treated differently from a temporary snack? The question is a genuine one: if the logic of *achilat arai* is that a small amount does not constitute the kind of fixed presence the *mitzvah* demands, why doesn't the same reasoning apply to a brief nap?

## Shenat Arai Debate

### Gezeirah Shema Yiradem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַשִׁי: גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִרָּדֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: It is a rabbinic decree, for fear that he will fall into a deep sleep.**

*Amar Rav Ashi*: the answer is *gezeirah shema yiradem* — a rabbinic decree based on the concern that one might fall into a deep sleep. Technically, a nap itself is not the problem; rather, we are afraid of where it might lead.

### Tefillin Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָאָמַר אָדָם יָשֵׁן בִּתְפִילִּין שְׁנַת עֲרַאי, אֲבָל לֹא שְׁנַת קֶבַע? הָא לִגְזֵרָה הָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ אִלְתוֹמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But doesn't the Sages teach that a person may take a brief nap while wearing phylacteries, but not a permanent sleep? If the decree** of falling into a deep sleep is **the concern, then this decree would** also **apply** to **the case of phylacteries!**

Abaye immediately challenges this reasoning: if every permitted nap carries the risk of becoming a deep sleep, how can the *halacha* ever draw a meaningful distinction between the two? For there is a *braitah* that explicitly rules *yeshan adam shenat arai bitfillin aval lo shenat keva* — a person may take a temporary nap while wearing *tefillin*, but not a permanent sleep. According to Rav Ashi's logic, that nap too should be prohibited by the same *gezeirah* — since a nap can always slide into deep sleep, how can any *halachic* framework permit it?

### Moser Shinato

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִילַּאי: בְּמוֹסֵר שִׁנָּתוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef, son of Rav Ilai, said: It is in a case where he has entrusted his sleep to others,** i.e., designated them to wake him up.

*Amar Rav Yosef brei deRav Ilai*: the case of the permitted nap in *tefillin* is a special one — *b'moser shinato l'acherim* — where one has designated others to wake him up. That special circumstance accounts for why a nap is permitted in that context: the concern of the *gezeirah* is neutralized when someone else is serving as a guarantor.

### Co-Guarantor Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: הַאי דַּמּוֹסֵר שִׁנָּתוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים, הַאי בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ לִנְטוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshia raises a difficulty: One who has entrusted his sleep to others — those others need to guard themselves** lest they, too, fall asleep.

*Matkif lah Rav Mesharsheya*: your co-signer himself needs a co-signer. How can you trust that the person you've asked to wake you won't himself fall asleep? This difficulty — that delegating vigilance to another person does not eliminate the underlying risk — is a real one. It arises in other contexts as well, such as whether one can rely on another person to remind him before a deadline. The conclusion here seems to be that this answer cannot stand: if the *gezeirah* applies, a human guarantor does not suffice to remove it.

### Rosho Bein Birkav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּמְנִיחַ רֹאשׁוֹ בֵּין בִּרְכָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: It is** the case **where he** lays **his head between his knees.**

*Ella amar Rabbah bar bar Chanah amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the resolution lies elsewhere entirely. The permitted nap in *tefillin* is a case of *bimeniach rosho bein birkav* — where one puts his head between his knees. This is not a matter of lying down on a couch for a real nap; one is simply putting one's head down on a desk, like trying to sleep on an airplane. In that position, there is no serious concern that the brief rest will turn into a full sleep. So fundamentally there is nothing wrong with a nap, but the concern is always that a nap might become a deep sleep. With *tefillin*, the case is only permitted because of this special circumstance in which that escalation is not realistically feared.

## Rava's Distinction

### Ein Keva LeSheinah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֵין קְבַע לִשְׁנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: There is no fixed** measure **for sleep.**

Rava rejects this entire approach. *Amar Rava*: *ein keva lesheinah* — there is no fixed category for sleep when it comes to *tefillin*. Rava is drawing a fundamental distinction between the two *mitzvot* and argues that the reasoning for prohibiting a nap outside the *sukkah* has nothing to do with the fear that it might turn into a deeper sleep.

### Keva as Dwelling

Rava's core argument is that by *sukkah*, the issue is not what a nap might become, but what a nap already is. Sleep — even a brief nap — is categorically different from eating as an expression of where one lives. When one is somewhere other than home, one might snack: on the street, in the office, wherever. Snacking is something that does not inherently speak to where one resides. But sleeping — even briefly — is strongly tied to where one sets down roots. It would be quite rare to sleep somewhere that is not one's home. And therefore, even a nap is considered an act that is central to and constitutive of the concept of living in a place. That is what *ein keva lesheinah* means in this context: even a brief sleep is already *keva* in the sense of defining where one dwells.

### Tefillin vs. Sukkah

By *tefillin*, the concern is not about sleep as an expression of residence but about something physiologically distinct: the concern is passing gas in the presence of *tefillin*, which would be a disrespect to the *mitzvah*. A brief nap does not present a serious risk of that — only a deep sleep does. And Rava maintains there is no meaningful concern that a nap will turn into a deep sleep; a nap is a nap. Therefore, by *tefillin*, where the sole issue is passing gas, a nap is permissible because it does not present that risk. By *sukkah*, however, the very act of napping — regardless of how long it lasts — is already defined as *keva*, because it is the kind of act that expresses where one is living. That is the conceptual difference: not what the nap might become, but what it already means.

## Three Braitot

### Contradictory Braitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָשֵׁן אָדָם בִּתְפִילִּין שְׁנַת עֲרַאי וְלֹא שְׁנַת קֶבַע. וְתַנְיָא: בֵּין קְבַע בֵּין עֲרַאי. וְתַנְיָא: לֹא קְבַע וְלֹא עֲרַאי. לֹא קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may sleep** with phylacteries in **a brief nap, but not substantial sleep. And it is taught** in another *baraita*: **Both substantial sleep and** a brief nap **are** permitted. **And it is taught** in yet another *baraita*: **Neither substantial sleep nor** a brief nap **is** permitted. The Gemara says: **This is not difficult;** the contradictions can be resolved by distinguishing the circumstances of each case.

Having established the theoretical framework for the distinction between napping and sleeping in the context of *tefillin*, the *gemara* now introduces three *braitot* that appear to contradict one another. The first says *yeshan adam bitfillin shenat arai velo shenat keva* — one may sleep temporarily but not permanently. The second says *bein keva bein arai* — both are permitted. The third says *lo keva velo arai* — neither is permitted. Rather than treating these as an unresolved three-way dispute, the *gemara* reconciles them: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty.

### Nekitum Bidei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא דְּנָקִיטִים בִּידֵי — אַפִּילּוּ שְׁנַת עֲרַאי אָסוּר. הָא דִּמְנִיחַ בְּרֵישֵׁיהּ — שְׁנַת עֲרַאי מוּתָּר, שְׁנַת קֶבַע אָסוּר. הָא דִּפָּרִישׂ סוּדְרָא אִלֵּוָיְיהּ — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **This** case, where the phylacteries are **being held in** one's **hands, even a brief nap is** prohibited. **This** case, where **he places** the phylacteries **on his head — a brief nap is** permitted, **and substantial sleep is** prohibited. **And this** case, where **he spreads a cloth around them** for protection — a brief nap is **permitted.**

*Ha denakit lehu bideih*: the strictest case — where even a nap is forbidden — is where one is holding the *tefillin* in one's hands. If one's hands relax during sleep, the *tefillin* will fall. Even though the issue of passing gas does not apply when the *tefillin* is not being worn, the physical risk of dropping what one is holding means that even a brief nap is too risky, and the prohibition applies *afilu arai*.

*Ha dmanach beireisheih*: the middle case — napping permitted but deep sleep prohibited — is where one is actually wearing the *tefillin* on one's head. It is notable that the *gemara* throughout tends to emphasize the *tefillin shel rosh* in these discussions, as with the famous phrase *karkafta dlo manach tefillin* — a skull that never had *tefillin* placed upon it. When one is wearing the *tefillin*, a nap is permitted because we are not afraid of passing gas during a brief rest, but a deep sleep is forbidden because it will eventually lead there.

*Ha defaris sudra ilaviyei*: the most lenient case — both napping and sleeping permitted — is where the *tefillin* is wrapped in a handkerchief and one is simply sleeping nearby it, not wearing it. Since it is wrapped and not being worn, the concern about disrespect through passing gas does not apply in the same way, and sleep near the protected *tefillin* is permitted.

## Shiur Shenat Arai

### Kedei Hiluch Me'ah Amah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנִי רָמִי בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל: כְּדֵי הִילּוּךְ מֵאָה אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Yechezkel taught: It is equivalent to** the time it takes for **walking one hundred cubits.**

Now that the *gemara* has distinguished — at least for *tefillin* purposes — between a nap and a deep sleep, it turns to the question of quantity: how much time constitutes a temporary sleep? *Tani Rami bar Yechezkel*: *kedei hiluch me'ah amah* — the time it takes to walk one hundred *amot*. To work out that duration: the *halachic* standard is that walking a *mil* — two thousand *amot* — takes eighteen minutes, or roughly twenty minutes for round figures. If two thousand *amot* takes twenty minutes, then one hundred *amot* takes approximately one minute. So a *shenat arai* is, by this measure, about one minute — a genuine power nap in the most extreme sense.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְנַמְנֵם הַיָּשֵׁן בִּתְפִילִּין וְרָאָה קֶרִי, אוֹחֵז בָּרְצוּעָה וְאֵינוֹ אוֹחֵז בַּקְּצִיצָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יָשֵׁן אָדָם בִּתְפִילִּין שְׁנַת עֲרַאי, אֲבָל לֹא שְׁנַת קֶבַע.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one is napping while wearing phylacteries and experienced a seminal emission, he may grip the strap, but he may not grip the housing, the words of Rabbi Ya'akov. And the Rabbis say: One may take a brief nap while wearing phylacteries, but not substantial sleep.**

The *gemara* continues with a supporting *braitah*: *ha-menamnem ha-yashen bitfillin vera'ah keri* — if one is napping with *tefillin* and has a seminal emission, *ocheiz baretzu'ah v'eino ocheiz baketzitzah* — one grasps the *tefillin* by the strap to remove it but not by the housing itself. That is the ruling of Rabbi Yaakov. The *chachamim* then add their familiar formulation: one may sleep temporarily but not permanently. Tosafot notes the ambiguity in Rabbi Yaakov's position — it is not entirely clear whether he is engaging with the *arai/keva* distinction or debating a separate question about when the concern of leading to a deeper sleep applies, perhaps specifically at night.

### Shinat HaSus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לְאָדָם לִישַׁן בַּיּוֹם יוֹתֵר מִשְׁנַת הַסּוּס. וְכַמָּה שְׁנַת הַסּוּס — שִׁיתִּין נִשְׁמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: It is prohibited for a person to sleep during the day longer than** the duration of **the sleep of a horse. And how long is** the duration of **the sleep of a horse? It is sixty breaths.**

The *gemara* next introduces a separate teaching of Rav regarding daytime sleep more generally — not specifically in the context of *tefillin*, but as a broader statement about one's conduct. *Asur le'adam lishan bayom yoter mishnat hasus* — it is forbidden to sleep during the day for longer than a horse sleeps. Rashi explains the prohibition in terms of *bittul Torah*. And how long does a horse sleep? *Shitin nishmei* — sixty breaths.

The precise duration of sixty horse breaths generates considerable uncertainty. The respiratory rate of a resting horse is approximately eight to sixteen breaths per minute, which would place sixty breaths somewhere in the range of four to seven and a half minutes. The authorities who have attempted to quantify this — including the *Sha'arei Teshuvah* and Rabbeinu Yonah — arrive at figures in the range of three to three and a half minutes for humans; the empirical basis for these calculations is, to say the least, not entirely transparent. The practical upshot is that a permitted daytime nap is a very short one.

### Abaye's Nap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שִׁנְתֵּיהּ דְּמָר כִּדְרַב, וּדְרַב כִּדְרַבִּי, וּדְרַבִּי כִּדְדָוִד, וּדְדָוִד כִּדְסוּסְיָא, וּדְסוּסְיָא שִׁיתִּין נִשְׁמֵי. אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה נָיֵם כִּדִּמְעַיֵּיל מִפּוּמְבְּדִיתָא לְבֵי כוּבֵּי. קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: ״עַד מָתַי עָצֵל תִּשְׁכָּב מָתַי תָּקוּם מִשְּׁנָתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: The sleep of** the **Master,** Rabba, **is like that of Rav, and that of Rav** is **like** the sleep **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And that of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is like that of** King **David, and that of** King **David** is **like that of a horse, and that of a horse is sixty breaths. Abaye would sleep** during the day **for** a period **equivalent** to the time it takes to **enter from Pumbedita to Bei Kuvi. Rav Yosef read** the following verse as **pertaining to** Abaye: **"How long will you sleep, sluggard? When will you arise from your sleep?"** (Proverbs 6:9).

*Amar Abaye*: the sleeping of his master — Rabbah — was *ke-deRav*, following the standard of Rav. And Rav learned how much to nap from his own *Rebbi*, and that *Rebbi* in turn learned from the practice going all the way back to David, who slept *ke-susya* — like a horse — meaning sixty breaths.

*Abaye hava noyam kedei me'al miPumbedita leVei Kuvi* — Abaye, however, would nap for the duration of the journey from Pumbedita to Bei Kuvi. Rashi, in another context, records that this distance is six *parsaot*. Since a *parsa* is four *mil* and a *mil* is eighteen minutes, six *parsaot* works out to roughly six hours — a rather dramatic figure, far removed from the one-minute *shenat arai* or the horse's sixty breaths. *Kari alei Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef, who was Abaye's teacher, said about him: *ad masai atzel tishkav masai takum mishinasecha* — "How long, O sluggard, will you lie down? When will you rise from your slumber?" — quoting the verse of rebuke. The irony is notable: on one extreme we have a one-minute power nap as the *halachic* standard, and at the other extreme, Rashi's reading of Abaye's practice suggests a six-hour rest.

## Tefillin Sleep Rules

### Day vs. Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לִישַׁן בַּיּוֹם, רָצָה — חוֹלֵץ, רָצָה — מַנִּיחַ. בַּלַּיְלָה — חוֹלֵץ וְאֵינוֹ מַנִּיחַ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי נָתָן. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הַיְּלָדִים, לְעוֹלָם חוֹלְצִין וְאֵינָן מַנִּיחִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרְגִילִים בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who enters** his bed **to sleep during the day, if he wishes, he** may **remove** his phylacteries, and **if he wishes, he** may **leave** them in place. One who enters to sleep **at night removes** his phylacteries **and** may **not leave** them in place while sleeping. This is **the statement of Rabbi Natan. Rabbi Yosei says: The young** men must **always remove** them **and not leave** them in place while sleeping **because they are accustomed to impurity,** as they are more likely to experience a seminal emission.

The *gemara* turns to a new *braitah* on the rules governing *tefillin* during sleep and under various conditions. *Ha-nichnas lishon bayom* — if one goes to sleep during the day, *ratza choletz ratza meniach* — if he wishes he may remove the *tefillin* and if he wishes he may leave them on. Since daytime sleep is presumably only a nap and not a deep sleep, there is latitude. *Ba-layla* — at night, however, *choletz ve'eino meniach* — one must remove them and may not leave them on, since nighttime sleep carries a greater concern of leading to a deep sleep, or perhaps even a nap at night more readily escalates than one during the day. Tosafot discusses this distinction between day and night.

### Young Men Rule

*Divrei Rabbi Nosson* — that is Rabbi Nosson's position. *Rabbi Yossi omer*: *ha-yeladim* — the young men — *le'olam chaltzim ve'einam menichim* — must always remove their *tefillin* and may never leave them on, even during the day. The reason given is *mipnei she-ragilim betumah* — because they are more accustomed to *tumah*, meaning they will more regularly experience seminal emission during sleep. The *gemara* considers whether this implies that Rabbi Yossi holds that a *baal keri* is forbidden from wearing *tefillin* altogether. That would be a far-reaching position — but the *gemara* notices an oddity in the very framing of the question. The concern here is not about someone who has already become a *baal keri* before putting on *tefillin*; it is about someone who might become a *baal keri* while wearing them. Yet the *gemara* treats these as presenting the same underlying question, which is itself striking, since the latter concern — desecrating the *tefillin* in the moment — would seem categorically more severe than the former.

### Wives Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בִּילָדִים וּנְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן עִמָּהֶן עָסְקִינַן, שֶׁמָּא יָבוֹאוּ לִידֵי הֶרְגֵּל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We are dealing with young** men whose **wives are with them,** and the concern is **lest they overlook the fact that they are donning phylacteries and inadvertently come to engage in matters** to which they are **accustomed,** i.e., relations with their wives.

*Amar Abaye*: the resolution is that we are not discussing young unmarried men who may have seminal emissions in their sleep, but rather *be-yeladim u-neshoseihem imahen* — young men who are with their wives. The concern is that even if one is only going to bed briefly, the proximity of his wife may lead to marital relations. What is also notable here — and the *gemara* does not explicitly flag it but the implication is clear — is that apparently men of more advanced age who lie in bed next to their wives while wearing *tefillin* do not raise the same concern. The *gemara* seems to assume that younger men are at particular risk of this escalation in a way that others are not.

### Shachach Veshmish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁכַח וְשִׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ בִּתְפִילִּין — אֵינוֹ אוֹחֵז לֹא בָּרְצוּעָה וְלֹא בַּקְּצִיצָה עַד שֶׁיִּטּוֹל יָדָיו וְיִטְּלֵם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַיָּדַיִם עַסְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one forgot** that he was donning phylacteries **and engaged in relations with his phylacteries** in place, **he** may **grip neither the strap nor the box until he washes his hands, and** only then may he **remove** the phylacteries. This is **because the hands are active** and tend to inadvertently touch parts of the body that are unclean.

*Shachach v'shimish mitato bitfillin* — if one forgot and had marital relations while wearing *tefillin*. All of this — and it is worth pausing here — reflects a world in which *tefillin* were worn throughout the entire day. The regulations governing what happens when one lies in bed, when one is with a spouse, when one forgets — all of this presupposes that *tefillin* are a constant presence, not a brief morning ritual. This is a powerful parallel to the situation with *sukkah*. Just as *sukkah* used to be a genuinely all-encompassing obligation of living for the entire week — with all the corresponding leniencies and exemptions flowing from that all-embracing character — so too *tefillin* used to be worn all day. And just as we have now narrowed the *sukkah* obligation into a series of very precise technical requirements, so too we have narrowed *tefillin* to a brief morning wearing, with the result that the situations described in these *braitot* seem almost inconceivably remote from our experience.

Returning to the *halacha*: *eino ocheiz lo biktzitzah velo birtzuo* — in the case of one who forgot and had relations while wearing *tefillin*, he may not take them off even by holding the straps. *Ad sheyitol yadav veyitlam* — he must first wash his hands. The reason is that while in the earlier case (seminal emission without relations), the concern was the person's own state but his hands were not implicated, here his hands have been in direct contact with places that are not clean — *mipnei sheyadav askaniyot hen* — because hands are constantly busy. Only after washing his hands may he come into contact even with the straps of the *tefillin*.

## Tefillin-Sukkah Parallel

### All-Day Tefillin

This digression into *tefillin* law, which might seem to be a departure from the *sukkah* discussion, in fact illuminates something deeper about the relationship between how these two *mitzvot* once functioned and how they function now. The scenarios the *gemara* addresses — lying in bed with one's wife while wearing *tefillin*, forgetting and having relations while wearing them, napping with them on — all presuppose a world in which *tefillin* were part of one's daily garb from morning until night. There are *gemarot* that speak at length about the constant consciousness required in connection with *tefillin*, particularly the *tefillin shel rosh*, comparing that awareness to the consciousness required of the *Kohen Gadol* wearing the *tzitz*. Whether anyone truly lived up to that level of sustained mindfulness is a separate question, but the baseline assumption of constant wearing is clear.

### Evolving Observance

There is a striking conceptual parallel. The *sukkah*, at its fullest expression, was understood as one's actual home for seven days — an all-encompassing form of dwelling, with all the latitude and all the depth that implies. Today we have translated that into a narrow technical obligation focused on precise quantities of specific foods, with sleeping removed from the picture almost entirely. The *tefillin* underwent an analogous evolution: from a full-day *mitzvah* with all the attendant regulations about sleeping, marital relations, and bodily functions, to the circumscribed morning practice most people observe today. Both *mitzvot* moved from being all-encompassing to being narrowly technical, and that shift profoundly changes the texture of the questions one asks — and the answers one gives — about each.

## Maase Stories

### Taste vs. Dates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַעֲשֶׂה וְהֵבִיאוּ לוֹ לְרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי לִטְעוֹם אֶת הַתַּבְשִׁיל, וּלְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שְׁנֵי כּוֹתָבוֹת וּדְלִי שֶׁל מַיִם. וְאָמְרוּ: הַעֲלוּם לַסּוּכָּה. וּכְשֶׁנָּתְנוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹכֶל פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה, נְטָלוֹ בַּמַּפָּה, וַאֲכָלוֹ חוּץ לַסּוּכָּה, וְלֹא בֵּירַךְ אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **An incident** occurred where **they brought a cooked dish to Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai** for him **to taste, and to Rabban Gamliel** they brought **two dates and a bucket of water. And they** each **said: Take them up to the *sukka*. And when they gave Rabbi Tzadok** less than an egg-bulk of food, **he took** the food **in a cloth and he ate it outside the *sukka* and did not recite a blessing after** eating **it.**

The *gemara* now presents several *maase* stories — accounts of how the *tanna'im* actually conducted themselves — and it is notable how many such stories appear throughout *Masechet Sukkah* relative to other *masechtot*. The *mishna* records: *maase ve-heviu lo le-Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai lit'om et hatavshil* — they brought Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai a taste of the stew — *u-l'Rabban Gamliel shenei kotevot u-dli shel mayim* — and to Rabban Gamliel two pressed dates and a bucket of water — *ve-omru ha'alum lasukkah* — and they said: bring it up to the *sukkah*. These amounts — a taste of stew, two dates — would seem to qualify as *achilat arai*, which we have just established may be eaten outside the *sukkah*. Why then were they brought inside?

### Baa Lehachmir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר? חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: אִם בָּא לְהַחְמִיר עַל עַצְמוֹ — מַחְמִיר, וְלֵית בֵּיהּ מִשּׁוּם יוּהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Is the mishna citing **an incident to contradict** the *halakha* cited earlier? The Gemara answers: The mishna is **incomplete,** as it is lacking a significant element, **and it teaches the following: If one seeks to impose a stringency upon himself** and eat nothing outside the *sukka*, **he** may **be stringent, and there is no** element of **presumptuousness** in adopting that stringency.

*Maase listor*? — is this a story that comes to contradict the preceding ruling? No: *chissurei mechsera* — the *mishna* is missing some words, and what it means to say is this: *im ba lehachmir al atzmo machmir, veleit bei mishum yuhara* — if one wishes to be strict upon oneself, one may be strict, and this does not constitute *yuhara* — religious arrogance. So the *mishna* is not establishing a contradiction; it is adding a clarification. The minimum thresholds are minima: one is not obligated to bring a small snack into the *sukkah*. But if one wishes to do so as a *chumrah*, that is entirely appropriate and does not cross the line into unseemly religious display.

The point is a nuanced one. Some acts of stringency might indeed constitute *yuhara* — particularly if the act being performed is not typically associated with a fixed domestic context, such as snacking on the street, so that insisting on doing even that in the *sukkah* might look like a performance of religiosity. But the *gemara* rules that even this does not cross that line. In fact, one might even argue that things like sitting and reading, watching and *schmoozing* — the kinds of leisure activities one naturally does at home — represent not even a *chumrah* but the *lechatchila* fulfillment of *sukkah* in its fuller sense.

### Rabbi Tzadok

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כְּבֵיצָה, בָּעֵי סוּכָּה. לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף וְאַבָּיֵי! דִּילְמָא פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה נְטִילָה וּבְרָכָה לָא בָּעֵי, הָא כְּבֵיצָה בָּעֵי נְטִילָה וּבְרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: Had they given him **an egg-bulk** of food, **he** would have been **required** to eat it in a ***sukka*. Let us say that this is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Yosef and Abaye.** The Gemara answers: No proof can be cited from here, because **perhaps** the reason the mishna emphasizes that Rabbi Tzadok ate less than an egg-bulk of food is that eating **less than an egg-bulk does not require washing** hands **and** reciting **a blessing** after eating it; **however,** eating **an egg-bulk requires washing and** reciting a **blessing.**

The *mishna* then records: *u-keshenatnu lo le-Rabbi Tzadok ochel pachat mi-kebeitzah* — when they gave Rabbi Tzadok bread less than an egg's worth, *netalo bemappah* — he would take it in a handkerchief — *ve-achalo chutz lasukkah* — and eat it outside the *sukkah* — *ve-lo berach acharayib* — and he did not bench.

### Shiur Netilah

The *gemara* asks: *ha kibeitzah basukkah*? — does it mean that once one has a full egg's worth one must eat in the *sukkah*? This would appear to be a direct challenge to Rav Yosef and Abaye, both of whom set the threshold for *achilat keva* at one egg's worth or more. But the *gemara* clarifies: the point of the Rabbi Tzadok story is not primarily about *sukkah* at all. What it is teaching is that *pachos mikibeitzah* — less than an egg's worth — exempts one from *netilat yadayim* and *Birkat HaMazon* as well. *Ha kibeitzah ba'ei netilah uvracha* — once one reaches an egg's worth, one is obligated in handwashing and *Birkat HaMazon* — though not necessarily yet in eating in a *sukkah*, which by Rav Yosef's standard only kicks in at two or three eggs.

Several practical points are worth noting here. Our current practice is to rule that *Birkat HaMazon* is required even for a *kezayit*, following the large Tosafot on that point. For handwashing, there is a debate among the *rishonim* about whether the threshold is a *kezayit* or a *kibeitzah*, and the practical ruling *halacha l'maaseh* requires careful review. As for the question of why Rabbi Tzadok wrapped the food in a handkerchief rather than washing his hands: Rashi understands this as simply a cleanliness measure. Tosafot, however, explains that Rabbi Tzadok was a *Kohen* who would eat his food as though it were *terumah*, and therefore even where general handwashing was not technically required, he would not touch the food directly, treating it with the level of care appropriate to *terumah* food.

The question of the bucket of water in the case of Rabban Gamliel — which clearly exceeds any egg-based threshold — is answered by returning to the food type question. Presumably, water is never considered a *keva* food regardless of quantity, in the same way that fruit requires no *sukkah* however much one consumes.

## Rabbi Eliezer Fourteen

### Arba Esrei Seudot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה סְעוּדוֹת חַיָּיב אָדָם לֶאֱכוֹל בַּסּוּכָּה, אַחַת בְּיוֹם וְאַחַת בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Eliezer says: A person is obligated to eat fourteen meals in the *sukka*,** one **during the day and one** at **night,** for all seven days.

The *mishna* now presents Rabbi Eliezer's ruling: *arba esrei seudot chayav adam le'echol basukkah achat bayom v'achat balailah* — a person is obligated to eat fourteen meals in the *sukkah*, one during the day and one at night, for all seven days.

### Chachamim View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין לַדָּבָר קִצְבָה, חוּץ מִלֵּילֵי יוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: There is no quota** for the number of meals **except for the** meal on the **evening of the first Festival** day **of *Sukkot*,** which one is required to eat in the *sukka*.

*Ve-Chachamim omrim*: the Sages disagree. *Ein ladavar kitzvah* — there is no fixed minimum. Importantly, this does not mean that one may eat outside the *sukkah* freely — the prohibition on *keva* eating outside the *sukkah* remains in force. Rather, it means there is no positive obligation to ensure that one eats a *keva* meal. One may eat fruit, eat tiny amounts, fast, or otherwise arrange one's eating so that no formal *seudah* is ever triggered — except on the first night.

### Tashlumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִי שֶׁלֹּא אָכַל לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן — יַשְׁלִים לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rabbi Eliezer said: One who did not eat** a meal on the **evening of the first day** of the Festival should **compensate** with a meal on the **evening of the last day of the Festival.**

*Ve'od amar Rabbi Eliezer*: Rabbi Eliezer adds a further ruling — *mi shelo achal leil yom tov harishon yashlim bleil yom tov ha'acharon* — if one did not eat the first night of *Sukkot*, one may make up that meal on the final night of the festival, meaning the night of *Shemini Atzeret*. This raises an internal difficulty: Rabbi Eliezer just said one is obligated in fourteen meals, giving equal weight to all of them. Why single out the first night as something specially compensable? And conversely, if it is specifically about the first night's meal, how does one discharge that makeup obligation on *Shemini Atzeret* when presumably one is no longer eating in a *sukkah*?

### Ein Tashlumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין לַדָּבָר תַּשְׁלוּמִין, וְעַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר: ״מְעֻוָּת לֹא יוּכַל לִתְקוֹן וְחֶסְרוֹן לֹא יוּכַל לְהִמָּנוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: There is no compensation for** this **matter, and with regard to** similar cases where it is impossible to rectify failure to fulfill a positive mitzva, **it is stated: "That which is crooked cannot be made straight; and that which is wanting cannot be numbered"** (Ecclesiastes 1:15).

*Ve-Chachamim omrim*: the Sages reject the possibility of any *tashlumin* for a missed *sukkah* meal. *Ve'al zeh ne'emar me'uvas lo yuchal liskon ve-cheisaron lo yuchal lehimanos* — "that which is crooked cannot be made straight, and that which is lacking cannot be counted" (Kohelet). Something missed cannot be retroactively repaired in this domain. The parallel to *Masechet Chagigah* — where one may bring a missed *chagigah* offering on any day of the *chag* up to and including the last day — was presumably in Rabbi Eliezer's mind, but the Sages reject its applicability here.

## Tashvu Ke'ein Taduru

### Dwelling Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״ כְּעֵין תָּדוּרוּ. מָה דִּירָה — אַחַת בַּיּוֹם וְאַחַת בַּלַּיְלָה, אַף סוּכָּה — אַחַת בְּיוֹם וְאַחַת בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer?** The Gemara answers that he derives his opinion from the verse: "In *sukkot* **shall you reside"** (Leviticus 23:42), which the Sages interpreted to mean: Reside **as you dwell** in your permanent home. Therefore, **just as** in one's **dwelling** one typically eats **one** meal **during the day and one** meal **at night, so too, in a *sukka*** one eats **one** meal **during the day and one** meal **at night.**

*Mai ta'ama deRabbi Eliezer*? — what is Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning for the fourteen-meal obligation? The verse says *tashvu* — you shall dwell — *ke'ein taduru* — as you dwell in a house. *Mah dirah achas bayom ve'achas balaila* — just as in a house one typically eats two meals a day, one during the day and one at night — *af sukkah achas bayom ve'achas balaila* — so too in the *sukkah*, one must eat one meal by day and one by night.

Tosafot notes that in the *Yerushalmi*, Rabbi Eliezer's position is grounded in a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *tashvu* appears both in the context of *Sukkot* and in the context of the *Mishkan*, where Aharon and his sons dwelt in the *Ohel Moed* for seven days — *yomam valaila shivas yamim* — and just as that dwelling made nights equivalent to days, so too in the *sukkah* the nighttime meals are as obligatory as the daytime ones. This is a rich parallel: not only is it about what it means to fully inhabit a place, but it also evokes the imagery of dwelling in the divine presence, in the sphere of the *Kodesh HaKodashim* with the *kapores* and the *keruvim*.

### Free Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? כְּדִירָה. מָה דִּירָה — אִי בָּעֵי אָכֵיל אִי בָּעֵי לָא אָכֵיל, אַף סוּכָּה נָמֵי — אִי בָּעֵי אָכֵיל אִי בָּעֵי לָא אָכֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** how do **the Rabbis** interpret that verse? The Gemara answers: They explain that a *sukka* is **like a** permanent **dwelling. Just as** in one's **dwelling, if one desires** to eat, **he eats, and if one does not desire** to do so, **he does not eat, so too,** in the ***sukka*, if one desires** to eat, **he eats, and if one does not desire** to do so, **he does not eat.**

*Ve-rabanan* respond: the verse says *ke-dirah* — like dwelling. And just as when one lives in a house one chooses whether to eat — no one compels a person to eat twice a day — *af sukkah ei ba'ei achil ei ba'ei lo achil* — so too in the *sukkah*, one may choose whether to eat. The *sukkah* is not supposed to be a prison. It is supposed to be treated as one would treat one's home — which means being granted the latitude and freedom of choice that one has at home.

## First Night Obligation

### First Night Chiyuv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי אֲפִילּוּ לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** then according to the Rabbis, **even** on **the first Festival evening as well** one should not be required to eat in the *sukka*.

If it is entirely volitional whether one has a meal — and one may construct one's eating over the entire *chag* such that no obligatory *seudah* is ever triggered — then how can there be any fixed obligation even on the first night?

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״, וְנֶאֱמַר ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ בְּחַג הַמַּצּוֹת. מָה לְהַלָּן — לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן חוֹבָה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ רְשׁוּת, אַף כָּאן — לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן חוֹבָה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak:** There is a verbal analogy between the festivals of Passover and *Sukkot*. **It is stated here,** with regard to *Sukkot*: "On the **fifteenth** day of this seventh month is the festival of *Sukkot* for seven days unto the Lord" (Leviticus 23:34). **And it is stated:** "And on the **fifteenth** day of the same month is the festival of *matzot* unto the Lord" (Leviticus 23:6) **with regard to** the festival of **Passover. Just as there,** with regard to Passover, on **the first night** there is **an obligation** to eat *matza* **and from that point onward it is optional,** as from that point onward the only obligation is to refrain from eating leaven, **so too here,** with regard to *Sukkot*, on **the first night** there **is an obligation** to eat in the *sukka* **and from that point onward it is optional.**

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak*: the answer lies in a *gezeirah shavah*. The fifteenth of Tishrei is linked by shared language to the fifteenth of Nisan, the first night of *Chag HaMatzot*.

### Matzah Comparison

*Pesach*, the first night of *matzah* is a firm obligation — *laila harishon chovah* — while from the second night onward it is *reshut*, optional. The verse *ba'erev tochlu matzot* is read as fixing the first night as obligatory, even though the broader context speaks of seven days. The word *ba'erev* isolates that first evening as uniquely obligatory. By the same *gezeirah shavah*, the same structure applies to *Sukkot*: *af kan laila harishon chovah, mikan va'eilach reshut* — here too the first night is obligatory, and from the second day onward it is volitional.

### No Obligation Days

The distinction between *sukkah* and *matzah* on the non-obligatory nights is worth spelling out precisely. After the first night of *Pesach*, one may entirely refrain from eating *matzah* on the remaining days — there is no circumstance in which eating will trigger a *matzah* obligation. By *Sukkot*, however, even after the first night, if one chooses to eat bread or *mezonot* in the requisite quantity, one becomes obligated to do so in the *sukkah* and makes a *bracha* — *leishev basukkah*. So it is not that the *sukkah* is irrelevant after the first night; it is that there is no positive obligation to bring oneself into a situation that requires it.

Tosafot raises an important implication: if there is no *chiyuv* of *se'udah* on the other days of *Yom Tov*, does that mean one has no obligation of a *Yom Tov seudah* on the other days of *Sukkot*? And if so, would forgetting *Ya'aleh VeYavo* in *bentching* carry no requirement to repeat, since the repetition obligation is tied to the existence of a mandatory *seudah*? Tosafot is inclined to accept this as a genuine consequence of the *gemara*'s logic. One might distinguish between a *kezayit* — which may still be technically required — and a *kibeitzah*, which is what triggers the full *sukkah* obligation, but Tosafot's analysis points in a striking direction.

## Rishonim Debates

### Shiur Comparison

A first question among the *rishonim* concerns the *shiur* — the minimum quantity of eating — required on the first night. If the first night is modeled directly on *matzah*, then perhaps one is *yotzei* with a *kezayit*. But if the first night is understood as an expression of *dirat sukkah* — the broader obligation of dwelling in the *sukkah* — that must be performed on the first night through the act of eating, then perhaps a *kibeitzah* or more is required, as that is the threshold for *achilat keva* that the *sukkah* obligation normally tracks.

### Food Type

A second question concerns the type of food. If we derive the first night's obligation from *matzah*, and *matzah* must be made from grain — *dagan* — then perhaps the first night's meal must specifically consist of bread, not merely *mezonot* and certainly not meat or other foods. *Rav Zeira* in the *Yerushalmi* raises exactly this question: *ima lehalan ad sheyochal kezayit dagan matzah af kan ad sheyochal kezayit dagan basukkah* — just as the *matzah* obligation requires grain, so too perhaps the first night of *sukkah* requires grain. This would rule out satisfying the first night's obligation through meat, potatoes, or fruit — only bread would do.

### Pesak on Rain

A third question is circumstantial: do the normal exemptions — rain, *mitzta'er*, illness — apply on the first night? This becomes the practical question that preoccupies *poskim* on the first night of *Sukkot* when it rains: until what hour does one wait for the rain to stop? After *chatzot*? Before? When can one give up and simply eat inside? If the first night's obligation is a form of *dirat sukkah* and the *mitzta'er* exemption applies to *dirat sukkah*, then perhaps rain exempts one from the first night as well. But if the first night is modeled strictly on *matzah* — a focused eating obligation that does not depend on whether the conditions for dwelling are met — then the normal *sukkah* exemptions would not apply. This remains a major *machlokes rishonim*.

### Tosafot Ta'avah

Tosafot cites a fascinating position from the *Yerushalmi* on a further implication of the *matzah* analogy. Just as one is cautioned not to eat heavily before the first night of *Pesach* so that one comes to the *matzah* eating with an appetite — *ta'avah* — perhaps the same applies to the first night of *sukkah*: *af kan ad sheyikanes lesukkah beta'avah* — one should enter the *sukkah* meal with an appetite. If the first night is truly modeled on *matzah*, then not only the quantity and food type but even the psychological state of genuine appetite may be part of the obligation.

## Tashlumin Details

### Mashlimin Bamah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁלִים בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּרִיפְתָּא, סְעוּדָה דְיוֹמֵיהּ קָא אָכֵיל. אֶלָּא, מַאי יַשְׁלִים — יַשְׁלִים בְּמִינֵי תַרְגִּימָא. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אִם הִשְׁלִים בְּמִינֵי תַרְגִּימָא — יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With what** will **he compensate** for his failure to eat the Festival meal? **If we say** that he compensates **with bread, he is** thereby **eating the** festive **meal of that** Eighth **Day** of Assembly; how is it obvious that it is compensation for a different meal? **Rather, what is** the meaning of: **He** should **compensate?** It means that **he** should **compensate by** adding **types of delicacies [*targima*]. That is taught** in a *baraita* **as well: If he compensated by** adding **types of delicacies, he fulfilled his obligation.**

The *gemara* now returns to the question of Rabbi Eliezer's *tashlumin*: with what does one make up a missed meal? *I-leima be-rifta* — if it is bread, that is simply the regular meal one would be having anyway, with nothing to distinguish it as a makeup. If one were eating a second meal on *Shemini Atzeret*, what marks it as the compensation for the missed first-night meal rather than merely one's regular meal? *Ella mai*: *yashlim beminei targima* — one makes it up with various delicacies, cooked dishes of distinction.

### Rashi Position

Rashi explains *minei targima* as cooked delicacies — various foods like fruit, legumes, roasted items, and cooked dishes — brought after the main meal has been cleared. The point is that the *minei targima* make the makeup visible and apparent: this additional course marks the occasion as a deliberate fulfillment of a missed obligation, not merely a regular meal.

### Tosafot Requirement

Tosafot adds a further layer: since what one is making up is an *achilah basukkah* — an eating that was required to take place in the *sukkah* — the *minei targima* used for the makeup must themselves be the type of foods that require a *sukkah*. Fruit, which by Tosafot's understanding does not require a *sukkah*, cannot serve as the *tashlumin* food. The *minei targima* must therefore be things like meat and cooked dishes — precisely those foods that Tosafot holds are categorically obligated in a *sukkah*. This again reflects Tosafot's position that there is a broader category of foods beyond bread and *mezonot* that require a *sukkah*.

### Braita Confirmation

*Tanya nami hachi*: a supporting *braitah* confirms that *im hishlim beminei targima yatza* — if one made up the missed meal with *minei targima*, one has fulfilled the obligation. Rashi's reading of this *braitah* is that *minei targima* clearly works, and *a fortiori* a full bread meal — *se'uda belebechem uvasar* — would certainly also work. This introduces some tension with the earlier discussion, where bread alone seemed insufficient to constitute a distinctive makeup. The *gemara* before had implied that the makeup must be distinct from a regular meal; the *braitah*'s language of *im hishlim* — "if one fulfilled it with" — sounds more like a standard that bread a *fortiori* exceeds rather than something that bread alone cannot satisfy.

## Agrippas Manager

### Single Meal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁאַל אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס שֶׁל אַגְרִיפַּס הַמֶּלֶךְ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כְּגוֹן אֲנִי, שֶׁאֵינִי רָגִיל לֶאֱכוֹל אֶלָּא סְעוּדָה אַחַת בַּיּוֹם, מַהוּ שֶׁאוֹכַל סְעוּדָה אַחַת וְאֶפָּטֵר? אָמַר לוֹ: בְּכׇל יוֹם וְיוֹם אַתָּה מַמְשִׁיךְ כַּמָּה פַּרְפְּרָאוֹת לִכְבוֹד עַצְמְךָ, וְעַכְשָׁיו אִי אַתָּה מַמְשִׁיךְ פַּרְפֶּרֶת אַחַת לִכְבוֹד קוֹנֶךָ?
>
> **Translation:** **The steward [*apotropos*] of King Agrippas asked Rabbi Eliezer: For** someone **like me, who is accustomed to eat only one meal a day, what is** the *halakha*? Is it sufficient **that I eat one meal** and **exempt** myself from the obligation to eat any more that day? Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: Each day you continue** eating **and taste various kinds of appetizers in deference to your own** desires, **and now you do not continue** eating even **one appetizer in deference to your Maker?**

*Sha'al apotropas shel Agrippas ha-melech et Rabbi Eliezer* — the manager of King Agrippas asked Rabbi Eliezer: a person such as himself, who is accustomed to eating only one meal a day, is being told by Rabbi Eliezer that he must eat two meals daily in the *sukkah* throughout *Sukkot*. *Mahu she-ochal se'uda achat ve-ipater* — may he fulfill the obligation with a single meal per day?

*Amar lo*: Rabbi Eliezer replies: every day you provide yourself with numerous delicacies — *parpara'ot* — for your own honor and pleasure. And now you cannot offer even one small delicacy — *parparet achat* — in honor of your Creator? The response is notable for two reasons. First, it confirms — consistent with Tosafot's reading — that Rabbi Eliezer did not retract his position about fourteen meals. Second, the concept of *parpara'ot* connects directly to the *minei targima* discussed earlier: the implication is that even without a full bread meal, one can count certain delicacies toward the fourteen, making the obligation somewhat more flexible in practice.

## Two Sukkot

### Splitting Meals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד שְׁאָלוֹ: כְּגוֹן אֲנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, אַחַת בִּטְבֶרְיָא וְאַחַת בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, וְיֵשׁ לִי שְׁתֵּי סוּכּוֹת אַחַת בִּטְבֶרְיָא וְאַחַת בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, מַהוּ שֶׁאֵצֵא מִסּוּכָּה לְסוּכָּה וְאֶפָּטֵר? אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא! שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַיּוֹצֵא מִסּוּכָּה לְסוּכָּה בִּטֵּל מִצְוָתָהּ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the steward **further asked** Rabbi Eliezer: **For** someone **like me, who has two wives, one in Tiberias and one in Tzippori, and has two *sukkot*, one in Tiberias and one in Tzippori, what** is the *halakha*? Can **I depart from** one ***sukka* to** another ***sukka* and exempt** myself from the obligation? In other words, is it permitted to fulfill the mitzva in one *sukka* for part of *Sukkot* and in another for the rest of the Festival? Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: No, as I say** that **anyone who departs from** one ***sukka* to** another ***sukka* has negated the mitzva of the first.** The obligation is to reside in the same *sukka* for all seven days.

The same manager poses a second question: he has two wives, one in Tiberias and one in Sepphoris, and correspondingly two *sukkot*, one in each city. *Mahu she-etzei mi-sukkah le-sukkah ve-ipater* — may he alternate between them, spending some nights in one *sukkah* and some in the other, and thereby fulfill his obligation?

*Amar lo*: Rabbi Eliezer says no. *She-ani omer kol ha-yotzei mi-sukkah le-sukkah biteil mitzvata shel rishona* — whoever leaves one *sukkah* for another has cancelled the *mitzvah* of the first one.

### One Bayit

The conceptual foundation of this ruling is essential to understanding Rabbi Eliezer's entire position. The *mitzvah* of *sukkah* is not merely a personal obligation to eat a certain number of meals in a *sukkah*-like structure. What makes the *sukkah* a legitimate fulfillment of the *mitzvah* is that it functions as one's *bayit* — one's actual house — for all seven days. A house is not a house if one uses it intermittently alongside another dwelling. If one splits one's presence between two *sukkot*, neither becomes one's *bayit*, and neither fulfills the *mitzvah*. The continuity of dwelling in a single structure for the entire duration of the festival is intrinsic to what the *sukkah* is supposed to be.

## Building Sukkah

### Chol Hamoed Building

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין יוֹצְאִין מִסּוּכָּה לְסוּכָּה, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין סוּכָּה בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יוֹצְאִין מִסּוּכָּה לְסוּכָּה, וְעוֹשִׂין סוּכָּה בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. וְשָׁוִין, שֶׁאִם נָפְלָה, שֶׁחוֹזֵר וּבוֹנָהּ בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: One** may **not depart from** one ***sukka* to** another ***sukka*;** he must reside in the same *sukka* for the entire Festival. **And one** may **not establish a *sukka* during the intermediate days of the Festival** if he failed to do so before the Festival. **And the Rabbis say: One** may **depart from** one ***sukka* to** another ***sukka*, and one** may **establish a *sukka* on the intermediate days of the Festival. And they** all, even Rabbi Eliezer, **agree that if** a *sukka* that one constructed before the Festival **collapsed, he** may **rebuild it during the intermediate days of the Festival.**

A *braitah* reinforces this principle. *Rabbi Eliezer omer*: *ein yotzin mi-sukkah le-sukkah* — one may not go from one *sukkah* to another — and *ein osin sukkah becholo shel mo'ed* — one may not construct a *sukkah* during *Chol HaMoed*. The reasoning follows directly: if the *sukkah* must be one's *bayit* for all seven days, beginning to build one's *sukkah* partway through the *chag* is self-defeating — it cannot be one's house for the full week and therefore cannot satisfy the *mitzvah*.

*Ve-Chachamim omrim*: the Sages permit both. One may go from *sukkah* to *sukkah*, and one may build a new *sukkah* during *Chol HaMoed*. Since the Sages reject the fourteen-meal framework and the requirement of continuous single-*sukkah* dwelling, neither restriction applies.

However — *ve-shavin* — both agree that *shenofla shechozer uvoneh bechol hamoed* — if a *sukkah* collapsed during *Chol HaMoed*, one may rebuild it. That rebuilding is considered a continuation of the original *sukkah* and not the construction of a new one.

### Ship Problem

This last point raises a fascinating conceptual question, one that echoes the ancient philosophical puzzle about the ship of Theseus — whether an object that has been entirely rebuilt from new components remains the same object. Tosafot holds that in order for the rebuilt *sukkah* to be considered the same *sukkah* in Rabbi Eliezer's terms, one must use the same wood that was used originally. If the original materials are replaced, the rebuilt structure is a new *sukkah*, not a continuation of the old one — and by Rabbi Eliezer's logic, it would fail the continuity requirement. Rashi, by contrast, seems to take the view that since the *sukkah* genuinely fell down, anything rebuilt on that conceptual foundation serves as a legitimate substitute, even if the wood differs or the location shifts slightly. The *sukkah*'s identity, on Rashi's reading, is tied more to the person's ongoing relationship with it than to the physical materials of which it happens to be composed.
