# Bava Kamma 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Toldot Regel Chatzi Nezek

### Chatzi Nezek Tzarorot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּחֲצִי נֶזֶק צְרוֹרוֹת, דְּהִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa was referring **to** one's liability to pay **half the** cost of the **damage** caused by **pebbles** in a case where they were inadvertently propelled by the foot of a walking animal, **which is a *halakha* learned through tradition** and not explicitly written in the Torah.

The Gemara picks up at the bottom of 3b, in the midst of Rav Pappa's discussion of whether the *toldot* of the *avot nezikin* are *keyotzei bahen* — whether the derivatives share the same legal characteristics as their parent categories. Rav Pappa's ruling was that most *toldot* are indeed *keyotzei bahen*, but there is one notable exception: *chatzi nezek tzarorot*, the half-damages owed when an animal, while walking, kicks up pebbles that in turn cause damage. This is the *toldah* of *regel*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לַהּ תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל? לְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if it is a unique halakhic category and the payment is different, **why is it characterized** as **a subcategory of Trampling?** The Gemara explains: It is due to the fact that it shares a common aspect with Trampling. One whose animal propels pebbles while it walks and thereby causes damage is liable **to pay** half of the damages **from** his **superior-quality** property, and he does not pay only from the body of his animal, as is the *halakha* concerning half the cost of the damage paid by the owner of an innocuous ox that gored a person or an animal.

The Gemara establishes: when did Rav Pappa say that *toldoteihem lav keyotzei bahen* — that some *toldot* are not like their *av*? It was specifically regarding the *toldah* of *regel*. But the Gemara immediately notes that we already established that the *toldah de-regel ke-regel* — the derivatives of *regel* are like *regel*. Whether an animal strikes something with its body as it walks, or with its tail, or in any analogous fashion, these are all governed by the same underlying characteristic: the animal caused damage *derech hilucho*, in the course of its ordinary movement, without *kavanato lehazik*, without any intent to damage, and full *nezek* is owed. So where is the exception — where is the case that is *lav keyotzei bahen*?

The Gemara answers: *be-chatzi nezek tzarorot de-hilkhata gemiri lah* — regarding *chatzi nezek tzarorot*, we learn through tradition, which is understood as *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, that only half damages are paid. The rationale is that while the animal is indeed walking — not intending damage, acting in the typical *regel* manner — the damage it causes is not through its body or anything attached to it, but through its *koach*: the indirect force by which it propels pebbles. This indirect mechanism is less connected to the animal than even the typical *regel* case. The pebbles were not part of the animal; they were set in motion incidentally, in a way analogous to what would be called *mitasek* if a human were involved. That attenuated causal link is why tradition establishes only half payment.

The Gemara then asks: *ve-amai kari lei toldah de-regel* — why is it called a *toldah* of *regel* specifically? What is the legal similarity that anchors it to *regel* rather than to *keren*, which also pays half? The answer is: *le-shalemi min ha-aliya* — that payment is made from the best of one's property, from one's general assets, not exclusively from the body of the damaging animal. This stands in contrast to the classic *chatzi nezek* of *keren* — the half-damages owed by a first-time goring — where the payment is *mi-gufo*, drawn only from the value of the animal itself.

### Rava's Query Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִבַּעְיָא בָּעֵי רָבָא – דְּבָעֵי רָבָא: חֲצִי נֶזֶק צְרוֹרוֹת – מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, אוֹ מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה מְשַׁלֵּם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rava raise a dilemma** with regard to that matter? **As Rava raised a dilemma:** With regard to one's liability to pay **half** the cost of **the damage** caused by **pebbles** propelled by the foot of his animal, does **one pay** damages exclusively **from** the **body** of his ox that caused the damage, **or** does **one pay** damages **from** his **superior-quality** property?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרָבָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, לְרַב פָּפָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Although **it is a dilemma for Rava, it is obvious to Rav Pappa** that one pays from his superior-quality property, and in that sense, he classified the pebbles that cause damage as a subcategory of Trampling.

The Gemara raises a complication. Rava had himself posed this question as an unresolved inquiry: *chatzi nezek tzarorot mi-gufo meshalem o min ha-aliya meshalem* — does the half-damage of *tzarorot* come from the animal's body, as with *keren*, or from the owner's general assets, as with *regel*? Given that *tzarorot* resembles *keren* in paying only half, perhaps the payment mechanism should also mirror *keren* — restricted to the body of the animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרָבָא דְּמִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, אַמַּאי קָרֵי לַהּ תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל? לְפוֹטְרָהּ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to Rava, who raises it as a dilemma, why is it characterized** as **a subcategory of Trampling** when its legal status is different both in terms of the extent of one's liability and in terms of the manner of payment? The Gemara explains: It is classified as a subcategory of Trampling in order **to exempt** from liability pebbles that cause damage **in the public domain,** just as damage in the category of Trampling is exempt in the public domain.

The Gemara resolves the apparent tension: *le-Rava de-mibaya lei, le-Rav Pappa peshita lei*. For Rav Pappa, there is no doubt — *chatzi nezek tzarorot* pays from the *aliya*. For Rava, the matter was genuinely uncertain. The Gemara accordingly asks: according to Rava, who was uncertain whether payment comes from the body of the animal, what is the basis for calling this a *toldah de-regel* at all? If *chatzi nezek tzarorot* combines two features that parallel *keren* — paying half and potentially paying only from the animal's body — then in what meaningful legal sense is it affiliated with *regel*?

### Reshut HaRabim Exemption

The answer the Gemara provides is: *liphtor bir'shus harabbim* — to teach that *chatzi nezek tzarorot* is exempt in the public domain. This is one of the most consequential distinctions between *regel* and *keren*. Since *keren* represents intentional damage, the owner bears responsibility for his animal's goring regardless of where it occurs; one cannot excuse willful violence on the grounds that it happened in a public space. *Regel* and *shen*, by contrast, are the natural, unremarkable behaviors of an animal going about its way. It would be an undue and impractical burden to hold owners liable whenever an animal in the public thoroughfare bumps into something or eats stray food. The animal's owner simply cannot be held to that standard in *reshut ha-rabim*.

Accordingly, the exemption in *reshut ha-rabim* marks *chatzi nezek tzarorot* as belonging to the *regel* family: even if there is uncertainty about whether payment comes from the body of the animal, the category is anchored to *regel* because it is exempt in the public domain.

### Peshiat HaNizak

This exemption can be understood through two complementary framings, which are two sides of the same coin. One framing is that in *reshut ha-rabim*, where animals constantly pass through, it is the responsibility of those who place their property in the public domain to protect it from the inevitable jostling of traffic. The other framing is *peshiat ha-nizak* — the negligence of the damaged party. If you leave your property exposed in a thoroughfare, the damage that results is attributable to your own failure to protect it, not to the owner of the passing animal. Who bears the duty of care determines liability, and in *reshut ha-rabim*, that duty rests with the one whose property was exposed.

Tosafot notes an additional point: since *chatzi nezek tzarorot* is already treated leniently — paying only half — it would be internally inconsistent to then be *stricter* and impose liability in *reshut ha-rabim*. The entire orientation of the rule is toward leniency, and so the *reshut ha-rabim* exemption follows naturally.

In sum: there is one case where a *toldah* is *lav keyotzei bahen* — the animal walking, incidentally propelling pebbles, causing damage. It is a *toldah de-regel* because the manner of damage is that of *regel*, and because it is exempt in *reshut ha-rabim* like *regel*. But it is *lav keyotzei bahen* in that only half damages are owed.

## Avot Nezikin Framework

### Torah Sources Overview

Before continuing, it is worth pausing to survey what the Gemara has accomplished across the opening pages of the tractate. The initial question — whether *toldot* are *keyotzei bahen* — was not the most pressing of inquiries; one would naturally assume they were, and indeed it takes considerable effort to identify even a single exception. But that question served as a vehicle for introducing and conceptually mapping all of the *avot nezikin*. Through it, the Gemara surveyed each category's *pasuk* source and its defining conceptual logic.

The *mishna* opens with *shor*, and within *shor* we examined *keren*, *shen*, and *regel* — the three modes of ox-damage — identifying the *pesukim* underpinning each. *Keren* is explicit in the Torah; *shen* and *regel* are derived from the phrase *ve-shilach et be'iro u-vi'er*. We then examined *bor*, characterized by the Gemara as *techilas asiyaso le-nezek* — from its very inception it was a hazard, a potential source of harm. And we discussed *maveh* — whose identity is yet to be resolved — and then *hav'er* and *esh*, the categories of fire.

### Three Characteristics

The three conceptual categories that emerged from that analysis are central to the entire tractate. *Keren* is *kavanato lehazik* — the animal's intent is to cause harm. *Regel* is characterized by *hezeiko matzui* — prevalent damage — or more precisely, *derech hilucho*, damage that occurs in the course of ordinary movement, incidentally, without intent to cause harm. *Shen* is *hana'ah lehizko* — the animal derives direct benefit through the very act that causes damage; its intent is not to harm but to enjoy, yet it is the eating itself that constitutes the damage.

These are not merely descriptions of which body part does the damage. They are conceptual categories. A case where the animal uses, say, its body incidentally while walking still falls under *regel* if the underlying characteristic is *derech hilucho* and *ein kavanato lehazik*. And one can ask what happens when multiple characteristics overlap — a question that remains for later discussion.

### Vemamoncha Role

Throughout the Gemara's treatment of why the owner is liable when his property damages, a phrase recurs: *u-mamoncha u-shmirasan alecha* — it is your property, and watching it is your responsibility. The Gemara applied this phrase to each *av*: *keren* is *kavanato lehazik u-mamoncha u-shmirasan alecha*; *esh* is *koach acher me'urav bo u-mamoncha u-shmirasan alecha*, and so forth. The *mishna* itself, in articulating the *tzad ha-shaveh*, the common denominator, uses the phrase *she-darkan lehazik u-shmirasan alecha*, replacing the specific characteristic of each category with the shared feature of propensity to damage, alongside the owner's duty of care.

The Gemara, however, consistently inserted the word *mamoncha* — it is your property — which raises a central question that will recur throughout the tractate. Is the obligation of the *avot nezikin* grounded in the fact that *your property* caused the damage? And if so, how do we conceptualize *bor* — a pit dug in a public domain — or *esh* — a fire that spreads — as belonging to you in any meaningful sense? The Gemara suggests that even in those cases, if you are the one who created the hazard or caused the spread, it is conceptually treated as yours. But whether that ownership-basis is genuinely central, or whether the obligation is better framed around negligence, is a question the word *mamoncha* brings to the fore. Tosafot raises the question of whether *mamoncha* was even in the *mishna*'s text at all — and that textual question is itself a lens on the conceptual one.

## Maveh Dispute

### Rav Maveh Adam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מַבְעֶה״? רַב אָמַר: מַבְעֶה זֶה אָדָם, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַבְעֶה זֶה הַשֵּׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***Maveh*? Rav says: *Maveh*, that** is the category of **Man,** which includes a person who causes damage, but not the property or animal of a person that causes damage. **And Shmuel says: *Maveh*, this** is **the** category of **Eating.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר מַבְעֶה זֶה אָדָם, דִּכְתִיב: ״אָמַר שֹׁמֵר אָתָא בֹקֶר וְגַם לָיְלָה, אִם תִּבְעָיוּן בְּעָיוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Rav says: *Maveh*, this** is the category of **Man, as it is written: "The watchman said: Morning has come, and also night; if you will inquire [*tivayun*], inquire [*be'ayu*]"** (Isaiah 21:12). The terms "*tivayun*" and "*be'ayu*," meaning inquire, refer to a person, who is able to inquire. Accordingly, *Maveh* relates to an action performed by a person.

The Gemara now turns to the mysterious term *maveh*, which appears in the *mishna* alongside *hav'er* — fire — as one of the four *avot nezikin*. *Hav'er* is recognizable as fire, but *maveh* requires interpretation. *Rav amar: maveh zeh adam* — Rav says *maveh* refers to a human being, the tortfeasor who is himself present and causing damage.

Rav derives this from the *pasuk* in Isaiah: *amar shomer ata voker v'gam layla im tivayun be'ayu* — the watchman said, the morning has come and also the night; if you would inquire, inquire. The act of inquiry — *be'ayu* — is a distinctly human activity, and from this verbal root Rav extracts *maveh* as a reference to the human agent.

### Shmuel Maveh Shen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר מַבְעֶה זֶה הַשֵּׁן, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֵיךְ נֶחְפְּשׂוּ עֵשָׂו, נִבְעוּ מַצְפֻּנָיו״. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? כְּדִמְתַרְגֵּם רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵיכְדֵין אִיתְבְּלֵישׁ עֵשָׂו, אִתְגַּלִּין מַטְמֹרוֹהִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: *Maveh*, this is the** category of **Eating, as it is written: "How is Esau searched out [*neḥpesu*]; how are his hidden places revealed [*nivu*]"** (Obadiah 1:6)? The terms *nivu* and *maveh* share a common root. The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred** that *nivu* connotes Eating? The Gemara explains that it is **as Rav Yosef translates** the verse into Aramaic: **How is Esau searched out [*itbelish*]; how are his hidden places revealed [*itgaleyan*]?** The meaning of the Aramaic term *itgaleyan* means revealed, and that is the meaning of the Hebrew term "*nivu*." *Maveh* references the animal's teeth, which are covered when its mouth is closed and revealed when it eats.

Shmuel disagrees: *maveh zeh ha-shen* — *maveh* means the tooth, i.e., the category of *shen*, an animal eating and causing damage through consumption. Shmuel derives this from the *pasuk* in Obadiah: *eich nichpesu Eisav, niv'u mitzpunav* — how has Esav been searched out, how have his hidden places been revealed? The tooth is sometimes hidden, sometimes revealed, and the verbal root *niv'u* — revealed — connects to *maveh*.

### Grammatical Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כִּשְׁמוּאֵל? אָמַר לָךְ: מִי קָתָנֵי ״נִבְעֶה״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rav, what is the reason** that **he did not say** that *Maveh* refers to Eating, **as** does **Shmuel?** The Gemara explains that Rav could have **said to you: Is it taught** in the mishna: ***Niveh*?** *Niveh* is the passive form of the word *maveh*, which would be the appropriate form if the reference was to teeth that are revealed. The term in the mishna is *maveh*, an active form of the verb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב? אָמַר לְךָ: מִי קָתָנֵי ״בּוֹעֶה״?
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Shmuel, what is the reason** that **he did not say** that *Maveh* refers to the category of Man, **as** does **Rav?** The Gemara explains that Shmuel could have **said to you: Is it taught** in the mishna: ***Bo'eh*?** *Bo'eh* is a form of the word *maveh* connoting an action taken directly, which would be appropriate were the reference to one's actions that cause damage. The term in the mishna is *maveh*, which connotes an action that one causes another to take.

The Gemara probes the grammatical argument between the two positions. Rav challenges Shmuel's reading: if the *pasuk* referred to the thing that is revealed — i.e., the tooth that becomes visible — it would read *niv'eh*, the passive form, the thing revealed. But *maveh* is an active construction, suggesting the actor — the one doing the revealing, the one who inquires. That fits *adam*, the human agent, not *shen*.

Shmuel responds in kind: if the *pasuk* were about one who inquires — as Rav claims — it should read *bo'eh*, the active participle. *Maveh* does not naturally carry that meaning. The Gemara then steps back and notes that *michdi kra lo kemar dayek velo kemar dayek* — the *pasuk* does not come down decisively for either reading. The grammatical arguments are suggestive but not definitive, so the Gemara moves to the underlying jurisprudential reasoning.

### Rav Shor Inclusive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי קְרָאֵי לָא כְּמַר דָּיְיקִי וְלָא כְּמַר דָּיְיקִי, רַב מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כִּשְׁמוּאֵל? תְּנָא שׁוֹר – וְכׇל מִילֵּי דְשׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **After all, the** meaning of the term in the **verses is not precise according to** one **Sage,** Rav, **and** it **is not precise according to** the other **Sage,** Shmuel. It is apparent that their dispute is not based upon their interpretation of the verses; it must be based on another issue. The Gemara asks again: As for **Rav, what is the reason** that **he did not say** that *Maveh* refers to Eating, **as** does **Shmuel?** The Gemara explains: Rav holds that the *tanna* **teaches** the primary category of **Ox** in the mishna. This includes damage caused by an ox **and all matters** involving damaging actions **that** are completed by **an ox,** i.e., the actions included in the categories of Goring, Eating, and Trampling. Perforce, *Maveh* refers to a different category of damage, i.e., Man.

Why did Rav not accept Shmuel's identification of *maveh* with *shen*? Because *tana shor ve-chol milei de-shor* — once the *mishna* says *shor*, the ox, that term is comprehensive. It encompasses all the forms of ox-damage: *keren*, *shen*, and *regel*. If *shor* already includes *shen*, there is no need for *maveh* to duplicate it. *Maveh* must therefore refer to something not already covered by *shor*, and that is *adam*.

### Shmuel Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי, הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ שׁוֹר! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: תְּנָא שׁוֹר – לְקַרְנוֹ, וּמַבְעֶה – לְשִׁינּוֹ. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא רְאִי הַקֶּרֶן – שֶׁאֵין הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ, כִּרְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁיֵּשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Shmuel too,** who interpreted *Maveh* as Eating, **doesn't** the mishna **teach** the category of **Ox,** which should include all matters involving damaging actions that are completed by an ox? **Rav Yehuda said** that according to Shmuel, the *tanna* **teaches Ox** specifically with regard **to** damage caused with **its horn, and** it teaches ***Maveh*** with regard **to** damage caused with **its tooth. And** when the mishna contrasts Ox with *Maveh*, **this is** what the *tanna* **is saying:** The defining **characteristic** of **the** primary category of **Goring, where there is no** inherent **pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage, is not similar to** the defining **characteristic** of **the** category of **Eating, where there is pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage.**

Shmuel's response, attributed to Rav Yehuda, is that *tana shor le-karno maveh le-shino* — *shor* in the *mishna* refers specifically to *keren*, goring, the paradigmatic ox-damage; and *maveh* refers to *shen*, eating. This is because in the Torah's cases involving an ox, the overwhelming focus is on goring. *Shen* and *regel* are not explicitly named; they are derived. When one thinks of an ox causing damage, the image that comes to mind is *keren*. So *shor* in the *mishna* means *keren*, and *maveh* supplies *shen*.

## Shmuel Mishnah Read

### Regel Propensity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: תְּנָא שׁוֹר – לְרַגְלוֹ, וּמַבְעֶה – לְשִׁינּוֹ. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא רְאִי הָרֶגֶל – שֶׁהֶזֵּיקָהּ מָצוּי, כִּרְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁאֵין הֶזֵּיקָהּ מָצוּי; וְלֹא רְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁיֵּשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ, כִּרְאִי הָרֶגֶל – שֶׁאֵין הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that according to Shmuel, the *tanna* **teaches Ox** specifically with regard **to** actions that cause damage with **its foot and** it teaches ***Maveh*** with regard **to** actions that cause damage with **its tooth. And** when the mishna contrasts Ox with *Maveh* **this is** what the *tanna* **is saying:** The defining **characteristic** of **the** primary category of **Trampling, where its damage is commonplace, is not similar to** the defining **characteristic of the** primary category of **Eating, where its damage is not commonplace, and** the defining **characteristic** of **the** primary category of **Eating, where there is pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage, is not similar to** the **characteristic of the** primary category of **Trampling, where there is no pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage.**

Having established that according to Shmuel, *shor* represents *keren* and *maveh* represents *shen*, the Gemara now explains how the *mishna*'s *lo rei zeh ke-rei zeh* formula operates — how each category has a strength the other lacks, making both necessary.

The *mishna* compares *shor* and *maveh* and states that neither is like the other, yet both share the characteristic that they possess *ruach chayim* — living breath. Under Shmuel's reading, *shor* is *keren* and *maveh* is *shen*, and the comparative formula works as follows. The strength of *regel* — and in Rava's later rereading under Shmuel's framework, *shor* will come to mean *regel* — is that the damage is *matzuyah*, prevalent. The animal is constantly walking; damage is an inevitable byproduct of ordinary motion. That propensity is what makes the owner liable.

The strength of *shen* is different: *sheyesh hana'ah lehizko* — the animal derives direct benefit from the very act that causes damage. It is not merely that damage is a probable side effect of normal behavior; it is that the animal's desired goal — eating — is itself the damage to the other person's property. The benefit to the animal is inseparable from the harm to the victim.

Because each category has a distinct logical basis for liability, neither can be derived from the other. Had the Torah stated only *regel*, one could not have concluded *shen*: *regel* imposes liability because damage is so frequent and inevitable, but *shen*, which damages only when the animal actively desires to eat, might not be covered. And had the Torah stated only *shen*, one could not have concluded *regel*: *shen* imposes liability because the animal benefits from the very damage it causes — a direct, motivated connection — but *regel*, which causes damage incidentally without any benefit to the animal, might not be covered.

The Gemara had initially formulated the *lo rei* in the reversed order — stating as the distinguishing quality of *keren* that it lacks *hana'ah*, and of *shen* that it lacks *kavanato lehazik* — and the Gemara challenged this with a *kal va-chomer*: if you're *chayav* by *shen*, which lacks *kavanato lehazik*, surely you're *chayav* by *keren*, which *does* have *kavanato lehazik*? The defense offered was: perhaps by *keren*, *kavanato lehazik* is actually a reason to be *patur*, analogous to the case of slaves, where even though they damage intentionally, the master is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא רְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁאֵין כַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק, כִּרְאִי הַקֶּרֶן – שֶׁכַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק; וְלֹא רְאִי הַקֶּרֶן – שֶׁאֵין הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ, כִּרְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁיֵּשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the defining **characteristic [*re'i*] of the** category of **Eating, where it is not the objective of** the animal **to cause damage, is not similar to** the defining **characteristic of the** category of **Goring, where the objective** of the animal **is to cause damage. And** the **characteristic of the** primary category of **Eating, where there is pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage, is not similar to** the **characteristic of the** primary category of **Goring, where there is no pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage.**

The Gemara initially attempts to preserve this logic: *itztarich* — the exemption of slaves is indeed relevant. Even though *aved* and *amah* have *kavanato lehazik*, the master is *patur*, because if masters were liable for their slaves' intentional acts, a resentful slave could ruin his master by deliberately destroying a neighbor's property and forcing the master to pay. That practical concern is unique to slaves who possess genuine human will and can act with strategic intent against their owner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַטּוּ עֶבֶד וְאָמָה לָאו טַעְמָא רַבָּה אִית בְּהוּ? שֶׁמָּא יַקְנִיטֶנּוּ רַבּוֹ, וְיֵלֵךְ וְיַדְלִיק גְּדִישׁוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ, וְנִמְצָא זֶה מְחַיֵּיב אֶת רַבּוֹ מֵאָה מָנֶה בְּכׇל יוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Is that to say** that **in** a case where one's **slave or maidservant** causes damage **there is not a substantial reason** to exempt the master? In that case there is concern that **perhaps his master will provoke him and** in retribution **he will go and set fire to another's stack** of grain, **and it is found** that **this** slave **renders his master liable** to pay **one hundred *maneh*,** ten thousand dinars, **each day.** Therefore, there is no basis for the notion that an ox that gores would be exempt just as a slave is exempt, as perhaps, fundamentally, one is liable to pay for damage caused by his slave. A master is exempt from payment to prevent a situation where a slave would exploit that situation to take revenge against his master.

Rav Ashi, however, objects: *atu aved ve-amah lav ta'ama rabba it behu* — is it not the case that there is a compelling, specific reason for the slave exemption, unrelated to the general principle of *kavanato lehazik*? The exemption exists precisely because of *shemma yaknitenu rabbo* — lest the master provoke the slave. That reason is unique to slaves with human agency and strategic motivation, not to animals that simply gore. Accordingly, the Gemara concedes and reverses the *lo rei* formula to its correct form: the strength of *keren* is *kavanato lehazik*, and the strength of *shen* is *hana'ah lehizko*, each stated as the positive reason for liability, each lacking in the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פָּרֵיךְ הָכִי: לֹא רְאִי הַקֶּרֶן – שֶׁכַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק, כִּרְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁאֵין כַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק; וְלֹא רְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁיֵּשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ, כִּרְאִי הַקֶּרֶן – שֶׁאֵין הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the mishna **refutes** any possibility to derive the *halakhot* of Ox from *Maveh* or *Maveh* from Ox in **this** manner: The **characteristic of the** category of **Goring, where** the **objective** of the animal **is to cause damage, is not similar to** the **characteristic of the** category of **Eating, where it is not the objective of** the animal **to cause damage, and** the **characteristic of the** category of **Eating, where there is pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage, is not similar to** the **characteristic of the** category of **Goring, where there is no pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage.**

It is worth noting a deeper question that Tosafot raises at this point. The Gemara has been framing liability in terms of the severity of the damaging act itself. But one can ask: should the focus be on the act as performed by the animal, or on the negligence of the owner? If the frame is the owner's negligence, then *kavanato lehazik* might actually *reduce* the owner's liability — because an animal that intends to damage is more dangerous and harder to restrain, whereas an animal that merely grazes without malice is easily watched, and failure to watch it reflects greater negligence. This question — whether we are analyzing the act of damage or the degree of the owner's negligence — is central to the conceptual structure of all *ממון המזיק* liability.

### Keren Absence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֶגֶל מַאי? שַׁיְּירֵיהּ?! ״כְּשֶׁהִזִּיק חָב הַמַּזִּיק״ – לְאֵתוֹיֵי רֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** According to Rav Yehuda's explanation of Shmuel's opinion, among the primary categories of damage caused by an ox, the mishna mentions only Goring and Eating. The Gemara asks: **But what** of the category of **Trampling;** did the *tanna* **omit it** from the mishna? The Gemara answers: The principle stated in general terms at the end of the mishna: **And when** a component of any of these categories **causes damage, the** owner or generator of the component that **caused damage is obligated** to remit payments of restitution for damage with best-quality land, serves **to include** the primary category of **Trampling.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנְיֵיהּ בְּהֶדְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the intent of the *tanna* is to teach the primary category of Trampling as well, **let him teach it explicitly.** Accordingly, Rav Yehuda's explanation of how Shmuel interprets the mishna is rejected.

This beautiful reading of the *mishna* — *shor* as *keren* and *maveh* as *shen* — runs into a difficulty: *ve-regel mai shirei* — what happened to *regel*? The *mishna* pairs *shor* and *maveh* and notes their common denominator, but under this reading *regel* has been left out entirely. The Gemara suggests that a subsequent *mishna* — the one that states the general rule *ke-she-hizik chav ha-mazik* — comes to include *regel*. But the obvious question is: if *regel* is a full-fledged *av*, why was it omitted from the principal *mishna* listing the *avot nezikin*?

This question proves fatal to the reading. Rather than defend the exclusion of *regel* from the primary *mishna*, the Gemara abandons the identification of *shor* with *keren* and moves to Rava's alternative reading of Shmuel's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: תְּנָא שׁוֹר – לְרַגְלוֹ, וּמַבְעֶה – לְשִׁינּוֹ. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא רְאִי הָרֶגֶל – שֶׁהֶזֵּיקָהּ מָצוּי, כִּרְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁאֵין הֶזֵּיקָהּ מָצוּי; וְלֹא רְאִי הַשֵּׁן – שֶׁיֵּשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ, כִּרְאִי הָרֶגֶל – שֶׁאֵין הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that according to Shmuel, the *tanna* **teaches Ox** specifically with regard **to** actions that cause damage with **its foot and** it teaches ***Maveh*** with regard **to** actions that cause damage with **its tooth. And** when the mishna contrasts Ox with *Maveh* **this is** what the *tanna* **is saying:** The defining **characteristic** of **the** primary category of **Trampling, where its damage is commonplace, is not similar to** the defining **characteristic of the** primary category of **Eating, where its damage is not commonplace, and** the defining **characteristic** of **the** primary category of **Eating, where there is pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage, is not similar to** the **characteristic of the** primary category of **Trampling, where there is no pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage.**

*Ella amar Rava: tana shor le-raglo u-maveh le-shino* — rather, says Rava, *shor* in the *mishna* represents *regel*, the ordinary locomotion of the ox, and *maveh* represents *shen*. Under this reading, the *mishna* pairs the two categories that are most naturally associated with ordinary animal behavior — walking and eating — both exempt in *reshut ha-rabim*, both derived from the same *pasuk*, and both characterized by the absence of *kavanato lehazik*.

Under Rava's reading for Shmuel, the respective strengths are reformulated: the strength of *regel* is *matzuyah* — damage is very prevalent, as the animal is constantly in motion — whereas *shen*'s strength is *hana'ah lehizko*, the animal's direct benefit from the act of damage. Neither can be derived from the other, and both are required. This is how the *lo rei* formula of the *mishna* operates according to Shmuel.

## Adam Exclusion

### Nizkei Mamon Gufo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקֶרֶן מַאי? שַׁיְּירֵיהּ?! ״כְּשֶׁהִזִּיק חָב הַמַּזִּיק״ – לְאֵתוֹיֵי קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** According to Rav Yehuda's explanation of Shmuel's opinion, among the primary categories of damage with regard to an ox, the mishna mentions only Goring and Trampling. The Gemara asks: **But what** of the primary category of **Goring;** did the *tanna* **omit it** from the mishna? The Gemara answers: The principle stated in general terms at the end of the mishna: **And when** a component of any of these categories **causes damage, the** owner or generator of the component that **caused damage is obligated** to remit payments of restitution for damage with best-quality land, serves **to include** the primary category of **Goring.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנְיֵיהּ בְּהֶדְיָא! בְּמוּעָדִין מִתְּחִילָּתָן קָמַיְירֵי, בְּתַמִּין וּלְבַסּוֹף מוּעָדִין לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the intent of the *tanna* is to teach the primary category of Goring as well, **let him teach it explicitly.** The Gemara explains: It is **with regard to** those categories of damage where the animal is **forewarned from the outset** and the owner is liable to pay for the entire damage the first time his animal causes damage that the *tanna* of the mishna **speaks;** but **with regard to** those categories of damage where the animal is initially **innocuous** and the owner is liable to pay only half the cost of the damage caused by his animal, **and** the animal is **ultimately forewarned,** the *tanna* of the mishna **does not speak** of them. With regard to Goring, the first three times an ox gores a person or an animal the owner of the ox pays only half of the damages. This is because an ox is considered forewarned with regard to damage categorized as Goring only after it attacks three times.

But now the Gemara is left with the same structural problem on the other side: *ve-keren mai shirei* — and what happened to *keren*? Rava's answer is that the subsequent *mishna* stating *ke-she-hizik chav ha-mazik* comes to include *keren*. The Gemara asks why not include *keren* explicitly in the first *mishna*, and the answer is: *be-muadim mi-techilasan ka-miyri, be-tamim u-le-sof muadim lo ka-miyri* — the *mishna* is discussing categories that are *muad* from the outset, meaning they pay full damages from the beginning; it is not discussing *tamim* cases that only later become *muad*. *Keren*, which pays only half in its initial tamim state, does not belong in this *mishna*. This exclusion, far from being a deficiency, reveals a powerful conceptual coherence to Shmuel's reading of the *mishna*: it contains only *nizkei mamon* — property damages — and only categories that are *muad* from the start.

### Rav Adam Included

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב? אָמַר לָךְ: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָדָם, הָא קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: שׁוֹר הַמּוּעָד, וְשׁוֹר הַמַּזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק, וְהָאָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** After addressing the reason that Rav did not say that *Maveh* refers to Eating in accordance with the explanation of Shmuel, the Gemara asks: **And** as for **Shmuel, what is the reason** that **he did not say** that *Maveh* refers to Man, **as** does **Rav?** Shmuel could have **said to you, if it enters your mind** to say that *Maveh* is **Man, isn't it taught in the latter clause** of the mishna (15b): Both **a forewarned ox, and an ox that causes damage in the domain of the injured** party, **and** any damage caused by **man?** Apparently, Man was mentioned in the latter clause of the mishna because it was not mentioned in the first clause.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי בְּרֵישָׁא! בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹן קָמַיְירֵי, בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the *tanna* **teach** Man among the primary categories of damage **in the first clause.** The Gemara explains: It is **with regard to** those categories of **damage** caused by one's **property** that the *tanna* of the mishna **speaks;** but **with regard to** the category of **damage** caused by **one's body,** the category of Man, the *tanna* of the mishna **does not speak.**

The Gemara now turns to examine Rav's reading, which holds that *maveh* is *adam*, the human tortfeasor, and that *shor* encompasses all three aspects of ox-damage. The question is then: according to Shmuel, why not read *maveh* as Rav does, as *adam*? Shmuel answers: *ha-ketani seifa: shor ha-muad ve-shor ha-mazik be-reshut ha-nizak ve-ha-adam* — a later *mishna* explicitly lists *adam* among those who pay full damages at the outset. Shmuel does not want *adam* to appear in the first *mishna* when it already appears — more appropriately, in his view — in the later one.

The Gemara presses: but why couldn't *adam* appear in both? And Shmuel's deeper answer is: *be-nizkei mamon ka-miyri, be-nizkei gufo lo ka-miyri* — the first *mishna* is discussing damage to property, not damage by a person's own body. When a human being personally causes harm, that is a conceptually distinct category. The *mishna*'s subject is *mamon ha-mazik* — one's property causing damage — not one's own person doing so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נָמֵי, הָא קָתָנֵי אָדָם בְּסֵיפָא! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: הָהוּא לְמֶחְשְׁבֵיהּ בַּהֲדֵי מוּעָדִין הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rav, too,** the same difficulty arises: **Isn't** damage caused by a **man taught in the latter clause** of the mishna? The Gemara answers: **Rav** could have **said to you: That** mention of damage caused by a man in the latter clause **comes to enumerate** that damage **together with** the other forms of damage in a case where the owner of the cause of damage or the generator of that damage is **forewarned.**

Rav, for his part, has no difficulty with *adam* appearing in both *mishnayot*. When *adam* appears later in the *mishna* listing those who pay full damages, it is not introducing the category anew — *adam* was already introduced as an *av nezikin* in the first *mishna*. The later *mishna* is merely restating the case in the context of listing full-payment categories; it adds no new information about what is *chayav*, only about the measure of payment.

### Shor Darko Lehazik

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי שׁוֹר דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַזִּיק?! בְּמוּעָד. וּמוּעָד – דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַזִּיק?! כֵּיוָן דְּאִיַּיעַד – אוֹרְחֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav's understanding that Ox, as mentioned in the mishna, includes all actions an ox performs that cause damage, including goring, based on the continuation of the mishna: **But is it the** typical **manner of an ox to cause damage** by goring? The Gemara answers: The statement in the mishna is **with regard to a forewarned** ox. The Gemara asks: **But is it the** typical **manner of a forewarned** ox **to cause damage** by goring? The Gemara answers: Yes, **once it was forewarned** after goring repeatedly, **its** typical **manner is** to cause damage by goring.

The Gemara then raises a pointed question that applies to Rav's reading in particular: *ve-chi shor darko lehazik* — is it really the way of an ox to cause damage? The *tzad ha-shaveh* of the *mishna* states *she-darkan lehazik*, that it is their way to damage. For Shmuel, who excludes *keren* from the *mishna*, this phrase applies comfortably to *shen* and *regel*, which are the natural, constant behaviors of an animal. But for Rav, *shor* includes *keren* — intentional goring — which is decidedly not the ordinary way of an animal. Goring is exceptional, aberrant behavior.

The Gemara suggests: *be-muad* — the *mishna* is referring to the case of a *muad*, an animal that has been warned three times. At that point, surely, goring has become its established pattern. But the Gemara does not let this pass easily: *u-muad darko lehazik* — even a *muad*, is goring really its *derech*? This is a striking moment. The Gemara entertains the possibility that even three incidents of goring do not make goring the animal's normal behavior; it remains an exceptional act. Ultimately the Gemara affirms: *keivan de-i'ad orchei hu* — once it has been established through repeated incidents, that is now considered its *derech*. But the question itself reveals that the category of *keren*, even as a *muad*, sits uneasily alongside *shen* and *regel* in a *mishna* premised on *darkan lehazik*.

### Muad Question

A parallel question arises regarding *adam*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָדָם דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַזִּיק?! בְּיָשֵׁן. יָשֵׁן דַּרְכּוֹ לְהַזִּיק?! כֵּיוָן דְּכָיֵיף וּפָשֵׁיט – אוֹרְחֵיהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav's understanding that *Maveh* is the primary category of Man: Is it **man's** typical **manner to cause damage?** The Gemara answers: The statement in the mishna is **referring to** the damage one causes while **sleeping.** The Gemara asks: **Is it the** typical **manner of** a person **to cause damage** while **sleeping?** The Gemara answers: **Since** one **contracts and extends** his limbs while sleeping, **it is his** typical **manner** to damage objects placed next to him.

*Adam darko lehazik* — does a person go about normally causing damage? The Gemara answers *be-yashen* — when asleep. A sleeping person tosses and turns and can damage things nearby. The Gemara presses further: *yashen darko lehazik* — is that really a person's ordinary way? And the Gemara replies: *keivan de-kayif u-fashit orchei hu* — since one stretches out and moves in one's sleep, yes, there is a genuine propensity to cause inadvertent damage in that state.

### Shmiratan Alecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ״?! אָדָם – שְׁמִירַת גּוּפוֹ עָלָיו הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** If *Maveh* is referring to Man, is it correct to state, as the mishna does in its enumeration of common denominators of the primary categories: **And** responsibility for **their safeguarding** is incumbent **upon you?** That formulation is appropriate in cases where one is safeguarding another person, animal, or item. When applied to the primary category of Man, it would indicate that responsibility to safeguard one person is incumbent upon another person. With regard to **man,** responsibility for **safeguarding his body is** incumbent **upon him** alone, not upon anyone else.

The *mishna*'s *tzad ha-shaveh* then says *u-shmiratan alecha* — watching them is your responsibility. The Gemara observes that this phrase is awkward when applied to *adam*. The formulation *shmiratan alecha* implies that the hazard is something external to the person, something one must watch over as a separate entity. When the tortfeasor *is* the person himself, the more appropriate phrase would be *shmirat gufo alav* — the responsibility to watch his own person rests upon himself.

### Rabbi Abahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְּתָנֵי קַרְנָא: אַרְבָּעָה אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין, וְאָדָם אֶחָד מֵהֶן; אָדָם – שְׁמִירַת גּוּפוֹ עָלָיו הוּא! אֶלָּא כְּדַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ לְתַנָּא: תְּנִי: אָדָם – שְׁמִירַת גּוּפוֹ עָלָיו;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning,** that the formulation: And their safeguarding is incumbent upon you, indicates that *Maveh* is not Man, there is a difficulty with **that which** the Sage **Karna taught** in a *baraita*: There are **four primary categories of damage, and Man is one of them.** That *baraita* continues and states that one common denominator is: Their safeguarding is incumbent upon you. With regard to that *baraita*, the same question arises: With regard to **Man,** responsibility for **safeguarding his body is** incumbent **upon him,** not upon anyone else, and the wording of Karna is imprecise. **Rather,** just **as Rabbi Abbahu said to the *tanna*,** who was reciting the *baraita* in the study hall: **Teach** the *baraita*: And with regard to **Man,** responsibility for **safeguarding his body is** incumbent **upon him;**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, תְּנִי: אָדָם – שְׁמִירַת גּוּפוֹ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **here too, teach** the mishna: And with regard to **man,** responsibility for **safeguarding his body is** incumbent **upon him.**

The Gemara resolves this by citing Karna's *brayta*: *arbah avot nezikin ve-adam echad mehen*, a *brayta* that lists *adam* among the *avot nezikin* and explicitly uses the phrase *adam shmirat gufo alav* — confirming that the appropriate formulation for the human tortfeasor is indeed that one bears responsibility for one's own body, not the general *shmiratan alecha* formula.

Rabbi Abahu teaches explicitly: the *tanna* who included *adam* in the *mishna* would not use *shmiratan alecha* when describing *adam*, but rather *shmirat gufo alav* — confirming that the appropriate formulation for the human tortfeasor is indeed that one bears responsibility for one's own body, not the general *shmiratan alecha* formula. The phrase *shmiratan alecha* in the *mishna*'s *tzad ha-shaveh* describes all the other cases — ox, pit, fire — where there is something external that one must supervise. When *adam* is described, the formulation shifts: you are responsible for yourself. So the *mishna*'s general *tzad ha-shaveh* phrase, while not perfectly calibrated to *adam*, is understood to carry that adjusted meaning when applied to it.

What this entire discussion brings into relief is the conceptual tension in Rav's reading. Shmuel's exclusion of *keren* and *adam* from the primary *mishna* preserves a tight, coherent category: things that are one's property, that have a genuine *darkan lehazik*, that are *muad* from the outset, and for which the responsibility-to-watch rationale applies naturally. Rav's inclusion of *adam* — and *keren* — forces one to extend the concepts of *darkan lehazik* and *shmiratan alecha* considerably. The notion that a human being failing to watch himself is analogous to a negligent animal owner is, by any measure, a striking claim. Yet that is the implication of Rav's reading that *maveh* is *adam*.

## Maveh Alternatives

### Water Suggestion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב מָרִי, וְאֵימָא ״מַבְעֶה״ זֶה הַמַּיִם, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״כִּקְדֹחַ אֵשׁ הֲמָסִים, מַיִם תִּבְעֶה אֵשׁ״! מִי כְּתִיב ״מַיִם נִבְעוּ״?! ״תִּבְעֶה אֵשׁ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari objects to** the Gemara's initial suggestion that the dispute between Rav and Shmuel with regard to the meaning of *Maveh* is based on biblical terms with similar etymology. He suggests: **But** why not **say** that ***Maveh*, this** is **the** primary category of damage caused by **water, as it is written: "As when fire ignites brushwood; fire boils [*tiveh*] water"** (Isaiah 64:1). The terms *tiveh* and *maveh* share a common root, and the reference is to water. The Gemara rejects this: **Is it written** in the verse: **Water boils [*nivu*]** from fire, with water, a plural noun in Hebrew, the subject of the intransitive plural verb, *nivu*? No, **"fire boils [*tiveh*] water" is written,** and since *tiveh* is a transitive singular verb, the subject is fire, which is a singular noun. Therefore, no proof can be adduced from this verse that *Maveh* refers to an action performed with water.

After resolving the dispute between Rav and Shmuel, the Gemara entertains two additional proposals for the meaning of *maveh* before closing the discussion. Rav Mari raises the possibility: *ve-eima maveh zeh ha-mayim* — perhaps *maveh* refers to water. He cites the *pasuk*: *ke-kadoch eish hamasim mayim tivei eish* — as fire kindles, it melts things, and the fire causes water to bubble. The verbal root *tivei* could link to *maveh*.

The Gemara rejects this: *mi-ksiv mayim nivu* — the *pasuk* does not say the water bubbled; *tivei eish ksiv* — it is the fire that causes the bubbling. Water is not the subject of the verb. 

### Fire Suggestion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב זְבִיד: וְאֵימָא ״מַבְעֶה״ זֶה הָאֵשׁ, דְּכִי כְּתִיב ״תִּבְעֶה״ – בְּאֵשׁ הוּא דִּכְתִיב! אִי הָכִי, מַאי ״הַמַּבְעֶה וְהַהֶבְעֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא פָּרוֹשֵׁי קָמְפָרֵשׁ; אִי הָכִי, אַרְבָּעָה?! שְׁלֹשָׁה הָווּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid objects to** the Gemara's initial suggestion that the dispute between Rav and Shmuel with regard to the meaning of *Maveh* is based on biblical terms with similar etymology. He suggests: **But** why not **say** that ***Maveh*, this is the** primary category of **Fire, as when** the term **"*tiveh*" is written** in the verse cited by Rav Mari, **it is written with regard to Fire.** The Gemara rejects this: **If so, what** is the meaning when the mishna enumerates the primary categories of damage: **The** category of ***Maveh* and the** category of **Fire,** indicating that they are two distinct categories? **And if you would say** that these are not two distinct categories but rather the mishna **is explaining** the meaning of *Maveh*, **if so,** why does the mishna say: There are **four** primary categories of damage? **There are** only **three.**

Next, Rav Zvid proposes: *ve-eima maveh zeh ha-eish* — perhaps *maveh* means fire, based on the same root *tivei* as applied to fire.

The Gemara dismisses this immediately: if *maveh* means fire, then what is *hav'er*? *Hav'er* already means fire. The *mishna* lists four *avot nezikin*; if *maveh* and *hav'er* are both fire, that collapses two categories into one.

### Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, תְּנָא שׁוֹר דְּאִית בֵּיהּ תַּרְתֵּי; אִי הָכִי, ״לָא זֶה וְזֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן רוּחַ חַיִּים״ – אֵשׁ מַאי רוּחַ חַיִּים אִית בֵּיהּ? וְתוּ, מַאי ״כַּהֲרֵי הָאֵשׁ״?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that *Maveh* is Fire, and there are four categories in the mishna, as the mishna **teaches** the primary category of **Ox, in which there are two** primary categories of damage, Eating and Trampling, the mishna remains difficult. **If** it is **so** that *Maveh* in the mishna is referring to Fire, what is the meaning of that which the mishna states with regard to the common denominator of *Maveh* and Ox: **And** the defining characteristics of **this** category of Ox **and that** category of *Maveh*, **in which there is a living spirit,** are **not** similar to the defining characteristic of the next category in the mishna, in which there is no living spirit. Accordingly, how can *Maveh* mean Fire; **what living spirit is there in Fire? And furthermore,** if *Maveh* is Fire, what is the meaning of the next phrase in the mishna: They **are not similar to** the defining characteristic of **the** category of **Fire,** in which there is no living spirit. Clearly, *Maveh* is not Fire.

One might respond that *shor* already encompasses two things — say, *shen* and *regel* — and that *maveh* and *hav'er* together form what is really *esh*, so the four are really only three. The Gemara rejects this: *ei hachi lo zeh ve-zeh she-yesh bahen ruach chayim, eish mai ruach chayim it bei* — the *mishna* explicitly says that *maveh* and *shor* share the characteristic of *ruach chayim*, living breath. Fire has no *ruach chayim*. Moreover, the *mishna* states that *maveh* is not like fire — the two are contrasted as distinct categories. It is therefore clear that *maveh* and *esh* are categorically separate, and water and fire are definitively ruled out as identifications for *maveh*.

## Thirteen Avot

### Thirteen Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר, וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר; נֶזֶק, צַעַר, וְרִיפּוּי, שֶׁבֶת, וּבוֹשֶׁת; וְאַרְבָּעָה דְמַתְנִיתִין – הָא תְּלֵיסַר.
>
> **Translation:** § Contrary to the mishna, where four primary categories of damage were enumerated, **Rabbi Oshaya taught** (*Tosefta* 9:1) that there are **thirteen primary categories of damage.** The thirteen categories consist of four bailees, five types of indemnity, and the four primary categories listed in the mishna. The four bailees are: The **unpaid bailee,** who is liable for damage caused by his negligence; **and the borrower,** who is liable for all damage; the **paid bailee, and the renter,** who is liable if the object is lost or stolen. The five types of indemnity one is liable to pay for injuring another person are: **Damage,** i.e., the decrease in the injured party's value; **pain; and medical costs; loss of livelihood; and humiliation** that the injured party suffered from the assault. **And** with **the four** primary categories enumerated in **the mishna, that** is a total of **thirteen.**

The Gemara now introduces a *brayta* of Rabbi Oshaya, which dramatically expands the list of *avot nezikin* from four to thirteen. The structure of the tractate has devoted its opening pages to the conceptual architecture of the four *avot*; Rabbi Oshaya's *brayta* asks how much broader the category of primary damage-obligations might be.

The first addition Rabbi Oshaya makes is the four *shomrim* — the four types of bailee. *Shomer chinam* is the unpaid watchman, who bears liability only for negligence; *sho'el* is the borrower, who pays even for accidents; *nosei sachar* is the paid watchman; and *socher* is the renter. The latter two pay for *geneiva va-aveidah* — cases of theft and loss where some degree of negligence is involved — but are exempt for complete accidents. These four categories cover the full spectrum of custodial responsibility under Torah law and together form a coherent sub-unit within Rabbi Oshaya's expanded list.

Rabbi Oshaya then adds the five payments owed by a human who wounds another human: *nezek*, *tzaar*, *ripuy*, *sheves*, and *boshes* — damages, pain, medical expenses, lost work, and humiliation. Together with the four *shomrim*, this brings the total to nine additional categories beyond the *mishna*'s four, yielding thirteen in all.

### Mishnah Limitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא דִּידַן – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא תָּנֵי הָנֵי? בִּשְׁלָמָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל – בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹן קָמַיְירֵי, בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ לָא קָמַיְירֵי; אֶלָּא לְרַב, לִיתְנֵי! תַּנָּא אָדָם – וְכׇל מִילֵּי דְאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason the *tanna* of our** mishna **does not teach these** nine categories and enumerates only four? **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Shmuel,** who says that *Maveh* is Eating, it is **with regard to** the categories of **damage** caused by one's **property** that the *tanna* of the mishna **speaks. With regard to** categories of **damage** caused by **one's body,** the *tanna* of the mishna **does not speak,** and the additional categories of Rabbi Oshaya are damage caused by one's body. **But according to** the opinion of **Rav,** who says that *Maveh* is Man, **let** the *tanna* of the mishna **teach** these nine categories as well. The Gemara answers: According to Rav, the mishna **teaches** the primary category of **Man, and** included in that category are **all matters** of damage that are caused by **man,** among them the categories added by Rabbi Oshaya.

*Ve-tani didan mai ta'ama lo tani hani* — why did the *tanna* of our *mishna* not include these? According to Shmuel, the answer is clean: *be-nizkei mamon ka-miyri, be-nizkei gufa lo ka-miyri* — the *mishna* deals exclusively with property damage, not personal injury. All of Rabbi Oshaya's additions either involve personal injury (*chavalah* and its five payments) or fall under a different framework (the *shomrim*). The *mishna*'s scope is the damages caused when one's property harms another's property.

According to Rav, who includes *adam* in the *mishna* and reads it as encompassing all human-caused damage, the explanation is: *tanna adam be-chol milei de-adam* — the *mishna*'s reference to *adam* is itself comprehensive, subsuming all the cases Rabbi Oshaya lists explicitly. Rabbi Oshaya's *brayta* was needed not because the *mishna* failed to cover these cases, but because Rabbi Oshaya wished to articulate them distinctly and explicitly.

## Chavala Distinct

### Two Types Adam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא נָמֵי, הָא תָּנֵי לֵיהּ אָדָם! תְּרֵי גַוְונֵי אָדָם; תְּנָא אָדָם דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם, וְתַנָּא אָדָם דְּאַזֵּיק שׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Oshaya too, doesn't** the mishna **teach** the primary category of **Man?** Why then did he enumerate the four bailees and the five types of indemnity? The Gemara answers: In his enumeration, Rabbi Oshaya distinguishes between **two types of** damage caused by **a man:** He **teaches** cases involving **a man who injures** another **person, and** he **teaches** the primary category of *Maveh*, which involves the cases of **a man who damages an ox** or damages other property belonging to another person.

The Gemara now works through the question of why exactly each of Rabbi Oshaya's additions was necessary — that is, why they could not have been absorbed by inference from the *mishna*'s existing categories. This discussion brings the fundamental distinction between property damage and personal injury into sharp relief.

There are two types of *adam* in the world of *nezikin*: *adam d'azik shor* — a person who damages property — and *adam d'azik adam* — a person who injures another person. The *mishna* is exclusively concerned with the first. The *mishna*'s *adam* is the person whose conduct damages another's property, analogous in form to the ox that damages property. But *chavalah* — wounding — is conceptually distinct. When one human being injures another, the law does not merely assess the market value of the harm and require payment of *nezek*. It recognizes *tzaar*, *ripuy*, *sheves*, and *boshes* as independent heads of liability, each requiring separate payment and each reflecting a different dimension of the injury. This is not a quantitatively larger version of property damage; it is a qualitatively different legal category.

### Shor Damages Human

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, שׁוֹר נָמֵי, לִיתְנֵי תְּרֵי גַוְונֵי שׁוֹר – לִיתְנֵי שׁוֹר דְּאַזֵּיק שׁוֹר, וְלִיתְנֵי שׁוֹר דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** with regard to **Ox as well, let him teach two categories of Ox. Let him teach** the case of **an ox that damages an ox** or other property belonging to another person, **and let him teach** the case of **an ox that injures a person.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? בִּשְׁלָמָא אָדָם דְּאַזֵּיק שׁוֹר – נֵזֶק הוּא דִּמְשַׁלֵּם, אָדָם דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם – מְשַׁלֵּם אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים; אֶלָּא שׁוֹר, מָה לִי שׁוֹר דְּאַזֵּיק שׁוֹר, מָה לִי שׁוֹר דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם; אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי נֶזֶק הוּא דִּמְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **What is this** comparison? **Granted,** in the case of **a man who damages** another's **ox** or other property, **he pays** merely for the **damage;** in the case of **a man who injures** another **person, he pays** an additional **four types** of indemnity payments: Pain, medical costs, loss of livelihood, and humiliation. **But** with regard to damage caused by **an ox, what** difference is there **to me** if it is **an ox that damages an ox** or other property belonging to another person and **what** difference is there **to me** if it is **an ox that injures a person?** In both **this** case **and that** case the owner of the ox **pays** merely for the **damage.** Therefore, unlike a case where a person causes the damage, there is no reason to distinguish between cases where an ox causes damage based on the victim of that damage.

The Gemara then asks: if the distinction is between property-object and human-person as the thing harmed, why not similarly distinguish between an ox that gores property and an ox that gores a human being? Should we not expect a separate category for *shor d'azik adam*? The Gemara's answer is instructive: *hai mai* — what is the comparison? When a human injures a human, the difference is categorical: *adam d'azik shor — nezek hu de-meshalem; adam d'azik adam — meshalem arba devarim*. The difference is not merely in how much is owed but in what *kind* of liability attaches. The five-payment framework for *chavalah* is a fundamentally different halachic structure from simple *nezek*.

By contrast: *shor d'azik shor — nezek hu de-meshalem; shor d'azik adam — nezek hu de-meshalem*. Halachically, when an ox injures a human being, the law still treats it as property damage. You assess the harm and pay *nezek*. There is no *tzaar*, no *boshes*, no *sheves* as independent obligations. The ox and the human victim are both treated as objects of property valuation. The damage is compensable, but it is compensated on the property-damage model, not the personal-injury model. There is therefore no halachically distinct category of *shor d'azik adam* that needs separate listing.

### Direct vs. Negligent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר, דְּאָדָם דְּאַזֵּיק שׁוֹר הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the assertion that Rabbi Oshaya added only cases involving a man who injures another person, the Gemara asks: **But aren't** there the categories of the **unpaid bailee and the borrower,** the **paid bailee and the renter, which are** categories that describe **a man who damages** another's **ox** or other property, as the cases referred to by Rabbi Oshaya are those where the bailee fails to return the deposit that he was entrusted to safeguard? **And** yet Rabbi Oshaya **teaches** each type of bailee as distinct categories and does not include them under the rubric of Man, one of the four categories enumerated in the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי הֶזֵּיקָא דִבְיָדַיִם, וְקָתָנֵי הֶזֵּיקָא דְמִמֵּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the reason why Rabbi Oshaya enumerates Man separately from the bailees is that he **teaches** one category, Man, referring to **damage that** is engendered **by** direct **action,** e.g., the five types of indemnity payments, **and** he **teaches** the four bailees, referring to **damage that occurs on its own,** e.g., failure of the four bailees to safeguard the deposit.

This brings the Gemara to Rabbi Oshaya's inclusion of the four *shomrim*. These are not cases of direct damage — *hezek de-be-yadayim* — but of indirect damage — *hezek de-meila*, damage that occurs on its own while the person is responsible through negligence or custodial obligation. The *mishna*, even for Rav who reads *adam* broadly, is discussing cases where the person directly performs an act of damage. Rabbi Oshaya needed to list the *shomrim* separately precisely because their liability arises from a different structural basis: not because they *did* something damaging, but because they were responsible for something that was damaged.

### Negligence Concept

This raises a conceptual question that the Gemara leaves open but which resonates throughout the tractate. When a *shomer* is *chayav*, is it because we conceptualize his negligence as having itself been the damaging act — treating him, in legal terms, as the *mazik*? Or is the framework simply that property was damaged and we are assessing who must bear the loss, without needing to characterize anyone as having committed an act of damage? The question is whether negligence is a species of *mazik*-liability or an independent basis for payment. The answer may vary depending on the degree of negligence — whether it is *peshiah* (clear negligence) or a more attenuated failure — and may even depend on whether the *shomer* in question is a *sho'el*, who pays even in cases of complete accident. Whether the liability of *shomrim* belongs in the same conceptual universe as the *avot nezikin* of property-damage, or merely in the same practical universe of "who pays," is a question that will continue to unfold in the pages ahead.
