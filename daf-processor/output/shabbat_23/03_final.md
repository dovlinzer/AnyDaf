# Shabbat 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hadlaka vs. Hanacha

### Core Question Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׁשִׁית שֶׁהָיְתָה דּוֹלֶקֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת מְכַבָּהּ וּמַדְלִיקָהּ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא הַדְלָקָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה — שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ הַנָּחָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה, הַאי מְכַבָּהּ וּמַדְלִיקָהּ, מְכַבָּהּ וּמַגְבִּיהָהּ וּמַנִּיחָהּ וּמַדְלִיקָהּ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **A lantern that continued to burn the entire day** of Shabbat, **at the conclusion of Shabbat one extinguishes it and lights it** again as a Hanukkah light. **Granted, if you say** that **lighting accomplishes** the **mitzva,** the requirement to extinguish the lantern and relight it in order to fulfill the mitzva of kindling the Hanukkah light **works out well. However, if you say** that **placing accomplishes** the **mitzva, this** statement, which stated that one **extinguishes it and lights it,** is imprecise. According to this opinion, **it needed** to say: **One extinguishes it and lifts it** from its place **and sets it down and lights it,** as only by placing the lamp in an appropriate place could one fulfill the mitzva of the Hanukkah light.

We pick up from the very last line of 22b. The question before us is how to understand the *mitzvah* of *ner Chanukah* — a core halakhic and conceptual issue. *Ner Chanukah* is interesting in two respects: first, who are the people obligated, given that the *Gemara* earlier spoke of *ner ish u'veito*, one lamp per household, then every person in the house, then *mehadrin min ha-mehadrin*; and second, what precisely is the obligation — to do what?

### Hadlaka or Hanacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד: מִדְּקָא מְבָרְכִינַן ״אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לְהַדְלִיק נֵר שֶׁל חֲנוּכָּה״ — שְׁמַע מִינָּה הַדְלָקָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Furthermore,** there is additional proof that lighting accomplishes the mitzva. **From** the fact **that we recite** the following blessing over the mitzva of kindling the Hanukkah light: **Who has made us holy through His commandments and has commanded us to light the Hanukkah light,** the Gemara suggests: **Conclude from this** that **lighting accomplishes** the **mitzva,** as it is over lighting that one recites the blessing.

The *Gemara* frames the question as follows: is it *hadlaka oseh mitzvah* — the act of lighting that constitutes the *mitzvah* — or *hanacha oseh mitzvah* — the act of placing the lamp down? The *Gemara* explores two scenarios where this distinction would make a practical difference. In the first, one lights indoors at a time when the interior is not a valid location, and then carries the lamp outside to the proper spot. The act of lighting was performed incorrectly, but the placement was done correctly. In theory, if *hanacha* is the *mitzvah*, this should suffice. The *Gemara*, however, finds a way to eliminate this difference: carrying the lamp from indoors to outdoors makes it look like a torch rather than a *menorah*, so even from the perspective of *hanacha* it does not work. A parallel scenario in reverse was also considered.

The *Gemara* then continues: *ta shma*, come and hear. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught that if one had a lantern burning continuously from *erev Chanukah* straight through *Shabbat* — a lantern that had been burning for twenty-four or forty-eight hours — and at *motzei Shabbat* wished to use it for *ner Chanukah*, he would need to extinguish it and relight it. The *Gemara* analyzes: if you say *hadlaka oseh mitzvah*, this makes perfect sense — the fact that it has been burning since *erev Shabbat* is insufficient, a fresh act of lighting is required. But if you say *hanacha oseh mitzvah*, why must one extinguish and relight? One should only need to lift it up and set it back down — that act of placement would be the *mitzvah*.

Tosafot raises the obvious question here: if *hanacha* is all that matters, why even extinguish it? Simply lifting and setting it back down should suffice. Tosafot answers that the scenario involves a lamp that was not lit originally for *Chanukah* but rather as a *Shabbat* light. Even granting that *hanacha* is the *mitzvah*, what must be placed is something defined as a *Chanukah* light. Since this was lit for *Shabbat*, it must be relit to acquire that status. The *Gemara*'s point remains, however: if *hanacha* is the *mitzvah*, the act of *hadlaka* alone — however defined — should be insufficient, and one would need to perform a distinct act of placement.

### Bracha as Evidence

The *Gemara* then adds a further proof: *midekamivarchinan asher kideshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu lehadlik ner shel Chanukah* — from the very fact that the *bracha* recited is *lehadlik*, to light, we derive *shma mina hadlaka oseh mitzvah*. This is a strong proof. It is well-attested among the *rishonim* — as in the parallel case of *shofar*, where the *Rambam* derives from the *nusach ha-bracha* of *lishmoa kol shofar* that the *mitzvah* is to hear rather than to blow — that the *nusach ha-bracha* reflects the conceptual definition of the *mitzvah*. This is not always an absolute one-to-one correspondence: when one puts on *tzitzit*, the *bracha* is *lehitatef b'tzitzit*, to wrap oneself, even though the *mitzvah* is not the act of wrapping but the obligation to attach *tzitzit* to a four-cornered garment. Similarly, on *erev Pesach* we recite *al bi'ur chametz* though the act being performed is checking, not burning — though there the *bracha* captures the conceptual purpose rather than the immediate act. Nevertheless, the presumption that the *nusach ha-bracha* mirrors how the *mitzvah* is defined is reasonable and here supports the conclusion: *hadlaka oseh mitzvah*.

### Who Can Light

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרִינַן הַדְלָקָה עוֹשָׂה מִצְוָה, הִדְלִיקָהּ חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְּלוּם. אִשָּׁה וַדַּאי מַדְלִיקָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת בְּנֵר חֲנוּכָּה שֶׁאַף הֵן הָיוּ בְּאוֹתוֹ הַנֵּס.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** the Gemara remarks, **now that we say** that **lighting accomplishes** the **mitzva,** there are practical ramifications. If **a deaf**-**mute, an imbecile,** or **a minor,** all of whom are of limited intellectual capacity and not obligated in mitzvot, **kindled** the Hanukkah light, **he did nothing** in terms of fulfilling the mitzva, even if an adult obligated in mitzvot subsequently set it down in its appropriate place. That is because placing a lit lamp does not constitute fulfillment of the mitzva. The lighting must be performed by a person with full intellectual capacity, obligated in mitzvot. However, **a woman certainly may light, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: Women are obligated in** lighting **the Hanukkah light, as they too were included in that miracle** of being saved from the decree of persecution.

Now that we have established that *hadlaka oseh mitzvah*, the *Gemara* draws a practical consequence: *hidlika cheresh shoteh v'katan lo asa v'lo klum* — if a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan* lights it, it counts for nothing. Had the *mitzvah* been *hanacha*, one might argue that the identity of the person performing the act of lighting is less significant, since what ultimately matters is the placement. But since the act of lighting is itself the *mitzvah*, it must be performed by someone who is obligated.

There is, however, an immediate exception: *isha vadai madlika* — a woman may certainly light, because she is certainly obligated. The basis for this is Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling: *nashim chayavot b'ner Chanukah she'af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* — women are obligated in the *mitzvah* of *Chanukah* because they too were part of the miracle.

## Women & Af Hen Hayu

### Women Obligated

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling — *nashim chayavot b'ner Chanukah she'af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* — establishes that women are fully obligated in *ner Chanukah*. The principle of *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* overcomes the general exemption of women from positive time-bound commandments (*mitzvot aseh she-hazman grama*), since women were themselves included in the miracle and therefore share in the obligation to publicize it.

## Hadlaka vs. Hanacha (II)

### Alternative Framework

Before proceeding further, it is worth pausing to consider a more contemporary *lomdus* approach to the *hadlaka*-*hanacha* debate — one that attempts to connect it to the earlier question of whether, if the *ner Chanukah* is extinguished, one is obligated to relight it (*kavta zakuk lah*).

The reframing runs as follows. Rather than treating *hadlaka* and *hanacha* as two distinct acts — lighting versus placing down — one might understand the debate as being about the act versus the duration: is the *mitzvah* the act of lighting, or is the *mitzvah* that the lamp is burning? If the *mitzvah* is the act, then once that act is performed correctly, one has fulfilled the obligation regardless of what happens afterward. If the lamp blows out, that is not one's responsibility — the *ma'aseh mitzvah* was completed. But if the *mitzvah* is the state of burning, then one bears ongoing responsibility to ensure that the lights remain lit throughout the required period — from *shkia* until *ad d'tichleh regel min ha-shuk*.

This alternative framing has a compelling rationale in terms of *pirsumei nisa*. If one lights and the lamp immediately blows out, one may have performed an act, but one has accomplished nothing in terms of publicizing the miracle. It makes conceptual sense that the *mitzvah* would be defined as ensuring that the lights are burning and fulfilling their purpose. This reading aligns neatly with the two disputed positions: *kavta zakuk lah* and *hadlaka oseh mitzvah* seem to form a coherent pair — if one must ensure that the lamp burns, one must relight it when it goes out, and the identity of who performs the lighting is less significant. Conversely, ruling that *hadlaka oseh mitzvah* and *kavta ein zakuk lah* — that if it blows out one need not relight — forms the other coherent pair.

One might also note that from a *hanacha*-as-duration perspective, it would be strange to require any special act of placement: who has ever heard that one must lift the lamp and set it back down? That oddity, already noted above, suggests that *hanacha* as a literal act of placement is not quite the right way to understand the opposing position. Nevertheless, it must be acknowledged that the *Gemara* itself does appear to interpret *hanacha* quite literally — it specifically contemplates the scenario of *mechaba u'magbi'a u'mani'ach u'madlika*, extinguishing, lifting, placing, and relighting, as distinct acts. The parallel to the *kavta* debate is illuminating and worth noting, even if the *Gemara*'s own formulation resists a clean reduction to that single axis.

## Women & Af Hen Hayu (II)

### Two Interpretations

Returning to the principle of *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes*: what precisely does this phrase mean? Among the *rishonim* there are two basic interpretations. The first, cited by Rashi, is that women were among the beneficiaries of the miracle — the decrees of *gezeirat Chanukah* threatened all of *Klal Yisrael*, and the *nes* of the war restored freedom of practice and self-governance to everyone, men and women alike. Focusing on the *nes* of the *pach ha-shemen*, that too was a miracle for all of Israel. On this reading, the principle simply means that women were saved along with everyone else.

The second interpretation, also hinted at in Rashi, is that women were among the *vehicles* of the miracle, not merely its recipients. This reading takes on more force when one notes that the *Gemara* invokes *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* in exactly three cases — *Chanukah*, *Purim*, and *arba kosot* of *Pesach*. By *Purim*, the active role of women is obvious: the redemption came through Esther. By *Pesach*, those who take this approach cite the principle that *nashim tzidkaniyot nigalu avoteinu mi-Mitzrayim* — it was through the merit of righteous women that our ancestors were redeemed. Regarding *Chanukah*, Rashi notes the decree of *she-gazru Yevanim al betulot ha-nissuot l'hiba'el la-tofsar techila* — the Greeks decreed that betrothed women would first be violated by the local governor — making women not merely recipients of the miracle but directly imperiled by the persecution that the miracle ended.

### Scope Limitations

This raises an important question that Tosafot addresses: if *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* is a valid principle, why does it not extend more broadly? Women are not obligated in *sukkah* and *lulav* — yet they were certainly present when the miracles of the desert occurred. Moreover, within *Pesach* itself, we use *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* to explain the obligation of *arba kosot*, yet women are already obligated in *matzah* through a biblical derivation — if one is prohibited in *chametz*, one is obligated in *matzah*. Why invoke *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* for *arba kosot* but not rely on it for other Passover obligations? What is the proper scope of the principle?

### Tosafot's Answer

Tosafot's answer is that *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* operates exclusively within the realm of rabbinic *mitzvot*. All three cases where the principle is applied — *Purim*, *Chanukah*, and *arba kosot* — are rabbinic in origin. The reasoning is as follows: when it comes to biblical *mitzvot*, the Torah itself has established a general framework of *mitzvot aseh she-hazman grama* that defines two categories of obligation, one for men and one for women, and we do not invoke *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* to override or modify that biblical structure. But when the rabbis are themselves instituting an obligation from the outset, they take into account the consideration of *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* in defining who is obligated — and *lechatchila* they obligate men and women equally.

### Rav Soloveitchik

A related but distinct explanation, building on Tosafot and perhaps extending it, was offered by Rav Soloveitchik. All three cases where *af hen hayu b'oto ha-nes* applies share not only the feature of being rabbinic, but a second, equally important common denominator: they all possess the quality of *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle. The *mitzvah* of *hadlaka* on *Chanukah* and the *mitzvah* of *Megillah* on *Purim* are explicitly characterized in the *Gemara* as acts of *pirsumei nisa*. Even *arba kosot*, though less obviously outward-facing, participates in this quality through the broader obligation of *l'har'ot atzmo k'illu hu yatza mi-Mitzrayim* — presenting oneself as though one personally left Egypt — which carries this character of demonstrating and publicizing the freedom that was wrought.

This connection between *pirsumei nisa* and the inclusion of women is not incidental. If the *mitzvah* were simply a matter of commemorating an event to which one was a witness or beneficiary, the logic of inclusion might not be so compelling. But if the essential purpose is to publicize the miracle as broadly as possible, then there is strong reason to include within the obligation everyone who was part of that miracle. *Pirsumei nisa* calls for maximum participation — and therefore the rabbis, in instituting these obligations, specifically defined them to include those who were part of the miraculous event.

On the practical question of whether a woman in a household where the man of the house is lighting can or should light herself: the *Gemara* is entirely clear that *isha vadai madlika*. Whatever hesitancy exists in some *Ashkenazi* practice may be rooted in considerations of *tzniut* or in questions about the nature of the obligation — whether it rests on the household as a corporate unit or on each individual. If one thinks of it as a household obligation, there may be a desire to see the head of the household as the one who performs it. But this is not stated explicitly in the *Gemara*. The *Gemara* also records the rulings of Rav Sheshet and Rav Zeira on related questions: Rav Sheshet holds that a guest (*achsannai*) is obligated in *ner Chanukah*, which implies that the obligation falls on individuals rather than purely on households. Rav Zeira relates that when he was a student lodging away from home, he would contribute a few coins (*mishtatfana b'fritei bahadi ushpiza*) to his host, thereby acquiring a share in the household's *ner Chanukah* lighting. After he married, he no longer needed to do this, since his household was lighting for him back home (*dekamadlikei alai b'go beisai*). These two rulings together suggest a baseline in which the obligation is anchored in the home as a unit, with the individual's obligation fulfilled through membership in or contribution to that unit — a picture that becomes more complex as one moves to the levels of *mehadrin* and *mehadrin min ha-mehadrin*.

## Oils & Configuration

### Choice of Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַשְּׁמָנִים כּוּלָּן יָפִין לַנֵּר, וְשֶׁמֶן זַיִת מִן הַמּוּבְחָר. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה מְהַדַּר מָר אַמִּשְׁחָא דְשׁוּמְשְׁמֵי. אָמַר: הַאי מְשִׁיךְ נְהוֹרֵיהּ טְפֵי. כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמַע לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, מְהַדַּר אַמִּשְׁחָא דְזֵיתָא. אָמַר: הַאי צְלִיל נְהוֹרֵיהּ טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: All the oils are suitable for the** Hanukkah **lamp, and olive oil is the most select** of the oils. **Abaye said: At first, my Master,** Rabba, **would seek sesame oil,** as he said: **The light of** sesame oil **lasts longer** and does not burn as quickly as olive oil. **Once he heard that** statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, he sought olive oil** because **he said: Its light is clearer.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught: *kol ha-shemanim kulan yafein la-ner* — all oils are suitable for the *Chanukah* lamp, *v'shemen zayit min ha-muvchar* — but olive oil is the preferred choice. This ruling is given in the context of *Chanukah*, not *Shabbat*, as is clear from the discussion that follows.

### Why Olive Oil

Abaye relates that originally his master — presumably Rabbah — would seek out sesame oil, explaining *hai mashcha nehora tfei*, that sesame oil burns longer, and longer burning may better serve *pirsumei nisa*. But once his master heard the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi praising olive oil, he changed his practice and specifically sought olive oil, reasoning *hai tzalil nehora tfei* — that olive oil produces a cleaner and brighter light. The practical question, then, is what better serves *pirsumei nisa*: duration of burning or quality of light? The ruling follows Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: a cleaner, brighter flame is preferred, even if it does not burn as long. Some *rishonim* offer the additional reason that olive oil is preferred because the original miracle of *Chanukah* involved olive oil — but that is not the reasoning given here in the *Gemara*.

### Ink Production

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל הַשְּׁמָנִים יָפִין לַדְּיוֹ, וְשֶׁמֶן זַיִת מִן הַמּוּבְחָר. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְגַבֵּל, אוֹ לְעַשֵּׁן? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר זוּטְרָא: כׇּל הַשְּׁמָנִים יָפִין לַדְּיוֹ, וְשֶׁמֶן זַיִת מִן הַמּוּבְחָר — בֵּין לְגַבֵּל בֵּין לְעַשֵּׁן. רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר זוּטְרָא מַתְנֵי הָכִי: כׇּל הָעֲשָׁנִים יָפִין לַדְּיוֹ, וְשֶׁמֶן זַיִת מִן הַמּוּבְחָר. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כׇּל הַשְּׂרָפִין יָפִין לַדְּיוֹ, וּשְׂרַף קְטָף יָפֶה מִכּוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: All the oils are suitable for** making **ink, and olive oil is the most select. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: What was Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's intention: Did he mean that olive oil is the most select in terms of being the best for use **to** mix and **knead** with the soot produced from a fire in manufacturing ink; **or** did he mean for use **to smoke,** i.e., burning olive oil to produce smoke is the most select method of producing the soot used in manufacturing ink? **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this from that which **Rav Shmuel bar Zutrei taught: All oils are suitable for ink, and olive oil is the most select, both to knead and to smoke. Rav Shmuel bar Zutra taught it this way: All types of smoke are good for ink, and olive oil is the most select.** Similarly, **Rav Huna said: All saps are good for** strengthening the **ink** compound, **and balsam sap is the best of all.**

On a related tangent: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi also taught that all oils are suitable for making ink, and olive oil is again the preferred choice. The *Gemara* raises the question of whether olive oil is preferred for the stage of *l'gabel* — mixing the soot with a binding medium to form ink — or for the stage of *le-ashen* — producing the soot itself by burning oil beneath an inverted vessel so that the smoke collects as a black residue. Rav Shmuel bar Zutra's teaching resolves the question by stating that olive oil is superior *bein l'gabel bein le-ashen* — both for mixing and for producing the smoke. Additionally, Rav Shmuel bar Zutra taught that while all smokes are suitable for use as the black pigment in ink, the smoke of olive oil is the most ideal. Rav Huna added that all types of resin or gum serve as a binding agent in ink, but balsam resin (*sraf kataf*) is superior to all others.

## Bracha Rules

### Lighting Requires Bracha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: הַמַּדְלִיק נֵר שֶׁל חֲנוּכָּה צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ. וְרַב יִרְמְיָה אָמַר: הָרוֹאֶה נֵר שֶׁל חֲנוּכָּה צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: One who lights a Hanukkah light must recite a blessing. And Rabbi Yirmeya said: One who sees a burning Hanukkah light must recite a blessing** because the mitzva is not only to kindle the light but to see the light as well. Therefore, there is room to recite a blessing even when seeing them.

Rabbi Chelbo in the name of Rav states: *ha-madlik ner shel Chanukah tzarich l'vareich* — one who lights *Chanukah* candles is obligated to recite a *bracha*. This had already been assumed in the earlier discussion but is now stated explicitly.

### Seeing Requires Bracha

Rabbi Yirmiyah adds that *ha-ro'eh ner shel Chanukah tzarich l'vareich* — one who merely sees *Chanukah* lights, without having lit them oneself, is also obligated to recite a *bracha*. This applies when one did not recite a *bracha* upon lighting, or when one did not light at all — for example, a guest whose household is lighting for him.

### Three vs. Two Brachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: יוֹם רִאשׁוֹן הָרוֹאֶה מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם, וּמַדְלִיק מְבָרֵךְ שָׁלֹשׁ. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, מַדְלִיק מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם, וְרוֹאֶה מְבָרֵךְ אַחַת. מַאי מְמַעֵט? מְמַעֵט זְמַן: וְנִימְעוֹט נֵס! — נֵס כׇּל יוֹמֵי אִיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** On **the first day** of Hanukkah, the **one who sees** burning lights **recites two** blessings, **and the** one **who lights recites three** blessings. **From there on,** from the second day of Hanukkah, **the** one **who lights recites two** blessings, **and the** one **who sees recites one** blessing. The Gemara asks: **What** blessing does he **omit** on the other days? The Gemara answers: He **omits** the blessing of **time:** Who has given us life, sustained us, and brought us to this time. The Gemara asks: **And let us omit** the blessing of the **miracle:** Who has performed miracles. The Gemara answers: The **miracle is** relevant on **all** of the **days,** whereas the blessing: Who has given us life, is only pertinent to the first time he performs the mitzva each year.

Rav Yehuda rules: *yom rishon ha-ro'eh m'vareich shtayim v'ha-madlik m'vareich shalosh* — on the first night, one who merely sees the lights recites two *brachot*, and one who lights recites three. *Mikan va-eilach* — from the second night onward — one who lights recites two and one who sees recites one. The question is which *bracha* is subtracted after the first night: *mimaeit zman* — the *bracha* of *zman*, that is, *shehecheyanu v'kiyemanu v'higiyanu la-zman ha-zeh*. *Shehecheyanu* is recited on the first night to mark the arrival of this new appointed time, just as it is recited on the first night of *Yom Tov* but not on subsequent days of the *chag*. Once it has been said, it covers the entire period. If for some reason one failed to say *shehecheyanu* on the first night, it may be said on a later night. But once it has been recited, it is not repeated.

### Shehecheyanu Once Only

The rationale for reciting *shehecheyanu* only once is consistent with how the *bracha* functions throughout the *mo'adim*: it marks the entry into a new period of sanctified time, not each individual day within that period. It is said once on the first night of *Yom Tov* and once at the outset of *Chanukah*, after which the time has already been acknowledged. For one who sees the lights rather than lights them: *shehecheyanu* and *she-asah nissim* are not dependent on performing the act of *hadlaka*. Seeing the lights draws one into the significance of the time and into the sphere of *pirsumei nisa*, and therefore both *brachot* are appropriate even for the one who does not light.

### Daily Miracle

The *Gemara* raises a further question: why is *she-asah nissim la-avoteinu* recited each day, rather than only once? Since it was seemingly one continuous miracle, is not one recitation sufficient? The *Gemara* answers: *neis kol yoma ita* — the miracle occurred anew each day. This answer fits most naturally with the emphasis on *neis pach ha-shemen*, where each of the eight days of burning constituted a distinct and separate miracle. One might alternatively argue that *she-asah nissim* need not assert that a new miracle occurred today versus yesterday — if the act of *hadlakat nerot* is itself *pirsumei nisa*, then each day's lighting calls forth a declaration of the miracle being publicized, regardless of how one counts the miraculous days. The *Bavli*, however, takes the approach that each day's miracle was distinct, and on that basis the daily recitation of *she-asah nissim* is fully justified.

The full set of *brachot* is thus: *asher kidshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'hadlik ner shel Chanukah*, *she-asah nissim la-avoteinu*, and on the first night, *shehecheyanu*. On the first night one who lights recites all three; on subsequent nights, two; one who only sees recites two on the first night and one thereafter.

## Rabbinic Authority

### Source of Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָן צִוָּנוּ? רַב אַוְיָא אָמַר: מִ״לֹּא תָסוּר״. רַב נְחֶמְיָה אָמַר: ״שְׁאַל אָבִיךָ וְיַגֵּדְךָ זְקֵנֶיךָ וְיֹאמְרוּ לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where did He command us?** The mitzva of Hanukkah is not mentioned in the Torah, so how is it possible to say that it was commanded to us by God? The Gemara answers that **Rav Avya said:** The obligation to recite this blessing is derived **from** the verse: "**You shall not turn aside** from the sentence which they shall declare unto you, to the right, nor to the left" (Deuteronomy 17:11). From this verse, the mitzva incumbent upon all of Israel to heed the statements and decrees of the Sages is derived. Therefore, one who fulfills their directives fulfills a divine commandment. **Rav Neḥemya said** that the mitzva to heed the voice of the Elders of Israel is derived from the verse: **"Ask your father, and he will declare unto you, your Elders, and they will tell you"** (Deuteronomy 32:7).

The *Gemara* then raises a striking question: *v'heichan tzivanu* — where did God command us? This is, after all, a rabbinic *mitzvah*. How can the *bracha* of *asher kidshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu* — "who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us" — be recited over something the Torah did not explicitly command? The question, it should be noted, could in principle be asked about many rabbinic enactments. But it carries particular force when applied to *Chanukah* and *Megillah*, where the rabbis did not merely add safeguards to existing Torah practice but instituted wholly new ritual observances, including the establishment of what amounts to a new *chag*. This raised a genuine question: from where do they derive the authority to institute such things, and how can a *bracha* that invokes divine command be recited upon them?

### Lo Tasur & She'al

Two answers are offered. Rav Avya says: *mi-lo tasur* — from the verse "you shall not deviate from what they tell you, right or left" (Devarim 17:11), which the Torah directs at the rulings of the rabbinic authorities. Since the Torah itself commands obedience to rabbinic rulings, a rabbinic enactment carries divine backing, and one can therefore say *v'tzivanu*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a second source: *she'al avicha v'yagedcha z'keinecha v'yomru lach* — "ask your father and he will tell you, your elders and they will say to you" (Devarim 32:7), which likewise directs the people to heed the tradition transmitted through the generations of rabbinic leadership.

### Rambam vs. Ramban

There is a significant dispute among the *rishonim* about how to read the *lo tasur* answer. The *Rambam* holds that violating a rabbinic enactment constitutes a biblical transgression — one violates the *d'oraita* prohibition of *lo tasur* whenever one disregards a rabbinic ruling. The *Ramban* disagrees: if every rabbinic enactment automatically carried the force of a biblical prohibition by virtue of *lo tasur*, then the entire distinction between *d'oraita* and *d'rabbanan* would collapse. On the *Ramban*'s view, the *she'al avicha* answer is in fact the more accurate description of the nature of rabbinic authority: the obligation to heed the rabbis is real and binding, and it is backed by the Torah's endorsement of that tradition, but it does not transform every rabbinic ruling into a full-fledged biblical prohibition. These two answers of the *Gemara*, taken literally, may thus map onto this fundamental *rishonic* dispute about the structure of rabbinic authority.

### Rav Amram's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב עַמְרָם: הַדְּמַאי, מְעָרְבִין בּוֹ וּמִשְׁתַּתְּפִין בּוֹ וּמְבָרְכִין עָלָיו וּמְזַמְּנִין עָלָיו וּמַפְרִישִׁין אוֹתוֹ עָרוֹם וּבֵין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ כׇּל מִדְּרַבָּנַן בָּעֵי בְּרָכָה, הָכָא כִּי קָאֵי עָרוֹם הֵיכִי מְבָרֵךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram raised an objection** from that which we learned in a mishna: With regard to **doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*],** i.e., grain that was acquired from an *am ha*'*aretz* about which there is uncertainty whether or not he tithed it; **one may** use it **to establish an *eiruv*,** i.e., joining of courtyards and joining of borders, **and to establish the merging of alleys, and one recites a blessing** before and after eating **it, and one invites a quorum** for recitation of Grace after Meals after eating **it.** Although the Sages said that one is required to separate tithes from *demai*, they allowed it to be used for specific purposes and in exigent circumstances. **And** they said that **one may separate** the tithe from *demai* when he is **naked and at dusk** Shabbat eve, a time when separating tithes from actual untithed produce [*tevel*] is prohibited. **And if you say** that **every** action instituted by **rabbinic ordinance requires a blessing,** as fulfillment of rabbinic ordinances is based on the mitzva: You shall not turn aside, **here, when** he **stands naked, how** can he **recite a blessing?**

Rav Amram then raises a challenge from a different angle. The *Gemara* had taught that *demai* — produce purchased from an *am ha-aretz* where there is uncertainty whether tithes were properly separated — may be used for an *eiruv*, for *shitufei mevo'ot*, and a *bracha* may be recited upon it. It may also be used for *birkat ha-mazon* and *zimmun*, and the *ma'asrot* may even be separated from it while one is unclothed, and even during *bein ha-shmashot* on *Shabbat*. The challenge is as follows: if any rabbinic obligation requires a *bracha*, then when separating *demai* in a state of undress, how does one recite the *bracha*? The verse *v'haya machanecha kadosh* — "your camp shall be holy" — teaches that one may not recite a *bracha* while unclothed.

### Definite vs. Doubtful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וַדַּאי דְּדִבְרֵיהֶם בָּעֵי בְּרָכָה, סָפֵק דְּדִבְרֵיהֶם לָא בָּעֵי בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** There is room to distinguish between the cases: In **a case** where there is a **definite** mitzva **by rabbinic law, a blessing is required.** In a case where there is a **rabbinic** ordinance instituted due to **uncertainty** with regard to the circumstances, as in the case of *demai*, which may or may not have been tithed already, **a blessing is not required.**

Abaye resolves this with a crucial distinction: *vadai d'divreihein ba'ei bracha, safek d'divreihein lo ba'ei bracha*. A rabbinic obligation that is definite — where the rabbis have unambiguously imposed an obligation — requires a *bracha*. But a rabbinic obligation that is itself rooted in doubt — where the rabbis legislated because of an uncertain circumstance rather than a certain one — does not require a *bracha*. *Demai* falls into the latter category: the rabbis did not say that tithes were definitely not taken; they said there is doubt whether they were taken. The rabbinic obligation is therefore conditional on the doubt, and since the situation is one of *safek*, no *bracha* is recited. Moreover, the *Gemara* notes that the rabbis treated *demai* more leniently than other rabbinic prohibitions — they permitted it to be fed to guests and to the poor — reflecting that the underlying obligation has a weaker character than a straightforward rabbinic prohibition.

### Yom Tov Sheni Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא יוֹם טוֹב שֵׁנִי דִּסְפֵק דִּבְרֵיהֶם הוּא, וּבָעֵי בְּרָכָה? הָתָם כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיזַלְזְלוּ בֵּהּ. רָבָא אָמַר: רוֹב עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ מְעַשְּׂרִין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't the second day of a Festival** in the Diaspora a **rabbinic** ordinance instituted due to **uncertainty** whether the first day or the second is the actual Festival, **and** nevertheless **a blessing is required?** On the second day of the Festival one recites the same blessings as he does on the first. The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of the second day of the Festival, the reason that blessings are required is **so that people will not treat it with contempt.** If Festival blessings were not required on the second day of the Festival, people would take its sanctity lightly. **Rava said** another reason: *Demai* is not considered to be an ordinance instituted by the Sages due to uncertainty. In fact, in **most** cases, an ***am ha*'*aretz* tithes.** The concern lest they do not tithe is not a full-fledged case of uncertainty. It is merely a case of suspicion for which the Sages did not institute a blessing. That is not the case with regard to the second day of a Festival. Even though it was instituted due to uncertainty, one must recite the Festival blessings. Since it was instituted by the Sages, one is obligated to recite a blessing just as he recites blessings for other rabbinic ordinances.

The *Gemara* challenges Abaye's distinction: how does one recite a *bracha* on the *mitzvot* of *Yom Tov Sheni*, which is itself a rabbinic institution rooted in the doubt over calendar calculation — a paradigmatic *safek d'rabbanan*? The *Gemara* answers: *ki heichi d'lo l'zalzulei bei* — so that people should not treat *Yom Tov Sheni* as less significant than *Yom Tov Rishon*. Were no *bracha* to be recited, it would undermine the sanctity of *Yom Tov Sheni* and invite laxity. Therefore the *bracha* is required there as a special measure to preserve the standing of the institution. This is not a general principle applicable to all *safek d'rabbanan* situations, but a specific ruling driven by the need to maintain the *kedusha* of *Yom Tov Sheni* on a par with *Yom Tov Rishon*. Rava offers a further perspective: *rov amei ha-aretz me'asrin hein* — in practice, most *amei ha-aretz* do tithe their produce, such that *demai* is not even a strong *safek* to begin with. The obligation in *demai* is therefore of a particularly attenuated character, providing additional grounds for not requiring a *bracha* in that case.

## Two Entrances

### Two Doors Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חָצֵר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי פְּתָחִים צְרִיכָה שְׁתֵּי נֵרוֹת. אָמַר רָבָא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת, אֲבָל מֵרוּחַ אַחַת — לָא צְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: A courtyard that has two entrances requires two lamps,** one lamp at each entrance, so that it will be obvious that the residents of this courtyard light properly. **And Rava said: We only said this** in a case where the two entrances face **two** different **directions. However,** if they both face in **the same direction one need not** light at more than one entrance.

Rav Huna asks: *chatzer she-yeish lah shenei pesachim* — a courtyard (or, as Rashi explains, a house within a courtyard) that has two openings — *tzrichah shenei neros* — does it require two sets of lights? The question presupposes that *pirsumei nisa* requires that the lights be visible from the street, and that a house with two doors accessible from different directions raises the concern that one side might appear unlit.

### Different Sides Needed

Rava qualifies: *lo amran ela mi-shtei ruchot* — we require two sets of lights only if the two doors are on different sides of the house. *Aval mei-ruach achat, lo tzarich* — if the two doors are on the same side, one does not need two sets. The reasoning is presented in terms of *chashada* — suspicion.

### Suspicion Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא — חֲשָׁדָא דְמַאן? אִילֵּימָא חֲשָׁדָא דְעָלְמָא — אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוּחַ אַחַת נָמֵי לִיבְעֵי! אִי חֲשָׁדָא דִּבְנֵי מָתָא — אֲפִילּוּ מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת נָמֵי לָא לִיבְעֵי. לְעוֹלָם מִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא דִּבְנֵי מָתָא, וְזִימְנִין דְּחָלְפִי בְּהַאי וְלָא חָלְפִי בְּהַאי, וְאָמְרִי: כִּי הֵיכִי דִּבְהַאי פִּיתְחָא לָא אַדְלִיק — בְּהָךְ פִּיתְחָא נָמֵי לָא אַדְלִיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies Rava's statement: **What is the reason** for this? **If you say** that it is **because** those who see the entrance without a lamp burning will harbor **suspicion** lest he does not kindle the Hanukkah light, **whose suspicion** concerns us? **If you say** that the concern is with regard to **the suspicion** of **people** who do not live in the city and are unfamiliar with the courtyard's tenants, **even** when both entrances face **the same direction let them be required** to light at both entrances because visitors are unaware that there are two entrances to that courtyard. **And if** the concern is with regard to the **suspicion of the residents of** that **city, even** when the two entrances face **two** different **directions let them not be required** to light at both entrances. The local residents know that only one person lives in the courtyard and will assume that if he did not light at one entrance he surely lit at the other. The Gemara answers: **Actually,** say that it is **because of the suspicion of the residents of** that **city, and sometimes** they **pass this** entrance **and do not pass that** one, **and they say: Just as he did not light in this entrance, in that** second **entrance he also did not light.** In order to avoid suspicion, it is preferable to light at both entrances.

The concern is that passersby will suspect the householder of not having lit *Chanukah* candles. Rashi explains the underlying assumption: a house with two doors may appear to observers to house two separate families. If one side has no visible *Chanukah* light, people will assume that the family associated with that entrance has failed to light.

### Local Suspicion

The *Gemara* presses further: whose suspicion are we concerned about? If it is strangers passing through — *chashada d'alma* — then even two doors on the same side should present a problem, since strangers do not know the internal arrangement of the house. If it is the townspeople — *chashada d'vnei masa* — then they know there is only one family in the house, and two doors on different sides should not matter either. The *Gemara* resolves: *l'olam mishum chashada d'vnei masa* — we are concerned about suspicion from the people of one's own town. And the reason two different sides matter even to townspeople is: *v'zimnin d'michalfei b'hai v'lo chalfei b'hai* — sometimes people will pass by one side and not the other. If they see only the unlit entrance, they will conclude — *ki heichi d'b'hai pitcha lo adlik* — that this person did not light at all, without realizing there is a lit entrance on the other side. When both doors are on the same side, a person passing that side will see the candles at one door even if not at the other, and no suspicion arises.

### Why Care About Suspicion?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לַחֲשָׁד? — דְּתַנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בִּשְׁבִיל אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה לְהַנִּיחַ פֵּיאָה בְּסוֹף שָׂדֵהוּ: מִפְּנֵי גֶּזֶל עֲנִיִּים, וּמִפְּנֵי בִּיטּוּל עֲנִיִּים, וּמִפְּנֵי הַחֲשָׁד, וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּכַלֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do you say that we are concerned about suspicion? As it was taught** in a *Tosefta* that **Rabbi Shimon said: On account of four things the Torah said** that one should **leave *pe*'*a*,** crops for the poor in the corner of his field, specifically **at the end of his field.** Only after one has cut virtually the entire field should he leave an uncut corner for the poor. He should not designate an area for *pe*'*a* in the middle of the field in the course of cutting the field. The reasons for this ruling are: **Due to robbing the poor, and due to causing the poor to be idle, and due to suspicion, and due to** the verse: **"You shall not wholly reap** the corner of your field" (Leviticus 23:22).

This prompts a natural objection: *u'mi chashishinan l'chashda* — are we really required to concern ourselves with what other people think of us? The *Gemara* notes that this concern is especially apt in the context of *ner Chanukah*, where *pirsumei nisa* creates a heightened interest in the perception that the entire community is participating in the observance. But the *Gemara* does not rely on this alone.

### Pe'ah Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי גֶּזֶל עֲנִיִּים — שֶׁלֹּא יִרְאֶה בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שָׁעָה פְּנוּיָיה וְיֹאמַר לִקְרוֹבוֹ עָנִי: ״הֲרֵי זוֹ פֵּיאָה״. וּמִפְּנֵי בִּיטּוּל עֲנִיִּים — שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ עֲנִיִּים יוֹשְׁבִין וּמְשַׁמְּרִין ״עַכְשָׁיו מַנִּיחַ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת פֵּיאָה״. וּמִפְּנֵי חֲשָׁד — שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיוּ עוֹבְרִין וְשָׁבִין אוֹמְרִים: תָּבֹא מְאֵרָה לְאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא הִנִּיחַ פֵּיאָה בְּשָׂדֵהוּ. וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּכַלֶּה״. אַטּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ לָאו מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּכַלֶּה״ נִינְהוּ? אָמַר רָבָא: מִפְּנֵי הָרַמָּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Due to robbing the poor;** so **that the owner of the house will not see a time** when the field is **unoccupied** and there are no poor people in the area. If he could designate *pe*'*a* as he wished, there is room to suspect that **he** might **say to his poor relative: This is *pe*'*a*,** in the place and at the time that he chooses. He would thereby conceal the fact that there is *pe*'*a* in his field from other poor people. The result would be that, for all intents and purposes, he robbed *pe*'*a* from those with whom he did not share the information. **And due to causing the poor to be idle;** so that the **poor,** who have no way of knowing when he is going to cut the grain and where in the field he is going to leave the *pe*'*a*, **will not be sitting and observing** until he designates the *pe*'*a* and constantly saying to themselves: **Now the owner** of the field **is placing *pe*'*a*.** Now that he leaves the *pe*'*a* in a defined area at the end of his field, and the poor people know exactly where they can receive their portion, they need not waste their time in anticipation. **And due to suspicion;** if one leaves the *pe*'*a* in the middle of the field, the poor will come and take their portion immediately when he designates the area of *pe*'*a*. When the owner then continues to cut and harvest the rest of the grain in the field, the *pe*'*a* will not be noticeable. Insisting that he leave *pe*'*a* at the end of the field ensures **that passersby will not say: A person who did not leave *pe*'*a* in his field should be cursed.** We learned that the fourth reason is **due to** the verse: **You shall not wholly reap.** The Gemara wonders: **Aren't all of** these reasons **due to: You shall not wholly reap?** All of the reasons explain that one may not reap his entire field and must leave *pe*'*a* at the end of his field. **Rava said:** The meaning of the last reason is that *pe'a* is separated that way **due to cheaters.** There is concern that a person would not leave *pe*'*a* at all. He would claim that he already separated it in the middle of his field and that the poor already came and took it. In order to bolster the mitzva of *pe*'*a*, the Sages instituted that it must be separated specifically at the end of one's field. In terms of the discussion in the Gemara, apparently, the desire to avoid arousing suspicion is a factor taken into consideration in determining *halakha*.

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches that the Torah required *pe'ah* — the corner of the field left for the poor — to be set aside at the very end of the harvest, not at some point in the middle, for four reasons. The *Gemara* enumerates: *mipnei gezel aniyim* — to prevent theft from the poor, since if *pe'ah* could be designated at any point, the landowner might call his poor relative at a moment when no other poor people are present and give it to him exclusively; *mipnei bitul aniyim* — to prevent wasting the time of the poor, who would otherwise have to wait and watch throughout the harvest not knowing when *pe'ah* would be left; *mipnei ha-chashad* — because of suspicion, since if the *pe'ah* was left in the middle of the harvest and is no longer visible by the time the field is fully reaped, passersby will assume the owner never gave *pe'ah* at all and will curse him; and *mishum bal tichleh* — the prohibition against harvesting the corners of one's field entirely. The *Gemara* queries: are not all four reasons ultimately expressions of the same underlying prohibition of *bal tichleh*? Rava answers: no — the concern for *chashad* is distinct from the concern for *mipnei ha-rama'in* — those who might exploit the flexibility to actually avoid giving *pe'ah* by claiming they gave it earlier. The *pe'ah* case thus demonstrates that avoiding suspicion — ensuring that one does not appear to have failed in a *mitzvah* — is a recognized halakhic concern in its own right, and the *Gemara* uses it as precedent for the concern underlying the requirement to light on both sides of a house with two entrances facing different directions.

## Oils & Configuration (II)

### Two-Spout Lamp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר רְדִיפָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: נֵר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי פִיּוֹת — עוֹלָה לִשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם. אָמַר רָבָא: מִילֵּא קְעָרָה שֶׁמֶן וְהִקִּיפָהּ פְּתִילוֹת. כָּפָה עָלֶיהָ כְּלִי — עוֹלָה לְכַמָּה בְּנֵי אָדָם. לֹא כָּפָה עָלֶיהָ כְּלִי — עֲשָׂאָהּ כְּמִין מְדוּרָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְאֶחָד נָמֵי אֵינָהּ עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Redifa said** that **Rav Huna said: Lighting an oil lamp that has two spouts,** with one wick placed in each of the spouts, **is considered** to have fulfilled the obligation of kindling the Hanukkah light **for two people.** Similarly, **Rava said: One who filled a bowl** with **oil and placed wicks all around it,** if **he overturned a vessel on top of it, it is considered** to have fulfilled the obligation of lighting the Hanukkah light **for several people,** corresponding to the number of wicks. By overturning a vessel atop the bowl, each wick appears to be burning independently. If **one did not overturn a vessel on top of it, he** thereby **made it** appear **like a type of bonfire.** From afar, the light from all of the flames appear to be a single flame. **And it is not even considered** to have fulfilled the obligation of lighting the Hanukkah light **for one** person because the mitzva is specifically to light a flame and not a bonfire.

Rav Yitzchak bar Radifa in the name of Rav Huna rules: *ner she-yeish lo shenei piyot oleh li-shnei b'nei adam* — a lamp that has two mouths — two separate points from which a wick can emerge — counts for two people. Rashi clarifies that this applies when two members of the same household are each fulfilling the *mitzvah* at the level of *mehadrin*, not when two separate households are involved, since the latter would require distinct lamps.

### Combined Flames Problem

Rava introduces a related ruling: *mila ke'arah shemen v'hikifah petilot* — if one fills a plate with oil and places wicks all around the rim, then *kafah aleiha keli* — if one places a vessel over it, covering the central space so that the flames remain separate — *keli oleh l'kamah b'nei adam* — many people can fulfill the *mitzvah* from this single vessel. However, *lo kafah aleiha keli* — if one does not place such a covering — *asa'ah k'min medurah* — the arrangement becomes like a bonfire or torch, as all the individual flames merge into a single large fire in the center. And *afilu l'echad nami eino oleh* — it does not even count for one person. The *mitzvah* of *ner Chanukah* requires a defined lamp or flame, not the appearance of a bonfire, which looks like light needed for practical illumination rather than a *mitzvah* flame. This ruling has implications for the configuration of the *chanukiah*: there are concerns about placing lights in an arc or arrangement where the flames might combine, particularly on the same horizontal plane.

## Priorities

### Household vs. Chanukah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, נֵר בֵּיתוֹ וְנֵר חֲנוּכָּה — נֵר בֵּיתוֹ עָדִיף, מִשּׁוּם שְׁלוֹם בֵּיתוֹ. נֵר בֵּיתוֹ וְקִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם — נֵר בֵּיתוֹ עָדִיף, מִשּׁוּם שְׁלוֹם בֵּיתוֹ. בָּעֵי רָבָא: נֵר חֲנוּכָּה וְקִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם מַהוּ? קִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם עֲדִיף — דִּתְדִיר, אוֹ דִילְמָא נֵר חֲנוּכָּה עֲדִיף — מִשּׁוּם פַּרְסוֹמֵי נִיסָּא? בָּתַר דְּבַעְיַהּ, הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: נֵר חֲנוּכָּה עֲדִיף, מִשּׁוּם פַּרְסוֹמֵי נִיסָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is obvious to me** that there is a fixed list of priorities. When a person is poor and must choose between purchasing oil to light **a** Shabbat **lamp for his home** or purchasing oil to light **a Hanukkah lamp,** the Shabbat **lamp for his home takes precedence.** That is **due to peace** in **his home;** without the light of that lamp, his family would be sitting and eating their meal in the dark. Similarly, if there is a conflict between acquiring oil to light **a lamp for his home** and wine for **the sanctification [*kiddush*] of** Shabbat **day, the lamp for his home takes precedence due to peace in his home.** However, **Rava raised a dilemma:** When the conflict is between oil for **a Hanukkah lamp** or wine for ***kiddush* of** Shabbat **day, what is** the ruling in that case? Does ***kiddush* of** Shabbat **day take priority** because it is **frequent,** i.e., it is performed every week, and there is a principle: When there is a conflict between a frequent practice and an infrequent practice, the frequent practice takes precedence? **Or, perhaps the Hanukkah lamp takes precedence due to publicity of the miracle? After he raised the dilemma, he then resolved it** on his own and he ruled that, in that case, the **Hanukkah lamp takes precedence due to publicity of the miracle.**

Rava rules on a series of priority questions when one lacks the resources to fulfill multiple obligations. First: if one must choose between *ner beito* — light in the home, which Rashi understands as *ner Shabbat* — and *ner Chanukah*, *ner beito adif* — the household light takes priority. The reasoning: *mishum shalom beito* — because having light in the home makes for peace in the home. A dark house leads to discomfort, discord, and difficulty. The *Chanukah* light, being placed outside, does not serve the interior of the home in any case. This ruling implicitly treats *ner beito* as itself a *mitzvah* — not merely a practical convenience — since it is being weighed against a recognized *mitzvah* of *ner Chanukah*. The *bracha* recited on *ner Shabbat*, incidentally, is a *Gaonic* institution not found in the *Gemara* itself, and the precise moment when the lighting of *Shabbat* candles crystallized as a formal *ma'aseh mitzvah* is itself a matter of discussion.

### Household vs. Kiddush

Second: if one must choose between *ner beito* and *kiddush ha-yom* — having wine for *kiddush* of *Shabbat* — again *ner beito adif*, again *mishum shalom beito*. The same principle applies: the light in the home ensures peace and prevents friction, and this concern takes priority even over *kiddush*. The comparison of these two *mitzvot* further underscores the status of *ner beito* as a serious *mitzvah*, since it is not merely said to be more convenient than *kiddush* but is assigned priority on the basis of the distinct value of *shalom bayit*.

### Chanukah vs. Kiddush

Third: if one must choose between *ner Chanukah* and *kiddush ha-yom*, where *shalom bayit* is no longer in play, what takes precedence? Rava posed this question and then himself resolved it: *ner Chanukah adif mishum pirsumei nisa* — the *Chanukah* candle takes precedence because of *pirsumei nisa*. The principle of publicizing the miracle carries sufficient weight to override *kiddush ha-yom*. Tosafot draws a related inference from this: on a *Shabbat* that coincides with *Rosh Chodesh Chanukah*, the *haftarah* read is that of *Chanukah* rather than that of *Rosh Chodesh*, precisely because *pirsumei nisa* takes precedence. This emerges as a broader principle: *pirsumei nisa*, as a defining purpose of the *Chanukah* observance, carries significant weight in resolving conflicts with other obligations.

## Mitzvot & Children

### Candles & Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הָרָגִיל בְּנֵר הָוְיִין לֵיהּ בָּנִים תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: One who is accustomed to** kindle **lights** on Shabbat and Hanukkah will be rewarded and **have children who are Torah scholars,** who will disseminate the light of Torah.

Rav Huna teaches: *ha-ragil ba-ner havin lei banim talmidei chachamim* — one who is scrupulous about the *mitzvah* of the lamp will be granted sons who are Torah sages. The verse *ner mitzvah v'Torah or* — "a *mitzvah* is a lamp and Torah is light" — establishes the symbolic connection: the light of the *mitzvah* begets the light of Torah.

### Other Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַזָּהִיר בִּמְזוּזָה — זוֹכֶה לְדִירָה נָאָה. הַזָּהִיר בְּצִיצִית — זוֹכֶה לְטַלִּית נָאָה. הַזָּהִיר בְּקִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם — זוֹכֶה וּמְמַלֵּא גַּרְבֵי יַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **One who is meticulous in** performing the mitzva of ***mezuza* merits a beautiful house** on which to affix his *mezuza*. **One who is meticulous in** performing the mitzva of ritual fringes **merits a beautiful garment. One who is meticulous in** performing the mitzva *of **kiddush*** **of the day merits and fills jugs of wine.**

The passage continues with parallel teachings. One who is careful in the *mitzvah* of *mezuzah* will merit a beautiful dwelling — *zocheh l'dirah na'ah*. One who is careful in *tzitzit* will merit fine garments — *zocheh l'tallit na'ah*. And one who is careful in *kiddush ha-yom* of *Shabbat* will merit cellars full of wine — *zocheh u'memalei garvei d'yayin*.

### Historical Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא הֲוָה רְגִיל דַּהֲוָה חָלֵיף וְתָנֵי אַפִּתְחָא דְרַבִּי אָבִין נַגָּרָא. חֲזָא דַּהֲוָה רְגִיל בִּשְׁרָגֵי טוּבָא, אֲמַר: תְּרֵי גַּבְרֵי רַבְרְבֵי נָפְקִי מֵהָכָא. נָפְקִי מִינַּיְיהוּ רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין וְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין. רַב חִסְדָּא הֲוָה רְגִיל דַּהֲוָה חָלֵיף וְתָנֵי אַפִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי נָשָׁא דְּרַב שֵׁיזְבִי. חֲזָא דַּהֲוָה רְגִיל בִּשְׁרָגֵי טוּבָא, אֲמַר: גַּבְרָא רַבָּא נָפֵק מֵהָכָא. נְפַק מִינַּיְיהוּ רַב שֵׁיזְבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Huna was accustomed to pass by and teach at the entrance of** the house of **Rabbi Avin the carpenter. He saw that** Rabbi Avin **was accustomed to** kindle **many lights** in honor of Shabbat. Rav Huna **said: Two great men** will **emerge from here.** Indeed, **Rav Idi bar Avin and Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin,** his two oldest sons, **emerged from their** family. On a similar note, the Gemara relates: **Rav Ḥisda was accustomed to pass by and teach at the entrance of Rav Sheizvi's** father's family home. **He saw that** Rav Sheizvi's father **was accustomed** to kindle **many lights** in honor of Shabbat. Rav **Ḥisda said: A great person** will **emerge from here.** Indeed, **Rav Sheizvi emerged from them.**

The *Gemara* provides historical illustrations. Rav Huna was accustomed to study near the doorway of Rabbi Abin the carpenter (*nagara*). He observed that Rabbi Abin was meticulous in his candle lighting — whether for *Shabbat* or *Chanukah* or both, as Rashi explains. He declared: *trei gavrei rabbei nafki me-hacha* — two great men will emerge from this house. And indeed, from Rabbi Abin's household emerged Rav Idi bar Abin and Rav Chiya bar Abin, both distinguished *amoraim*. Similarly, Rav Chisda was accustomed to study near the entrance of the household of Rav Shizbi — which, since the father had passed away, was now referred to as *d'bei nasha*, the women's household, i.e., his mother's home. He observed that the mother was meticulous in her candle lighting and declared: *gavra raba nafik me-hacha* — a great person will emerge from here. And indeed Rav Shizbi was the one who came forth from her home.

### Proper Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵיתְהוּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף הֲוָת מְאַחֲרָה וּמַדְלְקָה. אֲמַר לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: תָּנֵינָא ״לֹא יָמִישׁ עַמּוּד הֶעָנָן יוֹמָם וְעַמּוּד הָאֵשׁ לָיְלָה״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעַמּוּד עָנָן מַשְׁלִים לְעַמּוּד הָאֵשׁ וְעַמּוּד הָאֵשׁ מַשְׁלִים לְעַמּוּד הֶעָנָן. סְבַרָה לְאַקְדּוֹמֵי. אֲמַר לַהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: תָּנֵינָא, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַקְדִּים וְשֶׁלֹּא יְאַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Yosef's wife would kindle the** Shabbat lights **late. Rav Yosef said to her: Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"The pillar of cloud by day, and the pillar of fire by night, departed not** from before the people" **(**Exodus 13:22), this **teaches that the pillar of cloud would overlap** with **the pillar of fire?** The pillar of fire would appear slightly before nightfall. **And the pillar of fire** would **overlap** with **the pillar of cloud,** as well. The pillar of cloud would appear slightly before daybreak. Therefore, in lighting the Shabbat lights it is also appropriate to light earlier, beginning Shabbat slightly before dark on Shabbat eve. **She thought to** kindle the lights much **earlier,** on Shabbat eve, long before nightfall. **An Elder said to her, we learned: As long as he neither** lights too **early nor** too **late.**

The *Gemara* closes the daf with a teaching about timing. Rav Yosef's wife was accustomed to lighting late. Rav Yosef, citing a *baraita*, reminded her: *lo yamish amud he-anan yomam v'amud ha-esh laila* — the pillar of cloud and the pillar of fire did not cease, the one completing for the other before its time was up. The *baraita* derives from this verse that the pillar of cloud would not depart before the pillar of fire took over — each was in place before the transition, not a moment late. This teaches, Rashi understands, that one should not light *Shabbat* candles so close to sunset as to be almost too late. Hearing this, Rav Yosef's wife then overreacted and considered lighting very early. An elder (*saba*) then cited a parallel teaching: *uvilvad she-lo yakdim v'she-lo ye-acher* — provided one neither lights too early nor too late. The proper *mitzvah* is performed at its right time — not rushed, not delayed.
