# Nedarim 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pre-Annulment of Vows

### Opening Remarks

With this *daf* we move away from the extended discussion of the *aras* (groom) and the *yavam*, returning to what might be called more basic territory concerning the act of *hafarah* — though the cases examined remain anything but simple.

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ ״כׇּל הַנְּדָרִים שֶׁתִּדּוֹרִי מִכָּאן עַד שֶׁאָבֹא מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי הֲרֵי הֵן קַיָּימִין״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין״ — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֹמֵר: מוּפָר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מוּפָר. אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אִם הֵפֵר נְדָרִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לִכְלַל אִיסּוּר — לֹא יָפֵר נְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ לִכְלַל אִיסּוּר?!
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says to his wife: All vows that you will vow from now until I arrive from such and such a place are hereby ratified, has not said anything,** i.e., the vows are not ratified. However, if he states that all vows that she will take until then **are hereby nullified, Rabbi Eliezer said:** They are **nullified, while the Rabbis say:** They are **not nullified. Rabbi Eliezer said** in explanation: **If one can nullify vows that have reached the status of a prohibition,** i.e., that have already taken effect, **shall he not** be able to **nullify vows that have not reached the status of a prohibition?**

The *mishnah* presents the following case. A husband about to depart on a business trip says to his wife: *kol hanedarim she-tadori mi-kan v'ad she-avo mi-makom ploni, ha-rei hen kayamim* — "All the vows you make from the time I leave until I return, I hereby affirm." The Ran notes that the husband says *mi-kan*, from here, i.e., from the moment of his departure, rather than *me-achshav*, from right now.

The *mishnah* rules: *lo amar klum* — he has said nothing. If, however, he declares *ha-rei hen mufarim* — that they are hereby annulled — then Rabbi Eliezer rules *mufar*, they are validly annulled in advance. The *chachamim* rule *eino mufar*: pre-annulment of a vow that does not yet exist is ineffective.

### Mekayem vs. Meifer

The distinction between these two rulings rests on the conceptual difference between *mekayem* (affirming a vow) and *meifer* (annulling it). To be *mekayem* a vow is to strengthen its reality as a binding *issur*, and one cannot strengthen what does not yet exist. But negating a vow — the *chachamim* and Rabbi Eliezer dispute whether annulment requires the prior existence of a vow in the same way.

### Rabbi Eliezer Kal Vachom…

Rabbi Eliezer presses his case with a *kal va-chomer*: *im heifer nedarim she-ba'u li-khlal issur, lo yafer nedarim she-lo ba'u li-khlal issur?* — If the husband can annul vows that have already taken effect and manifested as real-world prohibitions, how much more so should he be able to annul vows that have not yet come into existence? As the *gemara* will soon explore, the precise import of this argument — whether Rabbi Eliezer means the pre-annulment *prevents* the vow from ever taking effect, or whether it *annuls* the vow at the instant of its formation — is itself an open question.

### Chachamim Respond

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵרֶנּוּ״. אֶת שֶׁבָּא לִכְלַל הָקֵם — בָּא לִכְלַל הָפֵר, לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל הָקֵם — לֹא בָּא לִכְלַל הָפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him** in response: The verse **states:** "Every vow, and every binding oath to afflict the soul, **her husband may ratify it, or her husband may nullify it"** (Numbers 30:14). This teaches: **That which has reached the status of** eligibility for **ratification,** i.e., a vow that she has already taken, **has reached the status of** eligibility for **nullification.** However, a vow that **has not reached the status of** eligibility for **ratification** has **not reached the status of** eligibility for **nullification** either, and it cannot be nullified.

The *chachamim* respond by invoking a scriptural link: *ishah yekimennu ve-ishah yeferennu* — "the husband shall affirm it and the husband shall annul it." From this verse they derive a parallel structure: *et she-ba li-khlal hakim, ba li-khlal hafer; lo ba li-khlal hakim, lo ba li-khlal hafer* — only something that is subject to affirmation can be subject to annulment. Since a vow that does not yet exist cannot be affirmed, it likewise cannot be annulled. The *kal va-chomer* may be logically appealing in principle, but the verse forecloses it.

### Act vs. Effect

The *mishnah* takes for granted that a husband may annul his wife's vows without having heard them — that question was addressed at length in the preceding discussion. The only issue here is whether annulment can precede the vow's very existence. One might ask whether the same conceptual move that was used earlier — splitting the act of *hafarah* from its effect, so that the act is performed now but takes hold only later — could be applied here to the *kiyum* side of the equation. The answer, however, is that such a maneuver cannot help the husband who wishes to be *mekayem*: since *kiyum* by definition requires a vow to strengthen, any attempted *kiyum* would necessarily await the vow's formation and would still be ineffective. The device of separating act from effect addressed only the *shemi'ah* problem; it has no traction here.

## Prevent vs. Annul

### Two Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מֵיחָל חָלִין וּבָטְלִין, אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא חָלִין כְּלָל? לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **According to Rabbi Eliezer,** do the vows that the husband nullifies in advance **take effect** momentarily **and are** then **canceled** immediately after? **Or perhaps they do not take effect at all.** The Gemara inquires: **In what** case is there **a difference** between these possibilities?

The *gemara* opens with a formal question — *iba'ya lehu* — concerning the nature of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling. Granted that the husband's pre-issued *hafarah* is effective, what precisely does it accomplish?

### Annul After Effect

The first possibility: the vow does take effect, but simultaneously with its taking effect it is annulled. There is no moment in real time during which the wife stands bound — the annulment is instantaneous — yet conceptually the vow exists and is then negated.

### Prevent Effect

The second possibility: the husband's prior *hafarah* is preventative. The pre-annulment acts as an ounce of prevention, blocking the vow from ever acquiring any force or reality whatsoever. It never takes effect at all — *lo chail klal*.

### Ve-ani Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּגוֹן דְּאַתְפֵּיס אַחֲרִינָא בְּהָדֵין נִדְרָא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ חָלִין — הָוְיָא תְּפִיסוּתָא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָא חָלִין — לָא אִיכָּא מְשָׁשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There is a difference in a case **where another** person **associated** his own vow **with this vow.** If someone else heard her vow and declared his vow to be like hers, the status of his vow depends on this question. **If you say** that such vows **take effect,** then **the association** of the other person's vow **takes effect. If you say** that such vows **do not take effect** at all, then the vow of the other person **has no substance,** as the vow with which he associated it never existed.

The practical difference (*nafka mina*) between these two positions concerns the case of *hatfasah*. Suppose the wife declares *hareini nezirah*, and a bystander immediately says *ve-ani* — transferring the *nezirut* status to herself. The husband had, a week earlier, issued a blanket pre-annulment covering his wife's vows. If the vow took effect and was then annulled — the first interpretation — the bystander's *ve-ani* attaches to a real status that existed, if only for a conceptual instant, and her *nezirut* stands. If the vow never took effect at all — the second interpretation — there is no status to transfer, and the *ve-ani* is meaningless.

## Hafarah vs. Hatarah

### Example Setup

The *gemara's* example: the wife takes a vow, a companion says *ve-ani*, and the husband then annuls the wife's vow. The question is what consequences follow for the companion.

### Hatarah Question

This raises a further question. What if, instead of the husband's *hafarah*, the original person who made the vow went to a *chacham* for *hatarah* — formal dissolution? Would the companion who said *ve-ani* also be released?

### Ran's Distinction

Here the Ran articulates a distinction that is central to this entire *perek* yet, somewhat surprisingly, has not been foregrounded until now. *Hatarah* — dissolution by a *chacham* — is *oker ha-neder me-ikaro*: it retroactively uproots the vow from its very source. The mechanism of *hatarah* is to reframe the original vow as a *neder ta'ut*, a vow made in error, which means the vow is deemed never to have taken effect. *Hafarah*, by contrast, operates *mikan u-le-ha'ba*: it cuts off the vow going forward but does not retroactively undo the past. The husband does not uproot the vow; he severs it.

### Consequence

This distinction has direct practical consequences for the *ve-ani* case. When the first person obtains *hatarah* from a *chacham*, the retroactive effect means the original vow never existed, so all subsequent *hatfasah* — the status-transfers effected by those who said *ve-ani* — collapses as well. When the husband performs *hafarah*, however, the vow genuinely did exist (even if only for a conceptual instant), and that real, if momentary, existence is what the companion's *ve-ani* latches onto. The *hafarah* stops the wife's *nezirut* going forward, but the companion who said *ve-ani* transferred the status at the moment it existed and therefore remains bound.

### Nazir Parallel

This is not merely a *sevarah*-level inference; there is an explicit *gemara* in Nazir, 79b, that poses precisely this question: *ba'al me'akar akar, o migaz gayiz* — does the husband uproot the vow entirely, or does he merely cut it off from continuing? The *nafka mina* given there is identical: a woman vows *nezirut*, her companion says *ve-ani*, and the husband annuls the wife's vow. If the husband is *me'akar* — uprooting retroactively — then the companion too is released. If he is *migaz gayiz* — cutting it off going forward — the wife is released but the companion remains bound. The *gemara* in Nazir concludes: *ba'al migaz gayiz*; a *chacham* is *oker me-ikara*. This is an important and fundamental distinction that is, ironically, spelled out more explicitly in Nazir than in Nedarim itself.

### Ve-ani Timing

The Ran raises a second question worth addressing. By the time the companion says *ve-ani*, the wife's vow has already been annulled — so the wife is no longer a *nezirah* at that moment. Why should *ve-ani* not mean "and I am like you are right now," in which case the companion is equally free?

### Shelamim Analogy

The Ran resolves this by reference to a *gemara* concerning meat of *zivchei shelamim*. Before the blood is dashed on the *mizbeach*, the meat is forbidden; once it has been dashed, it is permitted. If someone then declares *ve-zeh ka-zeh* — "and this like that" — using the *shelamim* meat as the referent, the *gemara* asks: *be-ikaro kamatfis* — does he mean to compare to the object's current status (now permitted) or to its earlier status (when it was forbidden)? The *gemara* concludes *de-hashta kamatfis* — he refers to its present status — and therefore no *hatfasah* results. The Ran acknowledges this parallel, but distinguishes the *ve-ani* case on two grounds. First, the verbal form *ve-ani*, without the explicit comparative *kamoah* ("like her"), does not naturally carry the nuance "like her as she is right now." Second, and more substantially: what stands before the companion is the wife's act of declaration — *hareini nezirah* — and the *hafarah* itself is not present or visible. Given that backdrop, *ve-ani* most naturally refers to the *nezirut* status that that declaration created. Even if that status evaporated an instant later, it did exist, and it is to that status that *ve-ani* attaches.

To summarize the *nafka mina*: if the pre-issued *hafarah* means the vow took effect and was then immediately annulled, the companion's *ve-ani* latches onto the status that existed in that conceptual instant and the companion remains bound. If the pre-issued *hafarah* prevented the vow from ever taking effect, then there was never any status to transfer and the companion is free.

## Mishnah Language

### Eliezer's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אִם הֵפֵר נְדָרִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לִכְלַל אִיסּוּר, לֹא יָפֵר נְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ לִכְלַל אִיסּוּר? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא חָלִין!
>
> **Translation:** **What,** then, is Rabbi Eliezer's opinion? **Come** and **hear** the mishna: **Rabbi Eliezer said: If one can nullify vows that have reached the status of a prohibition, shall he not nullify vows that have not reached the status of a prohibition? Learn from it** that such vows **do not take effect** at all, as they are described as not having reached the status of a prohibition.

The *gemara* returns to the precise language of Rabbi Eliezer's *kal va-chomer*: if the husband can annul vows *she-ba'u li-khlal issur* — that have already come into the category of prohibition — should he not *a fortiori* be able to annul vows *she-lo ba'u li-khlal issur* — that have not yet entered that category? The natural reading of this argument is that Rabbi Eliezer claims the pre-annulment should prevent the vow from ever taking effect. That reading would resolve the earlier *ibaya* in favor of *lo chaylin*.

### Shelo Ba'u

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי קָתָנֵי ״שֶׁאֵינָן בָּאִין״? ״שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ״ קָתָנֵי, עֲדַיִין לֹא בָּאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **Does** the mishna **teach** using the words: **That do not reach** the status of a prohibition? It **teaches: That have not reached** the status of a prohibition, which could mean that **they have not yet reached** the status of a prohibition, but they may take effect, momentarily, when she actually takes the vow.

The *gemara*, however, resists this reading. The phrase *shelo ba'u* does not mean vows that will never come into existence — *she-einan ba'in*. It means vows that have not yet come into existence — *adayin lo ba'u*. Rabbi Eliezer is not claiming that the pre-annulment defeats vows that would otherwise never take effect; he is speaking about vows that exist in the future but have not yet arrived.

### Challenged Status

The Ran offers a refined reading of the *kal va-chomer* in light of this correction. The argument is not that pre-annulment *prevents* the vow from taking effect. Rather: if the husband can annul a vow after it has taken hold in an unchallenged state, how much more so should he be able to annul a vow that comes into existence already challenged — *mumo imo*, bearing its defect from the very moment of its birth. The vow never existed in a pristine, unchallenged state; from the instant it came into being, the *hafarah* was already present. According to the Ran, this is Rabbi Eliezer's actual position: the vow does take effect, but it arrives already carrying the seeds of its own annulment.

### Own vs. Wife Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אִם הֵפֵר נְדָרִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לִכְלַל אִיסּוּר, לֹא יָפֵר נְדָרִים שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ לִכְלַל אִיסּוּר? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא חָלִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* from the *Tosefta* (*Nedarim* 6:5): **Rabbi Eliezer said to them: And just as in a situation where he cannot nullify his own vows once he has vowed, he can nullify his own vows before he vows** by stipulating beforehand that the vows he takes should not take effect, all the more so in **a situation where he can nullify his wife's vows** even **after she vows, is it not logical that he should** be able to **nullify his wife's vows before she vows?**

The *gemara* raises a further analogy. When a person makes a conditional pre-declaration — on the order of what is done on *Erev Rosh Hashanah* — that any future vows should be considered void, those vows do not take effect when subsequently made. Should we infer by *kal va-chomer* that a husband's pre-issued *hafarah* similarly prevents his wife's vows from taking effect?

### Two Mechanisms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, דְּאִשְׁתּוֹ דּוּמְיָא דִּילֵיהּ, מָה [הוּא] דְּלָא חָיְילִין — אַף אִשְׁתּוֹ נָמֵי דְּלָא חָיְילִין? לָא, הָא כִּדְאִיתָא וְהָא כִּדְאִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: **What, is it not referring to** where **his wife's** vows are **similar to his own** in that **just as his** vows, which were nullified in advance, **do not take effect** at all, **so too** the vows of **his wife do not take effect** at all when nullified in advance? The Gemara rejects this: **No, this** case of his nullifying his own vows prior to taking them is **as it is, and that** case of his nullifying her vows prior to her taking them is **as it is;** the similarity between the two cases is sufficient for an *a fortiori* inference, but they are not similar in all respects.

The *gemara* deflects this inference: *ha ke-d'ita ve-ha ke-d'ita* — each case operates by its own mechanism. When a person pre-empts his own vows, the mechanism is *ta'ut*: the conditional declaration frames any future vow as an error, as something he never truly intended. The vow does not take effect because the *ta'ut* mechanism retroactively undermines the act of vowing. *Hafarah* of a wife's vow operates through an entirely different mechanism — annulment, not error — and the two cannot simply be mapped onto each other.

### After to Before

The Ran, however, finds this answer unsatisfying if taken at face value as the *kal va-chomer's* foundation, and offers a brilliant reinterpretation. The *kal va-chomer* does not claim that what works for one's own vows must work identically for a wife's vows. Rather, the analogy is structural: for one's own vows, where the operative mechanism is *ta'ut*, the pre-emptive declaration works *before* (via a *tenai*) even though one cannot undo one's own vow *after* by oneself. The structural lesson is: what works *after* should certainly work *before*. Now transpose to the wife's vows. There, the operative mechanism is *hafarah*, which concededly works *after*. By the same structural logic — if it works after, it should work before — the *hafarah* should work before the vow comes into existence as well. The mechanisms are different, but the logical form of the *kal va-chomer* is the same in both cases, and that is the argument's real force.

## Mikveh Analogy

### Mikveh Cleans

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וּמָה מִקְוֶה שֶׁמַּעֲלֶה אֶת הַטְּמֵאִין מִטּוּמְאָתָן — אֵין מַצִּיל עַל הַטְּהוֹרִים מִלִּיטָּמֵא, אָדָם שֶׁאֵין מַעֲלֶה אֶת הַטְּמֵאִים מִטּוּמְאָתָן — אֵינוֹ דִּין הוּא שֶׁלֹּא יַצִּיל עַל הַטְּהוֹרִין מִלִּיטָּמֵא?
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **They said to Rabbi Eliezer:** Your *a fortiori* inference is flawed, as can be seen from this example: **And just as** with regard to **a ritual bath, which elevates the impure from their** ritual **impurity** when they immerse in it but **does not save the pure from becoming impure** if they come into contact with impurity after immersion, so too, with regard to **a person, who does not elevate the impure from their impurity,** e.g., when a person swallows a ritually impure object and then immerses in a ritual bath, the object remains impure, **is it not logical that he should not save pure** items **from becoming impure?** When one who has swallowed a ritually pure object comes into contact with impurity, the object he has swallowed should also become impure. This is not the case, as a swallowed object does not become impure when the person who swallowed it does. Therefore the *a fortiori* argument is flawed, and one cannot derive from the ability to change a status, like the husband's ability to nullify his wife's vows or the ability of the ritual bath to render something ritually pure, that the source of the change can also prevent a change of status or preserve that status.

The *chachamim* respond to Rabbi Eliezer's *kal va-chomer* with a counter-example from the laws of *tumah*: *u-mah mikveh she-ma'aleh et ha-teme'im mi-tum'atan* — just as a *mikveh* restores a *tamei* person to *taharah* after the fact...

...nonetheless, *ein matzil et ha-tahor min ha-tumah* — being immersed in a *mikveh* does not prevent *tumah* from attaching. If a person is standing in a *mikveh* and simultaneously comes into contact with something *tamei*, or a bodily condition arises that would render him *tamei* — *tovel ve-sheretz be-yado* — he is *tamei*. The *mikveh* removes *tumah* after the fact but does not block *tumah* from taking hold in the first place. The *kal va-chomer* that "if it works after it works before" therefore does not hold as a general principle.

### Person's Role

The text continues with an additional line, noting: a person — *adam* — who cannot make a *tamei* object *tahor* (*she-ein ma'aleh et ha-tamei mi-tum'ato*), is it not logical that he should be unable to prevent *tahor* things from becoming *tamei*? Yet consider the case of *tumah blu'ah*: an object swallowed inside a person's body is shielded from *tumah* — it cannot become *tamei* — even though the person himself cannot make *tamei* things *tahor*. In other words, the mechanisms run in exactly opposite directions: a *mikveh* removes *tumah* but does not prevent it; a person prevents *tumah* (when absorbed internally) but does not remove it.

### Context-Dependent

The Ran explains the double thrust of the *chachamim's* argument. Point one: the *mikveh* itself demonstrates that something can remove without preventing — *kal va-chomer* logic from "after" to "before" does not operate in a vacuum. Point two: the comparison between the *mikveh* and the *adam* case demonstrates that across *halakhah*, the direction in which a mechanism operates is context-determined and cannot be extrapolated by *kal va-chomer* alone. The *chachamim* are not necessarily claiming that Rabbi Eliezer's *kal va-chomer* is logically incoherent, but that this class of inference is not ironclad — its force depends on what the surrounding *halakhic* framework supports.

## Pre-dunking & Seeds

### Pre-dunking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא, אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אִם מַטְבִּילִין כְּלִי טָמֵא לִיטָּהֵר, יַטְבִּילוּ כְּלִי לִכְשֶׁיִּטְמָא לִיטָּהֵר? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ חָיְילִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion and refers back to the *baraita*. **Say the latter clause** of that *baraita*: **They said to Rabbi Eliezer: If one immerses an impure vessel to purify** it, **shall one immerse a vessel** in advance so that **when it will become impure it will** then **be purified? Learn from** this clause of the *baraita* that according to Rabbi Eliezer, vows nullified preemptively **take effect** momentarily and are then immediately nullified. The Rabbis' objection is that according to Rabbi Eliezer, prior immersion should purify an item that momentarily became impure.

The *chachamim* offer a second counter-example: *im matbilin kli tamei le-taharo* — if you can immerse a *tamei* vessel to render it *tahor*, can you pre-immerse a *tahor* vessel so that when it later becomes *tamei* the problem will have been solved in advance? The answer is obviously not. This is analogous to the person who says *echta ve-ashuv* — I will sin and preemptively repent. The *mikveh* does not work prospectively in that fashion.

### Suggests Haylan

Interestingly, the way the *chachamim* frame this counter-example — *yatbilu kli le-cheshe-yitma le-taharo*, "let them pre-immerse a vessel so that when it becomes *tamei* it will become *tahor*" — seems to presuppose that Rabbi Eliezer's mechanism is one in which the vow *does* take effect and is then reversed. The phrase *le-cheshe-yitma* implies: once it becomes *tamei*, the pre-immersion will kick in and render it *tahor*. This is the *haylan* interpretation — the vow takes effect, and the pre-annulment then resolves it.

### Conflicting Signals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: רַבָּנַן לָא קָיְימִי לְהוֹן בְּטַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְהָכִי קָאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מַאי סְבִירָא לָךְ? אִי סְבִירָא לָךְ דְּחָיְילִין וּבָטְלִין — תִּהְוֵי כְּלִי תְּיוּבְתָּךְ, אִי לָא סְבִירָא לָךְ דְּחָיְילִין — תִּהְוֵי מִקְוֶה תְּיוּבְתָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: One could **say** that **the Rabbis could not determine the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer, and this is what** they **said to him: What do you hold? If you hold that** preemptively nullified vows **take effect** momentarily **and are** then **nullified,** then the example of **a vessel will be your refutation,** i.e., will serve to refute your opinion. **If you do not hold that they take effect,** but rather that they do not take effect at all, then the example of **a ritual bath will be your refutation.**

At this point the *gemara* has two analogies pointing in opposite directions. The *mikveh*-in-use analogy — standing in a *mikveh* does not prevent *tumah* — suggests the *chachamim* are arguing against the *lo haylan* position: Rabbi Eliezer thinks pre-annulment prevents the vow from taking effect, and they counter that prevention does not work that way. The pre-dunking analogy, by contrast, suggests the *chachamim* are arguing against the *haylan* position: Rabbi Eliezer thinks the vow takes effect and is then reversed, and they counter that pre-applying a remedy for a future condition does not work. Two analogies; two different signals about what position they are attacking.

---

## Conclusion & Scripture

### Either Way

The *gemara* resolves this tension in a striking way. *Rabbanan lo kaymei lehu be-ta'ameih de-Rabbi Eliezer* — the *chachamim* themselves did not know precisely what Rabbi Eliezer's position was. Accordingly, *ve-hachi kamre lei* — their response was structured to cover both possibilities. They said to him: what is your view? If you hold *de-chayil u-mivtal* — that the vow takes effect and is then annulled — then the pre-dunking of the vessel is your refutation; we do not say that pre-applying the remedy solves the problem after the *tumah* attaches. And if you hold *de-lo chayil* — that the pre-annulment prevents the vow from taking effect entirely — then the *mikveh* is your refutation; being immersed in a *mikveh* does not prevent *tumah* from attaching.

---

## Pre-dunking & Seeds…

### Seeds Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וּמָה זְרָעִים טְמֵאִים, כֵּיוָן שֶׁזְּרָעָן בְּקַרְקַע טְהוֹרִין, זְרוּעִין וְעוֹמְדִים — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לָא חָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear: Rabbi Eliezer said to them: And just as ritually impure seeds, once one has sown them in the ground,** become **pure,** then with regard to those which **are already sown** and then come into contact with impurity, should they **not all the more so** be pure? Similarly, vows that have been preemptively nullified should be nullified, since a husband can nullify vows after they have been taken. **Learn from** this *baraita* that according to Rabbi Eliezer preemptively nullified vows **do not take effect** at all, just as seeds that were already sown do not become impure at all.

Rabbi Eliezer then responds with his own analogy. *U-mah zera'im teme'im, keivan she-zera'an be-karka taherun* — impure seeds, when planted in the ground, lose their *tumah* and become *tahor*. And we further know: *zeru'in ve-omdin lo kol she-ken* — things already planted cannot become *tamei* at all. Here is a case in which both mechanisms operate in tandem: planting removes existing *tumah* and simultaneously prevents future *tumah* from taking hold.

---

## Pre-dunking & Seeds……

### Suggests Lo Haylan

If that is Rabbi Eliezer's chosen analogy, it points strongly toward the *lo haylan* interpretation. The seeds, once planted, are entirely immune to *tumah*; they do not become *tamei* and then become *tahor* — they simply cannot become *tamei* at all. Rabbi Eliezer appears to be saying that the pre-issued *hafarah* operates like the planting of the seed: it shields the vow from ever taking hold.

---

## Conclusion & Scripture…

### Seeds Support

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *shema mina lo chaylin* — the seeds analogy supports the *lo haylan* interpretation, that according to Rabbi Eliezer the vow never takes effect at all. This resolves the *ibaya* in favor of the straightforward reading of the *kal va-chomer*: if *hafarah* can annul a vow that has taken effect, it should certainly be able to prevent a vow from taking effect in the first place.

### Uncertainty

Nevertheless, the *gemara* has already observed: *rabbanan lo kaymei lehu be-ta'ameih de-Rabbi Eliezer* — the *chachamim* themselves were uncertain which of these two positions Rabbi Eliezer held, and hence they armed themselves with counter-arguments sufficient to rebut either possibility.

### Scripture Over Logic

As for the *chachamim's* own ruling, the *gemara* notes that in the end their position does not rest solely on refuting the *kal va-chomer* but on a *gezerat ha-katuv*. The verse *ishah yekimennu ve-ishah yeferennu* establishes a parallel: only what can be established (*hakim*) can be annulled (*hafer*). A vow that does not yet exist cannot be established; therefore, it cannot be annulled. This is a scriptural limitation that operates independently of any logical inference.

### Ran's Synthesis

The Ran raises what seems like an obvious difficulty: if the *chachamim* have a *gezerat ha-katuv*, why did they spend so much effort deploying *kal va-chomer* counter-arguments and analogies from *tumah*? Why not simply cite the verse and be done with it? And conversely, if the *chachamim* reject this type of *kal va-chomer* as a matter of principle, why do we find elsewhere — as in the case of selling a daughter into slavery, where the *gemara* uses precisely this form of reasoning (*u-mekhurah kevar yotze'ah... eino din she-lo timakher*) — that the *chachamim* do accept "if it works to reverse, it works to prevent" as a valid *kal va-chomer*?

### Integrated Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן לָא דָּרְשִׁי קַל וָחוֹמֶר? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: יָכוֹל יִמְכּוֹר אָדָם אֶת בִּתּוֹ כְּשֶׁהִיא נַעֲרָה? אָמְרַתְּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר: מְכוּרָה כְּבָר — יוֹצְאָה, אֵינָהּ מְכוּרָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תִּימָּכֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And the Rabbis, do they not teach** *halakhot* based upon an ***a fortiori*** inference of this sort? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Can a person sell his daughter** as a maidservant **when she is a young woman? You** can **say an *a fortiori*** inference to show that he cannot: A maidservant who was **already sold goes free** upon becoming a young woman; with regard to one **who has not been sold, is it not logical that she cannot be sold** once she already is a young woman? This *baraita* shows that the Rabbis do utilize similar *a fortiori* inferences.

The Ran's answer weaves the two together. The *chachamim* do accept this class of *kal va-chomer* as a legitimate form of inference — it is not categorically invalid. What they are saying in the earlier analogies is not "your logic is incoherent" but rather "your logic is not ironclad." Look at the breadth of *halakhah*: the *mikveh* works to remove but not to prevent; the *adam* prevents but does not remove; *tumah* and *taharah* operate according to their own principles and do not always follow intuitive inference. The *kal va-chomer* is a starting point, but *pesukim* frequently override it. That is the lesson the *chachamim* were teaching through all the earlier analogies: this type of inference is defeasible. And here, a *pasuk* does indeed override it — *ishah yekimennu ve-ishah yeferennu* establishes that the capacity for *kiyum* and the capacity for *hafarah* are coextensive, and no *kal va-chomer* can undo that. The earlier argumentation, understood in this light, was not a frontal assault on the *kal va-chomer's* logical form, but a preparation for the conclusion: even granting the *sevara*, a *pasuk* takes precedence, and here we have precisely such a *pasuk*.
