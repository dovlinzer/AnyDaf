# Chagigah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Prior Review

### Opening

We pick up today on Daf 20, *amud bet*, three lines from the top, beginning with the passage *hatovel lechulin vehuchzak lechulin*.

### Theme Overview

This passage sits at the heart of a *perek* whose animating theme will carry through the following *perek* as well — a somewhat unusual structural phenomenon, though not without precedent. The subject is the laws of *tumah v'taharah* as they generate distinct levels of care required for increasingly elevated degrees of *kedushah*, and how those graduated requirements effectively stratify society. Those who eat their *chulin b'taharah* occupy a different social stratum from those who maintain the purity standards required for *terumah*, who in turn differ from those who handle *kodesh*. This entire structure of differentiation is, as we have seen, suspended on the *regel* in the interest of communal unity.

### Earlier Cases Summary

The *Mishnah* has already given us several cases, and in each the *Gemara* has moved to limit or qualify the stated rule in some measure. The first ruling concerned washing hands before eating *chulin*, which the *Gemara* restricted to a case of eating bread specifically — not a general obligation covering all handling of non-sanctified food. Next came the question of *kavanah* at the *mikvah*: the simple reading of the *Mishnah* would have suggested that immersion without intent is entirely without effect, but the *Gemara* clarified that absent *kavanah* the immersion is still valid for *chulin* purposes — a ruling that remains practically significant today for a *niddah* going to the *mikvah*, where *kavanah* is not treated as an impediment. Finally, and more surprisingly, the *Gemara* entertained the position that open-ended *kavanah* — or the absence of any specific intent — can be retroactively defined at a higher level, provided one is still wet from the immersion. In each instance, the *Gemara* is engaged in a project of measured mitigation.

### Post-Churban Shift

It is worth pausing to note that the entire institution of *chaberim* — those who consumed their *chulin b'taharah* and who constituted the most punctilious stratum of this social structure — appears to have faded substantially after the *Churban HaBayit*, or at least within a generation or two of it. This makes sense: these are fundamentally Temple-oriented practices, and it is not even entirely clear that *Chazal* themselves instituted them. They may have emerged organically from Temple-adjacent circles, possibly with connections to more Temple-centered groups. By a later stage in the *Gemara*, these practices are largely no longer observed.

What *Chazal* did preserve, and indeed universalized, was a different set of mechanisms for introducing *kedushah* into the act of eating. The washing of hands before bread — which everybody does equally — evokes the world of *tumah v'taharah* while eliminating the sharp stratification. Similarly, the requirement to recite *brachot* before eating, about which the *Gemara* states that one who eats without a *bracha* has acted as though committing *me'ilah* against *hekdesh*, brings an awareness of *kedushah* to every meal in a way that is uniformly accessible. These are practices that carry the spirit of *taharat ha-kodesh* into daily life without fracturing the community.

## Hierarchy Mishna

### Mishna Text

With that context in mind, we reach the passage itself. The *Mishnah* states: one who immerses for *chulin* purposes and is established as intending purity for *chulin* — *Rashi* reads *huchzak* as denoting that intent — achieves a status fit for *chulin* but not for *maaser*. One who immerses for *maaser* achieves fitness for *maaser* but not for *terumah*. One who immerses for *terumah* achieves fitness for *terumah* but not for *kodesh*, and so forth.

### Sages vs Rabbi Meir

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mani matnitin* — whose position does our *Mishnah* follow? The answer is *Rabbanan hi* — it reflects the position of the Sages in the *Baraita* cited earlier. Recall the dispute between the Sages and *Rabbi Meir* over whether one must wash hands before eating *maaser*. The Sages held that *maaser* does occupy a distinct level above *chulin*, and this *Mishnah*, which treats *maaser* as a separate rung in the hierarchy, reflects precisely that view rather than *Rabbi Meir*'s, who treated *maaser* and *chulin* as equivalent in this regard.

### Seifa Clothes Status

The *Gemara* then says *eima seifa* — let us examine the end of the *Mishnah*. There it states: *bigdei am ha'aretz midras le-perushim* — the garments of an *am ha'aretz* are considered *midras*, that is, they carry the status of having been sat upon by a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah*, making them an *av hatumah* for *perushim*, those who eat their *chulin b'taharah*. The *Mishnah* then states that the garments of *perushim* are *midras* for those who eat *terumah*.

### Maaser Gap Problem

One notices immediately that the group of people who are careful specifically for *maaser b'taharah* is entirely absent. The hierarchy leaps directly from those careful for *chulin* to those careful for *terumah*, bypassing the intermediate *maaser* category entirely. The *Gemara* notes: *atan le-Rabbi Meir* — we have now arrived at the position of *Rabbi Meir*, who holds that *chulin* and *maaser* are treated identically and therefore generates no separate social stratum for those careful about *maaser*. This creates an apparent inconsistency: the *reisha* of the *Mishnah* seems to follow the Sages, while the *seifa* follows *Rabbi Meir*. The *Gemara* considers whether the *Mishnah* could simply combine two positions from different authorities, and suggests that perhaps that is indeed the case — *reisha Rabbanan u-seifa Rabbi Meir*. Since both parts of the *Mishnah* are anonymous, there is nothing formally objectionable about attributing them to different schools.

### Rav Acha's Amendment

*Rav Acha bar Adda* takes a different approach. He reports that he would teach the *seifa* with an additional, fifth level inserted — a group of people who are careful about eating *maaser b'taharah* — and on that reading the entire *Mishnah* is consistent with the Sages throughout, reflecting throughout the position that *maaser* constitutes a distinct stratum.

## Upward Mobility Question

### Rav Mari Position

The *Gemara* now introduces a new question by way of *Rav Mari*: *amar Rav Mari shema mina chullin she-naasu al taharat ha-kodesh ke-kodesh dami* — if someone treats his *chulin* with the same degree of care as *kodesh*, maintaining the same stringent *kavanah* at the *mikvah* and the same vigilance against any form of contamination that would compromise *kodesh* even if it would not affect *chulin*, then that *chulin* has the status of *kodesh*.

The practical question this generates is the following. Consider a person who is not a *kohen*, who does not eat *korbanot*, who has not recently been in the *Beis Hamikdash* — in short, who eats only *chulin*, never *kodesh* in any literal sense. Yet every single thing he eats, he treats as though it were a *korban*. What is the status of his garments? Are they *midras* to *ochlei terumah* because, at the end of the day, he is a man who eats only *chulin b'taharah*? Or, since the level of care he exhibits is that of *al taharat ha-kodesh*, do we place him in that category — such that his clothes are entirely *tahor* for everyone except someone handling a *parah adumah*? This is the question of whether exhibiting a higher level of care can elevate a person into a higher social stratum, even without the technical act of eating *kodesh*.

### Category Inference

The *Gemara* presses: *mimai* — from where does *Rav Mari* derive his inference? He argues from the fact that the *Mishnah* does not list someone who eats *chulin al taharat ha-kodesh* as a separate, distinct category. Since no new stratum is created for such a person, *Rav Mari* reasons that the *Mishnah* must be absorbing him into the existing *kodesh* category.

The *Gemara* rejects this inference immediately. The absence of a separate stratum does not tell you which existing group he falls into. Perhaps he belongs in the *chulin* group, since at the end of the day his food remains *chulin*. Or perhaps, as a compromise reflecting that he exhibits *kodesh*-level care but eats only *chulin*, he falls into the *terumah* group. Or perhaps *Rav Mari* is right and he belongs in the *kodesh* group. The *Mishnah*'s silence simply cannot determine the question.

### Baraita: No Elevation

The *Gemara* therefore turns to a *Baraita*: *chullin she-naasu al taharat ha-kodesh — harei ke-chulin*. One who treats his *chulin* with the purity level of *kodesh* — it remains, nonetheless, as *chulin*. The extra care confers no upward mobility whatsoever. This is a striking position: no matter how scrupulous one's behavior, if one does not actually eat *kodesh*, one cannot exit the *chulin* category. There is stratification without any upward mobility — a society divided into levels with no mechanism for ascending.

### Elazar Bar Tzadok

*Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok* dissents: *harei ki-trumah* — such a person achieves the level of *terumah*. This is something of a middle path. It does not go all the way to *kodesh*, but it does allow some upward mobility. Demonstrating the care of *al taharat ha-kodesh* lifts you at least one level above your actual eating category.

### Historical Examples

The *Gemara* then cites evidence from the end of our *Mishnah* itself. *Yosei ben Yoezer*, identified as *chasid shebakehuna*, one of the most pious of *kohanim*, nonetheless could never get his garments out of *midras* status for those eating *kodesh* — his *mitpachto* remained an *av hatumah* for *ochlei korbanot*. By contrast, *Yochanan ben Gudgada*, who ate all his food *al taharat ha-kodesh* — the language itself suggesting that he was not in fact eating actual *kodesh* but was maintaining that level of care — found that his garments were considered *midras lachatas*, that is, problematic for someone handling a *parah adumah*, but *lo le-kodesh* — not a problem for those eating *korbanot*. This appears to demonstrate clearly that eating *chulin al taharat ha-kodesh* is recognized as equivalent to *kodesh* status for purposes of the social hierarchy.

### Mishna Position

The *Gemara* concludes: *alma kasavar chullin she-naasu al taharat ha-kodesh ke-kodesh dami* — our *Mishnah* holds that if one exhibits *kodesh*-level care, one enters the *kodesh* category, allowing full upward mobility. This, notably, is yet another instance of the *Gemara* introducing a mitigating or democratizing element: the structure remains stratified, but the stratification is at least permeable. Anyone willing to exhibit the requisite level of care can place themselves in the higher group, regardless of their technical eating category.

## Conscious Attention Rule

### Fallen Scarf Case

*Rabbi Yochanan ben Elazar* teaches: if one's scarf (*mafrato*) fell from him, and he said to his friend, "Please hand it to me," and the friend handed it back, it is considered *temeiah*.

### Care Explanation

The reasoning requires unpacking. Even if the friend is himself *tahor*, the moment someone else is handling the object, the original owner is no longer in a position to exercise the same direct, unmediated care. The owner, consciously or not, relaxes his vigilance — he asked the friend without first vetting him, without asking "are you *tahor*? to what level are you careful?" The absence of that inquiry signals that the owner is not operating at the full level of concern, and therefore we cannot assume the garment has been maintained at the required standard.

This introduces what will become one of the most demanding and, one might say, unsettling principles of this entire discussion: it is not merely the objective condition of an object that determines its *taharah* status, but the active, conscious attention of the person responsible for it. The moment that attention is compromised — by delegating handling to another, by momentary confusion about the identity of the object — the presumption of *taharah* dissolves.

The *Gemara* then considers whether the physical location where the object fell should matter — say, if it fell in a *reshus ha-rabim*. The *Gemara* proceeds to address this in context, and we will follow its lead.

### Supporting Cases

Two additional cases extend the principle. *Rabbi Yochanan ben Amram* teaches: if one went to one's closet intending to take out weekday clothes and accidentally took out Shabbat clothes — *nischalafu lo keilim shel Shabbos b'keilim shel chol* — and dressed in them, they are considered *tamei*. This seems entirely bizarre: these are your own clothes, in your own closet, and you simply grabbed the wrong ones. *Rashi*'s reading of the case has it that the person wanted the weekday garments and inadvertently took the Shabbat ones.

A further case: *Elazar ben Tzadok* reports an incident involving two women who were *chaveros* — that is, they were not *amei ha'aretz* but women careful about *taharah* — whose garments became mixed up in the bathhouse (*beis ha-merchatz*). They came before *Rabbi Akiva*, who ruled that the garments were *tamei*. The common thread in the last two cases is that whenever one is not handling one's own identified object with the expected level of care, the presumption of *taharah* fails.

### Gemara Challenge

*Rav Oshaya* raises a strong objection. If the rule is simply that any lapse in full conscious attention renders an object *tamei*, then consider: one reached into a basket to take a wheat roll and a barley roll came up in one's hand — does that too become *tamei*? And if one says, yes, why not, a *Baraita* contradicts this: *ha-mishamer et ha-chavis be-chezkat she-hu yayin ve-nimtza she-hu shemen — tehorah mi-litamei*. If one was guarding a barrel assuming it was wine and it turned out to be oil, it remains *tehorah* — it will not be *metamei* other things. So confusion about the identity of the object seems not to matter there.

But the *Gemara* notes that *Rav Oshaya* did not quote the end of that *Baraita*: *ve'asurah me'achol* — it may not be eaten. So we have a strange middle position: the barrel does not transmit *tumah* to other things, yet the person who maintains *taharah* standards cannot eat from it. How can something be *tahor* enough not to spread *tumah* yet simultaneously be disqualified from consumption? The resolution, offered by *Rabbi Yirmiyah*, is that the owner declared: *shimartia mi-davar ha-metame'ah velo mi-davar ha-poslah* — "I guarded it against something that would make it *tamei* [i.e., able to pass on *tumah*], but not against something that would merely render it *pasul* [i.e., *tamei* at a level too low to pass on]." There is, in other words, selective *shemirah* — the owner was deliberately guarding against an *av ha-tumah* or *rishon* reaching the barrel, but was not concerned about a *sheni le-tumah* touching it.

## Barrel & Provision C (II)

### Basket Figs Shovel

The *Gemara* pursues the logic of selective *shemirah* further with a new *Baraita*. One extended one's hand into a basket, carried the basket on one's shoulder, and inside the basket there were figs along with a type of shovel (*magrefa*) — *Rashi* explains this was used to separate or break up the strings of figs. *Ve-haya be-libo al ha-sal velo haya be-libo al ha-magrefa* — when one was protecting the basket, one's attention was on the figs; one was not even aware the shovel was in there. The ruling: *ha-sal tahor ve-ha-magrefa tme'iah* — the basket and its figs are *tahor*, because that is what the owner had in mind, but the shovel is *tamei*, even though it was sitting right there in the same basket throughout.

### Selective Tumah

The *Gemara* then probes: granted the shovel cannot render the basket directly *tamei* — *ein kli metamei kli*, a vessel cannot impart *tumah* to another vessel at a level beyond *rishon* — but let the shovel, as a *rishon*, render the food (the figs) *tamei* at the level of *sheni*? *Ravina* answers with the familiar formula: *be-omer sheimartiv mi-davar she-metame'o velo mi-davar ha-poslo* — the owner declared that he protected the shovel from an *av ha-tumah* reaching it, so it could not become a *rishon*; but he did not protect it from a *rishon* reaching it, so it may have become a *sheni*. Since a *sheni* cannot render a vessel *tamei* — *ein kli metamei kli* — the basket remains *tahor*. The shovel itself is *pasul* but not capable of passing on *tumah* at the level of *tamei*.

### Gemara Objection

The *Gemara* raises its *teima*: *umi ika netirusa la-palga* — is it really possible to watch something in such a selective and stratified way, carefully guarding against an *av ha-tumah* but not against a *rishon*? This seems extraordinarily improbable as a lived reality. How does one say, in effect, "I will protect this from *tumah* of category A but not category B"? The *Gemara* answers *in* — yes, it is possible — and cites the *Baraita* of the basket as proof that such selective *shemirah* can in fact operate. But the *Gemara*'s own question registers the genuine strangeness of this world: an entire cognitive and spiritual discipline of attention in which one must track not only what one is protecting but from which precise categories of *tumah* one is protecting it.

## Woman Weaver Case

### Woman's Claim

A woman came before *Rabbi Yishmael* and said to him: *Rabbi, beged zeh aragtihu be-taharah velo haya be-libi leshomro be-taharah* — "Rabbi, I wove this garment in a state of *taharah*, in the sense that nothing happened to contaminate it, but I was not consciously and intentionally guarding it for purposes of *taharah*." She is reporting on a factual outcome: nothing came in contact with the garment. But she acknowledges that this outcome was not the product of deliberate, active *shemirah*; it simply happened.

### Investigation Discovery

*Rabbi Yishmael* then questioned her closely — *umitoch bedikot shehayah Rabbi Yishmael bodek* — and in the course of his investigation something came to light. She said to him: *Rabbi, niddah mashchah imi ba-kevel* — a woman who was a *niddah* had helped her set up the frame of the loom, the rope on which the garment would be woven, and it was possible that she had actually touched the garment in the process.

### Active Guard Requirement

The lesson is unambiguous. It is insufficient to report retroactively that nothing bad happened. Active, conscious guarding is required — not a factual accounting after the fact. The moment that active protection is absent, one must assume the worst case. This principle connects directly to everything we have seen: when the scarf is handed back by a friend, when the wrong clothes are taken from the closet, when the shovel lies unattended in the basket — in each case, the instant that conscious attention is not specifically directed at the object, we do not rely on the favorable outcome. We require the *shemirah* itself, not the result.

### Sages' Wisdom

*Rabbi Yishmael* responds: *kama gedolim divrei chachamim she-hayu omerim be-libo lishmor — tahor; ein be-libo lishmor — tamei*. How great are the words of the Sages, who said: only when one is intentionally guarding it is it *tahor*; if one's intent is not directed at guarding it, it is *tamei* — even if one can report that factually nothing occurred. *Rabbi Yishmael*'s exclamation is not merely formal praise; it reflects the insight that human attention is itself fallible, and the case of the *niddah* who helped with the loom frame demonstrates exactly how something can go wrong without the owner's awareness, even when she reports confidently that "nothing happened." The Sages foresaw this, and the requirement of *belibo lishmor* is the safeguard.

### Tablecloth Case

A second, closely parallel incident follows. Again a woman came before *Rabbi Yishmael*: *Rabbi, mappa zu argatiha be-taharah velo haya be-libi leshomrah* — "I wove this tablecloth and it happened to be *be-taharah*, but I was not consciously intending to guard it." The investigation (*bedikot*) that *Rabbi Yishmael* conducted revealed the following: *nima nifseka li ve-kashartiha be-fi* — a thread broke at some point during the weaving and she held it with her mouth to tie it. *Rashi* explains that she was a *niddah* when she was setting up the frame, before the garment had reached the minimum size of three by three finger-breadths required for *tumah* susceptibility. She then went to the *mikvah* and continued weaving. But once the garment reached the requisite size, some of her saliva from the broken thread was still on it, and the garment became *tamei*. *Tosafot* offers an alternative reading, but the details are secondary. The principle is the same. *Rabbi Yishmael* repeats his formulation verbatim: *kama gedolim divrei chachamim she-hayu omerim be-libo lishmor — tahor; ein be-libo lishmor — tamei*.

## Barrel & Provision C (II)

### Barrel Distance Case

The *Gemara* now returns to resolve the outstanding tension. A *Baraita* teaches: if one watched a barrel of wine, assuming it was being kept *tahor*, even from a distance, it remains *tahor*.

### Partial Watching

*Rashi* interprets this as precisely the case of selective *shemirah* we have been discussing: the person watched specifically for things that would make the barrel *tamei* in the full sense — that is, *metamei* other things — but not for things that would merely *pasul* it, i.e., render it *tamei* at a low enough level that it could not pass on *tumah*. This is why the barrel remains *tahor* from the standpoint of not spreading *tumah*, even though it may be *pasul* for eating.

The cases of the Shabbat and weekday clothes, and the garments in the bathhouse, operate on the principle of *mesiach da'at* — a withdrawal of attention — in the following way. In the bathhouse case, *bishlema le-Rabbi Elazar ben Tzadok*, each woman says to herself: *chaverti eishet am ha-aretz* — my friend who took my garment is probably married to an *am ha'aretz* — *u-mascha da'ata minah* — and therefore she stops paying attention to it. She does not know who took her garment or how careful that person will be, and so she ceases to maintain the vigilance required. That is the easiest case.

In the case of *Rabbi Yonatan ben Amram* — the Shabbat and weekday clothes — the explanation is: *keivan de-mane de-Shabbos avid lehu shimur tefei* — one is normally more careful about one's Shabbat clothes, keeping them at a higher standard of *taharah*. When one reached into the closet on a weekday and took out what one thought were weekday clothes, one was operating with the level of care appropriate to weekday clothes — slightly less than the level one would bring to Shabbat garments. Even though one's weekday level of care is itself sufficient, the Shabbat clothes were handled at a level below their usual standard, and that differential is enough to compromise their *taharah* status. This is parallel to the basket case: the fruit receives full protection because one's attention is directed at it, while the shovel receives less because one's attention is not.

The remaining puzzle is the case of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Elazar* — where one's scarf falls and a friend hands it back while one is watching the entire time. The *Gemara* asks: why should this not work? One is observing the object every second. The *Gemara* answers with *Rav Yochanan*'s principle: *chazakah ein adam meshamer mah she-beyad chavero* — there is a presumption that a person does not exercise adequate *shemirah* over something that is in someone else's hands. Even if you are watching, once another person is handling it, you unconsciously rely on that person in some measure, and your level of effective vigilance falls below what is required.

## Workers Exception

### Workers Tahor Case

A *Baraita* now introduces what seems, in context, a surprising exception. If one's donkey drivers and workers were carrying *tahor* objects — *Rashi* specifies sealed pottery vessels, which can only become *tamei* if something touches the *inside*, so the workers' handling of the exterior does not render the contents *tamei* — and one separated from them by more than a *mil* (roughly a kilometer), the vessels remain *tehorot*. One need not worry that the workers tampered with the contents.

### Nafcha Explanation

*Rav Yitzchak Nafcha* explains the *reisha*: *be-metaher chamarav u-fo'alav le-kach* — this is a case where the owner specifically immersed his donkey drivers and workers in the *mikvah* beforehand, deputized them, and instructed them to maintain *taharah*. Furthermore, they remain vigilant because the owner might arrive by an unexpected shortcut at any moment — *de-ba'atu mi-derech akalton* — and so they do not relax their care.

### Lost Vigilance

But if the owner said to them, *lechu va'ani avo achareichem* — "go on ahead, I'll catch up" — then *keivan she-nitalmu einav mehen tehorot tame'iot*: once they are out of sight and the workers know the owner is not going to arrive suddenly, they relax — *meyatva yatva da'ataihu* — their minds settle, and we must assume the vessels became *tamei*. The threat of an unexpected visit is removed, and with it the pressure that sustained their vigilance.

### Contradiction Tension

This case stands in a certain ironic tension with everything preceding it. There, we saw that taking the wrong clothes out of one's own closet, or having a friend hand back one's scarf while one is watching, renders the object *tamei* — the standard is impossibly demanding. Here, workers who are *amei ha'aretz* carry sealed vessels for over a kilometer out of the owner's sight, and we rule the contents remain *tahor* — because the owner made them *tahor* and the threat of an unannounced inspection looms. It is a notable contrast.

### Tosafot Takanas

*Tosafot* raises a related difficulty: if *amei ha'aretz* are treated as having the status of a *zav* (since we presume they sit anywhere and their seats carry *midras* status), then simply hiring workers who are *amei ha'aretz* should render everything they handle *tamei* immediately. *Tosafot* resolves this by invoking *takanas ha-shuk* — a rabbinic accommodation to market realities. In order to make ordinary commercial activity viable, to allow one to hire day laborers who would naturally come from the broader population rather than exclusively from rabbinic circles, the law is shaped in a way that does not press every principle to its logical extreme. This *takanas ha-shuk* explains why the seemingly incoherent juxtaposition of extreme demands in certain cases and considerable latitude in others is not simply inconsistent, but reflects a deliberate calibration of *halacha* to the practical necessities of a functioning society.

## Second Mishna Intro

### Mishna Text

We now arrive at a new *Mishnah*: *chomer ba-kodesh mi-be-trumah* — there are greater stringencies governing *kodesh* (the consumption and handling of *korbanot*) than *terumah*. The *Mishnah* proceeds to enumerate them systematically, and the *Gemara* will spend the coming days unpacking each one.

### Vessel Immersion

The first stringency: *she-matbilin kelim be-toch kelim la-trumah aval lo la-kodesh* — for *terumah* purposes, vessels may be immersed stacked one inside the other, since we assume the *mikvah* water permeates adequately and there is no concern of *chatzitzah* from one vessel merely resting in another. For *kodesh*, however, each vessel must be immersed individually.

### Vessel Sections

Second: *achorayim ve-toch ve-beis ha-tzvita be-trumah aval lo be-kodesh*. For *terumah* purposes, different sections of a vessel — the exterior back, the interior, and the grip — are treated as having distinct *tumah* statuses. If the exterior becomes *tamei* through a rabbinic *tumah*, only the exterior is *tamei*; the interior remains *tahor*. This is because rabbinic *tumah* is deliberately calibrated to be less encompassing than biblical *tumah*, so that only the section touched is affected. (The parallel with hand-washing is precise: by rabbinic *tumah*, hands can become *tamei* without the body, exactly as the exterior of a vessel can become *tamei* without the interior.) For *kodesh*, this leniency does not apply — any *tamei* section renders the entire vessel *tamei*, as is the standard rule by biblical *tumah*.

### Carrying Restrictions

Third: *ha-noseh et ha-midras noseh et ha-trumah aval lo et ha-kodesh*. One may simultaneously carry on one shoulder a *midras* — an object sat upon by a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah*, which carries *av ha-tumah* status — and on the other shoulder a sealed pottery barrel of *terumah*. Since a pottery vessel becomes *tamei* only through contact with its interior, and carrying on one's shoulder does not constitute contact with the interior, the *terumah* remains *tahor*. For *kodesh*, this leniency does not apply; one may not carry the two together.

### Clothes Midras

Fourth: *bigdei ochlei trumah midras la-kodesh*. The garments of those who eat *terumah* — who are themselves careful and maintain a high standard of *taharah* — are nonetheless treated as *midras* for those who eat *kodesh*. The social hierarchy we have been tracing throughout this *perek* thus appears once more: each rung below is *midras* for the rung above.

### Garment Prep

Fifth: *she-ba-kodesh matir u-menageiv u-matbil ve-achar kach kosher* — for a garment to be immersed for *kodesh* purposes, if it is knotted (as garments often were when sent for laundering), one must first untie the knots, dry off any external moisture (which would constitute a *chatzitzah*), then immerse in the *mikvah*, and only afterward retie. *Uv-trumah kosher ve-achar kach matbil* — for *terumah*, one may leave the garment knotted and with liquid on it and immerse in that condition, just as vessels may be immersed stacked.

### New Vessels

Sixth: *keilim ha-nigmarim be-taharah tzerichin tvilah la-kodesh* — even a newly completed vessel, made entirely under conditions of *taharah*, with the maker consciously guarding it throughout the production process, nonetheless requires immersion in the *mikvah* before its first use for *kodesh*. For *terumah*, no such requirement applies to a vessel made in *taharah*.

### Contents Unit

Seventh: *ha-kli metzaref mah she-be-tocho la-kodesh aval lo la-trumah*. For *kodesh* purposes, a vessel unifies its contents: if one of ten apples inside a vessel is touched and thereby rendered *tamei*, all ten apples are treated as a single unit and all become equally *tamei*. For *terumah*, each item inside the vessel retains its individual status; only the apple actually touched is affected.

### Derivation Levels

Eighth: *ha-revi'i ba-kodesh pasul ve-ha-shlishi ba-trumah*. The chain of derived *tumah* extends one level further for *kodesh* than for *terumah*. A fourth-level derivation (*revi'i*) disqualifies (*pasul*) food designated for *kodesh*. For *terumah*, only a third-level derivation (*shlishi*) has that effect; a *revi'i* does nothing.

### Hand Washing

Ninth: *uv-trumah im nitmas achat mi-yadav chavertah tehorah* — for *terumah* purposes, if one hand becomes *tamei* through a rabbinic *tumah*, the other hand, if carefully watched, remains *tahor* and need not be washed. Since rabbinic *tumah* of the hands does not affect the rest of the body, and since the two hands are, to that extent, treated as distinct, one *tamei* hand does not compromise the other. *Uv-kodesh matbil shteihen she-ha-yad metamei et chavertah ba-kodesh aval lo be-trumah* — for *kodesh*, one hand imparts its *tumah* to the other, so both must be immersed (or washed), whereas for *terumah* this is not the rule.

### Dry Food Rule

Tenth: *ochlin neguvim be-yadayim mesoavos be-trumah aval lo be-kodesh*. Food that has never come in contact with liquid — *neguvim* meaning not merely dry but never wetted — cannot become susceptible to *tumah* at all, and therefore even if one's hands are *tamei* (*mesoavot*), one may eat such food for *terumah* purposes, since there is no mechanism by which the hands could render the food *tamei*. For *kodesh*, however, even if the food could not possibly become *tamei*, one must still wash one's hands before eating it.

### Onein Rule

Finally: *ha-onein u-mechusar kipurim tzerichin tvilah la-kodesh* — two categories of person who require immersion before eating *kodesh*, even though in each case one might suppose they are technically done with their *tumah* process.

An *onein* is a mourner in the period between death and burial. If the *onein* did not become *tamei* in any technical sense during this period — if, say, they had no contact with the *meit* — they nonetheless require immersion before eating *kodesh*. A *mechusar kipurim* is a person — such as a *zav*, *zavah*, or *metzora* — who has gone through the full *taharah* process including immersion, waited the requisite period, and now needs only to bring the completing *korban*. According to Torah law, once that *korban* is brought, the person is entirely *tahor*. Yet even after bringing the *korban*, a *mechusar kipurim* must immerse again in the *mikvah* before eating *kodesh*. And notably, having immersed previously for the *tumah* itself, this additional immersion is specifically a *kodesh* requirement and does not apply to *terumah*. These distinctions will occupy our attention in the coming days.
