# Gittin 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dual Shlichus Problem

### Wife's Shaliach Reception

The daf opens in the middle of 63b, continuing an extended discussion about a fundamental question in the laws of *gittin*: can a wife appoint a *shaliach* to receive her *get* at the very same time that the husband appoints a *shaliach* to deliver it? Two concerns animate this question. The first is whether this arrangement resembles *chatzer haba mi-kochi* — that the wife is somehow not actively involved in receiving the *get* — raising doubts about whether her appointment of a *shaliach* constitutes genuine reception. The second is whether the husband would fundamentally object to such an arrangement and therefore never truly consent to it.

### Shaliach Role Switch

The Gemara addresses this through a narrative case. A man sent a *get* to his wife. The *shaliach* went and found her kneading dough — her hands were occupied, which is why she did what she did next. The *shaliach* said to her, *heilach gita'ich*, here is your *get*. She responded: *yehei b'yadcha* — hold onto it. In other words, she appointed this very same man, the husband's *shaliach*, to now serve as her *shaliach l'kabala*.

### Chazar Shlichus Principle

Rav Nachman said: if we were to rule in accordance with Rabbi Chanina — that a wife can indeed appoint a *shaliach* to receive the *get* from the husband's *shaliach* — then I would have ruled that this case is valid. After all, the husband had appointed the man as his *shaliach*, and the wife subsequently appointed him as her *shaliach* to receive it.

### Simultaneous Representation

Rava objected: even if we rule like Rabbi Chanina, this case would still be problematic — *lo chazar shlichus eitzel ha'ba'al*. There is a principle that the *shaliach*'s role as the husband's representative must persist through the completion of his *shlichus*, such that he can return and report that he fulfilled his mission. This does not mean literally that he must remain alive afterward, nor that the *shlichus* cannot be time-limited. Rather, the problem here is that at the very moment of giving the *get*, the *shaliach* cannot simultaneously occupy both roles — representing the husband in the act of giving while also representing the wife in the act of receiving. When he switches to become the wife's *shaliach*, he ceases to represent the husband. There is also a lack of a proper act of *nesina*. He has essentially given the *get* to himself, which is no giving at all. The *shlichus* on the husband's side therefore fails at the critical moment.

### Rabbi Ami's Opinion

The question was sent to Rabbi Ami for his ruling. He responded: *lo chazar shlichus eitzel ha'ba'al* — the *shlichus* did not return to the husband, meaning this arrangement is invalid.

### Safek Megureshes Status

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba declared that the matter requires further investigation — it is a significant question with major consequences. They sent the question back to him, and after some time, sent it again asking what he had concluded. His response, offered with a certain wry humor given the context of *shlichus*, was: just as you have repeated doubts and keep asking me, I too have doubts. I cannot resolve this question.

Since it involves *davar she'be'erva* — a matter affecting personal status — he was compelled to rule with caution: she is a *safek megureshes*. Practically, this means that if the husband were to die and there is a *yavam*, she cannot perform *yibbum* because she might already be a *gerusha*, yet she is not free from the *yibbum* obligation entirely because she might be an *almana*. She is therefore required to perform *chalitza*. The case thus closes with a formal *safek* as to whether the principle of *chazar shlichus eitzel ha'ba'al* invalidates this arrangement entirely.

### Nafsha Case & Shlichus Completed

The Gemara now introduces a case involving a man known as Nafsha. Witnesses were sent to write his *get*, but they wrote the name "Tafsha" instead. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta ruled in the name of Rav: the witnesses have completed their *shlichus*. Since the husband appointed them as his *shlichim* to write the *get* and they wrote one — even if incorrectly — they have discharged their agency. If a new *get* is needed, the husband must reappoint them.

### Rewriting Defective Get

Rava challenged this: when the husband said "write," did he say *kesuvu v'chasuvu* — write a piece of pottery? He did not tell them to write just any document. Writing a *get* means writing a *kosher get*. Since they did not write a proper *get*, they have not yet fulfilled their *shlichus*, and they may rewrite it.

Rava then addressed a different scenario: suppose the witnesses wrote a perfectly valid *get* but it was subsequently lost before they could deliver it. In that case, *asu eidim shlichusam* — the witnesses have completed their agency of writing. If the husband wants another *get* written, he would need to appoint them anew.

### Unified Writing & Delivery

Rav Nachman challenged Rava's latter position: when the husband said "write a *get*," did he say *kesuvu v'anchu b'kisaychu* — write it and keep it in your pocket? The entire purpose of writing a *get* was to write it and deliver it. Writing and delivery are one indivisible package. Until a kosher *get* has actually been delivered to the wife, the *shlichus* is not complete, and the witnesses may write and rewrite as many times as necessary — *kosvin v'nosnin afilu mea pe'amim*.

### Tosafot: Bad vs. Lost Get

Tosafot raises a careful distinction. Tosafot understands that where a *get* was written and is not clearly defective but its validity is uncertain — one does not know whether it was written properly — the witnesses have completed their *shlichus* and it is considered *kasher*. They should not write another *get* in that circumstance.

This position seems puzzling at first glance. If we are uncertain whether they completed their *shlichus*, why not simply write another *get*? If the first was valid they did not need the second, and if it was not, the second would fix the problem. Tosafot's concern is that one should not give the woman a *get* that is only *safek kasher*. Since you do not wish to hand her a document of uncertain validity, and yet if it was in fact valid you are no longer authorized to write another, the resulting difficulty is that you cannot hand over a *get* of which you are proud. Tosafot therefore concludes: it is best to instruct the husband to say explicitly that he authorizes the witnesses to do whatever is necessary until a fully kosher *get* is delivered with certainty. That formulation resolves all the potential difficulties.

### Get Given to Shaliach Lost

A question was then posed to Rava and Rav Nachman: suppose the husband says to witnesses *kitvu u-tenu le-shaliach* — write the *get* and give it to the *shaliach*. The witnesses do so, but the *shaliach* subsequently loses the *get* or dies in transit before delivering it. What is the rule? Have the witnesses been *siluki* — effectively dismissed, their role complete — such that they would need to be reappointed? Or perhaps the husband's instruction to give the *get* to a *shaliach* rather than deliver it themselves was merely a concession to spare them the trouble of the journey, while their underlying obligation still encompassed ensuring the delivery. Since the *get* was never in fact delivered, they remain empowered to rewrite.

The question turns on how one reads the instruction: did the husband intend the witnesses' mandate to end the moment they handed the document to the *shaliach*, or did he simply mean to make their task easier while still expecting them to see the *get* through to completion? The Gemara leaves the matter unresolved — *teiku*.

### Ravina's Refinement

Ravina posed a related refinement to Rav Ashi: what if the husband says not merely "give it to the *shaliach*" but *ve-yolich* — "give it to the *shaliach* and let him deliver it to her"? In that formulation, the husband explicitly mentions to the witnesses that the *shaliach* is to be the one to hand it over. Perhaps this explicit mention suggests that part of the witnesses' responsibility is to ensure the delivery occurs, and until it does, their *shlichus* is incomplete. This too is left as *teiku*.

### Kabbalah Language Baraita

The *Mishnah* concludes with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel ruling that even a woman who says *ha-motzi li gitti* — take from me my *get* — has appointed the recipient as a *shaliach kabbalah*, not a *shaliach holachah*, and the husband therefore cannot retract after handing over the *get*.

A *Baraita* elaborates on the language of reception: *toli*, *sali*, *yehei b'yadcha* — "lift it for me," "carry it for me," "let it be in your hand" — all of these are formulations of *lashon kabbalah*, not formulations of conveyance. The Gemara makes clear that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is not introducing a novel controversy but is rather clarifying a matter on which there is no real dispute. Thus, the determination of whether a *shaliach* is a *shaliach holachah* or a *shaliach l'kabala* depends both on the language used and on the context in which that language was spoken — and both of these have significant consequences for whether the husband retains the ability to retract and for what type of *shlichus* the parties have created.

## Evidence & Proof Requirements

### Two Sets of Witnesses

The Gemara now turns to a different and very important problem within the law of *shlichus* in *gittin*: how one proves that a valid *get* was in fact delivered through a *shaliach*. This question operates on two levels. First, there is the evidentiary question of how the woman demonstrates that she is divorced. Second, and more fundamentally, there is the question of whether *eidei kiyum* — witnesses whose presence makes an act halachically binding, not merely provable after the fact — are required for the *gerushin* itself. This second question implicates the principle that *gerushin* is a *davar she'be'erva* and *ein davar she'be'erva pachot mi-shnayim*.

The *Mishnah* rules: a woman who said to someone *hitkabel li giti* — receive my *get* for me — requires two sets of witnesses. Two witnesses must testify that she appointed him as her *shaliach* in their presence, and two witnesses must testify that he actually received the *get* from the husband and tore it up in their presence. The tearing, the Gemara notes, reflects a particular historical circumstance — a period of Roman persecution during which carrying documents connected to Jewish religious practice was dangerous. The essential testimony, however, is that the *get* was received, not that it was torn.

### Tosafot's Challenge

Tosafot raises a serious question. The *Mishnah* then adds: *afilu hen ha-rishonim ve-hen ha-acharonim* — even if the same witnesses appear at both the appointment and the reception, that is sufficient. Tosafot asks: what is the force of *afilu* here? One might have expected the opposite — that the same witnesses who were present at both stages would be *preferable*, since they can testify as a single body to everything they witnessed without any concern about combining half-testimonies from different groups.

The problem Tosafot is pointing to is the principle of *davar v'lo chatzi davar*: witnesses testify to a matter, not to half a matter. If the witnesses who saw the *get* received knew nothing about the prior appointment, how can their testimony about reception alone constitute valid *eidut* on the act of *gerushin*? The *get* was given to some man by the husband, but those witnesses may not have known he was the wife's *shaliach*.

One might ask why the woman's own testimony cannot simply resolve the matter — she appointed him, after all. The answer is that her testimony alone does not constitute objective evidence. The husband may be contesting the validity of the *get*, and merely because he trusted the *shaliach* at the time of delivery does not mean his wife's word alone suffices to prove retroactively that the *shaliach* was properly authorized. A third party who held himself out as an agent and was believed at the time cannot thereby establish the transaction's validity if the principal later contests it without independent objective evidence.

Tosafot therefore explains the *afilu* differently: it tells us that we do not suspect the wife of having coordinated false witnesses. Ordinarily, the very reason one uses a *shaliach* is that the wife is in a distant location and cannot receive the *get* herself. Given that, it would be quite unlikely that the same witnesses happened to be present both in the location where she appointed him and in the entirely different location where he received the *get*. One might therefore suspect collusion. The *afilu* dispels this concern: even if the same witnesses appear at both events, they are believed and we do not suspect the wife of bribery.

As for the underlying question of *chatzi davar*, Tosafot notes that there are circumstances under which additional parties — such as the *shaliach* himself — could be believed to supply the missing information about his appointment, or the husband could be believed in certain respects. When such alternative means exist to fill the gap, it may not constitute a true case of *chatzi davar* that disqualifies the testimony.

### Same or Different Witnesses

The *Mishnah* further states: *v'echad min ha-rishonim v'echad min ha-acharonim v'echad mitztaref imahem* — even if a single witness overlaps between the two groups, that is also valid. Tosafot explains the novelty here as well: one might have suspected that a single individual who appears at both stages — say, Shimon — was hired by the wife to stitch together a false narrative, testifying alongside Reuven that the *shaliach* was appointed and alongside Levi that he received the *get*. The *Mishnah* tells us we do not entertain this suspicion. The upshot is that it does not matter whether the witnesses at the appointment and the reception are entirely the same, entirely different, or partially overlapping — the requirement is simply that there be witnesses at both stages.

### Shaliach's Credibility

The Gemara now asks: the *Mishnah* speaks of a *shaliach l'kabala* who received the *get* and tore it up. Since he is no longer holding the *get* intact, why are all these witnesses necessary? If he were still holding the *get* whole, might his own credibility suffice to establish what occurred?

### Rav Huna vs. Rav Chisda

A dispute is recorded regarding a *shalish* — a third party holding the *get* — where the husband claims he gave it to him only as a *pikadon*, for safekeeping, while the *shaliach* claims it was given to him as a *get* for the wife to be divorced through. Rav Huna rules that the husband is believed. Rav Chisda rules that the *shaliach* is believed, on the grounds of *d'haimneih* — by entrusting the document to him, the husband has implicitly accepted that whatever the *shaliach* says about it is reliable.

### Same Town Qualifier

Tosafot qualifies the entire dispute: it applies only when all three parties — the husband, the *shaliach*, and the wife — are present in the same town. Rav Huna's reasoning for believing the husband is that if he truly intended to divorce his wife, he could and would have handed the *get* to her directly without any intermediary. That argument collapses if the wife is in a distant city. When the wife is elsewhere, the husband obviously could not have given it to her directly, and therefore the very fact that he chose to use a *shaliach* cannot be taken as evidence against the *get* being for divorce. This also explains, Tosafot notes, why a *shaliach* delivering a *get* from overseas is believed when he says *b'fanai nichtav u-v'fanai nechtam* — the husband has no plausible argument that the *get* was only a *pikadon*, since the wife is plainly not available to receive it herself.

### Hemnei Principle

Rav Chisda's position rests on the principle of *hemnei*: when one entrusts an object to a third party, one implicitly agrees to be bound by whatever that third party later asserts about the nature of the transaction. Tosafot presses the point: even if we accept this principle of implicit trust, how does the *shaliach*'s say-so create an act of *gerushin*? The husband may have trusted him, but if no *get* was actually given as a *get*, the wife is simply not divorced regardless of what the *shaliach* claims.

### Money vs. Get Distinction

The Gemara addresses this through a *Baraita* that records: *hoda'at ba'al din k'me'ah edim dami* — an admission by a party against his own interest is equivalent to a hundred witnesses. And: *v'shalish ne'eman mishneihem* — the third-party custodian is believed more than either principal. The *Baraita* illustrates a case where two parties dispute the context in which funds were deposited with a third party, and the third party's account is definitive.

The Gemara draws the parallel: here too, the husband and wife dispute the context of the *get* entrusted to the *shaliach*, and by analogy the *shaliach* should be believed. The Gemara then asks whether this principle extends to *gittin*. The response is a critical distinction: *shani mamon d'it-yahiv l'mechila* — money is different because one can be *mochel* on it. By entrusting money to a third party, you can implicitly say: whatever he decides about this money, I will acquiesce, including simply giving it away. That implicit acquiescence — *hemnei* — works because money is transferable and the principal can effectively ratify whatever the custodian asserts retroactively, simply by choosing not to contest it.

A *get*, however, is different. Even if the husband implicitly trusted the *shaliach*, if he never actually gave the *get* as a *get* — with *da'at* to divorce his wife — the act of *gerushin* never occurred. The implicit agreement to trust the custodian cannot conjure a *da'at* of divorce where none existed. A *get* cannot be given away by mere acquiescence the way money can be waived. The Gemara does entertain the possibility that the *hemnei* principle could operate here as well — that when you hand a *get* to a *shaliach* you are implicitly saying that whatever he later says about its purpose will retroactively define your intent, meaning that if he says you meant to divorce your wife, that becomes your *da'at* at the time of the giving. But the Gemara acknowledges this is a far greater leap than the monetary analogy.

### Whole Get Held

Returning to the *Mishnah*'s requirement of witnesses, the Gemara asks: the *Mishnah* is discussing a case where the *shaliach* tore the *get*. But if he were still holding the *get* intact, why would witnesses be needed at all? If he is holding a whole, signed *get*, that fact itself constitutes circumstantial evidence that the husband gave it to him. Given that he is holding it, he should have the special standing of a custodian who is believed regarding the context of what he holds. The *shaliach* could then simply state that he received it as a *get*, and the wife would be divorced.

Tosafot refines this: why isn't the *shaliach* believed even when the *get* is torn, on the basis that we can see he is holding torn pieces? The answer is that torn pieces of paper could have come from anywhere — there is no way to verify that these particular fragments were once the husband's *get* and were torn only after reception. A whole, signed *get* in the *shaliach*'s hands is a different matter: it carries with it the implicit credential that the husband entrusted him with it, and that credential supports his believability about the circumstances of the transfer.

### Eidei Mesira Kartei

But even granting that a *shaliach* holding a whole *get* has enhanced credibility, there is a further requirement. The Gemara says: *ha mani? Rabbi Elazar hi d'amar edei mesirah kartei* — this reflects the position of Rabbi Elazar, who holds that witnesses to the actual handover are what effects the *get*, not the witnesses who signed the document. According to Rabbi Elazar, *edei mesira kartei* — it is specifically the witnesses who witness the act of the *get* being handed over that create the binding act of *gerushin*.

This is significant. It is not enough to have circumstantial evidence, however strong, that the *get* was received. Even if one could deduce from the *shaliach*'s possession of an intact document that he must have received it from the husband, that would not satisfy *edei mesira*, because *edei mesira* does not mean proving after the fact that a handover occurred. It means that witnesses must directly witness the act of the *get* being passed from hand to hand. The *gerushin* itself, not merely the evidentiary record of it, requires that act to have been witnessed.

It follows that even if a *shaliach* is holding a whole *get* and would be believed regarding what the husband intended, that would still not replace the need for witnesses to the delivery itself — at least according to Rabbi Elazar.

A note on what requires witnesses: there is an asymmetry worth observing. The husband's *shaliach* appears to carry his own credential in the form of the *get* itself — the fact that he possesses the document is in itself a form of testimony to his appointment. The wife's *shaliach*, by contrast, derives his status entirely from her words, which are unaccompanied by any physical credential. This may explain why the need for witnesses to the appointment was felt most acutely with respect to the wife's *shaliach*, while the husband's *shaliach* gains some implicit credibility from the document he carries.

### Torn Get, No Holdings

The Gemara now considers the situation where the *get* was torn, the *shaliach* is not holding it, and only testimonies are available. The husband says he gave the *get* to the *shaliach l'holacha* as his agent to deliver it. The *shaliach* says yes, the husband gave it to him as a *shaliach l'holacha*, and he duly delivered it to the woman. The woman says she received it and it has since been lost. The husband does not contest his having given it; no one challenges the basic facts. Yet there is a problem: how do we know the woman is actually divorced?

The husband's admission that he appointed the *shaliach* establishes that part of the chain. But the *shaliach*'s testimony that he delivered it is that of a single witness with no document in hand, giving him no special standing. The woman's testimony that she received it is also that of a single witness without a document. There are no *edei mesirah*. The Gemara asks why we cannot simply believe the *shaliach*, and the answer already established is that *mi ka nafik gita mi-tusei yadei l'heimneih* — the *shaliach* is believed only when the *get* is in his hands. Without it, he has no special credibility.

The Gemara then asks: let us at least believe the husband. Rav Chiya bar Avin said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a husband who says "I divorced my wife" is believed, because he could do so now if he wished. By the same logic, the husband's statement that he appointed this man as his *shaliach* should be believed. Indeed, the Gemara accepts this: we believe the husband that he made the man a *shaliach l'holacha*. What we do not know is whether the *shaliach* actually delivered it, and for that we cannot rely on the *shaliach*'s unaccompanied word.

### Chazaka Shaliach

The Gemara asks: rather than requiring witnesses, why not simply invoke the *chazaka* that a *shaliach* performs his mission? Rabbi Yitzchak taught: if one says to his *shaliach*, "Go and betroth a woman for me," and the *shaliach* dies without reporting back, the husband is forbidden to marry any woman in the world, lest any one of them be his betrothed wife's relative. Tosafot notes that this seems extreme — one should also bear in mind that the woman, whoever she is, would herself presumably know she was betrothed. Tosafot therefore suggests this is a *knas*, a punitive measure for irresponsible conduct, but it is grounded in the underlying presumption that the *shaliach* did carry out his mission. This demonstrates the principle of *chazaka shaliach oseh shlichuso*.

Why then can that *chazaka* not resolve the present case? The Gemara answers: *hani mili l'chumra* — the *chazaka* is applied strictly, to create prohibitions. *Aval l'kula lo* — we do not invoke it leniently, to permit a woman to remarry. Presuming the *shaliach* delivered the *get* would mean permitting the woman as a divorced woman, and for that leniency one cannot rely on presumption alone.

### Woman's Chazaka

The Gemara then asks: why not believe the woman herself? Rav Hamnuna taught: a woman who says to her husband "you divorced me" is believed, on the basis of a *chazaka* — *ein isha me'izah paneiha bifnei ba'alah*, a woman would not be so brazen as to lie to her husband's face about something of this magnitude unless it were true. This is a striking ruling: after establishing that a very high standard of proof is required at every stage of this chain, the Gemara now considers whether a single *chazaka* about human behavior can replace all of that.

### Witness Synthesis

The Gemara resolves the tension. Rav Hamnuna's *chazaka* applies only when there is nothing that supports her claim — when she stands entirely alone in her assertion, she is presumed to be telling the truth because brazenness without any corroboration is implausible. But here, the *shaliach* is backing up her account, and the husband himself does not know whether the delivery actually occurred. Given that backdrop, she is not without support, and therefore it would not take unusual brazenness for her to make a false claim under these circumstances. The *chazaka* therefore does not apply.

This brings the discussion back to the *Mishnah*: witnesses are essential precisely in cases where neither the *shaliach* nor the woman holds the *get*, and neither has the special credibility that would otherwise come with possession of the document. The witnesses serve two functions simultaneously: they provide the objective evidence that the chain of *shlichus* was properly established and executed, and — depending on the position one holds regarding *edei kiyum* — they may also constitute the required witnessing of the act of *gerushin* itself. When the *get* is in someone's hands, that physical presence may supply some of what the witnesses would otherwise need to cover. When it is not — when the *get* has been torn, lost, or is otherwise unavailable — witnesses become indispensable to the entire construct.

## Minor Girl & Father

### Naara Me'urasa Status

The next *Mishnah* addresses a case of pre-assigned *shlichus* that arises naturally from a particular legal status. The subject is a *naara me'urasa* — a girl who is betrothed and who has reached the age of twelve but is not yet twelve and a half, and therefore has not yet achieved the status of *bogeres*. During this window, she is legally an adult in certain respects, yet her father retains authority over her. She is simultaneously *me'urasa* — betrothed — and still under paternal control. The reason the *Mishnah* focuses on this specific category is that it requires all three conditions at once: she must be an adult (*naara*), she must be under her father's authority, and she must be in a state of marriage from which a *get* is required. A *nesua* — one who has entered *chuppah* — would be fully out of her father's control and is therefore outside this particular category.

### Get Reception Authority

The *Mishnah* states: *hi v'aviha mekablin et gittah* — she and her father can receive her *get*. Both represent her interests. But Rav Yehuda rules: *ein shtei yadayim zachot ke'achat* — two hands cannot acquire simultaneously. The question then is whose hand prevails. Rav Yehuda rules: *aviha mekabel et gittah bilvad* — the father alone receives her *get*. Where the father is present, his authority is dominant, and the *get* must be given to him rather than to her.

The *Mishnah* then adds a general principle: *ve-chol she-eina yechola lishmor et gittah eina yechola l'hitgareish* — any woman who cannot safeguard her own *get* cannot be divorced.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot: Understanding Divorce

Rashi and Tosafot dispute the meaning of this principle. Rashi understands it to mean that even when the father receives the *get*, if the child is so young that she does not understand what *gerushin* means — if she cannot grasp that she is divorced and act accordingly, described in the Gemara with the phrase *meshaleches v'eina chozeres*, that she goes away and does not return the next day thinking nothing has changed — then the *get* cannot effect a divorce even through the father's reception. Rashi requires a minimal level of comprehension on the girl's part as a condition of the *gerushin* taking effect, even if she is not the one receiving it.

Tosafot disagrees. Tosafot argues that the issue of understanding pertains only to the act of reception by the girl herself. If the *get* is given to the father, it is entirely irrelevant how young or uncomprehending the girl is. After all, the Talmud elsewhere permits a father to betroth his daughter when she is extremely young — if one can be married off as an infant, one can presumably be divorced under the same conditions. For Tosafot, *yoda'at lishmor gittah* is a condition for when the girl herself receives the *get*, not for when the father does so on her behalf.

### Torah's Extra Hand Debate

The Gemara asks: what is the underlying dispute between the *Rabbanan* who say *hi v'aviha* and Rabbi Yehuda who says only the father? The *Rabbanan* hold *yadi yeseira zachei lah Rachmana* — the Torah granted her an extra hand. That is, the Torah provided her with her own independent capacity to receive the *get* in addition to, not instead of, the father's authority. Both hands are available. Rabbi Yehuda holds: *bimkom aviha yadah dilah lav klum hi* — when the father is present, her hand is nullified entirely. His authority does not merely supplement hers; it displaces it. She has no standing of her own as a recipient when her father is there to act on her behalf.

Tosafot raises a further dimension of this dispute: even regarding when the girl can receive the *get* on her own — either when there is no father or in a case where one might give it to her — when precisely does that right begin? Tosafot's view is that even as a *ketana*, she may receive a *get*, because receiving a *get* is a passive act requiring no legal capacity to transact. The point about *naara* is therefore not that she newly acquires this right upon reaching twelve; rather, it highlights that even once she becomes a *naara* and the father's authority would otherwise persist, the status of *naara* does not remove the father from the picture. We will continue with this discussion in the next session.
