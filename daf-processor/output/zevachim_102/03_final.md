# Zevachim 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Moshe as Kohen

### Moshe Kohen Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין זָר רוֹאֶה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים! וְאִם תֹּאמַר אַהֲרֹן הִסְגִּירָהּ – אַהֲרֹן קָרוֹב הוּא, וְאֵין קָרוֹב רוֹאֶה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים! אֶלָּא כָּבוֹד גָּדוֹל חָלַק לָהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמִרְיָם אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה: אֲנִי כֹּהֵן וַאֲנִי מַסְגִּירָהּ, אֲנִי חוֹלְטָהּ וַאֲנִי פּוֹטְרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **and a non-priest may not inspect the** shades of leprous **marks** to diagnose them. **And if you say** that **Aaron quarantined her,** that is difficult, as **Aaron** was **a relative,** Miriam's brother, **and a relative may not inspect the** shades of leprous **marks. Rather, the Holy One, Blessed be He, bestowed a great honor on Miriam at that time,** and said: **I** Myself **am a priest, and I** will **quarantine her** for seven days to see if the shades of leprous marks persist, and **I** will **declare her** a leper if she is impure, **and I** will **exempt her** if she is not impure.

The daf opens in the middle of a rich discussion about whether Moshe was a *kohen* — or even a *kohen gadol*. The question emerged from an earlier analysis of *aninuth*: why was the *chatas* burned on the eighth day of the *miluim*? The most straightforward answer is *aninut*, which the *pesukim* themselves make apparent. But this raises a follow-up: why not simply have Pinchas perform the *avodah*? The answer given is that Pinchas was not yet a *kohen* at that point, despite being a descendant of Aharon. And why not give it to Moshe, if Moshe was a *kohen*? The answer is that Moshe was occupied with God. That exchange then opens up the broader question: was Moshe in fact a *kohen*?

### Kehunah Origins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: מֹשֶׁה זָר, וְאֵין זָר רוֹאֶה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים!
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** the midrash **teaches: Moses** was **a non-priest, and a non-priest may not inspect the** shades of leprous **marks,** which contradicts the statement of Rav that Moses was a priest.

What this question really does is reveal something conceptually important: *kehunah* is not, at its foundation, a status defined by descent from Aharon. The *pesukim* themselves attest to this. Malki Tzedek Melech Shalem is described as *vehu Kohen l'El Elyon* — he is a *kohen* to God Most High — and Yitro is called *Kohen Midian*. The original *kehunah* belonged to the *bechorim*, the firstborn. In each generation, certain individuals were designated to serve God — sometimes hereditary, sometimes recognized. The sons of Aharon represent one particular instantiation of this broader status, a hereditary channel. But *kehunah* conceptually precedes it and is not exhausted by it.

What the *gemara* is suggesting, then, is that Moshe may have been included within the class of *kohanim* during his time — a *kehunah* that simply did not pass to his descendants.

### Pinchas Non-Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: שָׁאנֵי מַרְאוֹת נְגָעִים, דְּאַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו כְּתוּבִין בַּפָּרָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The *halakhot* of the examination of **shades of** leprous **marks are different, because** specifically **Aaron and his sons,** and not Moses, **are written in the passage** that discusses them: "Then he shall be brought unto Aaron the priest, or unto one of his sons the priests" (Leviticus 13:2). Therefore, there is no proof from this *baraita* that Moses was not a priest.

The other side of this coin is equally striking: Pinchas was a descendant of Aharon and yet, at least initially, was not a *kohen*. The *kehunah* status was granted to Aharon and his four sons — not to children already born to those sons. It is entirely conceivable that there were other grandchildren of Aharon who were alive at the time the *mishkan* was inaugurated, who never performed an act like Pinchas's killing of Zimri, and who therefore never received the *kehunah*. Indeed, there is a *midrash* that a descendant of Moshe himself became a *kohen* to idols — a reminder of how fluidly the Talmud can treat the boundaries of this status.

So we have two paradoxes running simultaneously: Moshe, who was not a descendant of Aharon, may have been a *kohen*; and Pinchas, who was a descendant of Aharon, was initially not one.

### Moshe Zar Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חָמֵשׁ שְׂמָחוֹת הָיְתָה אֱלִישֶׁבַע יְתֵירָה עַל בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל – יְבָמָהּ מֶלֶךְ, אִישָׁהּ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, בְּנָהּ סְגָן, בֶּן בְּנָהּ מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה, וְאָחִיהָ נְשִׂיא שֵׁבֶט; וַאֲבֵילָה עַל שְׁנֵי בָּנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav's statement from a *baraita*: **Elisheva,** the daughter of Amminadav and the wife of Aaron, **had five more** reasons for **joy than** the other **daughters of Israel** on the day the Tabernacle was dedicated: **Her brother-in-law,** Moses, was **a king; her husband,** Aaron, was the **High Priest; her son,** Elazar, was the **deputy** High Priest; **her son's son,** Pinehas, was the priest **anointed for war,** who would lead the army out to battle; **and her brother,** Nahshon, son of Amminadav, was **the prince of** the **tribe** of Judah, who brought his offering on that day, as the first of all the princes. **But** on that same day of joy she was **in mourning for her two sons,** Nadav and Avihu, who died on that day.

The *gemara* now challenges the proposition that Moshe was a *kohen*, citing a *baraisa* from the final line of 101b. The *meisivei* asks: *Miriam mi hisgira?* — who declared Miriam to be a *metzora* and formally placed her into her *tzara'at* status? The declaration requires a *kohen*. If you say Moshe did it — *Moshe zar hu* — Moshe was a *zar*, a non-*kohen*, and *ein zar ro'eh es hanega'im*, a non-*kohen* cannot examine *nega'im*. If you say Aharon did it — *Aharon karov hu* — Aharon was a relative, and *ein karov ro'eh es hanega'im*, a relative cannot rule on *nega'im*. This is, as Tosafot notes, actually a *machlokes* — the position that a relative cannot examine *nega'im* reflects Rabbi Meir's view, as stated in Masechet Negaim Perek 2, while the *tanna kamma* there holds that only one's own *nega'im* are excluded. The *baraisa* here follows Rabbi Meir.

But why not have Elazar do it? The question of how far the category of *karov* extends — whether it reaches nephews — is not fully resolved. It may be that having a nephew rule on his aunt would not be appropriate *kavod* for Miriam.

The *gemara*'s resolution is striking in its own right: *kvod gadol chalak haKadosh Baruch Hu leMiriam* — God Himself gave Miriam great honor in that moment. *Va'ani masguira va'ani potrah* — I will place her in her status as a *metzora*, and I will release her. There is something arresting about this formulation. God acting as a *kohen* to the extent of serving the people — teaching, ruling, imparting justice — is not as paradoxical as it might initially seem. The *kohanim* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* function as teachers of Torah, as members of the *beit din*, as examiners of *tzara'at*. In all of these capacities they represent God to the people. God can perform these same functions; what would be theologically incoherent is God serving in the *Beit HaMikdash* — serving Himself. But the outward-facing functions of *kehunah* have a natural divine analog.

What is halachically relevant for our discussion, the *gemara* says, is simply this: *Moshe zar*. The source explicitly calls Moshe a *zar*, and *ein zar ro'eh es hanega'im* — so you see that Moshe was not a *kohen*.

### Negaim Aharon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא: מֹשֶׁה זָר, וְאֵין זָר רוֹאֶה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים!
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** the midrash **teaches: Moses** was **a non-priest, and a non-priest may not inspect the** shades of leprous **marks,** which contradicts the statement of Rav that Moses was a priest.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak qualifies this proof: *shani mar'os nega'im de-Aharon u-vanav ketuvim be-parsha* — the examination of *nega'im* is a special case, because the *pasuk* does not merely say *ha-kohen*; it says *ve-huva el Aharon ha-kohen o el achad mi-banav ha-kohanim* — it is brought to Aharon the *kohen* or to one of his sons the *kohanim*. For *nega'im* specifically, it is not enough to be a *kohen*; one must also be a descendant of Aharon. So the fact that Moshe could not examine *nega'im* does not prove he was not a *kohen* — it only proves he was not a descendant of Aharon, which we already knew. (Of course, God also does not fall into this category of Aharon's descendants, but the *gemara* brackets that point.)

## Pinchas Kohen Status

### Elisheva Five Joys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: יְבָמָהּ מֶלֶךְ – מֶלֶךְ אִין, כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לָא! אֵימָא: אַף מֶלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches: Her brother-in-law,** Moses, was **a king.** From this one can infer that **yes,** he was **a king,** but he was **not a High Priest,** contrary to Rav's statement. The Gemara responds: **Say** that the *baraita* means: Moses was **a king as well,** in addition to being a High Priest.

The *gemara* continues testing whether Moshe was a *kohen*, now citing from *Seder Olam* a teaching about Elisheva, the daughter of Aminadav and sister of Nachshon — Aharon's wife. On the day the *mishkan* was dedicated, Elisheva had five sources of joy exceeding those of any other woman in Israel. Her *yavam* — her brother-in-law — Moshe, was a *melech*, a king; her husband Aharon was *kohen gadol*; her son Elazar was *sgan*, the deputy *kohen gadol*; her grandson Pinchas was *mashuach milchamah*, the one who would lead Israel into war; and her brother Nachshon was *nasi shevet*, the head of his tribe.

### Pinchas Future Status

The reference to Pinchas as *mashuach milchamah* is significant for our discussion. The *gemara* notes: *katani mihat* — what we can extract from this is that *yivamah melech*, her brother-in-law was a king, and the *baraisa* identifies him as simply a *melech* — not also as *kohen gadol*. If Moshe had been *kohen gadol*, would the *baraisa* not have listed that as a separate source of joy for Elisheva? The counter-response is *eima af melech* — we already credit *kohen gadol* status to her husband; that does not necessarily foreclose Moshe also having it. It is admittedly the weakest of the proofs accumulated thus far, but the overall weight of evidence is pointing toward Moshe not having been recognized as a *kohen*.

There is, however, an immediately striking implication in the reference to Pinchas as *mashuach milchamah*. This text is describing the day the *mishkan* was dedicated — long before the Zimri episode. If Pinchas was already designated as *mashuach milchamah* at that point, this presupposes that he already held *kohen* status, since the *mashuach milchamah* is a *kohen*. This is a direct challenge to the earlier discussion that Pinchas was not yet a *kohen*. The *gemara* has not yet fully resolved this tension, and it is a point a careful reader will notice.

## Moshe Kohen Tannaim

### God's Anger Result

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״וַיִּחַר אַף ה׳ בְּמֹשֶׁה״ – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל חֲרוֹן אַף שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ רוֹשֶׁם, וְזֶה לֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ רוֹשֶׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The question of whether Moses was a High Priest is **subject to** a dispute among ***tanna'im*,** as is taught in a *baraita*: When Moses was at the burning bush and expressed hesitation to deliver God's message to Pharaoh, the verse states: **"And the anger of the Lord burned against Moses,** and He said: Is there not Aaron your brother the Levite? I know that he can speak well. And also, behold, he comes forth to meet you, and when he sees you, he will be glad in his heart" (Exodus 4:14). **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** For **every burning anger that** is stated **in the Torah, its effect is** also **stated,** i.e., the Torah records an action or pronouncement as a consequence of that anger, **but** in **this** case **no effect of** the anger **is stated.**

The *gemara* now declares *katannai* — this question is in fact a *machlokes tannaim*. The entry point is the verse *vayichar af Hashem be-Moshe* — God's anger burned against Moshe when he persisted in refusing the mission, saying send someone else instead. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha states a general principle: *kol charon af she-ba-Torah ne'emar bo roshem* — every instance in the Torah in which anger burns against a person leaves a mark, some consequence. Rashi clarifies that this need not be a physical mark; it can be a public rebuke or some experienced consequence of that anger. The critical word is *be* — anger directed *at* someone, not merely burning within.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai applies this principle here: *af zeh ne'emar bo roshem* — this instance also left its mark. The proof is in the phrasing *halo Aharon achicha ha-Levi* — why call Aharon a *Levi*? He was going to be a *kohen*! Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai reads this as God saying: *ani amarti attah Kohen ve-hu Levi* — My original plan was that you, Moshe, would be the *kohen*, and Aharon would be the *Levi*. Now, because of all your resistance and complaints, it is reversed: Aharon is the *kohen*, and you, Moshe, will be the *Levi*. The phrase *Aharon achicha ha-Levi* is thus a pointed remark, emphasizing what was *supposed* to be Aharon's role — what he was going to be had Moshe not argued. The mark of God's anger was the removal of *kehunah* from Moshe.

### Chachamim Miluim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: אַף זֶה נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ רוֹשֶׁם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲלֹא אַהֲרֹן אָחִיךָ הַלֵּוִי״ – וַהֲלֹא כֹּהֵן הוּא! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי אַתָּה כֹּהֵן וְהוּא לֵוִי, עַכְשָׁיו הוּא כֹּהֵן וְאַתָּה לֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: Even in this** case the anger's **effect is stated, as it is stated** there: **"Is there not Aaron your brother the Levite?** I know that he can speak well." **But isn't** Aaron **a priest?** Why is he referred to as a Levite? **This** is what God **is saying** to Moses: **I** initially **said** that **you** would be the **priest and he** would be the **Levite; now he** will be the **priest and you** will be the **Levite.**

The *chachamim* disagree: *lo nitkahen Moshe ela shivat yemei hamiluim bilvad* — Moshe was *kohen* only during the seven days of *miluim*, and no more. As Rashi explains, the crucial distinction between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai is not about those seven days — both agree Moshe served as *kohen* during them — but about whether the *kehunah* had ever been intended for Moshe. According to Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai, the original divine plan had been for Moshe to be *kohen*, and God's anger stripped that from him. According to the *chachamim*, that was never the plan. Moshe simply served in a temporary capacity during the *miluim*, and there was nothing removed from him, because nothing had been designated for him beyond those seven days.

### Rav Kehunah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא נִתְכַּהֵן מֹשֶׁה, אֶלָּא שִׁבְעַת יְמֵי הַמִּלּוּאִים בִּלְבַד. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: לֹא פָּסְקָה כְּהוּנָּה אֶלָּא מִזַּרְעוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמֹשֶׁה אִישׁ הָאֱלֹהִים בָּנָיו יִקָּרְאוּ עַל שֵׁבֶט הַלֵּוִי״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן בְּכֹהֲנָיו וּשְׁמוּאֵל בְּקֹרְאֵי שְׁמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Moses became a priest** for **the seven days of inauguration alone,** and after that his priesthood expired. **And some say:** The **priesthood expired only for the descendants of Moses,** but Moses himself remained a priest, **as it is stated: "But as for Moses the man of God, his sons are named among the tribe of Levi"** (I Chronicles 23:14). This verse indicates that it was his sons who were Levites, but he himself remained a priest. **And** so too the verse **states: "Moses and Aaron among His priests, and Samuel among them that call upon His name,** did call upon the Lord, and He answered them" (Psalms 99:6).

A third position is that of Rav — the *yesh omrim* of the *baraisa*. Rav holds: *lo paska kehuna ela mizaro shel Moshe* — the *kehunah* was not withheld from Moshe himself; he was indeed a *kohen*. It simply was not passed on to his children. The *kehunah* was granted to Aharon and his descendants, and to Moshe — but unlike Aharon's *kehunah*, Moshe's did not continue through his lineage. The *pasuk* in Divrei HaYamim supports this: *u-Moshe ish ha-elohim banav yikare'u al shevet ha-Levi* — his *children* are attributed to the tribe of Levi, but Moshe himself is described separately, implying his own status was distinct. The *gemara* also cites *Moshe ve-Aharon be-kohannav* — Moshe and Aharon among His *kohanim* — as further evidence that Moshe himself held *kohen* status. A textual question arises: *kohannav* might be read as singular (*kohano*), which would create a parsing difficulty, but the *gemara* proceeds with this reading nonetheless.

## Anger & Kingship

### Charon Af Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל חֲרוֹן אַף שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ רוֹשֶׁם?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּצֵא מֹשֶׁה מֵעִם פַּרְעֹה בׇּחֳרִי אָף״, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי! אֲמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: סְטָרוֹ וְיָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa: **And** is it true that for **every burning anger that** is stated **in the Torah, its effect is** also **stated? But isn't it written** with regard to Moses: **"And he went out from Pharaoh in hot anger"** (Exodus 11:8)? **And** Moses **did not say anything to** Pharaoh. **Reish Lakish said:** Moses **slapped him and left.**

Having established the general principle of *charon af*, the *gemara* now examines it more carefully. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's claim is that *kol charon af she-ba-Torah ne'emar bo roshem* — every instance of burning anger recorded in the Torah leaves its mark on the target of that anger.

The *gemara* challenges this with a counterexample: *vayetze me'im Paroh bechari af* — Moshe left Pharaoh's presence in fiery anger. Where is the mark here? *Velo amar leh velo midi* — he didn't even publicly rebuke him. One might respond that Pharaoh had all the power, and Moshe was in no position to lash out. But the *gemara*'s point is about the meaning of the word: if *charon af* always implies that the anger was directed outward and had an impact on the other person, where is that impact here?

Resh Lakish responds: *setaro* — he slapped Pharaoh in the face. The verse does not say this explicitly, but Resh Lakish reads the *charon af* as implying an acted-upon anger. This prompts the question: would Resh Lakish really say such a thing? After all, elsewhere Resh Lakish interprets the verse *v'nitzavta likraso al sfat hay'or* — you will stand to greet Pharaoh at the river — as meaning Moshe should show Pharaoh appropriate respect: *melech hu, v'hasber lo panim*, he is a king; present yourself respectfully. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, reads *nitzavta* in an aggressive register, as in *Datan va-Aviram yatzu nitzavim* — a posture of confrontation, not deference. So the two positions need to be reversed: it is Rabbi Yochanan, not Resh Lakish, who holds the confrontational reading, and Resh Lakish who urges respect before Pharaoh.

### Fear of Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הָכִי?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְנִצַּבְתָּ לִקְרָאתוֹ עַל שְׂפַת הַיְאֹר״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מֶלֶךְ הוּא וְהַסְבֵּיר לוֹ פָּנִים, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: רָשָׁע הוּא וְהָעֵיז פָּנֶיךָ בּוֹ! אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And does Reish Lakish** actually **say this? But isn't it written:** "Go unto Pharaoh in the morning; lo, he goes out unto the water; **and you shall stand by the river's brink to meet him,** and the rod which was turned to a serpent shall you take in your hand" (Exodus 7:15); **and Reish Lakish says:** God meant: Pharaoh **is a king, and** so you should **be amiable toward him, and Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** God meant: Pharaoh **is wicked, and** so you should **be insolent toward him.** If according to Reish Lakish Moses was commanded to greet Pharaoh with respect, how could he say that Moses slapped Pharaoh? The Gemara answers: **Reverse** the names of the Sages in this dispute; it is Reish Lakish who says that Moses was commanded to be insolent.

Rabbi Yannai draws a broader lesson: *l'olam t'he eimas malchus alecha* — the fear of the king should always be upon you, even when the king is wicked, even when God is clearly on your side. The proof is Moshe's own formulation to Pharaoh: *v'yerdu kol avadecha eleh elai* — all your servants will come down to me. Pharaoh himself was the one who would ultimately come to Moshe and plead for Israel to leave — yet Moshe did not say "you will come down to me." He showed deference in his phrasing even in a moment of triumph. This is the posture of one who maintains *eimas malchus* even while pronouncing the downfall of a tyrant. Fear and genuine respect are not the same thing, of course; the *gemara* does not conflate them. But here they express themselves in the same outward behavior.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לְעוֹלָם תְּהֵא אֵימַת מַלְכוּת עָלֶיךָ; דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיָרְדוּ כׇל עֲבָדֶיךָ אֵלֶּה אֵלַי״ – וְאִילּוּ לְדִידֵיהּ לָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the respect accorded to a king, **Rabbi Yannai says: Fear of** the **kingship should always be upon you,** even when the king does not deserve respect, **as it is written** that Moses said to Pharaoh: **"And all these your servants shall come down unto me** and bow down unto me, saying: Get you out, and all the people that follow you, and after that I will go out" (Exodus 11:8). He mentioned Pharaoh's servants, **but he did not say** this **of** Pharaoh **himself,** even though this was the eventual outcome. Rather, he spoke to Pharaoh with the respect due to a king.

Rabbi Yochanan adds another illustration: *v'yad Hashem hayta el Eliyahu v'yeshannes motnav va-yaratz lifnei Achav* — God's spirit was upon Elijah, and he girded his loins and ran before Achav. Despite Elijah's prophetic authority and his total opposition to Achav's ways, he placed himself in the position of a forerunner before the king — the secondary, attendant position. The respect shown to the office of kingship is maintained regardless of one's own standing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וְיַד ה׳ הָיְתָה אֶל אֵלִיָּהוּ, וַיְשַׁנֵּס מׇתְנָיו וַיָּרׇץ לִפְנֵי אַחְאָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Derive the principle **from here: "And the hand of the Lord was on Elijah, and he girded up his loins and ran before Ahab** to the entrance of Jezreel" (I Kings 18:46). Elijah the prophet ran before the evil king Ahab out of respect.

### Moshe Kingship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: בִּקֵּשׁ מֹשֶׁה מַלְכוּת, וְלֹא נָתְנוּ לוֹ; דִּכְתִיב: ״אַל תִּקְרַב הֲלוֹם״, וְאֵין ״הֲלוֹם״ אֶלָּא מַלְכוּת – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִי אָנֹכִי ה׳ אֱלֹהִים [וְגוֹ׳] כִּי הֲבִיאֹתַנִי עַד הֲלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the roles of Moses: **Ulla says: Moses requested** that he be given the **kingship, but it was not given to him, as it is written: "Do not draw hither"** (Exodus 3:5); **and** the word **"hither"** refers to **nothing other than kingship, as it is stated:** "Then David the king went in and sat before the Lord, and he said: **Who am I, O Lord God,** and what is my house, **that You have brought me hither"?** (II Samuel 7:18).

Ulla introduces a striking teaching: *bikesh Moshe malchus ve-lo nitnu lo* — Moshe sought kingship and was not granted it. This requires clarification, since we have already referred to Moshe as a *melech*. What Ulla means by *malchus* is not mere political leadership but the full conception of hereditary kingship — a status that passes through the generations. The proof text is *al tikrav halom* — do not come close to *this place* — spoken to Moshe at the *sneh*. The word *halom* connotes royal status.

### Halom Hereditary

How do we know *halom* means kingship? By *gezeira shava* with the verse by David: *mi anochi Hashem Elokim ki heviatani ad halom* — who am I, Lord my God, that You have brought me to *this point*? David uses *halom* to describe his elevation to the throne. So *halom* at the *sneh* signals that kingship was being withheld from Moshe at the very moment he was being called.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: יְבָמָהּ מֶלֶךְ! אָמַר רָבָא: לוֹ וּלְזַרְעוֹ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** from the *baraita* cited earlier: **Rabbi Yishmael says:** Elisheva's **brother-in-law,** Moses, was **a king.** This teaches that Moses was in fact granted kingship. **Rava said:** Ulla **is saying** that Moses did not receive kingship **for himself and for his descendants,** i.e., it was not bequeathed to his sons.

Rava challenges: but the *baraisa* about Elisheva just called Moshe a *melech*! Rava resolves this: *lo l'zaro ka'amar* — what Moshe sought and did not receive was the hereditary dimension, the kingship that would continue through his descendants. The leadership he exercised was real, but it was not *malchus* in the dynastic sense.

The *gemara* then examines whether *halom* always implies hereditary kingship for all future generations. By Shaul, the word *halom* also appears: *havei od halom ish* — bring the man here. Yet Shaul's dynasty did not persist. Two responses are offered: first, Shaul's son Ish-Bosheth did in fact reign, even if briefly; second, and more fundamentally, *shani Shaul de-afilu begavei lo kayam* — what was granted to Shaul was indeed a hereditary kingship, but it was taken from him because of his failures. The grant was dynastic; the revocation was consequential.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הֵיכָא דִּכְתִיב ״הֲלוֹם״ – לְדוֹרוֹת הוּא?! וְהָא גַּבֵּי שָׁאוּל דִּכְתִיב: ״הֲבָא עוֹד הֲלֹם אִישׁ״ – הוּא אִין, זַרְעוֹ לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **And** is it so that **anywhere that it is written "hither"** the verse **is** referring to kingship **for** future **generations** as well? **But isn't it written about** the kingship of **Saul:** "They asked of the Lord further: **Is there yet a man who comes hither"** (I Samuel 10:22), and Saul was **indeed** a king, but **his descendants** were **not.**

This leads to a general principle stated by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *besha'ah she-poskin gedulah le-adam poskin lo u-lezaro ad sof kol ha-dorot* — when a position of greatness is decreed for a person from above, it is decreed for that person and for all his descendants until the end of time. The verse is *lo yigra mi-tzaddik einav v'es melachim l'kise* — it is not withheld from a righteous person, and kings remain on their throne. But this is not unconditional: *ve-im higis da'ato Hakadosh Baruch Hu mashpilo* — if a person becomes arrogant, God will bring him low. The verse continues: *ve-im asurim ba-zikkim yilachdun be-chevlei oni* — if they are bound with chains they will be caught in ropes of poverty. Greatness granted by heaven is generational in principle, but forfeitable through pride.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁפּוֹסְקִים גְּדוּלָּה לָאָדָם – פּוֹסְקִים לוֹ וּלְזַרְעוֹ עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יִגְרַע מִצַּדִּיק עֵינָיו וְאֶת מְלָכִים לַכִּסֵּא וְגוֹ׳״. וְאִם הֵגֵיס דַּעְתּוֹ – הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַשְׁפִּילוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם אֲסוּרִים בַּזִּקִּים יִלָּכְדוּן בְּחַבְלֵי עֹנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The second response is **in accordance with** that which **Rabbi Elazar** says that **Rabbi Ḥanina says: When greatness is apportioned to a person** in Heaven, it **is apportioned to him and to his descendants until the end of all generations, as it is stated: "He withdraws not His eyes from the righteous; but with kings upon the throne** He sets them forever, and they are exalted" (Job 36:7). **But if he** then **became arrogant, the Holy One, Blessed be He, humiliates him, as is stated** in the next verse: **"And if they be bound in fetters, and be held in cords of affliction"** (Job 36:8). This was the case with Saul.

## Ba'alei Mumim

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין, בֵּין בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כׇּל זָכָר״ – לְרַבּוֹת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Blemished** priests, **whether** they are temporarily **blemished** or permanently blemished, receive a share and partake of offerings, but do not sacrifice them. The Gemara explains: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the meal offering: **"Every male** among the sons of Aaron shall eat it" (Leviticus 6:11). The phrase "every male" serves **to include blemished** priests.

The *gemara* now turns from *aggadata* to the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states the principle that *ba'alei mumim* — *kohanim* with blemishes, whether permanent or temporary — do not perform the *avodah* but do receive a portion in the *korbanos*.

### Kol Zachar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי? אִי לַאֲכִילָה – הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״לֶחֶם אֱלֹהָיו מִקׇּדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים יֹאכֵל״! אֶלָּא לַחֲלוּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **For what** matter is this derivation necessary? **If** one suggests it is **for** the matter of **consumption, it is already stated** explicitly: "Any man of the seed of Aaron the priest who has a blemish…**He may eat the bread of his God, of the most sacred,** and of the sacred" (Leviticus 21:21–22). **Rather,** Leviticus 6:11 is necessary **for** the matter of **receiving a share,** teaching that blemished priests may do so along with the other priests. This is the source for the mishna's statement.

The *gemara* asks: *mina hanei milei* — where do we know this? The *rabbanan* taught: *kol zachar ba-kohanim yochal osah* — every male among the *kohanim* shall eat of it. This phrase appears three times in the Torah — by a *minchah*, by a *chatas*, and by a *shelamim* — and each occurrence serves to include a different category of *ba'al mum*.

The question in each case is *lemai* — for what purpose is the inclusion? If it is to permit eating, an explicit verse already states this: *lechem elohav mi-kodesh ha-kodashim u-min ha-kodashim yochel* — the *ba'al mum* may eat from the holiest and from the holy offerings. So the *kol zachar* cannot be coming to grant permission to eat — that is already established. Rather, it is coming to grant entitlement to a *portion*, meaning the *ba'al mum* actively receives his share.

The second *baraisa* addresses a second *kol zachar*: we have already used the first to establish that a *ba'al mum* receives a portion. But what if the *kohen* began without a blemish and developed one later? Perhaps the original *kol zachar* speaks only of someone who was whole and then became a *ba'al mum* — he had already established himself in the system, so perhaps he retains his entitlement. How do we know that someone born with a *mum* — a *ba'al mum me'ikara* — also receives a portion? The second *kol zachar* tells us so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״כׇּל זָכָר״ – לְרַבּוֹת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין. לְמַאי? אִי לַאֲכִילָה – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר! אִי לַחֲלוּקָּה – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר! שֶׁיָּכוֹל אֵין לִי אֶלָּא תָּם וְנַעֲשָׂה בַּעַל מוּם, בַּעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל זָכָר״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the sin offering: **"Every male** among the priests may eat thereof" (Leviticus 6:22). The phrase "every male" serves **to include blemished** priests. The Gemara explains: **For what** matter is this derivation necessary? **If** one suggests it is **for** the matter of **consumption, isn't** that **already stated** in Leviticus, chapter 21? **If** one suggests it is **for** the matter of **receiving a share, isn't** that **already stated** in verse 6:11? Rather, the verse is necessary **because** one **might** have thought that **I have** derived **only** the *halakha* with regard to a priest who was at first **unblemished and** later **became blemished. From where** is it derived that one **blemished from birth** also receives a share? **The verse states** here: **"Every male."**

The third *baraisa* then asks: eating is established, receiving a portion is established, and inclusion of one born with a *mum* is established. What does the third *kol zachar* add? *Yachol ein li ela ba'al mum kavua, ba'al mum over minayin?* — I only know that a permanent blemish qualifies; how do I know a temporary blemish also receives a portion? *Talmud lomar kol zachar* — every male — even one with a temporary blemish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״כׇּל זָכָר״ – לְרַבּוֹת בַּעַל מוּם. לְמַאי? אִי לַאֲכִילָה – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר! וְאִי לַחֲלוּקָּה – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר! וְאִי לְבַעַל מוּם מֵעִיקָּרוֹ – הֲרֵי כְּבָר אָמוּר! שֶׁיָּכוֹל אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּעַל מוּם קָבוּעַ, בַּעַל מוּם עוֹבֵר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל זָכָר״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the guilt offering: **"Every male** among the priests may eat thereof" (Leviticus 7:6). The phrase "every male" serves **to include a blemished** priest. The Gemara explains: **For what** matter is this derivation necessary? **If** one suggests it is **for** the matter of **eating, isn't** that **already stated? And if** one suggests it is **for** the matter of **receiving a share, isn't** that **already stated? And if** one suggests it is **for** a priest **blemished from birth, isn't** that **already stated?** Rather, the verse is necessary **because** one **might** have thought that **I have** derived **only** the *halakha* with regard to **a permanently blemished** priest. **From where** is it derived that a **temporarily blemished** priest also receives a share? **The verse states** here: **"Every male."**

### Avoda vs Achila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Isn't it the opposite [*kelapei layya*]?** Wouldn't I have thought that a priest with a permanent blemish would be treated more stringently than one with a temporary blemish? **Rav Sheshet said: Reverse** the wording as follows: One might have thought that I have derived only the *halakha* with regard to a priest with a temporary blemish; from where is it derived that one with a permanent blemish also receives a share?

At this point the *gemara* raises an objection: *k'lapei laya* — which way is this going? It seems counterintuitive. Surely one would more naturally assume that a temporary blemish is included — since it will pass — and the *pasuk* should be needed to include the more surprising case of a permanent blemish. Rav Sheshes says: *eipoch* — reverse it. The verse is coming to include permanent blemishes, not temporary ones.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ; וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually, do not reverse** the wording, **and** this is not difficult. It **was necessary** to teach that even a priest with a temporary blemish receives a share, because it might **enter your mind to say**

Rav Ashi disagrees: *l'olam lo teipoch* — do not reverse it. The verse is indeed needed for the temporary *ba'al mum*. Why might one have thought to exclude him? Because one might have analogized his status to that of a *tamei kohen*: just as a *tamei kohen* is only temporarily excluded and must wait until his *tumah* passes before eating, so too a temporary *ba'al mum* — perhaps wait until his blemish passes and then he will get his portion. The *pasuk* teaches that no — even now, while the blemish is present, he receives a portion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי טָמֵא; מָה טָמֵא – כַּמָּה דְּלָא טָהוֹר לָא אָכֵיל, אַף הַאי נָמֵי – כַּמָּה דְּלָא מִתְּקַן לָא אָכֵיל; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** that the *halakha* of this priest is **like** that of **a ritually impure** priest: **Just as an impure** priest **may not partake as long as he is not pure, so too, this** priest with a temporary blemish **may not partake as long as he does not become fit.** The verse therefore **teaches us** that he may receive a share even before his blemish heals.

### Rule Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי וְכוּ׳. וְלָא?! וַהֲרֵי בַּעַל מוּם, דְּלֹא רָאוּי לָעֲבוֹדָה, וְחוֹלֵק! וְתוּ, הָא רָאוּי לָעֲבוֹדָה חוֹלֵק?! הֲרֵי טָמֵא בְּקׇרְבְּנוֹת צִיבּוּר, דְּרָאוּי לָעֲבוֹדָה, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: The principle is: **Any** priest **who is unfit** for the service that day does not receive a share of the sacrificial meat. The Gemara objects: **But doesn't** he? **Isn't** there **a blemished** priest, **who is not fit for the service and** who nevertheless **receives a share** of the meat, as the mishna itself teaches? **And furthermore,** this principle indicates that only priests unfit for the service do not receive a share, **but** any priest who is **fit for the service** does **receive a share.** But **isn't** there **an impure** priest, **who, with regard to offerings of the community, is fit for the service, and** who nevertheless **does not receive a share?**

This brings the *gemara* back to the larger question: is the operative rule in the *mishnah* about *avodah* or about *achilah*? The *mishnah* states that whoever is unfit for *avodah* does not receive a portion. The *gemara* notes that this cannot be an absolute rule in either direction. A *ba'al mum* is unfit for *avodah* yet does receive a portion — that is an exception to "unfit doesn't get." Conversely, a *tamei kohen* in the context of *korbanos tzibur* is fit for *avodah* yet does not receive a portion — that is an exception to "fit gets." So the rule as stated fails on both sides.

### Achila Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **is saying** that any priest who is not **fit for partaking** of sacrificial meat does not receive a share. Blemished priests may partake of sacrificial meat, and impure priests may not.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *rauy l'achilah ka'amar* — read the *mishnah*'s rule as being about eating, not *avodah*. One who is unfit to eat does not receive a portion. This reading handles the cases of the *onen* and the *tamei* in *korbanos tzibur* cleanly: they cannot eat, so they do not receive portions. And the *ba'al mum* can eat, so he is entirely consistent with the rule — not an exception at all.

But a new counterexample emerges: *v'harei katan* — a minor can eat, yet does not receive a portion, because the verse *ish ke'achiv* teaches that only an adult (*ish*) shares. So the positive inference — anyone fit to eat does receive a portion — fails in the case of the *katan*. The *gemara*'s response is *ha lo katani* — the *mishnah* does not make that inference. Read only what the *mishnah* explicitly says: unfit to eat does not receive a portion. Do not infer its reverse. The *katan* who is fit to eat but still does not receive a portion is not a contradiction — it simply means the positive inference should not be drawn.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי קָטָן, דְּרָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק! הָא לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: This indicates that any priest who is fit to partake may receive a share. **But isn't** there a **minor, who is fit for partaking and** who **does not receive a share?** The Gemara answers: **This** inverse principle, that any priest who is fit to partake may receive a share, **is not taught.** The mishna means to teach only that any priest who is unfit does not receive a share.

The *gemara* then considers whether one could return to the *avodah* reading and similarly say *ha lo katani* — just read it as the explicit statement and not the inference. If unfit for *avodah* does not receive a portion, one does not infer that fit for *avodah* always does. The cases of *kohen gadol onen* and *tamei bekorbanos tzibur* — who are fit for *avodah* yet do not receive a portion — would then be read as the non-inferred cases, not as contradictions. But the problem remains: if one reads the *mishnah* as being about *avodah*, then the *ba'al mum* — who is unfit for *avodah* yet does receive a portion — is a genuine exception to the explicit statement itself, not merely to its inference. *Rachmana rabyei* — the Torah specially included him. If one reads the *mishnah* as being about *achilah*, the *ba'al mum* is not an exception at all. This is why the *achilah* reading is preferable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, לְעוֹלָם (כדקאמר) [כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ] מֵעִיקָּרָא; אִי מִשּׁוּם טָמֵא – טָמֵא לָא קָתָנֵי, וְאִי מִשּׁוּם בַּעַל מוּם – רַחֲמָנָא רַבְּיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Now that you have arrived at this** conclusion, that the mishna's statement only teaches what it says explicitly, one can say that the mishna **actually** means **what** the Gemara **said at the outset,** that no priest unfit for the service receives a share. **If** one raises an objection **with regard to an impure** priest, who is fit for the service of communal offerings but does not receive a share, answer that the mishna **does not teach** that every fit priest, even **an impure** one, receives a share, only the inverse. **And if** you raise an objection **with regard to a blemished** priest, who is unfit for the service but nevertheless receives a share, answer that **the Merciful One included him** as an exception by the phrase: Every male, as derived above (102a).

## Tumah Tahara

### Dam Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ טָמֵא בִּשְׁעַת זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, וְטָהוֹר בִּשְׁעַת הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים – אֵינוֹ חוֹלֵק. הָא טָהוֹר בִּשְׁעַת זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, וְטָמֵא בִּשְׁעַת הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים – חוֹלֵק;
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Even** if the priest was **ritually impure** only **at the time of the sprinkling of the blood** of the offering, **and** he was **pure at the time of the burning of the fats,** he still **does not receive a share** of the meat. The Gemara notes: **Consequently,** one can infer that a priest who was **pure at the time of the sprinkling of the blood,** even one who was **impure at the time of the burning of the fats, does receive a share.**

The *mishnah* turns to the question of *tumah* and *tahara* and when a *kohen*'s status is assessed for purposes of portion entitlement. The *mishnah* states: even if a *kohen* was *tamei* at the time of *zerikat ha-dam* — the throwing of the blood — and became *tahor* by the time of *haktar ha-chalavim* — the burning of the fat — he does not receive a portion. The key moment is the *dam*. Conversely, a *kohen* who was *tahor* at the *dam* and *tamei* at the *haktar ha-chalavim* does receive a portion — the *mishnah* implies *cholek*, he shares.

### Abba Shaul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל. דְּתַנְיָא, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא טָהוֹר מִשְּׁעַת זְרִיקָה עַד שְׁעַת הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים; דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״הַמַּקְרִיב אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים וְאֶת הַחֵלֶב״ – דַּאֲפִילּוּ הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים נָמֵי בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Abba Shaul says:** A priest **never partakes,** i.e., receives a share, of sacrificial meat, **unless he is pure from the time of sprinkling until the time of the burning of the fats, as the verse states:** "He among the sons of Aaron **who sacrifices the blood of the peace offerings and the fat** shall have the right thigh for a portion" (Leviticus 7:33). The verse **requires** that the priest be pure **even** at the time of **the burning of the fats.**

The *gemara* notes: *matnitin delo ke-Abba Shaul* — our *mishnah* does not follow the position of Abba Shaul. For Abba Shaul holds more stringently: *le'olam eino ochel ad she-yeheh tahor mi-she'at zrikah ve-ad she'at haktar ha-chalavim* — a *kohen* must be *tahor* at both the *dam* and the *chalavim* in order to receive a portion. The proof text is *ha-makriv et dam ha-shelamim ve-et ha-chelev* — the verse links blood and fat together, implying both must be performed by someone in a state of *tahara*. The verse then continues: *lo tihyeh shok ha-yamin le-manah* — the right thigh shall be his portion — meaning only if he has performed both.

### Rav Ashi Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: נִטְמָא בֵּינְתַיִם מַהוּ? בִּשְׁעַת זְרִיקָה וּבִשְׁעַת הַקְטָרָה בָּעֵינַן, וְהָאִיכָּא; אוֹ דִּלְמָא עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא טָהוֹר מִשְּׁעַת זְרִיקָה וְעַד שְׁעַת הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** According to the opinion of Abba Shaul, in a case where the priest **became impure between** the sprinkling of the blood and the burning of the fats but became pure again before the latter, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we require** only that he be pure both **at the time of sprinkling and at the time of** the **burning** of the fats, **and there is** purity at these times? **Or perhaps** Abba Shaul meant that the priest may not receive a share in the meat **unless he is pure from the time of sprinkling until the time of the burning of the fats,** without interruption. The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Ashi raises an unresolved question within Abba Shaul's position: *nitma beintayim mahu?* — what if the *kohen* was *tahor* at the *dam*, became *tamei* in between, immersed in a *mikveh*, had *haarev shemesh*, and was again *tahor* by the time of *haktar ha-chalavim*, which is performed at night? Is Abba Shaul's requirement satisfied — *bishat zrikah uvishat haktara beinun*, you only need *tahara* at those two points, and both are present — or does Abba Shaul require an unbroken *tahara* throughout the entire interval from *zrikah* to *haktar ha-chalavim*? The *gemara* leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved.

## Tvul Yom Plea

### Mincha Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי דִּינָא – מֵרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן גְּמִירְנָא, דְּאָמַר בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא דַּנְתָּהּ – בָּא טְבוּל יוֹם וְאָמַר: תֵּן לִי מִמִּנְחָה וְאוֹכַל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that an impure priest who immersed that day, such that he will not be pure until sunset, and likewise an acute mourner and one who has not yet brought an atonement offering, do not receive a share of sacrificial meat in order to partake of it in the evening. **Rava says: I learned this *halakha* from Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who said in the bathroom: You** can **contend** by way of a story: A priest **who immersed that day came and said** to a pure priest of the same patrilineal priestly family serving in the Temple that day, who was apportioning the sacrificial food: **Give me** a share **of a meal offering, and I will partake** of it in the evening.

Rava introduces a teaching: *hai dina de-Rabbi Eliezer berbi Shimon gmirna* — the following line of reasoning I learned from Rabbi Eliezer the son of Rabbi Shimon. He adds that Rabbi Eliezer told him he worked it out *be-veit ha-kisei* — in the bathroom, a detail the *gemara* will return to.

The discussion concerns the *tvul yom* — a *kohen* who has immersed in a *mikveh* that day but has not yet had *haarev shemesh* and is therefore still in a transitional state of *tumah*. Rather than presenting the rules abstractly, the *gemara* frames it as a drama: the *tvul yom* comes before his fellow *kohanim* and makes a series of requests for portions, each of which is refused via a *kal va-chomer*.

The *tvul yom* begins: *tenu li min ha-minchah ve-ochel* — give me a portion of the *minchah* and I will eat. His fellow *kohen* responds with a *kal va-chomer*: in the world of *chatas*, you — the *tvul yom* — have stronger entitlement. Why? Because when a *kohen* brings his own *chatas*, the meat belongs to him, rather than being divided among the *kohanim* of the *mishmar*. Despite that stronger position, we know that when a *tamei kohen* cannot participate in a *chatas* of a *yisrael*, he receives no portion. Now in the world of *minchah*, your position is even weaker — a *kohen*'s own *minchah* is entirely burned; no *kohen* eats from his own *minchah* at all. *Eino din she-adichacha mi-minchat Yisrael?* — how much more so should I be able to push you away from a *yisrael*'s *minchah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: וּמָה אִם בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחֲךָ – בְּחַטָּאתֶךָ, דְּחִיתִיךְ מֵחַטַּאת יִשְׂרָאֵל; מְקוֹם שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחֲךָ – בְּמִנְחָתֶךָ, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֶדְחֲךָ מִמִּנְחַת יִשְׂרָאֵל?!
>
> **Translation:** The pure priest **said** an *a fortiori* inference **to him: Just as with regard to** a matter **where your right is superior,** i.e., **in** the case of **your** own **sin offering,** to which you have a right even when your family is not serving in the Temple, **I** can nevertheless **deny you** a share **of the sin offering of an Israelite** because you immersed today; **is it not logical that** with regard to a matter **where your right is inferior,** i.e., **in** the case of **your** own **meal offering,** as the meal offering of a priest is not eaten at all, that **I** can **deny you** a share **of the meal offering of an Israelite?**

The *tvul yom* pushes back: perhaps you were able to exclude me from a *chatas* precisely because in that world your rights were stronger as well as mine. But by a *minchah*, where *my* rights are weaker, *your* rights are also weaker — so perhaps we are on more equal footing and you cannot exclude me. The *gemara* then says: this is why the verse *v'ha-kohen ha-makriv osah lo tihyeh* — the *kohen* who brings it, it shall be his — is needed specifically for the *minchah*. It teaches that even in the *minchah* context, the rule applies: *bo hakrev ve-echol* — come forward and perform the *avodah*, then you may eat. Since the *tvul yom* cannot perform the *avodah*, he receives no portion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לַכֹּהֵן הַמַּקְרִיב אֹתָהּ לוֹ תִהְיֶה״ – בֹּא הַקְרֵב וֶאֱכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The pure priest responded: The verse **states:** "And every meal offering that is baked in the oven, and all that is dressed in the stewing pan, and on the griddle, **shall be the priest's that offers it"** (Leviticus 7:9). If you wish to receive a share of a meal offering, **come sacrifice and partake** of one. Since you cannot sacrifice a meal offering, having immersed only today, neither can you receive a share.

### Chatas Pivot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּן לִי מֵחַטַּאת יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאוֹכַל –
>
> **Translation:** The priest who immersed that day made another demand: **Give me** a share **of the sin offering of an Israelite, and I will partake** of it in the evening when I am pure.

The *tvul yom*, denied a *minchah* portion, now pivots: *ten li mi-chatas Yisrael ve-ochel* — at least give me a portion of a *yisrael*'s *chatas*. His fellow *kohen* now turns the *kal va-chomer* around: if I was able to exclude you from a *minchah* even though my rights there are weak, in the world of *chatas* where my rights are strong — *makom she-yafeh kochi b'chatasi* — how much more so can I exclude you. The *tvul yom* again deflects: you could exclude me from the *minchah* precisely because your rights and mine were both weak. In the *chatas* world, yes, your rights are stronger — but so are mine. Perhaps the stronger rights on both sides means you cannot push me away. This is why a verse is needed for *chatas* as well: *ha-kohen ha-mechate osah yochlena* — the *kohen* who performs the *chatas avodah* shall eat it. *Bo chatei ve-echol* — come and perform the *avodah*, then you may eat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחִי – בְּמִנְחָתִי, דְּחִיתִיךְ מִמִּנְחַת יִשְׂרָאֵל; מְקוֹם שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחִי – בְּחַטָּאתִי, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֶדְחֲךָ מֵחַטַּאת יִשְׂרָאֵל?!
>
> **Translation:** The pure priest **said to him: Just as with regard to** a matter **where my right is inferior,** i.e., **in** the case of **my** own **meal offering,** which is not eaten, **I** can **deny you** a share **of the meal offering of an Israelite,** as I explained; so too, **is it not logical that** with regard to a matter **where my right is superior,** i.e., **in** the case of **my** own **sin offering,** to which I have a right even when my family is not serving in the Temple, **I** can **deny you** a share **of the sin offering of an Israelite?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הַכֹּהֵן הַמְחַטֵּא אֹתָהּ יֹאכְלֶנָּה״ – בֹּא חַטֵּא וֶאֱכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The pure priest responded: The verse **states** with regard to the sin offering: **"The priest who effects atonement shall eat it"** (Leviticus 6:19). If you wish to receive a share of a sin offering, **come effect atonement and partake** of one. Since you cannot perform the service of a sin offering, having immersed only today, you cannot receive a share in its meat either.

### Shelamim Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לִי מֵחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק וְאוֹכַל –
>
> **Translation:** The priest who immersed that day made another demand, and **said to him: Give me** a share **of** the **breast and thigh,** the portions that priests receive of peace offerings, **and I will partake** of it in the evening when I am pure.

Denied again, the *tvul yom* makes a final request: *ten li me-chaze ve-shok ve-ochel* — give me the breast and the thigh of the *shelamim*. His fellow *kohen* responds: if I was able to push you away from a *chatas* where you have stronger rights, in the world of *shelamim* where your rights are weaker — *she-ein lecha bahen ela chaze ve-shok*, all you receive are the breast and the thigh — how much more so should I be able to exclude you? The *tvul yom* replies: perhaps you could exclude me from *chatas* because my rights and yours were both weaker with respect to *nashi ve-avadai* — in *chatas*, a *kohen* cannot give his portion to his wife and household servants. But in *shelamim*, even though I receive less meat, I have broader rights in what I do receive — I can share it with my wife and slaves. So perhaps the enhanced quality of my rights in *shelamim* means you cannot push me away. A third verse is therefore needed: *la-kohen ha-mazrek et dam ha-shelamim lo yihyeh* — it shall belong to the *kohen* who throws the blood of the *shelamim*. *Bo zerok ve-echol* — come and throw the blood, then you eat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחֲךָ – בְּחַטָּאתֶךָ, דְּחִיתִיךְ [מֵחַטַּאת יִשְׂרָאֵל] (מֵחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק); מְקוֹם שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחֲךָ – בִּשְׁלָמִים, שֶׁאֵין לָךְ בָּהֶן אֶלָּא חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֶדְחֲךָ?!
>
> **Translation:** The pure priest **said to him: Just as with regard to** a matter **where your right is superior,** i.e., **in** the case of **your** own **sin offering,** to which you have a right to its meat in its entirety, **I** can **deny you** a share **of the sin offering of an Israelite,** as I explained, so too **is it not logical that** with regard to a matter **where your right is inferior,** i.e., **in** the case of **a peace offering, of which you have only** the right to a share of the **breast and thigh, I** can **deny you** a share?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לַכֹּהֵן הַזּוֹרֵק אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים לוֹ יִהְיֶה״ – בֹּא זְרוֹק וֶאֱכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The pure priest responded: The verse **states** with regard to the peace offering: **"It shall be the priest's that sprinkles the blood of the peace offerings** against the altar" (Leviticus 7:14). If you wish to partake of a peace offering, **come sprinkle** its blood **and partake** of it. Since you cannot perform this service, having immersed only today, you cannot receive a share of its meat either.

### Dejected Exit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא טְבוּל יוֹם קוּלָּיו וַחֲמוּרָיו עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, אוֹנֵן מִימִינוֹ, מְחוּסַּר כַּפָּרָה מִשְּׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The story concludes: The priest **who immersed that day left** in disappointment, with **his *a fortiori*** inferences **upon his head,** as they did not help him. And along with him walked **an acute mourner on his right** and a priest **who had not yet** brought an **atonement** offering **on his left.** They too were denied shares, because they were unfit for the Temple service.

*Yatza tvul yom* — the *tvul yom* walks out. The image is vivid: he leaves despondent, *kal va-chomer al rosho* — the weight of all the *kal va-chomer* arguments piled upon his head. Walking alongside him, *onein mi-yimino, mechusar kippurim mi-smolo* — the *onen* on his right, the *mechusar kippurim* on his left — all of them together, dejected, having tried every avenue and been turned away at each one.

## Rav Achai Bechor

### Bechor Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרֵיךְ רַב אַחַאי, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: תֵּן לִי מִבְּכוֹר וְאוֹכַל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥai refutes** this: Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, could have added to the contentions described in the story. **Let** the priest who immersed that day **say to** the pure priest: **Give me** a share **of a firstborn** offering, **and I will partake** of it in the evening.

Rav Achai poses a challenge: why did the *tvul yom* not also request a portion of a *bechor*? That would open yet another *kal va-chomer*. His fellow *kohen* could say: if I was able to exclude you from a *chatas* where your rights are weaker — you cannot share it with your wife and household — how much more so can I exclude you from a *bechor*, where my rights are at their strongest: *de-chulo dilei*, the entire *bechor* belongs to me alone. The *tvul yom* could retort: you could exclude me from *chatas* because your rights and mine were equally weaker there; in a *bechor*, just as your rights are stronger, so too mine are stronger — so perhaps you cannot exclude me. And then the verse would respond: *es damam tizrok al ha-mizbeach ve-es chelbam taktir u-vesaram yihyeh lach* — it belongs to you only when you perform the *dam* and the *chelev*. *Bo zerok ve-echol*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחִי – בְּחַטָּאת, אֵצֶל נָשַׁיי וַעֲבָדַיי, דְּחִיתִיךְ מֵחַטַּאת יִשְׂרָאֵל; מְקוֹם שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחִי – בִּבְכוֹר, דְּכוּלֵּיהּ דִּילִי הִיא – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁאֶדְחֲךָ מִמֶּנּוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Perhaps it is **because** the pure priest could **say to him: Just as with regard to a sin offering, where my right is inferior with regard to my wives and my slaves,** since it may be eaten only by male priests, **I** can nevertheless **deny you** a share **of the sin offering of an Israelite,** as I explained; so too **is it not logical that with regard to a first-born, where my right is superior because all of** its meat **is mine,** as firstborn offerings are given to a specific priest, **I** can **deny you** a share **of it?**

The question is slightly odd, as Rashi notes: a *bechor* is not divided among the *kohanim* of the *mishmar* — it is given directly by the owner to whichever *kohen* he chooses. So the entire framing of the *tvul yom* requesting a share from the group does not quite fit the *bechor* case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה אִם דְּחִיתַנִי מֵחַטַּאת – שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחֲךָ כָּךְ הוֹרַע כֹּחִי; תִּדְחֵינִי מִבְּכוֹר – שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחֲךָ כָּךְ יִפָּה כֹּחִי?!
>
> **Translation:** But the priest who immersed that day could respond: **But if you** can **deny me** a share **of a sin offering, where just as your right is inferior, so is my right inferior;** is it necessarily so that **you** can **deny me** a share **of a firstborn** offering, **where just as your right is superior, so my right is superior?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֶת דָּמָם תִּזְרֹק עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְאֶת חֶלְבָּם תַּקְטִיר... וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לְךָ״ – בֹּא זְרוֹק וֶאֱכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The pure priest could respond: The verse **says** with regard to a firstborn offering: **"You shall sprinkle their blood on the altar and shall make their fat smoke** for an offering made by fire, for a pleasing aroma to the Lord. **And the flesh of them shall be yours,** as the breast and as the thigh, it shall be yours" (Numbers 18:17–18). If you wish to receive a share in the meat of a firstborn, **come sprinkle** its blood **and partake** of it. Why did Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, not include this exchange as another stage in his story?

### Tosafot Achai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ פָּרֵיךְ: מִי כְּתִיב ״וּבְשָׂרָם לַכֹּהֵן הַזּוֹרֵק״?! ״וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לָךְ״ כְּתִיב – אֲפִילּוּ לְכֹהֵן אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **But the other** priest, the one who immersed that day, could **refute** this proof: **Is it written: And the flesh of them shall be for the priest who sprinkles,** as is written with regard to a meal offering, a sin offering, and a peace offering? Rather, it **is written: "And the flesh of them shall be yours,"** indicating that it can be given **even to a priest other** than the one who sacrificed it. This is why Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, did not include this exchange in his story.

Tosafot raises a broader academic observation: *be-chol makom leshona meshuna* — in every instance where Rav Achai appears with a *perach* or a *pashit*, his language is somehow unusual or slightly off. Tosafot cites this pattern as it appears at the beginning of Kesuvot as well. Based on this pattern, Tosafot's teacher Rabbeinu Shmuel suggested that the Rav Achai in these passages is in fact Rav Achai Gaon — the same Rav Achai who authored the *She'iltos* — and that the *Rabbanan Savorai* recorded that his words appear *basar Ravina ve-Rav Ashi*, after the main redaction of the *gemara*. The implication is that Rav Achai Gaon's glosses were written into the margins and subsequently incorporated into the body of the text. Tosafot does note a complication: in Kesuvot, Rav Ashi appears to respond to Rav Achai's question, which would argue against Rav Achai being post-redactional there. The observation is recorded as an interesting textual and historical note without final resolution.

## Bathroom Torah

### Bathroom Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּכׇל מְקוֹם מוּתָּר לְהַרְהֵר, חוּץ מִמֶּרְחָץ וּמִבֵּית הַכִּסֵּא! לְאוֹנְסוֹ שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to Rava's statement that Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, taught this *halakha* in the bathroom. **And how did** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **do this? But doesn't Rabba bar bar Ḥana say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is **permitted to contemplate** matters of Torah **in all places except the bathhouse and the bathroom?** The Gemara answers: A case where the matter was **involuntary is different.** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, was so preoccupied by the issue that he taught it even though he was in the bathroom.

The *gemara* closes with a question that arises from Rava's earlier attribution: he said the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer the son of Rabbi Shimon was worked out *be-veit ha-kisei* — in the bathroom. But how could Rabbi Eliezer contemplate Torah there?

### Bathhouse Prohibition

The answer comes from a statement of Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *be-chol makom mutar le-harhir chutz mi-merchatz u-mi-beit ha-kisei* — contemplation of Torah is permitted in every place except the bathhouse and the bathroom. The *gemara*'s resolution: *le-onsho shani* — if one cannot control it, that is different. Sometimes the mind simply cannot be stopped. Torah enters unbidden.

### Torah vs Korbanot

This closing episode frames the entire discussion in a way that is worth dwelling upon. Throughout this *sugya*, the world of *korbanos* has been defined by rigid boundaries: who is included, who is excluded, who may approach and who must stand back. The *tvul yom*, the *onen*, the *mechusar kippurim* — each one is denied access. The *ba'al mum* gets a portion but cannot perform *avodah*. The sacred geography of *korbanos* is one of strict demarcation, of people being turned away, of entitlement carefully parceled out and withheld.

Torah, by contrast, transgresses every boundary. It enters even the bathroom — the place most removed from sanctity, the place from which Torah is explicitly supposed to be excluded. And not only does it enter, but the insight generated there is transmitted and becomes *halacha*. The contrast is stark: *korbanos* is a world of exclusion, of limited and bounded sacred space. Torah is a force that cannot be contained, that finds its way everywhere, that keeps no one out.
