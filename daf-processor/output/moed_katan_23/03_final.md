# Moed Katan 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Batei Midrashim & Beit Haknesset

### Death of Chacham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חָכָם שֶׁמֵּת — בֵּית מִדְרָשׁוֹ בָּטֵל, אַב בֵּית דִּין שֶׁמֵּת — כׇּל בָּתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת שֶׁבְּעִירוֹ בְּטֵילִין. וְנִכְנָסִין לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וּמְשַׁנִּין אֶת מְקוֹמָן: הַיּוֹשְׁבִים בַּצָּפוֹן — יוֹשְׁבִים בַּדָּרוֹם, הַיּוֹשְׁבִים בַּדָּרוֹם — יוֹשְׁבִים בַּצָּפוֹן. נָשִׂיא שֶׁמֵּת — בָּתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת כּוּלָּן בְּטֵילִין, וּבְנֵי הַכְּנֶסֶת נִכְנָסִין לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְקוֹרִין שִׁבְעָה
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: When **a Sage dies, his study hall ceases** its regular study as a sign of mourning over him. When **the president of the court dies, all of the study halls in his city cease** their regular study, and everyone **enters the synagogue and changes their places** there as a sign of mourning over him. **Those who** ordinarily **sit in the north** should **sit in the south, and those who** ordinarily **sit in the south** should **sit in the north.** When **a *Nasi* dies, all study halls cease** their regular study. On Shabbat, **the members of the synagogue enter the synagogue** for public Torah reading, which requires a congregation of ten, **and seven** people **read** from the Torah.

The daf opens three lines in, picking up from a *braita* that was interrupted at the end of the previous session. The *braita* had been discussing what occurs when an *av beit din*, a *nasi*, or a *chacham* dies. It did not spell out the full aspects of *shiva* and *kriya*, although the *gemara* earlier had addressed *kriya* for a *nasi* in the context of the death of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The emphasis here, however, is on the emptying of the study halls. The *braita* does not specify for how long, but it states that the *batei midrashot* are *beteilim* — and it conveys a sense of public mourning, with the irony that mourning is not about gathering together with everyone else, but about being separated from the community.

### Silent Community

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹצְאִין. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא שֶׁיֵּלְכוּ וִיטַיְּילוּ בַּשּׁוּק, אֶלָּא יוֹשְׁבִין וְדָוִוין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** then **they leave** and pray on their own. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says:** It is **not that they stroll** afterward **in the marketplace,** but **rather they sit** at home **in silent** mourning.

On that point, the *braita* states *nichnasim l'beit haknesset v'korim sheva* — they come to *shul* and read seven *aliyot*, presumably on *Shabbat*. Rabbeinu Gershom there notes *b'sefer Torah d'ein mitpalelim yachad b'beit haknesset elah kol echad v'echad mitpalel b'veito* — that is, rather than davening together communally in the *beit knesset*, everyone prays individually at home. There is an interesting effort to strike a balance: some form of communal gathering or expression is maintained, while simultaneously preserving the sense of communal loss that attends this period.

The *braita* continues with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who states *lo she-yochlu v'yitayalu bashuk* — that they should not walk around socializing in the marketplace — *elah yoshvin v'domim*, rather they should sit together in silence. When people leave their homes in some communal expression of mourning, it is without the social component: they gather, but they do so silently. This is an interesting comparison to the reality of Tisha B'Av. The communal dimension is preserved, but stripped of its usual sociability.

### No Shmua v'Aggada

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין אוֹמְרִים שְׁמוּעָה וְאַגָּדָה בְּבֵית הָאֵבֶל. אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שֶׁהָיָה אוֹמֵר שְׁמוּעָה וְאַגָּדָה בְּבֵית הָאֵבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, **one may not speak** about ***halakha* or *aggada* in a house of mourning,** as this is an activity that brings people joy. **It was said of Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel that he would speak about *halakha* and *aggada* in a house of mourning.**

Tagged on to the end of this *braita* — which appears to be a general ruling and not limited solely to the death of a *chacham* or *nasi* — is the statement: *v'ein omrim shmua v'aggada b'veit ha-avel*. We do not recite *shmua* and *aggada* in the house of the mourner. We had already established that an *avel* is *assur b'talmud Torah*, and a long list of prohibitions was catalogued earlier. What this ruling adds is something more specific: *shmua v'aggada* addresses not the most rigorous analytical *Torah* learning but precisely the kind of religious discourse — *hashkafic* material, stories, inspirational teachings — that might be more likely to occur during a *nichum aveilim* visit. Even that is prohibited.

Equally significant is the phrase *b'veit ha-avel* — in the house of the mourner. The ruling is about the space, not just about the *avel* himself. Even if the *avel* momentarily steps away, visitors standing in the room should not be discussing what they learned in the day's *daf* or the weekly *parasha*. This reading makes sense within the context of the *braita*, because the primary mourning response that it articulated for the death of a *chacham* was not *kriya* or the formal practices of *shiva* — it was *batei midrashim beteilim*. The impact is on *Torah* itself. When a great figure of *Torah* is lost, *Torah* is diminished. In the *beit ha-avel* for an ordinary person, *Torah simcha* is absent — and that absence belongs to the space, not merely to the *avel's* personal status.

One might note, in connection with this passage, that we do recite some *divrei Torah* in the house of mourning today in order to enable *kaddish* to be said. The institution of *kaddish* receives only a single reference in the *gemara*, and its full development is a later phenomenon. It seems likely that the practice of learning *Torah* in the *avel's* house and the development of *kaddish* went hand in hand — the one enabling the other — though this remains a fascinating historical question deserving its own investigation.

### Chananya's Exception

The *gemara* then records the statement about Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel. The phrasing *haya omer* suggests something more than occasional inadvertent lapses; it implies an intentional and perhaps even regular practice, possibly in the context of a public teaching. One wonders whether this relates to *nichum aveilim* specifically — whether he was offering a religious discourse as part of consoling the mourners, as we do find in the *gemara* instances of *nichum aveilim* that incorporate *Torah* and *aggada* (invoking, for example, the death of Aharon). The tension between the general prohibition and Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel's apparent practice is notable and is left unresolved.

### Simchat Mereus

Before continuing to the next topic, it is worth pausing to address the issue of *simchat mere'us* that was discussed in the previous session, given its considerable practical relevance. The *gemara* had distinguished between general *simcha* — which seems more problematic — and *simchat mere'us*, which is about gathering with friends in a celebratory context. Yet by the end of that discussion, the *gemara* appeared to have largely conflated the two categories, concluding that both are prohibited during *shloshim* for most people and during twelve months for one's father or mother, except in very particular circumstances.

This raises the natural question: are there exceptions, and what does a *simcha shel mitzvah* do to the calculus? Is a *mitzvah* occasion better or worse? Tosafot on 22b takes up exactly this question, citing *Masechet Semachot* which states *im haya seudat mitzvah mutar* — if it was a *seudat mitzvah* it is permitted — but immediately acknowledges: *lo yadanu mahu karui seudat mitzvah l'hatir b'avel*, we do not know precisely what qualifies as a *seudat mitzvah* sufficient to permit an *avel* to attend. One suggestion mentioned is *seudat kiddush Rosh Chodesh*, though the meaning of that is itself unclear. Avi ha-Ezri in the name of Rabbeinu Shimshon rules that a *seudat bris milah* is permitted, on the grounds that it does not have the character of *simcha* — as we find in the first chapter of Ketubot that the formula *she-hasimcha b'ma'ono* is not recited there because people are preoccupied — *de-tridi v'leika simcha*. Yet this leads to the further question noted by Tosafot: if *bris milah* is less problematic than *simchat mere'us*, is that so obvious? Perhaps in some respects it is more problematic.

The practical questions multiply: is it permitted to eat with the service staff rather than at the main table? Tosafot rules *ein lachaleik bein seudas hamishamshim useudah atzmah* — one cannot make that distinction. However, *khnisah belo achilah shari de'ein simchah belo achilah ushtiyah* — simply entering without eating is permitted, since there is no *simcha* without eating and drinking. Tosafot ha-Rosh rules in accordance with the more permissive view among those cited.

## Remarriage Law

### Simchat Mitzvah

The Rosh addresses the more fundamental question: is a *seudat mitzvah* better or worse than a *seudat hareshut*? The Ra'avad raises the objection that from the *gemara* here it seems that *seudas mitzvah* carries a *more* severe prohibition than *seudas hareshut*, since *simchat mitzvah* appears more serious than *simchat mere'us*, which would make it worse rather than better. *Aveil Rabbati* teaches: *kol sheloshim yom assur leileich leveit hamishteh* — for the full thirty days one may not go to a *beit hamishteh*, and for a father or mother the restriction extends until *sheyiga'aru bo chaveirav* — until one's friends effectively reproach him for not attending. This is notable: according to this source, the period for parents is not twelve months as we rule, but simply until social pressure compels one's return. And then the *Aveil Rabbati* adds: *ela im kein haysah lesheim shamayim* — unless the gathering was *lesheim shamayim*, for the sake of Heaven. The *alma* drawn from this is *de-seudas mitzvah yeish bah heter yoseir miveseudas hareshut* — a *seudat mitzvah* carries greater leniency than an ordinary gathering.

The Rosh then quotes Rav Yehudah Bartzeloni: *yeish shenohagim shenichnasim besoch yud beis chodesh leveit hachupah lishmoa habrachos ulevakeir* — there are those who customarily enter the *chupah* during the twelve months to hear the blessings and pay respects — *aval lo yochal*, but they do not eat. And there are those who are stringent and do not enter at all. The question of whether there is *simcha* in *simchas chupah* even without *achilah ushtiyah* is left open. This entire range — whether *mitzvah* makes things better or worse, whether presence without eating helps, whether being *lesheim shamayim* changes the status — remains substantially unclarified and continues to generate *she'eilot* in practice.

## Movement in Shiva

### Four Week Progression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָבֵל, שַׁבָּת רִאשׁוֹנָה — אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ, שְׁנִיָּה — יוֹצֵא וְאֵינוֹ יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ, שְׁלִישִׁית — יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר, רְבִיעִית — הֲרֵי הוּא כְּכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: During the **first week** after his bereavement, **the mourner may not go out of the opening of his house.** During the **second** week, **he may go out, but he may not sit in his** usual **place** in the synagogue. During the **third** week, **he may sit in his** usual **place but he may not speak.** During the **fourth** week, **he is like any** other **person.**

A new topic now comes into focus — one that has been, surprisingly, in the background until this point. Until now, the *gemara* had not stated as a formal *halacha* what most people would probably identify as the central feature of *shiva*: that the *avel* stays home and people come to him. That is now addressed directly. *Tanu rabbanan*: the *avel* during the first *shabbat* — meaning here the first week — does not go out from the door of his house. The second *shabbat* he goes out but does not sit in his regular place. The third week he sits in his regular place but does not speak. By the fourth week *harei hu kechol adam* — he is like everyone else.

### Weekly Restrictions

The *baraita* thus outlines a gradual progression over four weeks, describing the *avel's* reintegration into the community. The restrictions by week are: *shabbat rishona eino yotzei mipetach beito* — he does not leave his home; *shniya yotzei ve'eino yoshev bimkomo* — he leaves but does not sit in his regular place; *shlishis yoshev bimkomo ve'eino medaber* — he sits in his place but does not speak; *revi'is harei hu kechol adam* — he is fully restored. This last stage falls within *shloshim* but before it concludes.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא הוּצְרְכוּ לוֹמַר שַׁבָּת רִאשׁוֹנָה לֹא יֵצֵא מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁהֲרֵי הַכֹּל נִכְנָסִין לְבֵיתוֹ לְנַחֲמוֹ. אֶלָּא: שְׁנִיָּה — אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ, שְׁלִישִׁית — יוֹצֵא וְאֵינוֹ יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ, רְבִיעִית — יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר, חֲמִישִׁית — הֲרֵי הוּא כְּכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: They did not need to say** that **during the first week** the mourner **may not go out of the opening of his house.** This teaches us nothing new, **as** at that time **everyone goes into his house to console him. Rather,** during the **second** week, he may **not go out of the opening of his house.** During the **third** week, **he may go out, but he may not sit in his** usual **place** in the synagogue. During the **fourth** week, **he may sit in his** usual **place, but he may not speak.** During the **fifth** week, **he is like any** other **person.**

Rabbi Yehuda offers a different formulation: *lo hutzrechu lomar shabbat rishona lo yetzei mipetach beito* — one does not need to state as a *halacha* that the *avel* does not leave during the first week. *Sheharei hakol nichnasim leveito lenachamo* — that is simply what *shiva* is: everyone comes to be *menachem avel* and the *avel* stays home. This captures something essential. Rabbi Yehuda seems to be pointing not to a formal prohibition but to the nature of the experience itself. Staying home during the first week is not a rabbinic rule that requires statement — it is the essence of what *shiva* is. The *halacha* that Rabbi Yehuda does articulate is that during the second week the *avel* still does not leave — and this is genuinely demanding, since people are no longer coming to be *menachem*, yet the *avel* remains at home, now without the social dimension of receiving visitors.

### Essence of Shiva

Rabbi Yehuda's framing invites a deeper question about what *shiva* is essentially, as opposed to what its specific practices are. The *halacha* that the *avel* remains home is perhaps not a *halacha* of *aveilus* at all in the conventional sense — it is the definition of *aveilus*, the very thing the institution is about. This is one reason why, according to some, it does not appear in the standard enumeration of *minhagei avel*: it is the frame within which all other practices occur, not one item in a list alongside bathing and wearing shoes.

Rabbi Yehuda's version of the *baraita* extends the progression by a week: the second week *eino yotzei mipetach beito*, the third week *yotzei ve'eino yoshev bimkomo*, the fourth week *yoshev bimkomo ve'eino medaber*, and only by the fifth week *harei hu kechol adam*.

### Sitting & Speaking

Tosafot on *Rabbi Yehuda omer lo hutzrechu* notes that the *Yerushalmi* rules *halacha kedivrei hamosif beyamim* — the law follows the one who adds days — which is striking given that we generally say *halacha k'divrei ha-meikel b'avel*. Tosafot then relates a family tradition: Rabbeinu Yitzchak Zikni would sit in his place until the fourth week, following the more stringent opinion, but Tosafot himself did not understand his reasoning. He speculates: *shema hayah sover k'Rabbi Yehuda* — perhaps he held like Rabbi Yehuda — but then notes the difficulty, since if one follows Rabbi Yehuda then during the second week one should not leave the house at all, and no one practices that. If one is already leaving the house after the first week, how can one simultaneously practice Rabbi Yehuda's position regarding *yoshev bimkomo*?

Tosafot then offers a subtle resolution: *yeish lomar b'ha ashkechan tuva lechiyuva d'aveilus shiva* — perhaps *shiva* ends the period of stricter *aveilus* and overrides, and we hold accordingly. But then he proposes another possibility: perhaps Rabbeinu Yitzchak Zikni actually held like the *rabbanan*, not like Rabbi Yehuda — *v'sevara leih taama k'rabbanan*. According to the *rabbanan*, the third week the *avel yoshev bimkomo* but *eino medaber*. Tosafot then draws a fine conceptual point: *umishum d'tzarich leyshev bimkomo ve'eino medaber — vetov yoter sheyeishev chutz limkomo deshema ein issur ledaber ki im bimkomo* — since the restriction on speech is tied to sitting in one's *makom*, if he avoids sitting in his *makom*, he avoids the restriction on speech. The prohibition is not a general ban on speaking with people; it is contextually linked to the act of being *yoshev bimkomo*, of publicly resuming one's full standing in the community. To speak while *yoshev bimkomo* signals the full restoration of one's communal status — which is precisely what is being delayed.

This conceptual point raises a further question: what exactly is the *makom* in question? The most natural reading, and the one that emerges from our current practice, is one's seat in *shul*. But the *gemara* itself is not explicit, and the restriction might equally apply to any designated public space where an individual has a fixed position — a seat at the theater, a place in the *beit midrash*, or in principle any place where one's presence signals a return to normal communal standing.

A further question raised about the *baraita* is whether *shabbat* here means *Shabbat* specifically or the week (*shavua*). This is a significant debate among the *rishonim*. If the reference is to *Shabbat* itself, then the *halacha* about not going to *shul* during the first week would apply even on *Shabbat*, and visitors would be coming for *nichum aveilim* on *Shabbat* — which is indeed what the *baraita* implies.

### Exceptions & Latitudes

On this question, the Rosh in *siman* 46 records that the custom in Ashkenaz was as follows: *ha-avel holech leveit haknesses b'shabbos* — the *avel* goes to *shul* on *Shabbat*. After prayer, *ha-avel yotzei techilla* — the *avel* leaves first — *veyoshev lifnei beit haknesses* — and sits outside the *shul* — *vechol hakahal yotzim veyoshvim etzlo* — and the entire community comes out and sits with him — *va-omer ha-avel veholech leveito, vechol hakahal holchim imo, veyoshvin sham sha'ah achas* — the *avel* returns home and the entire community walks with him and sits with him for a time. This is a fully communal, public act of *nichum aveilim* on *Shabbat* — and its scope, involving the entire congregation, is quite striking. Rabbeinu Hai Gaon adds that *va-afilu b'Shabbat hakol k'minhag hamedina* — even regarding whether one leaves home on *Shabbat* during the first week, everything depends on local custom. In places where it was customary to come early to *shul*, the *avel* would come; in places where it was customary to remain home on *Shabbat*, he would remain home.

As for the absolute-sounding language of the *braita* — *eino yotzei mipetach beito* — the *Rema* clarifies that the prohibition is specifically *letayel o lemassa umattan*, for leisure or commerce. He further rules *hameikel latzeit b'layla mipnei ha-tzorech lo hifsid* — one who is lenient about going out at night for a practical need has not lost anything, since going out at night lacks the public social dimension of daytime activity. The *Rema* also permits leaving for cases of significant need — if a *moshel* summons the *avel*, if travel is required, or in cases of *davar ha-avud*, a significant financial loss. The home is not meant to be a prison; the halachic frame permits real latitude when genuine need arises.

## Remarriage and Mourning

### Shloshim Remarriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם לַנִּישּׂוּאִין. מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ — אָסוּר לִישָּׂא אִשָּׁה אַחֶרֶת עַד שֶׁיַּעַבְרוּ עָלָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה רְגָלִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: רֶגֶל רִאשׁוֹן וְשֵׁנִי — אָסוּר, שְׁלִישִׁי — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** another *baraita*: During **the entire thirty-day** period of mourning, it is prohibited **to marry.** If **one's wife died, it is prohibited to marry another wife until three Festivals pass** since her death. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Until the **first and second** Festivals have passed, **he is prohibited** from marrying; before the **third** Festival, however, he is **permitted** to do so.

The *gemara* now turns to another topic: *kol shloshim yom lenissuin* — for all thirty days a person may not get married. This is framed, as will be seen, largely from the man's perspective. If a man's wife dies, the prohibition extends further: *assur lissa isha acheret ad she-ya'avru alav shalosh regalim* — he may not remarry until three *regalim* have passed. This is not primarily a matter of *minhagei aveilus* but of respect for his first wife and marriage.

### Yehuda's Leniencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אֵין לוֹ בָּנִים — מוּתָּר לִישָּׂא לְאַלְתַּר מִשּׁוּם בִּיטּוּל פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה. הִנִּיחָה לוֹ בָּנִים קְטַנִּים — מוּתָּר לִישָּׂא לְאַלְתַּר מִפְּנֵי פַרְנָסָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he does not have children, he is permitted to marry** another wife **immediately due to** the need to not **neglect** the mitzva to **be fruitful and multiply.** Since he has not yet fulfilled the mitzva of procreation, he is still required to marry a wife. Any delay might result in a lost opportunity for marriage. Similarly, if his wife died and **left him young children, he is permitted to marry** another wife **immediately, so that** she might **take care of them.**

Rabbi Yehuda rules more leniently: *regel rishon v'sheni assur, shelishi mutar* — the first two *regalim* are prohibited, by the third it is permitted. Two exceptions allow remarriage immediately (*le'altar*): if a man has no children, *mutar lissa le'altar mishum bittul pru urevu* — he may remarry right away because of the *mitzvah* of *pru urevu*; and if he has left with young children who need care, *mutar lissa le'altar mipnei parnasatam* — he may remarry right away for their support. The question of what *le'altar* precisely means becomes important in the story that follows.

### Joseph's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁמֵּתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף הַכֹּהֵן, וְאָמַר לַאֲחוֹתָהּ בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת: לְכִי וּפַרְנְסִי אֶת בְּנֵי אֲחוֹתֵךְ, וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן לֹא בָּא עָלֶיהָ אֶלָּא לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה. מַאי לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** There was **an incident when the wife of Yosef the Priest died, and he said to her sister at the cemetery** immediately after the funeral: **Go and care for your sister's children.** In other words, he alluded that he wished to marry her immediately. **But even though** he married her immediately, **he did not engage in sexual relations with her for a long time** afterward. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term: **A long time? Rav Pappa said: After thirty days.**

A case is recounted: *ma'aseh shemeta ishto shel Yosef HaKohen* — the wife of Yosef HaKohen died. *Ve'amar la'achosa b'veit hakevarot* — he proposed to her sister right there at the cemetery during the burial. *Lechi u-farnasi es bnei achotei* — he asked her to come and support his sister's children. And yet, *af al pi kein lo ba aleha ela l'zman meruba* — even so, he did not consummate the marriage until a significant time had passed. Rav Pappa explains: *le-achar shloshim yom* — he waited thirty days, since that is the period of *simcha*. So on the one hand there is an allowance — greater than the general rule, since he does not need to wait for *shalosh regalim* or even *shnei regalim* — but on the other hand, since the allowance is about raising the children and the element of *simcha* and its associated aspects of marriage remain a concern, he waited until the end of the month. In a case where the exception is based on *pru urevu* rather than childcare, Tosafot indicates that relations would be permitted immediately upon marriage, since that is precisely what the allowance is for.

### Tosafot Objection

Tosafot raises a significant objection to this story. The idea that a marriage proposal — and apparently the marriage itself — could occur during *shiva*, or even at the cemetery during the burial, is deeply troubling to Tosafot, based both on intrinsic discomfort with the notion and on other *sugyot*. Tosafot therefore offers two ways of softening the *gemara's* account: either *beit hakevarot* is being used metaphorically rather than literally, or the proposal was made at the cemetery but the actual marriage did not take place until after *shiva*. In any case, Tosafot is clearly unsettled by the *gemara's* quite vivid and memorable formulation.

A separate conceptual question addressed at length in Tosafot and the Rosh concerns the nature of the *shalosh regalim* requirement itself. Tosafot asks: what is the reason for waiting *shalosh regalim*? He offers two explanations. The first is *v'lo yishkach ahavas ishto* — so that the memory of his first wife and the love of that marriage will stay with him through the experience of *shalosh regalim* without her. The second explanation is framed around the second wife: *kdei she-yisa isha acheres v'lo yihiyeh zachur min ha-rishona she-lo yihiyeh shtei deiot b'mita* — so that when he remarries, the memory of his first wife will have faded sufficiently that he will not effectively be thinking of her during intimacy with his second wife, which the *gemara* elsewhere treats as a form of virtual betrayal. A third concern is *pen yazkirena ahavas ishto l'kantra* — lest he mention his first wife to the second in a way that wounds her, for example by comparing her unfavorably to his late wife in small domestic matters.

Tosafot then cites Rabbeinu Yonah for an important conceptual clarification: one cannot apply the principle *halacha k'divrei ha-meikel b'avel* to this discussion in order to rule like Rabbi Yehuda's position of *shnei regalim* rather than *shalosh regalim*. The reason is *d'hani mili d'pligi b'hacha ein zu b'avel d'yimei aveilus kalu lahen* — this debate is not about *hilchos aveilus* at all. By the time we are discussing *shalosh regalim*, the *avel's* period of *aveilus* is long over. The waiting period is a *halacha* about respect for one's first or second wife — about the concerns of *lo yishkach* or *shtei deiot b'mita* — and therefore the principle of leniency in *aveilus* simply does not apply. This is a striking clarification: the prohibition on remarriage for *shalosh regalim* is not a *halacha* in *hilchos aveilus*; it is a *halacha* in a different register entirely.

## Gihutz Restrictions

### Gihutz Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם לְגִיהוּץ, אֶחָד כֵּלִים חֲדָשִׁים וְאֶחָד כֵּלִים יְשָׁנִים יוֹצְאִין מִתּוֹךְ הַמַּכְבֵּשׁ. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: לֹא אָסְרוּ אֶלָּא כֵּלִים חֲדָשִׁים בִּלְבָד. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא אָסְרוּ אֶלָּא כֵּלִים חֲדָשִׁים לְבָנִים בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** yet another *baraita*: During **the entire thirty-day** period of mourning, it is prohibited **to** wear **ironed** garments, **whether they are new garments or old garments taken out of the press,** as ironed garments appear to be new. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi disagrees and **says:** The Sages **prohibited** wearing **only new garments. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: They prohibited** wearing **only new white garments.**

*Tanu rabbanan: kol shloshim yom legihutz* — for all thirty days there is a prohibition on *gihutz*, which is essentially a thorough pressing or dry-cleaning of clothing, as distinct from ordinary laundering. While the prohibition on laundering clothes in general applies only during the first week of mourning, the prohibition on *gihutz* extends for the entire month of *shloshim*.

### Clothing Disputes

The *baraita* continues: *echad keilim chadashim ve'echad keilim yeshanim* — whether new or old garments, *gihutz* is problematic, because even old clothes taken from the press look fresher than they would otherwise. *Yotzim mitoch hamachbesh* — if clothes come directly from the press, that is an issue regardless of age. Rabbi rules more leniently: *lo asru ela keilim chadashim bilvad* — only new clothes, since old clothes even after *gihutz* will never truly look new again, so there is no real concern. Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon is stricter in one sense and narrower in another: *lo asru ela keilim chadashim levanim bilvad* — only new white clothes, since only white fabric can be restored through pressing to the appearance of true freshness.

### Abaye & Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי נָפֵיק בִּגְרָדָא דְסַרְבָּלָא כְּרַבִּי. רָבָא נָפֵיק בְּחִימּוּצְתָּא רוֹמִיתָא סוּמַּקְתָּא חַדְתִּי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye went out** while he was in mourning **in an old white garment, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rava,** on the other hand, **went out in a new, red Roman cloak,** as he acted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.**

Two *amoraic* stories illustrate how these opinions played out in practice. Abaye (*Abaye nafik begarda desarvela*) went out wearing an old white cloak — following Rabbi's ruling, since it was not new. Rava (*Rava nafik bechimutzta d'romita sumakta charti*) went out wearing a new red Roman cloak — following Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon's ruling, since it was not white. In practice, then, the actual parameters of what is prohibited during *shloshim* in the category of *gihutz* are rendered quite narrow.

### Laundering Debate

Despite the apparent clarity of this *baraita*, there is a substantial Tosafot here because other *sugyot* seem to suggest a prohibition on *kibbus* — actual laundering — for the full thirty days, not just for the first week. Tosafot works through several possible ways of reconciling these sources. Perhaps in those other *sugyot* the term *kibbus* (laundering) actually meant *gihutz*. Or conversely, perhaps *gihutz* here does not mean mere pressing but laundering with soap, so that what is prohibited for the first week alone is laundering without soap, while laundering with soap — which constitutes *gihutz* — is what is prohibited for the month. There is an additional discussion arising from the view that outside *Eretz Yisrael* one does not know how to perform *gihutz* to the same standard as was practiced there, and therefore nothing in the diaspora would qualify as *gihutz*, rendering the entire category of the month-long prohibition effectively inapplicable outside of *Eretz Yisrael*. The result is that the actual *halacha* remains genuinely contested, as is also evident in the *Shulchan Aruch*, and what is prohibited in this category after the first week turns out to be very limited in scope.

## Aveilus on Shabbat

### Shabbat Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: שַׁבָּת עוֹלָה וְאֵינָהּ מַפְסֶקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: This is **because** the Sages **said** that **Shabbat counts** as one of the days of mourning, **but it does not interrupt** the mourning period, which continues after Shabbat.

The *mishnah* states: *Shabbat olah v'einah mafsekes* — *Shabbat* counts toward *shiva* but does not interrupt it. This is a foundational rule: every *shiva* runs through *Shabbat*, and *Shabbat* is counted among the seven days.

### Yehuda vs. Galil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה וּבְּנֵי גָלִילָא. הָנֵי אָמְרִי: יֵשׁ אֲבֵילוּת בְּשַׁבָּת, וְהָנֵי אָמְרִי: אֵין אֲבֵילוּת בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated that with regard to mourning on Shabbat, there is a difference in practice between **the residents of Judea and the residents of the Galilee. These say: There is** some **mourning on Shabbat,** i.e., with regard to mourning rites that can be observed privately in the mourner's home and will go unnoticed by other people; **whereas those say: There is no mourning on Shabbat** at all.

What follows is a striking *gemara*: rather than a dispute between named rabbinic authorities, we have a geographic debate between *Bnei Yehuda* and *Bnei Galil* — two regions with different practices. The *gemara* will treat this as a substantive halachic debate, even though it begins as an account of different regional customs.

### Aveilus Practiced

*Honey amri yesh aveilus b'Shabbat* — one group says that *aveilus* is practiced on *Shabbat*. What exactly this means requires examination. Does it mean full *aveilus*, with all its practices? Or does it mean only the private dimension of *aveilus* — *devarim she'betzina*? The *gemara* does not specify. Rashi and Tosafot understand the debate to be specifically about *devarim she'betzina*, the practices that do not publicly announce one's status as an *avel*, such as refraining from marital relations and perhaps certain forms of bathing. But this reading is not explicitly forced by the *gemara's* language.

### No Aveilus

*Ve-honey amri ein aveilus b'Shabbat* — the other group says *aveilus* is not practiced on *Shabbat* at all. This position is not self-evident. One might ask: if *regel* interrupts *aveilus* because *regel* is about *simcha*, why should *Shabbat* limit *aveilus*? *Shabbat* is not characterized by *simcha* in the same way as *regel* — rather, it is characterized by *kavod* and *oneg*. If *Shabbat* does not carry the power of *simcha* to terminate *aveilus*, why would it have the power to suspend *aveilus* practices? One might argue that full *aveilus* should continue on *Shabbat* uninterrupted.

### Private Practices

Tosafot offers the key reasoning: even if *Shabbat* lacks the character of *simcha* and therefore does not have the power of *regel* to be *mafsik*, *Shabbat* is nevertheless a day of *kavod* and *oneg* — enjoyment and honor. This is inconsistent with practices that place a person in a state of discomfort or public mourning expression. The logic that *Shabbat* creates a "middle state" — neither *simcha* nor *tza'ar* — is what underlies the ruling that *devarim she'betzina noheg*, private mourning practices continue, while public expressions of mourning are suspended.

One broader question lurks here: are the practices of *aveilus* meant to express an inner experience that already exists, or are they meant to create and cultivate that inner experience? This is a genuine tension in how one understands the function of *halachic* mourning practices. Rav Soloveitchik's well-known treatment of *hilchos aveilus* emphasizes that these practices are expressions of an inner emotional and existential reality, not merely formal behaviors. At the same time, the general power of ritual is precisely that it helps internalize states that might not arise spontaneously — it helps ground the mourner in the reality of loss. Both understandings seem valid, and they need not be mutually exclusive.

### Shabbat's Middle Way

Tosafot makes this conceptual point explicit. The reason *regel* terminates *aveilus* is *mishum de'ketiv v'samachta* — because *regel* is explicitly associated with *simcha*, which is fundamentally incompatible with *aveilus*. *Shabbat*, by contrast, does not carry that explicit *simcha* verse, and therefore does not interrupt. Yet the *Yerushalmi* explains why *aveilus* is nonetheless not practiced publicly on *Shabbat*: *mishum de'ketiv birchas Hashem hi ta'ashir v'lo yosif etzev imah* — the blessing of God enriches and adds no suffering. *Uve-Shabbat ketiv bei bracha* — *Shabbat* is characterized by blessing, and a day of blessing is not a day of *etzev*, of pain or sadness. So *Shabbat* occupies this middle position: not *simcha* (and therefore not *mafsik* like *regel*), but also not *tza'ar* (and therefore not fully consistent with public mourning).

### Mishnah Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ אֲבֵילוּת בְּשַׁבָּת, דְּקָתָנֵי ״עוֹלָה״. מַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין אֲבֵילוּת בְּשַׁבָּת, דְּקָתָנֵי ״אֵינָהּ מַפְסֶקֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The ones who said** that **there is** some **mourning on Shabbat** rely on **that** which is **taught,** that Shabbat **counts** as one of the days of mourning, implying that some degree of mourning applies on that day. **The ones who said** that **there is no mourning on Shabbat** at all base this on **that** which is **taught** that Shabbat **does not interrupt** the mourning period.

The *gemara* then raises a question about the language of the *mishnah* itself. According to the opinion that *ein aveilus b'Shabbat*, why does the *mishnah* say *olah* — that *Shabbat* counts toward *shiva*? If *aveilus* is not being practiced at all on *Shabbat*, what is being "counted"? The *gemara* answers: *aidi d'ka'baei le-mitni seifa eino oleh* — since the *mishnah* was going to state regarding *regalim* that they do *not* count, it stated in the opening clause that *Shabbat* does count. The language of *olah* is not meant to imply that *aveilus* is actively practiced on *Shabbat* and therefore counts; it is simply the counterpart phrasing to *ein olin* in the case of *regalim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךְ יֵשׁ אֲבֵילוּת בְּשַׁבָּת, הַשְׁתָּא אֲבֵילוּת נָהֲגָא, אַפְסוֹקֵי מִיבַּעְיָא? וְאֶלָּא הָא קָתָנֵי ״עוֹלָה״! אַיְּידֵי דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵא סֵיפָא ״אֵינָן עוֹלִים״, תְּנָא רֵישָׁא ״עוֹלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The latter argue as follows: **If it should enter your mind** to say that **there is** some **mourning on Shabbat,** there is a difficulty, for **now** that it has been stated that actual **mourning applies** on Shabbat, **is** it **necessary** to teach us that this day does not **interrupt** the mourning period? Rather, the conclusion must be that there is no mourning on Shabbat whatsoever. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the mishna that Shabbat **counts** as one of the days of mourning, implying that it is just like the other days of mourning, and at least some mourning rites are practiced on it? The Gemara answers: **Since** the mishna **wished to teach** in **the latter clause** that the days of a Festival **do not count** toward the requisite days of mourning, **it taught** also **in the first clause** that Shabbat **counts** as one of the days of mourning, although no mourning rites are practiced on it.

The reciprocal question is then posed: according to the opinion that *yesh aveilus b'Shabbat*, why does the *mishnah* say *einah mafsekes* — that *Shabbat* does not interrupt? If *aveilus* is being practiced, obviously it does not interrupt. The answer is the same: *mishum d'ka'baei le-mitni seifa mafsikin* — since the *mishnah* was going to say that *regalim* do interrupt, it stated in the opening clause that *Shabbat* does not, even though this is obvious according to this opinion. So both opinions can account for both halves of the *mishnah's* language through the same structural explanation.

## Onein & Aveilus

### Onein Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: מִי שֶׁמֵּתוֹ מוּטָּל לְפָנָיו — אוֹכֵל בְּבַיִת אַחֵר. אֵין לוֹ בַּיִת אַחֵר — אוֹכֵל בְּבֵית חֲבֵרוֹ, אֵין לוֹ בַּיִת חֲבֵרוֹ — עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ מְחִיצָה עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. אֵין לוֹ דָּבָר לַעֲשׂוֹת מְחִיצָה — מַחְזִיר פָּנָיו וְאוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us say** that this is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to whether or not some mourning rites are observed even on Shabbat, for it was taught in a *baraita*: **One whose deceased** relative **is laid out before him eats in another room.** If **he does not have another room, he eats in the house of a friend. If he does not have a friend's house** available, **he makes a partition ten handbreadths** high between him and the deceased, so that he may eat. If **he does not have material with which to make a partition, he averts his face** from the dead **and eats.**

The *gemara* now introduces the figure of the *onein* — one whose deceased relative has not yet been buried, *meiso mutal lefanav*. A *baraita* details the *onein's* eating arrangements: *mi she-meiso mutal lefanav ochel b'vayis acher* — he eats in another house. If he has no other house, he eats at a friend's house. If that is also unavailable, *oseh lo mechitzah asarah tefachim* — he makes a partition ten *tefachim* high, creating a distinct zone. If even that is impossible, *machzir panav v'ochel* — he turns his face away from the *meit* and eats, so as not to be literally chewing in the presence of the deceased.

### Onein vs. Aveilus

What is striking about this *gemara* is that it treats *aninus* and *aveilus* as states that can be compared and from which inferences can be drawn in either direction. This is conceptually significant because normally these two states are kept rigorously separate. The question of their relationship underlies a major dispute among the *rishonim*. Tosafot (and what appears to be a *Rita*) takes the position that these are completely distinct states with no intrinsic relationship to one another. The Rambam, by contrast, and partly because of *gemarot* like this one, proposes a model of fundamental continuity: *aninus* and *aveilus* are essentially one ongoing state of mourning from the time of death through the end of *shiva*. The special features of *aninus* — the additional restrictions on meat, wine, *berachot*, and *mitzvot*, as well as certain leniencies like being permitted to bathe and wear shoes in order to tend to the *meit* — are all overrides or modifications triggered by the specific circumstance of *meiso mutal lefanav*, but they do not constitute a categorically different state. In this conceptual model, the default assumption is that an *onein* practices all the practices of *aveilus* unless there is a specific reason to make an exception; the leniencies we find are all justified as enabling the *onein* to attend properly to the burial. This is conceptually unusual, but *gemarot* like this one push certain *rishonim* in that direction.

### Onein Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ מֵיסֵב וְאוֹכֵל, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל בָּשָׂר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן, וְאֵין מְבָרֵךְ וְאֵין מְזַמֵּן, וְאֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו וְאֵין מְזַמְּנִין עָלָיו, וּפָטוּר מִקְּרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִן הַתְּפִלָּה וּמִן הַתְּפִילִּין, וּמִכׇּל מִצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in any case, **he does not recline while he eats,** as reclining is characteristic of a festive meal; **and he neither eats meat nor drinks wine; and he does not recite a blessing** before eating to exempt others from their obligation; **and he does not** recite the formula to **invite** the participants in the meal to join together in the Grace after Meals, **and they do not recite a blessing over him nor do others invite him** to join in the Grace after Meals, as he cannot be a member of the three required to recite the formula. **And he is exempt from the recitation of *Shema*, and from** the *Amida* **prayer, and from** donning **phylacteries, and from** performing **all** of **the mitzvot mentioned in the Torah.**

The *baraita* enumerates the *onein's* restrictions in detail: *v'eino meisav v'ochel* — he does not recline while eating, a posture associated with luxury and relaxed enjoyment, since his attention should be on the *meit*; *v'eino ochel basar v'eino shotheh yayin* — he does not eat meat or drink wine; *v'ein mevarech v'ein mezamein* — he does not recite *berachot* and does not lead or participate in *zimun*; *ve'ein mevarchin alav v'ein mezamnin alav* — others do not include him in *zimun* either. Further: *u'patur mikrias shema u'min hatfillah u'min hatfillin u'micol mitzot ha'amurot batorah* — he is exempt from *keriat shema*, prayer, *tefillin*, and all *mitzvot* of the Torah.

The abstraction of the concept of *meiso mutal lefanav* is worth noting here. The *baraita* begins with the literal case — the body is physically present in the room — but the halachic status of *aninus* extends more broadly to anyone whose deceased relative has not yet been buried, even when the person is in a different city and cannot physically be present at the burial. The conceptual category of *mutal lefanav* becomes larger than its literal meaning.

### Onein on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשַׁבָּת — מֵיסֵב וְאוֹכֵל, וְאוֹכֵל בָּשָׂר וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן, וּמְבָרֵךְ וּמְזַמֵּן, וּמְבָרְכִין וּמְזַמְּנִין עָלָיו, וְחַיָּיב בִּקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּבִתְפִילָּה וּבִתְפִילִּין, וּבְכׇל מִצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב בְּאֵלּוּ — נִתְחַיֵּיב בְּכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **But on Shabbat he reclines** at the meal, as per his custom, **and eats; and he eats meat and drinks wine; and he recites blessings** to exempt others from their obligation; **and** he recites the formula to **invite** the participants in the meal to join together in the Grace after Meals, **and others may recite blessings on his behalf and invite** him to join in the Grace after Meals. **And he is** also **obligated in the recitation of *Shema*, and in** the *Amida* **prayer, and in** the mitzva of **phylacteries, and in all the mitzvot mentioned in the Torah. Rabban Gamliel says: Since he is obligated** to fulfill **these** mitzvot associated with Shabbat, **he is obligated** to fulfill **all** of the mitzvot on Shabbat.

On *Shabbat*, when burial is impossible and therefore the *onein's* entire focus on attending to the *meit* is suspended, the picture changes dramatically. *Meisav v'ochel* — he may recline; *ochel basar v'shotheh yayin* — he may eat meat and drink wine; *u'mevarech u'mezamein u'mevarchin u'mezamnin alav* — he recites *berachot* and participates in *zimun* and others include him; *v'chayav bikrias shema u'vitfillah u'vitfillin u'vchol mitzot ha'amurot batorah* — he is obligated in all *mitzvot* of the Torah. (The inclusion of *tefillin* in this list is noted, since one does not wear *tefillin* on *Shabbat* anyway; it is included for completeness of the formula.)

### Sex on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תַּשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Gamliel's statement is vague. The following clarifies it: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The practical difference **between them,** the opinion of the anonymous first *tanna* and Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion, is with regard to **sexual relations.** According to Rabban Gamliel, the acute mourner is obligated in the mitzva to engage in marital intercourse with his wife on Shabbat, just as he is obligated in all the other mitzvot.

Rabban Gamliel adds: *mitoch shenischayev be'elu, nischayev b'chulan* — since the *onein* has become obligated in all those practices on *Shabbat*, he is obligated in all of them. Rabbi Yochanan clarifies what this adds: *tashmish hamitah ika beiniyahu* — the point of dispute between Rabban Gamliel and the earlier formulation is whether the *onein* on *Shabbat* is permitted to have marital relations. This is something that was not mentioned in the earlier enumeration of permissions on *Shabbat*, and Rabban Gamliel holds that once one is *chayav* in all the other practices, one is *chayav* in everything — including *tashmish hamitah*.

This is a striking *gemara*. The *onein* who cannot have marital relations on a weekday — we had not actually stated this about an *onein* explicitly, only about an *avel* — now on *Shabbat* is being debated as to whether it is permitted. The inference from *onein* to *avel* and back is operating here in complex ways.

### Continuity Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: יֵשׁ אֲבֵילוּת בְּשַׁבָּת, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין אֲבֵילוּת בְּשַׁבָּת! מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא הָתָם, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּמֵתוֹ מוּטָּל לְפָנָיו. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאֵין מֵתוֹ מוּטָּל לְפָנָיו — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What, is it not with regard to this** issue that **they disagree:** Is it not **that** one **Sage,** the anonymous first *tanna*, **holds** that **there is** some **mourning on Shabbat** with regard to private issues, and therefore the mourner does not engage in sexual relations; **and** one **Sage,** Rabban Gamliel, **holds** that **there is no mourning on Shabbat** at all? The Gemara rejects this argument: **From where** do you reach this conclusion? **Perhaps the first *tanna*** is **saying** that it is prohibited for the grieving relative to engage in sexual relations in the case dealt with **only there, because his deceased** relative **is laid out before him** and has not yet been buried. **But here,** with regard to the period of mourning, when **his dead** has been buried and **is no** longer **laid out before him,** sexual relations are **not** prohibited.

The *gemara* then asks: *leima ki-tanna'ei* — is this debate between *Bnei Yehuda* and *Bnei Galil* about *aveilus b'Shabbat* actually rooted in this earlier *tannaic* dispute about the *onein* on *Shabbat*? The comparison hinges on whether *aninus* and *aveilus* are the same kind of thing: if one holds *yesh aveilus b'Shabbat*, one would hold the *onein* cannot have relations on *Shabbat* either; if one holds *ein aveilus b'Shabbat*, one would permit it. The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps these are independent questions. Perhaps *aninus* is simply more severe than *aveilus* — either because it is a more intense form of mourning, or because the circumstance of *meiso mutal lefanav* adds its own restrictions — such that even one who holds *ein aveilus b'Shabbat* might hold that *aninus* restricts relations on *Shabbat*. Or conversely, Rabban Gamliel's leniency on *Shabbat* might be specifically because the person is not yet an *avel* — *aninus* is not *aveilus*, and the prohibition on relations might only be a *halacha* of *aveilus* proper, not of *aninus* at all. In that case, one who holds *yesh aveilus b'Shabbat* might still permit relations for an *onein* on *Shabbat* since the *avel's* prohibition has not yet begun.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָתָם, דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא חָל אֲבֵילוּת עֲלֵיהּ. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּחָל אֲבֵילוּת עֲלֵיהּ — הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** alternatively, perhaps **Rabban Gamliel is saying** that the grieving relative is permitted to engage in sexual relations **only there, where** the *halakhot* of **mourning have not** yet **taken effect,** as mourning begins only after the burial. **But here, where** the *halakhot* of **mourning** have already **taken effect,** he may **also** prohibit sexual relations.

In these closing lines, the *gemara* — without stating a definitive conclusion — is interrogating the very question that the large Tosafot here makes its central concern: are *aninus* and *aveilus* fundamentally distinct states with no intrinsic relationship, or are they essentially one continuous state with situationally triggered modifications? The *gemara's* willingness to draw inferences in both directions, and then to question those inferences, reflects the genuine conceptual difficulty. The answer to the final question — whether an *onein* on *Shabbat* is permitted in *tashmish hamitah* — remains to be concluded in the next *daf*.
