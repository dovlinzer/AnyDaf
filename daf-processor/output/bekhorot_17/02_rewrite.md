# Bekhorot 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jew Rights & Bechor

### Sharecropping Setup

The daf begins at the bottom of 16b, continuing the discussion of a sharecropping arrangement involving the flock of a non-Jew. The scenario is essentially this: a Jew accepts a flock of sheep from a non-Jew under an arrangement in which the non-Jew retains rights in the principal. Because the non-Jew has rights in the principal animals, he may also seize the offspring to make up the value of the principal should any animals die. This right of seizure is what generates the exemption from *bechor*: since the offspring are, in a meaningful sense, available to the non-Jew, they cannot be fully consecrated as *bechor*.

### He'emid v'ladot

The Mishnah then presents a further case: *he'emid v'ladoteihem tachat imoteihem* — he designated the children in the place of the mothers. According to Rashi, this means the owner explicitly told the non-Jew that the children could be used to make up the difference; according to Tosafot, it means the original mother animals actually died and the children were substituted for them. Either way, the children now take on the primary status as if the non-Jew owns them, and this extends the non-Jew's rights one generation further. The Mishnah concludes: *v'ladei v'ladot paturin* — the grandchildren are exempt. In other words, when the children formally substitute for the mothers, the grandchildren of the original flock become the new exempt generation, and only the great-grandchildren would be obligated.

### Rav Huna Position

Rav Huna reads the Mishnah in its simplest sense. In the ordinary case, without the special designation, only the children (*v'ladot*) are exempt — the grandchildren (*v'ladei v'ladot*) are obligated. The *he'emid* case in the Mishnah introduces a one-generation extension, nothing more.

### Rav Yehuda Position

Rav Yehuda reads the Mishnah more expansively. He holds that even in the ordinary case, without *he'emid*, the grandchildren are also exempt; it is only the great-grandchildren who are obligated. His reasoning is that the non-Jew has inherently broader rights to seize future animals than Rav Huna acknowledges, extending naturally into the second generation.

### Rejection of Rav Yehuda

The Gemara attempts to refute Rav Yehuda's reading from the Mishnah itself. The inference is straightforward: the Mishnah says *ta'ama de-he'emid* — only because he designated the children as substitutes are the grandchildren exempt. *Ha lo he'emid* — without that designation — it would not be so; only the children would be exempt, not the grandchildren. This is a direct *tiyuvta* of Rav Yehuda.

Rav Yehuda responds with a difficult re-reading. He argues that in truth his position always held — even without *he'emid*, the grandchildren are exempt. The reason the Mishnah chose to state the case of *he'emid* is to teach something that might otherwise be missed: one might have thought that since a non-Jew's customary practice (*orchei d'ovdei kochavim*) is to seize offspring beyond what he is strictly entitled to, perhaps in the case of *he'emid* the non-Jew's rights extend yet further — to three or four generations. The Mishnah therefore informs us that even with *he'emid*, his rights do not exceed two generations, just as without *he'emid*. In Rav Yehuda's reading, the Mishnah is not drawing a contrast between *he'emid* and the ordinary case; it is drawing a ceiling on *he'emid* itself. The Gemara acknowledges this is a challenging re-read of the Mishnah, though it does not finally resolve the dispute.

The Gemara also cites a *braita*: *ha-mekabel tzon barzel min ha-akum, v'ladot paturin, v'ladei v'ladot lo* — in the *tzon barzel* arrangement with a non-Jew, the children are exempt but the grandchildren are not. This appears to be a direct contradiction of Rav Yehuda. Rav Yehuda responds by reinterpreting the word *v'ladot*: read it as *hein u-vladoteihen* — they and their children — meaning the children and their children are exempt. The Gemara records an alternative text that explicitly says *hein u-vladoteihen patur*, which would then instead challenge Rav Huna. Rav Huna reads that formulation as simply restating that the children (*hein*) are exempt and the grandchildren (*v'ladei v'ladot*) are obligated. The Gemara concludes without full resolution, though Rav Huna's reading remains the more straightforward understanding of the Mishnah. The operative principle accepted by all is that whatever generation the non-Jew retains the right to seize — that generation is exempt even in its capacity as future mothers.

### Ten Generations View

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, at the end of the Mishnah, declares: *afilu ad asara doros pturin* — even up to ten generations are exempt, because the non-Jew can always seize future offspring to make up the difference.

The Gemara observes that this position fits naturally with Rav Yehuda's reading: since Rav Yehuda already holds that the *tanna kamma* counts generations and reaches the second, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's contribution is to push against that limit and say it goes on indefinitely. There is a meaningful progression from two to ad infinitum that requires explicit statement.

But according to Rav Huna, who holds that the *tanna kamma* never counted generations at all — only said the children are exempt — what force does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's statement carry? Why must he say *ten generations* rather than simply say *it extends into the generations at all*?

The Gemara formulates this as a question on Rav Huna: *mai afilu ad asara doros* — once the first position does not even speak in terms of generations, a response saying it goes *into the generations* should be sufficient; why enumerate ten?

Rav Huna responds: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is specifically addressing the case of *he'emid*. Even Rav Huna concedes that in the *he'emid* scenario, the Mishnah does speak in terms of an additional generation — the *tanna kamma* there reaches the grandchildren. It is against that backdrop that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel needs to say not just *it goes further* but *it goes all the way to ten* — because once we are counting generations, there is a range of possible limits, and he must be explicit that there is no limit.

## Nidmeh Exemption

### Rabbi Meir Baraita

The Gemara now turns to the question of the *nidmeh* — an animal that does not resemble its mother. The earlier teaching established that if a sheep gives birth to something that looks like a goat, or a goat to something that looks like a sheep, it is exempt from *bechor*. Rav Hoshaya from Nehardea arrived with a *braita* in hand: *rachel bat ez v'ez bat rachel* — a sheep born of a goat or a goat born of a sheep — *Rabbi Meir mechayev v'chachamim potrin*. Rabbi Meir holds it is obligated; the Chachamim exempt it. This appears to invert our Mishnah's ruling, and the question immediately arises: in what category of law is Rabbi Meir declaring an obligation?

### Hoshaya Transmission

Rav Hoshaya posed the question to Rabba: when you go before Rav Huna, ask him — *Rabbi Meir mechayev l'mai* — to what law is Rabbi Meir's ruling of *chayav* addressed?

If it refers to *bechora*, this is difficult — does Rabbi Meir not accept the principle that the verse *ach bechor shor* requires that both the mother and the firstborn offspring must be an ox, i.e., they must resemble one another? The Gemara takes it as axiomatic that no one disputes this principle for *bechor*.

Could it then refer to *reishit hagez* — the obligation to give the first shearing of wool to the *kohen*? Here too there is a problem: the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught that sheep with hard wool — the kind that does not retain warmth — are exempt from *reishit hagez*, based on the verse *u-migez kvasai yitchamam*, "from the shearing of my sheep he shall be warmed." A goat born of a sheep would have goat-like hair rather than proper wool, and would thus be exempt for that independent reason, quite apart from the *nidmeh* question.

Rabba then proposes that rather than sending the question to Rav Huna, they reason it through themselves — *nechzei anan*. He suggests a specific case: a sheep that gave birth to something resembling a goat, where the father was also a goat (*v'aviv tayish*). This is not a pure *nidmeh* case alone; it introduces the question of mixed parentage, a *kilayim*-adjacent concern.

### Father's Lineage Issue

The Gemara explains that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim concerns the halacha of *oto v'et bno* — the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its parent on the same day. And the underlying question is *choshshim l'zera ha-av* — do we give legal weight to the father's lineage in the animal world?

To clarify the term: *choshshim l'zera ha-av* does not mean we are uncertain who the biological father is. It means we are asking whether the father's biological relationship to the offspring carries any halachic significance in the animal world, where there is no family structure in the way there is for humans. Rabbi Meir holds *choshshim l'zera ha-av* — we do recognize the father's relationship. Therefore, since the father was a goat and the child resembles a goat, the child is connected to the father for purposes of *oto v'et bno*. The Chachamim hold *ein choshshim l'zera ha-av* — the father's lineage is irrelevant, and even in this case, the connection through the mother's species governs.

The Gemara then questions whether this is the right context for this debate: if Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim are really disagreeing about *choshshim l'zera ha-av*, why formulate the dispute specifically in the case of *nidmeh*? Let them argue it in the broader debate already conducted between Chananya and the Rabbis on that very question. The Gemara takes this objection as evidence that *nidmeh* is not the vehicle for the *choshshim l'zera ha-av* argument after all.

The Gemara therefore returns to the possibility that the dispute is indeed about *bechora*. The difficulty returns: how could Rabbi Meir say *chayav* for *bechor* when the child does not resemble the mother? The Gemara now offers a new case: *berachel bat rachel bat ez* — a sheep, whose mother was a sheep, whose grandmother was a goat. The child looks like the mother; but the mother herself was a *nidmeh*. The question thus becomes: is the *nidmeh* exemption a narrow halachic rule requiring only that the child resemble its immediate mother? Or is *nidmeh* a categorical label, such that once an animal is stamped as *nidmeh*, all its descendants carry that label indefinitely?

## Purity of Lineage

### Multi-Gen Definition

This question — whether *nidmeh* is a label attached to a lineage or simply a rule about resemblance to the immediate mother — opens into a broader discussion about how *nidmeh* is defined across generations.

One view holds *zil basar imei* — go after the mother. If the animal looks like its mother, that is sufficient; it is not a *nidmeh*, regardless of what the grandmother looked like. On this reading, *nidmeh* is not a permanent categorical designation.

The opposing view holds *zil basar imei de'imei* — you must trace back to the grandmother. If the grandmother was off-species, the mother was technically a *nidmeh*, and the grandchild inherits that status even if it now resembles its immediate mother.

A third formulation presents the inverse: *berachel bat ez bat rachel* — the sequence runs sheep, goat, sheep. The grandchild looks like a sheep, but its mother was a goat. On the one hand, the child does not resemble its mother and would seem to be a *nidmeh*. On the other hand, one could argue that the sheep-identity has reasserted itself — *chazra seyusa limkoman* — returning to the original species-line. Against this, the counter-view holds *lo chazra seyusa limkoman*: the genetic line, so to speak, remains tainted, and the child is still halachically classified as a *nidmeh* because it does not resemble its immediate mother, even if it has reverted to the appearance of its ancestral species.

## Purity of Lineage (II)

### Korban Chovah Requirement

Rav Yochanan now declares: *modeh Rabbi Meir b'se'ir shel Rosh Chodesh* — even Rabbi Meir concedes that the goat brought as a communal *chatat*, the *musaf* offering, requires that the animal be *ben se'ira*, born of a goat through a pure line. The verse says *v'se'ir izim echad l'chatat* — Rashi notes that *Rosh Chodesh* here is not to be taken literally; it refers to the goat of the *musafim* generally. The word *echad* is read as *hameyuchad uva misheshes yemei bereishit* — pure and distinct going back to the days of creation, with no interruption in the line. Even if at some point an ancestor merely *looked* like another species — not even a genuine crossbreed, just a *nidmeh* — the line is considered tainted and the animal is disqualified.

### Kilayim Exclusion

The Gemara then raises the question: is this requirement derivable from elsewhere? The verse states *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled* — an ox, sheep, or goat when it is born. From this formulation we learn: *shor* — *perat l'kilayim*, to exclude a genuine crossbreed; *o ez* — *perat l'nidmeh*, to exclude even an animal that merely resembled the other species. And crucially, this applies not only to the animal itself but to any ancestor that was a *nidmeh* in the line.

The Gemara answers *tzericha* — both sources are necessary. Without the second verse, one might have thought the concern applies only when the animal itself is a *nidmeh*, not when it is a *nidmeh* several generations removed. Without the first verse, one might have thought the purity-of-line requirement applies only to communal obligatory offerings (*korban tzibur chova*) where standards are strictest, but not to free-will offerings (*nedavah*). Together, both sources are required to establish that across all categories of *korbanos*, neither a *nidmeh* animal nor one with a *nidmeh* ancestor anywhere in its line may be brought.

### Tzemmer Purity

The Gemara extends this logic to *shatnez*. The position is stated: *hakol modim she'ein lokin al tzemmer mishum kilayim* — universally, one is not liable for *kilayim* with wool that is impure in this sense. The verse *lo tilbash shatnez, tzemmer u-fishtim yachdav* — do not wear a mixture of wool and linen — is read with an analogy: *mah pishtan shelo nishtanah* — just as flax never undergoes this kind of change (the reason for which the Gemara will return to), *af tzemmer shelo nishtanah* — so too the wool must be of an unaltered line. Wool from an animal whose lineage was interrupted by a *nidmeh* at any stage does not qualify as halachic wool for the purposes of *shatnez*.

### Tzaraat Immunity

This same principle is then applied to *tcheiles* for *tzitzit* and *bigdei kehunah*. Rav Pappa declares: *hakol modim she-tzemmer pasul l'tcheiles* — universally, wool of a tainted line is unfit for *tcheiles*. The source is the juxtaposition of the verses *lo tilbash shatnez* and *gedilim ta'aseh lach* — do not wear *shatnez*, make for yourself fringes. Since *tzitzit* with *tcheiles* is placed adjacent to the *shatnez* prohibition, the wool requirement for *tcheiles* is bound by the same purity standard: *mah pishtan shelo nishtanah, af tzemmer shelo nishtanah*.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then states: *hakol modim she-ein tzemmer mitamei b'nega'im* — universally, such wool does not contract *tum'ah* from *nega'im*. The verse reads *b'veged tzemmer o b'veged pishtim* — in a garment of wool or linen. Once again: *mah pishtim shelo nishtanah, af tzemmer shelo nishtanah* — just as linen is pure and unaltered, the wool must likewise be pure to be susceptible to *tum'at nega'im*.

### Wine Purity

Rav Ashi joins the series: *af anu nomar* — let us also say the following. If one trained a grapevine to grow over a fig tree (*hidla hagefen al gabei te'enah*), the resulting grapes are presumably affected in flavor. Rav Ashi rules that *yeino pasul l'nesachim* — the wine from such grapes is disqualified for the *nesachim*, the wine libation accompanying *korbanos* on the *mizbe'ach*. The source is the verse *zevach u-nesachim* — the sacrifice and the wine offering are compared. Just as *mah zevach shelo nishtanah* — the animal must be of an unaltered line — so too *af nesachim shelo nishtanah* — the wine must be pure and unaffected.

### Ravina Objection

Ravina challenges Rav Ashi with a pointed objection: *hidla pishtan al gabei higah* — what if one trained flax around a thorn bush? Would that also mean the flax is no longer pure flax? If so, then *lo matzinu mah pishtan shelo nishtanah* — the very premise of all the foregoing analogies collapses. All the previous *drashos* — for *shatnez*, *tcheiles*, *tzaraat* — rested on the claim that flax is inherently unchangeable. If plant products can be altered by how they are grown, then flax is not a reliable baseline of purity, and the entire series of analogies breaks down.

Ravina's challenge is, in essence: you cannot use an admittedly alterable medium — plant products — as the anchor of a purity argument.

### Plant Nature Distinction

The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing between different plants by their nature. *Amar lei zeh nishtaneh reicho v'zeh lo nishtaneh reicho* — this one, the grape, has its smell and taste affected; that one, flax, does not. Flax, by its intrinsic nature, cannot be affected by environmental manipulation short of an actual crossbreed. Its identity remains fixed regardless of what it is grown near or around. Therefore flax remains a valid baseline of purity, and all the preceding analogies hold.

Grapes, by contrast, are genuinely susceptible to the influence of what they grow alongside — as one can observe in the way wine takes on characteristics of the flowers or plants near which grapes are cultivated. It is therefore entirely coherent to say that a vine grown around a fig tree produces wine that has been altered in identity, rendering it unfit for *nesachim*, without undermining the foundational assumption that flax is invariably pure.

This entire sugya, it is worth noting, reflects something conceptually striking. The Gemara is operating with a category of *nidmeh* that functions beyond mere physical resemblance. Even if the animal is biologically a sheep, even if it functions in every practical respect as a sheep — if somewhere in the ancestral line an animal merely *appeared* different, the line is considered halachically tainted for *Mikdash* purposes. Whether the Gemara views *nidmeh* as a genuine biological deviation or purely a matter of appearance is left somewhat open; but the purity-of-line requirement for *korbanos*, *shatnez*, *tcheiles*, and *nega'im* all proceed from this premise.

## Nidmeh Exemption (II)

### Rav Ashi's Answer

Rav Ashi offers a simpler resolution to the original dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim regarding the *nidmeh* case. Rather than invoking multi-generational lineage questions or *choshshim l'zera ha'av*, he proposes that the case in the *braita* is simply one in which the animal in question bears *miktzat simanim* — some characteristics of the mother's species and some of the other. The animal partially resembles the mother.

On this reading, Rabbi Meir holds it is obligated because partial resemblance to the mother suffices. The Chachamim who exempt it are identified as Rabbi Shimon, who elsewhere required that *rosho v'rubo domeh l'immo* — the head and the majority of the animal must resemble the mother. A few resembling features is not enough for Rabbi Shimon; Rabbi Meir requires less. The debate, in Rav Ashi's framing, is straightforwardly about the threshold of resemblance required, not about lineage across generations or paternal identity. The Gemara accepts this as a viable resolution, noting that sometimes the same pair of positions — Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon — appear with one framed as the *stam* majority view and the other as the dissent, and sometimes with those roles reversed.

## Twins Simultaneity

### Twin Scenario

The Mishnah presents a new case: *rachel shelo bikhrah v'yalda shnei zacharim* — a sheep that has never previously given birth delivers two male twins simultaneously — *veyatze'u shnei rasheihem ke'echad*, their two heads emerging as one. Both are firstborns in the sense that neither preceded the other. Who receives them?

Rabbi Yose HaGlili rules: *shneihem la-kohen* — the *kohen* receives both. The verse says *ha-zacharim la-Shem* — the males are for God — and since it is possible for both to be *bechor* simultaneously, both are obligated.

The Chachamim rule: *iy efshar l'tzamtzem* — it is impossible to achieve or confirm such precision. Even if the births appeared simultaneous, one must have preceded the other, even if imperceptibly. Since we cannot determine which was first, the *kohen* cannot claim both. The Mishnah continues: one goes to the *kohen* and one is retained by the owner. Rabbi Tarfon says the *kohen* chooses the finer of the two. Rabbi Akiva says the value difference between the two is estimated and the *kohen* receives half that difference in addition to the lesser animal — effectively splitting the premium between them. The second animal must be left to graze until it acquires a *mum*, a disqualifying blemish, at which point it may be eaten as ordinary meat, since neither animal can be brought as a *korban* — doing so would risk bringing *chullin la-azara* — and neither can be formally consecrated.

### Yose HaGlili Position

Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position is that *efshar l'tzamtzem b'yedei shamayim* — precision is possible even in a natural event such as twin births. And *v'chol shekein b'yedei adam* — certainly when a human being constructs or controls the scenario. In natural events, the world itself can produce exact simultaneity, and halachically we may credit that. When a human agent is involved, there is even greater reason to accept precision.

### Chachamim Position

The Chachamim maintain the inverse. *B'yedei shamayim iy efshar l'tzamtzem* — in natural events, precision is impossible; we cannot trust the appearance of simultaneity and must assume one preceded the other. But a question arises: what do the Chachamim say about cases *b'yedei adam* — human-constructed or human-measured scenarios? Do they concede precision there?

Two distinct meanings of *b'yedei adam* are worth noting. One reading focuses on who constructed the scenario: if you deliberately engineered the situation to be simultaneous or equidistant, perhaps that earns halachic credit as precise. The other reading focuses on measurement opportunity: even if the event happened naturally, if you had the time and instruments to measure, perhaps that is sufficient. As will become apparent from the proofs brought below, it is the latter understanding — the availability of measurement, not the construction of the scenario — that the Gemara ultimately finds more compelling.

### Altar Line Proof

The Gemara attempts to bring evidence from the *mizbe'ach*. The altar in the *Beit HaMikdash* was girded at its midpoint with a red line (*chut shel sikra*) to divide the upper half, where certain sacrificial blood was applied, from the lower half, where other blood was applied. This line was drawn to demarcate the midpoint precisely. But if even human construction and measurement cannot be trusted, the line is useless — one might apply the blood of an upper-half *korban* just above the line, only to discover that due to imprecision the line was placed too high, and the blood was actually on the lower half.

The Gemara deflects: *d'marvach lei porta* — the line was made slightly thick, providing a buffer zone. Total precision is not claimed; the line's thickness itself creates the necessary margin. This answer does not establish that precision is achievable; it only shows that the system was designed to tolerate some imprecision.

### Best Effort Standard

The Gemara then raises a more fundamental challenge from the *shiurim* of the *Mikdash* vessels — the dimensions of the *shulchan*, the *menorah*, the *mizbe'ach* itself. The Torah specifies exact measurements. How can we ever say these requirements were met, if precise measurement is inherently unattainable?

The Gemara answers: *shani hatam d'rachmana amar avid, u-v'chol d'matzi l'me'avad nicha lei* — that case is different, because the Torah said *make it*, and by that it implicitly means: do the best you can. Whatever one is able to construct that matches the specified dimensions as faithfully as possible counts as having fulfilled the requirement. The accompanying verse — *ha-kol bi-chtav miyad Hashem alai hiskil*, all was revealed to me by the hand of God — suggests Divine communication of the standards in a manner compatible with human execution.

This answer is suggestive. One can read it as Rabbi Yose HaGlili's underlying position writ large: halacha does not demand scientific or metaphysical exactitude; it demands the best human effort. If to all observation and measurement the twins emerged simultaneously, halachically that counts as simultaneous. But the Gemara resists generalizing this principle, reserving it as a *davar she-tzarich* — applicable where the Torah explicitly commands the act, thereby implicitly accepting human-level execution, but not necessarily in other halachic contexts.

### Pottery Exception

Another attempted proof: if one breaks a pottery vessel into two pieces of apparently equal size, both pieces remain *tamei*, because we say *iy efshar l'tzamtzem* — you cannot be confident that the break was perfectly even. One piece might actually constitute the majority of the vessel, which would render it *tamei*, and the other the minority, which would be *tahor*. Since we cannot establish that either is truly the minority, both remain *tamei*. This is explicitly a *b'yedei adam* case — a human act — yet the Gemara rules *iy efshar l'tzamtzem*.

The Gemara responds: *shani kli cheres d'it bei gumot* — pottery is different because of its jagged edges. A pottery shard broken in two will have irregular, uneven fractures; no one could seriously maintain that such a break is perfectly equal. The case therefore does not demonstrate anything about situations where careful measurement is genuinely possible. It says nothing about human-made constructions that are controlled and measured in advance.

### Egla Arufa Case

The Gemara raises the case of *egla arufa*: if a slain body is found equidistant between two cities, Rabbi Eliezer rules that both cities bring a calf each. Now, if a body is found between two cities and the measurement comes out exactly equal, we could simply stipulate — if this city is closer, this is its calf; if that city is closer, that is its calf — and only one calf would need to be brought conditionally. The fact that Rabbi Eliezer requires *two* calves without qualification suggests he believes the body may genuinely be equidistant, and that each city is truly the closest, simultaneously. This implies *efshar l'tzamtzem b'yedei adam*.

The Gemara responds: this is the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds like Rabbi Yose HaGlili — *efshar l'tzamtzem* generally, both *b'yedei shamayim* and *b'yedei adam*. The question before the Gemara was what the Chachamim hold regarding *b'yedei adam*; this proof only tells us what Rabbi Eliezer holds, which is entirely consistent with his identification as a *tanna* who agrees with Rabbi Yose HaGlili.

### Measurement Opportunity

The final and most analytically productive observation concerns what makes the *egla arufa* case halachically classified as *b'yedei adam* at all. No human being placed the body there to be equidistant; the body was discovered where it fell. What makes the case *b'yedei adam* is not that a person constructed the scenario but that a person *had the opportunity to measure*. When investigators arrived at the scene, they could take out instruments and determine the distance. This is the meaningful distinction.

Accordingly, the Chachamim's position may be understood as follows: *b'yedei shamayim* — when an event occurs naturally and there is no opportunity for measured observation, such as the simultaneous birth of twins where you are a passive observer with no ability to instrument the measurement in real time — *iy efshar l'tzamtzem*, we do not credit apparent precision. But *b'yedei adam* — when you had the time and ability to measure — the question remains open: do the Chachamim say that even then, we cannot trust the measurement, or do they concede that a carefully measured equality is halachically credited as precise? The daf ends with this question unresolved, and the discussion will continue on the following day.
