# Bava Metzia 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Money vs Object

### Perek Overview

Daf 44 opens the fourth *perek* of Bava Metzia, *Perek Hazahav*, understood to be among the more challenging *perakim* in *Shas*. The *masechet* has been shifting in its thematic focus: after two *perakim* on *Metzia* and one ostensibly about *shomrim* — though notably without ever establishing the basic principles of *shomrim* — the tractate turns to matters of monetary transactions and *kinyanim*. The fifth *perek* will address lending and *ribbis*; this *perek* begins with the exchange of money for money, and more centrally, with the conceptual framework for what constitutes *kinyan* in purchasing transactions.

The topic of *kinyanim* was touched on in *Kiddushin*, where the question was which act — *hagbahah* or *meshichah* — effects acquisition on a given object. Here, however, the question is different: how does *halacha* conceptualize what counts as money and what counts as object within a given transaction?

### Sale Finality Principle

The foundational *halacha* is as follows: when one sells chattel — *metaltelin*, movable property as opposed to *karka* — the sale is finalized when the buyer takes physical possession of the object, whether by *hagbahah* (lifting it) or *meshichah* (drawing it toward oneself). Payment of money, by itself, does not finalize the sale. The rule is thus clear when dealing with an ordinary commercial transaction: we know what cash is, we know what an object is, and the acquisition is effected by taking hold of the object.

### Currency Exchange Problem

The difficulty arises when both items being exchanged are themselves currency. In the modern context, one might think of exchanging dollars for euros or dollars for shekels — but the *Gemara* is not addressing cross-national currency exchange. Rather, it is concerned with exchanges of different types of coins within a single monetary system. If one person holds gold coins and the other holds silver coins, and they are to be exchanged, which one is the *object* and which is the *money* for purposes of *kinyan*? One cannot say they are both money, because then the sale could never be finalized — *kinyan* applies to the object, not to the money. Yet calling them both objects would be anomalous, since at least one of them is functioning as currency. This is the question that opens the *perek*.

## Currency Hierarchy

### Gold/Silver Exchange

The first *mishna* states: *hazahav koneh es hakesef* — gold acquires silver. This requires a careful reversal of intuitive thinking. When we hear "gold buys silver," we might instinctively read *gold* as the buyer, i.e., the money. But that is not what the *mishna* means. The *mishna*'s logic is the opposite: the thing that is *koneh* is the object, and the thing it acquires is the money.

### Koneh Terminology

To understand the terminology: when the *mishna* says X is *koneh* Y, it means X is the object and Y is the money. The *kinyan* is effected by taking possession of X, and at that point Y — the money — is owed. So *hazahav koneh es hakesef* means that in an exchange of gold coins for silver coins, the gold is the object and the silver is the money. If one takes possession of the gold, the silver becomes legally owed. The reverse, however, is not true: *hakesef eino koneh es hazahav* — if one takes possession only of the silver, the transaction is not yet finalized.

Later in the same *mishna*, the statement *kol hametaltelin konin es hamatbeah* — all movable objects acquire currency — illustrates the paradigm case: chattel is the object, coins are the money. The phrasing may seem counterintuitive (we might say "coins buy cattle," not "cattle acquires coins"), but the *mishna*'s language consistently identifies the *koneh* as the object. It is also worth noting that when *metaltelin* are said to "acquire" the *matbeah*, this does not mean one literally owns the specific coins in the other party's pocket; rather, it means the transaction is finalized and the money becomes owed.

### Full Hierarchy Listed

The *mishna* then presents a full hierarchy of currency types. Gold coins are the object vis-à-vis silver — *hazahav koneh es hakesef*. Copper coins are the object vis-à-vis silver — *hanechoshet koneh es hakesef* — while silver is currency vis-à-vis copper. Bad money (*maot raot*) is the object vis-à-vis good money (*hayafot*). The term *maot raot* does not refer to counterfeit currency, which would be too obvious a case. Rather, as *Tosafot* explains, it refers to coins that are not universally accepted — perhaps because they are locally limited in circulation, or because the coins themselves are somewhat damaged and therefore rejected in certain transactions. The widely accepted, reliable currency is the money; the less-accepted is the object.

The *mishna* further addresses the *asimon* — an unminted coin, a blank that has not yet been stamped. Its precious metal content may be identical to a minted coin, but the mint's stamp is what certifies purity and weight and thus confers trustworthiness. The *asimon* is therefore the object vis-à-vis the minted *matbeah*: *asimon koneh es hamatbeah*. The minted coin, functioning as recognized currency, is the money.

### Metaltelin vs Matbeah

The general principle is stated explicitly: *hametaltelin konin es hamatbeah* — movable objects effect *kinyan* on coins — and *hamatbeah eino koneh metaltelin* — coins do not effect *kinyan* on movable objects. This is the paradigm: the object is the chattel, the money is the *matbeah*. One cannot have two moneys in a single transaction, for then no *kinyan* could ever be effected.

### Barter Principle

A different rule applies in barter. *Kol hametaltelin konim zeh es zeh* — when two objects are being exchanged for one another, both function as objects and either one can effect the *kinyan*. If one party takes possession of a cow and the other of a donkey, the transaction is finalized for both. In that scenario, we are not talking about a debt being created — one party literally acquires the concrete object the moment the other takes possession.

## Fruit Sale Example

### Drawing Without Paying

The *mishna* now turns to the paradigm case illustrating these principles: a sale of fruit. *Mashach himenu peirot velo nasan lo maos* — if the buyer drew the fruit toward himself, performing *meshichah*, but has not yet paid — *eino yachol lachzor bo* — he cannot retract. The *kinyan* is complete. The object has been taken into possession and the sale is final.

### Paying Without Taking

*Nasan lo maos velo mashach himenu peirot* — if the buyer paid the money but has not yet performed *meshichah* on the fruit — *yachol lachzor bo* — either party may retract. The payment of money alone does not finalize the sale of *metaltelin*.

### Mi Shepara Curse

This creates an ethical problem: if the buyer has paid and the seller can still retract, what prevents the seller from simply taking the money and refusing to deliver? The *mishna* addresses this with the institution of the *mi shepara*. The *beit din* pronounces: the One who exacted retribution from the people of the generation of the flood and the generation of the dispersion will exact retribution from one who does not stand by his word — *hu asid lipara mimi she'eino omed bediburo*. The invocation of *dor hamabul* and *dor hapalaga* is striking, and the *Gemara* will explore the connection further. For now, the point is that while there is no binding legal obligation to complete the transaction, withdrawing after payment has been made incurs this rabbinic censure — a form of curse from the *beit din*.

## Fruit Sale Example…

### Rabbi Shimon's Rule

*Rabbi Shimon* takes a distinct position: *kol hakesef beyado yado al ha'elyona* — whoever holds the money has the upper hand. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, the ability to retract is not symmetric. When money has been paid but the object not yet taken, it is specifically the one holding the money — the seller — who retains the option to reverse the transaction. The buyer, having relinquished the money, has surrendered his position of control. The practical implication is pointed: paying money without taking the object places all power in the other party's hands. The transaction is not finalized, but only the money-holder can dissolve it.

## Rebbi's Position Shift

### Youth vs Old Age

A fascinating *sugya* now emerges. The *Gemara* reports that *Rebbi* taught this *mishna* to his son *Rabbi Shimon* (not the *Rabbi Shimon* of the *mishna*, who is *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai*, but *Rebbi*'s own son). His son pointed out a contradiction: in his youth, *Rebbi* had taught *hakesef koneh es hazahav* — that silver is the object and gold is the money — but now in his old age, he was teaching *hazahav koneh es hakesef* — that gold is the object and silver is the money. The *Gemara* interjects to explain what had changed.

### Youth: Gold Standard

*Beyaldusei savar* — in his youth, *Rebbi* held: *dahava de-chashiv havei tiva* — gold, being the most prestigious and valuable, is the standard currency, the *tiva*. Silver, being of lesser standing, is considered *peira* — the fruit, the object. *Vekani lei peira letiva* — the object (*peira*) is what effects *kinyan* on the currency. In this view, everything is defined relative to gold. When the ratio of silver to gold shifts — say, from 25 silver coins per gold to 20 — one articulates this as "the price of silver went up," not "the price of gold went down," because gold is the fixed reference point. The concept is recognizable from the gold standard of monetary history: one defines the value of everything else in terms of gold, and fluctuations are attributed to the other metals.

### Age: Silver Standard

*Biziknusei savar* — in his old age, *Rebbi* changed his position: *kaspah d'charif havei tiva* — silver, being far more widespread in everyday use, is the true currency. Gold may be more valuable per unit, but very few people possess or transact in gold. The currency that everyone uses, in which prices are quoted and transactions are conducted, is silver. Therefore silver is the *tiva* and gold is the *peira*. *Dahava d'lo charif havei peira veka'rei leh peira* — gold, which is not widespread, is considered the object. On this view, our *mishna* — which says *hazahav koneh es hakesef* — reflects *Rebbi*'s mature opinion: gold is the object, silver is the currency.

It is worth noting that the *Yerushalmi*'s *girsa* of this *mishna* is the opposite: *hakesef koneh es hazahav*, giving this *perek* the name *Hakesef* rather than *Hazahav* — the only known instance in *Shas* where a *perek* carries a different name in the two *Talmuds* because of a variant first word in the *mishna*. Additionally, historical context is pertinent here: within the Roman Empire, silver was apparently the standard metal for coins, which would align with *Rebbi*'s older position.

### Value vs Widespread Use

*Rav Ashi* weighs in: *ki yalduso mistabra* — *Rebbi*'s position in his youth, that gold is the standard, is the more logical one. The proof comes from the end of the *mishna* itself, which states *hanechoshet koneh es hakesef* — copper is the object vis-à-vis silver. Now if the principle were that the more widespread currency is the *tiva*, then copper — which in many localities is used far more frequently than silver — ought to be considered the *tiva* vis-à-vis silver, not the *peira*. If *Rebbi*'s older position held that widespread usage determines the *tiva*, then the hierarchy *zahav, kesef, nechoshet* — with each lower metal being the object vis-à-vis the higher — would not follow logically, since nechoshet is more widely circulated than kesef in many communities.

The *Gemara* responds that nevertheless the *mishna* would need to state the copper-silver rule explicitly, because one might have thought: *salka daitach amina hani priti be-atra de-sagi inu charifi tfei* — in the locale where copper coins circulate, they may actually be more widespread than silver, and therefore one might have thought copper should be the *tiva* there. The *mishna* teaches: *keivan d'ika dukhta d'lo sagi bei, peira havei* — since there are places that do not accept copper at all, and silver always functions as the reference currency even where copper is the daily medium of exchange, copper retains the status of *peira*. The *tiva* is defined not merely by local prevalence but by universal acceptability as the reference standard. Silver meets that criterion; copper does not.

The upshot of *Rav Ashi*'s analysis is that the hierarchy proceeds by metal value, not by circulatory breadth. More valuable metal is the *tiva* vis-à-vis the less valuable, and the *mishna* as we have it — *hazahav koneh es hakesef* — reflects *Rebbi*'s younger position, which is the more coherent one.

## Gold Loan Case

### Dinarei Loan

The *Gemara* now cites a case directly bearing on the *tiva*-versus-*peira* question. *Rav* borrowed gold *dinarei* from *Rebbi Chiya*'s daughter — herself evidently a businesswoman of means. *L'sof ikru dinarei* — in the end, the gold coins appreciated in value. At the time of the loan, ten gold coins might have exchanged for 250 silver; now they were worth 300 silver. When it came time to repay, *Rav* was concerned about *ribbis*: would returning ten gold coins constitute paying back something of greater value than he had borrowed?

### Rebbi Chiya Ruling

*Rav* came before *Rebbi Chiya*, who ruled: *zil shlim la tavin v'takilin* — go pay her good, full-weight coins. That is, repay the same number of gold coins, and do so without concern.

### Gold Standard Solution

*Iy amart bishlama dahava tiva havei, shapir* — if gold is the *tiva*, the ruling is perfectly coherent. What has happened, on this view, is not that the gold became worth more, but that silver became worth less. Gold, as the standard currency, always retains the same value by definition — ten gold coins are always worth ten gold coins. The fluctuation is entirely attributed to silver. Therefore, returning ten gold coins to someone who lent ten gold coins is a straightforward repayment with no *ribbis* concern whatsoever.

### Silver Standard Problem

*Ela iy amart peira havei* — but if gold is the *peira*, the object, and silver is the fixed *tiva*, then what one borrowed was an object worth 250 silver, and what one is returning is an object now worth 300 silver. That constitutes repaying more than one borrowed — which is the very definition of *ribbis*.

### Se'ah B'se'ah

This brings into view the principle of *se'ah b'se'ah*, which will be developed more fully in the following *perek* but is essential to introduce here. Even when there is no actual change in price between the time of borrowing and the time of repayment, the rabbis prohibited borrowing an object with the intention of returning an identical object — the paradigmatic case being "lend me two eggs." The concern is that even if prices have not in fact changed, they might have, and therefore returning two eggs for two eggs risks returning something of greater value than what was borrowed. The prohibition is rabbinic and based on potential, not actual, gain. The value of a loan is always measured in monetary terms; one is not borrowing eggs, one is borrowing the money-equivalent of eggs at the time of the transaction.

In *Rav*'s case, the concern is not potential but actual — the gold coins are demonstrably worth more silver now than when he borrowed them. If silver is the *tiva*, he is unambiguously repaying more than he received, which is forbidden *ribbis*. The *Gemara* concludes that *Rebbi Chiya*'s ruling permitting repayment of the same gold coins proves that gold, not silver, is the *tiva* — and therefore no *ribbis* problem arises.

### Home Coins Solution

The *Gemara* offers an alternative explanation to avoid this conclusion. Perhaps gold is in fact the *peira* and silver the *tiva*, yet *Rav*'s case was exceptional: *Rav dinarei hava lei* — *Rav* actually had gold coins of his own at home. The principle is that if a borrower possesses the item at home but simply cannot retrieve it at the moment — analogous to saying *hav li ad she'yavo beni o ad she'emtza mafte'ach*, "lend me this until my son comes home or until I find the key" — the transaction is conceptualized not as a loan over time but as an immediate swap. Since the *se'ah b'se'ah* concern is rabbinic, this conceptual fiction suffices to resolve it: one is treating the coins already at home as immediately transferred, so there is no problematic loan of an object over time. On this reading, *Rebbi Chiya*'s ruling does not prove the gold standard; it merely reflects the special circumstances of *Rav*'s case. The question of which metal is the *tiva* therefore remains open from this episode alone.

## Currency Exchange Proofs

### Perutah Definition

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita* to press the question further. The *baraita* — drawn from *Mishnah Eduyot* — defines the *perutah* mentioned throughout rabbinic literature: *echad mi-shmoneh be-issar ha-italki* — one-eighth of an Italian *issar*. The stated relevance is *le-kiddushei ishah*: kiddushin takes effect with something worth at least a *perutah*, so knowing what a *perutah* is worth is of direct consequence for determining a woman's marital status.

### Issar-Dinar Ratio

The *baraita* continues: one Italian *issar* equals 1/24th of a silver *dinar*. The stated relevance is *le-mekach u-mimkar* — for commercial exchange. *Rashi* understands this as the prevention of fraud in money-changing: knowing the precise ratio of *issars* to silver *dinarim* ensures that neither party in a currency exchange is cheated. Just as there are fixed legal ratios of currency units — one hundred pennies to a dollar — the *issar*-to-*dinar* ratio establishes a standard against which exchange rates are measured.

### Silver-Gold Ratio

The *baraita* then states: one silver *dinar* equals 1/25th of a gold *dinar*. The relevance stated is *le-pidyon haben*: *pidyon haben* requires the equivalent of five *shekalim*, and a *shekel* is four silver *dinarim*, making the total twenty silver *dinarim*. If a gold *dinar* is worth twenty-five silver *dinarim*, then 4/5ths of a gold *dinar* suffices for *pidyon haben*, with the *Kohen* returning the remaining fifth.

### Pidyon Haben Fixed

*Iy amart bishlama tiva havei* — if gold is the standard currency, this all works cleanly. The amount required for *pidyon haben* is defined as a fixed fraction of a gold coin. No matter how the price of silver fluctuates, the *pidyon haben* obligation is stable: it is always 4/5ths of a gold *dinar*. Everything else — silver *dinarim*, *issars*, *perutot* — is defined relative to gold, and gold itself is the fixed reference point. This is coherent precisely because gold is the *tiva*.

### Silver Standard Problem

*Ela iy amart peira havei* — but if gold is the *peira* and its value fluctuates relative to the silver standard, then fixing *pidyon haben* as "4/5ths of a gold coin" becomes meaningless as a stable measure. *Zimnin demahadar lei kahana vezimnin demosif la-hu la-kahana* — sometimes the *Kohen* would have to return more than a fifth of the gold coin's silver-equivalent value, and sometimes less, depending on the current silver price of gold. A fixed *mitzvah*-obligation cannot be expressed in terms of something whose value is in constant flux. The fact that the *baraita* defines *pidyon haben* in terms of a fixed fraction of the gold *dinar* therefore implies that gold is the constant — the *tiva* — and everything else fluctuates around it.

### Ulla's Counter

*Ulla* raises a counterpoint. If gold's status as *tiva* is proven by the *pidyon haben* clause, what about the beginning of the same *baraita*, which defines the *perutah* in terms of the *issar* and the *issar* in terms of the silver *dinar*? The *perutah* is relevant for *kiddushei ishah*. If one wanted to prove that silver — not gold — is the fixed standard, one could equally argue from the fact that the *perutah* is defined relative to silver: it is 1/192nd of a silver *dinar*, and a woman is *mekudeshet* if she receives at least that amount. Does this not suggest that silver is the reference point for determining minimum value?

### Tosafot Resolution

*Tosafot* offers a resolution. The *baraita* presents an ascending chain of definitions: *perutah* → *issar* → silver *dinar* → gold *dinar*. This ascending structure means that everything is ultimately defined in terms of the highest denomination — the gold *dinar*. The *perutah* is not simply 1/192nd of a silver *dinar*; it is ultimately a fixed fraction of a gold *dinar*, mediated through the intermediate steps. Therefore, to determine whether a given *perutah* coin is genuinely worth a *perutah* for purposes of *kiddushin*, one does not simply compare it to silver; one traces the chain all the way up to gold. If the coin is worth at least 1/5000th of a gold *dinar* (approximately — combining the 1/8 issar ratio with the 1/24 silver-dinar ratio and the 1/25 gold-dinar ratio), it is a valid *perutah* and the *kiddushin* is effective. The gold standard is the ultimate reference for all valuations up and down the chain.

### Historical Ratio Change

*Tosafot* appends a historical note from Rabbeinu Tam. The *baraita*'s statement that one gold *dinar* equals 25 silver *dinarim* troubled him in light of contemporary reality: *kashya le-Rabbeinu Tam eich haya beyamav shehaya zahav yakar kol kach* — how could it be that in antiquity, one unit of gold was worth 25 units of silver of the same weight, when in his own medieval time, one unit of gold was worth at most 12 units of silver? A shift from a 1:25 ratio to a 1:12 ratio seemed implausibly dramatic. Rabbeinu Tam's resolution is that the gold *dinar* was not the same size as the silver *dinar* — gold being denser, and the gold coin perhaps also thicker — such that one gold *dinar* actually contained more weight than one silver *dinar*. Therefore the 1:25 ratio in terms of coin-count need not reflect a 1:25 ratio in terms of weight, and the discrepancy with medieval gold-to-silver ratios is smaller than it appears.

## Ma'aser Sheni Dispute

### Ma'aser Transfer Q

The *Gemara* now turns to a *mishna* elsewhere that bears directly on the *tiva*-versus-*peira* question: the laws of *ma'aser sheni*. The *halacha* of *ma'aser sheni* permits transferring the *kedushah* of the second tithe from produce to coins (*mechallel* onto money), which allows one to travel to Jerusalem more easily — one brings the coins and purchases food there. The question is whether one may take a further step: transferring *kedushah* already on silver coins onto gold coins, which are even more portable. This is precisely the gold-versus-silver debate of our *perek*.

### Beit Shammai View

*Beit Shammai* says: *lo ya'aseh adam sla'im dinarei zahav* — one may not convert silver *sela'im* into gold *dinarim* for *ma'aser sheni* purposes. Their reasoning, as explained by the *Gemara*, is that silver is the *tiva* and gold is the *peira*. The rule for *ma'aser sheni* is that one may transfer *kedushah* from *peira* (fruit/object) to *tiva* (currency), but not from *tiva* to *peira*. Since silver is the *tiva* and gold is the *peira*, moving *kedushah* from silver to gold would constitute transferring from *tiva* to *peira*, which is forbidden.

### Beit Hillel View

*Beit Hillel* permits it: *matirin*. They hold the opposite: *kaspa peira ve-dahava tiva* — silver is the *peira* and gold is the *tiva*. Moving *kedushah* from silver to gold is therefore moving from *peira* to *tiva*, which is permitted. This is the position of *Rebbi* in his youth and of *Rebbi Chiya*, as established earlier in the *sugya*.

### Kedusha Transfer Rule

The underlying rule is thus: *mechallelin* from *peira* to *tiva*, but not from *tiva* to *peira*. One may always transfer *kedushah* from the object/fruit to the currency, never the reverse. The machlokes between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* is therefore directly and entirely a consequence of which metal each house regards as the *tiva* — and that is precisely the dispute of *Rebbi* in his youth versus his old age.

### Fruit to Coins Fine

On one position of *Rabbi Yochanan* or *Reish Lakish* (they dispute which formulation is correct), the machlokes between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* is specifically about the silver-to-gold transfer. But transferring *kedushah* directly from actual produce (*peirot*) to gold *dinarim* — bypassing silver — would be permitted according to all opinions. *Divrei hakol mechalelin* — there is no dispute that one may go from real fruit to gold, because gold is unquestionably currency relative to actual agricultural produce. *Midi de-hava a-kessef le-Veit Hillel*: just as *Beit Hillel* permits silver to be the object of *challul* from fruit even though silver is considered *peira* vis-à-vis gold, so too *Beit Shammai* would agree that from real *peirot*, even gold coins — despite being *peira* vis-à-vis silver — are nonetheless *tiva* relative to actual produce. The question of which is *tiva* and which is *peira* is always relative and contextual.

### Fruit-Gold Transfer

The second position of *Rabbi Yochanan* or *Reish Lakish*, however, disputes even this. On this reading, the machlokes between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* extends even to whether one may transfer *kedushah* directly from produce to gold *dinarim*. The argument would be that gold is so removed from everyday commerce — more like a commodity or a collectible than a true circulating currency — that even relative to actual *peirot*, gold might not qualify as *tiva*. One might think of the modern analogy of gold Krugerrands: theoretically a currency, but in practice purchased only at specialized dealers and not used in everyday transactions. If that is how gold functions, one could argue that even vis-à-vis produce, gold is the *peira* and the transfer of *kedushah* from produce to gold coins would itself be disputed between the two houses. The *Gemara* leaves this question open, to be resumed in the following *daf*.
