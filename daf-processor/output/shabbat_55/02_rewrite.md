# Shabbat 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Animal Leash Laws

### Leash Requirements

Before turning to the substance of today's daf, which is largely *agadati* in character, it is worth concluding the practical discussion of animal transport from the previous session by examining the relevant passage in *Shemiras Shabbos Kehilchasa*. In section 7, the author states: *v'inyan shevisas beheima hu sheba'al hachayim yidag kach shelo tetzei habeheima l'reshus harabim v'lo l'karmelis b'sha'as shemasa al gabeha* — the obligation of *shevisas behemto* requires the animal's owner to ensure that the animal does not go out into a *reshus harabim* or a *karmelis* while carrying a burden.

In section 8, the *Shemiras Shabbos Kehilchasa* rules: *beheimah she-l'shem shemirasah yesh bitzavar chevel saviv tzavarah, muttar lo l'holichah birshus harabim b'chevel zeh u-vilvad she-ketzehu lo yivlot tefach echad o yoser mitachas yad hamolicho* — an animal that requires a rope or collar for its protection may be led through a *reshus harabim* on that leash, provided that no more than a *tefach* of the leash extends beyond the handler's hand. This is precisely the *psak* of the *Gemara* applied in practical terms: one may walk one's dog or animal on a leash even in a *reshus harabim*, but must ensure the leash does not come within a *tefach* of the ground. The text further specifies: *v'gam yakpid she-chelek hachevel she-bo hu molich es habeheimah lo yikarav k'dei tefach echad samuch la-aretz*.

Additionally, when wrapping the leash around the animal's neck on *Shabbos*, one must be careful not to lean on the animal: *u-cheshe-koreich es hachevel bi-Shabbos saviv tzavar habeheimah yizaher shelo lishaen aleha*. This reflects the assumption, operative throughout this section, that animals are *muktzah*. The question arises whether work animals are likewise *muktzah*, and the answer is yes — the general presumption is that all animals are *muktzah*, and although there is contemporary discussion regarding pets specifically, the *Shemiras Shabbos Kehilchasa* operates with the standard assumption. Beyond the *muktzah* concern, there is a separate prohibition of being *mishtamesh* in *ba'alei chayim* — making use of a living creature — which applies independently.

### Pet Tags & Chips

Section 9 addresses the issue of identification tags on animals, which arose in the previous session in the context of strings tied to chickens. The *Shemiras Shabbos Kehilchasa* rules: *siman heker hatalui al tzavarah shel beheimah, tag, eino shemirah regilah labeheimah, v'assur lehaniach lah latzeit bo birshus harabim* — an identification tag hanging from an animal's neck does not constitute a normal protective covering for the animal, and it is therefore forbidden to permit the animal to go out with it into a *reshus harabim*. This is a direct application of the *Gemara*'s *psak*. Consequently, owners of dogs must take care not to take their dogs out in a place without an *eruv* when an identifying tag is on the animal's collar.

The final clause of this section introduces a distinction: *v'hu siman heker hakavu'a b'ozen habeheimah b'masmer she-alav mispar zihui v'sha'ar pratum eino bichlal zeh, mikeivan shelo nitan le-hasiro* — a mark that is permanently affixed and cannot be removed falls into a different category. This would apply to a microchip implanted in the animal, which by its nature cannot be taken off. In most places, dogs are legally required to carry tags, which means this ruling has direct and immediate practical application.

## Leadership & Protest

### Officials' Liability

The *Gemara* yesterday established that Rabbi Eliezer was held liable for his neighbor's cow, because he failed to protest her behavior. The *Gemara* now extends this principle to communal leadership. *Amar Papa: v'hanei d'vei Reish Galusa nispas al kulei alma* — the members of the household of the *Reish Galusa*, who held political authority and were in a position to intervene in the conduct of the entire community, are held responsible for everyone's wrongdoing, since they had the ability to do something about it.

### Elders & Officers

This is supported by a statement of Rabbi Yochanan. The *Gemara* asks: *mai d'chasiv, Hashem b'mishpat yavo im ziknei amo v'sarav* — what is the meaning of the verse that God will come to judgment with the elders of His nation and its officers? If the officers sinned — that is, the political heads who failed to lead the people properly — what did the elders, the sages, do wrong? The *Gemara* answers: *ella eima al zekeinim shelo michu b'sarim* — rather, understand it as God coming in judgment with the elders because they failed to protest against the officers. Sages and religious figures who possess a voice that can be heard, and who are positioned to protest, bear responsibility when they remain silent. They too are held accountable.

### Woman's Plea

The *Gemara* relates that Rav Yehuda was sitting before Shmuel when a certain woman came and cried out before him, complaining of some injustice done to her. Shmuel paid her no attention. Rav Yehuda said to him: does the master not hold by the verse, *otem azno mi-za'akat dal, gam hu yikra v'lo ye'aneh* — one who seals his ear from the outcry of the poor will himself cry out and not be answered? How could you ignore this woman?

Shmuel replied: *Shina'na, reishach b'kriri, reisha d'reishach b'chamimei* — sharp one, if I, your superior, am judged with cold water, then the head above me will be judged with scalding water. In other words, Shmuel does not fully justify his inaction, but points out that the primary responsibility rests with whoever stands above him in the hierarchy. He was not exactly defending what he was doing; he was indicating that someone else bore a far greater share of the responsibility. There may be truth to this — if one is a cog in a system and the system is not functioning properly, there is often a limit to what any individual within it can do. Nevertheless, this did not help the woman, and Shmuel's response has the character of passing the buck, even if it contains a kernel of genuine insight.

### Mar Ukva's Role

The *Gemara* continues: *hava yasiv Mar Ukva* — Mar Ukva was sitting. Mar Ukva is here identified as the one who truly held authority — he was the head of the *beis din*. And it is he who will be judged harshly, for it says: *Beis David koh amar Hashem, dinu lavoker mishpat, v'hatzilu gazul miyad oshek, pen tetzei ka'esh chamasi u-va'arah v'ein mechabeh, mipnei ro'a ma'aleihem* — House of David, so says God, administer justice in the morning, save the one being robbed from the oppressor, lest My anger go forth like fire and burn with none to extinguish it, because of the evil of their deeds. Mar Ukva, the genuine political head who had the capacity to build a functioning system of justice, was not doing so, and thus he is the one who will be judged with scalding fire.

The scenario, as the *Gemara* presents it: Rav Yehuda and Shmuel are present, Mar Ukva is not. Rav Yehuda presses Shmuel on how he can turn a deaf ear to a woman crying out for help. Shmuel does not fully defend his behavior, but deflects the primary accountability upward to Mar Ukva, who as head of the *beis din* had not established the structures necessary for justice to be served. It is striking that the *Gemara* is willing to record such a pointed indictment — evidently the rabbis themselves applied this standard to their own institutional failures. That it did not help the woman in the immediate moment is a sobering reality that the text does not conceal.

### Must Protest

The *Gemara* now presents a related exchange. Rav Zeira said to Rav Simon: *lochechanu mar l'hanei d'vei Reish Galusa* — you, Rav Simon, should rebuke, give *hochecha*, to the members of the household of the *Reish Galusa*, for they are not conducting themselves as they should, whether through their own misconduct or their failure to create a just system. Rav Simon replied: *lo mekablun minai* — they will not listen to me. Rav Zeira responded: *af al gav d'lo mekablun, lochechanu mar* — even if they will not accept your *mussar*, you must nonetheless stand up and protest.

This leads directly into the principle cited by Rav Acha in the name of Rabbi Hanina: *me'olam lo yatzta middah tovah mipi HaKadosh Baruch Hu v'chazar bah l'ra'ah* — never did a good decree go forth from the mouth of the Holy One Blessed Be He and then be retracted for an evil one. This principle is significant for the *Rambam* as well: *Rambam* teaches that a *navi* is proven genuine when his prophecy is fulfilled — but then how do we account for the fact that God sometimes appears to reverse Himself, as in the case of Nineveh, which He threatened to destroy but did not? *Rambam* answers that God may retract a negative prophecy when people do *teshuvah*, but a positive declaration, a promise of good, God does not reverse — with one exception, as the *Gemara* is about to explain.

## Yechezkel Vision

### Good Decrees

The one exception to the principle that God never retracts a good decree is what the *Gemara* now elaborates from the vision of Yechezkel. *Chutz midavar zeh* — except for this one matter, as it is written...

### Idolatry & Injustice

The prophecy in question comes from chapters 8 through 10 of Yechezkel. Yechezkel went into exile approximately ten years before the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*, and in this section of his prophecy he describes a series of terrible sins: people worshipping *avodah zarah* within the *Beis HaMikdash* itself, people turning their backs to the Holy of Holies and desecrating it, and beyond the ritual sins, serious injustice as well. He then envisions angels coming to destroy Yerushalayim, but among them one figure — a man dressed in linen — is given the command to place a mark on the foreheads of the righteous, so that when the city is destroyed, the righteous will be protected.

The *pasuk* reads: *vayomer Hashem elav, avor besoch ha'ir besoch Yerushalayim, v'hisvisa tav al mitzchot ha'anashim hane'enachim v'hane'enakim al kol hato'evos hana'asos besochah* — God said to him: pass through the city, through Yerushalayim, and place a mark on the foreheads of all the people who moan and groan over all the abominations being done within it. These are the righteous who bemoan the wrongdoing. Place a mark on their foreheads; they will be saved and protected when the city is destroyed.

### Gabriel's Mark

The *Gemara* now elaborates on this vision. *Amar lei HaKadosh Baruch Hu l'Gavriel* — God said to Gavriel, for the *Gemara* understands the man in linen to be the angel Gavriel, who had the writing implements of a scribe on his person: *lechi v'rishmi al mitzchan shel tzaddikim tav shel dyo* — go and mark on the foreheads of the *tzaddikim* a *tav* in ink, *shelo yishletu bahem malachei chabalah* — so that the *malachei chabalah*, the angels of destruction, will have no power over them. *V'al mitzchan shel resha'im tav shel dam* — and on the foreheads of the *resha'im*, a *tav* of blood, *kedei sheyishletu bahen malachei chabalah* — so that the angels of destruction may proceed to destroy them.

### Din's Intervention

Now the *midat hadin* intervenes — surprisingly, not on the side of the *resha'im*, but against the *tzaddikim*. *Amrah midat hadin lifnei HaKadosh Baruch Hu: Ribbono shel Olam, mah nishtanu elu me'elu* — Master of the Universe, why are you treating the *tzaddikim* differently from the *resha'im*? God responds: *halalu tzaddikim gemurim v'halalu resha'im gemurim* — these are fully righteous and those are fully wicked. The *midat hadin* responds: *Ribbono shel Olam, haya b'yadam limchot v'lo michu* — Master of the Universe, do not let the *tzaddikim* go free. They had the ability to protest and they did not protest. They bear equal responsibility.

God replies: *galuy v'yadu'a lefanai she'im michu bahem lo yikablu mehem* — it is revealed and known before Me that even had they protested, the others would not have listened. Therefore, you cannot hold them responsible. The *midat hadin* responds: *im lefanecha galuy lahem mi galuy* — if it is revealed before You, it was not revealed to them. They still bore the responsibility to protest, and their silence is their accountability. It does not matter whether God knew in advance that it would have made no difference. They did not live up to their obligation, and silence is treated as complicity.

### Reversal & Guilt

*V'haynu d'chasiv* — and this is why, against what might have been expected, God reversed Himself: *zaken bachur u'vetulah taf v'nashim tahargu l'mashchis* — wipe them all out: elder and young, virgin and woman, infants and women, destroy. The word *zaken*, elder, which would normally connote a sage or a righteous figure, is now included in the destruction. This is, the *Gemara* acknowledges, not the *pshat* of the *pasuk* — according to the plain meaning, the righteous were saved. The *agadata* is that God reversed Himself, and the righteous were destroyed as well.

The *Maharsha* explains this reversal: had it been merely the *tzaddikim*'s own personal sins in question, their own righteousness would have outweighed those sins. But they are being held accountable for the sins of thousands of others — sins for which they bore communal responsibility but did nothing to address. That accumulated accountability outweighs their personal righteousness, and therefore God reversed the good decree.

The end of the *pasuk* seems, on its *pshat*, to confirm the righteous were protected: *v'al kol ish asher alav hatav al tigshu, u-mimikdashi tachelu* — do not draw near any person who bears the mark, and begin the destruction from my sanctuary. Yet immediately thereafter: *vayachelu ba'anashim hazkeinim asher lifnei habayis* — it began with the elders who were before the *Beis HaMikdash*. These elders, understood as the sages, the very ones who would have been among the righteous, were the first to be held accountable, precisely because they were in the greatest position to know better and to act.

### Rav Yosef

*Tani Rav Yosef: al tikrei mimikdashi, ela mimekudashai* — do not read it as "from My sanctuary," but rather as "from those sanctified to Me." Begin from the righteous. *Eilu bnei adam shekiyemu es haTorah kullahm'aleph v'ad tav* — these are the people who fulfilled the entire Torah from *aleph* to *tav*. Here there is a wordplay: *tav*, meaning mark, and *tav*, the final letter of the *aleph-beis*, suggesting they fulfilled the Torah in its entirety. What then of the *pasuk* that says not to harm the righteous? Rashi explains that this is precisely where God reversed Himself — first it said do not harm the righteous, but after the *midat hadin* intervened, the end of the verse — *mimikdashi*, read as *mimekudashai* — indicates that they too were destroyed.

And immediately: *v'hinei shishah anashim ba'im miderech sha'ar ha'elyon asher mifneh tzafonah, v'ish kli mafatzo b'yado* — six men came from the upper gate facing north, each with the instruments of destruction in his hand. These are the *malachei chabalah*. Alongside them, as introduced earlier: *v'ish asher besocham levush habadim, u-keses hasofer b'mosnav* — and the man in their midst clothed in linen, with the scribal case at his waist, who would make the marks. *Vayavo'u vaya'amdu etzel mizbe'ach hanechoshes* — and they stood by the copper altar.

The *Gemara* interjects: *mizbe'ach hanechoshes mi havah* — there was no copper altar at that time. That was from the time of Moshe; by the time of Shlomo they had a stone altar. God then says: *hatchilu mimakom she'omrim shirah lefanai* — begin from the place where they sing song before Me. *Nechoshes* is understood here as a reference to the copper musical instruments of the *Leviyim* — an indication, once again, that the destruction must begin from those closest to God, those in the greatest position to know better and to speak out.

Who were the six men? *Amar Rav Chisda: ketzef, af, v'chemah, u-mashchis, u-meshaber, u-machleh* — fury, anger, wrath, destroyer, breaker, and annihilator. Whether these are their names or their defining attributes, they are clearly angels of destruction, each embodying a distinct dimension of the divine reckoning.

### Tav Meaning

The *Gemara* asks: *u-mai shna tav* — why specifically the letter *tav*? *Amar Rav: tav tichyeh, tav tamus* — a *tav* means "live," placed on the righteous, and a *tav* means "die," placed on the wicked. This is striking: how does one tell the difference? Perhaps it was the ink versus the blood. But the deeper point the *Gemara* may be pressing is precisely that it is the same letter — there is very little difference between the two. Once one is held accountable for failing to protest the sins of others, the distinction between righteous and wicked collapses. *Tav tichyeh, tav tamus* — the exact same letter, pointing to the theme that silence in the face of injustice renders one morally indistinguishable from the wrongdoer.

### Merit Dispute

*U-Shmuel amar: tamah zechus avos* — Shmuel says the *tav* signifies that the merit of the forefathers had been exhausted, and therefore they would not be saved by *zechus avos*. *Rabbi Yochanan amar: tachon zechus avos* — Rabbi Yochanan says, the merit of the fathers should rest upon them. The *Maharsha* reads the debate as follows: according to Shmuel, *zechus avos* had ended, though the *tzaddikim* might still have been protected by their own merit — until the *midat hadin* reversed that as well. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the *zechus avos* should have protected them, particularly the righteous, though presumably not the wicked.

*Reish Lakish amar: tav sof chotamo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — the *tav* is the final letter of God's seal. *D'amar Rav Chanina: chotamo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu emes* — God's seal is *emes*, truth. This connects to the philosophical foundation of the *Rambam*'s *Mishneh Torah*: God is the ultimate cause, the one whose existence is truly absolute, while all else exists only contingently and dependently. God is ultimate truth in both the existential and the moral sense.

Rashi notes — in a remark worth pausing over: *emtza'i l'otiyos v'rishon v'acharon* — the *mem* is the middle letter of the *aleph-beis*, the *aleph* is the first and the *tav* is the last. *Mishum ani rishon v'ani acharon v'ani hu* — I am first, I am last, and I am the One. The word *emes* thus encompasses beginning, middle, and end, the fullest possible expression of divine existence and constancy.

It is worth noting that *Rabbeinu Nissim Gaon*, writing before Rashi and Tosafos and the era of systematic cross-referencing, records with evident delight that he discovered the source for Rav Chanina's statement in the *Yerushalmi* in *Sanhedrin*: *v'nir'eh li l'kotvah henah, v'hachi amru: mah chotamo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu? Rav Bibi b'shem Rabbi Reuven amar: emes* — and he notes the explanation of *Reish Lakish* in the *Yerushalmi* as *ta'am na'eh beyoter*, a particularly beautiful explanation. This offers a window into what scholarship looked like before everything was cross-referenced — the joy of a fresh discovery from a *Yerushalmi* that illuminates the *Bavli*.

*Amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani: eilu bnei adam shekiyemu es haTorah kullahm'aleph v'ad tav* — this is the position that they fulfilled the Torah completely, from *aleph* to *tav*, and nonetheless were held accountable. This is an extraordinarily strong indictment, and the *Gemara* is pressing it deliberately: those in a position to make a moral and religious voice heard must do so. Even if the outcome seems certain, even if the protest appears futile, the obligation to speak remains.

On the question of whether one is ever exempt from protest on the grounds that the sinner will certainly not listen, Tosafos addresses the *Gemara* elsewhere that says *mutav she-yihyu shogegin* — better that people sin inadvertently than sin deliberately. Tosafos clarifies: *haynu heicha d'safek i mekablei* — that principle applies only when there is genuine uncertainty about whether protest will be accepted. The simple implication of the present *Gemara* is that one can never be certain enough to be exempt: you never know, and that uncertainty alone is sufficient to obligate protest.

Several distinctions further delimit when *mutav she-yihyu shogegin* applies. First, that principle addresses concern about an individual transgression, whereas here an entire city is steeped in wickedness, which may impose greater rather than lesser obligation. Second, *mutav she-yihyu shogegin* may apply to ritual sins between a person and God; it cannot apply to sins *bein adam l'chaveiro* — between people — where someone is actively suffering as a result of the wrongdoing. Third, according to some opinions, *mutav she-yihyu shogegin* does not apply to matters explicitly stated in the Torah, matters that are clearly known. One must still protest where the wrong is unambiguous.

There is a further dimension worth noting: even where protest will demonstrably not change anyone's behavior, it still has independent value. Silence in the face of wrongdoing reads as acquiescence, as acceptance, as a failure to be offended. To raise a voice of protest, even one that is not heeded, asserts that the standard has not been abandoned — that this is not acceptable. It defends the moral norm even when it cannot enforce it. The *Gemara* makes this all the more poignant by embedding it in the story of the woman who came crying for help and was ignored: that was not a case of abstract principle. She was there, present, asking. And she was turned away.

## Merit Ended When?

### Rav: Hoshea

The *Gemara* now turns to the question raised by Shmuel's reading of the *tav* — that *zechus avos* had been exhausted. *V'eimasai tamah zechus avos?* — When did the merit of the forefathers end? Rav says: *miyamav shel Hoshea ben Be'eri* — from the days of the prophet Hoshea. *Shene'emar: egaleh es nevulasah l'einei me'ahaveha v'ish lo yatzilena miyadi* — I will expose her disgrace before those who love her and no one will save her from My hand. The word *me'ahaveha* may refer here to those who would otherwise have interceded — perhaps the *avos* themselves. But God declares that none will save her from Him, indicating that from this point forward, *zechus avos* will no longer be operative.

### Shmuel: Chazael

Shmuel says: *miyamei Chazael* — from the days of Chazael, king of Aram, at the end of the period of the *Bayis Rishon*, who afflicted Israel throughout the reign of Yehoachaz. The verse reads: *vayachan Hashem osam vayerachamem vayifen aleihem l'ma'an brito es Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov v'lo avah hashchisam v'lo hishlicham mei'al panav ad atah* — God had compassion on them and turned to them for the sake of His covenant with Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and He did not wish to destroy them or cast them from His presence — until now. Rashi explains: until now the *zechus avos* and the *bris* were working. From this point forward, they would not.

### R. Yehoshua: Elijah

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says the merit ran out even earlier: *miyamei Eliyahu* — from the days of Eliyahu. When Eliyahu confronted the prophets of the Baal, he called out: *vayehi ba'alos haminchah vayigash Eliyahu haNavi vayomar: Hashem Elohei Avraham Yitzchak v'Yisrael, hayom yivada ki Atah Elohim b'Yisrael* — let it be known today that You are God in Israel, I am Your servant and have done all this at Your word. The word *hayom* — today — implies that from this day forward, one could no longer rely on the *zechus avos*; their invocation here was itself already an appeal to something fading.

### R. Yochanan: Chizkiyahu

Rabbi Yochanan says: *miyamei Chizkiyahu* — from the days of Chizkiyahu. *Shene'emar: l'marbeh hamisrah u-l'shalom ein ketz, al kisei David v'al mamlachto l'hachin osah u-l'sa'adah b'mishpat u-v'tzedakah, mei'atah v'ad olam* — for the increase of sovereignty and for boundless peace, on the throne of David and on his kingdom, to establish it and sustain it with justice and righteousness, from now and forever. *Kinat Hashem Tzevakos ta'aseh zos* — the zealousness of God of Hosts will bring this about. From this point forward, Rabbi Yochanan reads, it will be *mishpat u-tzedakah* — justice and righteousness — alone that governs, and *zechus avos* will no longer be invoked.

### Tosafos' Distinction

The difficulty with this entire *Gemara* is apparent: we are always invoking *zechus avos* in prayer. The first *berachah* of *Shemoneh Esrei* is entirely about the *avos*. If *zechus avos* has ended, what are we doing?

Tosafos addresses this directly: *amar Rabbeinu Tam: zechus avos tamah aval bris avos lo tamah* — Rabbeinu Tam distinguishes between the *zechus* and the *bris*. The *zechus* — the accumulated credit of the forefathers' deeds, a kind of spiritual trust fund from which later generations drew — has been exhausted; we have, over centuries, drawn down the principal and not merely the interest. But the *bris avos* — God's covenantal commitment to us on account of the forefathers — remains intact. God remains bound by His promise; that is not contingent on the *zechus* being maintained.

Tosafos also raises the possibility at the end of the discussion that *zechus avos* may have ended only for the *resha'im*, not for the *tzaddikim*, who may still benefit from it. And Tosafos concludes: *v'al zeh anu somchim lehazkir zechus avos* — this is why we continue to invoke *zechus avos* in prayer; we believe it still functions, at least for those who merit it.

### Later Views

Later authorities engage this question from different angles. The *Ramban* on the *Torah* takes a stark position: *ki b'galusenu atah tamah zechus avos, v'ein lanu hatzalah miyad ha'amim* — in our present exile, *zechus avos* has ended and we have no protection from the nations except *rak ba'avur shemo* — for the sake of God's name. God acts to prevent the desecration of His name that would result from our destruction; it is not the *bris avos* that protects us, not even a covenant — only the concern for divine honor. The *Tur*, also reflecting the *Sefard* tradition, reads the first *berachah* of *Shemoneh Esrei* accordingly: *u-mevi go'el livnei veneihem l'ma'an shemo* — God brings a redeemer to the children's children for His own sake, no longer for the *zechus avos*.

Rashi, interestingly, offers a counterbalancing perspective in his commentary on the *pasuk* where God reveals the *yud-gimmel middos*. God says to Moshe: *higia sha'ah she-tirah bichvodi mah she-atah tzarich lehazkir b'eis charon af* — the time has come for you to see what you must mention in a moment of divine anger. *U-lefi she-ani rotzeh lelamdecha seder tefillah, keshe-nitzrachta levakesh rachamim al Yisrael hazkiru li zechus avos* — I wish to teach you the order of prayer: when you need to ask for compassion for Israel, invoke *zechus avos*. Rashi then notes: perhaps you thought that if *zechus avos* were to run out there would be no hope? Therefore *ani a'avir kol middat tuvi* — I am going to reveal to you the *yud-gimmel middos*, an alternative path to divine compassion. God anticipated the problem and provided a remedy.

The *Machzor Vitry*, from Rashi's school, holds that we do in fact have *zechus avos*, ruling according to the *midrash* cited in Tosafos that it remains operative. The *Raviyah*, representative of the *Ashkenazi* tradition, takes a nuanced position: *tamah zechus avos perush einah mesayei'at mei'eleha* — *zechus avos* no longer acts on its own initiative. But when a community or an individual invokes it in prayer — *zechor lanu bris avos* — it is possible that the prayer will be heard. The invocation itself activates what would otherwise lie dormant.

### Sfat Emet

The *Sfat Emet* adds a beautiful dimension. Even granting that *zechus avos* — the merit of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov — may have been exhausted, we still have the *zechus* of the *shevatim*, the twelve tribes themselves, or even the *zechus* of the *shivim nefesh* who descended to Egypt, as vehicles through which the merit of the earlier generations continues to reach us, even if the direct line to the *avos* has grown distant.

Most strikingly, the *Sfat Emet* comments on the *pasuk* from *Shir HaShirim*: *medalleg al he'harim, mekapeitz al ha'gevaot* — leaping over the mountains, bounding over the hills. He says: *gam harim hem avos, u-gevaot hen zechus imahot* — the mountains are the *avos*, the hills are the *zechus imahot*, the merit of the mothers. *V'hanashim rachmaniyot beyoter* — women are possessed of greater compassion, as the *Gemara* teaches, which is why they sent to Chuldah the prophetess for her intercession, expecting that her prophecy would be colored by compassion. Even according to the opinion that *zechus avos* has ended, *zechus imahot lo tamah* — the merit of the mothers has not ended. It is they, through their greater identification with *middat harachamim*, who will ultimately intercede.

One further voice deserves mention. A *chassidic* master argues that the *zechus* of three of the *avos* may have been spent, but the *zechus* of Avraham specifically remains, because Avraham was *av hamon goyim* — a father to multitudes, including the *gerim*. Anyone who draws close to God through *teshuvah* and good deeds becomes like a *ger*, and for such a person the *zechus* of Avraham remains operative: *aval zechus shel Avraham l'olam omedet lahem l'Yisrael lefi she-mekarvim atzmam l'Hashem Yitbarach bi-teshuvah u-ma'asim tovim ve-na'asim k'gerim* — the *zechus* of Avraham stands forever for Israel, insofar as they draw close to God through *teshuvah* and good deeds and thereby become like converts.

## Death & Sin

### Rav Ami

The *Gemara* now takes up a related but distinct theological question. *Amar Rav Ami: ein mitah b'lo cheit v'ein yissurin b'lo avon* — there is no death without sin and no affliction without iniquity. The connection to the preceding discussion is clear: the *tzaddikim* died in the vision of Yechezkel, and the *Gemara* has been explaining why. But another explanation suggests itself: perhaps in a time of mass destruction, righteous people simply die along with everyone else, with no theological accounting required. Rav Ami rejects this entirely — death is always connected to sin, and suffering is always connected to iniquity.

Tosafos notes an important distinction between the two terms: *cheit* is an inadvertent sin, while *avon* is a willful transgression. This produces a counterintuitive hierarchy: there is no death without at least an inadvertent sin — everyone can be said to have committed some *cheit* that provides the basis for death. But suffering, *yissurin*, requires actual willful sinning, *avon*. One might have expected *yissurin* to require a lower threshold than death, but Tosafos reads it in the opposite direction.

### Yechezkel Quote

Scriptural support: *d'chasiv hanefesh hachote'it hi tamut* — the soul that sins shall die. *Ben lo yisa ba'avon ha'av, av lo yisa ba'avon haben* — the son shall not bear the father's iniquity, the father shall not bear the son's iniquity. *Tzidkat hatzaddik alav tihyeh v'rish'at harasha alav tihyeh* — the righteousness of the righteous shall be upon him, and the wickedness of the wicked shall be upon him. This is the passage from Yechezkel affirming individual divine justice: no one suffers for anyone else's sins. The only death, therefore, is death connected to one's own sin.

### Angels' Query

*Metivei: amru malachei hasharet lifnei HaKadosh Baruch Hu: Ribbono shel Olam, mipnei mah kanasta mitah al Adam HaRishon?* — The ministering angels asked God: why did You decree death upon Adam HaRishon? God answered: *mitzva kalah tzivisi v'avar aleha* — I gave him an easy commandment and he transgressed it, and death was the consequence. They pressed further: *halo Moshe v'Aharon shekiyemu kol haTorah kullam u-metu?* — But Moshe and Aharon fulfilled the entire Torah — why did they have to die? God replied: *mikreh echad la-tzaddik v'la-rasha la-tov* — one fate awaits the righteous and the wicked alike, the good and the bad — once death exists in the world, it exists for all. This appears to constitute direct evidence against Rav Ami: here it seems that Moshe and Aharon died without sin.

### Four Sinless

The *Gemara* responds: *hu d'amar ki hai tanna* — Rav Ami holds in accordance with another *tanna*, who teaches: *arba'ah metu b'ito shel nachash* — four people died solely on account of the seduction of the *nachash* in the Garden of Eden, not through any sin of their own. These are: Binyamin ben Yaakov, Amram the father of Moshe, Yishai the father of David, and Kil'av the son of David. It is notable that none of these figures are prominent actors on the biblical stage — which may reflect that living entirely without sin requires removing oneself from active engagement with the world. For those at the center of events, some *cheit* is almost inevitable.

### Yishai/Nachash

Most of this list is known by tradition, but Yishai's case is derived explicitly from the text. The verse says of Amasa: *v'Amasa ben ish u-shemo Yitra ha-Yisraeli asher ba el Avigail bat Nachash achot Tzeruya em Yoav* — Amasa was the son of Yitra the Israelite, who came to Avigail daughter of Nachash, sister of Tzeruya, mother of Yoav. But the *Gemara* objects: *v'chi bat Nachash havaya, v'halo bat Yishai havaya?* — Was Avigail the daughter of Nachash? She was the daughter of Yishai! As it is explicitly stated elsewhere that Tzeruya and Avigail were among the daughters of Yishai. The *Gemara* resolves this: *ela bat mi she-mes b'ito shel nachash* — she is called the daughter of Nachash because her father Yishai died solely on account of the *nachash*, not through his own sin. *Nachash* becomes a designation for Yishai, marking him as one who died by the primordial sin of the serpent and not his own transgression.

### Rav Ami Refuted

The *Gemara* then asks: whose position is represented by this list of four? If it is the position of the story about the angels — in which Moshe and Aharon are presented as having died without sin — then the count would not be four but six. It must therefore be the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who holds that Moshe and Aharon did indeed die as a result of their sin at Mei Meriva: *ne'emar ya'an lo he'emantem bi* — because you did not believe in Me. Had they believed, *adayin lo haya zmanechem l'hipater min ha'olam* — they would not yet have reached the time of their death.

The obvious question is how Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar can reconcile this with the fact that they were already told they would not enter the Land — wasn't that punishment enough? The *Gemara*'s implicit answer is that the sin at Mei Meriva may have been sufficient to deny them entry to *Eretz Yisrael*, but insufficient to have justified their deaths — unless one holds like Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar that it did justify their deaths as well. Thus even according to the position that holds *ein mitah b'lo cheit*, the standard required for death is a meaningful one; the tiniest inadvertent sin does not necessarily justify dying.

*Tiyuvta d'Rav Ami tiyuvta* — and this constitutes a definitive refutation of Rav Ami. The conclusion is significant: the *Gemara* ends this discussion with apparent finality, acknowledging that there is death and suffering in this world that cannot always be attributed to individual sin or to a precise system of *s'char v'onesh*. This connects to the broader theological discussion opening *Brachot* about *tzaddik v'ra lo, rasha v'tov lo* — the just person who suffers and the wicked who prosper — and whether we can expect perfect justice to be manifest in this world. Other *Gemarot* do not necessarily adopt this conclusion, and the matter remains theologically contested.

## Reuben Debate

### Rabbi Jonathan

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: kol ha-omer Reuven chata eino ela to'eh* — anyone who says Reuven sinned is simply making a mistake. The context of this statement is the broader inquiry the *Gemara* has been conducting: did great biblical figures sin, or were they misunderstood? The case of Reuven is among the most dramatic, given the explicit verse about his conduct with Bilhah.

### Proof: Equally

*Shene'emar: vayihyu venei Yaakov shneim asar* — it is written that the sons of Yaakov were twelve, and this statement appears immediately following the account of what Reuven did. Why insert this declaration at that moment? *Melamed she-kulam shekulim k'echat* — it teaches that all twelve sons were equivalent in righteousness. If Reuven had genuinely committed what the verse implies, he could not have been listed among the twelve with no distinction.

How then does one read the verse *vayishkav es Bilhah pilegesh aviv* — and he lay with Bilhah, his father's concubine? *Melamed she-bilbel matzo shel aviv* — it teaches that he disturbed, overturned, the bed of his father. He did not actually lie with her; rather, he interfered with her sleeping arrangement. *U-ma'aleh alav hakatuv k'ilu shakhav imah* — the Torah writes of him as though he had lain with her, because his intervention in his father's domestic arrangements was of such gravity that Scripture treats it as equivalent.

### R. Shimon: Defended Moth…

*Tanya: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer: mutzal oto tzaddik mei-avon zeh* — that righteous man Reuven was saved from that sin. The phrase "was saved" is suggestive — perhaps implying that there was a desire or temptation involved but that he ultimately did not succumb. *V'lo ba ma'aseh zeh l'yado* — and indeed, the action never came to his hand at all. How could it have been otherwise? *Efshar atid zaro la'amod al Har Eival v'lomar?* — Is it conceivable that his descendants would one day stand on Har Eival — where Reuven's tribe was assigned to participate in the proclamation of the *klalos* — and God would have allowed him to have committed such a transgression?

What then prompted Reuven's intervention? *Elbon imo tava* — he came to avenge the insult to his mother. Reuven reasoned: his mother's sister Rochel was a rival wife to Leah while Rochel was alive — that is painful but comprehensible. But now Rochel had died. *Shifchas achos imi t'hei tzarah l'imi* — was the handmaid of his mother's sister now going to take his mother's place? Was Yaakov going to favor Bilhah as Rochel's proxy over Leah? Reuven acted — *amad u-bilbel es matzah* — he went and overturned her bed, acting in defense of his mother's honor, not out of any improper desire.

### Bed Confusion

Others say: *shtei matzaos bilbel* — he disturbed two beds, not one. *Achas shel Shechinah, v'achas shel aviv* — one of the *Shechinah* and one of his father. The verse in Bereishit records Yaakov saying to Reuven: *az chillalta yetzu'i alah* — then you desecrated my bed that ascended. Rashi explains that *yetzu'i alah* refers to the bed upon which the *Shechinah* rested. Rashi's understanding is that the *Shechinah* dwelled in the tents of the *tzaddikim* before there was a *Mishkan*, and Yaakov had established a bed to represent the presence of the *Shechinah* — evoking the *Gemara*'s teaching that *ish* and *ishah*, with God present between them, make possible a sacred encounter even in the most intimate of circumstances. When Reuven intervened, he disturbed not only Yaakov's domestic arrangement but this representation of divine presence as well. Yet the crucial point is that he did not sleep with Bilhah.

### Four Opinions

*K'tannai: pachaz kamayim al totar*. Four opinions appear in this *baraita* regarding what these words mean when Yaakov addresses Reuven. Rabbi Eliezer reads *pachaz* as an acronym: *pasa'ta, chavta, zalta* — you were impetuous, you incurred liability, you debased yourself — holding that Reuven did indeed sin. Rabbi Yehoshua similarly reads: *pasa'ta al das, chatasa, zanisa* — you transgressed the faith, you sinned, you committed adultery — affirming the sin.

Rabban Gamliel reads it entirely differently: *pillalta, challta, zarcha tfilasecha* — you prayed, you implored God, and your prayer shone forth. On this reading, Reuven was in danger of sinning, prayed for strength to resist, and was saved through his prayer. *Al totar* — "you shall not excel" — perhaps reflects the consequence of having come so close. Rabban Gamliel himself adds: *adayin tzrichin anu l'Moda'i* — we still need Moda'i, that is, Rabbi Eliezer HaModa'i, renowned for his mastery of *aggadeta*. Rabbi Eliezer HaModa'i then reads the letters in reverse — not *peh-ches-zayin* but *zayin-ches-peh*: *zi'azata, charadta, u-farcha cheit mimcha* — you trembled, you shook with inner struggle, and then the sin flew away from you. You overcame your evil inclination and returned to your proper state. *Chazarta l'sikanecha*.

### Rava's View

*Rava omer, v'amri lah Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba: zacharta onsho shel davar* — he remembered the punishment he would incur if he were to sin. *Chalis atzmecha choli gadol* — he made himself sick, he suffered greatly in his inner struggle between desire and conscience. *Pirashta mimalachtecha* — and in the end, he separated himself from the sin entirely.

It is important to note that the *Gemara* here records a genuine dispute. Some *tanna'im* — Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua — maintain that Reuven did sin. The approach that seeks to exonerate the great figures of *Tanach* is one rabbinic tendency, but not the only one. The tension between *aggadeta* and *peshat* — between the homiletical drive to protect the honor of the *tzaddikim* and the plain meaning of the text — runs through this entire *sugya* and surfaces in the very debate recorded here. When Steinsaltz wrote his *Biblical Images* and described the actions of *Tanach* figures according to their *pshat*, he was himself aligning with one side of a debate the *Gemara* openly acknowledges. *Ein mikra yotzei midei pishuto* — a verse does not lose its plain meaning — is a principle that stands in real tension with the *aggadic* impulse to read the great figures of Israel as having never truly fallen. The *Gemara* will continue this discussion in the coming daf.
