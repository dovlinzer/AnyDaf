# Sanhedrin 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hereg / Sword

### Hereg Mishna

The present daf opens at the *mishna* in the middle of 52b, immediately following the discussion of *sreifah* — the execution by pouring molten lead down the throat, derived from the principle of *ke'ein misa*, resembling natural death. The *mishna* now turns to *hereg*, execution by sword.

*Mitzvas haneheragim*: the method of decapitation. The *mishna* states: *hayu matizin es rosho besayif kederech shehamalchus osa* — they would decapitate him with a sword, in the manner that the government does. Rabbi Yehuda objects: *nival hu lo* — this is a disfiguring of the body. His preferred alternative is to place the condemned man's head on a chopping block and sever it with an axe. The *chachamim* respond with what seems like an obvious rejoinder: *ein misa menuvelet mizu* — there is no more disfiguring death than this. If Rabbi Yehuda's concern is disfigurement, the axe is hardly an improvement.

### Roman Execution

The *mishna*'s phrase *kederech shehamalchus osa* — like the government does — invites a brief historical note. Roman execution methods were not uniform: crucifixion was largely reserved for non-citizens. For citizens condemned to death, execution was carried out by the sword, though early Roman practice apparently employed the axe. The transition from axe to sword seems to have reflected a judgment that the sword was the more honorable death — associated with battle and warfare, while the axe was associated with inanimate objects or animals. Both methods, then, were attested in Roman practice, though the sword came to predominate. This historical context is significant, as it clarifies why the *chachamim* describe their method as *kederech shehamalchus osa*, and it also clarifies what Rabbi Yehuda is reacting against.

### Secular Mimicry

A *tosefta* now makes Rabbi Yehuda's true concern explicit. In the *braita*, he says to the *chachamim*: *af ani yodeia shemisa menuvelet hi* — I too know that the axe is a disfiguring form of death. *Aval ma e'eseh sheharei amra Torah: uvchukoseihem lo seleichu* — but what choice do I have? The Torah commands that we not follow in their ways. For Rabbi Yehuda, adopting an execution method that directly parallels the practice of the Roman government constitutes a violation of *lo titnu bechukoteihem*. His use of the word *nival* in the *mishna* is thus somewhat oblique; his real objection is not aesthetic but halakhic. A condemned man executed by *beis din* should not be made to feel like someone being put to death by the Romans. The axe, whatever its own aesthetic disadvantages, at least avoids that association — at least at the time Rabbi Yehuda was writing, when the sword had become the dominant Roman method.

### Torah-Derived Practice

The *chachamim*'s response cuts to the heart of the matter: *keivan dechsiv sayif baTorah, lav minaihu ka gamrinan* — since the Torah itself speaks of execution by sword, we are not learning this from them. The parallel between our practice and theirs is coincidental; its source is internal to Torah law, and therefore the prohibition of *lo titnu bechukoteihem* does not apply.

The *gemara* supports this principle with a parallel case. A *braita* teaches: *sorefin al hamelachim velo midarchei ha'emori* — one may conduct a funeral pyre for deceased kings, and this is not considered an Amorite practice. The question arises immediately: *heichi sarfinan? Ve'hachtiv uvchukoseihem lo telechu* — how can we do this, given the prohibition against following their practices? If the *gemara* had to specify that it is *not* an Amorite practice, this implies that the pagans themselves did the same thing. So why is it permitted?

The *gemara* answers: *ella keivan de'chtiv sreifa b'Oraita* — since burning funeral pyres appears in the Torah itself, as the verse states *uv'misrefot avoteicha* — in the burnings of your forefathers — this practice has independent Jewish sanction. *Lav minaihu ka gamrinan* — we are not deriving it from them. The governing principle is clear: where our practice parallels that of the gentiles but derives independently from a Torah source, there is no violation of *lo titnu bechukoteihem*.

### Bechukoteihem Parameters

This *sugya* opens an important window onto the parameters of *lo titnu bechukoteihem*. One might ask: is the prohibition purely about intent — that one must not consciously adopt gentile practices — or does it extend even to practices that merely *look* like theirs, regardless of intent? Must the Jewish practice be explicitly written in the Torah, or is it sufficient that it developed independently? These questions generate genuine complexity. For example, visiting graves or reciting formal prayers — practices found across religious cultures — raise the question of whether they represent parallel developments or problematic mimicry.

The *rishonim* identify two categories that may be subject to a stricter standard: practices rooted in *avoda zara*, and practices associated with sexual licentiousness. For these, some *rishonim* suggest it may not matter whether one intends to imitate — the practices themselves carry an intrinsically non-Jewish or idolatrous character, and that alone may render them problematic under *bechukoteihem*. For practices that are merely coincidentally shared — like the manner of execution — the decisive question is whether one is deriving and learning from them, or whether the source is genuinely internal. This *sugya* is thus a foundational text for the entire discussion of *bechukoteihem*.

## Hereg Sources

### Hereg Categories

The *gemara* now identifies who receives *hereg* and where its scriptural basis lies. The *mishna* later in the chapter will state: *elu hen ha'neheragin* — the following receive the sword: *ha'rotzei'ach ve'anshei ir ha'nidachat* — the murderer, and the inhabitants of an *ir ha'nidachat*, a city that has gone over to idolatry collectively.

The case of *ir ha'nidachat* is straightforward: the Torah explicitly states *lefi charev* — by the edge of the sword — in describing that punishment. One might have thought this itself a sufficient response to Rabbi Yehuda's objection, since a hatchet is not a sword. That question — how precisely the Torah's specification of *cherev* controls the instrument of execution — remains open, and it seems that within the general category of death by sword, some latitude exists. In any case, *lefi charev* for *ir ha'nidachat* is explicit. The more interesting case is the murderer.

### Nekem Yinakeim

For the murderer, the *gemara* asks: *minalan* — from where do we derive this? A *braita* is cited: *nakom yinakeim* — vengeance shall be taken — which appears in the context of killing a slave: *ki yakeh ish es avdo... u-meis tachas yado nakom yinakeim*. If a man strikes his slave and the slave dies under his hand, vengeance shall be taken. It is worth pausing on this: the fact that the Torah mandates execution for a free person who kills his own non-Jewish slave — even without intent to kill — represents a remarkable assertion of the slave's humanity in the ancient world.

But the text says only that *nekima* — vengeance — is taken. *Nekima zo eini yodeia mah hi* — I do not yet know what form this vengeance takes.

### Nekima Sword

The *gemara* resolves this through a verbal *gezeirah shavah*. The word *nekima* also appears in the curses of *Vayikra*: *veheiveisi aleichem cherev nokemes nekam bris* — I shall bring against you a sword that takes the vengeance of the covenant. Here, *nekima* is explicitly associated with the sword. Therefore, wherever the Torah says *nakom yinakeim*, it means death by sword.

A further question arises: perhaps *nekima* by sword means not cutting but stabbing — piercing the condemned through the torso? The *gemara* dismisses this: *lefi charev ksiv* — the verse specifies *the edge* of the sword, *pi* meaning the lip or edge, indicating a cutting rather than a stabbing motion.

A second question: perhaps the cutting should be done not at the neck but along the length of the body? This leads directly to the first invocation of *ve-ahavta le-reiacha kamocha*.

## Egla Arufa

### Ve-Ahavta

Rav Nachman said in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: the verse *ve-ahavta le-reiacha kamocha* — love your fellow as yourself — mandates *berur lo misa yafa*, that we select for the condemned person the most dignified death available. This principle is striking in its application: even in the act of execution, we are obligated to consider the dignity and experience of the condemned. Cutting at the neck is both more immediate and less disfiguring than cutting the body lengthwise; *ve-ahavta le-reiacha kamocha* therefore dictates the neck.

## Hereg Sources…

### Kal Vachomer

Having established that killing a slave is punished by the sword, the *gemara* asks: *ben chorin minalan* — what is the source for the murderer who kills a free person? The proposed answer is a *kal vachomer*: if killing a slave is punishable by the sword — a weightier form of execution than strangulation — then surely killing a free person should warrant at least as weighty a punishment. Therefore, the murderer of a free person is also executed by *hereg*.

### Severity Debate

The *kal vachomer* immediately runs into a difficulty. *Hani nicha le-man de-amar chenek kal* — this reasoning works well for the view that *chenek* is the lightest form of execution. In that case, the sword is indeed weightier, and the *kal vachomer* holds: if the killer of a slave receives the weightier punishment, the killer of a free person surely should too. *Ella le-man de-amar chenek chamur* — but according to Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *chenek* is actually the *heavier* of the two, what can be said? On that view, the ordinary unspecified execution — *chenek* — is heavier than the sword, and the *kal vachomer* would run in reverse: the killer of a free person might *more* appropriately receive the heavier *chenek* rather than the sword. So the *kal vachomer* approach is insufficient on its own.

---

## Egla Arufa…

### Hukshu Paradigm

The *gemara* therefore turns to a different derivation. The verse *ve-ata teva'er hadam hanaki mikirbecha* — you shall cleanse the innocent blood from your midst — appears in the context of *egla arufa*, the ritual of the broken-necked calf performed when a murder victim is found and the perpetrator is unknown. This verse, which speaks generally about purging spilled blood, links all murderers conceptually to the paradigm of *egla arufa*: *hukshu kol shofchei damim le-egla arufa* — all who shed blood are associated with *egla arufa*.

### Egla Method

The *egla arufa* ceremony itself involves breaking the calf's neck with a sword, and specifically at the neck. From this: *mah lahan besayif u-min hatzavar, af kan besayif u-min hatzavar* — just as there the instrument is a sword and the location is the neck, so too in the execution of the murderer. This paradigm thus provides an independent and more comprehensive basis for *hereg* by sword at the neck, covering all murderers — not merely the killer of a slave.

A question arises: perhaps we should learn from *egla arufa* that the instrument is an axe and the location is the back of the neck, since the *egla* itself is struck from behind at the back of the neck. If so, this would actually support Rabbi Yehuda's position. Rav Nachman responds again by invoking *ve-ahavta le-reiacha kamocha* — *berur lo misa yafa*: even granting that *egla arufa* is our paradigm, it provides a general framework rather than a hyper-precise prescription. Within the latitude the paradigm provides, we optimize for the most dignified death. A cut at the front of the neck by sword is more immediate and less disfiguring than a blow with an axe at the back of the neck, so the former is the correct method.

## Chenek

### Chenek Method

The *gemara* now turns to the last of the four executions: *chenek*, strangulation, which is the default or generic form of execution. The *mishna* describes the method: *hayu mashki'in oto b'zevel ad arkubotav* — they would stand the condemned in dung up to his knees, apparently to keep him steady. *V'noten sudar kasheh l'toch harakah* — they would wrap a hard cord within a soft scarf, *v'korech al tzavaro* — and wrap it around his neck, *zeh moshech etzlo v'zeh moshech etzlo* — each person pulling one end in the opposite direction, *ad she'nafsho yotzeit* — until he expires. As with the other methods, the soft wrapping serves to reduce disfigurement and pain.

### Mot Yumat

The *gemara* now addresses the scriptural basis for *chenek* as the default execution. A *braita* is cited, beginning with the verse about adultery: *ish asher yinaf et eishet ish* — a man who commits adultery with a married woman. The *braita* parses this phrase before arriving at its main point.

*Ish prat l'katan* — "a man," to exclude a minor, who is not executed. However, it should be noted that if a married woman has relations with a boy over the age of nine, though the boy is not punished, the act is still halakhically considered an act of adultery with respect to the woman, who remains liable. *Prat l'eishet katan* — the phrase "a married woman" excludes the wife of a minor. Since *kiddushin* effected by a minor boy is not fully binding, such a woman does not bear the status of a married woman for purposes of adultery law.

Rashi explains that the verse is simply affirming the fact that a minor boy cannot effect binding *kiddushin*. Tosafot notes that there is one exceptional case: *yibum*. If a minor boy's older brother died leaving a widow, the minor could effect *yibum* through intercourse — since *yibum* operates through the act itself rather than through a formal act of *kinyan* that requires an adult — and in that case there would actually be a form of legally recognized marriage with a minor. Nevertheless, the verse teaches that such a marriage is not substantial enough to trigger adultery law while the boy remains a minor.

*Eishet rei'eihu* — "his fellow's wife" — *prat l'eishet acherim*, excluding the wife of a non-Jew. From a Jewish legal perspective, intercourse with a married non-Jewish woman does not constitute *niuf*, adultery. This is true in two dimensions: a Jewish woman married to a non-Jewish man (since *kiddushin* does not take effect between a Jew and a non-Jew), and a Jewish man who has relations with a married non-Jewish woman. The laws of *Bnei Noach* do recognize adultery as prohibited for non-Jews among themselves, and non-Jewish marriage is legally meaningful within that framework. The *gemara* in Rashi's reading (which corrects the censored *Kusi* to *Goy*) states: *ashmienan she'ein kiddushin l'Goy* — this teaches that there is no binding *kiddushin* with a non-Jew.

Tosafot adds an important nuance: even if adultery per se does not apply to the case of a Jewish man with a married non-Jewish woman, the Torah's express permission of *yefas to'ar* — the beautiful captive woman — even when she is married, demonstrates that absent that special dispensation some prohibition would obtain. This proves that non-Jewish marriage, while not creating adultery liability for a Jew, is not entirely without halakhic significance.

Now the *braita* reaches its main point. The verse continues: *mos yumat ha-no'ef ve-ha-no'afet* — both the adulterer and the adulteress shall be put to death. *Mot yumat* — surely put to death — in *chenek*. But is this certain? *Atah omer b'chenek o eino ela achas mikol misos ha'amuros baTorah* — perhaps it means one of the other executions that are more explicitly prescribed in the Torah, since the Torah never explicitly mentions strangulation as a form of capital punishment?

The *braita* answers: *amarta kol makom shenemar misah baTorah stam, ei atah rasha'i limshoch l'hachmir aleha ela l'hakel aleha* — wherever the Torah prescribes death without specifying the method, one has no right to impose a heavier execution; one must assume the most lenient. This is the position of Rabbi Yoshiyah.

### Lightest Default

Rabbi Yoshiyah's approach is thus: *chenek* is the default unspecified execution because it is the lightest. If the Torah leaves the method unspecified, we are bound to select the most lenient available option.

## Four Executions

### Logical Corollary

The logical corollary is immediate: if we may not impose a heavier execution than what the Torah specifies, and the Torah's silence is read as requiring the lightest option, then the generic cases — all those where the Torah prescribes death without further detail — must be assigned to whichever mode is least severe. This reasoning presupposes that there is a defined set of executions and a clear hierarchy among them. Without knowing what options exist and how they rank, the principle cannot be applied. This sets up the question of how we identify *chenek* as an option at all.

---

## Chenek…

### Natural Death

Rabbi Yonatan offers an alternative approach. *Lo mipnei shekalah* — it is not because *chenek* is the lightest that it serves as the default. That reasoning depends on the ranking debate between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis. Rabbi Yonatan's basis is different: *kol misah amurah baTorah stam einah ela chenek* — any death prescribed by the Torah without specification is simply *chenek*, not because of its relative severity but because of what the word *misah* connotes.

### Divine Death

Rabbi Yonatan's derivation, as explained by Rebbe, runs as follows. The Torah uses the language *mot yumat* — which carries the connotation of dying, of natural death — both in human execution contexts and in contexts of *misah bidei shamayim*, death at the hand of Heaven. *Mah misah amurah bidei shamayim misah she'ein bah roshem* — just as death by Heaven's hand leaves no visible mark or disfigurement on the body, so too *misah amurah bidei adam*, death at human hands designated by the same language, must be a death that leaves no mark.

Of the four executions, *chenek* — strangulation — most fully satisfies this criterion. Done properly, it leaves no external mark. Moreover, there is a deeper resonance: strangulation is death by the deprivation of breath, of *nefesh*. The word *nefesh* is etymologically connected to breathing — *vayinafash*, and he rested, carries the sense of drawing breath. Death by *chenek* strikes most directly at the *nefesh*, the breath of life, making it the most inherently natural-seeming form of death.

This frames a significant conceptual question: is the absence of *roshem* — disfigurement — valued because of inherent respect for the human body as such? Or is it valued because it mirrors *misah bidei shamayim*, divine death, and thus represents an ideal of execution that approximates God's own manner of taking life? These are two meaningfully different ways of understanding the principle.

The *gemara* then asks: why not say the default execution is *sreifah* — burning — which also, at least in its prescribed form (pouring molten lead down the throat), leaves the external body largely unmarked? The answer: since the Torah prescribes *sreifah* specifically for the *bat kohen* who commits adultery — a manifestly heavier punishment — this demonstrates by contrast that the standard case of adultery does not receive *sreifah*. *Michlal deha lav bas sreifah hi* — we infer that the ordinary adultery case falls outside the *sreifah* category. Therefore, *sreifah* cannot be the generic default, and *chenek* remains.

---

## Four Executions…

### Rava's Tradition

The *gemara* then asks from Rabbi Yoshiyah's perspective: how do we even know that *chenek* is among the executions? If we assign unspecified cases to the lightest execution, we still need to know what options exist. Perhaps there are only three modes of execution and the lightest of those three should be the default. *Ve'eima sayaf* — perhaps the sword is the lightest and serves as the default?

Rava resolves this: *arba misos gemara gemiri lehu* — we have a received tradition that there are exactly four modes of execution. Given this tradition, once the other three are accounted for by specific cases, the fourth — *chenek* — must exist, and the unspecified cases are assigned to it.

### Unspecified Cases

Rashi elaborates: the tradition specifies not only that there are four modes but which four they are. Since the Torah never assigns *chenek* explicitly to any particular case, and since we have a further principle that unspecified executions must be read as the most lenient, the conclusion follows: *chenek* is the lightest, and all unspecified cases receive *chenek*. Rashi puts it plainly — since we are not entitled to impose the heavier form when the Torah is silent, the entire category of unspecified cases gravitates naturally to *chenek*. The tradition and the principle together close the circle.

## Sekilah Basis

### Which Phrase?

The *gemara* now shifts to the question of *sekilah*, stoning, and specifically to the puzzle of which scriptural phrase marks a given transgression as warranting that execution. Rabbi Zeira poses the question to Abbaye: for those cases where *sekilah* is not stated explicitly but is derived from the paradigm case of *ov ve-yidoni* — the necromancer and the diviner — by which phrase is the derivation made? Is it from *mot yumasu* — they shall surely be put to death — or from *demeihem bam* — their blood is upon them?

This question matters because the phrase that carries the meaning of *sekilah* is the one that, when it appears elsewhere, signals that the transgressor receives stoning. The phrase *mot yumat* (or *mot yumasu*) appears in many contexts — including adultery, which we have established receives *chenek*. The phrase *demeihem bam* is far more restricted in its distribution. The answer to Rabbi Zeira's question will determine which cases receive *sekilah* through derivation.

### Ov Yidoni

The paradigm case is *ov ve-yidoni*, whose verse states explicitly: *mos yumatu ba-even yirgemu otam demeihem bam* — they shall surely be put to death; they shall be stoned; their blood is upon them (*Vayikra* 20:27). This verse contains both phrases — *mot yumasu* and *demeihem bam* — alongside the explicit mention of stoning (*ba-even yirgemu otam*). It is thus the anchor from which all other *sekilah* cases are derived. The question is which of its two non-explicit phrases carries the *sekilah* meaning when extended to other cases.

### Demihem Bam

Abbaye's answer: *bidmeihem bam gmiri* — the derivation is from *demeihem bam*. His reasoning proceeds by elimination. If *mot yumasu* were the operative phrase, what function would *demeihem bam* serve? Every phrase in a verse must be doing halakhic work, and if *mot yumasu* already communicates *sekilah*, *demeihem bam* becomes superfluous. But *demeihem bam* is a distinctively unusual expression — evoking, perhaps quite literally, the flowing blood of the victim of stoning — and it must be carrying halakhic significance.

The *gemara* immediately turns this reasoning around: if *demeihem bam* carries the meaning of *sekilah*, what does *mot yumasu* accomplish? The answer emerges from a separate *braita* about the murderer: *mos yumos hamakeh rotzei'ach hu* — he shall surely be put to death, he is a murderer. The teaching derived from *mot yumat* there is: *ein li ela be-misah haksivah bo* — I know only that you execute him by the assigned method. But *bo minayin she-im ee atah yachol la-hamiso be-misah haksivah bo she-atah memiso be-chol misah she-atah yachol la-hamiso* — from where do I know that if for some reason the standard execution cannot be carried out, you may use any available means? *Talmud lomar mos yumos hamakeh mi-kol makom* — the doubling of the death language teaches: he must be executed one way or another. This is the function of *mot yumat*: it confers a broader license to execute by other means when the prescribed method is unavailable. With this, both phrases carry distinct meanings, and the structure is coherent.

### Mot Yumasu License

To sharpen the point: *mot yumat* licenses execution by any means when the standard method fails. *Demeihem bam* identifies *sekilah* as the prescribed method for those to whom it applies. These two functions are independent and non-overlapping, which is why both phrases are necessary and neither is superfluous.

A further question is implicit: does this broader license — to execute by any available means — apply to all those under capital sentence, or only those for whom the verse explicitly states *mot yumat*? The *gemara* leaves this for another occasion, but the license itself is established.

### Mot Yumasu Disqualified

Rav Achai of Difti presses the question further in a dialogue with Ravina: what precisely is the difficulty with using *mot yumasu* as the *sekilah* indicator? The *gemara* reasons through several candidates.

If the adultery case were the concern — *ish asher yinaf et eishet ish... mos yumat ha-no'ef ve-ha-no'afet*, which says *mot yumat* and yet receives *chenek* — one might say: let us simply derive from *ov ve-yidoni* that *mot yumat* means *sekilah*! But the response is: *mide'amar rachmana arusah bi-skila miklal de-nesuah lav bi-skila* — since the Torah prescribes *sekilah* for the *na'arah me'orasah* (the betrothed maiden), we infer that the ordinary *eshet ish* (the married woman) does not receive *sekilah*. The contrast between the two cases does the work; we can therefore absorb the adultery case without difficulty.

What about hitting a father or mother, where the verse also says *mot yumat* yet the execution is *chenek*? The *gemara* suggests: derive by contrast from the *eshet ish* case, which establishes *chenek* as the meaning of *mot yumat* in its non-*sekilah* application. This too can be absorbed.

The *gemara* ultimately identifies the core problem. If *mot yumasu* were the *sekilah* phrase, and we tried to limit its *sekilah* meaning only to some cases by learning the others out from lenient precedents, we would face an impossible situation: either every case with *mot yumasu* gets *sekilah*, or every case gets *chenek* by analogy to the lenient comparator. There is no stable middle ground. The only clean solution is that *mot yumasu* does not carry *sekilah* at all — it carries the broader execution license — and *demeihem bam* is the sole marker of *sekilah*. That phrase appears only in a narrow, consistent set of cases, all of which do in fact receive *sekilah*, and its distinctiveness — including the vivid image of blood flowing from stoning — makes it the natural and unambiguous indicator.

## Sekilah Categories

### Full List

The *mishna* now presents the full list of those who receive *sekilah*. The cases fall into several recognizable clusters, though the *mishna*'s ordering passes between them twice in a way that is somewhat irregular.

The first cluster is sexual transgressions: one who has intercourse with his mother (*haba al ha-eim*), with his father's wife (*eshet ha-av*), with his daughter-in-law (*ha-kallah*), with another male (*ha-zachar*), or with an animal (*ha-beheimah*); and a woman who brings an animal upon herself. The second cluster is offenses against God: the blasphemer (*ha-migadef*), the idolator (*oveid avodah zarah*), one who gives his offspring to *Molech* — listed separately from idolatry, suggesting it is treated as its own category — and the practitioner of *ov ve-yidoni*, whose verse is the very paradigm case. Also in this cluster: *mechalel Shabbat*, the Sabbath violator, whose gravity is comparable to idolatry. The third cluster is offenses against parents: *ha-mekalel aviv ve-imo*, cursing one's father or mother, and the *ben sorer u-moreh*, the wayward and rebellious son.

Then the *mishna* adds the *na'arah me'orasah* and the cases involving *masit* and *madiach* — those who seduce an individual or an entire city into idolatry — and the *mechashef*, the sorcerer. The *mishna* appears to pass through its thematic clusters twice, a structural feature that invites further analysis.

### Cursing Parents

Rashi notes an implicit difficulty: why does *mekalel aviv ve-imo* — cursing one's parents — receive *sekilah*, the weightiest execution, while *makeh aviv ve-imo* — *striking* one's parents, seemingly a graver physical act — receives *chenek*, a lighter execution? Rashi explains: *hichmir mibemakah mishum de'ika tarti* — the cursing is treated more severely because it involves two elements simultaneously. First, *kalon aviv ve-imo* — a disgracing of the parents' honor. Second, *umotzi shem shamayim l'vatalah* — the invocation and misuse of God's name, since one who curses a parent does so by invoking a divine curse, making God's name complicit in this desecration of parental honor. The comparison is apt: just as *birkat Hashem* — cursing God — represents the apex of religious offense, cursing parents with God's name approaches that same register. The double gravity — dishonoring parents who represent divine authority, and doing so through God's name — explains the elevated punishment.

### Mother Case

The *mishna* then examines the case of intercourse with one's mother in detail: *haba al ha-em chayav aleiha mishum em u-mishum eshet ha-av* — if the mother is also the father's wife, the son is liable for two distinct prohibitions simultaneously: the prohibition of the mother and the prohibition of the father's wife. These are recognized as genuinely independent prohibitions, since each can exist without the other: one can have a stepmother who is not one's mother, or a mother who was never married to the father. When a single woman instantiates both, both prohibitions are violated. The practical significance includes: if the warning (*hasra'ah*) addressed only one of the two prohibitions, that is sufficient for the relevant execution; and if the act were done unintentionally, two separate *korbanot* would be required.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *eino chayav ela mishum ha-em bilvad* — one is liable only for the mother category, not for the father's wife category. For Rabbi Yehuda, the term *eshet av* — father's wife — is definitionally limited to a woman who is *not* the man's mother. It refers exclusively to a stepmother. A biological mother, in Rabbi Yehuda's view, simply does not fall within the *eshet av* prohibition, even when she was in fact married to the father.

## Braita Debate

### Braita Version

A *braita* now presents Rabbi Yehuda's position in what appears to be a more restricted form. Rather than the sweeping claim of the *mishna* — that a mother is categorically excluded from *eshet av* — the *braita*'s Rabbi Yehuda says: *im lo hayesa imo reuia le-aviv eino chayav ela mishum ha-em bilvad* — if the mother was not fit for the father, the son is liable only for the mother category. The *braita* thus suggests Rabbi Yehuda's position applies only in cases where the mother was somehow unfit for the father's marriage; in normal cases, presumably, Rabbi Yehuda would agree that both categories apply.

### Not Fit

The *gemara* asks: *mai eina reuia lo* — what does "not fit" mean? The first possibility: she was forbidden to him through *chayavei krisos* or *chayavei misos beis din* — relatives or others whose marriage is biblically invalid at the most serious level. But this cannot be the case, because in such a situation *kiddushin* would not take effect at all — *harei leis kiddushin begavah* — and the woman would simply not be the father's wife in any sense. The *chachamim* surely would not need to argue that both prohibitions apply in a case where the marriage was never binding. Both sides of the debate would agree she is not the *eshet av* in that scenario.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela chayavei lavin* — the debate concerns prohibitions involving only a negative commandment. The father married a woman who was forbidden to him by a *lav* alone — for example, a *mamzeres* — a marriage that went through formal ceremonies but that some hold is not fully binding at the biblical level.

### Rabbi Akiva

The connection becomes clear: *Rabbi Yehuda savar lah ke-Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Yehuda holds like Rabbi Akiva, who rules *ein kiddushin tofsin bechayavei lavin* — *kiddushin* does not take effect even with women forbidden by a simple negative prohibition. If Rabbi Akiva's position is that such a marriage is not binding, then the woman in question was never legally the father's wife, and therefore cannot be in the *eshet av* category. Rabbi Yehuda's position in the *braita* is thus not really a dispute about the *eshet av* prohibition per se; it is a dispute about when *kiddushin* is valid.

### Rav Oshaia

Rav Oshaia challenges this interpretation. If Rabbi Yehuda truly holds like Rabbi Akiva that *kiddushin* is not binding in cases of *chayavei lavin*, there is a significant practical consequence: women in that category would not require *chalitza* and would not be eligible for *yibum*. They would be completely outside the framework of *yibum* entirely, just as women forbidden with *karet* are. Yet a *mishna* teaches: *issur mitzvah ve-issur kedusha, choletzes velo misyabemes* — women in the lighter prohibition categories must perform *chalitza* but may not do *yibum*. These women — including, for example, an *almanah* to a *kohen gadol*, or a *gerushah* to a regular *kohen*, both cases of negative prohibitions — are still within the *yibum* framework sufficiently to require *chalitza*. If *kiddushin* were not binding in their cases, they would be wholly outside the framework and *chalitza* would not apply.

### Terminology

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *le-divrav de-Tanna Kama ka-amar ve-lav sevira lei* — Rabbi Yehuda is speaking according to the first *tanna*'s framework, not expressing his own view. Rabbi Yehuda's actual dispute with the *mishna* is purely terminological. He agrees with the *tanna kama* that these women require *chalitza* but not *yibum*; he disagrees only about what the two categories should be *called*. The *mishna* calls the rabbinic-prohibition category *issur mitzvah* and the *kehuna*-based prohibition category *issur kedusha*. Rabbi Yehuda reverses the labels, calling the *kehuna* category *issur mitzvah* (since the *kohen* is commanded in holiness) and the rabbinic category *issur kedusha* (since the rabbinic enactments extend biblical *kedusha*). He agrees entirely with the underlying *halacha* — he simply contests the nomenclature. Whether the *halacha* reflects his own view or he is merely noting what *the other view* would call these categories is left as a curiosity, but the practical dispute about *chalitza* dissolves entirely.

## Mother Exception

### Rav Yitzchak

The *gemara* now sets aside the *braita*'s more restricted debate and returns to the plain reading of the *mishna* — that Rabbi Yehuda's position is a categorical one. When Rav Yitzchak came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he brought with him a *braita* that matches the *mishna*'s formulation: *Rabbi Yehuda omer eino chayav ela mishum ha-em bilvad* — in any case where the woman is the man's mother, Rabbi Yehuda holds the liability is for *mother* only, not for *eshet av*. This is not a limited ruling about marriages that were unfit; it is the general position that a biological mother is categorically excluded from the *eshet av* prohibition.

### Imcha Hi

Abbaye provides the scriptural basis for this view. The verse states: *imcha hi, lo segaleh ervatah* — she is your mother; do not uncover her nakedness (*Vayikra* 18). The emphasis of the phrase *imcha hi* — she is your mother — operates as a halakhic limitation: *mishum ima atah mechayvo ve-ei atah mechayvo mishum eshes av* — the verse defines her exclusively through the lens of motherhood. Once the Torah says *imcha hi*, it assigns her entirely and solely to the mother category. No other prohibition applies to her through this relationship. The *eshet av* prohibition, which the Torah establishes elsewhere as a separate category, simply does not extend to cover someone who is first and foremost defined as *imcha* — your mother.

This will serve as the foundation for Rabbi Yehuda's position, and the *gemara* will continue to develop the implications and alternative explanations in subsequent discussion.
