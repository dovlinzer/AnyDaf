# Bava Metzia 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Eliezer vs. R. Yochan…

### Ribbis vs. Gezel

The daf opens in the midst of a debate concerning *ribbis d'oraisa* — biblical-level interest — and its relationship to *gezel*, outright theft. The point of departure is a shared assumption: money taken as *ribbis* is not treated like stolen property, and therefore does not require restitution when it passes to the original lender's heirs. The question is whether the lender himself bears an obligation to return it.

*Rabbi Eliezer* holds that *ribbis d'oraisa* must be returned by the person who took it, while *Rabbi Yochanan* maintains that even *ribbis d'oraisa* carries no restitution obligation, even for the lender himself. The underlying logic reflects a deeper disagreement about the nature of the transgression. For *Rabbi Eliezer*, the obligation to return derives from the verse *v'chai achicha imach* — the mandate to sustain one's brother — which speaks to the corrupted relationship between lender and borrower rather than to a theft of property. The money, after all, was transferred by contractual agreement, not seized against the borrower's will.

*Rabbi Yochanan*, by contrast, reasons that because *ribbis* is not simply about the money that changes hands but about the corruption of the power dynamic itself — something that cannot be undone — there is no mechanism of restitution that would be meaningful. What all opinions agree upon is that *ribbis d'rabbanan*, referred to as *avak ribbis*, carries no restitution obligation whatsoever. The Gemara is candid about the practical danger this creates: where there is no consequence, the temptation to transgress is considerable, and as this entire *perek* makes clear, *Chazal* were acutely aware that many people were in practice lending with *ribbis* — at minimum at the *d'rabbanan* level.

### Mishnah: Children & Ribb…

The Gemara now introduces a *mishnah* from Bava Kamma as a challenge. The *mishnah* states: if a father leaves behind *ma'ot shel ribbis* — money that derived from lending with interest — the children, even knowing the money's origin, are not obligated to return it. The Gemara immediately draws an inference: *ha-avihan chayav lehachzir* — the implication is that the father himself would have been obligated to return it. This appears to support *Rabbi Eliezer* against *Rabbi Yochanan*.

### Gemara Clarification

The Gemara resists this inference. In truth, the father too would not have been obligated to return mere money received as *ribbis* — even according to *Rabbi Eliezer*, the case may be more nuanced. The reason the *mishnah* focuses on the children is structural: the *mishnah* wishes to teach the *seifa*, the latter clause, which states that if the father left behind a cow, a *talis*, or any *davar mesuyam* — a specifically identifiable object — the children would be obligated to return it, for the sake of their father's honor. To introduce this case, the *mishnah* naturally frames the *reisha* in terms of the children as well, even though the point is not to imply that the father would have been obligated in the case of mere money. The *mishnah*'s thrust is to define the circumstance in which even the children would be compelled to return something: not money, which is fungible and untraceable, but a specific identifiable object, which would remain visibly associated with the father's transgression and thereby impugn his honor.

### Father's Honor Question

This reading, however, prompts a striking question within the *mishnah* itself. The obligation to return the specific object is said to rest on *mipnei kevod avihem* — for the sake of their father's honor. But there is a *drashah* on the verse *v'nasi b'amcha lo ta'or* — do not curse a leader among your people — which derives from the phrase *b'amcha*, specifically *b'oseh ma'aseh amcha*: the obligation to honor a person applies only when that person conducts himself as a member of the community in good standing. If the father was a *rasha* — and lending with *ribbis* makes him precisely that — why should the children be obligated to protect his honor at all? This question touches on a broader issue of how extensively one transgression defines a person's status as *lo oseh ma'aseh amcha*, but the Gemara raises it here as a genuine textual difficulty.

### Teshuvah Resolution

The Gemara resolves the question by citing a principle transmitted by *Rav Pinchas* in the name of *Rava*, originally stated in a different context: the *mishnah* is dealing with a case where the father performed *teshuvah* before his death. Having repented, he is once again within the category of *oseh ma'aseh amcha*, and his honor is therefore worth protecting. The Gemara then asks a follow-up: if he did *teshuvah*, why does the specific object still remain in his possession? The answer is *shelo hispik lehachzir ad shemet* — he did not have the opportunity to return it before he died.

## Thieves & Ribbis

### Baraita Citation

The Gemara now presents a fresh challenge. A *baraita* states: *hagazlanim u-malvei ribbis af al pi shegavu machzirin* — thieves and those who lend with *ribbis*, even after they have already collected, must return what they took. Before addressing the *machzirin* question — which bears directly on the *Rabbi Eliezer*–*Rabbi Yochanan* debate — the Gemara first examines the opening term.

### Parsing 'Gazlanim'

The Gemara asks: what does it mean for a *gazlan* — a thief — that the clause qualifies him with "even after he has collected"? With a thief, once he has stolen, the act is complete; and before he steals, you cannot yet call him a *gazlan*. The phrase *af al pi shegavu* makes no natural sense in the context of theft. The Gemara therefore resolves: read *gazlanim* here as referring to *malvei ribbis* — those who lend with *ribbis* are called *gazlanim* by the *baraita*. The full statement then reads: people who lend with *ribbis* — whom we call *gazlanim* — even after they have collected their loans, must return the interest. The identification of lenders of *ribbis* with *gazlanim* is suggestive and deliberately provocative, but as the Gemara has already established, it is not a precise equivalence: the children of such a lender do not bear the same restitution obligation that would apply in a genuine case of *gezel*. They constitute a distinct and carefully bounded category.

### Tannaim Dispute

This *baraita*, which demands return of *ribbis*, appears to contradict *Rabbi Yochanan*. *Rabbi Yochanan* responds that this reflects only one *tannaitic* position; other *tannaim* agree with him. He invokes a *baraita* in which *Rav Nechemia* and *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* exempt the lender and the guarantor from punishment *mipnei sheyesh bahen kum aseh* — because a positive commandment is available to them. The language of *potrin*, exemption, and *kum aseh*, positive commandment, signals that the discussion here is about *malkos* — lashes — rather than monetary restitution. This shift itself is significant. By *gezel*, one does not ordinarily receive *malkos* because the transgression belongs to the category of *davar shebemamon*, a monetary matter capable of financial remedy. The fact that *ribbis* does carry a potential for *malkos* reflects that its prohibition is not purely civil in nature.

Since *Rav Nechemia* and *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* are explicitly exempting the lender, there must be an implicit *tanna kamma* who holds the opposite: the lender is not exempt, meaning there is no *kum aseh* available to him. The Gemara identifies this as the position that there is no *mitzvah* to return the *ribbis*, and consequently no *kum aseh* to speak of — and therefore *malkos* would apply. The competing position of *Rav Nechemia* and *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* is that there is a positive commandment, which functions as a *kum aseh* analogous to the release of the mother bird after one has wrongly taken her together with her young: not a monetary remedy, but a positive act of rectification, which precludes *malkos*.

### Qum Aseh Meaning

The Gemara now asks: what is this *kum aseh*? The initial instinct is that it refers to returning the *ribbis* itself — that when the Gemara says *kum ahadori*, it means the requirement to give the money back, and that this positive commandment preempts the *malkos*. If that were so, the *tanna kamma* who exempts would be teaching that there is no obligation to return the *ribbis*. This reading, in turn, would establish the debate as a *tannaim* dispute squarely on *Rabbi Yochanan*'s question, providing him a *tanna* who agrees with his position.

However — and here the Gemara pivots in a direction that *Tosafot* reads carefully — the Gemara rejects this interpretation of *kum aseh*. The *kum aseh* does not refer to returning the collected interest; it refers to *likroa shtar* — tearing up the loan document. The transgression at issue is not the act of collection but the *placement* of the loan itself, the act of *lo tesimun alav neshech*. Once the *shtar* is established with its interest terms, a prohibition has been violated. The *kum aseh* is that one can tear up the *shtar* and thereby remove the debt, which constitutes a positive act that, in principle, could preclude *malkos*.

The Gemara then probes this reading: if the *shtar* has not yet been used to collect, what exactly is the problem? If one holds that a *shtar ha-omed ligvos k'gavuy dami* — a document poised for collection is legally equivalent to money already collected — then the violation has already been fully actualized, and tearing it up after the fact does not undo the completed transgression. Conversely, if the document is not treated as equivalent to collected funds, then prior to actual collection nothing has yet been violated. The Gemara resolves this by clarifying: we are dealing with the view that *shtar ha-omed ligvos lav k'gavuy dami* — the document is not treated as though the money were already collected. And what the teaching comes to establish is *de-shuma milta hi* — that the very act of placing the loan, the *shimah*, is itself a substantive prohibition under *lo tesimun alav neshech*, independent of collection. And that prohibition, created by the placing of the loan, can be at least partially addressed by tearing up the *shtar*.

The important consequence of this reading is that *Rabbi Yochanan* has not, in the end, found a *tanna* who clearly agrees with his position that there is no obligation to return *ribbis d'oraisa*. The debate within the *tannaim* here is about *malkos* and the mechanism of *kum aseh* — not about whether collected interest must be returned.

### Who Transgresses

The Gemara clinches this point by turning to the *mishnah* at the end of the *perek*, which enumerates those who transgress: *ha-malveh v'ha-loveh, ha-arev v'ha-edim* — the lender, the borrower, the guarantor, and the witnesses. The inclusion of witnesses is telling. If the transgression were located in the act of collection, the witnesses — who signed the document but play no role in the eventual payment — would have nothing to do with it. The fact that witnesses are included in the prohibition demonstrates definitively that *de-shuma milta hi* — the placing of the loan itself is the act of transgression. This the Gemara accepts: *shma minah*.

The broader picture that emerges is that *ribbis* constitutes a unique category. It is not *gezel*, yet there appears to be an obligation — at least on the person himself, at the *d'oraisa* level — to make restitution. The halakha ultimately follows the position of *Rabbi Eliezer* that *ribbis ketzutza* — fixed, contractual interest — is *yotzei bedayanim*, subject to judicial extraction. And crucially, the prohibition attaches even to the moment of placing the loan, not only to collection.

## Ribbis Classification

### Rav Safra's Test

The Gemara now turns to a practical principle articulated by *Rav Safra* for distinguishing *ribbis d'oraisa* from *ribbis d'rabbanan*. *Rav Safra* formulates it as follows: any arrangement that, if brought before a non-Jewish court, that court would enforce — compelling the *loveh* to pay the *malveh* — is *d'oraisa ribbis*, and therefore *bidineinu machzirin mimalveh l'loveh*: in Jewish law we would extract that money back from the lender and return it to the borrower. Conversely, anything a secular court would not enforce — because it lacks the character of a binding contractual obligation — is not *d'oraisa*, and we would not compel its return. The principle elegantly bypasses the need to invoke the terms *ribbis ketzutza* or *ribbis d'oraisa* directly: the test case is contractual enforceability in secular law.

### Sa'ah B'Sa'ah

*Abaye* challenges this principle immediately. He raises the case of *sa'ah b'sa'ah* — lending a measure of food and receiving back the same measure — and points out that in secular courts such an agreement would indeed be enforceable: if one borrowed a quantity of grain, a court would compel repayment of that same quantity. Yet *sa'ah b'sa'ah* is universally recognized as only *ribbis d'rabbanan*, and we do not compel return of the excess value. According to *Rav Safra*'s test, it ought to be treated as *d'oraisa*.

### Sa'ah Explanation

The classic case is familiar: a person borrows two eggs from a neighbor when eggs cost a certain amount, and returns two eggs later when the price has risen. The borrower has returned the same quantity, but the repayment is worth more in monetary terms. There is, moreover, no explicit contractual agreement to pay more; prices could equally have fallen. Nevertheless, *Chazal* prohibited the practice. Yet it is clearly *d'rabbanan*, not *d'oraisa*. If a secular court would enforce return of the same quantity of a fungible commodity, then by *Rav Safra*'s formulation, this should qualify as *d'oraisa ribbis* — but it does not.

### Pikadon Response

*Rav Safra* responds that the reason *sa'ah b'sa'ah* does not fall under his principle is that secular courts do not treat such a transaction as a loan at all. Rather, *inhu b'toras pikadon asa l'yadei* — they regard the transaction as though the object were given as a *pikadon*, a deposit, and returned in kind. Because it is not framed as interest accruing on a loan but as the return of the same commodity, it is not identified as interest in the secular legal framework, and therefore it lies outside *Rav Safra*'s test case.

### Mashkanta Example

*Abaye* then raises a second counterexample: *mashkanta b'lo nechiyata* — a mortgage arrangement in which the lender harvests the produce of the borrower's field as collateral without deducting the value from the principal of the loan. This is a well-recognized form of *ribbis*: the lender gains the benefit of the field's produce while the full loan amount remains outstanding, creating a clear financial advantage flowing from the loan. And yet this case too is classified as only *ribbis d'rabbanan*. In secular law, such a mortgage arrangement — where the lender has contractual rights to the field's use — would be fully enforceable. The borrower would have no legal recourse to demand that the produce value be credited against the loan. By *Rav Safra*'s test, this should be *d'oraisa* — yet it is not.

### Liens Response

*Rav Safra* responds: secular courts do not characterize the lender's right to harvest the field as *interest* on the loan. Rather, *inhu b'toras zivini ata l'yadei* — they treat the liens created by the mortgage as conferring a form of partial ownership on the lender. Because the lender has quasi-ownership rights over the field derived from the liens themselves, the benefit he derives from working the field is understood as flowing from ownership, not from the loan. It is therefore not categorized as interest in secular law, and again falls outside *Rav Safra*'s formulation. One might reasonably question what content remains in the principle after these carve-outs, and the Gemara itself raises exactly this point.

### Refined Principle

The Gemara asks: after accommodating *sa'ah b'sa'ah* and *mashkanta b'lo nechiyata* with special explanations, what is *Rav Safra* actually adding beyond what we already knew from the terms *ribbis ketzutza* and *ribbis d'oraisa*? The answer the Gemara offers is twofold. In the affirmative direction, *Rav Safra* is confirming the ruling of *Rabbi Eliezer* that *ribbis ketzutza* — a straightforward fixed-interest arrangement, where one borrows a sum and contractually agrees to repay a larger sum — is *yotzei bedayanim*, extractable by a Jewish court, in accordance with the halakha as we rule it. In the negative direction, *Rav Safra* is informing us of a category of cases that, while they share certain surface features with enforceable agreements, are nonetheless only *ribbis d'rabbanan* and would not be subject to judicial extraction.

### Mukdemet/Me'ucheret

The specific cases that *Rav Safra*'s negative principle captures are *ribbis mukdemes* and *ribbis me'ucheres* — early interest and late interest. These refer to situations where the additional payment is not embedded in a contractual obligation. A borrower who gives the lender a gift before the loan is extended in order to secure the loan, or one who gives a gift after repayment as a gesture of gratitude — these are forms of *ribbis* that involve a real transfer of value connected to the loan, but without a binding contractual obligation to pay more. Because there is no contractual obligation, a secular court would not enforce payment, and therefore *Rav Safra*'s principle correctly places them in the category of *ribbis d'rabbanan*, for which there is no judicial return.

## Grain/Wine Case

### Case Setup

The Gemara now turns to a *mishnah* that presents a more complex set of cases involving deferred delivery of commodities. The scenario begins with an agreement to purchase a *kor* of grain at the harvest price — twenty-five silver *dinarim*, which is one gold *dinar* — for delivery at a later time, when the price may be higher. This initial arrangement is characterized as permissible. Since the buyer has paid the money at the time the market price was established and the commodity was readily available, the transaction has the character of a purchase rather than a loan. The buyer effectively already owns the grain; the seller is merely delayed in his delivery.

### Market Price Set

The *mishnah* then cites a *baraita* that articulates the governing principle: *ein poskin al peirot ad she-yatzei ha-sha'ar* — one may not commit to delivering produce at a future time until the market price has been established. But once the market price has been set — *yatza ha-sha'ar poskin af al pi she-ein lo, yesh lo* — once the price is public and fixed, one may enter into such an arrangement even if the seller does not personally possess the commodity, provided others in the market do. The rationale is that the transaction takes on the character of an immediate purchase: since there is a fixed price and the commodity is freely available, the money given is equivalent to a purchase already made, and later delivery is merely logistical.

### Wine Conversion

The complication in the *mishnah* arises when the seller, having undertaken to deliver a *kor* of grain worth thirty *dinarim* by the time delivery is due, now proposes instead to deliver wine — because the buyer wishes to use the proceeds to purchase wine. The seller says: rather than owing you thirty *dinarim* in grain, I will owe you thirty *dinarim* worth of wine. The *mishnah* identifies this as a problem specifically because the seller does not have wine. If he had wine, it would replicate the permitted case of grain: the commodity is in his possession, delivery is simply deferred, and no issue arises. But because he lacks the wine, he is in effect taking on a new obligation — to deliver wine worth thirty *dinarim* at some future point — when the wine may by then be worth fifty *dinarim*. This looks like borrowing thirty and returning fifty, which is *ribbis*.

### Rava's Reading

*Rava* proposes a reading of the *mishnah* that addresses the difficulty. He argues that the crucial distinction is not between having and not having the wine, but between a transaction in which money actually changed hands and one in which it did not. The *mishnah*'s case, *Rava* suggests, is one of *ba lachov be-dameha* — a debt being renegotiated without any money changing hands. In the permitted case, the buyer gave twenty-five silver *dinarim* at the outset; having cash in hand allows the seller, at any moment, to purchase the commodity from a neighbor and satisfy the obligation. The transaction is therefore purchase-like from the beginning. But in the *mishnah*'s case, no money was given: the seller simply owes a debt for the wheat, proposes to pay it in wine, and has neither the wine nor the funds to acquire it. The *mishnah*'s restriction is thus: *matnitin be-va lachov be-dameha askinan* — our *mishnah* deals with a case where the debt is being renegotiated without the exchange of actual payment.

*Rava* supports this reading from a *baraita*: if someone owed a creditor money, and the creditor came to the threshing floor and asked for repayment in grain at the current market price, and the debtor agreed to provide a certain quantity of grain over the following months — that arrangement is *assur*, forbidden. The reason: *d'lav ke-issara de-ati l'yadei dami* — it is not comparable to a case where a coin came into the seller's hand. The cash transaction creates the legal fiction of an immediate purchase; the absence of cash means one is dealing with a debt being paid in commodity at a later date, which edges into *ribbis*. The *baraita* confirms that without money in hand, the arrangement is impermissible even when the seller possesses the produce.

## Ha'aramas Ribbis

### Ha'aramas Concept

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* taught by *Rav Safra* drawn from the *ribbis* teachings of *Rabbi Chiya*: *yesh devarim she-heim muttarim ve-asurim mipnei ha'aramas ribbis* — there are things that are technically permissible and yet are forbidden because they constitute *ha'aramas ribbis*, a deceptive circumvention of the *ribbis* prohibition. This is a distinct category from *avak ribbis*. Where *avak ribbis* involves acts that are intrinsically problematic, *ha'aramas ribbis* describes arrangements in which each individual step is technically lawful but the overall design is a transparent maneuver to achieve the functional equivalent of interest-bearing lending.

---

## Abaye's Reading

### Abaye Proposal

*Abaye* proposes that this *baraita* about *ha'aramas ribbis* is precisely what the *mishnah*'s grain-wine case is illustrating. The *mishnah*, on *Abaye*'s reading, is not a case about deferred delivery at all, but rather an instance of the prohibited *ha'aramas ribbis* pattern described in *Rav Safra*'s teaching — a transaction that is constructed to look like ordinary commerce but is in practice a loan with disguised interest.

---

## Abaye's Reading…

### Sell/Buy Scenario

*Abaye* elaborates his reading with a concrete scenario. *Shimon* says to *Reuven*: I want to lend you money, but I don't have cash on hand. I do, however, have wheat worth thirty *dinarim*; I'll give you the wheat and you'll owe me thirty *dinarim*. *Shimon* gives *Reuven* the wheat. Then, immediately, *Shimon* says: look, I've just found that I have twenty-five *dinarim* in my pocket — will you sell me back that wheat for twenty-five? *Reuven* agrees, sells back the wheat for twenty-five *dinarim*, and now has twenty-five *dinarim* in cash — which was what he wanted from the beginning. But he owes *Shimon* thirty *dinarim*.

### Net Result

The net economic effect is transparent: *Shimon* gave *Reuven* twenty-five *dinarim* in cash, and *Reuven* will pay back thirty *dinarim*. No single step was formally a *ribbis* transaction — the wheat was given and sold back at market prices — but the ensemble is functionally a loan of twenty-five repaid as thirty. This is the *ha'aramas ribbis* that *Rav Safra*'s *baraita* prohibits: technically permissible in each component, but forbidden as a whole because it is clearly designed to produce the economic outcome of interest-bearing lending.

---

## Ha'aramas Ribbis…

### Lend & Buy Back

The *baraita* itself presents a parallel case. *Shimon* says to *Reuven*: I don't have money, but I have wheat worth twenty-five *dinarim* — I'll give it to you and you'll owe me twenty-five. He gives the wheat. Then *Shimon* returns and offers to buy the wheat back from *Reuven* for twenty-four *dinarim*. *Reuven* sells it back.

### Analysis Result

The question is how to characterize the transaction. Whether one views *Shimon* as having lent the wheat or sold the wheat, *Reuven* now holds twenty-four *dinarim* in cash and owes *Shimon* twenty-five *dinarim*. The sum given and the sum owed are effectively twenty-four and twenty-five respectively, a difference of one *dinar*.

### Produce vs. Cash

The *baraita* then distinguishes between two possible outcomes. If *Reuven* repays the debt in produce — in wine, for instance — the arrangement is permitted. The involvement of a commodity on the repayment side breaks the visual appearance of a pure cash loan with interest and is sufficiently removed from the *ribbis* pattern that *Chazal* allowed it. But if *Reuven* repays in cash — paying back twenty-five *dinarim* where twenty-four were received — then it is forbidden as *ha'aramas ribbis*, because the net result is a straightforward cash loan with interest, even if each individual step was technically unobjectionable.

---

## Rava's Objection

### Language Mismatch

*Rava* raises a sharp objection to *Abaye*'s proposed reading of the *mishnah*. The language of the *mishnah* does not match the scenario *Abaye* describes. The *mishnah* has the buyer saying *ten li chitai* — give me my grain — as though demanding delivery of something already owed to him from a prior purchase. But in *Abaye*'s scenario, the buyer would naturally say *ten li demai chitai* — give me the money for the grain — because on *Abaye*'s reading the transaction was a loan of wheat that was immediately bought back, and what is owed is money, not grain.

*Abaye* suggests emending the *mishnah* to read *ten li demai chitai*, but *Rava* continues pressing. The *mishnah* also uses the phrase *shani mocharan* — that I am selling them — whereas on *Abaye*'s scenario it should read *she-machartim lach*, that I sold them to you. *Abaye* accepts this emendation as well.

### Debt Amount

*Rava* then identifies a further problem. The *mishnah* states that the grain debt becomes thirty *dinarim* at the point of the conversion to wine — the thirty emerges from the subsequent appreciation. But on *Abaye*'s reading, the thirty *dinarim* was the original amount of the transaction from the beginning: *Shimon* gave wheat worth thirty and *Reuven* owed thirty from the outset, and the twenty-five was what he received when selling the wheat back. The *mishnah*'s framing — that thirty is what the debt becomes, not what it was initially — cannot be reconciled with *Abaye*'s scenario.

*Abaye* responds that the *mishnah* intends: give me the money for the grain according to the thirty *dinarim* valuation you established it at — *bedamei chitecha she-asita alai b'shloshim dinar* — meaning *Reuven* is referencing the original price at which the wheat was transferred. But even this reinterpretation strains the *mishnah*'s natural language.

### Conclusion

*Rava* concludes that no accumulation of emendations can rescue *Abaye*'s reading. Each adjustment *Abaye* proposes conflicts with some other feature of the *mishnah*'s actual language. The *pshat* of the *mishnah* remains unresolved under *Abaye*'s approach, and his reading of the *mishnah* as a case of *ha'aramas ribbis* cannot be sustained against the text as it stands.

The Gemara thus leaves us at an impasse. *Rava*'s initial reading — that the *mishnah* deals with a case where no money changed hands, making the arrangement a debt renegotiated into commodity form rather than a purchase — is textually closer to the *pshat*. But *Abaye* had pointed out that even on that reading, a further difficulty arises: if the problem is the absence of money in hand, then it should be a problem even when the seller does possess the produce, yet the *mishnah* implies the opposite. The Gemara's resolution, which will turn on a second *baraita* permitting the arrangement when the seller actually has the produce, will be taken up in the next session.
