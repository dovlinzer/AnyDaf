# Eiruvin 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv in Tree Basics

### Tree Placement Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נְתָנוֹ בְּאִילָן, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. נְתָנוֹ בְּבוֹר, אֲפִילּוּ עָמוֹק מֵאָה אַמָּה — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one placed** his *eiruv* **in a tree above ten handbreadths** from the ground, **his *eiruv* is not a** valid ***eiruv*;** if it is **below ten handbreadths, his *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv*.** If **he placed** the *eiruv* **in a pit, even** if **it was a hundred cubits deep, his *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv*.**

The *mishnah* under discussion presents the case of placing an *eruv* in a tree. The question is: what happens when the base of the tree stands in a *reshus harabbim*, and the *eruv* is placed in the tree itself? If the branch or platform is higher than ten *tefachim* and is four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* in area, then the tree constitutes a *reshus hayachid*. This creates a fundamental problem: you are standing in a *reshus harabbim* while your *eruv* is in a *reshus hayachid*, meaning you and your *eruv* are not in the same halachic space. For an *eruv techumin* to function, you must be able to access your *eruv*.

Compounding this difficulty is the rabbinic prohibition against using a tree on *Shabbos* — the tree is *mechubbar lekarka*, attached to the ground, and therefore forbidden to handle. This prohibition, however, is only rabbinic in nature. The entire discussion that follows proceeds according to the position of Rebbi, who holds that rabbinic violations are not a concern during *bein hashmashos*, the twilight period when the *eruv* takes effect.

### Two Domains Problem

The core tension is between two categories of domain. When your *eruv* sits in a tree more than ten *tefachim* off the ground on a platform of four by four *tefachim*, it occupies a *reshus hayachid*, while the person seeking to rely on it stands in a *reshus harabbim*. Moving an object between these two domains constitutes a biblical violation. Even granting Rebbi's leniency regarding rabbinic prohibitions at *bein hashmashos*, a biblical obstacle cannot be waived in this manner.

### Rava's Four Amot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּאִילָן הָעוֹמֵד חוּץ לְעִיבּוּרָהּ שֶׁל עִיר, אֲבָל אִילָן הָעוֹמֵד בְּתוֹךְ עִיבּוּרָהּ שֶׁל עִיר — אֲפִילּוּ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב, דְּמָתָא כְּמַאן דְּמַלְיָא דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in continuation of this discussion: **They only taught** this law **with regard to a tree that stands beyond the outskirts of the city,** i.e., outside a radius of seventy and two-thirds cubits around the city. **However,** with regard to **a tree that stands within the outskirts of the city, even** if the *eiruv* was placed **above ten** handbreadths, **it is a** valid ***eiruv*, as the city is considered as though it were filled in** with earth, so that anything located at any height within the town itself or its outskirts is regarded as being in the same domain. Even though the person intended to establish his Shabbat residence below the tree in the public domain, we view the ground as raised to the height of the *eiruv*, and his *eiruv* is therefore valid even though he cannot actually remove it from the tree during the twilight period.

Into this difficulty steps Rava, whose principle is introduced at the bottom of 32b. Rava states: *lo shanu ella be-ilan ha-omed chutz le-ibura shel ir* — this restriction applies only when the tree stands outside the *ibura shel ir*, the environs of the city. But if the tree stands within the *ibura shel ir*, then even if it is higher than ten *tefachim*, the *eruv* is valid, as the *gemara* states: *demasa keman demalya damya* — a city is considered as if it were filled. This echoes the earlier principle of *beisa keman demalya damya* — a house is considered as if it is filled — which we encountered at the beginning of *maseches Shabbos*.

### Ibura Shel Ir

The *ibura shel ir* is the halachic concept that the outer boundary of a city extends an additional 70⅔ *amos* beyond its outermost structures. This figure, equivalent to the square root of 5000 and connected to the dimensions of a *beis se'ah*, represents the zone within which all houses are treated as continuous with the city itself. Within this zone, any two locations are treated as part of one unified space for purposes of *hilchos eruv*.

Rashi explains why one might even need to place an *eruv techumin* within this zone. If a person's city is at point A and another city is at point B, and the two cities are within 2000 *amos* of each other, someone starting *Shabbos* in city A might still lack enough range to reach a desired destination beyond city B. Such a person might place an *eruv* within the *ibura* of city B in order to extend their range. It is in precisely this scenario that Rava's principle applies.

Rava's underlying claim is striking: although the *eruv* may be located in a *reshus hayachid* and the person stands in a *reshus harabbim*, if both are within the *ibura shel ir*, it is as if they occupy the same space. This is not a statement about the normal laws of carrying — one may still not carry from the tree to the street — but rather a conceptual determination that for purposes of the *eruv*, the person and the *eruv* are considered collocated. The city's domains are treated as compressed into a single unified space for *eruv* purposes, much as the city itself is considered to occupy no distance when calculating *techumin*.

## Gemara Questions Rava

### Challenge Formulated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, חוּץ לְעִיבּוּרָהּ שֶׁל עִיר נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הַנּוֹתֵן עֵירוּבוֹ יֵשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, הָוְיָא לַהּ רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — וּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד עוֹלָה עַד לָרָקִיעַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the tree stood **beyond the outskirts of the town,** there should **also** be no difference whether the *eiruv* is above or below the height of ten handbreadths. **Since Rava** himself **said: One who places his *eiruv*** in a particular location **has four cubits** surrounding him that are considered as a private domain, here too, the area should be considered **a private domain; and a private domain rises to the sky.** Since the tree stands within this area, all parts of the tree should be regarded as a private domain regardless of their height.

The *gemara* immediately presses Rava's logic: *ei hachi* — if this is true, if the reason the *eruv* is valid within the *ibura shel ir* is because everything within the city environs is considered one compressed space, then the same reasoning should apply even outside the *ibura shel ir*. Why? Because Rava himself articulates another principle: *hanosen eiruvo yesh lo daled amos* — wherever a person places his *eruv*, he is granted four *amos* in every direction.

### Rava's Defense

This four-*amos* principle means that even if the *eruv* rolled slightly beyond the permitted boundary just before *bein hashmashos* — say, to 2003 *amos* from the city instead of 2000 — Rava would hold that within those four *amos* surrounding the *eruv*, everything is considered the same space, as if the *eruv* has not actually traveled. The four *amos* around the *eruv* are treated like a small enclosed domain: *havei lei reshus hayachid*, and *reshus hayachid ola ad l'rakia* — a *reshus hayachid* extends upward without limit.

### All-or-Nothing Logic

The *gemara* therefore frames a sweeping challenge: given that the four *amos* around the *eruv* are treated as a unified space regardless of domain distinctions, why should it ever matter whether the *eruv* is in a tree that is a *reshus hayachid*? Just as we compress the entire city into one space for *eruv* purposes, and just as we compress the four *amos* around the *eruv* into one space, the *eruv* in the tree should always be considered accessible, wherever it is placed.

This challenge drives the *gemara* toward a highly constrained reading of the *mishnah*. Rav Yitzchak son of Rav Mesharshiya proposes — at the top of 33b — that the *mishnah*'s problematic case involves a tree whose branch extends *outside* the four-*amos* zone: *hacha be-ilan hanoteh chutz le-arba amos askinan*. The branch curves outward and upward, so that the *eruv* placed upon it is situated both more than four *amos* from the person and more than ten *tefachim* above the ground.

## Branch Extension Case

### Rav Yitzchak's Fix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: הָכָא בָּאִילָן הַנּוֹטֶה חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עָסְקִינַן, וְנִתְכַּוֵּין לִשְׁבּוֹת בְּעִקָּרוֹ. וּמַאי ״לְמַעְלָה״ וּמַאי ״לְמַטָּה״ — דַּהֲדַר זָקֵיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Mesharshiya, said: Here, we are dealing with a tree that leans out** horizontally **beyond four cubits** from its trunk, **and he intended to establish his Shabbat residence at its base. And what** is the meaning of the terms **above and below,** as we said that this tree extends horizontally to the side, which indicates that it remains at a uniform height? After the tree leans horizontally beyond four cubits from the place of its roots, **it** rises once **again in an upright** position, and therefore the terms above and below are applicable.

Rav Yitzchak's resolution reframes the *mishnah* entirely. The tree in question is not a straightforward case of an *eruv* placed directly above the person's position. Rather, the branch extends laterally beyond the four-*amos* zone, and then curves upward. The critical variable — whether the *eruv* is above or below ten *tefachim* — is now reread: *u'mai l'ma'ala u'mai l'mata, d'hadar zakif* — what does it mean for the *eruv* to be "above" versus "below" ten? It refers to the point at which the branch bends upward.

### Branch Height Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִי בָּעֵי מַיְיתֵי לַהּ דֶּרֶךְ עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** it true that even if the *eiruv* is above ten handbreadths, **if one wants, he can** remove it from where it was deposited and **bring it by way of** the tree's **leaves,** i.e., its branches that are above ten handbreadths, to within four cubits of the place where he intended to establish his Shabbat residence? Therefore, the *eiruv* should be valid even though it is above ten handbreadths.

The diagram this generates is as follows. The tree has a branch that extends horizontally beyond four *amos*, and then curves upward. The portion of the branch at or below ten *tefachim* off the ground constitutes a *karmelis* — a rabbinically prohibited domain. Carrying from there to the *reshus harabbim* is only a rabbinic violation. Above ten *tefachim*, if the branch is four by four *tefachim*, it becomes a *reshus hayachid*, and transferring from there to the *reshus harabbim* where the person stands is a biblical violation.

So the rule of the *mishnah* can now be stated precisely: if the *eruv* is placed on the branch at or above ten *tefachim*, it sits in a *reshus hayachid*, and since the person is in a *reshus harabbim* and the *eruv* is more than four *amos* away, there is no access without a biblical violation — the *eruv* is invalid. If it is placed below ten *tefachim*, even though the branch extends outside the four-*amos* zone, accessing the *eruv* requires only a rabbinic violation (carrying through a *karmelis*), which is permissible according to Rebbi at *bein hashmashos*.

### Multi-Step Access Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁרַבִּים מְכַתְּפִין עָלָיו, וְכִדְעוּלָּא. דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: עַמּוּד תִּשְׁעָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְרַבִּים מְכַתְּפִין עָלָיו, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: We are dealing with a unique situation **where** the horizontal section of the tree is used by **the masses** to **shoulder** their burdens **on it,** i.e., to temporarily rest their loads on it, so that they can adjust them and easily lift them up again; **and** the *halakha* in that case **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Ulla, as Ulla said:** With regard to **a pillar** that is **nine** handbreadths high and situated **in the public domain, and the masses** use it to **shoulder** their loads upon it, **and someone threw** an object from a private domain **and it came to rest upon it, he is liable,** as this pillar has the status of a public domain. Consequently, in the case of the tree, one may not bring the *eiruv* by way of the tree's branches, as the horizontal section of the tree has the status of a public domain, and one may not carry from one private domain to another via a public domain.

One might object: even if the *eruv* is at the ten-*tefachim* level in a *reshus hayachid*, cannot the person retrieve it in stages without any biblical violation? Tosafot's approach to this question is that one could first move the *eruv* from the upper portion of the branch (the *reshus hayachid*) down to the lower portion of the branch (the *karmelis*), and from there bring it to the *reshus harabbim*. Each individual transfer involves only a rabbinic violation. Rashi's approach similarly allows multiple small movements, each within four *amos*, to accomplish the transfer incrementally.

This is precisely the *gemara*'s internal question: *d'ha i ba'ei meisi lei derech alav* — if one wanted, one could bring it down by moving it across the branch in stages. If this is true, why does placement above ten *tefachim* invalidate the *eruv* at all?

### Tosafot Suspension Q

Tosafot raises an independent question at this point. If the branch is ten *tefachim* off the ground — elevated and suspended — we would normally not treat it as a *reshus hayachid* simply by virtue of its height and dimensions, because a suspended *mechitza* does not typically create a valid enclosed domain. A platform suspended more than three *tefachim* off the ground, through which animals or people can pass beneath, does not generally function as a *reshus hayachid*. Why should such a branch constitute a *reshus hayachid* here?

Tosafot's answer is that the case involves a branch that is itself ten *tefachim* tall — meaning the branch has height in its own right, not merely elevation off the ground. Because the branch itself possesses the requisite height of ten *tefachim* as a structure, it constitutes a *reshus hayachid* in its own right, independently of any consideration about the space beneath it.

## Branch Usage Effect

### Ulla's Platform Rule

The *gemara*'s answer to the multi-step access theory invokes a principle of Ulla. Ulla teaches: *amud tisha bi'reshus harabbim, rabbim mekatfin alav, ve-zorak ve-nach al gabei, chayav* — if there is a platform nine *tefachim* high in a *reshus harabbim*, and the public regularly uses it as a resting place for their burdens, and one throws an object from a *reshus hayachid* that lands on it, one is liable. This is counterintuitive: ordinarily, airspace above three *tefachim* in a *reshus harabbim* is a *karmelis*, not part of the *reshus harabbim* itself. But because this platform is at a usable height and is actively used by the public, it is absorbed into the *reshus harabbim*.

### Public Usage Bars Access

Applying Ulla's principle to the branch: since the lower portion of the branch — the section below ten *tefachim* — is at a height regularly used by the public for resting packages, it too becomes part of the *reshus harabbim*. There is therefore no intermediate zone through which the *eruv* could be brought step by step without a biblical violation. Moving the *eruv* from the *reshus hayachid* (above ten *tefachim*) down to the branch (which is *reshus harabbim* by virtue of public use), and then to the person standing in the *reshus harabbim*, constitutes a direct transfer between *reshus hayachid* and *reshus harabbim* at every stage. There is no *karmelis* stopping point available.

This is why, in this particular configuration, placement above ten *tefachim* is genuinely problematic: every potential intermediate step is itself *reshus harabbim*. Below ten *tefachim*, the *eruv* is no longer in a *reshus hayachid*, and so the transfer problem disappears.

It must be acknowledged that this entire reading of the *mishnah* is, as the *gemara* itself suggests, a *dachak* — a strained construction. *Eichpal tanna lashmi'inan kol hai* — would the *tanna* really go to such lengths to teach us this unusual scenario? The answer, honestly, is that this reading is necessitated entirely by the need to preserve Rava's position. If one were to ask whether this is the straightforward *peshat* of the *mishnah*, the answer is clearly no.

## Rabbi vs. Chachamim

### Baraita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: נְתָנוֹ בְּאִילָן, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, וְאָסוּר לִיטְּלוֹ. בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה — מוּתָּר לִיטְּלוֹ. נְתָנוֹ בְּכַלְכַּלָּה וּתְלָאוֹ בְּאִילָן, אֲפִילּוּ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָסוּר לִיטְּלוֹ — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the source of the disagreement: **As it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **If one placed** his *eiruv* **in a tree above ten handbreadths** from the ground, **his *eiruv* is not** a valid ***eiruv*.** If he placed it **below ten handbreadths, his *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv*, but he is prohibited to take it** on Shabbat in order to eat it because it is prohibited to use the tree on Shabbat. However, if the *eiruv* is **within three** handbreadths of the ground, **he is permitted to take it** because it is considered as though it were on the ground and not in a tree. If **one placed** the *eiruv* **in a basket and hung it on a tree, even above ten handbreadths, his *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis** disagree and **say:** In **any** situation in which the *eiruv* was placed in **a location where it is prohibited to take** it, **his *eiruv* is not** a valid ***eiruv*.**

Having established the framework of Rava's position and the branch case, the *gemara* now asks: what precisely is the dispute between Rebbi and the *Chachamim*? The answer is drawn from a *baraita*: *nasno be-ilan lemala me-asara tefachim ein eruvo eruv* — if one placed the *eruv* in the tree above ten *tefachim*, it is invalid. *Lemata me-asara tefachim eruvo eruv* — below ten *tefachim*, it is valid. However: *ve-asur litlo* — it is forbidden to remove it from the tree on *Shabbos* itself, because doing so would constitute using something *mechubbar lekarka*.

Additionally, if the *eruv* is within three *tefachim* of the ground — even if technically on the tree — it is treated as though it is on the ground, and one may even take it. Within three *tefachim*, the halachic principle of *lavud* treats it as ground-level. Between three and ten *tefachim*, the *eruv* is valid but may not be physically retrieved on *Shabbos*.

### Rabbi Permits Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַסֵּיפָא — לֵימָא קָסָבְרִי רַבָּנַן, צְדָדִין אֲסוּרִין?! אֶלָּא אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: With regard **to which** statement did **the Rabbis state** their opinion? **If you say** they were referring **to the latter clause** with respect to the basket hanging from the tree, **let us say that the Rabbis hold** that using even **the sides** of a tree **is prohibited,** as making use of the basket is considered using the sides of a tree. **Rather,** the Rabbis' statement must refer **to the first clause,** in which Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says that if one put the *eiruv* below ten handbreadths, his *eiruv* is valid, but he is prohibited to move it.

Rebbi's position is that even though one cannot remove the *eruv* from the tree — because doing so would violate the rabbinic prohibition against using *mechubbar lekarka* — the *eruv* nevertheless counts. Rebbi holds that a rabbinic obstacle does not render the *eruv* inaccessible in the relevant halachic sense. An *eruv* that can only be reached by incurring a rabbinic violation is still considered a reachable *eruv*.

Furthermore, regarding the basket: *nasno be-kalkala, afilu lemala me-asara tefachim eruvo eruv* — if the *eruv* was placed in a basket suspended from the tree, even above ten *tefachim*, it is valid. This is Rebbi's position: *divrei Rabbi*.

### Chachamim Require Access

The *Chachamim* disagree: *kol makom she-asur litlo ein eruvo eruv* — wherever it is forbidden to take the *eruv*, it does not count as an *eruv*. The *gemara* clarifies which case the *Chachamim* are disputing. If they are arguing about the basket case specifically, this would imply they hold *tzedadin asurin* — that using an item connected to a tree even indirectly is forbidden. Since we do not want to lock the *Chachamim* into that position, the *gemara* concludes that they are disputing the middle case: the *eruv* placed between three and ten *tefachim*, which may not be removed from the tree. Rebbi says this counts; the *Chachamim* say it does not, because if you cannot take it, it is not considered accessible.

### Generalization to Bein H…

What is remarkable about how the *gemara* processes this dispute is its degree of abstraction. The *baraita* seems, on its face, to be presenting a narrow halachic ruling within *hilchos eruvin*: in this specific scenario, does a rabbinic obstacle prevent the *eruv* from counting? One might have thought this was purely an internal principle of *eruv* law — that within the conceptual framework of *eruvin*, we evaluate accessibility in a particular way.

But the *gemara* generalizes Rebbi's position to a broad halachic principle: according to Rebbi, one may perform rabbinic prohibitions during *bein hashmashos*. This is not merely a rule about what counts as an accessible *eruv*; it is a general ruling that rabbinic *issurim* are suspended during the twilight period. It should be noted that we rule like Rebbi, but with two important qualifications. First, many *poskim* hold that this leniency applies only *l'tzorech mitzvah*, in the context of a mitzvah need such as making an *eruv*. Second, if one has already accepted *Shabbos* through *Kabbalas Shabbos*, or on the outgoing end has not yet made *Havdalah*, the subjective declaration of *Shabbos* or the continuation of *Shabbos* applies, and the leniency does not help.

## Suspended Basket Case

### Why Basket Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: רֵישָׁא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה, סֵיפָא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה וְכַלְכַּלָּה מַשְׁלִימָתוֹ לְאַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: The first clause** is referring to a case **where** the tree **is four** by four handbreadths wide. The *eiruv* is not valid if it was placed above ten handbreadths because the tree at that height constitutes a private domain, and the *eiruv* cannot be brought to the public domain below, where one wishes to establish his Shabbat residence. **The latter clause,** however, is referring to a case **where** the tree **is not four** by four handbreadths wide, **and the basket completes** the width of the tree at that spot **to four.**

The *gemara* now presses a question about the basket case. We have established that when the *eruv* is on a branch more than four *amos* away and above ten *tefachim*, it is in a *reshus hayachid* and inaccessible without a biblical violation. But the *baraita* states that placing the *eruv* in a basket suspended from the tree — even above ten *tefachim* — is valid according to Rebbi. Why? The same problems should apply. The basket above ten *tefachim* would constitute a *reshus hayachid*, and the person stands in a *reshus harabbim*.

The *gemara* first asks the diagnostic question: *hai ilan heichi dami?* What exactly are the dimensions of this tree? If the branch or platform lacks four by four *tefachim*, it is a *makom patur* — an insignificant space — and one may carry freely from it to any domain. If it does have four by four *tefachim*, then it is a *reshus hayachid* and we have the problem. Since the entire discussion presumes a problem exists, we must be dealing with the case where the branch is four by four. Then why does the basket make a difference?

### Ravina's Dimensions

Ravina resolves this by distinguishing the two cases on grounds of dimension. In the problematic case — where the *eruv* on the branch above ten *tefachim* is invalid — the branch itself is four by four *tefachim*, and that is why it constitutes a *reshus hayachid*. In the basket case, however, the branch itself is not four by four — for example, it is four by two — and only together with the basket does the combined space reach four by four *tefachim*: *ve-kalkala mashlimta le-arba* — the basket completes it to four. So in the basket scenario, the branch alone is insufficient to constitute a *reshus hayachid*, and the combined measurement requires a separate legal mechanism to be meaningful.

### Chokekin Lehashlim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and he** also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

This is where the *gemara* invokes two tannaitic principles simultaneously, attributing them both to Rebbi in a famous formulation: *ve-Rebbe savar lah ke-Rabbi Meir, ve-savar lah ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — Rebbi holds like Rabbi Meir in one respect and like Rabbi Yehuda in another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר חוֹקְקִין לְהַשְׁלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **He holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said** the following in the case of an arched gateway in which the lower, straight-walled section is three handbreadths high, and the entire arch is ten handbreadths high: Even if, at the height of ten handbreadths, the arch is less than four handbreadths wide, one considers it as if he **carves out** the space **to complete** it, i.e., the arch has the legal status as though it were actually enlarged to a width of four handbreadths. Similarly, in our case the basket is taken into account and enlarges the tree to a width of four handbreadths.

The first principle is that of Rabbi Meir: *chokekin lehashlim* — we envision a space as though it has been carved out in order to complete the required measurement. The classic example of *chokekin lehashlim* involves an arched doorway that has the required four-*amah* width at the bottom but narrows toward the top: Rabbi Meir permits us to envision the archway as if the requisite rectangular space were carved into it, thereby treating it as meeting the dimensional requirement for a *mezuzah*. Similarly here: even though the branch is only four by two, we envision the remaining space as carved out of the branch, so that together with the basket, a four-by-four *tefachim* space is conceptually present.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Space Req

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר בָּעִינַן עֵירוּב עַל גַּבֵּי מְקוֹם אַרְבָּעָה, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And he** also **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said: We require** that the ***eiruv*** rest **on a place** that is **four** by four handbreadths wide, **and** here **there is not** a width of four handbreadths without taking the basket into account.

The second principle is that of Rabbi Yehuda: *ba'inan eruv al gabei makom arba'ah* — an *eruv* must rest on a space of four by four *tefachim* to be valid. If the *eruv* rests on a space smaller than four by four, it is not considered to occupy a real, significant location and therefore cannot function as an *eruv*. Without *chokekin lehashlim*, the basket case would fail on this ground: the basket sits in a space of four by two, which is below the threshold. But precisely because we apply *chokekin lehashlim*, we envision the full four by four, and Rabbi Yehuda's requirement is satisfied.

The *chiddush* of the basket case thus emerges: (a) the basket completes the four-by-four dimension; (b) *chokekin lehashlim* allows us to treat the completed dimension as halachically real; (c) the *eruv* thereby meets Rabbi Yehuda's space requirement. But a question immediately surfaces — if we have just established a four-by-four space above ten *tefachim*, haven't we recreated the *reshus hayachid* problem?

## Base vs. Top Question

### Rashi Base Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נָעַץ קוֹרָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִנִּיחַ עֵירוּבוֹ עָלֶיהָ, גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה וּרְחָבָה אַרְבָּעָה — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now asks: **What is** the source of the ruling of **Rabbi Yehuda? As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: If one stuck a** cross **beam** into the ground **in the public domain and placed his *eiruv* upon it,** if the cross beam is **ten** handbreadths **high and four** handbreadths **wide,** so that it has the status of a private domain, **his *eiruv* is a** valid ***eiruv*; but if not, his *eiruv* is not a** valid ***eiruv*.**

Rashi resolves this difficulty by introducing a crucial distinction. A *reshus hayachid* requires four by four *tefachim* not only at the top of the structure but at the base. If the base of the structure — the column or trunk supporting the elevated platform — is not four by four, the elevated platform does not constitute a *reshus hayachid*, even if the top surface is four by four.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, הוּא וְעֵירוּבוֹ בִּמְקוֹם אֶחָד! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה — צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּרֹאשָׁהּ אַרְבָּעָה, אֵין גְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה — אֵין צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא בְּרֹאשָׁהּ אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **On the contrary,** if the cross beam is not ten handbreadths high, why shouldn't his *eiruv* be valid? **He and his *eiruv* are in the same place,** i.e., in the public domain. **Rather, this is what he said:** If the cross beam is **ten** handbreadths **high, it is necessary that its top be four** handbreadths wide, so that it can be considered its own domain; but if it is **not ten** handbreadths **high, it is not necessary that its top be four** handbreadths wide because it is considered part of the public domain.

In the basket case, the branch from which the basket hangs is four by two. Even after *chokekin lehashlim* conceptually completes the space to four by four at the top, the base of the structure remains four by two. Because the base is not four by four, the space does not qualify as a *reshus hayachid*. The *eruv* can therefore sit in a space that is virtually four by four for purposes of Rabbi Yehuda's placement requirement, without that space simultaneously constituting a *reshus hayachid* that would make the *eruv* inaccessible.

### Pole Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נָעַץ קָנֶה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִנִּיחַ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ טְרַסְקָל, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו — חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **In accordance with whose** opinion did Ravina offer his explanation, which maintains that we are dealing with a basket that completes the dimension of the tree to four handbreadths and yet it is not treated as a private domain? **It is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** If **one stuck a reed** into the ground **in the public domain, and placed a basket [*teraskal*]** four by four handbreadths wide **on top of it, and threw** an object from the public domain, **and it landed upon it, he is liable** for carrying from a public domain to a private domain. According to Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, if a surface of four by four handbreadths rests at a height of ten handbreadths from the ground, this is sufficient for it to be considered a private domain. Ravina's explanation of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's position, however, does not appear to accept this assumption.

This distinction becomes clearest when the *gemara* introduces the case of a pole. The *baraita* cited in the name of Rabbi Yehuda reads: *na'atz koreh bi'reshus harabbim ve-hinnicho eiruv aleha, gavo'ah yud ve-rochavo arba'ah eruvo eruv* — one drove a post into the *reshus harabbim* and placed an *eruv* on top of it; if the post is ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide, the *eruv* is valid. If not — *ein eruvo eruv*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָתָם — הָדְרָן מְחִיצָתָא. הָכָא — לָא הָדְרָן מְחִיצָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this and claims that this proof is not conclusive: **Even if you say** that Ravina's explanation is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** a distinction can be made: **There,** in the case of the basket resting on a reed, the sides of the basket constitute **partitions** that **surround** the reed on all sides, and we can invoke the principle of: Lower the partition, according to which the partitions are viewed as extending down to the ground. Consequently, a kind of private domain is created within the public domain. **Here,** in the case of the basket hanging from the tree, the **partitions** of the basket **do not surround** the tree, and so they do not suffice to create a private domain.

This initially seems backwards. Ten high and four by four usually means *reshus hayachid*, which should be a problem. The *gemara* clarifies the reading: *gavo'ah asarah tzarich she-ye-hei be-rosho arba'ah* — if the post is ten *tefachim* high, it must have four by four at the top. *Ein gavo'ah asarah, ein tzarich she-ye-hei be-rosho arba'ah* — if it is not ten *tefachim* high, it need not be four by four.

The logic proceeds as follows. Below ten *tefachim*, the airspace of the *reshus harabbim* extends to that height, and the *eruv* on the post is effectively within the same domain as the person standing below — at least for *eruv* purposes. The post need not have its own four-by-four designation because it is, conceptually, continuous with the ground-level domain. But above ten *tefachim*, the post protrudes beyond the *reshus harabbim* airspace. At that height, the *eruv* only "counts as being somewhere" if it occupies a genuine space of four by four *tefachim*. Without that, it is a *makom patur* — too insignificant to be a valid location for an *eruv*.

And what of the concern that ten high and four by four is a *reshus hayachid*? Here Rashi again invokes the four-*amos* principle: within four *amos* of where the *eruv* is placed, everything is considered unified space. One does not worry about the domain distinction between the *reshus hayachid* on top of the pole and the *reshus harabbim* at ground level, because they are within four *amos* of each other and treated as the same space for *eruv* purposes.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

The *gemara* now raises the question: *keman?* — whose position does the basket case reflect? The *gemara* answers that it does not accord with the opinion of Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda.

The *baraita* records: Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda says, *na'atz kaneh bi'reshus harabbim ve-hiniach be-rosho terraskal* — one drove a pole into the *reshus harabbim* and placed a basket on top — *ve-zorak ve-nach le-gabav, chayav* — if one threw an object that landed in that basket, one is liable. This means that a basket atop a pole, even with no four-by-four base, constitutes a *reshus hayachid* sufficient to generate liability. Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda does not require the base to be four by four; the four-by-four dimension at the top alone is sufficient.

If so, the basket in our case — which sits above a two-by-four branch — would also constitute a *reshus hayachid* according to Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda, recreating the problem of the person standing in a *reshus harabbim* while the *eruv* sits in a *reshus hayachid*.

### Mechitza Descent Diff

The *gemara* deflects this challenge: *afilu teima Rabbi Yosi be-Rabbi Yehuda* — even according to Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda, the basket case works. Why? *Hatam hadra mechitzta, hacha lo hadra mechitzta* — there, in the pole-and-basket case, the walls of the basket descend and surround the space below, creating a genuine enclosed domain down through the pole. Here, in our tree-and-basket case, the walls do not descend in the same way. Because the branch is four by two and the basket adds only two more on one dimension, envisioning the walls descending creates not an open enclosed space but rather a column in which the branch itself occupies much of the interior. The *gud achit* principle — envisioning walls as extending downward — cannot produce a clean four-by-four enclosed space below, and therefore even Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda would not classify this as a *reshus hayachid*. Rebbi's basket case therefore works even on Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehuda's view.

## Tilting Basket Access

### Tilt Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר: שָׁאנֵי כַּלְכַּלָּה, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לִנְטוֹתָהּ וְלַהֲבִיאָהּ לְתוֹךְ עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi in the *Tosefta* can be explained in an entirely different manner: **A basket is different, since one can tilt it and** in that way **bring it to within ten** handbreadths of the ground. Without moving the entire basket, one can tilt it and thereby remove the *eiruv* in order to eat it, without carrying it from one domain to another.

Rebbi Yirmiyah offers an entirely different explanation for why placing the *eruv* in a basket suspended from the tree works, even when the basket is above ten *tefachim*. His approach sets aside entirely the analysis of *reshus hayachid*, *chokekin lehashlim*, and base-versus-top distinctions. Instead, he focuses on a practical mechanism of access: *shani kalkala, ho'il ve-yachol le-hatotah u-le-havi'ah le-toch asarah* — the basket is different, because one can tilt it, bringing its contents to within ten *tefachim* of the ground.

When the basket is tilted, the food inside slides or shifts downward, and the *eruv* — now at a position below ten *tefachim* — is no longer in the *reshus hayachid*. At that lowered position, it occupies a *karmelis*, not a *reshus hayachid*.

### Two-Stage Method

The access, according to Rebbi Yirmiyah, thus proceeds in two stages. First, by tilting the basket, one moves the *eruv* from the *reshus hayachid* down into the *karmelis* (below ten *tefachim*). This first movement involves only a rabbinic violation — transferring from *reshus hayachid* into *karmelis* — which Rebbi permits at *bein hashmashos*. Second, from the *karmelis*, one takes the *eruv* to oneself in the *reshus harabbim* — again only a rabbinic violation.

The elegance of Rebbi Yirmiyah's explanation is that it avoids entirely the complex theoretical apparatus of the previous explanation. One does not need to invoke *chokekin lehashlim*, analyze the dimensions of base versus top, or distinguish between the basket's walls descending or not. The reason the basket case works is simply that the basket is tiltable, and tilting it provides a two-step path to the *eruv* that never requires a biblical violation.

This is, as the lecturer notes, a considerably simpler explanation than Ravina's. The reason a basket works, and a bare branch does not, is not because of dimensional analysis but because a basket can be physically tilted — something a fixed branch cannot replicate.

## Physical Placement Q

### Radical Access Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב פָּפָּא וְקָא אָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב בַּר שַׁבָּא לְרַב פָּפָּא: כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? מוֹלִיכוֹ בָּרִאשׁוֹן וּמַחְשִׁיךְ עָלָיו, וְנוֹטְלוֹ וּבָא לוֹ. בַּשֵּׁנִי, מַחְשִׁיךְ עָלָיו וְאוֹכְלוֹ, וּבָא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa sat and recited this *halakha*. Rav bar Shabba raised an objection to Rav Pappa** from the following mishna: **What does one do** if a Festival occurs on Friday, and he wishes to establish an *eiruv* that will be valid for both the Festival and Shabbat? **He brings** the *eiruv* to the location that he wishes to establish as his residence **on** the eve of the **first** day, i.e., the eve of the Festival, **and stays** there **with it until nightfall,** the time when the *eiruv* establishes that location as his residence, **and** then **he takes it** with him **and goes away,** so that it does not become lost before Shabbat begins, in which case he would not have an *eiruv* for Shabbat. **On** the eve of the **second** day, i.e., on Friday afternoon, he takes it back to the same place as the day before, and **stays** there **with it until nightfall,** thereby establishing his Shabbat residence; and then he may then **eat** the *eiruv* **and go away,** if he so desires.

Rav Pappa was sitting and presenting this entire discussion when Rav Shava challenged him with a question that takes all of the preceding theoretical arguments to their logical — and troubling — conclusion. If the principle is that theoretical accessibility suffices — if the *gemara* has been permitting various indirect, multi-step, or hypothetical methods of reaching the *eruv* — then why does one ever need to physically place the *eruv* anywhere at all?

The question can be stated as follows: *naima, keivan d'ee bei lei matzi mati lei* — since in theory one could bring the *eruv* to oneself, *af al gav d'lo mati lei* — even if one did not actually bring it, *ke-man d'mati lei dami* — it is as if one had brought it. If the reasoning that "one could get there in stages" justifies an *eruv* on a branch six *amos* away, then surely "one could hypothetically travel to retrieve any *eruv*" justifies leaving the *eruv* wherever it happens to be.

### Yom Tov/Shabbat Case

The scenario that frames Rav Shava's challenge involves a case where *Yom Tov* falls immediately after *Shabbos* — that is, *Shabbos* leading into Sunday which is *Yom Tov*. One wants to make an *eruv techumin* that will be valid both for *Shabbos* and for the subsequent *Yom Tov*. The problem is that the *eruv* must remain in place through both days, yet the *eruv* food might get eaten or spoiled. The *baraita* prescribes: *molicho ba-rishon* — bring it out to the required location on the first day (*Erev Yom Tov*, Thursday); then take it back home; then on the second day (Friday, which is *Yom Tov*), bring it back to the same location at dusk, *machshich alav* — remain there as darkness falls, and *ochlo* — eat it.

Rashi notes that this scenario assumes the person does not personally go to the destination; rather, he relies on an agent, invoking the principle of *chazakah shliach oseh shelichuso* — we presume an agent completes his mission. Since the agent's presence substitutes for the food, not for the person himself, the food must actually be placed at the destination.

### Rabbi Zeira Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם יוֹם טוֹב שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת אַחַר שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** The fact that one must bring his *eiruv* the day before to the spot that he wishes to establish as his place of residence, and the potential to bring it there does not suffice, is **a decree due to a Festival that occurs after Shabbat.** In that case, the *eiruv* is valid for the Festival only if it was brought there before Shabbat, for it cannot be carried there on Shabbat. Since one cannot actually bring the *eiruv* there, it cannot be said: It is considered as though he did bring it there because had he wished to bring the *eiruv* he could have. Consequently, the Sages decreed that in all cases, the *eiruv* is only valid if it was actually brought to the designated spot, lest one come to think that even on a Festival that occurs after Shabbat it need not be brought there.

Rabbi Zeira supplies the answer to the radical challenge. The only reason one ever needs to physically place an *eruv* at its destination is because of the case of *Yom Tov* that falls after *Shabbos*: *gezeirah mishum Yom Tov she-chal lihiyot achar Shabbos*. When *Yom Tov* falls on Sunday, one must have the *eruv* physically present at the destination as darkness falls on *Shabbos* Friday night leading into Saturday. During that *bein hashmashos*, one cannot even hypothetically bring the *eruv* to the destination, because *Shabbos* is already in effect — one cannot carry on *Shabbos*. There is no theoretical path by which the *eruv* could be brought there. In that case, physical placement is genuinely necessary.

### Theory vs. Practice

In every other standard case — a Friday leading into *Shabbos* — one could, in theory, say: at *bein hashmashos*, I could have brought the *eruv* there through a series of steps involving only rabbinic violations, which Rebbi permits. If that theoretical accessibility is legally sufficient, then physical placement should never be required in the standard case.

The implication is far-reaching: according to this analysis, the entire requirement to physically place one's *eruv* at the desired location may exist only as a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic safeguard — driven by the edge case of *Yom Tov* following *Shabbos*. For the standard *eruv techumin* on an ordinary Friday, the theoretical availability of access may be all that is halachically required.

This is a striking conclusion, and it represents the *gemara*'s own moment of stepping back from all the preceding cases — the branch, the basket, the four-*amos* zone, the theoretical multi-step access — and asking what follows if we take all of these principles seriously. The *gemara* does not reject the logic; it channels it into a specific ruling about physical placement and the *Yom Tov* case, and signals that the fuller implications remain to be worked out in the discussion that continues on the following *daf*.
