# Yevamot 33b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Chiyya vs. Bar Kap…

### Three Prohibition Cases

The sugya on 33b picks up in the middle of a discussion concerning *issur chal al issur* — the question of whether a second prohibition can take effect upon an object or person already subject to a prior prohibition. Three specific cases had been presented: a *zar* (non-*kohen*) who performs the *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash* on *Shabbat*; a *kohen* who is a *ba'al mum* and performs the *avodah* in a state of *tumah* during a circumstance of *tumah hutrah b'tzibbur*; and a *zar* who eats a bird upon which *melikah* had been performed — an act of slaughter that is valid for *kohanim* but renders the bird forbidden to non-*kohanim* at the very same moment it takes effect. The question in each case was whether the individual transgresses two distinct prohibitions or only one.

### Misattribution Question

The debate was attributed to Rabbi Chiyya and Bar Kappara, both of whom swore that Rebbe had taught them his ruling — Rabbi Chiyya reporting that Rebbe said two transgressions, Bar Kappara reporting that Rebbe said one. The *Gemara* had concluded that their dispute was really about *issur b'vas achas* — prohibitions that take effect simultaneously — and whether Rabbi Shimon, who generally holds *ein issur chal al issur*, would concede that when two prohibitions arrive at exactly the same moment, both take effect. That explanation seems coherent. But it raises a sharp difficulty: if Rabbi Chiyya is correct that even Rabbi Shimon would agree to liability in a case of *b'vas achas*, from where did Bar Kappara ever hear that Rebbe taught only one transgression? The *Gemara* asks directly: *l'Rabbi Chiyya, Bar Kappara shikora kameshakeir* — is Bar Kappara simply lying?

### Kolel vs. Bas Achas

The *Gemara* resolves this by having Rabbi Chiyya explain that Bar Kappara did not misrepresent Rebbe deliberately — he misremembered. Specifically: *ki asnaya Rebbe l'didi, tarti l'ftor asnaya* — when Rebbe taught Bar Kappara, and said one is exempt (i.e., only one transgression), he was teaching only two of the three cases, not all three.

The first two cases — the *zar* performing *avodah* on *Shabbat*, and the *ba'al mum* performing *avodah* in a state of *tumah* — can each be understood as sequential. In the first, a person performs *avodah* and *Shabbat* then arrives; in the second, a person is a *ba'al mum* and then becomes *tamei*. In both cases, a first prohibition is already in place and a second prohibition descends upon it afterward. This is the domain of *issur kolel* — where the arriving prohibition does not render the object forbidden to a wider set of people, but rather expands the category of forbidden acts while something already forbidden to the whole world becomes forbidden in a broader sense. In cases of *issur kolel*, even opinions that are more permissive regarding *issur chal al issur* may not grant double liability. Rebbe taught Bar Kappara these two sequential cases according to the view of Rabbi Shimon, who says that even *issur mosif* does not take effect — and certainly *issur kolel*, which is a weaker category, would not — and therefore in these two cases one is liable only once.

The critical distinction is that *issur kolel* is a concept that applies only in sequential settings: first prohibition A, then prohibition B descends and encompasses a broader class of forbidden acts. It is incoherent to speak of *issur kolel* when both prohibitions arrive simultaneously — in a *b'vas achas* scenario, there is no "first" and "second," no earlier prohibition that could block a later one. Bar Kappara heard Rebbe's teaching about the two sequential cases, understood that in those cases (as *issur kolel*) one is liable only once according to Rabbi Shimon, and then — as the *Gemara* explains — inadvertently mixed in the third case of the *zar* who ate the bird on which *melikah* was performed.

### Third Case Confusion

That third case — *melikah* by a non-*kohen* — is categorically different. The moment *melikah* is performed, the bird simultaneously becomes a valid *korban* (and thus forbidden to non-*kohanim*) and becomes forbidden in the sense relevant to the act of *melikah* itself. This case cannot be sequential; it is inherently *b'vas achas*. When Bar Kappara added it to the set in his memory, the combined set of three cases could no longer be characterized as *issur kolel* operating sequentially. The only common denominator he could identify across all three was *b'vas achas*. He then reasoned: since Rebbe taught that in these cases one is liable only once, and these cases are all *b'vas achas*, Rebbe must have ruled that even *b'vas achas* yields only one transgression.

The *Gemara* states this explicitly: *v'aydi d'damya lei arvibihu* — because the third case was similar to the other two (all involving acts normally permitted when done correctly, all involving non-*kohanim* or disqualified *kohanim*), Bar Kappara mixed it in. *V'lo ashkach ela b'vas achas* — examining the merged set, the only explanatory framework he found was *b'vas achas*. *V'savar mid'ha b'vas achas, ha nami b'vas achas* — he concluded that since the *melikah* case is *b'vas achas*, the whole teaching must concern *b'vas achas*. *U'mid'ha l'pattur, ha nami l'pattur* — and since Rebbe had taught exemption, he concluded that *b'vas achas* generally yields only one transgression. He had honestly misremembered — not lied — and that misremembering is what the *Gemara* sets out to explain.

## Three Issur Types

### Mosif, Kolel, Achas

Having worked through the Bar Kappara episode, the *Gemara* pauses to present the systematic framework governing *issur chal al issur* in its three recognized forms. *Issur mosif* is when the newly arriving prohibition expands the range of persons to whom the object is forbidden — it "adds" to the audience of the prohibition. *Issur kolel* is when the new prohibition does not change who is forbidden the object, but broadens the category of acts forbidden in connection with it, encompassing it within a wider class. And *issur b'vas achas* refers to the case where two prohibitions descend simultaneously, with no temporal priority between them — the scenario where the very question of one prohibition "blocking" another loses its footing, because neither arrived first.

### Baraita Evidence

Having established that the debate of Rabbi Chiyya and Bar Kappara concerns whether Rabbi Shimon would concede double liability in a case of *b'vas achas*, the *Gemara* attempts to test this with a *baraita*. The *baraita* states: a *zar* who performs *avodah* on *Shabbat*, or a *ba'al mum* who performs *avodah* in a state of *tumah* even when there is a *korban tzibbur* — in each case there are two transgressions: transgressing as a *zar* and transgressing *Shabbat*, or transgressing as a *ba'al mum* and transgressing *tumah*. This is the position of Rabbi Yose. Rabbi Shimon, however, says one is liable only for the first prohibition — the *zar* status or the *ba'al mum* status — not for the prohibition that arrived afterward.

Crucially, the *baraita* discusses only two of the three cases. The *melikah* case — the *zar* who ate the bird — is absent. The *Gemara* asks: *l'man shiyarah* — for whom's sake was it left out? It cannot have been omitted on account of Rabbi Yose, since Rabbi Yose, who holds *issur chal al issur* even sequentially and even in *issur kolel*, would certainly hold it in a case of *b'vas achas* as well — *hashta Rabbi Yose b'issur kolel mechayev shtayim, b'vas achas mibaya*. If he permits double liability when prohibitions arrive sequentially, he would surely permit it when they arrive simultaneously. There is thus no reason the *baraita* would have omitted the *melikah* case on Rabbi Yose's account.

### Shimon Exemption

It must therefore be Rabbi Shimon for whose sake the case was left out — meaning that Rabbi Shimon would not have the same ruling in the *melikah* case as in the other two. Since Rabbi Shimon says only one transgression in the sequential cases, the *baraita*'s omission of the *melikah* case implies that Rabbi Shimon would say something different there — and the *Gemara* concludes that he would say *two* transgressions when prohibitions arrive simultaneously. *U'b'issur kolel hu d'pattar* — it is specifically in the *issur kolel* sequential scenario that Rabbi Shimon exempts from double liability. *Aval b'issur b'vas achas mechayev* — but in *b'vas achas*, he concedes double liability. This yields the conclusion: *tyufta d'Bar Kappara tyufta* — a refutation of Bar Kappara's position.

It is worth flagging that the force of this proof depends on accepting the *Gemara*'s prior conclusion that the distinction between the *melikah* case and the other two cases is precisely the *b'vas achas* character of the former. Given that, the omission of *melikah* from the *baraita* is indeed significant evidence that Rabbi Shimon treats *b'vas achas* differently — and would hold double liability there. Whether this cleanly refutes Bar Kappara's specific position (as opposed to simply proving that Rabbi Shimon agrees *b'vas achas* yields two transgressions) is a fair question, but the *Gemara* presents it as a refutation.

## Zar on Shabbat

### Which Act Violates

The *Gemara* now turns to a focused question that arises from one of the cases in the *baraita*: the *zar* who performs *avodah* on *Shabbat*. The ruling is that he transgresses twice — once for acting as a *zar* and once for *chillul Shabbat*. But this prompts a precise question: *b'mai* — in what act exactly did he violate *Shabbat*? Every prohibited *melachah* he might have performed in the context of *avodah* is subject to scrutiny.

If he performed *shechitah* — the answer is immediate: *shechitah b'zar kesherah* — a non-*kohen* may perform *shechitah* of a *korban*. This is actually a striking *halachah*: even in the *Beit HaMikdash*, even on *Shabbat* when the *avodah* is permitted, even a woman may perform *shechitah* of a *korban tzibbur*. So *shechitah* by a *zar* is not prohibited, and therefore cannot constitute a *Shabbat* violation in this context. If it was *kabalah* or *holachah* — catching the blood or carrying it to the *mizbeach* — that is merely *tiltul b'alma*, movement within an enclosed space, which does not rise to the level of a *Shabbat* violation in the usual sense. And if it was *haktarah* — burning the limbs on the *mizbeach* — one faces the position of Rabbi Yose himself, that *hava'arah l'lav nitna*: making a fire on *Shabbat*, according to Rabbi Yose, is only a *lav* and not a *chiyuv misah*, since the Torah singles it out explicitly — *lo tivaru eish b'chol moshvoseichem b'yom ha-Shabbat* — and Rabbi Yose derives from that singling-out that it was separated from the general category of *melachot* to teach that it is less severe. Since the discussion concerns equivalent-weight transgressions (carrying *chiyuv* for *korban* purposes), *haktarah* would not serve.

### Par on Yom Kippur

Rav Acha bar Yaakov resolves the difficulty: the case is not *Shabbat* in the ordinary sense but *Yom Kippur*, and the specific act is *shechitah* of the *par* — the ox of the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. The *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is performed exclusively by the *Kohen Gadol*, and according to the opinion that even *shechitah* of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *par* requires the *Kohen Gadol* himself, a *zar* performing that *shechitah* commits a prohibited act — and *shechitah* is universally agreed to constitute a *melachah* on *Shabbat* (or *Yom Kippur*). So the case involves a *zar* who performed the *shechitah* of the *par* on *Yom Kippur*: one transgression for acting as a *zar*, one for violating *Yom Kippur* through *shechitah*.

The *Gemara* then raises a further difficulty: *mai irya zar* — why is the case specifically a *zar*? If the ruling follows the opinion that any non-*Kohen Gadol* cannot perform this *shechitah*, then even an ordinary *kohen* (*kohen hedyot*) would be disqualified. The answer given is *she-zar etzlo ka'amar* — when the case says "*zar*," it means someone who is a *zar* relative to this particular *korban*, i.e., anyone who is not the *Kohen Gadol*. The *Gemara* itself notes that this is a significant stretch in the language.

### Rav Ashi Response

Rav Ashi cuts through the difficulty with a sharper observation. He asks: *midi chataos katani* — did the *baraita* say anything about how many *chataot* one must bring? *O lavei katani* — or about how many *lavin* were transgressed? *Ella issurei b'alma ka chashiv* — the *baraita* is simply counting how many prohibited acts were committed, not calibrating *chiyuvim* of lashes or *korbanos*. The *nafka mina*, Rav Ashi implies, is not the practical consequence of multiple sin-offerings or floggings — it is the more abstract question of how grievous a *rasha* one is for having committed one wrong or two. This observation, while somewhat reductionist in its immediate context, preserves the force of the debate: when the stakes are higher — multiple *chataot*, multiple sets of lashes — the same logic would apply with weightier consequences. Here, the practical implication is simply the moral accounting of the transgression.

## Wife Exchange Case

### Chuppah Exchange

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah*, whose subject matter is closely related to all that preceded it. The *Mishnah* states: *shenayim she-kiddeshu shtei nashim* — two men married two women; the men were unrelated to each other, the women were unrelated to each other. *Uvi-sh'at knisatan la-chuppah* — when the time came to enter the *chuppah* (a euphemism for the consummation of the marriage), *hechlifu et shel zeh la-zeh* — they accidentally exchanged wives. This is reminiscent of the exchange of Rachel and Leah, except that here it is more serious: each man has now had relations with the other man's wife. The critical point is that the *kiddushin* — the formal betrothal — had preceded the *chuppah*: each man was already fully married to his own wife when the exchange occurred. Whether the *chuppah* was a literal canopy under which faces were veiled or a euphemism for the bedroom, the legal reality is identical: each man had relations with another man's wife.

### Four Transgressions

The *Mishnah* then enumerates the transgressions. *Harei elu chayavim mishum eshet ish* — each man transgresses the prohibition of adultery with a married woman. *V'im hayu achim, mishum eshet ach* — if the two men happened to be brothers, there is an additional transgression of relations with a brother's wife. *V'im hayu achayot, mishum isha el achotah* — if the two women happened to be sisters, there is a further transgression, since each man's wife is the other woman's sister, making the illicit partner his wife's sister. *V'im hayu nidot, mishum niddah* — and if the women were *nidot* at the time, there is a fourth transgression. These four can combine in a single case: four transgressions arising from a single act.

### Minors Exception

The *Mishnah* adds: *ve-im hayu ketanot she-ein re-uyot le-yeled, machzirin otan mi-yad* — if the women were minors who were incapable of bearing children, they are returned immediately to their lawful husbands. This exception follows from the principle that *pituy ketanah ones ninhu* — the seduction of a minor is treated legally as coercion, regardless of any apparent consent. A minor's consent is legally meaningless; it constitutes statutory rape in *halachic* terms. Accordingly, even if the act were intentional on the man's part, the woman — being a minor — would not be rendered forbidden to her husband, since her status is that of one who was violated against her will.

### Accident Proof

The *Mishnah* also specifies: *u-mafrishin otan sheloshah chodashim, shema me-ubarot hen* — the women are kept separate from their husbands for three months lest they be pregnant, since a pregnancy by the wrong man would create serious questions of lineage. The *Gemara* engages with the word *hechlifu* — they exchanged — and asks whether this implies intentional wife-swapping, which would be incongruent with the rest of the *Mishnah*. Rav Yehudah rules that the correct reading is *huchlefu* — they were exchanged, passively, accidentally. This reading is confirmed by the *Gemara* from the clause requiring three months of separation: *ve-ei be-meizid, mi sharya* — if it were intentional, how could the women go back to their husbands at all? An intentional act of adultery renders a woman forbidden to her husband. The fact that the *Mishnah* contemplates their return — conditional only on confirming the absence of pregnancy — demonstrates conclusively that the act was unintentional, and the participants are liable to a *korban chatat* rather than subject to the severer consequences of deliberate transgression.

The *Gemara* also notes, from the *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya, that if there are four prohibitions and four parties (two men, two women, each potentially liable), and each brings four *chataot*, the total is sixteen sin-offerings — *sheish esrei chataot*.

## Rabbi Meir Framework

### Four from One Act

With the *Mishnah* established as a case of *shogegin* (inadvertent transgressors), the *Gemara* raises a conceptual question. How can a single act give rise to four distinct transgressions? The case in the *Mishnah* involves prohibitions that did not all exist simultaneously at the moment of betrothal; they accrued at different times. The woman was first an *eshet ish* — a married woman — from the moment of *kiddushin*. She then became an *achot isha* — a sister to someone's wife — only when her sister was also married. She became a *niddah* at a different moment entirely. Each of these prohibitions arrived at a different time. This is precisely the domain of *issur chal al issur*: can a later prohibition take effect upon a person or object already subject to a prior prohibition?

*U-man hai tanna d'it leh issur kolel v'issur mosif v'issur bas achas* — which *tanna* holds that all three categories of *issur chal al issur* are operative? Rav Yehudah said in the name of Rav: it is Rabbi Meir — *Rebbe Meir hi*.

### Chelev Example

The proof comes from a *baraita* in Rabbi Meir's name: *yesh ochel achilah achas v'chayav aleha arba chataot v'asham echad* — there is a case where one can eat a single item in a single act and incur four *chataot* and one *asham*. The scenario: one is *tamei* and eats *chelev* that comes from a *korban*, and this *chelev* is *notar* (left over beyond its permitted time), and it is from the sanctified *mukdashim*, and one does this on *Yom Kippur*. Each of these elements — eating *chelev*, eating as a *tamei* person from *kodshim*, eating *notar*, eating on *Yom Kippur* — independently carries *chiyuv karet* and obligates a *chatat*. The *asham* comes from *me'ilah*, the additional prohibition of deriving benefit from *hekdesh*. That is five in total — four *chataot* and one *asham*. Rabbi Meir adds: if it was *Shabbat* and the person was carrying the food in his mouth while walking through a public domain, there is a sixth. The Sages respond: *eino min ha-shem* — that is not of the same type, since the act of carrying is categorically distinct from the act of eating.

*Tosafot* engages seriously with the Sages' objection, suggesting that perhaps Rabbi Meir's point was that the act of swallowing constitutes a kind of *hanachah* — the placing-down that completes the *melachah* of carrying — making it more integrally connected to the act of eating than it might appear. This remains a somewhat difficult argument, and the *Gemara* does not fully resolve it.

### Chelev Analysis

The *baraita* involving Rabbi Meir's *chelev* case contains all three types of *issur chal al issur*. Rashi explains the structure as follows. The *chelev* begins as a non-sanctified animal (*chullin*), and at that stage the *chelev* is prohibited in eating but permitted in benefit. When the animal is sanctified (*hukdeshah*), an additional prohibition of *hana'ah* — benefit — takes effect on the *chelev*. Rashi identifies this as *issur mosif*, since the sanctification adds the prohibition of *issur hana'ah* on top of the existing *issur achilah*. It must be noted that this is a somewhat unusual application of *issur mosif*: the classic formulation of *issur mosif* involves the object becoming forbidden to a wider group of people, whereas here the object was already forbidden to everyone — the new prohibition simply adds a more stringent dimension (*issur hana'ah*) to the same object for the same people. Rashi's categorization should be noted as contested.

The *chelev* then becomes *notar*, left over past its permitted time. At that moment, as Rashi explains, the object becomes forbidden to the *mizbeach* as well — it can no longer be offered on the altar — *migo d'itosif issura la-mizbeach*. Since it is now forbidden to an additional party (the altar itself), this too constitutes *issur mosif* in the sense of expanding the domain of what is forbidden.

The *tamei* person then arrives and eats. Rashi identifies this as *issur kolel*: the *tamei* person was previously permitted to eat non-sanctified *chelev*, and now, having become *tamei*, he is forbidden from eating all *kodshim* — not just this piece of *chelev* but the entire category. The prohibition does not change the status of the *chelev* but expands the category of things forbidden to this person, which in turn includes this piece. That is the hallmark of *issur kolel*.

### Chelev Analysis

What, then, is the *issur bas achas* in the *chelev* case? Rashi's explanation is acknowledged to be complex. *Tosafot* proposes that Rabbi Meir's addition of *Shabbat* alongside *Yom Kippur* provides the *bas achas* element: on a day that is simultaneously *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur*, the two prohibitions of *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbat* arrive at exactly the same moment and are not sequential. This is the case of *bas achas* — both *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* are in effect simultaneously, so there is no question of one blocking the other, and both are independently operative.

### Meir Following Whom

The *Gemara* then asks: *l'Rabbi Meir aliba d'man* — Rabbi Meir's ruling that all four transgressions in the *Mishnah* are operative: does it align with Rabbi Eliezer or with Rabbi Yehoshua on the separate question that must now be introduced?

## Mitzvah Attempt

### Yehoshua Exemption

There is an independent debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua: *to'eh bid'var mitzvah patur* — one who makes an error in the course of performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from bringing a *korban chatat*. Rabbi Yehoshua holds this exemption; Rabbi Eliezer does not. In the *Mishnah*'s case of the wife exchange, the man who inadvertently had relations with the wrong woman was attempting to fulfill the *mitzvah* of marital relations with his wife — *onah*, the conjugal obligation. If Rabbi Yehoshua exempts one who errs in the course of performing a *mitzvah*, why is the man in the *Mishnah* liable to a *chatat* at all?

The *Gemara* asks: if the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehoshua, the four *chataot* should be inapplicable — since the man erred while attempting a *mitzvah*. It must therefore be that the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Eliezer, who does not grant this exemption. Alternatively — *i ba'is eima* — even if it follows Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption is not absolute.

### Brit Milah Case

Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption is illustrated by the case of a *mohel* who performs *brit milah* on *Shabbat* and accidentally circumcises the wrong infant — the baby born on Sunday rather than the one born on Saturday, whose *brit milah* is duly scheduled for that *Shabbat*. The *mohel* was attempting to perform a *mitzvah* and erred in its execution, and for this error he is exempt from a *chatat* according to Rabbi Yehoshua. Why? Because *d'zmano bahul* — there is a time pressure involved. The *Shabbat milah* must be performed on *Shabbat* specifically, and that urgency creates a mitigating context. Combined with the fact that the person was genuinely engaged in performing a *mitzvah*, the result is that the error falls into the category of an exempted inadvertence.

Two factors thus converge: the attempt to perform a *mitzvah*, and the urgency of a time-bound obligation. One may understand these as jointly producing a kind of *ones* — an externally imposed compulsion that reduces culpability — or alternatively as the *mitzvah*-attempt providing a moral dispensation that can only operate when the degree of negligence is sufficiently low. The time pressure reduces the fault level to the point where the *mitzvah* exemption can take hold.

### No Time Pressure

In the case of the wife exchange, neither mitigating factor is present in full. The man was indeed attempting to fulfill a *mitzvah* — marital relations — but there was no *zman u'vahul*, no time pressure compelling him to act at that moment. Marital relations are not a time-bound obligation in the same way as a *brit milah* scheduled for a specific *Shabbat*. Without the time pressure, the fault level is not mitigated enough for Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption to apply. Accordingly, even according to Rabbi Yehoshua, the man in the *Mishnah* would be liable to bring a *chatat*.

### Terumah Parallel

The *Gemara* raises what appears to be a challenge: the case of *terumah*. A *kohen* eating *terumah* — which is a *mitzvah* — subsequently discovers that he is actually a *chalal* (the son of a divorced woman or a *chalutzah*), and therefore disqualified from *kehunah* and prohibited from eating *terumah*. Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him from having to pay the principal plus the additional fifth (*keren v'chomesh*) that a *zar* who inadvertently eats *terumah* would normally owe. Yet there is no particular time pressure in eating *terumah* — one can eat it at any time. Does this not contradict the claim that Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption requires *zman u'vahul*?

Rav Bibi bar Abaye resolves this by positing that the *terumah* case in question was specifically on *Erev Pesach*: the *kohen* was eating *chametz terumah* before the deadline and was under time pressure to consume it before *Pesach* began, creating exactly the *zman u'vahul* needed. Alternatively — *v'ibayis eima* — the *Mishnah*'s case of the wife exchange may be read as *issur bas achas*, as will now be explored, and the entire preceding analysis may be reconfigured accordingly.

## Bas Achas Reading

### Simultaneous Kiddushin

The *Gemara* now offers an alternative reading of the entire *Mishnah*: perhaps all four prohibitions — *eshet ish*, *eshet ach*, *achot isha*, and *niddah* — applied simultaneously, making this a pure case of *issur bas achas*. If so, the *Mishnah* could hold even according to Rabbi Shimon, who normally denies *issur chal al issur* — since Rabbi Shimon concedes double liability in *bas achas* cases, as the *baraita* proved earlier.

The challenge is to construct a scenario in which all statuses applied at literally the same moment. *Eshet ish* and *eshet ach* are not difficult to synchronize in principle — if two brothers betroth two women simultaneously, both statuses (*married woman* and *brother's wife*) arise at the same instant. Simultaneity of *kiddushin* can be achieved through a *shaliach*: the two brothers appoint a single representative, and the two women appoint a single representative, and the two *shaliachs* execute the *kiddushin* in a single transaction — *paga shliach b'shliach*. In that moment, each woman becomes simultaneously an *eshet ish* and an *eshet ach*, and additionally an *achot isha* (the wife's sister to her brother-in-law).

### Niddah Simultaneity

The *niddah* status, however, is more difficult to synchronize. Either the woman was already a *niddah* before the *kiddushin* — in which case the *niddah* prohibition preceded the others and the question of *issur chal al issur* applies in reverse — or she became a *niddah* afterward, in which case it is again sequential. A stopwatch-precise coincidence of menstruation and *kiddushin* at the same moment seems impossible to engineer.

### Under Three Solution

The *Gemara* resolves this with a striking answer attributed to Rabram in the name of Rav: *b'shofet mitoch gimel l'achar gimel* — the girl was a *niddah* from before her third birthday, and that *niddah* status continued past her third birthday. In practical terms: the girl experienced her first menstrual flow before turning three, and it continued through that birthday. This means that at the moment she turned three years old, she was simultaneously a *niddah* and, for the first time, subject to the full legal consequences of the prohibitions that had already been formally created.

This solution exploits a fundamental *halachah*: sexual relations with a girl under the age of three are not considered *bi'ah* — a legally cognizable act of intercourse — from the Torah's perspective. Consequently, even though the brothers had formally married these girls (their father acting as *shaliach* to accept the *kiddushin* on their behalf), the prohibitions of *eshet ish*, *eshet ach*, and *achot isha* were technically in place but had no practical operative force — because any physical act with them would not constitute *bi'ah*. The *niddah* prohibition similarly was in place but not yet operative as a practical matter.

The moment the girls turned three, all of these prohibitions became simultaneously operative: the *bi'ah* prohibition of *eshet ish*, *eshet ach*, and *achot isha* took effect at that same instant, and the *niddah* prohibition — which had been in effect since before the third birthday and had never been removed by *tevilah* — was equally operative at that moment. Thus, from the standpoint of practical *chiyuv*, all four prohibitions descended simultaneously, constituting a genuine *issur bas achas*.

The *Gemara* further notes that this analysis applies to the men's liability — *l'chiyuvinuhu* — which takes effect when the girls turn three. For the women's own liability, the relevant moment is when they reach *bat mitzvah* (twelve years old). For the *bas achas* analysis to hold for the women as well, the *niddah* status must have been continuously in effect from before age three through age twelve without any intervening *tevilah* — an extended period of nine years. The *Gemara* accepts this as the structure of the case.

## Pregnancy Concern

### First Act

Returning to the three-month separation requirement, the *Gemara* confronts what might seem a logical difficulty. If the wife exchange was an isolated incident — a single mistaken act — why should there be a concern about pregnancy requiring three months of separation? The *Gemara* invokes a widespread belief of the ancient world: *ein ishah misaberet bi-vi'ah rishonah* — a woman does not conceive from the first act of intercourse. If this is so, the one-time error should not produce pregnancy, and no waiting period should be necessary.

The *Gemara* resolves this with the answer of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *sheba'u v'shanu* — they did not err only once; they had relations a second time as well. That second act creates the possibility of pregnancy and justifies the three-month separation. The *Gemara* then notes that if they had relations twice, Rabbi Chiyya's count of sixteen *chataot* might seem too low — shouldn't there be thirty-two? The answer is that the count of sixteen is reckoned from the first act alone, and the question of how many subsequent acts there were (and indeed, according to Rabbi Eliezer, how many distinct *kochos* — thrusts — occurred within a single act) is left aside. The *baraita* counts from the initial *bi'ah*.

### Tamar Question

Rava raises a challenge to the principle that a woman cannot conceive from the first act of intercourse: *veha Tamar d'vi'ah rishonah i'abra* — Tamar became pregnant with Judah from what was apparently a single act, since the verse says *v'lo yasaf od lada'atah* — he did not know her again. If the first act cannot produce pregnancy, how did Tamar conceive?

This question presupposes that Tamar was a virgin when she had relations with Judah — otherwise she might have had prior acts of intercourse and this was not technically her "first." The *Gemara* will now address this presupposition.

### Hymen Removal

Rav answers: *Tamar b'etzba mi'acha* — Tamar had removed her own hymen with her finger before the encounter with Judah. The *Gemara* then cites Rav Yitzchak's tradition: *kol mu'achos shel beit Rebbi, Tamar sheman* — all the girls in the household of Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi who engaged in this practice were given the name Tamar, or were associated with this nickname, because this was understood to be what Tamar herself had done.

This *Gemara* is remarkable in its assumptions about ancient practice and touches on the *halachic* category of *dam besulim* — hymenal blood. The *halachah* (developed toward the end of the Talmudic period and formalized post-Talmudicaly) treats hymenal blood with *niddah*-like stringency rabbinically. The discussion here implicitly engages with the reality that hymenal blood would not be present if the hymen had been previously removed, which has *halachic* ramifications for the wedding night. The Gemara's concern is not the *halachah* of *dam besulim* per se but rather the question of whether Tamar could truly have been a virgin — and it answers that she was technically a virgin (in terms of never having had vaginal intercourse) while not having an intact hymen.

### Er Onan Sin

But if Tamar was a virgin in the relevant sense, what then were Er and Onan doing? The *Gemara* states: *Er v'Onan shimeshu shelo k'darkah* — they practiced non-vaginal intercourse (*bi'ah shelo k'darkah*, anal intercourse), which is why the hymen remained intact and why no conception occurred. This reading of the verse *va-yishchet artzah* — Onan "spoiled [his seed] to the ground" — differs from the *peshat*, which most naturally reads as a description of withdrawal and external ejaculation. The *Gemara* here reads the verse as describing the inherent nature of the act (non-vaginal intercourse necessarily results in the seed going to the ground), not as a separate act of withdrawal mid-intercourse.

## Zera Le-Vatalah

### Nursing Women

The *Gemara* now cites the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer regarding a nursing woman: *kol esrim v'arba chodesh dash m'bifnim v'zoreh m'bachutz* — for the full twenty-four months of nursing, a husband should thresh on the inside and winnow on the outside. The agricultural metaphor — *dash* refers to the threshing that loosens grain from chaff, *zoreh* to the winnowing that separates them through the wind — describes intercourse with external ejaculation (early withdrawal). Rabbi Eliezer's rationale is that pregnancy would endanger the nursing infant by potentially disrupting the milk supply, and he prescribes this method as a form of birth control for the nursing period.

### Withdrawal Critique

The Sages respond sharply: *halalu eino ela k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan* — this practice is like the sin of Er and Onan. The objection is categorical: early withdrawal, which results in seminal emission outside the wife, constitutes *zera l'vatalah* — the destruction of seed — and is prohibited.

### Tamar Proof

The *Gemara* then connects the Onan case to the earlier discussion of Tamar. Since Onan's sin is described as *va-yishchet artzah*, and we now know this meant *bi'ah shelo k'darkah* rather than simple withdrawal, the Sages' equation of Rabbi Eliezer's practice with *ma'aseh Er v'Onan* requires clarification: in what sense is early withdrawal "like" what Er and Onan did, if Er and Onan did something categorically different (non-vaginal intercourse)?

The *Gemara* resolves this with a careful formulation: *k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan v'lo k'ma'aseh Er v'Onan* — similar to but not identical with what Er and Onan did. The similarity is that in both cases the semen is emitted outside the body of the wife — *va-yishchet artzah*, the seed is destroyed. The difference is that Er and Onan engaged in *bi'ah shelo k'darkah* (non-vaginal intercourse), while Rabbi Eliezer's practice involves normal vaginal intercourse (*k'darkah*) with external ejaculation. The common thread is the destruction of seed; the manner differs.

As for Er's sin specifically — the *Gemara* asks: *Onan* we understand, since the verse says *va-yishchet artzah* of him explicitly. *Er minalan* — how do we know Er sinned in the same way? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak derives it from the word *gam* — *va-yamet gam oto*, "he also died": the *gam* indicates that Er died for the same sin as Onan. And why did Er do it? *K'dei shelo tisaber v'yachish yofyah* — so that Tamar would not become pregnant and her beauty would not be diminished by pregnancy.

### Tosafot Views

*Tosafot* engages directly with the apparent tension in this *Gemara*. On the one hand, the *Gemara* here condemns external ejaculation as akin to *ma'aseh Er v'Onan*. On the other hand, a *Gemara* in Nedarim appears to permit *bi'ah shelo k'darkah* as a legitimate mode of marital intercourse, which necessarily involves seminal emission outside the wife. *Tosafot* presents two answers in the name of the *Ri*. The first: the *Gemara* in Nedarim speaks only of *bi'ah shelo k'darkah* performed without seminal emission — *b'lo hotza'at zera d'shari* — and the prohibition of *ma'aseh Er v'Onan* applies only when there is actual destruction of seed. Without emission, the act is not *zera l'vatalah* regardless of the manner of intercourse. The second answer of the *Ri* is broader and more permissive: the prohibition of *zera l'vatalah* applies only when the husband specifically intends to destroy the seed — *k'she-mitkaven l'hashchit ha-zera* — and when this is a habitual practice — *v'ragil la'asot ken tamid*. But if the act arises spontaneously from desire for one's wife, and is not motivated by the purpose of avoiding procreation or destroying seed as such, then it is permitted — *d'amar b'Nedarim kol mah she-adam rotzeh la'asot b'ishto ya'aseh*.

### Rama's Ruling

This second position of the *Ri* is of considerable practical *halachic* significance. The *Rama* rules in accordance with this second interpretation of *Tosafot*: within the context of marital relations, the prohibition of *zera l'vatalah* is not triggered by the place or manner of seminal emission but only by the intent and habitual purpose of destroying seed to avoid procreation. Any form of sexual activity between husband and wife, without that specific intent, is not categorized as *zera l'vatalah*. This ruling is fundamental to understanding the *halachic* parameters of marital intimacy, and represents the authoritative *Ashkenazic* position on the question.
